# ABSTRACT

As one of the most important crops in the world, rice (Oryza sativa) is a model plant for metabolome research. Although many studies have focused on the analysis of specific tissues, the changes in metabolite abundance across the entire life cycle have not yet been determined. In this study, combining both targeted and nontargeted metabolite profiling methods, a total of 825 annotated metabolites were quantified in rice samples from different tissues covering the entire life cycle. The contents of metabolites in different tissues of rice were significantly different, with various metabolites accumulating in the plumule and radicle during seed germination. Combining these data with transcriptome data obtained from the same time period, we constructed the Rice Metabolic Regulation Network. The metabolites and co-expressed genes were further divided into 12 clusters according to their accumulation patterns, with members within each cluster displaying a uniform and clear pattern of abundance across development. Using this dataset, we established a comprehensive metabolic profile of the rice life cycle and used two independent strategies to identify novel transcription factors—namely the use of known regulatory genes as bait to screen for new networks underlying lignin metabolism and the unbiased identification of new glycerophospholipid metabolism regulators on the basis of tissue specificity. This study thus demonstrates how guilt-byassociation analysis of metabolome and transcriptome data spanning the entire life cycle in cereal crops provides novel resources and tools to aid in understanding the mechanisms underlying important agronomic traits.

# INTRODUCTION

As sessile organisms, plants need to produce a huge arsenal of metabolites during their growth and development in order to adapt to changing environments and various stresses (Wang et al., 2019; Alseekh et al., 2021). In addition, various internal and external factors may lead to changes in abundance of specific metabolites and metabolic fluxes or, alternatively, the coordinated regulation of multiple metabolic pathways. Rice 

(Oryza sativa L.) is one of the most important food crops in the world, feeding more than half of the world’s population. Moreover, rice is a monocotyledonous model plant, and the study of its abundant metabolites contributes considerably to rice basic research and breeding practices (Kang et al., 2019;

Mutuku et al., 2019). The phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway is one of the major secondary metabolic pathways of higher plants, and its products include lignin, flavonoids, and hydroxycinnamate amides, which play important roles in plant development and plant–environment interactions (Dong and Lin, 2021). Lignin provides not only the structural basis for the formation of industrial products, such as ducts and fiber molecules, but also serves as a barrier against pest and disease attack. As such, its synthesis is related to the normal growth of plants and resistance to disease and stress (Vanholme et al., 2019). Among other metabolites that affect plant growth and development are lipids, mainly triglycerides, fatty acids, phospholipids, and waxes. These compounds are widely involved in growth and development processes, such as cell membrane remodeling, anther fertility, seed formation, and responses to adverse stresses such as cold and drought (Wan et al., 2020). Specifically, phospholipids are widely involved in remodeling, signal transduction, and the regulation of biofilm formation, and phospholipid metabolism in rice also affects rice quality (Amagliani et al., 2016; Colin and Jaillais, 2020). They can, furthermore, influence other aspects of plant growth, development, and nutritional quality that are important in rice production, so they have great potential as molecular breeding targets. To sum up, it is necessary to study the changes of metabolites and their underlying regulatory mechanisms from a more macroscopic perspective, namely the "metabolome," and the plant metabolome currently plays a vital role in modern plant biology research and biotechnology applications (Luo, 2015; Kosmacz et al., 2020). 

Thanks largely to the establishment of a high-throughput metabolite detection technology for rice (Chen et al., 2013), metabolome detection has been applied to many different aspects of rice biology. During rice growth and development, metabolome detection has been used to study the relationship between grain metabolism and agronomic traits, with diverse metabolic shifts being observed during grain development as well as during internode elongation and seed development (Chen et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2016, 2020). Moreover, in terms of resistance to disease and stress in rice, metabolomics has been used to study the physiological changes of rice in response to pests, diseases, and parasitism (Kang et al., 2019; Mutuku et al., 2019). Furthermore, in breeding programs, metabolomics can be used as a core strategy to predict the yield and grain weight of hybrid rice under different environments (Xu et al., 2016; Dan et al., 2019). Finally, comparative metabolomics has been used to define how domestication shaped chemical composition in rice (Deng et al., 2020; Alseekh et al., 2021). However, most of the previous studies have focused either on whole plants or on specific single tissues. In fact, many differentially expressed metabolites and genes display not only dynamic changes during different developmental periods in specific tissues but also different tissue specificities. Such spatio-temporal diversity limits the insight gained from the study of whole plants or single tissues, which are too mixed and provide insufficient coverage, respectively. In recent years, there have been some reports of using multiple tissues to study the changes of metabolite abundance in rice. A broadly targeted metabolomics approach was used to quantify 90 flavonoid and 16 phenolamide metabolites in rice seedlings, leaves, culms, panicles, and roots and to identify two spermidine hydroxycinnamoyl transferases (Dong et al., 2014, 2015). 

However, the distribution of a large number of important metabolites in rice tissues is still unknown. Therefore, it is necessary to establish a rice metabolic database that covers the entire growth period. 

In recent years, metabolomics has been increasingly used in conjunction with other tools for the identification of gene functions and the elucidation of metabolic network regulation (Hoffmann et al., 2012). For example, combining the metabolome and genome to study oil synthesis in maize kernels identified candidate genes for important metabolites during growth and development in rice leaves (Li et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014; Zhan et al., 2020). Similarly, combined metabolome and proteome data were used to study physiological responses under cold stress in maize (Urrutia et al., 2021), while combined metabolome, genome, and transcriptome data were used to catalog the metabolic changes in tomato during domestication and improvement (Zhu et al., 2018). Given the influence of transcriptional regulation on metabolite biosynthesis, combining metabolome and transcriptome analysis can be a highly efficient method by which to identify metabolic pathways and their regulatory genes (Wang et al., 2018). Currently, the association between the plant metabolome and transcriptome is mostly studied for specific tissues, such as, for example, roots under nitrogen treatment conditions (Xin et al., 2019) and stem tips of tea leaves under drought stress (Li et al., 2020a). 

Recent advances in high-throughput transcriptomics and metabolomics have made it possible to conduct integrated analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome on a larger scale. Following this route, association analysis in specific tissues and at different developmental stages has been carried out; for example, association analysis has been performed for different stages of red maple leaf color formation (Lu et al., 2020) and colored cotton fiber formation (Sun et al., 2019). This approach aids in tracking the dynamics of metabolites and their corresponding regulatory genes within the same tissues. Therefore, association analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome covering the whole growth period could be an effective tool by which to analyze the factors underlying metabolite abundance. As a recent example, in tomato, 20 samples covering different tissues and developmental stages were used for parallel metabolome and transcriptome detection to draw a high-resolution map of the metabolic regulatory network during key developmental stages (Li et al., 2020c). This study was the first metabolomic and transcriptomic association analysis covering the whole growth period of a dicotyledonous plant, and thus provided new ideas on how to reveal the regulatory mechanisms underlying metabolic pathways. A gene expression atlas covering the entire life cycle was reported for rice, an important food crop and monocotyledonous model plant (Wang et al., 2010); however, correlation analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome across the whole growth period has not yet been carried out.

# RESULTS

Metabolomics analysis of rice across the entire life cycle 

To comprehensively assess and characterize the spatiotemporally distributed metabolic regulatory networks during the life cycle of rice, we produced the RMRN dataset from two conventional indica varieties, Zhenshan 97 and Minghui 63, the parents of Shanyou 63, which is one of the most widely cultivated rice hybrids in China (Figure 1). This dataset comprises parallel metabolic profiling and gene expression atlas data for samples collected from all major development stages and tissues or organs of rice. We collected 20 tissue or organ types: plumules and radicles (germinated seeds grown under light or dark conditions for 48 h), shoots and roots (from seedlings at the two-tiller stage), leaves 

(from plants with young panicles less than $1 \ \mathsf { m m }$ , with young panicles $4 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ , at 5 days before heading, and at 14 days after pollination), sheaths (from plants with young panicles less than 1 mm and young panicles $4 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ ), stems (from plants 5 days before heading and at the heading stage), panicles (from plants with $4 0 { - } 5 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ young panicles and at 14 days after pollination), and endosperm (7, 14, and 21 days after pollination) (Figure 1 and Supplemental Data 1). Three biological replicates, each of which was a mixed sample from at least three plants, were analyzed for all 40 development stages and tissues or organs collected.

# Metabolite accumulation patterns during development

To analyze the accumulation patterns of metabolites during rice developments, we performed a hierarchical clustering analysis of the contents of these 825 metabolites within 40 different samples and showed that tissue specificity was greater than variety specificity; no metabolites were detected only in Zhenshan 97 or Minghui 63 (Figure 3A). Differential metabolite analysis showed that only a small proportion of metabolites were significantly different between these two varieties (Supplemental Data 3). Hierarchical clustering analysis revealed that they could be divided into their eight component tissues, namely plumules, radicles, leaves, sheaths, stems, roots, panicles, and seeds (Figure 3A). Further analysis showed that they could be classified into three large groups: metabolites present in germinating tissues (plumules and radicles), vegetative tissues (roots, stems, sheaths, and leaves), and reproductive tissues (panicles and seeds). This result shows that different tissues in the same group have similar metabolite patterns, which are significantly different from those of other groups. For example, metabolites such as lipids, hydroxycinnamic acid and derivatives, organic acids, phenolamides, phytohormones, and vitamins accumulated preferentially in germinating tissues of rice, whereas flavonoids, terpenes, and some phenolamides were more abundant in vegetative tissues, and sugars, some other lipids, and amino 

acids mainly accumulated in reproductive tissues (Figure 3A). Since flavonoids, phenolamides, and lipids can be classified into different subclasses according to their structure or type of modification, we analyzed these metabolites in detail. Consistent with previous results, most mono-C/O-glycosylflavonoids, bis-Oglycosylflavonoids, and C-glycosylflavonoid-O-hexosides were enriched in the leaves and sheaths in comparison with their levels in other tissues (Supplemental Figure 3) (Dong et al., 2014). Four anthocyanins, cyanin, cyanidin 3-glucoside, cyanidin 5-O-glucoside, and cyanidin O-diglucoside, showed a seed-specific accumulation pattern, which is consistent with the observation that high levels of anthocyanins accumulate in Arabidopsis seeds (Routaboul et al., 2006). In addition, we found that the contents of most of the flavonoids increased during panicle development, especially naringenin, delphinidin, and quercetin, all of which peaked in the panicle at the heading stage (Panicle 2). Only a limited number of phenolamides have previously been detected, and these were mainly found to be concentrated in rice roots (Dong et al., 2015). By contrast our results suggest that phenolamides accumulate mainly in panicles, followed by plumules, radicles, leaves, sheaths, stems, roots, and finally seeds (Supplemental Figure 4). In addition, we found that most of the mono-HCAA (hydroxycinnamic acid)-substituted and bis-HCAA-substituted phenolamides accumulated in the plumules and radicles, while the tris-HCAA-substituted phenolamides accumulated in the leaves, sheaths, and stems. In this study, lipids were classified as fatty acids (FFAs), glycerolipids, LPC, LPE, PC, PE, sphingolipids, or sterol lipids. The plumules and radicles contained the highest levels of most lipids, followed by the seeds and roots. By contrast, leaves, sheaths, panicles, and stems displayed the lowest accumulation of most lipids (Supplemental Figure 5). However, we did observe a large accumulation of fatty acids in germinating tissue, which may be a consequence of lipid catabolism during seedling establishment (Cai et al., 2020). Interestingly, we found that almost all monoacylglycerophospholipids (LPC, LPE) were enriched in seeds and a large number of diacylglycerophospholipids (PC, PE) accumulated in tissues other than seeds (Supplemental Figure 5). Taken together, these results suggest that primary metabolites are more highly abundant in reproductive tissues, secondary metabolites are more abundant in vegetative tissues, and almost all metabolites are abundant in germinating tissues. This may be because a large number of metabolites are required to participate in seedling establishment during germination (Shaik et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2019; Cai et al., 2020), while secondary metabolites are produced in large quantities in response to biotic and abiotic stresses during vegetative growth (Murcia et al., 2017), and primary metabolites, such as sugars and oils, accumulate during reproduction (Baud et al., 2007; Hajduch et al., 2011; Sew et al., 2016). 

We next performed PCA, and in line with the hierarchical clustering analysis the tissues were divided into 8 groups according to the 825 metabolites, each associated with one specific tissue type (Figure 3B). Samples of the same tissue from different varieties at different developmental stages had similar metabolic patterns. Stems, leaves, and sheaths were clustered together in the PCA score plot, indicating that the metabolic profiles among these tissues were similar.

Phylogenetic analysis revealed that the MYB family proteins encoded by OsMYB55 and OsMYB110 belong to the R2R3- type MYBs (Supplemental Figure 7B), which are highly homologous to OsMYB30. These data suggest that OsMYB55 and OsMYB110 are indeed involved in the lignin pathway. 

We then analyzed the expression levels of OsMYB55 and Os-MYB110 at different developmental stages and in different tissues. Our gene expression atlas analysis indicated that expression of OsMYB55 and OsMYB110, like that of known lignin biosynthetic genes, was high in multiple tissues, including plumules, radicles, roots, and mature leaves (Figure 5B). A similar pattern was also found when observing lignin precursor metabolites (Figure 5B). qRT–PCR analysis confirmed the expression patterns of OsMYB55 and OsMYB110 (Supplemental Figure 10A–10C). Subcellular localization showed that OsMYB110 was exclusively located in the nucleus (Supplemental Figure 11). 

To further investigate whether OsMYB110 directly induces the expression of lignin biosynthetic genes, we performed dual luciferase reporter assays using rice protoplasts. We cloned the promoters of three OsMYB110-induced biosynthetic genes involved in the lignin precursor pathway (OsPAL1, OsPAL2, and OsPAL3). The results showed that the activities of these promoters were all significantly higher when OsMYB110 was expressed than in the control (Figure 5C), indicating that OsMYB110 is a positive regulator of the lignin biosynthetic pathway. Furthermore, results of a yeast-one-hybrid assay indicated that OsMYB110 binds to the OsPAL1, OsPAL2, and OsPAL3 promoters in addition to the Os4CL3 and Os4CL5 promoters (Figure 5D). This demonstrated that OsMYB110 not only regulates Os4CL3 and Os4CL5 but also regulates and activates OsPAL1, OsPAL2, and OsPAL3; thus, it participates in lignin synthesis. 

To investigate the potential function of OsMYB110, we generated transgenic rice plants overexpressing OsMYB110 driven by the maize ubiquitin promoter. We then performed qRT–PCR analysis of OsMYB110-ox transgenic lines and WT leaf samples at the two-tiller stage, and found that the expression of lignin biosynthesis genes, including Os4CL3, Os4CL5, OsPAL1, OsPAL2, and OsPAL3, was significantly higher in the transgenic lines (Figure 5E). We further found that OsMYB110-ox leaves contained approximately $4 8 \%$ more lignin compared with ZH11 leaves (Figure 5F). OsMYB110-ox transgenic lines developed significantly smaller lesions 14 days after infection with the bacterial pathogen Xoo (PXO99 and 341) than the WT (ZH11) (Supplemental Figure 12A and 12B). Therefore, our combined results confirmed that overexpression of OsMYB110 increases lignin content, which can increase the disease resistance of plant leaves. 

We also generated OsMYB55 overexpression and RNAi transgenic rice plants. We then performed qRT–PCR analysis of OsMYB55-ox, OsMYB55-RNAi, and WT leaf samples at the two tillers stage and found that the expression of lignin biosynthesis 

genes, including Os4CL3, Os4CL5, OsPAL6, and OsC4H, was significantly higher in the MYB55-ox lines, but lower in the MYB55-RNAi lines (Supplemental Figure 13A). We further found that OsMYB55-ox leaves contained approximately $1 5 \%$ more lignin and that MYB55-RNAi leaves contained approximately $1 3 \%$ less lignin compared with ZH11 leaves (Supplemental Figure 13C). OsMYB55-ox transgenic lines developed significantly smaller lesions 14 days after infection with the bacterial pathogen Xoo (PXO99 and 341) than ZH11, while the lesions of OsMYB55-RNAi transgenic lines were significantly larger than those of ZH11 (Supplemental Figure 12C and 12D). Therefore, these results suggest that OsMYB55 may control lignin synthesis and thus increase resistance to pathogens by regulating Os4CL3, Os4CL5, OsPAL6, and OsC4H. In summary, our spatio-temporal data show that there is a strong relationship between the production of metabolites and changes in gene expression, indicating that RMRN data can be used to study the regulation of important metabolic pathways in rice. 

# Identification of a novel transcription factor that regulates glycerophospholipid metabolism

The fundamental role of lipids in plants has led to much research on them (Correa et al., 2020). A variety of transcription factors, including those in the MYB, AP2, and B3 classes, have been found to be involved in the regulation of lipid synthesis (Song et al., 2017). In particular, the regulation of wax and fatty acid synthesis by AP2-like transcription factors has attracted extensive attention (Aharoni et al., 2004; Broun et al., 2004; Kannangara et al., 2007; Djemal and Khoudi, 2019). For example, wax is a protective cover on the plant surface, and both Arabidopsis AP2-like transcription factor RAP2.4 and rice AP2-like transcription factor OsWR1 can promote wax synthesis on the leaf surface under drought stress by activating the expression of wax synthesis genes, thus achieving drought resistance (Wang et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2020). Metabolism of the important plant lipids glycerophospholipids is initiated with glycerol 3-phosphate; after the synthesis of 1,2-diacyl-sn-glycerol 3-phosphate catalyzed by glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase, lysophosphatidic acid acyltransferase, and phosphatidic acid phosphatase, a series of glycerophospholipid metabolism pathway enzymes catalyze diverse downstream reactions, leading to the biosynthesis of glycerophospholipids (Supplemental Figure 14A). In the co-regulation network that we constructed, 41 lipids out of 80 metabolites were found to be specifically accumulated in seeds (cluster VIII), 31 of which were glycerophospholipids (Supplemental Figure 15A, Supplemental Data 5, cluster VIII). We further found that AP2/ERF family genes are coexpressed with these lipids (Supplemental Figure 15B). A strong association between AP2-like transcription factors and lipid metabolism has been previously reported, but the relationship with glycerophospholipid metabolism remains unclear. 

We next attempted to use the RMRN to identify AP2/ERF regulators that control glycerophospholipid metabolism because these primary compounds are important for rice growth and seed 

quality. Among the 825 metabolites, 26 monoacylglycerophospholipids (LPC and LPE) were detected mainly in seeds, while the other diacylglycerophospholipids (PC, PE, and PS) were mainly found in multiple tissues other than seeds, consistent with the previous hierarchical clustering analysis results (Figure 6A and Supplemental Data 7). Furthermore, we found that 12 AP2/ERF transcription factors and 7 genes related to glycerophospholipid metabolism were also enriched in cluster VIII (seeds) (Supplemental Figure 14B). We searched this cluster and found that a gene, OsDREB2A (LOC_Os01g07120), encoding an AP2/EREBP domain transcription factor, was also strongly co-expressed with these glycerophospholipid metabolism genes and co-accumulated with glycerophospholipid metabolites (Figure 6A). Among them, OsPLAIVa was significantly co-expressed with OsDREB2A, with a coexpression coefficient of 0.71 (Figure 6B). KEGG analysis of the related genes and metabolites revealed that their molecular functions were enriched in glycerophospholipid metabolism, which suggested that OsDREB2A could potentially modulate glycerophospholipid metabolism (Supplemental Figure 16). Phylogenetic analysis revealed that the AP2 domain-containing protein encoded by OsDREB2A belongs to the DREB subfamily (Sakuma et al., 2002), members of which are homologous to AtDREB2A (Supplemental Figure 17A). By recognizing DRE with the A/GCCGAC core motif, genes of the DREB subfamily have been found to play a major role in plant resistance to abiotic stresses. (Zhang et al., 2013). The JASPAR website was also used to predict the presence of OsDREB2A binding elements on the promoters of glycerophospholipid metabolism genes, such as OsPLAIVa, OsPLAIIIa, OsPLDa, and OsMHZ11 (Supplemental Figure 17C). It was found previously that overexpression of the DREB transcription factor GMDREBL in Arabidopsis thaliana could increase the content of fatty acids in seeds (Zhang et al., 2016). These data suggest that OsDREB2A may be involved in glycerophospholipid metabolism, which is a novel finding. 

To further investigate whether OsDREB2A directly induces the expression of glycerophospholipid metabolism genes, we performed dual luciferase reporter assays using rice protoplasts. We cloned the promoters of three OsDREB2A-induced biosynthetic genes involved in glycerophospholipid metabolism (OsPLAIVa, OsPLAIIIa, and OsPLDa). The results showed that the activities of these promoters were all significantly higher when OsDREB2A was expressed than in the control (Figure 6C), indicating that OsDREB2A is a positive regulator of glycerophospholipid metabolism. 

To investigate the potential metabolic function of OsDREB2A, we generated transgenic rice plants overexpressing the gene under the control of the maize ubiquitin promoter. We then performed qRT–PCR analysis of OsDREB2A-ox transgenic lines and WT seed samples during the pustulation period, and found that the expression of glycerophospholipid metabolism genes, including OsPLAIVa, OsPLAIIIa, $O s P L D \alpha$ , and OsMHZ11, was significantly higher in the transgenic lines (Figure 6D). We noted that expression of the OsPLAIVa gene was stable and significantly $( P = 3 . 5 \mathsf { E } \mathsf { - } 4 )$ ) increased by about two-fold in the overexpression lines. Phylogenetic analysis revealed that OsPLAIVa is highly homologous to OsPLAIIIa and OsMHZ11 (Supplemental Figure 17B), both of which belong to the PLA family, which is 

involved in glycerophospholipid degradation (Liu et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2020). We subsequently found that most of the contents of monoacylglycerophospholipids (LPC and LPE) in seeds of the overexpressors were significantly higher and that diacylglycerolphospholipids (PC, PE, and PS) were lower in OsDREB2A-ox seeds compared with ZH11 control seeds (Figure 6E). On the basis of these results, we speculate that OsDREB2A may control glycerophospholipid metabolism by regulating OsPLAIVa. Based on the known roles of DREB subfamily members, experiments assessing resistance to abiotic stress, dehydration, and salt stress experiments were carried out (Cui et al., 2011; Mallikarjuna et al., 2011). We found that the survival rate of OsDREB2A-ox seedlings was higher than that of ZH11 control seedlings under both PEG6000 and NaCl stress, and growth performance in terms of shoot and root length was also significantly higher than that of ZH11 seedlings (Supplemental Figure 18). Thus, this demonstrates that the RMRN dataset can identify new transcription factors and reveal metabolic regulatory networks that play an important role in the developmental cycle of rice. 

# DISCUSSION

Here, we present a comprehensive analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome covering both the major tissues and growth stages of rice. The RMRN dataset generated in this study includes data for 825 metabolites and 38 793 expressed genes that were detected in at least one tissue or stage. According to the analysis of the metabolomes and transcriptomes of different tissues and stages, we determined that 825 metabolites and 18 757 genes can be divided into 12 clusters, and that the accumulation pattern of metabolites in each cluster is consistent with the gene expression pattern (Figure 4 and Supplemental Figure 6A and 6B). 

In this study, targeted and nontargeted metabolomics assays were used to cover as many of the major metabolites as possible. However, partial metabolic signals detected by untargeted metabolomics were not used for further analysis. The lack of adequate annotation of these signals and the limitations of the detection methods may thus result in some important metabolites not being quantified. A high-resolution fragment database can be established by performing nontargeted metabolomics detection in multiple species. Based on statistics of metabolite fragmentation rules, combined with the authentic fragments from standards or published databases or literature, metabolites can be annotated with the aid of bioinformatics methods, such as fragment comparison or structure group assembly, which may help to establish a more comprehensive metabolic database with secondary mass spectral tags (Neumann and Bocker, 2010; Wang et al., 2016; Usade et al., 2018; Perez de Souza et al., 2019; Duhrkop et al., 2019;Qi et al., 2021). Therefore, in addition to the 12 metabolite clusters identified in this paper, future metabolome analysis of the same sample may reveal the accumulation patterns of new metabolic networks. When a metabolome database and transcriptome data with broader coverage are available, the information obtained from metabolite-gene association analysis may be further increased, providing a yet richer resource for thorough study of metabolic regulatory networks throughout the entire life cycle of rice. 

In recent years, the multi-omics studies of crops have largely been limited to a single tissue or a mixture of several tissues, which makes it difficult to effectively find genes that control a metabolite or pathway if they is not expressed in these tissues. Since our RMRN database covers the major tissues or organs during the entire growth period of rice, it can be used to identify new regulatory networks independently or in combination with other multiomics data. Using the RMRN dataset, we constructed a global map of metabolic changes during rice growth, which allowed us to make new discoveries, including significant metabolic differences between the vegetative tissues and germinating and reproductive tissues (Figure 3). In addition, a large number of metabolites, such as amino acids and most lipids, including fatty acids and phospholipids, were found to be enriched during rice seed germination (cluster IX, Figure 3A and Table 1), and phenylpropanoids, such as flavonoids and partial phenolamides, were found to be abundant in vegetative tissues (cluster X, Supplemental Figures 3 and 4). During rice maturation (reproductive tissues), the content of phenylpropanoids decreases significantly, except for anthocyanins, which are enriched in seeds (cluster IV and X, Figure 5B and Supplemental Figure 3). Monoacylglycerophospholipids (LPC and LPE) gradually increase during seed maturation, while diacylglycerolphospholipids (PC, PE, and PS) decrease during rice maturation (cluster VIII, Figure 6A). The changes of these and other important metabolites are consistent with the corresponding transcriptional changes (Figure 4, Supplemental Figure 6). It is noteworthy that, although the tissue specificity was higher than the variety specificity in this dataset, the differences between the two varieties, Zhenshan 97 and Minghui 63, at the seed stage (especially 21 days after pollination) were significantly greater than those in the other tissues (Figure 3B). Since the quality of the rice grain is a major breeding target, the difference between varieties at this stage may be the result of breeding activities. The RMRN dataset can provide a valuable resource for the comprehensive study of rice metabolism regulation. 

Utilizing the RMRN dataset, in addition to confirming previously known transcription factors, we also identified new metabolic regulatory networks. Previous studies have demonstrated that OsMYB30, OsMYB55, and OsMYB110 accumulate lignin by activating the cinnamate/monolignol pathway (Kishi-Kaboshi et al., 2018). Moreover, OsMYB30 can directly bind Os4CL3 and Os4CL5 to increase the accumulation of lignin subunits G and S (Li et al., 2020b). In this paper, we found that in addition to regulating Os4CL3 and Os4CL5, OsMYB110 also regulates and activates OsPAL1, OsPAL2, and OsPAL3 to participate in lignin synthesis (Figure 5), which contributes to resistance to disease and stress (Vanholme et al., 2019). Similarly, we also found that OsMYB55 may control lignin synthesis by regulating Os4CL3, Os4CL5, OsPAL6, and OsC4H (Supplemental Figure 13). Therefore, we verified that OsMYB30, OsMYB55, and OsMYB110 can regulate genes of the lignin synthesis pathway and thereby regulate lignin synthesis. This finding provides a theoretical basis for breeding varieties with excellent agronomic traits, such as disease and lodging resistance. 

The RMRN dataset also provided an unbiased resource to screen for other regulators of glycerophospholipid metabolism. Previous studies suggested a strong link between AP2-like transcription 

factors and metabolism of fatty acids, but the relationship of these transcription factors with glycerophospholipid metabolism remains unclear (Baud et al., 2007; Maeo et al., 2009; Trinh et al., 2019). Based on tissue specificity, we found that OsDREB2A is strongly co-expressed with these glycerophospholipid metabolism genes and glycerophospholipid metabolites (Figure 6A and 6B). We found that overexpression of OsDREB2A activated the expression of major glycerophospholipid metabolism genes (such as OsPLAIVa, OsPLAIIIa, OsPLDa, and OsMHZ11) and enhanced the production of monoacylglycerophospholipids (LPC and LPE) (Figure 6D and 6E). In addition, a LUC assay revealed that OsDREB2A can activate the OsPLAIVa, OsPLAIIIa, and OsPLDa promoters (Figure 6C). These data indicated that OsDREB2A is a new regulator of glycerophospholipid metabolism in rice seeds, which will likely prove helpful in breeding highly nutritional rice. 

In conclusion, we established a high-quality rice metabolic regulatory network covering the main tissues and organs of rice throughout its growth period. We tried two strategies for metabolome and transcriptome association analysis: (1) using known regulatory genes as bait and (2) unbiased screening based on tissue specificity. Using these approaches we identified the lignin synthesis regulatory genes MYB55 and MYB110 and the seed glycerophospholipid metabolism regulatory gene OsDREB2A, respectively, thereby demonstrating the effectiveness of both strategies. The study of the molecular mechanisms whereby the identified transcription factor genes regulate complex biological processes, such as lignin synthesis and seed glycerophospholipid accumulation in rice, will help to provide new tools for agronomic trait improvement and molecular breeding in rice by regulating metabolic pathways for growth control, disease and stress resistance, and rice quality. Moreover, use of this approach in other species will likely greatly aid in understanding the mechanisms underlying important agronomic traits within these species. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials

Two rice varieties, Zhenshan 97 and Minghui 63, were used in this study. Materials are grown and obtained as described previously (Wang et al., 2010). The samples used in this study were from throughout the life cycle of the rice plant (Figure 1, Supplemental Data 1). Rice seeds were soaked in water at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (Seed 1), and then cultured under light or dark conditions at $_ { 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for $4 8 \ \mathsf { h } ,$ , after which the plumules and radicles were collected (Plumule 1, Plumule 2, Radicle 1, and Radicle 2). Some of the seedlings were grown hydroponically, and shoots and roots were collected at the two-tiller stage (Leaf 1 and Root). Other seedlings were planted in the experimental field of Huazhong Agricultural University, Wuhan, China. Leaves and sheaths were collected when young panicles were less than 1 mm (Leaf 2 and Sheath 1). Leaves, sheaths, and panicles were collected when young panicles were $4 0 { - } 5 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ (Leaf 3, Sheath 2, and Panicle 1). Flag leaves and stems were collected 5 days before rice heading (Leaf 4 and Stem 1). Stems and panicles were collected when rice plants were in the heading stage (Stem 2 and Panicle 2). Finally, endosperm (7, 14, and 21 days after pollination) and flag leaves (14 days after pollination) of rice were collected (Seed 2, Seed 3, Seed 4, and Leaf 5). All samples were harvested between 16:00–18:00 h and placed in liquid nitrogen immediately. Three biological replicates, each of which was a mixed sample from at least three plants, were used in later metabolome analysis. 

# Preparation of metabolic samples

Rice samples were extracted as described previously (Chen et al., 2013). In brief, samples were first freeze-dried and then ground into powder using a mixer mill (MM400, Retsch) for 1.5 min at $_ { 3 0 } \mathsf { H z }$ . Powder $( 1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g } )$ was weighed and extracted overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ 7 0 \%$ aqueous methanol (methanol: ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , 70:30, v/v) and pure methanol, respectively, followed by centrifugation for $1 0 \ \mathsf { m i n }$ at 10 000 g. The supernatants were collected separately and combined together, followed by filtration (SCAA-104, $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathsf { m }$ pore size; ANPEL Shanghai, China, www.anpel. com.cn/) before LC–MS analysis. To investigate the intertissue differences in metabolites, samples were mixed into eight different tissue samples (namely plumule, radicle, leaf, sheath, stem, root, panicle, and seed) for untargeted metabolomics analysis. The samples were also equally mixed into multiple quality control samples for testing instrument stability. 

# Metabolome analysis

For metabolome analysis, samples were analyzed using an ultrahigh-performance liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectroscopy (UPLC– MS/MS)-based targeted method combined with a nontargeted metabolic profiling method (Chen et al., 2013; Monchgesang et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017; Desmet et al., 2021; Onat et al., 2021; Urrutia et al., 2021). Nontargeted metabolic profiling analysis using LC-ESI-QqTOF-MS/MS (TripleTOF $5 6 0 0 +$ , Applied Biosystems, USA) was performed in the TOF MS scan mode. Data were acquired using the following settings: ion source gas 1, 55 psi; ion source gas 2, 55 psi; curtain gas, 35 psi; interface heater temperature, $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; ion spray voltage floating, 5500 V. Scanning mass range was from m/z 100 to m/z 1000 with an accumulation time of $0 . 1 0 \ \mathsf { s } .$ , in the TOF MS mode. For IDA, the CEs were set at 30 and $5 0 \lor$ , survey scans were acquired in 0.08 s, and as many as 10 product ion scans were collected. Total cycle time was fixed to 0.79 s. Aminoheptanoic acid at m/z 146.11756, amino-dPEG 4- acid at m/z 266.15981, clomipramine at m/z 315.16225, amino-dPEG 6- acid at m/z 354.21224, amino-dPEG 8-acid at m/z 442.26467, reserpine at m/z 609.28066, amino-dPEG 12-acid at m/z 618.36953, and hexakis (2,2,3,3-tetrafluoropropoxy) phosphazene at m/z 922.0098 were used for external mass calibration. The data recorded were processed with Analyst 1.7 TF software. 

Targeted metabolic profiling analysis was done using scheduled multiple reaction monitoring using LC-ESI-QQQ-MS/MS (LCMS-8060, SHI-MADZU, Japan). The ESI source operation parameters were as follows: nebulizing gas flow, $3 \mid \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; heating gas flow, $1 0 \mid \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; interface temperature, $5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ; DL temperature, $\boldsymbol { 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ ; heat block temperature, $4 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ; drying gas flow, $1 0 \mid \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . Total loop time was fixed to 1.07 s. The data recorded were processed with LabSolutions 5.91 software. Instrument tuning and mass calibration were performed in Q1 scan and product ion modeswith $1 . 5 ~ \mu \nu ^ { - 1 }$ polyethylene glycol 200, $0 . 1 \ \mu \mid \mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 }$ polyethylene glycol 600, $1 5 . 0 ~ \mu \nu ^ { - 1 }$ polyethylene glycol 1000, $1 0 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ $1 ^ { - 1 }$ polyethylene glycol 2000, and $1 5 . 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ } | ^ { - 1 }$ cotton candy solutions. 

# Statistical data analysis

Transcriptome profiling data were publicly accessible in the database CREP (Collections of Rice Expression Profiling, http://crep.ncpgr.cn) (Wang et al., 2010). Co-regulation analysis was performed on samples from 40 different time points and tissues using MeV (version 4.9) with the $k$ -means method (Gasch and Eisen, 2002). Transcriptome and metabolome data were log2-transformed for statistical analysis to improve normality and normalized. Hierarchical clustering and PCA were performed using R (www.r-project.org/) software with default settings. Z score normalization was used to normalize the expression values of genes and metabolites, then hierarchical clustering analysis and PCA were performed. For comparison of individual treatments with their relevant controls, unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-tests were used, and $P \leq 0 . 0 5$ was considered significant. The omics data used for calcu-

lation and analysis in this study were the average of biological replicates (Supplemental Data 2 and 4). 

# Establishment of the RMRN

Pearson’s correlation algorithm was used to construct gene and metabolite regulatory networks (Bishara and Hittner, 2012). The expression level correlation between genes and metabolites was calculated by R software. Visualization of all associations between genes and metabolites was carried out using Cytoscape software (Kohl et al., 2011). 

# Phylogenetic analysis

The amino acid sequences were aligned using the MUSCLE algorithm in MEGA7 software (http://megasoftware.net/). A neighbor-joining tree was constructed in MEGA7 using the aligned full-length amino acid sequences. Bootstrap values were calculated using 1000 replicates. 

# RNA extraction and expression analyses

RNA extraction was performed using an RNA extraction kit (TRIzol reagent; Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA (3 mg) was used for synthesis of the first-strand complementary DNAs (cDNAs) in $2 0 \ \mu \up$ of reaction mixture using the EasyScript One-Step gDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis SuperMix (TransGen, Beijing, China). Real-time quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT–PCR) was performed using the SYBR Premix ExTaq kit (TaKaRa, Tokyo, Japan) on the ABI7500 Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). The rice OsUBI13 gene was used an internal reference. The primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Data 8. 

# Construction of transgenic lines

The overexpression constructs of OsMYB55 (LOC_Os01g07120), Os-MYB30 (LOC_Os04g43680), and OsMYB110 (LOC_OsOs10g33810) were generated by directionally inserting the full-length cDNAs from Nipponbare first into the entry vector DONR207 and then into the destination vector pJC034 with the maize ubiquitin promoter using the Gateway recombination reaction (Invitrogen). The construct vector was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and then transferred into ZH11. 

# Subcellular localization

The full-length coding sequence of OsMYB110 was amplified using ZH11 total cDNA as a template. The PCR product was cloned into the PJC032 (pH7WGF2.0) vector to generate an OsMYB110-YFP fusion construct driven by the constitutive cauliflower mosaic virus promoter (35S: OsMYB110-YFP). 35S: OsMYB110-YFP and 35S:OsGhd7-CFP were transiently expressed in rice protoplasts by A. tumefaciens-mediated infiltration as described previously (Sun et al., 2020). DAPI was used to stain the nucleic acids of the protoplasts. The fluorescence signal was observed with confocal laser scanning microscopy (FV1000; Olympus, Tokyo, Japan) at 2 days after transformation. 

# Transient dual luciferase reporter assay

The promoter sequences of OsPAL1, OsPAL2, OsPAL3, OspPLAIVa, OspPLAIIIa, and OsMHZ11 (2000 bp upstream of these genes) were amplified and cloned into the modified pH2GW7 vector containing the firefly luciferase (fLUC) gene and the Renilla luciferase gene (rLUC) as reporters, while the OsMYB110 and OsDREB2A cDNAs were cloned into the pEAQ-HT-DEST2 vector as effectors. The plasmids were transferred into A. tumefaciens EHA105 by electroporation and co-infiltrated into a tobacco leaf. The luciferase activities were measured using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The relative reporter gene expression levels were expressed as the ratio of firefly LUC to Renilla luciferase (LUC/ REN). Five independent transformations for each sample were performed. 

# Molecular Plant

# Pathogen inoculation

Evaluation of leaf bacterial blight disease was performed by inoculating rice at the booting stage with PXO99 and PXO341 using the leafclipping method (Xu et al., 2017). Xoo strains PXO99 and PXO341 were grown on a nutrient agar medium $( 0 . 1 \%$ yeast extract, $0 . 3 \%$ beef extract, $0 . 5 \%$ polypeptone, and $1 \%$ sucrose) at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 2 days. Resuspension and dilution were performed with sterile water to reach $A _ { 6 0 0 \mathsf { n m } } = 0 . 5$ for inoculation. About 14 days after inoculation, the length of lesions on 10 inoculated leaves was measured. The inoculated leaves were photographed using a scientific scanner (Image Scanner III, GE Healthcare, Uppsala, Sweden). 

# Dehydration and salt stress experiments

Seeds from transgenic rice lines and genetic background lines (ZH11) were surface sterilized and incubated in $^ 1 / _ { 2 }$ MS liquid medium with PEG6000 (0, $1 5 \%$ , $2 0 \% )$ ) for the dehydration stress test and NaCl (0, 100, 200 mM) for the salt stress test (Cui et al., 2011; Mallikarjuna et al., 2011). The percentage of surviving seedlings was estimated after 7 days. Shoot and root growth per plant were estimated in surviving seedlings of ZH11 and transgenic lines. Data for 10 plants of ZH11 and transgenic lines for each experiment were collected and used for subsequent statistical calculations.
