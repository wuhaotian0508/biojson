# A balance between glycitein and glyceollins governed by isoflavone 6-hydroxylase confers soybean resistance to Phytophthora sojae

Isoflavonoids, predominantly found in legumes, are specialized metabolites with antioxidant properties that benefit both plant resilience and human health. Using metabolic genome-wide association studies (mGWAS), we identified the cytochrome P450 gene (Glyma.11g108300), GmIF6H1, as a key determinant of glycinein biosynthesis in soybean [Glycine max (L.) Merr.]. Biochemical assays together with in planta stable-isotope tracing demonstrated that GmIF6H1 catalyzes the 6-hydroxylation of daidzein, establishing a previously unrecognized and predominant biosynthetic route for glycitein. A single amino acid substitution in GmIF6H1 accounts for the domestication-associated reduction of glycitein-type isoflavonoids. Upon Phytophthora sojae infection, (malonyl) glycitins undergo sustained deglycosylation to release glycitein aglycone, underscoring its defensive role. Strikingly, both loss- and gain-of-function alleles increase susceptibility to $P_{\cdot}$ sojae, indicating that precise tuning of GmIF6H1 expression is essential for effective resistance. Metabolite profiling further reveals complementary daidzein-centered defense strategies: Glycitein-type isoflavonoids (via daidzein 6-hydroxylation) function as phytoanticipins, whereas glyceollins (via daidzein 2'-hydroxylation) act as inducible phytoalexins. Together, these findings clarify the biosynthetic origin of the glycitein and underscore the synergistic action of glycitein and glyceollins in pathogen resistance, offering opportunities for engineering disease-resilient soybean cultivars.

Isoflavonoids, predominantly distributed in leguminous plants, belong to an important group of plant specialized metabolites. Clinical studies have demonstrated a positive effect of isoflavonoids in human health and nutrition, such as a decreased risk of heart disease, hormonally dependent cancers, and cardiovascular disease (1-5). Soybean is an exceptionally rich source of dietary isoflavones, where the average isoflavone content is 1 to 2 mg/g dry weight in seeds (6). In soybean, three main types of isoflavonoids, daidzein-, genistein-, and glycitein-derived compounds, occur primarily as glycosides and malonylglycosides. Notably, certain isoflavonoids, such as glyceollins synthesized via daidzein $2^{\prime}$ -hydroxylation, function as phytoalexins, playing a critical role in defending soybean and other legumes against pathogen attacks (7-10). 

Due to their health-related benefits, there is great interest in soyfoods and purified soybean isoflavonoids as nutraceuticals. Consequently, the biosynthetic pathway of isoflavonoid compounds has been well investigated. Most of the relevant structural genes involved in this pathway have been isolated and well characterized, at least in vitro. Briefly, isoflavonoid and flavonoid share the upstream biosynthetic pathway from phenylalanine to naringenin chalcone (Fig. 1), which sequentially catalyzed by PAL (phenylalanine ammonia-lyase), C4H (cinnamate-4-hydroxylate), 4CL (4-coumarate-CoA-ligase), CHS (chalcone synthase). As shown in Fig. 1, GmF6Hs (Flavonoid 6-hydroxylase (F6H1/ CYP71D9, F6H2/CYP71D101, and F6H3/CYP71D102 are encoded by Glyma. $18\mathrm{g}080400$ Glyma. $18\mathrm{g}080200$ , and Glyma. $08\mathrm{g}326900$ , respectively) catalyzed the 6-hydroxylation of flavanones and no activity toward isoflavones in vitro. It thus had been proposed that the 6-hydroxylation of the A-ring occurs before the 2,3-aryl migration of the flavonoid B-ring during isoflavanone formation in soybean (11, 12). Three main flavanones (naringenin, liquiritigenin, and 6-hydroxyliquiritigenin (also known as $6,7,4^{\prime}$ -trihydroxyflavanone) were converted to isoflavones (genistein, daidzein, and glycitein) by combination of IFS (2-hydroxyisoflavanone synthase, a P450 enzyme belonging to the CYP93C subfamily) and 2HID (2-hydroxyisoflavanone dehydratase). # Significance

In this study, we identified GmIF6H1 (Glyma.11g108300) as a key gene for glycinein biosynthesis in soybean, catalyzing daidzein 6-hydroxylation rather than the previously assumed liquoritigenin route. Infection by Phytophthora sojae induced glycinein release, yet both knockout and overexpression of GmIF6H1 increased susceptibility, underscoring the need for precise regulation. We further revealed a complementary daidzein-based defense strategy in soybean: glycinein-type isoflavonoids (via daidzein 6-hydroxylation) as phytoanticipins and glyceollins (via daidzein $2^{\prime}$ -hydroxylation) as phytoalexins. These findings not only clarify the biosynthetic origin of the glycinein but also highlight its pivotal contribution to soybean pathogen resistance.

Linkage and association mapping studies of seed isoflavonoid also have provided important insights into the genetic control of isoflavonoid biosynthesis. For example, 44 QTLs for total isoflavone content were detected using 366 soybean landraces under four environments (15, 16). To date, SoyBase (www.soybase.org) lists approximately 300 isoflavone QTLs generated from biparental populations (61 for daidzein, 68 for genistein, 71 for glycitein, and 85 for total isoflavone. However, only a limited number of candidate genes underlying these loci have been functionally validated and mechanistically characterized. Notably, two R2R3-type MYB transcription factors, GmMYB29 (Glyma.20g209700) and GmMYB77 (Glyma.04g036700), identified through metabolic genome-wide association studies (mGWAS), were shown to oppositely regulate the transcriptional expression of CHS and IFS genes (17, 18). More recently, a major QTL on chromosome 11 (qMGly_11) for malonylglycitin content was identified and characterized using two biparental populations. One parental line (Kumaji-1) from these populations is devoid of (malonyl)glycitin (19). Within this region, a cytochrome P450 gene (Glyma.11g108300) has been demonstrated as a candidate gene responsible for (malonyl)glycitin variation (20-22). Glyma.11g108300 was even tentatively designated as F6H4, in reference to F6H1-3, which are believed to be involved in glycitein biosynthesis in soybean for a long time (Fig. 1) (22). However, the precise biochemical function of Glyma.11g108300, as well as the physiological role of glycitein-type isoflavonoid in plants, has remained unclear. 

In this study, we investigated natural variation in isoflavonoid composition and content in the seeds of a soybean natural population comprising over 700 accessions including wild soybeans, landraces, and cultivars. We found that most isoflavonoid compounds decreased progressively during domestication from wild soybeans to landraces and modern cultivars. mGWAS pinpointed the strongest association signals for glycitein traits to locus on chromosome 11, which could be resolved to a single causal gene, GmIF6H1 (Glyma.11g108300). Biochemical and genetic data reveal that GmIF6H1 catalyzes the conversion of daidzein to 6-hydroxydaidzein, a key step in the biosynthesis of glycitein in soybean. This conclusion was further supported by stable isotope labeling experiments using daidzein- $\mathrm{d}_6$ . These findings reveal a predominant biosynthetic route from daidzein to glycitein, thereby expanding the long-standing model centered on flavanone 6-hydroxylation (Fig. 1). Moreover, we uncovered a complementary daidzein-centered defense strategy in soybean: Glycitein-type isoflavonoids, formed via daidzein 6-hydroxylation, act as phytoanticipins, whereas glyceollins, produced via daidzein 2'-hydroxylation, function as phytoalexins. 

# Results

Isoflavonoid Content in Soybean Natural Population. Seed samples of soybean population previously used for fatty acids, amino acid, and tocopherol profiling (23, 24) were also analyzed for isoflavonoids profiling by using LC-qToF-MS (liquid chromatography-quadrupole/time-of-flight mass spectrometry; for detailed information, see Datasets S1 and S2). A total of 11 

isoflavonoids, including genistein, daidzein and glycinein, and their glucosidic forms, were detected and quantified in this study (Fig. 1). Analysis of the 727 diverse soybean accessions grown in Beijing, China in 2013 revealed extensive variation in seed isoflavonoid content (Fig. 2). Interestingly, the Soja group exhibited the highest total isoflavonoids content $(5.65 \pm 1.16 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 52$ ), followed by landraces $(4.79 \pm 1.42 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 386$ ) and cultivars $(4.34 \pm 1.41 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 329$ ). Notably, a progressive decrease in the content of all detected individual isoflavonoids, as well as the total isoflavonoids, was observed during soybean domestication. However, no significant difference was found between landraces and improved cultivars (Fig. 2 and Dataset S1). 

A similar trend was observed in soybean samples grown across three different locations in 2014 (Heilongjiang Province, Henan Province, and Beijing; see Dataset S2 for details), the Soja population again showed the highest total isoflavonoid content (all grown in Beijing; $5.75 \pm 1.33 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 53$ ), followed by landrace ( $4.64 \pm 1.39 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 430$ ) and cultivars ( $4.66 \pm 1.19 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 351$ ). Additionally, isoflavonoid analysis indicated that the growth latitude, whether high (Heilongjiang Province) or low (Henan Province), had no significant impact on the total isoflavonoid content ( $4.70 \pm 1.24 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 150$ vs. $4.65 \pm 1.31 \mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{g}$ D.W., $n = 624$ ; SI Appendix, Fig. S1 and Dataset S2).

Genetic Dissection of Isoflavonoid Traits in Soybean Seeds. A GWAS was performed on the isoflavonoid traits, encompassing 11 individual isoflavonoids and total isoflavonoid content, which identified 11 significant SNP loci distributed across 10 soybean chromosomes (Chrs.) (the cutoff value is $-\log_{10}P > 8.0$ ; SI Appendix, Fig. S2 and Dataset S3). Candidate genes were screened within a 200-kb region (100 kb upstream and downstream of the peak SNP), based on the linkage disequilibrium threshold in the soybean genome. Among 11 loci, we first paid attention to the strongest GWAS signal for glycinein/(malonyl)glycinin on Chr. 11, which had been previously reported (19, 20). The 200-kb region around the lead SNP (8,259,672 bp on Chr. 11) contained a total of 28 genes, including a P450 enzyme which belongs to CYP76 subfamily (GmCYP76F17 encoded by Glyma.11G108300) and several transcription factors (Fig. 3A and Dataset S3). This locus had been identified in prior studies, with GmCYP76F17 recently emerging as a candidate gene for glycinein-type isoflavonoid production in soybean (19-22). LC-MS analysis revealed that glycinein, glycitin, and malonylglycin were significantly increased in the GmCYP76F17-OE hairy roots (KF-1 cultivar), in which the expression level of GmCYP76F17 was significantly upregulated (Fig. 3B). Meanwhile, other isoflavonoids such as daidzein and genistein were almost unaffected when compared to those in control hairy roots (Fig. 3B). We also tested other two candidate transcription factor genes, GmbZIP1 and GmMYB1. GmMYB1 expression was nearly undetectable in all tissues, and altering GmbZIP1 expression did not impact isoflavonoid content in the hairy root system (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). GmbZIP1 and GmMYB1 were thus excluded from further investigation. 

Characterization of Isoflavone 6-Hydroxylase from Leguminous Plants. To clarify the biochemical function of GmCYP76F17, we employed a yeast substrate-feeding assay. A yeast codon-optimized coding sequence of GmCYP76F17 from W82 (Hap1 or Hap2) was expressed in Saccharomyces cerevisiae WAT11 using the pESC-His vector. Cultures were supplied with a panel of (iso)flavonoids commonly found in soybean: daidzein, genistein, glycitein, liquiritigenin, and isoliquiritigenin chalcone. Of these, only daidzein yielded a product (Fig. 3C), identified as 6-hydroxydaidzein, which undergoes 6-O-methylation to form glycitein (Fig. 1). No additional products were detected with the other substrates (Fig. 3C). Microsomal assays from GmCYP76F17-expressing yeast showed that $\mathrm{Mn}^{2+}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ enhanced catalytic activity, whereas $\mathrm{Fe}^{2+}$ , $\mathrm{Fe}^{3+}$ , and $\mathrm{Cu}^{2+}$ strongly inhibited it. The enzyme exhibited optimal activity at pH 7.0 and $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). The $K_{m}$ value of GmCYP76F17 toward daidzein was then determined as $7.49 \pm 0.73 \mu\mathrm{M}$ ( $n = 3$ ). Subcellular localization analysis showed that GmCYP76F17-GFP colocalized with an endoplasmic reticulum (ER) marker, consistent with ER-associated plant P450s (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). 

Phylogenetic analysis of GmCYP76F17 and related P450 proteins (with $>40\%$ identity) from seven plant species showed that GmCYP76F17 closely clustered with the P450s from leguminous plants, such as Medicago truncatula and Lotus japonicus (Fig. 3D). To further ascertain the conservation of the functionality of GmCYP76F17, we employed the above-mentioned yeast assay to test the biochemical functions of the closest homologs including Lj0g3v0208479, Lj0g3v0208459, and Lj0g3v0208499 from $L.$ japonicus, and Medtr4g078110 from $M.$ truncatula. A broader spectrum of 6-hydroxyisoflavonoids, which are likely synthesized from formononetin and biochanin-A has been previously reported (25). However, no such 6-hydroxyisoflavonoids have been reported in $L.$ japonicus. Consistent with prior chemical profiling, Medtr4g078110 catalyzed the conversion of formononetin to 

6-hydroxyformononetin and of biochanin A to a new compound (Fig. 3 $C$ and $E$ ). The mass spectrum of the new compound suggested that one hydroxyl group addition occurred during the feeding, putatively annotated as 6-hydroxybiochanin A here, although the exact hydroxyl position needs further determination (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). No catalytic activities were detected for all three homologs from L. japonicus (SI Appendix, Fig. S7). Notably, GmCYP76F17, like Medtr4g078110, exhibited approximately fivefold higher catalytic activity toward formononetin and biochanin-A than toward daidzein (Fig. 3E). 

Altogether, GmCYP76F17 was redesigned as GmIF6H1 (isoflavone 6-hydroxylase 1) hereafter. 

GmIF6H1 T248A Substitution Underlies the Domestication-Associated Reduction of Glycinein-Type Isoflavonoids. The GWAS analysis showed that the metabolites associated with GmIF6H1 are all glycinein and its derivatives (Fig. 3A). Accordingly, we analyzed the content of these compounds across different GmIF6H1 haplotypes in the natural soybean population. GmIF6H1 contains one intron, encoding a 507 amino acid-peptide. The sequence variation of GmIF6H1 was further investigated in the representative population of 528 soybean accessions (including 233 cultivars, 267 landraces, 18 soja). There are two representative haplotypes in the GmIF6H1 region as determined by one SNP (+742 position in coding region, G and A), which led to the substitution of Ala $^{248}$ (haplotype 1, Hap 1) with Thr $^{248}$ (haplotype 2, Hap 2; Fig. 4A). Accessions carrying Hap1 $(n = 240)$ contained significantly lower levels of glycinein and its derivatives compared to those carrying Hap2 $(n = 278$ ; Fig. 4B). All these results suggested that Thr $^{248}$ probably plays an important role in GmIF6H1 activity, which has also probably been selected during soybean domestication and improvement based on the frequency distribution of Hap2 in wild soybeans $(83.33\%)$ , landraces $(52.81\%)$ , and improved cultivars $(52.36\%)$ ; Fig. 4A). To test this hypothesis, we compared the enzymatic activities of Hap1 and Hap2 protein variants via a yeast feeding assay. Western blot analysis ensured the similar GmIF6H1 protein level in the engineered yeast strains (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). The results were consistent with the results of the natural population, and the 6-hydroxydaidzein content produced by yeast harboring Hap2 gene was significantly higher than that of Hap1 (Fig. 4C). Further detailed biochemical characterization showed that the Hap2 protein $(K_{cat} / K_{m} = 1.48 \pm 0.17 \, \text{s}^{-1} \, \text{M}^{-1}, n = 3)$ exhibited fivefold higher catalytic efficiency than Hap1 $(K_{cat} / K_{m} = 0.28 \pm 0.042 \, \text{s}^{-1} \, \text{M}^{-1}, n = 3)$ toward daidzein (Fig. 4D). Although residue 248 (Thr/Ala) of GmIF6H1 does not lie within the five canonical conserved motifs of plant P450s (SI Appendix, Fig. S9), $\mathbf{T}^{248}$ is positioned near the $4^{\prime}$ -hydroxyl of daidzein in the docking model (Fig. 4E). To test its functional importance, we mutagenized this site and found that only variants bearing Thr, Ala, Ser, Val, or Cys at position 248 retained activity toward daidzein, with the $\mathbf{T}^{248}$ variant showing the highest activity (Fig. 4F).

Combined Roles of Glycinein and Glycoollins in Soybean Defense against Phytophthora sojae. Isoflavonoids were well documented as key players in plant-microbe interactions, serving as phytoalexins against bacteria, nematodes and fungal pathogens, or as signals to attract rhizobia to the soybean rhizosphere (15, 27, 28). To investigate whether deficiency in glycitein-type isoflavonoids affect nodulation formation, we conducted rhizobium inoculation experiments in a controlled laboratory environment using both W82 and GmIF6H1 transgenic plants. Seven-day-old soybean seedlings were inoculated with the slow-growing rhizobium strain USDA110. After 14 days postinoculation (dpi), we quantified the number and size of nodules on the roots of each soybean seedling. Observation of the root systems revealed no significant differences in nodule number or size among the W82, gmif6h1 mutants, and GmIF6H1OE-1 plant (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). 

We next tested whether glycinein and/or its derivatives are involved in the resistance to $P_{\cdot}$ sojae, the oomycete that causes soybean root rot. Using 3-d-old etiolated seedlings, we observed that lesion severity was greater in the gmif6h1 mutants than in W82 after 2 dpi (Fig. 6A). Consistently, the relative biomass of $P_{\cdot}$ sojae was significantly higher in the mutants than in W82 (Fig. 6B). LC-MS analysis further revealed that, upon $P_{\cdot}$ sojae infection, only the contents of glycinein and its derivatives were altered in the gmif6h1 mutant compared with W82 (Fig. 6D). These results suggested that the absence of glycinein and its derivatives contributes to the enhanced susceptibility of the gmif6h1 mutant to $P_{\cdot}$ sojae compared to W82. Interestingly, at 3 dpi, we detected a slight but notable increase in glycinein accumulation in gmif6h1 seedlings $(0.015\mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{g}^{-1} \mathrm{D.W.}, n = 3$ Fig. 6D). To directly test the defensive role of glycinein, we conducted chemical complementation by applying $200\mu \mathrm{M}$ glycinein or DMSO (solvent control) to hypocotyl wound sites of W82 and gmif6h1 seedlings, incubating for $30\mathrm{min}$ prior to inoculation. Exogenous application of glycinein restored resistance in the gmif6h1 mutants to W82 levels, demonstrating that glycinein is required for effective defense against $P_{\cdot}$ sojae (Fig. 6 E and F). 

Unexpectedly, soybean plants overexpressing $GmIF6H1$ were also more susceptible to $P_{\cdot}$ sojae than W82. Transcriptome profiling revealed no significant differences in the expression of isoflavonepathway genes between GmIF6H1OE-1 and W82 (Fig. 6C). However, LC-MS analysis showed that although glycitein levels were markedly higher in the overexpression line $(0.57\mu \mathrm{mol}g^{-1}$ D.W.at 2 dpi, $n = 3$ ) than in W82 $(0.047\mu \mathrm{mol}g^{-1}$ D.W.at 2 dpi, $n = 3)$ ,glyceollin I,a major phytoalexin inhibitory to $P_{\cdot}$ sojae, was markedly reduced (5.90 vs. $9.36~{\mu\mathrm{mol}}~\mathrm{g}^{-1}$ D.W.at 2 dpi), approximately 1.6-fold lower in GmIF6H1OE-1 (Fig. 6D). These findings indicate that GmIF6H1 overexpression reroutes metabolic flux away from glyceollin biosynthesis during $P_{\cdot}$ sojae infection, leading to reduced glyceollin accumulation and increased pathogen susceptibility.Notably,GmIF6H1 expression itself remained unchanged upon $P_{\cdot}$ sojae infection, showing neither induction nor repression (Fig. 6C). 

It is noteworthy that the daidzein pool was rapidly and strongly induced, by several tens of fold, upon $P.$ sojae challenge, and this pattern was unaffected by either loss or overexpression of GmIF6H1. Moreover, formononetin-type isoflavonoids were likewise induced by $P.$ sojae infection, but this induction was markedly attenuated in GmIF6H1OE-1. Conversely, afromosin-type isoflavonoids, which converted by GmIF6H1 from formononetin (Fig. 1), accumulated to high levels in GmIF6H1OE-1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). 

# Discussion

The daily intake of soybean isoflavones is beneficial to human health, which has driven long-term interest in the biosynthesis and regulation of these compounds (1, 29). Soybean isoflavones can be categorized into three major types: daidzein-type, genistein-type, and glycinein-type, along with their glycosylated and subsequently malonylated derivatives. While the upstream genes in the isoflavone biosynthetic pathway have been extensively studied, the downstream genes and the physiological functions of these compounds incompletely characterized. Here, using a metabolome-wide genome-wide association study (mGWAS), we identify a cytochrome P450 gene, GmIF6H1 (CYP76F17), as a key determinant of glycitein production. This finding is consistent with recent reports (21, 22). 

Beyond providing in planta evidence that $GmIF6H1$ predominately governs glycinein biosynthesis in soybean, we demonstrate, biochemically and in plants, that glycinein production mainly proceeds from daidzein rather than from liquoritigenin. Earlier studies posited that flavonoid 6-hydroxylases (F6H1/CYP71D9 and F6H2/CYP71D101) may be responsible for the production of glycinein-type isoflavone in soybean plants (30, 31), and GmIF6H1 had even been tentatively referred to as F6H4 (22). Our detailed assays show that GmIF6H1 exhibits strict substrate preference for isoflavones with an unmodified A-ring C6 position, catalyzing A-ring C6 hydroxylation of daidzein, formononetin, and biochanin-A, while displaying no activity toward glycinein, isoliquiritigenin (chalcone), or liquoritigenin (flavanone). Although its specific activity toward daidzein is $\sim 20$ -fold lower than toward formononetin and biochanin-A, multiple lines of in planta evidence establish daidzein as the bona fide substrate in soybean: i) 6-hydroxydaidzein, the direct product of GmIF6H1 acting on daidzein, is undetectable in $g m i f 6 h 1$ mutants but strongly accumulates in GmIF6H1OE-1 plants, a pattern corroborated by daidzein- $\mathrm{d}_6$ feeding; and ii) under normal growth conditions, formononetin and biochanin-A are essentially absent from hypocotyls, the tissue with the highest glycinein-type isoflavonoid levels, so substrate availability dictates that GmIF6H1 predominantly hydroxylates daidzein in planta. Moreover, we cannot rule out the possibility that GmIF6H1 forms a metabolon (multienzyme complex) with other proteins in planta, such as IFS and 2HID illustrated in Fig. 1. This study provides identification of an A-ring C6 hydroxylase functionally embedded within the plant isoflavone biosynthetic network. Notably, trace amounts of glycinein were still detected in $g m i f 6 h 1$ seedlings 3 d after P. sojae infection (Fig. 6D), suggesting that, beyond the predominant $GmIF6H1$ -mediated route from daidzein, alternative pathways may contribute to glycinein biosynthesis under specific physiological conditions, for instance, the previously proposed F6H-mediated pathway from liquoritigenin (11, 12).

Soybean isoflavones play critical roles in nodulation and resistance to pathogenic infection (28, 36-39). The identification of a glycinein-type isoflavonoid-deficient mutant (gmif6h1) provided an opportunity to investigate the specific functions of this class of isoflavonoids in these processes. Our results demonstrate that the absence of glycinein-type isoflavonoids does not affect soybean nodulation (SI Appendix, Fig. S12), indicating that the daidzein-and genistein-type isoflavonoids might have a more significant impact on this process. 

Interestingly, we found that the lack of glycinein-type isoflavonoids compromises soybean resistance to $P.$ sojae. Infection by $P.$ sojae significantly induces the expression of isoflavonoid biosynthesis genes, including those involved in glyceollin synthesis and regulation, with transcript levels increasing several to tens of folds (Fig. 6). Correspondingly, glycollin accumulation in the soybean plants was elevated by tens of folds, which are consistent with previous reports (10). However, apart from the loss of glycinein-type isoflavonoids, the levels of glyceollins and related metabolites did not differ significantly between W82 and the gmif6h1 mutants during infection (Fig. 6D). These observations indicate that the heightened susceptibility of gmif6h1 primarily results from the deficiency of glycinein-type isoflavonoids, a conclusion further supported by the glycinein rescue experiment. Unexpectedly, plants overexpressing GmIF6H1 also exhibited increased susceptibility to $P.$ sojae. Detailed isoflavonoid profiling showed that this phenotype was mainly attributable to reduced glycellin I. Notably, daidzein, the shared precursor of glycinein and glycellin I, remained unchanged in the overexpression lines, suggesting that excess GmIF6H1 protein may perturb the biochemical function of GmI2'H, either individually or within a protein complex; the precise mechanism remains to be elucidated. Unlike other isoflavonoid-related genes, GmIF6H1 itself is not induced by $P.$ sojae. During infection, glycitin and malonylglycitin decline rapidly, whereas glycitein (the aglycone) increases markedly (40). By contrast, glycellin accumulation depended on transcriptional induction and de novo biosynthesis. We therefore propose that daidzein is partitioned between glycitein-type isoflavonoids (via GmIF6H1) and glycollins (via GmI2'H) to establish a synergistic defense against $P.$ sojae, with glycitein acting as a phytoanticipin and glycollins as phytoalexins (Fig. 6G). Upon $P.$ sojae infection, the release of isoflavone aglycones, reflecting a phytoanticipin function, precedes the transcriptionally induced biosynthesis of glycollins (phytoalexin function). Whether glycitein and glycollins inhibit $P.$ sojae through shared or distinct mechanisms warrants further investigation. 

Soybeans are a primary dietary source of isoflavones for humans. Upon ingestion, these isoflavones are metabolized by gut microbiota into equol, a compound with mild estrogenic activity that mediates their biological effects (41-44). Recent clinical studies and the equol-equivalent concept suggest that daidzein and 

genistein provide greater health benefits to humans compared to glycitein (15, 42, 45). In this context, the newly developed glycitein-deficient soybean material, which demonstrates a 1.5-fold increase in daidzein-type isoflavonoids in germinating seeds, merits further investigation for its potential to enhance health benefits. Moreover, the candidate genomic regions identified by our mGWAS provide a solid foundation for precision breeding of soybean lines with health-promoting isoflavonoid profiles.

Hairy root system: Kefeng-1, rather than W82, soybean seedlings were grown in vermiculite under controlled greenhouse conditions (22 °C; 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod) for 6 d. Seedlings were then inoculated at the hypocotyl with Agrobacterium rhizogenes (strain K599) harboring the indicated constructs (46). Hairy roots emerged from the wound sites and were allowed to develop for an additional 2 wk. The primary root was subsequently excised, and plants were transferred to hydroponic culture for a further 2 wk to enrich transgenic hairy roots for downstream analyses. 

The plant samples for tissue-specificity analysis were collected by following a previously published protocol (47). The treated root tissues were collected for RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis. 

RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR Analysis. Total RNA extraction RT were performed with an RNAprep Pure Plant kit (Tiangen) and qPCR-RT Master Mix (Toyobo), respectively, according to the manufacturers' instructions. Quantitative PCR was performed using a CFX96 Real-Time PCR Detection System (BioRad) and Ultra SYBR Mixture (CWBio) (48). The comparative CT method (49) was used for relative gene expression analysis by normalizing to the reference gene GmUbiquitin (Glyma.02g236900). Primers are listed in Dataset S5. 

Subcellular Localization of CYP76F17. In the tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) system, CYP76F17-GFP (pCambia1300 vector) and ER-localized mCherry marker protein constructs [plasmid# CD3-959; (50)] were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The agrobacterium mix (1:1 ratio) was infiltrated into tobacco leaves with a needleless syringe. Fluorescence from leaves was examined using Axio Imager Z2 fluorescence microscopy (Zeiss) 3 d after transformation. For detailed primer information, see Dataset S5. 

Isoflavonoid Quantification Using LC-MS. Soybean seeds were first ground to a fine powder, and $1\mathrm{mL}$ of a methanol $/ \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}$ mixture (4:1, v/v) was then added for approximately $40\mathrm{mg}$ dry weight seed powder in a $2\mathrm{mL}$ Eppendorf tube. After that, ultrasonic extraction was conducted for $20\mathrm{min}$ . After centrifugation at $13,500\mathrm{g}$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ , $1\mathrm{mL}$ of clear liquid was transferred into a $2\mathrm{-mL}$ syringe and filtered through a polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) syringe filter $(0.22\mu \mathrm{m})$ before LC-MS analysis (UPLC-qToF-MS/MS, UPLC: Agilent 1290; MS: Agilent 6550 qToF). Separation was achieved on an Agilent ZORBAX Extend C18 column $(100\mathrm{mm} \times 2.1\mathrm{mm}, 1.8\mu \mathrm{m})$ , and the column temperature was maintained at $35^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . Solvent 

A was $0.1\%$ (v/v) formic acid aqueous solution, and solvent B was acetonitrile containing $0.1\%$ formic acid (by volume). The chromatograph program was as follows at a flow of $0.40 \mathrm{~mL} / \mathrm{min}$ : 0 to $0.1 \mathrm{~min}$ , $1\%$ solvent B (v/v); 0.1 to $15.5 \mathrm{~min}$ , a linear gradient to $99.5\%$ of B, and maintained for $1.5 \mathrm{~min}$ (15.5 to $17.0 \mathrm{~min}$ ). The MS detection parameters (in positive mode) were as follows: capillary voltage, $4,000 \mathrm{~V}$ ; gas temperature, $225^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ; drying gas flow, $13 \mathrm{~L} / \mathrm{min}$ ; sheath gas temperature, $350^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ; and sheath gas flow, $12 \mathrm{~L} / \mathrm{min}$ . 

Metabolites were identified by comparing their retention times and mass fragments against those of standard compounds. When corresponding standards were not available, compounds were putatively identified by comparing their retention times and mass spectra (molecular weight and fragmentation pattern; mass accuracy was set to 20 ppm for selected ion monitoring mode) to those described in the literature. 

Labeling Study. Hypocotyls from the 2-d-old germinating soybean seed were incubated in $20~\mu \mathrm{M}$ of daidzein- $\mathsf{d}_6$ for 1 d. Wash the surface of hypocotyls and collect the samples. Soybean materials were first ground to a fine powder, and 0.8 mL of a methanol/ $\mathsf{H}_2\mathsf{O}$ mixture (4:1, v/v) was then added for approximately 6 mg dry weight powder in a 2 mL Eppendorf tube. After then, ultrasonic extraction was conducted for 20 min. After centrifugation at 13,500 g for 10 min, 1 mL of clear liquid was transferred into a 2-mL syringe and filtered through a PTFE syringe filter (0.22 μm) before LC-MS analysis (Agilent 1290 Infinity LC tandem Triple Quad 6495 MS). Separation was achieved on an Agilent ZORBAX Extend C18 column (100 mm × 2.1 mm, 1.8 μm), and the column temperature was maintained at 35°C. Solvent A was 0.1% (v/v) formic acid aqueous solution, and solvent B was acetonitrile containing 0.1% formic acid (by volume). The chromatograph program was as follows at a flow of 0.40 mL/min: 0 to 0.5 min, 15% B (v/v); 0.5 to 3.5 min, 76% B; 3.5 to 5.5 min, 73% B; 5.5 to 6 min, 58% B; 6 to 9 min, 99% B; 9 to 12 min, 99% B; 12 to 12.1 min, 15% B; 12.1 to 15 min, 15% B. In each run, 2 μL of the sample was analyzed. The multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) parameters for the quantitative detection of isoflavonoids are provided in Dataset S4. 

Isoflavonoid GWAS Analysis. SNP data from our previous study (23) were used for the genetic diversity analysis of isoflavonoid related genes in soybean. Notably, the resequencing data of 53 soja accessions (around $1 \times$ coverage) are also included in the GWAS analysis. The SNPs with missing data $>10\%$ or MAF $< 5\%$ were filtered. The soybean accessions were divided into three populations: soja, landrace, and cultivar. The overall isoflavonoid content of the soybean accessions (2 y data) were also calculated using the best linear unbiased prediction for GWAS analysis. 

Heterologous Expression in Yeast and Identification of CYP76Fs Products. All CYP76F genes tested in this study were integrated into the high-copy plasmid pESC-His (Agilent Technologies, USA) for expression of Flag-tagged CYP76F in the WAT11 yeast strain (48). Related primers used in the constructs are listed in Dataset S5. The resulting positive yeast clones were cultured in SD dropout medium $(-Ura, -His)$ , and then harvested and induced with $6\mathrm{mL}$ of SG dropout medium $(-Ura, -His)$ at $30^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 1 d in a shaking incubator (200 rpm). The medium was diluted with fresh SG medium to $\mathrm{OD}_{600} = 0.4$ , $6\mathrm{mL}$ of the diluted medium was incubated with a final concentration of $1\mu \mathrm{M}$ different flavone or isoflavone for an additional $18\mathrm{h}$ . Ultrasonic extraction of these recombinant cultures with an equal volume of ethyl acetate was evaporated to dryness by nitrogen flux. The dried chemicals were then resolved into methanol/ $\mathsf{H}_2\mathsf{O}$ mixture (4:1, v/v). One microliter of analytical sample was loaded onto the LC-MS for analysis using the abovementioned program. 

Yeast cells harboring CYP76F17 plasmid were sedimented and lysed to prepare the yeast microsome following the previous published protocol (51). The enzymatic assay was performed in a $100\mu \mathrm{L}$ system containing $100\mathrm{mM}$ Tris-HCl $(\mathsf{pH}7.0)$ , $10\mathrm{mM}$ $\mathrm{MnCl}_2$ , $0.2\mathrm{mg}$ of microsomal protein and $50~\mu \mathrm{M}$ substrate and $1\times$ NADPH regenerating system. $10\times$ NADPH regenerating system was equilibrated at room temperature for $20\mathrm{min}$ including $5\mathrm{mM}$ nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate $(\mathrm{NADP^{+}})$ , $50~\mathrm{mM}$ glucose-6-phosphate, 10 unit of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, $20~\mathrm{mM}$ dithiothreitol. The reactions were carried out at $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $4\mathrm{h}$ and stopped by adding $80\%$ methanol $(\mathrm{v / v})$ . After centrifugation at $12,000~\mathrm{g}$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ at room temperature and filtration by a PTFE syringe filter $(0.22~\mu \mathrm{m})$ the products were analyzed by LC-MS as mentioned above. 

Western Blot Assay. $0.2\mathrm{mg}$ of microsomal proteins was separated by $10\%$ SDS-PAGE, transferred to a polyvinylidene difluoride membrane, and blotted with primary antibody directed against the FLAG-tag (1:5,000; purchased from CWBio), and goat anti-mouse igG $(\mathsf{H} + \mathsf{L})$ -HRP was used as the secondary antibody (1:10,000). 

Kinetic Analysis. The Km values were determined using serial concentrations from 2.5 to $200\mu \mathrm{M}$ in $100~\mu \mathrm{L}$ enzyme assays. These also contained $\sim 0.2$ mg of microsomal protein, Tris-HCl (pH 7.0), $10\mathrm{mM}$ $\mathrm{MnCl}_2$ , and NADPH regenerating system mentioned above. The reactions were initiated by substrate addition and incubated with shaking at $28^{\circ}C$ for $30\mathrm{min}$ . Extraction was then stopped by adding $200\mu \mathrm{L}80\%$ methanol (v/v). After centrifugation at $12,000\times g$ for $10\mathrm{min}$ at room temperature and filtration by a PTFE syringe filter $(0.22\mu \mathrm{m})$ , the products were analyzed by LC-QQQ-MS/MS. LC-QQQ-MS/MS was performed with an Agilent 1290 Infinity LC tandem Triple Quad 6495 MS (Agilent). We used an Agilent ZORBAX Extend C18 column $(100\mathrm{mm}\times 2.1\mathrm{mm},1.8\mu \mathrm{m})$ and the column temperature was maintained at $35^{\circ}C$ . Solvent A was $0.1\%$ (v/v) formic acid aqueous solution, and solvent B was acetonitrile containing $0.1\%$ formic acid (by volume). A flow rate of $0.4\mathrm{mL}\mathrm{min}^{-1}$ was used, and the gradient program was as follows: 0 to 0.1 min, $20\%$ B (v/v); 0.1 to 3 min, $30\%$ B; 3 to 3.5 min, $99.5\%$ B; 3.5 to 5 min, $99.5\%$ B; 5 to 5.1 min, $20\%$ B; 5.1 to 8 min, $20\%$ B. In each run, $2\mu \mathrm{L}$ of the sample was analyzed. The MRM parameters for the quantitative detection of 6-hydroxydaidzein and daidzein are provided in Dataset S4. 

Nodulation Phenotype Observation. Soybean nodulation phenotype analysis protocol was modified from previous report (52). All soybean seeds (W82 and GmIF6H1 transgenic plants) were planted in plastic pots containing vermiculite and grown for 1 wk (16 h of light/8 h of dark, $22^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $50 \%$ relative humidity). They were then inoculated with a suspension of USDA110 ( $\mathrm{OD}_{600} = 0.06$ ). The nodulation phenotype was quantitatively analyzed 21 d after inoculation. 

P. sojae Inoculation Experiments. All soybean materials were planted in plastic pots filled with vermiculite and grown for 3 d under controlled conditions (all day in the dark, $22^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , $50\%$ relative humidity). The hypocotyls of 3-d-old etiolated soybean seedlings were inoculated with 1.5-cm-diameter agar blocks containing 5-d-old pathogen mycelia and incubated in the dark. The inoculation sites were wrapped with water-soaked absorbent paper. Pathogen virulence was assessed 5 d after inoculation. The relative biomass of P. sojae was quantified using RT-qPCR with specific primers targeting the P. sojae Actin gene (VMD GeneID: 108986) and the soybean Actin11 gene (53). The same plant materials were also used for isoflavonoid profiling and biosynthetic gene expression analysis. The compound complementation experiment was conducted by adding $200\mu \mathrm{M}$ DMSO (control) or $200\mu \mathrm{M}$ glycitein to the wound of hypocotyls in W82 and gmif6h1 mutants and allowing it to stand for 30 min prior to P. sojae infection. 

Molecular Phylogenetic Analysis. The protein sequences of the CYP76 subfamily from soybean and other plant species were extracted Phytozome 13.0, Lotus base and NCBI. A maximum likelihood tree was constructed using MEGAX software with 1,000 boot-strap replicates (54).
