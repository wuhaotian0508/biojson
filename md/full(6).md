# Abstract

Iron (Fe) deficiency significantly effects plant growth and development. Plant symptoms

under excess zinc $( Z \boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } )$ resemble symptoms of Fe-deficient plants. To understand cross-talk

between excess Zn and Fe deficiency, we investigated physiological parameters of

Arabidopsis plants and applied iTRAQ-OFFGEL quantitative proteomic approach to examine

protein expression changes in microsomal fraction from Arabidopsis shoots under those

physiological conditions. Arabidopsis plants manifested shoot inhibition and chlorosis

symptoms when grown on Fe-deficient media compared to basal MGRL solid medium.

iTRAQ-OFFGEL approach identified 909 differentially expressed proteins common to all

three biological replicates; the majority were transporters or proteins involved in

photosynthesis, and ribosomal proteins. Interestingly, protein expression changes between

excess Zn and Fe deficiency showed similar pattern. Further, the changes due to excess Zn

were dramatically restored by the addition of Fe. To obtain biological insight into Zn and Fe

cross-talk, we focused on transporters, where STP4 and STP13 sugar transporters were

predominantly expressed and responsive to Fe-deficient conditions. Plants grown on

Fe-deficient conditions showed significantly increased level of sugars. These results suggest

that Fe deficiency might lead to the disruption of sugar synthesis and utilization.

# Significance of the study

Plant symptoms by Fe deficiency are similar to those seen under excess Zn. This is because excess Zn leads to Fe deficiency in cells. However, underlying mechanisms including the protein components involved therein remain unknown. Here, we examined a cross-talk between Fe deficiency and excess Zn at the level of the proteome, and their elemental and sugar concentrations levels. Interestingly, excess Zn and Fe deficiency showed a similar trend not only on cellular elemental concentrations but also on protein expression changes. Moreover, these changes by Fe deficiency were largely recovered by excess Zn. To obtain biological insight into Zn and Fe cross-talk, we mainly focused our attention on the identified transporters and found that the STP4 and STP13 sugar transporters were predominantly expressed under Fe-deficient conditions, created by deficiency of Fe or excess of Zn. Indeed, a significant increase in sugar level was observed in the plants grown on Fe-deficient conditions. As such, our findings suggest that Fe deficiency affects sugar export and accumulation in Arabidopsis shoots, which in turn has a negative effect on the expression levels of proteins involved in photosynthesis.

# Main text

Proteomics is being increasingly used to further our understanding of plant growth and

development under both normal and abnormal environmental conditions [1]. Recently,

gel-free proteomic approaches such as iTRAQ labeling have been tailored to plant proteomics

[2,3]. Lan and coworkers have used the iTRAQ method to examine protein regulations

involved in Fe homeostasis in Arabidopsis shoots [2]. In our laboratory, iTRAQ analysis was

performed to understand the effects of excess Zn on plant cells using microsomal fractions

from Arabidopsis roots [3]. It was found that IRT1, which is a major inducible protein due to

Fe deficiency, is involved in the protection of plants from Zn toxicity; this protein was highly

expressed under excess Zn conditions. To further understand the mechanisms at the level of

the proteome, we performed the same iTRAQ analysis using microsomal fractions from

Arabidopsis shoots. However, highly-abundant proteins such as chloroplast proteins were

mainly identified (Fukao Y, personal communication), and which most probably suppressed

the ionization of peptides from low-abundance proteins during MS analysis. Therefore, we

could not obtain the desired information on proteins responsive to excess Zn. To overcome

this bottleneck, we have been applying the iTRAQ-OFFGEL method in our research [4]; this

method is highly efficient and resulted in the identification of a large number of proteins including the elusive low-abundance proteins.

In the present study, the iTRAQ-OFFGEL method has been applied to establish a quantitative microsomal proteome of Arabidopsis shoot to further our understanding of cross-talk between excess Zn and Fe deficiency at the protein level. To examine the cross-talk between Zn and Fe at protein expression levels, Arabidopsis seeds were grown on basal MGRL media (hereafter, basal) or with/without various concentrations of $\mathrm { Z n S O _ { 4 } }$ and $\mathrm { F e S O _ { 4 } }$ . The fresh weight of two-week-old shoots was decreased on basal media with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathrm { Z n S O _ { 4 } }$ (hereafter, 100-Zn or excess Zn) and that was comparable to basal media without Fe (hereafter, 0-Fe or Fe deficiency), although it was not affected on 10-Zn and 50-Zn

(Supplementary Fig. 1A). The chlorophyll contents were also significantly decreased on 100-Zn and 0-Fe compared to basal media (Supplementary Fig. 1B). The growth inhibition due to excess Zn was sufficiently recovered by addition of 100-Fe (hereafter, 100-Zn $+ 1 0 0$ -Fe). Therefore, we defined the growth conditions (i.e., basal media, 100-Zn, 100-Zn+100-Fe or 0-Fe) for the subsequent downstream proteomics by iTRAQ analysis (Fig.

1). Then, to confirm if the phenotype could be attributed to Fe deficiency in the plant, we analyzed two-week-old shoots for Fe content using ICP-MS (Supplementary Table 1). In This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

the case of 100-Zn and 0-Fe, a decrease in Fe concentration was observed compared to basal, which corresponded to their phenotypes (Fig. 2A). Moreover, decreased Fe concentration due to excess Zn (100-Zn) was recovered by addition of 100-Fe as inferred from restoration of the chlorosis from Fe-deficient shoots (Fig. 2A; Supplementary Fig. 1). Compared to basal, Zn concentrations were found to be increased in all three conditions i.e., 100-Zn, 100-Zn+100-Fe, and 0-Fe (Fig. 2B). These results indicated that Fe and Zn concentrations in Arabidopsis shoots inversely change by the concentration of any one of these two nutrients, which is similar to previous reports [4-6]. Moreover, the concentrations of Mn, Co, Mo, and Cu showed similar varying tendencies to Zn, namely their concentrations were increased by Fe deficiency (Supplementary Table 1). These results indicated that cellular Fe and Zn concentrations showed opposite changes under the Fe deficiency condition.

iTRAQ-OFFGEL analysis was performed using microsomal fractions from Arabidopsis shoots grown on four different conditions as depicted in Figure 1. The main purpose of iTRAQ analysis was to examine large numbers of low-abundance proteins whose expression varied under different concentrations of Fe and Zn. As a result, a total of 909 proteins were commonly identified and quantified with less than $1 \%$ FDR in three biological replicates (Supplementary Table 2). Among them, 883 proteins were commonly identified This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

in all the three growth conditions (Fig. 3A). Interestingly, 62 and 81 proteins were found increased more than 2.0-fold in 100-Zn and 0-Fe, respectively, but no protein was found to be increased in 100-Zn+100-Fe (Fig. 3B; Supplementary Table 3). Furthermore, 272, 160, and 271 proteins were increased more than 1.5-fold in 100-Zn, 100-Zn+100-Fe, and 0-Fe, respectively. Moreover, expression of 48 and 68 proteins showed decreased signal (< 0.5-fold) in 100-Zn and 0-Fe, respectively (Fig. 3B; Supplementary Table 4). Only one protein was found showing less than 0.5-fold expression level in 100-Zn+100-Fe. Further, 116, 3, and 119 proteins showed a decrease in expression of less than 0.667-fold in 100-Zn, 100-Zn+100-Fe, and 0-Fe, respectively. Hence, we could see the effect of excess Zn and Fe deficiency not only on cellular elemental concentrations but also on protein expression changes. Surprisingly, addition of Fe into excess Zn medium largely restored the proteins expression changes caused due to Fe deficiency, except multidrug and toxic compound extrusion (MATE) efflux family protein (AT2G04050) and sulphotransferase 12 (SOT12; AT2G03760) (Fig. 3C-D), even if higher amounts of Zn and Fe were present in the growth medium. In particular, Zn concentration was 2.7-fold higher than that of shoots on basal medium, although it was dramatically decreased by addition of Fe (Fig. 2; Supplementary

Table 1). This might also explain that the observed growth defects in our study were due to Fe deficiency rather than excess Zn.

Two sugar transporters, major facilitator super family protein (STP13; AT5G26340) and sugar transporter 4 (STP4; AT3G19930) have shown higher expression levels under Fe-deficient conditions in our study. STP13 and STP4 protein expressions were increased to 5.538- and 8.179-fold and 2.466- and 1.968-fold in 100-Zn and 0-Fe, respectively

(Fig. 3E-F). Members of STP13 are known to be involved in transport of sucrose, glucose and hexose [7-9], while STP4 has been identified as a monosaccharide transporter [10]. These proteins were also identified as inducible proteins in Arabidopsis shoots (Col-0) due to

Fe deficiency in our previous work [4]. Here, we have verified whether sugar concentration increased or not by Fe deficiency by examining the extracted sugars from two-week-old

shoots. Indeed, the levels of sucrose, fructose, and glucose were significantly increased in

shoots of 100-Zn and 0-Fe compared to basal condition (Fig. 4). The higher expression of

sugar transporters correlates with sugar concentrations in shoots. These results suggested that

sucrose, fructose, and glucose synthesis and utilization were out of balance, or that Fe

deficiency somehow interfered with the export of these sugars. Sucrose and hexoses may be

important signaling molecules in source/sink regulation and in responses to biotic stress [11].

On the other hand, the increased sugar contents due to excess Zn did not decrease to the same levels of those grown on basal medium with the addition of excess Fe (Fig. 4). These results indicate that the sugars may simply increase because of unconsumed carbon derived from the seed storage reserves after germination.

Utilization of iTRAQ-OFFGEL quantitative proteomics approach resulted in an inventory of 909 proteins expressed in the microsomal fractions of Arabidopsis shoots under four different growth conditions: basal, 100-Zn, 100-Zn + 100-Fe, and 0-Fe. A good correlation was found and discussed between the observed phenotype of Arabidopsis plants and the expression levels of proteins possibly involved in a cross-talk of excess Zn and Fe deficiency. The established quantitative proteomes in this study serves as a good resource for the scientific community to expedite the process of understanding the cross-talk between excess Zn and Fe deficiency as exemplified above for sugar transporters. Furthermore, identified protein inventory can also be exploited to propose a working biological model

(Supplementary Fig. 2). It is well know that Fe deficiency leads to the decreasing of some chloroplast proteins such as PSI proteins [12,13]. In addition, our findings might suggest that Fe deficiency affect sugar export and accumulation in Arabidopsis shoots, which in turn have a negative effect on the expression levels of proteins involved in photosynthesis. Indeed, it This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

has been reported that sugar accumulation suppresses expression of photosynthetic genes and inhibits photosynthesis [14-17]. Despite the above evidences and discussion therein, further studies will be required linking sugar signaling and regulation with other physiological and biochemical processes.

# Experimental procedures

Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype wild-type Col-0 seeds were germinated on sterile plates of MGRL medium containing minerals (3 mM KNO3, 2 mM $\mathrm { C a } ( \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 } { \bullet } 4 \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } .$ , 1.75 mM $\mathrm { N a H _ { 2 } P O _ { 4 } \bullet 2 H _ { 2 } O _ { 4 } }$ , 1.75 mM Na2HPO4•12H2O, 1.5 mM MgSO4•7H2O, 1.5 mM K2SO4, 67 μM Na2-EDTA•2H2O, $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ , $1 0 . 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ MnSO4•5H2O, 8.6 μM FeSO4•7H2O, 1 μM $\mathrm { \Delta C u S O _ { 4 } { \bullet } 5 H _ { 2 } O }$ , 130 nM $\mathrm { C o C l } _ { 2 } { \bullet } 6 \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ , and 24 nM $( \mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ) _ { 6 } \mathrm { M o } _ { 7 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 4 } { \bullet } 4 \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ ), 2.3 mM MES-KOH (pH 5.7), $1 . 0 \%$ (w/v) sucrose, and $1 . 2 \%$ purified agar (NacalaiTesque). The seedlings were grown on basal media, 100-Zn, 100-Zn $+ 1 0 0$ -Fe, 0-Fe at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ under a 16-h light/ 8-h dark regime for two weeks.

For chlorophyll quantification, shoots (approximately $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ fresh weight) were homogenized with $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $8 0 \%$ acetone and vortexed for 15 min. The homogenates were centrifuged at $1 0 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min and the obtained supernatants were pooled in a This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

new 2.0 mL microfuge tube. Then, residues were vortexed with $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $8 0 \%$ acetone for 15 min and centrifuged at $1 0 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The supernatants were pooled in the same $2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ tube and brought up to $2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ using $8 0 \%$ acetone. Chlorophyll contents were measured based on absorbance of the extract at 663 and $6 4 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ using the reported equations [18].

For preparation of microsomal fractions and iTRAQ labeling, shoots (approximately $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m g }$ fresh weight) were homogenized with buffer A (50 mM HEPES-KOH, pH 7.5, 5 mM EDTA, 400 mM sucrose, and protease inhibitor cocktail). The homogenates were centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 \times g$ at $4 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and obtained supernatants were centrifuged at $8 0 0 0 \times g$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The supernatants were again centrifuged at 100000 $\overset { \cdot } { \times } g$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ for 60 min to prepare the microsomal fractions. The pellets were washed by buffer A twice under the same condition and then dissolved in $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of iTRAQ buffer (Applied Biosystems). The protein concentration was determined using NanoDrop (Thermo Scientific). Three replicates were prepared from three independent experiments of plants grown at different times. iTRAQ-OFFGEL fractionation and LC-MS/MS analysis was performed as described in [4]. The iTRAQ data for three biological replicates was analyzed by MASCOT server (version 2.4; Matrix Science), and only the data with $\mathrm { F D R } < 1 \%$ were used This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

for subsequent analyses. Only proteins that were identified in all three independent experiments were considered. The $P$ -values were calculated by assuming that measurements approximated to a normal distribution.

Two-week-old shoots were harvested and dried at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 days for determination of metal concentration. Dried samples weighing more than $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ were then digested with ultrapure $\mathrm { H N O } _ { 3 }$ using a microwave digestion system (START D; Milestone General). Elemental concentration in the digests were determined by inductive coupled plasma MS (ICP-MS) (Agilent 7500cx; Agilent Technologies). Measurements were performed on three independent biological replicates.

The sugar concentration was determined from two-week-old shoots (approximately 100 mg fresh weight). Shoots were harvested, weighed and quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen in microfuge tubes and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ till sample preparation. Each shoot sample was homogenized with $8 0 \%$ (v/v) methanol (MeOH) and filtered through a DISMIC-13HP syringe filter (Advantec). Each filtrate was diluted 1:100 with an aqueous solution of MeOH $( \mathrm { M e O H / H _ { 2 } O } = 1 { : } 1 , \mathrm { v / v } )$ ) immediately before LC-MS/MS analysis. LC-MS/MS analyses were performed using HPLC (1100-series; Agilent) and MS (API3000; Applied Biosystems) according to [19].
