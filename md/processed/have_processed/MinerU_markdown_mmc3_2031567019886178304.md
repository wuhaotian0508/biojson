# Combinatorial screening in tobacco enables engineering of rose-terpenoid-enriched rice endosperm

Dear Editor, 

Earth faces escalating multidimensional crises, including global pandemics, extreme weather events, and ecological disruptions, that devastate human health, food security, and ecosystems. Consequently, the development of sustainable agricultural systems has become imperative for ensuring global food security. Conventional breeding often fails to achieve targeted trait improvements due to limited natural genetic variation in crop germplasms. Biofortification based on metabolic engineering thus represents a crucial strategy for the precise enhancement of crop nutritional value. Plant-based platforms offer cost-effective, scalable, and sustainable production of bioactive metabolites. In particular, the rice endosperm outperforms alternative systems in terms of biosafety, storage stability, and processing efficiency (Zhu et al., 2022). Several phytonutrient-enriched rice varieties have been successfully developed, including $\beta$ -carotene-fortified ‘‘golden rice’’, anthocyanin-rich ‘‘purple endosperm rice’’, astaxanthin-bioengineered ‘‘aSTARice’’, and catechin-enriched ‘‘tea rice’’ (Zhu et al., 2025), all demonstrating significant health benefits. However, aroma biofortification with high-value aromatic metabolites remains largely unexplored. 

Terpenoids, a major class of fragrant metabolites, are key contributors to diverse floral and fruity aromas. Beyond their role in eliciting sensory pleasure, many terpenoids also possess significant medicinal properties. Among these, terpenoids abundant in roses are widely utilized in perfumery, functional foods, and as broad-spectrum antimicrobial agents (Wang et al., 2022). However, natural production suffers from yield instability, and heterologous platforms (Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Escherichia coli, and Solanum lycopersicum) achieve subindustrial titers (Gutensohn et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2017). Rice is an ideal metabolic chassis for these compounds, benefiting from a mild flavor that can be enriched without disrupting existing notes. Furthermore, its whole-grain cooking tradition optimally releases volatile aromas. To address this, we engineered de novo rose terpenoid biosynthesis in the rice endosperm via tissue-specific metabolic pathway reconstruction, thereby generating grains with enhanced antioxidant capacity and uncompromised agronomic performance. 

First, we systematically elucidated the terpenoid biosynthetic pathway in rose. The pathway begins with the compartmentalized synthesis of isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) through the plastidial methylerythritol phosphate and the cytosolic mevalonate (MVA) pathways. These universal precursors are then condensed by geranyl diphosphate (GPP), farnesyl diphosphate (FPP), and geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthases, yielding the direct precursors for 

rose terpenoids (Supplemental Figure 1). Specifically, GPP proceeds through two distinct enzymatic mechanisms: plastidlocalized geraniol synthase (GES) (Iijima et al., 2004) or dephosphorylation of GPP by Nudix hydrolases, followed by dephosphorylation by phosphatase to geraniol (Conart et al., 2023). Heterodimeric GPPS complexes, consisting of a catalytically inactive small subunit and a bifunctional large subunit (geranylgeranyl diphosphate), drive GPP production (Gutensohn et al., 2013). In Rosa chinensis, the bifunctional G/FPPS1 synthesizes both GPP and FPP (Conart et al., 2023). This bifunctionality contrasts with the metabolic plasticity observed in Camellia sinensis (tea), which relies on two enzymes, CsNudix26 and CsTPS1 (terpene synthase) (Cheng et al., 2024). 

To evaluate the feasibility of rose terpenoid metabolic engineering in Oryza sativa (rice), we analyzed the coding sequences and expression levels of terpenoid genes. Critically, the low expression of OsDXS2in rice seeds, which encodes the ratelimiting enzyme synthesizing IPP/DMAPP, prompted a functional comparison of rose terpenoid biosynthetic routes (You et al., 2020) (Supplemental Figure 2). Thus, we employed a combinatorial approach to express five genes covering the complete rose terpenoid biosynthetic pathway (Supplemental Tables 1 and 2). To rapidly evaluate these gene combinations for the synthesis of rose terpenoids, we cloned each target gene into the tobacco overexpression vector pEAQ-HT-DEST2 and co-infiltrated mixed Agrobacterium suspensions into leaves of 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana. Volatilome profiling demonstrated increased accumulation of terpenoids, ketones, alcohols, aldehydes, esters, benzene derivatives, and other volatiles across all 16 vector combinations (Supplemental Figures 3A and 3B). However, rose terpenoid production was observed in only six vector constructs with successful co-expression (Figures 1A–1D and Supplemental Figure 3C). Although singleand double-gene constructs failed to produce most rose terpenoids, a three-gene combination spanning the entire pathway produced high yields (up to $2 5 4 1 \ \mu { \sf g } / \ k { \sf g }$ fresh weight/FW), performing as effectively as a five-gene system (Supplemental Figures 4 and 5). Thus, our combinatorial strategy in tobacco provided a rapid screening platform and identified optimal combinations for high-level production of rose terpenoids.

Two plants were selected from each of the nine DH and 14 DPG lines. RT–qPCR analysis of the T2-generation endosperm confirmed constitutive transgene overexpression, with transcript levels up to 15-fold higher than those in ZH11 (Supplemental Figures 7D and 7E). Consistent with this, the gene flux through the rose terpenoid biosynthetic pathway was universally upregulated in transgenic lines. Notably, MVA pathway genes were significantly upregulated in DH lines, whereas in DPG lines, only OsHMGS expression showed significant changes among MVA pathway genes (Figure 1E; Supplemental Figure 8). This indicates the critical role of HMGS as the likely rate-limiting enzyme in this pathway. Volatilome analysis revealed elevated accumulation of volatile metabolites in DPG compared with DH lines, along with increased IPP/DMAPP levels in both transgenic lines relative to ZH11 (Supplemental Figures 9A, 9B, 10A, and 10B). Rose terpenoids were exclusively detected in DPG lines (up to 1740 μg/kg FW), confirming the essential roles of the minimal gene set (Figures 1F–1K; Supplemental Figure 9C). Both DH and DPG rice grains exhibited a significant enhancement in antioxidant activity, attributable to the increased IPP/DMAPP levels and rose terpenoid contents (Supplemental Figure 10). Based on these results, we conducted a sensory evaluation. After treatment with TPS solution, most evaluators described the DPG grains as having a floral aroma (Supplemental Figures 11A and 11B). Furthermore, key agronomic traits showed no significant differences from ZH11, indicating stable yield performance (Supplemental Figures 11C–11J). Although tobacco served as a highthroughput screening chassis, implementing the system in rice endosperm provides a more viable biofortification platform due to its superior biosafety and storage stability. 

Collectively, this study establishes a highly efficient two-step strategy for crop aroma biofortification. By leveraging a highthroughput combinatorial screen in tobacco, we identified a minimal gene set for rose terpenoid biosynthesis and subsequently implemented it in the rice endosperm. This targeted approach enabled the de novo production of rose terpenoids, endowing grains with a floral aroma and enhanced antioxidant capacity without compromising agronomic yield. Future efforts could further boost terpenoid production by refining metabolic flux, for example, by channeling key enzymes (HMGS and GPPS) into plastids, thereby amplifying their expression while downregulating competing pathways. Our work paves the way for the development of aroma-enriched functional foods.

# Plant materials and growth conditions

For germination assays, surface-sterilized seeds were placed on moistened filter paper in distilled water and incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 48-72h. Uniform seedlings were transferred to hydroponic systems containing distilled water for 7 days under controlled conditions (12 h photoperiod, $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ day/night). Seedlings were subsequently transplanted to experimental fields in Yazhou District, Hainan, China ( $1 0 9 ^ { \circ } 1 7 ^ { \prime } \mathsf E$ , $1 8 ^ { \circ } 3 5 ^ { \prime } N$ ). Transgenic rice lines and wild-type ZH11 were cultivated in replicated paddy field trials (three biological replicates per genotype). At physiological maturity, grains were harvested from six plants per accession for volatile metabolite analysis. 

# RT-qPCR analysis

First-strand cDNA was diluted 1:3 prior to analysis. Reactions were performed using the QuantStudio™ 7 Flex Real-Time PCR System (Thermo Fisher Scientific) with the following 10-μL composition: 5 μl $2 \times \mathsf { Q 3 }$ SYBR qPCR Master Mix (ToloBio), 0.3 μl each of forward/reverse primer $( 1 0 ~ \mu \ M )$ , 1.5 μl cDNA template, and nuclease-free water. Thermal cycling parameters: $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 sec; 40 cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 sec and $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 sec. Three technical replicates were run per biological replicate $\scriptstyle ( \ n = 3 )$ . OsActin served as the endogenous reference. Relative expression was calculated via the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C } \mathsf { T } }$ method. Amplification specificity was confirmed by melt curve analysis. Primer sequences are provided in Table S4. 

# Bioinformatic analysis

Genomic data were retrieved from: National Rice Data Center (https://www.ricedata.cn/gene/), NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/), and Phytozome v13 (https://phytozome-next.jgi.doe.gov/); Tissue-specific expression patterns were analyzed using: Rice Expression Database (http://expression.ic4r.org), RiceVarMap (http://ricevarmap.ncpgr.cn), Singlecell transcriptomic data were obtained from REGSTA Database (http://124.220.48.30:3838/REGSTA). 

# GoldenBraid 2.0 modular cloning system and transgenic line generation

Multigene constructs were assembled per (Sarrion-Perdigones et al., 2013): Amplify genetic elements (promoters/CDS/terminators) with GoldenBraid 

adapters, Clone into pUPD2 entry vector via BsmBI/T4 ligase, Assemble transcription units in pDGB3_α vectors using BsaI/T4 ligase, Iteratively combine units in pDGB3_Ω vectors, Reaction components: BsmBI/BsaI restriction enzymes, T4 DNA ligase (NEB), Tango/CutSmart buffers, and destination vectors (pUPD2, pDGB3_α1/α2, pDGB3_Ω1/Ω2). Expression cassettes (DH and DPG) were stably transformed into ZH11 embryogenic calli via Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation. T0 transformants were validated by junction-specific PCR primers (Table S4). 

# Transient expression of rose terpenoids genes in N. benthamiana by Agrobacterium-mediated infiltration

pEAQ-HT plasmids containing the biosynthesis pathway genes of rose terpenoids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain GV3101) via freeze-thaw. Cell patches were resuspended in 1-2 ml Agrobacterium induction buffer (10 mM MES pH 5.6, 10 mM MgCl2, 150 μM acetosyringone) in $2 \ m |$ tubes. The infiltration solution was syringe-infiltrated (needleless) into the leaves from four-week-old N. benthamiana. Each experiment used leaves 6, 7, and 8 from the same plant as biological replicates. 

# Volatile metabolites detection

To 1 g of powder, 2 g of ${ \mathsf { C a C l } } _ { 2 } { \cdot } 2 { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and $2 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { l }$ of EDTA solution (pH 7.5) were added, transferred to a $^ { 2 2 \mathsf { m } }$ headspace vial, sealed, and mixed. The fiber was desorbed at the GC inlet for 25 s. The ion source temperature was $2 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , quadrupole temperature $1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , electron energy $7 0 \mathtt { e V }$ , carrier gas $( \mathsf { H e } , 9 9 . 9 9 9 \% )$ flow rate $1 . 0 \ : \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ , and MS scanned $3 5 { - } 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m / z }$ . # Determination of rose terpenoids standard by GC-MS

Standard solutions of six rose terpenoids (trans- $\beta$ -ionone, $\beta$ -damascenone, $\beta$ - myrcene, α-terpineol, linalool, and geraniol) (Mannschreck et al., 2011; Quan

# Antioxidant activity assay

DPPH (1,1-Diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl) radical scavenging capacity was determined as briefly: homogenize 0.1g brown rice in $1 m | 8 0 \%$ methanol (1:10 w/v), sonicate in ice bath (5 min), centrifuge ( $1 2 , 0 0 0 { \times } { \mathsf { g } }$ , 10 min, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ), mix 100 μl supernatant with 900μl 0.1 mM DPPH methanol solution, incubate in dark $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 30 min), centrifuge ( ${ 4 , 0 0 0 \times 9 }$ , 5 min), measure A517 against methanol blank. Scavenging activity(%)=[1-(Asample - Acontrol)/Ablank] $\times 1 0 0 \%$ . 

# Agronomic trait evaluation

Plants were cultivated at $2 0 ~ \times ~ 2 0$ cm density under standard agronomic practices. Measurements included: Plant height: Base-to-tip distance at maturity $( { \mathsf { n } } \geq 1 0$ plants); Flag leaf dimensions: Length (ligule-to-apex) and maximal width at heading ( $\ln \geq 1 0$ plants); 1000-grain weight: Mean mass of three 1000-seed replicates $( \mathsf { n } ~ \mathsf { \Omega } \geq ~ 4 )$ ; Grain morphology: Length/width of 20 random seeds (digital caliper). Data were analyzed using Excel (Microsoft). 

# Sensory evaluation

Sensory evaluation was performed as described previously (Li et al., 2024). Briefly, a panel of ten trained assessors (5 male, 5 female) evaluated all rice accessions. For evaluation, 0.3 g of hulled rice grains were combined with 900 μl of TPS buffer (100 mM Tris-HCl, 10 mM EDTA, 1 M KCl, pH 8.0) and incubated at $7 5 \textdegree$ for 20 minutes to release volatile compounds. Panelists were then asked to smell the samples and rate the overall fragrance intensity on a scale from 0 (neutral) to 5 (extremely strong). To prevent olfactory fatigue, a 30s break was enforced between samples. The order of sample presentation 



was randomized for each assessor. The final aroma rating for each accession was calculated as the total score from all panelists.
