# Summary

Plants produce a variety of secondary metabolites, and their biosynthesis is often regulated by multiple transcription factors. However, the potential connections between the biosynthesis of different types of metabolites remain unclear. In this study, we present a novel mechanism in which the MYB39-MYB111-bHLH51-TTG1 module synergistically regulates both the phenolic and diterpenoid biosynthetic pathways in the medicinal model plant Salvia miltiorrhiza. 

SmMYB39 acts as a repressor, while the MYB111-bHLH51-TTG1 (SmMBW) complex functions as an activator of these pathways. SmMYB39 interferes with the formation of the SmMBW complex by interacting with all its components. Additionally, SmMYB39 inhibits the transcriptional activity of SmbHLH51 on its target genes. Expression of SmMYB39 is suppressed by exogenous MeJA, whereas the expressions of SmbHLH51, SmMYB111 and SmTTG1 are induced. Furthermore, SmMYB39 interacts with SmJAZ1, a core repressor in the jasmonic acid (JA) signalling pathway. These findings suggest that the SmMYB39-SmMBW module mediates JA signalling and synergistically regulates the biosynthesis of phenolics and diterpenoids in S. miltiorrhiza. Our findings offer important insights into the coordinated regulatory mechanisms of transcription activators and repressors that control distinct metabolic pathways in plants. 

# Introduction

Plants are vital sources of food, medicine, and industrial raw materials. Medicinal plants, the oldest form of medicine, have been integral to traditional healthcare systems worldwide for thousands of years (Li and Weng, 2017). Currently, up to $80 \%$ of the population in developing countries primarily relies on plant-derived medicines (Aziz et al., 2018). Most pharmacologically active compounds are secondary metabolites from plants, such as artemisinin, caffeine and paclitaxel (Coombe-Tennant et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020c). However, many medicinal natural products are complex and present in low concentrations within plants. Enhancing the concentration of multiple components simultaneously is an effective strategy to increase the value of these plants. 

As secondary metabolites, the natural products are being synthesized by plants to adapt to environmental changes (Erb and Kliebenstein, 2020). These metabolites are influenced by both biotic and abiotic factors (Zhang et al., 2022). To achieve high levels of natural products, the effects of environmental factors (e.g. light, temperature, water and fertilizer) and phytohormones 

(e.g. jasmonic acid [JA], auxin, abscisic acid [ABA], gibberellin [GA] and salicylic acid [SA]) on plant secondary metabolism have been extensively studied (Anzano et al., 2022). Transcription factors (TFs) regulate the expression of multiple genes simultaneously (Spitz and Furlong, 2012). Transcriptional regulatory networks controlling active ingredient biosynthesis shed light on the molecular mechanisms by which biotic and abiotic factors influence the synthesis of these metabolites. The use of TFs may be an effective strategy for enhancing the production of pharmaceutically active compounds in plants. Many TFs, including MYBs, bHLHs, WRKYs and ERFs, are involved in the regulation of natural product biosynthesis, such as anthocyanins, artemisinin and tanshinone (Zheng et al., 2023). Many TFs function as protein complexes. Previous studies have shown that MYB, bHLH and WD-repeat (WDR) proteins often form the conserved MBW transcriptional activator complex (Lloyd et al., 2017). The MBW complex has been implicated in hormone signalling, root differentiation and the metabolism of anthocyanins and terpenes (Allen et al., 2023; Muhammad et al., 2025; Ramsay and Glover, 2005). The MBW complex may exert a greater regulatory effect than individual TFs (Pireyre and Burow, 2015). However, 

few studies have explored the role of the MBW complex in regulating other forms of secondary metabolism beyond flavonoid biosynthesis. 

The genus Salvia (Lamiaceae) comprises economically and medicinally significant plants, with approximately 1000 species distributed primarily across tropical and temperate regions (Jia et al., 2021). Salvia miltiorrhiza Bunge is a key medicinal resource used in the prevention and treatment of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases (Li et al., 2018b). In China, the cultivation area of S. miltiorrhiza exceeds $2 0 \ 0 0 0 \ \mathsf { h m } ^ { 2 }$ , with a total output value surpassing 20 billion CNY. Previous studies have shown that hydrophilic phenolic acids (PAs), such as rosmarinic acid (RA) and salvianolic acid (SAB), are derived from the phenylpropanoid and tyrosine pathways, while lipophilic tanshinones (TAs), including tanshinone I (T-I), tanshinone IIA (T-IIA), cryptotanshinone (CT) and dihydrotanshinone I (DT-I), are synthesized through the mevalonate (MVA) pathway in the cytosol and the methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway in plastids (Shi et al., 2019). These two groups of compounds work synergistically to treat cardiovascular diseases by exhibiting anticardiac fibrosis, antithrombotic, antiapoptotic, antioxidant anti-inflammatory, platelet aggregation inhibitory, angiogenesis inhibitory and vasoprotective effects (Li et al., 2018b; Wu and Wang, 2012). Due to its pharmacological properties and commercial value, the demand for high-quality S. miltiorrhiza, rich in PAs and TAs, is increasing. The relatively simple genome of S. miltiorrhiza and its well-established transgenic system make it a model plant for studying the biosynthesis and regulation of secondary metabolites (Zhou et al., 2022). As a result, many studies in genetic and metabolic engineering have focused on enhancing the production of PAs and TAs in S. miltiorrhiza (Xu et al., 2016). 

Many TFs have been identified as regulators of PAs or TAs biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza. Among these, members of the MYB and bHLH families have been the most extensively studied. SmMYB39 acts as a transcriptional repressor, negatively regulating PAs biosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2013). SmMYB36 can inhibit the biosynthesis of PAs but promotes the metabolism of TAs (Ding et al., 2017). Both SmMYB98 and SmMYB98b had positive regulatory effects on TAs, with SmMYB98 also enhancing PAs metabolism (Hao et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020). SmMYB9b facilitates TAs accumulation by activating the expression of MEP pathway genes (Zhang et al., 2017). Among bHLH TFs, SmMYC2 and SmbHLH51 promote PAs accumulation, whereas SmbHLH10 enhances TAs accumulation (Sun et al., 2019; Xing et al., 2018b; Yang et al., 2017). SmbHLH3, SmbHLH37 and SmbHLH92 have the opposite functions to SmMYC2 (Du et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020a,b). Additionally, SmMYC2a/b, SmbHLH148, SmTTG1 and SmWD40-2 promote the metabolism of both PAs and TAs (Li et al., 2018a; Xing et al., 2018c, 2023; Zhou et al., 2016). Notably, most TFs either regulate only one group of bioactive compounds or exert opposing effects on PAs and TAs. However, for S. miltiorrhiza to be more effective as a medicinal plant, it is essential to enhance the content of both PAs and TAs simultaneously to improve its overall quality. 

In this study, we found that SmMYB39 not only negatively regulated the biosynthesis of PAs but also TAs in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots. Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) results revealed that SmMYB39 interacted with the positive regulator SmMYB111, a member of the SmMYB111–SmbHLH51–SmTTG1 (SmMBW) 

complex (Li et al., 2018a). Furthermore, we observed that SmMYB39 can inhibit the formation of the SmMBW complex by interacting with all three of its components. Transgenic analysis of the SmMBW members in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots showed that they can simultaneously induce the biosynthesis of both PAs and TAs. Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) and electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) indicated that SmMBW bind to key gene promoters, activate their transcription and promote the accumulation of PAs and TAs. SmMYB39, however, suppressed the transcriptional activity of SmMBW on its target genes. Additionally, these four TFs participated in JA signalling, and SmMYB39 interacted with SmJAZ1. Our results revealed a coregulatory mechanism that governs both PAs and TAs through the interaction between the negative regulator SmMYB39 and the positive SmMYB111–SmbHLH51–SmTTG1 complex. This provides new insights into the transcriptional regulation of metabolites that accumulate simultaneously in plants from two distinct biosynthetic pathways. 

# Results

# SmMYB39 is a negative regulator of PA and TA biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza

In our previous study, we demonstrated that SmMYB39 negatively regulates the biosynthesis of PAs in S. miltiorrhiza (Zhang et al., 2013). To investigate whether it also plays a role in TAs accumulation, we generated SmMYB39 overexpressing and RNA interference (RNAi) S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots (Figure 1a). It has been reported that more tanshinones would be obtained in the redder roots of S. miltiorrhiza (Wang et al., 2014). Hairy roots were much redder in the RNAi lines than WT, while overexpression lines showed lighter than WT. These results indicated that there was a lower level of tanshinones in the SmMYB39 overexpression lines (Figure 1a). The HPLC results showed that SmMYB39 is a negative regulator of both PAs and TAs biosynthesis (Figure 1b). For example, the levels of RA and SAB in the MYB39-OE-23 (SmMYB39 overexpression line) were $5 \%$ and $2 2 . 6 \%$ of those in the wild-type (WT) control, respectively. The contents of CT, T-I, DT-I and T-IIA were $4 1 . 6 7 \%$ , $1 5 . 0 \%$ , $3 7 . 5 7 \%$ and $2 4 . 5 4 \%$ of those in the WT control, respectively. In the SmMYB39 RNAi lines, both PAs and TAs levels were significantly $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ ) increased. Specifically, the contents of RA and SAB in MYB39-mu-4 were approximately 4.95-fold and 1.14-fold higher than those in the control, respectively. Among the four TAs, CT, T-I and T-IIA in MYB39- mu-4 were 3.08, 2.48 and 2.23 times higher than those in the control (Figure 1b). 

The expression of genes involved in the PAs and TAs biosynthetic pathway was assessed in both the transgenic hairy root lines and the control lines. Pathway gene expression was repressed by SmMYB39 (Figure 1c). For example, the expression of SmPAL1, SmC4H1, Sm4CL2, SmTAT and SmHPPR was only $1 5 \%$ , $31 \%$ , $3 9 \%$ , $4 7 \%$ and $4 9 \%$ of those in the control, respectively, in the MYB39-OE-23 line. Additionally, the expression of SmDXS2, SmDXR, SmHMGR1, SmGGPPS and SmKSL1 in the tanshinone biosynthesis pathway was also significantly reduced. In the SmMYB39 RNAi lines, the expression of these genes was upregulated. # The SmMYB111–SmbHLH51–SmTTG1 complex positively regulates PA and TA biosynthesis

To investigate the regulatory roles of the SmMYB111–- SmbHLH51–SmTTG1 complex in the biosynthesis of PAs and TAs, we generated transgenic hairy roots of SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 in this study (Figures S4–S6). As shown by the HPLC results, all three members of this complex act as positive regulators of PAs and TAs biosynthesis. 

Compared to the control, the RA and SAB contents in MYB111-OE-5 increased approximately $1 1 9 \%$ and $3 6 5 \%$ , respectively. The contents of CT, DT-I, T-I and T-IIA in MYB111- OE-5 were 4.67-, 2.10-, 1.19- and 1.69-fold greater than those in the control. In contrast, the RA and SAB contents decreased by approximately $78 \%$ and $8 7 \%$ in MYB111-mu-3, respectively, compared to the control. Furthermore, the contents of DT-I, T-I and T-IIA in MYB111-mu-1 decreased by $4 2 \%$ , $50 \%$ and $7 2 \%$ , respectively (Figure 3a). 

The expression of genes involved in both the PAs and TAs biosynthetic pathways was altered in the SmMYB111 transgenic hairy roots. When SmMYB111 was overexpressed, most of the pathway genes in PAs biosynthesis were upregulated. Conversely, when SmMYB111 was silenced, the expression of these genes was downregulated. For example, the expressions of SmPAL1 and SmC4H1 in MYB111-OE-5 increased by 8.12- and 7.23-fold, respectively, compared to the control. In contrast, the expression of SmPAL1, Sm4CL2, SmC4H1 and SmRAS in MYB111-mu-1 decreased by $32 \%$ , $9 3 \%$ , $6 1 \%$ and $80 \%$ , respectively. Similarly, the expression of genes in the TAs biosynthetic pathway was also induced by SmMYB111 overexpression and significantly decreased when SmMYB111 was silenced. For instance, the expression of SmCPS1, SmHMGR1 and SmKSL1 in MYB111-OE-3 increased by 5.72-, 4.86- and 6.06-fold, respectively, while the expression of SmKSL1 and SmHMGR1 in MYB111-mu-1 decreased by $2 1 \%$ and $5 5 \%$ , respectively, compared to the control (Figure 3b).

SmTTG1 also acted as a positive regulator of PAs and TAs biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza. In the TTG1-OE-3 overexpression line, the contents of RA and SAB increased by 1.54- and 3.20- fold, respectively, while the contents of CT, DT-I, T-I and T-IIA increased by 20.89-, 1.49-, 3.80- and 1.89-fold, respectively, compared with the control (Figure 5a). The expressions of most biosynthetic genes involved in PAs and TAs biosynthesis were influenced by SmTTG1. For instance, expression levels of SmPAL1, SmC4H1, SmTAT1, SmHPPR, SmRAS and SmCYP98A14 were 7.06-, 5.91-, 6.77-, 3.85-, 3.70- and 22.06-fold higher in TTG1-OE-3, respectively, compared to the control. # SmMYB39-SmMBW module responses to JA in S. miltiorrhiza

In addition to SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1, we screened SmJAZ1, the key factor in the JA signalling pathway, as a potential interacting protein of SmMYB39 using the high-throughput Y2H 

system. We then confirmed the SmMYB39-SmJAZ1 interaction through Y2H, BiFC and Co-IP assays, respectively (Figure 7a–c). JA has been reported as a critical phytohormone involved in the accumulation of PAs and TAs in S. miltiorrhiza (Liu et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2018a). To investigate whether the SmMYB39-SmMBW (SmMYB111–SmbHLH51–SmTTG1) module is part of the JA signalling regulatory network, we treated the S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots with MeJA and analysed the expression of the five genes mentioned above in the treated groups. The results showed that the transcript levels of SmMYB39 were significantly reduced at each time point following MeJA treatment. In contrast, the transcripts of SmMYB111, SmbHLH51, SmTTG1 and SmJAZ1 were significantly upregulated at nearly all timepoints after MeJA treatment (Figure 7d). These findings suggest that the SmMYB39-SmMBW module functions downstream of the JA signal and mediates the JA-induced accumulation of PAs and TAs in S. miltiorrhiza. The functional relationship between SmJAZ1 and the SmMYB39-SmMBW module requires further investigation.

# Discussion

The specific accumulation of various secondary metabolites in plants is governed by transcriptional regulatory networks. For instance, the biosynthesis of artemisinin is regulated by AaMYC2, AaWRKY1, AaERF1, AabZIP1 and others (Zheng et al., 2023), while flavonol production in Arabidopsis thaliana is upregulated by AtMYB111, AtMYB12 and AtMYB11 (Pandey et al., 2014). Similarly, CjWRKY1 is known to regulate berberine biosynthesis in Coptis japonica (Kato et al., 2007), and SbMYB3 promotes root-specific flavone biosynthesis in Scutellaria baicalensis (Fang et al., 2023). However, except in S. miltiorrhiza, there are few reports that exist on the regulation of different types of metabolites by a single TF. In this study, we found that SmMYB39 not only negatively regulated PAs biosynthesis but also TAs. The content of PAs and TAs in the hairy roots overexpressing SmMYB39 was significantly lower than that in the control group, while silencing SmMYB39 resulted in an increased accumulation of PAs and TAs. The SmMYB39 has the conserved protein motifs of subgroup 4 R2R3-MYBs, which is the majority of MYB repressors to which it belongs (Zhang et al., 2013). The RTqPCR results showed that SmMYB39 negatively affected most of the PAs and TAs biosynthetic gene’s expressions. These results indicated that SmMYB39 is a negative regulator of both PAs and TAs biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza. 

Except for subgroup 4, most of the R2R3-MYBs are activators of plants secondary metabolism. For example, overexpression of 

AaMYB1 or AaMYB17 in Artemisia annua increased the artemisinin content (Qin et al., 2021). There are also MYBs, such as SmMYB1, SmMYB9b, SmMYB36, SmMYB52, SmMYB97, SmMYB98, SmMYB98b, SmMYB111, etc., which positively regulate PAs or TAs biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza. It was reported that MYB repressor and activator always form a repressor– activator module with other TFs (Mu et al., 2023). In the present study, we found that MYB111 interacted with SmMYB39 by screening the Y2H library of S. miltiorrhiza. SmMYB111 was reported as a positive regulator of PAs biosynthesis (Li et al., 2018a). Additionally, we found it also positively regulated TAs accumulation by overexpressing and silencing it in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots. SmMYB111 regulated PAs and TAs biosynthesis by activating key genes (e.g. SmPAL1, SmRAS, and SmC4H1 etc.) involved in their biosynthetic pathways. Our results indicated that the MYB repressor (SmMYB39) and activator (SmMYB111) coregulate both PAs and TAs biosynthesis. 

MYB transcription factors typically function by interacting with other TFs to form complexes. The MBW protein complexes, which include MYB, bHLH and WD40 repeat factors, are known transcriptional regulators of secondary metabolite production, such as proanthocyanidins and anthocyanins, in many plant species (Xu et al., 2015). Previous reports have shown that SmMYB111 can form an MBW complex with SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 (Li et al., 2018a). The SmMBW complex (SmMYB111–- SmbHLH51–SmTTG1) acts as a positive regulator of PAs 

biosynthesis. Our results further demonstrated that SmMYB111 can interact with SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 to form an MBW complex that promotes the accumulation of both PAs and TAs. 

Transcription factors typically activate or inhibit downstream gene transcription by directly binding to gene promoter regions (Inukai et al., 2017). For example, AcMYB123 and AcbHLH42 induce anthocyanin biosynthesis by activating the promoters of AcANS and AcF3GT1 (Wang et al., 2019). We observed that the expression of genes involved in PAs and TAs biosynthesis (e.g. SmPAL1, SmC4H1, SmRAS, SmDXS2, etc.) was significantly altered in the SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 transgenic lines. Y1H results revealed that SmbHLH51 can bind to the promoters of SmC4H1 and SmKSL1, activating their transcription. Therefore, we hypothesize that the SmMBW complex upregulates PAs and TAs biosynthesis by activating the transcription of SmC4H1 and SmKSL1. 

In many plants, MYB repressors have been shown to impair the formation of the MBW complex by directly interacting with either the MYB or bHLH component, thereby inactivating the function of the complex (Li et al., 2020; Mu et al., 2023). These MYB repressors are termed MBW interferers (MBWi). MBWi have been identified in many plants, such as AtMYBL2, PpMYB18 and PnMYB4 (Man et al., 2023; Matsui et al., 2008; Zhou et al., 2019). It remains unclear whether SmMYB39 functions as a MBWi and how it synergistically regulates both PAs and TAs biosynthesis with the SmMBW activator. We found that SmMYB39 interacted with all three members of the positive SmMBW complex—SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1. Luciferase complementary imaging assays demonstrated that SmMYB39 interfered with the interactions between SmMYB111–SmbHLH51 and SmMYB111–SmTTG1. Consequently, SmMYB39 negatively regulated PAs and TAs biosynthesis by preventing the formation of the MBW complex. Furthermore, we observed that SmMYB39 downregulated the activation of SmbHLH51 on its target genes, SmC4H1 and SmKSL1. These findings are consistent with those observed in A. thaliana (AtMYBL2), which competes with the positive regulator MYB to bind TT8, transforming the positive regulator complex into a negative one, inhibiting the expression of the DFR gene, and thus suppressing anthocyanin biosynthesis (Matsui et al., 2008). Similarly, PpMYB18 inhibits anthocyanin and proanthocyanidin metabolism by competitively binding with PpMYB10 or PpMYBPA1 to the positive regulator bHLH, isolating the MBW complex from its target genes, and eliminating its activation function (Zhou et al., 2019). In Panax notoginseng, PnMYB4 competes with PnMYB1 for binding to PnbHLH, repressing the activation of saponin structural gene promoters induced by the PnMYB1-PnbHLH complex (Man et al., 2023). 

Jasmonic acids are important lipid hormones that regulate plant secondary metabolism (Farhangi-Abriz and Ghassemi-Golezani, 2019). Specifically, the biosynthesis of flavonoids, terpenoids and alkaloids is modulated through the JA signalling pathway (Wasternack and Song, 2017). Previous studies have shown that methyl jasmonate (MeJA) induces the accumulation of PAs and TAs (Xing et al., 2018a). However, the involvement of the SmMYB39-SmMBW module in the regulation of PAs and TAs through JA signalling remains unclear. In this study, we found that MeJA treatment effectively promoted the transcription of SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1, while inhibiting the expression of SmMYB39. These results suggest that the upregulation of PAs and TAs accumulation may be mediated 

by the inhibition of SmMYB39 and the activation of the SmMBW complex. Additionally, we identified SmJAZ1, which interacted with SmMYB39. JAZ (Jasmonate ZIM Domain) proteins are key regulators in the JA signalling pathway (Zhang et al., 2015). JAZ proteins interfere with the function of other transcription factors by interacting with them. For instance, AtJAZ1 in A. thaliana can interact with bHLHs (TT8, GL3 and EGL3) and R2R3-MYBs (MYB75 and Glabra1) to inhibit anthocyanin accumulation and trichome initiation regulated by JA (Qi et al., 2011). JAZs are also being found to play important roles in PAs or TAs biosynthesis. SmJAZ3, SmJAZ4 and SmJAZ8 were reported to negatively regulate PAs accumulation (Ma et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2023). Our previous study indicated that SmJAZ1 is an activator of JA-induced RA and TAs biosynthesis (Ma et al., 2022). Based on the above results, we speculate that the addition of exogenous MeJA promoted the expression of SmJAZ1, leading to its interaction with SmMYB39 and the release of the SmMBW complex. These findings suggest that, unlike SmMYB39, SmJAZ1 can serve as an interfering factor of SmMYB39 and SmMBW, thereby promoting the formation of the SmMBW complex. However, the specific mechanism requires further investigation.

# Methods

# Hairy root culture and treatment

Transgenic hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza were generated by infecting aseptic leaves with Agrobacterium rhizogenes (ATCC15834) carrying the Ri (root-inducing) T-DNA (transfer DNA). The methods used for hairy root culture and treatment were described in our previous report (Xing et al., 2023). 

For content and gene expression detection, the transgenic hairy roots were harvested at 25th day after subculture. For hormone treatments, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ methyl jasmonate (MeJA) (Sigma-Aldrich, 392707) was applied, while roots treated with ethanol served as the control. Elicitation was conducted on the 18th day after inoculation. Hairy roots were harvested from the culture media at 0, 12, 24, 48 and 72 h after treatment. The experiments were performed in triplicate, and the results are presented as the means SDs. 

# Total RNA and DNA extraction

Total RNA was isolated from frozen S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots using the RNAprep Pure Plant Kit (TIANGEN, China). The RNA was subsequently reverse-transcribed to synthesize first-strand cDNA according to the manufacturer’s instructions for the PrimeScriptTM II 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Takara, Japan). Genomic DNA was isolated using an improved cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) method. The quality and concentration of the genomic DNA and RNA were assessed by agarose gel electrophoresis and spectrophotometer analysis (Thermo Scientific NanoDrop 2000). 

# Acquisition of transgenic hairy roots

The coding regions of SmMYB39, SmMYB111, SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 were amplified, cloned and inserted into pCAMBIA1300 or pK7G2R binary vectors under the control of the CaMV35S promoter and the NOS terminator. For the RNAi experiment, approximately 200 bp from nonconserved regions of SmbHLH51 or SmMYB39 were cloned and inserted into the pK7GWIWg2-R vector. The sgRNA targets were designed according to the genome sequences of SmMYB111, SmTTG1 and pICSL002218A for gene editing. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into A. rhizogenes (ATCC15834). Hairy root lines were generated by transforming leaf explants from sterile S. miltiorrhiza plantlets. Positive transgenic lines were identified by PCR. The primers used for gene cloning and gene editing are listed in Table S1, while the primers for PCR identification are listed in Table S2. 

# Content analysis of PA and TA

The dried hairy roots were ground using a homogenizer (Bioprep-24, Hangzhou, China). A 20 mg aliquot of the powder was extracted with 2 mL of $70 \%$ methanol under ultrasonic treatment for 45 min. The mixture was then centrifuged at $8 0 0 0 \times { \pmb g }$ for 10 min, and the supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ organic membrane filter before being analysed by HPLC. Metabolite concentrations were determined and calculated as described in previous studies (Xing et al., 2018a, 2023). Briefly, the sample injection volume was $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ and the PDA wavelengths used for the detection of the lipid-soluble diterpenoids and water-soluble phenolic acids were $2 7 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ and 280 nm, respectively. Separation was achieved by elution using a linear gradient with solvents A (acetonitrile) and B $( 0 . 0 1 \%$ phosphoric acid solution). On the basis of the retention time of standard substances, effective components were confirmed by comparison. The standard substances were purchased from the National Institutes for Food and Drug Control (Beijing, China). 

# Gene expression analysis

The total RNA reverse transcribed to generate first-strand cDNA for RT-qPCR by using PrimeScriptTM RT Master Mix (Perfect Real Time) (Takara, Tokyo, Japan). The primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Table S3. The SmActin gene was used as a reference. RTqPCR was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions for TB GreenTM Premix Ex TaqTM II (TliRNaseH Plus, Takara). The programme was performed on the QuantStudioTM 6 Flex Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, Massachusetts). Gene expression was quantified via the comparative CT method $( 2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T } )$ . The experiments were performed in triplicate, and the results are presented as the means $\pm$ SDs. 

# Y1H assays

Y1H assays were conducted to determine whether SmMBW directly interacted with the promoters of key genes in the PAs and TAs biosynthetic pathways, following the instructions provided in the MATCHMAKER Gold Yeast One-Hybrid system manual (Clontech). The promoter elements of key genes involved in the PA and TA biosynthesis pathways were analysed with the Plant CARE online server (http://bioinformatics.psb. ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/). Fragments from the promoters of C4H1 and TAT, HPPR, DXS1, DXR, KSL1, CPS1 and CYP76AH1, which contain G-box elements, were amplified. These fragments were then inserted into the pAbAi vector using an In-Fusion Cloning Kit (Clontech). All bait constructs were linearized and integrated into the genome of the Y1H Gold yeast strain, which was selected on synthetic defined SD/- Ura agar medium plates and cultured at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 days. The prey construct consisted of the SmbHLH51 coding sequences (CDS) fused in-frame with the yeast GAL4 transcription activation domain (GAL4 AD) in pGADT7, also using an In-Fusion assay. The prey construct was introduced into yeast cells that had been previously transformed with the bait constructs, with the blank pGADT7 plasmid serving as a control. Positively transformed clones were cultured at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ on SD/-Leu media supplemented with various concentrations of AbA. 

# High-throughput Y2H system screening

The total mRNA of S. miltiorrhiza was reversed to cDNA using the SMART cDNA synthesis kit (Takara, Japan). The cDNA was 

constructed to the pGADT7 vector to make a cDNA library. The CDS of SmMYB39 was inserted into the pGBKT7 vector. To screen the interact proteins, SmMYB39-BD and the cDNA library were co-transferred into AH109 and inoculated on plates containing SD/-T/-L/-H. After incubating at a constant temperature of $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3–4 days, the monoclonal strains were collected using YPDA. The yeast-positive clones were amplified by using the Yeast Colony Rapid Detection Kit. The PCR products were sequenced and analysed. 

# Y2H assays

The full-length CDS of SmbHLH51, SmMYB111, SmTTG1 and SmJAZ1 was cloned into the pGADT7 vector. The constructs SmMYB39-BD and SmbHLH51-AD, SmMYB111-AD, SmTTG1-AD or SmJAZ1-AD were co-transformed in pairwise combinations into the yeast strain AH109. The transformants were subsequently cultured on SD/Trp-Leu-His-Ade/X-a-gal selective medium for 2–5 days. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay

Bimolecular fluorescence complementation was employed to confirm the interaction between SmMYB39 and SmMYB111, SmbHLH51, SmTTG1 or SmJAZ1 using a transient expression system in tobacco leaves. Expression vectors for SmbHLH51-nYFP, SmMYB111-nYFP, SmTTG1-nYFP, SmJAZ1-nYFP and SmMYB39- cYFP were constructed. The recombinant vectors were subsequently introduced into tobacco leaves via A. tumefaciens (GV3101). The tobacco was incubated in the dark for $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ , and the leaves were examined for fluorescence using a confocal microscope (Nikon A1, Tokyo, Japan). 

# Co-immunoprecipitation assay

The CDS of SmMYB39 was inserted into the pCV-4HA-N1 vector to generate HA-SmMYB39. The CDSs of SmMYB111, SmbHLH51, SmTTG1 or SmJAZ1 were inserted into the pCV-3myc-N1 vector, yielding the recombinant vectors SmMYB111- myc, SmbHLH51-myc, SmTTG1-myc and SmJAZ1-myc. To assess protein–protein interactions, 35S:HA-SmMYB39 with 35S: SmbHLH51-Myc, 35S:SmMYB111-Myc or 35S:SmTTG1-Myc were co-transformed into the A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Then the GV3101 containing pairs of these constructs was coinfiltrated into the leaves of 4-week-old tobacco plants. Proteins were isolated $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ later and solubilized in $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Bis-Tris–HCl (pH 7.0), $20 \%$ glycerol and $2 \%$ dodecyl- $\beta$ -D-maltopyranoside. After incubating on ice for 1 h, the samples were centrifuged at $1 6 ~ 0 0 0 ~ \times ~ \pmb { g }$ for 20 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The supernatant was incubated gwith Anti-HA Beads at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight. The immunoprecipitates were washed four times with IP wash buffer (Tris–HCl [100 mM, pH 8.0], NaCl $\left[ 1 5 0 \mathrm { \ m M } \right]$ , EDTA [1 mM], DTT [1 mM], and NP40 $[ 0 . 5 \% ] )$ , separated by SDS-PAGE, and transferred onto a membrane for western blotting. 

# Luciferase complementary imaging analysis

The CDS of SmMYB39 was subsequently fused with the pGreen II 62-SK vector. The CDSs of SmbHLH51 and SmTTG1 were separately inserted into the C-terminal domain of firefly luciferase (cLUC) in the pCMBIA1300-cLUC vector to produce the recombinant vectors pSmbHLH51-cLUC and pSmTTG1-cLUC, respectively (Figure 6a). Additionally, the CDS of SmMYB111 was fused to the N-terminal domain of firefly luciferase (nLUC) in the pCMBIA1300-nLUC vector to generate pSmMYB111-nLUC. The recombinant vectors were then transformed into the 

A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Cultures containing each nLUC and cLUC construct were co-infiltrated into fully expanded tobacco leaves. After 72 h of incubation, fluorescence was observed using the IVIS Spectrum system (Xenogen, United States). 

# Dual-luciferase reporter (dual-LUC) assay

The promoter regions of SmC4H1 and SmKSL1 were cloned and inserted into the pGreenII0800-LUC vector to generate reporters, respectively (Figures 4e and 6d). The 35S-SmbHLH51 and 35S-SmMYB39 vectors were used as effectors. These reporters and effectors were then separately transferred into GV3101. The GV3101 strains containing reporters and GV3101 strains carrying 35S-SmbHLH51 were co-infected in tobacco leaves. Subsequently, the GV3101 strains containing reporters and the two GV3101 strains carrying effectors were co-infiltrated into tobacco leaves. After 2 days of incubation, the leaves were harvested for luciferase activity analysis. GloMax20/20 luminometers (Promega) instrument and dual-Luciferase $\textcircled{1}$ Reporter Assay System were used to determine the results. The results were performed in three biological replicates. 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS Statistics (v.27) and GraphPad Prism (v.10.1) software. Statistical significance was determined using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison test $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ or two-tailed Student’s t-tests $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Data for bar graphs are presented as fold changes or percentages relative to the control, with standard deviations (SD) calculated from three independent experiments. All figures were generated using GraphPad Prism (v.10.1), Adobe Photoshop (v.2024) and Adobe Illustrator (v.2024).
