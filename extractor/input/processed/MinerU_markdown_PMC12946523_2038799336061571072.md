# Summary

Coexpression of genes involved in carbon (C) and nitrogen (N) metabolism offers a promising avenue for improving crop yield. This study investigated the impact of coexpressing phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase [ZmPEPC (P)], aspartate aminotransferase [GmAspAT (A)] and glutamine synthetase [NtGS (G)] in Brassica juncea to enhance plant yield through improved C and N utilization. Plants coexpressing three genes (BjPAG) showed a significant increase in plant growth, biomass, chlorophyll and carotenoid content, while exhibiting decreased anthocyanin and hydrogen peroxide production. These improvements were observed under both ambient ${ \sim } 4 0 0$ ppm; $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) and low ( 200 ppm; $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { , \ d }$ atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Chlorophyll fluorescence study revealed that BjPAG plants had higher photosynthetic efficiency, particularly increased maximum quantum yield of PSII and performance index. This facilitated increased ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation, total soluble sugar and starch accumulation, along with altered stomatal traits under both $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Additionally, BjPAG plants accumulated less ammonium than control plants under both $C O _ { 2 }$ levels, indicating effective functioning of the overexpressed genes. Improved performance (enhanced photosynthetic rates, biomass and seed yield) of BjPAG plants under various N environments (6, 3 and $0 . 3 \mathsf { m m N }$ ) also supported improved N utilization. NMR analysis suggested that PAG-coexpression resulted in a higher flux of assimilated ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ towards sugars, amino acids, organic acids, polyphenols and glucosinolates. In conclusion, this study highlights the effectiveness of coexpressing ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS in B. juncea, leading to efficient C and N utilization, ultimately resulting in enhanced photosynthesis, plant biomass and seed yields, and as a promising strategy for increasing the yield of food crops. 

# Introduction

The global human population is projected to reach 10 billion by 2050 (Garcia et al., 2023; Lal, 2016), necessitating a significant increase in food production. Estimates suggest a $60 \%$ rise in agricultural crop output is required (Springmann et al., 2018; Tester and Langridge, 2010; Tilman et al., 2011; Zhu et al., 2010). This underscores the urgency of enhancing agricultural productivity and sustainability. Genetic engineering offers a promising avenue to achieve this goal. By improving plant C and N assimilation, this technology holds the potential to boost plant biomass and yield (Coruzzi and Bush, 2001; Hasnain et al., 2020; Makino et al., 2022; Nunes-Nesi et al., 2010; Ruan et al., 2012; Seger et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2016). Plant yield, however, is a complex phenomenon influenced by a multitude of genetic and environmental factors. Among these, C and N assimilations play pivotal roles in plant growth and development (Goel et al., 2016; Lawlor, 2002; Sinclair, 2004). Coordinated interaction between 

these pathways is crucial for maintaining a balanced C:N ratio. Consequently, simultaneous enhancement of C and N assimilations is a promising strategy to increase plant biomass and yield. 

Photosynthesis, the fundamental process by which plants convert atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ into carbohydrates, provides energy and carbon skeletons for various metabolic pathways. However, $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ crops like wheat, rice and barley experience a loss of fixed carbon through photorespiration, a consequence of the oxygenase activity of the enzyme Rubisco (Andrews et al., 1971; Furbank et al., 2013; Lundgren, 2020; Sharkey, 1988). This nine-step enzymatic cycle, spanning multiple cellular compartments, leads to $C O _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ production through glycine decarboxylation in the mitochondria, resulting in a net loss of both fixed C and N (Sharkey, 1988; Timm and Hagemann, 2020). While other processes like respiration and amino acid catabolism also contribute to C and N losses (Barbour and Hanson, 2009; Leegood et al., 1995), photorespiration accounts for a significant portion, estimated at around $30 \%$ (Sharkey, 1988). The 

photorespiratory pathway highlights the intricate link between C and N metabolism within leaves. The simultaneous release of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ during photorespiration underscores the importance of efficient $N H _ { 3 }$ reassimilation in the chloroplast through the photorespiratory nitrogen cycle in plants (Keys et al., 1978). This process, facilitated by the glutamine synthetase (GS) and glutamate synthase (GOGAT) pathway, requires both energy and a carbon skeleton. Efforts to eliminate photorespiration entirely for increased plant productivity have been undertaken (Peterhansel and Maurino, 2011; South et al., 2018; Walker et al., 2016). However, transgenic studies in potato and rice suppressing photorespiratory enzymes resulted in reduced growth and productivity, suggesting its multifaceted roles beyond the negative impact on $C 0 _ { 2 }$ fixation (Schjoerring et al., 2006; Xu et al., 2009). Recent research proposes that photorespiration might be linked to other metabolic processes (Busch, 2020; Hodges, 2022; Timm and Hagemann, 2020). It is hypothesized that photorespiratory carbon could be diverted towards amino acid synthesis, potentially increasing net ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation (Busch et al., 2018; Kaachra et al., 2018). Interestingly, $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plants like rice and wheat possess a unique mechanism for trapping and reassimilating photorespired ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ , further contributing to crop productivity (Busch et al., 2013). The $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ photosynthetic pathway efficiently fixes $C 0 _ { 2 }$ by concentrating it around Rubisco in bundle sheath cells, where phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase (PEPC) in mesophyll cells initially fixes $C 0 _ { 2 }$ into oxaloacetate (Hatch, 1987; Leegood, 2013; Taniguchi et al., 2008). This mechanism improves the $C O _ { 2 }$ assimilation rate, minimizes photorespiratory losses and enhances nitrogen use efficiency (NUE) (Kandoi et al., 2016; Osmond et al., 1982; Yadav et al., 2020). Engineering $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plants with $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ pathway enzymes offers an alternative approach to enhance photosynthetic efficiency, potentially leading to improved biomass production and yield (Abbasi et al., 2021; Behera et al., 2023; Yadav and Mishra, 2019). Recent reports indicate the successful introduction of $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ photosynthesis-related genes, including carbonic anhydrase (CA), phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase (PEPC ), pyruvate orthophosphate dikinase (PPDK ), NADP-malate dehydrogenase (NADP-MDH) and NADP-malic enzyme (NADP-ME), alone or in combination, into transgenic rice. These modifications improved agronomic, physiological, morphological, photosynthetic and even nutritional quality (Bandyopadhyay et al., 2007; Ermakova et al., 2021; Kandoi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2015; Lian et al., 2014; Yadav et al., 2020). Overexpression of $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ PEPC in tobacco and Arabidopsis has also been reported to enhance their photosynthetic efficiency (Kandoi et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2021). Notably, overexpressing ZmNADP-MDH and ZmPEPC in Arabidopsis demonstrated significant improvements in photosynthesis (Kandoi et al., 2016, 2018). Furthermore, overexpressing ZmPEPC in transgenic rice resulted in increased carbon levels, altered regulation of the photorespiratory pathway, improved resistance to low N stress and enhanced grain yield per plant (Tang et al., 2018). 

In addition to efficient carbon fixation through photosynthesis, ensuring optimal plant nutrition plays a crucial role in influencing overall growth and development. Nitrogen (N) stands out as a key limiting factor for plant productivity. It is essential for agricultural production and serves as the primary component of various macromolecules, metabolites and signalling compounds vital for plant development (Hawkesford et al., 2012; Iqbal et al., 2019). The widespread use of synthetic N fertilizers has significantly increased crop yields over the past five decades, but with 

environmental consequences (Gojon, 2017; Qiao et al., 2012). To address this challenge, improving NUE is crucial to increase agricultural output, lower production costs and mitigate environmental degradation (Fageria and Baligar, 2005; Han et al., 2015). Consequently, researchers have utilized important N metabolism genes, such as glutamine synthetase (GS ), aspartate aminotransferase (AspAT ), nitrate reductase (NR), nitrite reductase (NiR), alanine aminotransferase (ALT ) and asparagine synthetase (ASN ), to genetically engineer both model and crop plants, aiming to improve NUE and achieve higher yields (Good and Beatty, 2011; Lebedev et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Pathak et al., 2008). Overexpression of endogenous genes, HvGS1-1 in barley and TaGS2-2Ab in wheat, enhanced grain yields and NUE under both high- and low-nitrogen conditions (Gao et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2018). Similarly, in rice, overexpression of TaGS1 (Wu et al., 2021a) and coexpression of OsGS1.1 and OsGS2 (Lal et al., 2023) in a single construct increased NUE, biomass and grain production in transgenic plants. The ability of AspAT-catalysed transaminase processes depends on the availability of an amino group, provided by glutamate produced through the GS-catalysed reaction (Hodges, 2002). However, these attempts to improve plant growth and yield through increased NUE have shown limited success, possibly due to insufficient carbon supply to support higher GS activity (Thomsen et al., 2014). Therefore, to improve growth and productivity in both model and crop plants, it is critical to simultaneously overexpress both carbon and nitrogen metabolism genes. 

This growing interest in coordinated manipulation stems from the understanding that optimal plant growth depends on the tight coordination of C and N metabolism (Foyer et al., 2001; Han et al., 2020; Lawlor, 2002). High ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation rates in photosynthesis require a substantial supply of N, while the assimilation of inorganic N into amino acids necessitates significant amounts of photosynthetically fixed C, highlighting the intricate interdependence between C and N utilization in plants (Coruzzi and Bush, 2001; Stitt and Krapp, 1999). Despite this established link, research focusing on simultaneously enhancing both metabolic pathways remains scarce. For instance, coexpression of AtDof1 and AtGS1 enhanced the activities of both C and N metabolic enzymes, which in turn resulted in improved growth and seed yield in transgenic tobacco plants under low N conditions (Wang et al., 2013). We previously demonstrated that the heterologous expression of ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS in Arabidopsis led to improved reassimilation of photorespired ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ , resulting in increased plant growth (Kaachra et al., 2018). Here, we tested the possibility of efficient C and N utilization by coexpressing ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS in Brassica juncea to improve plant growth and yield under ambient and low $C 0 _ { 2 }$ environments and different nitrogen concentrations. 

# Results

# Development of Brassica juncea plants coexpressing ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS (BjPAG)

The plant expression vector pCAMBIA1302 was used to create expression constructs for the coexpression of ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS. These genes were placed under the control of the CaMV35S promoter and the nos transcriptional terminator (Figure 1a). PCR analysis confirmed the presence of all targeted genes within the coexpression construct in the Agrobacterium 

tumefaciens strain GV3101 (Figure S1). Hypocotyl explants were transformed with A. tumefaciens to generate B. juncea transgenic plants coexpressing ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS. Control plants consisted of two types: wild-type plants (BjWT ) regenerated from hypocotyl explants and vector control plants (BjVC ). The BjVC plants were generated by transforming A. tumefaciens with the empty vector pCAMBIA1302 (i.e. the vector backbone without the gene inserts) and subsequently using this to transform the plants. Figure S2 illustrates the different stages of B. juncea transformation and the in-vitro development of putative transgenic plants ${ { T } _ { 0 } }$ generation). A total of eight putative transgenic plants were identified as positive for the hygromycin phosphotransferase II (hptII ) gene (Figure S3a). Similarly, five independent vector control lines tested positive for the hptII gene (Figure S3d). QD-PCR was used to identify transformants harbouring a single copy of the inserted T-DNA (Figure S3b). Three transgenic lines (L5, L8 and L11, designated as BjPAG5, BjPAG8 and BjPAG11, respectively) were found to harbour a single copy of the T-DNA insert. Among the vector control lines, the BjVC5 line was confirmed to contain a single copy of the T-DNA insert. This line was selected as the representative empty vector control and is referred to as BjVC in all subsequent experiments (Figure S3e). In the ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ generation, genomic DNA-PCR was used to confirm the presence of the transgenes in the selected lines (Figure S3c). QD-PCR analysis was further employed to identify homozygous transgenic plants within these lines (Figure S4a). Selected lines were then advanced to the ${ \sf T } _ { 2 }$ generation, where RT-PCR confirmed the expression of target genes (Figure S4b). These lines were subsequently advanced to the ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ generation for further investigation. As shown in Figure S4c, B. juncea wild-type and selected vector control plants (BjVC ) exhibited comparable performance across various parameters including leaf number, leaf area and shoot biomass. However, the BjPAG plants significantly outperformed both control groups. Specifically, the BjPAG plants exhibited a $34 \% - 3 6 \%$ increase in shoot fresh weight and a $4 3 \% - 4 8 \%$ increase in shoot dry weight (Figure S4d, e). For the remainder of the study, the vector control plants (BjVC ) were used as the primary control group. 

# BjPAG plants displayed enhanced activities of key metabolic enzymes (PEPC, AspAT and GS), increased plant growth and biomass under both ambient $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and low ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) conditions

Enzymatic assays of leaves from 45-day-old vector control (BjVC ) and BjPAG plants grown under ambient ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ conditions (Figure 1c) revealed significantly increased activities of PEPC $( 1 0 2 \% - 1 3 4 \% )$ ), AspAT $( 5 0 \% - 7 4 \% )$ and GS $( 3 3 \% - 4 7 \% )$ ) compared with BjVC plants (Figure 1b). These activities represent the sum of native and overexpressed enzyme activities in the transgenic lines. To investigate the impact of PAG-coexpression on plant growth and responses to different $C 0 _ { 2 }$ levels, both BjVC and BjPAG plants were cultivated under $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions in a controlled environment chamber. BjPAG plants demonstrated enhanced growth under both $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions (Figure 1c,d). After 45 days of growth, their average leaf area increased by $3 2 \% - 5 6 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $3 0 \% - 4 4 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ compared with BjVC plants (Figure 1e). Furthermore, 45- day-grown BjPAG plants showed increased shoot fresh weight by $2 6 \% - 4 6 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) and $3 0 \% - 5 3 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ (Figure 1f), while shoot dry weight was increased by $4 7 \% - 6 7 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $6 0 \% - 9 3 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ compared with BjVC plants (Figure 1g). Fresh weight and dry weight were also measured at two different plant 

development stages (30 and 65 days) for plants grown in $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ environments (Figure S5a,b and Table S1). At 30 days of plant growth, BjPAG plants exhibited significant increases in plant fresh weight by $3 9 \% - 4 9 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) and $3 9 \% - 5 7 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ , while the dry weight was higher by $7 0 \% - 7 9 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $4 7 \% -$ $60 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ than BjVC plants. The percentage increase in fresh weight of 65-day-old BjPAG plants was higher by $3 3 \% - 4 5 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $3 7 \% - 5 3 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ , whereas the dry weight increased by $1 7 \% - 3 2 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $4 3 \% - 7 1 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) compared with BjVC plants. 

# Chlorophyll fluorescence and photosynthetic performance of BjPAG plants under ${ \mathsf { A C O } } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ environments

To assess the photosynthetic performance of BjPAG and BjVC Brassica plants under varying $C 0 _ { 2 }$ conditions, different chlorophyll fluorescence parameters were measured. Plants grown in both $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ environments exhibited significant increases in their photosynthetic performance when coexpressing PAG genes. PAG-coexpressing lines displayed a considerable increase in maximum quantum yield of photosystem II [Y(II)], electron transport rate (ETR) and photochemical quenching (qP) as light intensities increased (0 to $8 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photons $\prime \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s }$ ). At the highest light intensity $( 8 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photons/m2 /s), Y(II) was significantly increased by $4 8 \% - 6 5 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $4 6 \% - 6 7 \% \left( \mathrm { L C O } _ { 2 } \right)$ in PAG-coexpressing plants compared with BjVC plants (Figure 2a,b). Similarly, qP was significantly higher by $5 4 \% - 7 2 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $2 7 \% - 5 4 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ (Figure 2c,d). Interestingly, non-photochemical quenching (NPQ) did not differ significantly between BjVC and PAG-coexpressing plants at either $C 0 _ { 2 }$ level (Figure S5c,d). ETR, which reflects the electron transport rate through photosystem II, was also significantly higher in PAG-coexpressing plants compared with BjVC plants at the highest light intensity $( 4 3 \% - 6 1 \%$ increase under $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $4 6 \% - 6 7 \%$ increase under $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ ) (Figure 2e,f). Furthermore, $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } ,$ a measure of maximum PSII efficiency, was slightly increased by $4 \% - 5 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $4 \% - 8 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ in PAG-coexpressing lines. The performance index $( P l _ { A B S } )$ , which integrates various parameters to assess overall photosynthetic performance, showed a significant increase of $2 4 \% - 5 6 \%$ ， $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and a more substantial increase of $1 1 3 \% - 1 3 7 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ in PAG-coexpressing plants compared with BjVC plants (Figure 2g,h).

# Enhanced photosynthetic pigments, increased carbohydrate production and reduced stress markers in BjPAG plants

To further analyse the effect of PAG coexpression on Brassica plants, various biochemical parameters such as the content of photosynthetic pigments, carbohydrate, ammonium, anthocyanin and reactive oxygen species were measured in BjPAG plants grown under both $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions. BjPAG plants displayed significantly higher levels of photosynthetic pigments compared with BjVC plants. Chlorophyll content 

increased by $2 0 \% - 2 3 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ and $2 9 \% - 3 6 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ ), while carotenoid content increased by $1 9 \% - 2 2 \%$ $( \mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) and a more substantial $6 0 \% { - } 6 8 \%$ $( \mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ (Figure 4a,b). # Analysis of plant metabolites in BjPAG plants

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy was performed to analyse metabolite levels in leaves of 45-day-old BjVC and 

BjPAG plants to understand the effect of PAG-coexpression on different metabolites. The $^ 1 \mathsf { H }$ NMR spectra of PAG-coexpressing plants revealed three distinct regions corresponding to different metabolite groups: amino acids (δ 1.0–3.0 ppm), carbohydrates (δ 

3.0–6.0 ppm), and phenolic acids and flavonoids (δ 6.0–9.0 ppm) (Figure 7j). By comparing these spectra with existing data, we were able to identify 40 different metabolite classes in the Brassica leaf samples, including sugars, amino acids, organic acids, polyphenols, glucosinolates, vitamins and sterols (Table S5). The relative abundance of each metabolite was determined by comparing the intensity of its characteristic peak in the $^ 1 \mathsf { H }$ NMR spectra (Figure 7j) to a known internal standard (TSP) using a previously established method (Kumar et al., 2016). More details on metabolite identification and quantification can be found in the Supplementary Method. BjPAG plants displayed a higher proportion of carbohydrates compared with BjVC plants, including ${ \bf \delta u }$ -glucose, $\beta$ -glucose, maltose, fructose, sucrose/stachyose and ${ \bf \delta u }$ -galactose (Table S5 and Figure S13a). Notably, $\alpha$ -glucose levels were highest in all PAG-coexpressing lines, while maltose was particularly abundant in BjPAG5 and BjPAG8 and fructose levels were highest in BjPAG8 and BjPAG11, respectively. Sucrose/stachyose and ${ \bf { \alpha } } ^ { \bf { \alpha } }$ -galactose also showed higher levels in BjPAG plants compared with BjVC plants. 

The $^ 1 \mathsf { H }$ NMR spectra revealed the presence of various amino acids in both BjVC and PAG-coexpressing plants, including aspartic acid, methionine, glutamine, proline, arginine, alanine, phenylalanine, tryptophan, histidine, serine, glycine and cysteine (Table S5 and Figure S13b). Interestingly, phenylalanine, tryptophan and cysteine were only detected in BjPAG plants and not in BjVC plants. Additionally, aspartic acid, histidine and serine were more abundant in BjPAG plants compared with BjVC plants. Notably, BjPAG5 and BjPAG8 lines displayed higher levels of methionine, glutamine, proline, arginine, alanine and glycine compared with BjVC plants. Glycine and serine, which are crucial one-carbon unit sources in plant cells and essential for $\mathsf { C } _ { 1 }$ metabolism, were also found in higher quantities in BjPAG plants. The relative percentage of organic acids showed that quinic acid was primarily present in BjPAG5 and BjPAG8 lines, while formic acid was exclusively found in PAG-coexpressing plants (Table S5 and Figure S13c). BjPAG plants also displayed higher levels of secondary metabolites, including phenolic compounds, flavonoids, glucosinolates and vitamins (Table S5 and Figure S13d–h). Phenolic compounds such as caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid were highest in BjPAG plants, whereas gallic acid and protocatechuic acid were present only in BjPAG8 and BjPAG11 lines. Flavonoids such as quercetin-3-o-galactoside, rutin and apigenin glycoside derivative were higher in BjPAG plants, while quercetin conjugate, quercetin and kaempferol glycoside were more abundant in BjPAG8 and BjPAG11 lines. Myricetin was abundant in the BjPAG5 and BjPAG8 lines. Glucosinolates such as glucoraphanin and glucobrassicin were higher in BjPAG5 and BjPAG8 plants, with indole-3-carbinol being higher in all BjPAG plants. Vitamins such as riboflavin and niacin were higher in BjPAG8 and BjPAG11 lines, with thiamine only present in BjPAG plants. Other compounds such as choline, inositol and stigmasterol were identified, with choline higher in BjPAG8 and BjPAG11 lines, and inositol and stigmasterol abundant in all BjPAG plants. The specific functions of these secondary metabolites in plant physiology are detailed in Table S7. 

# Increased oil content and favourable fatty acid profile in BjPAG plants

The coexpression of targeted genes significantly increased oil content in BjPAG lines $( 1 4 \% - 2 6 \% )$ ) compared to BjVC plants. The fatty acid composition of the seed oil was analysed using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) (Figure S14). The 

GC–MS chromatograms and data in Table S6 revealed that B. juncea seed extracts contain a complex mixture of various organic compounds. The identified components accounted for $8 6 . 4 \% - 9 8 . 5 \%$ of the total oil content across all samples. The main fatty acids identified in all samples were pentadecane $( 1 . 6 \% - 4 . 7 \%$ ), 2,4-di-tert-butylphenol $( 1 . 5 \% - 4 . 9 \% )$ ), palmitic acid $( 5 . 1 \% - 7 . 9 \% )$ ), linoleic acid $( 9 . 5 \% - 1 4 . 9 \% )$ ), oleic acid $( 0 \% - 2 0 . 1 \% )$ , methyl hexadecadienoate $( 1 2 . 9 \% - 2 0 . 6 \% )$ ), stearic acid $( 1 . 9 \% - 3 . 9 \% )$ ) and erucic acid $1 5 . 2 \% - 2 3 . 6 \% )$ ). A previous study (Sharafi et al., 2015) confirmed that these fatty acids are key metabolites in B. juncea. BjPAG seed samples contained a low proportion of saturated fatty acids (oleic acid and stearic acid) compared with the BjVC sample.

# Engineering C and N metabolism for improved plant growth in B. juncea

Plant growth and photosynthesis rely heavily on both C and N utilization. A significant portion of a plant’s nitrogen budget goes towards building proteins and chlorophyll, essential components of the photosynthetic apparatus (Foyer et al., 2001). Therefore, optimizing the use of both C and N is crucial for maximizing plant yield within limited resources. This study investigates the potential of coexpressing genes from both C and N metabolic pathways in B. juncea plants. The goal is to achieve better plant growth, photosynthetic efficiency and yield compared with manipulating single genes in either pathway. In this research, transgenic B. juncea lines were created that coexpress genes ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS. These genes are involved in both C and N metabolism. The goal was to evaluate how coexpression impacts C and N utilization, ultimately leading to increased photosynthesis, biomass and yield. 

In $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plants, the enzyme PEPC plays multiple roles. It provides carbon skeletons for various biosynthetic processes and regulates functions like stomatal opening, fruit ripening, seed maturation and cellular pH (Chollet et al., 1996; Melzer and O’Leary, 1987). By contrast, $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plants primarily use PEPC for ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fixation, while AspAT and GS enzymes are involved in nitrogen assimilation. Moreover, $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plants have a natural advantage over $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plants due to their higher photosynthetic efficiency. Therefore, a promising strategy to enhance photosynthetic efficiency in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ crops like rice, wheat, tobacco and potato is to introduce genes encoding $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 } .$ - specific PEPC (Ku et al., 1999; Tang et al., 2018; Behera et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2012; Hudspeth et al., 1992; Kogami et al., 1994; Gehlen et al., 1996; Rademacher et al., 2002). However, it is important to note that expressing PEPC alone may not always improve photosynthesis. The significantly increased activity of PEPC, AspAT and GS enzymes in BjPAG plants confirms the successful coexpression of all target genes. 

# Enhanced growth and biomass in BjPAG plants under varying ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and N levels

PAG-coexpressing Brassica plants surprisingly displayed a significant increase in biomass under ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Several studies have shown that plants grown in low $C 0 _ { 2 }$ environments reduce growth by approximately $50 \%$ due to photorespiratory conditions (Sage and Coleman, 2001). This unexpected growth boost is attributed to elevated photosynthesis, as evidenced by increased Y(II), qP and ETR with increasing PAR and $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ and $\mathsf { P l } _ { \mathsf { A B S } }$ . These factors are known to contribute at least $90 \%$ to biomass production (Kurai et al., 2011; Zelitch, 1982). Additionally, PAG-coexpression likely led to increased ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation at $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions, further contributing to higher biomass under 

optimal growing conditions. The improved biomass observed in BjPAG plants across varying N concentrations suggests enhanced NUE due to the coexpression of genes involved in both N and C metabolism. While plant growth decreased under low N, this may be attributed to the significant allocation of N ${ \sim } 3 0 \%$ in rice) towards Rubisco and chlorophyll, both crucial for photosynthesis (Mu and Chen, 2021; Yanagisawa et al., 2004) under N-deficient conditions. The coordinated action of the three coexpressed genes (PEPC, AspAT and GS) is believed to be responsible for the increased biomass. These genes work synergistically to conserve C and N, ultimately stimulating plant growth and enhancing productivity. This highlights the well-established close relationship and coordination between C and N metabolism for optimal plant growth (Lawlor, 2002). 

Nitrogen assimilation requires carbon skeletons, creating an intricate link between N assimilation and C metabolism (Stitt and Krapp, 1999). Overexpression of GS1 has been identified as a promising target for improving plant growth through enhanced NUE (Thomsen et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2021a). For example, Lotus corniculatus transformed with soybean GS1 under a strong promoter showed increased shoot and root biomass (Vincent et al., 1997). Similarly, coexpressing NtGS with either ZmPEPC or both ZmPEPC and GmAspAT resulted in higher shoot dry weight accumulation under normal and lownitrogen conditions (Kaachra et al., 2018). This highlights the importance of the PEPC-catalysed reaction in generating sufficient carbon skeletons to support increased GS activity. However, overexpression of AspAT alone has not shown improvements in NUE or yield in rice, rapeseed and Arabidopsis plants (Kaachra et al., 2018; McAllister et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2009). The AspAT-catalysed reaction relies on both carbon skeletons and amino groups (Hodges, 2002), both of which are supplied by PEPC and GS, respectively. These findings collectively suggest that the cooperative action of PEPC, AspAT and GS plays a critical role in promoting plant biomass in PAG-coexpressing lines, even under limiting ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and N conditions. 

# Enhanced photosynthetic efficiency in BjPAG plants

Chlorophyll fluorescence is a technique used to assess the efficiency of Photosystem II (PSII) in capturing light energy for photosynthesis. It can also indicate how plants respond to environmental stresses and nutrient deficiencies (Maxwell and Johnson, 2000; ˇZivcˇak´ et al., 2014). The efficiency of PSII is directly linked to a plant’s ability to fix ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ (Genty et al., 1989; Harbinson et al., 1990). PAG-coexpressing Brassica plants displayed improved chlorophyll fluorescence parameters $( F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } ,$ PIABS, Y(II), qP and ETR) under various ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and nitrogen conditions. These results suggest that BjPAG plants have a greater capacity to utilize the light energy absorbed by chlorophyll for photosynthesis (Jamil et al., 2007; Mauromicale et al., 2006).

In healthy plants under normal conditions, the $F _ { \mathrm { { v } } } / F _ { \mathrm { { m } } }$ ratio typically ranges from 0.7 to 0.8 (Maxwell and Johnson, 2000). However, this value decreases when plants experience environmental or nutritional stress (Fu et al., 2012). The higher $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ and PIABS values observed in BjPAG plants indicate efficient photosynthetic performance and suggest that these plants are not under stress. $\mathsf { P l } _ { \mathsf { A B S } }$ reflects the overall health of PSII based on its light absorption efficiency. Higher ETR values in BjPAG plants indicate a greater operating efficiency of electron transport through PSII. This translates to a higher quantum yield, which is directly related to increased $C 0 _ { 2 }$ assimilation. Enhanced Y(II) suggests that the photosynthetic system in BjPAG plants is functioning efficiently, converting a greater proportion of absorbed light energy into usable chemical energy (ATP and NADPH). These energy molecules are essential for ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fixation and other vital plant metabolic processes (Furutani et al., 2022; Krall and Edwards, 1991). Higher qP values in BjPAG plants indicate that a larger portion of absorbed light energy is channelled towards photosynthesis. Interestingly, no significant difference in NPQ was observed between BjVC and BjPAG plants, suggesting that both groups dissipate excess light energy from PSII at similar rates (Lefebvre et al., 2005). 

# BjPAG plants thrive under varying $\mathsf { C O } _ { 2 }$ and N levels

This study investigated how PAG-coexpressing Brassica plants respond to different ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. We found that these transgenic plants exhibit improved photosynthetic rates even under ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. This translates to increased plant biomass due to higher levels of $C 0 _ { 2 }$ captured within the leaves. By contrast, low atmospheric $C 0 _ { 2 }$ levels (180–220 ppm) can stress plants and negatively impact their carbon balance, leading to decreased photosynthesis and reduced biomass production (Farquhar and Von Caemmerer, 1982; Sage and Coleman, 2001). 

BjPAG plants displayed enhanced light-utilization efficiency under $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions, as evidenced by higher LCP and $R _ { \mathrm { d } }$ . This suggests they can adapt to various environmental conditions (Barro et al., 1996). However, under ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, BjPAG plants showed a reduced LCP compared with control plants, likely due to their lower dark respiration rates. At both ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ and varying N concentrations, BjPAG plants exhibited higher $P _ { \mathsf { N } } ,$ $g _ { 5 }$ and E. This indicates increased gas exchange and transpiration through leaf stomata, ultimately leading to greater ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation (Tanaka et al., 2013; Wong et al., 1979; Yamori et al., 2020). Our findings also showed that BjPAG plants have higher stomatal density compared with control plants grown under low $C 0 _ { 2 }$ and N levels (6 and 3 mM, respectively). This increase in stomatal density can enhance ${ \mathfrak { g } } _ { \mathsf { s } } ,$ leading to an improved photosynthetic rate and plant growth (Tanaka et al., 2013; Xia et al., 2022). Stomatal density can be affected by various environmental factors, including temperature, water availability, soil nutrients, ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and humidity (Bertolino et al., 2019). However, effective gas exchange is influenced not 

only by operating $g _ { s }$ but also by its distribution across leaf surfaces. More uniform $g _ { 5 }$ distribution improves carbon dioxide diffusion within the leaf without increasing water loss, as the substomatal air remains saturated (Fanourakis et al., 2015). In the present study, stomatal conductance did not vary between control and PAG-coexpressing plants, likely due to similar stomatal functionality, regulation and uniform environmental conditions. The nitrogen content in leaves can influence stomatal functioning, such as density and aperture size, thereby affecting a plant’s water use efficiency. By contrast, another study found that ammonium nutrition under ambient ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions leads to toxic effects, manifesting as stress symptoms and reduced stomatal conductance, ultimately decreasing the rate of photosynthesis (Torralbo et al., 2019). 

Biochemical analysis revealed that BjPAG plants produce more photosynthetic pigments, total soluble sugars and starch even under $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ and N-deficient conditions. This further supports their enhanced photosynthesis and biomass production (Lefebvre et al., 2005). In contrast, deficiencies in C and N are known to decrease photosynthesis and biomass production (Mu and Chen, 2021; Sage and Coleman, 2001). Importantly, BjPAG plants displayed reduced free radical accumulation under photorespiratory and N-deficient conditions. This highlights the role of ROS scavengers in improving stress tolerance. Stress conditions like C and N deficiency can increase free radical production in plants, causing damage (Hoffmann et al., 2005; Zakari et al., 2020). Increased GS activity in BjPAG plants is believed to enhance the photoprotection of the photosynthetic machinery, leading to better recovery from abiotic stresses and improved grain filling and yield (Brauer et al., 2011). These findings collectively suggest that the increased activity of PEPC, AspAT and GS in BjPAG plants helps to conserve C and N. This facilitates a higher intracellular $C 0 _ { 2 }$ concentration for enhanced photosynthesis, ultimately leading to increased biomass production.

This suggests they may have improved nitrogen use efficiency, leading to greater biomass production and seed yield. Additionally, these plants displayed a stronger ability to reabsorb ammonia $( N H _ { 3 } )$ released during photorespiration, indicating enhanced nitrogen utilization (Kaachra et al., 2018). Our study using NMR metabolomics revealed that BjPAG plants produce higher levels of essential metabolites compared with control plants. These metabolites include sugars, amino acids, organic acids, polyphenols and glucosinolates. These molecules play vital roles in plant metabolism, stress defence and overall growth. The photorespiratory pathway highlights the close connection between C and N metabolism in leaves, as both ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ are released during this process (Keys et al., 1978). Our results showed that BjPAG plants accumulated less ammonium than control plants under various $C 0 _ { 2 }$ conditions. This indicates that the coexpressed enzymes ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS effectively utilize ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ . 

Researchers are exploring metabolic engineering strategies to minimize fixed C and N losses caused by photorespiration to enhance agricultural productivity. These strategies include reducing photorespiratory losses and reutilizing photorespiratory byproducts (Eisenhut et al., 2019; Nowicka et al., 2018; Timm, 2020). Some studies have demonstrated that overexpressing enzymes involved in the photorespiratory pathway can significantly enhance growth and biomass production (Lopez-´ Calcagno et al., 2019; Simkin et al., 2017; Timm et al., 2012). Modifying the Rubisco enzyme is one approach, but its complex 

structure poses challenges. Research suggests that reassimilating photorespired ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ through the photorespiratory N cycle (including glycine decarboxylation and chloroplastic ammonia reassimilation pathways) may be more effective than simply blocking photorespiration altogether (Coschigano et al., 1998; Keys et al., 1978; Nunes-Nesi et al., 2010). Several studies have shown that overexpressing cytosolic GS in $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plants provides an additional route for reassimilating photorespired $N H _ { 3 }$ in the chloroplast, leading to improved growth and NUE (Oliveira et al., 2002; Ortega et al., 2004; Thomsen et al., 2014). Busch et al. (2018) proposed that diverting photorespired carbon towards amino acid synthesis could increase $C 0 _ { 2 }$ uptake through the photorespiratory pathway, particularly in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plants that lack mechanisms to compensate for photorespiratory losses. Additional research suggests that $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plants may have mechanisms to recover photorespired ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ that are not fully understood. Some studies suggest $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plants may enhance retrieval of photorespired $C 0 _ { 2 }$ by strategically positioning chloroplasts or utilizing stromules to minimize carbon loss during photorespiration (Betti et al., 2016; Busch et al., 2013; Sage and Sage, 2009). Beyond these approaches, overexpression of key photorespiratory genes has demonstrated significant potential for improving crop productivity. For instance, overexpression of glycine decarboxylase H-protein (GDC-H) in transgenic plants has been shown to improve photosynthesis and vegetative biomass by reducing photorespiratory metabolite accumulation and optimizing carbon flow through the Calvin cycle (Lopez-Calcagno´ et al., 2019; Timm et al., 2012). Additionally, coexpression of GDC-H with sedoheptulose 1,7-bisphosphatase (SBPase) and fructose 1,6- bisphosphate aldolase (FBPA) in Arabidopsis significantly enhanced photosynthesis, leaf area and biomass under both low and high light conditions (Simkin et al., 2017). In our case, overexpression of these three genes facilitated the reutilization of photorespired ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ , ultimately leading to increased plant growth and biomass. 

Coexpressing genes for ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS in Brassica plants led to a coordinated increase in genes involved in building C and N skeletons. This resulted in a larger leaf area, greater shoot dry weight, more photosynthetic pigments, increased carbohydrates and higher seed yield under various N conditions. Analyses using biochemistry, photosynthesis measurements and NMR revealed that BjPAG plants utilized ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ more efficiently to produce sugars and amino acids. This ultimately improved their NUE, leading to increased biomass, seed and oil yield of Brassica plants. Moreover, the fatty acid profiles of BjPAG seed oil displayed interesting differences. Notably, the BjVC sample had a higher proportion of oleic acid than BjPAG lines, suggesting potential benefits for engineering oil composition in BjPAG plants. In addition, it is also evident that BjPAG seed oil could be a valuable natural source of unsaturated fatty acids. For example, in comparison with the BjVC sample, the BjPAG seed samples had a lower proportion of saturated fatty acids (oleic and stearic acid). 

In summary, coexpressing these targeted genes improved photosynthesis and NUE in BjPAG plants compared with BjVC plants. This translated to better growth and seed production under both sufficient and limited nitrogen supply. This research highlights the important connections between different metabolic pathways that are essential for plant health and function. Genetic engineering approaches that target both C and N metabolism simultaneously have shown promise for improving plant performance. For example, manipulating key genes like the 

Dof1 transcription factor from maize in rice and Arabidopsis plants can increase the expression of genes involved in C and N metabolism. This leads to improved NUE and enhanced growth under low-nitrogen conditions (Kurai et al., 2011; Yanagisawa et al., 2004). Overexpressing specific genes like AtNLP7, OsNLP1, OsNLP3 and OsNLP4 in various transgenic plants has been shown to improve N utilization, enhance photosynthetic carbon assimilation, increase yield and improve NUE (Alfatih et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2022). Similarly, overexpressing TaDof1 in wheat under low-nitrogen conditions resulted in improved agronomic traits, increased N uptake and enhanced expression of genes involved in ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ assimilation (Hasnain et al., 2020). These findings support our research, which demonstrates that coexpressing ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS improves $C 0 _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ utilization, ultimately promoting plant growth and seed yield. With rising atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ levels and the accompanying global warming driven by human activity, developing climate-resilient crops is a critical endeavour. Therefore, evaluating the performance of BjPAG-coexpressing plants under elevated $C 0 _ { 2 }$ is highly relevant. We anticipate that these plants will exhibit enhanced photosynthesis and stress tolerance, potentially through the suppression of photorespiration. The ZmPEPC component facilitates the initial fixation of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ producing oxaloacetate (OAA), which is then converted to aspartate (Asp) by aspartate aminotransferase (GmAspAT ). This effectively channels fixed carbon into amino acid synthesis. However, elevated $C 0 _ { 2 }$ often leads to reduced nitrogen concentrations in plant tissues, including grains, which can disrupt the crucial carbon-to-nitrogen balance and negatively impact growth and efficiency (Ainsworth and Long, 2005; Fernando et al., 2017; Gojon et al., 2023; Kundu et al., 2022). This emphasizes the interdependence of nitrogen assimilation and carbon metabolism, where carbon provides the necessary energy and substrates for nitrogen uptake and assimilation (Osanai et al., 2017; Rubio-Asensio and Bloom, 2017; Takabayashi et al., 2016). The NtGS component, cytosolic glutamine synthetase, plays a vital role in nitrogen remobilization, particularly from senescing leaves (Habash et al., 2007; Hirel et al., 2007). Overexpression of NtGS is expected to enhance glutamine synthetase activity in these leaves, improving nitrogen remobilization and potentially contributing to increased grain yield and NUE (Gao et al., 2019). Furthermore, the enhanced nitrogen assimilation capacity provided by NtGS overexpression may help mitigate the decline in grain protein levels that is sometimes observed under elevated ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Overall, this multi-gene approach, targeting key enzymes involved in carbon and nitrogen metabolism, offers a promising strategy for developing crops better adapted to elevated $C 0 _ { 2 }$ environments. It highlights the potential of these transgenic lines to address the challenges posed by changing atmospheric conditions and ultimately improve growth, NUE and grain quality. 

# Conclusion

A fascinating finding revealed by this study is that coexpressing the ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS genes in Brassica plants significantly boosted both the photosynthetic rate and biomass production. This improvement is attributed to the plants’ enhanced ability to utilize $C 0 _ { 2 }$ and $N H _ { 3 }$ . Furthermore, our work demonstrates that coexpressing these genes involved in C and N metabolism significantly contributes to increased seed yield under various nitrogen conditions. 

# Materials and methods

# Generating transgenic Brassica juncea plants

This study describes the development of transgenic Brassica juncea plants expressing three genes: ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS. These genes are coexpressed within a single plant expression construct, as shown in Figure 1a. The presence of all three genes in the construct was confirmed by PCR analysis. The construct was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101, which was previously used in our lab (Kaachra et al., 2018). This strain was then used to genetically transform Brassica juncea cv. Varuna plants. Seeds for these plants were obtained from IARI, New Delhi. 

# Transformation and selection process

Hypocotyl explants were used for Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of B. juncea. The transformation procedure was based on methods established by Pental et al. (1993) and Mehra et al. (2000) with some modifications (detailed in the Supplementary Methods). Brassica juncea vector control (BjVC ) plants were generated using Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 containing an empty vector, pCAMBIA1302. Regenerated putative transformants $T _ { 0 }$ generation) were identified by growing them on selection media containing hygromycin $( 2 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ . 

# Plant growth conditions

Following initial screening, rooted plants ${ { T } _ { 0 } }$ generation) were transferred to individual pots $1 0 \mathsf { c m }$ high and $9 . 5 \mathsf { c m }$ diameter) containing a standard 1:1 mixture of soil and sand. The plants were grown in an environmentally controlled chamber (Percival Scientific) set at $2 3 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a 16-h light/8-h dark cycle. Light intensity was maintained at $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s }$ [Photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD)] and relative humidity at $70 \%$ . Distilled water was provided for irrigation as needed. 

# Molecular analysis of transgenic lines

Genomic DNA-PCR was carried out to confirm the presence of the hygromycin phosphotransferase II gene and targeted genes (ZmPEPC, GmAspAT and NtGS ) in BjVC and selected BjPAG5, BjPAG8 and BjPAG11 plants using vector-specific forward (35S F) and gene-specific reverse primers. Quantitative dual-target PCR (QD-PCR) analysis (Kihara et al., 2006) was used to evaluate T-DNA copy number and homozygosity of transgenic plants using two sets of primers (hpt/ACCg8). Reverse transcription (RT)-PCR was performed to check the expression of the target genes using gene-specific primers (primer sequences and PCR cycling profiles are provided in Table S8). Details of the molecular characterization of transgenic plants are provided in Supplementary information. The presence of both marker and targeted genes was confirmed in each generation. 

# Evaluating plant growth under different ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and nitrogen conditions

The growth of transgenic Brassica plants was investigated under two controlled atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ levels: ambient ${ \sim } 4 0 0$ ppm; $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 } )$ ) and low $( { \sim } 2 0 0 \mathsf { p p m } )$ . For initial screening, transgenic plants were grown in seedling trays, then transferred to pots ( $1 0 \mathsf { c m }$ in height and $9 . 5 \mathsf { c m }$ in diameter) containing a 1:1:1 mix of vermiculite, perlite and cocopeat. The plants were irrigated with half-strength MS medium and maintained in a controlled growth chamber at $2 3 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a 16-h light/8-h dark cycle 

(PPFD, $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s } )$ and $70 \%$ humidity. After 45 days, leaf area and shoot fresh and dry weight were analysed. The leaf area was determined using ImageJ software (https://imagej.nih.gov/ij/) by drying leaves between paper towels and photographing them with a ruler for scale. Dry weight was measured by oven-drying plant material at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ . 

To assess the impact of N levels on plant growth, both BjVC and BjPAG plants were grown in an MS medium with different N concentrations: 6, 3 and $0 . 3 \mathsf { m w N }$ (Goel et al., 2016; Yanagisawa et al., 2004). All other growth conditions remained unchanged. Additionally, in a separate experiment, the complete plants (shoot + root) fresh and dry weight of BjVC and BjPAG plants at varying ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and N concentrations were measured at two development stages: 30 and 65 days after planting. 

# Plant growth and seed yield analysis

Various growth characteristics and seed yield parameters in transgenic lines grown under different N conditions were also evaluated. These parameters included plant height, leaf count, flowering time, number of pods/plant, number of seeds/pod, total seed weight/plant and weight of 100 seeds. 

# Enzyme activity and biochemical analyses

The activity levels of three key enzymes [phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase (PEPC), aspartate aminotransferase (AspAT) and glutamine synthetase (GS)] were measured using protein extracts isolated from leaves of 45-day-old plants, following illumination for $2 \mathsf { h }$ . Established methods from previous studies were used for these enzyme assays (Kaachra et al., 2018; Kumar et al., 2006; Liley and Walker, 1974). 

# Analysis of plant metabolites and stress markers

Levels of various metabolites and stress markers were analysed in the leaves of 45-day-old BjVC and BjPAG plants grown under different ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ and N conditions. Established methods were used to measure the total chlorophyll content (Lichtenthaler and Buschmann, 2001), total soluble sugar (Masuko et al., 2005), starch (Hansen and Moller, 1975; Rose et al., 1991), total leaf-ammonium (Rasco-Gaunt et al., 1999), anthocyanin (Feinbaum and Ausubel, 1988) and $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ content (Junglee et al., 2014; Sergiev et al., 1997). For hydrogen peroxide $( H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide ions $( \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ visualization, histochemical staining on leaf samples was performed using previously described methods (Guan et al., 2019; Rasheed et al., 2021). Detailed descriptions of the biochemical assays can be found in the Supplementary Methods section. 

# Chlorophyll fluorescence and gas exchange study

The effects of varying $C 0 _ { 2 }$ and nitrogen levels on plant photosynthesis were investigated using a chlorophyll fluorescence and gas exchange measurement. Photosynthetic efficiency was assessed in 45-day-old plants by measuring chlorophyll fluorescence parameters like maximum quantum yield of photosystem II $( F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ , photochemical quenching (qP), effective quantum yield [Y(II)], electron transport rate (ETR) and non-photochemical quenching (NPQ) using a portable chlorophyll fluorometer (JUNIOR-PAM, Heinz Walz GmbH, Effeltrich, Germany). Plants were dark-adapted for 20 min before measurements under various light intensities ranging from 0 to $8 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s }$ . A separate fluorometer (FluorPen, Photon Systems Instruments, Drasov, Czech Republic) was used to measure the performance´ index on an absorption basis $( P l _ { A B S } )$ after achieving dark adaptation with leaf clips. 

Gas exchange parameters were evaluated on 45-day-old plants grown under $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions using LI-6400XT portable photosynthesis system (LI-COR, Lincoln, NE, USA). Light and $C 0 _ { 2 }$ response curves were generated using fully expanded $3 ^ { \mathrm { r d } }$ leaf from the top of five different plants grown in separate pots. These plants were previously acclimated to saturating light $( 8 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s } )$ ) conditions for 15 min. Net photosynthetic lightresponse curves $( P _ { \mathsf { N } } / \mathsf { I } )$ were obtained by incrementally increasing PPFD from 0 to $1 6 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s }$ while maintaining a saturating $C 0 _ { 2 }$ concentration in the cuvette $( 4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l / m o l } \ C O _ { 2 }$ for $\mathsf { A C O } _ { 2 }$ and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l / m o l } \mathsf { C O } _ { 2 }$ ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ for $\mathsf { L C O } _ { 2 } )$ . This method was adapted from previous studies (Lu et al., 2017; Sami and Hayat, 2019). A/C curves were generated following Lu et al. (2017) using saturating PPFD in the cuvette. Parameters like net photosynthetic rate $( P _ { \mathsf { N } } )$ , internal $C 0 _ { 2 }$ concentration $\textstyle ( C _ { \mathrm { i } } ) _ { i }$ , stomatal conductance $( g _ { 5 } )$ and transpiration rate (E ) were derived from the light-response curve data. Additional measurements included light compensation point (LCP), dark respiration rate $( R _ { \mathrm { d } } )$ and quantum yields of $C 0 _ { 2 }$ assimilation, assessed at low light intensities $( 0 { - } 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photon/m2 /s) as described by Wang et al. (2001). These same photosynthetic parameters $( P _ { \ N } , \ g _ { \ s } , \ C _ { \mathrm { i } }$ and E ) were also measured in fully expanded leaves from six different plants grown under varying nitrogen concentrations (6, 3 and $0 . 3 \mathsf { m m }$ ) in separate sand-filled pots. 

# Stomatal study

Stomatal morphology, including density, aperture width and aperture length, was quantified using a Hitachi S-3400N Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM). The measurement procedure followed the methods described by Bai et al. (2023). Detailed information on sample preparation for SEM observation of stomata is provided in the Supplementary Methods. 

# NMR analysis

BjVC and BjPAG plants (45-days-old) were analysed using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy following the method of Kim et al. (2010). Samples were pooled from three replicates per line before NMR analysis. Details on sample extraction and NMR methodology are provided in the Supplementary Methods. 

# Oil extraction and fatty acid profiling

For oil content analysis, triplicates of 3 g seeds each were collected from BjVC and PAG-coexpressing plants. Pooled seeds were dried at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $4 \mathfrak { h }$ to reach a moisture content of $5 \%$ . After coarse grinding, oil was extracted using a Soxhlet apparatus with petroleum ether as the solvent for 6 h, following the AOCS method (AOCS, 1998). Oil content was then determined for each sample. For fatty acid analysis, the extracted oil from BjVC and PAG-coexpressing plants was converted to fatty acid methyl esters (FAMEs) using the anhydrous methanolic HCl method developed by Ichihara and Fukubayashi (2010). Details on the GC/MS analysis of fatty acids are provided in the Supplementary Methods.
