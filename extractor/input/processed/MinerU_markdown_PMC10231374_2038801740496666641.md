# Abstract

Auxin can inhibit or promote fruit ripening, depending on the species. Melting flesh (MF) peach fruit (Prunus persica L. Batsch) cultivars produce high levels of ethylene caused by high concentrations of indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), which leads to rapid fruit softening at the late stage of development. In contrast, due to the low concentrations of IAA, the fruit of stony hard (SH) peach cultivars does not soften and produces little ethylene. Auxin seems necessary to trigger the biosynthesis of ethylene in peach fruit; however, the mechanism is not well understood. In this study, we identified miRNA gene family members ppe-miR393a and ppe-miR393b that are differentially expressed in SH and MF fruits. RNA ligase-mediated $5 ^ { \prime }$ rapid amplification of cDNA ends and transient transformation of Nicotiana benthamiana revealed TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE 1 $( P p T I R 1 )$ , part of the auxin perception and response system, as a target of ppe-miR393a and b. Yeast 2-hybrid assay and bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay revealed that PpTIR1 physically interacts with an Aux/IAA protein PpIAA13. The results of yeast 1-hybrid assay, electrophoretic mobility shift assay, and dual-luciferase assay indicated that PpIAA13 could directly bind to and trans-activate the promoter of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid synthase 1 $( P p A C S 1 )$ , required for ethylene biosynthesis. Transient overexpression and suppression of ppe-miR393a and $P p I A A 1 3$ in peach fruit induced and repressed the expression of $P p A C S 1$ , confirming their regulatory role in ethylene synthesis. Gene expression analysis in developing MF and SH fruits, combined with postharvest $\mathfrak { a }$ -naphthalene acetic acid (NAA) treatment, supports a role for a ppe-miR393-PpTIR1-PpIAA13- PpACS1 module in regulating auxin-related differences in ethylene production and softening extent in different types of peach. 

# Introduction

Fleshy fruit ripening and softening make critically important contributions to fruit quality and are strongly influenced by genetic background, plant hormones, and environmental 

factors. The process of ripening also enhances fruit perishability and marketing (Kumar et al. 2014), negatively impacting fruit production, transport, and quality. Climacteric fruit ripening and softening appear to be regulated predominantly 

through ethylene synthesis and action. Many studies have indicated that there are interactions between indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) and ethylene in fruit ripening, and these 2 hormones can either interact antagonistically or synergistically. Auxin is commonly regarded as a negative regulator of fruit ripening and, in this mode, is antagonistic to ethylene (Li et al. 2021a). In many fruits, decreases in auxin levels occur during the initial stage of fruit ripening (Zaharah et al. 2012), and IAA-treated fruits [e.g. banana (Musa acuminata), tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), kiwifruit (Actinidia chinensis), and papaya (Carica papaya)] produce less ethylene, leading to delayed ripening (Su et al. 2015; Li et al. 2016; Gan et al. 2019; Zhang et al. 2020; Kou et al. 2021). In papaya, auxin counteracts the effects of ethylene on fruit ripening via the downstream auxin response factor 2–ethylene insensitive3- like1 (CpARF2–CpEIL1) transcriptional complex to prolong shelf life (Zhang et al. 2020). In some fruits, however, auxin promotes ripening by inducing ethylene emission. Early-ripening plum (Prunus salicina) cultivars and melting flesh (MF) peaches (Prunus persica L. Batsch) exhibited high endogenous auxin levels during fruit ripening (Tatsuki et al. 2013; El-Sharkawy et al. 2014). A similar phenomenon was also observed during the postharvest ripening of durian fruit (Durio zibethinus Murr.), in which an increase in the auxin level occurred concomitantly with the rise in climacteric ethylene biosynthesis (Khaksar et al. 2019; Khaksar and Sirikantaramas 2020). Treatment with exogenous auxin has been shown to increase ethylene production in plum, durian, and peach fruit (Tatsuki et al. 2013; El-Sharkawy et al. 2016a; Khaksar and Sirikantaramas 2020). Molecular studies have shown that the transcription factors (TFs) of the auxin signaling pathway are involved in ethylene modulation; for example, auxin response factor 5 (MdARF5) and auxin response factor 2A (DzARF2A) activate genes for ethylene synthesis in apple and durian, respectively (Yue et al. 2020; Khaksar and Sirikantaramas 2020). In tomato, SlARF2 was characterized as an essential component of the regulatory network controlling fruit ripening (Hao et al. 2015). Although several studies have documented the influence of auxin on ethylene biosynthesis and fruit softening, few have investigated how auxin, acting through microRNA (miRNA) and TFs, regulates ethylene biosynthetic genes. 

Different types of peach display different distinct softening patterns and textural changes during postharvest ripening and can be classified into 3 types: MF, nonmelting flesh (NMF), and stony hard (SH) (Sandefur et al. 2013). The MF fruits soften rapidly due to high levels of ethylene (Haji et al. 2003). The inability of SH fruits to produce ethylene is due to the low IAA concentrations which inhibit the expression of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid synthase 1 $( P p A C S 1 )$ , and the fruits maintain a degree of hardness after harvesting (Tatsuki et al. 2013; Pan et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2021a). This failure of SH peaches to soften is caused by mutations of the auxin biosynthesis gene YUCCA flavin monooxygenase 11 $( P p Y U C 1 1 )$ (Cirilli et al. 2018; Tatsuki et al. 2018). Consequently, application of the synthetic auxin 

$\mathfrak { a }$ -naphthalene acetic acid (NAA) to SH fruit induces high $P p A C S 1$ expression levels, leading to substantial ethylene production and fruit softening. Therefore, for peach, a high concentration of an auxin is necessary to produce large amounts of System II ethylene (Tatsuki et al. 2013). However, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain unclear. 

miRNAs are small sequences of RNA of 20 to 24 nucleotides (nt), involved in posttranscriptional gene regulation by targeting mRNAs for cleavage or translational repression (Wang et al. 2020). miRNA-mediated regulation of auxin homeostasis is a highly conserved process (Jodder 2020). Several major miRNA gene families are involved in determining the balance of auxin concentration and signaling, including miR160/167/393/847, and they play a role in plant growth, development, and stress responses. Of these, miR160 and miR167 target auxin response factors (ARFs), thereby regulating their downstream auxin responsive genes related to both development and stresses in plants (Rubio-Somoza et al. 2009; Jodder 2020). miR393 targets auxin receptors, TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE 1 (TIR1) and AUXIN SIGNALING F-BOX (AFB), to regulate growth, development, and stress responses in various plant species (Si-Ammour et al. 2011; Iglesias et al. 2014). miR847 positively regulates meristematic competence by cleaving IAA28 mRNA to upregulate auxin signaling, thereby determining the duration of cell proliferation and lateral organ growth in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) (Wang and Guo 2015). Although the important role of miRNAs in the auxin signaling pathway is clear, whether miRNAs are involved in peach auxin signaling and fruit ripening is unknown and the underlying mechanism of the auxin signaling cascade regulating peach softening requires investigation. 

Here, we identified an auxin-induced miRNA gene family, ppe-miR393a/b, which is activated by NAA and which decreases the transcripts of PpTIR1. This causes the accumulation of PpIAA13, which can directly bind to the promoter of $P p A C S 1$ and activate its transcription, resulting in an increase in ethylene production and fruit softening. Overexpression of PpIAA13 and ppe-MIR393a in peach fruit promoted the expression of $P p A C S 1$ and confirmed the role of the ppe-miR393-PpTIR1-PpIAA13-PpACS1 module in auxin-induced peach fruit softening.

# Changes in expression of candidate genes in the auxin signaling pathway

To explore the mechanism by which ppe-miR393a/b participate in the auxin signaling pathway during melting peach softening, the expression of components in the auxin signaling pathway, including all TIR1/AFB genes, AUXIN/ IAA-INDUCIBLE (AUX/IAA) genes, and ARF genes, was investigated. A total of 4 TIR1/AFBs, 23 Aux/IAAs, and 17 ARFs have been characterized and named in the peach genome (Guan et al. 2019; Diao et al. 2020) and all of these genes, except for 1 IAA gene, were expressed in fruit tissue [fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments mapped $( \mathsf { F P K M } ) > 0 ]$ (Supplemental Fig. S2). Auxin promotes ethylene release in peach fruits by inducing $P p A C S 1$ expression (Pan et al. 2015), and the low activity of 1-aminocyclopropanecarboxylic acid (ACC) synthase in SH peach is responsible for the inhibition of ethylene production. ACC oxidase is functional, however, since the application of ACC could restore the softening phenotype (Haji et al. 2003; Hayama et al. 2008). Thus, the transcript profiles of TIR1/AFBs, IAAs, and ARFs were compared with that of $P p A C S 1$ , the key gene catalyzing the formation of the ethylene precursor ACC (Supplemental Fig. S2, A and B). Transcript levels of 3 TIR1/AFBs, 2 PpIAAs, and 5 PpARFs were negatively correlated $\left( R < - 0 . 5 \right)$ with $P p A C S 1$ , while 13 PpIAAs and 5 $P p A R F s$ were positively correlated $\langle R > 0 . 5 \rangle$ with $P p A C S 1$ during peach fruit ripening (Supplemental Fig. S2, C and D), and all these auxin signaling pathway genes are thus candidates for the regulation of ethylene production and melting peach softening.

# PpTIR1 interacts with PpIAA13

In the canonical TIR1/AFB signaling pathway, auxin induces the formation of a TIR1–auxin–Aux/IAA complex. To determine whether PpTIR1 interacts with PpIAA13, we performed a yeast 2-hybrid (Y2H) assay. The interaction of PpTIR1–PpIAA13 was evaluated on Quadruple dropout (QDO) selective medium supplemented with a range of concentrations of IAA $( 0 \mu m ,$ 10 μM, 50 μM, and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ ) (Figs. 4A and S4A). Strains of yeast containing bait PpTIR1 (BD) and prey PpIAA13 (AD) vectors were grown on QDO selective medium with X-ɑ-gal and $2 0 0 \mu m$ IAA (Fig. 4A), indicating the interaction between the PpTIR1 and PpIAA13 proteins in yeast needs supplementation of exogenous IAA. To verify the interaction between PpTIR1 and PpIAA13 in planta, bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BIFC) assays were performed in N. benthamiana treated with different concentrations of IAA $( 0 \mu m , 1 0 \mu m ,$ and $5 0 \mu { \scriptscriptstyle M } )$ (Figs. 4B and S4B). Bright yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) fluorescence was observed in the nucleus when PpTIR1-YFPC and PpIAA13-YFPN were co-transformed into N. benthamiana leaves at any concentration of IAA treatment, suggesting that there is sufficient endogenous IAA in N. benthamiana cells, while no fluorescence was detected in the leaves transformed with PpTIR1-YFPC or PpIAA13-YFPN alone (Figs. 4B and S4B). These results demonstrate that in vivo interactions occur 

between PpTIR1 and PpIAA13 in yeast and plant cells in the presence of IAA. 

# ppe-miR393/PpTIR1-PpIAA13-PpACS1 modulate fruit softening during ripening and NAA postharvest treatment of MF and SH peaches

To uncover the molecular pathway affected by ppe-miR393 in peach softening, we checked the expression profiles of ppe-miR393a/b, PpTIR1, PpIAA13, and $P p A C S 1$ during ‘YL’ (MF peach) (Fig. 5) and ‘Xiacui’ (‘XC’) (SH peach) ripening and following postharvest treatment with NAA (Fig. 6). ppe-miR393a/b, PpIAA13, and $P p A C S 1$ were expressed at low levels in both the peach melting ‘Fenglu’ (‘FL’) and the SH ‘XC’ fruits at the S1 to S3 developmental stages but were highly induced at the S4 stage in ‘FL’, but not ‘XC’. In contrast, PpTIR1 showed the opposite expression pattern to miR393a/b transcripts. The abundance of $P p T I R 1$ in both cultivars remained constant during the S1 to S3 stages, whereas at the S4 stage, the expression of PpTIR1 in ‘FL’ peach was half of the level in ‘XC’ peach (Fig. 5B), indicating a potential role of these genes in auxin-mediated ethylene production during peach fruit ripening and softening. 

Analysis of the ppe-MIR393a/b promoter by PlantCARE showed that the ppe-MIR393a/b promoter contained auxin response elements (Supplemental Fig. S5), suggesting the possibility that ppe-miR393a/b responds to auxin. To verify whether ppe-miR393a/b can respond to auxin treatment, we treated ‘XC’ (SH peach), which lacks endogenous IAA, with the synthetic auxin NAA. In 1 mM NAA-treated fruit, ethylene production was massively enhanced and rapid fruit softening was promoted over several days (Fig. 6A). The transcripts of ppe-miR393a were higher in NAA-treated fruit compared with the control at 4 d and $6 \mathrm { d } ,$ , and transcripts of ppe-miR393b were higher at 2 to 6 d after treatment (Fig. 6B). PpTIR1 was inhibited after NAA treatment and this is believed to be due to cleavage action of ppe-miR393a/b. PpIAA13 transcripts were markedly elevated at 4 to 6 d in NAA-treated fruit and $P p A C S 1$ showed a parallel trend (Fig. 6B), which is consistent with their expression patterns during fruit ripening (Fig. 5B). A further 3 SH peach cultivars [‘Huayu’ (‘HY’), ‘Youmingbaitao’ (‘YMBT’), and ‘Qinwang’ (‘QW’)] and 1 MF peach [‘Hujingmilu’ (‘HJML’)] were also treated with NAA and in all cases enhanced ethylene emission and fruit softening was promoted. Meanwhile, the expression profiles of ppe-miR393a/b, PpTIR1, PpIAA13, and $P p A C S 1$ were consistent with the ethylene and firmness phenotype (Supplemental Fig. S6). Our results suggested a model, in which NAA induces accumulation of ppe-miR393a/b transcripts which in turn cleaves the transcripts of $P p T I R 1$ gene and leads to an increase in the level of PpIAA13 transcripts, thereby increasing the expression of $P p A C S 1$ and ethylene production in peach fruit. # Discussion

Biosynthesis of System II ethylene in ripening MF peach requires higher levels of auxin (Tatsuki et al. 2013) and this is associated with softening. SH peach produces much less ethylene and does not show the same level of softening, which is due to the low content of IAA during ripening (Pan et al. 2015). Treating SH peach with exogenous NAA results in increased expression of $P p A C S 1$ and a major increase in ethylene production and fruit softening (Tatsuki et al. 2018). Recent studies have shown that several genes coordinate auxin and ethylene signals during peach fruit ripening (Wang et al. 2021a, b, 2019a). However, whether miRNAs are involved in the molecular mechanism of peach softening has until now remained unclear. Here, we uncovered a ppe-miR393-PpTIR1-PpIAA13-PpACS1 module involved in ethylene synthesis and peach fruit softening.

# The ppe-miR393/PpTIR1 module is involved in auxin-induced fruit ripening

The response of miR393 to auxin has been investigated in several studies. For example, it has been reported that miR393 is required for the production of proper auxin signaling outputs (Windels et al. 2014). Exogenous IAA treatment increases miR393 transcript levels, and the posttranscriptional repression of TIR1/AFB genes by miR393 alters plant auxin responses in Arabidopsis (Chen et al. 2011) and creeping bentgrass (Agrostis stolonifera) (Zhao et al. 2019). Auxin can promote the expression of mdm-MIR393b, which affects the formation of apple rootstock through modulating MdTIR1A (Li et al. 2021b). The accumulation of miR393 is also markedly increased in auxin-induced somatic embryogenesis (Wójcik et al. 2016). The results of these studies are consistent with our findings that ppe-miR393a/b is significantly induced by NAA treatment throughout the postharvest storage of peach fruit (Figs. 6B and S6). 

The functions of miR393 in fruit softening have rarely been investigated. Previous reports in sweet cherry implied a potential role for miR393 in ripening under high ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ (Wang et al. 2019b). Omics-based studies in papaya have identified several miRNAs and target genes associated with the ethylene and auxin signaling pathways, including miR393, and levels of miR393 transcripts decreased during papaya fruit ripening (Cai et al. 2021). In melon, cme-miR393 transgenic plants showed delayed fruit ripening time (Bai et al. 2020). Our experiments confirmed that ppe-miR393 accumulated during MF peach fruit ripening (Fig. 5) and overexpressing or silencing ppe-miR393a in peach fruit elevated and reduced $P p A C S 1$ expression, respectively, which suggested a function for miR393 in contributing to ethylene biosynthesis during ripening (Fig. 7). Guo et al. (2016) suggested that the free IAA levels were significantly higher in miR393 overexpression lines and significantly lower in miR393 target mimic lines, indicating that miR393 may induce IAA accumulation. Whether ppe-miR393 can affect peach fruit IAA level will be difficult to test, since this would require further stable transformation work, methods for which are currently unavailable for peach fruit. 

The action of a miR393/TIR1 control module has been observed to be a very common feature of growth, development, and stress responses in various plant species (Bian et al. 2012; Jodder 2020). In our study, PpTIR1 was also identified as the target of ppe-miR393 (Fig. 2), suggesting that the miR393/TIR1 

module has been conserved among various species, although the study of TIR1 in fruit softening is still limited. Trainotti et al. (2007) indicated that expression of auxin receptors was ripening-related, and NAA treatments decreased their expression. These results imply a potential role for TIR1 in peach softening. In this study, we found the target gene of ppe-miR393, PpTIR1, could interact with PpIAA13, suggesting that ppe-miR393-PpTIR1 can regulate ethylene production through PpIAA13 (Fig. 4). Transient overexpression of PpTIR1 in peach fruit also decreased ethylene production and led to higher firmness compared with the control (Supplemental Fig. S9B), while the expression levels of PpIAA13 and $P p A C S 1$ in transient transgenic fruits were downregulated (Supplemental Fig. S9A). In addition to PpTIR1, PpAFB2, which is also 1 of the predicted targets of ppe-miR393a/b, is also implicated in the possible regulatory function for ppe-miR393-PpAFB2 (Supplemental Fig. S3). However, the Y2H and BIFC results showed that PpAFB2 did not interact with PpIAA13 (Supplemental Fig. S10). We deduce from this that the function of ppe-miR393 targeting PpTIR1 might involve auxin-induced ethylene regulation at the maturation stage, without involving the targeting of PpAFB2. It remains possible, however, that ppe-miR393-PpAFB2 may be involved in other biochemical and biological processes in peach. ppe-miR393a and ppe-miR393b may work cooperatively in NAA-triggered softening in ‘XC’. There was no change in the expression level of ppe-miR393a at 2 d after NAA treatment, but fruit softening initiated (Fig. 6). The time lag between ppe-miR393a expression changes and fruit softening may be compensated by miR393b, as miR393b was induced by NAA about 2-fold at 2 d (Fig. 6B). We also noticed that previously characterized ethylene biosynthesis regulators were induced by NAA (Gu et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2021b) (Supplemental Fig. S11), suggesting that, in addition to the ppe-miR393/ PpTIR1 module, other pathways may be involved in auxin-induced peach fruit softening. Moreover, $P p T I R 1$ transcripts in control (CK) fruit at 1 d or 2 d were more abundant than 0 d, whereas they were significantly lower in NAA-treated fruit at 1 d or 2 d compared with 0 d and also control fruit at an identical period (Figs. 6B and S6). This suggests that other regulators may also affect the transcription of $P p T I R 1$ , while the induction of ppe-miR393a and b by NAA treatment could counteract these effects. It has been reported in previous research that TIR1 can be regulated by TFs belonging to MADS-box and NAC families in Arabidopsis and soybean (Guo et al. 2005; Zheng et al. 2016). 

# A lower level of PpTIR1 may contribute to stabilization and accumulation of PpIAA13

In the canonical auxin action model, when auxin appears in the nucleus, it binds to TIR1, the component of the SCF (SKP1, CUL1, and F-box protein) ubiquitin ligase complex, leading to increased ubiquitination and turnover of Aux/ IAA proteins (Stuttmann et al. 2009; De-la-Peña et al. 2017). RLM- ${ \cdot } 5 ^ { \prime }$ RACE and GUS staining assay confirmed that ppe-miR393a/b could target and cleave PpTIR1 (Fig. 2). Our 

results showing that NAA triggered miRNA393a/b cleavage of PpTIR1, promoting the expression of PpIAA13, may at first seem counterintuitive since higher auxin concentrations promote the interaction between the auxin receptor TIR1 and Aux/IAAs, after which Aux/IAAs undergo ubiquitin-mediated degradation. And, similar results have been reported, in Arabidopsis, that increased miR393 levels contribute to TIR1/AFB2 and AFB3 mRNA repression, leading to downregulation of TIR1 protein, resulting in the stabilization and increase level of AXR3/IAA17 protein (Navarro et al. 2006). Conversely, in Arabidopsis protoplasts, increasing TIR1 led to enhanced auxin-dependent turnover of SHY2/IAA3, resulting in a decrease in the levels of SHY2/IAA3 protein (Maraschin et al. 2009). These results were also consistent with the previous reports in tomato (Ren and Wang 2016) that SlTIR1 transcript accumulation is negatively regulated by NAA in tomato seedlings. SlIAA7, 9, and 14 were strongly upregulated in antisense-TIR1 tomato, while SlIAA9 obviously decreased in SlTIR1 overexpressing lines. The explanation may be that AUX/IAA genes are early auxin response genes that encode short-lived nuclear proteins (Maraschin et al. 2009), and levels of AUX/IAA protein increased after auxin treatment due to the rate of de novo production exceeding the turnover rate (Maraschin et al. 2009). For example, the pea proteins PS-IAA4/5 and PS-1AA6 proteins have a remarkably short $t _ { 1 / 2 }$ (half-lives) (Abel et al. 1994), and Oeller and Theologis (1995) showed that PS-IAA4/5 and PS-IAA6 protein abundance drops dramatically without IAA treatment, but their level substantially increased after IAA treatment. In this study, ppe-miR393 cleaved the transcript of $P p T I R 1$ , lowering the mRNA concentration which may lower TIR1 protein level and trigger stabilization of PpIAA13, leading to the accumulation of PpIAA13 protein (Fig. 8). 

In conclusion, ethylene is a key ripening hormone in climacteric fruits, whereas abscisic acid (ABA) performs this function in strawberry (Li et al. 2022) and other nonclimacteric fruits. ABA, brassinosteroids, and jasmonic acid can also promote ripening in climacteric fruits, where they modulate the concentration of ACO and/or ACS transcripts to enhance ethylene levels (Li et al. 2021a). In peach, auxin can stimulate ethylene synthesis and softening, and we identified auxin-induced miRNAs, ppe-miR393a/b, in fruit and showed that they target the auxin receptor PpTIR1, which regulates $P p A C S 1$ transcripts via PpIAA13. We propose that following NAA treatment or endogenous IAA increase in peach, ppe-miR393a and b levels are increased and promote the cleavage of $P p T I R 1$ , leading to the accumulation of PpIAA13. High levels of PpIAA13 result in high expression of $P p A C S 1$ , which increases ethylene production and peach fruit softening (Fig. 8). This contrasts with the role of auxin signaling in inhibiting ripening in tomato fruit (Hao et al. 2015; Su et al. 2015). These results improve the understanding of the regulation by auxin-induced peach fruit softening and provide potential targets to prevent quick softening and extend shelf life of peach fruit.

# Plant materials and treatment

Four accessions of melting peaches (P. persica) [‘HJML’, ‘Yuanchunbai’ (‘YCB’), ‘Jinmali’ (JML), and ‘FL’] and 4 accessions of SH cultivars (‘XC’, ‘HY’, ‘QW’, and ‘YMBT’) were used in this study. ‘HJML’ were harvested from the Melting Peach Research Institute of Fenghua, Zhejiang Province, China. ‘YCB’, ‘JML’, ‘FL’, ‘XC’, ‘QW’, ‘HY’, and ‘YMBT’ were collected from the National Fruit Germplasm Peach Repository, Jiangsu Academy of Agricultural Sciences, Jiangsu Province, China. Immature ‘HJML’ fruit (S3 stage) and mature ‘XC’, ‘HY’, ‘YMBT’, and ‘QW’ (S4 stage) were used for NAA treatment. ‘HJML’ was sampled at 0 d, 0.25 d, 0.75 d, 1.25 d, 2 d, and 3 d after NAA treatment; ‘XC’ were sampled at 0 d, 2 d, 4 d, and 6 d after treatment; ‘HY’, ‘YMBT’, and ‘QW’ were sampled at 0 d, 1 d, and 2 d after treatment. Fruits treated 

with an aqueous solution of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { L }$ Silwet L-77 were used as controls. Three biological replicates consisting of 2 fruits for each replicate were sampled and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at $\scriptstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . 

# Ethylene and texture measurements

Eight intact peach fruits, divided into 4 replicates, were sealed in an airtight container at room temperature for $^ { 1 \ h , }$ and each transiently infected mesocarp segment was sealed in a $1 0 0 \cdot \mathsf { m L }$ airtight container at room temperature for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Then, 1 mL of headspace gas from each container was sampled and measured by gas chromatography (Agilent Technologies 7890A GC System) to determine ethylene production. Firmness was assessed at 2 positions $9 0 ^ { \circ }$ apart at the equator of intact fruit, with a 5-mm-diameter probe, using a TA-XT2i texture analyzer (Stable Micro systems, UK). The TA setting was as follows: pretest speed $1 0 \mathrm { \ m m } / \mathsf { s } ,$ test speed $1 . 0 \ : \mathrm { m m } / \mathsf { s } ,$ and posttest speed $1 0 . 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } / \mathsf { s } ,$ within a $1 0 \mathrm { - } \mathsf { m m }$ depth of flesh. Before measurement, the peel at the measurement positions was removed. Firmness was measured on each of 9 fruits per treatment at each time point. The firmness of each fruit segment was measured in the same way as that for whole fruit, except that the flesh was only measured to a depth of $5 \mathsf { m m }$ . 

# Expression profile analysis

Total RNA was extracted using a modified cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) method (Chang et al. 1993). RNA-Seq data used for different developmental stages of ‘HJML’ peach fruit can be found in the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) Short Read Archive database (accession number PRJNA576753) according to a previous study (Wei et al. 2021). NAA-treated ‘XC’ fruit and the corresponding control fruit at 4 d were processed for Illumina RNA-Seq analysis. The cultivated peach genome– based reads used a reference for transcriptome analysis. FPKM were used to estimate gene expression levels. miRNA RT-qPCR was performed as described by Bai et al. (2020). mRNA RT-qPCR analysis was carried out as described by Zhang et al. (2022). RT-qPCR was performed on a CFX96 Tough Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, CA). Each RT-qPCR analysis consisted of 3 replicates. The relative expression levels of each gene were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ algorithm. PpTEF and 5.8S rRNA were used as reference genes for mRNA and miRNA, respectively. The primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table S1. 

# Dual-luciferase assay

Full-length coding sequences of $P p I A A s$ and $P p A R F s$ were amplified and inserted into the pGreen II 0029 62-SK vector and the promoters of $P p A C S 1$ were constructed in the pGreen II 0800-LUC vector. Primers used for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table S1. All constructs were confirmed by sequencing and electroporated into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (GV3101). Dual-luciferase assay was performed in N. benthamiana leaves according to the 

previous protocol (Zhang et al. 2017). The relative LUC/ REN value of the empty SK vector mixed with the promoter was set as 1. Five biological replicates were performed for individual experiments. 

# Y1H assay and Y2H assay

The Matchmaker Gold Yeast One-Hybrid Library Screening System (Clontech) was used to test the interaction of PpIAA13 with the $P p A C S 1$ promoter. The 2,000-bp promoter of $P p A C S 1$ was amplified and inserted into pAbAi vector and the full-length PpIAA13 was cloned into pGADT7 vector. The recombinant $P p A C S 1$ promoter-pAbAi vector was linearized and transformed into the Y1HGold yeast strain to test the promoter autoactivation according to manufacturer’s instructions. The combination of pGADT7-PpIAA13 and the pAbAi-PpACS1 was transformed together into Y1HGold. Additionally, combinations of the empty vector pGADT7 and $P p A C S 1$ promoter were co-transformed into Y1HGold as negative controls. A yeast 2-hybrid assay was performed to test the interaction between PpTIR1/PpAFB2 and PpIAA13 using the Matchmaker Gold Yeast Two-Hybrid system (Clontech). Full-length PpTIR1/PpAFB2 and PpIAA13 were separately cloned into pGADT7 and pGBKT7 vectors. pGBKT7 and pGADT7 vectors containing target genes were co-transformed into the Y2H strain and the interactions were detected on QDO (SD/-Ade/-His/-Leu/-Trp) in the presence of different IAA levels (0 μM, 10 μM, 50 μM, and $2 0 0 \mu m )$ and $0 . 0 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ X-ɑ-gal. The primers for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table S1. 

# BIFC assay

The coding sequences of PpTIR1/PpAFB2 and PpIAA13 without stop codons were cloned into either C-terminal or N-terminal fragments of YFP vectors, respectively. The constructs (PpTIR1-cYFP and PpIAA13-nYFP) were confirmed by sequencing and transformed separately into GV3101. The cultures were adjusted to an ${ \mathrm { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 1.0 with infiltration buffer (10 mM MES, 10 mM MgCl2, and $1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ acetosyringone, pH 5.6) and then co-injected into 5-wk-old N. benthamiana leaves treated with $1 0 \mu m$ and $5 0 \mu \boldsymbol { M } ,$ ) or without IAA. Fluorescence signal was observed 2 d after infection, using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Nikon A1-SHS, Japan). The experimental setup was as follows: excitation/ emission laser wavelengths for YFP were $4 8 8 ~ \mathrm { n m } / 5 2 0$ to $5 4 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ . The gain was 60, offset was 0, and pinhole was 1.2 AU. Primers used are listed in Supplemental Table S1. 

# EMSA

The full-length coding sequence of PpIAA13 without a stop codon was inserted into pET-32a vector to generate the PpIAA13-His reconstructed plasmid and transformed into Escherichia coli strain BL21. The transformed cells were treated with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ isopropyl $\beta$ $\beta - \mathsf { D } - 1$ -thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) followed by incubation at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 2 0 \mathrm { { h } } }$ . The E. coli cells were lysed with $1 \times \mathsf { P B S }$ buffer and then subjected to sonication on ice at 200 W with a 3-s/3-s on/off cycle for 20 min and centrifuged at 

$1 0 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 20 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ The supernatant was then purified using a HisTALON Gravity Column (Clontech) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

For EMSA analysis, a Lightshift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Thermo) was used to perform EMSA experiments according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Two micrograms of PpIAA13 protein was added in the EMSA assay. Imaging was performed using ChemiDoc ${ \tt X R S + }$ (Bio-Rad, USA). Signal accumulation mode was set to 1 s for the first image, 300 s for the last image time, and total number of images was 60. Primers used and sequences of the probes are described in Supplemental Table S1. 

# RLM-5′ RACE

The RLM- $. 5 ^ { \prime }$ RACE assay was performed using FirstChoice RLM-RACE Kit (Invitrogen AM1700) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, to verify the miR393-mediated cleavage events predicted. Two micrograms of total RNA was ligated with $5 ^ { \prime }$ RACE adapter by T4 ligase. The adapterligated RNA was then reverse transcribed by Moloney Murine Leukemia Virus (M-MLV) reverse transcriptase. The cDNA was amplified by nested PCR, and the PCR products were purified, ligated to pGEM-T Easy Vector (Promega, USA), and transformed into DH5ɑ, and at least 10 single clones were sequenced. These clones were aligned to the predicted target mRNA sequences for detection of cleavage sites. The primers are listed in Supplemental Table S1. 

# GUS assay

For GUS reporter assay, the full-length coding sequence of PpTIR1 was cloned into pBI121-GUS. Sequences of 495 bp and 403 bp genomic DNA sequences surrounding ppe-MIR393a and ppe-MIR393b were amplified. The STTM393a/b, a fragment for the destruction of endogenous miRNAs, was designed as described by Xing et al. (2021). The ppe-MIR393a/b and STTM393a/ b fragments were inserted into pGreen II 0029 62-SK vector, respectively. All recombined constructs were sequenced and transformed into GV3101. Four mixture groups (35S:PpTIR1-GUS + 35S:Empty-SK; $3 5 5 { : } P p T I R 1 { \cdot } G U 5 + 3 5 5 { : } M I R 3 9 3 a / b ;$ ; 35S:PpTIR1 $- G U S + 3 5 5 . 5 T T M 3 9 3 a / b ;$ and $3 5 5 ; M I R 3 9 3 a / b )$ were infiltrated into the same N. benthamiana leaves. N. benthamiana leaves injected with different combinations of recombinant plasmids were cultured in light/dark cycles of $1 6 { : } 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ for 3 d and used for GUS staining using 5-bromo-4-chloro-3 -indolyl- $\cdot \beta$ -glucuronide (X-Gluc) as a substrate according to GUS Stain Kit (Real-Times, RTU4032) manufacturer’s instructions. Injected leaves were vacuum infiltrated with staining solution for 30 min and incubated overnight at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark. Afterward, the leaves were successively decolorized in $70 \%$ (v/v), 90% (v/v), and 100% (v/v) ethanol. 

# Transient transformation assays in peach fruit

Transient transformation in melting peach fruit ‘Dongtao’ was performed according to a previous study (Wei et al. 2021). For PpIAA13-overexpressing construct, the full-length coding sequence of $P p I A A 1 3$ was inserted into pGreen II 

0029 62-SK vector. A total of 495 bp genomic DNA sequences surrounding the ppe-MIR393a were amplified and inserted into pGreen II 0029 62-SK vector. The sequence of STTM393a was synthesized and inserted into the pGreen II 0029 62-SK vector to repress ppe-miR393a function. The specific 300 bp fragment in the CDS region of PpIAA13 was cloned and inserted into the pTRV2 vector to form the recombinant vector pTRV2-PpIAA13. All constructs were confirmed by sequencing and transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101 separately. The Agrobacterium cultures expressing pTRV1 with pTRV2 or pTRV2-PpIAA13 were mixed in a 1:1 ratio and infiltrated into the flesh segments (1 cm thick) cut from opposite sides of mesocarp perpendicular to the fruit suture and then cut into segments with the basal area of ${ \sim } 6$ to $8 \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ . Infiltration was performed by submerging peach flesh segments into Agrobacterium suspension and applying a vacuum $( - 7 0 \ k \mathsf { P a } )$ for 30 min. Infiltrated flesh segments were cultured on MS medium with or without 1 mM NAA for 3 d. The infiltrated segments were first used for ethylene measurement and measured subsequently for firmness before freezing in liquid nitrogen immediately for subsequent gene expression analysis. Segments cut from the same peach fruit were used as transient transformed tissue and control and were treated as 1 line. Three transgenic lines were used as 3 biological replicates. Each line measurement utilized 1 transgenic segment and 1 control segment from the same peach fruit. 

# Statistical analyses

Statistical differences were tested by unpaired Student’s 2-tailed t test using PRISM8 software (GraphPad). Figures were produced using GraphPad Prism 8.0. The heat maps were constructed using TBtools (Toolbox for Biologists) v1.071 software (Chen et al. 2020). PlantCARE database (http://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/ plantcare/html/) was used to identify cis-acting elements related to auxin (Higo et al. 1999). psRNATarget (http:// plantgrn.noble.org/psRNATarget/) was used to predict the target of miRNA (Dai et al. 2018).
