# Mini-review

# Prolyl 4-hydroxylases, the key enzymes of collagen biosynthesis

Johanna Myllyharju*

Collagen Research Unit, Biocenter Oulu and Department of Medical Biochemistry and Molecular Biology, University of Oulu, FIN-90014 Oulu, Finland

Received 30 July 2002; accepted 2 January 2003

# Abstract

The collagen prolyl 4-hydroxylases (P4Hs), enzymes residing within the endoplasmic reticulum, have a central role in the biosynthesis of collagens. In addition, cytoplasmic P4Hs play a critical role in the regulation of the hypoxia-inducible transcription factor $\mathrm { H F } \alpha$ . Collagen and HIF P4Hs constitute enzyme families as several isoenzymes have been identified. Two catalytic $\alpha$ subunit isoforms have been cloned and characterized for collagen P4Hs from vertebrates, both of them assembling into $\alpha _ { 2 } \beta _ { 2 }$ P4H tetramers in which protein disulfide isomerase (PDI) acts as the $\beta$ subunit. The catalytic properties of the two isoenzymes are very similar, but distinct differences are found in the binding properties of peptide substrates and inhibitors, and major differences are seen in the expression patterns of the isoenzymes. The nematode Caenorhabditis elegans has five P4H a subunit isoforms, PHY1-PHY5. The C. elegans PHY1 and PHY2, together with PDI, are expressed in the collagen synthesizing hypodermal cells and three P4H forms are assembled from them, a PHY-1yPHY-2 $\mathrm { \ P D I } _ { 2 }$ mixed tetramer and PHY-1yPDI and PHY-2yPDI dimers. The mixed tetramer is the main P4H form in wild-type C. elegans. PHY-3 is much shorter than PHY-1 and PHY-2, has a unique expression pattern, and is most likely involved in the synthesis of collagens in early embryos. The genome of Drosophila melanogaster contains approximately 20 P4H a subunit-related genes, and that of Arabidopsis thaliana six. One A. thaliana P4H has been cloned and shown to be a soluble monomer with several unexpected properties. It effectively hydroxylates poly(Lproline), $\left( \mathrm { P r o - P r o - G l y } \right) _ { 1 0 }$ and many other proline-containing peptides.

# 1. Introduction

Collagen prolyl 4-hydroxylases (P4Hs, EC 1.14.11.2) are located within the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum and catalyze the formation of 4-hydroxyproline by the hydroxylation of prolines in -X-Pro-Gly- sequences in collagens and more than 15 other proteins that have collagen-like domains (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998; Myllyharju and Kivirikko, 2001). P4Hs have a central role in the biosynthesis of collagens, as 4-hydroxyproline residues are essential for the formation of the collagen triple helix.

In addition, a novel and distinct family of cytoplasmic prolyl 4-hydroxylases playing a critical role in the regulation of the hypoxia-inducible transcription factor $\mathrm { H I F } \alpha$ has recently been identified (Bruick and Mc-Knight, 2001; Epstein et al., 2001). No overall amino acid sequence homology is detected between the collagen and HIF P4Hs, with the exception that the catalytically critical residues are conserved.

Collagen P4Hs from all vertebrate sources studied are $\alpha _ { 2 } \beta _ { 2 }$ tetramers in which the $\beta$ subunit is identical to protein disulfide isomerase (PDI) (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). Several isoforms of the catalytic $\alpha$ subunit are found in human and mouse tissues, Caenorhabditis elegans and Drosophila melanogaster (Veijola et al., 1994; Helaakoski et al., 1995; Annunen et al., 1997, 1999; Friedman

Successful production of active recombinant P4H by coexpression of the $\alpha$ and $\mathrm { P D I } / \beta$ subunits in insect cells (Vuori et al., 1992) and yeast (Vuorela et al., 1997) has made it possible to study the molecular and functional properties of various P4Hs in detail, and to develop high-level recombinant expression systems for human collagens in insect cells, yeasts and plants (Lamberg et al., 1996; Myllyharju et al., 1997; Vuorela et al., 1997; Toman et al., 2000; Nokelainen et al., 2001a; Merle et al., 2002).

# 2. Collagen P4H families

# 2.1. Vertebrate collagen P4Hs

P4H had long been assumed to be of one type only, with no isoenzymes, but this concept changed when a second $\alpha$ subunit isoform was cloned from human (Annunen et al., 1997) and mouse sources (Helaakoski et al., 1995). Two additional genes encoding human and mouse $\alpha$ subunit-like polypeptides have been identified in genome databases and are currently being cloned and characterized (L. Kukkola, R. Hieta, K.I. Kivirikko and J. Myllyharju; P. Koivunen, P. Tiainen, K.I. Kivirikko and J. Myllyharju, unpublished). Both the $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunits associate with the same $\mathrm { P D I } / \beta$ subunit to form $[ \alpha ( \mathrm { I } ) ] _ { 2 } \beta _ { 2 }$ or $[ \alpha ( \mathrm { I I } ) ] _ { 2 } \beta _ { 2 }$ tetramers, called the type I and type II enzymes, respectively (Fig. 1) (Helaakoski et al., 1995; Annunen et al., 1997). Insect cell coexpression data strongly argue against the existence of mixed vertebrate $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } ) \alpha ( \mathrm { I I } ) \beta _ { 2 }$ tetramers (Annunen et al., 1997).

The human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ subunit consists of 517 amino acids and a signal peptide of 17 additional residues, whereas the $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunit consists of 514 amino acids and a signal peptide of 21 residues (Fig. 2). The overall amino acid sequence identity between the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunits is $6 4 \%$ ，the highest degree of identity,

$8 0 \%$ , being observed within the catalytic C-terminal regions (Helaakoski et al., 1989; Annunen et al., 1997). The $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunits contain five conserved cysteine residues (Fig. 2), and site-directed mutagenesis studies have indicated that essential intrachain disulfide bonds are formed between the second and third conserved cysteines and between the fourth and fifth (John and Bulleid, 1994; Lamberg et al., 1995). Interchain disulfide bonds are not formed between the $\alpha$ and $\beta$ subunits of the P4H tetramer. Both $\alpha$ subunits contain two attachment sites for asparagine-linked oligosaccharides (Fig. 2), but site-directed mutagenesis studies have shown that glycosylation has no role in tetramer assembly or in the catalytic activity (Lamberg et al., 1995). Two forms of mRNA resulting from mutually exclusive alternative splicing are generated from the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunit genes (Helaakoski et al., 1994; Nokelainen et al., 2001b). Although the exon–intron structures of the $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunit genes are very similar (Nokelainen et al., 2001b), this alternative splicing affects the homologous 71-bp exons 9 and 10 in the $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ subunit gene (Helaakoski et al., 1989,1994),while in the $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunit gene the 60-bp exon 12a and 66- bp exon 12b are alternatively spliced (Nokelainen et al., 2001b).

Type I P4H is the main form in most cell types and tissues, while the type II enzyme has been shown to represent approximately $3 0 \%$ of the total P4H activity in cultured human WI-38 and HT-1080 cells and approximately $5 - 1 5 \%$ in various chick embryo tissues (Annunen et al., 1997). The type II enzyme has nevertheless been shown to be the major form in chondrocytes, osteoblasts, endothelial cells and cells in epithelial structures (Annunen et al., 1998; Nissi et al., 2001). The type II P4H represents at least $7 0 \%$ and $8 0 \%$ of the total P4H activity in cultured mouse chondrocytes and cartilage, respectively (Annunen et al., 1998), and is thus likely to have a major role in the development of cartilage, cartilagenous bone and capillaries.

# 2.2. Nematode collagen P4Hs

The nematode C. elegans has a large gene family of more than 150 members that encodes cuticle collagens (Johnstone, 2000). In addition, type IV and XVIII-like collagens are found in its basement membranes (Ackley et al., 2001). The C. elegans genome contains two conserved genes, phy-1 and phy-2, encoding P4H $\alpha$ subunits that are involved in the synthesis of cuticle collagens (Veijola et al., 1994; Friedman et al., 2000; Hill et al., 2000; Winter and Page, 2000). The processed C. elegans PHY-1 and PHY-2 polypeptides consist of 543 and 523 amino acids, respectively, both being synthesized in a form containing a signal peptide of 16 additional residues (Fig. 2) (Veijola et al., 1994; Friedman et al., 2000; Winter and Page, 2000). The degree of amino acid sequence identity between PHY-1 and

PHY-2 is $5 4 \%$ , while that between PHY-1 and PHY-2 and the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ and $\alpha ( \mathrm { I I } )$ subunits ranges from 42 to $4 6 \%$ (Veijola et al., 1994; Winter and Page, 2000).

Recombinant expression of PHY-1 in insect cells together with a human or C. elegans PDI surprisingly showed, that in contrast to vertebrate P4Hs, the active enzyme formed was an $\alpha \beta$ dimer (Fig. 1) (Veijola et al., 1994, 1996). Recombinant coexpression studies with C. elegans PHY-1, PHY-2 and PDI showed, however, that three unique P4Hs are assembled from these polypeptides, a $\mathrm { P H Y  – 1 / P H Y  – 2 / P D I _ { 2 } }$ mixed tetramer, and PHY-1yPDI and PHY-2yPDI dimers (Fig. 1) (Myllyharju et al., 2002). Formation of the recombinant mixed tetramer is highly species-specific, as $\alpha$ subunits and PDIs from other species cannot replace the C. elegans polypeptides (Myllyharju et al., 2002). Studies with hybrid recombinant PHY polypeptides localized the

critical residues required for mixed tetramer assembly to the PHY-1 residues Gln121-Ala271 and PHY-2 residues Asp1-Leu122 (Myllyharju et al., 2002).

The $\mathrm { P H Y  – 1 / P H Y  – 2 / P D I _ { 2 } }$ $\mathrm { P D I } _ { 2 }$ mixed tetramer is the main P4H form in wild-type C. elegans in vivo, along with a small amount of PHY-1yPDI dimers, while PHY-2yPDI dimers are not detected (Myllyharju et al., 2002). The phy- $2 ^ { - / - }$ nematodes, which are phenotypically wildtype (Friedman et al., 2000; Winter and Page, 2000), can fully compensate for the lack of the mixed enzyme tetramer by increasing assembly of the PHY-1yPDI dimer (Myllyharju et al., 2002). The phenotypically dumpy phy- $. I ^ { - / - }$ nematodes (Friedman et al., 2000; Winter and Page, 2000) likewise show increased assembly of the PHY-2yPDI dimer, but to a much lesser degree, leading to only partial compensation for the lack of the two other forms and a marked reduction in total P4H activity (Myllyharju et al., 2002). The PHY-1, PHY-2 and PDI polypeptides are coexpressed in the cuticle collagen synthesizing hypodermal cells at times of maximal collagen synthesis, in larval stages 1–4 and in adult nematodes (Winter and Page, 2000; Myllyharju et al., 2002).

Three additional $\alpha$ subunit-like polypeptides are encoded in the C. elegans genome (Winter and Page, 2000). One of them, phy-3, has recently been cloned and characterized and found to consist of only 295 amino acids (Fig. 2) (Riihimaa et al., 2002). Its amino acid sequence is $1 7 - 2 0 \%$ identical to the approximately 290 C-terminal residues of the vertebrate $\alpha$ subunits and the C. elegans PHY-1 and PHY-2 polypeptides (Riihimaa et al., 2002). Recombinant PHY-3 forms an active P4H when coexpressed with a C. elegans PDI isoform that does not associate with PHY-1 or PHY-2 (Fig. 1) (Veijola et al., 1996; Riihimaa et al., 2002; Myllyharju et al., 2002). The phy-3 gene is expressed in embryos, late larval stages and adult nematodes, expression in the adults being restricted to spermatheca, a specialized region of the gonad where oocytes are fertilized (Riihimaa et al., 2002). The phy- $. 3 ^ { - / - }$ adult nematodes are phenotypically wild-type and fertile, but the 4-hydroxyproline content of early mutant embryos was reduced by approximately $9 0 \%$ (Riihimaa et al., 2002). It is thus likely that PHY-3 is not involved in the synthesis of cuticle collagens, but hydroxylates proline residues in early embryos, probably those in the egg shell collagens (Riihimaa et al., 2002).

P4Hs have also been cloned and characterized recently from the parasitic filarial nematodes Onchocerca volvulus and Brugia malayi (Merriweather et al., 2001; Winter et al., 2003). Detailed characterization of these parasite enzymes could lead to the development of specific inhibitors.

# 2.3. Drosophila melanogaster collagen P4Hs

One P4H $\alpha$ subunit from $D$ . melanogaster that has been cloned and characterized consists of 516 amino acid residues and shows $3 4 { - } 3 5 \%$ and $3 1 \%$ sequence identities to the vertebrate $\alpha$ subunits and the C. elegans PHY-1, respectively (Fig. 2) (Annunen et al., 1999). The recombinant Drosophila $\alpha$ subunit assembles into an active P4H tetramer with either human or Drosophila PDI (Fig. 1) (Annunen et al., 1999). The Drosophila P4H $\alpha$ subunit mRNA was found to be expressed in larvae, but not in embryos or adults (Annunen et al., 1999), although later mRNA expression has been detected in embryonic mouth-part precursors (Abrams and Andrew, 2002).

Altogether 19 P4H $\alpha$ subunit-related genes have been identified in the $D$ . melanogaster genome by sequence homology comparisons (Abrams and Andrew, 2002). The lengths of the encoded $\alpha$ subunit-like polypeptides range from 481 to 550 amino acids (Abrams and Andrew, 2002), and eight of them have been cloned, including the previously characterized one, six being shown to have tissue-specific embryonic expression: two in the salivary gland, the previously identified one in mouth-part precursors, and two others in the proventriculus and epidermis, respectively (Abrams and Andrew, 2002). One of the genes has a broader expression pattern, its mRNA being found in the anterior and posterior midgut primordia, the fat body, the hemocytes and the epidermis (Abrams and Andrew, 2002). The large number of P4H $\alpha$ subunit-like genes in Drosophila is highly unexpected, as its genome contains only three genes coding for two types of collagen, types IV and XVIII (Hynes and Zhao, 2000; Ackley et al., 2001). Therefore, it may be possible that some of the Drosophila P4Hs hydroxylate proline residues in proteins other than collagens.

# 2.4. Plant and viral P4Hs

Although plants have no collagens, 4-hydroxyproline is found in many plant glycoproteins (Kivirikko et al., 1992). Unlike the animal P4Hs, partially purified P4Hs from unicellular and multicellular green algae have been shown to be $6 0 ~ \mathrm { k D a }$ monomers (Kaska et al., 1988), and the higher plant P4Hs are also likely to be monomers (Fig. 1) (Kivirikko et al., 1992; Wojtaszek et al., 1999). Sequence homology searches have indicated that the Arabidopsis thaliana genome contains six genes encoding P4H $\alpha$ subunit-like short polypeptides of 280–332 residues that show $2 1 - 2 7 \%$ amino acid sequence identity to the catalytic C-terminal regions of the human P4H $\alpha$ subunits (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002). One of these cDNAs, encoding a 283-residue soluble monomer (Figs. 1 and 2), At-P4H-1, has been cloned and char-

acterized and shown to have many unexpected properties (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002).

Partly purified plant P4Hs have been shown to effectively hydroxylate poly(L-proline) (Kivirikko et al., 1992), whereas free proline, $\left( \mathrm { P r o } \right) _ { 2 }$ , and $\left( \mathrm { P r o } \right) _ { 3 }$ are not hydroxylated by plant P4Hs, suggesting that a poly(Lproline) type II helix conformation may be required in the substrate for hydroxylation (Kivirikko et al., 1992). None of the animal P4Hs use poly(L-proline) as a substrate, but it is an effective competitive inhibitor for some of them (Fig. 1, Table 1) (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). The recombinant At-P4H-1 was shown to effectively hydroxylate poly(L-proline), the $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values for poly(L-proline) $M _ { \mathrm { r } }$ 5000 and 20 000 being 2 and $0 . 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively (Table 1) (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002). Plant P4Hs have previously been reported either not to hydroxylate a collagen-like peptide $\left( \mathrm { P r o - P r o - G l y } \right) _ { 1 0 }$ or protocollagen at all or to do so only at a very low rate (Kivirikko et al., 1992), but surprisingly, the recombinant At-P4H-1 effectively hydroxylated $\left( \mathrm { P r o - P r o - G l y } \right) _ { 1 0 }$ , with a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ similar to those reported for animal P4Hs (Table 1) and a $V _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ close to that obtained with poly(L-proline) (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002). Furthermore, sequencing of the partially hydroxylated $\left( \mathrm { P r o - P r o - G l y } \right) _ { 1 0 }$ showed that the recombinant At-P4H-1 preferentially hydroxylates the proline residues preceding glycine, with an asymmetrical hydroxylation pattern similar to that produced by vertebrate P4Hs, i.e. the 9th -Pro-Pro-Gly- triplet being most efficiently hydroxylated (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002). As peptides containing only one proline residue were also shown to serve as substrates, the At-P4H-1 clearly does not require a poly(L-proline) type II helix conformation for hydroxylation (Hieta and Myllyharju, 2002).

4-Hydroxyproline has been believed to be absent from viral and bacterial proteins, but viral and bacterial genomes are now also known to encode polypeptides with proline-rich repeats and even short collagen-like sequences (Smith et al., 1998; Eriksson et al., 1999; Xu et al., 2002). A viral P4H has been cloned from a eukaryotic algal virus, Paramecium bursaria Chlorella virus-1 (PBCV-1), the recombinant enzyme being a 210-residue soluble monomer (Figs. 1 and 2) that resembles A. thaliana P4H in that it hydroxylates both $\left( \mathrm { P r o - P r o - G l y } \right) _ { 1 0 }$ and poly(L-proline) (Table 1), and also several synthetic peptides such as (Pro-Ala-Pro-Lys) , n (Ser-Pro-Lys-Pro-Pro)5 5, and (Pro-Glu-Pro-Pro-Ala) corresponding to proline-rich repeats coded by the PBCV-1 genome (Eriksson et al., 1999). PBCV-1 P4H was found to hydroxylate prolines in both positions in the -Pro-Ala-Pro-Lys- repeats, but those preceding the alanines were hydroxylated more efficiently (Eriksson et al., 1999). It differs from the animal and A. thaliana P4Hs, however, in that no asymmetric hydroxylation pattern was observed in the (Pro-Ala-Pro-Lys)n substrate (Eriksson et al., 1999).

# 3. Catalytic properties of P4Hs

The P4Hs belong to the group of 2-oxoglutarate and non-heme-Fe(II)-dependent dioxygenases. The hydroxylation reaction catalyzed by them requires $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ , 2- oxoglutarate, $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and ascorbate and involves oxidative decarboxylation of 2-oxoglutarate (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). The 2-oxoglutarate is stoichiometrically decarboxylated during hydroxylation, with one atom of the $\mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ molecule being incorporated into the succinate and the other into the hydroxy group formed on the proline residue. Ascorbate is not consumed stoichiometrically, and P4Hs can catalyze a number of reaction cycles in its absence. However, even in the presence of saturating peptide substrate concentrations, P4Hs also catalyze uncoupled decarboxylation of 2-oxoglutarate, i.e. decarboxylation without subsequent hydroxylation of a proline residue, and ascorbate is consumed stoichiometrically and acts as an alternative oxygen acceptor in these cycles.

A catalytic site in the P4H $\alpha$ subunit is likely to comprise a set of separate locations for binding of the cosubstrates (Fig. 3) (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). The $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ is probably located in a pocket coordinated with the $\alpha$ subunit by three side-chains. Extensive site-directed mutagenesis studies have identified the residues His412, Asp414, and His483 as the $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ -binding ligands in the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ subunit (Fig. 3) (Lamberg et al., 1995; Myllyharju and Kivirikko, 1997). The 2-oxoglutarate binding site can be divided into at least two distinct subsites: subsite I is a positively charged P4H residue that ionically binds the C5 carboxyl group, while subsite II consists of two cis-positioned coordination sites of the enzyme-bound $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 \bar { + } }$ and is chelated by the C1–C2 moiety. Site-directed mutagenesis studies indicate that subsite I is formed by the Lys493 residue in the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ subunit (Fig. 3) (Myllyharju and Kivirikko, 1997). The iron and 2- oxoglutarate binding residues are conserved in all collagen P4Hs and in the plant and viral P4Hs studied (Fig. 2). His501 in the human $\alpha ( \mathrm { I } )$ subunit has been shown to be an additional critical residue at the catalytic site and is probably involved in both the binding of the C1 carboxyl group of 2-oxoglutarate and the decarboxylation of this cosubstrate (Fig. 3) (Myllyharju and Kivirikko, 1997). This residue is replaced by an arginine in the C. elegans PHY-3 polypeptide, the characterized D. melanogaster P4H $\alpha$ subunit, At-P4H-1 and PBCV-1 P4H (Fig. 2). In addition to the collagen P4Hs, the catalytically critical $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ binding residues, two histidines and an aspartate, are conserved in all 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases studied (Passoja et al., 1998; Valegard et al., 1998; Zhang et al., 2000; Bruick ˚ and McKnight, 2001; Clifton et al., 2001; Epstein et al., 2001; Elkins et al., 2002; Wilmouth et al., 2002). The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values of the animal collagen P4Hs and plant and viral P4Hs for their cosubstrates are very similar (Table 1). The chain length of the peptide substrate has a major effect on the $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ , which decreases with increasing chain length (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). These findings suggest that there

must be some distinct differences in the structures of the peptide binding sites between the collagen P4Hs.

# 4. Structural properties of P4H

Fibrotic diseases constitute a major problem in medicine, and P4Hs are regarded as particularly suitable targets for antifibrotic therapy. Detailed knowledge of the structure of the catalytic and peptide substrate binding sites of P4H would have a significant impact on the rational design of potent P4H inhibitors, but unfortunately the three-dimensional structure of P4H is currently not known. Crystal structures have nevertheless been solved for five members of the 2-oxoglutaratedependent dioxygenase family, namely deacetoxycephalosporin C synthase, anthocyanidin synthase, proline 3-hydroxylase, clavaminate synthase and taurineyaketoglutarate dioxygenase, and for a related enzyme, isopenicillin N synthase (Roach et al., 1995; Valegard ˚ et al., 1998; Zhang et al., 2000; Clifton et al., 2001; Elkins et al., 2002; Wilmouth et al., 2002). The three-dimensional structure of the multifunctional enzyme PDI that serves as the $\beta$ subunit in animal collagen P4Hs is likewise not known. PDI is a modular protein consisting of domains a, b, b9 and $a ^ { \prime }$ , plus an acidic C-terminal extension (Kivirikko and Myllyharju, 1998; Kivirikko and Pihlajaniemi, 1998). The $a$ and $a ^ { \prime }$

domains, both of which contain the catalytic site sequence -Cys-Gly-His-Cys-, show amino acid sequence similarity to thioredoxin, and have a thioredoxin fold (Kemmink et al., 1996; Dijkstra et al., 1999). The homologous $^ b$ and $b ^ { \prime }$ domains have no catalytic site sequences and show no sequence similarity to thioredoxin, but NMR studies suggest that the $^ b$ domain likewise has a thioredoxin fold (Kemmink et al., 1997). The PDI polypeptide is thus likely to consist of two catalytically active thioredoxin modules and two inactive ones (Kemmink et al., 1997). The acidic C-terminal domain of PDI is not required for P4H tetramer assembly (Koivunen et al., 1999), the minimum sequence requirement for assembly being fulfilled by a PDI domain pair b9a9 (Pirneskoski et al., 2001).

# 5. HIF P4Hs

It has recently been shown that the hypoxia-inducible transcription factor HIFa is regulated by prolyl 4- hydroxylation (Ivan et al., 2001; Jaakkola et al., 2001). This transcription factor is synthesized continuously, and a critical proline residue in a -Leu-X-X-Leu-Ala-Prosequence is hydroxylated under normoxic conditions (Ivan et al., 2001; Jaakkola et al., 2001) (Fig. 4). This proline is not hydroxylated by collagen P4Hs (Jaakkola et al., 2001), but instead by a novel cytoplasmic HIF P4H family (Bruick and McKnight, 2001; Epstein et al., 2001) (Fig. 4). The human HIF P4Hs consist of 239–426 amino acid residues, while the C. elegans HIF P4H, EGL-9, consists of 723 amino acid residues (Bruick and McKnight, 2001; Epstein et al., 2001). The human HIF P4Hs show a $4 2 - 5 9 \%$ sequence identity to each other but no distinct sequence similarity to the collagen P4Hs. The catalytically critical residues are conserved in HIF P4Hs, but the arginine that binds the C5 carboxyl group of 2-oxoglutarate is located in position $+ 9$ instead of position $+ 1 0$ with respect to the second iron-binding histidine (Bruick and McKnight, 2001; Epstein et al., 2001).
