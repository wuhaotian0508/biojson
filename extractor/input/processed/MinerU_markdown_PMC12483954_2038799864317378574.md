# Summary

Cold stress significantly affects the growth and productivity of tomatoes. Despite the known involvement of jasmonate (JA) in cold stress responses, the underlying mechanism remains to be elucidated. Here, we observed that JA peaked $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ after cold treatment. The expression of the SlLOXD gene, a key player in JA biosynthesis, also peaked at $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of cold exposure, and mutation in $S I L O X D$ reduced JA content and cold tolerance. Downstream of JA signalling, the transcription factor SlMYC2 was implicated in enhancing cold resistance by directly binding to the SlCBF1/2 promoters. Furthermore, the SlMYC2-silenced plants and mutants exhibited increased sensitivity to cold damage. Additionally, SlMYB15 directly bound to the $S I L O X D$ and SlMYC2 promoters. Within 6 h of cold stress, SlMYB15 activated $S I L O X D$ expression while repressing SlMYC2 expression. Between 6 and $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ , the expression level of SlMYB15 decreased, thereby alleviating the repression of SlMYC2 expression. SlMYC2 further enhanced JA signalling through the transcriptional activation of $S I L O X D$ , thus improving cold tolerance in tomato plants. These findings provide valuable insights into the dynamic regulation of the SlLOXD–SlMYC2 –CBF1/2 module by SlMYB15 and its critical role in tomato cold stress responses. 

# Introduction

Tomato, a key crop in global food production, is frequently exposed to various biotic and abiotic stresses (Kim et al., 2024). Among the abiotic challenges, cold stress stands out for its detrimental effects on plant growth, development and survival. Cold stress can disrupt cellular homeostasis, leading to reduced photosynthesis, membrane damage and ultimately, lower crop yields (Guo et al., 2018). As global climate patterns continue to change, understanding plant responses to cold stress has become increasingly important for ensuring agricultural sustainability and food security. 

Plants respond to cold stress through complex mechanisms involving multiple pathways and signalling molecules. A key component in this response is the C-repeat binding factors (CBFs), also known as dehydration response element binding factors (DREBs). These transcription factors play a crucial role in activating the transcription of cold-responsive (COR) genes (Chinnusamy et al., 2007), making the CBF pathway central to the cold stress responses. In addition to the CBF pathway, several other signalling processes contribute to cold stress tolerance, including calcium signalling (Li et al., 2019), hormone signalling (Wang et al., 2016, 2023), reactive oxygen species (Diao et al., 2017) and mitogen-activated protein kinases (Lv et al., 2018). These pathways help plants perceive stress signals and activate appropriate defence mechanisms. Among plant hormones, abscisic acid (ABA) is important in regulating cold stress responses 

by rapidly accumulating upon cold stress exposure, enabling plants to mitigate the damage caused by cold stress. 

The rapid accumulation of ABA under cold stress mainly relies on SlMYB15, which directly regulates the ABA biosynthesis genes SlNCED1 and SlABF4 (Zhang et al., 2022), and activates the expression of SlCBF1/2/3 genes in the CBF pathway (Zhang et al., 2020). Therefore, SlMYB15 is identified as a key regulator in the initial response of tomato plants to cold stress (Zhang et al., 2020, 2022). However, prolonged high levels of ABA signalling can negatively impact tomato growth. It has been demonstrated that a microRNA (miRNA), sly-miR156e-3p, inhibits the overaccumulation of ABA at low temperatures by repressing the transcription of SlMYB15. After 6 h of treatment at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , the expression of sly-miR156e-3p begins to increase, resulting in a reduction in SlMYB15 transcripts and a subsequent decrease in ABA content (Zhang et al., 2022). Despite this decrease in ABA, tomato plants do not typically exhibit increased sensitivity after this time point. Research suggests that other hormones, such as jasmonic acid (JA), show sustained increases following the initial ABA response, contributing to continued cold stress tolerance in tomatoes (Wang et al., 2016). 

JA and its derivatives, collectively known as jasmonates, are a group of versatile plant hormones that are involved in regulating various stress responses and developmental processes. Initially recognized for their roles in defence against herbivores and pathogens, jasmonates have since been implicated in various abiotic stress responses, including cold stress. The JA biosynthesis 

pathway begins with the production of the C18 fatty acid a-linolenic acid, which undergoes a series of sequential reactions catalysed by enzymes, including lipoxygenase (LOX), allene oxide synthase (AOS), allene oxide cyclase (AOC) and 12-oxo-phytoene reductase (OPR). A family of proteins known as JASMONATE-ZIM structural domain (JAZ) proteins was first identified in Arabidopsis thaliana (Chini et al., 2009) as key regulators of the JA signalling pathway. These proteins are essential for hormone accumulation and perception, and their decreased levels relieve repression of MYC2 activity, thereby activating the JA response (Min et al., 2021; Turner et al., 2002). 

In the context of jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis and signalling, several genes have been identified as potential candidates for enhancing cold stress tolerance in tomatoes, with particular focus on SlLOXD and SlMYC2 (Upadhyay et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2013). SlLOXD (also known as TomloxD) is a chloroplast-localized lipoxygenase involved in JA biosynthesis (Hu et al., 2013). Overexpression of SlLOXD has been shown to increase JA production in tomato plants (Yan et al., 2013). The spr8 (mediated response inhibitor 8) mutant, characterized by a point mutation in the catalytic domain of SlLOXD, exhibits multiple JA-dependent immunodeficiencies (Yan et al., 2013). In addition, $S I L O X D$ is implicated in the regulation of tomato responses to heat and cold stresses, with its overexpression boosting heat tolerance, and its expression significantly up-regulates in leaves following low-temperature treatment (Upadhyay et al., 2019). MYC2 is a well-studied transcription factor known to be involved in JA signalling (Chini et al., 2009). It is versatile in regulating plant developmental and physiological processes, coordinating hormonal and environmental signalling and modulating defence responses. Its involvement in cold stress responses has been validated in a variety of plant species (Ming et al., 2021). For example, PtrMYC2 integrates JA signalling to directly regulate cold-induced glycine betaine accumulation by adjusting the expression of the betaine aldehyde dehydrogenase (BADH )-like gene, thereby enhancing low-temperature refractoriness in clover (Ming et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2013). Similarly, MaMYC2 is reported to improve the cold-refractory capacity in clover through physically interacting with MaICE1. There is evidence suggesting that MYC2 transcription factors may participate in MeJA-induced cold tolerance in banana fruit through functional coordination (Zhao et al., 2013). Despite these insights, the precise mechanism by which JA signalling enhances tomato tolerance to cold stress, particularly through the SlLOXD-JA-SlMYC2 module, remains unclear. 

Although SlMYB15’s expression is suppressed by microRNA after 6 h of cold exposure. Concurrently, the ABA content also decreases, which, however, does not affect tomato stress tolerance. This observation leads us to hypothesize that JA, which continued to rise after $6 \textrm { h }$ of cold exposure, may act as a subsequent signal for tomato plants to cope with low-temperature stress. In this study, we explored the molecular mechanisms of the SlLOXD-JA-SlMYC2 module in response to cold stress. We propose that SlMYB15 activates SlLOXD transcription within the first 6 h of low-temperature exposure. After $6 \ \mathsf { h } _ { \iota }$ when SlMYB15 is inhibited by microRNA, SlMYC2 takes over to sustain the activation of SlLOXD (Yan et al., 2013), thereby maintaining JA signalling and enhancing tomato tolerance to cold stress. This study delves into the regulatory mechanisms affected by hormonal signalling in response to cold stress in tomato plants, with potential applications for improving crop resilience to cold stress. 

# Results

# ABA and JA in tomato leaves increase under cold stress

ABA and JA are crucial endogenous hormones that play a significant role in plant responses to low-temperature stress. Previous studies have established their involvement in the signalling pathways that mediate cold tolerance in tomato plants (Postiglione and Muday, 2020; Wang et al., 2023). In this study, the contents of ABA and JA significantly increased under cold stress. However, their peak levels occurred at different time points during cold stress (Figure 1). Specifically, the ABA level in tomato leaves continuously increased over $1 6 8 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of exposure to $4 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ , reaching its highest level at $6 \textrm { h }$ with a 4.28-fold increase above the baseline (Figure 1a). In contrast, the JA level peaked at $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ treatment, showing a 3.47-fold increase (Figure 1b). 

# SlLOXD regulates cold tolerance in tomato plants

SlLOXD is a chloroplast-localized lipoxygenase involved in JA biosynthesis (Yan et al., 2013). JA accumulation has been positively correlated with the expression of $S I L O X D$ . To further investigate the role of SlLOXD and JA in tomato cold tolerance, we examined the expression patterns of $S I L O X D$ via RT-qPCR in different tomato tissues under cold treatment. Our results revealed that $S I L O X D$ expression was significantly higher in leaves, flowers and fruits compared to roots and stems (Figure 2a; Figure S1a). Additionally, the relative expression of SlLOXD in leaf tissue peaked after $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figure 2b), a finding supported by GUS staining of leaves from SlLOXDpro:: GUS stably transformed tomato plants (Figure S1b). Moreover, the $S I L O X D .$ -overexpressing lines, LOXDOE-14 and LOXDOE-16 generated in our previous work (Li et al., 2024), showed better growth after 7 days at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . These lines displayed lower relative electrolyte leakage and a higher Fv/Fm ratio compared to wild-type tomatoes, while opposite trends were observed in the loxd mutant lines (loxd-6 and loxd-7) (Figure 2c). 

Transcription factor SlMYC2 is widely recognized as being pivotal in transmitting the JA signal (Yan et al., 2013) and regulating SlLOXD expression at the transcriptional level (Figure 2d). JA content and SlMYC2 expression were significantly up-regulated in the leaves of SlLOXD-overexpression lines compared to wild-type plants at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ , whereas opposite changes were detected in the loxd mutants (Figure 2e,f). Furthermore, we observed a positive correlation between the expression levels of SlCBF1/2 in transgenic lines and those of SlLOXD and SlMYC2. The highest expression levels of SlCBF1/2 were found in the SlLOXD-overexpression lines, and the lowest levels were detected in the loxd mutants after $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figure 2f). These findings indicate that the SlLOXD-JA-SlMYC2 system plays a crucial role in regulating tomato cold stress tolerance.

# SlMYB15 directly binds to the SlLOXD promoter to activate SlLOXD expression and increase JA content

The synthesis of ABA and JA in response to cold stress is regulated by low-temperature-inducible transcription factors. It has been previously shown that the expression levels of SlABF4 and SlNCED1 are up-regulated in SlMYB15-overexpression lines compared to the wild type, thereby increasing ABA content in tomato leaves and enhancing low-temperature tolerance (Zhang et al., 2022). To determine whether the increase in JA content in tomato leaves under cold stress was related to SlMYB15, we examined the JA levels and the relative expression of JA synthesis pathway genes (SlLOXD, SlAOS2, SlAOC and SlPOR3) in SlMYB15-overexpression lines (MYB15OE-18) from Zhang et al. (2022) after $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of cold treatment at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figure 4a,b; Figure S4). Our results showed that JA levels were significantly higher in MYB15OE-18 lines. Additionally, the expression levels of SlLOXD, SlAOS2 and SlAOC were elevated by 5.63-fold, 1.48-fold and 2.54-fold, respectively, compared to the wild type after $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of cold exposure at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figure 4a,b; Figure S4). 

Among the JA biosynthesis genes, SlLOXD expression was most affected by the increase in SlMYB15 expression level. To determine whether SlMYB15 binds to the promoter of SlLOXD in vivo, SlMYB15-HA protein from transgenic lines overexpressing SlMYB15 was immunoprecipitated using an HA antibody for ChIP-qPCR analysis. The MYB15-HA protein incubated with HA significantly enriched the TAACAA-containing region of the SlLOXD promoter compared to the wild type (WT), while negative controls incubated with IgG antibody did not show any significant difference (Figure 4c). These results suggest that SlMYB15 effectively binds to the SlLOXD promoter in vivo. The transcriptional activity of SlMYB15 on the SlLOXD promoter was examined using a dual-luciferase assay. Tobacco leaves co-expressing the transcription factor SlMYB15 and the SlLOXD promoter-driven reporter gene showed significantly higher luciferase (LUC) expression levels and LUC/Renilla (REN) ratios compared to the control (Figure 4d). The binding interaction between SlMYB15 and SlLOXD promoters was further assessed by EMSA and Y1H assays. The EMSA results showed that His-SlMYB15 bound to a biotin-labelled probe of the SlLOXD promoter, causing a mobility shift. However, the biotin-labelled mutated probe (TGTGTA) failed to bind to the His-SlMYB15 protein. The addition of an unlabelled competitor probe reduced SlMYB15 binding (Figure 4e). In the Y1H assay, the growth of yeast transformed with pAbAi-SlLOXDpro construct alone was inhibited on selective medium (SD-Ura-Leu with $1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Aureobasidin A (AbA)); however, yeast cells co-transformed with pGADT7-SlMYB15 and pAbAi-SlLOXDpro grew normally (Figure 4f). The results collectively suggest that SlMYB15 directly interacts with the SlLOXD promoter, both in vivo and in vitro, leading to activated SlLOXD expression and increased JA content under cold stress in tomato plants.

# Discussion

ABA and JA hormonal signalling pathways differ in response rates in tomato plants under cold stress 

Phytohormones play an important role in plant tolerance to extreme environmental conditions. It has been well documented that both ABA and JA contents are elevated under cold stress, acting as signals to activate the synthesis of cold-resistant compounds in plants (Tian et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). However, our observations indicate that ABA and JA respond to 

cold stress at different rates. Specifically, in tomato leaves treated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , ABA levels peaked at $6 \textrm { h }$ , whereas JA reached the highest level at $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ (Figure 1). This pattern remained consistent under cold stress combined with varying light conditions (Wang et al., 2016). Similar results were observed in cold-sensitive varieties of Zanthoxylum bungeanum Maxim (Tian et al., 2022).

MYC2 can interact with ICE1 (inducer of CBF expression 1) (Zhao et al., 2013) and directly regulate the expression levels of CBFs (Figure 3). In banana, it is suggested that MaMYC2 physically and functionally interacts with MaICE1 to regulate MeJA-induced cold tolerance (Zhao et al., 2013). Our study extends this understanding by demonstrating that SlMYC2 activates the promoters of SlCBF1/2, thereby enhancing cold stress tolerance in tomato leaves (Figure 3). The transcription factor MYC2 interacts with JAZ proteins, which inhibit MYC2 activity by preventing its transcriptional functions. However, elevated jasmonic acid (JA) levels suppress JAZ protein activity, thereby releasing MYC2 and allowing it to activate downstream signalling pathways (Chini et al., 2009). During cold stress, JAZ proteins play an antagonistic role relative to MYC2. Studies have demonstrated that the overexpression of JAZ1 or JAZ4 reduces cold tolerance in Arabidopsis. These JAZ proteins negatively regulate cold stress responses by inhibiting the transcriptional activity of ICE1 and down-regulating its expression (Hu et al., 2013). A similar mechanism has been reported in apple, where JAZ proteins suppress cold tolerance by repressing CBF pathway genes (An et al., 2021). The interaction between JAZ and MYC2 forms a complex regulatory network with the ICE-CBF pathway, establishing a functional link between JA signalling and the CBF pathway in plant cold stress responses. 

Lipoxygenase (LOX) is a key enzyme in JA biosynthesis, catalysing the oxidation of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic acid (18:3) to produce 13-hydroperoxyoctadecatrienoic acid (HPOT), which is subsequently converted into JA via the allene oxide synthase (AOS) pathway (Viswanath et al., 2020). Recent studies have highlighted the involvement of LOX in abiotic stress responses in plants. In tomato plants, SlLOXD, a member of the 13-LOXs family, has been shown to respond to temperature fluctuations, with its expression significantly increasing under both cold and heat stresses (Hu et al., 2013; Upadhyay et al., 2019). Compared to wild-type plants, SlLOXD-overexpressing lines exhibit significantly elevated lipoxygenase activity, increased endogenous JA contents and enhanced expression of heat defence genes such as LeHSP90, LePR1, LePR6 and LeZAT. These transgenic tomatoes also demonstrate enhanced tolerance to high temperature (Hu et al., 2013). The discovery of a heat shock response element (AAAAAATTTC) within the promoter of SlLOXD ( 1586 bp) might explain its response to elevated temperature. Interestingly, SlLOXD expression also increases in response to cold stress (Upadhyay et al., 2019). Our experiments further indicate that slloxd mutants are more sensitive to cold stress, while overexpression alleles demonstrate enhanced cold tolerance compared to wild-type plants (Figure 2). Despite the absence of low-temperature response elements in the promoter of SlLOXD (Upadhyay et al., 2019), several MYB-binding sites were identified in this study (Table S1). 

MYB transcription factors are well-known regulators of plant responses to cold stress. For instance, OsMYB2 enhances cold tolerance in rice (Yang et al., 2012); while AtMYB96 integrates ABA and CBF-COR pathways to boost cold tolerance (Lee and Seo, 2015), whereas AtMYB15 represses CBF1/DREB1 expression to negatively regulate freezing tolerance in Arabidopsis (Agarwal et al., 2006); however, in tomato, SlMYB15 is reported to enhance cold tolerance (Zhang et al., 2020, 2022). This occurs because AtMYB15 interacts with AtICE proteins and represses the transcriptional activation of AtCBF1 by AtICE, thereby reducing 

cold stress tolerance in Arabidopsis. Conversely, SlMYB15 directly activates SlCBFs at the transcriptional level to improve cold stress tolerance in tomato. We indicate that the transcriptional activity of SlLOXD is influenced by SlMYB15 in response to cold stress. In Y1H, Luciferase and EMSA experiments, SlMYB15 was identified as a direct binding factor to the SlLOXD promoter, activating SlLOXD transcription (Figure 4c–f). Furthermore, SlMYB15- overexpressing lines display increased SlLOXD expression and higher JA levels compared to wild-type plants (Figure 4a,b). The results demonstrated that SlMYB15 positively influences the expression of SlLOXD. This, in turn, activates the SlLOXD-JA-SlMYC2-SlCBF1/2 module, thereby enhancing plant cold stress tolerance (Figures 3 and 4). 

# SlMYB15 activates multiple signalling pathways in response to low-temperature stress

Our study highlights the role of SlMYB15 in enhancing cold tolerance in tomato plants by increasing JA content. Additionally, SlMYB15 also appears to influence two additional pathways: the CBF pathway and the ABA pathway (Zhang et al., 2020, 2022). The SlHY5-SlMYB15 directly binds to the promoters of SlCBF1/2/3 to activate their expression, enhancing cold stress tolerance in tomato seedlings (Zhang et al., 2020). SlMYB15 has been reported to improve cold tolerance in tomatoes through transcriptionally activating the promoters of SlNCED1 and SlABF4, both of which are key players in ABA biosynthesis and signalling (Zhang et al., 2022). Interestingly, although ABA levels begin to decrease after 6 h of cold treatment, tomato seedlings do not exhibit a significant decrease in cold tolerance beyond this period (Zhang et al., 2022). This indicates that alternative signalling pathways may take over to maintain tolerance in the later cold stress stage in tomato plants. Our findings support this notion, as JA content continued to rise after 6 h of treatment at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until it reached its peak at $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ (Figure 1b). This persistence in JA signalling likely contributes to sustained cold stress tolerance in tomatoes. Notably, all these pathways are influenced by SlMYB15, underscoring its central role in coordinating the plant’s response to cold stress. 

# SlMYB15 expression is repressed by the SlMYC2-SlLOXD module in response to low-temperature stress

The transcription factor SlMYB15 is a key regulator of the SlCBFs, ABA and JA pathways in tomatoes. Our analysis shows that the peak expression of SlMYB15 coincides with the maximum accumulation of ABA but differs from that of JA (Figure 1; Figure S5). During the first 6 h of exposure to $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , the expression levels of SlMYB15, along with the contents of ABA and JA, continued to increase (Figure 1; Figure S5). Interestingly, after this 6-h window, the ABA content and SlMYB15 expression decreased, likely due to inhibition by the microRNA sly-miR156e-3p. Zhang et al. Importantly, the expression of sly-miR156e-3p declined at the onset of cold stress, leading to reduced transcriptional repression of SlMYB15 by sly-miR156e-3p. After $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of cold stress, the expression of SlMYB15 is repressed by sly-miR156e- $3 p$ , which in turn increases the expression of SlMYC2. Subsequently, SlMYC2 enhances the activity of the $S I L O X D$ promoter and promotes the expression of cold-responsive genes SlCBF1 and SlCBF2. These findings provide valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying tomato plant responses to cold stress, highlighting a dynamic regulation of key transcription factors and their roles in enhancing cold tolerance. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The SlLOXD-overexpression lines and loxd mutants were derived from our previous research (Li et al., 2024). The SlMYB15- overexpressing plants and myb15 mutants were kindly provided by Professor Zhou from Zhejiang University, Hangzhou, China (Zhang et al., 2020). 

Seeds of wild-type tomato Solanum lycopersicum ‘Ailsa Craig’ (AC) and the transgenic lines were germinated and then transferred to 72-well plates containing growth substrates. The seedlings were then grown in a light incubator (LRX-1400D-LED, PRANDT, Ningbo, China) under the following controlled growth conditions: $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ during the day and $1 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ during the night, with a 12 h light/12 h dark cycle, a photosynthetic photon flux 

density of $3 5 0 \ \mathrm { \textmu m o l } / ( \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } { \cdot } 5 )$ , and $70 \%$ humidity. Once the third true leaf was fully expanded, the seedlings were transplanted into pots filled with substrates (cow dung: mushroom scraps: perlite $= 3 { : } 3 { : } 4 ,$ ), and the growth conditions were unchanged and maintained consistently (Li et al., 2022). 

# Low-temperature treatment and plant phenotyping

When the fifth true leaf of tomato seedlings was fully expanded, the seedlings were subjected to continuous low-temperature treatment at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 7 full days. All other environmental growth conditions remained unchanged throughout the treatment period. The treated tomato seedlings were positioned in front of a black cloth, and the plant phenotypes were documented using a camera under the identical light source. 

# Cold tolerance assays

0.1 g of tomato leaves was placed into 5 mL of deionized water to measure the relative electrolyte leakage (REL). The initial conductivity of the deionized water was measured as C0 before the leaf was put into the deionized water. After shaking the deionized water with the samples at room temperature for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ the conductivity was measured as C1. Then, heat was applied in boiling water for 15 min, and the conductivity was measured as C2 after it completely cooled down to room temperature. The value of REL was obtained as C1-C0/C2-C0. Plants were darkened for 30 min before the maximum photochemical quantum yield of PSII (Fv/Fm) was measured. Values were then measured using HEXAGON-IMAGING-PAM (Walz, Efficient, Germany) and fluorescence imaging was performed. The specific methods for measuring Fv/Fm and REL were referred to Wang et al. (2022). 

# Separation and analysis of hormones

Hormones were extracted and quantified following the method of Muller and Munn€ -e-Bosch (2011). Leaf samples were first ground in liquid nitrogen, and approximately 0.1 g of the ground tissue was weighed and mixed with 1 mL of extraction solution consisting of isopropanol, water and hydrochloric acid in a ratio of 2: 1: 0.002 (v: v: v). After thorough vortexing, the samples were incubated overnight at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Then, the supernatant was obtained after centrifugation at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min and mixed with 1 mL of dichloromethane. This mixture was shaken at $1 5 0 ~ g$ for $1 \ \mathsf { h } _ { \iota }$ , followed by another round of centrifugation at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min. The supernatant was then evaporated to dryness under nitrogen gas, and the residue was dissolved in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of chromatography-grade methanol. Throughout the process, the samples were kept at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark. Hormone concentrations were determined using HPLC-MS/MS (ABSCIEXTripleTOF5600+, MA, USA). Chromatographic conditions: Shimadzu InertSustainTM AQ-C18 column $( 1 5 0 \times 4 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , packed with $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } } )$ , flow rate of 0.3 mL/min, column temperature box $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Mobile phase A: $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid; mobile phase B: methanol. Mass spectrometry conditions: electrospray ionization source (ESI), positive ion mode, ionization voltage $5 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k V } ,$ , heater gas temperature of the ion source TurboIonSpray probe was $6 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , spray gas (GS1) and auxiliary heater gas (GS2) were 60 psi, and the gas curtain gas was 35 psi, respectively. 

# RNA isolation and qPCR

The leaves were ground using a high-throughput tissue grinder (SCIENTZ Ningbo, China). Total plant RNA was extracted utilizing the PLANTpure Plant RNA Kit (Aidlab Biotechnologies, Beijing, 

China). cDNA was synthesized from the total RNA using the highspeed-strand cDNA synthesis plus kit (Cofitt, Hong Kong, China). Real-time quantitative analysis (qPCR) was performed using Taq SYBR Green qPCR Premix (BestEnzymes, JiangSu, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The actin gene (Solyc03g078400) and the ubiquitin gene (Solyc01g068045) were used as reference genes for normalization (Sang et al., 2017). qPCR reactions were conducted on a LightCycler 96 real-time PCR system manufactured by Roche in Germany (Li et al., 2024). The fluorescent dye SYBR Green 1, included in the TransStart- Tip Green qPCR SuperMix (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China) was used for detection. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C t }$ method. To ensure data reliability, all qRT-PCR analyses were performed in triplicates. All primers used for these analyses are listed in Table S2. 

# GUS staining and enzyme activity detection

Primers for cloning of the SlLOXD promoter sequence were designed based on the data from Phytozome 13.0 (Solanum lycopersicum ITAG5.0; https://phytozome-next.jgi.doe. gov/info/Slycopersicum_ITAG5_0). A 2000 bp upstream of the ATG start codon was amplified using high-fidelity DNA polymerase (Accurate Biology, Hunan, China) and inserted into the pGWB433 vector using the Gateway cloning method (Goshima et al., 2008). The resulting pGWB433-SlLOXDpro::GUS was introduced into the AC tomato plants via A. tumefaciensmediated transformation, generating genetically stable transgenic lines. Tissues of these transgenic tomato plants under designated $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ treatment were sampled and immediately histochemically stained using a GUS staining kit (Coolaber, Beijing, China) and photographed (He et al., 2020). GUS activity was carried out using GUS gene quantitative detection Kit (Coolaber, Beijing, China) as described by the manufacturer. The reaction solution was prepared by using $0 . 1 \ 9$ of tomato leaves treated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ following the supplier’s instructions. Subsequently, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of the reaction solution was sampled at 10, 20 and 30 min and combined with $9 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of termination solution. The fluorescence spectrophotometer was employed to quantify GUS activity by measuring the fluorescence yield of total protein over time. Primer sequences for vector construction are listed in Table S3. 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assay

ChIP experiments were carried out by taking 1 g of SlMYB15 overexpression plants and wild-type tomato leaves according to the instructions of ChIP Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). The leaves of the plants were cross-linked by vacuuming with fixative containing $1 \%$ formaldehyde $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Tris–HCl, pH 8.0, 0.44 M sucrose, 5 mM $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol), and chromatin immunoprecipitation of chromatin was carried out using a monoclonal antibody against HA (Pierce, 26 183), and the negative control was performed using goat anti-mouse IgG (Millipore, AP124P). Immunoprecipitated DNA and input DNA values were analysed using real-time fluorescent quantitative PCR (Light Cycler, Roche). All ChIP-qPCR experiments were randomly designed with 3 replicates. Specific primers were designed according to the promoter sequences of the SlLOXD and SlMYC2 genes (Table S3) for ChIP-qPCR analysis. 

# Tomato mutants

We utilized the CRISPR-P tool (http://cbi.hzau.edu.cn/crispr/) to select appropriate sgRNAs, and two were chosen for each gene. These selected sgRNAs were cloned into the CRISPR/Cas9 vector 

pCS4106 (http://www.wimibio.com) carrying the aminoglycoside adenyl transferase gene (aadA) for selection, generating pCS4106-slmyc2 knockout vectors. The knockout vector (pCS4106-slmyc2) was transformed into AC tomatoes using Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101-mediated stable transformation methods (Li et al., 2024). Knockout mutants were checked for hygromycin resistance $( 6 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ ). Primer sequences used to characterize transgenic lines are listed in Table S3. 

# Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS)

The expression of SlMYB15 and SlMYC2 was silenced using the VIGS technique using tobacco rattle virus (TRV). Gene fragments for silencing were designed using the SGN VIGS Tool (https://vigs. solgenomics.net/). The pTRV2 plasmid (negative control), pTRV2-PDS, pTRV2-SlMYB15 and pTRV2-SlMYC2 recombinant plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101, respectively. Transfection of pGWB433-SlLOXDpro:: GUS lines and WT tomato cotyledons, as well as assessment of silencing efficiency, were carried out according to the method of Wang et al. (2016). The primers used for amplification are listed in Table S3. 

# Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay

The promoters of SlLOXD and SlMYC2 were cloned and inserted into the pAbAi plasmid (Li et al., 2024), resulting in recombinant pAbAi-SlLOXDpro and pAbAi-SlMYC2pro plasmids to serve as baits. These bait plasmids were linearized using the restriction endonuclease BbSI (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) and then transformed into Y1H cells, respectively. The CDS sequence of SlMYB15 (excluding the stop codon) was cloned and inserted into the pGADT7 plasmid to serve as prey. Y1H cells containing each bait were transfected with either the pGADT7 plasmid (negative control) or the pGADT7-SlMYB15 recombinant plasmid. Successfully transformed yeast cells were screened on SD/-Ura/ Leu medium supplemented with $1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ of Aureobasidin A (AbA). All procedures were performed following the protocol provided by the Frozen Yeast Transformation Kit (Coolaber, Beijing, China). Primers used for cloning are listed in Table S3. 

# Dual-LUC assay

To assess transcriptional regulation, the promoters of SlCBF1, SlCBF2, SlLOXD and SlMYC2 were cloned and inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC plasmid between the KpnI and HindIII sites, serving as reporters. The CDS sequences of SlMYB15 and SlMYC2 were cloned and inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK plasmid between the BamHI and HindIII sites, serving as effectors. The transfection of tobacco leaves and Dual-LUC assay were performed as described by Du et al. (2023). Primers used for amplification are listed in Table S3. 

# Recombinant proteins and electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

His-MYB15 recombinant protein was induced following the method adopted by Zhang et al. (2022). The protein was purified using a His-tag Protein Purification Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). The MBP-MYC2 recombinant protein was similarly induced and purified as described by Wu et al. (2024). Probe labelling and EMSA experiments were carried out following the method described by Liang et al. (2023). Primers used are listed in Table S3. 

# Accession numbers

SlLOXD (SlLOX4; TomLOXD), Solyc03g122340; SlMYB15, Solyc03g005570; SlMYC2, Solyc08g076930. 

# Statistical analysis

All leaf samples were collected from the second and third leaf positions (from top to bottom) of randomly selected tomato plants. Each treatment comprised at least three biological replicates. Data were analysed for statistical significance using one-way ANOVA or Student’s t-test in IBM SPSS Statistics 26.0. Significant differences between treatments were indicated by different letters $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ ).
