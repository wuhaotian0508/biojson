# The phenol-2-monooxygenase FgPhm1 regulates DON synthesis, pathogenicity and environmental stress response in Fusarium graminearum

Zenghong Luoa,b, Xuzhao Maob,c, Minghui Pengb , Chenxuan Huangc , Jingkun Liangd , Yunsi Xiaoc , Yakubu Saddeeq Abubakarb,c, Wenhui Zhengc , Yan Xiongb , Zonghua Wanga,b, Meilian Chena , and Huawei Zheng $\oplus ^ { a }$ 

a Fujian Key Laboratory on Conservation and Sustainable Utilization of Marine Biodiversity, College of Geography and Oceanography, Minjiang University, Fuzhou, China; b Fujian Universities Key Laboratory for Plant-Microbe Interaction, College of Life Sciences, Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University, Fuzhou, China; c State Key Laboratory of Agricultural and Forestry Biosecurity, Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University, Fuzhou, China; d College of Resources and Environment, Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University, Fuzhou, China 

# ABSTRACT

Fusarium graminearum is a devastating plant pathogenic fungus that produces mycotoxins and is the predominant pathogen causing Fusarium head blight. Phenol-2-monooxygenase is an oxidoreductase that is responsible for the conversion of phenol to catechol. However, the role of phenol-2-monooxygenase in filamentous fungi remains unclear. This study showed that the deletion of the phenol-2-monooxygenase gene FgPHM1 leads to defects in vegetative growth, conidia production, sexual reproduction, and plant infection. FgPhm1 is localized to the nucleoplasm and regulates DON production. In addition, FgPhm1 is required for the formation of toxisomes and regulates the expression of TRI genes. RNA-seq analysis showed that the differentially expressed genes in the mutant were enriched in secondary metabolite biosynthesis, carbon metabolism, and pyruvate metabolism. Interestingly, the FgPHM1 deletion mutant was tolerant to phenol stress, whereas it was sensitive to catechol and oxidative stress. FgPhm1 is required for reactive oxygen species production in F. graminearum, and the expression level of FgPHM1 gene is induced by ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ . Moreover, FgPhm1 regulates autophagy in F. graminearum under nitrogen starvation or rapamycin treatment in F. graminearum. Taken together, this study demonstrated that FgPhm1 is essential for the development and production of DON and plays important roles in the pathogenicity and stress response of F. graminearum, and RNA-seq revealed that FgPhm1 was involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites in F. graminearum.

# Introduction

Oxygenases are enzymes that catalyze oxidation reactions by incorporating oxygen or oxygen-containing radicals into their substrates [1]. Oxygenases are involved in several metabolic pathways. They are classified into monooxygenases and dioxygenases. Monooxygenases facilitate the incorporation of a single hydroxyl group into their substrates, whereas dioxygenases catalyze the integration of two oxygen atoms from molecular oxygen into their substrates [1]. Oxygenases encompass various enzymes, such as cytochrome P450 monooxygenases, kynurenine-3-monooxygenase, phenol 2-monooxygenase, lipoxygenases, flavoprotein monooxygenases, methane monooxygenase, and benzoate 4-monooxygenase [2,3]. These enzymes play crucial roles in catalyzing essential cellular and metabolic processes, including the biosynthesis of trichothecene [4], glycopeptide [5], taxol [6], jadomycin [7] and meroterpenoid [8]. Notably, kynurenine monooxygenase (KMO) is an enzyme within the kynurenine 

pathway (KP), which represents the primary catabolic route for tryptophan metabolism [3]. 

Phenol-2-monooxygenase, also known as phenol hydroxylase, catalyzes the conversion of phenol to catechol, representing the initial and rate-limiting step in the phenol degradation pathway [9]. To date, two distinct types of phenol hydroxylases have been identified in bacteria: single-chain flavoproteins and multicomponent hydroxylases, both of which exhibit widespread distribution across bacterial species [9]. Phenol is first oxygenated by phenol hydroxylase to form catechol [10]. Interestingly, a previous study indicated that catechol can be formed from salicylate either through direct decarboxylation by salicylate 1-monooxygenase or in a two-step reaction involving the conversion of salicylate 2,3-dihydroxybenzoate by phenol-2-monooxygenase, followed by a non-oxidative decarboxylation step catalyzed by 2,3-dihydroxybenzoate carboxylase [2,11]. A phenol-degrading fungus, Fusarium oxysporum GJ4, 

can use phenol as the sole carbon and energy source [12]. The model filamentous fungus Neurospora crassa possesses the phenol-2-monooxygenase homologous gene NCU03023 although its function remains unknown. Generally, the role of phenol 2-monooxygenase in plant pathogenic fungi is poorly understood. 

Fusarium graminearum is one of the most prevalent and aggressive pathogens in small-grain cereal crops, causing Fusarium head blight (FHB) [13]. It was recently reported that F. graminearum spores alter the composition and metabolome of the gut microbiota in mammalian cells [14], suggesting a potential direct threat to mammals. This fungus infects the host through vulnerable openings, stomata, or lobate appressoria and infection cushions that penetrate the host surface [15]. F. graminearum produces exocellular enzymes, mycotoxins, effectors, linear non-ribosomal octapeptides, and fusarium-specific xylanases to inhibit host immunity [16–19]. Mycotoxins, particularly deoxynivalenol (DON), present a significant threat to both human and animal health [4], and it inhibits protein synthesis by binding to ribosomes [20]. Given the economic significance and toxicological impact of mycotoxins, F. graminearum has been ranked among the top ten fungal plant pathogens [21]. The control/management of the pathogen involves different approaches, including genomics, genetic engineering, use of fungicides, and biocontrol approaches [22,23]. During the past three decades, the regulatory mechanisms of F. graminearum virulence and DON biosynthesis have been extensively investigated [22,24–26]. The proteins involved in the biosynthesis of DON (trichothecene) are encoded by 15 TRI genes. Among them, Tri1 and Tri4 are cytochrome P450 monooxygenase genes [4] that catalyze the early and late steps of trichothecene biosynthesis. To date, several transcriptional regulators, including Tri6, Tri10, AreA, and StuA, have been identified to regulate DON biosynthesis in response to extracellular conditions [27–31]. Overproduction of the mycotoxin biosynthetic enzymes FgTri1 or FgTri4 triggers toxisome formation via endoplasmic reticulum remodeling in F. graminearum [32]. A recent report found that long non-coding RNA is required for the expression of TRI5 to resulting in DON biosynthesis in F. graminearum [33]. The nucleoside diphosphate kinase FgNdpk interacts with FgTri4 to mediate DON production by regulating toxisome formation [34,35]. 

In this study, the roles of phenol-2-monooxygenase FgPhm1 were investigated in F. graminearum and found to be essential for DON production by regulating the expression of trichothecene biosynthesis genes and toxisome formation. In addition, we showed that FgPhm1 was indispensable for environmental stress 

tolerance. Moreover, the FgPHM1 deletion mutant was tolerant to phenol-induced stress, but sensitive to catechol and oxidative stress factors. Furthermore, FgPhm1 has also been shown to play an important role in the pathogenicity of F. graminearum. To our knowledge, this is the first study to systematically analyze the roles of phenol-2-monooxygenase in plant pathogenic fungi. 

# Results

# Identification of phenol-2-monooxygenase genes in F. graminearum

We used a bioinformatics approach to identify phenol-2-monooxygenase genes in F. graminearum as outlined in the Materials and Methods section. The results showed that the F. graminearum genome contains six phenol 2-monooxygenase homolog genes with the gene IDs FGSG_00489, FGSG_03199, FGSG_04302, FGSG_09110, FGSG_13839, and FGSG_13983, all of which possess the conserved phenol-2-monooxygenase domain (Figure S1). To understand the biological functions of the phenol-2-monooxygenase genes in F. graminearum, a targeted gene replacement strategy was used to delete all phenol-2-monooxygenase genes in the wild-type PH-1 background. Phenotypic analysis of the gene deletion mutants showed that null mutants of FGSG_03199, FGSG_04302, FGSG_09110, FGSG_13839, and FGSG_13983 had phenotypes similar to those of PH-1 (Figure S2), whereas the mutant FGSG_00489 grew significantly less than PH-1 (Figure 1), as the gene code for phenol-2-monooxygenase 1 (hereafter referred to as FgPhm1). Phylogenetic analysis revealed that FgPhm1 exhibits evolutionary conservation across eukaryotes, with particularly high sequence conservation observed in the filamentous fungi Fusarium oxysporum, Fusarium verticillioides, Magnaporthe oryzae and N. crassa (Figure S3). From the multiple independent FgPHM1 mutants generated, we prioritized three transformants (ΔFgphm1–2, ΔFgphm1–3, and ΔFgphm1–4) for further characterization. Southern blot analysis confirmed the successful deletion of FgPHM1 in these mutants, ΔFgphm1–2 and ΔFgphm1–4 are correct single-copy transformants, with hybridization signals corresponding to a 4.9-kb fragment in the mutants compared to the 5.9-kb band observed in the wild-type strain PH-1 (Figure S4), consistent with the expected genomic rearrangement at the target locus. # FgPhm1 plays a critical role in vegetative growth

To assess the functional role of FgPhm1 in vegetative growth and colony morphogenesis of F. graminearum, we conducted a comparative growth assay using the wild-type strain PH-1, the FgPHM1 deletion mutant (ΔFgphm1), and the complemented strain (ΔFgphm1- 

C). Strains were cultured for 3 days under standardized conditions in three distinct media: complete medium (CM), starch-yeast medium (SYM), and minimal medium (MM). As shown in Figure 1(a,b), the ΔFgphm1 mutant showed significantly reduced vegetative growth in the three different media compared to the PH-1 and ΔFgphm1-C strains. Interestingly, the ΔFgphm1 mutant showed flattened aerial mycelia on these media (Figure $1 ( \mathsf { a } , \mathsf { c } ) )$ ). Next, we measured the hyphal length of PH-1 and ΔFgphm1 mutant in glass tubes containing CM agar and found that the length of the hyphae of the ΔFgphm1 mutant was significantly reduced compared 

to that of PH-1 (Figure 1(d,e)). Furthermore, we observed that when $2 . 5 \%$ bromophenol blue was placed on the surface of the PH-1 hyphae, discrete spherical droplets were formed and the shape persisted for more than 5 min; when this was placed on the mutant’s hyphae, it immediately spilled and became dispersed, suggesting that FgPhm1 is important for surface hydrophobicity of the aerial hyphae (Figure 1(f)). To further verify this, we analyzed the expression levels of the hydrophobin synthesis gene FGSG_09066 [27] and found that the expression of this gene was significantly reduced in the mutant compared to that in PH-1 (Figure 1(g)). Taken together, these results suggest that FgPhm1 is important for vegetative growth of F. graminearum. 

# FgPhm1 regulates asexual and sexual reproduction in F. graminearum

Conidia are believed to play an important role in infecting flowering wheat heads in F. graminearum [15,36]. To understand the function of FgPhm1 in conidia formation, PH-1, ΔFgphm1 and ΔFgphm1-C strains were cultured in liquid carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) media for 3 days to induce conidia production. Our results showed that conidiation of the ΔFgphm1 mutant was drastically reduced compared with that of the PH-1 and ΔFgphm1-C strains (Figure 2(a,b)). The expression level of FgPHM1 increased significantly at different time points (0, 12, and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ ) during conidiation (Figure 2(c)). Furthermore, we found that the conidia produced by the ΔFgphm1 mutant were smaller than those produced by PH-1 and complemented strains (Figure 2(a)). The average length of the conidia produced by ΔFgphm1 mutant was $1 3 . 4 3 \mu \mathrm { m }$ while the wild-type PH-1 and complemented strain ΔFgphm1-C had average conidial lengths of 33.82 and $3 3 . 4 2 \mu \mathrm { m } .$ , respectively (Figure 2(d)). Similarly, the average width of ΔFgphm1 mutant conidia was $3 . 4 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ while those of PH-1 and ΔFgphm1-C were 4.58 and $4 . 5 3 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , respectively (Figure 2(e)). In addition, the average number of septa per conidium of the ΔFgphm1 mutant was significantly lower than that of the wild-type PH-1 and ΔFgphm1-C strains (Figure 2(f)). Taken together, these results suggest that FgPhm1 plays an important role in conidiation and conidial morphology of F. graminearum. 

The ascospores produced by F. graminearum function as the primary inoculum for host infection [15]. Thus, we examined the sexual development of the PH-1, ΔFgphm1 and ΔFgphm1-C strains on carrot agar plates and found that ΔFgphm1 mutant lost the ability to produce perithecia (Figure $2 ( \mathbf { g } )$ ), suggesting that FgPhm1 is essential for fungal sexual development. Collectively, these results indicate that FgPhm1 is 

required for normal asexual and sexual development of F. graminearum. 

# FgPhm1 is required for pathogenicity to wheat heads, coleoptiles, leaves, and corn silks

Considering that $F .$ . graminearum is an important fungal pathogen in cereal crops, pathogenicity tests were performed on wheat heads, wheat coleoptiles, wheat leaves, and corn silks to determine the role of FgPhm1 in the pathogenesis of $F$ graminearum. As shown in Figure 3(a,b), the wild-type PH-1 and the complemented strains ΔFgphm1-C were able to infect almost the entire wheat head and showed typical FHB symptoms, while the ΔFgphm1 mutant did not show any symptoms and failed to infect the inoculation site. Similar results were obtained in the infection experiments on wheat coleoptiles (Figure 3(c,d)), wheat leaves (Figure 3(e,f)), and corn silks (Figure $3 ( \mathrm { g , h } ) ,$ ). To explore the underlying mechanisms of ΔFgphm1 in regulating virulence, we investigated the penetration ability of the mutants using a cellophane sheet assay. As shown in Figure 3(i), ΔFgphm1 failed to penetrate the cellophane membrane. Accordingly, transcriptome analysis showed that genes involved in host penetration, including the gene for glucosidase synthesis (FGSG_08265) and the gene encoding xylanase synthesis (FGSG_02687) [27], were significantly downregulated in the mutant as compared to those in the wildtype PH-1 (Figure 3(j)), suggesting that FgPhm1 is required for host penetration. Taken together, these results indicate that FgPhm1 plays a crucial role in F. graminearum. 

# Intracellular localization of FgPhm1-GFP in F. graminearum

To observe the subcellular localization of FgPhm1-GFP, the complemented strain ΔFgphm1-C was used to study the subcellular localization of FgPhm1 in both the conidia and mycelia. The GFP signal of FgPhm1-GFP was distributed throughout the cytoplasm in the hyphae, conidia, and conidiophores of the fungus (Figure 4). To determine whether FgPhm1 was localized in the nucleoplasm, the nucleoplasmic marker FgNcbp2-mCherry [37] was co-transformed with FgPhm1-GFP into PH-1 protoplasts. As shown in Figure 4, FgPhm1-GFP colocalized with the nucleoplasmic marker FgNcbp2-mCherry. These results indicate that FgPhm1 mainly localizes to the cytoplasm and nucleoplasm of F. graminearum.

# The FgPHM1 deletion mutant is tolerant to phenol but sensitive to catechol stress factors

Phenol-2-monooxygenase is responsible for converting phenol to catechol [9]. To investigate the relationship between FgPhm1 and phenol in F. graminearum, wildtype PH-1 was treated with phenol for $^ { 6 \mathrm { h } }$ and then RNA was extracted to analyze FgPHM1 gene expression levels. ΔFgphm1-C strains were grown in CM supplemented with different concentrations of phenol $( 0 \%$ , $0 . 0 1 \%$ , $0 . 0 1 5 \%$ , $0 . 0 2 \%$ ). Furthermore, the response of other phenol-2-monooxygenase gene deletion mutants to phenol and catechol stresses was evaluated, as shown in Figure S6, and the inhibition rates of the ΔFg03199, ΔFg04302, ΔFg09110, ΔFg13839 and ΔFg13983 mutants were not significantly different from those of the wild type.

# Discussion

Phenol-2-monooxygenase is an oxidoreductase that converts phenol into catechol [9]. Oxidordeuctases are 

extensively studied in various species, but the functions of phenol-2-monooxygenase proteins are poorly understood, especially in filamentous fungi. In this study, we systematically identified the functions of six phenol-2-monooxygenase domain-containing proteins in the wheat fungus, F. graminearum. Among these, FgPhm1 is localized in the nucleus and is crucial for vegetative growth, sexual and asexual reproduction, DON production, autophagy, and pathogenicity. Additionally, FgPhm1 is involved in ROS production and regulates various environmental stress factors. Interestingly, the FgPHM1 deletion mutant was tolerant to phenol stress but sensitive to catechol stress, suggesting that FgPhm1 is involved in the metabolism of phenol and catechol. A previous comparative study of 75 prokaryotic genomes showed horizontal gene transfer events in the evolution of the phenol-2monooxygenase gene [9]. However, phenol-2–monooxygenases have been less studied in fungi, and the evolutionary tree suggests that phenol-2–monooxygenase, FgPhm1, is conserved in filamentous fungi. As a phenol-2-monooxygenase, the expression of FgPHM1 was induced by low concentrations of phenol in F. graminearum. Moreover, the FgPHM1 deletion mutant was tolerant to phenol stress but sensitive to catechol stress, which appears contradictory to the enzyme’s detoxification role maybe due to the complexity of metabolic networks. The observed phenol tolerance in the mutant could potentially be attributed to compensatory activation of alternative degradation pathways, such as the phenol hydroxylase system [10]. Although Phm1 catalyzes the conversion of phenol to catechol, catechol may be further 

metabolized into more easily excreted compounds through downstream pathways. The FgPHM1 deletion mutant may impair downstream catechol degradation pathways, resulting in catechol accumulation and cytotoxicity. Previous studies have shown that Fusarium spp. QHM and BWC1 have been applied to phenol wastewater in acidic or neutral pH environments [45]. Phenol degradation by Fusarium oxysporum GJ4 is affected by toxic catalytic polymerization mediated by copper oxide [12]. These results suggest that different Fusarium species have conserved roles in phenol metabolism. 

The exact localization of phenol-2-monooxygenase remains largely unknown. In the present study, FgPhm1 was found to mainly localize to the nucleus in F. graminearum, especially in the nucleoplasm, suggesting that the FgPhm1-mediated metabolic process may mainly occur in the nucleoplasm. The FgPHM1 deletion mutant is more sensitive to various environmental stress factors, including the cell wall-damaging agents CFW and CR, plasma membrane-damaging agent SDS, and hyperosmotic stress-inducing agents NaCl and KCl. This indicates that 

FgPhm1 regulates the response of F. graminearum to cell wall, cell membrane, and osmotic stress-inducing agents. 

DON is a key virulence factor in F. graminearum [38]. Our genetic study showed that the FgPHM1 deletion mutant was severely defective in DON production. The reduction in DON production may be due to the downregulation of trichothecene biosynthesis genes and inability to form toxisomes. As cytochrome P450 monooxygenase is the key gene that regulates trichothecene biosynthesis [4,29], there may be a potential link between phenol-2-monooxygenase and DON production in F. graminearum. Furthermore, RNA-seq revealed that FgPhm1 was involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites in F. graminearum under TBI conditions. 

The production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) is associated with various developmental processes and host-pathogen interactions in phytopathogenic fungi [46]. Functional analysis of FgPhm1 as an oxidoreductase revealed its importance for ROS production. The ΔFgphm1 mutant decreased the ability to survive on CM media supplemented with 0.03% $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } .$ , and ROS accumulation in the mutant was significantly reduced. The ΔFgphm1 mutant had a reduced growth rate with increased sensitivity to $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } .$ , which may contribute to defects in plant infection. Peroxidases are a group of ROS-detoxifying enzymes involved in oxidative stress response and a variety of physiological processes [42]. Transcriptome sequencing showed that the expression of some oxidative stress-related genes was significantly downregulated. Therefore, it is possible that the pathogenicity defect of the ΔFgphm1 mutant was due to either the growth defect of the mutant or its failure to overcome plant defense responses. 

Macroautophagy (autophagy) is induced by nutrient starvation or rapamycin treatment [47]. The TOR signaling pathway regulates vegetative development and virulence in F. graminearum [48]. This study showed that the ΔFgphm1 mutant is sensitive to rapamycin treatment, which is consistent with the inhibition of GFP-FgAtg8 degradation in the mutant under nitrogen starvation or treatment with rapamycin. 

In phytopathogens, monooxygenases have been demonstrated to be important for virulence in hosts and mitigation of host innate immunity [49–52]. In M. oryzae, polysaccharide monooxygenase MoPmo9A is required for infection [51]. Biosynthesis of monooxygenase-derived jasmonate attenuates host innate immunity in M. oryzae [50]. Trichoderma hamatum monooxygenase gene is involved in the antagonistic activity against fungal plant pathogens [53]. In this study, the pathogenicity assay indicated that FgPhm1 plays a critical role in wheat head, leaf, coleoptile, and 

corn silk infections. The significant reduction in virulence of the ΔFgphm1 mutant may be due to the loss of penetration ability, decreased expression of penetration-related genes, DON production, and ROS generation. Taken together, this study expands our understanding of the role of phenol-2-monooxygenase FgPhm1 in F. graminearum. Because it plays an important role in DON production and pathogenicity, FgPhm1 could serve as a potential target for FHB control in F. graminearum. 

# Experimental procedures

# Strains and culture conditions

All experimental strains, including the wild-type F. graminearum PH-1 (WT) and its isogenic mutant derivatives, are comprehensively cataloged in Table S1. For comparative phenotypic analysis, strains were cultured under standardized conditions ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } } ,$ 3-day incubation) in three distinct media: minimal medium (MM), complete medium (CM), and starch-yeast medium (SYM) [54]. Sexual reproduction and conidiation were assayed as previously described [55,56]. $0 . 0 1 \%$ SDS, $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ calcofluor white (CFW), $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ Congo red (CR), $0 . 0 3 \%$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } .$ , 1.2 M KCl, 1.2 M NaCl, catechol (1, 10, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ , and phenol $( 0 . 0 1 \%$ $0 . 0 1 5 \%$ , $0 . 0 2 \%$ ) were added to CM media for sensitivity testing. 

To analyze the host penetration ability, each strain was inoculated on a cellophane membrane placed on minimal medium and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After 2 days of incubation, the cellophane membranes were removed and the plates were further incubated for another 4 days before mycelial growth on each plate was examined. All experiments were repeated thrice. 

# Fungal transformation and generation of gene deletion mutants

Protoplasts were enzymatically generated from F. graminearum mycelia using a lytic enzyme cocktail under osmotically stabilized conditions [57], in brief, the germlings collected from 16-h CM cultures were treated with Driselase to produce the protoplasts and then F. graminearum transformation was mediated by polyethylene glycol (PEG). A split-marker approach [58] was used to generate phenol-2-monooxygenase gene mutants, using the primers listed in Table S2. Transformants were screened by PCR and confirmed by Southern blotting. 

# Construction of pFgphm1-GFP vector and complementation

For the pFgPhm1-GFP fusion vector, the primers FgPHM1-CF and FgPHM1-CR were used to amplify the full-length sequence of the FgPHM1 gene, together with its native promoter, using genomic DNA from wild-type PH-1. The primers used are listed in Table S2. The construct was then cloned into the pKNTG2 vector using a One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme Biotech Co. Ltd., Nanjing, China), and the chimeric DNA was verified by sequencing. The pFgPhm1-GFP plasmid was transformed into protoplasts of the ΔFgphm1 mutant for complementation. 

# Plant infection and DON production assays

Infection assays of PH-1 and mutants on flowering wheat heads, leaves, coleoptiles of Jimai 22 (developed by Zhendong Zhao and his group in China) were conducted as previously described [54,55], mycelial plugs were inoculated on the flowering wheat heads or wheat leaves, and observed symptoms 14 days or 7 days after inoculation, respectively. Fresh corn silks of maize B73 (developed by Gang Li and his group in China) were collected from young corn ears and sliced into 5-cm fragments, then exposed portions of corn silks were discarded and only the portions originating from inside husks were used for infection assays and observed symptoms 7 days after inoculation [59]. For DON production assays, all the strains were grown in liquid trichothecene biosynthesis induction (TBI) media at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days in the dark; the liquid was used for DON production assay while the mycelia were quantified after drying following a previous report [34,60]. All experiments were repeated thrice. 

# Live cell imaging of F. graminearum

A Nikon A1R laser scanning confocal microscope system (Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) was used for live-cell fluorescence imaging of F. graminearum. For hyphal localization assays, a mycelial block from SYM agar containing the leading hyphae was excised, placed upside down on a coverslip, and observed directly. GFP and mCherry excitations were set at 488 and $5 6 1 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. To examine the formation of the DON toxisome, FgTri1-GFP and FgTri4-GFP fusion proteins were constructed as described previously [61] and then transformed into protoplasts of PH-1 and ΔFgphm1, respectively. Transformants were observed under a confocal microscope, as described above. 

# RNA-seq assay and RT-qPCR

For the RNA-seq assay, WT and mutant strains were incubated in TBI medium (toxin induction medium) for 3 days in the dark. Fresh mycelia from each strain were frozen in liquid nitrogen. The samples were sent to Novogene Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China) for transcriptome sequencing. Total RNA extraction and subsequent synthesis of first-strand cDNA were performed as previously described [12]. The primer pairs used to amplify the selected genes in the qRT-PCR are listed in Table S2. All qRT-PCR assays were conducted in triplicate for each sample, and each experiment was repeated thrice. 

# Autophagy and western blot

For the autophagy assay, mycelia were cultured in liquid CM medium for approximately 16 h. Mycelia were harvested, washed twice with water, and then transferred to nitrogen-limiting medium (MM-N) in the presence of 2 mM PMSF for 4 h. Mycelia were collected at the indicated time points from which total protein was extracted, and western blotting was conducted to check the degradation level of GFP-FgAtg8 with an anti-GFP antibody, the catalog number of the molecular marker used in the western blot experiment is Vazyme (MP102–01). 

# Bioinformatics and statistical analyses

The phenol-2-monooxygenase gene sequence in F. graminearum was identified by a BLAST search of the amino acid sequences of phenol-2-monooxygenase proteins from Aspergillus nidulans in the fungal database (https://fungidb.org/fungidb/app). All experiments were carried out in three independent replicates, and the results were statistically evaluated using one-way ANOVA for multiple comparisons adapted by Tukey’s test. Differences were considered statistically significant at $\textstyle p < 0 . 0 5$ . GraphPad Prism 5.0 (GraphPad Software, Inc., USA) was used to draw graphs.
