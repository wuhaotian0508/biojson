# Summary

Leaf colour mutants are ideal germplasm resources for investigating the mechanisms of chlorophyll (Chl) synthesis, chloroplast development and photosynthesis. In this study, we obtained a yellow-leaf mutant, designated SN288-2. The variant presented a yellow-leaf phenotype and halted the development of chloroplasts at the seedling stage, with reduced accumulation of Chl. The yellow-leaf phenotype reverted to the normal phenotype in the wheat revival stage. In addition, the ratio of the crucial Chl precursors protoporphyrin IX (Proto IX) and Mg-protoporphyrin IX (Mg-Proto IX) was relatively high in yellow leaves. Bulked segregant analysis sequencing (BSA-Seq) revealed that the aberrant phenotype was controlled by two recessive genes located on chromosomes 7A and 7D, designated Y1-7A and Y2-7D, respectively. Subsequent research focused on Y1-7A. We identified TraesCS7A03G1163900 as a viable candidate for Y1-7A, encoding a major subunit of Mg-chelatase that is essential for Chl synthesis. Whole-genome resequencing and Sanger sequencing revealed a $5 . 3 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ deletion on the long arm of chromosome 7A in SN388-2 that encompasses the entire Y1-7A sequence. Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT–PCR) revealed that the Y1-7A gene was predominantly expressed in green tissues and that the encoded protein was localized within the chloroplast. Moreover, weighted gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA) revealed a gene module associated with leaf development and Chl content restoration. Consequently, these results provide a new theory regarding the regulation of Chl synthesis and chloroplast development. Overall, the loss of Y1-7A impaired the function of Mg-chelatase and blocked the conversion of Proto IX to Mg-Proto IX. 

Keywords: common wheat, yellowleaf variant, BSA-Seq, protoporphyrin IX, deletion, WGCNA. 

# Introduction

Wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) is one of the most important major global food crops, and increasing wheat yield is still the main goal of crop breeding (Song et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022). The conversion of solar energy and carbon dioxide $( \mathsf { C O } _ { 2 } )$ to organic compounds via photosynthesis forms the physiological basis of grain formation; consequently, increased photosynthesis can increase crop yield (Emamgholizadeh et al., 2015; Zhu et al., 2010). Chl, a crucial photosynthetic pigment in higher plants, is responsible for both light harvesting and solar energy transfer in the photosynthetic reaction centres of photosystems I and II. It is widely believed that an increased Chl content could increase photosynthesis rates. Thus, Chl is essential for yield improvement and agricultural production (Chen and Blankenship, 2011; Zhang et al., 2009). The entire Chl biosynthesis pathway from glutamyl-tRNA to Chl a and Chl b consists of approximately 20 different enzymatic steps (Nagata et al., 2005). Any mutations in these genes potentially lead to changes in Chl content and abnormal chloroplast development, triggering leaf colour changes (Wu et al., 2018a). Therefore, leaf colour mutants are highly valuable materials for studying chlorophyll metabolism, chloroplast development, photosynthesis, and gene functions. Among the many leaf colour mutants, the formation of yellow-green leaves is a major phenotype. Yellow–green leaf colour mutants have been observed in many higher plants, such 

as rice (Oryza sativa L.) (Deng et al., 2017; Moon et al., 2008), maize (Zea mays L.) (Ezura et al., 2016; Li et al., 2021b), barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) (Qin et al., 2015) and wheat (Wu et al., 2018a; Zhang et al., 2009). 

To date, most yellow–green leaf loci have been mapped in rice, a model crop, and several have been further cloned. For example, chlorina-1, chlorina-9 and OsCHLH encode the CHLD, CHLI, and CHLH subunits of Mg-protoporphyrin IX chelatase (Mg-chelatase), respectively (Jung et al., 2003; Zhang et al., 2006). As a vital regulatory catalyst, Mg-chelatase facilitates the insertion of ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ into Proto IX to generate Mg-Proto IX. Disruption of this process may lead to decreased Chl content and precipitate a yellow–green leaf phenotype (Jung et al., 2003; Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2006). YL-1 encodes a subunit of magnesium-protoporphyrin IX monomethyl ester cyclase (MPEC), which catalyses the conversion of MPME to divinyl protochlorophyllide and is involved in Chl biosynthesis (Chen et al., 2017). YLC1 encodes a chloroplastlocalized protein belonging to the DUF3353 superfamily, which plays a critical role in Chl accumulation and chloroplast development during early leaf development in rice (Zhou et al., 2013). In maize, ZmCAO1 encodes chlorophyll a oxygenase (CAO), which drives the synthesis of Chl b from Chl a. Lower enzyme activity of CAO1 may result in reduced Chl a and Chl b concentrations, ultimately causing the yellow–green leaf phenotype (Li et al., 2021b). 

Wheat is one of the most important crops in the world. Substantial progress has been made in mapping loci associated with leaf colour mutants, contributing to an enhanced understanding of Chl biosynthesis and photosynthesis mechanisms in wheat. However, only a few studies have succeeded in cloning corresponding genes or elucidating related molecular mechanisms because of their large genomes and high proportions of repetitive sequences (Guo et al., 2020; Walkowiak et al., 2020). 

These studies predominantly focused on biochemical characteristics, photosynthetic characteristics and genetic mapping. For example, Li located two yellow–green genes (ygld1 and ygld2) on chromosomes 5AL and 5BL, where ygld1 and ygld2 cosegregated with the simple sequence repeat (SSR) markers wmc110 and wmc28, respectively (Li et al., 2013). Furthermore, the chlorophyll-deficient clm1 mutant from the diploid wheat Triticum monococcum L. was mapped between the Xgwm473 and Xwmc96 SSR markers, with genetic distances of 2.1 and 2.6 cM, respectively, on the 7AmL chromosome (Ansari et al., 2013). The spontaneous yellow–green leaf colour-related mutant Y1718 of common wheat was located on chromosome 2BS (Zhang et al., 2017). Moreover, the leaf delayed virescence mutant (dv4) of the cultivar Guomai 301 was finely mapped to a 1.46 Mb region on chromosome 7DS (Zhang et al., 2024). One notable discovery was the identification of TraesC-S7A01G480700, a candidate gene found in the wheat yellow– green leaf mutant chli, which encodes the Mg-chelatase ChlI (Wang et al., 2020). 

Recently, we acquired a yellow–green leaf line, named SN388- 2, from the composite hybridization of the wheat cultivar Jimai22 (JM22), SN4096-1 and the hexaploid triticale SN20090148. The leaves of SN388-2 seedlings appeared yellow in the field because of the reduced Chl content. These yellow leaves progressively turned green from the wheat revival stage, achieving normal green coloration at the heading stage in the field. SN388-2 presents an opportunity to undertake a systemic, molecular genetic study of photosynthesis in yellow–green leaf mutant wheat. In this study, we investigated the inheritance of SN388-2, finely mapped the mutated gene and predicted candidate genes. The results of this investigation are presented herein. 

# Results

# SN388-2 exhibited reduced Chl levels and impaired chloroplast development

Under normal field conditions, significant differences were observed between the leaves of the wheat cultivars CS and SN388-2. While CS presented dark green leaves, SN388-2 presented distinctively yellow leaves. These yellow leaves turned dark green when they were moved into an artificial climate chamber, similar to the CS leaves (Figure 1a). To characterize the yellow-leaf phenotype of SN388-2, we initially measured the pigment content of SN388-2 under different growth conditions. In comparison, significantly reduced pigment levels were detected in yellow leaves, particularly the Chl content, with $6 8 . 9 \%$ , $6 2 . 1 \%$ , and $2 5 . 8 \%$ reductions in the Chl a, Chl b, and Caro levels, respectively (Figure 1b). These findings suggest that reduced chlorophyll levels result in a yellow-leaf phenotype. 

Additionally, we evaluated the contents of Proto IX and Mg-Proto IX and the Proto IX to Mg-Proto IX ratio during the transition from yellow leaves to green leaves. Interestingly, 

decreased levels of both Proto IX and Mg-Proto IX were observed, whereas a significant increase in the Proto IX to Mg-Proto IX ratio was noted (Figure 1c). Many studies have shown that the integration of ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ into Proto IX is a crucial step in the Chl biosynthesis process (Figure 1c) (Jung et al., 2003; Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2006). The results revealed that a bottleneck in the biosynthesis of Mg-Proto IX resulted in SN388-2 exhibiting a reduced Chl content and a yellow-leaf phenotype. 

To investigate whether inhibition of Chl synthesis impacts the development of chloroplasts, a final observation of the ultrastructures of the chloroplasts was conducted in SN388-2 and CS, each with different leaf colours, under various growth conditions (Figure 1e). TEM (transmission electron microscopy) analysis revealed no substantial differences in the number, size, shape or structure of chloroplasts between SN388-2 (green leaves) and CS. Furthermore, the chloroplasts in the green leaves presented a well-developed membrane structure, with densely packed thylakoids arranged in the grana and membranes interconnecting the grana. However, compared with green leaf chloroplasts, chloroplasts in the mesophyll cells of SN388-2 (yellow leaves) exhibited irregular shapes and structures. A majority of the chloroplast membrane structures were fragmented, and the grana and thylakoids were exposed. 

# Genetic analysis and BSA-Seq indicated that the yellow-leaf phenotype was controlled by two recessive genes

To determine the inheritance pattern of the yellow-leaf phenotype, SN388-2 was crossed with the cultivars CS, YN15, and ZM18, which are known for their typical green-leaf phenotype. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants from all the crosses presented a green-leaf phenotype. The segregation ratios of individuals in the three $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ populations with normal and yellow leaves fit the Mendelian ratio of 15:1 $( \chi ^ { 2 } < \chi ^ { 2 ( 0 . 0 5 ) } = 3 . 8 4 )$ (Table S1), implying that two pairs of recessive genes control the yellow-leaf phenotype. 

BSA-Seq is an efficient and rapid technique for gene mapping that is especially beneficial for plants with large genomes, such as those of wheat. Thirty-five yellow-leaf plants from $1 3 7 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals of the SN388-2/CS population were selected to constitute the yellow bulk. Individual DNA libraries were subsequently prepared for SN388-2, CS, and the yellow bulk. In total, BSA-Seq generated 607.9, 599.9 and 1269.5 Gb of raw sequencing data for SN388-2, CS, and the yellow bulk, respectively. After filtering, 590.1 Gb (SN388-2), 589.7 Gb (CS) and 1232.8 Gb (yellow bulk) of clean data were retained, with mapping rates of $9 9 . 8 5 \%$ , $9 9 . 9 5 \%$ and $9 9 . 9 1 \%$ , respectively, indicating that the data were of considerably high quality. 

The delta SNP index, a measure of the physical location associated with a trait, is based on the genotype of the parents and yellow bulk. Finally, we identified two significant peaks correlated with the yellow-leaf phenotype on chromosome 7A (542 681 065–705 821 228 bp) (Figure 2a) and chromosome 7D (94 352 461–619 630 402 bp) (Figure 2b), denoted Y1-7A and Y2-7D, respectively.

# Deletion of Y1-7A confers the yellow-leaf phenotype of SN388-2

Y1-7A was mapped to the physical interval spanning from 677.3 Mb to 677.3 Mb on the distal end of chromosome 7AL. Within this mapping interval, the Chinese Spring RefSeq v2.1 gene annotation indicated the presence of seven highconfidence genes (Table S6). Among all the gene annotations, only TraesCS7A03G1163900 was identified as a major subunit of Mg-protoporphyrin IX chelatase (Mg-chelatase), which is related to Chl synthesis. Mg-chelatase catalyses the insertion of ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ into protoporphyrin IX, a critical, simple reaction that is potentially one of the most crucial steps in the Chl synthesis pathway (Jung et al., 2003; Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2006). Considering the annotated functions of the genes, TraesCS7A03G1163900 was predicted as a potential candidate for the Y1-7A gene. 

After the sequencing reads were aligned with the Chinese Spring wheat reference genome (IWGSC, Refseq v2.1), we identified the region for TraesCS7A03G1163900 with a 5311 bp deletion (from positions 677 482 258 to 677 487 569) in SN388-2 and the yellow bulk using Integrative Genomics Viewer (IGV) (Figure 3a) (Robinson et al., 2011). On the basis of the sequence of the reference genome, six independent primer sets (Table S4) were designed to amplify the genomic DNA from SN388-2 and CS (Figure 3b). Each of these primer pairs (1F/1R, 2F/2R, 3F/3R, 4F/4R, 5F/5R) amplified the corresponding fragment in CS but not in SN388-2; simultaneously, the primer pair 1F/5R amplified the genomic region spanning the deletion in SN388-2 and was not able to amplify the region in CS due to the long physical distance (Figure 3c). Similarly, 1F/5R amplified a fragment in individual yellow-leaf plants of the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population that was the same size as that in SN388-2 (Figure 3d). In parallel, the amplicons of these markers were sequenced, and the sequencing results also revealed a $5 . 3 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ deletion (Figure 3e). 

In addition, 3F/3R and 1F/5R together behaved as codominant markers to differentiate homozygotes from hemizygotes (Figure 3f). All of the tested homozygous yellow-leaf plants of $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ contained the same PCR band as SN388-2. In contrast, the tested homozygous green plants of $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ presented the same PCR band as CS, whereas the heterozygous green plants of $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ presented the same PCR band as both SN388-2 and CS. Thus, TraesCS7A03G1163900 is a compelling candidate for Y1-7A. 

SN388-2 is a progeny of the hexaploid triticale SN20090148, common wheat SN4076-1, and JM22. The deletion-specific DNA markers 3F/3R and 1F/5R were utilized to amplify SN388-2 and its parent lines SN20090148, SN4076-1, and JM22. The results demonstrated that the marker 1F/5R specifically amplified the segment in SN388-2 encompassing the deletion junction, excluding the SN20090148, SN4076-1, and JM22 genomic regions (Figure 3g). This finding indicates that the deletion fragment is unique to SN388-2. The reasons for this deletion can be attributed to two primary molecular mechanisms. First, the deletion may result from chromosome fragments and gene recombination in the process of distant hybridization, known as “genome reshuffling”. Second, several TEs were identified in the flanking region of TraesCS7A03G1163900 within this deleted region. Another plausible explanation for the loss of this gene could be the “genome shock” triggered by the 

mobilization of transposons (Catoni et al., 2018; Drongitis et al., 2019). 

# Transcription profiling of TraesCS7A03G1163900 and subcellular localization of its product

Given that TraesCS7A03G1163900 is a candidate gene for Y1-7A, we determined its transcription levels in different tissues. Y1-7A was transcribed from the leaves, stems, glumes, seeds and roots. The expression patterns of Y1-7A in these various tissues were investigated via qRT–PCR. As we expected, Y1-7A presented abundant expression in green tissues, especially in leaves, but presented weaker expression in seeds and roots, which lack chloroplasts (Figure 4a). 

To examine the subcellular localization of the Y1-7A protein, the Y1-7A-GFP fusion protein was transiently expressed in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Figure 4b). The results revealed that the expressed Y1-7A protein was targeted to chloroplasts, which suggests that it impacts chloroplast development, which aligns with the TEM observations of chloroplasts. 

# WGCNA of the effect of the variation pattern of the Chl content in SN388-2

SN388-2 plants were cultivated under normal field conditions before being transferred to an artificial climate chamber, where their leaf colour transitioned from yellow to deep green. RNA samples were collected from each individual plant at 0 h (A group), 48 h (B group) and 96 h (C group) following transfer, and the corresponding chlorophyll content was determined. Six biological replicates were maintained for each time point. The Log2FoldChange was used as an indicator of gene expression, and a total of 111 716 genes were differentially expressed as the Chl content increased. To comprehensively investigate the regulatory network involved in leaf development and identify specific genes strongly correlated with changes in chlorophyll content in SN388-2, we performed WGCNA. A total of 11 716 DEGs were used to construct a scale-free coexpression network on the basis of soft-thresholding power. Clusters of highly interconnected genes were delineated as modules, with genes within the same module demonstrating high correlation coefficients. According to the WGCNA results, a total of nine modules were identified via the dynamic tree cut method, each represented by a distinct colour (Figure 5a). 

With respect to the correlations between the Chl content and modules, the paleturquoise module presented the highest correlation (Figure 5b), indicating that genes within this module share similar expression patterns related to Chl. The paleturquoise module encompasses 5250 genes; for each of these genes, we graphed the module membership (MM) against gene significance (GS) as a scatter plot and observed a high correlation between GS and MM within the paleturquoise module (Figure 5c), illustrating that genes significantly associated with Chl are pivotal components of this module. We subsequently performed gene network visualization and gene connectivity analysis for the paleturquoise module genes with the aim of constructing a gene co-expression network and mining hub genes (Figure 5d). Finally, six key hub genes that are thought to participate in chloroplast development and pigment synthesis were identified (Table 1).

# Discussion

# SN388-2 is a new wheat leaf colour mutant

Leaf colour formation is closely related to chloroplast development and photosynthetic pigments. In recent years, many leaf colour mutants have been identified in wheat, and they are ideal materials for investigating pigment biosynthesis and plant photosynthesis (Ansari et al., 2013; Li et al., 2013). To date, the reported genes associated with wheat leaf colour are located on chromosomes 2BS (Zhang et al., 2017), 2DS (Zhang et al., 2020), 5AL, 5BL (Li et al., 2013), 7AL (Ansari et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2020), 7DS (Zhang et al., 2024), etc. However, only a few genes have been finely mapped and cloned in common wheat. Here, we report a spontaneous yellow–green mutant leaf germplasm, SN388-2, whose Chl deficiency results in pale yellow leaves at the seedling stage, which then gradually turn dark green (Figure 1). Genetic analysis revealed that the phenotype was associated with two pairs of recessive genes (Table S1), which is inconsistent with previous reports showing that the phenotype of most yellow-leaf mutants are controlled by a single gene. 

In addition, the value of SN388-2 as an important marker is significant for several reasons. First, SN388-2 has distinct yellow 

leaf phenotype, allowing researchers to investigate the genetic pathways involved in chlorophyll biosynthesis. By studying this mutant, scientists can gain a clearer understanding of the interactions between genes and their roles in pigment production. Second, the unique characteristics of SN388-2 provide valuable information for crop improvement programs. The insights gained from this marker can be used to develop new wheat varieties that may exhibit enhanced photosynthetic efficiency, increased yield, and better stress tolerance. This is particularly important as food security becomes a global challenge. Finally, SN388-2 can serve as a reference point in molecular breeding strategies. By identifying and using genetic markers associated with the yellow leaf trait, breeders can more effectively select for desirable traits in their populations, improving the efficiency of developing new cultivars. 

# Strategy for fine mapping of two typical qualitative trait-associated genes

The purpose of the present study was to precisely map the Chl deficiency-related genes in SN388-2. Marker density and exchange frequency are essential for fine mapping (Dong et al., 2022), and advanced genome sequencing technologies have facilitated achievement of this goal (Li et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021). To accomplish this, first, we applied a BSA-Seq approach to a well-segregated $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population to localize the corresponding locus rapidly (Li et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2024). Wheat is a polypoid species with a large genome and high repetitive-sequence proportion, which can complicate the characterization of the genes that cause the desired phenotype (The International Wheat Genome Sequencing et al., 2018; Walkowiak et al., 2020). To reduce the influence of genome complexity, most studies have used BSR-Seq to clone important genes in wheat (Li et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018). The RNA-Seq method has limitations because it requires the expression of underlying genes at the time of sample collection. In contrast, the whole-genome sequencing approach can not only determine the major chromosomal locations of potential traits but also provide ultrahigh SNP density that can be used to develop markers for fine mapping (Li et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2024). Indeed, the high-density SNPs that we detected in the current study were paramount for developing SNP markers to support precise localization. Here, we mapped two loci associated with yellow leaves: Y1-7A and Y2-7D (Figure 2). 

In addition, a population with a Mendelian ratio of 1:3 is frequently used to narrow down target regions of a single gene (Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022, 2024). However, most excellent agronomic phenotypes are not controlled by a single locus in reality (Chen et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2022; Li et al., 2013). It is more time-consuming and labor-intensive to construct the ideal $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population for mapping two loci simultaneously, which is more suitable for developing a secondary mapping population with a Mendelian ratio of 1:3. To develop secondary mapping populations, we initiated selection and self-pollination processes within the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population. The selection was based on genotypes marked with MA910, K257, Xgwm437 and Barc172. We ensured that all selected plants had a heterozygous background limited to the Y1-7A region, whereas the Y2-7D background consistently presented as recessive homozygous. This selection principle ensured that segregation occurred only within the Y1-7A region. Finally, Y1-7A was successfully mapped to a 0.3 Mb interval, which contained 7 candidate genes (Figure 2e). The difficulty in cloning the Y2-7D locus stems from two main factors related to its genomic context. The first reason is the proximity of the Y2-7D locus to the centromere. if Y2-7D is located near the centromere on chromosome 7D, this may complicate cloning efforts. Centromere contain highly repetitive DNA and complex structures, which make their analysis and sequencing challenging. The second reason is the presence of foreign material in rye. the introduction of relative species from rye can further complicate the genetic landscape around Y2-7D. These heterologous sequences may lead to structural variations such as translocations that perturb the normal genomic environment. This makes it difficult to identify and isolate genes associated with the yellow leaf phenotype. 

# Conjecture of the deletion of the candidate gene TraesCS7A03G1163900

Map-based cloning suggests that Y1-7A encodes the protein CHLI, a major subunit of the Mg-chelatase that is essential for Chl synthesis; this protein catalyses the conversion of protoporphyrin IX to Mg-protoporphyrin IX (Jung et al., 2003; Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2006). Previous studies have shown that CHLI belongs to the putative DNA-dependent ATPase superfamily and contains three highly conserved motifs (Walker and 

Willows, 1997). Wang et al. reported that mutation of amino acids of CHLI in common wheat resulted in a chlorophyll-deficient phenotype (Wang et al., 2020). This phenomenon was also confirmed in the cucumber mutant C528 (Gao et al., 2016), rice mutant chlorina-9 (Zhang et al., 2006), soybean mutant yll (Campbell et al., 2014) and maize mutant Oy1 (Sawers et al., 2006). In conclusion, amino acid substitution in the conserved motif may impair the activity of Mg-chelatase and then block the biosynthesis of Chl a and Chl b. In this study, SN388-2 was the first mutant with complete deletion of the genes encoding the protein CHLI. BSA-Seq and Sanger sequencing analyses revealed that deletion of the Y1-7A genes resulted in Chl deficiency and a yellow-leaf phenotype (Figure 3f). 

SN388-2 descends from hexaploid triticale 20 090 148, common wheat SN4076-1, and JM22. The uniqueness of the $5 . 3 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ deletion in 388-2 was confirmed through the deletion-specific DNA markers 3F/3R and 1F/5R (Figure 3g). It is hypothesized that two main molecular mechanisms underlie this deletion. First, the deletion may stem from chromosome fragmentation and gene recombination during distant hybridization, a phenomenon referred to as “genome reshuffling”. For species derived from distant crossing, the main reasons for species divergence are the insertion and deletion of gene fragments (Li et al., 2021a; Lin et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2018b). These genetic alterations likely stemmed from deletion events during “genome reshuffling” 

processes. In addition to large chromosomal fragment rearrangements, minor genomic alterations, such as the elimination and insertion of short stretches, also contribute significantly to the evolutionary processes observed in interspecific hybrid plants. Additionally, the deletion may be attributable to the activation of transposable elements (TEs) that initiate mutations referred to as “genome shock” (Gaeta and Chris Pires, 2010). Within the deleted region, multiple TEs were discovered in the flanking region of TraesCS7A03G1163900. Another possible reason for gene loss might be the “genome shock” prompted by transposon mobilization (Catoni et al., 2018; Drongitis et al., 2019). 

# New insights into the molecular mechanisms of yellowleaf formation

Many studies have shown that plant transcriptome sequencing and WGCNA play important roles in mining functional genes associated with plant phenotypes (Yu et al., 2016). By analysing the DEGs, we found that Chl biosynthesis-related genes were significantly downregulated, except ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ transporter genes, whereas chloroplast development-related genes were significantly upregulated (Figure 6). In summary, we proposed a novel molecular regulatory network in SN388-2 that is associated with the transition of leaves from yellow to green. The deletion of Y1- 7A led to the accumulation of a range of metabolites from Lglutamyl-tRNA to protoporphyrin IX. As the leaves transitioned, the expression of genes (HEMA, GSA, ALAD, HEMD, HEME, HEMF and $P P O X$ ) decreased gradually, whereas the expression of genes encoding ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ transporters increased significantly, resulting in the lower accumulation of metabolites over time, and the Chl biosynthesis process normalized. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The materials used in this study included the wheat cultivars Jimai22 (JM22), Zhengmai18 (ZM18), Yannong15 (YN15) and Chinese Spring (CS); an elite wheat line, SN4076-1, which was bred by our group; and a hexaploid triticale line, SN20090148 $2 n = 6 x = 4 2$ , AABBRR), which was introduced from CIMMYT. The yellow–green leaf line SN388-2 was obtained from the ${ \sf B C } _ { 2 } \sf F _ { 6 }$ generation of the SN20090148/SN4076-1//JM22 population. In 2020, crosses of SN388-2 with YN15, ZM18 and CS were carried out, and the $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid plants were planted; the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ segregating populations were planted in 2021. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ segregating populations were planted in 2022. Preliminary localization of the mutant gene was carried out using $1 3 7 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ segregating populations derived from a cross of CS/SN388-2, and the $2 1 6 4 \mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ population of CS/SN388-2 were applied for fine mapping. 

# Measurement of the Chl a, Chl b, carotenoid (Caro), Proto IX and Mg-Proto IX contents

To extract the photosynthetic pigments, 200 mg samples of fresh leaves were cut into pieces and immersed in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ethanol for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ in the dark (Wu et al., 2007). Afterward, the plant debris was removed via centrifugation, and the absorbance of the supernatant was analysed via spectrophotometric scanning at 665, 649 and 470 nm. The Chl a, Chl b and Caro contents were determined according to the methods described by Arnon (1949). Three biological replicates were analysed for each sample. A Proto IX ELASA kit and a Mg-Proto IX ELASA kit (MEIMIAN Biotech) were used to determine the contents of Proto IX and Mg-Proto IX. 

# Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis

SN388-2 and CS were analysed by TEM using the methods described below. First, the leaves were cut into small pieces, fixed in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in phosphate buffer for $4 \textrm { h }$ , rinsed and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in $1 \%$ OsO4. Next, the samples were dehydrated with an ethanol series, soaked with a graded series of epoxy resin, and then embedded in Epon 812 resin. Finally, thin sections were sliced using a diamond knife and a Reichert OM2 ultramicrotome, stained with $2 \%$ uranyl acetate and $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ lead citrate, pH 12, and observed via transmission electron microscopy (JEM-1400PLUS, JEOL). 

# Genetic analysis

Leaf colour was observed and recorded from the seedling stage to the wheat revival stage. Phenotypic identification was carried out on the parents, $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid plants, and individual plants in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ populations. The genetic analysis was carried out by calculating the ratios of individuals with yellow-green leaves and normal individuals in the segregating populations. The Mendelian ratio was tested with chi-square $( \chi ^ { 2 } )$ and t tests. 

# Bulked segregant analysis sequencing (BSA-Seq) and SNP marker development

Thirty-five yellow-leaf plants from the SN388-2/CS population $1 3 7 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ plants) were selected to form the yellow bulk. The DNA libraries were prepared individually for SN388-2, CS and the yellow bulk. Genomic DNA was extracted from young leaves of the experimental lines via the CTAB method (Riede and Anderson, 1996). The DNA library was sequenced on the DNBSEQ platform at Benagen Technology Co., Ltd. (Wuhan, China). After quality control, the clean reads were aligned to the reference genome of Chinese Spring wheat v2.1 (https://urgi. versailles.inra.fr/download/iwgsc/IWGSC_RefSeq_Assemblies/v2. 

1/) via BWA software with default parameters (Li, 2009). SNP calling was conducted using GATK software (McKenna et al., 2010). 

The corresponding sequences of the SNPs for the targeted regions were submitted to dCAPS Finder 2.0 (http://biology4. wustl.edu/dcaps/), NEBcutter (https://nc3.neb.com/NEBcutter/) and PolyMarker (https://www.polymarker.info/) for the development of dCAPS/CAPS markers and KASP markers, respectively. The newly developed dCAPS/CAPS markers and KASP markers are listed in Tables S2 and S3, respectively. 

# Cloning and prediction of candidate genes

A total of $1 3 7 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ plants and $2 1 6 4 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ plants from a cross (SN388-2 and CS) were screened to identify recombinants in the Y1-7A interval. DNA was extracted from the leaves and subsequently analysed via a total of 18 molecular markers, comprising 1 SSR marker, 5 dCAPS/CAPS markers, and 12 KASP markers. Based on the clone, the sequence of the candidate region was submitted to the allohexaploid wheat JBrowse database (http://wheatomics.sdau.edu.cn/) for the identification of candidate genes (Ma et al., 2021). The candidate genes were predicted on the basis of their annotated functions and expression levels within the mapping interval from both SN388- 2 and CS. Moreover, we sequenced the candidate regions from both SN388-2 and CS, and the primers used are listed in Table S4. The sequencing was carried out at Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). 

# Relative gene expression analysis by quantitative realtime PCR (qRT–PCR)

To quantify the relative expression of related genes and identify the expression patterns of candidate genes, different tissues, such as leaves, roots, spikes, seeds, stems of SN388-2 and CS, were sampled at the corresponding stages. Total RNA was extracted with an EasyPure Plant RNA Kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China). The gDNA was eliminated and the RNA was reverse transcribed to cDNA via a Transcript One-Step gDNA Removal Kit and a cDNA Synthesis SuperMix Kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China), respectively. qRT–PCR was performed on a QuantStudioTM 12 K Flex RT–PCR System (Applied Biosystems) with a SYBR Green Pro Taq HS qPCR Kit (Accurate Biology, Hunan, China). The qRT–PCR primers used were designed according to the Chinese Spring wheat v2.1 reference genome (Table S5). The experiment was conducted with three technical replicates. Relative expression levels were determined via the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T }$ method, with the actin gene used as an internal reference (Livak and Schmittgen, 2000). 

# Subcellular localization

The full-length CDS of the candidate gene was fused into the pBi121-GFP vector via homologous recombination. The pBi121-Y1- 7A-GFP construct (Y1-7A-GFP) was subsequently transformed into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. pBi121-GFP was used as a negative control. The GFP fluorescence signals were captured via a Leica laser scanning confocal microscope (TCS SP8). 

# RNA sequencing and weighted gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA)

During the leaf colour recovery process, the Chl content gradually increased. To further examine the influence of Chl content variation patterns on the regulatory network in response to Chl synthesis patterns and to identify the hub genes closely associated with physiological changes due to Chl content variations in wheat, we performed WGCNA. 

At the seedling stage, SN388-2 exhibited notably yellow leaves. When transported to an artificial climate chamber, these leaves changed colour from yellow to green, and samples were taken at various stages during this transition. The Chl content of each sample was evaluated, and RNA was extracted concurrently. Thereafter, RNA sequencing and WGCNA were conducted by Benagen Technology Co., Ltd. (Wuhan, China).
