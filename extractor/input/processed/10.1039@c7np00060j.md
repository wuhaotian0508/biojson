# Expanding the roles for 2-oxoglutarate-dependent oxygenases in plant metabolism

J. M. Hagel and P. J. Facchini *

Covering: up to 2018

2-Oxoglutarate-dependent oxygenases (2ODOs) comprise a large enzyme superfamily in plant genomes, second in size only to the cytochromes P450 monooxygenase (CYP) superfamily. 2ODOs participate in both primary and specialized plant pathways, and their occurrence across all life kingdoms points to an ancient origin. Phylogenetic evidence supports substantial expansion and diversification of 2ODOs following the split from the common ancestor of land plants. More conserved roles for these enzymes include oxidation within hormone metabolism, such as the recently described capacity of Dioxygenase for Auxin Oxidation (DAO) for governing auxin homeostasis. Conserved structural features among 2ODOs has provided a basis for continued investigation into their mechanisms, and recent structural work is expected to illuminate intriguing reactions such as that of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid oxidase (ACCO). Phylogenetic radiation among this superfamily combined with neo- and subfunctionalization has enabled recruitment to highly specialized pathways, including those yielding medicines, flavours, dyes, poisons, and compounds important for plant-environment interactions. Catalytic versatility of 2ODOs in plants and across broader taxa continues to inspire biochemists tasked with the discovery of new enzymes. This highlight article summarizes recent reports up to 2018 of 2ODOs within plant metabolism. Furthermore, the respective contributions of 2ODOs and other oxidases to natural product biosynthesis are discussed as a framework for continued discovery.

# 1 Introduction

Enzymes of the 2-oxoglutarate-dependent oxygenase (2ODO) superfamily are widely distributed in nature and catalyze diverse reaction types including hydroxylation, dealkylation, desaturation, epoxidation, epimerization, halogenation, cyclization, peroxide formation and ring expansion/contraction. While 2ODO enzymes are arguably less studied than hemedependent oxygenases such as cytochromes P450 monooxygenases (CYPs), they exhibit comparable versatility. Like CYPs, 2ODO enzymes utilize a high valence iron-oxo intermediate to initiate otherwise difficult reactions at unactivated sites.1 All members of this superfamily depend on the presence of free iron, which is chelated by a conserved “facial triad” motif comprised of two His and one Asp or Glu residue $\left( { \mathrm { H X D } } / { \mathrm { E X } } _ { n } \mathbf { H } \right)$ ). The iron is further coordinated by 2-oxoglutarate (2OG), whose decomposition to carbon dioxide and succinate allows the creation of the reactive iron-oxo intermediate from molecular oxygen. In addition to the facial triad, a second feature common to all 2ODOs is the presence of a double-stranded $\beta$ -helix (DSBH) fold, which in turn houses the facial triad.2 CYPs and

2ODOs represent the rst and second largest enzyme superfamilies in plants, respectively; for example, in the Arabidopsis thaliana genome there are 245 and ${ \sim } 1 3 0$ sequences respectively encoding CYPs and 2ODOs.3 2ODOs participate in a broad array of metabolic activities in plants, catalyzing key reactions in conserved pathways of cell maintenance and hormone metabolism but contributing as well to the production of highly specialized metabolites.4,5 Thus far, the best-studied examples of 2ODOs include those involved in the biosynthesis of the gibberellin phytohormones and UV-protectant avonoid pigments. However, rapid accruement of transcriptome and genomic sequence data over the past 10 years has promoted a plethora of new discoveries, revealing novel 2ODOs contributing to the production of commercially and/or environmentally important compounds such as opiates,6 chemotherapy drugs,7 food toxins,8 potential cardiovascular treatments,9 plant defense compounds10–12 and metabolites promoting microbiome interactions.13 Recent reports have highlighted the involvement of Dioxygenase for Auxin Oxidation 1 (DAO1) in auxin catabolism, solving a longstanding puzzle in hormone homeostasis.14–16 Signicantly, phylogenetic analysis of 2ODOs has revealed substantial diversication of this superfamily within land plants, prompting speculation that 2ODO evolution is partly responsible for the complex array of plant specialized

metabolites.3 This article summarizes mechanistic, evolutionary and metabolic features of plant 2ODOs, with a focus on recent discoveries. Parallels are drawn between the functionality of 2ODOs and other plant oxidative enzymes to debate putative involvement of specic families in unresolved biosynthetic pathways. Recent reports of microbial 2ODOs capable of endoperoxide formation,17 ring contraction,18 multistep oxidations,19 orthoester linkage and methylene dioxy bridge formation20 continue to expand the catalytic repertoire of 2ODO enzymes, a learning that can inspire plant biochemists tasked with enzyme discovery.

Greater phylogenetic resolution of the DOXC clade revealed over 50 sub-clades of characterized or putative 2ODOs. Many clades contained enzymes participating in single or functionally related pathways. For example, 2ODOs involved in avonoid metabolism were classied into two distinct clades: DOXC28 or DOXC47, comprised of (1) avanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H) and avone synthase (FNS I) enzymes, or (2) avonol synthase (FLS) and anthocyanidin synthase (ANS) enzymes, respectively.3 Conversely, phylogenetic analysis supported surprising relationships between functionally unrelated 2ODOs. # 3 Mechanism and structure of plant 2ODOs

Like other non-heme, iron-dependent oxygenases, 2ODOs activate molecular oxygen using a redox-active iron, coordinated octahedrally within their active site. Resting-state enzymes coordinate their iron centers with three water molecules, along with the side-chains of the three key amino acids comprising the 2-His-1-carboxylate facial triad. The liganding arrangement of most 2ODOs is $\mathrm { H X ( D / E ) X _ { 5 0 - 2 1 0 } H }$ and in plants, as with other organisms, most 2ODO enzymes require 2-oxoglutarate as a cosubstrate.29 The active site is housed within the DSBH motif, which is also called the jelly-roll, cupin, or JmjC fold (Fig. 2A–D). The DSBH motif is comprised of eight antiparallel $\beta \cdot$ -strands forming a $\beta$ -sandwich structure exhibiting two, four-stranded antiparallel $\beta$ -sheets.2 It is worth pointing out that a separately evolved but mechanistically similar class of enzymes including 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate dioxygenase (HPPD) (Table 1) and hydroxymandelate synthase (HMS) exhibit an entirely different structure and spacing of their facial triad is distinctive $\left( \mathrm { H X } _ { \sim 8 0 } \mathrm { H X } _ { \sim 8 0 } \mathrm { E } \right)$ .29 Compared with microbial and human enzymes, relatively few plant 2ODOs have been structurally elucidated. The structure of A. thaliana anthocyanidin synthase (AtANS) (Fig. 2A and E) with substrates coordinating an iron metal center provided an early benchmark achievement against which new structures continue to be modelled.30 A recent study of A. thaliana feruloyl-CoA $6 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase $\left( \mathrm { A t F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } \right)$ yielded an apo enzyme crystal structure with a sodium-substituted metal center (Fig. 2B and F).31 AtF60 H ortho-hydroxylates feruloyl-CoA, which subsequently undergoes spontaneous trans/cis isomerization and lactonization yielding scopoletin (Table 1). Active site modelling with ferulate and 2OG was guided by ANS structural attributes featuring the standard bidentate coordination of 2OG at the metal center via its 2-keto and 1-

carboxylate groups (Fig. 2F). Members of the plant 2ODO superfamily oen exhibit a conserved RXS motif, preceded by a conserved tyrosine, with importance in 2OG binding. In AtANS, the Arg298 of RXS coordinates the 5-carboxylate of 2OG (Fig. 2E), a feature possibly conserved in $\mathrm { A t F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ although more evidence will be necessary to support the current model. The RXS motif of AtF60 H is comprised of Arg303 and Ser305, preceded by a conserved Tyr220 (Fig. 2F). Congruent with the dihydroquercetin substrate of the AtANS structure, the ferulic acid moiety was modelled using electrostatic interactions with residues Tyr151, Asn216 and S153, in addition to $\boldsymbol \pi$ stacking stabilization with Phe309.31 It has been proposed that the DSBH $\beta$ -strands of 2ODOs furnish selectivity toward their primary substrates, in addition to providing structural support for the active site.32 Indeed, coordination of both quercetin and 2OG is managed by DSBH residues in AtANS, a feature likely common with $\mathrm { A t F 6 ^ { \prime } H }$ . Both AtANS and $\mathrm { A t F 6 ^ { \prime } H }$ catalyze hydroxylation reactions, followed by spontaneous elimination/dehydration or cyclization, respectively. The consensus mechanism for hydroxylation has been reviewed2 and is summarized in Scheme 1A for comparative purposes. Briey, 2OG binds the $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ center in bidentate conguration, replacing two water molecules. The primary substrate then binds to the enzyme active site – but not to the iron center – displacing the third water molecule. This triggers an available site for ${ \bf O } _ { 2 }$ binding, creating an $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 3 + }$ superoxo intermediate that oxidatively attacks and decomposes 2OG to $\mathbf { C O } _ { 2 }$ and succinate. This reaction generates the reactive $\mathrm { F e ^ { 4 + } }$ -oxo intermediate which abstracts a hydrogen atom from the primary substrate creating a radical, followed by hydroxyl radical rebound yielding hydroxylated product. There are outstanding questions with this model; for example, an alternative pathway involving base-catalyzed deprotonation of $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 3 + } { \mathrm { - } } \mathrm { O H }$ has been proposed.2 The mechanism by which 2ODOs catalyze hydroxylation similarly applies to dioxygenases operating on protein substrates, such as prolyl 4-hydroxylase (P4H). Despite obvious differences in substrate and considerable phylogenetic distance from DOXC-clade enzymes, DOXB-clade P4H shares numerous structural features with other plant 2ODOs such as AtANS.33

A remarkable variation of this consensus mechanism was recently proposed to explain the activity of a newly identied 2ODO operating in benzoxazinoid metabolism. BX13 catalyzes the formation of TRIMBOA-Glc from DIMBOA-Glc in maize (Zea mays), a reaction involving ortho-rearrangement of a methoxy group (Table 1).11 This surprising activity was likened to that of HPPD, an enzyme which similarly catalyzes oxidation of an aromatic ring and subsequent ortho-rearrangement of an acetate side chain (Table 1).29 Handrick and colleagues11 proposed a mechanism by which C7 hydroxylation of DIMBOA-Glc occurs through electrophilic attack by an iron-oxene species, followed by 1,2-shi of the methoxy group favoured by a positive charge at C8 (Scheme 1B). While further study of the mechanism is required, the distinctive nature of this reaction highlights the capacity of 2ODOs for unusual chemistries. FNSI and FNSII enzymes belong to 2ODO and CYP superfamilies, respectively, and they both catalyze desaturation without the need for a hydroxylated intermediate. The mechanism of desaturation is thought to proceed through a radical mechanism involving either a two-step, or concerted one-step, syn-elimination process.34 Signicantly, both FNSI and FNSII enzymes show additional capacity as hydroxylases, in which case a competing reaction takes place and the second hydrogen is not abstracted. Insight regarding the structural features governing these competing reactions was obtained through the characterization of liverwort (Plagiochasma appendiculatum) FNSI, which simultaneously catalyzes both desaturation and hydroxylation of naringenin substrate to yield apigenin and 2-hydroxynaringenin, respectively (Table 1).35 Modelling based on ANS structure highlighted the importance of Tyr240 within the FNSI active site, and site-directed mutagenesis (Y240P) ablated capacity for hydroxylation, allowing only apigenin product.

Although most plant 2ODOs rely on enzyme-catalyzed decomposition of 2OG to generate the reactive $\mathrm { F e ^ { 4 + } }$ -oxo intermediate, a notable exception includes 1-aminocyclopropane-1- carboxylic acid oxidase (ACCO). Plant ACCO and bacterial ethylene-forming enzyme are structurally similar to other 2ODO superfamily members, and both exhibit the archetypical DSBH fold. Ethylene-forming enzyme from Pseudomonas syringae uses 2OG co-substrate as part of a highly unusual, argininedependent, 4-electron oxidation reaction.36 In contrast, plant ACCO is thought to follow a radical mechanism involving two single-electron oxidations to yield ethylene, $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , and cyanide from 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) and molecular oxygen (Table 1).37 Despite over two decades of study, the ACCO reaction mechanism remains controversial; for example, the apparent requirement for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ (or bicarbonate ion) is not well understood. Only recently it was proven that plant ACCO employs a cyanoformate intermediate as a means of safely releasing toxic CN product, thus avoiding destruction of its own iron active site.38 While more work is needed to fully understand this reaction, it is well-established that ACC coordinates iron in a bidentate manner, similar to how 2OG is bound in more typical plant 2ODOs. Further, the mechanism involves a reactive $\mathrm { F e ^ { 4 + } }$ -oxo intermediate, although the role(s) of cofactors, the order of substrate binding, and sequence of electron transfer steps is uncertain. The mechanism shown in Scheme 1C is based on a proposal by Rocklin and colleaugues39 although alternatives exist.40 The rst ACCO structure was reported in 2004 using the petunia (Petunia hybrida) homologue in apoform complexed with $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ or $\mathrm { C o } ^ { 2 + }$ .41 More recently, the structure of petunia ACCO (PhACCO) complexed with ACC substrate and a $\mathrm { N i } ^ { 2 + }$ center was achieved with 2.6 A resolution (PDB ID: ˚ $5 \mathrm { T C V } ) ^ { 4 2 }$ (Fig. 2C and G). In both cases, Lys158 – the residue proposed to interact with ascorbate or bicarbonate ion43 – projects directly into the PhACCO active site. Arg244 and Ser246 comprise the conserved RXS motif, along with the preceding Tyr162. It was noted in the apo-form of PhACCO that the Arg244 side chain was directed away from the active site, a curious result in light of its purported role in binding the ACC

carboxylate.41 It was suggested that Arg244 may rotate toward the active site upon binding of ACC, although the recent ACCbound structure also adopts this conguration (Fig. 2G). Additional structural work, together with mechanistic studies centered on new substrate analogues37 will undoubtedly illuminate unresolved aspects of this complex reaction. Notably, a chemical genetics study aimed at identifying inhibitors of ethylene biosynthesis provided additional insight into the nature of the ACCO active site44 (Fig. 2D and H). Pyrazinamide is a drug used clinically for tuberculosis treatment, and is converted in plant cells to pyrazinecarboxylic acid (POA). Fig. 2H shows the crystal structure of A. thaliana ACCO complexed with POA, in which the POA carboxylate group and the nitrogen of the pyrazine ring coordinate the metal center. This bidentate binding mode is comparable to that of ACC-binding in which the carboxylate and amino groups coordinate the central metal (Fig. 2G). As such, it may be theorized that POA binding to the ACCO active site would effectively exclude the natural ACC substrate.

# 4 New roles for 2ODOs in plant metabolism

The increasing wealth of sequence data and related molecular tools for non-model plants has prompted a surge in discoveries over the past decade, particularly in the realm of specialized metabolism. Isolation of previously unknown 2ODOs has been reported in the context of many independently evolved pathways, revealing repeated and ongoing recruitment of these versatile catalysts. Advances have not been restricted to specialized metabolism, as underscored by a recent breakthrough in auxin biochemistry. Auxin is possibly the most important signaling molecule in plants, as it governs female gametophyte patterning, embryogenesis, organogenesis, apical dominance, tissue regeneration, and tropisms.45 This regulation is achieved through a nely tuned maintenance of auxin gradient throughout the plant, and only latterly has the enzymatic basis for this balance been elucidated. Auxin is degraded to 2-oxindole-3-acetic acid (oxIAA), a catabolite with little biological activity formed rapidly and irreversibly in response to increased auxin levels.46 A rice 2ODO functioning in reproductive development was shown to convert auxin to oxIAA in vitro, 47 a key nding that prompted concomitant reports of A. thaliana homologues dioxygenase for auxin oxidation, DAO1 and DAO2 (ref. 14–16) (Table 1). Like other plant 2ODOs of hormone metabolism, DAO1 and DAO2 belong to the large and diverse category of DOXC enzymes (Fig. 1).3 DAO1 oversees the major route for auxin oxidation in A. thaliana, and disrupting DAO1 elevates levels of auxin conjugates ${ \sim } 5 0 \ – 2 8 0$ -fold.14

Searching for new catalysts using sequences of 2ODOs with established involvement in related (or identical) metabolic pathways has allowed many discoveries, since gene duplication followed by sub- or neofunctionalization is an inherent feature of plant genomes.48 Homology-based, forward genetics approaches underpinned the discovery of new 2ODOs from opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) with widespread roles in

benzylisoquinoline alkaloid metabolism. Specically, known 2ODOs catalyzing O-demethylation in opiate alkaloid biosynthesis were used to query transcriptomic resources, identifying new 2ODOs that $o$ -demethylate and O,O-demethylenate various other poppy alkaloids (Table 1).49,50 2ODOs of opium poppy are thought to catalyze dealkylation through hydroxylation and subsequent formaldehyde release. Homology to poppy 2ODOs aided in the selection of avone O-demethylation candidates from basil.51 Similarly, availability of a rice 2ODO sequence encoding FNSI enabled the identication of new maize and A. thaliana enzymes functioning in avone metabolism.52 Isolation of FNSI homologues from liverwort,35 rice, A. thaliana and maize is signicant as it was previously held that Type I avone synthases were restricted to members of the Apiaceae family.53 Beyond avonoids, discovery of 2ODO involvement has been made for other phenylpropanoid-derived plant metabolites such as furanocoumarins. Like $\mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ , 4-coumaroyl-CoA $2 ^ { \prime } \cdot$ hydroxylase $( \mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } )$ catalyzes ortho-hydroxylation of phenylpropanoid substrate, initiating subsequent rearrangement to the coumarin umbelliferone (Table 1). In parsnip, umbelliferone serves as precursor to defensive furanocoumarin metabolites, although the biochemistry is not fully elucidated. Sequencing of a parsnip (Pastinaca sativa)-derived BAC clone harboring the furanocoumarin biosynthetic gene 6-prenyltransferase-1 (PT1) revealed a second, novel gene encoding a 2ODO (PsDIOX).10 In vitro assays of recombinant PsDIOX with 4-coumaroyl-CoA substrate yielded umbelliferone, indicating $\mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ activity. Genomics data in combination with expression proling was used to identify a novel 2ODO participating in tanshinone metabolism. Tanshinones are highly oxidized natural products derived from diterpenoid precursor and they occur in the roots and rhizomes of the medicinal plant, danshen (Salvia miltiorrhiza). A genome-wide phylogenetic survey of danshen 2ODOs highlighted a variety of genes with putative roles in specialized metabolism, although only one (2OGD5) exhibited signicant, root-specic expression. RNAi silencing of 2OGD5 in transgenic danshen root culture diminished several tanshinone products, including miltirone, cryptotanshinone and tanshinone IIA.9

Genetic mapping in a mutant line of radish (Raphanus sativus) revealed a 2ODO functioning in glucosinolate metabolism.12 Radish uniquely accumulates glucoraphasatin, a metabolite accounting for ${ \tt > } 9 0 \%$ of radish's glucosinolate content. As glucoraphasatin is absent in better-studied members of the Brassicaceae family including A. thaliana and cabbage (Brassica oleraceae) its biosynthesis was unresolved until recently. Genetic analysis indicated that glucoraphasatin content was controlled by a single recessive gene, and map-based cloning of the mutant gene from glucoraphasatin-free radish exposed a new 2ODO designated glucoraphasatin synthase I (GRS1). Phylogenetic analysis showed that GRS1 belongs to the DOXC31 subclade along with the benzoxazinoid biosynthetic enzyme BX6, the monoterpenoid indole alkaloid hydroxylase D4H (Table 1) and the related A. thaliana glucosinolate-pathway oxidase, GSL-OH. Unlike other members of clade DOXC31 which catalyze hydroxylation, GRS1 is thought to desaturate its substrate, although hydroxylated intermediate cannot be ruled

out. The remarkable maize enzyme BX13 which catalyzes the unusual 1,2-shi of a methoxy group (Table 1) (Scheme 1B) was identied by quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping.11 Maize lines exhibiting natural variation in levels of target benzoxazinoid metabolites were mapped to arrive at three QTLs, one which contained a novel 2ODO-encoding gene. It was originally hypothesized that this 2ODO, designated BX13, would catalyze hydroxylation at C8, a reaction ensued by 8-O-methylation to yield the nal product, $\mathrm { D I M _ { 2 } B O A { - } G l c }$ . However, assays of recombinant BX13 showed hydroxylation at C7, supporting an alternative biosynthetic route with 7-O-methylation to turnover $\mathrm { D I M _ { 2 } B O A { - } G l c }$ . Serendipitously, the previously described Omethyltransferase BX7 was found to catalyze this 7-O-methylation reaction11 thereby affirming the alternative pathway model.

Transcriptomics and reverse genetics methods were applied in the discovery of a 2ODO implicated in A. thaliana strigolactone biosynthesis.13 Strigolactones are derived from $\beta$ -carotene through the central intermediate carlactone, which is a common precursor to a variety of metabolites in A. thaliana and rice. In A. thaliana, carlactone is initially oxidized by the CYP MAX1, then O-methylated by an unknown enzyme to yield methylcarlactonoate. Evidence presented by Brewer and colleagues13 pointed to Lateral Branching Oxidoreductase (LBO) as the 2ODO catalyzing the turnover of methylcarlactonate to an unknown oxidized product $^ { + 1 6 }$ Da) required for the control of shoot branching (Table 1). Mining of organ-specic transcriptome data, together with targeted inducible expression analyses lead to the discovery of a new 2ODO along with ve additional enzymes to complete the biosynthetic pathway to $( - ) { - } 4 ^ { \prime }$ -desmethylepipodophyllotoxin in mayapple (Podophyllum hexandrum).7 Podophyllotoxin, a lignan from mayapple, is the natural product precursor to etoposide which is a widely applied chemotherapy treatment. Cyclization of the pathway intermediate $( - ) \cdot$ -yatein is needed to establish the podophyllotoxin skeletal structure. A small number of CYPs, along with a single 2ODO were selected as oxidative candidates based on tissue-specic and wound-inducible expression proles matching those of known podophyllotoxin enzymes. The 2ODO, designated 2ODD, was found to catalyze a key cyclization reaction (Table 1) which sequentially takes place aer two CYPs and an $o$ -methyltransferase, but before two additional CYPs in the pathway. 2ODD likely catalyzes hydroxylation to initiate a cascade of dehydration, stereoselective cyclization, and basecatalyzed deprotonation to restore aromaticity.

# 5 Lessons for plant biochemists: 2ODOs, CYPs, and the expanding list of plant oxidases to consider

Remarkable progress has been made in assigning roles for 2ODO enzymes in both primary and specialized metabolism. In the past, plant biochemists faced with unsolved pathways would endeavor to purify an enzyme to ascertain its identity, or conduct assays with various cofactors to gain insight into the type of enzyme involved. These approaches have been replaced largely – but not entirely – with methods dependent on ready

access to transcriptomic and genomic sequence data. The most obvious and convenient strategy includes the use of known oxidases to query sequence libraries in search of candidate genes, which are in turn tested for functionality in target pathways. Certainly, this approach is inherently risky and potentially na¨ıve without sufficient understanding of established or hypothesized pathway chemistries. Candidate selection is generally accompanied by one or more parallel lines of evidence, such as gene expression proling, genomic linkage, in planta silencing, QTL or knock-out line analyses, and complementation assays. Notwithstanding these additional tools (which aren't always available, especially for non-model plants) the process of novel catalyst discovery oen entails educated guesswork. In some situations, numerous enzyme classes capable of catalyzing a target reaction may present themselves for consideration, extending the candidate list beyond practical measures. In other cases, parallel lines of evidence can be misleading. As a concrete example, the O-demethylations of thebaine and codeine occurring in opium poppy as part of morphine biosynthesis remained unsolved for years, owing to (1) the established involvement of multiple CYPs and avinylated oxidases in benzylisoquinoline alkaloid metabolism, but not 2ODOs; (2) animal CYPs known to O-demethylate these same substrates; and (3) misleading expression proles of many uncharacterized oxidases, including CYPs, matching those of established enzymes in the pathway.6,54 Prior to 2010, 2ODOs were not known to catalyze O-demethylation, although they were known to $N \cdot$ -demethylate nucleotide bases and histone lysine/arginine residues.5 Perhaps had these analogous reactions been considered, thebaine 6-O-demethylase (T6ODM) and codeine O-demethylase (CODM) would have been tested years earlier as they are among the most abundant proteins in alkaloid-synthesizing latex tissue.55

Due in part to a plethora of discoveries over the past decade, 2ODOs are now routinely considered as potential catalysts in plant pathways. Nonetheless surprises continue to surface, such as the discovery of a Rieske-type oxygenase responsible for avone hydroxylation56 and the emerging importance of avinylated oxidases in plant specialized metabolism.57 When considering target enzyme classes for unsolved reactions, perspective can be gained through comparison with established pathways from a wide variety of taxa. It was recently shown that methylenedioxy bridge formation in actinomycete bacteria was catalyzed by 2ODO enzymes as part of orthosomycin biosynthesis.20 By comparison, several examples of methylenedioxy bridge closures by CYPs are known in plants.1 The example of a two-bridge closure in $\left( + \right)$ -sesamin formation is shown in Table 2, a reaction governed by the protein CYP81Q in Sesamum species. on several examples of CYPs catalyzing this reaction in plants, one may suspect the involvement of a CYP in safrole production. However, the example of a bacterial 2ODO catalyzing bridge formation indicates that other enzyme classes should not be ruled out.

$N$ -Demethylation is an important reaction in the context of DNA repair. Homologues of the bacterial 2ODO AlkB are widespread in nature, and catalyze N-demethylation of methylated nucleotide bases (Table 2).5 However, $N \cdot$ -demethylation is also central to detoxication and/or degradation pathways of humans, microbes and plants. Several enzyme types have been recruited as detoxifying N-demethylases, including human CYPs which degrade opiates6 and bacterial Rieske-type oxygenases which degrade caffeine.59 The avoenzyme berberine bridge enzyme (BBE) is known to N-demethylate the alkaloid $N \mathrm { \mathrm { - } }$ methylcoclaurine60 and nicotine is degraded by CYPs in both Nicotiana tabacum and humans (Table 2).61 In regard to plant specialized metabolism, more is known about biosynthesis than degradation, leaving many catabolic reactions unsolved. $N -$ Demethylase(s) likely play a detoxication role in plant tropane alkaloid metabolism. For example, evidence shows that hyoscyamine and littorine are probably N-demethylated in pricklyburr (Datura innoxia) (Table 2).62 The wide variety of proteins known to catalyze N-demethylation should alert those tasked with enzyme discovery against bias toward any particular enzyme family. This is generally true for a widening array of reaction types, as new enzymes catalyzing diverse chemistries continue to be found. To illustrate further, epoxidation from alkene substrate may be catalyzed by a variety of enzymes, including CYPs, avoenzymes, and 2ODOs. Thus, several possible enzyme types could potentially catalyze unresolved epoxidations such as that potentially leading to epoxeurycomanone from alkene precursor in the medicinal plant tongkat ali (Eurycoma longifolia) (Table 2).63 The obvious requirement for a broad-scope approach to pathway elucidation stresses the use of as many parallel lines of evidence as possible to quickly and correctly identify new enzymes.

Oxidative cyclization in natural product biosynthesis was recently reviewed.1 In nature, a wide variety of enzymes is known to enable oxidative cyclization, including CYPs, 2ODOs and other non-heme oxygenases, radical SAM superfamily enzymes, copperdependent tyrosinases, avoenzymes, and NAD(P)H-dependent oxidoreductases/dehydrogenases. Not all of these classes are represented in plants; for example, radical SAM enzymes are rarely found in eukaryotes. Nonetheless, the variety of catalysts capable of oxidative coupling to yield new scaffolds is exciting, particularly for those endeavoring to learn more about entirely unexplored natural product pathways. Signicantly, this catalyst diversity is not well-represented among known oxidases of plant metabolism. Most known cyclizing oxidases in plants derive from CYP, and to lesser extents 2ODO or avoenzyme superfamilies. The reasons underpinning this observation are not fully understood, although some rationale is established in terms of numbers; that is, CYPs and 2ODOs represent the two largest enzyme families in plant genomes. # 6 Conclusions

A survey of 2ODO involvement in plant metabolism provides convincing evidence of this superfamily's versatility, both in terms of catalytic capacity and metabolic functionality. 2ODOs have been shown to catalyze reactions beyond those commonly observed in plants, such as halogenation and endoperoxide formation. Further, certain reaction types occurring as part of

specialized plant metabolism such as phenol–phenol coupling reactions to form new ring structures has thus far been ascribed solely to CYPs. However, advancing knowledge of 2ODO biochemistry across divergent taxa supports the hypothesis that plant 2ODOs are not only capable of such reactions, but likely perform these transformations in as-yet unresolved pathways. Assimilating information from across research elds including structural/chemical biology, metabolism, and phylogenetics allows a comprehensive picture of how 2ODOs function. Continued progress will unearth a variety of new oxidases, including CYPs, 2ODOs, and other enzymes representing completely different evolutionary origins yet convergent in their ability to bind – and act on – intermediates of the same metabolic processes.
