# Abstract

Root-knot nematodes (RKNs) inflict extensive damage to global agricultural production. Intercropping has been identified as a viable agricultural tool for combating RKNs, but the mechanisms by which intercropped plants modulate RKN parasitism are still not well understood. Here, we focus on the cucumber–amaranth intercropping system. We used a range of approaches, including the attraction assay, in vitro RNA interference (RNAi), untargeted metabolomics, and hairy root transformation, to unveil the mechanisms by which weak host plants regulate Meloidogyne incognita chemotaxis towards host plants and control infection. Amaranth roots showed a direct repellence to M. incognita through disrupting its chemotaxis. The in vitro RNAi assay demonstrated that the Mi-flp-1 and Mi-flp-18 genes (encoding FMRFamide-like peptides) regulated M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber and controlled infection. Moreover, M. incognita infection stimulated cucumber and amaranth to accumulate distinct metabolites in both root tissues and rhizosphere soils. In particular, naringenin and salicin, enriched specifically in amaranth rhizosphere soils, inhibited the expression of Mi-flp-1 and Mi-flp-18. In addition, overexpression of genes involved in the biosynthesis of pantothenic acid and phloretin, both of which were enriched specifically in amaranth root tissues, delayed M. incognita development in cucumber hairy roots. Together, our results reveal that both the distinct host status and disruption of chemotaxis contribute to M. incognita inhibition in intercropping.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Root-knot nematodes (RKNs; Meloidogyne spp.) are devasting pathogens of numerous crops and parasitize almost every species of vascular plants around the world (Rutter et al., 2022). Traditionally, 

RKN disease is controlled by the application of nematicides and soil fumigants, which are often toxic to nontarget soil organisms and humans (Zasada et al., 2010). To ensure the safe production of agricultural products, intercropping with weak host plants has been increasingly recognized as an economical and effective agronomic

practice for RKN disease control (Abd-Elgawad, 2021; Chadfield et al., 2022). Generally, under field conditions, weak host plants can disrupt the location of host roots by RKNs and thereby block the infection. Furthermore, the chemotaxis of RKNs in soils may be opposite between weak host plants and host plants in intercropping systems (Wang et al., 2018). It has been demonstrated that chemical signals in the rhizosphere serve as regulators between plant species (Massalha et al., 2017), which may translate into direct or indirect effects on nematode behaviours. 

Chemical gradients released from host roots, such as carbohydrates, amino acids, phenolic compounds, redox potentials, and chelating compounds, are vital regulators of nematode behaviour (Perry & Wright, 1998). About $20 \%$ of the photosynthetically fixed carbon is transferred to the rhizosphere through root exudates to shape chemical cues that have the potential to attract or repel RKNs (Rasmann et al., 2012). The major sources of root exudates include the root tip and the elongation and root hair zones, all of which are attractive or repellent to the pre-infective second-stage juveniles (pre-J2s) of RKNs (Sasse et al., 2018). 

During RKN parasitism, host recognition is essential for nematodes to exactly locate host roots, and this process needs RKNs to express host-specific genes as a response to root exudate signals. For instance, in the face of host plants, Pratylenchus coffeae up-regulates the expression levels of the cell wall-degrading enzyme-encoding genes Pc-eng-1 (β-1,4-endoglucanase) and Pc-xyl (β-1,4-endoxylanase), which are determined by the concentration of cellulose or xylan within the root exudates (Bell et al., 2019). Similarly, Meloidogyne incognita, the most widespread RKN species worldwide, is able to change its behaviour and gene expression when exposed to root exudates from the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana (Teillet et al., 2013). This indicates the transcriptional alteration of M. incognita on the way to locate the host roots by chemical signals. Generally, neuropeptide-mediated olfaction is a vital sensory modality that enables parasitic nematodes to sense root exudate signals (Rengarajan & Hallem, 2016). The largest family of nematode neuropeptides is constituted by the FMRFamide-like peptides (FLPs), which are involved in multiple parasitic activities (McCoy et al., 2017). A total of 31 genes encoding FLPs were identified in Caenorhabditis elegans 

(Nelson et al., 1998), and 19 genes were found in M. incognita (Abad et al., 2008). To date, the genes flp-1, 7, 12, 14, 16, and 18 have been reported to have transcriptional functions (Banakar et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2013). 

Despite the remarkable progress in terms of elucidating the root exudate signals for nematodes, most previous studies were carried out using hydroponic growth techniques due to the convenience to perform (Bell et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2014). However, RKNs are soilborne pathogens that reside within water films on soil particles and generally interact with plants under soil conditions. Plants grown in soils often have different root metabolite profiles as compared to those grown under hydroponic conditions (Pétriacq et al., 2017). Under soil conditions, the rhizosphere of roots is the area with the most intense root–nematode interactions. Several studies have demonstrated that plant metabolism can change root exudates under biotic stresses (Allwood et al., 2021; Balmer et al., 2013; Shulaev et al., 2008) and shape the chemical environment of the rhizosphere (Hu et al., 2018; Stringlis et al., 2018). Therefore, theoretically, in the presence of RKNs, plant roots may change their rhizosphere metabolism as a response to nematode infection. However, to date, little information is available regarding RKN-induced metabolic variations in the root tissues and rhizosphere of plants grown under soil conditions, especially in intercropping. 

Because the variation of metabolism is a species-specific response of plants towards biotic stress (Allwood et al., 2021; Balmer et al., 2013; Shulaev et al., 2008), we hypothesized that RKNinduced metabolic variations in root tissues may be crucial for weak host plants to inhibit nematode infection. In addition, because root exudates are key signals that attract or repel the pre-J2s of RKNs (Rasmann et al., 2012; Sasse et al., 2018), we also hypothesized that RKN-induced metabolic variations in rhizosphere soils of weak host plants could play a vital role in disrupting nematode chemotaxis towards host plants in intercropping. Examining these two hypotheses could allow us to uncover the mechanisms by which weak host plants modulate RKN parasitism in host plants in intercropping.

# 2  |  RESULTS

# 2.1  |  Cucumber and amaranth have distinct host status to M. incognita

Under field conditions, the cucumber–amaranth intercropping system significantly reduced the extent of root damage and the root gall index in cucumber $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ ) (Figure 1a–d). Observations of fuchsin-stained roots showed that amaranth roots had a significantly lower total number of M. incognita including all the developmental stages at all sampling times (Figure 1e) and delayed M. incognita development by decreasing the number of M. incognita at different life stages (Figure S1) as compared to cucumber roots. Moreover, the giant cell area, an indicator reflecting feeding site development, was consistently lower in amaranth roots than in cucumber roots (Figure S2). Because roots provide nutrition for nematodes, root physiological characteristics (nutrient profile, total phenolic content, and phenylalanine ammonia-lyase [PAL] activity) were also measured to evaluate the response of plants to M. incognita infection (Figure S3). In general, root physiological characteristics differed in either plant type (uninfected amaranth [Am] vs. uninfected cucumber [Cs], nematode-infected amaranth [AmN] vs. nematode-infected cucumber [CsN]) or nematode treatments (AmN vs. Am, CsN vs. Cs) (revealed by principal component analysis [PCA]; Figure 1f), indicating the plant species-specific responses during nematode infection. It was noted that both total phenolic content and PAL activity were significantly higher in amaranth roots compared with cucumber roots at 3 days after inoculation (DAI) under nematode infection (Figure 1g), emphasizing that amaranth roots might produce more defence-related secondary metabolites when challenged by M. incognita infection. In addition, amaranth roots inhibited the reproduction of M. incognita as compared to cucumber roots (Figure 1h) through reducing the number of galls, egg masses, and J2s in soil at 42 and 56 DAI (Figure 1i). Coincidently, when the soils collected at 42 and 56 DAI were replanted with cucumber, the previous amaranth also suppressed M. incognita reproduction in the following cucumber generation (Figure S4), indicating the inhibitory legacy effects of amaranth on M. incognita disease in cucumber. 

# 2.2  |  Cucumber and amaranth roots inversely affect the chemotaxis of M. incognita

Host root seeking is a critical step for successful M. incognita infection and survival. The one-choice attraction assay showed that amaranth root tip was less attractive to nematodes than cucumber root tip (Figure 2a–e), resulting in a lower ratio of J2s in the root tip zone (RTZ) (Figure 2b) and a reduced chemotaxis index and J2 number in amaranth root tip (Figure 2c–e). Similarly, the dual-choice attraction assay provided further confirmation of the opposite effects of cucumber and amaranth roots on M. incognita chemotaxis (Figure 2f,g). Specifically, cucumber root tip displayed positive chemotaxis, while amaranth root tip showed negative chemotaxis, as compared to water (Figure 2g). Furthermore, J2s tended to migrate to the side of cucumber root tip on the Cs-Am Petri dish (Figure 2g). To further explore the response of M. incognita chemotaxis to intercropping, we also conducted a potbridge-pot (PBP) experiment that allowed for comparison of intercropping and monocropping in the same system (Figure 2h). Intercropping with amaranth clearly inhibited M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber, causing a significant reduction in the number of root galls and J2s in soils at the intercropping side compared with the monocropping side (C/A vs. C/C; Figure 2i,j). Together, these results confirmed the capacity of amaranth to disrupt M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber. 

# 2.3  | Mi-flp genes regulate M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber and control infection

To identify genes responsible for M. incognita chemotaxis and infection, we examined six flp genes (flp-1, 7, 12, 14, 16, and 18) that have been reported to have transcriptional functions. There were significantly higher transcript levels of flp-1, 12, 14, and 16 in infective J2s compared with pre-infective J2s and relatively low transcript levels of all six flp genes in J4s/females (Figure S5). The nematodes collected from the PBP experiment exhibited a significant reduction in expression of flp-1, 14, 16, and 18 at the intercropping side compared with the monocropping side (Figure 3a), inferring the role of these genes in regulating host root seeking. To further test the direct response of flp genes to root signals, M. incognita J2s were treated with root exudates of cucumber (Cs) or amaranth (Am). However, only flp-1 and 18 showed a significant decrease under Am treatment as compared to Cs treatment (Figure 3b). 

To confirm the function of flp genes in M. incognita chemotaxis, the one-choice attraction assay was carried out to determine the effects of RNAi on nematode chemotaxis. The fluorescence of fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) (Figure 3c) and the expression of flp genes after soaking in buffer containing double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) (Figure S6) indicated uptake of dsRNA and the success of in vitro RNAi. The relative abundance of nematodes treated with dsflp-1 significantly decreased in both the nematode movement zone (MZ) and the RTZ (Figure 3d), indicating the crucial role of flp-1 in regulating M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber. # 2.5  |  Overexpression of CsPTS and CsCHS delays M. incognita development in cucumber hairy roots

By screening and analysing the DMs of amaranth tissues (Figure S10b), we identified two specific metabolites, pantothenic acid (C00864) and phloretin (C00774) (Figure 4c). These two amaranth DMs were significantly up-regulated in pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis/ $\beta$ -alanine metabolism and flavonoid biosynthesis, both of which were also present in cucumber (csv00770/csv00410, csv00941). We further screened out the homologous genes CsPTS (pantothenate synthetase, CsaV3_3G028840) and CsCHS (chalcone synthase, CsaV3_3G027830), which are involved in the biosynthesis of pantothenic acid and phloretin, through KEGG pathway enrichment analysis in the cucumber genome database (Figure 4d). To visualize the spatial expression pattern and the response of CsPTS and CsCHS to M. incognita infection, we performed $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) staining on cucumber transgenic hairy roots containing a GUS reporter construct. Specifically, at 14 DAI, J3s and J4s were in the majority in the control transgenic roots, while the J2s were the most abundant in transgenic hairy roots overexpressing CsPTS and CsCHS (Figure4h,i). 

# 2.6  |  Metabolic variations in amaranth rhizosphere soils affect M. incognita egg hatching and infection

We also characterized M. incognita-induced metabolic variations in rhizosphere soils (Figures 5 and S14, Tables S10–S13). PCA demonstrated the distinct metabolic patterns between cucumber and amaranth rhizosphere soils (Figure 5b). Interestingly, nematode infection caused obvious metabolic changes in both positive $( \mathsf { E S l ^ { + } } )$ and negative (ESI− ) ions of rhizosphere soils in amaranth but not in cucumber (Figure 5b). There were 155 and 66 DMs enriched specifically in amaranth and cucumber rhizosphere soils, respectively, under M. incognita stress (Figures 4c,d and S14). To further explore the role played by enriched DMs (induced by M. incognita) of amaranth rhizosphere soils in modulating M. incognita parasitism, we screened amaranth-specific DMs (Figure 5c) and chose naringenin (C00509) and salicin (C01451) to evaluate their effects on nematode egg hatching and infection in cucumber roots (Figures 6 and S15). Both naringenin and salicin exhibited dose-dependent inhibitory effects on both egg hatching and the number of root galls in cucumber (Figure 6a,b). It was noted that cucumber root growth was also 

significantly inhibited by both naringenin and salicin at concentrations higher than $1 \mathsf { m M }$ (Figure S15). However, at relatively low concentrations $( 0 . 1 - 0 . 8 \mathsf { m M } )$ ), these two amaranth-specific DMs had no adverse effects on cucumber root growth, but showed inhibitory effects on nematode egg hatching and root gall formation (Figures S14 and 6a,b). More importantly, both naringenin and salicin reduced the expression of flp-1 and flp-18 in M. incognita at low concentrations (Figure 6c), indicating their inhibitory effects on M. incognita chemotaxis and infection. 

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

Although intercropping with weak host plants has been identified as an efficient agricultural strategy to combat RKNs, the mechanisms by which weak host plants modulate RKN parasitism in host plants are still not well understood (Chadfield et al., 2022). The present study clearly showed that amaranth had distinct host status to M. incognita as compared to cucumber (Figure 1). Coincidently, intercropped amaranth had also been found to suppress insect pests such as cucumber beetles and fruit flies (Pitan & Esan, 2014). The importance of the distinct host status in M. incognita inhibition can be confirmed by multiple findings, including (1) the lower reproduction of M. incognita in amaranth root as compared to cucumber root (Figure 1i), (2) the decreased number of M. incognita J2s in amaranth soils compared with cucumber soils (Figure 1i), and (3) the inhibitory legacy effects of previous amaranth on M. incognita disease in the following cucumber generation (Figure S6). These results showed that amaranth is a weak host plant to M. incognita. Thus, the distinct host status-caused reductions in M. incognita reproduction and J2 population in soils were the foundation of intercropping in terms of RKN disease control (Chadfield et al., 2022; Kihika-Opanda et al., 2022). Parasitism of host plants is characterized by the formation of giant cells as permanent feeding sites for RKNs. It is noteworthy that amaranth inhibited the formation and expansion of feeding sites (reflected by the giant cell area in root galls; Figure S5), indicating that the distinct host status was also responsible for the delayed nematode development (Bartlem et al., 2014). 

Due to the distinct host status, amaranth and cucumber had significantly different root physiological characteristics under M. incognita infection (Figure 1f). In particular, the total phenolic content was sharply increased by M. incognita infection at the early infection stage (3 DAI) and was significantly higher in amaranth roots than in cucumber roots (Figure 1g). Phenolic compounds are involved in plant defence and hence provide resistance against nematode attack (Ohri & Pannu, 2010; Sharma et al., 2022). Thus, the M. incognita-induced increase in total phenolic content contributed to the early defence against root invasion by nematodes, especially for amaranth (Adss et al., 2021). In addition to the total phenolic content, PAL activity was also significantly higher in amaranth roots than in cucumber roots at the early stage of nematode parasitism (Figure 1g). The disruption of nematode chemotaxis plays a key role in suppressing RKN disease in intercropping systems (Abd-Elgawad, 2021; Chadfield et al., 2022). Together, these findings validated the capacity of intercropped amaranth to disturb M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber and infection. 

The chemotaxis of M. incognita is generally mediated by neuropeptides that are crucial for RKNs to sense host signals (Rengarajan & Hallem, 2016). Theoretical and experimental studies have indicated that genes encoding neuropeptides play a vital role in nematode chemotaxis and infection (Abad et al., 2008; Banakar et al., 2020; Rao et al., 2013). Similarly, the present study (Figure 3) revealed the vital function of flp-1 and flp-18 in the regulation of M. incognita chemotaxis towards cucumber roots and infection. The flp genes encode FMRFamide-like peptides that are essential to nematode infection, 

feeding, and reproduction (Atkinson et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2017; McCoy et al., 2017). The vital function of the flp-1 and flp-18 genes is confirmed by the fact that (1) the flp-1 and flp-18 transcript level changes of M. incognita treated with root exudates exhibited opposite trends between cucumber and amaranth (Figure 3b), (2) the M. incognita J2s separated from the intercropping side of the PBP experiment showed a significant decrease in the transcript levels of flp-1 and flp-18 as compared to monocropping (Figure 3a), and (3) RNAi of flp-1 and flp-18 delayed M. incognita development and inhibited M. incognita reproduction in cucumber roots (Figures 3e,f and S10). These findings are in agreement with several previous studies confirming the importance of flp-1 and flp-18 in regulating the chemotaxis and infection of M. incognita (Banakar et al., 2020) and Meloidogyne graminicola (Hada et al., 2020). It is noteworthy that RNAi of flp-1 caused the strongest inhibition of M. incognita chemotaxis (Figure 3d). Among the flp genes, flp-1 is one of the few that are universally found in nematodes (Buntschuh et al., 2018). Similarly, loss of the flp-1 gene in C. elegans can inhibit its movement and reduce its egg laying rate (Chang et al., 2015). 

Nematode infection resulted in significant metabolic variations in root tissues (Figure 5a,b). The resistant plants usually induce several metabolic alterations to disrupt the development of RKNs (Bell, 1981; Machado et al., 2012). The metabolic pathways enriched in the amaranth and cucumber root tissues were exactly opposite (Figure S11). Linoleic acid and $\alpha$ -linolenic acid metabolism were most strongly affected by M. incognita infection in cucumber roots (Figure S11). This pattern is frequently observed in plant immune systems (Pretorius et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). In contrast, the TCA cycle was the most enriched pathway in amaranth 

roots under M. incognita stress (Figure S13). The TCA cycle is a core pathway for the metabolism of sugars, lipids, and amino acids, all of which are essential for plant disease resistance (Bezrutczyk et al., 2018; Seifi et al., 2013). Particularly, isocitric acid, citric acid, oxoglutaric acid, and aconitic acid, all of which are TCA intermediates capable of inducing plant defence against pathogens (Balmer et al., 2013), were accumulated in amaranth root tissues after M. incognita infection (Figure S10). Similar accumulation of TCA intermediates was observed in the leaves of Brassica nigra attacked by caterpillars (Papazian et al., 2019). The different changes in root metabolic pathways may result in the different host status between amaranth and cucumber. Additionally, the enrichment of phenolic compounds (involved in plant defence towards nematode attack; Ohri & Pannu, 2010; Sharma et al., 2022) in M. incognitainfected amaranth roots (Figure 1g) also highlighted the importance of metabolic variations as a response to M. incognita stress. This can be further supported by the fact that overexpression of homologous genes involved in the biosynthesis of pantothenic acid and phloretin, both of which were enriched specifically in amaranth root tissues, delayed M. incognita development in cucumber hairy roots (Figure 1c–i). These resistance responses in amaranth roots could contribute to controlling RKN disease by affecting nematode development. Taken as a whole, these findings validated our first hypothesis, that is, RKN-induced metabolic variations in root tissues are crucial for weak host plants to inhibit nematode infection. 

Our results (Figures 5 and 6) confirmed our second hypothesis, that is, RKN-induced metabolic variations in rhizosphere soils of weak host plants play a vital role in disrupting nematode chemotaxis. Several previous studies have shown that a variety of metabolites are capable of repelling RKNs, such as fatty acids, terpenoids, and phenolic acids (Chitwood, 2002). In the present study, salicin and naringenin (enriched specifically in amaranth rhizosphere soils; Figures 5 and S14) suppressed the invasion of cucumber roots by M. incognita J2s (Figure 6). Salicin belongs to the salicinoids (phenolic glycosides), which act as defence compounds to mediate plant–parasite interactions in ecological environments (Mason et al., 2021). Similarly, herbivore attack also induced the enrichment of salicin in the leaves of poplar (Padmanaban et al., 2022). Naringenin is a phenolic substance that has been shown to inhibit the egg hatching of RKNs (Ohri & Pannu, 2010). In addition to the direct inhibitory effects on M. incognita infection, both salicin and naringenin reduced the transcript levels of flp-1 and flp-18 in M. incognita even at low concentrations (Figure 6c), further demonstrating the capacity of these two metabolites to disrupt M. incognita chemotaxis. 

In conclusion, our study demonstrates that when challenged by M. incognita, amaranth accumulates metabolites that are different from those accumulated by cucumber in both root tissues and rhizosphere soils in intercropping systems. Metabolic variations in amaranth rhizosphere soils (enrichment of naringenin and salicin in particular) result in a direct repellence to M. incognita, particularly by inhibiting the expression of the Mi-flp-1 and Mi-flp-18 

genes, both of which are crucial for the chemotaxis and infection of M. incognita. Furthermore, overexpression of genes encoding key enzymes involved in the biosynthesis of DMs enriched in amaranth root tissues (pantothenic acid and phloretin in particular) delayed M. incognita development in cucumber transgenic hairy roots. These findings emphasize that metabolic variations in root tissues and rhizosphere soils of weak host plants potently lead to distinct host status and chemotaxis regulation of M. incognita in intercropping. 

# 4  |  EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# 4.1  |  Plant materials, growth conditions, and nematodes

Cucumber (C. sativus ‘Xintaimici’) and amaranth (Amaranthus tricolor ‘Jingxian No. 1’) were used in this study. The cucumber and amaranth seeds were surface sterilized by soaking in $70 \%$ ethanol for 1 min and $3 \%$ NaOCl for $1 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ (Li et al., 2021). Cucumber seeds were placed on moist sterilized filter paper and put in the dark at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ until germination, while amaranth seeds were directly sown in soils. 

The soil used in this study was collected from a temperate deciduous forest site (Beijing, China; $4 0 ^ { \circ } 4 0 ^ { \prime } 9 8 ^ { \prime \prime } |$ , $1 1 5 ^ { \circ } 8 9 ^ { \prime } 7 0 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathsf { E } )$ ) and sterilized by $\gamma$ -irradiation to remove the interference of soil biota (for more details, see Li et al., 2021). It had a bulk density of $\phantom { + } 1 . 1 8 \phantom { + } \mathrm { g } /$ $\mathsf { c m } ^ { 3 }$ , an electrical conductivity of $0 . 1 8 \mathsf { m S / c m }$ , and a pH of 7.39, and it contained $2 2 . 6 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { k g }$ of organic matter, $0 . 8 6 \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { k g }$ of total N, $0 . 4 1 \mathrm { g } /$ kg of total P, and $8 . 8 7 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { k g }$ of total K. All seedlings were grown at day/night (16/8 h) temperatures of $2 5 / 1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ under approximately $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } { \cdot } \sigma ^ { - 1 }$ photosynthetically active radiation in a growth chamber (RXZ-1000D, Ningbo Jiangnan Instrument Factory). 

M. incognita was propagated and harvested on cucumber. Egg masses were collected, sterilized with $0 . 0 5 \%$ NaOCl for 3 min, and then washed with sterile water on a 500-mesh screen (Hussey, 1973). Further details of materials and methodology for nematodes are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.2  |  Evaluation of host status of cucumber and amaranth to M. incognita

To evaluate the host status of cucumber and amaranth to M. incognita, three separate experiments were conducted. In Expt. 1, we aimed to verify the efficiency of intercropping with amaranth in alleviating M. incognita infection in cucumber under field conditions. An M. incognita-invaded vegetable field (Beijing, China; $4 0 ^ { \circ } 0 2 ^ { \prime } 3 8 ^ { \prime \prime } |$ , $1 1 6 ^ { \circ } 2 8 ^ { \prime } 1 6 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathsf { E }$ ) was selected to conduct treatments including cucumber monocropping and cucumber–amaranth intercropping systems. The root gall index of cucumber roots, which is used to evaluate the RKN infection levels using a rating chart, were determined after the final harvest (Zeck, 1971). Further details of materials and methodology for Expt. 1 are provided in Text S1. 

In Expt. 2, we aimed to measure the developmental dynamics of M. incognita in cucumber and amaranth roots. Germinated seeds were sown using sterile tweezers in $5 \times 5 { \mathrm { c m } }$ pots filled with soil at a density of one cucumber seed or three amaranth seeds per pot. Plant samples were collected at 3, 7, 14, 21, 28, and 35 DAI. The number of M. incognita at different developmental stages (J2s, J3s, J4s, and females) in root tissues was recorded using the fuchsin staining method (Bybd et al., 1983), and the giant cell area was measured based on the histological analysis of root galls. Further details of materials and methodology for Expt. 2 are provided in Text S1. 

In Expt. 3, we aimed to examine the reproductive capacity of M. incognita in cucumber and amaranth roots. Germinated seeds were sown in $2 0 \times 3 0 \mathsf { c m }$ pots filled with soil and inoculated with $5 0 0 M$ . incognita pre-J2s. The root galls and egg masses of cucumber and amaranth were measured by the root fresh weight. Host susceptibility was evaluated based on a reproduction factor (Rf), which was calculated as the final egg counts extracted from total roots divided by the initial number of nematodes (500 M. incognita pre-J2s) inoculated to plants. To ensure the completion of the nematode life cycle, plants and soils were sampled at 42 and 56 DAI. We also used the soils collected at 42 and 56 DAI to replant cucumber in order to investigate the legacy effects of previous plant cultivation on the reproduction of M. incognita in cucumber. Further details of materials and methodology for Expt. 3 are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.3  |  Measurement of root physiological characteristics associated with M. incognita infection

We examined several root physiological characteristics of cucumber and amaranth during M. incognita parasitism in pot experiments. Plant roots (uninfected cucumber, Cs; nematode-infected cucumber, CsN; uninfected amaranth, Am; nematode-infected amaranth, AmN) were sampled at 3, 7, 14, 21, 28, and 35 DAI. The whole root system was dried, ground to powder, and sieved through a $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { - } \mathsf { m m }$ mesh. The contents of C and N in roots were determined by the dry combustion method (Dumas method) using a CN elemental analyser, while the root ionome profile was analysed using inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES). The total phenolic content was determined by the Folin–Ciocalteu method (Ainsworth & Gillespie, 2007). The activity of PAL in roots was measured using the method as described in Solekha et al. (2020). For each treatment at each sampling time, there were three biological replicates, with five pooled roots for each replicate. 

# 4.4  |  Histological analysis of root galls

For histological analysis, root galls of cucumber and amaranth were sampled at 7, 14, 21, 28, and 35 DAI and fixed overnight in FAA solution $50 \%$ ethanol, $5 \%$ acetic acid, and $4 \%$ formaldehyde in water). For each sampling time, at least eight representative galls of similar 

size from each individual plant were harvested. The samples were embedded in paraffin, sliced at a thickness of $6 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , and stained using aqueous toluidine blue $0 . 0 1 \%$ toluidine blue in $0 . 1 \%$ sodium tetraborate). The giant cell area was calculated to evaluate the feeding site development (Cabrera et al., 2014). 

# 4.5  |  Nematode attraction assay and travelling direction evaluation

To examine whether amaranth has the potential to regulate the chemotaxis and parasitism of M. incognita, we used a range of approaches including the one-choice attraction assay (Tsai et al., 2019), the dual-choice attraction assay (Shivakumara et al., 2018), and the PBP experiment (Dong et al., 2014). In the one-choice attraction assay, a root tip of cucumber or amaranth was put into a round Petri dish (7 cm diameter) full of $23 \%$ (wt/vol, mixed overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ ) Pluronic F-127 gel (Sigma-Aldrich). The Petri dish was divided into three zones: the RTZ, the nematode MZ, and the inoculation zone (IZ). First, $5 0 0 \mu \updownarrow \mathsf { F } \cdot 1 2 7$ gel containing $1 0 0 M$ . incognita J2s was placed into the IZ. At 8 h after inoculation, the M. incognita J2s were observed using a stereomicroscope and counted to compare the ratio of M. incognita J2s in three different zones. To quantify the attraction behaviour of M. incognita J2s towards root tips, the chemotaxis index was calculated as the number of M. incognita J2s in the RTZ divided by the total number of M. incognita J2s in all three zones. For each plant type treatment, there were six replicates with five root tips for each replicate. 

In the dual-choice attraction assay, root tips of cucumber and amaranth were simultaneously put into a square Petri dish $( 1 3 \times 1 3 \mathsf { c m } )$ to reflect the attractive/repellent features of root tips to M. incognita. Sterilized solid agar gel was poured onto the Petri dish and allowed to solidify. The solid agar was scooped out from the area between two parallel imaginary lines (each equidistant at $3 . 5 \mathsf { c m }$ from the centre line parallel to the Petri dish edge) and filled with $23 \%$ Pluronic F-127 gel to create an M. incognita IZ and MZ. The solid agar retained in each side of the Petri dish was regarded as the RTZs. The number of M. incognita J2s in each area was counted and divided by the total of M. incognita J2s inoculated onto the Petri dish. For each treatment, there were six replicates with five square Petri dishes for each replicate. 

In the PBP experiment, we aimed to evaluate the effects of intercropping and monocropping on M. incognita chemotaxis. The PBP experimental system consisted of two plastic pots $1 0 \mathsf { c m }$ diameter) filled with sterilized soil, which were connected by a PVC tube (3 cm diameter and $1 5 \mathrm { c m }$ length; the lower half was filled with sterilized soil) covered with a 7-mm mesh plastic net at both ends. There was a hole (1 cm diameter) used to inoculate nematodes in the middle of the PVC tube. There were two combinations, including monocropping–monocropping (C/C–C/C) and monocropping–intercropping (C/C–C/A). For each combination, there were four replicates with three square PBP devices for each replicate. On day 14 after nematode inoculation, the number of M. 

incognita and root galls was counted from the individual cucumber plant. Further details of materials and methodology for the PBP experiment are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.6  |  Analysis of Mi-flp gene expression pattern

In order to collect M. incognita at different developmental stages (pre-infective J2s, infective J2s, J3s, and J4s/females), cucumber seedings with two true leaves were inoculated with 10,000 M. incognita pre-J2s and roots with galls were harvested at 3, 7, 14, and 28 DAI. Different developmental stages of M. incognita were separated from galls using the enzymatic digestion method (Araya & Caswell-Chen, 1993) and then used to analyse the relative expression pattern of Mi-flp genes including flp-1, 7, 12, 14, 16, and 18. Four biological replicates were included for gene expression analysis at each developmental stage. 

To investigate the effects of monocropping and intercropping on the expression of Mi-flp genes, total RNA was extracted from M. incognita J2s, which were separated from the PBP experiment, to analyse the relative expression pattern of Mi-flp genes, including flp-1, 7, 12, 14, 16, and 18. Furthermore, in order to explore the direct effects of cucumber and amaranth root exudates on Mi-flp gene expression, cucumber and amaranth plants were grown in sand/vermiculite mix (1:1, vol/vol). Further details of materials and methodology for collection of root exudates are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.7  |  Analysis of Mi-flp gene functions using in vitro RNAi

dsRNAs of Mi-flp genes were synthesized and purified with a T7 RNAi Transcription Kit (Vazyme) according to the instructions of the manufacturer using linearized plasmid templates. Two separate clones having the same target sequence of these flp genes were in opposite orientations. The pGEM-3Zf(−) vector served as a template for in vitro transcription for dsRNA synthesis (Promega). The primers for construction of the pGEM-3Zf(−) vector are listed in Table S1. 

For silencing of Mi-flp genes, about 10,000 freshly hatched and sterilized J2s were immersed in $1 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ Mi-flp dsRNA in soaking buffer with $0 . 1 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ FITC and $3 0 \mathsf { m M }$ octopamine. FITC was used as a tracer to assess the J2 uptake efficiency of dsRNA. Green fluorescent protein (GFP) dsRNA was set as a control, and the synthesis method followed the process described above. Soaked J2s were incubated for $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ in the dark at room temperature on a rotator for dsRNA digestion. Three independent experiments were performed. 

Total RNA was extracted from 5000 J2s that were soaked for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ in soaking buffer with 1 mg/mL Mi-flp dsRNA, soaking buffer with $1 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ gfp dsRNA, or soaking buffer alone. The converted first-strand cDNA was used as a template for quantitative PCR (qPCR). The primers for qPCR are listed in Table S2. 

To evaluate the RNAi effects on nematode chemotaxis and infection, the one-choice attraction assay (for more details, see the 

Nematode attraction assay section) was conducted with nematodes treated with dsRNA towards cucumber root tips. The $5 \times 5 { \mathrm { c m } }$ pot experiment (for more details see Expt. 2) was conducted to evaluate the effects of dsRNA treatment on nematode growth on cucumber roots. Root galls, egg masses, and the number of M. incognita at different developmental stages were counted at 14, 28, and 35 DAI. For each treatment, there were three biological replicates with five independent root tips for each replicate. 

# 4.8  |  Untargeted metabolomics analysis

To measure M. incognita-induced metabolic variations in the root tissues and rhizosphere soils of cucumber and amaranth, we conducted a pot experiment that had four treatments: (1) mock-inoculated cucumber (Cs), (2) M. incognita-inoculated cucumber (CsN), (3) mockinoculated amaranth (Am), and (4) M. incognita-inoculated amaranth (AmN). In each pot, one cucumber seedling or five amaranth seedlings were planted. For M. incognita inoculation, 100 M. incognita pre-J2s were inoculated per pot when cucumber seedlings had a true leaf or amaranth seedlings had three true leaves. Root tissues and rhizosphere soils of cucumber and amaranth were collected at 14 DAI to perform untargeted metabolomics analysis. Further details of materials and methodology for untargeted metabolomics analysis are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.9  |  Selection of candidate metabolites and regulatory genes associated with M. incognita infection

To set up an M. incognita-triggered candidate metabolite list, the DMs were selected from all the datasets (AmNR_AmR, CsNR_CsR, $\mathsf { A m N S \_ A m S }$ , and CsNS_CsS; R: root tissues, S: rhizosphere soils). On the basis of the combination of a statistically significant threshold of variable importance of projection (VIP) values obtained from the PLS-DA model and $p$ values from a two-tailed Student's t test on the normalized peak areas, metabolites with ${ \mathsf { V } } { \mathsf { I P } } { > } 1 . 0$ and $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ were considered as DMs. 

KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was performed based on the DMs in cucumber and amaranth root tissues under M. incognita stress. To explore whether the up-regulated enriched pathways of amaranth can affect M. incognita parasitism in cucumber, the KEGG pathways in which homologous genes in the cucumber genome database (CuGenDB, http://cucurbitgenomics.org/) are involved were screened. 

The candidate metabolites (naringenin and salicin), enriched specifically in amaranth rhizosphere soils (datasets AmNS_AmS), were selected to measure their effects on the relative expression of Mi-flp genes, M. incognita egg hatching, and M. incognita infection in cucumber roots. Further details of materials and methodology for the measurement of effects of candidate metabolites on M. incognita disease in cucumber are provided in Text S1. 

# 4.10  |  Cucumber hairy root transformation using Rhizobium rhizogenes

Cucumber hairy root transformation was performed using R. rhizogenes K599 as detailed in Zhang et al. (2023). A promoter region of about 2000 bp of CsPTS (CsaV3_3G028840) and CsCHS (CsaV3_3G027830) was amplified and cloned into the pCAMBIA 1391-GUS vector. The CsPTS and CsCHS overexpression vectors were constructed using the pCAMBIA 1305 vector carrying a GFP tag to screen the transgenic hair roots. All primers for amplification are shown in Tables S3–S5. GUS staining of promoter:GUS transgenic hairy roots was performed using a GUS staining kit (Aolaibo). Hairy roots expressing GFP were easily visible under a fluorescence stereomicroscope (M165 FC; Leica). 

# 4.11  |  Gene expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted from nematode and root samples using the Eastep Super Total RNA Extraction Kit according to the manufacturer's protocol including DNase treatment (Promega). First-strand cDNA was synthesized from $7 5 0 \mathsf { n g }$ RNA using the HiScript II 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit following the manufacturer's protocol (Vazyme). qPCR was carried out with SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (Vazyme) in an ABI 6500 Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems). Relative target gene expression was analysed using the comparative $C _ { \mathrm { t } }$ method. M. incognita actin (accession BE225475) and CsTUA (accession Csa4G000580) were taken as reference genes for the normalization of gene expression. Three technical replicates were performed for each of the samples during qPCR analysis. 

# 4.12  |  Statistical analyses

Besides the methods for metabolomic analysis described above, all statistical analyses (one-way analysis of variance and Student's t test applied when appropriate, as indicated in the figure legends) were conducted using SPSS v. 22.0. PCA was conducted to assess how root physiological characteristics and metabolites could be partitioned into variation attributable to M. incognita treatments and plant types.
