# ABSTRACT

The “salt tree”, Rhus chinensis, holds significant economic and medicinal value due to its ability to produce Galla chinensis (Chinese gall/gallnut), a plant-derived medicinal material used in both traditional Chinese and modern medicine that is rich in tannins and flavonoids. It is also renowned for its remarkable stress tolerance. However, the genetic basis underlying its tannin and flavonoid biosynthesis and stress adaptation remains largely unexplored. Here, we assembled a chromosome-level genome of R. chinensis with a size of 357.62 Mb. A significant expansion of defence-related genes, particularly those involved in chitin catabolism and flavonoid biosynthesis, explains the tree's extensive environmental adaptability. We identified key genes involved in tannin biosynthesis and hydrolysis, with RcTA1 playing a central role in gallic acid accumulation, a precursor of hydrolyzable tannins. Notably, RcDIV1 promotes tannin hydrolysis by directly activating RcTA1 transcription. Additionally, we uncovered that well-developed multicellular glandular trichomes, regulated by RcGL2, along with an expanded array of transporters (e.g., ABCGs) and an enhanced ABA response, play critical roles in mediating salt tolerance. These factors collectively drive the production of salt-like secretions, including phenolic and organic acids, which coat the fruit surface. Our study provides profound insights into the genetic mechanisms governing abundant tannin accumulation, flavonoid biosynthesis, glandular trichome development, and stress resilience, offering valuable genetic resources for improving the medicinal and ecological traits of this species.

# 1 | Introduction

Rhus chinensis Mill. (Anacardiaceae), commonly known as “Chinese sumac”, “nutgall tree”, and “salt tree”, is an important representative of the genus Rhus, which comprises over 250 individual species that are widely distributed in the temperate and tropical regions worldwide (Djakpo and Yao  2010; Liang, Zhang, et al. 2019; Shi et al. 2019). This deciduous tree features distinctive odd-pinnately compound leaves that transition from brownish-red in spring to bright red in autumn (Figure 1B–D), along with creamy-white flowers (Figure  1A–E) and clustered seeded fruits coated with white crystalline substances (Figure  1F–G), resembling salt crystals. Moreover, its exceptional stress tolerance, rapid growth, and root tillering capacity enable it to thrive as a pioneer species in calcareous, alkalisaline, and barren soils (Zhou et al. 2017). 

Beyond its ecological value, R. chinensis is well-known forits traditional and modern therapeutic uses. Various tissues(leaves, bark, and stems) are employed as medicinal materi-als for treating coughs, fevers, malaria, and dysentery (Tianet  al.  2009; Qiu et  al.  2014; Rymbai et  al.  2016). The seedsand fruits contain polyunsaturated fatty acids and flavo-noids, offering antioxidant, antitumor, and antibacterialbenefits (Djakpo and Yao  2010; Wang et  al.  2006; Chen andChen  2011). Notably, its gallnuts, Galla chinensis (GAC, 五倍子), form via aphid (Melaphis chinensis) parasitism onyoung leaves or branches (Figure 1G). These galls are abun-dant in gallotannins and other medicinally active compounds(Tian et  al.  2009) and serve as a primary industrial sourcefor gallotannins and gallic acids (GAs) (Tian et  al.  2009;Heirangkhongjam and Ngaseppam  2018; Ren et  al.  2021).Despite the plant's economic and therapeutic importance, thegenetic mechanisms responsible for its bioactive compoundsremain largely uncharacterized.

Trichomes, hair-like epidermal outgrowths on aerial tissues, are classified as glandular or non-glandular based on their morphology and secretion (Huchelmann et  al.  2017). Glandular trichomes typically feature basal, stalk, and apical cells specialized for synthesizing, storing, and releasing metabolites (Tissier 2012; Schuurink and Tissier 2020). Capitate trichomes, the predominant glandular type, include a secretory head supported by a stalk (Huchelmann et al. 2017). They protect against herbivores, pathogens, and abiotic stresses such as UV, drought, salinity, and intense light (Wagner et al. 2004). For example, in Conyza blinii, UV-B induces increased trichome density and elevated secondary metabolites (Zhan et al. 2024), while Solanum habrochaites secretes sesquiterpenes for herbivore defense (Glas et al. 2012). Glandular trichomes also influence ion transport (e.g., $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ , $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ ) under environmental stress (Huchelmann et al. 2017; Smaoui et al. 2011; Yuan et al. 2022). In R. chinensis, dense glandular trichomes produce a fascinating salt-like crystal layer on its fruit surface (Figure 1G), but the developmental and genetic bases of these trichomes remain unknown. 

To unravel the genetic basis of gallotannin, GA, and flavonoid biosynthesis, glandular trichome development, and stress tolerance in this “salt tree,” we generated a chromosome-level genome assembly of R. chinensis. We identified expanded gene 

families and validated their potential roles in gallic acid and flavonoid production, uncovering an MYB regulatory module for tannin hydrolysis. We further investigated the responses of flavonoid biosynthesis and chitinase genes to various abiotic stresses. Finally, we identified and profiled candidate genes associated with glandular trichome development, secretion, and ion/metabolite transport in R. chinensis. Our findings provide valuable genomic insights into the biosynthesis of bioactive compounds in galls, as well as the mechanisms underlying glandular trichome development and stress adaptation. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Genome Sequencing, De Novo Assembly, and Annotation

We sequenced and assembled the genome of Rhus chinensis using a combination of Illumina HiSeq short reads, PacBio Sequel continuous long reads (CLR), Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) long reads, and Hi-C scaffolding data. A K-mer analysis $( K = 1 7 )$ ) of 39.2 Gb Illumina paired-end reads $2 \times 1 5 0$ bp; $1 1 8 \times$ coverage) estimated a genome size of 339.97 Mb, with $1 . 1 3 \%$ heterozygosity and $5 5 . 9 4 \%$ repetitive sequences (Figure  S1). For de novo assembly, we generated 18.9 Gb of PacBio CLR data $_ { ( 5 6 . 9 3 \times }$ coverage) and $4 0 . 5 \ \mathrm { G b }$ of ONT reads ${ \mathrm { 1 0 3 . 7 } } \times$ coverage). Independent assemblies were constructed from each long-read dataset (PacBio, ONT) and the Illumina short-read dataset (Tables S1–S3). These assemblies were integrated using HABOT2, followed by polishing with Illumina reads via Pilon to enhance base accuracy. The final assembly spans 357.61 Mb with a contig N50 of 7.9 Mb (Table S4). 

Karyotyping identified 15 chromosome pairs $2 n = 2 \mathrm { X } = 3 0$ , Figure  1I), and this was further confirmed by fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) with the telomeric probe pATt4, which clearly labelled both ends of each of the 30 chromosomes in R. chinensis (Figure  1J). Hi-C-based scaffolding yielded a final genome of 357.62 Mb with a scaffold N50 of 22.38 Mb (Table S5). $9 0 . 3 0 \%$ of the genome was anchored onto 15 pseudo-chromosomes (Figure  1K), with lengths between 15.93 Mb and 28.81 Mb (Figure  S2). Merqury analysis estimated a consensus accuracy of $Q \mathrm { V } = 2 8 . 1 7$ , and BUSCO analysis showed that $9 5 . 9 \%$ and $9 1 . 1 \%$ of core embryophyte genes were complete at the genome and protein levels, respectively (Table S6). Read coverage analysis showed that an average of $9 0 . 9 \%$ of independent Illumina short reads aligned to the final assembly (Table  S7). Additionally, chromosomal collinearity analysis revealed one-to-one correspondence between R. chinensis and closely related species Pistacia vera and Pistacia chinensis subsp. integerrima (hereafter, P. integerrima) (Figure S3A). These metrics affirmed the high quality of the R. chinensis genome assembly. 

A total of 189.98 Mb, constituting $5 3 . 1 7 \%$ of the assembled R. chinensis genome, was identified as non-redundant repetitive sequences (Figure 1K and Table S8). Retrotransposons accounted for $4 7 . 8 3 \%$ of the genome, with long terminal repeats (LTRs) comprising $4 7 . 2 3 \%$ and non-LTRs $0 . 6 0 \%$ $0 . 5 6 \%$ LINE and $0 . 0 4 \%$ SINE). # 2.2 | Phylogenetic and Comparative Genome Analysis Highlights Metabolite- and Defence-Related Gene Expansions

We examined the evolutionary relationships of R. chinensis with other plant genomes, including representatives from the basal angiosperms, monocotyledons, and dicotyledons (see Materials and Methods). In total, 30 910 gene families were clustered across all 17 species. Among the 16 310 gene families identified in four species of the Sapindales, 514 R. chinensis-specific orthogroups (gene families; i.e., orthologous clusters absent from the 4 comparator species) were identified, while 10 162 were shared (Figure S3B). Phylogenetic analysis based on 132 singlecopy gene families supported the placement of R. chinensis with P. vera and S. birrea in the Anacardiaceae (Figure S3C). Further analysis indicated that R. chinensis diverged from P. vera, S. birrea, and C. sinensis approximately 14.6, 38.6, and 58.9 million years ago (Mya), respectively. 

We investigated whole genome duplication (WGD) events in R. chinensis using synonymous substitution rates (Ks) of 5379 paralogous gene pairs. A peak at Ks 1.5–1.7 was observed, corresponding to the WGD events in C. sinensis and P. vera (Figure S3D). This suggested that R. chinensis shared an ancient WGD event (referred to as Ad-γ in eudicots) with C. sinensis (Xu et  al.  2013) and P. vera. This was also further confirmed by genomic syntenic analysis by revealing 1:2 syntenic patterns within the R. chinensis genome (1806 gene pairs) (Figure S4). 

Additionally, we identified 482 gene families (1848 genes) that significantly expanded in R. chinensis (Figure S3C). Functional annotation indicated enrichment in secondary metabolite biosynthesis, phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, ABC transporters, and fatty acid metabolism (Figure S5 and Table S12). Notably, defence-related genes were highly enriched in pathways such as biotic stimulus response (GO:0009607), defence response (GO:0006952), and chitin catabolism (GO:0006032, $p { < } 0 . 0 0 1 $ ), suggesting an enhanced resistance role for these genes. Conversely, 1682 gene families (2359 genes) showed contractions in R. chinensis, with significant enrichment in functions related to zeatin biosynthesis, photosynthesis–antenna proteins, and monoterpenoid biosynthesis (Table S13). 

# 2.3 | Expansion of Chitinase and Flavonoid Biosynthesis Gene Families Facilitates Stress Defence

Chitinases, classified as glycosyl hydrolases (GH), are key players in plant defense against pathogens and abiotic stresses like low temperatures, salinity, and drought. In the R. chinensis genome, we identified 53 chitinases, categorized into GH18 (class V, III) and GH19 (class I, II, and IV). This number exceeds those in other species, including A. thaliana, C. sinensis, X. sorbifolium, S. birrea, and P. vera (Figure S6A,B and Table S14), indicating an expansion, particularly in classes II and IV of GH19. This expanded chitinase family is common in Anacardiaceae species, including S. birrea, P. vera, and R. chinensis (Figure S6B,C). The results of gene gain/loss analysis and extensive tandem duplications (Figure  S6C) confirmed this expansion (especially in class IV), with over $6 0 \%$ $( K s \leq 1 . 0 )$ of chitinase gene pairs undergoing recent duplications (Figure S6D). In addition, microsynteny analysis revealed that the tandem chitinase gene clusters on Chromosome 10 of R. chinensis show 2:1 collinearity with their orthologs in closely related Anacardiaceae species (P. integerrima, P. vera, and T. vernicifluum), indicating evolutionary conservation of these clusters (Figure S6E). 

RNA-Seq analysis of six tissues (root, seed, leaf, flower, young fruit, mature fruit) showed GH19 chitinase genes are highly expressed in young and mature fruit, roots, and flowers, while GH18 genes display generally low transcript levels in all tissues except roots and leaves (Figure  S7A,B and Table  S15). Cis-regulatory elements related to stress, hormones, and light responses were abundant in chitinase genes, suggesting their involvement in environmental stress responses (Figure S8A,B). Indeed, chitinase gene expression showed distinct responses to abiotic stresses. Most were downregulated under heat and drought but upregulated under salt stress, with certain GH18 class III genes showing increased expression under high salinity (Figure S6F and Table S15). We further performed time-course qRT-PCR on six representative genes from the expanded class II, IV, and V subfamilies in R. chinensis seedlings subjected to heat shock $( 4 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ or salt stress (400 mM NaCl) for $_ { 0 - 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ . As shown in Figure  S9, all selected genes were rapidly and strongly induced by at least one treatment. For example, RcChiC (YFM011904) and RcCTL1 (YFM002752) reached $> 1 5 \cdot$ - and ${ > } 2 5$ -fold higher transcript levels, respectively, after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of salt exposure, whereas YFM002510 and YFM002392 peaked within $_ { 3 - 6 \mathrm { h } }$ of heat stress. Accordingly, we selected two chitinase genes (RcChiC and RcCTL1) for functional validation. Using an Agrobacterium-mediated transient assay in R. chinensis leaf discs, overexpression of either gene improved tolerance to $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ heat (2 d) and 400 mM NaCl (3 d), accompanied by elevated CAT/ 

POD activities and reduced ROS accumulation $\mathrm { ( H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , O •−, and its production rate) (Figure S10A–I). Consistently, yeast expressing RcChiC or RcCTL1 showed markedly higher survival after $5 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ heat shock (4 h) and 5 M NaCl (3 d), with ~2- to ${ \sim } 3 . 5$ -fold increases in CFUs under heat and ~1.1–1.4-fold increases under salt relative to empty-vector controls (Figure  S11). Together, these results demonstrate that RcChiC and RcCTL1 enhance tolerance to heat and salinity and support the conclusion that the expanded chitinase family contributes significantly to the stress resilience of R. chinensis. 

Additionally, we found an expansion in genes involved in phenylalanine biosynthesis, flavonoid biosynthesis, and fatty acid metabolism in R. chinensis. Notably, the putative phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) and chalcone synthase (CHS) gene families exhibited prominent expansions, with R. chinensis containing 12 putative PAL and 28 CHS genes, significantly higher than those in A. thaliana, X. sorbifolium, S. birrea, and C. sinensis (Figure  2A, red star). These expansions, underlain by tandem duplications, are especially pronounced in the Anacardiaceae family (Figure 2B and Table S16). Phylogenetic analysis grouped PAL and CHS genes into three and four clades, respectively (Figure S12). Among the putative PAL genes, 8 out of 12 in group III formed a gene cluster with recent tandem duplications $( K s < 0 . 3 )$ in Chromosome 14 (Figure  2C). Several PAL genes showed strong synteny with genes from other species in the Anacardiaceae, Sapindaceae, and Rutaceae families (Figure  2D). Two PAL genes (YFM022723 and YFM022722) underwent recent tandem duplications $( K s < 0 . 2 7 )$ , forming a cluster of eight PAL genes, contributing significantly to the expansion of the PAL family in R. chinensis. Similarly, 22 out of 28 putative CHS genes exhibited recent tandem duplications on Chromosome 10 $( K s < 0 . 3 6 )$ (Figure 2C), with multi-species collinearity analysis showing strong conservation with orthologs in closely related species (Figure 2E). Notably, the three gene clusters formed by these five CHS genes and their tandem duplicates have significantly contributed to the expansion of the CHS gene family in R. chinensis, highlighting their potential significance in flavonoid biosynthesis. 

Gene expression analysis revealed that most PAL (groups II and III) and CHS (groups II and III) genes were highly expressed in the root, flower, and fruit (Figure 2F and Table S17), correlating with higher flavonoid content in these tissues (Figure S13A). In response to abiotic stresses, most PAL genes were upregulated under salt and drought stress but downregulated under heat stress (Figure S14A), while certain CHS genes were upregulated under salt and drought stress (Figure S14B). To further explore the functional roles of the PAL and CHS genes, we selected two genes (YFM22723 and YFM002893), which exhibited high expression in various organs and stress conditions, for subcellular localization and transgenic validation using tobacco leaf transformation (Figure S13B). The results showed that both RcPAL and RcCHS were localised in the cell nucleus and membrane (Figure  2G), and transient overexpression of either gene significantly increased total flavonoid content (Figure 2H). Yeast sensitivity assays showed that expression of RcPAL or RcCHS approximately doubled survival after a $5 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ heat shock (4 h) and increased survival by ~1.7–2.0-fold after exposure to 5 M NaCl for 3 d, relative to empty-vector controls $\cdot p < 0 . 0 1$ , Figure  2I). Consistent with these results, overexpression in tobacco leaves 

conferred greater tolerance than EV to heat $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 36 h) and salt (300 mM NaCl, 24 h) (Figure S15A–D), as evidenced by higher CAT and POD activities and lower $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } { \bullet } ^ { - }$ accumulation together with a reduced $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } { \bullet } ^ { - }$ production rate (Figure S15E–I). These data support roles for RcPAL and RcCHS in flavonoid biosynthesis and abiotic-stress defence. 

# 2.4 | Duplicated Tannase Genes Regulate Tannin Biosynthesis and Hydrolysis in Galla Chinensis

Galla chinensis (GAC), a critical raw material for both traditional Chinese medicine and modern medicine (Tian et  al.  2009), is formed by aphids parasitizing on the leaves or petioles of $R$ . chinensis. LC–MS/MS profiling of freshly harvested GAC identified 1003 metabolites, of which phenolic acids (203, $2 0 . 2 \% )$ ), flavonoids (184, $1 8 . 3 \%$ ), and lipids (124, $1 2 . 4 \%$ ) constituted the three largest metabolite classes (Figures  S16A–D and S17A,B; Table S18). Within this data set, citric acid and gallic acid displayed the highest ion intensities, marking them as the predominant bioactive constituents detected. Additionally, 21 tannin compounds were detected, with ellagic acid (hydrolyzable tannins, HTs) being the predominant compound, followed by ellagic acid-4-O-glucoside, isocorilagin, and procyanidin (condensed tannins, CTs) (Figure S17C). To quantify the content of the main bioactive components (tannins and GA) in GAC, we performed HPLC experiments with standards and confirmed that GAC indeed possesses higher tannin compounds (consisting of HTs and CTs) and GAs compared to leaves (Figure S17D,E). 

Plant tannins, mainly classified as condensed tannins (CTs, proanthocyanidins) and hydrolyzable tannins (HTs, gallotannins and ellagitannins) (Smeriglio et  al.  2017; Dai et  al.  2020), are synthesized via the shikimate pathway, with GA playing a key role in HT biosynthesis. We identified four shikimate dehydrogenases (SDHs), 151 glycosyltransferases (UGTs), and three tannases (TAs) involved in tannin biosynthesis and hydrolysis in R. chinensis (Figure 3A,B and Figure S18A; Table S19). The four RcSDH genes show strong collinearity with orthologs in closely related species and V. vinifera, indicating evolutionary conservation (Figure S19A). Gene gain and loss analysis revealed no SDH family expansion in Anacardiaceae species (Figure S19B), but gene clusters were more common in species rich in GA (Figure  S19C and Table  S20), suggesting clustered SDH genes may enhance GA accumulation. The three RcTA genes exhibit conserved motifs and tandem duplications on Chromosome 15 (Figure  S20), with RcTA2 and RcTA3 likely originating from RcTA1 (Figure 3C). Notably, species with high HT content have tandemly duplicated TA genes, suggesting their potential role in HT biosynthesis (Figure 3D and Table S20). In addition, the 151 UGT genes were classified into 21 groups, with higher expression in GAC than leaves for classes B, G, I, J, K, L, and O (Figure S18B,C).

# 2.5 | RcDIV1 Promotes the Hydrolysis of Tannins by Activating the Transcription of RcTA1

Previous studies have revealed that MYB transcription factors (TFs) can participate in the regulation of the synthesis of GA and HTs derived from the shikimate pathway (Maeda and Dudareva 2012; Ye et al. 2024). We constructed a regulatory network correlating transcription factors with metabolites and discovered that many MYB TFs exhibit significant correlations with the content of GA and tannic acid in R. chinensis (Figure  S24A,B and Table  S21). Analysis of the RcTA1 promoter sequence revealed the presence of multiple MYB binding sites (MBS) (Figure  4A). By integrating the expression of MYB genes with the construction of phylogenetic trees (with potential functional genes) (Figure S20B,C), three MYBs [YFM017464 (RcDIV1), YFM023616 (RcDIV2), and YFM023160 (RcDIV3)] were identified that are highly expressed in GAC relative to leaves (Table S21). Yeast one-hybrid experiments confirmed that only RcDIV3 can directly bind to the MBS (“TAACCAGTCGTG” at ~ −959 to −971 bp) from the RcTA1 promoter (RcTA1-1, −1559 bp to −807 bp) (Figure  4B 

and Figure S25A–C). Further dual-luciferase assays in tobacco leaves and LUC assays in Arabidopsis protoplasts showed that co-expression of RcDIV1 increased RcTA1 promoter (RcTA1-1) activity 2.3-fold relative to the promoter-only control (RcTA1- 1::LUC, $p < 0 . 0 1$ , Figure 4C,D). Subcellular localization assays revealed that RcDIV1 is localized to the nuclear membrane (Figure S21A), a result consistent with the observed localization of the RcTA1 gene (Figure  3G). Furthermore, transient overexpression of RcDIV1 in both R. chinensis and N. benthamiana leaves upregulated RcTA1 and NbTA1, respectively, and increased GA accumulation in the infiltrated tissue of tobacco (Figure  S26B,C). These findings indicate RcDIV1 could promote the production of GA and hydrolysable tannins through its binding to the RcTA1 promoter and activating its transcription.

# 2.7 | Glandular Trichome Contributes to Ion/ Metabolite Secretion and Transport, and Salinity Adaptation

Glandular trichomes on the epidermis of R. chinensis play key roles in ion and metabolite transport (Huchelmann et al. 2017; Yuan et al. 2022). LC–MS/MS profiling of glandular pericarp secretions annotated 392 metabolites. Based on metabolite counts, flavonoids $( 1 8 \%$ , 77), phenolic acids $( 1 7 \%$ , 75), lipids $( 1 4 \%$ , 64), and amino acid derivatives $( 1 2 \%$ , 52) constituted the four largest classes, whereas hydrolysable tannins represented only $0 . 0 3 \%$ (11) of the total compounds detected (Figure  6A). Elemental analysis showed elevated concentrations of N, Cl, S, and Ca, while K and Na levels were lower, consistent across pericarp and kernel tissues (Figure 6B). 

To identify genes involved in the transport of crystalline substances on the fruit surface, RNA-Seq analysis was performed on leaf and pericarp tissues. This revealed significant enrichment of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) involved in ion transport, including anion transport, metal ion transport, and substrate-specific transporter activity (Figure  6C). # 3 | Discussion

In this study, we assembled a high-quality chromosome-level genome of R. chinensis. While previous studies have explored the evolutionary relationships within the Anacardiaceae family 

and the Sapindales order using chloroplast and nuclear DNA sequences (Liang, Zhang, et al. 2019; Zuo et al. 2019; Lee et al. 2016), the precise phylogenetic positions of Anacardiaceae, Rutaceae, and Sapindaceae within the Sapindales order remain uncertain. Notably, several chromosome-level genomes—such as C. sinensis (Rutaceae) (Xu et al. 2013), S. birrea (Anacardiaceae) (Chang et al. 2019), P. vera (Anacardiaceae) (Zeng et al. 2019), and X. sorbifolium (Sapindaceae) (Liang, Li, et  al.  2019)—have advanced our understanding of phylogenetic relationships and gene expansions/contractions in these lineages. Our comparative genome analysis showed that X. sorbifolium (Sapindaceae) diverged from Anacardiaceae at approximately 65.0 Mya, earlier than the 58.9 Mya split between C. sinensis (Rutaceae) and Anacardiaceae, suggesting that Rutaceae may be more closely related to Anacardiaceae than Sapindaceae. Within Anacardiaceae, P. vera and R. chinensis diverged around 14.6 Mya, more recently than the 38.6 Mya divergence between R. chinensis and S. birrea, indicating a closer relationship between Pistacia and Rhus. Syntenic analyses revealed no lineage-specific WGD in R. chinensis after the eudicot-wide γ hexaploidization (Jaillon et al. 2007; Chen et al. 2018), consistent with findings in C. sinensis and P. vera. Population history analysis suggests that R. chinensis did not undergo a post–last glacial maximum expansion but rather persisted in situ (Liang, Zhang, et al. 2019). Despite this, R. chinensis exhibits a notable adaptability to harsh environments, evidenced by the expansion of gene families involved in defence-related secondary metabolism, phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, ABC transporters, chitin catabolism, and fatty acid biosynthesis. These expanded gene families may contribute to the enhanced resistance of R. chinensis to various stresses. 

Chitin, composed of $\beta$ -(1,4)-linked N-acetyl-glucosamine units, is a primary fungal cell wall component but is absent in plants (Moussian 2019; Chen et al. 2022). Nevertheless, plants synthesise chitinases—pathogenesis-related (PR) proteins—to protect against pathogens (e.g., fungal infections) and various stresses (e.g., temperature fluctuations, high salinity, wounding) (Chen et al. 2018; Salzer et al. 2000; Su et al. 2014; Cheng et al. 2021). In this study, we identified 53 chitinase genes in R. chinensis, substantially more than in related species, suggesting a pronounced expansion of this gene family. This expansion, particularly within the GH19 subfamily (classes II and IV), is also observed in other Anacardiaceae species, including Pistacia vera and Sclerocarya birrea. Our analyses indicate that tandem duplication primarily underlies this expansion, with Ks-based divergence times pointing to recent duplications following the WGD in R. chinensis. The expansion of chitinase genes in phylogenetically related species (e.g., P. vera) further highlights their contribution to stress adaptation. Notably, RcChiC and RcCTL1 were identified as stress-response candidates based on expression patterns, and both yeast sensitivity assays and transient overexpression in the native R. chinensis system confirmed that they enhance tolerance to salinity and heat, demonstrating their potential roles in stress resilience. 

Flavonoids, widespread polyphenolic compounds in fruits, vegetables, and other crops, enhance human health and plant defence against both biotic (e.g., herbivores, bacterial infections) and abiotic (e.g., UV radiation, salinity) stressors (Shen et al. 2022; Zeng et al. 2024; Du et al. 2024). Rhus species are notably rich in flavonoids (Chen and Chen 2011; Sharma et al. 2015), and their extracts exhibit strong antioxidant and free-radical-scavenging activities (Lee et  al.  2002; You et  al.  2013; Liu et  al.  2019). Consistent with previous findings (Chen and Chen  2011), we detected abundant flavonoids in R. chinensis, especially in fruit tissues. However, the molecular basis underlying flavonoid biosynthesis in Rhus remains poorly characterised. Here, we identified a significant tandem expansion of phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) and chalcone synthase (CHS) genes, suggesting that increased gene copy numbers contribute to R. chinensis's high flavonoid content. Similar CHS duplications have been detected in other Anacardiaceae, including mango, where they participate in urushiol and phenol biosynthesis (Wang et al. 2020), as well as in pistachio (Zeng et al. 2019), implying a widespread role of these duplications in phenolic accumulation within the family. Transgenic assays confirmed that RcPAL and RcCHS promote flavonoid biosynthesis, while expression analyses revealed that PAL and CHS genes are induced under salt, heat, and drought stress. Yeast sensitivity assays and transient overexpression in tobacco leaves further demonstrated that RcCHS and RcPAL enhance tolerance to salt and heat. Together, these results indicate that the elevated flavonoid production driven by the expanded PAL and CHS families contributes to the stress resilience of R. chinensis. 

Galla chinensis (Chinese gallnuts) is rich in tannins and has long been used in traditional medicine for treating inflammation, dysentery, and toxicosis (Tian et al. 2009; Ren et al. 2021). Although gallnuts from Rhus species exhibit remarkable antioxidant and antibacterial properties (Tian et al. 2009; Heirangkhongjam and Ngaseppam  2018), the overall metabolic profiles of fresh gallnuts and the mechanisms governing tannin (gallotannin and ellagitannin) biosynthesis and hydrolysis in R. chinensis remain largely unexplored. Our results showed that phenolic acids and flavonoids are the most abundant compound classes, with gallic acid (GA) emerging as the primary bioactive phenolic acid in gallnuts. Interestingly, even though gallnuts form due to aphid parasitism on young leaves or petioles, both tannins and GA also accumulate—albeit at lower levels—in normal leaves, indicating that leaves can synthesise these compounds independently. We further broadened the metabolic landscape of R. chinensis by examining the metabolite composition across various tissues and organs, integrating previous phytochemical datasets (Zhang et al. 2022) (Table S26). This comprehensive analysis provides a valuable resource for optimising the use of R. chinensis. 

Plant tannins, which include condensed tannins (CTs) and hydrolyzable tannins (HTs), are integral to defence against biotic and abiotic stress (Bains et al. 2009; Tahara et al. 2014). Tannase (TA), an enzyme responsible for tannin hydrolysis, typically occurs as a single copy in most plants (Dai et al. 2020). Notably, we identified three tandem-duplicated TA genes in R. chinensis, similar to other HT-rich species (e.g., Juglans regia and Vitis vinifera), where TA duplications strongly correlate with elevated HT levels. Consistent with HPLC results, transcriptome data revealed that all three TA genes are highly expressed in gallnuts, implying key roles in tannin hydrolysis. Transgenic tobacco and in  vitro assays confirmed that RcTA1 is crucial for accumulating GA and EGC—major tannin derivatives in R. chinensis. Together with previous work (Dai et al. 2020; He et al.  2015), these findings elucidate how abundant tannins and GA accumulate in gallnuts and inform a potential tannin hydrolysis pathway. 

MYB TFs are key regulators of secondary metabolite biosynthesis, including phenolics derived from the shikimate pathway (Liu et  al.  2015). In Canarium album, MYB CaMYBR04 enhances the levels of hydrolyzable tannins such as GA and eugenin (Ye et al. 2024). Here, we identified RcDIV1 as an MYB regulator of hydrolyzable tannin biosynthesis in R. chinensis, which directly activates RcTA1 transcription. This report offers 

the first evidence of such a regulatory mechanism in tannin-rich plants, highlighting the broader importance of MYB TFs in hydrolyzable tannin biosynthesis (Ye et al. 2024). 

Glandular trichomes, specialised epidermal structures with multicellular heads, are present in approximately $3 0 \%$ of vascular plant species (Schuurink and Tissier  2020; Hao et al. 2024). In R. chinensis, these capitate trichomes possess multiple secretory cell layers atop a stalk, with pericarp trichomes showing higher density, larger glands, and shorter stalks than leaf trichomes, suggesting a primary role in metabolite secretion. Despite limited understanding of multicellular trichome development and evolution, homologues of regulatory genes (e.g., RcGL1, RcGL3, RcTTG1) in R. chinensis highlight the conserved MBW regulatory complex (Gao et al. 2021; Yuan et al. 2015). The absence of certain trichome repressors (ETC2, TCL1, TRY) (Pattanaik et al. 2014) points to a distinct regulatory network. RcGL2 is highly expressed in trichomerich tissues and promotes trichome formation in Arabidopsis, indicating its central function in R. chinensis trichome development—likely aiding stress tolerance and ion excretion (Rerie et  al.  1994; Han et  al.  2022). In Arabidopsis, RcGL2 overexpression enhances root growth and trichomes exhibit higher per-trichome $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ efflux than WT under salinity. This likely reflects a stress-evoked property of trichomes, potentially due to increased membrane permeability that permits passive $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ release and/or functional coupling to mesophyllderived $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ export via intercellular signalling, rather than a conversion to a glandular identity. RcGL2 also appears to mitigate oxidative stress, probably by activating GL2-dependent transcriptional programs that raise epidermal antioxidant capacity (CAT, SOD, POD, APX). Increased trichome abundance may further amplify epidermal stress signalling and ROS buffering. Together, enhanced trichome development, increased $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ extrusion capacity, and enhanced antioxidant defences provide a coherent mechanism for the salt-tolerant phenotype associated with RcGL2. 

Multicellular glandular trichomes are crucial for storing and secreting specialised metabolites such as fatty acid derivatives, flavonoids, and terpenoids (Schuurink and Tissier 2020; Adebesin et al. 2017; Wang et al. 2025). Trichome secretions are rich in flavonoids, phenolic acids (e.g., digallic and tetragallic acids), and tannins (e.g., gallic and ellagic acids), implicating these trichomes in metabolite transport and storage. Similar to recretohalophytes (Debez et al. 2006; Chen et al. 2010), R. chinensis trichomes harbour diverse ion transporters (e.g., $\mathrm { H ^ { + } }$ -ATPase, $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / H ^ { + } }$ antiporters, HKT1, and NSCC), many of which are upregulated under salinity (Yuan et  al.  2016). Indeed, R. chinensis displays higher copy numbers of HKTs, CLCs, and ABCGs than other plant species, with genes such as NHX2, HKT1, AKT2, and CBL1 showing increased expression during salt treatment, consistent with active salt secretion observed on leaf surfaces. 

ABA is a central regulator of downstream stress responses, including ion transport and antioxidant defence (Zhu 2016; Baek et al. 2023). R. chinensis contains expanded families of NCED, SDR, and ABCG genes, mirroring patterns in other salt-tolerant species (e.g., Chenopodium quinoa, Excoecaria agallocha, mangroves), where elevated ABA-related gene copy numbers correlate 

with enhanced stress tolerance (Zou et  al.  2017). Notably, the high ABA gene content in Anacardiaceae suggests a shared salinity-adaptation strategy. Many SDRs and ABCGs in R. chinensis are differentially expressed under salt and drought stress, aligning with enriched ABA-responsive pathways (SnRK2s, MAPKs) in GO analyses. Collectively, these data suggest that expanded transporter families and robust ABA signalling may contribute to the transporter activity and remarkable salt tolerance of R. chinensis. 

In R. chinensis, the formation of a salt-like coating on fruits remains intriguing, with its exact biological function still unclear. Because the hard waxy layer impedes water and air uptake, seeds struggle to germinate naturally, leading to hot-water treatments in artificial cultivation. Our evidence indicates that glandular trichome development, coupled with metabolite transport, underlies the deposition of salt-like crystals. Field observations further suggest that these crystals attract birds, whose acidic digestion dissolves the waxy coat and facilitates germination and dispersal. Thus, this salt-like covering may be an adaptive trait promoting seed dissemination and survival under challenging conditions. 

In summary, we present a high-quality R. chinensis genome assembly that advances research on gall formation and glandular trichome development. Our study clarifies the genomic basis of hydrolyzable tannin and flavonoid biosynthesis, as well as the functional roles of chitinases, glandular trichomes, and ion transporters. These findings provide critical insights into the production of bioactive compounds, improved stress adaptation, and the medicinal potential of R. chinensis (Figure 7).

After removing coverslips, slides were washed in $2 \times \mathrm { S S C }$ at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min, followed by two additional washes in $2 \times \mathrm { S S C }$ at room temperature for 5 min each. After air drying, $1 3 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of DAPI was added to each slide and then covered with a coverslip. Grayscale images for each colour channel were acquired using a Zeiss A2 or Olympus BX61 fluorescence microscope equipped with a micro-CCD camera.

gDNA was isolated from fresh R. chinensis leaves using the Plant Genomic DNA Kit (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing, Co. Ltd.). For Illumina paired-end sequencing, the extracted DNA was fragmented and subsequently used to construct Illumina PCRfree libraries with insert sizes of 300–500 bp, following the protocols of the Illumina TruSeq library construction kit. Finally, we conducted 300-bp paired-end sequencing using the Illumina MiSeq platform. For single-molecule real-time (SMRT) sequencing, genomic DNA was purified using the Mobio PowerClean Pro DNA Clean-Up Kit. DNA quality was assessed using agarose gel electrophoresis and a Qubit 2.0 fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). A total of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of qualified genomic DNA was sheared using AMPure beads, and a 20-kb insert-size SMRTbell library was constructed according to the manufacturer's instructions and sequenced on the PacBio Sequel platform. For nanopore sequencing, a library with about $2 0 \mathrm { k b }$ insert size was constructed and sequenced according to the standard protocols on the ONT platform. 

For Hi-C sequencing, a Hi-C library was prepared using fresh young leaves as previously described by Liu et  al.  (2020) and sequenced on the Illumina HiSeq X Ten platform with 150-bp paired-end reads. 

# 4.5 | Assembly of R. chinensis Chromosomes

A high-quality genome assembly of R. chinensis was achieved through the integration of sequences obtained from Illumina, ONT, and PacBio platforms. In brief, Illumina raw reads were filtered by removing the sequencing adaptors, contaminated reads, and low-quality reads. A total of 39 213 792 930 clean short reads were obtained, and the $k$ -mer distribution was estimated using Jellyfish v2.2.10 (Marçais and Kingsford  2011). Subsequently, the genome size, heterozygosity, and repeat content were evaluated using GenomeScope v1.0 (Vurture et  al.  2017). The short, clean reads were assembled using DISCOVAR de novo with default parameters. PacBio long reads were self-corrected and subjected to primary de novo assembly using CANU v1.8 (Koren et  al.  2017) with the following parameters: -correct; saveOverlaps $=$ true; maxThreads $= 4 0$ ; gridEngineMemoryOption, “-l vf $\mathop { \bf { \doteq } }$ MEMORY”; gridEngineThreadsOption $= ^ { * }$ “-pe make THREADS”; gridOptions $\ v { U } = \ v { U } ^ { * } - \ v { V } ^ { * }$ ; genomeSize $= 5 \mathrm { m }$ . Similarly, after being corrected and trimmed by NextDenovo v2.5 (https://github.com/Nextomics/NextDenovo), the ONT long reads were de novo assembled using WTDBG v2.2 (https://github.com/ruanjue/wtdbg) (Ruan and Li 2020). To obtain a high-quality genome assembly, the individual assemblies from Illumina, PacBio, and ONT data were integrated using the HABOT2 (https://github.com/asarum/HABOT2) software. Finally, Pilon v.1.22 (https://anaconda.org/bioconda/pilon) (Walker et  al.  2014) was employed to calibrate the consensus contigs using Illumina paired-end short reads. 

Hi-C sequencing reads were aligned to the draft assembly (superscaffolds) through Juicer v1.5 software (Durand et al. 2016). The resulting matrices and draft assembly were used for constructing Hi-C scaffolding with the 3D-DNA pipeline (Dudchenko 

et al. 2017) following a procedure previously described by Liu et  al.  (2020). To improve the assembly, the ALLHiC pipeline was used to construct phased chromosomal-level assemblies (Zhang et al. 2019). A total of 15 chromosomes were obtained, with $9 0 . 3 0 \%$ of them anchored on the draft assembly genome of R. chinensis. 

To assess the quality of the assembly, we mapped the Illumina pair-end reads to the assembly using BWA-MEM (Li and Durbin  2009) to calculate their mapping rate. Approximately a $9 7 \%$ mapping rate indicates the high quality and integrity of the assembly. The assembly continuity was evaluated using the LTR assembly index program. Additionally, a Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs (BUSCO) (v5.7.1) evaluation was performed to examine the completeness of gene content with the Eukaryota odb10 database (Manni et al. 2021). 

# 4.6 | RNA Library Construction and Sequencing

RNA libraries were constructed and sequenced (RNA-Seq) to predict genes and analyze their expression patterns. Different tissues, including the root, leaf, seed, flower, mature fruit, and young fruit of R. chinensis, were sampled and promptly frozen in liquid nitrogen. Additional plant materials were prepared for RNA isolation. To investigate transcriptional responses to abiotic stress, R. chinensis seedlings were grown in a greenhouse at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a photoperiod of $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light and 8 h of dark for 3 months. Subsequently, seedlings were subjected to different stress environments, including outdoor exposure to dried basin soil (drought), 400 mM NaCl (salinity), and $4 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (heat, conducted in a climatic chamber). Control plants grown under the same environment $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 16/8 light/dark) without added stressors were used for comparison. Each treatment and control included three independent biological replicates. The leaves were collected 6 h after exposure to stress. 

To observe the prolonged effects of salt stress, 3-month-old R. chinensis seedlings were watered with salt solution at various concentrations (0, 50, 100, and 200 mM NaCl) at 7-day intervals for a total of 14 days. Leaves were observed with a stereo microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan) and collected at 0, 7, and 14 days of each salt treatment for RNA extraction. 

To investigate the biological processes associated with the accumulation of white crystalline substances on the fruit surface, we focused on identifying candidate genes involved in their transport. Due to the technical challenges associated with direct sampling of glandular tissues, which are embedded within trichomes, we collected fresh leaf and pericarp samples in October for RNA-seq analysis. In September, gallnut aphids (Figure S9E–G) were removed, after which the fresh GAC and mature leaves were separately sampled for RNA-Seq (three biological replicates). Total RNA was extracted using the FastPure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme Biotech, China) following the manufacturer's protocol, and cDNA libraries were prepared using the NEBNext Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB). The libraries were subsequently sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 4000 platform, generating 150 bp paired-end reads. Raw sequencing data in fastq format were initially processed using in-house Perl scripts to remove 

adapter sequences, poly-N reads, and low-quality reads, resulting in clean data. Data quality was assessed by calculating Q20, Q30 scores, and GC content. Gene expression levels were quantified using FPKM values normalised by gene length. Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 software (v1.16.1) with a significance threshold of adjusted $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ and absolute fold change $> 2$ . Gene ontology (GO) enrichment analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) was conducted using Cluster Profiler (v3.4.4) with correction for gene length bias, considering GO terms with corrected $p$ -values $< 0 . 0 5$ as significantly enriched. Pathway analysis of DEGs was performed using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database through Cluster Profiler (v3.4.4). 

# 4.7 | Genome Annotation

To identify and categorize transposable elements (TEs), we employed a combination of homology-based and de novobased methods at the DNA and protein levels. In detail, we constructed a homology-based repeat library by aligning our genome assembly against a known repeat library (Rebase v. 20.01) (Bao et  al.  2015) using RepeatMasker v4.0.6 (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen  2009). This process allowed us to identify repetitive elements within the R. chinensis genome. To detect TEs in the R. chinensis genome at the protein level, we employed RepeatProteinMask v4.0.3 (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen  2009), which runs WU-BLASTX against the TE protein database. In addition, a de novo-based repeat library was constructed using RepeatModeler (http://www.repeatmasker.org/; v1.05), Piler, RepeatScount (Price et  al.  2005), and LTR_FINDER (Xu and Wang 2007). This integrated repeat library, comprising elements identified through multiple approaches, was annotated using RepeatMasker. After the removal of redundant elements, a final set of TEs in R. chinensis was obtained. 

To annotate protein-coding genes in R. chinensis, we employed a combination of de novo-based, homology-based, and RNA-Seqbased prediction methods. De novo predictions were conducted using ab  initio predictions, which include AUGUSTUS v3.0.1 (Stanke et al. 2006), GlimmerHMM v3.0.1 (Majoros et al. 2004), and Genscan v1.0 (Li et al. 2010). For homology-based predictions, homologies from S. birrea, P. vera, X. sorbifolium, and A. thaliana were used as protein evidence for predicting gene sets using GeneWise v2.3.0 (Birney et al. 2004). For RNA-Seq-based predictions, RNA-Seq reads from five tissues were first mapped to the R. chinensis genome using HISAT2 (Pertea et  al.  2016) and then assembled into transcripts based on a genome-guided strategy by StringTie (Pertea et al. 2015) and Trinity (Grabherr et al. 2011). In addition, RNA-Seq data were also de novo assembled into transcripts with Trinity (Grabherr et al. 2011). All transcripts were merged, and redundant transcripts were filtered out using a cutoff of $9 5 \%$ identities. Annotations from RNA-Seqbased, homology-based, and de novo-based predictions were integrated using the MAKER pipeline (Holt and Yandell 2011) to generate a comprehensive protein-coding gene set. Finally, 24 039 non-redundant, consensus protein-coding gene sets were obtained. 

Gene functional prediction was performed by aligning gene sequences against InterPro, Swiss-Prot (http://www.uniprot.org/), 

TrEMBL, GO, KEGG, and NCBI-NR databases with an E-value cutoff of $\mathrm { 1 e ^ { - 5 } }$ . tRNA genes were predicted using tRNAScan-SE v2.0 with default parameters. rRNA, miRNA, and sncRNA genes were identified using homologous searching against the Rfam database (Griffiths-Jones et  al.  2005) with INFERNAL v1.1.2 software (Nawrocki et al. 2009). 

# 4.8 | Comparative Genome Analysis

Genomes of 16 species, including representatives from the basal Angiosperms (Amborella trichopoda), monocotyledons (Oryza sativa), and dicotyledons (Vitis vinifera, Populus trichocarpa, Solanum lycopersicum, Theobroma cacao, Prunus persica, Eucalyptus grandis, Brassica rapa, Pistacia vera, Sclerocraya birrea, Citrus sinensis, Xanthoceras sorbifolium, Arabidopsis thaliana, Medicago truncatula, and Glycine max), and R. chinensis, were used to identify orthologous groups (gene family groups) and infer phylogenetic relationships. Low-confidence protein sequences (length $< 5 0 \AA$ ) from all species were removed, and then all protein sequences were clustered. Orthologous gene groups shared between R. chinensis and 16 other species were identified using OrthoMCL (version 2.0.9) (Li et al. 2003), with the thresholds being an $E$ -value of $\mathrm { l e } ^ { - 5 }$ , coverage of $3 0 \%$ , and similarity of $3 0 \%$ . We define an orthogroup (gene family) as species-specific when it contains at least one protein-coding gene from a species (e.g., R. chinensis) and no genes from any other genome. Accordingly, among four Sapindales species, R. chinensis-specific orthogroups (gene families) were identified. The expanded and contracted gene families for each species were determined using CAFÉ (v4.2.1) (Han et al. 2013) with default parameters. KEGG and GO enrichment of the expanded and contracted gene families in R. chinensis were performed using clusterProfiler (v3.8.1) (Yu et al. 2012). To infer the phylogenetic placement of R. chinensis, 132 shared single-copy genes were identified using the OrthoMCL program (v2.0.9) from the 16 seed plants. For each single-copy gene, amino acid sequences were aligned and calibrated using MUSCLE (v3.8.31) (Edgar 2004). The phylogenetic tree was constructed using the maximum likelihood method with the HKY85 model implemented in PhyML (v3.1) (Guindon et al. 2010). The divergence time among 17 species was calculated using the MCMCtree program of PAML (v4.9) (Yang  2007) with parameters set at burn-in $= 1 0 0 0 0$ , sample number $= 1 0 0 0 0 0$ , and sample frequency $= 2$ . For age estimation, the tree was calibrated with calibration points from articles and the TimeTree website (http:// www.timetree.org) (Kumar et  al.  2017), including angiosperm crown age (177–251 MYA) (Li et  al.  2019), V. vinifera and A. thaliana (105–115 MYA), P. trichocarpa and A. thaliana (97–115 MYA), O. sativa and A. thaliana (148–178 MYA), T. cacao-A. thaliana (87–95 MYA), and the monocot and eudicot (130–240 MYA) (Jaillon et al. 2007). 

# 4.9 | Whole-Genome Duplication and Genome Synteny Analysis

The WGD event of R. chinensis and the divergence time between species were determined based on the distribution of substitutions per synonymous site (Ks) profile using the codeml approach implemented in the PAML package (v4.9) (Yang 2007). 

To further identify the potential WGD event of R. chinensis, the genome synteny search for comparisons within R. chinensis, between R. chinensis and C. sinensis, and between R. chinensis and P. vera, was performed using MCScanX (Wang et al. 2012). A dot plot of R. chinensis-R. chinensis genomes was generated with TBtools (Chen et al. 2020) to visualise the paleopolyploidy level of R. chinensis.

# 4.20 | Immunohistochemical Localization and Observation

Because the outer fruit pericarp of R. chinensis was covered by a large number of glandular trichomes, we selected the fruit pericarps to investigate the potential roles of RcGL2 in the development of glandular trichomes using an immunohistochemical localization assay, which was performed as previously described (Tan et al. 2011; Krishnan et al. 2022) with minor modifications. Briefly, fresh fruit pericarps were isolated, dissected into small pieces with a razor, and immediately fixed in FAA solution at room temperature for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . The fixed tissues were sequentially dehydrated through a graded ethanol series and embedded in paraffin. The paraffin sections were sliced as approximately $5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ sections, mounted on slides, and then subjected to dewaxing in xylene, rehydration through graded concentrations of ethanol, and a final rinse with distilled water. The sections were soaked in $3 \%$ hydrogen peroxide for $2 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at room temperature. After three washes with phosphate-buffered saline 

(PBS) (pH 7.4) for 5 min each, the sections were blocked in $5 \%$ bovine serum albumin (BSA) for 30 min at room temperature. After removing the excess BSA solution, the sections were incubated with the generated rabbit GL2 polyclonal antibody in a moist chamber at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. After three rinses with PBS (pH 7.4) for 5 min each, the sections were further incubated with horseradish peroxidase-labelled polymer-conjugated anti-rabbit antibodies at room temperature for $5 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Following three washes with PBS, the bound antibodies were visualised by incubation with $_ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine solution for $3 { - } 5 \mathrm { m i n }$ . At last, the sections were counterstained with Meyer's haematoxylin, dehydrated with ethanol, and cover-slipped for microscopic observation. Images of the stained sections were taken using a light microscope (Nikon Eclipse CI, Nikon, Japan). In addition, control slides were prepared similarly, substituting the antibody with BSA. 

# 4.21 | Morphological Observations of Glandular Trichomes

For scanning electron microscope (SEM) observation, leaves and pericarps collected at the same stage were fixed in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) for 2 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After three rinses with $0 . 1 \mathbf { M }$ phosphate buffer for $1 5 \mathrm { m i n }$ per rinse, samples were dehydrated in a graded ethanol series $3 0 \%$ , $5 0 \%$ , $7 0 \%$ , $8 0 \%$ , $9 0 \%$ , $9 5 \%$ , and $1 0 0 \%$ ) for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ each and dried using an automatic critical point drier (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). After sputter coating (Bal-Tec, Balzers, Switzerland), the samples were examined, imaged, and measured using SEM (Hitachi High-Technologies Corporation, Tokyo, Japan) at $1 5 . 0 \mathrm { k V } .$ . The number and size measurements under SEM were based on three samples randomly selected from leaves and fruits. Averages and standard deviations were calculated using SPSS software (version 23.0; IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). 

For semi-thin section observations, the leaf and pericarps were cut into $\mathrm { 1 m m } \times 2 \mathrm { m m }$ pieces using a razor blade. Each sample was prefixed in $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) glutaraldehyde in $0 . 1 \mathrm { m o l / L }$ phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. Following three washes with $0 . 1 \mathbf { M }$ phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) for 10 min each, samples were dehydrated in an ethanol series $3 0 \%$ , $5 0 \%$ , $7 0 \%$ , $8 0 \%$ , $9 0 \%$ , and $9 5 \%$ ) for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ each, washed three times with $1 0 0 \%$ ethanol for 30 min each, and treated with a series of ethanol/propylene oxide by adjusting ethanol/propylene oxide to 7:3, 5:5, and 3:7. After three washes with pure propylene oxide for 10 min each, samples were gradually infiltrated with Spurr resin (Wang et al. 2016) under agitation in an oscillator for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ , over-dried, prepared as 70-nm-thick sections using a Leica EM UC6 ultramicrotome (Leica), and stained with toluidine blue. The cells were then observed and photographed under a Primo Star biological microscope (ZEISS, Oberkochen, Germany). 

# 4.22 | Construction of Transgenic Arabidopsis, Phenotypic Observation, Physiological Measurements, and Salt-Tolerance Assay

The overexpression plasmid 35S::RcGL2-GFP was delivered to 5-week-old Arabidopsis plants by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation using the floral dip method. After 2 consecutive 

self-pollinations of T1 transgenic Arabidopsis, homozygous T3 lines were screened with antibiotic, and the positive transgenic lines were confirmed by PCR. For the phenotypic measurement of leaf trichomes, sterilised transgenic and non-transgenic Arabidopsis seeds (controls) were sown on $\scriptstyle 1 / _ { 2 }$ MS medium with the plates placed vertically. On the ninth day after germination, the number and morphology of trichomes on the first two true leaves from transgenic lines and controls were counted and photographed. 15 replicate observations of three lines were performed. For the salt-tolerance experiment, all germinated transgenic lines and WT (with the same root length) were transplanted together to $\scriptstyle 1 / _ { 2 }$ MS medium containing 100 mM NaCl, with the plates kept vertical. After NaCl treatment for 7 day, the root length from at least five individuals from each line was measured using ImageJ, and the phenotype was photographed and recorded on the eighth day. At the same time, the relative electrolyte leakage of all transgenic lines and WT was determined, and at least three replicates per line were measured. 

For the soil salt-tolerance experiment, transgenic and WT Arabidopsis seeds were sown in soil containing peat and vermiculite and grown in a growth chamber under a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod, respectively. At 4 weeks of age, the transgenic and non-transgenic plants (as controls) were irrigated with $2 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, and the phenotype was photographed. ROS indices—hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ and superoxide $( \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } { \bullet } ^ { - } )$ —and activities of the antioxidant enzymes catalase (CAT), peroxidase (POD), superoxide dismutase (SOD), and ascorbate peroxidase (APX) were quantified using commercial kits (Comin Biotechnology, Suzhou, China), following the manufacturer's protocols. Three biological replicates were analysed per treatment. Transcript levels of four antioxidant-related genes were measured by qRT-PCR (primer sequences in Table S28). For histochemistry, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ was visualised by DAB staining $\mathrm { \Omega _ { \mathrm { 1 } \mathrm { m g } m L ^ { - 1 } } }$ DAB in $1 \times$ PBS, pH 7.0). Detached leaves were vacuum-infiltrated for $2 0 { - } 3 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , incubated in the dark at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ , rinsed with deionised water, and cleared in $9 5 \%$ ethanol at ${ \sim } 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } { \cdot } 9 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 0 { - } 3 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ . O •− was detected by NBT staining $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { { m g m L ^ { - 1 } } }$ NBT in $1 \times$ PBS) using the same procedure. Brown (DAB) and blue (NBT) precipitates were photographed after complete chlorophyll removal. 

# 4.23 | Measurement of $\mathbf { N a } ^ { + }$ Flux From Arabidopsis Trichomes by Non-Invasive Micro-Test Technology (NMT)

Net $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ flux at trichome tips was measured in 4-week-old Arabidopsis thaliana plants, comparing wild type (WT) and RcGL2 overexpression (OE-RcGL2) lines. For salt-stress measurements, plants were irrigated with 200 mM NaCl for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ before recording; mock-treated plants served as controls. Fluxes were quantified with an NMT Physiolyzer system (YoungerUSA LLC Amherst, MA; Xuyue [Beijing] Sci. and Tech. Co. Ltd.). $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ - selective microelectrodes were prepared and calibrated according to the manufacturer's instructions. Individual leaves were immobilised in a Petri dish using narrow strips of filter paper and small resin blocks to expose the target trichome. A standard measuring solution was added to immerse the trichome and equilibrated for ${ \sim } 2 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , then replaced with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ fresh solution to start acquisition. Under a microscope, the $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ microsensor was positioned ${ \sim } 1 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ from the trichome tip, and net flux from 

a single trichome was recorded for 300 s (5 min) per sample. For each treatment, six independent trichomes $( n = 6 )$ were measured. Signals were collected with imFluxes v2.0 (YoungerUSA) and expressed as pmol $\mathrm { c m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ ; positive values denote net efflux and negative values denote net influx. 

# 4.24 | Ion Content Assay

To determine the ion composition of the secretion produced by the glandular trichomes of R. chinensis fruits, the secretion, inner kernel, and outer coat from mature fruits (harvested in November) were isolated separately and analyzed as previously reported (Peng et al. 2017). Samples were obtained from at least three healthy female trees. In brief, $2 5 0 \mathrm { g }$ of the secretion, inner kernel, and outer coat were washed twice with ultrapure water, followed by a wash with 25 mM $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ (pH 5.0) and ultrapure water for 15 min each. After drying for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , all samples were digested with $7 0 \%$ nitric acid at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h and diluted with ultrapure water for ion determination. Ion contents in each sample were determined using inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry in the test center of Yangzhou University. 

# 4.25 | qRT-PCR

qRT-PCR was performed as described previously (Lu et al. 2022) with gene-specific primers designed using Primer 5.0 (Table S28) to determine the expression of selected candidate genes involved in salt gland development, salt stress response, and transgenic validation. Relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method with actin as an internal control. All reactions were conducted with three biological replicates.

All statistical analyses were performed using R Studio (version 4.3.3) with the Stats analysis package (R Development Core Team, Vienna, Austria) and SPSS software (version 23.0; IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). $p$ -values $< 0 . 0 5$ , $< 0 . 0 1$ , and $< 0 . 0 0 1$ were interpreted as statistically significant, highly significant, and extremely significant, respectively. Data are presented as means $\pm$ standard deviation (SD) based on at least three independent biological replicates.
