# A B S T R A C T

Nitrogen (N) is the driving force for crop yields; however, excessive N application in agriculture not only increases production cost, but also causes severe environmental problems. Therefore, comprehensively understanding the molecular mechanisms of N use efficiency (NUE) and breeding crops with higher NUE is essential to tackle these problems. NUE of crops is determined by N uptake, transport, assimilation, and remobilization. In the process of N assimilation, nitrate reductase (NR), nitrite reductase (NiR), glutamine synthetase (GS), and glutamine-2-oxoglutarate aminotransferase (GOGAT, also known as glutamate synthase) are the major enzymes. NR and NiR mediate the initiation of inorganic N utilization, and GS/GOGAT cycle converts inorganic N to organic N, playing a vital role in N assimilation and the final NUE of crops. Besides, asparagine synthetase (ASN), glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH), and carbamoyl phosphate synthetase (CPSase) are also involved. In this review, we summarize the function and regulation of these enzymes reported in three major cropsdrice, maize, and wheat, also in the model plant Arabidopsis, and we highlight their application in improving NUE of crops via manipulating N assimilation. Anticipated challenges and prospects toward fully understanding the function of N assimilation and further exploring the potential for NUE improvement are discussed.

# Introduction

Rice (Oryza sativa L.), maize (Zea mays L.), and wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) are the three major crops worldwide. The production of these three crops accounts for about $90 \%$ of the total crop yields in China. To increase grain yields, large amounts of fertilizers, especially nitrogen (N) fertilizers, are applied during agricultural production; however, only $3 0 \% { - } 5 0 \%$ of the applied N fertilizers could be absorbed and incorporated into crops (Raun and Johnson, 1999). As a result, excessive N application not only caused increased production input, but also resulted in severe environmental pollution. Therefore, improving the N use efficiency (NUE), which could be defined as the total grain yield or biomass per unit of N applied (Li et al., 2017), of crops is an effective way to tackle this challenge, which requires comprehensive dissection of the genetic basis of NUE

in plants. NUE of crops is determined by a series of interconnected processes including N uptake, transport, assimilation, and remobilizationdeach of them is critical for NUE. In this review, we focus on the processes of N assimilation, including nitrate reduction and ammonium assimilation.

In plants, nitrate and ammonium are two major inorganic N source. For nitrate, after uptake from root, it is first reduced into nitrite by nitrate reductase (NR) in cytoplasm, then nitrite is transported into plastid and reduced into ammonium by plastidic nitrite reductase (NiR) (Fig. 1A). Members of NR reported in different plants are varied, with two in Arabidopsis, three in rice, four in maize, and three in wheat; similar for NiR, with one in Arabidopsis, three in rice, three in maize, and two in wheat reported (Table 1). Ammonium assimilation is catalyzed by the glutamine synthetase (GS)/glutamine-2- oxoglutarate aminotransferase (GOGAT, also known as glutamate synthase) cycle, by which inorganic N is converted into organic N. In this cycle, GS catalyzes glutamine (Gln) synthesis by incorporating a molecule of ammonium into glutamate (Glu) in an adenosine triphosphate-dependent manner,

In higher plants, there are two types of GS with distinct subcellular localization: cytosolic GS1, encoded by three to five genes depending on the species (AtGLN1.1-1.5 in Arabidopsis, OsGS1;1- 1;3 in rice, ZmGLN1.1-1.5 in maize, and TaGS1.1-1.3 in wheat), and plastidic GS2, encoded by one gene (AtGLN2 in Arabidopsis, OsGS2 in rice, ZmGLN2 in maize, and TaGS2 in wheat). There are also two types of GOGAT: nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH)- GOGAT, using NADH as a reductant (AtGLT in Arabidopsis, OsNADH-GOGAT1 and OsNADH-GOGAT2 in rice, ZmNADH-GOGAT1 in maize, and TaNADH-GOGAT1 in wheat), and ferredoxin (Fd)-GOGAT, using Fd as a reductant (AtGLU1 and AtGLU2 in Arabidopsis, OsFd-GOGAT1 in rice, ZmFd-GOGAT1 in maize, and TaFd-GOGAT1 in wheat); both are located in plastid and encoded by one or two genes (Table 1). In this review, we comprehensively summarize the progress on functionally characterized members of NR, NiR, GS, GOGAT, and other enzymes involved in N assimilation in three major cropsdrice, maize, and wheat, also model plant Arabidopsis, and their application in improving NUE in crops was highlighted. The future work on comprehensively understanding the function and exploring the application potential of NR, NiR, GS, GOGAT, and other enzymes in plants was discussed.

# Nitrogen assimilation in plants

# Nitrate reductase and nitrite reductase

Most of the nitrate taken up by roots will be transported to the shoots and reduced in mesophyll cells (MCs), and it can also be reduced into ammonium in roots.

In Arabidopsis, although both transcripts of NR, AtNIA1 and AtNIA2 in roots and leaves are detected, the exact expression patterns differed, indicating different functions of AtNIA1 and AtNIA2 (Cheng et al., 1991). Mutation of AtNIA2 results in $90 \%$ decreased NR activity in leaves. nia1 nia2 double mutant only retains $0 . 5 \%$ NR activity of wild type (WT), and displays severe growth retardation when nitrate was used as the N source (Wilkinson and Crawford, 1993). OsNR1.2 is mainly expressed in the node and leaf sheath, and osnr1.2 mutant displays decreased growth. Similar expression patterns of OsNR1.1 and OsNR2 are detected, which are also higher in shoots than that in roots (Han et al., 2022). Knock-down expression of OsNR2 results in significantly decreased tiller number and grain yield (Gao et al., 2019). The detailed functional characterization of NiR in crops is rarely reported, except that the transcriptional level of OsNiR1 is positively related to rice growth (Yu et al., 2021).

The function of OsGS1;1 for $\ N H _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ in roots is also non-negligible (Ishiyama et al., 2004a). Moreover, ammonium also significantly induced the accumulation of ZmGLN1.1 and ZmGLN1.5, and the expression of TaGS1.1 and TaGS1.3 in the roots of maize and wheat, respectively (Prinsi and Espen, 2015; Wei et al., 2020). TaGS1.2 is mainly localized in the surrounding vessels of the xylem (Wei et al., 2020), possibly facilitating Gln loaded to the xylem and translocated to the shoot by amino acid transporters. Except assimilated in roots, $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ could be translocated from the root to the shoot. AtGLN1.2 might be the main isozyme of GS1 in the shoot responsible for the reduction of $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ transported from the root (Guan et al., 2016).

# Reassimilation of photorespiratory ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$

Photorespiration, an essential but high energy consumption process, reduces crop photosynthetic efficiency, and causes yield loss. During this process, ammonia is generated in mitochondria by glycine decarboxylase activity. Then, ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ is transported to chloroplasts via an uncertain pathway. It is reported that the amount of ammonia released from photorespiration is up to 10 times of that from nitrate reduction (Potel et al., 2009), which may be toxic to the cells. Besides, once reassimilation was blocked, it would not only result in an imbalance of metabolism, but also ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ loss from plants. Thus, efficient reassimilation of photorespiratory ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ is critical for plants, while GS2 and Fd-GOGAT play a major role in this process.

Arabidopsis AtGLN2 is present in both mitochondria and chloroplasts of MCs and the GS activity in both mitochondria and chloroplasts is induced by photorespiratory stress (Taira et al., 2004). Growth and N metabolism of atgln2 mutant was impaired under photorespiratory conditions (Ferreira et al., 2019). Loss-of-function mutant of AtGLU1 is chlorotic and lethal under air conditions with permitted photorespiration (Coschigano et al., 1998; Jamai et al., 2009; Potel et al., 2009). Consistent with AtGLN2, AtGLU1 isoenzyme (Fd-GOGAT) is targeted to both mitochondria and chloroplasts (Jamai et al., 2009), which contributes to the coordination of AtGLN2 and AtGLU1 during photorespiration. Furthermore, AtGLU1 in mitochondria is able to interact with serine hydroxymethyltransferase1 (SHMT1), an important enzyme in the cellular one-carbon pathway, and to regulate the SHMT activity (Jamai et al., 2009). Unlike the localization of AtGLU1 in Arabidopsis, OsFd-GOGAT1 is localized in the chloroplast in rice (Sun et al., 2017). High expression of OsGS2 and OsFd-GOGAT1 was detected in leaves (Bao et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2017; Zeng et al., 2017). Dysfunction of OsGS2 and OsFd-GOGAT1 causes chlorosis phenotype and severe growth retardation (Bao et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2017; Zeng et al., 2017), which might result from the defected photorespiration and the imbalance of carbon-nitrogen metabolism (Bao et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2017). Different from ${ \sf C } _ { 3 }$ plants with photorespiration occurring in MCs, in ${ \mathsf { C } } _ { 4 }$ plants such as maize, the photorespiratory enzymes are mainly localized in bundle sheath cells (BSCs). Therefore, ZmGLN2 and ZmFd-GOGAT1, located in the chloroplast of BSCs in leaves, are the major enzymes for ammonia assimilation released from photorespiration (Redinbaugh and Campbell, 1993; Becker et al., 2000).

# Nitrogen remobilization during leaf senescence

With aging or growth under N-deficiency condition, the primary N assimilation in senescent leaves was decreased, protein and nucleic acids were degraded, and the released N was remobilized into developing tissues, including new leaves and developing seeds or grains (Have et al., 2017 ). It is estimated that up to $6 5 \%$ , $90 \%$ , and $9 5 \%$ total N in grains of maize, rice, and wheat, respectively, are remobilized from the vegetative organs (Hirel et al., 2007), indicating an indispensable role of N remobilization during senescence for grain filling, not only related to crop yield, but also to grain quality. During leaf senescence, free $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ is also generated from various metabolic processes, such as Glu deamination reaction by GDH. Expression of genes encoding ammonium transporters is increased during leaf senescence (Have et al., 2017  ), suggesting that N may be retranslocated in the form of $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ . However, it is widely accepted that Gln and Asn are the major N forms transported from senescing organs to sink organs via the phloem. Therefore, GS/GOGAT cycle plays a vital role for N conversion of inorganic N to organic N and N retranslocation during leaf senescence.

In Arabidopsis, it was shown that the expression of AtGLN1.1, AtGLN1.2, AtGLN1.3, and AtGLN1.4 is induced during leaf

senescence (Have et al., 2017  ; Li et al., 2017). AtGLN1.1, AtGLN1.2, and AtGLN1.3 were detected in the phloem companion cells in leaves (Moison et al., 2018). Only the triple mutant atgln1.1/atgln1.2/atgln1.3 performs suppressed N remobilization to seeds, demonstrating their essential and complementary role in N remobilization (Lothier et al., 2011; Moison et al., 2018). OsGS1;1 and OsNADH-GOGAT2 that coexpressed in phloem companion cells and phloem parenchyma cells associated with vascular bundles in mature leaf blades, are believed to mediate N remobilization during leaf senescence (Tamura et al., 2011; Yamaya and Kusano, 2014). Disruption of either causes severe growth retardation (Tabuchi et al., 2005; Tamura et al., 2011). After silking, the ZmGLN1.4 transcript is increased gradually and the ZmGLN1.3 transcript is stable at a detectable level in leaves. ZmGLN1.3 is mainly localized in MCs, while ZmGLN1.4 in BSCs. Moreover, mutations in ZmGLN1.3 result in reduced kernel number, while mutations in ZmGLN1.4 result in reduced kernel size, indicating their essential but nonredundant roles for maize production (Martin et al., 2006). Expression of TaGS1.1 and TaGS1.2 in leaves is increased at grainfilling stage. Because TaGS1.1 mRNA (messenger RNA) was mainly present in the parenchyma and mestome sheath cells surrounding the vascular bundles, while TaGS1.2 mRNA in vascular phloem cells, suggesting that TaGS1.1 and TaGS1.2 might be involved in ammonium assimilation during N remobilization (Bernard et al., 2008). Furthermore, changed N remobilization in rice fd-gogat mutant and transgenic wheat overexpressing TaGS2-2A suggest their possible role in N translocation from senescing organs to grains (Zeng et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2018).

# Nitrogen assimilation during grain filling

A large amount of free $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ was detected during grain development, and GS and GOGAT also play an essential role in this process (Wei et al., 2021). In developing seeds of Arabidopsis, expression of AtGLN1.2 was detected (Lothier et al., 2011). With high expression in spikelets, OsGS1;3 and OsNADH-GOGAT1 are assumed to play an important role in grain filling in rice (Yamaya and Kusano, 2014). In maize, the transcript of ZmGLN1.2 is specifically accumulated in the kernel pedicel tissues, while ZmGLN1.3 is constitutively expressed (Rastogi et al., 1998). Besides, the QTLs for kernel number and thousand-kernel-weight are coincidently located with ZmGLN1.2 and ZmGLN1.3, respectively (Hirel et al., 2001), suggesting their potential role for kernel growth. In wheat, different members of GS family display differential subcellular localization and expression patterns with grain developing, indicating their distinct roles in grain development, especially TaGS1.3, which is stably expressed during grain filling. The NADH-GOGAT activity is much higher than Fd-GOGAT activity, suggesting the predominant role of TaNADH-GOGAT1 in grain filling (Wei et al., 2021).

# Other enzymes involved in N assimilation

# Asparagine synthetase

Reciprocal expression patterns suggest distinct functions of AtASN1 and AtASN2. It has been shown that AtASN1 was critical for N utilization during seed development (Gaufichon et al., 2017). AtASN2 not only participated in primary ammonium metabolism (Wong et al., 2004), but was also involved in N remobilization from source tissues (Gaufichon et al., 2013). With low expression levels in different organs, disruption of AtASN3 results in no visible phenotype (Gaufichon et al., 2016). In rice, OsASN1 is mainly expressed in root surface in $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -dependent manner (Ohashi et al., 2015) and osasn1 mutants show defected development (Lee et al., 2020b), suggesting its role in primary $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ assimilation. Function of

OsASN2 is still unknown, even though high expression was detected in rice leaves (Ohashi et al., 2015). ZmASN3 and ZmASN4 might be involved in amino acid conversion and N remobilization of aborted kernels (Canas et al., 2010 ~ ). With high expression level in grain, TaASN2 plays an essential role in Asn synthesis in grain, and lower free Asn concentration was observed in taasn2 mutant (Raffan et al., 2021).

# Glutamate dehydrogenase

AtGDH1, AtGDH2, and AtGDH3, which can be assembled into homohexamers or heterohexamers, are three NADH-dependent GDHs in Arabidopsis. Expression of AtGDH1 and AtDGH2 was detected in both root and shoot (Miyashita and Good, 2008). The expression of AtGDH1 is induced by C starvation and exogenous ammonia. Compared with WT, gdh1 mutant displays impaired growth under exogenous inorganic N supply, indicating its essential role in N assimilation under such condition (Melo-Oliveira et al., 1996). Accumulation of AtGDH2 is promoted under long-term darkness (Miyashita and Good, 2008). Under C starvation, gdh1 gdh2 double mutant displays lower survival rate than that of WT, which can be rescued by supplied sucrose, while neither gdh1 nor gdh2 single mutant shows distinguished phenotype with WT, supporting the redundant role of AtGDH1 and AtGDH2 under C starvation (Miyashita and Good, 2008). AtGDH3, which is mainly localized in the mitochondria of phloem companion cell in root, is induced by N starvation and sucrose (Fontaine et al., 2012; Marchi et al., 2013).

In Arabidopsis, there are two genes for CPSase, carA and carB, which are expressed in MC and vascular-associated tissues (Potel

et al., 2009). Both carA and carB are indispensable to mesophyll development, due to their function in arginine biosynthesis (Molla-Morales et al., 2011).

# Multilayer regulation for N assimilation

# Transcriptional regulation for N assimilation

The functions of the components of N assimilation are largely associated with their specific temporal-spatial expression patterns. Therefore, transcriptional regulation plays an essential role in modulating their functions (Fig. 2).

# Transcriptional regulation of NR and NiR

Expression of NR and NiR is induced by ${ \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ . Arabidopsis NIN-like protein 7 (NLP7) is the master regulator of the primary nitrate response. After ${ \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ treatment, AtNLP7 is accumulated in the nucleus, and activates the expression of AtNIA1, AtNIA2, and AtNiR1 (Marchive et al., 2013). AtNIA1, AtNIA2, and AtNiR1 are also upregulated by AtNLP6 (Konishi and Yanagisawa, 2013). As homologues of AtNLP7, OsNLP4 and ZmNLP5 play the similar function in rice and maize, respectively. Expression of OsNR1.1, OsNR2, and OsNiR1 is directly activated by OsNLP4 (Wu et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021). Expression of ZmNiR1.1 is increased by ZmNLP5 via binding the nitrate response element (NRE) in ${ } ^ { 5 ^ { \prime } }$ untranslated region (UTR) of ZmNiR1.1. However, ZmNLP5 is unable to bind the ${ } ^ { 5 ^ { \prime } }$ UTR of ZmNiR1.2, even though NRE exists in that region (Ge et al., 2020).

HY5 is reported to bind the promoter region of AtNIA2 (Lee et al., 2007). Taken together, HY5 might directly regulate the light response of AtNIA2 at transcriptional level.

TCP20, a transcriptional factor that belongs to TEOSINTE BRANCHED1/CYCLOIDEA/PROLIFERATING CELL FACTOR1 family, enables binding to the promoter region of AtNIA1 and upregulates its transcript level (Guan et al., 2014). Expression of OsNR1.2 is activated by a C2H2 zinc finger transcription factor, Drought and Salt Tolerance (DST), that positively regulates N assimilation, while negatively related to drought tolerance (Han et al., 2022).

It has also been reported that methylation of AtNIA2 mediated by chromomethylase 3 (CMT3) inhibited AtNIA2 transcriptional level, which is suppressed by $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ treatment (Kim et al., 2015), indicating that epigenetic regulation is also involved in nitrate assimilation process.

# Transcriptional regulation of GS and GOGAT

The spatial-temporal expression of TaGS2 is regulated by H3K4me3, cytosine methylation, and nuclease accessibility. Higher H3K4me3, less cytosine methylation, and open conformation at the promoter region lead to a higher expression levels of TaGS2-2B than TaGS2-2A and TaGS2-2D, suggesting the important function of TaGS2-2B in wheat (Zhang et al., 2017). During endosperm development, OsGS1;3 is activated by an endosperm-specific putative nuclear factor-Y transcription factor subunit C, NF-YC12, resulting in the fine-tuning of seed storage accumulation (Xiong et al., 2019). The expression of OsFd-GOGAT1 is suppressed by Nhd1 (N-mediated heading date-1), the key transcription factor regulating flowering time of rice (Zhang et al., 2021b). AtGLU1, the key component for N assimilation in green tissue, is the potential target of the paralog GATA transcription factors GATA, NITRATE-INDUCIBLE, CARBON-METABOLISM-INVOLVED (GNC) and CYTOKININ-RESPONSIVE GATA 1/GNC-KILE (CGA1/GNL) (Hudson et al., 2011). In wheat, TabZIP60, a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor, can directly bind to the promoter of TaNADH-GOGAT1-3B to repress its expression, by which they collectively regulate N utilization (Yang et al., 2019).

Except AtNIA1, AtNIA2, and AtNiR1, the expression of AtGLN2 is also activated by AtNLP7 (Marchive et al., 2013). In wheat, a nitrateinducible NAM, ATAF, and CUC (NAC) transcription factor, TaNAC2- 5A, is able to bind to the promoter region of TaGS2-2A to activate its expression. Interestingly, phylogenetic analysis found that TaNAC2-5A belongs to a cereal-specific clade, while OsNAC19 and ZmNAC67 in this clade are also dramatically induced by nitrate in rice and maize, suggesting that these three NAC transcription factors might have a conserved function in different species (He et al., 2015).

N assimilation and carbon (C) metabolism are tightly linked and subtly regulated in plants. It is reported that CIRCADIAN CLOCK ASSOCIATED 1 (CCA1), a MYB-related transcription factor regulating the circadian rhythm of Arabidopsis, directly activates AtGLN1.3 and other N assimilation-related gene expression, which might, at least in part, account for circadian N assimilation (Gutierrez et al., 2008  ). As a key factor integrating N assimilation, carbon fixation, and rice growth, GROWTH-REGULATING FACOTR 4 (GRF4) can also activate the expression of OsGS1;2 in rice (Li et al., 2018).

Besides, OsMYB55, a R2R3-MYB transcription factor involved in the high temperature response in rice, could directly bind to the promoter region and activate the expression of OsGS1;2, suggesting the possible role of OsGS1;2 in the stress response (El-Kereamy et al., 2012).

# Transcriptional regulation of other genes involved in N assimilation

The expression of AtASN1 is directly activated by the transcription factor bZIP11, which plays an important role in sugar signaling (Hanson et al., 2008). SENSITIVE-TO-PROTON-RHIZOTOXICITY1 (STOP1), a transcription factor related to stress tolerance, directly activates the expression of GDH1 and GDH2 to regulate aluminum resistance in Arabidopsis (Tokizawa et al., 2021). During anoxiareoxygenation, GDH2 is one of the targets of EIN3 ETHYLENE INSENSITIVE3 (EIN3), the master regulator of ethylene signaling pathway (Tsai et al., 2016).

# Post-translational modifications for N assimilation

Except for transcriptional regulation, post-translational modifications (PTMs) also play a critical role in regulating the functions of many N utilization-related proteins in plants. Therefore, many efforts have been made to explore the molecular mechanisms of PTMs for regulating N assimilation (Fig. 2).

# Post-translational modifications of NR and NiR

Phosphorylation and dephosphorylation of NR play an important role in regulating NR activity. Phosphorylation of serine residue (Ser534) of AtNIA2 was confirmed to be essential for 14-3-3 binding, resulting in reduced NR activity, which is possibly due to the interruption of electrons transfer from heme to nitrate (Lambeck et al., 2010, 2012). This Ser residue is conserved among all NR members in rice, maize, and wheat (Fig. 3A), suggesting its fundamental role in regulating NR activity. It has been reported that phosphorylation of Ser534 is mediated by calcium-dependent protein kinase 17 (CPK17) in vitro (Lambeck et al., 2010). Interaction of AtNIA2 and $\beta$ -subunits of SnRK1 kinase complex suggests that SnRK1 might be involved in the phosphorylation of AtNIA2 (Polge et al., 2008). B55, the structural subunit of PP2A Protein Phosphatase 2A (PP2A), interacts with AtNIA1 and AtNIA2, resulting in their dephosphorylation to activate NR activity (Heidari et al., 2011). Furthermore, the catalytic subunit C2 of PP2A is critical for NR activity (Creighton et al., 2017). Induced NR activity by light in maize and under low temperature treatment in wheat is also involved in dephosphorylation of NR (Chandok and Sopory, 1996; Yaneva et al., 2002). During later root development, Ser627 of AtNIA2 is phosphorylated by mitogen-activated protein kinase 6 (MPK6), leading to increased NR activity, which is involved in nitric oxide (NO) synthesis induced by ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ (Wang et al., 2010). Ser627 of AtNIA2 is also conserved in NR of rice, maize, and wheat (Fig. 3B).

With the E3 small ubiquitin-related modifier (SUMO) ligase activity, AtSIZ1 enables SUMOylation of AtNIA1 and AtNIA2 at lysine residue 356 (Lys356) and Lys353, respectively, resulting in increased NR activity (Park et al., 2011). Conserved Lys is observed in most NR (Fig. 3C), indicating essential role of SUMOylation for NR activity. Containing the molybdenum cofactor (Moco) domain, AtNIA2 was detected to interact with CNX1, a Moco biosynthesis enzyme, which might facilitate AtNIA2 activity (Kaufholdt et al., 2016).

# Post-translational modifications of GS

There are quite a number of PTMs reported toward GS, such as phosphorylation, oxidation, tyrosine nitration, and S-nitrosylation, but the regulatory mechanisms underlying are still far from being fully understood. The activity of AtGLN1.1 can be modulated via its phosphorylation in which CDPK-related kinase 23 (AtCRK3) is involved (Li et al., 2006). Oxidative turnover of wheat GS2 (Palatnik et al., 1999) and phosphorylation of GS isoforms in maize roots (Prinsi and Espen, 2015) were also reported.

# Improving cereal NUE by manipulating nitrogen assimilation

As the pivotal role of N assimilation in crop growth and yield, manipulation of N assimilation is always considered as a potential strategy to improve NUE in crops (Table 2).

OsNR2 is divergent between indica and japonica, and OsNR2- indica displays a higher NR activity. Overexpression of OsNR2- japonica and OsNR2-indica results in the increased grain yield, and overexpression of the later one causes higher enhancement. Besides, introduction of OsNR2-indica into japonica variety also boosts NR activity and grain production (Gao et al., 2019). Increased expression level of OsNiR1 is consistent with increased panicle branches and grain yield (Yu et al., 2021). OsNLP4, the key transcriptional factor of rice NUE, directly activates not only N assimilation genes, but also N transporter genes (Wu et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021). Therefore, overexpression of OsNLP4 results in increased grain yield and NUE under different N conditions (Wu et al., 2021).

In rice, overexpression of OsGS1;2 in a constitutive manner led to enhanced N assimilation, while the yield was only increased under particular conditions (Cai et al., 2009; Brauer et al., 2011; Bao et al., 2014). Increased expression of OsGS1;1 constitutively caused decreased grain yield (Cai et al., 2009; Bao et al., 2014), and a similar result was observed in OsNADH-GOGAT1 activation tagging lines (Lee et al., 2020a). However, both overexpression of OsNADH-GOGAT1 under its native promoter or concurrent activation of OsAMT1;2 and OsNADH-GOGAT1 resulted in enhanced grain yield

(Lee et al., 2020a; Yamaya et al., 2002). ARE1 was screened out as a down-regulator of rice NUE, and knock-out of ARE1 results in the recovery of decreased Fd-GOGAT activity in osfd-gogat1 mutant and increased grain number per panicle, and eventually $10 \% { - } 2 0 \%$ increased grain yield under N-limited conditions (Wang et al., 2018). The haplotypes of ARE1 in 9311 and Minghui63 (MH63) were considered as the elite allele, and the near-isogenic lines containing $A R E 1 ^ { 9 3 1 1 }$ and $A R E 1 ^ { M H 6 3 }$ displayed increased grain yield by $1 0 . 2 \% -$ $2 8 . 4 \%$ and $1 0 . 4 \% - 2 6 . 2 \%$ , respectively, under low N conditions (Wang et al., 2021). Besides, ARE1 is highly conserved among different plants, knock-out of TaARE1 also causes increased grain yield in the field (Guo et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a). ZmGLN1.3 is the key factor for kernel number, and increased ZmGLN1.3 expression driven by cassava vein mosaic virus promoter (pCsVMV) resulted in $\sim 3 0 \%$ increased grain yield (Martin et al., 2006). Overexpression of TaNAC2-5A, a transcription factor that directly activates the expression of several nitrate transporter genes and TaGS2-2A, resulted in increased N uptake and assimilation, and further enhanced grain yield by $6 . 7 \% - 1 0 \%$ and $5 \% - 1 7 . 1 \%$ under low N and high N conditions, respectively (He et al., 2015). The transgenic lines overexpressing TaGS2-2Ab, the elite allele of TaGS2-2A, under the control of its native promoter, not only increased N uptake, but also enhanced the remobilization of N to grains. Furthermore, the grain yield of transgenic lines was increased $5 . 4 \% - 1 1 . 1 \%$ and $8 . 4 \% - 1 3 . 5 \%$ under low N and high N conditions, respectively (Hu et al., 2018). Knock-down of TabZIP60, the repressor of N utilization, led to $5 3 . 2 \% - 7 7 . 8 \%$ increased NADH-GOGAT activity and $2 5 . 1 \% - 3 9 . 6 \%$ increased grain yield. # Perspectives

N assimilation is a main process for N utilization in plants. NR, NiR, GS, GOGAT, and enzymes related to N assimilation are encoded by a small multigene family. Although a number of works have reported their functions, the functions of each member are still not fully elucidated yet, especially for functions of ASN, GDH, and CPSase in crops (Fig. 1B). It is widely accepted that GS/GOGAT is the predominant pathway for $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ assimilation. However, the biological significance of ASN, GDH, and CPSase in $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ assimilation is nonnegligible (Potel et al., 2009; Moison et al., 2018). NO is an important regulator for the growth and development of plants. One of the origins of NO is reduction via NR. NR participates in both nitrate reduction and NO generation processes; how NR is precisely regulated for balancing these two distinct processes is a very interesting topic. As the products of GS/GOGAT cycle, glutamine and glutamate are not only the participants of amino acid metabolism, but also the potential signal molecules involved in different pathways (Forde and Lea, 2007; Zhang et al., 2021b), suggesting that the role of GS/ GOGAT is not limited to N assimilation. For example, flowering time of rice is regulated by glutamine (Zhang et al., 2021b). From the biotechnological point of view, GS is also the target of a broadspectrum herbicide phosphinothricin (PPT), which is the structural analog of glutamate and is able to occupy the glutamate binding site of GS. The transgenic crops with high PPT tolerance were created by increasing GS expression levels (Cai et al., 2009). Besides, GS/ GOGAT is also involved in conferring tolerance to stress (Sun et al., 2017). Taken together, components of N assimilation participate in different aspects of plant growth and development, thereby N assimilation needs to be regulated precisely at both transcriptional and post-translational levels. More efforts on exploring the components regulating N assimilation should be paid in the future, especially for NiR and GOGAT, as very limited knowledge about its regulation is reported so far (Fig. 2).

In plants, C and N metabolism are tightly interacted: the energy and C-skeletons from C metabolism are essential for N utilization, and organic N generated by N assimilation, such as amino acids are components of proteins, including indispensable proteins for C metabolic reactions. Therefore, the internal balance of C and N is critical for plant growth. HY5 is a key transcription factor that integrates C and N metabolism by regulating the expression of genes not only related to photosynthesis, but also involved in N assimilation, such as AtNIA2. Intriguingly, with light, HY5 protein synthesized in shoot moves to root and enhances nitrate uptake by activating AtNRT2.1 expression (Chen et al., 2016), which makes HY5 to work as a signal for mediation of C/N balance. 2-OG, the substrate of GOGAT, the catalysate of GDH, and carbon skeleton for amino acid synthesis, is a key organic acid of the tricarboxylic acid cycle, making the pivotal role of GS/GOGAT, and GDH in N and C metabolic balance. In cyanobacterial, 2-OG acts as an indicator of higher C/N ratio, and regulates activity of a transcriptional repressor to change the expression of genes related to carbon metabolism (Jiang et al., 2018). Moreover, high 2-OG content is consistent with enhanced nitrate uptake via phosphorylated PII, a regulator of nitrate uptake (Lee et al., 2000). In plants, the level of 2-OG fluctuates depending on C/N status (Hodges, 2002); therefore, whether 2-OG worked as a signal to regulate C/N metabolism is deserved to be explored. Besides, the expression of AtGLN1.3 and N assimilation is regulated by the circadian rhythm regulating factor CCA1, whose expression is under the control of organic N, the product of N assimilation (Gutierrez et al., 2008 ). Except for that mentioned above, it is

reasonable to hypothesize that there are other factors coupling C/N metabolism together to maintain C/N balance in plants, for example, components of C metabolism directly regulate N utilization, and vice versa.

Even though multiple attempts have been made to increase crop NUE via regulating N assimilation, less attempt via regulating NR and NiR activity, which deserve more efforts for their function and application, is reported, and the ideal results in the improvement of grain yield were rarely achieved, especially for rice (Cai et al., 2009; Brauer et al., 2011). Strict temporal and spatial expression of GS and GOGAT and complicated regulation of PTMs may be critical for their function and maintaining C/N balance in plants. Therefore, the decreased growth and grain yield of transgenic lines constitutively expressing OsGS1;1/OsGS1;2 might result from the imbalance in C/ N metabolism (Bao et al., 2014). For wheat, an allohexaploid species, the large genome might provide enhanced genome buffering capacity and less C/N imbalance by heterologous expression, so that overexpression of TaNADH-GOGAT1-3B leads to enhanced grain yields dramatically even though it was constitutively expressed (Yang et al., 2019). Elevating GS/GOGAT levels by their native promoters or by tissue-specific promoters, or modifying GS/GOGAT activity by their regulators, like TFs or partner proteins involved in PTMs might be effective strategies. Furthermore, exploration of their natural variations in different germplasms might also be instrumental for crop improvement.
