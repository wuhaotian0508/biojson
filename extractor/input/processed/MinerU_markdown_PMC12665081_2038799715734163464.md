# ABSTRACT

Phosphorus is a crucial nutrient for the normal growth and development of rice. Enhancing the efficiency of phosphorus acquisition in plants is vital for optimising soil phosphorus utilisation. The SUMO E3 ligase OsSIZ1 is an important factor for regulating plant phosphorus acquisition and starvation response in rice. However, how its activity responds to Pi-supplied conditions remains unknown. In this study, we discovered that the mutations in OsCK2β3, which encodes a regulatory subunit of CK2, influenced the Pi starvation response. The global level of SUMOylation was found to decrease in Osck2β3 mutants, mirroring the effects observed under Pi-deficiency treatment. Additionally, we identified that OsCK2 can interact with and phosphorylate OsSIZ1, thereby impacting its stability. Transcriptome analysis with Ossiz1 and Osck2β3 mutants revealed that the ‘OsCK2- OsSIZ1’ module primarily regulates defence responses by influencing the expression of genes associated with the biosynthesis of diterpenoid phytoalexins and pathogenesis-related genes, in addition to impacting the response to phosphate starvation. Notably, the overexpression of a variant of OsSIZ1, in which the phosphorylation sites targeted by OsCK2 were mutated, enhanced phosphorus acquisition and defence responses while preserving normal growth. Consequently, we not only showed how OsSIZ1 activity responds to Pi starvation but also created a variant of OsSIZ1 that improves phosphorus acquisition efficiency and enhances resistance against multiple pathogens, all without inducing growth defects. 

# 1 | Introduction

Phosphorus is one of the mineral nutrients necessary for the normal growth and development of rice (Raghothama  1999; Lopez-Arredondo et al. 2014). To ensure a stable and high rice yield, phosphorus fertilisers are extensively applied in the fields. However, due to their low utilisation rate, a significant amount of unused phosphorus fertilisers can leach into rivers, leading to pollution (Vance et  al.  2003). Additionally, phosphate rock, the primary source of phosphorus fertilisers, is projected to be depleted within 50 to 100 years at the current usage rate (Baker 

et al. 2015). Therefore, enhancing phosphorus acquisition and utilisation efficiency in rice is vital for fostering efficient and sustainable agricultural practices. In response to the phosphate (Pi) deficiency, plants enhance the expression of signalling genes related to phosphorus acquisition and utilisation, thereby alleviating the growth restriction caused by phosphorus deficiency (Lopez-Arredondo et al. 2014; Jia et al. 2023). Therefore, analysing the molecular regulatory network governing phosphorus uptake and utilisation in rice can provide a theoretical foundation for the genetic enhancement of phosphorus nutrient efficiency in rice cultivation. 

SIZ1 (SAP and Miz 1), a SUMO E3 ligase localised to the nucleus, is an important factor in regulating plant Pi homeostasis and starvation response in plants (Miura et al. 2005; Wang et al. 2015). Protein SUMO modification is to covalently bind the mature SUMO protein to the substrate protein, facilitated by SUMO-activating enzyme (E1), SUMO-binding enzyme (E2) and SUMO ligase (E3), to regulate the stability, subcellular localisation and protein activity of the targets (Datta et  al.  2018). Mutations AtSIZ1 in Arabidopsis thaliana and OsSIZ1 in rice result in increased Pi accumulation (Miura et al. 2005; Wang et al. 2015). Plant SIZ1 protein has five conserved domains, namely SAP, PHD, PINIT, SP-RING and SIM (Miura et  al.  2005). The SIM motif promotes the interaction between SUMO and SIZ1 (Park et al. 2011; Rytz et al. 2018). On the basis of the expression of Pi starvation-induced (PSI) genes, previous studies have indicated that AtSIZ1 plays both positive and negative roles in regulating Pi starvation response (Miura et al. 2005). Furthermore, Arabidopsis AtSIZ1 has the capacity to modify AtPHR1 in vitro, a key transcription factor that governs the Pi starvation response in plants, although the biological significance of this SUMO modification remains unverified (Miura et  al.  2005). Notably, the expression levels of rice OsSIZ1 and Arabidopsis AtSIZ1 do not change in response to Pi supplied conditions, suggesting that post-translational modifications of SIZ1 may be the mechanism that responds to such conditions. 

Protein kinase CK2 is a highly conserved serine/threonine protein kinase that is widely present in eukaryotes, which is a heterotetramer composed of two regulatory subunits, $\mathrm { C K } 2 \beta$ and two kinase subunits, $\mathrm { C K } 2 \alpha$ (Riera et  al.  2011; Mulekar and Huq  2014). The substrate specificity of CK2 is characterised by the presence of multiple acidic amino acids flanking serine or threonine residues. In plants, CK2 subunits are primarily localised in the nucleus and cytoplasm (Salinas et al. 2006; Chen et al. 2015; Rodiger et al. 2020). Under Pisufficient conditions, the rice OsCK2 holoenzyme, consisting of OsCK2α3 and OsCK2β3, phosphorylates OsPHT1 family proteins, which results in their retention in the endoplasmic reticulum and prevents Pi overaccumulation (Chen et al. 2015; Yang et al. 2020). Additionally, OsCK2 plays a role in regulating phosphorus translocation from roots to shoots in rice by phosphorylating the ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme OsPHO2 (Wang et al. 2020). Our phosphorylation proteomics analysis indicated that Pi deficiency caused significant alterations in the phosphorylation levels of proteins in the roots, with approximately $2 1 . 5 \%$ of these proteins predicted to be substrates of OsCK2 (Yang et al. 2019). The interactions between OsCK2 and OsPHT1 or OsPHO2 occur within the endoplasmic reticulum (Chen et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2020). However, both OsCK2α3 and $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ are also localised in the nucleus, which serves as the primary site for gene expression regulation. Consequently, it remains unclear whether OsCK2 is involved in regulating the phosphorus starvation response in rice. 

Pi excess and Pi deficiency increase susceptibility and resistance to pathogen infection, respectively (Campos-Soriano et  al.  2020). Diterpenoid phytoalexins play crucial roles in resistance against multiple pathogens. On the basis of their structures, diterpenoid phytoalexins in rice can be categorised 

into three major groups: phytocassanes, momilactones and oryzalexins (Valletta et  al.  2023; Bian et  al.  2024). The biosynthetic pathways of diterpenoid phytoalexins in rice have been elucidated, with geranylgeranyl diphosphate serving as the common precursor for these phytoalexins. Key synthases involved in this process include copalyl diphosphate synthase (OsCPS), kaurene synthase-like (OsKSL), cytochrome P450 monooxygenase (OsCYP99A3) and dehydrogenase (OsMAS) (Yamamura et al. 2015; Lu et al. 2018; Mao et al. 2020; Valletta et  al.  2023). Nine transcription factors have been identified to directly regulate the expressions of these genes such as OsbZIP87/79, OsbHLH5/25/26 and OsWRKY10/45/53/76 (Bian et  al.  2024; Zhou et  al.  2024; Liao et  al.  2025; Lin et al. 2025). However, the potential role of diterpenoid phytoalexins in modulating resistance associated with Pi deficiency remains unexplored. 

In this study, we found that OsCK2β3 affected Pi starvation response in rice. Additionally, we observed a reduction in global SUMOylation levels in Osck2β3 mutants, mirroring the effects of Pi deficiency treatment. Further, we revealed that OsCK2 interacts with and phosphorylates OsSIZ1. Transcriptome analysis indicated that the ‘OsCK2-OsSIZ1’ module plays a role in mediating rice disease resistance by regulating the expression of genes involved in the biosynthesis of diterpenoid phytoalexins. Finally, we confirmed that the overexpression of non-phosphorylatable OsSIZ1 not only improved Pi accumulation without growth penalty, but also induced the expression of genes responsible for the biosynthesis of diterpenoid phytoalexins, thereby enhancing resistance against multiple pathogens.

Our previous phosphorylation proteomics analysis revealed that phosphorylated OsSIZ1 decreased following Pi deficiency, with the identified phosphorylation site being a typical CK2 phosphorylation motif (Yang et  al.  2019). We subsequently generated Ossiz1 mutants by CRISPR-Cas9 to evaluate expressions of PSI genes (Figure S1B). # 2.2 | OsCK2 Could Interact With OsSIZ1

To explore the potential direct interaction between OsCK2 and OsSIZ1, we initially conducted a yeast two-hybrid experiment. This involved cloning two $\mathrm { C K } 2 \alpha$ -coding genes, OsCK2α2 and OsCK2α3, along with two $\mathrm { C K } 2 \beta$ -encoding genes, OsCK2β1 and OsCK2β3, into bait vectors, while inserting the OsSIZ1 coding sequence into a prey vector. Following the co-transformation of these vectors into AH109 yeast cells, the combination of OsCK2β3 and OsSIZ1 constructs exhibited growth on -Leu-Trp-His-Ade medium, indicating that $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ interacts with OsSIZ1 in yeast (Figure  2A). To pinpoint the $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ -interacting domain of OsSIZ1, we divided the OsSIZ1 protein into two segments: OsSIZ1-N (amino acids 1–450, which includes the SAP, PHD, PINIT and SR-RING domains, as well as the first SIM domain) and 

OsSIZ1-C (amino acids 410–876, which contains the second SIM domain) (Figure S3). The results showed that OsCK2β3 strongly interacted with the N terminus of OsSIZ1, but not with the C terminus, in yeast. A pulldown assay further demonstrated that His-OsSIZ1 could be pulled down by GST-OsCK2β3, but not by GST alone, thereby confirming their interaction in  vitro (Figure  2B). To validate this interaction in plants, we expressed OsSIZ1-Flag alongside $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ -GFP or GFP in tobacco leaves. Following immunoprecipitation with a GFP antibody, OsSIZ1-Flag was detected in conjunction with $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ -GFP, but not with GFP alone (Figure 2C). The split bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay clearly illustrated that their interaction occurred within the nucleus of the plant (Figure 2D). Additionally, we discovered that the kinase subunit OsCK2α3 could interact with OsSIZ1, facilitated by $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ in plants. Collectively, these findings suggest that the OsCK2 holoenzyme can interact with OsSIZ1 both in vivo and in vitro. 

# 2.3 | OsCK2 Could Phosphorylate OsSIZ1

To investigate whether OsCK2 phosphorylates OsSIZ1, we initially predicted the potential phosphorylation sites in OsSIZ1 targeted by OsCK2 using the Group-based Prediction System (GPS). This analysis identified 12 serine/threonine (S/T) residues as potential CK2 substrates (Figure S4). A kinase assay was conducted utilising Phos-tag Biotin, which allows for the detection of phosphorylated proteins with HRP-labelled Streptavidin. The results indicated that OsSIZ1 could be phosphorylated by the OsCK2 holoenzyme, but not by GST alone (Figure 3B). To refine the identification of target sites, we compared the protein sequences of OsSIZ1 and AtSIZ1, revealing six conserved sites— $\cdot S ^ { 3 4 8 }$ , $\mathrm { S } ^ { 3 5 0 }$ , S607, S609, $\mathrm { T } ^ { 8 7 3 }$ and $S ^ { 8 7 5 }$ —as the most likely phosphorylation targets of OsCK2 (Figure  S5). The phosphorylated band was absent when all six conserved sites were mutated to alanine (His-OsSIZ16A) (Figure  3B). To pinpoint the specific sites targeted by OsCK2, we created three pairs of mutated OsSIZ1 proteins: His-OsSIZ1S348/350A, His-OsSIZ1S607/609A and His-OsSIZ1T873A/S875A (Figure  3A). Only His-OsSIZ1S607/609A was unable to be phosphorylated by OsCK2, indicating that one or both sites are direct targets (Figure 3B). 

Mass spectrometry is a valuable tool for directly identifying phosphorylation sites. However, it is most effective for detecting peptides ranging from 5 to 20 amino acids. Common proteolytic enzymes such as Trypsin, Glu-C, or Lys-C are typically employed to digest proteins. Unfortunately, no suitable peptides could be generated after digestion with these enzymes based on predictions. Consequently, we constructed the GST-OsSIZ1-pep protein (Figure 3C), which included a short peptide (ATDVIVLS ${ } ^ { 6 0 7 } \mathrm { D S } ^ { 6 0 9 }$ DEENDAMV) expressed in-frame following GST, incorporating a lysine residue that serves as a cleavage site for Chymotrypsin. The phosphorylation level decreased when either $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ or $S ^ { 6 0 9 }$ was mutated to alanine. Results indicated that overexpression of $O s S I Z 1 ^ { A A }$ -Flag but not OsSIZ1-Flag significantly enhanced root hair length compared to its wild type under Pi starvation (Figure  S2). These findings suggest that the non-phosphorylated variant of OsSIZ1 plays a dominant positive role in regulating Pi homeostasis and Pi starvation response in rice. 

# 2.5 | Mutation of S607 and S609 in OsSIZ1 Affected Its Stability in Response to Pi Starvation

To investigate the impact of non-phosphorylated $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 9 }$ on the localisation of OsSIZ1 in plants, we expressed OsSIZ1-GFP, OsSIZ1AA-GFP and GFP alone in tobacco leaves (Figure  S6A). While GFP was detected in both the nucleus and cytoplasm, OsSIZ1-GFP and OsSIZ1AA-GFP were exclusively localised in the nucleus. Pi starvation resulted in approximately a $5 0 \%$ reduction in OsSIZ1-Flag protein levels. Both His-OsSIZ1 and His-OsSIZ1AA were gradually degraded after incubation in a cell-free degradation solution containing proteins extracted from leaves of plants grown under Pi-sufficient and -deficient conditions, but this degradation was inhibited by MG132, a 26S proteasome inhibitor (Figure 5C). His-OsSIZ1 was degraded more rapidly in the solution containing proteins from Pi-sufficient cultured plants compared to those from Pideficient cultured plants. Additionally, His-OsSIZ1AA was degraded faster than His-OsSIZ1 in the solution with proteins 

from Pi-sufficient cultured plants, and even more quickly than in the presence of proteins from Pi-deficient cultured plants. These findings suggest that OsCK2 phosphorylates OsSIZ1 at $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 9 }$ in response to Pi-supplied conditions, thereby inf luencing its stability.

# 3 | Discussion

Plant CK2 subunits are predominantly localised to the nucleus and cytoplasm (Salinas et  al.  2006; Chen et  al.  2015). In rice, the OsCK2 holoenzyme, composed of OsCK2α3 and phosphorylated $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ , regulates Pi homeostasis by phosphorylating OsPHT1 family proteins and the ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme OsPHO2. However, the interaction between OsCK2 and OsPHT1 or OsPHO2 occurs in the endoplasmic reticulum (Chen et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2020). The nucleus serves as the primary site for gene expression regulation. Consequently, it remains unclear whether OsCK2 also influences the Pi starvation response in rice. In this study, we discovered that OsCK2 regulates gene expression primarily through OsSIZ1 in rice, generating a variant of OsSIZ1 that enhances Pi absorption and plant defence without incurring growth penalties. 

Our findings indicate that OsCK2 can interact with OsSIZ1 in vitro and specifically phosphorylate OsSIZ1 at $\mathbf { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $S ^ { 6 0 9 }$ (Figure 3). Our phosphoproteome depends on the enrichment of phosphorylated proteins using TiO2 bulk beads, which suggests that the phosphorylation level of OsSIZ1 is diminished, even though the phosphorylation sites identified in this study were not observed (Yang et al. 2019). The protease trypsin used in our earlier research could only digest proteins at the carboxyl side of lysine or arginine (Yang et al. 2019), resulting in a 53-amino acid fragment of OsSIZ1 containing $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $S ^ { 6 0 9 }$ , which is outside the effective range of mass spectrometry detection. In this study, by mutating $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $S ^ { 6 0 9 }$ to alanine in the kinase assay and truncating OsSIZ1 for mass spectrometry detection, we ultimately confirmed the phosphorylated sites in OsSIZ1 for OsCK2 (Figure 3). Interestingly, PIAS1, the human homologue of OsSIZ1, can be phosphorylated by CK2 

at the same SIM domain (Stehmeier and Muller 2009), indicating a conserved regulatory pathway. 

As the interaction between OsCK2 and OsSIZ1 occurs in the nucleus, gene expression modification is expected to be the outcome of this interaction. The overexpression of the non-phosphorylatable OsSIZ1 improved Pi accumulation without growth penalties in shoot. The expression of many PSI genes could be adjusted to improve phosphorus absorption in rice; however, this process concurrently hinders plant growth (Zhou et al. 2008; Wang et al. 2014; Jia et al. 2017; Xu et al. 2019). We aimed to identify the general pathway regulated by the ‘OsCK2-OsSIZ1’ module in shoots. Transcriptome data revealed that OsPT2 and OsPAP21b were also induced in the shoots of both Osck2β3 and Ossiz1 mutants (Table S1). In roots, OsPT8 was induced in both Osck2β3 and Ossiz1 mutants, while OsIPS1 remained unchanged. This phenomenon is reminiscent of the Atsiz1, where the PSI genes AtPT2, AtPS2 and AtPS3 were induced, but AtRNS1 was reduced in Arabidopsis (Miura et al. 2005). Furthermore, the upregulation of OsPT2 and OsPAP21b, but not other PSI genes such as OsIPS1, suggests that OsSIZ1 may regulate Pi homeostasis through transcription factors other than OsPHR2 in rice, as OsPHR2 broadly regulates PSI genes (Zhou et al. 2008; Wang et al. 2014). Transcriptome analysis indicated that the majority of the induced genes in the Osck2β3 mutant were also upregulated in the Ossiz1 mutant. However, the induced genes accounted for only one-quarter of the upregulated genes in the Ossiz1 mutant (Figure 6). Notably, most of the upregulated gene expression levels were higher in Ossiz1 mutants than in Osck2β3 mutants. These results suggest that $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ regulates gene expression primarily through OsSIZ1 in rice, but OsCK2 only modifies a small portion of OsSIZ1's function. 

The SIM domain promotes the binding of SUMO to human PIAS1, the homologue of OsSIZ1, as mutation of the SIM domain to a non-phosphorylatable version (S to A) decreased its ability to bind to SUMO paralogs (Stehmeier and Muller 2009). However, our results indicated that mutation in the SIM domain of OsSIZ1 did not alter its ability to bind to SUMO1 (Figure S6B), similar to the effect observed with the SIM domain mutation of AtSIZ1 in Arabidopsis (Cheong et al. 2009). Degradation assays revealed that OsSIZ1 was more stable under Pi-sufficient conditions. As $\mathrm { O s C K } 2 \beta 3$ tends to degrade after Pi starvation (Chen et  al.  2015), OsSIZ1AA, mimicking the non-phosphorylatable form, exhibited a faster degradation rate than OsSIZ1, further demonstrating the role of the SIM 

domain in regulating OsSIZ1 stability in rice. Overexpression of $O s S I Z 1 ^ { A A }$ induced PSI genes and genes involved in diterpenoid phytoalexin biosynthesis, similar to the effects of OsSIZ1 mutation, suggesting that this non-phosphorylatable OsSIZ1 seems to be a loss-of-function mutant. However, overexpression of $O s S I Z 1 ^ { A A }$ enhanced global SUMOylation levels without inhibiting growth (Figures  4 and 5), indicating that its SUMO E3 ligase activity may not be compromised by the mutation. Indeed, complementing the AtSIZ1AA, which involved mutating the same double serines to alanines, nearly fully restored the growth and SUMOylation levels in the Arabidopsis Atsiz1 mutant (Cheong et  al.  2009). Previous studies have shown that the SIM domain can act as a bridge to interact with SUMOylated proteins (Lascorz et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2023). Thus, we speculate that the phosphorylated status of OsSIZ1 at $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 7 }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 6 0 9 }$ may also influence its interaction with certain SUMOylated proteins. 

Venn analysis indicated that the majority of the induced genes in Osck2β3 mutants were also induced in Ossiz1 mutants, confirming that OsCK2 regulates gene expression primarily through OsSIZ1, albeit affecting only a small portion of OsSIZ1's function in rice. Pi starvation not only activates the Pi uptake system but also triggers defence responses (Campos-Soriano et al. 2020; He et al. 2024). GO analysis revealed that the ‘OsCK2-OsSIZ1’ module mainly regulates diterpene phytoalexin metabolism and defence responses in addition to the Pi starvation response in the shoot (Figure 6). Diterpenoid phytoalexins have been widely reported to play a role in defence responses (Valletta et al. 2023; Bian et  al.  2024). In this study, genes encoding enzymes involved in phytocassane (OsCPS2 and OsKSL7), momilactone (OsCPS4, OsCYP99A3, OsKSL4, OsMAS1 and OsMAS2) and oryzalexin (OsKSL8 and OsKSL10) were induced in Osck2β3 and Ossiz1 mutants. These genes, along with pathogenesis-related genes, have been shown to function in plant defence responses (Okada et  al.  2009; Yamamura et  al.  2015; Zhou et  al.  2024). Interestingly, $O s S I Z 1 ^ { A A }$ -Flag lines exhibited increased resistance to M. oryzae, R. solani and Xoo through the induction of gene expressions involved in the biosynthesis of diterpenoid phytoalexins and pathogenesis-related genes. Thus, the nonphosphorylated variant of OsSIZ1 plays a dominant positive role in regulating defence responses in rice. 

In summary, we found that rice OsCK2 could interact with and phosphorylate OsSIZ1 to regulate Pi starvation response, including genes involved in Pi uptake and defence response in rice (Figure S7). Importantly, we discovered that overexpressing the non-phosphorylatable OsSIZ1 not only improves phosphorus absorption efficiency but also enhances defence responses against pathogens without growth deficits. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

Ossiz1 mutants and Osck2β3 mutants were generated via CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated genome editing in the background of Oryza sativa cv. Zhonghua 11 (wild type). To develop OsSIZ1-Flag and the phospho-deficient OsSIZ1AA-Flag (carrying S607A/S609A substitutions) overexpression lines, the full-length CDS of OsSIZ1 or 

$O s S I Z 1 ^ { A A }$ were subcloned into the Ubipro-Flag and then transformed into rice calluses using Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 (Yan et al. 2024). Germinated seeds were transplanted into Kimura nutrient solution for hydroponic cultivation under controlled greenhouse conditions, which included a 12-h light $\left( 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \right) / 1 2$ -h dark $( 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ photoperiod, a photosynthetic photon flux density of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ and approximately $6 0 \%$ relative humidity. After 1week of growth, seedlings were replenished with fresh Kimura solution and allowed to equilibrate for 3days before experimental treatments. For physiological phenotyping, plants were subjected to Pi concentrations of 5 and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for another 3 to 4weeks. For RNA and protein extraction, seedlings were exposed to $0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ or $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi for 1week. 

# 4.2 | Yeast Two-Hybrid Protein Interaction Assay

The full length OsSIZ1 (2631 bp), N-terminal region (1350 bp) and C-terminal region (1398 bp) were cloned into the pGADT7 (AD) vector to generate AD-OsSIZ1, AD-OsSIZ1-N and AD-OsSIZ1-C. The coding regions of OsCK2α2, OsCK2α3, OsCK2β1, OsCK2β3 and OsSUMO1 were cloned into the pGBKT7 (BD) vector. The pre-mixed experimental plasmids were co-transformed into Saccharomyces cerevisiae strain AH109 using the lithium acetate method. The transformed yeast cells were plated onto SD/-Leu/-Trp double dropout (DDO) agar plates (COOLABER, China) and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 36 to $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . After colony formation, individual colonies were resuspended in sterile water and spotted onto both DDO and SD/-Ade/-His/-Leu/-Trp quadruple dropout (QDO) plates (COOLABER, China) to validate protein– protein interactions. pGADT7 (AD) and pGBKT7 (BD) served as negative controls. 

# 4.3 | GST Pull-Down Assay

The coding sequence for the short peptides (ATDVIVLS607 $\mathrm { D S } ^ { 6 0 9 }$ DEENDAMV) and its mutations, as well as the coding regions of OsCK2α3 and OsCK2β3, was individually inserted into the pGEX4T-1 vector. The full-length OsSIZ1 and its mutations were also separately cloned into the pET-HIS vector. Protein purification was performed using affinity beads specific to the protein tags; for instance, GST-tagged proteins were purified with glutathione Sepharose 4 Fast Flow beads (Cytiva, USA), while His-tagged proteins were isolated using Ni-NTA agarose beads (QIAGEN, Germany). A GST pulldown assay was conducted as previously reported (Yang et al. 2020). The proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and analysed through immunoblotting using an anti-GST antibody (Transgen Biotech, China) and an anti-His antibody (Transgen Biotech, China). 

# 4.4 | BiFC Assays

A split YFP system was used to perform the BiFC assays (Yang et  al.  2020). The coding sequence of OsCK2α3 and OsCK2β3 was individually cloned into the N-terminal fragments of YFP vectors to generate OsCK2α3-YFPN and OsCK2β3-YFPN. The coding sequences of OsSIZ1 were cloned into the C-terminal fragments of YFP vectors to generate OsSIZ1-YFPC. The coding sequence of OsCK2β3 was cloned into the C-terminal fragments 

of the ProUbi:Flag vector. All constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium EHA105. Single colonies of transformed EHA105 were inoculated in YEP medium and cultured overnight at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking $( 2 2 0 \mathrm { r p m } )$ . Bacterial suspensions were mixed equally, centrifuged at $3 0 0 0 \times g$ (room temperature, 10 min) and resuspended in 1/2 MS medium supplemented with $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone. The mixture was infiltrated into the abaxial leaf surface using a needle-free syringe. Post-infiltration, plants were incubated in darkness for 12–16 h, followed by $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ under normal light (16/8 h light/dark cycle). BiFC fluorescence signals were captured using a Leica TCS SP5II confocal microscope. 

# 4.5 | Co-IP Assays

The coding sequence of OsCK2β3 was individually cloned into pCAMBIA1300-GFP (Pro35S:GFP) vector to generate Pro35S:OsCK2β3-GFP. Following the transient transformation described in the section of BiFC assays, leaves were harvested, homogenised and total protein was extracted in binding buffer (50mM Tris–HCl pH7.5, 150mM NaCl, $0 . 1 \%$ NP-40). Input samples were reserved for verification. GFP-tagged proteins were immunoprecipitated using GFP-Trap magnetic beads (ChromoTek, Germany) with 2 h rotation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After washing three times, beads were resuspended in SDS loading buffer, boiled at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min and subjected to Western blotting. 

# 4.6 | In Vitro Kinase Assays and MS Analysis

Following thawing and concentration adjustment of purified proteins, kinase and substrate were mixed at a 1:3 M ratio in $5 \times$ reaction buffer $\mathrm { 1 0 0 m M }$ Tris pH 8.0, 50 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5mM DTT, 5 mM EGTA), with or without supplementation with $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ATP (Sangon Biotech, China). The reaction mixture was incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, then divided into two aliquots: one portion was flash-frozen at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for subsequent MS analysis, while the other was mixed with SDS loading buffer, boiled and subjected to SDS-PAGE. Phosphorylation was detected using Phos-tag Biotin (Wako Pure Chemical Industries, Japan) via streptavidin-HRP blotting. MS analysis of frozen samples was performed with the help of APTBIO company. 

# 4.7 | Global SUMOylation Detection

Plant materials cultivated with or without $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi were flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground, followed by direct homogenisation in SDS loading buffer. Total proteins were extracted by boiling at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min under denaturing conditions, separated by SDS-PAGE $1 2 \%$ gel) and transferred to PVDF membranes (Millipore, USA). Global SUMOylation levels were analysed using an anti-SUMO1 antibody (1:2000 dilution, Abcam). 

# 4.8 | Cell-Free Degradation

For protein extraction, the shoots of WT cultivated under ${ 0 } { \mu } { \bf { M } }$ and ${ 2 0 0 } \mathrm { M m }$ Pi conditions were homogenised. Then, the 

purified His-tagged proteins were adjusted to equal concentrations and aliquoted into EP tubes with gradient time points (0, 30, 60, 90, 120 min, including $1 2 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132 protease inhibitor). Next, the reaction mixtures containing ATP (5 mM) and DTT (1 mM) were incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . At each time point, the samples were immediately terminated by adding SDS loading buffer, boiled at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C - } 1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Finally, after all treatments, the samples were subjected to Western blotting using anti-His antibodies to detect protein degradation patterns. 

# 4.9 | Magnaporthe oryzae Cultivation and Infection

The Zhong 10-8-14 strain and experimental protocols were from Prof. Chen Xuewei's laboratory at Sichuan Agricultural University (Liao et al. 2025). Regarding subculturing, mycelial plugs from stock cultures were transferred to PDA medium and incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until nearly confluent growth (about 7 days). Then, 3 mL of sterilised water was added and the mixture was pipette-mixed to fragment the mycelia, which were then spread onto oat-tomato agar. After 2 days of static incubation at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , sterile cotton swabs were used to remove surface hyphae. Next, the plates were rinsed with sterile water, sealed with doublelayer gauze and exposed to light for 2 to 3 days to induce sporulation. Rice plants cultivated for 1 month under 5 and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi conditions were selected for blast resistance assessment. At 2–3 days post-sporulation of Magnaporthe oryzae, 6 cm leaf segments from identical nodal positions were excised and immediately immersed in $3 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ 6-BA solution within 9 cm Petri dishes. Leaf surfaces were gently wounded at $1 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ intervals using sterile pipette tips, then returned to the 6-BA solution. Fungal plates were hydrated with $4 { - } 5 \mathrm { m L }$ sterile water and hyphae were fragmented using pipette tips to collect spore suspensions. Spore concentration was quantified via haemocytometer under microscopy and adjusted to $3 { - } 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores/mL. Each wounded leaf received $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ spore suspension at three sites (with resuspension between applications). After $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ of dark incubation at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , samples were transferred to light conditions $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 14 h/10 h photoperiod) for 7 days for symptom development, followed by photographic documentation and calculation of the average lesion length (mm) of $\geq 2 1$ individual lesions as the resistance index. 

# 4.10 | Rhizoctonia solani Cultivation and Infection

Rice plants were cultivated for 30 days under 5 and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi conditions. Subsequently, fully expanded leaves with excised tips were detached and placed on water-saturated filter papers in trays. Additional moistened filter papers were used to cover both leaf termini to prevent desiccation. The R. solani strain AG-1 IA was inoculated onto potato–sucrose–agar medium at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ until complete mycelial colonisation. 5-mm-diameter mycelial plugs were aseptically excised from the colonised plates and placed adaxially on the leaf surfaces. Lesion development was monitored until appropriate symptoms. Then, photographs were taken and the length of the lesion was measured (Wang et al. 2025). 

# 4.11 | Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae Cultivation and Infection

Rice plants were cultivated for 30 days under 5 and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi conditions. Xoo strain PXO99 was inoculated onto potato–sucrose–agar medium at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . Bacterial suspensions were prepared to an optical density of $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8 { - } 1 . 0$ using sterile water. Inoculation was performed by dipping sterile scissors into the bacterial suspension and cutting approximately 2 cm from the tips of fully expanded leaves. After a 12-day infection period, all inoculated leaf samples were collected for photography and measurement of lesion length (Wang et al. 2025). 

# 4.12 | RNA Extraction, qRT-PCR and Transcriptome Analysis

RNA was extracted from roots or shoots of 17-d-old plants grown with or without $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Pi conditions. The qRT-PCR and RNA sequencing and analysis were described previously (Yan et  al.  2024). The sequence data can be accessed in the NCBI (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) with accession number PRJNA1225990.
