# Abstract

Some herbicide-resistant weeds become resistant by generating additional copies of specific loci. For example, amplification of the locus encoding chloroplastic glutamine synthetase (GS2) produces herbicide resistance in the glufosinate-resistant Palmer amaranth (Amaranthus palmeri) accession MSR2. Previously, overamplification of the glyphosate-resistant gene encoding 5-enolpyruvylshikimate-3-phosphate synthase (EPSPS) in Palmer amaranth was determined to be driven by an extrachromosomal circular DNA (eccDNA). Here, we describe a rearranged eccDNA that confers resistance to both glyphosate and glufosinate ammonium due to the coduplication of the native chromosomal regions that contain the genes that encode for these herbicides target proteins. In addition to EPSPS, the replicon carries 2 GS2 isoforms (GS2.1 and GS2.2) and other genes. MSR2 samples harbored eccDNA carrying only EPSPS coexisting with eccDNAs harboring both EPSPS and GS2. A second glufosinate-resistant Palmer amaranth accession (MSR1) showed distinct GS2.1 and GS2.2 amplification patterns from MSR2, suggesting the existence of diverse replicons in Palmer amaranth. EPSPS copy number was correlated with both GS2 isoforms copy number in MSR2, further supporting the coexistence of these genes in the same replicon. These findings shed light on the complexity of eccDNA formation in plant systems, with the collection and accumulation of extra pieces of DNA. 

# Introduction

Weeds have evolved several mechanisms to survive herbicides; single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in the herbicide targetsite gene and increased capacity to metabolize the herbicide are among the most common (Powles and Yu 2010). However, several other mechanisms appear less frequently, such as genomic rearrangements that result in the overexpression of herbicide target proteins, commonly referred to as copy number variation (CNV) (Gaines et al. 2010). To date, this mechanism has been primarily found in glyphosate-resistant populations of both monocot and eudicot weeds. 

The enzyme 5-enolpyruvylshikimate-3-phosphate synthase (EPSPS) is the target site of the herbicide glyphosate (Steinrücken and Amrhein 1980). Increases in the number of EPSPS gene copies result in increased EPSPS-mRNA and proportionally increased EPSPS-protein. It takes a greater amount of applied glyphosate to inhibit the increased EPSPS-protein pool; therefore, with only a few additional gene copies, weedy plants can resist typical field doses of glyphosate. Several species have evolved EPSPS-CNVs, each through unique structural rearrangements. Kochia (Bassia scoparia) has the EPSPS tandemly amplified at a single locus 

(Jugulam et al. 2014; Patterson et al. 2019); goosegrass (Eleusine indica) has EPSPS tandemly amplified in the subtelomere region of one of its chromosomes (Zhang et al. 2023); waterhemp (Amaranthus tuberculatus) has EPSPS tandemly amplified close to its native locus, near the centromere, but also extra copies on an extra chromosome (Dillon et al. 2017). However, the first CNV identified in herbicide-resistant weeds was reported in Palmer amaranth (Amaranthus palmeri; Gaines et al. 2010), and the most recent was found Italian ryegrass (Lolium perenne ssp. multiflorum; Koo et al. 2023), showing EPSPS being duplicated through a genome-independent, autonomously replicating, circular piece of DNA called extrachromosomal circular DNA (eccDNA) (Koo et al. 2018b). Thus far, the nature and origin of these unique structural rearrangements are not yet fully understood, with some hypotheses suggesting transposable element activity and unequal crossing over (Jugulam et al. 2014; Hall et al. 2025); however, now that researchers are investigating these structures more deeply, they appear to be quite common (Fu et al. 2023). 

The first report of eccDNAs dates back to 1965 (Hotta and Bassel 1965). The functions and phenotypic consequences of eccDNAs are still being unveiled but are much better understood in humans 

than in plants, where several functions have been characterized (Zuo et al. 2022). For instance, eccDNAs can cause drug resistance in cancer cells by carrying oncogenes and/or chemotherapyresistant genes (Turner et al. 2017). In yeast, eccDNAs have been shown to carry the copper resistance gene CUP1 under elevated environmental copper conditions (Hull et al. 2019). Interestingly, there are several near-direct parallels in plants with the Palmer amaranth having an eccDNA that confers herbicide resistance due to EPSPS amplification (Koo et al. 2018a) and an eccDNA in blackgrass (Alopecurus myosuroides), which contains the herbicide metabolism gene GSTF1 (Fu et al. 2023). EccDNA formation is still not fully understood. It has been hypothesized that stress might generate fragments of DNA, which are consequently recognized and repaired by DNA damage-repair mechanisms to form eccDNAs (Zuo et al. 2022), that they may form from the circularization of extrachromosomal linear DNA of active transposable elements (Møller et al. 2016; Lanciano et al. 2017), or even that they arise as a result of random DNA rearrangement processes (Zhuang et al. 2024). Considering the first hypothesis, eccDNAs may rapidly emerge as a defense or resistance mechanism under physiological stress in eukaryotes, which is an attractive explanation for the rapid rate at which cancer drug and herbicide resistance accumulate (Molin et al. 2020b; Luo et al. 2023; Zhuang et al. 2024). 

Palmer amaranth has become one of the most problematic weeds in row crop fields worldwide, causing yield losses of up to $9 1 \%$ in row crop production systems (Massinga et al. 2001). One of the reasons for its meteoric rise in importance is its ability to evolve resistance to herbicides, which reduces the already limited postemergence options. To date, it has evolved resistance to almost all critical herbicides, such as glyphosate, 2,4-D, 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate dioxygenase inhibitors, protoporphyrinogen oxidase inhibitors, and acetolactate synthase (ALS) inhibitors, usually through independent mechanisms (Gaines et al. 2010; Küpper et al. 2017; Varanasi et al. 2018; Hwang et al. 2023). Rampant glyphosate resistance has led growers to lean more heavily on alternative chemistries that target multiple herbicide sites of action (Norsworthy et al. 2012). With the commercialization of glufosinate-resistant cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) and soybean (Glycine max), glufosinate ammonium became a cornerstone in chemical control programs targeting Palmer amaranth populations carrying resistance to other herbicides (Norsworthy et al. 2016). This nonselective herbicide inhibits the enzyme glutamine synthetase (GS), which has 2 isoforms in plants: GS1 in the cytosol and GS2 in the chloroplast (Bayer et al. 1972; McNally et al. 1983). Glutamine synthetase catalyzes the condensation of glutamate and ammonia to form glutamine (Hoerlein 1994). Inhibition of the GS enzyme leads to ammonia buildup and reactive oxygen species overproduction, which subsequently causes lipid peroxidation and plant death (Takano et al. 2020). As with other herbicides, recurrent glufosinate ammonium application has likely led to resistance evolution in Palmer amaranth (Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022; Noguera et al. 2022; Priess et al. 2022). The mechanism of glufosinate ammonium resistance in Palmer amaranth is still being investigated, and both target-site and nontarget-site resistance mechanisms have been described in resistant weeds (Brunharo et al. 2019; Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022; Noguera et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2022). Target-site mechanisms conferring glufosinate ammonium resistance have been reported for the Ser59Gly substitution in GS1 in goosegrass (Zhang et al. 2022) and GS2 amplification in Palmer amaranth (Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022; Noguera et al. 2022). 

The genomic structural rearrangement driving the GS2 amplification is unknown in glufosinate ammonium-resistant accessions. One hypothesis is that the gene amplification of GS2 

is in an EPSPS eccDNA molecule, as reported for glyphosate resistance (Koo et al. 2018b). Therefore, the objective of the current work was to characterize the genomic rearrangement involved in GS2 amplification in a Palmer amaranth accession resistant to both glufosinate ammonium and glyphosate. Moreover, this study investigated the relationship between GS2 CNV and eccDNA-driven amplification of the glyphosate target gene EPSPS. 

# Results

# Identification of an eccDNA rearrangement in Palmer amaranth

In this study, a Palmer amaranth eccDNA is reported that confers resistance to both glyphosate and glufosinate ammonium by the coduplication of the 2 loci that contain the genes that encode the target proteins in the same replicon. The glufosinate ammoniumresistant accession MSR2 was previously characterized as having an increased gene copy number and transcription of the glufosinate target gene GS2 (Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022). Additionally, it has been confirmed that the same accession is also resistant to glyphosate (Supplementary Fig. S1). To uncover the specific structural variant responsible for GS2 amplification, a rearranged eccDNA was assembled from shotgun sequencing and PacBio HiFi reads. It is 426,133 bp long, which is 26,698 bp longer than the previously identified eccDNA in Palmer amaranth (Molin et al. 2020a). The 2 eccDNAs show high levels of similarity and synteny $5 9 2 \%$ similarity) (Supplementary Fig. S2); however, a region in the original eccDNA has been swapped with the locus that contains the tandemly duplicated GS2.1 and GS2.2 genes in the genome (Fig. 1C). The fragment spanning 84,958 to 106,910 bp (21,952 bp total) of the previously known Palmer amaranth eccDNA was removed in the rearranged assembly, with a 53,159-bp-long sequence inserted in its place (Fig. 1A). Most of this inserted sequence shares synteny $( 7 8 . 6 \%$ similarity) with Chromosome 15 of the Palmer amaranth genome; however, a 5,476-bp-long sequence at the beginning of the insertion is unknown and does not match any sequence from the Palmer amaranth assembly. This sequence replacement event in the rearranged eccDNA did not affect the EPSPS gene locus, which also appears to be coduplicated in the same replicon ∼24,600 bp downstream of the Chromosome 15 insertion (Fig. 1B). EPSPS from the eccDNA shows synteny with its native location on Chromosome 7 and was inserted along with 5,572 bp upstream and 1,124 bp downstream of its native region (Fig. 1A). In addition, reads from the same individual in which the rearranged eccDNA was discovered also support the presence of the original replicon (with only EPSPS), indicating that both the original and rearranged eccDNAs coexist and independently replicate (Fig. 1, B and E). 

The detailed visualization of the rearrangements shows that coding genes were deleted and inserted in the rearranged eccDNA. The 21,952-bp fragment that was deleted contained 3 genes encoding a gene of unknown function, a zinc finger (ZNF) gene, and a ZNF transposase gene, according to the eccDNA sequence previously published (Fig. 1C). The sequence replacing this locus has 4 complete protein-coding genes: chloroplastic ATP sulfurylase (APS), 2 tandemly arranged glutamine synthetase 2 (GS2) genes, and a VAN3-binding protein (VAB), as well as a truncated, partial gene of unknown function (containing 2 out of 8 exons) (Fig. 1C). # Validation of the glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon assembly

Four primer sets were designed and tested to validate the glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon assembly (Fig. 3A). The PCR assays were performed by comparing 2 susceptible, 2 glyphosate-resistant (Gly-R), and 4 glufosinate-resistant (MSR2) individuals, who were different from those that were sequenced using PacBio HiFi. Importantly, the MSR2 individuals used in this study were also resistant to glyphosate. The PCR Markers 1 (577 bp) and 2 (948 bp) were specific to glufosinate resistance, targeting the 

junctions of the rearrangement in eccDNA, whereas Markers 3 (635 bp) and 4 (743 bp) were specific to glyphosate resistance, targeting the junctions of EPSPS insertion in the eccDNA and the original region that was deleted in the rearranged eccDNA, respectively (Fig. 3, A and B). The PCR-based assays showed amplification of Markers 1 and 2 in MSR2 individuals, which corroborates the assembly of the glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon as well as its occurrence in glufosinate-resistant individuals. Markers 3 and 4 (specific to glyphosate resistance) were present in both Gly-R and MSR2 populations (Fig. 3B-3 and B-4); however, the MSR2-plant 1 individual did not show this amplicon (Fig. 3B, Marker 4). Susceptible individuals did not show amplification of either glufosinate-resistant- or glyphosate-resistant-specific markers.

# Origins of the eccDNA in Palmer amaranth genome

A synteny analysis comparing the rearranged eccDNA with the entire Palmer amaranth genome was performed to identify the origins of the whole sequence in the rearranged eccDNA. When analyzing small synteny blocks (> 2000 bp), it is possible to identify many eccDNA fragments that are syntenic to several loci in the genome, most of which contain predicted transposable elements (Fig. 4). The origin of the TE sequences in the eccDNA for these regions is opaque, as they map to multiple loci in the genome; however, it does highlight the interaction of genomic TEs and the replicon. Surprisingly, a considerable portion of the eccDNA had similarity ${ \tt > } 1 , 0 0 0$ bp with no sequence in the Palmer amaranth genome (Supplementary Fig. S3). Next, there was an effort to determine whether transposon activity is involved in the GS2 insertion event. Transposable elements in the rearranged eccDNA assembly originated from Chr07 and 15, and associated flanking regions were investigated. The segment deleted from the original eccDNA did not have any transposable element within or flanking the inserted sequence (Supplementary Fig. S4). In the insertion containing GS2 genes in the rearranged eccDNA, there were 3 transposons: 2 hAT (MITEs) and 1 CACTA, both Class II (cut-and-paste) type TEs, located at the beginning of each side of the insertion (Supplementary Fig. S4). No transposons were annotated in the 20,000-bp region flanking the new insertion. There were 2 transposable elements annotated in the insertion with EPSPS in both original and rearranged eccDNA sequences, a Mutator and a Tc1/mariner, also Class II transposons (Supplementary Fig. S5). A hAT (MITE) was identified in the eccDNA sequence flanking the EPSPS insertion (Supplementary Fig. S5). When analyzing the GS2 insertions native chromosomal region in Chr15, only 1 hAT 

(MITE) transposon was identified downstream of the segment transferred to the eccDNA (Supplementary Fig. S6). The EPSPS native region in Chr07 has a Tc1/mariner in the region inserted into the eccDNA, and a Mutator flanking the region (Supplementary Fig. S6). When comparing the Tc1/Mariner and Mutator transposons associated with the EPSPS insertion in eccDNA and the Chr07 native region, they are located in different positions. In the native location in Chr07, the Mutator is outside of the sequence rearranged, while it is in the inserted sequence in the rearranged eccDNA; the Tc1/mariner is upstream of the EPSPS in the Chr07 native region, while it is downstream of EPSPS in the sequence inserted in the eccDNA (Supplementary Fig. S6).

# Discussion

Gene amplification and over expression are evolutionary mechanisms associated with adaptive traits, such as resistance to antibiotics, pathogens, and pesticides (Gaines et al. 2010; Almal and Padh 2012; Picart-Picolo et al. 2020). Resistance to herbicides due to gene amplification was first reported in glyphosate-resistant Palmer amaranth (Gaines et al. 2010). Following this initial report, this mechanism has been observed in both monocotyledonous and eudicotyledonous species resistant to herbicides pertaining to different sites of action, such as glyphosate, glufosinate, and cyhalofop (Chen et al. 2015; Laforest et al. 2017; Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022; Koo et al. 2023). In glyphosate-resistant Palmer amaranth, eccDNAs carrying extra copies of the EPSPS gene have been detected and are directly associated with spreading herbicide resistance (Koo et al. 2018b; Molin et al. 2020a). 

A glufosinate-resistant accession (MSR2) was previously characterized with a higher GS2 copy number, without any GS1 copy number changes, and no GS1 and GS2 mutations were detected (Carvalho-Moore et al. 2022). Since this accession also showed glyphosate resistance (Supplementary Fig. S1), the initial hypothesis of this study was that gene amplification of GS2 was also located in an eccDNA molecule, as reported for glyphosate resistance (Koo et al. 2018b). This hypothesis was confirmed by detecting a rearranged eccDNA of 426,133 bp, including the 2 GS2 isoforms and EPSPS genes cooccurring in the same eccDNA from the MSR2 accession. The assembly was validated by PCR amplification of the junctions and by detecting increased copy numbers of genes coinserted with GS2 isoforms. However, the same plant also carried the previously described Palmer amaranth eccDNA (EPSPS eccDNA) responsible for carrying only extra copies of the glyphosate target EPSPS (Koo et al. 2018b; Molin et al. 2020a). The rearranged eccDNA is characterized by the excision of 21,952 bp and replacement by a 53,159-bp fragment from Chromosome 15 compared with the first reported eccDNA. Except for this rearrangement, the 2 eccDNA versions are similar. Previous resequencing of divergent glyphosate-resistant Palmer amaranth populations showed that the shared eccDNAs are similar, without any structural variation (Molin et al. 2018). The fragment deleted from the eccDNA included regions encoding an unknown protein (UNK), a ZNF, and a ZNF transposase, which can induce targeted double-strand DNA breaks and movement of transposable elements (Kim et al. 1996; Rice and Baker 2001). The mechanism of detaching and substituting the DNA fragment by the Chromosome 15 region is unknown. Individuals carrying EPSPS $+ G S 2$ eccDNAs were likely randomly selected through frequent use of glufosinate. It is important to note that the native GS2 sequence was still present in its native location in Chromosome 15 of the MSR2 containing EPSPS + GS2 eccDNA, which would indicate DNA transposition. However, the presence of introns in the genes inserted into the rearranged eccDNA rules out retrotransposition as the mechanism driving the rearrangement. The genes APS, VAB, and a partial gene encoding an unknown protein were also coduplicated with the 2 GS2 isoforms in the rearranged eccDNA. The effect of additional gene copies encoding APS and VAB still must be 

investigated. The rearranged eccDNA containing $E P S P S + G S 2$ was completely assembled, and besides the GS2 isoforms and EPSPS, no other genes encoding herbicide-targeted enzymes were inserted into the new replicon. Therefore, further investigations will be needed to determine why and how the GS2 insertion occurred. 

Even with the cooccurrence of 2 eccDNA versions, MSR2 showed different proportions of both versions; usually, the one with only EPSPS is more abundant. This result may indicate a high variation in how eccDNAs are transmitted across glufosinate-resistant individuals, which was, in some way, expected. According to Koo et al. (2018b), there are somatic variations among cells in glyphosate-resistant Palmer amaranth carrying the EPSPS eccDNA. In our studies, different MSR2 individuals showed a matching and mismatching copy number of targeted genes present in the EPSPS and $E P S P S + G S 2$ replicons, corroborating this finding. Koo et al. (2018a, 2018b) also found that the EPSPS eccDNA is not integrated into chromosomes and follows non-Mendelian segregation in gametes during meiosis, ultimately leading to cells with multiple or no EPSPS eccDNA. Individuals with higher gene copy numbers will have an undeniable survival advantage under herbicide selection pressure. This variability has been confirmed in previous works in which EPSPS copy number segregation in progenies carrying EPSPS eccDNA was random and unpredictable (Chandi et al. 2012; Mohseni-Moghadam et al. 2013; Giacomini et al. 2019). 

Neither the rearranged EPSPS + GS2 nor the original EPSPS eccDNA was detected in the susceptible plants used as controls. However, the existence of different and smaller eccDNA sequences natively occurring has been detected in susceptible plants (Wang et al. 2021; Fu et al. 2023; Zhuang et al. 2024). In Palmer amaranth, eccDNAs ranging from 27 bp to 27,000 bp were identified in glyphosatesusceptible and resistant populations (Spier Camposano et al. 2022). Additionally, the eccDNA abundance might change under biotic or abiotic stress (Turner et al. 2017; Wang et al. 2021; Zhuang et al. 2024). For instance, a significantly higher amount of eccDNAs were quantified in rice leaves under darkness or UV irradiation treatments compared with control plants maintained under normal light conditions (Zhuang et al. 2024). Most of the eccDNAs have the characteristic of rapid degradation. However, the ones that have the ability to amplify themselves and provide an adaptive advantage can survive (Zuo et al. 2022). 

The presence of different GS isoforms was previously detected in Palmer amaranth accessions from Missouri and Italian ryegrass from Oregon (Brunharo et al. 2019; Noguera et al. 2022). In both species, the GS1 isoforms were not amplified or overexpressed. In our studies, MSR1 showed amplification of GS2.1 but not GS2.2, similar to what was found in a Palmer amaranth population from Missouri reported by Noguera et al. (2022). Therefore, it is likely that the MSR1 population from Arkansas and the population from Missouri studied by Noguera et al. (2022) share the same mechanism of resistance. This resistance trait may have spread across the states through seed or pollen movement. In contrast, MSR2 individuals showed amplification of both GS2.1 and GS2.2, which is different than MSR1. Moreover, MSR2 individuals showed a much higher abundance of GS2.2 compared with GS2.1. Koo et al. (2018a, 2018b) found that eccDNAs differ from each other by polymorphisms or even larger insertions or deletions. Since the pattern of GS2.1 and GS2.2 amplification differed between MSR1 and MSR2, it is likely that there are different eccDNA replicons with the arrangement carrying EPSPS + GS2 among different Palmer amaranth populations, or even different types of genomic rearrangements. Circular DNAs of different sizes were identified in herbicidesusceptible and resistant plant populations, which may serve as a source of genetic diversity during evolution with the possibility 

of different replicon structures (Spier Camposano et al. 2022; Fu et al. 2023). 

The identification of EPSPS amplification as a resistance mechanism, followed by the discovery of eccDNAs carrying it (Gaines et al. 2010; Koo et al. 2018b; Molin et al. 2020b), changed weed scientists’ perception of herbicide resistance evolution. Due to the eccDNA’s autonomous replication, cross-pollination characteristic of Palmer amaranth, and small seeds favorable to dissemination, glyphosate resistance due to EPSPS amplification is widely spread in Palmer amaranth accessions across the world. The findings of this study showed that GS2 was also inserted in eccDNAs, and a quick spread of resistance is expected due to the Palmer amaranth characteristics, as verified for glyphosate. The eccDNA possesses the ability to aggregate and amplify multiple genes encoding herbicide targets, as shown in this study, but also genes associated with herbicide resistance mechanisms, like GSTF1 in blackgrass (Fu et al. 2023). Consequently, the progeny carrying the eccDNAs accumulating resistance to distinct herbicide sites of action will undoubtedly have an advantage under different selection pressure scenarios. Our findings show the ability of Palmer amaranth to collect and accumulate extra pieces of DNA in existing replicons, displaying the complexity of eccDNAs and their importance to weed evolution dynamics. Rapid adaptation to different herbicides is also a threat to individuals carrying eccDNAs due to the inherent possibility of this event occurring again, incorporating other herbicide targets. Glyphosate and glufosinate are major allies in the current practices used in food production, and a single event driving the resistance to both herbicides has massive impacts on agriculture. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant material

A glufosinate-resistant Palmer amaranth accession from Arkansas (MSR2) confirmed to show high resistance levels (R/S ratio: 24-fold; Priess et al. 2022) was selected for this study. The resistant accession was sprayed with a $4 \times$ glufosinate dose ( $\ L _ { 1 \times }$ labeled rate $= 6 5 6 \mathrm { g }$ ai $\mathrm { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ , and survivors were cross-pollinated. Importantly, this accession was also confirmed resistant to glyphosate (Supplementary Fig. S1). Additionally, 1 susceptible accession collected in 1986 was used for comparison. The MSR2 and susceptible seeds were sown into 7-cm-diameter plastic pots filled with potting mix (Sun Gro Horticulture, Agawam, MA, USA). Seedlings were grown in a greenhouse at 25 to $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , with a 16-h/8-h day/night period at the Milo J. Shult Agricultural Research and Extension Center in Fayetteville, AR, USA. Leaf tissues from 3 MSR2 $( n = 3 )$ and 3 susceptible $( n = 3 )$ untreated seedlings were used for genomic high-molecular-weight DNA isolation using the Quick DNA HMW MagBead Kit (Zymo Research, Irvine, CA, USA). DNA integrity, concentration, and quality were verified, and the average fragment size was above 40 kb. 

# Sequencing and assembly of Palmer amaranth accessions

High-molecular-weight DNA from 3 MSR2 and 3 susceptible individuals was submitted to whole-genome sequencing by the Pacific Biosciences (PacBio) Revio system with high-fidelity (HiFi) library preparation. The output averages of bases per sample and read length were 12.16 gbp and 15.29 kbp for MSR2 and 13.48 gbp and 16.61 kbp for susceptible, ${ \sim } 3 1 \times$ and $3 4 . 5 \times$ coverage, respectively. The eccDNA was assembled utilizing PacBio HiFi long reads and Flye v. 2.9.2 assembler (Kolmogorov et al. 2019). The contigs obtained from Flye were connected manually by searching for the best reads to lengthen the eccDNA sequence until the contigs 

were connected. This manual curation used BLAST + v.2.5.0 (Altschul et al. 1990), SAMtools v1.18 (Li et al. 2009), Minimap2 v2.17 (Li 2018), RagTag v2.1.0 (Alonge et al. 2022), and Mummer3 v. 3.23 (Kurtz et al. 2004) software to identify overlapping regions of similarity between them. We also utilized the published version of the eccDNA (NCBI accession: MT025716; Molin et al. 2020a) to validate our assembly. After completion, the complete assemblies with long reads supporting were visualized in the Integrative Genomics Viewer v2.11.0 (Robinson et al. 2011). Due to the random segregation nature of eccDNA (Koo et al. 2018b), we noticed that 1 out of 3 MSR2 individuals sequenced only had the rearranged eccDNA carrying chloroplastic glutamine synthetase (GS2) isoforms + EPSPS (utilized to assemble the rearranged eccDNA in our study), 1 had only the original eccDNA carrying only EPSPS, and a third did not have either eccDNA, probably susceptible. We cannot discard the hypothesis that the MSR2 individual characterized with only the original EPSPS eccDNA has a different eccDNA not assembled in our study carrying 1 or more GS2 isoforms. 

# Synteny analyses

Syntenic analyses were generated utilizing Minimap2 v2.17 (Li 2018), RagTag v2.1.0 (Alonge et al. 2022), and Mummer3 v. 3.23 (Kurtz et al. 2004). RIdeogram package (Hao et al. 2020) in R was utilized to generate the images. 

# PCR-based validation of glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon in Palmer amaranth

PCR-based markers were developed to validate the assembly of the glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon. Primers were designed to amplify fragments that include the junctions of the region inserted in the glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon (Markers 1 and 2, Fig. 3A) and deleted from the glyphosate-resistant eccDNA replicon (Markers 3 and 4, Fig. 3A). DNA from 2 susceptible and 4 MSR2 individuals was used in this assay. Also, DNA from 2 glyphosate-resistant (Gly-R) individuals, previously characterized (Sulzback et al. 2024), was included. A single run included 1 individual reaction with DNA from each biological replicate. Approximately $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of young leaf tissue was ground using a plastic pestle in a conical 1.5-ml microcentrifuge tube to obtain a fine powder. High-molecular-weight DNA was extracted utilizing the Wizard HMW DNA Extraction Kit (Promega Corporation, Madison, WI, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocol. The DNA obtained was diluted to a final concentration of $5 0 \mathrm { n g } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ for further use. The reactions were performed with $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of DNA $( 5 0 \mathrm { n g } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of forward and 1 µL of reverse primers (10 mM), $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of MgCl₂ (25 mM), $7 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of nuclease-free water, and 12.5 µL of GoTaq G2 Green Master Mix (Promega), with a total of $2 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ per reaction. The thermal cycler settings were as follows: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min, then $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s, followed by the annealing temperature for 30 s, $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ during the extension time, repeated for 40 cycles, and a final extension at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min. Primer sequences, annealing temperature, extension time, and the length of the amplicons are described in Supplementary Table S3. The presence of amplification was observed in an agarose gel $( 1 . 5 \% )$ (Fig. 3B). 

# qPCR-based validation of glufosinate-resistant eccDNA replicon in Palmer amaranth

A CNV assay was also developed to support the glufosinateresistant eccDNA replicon assembly. In this assay, the number of chloroplastic ATP sulfurylase (APS), VAB, and the unknown (UNK) partial sequence genes (Fig. 3A) are predicted to correlate with GS2 in glufosinate-resistant individuals, since these genes 

are cooccurring in the rearranged eccDNA replicon assembled; primers targeting GS2 were binding both isoforms GS2.1 and GS2.2. Similarly, the number of EPSPS, unknown protein (UNK), ZNF, and ZNF transposase genes is predicted to correlate with DNA from glyphosate- and glufosinate $^ +$ glyphosate-resistant individuals. The DNAs from9 susceptible, 9 MSR2, and 7 Gly-R individuals were included in this assay. Primers (Supplementary Table S4) were designed to amplify the different targets in the eccDNA assembled in this study. The reactions were performed with $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of DNA $( 1 5 \mathrm { n g } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ , $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of forward and $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of reverse primers (10 mM), $6 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of nuclease-free water, and $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of SsoAdvanced Universal SYBR Green Supermix (Bio-Rad), with a total of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ per reaction. The real-time qPCR cycles started with 3 min at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and were followed by 39 cycles at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s and $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s, with standard melting curves included in a CFX 96 Real-time system thermocycler (Bio-Rad). The assay was performed with 3 technical replicates per biological sample tested. The copy number of targeted genes was estimated relative to ALS in each biological replicate utilizing the method 2-ΔCt. The average gene copy number of each targeted gene was compared across biological replicates by Tukey’s HSD $\left( \mathfrak { a } = 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . 

# Copy number of GS isoforms and EPSPS in different glufosinate-resistant Palmer amaranth accession

Besides MSR2, a different glufosinate-resistant accession (MSR1), also showing glufosinate ammonium/glyphosate resistance and GS2 amplification, was identified in Arkansas (Priess et al. 2022). Different assays were conducted to verify whether the rearranged replicon arrangement found in MSR2 was present in MSR1 plants. Additionally, the gene CNV of each GS isoform and EPSPS was quantified for MSR1 and MSR2. Susceptible plants were also included for comparison. For the preparation of the DNA, leaf samples $( 0 . 5 \thinspace { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { 2 } )$ of 24 individual plants from each Palmer amaranth accession were transferred into a sample tube (collection microtubes; Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). The samples were then homogenized in a shaker mill (TissueLyser II; Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) with steel beads. DNA extraction was performed in the KingFisher Flex Magnetic Particle Processors (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Schwerte, Germany) using the Chemagic Plant 400 kit (Perkin Elmer, Rodgau, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

Nanowell-based dPCR was performed to determine the CNV of GS1.1 and GS1.2 isoforms and EPSPS. The dPCR was conducted in a final volume of $1 2 \mu ]$ using $1 . 4 8 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of DNA, $0 . 4 8 \mu \mathrm { l }$ $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ of specific primers (Supplementary Table S5) and $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ of probe (biomers.net GmbH, Ulm, Germany), $3 \mu ]$ of QIAcuity Probe PCR Kit mix, and $3 . 9 2 \mu \mathrm { l }$ PCR-Grade $_ \mathrm { H _ { 2 } O }$ for the triplex dPCR. The assay was performed in a dPCR thermal cycler (QIAcuity One, 5plex Device, Qiagen, Hilden Germany) in a 96-well nanoplate with 8.500 nanowells for each sample (QIAcuity Nanoplate 8.5k 96-well) under the following conditions: 2 min at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 55 cycles of 15 s denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; 40 s annealing, elongation, and detection at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Partitions were imaged with the following conditions. FAM and HEX, 500 ms exposure time, gain set to 6; ROX 400 ms exposure time, gain set to 6. Qiagen’s QIAcuity Software Suite (version 2.1.8) was used to determine sample thresholds using positive, negative, and no-template control wells, and the CNV. 

TaqMan technology was used to determine the gene copy number and expression of the GS2.1 and GS2.2 isoforms. The TaqMan assays were designed to allow a multiplex approach for the target and reference genes (Supplementary Table S5). The real-time quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was performed in a final volume of $2 5 \mu 1$ using $5 \mu ]$ of DNA, $1 \mu \mathrm { l }$ $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ of specific primers and 

$0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ of probe (biomers.net GmbH, Ulm, Germany), $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of SNP PolTaq DNA polymerase and $2 . 5 \mu 1$ ${ 1 0 \times }$ amplification buffer (Genaxxon bioscience GmbH, Ulm, Germany), $0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ (10 mM) dNTP mix, and $1 1 . 7 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ PCR-grade $_ \mathrm { H } \sb { 2 } \boldsymbol { \mathrm { O } }$ for the triplex RT-qPCR (GS2.1, GS2.2 and Actin). Three technical replicates were used for each sample. RT-qPCR was performed in a qPCR thermal cycler (CFX96 Touch Real-Time PCR Detection System, Bio-Rad Laboratories GmbH, Germany) under the following conditions: 5 min at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 35 cycles of 10 s denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; 30 s annealing, elongation, and detection at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The evaluation, according to the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \mathrm { T } } }$ method, was carried out with the software Bio-Rad CFX Maestro 2.2 version 5.2.008.0222. 

# Genes and transposable elements annotation

The eccDNA sequences $> 2 , 0 0 0$ bp with synteny to the Palmer amaranth genome had the coding genes annotated based on the annotation file of the Palmer amaranth genome from the International Weed Genomics Consortium (Montgomery et al. 2024; Raiyemo et al. 2025). A synteny was performed between the eccDNA and the genome, and the coordinates were compared to the Palmer amaranth annotation file, checking the genes in the coordinates interval. The transposable elements were annotated utilizing Extensive de novo TE Annotator (EDTA) pipeline (Ou et al. 2019). 

# Statistical analysis

Data visualization and statistical analyses were performed with JMP Pro 18 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA), including ANOVA followed by Tukey’s HSD test, or RStudio using the packages “GGally,” “ggplot2,” and “ggpubr.” Significant differences were determined at $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5$ .
