# Abstract

Sugarcane smut caused by Sporisorium scitamineum seriously impairs sugarcane production and quality. Sexual mating/filamentation is a critical step of S. scitamineum pathogenesis, yet the regulatory mechanisms are not fully understood. In this study, we identified the SsAGA, SsODC, and SsSAMDC genes, which are involved in polyamine biosynthesis in S. scitamineum. Deletion of SsODC led to complete loss of filamentous growth after sexual mating, and deletion of SsAGA or SsSAMDC caused reduced filamentation. Double deletion of SsODC and SsSAMDC resulted in auxotrophy for putrescine (PUT) and spermidine (SPD) when grown on minimal medium (MM), indicating that these two genes encode enzymes that are critical for PUT and SPD biosynthesis. We further showed that low PUT concentrations promoted S. scitamineum filamentation, while high PUT concentrations suppressed filamentation. Disrupted fungal polyamine biosynthesis also resulted in a loss of pathogenicity and reduced fungal biomass within infected plants at the early infection stage. SPD formed a gradient from the diseased part to nonsymptom parts of the cane stem, suggesting that SPD is probably favourable for fungal virulence. Mutants of the cAMP–PKA (SsGPA3–SsUAC1– SsADR1) signalling pathway displayed up-regulation of the SsODC gene and elevated intracellular levels of PUT. SsODC directly interacted with SsGPA3, and sporidia of the ss1uac1ΔodcΔ mutant displayed abundant pseudohyphae. Furthermore, we found that elevated PUT levels caused accumulation of intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS), probably by suppressing transcription of ROS-scavenging enzymes, while SPD played the opposite role. Overall, our work proves that polyamines play important roles in the pathogenic development of sugarcane smut fungus, probably by collaboratively regulating intracellular redox homeostasis with the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Sugarcane smut is one of the main fungal diseases that seriously reduce sugarcane production. The causal pathogen, Sporisorium scitamineum, is a typical dimorphic fungus. Its life cycle has three stages, namely, the haploid sporidium stage, the dikaryotic hypha stage, and the diploid teliospore stage (Piepenbring et al., 2002). Haploid yeast-type sporidia are unable to infect the host and are of two opposite mating types, MAT-1 and MAT-2. Dikaryotic hyphae are formed after sexual mating of haploid sporidia of different mating types, and are capable of infecting host canes (Yan, Zhu, et al., 2016). After sexual mating, the b locus encoding a heterodimeric transcription factor complex consisting of bE and bW proteins from different alleles controls filamentous growth in S. scitamineum (Yan, Zhu, et al., 2016). The dikaryotic hyphae grow within the sugarcane stem and form teliospores wrapped by plant tissue and as a “whip” shape, the typical symptom of sugarcane smut disease. Therefore, understanding the regulatory mechanism underlying sexual mating/filamentation and teliospore formation of S. scitamineum is critical for the development of efficient disease management strategies. 

Two classical signalling pathways, the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and cAMP/protein kinase A (PKA) pathways, regulate the sexual mating process in smut fungi (Chang et al., 2019; Deng et al., 2018; Kaffarnik et al., 2003; Muller et al., 1999, 2003). In Ustilago maydis, the MAPK and cAMP–PKA pathways coordinate to regulate sexual mating and the yeast-to-hypha dimorphic transition (Lengeler et al., 2000). The core elements of the cAMP–PKA pathway include the G-protein GPA3, the adenylyl cyclase UAC1, and the cAMP-dependent PKA ADR1 (Lee et al., 2003; Leveleki et al., 2004). Pheromones could be perceived by pheromone receptors and/or GPCR, and the signal transduction depends on the GPA3–UAC1– ADR1 (cAMP–PKA) signalling pathway and the Kpp4–Fuz7–Kpp2 (MAPK) signalling cascade, with partial overlap, commonly inducing the a locus, which encodes the pheromone precursors Mfa1/2 and receptors Pra1/2, through the transcription factor Prf1 (Kaffarnik et al., 2003; Muller et al., 1999, 2003; Zhu et al., 2019), to trigger mating, filamentation, and virulence. 

Polyamines, which are biologically active aliphatic nitrogencontaining bases, are ubiquitously present in living organisms and have important biological functions as signalling molecules (Rocha & Wilson, 2019). According to the number of amino groups in the chemical structure, amines are divided into monoamines, including putrescine (PUT) and cadaverine (CAD); triamines, including spermidine (SPD); and tetramines, including spermine (SPM). Many fungal species do not produce SPM. PUT, SPD, and SPM 

are important for cell growth, and their biosynthesis pathways differ among plants, bacteria, animals, and fungi (Miller-Fleming et al., 2015; Valdes-Santiago, Cervantes-Chavez, Leon-Ramirez et al., 2012). So far, two pathways for PUT biosynthesis have been reported, in which l-arginine is either hydrolysed by arginase (AGA) to generate ornithine, which is then decarboxylated by ornithine decarboxylase (ODC) to produce PUT, or catalysed sequentially by arginine decarboxylase (ADC), agmatinase (AGT), and agmatine iminohydrolase (AIH) to produce PUT. Meanwhile, S-adenosyl methionine (SAM) is decarboxylated by SAM decarboxylase (SAMDC) to generate decarboxylated SAM (dcSAM), which is a critical substrate for the synthesis of SPD and SPM from the upstream metabolites, PUT and SPD, respectively; these reactions are catalysed by SPD synthase (SPDS) and SPM synthase (SPMS), respectively (Shah & Swiatlo, 2008; Valdes-Santiago, Cervantes-Chavez, Leon-Ramirez, et al., 2012; Valdes-Santiago & Ruiz-Herrera, 2013). At present, little is known about polyamine biosynthesis and their mechanism of action in fungi, including S. scitamineum. 

In the present study, we identified the polyamine biosynthesis genes SsAGA, SsODC, and SsSAMDC in S. scitamineum and characterized their functions by reverse genetics. Our study provides evidence that polyamine homeostasis is important for sexual mating/filamentation in S. scitamineum. Low concentrations of PUT promoted and high concentrations of PUT suppressed filamentous growth after sexual mating in S. scitamineum. SPD promoted filamentation at a wide range of concentrations. The polyamine synthesis pathway genetically and physically interacted with the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway to regulate sexual mating/filamentation and mediate the S. scitamineum–sugarcane interaction, probably by modulating intracellular redox homeostasis. Overall, our work reveals the roles of polyamines in regulating growth and pathogenic development of S. scitamineum and the underlying mechanism, thus providing a theoretical basis for designing prevention and control strategies of sugarcane smut disease.

As shown in Table 1, a putative arginase (CDU25136.1, named SsAGA), an ornithine decarboxylase (CDU24605.1, named SsODC), and a SAM decarboxylase (CDU24548.1, named SsSAMDC) were identified based on amino acid sequence similarity. We performed phylogenetic analysis using these proteins and their orthologues from smut fungi or ascomycetes (Table S1). The results showed that SsAGA, SsODC, and SsSAMDC were conserved in a diverse range of fungal species, closest to the orthologues in Sporisorium reilianum, forming a clade with the orthologues from smut fungi and separate from the clade formed by ascomycetous orthologues (Figure S1). 

By homologous recombination we generated deletion mutants of SsAGA, SsODC, and SsSAMDC in both MAT-1 and MAT-2 backgrounds, as verified by Southern blotting (Figure S2a–c). We named the single-deletion mutants ss1agaΔ and ss2agaΔ, ss1odcΔ and ss2odcΔ, and ss1samdcΔ and ss2samdcΔ, respectively. The genetic complementation strains for ss1odcΔ, ss2odcΔ, ss1samdcΔ, and ss2samdcΔ were generated, namely, Ss1ODC-COM, Ss2ODC-COM, Ss1SAMDC-COM, and Ss2SAMDC-COM, and verified by PCR (Figure S2b,c) or fluorescence microscopic observation of the fused SsODC-mCherry and SsSAMDC-mCherry proteins (Figure S3b). We also generated an ss2odcΔsamdcΔ double mutant (Figure S2d). For all the deletion mutants and complementation strains, at least two independent strains were obtained and used for the characterization of polyamine biosynthesis and the biological functions. The results are described below. 

We found that loss of SsSAMDC led to an obvious defect in sporidial growth on minimal medium (MM), which could be restored by supplementation with SPD (1 mM; Figure 1a). This indicates that SsSAMDC is critical for SPD biosynthesis, probably using PUT as a substrate. Sporidial growth of the ss2agaΔ and ss2odcΔ mutants was comparable to that of the wild-type (WT) MAT-2 strain (Figure 1a). Furthermore, we found that the double mutant ss2odcΔsamdcΔ was auxotrophic when grown on MM in low-inoculum cultures $( 1 0 ^ { 3 } – 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ cells), which could be restored by supplementation with PUT or SPD (both $\mathsf { 1 m M }$ ; Figure 1b). Interestingly, such auxotrophy of the ss2odcΔsamdcΔ double mutant was not obvious when sporidia were grown on yeast extract-peptone-sucrose medium (YePS) or potato dextrose agar (PDA) (Figure 1b). We infer that YePS or PDA contained PUT and/or SPD, which could be utilized by ss2odcΔsamdcΔ, thus masking its PUT or SPD auxotrophy. 

Given that the ss2odc samdc double mutant was auxotrophic for PUT and SPD, we assessed the roles of SsODC and SsSAMDC in S. scitamineum polyamine biosynthesis by liquid chromatography– mass spectrometry (LC-MS) analysis. As WT or mutant sporidia could not grow in liquid MM, we cultured the sporidia in liquid YePS medium, to reach $1 0 ^ { 7 }$ cells for polyamine extraction. Serially diluted PUT (0.004, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, $0 . 0 5 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ ) and SPD (0.004, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, $0 . 0 5 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ ) standards were detected as peaks with $m / z$ ratios of 297.16 and 458.24, respectively. A peak with a retention time (RT) of 2.60 min corresponds to PUT, and a peak with an RT of $4 . 5 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ corresponds to SPD (Figure 1c). Indeed, PUT and SPD could be 

detected in YePS medium (Figure 1c). Subsequently, we compared the intracellular levels of PUT and SPD in ss2odcΔsamdcΔ sporidia to those in MAT-2 sporidia. The result showed that the PUT content in ss2odcΔsamdcΔ was as low as $1 2 . 7 3 \%$ of that in MAT-2, and the SPD content in ss2odcΔsamdcΔ was about $1 0 . 6 7 \%$ of that in MAT-2 (Figure 1d). 

Overall, these results confirm that SsSAMDC is critical for SPD biosynthesis, and both SsODC and SsSAMDC are likely to contribute to PUT biosynthesis. 

# 2.2  |  Assessing the roles of polyamines in S. scitamineum mating/filamentation

To determine the role of polyamines in S. scitamineum mating/filamentation, dikaryotic hyphal growth after asexual mating was assessed in the mutants. The results showed that ss1odcΔ × ss2odcΔ had almost no dikaryotic hyphae at all, and filamentation of ss1agaΔ×ss2agaΔ, ss1samdcΔ×ss2samdcΔ, or MAT-1×ss2odcΔsamdcΔ was reduced compared to $M A T – 1 \times M A T – 2$ when grown on PDA (Figure 2a). When cultured on MM, neither WT MAT-$1 \times M A T - 2$ nor MAT-1×ss2odcΔsamdcΔ was able to mate and form dikaryotic hyphae at all (Figure S3a). 

To further verify the roles of polyamines in S. scitamineum sexual mating/filamentation, we first determined the dose-dependent effect of PUT, SPD, or SPM on WT MAT-1×MAT-2 grown on PDA. The results showed that PUT at $1 0 { - } 1 0 0 \mathsf { m M }$ could slightly promote filamentation, while high concentrations of PUT suppressed filamentation (Figure 2b). SPD at 10–1000 mM and SPM at $1 0 { - } 1 0 0 \mathsf { m M }$ could also promote filamentation (Figure 2b). Next, we supplied $1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ PUT, $1 0 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ SPD, or 100 mM SPM to the mating cultures of ss1agaΔ × ss2agaΔ, ss1odcΔ × ss2odcΔ, ss1samdcΔ $\times s s \ –$ - 2samdcΔ, and MAT-1×ss2odcΔsamdcΔ. The results showed that 1,000 mM SPD or 100 mM SPM could slightly restore filamentation of ss1agaΔ×ss2agaΔ, ss1samdcΔ×ss2samdcΔ, and $M A T – 1 \times s s 2 o d \cdot$ - cΔsamdcΔ. Similarly, exogenous supplementation of SPD $( 1 0 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M } )$ ) or SPM $( 1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M } )$ ) could slightly induce hyphal growth for MAT-1×ss2odcΔsamdcΔ grown on MM (Figure S3a). 

Sexual mating/filamentation could be restored in the complementation strains Ss1ODC-COM×Ss2ODC-COM and Ss1SAMDC-COM×Ss2SAMDC-COM to a comparable level as WT (Figure S3b), confirming that the SsODC and SsSAMDC genes are indeed required for S. scitamineum sexual mating/filamentation. Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis was performed in mutants versus WT to evaluate the relative expression levels of the genes known to be involved in sexual mating/filamentation, including SsGPA3, SsUAC1, SsKPP2, SsbE, SsbW, and SsPRF1. We noticed that the relative expression level of SsUAC1 was down-regulated in ss1odcΔ×ss2odcΔ, while SsbW and SsPRF1 were down-regulated in ss1samdcΔ×ss2samdcΔ (Figure 2c). 

Overall, our results showed that the SsAGA, SsODC, and Ss-SAMDC genes are all important for sexual mating/filamentation of S. scitamineum, probably by contributing to polyamine biosynthesis.

To explore the possible role of polyamine biosynthesis in the early stage of S. scitamineum infection, we measured the fungal biomass in the infected seedlings by qPCR at 3 and 7 days post-infection (dpi). The results showed that the biomass of mutants at 3 dpi was comparable to that of WT MAT-1×MAT-2, while at 7 dpi the biomass of ss1odcΔ×ss2odcΔ and ss1samdcΔ $\times$ ss2samdcΔ was much lower than that of WT (Figure 3c). 

To investigate the functional mechanism of fungus-derived polyamines during host infection, we quantitatively assessed the expression levels of polyamine metabolism genes at 0, 3, and 7 dpi. The results showed that SsSAMDC and SsSPDS/SPMS (SPSC_01548) were up-regulated 1.81-fold and 1.57-fold at 3 dpi and 1.79-fold and 1.91- fold at 7 dpi, respectively (Figure 3d). In contrast, SsAGA and SsPAO (SPSC_01582) were down-regulated to 0.50-fold and 0.46-fold at 3 dpi and to 0.14-fold and 0.28-fold at 7 dpi, respectively (Figure 3d). The transcriptional profiles suggest that at the early stage of infection, S. scitamineum tends to down-regulate genes for PUT biosynthesis but up-regulate genes for SPD biosynthesis. 

We also measured polyamine levels in diseased tissue—the “whip” form at the top of cane stems—and compared these to those in the middle and bottom parts of the stems of the infected sugarcane seedlings. Uninfected, healthy sugarcane seedlings served as a control. The results showed that PUT and SPD contents were significantly higher in the infected sugarcane stems than in healthy sugarcane stems (Figure 3e). In addition, SPD content displayed a gradient from the top (“whip”) to the bottom of infected sugarcane, while SPD was not present in healthy sugarcane (Figure 3e). The results indicated that SPD accumulates in the top stems of infected sugarcane, where the dikaryotic hyphae grow towards, and the whip symptoms develop as disease progresses. Therefore, we infer that S. scitamineum may induce SsSAMDC and SsSPDS/SPMS expression in the early stage of infection, probably to generate SPD to facilitate infection and promote virulence. 

Overall, we conclude that disruption of the polyamine biosynthesis pathway results in reduced or loss of pathogenicity/virulence of S. scitamineum. 

# 2.4  |  The polyamine biosynthesis pathway interacts with the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway

It has previously been reported that the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway, which is composed of SsGPA3, SsUAC1, and SsADR1, controls sexual mating/filamentation in S. scitamineum (Chang et al., 2019). To investigate the relationship (if any) between polyamines and the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway, we exogenously added polyamines to MAT-1 (WT), ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, ss1adr1Δ, and ss1prf1Δ and observed the effect on sexual mating/filamentation. SPM $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ slightly increased sexual mating/filamentation of WT, ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, and ss1adr1Δ. Meanwhile, SPD (1 mM) could effectively 

restore sexual mating/filamentation of ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, and ss1adr1Δ (Figure 4a). Dikaryotic hyphal growth was completely blocked in ss1prf1Δ (Figure 4a) and could not be restored by exogenous addition of polyamines. 

We analysed the relative expression levels of polyamine metabolism-related genes in MAT-1, ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, ss1adr1Δ, and ss1prf1Δ by RT-qPCR. SsODC was up-regulated 6.48-, 1.80-, and 8.44-fold in ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, and ss1adr1Δ, respectively (Figure 4b). Correspondingly, the PUT content was significantly higher in ss1gpa3Δ, ss1uac1Δ, ss1adr1Δ, and ss1prf1Δ than in MAT-1, while SPD or SPM levels were comparable to WT (Figure 4c). 

On the other hand, we noticed that cAMP at high concentrations inhibited sexual mating/filamentation of $M A T – 1 \times M A T – 2$ and could not restore the sexual mating/filamentation of polyamine mutants (Figure 4d). In addition, cAMP could effectively restore sexual mating/filamentation of ss1gpa3Δ and ss1uac1Δ but not ss1adr1Δ (Figure S3c), consistent with a previous report (Chang et al., 2019). Therefore, the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway may act upstream of polyamine biosynthesis. 

To evaluate the physical interaction between polyamine biosynthesis and the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway, we carried out yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) analysis. The results showed that SsODC directly interacted with SsGPA3 (Figure 5a), but not with SsUAC1 or SsADR1 (Figure 5b). In addition, SsODC formed a homodimer (Figure S3d), consistent with a previous report (Wu et al., 2015, 2022). Next, we generated ss1uac1ΔodcΔ double-deletion mutants by deleting SsODC in the ss1uac1Δ background (Figure S2e). Strikingly, ss1uac1ΔodcΔ sporidia showed a filamentous phenotype under control conditions (Figure 5c), and the sporidia showed a short, coarse, and irregular elliptic shape under the microscope, which was obviously different from that of MAT-1, ss1uac1Δ, or ss1odcΔ (Figure 5d). High-concentration cAMP $( 1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M } )$ inhibited the filamentous phenotype of ss1uac1ΔodcΔ without changing sporidial morphology (Figure 5c,d), which is similar to the previously reported U. maydis uac1 mutant, which has a constitutively filamentous phenotype that could be restored by exogenous cAMP (Gold et al., 1994). 

Overall, our results demonstrate a direct interaction between SsGPA3 of the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway and SsODC of the polyamine biosynthesis pathway, suggesting a cross-talk between the intracellular levels of polyamines and cAMP signalling to regulate S. scitamineum sexual mating/filamentation.

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

Polyamines are aliphatic nitrogen-containing bases that are widely present in organisms, having biological functions in important processes including cell growth, proliferation, development, and differentiation (Kusano et al., 2008; Michael, 2016; Miller-Fleming et al., 2015). Polyamines are reported to regulate ion channel function (Williams, 1997), nucleic acid packaging, DNA replication, apoptosis, gene transcription, and translation (Ruan et al., 1994). Polyamines maintain nucleic acid stability and regulate protein synthesis by acting as electrostatic bridges between the phosphate charges of DNA/RNA and various other highly charged linear chains, such as actin filaments and microtubules (Tang et al., 1996), using their multivalent cations. Plants utilize polyamines to cope with abiotic stress or microbial infection, as well as for ROS production (Walters, 2003a). Recent studies have also demonstrated that polyamines are crucial for bacterial pathogenicity (Shi et al., 2019) and fungal growth, development, and pathogenicity (Valdes-Santiago, Cervantes-Chavez, Leon-Ramirez, et al., 2012). However, detailed information about the metabolism, biological functions, and regulatory mechanisms of polyamines (including PUT, SPD, and SPM) is still lacking in the sugarcane smut fungus S. scitamineum. 

Sexual mating and the following filamentation are critical for virulence in dimorphic fungi, including U. maydis (Kahmann et al., 1995; Spellig et al., 1994), Candida albicans (Mitchell, 1998), Cryptococcus neoformans (Wickes et al., 1996), and also S. scitamineum (Chang et al., 2019; Yan, Zhu, et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2019). In the present work, we demonstrated that PUT at high concentrations suppressed S. scitamineum sexual mating/filamentation (Figure 2b). In contrast, SPD promoted filamentation (Figure 2b). We further showed that SsAGA, SsODC, and SsSAMDC are involved in different steps of polyamine biosynthesis in S. scitamineum. It is known that AGA and ODC contribute to PUT production through the ornithine pathway; in parallel, the arginine pathway contributes to PUT production through sequential reactions catalysed by ADC, AIH, and AGT (Miller-Fleming et al., 2015; Valdes-Santiago, Cervantes-Chavez, Leon-Ramirez, et al., 2012). Our study showed that loss of SsODC and SsSAMDC caused auxotrophy for PUT and SPD (Figure 1a,b), indicating that the ornithine pathway plays a predominant role in PUT biosynthesis in S. scitamineum. Sexual mating/filamentation were defective to various degrees in polyamine mutants, which 

differentially responded to exogenous addition of PUT or SPD (Figure 2a). We infer that the intracellular levels of polyamines need to be maintained at a reasonable concentration, and excessive polyamines within a cell may be detrimental. Interestingly, SPM is absent in U. maydis (Valdes-Santiago et al., 2009), but it was detectable in S. scitamineum in the present study. The importance of polyamines has been demonstrated in other phytopathogenic fungi, including the corn smut fungus U. maydis. The U. maydis spe and samdc mutants, which are defective in SPD biosynthesis, are compromised in dimorphic transition and hypersensitive towards different stress conditions (Guevara-Olvera et al., 1997; Valdes-Santiago, Cervantes-Chavez, Winkler, et al., 2012). It has also been reported that the ODC gene of Stagonospora (Septoria) nodorum is required for virulence towards wheat (Bailey et al., 2000). For Fusarium species, SPD is essential for growth, development, production of the mycotoxin deoxynivalenol (DON), and virulence (Tang et al., 2021). 

A striking increase in maize polyamine biosynthesis, mainly free and conjugated PUT, occurs in tumours induced by the fungus and in the neighbouring plant tissues during the interaction with the biotrophic pathogenic fungus U. maydis (Rodriguez-Kessler et al., 2008; Rodriguez-Kessler & Jimenez-Bremont, 2009). A strong increase in ODC and ADC transcript levels and the consequent accumulation of PUT were detected following inoculation of Fusarium graminearum to host plants (Gardiner et al., 2010). Polyamine levels increase in smut teliospores after the perception of sugarcane glycoproteins (Sanchez-Elordi et al., 2019). SPM plays an important role in the infection of rice leaves by Magnaporthe oryzae, probably through redox buffering in the endoplasmic reticulum (Rocha et al., 2020). Similarly, deletion of ChODC in Colletotrichum higginsianum results in defective hyphal and conidial development, significantly affecting appressorium-mediated infection, which cannot be restored by addition of polyamines (Yan et al., 2022). These studies demonstrate that polyamines act as a virulence factor of phytopathogenic fungi. Similarly, our results proved that the fungal SsSAMDC and SsSPDS/SPMS genes were induced in the early stage of sugarcane infection, probably to promote SPD production to facilitate infection (Figure 3d). Correspondingly, SPD accumulated in the diseased tissue (“whip”) and the proximal part of the stem below the whip (Figure 3e). We infer that S. scitamineum may utilize SPD to promote filamentation and help colonizing the host cane. On the other hand, plants also utilize polyamines to regulate immunity against pathogens, notably by amplifying pattern-triggered immunity responses through the production of ROS (Gerlin et al., 2021). PUT, SPD, and SPM are ubiquitous in plant cells, existing in free form, as cations covalently bound to proteins, or conjugated with hydroxycinnamic acid to form phenolic amides (HCAAs; Walters, 2003b), which are a diverse group of specialized metabolites involved in plant resistance to biotic or abiotic stress (Fang et al., 2021). Therefore polyamines, either sourced from the fungus or the plant, may mediate interactions between pathogen and host, likely contributing to disease occurrence and/or severity. 

The cAMP–PKA signalling pathway is essential for proper mating and filamentation, and thus critical for S. scitamineum virulence (Chang et al., 2019). In the present study, we showed that 

the SsODC gene was significantly up-regulated in the ssgpa3Δ, ssuac1Δ, and ssadr1Δ mutants (Figure 4b), and exogenous addition of SPD or SPM could partially restore the sexual mating/filamentation defect in these mutants (Figure 4a). We speculate that the sexual mating/filamentation defect in these mutants defective in the cAMP–PKA pathway is partially due to up-regulation of the SsODC gene and the consequent elevated level of PUT, which was proved to suppress sexual mating/filamentation (Figure 2b). Overall, these results indicate that the cAMP–PKA pathway regulates S. scitamineum sexual mating/filamentation by affecting polyamine biosynthesis. It has been reported that polyamines reduce intracellular cAMP levels in the rice blast fungus M. oryzae, leading to the inhibition of appressorium formation (Choi et al., 1998). In the present study, disrupted polyamine biosynthesis in the sssamdcΔ mutant did not cause any changes in the transcript levels of genes involved in the cAMP–PKA pathway. We found a direct interaction between SsGPA3 and SsODC (Figure 5a), further supporting cross-talk between the cAMP–PKA pathway and the polyamine biosynthesis pathway in S. scitamineum. 

ROS are tightly associated with plant defence responses to bacterial and fungal pathogens. One of the initial defence reactions in plants after pathogen recognition is the ROS burst (Torres, 2010). Smut pathogens modulate ROS production and scavenging to affect defence responses in host plants (Peters et al., 2017). Polyamines play dual roles in maintaining cellular redox homeostasis by both protecting against free radical-mediated damage and acting as substrates for enzymes that produce ROS (Murray Stewart et al., 2018). Based on the comparative transcriptome analysis of ss1agaΔ versus MAT-1 and ss1agaΔ×ss2agaΔ versus $M A T – 1 \times M A T – 2$ , we found that ROS marker genes encoding catalase (CAT1) and glutathione S-transferase (GST) were both significantly down-regulated in the ssagaΔ mutant (Dataset S2), which displayed significantly elevated intracellular ROS levels (Figure 6a). This indicates a connection between polyamines and intracellular redox homeostasis. Besides, polyamine mutants were more sensitive to ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ compared to WT (MAT-2) (Figure 6b). We infer that PUT may cause accumulation of ROS while SPD has the opposite role. Supporting this hypothesis, addition of PUT led to down-regulation of antioxidant enzyme genes, while SPD could promote up-regulation of these genes (Figure 6c). It has been reported that in Arabidopsis thaliana, PUT content is positively related to ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ generation via activating the copper-containing amine oxidase CuAO (Liu & Alcazar, 2021). Consistently, we found that the CuAO-encoding gene AO1 (SPSC_00335) was up-regulated in ss1agaΔ compared to the WT MAT-1 (Dataset S2), based on the comparative transcriptome analysis. We speculate that excessive PUT in ss1agaΔ can be oxidized by AO1 to produce ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , causing defective filamentation. On the other hand, it has been reported that SPD could reduce intracellular ROS levels and relieve endoplasmic reticulum stress in diabetic mouse hearts (Sun et al., 2023). Our finding that SPD suppresses intracellular ROS production by inducing expression of antioxidant enzyme genes is consistent with what has been reported in animal cells. 

In summary, we elucidated the polyamine biosynthesis pathway, which is probably dependent on the ornithine pathway (SsAGA and 

SsODC) for PUT production and on SsSAMDC for PUT-to-SPD conversion, in S. scitamineum. Our study provides evidence that polyamine biosynthesis and the cAMP–PKA signalling pathway physically and genetically interact with each other, to promote sexual mating/ filamentation by decreasing intracellular PUT levels while increasing SPD levels. Homeostasis of intracellular polyamines contributes to interaction between S. scitamineum and sugarcane, probably by modulating intracellular redox homeostasis. In the future, it is worth exploring the molecular mechanism of polyamines involved in phytopathogen–host interaction, particularly the direct target(s) of polyamines, in both fungi and plants.

# 4.8  |  Extraction and quantification of polyamines

Polyamines were extracted and detected as previously described with slight modifications (Shi et al., 2019). For the construction of PUT, SPD, and SPM standard curves, $0 . 1 \mathsf { m M }$ standard stock solution was prepared, $4 0 \mu \ L$ of the above standard solution was taken and put into a $1 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ centrifuge tube, and $1 0 \mu \iota$ of benzoyl chloride was added, followed by the addition of 1 mL of 2 M NaOH solution. The mixture was vortexed for 20 s, and the reaction was allowed to take place in a water bath at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ . Then, $_ { 2 \mathsf { m L } }$ of saturated NaCl solution was added, and after mixing, 2 mL of petroleum ether was added for extraction. After centrifugation at $\Im { 5 0 0 } \ g$ for 10 min, 1 mL of the ether phase was put into a $1 . 5 \mathrm { - m L }$ centrifuge tube and evaporated to dryness at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ or evaporated to dryness 

with nitrogen flow. The remaining sample was dissolved with $1 0 0 \mu \iota$ methanol by vortexing and passed through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter membrane. The filtrate was taken and solutions of 0.004, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, and $0 . 0 5 \mathsf { m M }$ were prepared. The peak area was taken as the ordinate and the polyamine standard concentration was taken as the abscissa to draw a standard curve and calculate the correlation coefficient. 

For the determination of intracellular and extracellular polyamine levels, fresh haploid sporidia were cultured in liquid YePS medium at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ and 200 rpm overnight. This seed solution was added to $1 0 \mathsf { m L }$ of fresh liquid YePS medium at 1:100 and cultured for 2 days. After washing twice with sterile water, the ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ was adjusted to 1.0, and $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of culture was centrifuged at ${ 1 5 0 0 ~ g }$ for 10 min. Frozen cells were homogenized in $4 \mathrm { m L }$ of $5 \%$ (wt/vol) cold perchloric acid solution, the homogenate was kept on ice for 60 min and centrifuged at ${ 1 4 , 0 0 0 g }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $3 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ , and $5 0 0 \mu \ L$ of supernatant was transferred to a 10-mL centrifuge tube. The polyamine derivatization procedure was the same as that for the standards, except that finally $3 0 0 \mu \ L$ of chromatography-grade methanol was used to dissolve by vortexing, followed by passing through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter. The sample solution was diluted 10-fold with chromatography-grade methanol and analysed by LC-MS using a Thermo Scientific UltiMate 3000 Rapid Separation Quaternary System with a reverse-phase C18 column (Supelco Discovery, $2 5 \mathsf { c m } \times 4 . 6 \mathsf { m m }$ , particle size $5 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ with the following parameters: single ion monitoring, single ion detection scan mode; column temperature, $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ; flow rate, $0 . 2 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ ; injection volume, $1 0 \mu \iota$ ; benzoylated polyamines with $60 \%$ (vol/vol) methanol; elute with water. The following $m / z$ values were obtained: PUT, $2 9 7 . 1 6 m / z$ ; SPD, $4 5 8 . 2 4 m / z$ ; SPM, $6 1 9 . 3 3 m / z$ . Data analysis was performed using Thermo Xcalibur software (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

# 4.9  |  Relative fungal biomass and pathogenicity assays

The fungal biomass assay of the inoculated sugarcane seedlings was carried out according to Sun et al. (2019). The same amount of S. scitamineum sporidia (mixture of compatible mating types) was injected into sugarcane seedlings, and total DNA was extracted from the inoculated sugarcane tissue at 3 and 7 dpi. The relative fungal biomass was determined using the fungal ACTIN gene as a reference, whereas the sugarcane glyceraldehyde dehydrogenase (GAPDH) gene served as an internal control. 

For assessment of pathogenicity, the highly susceptible sugarcane cultivar ROC22 was used for the pathogenicity assay as previously described (Chang et al., 2019). The haploid sporidia of S. scitamineum strains were grown on YePS medium in a shaking incubator at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 1–2 days. The fresh haploid sporidia were collected, resuspended in sterile ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ , adjusted to $1 0 ^ { 7 }$ cells/mL, and mixed with haploid sporidia of opposite mating types in equal volumes (1:1, vol/vol). Next, 1 mL of such mixture was injected into sugarcane seedlings as previously described (Yan, Zhu, et al., 2016). WT mixture served as a positive control and ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ was inoculated as a negative control. Inoculated plants were kept in a greenhouse 

with at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ with $80 \%$ humidity and a $1 2 \mathsf { h } / 1 2 \mathsf { h }$ light/dark cycle for 3–6 months. The pathogenicity assay was repeated two times with at least 15 plants per condition. The “black whip” symptoms were documented and photographed at about 6 months post-inoculation. Moreover, plant height was measured. The proportion of “black whip”/total seedlings was calculated.
