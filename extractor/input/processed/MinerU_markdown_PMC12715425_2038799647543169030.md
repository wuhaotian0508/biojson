# ABSTRACT

Activation of plant immunity is commonly associated with the biosynthesis of defense-related metabolites, such as jasmonic acid (JA) and salicylic acid (SA). However, constitutive activation of the immune response or excessive accumulation of defense metabolites often negatively impacts plant growth. The regulatory mechanisms underlying the trade-off between plant immunity and growth remain elusive. In this study, we identified a homeobox transcription factor, HB34, as a key regulator that balances plant immunity and growth by differentially regulating the expression of JA biosynthetic and growth-related genes in Arabidopsis. Loss of HB34 enhances plant resistance to the necrotrophic pathogen Botrytis cinerea but impairs plant growth and development, accompanied by constitutive activation of JA-responsive genes. Mechanistically, HB34 negatively regulates the transcription of JA biosynthetic genes to suppress JA accumulation, and blocking JA biosynthesis attenuates the enhanced Botrytis cinerea resistance of hb34. Conversely, HB34 enhances the transcription of growth-related genes, whereas overexpression of these genes partially rescues growth defects, thereby decoupling the trade-off between enhanced defense and impaired plant growth. Our findings reveal a novel mechanism whereby a single transcription factor fine-tunes the trade-off between plant growth and immunity by differentially regulating JA biosynthetic and growth-related genes in Arabidopsis.

# INTRODUCTION

As sessile organisms, plants are exposed to various biotic and abiotic stresses throughout their growth and development. To cope with biotic challenges, plants have evolved a range of immune strategies, collectively referred to as innate immunity, which enable them to recognize pathogens and activate defense responses (Wang et al., 2022). The plant immune system consists of two primary layers: pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), which is activated by the recognition of conserved pathogen-associated molecular pat-

terns, and effector-triggered immunity (ETI), which recognizes pathogenic effector proteins (Ngou et al., 2021; Bernoux et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). There is typically overlap between PTI and ETI downstream signaling pathways and immune responses (Ngou et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2024), which play an active role in the accumulation of plant defense hormones such as salicylic acid (SA), jasmonic acid (JA), and ethylene. These responses also induce the expression of numerous genes and reprogram various cellular processes (Wan and Xin, 2022; Jones et al., 2024). 

However, activated immunity consumes energy and results in a defense/growth trade-off, which is often accompanied by reductions in plant growth and crop yield (Huot et al., 2014; Deng and He, 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Jones et al., 2024). Upon pathogen invasion, plants rapidly activate immune responses, which are accompanied by suppressed growth signaling (Huot et al., 2014; Ballare´ and Austin, 2019; Berry and Argueso, 2022). However, the underlying molecular mechanisms that regulate this process remain elusive. Previous studies have shown that metabolic status plays a critical role in regulating this trade-off, while more recent evidence suggests that the plant growth/ defense trade-off also involves complex regulatory mechanisms and genetic pathways mediated by the coordination of transcription factors and phytohormone signaling (Denance´ et al., 2013; Guo et al., 2018; Berry and Argueso, 2022; Xie and Duan, 2023). Thus, understanding how transcription factors and plant hormones regulate plant growth and defense will provide valuable insights into how this trade-off can be uncoupled and help guide the development of disease-resistant crops (Gao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). 

Plant hormones play vital roles in regulating a wide range of developmental processes and defense responses, acting as signaling molecules that ensure plants can adapt to changing environmental conditions (Spoel and Dong, 2008; Denance´ et al., 2013; Li et al., 2024). Among key defense-related hormones, SA is central to defenses against biotrophic and hemibiotrophic pathogens, whereas JA plays a pivotal role in countering necrotrophic pathogens (Glazebrook, 2005; Yan and Xie, 2015; Howe et al., 2018; Yuan et al., 2021). As a lipid-derived plant hormone, JA is rapidly synthesized in response to the invasion of various phytopathogens and serves as a critical component of defense metabolism (Wasternack and Song, 2016; Nguyen et al., 2022; Wan and Xin, 2022). JA biosynthesis generally occurs in two distinct stages. First, $\alpha$ -linolenic acid is oxidized by lipoxygenase (LOX) in the plastids, followed by enzymatic reactions catalyzed by allene oxide synthase and allene oxide cyclase (AOC), resulting in the formation of the JA precursor 12-oxo-phytodienoic acid (OPDA). Subsequently, OPDA undergoes a series of reactions in the peroxisome— including reduction by OPDA reductase, three rounds of $\beta$ -oxidation, and isomerization—ultimately yielding bioactive JA (Wasternack and Song, 2016; Howe et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2022). Therefore, endogenous JA levels are determined by the dynamic balance between its biosynthesis and catabolism. While numerous studies have reported that transcription factors influence plant immunity in a JA-dependent manner, the mechanisms by which they regulate JA biosynthetic genes remain poorly understood. 

In Arabidopsis, the homeobox (HB) transcription factor family, which includes 14 members, plays critical roles in plant growth, development, and responses to biotic stresses (Tan and Irish, 2006). For example, overexpression of HB33 (ZHD5) promotes vegetative growth by significantly increasing leaf area and extending leaf lifespan (Ferela et al., 2023). HB23 and HB24 promote hypocotyl and root hair elongation, respectively, in Arabidopsis (Perrella et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2021). The HB34- miR157-SPL10 module regulates plant branching and inflorescence architecture in Arabidopsis (Lee et al., 2022). Overexpression of HB25 increases gibberellic acid (GA) levels, 

promotes seed longevity (Bueso et al., 2014), and enhances plant drought tolerance (Tran et al., 2006). HB21, together with HB40 and HB53, promotes the arrest of floral buds, a process accompanied by the accumulation of abscisic acid (Sa´ nchez-Gerschon et al., 2024). However, whether HB transcription factors are involved in plant immunity and how they coordinate immune responses with growth regulation remain largely unknown. 

In this study, we demonstrate that the homeobox transcription factor HB34 plays a key role in regulating the trade-off between plant defense and growth. Under normal conditions, HB34 represses inappropriate activation of JA-pathway genes and prevents excessive JA accumulation, thereby promoting plant growth. Upon pathogen infection, the transcription of HB34 is downregulated, leading to effective de-repression of JA biosynthetic genes and rapid activation of plant resistance—albeit at the expense of defective plant growth and development. Together, these findings identify HB34 as a molecular switch that fine-tunes the trade-off between plant immunity and growth by modulating JA biosynthesis and growth-related pathways in Arabidopsis. 

# RESULTS

# Loss of HB34 enhances resistance to Botrytis cinerea but suppresses plant growth

Genetic screening was performed to identify regulators of the plant immune response in Arabidopsis. A T-DNA insertion mutant, hb34-1 (Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B), which disrupts a homeobox transcription factor gene, exhibited significantly increased resistance to the necrotrophic fungal pathogen Botrytis cinerea, as evidenced by lesions with an obviously smaller diameter compared to the wild-type (WT) control (Figure 1A and 1B). Quantitative real-time PCR analysis of fungal DNA accumulation in infected leaves further confirmed that hb34-1 plants had less fungal biomass than WT plants (Figure 1C). To further investigate whether this diseaseresistance phenotype was associated with the loss of HB34, two additional mutants (hb34-2 and hb34-3) were generated using CRISPR-Cas9 technology, resulting in a 1-bp insertion and a 2-bp deletion, respectively, within the exon (Supplemental Figure 1A). The homozygous hb34-2 and hb34-3 mutants also displayed phenotypes similar to hb34-1, including lesions with smaller diameter and reduced fungal DNA accumulation compared to WT (Figure 1A–1C). These results suggest that loss of HB34 enhances plant resistance against B. cinerea. 

Because constitutive activation of the plant immune response or defense-responsive genes is usually accompanied by growth penalties (Huot et al., 2014; Deng and He, 2024), we next investigated the role of HB34 in plant growth and development. Under long-day conditions, 5-day-old hb34 seedlings exhibited significantly shorter hypocotyls than WT seedlings (Figure 1D). At 8 days old, hb34 mutants showed a $5 0 \% { - } 7 0 \%$ reduction in leaf blade area compared to WT (Figure 1E). By 18 days old, the fresh weight of hb34 mutants was reduced to nearly $50 \%$ of that of WT plants (Figure 1F). These results indicate that HB34 is required for normal plant growth under non-stress conditions.

To investigate potential functional redundancy between HB34 and its closely related homolog HB23—which clusters with HB34 and exhibits a similar expression pattern (Supplemental Figure 1C and 1D)—an hb34-1 hb23 double mutant was generated through genetic crossing, and the phenotypes were examined. Compared to WT, single deletion of hb23 resulted in enhanced resistance and a shorter hypocotyl, although these effects were less pronounced than in hb34-1 (Supplemental Figure 2). Furthermore, the immunity and growth phenotypes of the hb34-1 hb23 double mutant were similar to those observed in hb34-1, indicating that HB23 functions dependently on HB34 (Supplemental Figure 2). Therefore, only HB34 was utilized for further experiments. 

# Overexpression of HB34 promotes plant growth but reduces disease resistance

To determine whether the increased resistance and defective growth observed in hb34 mutants were indeed caused by the 

loss of HB34, HB34 overexpression lines (p35S::HB34-GFP) were generated in the hb34-1 background (Supplemental Figure 3). Compared to WT, two independent p35S::HB34-GFP hb34-1 lines exhibited increased susceptibility to B. cinerea, lesions with a larger diameter, and increased fungal DNA accumulation but an increased hypocotyl length and larger leaf blade area (Supplemental Figure 4A–4F), indicating that the phenotypes observed in hb34-1 mutants were largely suppressed by HB34 overexpression. These results indicate that HB34 plays an important role in balancing plant immunity and growth.

# Loss of HB34 leads to constitutive activation of the JA pathway

JA plays a vital role in plant resistance against necrotrophic pathogens (Glazebrook, 2005; Howe et al., 2018; Wan and Xin, 2022). Since ‘‘regulation of JA’’ was among the enriched terms for upregulated genes in hb34-1, we examined the expression level of HB34 following JA treatment and B. cinerea infection. Samples were collected from 12-day-old seedlings treated with methyl jasmonate (MeJA) and from 28-day-old plants infected with B. cinerea for further analysis. Compared to mock-treated controls, HB34 expression was significantly decreased following both MeJA treatment and B. cinerea infection (Figure 3A and 3B). To further explore the regulatory relationship between JA and HB34, we analyzed HB34 expression in several JA-related mutants, including opr3, coi1, and myc2, which encode a JA biosynthetic enzyme, a JA receptor, and a master regulator of JA downstream signaling, respectively (Wasternack and Song, 2016; Li et al., 2021). As shown in Figure 3C, HB34 transcription was upregulated in these mutants, suggesting that its expression is negatively regulated by the JA pathway. Disruption of JA biosynthesis or signaling relieves this repression, resulting in elevated HB34 expression. 

We further examined other JA-related phenotypes, including JAinduced root growth inhibition and anthocyanin accumulation, in the lines described above (Huot et al., 2014; Berry and Argueso, 2022). Under normal conditions, loss of HB34 resulted in enhanced JA sensitivity, as indicated by shorter root length and increased anthocyanin accumulation compared to WT, whereas both phenotypes were restored to WT levels in the $p 3 5 S { \mathrm { : } }$ HB34-GFP hb34-1 lines (Supplemental Figure 6A–6C). Specifically, MeJA treatment reduced root length to $5 1 . 1 \%$ of mock-treated WT and to approximately $40 \%$ in hb34-1, whereas the two HB34 overexpression lines showed more moderate reductions $( 5 8 . 3 \%$ and $5 9 . 0 \%$ , respectively). Similarly, MeJA-induced anthocyanin accumulation increased 8.2-fold in WT and approximately 10-fold in hb34-1, while the two HB34 overexpression lines exhibited lower induction (5.4- and 6.1- fold, respectively) (Figure 3D and 3E). These results suggest that disruption of HB34 leads to increased JA sensitivity, which can be alleviated by HB34 overexpression. 

To further assess the role of HB34 in JA pathway, we compared MeJA sensitivity among hb34-1, HB34 overexpression lines, and the JA-related mutants described above. Notably, hb34-1 exhibited markedly increased JA sensitivity, with reduced relative root length and increased relative anthocyanin accumulation compared to WT. In contrast, the p35S::HB34-GFP hb34-1 line partially suppressed the increased sensitivity observed in hb34-1, whereas the JA-related mutants exhibited differentially reduced sensitivity to JA (Supplemental Figure 6D and 6E). These results further support the role of HB34 as a negative regulator of the JA response. 

We then examined the expression levels of several JA-responsive genes. Under normal conditions, expression of JA-pathway genes such as PDF1.2C, PDF1.3, PR3, AOC1, LOX2, and VSP2 was significantly upregulated in hb34-1 compared to WT, whereas their expression was markedly reduced to WT levels or lower in HB34 overexpression lines (Figure 3F; Supplemental Figure 6F). Following MeJA treatment, the expression of these genes was induced to varying degrees across all genotypes. However, their expression levels remained consistently higher in hb34-1 than in WT. In contrast, expression of these genes in the p35S::HB34-GFP hb34-1 lines was reduced to WT levels or below, suggesting that HB34 overexpression partially suppresses JA-induced transcription (Figure 3F and Supplemental Figure 6F). These results further support the conclusion that constitutive activation of JA pathway in hb34-1 is associated with inappropriate activation of defense-related genes.

# Blocking JA biosynthesis suppresses the hb34 mutant phenotypes

To determine whether the increased resistance observed in hb34-1 was dependent on JA accumulation, we introduced the opr3 and coi1 mutations—disrupting a JA biosynthetic enzyme and a JA receptor, respectively (Li et al., 2021; Wan and Xin, 2022)—into the hb34-1 background via genetic crossing. Phenotypic analyses were conducted on single and double mutants. As expected, due to impaired JA accumulation or signaling, both opr3 and coi1 single mutants were susceptible to B. cinerea infection (Figure 5A–5C). Notably, the hb34-1 opr3 and hb34-1 coi1 double mutants were also susceptible to infection, resembling the opr3 and coi1 single mutants, in contrast to the increased resistance observed in hb34-1 (Figure 5A–5C). We then compared the expression levels of JA-pathway genes between the single and double mutants. Under normal conditions, the expression of JA-pathway genes such as PDF1.2C, PDF1. 3, AOC1, and LOX2, which were inappropriately activated in hb34-1, was restored to WT levels in the hb34-1 opr3 and hb34-1 coi1 double mutants (Figure 5D). After infection with B. cinerea, induction of these JA-pathway genes was differentially 

suppressed in the opr3 and coi1 double mutants, whereas their expression remained highly induced in hb34-1 (Figure 5D). These results indicate that the increased resistance in hb34-1 is dependent on JA biosynthesis and signaling. 

We further investigated additional JA-related phenotypes in these single and double mutants. Unlike the JA-sensitive phenotypes observed in hb34-1, the opr3 single mutant, coi1 single mutant, and double mutants exhibited similar insensitivity to JA, as evidenced by reduced anthocyanin accumulation and longer root length (Supplemental Figure 9). These results suggest that HB34 acts genetically upstream of OPR3 and COI1 to regulate plant immunity and the JA response. 

To determine whether the growth defects observed in hb34-1 were caused by excessive JA accumulation, we measured hypocotyl length, leaf area, and fresh weight in the single and double mutants. Compared to WT, both JA-deficient single mutants opr3 and coi1 exhibited longer hypocotyl, larger leaf area, and heavier fresh weight (Figure 5E–5G), which consistent with previous results that JA negative regulates plant growth (Yan and Xie, 2015; Major et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2018). Moreover, the hb34-1 opr3 and hb34-1 coi1 double mutants showed phenotypes similar to those of the opr3 and coi1 single mutants (Figure 5E–5G). These results further suggest that HB34 acts genetically upstream of OPR3 and COI1 in regulating plant growth and development.

# DISCUSSION

In this study, we elucidate the mechanism by which the homeobox transcription factor HB34 regulates the trade-off between plant defense and growth. Under normal conditions, HB34 represses the inappropriate activation of JA-pathway genes and prevents excessive JA accumulation, thereby promoting plant growth. Upon pathogen infection, HB34 expression is downregulated, leading to de-repression of JA biosynthetic genes and a rapid increase in JA levels, which enhances resistance at the cost of growth and developmental inhibition (Figure 7). 

Activation of plant immunity is typically associated with the biosynthesis of defense-related metabolites, such as JA, which is rapidly synthesized after infection by necrotrophic pathogens (Glazebrook, 2005; Wang et al., 2022). Although extensive knowledge of JA biosynthesis has been gained over the past 2 decades, the mechanisms directly modulating the activity of biosynthetic enzymes or genes remain largely unknown (Nguyen et al., 2022; Wan and Xin, 2022). Recently, an interesting study reported that ac4C mRNA modification modulates JA biosynthesis in rice by regulating translation of AOC during immune responses (Lu et al., 2024). Here, we found that loss of HB34 results in constitutive activation of the JA response and excessive JA accumulation. We further demonstrated that HB34 directly binds to the promoters of JA biosynthetic genes, including AOC1, LOX2, and OPR3, to suppress their expression. Blocking JA biosynthesis suppresses the phenotypes observed in hb34-1. This is consistent with findings in the jaz quintuple mutant (jazQ), in which constitutive JA signaling is 

largely abolished by blocking JA synthesis or perception (Major et al., 2017). Together, these results support the conclusion that HB34 inhibits the inappropriate activation of JA biosynthetic genes and prevents JA over-accumulation, thereby promoting plant growth under normal conditions. Upon pathogen infection, decreased expression of HB34 derepresses these JA biosynthetic genes, facilitating the rapid synthesis of JA and activation of the defense response. 

In contrast to the growth-inhibiting effects of JAs, auxins promote plant growth and development, with indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) being the most extensively studied active form (Mano and Nemoto, 2012; Li et al., 2022). In Arabidopsis, YUCCA (YUC) flavin-containing monooxygenases play key roles in regulating IAA biosynthesis (Cheng et al., 2007). Overexpression of YUC genes leads to typical auxin overproduction phenotypes, including epigastric cotyledons and increased hypocotyl length (Cheng et al., 2007; Kim et al., 2007), whereas the yuc1 yuc4 yuc10 yuc11 quadruple mutant fails to develop a hypocotyl or root meristem (Cheng et al., 2007). Additionally, upregulation of YUC genes under shade conditions induces rapid IAA accumulation, promoting hypocotyl and petiole elongation (Qu et al., 2012). In our study, we found that hb34 mutants exhibited reduced growth compared to WT. ChIP-seq assays further confirmed the direct binding HB34 to the promoters of several growth-related genes, including YUC8 and SAUR65, to enhance their transcription. Moreover, overexpression of either YUC8 or SAUR65 uncouples the trade-off between growth and defense. These findings suggest that HB34 activates the expression of these growth-related genes to promote plant growth under normal conditions. Upon pathogen infection, HB34 expression is decreased, suppressing growth-related gene expression and thereby sacrificing plant growth to effectively activate the defense response. 

Understanding the mechanism underlying this trade-off is crucial for improving crop disease resistance without yield penalty. Regulation of the balance between plant growth and immunity involves not only transcription factors and JA biosynthesis but also other regulators and mechanisms, such as hormonal crosstalk, SA biosynthesis, and epigenetic regulation (Huot et al., 2014; Berry and Argueso, 2022). For example, in rice, JA signaling simultaneously activates defense responses and GA catabolism, allowing plants to rapidly optimize resource allocation and maintain this trade-off, highlighting the key role of hormonal crosstalk

The homeobox transcription factor HB34 negatively regulates the expression of JA biosynthetic genes while positively modulating growth-related genes in Arabidopsis. Loss of HB34 enhances resistance to the necrotrophic pathogen B. cinerea but impairs plant growth and development. Under normal conditions, HB34 represses inappropriate activation of JA-responsive genes and prevents excessive JA accumulation, thereby promoting growth. Upon B. cinerea infection, HB34 transcription is downregulated, leading to de-repression of JA biosynthetic genes and rapid JA accumulation, which enhances immunity but compromises plant growth and development. These findings reveal that a single transcription factor fine-tunes the tradeoff between growth and immunity by differentially regulating JA biosynthesis and growth-related genes. 

(Jin et al., 2023). In Arabidopsis, the NAC-type transcription factor NAC90 suppresses the biosynthesis of SA and N-hydroxy pipecolic acid to control this trade-off (Cai et al., 2024). Additionally, long non-coding RNA SA biogenesis controller 1 acts as a molecular switch, orchestrating the balance between defense and growth by inhibiting SA production in Arabidopsis (Liu et al., 2022). In rice, the valine-glutamine (VQ) motif-containing protein OsVQ25 balances broad-spectrum disease resistance and plant growth by interacting with an E3 ligase and a transcription factor at both the transcriptional and posttranslational levels (Hao et al., 2022). Furthermore, the Pigm locus, which encodes two proteins, PigmR and PigmS, has undergone long-term domestication selection in rice and differentially regulates rice blast resistance and grain yield, respectively (Deng et al., 2017). In our study, we found that HB34 directly binds to the promoters of JA biosynthetic genes AOC1, LOX2, and OPR3 and negatively modulates their expression, thereby reducing JA accumulation. Moreover, blocking JA biosynthesis suppressed both the enhanced resistance and impaired growth phenotypes in hb34-1, collectively suggesting that JA biosynthesis plays a key role in regulating the growth–defense trade-off. 

In summary, we reveal a novel mechanism by which the transcription factor HB34 differentially regulates the expression of JA biosynthetic and growth-related genes to fine-tune the balance between plant defense and growth. Overexpression of growth-related genes maintains plant immunity while uncoupling it from growth penalties, providing valuable insights for improving crop disease resistance without compromising yield. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The WT Arabidopsis thaliana used in this study was the Columbia-0 (Col-0) ecotype. The T-DNA insertion lines hb34-1 (SALK_085482C), hb23 (SALK_006408C), and opr3 (SALK_201355C) were obtained from Arashare (https://www.arashare.cn/index/). The hb34-2 and hb34-3 mutants were generated using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated editing in the Col-0 background. The coi1-2 mutant was generously provided by Dr. Jian Ye (Institute of Microbiology, Chinese Academy of Sciences). Higher-order mutants were obtained through genetic crossing. The primers used for genotyping are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

Stratified seeds were germinated on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium for 7 days and then transplanted into a 1:1 mixture of peat and vermiculite. Arabidopsis plants were cultivated in a greenhouse at ${ } ^ { 2 2 } { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod (long-day condition, [LD]) or a 10-h light/14-h dark photoperiod (short-day condition, [SD]). For growth phenotype analyses, 5-day-old and 8-day-old seedlings grown under LD were used to measure hypocotyl length and leaf blade area; 18-day-old plants grown under SD were used to measure fresh weight. 

# Plasmid construction

To generate the hb34-2 and hb34-3 mutants, guide RNAs targeting the exon of HB34 were cloned into pCambia1300-M-UBQ10 (Wang and Chen, 2019). For the p35S::HB34-GFP construct, a 936-bp genomic DNA fragment containing the full-length coding sequence of HB34 fused with GFP was cloned into the binary vector pLZ-B2GW7 using Gateway technology (Invitrogen). The resulting construct was transformed into the hb34-1 background (Karimi et al., 2005). For construction of HB34- ER, the full-length coding sequence of HB34 was cloned into the pER8 vector and subsequently introduced into the Col-0 background (Zuo et al., 2002). 

# B. cinerea inoculation assays

B. cinerea inoculation assays were performed as previously described (Lin et al., 2024). The strain was cultured on V8 juice solid medium $3 6 \%$ V8 juice, $0 . 0 2 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { C a C O } _ { 3 }$ , and $2 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / \mathsf { L }$ agar) for 12 days in the dark at room temperature. Fresh conidia were suspended in one-third-strength potato dextrose agar (P2182, Sigma-Aldrich) and adjusted to a concentration of 2 $\times ~ 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores $\mathsf { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ . For inoculation, 4-week-old plants grown under SD conditions were inoculated with $3 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ conidial suspension or sprayed with the conidiophore suspension. Inoculated leaves were photographed 2-3 days post-inoculation, and lesion diameters were measured using ImageJ (http://rsb.info.nih.gov/ij/). DNA was extracted from infected leaves at $4 8 ~ \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation, and fungal growth was quantified by real-time PCR. 

# RNA extraction, RT-qPCR, and RNA-seq analysis

Total RNA was extracted using NGZOL reagent (HLingene, NG303M) and reverse-transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using a commercial kit (TOYOBO, FSQ-301). Real-time qPCR was performed using SYBR Green qPCR Master Mix (TRANS, AQ601) on the C1000 Touch™ Thermal Cycler (YEASEN). ACTIN2 was used as the internal control to normalize the amount of cDNA template. The primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

RNA-seq was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform using the HiSeq-PE150 sequencing strategy by Novogene. Clean reads were aligned to the Arabidopsis thaliana genome (TAIR10) using Hisat2 (v2.1.0). Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 (v1.30.1) based on three biological replicates. Gene Ontology enrichment analysis was performed using the DAVID web portal (https://david.ncifcrf. gov/home.jsp). Statistical analysis of the overlap between two gene sets was conducted using the webtool (http://nemates.org/MA/progs/ overlap_stats.html), with the total number of genes in A. thaliana set at 34 218. 

# EMSAs

The full-length coding sequence of HB34 fused with MBP was cloned into the pMAL-c5X vector and introduced into Escherichia coli strain BL21. Recombinant MBP-HB34 protein was induced at ${ } ^ { 2 0 } { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight with 1 mM isopropyl $\beta$ -d-1-thiogalactopyranoside and purified using an amylose resin column (NEB, E8021V). Biotin-labeled probes were synthesized by GenScript. Binding reactions, containing binding buffer, $2 \ \mu \ g$ MBP-HB34 or MBP, and $1 ~ \mu \ L$ biotin-labeled or unlabeled probes, were incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 8 } { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 20 min and then transferred onto Amersham Hybond-NC nylon membranes (GE Healthcare, INYC00010). Signals were detected using the Chemiluminescent Biotin-labeled Nucleic Acid Detection Kit (Beyotime Biotech, D3308). For competition assays, a 20- fold excess of unlabeled probes was added to the binding reactions. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assays

The $2 . 0 – \mathsf { k b }$ promoter fragments of LOX2, AOC1, OPR3, YUC8, and SAUR65 were cloned into the pGreen II0800-LUC vector to generate individual reporter constructs. The reporter and effector constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens and co-infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. Relative LUC and Renilla (REN) activities were measured using a dual-luciferase assay kit (Vazyme, DL101-01). The LUC/ REN ratio was calculated, with the control effector set to ‘‘1.’’ Three biological replicates were performed. 

# Anthocyanin measurement

Ten-day-old seedlings grown on half-strength MS medium supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose were harvested for anthocyanin measurement. Anthocyanin extraction followed the method described previously by Lei et al. (2011). Samples were extracted with methanol containing $1 \%$ HCl (v/v), followed by the addition of deionized water $( 0 . 5 \times$ volume) and chloroform $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { 1 } \times$ volume) to remove chlorophyll. The supernatant was collected for spectrophotometric analysis at $5 3 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ and 657 nm. 

Relative anthocyanin accumulation was calculated as $( \mathsf { A } _ { 5 3 0 } – \mathsf { A } _ { 6 5 7 } )$ per gram of fresh weight. All measurements were performed with three biological replicates. 

# ChIP and ChIP-qPCR

ChIP assays were performed according to the method of Lin et al. (2024). Briefly, $0 . 5 { \mathfrak { g } }$ of 11-day-old seedlings was fixed in $1 \%$ formaldehyde, and the reaction was quenched with $1 2 5 \mathsf { m M }$ glycine. Samples were ground in liquid nitrogen and resuspended in nuclei isolation buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl $\mathsf { p H 8 } . 0$ , 10 mM sodium butyrate, $4 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sucrose, $0 . 1 \mathsf { m M }$ PMSF, 5 mM $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol, and freshly added $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail [MCE, HY-K0010]). Isolated nuclei were lysed in $1 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 10 mM sodium butyrate, $0 . 1 \%$ deoxycholate, $0 . 1 \%$ SDS, $1 \%$ Triton X-100, 1 mM PMSF, and freshly added $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail). Chromatin was sonicated into 200-500 bp fragments using a Diagenode Bioruptor with 18 cycles at high setting (30 s on/30 s off). Immunoprecipitation was performed using anti-GFP antibodies (Abcam, ab290) and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The recovered DNA was used as templates for real-time PCR or ChIP-seq analyses. Relative enrichment was normalized to the internal control TUB2 with two biological replicates. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# ChIP-seq analysis

DNA samples from ChIP assays were used for library preparation with the Scale ssDNA-seq Lib Prep Kit (ABclonal, RK20228). High-throughput sequencing was performed on the Illumina HiSeq-PE150 platform by LC-Bio Technology. The raw data were cleaned using Fastp (v0.23.2) to filter out adapters and reads with low-quality scores using default parameters. The remaining reads were aligned to the A. thaliana TAIR10 reference genome using Bowtie2 (v2.2.5). Unmapped reads or reads mapped to multiple positions on the genome were removed by SAMtools (v1.9). Peak calling was performed using MACS2 (v2.2.6) with the parameters ‘‘-f BAMPE –keep-dup all -g 119481543 -q 0.01 –nomodel –extsize 200.’’ Read coverage signals were calculated using bamCoverage from DeepTools2 (v3.5.2). Data visualization was carried out with IGV. Comparative analysis of average enrichment at specific genomic regions between HB34-IP and HB34-input samples was conducted by plotProfile and plotHeatmap from DeepTools. To assign peaks to proximal genes, regions within $1 . 5 \mathsf { k b }$ upstream and downstream of TSS were extracted. 

# JA measurement

Twenty-eight-day-old seedlings grown under SD conditions were sprayed with B. cinerea spores $( 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores mL− 1 ). Samples were collected 2 days post-treatment from both infected and uninfected seedlings, with three biological replicates. JA extraction and quantification were performed by Nanjing Ruiyuan Biotechnology following the protocol described by Fu et al., 2012. 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using two-tailed Student’s t-tests and one-way ANOVA with GraphPad Prism and IBM SPSS Statistics 27.
