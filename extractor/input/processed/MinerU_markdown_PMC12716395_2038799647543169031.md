# Natural variants of AGL80.5 and FPA.3 contribute to bud break timing in poplar by controlling auxin biosynthesis

Lianzheng Li ${ ^ { 1 , 2 , 3 , 4 } \dag , }$ , Rui Huang1,2,3 †, Zhuoying Jin1,2,3 †, Donghai Zhang1,2,3 , Mengjiao Zhang1, 2,3 , Liang Xiao1,2,3 , Peng Li1,2,3 , Jiaxuan Zhou1,2,3 , Chongde Tian1,2,3 , Leishi Zhong1,2,3 , Mingyang Quan1,2,3 , Lijun Liu5 , Qinsong Yang1,2,3 , Deqiang Zhang1,2,3 , Qingzhang Du1,2,3 * 

The molecular mechanisms governing phenological characteristics and local adaptation in woody perennials remain largely unexplored. Here, we identified that AGAMOUS-LIKE 80 (PtoAGL80.5) and FLOWERING TIME CONTROL PROTEIN FPA (PtoFPA.3) were simultaneously associated with photoperiod factors and bud break (BB) timing in Populus tomentosa. Transgenic experiments in growth chamber and two latitudinal field trials confirmed their positive roles in promoting BB. Both are activated by the canonical phenological regulator SUPPRESSOR OF OVER-EXPRESSION OF CO 1, which, in turn, up-regulates the auxin biosynthesis gene TRYPTOPHAN AMINOTRANSFERASE– RELATED (PtoTAR2.1), accelerating BB via auxin pathway. As superior haplotypes, PtoFPA. $3 ^ { \mathsf { H A P 1 } }$ binds PtoAGL80.5HAP1 messenger RNA to elevate protein, up-regulating PtoTAR2.1 and increasing indole-3-acetic acid accumulation. Under future climate scenarios, frequencies of PtoFPA. $3 ^ { \mathsf { H A P 1 } }$ and $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathsf { H A P 1 } }$ are predicted to decline, narrowing BB variance across populations. This genetic regulatory network governing BB timing via the auxin pathway in perennial plants provides insights for molecular design breeding and ecological conservation amid climate change.

# INTRODUCTION

Numerous terrestrial plants exhibit diverse distribution patterns across varied environments within their geographic range, with localized selection pressures driving adaptation to distinct habitats (1). Compared to annual plants, perennials, especially those inhabiting temperate and boreal regions, often have extended life cycles and are distributed across specific geographical areas (2, 3). However, notable geographic gradients across geographic gradients shape distinct local adaptations, even in the presence of substantial gene flow (4). In this context, phenology traits play a pivotal role in balancing survival and growth throughout a plant’s life cycle, representing a fundamental aspect of plant developmental stages (5, 6). Perennial woody plants undergo distinct annual phenological cycles, including bud overwintering, followed by the onset of spring bud break (BB) and subsequent seasonal growth (7). Within this cycle, spring BB serves as a crucial adaptive mechanism, optimally balancing the need for an extended growing season against the risks of spring frost damage or dehydration caused by premature BB (8, 9). In general, BB is influenced by regular environmental fluctuations associated with temperature and photoperiod (10, 11). In some cases, an extended photoperiod promotes cell growth, potentially compensating for insufficient chilling during winter dormancy (12,  13). Thus, given the intricate interplay between environmental factors and 

genetic regulation, the molecular mechanisms underlying the biological variation and local adaptation of BB remain largely elusive. 

Both environmental factors and hormones influence the survival, productivity, and spatial distribution of perennial woody plants by regulating spring BB timing (14). Genetic dissection of BB across woody taxa has been ongoing, leading to the identification of genes associated with this process (15, 16). In poplar, the SHORT VEGE-TATIVE PHASE–like (SVP) gene is essential for maintaining and releasing endodormancy by regulating the antagonistic interactions between abscisic acid (ABA) and gibberellin (GA) (17). Moreover, SUPPRESSOR OF OVEREXPRESSION OF CO1 (PtoSOC1) exhibits a seasonal expression pattern during dormancy release, while GA4 plays a role in bud release in apricot (18, 19). Thus, plant hormones (e.g., ABA and GA) play pivotal roles in regulating dormancy release and BB (20,  21). However, in poplar, BB timing is also regulated by an APETALA2/ethylene-like responsive factor, Early bud-break 1 (EBB1), which putatively targets transcription factors involved in the regulation of auxin [i.e., indole-3-acetic acid (IAA)] biosynthesis. During this process, several genes associated with auxin metabolism exhibit significant expression changes (22, 23). Few studies have investigated bud reinitiation after dormancy via the auxin pathway (24, 25). The genetic regulatory network associated with IAA-mediated spring BB in perennial plants offers deeper insights into the complex genetic mechanisms underlying this process. 

The genetic mechanisms underlying BB timing in woody plants are complex and remain largely unclear. Deciphering genetic variants associated with BB timing and local adaptation to environmental gradients presents a central challenge. While genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and phenotypic measurements are essential for uncovering genetic mechanisms, identifying the selective pressures shaping local adaptation remains difficult, given the inherent complexity of long-lived species and their prolonged life cycles (26). Recent advancements in genome-environment association (GEA) studies have provided a robust framework for identifying natural variants responsible for climate adaptation in populations. This approach is instrumental in elucidating the fine-scale genetic 

architecture of local adaptation and understanding species’ responses to climatic change (27–29). Furthermore, given the wide distribution and prolonged life cycles of trees, adaptation may occur at the individual organism level (30). In this context, validating the historical evolutionary trajectories of climate-associated alleles and developing predictive models are crucial for assessing the potential impacts of future climatic changes on local adaptation (31, 32). 

In this study, we investigated the genetic architecture and local adaptation of spring BB timing in a natural population of Populus tomentosa. We identified two candidate genes, AGAMOUS-LIKE 80 (PtoAGL80.5) and FLOWERING TIME CONTROL PROTEIN FPA (PtoFPA.3), which significantly influence BB timing variation and photoperiod response. Through transgenic poplars grown in a growth chamber and two field trials at different latitudes, we demonstrated the roles of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 in accelerating BB timing and constructed an integrated regulatory network centered on them. Upon activation by PtoSOC1, PtoFPA.3 enhances PtoAGL80.5 protein accumulation through posttranscriptional regulation, while PtoAGL80.5 promotes auxin synthesis by activating TRYPTOPHAN AMINOTRANSFERASE–RELATED (PtoTAR2.1). Distinct haplotypes of these genes drive BB variation among subpopulations, shaped by natural selection and local adaptation. These results advance our understanding of the genetic regulatory network underlying spring BB in perennial plants, with valuable implications for forestry and ecological conservation in the context of climate change. 

# RESULTS Latitudinal differentiation influences bud phenology variation in P. tomentosa

To detect population variation patterns of BB in Populus, we used a dataset consisting of 300 accessions of $P .$ tomentosa sourced from temperate and boreal regions within the species’ natural range. These accessions were planted in a common garden in Guanxian $( 3 6 . 3 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } ,$ , $1 1 5 . 7 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) in 2017. The timing of vegetative BB, measured in Julian days (JDs), showed consistency between two consecutive years (2019 and 2020; fig.  S1A and table  S8). The BB timing was advanced with the shortening of winter chilling and the extension of the spring warming period (Fig. 1A and fig. S1, A and B), indicating that temperature and photoperiod play crucial roles in the variation of BB timing in P. tomentosa. Furthermore, we found a clear linear pattern between BB characteristics and the latitudes of the source provenances when all accessions were grown together in Guanxian garden (mid-latitude site; $3 6 . 3 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } ,$ , $1 1 5 . 7 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ). 

The BB date range was 67 to 115 days (mean $\approx 9 0$ days) (Fig. 1A, fig.  S1, A and B, and table  S9), indicating substantial phenotypic differentiation between each pair of the three latitude provenances. Accessions from low-latitude provenances (L $; n = 1 3 0$ , Southern climatic region) exhibited the earliest mean BB $( 7 9 . 4 9 \pm 1 1 . 6 8 $ days in 2019 and $8 6 . 5 8 \pm 1 3 . 0 6$ days in 2020), whereas those from high-latitude provenances (H; $n = 7 7$ , Northwest climatic region) showed substantially later BB $( 1 0 0 . 8 1 \pm 1 4 . 1 0 $ days in 2019 and $9 9 . 1 3 \pm 9 . 1 3$ days in 2020), corresponding to an average difference of 21.32 days in 2019, 12.55 days in 2020, and 16.94 days across both years. The medium-latitude group (M; $n = 9 3$ ) showed intermediate BB values $( 8 6 . 4 3 \pm 1 1 . 3 3 $ days in 2019 and $9 2 . 4 9 \pm 1 2 . 9 1$ days in 2020), lying between the L and H groups. Theoretically, low-latitude genotypes, when relocated to a mid-latitude environment, would likely fulfill their chilling requirements more swiftly, resulting in an earlier BB. 

In contrast, high-latitude genotypes would require a prolonged chilling period to meet their needs under the same mid-latitude conditions, potentially leading to a delay in BB. These findings highlight the strong influence of geographic origin on the timing of BB, reflecting the local adaptation of P. tomentosa provenances to their native climatic conditions. 

# Genetic basis underlying poplar BB timing over two consecutive years

For the P. tomentosa natural population, all 300 accessions were divided into three subpopulations through population structure studies (33–36), which is consistent with three geographical latitude regions (L, M, and H for low-, middle-, and high-latitude regions). We then conducted GWAS and identified 84 significant single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) (annotated to 58 candidate genes) associated with the BB timing using the best linear unbiased prediction (BLUP) and mean BB timing over two consecutive years (2019 and 2020), including 21, 29, and 34 SNPs for BB_BLUP, BB_2019, and BB_2020, respectively. Of these, 34 were located in promoter regions, nine in exon regions (including seven missense mutations and two synonymous mutations), and the remaining in downstream and intron regions (Fig. 1B, fig. S2, A and B, and table S1). 

Among these, two significant pleiotropic SNPs, Chr12_4079772 and Chr14_5785880, associated with BB timing over two consecutive years (2019, 2020, and BLUP) were identified. SNP Chr12_4079772 (G/T; Arg to Ile) is located within the exon region of Ptom012G00388, which is a homolog of a Populus trichocarpa gene annotated as “agamous-like MADS-box protein AGL80” harboring the conserved MADS-box domain based on the phylogenetic analysis (Fig. 1B and figs. S2, A and B, and S14). Moreover, SNP Chr14_5785277 (synonymous mutation) was located in the exon region of Ptom014G00573, which encodes PtoFPA.3 that has a spen paralog and ortholog Cterminal (SPOC) domain and two RNA recognition motifs (Fig. 1B and fig. S2, A and B). Phylogenetic analysis indicated that Ptom014G00573 is a homolog of PtrFPA and AtFPA (fig. S15), which are involved in the regulation of phenological transition in spring (37). Spatiotemporal expression patterns showed that PtoAGL80.5 was expressed in the apex, whereas PtoFPA.3 exhibited expression across multiple developmental tissues (especially the apex and immature leaves) (fig. S1C), supporting their potential roles in mediating BB. 

Notably, candidate gene–based association study revealed that SNPs in the exon regions of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 exhibited the strongest association with BB and were in strong linkage disequilibrium (LD) with adjacent SNPs in the P. tomentosa natural population (Fig. 1D and figs. S2D and S18). # Adaptive contribution of BB timing by PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3

Combining the GWAS and GEA analyses, we specifically focused on PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3, which are significantly associated with BB (2019, 2020, and BLUP) and photoperiod (PP_DOR) (Figs. 1B 

and  2C and fig.  S2). To assess the potential adaptive variants of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3, we analyzed nucleotide diversity (fixation index $F _ { S T }$ and nucleotide diversity $\pi$ ) across their genomic regions in different geographical subpopulations. Strong evidence of natural selection was detected in the regions surrounding PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 (Fig. 2, D and E), suggesting their potential roles in the BB process of $P _ { \ast }$ tomentosa across diverse latitudinal environments. Furthermore, the genomic regions surrounding PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 exhibited a higher concentration of significant hits using the integrated haplotype score test at the chromosome scale (fig. S6, A and B). Compared to randomly selected SNPs, stronger signatures of positive selection near the PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 loci were identified by a composite-likelihood–based test (fig. S6, C and D). Collectively, PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 are located within regions undergoing selective sweeps, highlighting their potential roles as core adaptive genes involved in mediating BB timing. The frequencies of the PtoAGL80.5HAP1 $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { \# A P 1 } }$ alleles in H accessions were 18.0 and $9 . 8 4 \%$ , respectively. Meanwhile, their frequencies increased progressively in M accessions (26.56 and $6 7 . 1 9 \%$ ) and L accessions (59.50 and $9 4 . 6 0 \%$ ) (Fig. 2G). Conversely, the PtoAGL80.5HAP2 $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ alleles exhibited the opposite geographic distribution pattern. Notably, this geographic shift in allele frequencies coincided with a climatic gradient across the distribution of P. tomentosa, which, in turn, paralleled the phenotypic differentiation in BB timing (fig. S6, E to G). 

# Transgenic evidence confirms BB timing promotion of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3

To explore the role of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 in BB regulation, we generated overexpression (OE) lines and knockdown [RNA interference (RNAi)] mutants using specific gene sequences $( P t o \dot { A } G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ and $\ r { P t o F P A . 3 } ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ ) derived from L accessions with early BB timing. These transgenic lines were developed in the 84K clone (Populus alba × Populus glandullosa) genetic background [wild type (WT)], in which the AGL80.5 and FPA.3 sequences (table S7) (38), including untranslated regions and RNAi target regions, are highly conserved with those of P. tomentosa “LM50,” thereby validating the reliability of the OE and RNAi constructs (Fig. 3, A and B, and fig. S19). These experiments were conducted under controlled growth chamber conditions and in two field locations at different latitudes. Under growth chamber conditions, PtoAGL80.5-OE and PtoFPA.3-OE lines exhibited a hypersensitive phenotype, with significantly advanced BB timing compared to WT when transferred from short daylength of 8 hours $( \mathrm { S D } ^ { \mathrm { 8 h o u r s } } )$ to long daylength of 16 hours $( \mathrm { L D } ^ { \mathrm { 1 6 h o u r s } } )$ ) (Fig. 3, C to E, and fig. S7). Conversely, PtoAGL80.5-RNAi and PtoFPA.3- RNAi lines displayed a hyposensitive phenotype, delaying BB timing by 14 days relative to WT. Furthermore, field BB timing observations of these transgenic lines were conducted simultaneously in Beijing $( 4 0 . 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } ,$ , $1 1 6 . 3 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) and Guanxian gardens $3 6 . 3 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 1 5 . 3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) (Fig.  3D and fig.  S8). In both locations, PtoAGL80.5-OE and PtoFPA.3-OE lines exhibited accelerated growth initiation and earlier BB timing compared to WT, whereas PtoAGL80.5-RNAi and PtoFPA.3-RNAi lines showed significantly delayed BB timing (Fig. 3D). Notably, BB timing occurred earlier in the Guanxian garden compared to Beijing, confirming that latitude differences have practical implications for the BB timing of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 transgenic lines. 

# PtoFPA.3HAP1 enhances PtoAGL80.5HAP1 protein accumulation to control poplar BB timing

To elucidate whether PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 act independently or synergistically in regulating poplar BB, we performed an immunoblot assay to analyze PtoAGL80.5 protein levels in all transgenic lines. We found that PtoAGL80.5 protein levels were increased in PtoFPA.3-OE lines, while the transcript levels of PtoAGL80.5 were similar among the WT, PtoFPA.3-OE, and -RNAi lines, suggesting that PtoFPA.3 positively regulates the expression of PtoAGL80.5 posttranscriptionally, potentially by enhancing its protein stability or translation efficiency (Fig. 3F). FPA encodes a functional protein characterized by three RNA recognition motifs and is involved in posttranscriptional regulation (39). To confirm this, we treated 2-month-old PtoFPA.3-OE leaves with actinomycin D to block transcription. Compared to the controls, PtoAGL80.5 mRNA levels remained higher in PtoFPA.3-OE lines, indicating that PtoFPA.3 stabilizes PtoAGL80.5 at the RNA level (Fig. 3H). Subsequently, we introduced the two main haplotype combinations, PtoFPA.3HAP1/ $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P i } } /$ PtoAGL80.5HAP1 and PtoFPA.3HAP2/PtoAGL80.5HAP2, into poplar leaf protoplasts. Compared to the latter, cotransformation with PtoFPA.3HAP1/PtoAGL80.5HAP1 resulted in higher PtoAGL80.5 protein accumulation in poplar protoplasts (Fig. 3G). Moreover, we performed an RNA–electrophoretic mobility shift assay (R-EMSA), which showed that PtoFPA.3 binds to PtoAGL80.5HAP1 mRNA more strongly than to PtoAGL80.5HAP 2 mRNA, while the two PtoFPA.3 haplotypes bind almost equivalently (Fig. 3I). These results demonstrate that PtoFPA.3 protein binds to PtoAGL80.5HAP1 mRNA with 

high affinity and promotes its protein accumulation at the posttranscriptional level, thereby synergistically regulating poplar BB timing. 

# PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 promote BB timing through IAA signaling pathway

# PtoSOC1 regulates BB timing via PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 pathway

Sequence analysis revealed that the promoters of both PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 contain the binding sequence for Ptom014G0532 (PtoSOC1). PtoSOC1 encodes a MADS-box domain–containing protein with a K-box coiled-coil region, whose homolog in Arabidopsis, AtSOC1, regulates phase transitions during the vegetative stages of development, as supported by phylogenetic analysis (fig. S16) (43). When promoter–firefly luciferase (LUC) reporter constructs of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 were cotransformed with PtoSOC1 into N. benthamiana leaves, respectively, luciferase luminescence was detected, with significantly higher luminescence compared to the negative controls (fig. S10, D and E). In addition, Y1H assays revealed that PtoSOC1 binds to and activates the promoters of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 promoters (fig. S10F). On the basis of these findings, we posit that PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 are regulated by PtoSOC1, which underlies BB in P. tomentosa. 

To validate this hypothesis, we further explored the regulatory relationship between PtoAGL80.5, PtoFPA.3, and PtoSOC1 using PtoSOC1-OE and PtoSOC1-RNAi lines. PtoSOC1 regulated the transcription of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3, as their expression levels were up-regulated in the PtoSOC1-OE lines and significantly down-regulated in PotSOC1-RNAi lines (fig. S10C). Artificial growth chamber experiments further demonstrated that PtoSOC1-OE lines exhibited advanced BB timing, whereas PtoSOC1-RNAi lines showed delayed BB (fig. S10, B and C). Consistently, dynamic transcriptome data during the BB process also revealed a significant up-regulation of PtoSOC1, whereas PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 remained largely unchanged, with PtoAGL80.5 showing a transient decrease (Fig. 1C and fig. S10G). Weighted correlation network analysis (WGCNA) revealed that PtoSOC1 exhibited prominently higher coexpression levels (weight $> 0 . 2$ ) and shared similar expression patterns with both genes, reinforcing the notion that these genes function within a coordinated network to regulate BB timing in response to environmental cues (table S5). 

# Evolutionary allele shifts of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3

To investigate the origins and evolutionary shifts of distinct haplotypes of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3, we analyzed the coding sequences of their orthologous genes from three annual plants (Arabidopsis thaliana, Arabidopsis lyrata, and Oryza sativa) and five other Populus species (Populus tremula, Populus euphratica, P. trichocarpa, Populus deltoides, and Populus simonii) to construct phylogenetic trees. Phylogenetic analysis revealed that AGL80.5 and FPA.3 emerged earlier in annual plants (e.g., Poaceae and Brassicaceae) (fig. S11). A fixed haplotype pattern (AGL80HAP2 $( A G L 8 0 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $F P A ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ ) in Populus was identified following the emergence of P. tremula. Moreover, haplotypes in P. tomentosa exhibited the closest genetic relationship to P. tremula, consistent with the palaeohistory of Populus (33). However, AGL80.5HAP2 $A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A } ^ { \bullet } \mathsf { P } 2 }$ remained stably distributed in the common gardens of Seval (northern Sweden; $6 3 . 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } .$ , $2 0 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) and Exebo (southern Sweden; $5 5 . 9 \mathrm { ^ { o } N } .$ , $1 3 . 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) without allelic differentiation, which may be attributable to the stabilizing effect of the highlatitude, extremely cold habitats on the genomic landscape (44). 

Specifically, $A { \dot { G } } L 8 0 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ originated approximately 1.98 million years ago (Ma), around 6.95 million years after the speciation event that separated P. tomentosa and P. tremula. In contrast, PtoAGL80.5HAP1 emerged approximately 1.49 Ma (Fig. 6A). Similarly, FPAHAP2 p $F P A ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ redates the divergence of AGL80.5 in P. tomentosa and P. tremula $\left( { \sim } 4 . 6 9 \mathrm { M a } \right)$ ; however, its differentiation within the P. tomentosa population only began 0.16 Ma (Fig. 6A). Hence, PtoAGL80.5HAP2 and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ , specialized for high latitudes, are considered ancestral forms. This comprehensive analysis provides crucial insights into the evolutionary dynamics of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 alleles, shedding light on their temporal origins and adaptive significance.

# DISCUSSION

Vegetative BB, the first visible indication of growth recovery following winter freezing and dehydration stress, is a critical adaptive mechanism in perennial species of temperate and boreal regions. Here, we proposed a comprehensive allelic gene module involving PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3, which modulates spring BB in P. tomentosa. These findings provide valuable insights into the fundamental mechanisms underlying vegetative BB, contributing to a more holistic understanding of BB regulation in perennial woody plants. 

# Genetic regulatory network modulating BB in P. tomentosa

Spring BB timing is of critical ecological importance for perennial woody plants in temperate and boreal ecosystems. Premature BB increases vulnerability to late cold snaps, potentially leading to fatal damage, whereas delayed BB may reduce competitive advantage. In this study, BB exhibited significant latitudinal differentiation, with accessions from lower latitudes displaying earlier BB compared to those from higher latitudes (Fig. 1A and fig. S1, A and B). However, early BB in L accessions could heighten the risk of frost damage, potentially hindering growth. Conversely, delayed BB in H accessions may shorten the growing season, preventing plants from fully using the optimal growth window. As a result, only about onefourth of the L subpopulation and one-sixth of the H subpopulation exhibited the expected BB patterns, suggesting that additional environmental factors (e.g., precipitation and photoperiod) likely 

influence bud phenology beyond chilling requirements. We identified PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 as key regulators of spring BB, with their selection influenced by PP_DOR in P. tomentosa (Figs.  1B and 2C and fig. S2A). PtoAGL80.5 belongs to the type I MADS transcription factor family and is specifically expressed in the apex (fig. S1C). In trees, MADS-box transcription factors (e.g., FLOWER-ING LOCUS C and Dormancy-Associated MADS-Box genes) play a crucial role in regulating the transition from dormancy to growth during both vegetative and reproductive development (45–47). Similarly, PtoFPA.3 is associated with the BB timing and photoperiod (Figs. 1B and 2C and fig. S2A). Its Arabidopsis homolog function as a key regulator of flowering time in response to daylength. Dynamic transcriptome analysis did not reveal bulk up-regulation of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 in poplar buds throughout winter and spring; their levels were largely stable, and PtoAGL80.5 transiently decreased at BB (Fig. 1C). This observation was further validated through genetic transformation in poplar, confirming PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 as key mediators of environmental cues and establishing their roles as positive regulators of BB (Fig. 3, A to E, and figs. S7 and S8). The observed up-regulation of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 mirrors that of the bud promoting factor GA20 oxidase (GA20ox), Flowering Locus T (FT), and other phenology genes associated with BB (48, 49). Moreover, FPA, an autonomous pathway gene, regulates gene sense transcripts (50). We also found that PtoFPA.3 directly bind to the RNA of PtoAGL80.5, influencing its protein content posttranscriptionally (Fig. 3, F to I). IAA quantification and bud transition within transgenic lines (growth chamber and fields) demonstrate that long-day and warm environments promote BB occurrence. These findings suggest that PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 may be previously unidentified components of the regulatory network modulating poplar phenology. 

Previous studies have established that auxin is essential for different stages in the development of woody perennials, particularly in dormancy release and BB (22, 51). In this study, we found a strong correlation between the up-regulation of IAA biosynthesis genes and BB progression, with IAA levels significantly elevated in transgenic plants (Fig. 5I, fig. S9, and table S4). In contrast, although ABA and GA are generally regarded as key regulators of BB in plants, their roles in this study were less pronounced, as indicated by DEG analysis, dynamic transcriptomics, and hormone quantification. As a crucial hormone orchestrating BB, ABA is conventionally considered antagonistic to IAA in many plants developmental processes (14, 48). In our P. tomentosa dataset, we did not find evidence that ABA is a major driver of natural variation in BB timing. DEG analysis revealed that several genes involved in ABA metabolism exhibited differential expression during the transition. Among them, PtoCYP707A2, which catalyzes ABA degradation, was up-regulated, coinciding with decreased ABA levels in OE lines (table S4). This reduction may subtly influence dormancy release and BB, hinting at a potential ABA-IAA interplay, but does not indicate a major role for ABA in natural BB variation. Similarly, while GA is widely known for its role in dormancy release and early BB in other species (52), in our P. tomentosa dataset, GA levels remained unchanged and no GA-pathway DEGs were detected, indicating that GA is not a major contributor to natural variation in BB timing (Fig. 4, A and C, and table S4). However, GA may still play an indirect role under varying environmental conditions. While auxin is primarily recognized for its crucial role in paradormancy, its specific function in BB remains a subject of ongoing investigation (53). We found that 

PtoAGL80.5 positively regulates PtoTAR2.1 expression (Fig. 5, C to K). In Arabidopsis, the Trp aminotransferase TAA1 and its homologs TAR1 and TAR2 convert l-Trp to IPyA (54), which represents the primary mechanism for de novo auxin biosynthesis in plants (40, 55). Although two TAR2 homologs are present in P. tomentosa (fig. S17), only the promoter motif of PtoTAR2.1 specifically bound to PtoAGL80.5, as evidenced by both in  vivo and in  vitro experiments (Fig. 5, C to E). Furthermore, dynamic transcriptome analysis revealed significant differences in the expression levels of PtoTAR2.1 in accessions from different latitudes before and after BB (Fig. 5F). This was consistent with the observation that up-regulated expression of PtoTAR2.1 correlated with increased IAA levels in transgenic plants (Fig. 5I). 

We found that significant changes in the expression levels of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 are driven by the transcription factor PtoSOC1. As a multifaceted protein integrating vernalization, photoperiodic, hormone, and flowering signals, PtoSOC1 plays a crucial role in activating meristem activity in Arabidopsis and contributes to bud release in woody species (19, 43, 56). DLRA, Y1H assays, and transgenic poplar experiments confirmed that PtoSOC1 activates PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 expression, thereby promoting early BB (fig. S10, D to F). Previous studies have reported that BB timing in poplar involves the EBB1-SVL regulatory mechanism (17, 22, 57). This process appears to be most influential during the transition from dormancy to growth, as evidenced by the pronounced changes in the expression profiles of these genes during this critical period (Fig. 1C and fig. S10G). PtoSVL was down-regulated, whereas other genes were up-regulated during dormancy release and BB. This suggests that PtoSOC1 up-regulation during dormancy release may, at least in part, result from the alleviation of suppression due to declining PtoSVL levels. The mutual regulation among genes within the MADS-box family, which influences tree phenology, is a recurring theme (18, 58). Our in vitro experiments confirmed the interaction between PtoSVL and the PtoSOC1 promoter (fig. S10, D to F). Furthermore, coexpression analysis demonstrated the joint involvement of these genes in meristem reinitiation in Populus (table S5). However, additional genetic evidence is required to determine whether PtoEBB1 and PtoSVL are involved in the regulatory network modulating spring BB of $P _ { \ast }$ tomentosa. 

Our study proposes a PtoSOC1-PtoFPA.3-PtoAGL80.5-PtoTAR2 cascade regulatory network in poplar that modulates the spring BB response to local adaptation (Fig. 7). According to this model, PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 are primarily activated by PtoSOC1. Functional assays confirmed that PtoFPA.3 binds to PtoAGL80.5HAP1 mRNA more strongly than $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ , whereas PtoFPA.3HAP1 and PtoFPA.3HAP 2 exhibit comparable binding to a given transcript (Fig.  3, G to I). Furthermore, $\mathrm { P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { H A P 1 } }$ exhibits stronger binding to and greater activation of the PtoTAR2.1 promoter than PtoAGL80.5HAP2, $\mathrm { P t o A G L 8 0 . } 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A } \mathrm { \check { P } } 2 }$ supporting haplotype-dependent regulation of PtoTAR2.1 (Fig. 5, E to K). Consistent with this pathway, altering PtoTAR2.1 dosage changed IAA accumulation, ultimately leading to early BB (Fig. 5I and fig. S10, A and C). On the basis of BB observation in the $P _ { \ast }$ tomentosa natural population, accessions harboring PtoAGL80.5HAP1 $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ and PtoFPA.3HAP1 $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A } \hat { \mathrm { P } } 1 }$ predominantly originate from low latitudes and exhibit earlier BB. In contrast, those carrying $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P } 2 }$ are primarily distributed in higher latitudes, displaying delayed BB. Photoperiod and temperature likely act upstream, but their impacts on PtoAGL80.5 and IAA were not directly quantified; thus, we present a field-informed 

conceptual model of BB regulation under ecological settings, providing an informative framework for hormone-mediated BB regulation via the IAA pathway in poplar. 

# Allelic variation in PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 influences spring BB timing in P. tomentosa

In this study, multiple genomic approaches confirmed that natural selection has left strong adaptive imprints on PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 (Fig. 2, D and E, and fig. S6, A to D) (59), suggesting their long-term adaptive potential throughout the life history of $P _ { \ast }$ tomentosa. We identified two haplotype blocks within the exons of PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 that are significantly associated with spring BB and photoperiod (Figs. 1, B and D, and 2C and fig. S2). These functional variants shape gene function in the context of local adaptation (34, 35, 60). In contrast, no allelic effects associated with BB timing or environmental factors were detected in the other genes within the regulatory network. While these findings support the notion that phenological adaptability is governed by multiple genes, a few loci may play pivotal roles in the adaptability of widely distributed populations (61). 

Local adaptation plays a crucial role in shaping phenotypic variability in forest tree phenology, particularly in species with broad geographic distributions that span diverse environmental conditions (62). Phenological traits vary across the annual growth cycle and among populations, often reflecting local adaptation driven by intraspecific genetic variation. In this study, some genes were primarily involved in regulating BB timing (table S1), whereas others played key roles in responding to environmental factors (e.g., PtoMYB17 and PtoSPL8 for temperature; fig. S5). Thus, during adaptation to environmental changes, different genes assume distinct functional roles to facilitate survival, reproduction, and adaptation under specific environmental conditions (63). These varied roles exemplify biodiversity, as the functional specialization of different genes enables organisms to thrive across diverse environmental conditions. 

Nevertheless, PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 exhibit synergistic responses to phenology and environmental factors within the natural population of $P .$ tomentosa, underscoring their role in adaptation. On the basis of these genetic variants, the natural population is categorized into two primary haplotype groups, representing alleles associated with early (PtoAGL80.5HAP1 $( P t o \dot { A } G L \dot { 8 } 0 . \dot { 5 } ^ { \mathrm { R A P \tilde { 1 } } }$ and $P t o \ ' { F P A . 3 } ^ { \mathrm { H A P } \mathrm { 1 } }$ ) and late $( P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P } 2 }$ ) BB timing (Fig.  1E). Accessions from low-latitude regions are situated at the intersection of subtropical monsoon and temperate monsoon climates, leading to spring BB occurring ${ \sim } 2 0$ days earlier than in high-latitude accessions, which experience temperate monsoon or temperate continental climates. Accordingly, the allele frequencies of candidate haplotypes show distinct variations across different latitudinal regions (Fig. 2G and fig. S6, E to G). Local adaptation has evolved as a climatic response by modulating the frequency of alleles associated with phenology, leading to adaptive differentiation (30). Therefore, the earlier BB conferred by $P t o \dot { A } G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 } }$ suggests that these preferential loci have undergone adaptive selection driven by long-term divergent evolution and natural selection (36).

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant materials, phenotypic data, and climatic parameters

An extensive collection of wild-collected P. tomentosa genotypes was used for field tests, association studies, and phenological trait validation. A total of 300 accessions was obtained from individuals across 83 locations spanning the distribution range along the Yellow 

River and subsequently planted in Guanxian County, Shandong Province, China $( 3 6 . 3 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } ,$ , $1 1 5 . 7 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ), at an elevation of $4 4 ~ \mathrm { m }$ above sea level. Phenological trait data were collected with at least three replicated measurements per sample between 2019 and 2020 at the Guanxian County field site. Spring BB was identified as the emergence of leaf tips from the terminal bud of individual trees during early spring. BB timing was measured daily and recorded using JDs. We obtained data from WorldClim (www.worldclim.org/) and the China Meteorological Data Service Centre (http://data.cma.cn), which represent the current (1971 to 2010) environmental conditions, initially covering 12 environmental factors, including latitude, longitude, elevation, and climatic variables (table S2). To classify the 300 accessions into latitude groups, we first standardized site-level climate variables and performed hierarchical clustering on the two variables most associated with population structure-annual mean temperature (TM_AN) and annual mean precipitation (PR_AN) (table S2). Clustering used Euclidean distance and Ward.D2 linkage [R package stats (75)]; the number of clusters $\left( K = 3 \right)$ was chosen by the gap statistic and average silhouette width (silhouette $\approx 0 . 5$ ) and was robust when adding other temperature/precipitation summaries or when using $K$ -means. The three clusters aligned along the north-south gradient; for reporting clarity, we labeled them L/M/H and mapped them to operational latitude bands that coincide with the empirical cluster boundaries: $\mathrm { L } < 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , $\mathrm { M } = 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ to ${ } ^ { 3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } , }$ , and $\mathrm { H } > 3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ . These latitude bands are consistent with previous geographic/genetic stratifications of $P _ { \ast }$ tomentosa populations (76, 77), and accession-level assignments are listed in table S8. 

# Genome resequencing and SNP calling

We obtained SNP data from $3 0 0 ~ P .$ tomentosa accessions collected across the distribution range along the Yellow River. The accessions were resequenced using the Illumina GA II platform to generate raw reads [100–base pair (bp) or 150-bp paired-end reads]. After excluding reads with $\ge 5 0 \%$ low-quality nucleotides $\left( Q < 2 0 \right)$ ), the remaining reads were aligned to the P. tomentosa reference genome (33). SNP calling and alignment were performed using the SAMtools (v1.14) (78) and Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK) (79). We filtered the original set of SNPs, retaining those with a minor allele frequency $\ge 0 . 0 5$ and a missing genotype rate $\geq 0 . 2 0$ . Furthermore, SNPs were removed if more than three SNPs occurred within a 10-bp window or if they were located within 5 bp of Indels, using GATK and BCFtools (78, 79). 

# Phenotypic GWAS analysis

The mean and the BLUP of BB timing were used to perform phenotypic GWAS. The BLUP was calculated using the R package lme4 (v1.1-31) (80). Phenotypic GWAS was performed using a mixed linear model implemented in EMMAX (81). A total of 1,251,486 SNPs was kept for GWAS. The top three principal components were used to build the Q matrix for population structure correction with AD-MIXTURE (v1.3.0), with $K$ values ranging from 2 to 8 (minimum cross-validation error value obtained at $K = 3$ ) (82). The genetic kinship matrix was estimated using EMMAX to construct the $K$ matrix. The genome-wide significance threshold was set at $P < 8 . 2 6 \times 1 0 ^ { - 7 }$ ( $^ { 1 } / _ { n } ,$ where $n$ is the effective number of independent SNPs). The R package qqman was used to generate Manhattan plots and quantilequantile plots (83). Each gene located within a 5.0-kb window adjacent to a significant SNP was defined as a candidate gene. 

# Geographic and environmental association analysis

Environmental GWAS analysis was conducted using two approaches: the LFMM and RDA using the same SNP set as in the section “Phenotypic GWAS analysis.” This analysis included 12 bioclimatic variables at the natural provenances of 300 accessions over four decades (1971 to 2010) (table S2). LFMM, implemented in the R package LEA (v2.8.0) (84), was used to identify associations between genetic markers and 12 environmental variables, including geographic factors (latitude, longitude, and altitude), as well as annual, growth season, and dormancy period trends (temperature, precipitation, and photoperiod). Environmental GWAS analysis was performed using the lfmm function, accounting for population structure. The Gibbs sampler algorithm was executed five times for 10,000 cycles, following a burn-in of 5000 cycles. P values from all five runs were averaged for each variant and adjusted for multiple testing using a false discovery rate threshold of 0.05. After accounting for correlations among the 12 environmental variables, seven (latitude, longitude, altitude, annual mean temperature, annual mean precipitation, photoperiod of annual, and photoperiod of dormancy; $\left| r \right| < 0 . 8 )$ were retained for further RDA using the R package vegan (85). Significant environmental associations were identified as those with loadings in the extreme tails of the distribution, using a three-SD cutoff along one or more RDA axes. 

# Population-specific selective sweeps

The methods for detecting population-specific selective sweeps are described in methods S1. 

# RNA extraction, sequencing, and analysis

To investigate the expression patterns across different tissues, RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis was performed on various tissue types, including bark, phloem, cambium, developing xylem, mature xylem, developing leaves, mature leaves, apex, and roots of P. tomentosa LM50 clones planted in Guan Xian County. Furthermore, leaf buds were collected across the dormant-to-budding transition for transcriptome sequencing. These buds were collected from 12 genotypes, including six from the highest and six from the lowest latitude regions. Bud samples for transcriptome analysis were collected at noon every 14 days from a natural population of P. tomentosa (at least six biological replicates per genotype). To identify DEGs in PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 transgenic lines, buds from PtoAGL80.5-OE and PtoFPA.3-OE transgenic plants grown under natural conditions, along with those from WT, were subjected to RNA-seq. Samples were immediately immersed in RNAlater solution (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before RNA extraction. RNA extraction, RNA-seq, and data analysis are described in methods S2. 

# Construction of poplar transformants

To construct the OE vectors for PtoAGL80.5, PtoFPA.3, PtoTAR2.1, and PtoSOC1, full-length coding sequences were amplified from P. tomentosa LM50 (M group; carrying PtoAGL80.5HAP1 and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ ), cloned into the pBI121 vector containing the 35S promoter, and introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101. To construct the RNAi vector of PtoAGL80.5, PtoFPA.3, PtoTAR2.1, and PtoSOC1, 216-bp (PtoAGL80.5), 218-bp (PtoFPA.3), 175-bp (PtoTAR2.1) and 168-bp (PtoSOC1) fragments were cloned into the pBI121 vector and introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101. Both OE and RNAi vectors were 

transformed into poplar $\mathit { \Omega } ^ { \mathrm { { c c } } } 8 4 \mathrm { { K } } ^ { \mathrm { { ? } } }$ (P. alba × P. glandulosa), designated as WT. 84K was chosen for its well-established transformation system and high regeneration efficiency, facilitating functional validation of candidate genes. In addition, its close phylogenetic relationship with P. tomentosa ensures the conservation of target genes, enhancing the relevance of functional analyses. Vegetative bud development was photographed every 6 days. Primers used for gene cloning are listed in table S6. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

cDNAs of PtoSVP, PtoSOC1, and PtoAGL80.5HAP1/2 were cloned into the effector plasmid pGreenII 62-SK. Furthermore, the promoters of PtoSOC1, PtoAGL80.5, PtoFPA.3, and PtoTAR2.1 were cloned into the reporter plasmid pGreenII 0800-LUC. Negative controls were constructed without promoter sequences. Detailed procedures are described in methods S3. The primers and promoters used in these experiments are listed in table S6. 

# Y1H assay

Segments of PtoSOC1, PtoAGL80.5, PtoFPA.3, and PtoTAR2.1 promoters were cloned into the pBait-AbAi vector using the EcoRI and XhoI restriction sites to analyze interactions between PtoSVP, PtoSCO1, and PtoAGL80.5 and their corresponding promoters. Recombinant pGADT7-PtoSVP, pGADT7-PtoSOC1, and pGADT7-PtoAGL80.5 constructs were generated by amplifying and cloning the coding sequence into the pGADT7 vector. Y1H assays were performed using the Matchmaker Gold Y1H Library Screening System (Clontech Laboratories, Mountain View, CA, USA) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. All Y1H assays were repeated three times, with representative results are shown. Primers used for these assays are listed in table S6. 

# DAP-seq sampling and data analysis

DAP-seq of PtoAGL80.5 was performed following previously established protocols (86). Detailed methods for DAP-seq and data analysis are provided in methods S4. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

Full-length coding sequences of PtoAGL80.5HAP1/2 and PtoFPA.3HAP1/2 were amplified and cloned into the Pet-32a vector to express recombinant proteins. The recombinant proteins were purified using a HIS-tag Protein Purifying Kit (P226, Beyotime, Shanghai, China). Oligonucleotide probes were synthesized and labeled with the biotin (Sangon, Shanghai, China). EMSA was performed using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (GS009, Beyotime) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Probe sequences are listed in table S6. 

# Hormone quantification

For each sample, ${ \sim } 1 . 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ of young newly emerged leaves was immediately immersed in liquid nitrogen for rapid freezing and ground into fine powder. IAA, ABA, and GA were extracted using commercial kits (Wuhan Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd., Hubei, China). Hormone levels were quantified using an ultra-performance liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-MS/MS) system (UPLC, Shim-pack UFLC SHIMADZU CBM30A, Kyoto, Japan; MS, Applied Biosystems, Forster City, CA, USA). This process used an external standard calibration method for accurately hormone quantification, with results expressed as nanograms per gram of 

fresh sample weight for IAA, ABA and GA. Three biological replicates were used to ensure result accuracy. 

# Auxin and auxin inhibitor treatment

For hormone treatments, 20 and $4 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ 3-IAA (Cas 87-51-4, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) and l-Kyn (IAA biosynthesis inhibitor; Cas 2922-83-0, Sigma-Aldrich) were applied to the buds of five lines, including poplar 84K (designed as WT) and transgenic PtoAGL80.5 and PtoFPA.3 lines (-OE and -RNAi). Plants were treated every 2 days for 30 days, with water serving as the control treatment. To assess BB timing following hormone treatments, vegetative bud development was documented via sequential photography. For each treatment, $n = 3$ biological replicates (three independent plants) were analyzed. Data evaluated by two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey’s post hoc test (assumptions checked by Shapiro-Wilk and Levene’s tests). All chemicals were stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# Protein immunoimprinting analysis

PtoAGL80.5 protein extracts from WT and all transgenic leaves were separated by SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and immunoblotted with an anti-PtoAGL80.5 antibody. Detection was performed using enhanced chemiluminescence, and relative protein levels were quantified using ImageJ software, with actin as a loading control. Furthermore, PtoFPA.3HAP1/2 and $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 1 / 2 } }$ cDNAs were cloned into $\boldsymbol { p }$ GreenII 62-SK and pBI121-GFP vectors, respectively. Four plasmid constructs (PtoAGL80.5HAP1, $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A \tilde { P } 2 } }$ , $P t o A \dot { G } L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A } \tilde { \mathrm { P } } 1 }$ and PtoFPA.3HAP1, and PtoAGL80.5HAP2 $P t o A G L 8 0 . 5 ^ { \mathrm { H A P 2 } }$ and $P t o F P A . 3 ^ { \mathrm { H A P } 2 } )$ ) were introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101. In parallel, protoplasts were isolated from poplar leaves, and plasmid DNA was transfected using polyethylene glycol–mediated transformation. Total proteins were extracted from poplar protoplasts, and the reactions were stopped by adding $5 \times$ SDS sample loading buffer. Proteins were separated on a $1 0 \%$ (v/v) SDS-PAGE gel. After incubation, an immunoblotting assay for PtoAGL80.5-GFP was conducted using an anti–green fluorescent protein (GFP) antibody (Abmart). 

# Actinomycin D treatment

Two-month-old PtoFPA.3-OE leaves were harvested and incubated in the appropriate liquid medium containing $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ actinomycin D (AbMole). Total RNA was extracted for quantitative reverse transcription (RT)–PCR to estimate the expression level of PtoAGL80.5. ACTIN was used as an internal control. The relative expression of each gene to ACTIN in actinomycin D–treated samples at different time points was normalized against that in mock-treated samples to measure relative RNA levels. All primers used are shown in table S6. 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation–quantitative polymerase chain reaction

Leaf samples were cross-linked with $1 \%$ (w/v) formaldehyde, quenched with glycine, and lysed to extract chromatin. The chromatin was sonicated using a Bioruptor (Diagenode, Liege, Belgium) and immunoprecipitated with anti-PtoAGL80.5 antibody for ChIP– quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR). After reversing crosslinks, DNA was purified and analyzed by qPCR using PtoTAR2.1 promoter–specific primers. Input chromatin served as a reference, and immunoglobulin G (IgG) immunoprecipitation was used as a negative control. 

# Phylogenetic tree construction and divergence time estimation

We retrieved reference genomes of diverse species, including A. thaliana, A. lyrate, Cassia fistula, Solanum lycopersicum, Physcomitrium patens, O. sativa, P. deltoides, P. tremula, P. euphratica, P. trichocarpa, and P. simonii from public databases (table  S7). Detailed methods of analysis are provided in methods S5. 

# Genomic vulnerability assessment and allele frequency shifts for P. tomentosa under future climate conditions

To assess the adaptive capacity of the juvenile P.tomentosa population under future climate conditions, we obtained projected environment data for 2081 to 2100 related to precipitation and temperatures from two climate models (ACCESS-CM2 and CMCC-ESM2; resolution of 2.5 arc min) via WorldClim (www.worldclim. org/). These data were averaged across the annual, growth season, and dormancy period. Each of the future environmental variables was modeled under two SSPs: SSP126 and SSP370. SSP126 represents a scenario with moderate greenhouse gas emissions, assuming strong climate policies and efforts to limit global temperature rise, while SSP370 assumes higher emissions driven by continued reliance on fossil fuels and limited mitigation actions. These pathways, developed within the SSP framework, provide projections under different socioeconomic scenarios and are used to assess the impacts of varying levels of climate action. We calculated RONA using pyRONA (v0.4.4) (87, 88) and modeled the rate of past evolutionary changes to predict future genomic shifts using the R package Allele-Shift (v1.1) (89). Detailed analysis is presented in methods S6. 

# Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were conducted using t tests unless otherwise specified. For comparisons involving multiple genotypes (e.g., WT, PtoAGL80.5-OE/RNAi, and PtoFPA.3-OE/RNAi), one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey’s test was performed to assess pairwise differences. All data are presented as mean $\pm$ standard error unless otherwise noted. A significance threshold of $^ { * } P < 0 . 0 5$ and $^ { * * } P < 0 . 0 1$ was used throughout. All plots were generated using the R packages ggplot2, ggpubr or rstatix.
