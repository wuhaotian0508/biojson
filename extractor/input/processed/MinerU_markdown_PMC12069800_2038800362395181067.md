# ABSTRACT

Genomic islands (GIs) are 20–500 kb DNA regions that are thought to be acquired by horizontal gene transfer. GIs that confer pathogenicity and environmental adaptation have been reported in Pseudomonas species; however, GIs that enhance bacterial virulence have not. Here, we identified $1 1 0 \mathrm { k b }$ and $1 0 3 \mathrm { k b }$ GIs in P. syringae pv. tabaci 6605 (Pta6605), the causative agent of tobacco wildfire disease, which has the ability to produce tabtoxin as a phytotoxin. These GIs are partially homologous to known genomic islands in Pseudomonas aeruginosa and P. syringae pv. phaseolicola and were designated PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2. Both PtaGIs conserve core genes, whereas each GI possesses different accessory genes. PtaGI-1 contains a tabtoxin biosynthetic gene cluster and three type III effector genes among its accessory genes, whereas PtaGI-2 also contains homologous genes to hsvABC, pathogenicity-related genes in Erwinia amylovora. Inoculation revealed that the PtaGI-1 mutant, but not the PtaGI-2 mutant, lost the ability to biosynthesise tabtoxin and to cause disease. Therefore, PtaGI-1 is thought to be a pathogenicity island. Both PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 have a pseudogene of tRNALys on the left border and an intact tRNALys gene on the right border. In a colony of Pta6605, both GIs can be excised at tRNALys, and PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 exist in a circular form. These results indicate that tabtoxin biosynthesis genes in PtaGI-1 are required for disease development, and PtaGI-1 is necessary for Pta6605 virulence. 

# 1 | Introduction

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) is the phenomenon of transferring genetic material from a non-parent organism. HGT is often involved in the acquisition of antibiotic resistance, virulence and environmental adaptability, and it contributes to the evolution of organisms (Gogarten and Townsend 2005). The mechanisms of prokaryotic HGT include conjugation, transformation and transduction. In conjugative gene transfer, integrative and conjugative elements (ICEs) are introduced from the donor to 

the recipient through a conjugative pilus. In transformation, competent cells acquire foreign DNA from the environment. In transduction, DNA is introduced by a bacteriophage. A part of genomic islands (GIs) has long been reported as genetic material introduced by conjugation. 

GIs are $2 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { k b }$ DNA regions that are thought to be acquired by HGT (Johnson and Grossman 2015). GIs are flanked at both ends by direct repeats of tRNAs, and their GC content is different from that of the core genome. Genes in GIs are classified 

as core genes or accessory genes according to their functions. Core genes encode proteins that are necessary to transfer and maintain the GI and are well conserved in a wide range of bacterial species. Accessory genes are genes other than the core genes and determine the function of each GI. When a GI contains pathogenicity-related genes as accessory genes, the GI is called a pathogenicity island (PI, Hacker and Carniel 2001). 

Pathogenicity islands have been reported in Salmonella spp. and other organisms, and the mechanisms of HGT functions of each GI have been reported. For example, the Salmonella pathogenicity island 7 (SPI7) identified in Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi contributes to its virulence (Pickard et  al.  2003). Homologues of SPI7 are widely conserved in animal- and plant-pathogenic and environmental bacteria of the $\beta$ - and $\gamma$ -proteobacteria, such as Haemophilus (Mohd-Zain et al. 2004), Yersinia (Collyn et al. 2004) and Xanthomonas (Li et al. 2022). In Pseudomonas, PAPI-1 of P. aeruginosa PA14, the clc element in Pseudomonas sp. strain B13 and PFGI-1 in P. protegens Pf5 are well understood (He et al. 2004; Gaillard et al. 2006; Takeuchi et al. 2014). In phytopathogens, PPHGI-1 of P. syringae pv. phaseolicola 1302A (Pph1302A) and $P s a _ { \mathrm { N Z 4 5 } } \mathrm { I C E }$ of P. syringae pv. actinidiae NZ45 (PsaNZ45) have been reported as PIs (Pitman et al. 2005; Colombi et al. 2017). In addition, rich genome information and computational analysis make it possible to identify the existence of a GI or potential PI in bacterial phytopathogens including Pseudomonas, Ralstonia, and four other species (Gonçalves et al. 2020; Colombi et al. 2024; Li et al. 2022; de Assis et al. 2022). Thus, reports of GIs/PIs of phytopathogens are increasing gradually. However, computer analysis of plant-pathogenic bacterial genomes has detected only GI-like sequences, but no functional analysis of GIs/PIs in the interactions between phytopathogens and host plants has been reported except for PPHGI-1 of $P p h 1 3 0 2 \mathrm { A }$ and $P s a _ { \mathrm { N Z 4 5 } } \mathrm { I C E }$ of PsaNZ45. Furthermore, an avr gene has been reported in PPHGI-1, and a copper resistance gene has been reported in $P s a _ { \mathrm { { N Z 4 5 } } } \mathrm { { I C E } }$ . However, to date, there have been no reports that PIs in $P .$ . syringae actually contribute to virulence. 

We have previously characterised the virulence factors of P. syringae pv. tabaci 6605 (Pta6605), a causal agent of wildfire disease in tobacco. We revealed that flagellar motility and chemotaxis are required for invasion (Ichinose et  al.  2003; Watanabe et  al.  2023), while a type III secretion system (Marutani et  al.  2005), a phytotoxin known as a tabtoxin (Taguchi et  al.  2010), and various other virulence factors are also required to evade plant immunity and induce disease symptoms. Tabtoxin is a phytotoxin produced by Pta6605 that causes chlorosis on host plants (Turner and Taha 1984). Tabtoxin is a $\beta$ -lactam-containing dipeptide and is a nonspecific toxin that is toxic to plants and bacteria by inhibiting glutamine synthase (Arrebola et al. 2011). We recently determined the complete genomic sequence of Pta6605 and revealed that the Pta6605 genome is composed of a single circular DNA (Matsui et al. 2021). In this study, we found two GIs in the genome of Pta6605, both of which are partially homologous to SPI7, PAPI-1 and PPHG-I. One of the GIs includes a cluster of tabtoxin biosynthetic genes. In this report, we investigated whether these GI-like sequences in Pta6605 are indeed mobile and if their deletion results in the loss of virulence. Here, we report the structure, dynamics and function of two GIs and potential distribution of tabtoxin 

biosynthetic genes by the HGT. This is the first report to identify genes in a GI (PI) directly involved in virulence and to analyse their functions. The structural characteristics of GIs and their contribution to virulence were investigated.

# 2.1 | Pta6605 Genome Has Two GIs That Contain the Highly Similar Core Genes of SPI7, PPHGI-1 and PAPI-1

Comprehensive analysis of the Pta6605 genome revealed that it has two genetic regions that show similarity to PAPI-1 in P. aeruginosa PA14, PPHGI-1 in Pph1302A, $P s a _ { \mathrm { { N Z 4 5 } } } \mathrm { { I C E \_ C u } }$ in PsaNZ45, ICEclc in Pseudomonas sp. B13, and SPI7 in S. enterica serovar Typhi (Figure  1). We designated them as PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2. Both PtaGIs have a pseudogene of tRNALys on the left border of GIs and an intact tRNALys gene on the right border. Two GIs in Pta6605 are located at different positions on the chromosome. PtaGI-1 is adjacent to tRNALys (Pta6605_RS20895, hereafter we omit Pta6605 in the gene locus), and PtaGI-2 is adjacent to another tRNALys (RS24860). PAPI-1, $P s a _ { \mathrm { { N Z 4 5 } } } \mathrm { { I C E \_ C u } }$ and PPHGI-1 also have tRNALys as an att site, but SPI7 and ICEclc have tRNAPhe and tRNAGly, respectively, instead of tRNALys. Both PtaGIs have more than 100 genes, some of which are core genes that encode proteins required for GI transfer. The genes on GIs can be classified into conserved core genes or unique accessory genes (Mohd-Zain et al. 2004). Multiple comparison analysis of the two PtaGIs, PPHGI-1, PAPI-1, $P s a _ { \mathrm { { N Z 4 5 } } } \mathrm { { I C E \_ C u } }$ , ICEclc and SPI7 revealed that each GI has a member of the core genes or accessory genes. As shown in Figure 1, we identified core genes that are conserved in at least five of the seven GIs and other genes as accessory genes. 

There are three types of core gene groups: a plasmid-related gene group (dark green in Figure 1), a pil gene group (light green in Figure 1) and an integrated conjugative element (ICE)-related gene group (yellow in Figure 1). The plasmid-related gene group includes the genes that function in plasmid replication (Mohd-Zain et al. 2004) and the genes that encode enzymes for DNA, such as helicase, topoisomerase and single-strand DNA-binding protein. The pil gene group codes for type IV pilus assembly proteins. An ICE gene group is located on the right side of GI and is separated into four regions by the insertion of accessory gene clusters (Figure 1).

# 2.4 | PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 Can Excise From the Chromosome to Become Circular Molecules

We found that PtaGIs were excised in the WT strain during the construction of ∆PtaGIs. To examine the dynamism of PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2, we carried out PCR analysis using a bacterial suspension derived from a colony as a template, following the method reported by Qiu et al. (2006) as shown in Figure 6. PCR products in lanes 3 and 4 show the left and right border sequences of PtaGI-1, respectively. Similarly, PCR products in lanes 7 and 8 show the left and right border sequences of PtaGI-2, respectively. The appearance of these PCR products means that PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 remain on the chromosome (Figure 6A,B). 

On the other hand, PCR products in lanes 2 and 6 show the presence of circular PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2, respectively; PCR products in lanes 1 and 5 show the excision of PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2, respectively (Figure 6). As a result of PCR, all expected sizes of DNA were amplified, suggesting that PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 can be excised from the chromosome, allowing it to circularise (Figure 6). The DNA sequence of PCR-amplified DNA was directly determined and confirmed excision and circularisation (Figure S1). Simultaneously, there were also chromosomes in which PtaGI-1 or PtaGI-2 was not excised. We investigated whether PtaGI-1 was integrated into tRNALys of RS24860 and PtaGI-2 was similarly integrated into tRNALys of RS20890, but no integration was confirmed in either case, suggesting the integration sites of PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 were site-specific (Figure S2). 

# 2.5 | Loss of PtaGI-1, but Not PtaGI-2, Results in Loss of Tabtoxin Production and Reduction in Virulence on Plants

The virulence of WT, ∆PtaGI-1_LR, ∆PtaGI-1_t and ∆PtaGI-2_t was investigated by the flood inoculation method. Inoculation of Pta6605 WT and ∆PtaGI-2_t resulted in severe necrosis of tobacco seedlings at 5 days post-inoculation (dpi), but inoculation of ∆PtaGI-1_LR and ∆PtaGI-1_t had little effect on the tobacco growth, and the tobacco plants appeared healthy (Figure  7A). Bacterial growth of ΔPtaGI-1_LR and ∆PtaGI-1_t in plants was significantly lower than that of WT at 3 dpi (Figure  7B). Tobacco leaves were also inoculated by infiltration of these bacteria. At a low inoculum concentration $( 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } \mathrm { C F U / m L } )$ ), WT- and ∆PtaGI-2_t-inoculated areas developed yellowed and necrotic zones at 11 dpi; however, little change was observed in ∆PtaGI-1_LR- and ∆PtaGI-1_t-inoculated areas (Figure  7C). 

Similarly, at a high inoculum concentration $( 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 } \mathrm { C F U / m L } )$ ), WT and ∆PtaGI-2_t caused water-soaking symptoms, but that of ∆PtaGI-1_LR and ∆PtaGI-1_t did not at 3 dpi (Figure 7D). 

Because PtaGI-1 possesses a tabtoxin biosynthetic gene cluster, ∆PtaGI-1 seemed to lose the tabtoxin biosynthetic ability. Therefore, tabtoxin production can be investigated by a bioassay using Escherichia coli as an indicator of tabtoxin (Figure 8). Growth inhibition zones due to tabtoxin were observed around WT and ΔPtaGI-2_t of Pta6605, as shown by arrows, but not around ΔPtaGI-1_LR and ΔPtaGI-1_t (Figure 8 and Figure S3). If this growth inhibition is caused by tabtoxin, bacteria are able to grow by exogenous application of glutamine. In this analysis, the inhibition zone was eliminated in the MG plate containing glutamine, indicating the inhibition zones were the result of inhibition of glutamine synthesis by tabtoxin. 

# 3 | Discussion

# 3.1 | PtaGI-1 Is a Pathogenicity Island Important for Infection

In this study, we identified two GIs, PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2. GIs containing pathogenicity-related genes have been reported in P. syringae pv. phaseolicola, PPHGI-1 (Pitman et al. 2005), and P. aeruginosa PA14, PAPI-1 (He et al. 2004). The core genes of these PIs are highly similar to those of PtaGI-1 and PtaGI-2 and are presumably derived from a common ancestor. However, their accessory genes are completely different. PAPI-1 has 10 potential virulence-related genes (He et al. 2004). Among them, the products of five genes (rscB, rscC, pvrR, pvrS and cupD) are related to biofilm formation (Mikkelsen et al. 2009), while PPHGI-1 in P. syringae pv. phaseolicola 1302A has avrPphB, which is a T3E gene that causes a hypersensitive response in the bean cultivar Tendergreen (Pitman et  al.  2005). PtaGI-1 contains three T3E genes and tabtoxin biosynthesis-related genes, and PtaGI-2 also contains the putative virulence gene, hsvABC, suggesting that both GIs can be PIs. 

To investigate the function of PtaGI-1 in pathogenicity, we constructed two types of ∆PtaGI-1 mutant. One was ∆PtaGI-1_LR in which PtaGI-1 was deleted by homologous recombination using regions 1 kb upstream and downstream, and the other was ∆PtaGI-1_t, in which PtaGI-1 was deleted by natural excision at a direct repeat of tRNALys. The inoculation test result with ∆PtaGI-1_LR and ∆PtaGI-1_t showed that both ∆PtaGI-1 strains lost the ability to cause severe symptoms of wildfire disease. The ∆PtaGI-1 also lost the ability to biosynthesise tabtoxin; therefore, loss of this ability is probably responsible for the defect in the ability to cause 

disease symptoms. This result is consistent with the results of inoculation with Tox− Pta11528 (Turner and Taha 1984). The Tox− mutant of Pta11528 grew similarly to the WT in tobacco 

leaves during the first 2 dpi; however, the bacterial population of the mutant decreased from 3 dpi (Turner and Taha 1984). This phenotype of the Tox− mutant of Pta11528 is similar to

# 3.4 | A Tabtoxin Biosynthetic Gene Cluster Is Conserved on GI-Like Elements in P. syringae

It is known that some Pseudomonas phytopathogens biosynthesise tabtoxin and the genes involved in tabtoxin biosynthesis are widely and partially distributed in Pseudomonas species, as evidenced by the molecular phylogenetic tree (Figure  S6, Baltrus et  al.  2011). Because an integrase gene (xerC) is present around the tabtoxin gene cluster in Pseudomonas BR2, the tabtoxin biosynthetic ability is thought to have been acquired by HGT (Kinscherf and Willis  2005). However, the genetic elements transferring tabtoxin biosynthetic genes have not been identified. Here, we report that PtaGIs may be mobile genetic elements transposable by conjugation and that PtaGI-1 has a tabtoxin gene cluster. We hypothesise that a tabtoxin biosynthetic gene cluster was transferred by HGT. BLAST homology searches revealed that the tabtoxin gene cluster is conserved at least in six strains of $P .$ . syringae pv. tabaci, five strains of P. syringae pv. oryzae (Por), one strain of $P .$ . syringae pv. coronafaciens (Pco), 36 strains of P. syringae pv. garcae $( P g a )$ , two strains of P. syringae pv. fabae $( P f a )$ , and P. syringae pv. tremae $( P t r )$ . The species P. syringae is classified into 13 phylogroups on the phylogenetic tree based on the citrate synthase (cts) gene as a housekeeping gene (Berge et al. 2014). On the phylogenetic tree that is a modified Berge's tree, Pta belongs to phylogroup 3 (Figure S6); Por, Pco, Pga and Ptr belong to phylogroup 4; and Pfa belongs to phylogroup 5. In addition, six unclassified strains of nonpathogenic Pseudomonas species and three strains of P. fluorescens have tabtoxin gene clusters (Figure  S6). P. sessilinigenes CMR12a, known as a biocontrol agent (Perneel et  al.  2007), and four strains of Pseudomonas sp. BIOMIG1 belong to the same clade as P. protegens Pf5. Pseudomonas sp. RU47 and S49 belong to the same clade as P. fluorescens Pfl01. Pta, Por, Pco, Pga 47, Ptr JD12 and P. sessilinigenes CMR12a have tabtoxin gene clusters with traI and xerC, which are core genes in GIs; the tRNALys gene exists downstream of xerC (Figure S7). These results suggest it is possible that a tabtoxin-biosynthetic gene cluster was transmitted to Pseudomonas species by HGT. Furthermore, the tabtoxin gene cluster is conserved not only in phytopathogens but also in nonpathogenic bacteria living in the plant rhizosphere and environment. Because tabtoxin is a nonspecific toxin, the ability to produce it may contribute to competition with other organisms in the environment, although nothing is actually known about it yet. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Bacterial Growth and Condition

Bacterial strains and plasmids are listed in Table  S3. Pta6605 strains were grown in King's B (KB) medium supplemented with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ nalidixic acid (Nal) at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Ichinose et  al.  2003). 

E. coli strains were grown in Luria-Bertani (LB) medium supplemented with appropriate antibiotics $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 } )$ at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.2 | Bioinformatic Analysis

Genome sequences were obtained from the National Center of Biotechnology Information database (https://www.ncbi.nlm. nih.gov/) or the Pseudomonas genome database (https://beta. pseudomonas.com). GI region sequences were extracted from whole-genome sequences and analysed with SnapGene (GSL Biotech LLC) or text editor software. Multiple GIs or gene clusters were compared and edited by Clinker (Gilchrist and Chooi 2021). 

# 4.3 | Generation of ∆PtaGI-1_LR Mutant Strain

To generate ∆PtaGI-1_LR in Pta6605, we first amplified 1 kb fragments (GI-1_L and GI-1_R as shown in Figure 2) of the left border and the right border using KOD One PCR Master Mix (TOYOBO) and PCR primers as shown in a Table S4. After amplification, 1 kb PCR products were cut with NheI, then ligated with pK18mobSacBN (Kashihara et  al.  2022). The resultant plasmid, pK-GI-1_LR, was introduced into E. coli S17-1 to conjugate with Pta6605 WT. After conjugation, Km-tolerant colonies that had pK18mobSacBN inserted in GI further spread on sucrose-containing KB plates, then ∆PtaGI-1-LR was selected by PCR using 20330-1 and 20890-1 as primers. Because sacB on pK18mobsacBN is a suicide gene in the presence of sucrose, only the strains that lost GI survived under selective pressure. 

# 4.4 | tRNA-Mediated Generation of ∆PtaGI-1_t and ∆PtaGI-2_t Mutant Strains

A PtaGI-1-specific DNA fragment (RS20695) and a PtaGI-2- specific DNA fragment (RS24785) were amplified by PCR using GoTaq Green Master Mix (Promega) with the primers shown in Table S4 and cloned in pGEM-T Easy. Inserted DNA fragments of RS20695 and RS24785 were excised with EcoRI and NotI, respectively, and introduced into a pK18mobsacBN plasmid digested by the same restriction enzymes. The resulting plasmids were introduced into E. coli S17-1 to conjugate with Pta6605 WT. After conjugation, Km-tolerant colonies were further selected on a sucrose-containing KB plate. Deletion mutants obtained by the integration of pK18mobSacBN into RS20695 and RS24785 were designated ∆PtaGI-1_t and ∆PtaGI-2_t, respectively. 

# 4.5 | Plant Materials and Inoculation Procedure

The virulence of Pta6605 WT and mutant strains was analysed using flood inoculation and infiltration methods as described (Tumewu et  al.  2020). Briefly, for flood inoculation, ethanolsterilised tobacco seeds (Nicotiana tabacum ‘Xanthi NC’) were sown on Murashige and Skoog (MS) plates supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose and vitamin solution (thiamine hydrochloride $3 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } }$ , nicotinic acid $5 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } }$ , pyridoxine hydrochloride $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } } )$ . After 2 weeks' incubation, seedlings were transplanted into $0 . 1 \%$ sucrose MS plates and grown for a further 

3 days. The inoculum was prepared in 10 mM $\mathrm { M g S O _ { 4 } }$ at an $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ of 0.004 $( 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { C F U m L ^ { - 1 } } )$ supplemented with $0 . 0 2 5 \%$ (vol/vol) Silwet L77 (OSI Specialties) to promote cell attachment. Tobacco seedlings were flooded by the inoculum for approximately 10 s. The inoculum was decanted, and the plate was left to dry on a clean bench for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . After 3 days of incubation at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (with a long photoperiod), two leaf disks (4 mm diameter) were collected and ground in 1 mL of sterile water. After serial dilutions, the suspension was spotted on KB agar plates with Nal. Recovered bacterial colonies were counted after a 2- day incubation at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and CFUs are obtained. Photographs of seedlings were taken at 3 and 5 dpi. Infiltration inoculation was done with 5- to 6-week-old tobacco leaves using a needleless syringe. Bacteria were infiltrated at low $( 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } \mathrm { C F U m L ^ { - 1 } } )$ or high $( 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 7 } \mathrm { C F U m L ^ { - 1 } } )$ ) concentrations. Each inoculated plant was incubated at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (long day photoperiod) and assessed at 11 dpi and 3 dpi, respectively. 

# 4.6 | Detection of Tabtoxin

Production of tabtoxin was detected by an agar plate diffusion test using E. coli DH5α as an indicator strain (Taguchi et  al.  2010). E. coli DH5α was grown in mineral salts–glucose (MG) medium (44 mM ${ \mathrm { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { H P O } } _ { 4 } ;$ , $2 2 \mathrm { m M }$ ${ \mathrm { K H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { P O } } _ { 4 }$ , $1 7 . 8 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { C l }$ , 11.3 mM NaCl, 1 mM $\mathrm { M g S O _ { 4 } , }$ , $0 . 1 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 2 \%$ glucose). An overnight culture of $E$ . coli was centrifuged, and cells were resuspended in $0 . 9 \%$ NaCl at an $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ of 0.6. One millilitre of $E$ . coli suspension was mixed with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ of MG medium with $0 . 7 \%$ agar and with/without glutamine, and overlaid onto the $1 . 5 \%$ agar MG plate. Because tabtoxin inhibits glutamine synthase, bacterial growth is inhibited by tabtoxin only in the absence of glutamine. Five microlitres of an overnight culture of the Pta6605 WT or each mutant was spotted onto sterile filter paper on the plates, followed by $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.7 | Data Analysis

The significance of differences in bacterial populations between mutant strains and WT was examined using analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Dunnett's test at the $9 5 \%$ confidence level. Analyses of all data were performed using R v. 4.2.3 (R Foundation), RStudio $2 0 2 3 . 0 6 . 0 + 4 2 1$ (posit) software and multcomp package (Hothorn et al. 2008).
