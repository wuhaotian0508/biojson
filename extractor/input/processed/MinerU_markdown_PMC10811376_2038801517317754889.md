# ABSTRACT

Rheum officinale, a member of the Polygonaceae family, is an important medicinal plant that is widely used in traditional Chinese medicine. Here, we report a 7.68-Gb chromosome-scale assembly of R. officinale with a contig N50 of 3.47 Mb, which was clustered into 44 chromosomes across four homologous groups. Comparative genomics analysis revealed that transposable elements have made a significant contribution to its genome evolution, gene copy number variation, and gene regulation and expression, particularly of genes involved in metabolite biosynthesis, stress resistance, and root development. We placed the recent autotetraploidization of R. officinale at $\sim 0 . 5 8$ mya and analyzed the genomic features of its homologous chromosomes. Although no dominant monoploid genomes were observed at the overall expression level, numerous allele-differentially-expressed genes were identified, mainly with different transposable element insertions in their regulatory regions, suggesting that they functionally diverged after polyploidization. Combining genomics, transcriptomics, and metabolomics, we explored the contributions of gene family amplification and tetraploidization to the abundant anthraquinone production of R. officinale, as well as gene expression patterns and differences in anthraquinone content among tissues. Our report offers unprecedented genomic resources for fundamental research on the autopolyploid herb R. officinale and guidance for polyploid breeding of herbs.

Recently, several chromosome-level autotetraploid genomes (e.g., alfalfa, sugarcane, and potato) and allopolyploid genomes (e.g., wheat, peanut, and cotton) have been assembled (Appels et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018, 2022a; Wang et al., 2019, 2022a; Zhuang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2022). Modern medical research has shown that R. officinale is rich in anthraquinones, anthrone, and stilbene, indicating that it has antimicrobial, antiviral, anti-inflammatory, and antitumor activities, and the accumulation of these metabolites varies markedly among different developmental stages and tissues (Cao et al., 2017; Yan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019). In this report, we present a chromosome-level, haplotyperesolved genome of the autotetraploid herb R. officinale. Comparative genomics analysis revealed the genomic and gene family evolution of metabolite biosynthesis and stress resistance in R. officinale and the genomic features of homologous chromosomes. We combined transcriptomic and metabolomic data to explore the genetic basis of differences in anthraquinone content among various tissues of R. officinale. This reference genome and multi-omics data provide insights into the evolution of Rheum and support future genetic studies of this genus. 

# RESULTS

# High-quality, allele-aware genome assembly and annotation of R. officinale

The genome size of R. officinale was estimated to be 6.90–7.04 Gb by flow cytometry (Supplemental Figure 1) and 7.60 Gb by $k \cdot$ -mer distribution analysis (Supplemental Figure 2). In total, 131 Gb of PacBio CCS reads with a contig N50 of 16 kb and 335 Gb of Illumina short reads were obtained (Supplemental Tables 1 and 2). De novo assembly using Canu yielded an initial 7.68-Gb contig-level assembly with an N50 of 3.46 Mb (Table 1), consistent with the genome size estimated by flow cytometry and $k$ -mer-based methods (Figure 1C) $( 2 \mathsf { n } = 4 \mathsf { x } )$ ). We used the ALLHiC algorithm (Zhang et al., 2019), which builds an allele-aware assembly for autopolyploid genomes, to scaffold the assembled contigs by integrating 204 Gb of Hi-C data

(Supplemental Table 3). The resulting assembly was further corrected with the assistance of 3D-DNA (Dudchenko et al., 2017) (Supplemental Figures 3 and 4). The final assembly contained 7.68 Gb in 44 chromosomes comprising 11 homologous groups with four allelic chromosomes named A, B, C, and D and 6.07 Mb of unplaced contigs (Figure 1D; Supplemental Table 4). We assessed the assembly quality by investigating the Hi-C contact matrix, which showed that the chromosome groups were clear-cut (Figure 1B). The four monoploid genomes contained $9 6 . 2 0 \%$ , $9 6 . 4 0 \%$ , $9 6 . 5 0 \%$ , and $9 4 . 3 0 \%$ complete benchmarking universal single-copy ortholog (BUSCO) genes (Simao et al., 2015) (Supplemental Table 5). 

A total of 1.536–1.675 Gb of repeats were identified, representing $8 3 . 1 7 \% - 8 3 . 6 4 \%$ of each monoploid genome. Long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons accounted for $6 4 . 9 1 \% - 6 5 . 7 0 \%$ of each monoploid genome, with Gypsy and Copia superfamilies accounting for $3 8 . 4 3 \% - 3 9 . 1 7 \%$ and $7 . 1 0 \% - 7 . 1 3 \%$ , respectively (Supplemental Table 6). We used an integrated strategy, including evidence-based methods and ab initio gene prediction, to annotate the protein-coding genes of the R. officinale genome (Supplemental Table 7). A final set of 190 916 protein-coding genes was obtained, 190 643 of which $( \sim 9 9 . 8 6 \% )$ were assigned to 44 chromosomes (Supplemental Table 8). The average gene length, protein sequence length, and exon number of the predicted genes were 2520 bp, 310 amino acids, and 4.4, respectively (Supplemental Figure 5; Supplemental Table 9). Among the annotated genes, 179 587 $( \sim 9 4 . 0 7 \% )$ were functionally annotated with the Non-Redundant Protein Sequence Database (NR), Gene Ontology (GO), Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG), Swiss-Prot, and InterPro databases. BUSCO analysis revealed that completeness of these genes reached $9 8 . 1 \%$ (Supplemental Tables 10 and 11), indicating the highquality of the R. officinale gene annotations. We also annotated noncoding RNA genes, obtaining 561 microRNA genes, 13 775 transfer RNA (tRNA) genes, 38 328 ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes, 807 small nuclear RNA (snRNA) genes, 3426 small nucleolar RNA (snoRNA) genes, and 6423 long noncoding RNA (lncRNA) genes in the four monoploid genomes (Supplemental Table 12). 

# Quality assessment of the haplotype-resolved genome assembly

To verify the accuracy of our haplotype-resolved genome assembly, ${ \sim } 1 2$ Gb of Oxford Nanopore ultralong reads with an N50 of 101 kb were obtained; $9 9 . 3 \%$ of these ultralong reads were mapped correctly to our assembled genome, with an average identity of $9 0 . 2 \%$ . There was no significant difference in the number of Oxford Nanopore ultralong reads that mapped to the four monoploid genomes, with mapping proportions of $2 6 . 4 0 \%$ , $2 5 . 2 0 \%$ , $2 4 . 5 3 \%$ , and $2 3 . 8 8 \%$ , respectively. We calculated the rate of false joins among monoploid genomes as $1 . 2 9 \%$ , with an estimated 0.07 switch errors per Mb (Supplemental Table 13). This highly contiguous assembly enabled us to identify 58 telomere regions (AAACCCT or AGGGTTT) along the 44 chromosomes (Supplemental Table 14). To further assess the quality of our assembly, we aligned the Illumina short reads to the assembled genome, resulting in a mapping rate of $9 9 . 8 8 \%$ and $9 9 . 8 4 \%$ coverage of the assembly. The read depth distribution of genomic regions also exhibited a similar pattern; most regions had an average depth of 41, and only $0 . 2 8 \%$ of the 100-kb

# Comparative genomics and gene family evolution analysis

We performed a comparative genomics analysis of R. officinale monoploid genome A and the genomes of 12 representative plant species to determine the phylogenetic placement of R. officinale (Supplemental Table 16). In total, 209 single-copy genes were identified and used to construct phylogenetic relationships. The results showed that R. officinale had the closest evolutionary relationship with the ancestor of Fagopyrum tataricum and Fagopyrum dibotrys, and their estimated divergence occurred ${ \sim } 3 3 . 4$ mya. Gene family clustering analysis showed that 8705 gene families were shared among all 13 species, and 2617 gene families were unique to R. officinale (Figure 2A; Supplemental Table 16). Functional enrichment analysis showed that the specific gene families in R. officinale were mainly related to betalain biosynthesis, glycosyltransferases, folate biosynthesis, steroid biosynthesis, plant hormone signal transduction, and plant–pathogen interactions (Supplemental Figure 8A). There were 2473 expanded and 3885 contracted gene families in R. officinale (Figure 2A). The expanded families were mainly enriched in root development; specialized aspects of metabolism such as phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, sesquiterpenoid and triterpenoid biosynthesis, cytochrome P450 activity, and malic enzyme (ME) activity; and stress responses such as responses to oxidative stress, defense responses, and plant–pathogen interactions (Figure 2B and Supplemental Figure 8B). Together, the specific and expanded gene families in R. officinale were mainly involved in synthesis of specialized metabolites and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses, which may partly explain why R. officinale is rich in medicinal ingredients and shows strong environmental adaptability as a wild species. 

ME, encoded by one of the expanded gene families, is a key protein that regulates plant growth and development and plays an important role in plant stress resistance (Zhou et al., 2011; Sun et al., 2019). Thirty-eight ME gene homologs were identified in R. officinale monoploid genome A, many more than in F. tataricum (10), F. dibotrys (eight), and Beta vulgaris (five) (Figure 2G; Supplemental Table 17) (He et al., 2022). We found that $3 9 . 4 7 \%$ (15 out of 38) of the ME genes were expressed in all three tested tissues (roots, stems, and leaves), nine of which were highly expressed in roots, four in stems, and two in leaves (Figure 2G).

Functional enrichment showed that the WGDGs were significantly enriched in plant–pathogen interactions, the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathway, and transcription factors, which may have improved the resistance of R. officinale to biotic and abiotic stresses. TDGs were mainly enriched in phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, betalain biosynthesis, and flavonoid biosynthesis (Supplemental Figure 10D), which may play crucial roles in the biosynthesis of medicinal substances by R. officinale. Therefore, the expanded duplicated genes in R. officinale may be important for synthesis of specific substances and strong environmental adaptation. 

# TEs have contributed greatly to genome evolution, gene copy number variation, and gene expression of R. officinale

The evolutionary history of chromosomes across the Polygonaceae and Chenopodiaceae lineages was examined by constructing the ancestral karyotype of R. officinale, F. tataricum, B. vulgaris, Spinacia oleracea, and V. vinifera. The results showed that the common ancestor of Polygonaceae and Chenopodiaceae, Ancestor1, contained eight chromosomes. It then underwent 45 chromosomal fissions and 47chromosomal fusions toform Ancestor3, containing six chromosomes. Ancestor3 underwent a WGD event to form 12 chromosomes. Seventy-nine chromosomal fissions and 80 chromosomal fusions formed the 11 chromosomes of R. officinale from Ancestor3, and 125 chromosomal fissions and 129 chromosomal fusions formed the eight chromosomes of F. tataricum (Figure 3A). These results provide insight into the evolutionary history of Polygonaceae chromosomes. In addition, we found that extensive chromosomal rearrangements occurred after the divergence of R. officinale and F. tataricum. Interestingly, these rearrangements were frequently located in TE-rich regions, which were inferred to be candidate pericentromeric regions on the basis of their GC content, gene density, and TE coverage (Figure 3A and Supplemental Figure 12). Therefore, we postulate that chromosomal breakage and rearrangement may be the outcomes of the transpositional or recombinational potential of TEs (Bennetzen, 2000). 

The monoploid genome size of R. officinale was ${ \sim } 1 . 9 2$ Gb on average, approximately four times that of F. tataricum (0.49 Gb). The repeat content of R. officinale $( 8 3 . 6 4 \% )$ was much higher than that of F. tataricum $( 5 0 . 9 6 \% )$ , F. dibotrys $( 7 2 . 2 5 \% )$ , and B. vulgaris $( 4 2 . 3 \% )$ . R. officinale had ${ \sim } 1 . 4 3$ Gb more repetitive sequences than F. tataricum, which accounted for $9 4 . 4 3 \%$ of the 1.51-Gb difference in genome size between the two species. The top eight families (four Gypsy and four Copia) of LTR-RTs represented ${ \sim } 3 5 . 8 7 \%$ of the R. officinale genome, with the most abundant elements being from Tekay (Figure 3E and Supplemental Figure 13B; Supplemental Table 20). These results suggest that the large number of LTRs in R. officinale is the main reason for its increased genome size, and exploration of TE composition and evolution revealed that TEs have greatly shaped the R. officinale genome. 

A total of 15 842 TRDGs were identified in R. officinale, substantially more than in F. tataricum (4120), F. dibotrys (2535), and B. vulgaris (3956) (Figure 2E; Supplemental Tables 18 and 21). Interestingly, these TRDGs had longer gene lengths, shorter CDSs, more exons, and lower expression levels compared with other genes (Supplemental Figure 14), probably because of gene redundancy and even functional differentiation after duplication. We identified TEs located within 2 kb upstream and downstream of the TRDG pairs and found that $5 9 . 8 5 \%$ (10 137) of the pairs were flanked by similar types of TEs (Supplemental Figure 15), providing further evidence that the burst of these TRDGs occurred recently (Wang et al., 2022c) (Supplemental Figure 10C). Interestingly, these TRDGs were mainly enriched in root development, and most of the genes annotated with the root development term encoded triphosphate tunnel metalloenzyme 3 (TTM3). The TTM3 gene family was expanded in R. officinale (19) compared with F. dibotrys (three), F. tataricum (two), and B. vulgaris (one) (Figure 3F), mainly via transposed duplication (16 out of 19) (Supplemental Table 22). A previous study showed that a transfer DNA (T-DNA) insertion knockout line of the homologous gene AT2G11890 in Arabidopsis thaliana displayed delayed root growth and reduced length and number of lateral roots (Moeder et al., 2013). Some of the TTM3 genes were highly expressed in roots, especially Scaffold_103.6 and Chr9A.2165 (Figure 3F), which may be key genes regulating root development. The quantitative real-time PCR (RT-qPCR) results indicated that these TRDGs were functional and highly expressed in the roots of R. officinale (Figure 3G), consistent with our transcriptome data. 

Previous studies have demonstrated that TE insertions can strongly influence gene regulation and expression. We therefore explored and compared the distribution of TEs in R. officinale and F. tataricum. TEs were highly enriched in genic regions and their adjacent regions, especially gene promoters (Figure 3D), indicating that TEs may have contributed to the diversification of gene regulation and expression in R. officinale. The average transcript abundance was significantly lower for genes that harbored LTR insertions than for genes without such insertions $( P < 0 . 0 0 0 1$ ; Figure 3H). We next compared the sequence similarity between monoploid genomes of autotetraploids (sugarcane, alfalfa, and potato), allotetraploids (cotton, peanut, oilseed rape, wild rice), and allohexaploid wheat (Appels et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Zhuang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020, 2021b; Yu et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2022). # Genes encoding key enzymes of terpenoid biosynthesis

Previous studies have shown that triterpenes act primarily as signaling molecules, are the material basis for the therapeutic effect of medicinal plants, and function in the stress response, whereas most monoterpenes have a strong aroma and physiological activity (Dvorakova et al., 2011; Xiao et al., 2018; Teng et al., 2019; Darshani et al., 2022; Paulino et al., 2022). TPS is the key enzyme in terpene biosynthesis, and OSCs catalyze the first step in the structural diversification of triterpenes (Singh and Sharma, 2015; Wang et al., 2022b). A total of 37, 33, 33, and 35 TPS genes were identified in the four monoploid genomes and were assigned to five TPS subfamilies: TPS-a, TPS-b, TPS-c, TPS-e/f, and TPS-g (Chen et al., 2011) (Figure 5F; Supplemental Table 28). We found that TPS-b and TPS-g genes were highly expressed in leaves (Figure 5G), consistent with the high monoterpenoid content of leaf tissue (Figure 5E). # DISCUSSION

Medicinal plants synthesize numerous specialized metabolites that play critical roles in treating disease and maintaining human health (Sun et al., 2022). Because R. officinale is a valuable medicinal herb with therapeutic potential, it is important to explore its genome evolution and the biosynthetic mechanisms of its active substances. 

In this study, we assembled a chromosome-scale, haplotyperesolved autotetraploid genome of R. officinale. Comparative genomics analysis revealed that the specific and expanded gene families in R. officinale were mainly involved in biosynthesis of specialized metabolites and response to biotic and abiotic stresses. TEs made a significant contribution to genome evolution, gene copy number variation, and the regulation and expression of genes involved in metabolite biosynthesis, stress resistance, and root development in R. officinale. We identified many genes with allele-differential and allele-specific expression, possibly owing to different TE insertions in their regulatory regions. Transcriptome and metabolome analyses revealed that significant expansion of genes involved in the anthraquinone pathway and the increase in gene number caused by tetraploidy 

may have contributed to the high anthraquinone content of R. officinale. Furthermore, higher expression of essential genes related to anthraquinone biosynthesis resulted in much higher anthraquinone content in roots than in other tissues. 

Recent advances in molecular technologies have enabled progress in the study of plant polyploidy and polyploid genome evolution. Polyploidy can generate new phenotypic traits and a large number of chromosomal-level variants, some of which are fixed by selection because they contribute to the environmental adaptation of the polyploid species (Devos et al., 1995; Zhang et al., 2013; Li et al., 2016; Luo et al., 2018). Polyploidy can also induce genome-wide changes in gene expression and gene product abundance (Zhang et al., 2013; Sun et al., 2017). The genome of R. officinale, a recently confirmed doubled autopolyploid, will be valuable for future research on differences in genome evolution, phenotype, and medicinal substance composition between it and diploid rhubarb. 

Our work provides insight into the underlying genomic basis of metabolic pathways in medicinal plant species, especially polyploid plants. We identified several genes potentially associated with metabolite biosynthesis and root development in R. officinale that had undergone significant expansion through transposed duplication. We hypothesized that Scaffold_103.6, whose highest expression in roots was validated by RT– qPCR, may be a key gene for rhizome development in R. officinale. Further exploration of these genes may have important implications for improving the quality and yield of R. officinale and for breeding programs for this species. Our results suggest that the increased number of important genes produced by transposable duplication and the expression of these genes could provide a genetic basis for phenotypic innovation in R. officinale. 

It is well known that metabolites are likely to be encoded by neighboring genes. We identified gene pairs and gene clusters involved in the anthraquinone biosynthesis pathway and found that gene clusters tended to be located on Chr7 and Chr10 of the R. officinale genome, whereas gene pairs tended to be located on Chr1 and Chr7. These gene clusters or gene pairs may be important candidate genes for anthraquinone biosynthesis. In particular, the genes on Chr7 may be obvious candidates for metabolic engineering and heterologous reconstitution of beneficial molecules. 

At present, published autotetraploid genomes include alfalfa, an important forage crop; sugarcane, an important sugar crop; potato, one of the four major food crops grown worldwide; and pepper, a famous spice in China (Hu et al., 2023). Until this study, no autotetraploid medicinal plant genomes had been reported. By assembly of an autotetraploid medicinal plant genome, our study offers unprecedented genomic resources for fundamental research and identification of genes associated with agronomically relevant traits and can guide the utilization, improvement, and polyploid breeding of rhubarb. Given the abundant TE content of R. officinale and its important influence on gene copy number and expression, this study also provides an important reference for detailed studies on the functional effects and dynamic activity of TE polymorphisms over long-term evolutionary history. considering the abundant TE con-

tent and important influences on gene copy number and expression in R. officinale. 

# METHODS

# Plant material and genome sequencing

One-year-old R. officinale collected from Lijiang, Yunnan Province, was maintained in a plant greenhouse at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathtt { - } 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with $1 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of light per day at the School of Life Sciences, Hebei University, China, in 2021. Genomic DNA was isolated from young leaves with the DNA Safe Plant Kit (TIANGEN) and used to prepare CCS libraries and Illumina shortread libraries, which were sequenced on the PacBio Sequel and Illumina HiSeq platforms, respectively, at Berry Genomic Corporation (Beijing, China). 

Fresh leaves were used to prepare chromatin crosslinked to DNA and fixed with formaldehyde, and the crosslinked DNA was digested with MboI. The resulting sticky ends were biotinylated and proximity ligated to form chimeric junctions. The processed DNA was further enriched and physically sheared into 300- to 500-bp fragments. Subsequently, all prepared DNA fragments were processed into paired-end sequencing libraries. These procedures were carried out at Berry Genomic Corporation. 

Total RNA was extracted from three replicate samples of R. officinale roots, stems, and leaves using the TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Sequencing was performed on the Illumina platform at Berry Genomic Corporation. 

# Genome size estimation

The genome size of R. officinale was estimated by flow cytometry (BD FACSCalibur) at Wuhan Generead Biotechnology (Chengdu, Sichuan, China) and by $k \mathrm { . }$ -mer distribution analysis. For flow cytometry, tomato and maize were used as internal controls based on equal proportions of fluorescence intensities and genome size. The average fluorescence intensity of tomato and maize was 20.52 and 52.96, respectively, and that of R. officinale was 80.48 and 80.97. The estimated R. officinale genome sizes were 6.90 and 7.04 Gb. Illumina short reads were used to create $k$ -mer sets using Jellyfish (v2.3.0) (Marcais and Kingsford, 2011) and Genome Characteristics Estimation (GCE) (v1.0.2) (Liu et al., 2013). The genome size was estimated using $k$ -mer $( k ~ = ~ 1 7 )$ frequency-based methods with $k$ -mer number/k-mer depth. The genome size of R. officinale was estimated as 7.60 Gb (2c) with a heterozygosity rate of $1 . 9 6 \%$ based on a $1 7 k$ -mer distribution. 

# Genome assembly and scaffolding

Clean CCS reads were used to assemble the R. officinale genome into contigs with Canu (v2.1.1) (Nurk et al., 2020) using the parameter genome size $= 4 9$ . This resulted in an assembly of 7.68 Gb with an N50 length of 3.6 Mb. The Hi-C reads were mapped to the contig-level assembly using Burrows–Wheeler Aligner (BWA, 0.7.17-r1198) with default parameters and then processed by Juicer (v1.5) (Durand et al., 2016). These contigs were reordered and scaffolded using the ALLHiC pipeline (Zhang et al., 2019) and the resulting assembly with the assistance of 3D-DNA (Dudchenko et al., 2017) and according to visualization of chromatin contact patterns. Potential misassemblies were manually checked and corrected using Juicebox (https://github.com/aidenlab/ Juicebox, v1.11.08) on the basis of the Hi-C map and mapping information. All Illumina short reads were mapped onto the assembled chromosome-level genome using BWA (0.7.17-r1198) (Li and Durbin, 2010) to estimate assembly quality. The LAI was calculated using LTR_retriever (v2.9.0) (Ou and Jiang, 2018). The accuracy and completeness of the genome assemblies and predicted genes were evaluated using BUSCO (v5.2.2) (Simao et al., 2015) with the Embryophyta odb10 database.

# Switch error evaluation

High-molecular-weight genomic DNA was prepared by the CTAB method and purified using a genomic kit. Ultralong reads were generated using the Oxford Nanopore high-throughput (ONT) sequencing platform at Wuhan Benagen Technology (Wuhan, China). The longest 10 000 ultralong ONT reads with an N50 length of 175 kb were used to assess the error rate. We divided each read into 10-kb windows and used each window to search for the best alignment across the assembled genome with BWA (0.7.17-r1198). The result was processed using an in-house script as described in a recent study (Zhang et al., 2022a): a spliced read was only defined as a mapped read when its length was ${ > } 5 0 \%$ of its own length. If at least three continuous windows in a read aligned with other monoploid genomes, the read may have indicated a potential switch error. 

# Repetitive sequences and gene annotation

A combination of ab initio and homology-based approaches was used to identify TEs and other repeat regions. Tandem Repeat Finder (TRF) (v.4.07b) (Benson, 1999) was used to identify tandem repeat sequences. For de novo searches, RepeatModeler (v.1.0.11) (http:// www.repeatmasker.org/RepeatModeler/), LTR_FINDER (v1.05) (Xu and Wang, 2007), LTRharvest (v1.5.11) (Ellinghaus et al., 2008), and LTR_retriever (v1.9) (Ou and Jiang, 2018) were used to construct de novo repeat libraries, and RepeatMasker (v.4.1.1) (http://www. repeatmasker.org/RepeatMasker/) (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen, 2009) was used to identify genome-wide repeat sequences based on the de novo library. For homology-based searches, repeat elements were identified by RepeatMasker using a known repeat library (Repbase 15.02) (https://www.girinst.org/server/RepBase/index.php). 

The repeat-masked genome was used for gene annotation. Protein-coding genes in the R. officinale genome were annotated by incorporating transcriptional evidence, homology support from related species, and ab initio methods. For ab initio prediction, PASA (v.2.3) (Haas et al., 2003) was used to predict the gene structure using transcripts assembled by Trinity (v.2.12) (Haas et al., 2013), which were then used for training the gene model in AUGUSTUS (v.3.2.3) (Stanke et al., 2006). For homology-based prediction, GenomeThreader (v.1.7.3) (Gremme et al., 2005) was used to search against protein sequences of closely related species. For transcriptome-based prediction, Trinity was used to assemble RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data into transcripts, and PASA software was used for gene structure prediction based on the transcriptome assembly. In addition, clean RNA-seq data were aligned to the genome with HISAT2 (v.2.2.1) (Kim et al., 2015), and transcripts were assembled with StringTie (v.2.1.6) (Pertea et al., 2016). The assembled transcripts were subsequently used for open reading frame (ORF) prediction using TransDecoder (v.5.1.0) (https://github. com/TransDecoder/TransDecoder). All gene structures predicted by the above methods were integrated into a nonredundant gene set using EVidenceModeler (EVM) (Haas et al., 2008). BUSCO was used with the embryophyta_odb10 dataset to evaluate the completeness of the final gene set. The resulting protein models were functionally annotated by integrating annotation information from the InterProScan (v5.48-83.0) (Jones et al., 2014; Blum et al., 2021), Nr (ftp://ftp.ncbi.nih.gov/blast/ db), and SWISS-PROT (https://www.uniprot.org/downloads) databases. KEGG orthology (KO) terms for each gene were assigned by homology searches against the hidden Markov model (KOfam) database using KofamScan (v1.3.0) with default parameters (Aramaki et al., 2020). 

We detected noncoding RNA using Infernal (v1.1.4) (http://eddylab.org/ infernal/) by comparison with the Rfam (https://rfam.org/) database (Nawrocki and Eddy, 2013; Kalvari et al., 2021) and predicted rRNAs, snRNAs, microRNAs, and so on. For annotation of lncRNAs, the assembled transcripts were filtered by exon number (>1) and predicted ORF length $_ { ( > 3 0 0 }$ bp). Then CPC (http://cpc.cbi.pku.edu.cn) and CNCI (https://github.com/www-bioinfo-org/CNCI) were used to remove transcripts with coding potential (Kong et al., 2007; Sun et al., 2013). We

ran PfamScan (https://github.com/aziele/pfam_scan) and performed BLAST searches against the Swiss-Prot database to remove known proteins. The final dataset was obtained after filtering by fragments per kilobase of exon per million mapped fragments (FPKM) $_ { ( < 0 . 1 ) }$ . 

# Gene family and phylogenetic analyses

All protein sequences from 11 species (S. oleracea, B. vulgaris, F. tataricum, A. thaliana, Theobroma cacao, Glycine max, V. vinifera, Solanum lycopersicum, Solanum tuberosum, Oryza sativa, Zea mays) obtained from the NCBI database and all protein sequences from F. dibotrys obtained from work by He et al. (2022) were used to construct a phylogenetic tree. Single-copy orthologous genes in these plants were identified using OrthoFinder (v2.5.4) (Emms and Kelly, 2019) with default parameters. A concatenated amino acid sequence alignment was created with MUSCLE (v3.8.1551) (Edgar, 2004) and used to construct a phylogenetic tree using RAxML software with the PROTGAMMAIJTTF model and 100 bootstrap replicates; rice and maize were used as outgroups. Divergence times among the 12 species were estimated using r8s with six corrected divergence time points from the TimeTree website (http://timetree.org/) (Kumar et al., 2017). Expansion and contraction of gene families were determined with CAFE´ (Han et al., 2013). GO and KEGG functional enrichment analysis was performed with the ClusterProfiler R package (Yu et al., 2012). 

# Genome synteny and WGD analysis

Collinearity between the R. officinale and F. tataricum genomes was analyzed using MCscan (Python version) (Tang et al., 2008) with default parameters. The protein sequences were aligned all-versus-all with BLASTP (E value $=$ 1e–5). Syntenic regions with collinearity of paralog pairs were identified using MCScanX (Wang et al., 2012) with default parameters. ParaAT (v2.0) (Zhang et al., 2012) was used to construct multiple protein-coding DNA alignments with the parameters (-m clustalw2 -f axt), and the result was used as input data for KaKs_Calculator (v2.0) (Wang et al., 2010) to calculate the Ks values for each paralog pair with default parameters. 

# Genome duplication analysis

We used DupGen_finder (Qiao et al., 2019) with default parameters to identify different modes of gene duplication: WGD, tandem duplication (TD), proximal duplication (PD; a distance fewer than 10 genes on the same chromosome), transposed duplication (transposed gene duplication [TRD]), and dispersed duplication (DSD). 

# Annotation of transcription factors

iTAK (v1.6) (Zheng et al., 2016) was used to identify transcription factors (TFs) and transcriptional regulators (TRs) among the protein sequences, and individual TFs and TRs were classified into different gene families. 

# Ancestral karyotype reconstruction

OrthoFinder was used to identify orthogroups among R. officinale, F. tataricum, B. vulgaris, S. oleracea, and V. vinifera, which were filtered to generate the input file for DRIMM-Synteny (Pham and Pevzner, 2010) following the instructions in the processDrimm pipeline (https:// github.com/xjtu-omics/processDrimm). Subsequently, the synteny and block information generated by DRIMM-Synteny was processed, and syntenic blocks that met the expected copy number were retained as the input file for IAGS using the Python script processDrimm.py (https:// github.com/xjtu-omics/processDrimm/blob/master/processDrimm.py). Finally, the ancestor genome structure was inferred according to the instructions of the IAGS pipeline (https://github.com/xjtu-omics/IAGS) (Gao et al., 2022). 

# TE classification and comparison

TEsorter (Zhang et al., 2022b) was used to classify LTRs, including class I and class II elements covered by the REXdb database (Neumann et al., 2019). We then used the RT domains to analyze relationships and 

construct a phylogenetic tree of retrotransposons with FastTree (v2.1.10) (Price et al., 2010). The phylogenetic tree was displayed with iTOL (https:// itol.embl.de/). TBtools (https://github.com/CJ-Chen/TBtools) was used to show the annotated gene structures. 

# SNP, indel, and SV identification

We selected monoploid genome A as the reference. The other three monoploid genomes of each chromosome were aligned to monoploid genome A using Mummer (v4.0) (Marcais et al., 2018) with the parameter -c 1000. SNPs were obtained by show-snps with the parameters -C -H -I -T -r -l. The raw alignment results were further filtered using delta-filter with parameters -m -i 90 -l 100. The resulting filtered delta files were used to detect structural variations using the SyRI pipeline (Goel et al., 2019) with default parameters. According to the definitions of sequence variation in SyRI outputs, we converted these variations into three types of SVs: presence/ absence variations (PAVs), inversions, and translocations, referring to https://schneebergerlab.github.io/syri/fileformat.html. 

# Detection of deleterious mutations

The sorting intolerant from tolerant (SIFT) algorithm (Vaser et al., 2016) was used to predict whether an amino acid substitution was likely to be deleterious. Monoploid genome A was used to create a genomic database with SIFT predictions (https://github.com/pauline-ng/SIFT4G_ Create_Genomic_DB), and inter-monoploid genome SNPs were annotated using SIFT4G_Annotator.jar. 

# Gene expression analysis

We used the four monoploid genomes of R. officinale for transcriptome analysis with reference to other autotetraploid genomes (Zhang et al., 2018; Bao et al., 2022). Clean reads from F. tataricum and R. officinale were mapped to their genomes with HISAT2 (v2.1.1). Gene expression was estimated using StringTie (v2.1.6) with the -G option. The expression of protein-coding genes was normalized as transcripts per kilobase of exon model per million mapped reads (TPM). F. tataricum transcriptome data (root, stem, and leaf) from a previous study were downloaded from NCBI (Zhang et al., 2017). 

The expression levels of each gene group were compared with a 1:1:1:1 correspondence across the four monoploid genomes. We standardized the relative expression of each gene in a group (1:1:1:1): the TPM of each was divided by the summed TPM of all, resulting in values between 0 and 1 for each gene. This normalized expression was calculated for the average across three samples with reference to a recent work (Kamal et al., 2022). 

# Validation of gene expression by RT–qPCR

RNA $( 1 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } )$ from each tissue was reverse transcribed to cDNA using ABmScript III RT Master Mix (Abclonal, RK20429) in a $2 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ $\mu \up$ reaction volume. mRT–qPCR was performed using 2X Universal SYBR Green Fast qPCR Mix (Abclonal, RK21203). The reactions were performed on a StepOne Plus Real-Time PCR System (Thermo Fisher): one cycle of predenaturation at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 30 s, followed by 40 cycles of $\mathfrak { s } \mathfrak { s } ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 5 s and ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 30 s. Melting curve analysis was performed to confirm PCR specificity with a heat dissociation protocol from ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ to $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ following the final PCR cycle. Relative expression of each gene in each tissue was calculated with the 2 Ct $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). 

# Metabolomic analysis

Three replicate samples of root, stem, and leaf tissue were collected from 1-year-old R. officinale, and metabolites were extracted according to the methods provided by Metware Biotechnology (Wuhan, China), as described previously (Tang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2022). In brief, freeze-dried samples were ground to powder using a mixer mill (MM 400, Retsch) for 1.5 min at $_ { 3 0 } ~ \mathsf { H z }$ . Next, $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m } \mathsf { q }$ of lyophilized powder was dissolved in 1.2 ml of $70 \%$ methanol extraction solution and vortexed for 30 s every 30 min. This mixing step was repeated six times. Following 

centrifugation at 12 000 rpm and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, the extracts were filtered through a microporous membrane (SCAA-104, 0.22-mm pore size; ANPEL, Shanghai, China). The sample extracts were analyzed on an ultra-performance liquid chromatography tandem mass spectroscopy (UPLC–MS/MS) system. Finally, a quality-control sample (mix) was prepared by mixing an equal amount of all samples to monitor the stability of the analytical conditions for assay analysis. The UPLC–ESI–MS/MS system was used to detect the metabolite profiles. Qualitative analysis of all metabolites was performed using the database built by Metware Biotechnology, Wuhan, China, and other public metabolite databases, including MassBank, KNAPSAcK, HMDB, and METLIN (Zhu et al., 2013; Yuan et al., 2022). Mass spectrometry data were analyzed and quantified using Analyst software v1.6.3 and MultiQuant software v3.0.2. 

# Co-expression analysis

We used the Mfuzz R package (Kumar and E Futschik, 2007), which implements soft clustering tools for analysis of expression data. The partition matrix was supplied and contained the complete set of membership values. This information was used to define 10 cluster cores consisting of highly correlated genes. 

# Identification of key gene families

TPS genes, OSC genes, and other genes encoding key enzymes hypothesized to participate in the anthraquinone biosynthetic pathway were obtained from previous studies and NCBI. BLASTP (E value $=$ 1e–5) was performed to search the genome, and genes containing Pfam domains were selected as candidates. Multiple sequences were aligned using MAFFT (v7.310) (Katoh et al., 2002), and a maximum-likelihood tree was constructed with FastTree (v2.1.0). The phylogenetic tree was displayed and annotated with iTOL (https://itol.embl.de/). 

# Gene pair and cluster identification

If fewer than two genes that were not involved in anthraquinone biosynthesis were located between any two genes that were involved in anthraquinone biosynthesis, the latter two genes were defined as ‘‘approaching.’’ Three or more approaching genes involved in anthraquinone biosynthesis formed a cluster, whereas two approaching genes involved in anthraquinone biosynthesis were considered to be a pair. This method referenced a previous report (Shang et al., 2022).
