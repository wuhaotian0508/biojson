# Abstract

Anthocyanin production in bicolored sweet cherry (Prunus avium cv. Rainier) fruit is induced by light exposure, leading to red coloration. The phytohormone abscisic acid (ABA) is essential for this process, but the regulatory relationships that link light and ABA with anthocyanin-associated coloration are currently unclear. In this study, we determined that light treatment of bicolored sweet cherry fruit increased anthocyanin accumulation and induced ABA production and that ABA participates in light-modulated anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry. Two B-box (BBX) genes, PavBBX6/9, were highly induced by light and ABA treatments, as was anthocyanin accumulation. The ectopic expression of PavBBX6 or PavBBX9 in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) increased anthocyanin biosynthesis and ABA accumulation. Overexpressing PavBBX6 or PavBBX9 in sweet cherry calli also enhanced light-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis and ABA accumulation. Additionally, transient overexpression of PavBBX6 or PavBBX9 in sweet cherry peel increased anthocyanin and ABA contents, whereas silencing either gene had the opposite effects. PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 directly bound to the G-box elements in the promoter of UDP glucose-flavonoid-3-O-glycosyltransferase (PavUFGT), a key gene for anthocyanin biosynthesis, and 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase 1 (PavNCED1), a key gene for ABA biosynthesis, and enhanced their activities. These results suggest that PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 positively regulate light-induced anthocyanin and ABA biosynthesis by promoting PavUFGT and PavNCED1 expression, respectively. Our study provides insights into the relationship between the light-induced ABA biosynthetic pathway and anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry fruit. 

# Introduction

Sweet cherry (Prunus avium L.) is an economically important deciduous fruit tree in the Rosaceae family. The appearance and color of sweet cherry fruit reflect its ripeness and flavor, which directly determine its commercial value. Fruit coloration in sweet cherry is the result of anthocyanin accumulation (Estia et al. 2002). The anthocyanin biosynthesis belongs to the branches of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, which is relatively well elucidated. Anthocyanin production is 

catalyzed by a series of enzymes including phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL), chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavanone-3- $\beta$ -hydroxylase (F3H), dihydroflavonol-4-reductase (DFR), anthocyanidin synthase (ANS), and UDP Glc-flavonoid-3-O glucosyltransferase (UFGT). DFR, ANS, and UFGT are known as late biosynthesis genes (Jaakola 2013), and their expression is critical for anthocyanin biosynthesis. Furthermore, UGFT, the final enzyme in the anthocyanin pathway, transfers the glucosyl moiety in UDP glucose to the 3-hydroxyl group of anthocyanin, 

which is crucial for the stability and water solubility of the anthocyanin pigment (Yoshihara et al. 2005). BrDFR1, BrANS1, and BrUF3GT2 are closely related to low temperature-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis in Chinese cabbage (Brassica rapa L.) (He et al. 2020). In pomelo (Citrus grandis), the expression of UFGT is positively associated with anthocyanin accumulation (Huang et al. 2018). In grape berries (Vitis vinifera), UFGT genes are expressed at substantially higher levels in red-skinned cultivars than white-skinned cultivars (Kobayashi et al. 2001). In Chinese sand pear (Pyrus pyrifolia), the expression level of PpUFGT2 is strongly associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis (Qian et al. 2014). In addition to these biosynthetic genes, the MYB/bHLH/WD40 (MBW) complex consisting of MYB transcription factors (TFs), basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) TFs, and WD-repeat proteins is a core regulator of anthocyanin accumulation (Espley et al. 2007, Allan and Espley 2018). The MBW complex directly activates the expression of DFR and UFGT, which function in the late stage of anthocyanin biosynthesis (An et al. 2019). 

Light is one of the most important factors affecting the development of fruit color (Jaakola 2013, Maier et al. 2013). Plants sense light signals through light receptors and transmit these signals to the nucleus. These signals are then transmitted to MYB TFs through a series of signal transduction steps, leading to the formation of MBW complexes to regulate the expression of downstream structural genes to promote anthocyanin accumulation (Stracke et al. 2010; Shin et al. 2013). The light signal is mainly transmitted to the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway through the CONSTITUTIVELY PHOTOMORPHOGENIC 1-ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL5 (COP1-HY5) pathway in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana). The basic leucine zipper (bZIP) TF ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) directly binds to the promoters of MYB TF genes and promotes their expression, thereby indirectly promoting anthocyanin biosynthesis (Bhatnagar et al. 2020). HY5 also directly binds to the promoters of anthocyanin biosynthesis structural genes such as Arabidopsis CHS, directly regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis (Lee et al. 2007, Shin et al. 2013). HY5 can also form complexes with multiple proteins including HY5 HOMOLOG (HYH) and B-box (BBX) proteins to activate the expression of anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway genes (An et al. 2019, Bai et al. 2019a, b). However, in the dark, HY5 and MYB TFs are ubiquitinated by the E3 ligase CONSTITUTIVELY PHOTOMORPHOGENIC 1 (COP1) and degraded via the 26S proteasome, thereby inhibiting anthocyanin biosynthesis (An et al. 2019, Bhatnagar et al. 2020). 

Other regulatory systems that function in anthocyanin biosynthesis are affected by the accumulation of plant hormones. Abscisic acid (ABA), ethylene, jasmonic acid, and other plant hormones are involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis (Jaakola 2013, Karppinen et al. 2018). ABA plays important roles in fruit softening, aroma, and anthocyanin biosynthesis (Jia et al. 2011, Jia et al. 2013, Shen et al. 2014). The ABA biosynthetic pathway has been extensively studied. 

Its critical reaction is the oxidative cleavage of 9-cisepoxyxanthophylls by 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase (NCED) to produce xanthoxin (Finkelstein 2013), which is considered to be the rate-limiting step in ABA biosynthesis. NCEDs are key enzymes that limit ABA biosynthesis (Schwartz et al. 2003). The downregulation of NCED1 results in a substantial decrease in ABA levels and in fruits lacking red coloration in many fruit crops, including strawberry (Fragaria $\times$ ananassa) (Jia et al. 2011), bilberry (Vaccinium myrtillus) (Karppinen et al. 2018), and sweet cherry (P. avium) (Shen et al. 2014). However, how light and ABA signals are integrated to directly regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis-related gene expression is poorly understood. 

BBX proteins are a family of zinc finger proteins containing one or two BBX motifs near their N-termini (Gangappa and Botto 2014). Some BBX family members also contain a CCT (CONSTANS, CONSTANS-LIKE, TIMING OF CAB2 EXPRESSION 1) motif at their C-termini (Khanna et al. 2009). BBX family members are involved in many biological processes, including flowering, photomorphogenesis, thermomorphogenesis, and stress resistance (Gangappa and Botto 2014, Khanna et al. 2009, Xu et al. 2016). To date, 32 BBX proteins have been identified in Arabidopsis, which were classified into five subgroups based on their domain characteristics (Khanna et al. 2009). AtBBX4, AtBBX20, AtBBX21, and AtBBX22 positively regulate photomorphogenesis (Datta et al. 2006, Zhao et al. 2020, Chang et al. 2008, Zhang et al. 2017), whereas AtBBX18, AtBBX19, AtBBX24, AtBBX25, and AtBBX32 inhibit this process (Fan et al. 2012, Gangappa et al. 2013a, b). 

BBX proteins were recently shown to regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis (An et al. 2019, Bai et al. 2019a). In apple (Malus domestica), MdBBX20 mediates ultraviolet-B (UV-B) light and low-temperature signaling to promote anthocyanin biosynthesis (Fang et al. 2019). Moreover, MdBBX22 regulates UV-B-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis by regulating the activity of MdHY5 (An et al. 2019), whereas MdBBX37 interacts with MdMYB1 and MdMYB9 to negatively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis via light signaling (An et al. 2020). In addition, in a recent study, overexpressing MdBBX22 promoted the expression of the microRNA (miR) mdm-miR858 to inhibit the accumulation of procyanidins in plants overexpressing MdMYB9/11/12 (Zhang et al. 2022). PpBBX16 and PpBBX18 directly associated with PpHY5 to induce PpMYB10 transcription to regulate light-induced anthocyanin accumulation in Chinese sand pear (P. pyrifolia) (Bai et al. 2019a, b). PtrBBX23 directly binds to the promoters of proanthocyanidin- and anthocyanin-specific genes to modulate the accumulation of these substances in black cottonwood (Populus trichocarpa) (Li et al. 2021). Although BBX-mediated flavonoid accumulation is mainly induced by the light signaling pathway, the interactions of BBX proteins with phytohormonal signals remain largely unknown. 

We previously demonstrated that light plays a major role in anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored but not dark-red cherry fruit and found that components of the ABA, 

gibberellin acid (GA), ETH, and auxin signaling pathways were sensitive to light in bicolored cherries based on transcriptome analysis (Guo et al. 2018). Here, we investigated how light and ABA regulate anthocyanin-associated coloration in bicolored sweet cherry and demonstrated that light is a key factor in this process and that ABA-regulated fruit coloration is highly dependent on light. Furthermore, we determined that the BBX genes PavBBX6 and PavBBX9, which we previously identified based on transcriptome and gene expression analysis (Wang et al. 2021), were strongly induced by light and ABA treatment. We individually overexpressed PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 in sweet cherry calli and fruit to explore their roles in anthocyanin biosynthesis. We discovered that PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 directly bind to the promoters of PavUFGT and PavNCED1, thereby participating in ABAmediated anthocyanin accumulation in fruit. Overall, our study provides insights into the molecular mechanism of anthocyanin accumulation in fruit and provides a theoretical basis for further enriching the genetic resources associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis in sweet cherry. 

# Results

# Light treatment promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis and ABA production in bicolored sweet cherry

To study the effects of light on fruit coloration in bicolored “Rainier” cherries, we placed black bags on developing cherries at 15 DAF (days after flowering) to block their exposure to light and removed the bags at 45 DAF. The fruits rapidly changed color after bag removal (Fig. 1A), and anthocyanin continuously accumulated starting $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after bag removal until the end of the experiment (120 h after bag removal) (Fig. 1B). We also measured ABA production under light treatment. We observed a significantly greater increase in ABA production in response to light treatment, with ABA levels rising and then falling (Fig. 1C). These results point to an association between ABA production and anthocyanin accumulation, suggesting that light may contribute to ABA production to increase anthocyanin biosynthesis in bicolored cherry fruit. 

We next measured the expression of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (PavCHS, PavCHI, PavF3’H, PavANS, PavDFR, and PavUFGT) in fruit exposed to light. Indeed, the expression of all these genes was induced by light treatment (Fig. 2). To further investigate the role of ABA in light-induced anthocyanin accumulation, we analyzed the expression levels of all NCED family members based on previously generated transcriptome deep sequencing (RNA-seq) data and found that ABA biosynthetic genes (PavNCED1, PavNCED2, and PavNCED3) were highly expressed with fruit ripening, and other members are almost not expressed in sweet cherry fruit (Zhai et al. 2022). In addition, Li et al. (2015) identified four CYP707 members and found that CYP707A2 is mainly involved in regulating ABA during the onset of ripening. Therefore, we identified the expression of these ABA synthesis and catabolism-related genes and found that the expression levels of ABA biosynthetic genes (PavNCED1, PavNCED2, 

and PavNCED3) showed the most significant increase upon exposure to light, and ABA degradation genes (PavCY P707A1, PavCYP707A3) were significantly downregulated under the same conditions (Fig. 2). These results suggest that light promotes ABA production via the expression of ABA biosynthesis genes. We hypothesized that this light-induced ABA production might in turn induce the expression of anthocyanin-related genes, resulting in fruit coloration. 

# ABA is involved in the light-induced anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry fruit

To investigate the role of ABA in light-induced anthocyanin accumulation, we collected “Rainier” shoots with the same numbers of leaves and fruits at 24 DAF and brought them to the laboratory for treatment with light or dark and different phytohormones. After 5 d of treatment, we examined the fruits (Fig. 3A). In the dark, neither control fruit nor treated fruit turned red, and no anthocyanin accumulated (Fig. 3, A and B). By contrast, in the light, the control fruits showed red coloration. ABA promoted fruit coloration and anthocyanin accumulation, while treatment with nordihydroguaiaretic acid (NDGA), an inhibitor of ABA biosynthesis, significantly inhibited anthocyanin accumulation in fruits (Fig. 3, A and B). Furthermore, we measured ABA contents following these treatments. In both the light and dark, ABA contents increased significantly after exogenous ABA treatment and decreased significantly after exogenous NDGA treatment (Fig. 3C). 

We also analyzed the expression of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (PavCHS, PavCHI, PavF3’H, PavANS, PavDFR, and PavUFGT) in “Rainer” fruits treated with light $^ +$ ABA. In the dark, ABA and NDGA lost their effects on the expression of all these genes; in the light, the transcript levels of anthocyanin biosynthesis-related genes rose upon ABA treatment and decreased by NDGA treatment (Fig. 3D). These results were consistent with the changes in fruit color (Fig. 3, A and B). In addition, we analyzed the expression levels of ABA biosynthesis (PavNCED1, PavNCED2, and PavNCED3) and ABA degradation genes (PavCYP707A1, PavCYP707A3, and PavCYP707A4) under light and ABA treatment. The expression of the ABA biosynthesis genes significantly increased, while the expression of the ABA degradation genes substantially decreased in response to exogenous ABA treatment (Fig. 3D). Together, these results indicate that light is a key factor in anthocyanin biosynthesis in bicolored sweet cherry fruit, and ABA is involved in the light-induced anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry fruit.

# PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 can bind to the promoter of PavNCED1 to activate its transcription

PavNCED1 is an important gene in the ABA biosynthesis pathway of sweet cherry that promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis (Shen et al. 2014). To investigate the relationship between PavBBX6/9 and PavNCED1 in anthocyanin biosynthesis, we performed a yeast one-hybrid assay and found that PavBBX6/9 binds to the PavNCED1 promoter (Fig. 8A). In an EMSA, recombinant PavBBX6/9 also bound to the G-box cis-element in the PavNCED1 promoter (Fig. 8B). 

Finally, transient LUC reporter gene assays showed that PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 significantly increase PavNCED1 promoter activity (Fig. 8, C to E). Thus, PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 directly bind to the PavNCED1 promoter and promote its transcription. Together, these observations suggest that ABA-mediated anthocyanin accumulation is also subject to feedback regulation by PavBBX6/9. 

# Discussion

# ABA participates in light-modulated anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry

The pericarp of bicolored sweet cherry fruit only produces a red halo on the side of the fruit exposed to the sun, while the flesh shows no red coloration (Estia et al. 2002, Guo et al. 2018), suggesting that light may be an environmental factor affecting the color of the fruit. In the current study, we showed that light was required for anthocyanin biosynthesis in bicolored cherry fruit (Fig. 1, A and B), which is consistent 

with previous findings (Guo et al. 2018). In addition to light, the plant hormone ABA plays a pivotal role in regulating the ripening of non-climacteric fleshy fruits, which is often accompanied by the accumulation of anthocyanin pigments (Luo et al. 2013, Shen et al. 2014, Jia et al. 2013). However, the regulatory relationships and mechanisms that link light and ABA with anthocyanin-associated coloration are not well defined. 

Growing evidence suggests that light interacts with many phytohormone signaling pathways, including GA, brassinolide (BR), and ETH, especially ABA signaling, to control various plant responses (de Wit et al. 2016, Vaishak et al. 2019, Wang et al. 2019). Low-red/far-red light conditions induce ABA accumulation and regulate the expression of several important ABA biosynthesis and signaling genes in seedlings (Kohnen et al. 2016; Ortiz-Alcaide et al. 2019). In addition, high-light treatment causes a dramatic and stable increase in ABA levels in seedlings and regulates the transcription of numerous genes in the ABA biosynthesis/signaling pathway (Huang et al. 2019). However, the role of light in regulating ABA biosynthesis in fruits, especially whether it enhances or inhibits ABA biosynthesis, remains elusive. 

In strawberry, light and ABA independently regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis via the activation of FaMYB10 expression (Kadomura et al. 2015). However, in the postharvest of strawberry, under red light, the expressions of ABA synthesis-related genes FaNCED1 and FaNCED3 were upregulated, while FaCYP707A1, a gene involved in ABA catabolism, was slightly downregulated; higher synthesis efficiency and lower catabolic efficiency could well explain the increase of ABA content in strawberry under red light irradiation and eventually lead to anthocyanin accumulation (Wang et al. 2022). In non-climacteric bilberry (V. myrtillus L.), red light and blue light treatment upregulated the expression of genes related to ABA biosynthesis, and red light treatment induced higher expression of NCED than control and blue light treatment (Samkumar et al. 2021). It is worth noting that a similar trend of increased NCED gene expression has also been observed in response to red light irradiation in grapevine (Kondo et al. 2014). Here, we confirmed the important role of light in regulating ABA biosynthesis in bicolored sweet cherry fruit. Light treatment significantly promoted ABA production in bicolored sweet cherry fruit by upregulating the expression of ABA biosynthesis genes (Fig. 1C and Fig. 2). Given all the results, it could be hypothesized that the irradiation of light promoted the accumulation of sweet cherry fruit anthocyanins via the ABA-related pathway (Fig. 9). Furthermore, we also discovered that ABA did not promote the red coloration of bicolored sweet cherry fruit in the dark and that combined light and ABA treatment had additive effects on anthocyanin accumulation in these fruits (Fig. 3, A and B). In our manuscript, we demonstrated that light, as initial signal, caused PavBBX6/9 and ABA to form a positive feedback loop (Fig. 9). # light-induced ABA biosynthesis and coloration

Fruit coloration is affected by light during anthocyanin biosynthesis in bicolored sweet cherry fruit (Guo et al. 2018). However, how light promotes anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry fruit has been unclear. In the present study, we determined that the expression of PavBBX6 and PavBBX9, encoding two TFs belonging to the BBX family, was induced by light treatment (Fig. 1, D and E). We therefore reasoned that PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 contribute to anthocyanin accumulation in bicolored sweet cherry fruit. PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 are homologs of Arabidopsis BBX21 and BBX22, respectively. AtBBX21 and AtBBX22 positively affect photomorphogenesis via HY5 and COP1 (Xu et al. 2016; Job et al. 2018, Datta et al. 2008). Similarly, in the present 

study, overexpressing PavBBX6 or PavBBX9 led to shorter hypocotyls in the light, suggesting that these genes are positive regulators of photomorphogenesis (Supplemental Fig. S2, A to E). 

Anthocyanin accumulation is a major component of plant light-mediated development. In apple, COL11 (CONSTANS-LIKE11, a homolog of Arabidopsis BBX22) responds to UV-B light and regulates anthocyanin accumulation (Bai et al. 2014). In addition, PpBBX18 and PpBBX21 antagonistically regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in pear (Bai et al. 2019b). In the current study, we confirmed that PavBBX6 and PavBBX 9 were positive regulators of light-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis by overexpressing these genes in both Arabidopsis and sweet cherry (Supplemental Fig. S2, Figs. 5 and 6). 

BBX21 and BBX22 form protein complexes with HY5 to regulate the expression of downstream genes, and they also directly bind to G-box elements in the CHI promoter to regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis (Xu et al. 2016, Datta et al. 2007; Datta et al. 2008). # Materials and methods

# Plant materials and treatments

Ten-year-old bicolored sweet cherry (P. avium cv. Rainier) trees were grown under standard field conditions and good management measures at the Beijing Institute of Forestry and Pomology. A fruit bagging experiment was conducted at 15 d after flowering (15 DAF). The bags were removed from sweet cherry fruits at 45 DAF. Samples were taken at 0 h, 3 h, 6 h, 9 h, 12 h, 24 h, 48 h, 72 h, and 96 h after bag removal. Three replicates were performed in the experiment, with 15 fruits per replicate. The fruits were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Subsequently, the fruits were used to detect the anthocyanin content and ABA 

production and analyzed the expression level of anthocyanin and ABA synthesis-related genes. 

At 24 DAF, the “Rainier” shoots with the same number of leaves and fruits were brought back to the laboratory for different phytohormone treatment. All the samples were cultivated for 1 d in the darkness to eliminate the difference between individuals on the effect of light previously, and subsequently, ABA and NDGA treatments were applied to the above samples under light and dark conditions, respectively. The specific methods are as follows: 

a) Light treatment: $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ light/8 h dark, normal hydroponic culture. 

b) Dark treatment: continuous dark treatment, normal hydroponics. 

c) Light $^ +$ ABA treatment: $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ light/8 h dark, the culture medium is $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ABA solution. 

d) Dark + ABA treatment: continuous dark treatment, and the culture medium is $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ABA solution. 

e) Light $^ +$ NDGA treatment: $^ { 1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ light $/ 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ dark, the culture medium is $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ NDGA solution. 

f) Dark $^ +$ NDGA treatment: continuous dark treatment, and the culture medium is $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ NDGA solution. 

Three replicates were performed in the experiment, with three shoots per replicate. After the above treatment for 5 d, the fruit phenotype was observed and stored in liquid nitrogen at $\scriptstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . 

# Measuring anthocyanin and ABA contents

The extraction of the total anthocyanin content was based on the method of Liu et al. (2011) with slight modifications, using methanol containing 0.1% (v/v) hydrochloric acid, extracting overnight at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark, and centrifuging at 8,000 rpm at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min, and the supernatant was obtained. The total anthocyanin content was determined by the differential pH method (Kirakosyan et al. 2009), and a UV spectrophotometer was used to detect the absorbance of each sample in 0.025 M potassium chloride solution (pH 1.0) and $0 . 4 ~ M$ sodium acetate buffer solution $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 4 . 5 \right)$ at 510 and 710 nm, respectively; the total anthocyanin content was expressed as mg cyanidin-3-O-rutinoside (CGE)/100 mg FW (fresh weight). Each sample was repeated three times. 

The ABA content was measured with an ELISA kit following the manufacturer’s instructions (Gelatins, China). 

# RNA isolation and reverse transcription quantitative PCR

Total RNA was extracted from the samples using a Plant Total RNA Extraction kit (Huayueyang Biotechnology, Beijing, China). All primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table S1. RT-qPCR was performed using SYBR Premix Ex Taq (Kangwei Century Biotechnology, Beijing, China) with the Rotor-Gene Real-Time PCR System. The RT-qPCR experiments were performed using the conditions in MIQE guidelines. About the data normalization, we 

screened five reference genes according to Wang et al. (2021) and Guo et al. (2018), including PavACT, PavPP2A, PavSRP19, PavCAC, and PavActin1, and we analyzed the variation between all samples (PavSRP19, PavPP2A and PavACT $< 2 . 0 \mathsf { C q } ,$ and PavCAC and PavActin1 $< 1 . 5 \mathsf { C q }$ ). PavCAC and PavActin1 were used and presented small variation between all samples; therefore, these were taken into account for relative abundance calculations. For the internal reference gene of Arabidopsis, we selected two internal reference genes, AtPP2A and AtACTIN2. Similarly, we found that there was no substantial difference in genes’ expression level for AtPP2A or AtACTIN2 as internal reference genes, and AtACTIN2 was selected as the reference gene for the presentation of genes’ expression level in the manuscript for data analysis. All experiments were carried out with three biological replicates. The relative expression value of each gene was quantified using the 2−△△Ct $2 ^ { - \triangle \triangle \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). 

# Cis-element prediction in the PavBBX6/9 gene promoters

The promoter sequences of PavBBX6/9 gene were extracted from P. avium whole genome scaffold data, and then, cis-elements were predicted using the PlantCARE online program (http://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/ html/). 

# Cell-free degradation assay

PavBBX6-GST and PavBBX9-GST fusion proteins were prepared for the in vitro protein degradation assay. Sweet cherry fruits under continuous bagging and bag removal treatment or control and ABA treatment were used for total protein extraction. The PavBBX6-GST and PavBBX9-GST fusion protein and total protein were mixed and incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for the indicated periods, which were then detected using a GST (Glutathione s-transferase) antibody to observe the amount of the residual protein (Kangwei Century Biotechnology, Beijing, China). 

# Genetic transformation of Arabidopsis (A. thaliana)

The full-length CDS (Coding sequence) of PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 were cloned into the binary vector pCambia1301 and used to transform A. tumefaciens strain EHA105. The transgenic Arabidopsis plants were obtained by the floral dip method (Clough and Bent 1998). ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ transgenic plants were grown in soil at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in a plant growth chamber with a 16 h day/8 h night regimen and selected by resistance to hygromycin, and after multiple branch breeding and screening, the ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ generation plants were obtained. 

# Measurement of Arabidopsis hypocotyls

Seeds of wild-type and transgenic lines were grown on 1/2 MS medium, which were subjected to a chilling treatment at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then transferred to white light at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ under long-day conditions (16 h light/8 h dark, 100 to $1 5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } }$ 

$\mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). Seven-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings were used for hypocotyl measurements and anthocyanin and ABA content determination. At least 15 seedlings were imaged, and hypocotyl lengths were measured using ImageJ software (https:// imagej.nih.gov/ij/). 

# Generation of transgenic sweet cherry calli

Sweet cherry calli were induced from the flesh cells of young fruit of “Rainer” as previously reported (Wang et al. 2021). The transformation of sweet cherry calli was performed as follows: sweet cherry calli were incubated with A. tumefaciens strain EHA105 harboring either the pCambia1302-PavBBX6 vector or the pCambia1302-PavBBX9 vector for 25 min. After 3 d coculture, the calli were then screened on Murashige and Skoog (MS) solid medium by $\mathrm { 8 ~ m g / L }$ hygromycin B under continuous dark conditions at $2 6 \textdegree$ and subcultured every 2 wk. For the light treatment, the freshly subcultured PavBBX6/9-containing calli were treated with a $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } / 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ (light/dark, 100 to $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \varsigma ^ { - 1 } ;$ photoperiod and observed after 7 d. 

# Transient transformation analysis in sweet cherry fruit

The overexpression vector pCambia1301-PavBBX6 or pCambia1301-PavBBX9 vector was generated by inserting the CDS of PavBBX6/9 into the NcoI/BglII sites of the pCambia1301 vector. An empty pCambia1301 vector was used as negative control. To construct the RNAi plasmid, we designed PavBBX6/9 for the unique and non-conserved sequence, which was cloned into RNAi vector pFGC5941 (digested with XhoI/SwaI for the forward fragment and with BamHI/XbaI for the converse fragment (McGinnis et al. 2005). An empty pFGC5941 vector was used as negative control. The four recombinant plasmids for overexpression and two empties were each transfected into A. tumefaciens strain EHA105, then infiltrating into “Rainer” fruit on trees using $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ syringes at 20 DAF (Shen et al. 2014). After taking photos, the peel around the injection hole was collected and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until use. 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

The coding regions of PavBBX6/9 were cloned into the pB42AD vector. The promoters of PavUFGT and PavNCED1 were isolated from “Rainer” DNA and cloned into the ${ \mathsf { p l a c Z i 2 } } { \mathsf { \mu } }$ vector. The Y1H assay was performed as described in Zhai et al. (2022). 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The full-length PavBBX6 and PavBBX9 were fused to the pET30a $( + )$ vector to construct the His-PavBBX6 and His-PavBBX9 expression vector, and the recombinant plasmid was transformed into Escherichia coli strain BL21 (DE3). The EMSA was performed using the method as described in Zhai et al. (2022). 

# Transient expression assay in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves

Transient expression assays in N. benthamiana leaves were performed as previously described (Wang et al. 2021). The PavUFGT and PavNCED1 promoter was amplified and cloned into pGreenII 0800-LUC vectors, which generated the reporter construct PavUFGT pro:LUC and PavNCED1 pro:LUC. The full-length ORFs of PavBBX6/9 were inserted into the effector vector pGreen II 0029 62-SK. The recombinant vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101(Psoup) and injected into N. benthamiana leaves. The agroinfiltrated leaves were photographed 2 d after infiltration, and the LUC image was captured using a low-light cooled CCD imaging apparatus. The leaves were sprayed with $2 5 ~ { \mathsf { m } } M$ luciferin and were placed in darkness for 10 min before detection. In addition, N. benthamiana leaves at the injection site were collected, and a fluorescence detection kit (Promega) was used to detect the fluorescence activity. 

# Statistical analysis

All data were analyzed using Student’s t-test with the software SPSS 25.0. The values are represented as the means ± SD. $P < 0 . 0 5$ was considered as statistically significant.
