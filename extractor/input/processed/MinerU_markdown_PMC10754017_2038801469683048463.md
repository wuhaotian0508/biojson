# Summary

Aluminium (Al) toxicity decreases crop production in acid soils in general, but many crops have evolved complex mechanisms to resist it. However, our current understanding of how plants cope with Al stress and perform Al resistance is still at the initial stage. In this study, the citrate transporter CcMATE35 was identified to be involved in Al stress response. The release of citrate was increased substantially in CcMATE35 over-expression (OE) lines under Al stress, indicating enhanced Al resistance. It was demonstrated that transcription factor CcNFYB3 regulated the expression of CcMATE35, promoting the release of citrate from roots to increase Al resistance in pigeon pea. We also found that a Long noncoding RNA Targeting Citrate Synthase (CcLTCS) is involved in Al resistance in pigeon pea. Compared with controls, overexpression of CcLTCS elevated the expression level of the Citrate Synthase gene (CcCS), leading to increases in root citrate level and citrate release, which forms another module to regulate Al resistance in pigeon pea. Simultaneous overexpression of CcNFYB3 and CcLTCS further increased Al resistance. Taken together, these findings suggest that the two modules, CcNFYB3-CcMATE35 and CcLTCS-CcCS, jointly regulate the efflux and synthesis of citrate and may play an important role in enhancing the resistance of pigeon pea under Al stress. 

# Introduction

Al is a substance of the highest content following oxygen and silicon in the Earth’s crust (Feng et al., 2019). It mostly exists in the form of non-toxic insoluble aluminosilicate or alumina in soil, but becomes toxic in the form of $\mathsf { A l } ^ { 3 + }$ ions when soil pH is lower than 5 (Li and Johnson, 2016). Therefore, Al toxicity is often a major threat to crop productivity in acid soils (Kochian et al., 2004; Ma, 2007). Due to industrial pollution or inappropriate agricultural practices, the proportion of acidic farmland has reached 40– $50 \%$ worldwide and there is therefore growing concern about the toxicity of Al (Gupta et al., 2013). Thus, it is critical to have a better understanding of the molecular processes in crop plants to assist in enhancing crop Al tolerance or Al toxicity resistance. 

Pigeon pea is an important crop worldwide and a major source of protein for over 1 billion people in developing countries (Varshney et al., 2011). In addition, it also provides great medicinal value (Gomes et al., 2021) and the extracts or active ingredients of this crop are reported to have therapeutic effects on many diseases, including diabetes, hepatitis, measles, and dysentery (Das et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2010). Pigeon peas are mainly distributed in tropical and subtropical regions where soil often has low pH values and thereby frequently experiences Al stress (Daspute et al., 2018). To cope with such Al stress, pigeon peas have evolved a high tolerance. Therefore, the analysis of its Al tolerance mechanism and the search for key Al tolerance genes will lay the molecular foundation for the selection and breeding of superior Al-tolerant plant varieties. 

Al toxicity can inhibit plant growth. It leads to rapid root growth inhibition, and subsequently, the leaves turn yellow and necrotic easily (Zhang et al., 2019). Under stress, plants experience a series of changes in various physiological and biochemical compounds (e.g., proline, callose, and malondialdehyde content; Mansour, 2013; Trovato et al., 2008; Weber et al., 2004). As reported, aluminium-induced callose formation, particularly in root tips, serves as a highly sensitive physiological marker for aluminium damage (Wissemeier and Horst, 1995). It also serves as an indicator of genotypic differences in aluminium sensitivity in crops such as maize (Collet et al., 2002), wheat (Zhang et al., 1994), and soybeans (Wissemeier et al., 1987). It has been reported that Al toxicity can lead to an increase in the proline content of plants, thereby reducing the osmotic potential of leaves and preventing cell dehydration (Eticha et al., 2005; Giannakoula et al., 2010). High Al tolerance maize and soybean varieties have higher proline content than low-Al-tolerant varieties under the same Al concentration (Zhang et al., 2007), so proline content can be used as an important indicator of plant Al tolerance. In addition to proline, organic acids are also shown to assist plants in resisting Al stress by chelating $\mathsf { A l } ^ { 3 + }$ (Ma et al., 2001; Pan et al., 2020; Ryan et al., 2001). These organic acids include citric acid, malic acid, and oxalic acid (Chen and Liao, 2016). Their secretion is known to be associated with citrate transporters, one of which belongs to the family of Multidrug And Toxic compound Extrusion (MATE) proteins and is commonly found in bacteria, fungi, plants, and mammals (Dong et al., 2019). Recent studies reported that MATE proteins from a variety 

of plants, localized at the plasma membrane and have the function of excreting citrate to enhance tolerance to Al toxicity, including barley (Hordeum vulgare L.), sorghum (Sorghum bicolor L.), Arabidopsis thaliana, wheat (Triticum aestivum), rye (Secale cereale L.), and maize (Zea mays; Liu et al., 2012; Magalhaes et al., 2007; Maron et al., 2013; Santos et al., 2020; Tovkach et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2013). However, the mechanism of how MATE proteins regulate Al resistance was differently performed in different plants. Currently, the mechanism of MATE proteins’ function regarding Al resistance in pigeon pea has not been thoroughly investigated. 

In addition to the increased efflux of citrate, which enhances Al tolerance in plants, the synthesis of citrate also plays a crucial role. It has been reported that plants can accumulate citric acid by increasing the activity of citrate synthesis enzymes (CS) (Sun et al., 2020). Overexpression of the CS gene in tobacco and papaya resulted in the overproduction of citric acid in plants, rendering these plants increased tolerance to Al toxicity (de la Fuente et al., 1997). Anoop et al. (2003) reported that the overexpression of Arabidopsis mitochondrial CS gene in canola oil also induced an increased amount of citrate efflux and a higher resistance to Al toxicity (Anoop et al., 2003; Nayak et al., 1966; Szoke et al., 1992). Still, it is unknown how CS gene regulates Al resistance/tolerance in pigeon pea. 

A previous study showed that the high tolerance of pigeon peas to Al stress is mainly due to the transport of citrate by the MATE protein, and this study focused on only one aspect of the causes affecting Al tolerance (Daspute et al., 2018). In our study, we applied RNA-seq analysis to figure out the expression pattern of MATE genes, CS genes along with their possible regulators of pigeon pea in response to Al stress. We aimed to reveal the role of MATE protein in response to Al stress and the possible influence of the synergistic regulation between citrate efflux and synthesis on plant tolerance to Al stress. With transgenesis phenotyping, biochemistry assays and physiological indicators analysis, we identified two MATE proteins which could enhance Al tolerance by increasing the citric acid efflux in pigeon pea with their positive regulator, a transcription factor CcNFYB3. In addition, a long noncoding RNA CcLTCS was found to regulate CcCS which affected the citric acid biosynthesis. Taken together, we proposed a mechanism model that CcNFYB3-CcMATE35 and CcLTCS-CcCS jointly regulate citrate efflux and synthesis to enhance Al tolerance in pigeon pea. 

# Results

# Identification of CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 highly expressed in pigeon pea in response to aluminium stress

To explore the molecular basis of Al tolerance, we performed a genome-wide transcriptome investigation on roots exposed to Al 

using Illumina high-throughput mRNA sequencing (RNA-seq) technology. Based on RNA-seq data, we analysed the changes in expression of all CcMATEs under Al stress. The results revealed that the expression levels of 21 CcMATE genes were significantly increased in roots treated with Al stress for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Among them, the expression of CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 increased by approximately 2.5 times compared with the level detected before Al treatment. Furthermore, we quantified the expression levels of CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 under individual aluminium stress treatments and observed that they exhibited upregulation in response to aluminium stress (Figure S2). 

Utilizing Clustal W and MEGA 6.0, we built a phylogenetic tree using the full-length proteins of the CcMATE35, CcMATE25, and 12 previously described MATE proteins from 10 other species to define the evolutionary connections among pigeon pea MATE proteins. In addition, structural domain predictions for CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 proteins were performed at the PFAM and TMHMM websites. The CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 proteins were found to be structurally similar to the MATE proteins reported in other plants, both having a conserved MATE family domain and 12 transmembrane structural domains (Figure 1a,b). According to evolutionary connections and comparative analyses, CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 belonged to the MATE family. MATE has been found in several plants to be a plasma membrane-localized protein (Morita et al., 2009; Omote et al., 2006; Yokosho et al., 2011). We conducted subcellular localization studies for CcMATE35 and CcMATE25 using tobacco transient transfection. We observed overlapping fluorescence patterns with FM4-64 dye and AtCBL1-mCherry, indicating their membrane localization. Thus, we conclude that they are membranelocalized proteins (Figure 1c; Figure S2). The tissue-specific expression patterns of CcMATE25 and CcMATE35 were investigated using real-time RT-PCR. CcMATE35 and CcMATE25 were found to be substantially expressed in plant roots during the study, and their expression was increased in roots under Al stress. These findings show that both proteins may be involved in distinct plant root activities (Figure 1d).

# CcNFYB3 increased CcMATE35 expression by binding to its promoter to promote citrate release and thus enhance Al tolerance in pigeon pea

We verified the abovementioned predictions using the yeast onehybrid (Y1H) experiment. Only CcNFYB3 was discovered to bind to the CcMATE35 promoter, out of the five transcription factors (Figure 3e). To validate their binding domains, the CcMATE35 promoter region was separated into four sections (N1-N4), and yeast two-hybrid experiments demonstrated that CcNFYB3 interacts with N3 (which contains the expected binding motif) (Figure 4a). Meanwhile, we used ChIP-PCR to verify their binding effect in vivo. And the P3 region of the CcMATE35 promoter (which contained the expected binding motif) was found to be significantly enriched (Figure 4b,c). Additionally, we examined CcMATE35 expression in CcNFYB3-OEs and Controls and discovered that enhanced CcNFYB3 expression positively correlated with CcMATE35 expression (Figure 4d). 

Using the pigeon pea transgenic system, we further investigated the function of CcNFYB3 in the resistance of pigeon peas to Al. We obtained overexpressed and repressed CcNFYB3 pigeon pea seedlings (CcNFYB3-OEs and CcNFYB3-RNAis). CcNFYB3 expression was more than thrice increased in CcNFYB3-OEs and more than two-fold decreased in CcNFYB3-RNAis as compared to the control (Figure 5b). We observed that the increased expression of CcNFYB3 led to enhanced aluminium stress tolerance in plants. Experimental results showed that under aluminium stress, CcNFYB3-OEs exhibited fewer root cell deaths and lower callose content, alongside increased root elongation and citrate efflux. Conversely, the phenotype of CcNFYB3-RNAi 

lines displayed the opposite characteristics (Figure 5; Figure S6). In addition, we discovered that CcNFYB3-OEs enhanced leaf chlorophyll content, proline content in roots, and citrate release in root than the Controls when Al stress was applied, and the root tip callose content and Al content of CcNFYB3-OEs were found to be lower than that of the control (Figure S7). These findings established that CcNFYB3 was required for pigeon pea resistance to Al stress. 

We transformed two vectors into pigeon peas concurrently to further investigate the regulatory role of CcNFYB3 on CcMATE35. We obtained transgenic pigeon pea plants overexpressing both CcNFYB3 and CcMATE35 (CcNFYB3-OE/CcMATE35-OEs); transgenic plants with suppressed expression of both CcNFYB3 and CcMATE35 (CcNFYB3-RNAi/CcMATE35-RNAis); transgenic plants with overexpression of CcNFYB3 but suppressed expression of CcMATE35 (CcNFYB3-OE/CcMATE35-RNAis); transgenic plants with suppressed expression CcNFYB3 and overexpressing CcMATE35 (CcNFYB3-RNAi/CcMATE35-OEs) (Figure 6b). In the absence of aluminium stress, there were no discernible differences in root elongation between the transgenic lines and the control (Figure S8). CcNFYB3-OEs, CcMATE35-OEs, and CcNFYB3-OE/CcMATE35-OEs were all shown to reduce root cell death and elongation inhibition caused by Al stress, with CcNFYB3-OE/CcMATE35-OEs demonstrating the highest Alresistance. When CcNFYB3-RNAi/CcMATE35-RNAis were inhibited, the cells were more sensitive to Al stress than when only CcNFYB3-RNAis were inhibited. And the alterations in other physiological indicators and leaf phenotypes were consistent with the aforementioned results. The above results speculate that CcNFYB3 has a regulatory effect on CcMATE35 when pigeon pea was subjected to Al stress (Figure 6; Figure S9). 

# CcLTCS increased CcCS expression to promote citrate synthesis and thus enhance Al tolerance in pigeon pea

Plants can enhance their Al tolerance by increasing their efflux citrate content. Thus, in addition to enhancing the expression of citric acid transporters, does increasing citric acid synthesis also play an essential role? To study the aforementioned, we analysed the transcriptome data of TCA cycle enzyme genes under Al stress. We discovered that as the duration of aluminium stress increased, the number of differentially significantly expressed genes in the TCA cycle pathway increased as well (Figure 7a). Among these genes, Only CcCS expression was proportional to the duration of Al stress (Figure 7b). To investigate the function of CcCS in Al stress, we generated CcCS-overexpressed and - repressed plants, CcCS-OEs and CcCS-RNAis (Figure 8a,b). We discovered that overexpression of the CcCS in pigeon pea improved the plant’s resistance to Al stress. Under Al stress, CcCS-OEs plants outperformed Controls, with significant increases in chlorophyll content in leaves, proline content, citrate content, and citrate release content in roots. Meanwhile, the expression level of CcMATE35 in CcNFYB3-OEs and CcNFYB3-RNAis tended to 

increase and decrease, respectively, with a corresponding increase and decrease in citrate efflux (Figure 6). Therefore, we hypothesized that CcNFYB3 can actively regulate the expression of CcMATE35 to promote the efflux of citrate in response to Al stress in pigeon pea. With double-gene transgenic plants, we found that CcNFYB3-RNAi/CcMATE35-OE lines had less citrate release than the control in the presence of Al stress (Figure 6). Based on these results, we speculated that the downstream Al tolerance gene regulated by CcNFYB3 may not only be CcMATE35, overexpression of CcNFYB3 might amplify the signals and compensate for the effect of lacking CcMATE35, otherwise the chlorophyll content of the combination should be roughly the same as the control or possibly similar to Garcia-Oliveira’s study (Garcia-Oliveira et al., 2014). In addition, in our experiments, we found the double RNAi line exhibited a further increase in callose concentration and a decrease in citrate release compared to the CcMATE35-RNAi and CcNFYB3-RNAi lines, which means 

CcMATE35 could exist unknown regulator, independent on CcNFYB3. We also tested the expression of other MATE and Alrelated genes in CcNFYB3-OE and found that the expression of some genes was significantly increased (such as CcALMT1 and CcALS1). These evidences also imply our above point of speculation (Figure S17). At the same time, in pigeon pea, multiple MATEs might have shared functional redundancy, which could also contribute to the compensation phenotype. 

In addition to enhancing Al tolerance in plants by enhancing the expression of citrate transporter proteins (MATEs) in plants, Al tolerance can also be influenced by increasing the expression of citrate synthase genes to affect the synthesis and exudation of citrate. CS has been overexpressed in many plants, including tobacco (Han et al., 2009), Arabidopsis (Koyama et al., 2000), canola (Anoop et al., 2003), and alfalfa (Barone et al., 2008). These findings suggest that overexpression of CS in transgenic plants is a versatile and effective method for enhancing Al resistance in plants. In this study we identified citrate synthase in pigeon pea and experimental data demonstrated that CcCS-OE lines exhibited higher Al tolerance than the control (Figure 8). 

LncRNAs play a wide range of roles in almost all biological processes in plants, especially in the process of mediating immunity to pathogens (Yu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2017) and in response to abiotic stresses such as drought (Li et al., 2019), salinity (Singh et al., 2017), heavy metals (Chen et al., 2018) and nutrient deficiencies (Shin et al., 2018). However, there are relatively few studies on the mechanisms of LncRNA in response to Al stress. In the present study, we investigated the role of CcLTCS in plant Al tolerance. We have tentatively identified the role of CcLTCS in regulating CcCS expression to increase citrate content in pigeon pea, which in turn enhances Al tolerance (Figure 9). In our experiments, it was observed that CcLTCS can positively regulate the transcription of CcCS. Additionally, protein-coding potential analysis of CcLTCS suggests that it cannot translate into a functional protein (Figure 7f). Consequently, CcLTCS might potentially regulate the transcription of CcCS through alternative mechanisms. For instance, CcLTCS might recruit the protein complex, leading to enhanced histone modifications on the CcCS gene, thereby promoting its transcription. Alternatively, CcLTCS could interact with transcription factors to modulate CcCS transcription, or it might bind to mRNA through base pairing, thus affecting mRNA splicing. These hypotheses serve as directions for our subsequent investigations. In our subsequent investigations, we plan to conduct a comprehensive elucidation of the regulatory mechanisms through experiments such as RNA pull-down, RNA-RIP, and CHIRP-seq. 

In this study, we found that the synthesis and efflux of citric acid played an important role in pigeon pea plants in response to Al stress, which provided a certain methodology for analysis of the molecular mechanism of metabolites in plant stress resistance. In addition, this study revealed for the first time that NFYB transcription factors have regulatory effects on MATE in plants, as well as it was the first time that lncRNA was proposed to be important in the regulation of plant Al tolerance in pigeon pea. These genes could be applied as indicator genes for screening Al resistance in plants. Taken together, these researches not only provided new input for understanding the Al resistance mechanism in plants but also laid down the molecular basis for the future screening of superior plant resistance varieties. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant culture and growth conditions

Pigeon pea seeds (Cajanus cajan ‘87119’) were provided by Northeast Forestry University in 2018. We cultivated these seeds in our growth incubator and harvested new seeds every year. The seeds used in this study were harvested in 2020. The seeds were soaked in deionized water for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ at room temperature, then sodium hypochlorite was used for surface sterilization. Then seeds were placed on sterile gauze moistened with a 1/5 strength Hoagland solution and allowed to stand for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ until germination. Germinated seeds were transferred to sterilized sand moistened with 1/5 strength Hoagland solution $\left( \mathsf { p H } = 6 . 6 \right)$ ) and grown for 2–3 days under greenhouse conditions at $3 0 / 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ day/night temperature (Hoagland and Arnon, 1950). Then the seedlings were planted in the greenhouse under 1/5 strength Hoagland hydroponic and soil. All soils were sterilized at $1 2 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 h before use. The soil used in this study had a 5:1:1 ratio of black soil to vermiculite and perlite, with a pH of 7.0 (the pH of the soil in the Al treatment was 4.5). After the pigeon pea seedlings were transferred to the soil, they were maintained by irrigating with 1/5 Hoagland solution every 5 days. 

# Al treatments

For Al treatments, seedlings were treated with 1/5 strength Hoagland solution with 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2 and 5 mM AlCl3 (pH 4.5) for 3 d, and then samples were collected for determination of root growth rate and leaf chlorophyll content. The results showed that the chlorophyll content and root growth rate of pigeon peas decreased significantly when treated with $2 \mathsf { m a A l C l } _ { 3 }$ (Figure S1), so 2 mM AlCl was selected to treat the pigeon pea in the subsequent experiment. 

# Plant RNA extraction and RT-qPCR

Referring to the method of Meng et al. (2019), the pigeon pea RNA was extracted using the CTAB method (Meng et al., 2019). One microgram of total RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the EasyScrip $\textcircled{8}$ All-in-One First-Strand cDNA Synthesis SuperMix for qPCR (TRANS). Gene transcript levels were tested with TransStart- Green qPCR SuperMix (TRANS) using genespecific primers (listed in Table S1). The expression of actin was used as an internal control and the data was analysed using the ddCT method (Pabinger et al., 2014). 

# RNA-sequencing

Three biological repeats were performed for four time points (0, 3, 6, 12 h). Using the Illumina HiSeq 4000 sequencer, the library was sequenced by Biomarker Technologies (Beijing, China) with 50 bp single-end sequencing as previously described. The significant DEG was calculated by Cuffdiff (Trapnell et al., 2012). We screened genes whose FPKM (Fragments Per Kilobase of exon model per Million mapped reads) greater than 1, fold change $( \mathsf { F C } ) \geq 2$ and $Q$ value $\le 0 . 0 5$ in at least one sample as DEG (Gao et al., 2020). According to the annotation of gene function in the transcriptome data, 951 differentially expressed transcription factors were selected. 

# Bioinformatics analysis

The cluster analysis was conducted using Mev software (Howe et al., 2010). We imported all transcription factor RNA-seq data into Mev (Gao et al., 2020) and performed Median Center Genes. 

The K-mean method was selected to generate the clusters (Saeed et al., 2003). 

CcMATE35, CcMATE25, ATDTX35, RcMATE1, RcEEF49069, MtMATE2, VvAM1, VvAM90007.1, ATDTX1, Nt-JAT1, MATE1 human, BexA Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron, MepA Staphylococcus aureus and Slc47a1 Mus musculus protein sequences are from NCBI database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). DNAMAN 7.0 (https://www.lynnon.com/) software was used to align CcMATE35, CcMATE25 with the MATE proteins of other plants. 

With reference to the method of Song et al. (2019), MUSCLE (http://www.ebi.ac.uk/Tools/msa/muscle/) was used for multiple sequence alignment in GENEDOC software using the alignment protein sequences of CcMATE35, CcMATE25 and other plants, animal and fungal MATE proteins (Edgar, 2004; Song et al., 2019). Next, we used the Neighbour-Joining (NJ) algorithm with 1000 bootstraps to construct a rootless phylogenetic tree in MEGA 7.0. The amino acid substitution model was p-distance, and missing data were processed by pairwise deletion (Kumar et al., 2016). 

# Evan’s Blue staining and cell death quantification of roots

Evan’s Blue absorption was employed to detect cell death. Root tips were incubated in a $0 . 2 5 \%$ Evan’s Blue solution for 20 min, followed by a 5-min rinse of the root tips. Subsequently, observations were made under a stereomicroscope. For cell death quantification, 30 stained root tips were immersed in 9 mL of N, N-dimethylformamide for 1 h. The absorbance of the extracts was then measured at 600 nm. 

# Measurement of citrate concentration

The root tips of pigeon pea seedlings were rinsed with deionized water, and the root tips $( 0 { - } 2 ~ \mathsf { c m } )$ ) were cut, weighed, and immediately grounded. The supernatant obtained by ultrasonication and centrifugation was used for citrate analysis (Chen et al., 2012; Delhaize et al., 1993). 

Referring to the method of Wang et al, uniformly growing seedlings were exposed to a $0 . 5 \mathrm { \ m m } \ C a C l _ { 2 }$ $\mathsf { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ (pH 4.5) solution overnight $( 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ and the root tips were then exposed to $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ $\mathsf { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 4 . 5 \right)$ solution containing 2 mM $A | C | _ { 3 }$ to collect and concentrate the root secretions (Wang et al., 2016; Yokosho et al., 2011). The concentration of citrate was analysed according to the enzymatic method of Delhaize et al. (1993; Chen et al., 2012). 

# Measurement of proline

Refer to the method of Song et al. (2020), we cut $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ samples into 5 mL $3 \%$ sulphosalicylic acid. Boiled for 10 min, then cool to room temperature. We mixed the supernatant with acetic acid and ninhydrin reagent in equal proportions in a tube, boiled for 30 min, and cooled on ice. We added 3 mL of toluene to each sample and shook it for 1 min, then let it stand for 40 min. We used a spectrophotometer to measure the absorbance of the supernatant at $5 2 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , and then used the standard curve to calculate the content of proline. Results are expressed as a mean of three independent measurements (Song et al., 2020). 

# Measurement of chlorophyll

Leaves were chopped and put in a centrifuge tube containing $5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of $90 \%$ ethanol. Shaked for 1 min and let stand for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Take 2 mL sample into the cuvette. The optical density was measured with a spectrophotometer at 645 nm, 663 nm and $6 5 2 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ wavelengths with $90 \%$ ethanol as a blank control. 

Finally, calculate the chlorophyll concentration $( { \mathsf { m g } } / \mathsf { L } )$ according to the relevant formula (Pan et al., 2007). 

# Measurement of callose content

Determination according to Kohle and Hirano€ et al. (1985, 2006): root tips were weighed into centrifuge tubes, fixed in 1 mL of ethanol for $1 2 \ \mathrm { h } .$ , removed, ground with 1 M NaOH, sonicated for 3 min and heated in a water bath at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $3 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ . after cooling to room temperature and centrifuged at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ to gobtain a supernatant containing callose. After treatment, it was placed in a fluorescence spectrophotometer to measure the fluorescence intensity (Hirano and Brunner, 2006; Kohle€ et al., 1985). In this method, Curdlan (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) was utilized as the standard. Callose concentration was counted as $\mu \ g$ curdlan equivalent (CE) $\mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ root mass. 

# Measurement of Al content

The Al content was determined by chromium azure S spectrophotometry. Root tips were cultured in centrifuge tubes and extracted with 1 mL of 2 M HCI for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The extract was transferred to $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ volumetric flasks and $2 \mathsf { m l }$ of ascorbic acid $( 1 0 ~ \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { L } )$ , $1 2 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of acetic acid–sodium acetate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 6 . 0 \right)$ , $4 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of CTAB $( 0 . 2 \ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathsf { L } } )$ were added to each volumetric flask in turn, mixed thoroughly and 1 mL of Triton $( 1 \% )$ was added. After thorough mixing add 2 mL of chromium azure S $( 0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { { g } / L ) }$ ). The Al standard solution was used at $1 \ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ and reacted with the sample to produce a standard curve. 

# Gene cloning and construction of expression vectors

Refer to the method of Yang et al. (2020), the full-length sequences of CcMATE35, CcMATE25, CcNAC47, CcNFYB3, CcMADS23, CcICE2, CcE2FE3, CcLTCS, and CcCS were cloned according to the corresponding primers. The cDNAs of CcMATE35, CcMATE25, CcNAC47, CcNFYB3, CcMADS23, CcICE2, CcE2FE3, CcLTCS, CcCS, and the GFP genes were cloned into the vector pROK2 vector controlled by the 35S promoter by homologous recombination according to Gateway Cloning Protocols (Thermo Fisher Scientific: Waltham, Massachusetts, USA) using the BP Clonase II enzyme mixture. Then we used pFGC5941 (BiovectorpFGC5941) to construct the gene RNAi vector following the protocols. Each vector plasmid carrying the gene of interest was transformed into GV3101 Agrobacterium by electric shock method and heat shock method, respectively (Yang et al., 2020). 

# Subcellular localization of CcMATE proteins

The pROK2 vector plasmid carrying the CcMATEs was transformed into GV3101 Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain (Yang et al., 2020). Centrifuged the Agrobacterium solution to remove the supernatant, then resuspended the pellet in the buffer (including $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ acetosyringone, $1 0 \mathrm { \ m u } \mathrm { M g } \mathsf { C l } _ { 2 }$ , and 10 mM MES). The resuspension was immersed in the leaves of Nicotiana benthamiana using a $1 \ \mathsf { m L }$ syringe and then incubated in a greenhouse at $2 2 { - } 2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ for $7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Leica confocal microscope (Leica SP8) was used to observe the fluorescence of the immersed tobacco leaves. 

In the co-localization experiment, pCambia300-CcMATE-GFP and pCambia200-AtCBL1-mCherry were individually introduced into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Subsequently, suspensions of both strains were co-injected into tobacco leaves. The remaining steps followed the previously mentioned protocol. AtCBL1 (AT4G17615) served as the plasma membrane localization marker. 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

The full-length transcription factor candidate sequences were subcloned into the pGADT7 AD vector and the interaction analyses were conducted according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Clontech: Shanghai, China). 

# ChIP-PCR

The ChIP analysis was performed as previously described (Kaufmann et al., 2010; Meng et al., 2018). In short, the overexpressed transgenic tobacco leaves were placed in $1 \%$ formaldehyde and cross-linked for 25 min. Then we separated the nuclei and resuspended them in a nuclear lysis buffer. The chromatin was sonicated into fragments with an average size of 100–500 bp, using Bioruptor (Diagenode: Denville, New Jersey, USA). The protein-DNA complex was immunoprecipitated with a GFP antibody (NewEast; Cat. #28003: Wuhan, China). After elution, reverse cross-linking was performed, and the enriched DNA was purified for qPCR. 

# Hairy root transgenic analysis

Following the method of Meng et al. (2019), we transformed the OE or RNAi vector into Agrobacterium tumefaciens K599, cultured it at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $1 2 \ \mathrm { h } _ { \ }$ , centrifuged at $7 1 0 4 \times 9$ for 10 min, resuspended the pellet in MS liquid medium, and adjusted the concentration to $\mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 1 . 0$ . After approximately one month of culturing, the transgenic hairy root was grown to a stage where the transgenic rate could be detected using RT-qPCR (Meng et al., 2021, 2019). Due to this one-month process, it takes about 10 days for the pigeon peas to start growing hairy roots and 20 days for the hairy roots to grow robustly. We therefore write 20-day seedlings in the manuscript. All hairy-root transgenic pigeon peas were cultivated in soil. CcMATE35, CcMATE25, CcNFYB3, CcLTCS, and CcCS gene overexpression plants obtained based on this method were named CcMATE35- OE, CcMATE25-OE, CcNFYB3-OE, CcLTCS-OE, and CcCS-OE, respectively. CcMATE35, CcNFYB3, CcLTCS, and CcCS gene repressed expression plants obtained based on this method were named CcMATE35-RNAi, CcNFYB3-RNAi, CcLTCS-RNAi, and CcCS-RNAi, respectively. Transgenic pigeon peas overexpressing CcNFYB3 and CcMATE35 genes were named CcNFYB3-OE and CcMATE35-OE, respectively, and similarly CcLTCS-OE and CcNFYB3-OE. Transgenic pigeon peas that suppress the expression of CcNFYB3 and CcMATE35 genes are named CcNFYB3-RNAi and CcMATE35-RNAi, respectively, and similarly CcLTCS-RNAi/CcNFYB3-RNAi. Transgenic pigeon peas that simultaneously repress the expression of the CcNFYB3 and overexpress the CcMATE35 gene are named CcNFYB3-RNAi and CcMATE35- OE, respectively, and similar transgenic pigeon peas are CcNFYB3- OE/CcMATE35-RNAi, CcLTCS-RNAi/CcNFYB3-OE, and CcLTCS-OE/CcNFYB3-RNAi.
