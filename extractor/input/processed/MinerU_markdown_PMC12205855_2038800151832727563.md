Somatic embryogenesis (SE) is the process of asexual reproduction in plants, where somatic cells undergo dedifferentiation and redifferentiation to form embryos, which subsequently develop into plants (Yang and Zhang, 2010). Cotton, a vital cash crop, serves as one of the primary sources of natural fibres and seed oil worldwide. Improving key traits in cotton, such as fibre quality, fibre yield and resistance to diseases and insects, often relies on Agrobacterium-mediated transformation combined with SE regeneration (Ribeiro et al., 2021), including initial cell dedifferentiation, callus proliferation and somatic embryonic development. However, researchers have dedicated considerable efforts to overcoming its inherent limitations, including long periods, low transformation efficiency, asynchronous embryo development and limited suitable varieties (Ge et al., 2023). 

Previous studies have demonstrated that various hormones directly or indirectly influence SE. Brassinosteroids (BRs), a class of phytosteroid hormones, include compounds such as castasterone (CS) and typhasterol (TY), which are essential for plant growth (Nolan et al., 2020). Treatment with different concentrations of BRs enhances the capacity of explants to form initial callus, embryogenic callus and somatic embryos (Azpeitia et al., 2003). Additionally, BRs play a role in regulating the cell cycle, controlling cell proliferation and promoting cell elongation (Manghwar et al., 2022). To date, some enzymes and regulators participating in BR biosynthesis have been identified, most of which belong to the cytochrome P450 (CYP450) family (Cheon et al., 2013). Exploring more functional genes involved in BR biosynthesis is crucial for improving SE efficiency. 

In eukaryotes, LSM1 (Sm-like 1) is a primary component of the LSM1-7 complex, which is involved in RNA uncapping and $5 ^ { \prime } { - } 3 ^ { \prime }$ decay. The Arabidopsis double mutant lsm1a lsm1b exhibits delayed growth, irregular leaf morphology and partially filled seeds (Perea-Resa et al., 2012). Other studies suggest that the LSM1 complex regulates hormone signalling and embryogenesisrelated proteins, such as WRKY, SnRK2 and LEA (Golisz et al., 2013; Perea-Resa et al., 2016). However, the specific function of LSM1B individually remains unclear. 

Two splicing variants of GhLSM1B were identified from cotton leaves, namely GhLSM1BS (318 bp) and GhLSM1BL (387 bp). GhLSM1BS had one inclusive exon shorter than GhLSM1BL (Figure 1a), which encoded a 23 AA fragment in the Sm domain (Figure S1). This difference may lead to functional diversity between GhLSM1BS and GhLSM1BL. Predicted 3D structures demonstrated that the C-terminal regions of both GhLSM1BS and GhLSM1BL formed similar $\propto$ helices, while the distinct Sm domain sequences resulted in two reverse $\beta$ -pleated sheet deficiencies in GhLSM1BS (Figure 1b). Tissue-specific expression analysis showed that GhLSM1BS was predominantly expressed in roots and stems, whereas GhLSM1BL is widely expressed across all examined tissues (Figure S2). Both GhLSM1BS and GhLSM1BL were localized in the nucleus of tobacco leaf cells (Figure S3). 

When cotton hypocotyls were infected with Agrobacterium carrying 35S::GhLSM1BS/L recombinant plasmids, hypocotyls expressing 35S::GhLSM1BS produced more calluses, which were significantly larger than CK during early SE. In contrast, hypocotyls expressing 35S::GhLSM1BL showed no notable morphological changes compared to CK (Figure S4a). Although the callus proliferation rate (CPR) values were similar between CK and 35S::GhLSM1BS/L callus at 15 days (D), those of 35S:: GhLSM1BS increased by approximately $1 8 \%$ and $8 \%$ at 30D and 45D, respectively. No significant differences in CPR were observed between 35S::GhLSM1BL and CK at any stages (Figure S4b). Cytological analysis revealed that the callus cells from 35S:: GhLSM1BS were primarily rod-shaped, whereas those from CK and 35S::GhLSM1BL were predominantly spherical. Additionally, 35S::GhLSM1BS callus cells were visibly larger (Figure S4c).

Compared to WT, all three transgenic lines produced more calluses and grew larger (Figure 1c). CPR values of GhLSM1BS-OE line 8, 12 and 17 at 15D were comparable to WT. However, by 30D, CPR values increased by about $20 \%$ , $34 \%$ and $1 5 \%$ , respectively, with further increases to $4 6 \%$ , $69 \%$ and $2 9 \%$ at 45D (Figure 1d). Notably, callus cells from WT were predominantly spherical, whereas those from GhLSM1BS-OE lines were primarily rod-shaped and significantly larger (Figure 1e). 

Transcriptome analysis of 25D calluses from WT and GhLSM1BS-OE #12 was analysed by sequencing (Table S1). Principal component analysis showed that the repeatability within the WT and GhLSM1BS-OE #12 group was high and reliable (Figure S6a). A total of 9361 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified (Figure S6b), with expression and annotation data provided in Table S2. Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis identified 2740 DEGs associated with 4871 biological process terms, including 10 terms related to BR metabolism (Table S3). KEGG pathway analysis assigned 1185 DEGs to 127 pathways, with 21 DEGs enriched in ‘brassinosteroid biosynthesis’ pathway (Figure S6c). Among them, 10 DEGs were highly expressed, all annotated as CYP450 family proteins (Figure 1f). Furthermore, BR metabolome analysis of 25D calluses detected 11 metabolites. The content of CS in GhLSM1BS-OE was about $0 . 0 0 5 ~ \mathrm { { n g / g } }$ , whereas it was nearly undetectable in WT. The mean content of TY in GhLSM1BS-OE increased by $1 7 . 5 \%$ compared to WT (Figure 1g). 

In summary, our study presents robust evidence that GhLSM1BS stimulates early SE both during Agrobacterium infection and in stable transgenic cotton. It also elucidates the 

mechanism of regulating BR biosynthesis. Overall, we provide a novel strategy for accelerating the molecular breeding process through improving genetic transformation efficiency in cotton.
