# ZmWRKY92-Mediated miR169s/NF-YA13 Module Confers Maize Resistance to Bipolaris maydis by Activating Flavonoid Biosynthesis

Keywords: Bipolaris maydis | disease resistance | flavonoid synthesis | zma-miR169s | ZmNF-YA13 | ZmWRKY92 

# ABSTRACT

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are pivotal regulators of plant immunity. While our prior work implicated zma-miR169s in maize defence against Bipolaris maydis, its upstream regulation and downstream signalling mechanisms remained elusive. Here, we decipher a complete signalling pathway that confers resistance to B. maydis. We show that the transcription factor ZmWRKY92 directly binds to the promoter of zma-miR169s to suppress its transcription. Consistent with its role as a positive regulator, loss of ZmWRKY92 function increased maize susceptibility to the pathogen. We further delineate downstream of this pathway, demonstrating that ZmNF-YA13, a nuclear-localised target of zma-miR169s induced upon infection, is a positive defence regulator. Overexpression of ZmNF-YA13 enhanced resistance, whereas knockout mutants were more susceptible. Integrated multi-omics analysis further revealed that ZmNF-YA13 activates the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, promoting the accumulation of antimicrobial compounds like gallocatechin, quercetin and chalcone. Collectively, our work establishes the ZmWRKY92–miR169s– ZmNF-YA13-flavonoid module as a key signalling pathway in maize antifungal immunity, providing novel targets for maize disease resistance improvement. 

# 1 | Introduction

Maize (Zea mays) is susceptible to infection by various pathogens throughout its growth cycle, leading to yield and quality losses. Among these pathogens, southern corn leaf blight, a foliar disease caused by the fungus Bipolaris maydis, is not only globally prevalent but also widespread in major maizeproducing regions of China. Typical symptoms initially appear as water-soaked or chlorotic spots on leaves, which later develop into elliptical, yellowish-brown lesions (Chen et al. 2023). In severe cases, extensive leaf necrosis may occur. In recent years, this disease has exhibited an increasing epidemic trend, driven by factors such as optimised planting structures, changes in cultivation practices, variety replacement and global climate 

change. Extensive research confirms that its epidemic outbreaks can lead to $1 0 \% { - } 6 8 \%$ yield losses, thereby posing a substantial risk to maize production systems (Dai et al. 2020). 

Plants have evolved a sophisticated innate immune system over their long-term evolution, which primarily consists of two tiers of defence mechanisms (Ngou et al. 2022). The first, pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), is initiated when patternrecognition receptors (PRRs) on the plant cell membrane recognise pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) (Hudson et  al.  2024). The second tier, effector-triggered immunity (ETI), is activated by the specific recognition of pathogen effectors by intracellular nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat receptors (NLRs) (Lonjon et al. 2024). Traditionally, the 

immune responses in both pathways have been attributed primarily to transcriptional reprogramming of defence-related genes. Among the key regulators, WRKY transcription factors (TFs) play crucial roles by coordinating a complex network involving phytohormone signalling, MAPK cascades and protein interaction (Wang et al. 2025). Beyond transcriptional control, recent studies have revealed that non-coding microRNAs (miRNAs) also serve as pivotal regulators in plant immune responses (Waheed et al. 2021). 

miRNAs are a class of endogenous non-coding RNAs, typically 20–24 nucleotides in length, that regulate target genes post-transcriptionally by degrading mRNA or inhibiting translation. They modulate key aspects of plant immune responses, including pathogen recognition, signal transduction and defence activation (Song et al. 2021). In pathogen recognition, miRNAs target two core immune receptors: membranelocalised PRRs (which activate PTI) and intracellular NLRs (linked to ETI) (Yu et  al.  2024). For example, Ath-miR172b targets TOE1 and TOE2, relieving their inhibitory effect on the plant immune receptor FLS2, thereby contributing to PTI activation (Zou et al. 2018). Beyond PTI, many miRNAs also modulate ETI by targeting highly conserved motifs in NLRs, such as the TIR2 and P-loop domains. For instance, the miR482/miR2118 family targets the conserved P-loop structure in NLRs (López-Márquez et al. 2023), while miR825-5p inhibits the expression of TIR-NB-LRR receptor genes by targeting TIR2 (López-Márquez et al. 2021). In signal transduction, receptor activation triggers calcium ion $( \mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } )$ inf lux, an elevation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the activation of MAPK cascades (Köster et  al.  2022; Yu et  al.  2024). MulmiR3954 targets and cleaves MuLnc1 to produce si161579, which further targets CML27, a member of the $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ sensor family and a calmodulin-like protein, thereby regulating downstream calcium signalling (Gai et al. 2018). In contrast, osa-miR528 negatively regulates disease resistance by cleaving its target gene, ascorbate oxidase, which in turn reduces ROS levels (Yao et  al.  2019). In defence activation, immune miR-NAs regulate overlapping responses such as defence-related gene expression, callose deposition and the synthesis of plant hormones. For example, Arabidopsis thaliana miR ${ } _ { 3 9 3 ^ { * } }$ targets MEMB12 to promote the secretion of pathogenesis-related protein PR1, ultimately conferring resistance to Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato avrRpt2 (Zhang et al. 2011). 

The miR169 family is a widely distributed and highly conserved group of miRNAs in plants, with over 400 members identified across monocots and dicots. This family primarily regulates genes of the nuclear factor YA (NF-YA) family, typically by mediating mRNA degradation. However, some members, such as Ath-miR169i/l, exert post-transcriptional regulation by modulating AGO protein sorting to suppress the translation of the target gene AtNF-YA5 (Du et al. 2017). Nuclear factor Y (NF-Y) is a heterotrimeric TF complex composed of three subunits: NF-YA, NF-YB and NF-YC (Chaves-Sanjuan et al. 2021). NF-YB and NF-YC first form a dimer in the cytoplasm, which then translocates to the nucleus, where it associates with NF-YA to form the mature heterotrimeric complex. This complex binds to the CCAAT box in the promoter regions of downstream genes, thereby regulating their expression (Yu et al. 2021; Zhao et al. 2024). Current research on the miR169/NF-YA module primarily has focused 

on its roles in plant growth, development and responses to abiotic stresses (Chen et al. 2024). For example, the target gene NF-YA2 of miR169d can bind to the promoter of the flowering repressor FLC, thereby regulating the expression of FLC and influencing flowering time in plants (Waheed and Zeng  2020). In tomato and Arabidopsis, miR169 specifically cleaves NF-YA2, alleviating its inhibitory effect on the heat shock TF HSFA7, thereby participating in the plant's adaptive response to heat stress (Rao et  al.  2022). Although research on miR169 in plant immunity regulation remains relatively limited, studies have shown that osa-miR169a and Ath-miR169a are involved in regulating plant disease resistance (Hanemian et al. 2016; Yan et al. 2017). Our previous study identified zma-miR169s as a novel member of the maize miR169 family and demonstrated its role as a negative regulator of maize defence responses against B. maydis. Further validation confirmed that ZmNF-YA13 is a direct target of zma-miR169s (Xie et  al.  2024). Nevertheless, the transcriptional regulation of zma-miR169s during defence responses remains unclear, and its full regulatory network has yet to be fully elucidated. In this study, we demonstrate that ZmWRKY92 suppresses the transcription of zma-miR169s by directly binding to its promoter, thereby relieving the repression of ZmNF-YA13 by zma-miR169s and ultimately enhancing maize defence responses against B. maydis.

# 2.1 | ZmWRKY92 Binds to the Promoter of zma-miR169s to Suppresses Its Transcription

Our previous study demonstrated that zma-miR169s, a novel member of the maize miR169 family, acts as a negative regulator of defence against B. maydis. To understand how its expression is controlled during immunity, we sought to identify its upstream transcriptional regulators. We first screened the zma-miR169s promoter for potential TF binding sites using the PlantRegMap tool within the Plant Transcription Factor Database (PlantTFDB). This analysis predicted potential binding sites for several TF families, including MADS, BBR-BPC, DOF and WRKY (Table  S1,S6). Given that the WRKY family is among the most well-characterised TF families in plant immunity, with numerous members directly regulating the expression of defence-related genes (Jiang et  al.  2025; Andersen et  al.  2018), we focused our subsequent investigation on this family. Bioinformatic analysis predicted three candidate WRKY TFs (ZmWRKY51, ZmWRKY71 and ZmWRKY92). To evaluate their potential roles in defence responses, we examined their expression dynamics upon B. maydis infection. The results showed that although ZmWRKY51 was transiently induced at 6 h postinoculation (hpi), ZmWRKY92 exhibited significant and sustained upregulation from 6, 12 and 24 hpi, declining to baseline levels by 36 hpi (Figure  1b). This persistent induction pattern suggested that ZmWRKY92 probably plays a more prominent role in mediating the response to pathogen infection. Based on this evidence, ZmWRKY92 was selected for subsequent functional characterisation.

# 2.2 | ZmWRKY92 Overexpression Enhances Resistance to B. maydis in Maize

To investigate the role of ZmWRKY92 in resistance to B. maydis, we generated transgenic maize lines that overexpress this gene. Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) verified that ZmWRKY92 transcript levels were substantially elevated in these overexpression lines (W92-OE) compared to wild-type (WT) plants (Figure  2a). Consistent with its predicted role as a transcriptional repressor of zmamiR169s, the expression levels of both pre-miR169s and mature zma-miR169s were downregulated in the W92-OE lines (Figure  2b,c). Following inoculation with B. maydis, W92-OE lines exhibited markedly attenuated disease symptoms and had significantly lower disease indices than the WT (Figure 2d,e). Consistent with this enhanced resistance, fungal biomass quantification showed a substantial reduction in B. maydis colonisation in the overexpression lines (Figure 2f). Conversely, two independent zmwrky92 ethyl methanesulphonate (EMS) mutants displayed heightened susceptibility to B. maydis, along with induced expression of zma-miR169s and 

its precursor (Figure  ). Taken together, these results demonstrate that ZmWRKY92 positively regulates maize resistance to B. maydis. 

# 2.3 | ZmNF-YA13, a Target of zma-miR169s, Localised to the Nucleus and Is Upregulated During Fungal Infection

ZmNF-YA13 has been confirmed as a target of zma-miR169s in our previous study (Xie et al. 2024). To further characterise ZmNF-YA13, its subcellular localisation was investigated. In the control group, green fluorescent protein (GFP) exhibited both cytoplasmic and nuclear localisation, while ZmNF-YA13-GFP was exclusively localised to the nucleus (Figure  3a). These results demonstrate that ZmNF-YA13 is a nuclear-localised protein. To examine its tissue-specific expression in maize, we analysed transcript levels across various organs. Quantitative analysis revealed that ZmNF-YA13 was expressed in roots, leaves, tassels, ears, husks and silks, with the highest expression in tassels and ears, and moderate levels in husks and leaves (Figure 3b).

# 2.4 | ZmNF-YA13 Positively Regulates Maize Defence Responses Against B. maydis

Given that ZmNF-YA13 is repressed by zma-miR169s (Xie et  al.  2024) and its expression levels are conversely associated with ZmWRKY92 activity (Figure  S2), we hypothesised that it functions as a downstream component of the ZmWRKY92- miR169s module in disease resistance. To test this hypothesis and define its direct role in defence, we investigated the function of ZmNF-YA13 in maize defence against B. maydis. We generated transgenic maize lines overexpressing ZmNF-YA13 (designated as A13-OE17 and A13-OE20). RT-qPCR and western blot analyses confirmed the successful generation of these lines, demonstrating significantly elevated levels of both ZmNF-YA13 

transcript and protein (Figure S3). Seven days after inoculation with B. maydis, the A13-OE17 and A13-OE20 lines developed scattered lesions and exhibited significantly reduced disease severity compared to the WT (Figure 4a,b). Additionally, the fungal biomass was correspondingly lower in the A13-OE17 and A13-OE20 lines compared to the WT (Figure 4c). To further validate the function of ZmNF-YA13, we generated two knockout mutants (A13-ko1 and A13-ko2) using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing. Two single-guide (sgRNAs) were designed to target the third and fourth exons of ZmNF-YA13. Sequencing analysis revealed that A13-ko1 contained a single-nucleotide A insertion at the first target site and a one-base deletion at the second target site. In the A13-ko2 mutant, a single-nucleotide T insertion was found at the first target site, along with a 23-nucleotide insertion at the second target site (Figure  S4). In both cases, these mutations introduced premature stop codons between the first and second target sites. In contrast to the overexpressing lines, the A13-ko mutants displayed exacerbated disease symptoms and higher disease indices than WT plants (Figure  4a,b). Quantitative PCR (qPCR) analysis further confirmed significantly greater fungal biomass of B. maydis in the A13-ko mutants, demonstrating that loss of ZmNF-YA13 function enhances host susceptibility to B. maydis (Figure 4c).

# 2.5 | ZmNF-YA13 Enhances Maize Defence Responses via Flavonoids Synthesis

# 3 | Discussion

Southern corn leaf blight, caused by B. maydis, is a devastating fungal disease that significantly reduces maize yield and grain quality, posing a major threat to global maize production. Resistance to this disease is a polygenic quantitative trait with a complex genetic architecture. Through extensive quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping, researchers have identified over 180 resistance-associated QTLs in the maize genome, distributed across all 10 chromosomes. Notably, chromosome 3 exhibits a particularly high density of these QTLs, followed by chromosomes 1 

and 9 (Balint-Kurti and Carson 2006; Carson et al. 2004). When categorised by bin locations, these QTLs cluster into 87 distinct genomic intervals. However, despite progress in QTL identification, the molecular characterisation of these loci remains limited, with only a few underlying resistance genes successfully cloned to date. For instance, via multipopulation joint analysis, Yang et al. (2017) identified ZmCCoAOMT2, a disease resistance gene encoding caffeoyl-CoA O-methyltransferase, within the major QTL locus qMdr9.02. This gene enhances resistance by regulating the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway to promote lignin biosynthesis. Similarly, using an integrated multi-omics strategy, Chen et  al.  (2023) cloned two resistance genes for southern corn leaf blight, ZmFUT1 and MYBR92. Beyond individual resistance genes, broader regulatory networks mediate maize defence against B. maydis. These include the MAPK signalling pathway, TF families, reactive oxygen species (ROS) metabolism and phytohormone signal transduction. For example, through comparative proteomics analysis, Zhang et  al.  (2022) identified ZmAPX1, a cytoplasm-localised ascorbate peroxidase. This antioxidant enzyme enhances resistance by scavenging hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ and activating the jasmonic acid (JA) signalling pathway. Although significant progress has been made in identifying and functionally characterising resistance genes against southern corn leaf blight, the disease has resurfaced in recent years, re-emerging as a major constraint to maize production. Given these challenges, there is a pressing need to expedite both the discovery and functional validation of disease resistance genes. 

The miR169 family, a conserved group of microRNAs, is well characterised for its roles in abiotic stress responses (Chen et al. 2024), and emerging evidence supports its involvement in plant immunity (Xie et al. 2024; Yan et al. 2017). However, the transcriptional regulation of miR169 during defence responses has remained unclear. In this study, we identified ZmWRKY92 as a key transcriptional repressor that directly binds to the promoter of zma-miR169s, suppressing its  transcription. This repression alleviates the zma-miR169s-mediated inhibition of ZmNF-YA13, thereby enhancing maize resistance to B. maydis. This finding reveals a novel regulatory mechanism in plant immunity, where a WRKY TF modulates miRNA expression to fine-tune defence responses. WRKY proteins are known to orchestrate immune signalling through various strategies, including direct activation of PR genes (Wang et al. 2025), modulation of salicylic acid/JA biosynthesis (Deng et  al.  2020), MAPK (mitogen-activated protein kinases) signalling cascades (Yang et al. 2022) and formation of regulatory complexes (Liu et al. 2023). Our study extends this repertoire by demonstrating, for the first time, that WRKYs can also exert immune functions through the transcriptional repression of miRNAs, thereby linking transcriptional control to post-transcriptional regulation via the ZmWRKY92-zma-miR169s-ZmNF-YA13 module. Notably, the function of ZmWRKY92 within this module relies on both its DNA-binding activity and its C-terminal domain. Biochemical assays revealed that an EMS mutant of ZmWRKY92 (zmwrky92-1), which harbours a premature stop codon causing the loss of 36 C-terminal amino acids, retained the ability to bind the zma-miR169s promoter in vitro (Figure 1f). Despite this intact DNA-binding capacity, the mutant conferred increased susceptibility to B. maydis in maize, demonstrating the critical role of the ZmWRKY92 C-terminal domain in immunity. This parallels the case of AtWRKY33, in which removal of its C-terminal domain also abolished disease resistance (Zhou et al. 2015). We propose that the C-terminal domain  functions by mediating specific protein–protein interactions, thereby dynamically finetuning downstream processes to precisely coordinate immune responses beyond mere DNA binding. 

Having established ZmNF-YA13 as the functional downstream component of the ZmWRKY92-miR169s module, we sought to determine the mechanism by which it enhances disease resistance. Our integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic analysis revealed a significant enrichment of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway in ZmNF-YA13-overexpressing maize lines, which was corroborated by a marked accumulation of specific antimicrobial flavonoids, including gallocatechin, quercetin and sakuranetin. This finding aligns with the established role of flavonoid induction as a conserved defence mechanism in plants. Notably, this pathway in tomato could be regulated by the SINF-YA/B/C complex by directly activating the SICHS1 promoter to promote flavonoid synthesis (Wang et al. 2021). Elevated flavonoid levels enhanced resistance to Botrytis cinerea in tomato and early defence in Pinus koraiensis against nematode infection (Wang et al. 2023; Yu et al. 2025). The elevated levels of specific flavonoids, such as quercetin and sakuranetin, are of particular functional significance. Quercetin is a potent antimicrobial agent known to inhibit pathogen growth by targeting essential processes like nucleic acid synthesis (Singh et al. 2021). Similarly, sakuranetin has been demonstrated to confer resistance in rice by disrupting fungal effector uptake (Jiang et  al.  2024). 

Therefore, we propose that the ZmNF-YA13-mediated accumulation of these key flavonoids constitutes a critical chemical defence barrier in transgenic maize, contributing to the observed resistance phenotype. 

In summary, our study demonstrates that ZmWRKY92 transcriptionally represses zma-miR169s by directly binding to its promoter. This repression alleviates zma-miR169s-mediated suppression of its target, ZmNF-YA13, thereby promoting ZmNF-YA13 accumulation as previously established (Xie et  al.  2024). The elevated ZmNF-YA13 level subsequently enhances flavonoid synthesis, which in turn improves maize resistance to B. maydis (Figure  7). Our findings reveal a novel role for a WRKY TF in modulating miRNA expression during plant immunity, elucidating a complex transcriptional and posttranscriptional network that confers maize resistance to B. maydis. Collectively, this WRKY-miRNA-NF-YA-flavonoid module could serve as a potential target for genetic improvement of maize disease resistance. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

This study used two maize (Z. mays) inbred lines, B73 and KN5585. Transgenic maize overexpressing ZmNF-YA13 and the ZmNF-YA13 CRISPR-Cas9 knockout mutants were generated by Weimi Biotechnology Science and Technology (Changzhou, China). Additionally, the ZmWRKY92 EMS mutants were generously provided by Prof. Cai of Anhui Agricultural University. Maize plants were grown at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod. 

# 4.2 | Plasmid Construction and Genetic Transformation

To construct the ZmNF-YA13 overexpression vector, the fulllength coding sequence (CDS) of ZmNF-YA13 was amplified and cloned into the plant overexpression vector pWMV013, under the control of the ubiquitin promoter. The ZmNF-YA13 mutant was generated using the CRISPR/Cas9 system. Two guide RNA sequences, listed in Table S3, were screened using the CRISPR-P software (http://crispr.hzau.edu.cn/CRISPR2). The construction of the ZmNF-YA13 CRISPR vector and genetic transformation were carried out by Weimi Biotechnology Science and Technology. 

# 4.3 | Gene Expression Analysis With RT-qPCR

Total RNA was isolated from the leaves of Zmwrky92 mutants, ZmNF-YA13-overexpressing transgenic plants, ZmNF-YA13 CRISPR-Cas9 knockout lines, and their corresponding WT controls using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen). Genomic DNA was removed through DNase treatment, and first-strand cDNA synthesis was performed using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit (TaKaRa). Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) was then conducted with SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme), and relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ method.

# 4.4 | Y1H Assay

Oligonucleotide containing three tandem repeats of the W-box motif (AAAGGTCAACA), derived from the promoter regions of zma-miR169s, was cloned into the pAbAi vector. The linearised pAbAi-zma-miR169s plasmid was then transformed into Y1HGold yeast strain to generate the bait strain. The fulllength coding sequence of ZmWRKY92 was cloned into the pGADT7 vector in-frame with the GAL4 activation domain, creating the prey construct (pGADT7-ZmWRKY92). Both the prey construct and empty pGADT7 (negative control) were transformed into the bait strain. Transformants were first selected on SD/−Leu medium. Subsequently, colonies were serially diluted and replica-plated onto SD/−Leu medium supplemented with $1 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ AbA to assess protein–DNA interactions. 

# 4.5 | Dual Luciferase Assay

To generate luciferase reporter constructs, a 2-kb promoter fragment of zma-miR169s was cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector. The coding sequence of ZmWRKY92 was PCR-amplified and inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK vector to create an effector construct, with the empty pGreenII 62-SK vector serving as a negative control. All recombinant plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 (pSoup). Bacterial suspensions containing either reporter or effector constructs were mixed in a 1:1 ratio and co-infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves using a needleless syringe. Luciferase signals were visualised $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post-infiltration using a 5200 multichemiluminescent imaging system (Tanona). Quantitative measurements of luciferase activity were performed using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (Yeasen) following the manufacturer's instructions. 

# 4.6 | EMSA

The GST-ZmWRKY92 recombinant expression vector was constructed by inserting the target gene into the pGEX-6P-1 vector. The constructed vector was then transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells to generate the expression strain. For protein induction, the bacterial culture was treated with 1 mM isopropyl thio- $\cdot \beta$ -d-galactoside (IPTG) and incubated at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 2 { - } 2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . The GST-ZmWRKY92 fusion protein was purified using GSTSeq Glutathione MagBeads according to the manufacturer's instructions. 

Cy5-labelled 50 bp DNA probes containing either the WT W-box element $( \mathrm { P _ { M i R 1 6 9 s } } )$ or the mutated W-box element $( \mathrm { m P _ { M i R N A 1 6 9 s } } )$ , along with unlabelled competitor DNA, were 

synthesised by Shanghai Sangon Biotechnology (Shanghai, China) based on the miRNA169s promoter sequence. The binding reaction was performed in a $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction buffer at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The DNA–protein complexes were separated by a $6 \%$ polyacrylamide gel. After electrophoresis, the complexes were visualised using a ChemiDoc MP Imaging System (Bio-Rad). 

# 4.7 | Inoculation Treatment With B. maydis

Mycelial agar blocks of B. maydis cultured on potato dextrose agar (PDA) were transferred to sorghum grain medium. Following 7 days of incubation at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the cultures were rinsed with sterile water and filtered through sterile gauze to harvest conidial spores. The spore suspension was adjusted to $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores/mL in $0 . 1 \%$ Tween 20 solution. 

For disease assays, seven-leaf stage maize plants were sprayinoculated with B. maydis conidial suspension $\mathrm { 1 0 ^ { 5 } }$ spores/mL). Leaf samples were collected at 3 days post-inoculation (dpi) for DNA extraction and subsequent qPCR analysis of fungal DNA content. Fungal biomass was quantified by normalising B. maydis 5.8S rDNA levels against maize ubiquitin DNA using qPCR. Disease severity was evaluated at 7 dpi, with disease indices calculated according to our previous study (Xie et al. 2024). Each treatment included at least nine maize plants and was repeated three times. 

# 4.8 | Subcellular Localisation Analysis

The coding sequence of ZmNF-YA13 (excluding the stop codon) was cloned into the pCAMBIA1305 expression vector, downstream of the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. For transient expression analysis, mesophyll protoplasts were isolated from 10-day-old etiolated maize seedlings and transformed using polyethylene glycol (PEG 4000)-mediated transfection. GFP fluorescence was examined $3 6 { - } 4 8 \mathrm { h }$ posttransformation with a LSM 800 (Zeiss) confocal laser scanning microscope. 

# 4.9 | Transcriptome Analysis and Metabolome Analysis

The transgenic overexpression lines (A13-OE20) and WT plants at the seven-leaf stage were inoculated with B. maydis conidial suspension $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores/mL). Leaf samples were collected at 0 and 48 hpi for transcriptome and metabolome analysis. A minimum of five leaves were pooled per replicate. Four libraries (WT, OE, WT-B and OE-B)  were constructed. Each treatment contained five plants and was repeated three times. 

The RNA libraries were constructed and sequenced at Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China). Transcript abundance was quantified as FPKM (fragments per kilobase of exon per million fragments mapped) using Cufflinks analysis. DEGs were identified with a threshold of $\log _ { 2 }$ fold change $\mathrm { ( F C ) } \geq | 1 |$ and adjusted $p$ -value $\leq 0 . 0 5$ . 

Non-targeted metabolomics analysis was performed by Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China). Differential metabolites were identified based on a combination of the variable importance in projection (VIP) scores from an OPLS-DA model and the absolute $\log _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C }$ . Metabolites with $\mathrm { V I P } > 1$ and $\log _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C } \geq | 1 |$ were considered statistically significant. Prior to OPLS-DA modelling, the data were preprocessed by $\log _ { 2 }$ -transformation and mean-centring. The model was constructed using the R package MetaboAnalystR, and its robustness was validated by a permutation test with 200 iterations to prevent overfitting. 

The contents of quercetin and gallocatechin in ZmNF-YA13- ovexpressing lines and WT plants, both before and after pathogen infection, were quantified by HPLC. This analysis was performed by Cavens testing technology Co. Ltd. (Nanjing, China). 

# 4.10 | Plate Antagonism Assay

A plate antagonism assay was conducted to evaluate the antifungal activity of flavonoids against B. maydis. The flavonoids quercetin, chalcone and sakuranetin were individually incorporated into the PDA at a specific concentration $( 0 . 3 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L }$ for quercetin and $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ for chalcone and sakuranetin). Upon solidification, a 5-mm mycelial plug of B. maydis was placed at the centre of each plate. The plates were incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7days. PDA plates without any flavonoid supplementation were used as the control group. The inhibition rate was calculated according to the formula: Inhibition rate $( \% ) =$ (Colony diameter of the control − Colony diameter of the treatment)/Colony diameter of the contro $\times 1 0 0$ . 

# 4.11 | Statistical Analysis

Data are presented as mean $\pm$ standard deviation (SD) from a minimum of three independent experiments. Statistical analyses were performed using Student's t test $^ { \ast } p < 0 . 0 5$ , $^ { * * } p < 0 . 0 1 ,$ ) or one-way ANOVA $\cdot ( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ , followed by Duncan's tests.
