Key words: defence, gibberellin (GA), growth, jasmonate (JA), steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs), tomato, $\alpha$ -tomatine.

- Steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) are protective metabolites constitutively produced by Solanaceae species. Genes and enzymes generating the vast structural diversity of SGAs have been largely identified. Yet, mechanisms of hormone pathways coordinating defence (jasmonate; JA) and growth (gibberellin; GA) controlling SGAs metabolism remain unclear.   
- We used tomato to decipher the hormonal regulation of SGAs metabolism during growth vs defence tradeoff. This was performed by genetic and biochemical characterisation of different JA and GA pathways components, coupled with in vitro experiments to elucidate the crosstalk between these hormone pathways mediating SGAs metabolism.   
- We discovered that reduced active JA results in decreased SGA production, while low levels of GA or its receptor led to elevated SGA accumulation. We showed that MYC1 and MYC2 transcription factors mediate the JA/GA crosstalk by transcriptional activation of SGA biosynthesis and GA catabolism genes. Furthermore, MYC1 and MYC2 transcriptionally regulate the GA signalling suppressor DELLA that by itself interferes in JA-mediated SGA control by modulating MYC activity through protein-protein interaction. Chemical and fungal pathogen treatments reinforced the concept of JA/GA crosstalk during SGA metabolism.   
- These findings revealed the mechanism of JA/GA interplay in SGA biosynthesis to balance the cost of chemical defence with growth.

# Introduction

To resist numerous threats from pests and pathogens, plants possess an array of defence responses including constitutive and/or induced accumulation of protective metabolites (Bednarek & Osbourn, 2009). Steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) are defence compounds synthesised predominantly in the Solanaceae family that includes crop plants such as tomato, potato and eggplant (Cárdenas et al., 2016). SGAs are well known for providing resistance against diverse natural enemies (Itkin et al., 2013; Paudel et al., 2017; Cárdenas et al., 2019). Several combinations of the steroidal alkaloid aglycone and sugar moieties create extensive structural diversity of SGAs. These structural peculiarities

determine the biocidal potency of specific SGAs against animals, pests and pathogens (Iijima et al., 2013). While SGAs manifest potent defence, their production demands a high metabolic cost reflected by compromised plant growth (Waller & Burstrom, 1969; Zust & Agrawal, 2017; Panda et al., 2021).

Jasmonate (JA) biosynthesis in plants is facilitated by the consecutive activity of diverse enzymes including JASMONATE RESISTANT 1 (JAR1) (Wasternack & Hause, 2013) converting jasmonic acid to $(+)$ -7-iso-jasmonoyl-L-isoleucine (JA-Ile), the known endogenous bioactive form of JA in vascular plants (Fonseca et al., 2009; Monte et al., 2018). The canonical JA signalling module comprises the JA-Ile co-receptor protein COI1 (CORONATINE INSENSITIVE 1) known as JAI1 in tomato

(Li et al., 2004), the JA signalling repressor JASMONATE ZIM DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins, NOVEL INTERACTOR of JAZ (NINJA), TOPLESS (TPL) proteins and the MYC2 transcriptional regulator (Supporting Information Fig. S1a) (Howe et al., 2018). JA signalling activates a diverse group of transcription factors regulating the biosynthesis of several classes of specialised metabolites. Specialised metabolites such as nicotine in Nicotiana tabacum, artemisinin in Artemisia annua, terpenoid indole alkaloids in Catharanthus roseus, withanolides in Withania somnifera and $\alpha$ -tomatine in tomato were shown to be induced upon JA treatment (Chen et al., 2019; Wasternack & Strnad, 2019; Min et al., 2020). In tomato, $\alpha$ -tomatine is the principal SGA in green tissues and its biosynthesis was extensively studied in the last decade (Fig. S1b) (Itkin et al., 2013; Sonawane et al., 2016). However, not much is known about the transcriptional and hormonal control of the SGA pathway. GLYCOALKALOID METABOLISM 9 (GAME9/JRE4), a JA responsive transcription factor, was proposed to act synergistically with MYC2 to regulate SGA biosynthesis in tomato and in potato (Cardenas et al., 2016; Thagun et al., 2016). Abdelkareem et al. (2017) reported that overexpression of GAME9 in the jai1 mutant background increases the accumulation of $\alpha$ -tomatine and therefore overcomes the strong JAZ repression.

Major aspects of plant growth and development are controlled by the gibberellin (GA) (Davière & Achard, 2016; Fig. S2a). Structural diversity of GA metabolites originates predominantly from the activity of four classes of GA-OXIDASEs; GA13-OXIDASEs, GA20-OXIDASEs and GA3-OXIDASEs participate in GA biosynthesis while GA2-OXIDASEs act in GA catabolism (Hedden, 2020) (Fig. S2a). The GA2-OXIDASE family includes subgroups I, II and III (Chen et al., 2016); while the substrates for subgroups I and II are primarily bioactive GAs (GA1 and GA4), subgroup III acts in earlier steps of the GA biosynthesis pathway (Hedden, 2020) (Fig. S2a). Eventually, the activity of all groups results in reduction of bioactive GA accumulation (Hedden, 2020). Apart from extensive studies reporting GA-dependent mechanisms in plant development, only limited reports demonstrate its role in regulating specialised metabolism (Masoud et al., 1968; Abbasi et al., 2012; Hong et al., 2012). The production of caffeic acid derivatives in Echinacea purpurea and the biosynthesis of sesquiterpenes in Arabidopsis are positively regulated by GA signalling (Abbasi et al., 2012; Hong et al., 2012). In Arabidopsis, overaccumulation of the GA response repressor DELLA, sequesters JA responsive MYC2, resulting in suppression of sesquiterpene synthase genes (TPS21 and TPS11; Hong et al., 2012). In addition, sucrose-induced anthocyanin accumulation in Arabidopsis is positively modulated by JA signalling (COI1 dependent) and negatively regulated by GA metabolism (Loreti et al., 2008). Heinrich et al. (2013) reported two JA suppressors in Nicotiana attenuata (calcium-dependent protein kinases, CDPK4 and CDPK5) that interact with the GA biosynthetic enzymes and regulate specialised metabolism. Downregulation of these suppressors inhibits the transcript accumulation of two core genes (GA20-OXIDASE and possibly GA13-OXIDASE) involved in GA biosynthesis, consequently inhibiting plant growth. Furthermore, genetic characterisation of

alkaloid deficient mutants in $N.$ attenuata during insect interaction showed strong JA-dependent growth suppression. Notably, GA complementation rescued the growth phenotype without diminishing the induction of defensive metabolites (Machado et al., 2017). The core mechanism driving the tradeoff between growth and defence is yet unclear. Resource limitation at the metabolite level is likely to be to one major factor in this process. Campos et al. (2016) showed that JA signalling and photoreceptors (jazQ phyB) mutants are able to grow and defend simultaneously. This provided evidence that JA-mediated growth-defence antagonism is not merely associated with 'metabolic competition' that shuttles resources to defence or growth according to plant needs.

In contrast with biosynthesis of specialised metabolites, the knowledge regarding the role of individual hormones in their control is yet unclear, we have no information with respect to the combined action of hormone pathways in regulating production of specialised metabolites. Here we demonstrated that constitutive and induced production of SGA metabolites in tomato leaves is under tight and harmonised control by the JA and GA signalling cascades and their downstream transcriptional regulators. Apart from a specific triad of JAZ proteins and the bHLH transcription factor MYC2, we discovered that MYC1, another bHLH-type protein, plays a key role in JA-mediated SGA biosynthesis. We provide evidence that the crosstalk between the GA and JA pathways with respect to SGA production in tomato is controlled by either DELLA-mediated sequestration of JAZ (therefore activating MYCs) or DELLA-MYCs physical interaction (that inhibits MYCs sequestration by JAZ), therefore modulating MYC's ability to transcriptionally regulate their target genes. In addition, we discovered that MYC1 and MYC2 could regulate GA metabolism and signalling by transcriptional activation of GA catabolism genes (GA2ox5 and GA2ox7) and DELLA, respectively. The results demonstrate that SGA defence metabolite production in tomato requires co-regulation by two hormone-signalling cascades. This hormonal signalling network balances between the needs for defence and development. Therefore, our study exemplifies how plants cope with the dilemma of 'defend or grow'.

# Materials and Methods

# Plant material and growth conditions

Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum cultivars Micro-Tom, Moneymaker and M82) and Nicotiana benthamiana plants were grown in a glasshouse with $24^{\circ}\mathrm{C}:18^{\circ}\mathrm{C},$ day: night temperatures and a $16\mathrm{h}:8\mathrm{h}$ , light: dark photoperiod cycle. In all experiments, second and third leaves from the shoot apex of multiple 4-5-wk-old plants were used to prepare one biological replicate. The following previously characterised mutant and transgenic lines were used: Allene Oxide Cyclase (AOC)-RNAi cv Micro-Tom (Goetz et al., 2012); jai1 cv Micro-Tom (mutant for COI1) (Li et al., 2004); MYC1-RNAi and myc1 cv Moneymaker (Xu et al., 2018); MYC2-RNAi cv M82 (Du et al., 2017); gad1TRI (Illouz-Eliaz et al., 2019), pro cv M82 (Nir et al., 2017) (Table S1).

# Plant treatments

For in vitro experiments tomato seeds were germinated on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (1/2MS) basal medium supplemented with aqueous solutions of methyl jasmonate (MeJA; 25 $\mu$ M; Xu et al., 2002) and paclobutrazol (PAC; $17\mu \mathrm{M}$ $(5\mathrm{mg}1^{-1})$ ) for 2 wk. For in planta experiments aqueous solutions of MeJA $(100\mu \mathrm{M}$ or $250~\mu \mathrm{M})$ , PAC $(34\mu \mathrm{M}$ $(10\mathrm{mg}1^{-1})$ or $68\mu \mathrm{M}$ $(20\mathrm{mg}1^{-1})$ and gibberellic acid $\mathrm{(GA_3}$ ; $100\mu \mathrm{M})$ were applied by root drench (Li et al., 1996; Livne et al., 2015). For in planta experiments, MeJA was applied to 4-wk-old tomato seedlings for $24\mathrm{h}$ , whereas PAC and GA were applied to 2-wk-old tomato seedlings with two true leaves, three times per wk, for 2 wk. In contrast with PAC or GA prolonged treatment, treatment with MeJA created severe stress to the plant in addition to suppression of JA-related transcripts. Therefore, we treated the plants with PAC and GA for 2 wk before MeJA treatment and the samples were collected at the same time from similar age plants. To avoid hormone treatment cross-contamination, the MeJA-treated plants were kept separately from the controls (i.e. in different growth rooms).

# Metabolite analysis

SGA extraction and analysis were performed as described by Itkin et al. (2013) and quantification of JA-Ile, $\mathrm{GA}_1$ and $\mathrm{GA}_4$ according to Kojima et al. (2009) with some modifications. Briefly, 200 g of ground frozen tomato leaves were extracted at $-20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ with methanol: water: formic acid $(75:20:5,\mathrm{v} / \mathrm{v})$ , containing stable isotope labelled JA-Ile, $\mathrm{GA}_1$ and $\mathrm{GA}_4$ as internal standards (IS). Extract was purified and fractionated by SPE and JA-Ile, $\mathrm{GA}_1$ and $\mathrm{GA}_4$ were detected by UPLC-TQ-MS operated in MRM mode. Quantification of JA-Ile, $\mathrm{GA}_1$ and $\mathrm{GA}_4$ was performed against external calibration curves, using analytes/IS peak ratio.

# Generation of transgenic plants

RNA-interference (RNAi) (JAZ1, JAZ6, JAZ7 and NINJA) and overexpression (OE) (JAZ1, JAZ2 and MYC2) constructs were generated using the Gateway system (Cárdenas et al., 2016). The JAR1, CRISPR/Cas9 construct was designed using two single guide RNAs, targeting two specific 20-bp sequences (selected using CRISPR-P v.2.0 crispr.hzau.edu.cn/) in the first exon of JAR1 under the control of the Arabidopsis U6-26 consensus promoter. Cloning was performed using the GoldenBraid system (Vazquez-Vilar et al., 2016) and oligonucleotides listed in Table S2.

# Quantitative real-time PCR analysis and trans-activation assays

Total RNA was isolated using TRIzol reagent and cDNA synthesised using the High-Capacity cDNA Reverse Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Santa Clara, CA, USA) with random oligonucleotides. Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed according to Cardenas et al. (2016). Transient expression (trans-activation) assays in $N.$ benthamiana leaves were

performed as described previously by Hellens et al. (2005). All cloning reactions were carried out using either by GoldenBraid cloning or by using the ClonExpress II, One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). All oligonucleotides are listed in Table S2.

# Proteomics analysis

Samples of leaf protein extract were subjected to tryptic digestion $(50\mu \mathrm{g}$ of total protein), using a modified Filter-Aided Sample Preparation protocol (FASP) (Kazachkova et al., 2021). Peptides were acidified with trifluoroacetic acid, dried in a Speedvac concentrator and analysed using nano-UPLC (Waters, Milford, MA, USA), coupled to a nano-ESI emitter $(10\mu \mathrm{M}$ tip; New Objective, Cambridge, MA, USA) and to a quadrupole Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Q Exactive Plus; Thermo Scientific, Bremen, Germany) as described in Szymanski et al. (2017). Protein identification and label-free quantification was performed using PROTEOME DISCOVERER software (v.2.2) (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The following parameters were set for searching the Sequest HT engine against the ITAG 3.2 database (SGN): (1) maximum two missed tryptic cleavages; (2) precursor mass tolerance $10~\mathrm{ppm}$ ; (3) fragment mass tolerance $0.02\mathrm{Da}$ ; and (4) fixed and variable modifications such as carbamidomethylation of Cys and oxidation of Met. The data were filtered for a minimum of two peptides matching the protein and FDR value $\leq 0.01$ (both for protein and peptide).

# Yeast-two-hybrid, split luciferase and fungal pathogen assays

The yeast-two-hybrid (Y2H) assay was performed according to Illouz-Eliaz et al. (2019). Split luciferase complementation imaging assays were performed according to the method described by Cai et al. (2018). The oligonucleotides used for cloning are listed in Table S2. Fungal infection of leaves was carried out according to Flors et al. (2007). Disease scoring was performed by measuring the diameter of the necrotic lesion after 3 d of spore application (average of 15 leaflets per genotype).

# Results

# Altered levels of JA and GA affect plant growth in coordination with SGA production in tomato

To investigate the effect of JA and GA on plant growth and SGA production, we grew tomato seedlings in vitro, supplemented with MeJA and PAC (GA biosynthesis inhibitor) for 2 wk. We first measured hypocotyl length of seedlings and found that both MeJA and PAC treatments led to severe reduction in plant growth (Fig. 1a,b). We next quantified the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine (the major tomato SGA) in seedlings and found that MeJA or PAC treatments led to significant induction of $\alpha$ -tomatine levels (Fig. 1c). We also analysed other SGAs in the same samples and found that they followed a similar pattern to the one observed for $\alpha$ -tomatine (Table S3). For better understanding of JA and GA-mediated SGAs production we evaluated $\alpha$ -tomatine

accumulation in a time course and in a dose-dependent manner. We applied two different concentrations of aqueous solution of MeJA (100 $\mu$ M and 250 $\mu$ M) and PAC (34 $\mu$ M and 68 $\mu$ M) by root drench to 2-wk-old soil-grown tomato seedlings. For SGA quantification, leaves were collected at four different time points (Fig. 1d,e). The results showed that both MeJA- and PAC-mediated SGA accumulation increased with time in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 1d,e). Together, our finding suggested that exposure to MeJA or inhibiting GA biosynthesis both reduced plant growth and induced SGA production in tomato.

# Altering the JA biosynthesis components AOC and JAR1 affects SGA production in tomato

We used a previously characterised AOC-silenced tomato line (i.e. AOC-RNAi; Table S1; Goetz et al., 2012) to examine the effect of JA level on SGA production. In AOC-RNAi leaves, the basal level of $\alpha$ -tomatine was two-fold lower compared with wild-type (WT) leaves (Fig. 2a). MeJA application induced $\alpha$ -tomatine in WT and AOC-RNAi leaves. Yet, in AOC-RNAi,

$\alpha$ -tomatine did not rise above the levels detected in untreated WT leaves (Fig. 2a). We next examined the impact of altered JA production (i.e. in AOC-RNAi) on the expression of core genes in: (1) JA biosynthesis and signalling (JAR1, COI, JAZ1, JAZ2, JAZ6, MYC1 and MYC2); (2) SGA and sterol precursors (STEROL SIDE CHAIN REDUCTASE2 (SSR2), STEROL METHYLTRANSFERASE1 (SMT1)); and (3) SGA biosynthesis (GAME11, GAME4, GAME12) and the transcriptional regulation of the SGA pathway (GAME9) (Fig. 2b). Leaves of AOC-RNAi plants showed a significant reduction in a single SGA biosynthetic gene (i.e. GAME11; Fig. 2b). In addition, MeJA application altered the expression of all the tested genes in the same way in both the AOC-RNAi and WT genotype backgrounds (Fig. 2b). For example, MeJA treatment induced the expression of AOC, JAR1, JAZs, MYC2, SSR2, GAME11, GAME4 and GAME12 genes (Fig. 2b).

Previous work by Abdelkareem et al. (2017) demonstrated that in the jai1 receptor mutant the basal level of $\alpha$ -tomatine was significantly lower compared with WT tomato leaves (Li et al., 2004; Fig. S4a; Tables S1, S3); however, they did not reveal the jai1 downstream signalling cascade associated with SGA metabolism. We found that transcript abundance of most JA signalling and SGA biosynthesis genes examined (apart from JAZ5, MYC1 and MYC2) was severely reduced in jai1 either with or without MeJA application (Fig. S4b). Apart from examining transcripts, we also checked the abundance of proteins required for $\alpha$ -tomatine biosynthesis (i.e. SSR2, GAME11, GAME4 and GAME12) using proteomic analysis and found them downregulated in jai1 compared with WT leaves (Fig. S4c). Our findings demonstrated that JAI1 mediated the activation of a set of SGA biosynthetic enzymes, and therefore regulated SGA metabolism.

# A triad of Jasmonate Zim Domain proteins (JAZ1, JAZ2 and JAZ6) are negative regulators of SGA accumulation in tomato

Jasmonate Zim Domain proteins (JAZs) are known negative regulators in the JA signalling pathway (Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007). The tomato genome contains 13 JAZ genes (Fig. S5; Chini et al., 2017); eight of them expressed in leaves (JAZ1-JAZ7 and JAZ13; Fig. S6). Previous work has shown that JAZ2, JAZ6 and JAZ7 interact with JAI1 in tomato, while JAZ1, that shows high amino acid sequence similarity with JAZ2, does not interact with JAI1 (Ishiga et al., 2013). We therefore decided to characterise the association of these genes to SGA biosynthesis (Fig. S7a,b,d). In leaves of JAZ1 and JAZ2 overexpression lines (JAZ1-OE and JAZ2-OE; generated in this study; Table S1), $\alpha$ -tomatine levels were reduced by two-fold to three-fold (Figs 2e, S7c). Levels of $\alpha$ -tomatine in JAZ1 and JAZ6 RNAi lines (JAZ1-RNAi and JAZ6-RNAi; generated in this study; Table S1) were induced compared with nontransformed WT leaves (Figs 2e, S7c) while JAZ7-RNAi (generated in this study; Table S1) leaves did not show any change in $\alpha$ -tomatine accumulation (Fig. S7e). Ishiga et al. (2013) showed that JAZ2, JAZ6 and JAZ7 interacted with the NINJA protein

from tomato. Yet, leaves of $NINJA$ -RNAi lines generated here (Table S1) did not demonstrate a significant change in $\alpha$ -tomatine level (Fig. S7e). We have also tested if the RNAi could have cross-silencing effects on other JAZs, and found no silencing effects of other green tissue specific JAZs (Fig. S7f). The results presented here highlight the role of JAZ proteins (i.e. JAZ1, JAZ2 and JAZ6) in negatively regulating tomato SGA biosynthesis. However, we could not associate the function of NINJA with SGA pathway regulation.

# MYC1 and MYC2 bHLH factors regulate SGA biosynthesis in tomato

The tomato MYC2 and MYC1 transcription factors share $76\%$ sequence identity (amino acid level; Fig. S8a,b). The role of MYC2 in regulation of the tomato SGA pathway has been suggested previously (Cardenas et al., 2016), while recently Xu et al. (2018) demonstrated the role of MYC1 in terpene biosynthesis in tomato glandular trichomes. As SGAs are not produced in the trichomes (Balcke et al., 2017), MYC1 function in SGA control remains elusive. We therefore examined leaves of MYC1 loss-offunction (RNAi silenced and myc1 CRISPR-Cas9 mutant; Xu et al., 2018; Table S1), MYC2-RNAi (Du et al., 2017; Table S1) and MYC2 overexpression (MYC2-OE; generated in this study; Table S1) lines. The results suggested that MYC1 and MYC2 both activated SGA biosynthesis in tomato and are likely to be associated with controlling the

# MYC1 and MYC2 take part in a feedback loop controlling SGA biosynthesis in tomato

We examined the ability of MYC1 and MYC2 regulators, either alone or in combination with GAME9, to activate upstream regions (i.e. upr) of SGA biosynthetic genes. We analysed the $c$ . 2 kb uprs of GAME4 and GAME11 for the presence of G-boxes responsible for MYC binding. The results showed that uprGAME4 and uprGAME11 possessed four and two MYC TF G-boxes, respectively (Notes S1). Next, we performed transient trans-activation assay in tobacco, and the results showed that MYC2 alone or in combination with GAME9 activated the uprGAME4 (the putative promoter). Additionally, MYC1 and GAME9 independently or combined were unable to activate uprGAME4 (Fig. 3e). Nonetheless, MYC1 or MYC2 in combination with GAME9 activated uprGAME11 (Fig. 3e). MYC1 alone displayed weak activation of uprGAME11 while MYC2 and GAME9 individually were unable to activate this upr (Fig. 3e).

In addition to MYC1- and MYC2-mediated activation of SGA biosynthetic genes, we analysed the $c.2\mathrm{kb}$ uprs of JAZ1, JAZ2 and JAZ6 for the presence of G-boxes responsible for MYC binding. We found that $\mathrm{upr}JAZ1$ , $\mathrm{upr}JAZ2$ and $\mathrm{upr}JAZ6$ possessed two, six and 10 MYC TF G-boxes, respectively (Notes S1). Furthermore, we also tested the activation of JAZ (JAZ2 and JAZ6) uprs and found that MYC1 or MYC2 both activated $\mathrm{upr}JAZ2$ (Fig. 3f). However, $\mathrm{upr}JAZ6$ was activated only by MYC2 but not by the MYC1 (Fig. 3f). These results also corroborated the MYC2-mediated ChIP-seq data from tomato (Du et al., 2017; Table S4). These findings suggested that, apart from controlling the SGA pathway, MYC1 and MYC2 play a dual role in regulating JAZ repressor activity. This feedback loop in the JA signalling cascade occurred through physical interaction with JAZs as well transcriptional activation of their promoters.

# GA biosynthesis negatively affects JA signalling to regulate SGA metabolism in tomato

Recognising the antagonistic crosstalk between GA and JA pathways, we decided to examine if GA biosynthesis and signalling modulated SGA production through JA. To investigate the effect of individual or combined treatments of MeJA and PAC on SGA production, we used 4-wk-old soil-grown tomato plants. Profiling of $\alpha$ -tomatine showed that MeJA and PAC application alone increased the levels of $\alpha$ -tomatine in tomato leaves by 1.3-fold and 2-fold, respectively, compared with control samples (Fig. 5a). The combined effect of PAC and MeJA boosted $\alpha$ -tomatine accumulation compared with MeJA alone, but was not greater than the PAC treatment alone (Fig. 5a). PAC treatment induced

significantly the expression of JAZ6, MYC1, SSR2, GAME1, GAME17, GAME11, and GAME12 genes (Fig. 5b). The combined application of PAC and MeJA induced a greater number of genes including JAZ6, GAME11 (both induced in MeJA or PAC treatments), JAZ1, JAZ2, MYC1, MYC2, SSR2, and GAME12 (Fig. 5b). The expression of COI1 was downregulated by MeJA either separately or in combination with PAC treatment, while the transcript level of GAME25 was reduced by MeJA or PAC treatment, either alone or in combination (Fig. 5b). These findings demonstrated that reduced GA biosynthesis induced SGA production in tomato, probably due to an interaction between GA and JA pathways.

# Altered GA perception affects SGA biosynthesis in tomato

GA perception is mediated by the GID1 receptor; three putative $GID1$ genes have been identified in the tomato genome (GID1a, GID1b1 and GID1b2). In tomato, Illouz-Eliaz et al. (2019) characterised a triple $GID1$ CRISPR/Cas9 mutant termed $gid1^{TRI}$ for GA-mediated growth response. The authors showed that $gid1^{TRI}$ is null in all three GA receptors, therefore completely insensitive to GA. To examine the role of GA perception in SGA production, we analysed $gid1^{TRI}$ plants (Illouz-Eliaz et al., 2019; Table S1). Our results showed that $gid1^{TRI}$ leaves accumulated double $\alpha$ -tomatine compared with WT (Fig. 5c; Table S3). As $gid1^{TRI}$ plants displayed a severe growth retardation phenotype, induction of SGAs could be due to a pleiotropic effect and possible alteration in water content. To examine this, we compared the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine in fresh and lyophilised leaf samples and did not find any differences (Fig. S13a). In addition to the induced $\alpha$ -tomatine level, $gid1^{TRI}$ plants showed reduced lesion diameter compared with WT, when challenged with Botrytis cinerea (Fig. S13b,c). We next studied the expression of JA signalling, SGA biosynthesis and phytosterol biosynthetic genes in leaves of $gid1^{TRI}$ plants (Fig. 5d). The gene expression data showed that, apart from COI1, JAZ1, GAME9 and GAME25, all examined JA signalling as well as sterol and SGA biosynthesis genes were induced in the leaves of $gid1^{TRI}$ (Fig. 5d). We also checked the abundance of proteins (using proteomic analysis) required for $\alpha$ -tomatine biosynthesis (i.e. SSR2, GAME11, GAME4 and GAME12) and found that, excluding GAME4, they were all upregulated in $gid1^{TRI}$ compared with WT (Fig. 5e). These results corroborated the negative impact of GA perception on SGA accumulation in tomato.

# Impact of altering GA biosynthesis on the JA signalling pathway components in tomato

In the next step, we aimed at identifying the potential JA pathway component(s), mediating the crosstalk with the GA pathway, predominantly with the DELLA. We found that PAC treatment increased $\alpha$ -tomatine level in leaves of JA-related lines exhibiting reduced SGA levels (i.e. JAZ1-OE, JAZ2-OE and MYC2-RNAi) (Fig. S14a). Yet, this was not the case for the myc1 mutant, indicating that MYC1 might be the JA signalling component (or associated with it) that is influenced by DELLA and/or other GA signalling factor(s). We next examined the interaction between JAZs, MYC1, MYC2 and the DELLA protein (using a Y2H assay). The interaction between DELLA and GID1a served as a positive control in these experiments (Illouz-Eliaz et al., 2019). The results showed that, in tomato, DELLA interacted with JAZ7, MYC1 and MYC2 proteins (Fig. 6a). As downregulation of JAZ7 (JAZ7-RNAi) did not affect the accumulation of SGAs in tomato leaves, its role in regulating the SGA pathway is not clear at this stage (Fig. S7e).

As described above, we found that MYC1 and MYC2 could activate the $\mathrm{upr}GA2ox5$ (Fig. 4c,d). To test the effect of DELLA, JAZ1 and JAZ2 on MYC1- and MYC2-mediated activation of the $\mathrm{upr}GA2ox5$ , we performed transient trans-activation assays (Fig. S14b). In the absence of MYC1 or MYC2, reporter expression was very low, whereas combining either one of them intensely activated $\mathrm{upr}GA2ox5$ driven reporter expression (Fig. 6b). Combining JAZ1 or JAZ2 with MYC1 or MYC2, or both, repressed reporter expression. Adding DELLA attenuated the repression of JAZ1 or JAZ2 on the $\mathrm{upr}GA2ox5$ driven reporter expression. The presence of DELLA alone (DELLA + MYC1/MYC2) did not affect MYC1 or MYC2 ability to activate reporter expression (Fig. 6b). Therefore, transcriptional activity of MYC1 and MYC2 was inhibited by JAZ1 and JAZ2 but promoted by DELLA. These results suggest that DELLA modulated JA signalling by affecting the MYC1 and MYC2 ability to transcriptionally regulate their target genes.

# Controlled GA and JA crosstalk is essential for balancing growth, SGA biosynthesis and defence against necrotrophic pathogen

We hypothesised that the crosstalk between GA and JA is likely to be an important factor in fine tuning SGA-related defence

and plant growth. We first used WT and pro plants treated for 2 wk with water (i.e. control), PAC and GA separately, and evaluated the internode length as a marker for growth. The results showed that the pro mutant possessed $40\%$ longer internode length compared with WT under control conditions (Fig. S15a,b). Following PAC treatment, both genotypes showed stunted growth and the internode length was reduced drastically (Fig. S15a,b), however internode length in pro plants was longer than in WT plants. By contrast, GA treatment increased internode length in WT and pro plants with no difference between the genotypes (Fig. S15a,b). We detected a weak GA response in the pro mutant, as it did not lose DELLA activity and GA response completely (Fig. S15a,b; Van Tuinen et al., 1999). We then treated the same set of plants with MeJA and analysed $\alpha$ -tomatine in leaves. The results revealed that in water-treated pro mutant, $\alpha$ -tomatine level was reduced compared with WT (Fig. 6c). MeJA application on control plants induced $\alpha$ -tomatine levels both in WT as well as in pro mutant leaves (Fig. 6c). Nevertheless, after PAC treatment, the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine was only enhanced in WT but not in the pro mutant (Fig. 6c). MeJA application on PAC-pretreated plants elevated $\alpha$ -tomatine levels only in pro mutants, but not in WT (Fig. 6c). Interestingly, in GA-pretreated plants the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine was reduced dramatically in the leaves of both WT and pro mutant, however MeJA could not induce its level in either genotype (Fig. 6c; Table S3). These results highlighted the antagonistic role of GA-mediated growth over JA-induced SGA production.

To correlate the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine with plant defence capacity, we used the same sample set in pathogen assays with the necrotrophic fungus $B$ . cinerea. The results showed that, without MeJA treatment, the pro mutant was hypersusceptible compared with WT (Fig. 6d,e); however, MeJA made both genotypes less susceptible to pathogen infection (Fig. 6d,e). PAC pretreatment resulted in reduced susceptibility against infection in WT plants, with or without MeJA application (Fig. 6d,e). However, for the pro mutant, PAC pretreatment alone could not reduce susceptibility, but only when combined with MeJA treatment the reduced susceptibility was restored (Fig. 6d,e). Most interestingly, GA-pretreated plants (either WT or pro) were hypersusceptible to $B$ . cinerea infection and MeJA treatment could not recover this phenotype (Fig. 6d,e). Our findings indicated that GA-mediated growth in tomato is at the expense of a JA-mediated defence response and the induction of SGA production.

# Discussion

Production of SGAs in the course of development or under stress conditions requires balancing with the metabolic needs for plant growth. Various reports have suggested that the interplay between JA and GA signalling adjusts between plant defence and growth (Yang et al., 2012; Campos et al., 2016; Major et al., 2020). In Arabidopsis, Navarro et al. (2008) reported the relationship between GA and JA signalling pathways in which DELLA proteins were found to promote JA responses. While JA is widely recognised as a signal for the biosynthesis of protective

specialised metabolites, GA is primarily associated with plant growth and development (Wasternack & Strnad, 2019). Whereas the broad role of JA in regulating SGA biosynthesis in tomato has been reported previously, a key discovery of this study is that

JA acts antagonistically with the GA signalling pathway and impacts GA metabolism genes to control the production of SGA defence metabolites in tomato (Fig. 7). This regulatory network most probably entails the action of additional hormone classes

Unlike JA, the molecular mechanism underlying GA-mediated modulation of plant-specialised metabolism remains under investigated. We demonstrated that inhibition of GA biosynthesis in tomato leads to enhancement of SGA production and that GID1 receptors are crucial to suppress SGA accumulation in tomato. Related to this, Illouz-Eliaz et al. (2019) recently reported that disrupting all GID1 receptors (i.e. $gid1^{TRI}$ mutant) in tomato caused excessive stunting due to overaccumulation of DELLA. Downregulation of DELLA (i.e. pro mutant) reduced the level of $\alpha$ -tomatine in tomato. Previous work in Arabidopsis suggests that this result in tomato is probably due to higher abundance of free JAZ repressors, as DELLA and MYC2 compete for physical binding to JAZs (Hou et al., 2010; Wild et al., 2012). Notably, our work in tomato showed that DELLA was able to bind a single JAZ protein (i.e. JAZ7) out of those expressed in tomato green tissues. Apart from JAZ, DELLA also binds both tomato MYC factors (MYC1 and MYC2) to modulate their downstream transcriptional activity. Increasing DELLA levels (by inhibition of GA biosynthesis through PAC treatment) enhanced SGA levels in JAZ1-OE and JAZ2-OE and MYC2-RNAi, but not in myc1 plants. This suggests that DELLA-modulated SGA production is most likely to be through MYC1 and not MYC2. Therefore, we suggest that DELLA dosage is crucial for appropriate modulation of SGA production by integration with JA signalling components (i.e. JAZ7, MYC1) in balance with DELLA-mediated plant growth (Fig. 7).

SGAs are produced constitutively, are several other known classes of secondary metabolites (e.g. glucosinolates in Arabidopsis; Howe et al., 2018), to the best of our knowledge in all green tissues of the tomato plant. In addition, biosynthesis of these defence metabolites is induced in response to JA-mediated activation of MYC1 and MYC2, suggesting their role in both constitutive as well as JA-dependent defence responses (Swinnen et al., 2020). This resembles the proposed mechanism for the regulation of glucosinolate accumulation in Arabidopsis (Schweizer et al., 2013). According to the model, in unstimulated

plants, JAZ repressors inhibit the interaction between MYCs (2, 3 and 4) and MYB type transcription factors (e.g. MYB34) regulating glucosinolate biosynthesis. This weakens the activation of glucosinolate pathway gene promoters by the MYC-MYB complexes. However, constitutive glucosinolate production occurs by MYBs binding to MYCs as part of the competition with JAZs (Major et al., 2017; Millard et al., 2019). Upon herbivory and activation of the JA signalling pathway, JAZ repressors are degraded and tight interaction between MYCs and MYBs leads to elevated glucosinolate levels. In a previous study, we showed the cooperative action of MYC2 and GAME9 in the regulation of SGA biosynthetic genes (Cárdenas et al., 2016). We suggest that, for SGAs, the competition between the AP2/ERF-type GAME9 factor (instead of a MYB type for glucosinolates) and JAZ proteins on binding to MYC1 and MYC2 is key for the production of SGAs under constitutive and JA-induced conditions.

In addition to mediating JA signalling, MYC2 binds directly to the DELLA RGA-LIKE3 (RGL3) promoter and regulates GA signalling in Arabidopsis (Wild et al., 2012). Our work shows that in tomato both MYC1 and MYC2 could transcriptionally activate the DELLA upstream region. However, we discovered that, in tomato, apart from acting through DELLA, MYCs transcriptionally regulate GA2-OXIDASEs (i.e. $GA2ox5$ and $GA2ox7$ and a $GA20$ -OXIDASE (i.e. $GA20ox2$ ) (Fig. S2a). GA2-OXIDASEs and GA20-OXIDASE2 are involved in the degradation and biosynthesis of active GAs, respectively, and therefore modulate GA-mediated growth. In addition to GA, alteration in sterol composition may serve as one explanation for the growth phenotype observed in the myc1 plants. It has been previously shown by Itkin et al. (2011) that altered sterol levels can lead to retarded growth phenotypes, as observed in GAME1-silenced plants. Therefore, specifically in tomato, the findings here suggest that the action of JA master regulators (i.e. MYCs) in modulating GA-regulated plant growth is associated with sterol metabolism. Following the above findings, we propose a model depicting the JA- and GA-mediated regulatory network of SGA biosynthesis in tomato (Fig. 7). Upon perception of the JA signal, COI1 interacts with the JAZ proteins and presents them to the 26S/UPS (ubiquitin proteasome system) for degradation. This removes the suppression over MYC1 and MYC2 and therefore induces the expression of SGA biosynthetic genes. Conversely, in a regulatory feedback loop, increased MYC1 and MYC2 activity upregulates $JAZs$ transcript levels and the newly synthesised JAZ proteins suppress the activities of both MYC1 and MYC2, through protein-protein interactions. We also introduce a role for GA signalling in SGA production, largely through the DELLA protein. DELLA might antagonise MYC1 and/or MYC2 activity by competing for the same binding sites on JAZ proteins or by competitive binding with MYC1 and/or MYC2, consequently regulating SGA production. In addition to the likely redundant function of MYC1 and MYC2 in SGA control, we demonstrate a yet undescribed role of these factors in transcriptional regulation of $GA2$ -OXIDASEs and modulation of GA-mediated plant growth. Collectively, our work reveals the mechanistic basis of JA-GA interplay in regulating the biosynthesis of SGAs to balance the cost of defensive specialised metabolite production with growth.
