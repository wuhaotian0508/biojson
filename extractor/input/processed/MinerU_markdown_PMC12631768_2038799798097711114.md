# Abstract

1-Deoxynojirimycin (DNJ) is an iminosugar with notable pharmacological properties, and mulberry (Morus spp.) represents the richest natural source of this compound, particularly in young leaves and roots. Despite its tissue-specific distribution, the transcriptional regulatory mechanisms underlying its biosynthesis remain poorly understood. In this study, we integrated transcriptomic profiling, molecular assays, and phytohormone analyses to uncover a multilayered transcriptional network controlling DNJ biosynthesis in mulberry. Weighted gene co-expression network analysis identified a DNJ-associated module (MEblue) that includes 4 potential transcription factors: zinc-finger homeodomain-6 (ZFHD6), HOMEODOMAIN GLABROUS11 (HDG11), TEOSINTE BRANCHED1/ CYCLOIDEA/PROLIFERATING CELL FACTOR-14 (TCP14), and ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA HOMEBOX-40 (ATHB40). ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40 directly interacted with and activated the MnGutB1 promoter, a key biosynthetic gene. Although HDG11 did not target MnGutB1 directly, it transcriptionally activated ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40, and formed protein complexes with TCP14 or ATHB40, thereby indirectly promoting DNJ biosynthesis. Furthermore, exogenous application of trans-zeatin riboside or abscisic acid induced HDG11 and MnGutB1 expression, concomitant with elevated DNJ levels. Our findings identify an HDG11-centered regulatory hierarchy that integrates phytohormonal and developmental signals to regulate DNJ biosynthesis, offering insights for metabolic engineering of DNJ production in plants. 

# Introduction

Iminosugars, prevalent as specialized metabolites, have molecular structures similar to sugars, with a nitrogen atom substituting for an oxygen atom in the cyclic structure (Parida et al. 2023). Due to their biological properties, iminosugars, with 1-deoxynojirimycin (DNJ) as a major representative, have undergone extensive clinical evaluation for the treatment of various acute and chronic diseases (Dragutan et al. 2012), driving significant research into the physiological activities of DNJ. As an α-glucosidase inhibitor, DNJ exhibits therapeutic potential for various diseases, including antihyperglycemic (Newbrun et al. 1983; Yatsunami et al. 2007; Kuriyama et al. 2008), anti-obesity (Kong et al. 2008; Kojima et al. 2010; Do et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2015), and antiviral (Durantel et al. 2001; Papandréou et al. 2002; Tanaka et al. 2006; Lazar et al. 2007; Chang et al. 2009). 

Although DNJ has been identified in microorganisms (Hardick et al. 1992; Paek et al. 1997; Wei et al. 2011; Lee 2013; Yamagishi et al. 2017) and certain plant species, such as Hyacinthus orientalis (Asano et al. 1998), Adenophora triphylla var. japonica (Asano et al. 2000), and Commelina communis var. hortensis (Shibano et al. 2001), its concentrations generally remain below levels feasible for commercial production. In contrast, mulberry leaves contain significantly higher DNJ levels (Nuengchamnong et al. 2007). Furthermore, DNJ accumulation in mulberry exhibits both tissue specificity, with young shoots containing substantially higher 

levels than mature leaves (Kimura et al. 2007; Yang et al. 2023), and environmental responsiveness, such as seasonal (Kimura et al. 2007; Nakanishi et al. 2011; Sugiyama et al. 2017; Han et al. 2018) and temperature-dependent regulation (Nakanishi et al. 2011; Sugiyama et al. 2017). These characteristics position mulberry as a highly promising natural source of DNJ for pharmaceutical applications. However, despite the high DNJ content in young leaves, their limited biomass poses a significant obstacle to largescale production. As the leaves mature, DNJ levels decline sharply (Vichasilp et al. 2012; Hu et al. 2013; Yang et al. 2023), thereby reducing the efficiency of mulberry as a sustainable DNJ source. Moreover, current understanding of the genetic and regulatory mechanisms governing DNJ biosynthesis in mulberry remains limited. A comprehensive elucidation of these mechanisms is crucial for increasing the yield of DNJ. Despite emerging insights into DNJ biosynthesis genes (Yang et al. 2023), the transcriptional regulatory architecture driving spatial-temporal accumulation remains elusive. 

The biosynthetic pathway of DNJ in plants is not well understood. Wang et al. (2018a) proposed that DNJ alkaloids are lysinederived polyhydroxy piperidine compounds, aligning with the early phytochemical synthesis theory by Robinson (1917). Furthermore, a study identified 5 CYP450 and 2 methyltransferase transcripts significantly correlated with seasonal variations in DNJ content in mulberry leaves, indicating potential involvement 

of these enzyme families in converting $\Delta ^ { 1 }$ -piperidine to piperidine (Liu et al. 2020). However, the mechanisms for the subsequent conversion of piperideine to DNJ are still largely speculative (Parida et al. 2023). This lysine-origin hypothesis for the DNJ carbon skeleton differs substantially from previously reported pathways in other plant species; Shibano et al. (2001) performed a [1-13C]glucose feeding experiment in C. communis, employing $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ NMR spectroscopy to trace carbon incorporation into metabolites. This study proposed a sugar-derived biosynthetic pathway where glucose undergoes C1-imination, followed by redox reactions to form nojirimycin (NJ), which is then dehydrated and reduced to yield DNJ. A subsequent study quantified DNJ and its precursors, 2-amino-2-deoxy-D-mannitol (ADM) and NJ, in various mulberry tissues, identifying a gene, MnGutB1, as crucial for DNJ biosynthesis. Functional validation through in vitro enzyme assays and in planta experiments confirmed MnGutB1’s role in the DNJ biosynthetic pathway (Yang et al. 2023). The study showed that varying expression levels of the MnGutB1 gene resulted in a progressive reduction of DNJ content as leaf position decreased. These findings support the previous hypothesis that variations in the expression of mulberry DNJ biosynthetic enzymes are the primary factors contributing to differences in DNJ content at different stages of leaf maturation, with young leaves exhibiting higher enzymatic activity (Hu et al. 2013; Parida et al. 2023). MnGutB1 is essential for mulberry DNJ biosynthesis, serving as a key focus for research into the regulatory mechanisms of DNJ production in mulberry. 

Phytohormones are crucial regulators in the biosynthesis of specialized metabolites. However, studies investigating the regulatory role of phytohormones in the biosynthesis of DNJ in mulberry remain limited. Despite extensive research into DNJ biosynthesis mechanisms and environmental factors, progress has been limited. Here we reveal a hierarchical transcriptional framework coordinating phytohormonal cues with DNJ metabolism in mulberry. The study identified HDG11 as a key transcription factor (TF) responsive to trans-zeatin riboside (tZR) and abscisic acid (ABA) signals. The TFs ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40 modulate DNJ synthesis in mulberry. Our findings reveal a regulatory mechanism where HDG11 enhances DNJ synthesis, offering valuable insights into the relationship between tZR and ABA in regulating DNJ synthesis in mulberry. 

# Results

# Identification of co-expression modules associated with DNJ

DNJ was first isolated and identified from the leaves and roots of mulberry (Yagi et al. 1976; Asano et al. 1994). Consistent with previous reports, DNJ levels were highest in buds and roots, while undetectable in callus tissues. Younger leaves and their purified extracts exhibited significantly higher DNJ concentrations than mature leaves (Yang et al. 2023). To explore the transcriptional regulation of DNJ biosynthesis, transcriptomic data from 7 tissues of Morus notabilis were analyzed. Using BMKCloud (www.biocloud. net), weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) identified 22 gene co-expression clusters. The eigengene expression patterns across tissues are visualized in Fig. 1A, facilitating visualization of tissue-specific associations (Supplementary Fig. S1). Among these modules, the MEblue module showed a strong correlation with DNJ biosynthesis, with genes predominantly expressed in buds (Fig. 1B). This module comprises 1,223 genes (Supplementary Table S1), including the key DNJ biosynthetic gene MnGutB1 and 156 TFs (Supplementary Table S2). To identify potential TFs regulating MnGutB1, the 2,500-bp upstream region of 

its transcription start site was analyzed for TF-binding motifs using PlantRegMap (Tian et al. 2019). A total of 78 TFs were predicted to bind MnGutB1 promoter region (Supplementary Table S3). By intersecting these TFs with those from the MEblue module, 8 candidate regulators were identified: ZFHD6, TCP14, ATHB40, MYB27, ATHB51, HDG11, GATA4, and GT2. 

Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was conducted to analyze the tissue-specific expression of MnGutB1 and the 8 candidate TFs (Fig. 2, A to I). Co-expression analysis revealed a strong positive correlation between MnGutB1 and HDG11 (Pearson’s $\rho = 0 . 9 7$ ), followed by TCP14 $\left( \rho = 0 . 8 5 \right)$ and ZFHD6 $( \rho =$ 0.72). ATHB40 showed a moderate correlation $\left( \rho = 0 . 6 4 \right)$ ), while the remaining 4 TFs did not exhibit significant positive correlations (Fig. 2J). The 4 selected TFs are as follows: HDG11, a homeobox-leucine zipper protein; TCP14, a Class I TCP family member; ZFHD6, a zinc-finger homeodomain protein; and ATHB40, a homeobox-leucine zipper protein. For subcellular localization analysis, the coding sequences of the 4 selected TFs were fused upstream of the enhanced green fluorescent protein (EGFP) reporter gene. In Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of onion epidermal cells, green fluorescence from the 35S::GFP positive control was detected in the nucleus and plasma membrane. Fluorescence from EGFP fused to the TFs ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 was solely observed in the nucleus, confirming their role as nuclear-localized TFs (Supplementary Fig. S2). 

# ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40 directly bind to the MnGutB1 promoter

A dual-luciferase reporter system was applied to verify ZFHD6, TCP14, ATHB40, and HDG11 directly affect MnGutB1 expression in planta. Effector and reporter constructs (Fig. 3A) were co-expressed in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves using Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40 significantly increased firefly luciferase (LUC) activity compared to the empty vector control, while HDG11 had no significant impact (Fig. 3B). These results suggest that ZFHD6, TCP14, and ATHB40 possess transcriptional activity in N. benthamiana. 

Analysis of the MnGutB1 promoter using PlantRegMap (Supplementary Table S3) identified 4 potential cis-regulatory elements and their predicted TF interactions: HD-ZIP-1 (ACACCAATCATGAATGATTTA) for ATHB40, ZF-HD-1 (ATCAGTGATTAATTA) for ZFHD6, HD-ZIP-2 (TCATTAAATAA) for HDG11, and TCP-1 (GGATATAATATACGTCCCAC) for TCP14. These elements were considered for subsequent functional analyses of promoter–TF interactions. A yeast 1-hybrid (Y1H) assay was conducted to study promoter–TF interactions, 4 putative cisregulatory elements—HD-ZIP-1, ZF-HD-1, HD-ZIP-2, and TCP-1— were cloned into the pAbAi vector to generate bait plasmids (Fig. 3C). All constructs grew normally on medium containing $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n g / m L } }$ aureobasidin A (AbA) but were inhibited at $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ (Supplementary Fig. S3A). Thus, $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ AbA was selected as the minimal concentration for subsequent screening. After genomic integration of the cis-element-pAbAi constructs and verification by autoactivation tests, bait strains were transformed with TF constructs and cultured on SD/-Leu medium supplemented with $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ AbA. The Y1H assay revealed that ATHB40 specifically bound to the HD-ZIP-1 motif, ZFHD6 selectively recognized the ZF-HD-1 motif, and TCP14 interacted with the TCP-1 motif. # Effect of ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 downregulation on DNJ accumulation in mulberry leaves

Given the high levels of DNJ in mulberry leaves and the elevated expression of the TFs ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 in these tissues (Fig. 2), we employed virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) to investigate the regulatory roles of these TFs in DNJ biosynthesis. The VIGS procedure was conducted following the method described by Xin et al. (2021) to specifically silence the genes encoding ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 in mulberry leaves. This approach sought to clarify the impact of these TFs on MnGutB1 expression, a crucial gene in DNJ biosynthesis, and its subsequent effect on DNJ accumulation. 

Following successful gene silencing, 3 independent lines were selected from each TF-silenced population for further analysis. Transcript levels of the targeted TFs were significantly reduced in the respective VIGS lines compared to plants transformed with the empty vector control. Notably, the downregulation of ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, or ATHB40 resulted in a substantial decrease in MnGutB1 expression. Metabolite profiling revealed a significant reduction in DNJ content across all 4 TF-silenced lines compared to the controls (Fig. 4, A to D). 

Collectively, these findings demonstrate that ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 function as positive regulators of DNJ biosynthesis in mulberry. These TFs likely contribute to DNJ production by activating the transcription of MnGutB1, a central gene. 

# ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 activate MnGutB1 expression in mulberry roots

To validate the regulatory functions of the TFs ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 in mulberry, we employed an Agrobacterium rhizogenes-mediated hairy root transformation system for their overexpression. Recombinant vectors (pZYGC-TF) tagged with EGFP and encoding ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, or ATHB40 were constructed and introduced into A. rhizogenes strain K599, as performed in the protocol by Yang et al. (2023). The bacterial suspension was administered at the intersection of the true leaves and cotyledons. After 8 wk of growth under soil conditions, hairy roots were harvested from the injection sites. Strongly fluorescent transgenic roots were selected, and total RNA was extracted from frozen, ground tissues for RT-qPCR analysis. RT-qPCR results confirmed significantly elevated expression of ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 in the corresponding transgenic hairy roots relative to the control (Fig. 4, E to H). Notably, the overexpression of each TF led to a pronounced upregulation of MnGutB1 expression. The findings suggest that ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40 act as transcriptional activators in the DNJ biosynthetic pathway by enhancing the transcription of the crucial gene MnGutB1. This regulatory function might explain the tissue-specific differences in DNJ content across various mulberry organs.

The results demonstrated that HDG11 did not interact with ZFHD6 (Fig. 5B) but interacted with TCP14 and ATHB40 (Fig. 5C). Notably, yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) signals were exclusively detected in the nuclei of onion epidermal cells 

when HDG11-nYFP was co-expressed with either TCP14-cYFP or ATHB40-cYFP (Fig. 5D). # Discussion

# Transcriptional regulation underlying tissue-specific DNJ biosynthesis in mulberry

Despite the recognized pharmaceutical value of plant-derived DNJ (Qu et al. 2021; Parida et al. 2023), its commercial utilization remains constrained by low natural abundance and the high cost of extraction relative to chemical synthesis. Mulberry, the richest natural source of DNJ (Nuengchamnong et al. 2007), is clinically approved for diabetes treatment (Qu et al. 2021). Enhancing DNJ content in mulberry and lowering production costs are essential challenges to enable its wider application. The initiation of specialized metabolite production is primarily regulated at the transcriptional level by specific TFs. Endogenous signals, activated during specific developmental stages or in specialized anatomical structures, modulate the function of these TFs and the expression of genes in metabolic pathways (Mehrtens et al. 2005; Suttipanta et al. 2011; Colinas and Goossens 2018; Xu et al. 2023; Lin et al. 2024). To date, the biosynthetic and regulatory pathways governing DNJ accumulation in mulberry remain poorly understood. 

Previous research has shown that DNJ accumulates at higher levels in young, apical tissues of mulberry leaves, suggesting that these tissues may possess enhanced biosynthetic capacity for DNJ (Vichasilp et al. 2012; Hu et al. 2013; Sugiyama et al. 2016). However, the tissue-specific expression of enzymes involved in DNJ biosynthesis remains largely uncharacterized. DNJ was initially isolated from mulberry root bark (Yagi et al. 1976), and while it is abundant in this tissue, it is absent in callus cultures (Ferrari et al. 1999), indicating that DNJ biosynthesis is tightly regulated and tissue-specific. In Artemisia annua, JA signaling enhances artemisinin biosynthesis by inducing the degradation of AaJAZ8, thereby allowing the AaORA–AaTCP14 complex to activate DBR2 and ALDH1 expression specifically in trichomes (Ma et al. 2018). Our findings reveal that DNJ biosynthesis in mulberry is intricately modulated by phytohormones, with 6 phytohormones—I3C, IAA, tZR, SA, JA, and ABA—showing significant correlations with both HDG11 expression and DNJ accumulation (Supplementary Table S4). A noticeable discrepancy was observed between the moderate induction of MaGutB1 and the relatively limited increase in DNJ accumulation. Based on prior studies showing mulberry’s accumulation of DNJ-derived alkaloids like N-methyl-DNJ and 2-O-α-dgalactopyranosyl-DNJ (Asano et al. 1994), along with our previous research linking MaGutB1 expression to DNJ derivative levels in various mulberry tissues (Yang et al. 2023), we hypothesize that DNJ is effectively transformed into its derivatives in mulberry. In summary, our study identifies HDG11-centered transcriptional networks as critical mediators of ABA and tZR signaling in the regulation of DNJ biosynthesis (Fig. 7). The application of ABA or tZR has been shown to enhance DNJ production. Future studies should focus on identifying the ideal concentrations and combinations of these phytohormones. Such efforts will not only deepen our understanding of metabolic regulation in plants but also contribute to the biotechnological optimization of therapeutic compound production. Our research offers important insights into the interaction between signaling pathways in DNJ biosynthesis and identifies a candidate gene for metabolic engineering to enhance DNJ production. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Chuansang (M. notabilis) plants were grown at the Mulberry Germplasm Nursery, Southwest University, Chongqing, in either a greenhouse or growth chamber. The controlled conditions 

included a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod, $7 5 \%$ relative humidity, and temperatures between 23 and $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . M. alba cv. Guiyou12 mulberry cultivar, transgenic mulberry lines, and N. benthamiana were cultivated under identical environmental conditions in an incubation room. These plants were subsequently used for metabolic analyses, RT-qPCR, and gene cloning experiments. 

# Transcriptome sequencing and analysis

Seven tissue types from M. notabilis—including young buds, leaves at different developmental stages, leaf veins, stems, roots, and in vitro-induced callus—were collected for RNA extraction. RNA-Seq libraries were prepared following standard protocols and sequenced by BMK Company in Beijing, China. Clean reads were aligned to the M. notabilis reference genome for quality control and structural analysis, enabling the detection of alternative splicing events, genes, and refinement of gene models. Gene expression levels were quantified as Reads Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were determined based on a false discovery rate below 0.05, an absolute log fold change greater than 1, and a P-value under 0.05. Functional annotation and enrichment analyses of DEGs utilized BLASTx alignments against public protein databases (NR, Swiss-Prot, KEGG) and were categorized by KEGG pathways and Gene Ontology terms. Supplementary Table S5 lists all primers utilized for RT-qPCR and gene cloning. 

# Analysis of DNJ levels in mulberry plant tissues

The DNJ content in mulberry tissues was analyzed using the method outlined by Yang et al. (2023). Specifically, leaves of M. notabilis (from VIGS assays) and hairy roots of M. alba (overexpression lines of M. alba cv. Guiyou12) were collected for analysis. Tissues were thoroughly washed, extracted, and analyzed using LC-ESI-MS/MS on a UHPLC system connected to a Q Exactive Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). Chromatographic separation was performed on a BEH-C18 column with a water–acetonitrile mobile phase containing $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid. Data acquisition and quantification were carried out under optimized full MS and data-dependent ${ \mathrm { M } } { \mathrm { S } } ^ { 2 }$ (dd-MS2 ) conditions. Instrument calibration and tuning followed standard protocols. For absolute quantification, a series of DNJ standard solutions at known concentrations were prepared to generate a calibration curve by plotting peak area versus concentration. Sample DNJ concentrations were then calculated by interpolating their peak areas on the standard curve. All measurements were performed in triplicate to ensure accuracy and reproducibility. 

# Subcellular localization of ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40

The open reading frames (ORFs) of candidate genes, excluding stop codons, were cloned into the pZYGC vector with an EGFP tag at the C-terminus, following the method outlined by Lv et al. (2018). Onion epidermal cells were transiently transformed with recombinant plasmids using Agrobacterium-mediated transfection. Samples were examined using an FV1200 laser scanning confocal microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan) after 2 d of culture. 

# Dual-luciferase assays

TF gene fragments were inserted into the pGreenII62-SK vector, while a 2500-bp promoter fragment of the MnGutB1 gene was cloned into the pGreenII0800-LUC vector. Each recombinant plasmid was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 and co-transformed with the helper plasmid pSoup19. Effector 

and reporter strains (2 mL each) were combined, incubated for $2 \mathrm { { h } }$ , and introduced into the leaves of 8-wk-old N. benthamiana plants. Dual-luciferase assays were conducted after 48 to $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ of dark incubation, following the protocol outlined by Fu et al. (2021). Promoter activity was assessed by determining the LUC/ REN activity ratio, comparing firefly luciferase to Renilla luciferase. All experiments were conducted with 3 technical replicates. 

# Y1H assays

Candidate TFs used in Y1H assays were selected based on the Venn diagram shown in Fig. 1C, representing genes in the MEblue module predicted to bind the MnGutB1 promoter. The Y1H assay was conducted using the Matchmaker Gold Y1H system (Clontech). The recombinant cis-element bait vector pAbAi was linearized with BstBI and transformed into Y1HGold competent cells using the lithium acetate method. Positive transformants were selected on SD/-Ura medium and subjected to an autoactivation assay to determine the minimum inhibitory concentration of aureobasidin A (AbA). The verified bait strains were then expanded, and competent cells were prepared for subsequent transformation with either the empty vector pGADT7 or pGADT7 fused with candidate TFs. Transformants were selected on SD/-Leu medium and further cultured on SD/-Leu plates supplemented with minimum inhibitory concentration of AbA. The growth status of yeast colonies under selective conditions was compared to evaluate the specific binding of TFs to the cis-elements, following the procedure described by Wang et al. (2023). 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assays

The MnGutB1 promoter regions were PCR-amplified from M. notabilis genomic DNA and biotinylated using the Biotin $3 ^ { \prime }$ End Labeling Kit (Beyotime) according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. In the EMSA, $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of TF protein was incubated with $1 0 \mathrm { n u }$ biotin-labeled oligonucleotides in Beyotime binding buffer for 20 min at room temperature. In competitive EMSA, varying concentrations of unlabeled positive or negative oligonucleotides were introduced to the reaction mixture 2 h before adding a fixed quantity of labeled positive oligonucleotides. The reaction mixtures were separated on 1-mm-thick, $1 5 \%$ nondenaturing polyacrylamide gels and then transferred to Immobilon-P SQ PVDF membranes (Millipore). DNA oligonucleotides were UV crosslinked to the membrane, and labeled probes were identified with the Light Shift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime). 

# Y2H assays and Y3H assays

Y2H assay was performed as described by Yuan et al. (2023). The full-length coding sequences of TFs were cloned into pGADT7 and pGBKT7 vectors and transformed into Saccharomyces cerevisiae Y2HGold cells using lithium acetate. The transformed cells were grown on selective synthetic dropout (SD) medium without leucine, tryptophan, histidine, and adenine (SD/-Leu/-Trp/- His/-Ade) to evaluate potential protein–protein interactions. 

Y3H assays were performed as described by Kariyazono et al. (2019) to assess the effect of HDG11 on the interaction between TCP14 and ATHB40, given that TCP14 and ATHB40 alone had previously been shown not to interact. ATHB40 and HDG11 were cloned into the MCSI and MCSII sites of the pBridge vector as baits, while TCP14 was cloned into the pGADT7 vector as prey. AH109 yeast cells were transformed using a standard PEG/LiAc method with pGADT7 and pBridge-ATHB40-HDG11 constructs. Briefly, 100 µL competent cells were mixed with plasmids and carrier 

DNA, incubated on ice, heat-shocked at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , washed, and plated on SD/-L/-W medium. Single colonies were selected to assess bait expression and auto-activation on various selective media, including SD/-L/-W-H, SD/-L/-W-M, SD/-L/-W-H-M, and SD/-L/-W-H-M supplemented with 10 mM 3-AT. For the interaction assay, pGADT7-TCP14 and pBridge-ATHB40-HDG11 constructs were co-transformed into AH109 cells and initially plated on SD/-Leu/-Trp selective medium. Serial dilutions $\mathrm { \Delta O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 2$ , 0.02, and 0.002) were prepared, and cell growth was monitored on various selective media, including SD/-L/-W-H, SD/-L/-W-M, SD/-L/-W-H-M, and SD/-L/-W-H-M supplemented with 10 mM 3-AT, at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 48 to 96 h. Growth on selective media indicates a positive interaction among the 3 proteins, whereas absence of growth suggests no interaction, allowing assessment of HDG11’s effect on the TCP14–ATHB40 interaction. 

# BiFC assay

The BiFC assay was performed as described previously (Guan et al. 2024). The open reading frames of ZFHD6, HDG11, TCP14, and ATHB40, without the termination codon, were amplified and cloned into the pSPYNE and pSPYCE vectors. Each plasmid combination was separately transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Onion epidermal cells were infiltrated with Agrobacterium suspensions containing the respective constructs and incubated in the dark for $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ . Afterward, the tissues were transferred to light conditions for 48 h. A laser-scanning confocal microscope was used to detect YFP signals. 

# VIGS assays

VIGS assays were performed in M. notabilis seedlings according to Yang et al. (2023). A 300-bp segment of the TF genes was inserted into the 2mDNA1 vector. The plasmids MMDaV, 2mDNA1, and 2mDNA1-TF, associated with Mulberry mosaic dwarf-associated virus (Yang et al. 2018), were each introduced into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. A 1:1 (v/v) mixture of MMDaV and 2mDNA1 or its derivatives was introduced into the cotyledons of 1-wk-old M. notabilis seedlings using a needleless 1-mL syringe. The seedlings were moved to a greenhouse following a 24-h dark incubation. Leaves were collected 30 d after infiltration for analysis using RT-qPCR and DNJ content assessment. 

# Transgenic system for mulberry hairy roots

Utilizing optimized transformation conditions from prior studies (Meng et al. 2019; Liu et al. 2021; Yang et al. 2023), the complete TF gene sequence was cloned into the pZYGC vector and introduced into A. rhizogenes K599. The bacterial suspension was injected at the junction of true leaves and cotyledons in M. alba cv. Guiyou12 seedlings. After 8 wk of growth, the entire root system of the injected mulberry seedlings was collected. Roots were chosen using a fluorescence microscope, then quickly froze in liquid nitrogen for grinding and analysis of TF gene expression levels and DNJ content. 

# Phytohormone analysis

For phytohormone profiling, 7 tissue types from M. notabilis— including young buds, leaves at different developmental stages, stems, roots, and in vitro-induced callus—were collected for sampling. All phytohormone assays were conducted by BMK Company (Beijing, China) following the protocol of Šimura et al. (2018). Samples were lyophilized, ground, and extracted using $5 0 \%$ acetonitrile containing isotopically labeled internal standards. Following cycles of vortexing, sonication, and homogenization, 

the samples were incubated at $- 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and then centrifuged. Supernatants were dried under nitrogen, reconstituted in $1 0 \%$ acetonitrile, and filtered for analysis. The UHPLC-MS/MS analysis utilized an EXIONLC system paired with a SCIEX 6500 QTRAP+ mass spectrometer featuring an electrospray ionization source. A Waters ACQUITY UPLC CSH C18 column facilitated chromatographic separation, utilizing $0 . 0 1 \%$ formic acid in water and acetonitrile as the mobile phases. MRM transitions were optimized by flow injection analysis, with the most sensitive Q1/Q3 pairs used for quantification and confirmation. Data acquisition and processing were conducted using MultiQuant 3.0.3 software. 

# Phytohormone treatment

Four-week-old Morus alba cv. Guiyou12 seedlings were used for phytohormone treatments. Six phytohormones—ABA $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , tZR $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , methyl jasmonate (MeJA, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , SA $( 3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , IAA $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , and I3C $( 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ —were dissolved in distilled water. Water was applied as the mock control. Phytohormones were applied via foliar spray until runoff, and leaves were harvested at 0, 2, 4, 6, and $^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ posttreatment for subsequent gene expression analysis and DNJ quantification. All experiments were conducted in triplicate under controlled growth conditions $\cdot 2 5 \pm 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod, $70 \%$ relative humidity). 

# RNA isolation and RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from plant tissues using CTAB-pBIOZOL reagent (Bioer, Hangzhou, China) following the manufacturer’s protocol. mRNA was subsequently purified with oligo(dT)-coated magnetic beads. First-strand cDNA synthesis was carried out using the PrimeScript RT reagent Kit with gDNA Eraser (Perfect Real Time; Takara), followed by second-strand synthesis. Quantitative PCR was performed with SYBR Green PCR Master Mix on the Step One and Step OnePlus real-time PCR systems (Applied Biosystems). The mulberry ribosomal protein L15 (RPL15) gene served as an internal control to normalize the expression of target genes (Qi et al. 2014). All reactions were conducted in 3 technical replicates, and relative gene expression levels were calculated using the comparative $2 ^ { - \triangle \triangle \mathrm { c t } }$ method.
