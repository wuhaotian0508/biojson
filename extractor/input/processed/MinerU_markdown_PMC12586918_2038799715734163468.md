# ABSTRACT

Boron toxicity (BT) is a significant environmental stressor that negatively affects plant development, yet its molecular mechanisms remain poorly understood. Interestingly, certain toxic concentrations of boron trigger hypocotyl elongation, suggesting a complex hormonal response. In this study, we focus on the role of brassinosteroids (BRs) in mediating this atypical growth. Our findings demonstrate that BT stimulates BR biosynthesis while simultaneously suppressing its inactivation, resulting in sustained BR activity throughout seedling development. Furthermore, we provide evidence that BT disrupts the normal BR negative feedback regulation, potentially converting it into a positive feedback mechanism that amplifies the elongation response. We also show that this response shares mechanistic similarities with thermomorphogenesis, particularly in its reliance on COP1, PIF4, and BR signalling pathways. Loss‐of‐function mutants of COP1 and PIF4 exhibited reduced hypocotyl elongation, underscoring their essential roles in this process. Although further research is needed to fully clarify the molecular details, our work reveals a previously unrecognised connection between BT responses and thermomorphogenic growth. We also propose a working model to better understand how BR signalling contributes to plant adaptation under BT stress conditions. 

# 1 | Introduction

Boron is an essential micronutrient for plants, playing a crucial role in various metabolic processes, including nucleic acid, carbohydrate, and protein metabolism, as well as photosynthesis, cell division, and elongation mechanisms (Warington 1923; Bolaños et al. 2023). Among its functions, the most extensively studied and well‐established is its role as a structural component of the cell wall (O'Neill et al. 2001). Specifically, boron facilitates the cross‐linking of the pectic polysaccharide rhamnogalacturonan II (RG‐II), promoting its 

dimerisation, which is critical for maintaining cell wall integrity and stability (O'Neill et al. 2001; Miwa et al. 2013). 

However, while boron is indispensable for plant development, its concentration must be tightly regulated, as both deficiency and excess can severely impair growth (Camacho‐Cristóbal et al. 2008). Boron availability in soil, where it primarily exists as boric acid, is a key factor influencing agricultural productivity (Tanaka and Fujiwara 2008; Camacho‐Cristóbal et al. 2008). In soil solution, boron is highly mobile and can be easily leached under high rainfall conditions, leading to plant

deficiencies. Conversely, in arid and semi‐arid regions, limited leaching results in boron accumulation, often reaching toxic levels that adversely affect plant growth (Camacho‐Cristóbal et al. 2008; Landi et al. 2019). In line with this, boron toxicity (BT) is widely recognised as a major constraint on crop yield and quality in several agricultural regions worldwide (Camacho‐Cristóbal et al. 2008; Landi et al. 2012; Landi et al. 2019) 

Excessive boron accumulation disrupts cellular homeostasis and interferes with vital physiological processes. At the structural level, BT alters cell wall composition and fluidity, leading to weakened cell walls, inhibited cell expansion, and ultimately growth retardation. In addition, excess boron impairs root, cotyledon, and leaf development, inducing physiological disorders such as chlorosis, necrosis, reduced chlorophyll content, and lower photosynthetic rates (Camacho‐Cristóbal et al. 2008; Landi et al. 2019). Furthermore, BT triggers oxidative stress and disrupts key metabolic pathways, negatively impacting plant health and productivity. Given its dual nature as both an essential and potentially toxic element, understanding boron's physiological roles and its interactions with soil dynamics is crucial for managing plant growth and ensuring agricultural sustainability in boron‐sensitive regions. 

The hypocotyl of dicotyledonous plants is the embryonic stem that connects the primary root to the shoot, positioned between the cotyledonary node and the root node (Krahmer and Fankhauser 2024). Hypocotyl growth follows a developmental programme influenced by environmental factors such as light and temperature, as well as internal cues regulated by various plant hormones (Gendreau et al. 1997; Kim et al. 2002; Vandenbussche et al. 2005; Stavang et al. 2009; Ma et al. 2018; Krahmer and Fankhauser 2024). Light signalling, together with plant hormones such as abscisic acid and cytokinins, suppresses hypocotyl elongation (Gendreau et al. 1997; Nemhauser and Chory 2002; Lorrai et al. 2018). In contrast, darkness, elevated temperatures, and hormones such as auxins and brassinosteroids (BRs) promote hypocotyl elongation (Clouse 1996; Gendreau et al. 1997; Kim et al. 2002; Vandenbussche et al. 2005; Martínez et al. 2018a). Interestingly, hypocotyl growth exhibits a dual response to BT, increasing at moderate boron concentrations but decreasing at higher concentrations (Kocábek et al. 2009). However, the molecular mechanisms underlying BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation remain unknown, highlighting the need for further research. In this study, we investigate hormonal alterations associated with hypocotyl growth in response to BT. Under toxic boron conditions, we observed disruptions in multiple plant hormone regulatory pathways, notably accompanied by a consistent overaccumulation of BRs. 

Thermomorphogenesis, defined as the set of morphological and physiological changes that plants undergo in response to moderately elevated ambient temperatures, includes enhanced hypocotyl and petiole elongation, leaf hyponasty, and alterations in hormone signalling pathways, particularly those involving auxins and BRs (Quint et al. 2016; Casal and Balasubramanian 2019). These growth responses are considered adaptive mechanisms that optimise light capture and promote cooling under high‐temperature conditions and are regulated by 

specific transcription factors, such as PIF4, and environmental sensing mechanisms. Given that several hormone pathways affected by BT overlap with those involved in thermomorphogenesis, comparing these processes may help uncover shared regulatory nodes of stress‐induced developmental plasticity. Accordingly, we examined the similarities between hypocotyl elongation triggered by BT and the thermomorphogenic response. Our findings suggest that key regulatory components of the thermomorphogenic programme are also involved in hypocotyl responses to BT. Finally, we propose a model of interaction centred on BRs, aimed at providing new insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation. 

# 2 | Materials and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Material and Growth Conditions

Arabidopsis thaliana ecotypes Columbia‐0 (Col‐0) and Wassilewskija (Ws), along with their respective mutants (bri1‐101 (Li and Chory 1997), pif4‐101 (Huq 2002), pif4pif5 (Leivar and Quail 2011), pifq (Leivar et al. 2008), cop1‐4, cop1‐6 (McNellis et al. 1994a), phyB‐9 (Reed et al. 1993), hy5‐215 (Oyama et al. 1997), and elf3‐8 (Yu et al. 2008) from Col‐0; and bin2- bil1bil2 (Yan et al. 2009) from Ws), were used in this study. 

For in vitro experiments, seeds were surface‐sterilised by incubating them in $7 0 \%$ ethanol for 2 min, followed by $5 0 \%$ bleach with $0 . 1 \%$ Tween‐20 for 8 min, and then rinsed four times with Milli‐Q water. Subsequently, seeds were stratified in water at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in darkness for $^ { 4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . 

To determine the optimal boric acid $\mathrm { ( H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 } ,$ ) concentration for maximum hypocotyl elongation under BT, Col‐0 seedlings were grown on half‐strength Murashige & Skoog (MS) (Duchefa Biochemie, Haarlem, The Netherlands) medium supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose (VWR Chemicals), $0 . 0 5 \%$ 2‐(N‐morpholino) ethanesulfonic acid (MES) (Duchefa Biochemie, Haarlem, The Netherlands), and $1 \%$ agar (Condalab, Madrid, Spain), with the pH adjusted to 5.7–5.8. Boron control conditions contained the standard 0.1 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ present in half‐strength MS medium, while BT treatments were supplemented with 1, 3, 4.5, and 6 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ . Seedlings were grown in a growth chamber at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 12 h light photoperiod until 5 days post‐ germination (DPG), when hypocotyl elongation was measured. Subsequent experiments were conducted using 3 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ as the standard BT treatment. 

Germination rate under control and BT treatments was assessed by analysing radicle emergence at 12, 24, 36, 48, and $^ \mathrm { 6 0 h }$ after sowing, based on radicle protrusion observed at each time point (Figure S1). The experiment included three replicates, each consisting of 30 seeds. For germination rate, plates were scanned using a CanoScan 8400 F system, and the resulting images were analysed with ImageJ software (v.1.53t, Wayne Rasband, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA). 

Seedlings from Col‐0, Ws, and their respective mutants were grown under control and BT conditions, and hypocotyl elongation was measured at $3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ postgermination (HPG), 2, 3, 5, 7, 

and 10 DPG. Hypocotyls from wild‐type Col‐0 seedlings grown at 3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG were collected by sectioning the tissue and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for further analysis. 

Hypocotyl length measurements were performed by scanning plates containing at least 20 seedlings per plate using a CanoScan $8 4 0 0 \mathrm { F }$ system (Figure S2). Image analysis was conducted with ImageJ software (v.1.53t, Wayne Rasband, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA). For each independent experiment, three plates per treatment were prepared and measured. Three independent experiments were performed in total. 

# 2.2 | Propidium Iodide Staining of Hypocotyl Tissue

To investigate the mechanisms of BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation, seedlings of the Col‐0 ecotype were grown under control and BT conditions until 5 DPG. Seedlings were stained with $\mathrm { 1 m g / m L }$ propidium iodide for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ in darkness, washed four times with distilled water, and visualised using a Leica TCS SP5 (Leica Microsystems) confocal microscope. Hypocotyl cell number and area were measured using ImageJ (v. 1.53t, Wayne Rasband, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA). 

# 2.3 | Phytohormone Quantification

Phytohormonal quantification analysis was performed using Col‐0 seedlings grown under control and BT conditions at 3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG. Hormone quantification was performed using more than 300 seedlings to reach $2 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of tissue, which was used for phytohormone quantification. 

The analysis of indole‐3‐acetic acid (IAA), jasmonic acid (JA), jasmonic acid‐isoleucine (JA‐Ile), abscisic acid (ABA), salicylic acid (SA), and cytokinins was carried out using a high‐ performance liquid chromatography–electrospray ionisation– high‐resolution mass spectrometry (HPLC–ESI–HRMS) system, allowing for precise quantification of these hormones in plant tissues. 

Extraction, purification, and quantification of IAA, JA, JA‐Ile, ABA, and SA were performed following the protocols described by Fuentes et al. (2023) and Olaetxea et al. (2024). Briefly, frozen hypocotyl samples were ground in liquid nitrogen, and hormones were extracted using a methanol/water/formic acid solution (90:9:1, v/v/v) containing deuterium‐labelled internal standards for each hormone. The extraction consisted of two sequential steps of shaking and centrifugation $( 1 6 , 0 0 0 { \mathrm { g } } )$ . Supernatants from both steps were combined, and 1 mL of the pooled extract was evaporated to dryness. The dried residue was re‐dissolved in $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of methanol/acetic acid, centrifuged, and transferred to injection vials. Chromatographic separation was carried out using a Dionex Ultimate 3000 UHPLC system equipped with a Synergi $4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Hydro‐RP $8 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } }$ column $( 1 5 0 \times 2 \mathrm { m m }$ , Phenomenex), coupled to an Exploris 120 mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Detection and quantification were performed using a Product Ion Scan experiment in negative‐ion mode, employing multilevel calibration curves with the respective internal standards. 

Cytokinin extraction, purification, and quantification were conducted according to the method described by Olaetxea et al. (2024). Frozen samples $( \sim 0 . 1 \mathrm { g }$ fresh weight) were ground in liquid nitrogen and extracted with precooled $( - 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ methanol/water/formic acid (15:4:1, v/v/v). Ten microliters of a $1 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ deuterium‐labelled internal standard solution were added to each sample. The extraction was performed overnight at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by an additional re‐extraction step. Purification was carried out using solid‐phase extraction with two cartridges in sequence (Sep‐Pak C18 followed by Oasis MCX). Elution was done using 0.35 M $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } O H }$ in $6 0 \%$ methanol $\mathbf { \Pi } ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ , and the eluate was evaporated to dryness using a RapidVap Evaporator (Labconco Co.). The dried residue was reconstituted in $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of methanol $\phantom { - } 0 . 0 4 \%$ formic acid (50:50, v/v), centrifuged at $2 0 { , } 0 0 0 \times \mathrm { g }$ for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and transferred to injection vials. Chromatographic separation was performed on a Dionex Ultimate 3000 UHPLC system equipped with a Tracer Excel 120 ODSA column $3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , $2 0 0 \times 4 . 6 \mathrm { m m }$ , Teknokroma). Detection and quantification of cytokinins were carried out using an Exploris 120 mass spectrometer operated in positive‐ion mode, using a Product Ion Scan experiment and multilevel calibration with internal standards. 

BRs brassinolide (BL), 24‐epibrassinolide (epiBL), homobrassinolide (HBL), castasterone (CS), 24‐epicastasterone (epiCS), homocastasterone (HCS), and typhasterol (TY) were analysed by high‐performance liquid chromatography‐ electrospray‐high‐resolution accurate mass spectrometry (HPLC‐ESI‐HRMS), using deuterium‐labelled ${ ^ { 2 } \mathrm { H } } _ { 3 }$ ‐brassinolide (D‐BL), ${ ^ { 2 } \mathrm { H } } _ { 3 }$ ‐castasterone (D‐CS), and ${ ^ { 2 } \mathrm { H } } _ { 3 }$ ‐typhasterol (D‐TY) as internal standards. All the standards were purchased from Ol-Chemim Ltd (Olomouc, Czech Republic). Extraction and purification involved homogenising $_ { 0 . 1 \mathrm { g } }$ of frozen plant material (previously ground in liquid nitrogen) with 1 mL of precooled $( - 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ acetonitrile/water (60/40, v/v) containing $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of a $4 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ stock solution of each deuterium‐labelled internal standard in methanol. Extraction was performed by shaking for 60 min in a Multi Reax shaker (Heidolph Instruments, Schwabach, Germany), followed by centrifugation at $1 3 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 10 min using a Biofuge Pico Centrifuge (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, Massachusetts, USA). The solid residue was re‐ extracted with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of the extraction mixture, shaken for 15 min, and centrifuged again. Supernatants were combined, mixed with $1 . 6 \mathrm { m L }$ of water, and passed through a Sep‐Pak C18 cartridge (Waters Co., Milford, MA, USA) preconditioned with methanol and acetonitrile/water (30/70, v/v). The cartridge was washed with 2 aliquots of $1 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ methanol/water (40/60, v/v), and BRs were eluted with $2 \mathrm { m L }$ of methanol, evaporated to dryness in a RapidVap Evaporator (Labconco Co., Kansas City, MO, USA), redissolved in $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of acetonitrile/water/formic acid $( 4 0 / 5 9 . 9 8 / 0 . 0 2 , \mathrm { v / v / v } )$ , and centrifuged at 20 000 RCF for 10 min using a Sigma 4‐16 K Centrifuge (Sigma Laborzentrifugen GmbH, Osterode am Harz, Germany) before injection into the HPLC‐ESI‐HRMS system. Quantification was performed using a Dionex Ultimate 3000 UHPLC system coupled to an Exploris 120 Mass Spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific), equipped with an OptaMax NG ion source, a quadrupole mass filter, a C‐Trap, an Ion‐Routing Multipole, and a High‐Field Orbitrap Mass Analyzer. Chromatographic separation was achieved on a ZORBAX Eclipse Plus C18 column $( 2 . 1 \times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) with a 

linear gradient of acetonitrile (A), water (B), and $1 \%$ formic acid in water (C): $4 2 \%$ A for $3 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , increasing to $9 5 \%$ A over 7 min, maintained at $9 5 \%$ A for 3 min, then decreasing to $4 2 \%$ A over $0 . 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , followed by a $5 . 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ stabilisation period. The percentage of C remained constant at $2 \%$ , with a flow rate of $0 . 2 0 \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ , an injection volume of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ , and column and sample temperatures of $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively. Ionisation source parameters were optimised (detailed in Table S1). Detection and quantification were performed using a Product Ion Scan experiment in the positive‐ion mode, employing multilevel calibration curves with internal standards. The resolution was set at 60,000 FWHM, Q1 resolution at $1 . 5 \mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ , AGC target at standard mode, maximum injection time in auto mode, and RF lens at $7 0 \%$ . Absolute Collision Energy (CE) depends on the molecule. A mass tolerance of 5 ppm was accepted. For each brassinosteroid, three fragment ions were analysed: the most intense fragment (fragment 1) was used for quantification, while fragments 2 and 3 were used for identity confirmation. In the case of typhasterol, only one fragment ion was significantly detected, and for internal standards, only the most intense fragment was analyzed. Instrument control and data processing were carried out using TraceFinder 5.1 EFS software, and accurate masses, CE values, and principal fragments of phytohormones and internal standards are reported in Table S2. 

# 2.4 | Pharmacological Treatments

Col‐0 seedlings were grown under control and BT conditions and supplemented with epiBL (Cayman Chemical Company, Michigan, USA), a bioactive BR at 0.1, 0.5, and $1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , and brassinazole (BRZ) (Tokyo Chemical Industry CO., LTD.), a brassinosteroid biosynthesis inhibitor at 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, and $0 . 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . Both compounds were dissolved in DMSO and added to the control and BT media before seedling growth. For all pharmacological treatments involving hormone inhibitors or analogues, corresponding mock controls were included by treating seedlings with the same concentration of the solvent (DMSO) used to dissolve the compounds. Hypocotyl elongation measurements were taken at 3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG. 

# 2.5 | Gene Expression Analysis

For gene expression analysis, hypocotyl tissue was collected at zeitgeber time (ZT) 7 from Col‐0. Each biological replicate consisted of RNA extracted from a pool of at least 40 hypocotyls per treatment, cut from seedlings, ensuring sufficient RNA yield and minimising variability at 3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG per treatment. RNA was extracted using the E.Z.N.A Plant RNA Kit (Omega Bio‐Tek, Norcross, USA) following the manufacturer's instructions. Total RNA was reverse‐transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using the Maxima First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (ThermoFisher Scientific, Madrid, Spain), following the manufacturer's instructions. For each reaction, $5 0 0 \mathrm { n g }$ of total RNA was used in a final volume of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The resulting cDNA was subsequently diluted 1:20 with nuclease‐free water, and $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of this dilution was used for each qPCR reaction. All reactions were performed in technical triplicates, and negative controls (without reverse transcriptase) were included to rule 

out genomic DNA contamination. Quantitative PCR (qPCR) was performed using Power SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (ThermoFisher Scientific, Madrid, Spain) on a QuantStudio 1 Real‐Time PCR System (ThermoFisher Scientific, Madrid, Spain). Each reaction was conducted in three biological replicates, each in duplicate. PCR conditions were as follows: Initial denaturation: $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min, 40 cycles of denaturation $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s), annealing $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min), and extension $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20s). Gene expression levels were analyzed based on the $2 ^ { - \Delta C T }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001), where ΔCt represents the difference between the Ct value of the target gene and that of the reference gene AtTIP41 (AT4G34270). This method allows the quantification of the relative expression normalised to the reference gene at each time point and condition, without the use of a single universal calibrator. Expression values were calculated separately for control and BT treatments at each developmental stage (3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG) to allow direct comparisons between conditions at each stage. The primer sequences used in this study are listed in Table S3. 

# 2.6 | Analysis of Thermal Growth

To determine the effect of warm temperatures on BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation, Col‐0 seedlings were grown under control and BT conditions at two temperature regimes of $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the case of control temperature and at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the case of warm temperature. Hypocotyl elongation measurements were taken at 3, 5, 7, and 10 DPG. 

# 2.7 | Statistical Analyses

Hypocotyl measurements were conducted using a pool of seedlings per condition. For hormonal quantification and gene expression analysis, three biological replicates $( n = 3$ ) were used per condition, with each replicate consisting of a pool of hypocotyl tissue. Statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism (v. 8.0.1, Boston, MA, USA). Data normality was assessed using the Shapiro‐Wilk test. For parametric data, statistical comparisons were performed using Student's t‐test (pairwise comparisons) or one‐way ANOVA followed by Tukey's post hoc test (multiple comparisons). For nonparametric data, comparisons were conducted using Mann‐Whitney tests (rank‐based comparisons) or Kruskal‐Wallis tests followed by Dunn's post hoc test (multiple comparisons). 

All statistical tests were performed at a $9 5 \%$ confidence level $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . 

# 3 | Results

# 3.1 | Boron Toxicity Modulates Hypocotyl Elongation in a Dose‐ and Time‐Dependent Manner

To assess the effects of BT on hypocotyl elongation, we analysed seedling growth under different $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ concentrations at 5 DPG. Our results showed that hypocotyl elongation was promoted with increasing $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ concentrations up to $3 \mathrm { m M }$ , 

beyond which, further increases in boric acid concentration began to suppress elongation (Figure 1a). The maximum hypocotyl elongation response to BT was observed at 3 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ supplementation and, as mentioned in the Materials and Methods section, this concentration was fixed as BT treatment. 

To further investigate the cellular basis of hypocotyl elongation under BT, we performed propidium iodide staining analysis, which revealed that exposure to 3 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { B O } _ { 3 }$ led to a significant increase in hypocotyl cell area (Figure 1b,c) while reducing the total number of cells constituting the hypocotyl tissue (Figure 1c). These findings suggest that BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation is associated with cell expansion rather than increased cell proliferation. 

We also monitored hypocotyl elongation over time in seedlings grown under control and BT conditions, measuring growth on different DPG. Initially, BT repressed hypocotyl elongation up to 2 DPG; however, from this point onward, the toxic effects of boron began to stimulate hypocotyl elongation, persisting throughout the remaining analysed days (Figure 2a–c). Additionally, the ratio of hypocotyl elongation between BT and control conditions increased progressively between 3 DPG and 7 DPG, after which it stabilised and remained constant up to 10 DPG (Figure 2c). These results highlight a time‐dependent 

biphasic response to BT, characterised by an initial inhibition followed by a sustained promotion of hypocotyl elongation.

# 3.4 | Brassinosteroid Signalling Mutants Confirm the Role of Brassinosteroids (BRs) in Boron Toxicity‐Induced Hypocotyl Elongation

Following pharmacological treatments, we employed a reverse genetic approach using two mutants with altered BR signalling to further investigate the role of BRs in BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation. The gain‐of‐function bin2bil1bil2 mutant exhibits constitutively active BR signalling due to the inactivation of BRASSINOSTEROID INSENSITIVE 2 (BIN2), BIN2 LIKE 1 (BIL1), and BIN2 LIKE 2 (BIL2) genes that encode repressors of BR signalling (Yan et al. 2009). Meanwhile, the loss‐of‐function bri1‐101 mutant carries a mutation in BRASSINOSTEROID INSENSITIVE 1 (BRI1), a BR receptor, leading to reduced sensitivity to BR (Friedrichsen et al. 2000). 

Analysis of the bin2bil1bil2 mutant under BT conditions revealed no differences between treatments at early developmental stages. However, at 5 and 7 DPG, BT significantly enhanced hypocotyl elongation in both genotypes, with the mutant displaying greater sensitivity than the wild‐type (Figure 4c). This enhanced sensitivity became more evident when hypocotyl elongation was expressed as relative values, revealing a significantly greater increase in the mutant compared to the wild‐type under BT conditions (Figure 4c, Table S5a). Moreover, a significant interaction between BT and genotype was detected, indicating that the mutation may potentiate the BT‐induced elongation response (Table S5b). 

Similarly, the bri1‐101 mutant exhibited a significant increase in hypocotyl elongation under BT conditions compared to control‐ grown seedlings at all analysed developmental stages. However, mutant seedlings displayed shorter hypocotyls than wild‐type seedlings under both conditions, with the reduction in growth being more pronounced under BT conditions (Figure 4d). From 7 DPG onward, a significant decrease in relative hypocotyl elongation was observed in the mutant (Table S5a). Notably, the mutation appeared to begin interfering with the BT response as early as 5 DPG (Table S5b). 

These findings suggest that BRs play a key role in BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation, as enhanced BR signalling increases the sensitivity to BT, while blocking this pathway appears to attenuate the response to boron excess. 

# 3.5 | Gene Expression Analysis Supports Brassinosteroid Involvement in Boron Toxicity Response

To further support previous findings, we performed a gene expression analysis examining the transcription levels of genes involved in BR biosynthesis, inactivation, and signalling under control and BT conditions. 

BR biosynthesis genes showed a significant increase in expression under BT from 3 DPG to 5 DPG in the case of BRASSINOSTEROID‐6‐OXIDASE 1 (BR6OX1), and from 3 DPG to 7 DPG for DWF4 and BRASSINOSTEROID‐ 6‐OXIDASE 2 (BR6OX2) (Figure 5a–c). By 7 DPG, BR6OX1 expression returned to basal levels, whereas BR6OX2 remained elevated until 10 DPG. However, at 10 DPG, both DWF4 and BR6OX1 displayed reduced expression under BT conditions (Figure 5–c). 

Expression of BAS1, a gene involved in BR inactivation (Turk et al. 2005), was generally downregulated under BT conditions, except at 5 DPG, where seedlings exhibited a transient increase in BAS1 expression compared to control conditions (Figure 5d). 

Lastly, genes related to BR signalling, including BRASSINA-ZOLE RESISTANT 1 (BZR1) and BRI1‐EMS‐SUPPRESSOR 1 (BES1) (Wang et al. 2002; Yin et al. 2002), displayed reduced expression at 3 DPG under BT conditions (Figure 5e,f). However, BZR1 expression increased at 5 and 7 DPG, while BES1 expression remained upregulated from 5 DPG to 10 DPG under BT. By 10 DPG, BZR1 expression no longer differed between treatments (Figure 5e,f). 

These results further support the critical role of BRs in BT‐ induced hypocotyl elongation, demonstrating that both biosynthesis and signalling pathways are dynamically regulated in response to boron stress.

At 5 DPG, significant differences appeared between BT‐grown seedlings and control seedlings at both temperatures, although warm temperatures showed increased elongation at both boron conditions. Similarly, at 7 DPG, seedlings exposed to BT displayed significant differences in hypocotyl elongation across temperature conditions, although at higher temperatures, elongation was slightly reduced (Figure 6f). At 10 DPG, differences in elongation between boron conditions continued showing a maximum increment at warm temperatures. Exploring the effect of warm temperatures on relative hypocotyl elongation, we observed a significant decrease in the differential elongation between boron treatments at 7 DPG under warm‐ temperature conditions (Table S5a). This was accompanied by a significant interaction between boron treatment and temperature, a highly significant main effect of BT conditions, and a marginal yet significant effect of temperature (Table S5b). 

# 4 | Discussion

# 4.1 | Developmental Stage‐Specific Effects of Boron Toxicity on Hypocotyl Growth

Previous studies have shown that hypocotyl growth primarily results from cell elongation rather than cell division (Gendreau et al. 1997). Experiments conducted under dark growth conditions demonstrated that, despite enhanced hypocotyl elongation compared to light conditions, there was no observable increase in epidermal cell division. In fact, a potential reduction was suggested (Gendreau et al. 1997). Similarly, under BT conditions, hypocotyl elongation was promoted through cell elongation mechanisms, despite a significant reduction in hypocotyl cell number, indicating a repressive effect on cell division (Figure 1b,c). Previous studies have reported a dose‐dependent effect of BT on hypocotyl elongation, where moderate boron concentrations stimulate elongation and excessive levels become inhibitory (Kocábek et al. 2009). In the context of our study, we define ‘moderate’ boron concentrations as those that elicit strong physiological responses, such as enhanced hypocotyl elongation and hormonal remodelling, without halting growth, whereas ‘excessive’ concentrations refer to levels that impair developmental processes and compromise overall plant viability. Consistent with these findings, our results show that concentrations of $3 \mathrm { m M }$ boric acid elicited the highest hypocotyl elongation response, while higher concentrations $\left( > 3 \thinspace \mathrm { m M } \right)$ began to inhibit elongation, with this negative effect becoming more pronounced at increasing doses (Figure 1a). These findings suggest that high boron‐induced hypocotyl elongation results from an enhancement of cell elongation mechanisms alongside repression of cell division, leading to the dose‐dependent response observed. 

BT has also been reported to delay seed germination in A. thaliana due to its physiological effects (Lv et al. 2017). Our 

results indicate that BT did not promote hypocotyl elongation until 3 DPG, after which its effect became significantly evident (Figure 2a–c). At earlier stages, hypocotyls of BT‐grown seedlings were shorter than those under control conditions, likely due to germination delay (Figure S1). However, from 3 DPG onward, BT‐treated seedlings exhibited accelerated hypocotyl growth compared to control seedlings. The relative difference in hypocotyl elongation between control and BT conditions was not constant but progressively increased until 7 DPG, at which point it stabilised. This suggests that the effect of BT on hypocotyl elongation is influenced by the distinct developmental processes occurring in the early stages, particularly during de‐ etiolation (Krahmer and Fankhauser 2024). In the first days, the plant undergoes major transitions, including the opening of the apical hook and cotyledon expansion. This means that growth responses to boron may differ significantly from those detected later, which explains the observed dynamic regulation of growth mechanisms over time. Thus, although BT delays germination (Figure S1), the morphological responses (and hormonal changes) reported at 3 DPG could reflect an active adaptation to stress in germinated seedlings, rather than a mere consequence of delayed development. Furthermore, the absence of a stimulatory effect of 3 mM B on hypocotyl length at 3 DPG (Figure 6) contrasts with earlier results (Figure 2b). This difference likely reflects developmental variability rather than a lack of reproducibility. Around 3 DPG, seedlings undergo the transition from germination to autotrophic growth, a stage highly sensitive to minor differences in seed vigour, germination timing, or microenvironmental conditions (Bai et al. 2017). These factors can transiently affect growth responses. We selected 3 mM B as the standard treatment based on its consistent promotion of hypocotyl elongation at later stages (e.g., 5 DPG), where phenotypes were more robust and reproducible. 

# 4.2 | Boron Toxicity Effect on Plant Hormone Accumulation

Several plant hormones and their interactions, along with environmental signals, are involved in regulating the hypocotyl elongation process (Oh et al. 2014a; Oh et al. 2014b). 

# 4.2.1 | Cytokinins Under Boron Toxicity

Trans‐Zeatin (tZ) is an active form of cytokinin (Sakakibara 2006; Jameson 2023), and its accumulation under BT conditions was observed only at 3DPG (Figure 3b). As previously mentioned, BT delays seed germination, which could imply that seedlings grown under these conditions at 3DPG might not have completed the seedling establishment process yet, unlike control seedlings. Previous reports performed using pharmacological treatment revealed that cytokinins promote germination and seedling establishment, opposing the inhibitory effect of ABA. These two hormones share an antagonistic relationship during the postgerminative stage, where cytokinins exert a promotional effect while ABA exerts a repressive effect (Subbiah and Reddy 2010; Hussain et al. 2019; Zubo and Schaller 2020; Yadukrishnan et al. 2020). At the same stage, under BT conditions, ABA displayed the opposite result 

compared to tZ, suggesting that the seedling establishment process is being enhanced, although it may not yet be completed at this stage (Figure 3a,b). Changes in hormone accumulation within this tissue might therefore be focused on completing this post‐germinative process. 

BT led to a notable increase in tZR and iPR levels, two conjugated forms of cytokinin, at 3 DPG (Figure 3b). Cytokinin ribosides serve as key transport forms and are the major cytokinin species in the xylem, facilitating long‐distance hormone signalling (Sakakibara 2006; Jameson 2023). The increase in tZR correlated with higher accumulation of its active form, tZ, suggesting an efficient conversion upon uptake into hypocotyl cells. In contrast, while iPR levels also increased, no corresponding changes were observed in its active form, indicating a different regulatory pattern. Cytokinin ribosides are transported through both xylem and phloem, undergoing enzymatic activation upon cellular integration (Sakakibara 2006; Osugi et al. 2017; Jameson 2023). The selective increase in tZ, directly linked to tZR accumulation, suggests that tZR is actively converted into tZ within hypocotyl cells, where it may contribute to seedling establishment. Meanwhile, the increased iPR levels could reflect enhanced transport through the hypocotyl to other tissues, where its activation might support distinct physiological functions. In accordance with previous studies, its active form may promote root growth during seedling establishment or enhance cotyledon expansion and greening by counteracting ABA effects (Singh and Sawhney 1992; Stirk et al. 2012; Guan et al. 2014). These findings highlight the differential roles of cytokinin transport and conversion in response to BT and their potential impact on early seedling development. 

BT had an opposite effect on cZR accumulation compared to tZR and iPR, leading to reduced cZR levels at 3 DPG, while cZ accumulation remained unchanged (Figure 3b). cZ is an active cytokinin, though it exhibits lower activity than other bioactive forms like tZ and iP (Spíchal et al. 2004). Despite cZ and tZ being isomers, they do not share the same biosynthetic pathway; instead, cZ is synthesised independently of iP and tZ from distinct precursors (Mok and Mok 2001; Hošek et al. 2020). The unchanged cZ levels, despite the decrease in cZR, suggest that cZR is actively converted into cZ to maintain cytokinin homeostasis. Previous studies have proposed that cZ plays a regulatory role in fine‐tuning cytokinin responses, particularly under growth‐limiting conditions (Gajdošová et al. 2011). Additionally, cZ has been suggested as a potential precursor for tZ, as cZ synthesised in roots can be transported to shoots, where light may trigger its isomerization into tZ (Bassil et al. 1993; Mok and Mok 2001). The increased incorporation of cZR into hypocotyl cells and its conversion into cZ could be enhancing the cytokinin response under BT at 3DPG. Alternatively, it might contribute to the higher tZ accumulation via isomerization, explaining why cZ levels remained stable while cZR levels declined. 

# 4.2.2 | Abscisic Acid Accumulation Under Boron Toxicity Does Not Repress Hypocotyl Elongation

Under BT conditions, ABA accumulation was reduced at 3 DPG (Figure 3a), which could be linked to its antagonistic interaction 

with cytokinins during the post‐germinative process, as previously described. However, from 5 DPG onward, ABA levels increased in response to BT (Figure 3a). Previous studies have shown that ABA biosynthesis is activated in roots under BT, with the newly synthesised ABA being transported to aerial tissues, where it promotes stomatal closure and reduces transpiration (Macho‐Rivero et al. 2017; Chen et al. 2023; Martínez‐ Mazón et al. 2023). This mechanism is thought to limit the movement of excess boron from the roots to the leaves, reducing BT effects (Macho‐Rivero et al. 2017; Macho‐Rivero et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2023; Martínez‐Mazón et al. 2023). ABA also maintains an antagonistic relationship with auxins and BRs, acting as a negative regulator of hypocotyl elongation, whereas auxins and BRs promote this process (Gendreau et al. 1997). However, despite the increased accumulation of ABA under BT, no repression of hypocotyl elongation was observed at any developmental stage. Instead, hypocotyls continued to exhibit accelerated growth under BT conditions, suggesting that ABA does not play a clear inhibitory role in this process. Consistent with these findings, the results indicate that ABA accumulation in hypocotyl tissue under BT may primarily result from its enhanced transport through this tissue, rather than exerting a significant repressive effect on BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation. 

# 4.2.3 | Brassinosteroid Biosynthesis and Signalling Are Key Regulators of Boron Toxicity‐Induced Hypocotyl Elongation

BRs are a class of plant steroid hormones that play essential roles in growth and development, particularly in hypocotyl elongation (Yin et al. 2002; Wang et al. 2012; Minami et al. 2019). They promote cell expansion by inducing cell wall relaxation (Wang et al. 1993; Zurek et al. 1994; Minami et al. 2019). Key genes in the BR biosynthetic pathway, including DWF4, BR6OX1, and BR6OX2, encode rate‐limiting enzymes that regulate BR production (Fujioka et al. 1995; Choe et al. 1998; Shimada et al. 2003; Hamasaki et al. 2020). DWF4 encodes a C‐22 hydroxylase involved in the hydroxylation of multiple BR precursors (Choe et al. 1998; Fujita et al. 2006), while BR6OX1 and BR6OX2 encode C‐6 oxidases which catalyse the synthesis of CS and BL, the most bioactive BR forms (Bishop et al. 1999; Shimada et al. 2001; Shimada et al. 2003; Kim et al. 2005b; Wei et al. 2017; Zhang et al. 2024). Gene expression analysis revealed that BT induced the upregulation of DWF4, BR6OX1, and BR6OX2 starting from 3 DPG, with sustained overexpression until 10 DPG, at which point DWF4 and BR6OX1 were downregulated, while BR6OX2 maintained stable expression (Figure 5a–c). The accumulation of CS under BT (Figure 3c) was correlated with the upregulation of these biosynthetic genes, DWF4, BR6OX1, and BR6OX2. 

The balance between BR biosynthesis and inactivation is crucial for maintaining BR homeostasis (Neff et al. 1999; Fujioka and Yokota 2003; Youn et al. 2016). BAS1 encodes a C‐26 hydroxylase that inactivates BRs by hydroxylating CS and BL, reducing their bioactive content (Turk et al. 2005; Sandhu et al. 2012; Youn et al. 2016). Under BT conditions, BAS1 expression was broadly repressed (Figure 5d), exhibiting an opposite trend to 

BR biosynthesis genes, which correlated with the increased accumulation of bioactive BRs. At 10 DPG, when BR biosynthesis genes were downregulated, CS levels remained high, possibly due to the sustained suppression of BAS1 expression, preventing its degradation. Nonetheless, at 5 DGP we observed an increase in BAS1 expression under BT treatment compared to control conditions, which might act as a localised homoeostatic mechanism to attenuate the increased BRs accumulation as well as the consequent enhanced signalling during the developmental transition from seedling establishment to seedling growth. 

However, BL, the most active BR (Wang et al. 2002; Turk et al. 2005), could not be detected under either condition. This is consistent with previous studies reporting the extreme difficulty of detecting this hormone due to its low abundance and the requirement for large amounts of tissue (Turk et al. 2003; Kim et al. 2005a; Chung et al. 2012). Nonetheless, several lines of evidence suggest that BL biosynthesis is likely enhanced under BT. First, we observed an increased accumulation of CS, the direct biosynthetic precursor of BL (Yokota 1997; Kim et al. 2005b). Second, key genes involved in BL biosynthesis, including DWF4, BR6OX1, and particularly BR6OX2, responsible for CS to BL conversion, were upregulated in BT conditions (Shimada et al. 2003; Wei et al. 2017; Hamasaki et al. 2020). Finally, we found downregulation of BAS1, a gene responsible for BL inactivation (Neff et al. 1999; Turk et al. 2003), further supporting increased active BL levels under BT 

BR signalling is mediated by the BRI1 receptor, which triggers a downstream signal transduction cascade that regulates BZR1 and BES1, two transcription factors that promote BR responses (Oh et al. 2012; Li et al. 2018). In the absence of BRs, BIN2 phosphorylates BZR1 and BES1, leading to their inactivation and degradation (Wang et al. 2002; Yin et al. 2002; Oh et al. 2012; Wei et al. 2017; Li et al. 2018). The bin2bil1bil2 triple mutant, which exhibits constitutive BR signalling, displayed heightened sensitivity to BT, showing enhanced hypocotyl elongation under this condition (Figure 4c). This suggests that BIN2 repression is not directly triggered by BT although it might become more pronounced at later stages, coinciding with the gradual slowdown of hypocotyl growth, possibly due to the cessation of hypocotyl development. This finding aligns with the positive effect of epiBL on hypocotyl elongation (Figure 4b), which occurs through BIN2 inhibition and the stabilisation of BZR1 and BES1. However, the bri1‐101 mutant (BR‐insensitive) exhibited significantly reduced hypocotyl elongation under BT (Figure 4d), indicating that BR signalling plays a fundamental role in this process. 

Given the central role of BZR1 and BES1 in BR signalling, their gene expression was analysed. Under BT conditions, we observed a transient repression of both BZR1 and BES1 genes at 3 DPG, which might be related to the delayed germination. Nonetheless, both genes were broadly and significantly upregulated at later stages (Figure 5e,f), which likely led to higher protein accumulation. This, combined with the increased levels of bioactive BRs (i.e., CS), may have resulted in greater stability and an increased fraction of non‐phosphorylated BZR1 and BES1 proteins, further reinforcing BR signalling activity. This response is similar to what has been observed in bzr1‐1D and bes1‐D gain‐of‐function mutants, where reduced BIN2 affinity 

leads to enhanced BZR1 and BES1 stability and activity (Wang et al. 2002; Yin et al. 2002). The increased bioactive BR levels under BT could have generated a bzr1‐1D/bes1‐D‐like phenotype, with higher levels of non‐phosphorylated BZR1 and BES1, characterized by enhanced BR signalling and sustained hypocotyl elongation. 

BR homoeostasis is regulated by a negative feedback loop in which active BR signalling suppresses biosynthetic gene expression (Neff et al. 1999; Fujioka and Yokota 2003; Youn et al. 2016). In bzr1‐1D and bes1‐D gain‐of‐function mutants, as well as in overexpression lines, overactive BR signalling represses biosynthetic genes, such as DWF1, DWF4, CPD, and BR6OX2, while inducing BAS1, ultimately reducing BR accumulation (Wang et al. 2002; Yin et al. 2002; He et al. 2005; Ryu et al. 2007; Ryu et al. 2010; Oh et al. 2012; Oh et al. 2014a; Oh et al. 2014b; Youn et al. 2016; Youn et al. 2018). A similar effect occurs when seedlings are treated with BL, which downregulates BR biosynthesis genes and upregulates BAS1, while BRZ produces the opposite effect (Tanaka and Fujiwara 2008; Yoshimitsu et al. 2011). However, under BT conditions, the expected negative feedback regulation did not occur, suggesting that BT (which showed an increased accumulation of the bioactive BR CS) disrupts this homeostatic mechanism, possibly converting it into a positive feedback loop, further enhancing BR signalling and promoting hypocotyl elongation. 

The gene BRI1 encodes a BR receptor, and its mutation results in BR insensitivity signalling (Clouse et al. 1996; Wang et al. 2001; Oh et al. 2012; Li et al. 2018). Our pharmacological analysis revealed that BRZ significantly suppressed hypocotyl elongation under BT, with high concentrations reducing elongation to control levels (Figure 4a). No BRZ insensitivity was observed, nor did BT suppress the bri1‐101 mutant phenotype (Figure 4d), suggesting that BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation depends on BR biosynthesis and signalling activation. 

# 4.2.4 | Auxins and Brassinosteroids Cooperate to Promote Hypocotyl Elongation Under Boron Toxicity

Together with BRs, auxins play a crucial role in hypocotyl elongation, acting as positive regulators of cell expansion (Rayle and Cleland 1992; Nemhauser et al. 2004; Reed et al. 2018; Yu et al. 2023). From 3 DPG onward, hypocotyls grown under BT conditions exhibited a significant increase in elongation rates, which continued to rise steadily until 7 DPG. Concurrently with the onset of enhanced hypocotyl growth under BT at 3 DPG, both auxin and BRs levels increased in the hypocotyl cells (Figure 3a,c). This suggests that these hormones play a central role in driving the rapid elongation response to BT. Although BR accumulation remained high throughout all studied stages, auxin accumulation peaked exclusively at 3 DPG, indicating a stage‐specific role in the early phases of hypocotyl elongation under BT. These findings indicate that auxins primarily promote hypocotyl elongation during the early stages of seedling development, whereas BRs sustain elongation throughout the entire growth period. The fact that auxin accumulation coincided with the initial surge in hypocotyl elongation rates further supports its critical role in early‐stage elongation under BT conditions. 

Previous studies have reported a tight interdependence between auxin and BR signalling pathways, indicating that both need to be active for proper hypocotyl elongation (Goda et al. 2004; Nemhauser et al. 2004; Hardtke 2007; Chung et al. 2011). Previous reports using loss‐of‐function mutants showed that the inactivation of one hormone's signalling pathway represses the other (Hardtke 2007; Tian et al. 2018; Peres et al. 2019). Conversely, mutants with constitutive BR signalling display increased activation of auxin‐related genes, highlighting their regulatory interplay (Hardtke 2007). Indeed, it has been proposed that BRs act as a limiting factor in auxin responses (Nemhauser et al. 2004). This suggests that, despite auxin accumulation occurring only at 3 DPG under BT, the continuous increase in BRs at later stages may enhance both BR and auxin signalling, thereby maintaining hormonal sensitivity even without further auxin accumulation. 

# 4.3 | Brassinosteroids and Temperature Signalling in Boron Toxicity‐Induced Hypocotyl Elongation

Previous studies have suggested that light signalling pathways may modulate the plant's response to BT (Kocábek et al. 2009). In addition, both darkness and warm temperatures promote hypocotyl elongation (Krahmer and Fankhauser 2024). However, the phenotypic outcomes of dark‐induced growth differ from those of temperature‐induced elongation. Dark‐grown seedlings display apical hook formation and complete suppression of cotyledon expansion. In contrast, thermomorphogenesis is characterized by elongation of the hypocotyl and petioles, accompanied by a reduction, but not complete inhibition, of cotyledon expansion (Quint et al. 2016; Casal and Balasubramanian 2019; Nieto et al. 2020; Delker et al. 2022). The latter phenotype closely resembles the response to BT in which cotyledon expansion is visibly reduced but not entirely suppressed (Figure 2a). These similarities led us to explore whether the mechanisms that drive thermomorphogenic growth are also involved in hypocotyl elongation under BT conditions. 

PIF4 is a transcription factor that integrates light and temperature cues to drive this growth (Koini et al. 2009; Delker et al. 2014; Gangappa and Kumar 2017). COP1, active in darkness, promotes degradation of photomorphogenesis factors like HY5, thereby indirectly supporting PIF4 function (Ang et al. 1998; Osterlund et al. 2000; Delker et al. 2014; Nieto et al. 2020). Our findings show that cop1 mutants (cop1‐4 and cop1‐6) display reduced hypocotyl elongation under BT (Figure 6b, Table S5a,b), suggesting that COP1 activity could be involved in BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation response, much like its role in thermomorphogenesis (Delker et al. 2014; Gangappa and Kumar 2017; Park et al. 2017; Nieto et al. 2022; Hwang et al. 2025). Interestingly, while warm temperatures do not alter COP1 expression (Nieto et al. 2020), BT conditions upregulated COP1 transcripts (Figure S3), pointing to a unique transcriptional response. These results suggest that BT enhances COP1 activity and expression, thereby reinforcing its role in promoting elongation. Future studies examining COP1 subcellular localisation under BT conditions will help clarify how COP1 activity is affected. 

Similar to cop1 mutant, loss‐of‐function pif mutants exhibited a significant reduction in BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation, with pif4 showing the strongest inhibition (Figure 6a). The double (pif4pif5) and quadruple (pifq) mutants displayed only moderate additional repression, suggesting that PIF4 plays a dominant role in this response, while other PIFs contribute to a lesser extent. Consistent with the finding that warm temperatures upregulate PIF4 expression (Delker et al. 2014; Gangappa and Kumar 2017; Park et al. 2017) BT also significantly increased PIF4 expression (Figure S3). Given the parallel responses observed between BT and warm‐temperature conditions, our results suggest that PIF4 may integrate thermomorphogenic growth and BT responses. Furthermore, the mechanisms driving PIF4 upregulation under BT could be shared with those in thermomorphogenesis, potentially through the suppression of ELF3 and PHYB repression activities. 

Previous studies have shown that BRs play essential roles in growth regulation by light and temperature (Oh et al. 2012; Ibañez et al. 2018; Martínez et al. 2018b; Nieto et al. 2020). Additionally, active BR biosynthesis and signalling pathways are required to promote thermomorphogenic growth under warm‐temperature conditions, as a blockage in its perception significantly reduces the thermomorphogenic response (Grey et al. 1998; Stavang et al. 2009; Oh et al. 2012; Ibañez et al. 2018; Martínez et al. 2018a; Nieto et al. 2020). Furthermore, similar to the observed results under BT, evidence suggests that BR biosynthesis and signalling pathways regulate the thermomorphogenic response through interactions with COP1 and PIF4 (Oh et al. 2012; Ibañez et al. 2018; Martínez et al. 2018a; Martínez et al. 2018b; Nieto et al. 2020). Likewise, warm‐ temperature conditions enhance BR biosynthesis pathways, increasing the expression of related genes such as DWF4 and BR6OX2 (Martínez et al. 2018a; Martínez et al. 2018b). Indeed, a blockage in BR biosynthesis has been shown to reduce thermomorphogenic hypocotyl elongation response (Grey et al. 1998; Stavang et al. 2009; Ibañez et al. 2018). Additionally, enhanced BR biosynthesis has been described to consequently promote BZR1 nuclear localisation under high temperatures (Ibañez et al. 2018). Similarly, under BT conditions, we observed a comparable involvement of BR biosynthesis, as both mechanisms exhibited a BR biosynthesis‐dependent induction, which might be enhancing BZR1 nuclear localisation and promoting BR signalling, as observed under warm temperatures. Moreover, under warm‐temperature conditions, auxins and BRs have been suggested to play key roles in inducing hypocotyl growth. While auxins are required at early stages, BRs are required at later stages (Stavang et al. 2009). Under BT conditions, similar results were obtained through hormonal quantification, which showed an increase auxins accumulation in hypocotyl tissue during early stages, whereas BR accumulation persisted at later stages (Figure 3a,c). These results suggested that both hormones are similarly involved in both mechanisms. 

In addition, previous studies have reported that PIF4 interacts with BZR1 and BES1 to regulate the transcription of several target genes implicated in the cell elongation process (Oh et al. 2012; Oh et al. 2014a; Martínez et al. 2018a). During the thermomorphogenic response, PIF4's positive regulation has been shown to depend on BR signalling. An increase in BZR1 activity and its overexpression led to increased sensitivity to 

warm temperature, significantly promoting PIF4 expression and hypocotyl elongation (Ibañez et al. 2018). Furthermore, it was observed that BZR1 and PIF4 heterodimerization appears to be required for BZR1's growth‐promoting activity, and conversely, BZR1 is required for PIF4‐mediated cell elongation (Oh et al. 2012; Ibañez et al. 2018). Also, PIF4 plays a critical role in the expression of several BR biosynthesis‐related genes under warm‐temperature conditions (Martínez et al. 2018a; Martínez et al. 2018b). As mentioned earlier, BR signalling can repress its biosynthesis gene expression; however, it has been reported that PIF4 can suppress the repressive activity of BES1 on BR biosynthesis (Martínez et al. 2018a; Martínez et al. 2018b). Moreover, warm‐temperature conditions enhance the formation of the BES1‐PIF4 heterodimer, which promotes the expression of several BR biosynthesis‐related genes, such as DWF4 and BR6OX2, under warm‐temperature conditions (Martínez et al. 2018a; Martínez et al. 2018b). According to previous reports, the present results suggest that, under BT conditions, the increased BR biosynthesis might be enhancing the signalling pathways, promoting BZR1, and, potentially BES1, nuclear localisation. Similar to warm‐temperature conditions, BZR1 might be promoting PIF4 upregulation, thereby enhancing BT‐ induced hypocotyl elongation response. Additionally, these conditions may promote BES1‐PIF4 heterodimer formation, which could enhance BR biosynthesis gene expression, potentially shifting the negative feedback regulation to a positive feedback loop. 

COP1 has been shown to promote plant growth by sequestering protein kinase BIN2, which represses BZR1 and BES1 activity (Ling et al. 2017; Nieto et al. 2020). Additionally, BRs promote COP1 nuclear localisation, indicating a bidirectional interaction where COP1 enhances BR signalling, while BR signalling further promotes COP1 localisation in the nucleus (Nieto et al. 2020). Our findings suggest that BT may increase COP1 activity, potentially stimulating BR signalling by inhibiting BIN2, leading to increased BZR1 and BES1 activity while also reinforcing COP1 nuclear localisation through this pathway. 

Our results regarding the involvement of the crosstalk between BT and the thermomorphogenic pathway likely apply only to the hypocotyl response after seedling establishment. As mentioned earlier, BT induces changes during germination and early seedling development, which may contribute to the observed variability. However, these early‐stage effects were not addressed in the present study and should be further investigated in future work, as they fall outside the scope of our current analysis. Furthermore, consistent with previous reports, our results suggest that while not all thermomorphogenic mechanisms are shared with BT‐induced hypocotyl elongation, a key overlap exists in the regulation of BR biosynthesis and signalling pathways, particularly through their interactions with PIF4 and COP1.
