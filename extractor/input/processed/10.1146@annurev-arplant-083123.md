# Abstract

Eukaryotic glycobiology revolves around nucleotide sugar transporters (NSTs), which are critical for glycan biosynthesis in the Golgi apparatus and endoplasmic reticulum. In plants, NSTs share similarities with triose phosphate translocators (TPTs) and together form the NST/TPT superfamily. Major research efforts over the last decades have led to the biochemical characterization of several of these transporters and addressed their role in cell wall polysaccharide and glycoconjugate biosynthesis, revealing precise substrate specificity and function. While recent insights gained from NST and TPT crystal structures promise to unravel the molecular mechanisms governing these membrane proteins, their regulation and dynamic behavior remain enigmatic. Likewise, many uncharacterized and orphan NSTs pose exciting questions about the biology of the endomembrane system. We discuss the progress in this active research area and stimulate consideration for the intriguing outstanding questions with a view to establish a foundation for applications in plant engineering and biopolymer production.

# 1. INTRODUCTION AND CONTEXT: GLYCOSYLATION IN THE ENDOMEMBRANE SYSTEM

Eukaryotic cells are generally believed to have originated from the incorporation of ancestral mitochondria, specifically alphaproteobacteria, into primitive archaea approximately 2 billion years

ago (38). While it is still debated how the acquisition of mitochondria has led to the development of the entire endomembrane system, it is widely accepted that the evolutionary origins of eukaryotic cells are closely linked to its development (68). The endomembrane system, including the nuclear envelope, endoplasmic reticulum (ER), Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, peroxisomes, autophagosomes, and dynamic vesicle trafficking, enabled the compartmentalization and specialization of cellular functions differentiating eukaryotes from prokaryotes. Its function significantly enhanced the efficiency of many crucial processes, including not only protein synthesis and trafficking but also synthesis of glycosylated membrane lipids and polysaccharides, thereby significantly contributing to the complexity of eukaryotic life.

Plants exhibit remarkable diversity in glycan structures, ranging from polysaccharides to cell surface glycoproteins, proteoglycans, and glycolipids. Unlike animal cells, much of the glycan is present in a complex wall of polysaccharides and glycoproteins that surrounds each plant cell. This complexity of plant polysaccharides and glycoconjugates requires a diverse array of nucleotide sugars, the sugar donors for intricate glycan architectures (12). Nucleotide sugars are activated donors that generally consist of a sugar moiety linked to a nucleoside diphosphate. Members of a large enzyme family referred to as glycosyltransferases (GTs) catalyze the attachment of the sugar molecule from the activated donor to a suitable acceptor, leading to the formation of glycostructures in a template-independent manner. There are more than 570 GTs encoded in plant genomes (64); the specificity of GTs for acceptors and donors, the diverse activated sugar donors, and the multitude of forms and configurations of monosaccharides as well as the ways these can be linked allow for the diversity of glycan structures needed for specialized physiological and biochemical functions in plants.

Biosynthetic GTs are present in different cellular compartments. Many cytosolic GTs facilitate the glycosylation of secondary metabolites, those at the plasma membrane make cellulose and callose, and matrix polysaccharides as well as attachments on glycoproteins and glycolipids are mainly assembled in the Golgi apparatus by membrane-bound type II GTs (Figure 1). The type II membrane protein structure is characteristic of Golgi GTs, where the N-terminal transmembrane domain anchors the enzyme to the membrane, while the catalytic domain faces the Golgi lumen, where it can interact with nucleotide sugars and form glycosidic linkages. The stem-like region between the transmembrane domain and the catalytic domain is believed to play a role in enzyme stability, localization, or regulation (16). Newly assembled glycans are typically trafficked from the Golgi apparatus to the trans-Golgi network (TGN) where they are sorted into vesicles and subsequently secreted to the cell wall (121).

# 1.1. Nucleotide Sugars Are the Universal Sugar Donors for Glycosylation Reactions

Nucleotide sugars serve as sugar donors for glycan synthesis due to their high-energy bond that confers a high group-transfer potential, allowing the formation of glycosidic linkages. In plants, at least 30 distinct nucleotide sugars have been described (32), and around 14 nucleotide sugars are predominantly used for glycosylation in terrestrial plants (Figure 1). These include the activated forms of the hexoses D-glucose (D-Glc), D-galactose (D-Gal), D-mannose (D-Man), and L-galactose (L-Gal); the 6-deoxy hexoses L-rhamnose (L-Rha) and L-fucose (L-Fuc); the pentoses D-xylose (D-Xyl), L-arabinopyranose (L-Arap), and L-arabinofuranose (L-Araf); the hexuronic acids D-glucuronic acid (D-GlcA) and D-galacturonic acid (D-GalA); the keto sugar 3-deoxy D-manno-octulosonate (D-Kdo); the branched sugar D-apiose; and the amino sugar N-acetyl-alpha-D glucosamine (D-GlcNAc). Other sugar residues, including 3-deoxy-D-lyxo-2-heptulosaric acid (D-Dha) and 3-C-carboxy-5-deoxy-L-xylose (L-aceric acid), are components of plant polysaccharides (12), but their activated forms remain elusive.

Most sugars utilize uridine nucleotides, some are conjugated to guanine nucleotides, and only Kdo is conjugated to cytosine nucleotides. CMP-3-deoxy-D-manno-octulosonate (CMP-Kdo) further represents an exception as it is the only nucleotide sugar that exists in the nucleotide monophosphate sugar configuration, while all others occur as nucleotide diphosphate (NDP) sugars. Nucleotide sugars are formed from carbohydrates from different sources. In plants, the main sources are fructose and fructose-6-phosphate (Fru-6-P) generated through oxygenic photosynthesis in source tissues and in sink tissue through sucrose transported from source organs (12, 98). Additionally, sugars released from storage polysaccharides, recycled from glycoproteins and membrane lipids, and degraded and remodeled from primary cell walls and secondary cell walls are also utilized.

Nucleotide sugars used for plant polysaccharide biosynthesis are mainly derived from Fru-6-P. Among them, UDP-D-glucose (UDP-Glc) and GDP-D-mannose (GDP-Man) can be directly produced from Fru-6-P, while other nucleotide sugars are formed through various interconversions from UDP-Glc or GDP-Man by nucleotide sugar interconversion enzymes. The most abundant nucleotide sugar in vascular plants is UDP-Glc. UDP-D-galactose (UDP-Gal), UDP-L-rhamnose (UDP-Rha), and UDP-D-glucuronic acid (UDP-GlcA) are directly interconverted from UDP-Glc. UDP-GlcA is interconverted to UDP-D-xylose (UDP-Xyl), UDP-D-apiose, and UDP-D-galacturonic acid (UDP-GalA). UDP-L-arabinose (UDP-Ara) results from an interconversion of UDP-Xyl. In contrast to UDP-conjugated sugars, GDP-L-galactose (GDP-Gal) and GDP-L-fucose (GDP-Fuc) are directly produced from GDP-Man (12).

CMP-Kdo is interconverted from D-ribulose-5-phosphate (Ru-5-P) into arabinose-5 phosphate (Ara-5-P), condensed with phosphoenolpyruvate to generate Kdo-8-phosphate (Kdo-8-P), which is finally activated by coupling to CMP (109). $N$ -acetylglucosamine is not part of cell wall polysaccharides but an important component of glycoproteins and glycolipids (19, 86). UDP $N$ -acetyl- $\alpha$ -D-glucosamine (UDP-GlcNAc) is synthesized from $N$ -acetylfructose-6-phosphate (FrcN-6-P), which is then interconverted to glucosamine-6-phosphate (GlcN-6-P). GlcN-6-P is acetylated to produce $N$ -acetylglucosamine-6-P (GlcNAc-6-P) and subsequently converted to GlcNAc-1-P. In the last step, GlcNAc-1-P is converted to UDP-GlcNAc by the action of an $N$ -acetylglucosamine-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase (GlcNAc1pUT) (70).

# 1.2. Glycosylation in the Lumen of the Golgi Apparatus and Endoplasmic Reticulum Depends on Nucleotide Sugar Transport

To overcome this issue, eukaryotes have evolved a family of nucleotide sugar transporters (NSTs), which in plants are part of the NST/triose phosphate translocator (TPT) superfamily (50). NSTs move nucleotide sugars, in exchange for their cognate nucleoside monophosphates (i.e., UMP, GMP, or CMP), from the cytosol into the Golgi apparatus and ER, where luminal GTs reside (22). Several shared features have been identified among characterized NSTs: (a) NSTs are antiporters, and the transport of a nucleotide sugar is most often coupled with the transport of the corresponding nucleoside monophosphate in the reverse direction (22); (b) the entire nucleotide sugar is moved across the membrane; and (c) the transport kinetics are saturable and dependent on concentration, time, and temperature. NST function is crucial for plant life, and failures in this function have been linked to multiple developmental defects (72, 90, 92, 101, 104).

# 2. NSTs BELONG TO THE NST/TPT SUPERFAMILY

NST genes are thought to have originated from the duplication of the EamA domain, also known as domain of unknown function 6 (DUF6) (117). NSTs capable of transporting UDP-Glc, UDP-Gal, and UDP-GlcNAc are present in protists and suggest that these may have been among the first NSTs to evolve (80). Over time, NST genes diversified to produce a range of proteins, each tailored to transport specific nucleotide sugars. Generally, there is a relationship between the complexity of glycoconjugates within a given species and the diversity of nucleotide sugars and NST genes. In plants, NSTs exhibit a high degree of similarity with TPTs, which export phosphorylated C3, C5, and C6 compounds from the chloroplast to the cytoplasm. It has been hypothesized that NSTs are likely the ancestors of TPTs (120).

# 2.1. The NST/TPT Superfamily in Arabidopsis thaliana

30 different nucleotide sugars have been reported in plants, and it is likely that some have not yet been reported due to their low abundance and poor stability. This relatively high number of nucleotide sugars, likely due to the complexity of polysaccharides present in the plant cell wall, also suggests that plants contain several NSTs. The search for NSTs in plants began by looking for orthologous sequences to those found in yeast and animal cells and confirming their function by complementing mutants deficient in NST transport activities. This approach led to the discovery of NSTs encoding GDP-Man, UDP-Gal, and UDP-Glc transporters (9, 10, 78).

Work by Knappe and colleagues (50) aiming to identify the plastidic TPT gene family in Arabidopsis also led to the identification of several genes encoding putative NSTs. This later allowed the description of the NST/TPT superfamily in Arabidopsis, which is composed of 51 genes distributed across six clades (93) (Figure 2). Five of these clades (I, II, III, V, and VI) contain genes confirmed as NSTs. By contrast, clade IV consists exclusively of genes encoding TPTs, which are restricted to plastid-containing organisms. These also include organisms containing apicoplasts present in the Apicomplexa, such as Toxoplasma gondii, which contains an apicoplast phosphate translocator (17) with similarities to Arabidopsis TPTs. Additionally, all members of this clade possess a predicted plastid transit peptide (Table 1), suggesting that they are all plastid specific. This classification highlights the evolutionary diversity and specialization within this gene family, reflecting the complex interplay between plant biochemistry and cellular transport mechanisms.

# 3. THE CHARACTERIZATION OF NST ACTIVITIES

To date, NSTs or families of NSTs that mediate the transport of donor molecules into the Golgi apparatus have been characterized biochemically for most major plant sugars (Table 2). Here, we discuss the NSTs that have been biochemically characterized.

# 3.1.UDP-L-Galactose Transporter

Gal is a monosaccharide present in hemicelluloses, pectins, glycoproteins, and glycolipids, indicating its wide presence in plant glycoconjugates. In 2002, Norambuena et al. (78) showed that UDP-Gal is transported into Golgi vesicles from Pisum sativum. In addition, they described the Arabidopsis gene AtUTr1 as showing transport activity for UDP-Gal and UDP-Glc when expressed in yeast. AtUTr1 also complemented the MDCK-RCA $^{\text{r}}$ cell line, which is deficient in transport of UDP-Gal. Later, Bakker et al. (9) showed that two other Arabidopsis genes, UDP-GalT1 and UDP-GalT2, were able to complement Chinese hamster ovary cells deficient in the transport of UDP-Gal and exhibited UDP-Gal transport activity when expressed in yeast. Finally, Rollwitz et al. (99) analyzed the activity of the Arabidopsis gene NST-KT1, expressing and purifying the protein from yeast and assessing the exchange with UMP in liposomes. The results showed that NST-KT1 transports UDP-Gal in exchange for UMP but not UDP-Glc, UDP-GlcA, or UDP-GlcNAc. In 2014, Rautengarten et al. (93) developed a novel approach to evaluate the transport activity of putative Arabidopsis NSTs, combining proteoliposomes and a nonradiolabeled method that included a set of the main nucleotide sugars present in plants. This approach expanded the analysis of substrate specificities beyond radiolabeled nucleotide sugars, mimicking cellular conditions by allowing the parallel testing of several substrates and giving the transporters a choice of substrate (Figure 2). This significantly enhanced the capability to characterize NSTs that play important roles in plants. The application of this approach resulted in the discovery of six members of the NST/TPT gene superfamily that transport UDP-Gal. The previously described UDP-Gal transporters UDP-GalT2 and NST-KT1 were among the identified genes. Notably, these six transporters also transport UDP-Rha but none of the other tested nucleotide sugars. These findings led to the identification of the UDP-Rha/UDP-Gal transporter (URGT) family (93).

# 3.3.UDP-D-Xylose Transporter

Xylose (Xyl) is an essential component of the plant cell wall polymers xylan and xyloglucan and $N$ -glycosylated proteins (103). UDP-Xyl serves as the activated sugar donor for xylosyltransferases and is generated through the decarboxylation of UDP-GlcA by UDP-XYLOSE SYNTHASE (UXS) (44). In contrast to most nucleotide sugars that are synthesized in the cytosol and require transport into the Golgi lumen, plant UXS family members have been identified in both Golgi and cytosolic fractions (44, 85). Moreover, UDP-Xyl is also formed alongside UDP-apiose (UDP-Api) by UDP-API SYNTHASE/UDP-XYL SYNTHASE (AXS) in the cytosol (71), demonstrating that there are three distinct pathways for UDP-Xyl biosynthesis in plants (12). One could argue that the biosynthesis of UDP-Xyl within the Golgi apparatus suggests that there may not be a need for a Golgi-localized UDP-Xyl transporter to enable the biosynthesis of polysaccharides and glycoproteins containing Xyl. However, contrary to this notion, Golgi-localized UDP-Xyl transporters (UXTs) exist in plants, and their function is crucial for the correct biosynthesis of Xyl-containing wall polysaccharides. In Arabidopsis, three Golgi-localized transporters belonging to NST-KT subfamily C could transport UDP-Xyl and, to a lesser extent, UDP-Arap in vitro when proteoliposomes were preloaded with UMP (13). The study of uxt mutants further confirmed the significance of the cytosolic UDP-Xyl pool and the importance of UXT family members in regulating cell wall composition and protein glycosylation.

# 3.4. UDP-L-Arabinofuranose and UDP-L-Arabinopyranose Transporter

Arabinose (Ara) is a crucial component of the pectic polymers RG-I and RG-II and hydroxyproline-rich glycoproteins, including arabinogalactan proteins (AGPs) and extensins. Ara

is also attached to the xylan backbone of grasses, to xyloglucans in certain plant species, and to signaling molecules and secondary metabolites (53, 65). Ara can exist in two distinct ring forms, the furanose and the pyranose ring. Most Ara in cell wall-associated polymers is present in the furanose form (Araf), though exceptions where Ara occurs in the pyranose form exist. This is unexpected since in solution Ara predominantly exists in the more stable pyranose form (Arap) (53, 65). Additionally, the Ara nucleotide precursor is first formed as a pyranose ring (UDP-Arap). UDP-Arap can be generated either de novo through the conversion of UDP-Xyl via 4-epimerization by UDP-Xyl 4-epimerases (UXEs) in the Golgi apparatus or through the Ara salvage pathway in the cytosol (65). The Golgi-localized pathway, however, appears to be the principal one, given that the Arabidopsis mur4 mutant contains significantly less cell wall Ara (20). Since the predominant form of Ara in plant cell walls is Araf, UDP-Arap needs to be interconverted to UDP-Araf before being incorporated. The enzymes responsible for this conversion are Ara mutases, also known as reversibly glycosylated polypeptides (RGPs) (52, 91). These enzymes are associated with the Golgi apparatus but function in the cytosol (27, 91). Therefore, to provide the GTs synthesizing Araf-containing cell wall polymers and glycoproteins with substrate, UDP-Araf transport from the cytosol into the Golgi lumen is essential. In 2017, Rautengarten et al. (90) identified four members of the NST/TPT family with specificity for UDP-Araf when proteoliposomes were preloaded with UMP. Given that the main route for UDP-Arap synthesis is in the Golgi apparatus, there should also be a transporter moving UDP-Arap out of the Golgi apparatus into the cytosol. However, such a transporter remains to be uncovered.

# 3.5. UDP-D-Glucuronic Acid and UDP-D-Galacturonic Acid Transporter

UDP-GalA is synthesized from UDP-GlcA in a reaction catalyzed by UDP-GlcA 4-epimerase. In plants, this enzyme is found in the Golgi apparatus (39), and UDP-GalA is believed to be synthesized in the Golgi lumen. UDP-GalA is primarily used in the Golgi for pectin biosynthesis (4). Saez-Aguayo et al. (101) screened seeds from mutants deficient in all the NSTs expressed during mucilage biosynthesis, which resulted in the identification of UUAT1, an NST that transports UDP-GlcA and UDP-GalA in vitro. The $K_{\mathrm{M}}$ of the transporter using UMP as the exchange molecule is $1.5 \mathrm{mM}$ , a value that is rather high compared to the $K_{\mathrm{M}}$ of other NSTs, which are in the micromolar range. Nevertheless, the analysis of uuat1 mutants is consistent with the fact that UUAT1 encodes a UDP-GlcA/UDP-GalA transporter (101).

# 3.6.UDP- $N$ -Acetyl- $\cdot \alpha$ D-Glucosamine Transporter

Generally, GlcNAc is a comparatively minor constituent in vascular plants, mostly present in evolutionarily conserved structures such as $N$ -linked and $O$ -linked glycoproteins, glycophosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors, and the glycosyl inositol phosphorylceramide (GIPC) class of sphingolipids. While GlcNAc is a significant part of the extracellular matrix of bacteria, fungi, and algae such as Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, it has not been detected in the cell walls of land plants (74, 89). The production of UDP-GlcNAc, the precursor of GlcNAcylation, involves a series of steps that take place in the cytosol (70). However, the incorporation of GlcNAc into $N$ -linked oligosaccharides of glycoproteins and GIPCs occurs within the Golgi lumen, requiring the transport of UDP-GlcNAc into the lumen. While screening for novel components of cytokinin system function, the rock1 allele was identified (75). Arabidopsis ROCK1 was characterized as an ER-localized UDP-GlcNAc and UDP-GalNAc transporter crucial for regulating cytokinin responses. Additionally, ROCK1 was linked to ER quality control, plausibly providing UDP-GlcNAc and UDP-GalNAc for as-yet-unknown protein glycosylation processes (75). Later, UDP-GlcNAc transporter 1 (UGNT1) was discovered in a transport activity screen of

the Arabidopsis NST/TPT family. This was unexpected given the reported role of ROCK1 in Arabidopsis. Both transporters belong to clade III of the NST/TPT superfamily but do not fall into the same subclade (31). In transport assays under competitive conditions, both UGNT1 and ROCK1 transported significant amounts of UDP-GlcNAc. However, ROCK1 was $\sim 50\%$ less efficient at transporting UDP-GlcNAc than under noncompetitive conditions, and when the kinetic properties of UDP-GlcNAc transport were investigated, ROCK1 did not reach saturability in a concentration-dependent manner (31). These findings indicate that, in contrast to UGNT1, ROCK1 may not effectively transport UDP-GlcNAc under physiological conditions, and it could be inhibited by similar substrates (31).

# 3.7. GDP-D-Mannose Transporter

GDP-Man serves as a substrate for the biosynthesis of $N$ -linked glycan precursors and GPI anchors in the ER. It is also the substrate for the biosynthesis of glucomannan and the Man-containing head group of GIPCs in the Golgi apparatus. Consequently, GDP-Man transport is required to produce glycoconjugates. The Arabidopsis GOLGI-LOCALIZED NUCLEOTIDE SUGAR TRANSPORTER (GONST) family was the first family of NSTs to be identified in plants (2). Based on homology to transporters from other organisms, GONST1-GONST4 were suggested as Golgi-localized GDP-sugar transporters and were confirmed in a yeast complementation assay where all four proteins could functionally complement the yeast GDP-Man transporter mutant $vrg4-2$ (10, 43). In 2013, Mortimer et al. (72) demonstrated that GONST1 can transport all four plant GDP-sugars in vitro. Similarly, GONST2 can transport GDP-Man and to a lesser extent GDP-Gal (92).

# 3.8. GDP-L-Fucose Transporter

Fucose (Fuc) is primarily found in $N$ - and $O$ -linked glycans, glycolipids, and cell wall polysaccharides such as fucoidan, xyloglucan, and pectic RG-II. Fucosylation relies on the activated sugar GDP-Fuc, which is synthesized de novo or can be salvaged from Fuc in the cytosol. To provide luminal GTs with GDP-Fuc, active transport into the endomembrane system is essential for glycosylation reactions since all GDP-sugars are synthesized in the cytosol. The requirement of a GDP-Fuc transporter was first demonstrated for the fucosylation of xyloglucan (123). Later, it was proposed that one of the GDP-sugar transporters of the GONST family is responsible for this transport activity (72). However, GONST1 and GONST2 were unlikely candidates, as mutant phenotypes did not link to fucosylation (72). Furthermore, the phenotypes of plants harboring a mutation in the MURUS 1 (MUR1) gene, which encodes an isoform of the GDP-d-mannose-4,6-dehydratase (GMD) (14) required for the synthesis of GDP-Fuc, suggest that GDP-Fuc is vital for normal plant development. The mur1 mutants are significantly dwarfed and have rosette leaves exhibiting increased brittleness compared to wild-type plants (14, 95). The previously identified GONST subfamily of the NST/TPT family has two subclades. While GONST1 and GONST2 cluster together, GONST3 and GONST4 form a second cluster (30, 92). Using a proteoliposome-based transport approach, Rautengarten et al. (92) demonstrated that, indeed, GONST4 specifically transports GDP-Fuc but not GDP-Man in exchange for GMP. Because of this newly identified function, its name was changed to GDP-Fuc transporter 1 (GFT1) (92).

# 3.9. UDP-D-Glucose Transporter

The transport of UDP-Glc in exchange for UMP was first observed in Golgi vesicles from $P.$ sativum (73). Subsequently, $OsNST1$ was identified as being involved in the transport of UDP-Glc into the Golgi apparatus of rice (126). However, the specific glycoconjugates in the Golgi

apparatus that require UDP-Glc for their biosynthesis remain unclear. AtUTr1, which transports both UDP-Glc and UDP-Gal, is localized to the ER (97). AtUTr3, the closest paralog to AtUTr1, transports UDP-Glc in exchange for UMP in proteoliposomes but does not transport UDPGal, UDP-GlcNAc, or UDP-GlcA (96). Although AtUTr3 is primarily found in the ER, it also shows some distribution in the Golgi apparatus. Interestingly, both AtUTr1 and AtUTr3 are up-regulated by tunicamycin and dithiothreitol (DTT), which are known activators of the unfolded protein response (UPR) (96). The UPR is a quality control system operating in the ER that uses the reglucosylation of unfolded N-glycoproteins to retain them in the calnexin/calreticulin cycle until they are properly folded (54). Since this process requires UDP-Glc, it has been proposed that both NSTs provide the substrate for the reglucosylation of glycoproteins in the ER (96).

# 3.10. Putative CMP-Kdo Transporter

Kdo is a sugar found in pectic RG-II, a vital component of plant cell walls. Kobayashi et al. (51) identified the enzyme responsible for synthesizing CMP-Kdo in plants, suggesting that CMP-Kdo is the nucleotide sugar needed for Kdo integration into RG-II. CMP-Kdo is similar to CMP-sialic acid, a nucleotide sugar required for incorporating sialic acid into glycoproteins in animal cells but not present in plants (23). Using mammalian cell lines deficient in CMP-sialic acid transport, Bakker et al. (8) cloned a CMP-sialic acid transporter (At5g41760) from Arabidopsis, which transported CMP-sialic acid in vitro using yeast vesicles and radiolabeled CMP-sialic acid. Additionally, Takashima et al. (111) identified two rice genes transporting CMP-sialic acid OSCSTLP1 and its closest homolog, OSCSTLP2 (ortholog of Arabidopsis At4g35335). Using the same approach, they showed that OSCSTLP1 but not OSCSTLP2 transported CMP-sialic acid in vitro. They looked for the orthologous genes in Arabidopsis and attempted to obtain homozygous mutants but were unsuccessful. Therefore, they concluded that these genes are essential and might be involved in the transport of CMP-Kdo, as it is needed for the biosynthesis of RG-II, a pectin crucial for plant development (111). However, further studies will be needed to confirm whether any of these genes indeed encode CMP-Kdo transporters.

# 3.11. Rare and Modified Nucleotide Sugars

The complex pectic polymer RG-II contains 13 different sugars, some of which are rare; for example, Dha, which is structurally related to CMP-Kdo, and aceric acid have so far only been found in pectic RG-II (12) and the plant-specific activated branched-chain sugar apiose that is present in RG-II but also in the apiogalacturonan of some aquatic plants (40, 71). The activated form of apiose is UDP-D-apiofuranose [3-C-(hydroxymethyl)-D-glycero-tetrose] (UDP-aiose); however, the substrates required for the incorporation of Dha and aceric acid into RG-II remain unknown. In addition to the well-studied nucleotide sugars, plants contain several less abundant nucleotide sugars (12). It is possible that some of the uncharacterized NSTs in plants may play a role in the transport of these less abundant nucleotide sugars. Given that the lack of UDP-Api is lethal (1, 127), some of these rare sugars may have a severe impact on plant development, and research addressing the transport of these sugars should clearly be undertaken in future.

# 4. NUCLEOTIDE SUGAR TRANSPORT IS INDISPENSABLE FOR MANY ESSENTIAL PROCESSES IN PLANTS

For some time, NST characterization in plants and other species largely relied on biochemical approaches, and in planta evidence for NST functions remained scarce. Now different lines of evidence have revealed the involvement of NSTs in the biosynthesis of glycosphingolipids, $N$ -glycosylated proteins, and cell wall polysaccharides. NST mutant studies link them to many

processes crucial to plant life, including signaling, defense, protein quality control, pollen development, and hormone activity. Here, we describe the approaches and outcomes that have shed light on the role of NSTs in planta.

# 4.1. Phenotypic Analyses of NST Mutants

To obtain a good understanding of how NSTs contribute in vivo to polysaccharide and glycoconjugate biosynthesis, as well as cell wall function, it is essential to identify mutants of NST genes. Since redundancy within the Arabidopsis NST family seems to be widespread, studying higher-order mutants is often required to characterize NST functions. If only a single gene encodes for a specific NST function, mutants are often lethal (92, 104). To overcome this problem, knockdown approaches are typically applied. Additionally, generating NST overexpression lines can produce unique phenotypic outcomes that can help link a function to a specific NST (90, 93). Evaluating such mutants for phenotypic changes and alterations in sugar composition could shed light on the roles of NSTs in vivo. Recently, biochemical and morphological phenotypic consequences of plants with altered NST expression have led to the confirmation of biochemical data and the assignment of NST function where biochemical studies are still lacking. Currently, plant NSTs are best studied in the laboratory model Arabidopsis. However, studies in other species, including Oryza sativa (rice) and Vitis vinifera L. (grapevine), exist (116, 126) and future research efforts should focus on expanding those.

4.1.1. URGTs. To explore URGBT function, transfer DNA (T-DNA) insertional lines for $URGT1$ and $URGT2$ were obtained and loss of gene function confirmed. While Arabidopsis $urgt1$ mutants contained less cell wall Gal than the control, other monosaccharides remained unchanged. By contrast, $urgt2$ mutants displayed less seed mucilage content and an altered mucilage pattern when stained with the pectin dye ruthenium red. They also contained less Rha and GalA, suggesting a decrease in RG-I content; however, no change in Gal or other monosaccharides was observed. When $URGT1$ was overexpressed under the control of a strong, constitutive promoter, an analysis of leaf material showed significant accumulation of pectic galactan but no impact on other wall polymers, while $URGT2$ overexpression caused only minor cell wall changes (93). $URGT1$ expression under the $URGT2$ promoter successfully rescued the seed mucilage phenotype, and $URGT2$ expression under the $URGT1$ promoter complemented the Gal reduction in rosette leaves, suggesting that both are functionally equivalent and their function is likely organ specific and/or dose dependent (24). To further explore the role of $URGT3$ in seed mucilage biosynthesis, an analysis of adherent and soluble seed mucilage extruded from single mutants in the six $URGT$ genes and higher-order mutants of $URGT2$ , $URGT4$ , and $URGT6$ was performed. This analysis exposed discrete functions for $URGT2$ , $URGT4$ , and $URGT6$ in mucilage RG-I biosynthesis. The $urgt2$ $urgt4$ and $urgt2$ $urgt4$ $urgt6$ mutants revealed more, although shorter, RG-I chains, suggesting that $URGT2$ and $URGT4$ are mainly involved in RG-I elongation. Interestingly, the increase of RG-I chains in seed mucilage was complemented by increases in xylan. Conversely, in $urgt6$ , only adherent mucilage biosynthesis was impacted (100).

4.1.2. UXTs. The in vivo function of Arabidopsis UXT1, UXT2, and UXT3 was evaluated using T-DNA lines as well as multiple higher-order mutants. To investigate the impact of UXT mutations, the monosaccharide composition of different plant tissues was analyzed. While cell walls of flowers and leaves showed no differences between mutants and wild type, stem tissue from $uxt1$ mutants exhibited a significant decrease in Xyl and GlcA. This effect was even more pronounced in young stem tissue than in mature stem tissue. Despite changes in monosaccharide composition, $uxt1$ mutants did not display noticeable morphological differences from wild-type plants (29). The $uxt2$ $uxt3$ double mutant also did not show any discernible phenotype. However, the

uxt1 uxt3 double mutant and the uxt1 uxt2 uxt3 triple mutant exhibited delayed growth, darker coloration, and shorter stems (128). In particular, the triple mutant had altered xylan content and a fine structure with collapsed vessels and thin interfascicular fiber cell walls similar to those seen in irx mutants (18, 88). In the uxt triple mutant, pollination was also defective, likely due to cell wall alterations leading to reduced filament growth and anther dehiscence (128). One aspect that has not yet been directly examined is how a lack of UDP-Xyl caused by mutations in UXTs impacts xyloglucan content and composition. Future studies could address this to help answer questions about possible substrate channeling involving NSTs.

4.1.3. UAfTs. The role of the four Arabidopsis UDP-L-arabinofuranose transporters (UAfTs) was investigated by analyzing mutant lines for each member. When grown under standard conditions, single mutants appeared morphologically indistinguishable from wild type. The monosaccharide composition of dry seeds was also unaffected, and only the cell walls of $uaf4$ mutant leaves contained significantly less Ara, strongly indicating possible redundant functions among the UAfTs (90). To further study UAfT functions, they were also fused to yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) and expressed in Arabidopsis under the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. Plants expressing the 35Spro:UAfT-YFP constructs exhibited stunted growth and early leaf yellowing, particularly in lines with a strong YFP signal. Analysis of leaf cell wall monosaccharide content revealed a significant increase in Ara of up to $30\%$ , in the UAfT2, UAfT3, and UAfT4 overexpression lines, with related phenotypic defects primarily seen in UAfT3 and UAfT4 and occasionally in UAfT2 overexpressors. Interestingly, glyceme profiling of UAfT3 and UAfT4 overexpression lines with a large suite of cell wall antibodies revealed differences in pectin and AGP epitope binding between these two lines (90). However, to evaluate the biological role of each of the individual UAfTs in detail, higher-order mutants must be investigated.

4.1.4. UUAT1. In a screen for Arabidopsis NSTs involved in seed mucilage biosynthesis, UUAT1 was identified (101). When the monosaccharide composition of the adherent and soluble seed mucilage of the uuat1 mutant were analyzed, less GalA, Rha, and Xyl were found in the soluble mucilage, and distal cell walls contained less arabinan. While Ara levels were also lower in the cell walls of roots and pollen tubes, no changes in GalA or Xyl content was noted in other tissues and cell types. Surprisingly, the GlcA content of stem glucuronoxylan remained unchanged. Interestingly, however, there was an increase in homogalacturonan methylation in the seed mucilage of uuat1 mutant plants. This suggests that the absence of UUAT1 has a significant impact on seed mucilage composition but causes pleiotropic effects on the plant extracellular matrix (101). Although only UUAT1 was identified in the Arabidopsis seed mucilage screen, it is conceivable that other members of the same NST subclade may have related functions in other tissues, but this is yet to be addressed.

4.1.5. GONST1 and GONST2. GONST1 was initially believed to provide GDP-Man for cell wall polysaccharides such as glucomannan (72). Arabidopsis gonst1 T-DNA insertional mutants display severe growth phenotypes; plants are dwarfed, have impaired seed set, and show constitutive plant defense responses. Furthermore, as they grow poorly on soil, they must be propagated in sterile culture. Surprisingly, though, gonst1 mutants revealed no noticeable changes in cell wall polysaccharides apart from cellulose. Instead, Man-containing GIPCs were impacted in gonst1, highlighting the pivotal role of GONST1 in providing GDP-Man for GIPC biosynthesis (72). GONST2, the closely related homolog of GONST1, also transports GDP-Man. This was confirmed through both in vitro assays (72, 92) and mutant studies (48, 72). In contrast to gonst1 mutants, the gonst2-1 mutant resembles wild type. However, a gonst1-1 gonst2-1 double mutant exhibits more severe developmental defects than gonst1 alone, indicating functional overlap

between these two transporters for GIPC glycosylation (20, 25). Since gonst2 mutants have only minor modifications to their GIPCs, they were used to study whether gonst mutants are more pathogen resistant. It was shown that gonst2 has increased resistance to the biotrophic pathogen Golovinomyces orontii but not to the necrotrophic pathogen Botrytis cinerea. Notably, GONST2 expression under the GONST1 promoter successfully rescued the gonst1 phenotype, indicating that they have the same function, though in a cell type-specific and/or dose-dependent manner (48).

4.1.6. GFT1 and GGLT1. GFT1 and GDP-Gal transporter 1 (GGLT1) (previously named GONST4 and GONST3, respectively), which are the other two GDP transporters from NST subfamily III, are closely related but seemingly have no functional copies in contrast to most other NSTs (Figure 2). Consequently, loss-of-function mutants of these NSTs are lethal (92, 104). This prompted their study through RNA interference using hairpin $(bp)$ constructs specifically targeting their transcripts. Soon after selection, $50\%$ of GFT1-silenced plants die, while surviving plants with different degrees of silencing contain very little cell wall Fuc, and, similarly, Fuc in most proteins is lacking. These reductions in Fuc cause severe developmental defects with strongly inhibited growth and plants being darker in color, confirming the requirement of Fuc transport for plant development (92). Silencing Arabidopsis GGLT1 reduces L-Gal levels in RG-II by up to $50\%$ , which causes decreased cross-linking and stability of RG-II dimers. GGLT1-silenced plants are also dwarfed, but this phenotype can be reversed by watering with increased boric acid levels, further corroborating its function as a GDP-Gal transporter. Similar to the mur1 mutant, where Fuc is replaced by L-Gal in RG-II, cell wall integrity is compromised in bpGGLT1 plants exposed to salt stress (104).

4.1.7. UGNT1 and ROCK1. UGNT1 and ROCK1 have been implicated in UDP-GlcNAc transport in the Golgi apparatus and ER, respectively. The $rock1$ mutant has a significantly increased size and activity of the inflorescence meristem, implying that $ROCK1$ is a negative regulator of shoot meristem development. $ROCK1$ seems to control cytokinin activity in meristematic cells since levels of endogenous cytokinins and cytokinin response genes are increased in $rock1$ inflorescences (75). Mutants in UGNT1, however, did not show obvious morphological phenotypes (31). When complex $N$ -glycans in leaves and sphingolipid profiles from seeds were tested in $rock1$ and $ugnt1$ mutants, both $N$ -glycan and sphingolipid patterns were altered in $ugnt1$ mutants. The $N$ -glycopeptide profiles of $ugnt1$ mutants showed a depletion of complex and hybrid $N$ -glycans, with an abundance of high-Man structures, indicating a lack of GlcNAc addition in the Golgi apparatus. Additionally, sphingolipid profiling of seeds demonstrated that the biosynthesis of GlcNAc-containing GIPCs is also dependent on UGNT1 (31). However, $N$ -glycan or GIPC profiles were not altered in $rock1$ mutants (14, 32). No reports of GlcNAc addition onto other structures beyond GlcNAc-containing $N$ -glycans and GIPCs have been documented for the endomembrane system yet. While the role of ROCK1 as a metabolic transporter in the ER is well-established, it may plausibly transport a different substrate with structural similarities to UDP-sugars into the ER. Transporters of the NST/TPT family have diverse substrate specificities, also transporting molecules such as triose phosphate and D-xylulose 5-phosphate into the plastid (46), thus making a different role in the ER for ROCK1 feasible.

4.1.8. UTr1 and UTr3. UTr1 and UTr3, two Arabidopsis NSTs belonging to subclade II, localize to the ER and to the ER and Golgi apparatus, respectively, and are linked to the UPR, suggesting a role during ER stress. While single mutants have no morphological defects, a double mutant targeting both genes is lethal (96, 97). The $utr1$ $utr3$ combination was not transmitted through pollen and had low transmission through ovules, with abnormalities observed in male and female

germ line development. These findings indicate that AtUTr1 and AtUTr3 are vital for pollen development and embryo sac progression in Arabidopsis (96).

4.1.9. UTr7. Loss-of-function mutants of Arabidopsis AtUTr7, another transporter belonging to NST subfamily III, showed accelerated lateral root growth and abnormal root hairs when grown in high sucrose. Additionally, there were differences in the distribution of low methyl-esterified homogalacturonan when lateral root tips of the mutant were compared to wild-type plants by immunolabeling with the JIM5 antibody. However, no changes to the total monosaccharide composition of 3-week-old seedlings were observed, indicating that either the transporter is tissue specific or transport is not directly linked to cell wall polymer composition (42).

# 4.2. Transcriptomic Analyses Unravel Other Potential Roles of NSTs

By examining the gene expression profiles of NSTs across different developmental stages and physiological responses, it is possible to delve into the roles of NSTs. These analyses complement NST mutant studies and offer a broader understanding of NST functions. For instance, transcriptomic data have shown that certain NSTs are upregulated during specific developmental stages, such as gametogenesis, where URT2, URT5, UTr1, UTr5, UTr4, and UTr7 exhibit increased expression (13). Similarly, in root cap development, genes such as URT4, UTr2, GONST2, and GGLT are upregulated, suggesting roles in this crucial developmental process (34). In the context of tension wood formation, which is vital for the mechanical support of trees, transcriptomic analyses have implicated UUAT3 and URT2 as important players (5). Furthermore, during grape development, the response to gibberellin involves significant upregulation of UXT1, suggesting its role in hormone-mediated growth processes (108). Additionally, the formation of exine, a component of pollen walls, is critically dependent on ROCK1, as revealed through transcriptomic studies (119). These findings underscore the multifaceted functions of NSTs in regulating essential plant processes, from reproduction and development to stress responses and hormonal regulation.

# 4.3. Genome-Wide Studies Hint at the Importance of NSTs for Specific Traits in Plants

Genome-wide studies provide evidence for the importance of NSTs for specific traits in plants. Genome-wide association studies (GWASs) allow the identification of genetic variants associated with specific traits. Some traits in plants have been associated with changes in NST genes, according to various studies. For example, UXT1 appears in a GWAS investigating variation in ecological traits in natural populations of Populus trichocarpa, which contain single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) associated with bud set, $100\%$ leaf yellowing, and leaf drop (69). In another study, a GWAS was performed in barley to investigate metabolite accumulation in a natural population. High levels of up to $65\%$ of the explained phenotypic variance of the respective metabolites could be attributed to a single methylation quantitative trait locus (mQTL) containing the ortholog of AtUTr5 (35). In addition, similar studies can be performed using transcriptome-wide association studies (TWASs). Li et al. (57) identified URGT2 in a TWAS aimed at uncovering the genetic regulation of fiber cell development in cotton.

SNPs represent a variation in the DNA sequence where a single nucleotide differs from the reference. In NST genes, SNPs can alter one or more codons, potentially affecting transporter function, including substrate specificity, efficiency, and localization. These changes can influence nucleotide sugar availability for biosynthesis, impacting cell wall composition, developmental processes, and stress responses. Additionally, SNPs might affect gene expression levels or be linked to nearby causal variants, influencing multiple traits (47). Follow-up studies are needed to confirm these mechanisms and to further elucidate the critical roles that NSTs play in plant biology.

# 5. REGULATION OF NST FUNCTION IN PLANTA

The biochemical and functional characterization of plant NSTs has seen major developments over the past decade, and phenotypical analyses of NST mutants from multimember families have indicated that nucleotide sugar transport and polysaccharide and glycoconjugate biosynthesis could be regulated and controlled in part by specific expression patterns (24, 100) but that they may also be regulated by other mechanisms present only in planta (29, 65, 90, 93). Alongside specific expression patterns, we discuss the suborganelle localization of NSTs and plausible interactions of NSTs with components of the glycosylation machinery.

# 5.1. Transcriptional and Posttranscriptional Regulation

NSTs are critical components in many biological processes, and their expression and activity are tightly regulated through multiple mechanisms. For instance, URGBT3 and ROCK1 are subject to circadian regulation, meaning their expression levels fluctuate according to the daily light-dark cycle, synchronizing their functions with the plant's metabolic needs over a 24-h period (45). Additionally, the expression of UXT1 is regulated by long intergenic noncoding RNAs (lincRNAs), which interact with transcriptional machinery to modulate gene expression levels under different conditions (33). Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) also play a role in regulating UAfT2, influencing its expression through mechanisms such as chromatin remodeling and transcriptional interference (59). MicroRNAs (miRNAs) regulate UAfT3 by binding to complementary sequences in its messenger RNA (mRNA), leading to degradation or inhibition of translation, thus allowing precise control of UAfT3 levels (122).

Moreover, ubiquitylation-mediated regulation affects UAfT3, marking the protein for degradation by the proteasome to maintain appropriate transporter levels and prevent cellular toxicity (62). The expression of the putative CMP-Kdo transporter (At4g35335) is regulated via recursive splicing, a splicing mechanism that removes a single intron from a precursor mRNA (pre-mRNA) transcript by two consecutive splicing steps (58). These regulatory processes highlight the complexity of NST regulation and underscore the importance of precise control mechanisms in maintaining cellular homeostasis and responding to environmental and developmental signals.

# 5.2. Mutant Analyses Suggest Regulation via Distinct Suborganelle Localization and Complex Formation

Regulation of NST function may also occur at the protein level. It is already well-established that interactions between GTs and complex formation involving GTs are necessary for proper cell wall biosynthesis in plants (6, 26, 61, 94, 125). Similarly, NSTs involved in the transport of UDP-Gal and UDP-Rha are suggested to form homodimers (93), an observation also reported for mammalian NSTs (63). Furthermore, there is convincing evidence that the localization of GTs to specific Golgi cisternae is essential for polysaccharide processing (25, 84, 102), and similar mechanisms can plausibly be hypothesized for NSTs. Earlier studies have stimulated discussions about substrate channeling being involved in polysaccharide and cell wall biosynthesis (105-107). This phenomenon has been described and experimentally validated for other key plant metabolic pathways, including glycolysis and photosynthesis (110). The selectivity of NSTs for specific nucleotide sugars along with evidence proposing their localization to specific sub-Golgi compartments (36) may indeed argue that the flow of nucleotide sugars is controlled to specific GTs and biosynthetic pathways through substrate channeling. This would mean that an individual NST may be responsible for substrate delivery for a particular pathway because its activity is spatially linked to the activity of one or a few GTs. This has been shown for the mammalian UDP-GlcNAc transporter

SLC35A3 and the UDP-Gal transporter SLC35A2, both of which form glycosylation-related complexes with mannoside acetylglucosaminyltransferases in the Golgi apparatus (66, 67). In Arabidopsis, NSTs have been observed to specifically impact certain wall polymers but not others (29, 72, 90, 93, 101). Such observations could also hint at substrate channeling, a phenomenon that could be particularly important for enzymes producing homopolymers such as the GAUT, IRXL, and GALS proteins (6, 28, 115). These are typically nonprocessive enzymes in vitro that release their product after each round of catalysis (3, 124). It can be speculated that interactions with a transporter or other components of their synthetic pathways allow these nonprocessive synthases to operate more efficiently. However, it is currently unclear to what extent NST-mediated transport is regulated by complex formation and NST organization in the Golgi apparatus. Future studies need to clarify how NSTs are organized and whether they occur in multiprotein complexes with relevant enzymes participating in glycan biosynthetic pathways.

# 6. NST/TPT STRUCTURE AND FUNCTION

# 6.1. The Crystal Structure of NST and TPT Proteins

The first crystal structure of an NST protein, the yeast GDP-Man transporter, was successfully determined in 2017 by Parker & Newstead (82). This groundbreaking study revealed that most of the protein is embedded within the membrane and confirmed the presence of 10 transmembrane domains (TMDs). In the same year, Lee et al. (55) elucidated the crystal structure of a TPT, demonstrating that it forms a dimeric structure. Subsequently, Parker et al. (81) showed that two monoolein lipid molecules promote the formation of a GDP-Man transporter dimer. Further progress has been made in determining the structures of NSTs that transport CMP-sialic acid. Two protein structures were solved: one from mouse (2) and another from Zea mays (77). The plant CMP-sialic acid transporter corresponds to an NST, which is likely the putative CMP-Kdo transporter since no sialic acid is present in plants.

# 6.2. The Transport Mechanism of NSTs and TPTs

NSTs and TPTs function as antiporters, exchanging substrates across membranes at a 1:1 ratio. This transport occurs through a rocker switch model, where symmetry-related conformational changes alternately expose binding sites on either side of the membrane (2, 55).

The crystal structures of the GDP-Man transporter and TPTs form dimers, but whether dimerization is essential for activity is still debated. Parker et al. (81) found that monomers retain activity even when mixed with inactive counterparts, suggesting that dimerization may not be required. However, the question of whether the dimers in the proteoliposome assay formed a 1:1 ratio of active to inactive monomers remains unresolved. Hadley et al. (41), using modeling studies, proposed that in CMP-sialic acid transport, the monomers in a dimer may cooperate, with each monomer binding its substrate on opposite sides of the membrane and then translocating them through coordinated conformational changes. The characterization of crystal structures of NSTs/TPTs has been a significant step forward in understanding their function, but whether they work as monomers or dimers requires further investigation.

# 7. OUTSTANDING QUESTIONS TO BE ADDRESSED

Studies around nucleotide sugar synthesis and transport functions have progressed significantly. However, despite recent advancements, compelling questions about this important transporter family remain unanswered. For example, the temporal and spatial dynamics of glycan biosynthesis and organization are not well-understood, and the substrates of many uncharacterized and orphan proteins of the NST/TPT family remain a mystery. In this section, we highlight some of the open questions to stimulate progress toward understanding NST function and regulation. Experimental advances in computational and network modeling, accompanied by new genomic resources and biochemical tools, will certainly boost this area of study. The new insight gained from such studies will advance the field of glycobiology, potentially informing studies in other plant species and organisms, and aid the development of a biological toolbox for applications involving modeling and engineering of plants for the production of synthetic biopolymers or functional foods.

# 7.1. Uncharacterized and Orphan Members of the NST/TPT Superfamily and Strategies to Examine Their Functions

Despite significant progress in understanding the function of NSTs in the model plant Arabidopsis, not all NSTs have been ascribed to a particular function. To date, 19 members covering all clades of the NST/TPT family remain uncharacterized. One issue contributing to the difficulties in assessing their function may be that they transport a substrate that has not been available for assays. So far, 15 nucleotide or nucleotide sugar substrates have been tested in proteoliposome assays, but some substrates are still not available. For instance, CMP-Kdo is labile, and despite the identification of putative CMP-Kdo transporters, their biochemical activity remains to be shown. Another example is GDP-Gal, which could not be tested in biochemical assays, as the transporter could not be expressed in yeast, although genetic analyses clearly indicate that At1g76340 is a transporter for GDP-Gal. Therefore, some of the orphan NSTs may remain uncharacterized due to the lack of appropriate substrate and counter-exchange substrate to assess their biochemical function. It is also possible that proteins in the NST/TPT family could transport substrates other than nucleotide sugars that have similar structures.

One approach to assess the potential role of the orphan NSTs is to perform genetic analysis using mutants. As a matter of fact, we mentioned in Section 4.1 that the characterization of mutants has been a tremendous tool in understanding the role of NSTs in plants. Therefore, collecting mutants for the orphan genes should provide some insights into their function. However, a drawback to this approach is the possibility that mutations in some of these genes may lead to lethal phenotypes, as has been observed for GFT1, GGLT1, and the putative CMP-Kdo transporters (92, 104, 111). This constraint needs to be considered when analyzing mutants for orphan NSTs. Other tools that can be useful in approaching the potential function of orphan NSTs include gene coexpression analysis. For instance, Temple et al. (113) reported the identification of S-adenosylmethionine transporters involved in the methylesterification of polysaccharides, including pectin. Interestingly, they performed gene coexpression analyses showing that GoSAMT1 and GoSAMT2 are coexpressed with UUAT1, UUAT3, and URGT2, which are NSTs involved in the transport of UDP-uronic acids and UDP-Rha, substrates required for pectin biosynthesis (83, 93, 101). Furthermore, genes reported to have pectin methyltransferase activity were also coexpressed with GoSAMTs and NSTs, suggesting that a gene coexpression network involved in the biosynthesis and methylesterification of pectin may be identified through gene coexpression analysis. This evidence supports the use of this approach to identify the roles of orphan NSTs.

# 7.2. NSTs as Part of the Toolbox for Synthetic Biology and Glycoengineering: Where Can New Knowledge Take Us?

Sugars and glycosylated products are key in many industries, including food, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, healthcare, and agriculture. Orthogonal hosts such as Escherichia coli, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Pichia pastoris, and others can be leveraged to produce tailored sugars that are produced to fit for a specific purpose (87). Such microbial systems can be used to heterologously synthesize entire cell wall polymers such as mannan and glucomannan (118). Plants, however, are more challenging to work with and can show adverse phenotypes but still offer promising options for producing sugars and glycoconjugates due to their ability to generate a wide range of sugar-derived compounds through photosynthesis. This contrasts with processes such as chemical synthesis or microbial fermentation, which have limitations in yield and sugar chemistry. As a promising alternative, genetic engineering has been proposed (15, 21, 112). Plants, for example, can be genetically modified to produce nonnative nucleotide sugars such as mammalian

CMP-sialic acid; thereby, the introduction of the CMP-sialic acid pathway enabled the production of mammalian sialylated proteins in plants for various applications (23).

Nucleotide sugars are the sugar donors for glycosylation reactions, and as such they and their respective transporters are linked with primary and specialized metabolism, which prompts questions of how nucleotide sugar concentrations, flow, and incorporation can be used to influence glycosylation. Modulating NST expression levels through overexpression or downregulation could boost or reduce nucleotide sugar levels, thereby tailoring substrate concentrations available for cell wall or glycoconjugate biosynthesis. In this way, the targeting of glycans toward specific polymers can be used to improve agronomic traits and boost the production of bioproducts. For example, with the goal of improving plant biomass for biofuel production, gene stacking approaches have been successfully applied. The overexpression of the UDP-glucose 4-epimerase UGE2 (11) together with the galactan synthase GALS1 (28, 60) resulted in plants containing $80\%$ more cell wall Gal in stems without noticeable morphological abnormalities (37). When URGT1 transporting UDP-Gal and UDP-Rha was overexpressed alongside UGE2 and GALS1, some plants accumulated up to $150\%$ Gal, with lines with the highest Gal contents showing substantial increases in URGT1 expression, highlighting the importance of upregulating the transporter for galactan production (7). Similarly, the Ara concentration in cell wall extracts increased by up to $30\%$ in Arabidopsis when UDP-Araf transporters were overexpressed (90). Conversely, downregulation and loss-of-function strategies could also be implemented. For example, the Arabidopsis uxt1 mutant contains significantly reduced stem xylan content but produced no obvious phenotypic alterations (29). However, modifying NST expression can also lead to phenotypic anomalies or impact plant fitness due to the central role that NSTs hold in the glycosylation pathway (72, 90, 92, 100, 101, 104). Instead, relocating cytosolic nucleotide sugar pathways to other compartments or subcompartments could be a strategy to enhance production efficiency between organelles without the need to alter NST expression. Moving forward, integrating diverse engineering strategies to redirect flux through nucleotide sugars, eliminate competing pathways, and stably integrate customized NSTs into the genome using different orthogonal hosts could be crucial for maximizing the potential of glycoengineering and boosting the production of sugar-derived compounds and novel plant-based biocomposites in future.

# SUMMARY POINTS

1. Significant progress has been made over the last two decades in uncovering nucleotide sugar transport activities and subsequently validating these activities in planta.   
2. Nucleotide sugar transporters (NSTs) are indispensable for the biosynthesis of many diverse glycoconjugates and polysaccharides that in turn impact a myriad of essential functions in plants.   
3. Recently determined crystal structures of NSTs have revealed key sequence motifs for ligand recognition and transport and open the door for a mechanistic understanding of these membrane proteins.   
4. More research is required to elucidate the dynamics and regulation of NSTs within the endomembrane as well as the substrate specificities of NST/triose phosphate translocator (TPT) superfamily members that are yet to be uncovered.   
5. The latest progress in genomics, biochemistry, and the development of analytical tools and instrumentation offers fascinating opportunities to answer remaining questions

about NST function and regulation and provide a foundation for plausible applications in glycoengineering.
