# Abstract

Salicylic acid (SA) biosynthesis in plants occurs via the isochorismate synthase (ICS) and phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) pathways. The critical steps from benzyl-CoA to SA in the PAL-mediated pathway remain unknown. To probe benzenoid metabolism, we generated CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockouts of benzaldehyde synthase in poplar. These plants produce less benzyl benzoate, benzyl salicylate and SA, yet accumulate more benzoic acid. We show that HSR203J encodes a carboxylesterase that specifically hydrolyzes benzyl salicylate. Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) of HSR203J in Nicotiana benthamiana led to reduced benzyl salicylate hydrolysis to SA. Based on these data, we propose a biosynthesis model and provide evidence that benzoyl-CoA is esterified to benzyl benzoate and converted to benzyl salicylate, which then releases SA. In addition, we identified a pathogen-induced cytochrome P450 encoded by HSR515 as a putative benzyl benzoate 2-hydroxylase. VIGS-mediated suppression of HSR515 in N. benthamiana reduced the conversion of benzyl benzoate to SA. Phylogenetic analyses indicated that Brassicaceae genomes do not contain HSR203J and HSR515 orthologs, whereas these genes are present in other vascular plants. These findings represent an important advance in our understanding of SA biosynthesis and identify missing steps in the PAL-mediated SA biosynthetic pathway. 

# Introduction

Salicylic acid (SA) is a critical plant hormone with roles in plant defense and development (Raskin 1992; Peng et al. 2021). It was originally identified in Salix bark extracts based on its analgesic properties in traditional medicine (Ding and Ding 2020; Mishra and Baek 2021). Its function as a signal in inducing immunity against plant viruses and other pathogens was discovered much later, but since then its role in plant defense has been the focus of intense research (Ding and Ding 2020; Peng et al. 2021). As a key phytohormone, SA has been shown to be involved in regulating leaf senescence (Zhang et al. 2013; Lihavainen et al. 2023), flowering timing and development (Martínez et al. 2004; Luo et al. 2022), seed germination (Rajjou et al. 2006; Lee et al. 2010), root growth and symbiosis (Lebeis et al. 2015; Pasternak et al. 2019), heat production via thermogenesis (Raskin et al. 1989), and other developmental processes including growth-defense tradeoffs (Rivas-San Vicente and Plasencia 2011; van Butselaar and Van den Ackerveken 2020). However, it is primarily known for its functions in orchestrating both local and systemic defense responses against viruses, bacteria, and fungi (Peng et al. 2021). In local defense, SA participates in both pattern-triggered immunity and effector-triggered immunity (ETI) (Liu et al. 2020), and controls programed cell death in ETI (Radojič ić et al. 2018). In 

systemic defense, SA induces the biosynthesis of the mobile signal, N-hydroxypipecolic acid in local tissue and is required for downstream transcription factor activation in systemic tissues (Huang et al. 2020; Nair et al. 2021). Key transcriptional regulators for the induction of SA biosynthesis have been described, including SAR-DEFICIENT 1 (SARD1) and CALMODULIN-BINDING PROTEIN 60-LIKEg (CBP60g), which directly activate SA biosynthesis genes in Arabidopsis thaliana (Truman et al. 2013; Peng et al. 2021) and in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) (Takagi et al. 2022). 

Despite its physiological importance and simple structure, the biosynthesis of SA is not completely understood. Two biosynthetic routes are known, beginning with either isochorismate or phenylalanine depending on the species (Peng et al. 2021). The isochorismatederived pathway was elucidated in Arabidopsis (Rekhter et al. 2019; Torrens-Spence et al. 2019). Isochorismate Synthase 1 (ICS1/ SALICYLIC ACID INDUCTION DEFICIENT 2, SID2) in the chloroplasts produces isochorismate from chorismate, which is transported to the cytosol and conjugated with glutamate by an amidotransferase (AVRPPHB SUSCEPTIBLE 3, PBS3). Isochorismoyl–glutamate A is then cleaved by the BAHD acyltransferase (ENHANCED PSEUDOMONAS SUSCEPTIBILITY 1, EPS1) or decays spontaneously to release free SA (Supplementary Fig. S1, Rekhter et al. 2019; Torrens-Spence et al. 2019). Arabidopsis ics1/sid2 mutants have greatly reduced SA 

content and are deficient in defense responses including systemic acquired resistance (Garcion et al. 2008). Such mutants can still produce small amounts of SA, however, suggesting an isochorismate-independent pathway (Peng et al. 2021; Wu et al. 2023). Importantly, neither EPS1 nor PBS3 orthologs are found outside of the Brassicaceae (Holland et al. 2019; Torrens-Spence et al. 2019), suggesting that the ICS-mediated pathway is not ubiquitous in the plant kingdom (Peng et al. 2021; Ullah et al. 2023). 

The second biosynthetic pathway to SA starts with phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL). Virus infection induces the expression of PAL genes in tobacco (Ogawa et al. 2006), and isotopic labeling experiments showed that SA can be synthesized from phenylalanine as well as benzoic acid (BA) (Chong et al. 2001). PAL produces trans-cinnamic acid, which is ligated to coenzyme A (CoA) by cinnamoyl-CoA ligase (CNL) to produce cinnamoyl-CoA (Wang et al. 2025a). This is then converted to benzoyl-CoA in the peroxisome via $\beta$ -oxidation. In tobacco, the $\beta$ -oxidative enzymes cinnamoyl-CoA hydratase/dehydrogenase (CHD) and 3-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase (KAT), as well as CNL, are all induced by elicitor treatment (Kotera et al. 2023). Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) of CHD and KAT in Nicotiana benthamiana led to reduced pathogen-induced SA accumulation (Kotera et al. 2023). Similarly, in rice the abnormal inflorescence meristem 1 gene was shown to encode a 3-hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase similar to CHD and be required for $\beta$ -oxidation as well as for SA biosynthesis (Xu et al. 2017). These studies demonstrate that the PAL route for SA biosynthesis begins with cinnamate, which is then converted to benzoyl-CoA via $\beta$ -oxidation. The colocalization of CNL, CHD, and KAT enzymes to peroxisomes supports this pathway (Kotera et al. 2023). The final steps of this pathway, including how benzyl-CoA is converted to SA, have not been clarified. While early work with crude protein extracts demonstrated a benzoate 2-hydroxylase activity (León et al. 1995), such an enzyme or its corresponding gene has not been identified. 

Additional work in tobacco and N. benthamiana identified 2 other genes needed for the SA-induced hypersensitive response and SA biosynthesis, HSR201 and HSR203J. HSR201 was shown to encode a benzoyl-CoA: benzyl alcohol O-benzoyltransferase (BEBT), a BAHD-type transferase that synthesizes benzyl benzoate from benzyl alcohol and benzyl-CoA (D’Auria et al. 2002). VIGS of HSR201 in N. benthamiana reduces SA accumulation after pathogen-derived effector infection (Takagi et al. 2022). As demonstrated for $\beta$ -oxidation enzymes, HSR201 is also localized to peroxisomes and this localization is required for SA production (Kotera et al. 2024). Like HSR201, HSR203J is rapidly induced in tobacco after challenge with an incompatible pathogen (Pontier et al. 1994). This gene encodes a carboxylesterase (CXE) and is required for pathogen-induced SA accumulation in N. benthamiana (Baudouin et al. 1997; Takagi et al. 2022). CXEs comprise a large gene family defined by a common GXSXG motif (Marshall et al. 2003), and involved in diverse biological process including plant growth and development, secondary metabolite biosynthesis and stress responses (Marshall et al. 2003; Cao et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2022). The biochemical role of HSR203J has not been established, although it encodes an esterase capable of hydrolyzing a model substrate (Baudouin et al. 1997). Its in planta substrate has not been identified, however, and how these genes contribute to SA synthesis from benzoyl-CoA or other metabolites has yet to be resolved. 

While investigating the biosynthesis of salicyl alcohol glucoside-derived antiherbivore metabolites (salicinoids) found in Populus and Salix, we discovered connections between benzenoid metabolism and SA accumulation. We demonstrated that 

when salicinoid biosynthesis is disrupted via gene editing of a key uridine diphosphate (UDP)-glucosyl transferase (UGT) enzyme, SA content increases substantially (Gordon et al. 2022), potentially due to the buildup of an unstable biosynthetic salicylate intermediate. Whether this represents the indirect effects of an interrupted metabolic pathway or an alternative SA biosynthetic pathway was not clear. Furthermore, a recent study by Huang et al. (2022) identified a dimeric enzyme, benzaldehyde synthase, which uses nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) to reduce benzoyl-CoA to benzaldehyde. In vivo labeling in petunia flower petals showed a conversion of benzaldehyde to benzyl alcohol, thus providing the other precursor needed to produce benzyl benzoate (Huang et al. 2022). This enzyme was identified in petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida), but also present in Arabidopsis, almond (Prunus dulcis), and tomato (Solanum lycopersicon). This conservation of function hints at a role in primary metabolism of plants. 

These data provide the basis for a proposed biosynthetic pathway from benzoyl-CoA to SA. We hypothesized that benzyl benzoate is a central intermediate for SA as proposed for salicinoids, and is formed by BEBT (HSR201) from benzoyl-CoA and benzyl alcohol derived from benzoyl-CoA via benzaldehyde. Benzyl benzoate is the proposed substrate for a 2-hydroxylase enzyme, thus giving rise to benzyl salicylate. Benzyl salicylate is then hydrolyzed by the HSR203J esterase to release SA (Fig. 1). Here, we provide evidence for this route in SA biosynthesis. Using benzaldehyde synthase knockout poplars, we first demonstrate that benzaldehyde is required for SA biosynthesis, confirming that downstream benzenoids benzyl alcohol and benzyl benzoate are required. We then show that N. benthamiana HSR203J encodes a benzyl salicylate esterase (BSE), which releases SA and that this gene is found in a broad diversity of plants. HSR203J orthologs tested in other species all have this benzyl salicylate-hydrolyzing activity, and silencing HSR203J in N. benthamiana reduced SA production from benzyl salicylate. We also show that benzyl benzoate is converted to SA in pathogen-infected leaves in N. benthamiana, and identify a candidate pathogen-induced cytochrome P450 (CYP) hydroxylase that contributes to SA biosynthesis in vivo. Our work provides a roadmap of how benzoyl-CoA is converted to SA and fills a major gap in our knowledge of the PAL pathway for SA biosynthesis. 

# Results

# CRISPR/Cas9 knockout of benzaldehyde synthase in poplars sharply reduces SA content and increases BA and BA-glucoside accumulation

To probe the role of benzaldehyde synthase in benzenoid metabolism and SA biosynthesis in poplar, we searched the Populus genome database for benzaldehyde synthase genes. The black poplar (Populus trichocarpa) genome contains 1 α subunit (Potri.016G035200) and $2 \beta$ subunit-encoding genes (Potri.001G327800 and Potri.017G067332). Thus, we generated transgenic poplar knockouts using CRISPR/ Cas9 directed against the α subunit (BAldSα) in a transformable hybrid poplar clone (P. tremula $\times \mathrm { P }$ . alba clone INRA 717-1 B4). Two gRNAs in exon 1 and exon 2 were designed to target the common sequences in both alleles of this hybrid. Positive transformants were selected and mutations confirmed by PCR-cloning and sequencing the target area. Six BAldSα knockout lines with clear indels, mostly $+ 1$ insertions, were confirmed (Supplementary Fig. S2 and Table S1). Line BAldSα-3 had a 12-nucleotide deletion which did not result in a frame shift. Thus, it likely had 1 functional allele and showed intermediate

The knockout plants showed no obvious morphological differences from the wild-type in tissue culture or in the greenhouse, but they showed reduced levels of salicinoids including salicin, indicating impacts on this phenolic pathway (Fig. 2B). When we quantified the accumulation of benzenoid metabolites benzyl benzoate and benzyl salicylate, we found that both were consistently low in the BAldSα knockout plants. Benzyl benzoate accumulation was reduced by $78 \%$ to $9 7 \%$ compared to the wild-type, and benzyl salicylate was reduced by $5 6 \%$ to $8 3 \%$ (Fig. 2, C and D). By contrast, we observed a dramatic overaccumulation of BA and BA-glucoside (BA-gluc) in the knockouts (Fig. 2, E and F). BA concentrations were below the detection limit in the wild-type plants, but in BAldSα knockouts BA concentration increased from 3.3 to $1 1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ fresh weight (FW). BA-gluc accumulation was 53 to 143 times greater in the knockouts compared to wild-type. The increases in BA and BA-gluc are likely the result of a buildup of the benzaldehyde synthase substrate benzyl-CoA. This intermediate can also be cleaved by a thioesterase to release BA, which is readily glucosylated (Lim et al. 2002; Adebesin et al. 2018). This overaccumulation of BA and BA-gluc is consistent with a blocked benzaldehyde synthase step in benzenoid metabolism. Benzyl alcohol and benzaldehyde were below our detection limit, however. 

Despite the increase in BA and BA-gluc, SA content was reduced by up to $6 9 \%$ in the knockout plants (Fig. 2G). SA-glucoside (SA-gluc), an inactive storage form of SA (Hennig et al. 1993) was also reduced by $7 7 \%$ to $9 8 \%$ in the knockouts (Fig. 2H). The contrasting effects of the BAldSα knockout on BA and SA and their respective glucosides contradicts the idea that SA is produced directly from BA (León et al. 1995; Widhalm and Dudareva 2015; Ding and Ding 2020). Rather, the parallel reductions of benzyl benzoate, benzyl salicylate, and SA in the BAldSα knockouts suggests they are biosynthetically connected. This pattern supports the hypothesis that these dibenzenoids are precursors of SA. 

# HSR203J encodes a specific BSE and releases SA

Since the hypersensitivity-related (HSR) gene HSR203J encodes a CXE and is required for SA synthesis in tobacco (Takagi et al. 2022), we tested if the benzenoids impacted in the BAldSα knockouts, benzyl benzoate and benzyl salicylate, are HSR203J substrates. We cloned and expressed the N. benthamiana HSR203J gene in Escherichia coli and purified the recombinant protein (Supplementary Fig. S3) for enzyme assays. The recombinant protein efficiently cleaved benzyl salicylate and released SA (Fig. 3). We optimized the assay pH, temperature, and linear range of the reaction. Benzyl salicylate is unstable at high pH and we thus tested pH optima up to pH 8. The enzyme reaction was greatest at pH 7 to 7.5 with an optimal temperature of 20 to $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and remained linear for 20 min (Supplementary Fig. S4). 

Additional assays demonstrated that the esterase activity had a strong preference for benzyl salicylate. Assays with benzyl benzoate produced only a small amount of product, converting only $4 \%$ of substrate under our assay conditions. Other structurally similar esters such as benzyl gentisate were not accepted by the enzyme (Supplementary Figs. S5 and S6). Likewise, the common hydroxycinnamate ester, chlorogenic acid, was not hydrolyzed (Supplementary Fig. S7). When we tested salicyl salicylate as a potential substrate under the standard assay conditions (pH 7), it became apparent that this compound was unstable and degraded within 10 min (Supplementary Fig. S8). At pH 5, the compound was more stable and only degraded partially during the assay. The lower pH buffer allowed us to test the activity of HSR203J with this substrate. Compared to the empty vector and mock assay controls, incubation with recombinant HR203J protein did not result in any increase in the quantity of SA released (Supplementary Fig. S8). This indicated that salicyl salicylate was not a substrate of the HSR203J esterase. We proceeded to obtain kinetic parameters for tobacco HSR203J using benzyl salicylate, and determined a Michaelis constant $\left( K _ { m } \right)$ of $5 0 . 7 6 \pm$ $1 . 9 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , maximum reaction rate $\left( \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { m a x } } \right)$ of $0 . 0 2 3 \pm 0 . 0 0 0 3 \mu \mathrm { M } \times$ $\mathsf { S } ^ { - 1 }$ and a conversion rate $( K _ { \mathrm { c a t } } )$ of $1 . 8 5 { \pm } 0 . 0 2 1 { \mathrm { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ (Table 1). Since benzyl salicylate has a low solubility in aqueous buffers, enzyme kinetics were obtained with substrate concentration between 5 and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ .

# Identification and in vivo testing of a candidate benzoyl benzoate hydroxylase

The involvement of both benzyl benzoate and benzyl salicylate in SA biosynthesis implies the presence of a 2-hydroxylase that can act on benzyl benzoate as part of this pathway. However, preliminary infiltration experiments indicated that, unlike benzyl salicylate, leaf infiltration of benzyl benzoate did not lead to enhanced SA production (Fig. 6D). This suggested that the predicted 2-hydroxylase was not expressed during normal growth but is induced during the defense response. Therefore, we searched for induced N. benthamiana candidate genes in previously published microarray data from elicitor-treated leaves (Kotera et al. 2023). These expression data demonstrate induced expression of BSE (HSR203J), BEBT (HSR201), and the $\beta$ -oxidation enzymes CHD and KAT previously investigated by Kotera et al. (2023). Within this dataset, 1 CYP gene (accession number: DQ350322, UniGene ID: Nta.3549) was present. This gene was annotated as a flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ $5 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3′5′H) or flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3′H) and is thus likely to encode a product with hydroxylase activity. A literature search revealed that this gene had been previously identified in N. tabacum as an uncharacterized tobacco HSR gene, HSR515, induced during defense against the pathogen Pseudomonas solanacearum (Czernic et al. 1996). We therefore refer to this N. tabacum CYP gene and putative hydroxylase as HSR515. The protein showed $7 3 \%$ sequence identity to a poplar CYP gene (Potri.003G066400) that we previously found to be coexpressed with salicinoid biosynthetic genes (Fellenberg et al. 2020). The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes database assigns HSR515 to the CYP92A clade, but to date only 2 genes in this group have been characterized. These genes encode similar sterol hydroxylases (DARK-INDUCED DWARF-LIKE PROTEIN 1, DDWF1) from pea (Pisum sativum) and common bean (Phaseolus vulgaris) and function in brassinosteroid biosynthesis (Kang et al. 2001; Kim et al. 2004). 

Based on these correlations, we hypothesized that HSR515 is the hydroxylase needed for benzyl salicylate biosynthesis and designed experiments to test this in N. benthamiana. We constructed VIGS vectors for this gene as above, but additionally adapted the leaf infiltration protocol. Specifically, 4 weeks after the VIGS was initiated, we induced a defense response in N. benthamiana by infiltrating local leaves with a control Agrobacterium strain (Fig. 6A). Following this treatment, a clear increase of HSR515 expression was observed, with an induction of ${ \sim } 2 0$ -fold compared to controls (Fig. 6B). From this induced level, VIGS treatment was effective in reducing HSR515 expression by $7 5 \%$ . As a control, we assayed expression of the defense marker gene, pathogenesis-related protein 1a (PR1a) (Uknes et al. 1993). This gene was similarly induced by the Agrobacterium-induced pathogen stress, but not affected by the either the benzyl benzoate or VIGS vectors (Fig. 6C). This demonstrated the specificity of the VIGS effect. The Agrobacterium infiltration also caused a strong increase in SA content from benzyl benzoate in the N. benthamiana leaves, which in turn was significantly suppressed by the HSR515 VIGS vector (Fig. 6D). We confirmed the effect of Agrobacterium infiltration on expression of BSE (HSR203J) and BEBT (HSR201). Transcripts of both genes clearly increased after Agrobacterium infiltration, but not in response to benzyl benzoate (Fig. 6, E and F). Together, these results suggested that HSR515 could encode a hydroxylase and contribute to the synthesis of SA from benzyl benzoate. 

To further confirm the importance of HSR515 in SA biosynthesis and to better understand the structure of the CYP92A clade, we conducted a detailed phylogenetic analysis on HSR515-like protein sequences. We used a similar set of species with sequenced genomes as for the CXE analysis but additionally included sequences from maize (Zea mays), N. benthamiana, soybean (G. max), and potato (S. tuberosum) as well as several characterized F3′H and F3′5′H enzymes from other species (Fig. 7; Supplementary Table S4). The phylogenetic tree clearly revealed 3 CYP subgroups, F3′H, F3′5′H, and CYP92A. F3′H and F3′5′H catalyze the hydroxylation of B-ring during flavonoid biosynthesis (Kaltenbach et al. 1999; Schoenbohm et al. 2000). F3′H is ubiquitous in vascular plants, while F3′5′H is somewhat less prevalent and absent from some plant families, for example the Rosaceae. All species except for Arabidopsis contained at least 1 gene in the CYP92A subgroup (Fig. 7). HSR515 and PtCYP92A19 (Potri.003G066400) cluster close together in the CYP92A subgroup. Interestingly, several other poplar hydroxylases were identified in the CYP92A group (PtCYP18, PtCYP21, PtCYP24 and PtCYP25). They were coexpressed with poplar salicinoid biosynthetic genes (Fellenberg et al. 2020), suggesting they could also be involved in benzenoid hydroxylation. 

The closest gene to HSR515 in Arabidopsis is AtF3′H, TT7 (TRANSPARENT TESTA 7, AT5G07990) (Schoenbohm et al. 2000), but as for BSE (HSR203J), no Arabidopsis genes in the CYP92A clade were found. To further verify this, we did BLASTP searches using the N. tabacum HSR515 sequence against all vascular plant genomes and constructed a phylogenetic tree using the protein sequences from each species with highest identity. Again there were no CYP92A orthologs in any the 32 Brassicaceae species available in the Phytozome database and the closest genes in the Brassicaceae species all group with the AtF3′H (TT7) genes (Supplementary Fig. S11). However, as for BSE (HSR203J), plants in other families within the order Brassicales including papaya (Caricaceae) and cacao (Malvaceae) in order Mavales do contain HSR515 orthologs. Overall, our findings indicate that HSR515 is a strong candidate for the benzoyl benzoate 2-hydroxylase needed for SA biosynthesis, completing the last 3 steps needed for SA production. 

# Discussion

Two independent biosynthetic pathways for SA biosynthesis are known in plants, the ICS and the PAL pathways. The origin of SA via the PAL pathway is well established, and requires peroxisomal $\beta$ -oxidation enzymes to convert cinnamoyl-CoA to benzoyl-CoA (Kotera et al. 2023, 2024). How benzoyl-CoA is transformed to SA has remained unresolved, however. Here we define the roles of previously identified HSR genes and characterize the missing biosynthetic steps from benzoyl-CoA to SA via benzyl benzoate and benzyl salicylate (Czernic et al. 1996; Takagi et al. 2022). Our proposed pathway is supported by diverse lines of evidence. First, the CRISPR/Cas9 generated poplar knockouts demonstrated that benzaldehyde synthase is required for SA biosynthesis, thus implicating benzaldehyde and benzyl alcohol as intermediates. Second, we show that the hypersensitivity-related HSR203J gene encodes an esterase that specifically hydrolyzes benzyl salicylate to release SA. We further use N. benthamiana infiltration and VIGS to show that this enzyme is needed in vivo to hydrolyze benzyl salicylate. This confirms a key step in the proposed biosynthetic route. Third, we identify a pathogen-inducible CYP gene (HSR515) as a promising candidate for the benzyl benzoate 2-hydroxylases and use VIGS in N. benthamiana to demonstrate that 

it is required for full pathogen-induced SA accumulation from benzyl benzoate. Fourth, we use phylogenetic analysis to show that HSR203J and HSR515 are conserved in vascular plants, but that both are missing in Arabidopsis and other sequenced Brassicaceae species. This is consistent with observations that Arabidopsis relies on the ICS but not the PAL pathway for inducible SA accumulation. 

In aggregate, our data provide evidence for SA biosynthesis from benzoyl-CoA via benzaldehyde, benzyl benzoate and benzyl salicylate, and that the required enzymes are found in diverse plant species. Several independent studies very recently reported the same steps from benzyl benzoate to SA in N. benthamiana and rice, but did not connect BAldS with the SA pathway as we do here (Liu et al. 2025; Zhu et al. 2025; Wang et al. 2025b). Since BAldS appears not to be induced by Agrobacterium infection in N. benthamiana (Hamel et al. 2024), whether it contributes to pathogen-induced SA biosynthesis in these species will require further study. 

# The conversion of benzoyl-CoA to SA requires benzaldehyde and benzyl benzoate

The peroxisomal heterodimeric enzyme, benzaldehyde synthase, was recently identified in petunia flowers which emit benzaldehyde as floral scents to attract pollinating insects (Huang et al. 2022). The genes encoding the α and $\beta$ subunits of the enzyme were found to be present in a broad diversity of plants suggesting a conserved function, but a relationship with SA biosynthesis was not considered. Our data demonstrate that the loss of the α subunit of the enzyme in CRISPR/Cas9 knockouts in poplar resulted in up to $6 9 \%$ reduction of SA accumulation and almost complete loss of SA-gluc (Fig. 2). This links benzaldehyde synthase to SA biosynthesis. This enzyme reduces benzoyl-CoA to benzaldehyde, which in N. benthamiana was rapidly converted to benzyl alcohol (Huang et al. 2022). Since pathogen-induced SA production in N. benthamiana requires BEBT (HR201), an enzyme which uses benzyl alcohol as cosubstrate (Takagi et al. 2022), the reduction of SA biosynthesis in our plants strongly suggests that benzoyl-CoA is converted to benzyl alcohol from benzaldehyde. Our methods did not allow us to directly measure benzaldehyde or benzyl alcohol in the BAldSα knockout poplars due to their low abundance, likely as intermediates with high turnover rates. However, their immediate downstream product, benzyl benzoate, was strongly reduced in concentration. This suggests an impact of the BAldSα knockout on benzyl alcohol production (Fig. 1). 

The reduction in benzyl benzoate, benzyl salicylate, and SA in the BAldSα knockout plants supports a biosynthetic model of SA biosynthesis via these benzenoid intermediates. Although the knockouts were effective, they retained between $3 3 \%$ and $5 0 \%$ of constitutive SA accumulation. Such residual SA biosynthesis could be due to minor benzaldehyde synthase activity of the $\beta$ -subunit alone, or the result of SA biosynthesis via an alternate pathway. Previous reports in soybean, tobacco and barley have suggested that both PAL and ICS pathways contribute to SA biosynthesis (Ogawa et al. 2006; Catinot et al. 2008; Shine et al. 2016; Hao et al. 2018; Qin et al. 2019). If additional SA pathways contribute to basal or pathogen-induced SA synthesis in poplar is not known. 

# The BSE (HSR203J) and BEBT (HSR201) enzymatic functions help define the PAL pathway to SA

Our discovery that HSR203J encodes a BSE provides important evidence for the proposed biosynthetic pathway. Our data show that 

BSE (HSR203J) is encoded by conserved genes in diverse plant species including a gymnosperm, monocot, and dicot, and that the distantly related gene products have the same benzyl salicylatehydrolyzing activity and similar kinetic parameters (Table 1), pointing to a primary role in plants. Conserved enzyme parameters were also reported for benzaldehyde synthase (Huang et al. 2022), which we now show is required for PAL-derived SA biosynthesis in poplar. The specificity of BSE for benzyl salicylate and lack of activity with benzyl gentisate or salicyl salicylate support the role of BSE (HSR203J) in a primary pathway. BSE belongs to the CXE family, a large group of enzymes which can carry out diverse functions. A number of CXEs have been found to function in the hydrolysis of flower or fruit volatiles (Souleyre et al. 2011; Goulet et al. 2012), but others are involved in the metabolism of plant hormones. For example, AtCXE15 and NtCXE22 and AtCXE20 participate in strigolactone catabolism or binding (Roesler et al. 2021; Xu et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2022). AtCXE10, AtCXE14, AtCXE19, and OsCXE10 function as soluble gibberellin receptors (Ueguchi-Tanaka et al. 2005; Iuchi et al. 2007). The function of BSE (HSR203J) in SA biosynthesis further illustrates the diverse functions of CXE enzymes in plant hormone biology. 

The discovery that HSR203J encodes a BSE resolves the previously unexplained involvement of BEBT (HSR201) in the tobacco defense response (Takagi et al. 2022). The discovery that HSR201 encodes the BEBT enzyme was made many years ago (D’Auria et al. 2002), but how it contributes to the pathogen response was not known. Since benzyl salicylate can be derived from benzyl benzoate, BEBT (HSR201) has now emerged as an important player in SA biosynthesis. We did not investigate this enzyme here as it was previously characterized. Importantly D’Auria et al. (2002) showed that it uses benzoyl-CoA, but not acetyl-CoA, as the acyl donor for benzyl alcohol. We previously characterized the orthologous PtBEBT and benzoyl-CoA: salicyl alcohol O-benzoyltransferase (PtSABT) from P. trichocarpa as part of our investigations on poplar salicinoid biosynthesis (Chedgy et al. 2015). In phylogenies, poplar PtBEBT, PtSABT, and tobacco NtBEBT (HSR201) fall within a small clade of BAHD acyltransferases comprised of enzymes which all utilize benzyl alcohol as a cosubstrate (Chedgy et al. 2015). This clade also includes CbBEBT from Clarkia breweri flowers (D’Auria et al. 2002) and benzyl alcohol acyl transferases required for flower or fruit volatile biosynthesis in petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida) and melon (Boatright et al. 2004; El-Sharkawy et al. 2005). To what extent these acyltransferases contribute to plant defense or SA synthesis has not been investigated. However, both CbBEBT and NtBEBT (HSR201) are wound-inducible genes (D’Auria et al. 2002), suggesting potential defensive functions and a role in SA biosynthesis. Arabidopsis does not contain a gene in this clade, and the most similar gene to NtBEBT (HSR515) encodes the acetyl-CoA: (Z)-3-hexen-1-ol acetyl transferase (CHAT) enzyme. This enzyme produces volatile hexenyl acetate in damaged leaves (D’Auria et al. 2007), but does not accept benzyl alcohol as a cosubstrate (D’Auria et al. 2007) and is thus not a BEBT. This is consistent with the lack of the other enzymes in benzenoid-derived SA synthesis in Arabidopsis. 

Tobacco NtBEBT (HSR201) was recently shown to contain a cryptic C-terminal targeting motif which localizes this protein to the peroxisome (Kotera et al. 2024). This localization further connects the BEBT enzymes to the $\beta$ -oxidation pathway in the peroxisome. The targeting motif comprises an essential C-terminal Leu residue, an adjacent amino acid residue with a small R-group, and 2 hydrophobic residues at positions −5 and −6. Interestingly, this motif of conserved and semiconserved amino acids was also seen in protein sequences within the smaller BEBT clade, including PtBEBT, PtSABT, and PhBPBT (Kotera et al. 2024). Inspection of BEBT-like BAHD genes 

indicates that other clades do not contain this motif. Thus, enzymes in the pathway from cinnamate to benzyl benzoate including CNL, CHD, KAT, benzaldehyde synthase and BEBT all localized to the peroxisome (Huang et al. 2022; Kotera et al. 2023, 2024). By contrast, BSE has no recognizable targeting sequence. It is unlikely to be present in peroxisomes, since its substrate benzyl salicylate is not stable at the high pH $\mathrm { ( p H > 8 ) }$ found in peroxisomes (Shen et al. 2013; Supplementary Fig. S4). However, the localization of BSE has not been experimentally tested yet. However, in silico protein subcellular localization prediction tools predicted that BSE is localized to the cytoplasm (Supplementary Table S5). In this scenario, one would predict benzyl benzoate to be transported from the peroxisome to the cytosol. Here, benzyl benzoate could be hydroxylated by a CYP 2-hydroxylase (see below), enzymes which are typically bound on the cytosolic side of the endoplasmic reticulum membrane. SA is also generally found in the cytosol (Rekhter et al. 2019; Li et al. 2021). 

# The HSR515 gene encodes a putative benzyl benzoate 2-hydroxylase and contributes to SA biosynthesis in N. benthamiana

To identify candidates for the elusive 2-hydroxylase, we looked for CYP genes in expression data from several sources and confirmed its activity using experiments in N. benthamiana. Our tentative conclusion that HSR515 encodes this activity is based on several lines of evidence. First, HSR515 was the only CYP gene coexpressed with HSR201 and HSR203J in elicitor-induced tobacco (Kotera et al. 2023). The same gene was previously found as a tobacco mutant (hsr515) causing hypersensitive response-deficiency (Czernic et al. 1996). Furthermore, data from Hamel et al. (2024) indicate that HSR515 is strongly induced following agroinfiltration and virus-like particle production in N. benthamiana together with known pathogen defense-related genes including chitinases, β-glucanases, and WRKY transcription factors. Likewise, in our previously published gene expression data from Melampsora medusae-infected hybrid poplar, the poplar HSR515 ortholog was in the top $5 \%$ of significantly upregulated genes 6 days after infection (Miranda et al. 2007). Second, VIGS suppression of HSR515 in N. benthamiana caused a significant reduction in pathogen-induced SA after feeding benzyl benzoate (Fig. 6). The VIGS treatment resulted in a $7 5 \%$ suppression of HSR515 transcript levels, and reduced SA by $3 4 \%$ . This represents a modest though significant suppression. For comparison, the reduction of SA concentration using VIGS suppression of BSE (HSR203J) was ${ \sim } 5 0 \%$ (Fig. 4), indicating that VIGS is only partially effective in eliminating SA. We note that VIGS of BSE and other defense genes reported in Takagi et al. (2022) also did not result in complete loss of effector-induced SA. In addition, it is possible that the incomplete silencing effect for both genes is the result of genetic redundancy, since our phylogenies for BSE (HSR203J) and HSR515 indicated the presence of closely related genes in the tobacco genome (Figs. 5 and 7). 

Third, HSR515 orthologs are conserved in major genomes we queried and found in both monocots and eudicots (Supplementary Fig. S11). Again, the corresponding genes could not be identified in Arabidopsis or other Brassicaceae, a pattern that we also observed for BSE (HSR203J). This suggests that HSR515 belongs to the suite of genes needed for SA biosynthesis in other plants but lost from the Brassicaceae. Outside of the Brassicacea, genes in the CYP92A clade are widely distributed and found in many plant species. However, the only characterized genes in this clade are DDWF1 genes from pea and common bean, which encode typhasterol/6-deoxotyphasterol 2 alphahydroxylase in brassinosteroid biosynthesis (Kang et al. 201; Kim 

et al. 2004). The CYP92A genes are also similar to genes encoding both flavonoid 3-hydroxylase and flavonoid $^ { 3 ^ { \prime } , 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -hydroxylase enzymes (Schoenbohm et al. 2000). 

While we demonstrate that HSR515 can contribute to SA biosynthesis, it will be important to determine if there are additional CYP hydroxylases with similar activities. Closer inspection of the CYP phylogeny identified other CYP92A genes in the P. trichocarpa genomes, some of which are also induced by pathogen stress (Miranda et al. 2007) and coexpressed with salicinoid enzymes in poplar (Fellenberg et al. 2020), which also requires 2-hydroxylation of dibenzenoids. We note that the BEBT clade in Populus also contains a number of closely related genes (Tuominen et al. 2011). Future work will test if there are other pathogen-induced enzymes which are important for SA biosynthesis. 

In conclusion, our data describe enzymes that convert benzoyl-CoA to SA via benzenoid intermediates and demonstrate that the corresponding genes are present in most major groups of vascular plants except the Brassicaceae. These enzymatic steps complete the PAL-based pathway for SA biosynthesis, and confirm that Arabidopsis and the Brassicaceae use a different route based on the ICS pathway (Rekhter et al. 2019; Torrens-Spence et al. 2019). The evolution of the ICS pathway within the Brassicaceae has been described, with recent reports pointing to a recent origin of the ICS route to SA evolving within the Brassicales only (Torrens-Spence et al. 2024; Hong et al. 2025). A broad analysis of SA-related signaling and biosynthesis genes suggests that the PAL pathway is the more ancient one, likely arising in the most recent common ancestor of green plants and late-emerging streptophyte algae (Jia et al. 2023). Our discovery of biosynthetic enzymes for SA biosynthesis from phenylalanine should provide additional tools to study the role of SA in plant evolution and adaptation.
