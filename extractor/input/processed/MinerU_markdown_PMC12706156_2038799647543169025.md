# Abstract

In plants, the initiation of fatty acid synthesis is catalyzed by acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACCase), which produces malonyl-CoA. The heteromeric form of ACCase (htACCase) is a holoenzyme consisting of biotin carboxylase and carboxyltransferase sub-complexes, both of which are subject to extensive regulation. Biotin carboxylase activity is controlled in part by the presence of the catalytic biotin carboxyl carrier proteins (BCCP1/2) and/or the non-catalytic, non-biotinylated, biotin/lipoyl attachment domain-containing proteins (BADC1/2/3) that associate with backbone biotin carboxylase (BC) protein. However, the mechanisms regulating BADC and BCCP interactions with BC and, consequently, ACCase activity in planta, remain unclear. Here, we demonstrate that the Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) regulatory protein PII modulates htACCase activity through independent interactions with BADC and BCCP proteins in a selective manner. Analysis of badc1 badc2 and badc1 badc3 mutant lines and the respective pii triple mutants revealed that changes in seed oil and protein accumulation of badc double mutants are PII/nitrogen dependent. Absolute quantification of htACCase subunits and PII in developing seeds suggests that Arabidopsis exerts tight regulation over individual protein stoichiometry to balance oil and protein accumulation. The effects on vegetative and seed development indicate that PII and BADC proteins have distinct yet overlapping roles in regulating plant metabolism. 

# Introduction

Seeds are among the highest value products produced by commercial agriculture, and breeding efforts have focused on improving both seed yield and quality. Seed crops from the Brassicaceae family are particularly valued for their oil and protein accumulation, which can be used for food, biofuel, and animal feed (Raboanatahiry et al. 2021; So and Duncan 2021). Genetic efforts to improve seed oil content have largely indicated that the ratio of seed oil content is generally inversely related to storage protein accumulation (Vigeolas et al. 2007; Schwender and Hay 2012; Jasinski et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2022). This trade-off likely stems from competition for carbon precursors, which are shared between amino acid (protein) and fatty acid (oil) synthesis (Schwender et al. 2003; Schwender and Hay 2012; Rolletschek et al. 2020; Song et al. 2023). As a result, seeds have evolved complex regulatory networks to balance oil and protein biosynthesis, integrating inputs from nitrogen (N) status, biotic and abiotic stress, cell determination, and a host of other factors (Su et al. 2021; Yang et al. 2022). Yet many aspects of the regulatory crosstalk between oil and protein metabolism in seeds are not yet fully understood. 

There is ample evidence that seed oil production can be enhanced by increasing fatty acid synthesis, fatty acid transport, lipid assembly, packaging of oil, and protection from catalytic 

breakdown (Baud 2018; Sagun et al. 2023). In vivo modulation of total fatty acid synthesis has been largely attributed to the regulation of acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACCase) which catalyzes the production of malonyl-CoA from acetyl-CoA and bicarbonate, and is the first committed step of fatty acid biosynthesis (Li-Beisson et al. 2013). Plants contain homomeric and heteromeric forms of ACCase, but it is the heteromeric form (htACCase) that confers modular protein complex assembly, and for which multifaceted regulation is known. In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), the htACCase enzyme consists of biotin carboxylase and carboxyltransferase complexes, with the biotin carboxylase complex formed by biotin carboxylase (BC) and catalytic biotin carboxyl carrier proteins (BCCP1/2) or a combination of BCCP and noncatalytic, non-biotinylated, biotin/lipoyl attachment domaincontaining proteins (BADC1/2/3) (Salie and Thelen 2016). The biotin carboxylase complex catalyzes ATP-dependent carboxylation of the BCCP-bound biotin which is then transferred to acetyl-CoA producing malonyl-CoA catalyzed by the carboxyltransferase subcomplex. The carboxyl transferase subcomplex consists of a heterotetramer of α and $\beta$ -CarboxylTransferase (α/β-CT) subunits. Plant htACCase has been hypothesized to have a similar subunit stoichiometry to that of E. coli, which consists of 2 biotin carboxylase complexes for each 

carboxyltransferase complex $[ \mathrm { ( B C C P / B A D C ) _ { 4 } ( B C ) _ { 2 } ( a \mathrm { - } C T ) _ { 2 } ( \beta \mathrm { - } C T ) _ { 2 } } ] ;$ ; however, this has yet to be experimentally verified in plants (Salie and Thelen 2016; Cronan 2021). 

Heteromeric ACCase is regulated by a large number of factors including redox and pH status of the stroma, protein phosphorylation, feedback inhibition, substrate availability, and most recently 3 regulatory effector proteins (Huerlimann and Heimann 2013; Salie and Thelen 2016; Ye et al. 2020a, 2020b; Zhou et al. 2025). The modulation of htACCase holoenzyme assembly has emerged as a key mechanism for fine-tuning de novo fatty acid synthesis. The carboxyltransferase complex is influenced by the sub-stoichiometric levels of $\mathfrak { a }$ -CT (Wang et al. 2022) as well as its anchoring to the plastid inner envelope membrane by carboxyltransferase interactor (CTI) and regulation of fatty acid synthesis proteins (Ye et al. 2020a; Zhou et al. 2025). In addition, there is mounting evidence that ACCase activity is in part controlled through the relative availability of BADC and BCCP subunits for biotin carboxylase subcomplex. BADCs are homologous to BCCP proteins, both contain folded structured domains and large intrinsically disordered regions, however, BADCs do not contain the conserved biotinylation motif required for covalent attachment of biotin necessary for catalytic activity (Ye et al. 2020b). BADC proteins were initially described and subsequently characterized as negative regulators (Salie et al. 2016; Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Ye et al. 2020b), however, recent evidence supports a more dynamic role for BADC proteins. In vitro measurements of BADC and BCCP interaction with BC demonstrated that pH, cationic conditions, and phosphate availability each affected binding affinity (Ye et al. 2020b). Reconstituted protein assays of htACCase and BCCP secondary structure shifts in response to interaction with BADC proteins suggest that BADCs are involved in the formation of the active holoenzyme through facilitation or stabilization of the BC/BCCP interaction (Shivaiah et al. 2020, 2025). Additionally, BADCs (and CTI1) are transcriptionally regulated by the WRI1 transcription factor, a master regulator of oil accumulation, and BADC expression in plant seeds is upregulated during developmental stages of rapid fatty acid biosynthesis and oil accumulation (Liu et al. 2019; Ye et al. 2020a, 2020b). There is also evidence of circadian regulation of BADC2 and BCCP2, however, the direct regulatory pathway has yet to be understood (Kim et al. 2023). 

Physiological studies have shown that double mutants badc1 badc2 (badc1badc2) and badc1 badc3 (badc1badc3) have significant effects on plant development. Arabidopsis badc1badc2 knockout lines have reduced root and rosette development, potentially through changes in auxin catabolism (Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Liu et al. 2019). Seeds of both badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 mutations exhibit increased seed oil content while the badc2 badc3 combination and triple mutant is embryo lethal (Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Shivaiah et al. 2020 ; Ye et al. 2020b). The badc1badc3 double mutant seed oil phenotype is also dependent on circadian regulation, likely as a result of light-induced changes to the stroma pH and ionic milieu leading to confirmation changes of BADC and BCCP proteins which alters their affinity to BC (Ye et al. 2020b) and demonstrates their ability for dynamic regulation based on the cellular metabolic status. Additional multi-omics analysis of developing badc1badc3 seeds have shown global development changes including both increased fatty acid synthesis and fatty acid turnover, as well as enhanced seed storage protein content (Kataya et al. 2025). Despite substantial prior research, the complex nature of BADC regulation including their interaction with biotin carboxylase, effects on fatty acid biosynthesis, and the coordination of fatty acid biosynthesis with protein synthesis are not yet fully understood. 

There is evidence that ACCase activity is co-regulated by the PII protein. PII is a highly conserved signaling protein that is 

ubiquitous across Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya (Forchhammer et al. 2022). It plays a central role in regulating nitrogen uptake, coordinating nitrogen and carbon metabolism, and maintaining cellular homeostasis in response to nitrogen and energy status (Gerhardt et al. 2020; Selim et al. 2020; Forchhammer et al. 2022). It is generally accepted that PII forms an active homotrimer that can interact with and regulate C/N metabolism specifically under high-energy conditions, specifically, high ATP/ADP ratios and low 2-oxoglutarate (2-OG) levels, the latter being consumed during N assimilation (Gerhardt et al. 2020; Selim et al. 2020). In bacteria, PII has been demonstrated to sense and respond to both cellular energy status and N status through competitive binding of ATP $( \pm \mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + } )$ ) or ADP, and 2-OG at metabolite binding sites which form at the lateral clefts between PII subunits (Zhang et al. 2007 ; Fokina et al. 2010; Truan et al. 2010). Substrate binding to these sites introduces structural changes in PII’s interaction domain (called the T-loop) which regulates PII binding to other proteins (Smith et al. 2003; Fokina et al. 2010, 2011; Truan et al. 2010; Gerhardt et al. 2020; Selim et al. 2020). Bacterial PII is also regulated through phosphorylation (Smith et al. 2004). In plants, active PII directly regulates the activity of N-acetyl glutamate kinase (NAGK), which catalyzes the second committed step in ornithine and arginine biosynthesis (Feria Bourrellier et al. 2009). Arabidopsis PII activity has been described as regulating nitrite import into the chloroplast (Ferrario-Méry et al. 2005, 2008). However, the direct protein targets of PII interaction have yet to be identified (Hsieh et al. 1998; Ferrario-Méry et al. 2005, 2008). Interestingly, PII’s role in N metabolism is glutamine-dependent in all higher plants except for Brassicaceae (Smith et al. 2003; Ferrario-Méry et al. 2006; Feria Bourrellier et al. 2009). PII regulation of fatty acid synthesis is also highly conserved from bacteria to higher plants. In Arabidopsis, prior research suggests that active PII (high energy/N status) binds to BCCP proteins as a negative regulator of fatty acid synthesis (Feria Bourrellier et al. 2010). However, in pii mutants, the loss of PII only conditionally affects seed oil and protein composition, suggesting a role in finetuning plastid fatty acid synthesis to correlate with fatty acid elongation in the cytosol (Baud et al. 2010). There is also limited evidence that PII may directly interact with BADC proteins (Baud et al. 2009). 

To ascertain whether the combined loss of putative negative regulators of biotin carboxylase (BADCs and PII) could be a strategy to improve seed oil content, piibadc1badc2 and piibadc1badc3 triple mutants were produced and analyzed. Contrary to our initial hypothesis, the results demonstrate that enhanced ACCase activity and seed oil content of badc double mutants is dependent on the presence of functional PII. The results further suggest that PII modulates ACCase activity in response to energy and N status through selective interactions with both BADC and BCCP subunits. Together, the results presented herein indicate more complex roles for PII and BADC than simple negative regulators and suggest they work together to regulate ACCase activity in response to cellular energy and N status.

# Leaf lipid composition is altered in badc double mutants and dependent on pii function

To further determine what effect each mutant combination may have on leaf lipid metabolism, lipids were extracted, separated by HPLC, derivatized to fatty acid methyl esters (FAMEs), and were quantified by GC-FID. Initial analysis of total fatty acid content of extracted leaf lipids indicated that both badc double mutants had increased levels of saturates (16:0, 18:0) and 7,10,13-hexadecatrienoic acid (16:3) at the cost of $\mathfrak { a }$ -linolenic acid (18:3) as compared to WT (Fig. 3a). The stacking of pii into the badc1badc2 background resulted in the largest decrease in 18:3 with corresponding increases in oleic acid (18:1) and 16:0 (Fig. 3a). The decrease in fatty acid content in badc1badc2 leaves (Fig. 2a) was primarily due to reduced monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) and phosphatidylcholine (PC) content (Fig. 3b). Interestingly, the piibadc1badc2 triple mutant largely returned membrane lipid contents back to WT (Fig. 3; Figure S3). Analysis of MGDG fatty acid composition demonstrated an increase in 16:3 and a corresponding decrease in 18:3 in the badc double mutant and pii triple mutant lines (Fig. 3c). However, only badc1badc2 mutants saw similar shifts in digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG) levels of 16:3 and 18:3 (Fig. 3d). Notably, although badc1badc2 exhibited a more pronounced leaf lipid phenotype, both badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 contained changes in thylakoid lipid ratios of 16:3/18:3, suggesting that both mutant backgrounds seem to have partially overlapping functions in vegetative tissue. Considering that 16:3 biosynthesis is through the chloroplast-localized prokaryotic pathway, the change in 16:3/ 18:3 ratio is consistent with relatively more galactolipids from the prokaryotic pathway than the eukaryotic pathway. This may suggest increased fatty acid biosynthesis in the plastid leading to higher acyl flux through the prokaryotic pathway and/or reduction of eukaryotic pathway galactolipid synthesis possibly due to turnover of excess fatty acids exported from the plastid. Therefore, the amount of leaf lipids and protein in badc1badc2 was dependent on the presence of PII, but the change in galactolipid fatty acid content was independent of PII presence and similar within both badc double mutant backgrounds.

# Seed oil and protein composition are altered in badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 lines in a PII and N fertilization dependent manner

To determine the concurrent effect loss of pii, badc1badc2, and badc1badc3, and their respective triple mutants have on seed metabolism, both seed oil and protein were analyzed. Further, the role of PII in the regulation of badc double mutant lines in response to N was investigated by fertilizing plants using a N free Hoagland solution with either added N (150 mg/plant) or no added nitrogenous compounds. Seeds of the well-fertilized (high N) badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 plants showed $1 2 \%$ and $6 \%$ increases in total fatty acid content, respectively, while mature pii seeds were not different than wild type (Fig. 5a), consistent with prior results 

(Baud et al. 2010; Keereetaweep et al. 2018). Interestingly, the loss of pii in either of the badc double mutant backgrounds reverted seed oil content back to WT levels (Fig. 5a), which supports that the badc seed oil phenotype is PII dependent. 

Many plant species demonstrate an inverse relationship between oil and protein accumulation in seeds; therefore, we also analyzed protein content of seeds of each line. On the contrary, seed protein was actually increased in both badc1badc2 and badc1- badc3 mutants by up to $1 8 \%$ (Fig. 5b), consistent with recent multi-omic analysis of badc1badc3 mutant seeds (Kataya et al. 2025). Analogous to the seed oil results, the pii/badc triple mutant seed protein levels were also reduced when compared to their respective double mutants, with levels between those of the double mutant and WT (Fig. 5b). However, the seed oil and protein phenotypes were absent or significantly reduced in badc double mutants grown under low N conditions (Fig. 5c and d). These results support that the badc double mutant seed oil and protein phenotypes are dependent on active PII (due to low 2-OG concentrations during high N) as N starved plants would be expected to have high levels of 2-OG and minimal PII function (Selim et al. 2020). When the seed fatty acid profiles of each mutant were examined, badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 demonstrated significantly higher levels of 18:3 and $\geq 2 0 \mathrm { C }$ fatty acids, at the expense of 18:1 and 18:2 in well fertilized plants (Fig. 5e). The corresponding pii/badc1/badc3 triple mutant (Fig. 5e) partially complemented badc1badc3 parent line indicating the effects of the badc1badc3 double mutant on fatty acid composition may be PII dependent. The composition of badc1- badc2 and its corresponding triple mutant showed similar trends, however, changes were quantitatively modest. In N starved lines the fatty acid composition was unchanged when compared to well-fertilized plants (Figure S4) which suggests that changes in seed fatty acid composition (rather than total oil amount) are N independent. # Discussion

Fatty acid synthesis is a complex process that is mediated by a host of factors including substrate–product relationships, cellular conditions, and physical regulation through the activity of regulatory proteins. These proteins include the BADCs and PII which are believed to have no direct catalytic function but have been posited to alter the activity of the htACCase biotin carboxylase complex through their binding (Salie et al. 2016; Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Shivaiah et al. 2020, 2025). Seed oil accumulation and fatty acid composition are altered in Arabidopsis badc and pii mutants, indicating a significant role for these proteins in regulating fatty acid synthesis of plants (Baud et al. 2010; Keereetaweep et al. 2018). While both PII and the BADCs were initially described as negative regulators, evidence in the literature and our own results suggest that regulation of the biotin carboxylase half-reaction of htACCase is more complex (Salie et al. 2016; Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Shivaiah et al. 2020, 2025). Here, we present evidence that 

both badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 mutants affect lipid and nitrogen metabolism and that the effects on N and C metabolism are dependent on PII such that most of the badc double mutant phenotypes trend back toward wild type in the pii/badc triple mutants (Figs. 1–6). Interestingly, the phenotypic differences among the various double and triple mutant combinations are not consistent across all measures, indicating that the observed phenotypes also depend on which BADC isoform (BADC2 or BADC3) remains in each genetic background. Additionally, through absolute quantitative proteomics we demonstrate that PII is more abundant than all the BADC and BCCP isoforms combined, and each badc double mutant had a differential compensating effect on htACCase subunit levels in developing seeds, which again trended back toward wild type with the additional loss of PII (Fig. 6). Together, these results support the conclusion that the regulatory roles of each BADC isoform and PII on carbon and nitrogen metabolism are overlapping and distinct, which is further supported by the differential binding of BADC and BCCP subunits of htACCase to PII (Fig. 7). Figure 8 displays a proposed model of the PII’s contribution to alter htACCase subunit stoichiometry and thus activity. 

# PII binds and independently regulates BADC and BCCP subunits of the ACCase complex to regulate fatty acid synthesis

The hypothesized inhibitor roles of the non-catalytic subunits of htACCase (BADCs and PII) were first described through in vitro assays where increasing amounts of BADCs or PII binding to ACCase reduced the in vitro activity (Feria Bourrellier et al. 2010; Salie et al. 2016). These roles appeared consistent with the mutant phenotypes of increased seed oil of the badc double mutants (Keereetaweep et al. 2018) and altered fatty acid composition of pii, suggesting unregulated fatty acid synthesis, which had 

downstream effects on fatty acid desaturation and elongation (Baud et al. 2010; Shivaiah et al. 2020). Interestingly, the enhanced oil content of badc1badc3 over that of wild type was not present when grown under $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ light, suggesting that the enhancement of seed oil accumulation due to the badc double mutants is dependent on additional factors such as regulation of stromal energy status, pH, and cation concentration or changes in circadian rhythm (Ye et al. 2020b; Kim et al. 2023). Likewise, here we demonstrate that the oil phenotype of both badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 is also dependent on N availability (Fig. 5a and c) further emphasizing the condition specific role of these regulators. The regulatory role of BADC proteins became more nuanced with results that indicated BADCs may be required for organization of the quaternary structure of htACCase. In vitro assembled HtACCase holoenzymes that only contained BCCP but not BADC subunits had very low activity (Shivaiah et al. 2020). The addition of BADC’s enhanced in vitro ACCase activity dependent on the BADC isoform with BADC2 and BADC3 showing the highest activity (Shivaiah et al. 2020). Follow-up NMR chemical shift perturbation analysis of BC-BCCP interactions indicated the addition of at least BADC3 led to structural changes of BCCP1 that facilitated interaction with BC, supporting the role of BADC3 in htACCase complex assembly (Shivaiah et al. 2025). Interestingly, the addition of BADC1 to in vitro ACCase assays improved activity only modestly but this may be due to BADC1 sensitivity to pH, see Ye et al. (2020a, 2020b), or alternatively that physiologically BADC1 may function as more of a negative regulator than other BADCs. Thus, if BADCs are required for htACCase holoenzyme formation, then they are not simple activator/inhibitors but together the 3 individual BADCs may act as a biological rheostat allowing for varying levels of activity in response to environmental stimuli and cellular status (Fig. 8). 

Our results support and build upon this nuanced hypothesis by demonstrating that PII is not limited to interaction with BCCP proteins. Protein–protein interaction studies in yeast demonstrate that apart from NAGK, PII can bind to the essential BCCP, BC and the regulatory BADC proteins independent of the larger ACCase complex (Fig. 7). These findings are consistent with initial affinity chromatography which identified both BCCP and the then as of yet named BADC subunits (Feria Bourrellier et al. 2010; Ye et al. 2020b). In yeast, beta-galactosidase assays demonstrated strong binding of PII to BADC1 and BCCP2 (Fig. 7) but had no affinity for BADC2 or BCCP1, which suggests that PII may not function as a simple negative regulator of active htACCase but may also have a selective role depending upon ACCase subunit composition. In developing WT seeds, PII is more abundant than all BADC and BCCP subunits combined (Fig. 6) indicating the capacity to effectively sequester/exchange free BCCP and BADC isoforms based on its binding affinity and cellular energy and N status, PII binding is activated during a high energy and high N state (Selim et al. 2020). From these results we hypothesize that PII functions to differentially regulate which BCCP and BADC isoforms are within the htACCase holoenzyme in response to cellular energy and N status (Figs. 5 and 7; Smith et al. 2003; Feria Bourrellier et al. 2009; Feria Bourrellier et al. 2010; Ye et al. 2020b), through selective interactions/sequestration of free BADC, BCCP, and BADC proteins (Fig. 8). 

Our results support the above hypothesis in that both badc double mutant backgrounds the high oil phenotype reported here and previously (Keereetaweep et al. 2018; Ye et al. 2020b) is dependent on the presence of active PII, likely optimizing BADC-BCCP isoform ratios to fine tune htACCase activity under changing cellular energy and nitrogen status (Figs. 5 and 8). The dependence on PII is 

supported by the piibadc1badc2 and piibadc1badc3 mutants which revert high oil phenotype of badc double mutant seeds to that of wild type (Figs. 5a and 8), similar to low N conditions (Fig. 5c) and constant light (Ye et al. 2020b). In a low N state, 2-OG levels are high which inhibits the binding of PII to ACCase components (Feria Bourrellier et al. 2010), analogous to the pii mutant (Fig. 8). While plant growth under constant light limits the dynamic pH and ionic conditions associated with fluctuating energy status which are known to regulate both BADC and PII binding ability (Smith et al. 2003; Feria Bourrellier et al. 2009, 2010; Ye et al. 2020b). Therefore, without PII modulation of the BCCP-BADC components of htACCase under changing conditions, the overall htACCase activity is reduced (Fig. 2c) eliminating many of the seed oil enhancements (Fig. 5). It should also be noted that badc1- badc2 and badc1badc3 differ in their compensatory regulation of htACCase subunit stoichiometry (Fig. 6). Which along with the selective protein–protein interactions of PII with BADC-BCCP (Fig. 7), differential plant growth, protein, and lipid phenotypes (Figs. 1–5) supports that protein-protein interaction with PII may be one of major differences between BADC isoforms. The selective interactions between PII and BADCs may lead to the differential phenotypes of the badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 mutants which have only BADC3 or BADC2 remaining, respectively. 

# Hypothesis 1: differential phenotypes of the badc double mutants are resultant of the remaining BADC isoform and PII’s affinity to the remaining BADC/BCCP subunits

The badc double mutants have similar increases in seed oil and protein content, and comparable small increases in seedling htACCase activity, but had differential effects on vegetative biomass lipid and protein content (Figs. 1–3). The differences in vegetative tissues as compared to seed tissue is likely due to seed function as a storage tissue for excess fatty acid and protein synthesis, whereas vegetative tissue must adapt and maintain homeostasis to unregulated ACCase in mutants and altered N metabolism under differential growth conditions. We hypothesize the differential leaf phenotypes of the badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 mutants are explained by the remaining BADC isoform in each double mutant, the selective binding of PII to the remaining BADC and BCCP isoforms, and the differential expression of htACCase subunits (Fig. 6) which together regulate ACCase activity (Fig. 8). 

In the badc1badc3 mutant, only BADC2 remains and the biotin carboxylase complex is limited to BC, 2 BCCP subunits, and BADC2. This combination has been hypothesized to be one of the most active BC-BCCP-BADC combinations (Shivaiah et al. 2020; Table S1), and the role of PII in modulating BCCP1/ 2-BADC2 ratios is confined to interaction with BCCP2 within the ACCase complex (Fig. 7). Consequently, the vegetative tissue of badc1badc3 plants had modest effects on plant development as well as leaf lipid and protein composition (Figs. 1–3), demonstrating that BADC2 is able to functionally complement the badc1badc3 mutation and/or that BADC2 plays a major role in regulating the ACCase complex in vegetative tissues. In at least Arabidopsis, BADC2 expression is co-regulated with genes involved in regulation of circadian rhythm which would support its role in maintaining cellular homeostasis (Kim et al. 2023). The further loss of pii in the badc1badc3 mutants reduced ACCase activity to levels lower than either parent line alone (Fig. 2c), underpinning PII’s role in BCCP/BADC regulation and ACCase activity even with limited interacting partners. The importance of these interactions was 

confirmed in seeds where the increased oil content of badc1badc3 mutants is dependent on PII (Fig. 5a). Consequently, recent transcriptomics and proteomics analysis of the badc1badc3 developing seeds also suggested changes in both beta-oxidation and fatty acid synthesis possibly in response to overproduction of fatty acids (Kataya et al. 2025), which would be consistent with a positive role of BADC2 at physiological concentrations. Protein quantification also measured a compensating effect in developing seeds of the badc1badc3 mutant through the reduction of BCCP2 (Fig. 6c), possibly to limit fatty acid over production, which was then alleviated by further loss of PII. While in vivo levels of ACCase were not measured in leaf or root tissue, it is likely that differential adaptation to the loss of badc expression also led to the differences during vegetative tissue growth. 

In the badc1badc2 mutant, only BADC3 remains. BADC3 demonstrated moderate binding to PII (Fig. 7) and would likely produce biotin carboxylase complexes (BC:BCCP1/2:BCCP1/2; BC:BCCP1/2: BADC3), which theoretically have intermediate ACCase activity and may not fulfill cell requirements for fatty acid synthesis (Shivaiah et al. 2020; Table S1). Interestingly, in developing seeds, α-CT levels (the limiting subunit of htACCase; Wang et al. 2022) increased in the badc1badc2 mutant, along with total BADC3 and PII (Fig. 6), which may be a compensatory mechanism for the reduced activity of the BADC3-containing htACCase complex. The corresponding badc1badc2 tissue demonstrated reduced plant development and leaf lipid content. However, it is still unclear if the presence of BADC3, the loss of BADC2, PII interactions with remaining ACCase subunits, or a combination of all these interactions within the ACCase complex are the defining factors. Changes in leaf protein as a result of PII interaction with NAGK or as-of-yet characterized N processes have also led to altered lipid content in badc1badc2 lines (Fig. 2a). However, the 16:3/18:3 ratio in leaf MGDG and DGDG of badc1badc2 plants was largely not PII dependent (Fig. 3a, c and d), which supports that the altered fatty acid production and enrichment of the chloroplast localized prokaryotic pathway of lipid biosynthesis corresponds to the loss of BADC1/2, while changes in total fatty acid synthesis and accumulation may result from altered PII regulation of N and C metabolism (Figs. 2 and 3). Curiously, badc1badc2 seeds demonstrated increased oil (Fig. 5a) along with increased abundance of ACCase subunits (Fig. 6c), suggesting that seeds may have a compensatory mechanism for moderate activity of the htACCase complex containing only BADC3. Subsequently, the return of ACCase subunit levels in piibadc1badc2 mutants supports that PII may have had negative effects on htACCase complex assembly in badc1badc2 double mutants. Together, these results suggest that the individual phenotypes of the badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 double mutants are related to the remaining BADC isoform, the ability of PII to modulate ACCase activity through interactions with the remaining BADC and BCCP subunits (Fig. 8), and the plant adaptation to those specific changes in ACCase enzyme activity within different tissues. More importantly, the selective interactions between PII and individual BADC and BCCP proteins may be the basis for posttranslational regulation of the biotin carboxylase subunit in response to changes both N and cellular energy status. 

# Hypothesis 2: BADC proteins co-regulate N metabolism through feedback regulation to PII

In Arabidopsis, there is clear evidence that PII has an important role in cellular N transport and metabolism within the chloroplast. PII is a major binding partner of NAGK and required for enzyme function, which is thought to be a key regulatory step in the 

production of the amino acid arginine (Ferrario-Méry et al. 2006). There is also evidence of PII regulation of intercellular N transport (Ferrario-Méry et al. 2005, 2008); however, the mechanism of action has yet to be confirmed (Selim et al. 2020). The loss of pii alone seems to have modest and largely non-significant effects on both N and seed protein accumulation in response to N availability (Fig. 5; Baud et al. 2010). By contrast, mutants of badc1badc2 and badc1badc3 show significant increases in leaf and/or seed protein when compared to WT (Figs. 2b and 5b and d). The corresponding pii/badc triple mutants demonstrated a relative decrease in seed protein that was not significantly different than WT, pii, or their respective double mutant badc background. Our results indicate that BADC, and possibly BCCP, may influence nitrogen metabolism through their interactions with PII, potentially by modulating its availability or activity. These interactions may involve sequestration or conformational changes that affect PII’s ability to engage with its typical partners in carbon and nitrogen metabolism. Contextually, the loss of BADC1, one of the most abundant BADC isoforms (Fig. 6) with strong interactions with PII (Fig. 7), may lead to a relative increase in free active PII. At a minimum, excess PII would positively affect NAGK and promote arginine biosynthesis while negatively affecting the hypothesized regulation of nitrite transport processes (Ferrario-Méry et al. 2008; Feria Bourrellier et al. 2009). Our results support that in the seeds of badc double mutants, possible changes in N assimilation and amino acid synthesis led to an increase in storage protein accumulation (Fig. 5b), which is consistent with recent results reporting increased seed protein in just badc1badc3 (Kataya et al. 2025). Interestingly, analysis of vegetative tissue showed a variance between badc1badc2 and badc1badc3, where badc1badc2 demonstrated both changes in leaf lipid and protein composition and increased nitrite toxicity, suggesting that at least chloroplast N and C transport and metabolism were altered (Figs. 2b, 3, and 4). These phenotypes were reverted to WT in the corresponding piibadc1badc2 triple mutant, supporting that PII interaction with ACCase components, or lack thereof in the case of badc1badc2, is a regulatory check and balance between the accumulation of oil and protein. Overall, changes in leaf and seed oil and protein, and nitrite assimilation suggest that the regulation of N/C metabolism may be in part dependent on the interactions between PII and BADC proteins, which together are able to inform the larger regulatory network. 

# Conclusions

# BCCP, BADC, and PII proteins function through competitive protein:protein interactions affecting both the stoichiometry of ACCase components/ activity and N metabolism

Mounting evidence increasingly suggests that the biotin carboxylase sub-complex of htACCase is regulated through individual subunit expression, the regulated binding of the BADC and BCCP subunits in response to stromal conditions and the interaction of the BADC and BCCP proteins to the N/C effector protein PII (Baud et al. 2010; Salie et al. 2016; Ye et al. 2020a). Our results support the conclusion that the binding of PII to each BADC and BCCP affects the stoichiometry of ACCase components and levels of free PII, which can have downstream effects on ACCase activity and N metabolism. Consequently, the binding affinity between PII and individual BADCs may be the distinguishing characteristic between individual BADC proteins, allowing for various ACCase complexes with different activities, and thus allowing for the 

rheostat of ACCase activity. Finally, our results support that the non-catalytic subunits of htACCase (BADCs) are more than just negative regulators of ACCase activity, also impacting N metabolism within the cell via direct interaction with the PII effector protein. Future studies are needed to determine the stoichiometry of all catalytic and non-catalytic ACCase effectors within active htACCase complexes under different cellular energy and N status conditions. This knowledge is required for a more detailed understanding of how non-catalytic proteins contribute to the regulation of this essential metabolic enzyme and our ability to control it for optimization of seed oil and protein content. 

# Materials and methods

# Identification of Arabidopsis triple mutants

Various genetic resources were used to create Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) lines containing different stacked mutations in the genes of interest. Wild type (c.v. Columbia (Col-0)) and T-DNA insertion mutants of GLB1 (pii; SALK_021878C; PIIS2, Ferrario-Méry et al. 2005), double mutants of badc1 (SALK_00817) and badc2 (SALK_021108c) (badc1badc2), badc1 and badc3 (CS2103834) (badc1badc3; for crossing and identification of double mutants see, Keereetaweep et al. 2018) were used as parent lines to produce the corresponding pii/badc triple mutants. Successful crosses of the T-DNA triple mutant of pii/badc1/badc2 and pii/ badc1/badc3 were screened by PCR (see, Table S2). Alternative pii/ badc1/badc3 triple mutants were also created by CRISPR genome editing. Multiple self-splicing ribozyme-flanked single guide RNAs (Gao and Zhao 2014) targeting BADC1 and BADC3 were combined and cloned into the NotI and SacII sites of plasmid vector K63 (Shockey 2020) to form plasmid J168 (Figure S6). The AtUBQ10promoter:gRNA:CaMV35Sterminator cassette from J168 was cloned into the AscI and AvrII sites of the YAOpromoter: Cas9 DsRed-selectable binary plasmid pE717 (Li et al. 2013; Yan et al. 2015; Shockey 2020) to produce plasmid E812, which was introduced into A. tumefaciens and used to transform pii T-DNA single mutant lines by floral dip (Clough and Bent 1998). Progression of gene editing in candidate triple mutant plants was monitored by PCR amplification of the targeted DNA regions (for primers see Table S2) followed by restriction digestion of the resulting amplicons for detection of insertions or deletions that arose from error prone nonhomologous end-joining (NHEJ) DNA repair occurring after Cas9 target site cleavage. In this case, NHEJ may be expected to destroy either DraIII and/or PvuII sites in BADC1 and AvrII and/or PstI sites in BADC3 near the protospacer adjacent motifs of the guide RNA target sites. Promising lines were propagated and monitored for complete editing as well as segregation away from the DsRed marker associated with the Cas9 T-DNA. The PCR amplicons from successfully edited and segregated triple mutant plants were sequenced to confirm null mutations (Figure S7). Finally, oil composition was compared to that of the T-DNA triple mutant to confirm the phenotype (Figure S1). For all of the following experiments the T-DNA mutant combinations of pii, badc1 badc2 (badc1badc2), badc1 badc3 (badc1badc3), pii badc1 badc2 (piibadc1badc2), as well as the pii T-DNA and badc1 badc3 CRISPR triple mutant (piibadc1bad3) were utilized. 

# Growth conditions

Seeds were surface sterilized by incubating in $70 \%$ ethanol for 5 min, then $0 . 1 \%$ SDS and $0 . 5 \%$ sodium hypochlorite for 10 min, followed by 5 rinses in sterile distilled water and stratification at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { { h } }$ . Sterile stratified seeds were sown directly onto soil 

and grown under a $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ photoperiod with light intensity of 125 to $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ in a walk-in growth room held at 21 to $2 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Plants were grown in $8 \mathrm { c m } \times 8$ cm square pots at a density of 1 plant per pot for seed yield, oil, and protein and 4 plants per pot for analysis of leaf lipids, and biochemical experiments. For biochemical experiments plants were fertilized once a week following germination with PETERs 20-20-20 at a concentration of 500 PPM N, P, K as needed until harvest (Peters Professional, Everris NA Inc., Dublin, OH, USA). Plants grown for seed oil analysis with low or high N were fertilized following germination with 50 mL of a modified N free Hoagland’s solution (Hoagland and Arnon 1950) in which both potassium nitrate $( 2 . 5 \mathrm { m M } )$ ) and calcium nitrate (2.5 mM) were omitted and replaced with potassium sulfate calcium chloride at their respective concentrations. For plants with grown under high N, ammonium nitrate was added to fertilization media at a concentration of $2 . 4 \mathrm { m M }$ (4.8 mM total N) for a total N delivery of 150 mg/plant based on Arabid response to N fertilization, see Perchlik and Tegeder (2018). For root length, ACCase activity, and nitrite growth studies, sterile stratified seeds were plated on media containing $1 \%$ sucrose $0 . 5 \times$ strength MS salts (Murashige and Skoog 1962) and $0 . 8 \%$ phyto-agar. For nitrite experiments N deficient media was used and supplemented with $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ potassium nitrite $( \mathrm { K N O } _ { 2 } )$ . All plates were sealed with Micropore tape (3M, St. Paul, MN, USA), then placed in a chamber set to conditions described above and allowed to grow for 10 d. 

# Protein:protein interaction analysis

Yeast 2-hybrid protein/protein interaction studies were performed using the DUAL membrane split-ubiquitin Y2H Hybrid kit 3 (Dual systems Biotech, Schlieren, Switzerland). The protein coding sequence of PII (GLB1) transcript, without its chloroplast localization signal, was amplified by PCR (Table S3) and cloned into the dual SfiI sites in bait plasmid pBT3-C to create a C-terminal Cub-LexA-VP16 fusion. Similarly, multiple prey sequences including BC, BADC 1-3, BCCP 1-2, and NAGK were amplified without their chloroplast, localization signal and cloned into prey plasmid pPR3-N to create NubG-HA epitope fusions (Table S3). Plasmid combinations were transformed into Saccharomyces cerevisiae strain NYM51 using the S.c. Easycomp transformation kit (Invitrogen, Walthum, MA, USA) and plated onto solid “SD-LW’ synthetic media $( 2 \% \mathrm { w } / \mathrm { v }$ glucose, $0 . 6 7 \%$ w/v yeast N base without amino acids, $2 \%$ bacto-agar, and yeast amino acid dropout mix) lacking tryptophan and leucine (Invitrogen). Following successful transformation, yeast containing both bait and prey constructs were diluted to an optical density of 0.5 $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Five microliters of these suspensions and 1:10 serial dilutions thereof were plated onto SD media lacking tryptophan, leucine, histidine, and adenine (Invitrogen), and allowed to grow on plates for $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ . The VP16 and LexA transcriptional activators present in the bait plasmid fusions regulate the LacZ reporter gene in strain NYM51, which allows for quantitative assessment of protein:protein interactions by in vitro galactosidase activity assays. Galactosidase activity was measured from 6 flesh independently grown yeast cultures diluted as described above using the yeast beta-galactosidase assay kit (Fischer Scientific, Hampton, NH, USA) following the manufacturer’s instructions for microplate-based stopped quantitative assays. 

# Vegetative analysis

Root length was measured from 10-d-old seedlings grown on solid media plates as described above. After 10 d plant roots were imaged, and root length was measured using ImageJ 

(Schneider et al. 2012) by tracing the developing roots using built in free hand line tool. Rosette mass was measured in 3-wk-old rosettes by first cutting the plant at the base of the epicotyl, then drying the entire rosette at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. Dried rosettes were weighed using a microbalance and analyzed for changes in rosette dry mass. 

Leaf lipids were analyzed from 3-wk-old wild type and mutants of both pii and badc proteins by quenching an entire rosette in 2 mL of $8 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ isopropanol for 15 min in an 8 mL glass tube with PTFE-lined cap. Lipids and total chlorophyll were extracted using a method modified from Bligh and Dyer (1959). In short, samples were ground in a polytron and 4 mL of a 4:1 chloroform: methanol (vol/vol) mix was added followed by centrifugation at $2 , 5 0 0 \times g$ for 5 min. The supernatant was transferred to a new 13 mL glass tube and the solvent extraction above was repeated with 6 mL 2:1 chloroform: methanol and centrifuged again. The supernatants were combined and 2 mL of $0 . 8 \%$ KCl was added, shaken, and organic and aqueous phases separated by centrifugation. The total extraction was dried down under $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ and resuspended in toluene containing $+ 0 . 0 0 5 \%$ BHT. Neutral and polar lipids were separated and collected from leaf lipid extracts using an Agilent 1260 Infinity HPLC system and attached fraction collector following the separation described in (Kotapati and Bates 2020) with modifications as indicated in Kotapati and Bates (2021) and Wang et al. (2022). 

# Leaf lipid and seed oil quantification

Total leaf and lipid fractions dissolved in toluene were analyzed by derivatizing lipids to FAMEs and analyzing the resulting FAMEs were separated by gas chromatography and flame ionization detection (GC-FID) following a method modified from (Garneau et al. 2025). First, 20 µg of a Tripentadecanoin (15:0) internal standard dissolved in 50 µL toluene and 1 mL of $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) sulfuric acid in methanol were added to 8 mL glass vials with PTFE lined caps. Samples were heated to $8 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 50 min. Following derivatization, resultant FAMEs were separated by the addition of 1 mL $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) KCl and 0.5 mL hexane and centrifugation for 5 min at $2 , 0 0 0 \times g$ . For seed oil analysis, including weight, seed counting, and FAME production, the method of (Hu and Zhang 2021; Garneau et al. 2025) was utilized. FAMEs were quantified against the 15:0 internal standard using an Agilent model 7890 GC-FID and a DB HEAVYWAX UI column (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA; $3 0 \mathrm { m }$ length, $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ inner diameter, $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ film thickness). The GC-FID was run in split mode at 1:10 for leaf and 1:40 seed samples; both GC injector and flame ionization detector were held at $2 5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a helium flow of $1 . 0 5 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ . GC oven temperature started at $1 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ramping at $2 0 ~ { ^ \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ to $2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , then at $5 ~ { ^ \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ to $2 6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by a 3-min hold. 

# Leaf and seed protein quantification

Total soluble protein was analyzed from 10 mg of fresh leaf material or 3 mg of dry seeds placed in a $2 . 0 \mathrm { m L }$ tube, which was then ground in a bead mill $3 0 \mathrm { H z }$ for 3 min, with a $2 . 4 \mathrm { m m }$ metal bead). Extraction buffer (1.8 mL; $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl, pH 8, $2 . 5 \%$ (w/v) SDS, $1 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ PMSF; $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail (Complete Mini EDTA-free; Rosche Diagnostics, Mannheim, DE) was added, and samples were thoroughly homogenized with the bead mill (15 Hz for 1 min). Samples were then heated at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min and seed material was sedimented by centrifugation at $1 5 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ using a benchtop centrifuge for 10 min, the resulting supernatant was transferred to a new 1.5 mL tube. Protein was quantified from a 1:2 dilution of the protein extract using the Pierce BCA protein assay kit for microplates (Thermo Scientific, 

Rockford, IL, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol and a microplate spectrophotometer (Spectramax 384 plus, Molecular Devices, San Jose, CA, USA; absorbance at 562 nm). 

# ACCase activity measurements

Leaf ACCase activity was measured in the developing Arabidopsis seedlings grown on plates (see above) at 10 d after germination following a protocol described in Salie et al. (2016). In detail, pairs of 10-day-old seedlings were harvested and quickly transferred to a pre-chilled 1.5 mL tube containing 150 µL cold extraction buffer containing 20 mM TES pH 7.5, $1 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, 5 mM EDTA, 1 mM Dithiothreitol (DTT), 2 mM Benzamidine, 2 mM PMSF, and $1 \%$ Triton X-100. Samples were ground with a micro-pestle on ice and spun down in a $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ centrifuge at $1 0 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 1 min. Next, 20 µL aliquots were then transferred to 1.5 mL tube containing 30 µL of either sample $\cdot 1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tricine pH 8.2, 100 mM KCl, 4 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 1 mM ATP, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ acetyl-CoA, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Haloxyfop (to inhibit homomeric ACCase), $1 \mu \mathrm { M N a H } ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 }$ [55.5 KBq]), or control (sample assay reagent with acetyl-CoA omitted) assay buffers. The samples were briefly mixed and incubated at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min. Enzymatic reactions were stopped with the addition of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 2 M HCl and the resulting solution was pipetted into a scintillation vial containing a $1 \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ square of filter paper. Samples were heated at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to remove excess $[ { } ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C } ] \mathrm { - C } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , 5 mL of Ecoscint added (National Diagnostics, Atlanta, GA, USA), and the resulting samples counted using a liquid scintillation counter (TRI-CARB 4910TR, Perkin Elmer, Walthum, MA, USA). 

# Analysis of seedlings grown on nitrite

A total of 50 Arabidopsis seeds were plated and grown on solid media containing $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ nitrite (described above) were grown for 3 wk under constant light (20 t $) 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ and constant temperature of 20 to ${ 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . At the end of 3 wk, seedlings were imaged, and the number of surviving seedlings that were green seedlings and had developed past the 2-cotyledon stage were counted. 

# Absolute quantitation of ACCase proteins

Protein extraction and quantitation were performed as described in Wilson and Thelen, (2018). Prior to mass spectrometry analysis, $1 0 \mu \ g$ of each sample was aliquoted and normalized to the same concentration (10 µL volume) in urea buffer. AQUA peptides were used to monitor the abundance levels of ACCase catalytic subunits (α-CT, $\beta$ -CT, BC, BCCP1, and BCCP2) and regulatory subunits (BADC1, BADC2, BADC3, CTI1, CTI2, CTI3, and PII). A 12.5 µL aliquot of AQUA peptide working solution was spiked into the biological samples before tryptic digestion, totalizing 1.25 picomoles of each AQUA peptide per sample. Reduction was performed at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min with 10 mM DTT in $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ammonium bicarbonate, followed by alkylation with $4 0 \mathrm { m M }$ iodoacetamide in $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ammonium bicarbonate for 1 h at room temperature in the dark. Digestion was carried out by adding trypsin twice at an enzyme-to-protein ratio of 1:12 and incubating at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $\neg 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ followed by an additional 6 h (final ratio of 1:6). After digestion, tryptic peptides were stored at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h, lyophilized via a centrifugal evaporator, and stored at $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until mass spectrometry analysis. Samples were resuspended in $1 2 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) formic acid in water Optima LC/MS, resulting in a final AQUA peptide concentration of 10 femtomoles µL-1 and protein digest concentration of 80 ng µL-1. An injection volume of 5 µL for each biological replicate was used. Liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry analyses were conducted using an Easy nLC 1200 system (Thermo Fisher, San Jose, CA) coupled to a TSQ Altis Plus 

mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher, San Jose, CA) over a 32-min gradient at a flow rate of 300 nL/min. Transitions were optimized using the Skyline MRM method (64-bit, version 23.1.0.380) (MacLean et al. 2010; Bereman et al. 2012), and integrated into a final, optimized scheduled MRM method with a 3-min retention time window. The summed intensity for 8 transitions of light and heavy peptides was extracted using Skyline software. All AQUA peptides were injected within the linear detection range as previously detailed (Wilson and Thelen 2018). The mass spectrometry proteomics data have been deposited to the ProteomeXchange Consortium via the PRIDE [1] partner repository with the dataset identifier PXD057351.
