# ABSTRACT

$C$ -glycosides represent a large group of natural products with a C-C bond between the aglycone and the sugar moiety. They exhibit great structural diversity, wide natural distribution, and significant biological activities. By the end of 2021, at least 754 $C$ -glycosides and their derivatives have been isolated and characterized from plants. Thus far, 66 functional $C$ -glycosyltransferases (CGTs) have been discovered from plants, and provide green and efficient approaches to synthesize $C$ -glycosides. Herein, advances in plant-derived $C$ -glycosides are comprehensively summarized from aspects of structural diversity and identification, bioactivities, and biotechnological production. New strategies to discover novel $C$ -glycosides and CGTs, as well as the applications of biotechnological methods to produce $C$ -glycosides in the future are also discussed.

# 1. Introduction

Glycosides are a huge family of natural products with one or more glycosidic bonds. Glycosylation is a common late-stage modification in the biosynthetic pathways of plants, and remarkably improves structural diversity of plant secondary metabolites. According to the type of glycosidic bond, glycosides can be classified into $O$ -, $C$ -, $S$ -, and $N$ -glycosides. Among them, $O$ -glycosides are the biggest group, followed by $C$ -glycosides (Talhi and Silva, 2012; Xiao et al., 2014).

C-glycosides show various bioactivities, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antiviral, and antitumor activities. Several C-glycosides like puerarin (daidzein 8-C-β-D-glucoside) have been developed into clinical drugs. On the other hand, some C-glycosides may be important phytoalexins, and could protect plants from external stimuli like UV-radiation, dry environment, insects, and microbes. Reported examples include schaftoside, isoschaftoside, maysin, and cucumerins A/B (Hooper et al., 2010; Hooper et al., 2015; McNally et al., 2003). Moreover, ellagitannin C-glycosides in oak barrels could interact with wine, and improve taste properties in the maturation process

(Marinov et al., 1997).

The phytochemical studies of $C$ -glycosides could date back to the $19^{\text{th}}$ century (Fig. 1A; Franz and Grun, 1983). The earliest isolated compounds were aloin (barbaloin) and scoparin reported in 1851, followed by bergenin (in 1880) and vitexin (in 1898). Their structures were not established until 1950s by nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR). With the development of chromatographic and spectroscopic techniques, the number of plant $C$ -glycosides has been growing rapidly in the past four decades (Fig. 1B).

$C$ -glycosyltransferases (CGTs) are the key biosynthetic enzymes catalyzing the $C$ -glycosylation reactions in plants. Since discovery of the first plant CGT in 2009, 66 CGTs have been reported from plants (Fig. 1B; Table 1). Many of them exhibit high regio-selectivity and high catalytic efficiency, and provide green and efficient methods to synthesize $C$ -glycosides.

A number of review articles have been published on structures, bioactivities, and biosynthesis of C-glycosides (Dai et al., 2021; Franz and Grun, 1983; Kitamura et al., 2018; Putkaradze et al., 2021; Talhi and Silva, 2012; Tegl and Nidetzky, 2020; Xiao et al., 2014; Yang and

Yu, 2017; Zeng et al., 2013). However, around 400 C-glycosides have been reported from plants since the previous phytochemical review in 2012. A series of novel CGTs have been discovered as well. In the present paper, we comprehensively summarize the advances in structural diversity, structural identification, bioactivities, and biosynthesis of plant-derived C-glycosides. We searched literatures on Web of Science, PubMed, and Chinese databases (like CNKI) published by the end of 2021. C-glycosides and C-glycosyltransferases were used as the key words.

# 2. Distribution and structural diversity of plant C-glycosides

At least 754 C-glycosides have been isolated and structurally elucidated from plants, thus far (Zhang, 2022). Although a big number of C-glycosides were characterized from plants by liquid chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry (LC/MS), their structures were not fully identified by NMR (Chen et al., 2013; Obmann et al., 2011; Qiao et al., 2011; Qiao et al., 2016; Singh et al., 2015). These tentatively characterized compounds were counted in this review.

The natural distribution of $C$ -glycosides is not as wide as that of $O$ -glycosides. Nevertheless, they have been reported from hundreds of plant species, covering Bryophyta (Basile et al., 2003), Pteridophyta (Matkowski et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2020), Gymnospermae, and Angiospermae. Vitexin (apigenin 8-C-glucoside) and isovitexin (apigenin 6-C-glucoside) may be the most popular $C$ -glycosides in the plant kingdom. Thus far, they have been reported from at least 452 plant species of 103 families (Fig. 2A). Likewise, their analogues orientin (luteolin 8-C-glucoside) and isoorientin (luteolin 6-C-glucoside) have been reported from at least 268 plant species of 72 families. Most plant $C$ -glycosides are from families of Leguminosae (Fabaceae), Poaceae, Asteraceae (Compositae), Rosaceae, and Passifloraceae. A number of medicinal plants contain abundant $C$ -glycosides as the major bioactive compounds (Fig. 2B). For instance, the flowers of Trollius chinensis (used as the Chinese herbal medicine Jin-Lian-Hua) contain abundant flavone 8-C-glycosides like vitexin and orientin (Zou et al., 2004; Zou et al.,

2005). The kernels of Ziziphus jujuba (used as the Chinese herbal medicine Suan-Zao-Ren) are rich in flavone 6-C-glycosides like spinosin and swertisin (Ni and Li, 2021). Scutellaria baicalensis (the roots of which are used as the Chinese herbal medicine Huang-Qin) contains a variety of di-C-glycosides like schaftoside (Wang et al., 2018). Pueraria lobata (the roots of which are used as the Chinese herbal medicine Ge-Gen) contains isoflavone 8-C-glycosides, and the content of puerarin is $>2.4\%$ (Song and Ye, 2014). The flowers of Carthamus tinctorius (used as the Chinese herbal medicine Hong-Hua) contain unique quinochalcone C-glycosides, and the content of hydroxysafflor yellow A (HSYA) could be up to $1.5\%$ (Zhao et al., 2020). Aloe species contain abundant C-glycosides of chromosomes and anthrones, and the content of aloin (barbaloin) could be up to $30\%$ of the dried leaf exudates (Patel et al., 2012). C-glycosides are also present in many cereal crops and edible plants like rice, maize, wheat, sorghum, barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) (Norbaek et al., 2000; Ferreres et al., 2008), tea (Ishida et al., 2009), cucumber, and tomato (Lycopersicon esculentum Mill.) (Slimestad et al., 2008). Moreover, some C-glycosides tend to accumulate in specific tissues of a plant. For instance, vitexin and hydroxysafflor yellow A are mainly present in the flowers of Trollius chinensis (Wei et al., 2018) and Carthamus tinctorius (Zhou et al., 2014), respectively. Flavonoid mono-C-glycosides are more abundant in the leaves of bamboo (Guo et al., 2008) and hawthorn (Li et al., 2015) than in the other tissues. In Scutellaria baicalensis, the C-glycosides of chrysin (5,7-dihydroxyflavone) are mainly present in the roots, whereas the C-glycosides of apigenin $(5,7,4^{\prime}$ -trihydroxyflavone) are mainly present in the aerial parts (Wang et al., 2018).

The structural diversity of plant $C$ -glycosides is mainly derived from aglycones, sugar residues, and acyl substitutions. Among the 754 $C$ -glycosides, 578 (77%) of them have a flavonoid aglycone, and 436 have a flavone aglycone. # 2.1. Flavonoid C-glycosides

For almost all flavonoid $C$ -glycosides, sugar residues are attached to C-6 or C-8 (or corresponding positions for chalcones or other types due to different carbon numbering rule) of the aglycone through a C-C bond. Only a few examples contain a sugar residue at C-3, C-5', or C-7, such as pakistoside A (6,7,4'-trihydroxyflavone 3-C-β-D-glucoside) from the aerial parts of Abutilon pakistaniicum (Malvaceae) (Hussain et al., 2008), luteolin $5^{\prime}$ -C-β-D-glucoside from the leaves of Cinnamosma fragrans (Canellaceae) (Nomoto et al., 2013), and $3,4^{\prime},6,8$ -tetrahydroxyflavone 7-C-glucoside from the aerial parts of Eriosema laurentii (Ateba et al., 2016). All the known $C$ -glycosides contain one or two $C$ -glycosidic sugar moieties, with 3,6,8-tri-C-β-D-xylosylapigenin from Asplenium viviparum (Aspleniaceae) as the only exception (Imperato, 1993). Popular flavone mono-C-glycosides include (iso)vitexins, (iso)orientins, (iso)swertisins, and (iso)scoparins. Cucumerins A and B from cucumber (Cucumis sativus L.) have unusual 4-hydroxy-1-ethylbenzene structures (McNally et al., 2003). Serjanione A from Serjania marginata has a rare cyclic ether linkage between C-8 and C-7 of apigenin (Heredia-Vieira et al., 2015). Some flavone di-C-glycosides contain the same sugar residues at C-6 and C-8 such as vicenin-2 (apigenin 6,8-di-C- $\beta$ -D-glucoside), whereas others have two different sugar residues, such as (iso) schaftosides containing one glucose and one arabinose. A rare biflavonoid C-glycoside (kudoubiflavonside) was isolated from the seeds of Sophora alopecuroides.

$O$ -glycosylation and acylation on the sugar moiety or aglycone of flavone $C$ -glycosides are commonly seen, like acacetin 6-C-(3''-O- $\beta$ -D-apiofuranosyl)- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranoside (Aquino et al., 2001) and apigenin 6-C- $\beta$ -D-boivinopyranosyl-7-O- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranoside (Ban et al., 2020). Flowers of Trollius spp. (Ranunculaceae) are rich sources of acylated $C$ -glycosylflavonoids, especially vitexin and orientin derivatives (Fig. 5; Qin et al., 2015; Zou et al., 2004; Zou et al., 2005). Seeds of Ziziphus genus (Rhamnaceae) are rich in $O$ -glycosyl and $O$ -acylated $C$ -glycosides, such as spinosin and its derivatives (Fu et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2011a; Wu et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2017). Gentiana species (Gentianaceae) contain $C$ -glycosides with $O$ -glycosylation on aglycone, such as isosaponarin (Bergeron et al., 1997).

A total of seven flavonol mono-C-glycosides have been reported from plants with a 3-OH at the aglycone skeleton. Kaempferol is the most popular aglycone, and glucose is the predominant sugar residue (Faizi and Ali, 1999; Rawat et al., 2009; Wu et al., 1997). A rare 7-C-glycoside has been isolated from Eriosema laurentii (Ateba et al., 2016).

Ten flavanone (also known as dihydroflavone, with a saturated bond at C ring) $C$ -glycosides have been reported from plants (Krafczyk and Glomb, 2008; Zheng et al., 2017). For most flavanones, C-2 has an S-configuration. Eriodictyol is a popular aglycone (Marais et al., 2000). A new rearranged derivative of flavanone $C$ -glycoside, saffloflavoneside B, has been identified from the florets of Carthamus tinctorius (He et al., 2014).

Eight flavanonol (also known as dihydroflavonol, with a 3-OH at C-ring) $C$ -glycosides have been identified from plants (Dokkedal et al., 2007; Francioso et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2013; Mohamed et al., 2017; Mohamed and Ibrahim, 2020). A rare 7-C-glycoside has been identified from Eriosema laurentii (Ateba et al., 2016).

All the known isoflavone $C$ -glycosides are from Leguminosae plants,

and most of them are from Pueraria species (Hu et al., 2020; Li et al., 2011; Kawaguchi et al., 1998; Kayano et al., 2012; Sun et al., 2008; Sun et al., 2019; Verma et al., 1986; Zhang et al., 2010). Popular aglycones include daidzein $(7,4^{\prime}$ -dihydroxyisoflavone) and genistein $(5,7,4^{\prime}$ -tri-hydroxyisoflavone). Puerarin is a widely known compound, and is the basic structure for many derivatives such as puerarin $6^{\prime \prime}$ -O-xyloside. For a series of unusual $C$ -glycosides including neopuerarins A and B from $P$ . lobata, the glucose residue occurs in a furanose form (Zhang et al., 2010).

For dihydrochalcone $C$ -glycosides, the $C$ -glycosyl residues are substituted at C-3' or C-5', which are equivalent to C-6 or C-8 of flavones (Jia et al., 2018; Slimestad et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2020). Phloretin is the most popular aglycone. Among the 34 dihydrochalcone $C$ -glycosides, 25 of them are from the fruits and leaves of Averrhoa carambola (Oxalidaceae), the commonly known star fruit, and 20 of them are acylated at $2^{\prime \prime}$ -OH of the $C$ -glycosyl residue (Jia et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2020). The only hitherto reported chalcone $C$ -glycoside is saffloflavoneside A from Carthamus tinctorius (He et al., 2014). It possesses a rearranged sugar residue containing a tetrahydrofuran ring at C-3'.

Quinochalcone $C$ -glycosides (QCGs) have a unique structure with a $C$ -glycosylated cyclohexanonedienol moiety. Thus far, they only occur in safflower (Carthamus tinctorius L., Asteraceae), the flowers of which are used as the popular Chinese medicine "Hong-Hua" (Kazuma et al., 2000; Yin and He, 2000; Yoon, 2008; Yue et al., 2014; Yue et al., 2016). QCGs contribute to the red color of Hong-Hua, and are also the major bioactive compounds responsible for the cardiovascular protective effects. Thus far, a total of 24 QCGs have been fully identified from C. tinctoirus, of which HSYA is the predominant one ( $>1.0\%$ of Hong-Hua crude drug) (Jiang et al., 2010; Jiang et al., 2013; Li et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2019; Sasaki and Takahashi, 2021; Zhou et al., 2014). It was firstly identified in 1981, and the structure was later revised into the 1-enol-3,7-diketo form following very careful NMR spectroscopic studies (Feng et al., 2013). Some QCGs have complicated structures like anhydrosafflow yellow B (an HSYB) (Kazuma et al., 2000).

The xanthone skeleton has three conjugated rings. Thus far, fourteen xanthone $C$ -glycosides have been reported from plants of Liliaceae, Polygonaceae, and Simaroubaceae families (Abdel-Mageed et al., 2014; Al-Khalil et al., 1995; Barreto et al., 2008; Kokotkiewicz et al., 2013; Olennikov and Chirikova, 2021; Pan et al., 2018). The most popular compound is mangiferin. It has been isolated from mango (Mangifera indica L., Anacardiaceae) and other plants. It has a series of derivatives

# 3. Structural identification of $C$ -glycosides

The structural identification of unknown $C$ -glycosides mainly relies on nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR) and mass spectrometry (MS). To fully establish the structure, it is necessary to identify the aglycone, the sugar residue(s), the linkage site, as well as $(\alpha/\beta)$ configuration of the glycosidic bond. For $O$ -glycosides, the sugar residues can be identified after hydrolysis by comparing with reference standards using gas chromatography (GC) or ion chromatography coupled with pulsed amperometric detection (IC-PAD) (Song et al., 2014). For $C$ -glycosides, however, it is challenging to fully identify the sugar residues because $C$ -glycosides could not be hydrolyzed readily.

# 3.1. MS/MS fragmentation features of C-glycosides

MS plays an important role in structural determination of $C$ -glycosides, particularly preliminary screening and structural characterization. Most glycosides are easy to be ionized in the electrospray ionization (ESI) source in both the negative and positive ion modes. Tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) could be used to distinguish $C$ - and $O$ -glycosides (Cooper et al., 2019). Due to cleavage of the $O$ -glycosidic bond and loss of the whole sugar ring, the $\left[\mathrm{M - H}\right]^{+}$ ions of $O$ -glycosides usually lose 162 Da, 146 Da, and 132 Da, respectively, for hexosyl residues (hexose) like glucose or galactose, deoxyhexosyl residues (deoxyhexose, or methylpentose) like rhamnose, and pentosyl residues (pentose) like arabinose, xylose, or apiose (Vukics and Guttman, 2010).

For C-glycosides, the C-C glycosidic bond is stable and uneasy to cleave in tandem mass spectrometry. Therefore, the fragments mainly result from cleavage of the sugar ring. For C-hexosyl glycosides, the pseudomolecular ions $\left[\mathrm{M - H}\right]^{-},\left[\mathrm{M + H}\right]^{+}$ , or $\left[\mathrm{M + Na}\right]^+$ could lose 120, 90, or 60 Da due to $^{0,2}\mathrm{X}$ , $^{0,3}\mathrm{X}$ , or $^{0,4}\mathrm{X}$ cleavages of the hexosyl ring, respectively. The corresponding neutral losses for C-pentosyl glycosides are 90, 60, or 30 Da (Abad-Garcia et al., 2008; Stobiecki, 2000). The $^{0,2}\mathrm{X}$ fragments are usually more abundant than the $^{0,3}\mathrm{X}$ fragments (Fig. 7A; Moura et al., 2021). For di-C-glycosides, the pseudomolecular ions could lose 240, 210, 180, or 150 Da, according to the type of substituted sugar residues (Qiao et al., 2016).

Moreover, MS/MS could be used to distinguish isomeric flavonoid 6-/8-C-glycosides. The $\left[\mathrm{M}-\mathrm{H}-90\right]^{+}$ fragments of flavone 6-C-glucosides are usually more abundant than those of 8-C-glucosides, as exemplified by vitexin and isovitexin (Fig. 7A; March et al., 2006). In certain cases, MS could help to determine the substitution sites of glycosyl or acyl groups on the C-glycosyl moiety. For instance, a neutral loss of $120\mathrm{Da}$ indicated that a feruloyl or galactosyl group was substituted at $2^{\prime \prime}$ -OH of orientin (Liu et al., 2021a; Qiao et al., 2011).

# 3.2. NMR structural identification

The NMR spectra of $C$ -glycosides are usually recorded with DMSO- $d_{6}$ , pyridine- $d_{5}$ , or methanol- $d_{4}$ as the solvent. $C$ -glycosides and $O$ -glycosides could be easily distinguished according to chemical shifts of the anomeric carbon (C-1" of the sugar residue) in ${}^{13}\mathrm{C}$ NMR spectra. The signals for C-1" usually appear at a relatively high magnetic field at $\delta 70-75$ for $C$ -glycosides, and at a low field at $\delta 99-105$ for $O$ -glycosides (Ji et al., 2015; Pelter et al., 1976). When the $C$ -glycosyl residue is substituted with an acyl group, the proton signal of the substitution position would shift downfield remarkably in the ${}^{1}\mathrm{H}$ NMR spectra (Qin et al., 2015; Wei et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2004; Zou et al., 2005).

The configuration of $C$ -glycosidic bonds could be determined by coupling constant of the anomeric proton (H-1" of the sugar residue) in ${}^{1}\mathrm{H}$ NMR spectra. For $\beta$ -glycosides, the coupling constant of H-1" is usually around $9.0\mathrm{Hz}$ due to the axial-axial coupling between H-1" and H-2". For $\alpha$ -glycosides, the coupling constant of H-1" is around $2.0\mathrm{Hz}$

# 4. Bioactivities

C-glycosides exhibit a wide spectrum of bioactivities, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, anti-diabetic, anticancer, hepatoprotective, antiviral, antibacterial, antinociceptive, and immunomodulatory activities (Bajracharya, 2015; Du et al., 2018; He et al., 2016; Kasangana et al., 2019). Several C-glycosides have been developed into clinical drugs. For instance, puerarin is used to treat cardiovascular diseases, and dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, and canagliflozin are used as sodium-dependent glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors to treat type 2 diabetes (Heerspink et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2013). Many other C-glycosides have also been reported to have SGLT2 inhibitory activities (Xie et al., 2020). Aloesin from aloe could inhibit hyperpigmentation induced by UV radiation and inhibit tyrosinase activity, and is an important bioactive ingredient of many skin care products (Patel et al., 2012).

It is generally considered that C-glycosidic bonds are stable, and that C-glycosides are not easy to be hydrolyzed by gastrointestinal acid or bacteria. Recently, several enzymes from intestinal bacteria have been reported to cleave the C-glycosidic bond (He et al., 2022; Mori et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2020).

Puerarin (daidzein 8-C-glucoside) is a major bioactive compound of the traditional Chinese medicine Ge-Gen (Song and Ye, 2014; Zhou et al., 2013). It exhibits potent cardiovascular protective effects, and has been developed into Puerarin Injection in China for the treatment of heart diseases including angina pectoris and myocardial infarction, and retinal vein occlusion (Zhang et al., 2013). In a fat diet-induced atherosclerosis rabbit model, puerarin could decrease the levels of total cholesterol, triglyceride, and low density lipoprotein-cholesterol in plasma, increase high density lipoprotein-cholesterol level, and prevent the formation and development of atherosclerosis plaques (Bao et al., 2015). The effects on atherosclerosis are related with down-regulation of lipid and energy metabolism-related genes, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor $\gamma$ (PPAR $\gamma$ ), and retinoid X receptors and downstream genes (Fu et al., 2014). In addition, puerarin can effectively treat the complications of diabetes. In a streptozotocin (STZ)-induced diabetic rat model, puerarin could ameliorate retinopathy by downregulating the expression of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and hypoxia-inducible factor-1 $\alpha$ (HIF-1 $\alpha$ ) (Teng et al., 2009).

Hydroxy safflower yellow A (HSYA) is a major bioactive compound of safflower $(\geq 1.0\%)$ , a traditional Chinese medicine (Hong-Hua) to invigorate the blood and dispel stasis. HSYA is currently a phase III investigational new drug for the treatment of acute ischemic stroke (Ao et al., 2018). The Safflower Yellow Injection with HSYA as the predominant component $(80\%)$ has been approved by the China State Food and Drug Administration and is used for the treatment of angina pectoris

and other coronary heart diseases (Ao et al., 2018; Tu et al., 2015). HSYA exhibits antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities through regulating NF- $\kappa \mathrm{B}$ , PI3K-Akt, MAPK (Erk1/2, JNK, p38), and Nrf2/HO-1 (Zhao et al., 2020). Moreover, HSYA is an antagonist of platelet activating factor (PAF), and shows potent anticoagulant activities. In PAF-induced platelet (WRP) aggregation and polymorphonuclear leukocyte (PMN) aggregation rabbit models, HSYA could dose-dependently inhibit WRP and PMN aggregation with $\mathrm{IC}_{50}$ of 0.99 and $0.70\mathrm{mmol / L}$ , respectively (Zang et al., 2002).

Mangiferin-rich plant extracts are used in a variety of food supplements and nutritional products (Mei et al., 2021). Mangiferin could be used to treat diabetes and its complications, and the mechanisms involve improving insulin resistance, protecting pancreatic $\beta$ cells, and promoting glucose uptake and glycolysis. It can also up-regulate AMPK phosphorylation and improve glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4), thus promote the utilization of glucose (Wang et al., 2022a). Mangiferin also has the potential to regulate metabolic disorders and to treat hyperlipidemia (Du et al., 2018).

Vitexin and isovitexin are the active compounds of many medicinal plants, such as trollflower, hawthorn, pigeon pea, passiflora, and wheat. They show anti-cancer, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular protective, and neuroprotective activities (He et al., 2016). Schaftoside and isoschaftoside are active compounds of licorice root (Glycyrrhiza uralensis), and exhibit antiviral, anti-inflammatory, anti-diabetic, and hepatoprotective activities. Very recently, our group found that schaftoside could inhibit SARS-CoV-2 virus as a $3\mathrm{CL}^{\mathrm{pro}}$ inhibitor. It also has anti-inflammatory activity, and could inhibit $\operatorname {IL - }1\beta$ IL-6, and TNF- $\alpha$ Schaftoside may be a promising therapeutic agent for the treatment of COVID-19 (Yi et al., 2022). Bergenin has significant antitussive effects (Bajracharya, 2015). It has been developed into a clinical drug (Fufang Yanbaicaisu Tablet) to treat chronic bronchitis.

Aside from medicinal applications, C-glycosides also play vital roles in agriculture and botany. (Iso)vitexins and (iso)orientins may act as phytoalexins (McNally et al., 2003). (Iso)schaftosides are related with drought tolerance of wheat leaves and ultraviolet resistance of rice seeds. The contents of (iso)schaftosides in rice shoots would increase remarkably after penetration of the rice blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae (Wang et al., 2020). Isoschaftoside is related with the allelopathic inhibition by Striga hermonthica, a parasitic weed that can devastated the maize crop (Khan et al., 2010). Cucumerins A and B from cucumber leaves could induce resistance against powdery mildew fungi (McNally et al., 2003). Maysin and its derivatives from corn contribute to the resistance to corn earworm larvae (Snook et al., 1995).

# 5. Biotechnological production of $C$ -glycosides

To prepare $C$ -glycosides, extraction and isolation from plant materials is currently the predominant route. This conventional procedure is time and labor consuming, and requires a lot of natural plant resources. A number of new methods have been developed in recent years for chemical synthesis of $C$ -glycosides (Kitamura et al., 2018; Yang and Yu, 2017). However, chemical synthesis still suffers from multiple reaction steps, harsh reaction conditions, low yields, and poor selectivities. Recently, biotechnological production of $C$ -glycosides has made great progress due to the discovery of versatile $C$ -glycosyltransferases (CGTs), enzymes catalyzing the formation of $C$ -glycosidic bonds.

CGT-mediated biosynthesis of $C$ -glycosides has several advantages. First, the $C$ -glycosylation reaction could take place in one single step in aqueous solutions under mild conditions. The reaction is facile and environment-friendly. Second, many CGTs exhibit high catalytic efficiency and high regio- and stereo-selectivity. The yields are high and the products are easy to be purified. Third, CGTs could be used for de novo biosynthesis of $C$ -glycosides through microbial fermentation, and thus exhibit the potential of large-scale production at a relatively low cost. Taken together, CGTs provide a green and efficient approach for directed biosynthesis of bioactive $C$ -glycosides.

# 5.1. Advances in the discovery of CGTs

The first plant CGT was OsCGT discovered from rice (Oryza sativa L. subsp. indica Kato) in 2009 (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2009). Thus far, a total of 66 functional CGTs have been discovered from plants, and over $3/4$ of them were reported during 2020-2022 (Table 1).

Plant CGTs are members of the UGT family, which are dependent of UDP (uridine $5^{\prime}$ -diphosphate) sugar donors. UGTs belong to GT

For instance, when UDP- $\alpha$ -D-glucose is used as the sugar donor, $\beta$ -D-glucosides will be produced. The widely recognized mechanism of inverting GTs is the process of second-order nucleophilic substitution $(S_{\mathrm{N}}2)$ which increases the nucleophile of the attacking group. Most of the CGTs prefer UDP-glucose (UDP-Glc) as the sugar donor, though some CGTs could also accept UDP-xylose (UDP-Xyl), UDP-galactose (UDP-Gal), and UDP-arabinose (UDP-Ara) with relatively low conversion rates. Recently, LaCGT1 from Lemna aequinoctialis was reported to prefer UDP-Xyl as sugar donor (Shaker et al., 2022).

The reported plant CGTs only catalyze the biosynthesis of flavonoid or related phenolic $C$ -glycosides. According to substrate structures, plant CGTs could be classified into two types, type I and type II. Majority of the plant CGTs belong to type I (Fig. 9; Table 1). They accept 2-hydroxyflavanones (tautomeric closed-ring form or open-ring form), dihydrochalcones, benzophenones, and similar structures. In some cases, the $C$ -glycosyl products could be further converted into flavone $C$ -glycosides following spontaneous dehydration (or enzymatic dehydration though no enzymes have been reported) (Fig. 9; Brazier-Hicks et al., 2009;

Falcone Ferreyra et al., 2013; Nagatomo et al., 2014). In recent years, a series of type I CGTs have been identified, such as AbCGT from Aloe barbadensis, ZmCGT1 from Zea mays, NnCGTs from Nelumbo nucifera, and DcaCGT from Dendrobium catenatum (Feng et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2020). Sun et al reported 32 type I CGTs (including 30 new ones) from gramineous plants which significantly expanded the CGT library (Sun et al., 2020). N4CGT1 and N4CGT2 from Hypericum perforatum could C-glycosylate xanthone directly, which were different from MiCGT (Uchida et al., 2021). MaCGT from Morus alba is a novel CGT which exhibited high regio-specificity toward coumarins. MaCGT is also the first characterized membrane-bound CGT from plants (Chen et al., 2021a). All type I CGTs belong to the UGT708 family. They have a highly conserved DPF motif near the N-terminal, which could be a sequence signature to help recognize type I CGTs from transcriptomes (Fig. 8). N4CGT1/2 and MaCGT could use xanthones and coumarins as substrates, respectively, and these functions are similar to those of type II CGTs described later. They were still classified into type I based on the DPF motif. Indeed, MaCGT could efficiently catalyze C-glycosylation of phloretin. Few studies have been conducted on significance of DPF motif on the function of type I CGTs. The D85A mutant of UGT708D1 lost the C-glycosylation activity, indicating the possible key role of this motif (Hirade et al., 2015).

Several type I CGTs such as GgCGT, SbCGTb, MiCGTb, FcCGT, and CuCGT could transfer a second sugar residue to the aglycone to produce di-C-glycosides, which could then be converted into flavone di-C-glycosides such as vicenin-2 (apigenin 6,8-di-C-glucoside) and (iso)schaftosides (Fig. 9). The di-C-glycosylation activity of GgCGT was due to the spacious substrate-binding tunnel near G389 (Zhang et al., 2020).

In addition, our group discovered seven pairs of CGTs (CGTa and CGTb) from higher plants including Scutellaria baicalensis, Glycyrrhiza uralensis, Oryza sativa ssp. japonica, and Zea mays. The CGTa enzymes could catalyze glucosylation of 2-hydroxyflavanones, and CGTb could sequentially catalyze arabinosylation of the obtained 2-hydroxyflavanone mono-C-glucosides. Subsequent dehydration would produce schaftoside (apigenin 6-C-glucoside-8-C-arabinoside) and isoschaftoside (apigenin 6-C-arabinoside-8-C-glucoside), which are popularly present in higher plants, including at least 184 plant species of 39 families (Wang et al., 2020). This novel two-step di-C-glycosylation pathway may be general in higher plants for the biosynthesis of (iso)schaftosides, as CGTa/b homologous genes were found in at least 119 other species. It was interesting that CGTa and CGTb from the same plant exhibited high homology but remarkably different sugar acceptor and donor selectivities. By analyzing the crystal structures of five CGTa and CGTb enzymes, we realized the functional switch of SbCGTb to SbCGTa by mutagenesis of seven key amino acids at the catalytic active pocket. NnCGT1 and NnCGT2 from sacred lotus (Nelumbo nucifera) showed similar catalytic functions as CGTa/b (Feng et al., 2021).

Type II CGTs could catalyze C-glycosylation at the flavone or related backbones directly (Fig. 9). Thus far, only four type II CGTs have been reported, including the flavone 8-C-glycosyltransferase TcCGT1 from Trolius chinensis, the flavone 6-C-glycosyltransferases GtUF6CGT1 from Gentiana triflora and WjGT1 from wasabi (Eutrema japonicum), and the isoflavone 8-C-glycosyltransferase PLUGT43 from Pueraria lobata (He et al., 2019; Mashima et al., 2019; Sasaki et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2017). Their sequences do not contain the conserved DPF motif and share low similarity with each other, and are thus distinct from type I CGTs in the phylogenetic tree (Fig. 8). They are considered to emerge independently by convergent evolution, which is different from CGTs in the UGT708 family (Mashima et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020). It appears that type II CGTs have higher sequence similarity with OGTs than with type I CGTs. For instance, WjGT1 possesses $82\%$ similarity with UGT84A1 (OGT) (Mashima et al., 2019). TcCGT1 is the first reported flavone 8-C-glycosyltransferase and exhibits broad substrate promiscuity (He et al., 2019). Our group determined its complex crystal structure with UDP, which represented the first crystal structure of plant CGTs. TcCGT1 is also the first glycosyltransferase with C-, O-, S-, N-glycosylation activities.

Compared with $O$ -glycosyltransferases (OGTs), CGTs exhibit conserved regio-selectivity. Type I CGTs usually catalyze $3^{\prime} - / 5^{\prime}$ -C-glycosylation (corresponding to C-6/C-8 of flavone skeleton) of 2-hydroxyflavanones, and type II CGTs catalyze $C$ -glycosylation of flavone or isoflavone skeleton at C-6 or C-8. The ortho-positions of $C$ -glycosylation sites are usually substituted with phenolic hydroxyl groups, which play an important role in initiating the glycosylation reaction (Zhang et al., 2020).

CGTs also show remarkable sugar acceptor and donor promiscuity. Some type I CGTs could accept different types of substrates, like flavones, flavonols, and isoflavones, though the conversion rates were remarkably low. A few CGTs exhibit broad substrate promiscuity. For example, MiCGT could catalyze $C$ -glycosylation of 35 compounds with regio- and stereospecificity. GgCGT catalyzes the di- $C$ -glycosylation of flopropione-containing compounds. It also exhibits $C$ -glycosylation activities toward flavones and isoflavones. The type II $C$ -glycosyltransferase TcCGT1 shows high promiscuity by catalyzing 8- $C$ -glycosylation of 36 flavonoids.

UDP-Glc is the most popular sugar donor for reported CGTs, and UDP-Gal, UDP-Xyl, and UDP-Ara may also be utilized. MiCGTb and its mutants showed the broadest sugar donor promiscuity and could utilize 11 donors. Some CGTs including GgCGT show the sugar donor preference of UDP-Glc > UDP-Xyl > UDP-Gal > UDP-Ara. Recently, our group obtained the complex crystal structures of GgCGT/UDP-Glc and GgCGT/ UDP-Gal, and found the preference may be determined by hydrogen-bonding interactions of sugar 4-OH with key amino acid residues like D390 (Zhang et al., 2020). Interestingly, some other CGTs like TcCGT1 and a number of OGTs like UGT73F17 from Glycyrrhiza uralensis have

the same sugar donor preference as GgCGT, indicating they may have similar mechanisms (He et al., 2018).

Some CGTs also exhibited O-glycosylation activities, such as UGT708A6, MiCGT, GgCGT, and TcCGT1. On the other hand, CGTs may be converted into OGT only by changing a few key amino acids. One example was the functional switch between OGT (PcOGT) and CGT (OsCGT) through exchange of the active-site motif Ile-Asp or Asp-Ile (Gutmann and Nidetzky, 2012).

# 5.2. Applications of biotechnological methods in producing C-glycosides

Given the high catalytic efficiency and substrate promiscuity of certain CGTs, they have been used as biocatalysts to prepare various $C$ -glycosides. By using different phenolic substrates and UDP-sugar donors, at least 90 new unnatural $C$ -glycosides have been synthesized and their structures were unambiguously identified by NMR (Zhang, 2022). These compounds include $C$ -glycosides of flavonoids, coumarins, benzophenones, xanthones, acetophenones, and other types of phenolics (Chen et al., 2018a; He et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020).

For instance, MiCGT and MiCGTb from mango produced a number of new benzophenone C-glycosides and 2-phenyl-acetophenone di-C-glycosides (Chen et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2018a). Their sugar acceptor and donor scope could be remarkably expanded by site-directed mutagenesis. Interestingly, the MiCGTb-GAGM mutant turned to be a coumarin 6-C-glycosyltransferase (Chen et al., 2018b). Xie et al. discovered AbCGT from Aloe barbadensis, and produced a series of C-glycosides as SGLT2 inhibitors. Chemo-enzymatic synthesis yielded compound 46a, which showed hypoglycemic activity in diabetic mice $(100\mathrm{mg / kg}$ oral administration). More importantly, 46a could be facilely biosynthesized with a cumulative yield of $3.95~\mathrm{g / L}$ by E. coli-AbCGT whole cell transformation (Xie et al., 2020).

Some OGTs could further expand structural diversity of $C$ -glycosides to produce new $O$ -glycosyl $C$ -glycosides. For instance, TcOGT4 from Trollius chinensis could regio-specifically catalyze $2^{\prime \prime}$ - $O$ -galactosylation of flavone 8- $C$ -glucosides. Its P361W mutant exhibited enhanced selectivity toward UDP-Glc. Enzyme-mediated catalysis yielded 21 products including 17 new compounds, and 5 of them showed COX-2 inhibitory activities (Liu et al., 2021a). ZjOGT38 from Ziziphus jujuba could regioselectively $O$ -glycosylate 2-hydroxyflavanone $C$ -glycosides, and produced $2^{\prime \prime}$ - $O$ -glucosides of flavone 6-/8- $C$ -glucosides following dehydration. De novo biosynthesis in $E.$ coli could produce isovitexin $2^{\prime \prime}$ - $O$ -glucoside with a yield of $49.18~\mathrm{mg / L}$ (Zhang et al., 2022).

Biotechnological strategies have been used for large-scale production of plant C-glycosides via heterologous expression systems, usually using Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae chassis cells (Shrestha et al., 2018; Vanegas et al., 2018). For example, biosynthesis of orientin and vitexin from luteolin and apigenin, respectively, has been achieved in E. coli using TcCGT1 as the key enzyme with a yield of 2324.4 and $5524.1\mathrm{mg / L}$ (Liu et al., 2021b). The conversion rates were around $90\%$ . De novo biosynthesis of apigenin di-C-arabinoside was constructed in yeast, and the yield could be $113.16\mathrm{mg / L}$ (Chen et al., 2021b).

# 6. Conclusions and future perspectives

Plant C-glycosides have shown increasing significance in pharmaceutical, agricultural, and food industry. While at least 754 C-glycosides have been reported, most of them are flavone C-glycosides. Novel structures will be discovered in the future with the development of sensitive and structural informative techniques especially LC/MS and NMR. The natural distribution and chemotaxonomy information summarized in this review article will also help to discover new compounds. Many C-glycosides show low oral bioavailability, including puerarin, HSYA, and mangiferin. New drug delivery systems need to be developed to improve water solubility and bioavailability for their drug development.

As an increasing number of CGTs have been discovered in the past

decade, more and more $C$ -glycosides can be synthesized by efficient and selective enzymes. However, the study on catalytic mechanisms of CGTs is still at the initial stage, mainly focusing on substrate selectivity. Thus far, the crystal structures of eight plant CGTs (TcCGT1, GgCGT, SbCGTa, SbCGTb, LpCGTa, LpCGTb, ZmCGTa, and UGT708C1) have been obtained, and provide important structural basis for detailed studies on the catalytic mechanisms. In future studies, structural biology to determine the complex enzyme structures with ligands, as well as other technologies such as quantum computational chemistry, hydrogen deuterium exchange, and site-directed mutagenesis could be comprehensively used to dissect the mechanisms of CGTs. The development of artificial intelligence may also realize de novo design of artificial CGT enzymes.

Novel CGTs need to be discovered to synthesize $C$ -glycosides of quinochalcones, anthrones, chromosomes, alkaloids, and terpenoids. In addition, it is highly demanded to further improve catalytic efficiency and selectivity of CGTs. Protein engineering technique is an essential method for functional modification of enzymes. The recently reported CGTs crystal structures would remarkably help modifying the functions of CGTs by rational design and directed evolution (Qu et al., 2020). It is inspiring that a few $C$ -glycosides could be produced de novo through fermentation of transgenic $E.$ coli and yeast (S. cerevisiae). Highly efficient production systems need to be established for large-scale production of $C$ -glycosides, including plant cultivation systems like Arabidopsis thaliana and Nicotiana benthamiana.
