# Flavin-containing monooxygenases in plants: looking beyond detox

Nikolaus L. Schlaich

RWTH Aachen University, Institut BioIII (Pflanzenphysiologie), Worringerweg 1, D-52056 Aachen, Germany

Flavin-containing monooxygenases (FMOs) are known in bacteria, yeast and mammals where they catalyze the transfer of one atom of molecular $\scriptstyle \mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ to low molecular weight substrates. The predominant physiological function of animal FMOs appears to be detoxification of a vast spectrum of xenobiotics but until recently very little was known about the function of FMOs in plants. In the last two to three years, genetic and biochemical characterization has shown that plant FMOs can catalyze specific steps in the biosynthesis of auxin or in the metabolism of glucosinolates, and, furthermore, have a role in pathogen defence. Thus, plant FMOs hint that further FMO functions might be identified also in nonplant organisms and could stimulate novel research in this area.

# Flavin containing monooxygenases

Flavin-containing monooxygenases (FMOs) were discovered during the 1960s in hepatic microsomes as an NADPH- and $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } }$ -requiring activity that converted N,N0 - dimethylaniline to its N-oxide [1]. In animals, there are five functionally expressed FMO genes (e.g. human FMO1– FMO5) [2]; however, FMOs have also been found in bacteria and unicellular eukaryotic organisms, for example, there is a single FMO gene in yeast [3]. FMOs can transfer hydroxyl groups to a large variety of small, nucleophilic, heteroatom-containing (e.g. nitrogen, sulfur, selenium or iodine) substrates, thus rendering them more polar and more readily excretable [4]. Plants, on the other hand, have many more FMO genes. In Arabidopsis, 29 genes are annotated as FMO-like (see below). This suggests that plants might make wider use of this enzyme family than animals do. In fact, research particularly within the past few years, revealed specific functions for plant FMOs in auxin biosynthesis and metabolism of glucosinolates, and also a role in pathogen defence, which will be the focus of this review.

# Broad substrate-spectrum detoxification processes of animal FMOs

FMOs appear to form a $4 \alpha$ -hydroperoxy flavin (Box 1) in the absence of substrate and this intermediate is stable for minutes to hours at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in vitro [5]. Because the energy required to drive the reaction is stored in the enzyme before the substrate is bound, a precise fit of the substrate (which usually helps to decrease the activation energy of an enzyme-mediated reaction) is not necessary. This is a

major reason why some FMOs can oxidize a diverse range of substrates provided that they can sterically interact with the enzyme-bound 4a-hydroperoxy flavin (FAD-OOH) [6]. Structure–activity studies suggest that, in addition to nucleophilicity, the size and charge of potential substrates are important parameters that determine access to the active site of the enzyme. Given the apparent similarity of the reaction mechanism of most FMOs, differences in the substrate specificities observed between the different FMOs can be mainly attributed to differences in the dimensions of the cleft or channel limiting access to the $4 \alpha$ -hydroperoxy flavin [7].

A role for detoxification of xenobiotics by FMOs has been shown for the European cinnabar moth Tyria jacobaea. Moth larvae feed upon Senecio jacobaea plants; these plants contain pyrrolizidine alkaloids (PA) that are toxic to unspecialized insects and vertebrates. T. jacobaeae utilizes senecionine $N$ -oxygenase (SNO), an FMO, to Noxidize efficiently the alkaloids, allowing larvae to feed on PA-containing plants [8].

Endogenous substrates with physiological roles have not yet been identified for animal FMOs. However, patients with the genetic disorder trimethylaminuria (‘fish-odour syndrome’) have a mutation in the gene encoding FMO3 [9]. The mutated FMO3 cannot convert food-derived trimethylamine to its odour-free $N$ -oxide. Therefore, patients suffer from an intense body odour resembling rotten fish, owing to accumulation of high amounts of trimethylamine.

A further (yet still speculative) role of FMOs is the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS). It has been demonstrated that a significant quantity of superoxide anions and hydrogen peroxide can be derived from the decomposition of FAD-OOH (see Figure Ih in Box 1) [10]. Thus, purified pig liver FMO1 produced superoxide anions at a rate of ${ \sim } 4 \%$ of the oxidized NADPH, whereas purified rabbit lung FMO2 produced $\mathrm { H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } }$ from up to $40 \%$ of the total NADPH oxidized [11]. The potential role of FMOs in the generation of ROS and, consequently, the regulation of the redox homeostasis of cells by these enzymes, has not yet been investigated in detail. However, evidence for a role in redox regulation was found in baker’s yeast, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which has only one gene encoding an FMO (yFMO) [3]. yFMO is involved in disulfide bond formation in proteins found in the endoplasmatic reticulum and regulates the thiol:disulfide ratio in cells by oxidizing glutathione [12]. Moreover, gene expression and enzyme activity of yFMO is regulated by changes in the redox state of the cell, thus corroborating a role for yFMO in redox regulation [13].

# Box 1. The FMO reaction cycle

The mechanism by which animal FMOs transfer a single oxygen atom to the substrate has been elucidated in detail. In the absence of substrate, the FMO enzyme forms a complex with NADPH (Figure Ia) resulting in a rapid reduction of the enzyme-bound single flavin molecule (Figure Ib,c). The reduced flavin–enzyme complex binds $\mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ at the $4 \alpha$ position of the flavin molecule, resulting in a hydroperoxy flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD-OOH; Figure Id). In this highly reactive conformation, the enzyme awaits the heteroatom-containing substrate, which needs a free electron pair (such as N or S) that can enter into the active site. Access to the active site is thus a crucial determinant of substrate specificity. Once the substrate is in place, one of the two oxygen atoms is transferred to the substrate (Figure Ie), whereas the other oxygen atom reacts to form water (Figure If). NADP is released (Figure Ig) and the binding of NADPH starts a new cycle.

Recent crystallization of an FMO from Schizosaccharomyces pombe essentially confirmed the biochemical model shown in Figure I, however, a few differences were found. Thus, in the S. pombe FMO, the reduced $\mathsf { F A D H } _ { 2 }$ coexists with molecular oxygen and forms the hydroperoxyflavin only once the substrate is bound in the active centre of the enzyme [14]. Furthermore, the substrate appears to replace the NADP, fitting into the same binding pocket as the nicotinamide of NADP. However, because this has only been shown for one substrate, methimazole, generalization might be premature.

# FMOs in plants

The first functional characterization of plant FMOs was reported in 2001. Except for one report on an FMO from Petunia x hybrida and one report on FMOs in Oryza sativa, the FMOs have been studied mostly in the model plant Arabidopsis, in which 29 distinct genes encode proteins that share motifs typically found in FMOs. Comparison of the putative FMO proteins of those plants for which the genome sequence is available revealed that Arabidopsis, Oryza and Populus FMOs fall into three clades: (i) Clade I contains two Arabidopsis proteins, FMO1 and a closely

related pseudogene, for which no expression could yet be detected, three FMOs from rice and six from poplar (Populus trichocharpa); (ii) in Clade II, 11 Arabidopsis YUCCA proteins are found together with eight FMOs from rice and 12 from poplar; (iii) Clade III is comprised of 16 Arabidopsis FMOs, of which one can $\boldsymbol { s }$ -oxygenate glucosinolates, seven FMOs from rice and four poplar FMOs (Figure 1).

# Structure/function analysis of FMOs

Animal and plant FMOs are proteins of ${ \sim } 5 0 \mathrm { k D a }$ . They bind the adenine moiety of FAD with an across-kingdomconserved ‘GxGxxG’ motif (Roßman fold) located close to the N-terminus of the protein (Figure 2) [14]. The identical, but less well conserved, NADP-binding motif $\mathbf { \bar { \rho } G x G x x G } ^ { \prime }$ is located more towards the centre of the protein [15]. A further motif, ‘FATGY’, thought to occur in proteins carrying out $N$ -oxidation, is located further towards the Cterminus of the protein. The ‘FATGY’ motif is conserved between bacterial, yeast and animal FMOs [15]. In Arabidopsis, it is found mainly as a ‘LATGY’ motif in the 11 YUCCA FMOs, the FMO1 and its closely related pseudogene (Figure 2). In the 16 Arabidopsis FMOs of Clade III, a similar motif, ‘HCTGYK’, is found, which shares the central three amino acids ‘TGY’ with the ‘LATGY’ motif. Interestingly, the ‘HCTGYK’ motif occurs before, whereas the ‘LATGY’ motif occurs after the so-called ‘FMO-identifying sequence’ motif (Figure 2). The FMO-identifying motif (‘FxGxxxHxxxY/F’) [16] is found in all known plant FMOs, although at different locations within the protein (Figure 2). Different locations of the FMO-identifying sequence motif within the protein have been reported [14,16].

# The Arabidopsis YUCCA gene family

The first functional characterization of a plant FMO was reported in 2001, almost 40 years after the enzymes had been discovered in animals. In an activation-tagging screen, two Arabidopsis mutants with longer hypocotyls have been isolated and termed ‘yucca’ [17]. These mutants have additional phenotypes resembling those of plants with elevated auxin levels, for instance, epinastic cotyledons, elongated petioles and shorter roots with longer and more abundant root hairs; in the yucca mutants, free auxin levels are increased by at least $50 \%$ compared with wild-type levels [17]. Physiological experiments, in which explants were placed on auxin-free medium, resulted in extensive root growth of the yucca mutants, whereas the wild-type explants showed no proliferation. This suggests that the increased auxin levels in the yucca mutants are sufficient for root development. Moreover, transformation of the bacterial iaaL gene (allowing conversion of free auxin to biologically inactive auxin–lysine conjugates) into the yucca mutants resulted in the loss of these morphological phenotypes, indicating that they are the consequence of elevated auxin levels. Molecular analysis of the yucca mutants showed overexpression of an FMO-like gene, termed YUCCA (YUC1; At4g32540). YUCCA probably catalyzes the rate-limiting step in auxin biosynthesis from tryptamine to $N$ -hydroxyl tryptamine because the recombinant YUCCA protein was able to catalyze this conversion in vitro [17]. # The FMO1 (AtFMO) gene

The accelerated cell death 11 (acd11) Arabidopsis mutant displays spontaneous cell death and constitutive pathogen defence activation. In this mutant, transcripts of the AtFMO gene $( A t \ = 1 g \ = 1 9 2 5 0 )$ are ${ > } 1 7$ -fold upregulated [24]. Elevated AtFMO mRNA levels were also found in another spontaneous cell death mutant, lsd1 [22]. Furthermore, AtFMO transcripts accumulated in response to superoxide anion $\left( \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } { } ^ { - } \right)$ , an ROS involved in regulating programmed cell death in pathogen defence [25]. Together, these data suggested that AtFMO can serve as a marker for plant cell death [26].

In 2006, three different laboratories independently renamed AtFMO to FMO1, and reported that this gene was involved in pathogen defence [27–29]. Thus, in an activation-tagging screen, a mutant was isolated that showed increased resistance to avirulent as well as virulent bacterial and oomycete pathogens. Overexpression of cloned FMO1 in stable transformants recapitulated these phenotypes. Furthermore, pathogen infection transcriptionally activated FMO1 whereas an fmo1 mutant displayed enhanced susceptibility to virulent microbes [28].

# FMO1 in EDS1/PAD4-mediated pathogen resistance

To understand the experimental design used by the two other groups that showed a role for FMO1 in pathogen defence, it is necessary to summarize how resistance is manifested and regulated in plants. In Arabidopsis, defence against pathogens is multi-layered. Basal resistance helps to limit the multiplication and spread of virulent pathogens and the severity of disease symptoms, to which pathogen-derived toxins also contribute. This kind of defence is not specific to a certain isolate or strain of pathogen [30]. In plants, another defence layer can be activated when host-adapted pathogens inject into the plant cell effector proteins that are recognized by resistance $( R )$ genes-encoded proteins. Most $\boldsymbol { R }$ genes encode nucleotide-binding, leucine rich repeat (NB-LRR)-containing proteins [31]. In Arabidopsis, two major classes of NB-LRR proteins have been defined based on the presence at their N-termini of either a coiled-coil domain (CC-NB-LRRs) or a domain showing sequence similarity to the Toll or Interleukin receptors of animals (TIR-NB-LRRs) [32]. The Arabidopsis EDS1 and PAD4 genes have been shown

previously to be involved in basal defence reactions, and also in defence reactions mediated by TIR- but not CC-NB-LRR-resistance proteins [33]. In a microarray study, Bartsch et al. identified FMO1 as a gene that is EDS1- and PAD4-dependent in its upregulation upon infection with avirulent bacteria [29]. A functional contribution of FMO1 to pathogen defence was shown because fmo1 mutants were impaired in defence reactions mediated by TIR-NB-LRRs, whereas CC-NB-LRR-mediated defence remained effective. Moreover, the fmo1 mutant also displayed enhanced susceptibility to virulent oomycete pathogens. Thus, FMO1 is important for basal defences as well as $\boldsymbol { R }$ gene-mediated defences, similarly to EDS1 and PAD4. Finally, overexpression of FMO1 derivatives, in which conserved residues in either the FAD- or NADPH-binding motifs were mutated, did not confer enhanced resistance phenotypes [29]. This indicated that enzymatic FMO activity is required for plant defence [29].

# FMO1 in systemic acquired resistance

Upon local infection with virulent or avirulent pathogens, plants establish in yet unknown ways a state of systemic acquired resistance (SAR) that enables them to resist subsequent infections by otherwise virulent pathogens [34]. In a search for genes that might be important for SAR establishment, Mishina and Zeier used publicly available expression data to reveal genes whose expression is upregulated after a local infection [27]. They reasoned that these are good candidates for being involved in SAR establishment, and thus analysed corresponding knockout lines. They found that fmo1 mutants were impaired for biological as well as molecular effects associated with SAR. Thus, infiltration of virulent or avirulent bacteria triggered SAR in wild-type but not in fmo1 mutant plants. Importantly, they found that FMO1 transcripts accumulated not only in the pathogen-treated leaf but also in untreated, systemic leaves. Moreover, physiological reactions observed in wild-type plants, such as the accumulation of salicylic acid or elevated expression of pathogenesis-related (PR) genes, were abolished in systemic tissues in the fmo1 mutant [27].

# What might be the function for FMO1 in pathogen defence?

The findings of the three groups prompt speculation on the role of FMO1 in pathogen defence. In analogy to the function of FMOs in animals, a possible role of FMO1 could be the detoxification of compounds produced by various plant pathogens. As a consequence, those pathogens would be less aggressive as manifested by reduced growth in planta (explaining the results from Koch et al. [28] and Bartsch et al. [29]) and, hence, might not elicit SAR. However, a more intriguing possibility that is in accordance with the findings of FMOs from yeast and animals is that the FMO1 protein can influence the redox state of plant cells. Changes in redox state are a recently recognized important mode of regulation of pathogendefence responses [35], particularly those mediated by EDS1 and PAD4 [33]. These redox changes might be brought about by either influencing the intracellular glutathione levels [3] or by production of ROS [10,11].

# The FMOGS-OX gene

In an attempt to identify enzymes involved in the formation of cancer-preventive isothiocyanates, which are derived from glucosinolates (GSLs), the Arabidopsis $F M O _ { G S - O X }$ gene (At1g65860) was found to be co-regulated with genes known to be involved in this pathway by screening publicly available expression databases. Recombinant expression showed that the FMOGS-OX protein can convert methylthioalkyl GSLs into methylsulfinylalkyl GSLs [36]. Plants overexpressing $F M O _ { G S - O X }$ showed a fivefold increase in methylsulfinylalkyl GSLs, indicating that $F M O _ { G S - O X }$ activity is rate limiting in wild-type plants. The S-oxygenating activity of FMOGS-OX is selective for the methylthioalkyl GSL, because precursors without the S-glucose group, such as methylthioaldoximes, are no substrate for the enzyme [36]. This shows again that plant FMOs do not show the broad substrate specificity reported for the animal FMOs.

# Future research directions

To gain insights into the physiological processes in which the various FMOs are involved, it is necessary to first identify the endogenous substrates. The biochemical function of the defence-involved FMO1 (AtFMO) protein and the other non-characterized FMO proteins is unclear so far. One possible way to identify substrates of these enzymes is the comparative analysis of the metabolome of overexpressing or knockout plants. In an overexpressing line, one might be able to identify the more abundant products of the reaction whereas, in the loss-of-function mutant, accumulating substrate might be identified, as was determined for the YUCCA and FMO plants [17,18,23,36].

Generation of constitutive FMO-overexpressing lines could also be used to gain insights into the physiological role of a particular FMO. So far, all FMOs have shown phenotypes in plants when overexpressed [17,18,22,23,28,29,36]. By contrast, loss-of-function approaches are more difficult owing to possible functional redundancy among the family members and, in some cases, require generation of multiple mutants before phenotypes can be observed [18]. Thus, whereas the genetic approach can establish the involvement of a particular FMO in certain physiological processes, the biochemical analysis is required to define the precise biochemical function of each FMO, that is, which substrate(s) is converted to which product(s).

Therefore, research on plant FMOs should change the currently somewhat simplistic view of FMOs as broad substrate-accepting detoxifying enzymes, because the plant enzymes appear to have highly specific physiological roles in the biosynthesis of various small molecules. Identification of the endogenous substrates for plant FMOs might also stimulate new research in the animal field and yield enzymes catalyzing interesting reactions for the biotechnology sector.
