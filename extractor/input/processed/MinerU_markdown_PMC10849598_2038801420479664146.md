# Haplotype-phased genome unveils the butylphthalide biosynthesis and homoploid hybrid origin of Ligusticum chuanxiong

Bao Nie1 †, Xueqing Chen1 †, Zhuangwei Hou1 †, Miaoxian Guo1 †, Cheng Li1 , Wenkai Sun1 , Jiaojiao Ji1 , Lanlan Zang1 , Song Yang1 , Pengxiang Fan2 , Wenhao Zhang3,4 , Hang Li5 , Yuzhu Tan6 , Wei Li1 , Li Wang1,7 * 

Butylphthalide is one of the first-line drugs for ischemic stroke therapy, while no biosynthetic enzyme for butylphthalide has been reported. Here, we present a haplotype-resolved genome of Ligusticum chuanxiong, a longcultivated and phthalide-rich medicinal plant in Apiaceae. On the basis of comprehensive screening, four Fe(II)- and 2-oxoglutarate–dependent dioxygenases and two CYPs were mined and further biochemically verified as phthalide C-4/C-5 desaturases (P4,5Ds) that effectively promoted the forming of (S)-3-n-butylphthalide and butylidenephthalide. The substrate promiscuity and functional redundancy featured for P4,5Ds may contribute to the high phthalide diversity in L. chuanxiong. Notably, comparative genomic evidence supported L. chuanxiong as a homoploid hybrid with Ligusticum sinense as a potential parent. The two haplotypes demonstrated exceptional structure variance and diverged around 3.42 million years ago. Our study is an icebreaker for the dissection of phthalide biosynthetic pathway and reveals the hybrid origin of L. chuanxiong, which will facilitate the metabolic engineering for (S)-3-n-butylphthalide production and breeding for L. chuanxiong.

# INTRODUCTION

dl-3-n-Butylphthalide (dl-NBP) is one of the first-line drugs for ischemic stroke therapy, which is the commonest type of stroke, attacking more than 7.63 million people and causing 3.29 million deaths in 2019 (1). Distinct from the side effects and limitations of recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (2, 3), dl-NBP is prominent for its good neuroprotection and safety in various animal models and humans (4–8) and was approved for marketing in China in 2004 (8). Moreover, the phase 2 clinical trial of NBP soft capsules in patients with ischemic stroke was started in the United States in 2017 (9). To date, the annual sales of dl-NBP have reached $\$ 1$ billion (https://doc.irasia.com/listco/hk/cspc/annual/2021/car2021.pdf). 

dl-NBP is a chemically synthetic racemic phthalide [an equal mixture of $d .$ -NBP and l-NBP; the absolute configurations of $d$ -NBP and l-NBP are (R)-NBP and (S)-NBP, which are adopted hereafter]. Compared with (RS)-NBP, the naturally distributed (S)-NBP demonstrated enhanced drug efficiency and reduced side effects. The effects of (S)-NBP on intracellular cyclic guanosine monophosphate and extracellular nitric oxide levels were antagonized by (R)-NBP (10), possibly resulting in a decrease in the drug efficiency of (RS)- NBP. Moreover, (S)-NBP is more potent than (RS)-NBP and (R)- NBP for antiplatelet (5), inhibiting apoptosis (11), hampering 

TWIK-related $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ channel 1 currents (12), preventing Alzheimer’s disease (13), indicating better neuroprotection potential. Thus, pure (S)-NBP is more promising for drug purposing than (RS)-NBP (14). However, (S)-NBP is less likely to be cost-efficiently produced through chemical synthesis than (RS)-NBP (15) and primarily relies on extracting from Apiaceous plants (e.g., Ligusticum chuanxiong) (16, 17). The low availability hampers the medicinal application of (S)-NBP. Metabolic engineering of biosynthetic pathways has been proven to be effective and environment-friendly in supplying plant secondary metabolites (18), which may be a promising approach to improve (S)-NBP production at a relatively low cost. 

However, little is known about phthalide biosynthesis. Previous feeding experiments using $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -labeled acetic acid in Levisticum officinale revealed that six C─C units were incorporated into ligustilide [4 in Fig. 1A, an analog of (S)-NBP] without rearrangement, which indicated a possible polyketide pathway for the phthalide skeleton forming (fig. S1) (19, 20). However, other possible pathways for the phthalide scaffold formation could not be excluded (21), such as cyclization of 12-C fatty acid (fig. S1). Moreover, it is very challenging to determine the intermediates in the polyketide biosynthesis pathway without standard chemicals, although numerous polyketide synthases have been identified (22). Thus, we shift our attention to the downstream steps. (S)-NBP belongs to 3-substituted phthalides (type 1 in Fig. 1B), which is the most broadly distributed type of phthalides in both plants and fungi (Fig. 1B). It was primarily found in the Apiales species (Fig. 1B) (15, 17, 23, 24), particularly in L. chuanxiong. Apart from (S)-NBP, L. chuanxiong contains at least 94 other 3-substituted phthalides (fig. S2) (25), among which butylidenephthalide, senkyunolide A, and ligustilide are the top three most abundant ones (2 to 4 in Fig. 1A) (25, 26). They distinguish from (S)-NBP only in $\mathrm { C } { - } 4 / \mathrm { C } { - } 5$ or C-3/C-8 olefinic bond (Fig. 1A), indicating that they are the likely precursors or derivatives of (S)-NBP. L. chuanxiong is a medicinal plant in Apiaceae, the dried rhizome of which is an important traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) used to treat heart diseases. Despite L. chuanxiong has been cultivated for thousands of years through asexual reproduction (vegetatively propagated with expanded stem nodes) (Fig. 2A) (27), no wild population has been reported to date (28, 29). Thus, L. chuanxiong was hypothesized to be a sterile cultivated variety (27–29). Several recent phylogenetic studies revealed that L. chuanxiong belonged to Sinodielsia clade and several related species of L. chuanxiong were recognized (30–32). Among these species, Ligusticum sinense was most frequently proposed to be the wild progenitor of L. chuanxiong based on limited evidence (27–30), which awaits further validation based on genomic evidence. The high richness of phthalide and the interesting biological background as a TCM make L. chuanxiong an ideal system to investigate the (S)-NBP biosynthesis pathway. 

Here, we present the chromosome-level and haplotype-phased genome of L. chuanxiong. By integrating multi-omics data and biochemical verification, we discovered that four Fe(II)- and 2-oxoglutarate–dependent dioxygenases (2OGDs) and two Cytochrome P450s (CYPs) potentially promoted the conversions among (S)-NBP and its three analogs. In addition, we unexpectedly found that L. chuanxiong was a homoploid hybrid based on comprehensive genomic and evolutionary analyses. These findings shed light on the knowledge gap of the phthalide biosynthetic pathway and the origin of L. chuanxiong, which paves the way for the metabolic engineering of (S)-NBP and provides insights into the future breeding of L. chuanxiong varieties. 

# RESULTS Genome assembly and annotation of L. chuanxiong

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) assay revealed 11 pairs of homologous chromosomes in each metaphase root tip cell, which confirmed that L. chuanxiong was a diploid species (28, 33), with $2 n = 2 X = 2 2$ (Fig. 2B and fig. S3A). Further examinations detected asymmetric telomere hybridization signals in at least three homologous chromosome pairs (fig. S3A), implying that structural variations (SVs) exist between haplotypes. 

On the basis of 120-Gb PacBio circular consensus sequencing (CCS) long reads, the genome of L. chuanxiong was de novo assembled to 7.01 Gb (tables S1 and S2), almost twice as the estimate by flow cytometry $( 3 . 4 5 \pm 0 . 3 8 \mathrm { \ G b }$ ) (fig. S3B), echoing the high genome heterozygosity $( 4 . 2 2 \% )$ . Given that the phenomenon was previously reported in other highly heterozygous genomes, e.g., Pogostemon cablin $( 3 . 6 9 \% )$ (34), we inferred that the assembled genome of L. chuanxiong contained two divergent haplotypes, which was further validated by Hi-C data. By Hi-C technology, most contigs $9 6 . 1 1 \%$ of the genome length) were anchored onto 22 chromosomes, and the predominant “1:1” Hi-C cross-link signals indicated 11 homoeologous chromosome pairs in L. chuanxiong (fig. S4). Using haplotype-specific $k$ -mers based on SubPhaser (35), we further phased the genome into two 11-chromosome haplotypes, hereafter 

referred to as “LcHapA” and $^ { \mathrm { e } } L c \mathrm { H a p B } ^ { \mathrm { * } }$ (fig. S5, A and B). Both haplotype genomes exhibited high completeness from Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Ortholog (BUSCO) $( 9 8 . 1 0 \%$ for LcHapA and $9 8 . 1 0 \%$ for LcHapB) (table S2). 

Multiple-tissue RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data, ab initio prediction, and homeolog protein evidence were combined for annotation, totally identifying 101,862 high-confidence protein-coding gene models (52,337 for LcHapA and 49,525 for LcHapB, respectively; tables S2 and S3). Among them, 26,694 pairs were allelic (syntenic and homoeologous) between LcHapA and LcHapB, while the other half was haplotype specific. A total of 10,760,310 repetitive elements were annotated, accounting for $7 8 . 4 5 \%$ of the genome, which is consistent with previous study (36). Long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons accounted for the largest proportion $( 7 6 . 0 2 \% )$ of repetitive elements (table S4). For both haplotypes of L. chuanxiong, expanded gene families mainly enriched in secondary metabolic pathways, while contracted gene families related to immune response, inflorescence meristem maintenance, and sexual reproduction (figs. S6 to S8). Consistent with the previous studies (37), we only detected one whole-genome duplication (WGD) event shared by the subfamily Apioideae and did not observe any recent specific WGD event for L. chuanxiong (figs. S6 and S9). 

# Exceptional haplotypic structure variations in L. chuanxiong

The high heterozygosity, asymmetric FISH signals, and the high proportion of haplotype-specific genes implied great divergence between the two haplotypes of L. chuanxiong. Further syntenic analysis revealed substantial SVs between LcHapA and LcHapB (Fig. 2C and fig. S10). In general, 39 translocations and 42 inversions larger than 1 Mb were detected, comprising $2 1 . 2 0 \%$ of the whole genome, and about half of the SVs were larger than 5 Mb (table S5). On average, each chromosome contained 3.55 inversions and 3.82 translocations larger than 1 Mb. SVs were most remarkable among Chr1, Chr2, Chr 9, Chr 10, and Chr 11, where the largest inversion $( \sim 3 5 \mathrm { M b } )$ ) and translocation (~37 Mb) occurred (Fig. 2C). 

Such scale and number of haplotypic SVs were exceptional. We compared the genomic percentage of SVs of L. chuanxiong with two other diploid species with either moderate or high heterozygosity $( 0 . 6 9 \%$ for Manihot esculenta and $1 . 2 8 \%$ for Malus pumila) (38, 39), two alloploid species (Perilla frutescens and Miscanthus lutarioriparius) (40, 41), and a diploid hybrid (Juglans regia $\times$ Juglans microcarpa) (42) (fig. S11). We found that the haplotypic SVs only accounted for 0.13 to $0 . 1 6 \%$ of the genomes in the two diploid species, which was 127- to 170-fold lower than that in L. chuanxiong. In contrast, the inter-subgenome SVs in hybrid or alloploid species, representing interspecific SVs, made up 3.70 to $2 2 . 0 9 \%$ of the genome, which were more comparable with the genomic percentage of haplotypic SVs in L. chuanxiong. These results jointly suggest the SVs between LcHapA and LcHapB are more prevalent than expected for the intraspecific level.

# Balanced ASE in L. chuanxiong

Although some level of imbalance between the gene expression of parental alleles in the hybrid has been revealed by several studies (43, 44), our data showed a non-bias pattern of the overall expression between LcHapA and LcHapB (Fig. 4A). To examine the allele-specific expression (ASE) of L. chuanxiong, we compared the expression level of all 26,694 syntenic and homoeologous gene pairs between LcHapA and LcHapB. We detected ASE in at least one tissue between 4381 $( 1 6 . 4 1 \% )$ allele pairs, while most alleles $( > 7 0 \% )$ did not exhibit significant ASE toward either haplotype in any tissue (Fig. 4B). Meanwhile, the ASE genes (ASEGs) toward each haplotype (A-bias or B-bias genes) were roughly even (2224 versus 2157) (Fig. 4B and fig. S14). These results indicate an overall balanced gene expression between the two haplotypes of L. chuanxiong, with limited level of ASE. 

We further investigated the distribution and functions of ASEGs. Despite exceptional haplotypic SVs in L. chuanxiong, we found no 

significant difference in the proportion of ASEGs between SV and non-SV regions in any tissue (Fisher’s exact test, $P > 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 4C and tables S8 to S10), suggesting that SVs did not affect ASEG distribution. Furthermore, we evaluated the evolutionary rate of ASEGs and found that ASEGs showed significantly higher $K _ { \mathfrak { a } }$ (nonsynonymous substitution rate) and $K _ { s }$ (synonymous substitution rate) than non-ASEGs (Student’s t test, $P < 0 . 0 5$ ; Fig. 4D and fig. S15, A and B). This observation indicates that the evolutionary rate of ASEGs is higher. These fast-evolving ASEGs were mainly enriched in adenosine $5 ^ { \prime }$ -triphosphate–binding Gene Ontology (GO) term (fig. S15, C and D) for both A-bias and B-bias genes.

# Four Lc2OGDs and two LcCYPs potentially promote the phthalide C-4/C-5 desaturations

The enzymatic activities of 2OGDs were verified in Escherichia coli system, while the functions of CYPs were characterized in Nicotiana benthamiana and yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae). The gene functions were characterized by both feeding assays and in vitro 

recombinant enzyme activity assays, as a solo method might be limited in elucidating the enzymatic catalysis activity under the noise generated by spontaneous processes. As a starting point, we transiently expressed each cloned 2OGD or CYP and fed the E. coli cells or N. benthamiana leaves with senkyunolide A and ligustilide and then detected (S)-NBP and butylidenephthalide. We found that four 2OGDs (LCX6BG001106, Lc2OGD1; LCX6AG001172, Lc2OGD2; LCX2BG002971, Lc2OGD3; and LCX7BG002917, Lc2OGD4) and two CYPs (LCX5AG000851, LcCYP72A1132; and LCX5BG004393s2, LcCYP716E110) could significantly promote the conversion from senkyunolide A to (S)-NBP, as well as ligustilide to butylidenephthalide (Fig. 6 and figs. S19 and S20). As anticipated, obvious signals of (S)-NBP or butylidenephthalide caused by spontaneous reactions were also detected in the empty vector (EV) groups (GC-MS peak plots in Fig.  6). However, signals in Lc2OGD1 to Lc2OGD4 and LcCYPs added groups were approximately stronger by 1.63- to 27.94-fold than EV groups (bar plots in Fig. 6). We found that fully identical oxidases were involved in the two steps of conversion, which may result from the same desaturation position (C-4/C-5 of phthalide) and similar structure of senkyunolide A and ligustilide. Lc2OGD1 and Lc2OGD2 were homoeologous alleles on Chr6, while Lc2OGD3 and Lc2OGD4 were located on Chr2B and Chr7B. The LcCYPs were distributed on Chr5A and Chr5B. As a result, two phthalide C-4/C-5 desaturases (P4,5Ds) were distributed in LcHapA and four in LcHapB. 

To further validate the activity of these P4,5Ds, we carried out in vitro enzymatic activity assay using purified recombinant enzymes from E. coli and microsomal protein from yeast. Compared with the EV groups, Lc2OGD2-, Lc2OGD3-, and both LcCYP-added groups revealed significantly higher (S)-NBP production, and all Lc2OGDs and LcCYPs showed significantly higher butylidenephthalide production (boxplots in Fig. 6). Kinetics parameters of these P4,5Ds revealed consistent catalysis efficiency with that observed in feeding assays. In both assays, $L c 2 \mathrm { O G D } 2$ was the most efficient 2OGD enzyme in producing (S)-NBP $( k _ { \mathrm { c a t } } / K _ { \mathrm { m } } = 0 . 3 8 \mu \mathrm { M } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), Lc2OGD1 was the best performing 2OGD enzyme in promoting butylidenephthalide production $( k _ { \mathrm { c a t } } ^ { - } / K _ { \mathrm { m } } = 0 . 1 7 \dot { \mu } \mathrm { M } ^ { - 1 } \dot { \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 } } )$ , and the two LcC-YPs exhibited different preference in promoting different conversions (Fig. 6 and fig. S21). The most reliable P4,5Ds were Lc2OGD2, Lc2OGD3, and both LcCYPs that consistently promoted the forming of (S)-NBP/butylidenephthalide in both feeding assays and in vitro enzymatic activity assays. Given the relatively weak enzymatic activity of these P4,5Ds $( K _ { \mathrm { m } } > 4 . 7 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , it cannot be excluded that the most efficient P4,5Ds in L. chuanxiong are not yet identified. Together, four of the six P4,5Ds effectively mediated the two investigated conversions and different enzymes demonstrated distinct preferences.

# DISCUSSION

Despite the wide application of NBP for ischemic stroke therapy (7, 14), no enzyme involved in its biosynthesis has been known to date. The present study is an icebreaker for the dissection of phthalide biosynthesis pathway, where four 2OGDs and two CYPs were proved to potentially promote the conversions from senkyunolide A to (S)-NBP and ligustilide to butylidenephthalide in L. chuanxiong. As senkyunolide A is the second abundant phthalide in L. chuanxiong (Fig. 5D), ${ \sim } 4 7$ -fold of the content of (S)-NBP, up-regulation of P4,5Ds in vivo would be a promising way to enhance the content of (S)-NBP in L. chuanxiong. Our discovery will facilitate the future molecular breeding of L. chuanxiong and metabolic engineering for (S)-NBP production. 

An unusual feature of P4,5Ds is that the enzymatic reactions involving spontaneous processes. Such reactions were also reported in terpenoid, hyoscyamine biosynthesis, and S-methyl thioester (55– 57). Because of the noise generated by spontaneous process, multiple lines of evidence and proper negative control settings are necessary to validate the function of enzymes. In this study, in vivo feeding assay and in vitro enzymatic assay brought out different revelation in validating the function of P4,5Ds. The feeding assay appeared to identify more P4,5Ds, which might be due to that the live cells in feeding assay may provide a more suitable and stable enzymatic reaction environment. However, the in vitro enzymatic assay using purified enzyme is particularly useful for eliminating false positive enzymes. In sum, we advocate integrative methods in investigating the role of enzymatic catalysis in such spontaneous reactions. 

The substrate promiscuity and function redundancy were featured for P4,5Ds. Jointly considering the data from feeding assay and in vitro enzymatic assay, at least four P4,5Ds could recognize both senkyunolide A and ligustilide that differ in C-3/C-8 bond, indicating that P4,5Ds are promiscuous to different phthalides and possibly mediate other C-4/C-5 desaturating reactions, such as from senkyunolide F to senkyunolide E (Fig. 7). Thus, the substrate promiscuity may greatly contribute to the high phthalide diversity in L. chuanxiong, as reported in other metabolic pathways (49, 58, 59). Moreover, P4,5Ds are independently recruited from both 2OGD and CYP lineages. To the best of our knowledge, the involvement of both 2OGD and CYP in the same reaction is rarely reported (49, 50, 60). An exception is flavone synthase (FNS) that belongs to 2OGD superfamily in Apiaceae but is CYP in other taxa (50). Noteworthy, the reactions that FNS mediated are also desaturation, converting the C─C in C-3 of naringenin (flavanone) to $\scriptstyle \mathrm { C = C }$ . This finding motivated us to speculated that more desaturation reaction in the forming of natural product might involve both 2OGD and CYP. From this perspective, our results unveil the probably underestimated 

gene function redundancy in plant secondary metabolite biosynthetic pathways and advocate a comprehensive survey of genes from different families with similar functions. 

In addition to the insights into (S)-NBP biosynthesis, the haplotype-phased genome also lifted the curtain of the origin of L. chuanxiong. Notably, our comparative genomic analyses showed that L. chuanxiong was a sterile hybrid from two different diploid progenitors that diverged more than 3.42 Ma. One of the parents of L. chuanxiong may be or is very related to L. sinense, explaining their high morphological and genetic similarities. However, the other parent is phylogenetically more distant and awaits further exploration with a denser sampling of related Ligusticum species. Because L. chuanxiong has been vegetatively cultivated for ${ > } 1 6 0 0$ years in China (27), rarely blooming and bearing mature seeds (28, 29), the taxonomic treatment has been a puzzling problem. L. chuanxiong has several potential synonyms, such as Ligusticum striatum $( = S e l i$ - num striatum) and Ligusticum wallichii (28). In addition, Pu (29) even treated it as a cultivated variety of L. sinense. Recent molecular systematic studies recognized more related species with L. chuanxiong, such as Ligusticum jeholense, Cnidium officinale, Ligusticum nematophyllum and Ligusticum tenuissimum (30–32). These species may be conspecific with or potential parents of L. chuanxiong and thus should be included in future studies to revolve the nomenclatural and taxonomic issues of L. chuanxiong. Although the sampling of related species is limited, our evidence supports L. chuanxiong as a distinct hybrid species from L. sinense and thus provides a more case for homoploid hybrid speciation (HHS) that is increasingly recognized in recent years (61–63). Among the HHS examples, the speciation of Ostrya intermedia via alternatively inheriting parental isolating allelic genes is impressive (62). However, L. chuanxiong revealed a different isolation mechanism through asexual reproduction that acts as an unbridgeable barrier for gene flow between L. chuanxiong and its parents. As many diploid hybrid species propagate asexually, such as Oenothera (Onagraceae) (64), Daphnia pulex (65), and Ranunculus (66), the loss of sexual reproduction may be an alternative common HHS mechanism. For L. chuanxiong, the sterility may result from several reasons. The substantial chromosomal SVs have been frequently reported to induce higher sterility levels because of the inappropriate pairing and separation during the meiosis process (64, 67, 68). Moreover, gene families related to inflorescence meristem maintenance and sexual reproduction were significantly contracted in LcHapA and LcHapB (fig. S7), which may limit the overall ability for flowering of L. chuanxiong. 

Although the two divergent haplotypes show substantial variation at both sequence and structure levels, they generally remain balanced in gene expression and seem to both contribute to the forming of (S)-NBP. Although our discoveries of P4,5Ds represent a small portion of the complete biosynthetic pathway of (S)-NBP (Fig. 7), the possibility that P4,5Ds are not dominant on solohaplotype but on both haplotypes indicates that the higher content of (S)-NBP in L. chuanxiong than its potential parent Ligusticum sinensis (fig. S27) might benefit from the effect of heterosis on the content of secondary metabolites (69). Thus, it highlights the significance of further investigation of the likely parental species of L. chuanxiong with a comprehensive sampling of species in Ligusticum and design artificial interbreeding experiments to evaluate the effect of heterosis on the content of important secondary metabolites, such as (S)-NBP, in the future study.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Chemical reagents

All standard chemical reagents were purchased from commercial vendors unless noted otherwise. The following phthalides were obtained from commercial sources: (S)-3-n-butylphthalide [(S)-NBP; Chengdu Must Bio-Technology Co. Ltd., Chengdu], butylidenephthalide 

(Chengdu Must Bio-Technology Co. Ltd., Chengdu), senkyunolide A (Chengdu Herb Substance Co. Ltd., Chengdu), and ligustilide (Chengdu Phytopurify Co. Ltd., Chengdu). 

# Plant materials

In November 2020, 50 seedlings of L. chuanxiong were collected from the top-geoherbalism region Pengzhou county (Sichuan, China), and 10 seedlings of L. sinense from Lushi county (Henan, China) were collected. These seedlings were subsequently transplanted to the greenhouse of the Agricultural Genomics Institute at Shenzhen (Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences, China). After growing for 6 months, those healthy plants with similar biomass were selected for genomic sequencing, RNA-seq, and phthalide extraction. The voucher specimens (vouchered as Nie1701 to Nie1703) of L. chuanxiong have been deposited in SZG (Herbarium, Fairy Lake Botanical Garden, Shenzhen, and Chinese Academy of Sciences). The SZG barcodes are SZG00120205 to SZG00120207. 

The 4- to 5-week seedlings of N. benthamiana were used for Agrobacterium-mediated gene transformation. The seedlings were grown at laboratory temperature under growth lights with a 16-hour/8-hour light/dark cycle. 

# Genomic DNA preparation, RNA extraction, and cDNA preparation

High–molecular weight genomic DNA was extracted from $_ 3 \mathrm { \textrm { g } }$ of fresh leaves of L. chuanxiong stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ using CTAB method and purified with a QIAGEN Genomic kit (QIAGEN, Germany). The DNA integrity and purity were monitored on $1 \%$ agarose gels and by NanoDropTM One UVVis spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). DNA concentration was further measured using the Qubit 4.0 Fluorometer (Invitrogen, USA). 

Total RNA was extracted from each tissue (rhizome, stem, petiole, and leaf) of L. chuanxiong using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Omega, USA). The first-strand cDNAs were synthesized by reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (PCR) from the total RNA samples as templates using the SuperScript III First-Strand Synthesis System with the oligo(dT) 20 primer (Thermos Fisher Scientific, USA). 

# Genome survey

The genome size of L. chuanxiong was estimated by flow cytometry assay. The fresh leaves of three biological replicates were chopped in LB01 lysis buffer $\mathrm { . } 1 5 \mathrm { m M }$ tris, $2 \mathrm { m M N a } ^ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { N a } ^ { 2 }$ EDTA, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ spermine tetrahydrochloride, 80 mM KCl, 20 mM NaCl, $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, and $1 5 \mathrm { m M } \beta$ $\beta$ -mercaptoethano (pH 7.0 to 8.0)] to release the nucleus. The resulting nucleus suspension was filtered through a $4 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ cell strainer and then treated with ribonuclease A $( 2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } ~ \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) and propidium iodide (PI; $2 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). After a 30-min incubation on ice in the dark, the fluorescence intensity of PI-stained nuclei was determined by flow cytometry (Beckman Coulter, CytoFLEX) with a sample flow rate of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { l / m i n }$ . We used fennel (Foeniculum vulgare) $\mathrm { ( 1 C = 1 } . 3 4 \mathrm { G b ) }$ ) (70) as the internal reference. All analyses were performed in triplicate for each sample. 

The genomic heterozygosity of L. chuanxiong was surveyed by $k$ -mer method based on 105-Gb Illumina short-read sequencing data $( \sim 3 0 \times )$ . Jellyfish (v2.2.10) (71) was performed to generate a 21- mer frequency distribution, which was then analyzed in Genome-Scope 2.0 (72) to estimate heterozygosity. 

# Cytogenetic and FISH assay to determine karyotype

FISH and cytogenetic assays were conducted to determine the ploidy level and chromosome number of L. chuanxiong. Two-centimeter root tips of seedlings were cut and treated with nitrous oxide on ice for 2 hours. The chromosomes were then fixed for 10 min with $9 0 \%$ glacial acetic acid. After washing twice with $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } ;$ , the samples were chopped and incubated in a 25-μl solution with a 3:1 mixture of $2 . 0 \%$ cellulase and $1 . 0 \%$ pectinase for 1 hour at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The enzymatically hydrolyzed root tips were further mashed with tweezers and rinsed thrice with $7 0 \%$ alcohol. The pretreated root-tip cells were suspended in $4 5 \%$ glacial acetic acid and dropped onto a clean slide with a cover glass at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The slides containing cells in metaphase were selected using a light microscope. 

The probes (TTTAGGG) for telomere hybridization were labeled with fluorescein-12–deoxyuridine triphosphate, a green fluorescein. Labeled probe solution of $0 . 2 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ mixed with $8 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of reaction buffer $[ 1 \times$ TE buffer and $2 \times$ SSC buffer (pH 7.0)] was added to each slide containing cells in metaphase. The slides were incubated at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min and then transferred to an in situ hybridization box at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 12 hours. After washing twice with $\begin{array} { r } { \mathrm { d d H H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } ; } \end{array}$ , the slides were stained with $8 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of $4 ^ { \prime } { , } 6$ -diamidino-2-phenylindole and covered with coverslips. The cells in metaphase after hybridization were observed and imaged using a high-resolution fluorescence microscope (Olympus BX50, Japan). 

# Genome sequencing, assembly, and quality control

For the PacBio library construction, $1 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } }$ of genomic DNA from each leaf sample of L. chuanxiong was fragmented to ${ \sim } 1 5 \mathrm { k b }$ using g-TUBEs (Covaris, USA). After removing short fragments and singlestrand overhangs, the retained fragments were converted into the proprietary SMRTbell library using the PacBio DNA Template Preparation Kit (Pacific Biosciences, CA, USA). Single-molecule real-time sequencing was performed on a PacBio Sequel II sequencing platform. 

For Hi-C library construction, chromatin was first fixed in place with formaldehyde in the nucleus and then extracted. The extracted chromatin was digested with Dpn II. The $5 ^ { \prime }$ overhangs of resulting fragments were then filled in with biotinylated nucleotides, and free blunt ends were ligated. After ligation, the DNA was purified from protein and treated following the Illumina Next Generation manufacturer’s instructions. The libraries were subsequently sequenced on Illumina Hiseq X, producing 296-Gb $2 \times 1 5 0$ –base pair paired-end reads. 

The raw data of PacBio subreads were filtered to HiFi reads by PBccs (v6.4.0) (https://github.com/PacificBiosciences/ccs) and subsequently assembled using Hifiasm (v0.16.0) (73). The initial assembled contigs were anchored to chromosomes by 3D-DNA pipeline (v201008) (74) and further manual adjustments were made to produce a chromosome-level genome. BUSCO (v5.4.3) (75) was used for benchmarking the genome with the “embryophyte_odb10” database. 

# Genome annotation

EDTA (v2.0.0) (76) was used to de novo identify, annotate, and classify the repetitive elements in the genome of L. chuanxiong. Before protein-coding gene annotation, the annotated repetitive elements in the genome were soft-masked using bedtools (v2.28.0) (77). RNA-seq raw reads of L. chuanxiong were filtered using fastxtoolkits (v0.0.14) (http://hannonlab.cshl.edu/fastx_toolkit/index. html) and then assembled through Hisat2 (v2.2.1) (78) and Stringtie (v2.2.0) (79). The raw assembly of transcripts was further validated by PASA (v2.5.1) (80), which were then incorporated into the MAKER (v3.01.03) (81) pipeline to automatedly identify protein-coding genes. Last, the gene models identified by MAKER (v3.01.03) (81) were updated by PASA (v2.5.1) (80). Functional annotations of the protein-coding genes were carried out by using BLASTP searches against entries in both NCBI nonredundant protein (www.ncbi.nlm. nih.gov/) and Swiss-Prot (www.uniprot.org/) databases. The prediction of conserved domains for the genes was performed by InterPro-Scan (v5.11-51.0) (82). The annotations of the GO terms (http:// geneontology.org/) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathways [(KEGG) www.genome.jp/kegg/] for the genes were added using eggNOG-mapper (v2.1.10-0) (83). 

# Haplotype phasing for the genome

SubPhaser (v1.2) (35) pipeline was used to phase the genome. Jellyfish (v2.2.10) (71) was first performed to generate a 17-mer frequency distribution. Then, a K-means algorithm was used to cluster $k$ -mers throughout the whole genome. The significance level of $k$ -mers clusters was evaluated by the bootstrap method. Last, subgroups within genome were identified by performing hierarchical clustering and principal components analysis. 

# Detection of SVs and SNPs

Given the large genetic distance between the two haplotypes of L. chuanxiong, it was challenging to accurately and directly align the 

whole genomic sequence of LcHapA and LcHapB. Thus, we used the syntenic gene block strategy to align and detect the SVs between haplotypes. Jcvi (v1.1.18) (84) was used to detect collinear gene blocks between LcHapA and LcHapB, and only those unique gene block pairs (1:1) were remained for SV detection. Possible duplications caused by ancient WGD events or other fragment duplication events were removed to avoid overestimating SVs. 

To compare the sequence similarity among LcHapA, LcHapB, and L. sinense, we performed SNP calling for LcHapA and L. sinense using LcHapB genome as the reference based on RNA-seq data. First, we performed RNA-seq for L. sinense. Second, we extracted the LcHapA-specific reads from the total reads initially mapped to a combined reference of two haplotypes using the LcHapA genome. Third, RNA-seq reads of LcHapA and L. sinense were mapped to LcHapB using Hisat2 (v2.2.1) (78). Subsequently, GATK (v4.2.4.0) (85) best-practice workflow was used to call SNPs with parameter “--sample-ploidy 1 --intervals.” Last, the SNP number scanned by 100-kb nonoverlapped sliding windows was compared between LcHapA and L. sinense. 

# De novo assembly of transcriptome of L. sinense

RNA-seq raw reads of L. sinense were filtered using fastx-toolkits (v0.0.14) (http://hannonlab.cshl.edu/fastx_toolkit/index.html) and then de novo assembled into transcripts using Trinity (v2.13.2) (86). The longest transcript was chosen by Trinity in-house scripts. TransDecoder (v5.5.0) (https://github.com/TransDecoder/Trans-Decoder) was used to predict CDS. The orthologous gene sets among LcHapA, LcHapB, and L. sinense were obtained using the bidirectional best matching (BBH) method performed through BLAST $( \mathrm { v } 2 . 1 3 . 0 + )$ (87). Last, the CDS identity of each orthologous gene pair among LcHapA, LcHapB and L. sinense was calculated by MEGA11 (88). 

# Comparative genomic analyses

To clarify the phylogenetic relationship and divergence time among LcHapA, LcHapB, L. sinense, and the other 10 species (Fig. 3C and table S7), we constructed maximum likelihood (ML) trees based on single-copy orthologous genes obtained from RNAseq data. In total, we identified 193 single-copy orthologs using OrthoFinder (v2.5.4) (89) with default settings and aligned each of them by MUSCLE (v5.1.linux64) (90). These aligned orthologs were concatenated by Phylosuite (v1.2.2) (91). ModelTest-NG (v0.1.7) (92) was used to detect the best-fit amino acid substitution model, based on which RAxML-NG (v1.1.0) (93) was used to construct the ML phylogeny with 1000 bootstrap analyses. MCMCTree tool of PAML package (v4.9j) (94) was used to estimate the divergence time, with three constraints for calibration (Fig.  3D and table S15). 

WGDI (v0.6.1) (95) was used to detect the WGD events in Apiales. The gene family expansion and contraction analyses were performed using CAFE5 (v5.0) (96), where the gamma rate was set from 1 to 7, and ${ } ^ { \infty } k = 5 ^ { \infty }$ was selected for best fit. GO enrichments were performed by ClusterProfiler (v4.6.0) (97) for those significantly expanded or contracted gene families. 

# ASE analyses

To determine the ASEs, we comprehensively compared gene expression levels between LcHapA and LcHapB in different genomic structure (linear, inversion, and translocation regions) and tissue 

(rhizome, leaf, petiole, and stem) types. We initially identified allele pairs in each syntenic gene block based on BBH methods by BLAST $( \mathrm { v } 2 . 1 3 . 0 + )$ (87). Then, we quantified the expression level of each allele based on RNA-seq data. Clean RNA-seq reads were mapped to LcHapA and LcHapB through Hisat2 (v2.2.1) (78) and assembled by Stringtie (v2.2.0) (79). The expression count matrix for all allele pairs was constructed by a script prepDE.py downloaded from the stringtie website (http://ccb.jhu.edu/software/ stringtie/dl/prepDE.py). On the basis of this matrix, ASEGs were identified using DESeq2 $[ P < 0 . 0 5$ , FDR < 0.05, $\log _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $> 1$ ] (98). $K _ { \mathfrak { a } }$ and $K _ { s }$ of all allele pairs were calculated by the $K _ { s }$ module of WGDI (v0.6.1) (95). Student’s t test was used to compare $K _ { \mathrm { a } } , K _ { \mathrm { s } } ,$ or $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ between ASEGs and non-ASEGs in each genomic structure and tissue type with a significance level of $P < 0 . 0 5$ . Fisher’s exact test was used to compare the ASEGs frequency between SV and non-SV regions with a significance level of $P < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Phthalide extraction and GC-MS analysis

Approximately $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } }$ of tissue samples were dissected and homogenized to a fine powder on a ball mill. Phthalides were extracted using 5 to 10 volumes (w/v) of ethyl acetate at room temperature for 1 hour. The extracts were centrifuged twice (13,000 rpm for $2 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } } .$ ), and supernatants were collected for GC-MS analysis. The conditions of GC-MS analyses for phthalide mainly followed other studies (99). 

GC-MS analysis was carried out on an Agilent 7890B GC machine (Agilent Technologies, Waldbronn, USA) with an Agilent 7000C mass selective detector at $7 0 \mathrm { e V }$ and a helium flow of $1 . 0 \mathrm { m l }$ $\operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The sample of $1 \mu \mathrm { l }$ was injected at a split ratio 50:1 and analyzed on an Agilent HP-5MS column. The column temperature was set at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for injection and then programmed at $5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The spectrometers were operated in electron-impact mode, and the phthalides were analyzed with the selected reaction monitoring mode. The inlet and ionization source temperatures were set at $2 8 0 ^ { \circ }$ and $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , respectively. The mass spectrums of (S)-NBP, butylidenephthalide, senkyunolide A, and ligustilide are presented in fig. S28. 

# Crude protein activity assay

The crude protein activity assay mainly followed the protocol of Vanholme et al. (100). Crude protein from ${ \sim } 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of ground rhizome tissue of L. chuanxiong was extracted by 1 ml of protein extraction buffer on ice for 1 hour (inverting the tubes every 5 to $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ ). We then centrifuged the extract at 14,000 rpm for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and transferred the supernatant to the other precooled tube. Protein concentration of the CPE was quantified by Pierce 660-nm Protein Assay Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific). At last, we mixed $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of CPE with $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ of each target phthalide [(S)-NBP, butylidenephthalide, senkyunolide A, and ligustilide] and subsequently detected signals for the other three by GC-MS. 

# Genome-wide mining for P4,5Ds

We initially excluded those genes with an FPKM ${ \le } 2 0$ and selected those significantly up-regulated genes in the rhizome [log2(fold change) $> 1$ , $P < 0 . 0 5$ , FDR $< 0 . 0 5$ ] compared with leaf, stem, and petiole using DESeq2 (96). We then calculated Pearson correlation coefficients between contents of (S)-NBP (also butylidenephthalide) and the FPKM of genes in rhizome using the R package psych 

(https://rdocumentation.org/packages/psych/versions/2.3.3). We selected those genes with $r > 0 . 9 5$ and $P < 0 . 0 5$ . 

HMMER3 (v3.3.2) (101) was used to search 2OGDs and CYPs with an e-value of $1 \times { 1 0 } ^ { - 6 }$ . HMMER profile PF00067 was used for CYPs search, and PF14226 and PF03171 were used to search genes in DOXC family (2OGD superfamily). The possible pseudogenes (length of predicted CYPs < 300 amino acids and 2OGDs $< 2 0 0$ amino acids) were discarded. Gene structures of all candidate 2OGDs and CYPs were manually adjusted using IGV-GSAman (https://gitee.com/CJchen/IGV-sRNA). 

At last, we constructed ML phylogenies using RAxML-NG (v1.1.0) (93) for candidate CYPs and 2OGDs. The protein alignment was obtained using MUSCLE (v5.1.linux64) (90). The best-fitting amino acid substitution model was selected as $\mathrm { L G + I + G 4 + F }$ for both CYPs and 2OGDs by ModelTest-NG (v0.1.7) (92). For each 2OGD (DOXC family) clade or CYP family, we only selected one to three highly expressed genes for cloning. 

# Cloning of candidate P4,5D genes

All biosynthetic genes tested in the present study were cloned from the cDNA of L. chuanxiong. CDS for each gene was amplified via PCR using the KOD One PCR master Mix (Toyobo). Oligonucleotide primers were designed to target the $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ homologous regions for subsequent homologous recombination assembly into the appropriate expression plasmid. Amplicon products of PCR were analyzed by gel electrophoresis on a $1 \%$ (w/v) agarose gel, and the positive bands were excised and purified using the Omega Gel DNA Recovery Kit (Omega Research). A list of all primers used for CDS cloning in this study is provided in table S16. 

# Functional characterization for candidate 2OGDs in E. coil system

Candidate 2OGDs were cloned into the pMAL vector, a bacterial expression vector based on maltose-binding protein tag, following the manufacturer’s handbook. The purified plasmids were transformed into Rosetta (DE3) strains of E. coli using heat shock transformation. Transformed cells were selected overnight on Lysogeny broth (LB) plates with ampicillin at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The positive colonies were screened by colony PCR and further cultured in $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of LB containing ampicillin at $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ and $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the optical density at $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m } \left( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } \right)$ absorbance reached 0.6 to 0.8, and, then, $0 . 5 \mathrm { \ m M }$ isopropyl- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ -d-thiogalactopyranoside (MedChemExpress) was added to induce protein expression. For in vivo enzyme activity characterization through feeding assay, the strains were fed with $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ senkyunolide A or ligustilide and further cultured at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 hours under 200 rpm. At last, the product was extracted with threefold volume of ethyl acetate. For in vitro enzyme activity characterization, the recombinant proteins were purified using the NEBExpress Amylose Resin (New England Biolabs). Assay for the purified enzyme activity was performed in a $2 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ assay buffer [10 mM 2-oxoglutarate, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ dithiothreitol, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium ascorbate, $5 \mathrm { m M F e S O _ { 4 } } ,$ , and $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.5)] containing $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of purified proteins and senkyunolide A/ligustilide at concentrations ranging from 5 to $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . The reaction was carried out at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 hours and then extracted with $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of ethyl acetate. The negative control of protein was prepared from E. coli harboring the EV pMAL. GC-MS analysis was performed to detect and quantify the reaction products, as described above. Last, GraphPad Prism software (v.10.0.2, GraphPad Software LLC) was used to plot a Michaelis-Menten model curve and calculate the kinetic parameters. 

# Functional characterization for candidate CYPs in tobacco and yeast system

For in vivo enzyme activity characterization through feeding assay in N. benthamiana, candidate CYPs were first cloned into the pSuper1300 vector and transformed into the GV3101 strains of Agrobacterium electrocompetent cells. The GV3101strains were cultured overnight in LB medium with kanamycin $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m l } )$ at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 8 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . Then, $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of Agrobacterium suspension was transferred into $3 0 \mathrm { m l }$ of fresh LB medium containing rifampicin $( 2 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m l } )$ and kanamycin $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m l } )$ and cultured overnight. Agrobacterium cells were collected by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 10 min and resuspended with induction medium $[ 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone, 10 mM MES, and $1 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 } ( \mathrm { p H } 5 . 6 ) ]$ for 1 to 2 hours at room temperature. The $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ absorbance of each strain resuspension was determined and diluted with induction medium to ~0.3. Each diluted strain was then infiltrated into the abaxial side of three leaves from different 4- to 5-week N. benthamiana seedlings using a 1-ml needleless syringe. To test the enzymatic activity, senkyunolide A or ligustilide (not present in N. benthamiana) solution $( 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrate in deionized water) was co-infiltrated into the Agrobacteriuminfiltrated leaves after 3 days. The infiltrated leaf region with substrate was marked and subsequently taken for phthalide extraction after 1 day. Infiltrations for all treatments regarding different genes and substrates were conducted in triplicate. 

The in vitro enzymatic function characterization of candidate CYPs was performed by heterologously expression in yeast (S. cerevisiae), microsomal protein extraction, and enzymatic activity assay. Candidate CYPs were cloned into the pESC-Ura vector and transformed into the WAT11 yeast strain. WAT11 transformed with empty pESC-Ura was used as negative control. A single positive colony was initially grown in $1 0 \mathrm { m l }$ of SD-Ura liquid medium for about 48 hours at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . The culture was then used to inoculate $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of fresh SD-Ura liquid medium at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ until the $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ absorbance reached 1.0 to 1.2. Cells were then collected and washed twice with sterile water. Protein expression was then induced with yeast extract peptone dextrose medium with $2 \%$ galactose for 16 hour at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . Then, the culture was immediately used for microsomal protein isolation following previous instructions (102). Enzyme reactions with CYP-enriched microsomal proteins were performed in tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.5) containing $5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of microsomal protein, $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ NADPH (reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ senkyunolide A or ligustilide, and NADPH Regeneration System (Promega) in a total reaction volume of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ . Following the addition of all components, reactions were incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 16 hours and then extracted with $3 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of ethyl acetate. The reaction products were detected through GC-MS, as described above.
