# Review

# Molecular regulation of the salicylic acid hormone pathway in plants under changing environmental conditions

Christina A.M. Rossi,1 Eric J.R. Marchetta,1 Jong Hum Kim,2 and Christian Danve M. Castroverde 1,3,* ,@

Salicylic acid (SA) is a central plant hormone mediating immunity, growth, and development. Recently, studies have highlighted the sensitivity of the SA pathway to changing climatic factors and the plant microbiome. Here we summarize organizing principles and themes in the regulation of SA biosynthesis, signaling, and metabolism by changing abiotic/biotic environments, focusing on molecular nodes governing SA pathway vulnerability or resilience. We especially highlight advances in the thermosensitive mechanisms underpinning SA-mediated immunity, including differential regulation of key transcription factors (e.g., CAMTAs, CBP60g, SARD1, bHLH059), selective protein–protein interactions of the SA receptor NPR1, and dynamic phase separation of the recently identified GBPL3 biomolecular condensates. Together, these nodes form a biochemical paradigm for how the external environment impinges on the SA pathway.

# Salicylic acid: a central plant hormone under changing environments

Molecular and hormonal mechanisms underlying plant growth, development, and immunity are sensitive to changing environments. A key plant hormone impacted by environmental conditions is salicylic acid (SA; see Glossary) (Figure 1, Key figure) [1–6], which plays a central role in local and systemic defenses against various pathogens [7–9]. SA is also implicated in abiotic stress tolerance [10–13] and plant growth/development [11,14–16]. SA biosynthesis, signaling, and metabolism are regulated by changing climate conditions, which pose serious threats to global food security and natural ecosystems [17–19]. Environmental regulation of the SA pathway directly impacts plant health and immunity [17,20–22]. Plant immune systems are particularly affected by abiotic and biotic factors (Figure 1), including elevated temperatures and altered microbial interactions associated with global warming [23,24].

A large body of literature has discussed how SA ameliorates abiotic [10,11,25] and biotic stresses [7–9], but less so about how these environmental factors mechanistically impinge on SA biosynthesis, signaling, and metabolism. In this review, we showcase how dynamically changing abiotic and biotic environments differentially influence the SA pathway at the molecular level (Figure 1). We first provide an overview of the abiotic regulation of the SA pathway, focusing on conditions associated with climate change. We then highlight emerging paradigms on temperature-regulated nodes governing the SA pathway. We also present new insights on how the plant microbiome interacts with the SA pathway. Overall, understanding these environmentally sensitive molecular mechanisms is important to advance plant biology and to generate translational frameworks to strengthen plant resilience.

# Highlights

Salicylic acid (SA) is an aromatic plant hormone mediating plant growth, development, and immunity, including local and systemic defenses against pathogens and pests.

Research for the past three decades has advanced our understanding of the SA pathway, from metabolic enzymes and receptors to signaling components and gene regulation.

Key components of SA biosynthesis, signaling, metabolism, and transport are impacted by changing abiotic (e.g., temperature, water availability) and biotic factors (e.g., commensal and beneficial microbes).

Temperature regulation of the SA pathway has led to the discovery of major thermosensitive nodes at various levels of gene/protein regulation.

Microbiome modulation of the SA pathway at the single species and community levels are being disentangled, revealing mechanisms that rely on canonical plant hormone crosstalk.

# Isochorismate synthase -mediated SA biosynthesis

The second SA biosynthesis pathway is mediated by ISOCHORISMATE SYNTHASE (ICS) and starts with conversion of chorismate into isochorismate by ICS1 in the chloroplast [34,40,41] (Figure 2A, left). Isochorismate is then exported to the cytoplasm by ENHANCED DIS-EASE SUSCEPTIBILITY 5 (EDS5) and is converted into SA by avrPphB SUSCEPTIBLE3 (PBS3) and ENHANCED PSEUDOMONAS SUSCEPTIBILITY (EPS1) [34,42,43]. At colder temperatures, there seem to be diverging trends with ICS1 expression, which could be attributed to differences in treatment durations. Dong et al. [30] demonstrated slight ICS1 induction at $8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ over the course of 120 h in C. sativus. Also, Kim et al. [44] showed significant ICS1 induction when A. thaliana was exposed to $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for a longer period (3 weeks) with or without infection of the bacterial pathogen Pseudomonas syringae. By contrast, Wu et al. [31] revealed ICS1 downregulation in A. thaliana after a short-term treatment (10 h) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ after P. syringae infection. However, they also identified upregulation of the functionally redundant ICS2 post-chilling, which suggests temporal regulation of the two ICS orthologs [31].

Like in colder temperatures, shifting from $9 5 \%$ to $6 5 \%$ relative humidity increased SA accumulation and upregulated expression of ICS1 (Figure 2A) and other SA-related genes EDS5, WRKY70, PATHOGENESIS-RELATED PROTEIN 1 (PR1), PHYTOALEXIN DEFICIENT 4 (PAD4), and ENHANCED DISEASE SUSCEPTIBILITY 1 (EDS1) in A. thaliana [28]. It would be interesting to investigate whether ICS pathway modulation by temperature, $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , humidity, salt stress, and other abiotic factors relies on the same sensing and/or signaling mechanisms.

ICS pathway genes (ICS1, EDS5, PBS3) are directly regulated by two master transcription factors, CALMODULIN BINDING PROTEIN 60-LIKE G (CBP60g) and SYSTEMIC ACQUIRED RESIS-TANCE DEFICIENT 1 (SARD1) (Box 1 and Figure 2A, left) [47–50]. Consistent with suppressed SA biosynthesis, CBP60g and SARD1 are downregulated after pathogen infection under warm temperature [2] and drought in A. thaliana (Figure 2A) [32]. CBP60g- and SARD1-mediated SA biosynthesis can be linked to upstream pathogen recognition [47–50], like the perception of pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) by pattern recognition receptors during pattern-triggered immunity (PTI) [51]. PTI induces intracellular calcium influx, which can upregulate CBP60g and SARD1 [7–9]. PTI also activates receptor-like cytoplasmic kinases PCRK1/PCRK2 that function between PAMP receptors and CBP60g/SARD1 [52,53]. Another prominent PTI marker gene is FLG22-INDUCED RECEPTOR-LIKE KINASE 1 (FRK1) [54], which is also downregulated by high temperature [46] and $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions in A. thaliana [26]. Whether CBP60g and SARD1 directly control FRK1 gene expression under changing temperature or $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ has yet to be tested.

# SA metabolism

To prevent unwanted signaling hyperactivation, SA may be interconverted into different forms (Figure 2C). For example, METHYL ESTERASE 9 (MES9) converts inactive methyl salicylate (MeSA) into active unmethylated SA in vitro [9,59]. Although the in vivo function of MES9 is unknown, MES9 transcript levels are enhanced by 10-h chilling at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ [31], resulting in more active SA and increased cold tolerance. Furthermore, transcriptome data show that warm temperature upregulates the pathogen-induced expression of SABATH METHYLTRANSFERASE 1 (BSMT1) [2], encoding an enzyme that catalyzes MeSA formation (Figure 2C) [9,60]. Collectively, SA metabolism is also dynamically regulated by changing abiotic environments.

# SA transport

Finally, the SA pathway is also regulated at the level of transport. Lim et al. [61] suggested that water transpiration controls distal SA transport. Cuticle formation mutants, acetyl carrier protein 4 (acp4) and mosaic death 1 (mod1), exhibit perturbations in distal SA transport, which can be rescued by high humidity [61]. A possible mechanism may be differential modulation of SA signaling in the guard cells, as mentioned previously [27]. Overall, little is known about how environmental conditions impact SA transport, but interplay with plant water homeostasis may be involved.

Emerging paradigms on temperature-regulated nodes governing the SA pathway In terms of climatic factors, exciting advances have emerged regarding molecular mechanisms underlying SA regulation across a broad temperature range (Figure 3). These include various nodes of cell signaling, from secondary messenger amplification (e.g., CNGCs and calcium signaling) to control of gene expression by crucial transcription factors (e.g., CAMTAs, CBP60g, SARD1, bHLH059) and transcriptional coactivators (e.g., NPR1, GBPL3). These nodes are important genetic and biochemical paradigms providing a mechanistic roadmap toward climateresilient plant bioengineering.

At ambient temperatures, CAMTAs normally repress the master immune regulatory genes CBP60g and SARD1, thereby suppressing ICS1-mediated SA production [44,65] (Figure 3). As temperatures drop, CAMTAmediated SA and CBP60g/SARD1 suppression are abrogated [44,63,64] via the following proposed mechanisms. First, CAMTAs (specifically CAMTA3) are presumably modulated at lower temperatures through calmodulin binding to its transcriptional activation domain (TAD) [64]. The CAMTA3 TAD can induce two cold-responsive genes, including C-REPEAT BINDING FACTOR (CBF) genes, by direct target gene promoter binding [64]. Additionally, CAMTA functions in the SA pathway also depend on calmodulin binding to the N-terminal repression module via both the calmodulin-binding domain and IQ motif [44]. Although CAMTA3 K907 does not mediate SA suppression, A855 (in the IQ domain) and phosphoserines S454 and/or S964 are key residues for CAMTA3-mediated suppression of SA-related genes [44]. It is still unknown what upstream cold-sensing mechanisms lead to differential calmodulin-CAMTA regulation (Figure 3).

Cold temperature regulation of the SA pathway via dynamic interactions of the SA receptor NPR1 and HSF transcription factors

Cold temperatures also control the SA receptor NPR1 to reprogram cold-related gene expression [66]. Mechanistically, cold temperature increases NPR1 protein levels, monomerization, and

nuclear localization [66], which increase cold tolerance and disease resistance in plants (Figure 3). However, in contrast to canonical SA signaling, NPR1 activates cold-induced genes independently of TGA transcription factors [66]. Rather, NPR1-dependent, cold-induced genes overlap with the target genes of certain heat shock factors (HSFs) in Arabidopsis (HSFA1a, HSFA1b, HSFA1d, HSFA1e), implying cooperation between NPR1 and HSFA1 [66] (Figure 4A). In agreement, NPR1 interaction with HSFA1 is favored at low temperatures [66], although whether synergistic interactions with the cold-CAMTA pathway occur remain unclear (Figures 3 and 4A).

# Warm temperature regulation of the SA pathway via phase separation of the transcriptional coactivator GBPL3

A key mechanism leading to plant immune activation is formation of unique membraneless organelles called guanylate-binding protein-like (GBPL) defense-activated condensates (GDACs) [68] (Figure 3). GDACs are formed through phase separation involving two key proteins, GBPL1 and GBPL3, to control transcriptional responses to pathogen infection and/or immune elicitation [68] (Figure 4B). A pseudo-GTPase GBPL1 sequesters GBPL3 under basal conditions but is displaced by GBPL3 phase separation following immune activation to drive GDAC formation [68]. Remarkably, heat reduction of GDACs directly controls CBP60g/SARD1 downregulation at warm temperatures, suppressing pathogen-induced SA biosynthesis [2] (Figures 3 and 4B). Selective loss of GDACs at warm temperatures leads to abolished recruitment of the general transcriptional machinery (RNA polymerase II and specific Mediator subunits) to CBP60g and SARD1 loci [2] (Figure 4B). GBPL3 has also been shown to be an essential component of the nuclear pore complex [69], but how changing temperatures affect nuclear pore complex assembly and import/export remain unclear.

Interestingly, loss of GDAC formation at warm temperatures does not occur globally in all SAresponsive genes, since GBPL3 recruitment is temperature-insensitive at the NPR1 locus, which exhibits temperature-resilient gene expression [2]. Additionally, NPR1 recruitment to the CBP60g/ SARD1 loci remained unchanged between the two temperatures, indicating that NPR1 is unlikely a temperature-susceptible node [2]. These results clearly show that warm temperatures selectively reprogram the SA-associated transcriptome through cooperation between temperature-resilient and susceptible modules. How the GDAC-CBP60g/SARD1 module interacts with CAMTAs and HSFs (as shown in colder temperatures) is an important future line of inquiry (Figure 3).

# Hot temperature regulation of the SA pathway potentially via calcium signaling and CNGC calcium channels

As noted earlier, at moderate temperatures $( 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , ICS1-mediated SA biosynthesis is differentially regulated by calcium signaling through CAMTAs [44,63–65] and/or CBP60g/SARD1 [2,47–50]. However, there is no clear mechanistic connection between calcium signaling and SA biosynthesis at heat stress temperatures. Previous studies have demonstrated that plant immune signaling is modulated by calcium ions generated via cyclic nucleotide-gated calcium channels (CNGCs) [70–73]. It is therefore possible that CNGCs may contribute to SA suppression at higher temperatures (Figure 3). In A. thaliana, CNGCs act as plasma membrane thermosensors by forming heteromeric complexes, which respond to temperature changes to elicit appropriate heat shock responses required for thermotolerance [74]. Whether heatmediated CNGC regulation leads to loss of SA and reduced immune responses at warm temperature is unclear, and how this relates to the HSF network requires further examination (Figure 3).

# Beneficial microbes and plant SA accumulation

Certain plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria, such as Bacillus sp. N11.37 and Stenotrophomonas sp. N6.8, can increase SA levels in A. thaliana [79] (Figure 2A, left). Smigielski et al. [80] also demonstrated increased SA accumulation and SA-responsive gene expression in Medicago truncatula inoculated with Sinorhizobium meliloti. Arthrobacter oxidans BB1 inoculation upregulated transcript levels of the SA marker gene PR1 (Figure 2B, right), although this was not observed with Bacillus sp. L81 [81], indicating species- or strain-specific modulation of the SA pathway by beneficial bacteria. Although these studies demonstrate transcriptional activation of SA genes, the molecular interactions between individual microbial species and plants remain unclear.

# Hormonal regulation of the SA pathway

The impact of rhizobacteria on SA biosynthesis and signaling can be gleaned from investigations of microbe-secreted hormones and its mechanistic linkage with SA [82]. However, the interactions between rhizobacteria-produced hormones [such as indole-2-acetic acid (IAA), cytokinin $( \mathsf { C K } )$ , gibberellins (GA), and ethylene] and the plant SA pathway has not been well dissected. Research has mostly focused on how exogenous hormones affect SA levels (Figure 2A,B), instead of real-time dynamics with actual microbes. Previous studies have revealed that exogenously applied hormones exhibit either antagonistic relationships with SA (JA, IAA, ethylene) or synergistic ones (CK, GA) [16,83–85]. On the synergistic side, Alonso-Ramirez et al. [86] identified that exogenous GA amplified NPR1 expression, resulting in increased SA levels and immunity (Figures 2B, left). CK also acts synergistically with SA by regulating PR genes to mediate immune responses [84] (Figures 2B, right). On the antagonistic side, exogenous IAA downregulates various SA pathway components, increasing pathogen susceptibility [16,83] (Figure 2A, right). Furthermore, ethylene is antagonistic to SA via inhibition of ICS1 expression, consequently modulating plant development [85]. Altogether, plant growth benefits conferred by specific rhizobacteria, such as Pseudomonas fluorescens WCS417 [87], may occur from intricate interactions of IAA, GA, ethylene, and JA to modulate SA biosynthesis and signaling.

# Antagonistic impact of rhizobacteria-activated JA pathway on the SA pathway

A detailed mechanism that has been explored is the microbiome-induced activation of JA, which is canonically antagonistic with SA [88] (Figure 2A, right; Figure 5). While SA is important for plant defenses against biotrophic/hemibiotrophic pathogens, JA mediates immunity to necrotrophs and herbivores [1,7–9,83]. SA–JA antagonism is reinforced by mechanisms converging on the NPR1 transcription activation complex in Arabidopsis, through its interactions with the transcription factor MYC2 and the Mediator complex subunit MED25 [88] (Figure 5). # Concluding remarks

Changing environmental conditions directly regulate aspects of SA biosynthesis, signaling, metabolism, and transport. There can be positive, neutral, or negative effects on various SA pathway components, depending on the environmental parameter. These molecular and biochemical trends may be conserved or different across species, although research in other plants beyond a few model organisms is still needed (see Outstanding questions). In terms of abiotic factors, an exciting molecular paradigm has developed in the regulation of SA biosynthesis and signaling across a broad temperature range. Various sensitive nodes have been discovered, including transcription factors (CBP60g, SARD1, CAMTAs, bHLH059), transcriptional coactivators (GBPL3, NPR1), and calcium channels (CNGCs). Apart from abiotic regulation of the SA pathway, there are also numerous biotic factors, including pathogens and the endogenous microbiome. Pathogen regulation of the SA pathway has been covered in other reviews [7–9,69], but newly apparent investigations have put a spotlight on how SA is mechanistically influenced by the overall microbiota and/or specific resident members.

Collectively, it would be valuable to harness the accumulating conceptual advances to formulate an integrative model with predictive power across species. Integration of genomic, transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolomic analyses is required to disentangle and reveal the complex interactions between the plant SA pathway and changing abiotic/biotic environments. Importantly, fundamentally understanding environmentally sensitive pathways such as SA biosynthesis and signaling provides a framework to engineer climate-resilient plant immune systems and enhance the long-term durability of microbiome-based technologies.

# Outstanding questions

Is SA important and necessary for environmentally resilient immunity in all plant species? Can we genetically engineer temperature-resilient SA signaling to strengthen plant immune systems based on current molecular/ biochemical paradigms?

Are the molecular mechanisms for temperature-sensitive SA biosynthesis and signaling conserved in the plant kingdom? Can we expect a similar pattern of thermoregulated SA pathways in higher temperature plants at various levels of gene regulation (epigenetic, transcriptional, post-transcriptional, post-translational)?

What are the major components and biochemical mechanisms associated with biomolecular condensates in the SA pathway (e.g., GDACs)? Are these condensates the enriched compartments of the transcriptional machinery for more efficient gene regulation?

How do plant cells achieve local, regional, and global regulation of the SA-responsive transcriptome in response to changing environmental conditions? What determines the level of sensitivity of SA-responsive gene clusters to dynamically changing abiotic and biotic factors?

How is the SA pathway differentially regulated by small molecules produced by the rhizosphere and phyllosphere microbiome? Can we distinguish between individual microbial species-level and global microbiome-level modulation of SA biosynthesis and signaling?
