# SUMMARY

Land plants have evolved a hydrophobic cuticle on the surface of aerial organs as an adaptation to ensure survival in terrestrial environments. Cuticle is mainly composed of lipids, namely cutin and intracuticular wax, with epicuticular wax deposited on plant surface. The composition and permeability of cuticle have a large influence on its ability to protect plants against drought stress. However, the regulatory mechanisms underlying cuticular wax biosynthesis in response to drought stress have not been fully elucidated. Here, we identified three AP2/ERF transcription factors (DREB26/ERF12, ERF13 and ERF14) involved in the regulation of water permeability of the plant surface. Transmission electron microscopy revealed thicker cuticle on the leaves of DREB26-overexpressing (DREB26OX) plants, and thinner cuticle on the leaves of transgenic plants expressing SRDX repression domain-fused DREB26 (DREB26SR). Genes involved in cuticular wax formation were upregulated in DREB26OX and downregulated in DREB26SR. The levels of very-long chain (VLC) alkanes, which are a major wax component, increased in DREB26OX leaves and decreased in DREB26SR leaves. Under dehydration stress, water loss was reduced in DREB26OX and increased in DREB26SR. The erf12/13/14 triple mutant showed delayed growth, decreased leaf water content, and reduced drought-inducible VLC alkane accumulation. Taken together, our results indicate that the DRE-B26/ERF12 and its closed family members, ERF13 and ERF14, play an important role in cuticular wax biosynthesis in response to drought stress. The complex transcriptional cascade involved in the regulation of cuticular wax biosynthesis under drought stress conditions is discussed. 

Keywords: drought stress response, transcriptional regulation, cuticular wax biosynthesis, Arabidopsis. 

# INTRODUCTION

During evolution, land plants have developed various strategies to survive under water deficit or drought stress conditions. In the aerial part of plants, epidermal tissues are covered by a thick cuticle layer that protects the plants from drought stress and strong sunlight (Bernard & Joubes, 2013; Ingram & Nawrath, 2017; Lee & Suh, 2013; 

Yeats & Rose, 2013). Cuticle protects the aerial plant parts not only from abiotic stresses such as drought and strong light but also from biotic stresses such as insects and fungal pathogens. Cuticle is composed of a hydrophilic cutin polyester matrix and intra- and epicuticular waxes. Cuticular waxes contain very-long chain fatty acids (VLC-FAs) and their derivatives including very-long chain-alkanes (VLC-

Alks), very-long chain-aldehydes (VLC-Alds), very-long chain-alcohols (VLC-alcohols), and very-long chain-wax esters (VLC-wax esters) (Lee & Suh, 2013; Yeats & Rose, 2013). 

The biosynthetic pathways of cuticular waxes have been extensively analyzed (Samuels et al., 2008, Hegebarth & Jetter, 2017; Lee & Suh, 2013). C16 and/or C18 FAs are synthesized in plastids and elongated to VLC-FAs by the fatty acid elongase (FAE) complex in the endoplasmic reticulum. VLC-FAs are modified to VLC aliphatic compounds via the alcohol-forming pathway. VLC-FAs are synthesized by the elongase complex through four steps: condensation by $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA synthase (KCS); reduction by $\beta \cdot$ -ketoacyl CoA reductase (KCR); dehydration catalyzed by $\beta \cdot$ - hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydratase; and reduction by enoyl-CoA reductase (ECR). The wax components are then produced via two different pathways: the acyl-reduction pathway, which generates VLC-primary alcohols (VLC-PAs) and VLC-wax esters, and the decarbonylation pathway, which produces VLC-Alks, VLC-Alds, VLC-secondary alcohols, and VLC-ketones. Then, the synthesized wax molecules are secreted through the plasma membrane by ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters (ABCG11 and 12), transferred to the extracellular space, and deposited on the epidermal cell surface. 

Contribution of increased wax amounts to drought tolerance has been widely described in different plant species such as apple (Gao, Zhang, Cheng, et al., 2022), Arabidopsis (Aharoni et al., 2004; Kosma et al., 2009; Patwari et al., 2019; Seo et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2011, 2020), barley (Shellakkutti et al., 2022), cotton (Yang et al., 2022), grass (Wu et al., 2019), rice (Jian et al., 2022), sesame (Kim et al., 2007), tobacco (Cameron et al., 2006), tomato (Wu et al., 2022), and wheat (He et al., 2022). Among cuticular waxes, VLC-Alks showed large increases in response to drought stress in various plant species (Cameron et al., 2006; Kim et al., 2007; Shellakkutti et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022) and required for plant drought tolerance (Kosma et al., 2009; Seo et al., 2011). The Arabidopsis ECERIFERUM1 (CER1) gene is predicted to encode an enzyme that controls VLC-Alk biosynthesis and is highly linked to biotic and abiotic stress responses (Bourdenx et al., 2011). Analyses of knockout and overexpression of CER1 demonstrated the important roles of CER1 for VLC-Alk biosynthesis that were required for drought tolerance (Bourdenx et al., 2011). CER1 has been identified in several plant species and shown to play key roles in VLC-Alk biosynthesis in cucumber, grass, tomato, and wheat (He et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2019, 2022). The wax ester load on Arabidopsis leaves and stems also increased during water deficiency. Analyses of knockout and overexpression of WSD1 demonstrated the important roles of WSD1 for wax ester biosynthesis that were required for drought tolerance (Patwari 

et al., 2019). Water deficit caused significant increase in cutin monomers, largely manifested as a high increase in C16 and C18 dioic acids (Kosma et al., 2009). The importance of exodermal suberin was shown in tomato drought tolerance (Canto-Pastor et al.,´ 2024). 

Several transcription factors (TFs) have been reported to function in the upregulation of wax biosynthesis in Arabidopsis under drought stress conditions (Kim et al., 2022; Lee & Suh, 2021; Lewandowska et al., 2020; Seo & Park, 2011; Suh et al., 2022). The APETALA 2/ethylene-responsive element binding factor (AP2/ERF) is a large family of plantspecific TFs that share a well-conserved DNA-binding domain (Mizoi et al., 2012; Nakano et al., 2006; Sakuma et al., 2002). AP2/ERF TFs are involved in the regulation of various biological processes, including growth and development, as well as responses to environmental stresses (Mizoi et al., 2012, Nakano et al., 2006). Especially, AP2/ERF TF has been extensively studied in the regulation of wax biosynthesis in response to drought (Lee & Suh, 2021). The Arabidopsis WIN1/SHN1 gene, which encodes the B6 subfamily of AP2/ERF, was found as the first TF that controls cuticular wax and cutin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis. WIN1/SHN1 upregulates the expression of KCS1, CER1, CER2, and LACS2, and its overexpression improves drought tolerance (Aharoni et al., 2004; Broun et al., 2004; Kannangara et al., 2007). Arabidopsis SHN2 and SHN3 were found as the WIN1/SHN1 homologs (Aharoni et al., 2004; Nakano et al., 2006), and WIN1/SHN1 orthologs have been characterized in several crops, including apple (Zhang et al., 2019), poplar (Meng et al., 2019), tomato (Al-Abdallat et al., 2014), and rice (Wang et al., 2012). Another AP2/ERF TF, Arabidopsis RAP2.4/WIND2, which belongs to the A6 subfamily of the AP2/ERF TF, is also induced by drought stress, and mediates cuticular wax biosynthesis under drought stress conditions (Yang et al., 2020). Arabidopsis rap2.4 mutants exhibit lower total wax content than wild-type (WT) plants. RAP2.4 activates cuticular wax biosynthesis by increasing the expression of KCS2 and CER1 in Arabidopsis leaves in response to drought stress. R2R3-type MYB TFs have also been reported to function in cuticular wax biosynthesis under drought stress conditions (Raffaele et al., 2008). Among them, Arabidopsis MYB96 functions as a transcriptional activator of KCS1, KCS2, KCS6, KCR1, and CER3 genes involved in wax biosynthesis (Seo et al., 2011). The MYB96 gene is induced by drought stress, and its homolog MYB94 regulates the expression of WSD1, KCS2, CER2, FAR3, and ECR genes (Lee & Suh, 2015b). MYB94 and MYB96 additively activate similar genes involved in wax biosynthesis (Lee et al., 2016). The total wax content of myb94myb96 double knockout mutant leaves is approximately half of that of WT leaves (Lee et al., 2016). Thus, cuticular wax biosynthesis is regulated by complex transcription networks under drought stress conditions. 

In this study, we found that the DREB26/ERF12/RAP2.6 gene, encoding a member of the A5 subfamily of the AP2/ERF TF family, functions in wax biosynthesis at an early stage of drought stress. We analyzed the effect of DREB26 overexpression and repression on cuticular wax biosynthesis in transgenic Arabidopsis overexpressing DREB26 and SRDX repression domain-fused DREB26, respectively. Measurement of wax levels in these transgenic plants revealed that DREB26/ERF12 regulates VLC-Alks formation in wax biosynthesis. We also analyzed the genes acting downstream of DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, ERF14 and their functions in wax biosynthesis and examined the transcriptional network regulating cuticular wax biosynthesis in erf12/erf13/erf14 mutant plants. Based on transcriptome analysis, we discussed the relationships among AP2/ERF and other TFs and their roles in the regulation of cuticular formation under drought stress conditions.

Additionally, we identified DRE-B26/ERF12 as one of the early drought-inducible genes encoding an AP2/ERF TF. To analyze the expression of DREB26/ERF12 and its homologs, ERF13 and ERF14, under drought stress, we performed a time-course analysis of their expression under drought stress from 24 to ${ 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ . # Expression pattern of wax biosynthesis genes under dehydration stress

The cuticle is mainly composed of lipids, namely cutin and intracuticular wax, with epicuticular wax deposited on the surface of plant organs, and is essential for drought stress tolerance. We analyzed the expression patterns of selected genes involved in cutin and wax biosynthesis under dehydration stress (Figure 2; Table S1). The expression levels of cutin biosynthetic genes such as CYP77A6, CYP86A4, LACS1, LACS2, and GPTA4 were decreased under dehydration stress (Table S1). In contrast, key wax biosynthetic genes were induced under dehydration stress. The dehydration stress response of selected wax biosynthetic genes is shown in Figure 2. Among the genes for VLC-FA elongation, the expression of KCS2, KCS3, KCS6, KCS19, CER2, and CER26L was transiently increased at $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ after the start of dehydration stress. WSD1 (involved in VLC-alcohol synthesis), CER1, and MAH (involved in VLC-Alk synthesis) were also upregulated $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ after dehydration stress. ABCG11 and ABCG12 (involved in wax transport to the plant surface) and SHN2 and SHN3 (involved in wax and cutin biosynthesis) were significantly upregulated at $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ after dehydration stress. These results revealed that wax biosynthetic genes are induced in response to dehydration stress and the timing of their induction is late compared with those of DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14. 

# Effect of DREB26 overexpression and repression on plant growth under normal and dehydration stress conditions

To understand the role of DREB26 in plant growth, we analyzed transgenic plants overexpressing the DREB26 cDNA under the control of the 35S promoter (DREB26OX plants). The DREB26OX plants showed fewer rosette leaves and earlier flowering than WT plants (Figure 3A,B). We also examined the effect of DREB26 repression on plant phenotype. The Chimeric Repressor Silencing Technology (CRES-T) is a gene silencing system, in which a TF fused to the EAR-motif repression domain (SRDX: Leu-Asp-Leu-Asp-Leu-Glu-Leu-Arg-Leu-Gly-Phe-Ala) dominantly represses the transcription of its target genes, regardless of the absence or presence of endogenous and functionally redundant TFs (Hiratsu et al., 2003). We generated transgenic plants expressing the SRDX-fused DREB26 cDNA under the control of the 35S promoter (DREB26SR). The DREB26SR plants showed reduced plant growth compared with the WT (Figure 3A), chlorosis (yellowing) in older 

leaves, and anthocyanin accumulation in leaves and petioles (Figure 3B). These changes led us to speculate that DREB26 might be involved in the development of leaf surface. We also observed the plant growth of the DREB26OX and DREB26SR plants during reproductive stages (Figure S6). The DREB26OX plants showed early flowering phenotype under long-day (16 h-light/8 h-dark) and short-day (12 h-light/12 h-dark) conditions. On the other hand, the DREB26SR plants showed late flowering phenotype under long-day conditions and this phenotype was clearly observed under short-day conditions. To analyze the function of DREB26 in leaf permeability, we performed the toluidine blue (TB) staining of the WT, DREB26OX, and DREB26SR leaves. The TB staining pattern of leaves showed no difference between DREB26OX and WT plants. On the other hand, the leaves of DREB26SR plants showed more intense staining than those of WT plants (Figure 3C). These results suggest that the DREB26 repression increases leaf permeability and that DREB26 plays important roles in cuticle formation in plants. Next, we observed the leaf surface of the DREB26OX and DREB26SR plants by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Figure 3D). The TEM observation of leaf surface showed that the high-density region, corresponding to the cuticle proper, was thicker in the DREB26OX leaves than in WT leaves (Figure 3D center). By contrast, the high-density region was thinner in the DREB26SR leaves than in WT leaves (Figure 3D right). It was observed that part of the proper cuticle structure was disconnected in the DREB26SR plants (Figure 3D right, arrows). Additionally, compared with the WT, the water content in aerial plant parts was higher in the DREB26OX plants and lower in the DREB26SR plants (Figure 3E). Water loss was enhanced in the DREB26SR plants compared with WT plants under dehydration stress (Figure 3F). These results suggest that DREB26 is involved in the regulation of cuticular wax formation during early dehydration stress to prevent water loss from plants.

# Transcriptional regulation of wax biosynthetic genes by DREB26

To analyze the direct transcriptional regulation of wax biosynthetic genes, we analyzed the transcriptional activation of the CER1 and the SHN3 promoters in Arabidopsis protoplasts upon transfection with the 35S promoter driven DREB26, ERF13, or ERF14 cDNA. Luciferase activity driven by the CER1 and the SHN3 promoters was clearly induced by the addition of DREB26, ERF13, and ERF14 cDNAs (Figure 4C). These results demonstrated that DREB26, ERF13, and ERF14 transcriptionally activate the 

CER1 and SHN3 promoters. Sequence analysis of the CER1 and SHN3 promoters revealed the presence of cis-acting elements, namely the CE1 element (CACCG) and the RYrepeat (TGCATG) (Figure 4D). These cis-acting elements may function in the induction of CER1 and SHN3 genes by DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 TFs. 

# Characterization of the triple mutant plants for DREB26/ERD12, ERF13, and ERF14 under drought stress

We mutated each of the three genes (DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14) separately in the WT background using the CRISPR/Cas9 technology and then crossed the resultant mutant plants to generate a triple mutant. The genotype carrying mutations in all three genes was named as the erf12/13/14 triple mutant, and its phenotype was characterized under drought stress conditions (Figure 5A). Leaf size in the erf12/13/14 plants was reduced under drought stress compared to WT plants (Figure 5B). Leaf water content in the erf12/13/14 plants was reduced under drought stress compared with WT plants (Figure 5C). We also analyzed the phenotype of mutants of dreb26, erf13, erf14, erf1314, and erf12/13/14 at flowering stage (Figure S3). The specific phenotype was not observed in flower development and fertility in the dreb26 and the erf12/13/14 mutants (Figure S3A,B). However, growth reduction in leaves was observed in the dreb26 and the erf12/13/14 mutants compared to the WT plants (Figure S3C). To analyze the effect of the mutation of DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 genes on wax biosynthesis, we examined the structure of the leaf surface in erf12/13/14 plants by scanning electron microscopy (Figure 5D). Leaf surface in the erf12/13/14 plants was not as smooth as that in the WT plants. These observations suggest that the mutation of DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 genes causes defects in the regulation of cuticular wax formation.

It is well known that modification of wax biosynthetic gene expression affects stress response in plants (Batsale et al., 2021). Arabidopsis kcs1 mutants showed less resistance to low humidity conditions (Todd et al., 1999). Additionally, transgenic Arabidopsis plants co-overexpressing KCS1 and AKR2A exhibited greater chilling tolerance than those overexpressing AKR2A or KCS1 alone, whereas akr2a kcs1 double mutant plants were more chilling sensitive than WT plants (Chen et al., 2020). In Arabidopsis, kcs5kcs6 double mutation blocked drought-induced wax production, indicating that the reactions catalyzed by KCS5 and KCS6 play a critical role in the wax biosynthesis in response to drought (Huang et al., 2022). Overexpression of KCS1 promoted wax production and drought tolerance in rapeseed and peanut (Todd et al., 1999; Wang et al., 2020). The transgenic Arabidopsis plants ectopically expressing MdKCS2 gene from apple promoted wax production and drought tolerance (Lian et al., 2021). Similarly, transgenic Arabidopsis plants ectopically expressing navel orange (Citrus sinensis) KCS6 (CsKCS6) showed increased survival under drought and salt stress treatments (Guo et al., 2020), while barley (Hordeum vulgare) HvKCS6 mutants showed slightly higher growth rate than the WT under water limiting conditions (Weidenbach et al., 2015). The overexpression of grapevine (Vitis vinifera L.) KCS12 and KCS14 improved wax biosynthesis and drought tolerance in Arabidopsis (Liu et al., 2023). Interestingly, OsCUT1 belonging to the rice 

KCS gene family was down-regulated by abiotic stress, and overexpression of OsCUT1 enhanced the wax accumulation and drought tolerance (Gao, Zhang, Zhang, & Huang, 2022). Arabidopsis CER1-overexpressing plants also showed reduced cuticle permeability, together with reduced soil water deficit susceptibility (Bourdenx et al., 2011). Moreover, several Arabidopsis mutants with defective cuticle biosynthesis (cer1, cer3, and cer6) were male-sterile under low humidity conditions, because their pollen grains would not become hydrated (Wolters-Arts et al., 1998). These studies support that defects in cuticular wax formation severely affect plant growth under drought stress conditions. 

We showed that genes involved in wax biosynthetic processes, such as VLC-FA elongation (KCS2, KCS6, KCS19, CER26L, and PAS2), VLC-alcohol synthesis (WSD1) and VLC-Alk synthesis (CER1, CER1L, and MAH ), were induced under drought stress (Figure 6A–C). We also showed that the expression of two TF genes (SHN2 and SHN3) was increased in response to drought stress (Figure 6D). Some of these genes, such as KCS2, KCS19, CER26L, CER1, CER1L, SHN2, and SHN3, showed a reduction in expression levels in the erf12/13/14 plants compared to the WT plants under drought stress (Figure 6A,B,D). These results demonstrate the upregulation of wax biosynthesis under drought stress and the potential role of the DREB26, ERF13, and ERF14 for regulator of wax biosynthetic pathway in response to drought. The chain lengths of wax components vary from 24 to 38 carbons as VLC-FA precursors originate from various elongase complexes. Several KCS genes (KCS1, KCS2, KCS6, KCS9, KCS10, KCS16, and KCS20) have been shown to be involved in wax synthesis in Arabidopsis and various crops (Haslam & Kunst, 2013; Hegebarth et al., 2017; Lee & Suh, 2015a). Additional genes, including KCS3, KCS5, KCS8, KCS12, and KCS19, showed high expression levels in young stem epidermal cells (Suh et al., 2005). Although these genes are actively involved in cuticular wax production, they have not been functionally characterized. Based on its expression pattern, mutant phenotype, and biochemical characterization, KCS6 appears to be the major KCS involved in the elongation of FAs longer than 26 carbons for the production of cuticular waxes in Arabidopsis (Fiebig et al., 2000; Millar et al., 1999). The characterization of the Arabidopsis CER2-LIKE genes such as CER2, CER26, and CER26L, in addition to their homologs in rice, lotus, maize, and poplar, has revealed that proteins encoded by these genes physically interact with the components of the FAE complex and play a key role in specifically modifying the chain-length specificity of KCS6, enabling the production of VLC-FAs longer than 28 carbons (Alexander et al., 2020; Gonzales-Vigil et al., 2021; Morineau et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016; Xue et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2018). Genes expression patterns and wax levels in the DREB26 transgenic plants (Figure 4A,B) and the erf12/13/14 triple mutant plants in our analyses (Figure 6) suggest that CER1 is key gene responsible for VLC-Alk accumulation under drought stress and DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 regulate the VLC-Alk biosynthetic pathway (Figure 6B,F). Similarly, SHN3, encoding a major AP2/ERF TF involved in cutin and wax biosynthesis, was upregulated in the DREB26OX plants and downregulated in the DREB26SR plants (Figure 4A). Under drought stress, SHN3 expression was lower in the erf12/13/14 mutant plants than in the WT plants (Figure 6D). Moreover, CER1 and SHN3 were induced by DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 in the transient transcriptional activation experiments performed using Arabidopsis protoplasts (Figure 4C). Since the typical AP2/ERF binding sites, CE1 (Niu et al., 2002), were found in CER1 and SHN3 promoters (Figure 4D), the transcription of SHN3 and CER1 genes is likely induced by DREB26/ERF12, which is induced rapidly by dehydration stress. These results suggest that transcriptional regulatory cascades regulate cuticle wax biosynthesis in response to drought stress, and DREB26/ERF12 and two closely related genes, ERF13 and ERF14, play important roles in the regulation of cuticle wax biosynthetic genes (Figure 7). The SHN transcription factor family, to which SHN3 belongs, is a key regulator of both cutin and wax biosynthesis pathways. The transcriptional upregulation of SHN3 by DREB26 is a crucial step in activating cuticle wax biosynthesis (Figure S5). However, it has been suggested that 

SHN1/WIN1 influences both the cutin and wax biosynthesis pathways, with the wax pathway responding later, potentially due to its downstream position in the regulatory process (Kannangara et al., 2007). They proposed that while these two processes are interconnected, they function within distinct regulatory networks. Interestingly, DREB26 specifically contributes to VLC-Alk biosynthesis in response to drought stress, whereas SHN1/WIN1 overexpression plants show enhanced biosynthesis of various wax components, such as VLC-FA, VLC-Ald, VLC-PA, and VLC-Alk. These findings indicate that the DREB26 family primarily contributes to VLC-Alk biosynthesis by activation of CER1, which is a part of the target genes of SHN. Further analysis is needed to clarify the molecular hierarchy within the transcriptional network that governs cuticle formation under drought stress. 

Consistent with the numerous roles of cuticle in the plant response to environmental stresses, the amount of cuticular wax is highly regulated in response to environmental constraints (Batsale et al., 2021; Lewandowska et al., 2020; Shepherd & Wynne Griffiths, 2006). Water deprivation, osmotic stress, and ABA treatment enhance wax accumulation in Arabidopsis through the transcriptional regulation of wax metabolism (Kosma et al., 2009). Phenotypic analysis of the erf12/13/14 triple mutant plants showed that DREB26/ERF12 ERF13 and ERF14 are mainly involved in the transcriptional induction of genes essential for wax biosynthesis. However, the phenotype of the DREB26SR plants was much stronger than that of the erf12/13/14 plants, which may be due to suppression of other related AP2/ERF TFs. In addition to DERB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 of the A5-subfamily AP2/ERF TFs, there are multiple TFs involved in the regulation of wax synthesis under drought conditions. For example, AP2/ERF TFs such as SHN2 and SHN3 (B3 subfamily) and WIND2/RAP2.4 (A6 subfamily) were reported to function in cutin and cuticle wax biosynthesis under drought stress (Aharoni et al., 2004; Broun et al., 2004; Kannangara et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2020). In addition, MYB TFs MYB94 and MYB96 are also reported to be involved in wax formation (Lee et al., 2016). These results indicate that multiple TFs act synergistically on wax synthesis during drought stress, which is thought to increase the drought resistance of above-ground plant parts such as leaves and stems. We performed the time-course expression analysis of TF genes (Figures S4 and S5) and cuticle wax biosynthetic genes (Figure 7; Figure S5) in response to dehydration stress in our experimental system (Urano et al., 2017). DREB26/ERF12 and ERF13 were transiently induced by dehydration stress at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ and downregulated at 48 h. ERF14 and RAP2.4/WIND2 were also induced at $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , but their induction levels were lower than those of DRE-B26/ERF12 and ERF13. MYB96 mRNA started to increase in response to dehydration stress at $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ and kept the 

expression level after $\neg 2 \mathsf { h }$ of dehydration. MYB94 mRNA started to increase in response to dehydration stress at $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ and kept the expression level after $\neg 2 \mathsf { h }$ of dehydration. SHN2, SHN3 and ABF3 were transiently induced after $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ of dehydration, while DREB2A, DREB2B, DREB2C, AREB1, and RAP2.4/WIND2 were strongly induced after $\neg 2 \mathsf { h }$ of dehydration. These induction profiles of TF genes suggest that DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 mainly function in the early phase of cuticular wax biosynthesis in response to dehydration stress (Figure 7; Figures S4 and S5). MYB94, MYB96, SHN2, SHN3, and RAP2.4/WIND2 constitute a transcriptional cascade that ensures the efficient synthesis of wax in Arabidopsis in response to dehydration stress. Similar roles for DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 have been reported in transcriptional regulation of drought stress responses. In Medicago, a DREB26 relative also functions in cuticle wax biosynthesis under drought conditions (Zhang et al., 2005, 2007). In rice, AP2/ERF and MYB TFs were reported to regulate cuticle wax synthesis (Jian et al., 2022). Therefore, cuticle wax synthesis is controlled by complex transcriptional regulatory cascades to respond to drought stress. 

DREB26/ERF12 is the ortholog of rice MULTIFLORET SPIKELET1 (MFS1) and is involved in flower formation (Chandler & Werr, 2020; Ren et al., 2013). Loss of ERF12 function affects reproductive development. During floral development, ERF12 functions as a transcriptional repressor and converges with the AP2 pathway. ERF13 functions in VLC-FA biosynthesis during lateral root development (Lv et al., 2021). These reports suggest that the A5 family of AP2/ERF TFs perform independent functions during plant development including floral development and lateral root formation. ERF14 is involved in the response to fungal infection by regulating the expression of defense genes such as PR genes (Onate-Sanchez et al., 2007; Yu et al., 2022). These reports suggest that A5-subfamily AP2/ERF TFs play multiple roles during organ development, in addition to mediating abiotic stress responses such as the drought stress response. 

In summary, the AP2/ERF TFs including DRE-B26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14 cooperatively participate in cuticular wax formation with other TF genes in the early process of drought stress response (Figure 7; Figure S5). DREB26/ERF12 is rapidly induced by drought stress and constitutes a complex transcription network that regulates cuticular wax biosynthesis under drought stress conditions. 

# EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype Columbia (Col-0; WT) was used for the generation of transgenic lines used in this study. Unless otherwise stated, plants were grown on Murashige and Skoog medium 

(Murashige & Skoog, 1962), supplemented with $3 \%$ sucrose and $0 . 8 \%$ agar (MS-agar), or in soil (Dio Professional for grafting, Innovex Co., Ltd., Tokyo) at $\scriptstyle 2 2 ^ { \circ } 0$ under long-day (16 h light/8 h dark) conditions and $6 0 \pm 1 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ photons $\mathfrak { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathfrak { s e c } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity, as described previously (Urano et al., 2004, 2005, 2022). 

# Plasmid construction and plant transformation

To generate 35Spro::sGFP_DREB26, 35Spro::sGFP_ERF13, and 35Spro::sGFP_ERF14 constructs, first, the sGFP reporter gene was amplified from the pGKX-sGFP vector (Urano et al., 2022) and cloned into the pRI 201-AN plasmid (TAKARA Bio, Kyoto, Japan) downstream of the 35S promoter using NdeI and SalI restriction enzymes. The resultant construct was names as pRI 201-sGFP. Then, coding sequences (CDSs) of DREB26, ERF13, and ERF14 were amplified by PCR using sequence-specific primers and individually cloned between the SalI and SacI sites of the pRI 201- sGFP vector. To generate the 35Spro::DREB26-SRDX and 35Spro:: DREB26 constructs, the DREB26 CDS minus the stop codon was cloned into pGKX-SRDX using SmaI and into pGKX using EcoRV, respectively, as described previously (Urano et al., 2022). To construct the DREB26pro::GUS and ERF13pro::GUS plasmids, the promoter regions of DREB26 and ERF13 were cloned into the pFAST-G04 vector through a destination vector using Gateway Technology (ThermoFisher; https://www.thermofisher.com/jp/ja/ home/life-science/cloning/gateway-cloning/gateway-technology. html), as described previously (Shimada et al., 2010). To construct the ERF14pro::GUS plasmid, the ERF14 promoter region was cloned into the pGKX-GUS plasmid between KpnI and XbaI restriction sites, as described previously (Urano et al., 2022). The CRISPR/Cas9-edited erf12/13/14 mutant was generated using the pKAMA-ITACHI system (Tsutsui & Higashiyama, 2017). The dreb26/erf12, erf13, and erf14 single mutants were generated first and then crossed with each other. Single-guide RNA (sg) design and vector construction were performed according to a previous report (Tsutsui & Higashiyama, 2017). Annealed complementary primers containing the sgRNA-targeting DREB26/ERF12, ERF13, and ERF14, with specified overhangs, were cloned into pKI1.1R using AarI. These constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain C58, which was then used to transform Arabidopsis plants via vacuum infiltration (Bechtold & Pelletier, 1998). Seeds of T2 or T3 plants were used for subsequent experiments. 

# Histochemical GUS staining and GFP detection in plants

Histochemical GUS staining was performed as described previously (Urano et al., 2004). We performed GUS observations of two independent lines of the DREB26pro::GUS and the ERF13pro::GUS plants under dehydration stress twice, and of two independent lines of the ERF14pro::GUS plants under dehydration stress once. The GUS activity was detected using M205C stereomicroscope equipped with DFC490 digital camera (Leica Microsystems). To determine the subcellular localization of DREB26, ERF13, and ERF14, the GFP fusions of these proteins were detected using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Olympus FLUOVIEW FV1000). 

# TB staining

Leaves of WT (control), DREB26OX, and DREB26SRDX plants grown in soil were stained with TB solution, as described previously (Tanaka et al., 2004), with a slight modification. Aerial parts (without inflorescence) of 1-month-old plants were submerged into a solution of $0 . 0 5 \%$ (w/v) TB for 1 min. Plants were then gently transferred to water and softly shaken to remove excess TB. 

Rosette leaves were separated and placed on $0 . 8 \%$ agar plates for taking photographs. 

# Transmission electron microscopy analysis

To observe the cuticle, the eighth rosette leaf of 4-week-old WT (control), DREB26OX, and DREB26SRDX plants was analyzed as described previously (Ukitsu et al., 2007), with a slight modification. Briefly, the top part of each leaf $( 5 \mathsf { m m } )$ was cut into $1 \mathsf { m m } \times 3 \mathsf { m m }$ rectangles, pre-fixed with $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde and $2 \%$ glutaraldehyde in $5 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ sodium cacodylate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 7 . 4 \right)$ , and then post-fixed with $1 \%$ osmium tetroxide. After dehydration with methanol series, the samples were embedded in epoxy resin (Epon812, TAAB). The ultrathin sections were stained with $4 \%$ uranyl acetate and lead citrate and then observed with a transmission electron microscope (JEM-1400; JEOL) at $8 0 \ k \ V$ . 

# Scanning electron microscopy of plant surfaces

Rosette leaves of WT and mutant plants were placed in fixation solution $( 2 \%$ [v/v] glutaraldehyde and $0 . 1 \mathsf { M }$ phosphate buffer) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight. After washing with 0.1 M phosphate buffer, the samples were fixed with $2 \%$ (v/v) osmium tetroxide for 2 h at room temperature. Then the samples were dehydrated in a graded ethanol series, replaced with $100 \%$ t-butyl alcohol, and freeze-dried using ES-2030 (Hitachi, Japan). These samples were coated with $\mathsf { P t } + \mathsf { P d }$ using an E102 ion sputter (Hitachi, Japan) for scanning electron microscopy (S4800; Hitachi, Japan).

# Wax content measurement

To measure the wax levels of 4-week-old WT (control), DRE-B26OX, and DREB26SRDX plants, cuticular wax was extracted from the 8th rosette leaf of four plants from each line. The samples were pooled, and six biological replicates were prepared. To measure the wax levels of WT (control) and erf12/13/14 plants after 9 or 11 days of drought stress treatment, cuticular wax was extracted from the whole rosette leaves of four plants from each line. These samples were also pooled, and six biological replicates were prepared. Total cuticular wax mixtures were extracted by 

immersing the samples in chloroform for 30 sec. Octacosane (C28 alkane), 1-tetracosanol (C24 primary alcohol), and margaric acid (C17 fatty acid) were added as internal standards. The extract was dried under $\mathsf { N } _ { 2 }$ stream, trimethylsilylated (TMS) with $B S T F A + 1 \%$ TMCS (Supelco) at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 h and then cooled. To measure the wax levels in the DREB26OX and the DREB26SRDX plants, $1 \mu \ L$ of the extract was injected into GC-2010/PARVUM2 (Shimadzu) equipped with an Rtx-1MS capillary column $( 3 0 \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ $\times 0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m } _ { i }$ ; Restek). Injection was carried out at $\mathsf { 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ in the splitless mode. Helium was used as carrier gas at $1 . 5 \mathsf { m L } \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . Column temperature was increased from 80 to $\scriptstyle 2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ at $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , then to $\mathfrak { 3 0 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ at $3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ and held for 10 min. The temperature of the interface and ion source was maintained at 300 and $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ respectively. A total ion chromatogram was obtained by MS with $m / z$ ranging from 40 to 600. Wax components were identified by MS spectra and quantified by comparison with standards, that is, octacosane for alkanes, aldehydes, and ketones (no TMS); 1- tetracosanol for primary and secondary alcohols (TMS-ether); and margaric acid for fatty acids (TMS-ester). To measure wax levels in erf12/13/14 plants, wax composition was analyzed by GC (GC2020; Shimadzu Inc., Kyoto, Japan), with the injector in splitless mode and the temperature programmed as follows: starting at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ;$ increasing to $\mathsf { 2 0 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ at a rate of $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; further increasing to $\mathfrak { 3 0 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ at a rate of $3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; and holding at $\mathfrak { 3 0 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 10 min. Mass spectrum data were obtained on a GCMS-QP2020NX mass spectrometer (Shimadzu) after impact ionization. Peaks were quantified using the GC–MS LabSolutions software (Shimadzu). 

# Growth phenotype assay of erf12/13/14 plants under drought stress

Four plants each of erf12/13/14 and WT (control) genotypes planted in a single pot were grown in soil under well-watered conditions and long-day photoperiod at $\scriptstyle 2 2 ^ { \circ } 0$ for 12 days. The soil water content of each pot was adjusted to $2 . 2 5 { \mathfrak { g } }$ water ${ \mathfrak { g } } ^ { - 1 }$ dry soil before the drought stress treatment. Three pots of WT (control) and erf12/13/14 plants were transferred into an empty tray and exposed dehydration stress by withholding watering. After 11 days of incubation, leaf area was measured using ImageJ and LWC was measured as described above. To analyze the expression of wax biosynthetic genes, total RNA was extracted from 12 plants of each genotype grown in three pots of WT (control) and erf12/13/14 under drought stress conditions for 9 days. To measure wax levels, cuticular wax was extracted from six pots of WT (control) and erf12/13/14 plants (four plants/pot) after 9 or 11 days of drought stress treatment. 

# Gene expression analysis by qRT-PCR

Total RNA was isolated from the whole rosette leaves of 3- week-old WT (control), DREB26OX, and DREB26SRDX plants grown in soil. Total RNA from 10 plants of each transgenic line was pooled. Total RNA was also isolated from the whole rosette leaves of WT and erf12/13/14 plants under drought-stress conditions for 9 days. Total RNA from 12 plants of WT and erf12/13/14 was pooled. Extraction buffer (0.2 M Tris–HCl [pH 9.0], 0.4 M LiCl, 25 mM EDTA, and $1 \%$ SDS) was used. Gene expression was analyzed by qRT-PCR as described previously (Urano et al., 2017). The AGI code and gene-specific primers were designed based on the sequence of a single exon using Primer Express 2.0 (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). All qRT-PCR reactions were performed in three technical replicates, and mRNA levels of genes were normalized relative to that of the constitutive control, At2g32170. 

# Transactivation analysis of CER1 and SHN3 promoters

Protoplasts were isolated from the peeled rosette leaves of 3–4- week-old WT Arabidopsis plants using the Tape Arabidopsis Sandwich method (Wu et al., 2009), as described previously (Sakamoto et al., 2020). Using the polyethylene glycol (PEG) transfection method (Sakamoto et al., 2016; Yoshida et al., 2013), two reporter constructs: one containing the firefly (Photinus pyralis) LUC gene under the control of the CER1 or SHN3 promoter, and the other containing the modified Renilla reniformis luciferase (REN ) gene under the control of the CaMV 35S promoter (phRLHSP; internal control) were co-transfected into the isolated WT protoplasts. The transfected protoplasts were incubated at $\scriptstyle 2 2 ^ { \circ } 0$ in the dark. The dual-LUC assay was carried out using the Pikka Gene Dual Assay Kit (Toyo Ink, Inc., Tokyo, Japan). LUC activity was normalized relative to REN activity.
