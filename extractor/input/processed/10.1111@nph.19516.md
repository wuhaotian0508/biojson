# Summary

ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5), a bZIP-type transcription factor, is a master regulator of light-mediated responses. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 binds to the promoter of c. 3000 genes, thereby regulating various physiological and biological processes, including photomorphogenesis, flavonoid biosynthesis, root development, response to abiotic stress and nutrient homeostasis. In recent decades, it has become clear that light signaling plays a crucial role in promoting nutrient uptake and assimilation. Recent studies have revealed the molecular mechanisms underlying such encouraging effects and the crucial function of the transcription factor HY5, whose activity is regulated by many photoreceptors. The discovery that HY5 directly activates the expression of genes involved in nutrient uptake and utilization, including several nitrogen, iron, sulphur, phosphorus and copper uptake and assimilation-related genes, enhances our understanding of how light signaling regulates uptake and utilisation of multiple nutrients in plants. Here, we review recent advances in the role of HY5 in light-dependent nutrient uptake and utilization.

# I. Introduction

Plants for their growth and development require 17 key elements. Some of these key elements, carbon (C), oxygen (O) and hydrogen (H), are obtained from the air or water (Grusak, 2001). By contrast, other important elements are taken up from the soil by roots, respectively. In addition to C, O and H, nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), potassium (K), calcium (Ca), magnesium (Mg) and sulphur (S) are also referred to as 'macronutrients' since they are needed in significant amounts by plants. As opposed to macronutrients, micronutrients that include iron (Fe), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn), manganese (Mn), nickel (Ni), boron (B), molybdenum (Mo) and chlorine (Cl) are needed in small amounts by plants (Grusak, 2001). Since the availability of macronutrients and micronutrients has a significant impact on plant development and crop yields, research into processes governing their regulation of uptake and utilization has been substantial.

Light is a crucial environmental component, which plays a vital role in nutrient absorption by roots and its utilization in aerial plant parts and roots. Light signals are received by various photoreceptors in plants. This activates multiple transcription factors belonging to bZIP, bHLH, MYB, zinc-finger, GATA, GTI, etc., family, which regulate the transcription of a large number of genes by binding on the light-responsive elements (LREs) (Quail, 2002; Jiao et al., 2007). Among these, elongated hypocotyl 5 (HY5), a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor, emerges as a crucial player in regulating light-mediated plant growth and development. The transcriptional activity of HY5 is dependent on its binding cofactors, and B-box containing proteins (BBX20-22) have been identified as one of the crucial partners of HY5 in the regulation of hypocotyl growth and anthocyanin levels (Bursch et al., 2020). The HY5 mutation is known to affect chlorophyll biosynthesis, inhibition of lateral root as well as hypocotyl development as the $by5$ mutant has elongated hypocotyl, a greater number of lateral roots and less chlorophyll content compared with the wild-type (WT) (Oyama et al., 1997). This review aimed to summarize the most intriguing and potentially significant recent studies on HY5 function in light-mediated nutrient signaling. It also aimed to highlight unanswered questions that still need to be resolved in the near future to fully understand the relevance of HY5 in nutrient signaling in plants.

# II. HY5 function is critical in light-dependent nutrient uptake

Nutrient variation triggers transcriptional reprogramming in plants. In Arabidopsis, gene coexpression analysis on 13 nutrient stress conditions identified HY5, PIF4, and NF-Y (which are known to play an important role in light signaling) as regulators for the global transcriptome reprogramming (Brumbarova & Ivanov, 2019). The light signals perceived by different wavelength specific photoreceptors are transduced to HY5, which regulates homeostasis of multiple nutrients (Table 1).

# III. Carbon

Carbon is an essential macronutrient for plant growth and development. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 regulates the carbon assimilation in shoots. Studies have shown that HY5 is involved in regulation of photosynthetic capacity by directly regulating the expression of genes involved in chlorophyll biosynthesis, stomatal development, and photosynthesis (Andronis et al., 2008; Catalá et al., 2011; Toledo-Ortiz et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2016; Burko et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). A study showed that HY5 is involved in coordinating the shoot and root growth in plants in response to light (Chen et al., 2016). ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 acts as a mobile signal, connecting shoot growth and carbon assimilation with root growth and nitrogen uptake, which helps plants to maintain a balance between carbon and nitrogen metabolism under different light conditions. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 regulates the transport of fixed carbon from shoot to sink tissues by directly activating the expression of TREHALOSE-6-PHOSPHATE SYNTHASE 1 (TPS1) and SUCROSE TRANSPORTER (SWEET11 and SWEET12) genes (Chen et al., 2016). TREHALOSE-6-PHOSPHATE SYNTHASE 1 is known to regulate carbohydrate utilization (Blázquez et al., 1998; Schluepmann et al., 2003). SWEET11 and SWEET12 encode sucrose efflux transporters and thereby involved in sucrose transport (Chen et al., 2012). These results suggest that HY5 regulates carbon metabolism by controlling both the initial fixation of carbon and the subsequent transport of fixed carbon.

# IV. Nitrogen

Nitrogen is a crucial macronutrient for plant growth and development. It affects plant growth and productivity as it is connected to regulate photosynthetic and respiratory rate. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 acts downstream to light and regulate nitrogen homeostasis both positively and negatively. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 positively regulates the expression of NITRATE REDUCTASE 2 (NIA2), which is involved in reducing nitrate to nitrite in the cytosol, and NITRITE REDUCTASE 1 (NIR1) which is known to convert nitrite into ammonium (Jonassen et al., 2008, 2009; Huang et al., 2015) (Fig. 1). On the other hand, some of the genes involved in nitrate uptake such as NRT1.1 and AMT1;2 are negatively regulated by HY5 (Jonassen et al., 2008; Huang et al., 2015) (Fig. 1). Under conditions of low nitrogen, the hy5 mutant had shorter primary root as compared to the WT, which suggests that HY5 positively regulates the primary root growth (Huang et al., 2015). It has been shown to positively regulate nitrate uptake by roots through the induction of NRT2.1 expression, whereas in shoots, it positively regulates carbon assimilation and translocation (Chen et al., 2016) (Fig. 1). A recent study in cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) has shown that GhHY5 is induced by light in shoots, and it gets transported to the root by the phloem, where it activates its own expression in the root. The GhHY5 in the root promotes nitrogen uptake of the root by inducing the expression of GhNRT1.1 (Wang et al., 2023). These reports have highlighted the crucial involvement of HY5 in

# V. Sulfur

Sulfur is an essential component of the amino acids cysteine and methionine and various cellular metabolites (Takahashi et al., 2011). The conversion of activated sulfate to sulfite is catalyzed by the enzyme ADENOSINE $5^{\prime}$ -PHOSPHOSULFATE REDUCTASE (APR) (Schmidt, 1972; Suter et al., 2000; Vauclare et al., 2002; Scheerer et al., 2010). The pathway's primary enzyme, APR, regulates the flux and rate of synthesis of reduced sulfur compounds and the formation of sulfate and other S-containing metabolites (Loudet et al., 2007; Grant et al., 2011). ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 plays a crucial role in the light-mediated sulfate assimilation. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 directly regulates and binds to the promoters of two important enzymes, APR1 and APR2, involved in sulfate assimilation (Lee et al., 2011) (Fig. 1). Also, HY5 positively regulates the expression of SULFATE TRANSPORTER 1;2 (SULTR 1;2), which is involved in sulfur

uptake under low nitrogen conditions by directly binding to its promoter (Lee et al., 2011) (Fig. 1). Nitrogen shortage is known to impact sulfate absorption and assimilation in the WT but not in the $hy5$ mutant, suggesting that HY5 is involved in coordinating nitrogen and sulfur assimilation (Lee et al., 2007, 2011). These studies have revealed that HY5 is crucial for coordinating sulfur assimilation in response to light and nitrogen conditions. However, further research on the specific mechanism by which light signals regulate HY5 to control sulfur assimilation needs to be investigated.

# VI. Phosphorus

Phosphorus (P) is an essential macronutrient for forming numerous organic compounds including nucleic acids, ATP, phosphoproteins, and phospholipids. The red light activates the uptake and accumulation of phosphate (Pi) through the phyB-HY5 signaling module in Arabidopsis. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 directly binds to the promoter of PHOSPHATE TRANSPORTER1;1 (PHT1;1) and positively regulates its expression. PHT1;1 is a key Pi transporter involved in Pi acquisition (Sakuraba

et al., 2018) (Fig. 1). The role of OsPhyB-mediated red light signaling in Pi acquisition has also been demonstrated in rice (Oryza sativa; Sakuraba & Yanagisawa, 2020). The activation of S1HY5 in the shoots through the perception of red light by SlphyB is also known to promote arbuscular mycorrhizal symbioses (AMS) development and P uptake under Pi-deficient conditions in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) (Ge et al., 2022). Blue light positively

regulates root growth inhibition under Pi deficiency, which is proposed to be essential for enhancing Pi uptake from the topsoil. The $by5$ as well as $cry1cry2$ double mutant failed to display this root growth inhibition under P-deficient conditions. # VII. Copper

Copper (Cu) is known to be an essential micronutrient for photosynthesis, respiration, and ATP production (Mir et al., 2021). SQUAMOSA PROMOTER BINDING PROTEINLIKE 7 (SPL7) is a Cu-responsive transcription factor. Under Cu-deficient conditions, SPL7 activates several miRNAs to regulate the usage of Cu by target cells and tissues (Yamasaki et al., 2009; Zhang & Li, 2013; Wang et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2015; Araki et al., 2018). A study has demonstrated that the HY5-SPL7 network mediates a crosstalk between light and Cu by regulating MIR408 transcriptionally (Zhang et al., 2014) (Fig. 1). This regulation leads to differential expression of microRNA408 (miR408) and its target genes, ultimately influencing Cu transport to the chloroplast and plastocyanin levels. So, this study has revealed the role of HY5 in the regulation of Cu homeostasis in response to changing light and Cu conditions, but the specific photoreceptors acting upstream of HY5 are not specified. Therefore, further research is necessary to determine the specific light signals involved in the regulation of Cu homeostasis through HY5.

# VIII. Iron

Iron (Fe) is a vital micronutrient for various physiological processes in plants, including respiration, photosynthesis, and chlorophyll biosynthesis. Light plays a crucial role in Fe uptake in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum L.). Red light is perceived by SlphyB, which

leads to SIHY5 protein accumulation in the shoots. SIHY5 is then transported to the roots, where it induces the SIHY5 expression in the roots. SIHY5 in the roots activates the expression of the SIFER transcription factor (Guo et al., 2021) (Fig. 1). The activation of SIFER expression leads to the activation of downstream Fe uptake genes including FERRIC REDUCTASE OXIDASE (SIFRO1), IRON-REGULATED TRANSPORTER (SIIRT1), and NATURAL RESISTANCE-ASSOCIATED MACROPHAGE PROTEINS (SINRAMPI). In apple (Malus baccata), it has been discovered that the MbHY5 positively regulates the expression of MbYSL7 under Fe deficiency, which leads to the alleviation of Fe deficiency caused chlorosis and promotion of Fe transport (Sun et al., 2022). In Arabidopsis, it has been demonstrated that HY5 function is essential for seedling growth under Fe deficiency, as the $by5$ mutant displayed higher reduction in root growth and chlorophyll content as compared to WT under Fe deficiency. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 has been shown to both positively regulate genes involved in Fe uptake (FRO2 and IRT1) and negatively regulate BRUTUS (BTS) and POPEYE (PYE) (Mankotia et al., 2023) (Fig. 1). BRUTUS is involved in negative regulation of Fe uptake genes, and PYE is known to act as a repressor of genes involved in Fe transport, storage, and assimilation (Long et al., 2010; Selote et al., 2015). A recent study has also demonstrated that blue light positively regulates the Fe uptake responses in Arabidopsis (Trofimov et al., 2022). Given that HY5 and blue light both positively regulate Fe deficiency responses in Arabidopsis, it is most likely that HY5 regulates Fe deficiency responses by acting downstream of blue light. Overall, these findings highlight the pivotal role of HY5 in the light-mediated regulation of Fe homeostasis in plants. Additionally, these findings also suggest potential directions for future studies, such as further identifying the photoreceptors involved in the regulation of Fe deficiency response in different plant species, including Arabidopsis, and the underlying molecular mechanism.

# IX. HY5 role in multi-nutrient uptake and utilization is conserved in plants

The roles of HY5 in nutrient uptake and utilization are conserved across species. In Arabidopsis, it has been shown that HY5 migrates

from shoot to root to promote nitrate uptake by activating NRT2.1 (Chen et al., 2016). Similarly, in apple (Malus domestica), MdHY5 positively regulates nitrate assimilation by regulating the expression of MdNIA2 and MdNRT1.1 positively (An et al., 2017). Recently, it has been demonstrated in cotton that GhHY5 gets activated by light in shoots, and it migrates to root to enhance nitrogen uptake by positively regulating the expression of GhNRT1.1 (Wang et al., 2023). Phosphorus acquisition in Arabidopsis is also known to be facilitated by the red light through HY5 (Sakuraba et al., 2018). In tomato, it has been shown that the activation of SIHY5 by red light facilitates P uptake under P-deficient conditions by promoting arbuscular mycorrhizal symbioses (AMS) development (Ge et al., 2022). Furthermore, phyB-HY5 module also induces Fe uptake by activating the expression of SIFERgene in tomato (Guo et al., 2021). Similarly, in Arabidopsis, HY5 has been shown to positively regulate Fe uptake by inducing the expression of genes involved in Fe uptake (Mankotia et al., 2023). In apple, it has been shown that MbHY5 plays a crucial role in regulating Fe transport and chlorophyll synthesis (Sun et al., 2022). These findings collectively imply that HY5 functions are conserved across plant species and are essential for coordinating the nutrient uptake and assimilation in response to light.

# X. Concluding remarks and future perspectives/standing questions

Based on several recent studies, it is evident that HY5 plays a crucial role in regulating the uptake and assimilation of multiple nutrients such as C, N, P, S, Cu, and Fe in response to light signals. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 acts as a central regulator connecting light signaling to nutrient homeostasis. It has been shown to directly regulate the expression of key genes involved in the uptake, transport, and assimilation of these nutrients. Additionally, it helps plants to adapt to nutrient stress conditions by modification of root system architecture. Although the role of HY5 in regulation of multiple nutrients has been well-established, some aspects need further research. The crop species in soils are generally subjected to combined nutrient deficiencies and different light conditions. So, it will be interesting to study the role of HY5 under combined nutrient deficiency stress and investigate whether manipulating the expression pattern of HY5 in specific cell types by using genome editing techniques under such circumstances enhances the growth and productivity. Some of the essential elements such as Fe in the human diet are directly or indirectly obtained from plants. While HY5's role in inducing Fe uptake and transport under Fe deficiency conditions has been somewhat elucidated, its function in Fe storage is still unclear. Further research on its role in regulating Fe transport and storage in edible parts of crop plants would be useful for improving Fe content of crops. Overall, a continued investigation of the molecular mechanisms underlying the light-mediated nutrient acquisition and the regulatory function of HY5 in different plant species is essential for enhancing our understanding of plant nutrient uptake as well as the potential application of these insights for improving crop productivity.
