# Summary

In plants, L-serine (Ser) biosynthesis occurs through various pathways and is highly dependent on the atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentration, especially in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ species, due to the association of the Glycolate Pathway of Ser Biosynthesis (GPSB) with photorespiration. Characterization of a second plant Ser pathway, the Phosphorylated Pathway of Ser Biosynthesis (PPSB), revealed that it is at the crossroads of carbon, nitrogen, and sulphur metabolism. The PPSB comprises three sequential reactions catalysed by 3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PGDH), 3-phosphoSer aminotransferase (PSAT) and 3-phosphoSer phosphatase (PSP). PPSB was overexpressed in plants exhibiting two different modes of photosynthesis: Arabidopsis $\mathrm { C } _ { 3 }$ metabolism), and maize (C4 metabolism), under ambient $( \mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 } )$ and elevated $( { \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 } ) \mathsf { C O } _ { 2 }$ growth conditions. Overexpression in Arabidopsis of the PGDH1 gene alone or PGDH1, PSAT1 and PSP1 in combination increased the Ser levels but also the essential amino acids threonine $( \mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 } )$ , isoleucine, leucine, lysine, phenylalanine, threonine and methionine $( \mathsf { e C O } _ { 2 } )$ compared to the wild-type. These increases translated into higher protein levels. Likewise, starch levels were also increased in the PPSB-overexpressing lines. In maize, PPSB-deficient lines were obtained by targeting PSP1 using Cas9 endonuclease. We concluded that the expression of PPSB in maize male gametophyte is required for viable pollen development. Maize lines overexpressing the AtPGDH1 gene only displayed higher protein levels but not starch at both $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, this translated into a significant rise in the nitrogen/carbon ratio. These results suggest that metabolic engineering of PPSB in crops could enhance nitrogen content, particularly under upcoming ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions where the activity of GPSB is limited. 

# Introduction

L-serine (Ser) is a proteinogenic amino acid that plays a crucial role in the catalytic and regulatory processes of numerous enzymes. Furthermore, Ser is actively involved in the production of various essential biomolecules required for cell proliferation and growth, including but not limited to L-glycine (Gly), and one-carbon (1C) units. In fact, Ser is considered part of the Ser-Gly-1C metabolic network. This network through its participation in the folate and S-AdenosylMethionine cycles drives the methyltransferase reactions governing epigenetic regulation and the supply of almost all methylated metabolites including nucleic acids and proteins in all living organisms (Watanabe et al., 2021). Moreover, 1C-units are also utilized to synthesize purines and pyrimidines (Gorelova et al., 2017; Hanson and Gregory III, 2011; Mohanta et al., 2019; Rebeille et al., 2006). In mammals, the Ser-Gly-1C metabolic network has been considered as a central integrator of nutrient status (Locasale, 2013). In a recent, study it has been demonstrated that this metabolic network orchestrates changes in the nitrogen and sulphur status in plants (Rosa-Tellez et al., 2024) highlighting its importance as a target to reprogram plant metabolism. 

The Phosphorylated Pathway of Ser Biosynthesis (PPSB) is the canonical pathway of de novo Ser biosynthesis in plants, animals and bacteria (Ho and Saito, 2001; Ichihara and Greenberg, 1957). In all these organisms, the PPSB comprises a three-step sequence of reactions catalysed by 3-phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PGDH), 3-phosphoSer aminotransferase (PSAT) and 3-phosphoSer phosphatase (PSP), which convert 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA) into Ser (Figure 1). However, while in mammals PPSB occurs in the cytosol, in plants, it is plastid localized and serves as a crucial junction for the 3-PGA produced during plastidial glycolysis and the Calvin-Benson cycle (Cascales-Minana~ et al., 2013; Toujani et al., 2013). Additionally, in plants, two alternative Ser biosynthesis pathways have been described, namely the glycerate and Glycolate Pathway of Ser Biosynthesis (GPSB), this latter associated with the photorespiratory activity (Ros et al., 2012, 2014). Due to its significance in maintaining cellular homeostasis, Ser metabolism is tightly regulated, although the relative importance of each biosynthetic pathway is still a subject of intense study.

The GPSB starts with the oxygenase activity of RuBisCO which produces one molecule of 3-PGA and one molecule of the potent enzyme inhibitor 2- phosphoglycolate, the precursor of the pathway (Figure 1). Ser-hydroxymethyl transferase (SHMT1) is the Ser-biosynthetic enzyme of the GPSB, driving the biosynthesis of Ser from Gly and 1C-unit provided by 5,10-methylene-tetrahydrofolate (Figure 1). As at least part of the GPSB Ser is recycled by photorespiration (PR) to 3-PGA (Figure 1), the absolute contribution of this pathway to the supply of Ser for plant metabolism and development is a matter of considerable debate (Abadie et al., 2016; Busch et al., 2018; Fu et al., 2023). Besides, since the photorespiratory oxygenase activity of RuBisCO depends on the atmospheric $C O _ { 2 } / O _ { 2 }$ ratio (Ogren, 2003; Ogren and Bowes, 1971), the currently increasing atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentrations could compromise Ser provision through the GPSB in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ metabolisms. In this respect, it is known that PPSB genes are up-regulated when Arabidopsis plants are grown under high $C 0 _ { 2 }$ conditions (Rosa-Tellez et al., 2024) probably to compensate for the reduced Ser supply through the GPSB (Figure 1). It is also known that even under high $C 0 _ { 2 }$ conditions, Arabidopsis biomass increases provided that PPSB remains functional, indicating that the PPSB-derived Ser contributes 

more to plant growth than the GPSB-derived Ser (Rosa-Tellez et al., 2024; Zimmermann et al., 2021). Altogether, these results indicated that PPSB is essential for the plant vegetative growth in the $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plant Arabidopsis, especially under high ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. On the other hand, species with $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ photosynthetic metabolism have evolved a mechanism of pumping ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ into bundle sheath cells to promote the carboxylation reaction of RuBisCO thus reducing its oxygenase activity. This implies a restricted, but not absent, photorespiratory activity in $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ metabolism (Zelitch et al., 2009). Therefore, the contribution of the PPSB to Ser metabolism could be of much greater importance in $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plants due to the low photorespiratory activity inherent in this photosynthetic metabolism. However, the function of PPSB in species with $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ metabolisms has not been investigated yet. 

In humans, PGDH is encoded by a single gene and has been described as the rate-limiting enzyme of the pathway (Reina-Campos et al., 2019). In Arabidopsis, three genes encode for PGDHs namely PGDH1, PGDH2 and PGDH3 (Ros et al., 2014). PGDH1 is the most important gene of the PGDH family in Arabidopsis, probably due to its broader expression pattern, especially in specific cell types such as root meristems (Benstein et al., 2013; Casatejada-Anchel et al., 2021; Cascales-Minana~ et al., 2013). However, it is not known to what extent the first reaction of the PPSB limits or controls the 

flow through the pathway. To clarify the relative contribution of the three PPSB reactions to the pathway, we overexpressed PGDH1, PSAT1 and PSP1 in Arabidopsis and compared the resultant lines to lines overexpressing PGDH1 alone. We also functionally characterized PPSB in Zea mays (maize), a $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plant of high agronomic interest, utilizing both gain- and lossof-function approaches. Our results demonstrate that up-regulation of PPSB in a $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plant raises protein and starch content without penalizing growth, while only protein content is positively enhanced in the $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plant maize. We argue that this is probably due to the different degree of interactions with PR between species. However, this fact notwithstanding overexpression in maize results in an increased nitrogen content demonstrating that it could represent an important breeding tool irrespective of the mode of photosynthesis utilized by the crop. 

# Results

# PPSB overexpression results in elevated amino acid, protein and starch content in Arabidopsis

Three triple overexpressing (Oex) lines of the genes PGDH1, PSAT1 and PSP1 (At TriOex) were selected for comparison with lines overexpressing PGDH1 alone (At OexPGDH1), (Figure S1a). Under ambient ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ $( \mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 } )$ growth conditions, overexpression of the three genes did not compromise shoot growth in two of the studied lines compared to the wild-type (WT) control (Figure S1b). When analysing the relative Ser content in the At OexPGDH1 and the At TriOex lines, an increase was observed in all of them compared to the WT (Figure 2a). When comparing Ser levels between the At TriOex lines and the At OexPGDH1 lines, there were either no significant differences between them or the At TriOex lines displayed lower values, suggesting that PSP1 and PSAT1 did not have an additional effect on PPSB-related Ser biosynthesis under these conditions. In addition to Ser, an increase in the content of other amino acids was observed in most of the Oex lines (Figure 2b and Table S1), especially those that are most abundant in plants (aspartate, glutamate) as well as the main forms of reduced nitrogen transported (glutamine). A general increase in the essential amino acid threonine was also observed (Figure 2b). However, no significant increases were observed in all these metabolites in the At TriOex lines when compared to the At OexPGDH1 lines. 

Since GPSB activity is strongly affected by the $C O _ { 2 } / O _ { 2 }$ ratio in the atmosphere, the Oex lines were also characterized in the presence of 2000 ppms of ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ $( \mathsf { e C O } _ { 2 } )$ . Under these conditions, all lines increased their biomass, on average between $4 1 \%$ and $6 8 \%$ compared to $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 } ,$ , and the majority of the Oex lines were not smaller than WT (Figure S1b). Metabolomic analysis indicated that, under these conditions, the relative Ser content of WT decreased by approximately $50 \%$ compared to ambient levels, and that of Gly decreased by over $80 \%$ (Figure 2a). However, in the At OexPGDH1 lines, Ser levels remained elevated being even above those observed in the WT in $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ . The At TriOex lines displayed similar or higher Ser values to those of the At OexPGDH1 lines. As was the case for Ser, Gly decreased less in the Oex lines than in the WTs under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. In addition to Gly and Ser, in the WT grown under $\mathsf { e C O } _ { 2 } ,$ a reduction in the levels of amino acids such as homoSer, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, phenylalanine, threonine, and 

methionine occurred, with most of the metabolites on this list being essential amino acids derived from aspartate (Figure 2b). However, the levels of the important N-rich transport amino acids asparagine and glutamine increased (Table S1). Soluble sugars such as glucose and fructose were also drastically elevated in the WT under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ compared to $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ (Table S1). In the Oex lines, the decrease observed in the WT for the amino acids homoSer, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, phenylalanine, and methionine under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions was attenuated (Figure 2b). In fact, in the case of the At TriOex lines, a decrease for most of these amino acids including homoSer, isoleucine, leucine, and lysine was not observed. Additionally, the levels of threonine were still higher than those of WT under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions, and those of asparagine and glutamine remained higher than those of WT under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Finally, unlike the levels of amino acids which tended to increase in the Oex compared to WT controls, soluble sugars clearly showed the opposite trend, especially in the At TriOex lines. Thus, there was a drastic decrease in the levels of soluble sugar fructose and glucose in the Oex lines, which in the case of At TriOex lines decreased by between $5 9 \%$ (fructose) and $70 \%$ (glucose) compared to the ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ control (Table S1). 

Due to the observed changes in amino acid and sugar metabolites, we next investigated how these changes affected protein and starch levels in shoots. Under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ growth conditions, there was a clear trend towards increased protein levels by approximately $1 0 { - } 3 0 \%$ in both At OexPGDH1 and At TriOex lines compared to the WT (Figure 2c). This trend was also significant when considering all single or triple Oex lines as a whole (Figure 2c). Additionally, there was a tendency to increase starch levels in some of the Oex lines, although it was only significant in one of them (Figure 2d). 

Under $\mathsf { e C O } _ { 2 } ,$ starch levels increased by $1 7 0 \%$ in the WT, but protein levels decreased by $20 \%$ compared to WT under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions (Figure 2c,d). This is reflected in the drastic reduction in the protein/starch ratio (Figure 2e). This trend of reduced proteins and increased starch content is also observed in the Oex lines. However, starch levels under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ increased significantly more in the Oex lines than in the controls (between $2 5 0 \%$ and $3 1 5 \%$ ), while most lines additionally also maintained higher protein levels (between $20 \%$ and $30 \%$ higher than controls). Indeed, there were even some lines where no reduction in protein content was observed when compared to growth under ambient ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ . Moreover, the increase in both starch and protein levels in the Oex lines resulted in a similar protein/starch ratio to that observed in WT. 

Considering all different single and all triple Oex lines, there were no differences between At OexPGDH1 and At TriOex lines in either $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ or ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ regarding the protein content. However, At Oex PGDH1 lines have a greater effect on starch content than At TriOex lines, with single lines accumulating more starch in both $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions than triple Oex lines.

# PGDH1 overexpression raises nitrogen and protein content in maize

The observation that PGDH1 is the rate-limiting step in the Arabidopsis PPSB led us to study this family in maize. We identified four putative PGDH-like genes (Zm00001d032114, Zm00001d046923, Zm00001d002051, Zm00001d026472) in the maize database (https://www.maizegdb.org/) located on chromosomes 1, 2, 9 and 10, respectively. According to the 

phylogenetic analysis, two of the four putative ZmPGDH proteins identified were more similar to the Arabidopsis PGDH1-PGDH3, (ZmPGDH-X2 and ZmPGDH-X10) while the other two (ZmPGDH-X1 and ZmPGDH-X9) could be more related to the Arabidopsis AtPGDH2 enzyme (Figure 3c). 

In Arabidopsis, two of the three PGDH genes, AtPGDH1 and AtPGDH2 (Casatejada-Anchel et al., 2021), are more expressed in roots than in the shoots, and only AtPGDH3 is mainly expressed in shoots (Casatejada-Anchel et al., 2021). Besides, AtPGDHs are up-regulated in shoots upon growth under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions (AtPGDH1 and AtPGDH2), (Rosa-Tellez et al., 2024) or darkness (AtPGDH1 and AtPGDH3), (Toujani et al., 2013). The three most expressed ZmPGDH genes showed high expression in shoots (ZmPGDH-X2. ZmPGDH-X9 and ZmPGDH-X10), and two of them, ZmPGDH-X9 and ZmPGDH-X10, were even more expressed in shoots than in roots (Figure 3d). In addition, none of these three ZmPGDH genes were up-regulated in shoots when plants were grown under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ or dark conditions. These features, which could be specific to plants displaying $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ metabolism, may suggest the preponderance of PPSB over the GPSB in maize shoots even under light growth conditions. 

To evaluate the effect of PPSB in plants with $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ metabolism, we overexpressed the AtPGDH1 gene in maize under the control of the Oryza sativa Ubiquitin 3 promoter. Three homozygous lines were selected for characterization (Figure 4a,b). Under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions, AtPGDH1 overexpression did not compromise the shoot growth in two of the lines compared to the WT control, with only the line with the highest level of AtPGDH1 expression (Zm OexPGDH1-L2) displaying a $1 7 \%$ decrease in growth (Figure 4c). 

The analysis of starch and protein in leaves when maize plants grew under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions revealed that changes in the Oex leaves were observed in protein levels, which increased by $10 \%$ $1 7 \%$ ( $1 4 \%$ average compared to the controls), while starch levels remained similar to controls (Figure 4d). Collectively, these changes contributed to a significant increase in the protein/starch ratio in the Oex lines leaves compared to controls. The same trend was observed in stems, where protein levels significantly increased in the Oex lines between 16 and $2 6 \%$ $2 2 \%$ average) but starch levels did not, in comparison to WT controls (Figure 4e). Finally, starch and protein content was measured in the kernel (Figure 4f). As in leaves and stems, changes in the starch levels were not observed between Oex lines and control. However, a $9 \%$ increase in the kernel protein content was found in one of the Oex lines which also led to a modified protein/starch ratio in this line.

Under these ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, the overexpression of AtPGDH1 did not compromise growth in two of the Oex lines, and as a whole, the Oex lines were no longer significantly smaller than the controls. When exposed to ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, the protein content in leaves and stems of WT lines tended to decrease, although this trend was not significant (Figure 4d,e). Thus, under these conditions, the differences in protein content between Oex and WT lines persisted or were even intensified, especially in leaves where increases of up to $2 8 \%$ were observed compared to WT under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ (Figure 4d). # Discussion

The atmospheric ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentrations have been increasing dramatically in recent years due to anthropogenic activity. In plants, Ser biosynthesis occurs through various pathways and is highly dependent on the atmospheric $C O _ { 2 } / O _ { 2 }$ ratio, especially in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ species. In this manuscript, we investigated the contribution of the PPSB to $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ metabolisms under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, and also the limiting step controlling the flow through the pathway. Regarding this latter point and taking as a criterion the levels of the product of PPSB activity, there were no higher levels of Ser in At TriOex compared to At OexPGDH1 under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions. Similarly, no significant differences were found in protein levels, and even starch levels were lower in At TriOex than in the single At OexPGDH1 lines. As such, these data collectively support the contention that PGDH is performing the limiting reaction of the pathway in Arabidopsis. Therefore, neither the overexpression of PSAT1 nor PSP1 has a considerable effect on the activity of the PPSB under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions. However, in 

${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ , both Ser levels and some essential amino acids were higher in the At TriOex lines than in the At OexPGDH1 lines, suggesting that under more limiting Ser conditions, triple overexpression might provide a greater supply of Ser and its derivatives, although this may not ultimately result in differences in protein levels. 

In $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions, Arabidopsis plants overexpressing PPSB displayed more Ser and other major amino acids such as aspartate and glutamate than controls, and this resulted in higher protein content. The PGDH reaction uses 3-PGA as a substrate, thus drawing intermediates from the Calvin-Benson cycle and thus diverting them to nitrogen metabolism (Figure 1). Given this, one could anticipate that overexpression of PPSB may have a negative effect on carbon metabolism. However, more starch was also observed in the overexpressing lines, so the ratio of protein/starch compared to controls remained unchanged. There is increasing evidence supporting the consideration of PR as a flexible and open pathway and that a proportion of the Gly-Ser that it generates may exit the cycle and contribute to nitrogen or sulphur metabolism (Abadie and Tcherkez, 2019; Busch et al., 2018; Fu et al., 2023; Rosa-Tellez et al., 2024). It may thus be possible that up-regulation of PPSB allows for greater recycling of the GPSB produced Ser towards carbohydrate synthesis in $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 }$ plants, thereby reducing the outflow of the cycle (Figure 5). This, would thus imply that, in addition to have an impact on nitrogen metabolism, a greater contribution of PPSB to plant metabolism would also enhance a greater recycling of photorespiratory Gly and Ser into carbohydrates (Figure 5). 

As expected, Arabidopsis plants grown under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ considerably increased their biomass, confirming the enhanced growth performance of $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ species under these conditions. However, this increased growth is accompanied by profound changes in their metabolism. Thus, the relative content of Ser in Arabidopsis WT lines grown under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions decreased by approximately $50 \%$ and that of Gly by more than $80 \%$ compared to $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ . Growth under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions was also associated with a reduction in other amino acids such as homoSer, isoleucine, lysine, phenylalanine, threonine, and methionine, which are essential and deficient in many crops, but also with an increase in other amino acids such as glutamine or asparagine, some of the main transport amino acids in the phloem of most species (Fischer et al., 1998).

However, the three maize PGDH genes with the highest expression levels in shoots are not induced by darkness or ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ levels, suggesting a greater relevance of the PPSB in photosynthetic organs of maize than in Arabidopsis, even during the day and under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ conditions (Figure 5). On the other hand, overexpression of the PPSB in maize primarily affected nitrogen metabolism, increasing protein rather than starch levels. This led to increases in the protein/starch ratio in the Oex lines, which were not observed in Arabidopsis. These results suggest a preponderance of PPSB over GPSB in $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plants regarding nitrogen metabolism, and a weaker interaction between PPSB and PR regarding carbon metabolism, probably associated with the lower flux of the photorespiratory pathway in these species (Figure 5).

For technical reasons, we did not characterize the kernels under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions, where we may expect better results. One of the reasons for the reduced effectiveness of PPSB overexpression in kernels may be the low protein/starch ratio in seeds, which is much lower compared to leaves or stems. This suggests a lower activity of nitrogen metabolism in this organ, particularly in the endosperm, where starch biosynthesis predominates. The endosperm, which mainly accumulates starch $( 7 0 \% - 9 0 \% )$ ), constitutes $8 0 \% { - } 9 0 \%$ of the mature maize kernel dry weight (Kowles and Phillips, 1988; Wu et al., 2022). Since the effect of PPSB on species with $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ photosynthesis primarily affects nitrogen metabolism, this could explain the reduced impact of PPSB on the grain due to an ‘endosperm dilution’ effect. According to gene expression databases (https://bar.utoronto.ca/eplant_maize/), PPSB genes such as PSP and PGDHs are expressed at much higher levels during the various stages of embryo development compared to the endosperm. There might be limited or no expression of PPSB genes in the endosperm, especially at late developmental stages, while high levels of PPSB gene expression are maintained in leaves (https://bar.utoronto.ca/eplant_maize/). This raises the question of whether PPSB is fully active during endosperm development. Therefore, to increase protein content 

in the endosperm, it might be necessary to express all three enzymes of the PPSB pathway. Indeed, it is worth considering that the low protein content in the endosperm might be linked to the low or absent activity of PPSB in this organ. 

It is beyond the scope of this work to cover the entire metabolic regulation of kernel development. However, there is a clear maternal effect on corn kernel composition. Therefore, source-sink regulation appears to be a crucial step in determining kernel composition in maize (Seebauer et al., 2010). The higher PPSB activity in leaves and stems may not necessarily result in a proportional increase in amino acid transport through the phloem to kernels. This has not yet been investigated, and thus increasing phloem transport of amino acids to edible plant organs may be a strategy to consider in future studies involving PPSB-overexpressing plants. 

Taken together, up-regulation of the PPSB has a positive effect on nitrogen metabolism in both $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ and $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ species (Figure 5). This opens new insights into the biotechnological application of the finding, as the PPSB is a conserved pathway not only in plants but also in other photosynthetic organisms. Based on our data, we believe we can extrapolate our conclusions to other crops, where not only seeds are used as food but also leaves, stems, fruits or roots. Obviously, several steps remain before the biotechnological 

application of our results can reach the market, from finding natural varieties with higher PPSB activity to applying gene editing to obtain endogenous promoters that enable the pathway overexpression, and finally conducting field trials under ambient and high ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. 

# Conclusion

The overexpression of PPSB in $\mathsf C _ { 3 }$ plants affects both nitrogen and carbon metabolism (Figure 5). In $\mathsf { C } _ { 3 } ,$ there is clear coregulation of the two Ser biosynthetic pathways, so that if PPSB activity is increased the protein content rises, but also excess Ser from GPSB can be recycled to a greater extent into carbohydrates, impacting starch levels. However, in $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 } ,$ overexpression of PPSB primarily affects nitrogen and sulphr metabolism (Figure 5). This indicates that PPSB up-regulation affects to a much lesser extent the recycling of PR Ser towards carbohydrate synthesis, and thus the interaction between PPSB and PR in $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 }$ plants is more restricted to nitrogen and sulphur metabolism. In both cases, PPSB overexpression increases protein levels, especially under ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. These results encourage the use of metabolic engineering of PPSB in crops to improve their nitrogen and sulphr content, particularly under upcoming ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ conditions. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant material, cloning and plant transformation

The original Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) seed stocks (ecotype Columbia-0) were supplied by the European Arabidopsis Stock Center (Scholl et al., 2000). To obtain the At TriOex lines overexpressing the three main PPSB genes (AtPGDH1, At4g34200; AtPSAT1, At4g35630; and AtPSP1, At1g18640), transgenic plants overexpressing the AtPGDH1 gene (Toujani et al., 2013) were used as a starting material. Two constructs were obtained in order to co-overexpress AtPSAT1 or AtPSP1 with AtPGDH1 carrying BASTA and kanamycin as plant selection marker, respectively, as described in Method S1. Double homozygous overexpressing lines for each construct (AtPSAT1 AtPGDH1, AtPSP1 AtPGDH1) obtained by self-fertilization for several generations were subsequently crossed with each other, and then self-fertilized for several generations until obtaining homozygous overexpressing plants for all three transgenes. 

Maize plants overexpressing the AtPGDH1 gene were obtained as described in Method S1. 

To disrupt PPSB activity we used Cas9 endonuclease technology targeting the ZmPSP1 gene as described in Method S1. 

# Growth conditions

Arabidopsis seeds were sterilized and sown on $0 . 8 \%$ agar plates containing one fifth strength Murashige and Skoog medium (1/5 MS) with Gamborg vitamins buffered with $0 . 9 \ 9 \ \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ MES, adjusted to $\mathsf { p H } ~ 5 . 7$ with Tris. For the selection of transgenic plants, one-half-strength MS plates supplemented with $0 . 5 \%$ (w/v) sucrose and appropriate selection markers were used. For analysis, transgenic plants were grown in 1/5 MS plates. After 2–4 days of stratification at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , plates were vertically placed in a growth chambers (IBERCEX H-900) at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a 16-hday/8-h-night photoperiod at $1 2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \ m } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \varsigma } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity and under $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ or ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ $( 2 0 0 0 ~ { \mathsf { p p m } } )$ conditions. Some seeds were also grown under greenhouse conditions in pots filled with a (1:1, v/v) mixture of vermiculite and fertilized peat (Kekkil€a 

50/50; kekkil€a Iberia, S.L.) irrigated with demineralized water or nutrient solution as required. Growth conditions consisted of $1 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ light at $2 0 0 { - } 4 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity, $5 0 \% { - } 7 0 \%$ relative humidity, an average temperature of $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ during the daytime and $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ during the night. For analyses, 14- to 18- day-old shoots from plates were harvested. Unless stated otherwise, the material was sampled at the middle of the light period. 

Maize plants were grown under greenhouse conditions in 0.2 or 35 L pots with a mixture of perlite and fertilized peat (1:3, v/v). Growth conditions consisted of $1 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ light at 400–- $7 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } ~ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity, $5 0 \% { - } 7 0 \%$ relative humidity, an average temperature of $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ during the daytime and $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ during the night and under ambient $\mathsf { a C O } _ { 2 }$ or ${ \mathsf { e C O } } _ { 2 }$ (2000 ppm) conditions. Whenever necessary, both for Arabidopsis and maize, these conditions were supplied with artificial light from sodium and mercury vapour lamps. For maize analyses, 2- to 3-week-old material from pots was harvested and separated into leaves and stems. In some experiments, plants were cultivated until cob production to analyse the kernels. Unless stated otherwise, the material was sampled in the middle of the light period. 

# qRT-PCR

For the gene expression analysis, 15–18 day-old Arabidopsis or 20–30 day-old maize plants were used. Total RNA was extracted using the NucleoSpin RNA II kit (Macherey-Nagel). Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR analysis was performed as described recently (Casatejada-Anchel et al., 2021). Each reaction was performed in triplicate with $1 ~ \mu \iota$ first-strand cDNA in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ . Data are the mean of at least three biological samples. PCR amplification specificity was confirmed with a heat dissociation curve (from 60 to $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C )$ . Reference genes were At1g13320 and At5g55840 for Arabidopsis and Zm00001eb366720 (ZmActin2 gene) for maize. Relative mRNA abundance was calculated using the comparative cycle threshold method according to Pfaffl (2001). When required, the obtained values were corrected by the efficiency of PCR amplification for each pair of primers (Czechowski et al., 2005). Primers used for PCRs are listed in Table S2. 

# Metabolite determination

Shoots of 15-d-old Arabidopsis plants grown on vertical plates were used to determine metabolite content in derivatized methanol extracts by gas chromatography-MS (GC–MS) as described by Lisec et al. (2006). Plants were sampled after 8 h under the light period (middle of the light period). Protein and starch contents were determined as described in Method S1.

# Bioinformatics and statistical analysis

Maize PGDH and PSP1 genes were initially identified in the maize genetics and genomics database (https://www.maizegdb.org/) by aligning sequences with the known Arabidopsis genes using the NCBI BLAST tool. Amino acid sequences were aligned using the ClustalX program (Thompson et al., 1997), version 1.83, and the GENEDOC program. Phylogenetic analyses were performed according to the Maximum Likelihood method and JTT matrixbased model with a discrete Gamma distribution (16 categories). The representation of the calculated consensus tree was drawn using the MEGA X software (Kumar et al., 2018). 

Experimental values represent mean values and standard error; n represents the number of independent samples. Significant differences as compared to WT or control treatment were analysed by Student’s t-tests algorithms (two-tailed) embedded in Microsoft Excel. Statistical differences between groups were analysed with a one-way ANOVA followed by Fisher’s least significant difference test by using IBM SPSS Statistics and R software.
