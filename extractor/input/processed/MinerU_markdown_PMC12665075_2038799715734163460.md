# ABSTRACT

Aquilaria sinensis, a plant of the genus Aquilaria in the family Thymelaeaceae, can form resinous and fragrant agarwood in response to wounding or infection. The resin physically occludes xylem vessels and ray parenchyma, limiting pathogen mobility and resource loss. The quality of agarwood is often evaluated using highly oxygenated 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromones, such as agarotetrol, 2-[2-(4-methoxy)phenylethyl]chromone, $4 ^ { \prime }$ -methoxyagarotetrol and 6,7-dimethoxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. Among the derivatives of 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone (PEC), the majority possess a hydroxyl or methoxy group at the C-6 position of the chromone, with subsequent substitutions occurring at the C-5, C-7 and/or C-8 positions. The hydroxylation at the C-6 position is a critical step in the biosynthesis of these 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromones (PECs). In this study, we investigated the gene expression differences and accumulation patterns of PECs in two-year-old wild A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis, using transcriptomic and LC–MS/MS analysis. Based on these analyses, we identified 52 candidate genes encoding cytochrome P450 (CYPs). Further investigations using a yeast expression system and functional analysis in Nicotiana benthamiana and A. sinensis revealed that AsCYP82G1 could hydroxylate 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone to form 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. Homology modelling and molecular docking demonstrated that the residues methionine (MET) and arginine (ARG) at positions 222 and 223 of the AsCYP82G1 protein are likely to play an important role in the catalysis of 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone to 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. Finally, the reaction mechanism of AsCYP82G1 was studied by quantum mechanical calculation. Our research provides new insights into the molecular mechanism of PECs biosynthesis and lays the foundation for the breeding of high-quality agarwood varieties and large-scale production of PECs using synthetic biology.

# 1 | Introduction

Aquilaria sinensis is a highly valued plant species renowned for producing a resinous and fragrant agarwood used in traditional medicine, incense and the perfume industry (Li et al. 2021). The distinctive scent and therapeutic properties of agarwood are largely attributed to a class of compounds known as 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromones (PECs), which are synthesised through the phenylpropanoid pathway (Das et al. 2023; Li et al. 2014; Wang, Johnston, et  al.  2018; Wang, Yu, et  al. 2018). PECs are the primary active ingredients and characteristic compounds of agarwood, exhibiting a range of pharmacological activities including cytotoxicity, neuroprotection, acetylcholinesterase inhibition, antioxidation, antiinflammation and antibacterial effects (Li et al. 2021). Variations in the quantity and quality of PECs contribute to the differences in the quality of agarwood (Fan et al. 2022). Agarotetrol ((5S)-5,6,7,8- tetrahydro- $. 5 \alpha , 6 \beta , 7 \beta , 8 \alpha$ -tetrahydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)-4H-1-benzopyran-4-one), 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone (PEC) and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -methoxyagarotetrol are the major PECs and the signature constituents responsible for the fragrance and pharmacological properties of agarwood (Hu et al. 2023; Takamatsu and Ito 2018; Yu et al. 2021). Among the oxygenated PECs, the majority possess a hydroxyl or methoxy group at the C-6 position of the chromone, with subsequent modifications occurring at the C-5, C-7 and/or C-8 positions. Therefore, the hydroxylation at the C-6 position is a crucial step in the biosynthesis of these compounds (Li et al. 2021). 

The biosynthesis of PECs involves several enzymatic steps, including the formation of the core skeleton of PEC, the subsequent oxygenated and methylated modifications of PECs (Figure S1). Recent studies have shed light on the occurrence, synthesis and biological activities of PECs. Wang et al. (2017) identified and characterised three type III polyketide synthases (PKSs) involved in PEC biosynthesis in A. sinensis calli, suggesting a complex interaction among various enzyme classes in the biosynthetic pathway of PECs. Furthermore, Wang et al. (2022) discovered a polyketide synthase responsible for producing diarylpentanoids and revealed that the cyclisation of diarylpentanoids with a C6-C5-C6 skeleton can generate PECs. Xiao et al. (2022) also demonstrated that PKSs are capable of catalysing the formation of 1,5-diphenylpentane-1,3-dione, a precursor potentially involved in the formation of the core skeleton PEC. Although there are several reports on the biosynthesis of the core structure of PECs, the enzymes responsible for the further hydroxylation of the core skeleton of PECs remain completely unclear. 

Extensive research has underscored the pivotal role of Cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYPs) in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, highlighting their crucial function in converting precursors into a various array of specialised compounds in plants (Hansen et al. 2021; Mizutani and Sato 2011). Previous research has demonstrated the significance of CYPs in diverse plant metabolic pathways, including the biosynthesis of terpenoids, alkaloids and flavonoids (Liu et al. 2020). CYPs are key catalysts in plant specialised metabolism, driving structural diversification of flavonoids, alkaloids and terpenoids through oxidation reactions. Since the discovery of CYPs, functional studies of CYPs have been conducted in various plants, including model species, crops and medicinal plants (Hansen et  al.  2021). However, CYPs of Aquilaria species that participate in the biosynthesis of PECs are not yet known. Recent 

advances in genomic and bioinformatic studies have provided information about the CYP superfamily in Aquilaria species, which have been implicated in the synthesis of terpenoids and phenylpropanoids, including PECs. Das et al. (2023) conducted a comprehensive genome-wide investigation of the Cytochrome P450 superfamily in Aquilaria agallocha, elucidating potential associations between CYPs and the biosynthesis of PECs. Such research on CYPs in Aquilaria species primarily provides indirect evidence for the important role of CYPs in the biosynthesis of PECs. However, direct evidence is still lacking. Although C-6 hydroxylation is recognised as a critical step in PEC diversification, the CYPs responsible for this modification remain unidentified, hindering the understanding of agarwood resin biosynthesis. 

In this study, we first assembled high-quality transcriptomic data for two A. sinensis varieties after drilling treatments, one is the ‘wild A. sinensis’, which produces a higher quantity of agarotetrol, and the other is ‘Qi-Nan variety of A. sinensis’, which produces a higher quantity of PEC and 2-[2-(4-methoxyphenyl)ethyl] chromone (Hu et al. 2023). Based on these experiments, associations between the response of the genes under drilling treatment in two germplasm varieties and the content of key PECs were established, which led to the identification of CYP genes that might be involved in the biosynthesis of PECs. Subsequently, CYP82G1 enzyme from A. sinensis was characterised by in vitro enzyme activity assays and in vivo transient expression methods, with the aim of providing insights into PECs formation. Finally, homology modelling, key active site analysis and computational analysis of this CYP82 enzyme were performed to lay the foundation for further exploration of its catalytic mechanism. By elucidating the enzymatic activity of this CYP82 enzyme, we aim to gain a deeper understanding of the biosynthetic pathway of PECs in agarwood, providing theoretical and technical support for the directed breeding of superior Aquilaria germplasms and large-scale PECs production using synthetic biology.

# 2.3 | Identification of Candidate Genes Involved in PECs Biosynthesis in A. sinensis

The Weighted Gene Co-expression Network (WGCNA) was constructed based on transcriptome data from the wounded stem regions after drilling, and the content of PECs. A power 

value of 8, which corresponds to the plateau phase of the correlation coefficient, was selected as a parameter for analysis. Additionally, the average connectivity of the genes was examined. As a result, a total of 20 co-expression modules were identified by calculating the Topological Overlap Matrix (TOM) and utilising dynamic pruning methods for co-expression patterns. To investigate the relationship between PECs and the 20 modules, Pearson correlation analysis was performed between the content of eight primary PECs and the module genes. The results revealed a strong positive correlation between the magenta, black, tan and turquoise modules and multiple PEC components (Figure  S13). The chemical diversity of PECs is significantly enhanced through various modification reactions, such as hydroxylation and methylation. The magenta module contains 16 CYP transcript sequences, the black module contains 12 CYP transcript sequences, the turquoise module contains 11 CYP transcript sequences, and the tan module contains 5 CYP transcript sequences. The AsCYPs genes from the aforementioned four modules and the highly expressed AsCYPs genes were combined, ultimately obtaining a total of 52 AsCYPs. While these 52 AsCYPs genes were significantly induced by drilling wound in A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis, they exhibited distinct expression patterns between A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis (Figure S14). These findings suggest that these genes may function as biosynthetic enzymes responsible for the hydroxylation of PEC.

A. sinensis is rich in various hydroxylated PECs. A. sinensis stems were utilised to investigate the role of 

AsCYP82G1 in PECs biosynthesis through the transient over-expression of pBI121-AsCYP82G1. These results suggested that AsCYP82G1 plays a significant role in promoting the accumulation of 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone in vivo in A. sinensis. 

# 2.7 | Mechanism for AsCYP82G1 Catalytic Activity

Analysis of the Ramachandran plot indicated that $9 6 . 9 2 \%$ of the amino acid residues in the AsCYP82G1 protein model are located within the allowed and additionally allowed regions, suggesting that the model adheres to stereochemical rules and is thus a reliable representation of the protein. 

Molecular docking of the modelled AsCYP82G1 protein with PEC was conducted using Autodock, and the results were visualised with PyMOL, as shown in Figure 6. # 3 | Discussion

Agarwood, a precious and highly sought-after forest resource, is well-known for its medicinal properties and distinctive aromatic qualities (Mei et  al.  2013; Yu et  al.  2022). The PECs are the signature constituents of the fragrance and pharmacological properties in agarwood. However, the mechanism of agarwood formation and the PECs biosynthesis pathway downstream of the core skeleton of PECs remain unknown. Here, the gene expression differences and accumulation patterns of PECs in A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis were studied. We identified 52 candidate genes encoding AsCYPs, and functional analysis indicated that AsCYP82G1 can hydroxylate 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone to form 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. Homology modelling and molecular docking revealed that methionine (MET-222) and arginine (ARG-223) at positions 222 and 223 of AsCYP82G1 protein are likely to play a critical role in the catalysis of PEC to 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. These results provide new insights into the molecular mechanism underlying PECs biosynthesis and lay the foundation for the breeding of high-quality agarwood varieties and largescale production of PECs using synthetic biology. 

It has been shown that the accumulation of PECs is associated with abiotic and biotic stresses such as fungal infection, 

wounding, ferrous sulfate stress, methyl jasmonate treatment and insect bites (Dong et al. 2018; Huang et al. 2017; Wu, Sun, et  al.  2024; Wu, Yan, et  al. 2024). The findings presented here highlight the significant role of drilling treatments in enhancing the accumulation of PECs, which is a critical factor that determines the quality and market value of agarwood. This is consistent with the results of several studies (Li et al. 2014; Yang et al. 2016). Previous studies have indicated that the content of PECs generally exhibits an increasing trend following drilling treatment. It is noteworthy that the early stages of PECs accumulation in wild A. sinensis showed a marked increase compared to Qi-Nan A. sinensis, indicating a differential response to mechanical induction among these varieties. This variance could potentially be attributed to inherent genetic differences or distinct physiological mechanisms regulating PECs biosynthesis. There is currently no reported analysis of the variation in agarotetrol content across different germplasms. This compound is a key medicinal component listed in the Pharmacopoeia of the People's Republic of China (Ding et al. 2018; Gao et al. 2021). Such insights have implications for the cultivation practices and processing techniques of agarwood, as they can guide strategies to optimise PECs production based on specific agarwood varieties. 

The content of PECs plays an important role in water solubility and medicinal activity of agarwood. Chemical modifications are of key importance to the biosynthesis of PECs. Hydroxylation and O-methylation are common chemical modifications for plant metabolites and are the basis for the structural and functional diversity of metabolites. Previously, we identified and characterised two O-methyltransferases involved in the biosynthesis of methylated PECs in agarwood, AsOMT11 and AsOMT16, that could methylate 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone to form 6-methoxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone (Wu, Sun, et  al.  2024; Wu, Yan, et al. 2024). Here, we investigated the hydroxylation modification of PECs. Cytochrome P450 enzymes are a class of widespread and important enzymes with diverse functions and biological significance. Members of CYP450 clustered in the same subfamily are more conserved with simpler functions. The orthologous clans CYP51, CYP74, CYP97, CYP711 and CYP710 are considered the most ancient and conserved CYP families (Nelson and Werck-Reichhart 2011). In contrast, the CYP71 clan is one of the largest and most versatile plant cytochrome P450 families. Its subfamilies (e.g., CYP71, CYP82 and CYP89) exhibit diverse substrate specificities. Subfamilies also drive tissue-specific metabolite accumulation, such as CYP71AN24.1 (CYP71A) mediating monoterpene glycoside (e.g., paeoniflorin) biosynthesis in Paeonia lactiflora roots via monoterpene skeleton modification (Xu et  al.  2025). Lineagespecific expansion of CYP71 subfamilies (e.g., CYP71, CYP81 in rice) enables rapid environmental stress adaptation, while their catalytic plasticity facilitates engineered microbial synthesis of plant natural products. The role of some CYP family members has been reported in hydroxylating the C6 position of flavonoids in various plants, such as Glycine max, Scutellaria baicalensis and Ocimum basilicum (Berim and Gang  2013; Latunde-Dada et al. 2001; Zhao et al. 2018). For example, in G. max, CYP71D9 hydroxylates the C6 position of the A ring of licorice to produce $6 { , } 7 { , } 4 { ^ \prime } .$ -trihydroxyflavanone (Latunde-Dada et al. 2001). Previous studies have shown that Cytochrome P450 enzymes play a crucial role in the biosynthesis of agarwood chromone (Das et al. 2023). However, to date, there have been no reports on functional 

studies of any Cytochrome P450 enzymes involved in the hydroxylation of PECs. In this work, transcriptomic analysis revealed that during the early wounding phase, both A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis showed a rapid cascade of responses to drilling treatment. Furthermore, the genes involved in their defence responses exhibited considerable divergence. We analysed and compared the gene expression levels and PECs content in two A. sinensis varieties subjected to drilling treatment, which revealed a strong positive correlation between PECs and four modules: magenta, black, tan and turquoise. Additionally, 52 candidate genes for AsCYPs were identified. Furthermore, based on the gene expression analysis of drilling-treated stems of wild and Qi-Nan A. sinensis, AsCYP82G1 was found to be a good candidate for functional studies. Previous results from Agrobacteriummediated transient expression in tobacco indicated that when using 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone as the substrate, the levels of 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone were significantly higher in leaves treated with AsCYP217 (AsCYP82G1) compared to those treated with AsCYP4 (Figure S25). Therefore, AsCYP217 was selected for further functional validation. Through methods such as yeast and E. coli expression, functional analysis in N. benthamiana, and transient expression in A. sinensis, we successfully showed for the first time that AsCYP82G1 is a hydroxylase involved in the PECs biosynthesis pathway in A. sinensis. This enzyme catalyses the hydroxylation of PEC at the C6 position to produce 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone, demonstrating substantial biotechnological potential. AsCYP82G1 belongs to the CYP82 family, members of which typically synthesise volatile terpenes in Arabidopsis (Lee et  al.  2010; Tholl et al. 2011). Our finding reveals its novel function in chromone hydroxylation, expanding the catalytic scope of this family. In S. baicalensis, SbCYP82D1.1 was reported to function as a flavonoid 6-hydroxylase (Zhao et al. 2018), and further studies will be conducted to compare the difference in substrate specificity or activity between AsCYP82G1 and SbCYP82D1.1. 

To gain a better understanding of its catalytic mechanism, a molecular docking analysis of AsCYP82G1 with its substrate was performed. The analysis revealed that the active center of the AsCYP82G1 protein predominantly contains amino acid residues such as ASP, ILE, THR, GLY, LEU, ARG, PHE, MET and LYS. These residues are consistent with the key amino acids identified in the soybean CYP82D26 enzyme that catalyses the conversion of naringenin to daidzein (Xia et al. 2023). Computational studies revealed that the relatively low energy barriers for AsCYP82G1 to catalyse PEC to form 6-hydroxy-2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone, confirming that the reaction is feasible. In this study, the QM cluster model approach was employed for simulations. However, this method has inherent limitations: it cannot adequately capture protein backbone constraints, long-range electrostatic effects or conformational flexibility, while environmental influences remain poorly represented. To address these constraints, future work will first perform molecular dynamics simulations to obtain stable conformations, followed by QM/MM calculations to investigate reaction pathways. 

In summary, this study functionally characterises AsCYP82G1, confirming its specific hydroxylation of the C-6 position of 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone (PEC) in A. sinensis. This breakthrough provides critical molecular tools for 

engineering agarwood resin biosynthesis. Heterologous expression in microbial hosts could enable fermentative production of 6-hydroxy-PECs—key quality markers of premium agarwood— while transgenic overexpression in Aquilaria or model woody plants may accelerate resin deposition. Mechanistic analysis identified catalytic residues Met222/Arg223, offering targets for engineering AsCYP82G1 variants with enhanced activity toward non-natural substrates to expand applications of engineered chromones. However, translating these advances to industrial-scale in vitro production requires: resolution of the complete PEC pathway, notably identifying the hydroxylases acting at the C-5, C-7 and C-8 positions, which remain unknown; systems-level studies to recapitulate the wound-induced spatial regulation of PEC polymerisation. Future functional characterisation of additional CYPs potentially involved in PEC diversification should employ the integrated gene-to-mechanism strategy established here.

# 4.2 | Plant Material and Treatment

Two-year-old trees of A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis were selected from the greenhouse of the Chinese Academy of Forestry, Beijing, China. Seedlings exhibiting similar growth conditions and robust health were subjected to drill-hole injury treatments. Drill holes were made at 5 cm intervals, starting $2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ above ground level, with a diameter of $0 . 2 \mathrm { c m }$ and a depth of $0 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ . Sampling was performed at 0, 2, 6, 12, 24, 48 and $9 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ postwounding. At each time point, stem segments ${ \mathrm { \approx } } 0 . 5 { \mathrm { c m } }$ length) were excised from branches above the main fork in A. sinensis and Qi-Nan A. sinensis to minimise interference from localised wound responses. Each segment comprised the vascular complex (integrated xylem and phloem tissues) (Figure S26). Three biological replicates were conducted at each time point. Stem segments from different treatments were rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for use. 

# 4.3 | RNA-Seq and Transcription Analysis

# 4.3.1 | RNA Extraction

Total RNA was extracted using the Trizol reagent kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer's protocol. RNA quality was evaluated with an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA) and confirmed by using RNase-free agarose gel electrophoresis. Only high-quality RNA samples, with RNA integrity number $\mathrm { ( R I N ) } { \geq } 7 . 0$ $\geq 7 . 0$ , were used for RNA-Seq library construction. 

# 4.3.2 | Library Construction and Sequencing

A total of 42 RNA libraries (21 from A. sinensis and 21 from Qi-Nan) were constructed and sequenced using Illumina NovaSeq 6000. In brief, after total RNA extraction, mRNA was enriched using Oligo(dT) beads (Epicentre, Madison, WI, USA). Subsequently, the enriched mRNA was fragmented into short fragments using a fragmentation buffer and reverse transcribed into cDNA employing random primers. Second-strand cDNA synthesis was performed utilising DNA polymerase I, RNase H, dNTPs and buffer. The cDNA fragments were then purified using the QiaQuick PCR extraction kit (Qiagen, Venlo, The Netherlands), subjected to end repair, had an A base added, and were ligated to Illumina sequencing adapters. Following ligation, the products were size selected via agarose gel electrophoresis, PCR amplified, and sequenced using the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. 

# 4.3.3 | De Novo Transcriptome Assembly

Filtered reads were utilised for de novo transcriptome assembly using the Trinity software, a short reads assembling program. Initially, Inchworm assembled reads through a greedy k-mer based approach, resulting in a collection of linear contigs. Subsequently, Chrysalis clustered related contigs representing portions of alternatively spliced transcripts or unique segments of paralogous genes, then constructed de Bruijn graphs for each cluster of related contigs. Lastly, Butterfly analysed the paths of reads and read pairings within the context of the corresponding de Bruijn graph, generating a linear sequence for each alternatively spliced isoform and transcripts from paralogous genes. The quality and completeness of the de novo assembly were further assessed through BUSCO analysis, with the unitranscripts containing 1440 BUSCO groups. 

# 4.3.4 | Functional Annotation of the Transcriptome

To annotate the unigenes, we employed the BLASTx program with an E-value threshold of $\mathrm { \Delta } 1 \mathrm { e } ^ { - 5 }$ against the NCBI non-redundant protein (Nr) database, the Swiss-Prot protein database, the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database and the COG/KOG database. Protein functional annotations were then derived based on the top alignment results. Advanced annotation of unigenes includes CDS prediction, SSR prediction, Pfam protein domain prediction, SMART protein domain prediction, Transcription Factor (TF) prediction (plant/animal), R-gene prediction (plant), PHI database annotation (bacteria/fungi) and post-translational modification site prediction. 

# 4.3.5 | Differential Expression Analysis

Differential expression analysis of unigenes was conducted using DESeq2 software for comparisons between different groups, and edgeR for comparisons between two samples. Genes meeting the criteria of a false discovery rate (FDR) below 0.05 and an absolute fold change of $\geq 2$ were deemed differentially expressed. Expression levels of unigenes in stems were normalised and quantified as fragments per kilobase of exon model per million mapped reads (RPKM) 

values during the assembly and clustering process. A false discovery rate $( \mathrm { F D R } ) < 0 . 0 0 1$ and an estimated absolute log2 fold-change (log2 FC) of $\geq 1$ were utilised as thresholds for identifying DEGs. 

# 4.4 | Quantitative Real-Time RT-PCR

Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was employed to assess gene expression levels. GAPDH was selected as the reference gene (Gao et  al.  2012), and the primer sequences are detailed in Table S1. The reaction mixture included $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $2 \times \mathbf { M } 5$ HiPer SYBR Premix EsTaq enzyme, $0 . 6 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of primers (at a concentration of $5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ), $1 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of template (at a concentration of $1 0 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ), and topped up to a total volume of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ with $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ . The PCR programme consisted of an initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 45 cycles of denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s, annealing at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20 s and extension at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s. The stems were sterilised by immersion in $2 \%$ sodium hypochlorite for 8 s, followed by rinsing three times with sterile water. The stems were then cut into uniform segments and set aside. Agrobacterium containing the recombinant plasmid pBI121- AsCYP82G1 or the empty control plasmid pBI121 was inoculated into LB medium supplemented with kanamycin $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ and rifampicin $( 3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ . The culture was incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the bacterial $\mathrm { \Delta O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ reached 0.6. The bacterial cells were collected by centrifugation and resuspended in an equal volume of MS liquid medium. The A. sinensis stem segments were immersed in the infection solution and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on a shaker for $^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ . Following this step, the infected stem segments were removed, washed 3–5 times with sterile water in a laminar flow hood, blotted dry with sterile paper, and placed on MS solid medium for dark incubation for 3 days. After incubation, the stem segments were collected and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The extraction and measurement of PECs were carried out as previously described. 

# 4.9 | Protein Modelling and Molecular Docking of AsCYP82G1

The SWISS-MODEL database was used to identify suitable templates for protein modelling of AsCYP82G1 (Li et al. 2023). Among the identified templates, the one with the highest similarity was selected for simulation. After completing the modelling, the pdb file of the protein's tertiary structure was downloaded to be used as the receptor protein. Homology modelling was performed to generate a 3D model of AsCYP82G1 based on the tertiary structure of A0A061FJG8.1.A using SWISS-MODEL. The conformational accuracy of the AsCYP82G1 model was assessed using Ramachandran plots. Additionally, the 3D model of the substrate 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone was retrieved from the PubChem database and used for further analysis. 

Hydrogenation and charge addition for the AsCYP82G1 protein were performed using AutoDockTools-1.5.7. Similarly, hydrogenation was applied to 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone. The Grid function was utilised to define the Grid box coordinates and dimensions for both AsCYP82G1 and PEC. Molecular docking was conducted using AutoDock Vina to investigate the interaction between AsCYP82G1 and PEC. The docking results were then filtered based on affinity scores and visualised using PYMOL-2.5.7. 

# 4.10 | Computation Study With Quantum Mechanical Calculation

Hybrid cluster-continuum (HCC) model calculations (Wang and Cao 2010) were performed using the Gaussian 16 software package, Revision C.01 (Frisch et  al.  2016). The QM cluster model was employed to calculate the hydroxylation reaction at the C6 position of 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromone catalysed by AsCYP82G1 Compound I. The structure optimisation and frequency calculation were conducted using the B3LYP functional and Def2SVP basis set, with dispersion correction applying Grimme's D3 method (Grimme 2004). The transition state optimisation algorithm utilised the Berny algorithm, and the transition state had exactly one imaginary frequency. The solvent model was the SMD continuous solvation model. During the epoxidation process of the substrate catalysed by Cpd I, Cpd I was truncated, retaining the Fe porphyrin ring and the sulfur atoms coordinated with Fe. The truncated covalent bonds were saturated with hydrogen atoms, and the overall cluster model was electrically neutral, with a total of 73 atoms. Subsequently, the ring opening of the substrate and the hydroxylation at the C6 position were carried out. In the HCC calculation (Wang and Cao 2010; Wang, Johnston, et al. 2018; Wang, Yu, et al. 2018), there were two distinct water molecules in the aqueous solution (no enzyme), with a total of 40 atoms. All calculations were completed in the singlet state. 

# 4.11 | Statistical Analysis

Statistical analysis was conducted using SPSS Statistics 19 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) for differential expression analysis. Significant differences between means were identified using the LSD test at a significance level of $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ .
