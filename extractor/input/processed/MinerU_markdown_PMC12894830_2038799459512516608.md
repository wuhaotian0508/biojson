# Pathogen promotes insect ovarian development by inducing a dual role of host ATPSyn-β

Q Check for updates 

Chenyang Yuan 1,2, Tianyuan Liu1,2, Jiayao Fan1,2, Ning Wang1,2, Yingzhe Yuan3 , Tao Peng3 , Yuxi Zhou1,2, Long Yi4 , Wei Dou 1,2 & Jinjun Wang 1,2 

Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus (CLas), a pathogenic bacterium causing citrus Huanglongbing, forms a mutualistic relationship with the Asian citrus psyllid, Diaphorina citri. However, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain unclear. We find that CLas enhances the development of testes, ovaries, and mating behavior in D. citri. Transcriptome analysis of ovaries reveals that CLas infection upregulates ATP synthesis genes, increasing ATP levels in D. citri. Silencing the ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ gene expression reduces ATP content in the ovary and triglyceride and glycogen content in the fat body, thus decreasing ovarian development, mating, and oviposition. ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ localizes to the oocyte cell membrane interacts with vitellogenin (Vg), suggesting that it facilitates Vg entry into ovaries. In conclusion, CLas promotes ovarian development in D. citri by upregulating ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ , which supports energy production and nutrient supply. This study provides valuable insights into CLas and may inform novel strategies to control citrus Huanglongbing by reducing D. citri populations. 

Microbial associations are widespread across the insects. Through longterm coevolution, symbiotic bacteria have formed mutualistic relationships with host insects. For example, female olive fruit flies, Bactrocera oleae, with symbiotic bacteria showed more oviposition attempts than axenic flies that received gut suspension from symbiotic flies or a symbiotic bacterium1 . The gut symbiont Burkholderia boosts juvenile hormone III skipped bisepoxide levels in the bean bug Riptortus pedestris, resulting in higher vitellogenin (Vg) protein levels and egg production2 . The Wolbachia-infected planthoppers Laodelphax striatellus and Nilaparvata lugens exhibit higher biotin and riboflavin levels and lay more eggs than their uninfected counterparts3 . The obligate symbiont of viviparous tsetse flies, Wigglesworthia glossinidia, can synthesize vitamin B6, and removing it from hosts with antibiotics decreases vitamin B6 levels and fecundity in lactating females4 . The intracellular symbionts Hamiltonella and Arsenophonus influence sex ratios of their host Bemisia tabaci by regulating fertilization and providing B vitamins5 . The gut symbionts Acinetobacter soli, Acinetobacter ursingii, Moraxella osloensis, and Empedobacter brevis promote lipid metabolism and the development of ovaries and testes in the ladybug Henosepilachna vigintioctopunctata6 . 

Pathogenic microbes, such as viruses, fungi, and bacteria, have complex relationships with vector insects that can manifest as facultative 

mutualism, parasitism, or commensalism7,8 . The tomato yellow leaf curl China virus accelerates ovarian development and increases fecundity in the Middle East-Asia Minor 1 whitefly B. tabaci9,10. Male Frankliniella occidentalis infected with tomato spotted wilt virus fed more than uninfected males11. The virus can be transmitted paternally through male sperm with minimal impact on the fitness of adult males or their offspring12. The tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV) induces apoptotic neurodegeneration in whitefly brains, which abolishes the steady preference of the whitefly for virus-infected plants. This enhances the probability of the virus to enter uninfected hosts, ultimately promoting its spread13. In whiteflies, the TYLCV also impairs the Janus kinase/signal transducer and activator of transcription pathway, thereby decreasing survival and reproductive capacity14. The pathogenic fungus Beauveria bassiana manipulates the host RNA-interference system in mosquitoes to suppress host immunity15. The presence of the pathogenic bacterium Candidatus Liberibacter solanacearum (CLso) inhibits the reproduction of potato psyllids Bactericera cockerelli16.

Upon acquisition of CLas from its citrus hosts Citrus reticulata or Citrus sunki, the preoviposition period of D. citri decreases, whereas the oviposition period increases. This leads to a significant increase in fecundity and population trend index of D. citri compared to those of D. citri on CLas-free plants20. The proportion of ${ \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + } D$ . citri mature oocytes is higher than that of CLas- D. citri, indicating that CLas facilitates ovarian development in D. citri21. The maturation of ovaries in D. citri is directly linked to mating behavior; however, it is currently unknown whether CLas enhances this behavior. 

Here, we found that CLas infection enhances the development of both testes and ovaries and promotes mating behavior in D. citri. Based on the mating results, $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ females made a greater contribution to egg production. To uncover the molecular mechanisms underlying the enhanced reproductive potential of $D .$ . citri induced by CLas, comparative transcriptomics of $\mathrm { C L a s ^ { + } } / \mathrm { C L a s ^ { - } } D .$ . citri ovaries were first conducted. In combination with RNAi, Western blot, yeast two-hybrid, and GST pull-down, we finally clarified that CLas increased the content of ATP and $\mathrm { V g }$ in ovaries and the content of glycogen and triglyceride in fat bodies by up-regulating the ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ gene expression. These findings suggest that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ plays a dual role in promoting D. citri ovarian development upon CLas infection. The study introduces a novel concept on how pathogens promote vector insect development, and provides valuable insights for potential targets to block Huanglongbing transmission through the reproductive strategy.

# CLas enhances the mating behavior and oviposition of D. citri

To examine whether CLas influences the mating behavior of D. citri, we used a camera to record daily mating behaviors (Fig. 2A, Supplementary Movie 1) of four combinations: $\mathrm { C L a s } ^ { + } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } \times \mathrm { C L a s } ^ { + } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } .$ CLas+_♀ × CLas- _♂, CLas- _♀ × $\mathrm { C L a s } ^ { + } \_ \hat { \cal { d } }$ , and $\mathrm { C L a s } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } \times \mathrm { C L a s } \_ { \mathscr { Q } }$ . The following mating behaviors were evaluated: the daily mating rate, cumulative mating frequency, average mating duration, number of eggs laid, and hatching rate of eggs. 

First, CLas significantly increased the daily mating rate, as the maximum daily mating rates of the four before mentioned combinations were $8 0 \%$ , $6 0 \%$ , $4 0 \%$ , and $5 0 \%$ , respectively (Fig. 2B). Second, CLas significantly increased the mating frequency, as the cumulative mating frequencies of the four combinations were 10.80, 7.10, 8.00, and 7.35 times, respectively (Fig. 2D). Third, CLas significantly prolonged the mating durations, with mean values of 48.45, 37.25, 43.05, and 34.75 min for the four combinations, respectively (Fig. 2E). Fourth, CLas significantly enhanced egg production, as the maximum number of eggs laid in 3 consecutive days were 98.55, 63.30, 50.35, and 43.05 for the four combinations, respectively (Fig. 2C). Moreover, CLas significantly increased the total number of eggs laid in 39 days, with mean values of 511, 426, 352, and 324 for the four combinations, respectively (Fig. 2F). However, CLas had no effect on the hatching rate of eggs, with values of $7 8 . 7 \%$ , $7 9 . 6 \%$ , $7 3 . 0 \% $ , and $7 5 . 4 \%$ for the four combinations, respectively (Supplementary Fig. 2). Altogether, these results demonstrate that CLas has positive effects on the mating behavior and oviposition of D. citri. 

# CLas increases ATP levels in ovaries of D. citri

Based on the mating results, $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ females contributed more significantly to egg production. Consequently, this study focused on females. To 

understand the molecular mechanisms underlying the CLas-induced promotion of ovarian development in D. citri, we dissected and sequenced six distinct groups of virgin D. citri ovaries (CLas+_2d, CLas+_4d, CLas+_6d, CLas- _2d, CLas- _4d, and CLas- _6d). The CLas+_2d/CLas- _2d group showed 81 differentially expressed genes, of which 56 were up-regulated and 25 were down-regulated. The CLas+_4d/CLas- _4d group showed 2290 differentially expressed genes, of which 1808 were up-regulated and 482 were downregulated. The CLas+_6d/CLas- _6d group showed 2175 differentially expressed genes, of which 2032 were up-regulated and 143 were downregulated (Fig. 3A). Analysis of the intersection of the CLas+_4d/CLas- _4d and CLas+_6d/CLas- _6d groups revealed 837 differentially expressed genes (Supplementary Data 2), including Vg and VgR genes that are closely related to reproduction. These genes were then followed by a Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis (Fig. 3B, C). Numerous genes related to ATP synthesis pathways, including the glycolytic pathway (hexokinase, pyruvate kinase, aldose 1-epimerase, and phosphoglucomutase), tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle pathway (citrate synthase, pyruvate carboxylase, and isocitrate dehydrogenase), and oxidative phosphorylation pathway (ATP synthase subunit alpha/beta/gamma/epsilon/b), displayed differential expression after D. citri acquired CLas. Real-time quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) confirmed that the expression levels of these genes significantly differed between ovaries of $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ and CLas- D. citri (Fig. 4A).

# Knockdown of ATPSyn-α and ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ impairs ovarian development, mating behavior, and oviposition

To examine the roles of ATP in ovarian development and mating behavior of D. citri, we fed double-stranded RNA via the membrane feeding method to knockdown the expression of the most highly differentially expressed ATP synthase subunits alpha (ATPSyn-α) and beta (ATPSyn-β). Continuously feeding dsATPSyn- $_ { \cdot \alpha }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ to day 3 virgin $D .$ . citri postemergence resulted in an ATPSyn- $_ { \alpha }$ gene silencing efficiency of $3 7 . 0 \%$ (Student’s t-test, $P < 0 . 0 5$ ), whereas it did not affect ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ expression. Similarly, feeding dsATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ resulted in an ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ gene silencing efficiency of $5 1 . 4 \%$ (Student’s t-test, $P { < } 0 . 0 1 $ ), whereas it did not affect ATPSyn-α expression. Simultaneously feeding dsATPSyn-α and dsATPSyn-$\beta$ $\ l ^ { 3 } \left( \mathrm { d } s A T P S y n - \alpha + \beta \right)$ resulted in an ATPSyn-α gene silencing efficiency of $3 9 . 2 \%$ , and an ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ gene silencing efficiency of $5 3 . 3 \%$ (Student’s t-test, $P < 0 . 0 5$ and $P < 0 . 0 1$ ) (Fig. 5A, B). The changes in protein content were similar to those of mRNA levels. After treatment with dsATPSyn- $_ { \alpha }$ and dsATPSyn- $\alpha + \beta$ , the levels of ATPSyn-α were reduced by $2 4 . 2 \%$ and $2 2 . 1 \%$ (Student’s t-test, $P { < } 0 . 0 0 1$ and $P < 0 . 0 1$ ), respectively. # ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ is located on the oocyte cell membrane

To determine whether ATPSyn- $\beta$ is located on the oocyte cell membrane, microsomal proteins were extracted from the ovaries and probed against the anti-ATPSyn- $\beta$ antibody by Western blot. The commercial anti-ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ antibody recognized a $5 5 \mathrm { k D a }$ band compatible with ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ in microsomal protein, strongly suggesting its localization in the cell membrane. These results show that ATPSyn- $\beta$ is localized on the 

oocyte cell membrane in D. citri, suggesting that it may function as a transport protein.

# Discussion

Previous studies have reported that CLas increases egg production and promotes ovarian development in D. citri18–21. In this study, we demonstrate 

that CLas induces ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ expression in D. citri, promoting ovarian development through two mechanisms. On the one hand, it boosts ATP content within ovaries and triglyceride and glycogen content within fat bodies. On the other hand, it facilitates the interaction between ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ and $\mathrm { V g } ,$ enhancing $\mathrm { V g }$ uptake by oocytes. As such, CLas endows ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ with a dual role in supplying energy and nutrients to promote the ovarian development of D. citri (Fig. 8).

A recent study found that CLas promotes oviposition by activating the juvenile hormone (JH) signaling pathway, inhibiting miR-275 expression, and enhancing VgR expression in D. citri21. We also identified differentially expressed genes in the JH signaling pathway by analyzing transcriptome data from $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ and CLas- D. citri ovaries. However, these genes do not determine CLas-induced ovarian development in D. citri. Interestingly, our transcriptome analysis revealed significant differences in gene expression related to ATP synthesis during glycolysis, the TCA cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation. From this perspective, our research investigated the effect 

of CLas on D. citri ovarian development by focusing on energy metabolism. Previous studies suggest that CLas increases the levels of ATP and key nucleotides in D. citri, with ATP levels showing a positive correlation with CLas titers22. Additionally, CLas increases ATPSyn- $_ { \cdot \alpha }$ and ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ levels and decreases ATPase levels and AMP/ATP and ADP/ATP ratios. Moreover, CLas affects glycolysis and the TCA cycle in D. citri23. 

Numerous studies have indicated that ATPSyn- $_ { \alpha }$ and ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ affect the development of insect ovaries. Silencing ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ in the leafhoppers Euscelidius variegatus and Scaphoideus titanus caused a depletion of mature eggs in ovaries and almost complete female sterility25,26. Similar to these observations, we observed that ATPSyn-α and ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ knockdown suppressed ovarian development in $D _ { * }$ . citri. Due to the dominant inhibition phenotype of interference with the latter gene, ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ was focused on for the subsequent studies. When the ATPSyn- $_ { \cdot \alpha }$ or ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ gene was 

individually silenced, the mating duration of D. citri did not change significantly. However, when both genes were silenced simultaneously, the mating duration decreased significantly (Fig. 5H, K, N). Therefore, we hypothesize that CLas prolongs the mating duration of D. citri by affecting the expression of multiple genes, potentially also including other genes related to energy metabolism. Interestingly, we discovered that high dsATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ concentrations can kill D. citri, similar to the effects observed in leafhoppers27. The genome of CLas includes ATP/ADP transporters, enabling it to absorb ATP. Hence, by increasing ATP levels in D. citri, CLas not only supports the growth and development of D. citri but also meets its own energy requirements2 9. As such, a facultative mutualistic relationship is formed. 

In the last decade, numerous studies have reported that ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ functions as a transport protein on the cell membrane in Panstrongylus megistus, which interacts with lipophorin to transport lipids to the midguts, fat bodies, and ovaries30–32. Knockdown of RpATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ in Rhodnius prolixus elevated triacylglycerol levels in the fat body and decreased lipid transfer to ovaries, consequently impairing ovarian development and oviposition33. Using 2D gel electrophoresis and liquid chromatographytandem mass spectrometry, Lu and Killiny (2017) analyzed mitochondrial, microsomal, and cytoplasmic proteins in $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ and CLas- D. citri and identified ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ within the microsomal proteins. Consistent with their finding, we also found that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ was located on the ovarian cell membrane, as revealed by western blot and immunofluorescence analysis. These results suggest that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ may also function as a transporter in D. citri. 

To identify proteins that interact with ATPSyn- $\cdot \{ \beta \} .$ , we conducted comprehensive analyses with CO-IP, LC-MS/MS, Y2H, and GST pull-down assays, which revealed an interaction between ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ and $\mathrm { V g } .$ Additionally, immunofluorescence analysis demonstrated the co-localization of $\mathrm { V g }$ and ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ on and within oocytes. Moreover, we found that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ knockdown decreased both $\mathrm { V g }$ gene and protein levels in oocytes. These results suggest that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ plays a role in the endocytosis of Vg by oocytes. In contrast, our examination of the interaction between $\mathrm { V g }$ and ATPSyn-α found no significant interaction, indicating that ATPSyn-α does not contribute to Vg endocytosis in oocytes. Specifically, Vg can participate in the vertical transmission of the TYLCV into whitefly ovaries34, and endocytosis is involved in the transfer process of viruses in insects35. Earlier work showed that CLas can be transmitted vertically to the next generation36. Our study has revealed that ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ can also interact with the myosin heavy chain and actin, which are involved in endocytosis. Ultimately, more research is needed to determine whether Vg participates in the vertical transmission of CLas and whether ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ participates in the transport of CLas in D. citri. 

In conclusion, this study demonstrates that CLas promotes ovarian development and mating in D. citri by enhancing energy and nutrient supply. On the one hand, increased ATPSyn- $\cdot \beta$ expression increases ATP levels in the ovaries and triglyceride and glycogen content within fat bodies, thereby supplying more energy for ovarian development. On the other hand, CLas facilitates the interaction between ATPSyn- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ and $\mathrm { V g }$ and enables more $\mathrm { V g }$ to enter the ovaries, thereby supplying more nutrients for ovarian development. Our study provides valuable insights into CLas that can be utilized for novel approaches to controlling citrus Huanglongbing by decreasing the D. citri population. 

# Materials and methods Insect

CLas- D. citri was originally collected from a wild citrus grove in Ganzhou, Jiangxi Province, China, and was maintained in an insect cage $( 6 0 \times$ $6 0 \times 9 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ at the National Navel Orange Engineering Research Center of Gannan Normal University for multiple generations. The insects were raised on potted Newhall navel orange seedlings under controlled conditions of $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , $7 0 { - } 7 5 \%$ relative humidity, and a $1 4 { : } 1 0 \mathrm { h }$ (light: dark) photoperiod. The $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ adults were obtained by placing healthy newly molted fifth-instar CLas- D. citri nymphs onto CLas-infected Newhall navel 

oranges until emergence24. $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { Z L a s } } ^ { + }$ and CLas- adults were separated and raised individually within 1 day of emergence. Virgin males and females aged 2-6 days were used in the experiments. RT-qPCR analysis was conducted to determine the CLas titer and infection rate in D. citri following the acquisition period21. A cycle threshold (Ct) value of 34.0 was established as the cutoff to distinguish between $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { { C L a s } ^ { + } } }$ and CLas- individuals37,38. Ten samples were considered a biological replicate, and four biological replicates were set for each age group. 

# Morphological determination

The impact of CLas on D. citri development was investigated by measuring body length, abdominal length and width of virgin D. citri (CLas+ and CLas- ) on days 2, 4, and 6 post-emergence using the SZ810 Stereo microscope (Chongqing Optec Instrument Co., Ltd, Chongqing, China) according to a very recent study39. Furthermore, the development stage of the testes and ovaries was observed and recorded after dissection. Each group consisted of 30 individuals. 

# Insect crosses

Virgin CLas+ and CLas- D. citri individuals were collected within 6 days post-emergence. One female and one male were transferred to a glass tube containing citrus shoots (4 cm in height and $2 \mathrm { c m }$ in diameter) in the following combinations: $\begin{array} { r } { { \cal C } \mathrm { L } a s ^ { + } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } \times { \cal C } \mathrm { L } a s ^ { + } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } , { \cal C } \mathrm { L } a s ^ { + } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } \times { \cal C } \mathrm { L } a s ^ { - } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } , { \cal C } \mathrm { L } a s ^ { - } \_ { \mathscr { Q } } \times } \end{array}$ $\mathrm { C L a s } ^ { + } \_ \delta$ , and CLas- $\_ { \mathcal { Q } \times \mathrm { C L a s } _ { - \mathcal { Q } } }$ , according to a previous study16. # Transcriptome sequencing of CLas+ and CLas- D. citri ovaries

CLas+ and CLas- D. citri females were collected on days 2, 4, and 6 postemergence, and their ovaries were dissected for sequencing. For each experimental group $\left[ \mathbf { C l a s } ^ { + } \mathbf { \Lambda } _ { - } \mathbf { 2 } \right.$ , $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + } \_ { 4 }$ , $\mathrm { C L a s } ^ { + } \_ { 6 }$ , CLas- _2, CLas- _4, and CLas- _6), four biological replicates were obtained. Each biological replicate consisted of 100 ovaries that were standardized in size. Subsequently, the samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Total RNA was extracted from the samples using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) following the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA concentration and purity were subsequently assessed with NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Wilmington, DE, USA). A cDNA library was constructed by enriching mRNA from total RNA, followed by fragmentation, reverse transcription into double-stranded cDNA, adapter ligation, and PCR amplification. The prepared libraries were then sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) to generate 150 bp paired-end reads. The quality of the raw data was assessed using FastQC (http://www.bioinformatics.babraham.ac. uk/projects/fastqc/), and the resulting high-quality reads were aligned to the D. citri genome assembly version 3.0 (https://citrusgreening.org). Gene expression was quantified in Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads (FPKM). The R package DESeq2 was used to identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs). $P$ -values were adjusted for multiple comparisons using the False Discovery Rate (FDR) method; transcripts with a $P$ value $\le 0 . 0 5$ and an absolute log (fold change) $\geq 2$ were considered significantly differentially expressed. Finally, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was performed to identify the biological pathways associated with these DEGs24,39.

# Determination of ATP content

The ATP content in $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { C L a s } } ^ { + }$ and CLas- D. citri was extracted and quantified using the ATP Content Assay Kit (Cat. No. BC0305; Solarbio, Beijing, China)41,42.
