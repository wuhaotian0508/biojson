# ABSTRACT

Plant benzoic acids (BAs) are building blocks or important structural elements for numerous primary and specialized metabolites, including plant hormones, cofactors, defense compounds, and attractants for pollinators and seed dispersers. Many natural products derived from plant BAs or containing benzoyl/benzyl moieties are also of medicinal or nutritional value to humans. Biosynthesis of BAs in plants is a network involving parallel and intersecting pathways spread across multiple subcellular compartments. In this review, a current overview on the metabolism of plant BAs is presented with a focus on the recent progress made on isolation and functional characterization of genes encoding biosynthetic enzymes and intracellular transporters. In addition, approaches for deciphering the complex interactions between pathways of the BAs network are discussed.

Key words: benzoic acids, biosynthesis, metabolic network, salicylic acid, volatiles, phenylpropanoids

# INTRODUCTION

Benzoic acids (BAs; see Table 1 for abbreviations) (Figure 1) are $\complement _ { 6 } – C _ { 1 }$ aromatic carboxylic acids that serve as precursors for a wide variety of essential compounds and natural products playing crucial roles in plant fitness. By definition, plant BAs encompass BA and all its $\mathsf { C } _ { 6 } – \mathsf { C } _ { 1 }$ hydroxy and amino substituents, including 4-hydroxy-BA (4HBA), 2-amino-BA (anthranilic acid), and $p$ -amino-BA (pABA) (Figure 1), just to name a few. Perhaps the most studied plant BA is the plant hormone salicylic acid (SA, 2-hydroxy-BA) (Figure 1), considered to be one of the key endogenous signals involved in defense response activation. SA is also an important signaling molecule for eliciting responses to several abiotic stresses (e.g. cold, drought, ultraviolet light, and ozone), and participates in developmental signaling cascades controlling processes such as flowering and senescence (see Vlot et al., 2009 for a more comprehensive review on SA function). Another class of plant hormones derived from BAs is the aromatic cytokinins, comprised of compounds such as meta-topolin (Figure 1), a benzyl–purine conjugate demonstrated to promote root and shoot formation and retard leaf senescence in many species (Werbrouck et al., 1996; Strnad, 1997; Tarkowska´ et al., 2003; Mutui et al., 2012). Other primary metabolites derived from plant BAs include the respiratory cofactor ubiquinone, which partially relies on 4HBA for its ring (Block et al., 2014; Tohge et al., 2014), and the folic acids (vitamin $\mathsf { B } _ { 9 } )$ , vital one-carbon transfer reaction cofactors containing a pABA moiety (Basset et al., 2004a; Basset et al., 2004b; Hanson and Gregory, 2011).

Plant BAs also provide carbon skeletons for numerous specialized metabolites. Volatile benzoyl (derived from BA), benzyl (derived from benzyl alcohol), and anthraniloyl (derived from anthranilic acid) compounds (Figure 1) function as aroma and flavor compounds that participate in the attraction of pollinators and seed dispersers (Wang and De Luca, 2005; Goff and Klee, 2006; Dudareva et al., 2006; Schwab et al., 2008; Dudareva et al., 2013). Beyond attraction, volatile BA compounds confer antimicrobial activities to valuable reproductive tissues and are involved in plant defense (Dudareva et al., 2004; Qualley and Dudareva, 2008; Ko¨ llner et al., 2010). The volatile form of SA, methyl-SA (MeSA) (Figure 1), for example, is known to function as an airborne defense signal for plant–plant communication and tritrophic plant–herbivore–predator/parasitoid interactions (Shulaev et al., 1997; Baldwin et al., 2006; Park et al., 2007; Qualley and Dudareva, 2008; Dempsey and Klessig, 2012). Nonvolatile benzoyl, benzyl, and anthraniloyl compounds, such as benzoyloxyglucosinolates (Kliebenstein et al., 2007; Lee et al., 2012), salicortin (Babst et al., 2010), and avenanthramide phytoalexins (Ishihara et al., 1999; Okazaki et al., 2004; Wise et al., 2009) (Figure 1), respectively, can also act as defense compounds. # THE SHIKIMATE PATHWAY PROVIDES PRECURSORS FOR PLANT BAS

Biosynthesis of all plant BAs and their products ultimately starts from the shikimate pathway. Plant BAs can be synthesized directly from the shikimate pathway intermediate 3-dehydroshikimate (3-DHS) or from chorismate; together they represent the shikimate/chorismatederived BAs (Wildermuth, 2006). Alternatively, a network of multiple routes from Phe via t-cinnamic acid (CA) leads to Phederived BAs (Figure 2). The distinguishing feature between shikimate/chorismate and Phe-derived BAs is that the former types preserve the carboxyl group of their precursors, while the carboxyl group of the latter is formed via shortening of the ${ \sf C } _ { 3 }$ side chain.

Flux toward the production of both shikimate/chorismate and Phe-derived plant BAs appears to be partially controlled at the shikimate pathway entry point reaction, which is catalyzed by 3- deoxy-D-arabino-heptulosonate 7-phosphate (DAHP) synthase (DAHPS). It has been found that many plant species contain two copies of DAHPS genes, one that is constitutively expressed and another that is up-regulated in response to demands for increased plant BAs, such as for defense (Tzin and Galili, 2010; Maeda and Dudareva, 2012) or emission of benzenoid floral volatiles (Langer et al., 2014). Moreover, although it has yet to be demonstrated, plant DAHPSs are likely subject to feedback inhibition by AAAs similar to their microbial counterparts (Tzin and Galili, 2010; Maeda and Dudareva, 2012). Constitutive over-expression of a bacterial feedback-insensitive DAHPS in Arabidopsis thaliana led to dramatically increased levels of shikimate compared with both wild-type Arabidopsis and plants over-expressing the bacterial wild-type DAHPS (Tzin et al., 2012). Introduction of the feedback-insensitive DAHPS also led to increased production of many shikimate-derived metabolites, including plant BA products (Tzin et al., 2012). Conversely, in flowers of Petunia hydrida cv Mitchell Diploid, RNAi down-regulation of developmentally regulated DAHPS1 resulted in significantly reduced emission of floral benzenoids (Langer et al., 2014).

# SHIKIMATE/CHORISMATE-DERIVED BAS

# BAs Derived from 3-Dehydroshikimate

The earliest shikimate pathway intermediate serving as a precursor for the synthesis of BAs is 3-DHS (Figure 2), which is formed from the dehydration of 3-dehydroquinate catalyzed by 3-dehydroquinate dehydratase (DHD). Whereas DHD acts as a monofunctional enzyme in bacteria, in plants DHD is fused with the next enzyme in the pathway, shikimate dehydrogenase (SDH), which reduces 3-DHS to shikimate (Figure 2). This fusion of DHD with SDH likely evolved to efficiently direct the flux of

3-DHS toward shikimate (Figure 2) (Maeda and Dudareva, 2012). Nonetheless, tracer experiments in leaves of certain species of Acer and Rhus trees suggested that 3-DHS can be aromatized to form 3,4,5-trihydroxybenzoic acid (gallic acid) (Figure 2) (Dewick and Haslam, 1969; Ishikura et al., 1984), the core component of free-radical scavenging gallo- and ellagitannins (Vogt, 2010). Later investigations of Rhus typhina leaves using $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ nuclear magnetic resonance retrobiosynthetic comparisons of labeling patterns in gallic acid versus amino acids (Werner et al., 1997), and measurements of natural oxygen isotope abundance (Werner et al., 2004), also revealed that an early intermediate of the shikimate pathway, likely 3-DHS, must be the precursor for gallic acid. Very little is known about the molecular players involved in the conversion of 3-DHS to gallic acid, although it has been proposed that 3-DHS may be directly oxidized to gallic acid or dehydrated to protocatechuic acid (3,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid), and then hydroxylated to gallic acid (Figure 2) (Dewick, 2009). A recent study by Muir et al. (2011) suggested that 3-DHS is directly converted to gallic acid, as heterologous expression of the separated SDH domain (of plant DHD-SDH) in E. coli and in planta (in plastids) led to increased production of gallic acid. Activity measurements from mung bean seedlings, on the other hand, indicate that 3-DHS can be converted to protocatechuic acid (Tateoka, 1968). Some pathogenic Bacillus species contain dehydratases (encoded within the asb petrobactin operon) that convert 3-DHS to protocatechuic acid to serve as the binding ligand of their ironchelating siderophore petrobactin (Fox et al., 2008; Pfleger et al., 2008), although homologs of these enzymes do not appear to be present in plants.

# BAs Derived from Chorismate

Many plant BAs originate from the shikimate pathway product chorismate, which serves as substrate for at least four types of competing plastidial enzymes: aminodeoxychorismate synthase (ADCS), anthranilate synthase (AS), isochorismate synthase (ICS), and chorismate mutase (CM) (Figure 2). ADCS converts chorismate to aminodeoxychorismate (Basset et al., 2004a), which undergoes removal of its pyruvyl side chain by aminodeoxychorismate lyase (ADCL) to form pABA (Figure 2) (Basset et al., 2004b). Due to its weak hydrophobic nature, pABA (in its protonated form) is able to diffuse out of the plastid (Quinlivan et al., 2003) and into mitochondria where it provides the benzenoid ring for folic acids (Hanson and Gregory, 2011), or remains in the cytosol where it can be conjugated with glucose by the UDP-glucosyltransferases (UGTs) UGT75B1, UGT74F1, and UGT74F2 for storage in the vacuole (Figure 2) (Eudes et al., 2008). Although the physiological significance is

unclear, pABA can be conjugated on its carboxyl group with amino acids by a GH3 acyl adenylase thioester-forming enzyme called PBS3 (Okrent et al., 2009).

A second type of chorismate-utilizing enzyme is AS, which synthesizes anthranilic acid (Figure 2), an amino-substituted BA that is the first intermediate in the Trp biosynthetic pathway. AS is a heteromultimeric enzyme made up of large $\pmb { \alpha }$ and small $\beta$ subunits (Romero et al., 1995a). Like ADCL, $\mathsf { A S } \mathsf { \alpha }$ performs a pyruvyl elimination reaction, but is also responsible for the amination activity at the ortho position using ammonia produced from glutamine by $\mathsf { A S } \beta$ (reviewed in Maeda and Dudareva, 2012). Plants contain multiple ASas (Tzin and Galili, 2010), and the differential regulation of their cognate genes has been shown to regulate the flux of anthranilic acid into primary and secondary metabolic pathways (Bohlmann et al., 1995). Once synthesized, it can be envisioned that similar to $p A B A$ , anthranilic acid diffuses across intracellular membranes. Outside the plastid, anthranilic acid is subject to modifications via hydroxylation, CoA ligation (Beuerle and Pichersky, 2002), and methylation (Wang and De Luca, 2005; Ko¨ llner et al., 2010), and serves as a precursor for the synthesis of anthraniloyl compounds (Figure 2) including avenanthramide phytoalexins (Ishihara et al., 1999; Okazaki et al., 2004) (Figure 1).

Like many bacteria, plants contain ICSs that catalyze the isomerization of chorismate to isochorismate (Figure 2), which serves as an intermediate in the production of ortho-succinylbenzoic acid (Gross et al., 2006), SA (Wildermuth et al., 2001; Mustafa et al., 2009), and 2,3-dihydroxybenzoic acid (2,3-DHBA) (Muljono et al., 2002; Bartsch et al., 2010) (Figure 2). While the first plant ICSs were isolated from Pythium aphanidermatum-elicited Catharanthus roseus cell cultures (van Tegelen et al., 1999), genetic evidence supporting in planta ICS function came from the demonstration that SA deficiency in Arabidopsis sid2 mutants (Nawrath and Me´ traux, 1999; Dewdney et al., 2000) is the result of mutations at the ICS1 locus (Wildermuth et al., 2001). Later biochemical characterization revealed that Arabidopsis ICS1 indeed converts chorismate to isochorismate and exhibits a $K _ { \mathsf { m } }$ low enough to compete with the other chorismate-utilizing enzymes in plastids (Strawn et al., 2007). Arabidopsis also contains a second plastidial ICS enzyme, encoded by ICS2, although its contribution to downstream products is much less significant than ICS1 (Gross et al., 2006; Garcion et al., 2008).

In plants, isochorismate is converted by a trifunctional enzyme called PHYLLO (Gross et al., 2006) to ortho-succinylbenzoic acid, an intermediate in the formation of phylloquinone (Figure 2), a one-electron carrier in photosystem I (reviewed in van Oostende et al., 2011). On the other hand, the biochemical steps involved in the conversion of isochorismate to 2,3-DHBA and SA in plants remain unclear. In Escherichia coli and Pseudomonas aeruginosa, 2,3-DHBA and SA, respectively, serve as the binding ligands of iron-chelating compounds called siderophores (reviewed in Crosa and Walsh, 2002). In E. coli, the sequential action of 2,3-dihydro-2,3-dihydroxybenzoate synthase (EntB) and 2,3-dihydro-2,3-dihydroxybenzoate dehydrogenase (EntA) is necessary to produce 2,3-DHBA from isochorismate (Gehring et al., 1997). While homologs of EntB and EntA exist in plants, their roles in the formation of 2,3-DHBA directly from isochoris-

mate have not been reported. It turns out that the most similar homologs of EntB in Arabidopsis actually function as nicotinamidases involved in the salvage of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) (Hunt et al., 2007). Further doubts about the existence of a bacterial-like pathway from isochorismate to 2,3-DHBA in plants have arisen from feeding experiments in Arabidopsis showing that labeled SA can be converted to 2,3-DHBA (Bartsch et al., 2010). The formation of 2,3-DHBA via SA was recently confirmed by the identification of the oxygenase, SA 3-hydroxylase (S3H, Figure 2), demonstrating that s3h knockouts lack detectable levels of 2,3-DHBA and 2,3-DHBA-xylose (2,3-DHBX) (Zhang et al., 2013). In P. aeruginosa, formation of SA from isochorismate occurs via an isochorismate pyruvate lyase (IPL), the cognate gene of which likely evolved from duplication of the CM gene (Gaille et al., 2002). While genes encoding homologs of bacterial IPLs appear to be absent from plant genomes, it should be further explored if plant CMs have convergently evolved side IPL activities since Arabidopsis CM1 was reported to possess weak IPL activity (Strawn, 2010).

After it is synthesized, SA is exported from plastids via a multidrug and toxin extrusion (MATE) transporter called Enhanced Disease Susceptibility 5 (EDS5) (Serrano et al., 2013). Expression of EDS5 is low under normal conditions but is induced upon stresses known to trigger SA synthesis, such as during pathogen attack or exposure to UV-C light (Nawrath et al., 2002). The fact that SA was found to accumulate in plastids of eds5 mutants engineered to over-produce SA (Serrano et al., 2013) suggests that diffusion of SA across cellular membranes is likely negligible or limiting. Out of the plastid, SA functions in its free form as a signaling molecule for coordinating responses to numerous (a) biotic stresses (see review from Vlot et al., 2009 and references within). Second, in the cytosol SA can undergo various modifications. SA can be methylated to form MeSA through the action of BA/SA carboxyl methyltransferases from a wide variety of species (Effmert et al., 2005) including Arabidopsis (AtBSMT1) (Chen et al., 2003) and petunia (BSMT1 and BSMT2) (Negre et al., 2003). This modification allows SA to be more membrane permeable and volatile for release into the atmosphere. Moreover, SA can be inactivated and stored in the vacuole through glucosylation (Dean et al., 2003, 2005). In Arabidopsis, UGT74F1, and to a much lesser extent UGT74F2 (AtSGT1), glucosylate SA on its hydroxyl group to produce SA 2-O-$\beta$ -glucoside (SAG) (Figure 2) (Lim et al., 2002). Over-expression of AtSGT1 in Arabidopsis was also found to result in the formation MeSA 2-O- $\beta$ -glucoside (MeSAG) (Song et al., 2008). At the same time, UGT74F2 and UGT75B1 were shown to glucosylate SA on its carboxyl group to produce salicyloyl glucose ester (SGE) (Lim et al., 2002). The same three glucosyltransferases conjugate glucose to the carboxyl group of $p A B A$ (Eudes et al., 2008). Moreover, SA can be conjugated at its carboxyl groups with amino acids, such as aspartic acid (Staswick et al., 2002; Zhang et al., 2007), although the physiological role of SA–amino acid conjugation is unknown. For a more thorough review on SA modifications, see Dempsey et al. (2011). Finally, outside the plastid, SA can serve as a precursor for the synthesis of salicyloyl-derived compounds. For example, SA can be converted to catechol, which can be methylated on one hydroxy group to form the smoky flavor compound guaiacol (Figure 1) (Mageroy et al., 2012) found in tomato, or on both hydroxy groups to

produce the pleasant-smelling compound veratrole (Figure 1) emitted by flowers of Silene latifolia (Gupta et al., 2012; Akhtar and Pichersky, 2013). As mentioned above, SA can be converted to 2,3-DHBA. Classic radiolabeling studies have also shown that SA is used by many plants to synthesize 2,5-DHBA (gentisic acid) (Ibrahim and Towers, 1959), possibly via an enzyme similar to S3H (Zhang et al., 2013). DHBAs can be modified by conjugation with glucose (Lim et al., 2002) or xylose (e.g. UGT89A2; Li et al., 2014) to produce conjugates that serve as inactive storage forms or important signaling compounds in plant defense (Dempsey et al., 2011).

The fourth type of chorismate-utilizing enzyme in plant plastids is CM, which is responsible for the first committed step in Phe and Tyr synthesis (Figure 2). CM catalyzes the conversion of chorismate to prephenate via a pericyclic Claisen rearrangement (Romero et al., 1995b). Plants contain at least two CM isozymes. Whereas CM1 is plastidial, allosterically regulated by AAAs, and participates in Tyr and Phe biosynthesis, CM2 is cytosolic, insensitive to AAAs, and has an unknown physiological function (reviewed in Tzin and Galili, 2010 and Maeda and Dudareva, 2012; Westfall et al., 2014). Arabidopsis and other Brassicaceae contain a third CM, CM3, which like CM1 is plastidial and allosterically regulated by AAAs (Mobley et al., 1999). In addition, cysteine and histidine were recently found to serve as negative effectors of Arabidopsis CM3 (Westfall et al., 2014). Sequence comparisons of CMs across multiple plant species suggest that residues at the effector site of plant CMs may allow for specialized regulation of this branch point enzyme in different species (Westfall et al., 2014). Moreover, CMs appear to be transcriptionally regulated as Arabidopsis CM1 and CM3 are inducible in response to elictors and pathogen treatments (Eberhard et al., 1996; Mobley et al., 1999) and petunia PhCM1 is co-regulated with emission of benzenoid volatiles (Colquhoun et al., 2010).

As discussed in the next section, many plant BAs and benzenoid products are derived from Phe (Figure 2). The subsequent conversion of prephenate to Phe proceeds via two alternative routes. In the arogenate pathway, prephenate aminotransferase (PPA-AT, Figure 2) (Graindorge et al., 2010; Maeda et al., 2011; Dal Cin et al., 2011) transaminates prephenate to arogenate, which can then be dehydrated/decarboxylated to Phe by arogenate dehydratase (ADT, Figure 2) (Cho et al., 2007; Rippert et al., 2009; Maeda et al., 2010). In the alternative phenylpyruvate pathway, these reactions occur in the reverse order. First, prephenate is dehydrated/decarboxylated to form phenylpyruvate, which is then transaminated to Phe (reviewed in Tzin and Galili, 2010 and Maeda and Dudareva, 2012). Whereas the arogenate pathway occurs entirely in the plastid, the recent identification of plant phenylpyruvate aminotransferase (PPY-AT, Figure 2) has revealed that the Phe produced from the alternative phenylpyruvate pathway is formed in the cytosol (Yoo et al., 2013). It remains unknown how Phe and phenylpyruvate produced from the arogenate and phenylpyruvate pathways, respectively, are exported from the plastid.

# PHENYLALANINE-DERIVED BAS

Phe-derived plant BAs are synthesized from CA and its hydroxy- and methoxy-derivatives (Figure 3) produced by the

phenylpropanoid pathway, which begins with the deamination of Phe to CA by L-Phe ammonia lyase (PAL, Figure 2) (for a review of the phenylpropanoid pathway, see Vogt, 2010 and Bonawitz and Chapple, 2010). Formation of BAs from CAs requires shortening of the ${ \sf C } _ { 3 }$ precursor side chain by two carbon units, an observation that was made as early as the 1950s (Geissman and Hinreiner, 1952). Foundational support for this hypothesis came from the finding that when wheat leaves were fed radiolabeled $p$ -coumaric acid (p-CA), the $\mathsf { C } _ { 1 } ,$ and $\complement _ { 2 } ,$ carbons of the precursor side chain were released as acetate/acetyl-CoA (Vollmer et al., 1965). Moreover, tracer experiments showed that labeling from U- $^ { 1 4 } \mathsf { C }$ -Phe and CA-b $^ { 1 4 } \mathsf { C }$ is retrieved in plant BAs (El-Basyouni et al., 1964). Similarly, tracer experiments in Nicotiana tabacum (Yalpani et al., 1993), rice (Silverman et al., 1995), potato (Coquoz et al., 1998), and silene (Akhtar and Pichersky, 2013) revealed that SA can be synthesized from Phe via CA and BA. Multiple pathways have been proposed for the side chain shortening reactions necessary to produce plant BAs from CA precursors: the CoA-dependent $\beta$ -oxidative route, the CoAdependent non-oxidative route, and the CoA-independent non-oxidative route (Figure 2).

# The b-Oxidative Pathway to Synthesize BAs

Using petunia flowers and Arabidopsis as model systems, all the genes involved in the core $\beta \cdot$ -oxidative route from CA to BA-CoA have recently been identified in plants (Van Moerkercke et al., 2009; Colquhoun et al., 2012; Klempien et al., 2012; Lee et al., 2012; Qualley et al., 2012; Bussell et al., 2014). It can be envisioned that the same or similar genes are involved in $\beta \cdot$ -oxidative side chain shortening reactions of other hydroxy- and methoxy-derivatives of CA to their respective BAs (Figure 3). The $\beta \cdot$ -oxidative route takes place in peroxisomes and is analogous to the $\beta \cdot$ -oxidation of fatty acids and some branched-chain amino acids. Indication for the existence of the $\beta$ -oxidative route first came from studies showing that conversion of CA to BA is stimulated by the addition of ATP and CoA in cell-free extracts of Quercus pedunculata (Alibert and Ranjeva, 1971). Later, feeding experiments with stable isotope-labeled $( ^ { 2 } \mathsf { H } _ { 6 } , ^ { 1 8 } { \mathrm { O } } ) 3$ -hydroxy-3- phenylpropanoic acid in cucumber (Cucumis sativus) and Nicotiana attenuata plants further supported a $\beta$ -oxidative route based on the absence of labeled benzaldehyde (BD), an intermediate of the non-oxidative pathways (Figure 2) (Jarvis et al., 2000). The $\beta \cdot$ -oxidative route begins with the activation of CA to the corresponding CoA-ester by cinnamoyl-CoA ligase (Ph-CNL) in petunia (Colquhoun et al., 2012; Klempien et al., 2012) and its homolog BZO1 in Arabidopsis (Figure 2) (Lee et al., 2012). A similar reaction, catalyzed by At4g19010 in Arabidopsis, occurs for initiating the $\beta$ -oxidative side chain shortening of $p$ -CA to 4HBA (Figure 2) (Block et al., 2014). Next, the ${ \sf C } _ { 3 } ,$ carbon of the cinnamoyl-CoA (CA-CoA) propyl side chain is hydrated to make 3-hydroxy-3-phenylpropanoyl-CoA, followed by oxidation of the newly formed hydroxyl group to produce 3-oxo-3- phenylpropanoyl-CoA, both reactions of which are catalyzed by the bifunctional CA-CoA hydratase/dehydrogenase (PhCHD) in petunia (Qualley et al., 2012). In Arabidopsis, a knockout of the PhCHD homolog, AIM1, has decreased levels of SA, hydroxybenzoylated choline esters (HBACs), and 3- and 4- benzoyloxyglucosinolates (3- and 4-BZOs) (Bussell et al., 2014). The final step of the $\beta$ -oxidative route is cleavage of the $\beta$ -keto thioester intermediate to produce BA-CoA (Figure 2), with the release of acetyl-CoA. This reaction is catalyzed by 3-ketoacyl thiolase 1 (PhKAT1) in petunia (Van Moerkercke et al., 2009), and a knockout of its Arabidopsis homolog, KAT2, was found to have reduced levels of HBACs and 3- and 4-BZOs (Bussell et al., 2014). Although it has not been demonstrated, it seems likely that these or other KATs catalyze similar reactions for the production of 4HBA-CoA.

Despite full elucidation of the molecular players involved in the core $\beta$ -oxidative route of plant BA synthesis, questions still

remain about the peroxisomal transport of phenylpropanoid substrates and their products. Reverse genetic studies have shown that the Arabidopsis ABC transporter PXA1 (also known as Comatose or AtABCD1) is necessary for the import of phenylpropanoid $\beta$ -oxidative precursors into peroxisomes (Figure 2) (Block et al., 2014; Bussell et al., 2014), although it is still not clear if the transported substrates are free acids or CoA esters. The recent demonstration that PXA1 contains intrinsic ATP-stimulated acyl-CoA thioesterase activity suggests that acyl-CoAs synthesized in the cytosol may be the imported form (De Marcos Lousa et al., 2013). This is consistent with the observation that pxa1 mutants accumulate acyl-CoAs (Footitt et al., 2002) and that the ATPase activity of PXA1 was found to be stimulated by fatty acyl-CoAs but not fatty acids (Nyathi et al., 2010). In this

scenario, the acyl-CoA (e.g. CA-CoA) binds to PXA1 on the cytosolic side of the peroxisomal membrane, the CoA moiety is then cleaved by PXA1, and the free acid (e.g. CA) is imported into the peroxisome where it is reactivated by a peroxisomal CoA ligase for $\beta$ -oxidation (De Marcos Lousa et al., 2013). This model implicates the involvement of cytosolic CoA-ligases (e.g. 4-coumarate CoA-ligases, 4CLs) in the $\beta$ -oxidative route. Even though it may be energetically wasteful, this process may control the distribution of flux between phenylpropanoid and benzenoid metabolism. Another unresolved question is how formed BA-CoA products are exported out of the peroxisome. The CoA moiety likely precludes free diffusion of BA-CoAs across the membrane and there is no precedent in general for the export of acyl-CoAs from peroxisomes. It is possible that peroxisomes contain transporters similar to PXA1 with thioesterase activity that accept BA-CoAs and release free BAs into the cytosol. Recently, BA-CoA thioesterase activity has been reported in the soluble fraction of petunia peroxisomes (Qualley et al., 2012) and Arabidopsis thioesterases with broad aromatic acyl-CoA thioesterase activity have been identified (Widhalm et al., 2012). These reports indicate that, in the peroxisomal lumen, BA-CoAs are hydrolyzed to BAs, which then may be exported via an unknown transporter or freely diffuse across the membrane to the cytosol.

# The Non-Oxidative Pathways to Synthesize BAs

The characteristic feature of the non-oxidative pathways is the presence of BDs as key metabolic intermediates in the formation of BAs (Figure 3B). The first committed step in the CoAindependent non-oxidative pathway is the hydration of free CAs to 3-hydroxy-3-phenylpropanoic acid intermediates, which are then subjected to side chain degradation via a reverse aldol reaction that releases acetate to produce BDs (Figure 2). Initial evidence for this route came from the finding that $p$ -CA fed to cell-free extracts of potato tubers resulted in the formation of 4-hydroxy-BD (4HBD) and 4HBA independent of the addition of CoA and ATP (French et al., 1976). Similar observations were made in cell-free extracts of Lithospermum erythrorizum (Yazaki et al., 1991) and from labeling studies with carrot cell suspension cultures (Schnitzler et al., 1992). Using tissue cultures from Vanilla planifolia, 4HBD synthase, an enzyme with high similarity to cysteine proteinases, was purified and shown to convert $p$ -CA to 4HBD and acetate without the addition of cofactors (Podstolski et al., 2002; Havkin-Frenkel et al., 2003). A comparable activity has also been reported in Nicotiana tabacum for the conversion of 2-hydroxy-CA to salicylaldehyde (Figure 3) (Malinowski et al., 2007). Recently, Gallage et al. (2014) have shown that the V. planifolia 4HBD synthase is a hydratase/lyase that can directly convert ferulic acid to vanillin (Figure 3), but the activity with $p$ -CA could not be confirmed. Introduction of the cognate gene (renamed VpVAN) into tobacco and barley resulted in the formation of vanillyl alcohol glucoside (Gallage et al., 2014).

The difference between the CoA-independent non-oxidative pathway described above and the CoA-dependent route is that the first step in the latter starts from CoA esters instead of free acids (Figure 2). The CoA-ester substrates are converted to 3-hydroxy-3-phenylpropanoyl-CoA intermediates, which undergo side chain shortening via a reverse aldol reaction with the

release of acetyl-CoA to form BDs (Figure 2). An enoyl-CoA hydratase/lyase was isolated from a species of Pseudomonas capable of using ferulic acid as a carbon source and found to convert feruloyl-CoA to vanillin with concomitant release of acetyl-CoA (Gasson et al., 1998). Over-expression of this enzyme in Datura stramonium hairy root cultures led to increased production of 4HBA and its derivatives (Mitra et al., 2002). A similar hydratase/lyase activity was found for the cleavage of CA-CoA to BD and acetyl-CoA in Hypericum androsaemum cell cultures (Abd El-Mawla and Beerhues, 2002), although the enzyme responsible has never been isolated.

The final shared step of the non-oxidative pathways is the oxidation of BDs to BAs by aldehyde oxidases (AOs) and/or aldehyde dehydrogenases (ALDHs) (Figure 2). Arabidopsis contains cytoplasmic AOs with broad substrate specificity shown to convert BDs to BAs (Seo et al., 2000; Schwarz and Mendel, 2006; Ibdah et al., 2009). One of these, AAO4, exhibits highest activity with BD and seeds of aao4 knockouts have reduced levels of BA and 3- and 4-BZOs (Ibdah et al., 2009). On the other hand, ALDHs capable of oxidizing BD substrates have been identified and characterized from Zea mays (RF2A, Liu and Schnable, 2002) and Antirrhinum majus (snapdragon) (BALDH, Figure 2) (Long et al., 2009). Over-expression of snapdragon BALDH in petunia flowers led to elevated levels of BA and emission of BA-derived volatiles (Long et al., 2009). In contrast to the cytosolic AOs, BALDHs are localized to mitochondria (Liu and Schnable, 2002; Long et al., 2009). By nature, aldehydes react spontaneously with nucleophiles, particularly with amino groups of nucleic acids and proteins (Linster et al., 2013). In eukaryotes, detoxification of aldehydes is connected with mitochondrial respiration and redox balance (Leiphon and Picklo, 2007). How the potentially toxic effects of BD substrates are contained and how the compounds are transported into mitochondria is an open question. How BAs produced in the mitochondria are exported to the cytosol is also unknown.

It is still unclear how much each of the different BA biosynthetic pathways contributes to synthesis of BAs. For example, stable isotope labeling combined with metabolic flux analysis of the BA biosynthetic network revealed that both the $\beta$ -oxidative and CoA-independent non-oxidative pathways contribute to the formation of benzenoid compounds in petunia flowers (Figure 1) (Boatright et al., 2004). Similarly, genetic studies in Arabidopsis have indicated that both the $\beta$ -oxidative (Lee et al., 2012; Bussell et al., 2014) and non-oxidative (Ibdah et al., 2009) pathways contribute to the formation of 3- and 4-BZOs. It should also be considered that flux from each BA biosynthetic pathway could be species-, tissue-, and stress-specific. Flux analysis revealed that the relative contribution of the $\beta \cdot$ -oxidative versus the non-oxidative pathways toward floral benzenoids changes over a daily light/dark cycle in petunia (Orlova et al., 2006).

# MODIFICATIONS OF BAS

As already briefly mentioned, BAs are subject to a number of modifications, including glucosylation, methylation, amino acid conjugation, and hydroxylation. Glucosylation of plant BAs may function to increase solubility for storage in the vacuole, prevent free diffusion across lipid bilayers, confer access to

transporters that recognize glycosyl moieties, or reduce toxicity (reviewed in Bowles and Lim, 2010). Several cytosolic UGTs with broad substrate specificity have been shown to act on the carboxyl and hydroxyl groups of a variety of plant BAs (Lim et al., 2002; Bowles and Lim, 2010). Methylation of plant BAs improves mobility across cellular membranes and in some cases increases volatility for release into the atmosphere. The methyltransferases responsible for methylating carboxyl groups of BA and SA have been identified in several species (Effmert et al., 2005). The activation/inactivation of plant BAs through conjugation with amino acids is relatively unexplored, but two Arabidopsis GH3-like proteins, GH3.5 (Staswick et al., 2002; Zhang et al., 2007) and PBS3 (Okrent et al., 2009), have been demonstrated to function in the formation of amino acid conjugates with SA and 4-substituted BA conjugates, respectively. Finally, plant BAs can be modified or converted to other hydroxy BA forms through hydroxylation. The plant hormone SA results from the 2-hydroxylation of BA (Leo´ n et al., 1993; Yalpani et al., 1994; Leo´ n et al., 1995). Benzoic acid 2-hydroxylase (BA2H; Figure 2), a soluble P450 monooxygenase that is responsible for the conversion of BA to SA, has been partially purified and characterized from TMVinoculated tobacco leaves (Leo´ n et al., 1995), although the gene responsible has not been isolated. Additional studies in tobacco have suggested that BA–glucose may be the substrate of BA2H (Chong et al., 2001). It is also a possibility that modifications may occur on BA intermediates, such as BA-CoAs, BDs, and/or benzyl alcohols.

# CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS

Rapid progress has been made over the past several years in the identification of genes and enzymes involved in the formation of plant BAs and their derivatives, despite many of these compounds being discovered over a half century ago. The advent of quick and cheap sequencing technologies has opened the door for the use of new model plants with high carbon flux toward specialized benzenoid compounds to facilitate elucidation of the plant BA biosynthetic pathways. Although much has been achieved in a short period of time, many genes in the BA biosynthetic network remain to be discovered. In general, complex metabolic networks in plants, including that for BAs (Figure 2), are highly compartmentalized and are spread across multiple subcellular locations (Heinig et al., 2013). Therefore, the involvement of protein-mediated trafficking steps, many of which are unknown, within and between pathways must be taken into consideration. Comparative transcriptomics, frequently used for the identification of unknown genes encoding enzymes (Yonekura-Sakakibara et al., 2013), has also emerged as a powerful tool for discovery of transporter genes (Bordych et al., 2013). Moreover, the explosion of available plant and microbial genomes, coupled with the introduction of user-friendly postgenomic tools, has galvanized comparative genomic strategies for determining the functions of unknown plant proteins (de Crecy-Lagard and Hanson, 2007; Hanson et al., 2010; Bradbury et al., 2013). Going forward now, coupling the identification of candidate genes with transient screens for assessing gene function in planta (e.g. Yoo et al., 2013) will greatly accelerate gene discovery.

Knowing the pathway enzymes alone is not enough to determine the contribution of multiple parallel pathways to a given product, especially if flux through such alternative pathways is dynamic. A perfect example of this is the formation of SA directly from chorismate (the ICS pathway) and via Phe (the PAL pathway) (Chen et al., 2009; Dempsey et al., 2011). In Arabidopsis, SA is believed to be primarily produced through the ICS pathway as ics1/ics2 double mutants contain only about $5 \%$ of SA levels compared with wild-type (Garcion et al., 2008). If the majority of SA is synthesized from the ICS pathway then it would be expected that the PAL pathway contributes minimally to SA. However, quadruple Arabidopsis pal mutants with only $10 \%$ PAL activity contain $50 \%$ less SA than wild-type (Huang et al., 2010). Such discrepancies may suggest unexplored cross-talk (i.e. feedback inhibition) between the ICS and PAL pathways, or may even indicate that SA biosynthesis utilizes intermediates from both pathways. Since the ICS and PAL pathways are spatially separated in the cell, metabolic profiling of pathway intermediates at the organelle level by non-aqueous fractionation is needed to determine if perturbations in one pathway affect the other. Furthermore, combining this with stable isotope labeling can provide quantitative insights about flux distribution between the parallel pathways under normal and stress conditions and in response to genetic perturbations. Since parallel pathways exist for the production of other plant BAs (Figure 2), similar approaches can be used to generate compartmentalized models for more robust and predictive engineering strategies.
