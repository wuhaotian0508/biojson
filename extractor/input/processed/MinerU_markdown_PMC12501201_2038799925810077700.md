# Nanotechnology-driven coordination of shoot–root systems enhances rice nitrogen use efficiency

Chuanxi Wanga,b, Bingxu Chenga,b, Zhenggao Xiaoa,b , Yahui Jia,b, Jiangshan Zhanga,b, Rongxin Zhoua,b, Xian-Zheng Yuanc , Melanie Kahd , Zhenyu Wanga,b,1 , and Baoshan Xinge,1

Enhancing nitrogen use efficiency (NUE) in agricultural production can reduce fertilizer input, mitigate greenhouse gas emissions and decrease water pollution incidence. However, improving NUE in farming systems without compromising food security remains challenging. Herein, we have successfully developed and applied selenium-based nanotechnology, which capitalizes on above- and belowground synergies to enhance field-scale NUE. Specifically, when N fertilizer application was reduced by $3 0 \%$ , foliar application of selenium nanomaterials significantly enhanced rice photosynthesis by $4 0 . 3 \%$ compared with reduced N fertilizer treatment $( { 1 8 9 \mathrm { ~ k g } \mathrm { ~ N } / \bar { \mathrm { h a } } } )$ . This enhancement promoted carbohydrate synthesis and translocation, providing abundant carbon sources for rhizosphere processes. These abundant carbon sources modulated rhizosphere N transformation processes, stimulating ammonification and nitrification while suppressing denitrification, thereby reducing methane, ammonia, and nitrous oxide emissions by 18.8 to $4 5 . 6 \%$ . In addition, compared with controls $( 2 7 0 \mathrm { ~ k g } \mathrm { N } / \mathrm { h a } )$ , our approach improved rice root growth and upregulated gene expression associated with N uptake and translocation, increasing rice NUE $( 4 8 . 3 \% )$ . While maintaining comparable yields to conventional practice, we observed significant improvements in rice quality parameters including crude protein, amino acids, and Se content. Furthermore, the application of this above- and belowground synergistic nano-regulation technology reduced environmental negative impacts by $4 1 . 0 \%$ and increased economic benefits by $3 8 . 2 \%$ per ton of rice produced, relative to conventional practices. This work elucidates how nano-enabled agricultural regulation achieves reduced input, enhanced efficiency, and increased income, emphasizing the high potential of nanotechnology in agricultural applications, particularly in improving the utilization efficiency of N fertilizers.

Global consumption of synthetic nitrogen (N) fertilizer currently amounts to approximately 110 million tons annually (2024), a level necessary to sustain the unprecedented growth in food production since the Green Revolution ( 1 ). However, N use efficiency (NUE) in major crops remains low, typically ranging from only 40 to $6 0 \%$ . Consequently, a substantial portion of applied N is not utilized by crops. This unused N either accumulates in the soil or contaminates water resources, leading to significant environmental degradation ( 2 ). Furthermore, human activities have pushed the planetary boundary for nitrogen deposition from ammonia $\left( \mathrm { N H } _ { 3 } \right)$ emissions past safe limits, exceeding it by several times ( 3 ). Critically, based on current agricultural practices, further increases in N fertilizer application will be required to meet the projected $5 0 \%$ rise in global food demand driven by population growth between now and 2050 ( 4 ). Ensuring sufficient food production while preserving the ecosystem health presents a profound long-term challenge. Rice cultivation lies at the heart of this dilemma, as the staple food for over 3 billion people, and rice production accounts for 15 to $2 0 \%$ of global synthetic N fertilizer application. Consequently, it is critical to improve NUE in rice systems without sacrificing rice yield. This urgency is particularly acute in China, a major global rice producer, where historical cropland NUE has remained near $3 0 \%$ , significantly lower than the global average of $5 5 \%$ ( 5 ). 

Current strategies to improve NUE include breeding techniques, optimized fertilizer application, and the so-called “4R” nutrient management, which have been shown to be effective in reducing N inputs and losses in site-specific trials ( 6 ). Nevertheless, these approaches face limitations due to high implementation costs, time intensiveness, or partial efficiency ( 7 ). Thus, novel technologies capable of boosting NUE to enhance plant growth and stress resistance, minimizing N losses, and maintaining high rice yields are therefore imperative. # Significance

While nanotechnology’s agricultural potential is recognized, its mechanistic role in modulating crop nutrient utilization, particularly nitrogen dynamics, remains unresolved. Herein, we elucidate how foliar application of selenium-based nanomaterials enhances nitrogen use efficiency and reduces greenhouse gas emissions in paddy field by stimulating photosynthetic activity and subsequently regulating rhizosphere processes. This approach yields superior environmental and economic benefits compared to conventional practices. Moreover, by systematically investigating the above- and belowground synergies, we provide insights into the mechanistic basis of nanotechnology-enabled enhancement of crop nutrient utilization. These findings underscore the nanomaterial’s potential to advance sustainable agricultural practices, offering a viable strategy for balancing food production needs with ecological preservation objectives in the context of global climate change.

Nanomaterial applications in agronomy primarily employ two delivery methods: root application and leaf application ( 11 ). For root application approaches, nanomaterials can modulate rhizosphere processes to enhance plant uptake of critical nutrients including N ( 12 ). Conversely, foliar application of nanomaterials can accelerate photosynthetic electron transfer rate via light conversion, directly boosting photosynthesis efficiency and yield ( 13 ). This method offers distinct advantages by bypassing soil geochemical constraints (e.g., heteroaggregation, solubilization, and redox transformations) that limit nutrient bioavailability ( 14 ). Critically, foliar application typically requires lower application rates than root application methods, substantially reducing potential environmental risks ( 15 ). Nevertheless, the potential for foliar-applied nanomaterials to synergistically amplify nutrient acquisition through above-belowground interactions remains unexplored ( 16 ). 

Selenium (Se), an essential micronutrient with proven immunomodulatory functions ( 17 ). Compared to traditional Se fertilizer, Se engineered nanomaterials (Se ENMs) demonstrate superior efficacy in regulating crop absorption of key nutrients (N, P, Mg, and Fe) ( 18 – 20 ). Notably, foliar applied Se ENMs outperform conventional approaches by simultaneously increasing yield and Se content while avoiding soil accumulation and associated environmental risks ( 18 ). This dual advantage drives market preference: consumers value Se-fortified products for preventing deficiency-related diseases (e.g., Keshan/Kashin-Beck disease) ( 21 ), while farmers benefit from enhanced crop value and market demand ( 22 ). This evidence raises a critical research question: can foliar application of Se ENMs improve N availability through photosynthetic-root interactions, 

enabling reduced N fertilizer inputs in rice production without yield? To address this, we established three field treatments ( Fig. 1 ), including normal N fertilizer input (CK), reduced N input (RF, $3 0 \%$ lower than CK), and RF + Se ENMs treatments. Our objectives are specifically to 1) quantify yield responses to Se ENMs under N reduction; 2) mechanistically elucidate photosynthetic-rootmicrobe interactions, and 3) evaluate environmental and economic benefits of Se-enabled N reduction. Altogether, the findings of this study will further substantiate the potential of nano-enabled agricultural management technologies in mitigating agricultural nonpoint source pollution. 

# Results and Discussion

Theoretical Framework of How Foliar Application Can Improve N Availability. We conducted field experiments to investigate the effects of foliar application of Se ENMs on rice yield and quality under conditions of reduced N fertilizer input $( 3 0 \% )$ . The theoretical framework of how foliar application of Se ENMs could enhance N availability includes four processes (Fig.  2). Thus, we hypothesized foliar application of Se ENMs may increase photosynthesis, leading to the accumulation and downward transport of carbohydrates (Fig.  2, $\textcircled{1}$ ). This process is anticipated to promote root growth and stimulate the production of root exudates, which in turn recruit beneficial rhizosphere microorganisms (Fig. $2 , \textcircled{2 } )$ ). We also hypothesized the aforementioned dynamic processes in the rhizosphere can trigger N transformation reactions, ultimately leading to an increase in available N content and enhancing the efficiency of N absorption and utilization by crops (Fig. 2, ③). Therefore, the enhanced root development is expected to improve effective tillering of rice, and ultimately maintain rice yield (Fig. 2, $\textcircled{4}$ ). A series of tests have been carried out to test the occurrence of those processes.

Se ENMs significantly enhanced rice net photosynthetic rate by $4 0 . 3 \%$ , compared to RF treatment (Fig. 3A). This enhancement was concomitant with increases in stomatal conductance $( 2 1 . 5 \% )$ ), transpiration rate $( 1 1 . 1 \% )$ ), and intercellular $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration $( 1 8 . 7 \% )$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Se and sulfur (S) belong to the same group in the periodic table and share many similar physiological properties and chemical behaviors (23, 24). Previous studies have demonstrated that Se can significantly enhance kinase-modulating activity through the allelic substitution of S with Se, which was previously attributed to the increased synthesis of ferredoxin and $\mathrm { F e } / \mathrm { S }$ proteins in plants (19). Therefore, the observed increase in photosynthetic efficiency in RF + Se ENMs accelerated the accumulation of carbohydrates in rice leaves (increased by $2 0 . 9 \%$ ) compared to RF (Fig.  3A). The result was consistent with a previous study, which reported that Se ENMs exposure induced a $6 4 . 1 \%$ increase of carbohydrate content in the cherry tomato by promoting photosynthesis (25). Therefore, Se ENMs increased the amounts of iron-S proteins and ferredoxin, leading to enhanced photosynthesis and accumulation of carbohydrates. 

Se ENMs Optimized the Rhizosphere Processes of Rice. The key carbohydrate transporter genes, specifically SUT (sucrose transporter) and SWEET (sugars will eventually be exported transporter) were significantly upregulated in RF + Se ENMs compared to the RF (Fig.  3B). Among them, the relative expression levels of OsSUT1, OsSUT2, OsSWEET4, OsSWEET5, OsSWEET13, and OsSWEET14 were upregulated by $1 3 9 . 6 \%$ , $3 5 8 . 7 \%$ , $2 3 1 . 8 \%$ , $1 8 8 . 2 \%$ , $3 2 . 4 \%$ , and $2 4 . 2 \%$ , respectively, compared with the CK group (Fig.  3B). OsSUT2, along with OsSWEET13 and OsSWEET14, could be involved in unloading large amounts of sucrose to the stem, which significantly reduced sugar exportability (26–28). OsSWEET4, which was proven as a hexose transporter at the basal endosperm transfer layer and localized on the plasma membrane, is required for this uptake (29). OsSWEET5 may function in the mobilization of galactose 

reserves into stem, root, and floral organs (30). In addition, OsBE1 (branching enzyme 1) is a starch branching enzyme gene and OsSSIIa (starch synthase IIa) is an amylopectin synthesis gene (31, 32), were significantly upregulated by $2 3 0 . 1 \%$ and $1 9 4 . 2 \%$ compared with the CK (Fig. $3 B$ ). These results indicate that not only carbohydrate synthesis was increased but also its transport was enhanced. 

Enhanced carbohydrate transport correlated with significant improvements in root development ( 33 ). The application of Se ENMs led to an increase in root length $( 6 5 . 2 \% )$ ), root tip number $( 5 6 . 3 \% )$ ), root surface area $( 7 5 . 1 \% )$ ), and root volume $( 9 4 . 9 \% )$ ) compared to RF treatment ( Fig. $3 C$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S2 ). The fresh and dry weight of rice roots were also increased by $\bar { 6 6 . 4 \% }$ and $4 5 . 6 \%$ , respectively, under Se ENMs application compared with RF treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 ). In contrast, reduced N fertilizer application significantly inhibited the development of rice roots. Concurrently, the application of Se ENMs enhanced transport of carbohydrates and the development of the root systems, which facilitated the secretion of root exudates and enriched the organic matter in the rice rhizosphere. Compared to the RF treatment group, the application of Se ENMs via foliar spray led to a $3 0 . 4 \%$ increase in dissolved organic matter (DOM) in the rhizosphere soil ( Fig. $3 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S4A ). Previous research suggested that Se ENMs can elicit the secretion of organic acids and amino acids from rice roots, ultimately resulting in an elevated level of soil DOM ( 18 , 34 ). These results imply that Se ENMs can enhance the synthesis and translocation of carbohydrates by initiating photosynthesis, which subsequently stimulates the excretion of rice root exudates. 

Root exudates can function as substrates, signaling molecules, or chemotactic agents, regulating soil microorganisms and enhancing soil nutrient cycling ( 35 ). Previous studies reported that Se ENMs stimulated the synthesis and secretion of rice root exudates, recruiting beneficial microorganisms such as Bacillus and Pseudomonas , which have N-fixing and phosphorus-solubilizing capabilities ( 34 ).

In this study, under Se ENMs treatment, the relative abundance of fluorescence intensity in component 1 (related with biological byproducts) was significantly increased by $2 4 . 0 ~ \%$ ( Fig. $3 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S4A ). The fluorescence index (FI) and bioluminescence index (BIX), which are related to microbial activity, were enhanced by $1 . 9 \%$ and $3 . 9 \%$ , respectively, after Se ENMs application, indicating a higher contribution to DOM from microbial activity in $\mathrm { R F } + \mathrm { S e }$ ENMs treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B ) ( 36 ). These results suggest that under reduced N fertilizer conditions, the application of Se ENMs improved rice photosynthesis, stimulated the abundance and activity of rhizosphere microorganisms of rice. The $\alpha$ -diversity of rhizosphere microorganisms across treatments indicated no significant differences (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). However, 

$\beta$ -diversity results highlighted pronounced variations among treatment groups (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 ). At the genus level, compared with RF treatment group, Se ENMs application significantly increased the relative abundances of bacteria such as Ellin6067 , Lysobacter , Sphingomonas , and Gemmatimonas by $5 2 . 3 \%$ , $3 8 . 8 \%$ , $1 9 . 5 \%$ , and $9 5 . 2 \%$ , respectively ( Fig. $3 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). Microorganisms such as Lysobacter and Gemmatimonas are primarily involved in soil nitrification processes, converting ammonium $\mathrm { ( N H _ { 4 } ^ { + } { - } N ) }$ ) or nitrite $( \mathrm { N O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } { - } \mathrm { N } )$ ) into nitrate $( \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - } { - } \mathrm { N } )$ ), thereby increasing soil available N content. Studies by Huang et al. found that the upregulation of nitrification genes (amoB ) in soil is closely related to increased Lysobacter abundance ( 37 ). Li et al. used co-occurrence network analysis to demonstrate a strong correlation 

between total soil N content and Gemmatimonas abundance ( 38 ). Additionally, Ellin6067 and Sphingomonas enhance soil N availability and promote plant root absorption of soil N ( 38 ). 

Overall, our results show that foliar application of Se ENMs enhanced transport of carbohydrates from aboveground to belowground parts, promoted development of roots and root exudates, modulating the rhizosphere microbial community composition of rice. 

Se ENM-Induced Alterations in Soil N Speciation and Bioavailability. The application of Se ENMs induced significant changes in rhizosphere soil microbial N transformation functional genes (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Specifically, the ammonia-oxidizing gene (amoA), the nitrification-related gene (nxrA), and the gene for dissimilatory nitrate reduction to ammonium (nrfA) exhibited upregulations of $3 3 6 . 1 \%$ , $5 7 6 . 9 \%$ , and $4 3 3 . 8 \%$ , respectively, following foliar application of Se ENMs compared to the RF (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). In contrast, the expression of denitrificationrelated functional genes (norZ2, norB, nirK, narK, and napA) in rhizosphere soil was markedly suppressed following Se ENMs application in comparison with the RF group (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S8). These results suggest that Se ENMs promoted soil ammonification and nitrification processes while concurrently inhibiting denitrification. Under reduced N fertilizer conditions, Se ENMs significantly increased plant-available N in the soil, with increases of $\bar { 1 } 4 . 8 \%$ for $\mathrm { N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N and $1 5 . 1 \%$ for $\mathrm { N O _ { 3 } } ^ { - }$ -N (Fig. 4C). Simultaneously, the gene families nitrate transporters (NRTs) and ammonium transporters (AMTs), responsible for N absorption and transport in rice roots, were also significantly up-regulated by Se ENMs (Fig. 4B). 

Compared to RF treatment group, the expression levels of OsNRT2.1 , OsNRT2.2 , OsNRT2.4 , and OsNRT8.2 were up-regulated by $4 3 4 . 6 \%$ , $2 9 8 . 2 \%$ , $5 3 4 . 6 \%$ , and $1 3 4 . 9 \%$ in Se ENMs application, respectively ( Fig. 4B ). Previous studies demonstrated that OsNRT2.1 , OsNRT2.2 , OsNRT2.4 , and OsNRT8.2 are high-affinity ${ \mathrm { N O } } _ { 3 } ^ { - }$ -N transporter and functions in NUE 

improvement and $\mathrm { N O _ { 3 } } ^ { - } { ^ { - } } \mathrm { N }$ remobilization in rice ( 39 – 41 ). Additionally, the expression levels of AMT1.1 , AMT1.3 , AMT3.2 , AMT5.1 , and AMT5.2 were up-regulated by $5 0 . 3 \%$ , $6 8 . 9 \%$ $4 4 0 . 6 \%$ , $9 6 . 2 \%$ , and $6 5 . 7 \%$ in the Se ENMs, respectively, compared to RF treated ( Fig. 4B ). AMT family genes contribute to efficient $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -N uptake ( 41 – 44 ). As expected, the total N content in both the shoot and root of rice were significantly lower in RF compared to the CK, which received full fertilization; Interestingly, there were no differences between CK and $\mathrm { R F } + \mathrm { S e }$ ENMs ( Fig. 4A and SI Appendix, Fig. S9 ), which can be explained by the up-regulation of gene families (NRTs and AMTs ) that control N uptake and transport capacity, together with the well-developed rice roots and microorganisms that promote plant root absorption of soil N (RB41 , Ellin6067 , and Sphingomonas ). 

Overall, our results support that Se ENMs increased soil available N (NH + -N, $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } ^ { - } { } ^ { - } { } \mathrm { - N }$ ) content through the promotion of soil ammonification and nitrification processes, and inhibition of denitrification. The nanomaterials amplify native physiological processes–particularly photosynthetic efficiency and rhizosphere microbiome recruitment– optimizing soil N acquisition under both full and reduced fertilization regimes. Concurrently, Se ENMs significantly up-regulated the expression of genes involved in the uptake and transport of available N $\mathrm { ( N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -N, $N { \mathrm { O } } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - } { - } \mathrm { N }$ ), promoting the uptake and utilization of N by crops synergistically. 

Se ENM-Mediated Regulation of Phytohormonal Homeostasis and Tiller Initiation in Rice. The generation of tillers directly depends on the supply of N and both the generation rate and number of tillers increase with increasing plant N utilization (45, 46). The number of tillers per plant was similar in the CK and $\mathrm { R F } + \mathrm { S e }$ ENMs treatment and significantly higher than in the RF treatment (Fig. 4D). The N use efficiencies in the three treatment groups were $3 8 . 7 \%$ , $2 7 . 4 \%$ , and $4 8 . 3 \%$ , respectively, indicating that the application of Se ENMs via foliage significantly enhances the NUE of rice (Fig.  4E). It is concluded that the application of Se ENMs did not adversely affect rice tiller formation when N fertilizer application was reduced by $3 0 \%$ .

Studies have shown that enhanced N uptake and utilization by rice can activate the NGR5 (N-mediated tiller growth response 5) transcription factor, thereby promoting the N-dependent recruitment of PRC2 (polycomb repressive complex 2) and facilitating the expression of tillering genes ( 47 ). Se ENMs also inhibit the expression of genes related to gibberellin synthesis in rice, suppressing gibberellin production and subsequently weakening the binding between gibberellin and its receptor GID1 (GIBBERELLIN INSENSITIVE DWARF1). This reduces the disruption of the NGR5 proteasome by the gibberellin–GID1 complex, thereby promoting tillering in response to increased N uptake and ultimately increasing the number of effective tillers. Therefore, under conditions of reduced N input, application of Se ENMs improves tillering processes by regulating gibberellin and gene expression. 

Se ENMs Stabilize Rice Yield While Reducing Soil N Loss. The results of our field experiments showed that reducing N input by $3 0 \%$ (RF) significantly reduced rice yield $( - 2 7 . 9 \%$ compared with CK) and that spraying Se ENMs prevented this loss in yield (Fig.  4F). In detail, spraying Se ENMs increased the effective tiller number, grain number, and thousand grain weight by $3 . 0 \%$ , $1 1 . 6 \%$ , and $3 . { \dot { 9 } } \%$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S11), respectively, compared to the RF treatment. A field study further confirmed that reducing N fertilizer input while spraying Se ENMs allowed to maintain levels of productivity equivalent to the full fertilization scheme $( 2 7 0 \mathrm { k g } { \cdot } \mathrm { h } \mathrm { \dot { a } } ^ { - 1 }$ , SI Appendix, Fig. S12). Besides, decreasing N input significantly reduced the quality of rice (i.e. lower content of starch, protein, and amino acids in rice grains, Fig.  5 A and $B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S13), Spraying Se ENMs also maintained rice quality to the levels of full fertilization (Fig. $5 A$ and $B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S13). In addition, the Se content in rice grain treated with Se ENMs was increased by $2 8 6 . 7 \%$ compared to CK (Fig.  5C), up to $3 9 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { ~ 1 0 0 ~ g } ,$ which meets the Chinese standard for Se enrichment in rice. The results from the largerscale field experiments confirmed that Se $\mathrm { E N M _ { S } }$ improved total starch, crude protein, amino acids, and Se content compared to the RF (SI Appendix, Fig. S14). Overall, our results from two field 

experiments suggested that spraying Se ENMs allows reducing the N fertilizer input in rice production, maintaining yield, and improving rice quality. 

Decreasing input of N fertilizers by $3 0 \%$ reduced $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ , $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ and $\mathrm { C H _ { 4 } }$ emissions by $1 3 . 2 \%$ , $1 9 . 7 \%$ , and $1 9 . 6 \%$ , when compared with CK. Emissions were further reduced by $1 4 . 1 \%$ , $4 . 3 \%$ , and $3 1 . 4 \%$ in the plots that received Se ENMs ( Fig. $5 D$ ). The primary reason for $\mathrm { C H _ { 4 } }$ emission mitigation was the significant increase in the abundance of methanotrophs (Rokubacteriales and Latescibacterota ) in soil. Specifically, upon exposure to Se ENMs, their abundances increased by $4 5 . 5 \%$ and $1 0 . 7 \%$ , respectively, compared to the CK (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). These microorganisms can effectively oxidize $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ in the soil, thereby inhibiting its emission ( 9 ). The reduction of $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ emissions was in part related to the decrease of N fertilizer application, and in part related to Se ENMs that increased the utilization rate of N fertilizer by promoting beneficial microorganisms that fixed N in soil (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ), up-regulating gene expression related to N transformation (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ), improving the efficiency of N absorption and utilization in rice ( Fig. 4 ). Meanwhile, inhibiting the expression of denitrification genes was also important driving factors for $\mathrm { C H _ { 4 } }$ emission reduction ( 48 ). 

Compared with the N-rate strategy of previous studies ( 49 ), the application of nanotechnology can reduce the total amount of reactive N emission by $3 9 . 9 \%$ , which is higher than the reported $2 4 \%$ reduction in the N-rate strategy. In summary, the developed Se ENMs treatment demonstrates great potential to reduce N pollution while maintaining rice productivity and improving quality.

Future Implications. Through field experiments on fertilizer reduction, it was proved that Se ENMs can enhance photosynthesis, regulate rice rhizosphere processes, subsequently improve the utilization efficiency of N fertilizer, maintain rice yields, improve the rice quality (Se content), and elucidate the specific mechanism underlying the above-ground and subsurface cooperative N fixation facilitated by Se ENMs. Further, our data demonstrate that the application of Se ENMs could lead to substantial environmental benefits not only associated with the decrease in N-fertilizer use but also lower energy consumption and GHG emissions. Our analysis also demonstrated that the proposed technology is economically viable in terms of farmers’ income and associated with lower cost and simpler implementation compared with other 4R technologies. Future priorities include developing scalable Se ENM formulations for commercial deployment, validating efficacy across diverse edaphic conditions, crop systems, and climate regimes, and conducting OECD-compliant toxicology studies to establish environmental and human safety profiles for regulatory submissions (EPA/FDA). In view of the very promising results generated in this study, we hope that support from authorities, research organization, and industry can be integrated and allow the further development of nano-agriculture technologies that can promote produce quantity and quality, while reducing the environmental footprint of agriculture.

# Experimental Design.

The cultivation management of rice field. The field experiment was conducted in Xuanwu, Nanjing, Jiangsu Province, China $( 3 2 ^ { \circ } 0 4 ^ { \prime }$ N, 118°88′ E) in 2023. The field verification experiment was in Kunshan, Suzhou, Jiangsu Province, China (31°38′ N, $1 2 1 ^ { \circ } 0 5 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { E }$ ) in 2024. The basic physical and chemical properties of the soil used for rice planting are shown in SI Appendix, Tables S2 and S3. The japonica rice variety used in this study is Nanjing 46. This experiment consisted of three treatments, including the control group (CK), reduced N fertilizer application by $3 0 \%$ (RF), and reduced N fertilizer application and spraying Se ENMs $( { \mathsf { R F } } + { \mathsf { S e } }$ ENMs). First, the pregerminated seeds were grown on the seedbed fo $2 8 \mathsf { d } ,$ , and then transplanted into the field. There were four parallel experimental groups for each treatment, and the plot planting area for each parallel experiment was $1 4 . 8 { \mathfrak { m } } ^ { 2 }$ . The entire growth period of rice lasted from June 2023 to October 2023. The field verification experiment in Kunshan, Suzhou, Jiangsu Province, has plot planting area for each parallel experiment of $1 0 0 { \mathrm m } ^ { 2 }$ . There were three parallel experimental groups for each treatment, and the entire growth period of rice lasted from June 2024 to October 2024 (Kunshan). 

During the rice cultivation, conventional fertilization application was $2 7 0 \mathrm { k g }$ $N \ h a ^ { - 1 } , 9 0 \ k g \ P h a ^ { - 1 }$ , and $9 0  { \mathrm { k g } }  { \mathsf { K h a } } ^ { - 1 } .$ , respectively. The N fertilizer (urea) application ratio was 3:3:4 among base, tiller, and panicle stages. P and K fertilizer $( \mathsf { K H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { P O } _ { 4 }$ and $\mathsf { K } _ { 2 } 0$ ) were applied at once during the base fertilizer application, and N fertilizer was applied once at tiller and panicle stages, respectively. Fertilizer was applied according to the conventional cultivation of rice in southern Jiangsu, and the irrigation and drainage of paddy field shall refer to the conventional method of planting rice. Additionally, Se ENMs (1 g in $2 0 \lfloor \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } 0 \rfloor$ ) were sprayed at a 0.05 $\operatorname* { m g } \cdot \lfloor \bar { } ^ { - 1 }$ during tillering/panicle stages via UAV. This concentration was optimized through prior field trials. 

Collection and Determination of Rice Samples. At the tiller stage, photosynthesis parameters of rice leaves were determined using a CIRAS–3 portable gas exchange system (Hansatech, USA). The root parameters of rice were analyzed by a root scanner (WinRHIZO Pro 2017b, Canada). Carbohydrate content of rice leaves was determined with reference to previous literature. Fresh rice leaves and roots were ground with liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \complement$ until further analysis. Then, rice leaves and roots were cured at $1 0 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 min and dried at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to a constant weight. 

At mature stage, representative fields (sampling sites selected using $\mathtt { a } \ge 5$ m buffer from field edges to minimize edge effects, ensure visual homogeneity in crop density $( \pm 5 \%$ variance), and maintain absence of visible anthropogenic disturbance) were selected to measure the effective panicles per square meter, grain number per panicle, seed setting rate, and 1,000-grain weight of rice, and calculate the theoretical yield. The yield calculation of rice is as follows: Yield $( \mathsf { k g } ) =$ effective panicles $\times$ grains per panicle $\times$ setting rate $\times \ 1 . 0 0 0$ - grain weight $( \mathfrak { g } ) \times 1 0 ^ { \dot { - } 6 }$ . The actual yield of rice was determined at the final harvest. After threshing and grinding the rice, quality indicators of the rice were measured, such as crude protein, starch, and amino acid content. The Se content in rice grains was analyzed by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP–MS, iCAP–TQ, Thermo Fisher, Germany) after digestion. The N content of rice shoots and roots was measured using an elemental analyzer (UNICUBE, Germany). 

Collection and Characterization of Soil Samples. At tiller stage, the rhizosphere soil was recovered by shaking the roots to collect the soil close to the root system (0 to 5 mm) and then sieved through a 2 mm mesh. Next, the sieved soil was divided into two parts. One part was air-dried for analysis of soil dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and microorganisms, while the remaining part was stored at $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \complement$ to analyze the soil metagenomics and qPT-PCR. The diluted soil solution was used to determine DOM content via 3D-EEMs by a spectrofluorometer 

(F-7000 FL Spectrophotometer, 2819-005), excitation wavelength (Ex), and emission wavelength (Em) were set to 200 to 600 nm. The detailed extraction, measurement, and analysis of soil DOM are shown in SI Appendix, Text S2. Rhizosphere microbial sequencing details are presented in SI Appendix, Text S7. 

# Collection and Testing of $\mathsf { N H } _ { 3 } ,$ , ${ \aleph } _ { 2 } \circ$ , and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ Emissions.

$N H _ { 3 }$ gas collection and testing. $N \mathsf { H } _ { 3 }$ was determined using field in situ measurement aeration method (50). This method consisted of a PVC plastic pipe with a diameter of 10 cm and a height of $2 5 \ \mathsf { c m }$ , and two pieces of sponge with a diameter of 10 cm and a thickness of 2 cm. Sponges were soaked with 15 mL of glycerol phosphate, prepared by mixing 50 mL of phosphoric acid and $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of glycerol to a volume of 1,000 mL. During the experiment, the lower layer sponge was used to absorb ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ evaporated from farmland soil, while the upper layer sponge absorbed ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ from the air and prevented it from entering the device and being absorbed by the lower layer sponge. ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ absorbed in the lower sponge layer was extracted with $1 . 0 \dot { \mathrm { m } } 0 { | \cdot | } ^ { - 1 }$ KCI solution, and ${ N H _ { 4 } } ^ { + }$ -N was analyzed by a continuous flow analyzer (Skalar $\mathsf { S a n } { + } +$ , Netherlands) in the extraction solution ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ was collected every day in the base, tiller, and panicle fertilizer stage for a week. The calculation formula for ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ volatilization is 

$$
F = \frac {\mathsf {C} \times V \times 1 0 ^ {- 6} \times 1 0 ^ {4}}{\mathsf {A} \times \mathsf {D} _ {\mathrm {i}} \times \mathsf {R}},
$$

where F is the ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ emission flux $( k g \cdot h \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { d } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), C is the amount of $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ -N in the extract $( \boldsymbol { \mathrm { m g \cdot L } } ^ { - \dagger } ) , \vee$ is the volume of the extract (mL), A is the effective area of the ammonia sponge $( \mathsf { m } ^ { 2 } ) ,$ , Di is the actual accumulated absorption time of ${ \mathsf { N H } } _ { 3 }$ by the device during the i-th sampling, and R is the $N H _ { 3 }$ recovery of the $N \mathsf { H } _ { 3 }$ volatilization capture device, which is set at $1 0 0 \%$ . 

$N _ { 2 } O$ and $C H _ { 4 }$ gas collection and testing. Four bases of 50 cm diameter made of cylindrical PVC material were buried in the soil for each treatment group. ${ \Nu } _ { 2 } 0$ and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ gas collection was performed on days 2, 4, 6 of the fertilization periods. The sampling device was a cylindrical PVC material with a diameter of 50 cm and a height of 120 cm. The sampling device was embedded in the base during gas collection to ensure its airtightness. After 10 min of airtight environment, the gas samples were then collected. A 100 mL syringe was used for gas collection and four gas samples were collected at 0, 10, 20, and 30 min. The concentrations of ${ \Nu } _ { 2 } 0$ and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ in the gas samples were determined using an Agilent 7890 gas chromatograph (USA), and the emission fluxes of ${ \Nu } _ { 2 } 0$ and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ were obtained by linear regression analysis of the concentration values of the four gas samples. The cumulative emissions of ${ \Nu } _ { 2 } 0$ and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ during the entire growth period of rice were obtained by integration. The emission fluxes of ${ \Nu } _ { 2 } 0$ and $\mathsf { C H } _ { 4 }$ are calculated as follows (51): 

$$
F = \frac {\rho \times h \times d C / d t \times 2 7 3}{2 7 3 + T},
$$

where F denotes the emission flux $( \mathsf { m g . m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { h } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { f o r N } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } , \mathsf { C H } _ { 4 } ) ; \mathsf { \rho }$ is the gas density in the standard state of the gas $( k g \cdot m ^ { = 3 } ) :$ h is the effective height of the sampling box (m); dC/dt denotes the rate of change of the concentration of the measured gas in the box; 273 is the parameter of the gas equation; and T is the average temperature inside the box at the time of sampling $( ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ . 

Calculation of NUE. NUE is calculated by the balance method (52), and the calculation formula is as follows: 

$$
N U E _ {b a l a} = \frac {N _ {1}}{N _ {2}} \times 100 \%
$$

where $\mathsf { N } _ { 1 }$ denotes total N uptake by crops, and $\mathsf { N } _ { 2 }$ denotes total N input.
