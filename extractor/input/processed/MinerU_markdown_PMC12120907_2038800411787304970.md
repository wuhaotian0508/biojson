# Summary

Soil salinity is a severe abiotic stress that damages plant growth and development. As an antioxidant and free radical scavenger, melatonin is well known for helping plants survive abiotic conditions, including salinity stress. Here, we report that the salt-related gene MsSNAT1, encoding a rate-limiting melatonin biosynthesis enzyme, is located in the chloroplast and contributes to salinity stress tolerance in alfalfa. We found that the MsSNAT1 overexpressing alfalfa lines exhibited higher endogenous melatonin levels and increased tolerance to salt stress by promoting antioxidant systems and improving ion homeostasis. Furthermore, through a combination of transcriptome sequencing, dual-luciferase assays and transgenic analysis, we identified that the basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor, MsbZIP55, is associated with salt response and MsSNAT1 expression. EMSA analysis and ChIP-qPCR uncovered that MsbZIP55 can recognize and directly bind to the MsSNAT1 promoter in vitro and in vivo. MsbZIP55 acts as a negative regulator of MsSNAT1 expression, thereby reducing melatonin biosynthesis. 

Morphological analysis revealed that overexpressing MsbZIP55 conferred salt sensitivity to transgenic alfalfa through a higher ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + } / { \mathsf { K } } ^ { + }$ ratio and lower antioxidant activities, which could be alleviated by applying exogenous melatonin. Silencing of MsbZIP55 by RNA interference in alfalfa resulted in higher expression of MsSNAT1 and promoted salt tolerance by enhancing the antioxidant system enzyme activities and ion homeostasis. Our findings indicate that the MsbZIP55-MsSNAT1 module plays a crucial role in regulating melatonin biosynthesis in alfalfa while facilitating protection against salinity stress. These results shed light on the regulatory mechanism of melatonin biosynthesis related to the salinity stress response in alfalfa. 

# Introduction

Soil salinity is a severe abiotic stress worldwide that hampers plant growth and development, causing reductions in crop yield (van Zelm et al., 2020). Salt stress triggers osmotic stress, ionic toxicity and the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), all of which inhibit plant photosynthesis, cause metabolic dysfunction, lead to growth arrest and even death (Zhao et al., 2020). Plants have developed various complex mechanisms to adapt to this adverse environment, such as enhancing detoxification through elevated salt-related protein activities and stress signal transduction (Miller et al., 2010; Zhu, 2002). ROS, including hydrogen peroxide, superoxide anions, hydroxyl radicals and singlet oxygen, are by-products of plant cellular metabolism and accumulate in plants under salinity stress conditions (Miller et al., 2010; Thorpe et al., 2013). Plants have enzymic and non-enzymic antioxidant systems to scavenge cellular ROS and alleviate oxidative damage. The antioxidant enzymes include superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), glutathione reductase (GR) and ascorbic acid peroxidase (APX) (Kostopoulou et al., 2015). Non-enzymatic antioxidants include ascorbic acid, flavonoids, vitamin E and other chemicals (Gu et al., 2022). Melatonin is a well-known free oxygen radical scavenger that 

neutralizes ROS, which is conserved in animals and plants (Back, 2021; Reiter et al., 2016). 

Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytrypamine) is an indoleamine synthesized from tryptophan and produced by animals, bacteria and plants (Hernandez-Ruiz et al., 2023). In plants, a variety of advances in exploring the roles of melatonin functions as a protective agent under abiotic stresses have been made since its discovery in 1995 (Arnao and Hernandez-Ruiz, 2021; Hattori et al., 1995; Yang et al., 2022b). During melatonin biosynthesis, serotonin is N-acetylated by serotonin N-acetyltransferase (SNAT) and then converted into melatonin by acetylserotonin methyltransferase (ASMT) (Byeon et al., 2014a). In plants, serotonin can also be converted into 5-methoxytryptamine by ASMT/COMT to produce melatonin after SNAT (Back, 2021). Melatonin biosynthesis-related genes have been characterized in different plants, such as SlTDC2, SlT5H, SlSNAT, SlCOMT2, SlASMT5 and SlASMT7 in tomato, and OsTDC, OsT5H, OsSNAT and OsASMT in rice (Byeon et al., 2014b; Park et al., 2013). Melatonin is reported to be catabolized into 2- hydroxymelatonin, cyclic-3-hydroxymelatonin and $N ^ { 1 }$ -Acetyl- $\cdot N ^ { 2 }$ -formyl-5-methoxykynurenamine (Hardeland, 2017; Yu et al., 2018). 

In recent years, the functions of melatonin in plants have been widely investigated. Several research groups have reported that melatonin is a versatile molecule and affects nearly all aspects of the 

plant life cycle (Arnao and Hernandez-Ruiz, 2019), from seed germination (Tiryaki and Keles, 2012), root morphogenesis (Pelagio-Flores et al., 2012), seedling growth (Yang et al., 2022a), development to flowering (Zhang et al., 2019) and fruit ripening (Cao et al., 2022). Melatonin is also proposed as a new plant hormone that participates in multiple physiological processes (Arnao and Hernandez-Ruiz, 2019). Notably, it has been widely reported that melatonin plays a vital role as an antioxidant in plants and responds to various biotic and abiotic stresses, such as heat (Deng et al., 2022), cold (Li et al., 2016), salt (Cao et al., 2024), drought (Zou et al., 2021), heavy metal (Li et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2022) and pathogen invasions (Zeng et al., 2022). 

Melatonin is crucial and indispensable for increasing stress tolerance, especially salinity tolerance, in diverse plant species. Exogenous application on plants could boost endogenous melatonin levels to improve plant growth and promote salt stress tolerance (Khan et al., 2022). In grapevines, melatonin enhances salt tolerance by promoting MYB108A-mediated ethylene biosynthesis (Xu et al., 2019). Furthermore, under stress conditions, endogenous melatonin levels and the transcripts of C-repeat binding factor/Dehydration-responsive element-binding protein 1 increase, leading to an increase in the transcript levels of multiple stress-responsive genes (COR15A, RD22 and KIN1), resulting in improved resistance to salt, drought and cold (Shi et al., 2015). Transcriptome analysis using RNA sequencing in cucumber with or without melatonin under salt stress revealed that many transcription factors (TF), including WRKY, MYB, NAC and the ethylene-responsive TFs, were differentially expressed (Zhang et al., 2014). These results indicate that melatonin increases the salt tolerance of plants by up-regulating the expression of related transcription factors. It has been reported that MeWRKY79 and MeHSF20 up-regulated the expression of MeASMT2, while MeRAV1 and MeRAV2 down-regulated the expression of MeTDC2, MeT5H and MeASMT1 to improve disease resistance in cassava (Wei et al., 2017, 2018). In tomato, Hsf1a binds to the promoter of COMT1, leading to its up-regulation and melatonin accumulation, which confers plant cadmium tolerance (Cai et al., 2017). Despite the roles of melatonin biosynthesis-related genes in plant growth regulations and stress tolerance, the upstream regulation of melatonin biosynthesis remains largely unknown. 

Alfalfa (Medicago sativa L.) is an essential perennial legume with extensive cultivation and wide distribution in the world (Yang et al., 2023). It is widely known as the ‘queen of forages’ due to its excellent yield and high feeding value (Wang and Zhang, 2023). However, soil salinity is a major abiotic stress that limits the yield and quality of alfalfa (Peel et al., 2023). Previous studies have found that application of melatonin improved salt tolerance in alfalfa (Cen et al., 2020). Nevertheless, the regulatory pathway that governs melatonin biosynthesis in alfalfa remains unclear. Our findings indicate that MsSNAT1 positively influences salinity stress tolerance in alfalfa by modulating melatonin biosynthesis. We further demonstrate that MsbZIP55 down-regulates the transcript levels of MsSNAT1, acting as a negative regulator under salt stress in alfalfa. 

# Results

# MsSNAT1 responds to salt stress in alfalfa

Melatonin acts as an antioxidant, protecting plants from abiotic stresses. We have previously shown that exogenous melatonin treatment improves salt tolerance in alfalfa, but the underlying mechanism remains unclear (Cen et al., 2020). Serotonin 

N-acetyltransferase (SNAT) is the rate-limiting enzyme in plant melatonin biosynthesis and determines melatonin levels (Back et al., 2016). To isolate SNATs in alfalfa, the amino acid sequences of SNATs from Arabidopsis and rice were retrieved from the NCBI GenBank and used to perform a BLAST search against the alfalfa protein database. This search identified MsG0780039449 as the alfalfa homologue of AtSNAT1 and OsSNAT1, which we named MsSNAT1 (Figure S1a). MsSNAT1 exhibits $6 3 . 5 7 \%$ and $5 9 . 4 5 \%$ amino acid (aa) identity with the SNAT1 proteins found in Arabidopsis and rice, respectively. 

To evaluate whether MsSNAT1 responds to salt, we first conducted RT-qPCR to analyse the changes in MsSNAT1 transcripts under salt treatment. 10-day-old alfalfa cv. ‘Zhongmu No. 1’ seedlings were transferred into a 100 mM NaCl salt solution and then harvested over a time course for examination. MsSNAT1 transcripts were induced, reaching a peak 6 h after salt treatment and then returning to pretreatment levels after $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ (Figure S1b), indicating that MsSNAT1 responds to salt stress. 

# Overexpressing MsSNAT1 increases salt tolerance in alfalfa

To characterize the function of MsSNAT1 in alfalfa, we examined its expression patterns and subcellular location. Roots and shoots of 10-day-old seedlings, single leaves, mature leaves, stems, flowers and nodules were harvested for RNA extraction. RT-qPCR revealed that MsSNAT1 was expressed in all detected tissues, with significantly higher levels in mature leaves and stems (Figure 1a). Next, green fluorescent protein (GFP) was fused to the C-terminus of MsSNAT1, and the construct was infiltrated into tobacco leaves for a transient expression assay to investigate its subcellular location. MsSNAT1 was mainly targeted to the chloroplast (Figure 1b) and was also localized to the nucleus and cytoplasm (Figure S2). 

To investigate the role of MsSNAT1 under salt conditions, we created transformants that overexpress MsSNAT1 through agro-mediated transformation. Two independent MsSNAT1 overexpression lines (MsSNAT1-OE) were selected for further phenotyping and physiological analysis. These lines were derived from 16 independent transgenic plants exhibiting higher expression levels than WT (wild type) (Figure S3). The relative expression levels of MsSNAT1 in MsSNAT1-OE#3 and MsSNAT1- OE#10 were 41.50-fold and 34.24-fold higher than in WT, respectively (Figure 1c). Since MsSNAT1 is the rate-limiting enzyme in the melatonin biosynthesis pathway, we next measured the endogenous melatonin content in alfalfa MsSNAT1 overexpression lines. Melatonin content in MsSNAT1-OE#3 and MsSNAT1-OE#10 was approximately 1.11-fold and 2-fold higher than in WT, respectively (Figure 1d). This indicates that increased MsSNAT1 transcript levels enhance the melatonin accumulation in alfalfa. 

Next, we analysed the phenotypes of the MsSNAT1-OE lines before and after salt treatment. Morphologically, there was no apparent difference between the WT and MsSNAT1-OE under normal conditions (Figure 1e). However, under salt stress, the WT plants displayed noticeable yellowing and wilting of leaves compared with those of the MsSNAT1-OE lines (Figure 1f,g), indicating that overexpressing MsSNAT1 enhances salt tolerance. In alignment with the phenotype, the MsSNAT1-OE lines showed higher chlorophyll content than the WT under salt treatment (Figure 2a). Under normal conditions, the activities of CAT, POD and Cu/Zn-SOD were similar in the WT and MsSNAT1-OE. We found that the MDA content and EL displayed no differences between WT and MsSNAT1-OE under control conditions. Still, after salt stress, the MsSNAT1-OE lines showed decreased MDA and EL compared with the wild type (Figure 2e,f), suggesting overexpressing MsSNAT1 in alfalfa could promote the antioxidant systems under salt stress conditions. 

Maintaining cellular Na+ /K+ homeostasis is an important adaptive trait that helps plants survive in saline environments. To understand whether MsSNAT1 is involved in this process, ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ 

and $\mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ levels in both the aboveground and underground parts of MsSNAT1-OE were measured. Under normal conditions, the ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ content in the leaves showed no significant difference. After salt treatment, less ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ accumulated in the MsSNAT1-OE than in WT, while ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ levels in the leaves remained unchanged, resulting in a lower ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + } / { \mathsf { K } } ^ { + }$ ratio in the MsSNAT1-OE lines (Figure 2g–i). We also noted a similar situation in the roots: ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ content decreased in the roots of MsSNAT1-OE under salt stress, while $\mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ content showed no significant difference compared with WT, resulting in a similarly lower Na+ /K+ ratio in the roots of MsSNAT1-OE (Figure 2j–l). The plasma membrane $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { H } ^ { + }$ antiporter Salt Overly Sensitive 1 (SOS1) and the sodium hydrogen exchanger 1 (NHX1) are well studied for their roles in protecting plants from excessive ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation by transporting ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ from the cytoplasm to the apoplast and by facilitating vacuolar partitioning of ${ N a } ^ { + }$ , respectively. RT-qPCR results displayed that MsSOS1 and MsNHX1 transcripts were up-regulated in the MsSNAT1-OE lines after salt treatment (Figure S5). These results indicated that MsSNAT1 participates in ion homeostasis and positively regulates alfalfa salt tolerance. 

To confirm that the up-regulated melatonin biosynthesis resulted from the increased expression of MsSNAT1, we knocked down the transcripts of MsSNAT1 using RNA interference (RNAi) technology. Eleven independent transgenic lines were created, 

and MsSNAT1 transcripts were down-regulated in seven MsSNAT1-RNAi lines (Figure S6a,b). Two independent MsSNAT1-RNAi lines (MsSNAT1-RNAi #10 and #14) exhibited the lowest expression levels and were selected for further analysis. We measured the melatonin content, and the results indicated that lower melatonin accumulated in the MsSNAT1- RNAi lines compared with WT (Figure S6c), suggesting that MsSNAT1 plays a crucial role in melatonin biosynthesis in alfalfa. Furthermore, morphological observations of the two independent MsSNAT1-RNAi lines revealed more pronounced chlorosis and leaf wilting than WT under salt conditions (Figure S6d,e). These findings suggest that MsSNAT1 is involved in melatonin synthesis and positively regulates salt tolerance in alfalfa.

Meanwhile, a $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) reporter system designed to mimic the LUC reporter using the same MsSNAT1 promoter confirmed the LUC detection results; co-expressing the MsG0780041366 effector resulted in elevated proMsSNAT1: GUS staining activity, while the MsG0880043078 and MsG0280006603 effectors resulted in reduced GUS activity compared with the GFP control (Figure S7c). We then generated transgenic plants overexpressing each of MsG0880043078, MsG0280006603 and MsG0780041366 in alfalfa and analysed the expression levels of MsSNAT1 in these transgenic plants using RT-qPCR. Five independent transgenic lines with higher expression levels of either MsG0780041366 or MsG0280006603 were obtained. Thirteen independent lines were generated for MsG0880043078 overexpression, and MsSNAT1 transcripts were

This suggests that MsG0880043078 overexpression decreases melatonin content by inhibiting the melatonin biosynthetic enzyme MsSNAT1. 

We constructed a phylogenetic tree using the full-length amino acid sequence of alfalfa MsG0880043078 and the Arabidopsis protein database. This phylogenetic analysis revealed that MsG0880043078 is the closest homologue of AtbZIP55, which we named MsbZIP55 (Figure S10). The subcellular localization of the MsbZIP55-GFP fusion protein, transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves, indicated that MsbZIP55 is primarily localized in the nucleus (Figure S11a), consistent with the characteristics of a transcription factor. To explore the role of MsbZIP55 in response to salt stress, we examined the MsbZIP55 expression under 100 mM NaCl treatment. Indeed, MsbZIP55 transcripts were significantly induced, peaking 3 h after salt treatment (Figure S11b), suggesting that MsbZIP55 responds to salt.

# Discussion

Soil salinization is becoming one of the most severe threats to agriculture worldwide. Melatonin, discovered in 1995, has been widely validated as a multifunctional regulatory molecule in plant growth and various stresses, particularly for its crucial role in enhancing plant salt tolerance (Li et al., 2019; Zhan et al., 2019). In plants, melatonin is synthesized through two pathways: mitochondrial and chloroplastic. This process involves key enzymes, such as tryptophan decarboxylase (TDC), tryptamine 5-hydroxylase (T5H), serotonin N-acetyltransferases (SNATs), Nacetylserotonin-O-methyltransferases (ASMTs) and caffeoyl Omethyltransferase (COMT) (Tan and Reiter, 2020). Mitochondria and chloroplasts are essential for melatonin production, and SNAT is a crucial enzyme in chloroplasts (Back et al., 2016; Tan et al., 2013; Tan and Reiter, 2020). In this study, we isolated and cloned the MsSNAT1 gene from alfalfa. The subcellular localization indicated that MsSNAT1 is located in chloroplasts (Figure 1b), consistent with the rice SNAT1 (Byeon et al., 2014a), cucumber CsSNAT (Qi et al., 2020), apple MzSNAT1 (Wang et al., 2017) and grape VvSNAT (Wu et al., 2021). We demonstrated that overexpression of MsSNAT1 promotes melatonin accumulation in alfalfa (Figure 1d). Conversely, alfalfa MsSNAT1-RNAi exhibited the opposite effect (Figure S6c). This finding supports previous reports in Arabidopsis (Yao et al., 2021), cucumber (Qi et al., 2020) and tomato SlSNAT (Wang et al., 2020). These results indicate that MsSNAT1 participates in melatonin biosynthesis through the chloroplast pathway in alfalfa. 

The significant saline effects on plants in the early stages include osmotic stress and ion toxicity. Several studies have shown that the external application or natural production of melatonin helps alleviate salt stress mainly by improving ion balance (Li et al., $2 0 2 2 a .$ ; Nie et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021). Applying exogenous melatonin helps plants tolerate salt better by reducing ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation in rice (Li et al., 2017). Increased endogenous melatonin production by up-regulating the synthesis-related gene SNAT significantly enhances salt tolerance through elevated antioxidant enzymes and improved photochemical efficiency (Wu et al., 2021). It is well known that maintaining the balance of sodium and potassium ions in the cytoplasm positively affects plant salt tolerance. We found that the $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ ratio in the leaves and roots of MsSNAT1-OE lines decreased (Figure 2i,l). Previous research has demonstrated that the ectopic expression of the alfalfa MsSNAT gene imparts salt stress tolerance in transgenic Arabidopsis by sustaining ion homeostasis (Zhao et al., 2019). In this study, we demonstrate that MsSNAT1 is negatively regulated by MsbZIP55 and MsbZIP55-RNAi lines exhibited salt tolerance and a reduced ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + } / { \mathsf { K } } ^ { + }$ ratio similar to those of the MsSNAT1-OE (Figures 1 and 7). NHX proteins mainly facilitate the exchange of ${ N a } ^ { + } \left( K ^ { + } \right)$ for $\mathsf { H } ^ { + }$ across membranes, and PvNHX-transgenic plants exhibited enhanced salt tolerance compared with WT (Huang et al., 2017). Here, we indicate that MsNHX1 was significantly up-regulated in the MsSNAT1-OE and MsbZIP55-RNAi lines after salt treatment (Figures S5b and S18b), suggesting that MsSNAT1 may enhance $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } ~ ( \mathsf { K } ^ { + } ) / \mathsf { H } ^ { + }$ antiporter activity by promoting the expression of the NHX gene, thus contributing to the maintenance of ion homeostasis under salt 

stress. The SOS pathway is a critical regulatory system for maintaining ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ and ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ homeostasis under salt conditions (Zhu, 2002). The expression levels of SOS1 were up-regulated in MsSNAT1-OE and MsbZIP55-RNAi plants compared with those in the WT (Figures S5a and S18a). These results indicate that increased ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ efflux and compartmentalization, such as sequestration into the vacuole, are two crucial strategies for plants’ melatonin-dependent salinity tolerance response to survive in challenging saline environments. 

Salt stress increases the accumulation of ROS, resulting in oxidative stress. Melatonin may help alleviate this stress because of its established antioxidant properties in animals and plants (Kolodziejczyk et al., 2021). A meta-analysis involving over 30 research groups revealed that the external application of melatonin significantly enhances the activities of the ROSscavenging enzymes SOD, POD, CAT and APX (Muhammad et al., 2022). These findings indicate that melatonin-induced improvement of antioxidant activity is the primary underlying reason for enhancing plant abiotic stress tolerance. Increasing endogenous melatonin biosynthesis has been shown to stimulate the antioxidant capacity in Arabidopsis, grape and apple (Chen et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2021). We observed that melatonin levels depend on the dosage of MsSNAT1 expression (Figures 1c, 3g and 7b). Independent MsSNAT1-OE lines showed significantly improved activities of antioxidant enzymes (CAT, POD and Cu/Zn-SOD), leading to higher chlorophyll content and reduced levels of MDA and EL, resulting in enhanced salt tolerance (Figure 1, Figures S4 and S5). Overexpression and RNA interference (RNAi) of MsbZIP55 in transgenic alfalfa plants exhibited opposing effects on MsSNAT1 transcripts, phenotypes and physiological indices. The MsbZIP55 overexpression lines displayed poorer salt tolerance than the WT control, while the MsbZIP55-RNAi lines demonstrated better salt tolerance (Figures 5 and 7). This indicates that MsbZIP55 acts as a negative regulator of salt stress tolerance by transcriptionally repressing MsSNAT1, which leads to reduced melatonin levels, resulting in decreased antioxidant activity and impaired ion homeostasis. These results contradict the transcripts that indicated MsbZIP55 was induced by salt conditions (Figure S11b), suggesting there may be modifications in protein levels that require further investigation through screening for interaction proteins in future research. 

Previous studies have indicated that the transcripts of vital catalytic enzymes responsible for melatonin biosynthesis were enhanced by salt treatment in various species, such as tomato SlCOMT1 (Liu et al., 2019), watermelon ClCOMT1 (Chang et al., 2021) and grape VvSNAT1 (Wu et al., 2021). These reports suggest that transcriptional regulation is vital during responses to salt stress. However, the upstream regulator of melatoninproducing enzymes remains unclear. Here, we found that MsSNAT1 was quickly up-regulated under 100 mM NaCl treatment (Figure S1b), consistent with earlier findings. To identify the transcription factors that govern MsSNAT1 expression, we employed transcriptome sequencing, dual-luciferase assays and transgenic plants, leading us to identify MsbZIP55 as a candidate (Figure 3a–c, Figure S8). EMSA and ChIP-qPCR analysis confirmed that MsbZIP55 can recognize and bind to promoter fragments containing the G-box of MsSNAT1 (Figure 4b,d). Furthermore, we found that exogenous melatonin application alleviates the effects of salt in MsbZIP55-OE alfalfa lines (Figure 6). This study contributes to the existing literature on the interplay between bZIP transcription factors and melatonin 

levels, as evidenced by HY5, a bZIP transcription factor family member, which directly represses AtSNAT6 expression and reduces melatonin’s role in promoting cotyledon opening (Wang et al., 2022). 

# Conclusion

In this study, we demonstrated that MsSNAT1 and MsbZIP55 are induced under salt conditions. Alfalfa lines overexpressing MsSNAT1 exhibited higher endogenous melatonin levels, leading to enhanced tolerance to salt stress by promoting antioxidant systems and maintaining ion balance. Further biochemical analysis revealed that MsbZIP55 functions as a negative regulator by directly binding to the MsSNAT1 promoter to repress its expression, which decreases melatonin levels and salt tolerance. In conclusion, our findings indicate that the MsbZIP55–MsSNAT1 module is crucial in regulating melatonin biosynthesis in alfalfa in response to salinity stress. These results provide new insights into the mechanisms of melatonin biosynthesis in plant responses to salt stress and could potentially be used in future agricultural practices to develop salt-tolerant alfalfa varieties in saline environments. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The study utilized a genotype of alfalfa known as ‘Zhongmu No. 1’. All alfalfa plants were grown under controlled conditions at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a light cycle of $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of light and 8 h of darkness in the growth chamber and greenhouse. 

Seeds of ‘Zhongmu No. 1’ were placed on moistened filter paper in a dish at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for germination. After 4 days, the seedlings were transferred into plastic containers filled with 1/4 Hoagland solution. Ten days following transplanting, the seedlings were treated with 0 or 100 mM NaCl in 1/4 Hoagland solution. The solutions were changed every 5 days during salt treatment. 

# Vector construction and alfalfa transformation

The full-length coding sequences (CDS) of MsSNAT1 and MsbZIP55 were cloned from the ‘Zhongmu No. 1’ alfalfa. For the overexpression constructs, the CDS for each of MsSNAT1 and MsbZIP55 was inserted into the pMDC32 vector driven by the CaMV35S promoter. To knock down the expression of MsbZIP55, a 200 bp specific fragment of the MsbZIP55 CDS was amplified and transferred into the pK7GWIWG2D(II) vector via attL/attR recombination reactions (Invitrogen). All primers used in this study are listed in Table S1. 

All recombinant vectors were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 through freeze–thaw transformation and then used to transform the ‘Zhongmu No. 1’ alfalfa leaf explants to obtain transgenic alfalfa. 

# Subcellular localization

For subcellular localization of MsSNAT1 and MsbZIP55, the CDS without stop codons were fused to GFP at the C-terminus and inserted individually into the pMDC83 vector. The fusion constructs (35S: MsSNAT1/MsbZIP55-GFP) and the control vector (35S: GFP) were transfected into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV2260, then the Agrobacterium containing each construct was separately infiltrated into 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After 48–60 h, the GFP fluorescent signals were visualized using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Nikon A1, Japan). 

# GUS activities and dual-luciferase (dual-LUC) assays

The promoter region $( { \sim } 2 . 7 ~ \mathsf { k b } )$ of MsSNAT1 was cloned into the vector pMDC162 to drive GUS as the reporter construct. The 35S promoter drove the full-length CDS of MsG0780041366, MsG0880043078 and MsG0280006603 to act as the effectors in GUS activity assays. The proSuper: LUC construct was injected alongside an internal standard in every injection. These constructs were transferred into Agrobacterium strain GV2260. Agrobacterium was configured into the desired experimental combination and injected into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After 3 days, the total protein was extracted to detect the GUS and LUC activities. The GUS/LUC ratio was used to determine the promoter activity. In the dual-LUC assay, the promoter sequence of MsSNAT1 was inserted into the pGreenII0800-Luc vector to generate the reporter construct. The CDS of MsG0780041366, MsG0880043078, MsG0280006603, MsG0880042953, MsG0 280006417, MsG0380017145, MsG0880044354, MsG018000 4019, MsG0480019379, MsG0280010252 and MsG07800 39432 screened from RNA-seq were fused with GFP at the Nterminus and driven by the 35S promoter to function as effectors in dual-LUC assays. These vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101(pSoup). Tobacco leaves were infiltrated as previously described. LUC activity was assayed using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (Vazyme biotechnology company, Nanjing, China) and subjected to chemiluminescence using a fully automated imaging analysis system (Tanon 4800). 

# Phylogenetic study of MsbZIP55 and analysis of the MsSNAT1 promoter

Seventy-five previously reported AtbZIP sequences were downloaded from the Arabidopsis thaliana database (https://www. arabidopsis.org/index.jsp). Subsequently, the amino acid sequences of all AtbZIP and MsbZIP proteins were analysed using ClustalW, and a neighbour-joining tree was constructed using MEGA11 with 1000 bootstrap replicates. 2.7-kb upstream genomic sequences before ATG of MsSNAT1 were submitted to PlantCARE to predict the putative cis-elements. 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis

The total RNA was extracted using the Beijing Hua Yueyang Kit, and $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of total RNA was used for reverse transcription to synthesize cDNA samples using a PrimeScript RT Reagent kit (Takara RR047A) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RT-qPCR was performed on a qTOWER3 G (Analytik Jena, Germany) machine using the SYBR Green Supermix (Takara, RR420A). The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method. MsActin (MsG0380016789.01.T01) was amplified with the target genes as an internal control. 

# EMSA

The coding sequence of MsbZIP55 was introduced into the pCold vector to create the His-MsbZIP55 fusion plasmid, which was then transformed into Escherichia coli Rosetta (DE3) cells. Next, $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ isopropylthio- $\beta$ -galactoside (IPTG) was added to induce the expression of the His-MsbZIP55 protein at $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight. Cells were collected at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and lysed through sonication, allowing the supernatant to be harvested. The recombinant His-MsbZIP55 protein was then purified using an affinity chromatography column (Beyotime). One gram of protein was used to prepare the corresponding combination for EMSA. Biotin-labelled 

probes containing the G-box of proMsSNAT1 fragments were synthesized and labelled using the Biotin $3 ^ { \prime }$ End DNA Labeling Kit (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). Meanwhile, identical unlabelled probes served as competitors, and probes containing mutated G-box sequences acted as mutant probes. Following the manufacturer’s instructions, EMSA was performed using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). 

# Western blot analysis

To verify the expression of the MsbZIP55 protein, leaves were collected from 35S: GFP-MsbZIP55#5 seedlings and ground in liquid nitrogen. Then, total protein was extracted using an extraction buffer, isolated by SDS-PAGE and analysed by western blot using an anti-GFP antibody (Abcam, AE078). 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assays

The transgenic line 35S:GFP-MsbZIP55 was generated for ChIPqPCR assays. In brief, 2 g of whole plants were cross-linked with $1 \%$ formaldehyde under vacuum for 15 min. Glycine was added to achieve a final concentration of 0.125 M, and the samples were vacuumed for an additional 5 min. The samples were washed with water and then frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground into a fine powder. The chromatin from the samples was extracted and fragmented by sonication. The sonicated chromatin was precleared using salmon sperm-sheared DNA/protein A agarose beads. Precleared chromatin $( 5 0 ~ \mu \ L )$ was set aside as input, while the remaining supernatant was divided into two equal volumes; one was incubated with $5 ~ \mu \iota$ of anti-GFP antibody (Abcam, AE078), while the other served as a control without antibody. After an overnight incubation at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , protein A agarose beads $( 4 0 ~ \mu \ L )$ were added to each sample, and the samples were incubated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The beads were then washed sequentially with low-salt, high-salt, LiCl and TE buffers, and they were subsequently resuspended in the elution buffer, incubated at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $4 \textrm { h }$ to reverse the cross-linking. DNA was purified and diluted 10-fold, with $3 ~ \mu \iota$ of DNA used as a template for each ${ \mathsf { q P C R } }$ amplification. 

# Transcriptome analysis

For transcriptome sequencing, 2-week-old seedlings were transferred to $1 0 0 \mathrm { \ m M \ N a C l }$ in a 1/4 Hoagland solution for 3 h. Three biological replicates were performed for each treatment. All samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for RNA extraction. RNA sequencing was performed on the Illumina Hiseq Novaseq platform by Biomarker Technologies. The ‘Zhongmu No. 1’ alfalfa genome served as the reference genome. Expression levels of each annotated gene were normalized using fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments (FPKM) with StringTie, and differentially expressed genes were identified based on a threshold of fold change ${ \geq } 2$ and $\mathsf { F D R } = 0 . 0 1$ . 

# Salt stress treatment and application of exogenous melatonin

Cuttings of both transgenic and wild-type alfalfa were propagated. After 4 weeks of regrowth, well-established and uniformsized plants were transplanted into vermiculite. Following another 4 weeks, treatment was conducted by watering with either 0 or 300 mM NaCl in 1/4 Hoagland solution until the salt-related phenotype emerged. The MsSNAT1-OE, MsbZIP55-RNAi, and WT plants were treated for another 6 weeks, while the MsbZIP55-OE 

plants were treated for 4 weeks. The leaves and roots of the alfalfa plants were collected to assess physiological indicators. 

For analysing phenotypes in MsSNAT1-RNAi lines, 3-week-old plants were transplanted into vermiculite after propagation. After another week, treatment was administered by watering with either 0 or $3 0 0 \ \mathrm { m M } \ \mathsf { N a C } |$ in a 1/4 Hoagland solution until the saltrelated phenotype emerged. 

For the exogenous application of melatonin, three treatments (Control, $\mathsf { M T } + \mathsf { N a C l }$ , NaCl) were administered to 8-week-old plants. In the control group, the plants were watered with 1/4 Hoagland solution every 3 days. In the ${ \mathsf { M T } } + { \mathsf { N a C l } }$ group, the plants were sprayed with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ melatonin in a buffer solution $( 0 . 1 \%$ ethanol and SILWET77 in water) every night for 2 weeks. Afterward, they were watered with a $3 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ NaCl solution in 1/4 Hoagland solution. The NaCl group was watered with 300 mM NaCl in 1/4 Hoagland solution and sprayed with the buffer solution. All plants were treated for another 4 weeks. 

# Measurement of electrolyte leakage and chlorophyll content

Plant electrolyte leakage was measured following the previous studies (Cen et al., 2020). Fresh leaves were soaked in deionized water and agitated at room temperature for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Conductivity was then measured, and the samples were sterilized for 30 min. Afterward, the leaves were shaken for another $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ , after which conductivity was measured again. Fresh leaves were soaked in $9 5 \%$ ethanol in the dark for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ to measure chlorophyll content. The light absorption values at $6 6 5 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , 649 nm and $4 7 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ were recorded using a spectrophotometer after diluting the extraction solution five times. 

# Measurement of the antioxidant enzymes activities

CAT, POD and Cu/Zn-SOD activities were measured using the micromethod kits following the manufacturer’s instructions (Nanjing Bioengineering Institute, Nanjing, China). Generally, $0 . 2 \ 9$ of leaves was ground to a fine powder in liquid nitrogen, resuspended in 2 mL extract solution and centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 30 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The supernatant was placed on ice for testing. 

# Detection of oxidative products

The MDA content was measured using MDA Kits (Suzhou Comin Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Suzhou, China). In brief, $0 . 1 \ 9$ of samples was ground into a homogenate in liquid nitrogen with 1 mL of the extract solution. After centrifugation at 12,000 g for 15 min, $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m L }$ of the supernatant was combined with $0 . 3 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ thiobarbituric acid (TBA) and then incubated at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min. The supernatant was used to measure absorbance at 532 and 600 nm using a microplate reader. 

The $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ content was measured using the $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ kit following the manufacturer’s instructions (Nanjing Bioengineering Institute, Nanjing, China). The accumulation of $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ was assessed by histochemical staining of DAB and NBT solution in leaves. 

# Measurement of ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ and ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ content

The leaves and roots were harvested, washed with deionized water and dried at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The dried samples were then powdered and decomposed in $5 \%$ $5 \% \mathsf { H N O } _ { 3 }$ for 2 days at room temperature (Munns et al., 2010). Following the instructions, the ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ and ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ content was measured using Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectrometry (ICP-OES, 5110VDV, Malaysia). 

# Measurement of melatonin content

The leaves of the transgenic (MsSNAT1-OE, MsSNAT1-RNAi and MsbZIP55-OE) and WT plants were collected and snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The leaves from control and salt-stress MsbZIP55- RNAi lines were harvested and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. Norminkoda Biotechnology Company (Wuhan, China) extracted and detected endogenous melatonin using UPLC-MS/MS. 

# Statistical analysis

The data were analysed using one-way ANOVA with Statistical Product and Service Solutions (SPSS) 29.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY) to determine significance. t-test analysis was used for transgenic materials, with WT as control, respectively. The figures were created using GraphPad Prism version 8 software.
