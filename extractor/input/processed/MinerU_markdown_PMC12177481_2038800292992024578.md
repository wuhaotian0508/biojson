# ABSTRACT

Cold stress substantially affects plant growth and productivity. Chloroplasts are primary sites for the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and biosynthesis of the defense hormone salicylic acid (SA) under environmental stress conditions. However, the mechanisms by which plants integrate ROS and SA signaling to adapt to stress remain elusive. Here, we report that Arabidopsis FILAMENTOUS TEMPERATURE-SENSITIVE H5/YELLOW VARIEGATED1 (FtsH5/VAR1), a thylakoid-localized ATPdependent zinc metalloprotease, is essential for plant cold-stress tolerance. The var1-1 mutant exhibits pronounced chlorosis and variegation, as well as retarded growth under cold stress conditions. We observed a strong correlation between elevated SA biosynthesis/signaling and the cold-sensitive phenotype of var1. Reducing SA accumulation, either by overexpressing the salicylate hydroxylase gene (NahG) or knocking out SA biosynthesis-related genes (ICS1, EDS1, or PAD4), partially suppressed the chlorosis phenotype of var1. Furthermore, we demonstrated that EXECUTOR1 (EX1)-mediated singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ signaling acts upstream of EDS1 to regulate the expression of SA-responsive genes (SARGs) in var1 under cold stress. Notably, we identified a critical role for EX2, in which the mutation in EX2 significantly suppressed the cold-sensitive phenotype of var1, in activating the expression of SARGs while repressing photosynthesis-associated nuclear genes. Collectively, our results suggest a vital role for VAR1 in plant cold tolerance and highlight the tight connection between $\mathbf { 1 } _ { \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } }$ and SA signaling, elucidating a previously unheeded function of EX2, which likely operates independently of EX1-mediated $\mathbf { 1 } _ { \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } }$ signaling. 

Key words: VAR1, cold stress, salicylic acid, singlet oxygen, EX2

# INTRODUCTION

Temperature is one of the most significant environmental cues affecting plant growth and crop yield. Chloroplasts are endosymbiotic organelles that harbor photosynthesis and many other primary and secondary metabolic processes. Decreased ambient temperatures adversely limit the ability of plants to utilize light energy for photosynthesis and often cause photooxidative damage (Foyer and Noctor, 2008; Ding et al., 2019; Chauhan et al., 2023). Recent studies have proposed that chloroplasts act as important environmental sensors involved in plant responses to various abiotic and biotic stresses, including cold, high light intensity, and pathogen infections (Caplan et al., 2015; de Zabala et al., 2015; Medina-Puche et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022; Schwenkert et al., 2022). Proper chloroplast function requires efficient biogenesis of the thylakoid membrane and the photosynthesis-

related complexes embedded within it, as well as effective removal of damaged or ‘‘unwanted’’ subunits during developmental and environmental transitions (Gao et al., 2023). 

Among the more than 20 proteases of prokaryotic origin identified in chloroplasts, ATP-dependent zinc metalloproteases of the FILAMENTOUS TEMPERATURE-SENSITIVE H (FtsH) family are predominant within the thylakoid membrane (van Wijk, 2015; Kato and Sakamoto, 2018). In the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana (Arabidopsis), there are 12 FtsHs, nine of which are located in chloroplasts. The thylakoid-localized FtsH proteins— including FtsH1, 2, 5, and 8—assemble into a heterohexameric complex composed of type A (FtsH1 and FtsH5) and type B (FtsH2 and FtsH8) subunits (Boehm et al., 2012; Moldavski et al., 2012; Kato and Sakamoto, 2018), with FtsH2 and FtsH5 being most abundant. Mutants deficient in FtsH2 (yellow

variegated2, var2) and FtsH5 (var1) exhibit leaf variegation and are highly susceptible to photosystem II (PSII) photoinhibition under high-light stress (Sakamoto et al., 2002, 2003). Extensive research has demonstrated that VAR1 and VAR2 play fundamental roles in degrading photodamaged subunits of photosynthesis-related complexes, especially the reaction center D1 (PsbA) subunit of PSII (Lindahl et al., 2000; Zaltsman et al., 2005; Theis and Schroda, 2016). In addition to eliminating damaged photosynthetic subunits, FtsHs are involved in the biogenesis of photosynthetic complexes; for example, FtsH2/ VAR2 and FtsH5/VAR1 participate in PSI biogenesis (Jarvi et al., 2016). Previous studies also revealed roles for FtsH in plant acclimation to excessive temperature stress. FtsH6 negatively regulates thermomemory in Arabidopsis by degrading the heat shock protein HSP21 during the memory phase (Sedaghatmehr et al., 2016), whereas the Arabidopsis ftsh2 and ftsh11 mutants display cold-sensitive phenotypes (Liu et al., 2010; John et al., 2024). Taken together, these studies illustrate the diverse roles of FtsH proteases in plant development and stress adaptation. 

Under environmental stress conditions, especially high-light and cold stress, excessive light energy cannot be fully quenched photochemically through photosynthetic electron transfer (PET) or dissipated by nonphotochemical quenching (NPQ). In this context, reactive oxygen species (ROS) are inevitably generated by both photosystems, including singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ , produced by PSII), superoxide anion $( \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } \cdot - )$ , and hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ , produced primarily by PSI; Li and Kim, 2022). Although these highly reactive ROS tend to damage macromolecules and oxidize membrane lipids and proteins, they are also involved in signaling cascades that alert other subcellular compartments to combat stress (Waszczak et al., 2018). Among the different types of ROS, signaling pathways of $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ have been characterized most substantially via study of the conditional fluorescent (flu) mutant in Arabidopsis (Goggin and Fischer, 2024). Genetic screening for suppressors of the flu mutant identified a putative sensor of $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ signaling, dubbed EXECUTOR1 (EX1), that mediates plastid retrograde signaling to upregulate the expression of nuclear $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ -responsive genes (SORGs) after a dark-to-light transition (Wagner et al., 2004). A second EX1-like gene, which further enhances the changes in gene expression in flu, was also identified and dubbed EX2 (Lee et al., 2007). Degradation of the EX1 protein—which occurs in an FtsH2-dependent fashion after oxidation at the 643-tryptophan residue (Trp643)—is necessary for $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ perception (Wang et al., 2016; Dogra et al., 2019). Meanwhile, the oxidization of Trp530 and subsequent FtsH-dependent proteolysis of EX2 appear to prevent the oxidation of EX1 and antagonize EX1-dependent $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ signaling (Dogra et al., 2022). However, mutation of both EX1 and EX2 in the flu background almost completely abrogates the upregulation of all SORGs in comparison to the partial suppression by EX1 single mutation (Lee et al., 2007), adding additional mystery to the functional interaction between EX1 and EX2. 

In the flu mutant, the release of $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ triggers the activation of salicylic acid (SA)-responsive genes (SARGs; Ochsenbein et al., 2006). SA is an essential plant defense hormone that participates in plant defense against biotic and abiotic stresses. SA is produced from chorismate via two independent

pathways: chloroplast-localized isochorismate synthase (ICS) and cytosolic phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL; Peng et al., 2021). ICS1 is the major form of ICS and accounts for ${ \sim } 9 0 \%$ of SA production (Garcion et al., 2008; Rekhter et al., 2019). ENHANCED DISEASE SUSCEPTIBILITY 1 (EDS1) and its direct partner PHYTOALEXIN DEFICIENT 4 (PAD4) promote ICS1 expression and SA accumulation by activating the expression of SAR-DEFICIENT1 (SARD1) and CALMODULIN-BINDING PROTEIN60-LIKE.g (CBP60G). These two transcription activators bind to the promoters of ICS1 and other SA biosynthesisrelated genes (Jirage et al., 1999; Zhang et al., 2010; Cui et al., 2017; Dongus and Parker, 2021). Interestingly, ROS induce the accumulation of SA, and vice versa (Durner and Klessig, 1995; Leon et al., 1995; Rao et al., 1997), providing evidence for a complex regulatory loop between SA and ROS. 

In a screen of chloroplast factors potentially involved in the response of plants to low ambient temperature, we identified the mutant of ftsh5 (var1-1) as being hypersensitive to decreased temperature. In this study, we reveal that SA is overaccumulated in cold-stressed var1-1, which in turn activates signaling cascades that are partially dependent on $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ signaling via the action of EX1. Notably, we demonstrate a crucial function of EX2, independent of EX1, in promoting SA biosynthesis and repressing the expression of photosynthesis-related genes. 

# RESULTS

# The var1-1 mutant is highly susceptible to low ambient temperature

To identify chloroplast factors involved in plant adaptation to cold stress, we screened a collection of Arabidopsis mutants with mutations in genes encoding chloroplast-localized proteins. The screen was conducted under mild cold conditions ( $\mathsf { 1 2 ^ { \circ } C }$ day $\scriptscriptstyle { } ^ { \prime } 8 { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ night; hereafter referred to as cold stress), identifying the var1 (var1-1) and var2 (var2-15) mutants. Under the control condition $( 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ , the var1-1 mutant exhibited a very mild variegated phenotype compared with the wild type (WT), whereas the var2-15 mutant displayed the typical variegated phenotype as previously described (Figure 1A; Supplemental Figure 1A; Chen et al., 2000; Sakamoto et al., 2002). Under cold stress, however, the growth of the var1-1 mutant was severely retarded, showing strong chlorosis (characterized by unevenly green sectors, hereafter referred to as chlorosis), along with impaired thylakoid biogenesis (Figure 1A; Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B). Fifty days were required for var1-1 plants to reach a developmental stage (seedling size and leaf number) similar to that of WT and var2-15 plants at 30 days (Figure 1A). The true leaves of var2-15 also displayed chlorosis (Figure 1A; Supplemental Figure 1); however, seedling size was comparable to WT, indicating less severe cold sensitivity compared with var1-1. Consistent with these growth phenotypes, the maximum quantum yield (Fv/Fm) of PSII and chlorophyll contents of var1-1 and var2-15 were significantly reduced under cold stress (Figure 1B–1D). The reduced cold tolerance of the var mutants could be complemented by genetic transformation of the VAR1 or VAR2 gene driven by their native promoters into the corresponding mutants (var1-1 com and var2-15 com; Figure 1).

# Transcriptomic and proteomic analyses reveal distinct cold responses in var1-1 and var2-15

Under control conditions $( 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ , the variegated phenotype of var1 is much milder than that of var2 (Sakamoto et al., 2002); however, the var1-1 mutant is more susceptible to cold stress (Figure 1). To understand the molecular basis of this difference, we first conducted transcriptomic analyses on var1-1, var2-15, and the WT (Supplemental Dataset 1). Under control 

conditions, transcriptome correlation coefficients with WT were very high: 0.9869 for var1-1 and 0.9923 for var2- 15 (Supplemental Figure 2A). We identified 954 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in var1-1, with 507 downregulated and 447 upregulated. In var2-15, 351 DEGs were identified, of which 259 were downregulated and 92 were upregulated (Figure 2A and Supplemental Dataset 1). In contrast, the number of DEGs increased significantly under cold stress, resulting in a reduced 

correlation to the WT transcriptome (Supplemental Figure 2A). In total, 4330 genes were differentially expressed between var1-1 and WT, including 2494 downregulated and 1836 upregulated genes. For var2-15, fewer DEGs (1989 in total, 1177 downregulated and 812 upregulated) were identified under cold conditions (Figure 2A and Supplemental Dataset 1). 

We then analyzed proteomic changes in the var1-1 and var2-15 mutants using label-free quantitative mass spectrometry. Consistent with transcriptomic data, the number of differentially accumulated proteins significantly increased in both mutants under cold stress in comparison with the control conditions, with substantially more in var1-1 (515 decreased and 460 increased) than in var2-15 (232 decreased and 170 increased; Figure 2B; Supplemental Figure 2B; Supplemental Dataset 2). Consistent with the chlorosis phenotype, a large portion of the proteins with reduced accumulation were related to photosynthesis and chloroplast ribosomes (Figure 2B and Supplemental Dataset 2), indicating a vital role of VAR1 and VAR2 in establishing the chloroplast proteome during cold stress. 

Deficiency of VAR1 or VAR2 could result in the accumulation of unassembled chloroplast protein complexes, especially those related to photosynthesis, potentially triggering the chloroplast unfolded protein response (UPR). Indeed, several chloroplast-localized chaperones and chaperonins were transcriptionally upregulated in var1-1 (two DnaJ chaperones and the chaperonin CPN60A1) and var2-15 (cpHSC70-1, ClpC2, and five chaperonins; Supplemental Dataset 1). However, these transcriptional upregulations did not translate into a detectable increase in protein levels (Supplemental Dataset 2). It is possible that the small molecular weight of certain proteins (e.g., CPN10 and CPN20) makes their detection by mass spectrometry challenging or that the actual increases in protein level were very mild. These observations suggest that the deficiency of VARs likely induces a mild chloroplast UPR. 

VAR1 and VAR2 have previously been reported to specifically participate in PSI biogenesis (Jarvi et al., 2016). To determine whether reduced cold tolerance in the var mutants was associated specifically with reduced PSI, we analyzed the accumulation of PSI and PSII subunits using our mass spectrometry data (Supplemental Figure 3A). Both PSI and PSII subunits accumulated at low levels in var1-1, particularly the reaction-center subunits of PSI (PsaA, PsaB, PsaC, and PsaD) and PSII (PsbA, PsbB, PsbC, and PsbD). By contrast, the decrease in nucleus-encoded subunits, especially those of the light-harvesting complex (LHC) (e.g., LhcB1.2, LhcB1.3, LhcA1, LhcA5, and LhcA6), was less pronounced. Such imbalanced accumulation of PS core and LHC subunits possibly leads to ROS accumulation. Proteomic data were further validated by immunoblotting with specific antibodies (Supplemental Figure 3B and 3C). Interestingly, while the VAR2 protein accumulated to a comparable level between var1-1 and var2- 15, VAR1 protein levels were higher in var2-15 than in var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 3B). 

Inhibition of plastid translation through mutations of plastid translation-related factors has been reported to suppress the leaf-variegated phenotype of var1 and var2 mutants and also 

alleviate chlorosis in var2 under chilling stress (Miura et al., 2007; Yu et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2010). Moreover, treatment with a low dose of spectinomycin (a specific inhibitor of plastid translation; Pestka, 1974) suppresses the leaf-variegated phenotype of var2 under normal growth conditions (Yu et al., 2008). However, spectinomycin treatment did not alleviate chlorosis in var1-1 and var2-15 (Supplemental Figure 4A), indicating a distinct mechanism underlying their cold sensitivity when plants were initially germinated and grown under cold stress conditions. 

The FtsH metalloprotease functions as a hexamer composed of both type A (FtsH1 and FtsH5/VAR1) and type B (FtsH2/ VAR2 and FtsH8) FtsHs (Zaltsman et al., 2005). To understand the molecular mechanism underlying the more pronounced cold-sensitive phenotype of var1 compared to var2, we analyzed transcript and protein levels of different thylakoid-localized FtsHs from our transcriptomic and proteomic data (Supplemental Dataset 1 and 2). Interestingly, all four thylakoid FtsHs were transcriptionally upregulated in the WT in response to cold stress, with VAR1 being the most highly upregulated (Supplemental Figure 5A). At the protein level, VAR1 accumulation also slightly increased under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 5B). In var1-1, transcript levels were comparable (FtsH1) or slightly elevated (VAR2 and FtsH8) compared to WT, whereas protein levels decreased to $1 5 \% -$ $3 8 \%$ of WT under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 5A and 5B). In contrast, although transcript levels of FtsH1 and FtsH8 were comparable (FtsH1) or slightly increased (FtsH8) in var2-15, the accumulation of VAR1 transcripts increased dramatically under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 5A). Importantly, at the protein level, FtsH8 (type B FtsH) accumulation was comparable to that of the WT under both control and cold-stress conditions, and it is possible that FtsH8 compensates for VAR2 (also type B) deficiency. However, in var1-1, protein levels of FtsH1 (type A), VAR2, and FtsH8 decreased compared to the WT (Supplemental Figure 5B), likely resulting in a significant reduction of FtsH hexamer formation due to insufficient amounts of type A FtsHs (FtsH1 and VAR1). This may explain the more pronounced cold hypersensitivity of the var1 mutant. 

Next, we focused on the var1 regarding its hypersensitivity to cold stress. Subcellular localization analysis was performed, and ${ \sim } 6 0 \%$ (311 out of 515) of the proteins displaying reduced accumulation were found to be chloroplast-localized. # SA biosynthesis and signaling are activated in var1-1 under cold stress

To further explore the role of VAR1 in cold-stress tolerance, Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analyses were performed. For the 1602 var1-specific downregulated genes under cold stress (Figure 2D), the enriched GO terms were related to ‘‘photosynthesis, light harvesting’’ and other cellular or developmental processes (Supplemental Figure 2D), consistent with the observed repression of photosynthesis and growth in var1-1 under cold conditions. In contrast, the 1334 upregulated genes were significantly enriched in GO terms associated with plant defense responses to various abiotic and biotic stresses, including responses to the plant hormones SA, jasmonic acid (JA), and abscisic acid (ABA), all of which are critical for plant stress responses (Figure 3A). 

To identify the pathways underlying the cold-hypersensitive phenotype of var1-1, we constructed a gene co-expression network using weighted gene correlation network analysis (WGCNA; Langfelder and Horvath, 2008) across different conditions (control vs. cold) and genotypes (WT, var1-1, and var2-15; Figure 3B and 3C). Genes with fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads $( \mathsf { F P K M } ) > 1$ in all three biological replicates of at least one genotype under one condition were included in the analysis. A total of 18 909 genes were assigned to the conserved network and grouped into 27 distinct consensus modules, each containing between 51 and 3437 coexpressed genes (Figure 3B). Among these modules, the turquoise module was strongly correlated with cold-stressed var1-1, as indicated by its module eigengene (Figure 3C). This module contains 3437 genes (Supplemental Dataset 4), and GO enrichment analysis revealed a significant overrepresentation of SA-related processes (Supplemental Dataset 4). Consistent with this, SA response and metabolic genes were also enriched in cluster 1 (Supplemental Figure 2C). The overlapping set of SA-related genes found in cluster 1 (Supplemental Figure 2C), the 1334 upregulated genes specific to cold-stressed var1-1 (Figure 2D), and genes in the turquoise module (Figure 3C and Supplemental Dataset 4) were selected for further analysis. These genes were substantially upregulated in var1-1 under cold stress (Figure 3D) and were involved in various aspects of SA biosynthesis, signaling, and metabolism. These include enzymes or key regulators of SA biosynthesis such as AvrPphB SUSCEPTIBLE3 (PBS3), EDS1, and PAD4; SA signaling components such as the SA receptors NONEX-PRESSER OF PR GENES1 (NPR1) and NPR3, and downstream factors CBP60G and SARD1, as well as WRKY transcription 

factors (Jirage et al., 1999; Cui et al., 2017; Ding and Ding, 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2021). These observations suggest a strong correlation between SA pathway activation and the reduced cold tolerance in var1-1. 

To determine whether these observed transcriptional changes were ultimately manifested at the protein level, we further analyzed our proteomic dataset (Supplemental Figure 6). Under cold-stress conditions, the var1 mutant exhibited a pronounced reduction in the abundance of proteins primarily associated with various aspects of photosynthesis (Supplemental Figure 6A and 6B). Notably, proteins with increased accumulation were significantly enriched in defense-related GO terms, including ‘‘response to SA’’ (Supplemental Figure 6C), consistent with the transcriptomic results. Heatmap analysis further confirmed the marked increase of SARG-encoded proteins in cold-stressed var1 plants (Supplemental Figure 6D). These proteomic results validate the above transcriptomic dynamics and reinforce the link between SA accumulation and the cold sensitivity of var1. 

# Overaccumulated SA in cold-stressed var1 was synthesized through the chloroplast ICS pathway

In plants, SA is produced via two independent pathways: ICS and PAL (Huang et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2021). Reverse transcriptionquantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analyses confirmed strong induction of the SA signaling genes CBP60G, WRKY70, PATHOGENESIS-RELATED 5 (PR5), and PR1 in var1-1 under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 7A). Meanwhile, ICS1, encoding the main isoform of isochorismate synthase and acting as a critical regulatory node in the ICS pathway (Wildermuth et al., 2001), was specifically upregulated in cold-stressed var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 7B). Moreover, expression of EDS1 and PAD4, two components of the plant immune response known to promote ICS1 expression and SA accumulation (Zhou et al., 1998; Wiermer et al., 2005; Wagner et al., 2013), was also elevated in var1-1 plants under cold stress compared with WT and var2-15. This activation of SA-biosynthesis-related genes resulted in an ${ \sim } 1 7 \cdot$ - fold increase in free SA levels in cold-stressed var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 7C). 

Approximately $10 \%$ of cellular SA in Arabidopsis is synthesized through the cytosolic PAL pathway (Garcion et al., 2008; Rekhter et al., 2019). Expression of PAL1 and PAL2 was significantly induced in WT and var2-15 under cold stress but remained unchanged in var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 8). Additionally, expression of both PAL3 and PAL4 was repressed in coldstressed var1-1 compared to control conditions (Supplemental Figure 8). These results suggest that SA accumulation in coldstressed var1-1 occurs primarily through the ICS rather than the PAL pathway.

# Mutation of SA-related genes restores cold-stress tolerance in var1-1

To examine the effects of SA accumulation on cold sensitivity in var1-1, mutations in ICS1, EDS1, or PAD4, each known to reduce endogenous SA levels (Zhou et al., 1998; Rekhter et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2021), were introduced into the var1-1 single mutant by crossing. The chlorosis and growth-retardation phenotype of var1-1 under cold stress was partially suppressed by the ICS1 mutation in the ics1 var1 double mutant, and this suppression was even more pronounced following disruption of EDS1 or PAD4 in the eds1 var1 and pad4 var1 double mutants (Figure 4A). These observations were further confirmed by measurements of fresh weight, chlorophyll content, and the Fv/Fm of PSII (Figure 4B–4D). The stronger suppression observed with EDS1 and PAD4 mutations is likely because these proteins not only promote SA biosynthesis but also sustain SArelated defense programs through the transcriptional activation of SARGs (Cui et al., 2017). Furthermore, overexpression of the bacterial salicylate hydroxylase gene NahG, which degrades SA and thus reduces endogenous SA levels (Gaffney et al., 1993; Duan et al., 2019), restored cold-stress tolerance of var1-1 to levels comparable to those seen with EDS1 and PAD4 mutations (Figure 4A–4D). These results suggest that the chlorotic phenotype of var1-1 under cold stress predominantly results from SA overaccumulation and excessive activation of SA signaling. Notably, spectinomycin treatment did not further enhance the suppressive effects conferred by the ICS1 mutation or NahG overexpression (Figure 4A; Supplemental Figure 4B), indicating that suppression of the cold-sensitive phenotype is not related to the restoration of chloroplast protein homeostasis. Interestingly, the smaller size of var1-1 plants under control conditions was also restored by SA depletion (Figure 4A and 4B). 

NPR1 was previously identified as an SA receptor that mediates downstream signaling cascades (Wu et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2020). However, the npr1 var1 double mutant exhibited greater susceptibility to cold stress than either var1-1 or npr1-1 single mutants, as evidenced by reduced fresh weight, chlorophyll content, and Fv/Fm values (Supplemental Figure 9). These results are consistent with a previous study demonstrating that NPR1 positively regulates cold-stress tolerance independently of SA signaling (Olate et al., 2018). 

# Singlet oxygen-triggered retrograde signaling contributes to cold-stress tolerance regulation in var1

Unsuccessful assembly and formation of the PET chain can generate harmful ROS due to inefficient quenching of excess light energy. Transcriptomic analysis revealed that numerous genes involved in ROS and oxidative stress responses were upregulated in the var1-1 mutant under cold stress (Figure 3A and Supplemental Dataset 3). To determine whether specific ROS contribute to the chlorosis phenotype of cold-stressed var1-1, we compared the expression of genes encoding different ROSscavenging enzymes among var1-1, var2-15, and WT plants 

(Supplemental Figure 10A). The expression of ascorbate peroxidase (APXS), which encodes a hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ - scavenging enzyme in the chloroplast stroma, was markedly induced in cold-stressed var1-1, but to a similar extent in var2- 15. A similar pattern was observed for the chloroplast-localized iron superoxide dismutase 3 (FSD3), which converts superoxide anion $( \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } \cdot - )$ into ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ (Supplemental Figure 10A). In contrast, FSD1, catalase 1 (CAT1) and CAT3, which encode enzymes with high ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ scavenging capacity, were specifically upregulated in var1-1 under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 10A), indicating that ${ \sf O } _ { 2 } \dot { { \bf \theta } } ^ { - }$ and/or ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ may be overaccumulated. However, histochemical staining with $^ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) and nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT) confirmed the overaccumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , but not ${ \sf O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { \cdots }$ , in both var1-1 and var2-15 under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 10B and 10C). Overexpression of chloroplast-localized CAT2—achieved by N-terminal fusion with the chloroplast transit peptide (cTP) from RBCS1A (RIBULOSE BI-SPHOSPHATE CARBOXYLASE/OXYGENASE (RuBisCo) SMALL CHAIN 1A) and removal of the peroxisomal targeting signal at the C terminus—successfully increases catalase activity in chloroplasts, degrading ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and conferring salt stress tolerance (Fu et al., 2023). However, overexpression of either CAT1 or CAT2 targeted to chloroplasts using the same strategy failed to suppress the chlorosis phenotype of var1-1 under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 11). These results suggest that ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ overaccumulation is not the primary cause of chlorosis in coldstressed var1-1. 

Singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 } )$ is an extremely short-lived ROS generated primarily by PSII or chlorophyll biosynthesis intermediates (Li and Kim, 2022). Interestingly, the expression of SORGs, including SIGMA FACTOR BINDING PROTEIN 1 (SIB1), WRKY33, and WRKY40, was specifically and strongly upregulated in coldstressed var1-1 (Figure 5A and Supplemental Dataset 3). Moreover, expression of ZINC FINGER OF ARABIDOPSIS THALI-ANA 10 (ZAT10) and ZAT12, which mediate responses to cold, oxidative, and several other abiotic stresses in Arabidopsis (Davletova et al., 2005; Li et al., 2023a), was strongly induced in var1-1 but showed much lower (ZAT10) or no induction (ZAT12) in cold-stressed var2-15 plants (Figure 5B). 

A previous study identified 168 early SORGs (eSORGs; Dogra et al., 2017). Notably, 54 of these 168 eSORGs were upregulated in cold-stressed var1-1, whereas only three were induced in var2-15 (Figure 5C), corroborating the specific involvement of $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ signaling in VAR1-related cold tolerance. Consistently, staining with singlet oxygen sensor green (SOSG), a highly selective fluorescent probe for $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ detection (Flors et al., 2006), revealed substantial $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ accumulation in cold-stressed var1-1 plants (Figure 5D and 5E). # Crosstalk between ${ } ^ { 1 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ and SA signaling in the context of VAR1-mediated cold-stress tolerance

To elucidate the role of $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ and SA signaling in the chlorosis phenotype of var1-1 under cold stress, we constructed a set of triple mutants, including ex1 eds1 var1, ex2 ics1 var1, ex2 eds1 var1, and ex2 pad4 var1, and analyzed their growth phenotypes, fresh weights, chlorophyll contents, and photosynthetic performance (Fv/Fm; Figure 5F and 5G; Supplemental Figures 12 and 13). Although chlorophyll content slightly increased, the fresh weight and photosynthetic performance of the ex1 eds1 var1 triple mutant showed no additive improvement compared to the ex1 var1 and eds1 var1 double mutants. Specifically, the triple mutant closely resembled the eds1 var1 double mutant (Supplemental Figure 12), indicating genetic epistasis of EDS1 to EX1. This observation aligns with previous studies (Ochsenbein et al., 2006; Geng et al., 2024), suggesting that EX1-mediated plastid $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ retrograde signaling acts upstream of EDS1. 

In contrast, although no additive effects were observed when the EX2 mutation was introduced into the eds1 var1, pad4 var1, and ics1 var1 double mutants, the resulting triple mutants (ex2 eds1 var1, ex2 pad4 var1, and ex2 ics1 var1) phenocopied the ex2 var1 double mutant. This indicates genetic epistasis of EX2 over these SA-related genes (Figure 5F and 5G; Supplemental Figures 12 and 13) and further suggests that EX2 may have an EX1-independent function in regulating the expression of downstream genes. 

# EX2 functions independently of EX1-mediated retrograde signaling

The results shown above suggest that EX2 may have a function independent of EX1. To explore this overlooked role, we conducted systematic transcriptomic analyses including samples of the WT and var1-1 single mutant, as well as the ics1 var1, eds1 var1, ex1 var1, and ex2 var1 double mutants grown under control and cold-stress conditions (Supplemental Dataset 5). C-means clustering analysis identified 20 gene expression profiles (Supplemental Figure 15). Genes in profile 13 were upregulated in cold-stressed var1-1 and were restored to near WT levels by mutations in ICS1, EDS1, or EX2, but showed only mild restoration in the ex1 var1 mutant by the EX1 mutation (Figure 6A; Supplemental Figure 15). SARGs were significantly enriched in this gene cluster (Figure 6B). Upregulation of these SARGs in cold-stressed var1-1 was substantially suppressed by mutations in ICS1 or EDS1 (in the ics1 var1 or eds1 var1 double mutants), moderately suppressed by the EX2 mutation (in ex2 var1), and only slightly suppressed by the EX1 mutation (in ex1 var1; Figure 6C). A similar pattern was observed for WRKY transcription factors (Supplemental Figure 16), many of which are involved in or regulated by SA signaling (van Verk et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2017; Niu et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024). 

The transcriptomic data were further verified by RT‒qPCR analyses of selected SA signaling marker genes, including WRKY70, CBP60G, PR1, and PR5 (Figure 6D). Consistent with the transcriptomic data, the expression of ICS1, EDS1, and PAD4— key enzyme or regulators governing SA biosynthesis— was strongly repressed in the ex2 var1 double mutant. In contrast, only mild repression of EDS1 (and no significant change in ICS1 or PAD4) was observed in the ex1 var1 double mutant (Figure 6E). Consequently, free SA content was substantially lower in the ex2 var1 double mutant than in the ex1 var1 double mutant and the var1-1 single mutant (Figure 6F). These results suggest that EX2 plays a critical role in regulating SARG expression.

# DISCUSSION

The sessile lifestyle of vascular plants critically depends on their ability to cope with challenging environmental conditions. In this study, we applied mild cold stress ( $\mathsf { 1 2 ^ { \circ } C }$ day $\scriptscriptstyle { } ^ { \prime } 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ night)—a condition plants frequently encounter throughout their lifespan, especially when subtropical or temperate crops are cultivated toward higher latitude, colder regions—to screen for key regulators 

involved in cold tolerance. We identified var1-1, a mutant deficient in the chloroplast FtsH5 metalloprotease, as hypersensitive to this low-temperature condition (Figure 1). Compared to var2 mutants, var1 mutants exhibit only a weak variegated phenotype under standard growth conditions (Sakamoto et al., 2002; Miura et al., 2007). However, var1-1 displayed a more pronounced chlorosis and growth-retardation phenotype under cold stress, highlighting a critical role for VAR1 in cold-stress tolerance. This finding is consistent with the substantial transcriptional induction of VAR1 under cold stress (Supplemental Figure 5A). The relative protein accumulation levels of type-A (FtsH1 and VAR1) and type-B (VAR2 and FtsH8) FtsHs likely underlie the severe cold-sensitive phenotype of var1. Specifically, FtsH1 protein accumulation was reduced in var1, whereas both VAR1 and FtsH8 accumulated to relatively high levels in var2 mutants (Supplemental Figure 5B). Moreover, the cold hypersensitivity of var1 and var2 was not suppressed by the inhibition of plastid translation (Supplemental Figure 4), suggesting a distinct mechanism compared to that causing the variegated phenotype under normal growth conditions (Yu et al., 2008). Although previous studies indicated that VAR1 and VAR2 specifically participate in PSI biogenesis (Jarvi et al., 2016), our proteomic and immunoblotting analyses revealed significant reductions of both PSII and PSI subunits in coldstressed var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 3), suggesting that the chlorotic phenotype of var1-1 under cold stress likely does not result solely from specific defects in PSI biogenesis. 

Genes specifically upregulated in var1 under cold stress were enriched in plant immune/defense-related processes. Interestingly, in-depth analysis revealed significant enrichment of genes involved in the biosynthesis and signaling of SA, a key defense-related hormone, in this cluster (Figure 3A). WGCNA further identified a strong correlation between SA accumulation and the cold- sensitive phenotype of var1 (Figure 3B and 3C), consistent with the significant upregulation of SARGs (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figure 7A and 7B) and dramatic overaccumulation of free SA in cold stressed var1 (Supplemental Figure 7C). The overaccumulation of SA largely accounts for the chlorosis phenotype of var1, as demonstrated by its suppression via SA depletion through overexpression of the salicylate hydroxylase gene NahG, or mutation of EDS1 or PAD4 (Figure 4)—two partner regulators of SA accumulation in plant immunity (Zhou et al., 1998; Dongus and Parker, 2021; Peng et al., 2021; Pruitt et al., 2021). SA levels were only moderately elevated in the cold-stressed var2-15 mutant, consistent with previous findings in var2-9 under continuous-light conditions (Duan et al., 2019). Notably, SA functions independently of NPR1 in this context, as mutation of NPR1 did not suppress, but rather exacerbated cold sensitivity in var1-1 (Supplemental Figure 9), consistent with the notion that NPR1 positively regulates cold tolerance independently of SA (Olate et al., 2018). In contrast, mutation of ICS1, which encodes a key enzyme in the SA biosynthesis pathway, had a relatively modest effect on suppressing cold sensitivity in var1-1 (Figure 4; Supplemental Figure 12). SA and ROS signaling pathways exhibit extensive bidirectional crosstalk, and their mechanistic interplay critically coordinates stress adaptation responses. High levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulated in the var1-1 mutant (Supplemental Figures 10B and Figure 5D, E). SA stimulates ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production by inhibiting the accumulation of scavenging enzymes such as CAT2 (Yuan et al., 2017). Ectopic overexpression of chloroplast-targeted CAT2 was previously shown to increase catalase activity and degrade ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ in chloroplasts, conferring salt-stress tolerance in plants by reducing plastid triose phosphate isomerase activity (Fu et al., 2023). By contrast, $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ specifically accumulated to higher levels in var1-1 (Figure 5D and 5E), accompanied by specific activation of SORGs (Figure 5A–5C). In chloroplasts, $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ is primarily generated at two sites: (1) at PSII, where excess excitation energy absorbed by LHCII that cannot be efficiently quenched by PET or dissipated as heat is transferred to molecular oxygen $( \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ , leading to $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ production; and (2) by tetrapyrrole intermediates with macrocyclic structures acting as photosensitizers, generating $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ upon exposure to light (Li and Kim, 2022). In the var1 mutant, differential declines in PSII subunit proteins were observed (Supplemental Figure 3A). For example, decreases in LHCII subunits (e.g., LhcB1.2 and LhcB1.3) were less pronounced compared to the chloroplast-encoded PSII core subunits (PsbA, PsbB, PsbC, and PsbD), accompanied by a mild upregulation of chloroplast UPR components. ance possibly results in insufficient quenching of absorbed energy and subsequent $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ generation. Such $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ overproduction due to imbalanced expression of PhANG and photosynthesisassociated plastid genes has been documented in a previous study (Lv et al., 2019). 

Futher proteomic analysis revealed that upstream chlorophyll biosynthesis enzymes in var1 exhibited smaller reductions in protein abundance under cold stress. In contrast, Mg-protoporphyrin methyltransferase (CHLM), which acts downstream of Mg-chelatase, was almost completely absent $( \sim 0 . 6 \%$ of WT levels; Supplemental Figure 18 and Supplemental Dataset 2). Although this requires further experimental validation, it is possible that upstream tetrapyrrole intermediates (e.g., Mg-Proto IX and Proto IX) overaccumulate in cold-stressed var1. These intermediates could act as photosensitizers under light conditions, exacerbating $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation. Collectively, these findings provide possible mechanistic insights into the cold-induced $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ hyperaccumulation observed in var1 (Figure 7F).

These results suggest that EX1 acts in the same pathway as EDS1 but plays a minor role in regulating SARG expression in coldstressed var1. This was further supported by the expression patterns of profile 13 genes (Supplemental Figure 15), enriched in SARGs and other defense-related genes, which were minimally or not suppressed in ex1 var1 (Figure 6A–6D).

Indeed, changes in gene expression were more extensive in the ex2 flu double mutant compared with the flu single mutant. However, the ex1 ex2 flu triple mutant suppressed almost all upregulated SORGs in flu (Lee et al., 2007), suggesting additive effects of EX1 and EX2. These additive effects were also observed in the suppression of the photobleaching phenotype in dark-grown pif1 pif3 (phytochrome-interacting factor) double mutants upon light exposure (Li et al., 2023b), as well as in dark-pretreated WT seedlings exposed to far-red light (Page et al., 2017). These observations suggest that EX1 and EX2 likely interact in a more complex, context-dependent manner under different circumstances. 

In this study, we observed a stronger suppression of var1-1 cold sensitivity by EX2 mutation compared with EX1 mutation (Figure 5F and 5G; Supplemental Figures 12 and 13). Moreover, the triple mutants combining ex2 with ex1 var1, eds1 var1, or pad4 var1 showed no additive effects and displayed phenotypes comparable to the ex2 var1 double mutant (Figure 5F and 5G; Supplemental Figure 13), suggesting a dominant role for EX2 in this pathway. Transcriptomic comparisons revealed that the EX2 mutation, on one hand, mildly repressed the activation of SARGs (Figure 6A–6E), particularly several key regulators of SA biosynthesis, including PAD4, CBP60G, WRKY28, and WRKY41 (Figure 6C and 6D; Supplemental Figure 16); on the other hand, significantly restored the repressed expression of PhANGs (Figure 7C–7E; Supplemental Figure 17D–17F). These results suggest a specific function for EX2, independent of EX1, in suppressing the expression of PhANGs in var1. Consistent with our observations, a previous study on far-red light-induced repression of PhANGs also reported that the mutation of EX2 had a stronger effect than EX1 in restoring PhANG expression under shorter (1 day) dark pretreatment conditions (Page et al., 2017). Future studies should investigate whether this dual role of EX2—promoting SA biosynthesis while repressing PhANG expression—represents a conserved function of EX2 in response to different environmental challenges. 

Under cold-stress conditions, deficiency of VAR1 (and, to a lesser extent, VAR2) leads to the accumulation of $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ (Figure 5D and 5E), which functions as a plastid retrograde signal. This $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ signaling exhibits dual regulatory effects (Figure 7F): it represses the expression of PhANGs, consequently repressing chloroplast biogenesis and plant growth, and simultaneously activates defense mechanisms through SA biosynthesis and associated signaling cascades (Figure 3; Supplemental Figure 7, and Supplemental Dataset 3). SA plays a critical role in plant responses to various abiotic and biotic stresses, including cold stress (Peng et al., 2021). For example, recent research in Citrus showed that SA activates expression of the proline synthesis enzyme P5CS1, increasing proline content and enhancing cold tolerance in Citrus (Xiao et al., 2025). However, excessive SA accumulation (as observed in cold-stressed var1; Supplemental Figure 7C) is detrimental and paradoxically impairs chloroplast development and plant growth (Figure 7F). 

Extensive genetic analyses conducted in this study delineate a sophisticated regulatory hierarchy in which EX1, the EDS1/PAD4 module, and EX2 form an intricate signaling network (Figure 7F). 

# VAR1 plays a critical role in cold-stress tolerance

Although discrepancies among individual studies have been reported (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2025), a recent study revealed that EX1 relocates to the nucleus during $^ 1 { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ signaling to regulate downstream gene expression. Whether EX2 similarly translocates to the nucleus remains an open question for future investigation. Our findings highlight the crucial importance of maintaining homeostasis and coordination between $^ 1 { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ signaling and SA-mediated responses for successful long-term adaptation to cold stress. 

# METHODS

# Plant material and growth conditions

All Arabidopsis lines used in this study were in the Columbia (Col-0) background. The var1-1, var2-15, ics1 (SALK_042603), pad4 (SALK_206548), eds1-2, npr1-1, ex1 (SALK_002088), and ex2 (SALK_012127) mutants, as well as the cpNahG transgenic line, were described previously (Cao et al., 1997; Sakamoto et al., 2002; Lee et al., 2007; Duan et al., 2019). All double and triple mutants were generated by crossing homozygous lines of the designated single or double mutants. Homozygous progeny were identified by PCR genotyping or sequencing of segregating F2 populations, except for cpNahG, which was selected based on Basta resistance. All primers used for genotyping are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

For plant growth under aseptic conditions, seeds were surface sterilized with $1 . 2 \%$ NaClO for 10 min or chlorine gas overnight and sown on halfstrength Murashige and Skoog medium (Murashige and Skoog, 1962) supplemented with $1 \%$ (w/v) sucrose and $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) agar. Seeds were germinated and grown at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under a long-day photoperiod (16-h light/8-h dark) with a light intensity of $1 2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } }$ photons $\mathfrak { m } ^ { - 2 } \thinspace \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . For long-term cold-stress treatment, seeds were germinated and grown either at the control temperature of ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ or under cold stress at $1 2 / 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (day/ night) for the indicated duration. For short-term cold-stress treatment, seeds were germinated and grown at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 4 days and then either maintained at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ or transferred to cold stress at $5 \textdegree C$ for an additional 5 days. 

# Vector construction and generation of transgenic lines

To generate the var1-1 and var2-15 complementation lines, the full-length coding sequence (CDS) of VAR1 and the genomic sequence of VAR2 without stop codons were amplified from Arabidopsis Col-0 cDNA and genomic DNA, respectively. The PCR products were cloned into the pEZR(H)-LN binary vector to create a C-terminal in-frame fusion with the coding sequence of GFP (VAR1-GFP or VAR2-GFP) using EcoRI and BamHI restriction sites via the Hieff Clone Universal One Step Cloning Kit (Yeasen, Shanghai, China). The 2-kb upstream sequence from the ATG start codon of VAR1 or VAR2 was then amplified from genomic DNA and cloned into the corresponding VAR1-GFP or VAR2-GFP vector at the HindIII and EcoRI restriction sites to drive expression from its native promoter (pVAR1::VAR1-GFP and pVAR2::VAR2-GFP). 

To overexpress catalase proteins (CAT1 or CAT2) in the chloroplasts of the var1-1 mutant, constructs with the chloroplast transit peptide (cTP) fused to CAT1 (cTP-CAT1) or CAT2 (cTP-CAT2) were generated as previously described (Fu et al., 2023). Briefly, the CAT1 or CAT2 CDS without the C-terminal peroxisomal targeting signal (1–1443 bp) and the CDS encoding the cTP from RBCS1A (1–237 bp) were amplified from cDNA. The cTP and CAT1/CAT2 fragments were fused in-frame and cloned into the pEZR (H)-LN binary vector (without GFP fusion), driven by the CaMV 35S promoter, to generate the p35S::cTP-CAT1 and p35S::cTP-CAT2 constructs. 

The resulting constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 and introduced into Arabidopsis plants via the floral-dip method (Clough and Bent, 1998). For transgenic plant 

selection, seeds were surface-sterilized and grown on half-strength Murashige and Skoog medium supplemented with $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m o } / 1$ hygromycin. All primers used for cloning are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Chlorophyll content and chlorophyll fluorescence measurements

Chlorophyll content was determined using the N,N-dimethylformamide method (Moran, 1982). In brief, 100 mg of aerial seedling tissues (excluding roots) were immersed in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of N,N-dimethylformamide and incubated in darkness at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Absorbance was measured at 664, 647, 625, and $6 0 3 \ \mathsf { n m }$ , and chlorophyll a and b contents were calculated according to the following formulas: $C \mathsf { h } | a = 1 2 . 8 1 \times A _ { 6 6 4 } - 2 . 1 6 \times A _ { 6 4 7 } +$ $1 . 4 4 \times A _ { 6 2 5 } - 4 . 9 1 \times A _ { 6 0 5 }$ ; Chl $b = - 4 . 9 3 \times A _ { 6 6 4 } + 2 6 . 0 1 \times A _ { 6 4 7 } +$ $3 . 7 4 \times A _ { 6 2 5 } - 1 5 . 5 5 \times A _ { 6 0 3 }$ . 

Chlorophyll a fluorescence was measured using the MAXI version of the Imaging-PAM (Heinz Walz, Nurnberg, Germany). Seedlings were darkadapted for $3 0 \ \mathsf { m i n }$ , and the maximum quantum yield of PSII (Fv/Fm) was determined according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# RNA isolation and RT‒qPCR

Total RNA was isolated using VeZol Reagent (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) and treated with DNase (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) to eliminate genomic DNA contamination. First-strand cDNA was synthesized using a FastKing RT Kit (Tiangen, Beijing, China). qPCR was performed using a CFX384 Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, Hercules, USA) with ChamQ Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). Relative gene expression was normalized to ACTIN2 (AT3G18780) using the comparative $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Pfaffl, 2001). Primers used for RT‒ qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Protein extraction and immunoblot analyses

Plant tissues were snap-frozen and ground in liquid nitrogen. Total protein was extracted as previously described (Cahoon et al., 1992). Protein concentrations were quantified using a Pierce BCA protein assay kit (Thermo Fisher, Waltham, USA). Equal amounts of total protein $( 4 0 ~ \mu \ g$ per sample) were separated by SDS‒polyacrylamide gel $1 0 \%$ w/v) electrophoresis (SDS‒PAGE) and blotted onto polyvinylidene difluoride membranes (Cytiva). Immunoblot detection was performed with specific antibodies using an enhanced chemiluminescence system (SparkJade, Qingdao, China), and signals were detected with a ChemiDoc imaging system (Bio-Rad). All antibodies were obtained from commercial suppliers: ACTIN (Sigma); PsbA, LHCB, and AtpA (Agrisera); PsaA, PsaB, PsbC, PetB, PetD, CHLM, VAR1, VAR2, GUN5, and PORB (Orizymes, Shanghai). Relative protein levels from immunoblots were quantified using ImageJ software, with ACTIN as the internal loading control. 

# Transmission electron microscopy

To examine the ultrastructure of chloroplasts, transmission electron microscopy was carried out as described previously (Su et al., 2024). Briefly, ${ \sim } 2 \times 2 { \cdot } \mathsf { m m }$ leaf pieces were immersed in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde in $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sodium cacodylate buffer (pH 7.4) containing $5 \mathsf { m M C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , vacuum infiltrated for 5 min, and fixed at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight. After fixation, the samples were post-fixed with $1 \% 0 { \mathsf { s O } } _ { 4 }$ and $) . 8 \% \mathsf { K } _ { 3 } \mathsf { F e } ( \mathsf { C N } ) _ { 6 }$ in 50 mM sodium cacodylate buffer (pH 7.4) for 2 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Subsequently, the samples were dehydrated through an ethanol gradient $( 5 0 \%$ , $70 \%$ , $90 \%$ , anhydrous), soaked in propylene oxide, embedded in resin, and polymerized at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Ultrathin sections $( 5 0 - 7 0 ~ \mathsf { n m } )$ were obtained using a diamond knife, mounted onto copper grids, and stained with $2 \%$ uranyl acetate followed by lead citrate. Images were acquired using a Tecnai G2 spirit Biotwin transmission electron microscope (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

# RNA sequencing and data analysis

Eleven-day-old seedlings grown under control conditions, 30-day-old WT and var2-15 seedlings, and 50-day-old var1-1 seedlings grown under 

cold-stress conditions were collected. Total RNA was extracted using Trizol reagent (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA integrity was assessed using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer with the RNA Nano 6000 assay kit (Agilent Technologies). mRNAs were enriched using oligo(dT) beads (Epicenter, Madison, WI, USA), and sequencing libraries were prepared using the TIANSeq Fast RNA Library Kit (Illumina) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform to generate 150-bp paired-end reads. Low-quality reads and adapter sequences were removed using Trimmomatic software (version 0.39; Bolger et al., 2014). Clean reads were aligned to the Arabidopsis TAIR10 reference genome using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2015). HTSeq (Anders et al., 2015) was used to count the number of reads mapped to each gene, and FPKM values were calculated accordingly. DEGs were identified using the edgeR package in R (Robinson et al., 2010). Genes with a false discovery rate (FDR) ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ and fold change (FC) $^ { > 2 }$ were considered significantly differentially expressed. 

Gene expression trends were analyzed using the fuzzy c-means algorithm with the R package Mfuzz (Kumar and Futschik, 2007). Venn diagrams were generated using the online tool VENNY (https://bioinfogp.cnb.csic. es/tools/venny/). GO enrichment analyses were performed using the DAVID online tool (Dennis et al., 2003), and results were visualized with the R package clusterProfiler (Yu et al., 2012). Heatmaps depicting gene expression were generated using the R package ComplexHeatmap (Gu et al., 2016). 

# Label-free quantification by mass spectrometry

For label-free quantification using mass spectrometry (MS), 11-day-old WT, var1-1, and var2-15 seedlings grown under control conditions, and 30-day-old WT and var2-15 and 50-day-old var1-1 seedlings grown under cold-stress conditions were collected. Samples (100 mg of plant material) were ground in liquid nitrogen and extracted with $2 0 0 \mu \up$ protein extraction buffer $( 4 \%$ SDS, $5 \%$ glycerol in 40 mM Tris–HCl, pH 6.8, and $_ { 2 \times }$ protease inhibitor cocktail, Roche) according to a previously published protocol (Cahoon et al., 1992). After protein quantification, $4 0 \mu \ g$ total protein per sample was digested overnight at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ using trypsin at a ratio of 1:80 (trypsin:total protein, $0 . 5 ~ \mu \up9$ trypsin). The resulting peptides were desalted using MonoSpin C18 columns (Shimadzu). 

For MS analysis, peptides were separated by nanoflow high-performance liquid chromatography (Easy-nLC1200, Thermo Fisher Scientific) using an analytical column (C18, $2 0 \mathsf { c m } \times 7 5 \mu \mathrm { m } .$ , $3 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and a 2-h acetonitrile gradient. The nLC system was coupled to a Q Exactive Plus (Thermo Fisher Scientific) high-resolution Orbitrap hybrid mass spectrometer. Proteins were identified based on information-dependent acquisition of fragmentation spectra of multiply charged peptides. The MS1 survey scan was conducted at 70 000 resolution, scanning an $\mathsf { m } /$ z range of 350–1800, with an automatic gain control (AGC) target of 3E6. The MS2 scan was performed at a resolution of 17 500 with an AGC target of 1E5. 

For protein identification and quantification, the raw mass spectrometry data were processed using MaxQuant software (version 1.6.7.0) with a label-free quantitation algorithm (Cox and Mann, 2008; Cox et al., 2014). Sequences were searched against the UniProt Arabidopsis FASTA database UP000006548 (39 280 entries) using the Andromeda search engine (Cox et al., 2011). The following settings were applied: multiplicity set to 1 (label-free quantification); fixed modification, carbamidomethyl (C); variable modifications, oxidized methionine (M) and N-terminal acetylation; digestion protease specified as trypsin, allowing up to two missed cleavages; a time window of 0.7 min was applied for the ‘‘match between runs’’ feature; common contaminants (e.g., trypsin, keratin) included in database search; and unique and razor peptides used for protein quantification. All other parameters were set to default values. Post-processing and statistical analyses were performed using

# SA measurements
