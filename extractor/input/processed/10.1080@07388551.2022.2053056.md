# ABSTRACT

Plants make different defense mechanisms in response to different environmental stresses. One common way is to produce secondary metabolites. Temperature is the main environmental factor that regulates plant secondary metabolites, especially flavonoids and terpenoids. Stress caused by temperature decreasing to $4 { - } 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ is conducive to the accumulation of flavonoids and terpenoids. However, the accumulation mechanism under cold stress still lacks a systematic explanation. In this review, we summarize three aspects of cold stress promoting the accumulation of flavonoids and terpenoids in plants, that is, by affecting (1) the content of endogenous plant hormones, especially jasmonic acid and abscisic acid; (2) the expression level and activity of important transcription factors, such as bHLH and MYB families. This aspect also includes post-translational modification of transcription factors caused by cold stress; (3) key enzyme genes expression and activity in the biosynthesis pathway, in addition, the rate-limiting enzyme and glycosyltransferases genes are responsive to cold stress. The systematic understanding of cold stress regulates flavonoids, and terpenoids will contribute to the future research of genetic engineering breeding, metabolism regulation, glycosyltransferases mining, and plant synthetic biology.

# Introduction

Flavonoids and terpenoids are important secondary metabolites of plants and have a wide range of pharmacological activities. Plant secondary metabolites are produced in response to environmental stress. As an important environmental factor, the temperature has an important influence on the synthesis of secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids and terpenes. For example, Panax ginseng, an important herbal medicine for a long time, grows in the cold northeast of China. P. ginseng mainly contains triterpenoids ginsenosides, which are widely used in medicine to treat cancer, obesity, and senile diseases [1]. Panax notoginseng, a precious medicinal plant, grows in the warm northwest of China and is used in the treatment of cardiovascular diseases, nervous system diseases, anti-inflammatory diseases, and anti-aging [2]. They both belong to the Panax genus and have a close evolutionary relationship, but their biological activities are quite different, which

may be attributed to the temperature of the growing area. After overwintering, the color of some Mikania micrantha changed from green to red as a result of anthocyanins accumulation, and the content of other flavonoids and total phenols were higher than that of green plants [3–6]. Therefore, plant secondary metabolites and temperature have a close relationship. The temperature has a significant effect on secondary metabolites [7].

Most of the reported studies on the regulation of plant metabolites by temperature are the effects of low temperature on the synthesis of flavonoids and terpenoids. It shows that low temperature has the greatest influence on the synthesis of flavonoids and terpenoids. Cold stress (CS) is considered to be the main limiting factor for plant productivity and distribution [8,9]. It can: damage photosynthetic mechanisms and efficiency, reduce membrane fluidity, and induce oxidative stress by causing the production of reactive oxygen

The antioxidant system, including superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), ascorbic acid peroxidase (APX), peroxidase (POD) can be activated; the antioxidant capacity can be improved by increasing non-enzyme molecules. When plants perceive CS, the corresponding signal molecules and regulatory factors will change accordingly. CS can promote the accumulation of various plant secondary metabolites through three aspects (Figure 1): firstly, CS can affect the level of endogenous phytohormones [12–14]. For example, CS can improve the expression level of jasmonic acid (JA) and abscisic acid (ABA) biosynthesis genes, and then cause the expression of hormone-related regulatory factors to promote the secondary metabolites. Secondly, CS will induce the expression of transcription factors (TFs), especially bHLH and MYB families. In addition, the decrease in temperature will also affect epigenetics and post-translational modification. Thirdly, CS can directly regulate the biosynthesis-related enzyme genes, especially the important enzyme genes located upstream of the biosynthesis pathway, such as phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) and 3-Hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase (HMGR), the rate-limiting enzyme in

In this review, we summarized the regulation mechanism of CS on flavonoids and terpenoids, and pointed out the current problems and future application prospects.

# Phytohormones first response to cold stress

Phytohormones play a vital role in response to environmental stress [37]. Many phytohormones: abscisic acid (ABA), ethylene (ETH), salicylic acid (SA), can also respond to environmental stress and perform signal transmission to affect secondary metabolites [14,38–40].

# Ethylene enhances the accumulation of flavonoids

ETH yield could be increased after CS stimulation (0 or $5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ shifting to $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Gene expression levels of ETH biosynthesis pathway, ACS (1-amino-cyclopropane-1- carboxylate synthase gene), and ACO (ETH-forming enzyme gene) were up-regulated (Figure 2) [41]. Consequently, total phenol, flavonoids, especially the total anthocyanins content in plums, increased after CS treatment for more than 14 days. ETH has been reported to promote anthocyanins biosynthesis by upregulating the gene expression in post-harvest plum fruits [42]. Therefore, ETH produced in plum was speculated to mediate the activation of anthocyanins biosynthesis when it was transferred to $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ after CS [6]. In addition, ETH has been reported to enhance anthocyanins biosynthesis by upregulating the gene expression via the activation of the EIN/EIL (INSENSITIVE/EIN-LIKE) and MYB TFs [43].

# Jasmonic acid and abscisic acid enhance the accumulation of terpenoids

# Jasmonic acid

JA is a well-known phytohormone. CS could upregulate the expression levels of JA biosynthetic genes, ALLENE OXIDE CYCLASE (AOC), LIPOXYGENASE (LOX), JA RESISTANT (JAR1), resulting in increasing endogenous JA production in plants [44,45] (Figure 2). What is more, JA plays an important role in promoting secondary metabolites production in plants [13,45–47]. The expression levels of jasmonate biosynthetic genes and the production of endogenous jasmonate in Artemisia annua were upregulated under CS, which induces the increase of artemisinin content [15]. JA and its derivatives, such as methyl jasmonate (MeJA), further promote plant secondary metabolites production by regulating JA responsive TFs [48–50]. JA-responsive AP2 family TFs, AaERF1, and AaERF2 were able to bind to both ADS and CYP71AV1 promoters [48]. AaORA, a JAresponsive and trichome-specific APETALA2/ETH-Response Factor, and AaTCP14 (Teosinte Branched 1/ Cycloidea/Proliferating cell factor 14) synergistically

activated the promoters of DBR2 (Double Bond Reductase 2) and ALDH1 (Aldehyde dehydrogenase 1) genes, both of which encode indispensable enzymes for artemisinin biosynthesis.

# Abscisic acid

Under CS, the gene expression level of the ABA biosynthesis pathway, such as CYP707As (cytochrome P450 family-707 subfamily-A polypeptide genes), NCEDs (nine-cisepoxycarotenoid dioxygenase genes), and ABA2 (ABAdeficient gene) were up-regulated [41]. In ABA-treated A. annua, the expression of most key genes, such as HMGR, farnesene (FPS), CYP71AV1, in artemisinin biosynthesis pathway significantly increased, resulting in a significant accumulation of artemisinin. The ABA response TF, AaABF3 can directly bind to the promoter of artemisinin biosynthesis key gene ALDH1 and activate its expression [51,52]. AaABF3 plays a prominent role in the regulation of artemisinin biosynthesis and promoting artemisinin accumulation (Figure 2).

# Transcription factors and post-translation modification are induced by cold stress

The effect of CS on gene expression depends on the regulation of TFs [32,53]. CS induces the activation of TFs (Table 2), which regulate gene expression through specific binding to cis-acting elements in the promoters of target genes. TFs involved in CS mainly belong to: MYB [54,55], AP2/ERF, and bHLH [65,66] families. In addition, the interaction between different TFs is also reported under CS. The biosynthesis of anthocyanins is coordinated and regulated by the conservative MYBbHLH-WD40/WDR (MBW) regulatory complex [67–69]. MBW complex components modulate each other’s functions. Some cold-responsive TFs have also been observed within this complex system [28–30].

# bHLH TFs, MYB TFs, and post-translation modification induced the biosynthesis of flavonoids

# bHLH TFs

The bHLH superfamily is one of the largest TF families in plants. It is named by a highly conserved basic/helixloop-helix domain [70]. The typical bHLH signature domain consists of about 60 amino acids and two functionally different regions, namely: the base region and the HLH region. The base region is located at the N-terminus and contains ${ \sim } 1 5$ amino acids involved in DNA binding. The HLH region is mainly composed of hydrophobic residues at the C-terminus and contains two amphipathic $\pmb { \alpha }$ -helices linked by a loop region with variable sequences to participate in the dimerization reaction [71]. Many previous studies have reported that ICE

(INDUCER OF C-repeat binding factors EXPRESSION) TFs, belonging to the bHLH TF family, play crucial roles in cold signaling [72,73], so cold-responsive bHLH TFs have also paid attention to the influence of secondary metabolites. The important interactions are shown in Figure 3.

MdbHLH3 has a complex relationship with other TFs and anthocyanins biosynthesis genes. $1 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ -induced MdbHLH3 was isolated from M. domestica and can interact with MdMYB1 at N-terminal. This discovery was confirmed in vitro and in vivo by Y2H assay and BiFC assay, respectively. Overexpressed MdbHLH3 enhanced the expression of MdMYB1, which acts as a crucial component of the MBW complex to regulate anthocyanins biosynthesis in red-skinned apples. EMSA verified that MdbHLH3 specifically binds to the MYC (CANNTG) and low-temperature-responsive (LTR, CCGAAA) cis-elements in the MdDFR and MdUFGT promoters to activate their expression as well as promote anthocyanins accumulation [30]. Furthermore, MdbHLH3 was discovered to bind to the LTR cis-element in the promoter of MdBBX20, which is a B-box TF that responded to $1 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ . MdBBX20 could also directly bind to promoters of MdMYB1, MdDFR, and MdANS to improve anthocyanins biosynthesis [74]. The bHLH-TF gene, AH identified through map-based cloning in tomato seedlings, was induced by low temperature $( 1 6 / 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ to control the anthocyanins accumulation by up-regulating the transcription level of anthocyanins biosynthesis genes. On the conditions of $1 6 / 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (light/dark), the anthocyanins content was increased by ${ \sim } 5$ -fold compared with 28/ ${ 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ [24]. # MYB TFs

MYB proteins share a highly conserved MYB domain at the N terminus, comprising one to four imperfect repeats. The DNA binding domain of MYB proteins is composed of 50–53 amino acids residues, which form each domain into three a-helices. Based on the number of imperfect adjacent MYB repeats (R1, R2, and R3) in

the MYB domain, the MYB proteins are primarily classified into four major subgroups: 4R-like MYB proteins, R1R2R3-type MYB proteins, R2R3-type MYB proteins, and MYB-related proteins.

The maize Lc gene was the first plant bHLH TF to be identified. Lc overexpression has been shown to significantly increase the anthocyanins content in several species [77,78]. NtAN2 as an R2R3-MYB TF was reported to coordinate the anthocyanins biosynthesis in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum L.) flowers [79]. Results from the accumulation of anthocyanins in Camellia sinensis suggested that CsR2R3-MYB genes were induced in response to cold [80]. CS can significantly induce the

expression of NtAN2, and then form an Lc-NtAN2 complex to activate anthocyanins biosynthesis (Figure 3) in Lc-transgenic tobacco lines [56]. It is common that CSinduced MYB family TFs participate in anthocyanins biosynthesis. BoPAP1, MdMYB308L, MdMYBPA1, and LlMYB3 are found in recent years can respond to cold and further promote anthocyanins accumulation [25,26,28,29]. Anthocyanins accumulation is basically due to the regulation of TFs on the expression of biosynthesis genes, especially: DFR, ANS, and UFGT. The presence of LTR elements or other binding elements in these genes is the reason why they can respond to CS. It was found that CS contributed to the effect of MdMYBPA1 on the ANS and UFGT promoter activity levels [29]. A cold-sensitive MYB-TF, MdMYB308L enhanced the binding of MdbHLH33 to the promoters of MdCBF2 and MdDFR through direct protein interaction. The overexpression of MdMYB308L increased anthocyanins accumulation [28]. Under CS, the total anthocyanins contents in the purple kale were about 50-fold higher than that grown in the greenhouse. An R2R3-type MYB TF, BoPAP1 can be strongly induced by cold. Quantitative real-time RT-PCR analysis proved that BoPAP1 activated a subset of anthocyanins biosynthesis genes, such as DFR and ANS in the purple kale [25]. BoPAP1 makes a great contribution to the extreme increase of anthocyanins. Thus far, 50-fold is the highest level of CS promoting metabolite accumulation. The role of BoPAP1 and its homologous families in the accumulation of anthocyanins or flavonoids should also be paid more attention.

Some MYB TFs are involved in the CBF pathway. Under $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ stress, MdMYB23 not only combines with the MdANR gene to directly increase anthocyanins content but also with MdCBFs to improve CS tolerance [31]. MdMYB88/124 was also induced at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and promoted anthocyanins accumulation. MdMYB88/124 directly regulated the expression of MdCCA1 (Circadian Clock Associated 1) by binding to the AACCG motif of its promoter. Activated MdCCA1 controlled the expression of MdCBF, whereas the direct mechanism of promoting accumulation is not clear [57]. CBF1 was discovered to regulate two Arabidopsis glycosyltransferases responsible for anthocyanins production [81]. MdMYB88 and MdMYB124 could also be possible to regulate anthocyanins accumulation by CBF-dependent pathway (Figure 3).

# Post-translation modification

Post-translation modification includes: protein phosphorylation, dephosphorylation, ubiquitination, etc. Cold enhanced the binding activity of MdbHLH3

protein to the promoters of MdDFR and MdUFGT [30] which is due to the phosphorylation of MdbHLH3 protein after cold exposure. The encoding of MdMIEL1 to RING E3 ubiquitin ligase in the apple was determined by a yeast screening assay. It ubiquitinated the protein of MdMYB308L and MdMYB1, causing their degradation [28,82] and resulting in the negative regulation on cold tolerance as well as anthocyanins accumulation. However, CS accelerated the degradation of the MdMIEL1 protein and reduced the adverse effects on plant cold tolerance and anthocyanins accumulation [28]. Besides MdMIEL1, MdBT2 is also a ubiquitination active protein in apple, which can cause MdMYB23 protein degradation. Treatment with $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ inhibited the transcription of MdBT2 and increased the stability of MdMYB23 protein, which is beneficial to the accumulation of anthocyanins [31]. Sumoylation can protect the protein from ubiquitination. $1 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ increased the expression level of a Small Ubiquitin-like Modifier (SUMO) E3 ligase MdSIZ1 and induced the sumoylation of MdMYB1. MdSIZ1 inhibited the conjugation of ubiquitin molecules to the MdMYB1 protein, thus promoting MdMYB1 protein stability and anthocyanins biosynthesis [83] (Table 3).

It can be seen that CS improves the accumulation of the metabolites through many post-translational modifications (Figure 3). Most modifications did not cause a particularly obvious accumulation of secondary metabolites, which is mainly related to the nature of the protein itself. What’s more, significant modifications ways and modification sites need to be discovered and verified in future exploration. Some modifications are involved in the degradation of beneficial proteins. CS can inhibit degradation, which also reflects the strict regulatory network that plants have evolved to adapt to the environment.

Most TFs are located in the nucleus through nuclear localization signals (NLSs) to regulate the transcription process. There are a few TFs called membrane-bound TFs (MTFs), which are anchored to the plasma membrane, endoplasmic reticulum, or nuclear membrane through a different transmembrane (TM) domain [84]. Under stress, the nuclear translocation of MTFs has been activated by posttranscriptional regulation mechanisms, such as alternate splicing, phosphorylation, and intramembrane proteolysis [85–88]. VaWRKY12 from Vitis amurensis was localized in the nucleus and cytoplasm at room temperature, only in the nucleus after cold treatment [89]. But the mechanisms of VaWRKY12 during translocation should be elucidated.

# bHLH TFs and AP2/ERF TFs induced the biosynthesis of terpenoids

# bHLH TFs

Cold responsive bHLH family TFs are also observed in A. annua (Figure 4). Dual-luciferase assays demonstrated that AabHLH112 enhances the promoter activity of artemisinin biosynthesis genes and AaERF1 gene. AaERF1, an AP2/ERF TF, directly and positively regulates the expression of artemisinin biosynthesis genes. Y1H assays indicated that AabHLH112 could bind to the AaERF1 promoter, which can significantly up-regulate AaERF1 expression. Overexpression of AabHLH112 significantly upregulated the expression levels of AaERF1 gene and artemisinin biosynthesis genes and consequently promoted artemisinin production [59]. Besides, AaMYC2-Like responds to a variety of abiotic stress, especially temperature stress. CS can effectively improve the transcription level of AaMYC2-Like and finally increase the contents of artemisinin by ${ \sim } 4$ -fold. In wild A. annua, artemisinin contents are very low, and it is indeed a pleasure to increase it 4-fold. Studies have

shown that a variety of phytohormones could also induce the expression of AaMYC2-Like [49], so this result could be due to the dual effects of CS. Firstly, CS directly induces the expression of AaMYC2-Like. Secondly, CS promotes the increase of phytohormones, then phytohormones indirectly induce the expression of AaMYC2-Like.

# AP2/ERF TFs

APETALA2/ETH RESPONSE FACTOR (AP2/ERF) family TFs have emerged as key regulators of diverse specialized metabolites, including steroidal glycoalkaloids in Solanum lycopersicum and S. tuberosum, nicotine in N. tabacum, and a variety of terpenoids with different medicinal values. AP2/ERF family is divided into five subfamilies: AP2 (APETALA2), ERF (ETH-responsive factor), DREB (dehydration-responsive element binding proteins), RAV (related to ABI3/VP1), and Soloist. AP2/ ERF TFs regulate target gene expression by binding to a cis-acting promoter region element known as the

dehydration-responsive element/C-repeat (DRE/CRT) element, or GCC-box.

ERF and DREB are the two largest AP2/ERF subfamilies genes and the constituent genes are mainly to be involved in responses to CS [90,91]. As central regulators of the CS signaling pathway, CBF1, CBF2, and CBF3 of the DREB subfamily can bind to cis-elements in the COR (COLD RESPONSE) gene promoter and activate their expressions [73,92]. A large number of studies have shown that a plant’s cell membrane is the primary site to sense temperature signals. Cold stimulation reduces the membrane fluidity and activates the calcium channel on the cell membrane, causing the $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ level in the cytoplasm to rise rapidly, affecting some calcium-related proteins. For example, the calciumdependent protein kinase CPK27 in tomatoes can regulate the production of ABA during the cold response by activating the production of ROS and NO and the MPKs cascade [93]. With the increase of ABA level, PYL-ABA-PP2C complex was formed to release SnRK2 kinase from PP2C, SnRK2 kinase phosphorylated ICE1 and played a role in the transcription regulation of the CBF family [94–97]. CBFs play an important role in the regulation of target genes under CS, including glucose metabolism, lipid metabolism, cell wall modification, and hormone signaling [98,99]. In the ERF subfamily, a cold-responsive ERF TF, VaERF092 from Vitis amurensis regulates VaWRKY33 by binding to its promoter of GCC-box, thereby enhancing CS tolerance [92]. CS promoted artemisinin biosynthesis by increasing the expression levels of AaERF1/2 and AaORA, which positively regulate artemisinin biosynthetic genes (Figure 4) [15].

# Other TFs

In plants, NAC (NAM, ATAF, and CUC) is a class of TFs involved in growth regulation and is associated with abiotic stress [62,63,100]. AaNAC1 expression could be induced by cold $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ , which suggests that it might be a key regulator of stress signaling pathways in A. annua [62]. In S. lycopersicum, SlNAC4 can be induced by CS and as a positive regulator to increase carotenoid accumulation [63]. Blinin is an important diterpenoid compound, which is a characteristic compound in Conyza blinii H. Lev. Under $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ treatment, the expression profile of CbWRKY24 achieved the highest at 6 h and then dropped, which was similar to CbSQE, Cbb-AS in the MVA pathway and CbDXR, CbDXS, CbGGPP in the MEP pathways and consequently affected the changes of blinin and total saponin content [64]. In V. amurensis, an AQUILO (AQ) TF was induced by cold, resulting in upregulating the expression of galactinol synthase (GoLS)

and raffinose synthase genes and promoting the accumulation of raffinose family oligosaccharides [61] (Table 2). The transcription level of PgMYB1 in P. ginseng increased by 3.9-fold after being treated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 6 { \mathfrak { h } }$ (Figure 4). PgMYB1 can also be induced by many hormones [60], but its effect on secondary metabolites of P. ginseng still needs to be further explored.

What is more, treatment at $4 { - } 1 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for several days resulted in significant increases of asiaticoside and madecassoside in Centella asiatica (L.) Urban [20]. The concentration of glycyrrhizin in Glycyrrhiza uralensis at cold conditions for 7 days twice increased than keep treatment at ${ 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ [21]. In the process of accumulation of these secondary metabolites, cold-responsive TFs need to be further explored.

# Cold stress promotes the gene expression and enzyme activity

Stress alleviation relies on numerous changes at the biochemical, physiological, and molecular levels. It is known that exposure to cold results in the up- and down-regulation of thousands of genes, which can be measured intuitively by qRT-PCR. This part summarizes the changes of gene expression in flavonoids and terpenoids biosynthesis pathways under CS.

# Cold stress promotes flavonoids biosynthesis genes and epigenetics

Flavonoids are a class of secondary metabolites widely found in plants. As precursors, chalcone and flavanone can be converted into other flavonoids under catalysis by different enzymes [101,102]. The study of the influence of temperature change on anthocyanins accumulation is particularly extensive. Anthocyanins are natural water-soluble pigments, belonging to the flavonoid family. They are distributed in many plants’ organs and can maintain plants’ normal metabolism under various abiotic stresses [8,103]. In addition, they have great medicinal value, such as reducing the risk of cardiovascular disease, relieving the oxidative stress in the liver, and treating liver fibrosis [104].

# Gene expression level related to flavonoids

PAL is the first key enzyme gene in phenylpropanoid metabolism and plays a key role in the biosynthesis pathways of various secondary metabolites, especially flavonol, anthocyanins, and other antioxidants. In addition, as the last step of anthocyanins biosynthesis, UFGT expression level and activity are particularly important for anthocyanins accumulation. The effect of

cold on anthocyanins accumulation in grape skin was investigated and it was found that overnight $( 1 0 - 1 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ treatment accelerated berry anthocyanins accumulation compared with the control $( 1 5 - 2 0 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } )$ . Molecular analysis revealed that the transcription of four key genes (CHS, F3H, MYBA1, and UFGT) in the anthocyanins biosynthesis pathway was increased [105].

In addition, the temperature has shown a mixed effect on the ratio of different metabolites. For example, with the decrease of temperature (from 9.7 to $0 . 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ , the ratio of quercetin-to-kaempferol increases. Quercetin accumulation is the result of the increase of rutin glucosidase activity. The high ROS caused by CS may be the main reason for this phenomenon. As the substrate of peroxidase, quercetin plays an important role in plant defense against ROS damage [106,107]. The relative ratio of the di- and tri-substituted B-ring of anthocyanins is also related to the temperature change [108]. Some studies have shown that the ratio of $F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H$ to $\textsf { F 3 ' H }$ expression is similar to that of tri-hydroxylated to di-hydroxylated anthocyanins in grape (Vitis vinifera L.) skin [109–112]. Delphinidin, petunidin, and malvidin are tri-hydroxylated precursors of blue and purple anthocyanins. The ratios of delphinidin-3-glucoside, petunidin-3-glucoside to the total anthocyanins content were greatly reduced under high night temperatures $( 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ compared to low night temperature conditions $( 1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ [113].

# Enzyme activity related to flavonoids

The effect of temperature on the enzyme activity of flavonoid biosynthesis genes is widely studied. The content of total flavonoids and the activity of: PAL, C4H, and 4CL in many plants increased significantly after cold treatment [5,6,114], and flavonoid glycosidase activity is increased in CS conditions [107]. In berries, anthocyanins accumulation at night under CS is higher than that at night under high temperatures. Enzyme activity test showed that PAL and UFGT activities decreased in berries growing at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and UFGT activity

is much more correlated with flavonoid glycosides accumulation than UFGT expression [113,115]. Therefore, CS increases the activity of key enzyme genes more directly than increasing their expression levels.

# Effect of cold stress on glycosylation

The modification, such as glycosylation and acylation of metabolite skeleton, contributes to metabolites diversity. First, CS promotes the production of sugar ligands. Sugar functions as an osmotic stabilizer, which helps plants resistant to CS. Therefore, the degradation of starch will participate in the response of plants to cold [116]. After long-term low-temperature storage $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ) ,$ potato tubers entered a dormant physiological state. Pho1a is involved in the degradation of stored starch in potato tubers and then accumulated monosaccharides [117]. The higher concentration of sugar can be used as a cryoprotectant for plants at CS [118].

Glycosyltransferases can utilize monosaccharides to form glycosides (Figure 5). Arabidopsis glycosyltransferase UGT79B2/B3 could catalyze the synthesis of cyanidin rhamnoside and 3-O-glucose cyanidin rhamnoside by adding UDP-rhamnose to cyanidin and cyanidin 3-Oglucoside, respectively. This process is strongly induced by CS. Low freezing temperatures for a short time induced the decrease of the flavonols proportion and formed complex flavonol glycosides [119]. Glycosylation modification also affects the flow of substances. The UGT76F1 and indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) biosynthesis gene YUC competes for the same precursor indole-3- pyruvic acid (IPyA), temperature and light affects UGT and controls the level of auxin [120].

Glycosylation can change the hydrophilicity, chemical stability, and biological activity of the receptor molecule, which is helpful for its transportation and storage in cells and organisms.

# Epigenetics related to flavonoids

Epigenetics is a rapidly developing discipline. More and more studies show that the epigenetic mechanism plays an important role in response to abiotic stress. The epigenetic mechanism mainly includes DNA methylation, histone modification, histone variation, and some changes of non-coding RNA (ncRNA), which affect the structure of chromatin and change gene expression [121,122]. DNA methylation can cause gene transcription inhibition, and its responses to temperature have been reported. In Prunus persica, CS resulted in lower expression levels of DNA methyltransferases gene and higher expression levels of DNA demethylase genes. High transcription levels of anthocyanins biosynthesis genes are related to low promoter methylation levels [123]. CsAN1 is R2R3-MYB TF. It can form an MBW complex with bHLH TF and WD-repeat protein to regulate anthocyanins accumulation in C. sinensis L. leaves[124]. CS-induced the demethylation of CsAN1 promoter resulting in up-regulation of gene expression, and then promoted anthocyanins accumulation [27]. In brief, cold-induced DNA demethylation is a key factor in promoting anthocyanins accumulation in plants.

# Cold stress promotes terpenoids biosynthesis genes and enzymes

Terpenoids include monoterpenoid, sesquiterpenoid, diterpenoid, and triterpenoid. Many terpenoids are isolated from medicinal plants, such as Catharanthus roseus, Taxus chinensis, A. annua, Salvia miltiorrhiza, and P. ginseng, which have a wide range of pharmacological effects [125–127]. CS can affect the specific biosynthesis genes of different terpenoids and promote their accumulation.

# Gene expression level related to sesquiterpenoid and diterpenoid

The increased gene expression of farnesene (FPS), sesquiterpene synthase (STS) led to the biosynthesis of extra sesquiterpenoids in post-harvest Chinese toon buds at 3 or 5 days of cold storage. Besides, some unigenes encoding CYP450, which efficiently convert terpenoid into its oxide, were highly expressed following cold storage [128]. As a sesquiterpenoid, artemisinin is well-known for its irreplaceable curative effect on the devastating parasitic disease, malaria. The change observed in artemisinin contents was due to temperature effects, the artemisinin contents of A. annua planted at Da Lat (about $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ were 3–4 times greater than those cultivated at Tho Duc Province (about $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ in Vietnam [17]. The mRNA levels of the artemisinin

biosynthetic pathway genes: ADS, CYP71AV1, DBR2, and ALDH1 are all increased. Cold exposure $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ induces ADS and CYP71AV1 mRNA levels increased, which were up to 10-fold and 7-fold as the control, respectively [129]. The expression of AaDXS was significantly upregulated at 6 h and $1 2 \mathsf { h }$ under cold $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ stress [130]. Artemisinic acid and dihydroartemisinic acid are the precursors of artemisinin. Interestingly, the ratio of artemisinin: artemisinic acid: dihydroartemisinic acid exhibits significant variations in different regions. The ratio of artemisinin: dihydroartemisinic acid can be significantly improved by $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ treatment $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ [15]. The ratio change is due to high ROS levels in plants caused by lower temperature, some of the substances that can react with ROS were decreased. As an antioxidant, dihydroartemisinic acid reacts with ROS under various stresses through oxidation rather than enzymatic reaction to produce artemisinin [16,131], which is the main reason for the change in ratio. Therefore, environmental pressure is very important for the proportion of different metabolites. Especially for medicinal plants, changes in the proportion of ingredients will cause efficacy changes.

In S. miltiorrhiza, SmGGPP synthetase gene is involved in diterpenoid biosynthesis and can also be induced by $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ due to the presence of LTRE (CCGAAA), a cis-element in the promoter region [132]. The total amount of volatile terpenes, especially sesquiterpenoids, and their oxidation products increased significantly after cold storage $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ in Toona sinensis. In addition, the cold caused the high expression level of the taxol biosynthesis key enzyme gene (DXR, HMGR, GGPP, DBAT) and maintain it for a longer time. Under low temperature, the genes related to the biosynthesis pathway of carotenoids in C. sinensis all enhanced [133]. Also, the synergistic induction of methyl JA (MJ) can improve the taxol production and make the production achieve 54.7 mg/L [18].

# Gene expression level related to triterpenoid

In P. ginseng, ginsenosides content increased by 2.53- fold after 7 days of stimulation at $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the early stage of adventitious root cultures [134]. The expression of PgCYP736B, a high homology with CYP716A12, could be increased by 8.3-fold at $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ after cold stimulation [135]. Moreover, PgCYP736B could be significantly induced by MeJA, so the gene is thought to be directly or indirectly involved in ginsenosides biosynthesis [135]. In addition, protopanaxadiol (PPD) type and protopanaxatriol (PPT) type ginsenosides are both contained in P. notoginseng and P. ginseng, whereas the ratio of PPT: PPD in P. ginseng is greater than in P.

notoginseng. Total saponins of P. notoginseng showed obvious anti-inflammatory effects, while total saponins of P. ginseng did not. The difference in the ratio may be one of the reasons for this distinction [136]. CYP716A53v2 is a key enzyme gene that catalyzes PPD to PPT. Its expression level was significantly negatively correlated with average temperature [137]. Whether or not this change is related to ROS (like artemisinin) needs to be further explored. In P. ginseng cell culture, intermittent cold stimulation (three cycles of culture at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $1 2 \mathsf { h }$ and $5 ^ { \circ } C$ for 12 h) can maximize the content of PPD-type ginsenosides compared with the control $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ group, while continuous cold $( 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } , \mathsf { \Omega } 1 2 \mathsf { h } )$ stimulated the accumulation of PPT-type ginsenosides [19].

# Problems and perspectives

Cold stress often results in the accumulation of secondary metabolites. Especially, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ is commonly used as the model temperature for mining transcription regulators. Under cold conditions, the research on anthocyanins is extensive and in-depth, and there are many studies on its transcriptional regulation and post-translational modification. Cold-induced: TFs, NtAN2, AH, BoPAP1, etc., can increase anthocyanins content by more than 5-fold, among which BoPAP1 is the most effective TF in anthocyanins synthesis. When cold strongly induced TF BoPAP1, anthocyanins content of purple kale increases by 50-fold, which is the highest level of temperature-induced accumulation of metabolites.

![](images/387da549f09047c0966f518a947b43e685e3f507d8a2143156f326ecb00f83db.jpg)  
As an important environmental factor in plant growth, cold stimulation has the following four applications. Application Figure 6.(A1): The related regulatory factors mined by omics technology under cold stress can also be used in genetic engineering breeding to endow plants with cold tolerance. A2: As an abiotic inducer, to analyze its mechanism and further increase the content of active components. A3: Mining cold stress induced glycosyltransferases involved in glycosylation of secondary metabolits. A4: Combined with the cold stress regulatory mechanism and the functional genes induced by cold stress, further high yield of plant secondary metabolites can be achieved through plant synthetic biology.

However, the relationship between secondary metabolites and temperature change is still not deep, such as alkaloids. In C. roseus, we only know that the content of bisindole alkaloids was higher in summer, while the content of vindoline was higher in winter and spring [138], The mechanism between this change and temperature or other environmental factors remains uncertain.

Cold is an important environmental factor. Its applications in medicinal plants are as given in Figure 6. Cold stress has a great influence on the accumulation of plant secondary metabolites. Firstly, the relationship between temperature and plant secondary metabolites is worth exploring, the cold-responsive factors mined by omics technology under CS can also be used in genetic engineering breeding to endow plants with cold tolerance. Secondly, the changing temperature can be used as an important metabolic regulation strategy to increase the content of active components in plants. Many secondary metabolites of plants, such as Astragalus membranaceus (Fisch.) Bunge. and P. ginseng, are mainly glycoside compounds. Cold stimulation can be used as a way to mine important UGT genes. Most importantly, the accumulation mechanism of important metabolites is revealed and applied to plant synthetic biology, which will expand the application scope of plant secondary metabolites in the food and drug industry.
