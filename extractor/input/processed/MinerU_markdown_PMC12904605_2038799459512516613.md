# ABSTRACT

Members of Fusarium sambucinum species complex (FSAMSC), major mycotoxigenic phytopathogens, affect cereal crops such as wheat and rice. The predominant members of FSAMSC, F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale, are distinguished by their trichothecene chemotypes, primarily nivalenol (NIV) and deoxynivalenol (DON) chemotype. While DON has been linked to virulence in certain FSAMSC species, NIV chemotypes exhibit increased aggressive in rice, whereas DON chemotypes preferentially infect wheat. However, the genetic factors driving these chemotype–host associations remain unclear. This study investigated the genetic linkage between DON/NIV chemotype and host preference through molecular and phenotypic analyses. RNA sequencing revealed host-dependent differential expression of trichothecene biosynthetic (TRI) genes during wheat and rice infection by DON and NIV chemotypes. Sequencing confirmed that NIV chemotypes carry a functional TRI13 gene encoding a trichothecene C-4 hydroxylase, whereas DON chemotypes harbour a nonfunctional TRI13 pseudogene (ψTRI13). Genetic manipulation of TRI13 confirmed its role in determining DON/NIV chemotype and host preference. TRI13 deletion in NIV strains converted them into a DON chemotype, while complementation of $\psi T R I I 3$ in DON strains generated a hybrid chemotype producing both toxins, with the 3-acetyl-DON chemotype showing a more pronounced hybrid profile than the 15-acetyl-DON chemotype. Pathogenicity assays revealed that TRI13 deletion reduced virulence in rice but enhanced colonisation in wheat. Conversely, TRI13 complementation increased rice aggressiveness and decreased wheat infection. This study provides the first genetic evidence for host preference driven by DON/NIV chemotypes in F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale, offering insights for improved mycotoxin surveillance and disease management strategies.

# 1 | Introduction

Members of the Fusarium sambucinum species complex (FSAMSC), encompassing the F. graminearum species complex (FGSC or known as Graminearum clade) and phylogenetically allied taxa, serve as primary aetiological agents of devastating cereal diseases globally, including Fusarium head blight (FHB) in wheat and barley (Goswami and Kistler  2004), Fusarium panic blight (FPB) in rice (sometimes referred to as Fusarium head blight or spikelet rot of rice) and Fusarium ear rot (FER) in maize worldwide (Dong et al. 2020; Kazan et al. 2012). These pathogens not only cause substantial yield losses but also contaminate grains with a diverse array of mycotoxins, exacerbating economic and food safety risks. Most FSAMSC species synthesise complex mycotoxin profiles in infected plant tissues, with type B trichothecenes, particularly deoxynivalenol (DON), nivalenol (NIV) and their acetyl derivatives, representing the most prevalent and agriculturally significant mycotoxins due to their severe threats to human and animal health (Chen et al. 2019; Goswami and Kistler 2004). 

Trichothecenes are classified into four types based on structural modifications of their tricyclic cores. Among these, type A (e.g., T-2 toxin, HT-2 toxin) and type B (e.g., DON, NIV) trichothecenes are the most prevalent in agricultural commodities. While both DON and NIV share a conserved A-ring carbonyl group at C-8 (Ichinoe et  al.  1983; Maeda et  al.  2016), they diverge at C-4, NIV possesses a hydroxyl group at C-4 that is absent in DON (Figure S1A). This structural distinction correlates with differential biosynthetic pathways regulated by strainspecific genetic determinants. Notably, type A trichothecenes, which lack a C-8 keto group, are produced by species such as F. sporotrichioides, F. armeniacum, and F. langsethiae (Marasas et al. 1987; McCormick et al. 2011; Nichea et al. 2015; Thrane et al. 2004; Witte et al. 2024). In contrast, type B trichothecenes are primarily synthesised by members of the Graminearum clade of FSAMSC, including F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale (Qiu et al. 2019), but are also produced by other Fusarium species outside this clade. These includes members of the FSAMSC, such as $F .$ kyushuense (Sambucium clade) and F. transvaalense (Brachygibbosum clade) (Laraba et al. 2021), as well as species within the F. incarnatum-equiseti species complex (FIESC), including F. arcuatisporum and F. luffae (Qiu et al. 2024). 

Within the FSAMSC, which comprises at least 41 formally described species (Laraba et  al.  2021; O'Donnell et  al.  2008; O'Donnell et al. 2004; O'Donnell et al. 2000; Sarver et al. 2011; van der Lee et al. 2015), strains exhibit distinct trichothecene chemotypes: DON and primarily 3-acetyl DON (3Ac-DON chemotype), DON and primarily 15-acetyl DON (15Ac-DON chemotype), and NIV and its acetylated derivative 4-acetyl NIV (NIV chemotype) (Miller et  al.  1991). A previous study indicated that a new type A trichothecene known as NX-2, along with its acetyl derivatives, was reported in $F .$ . graminearum (Varga et al. 2015). 

Fungal population studies have elucidated distinct biogeographic patterns of Fusarium species and their trichothecene chemotypes. F. graminearum is a widely distributed FHB pathogen globally (van der Lee et al. 2015), while F. asiaticum is largely 

responsible for the incidence of FHB in wheat and FPB in rice in Asia specifically in China, Japan, Nepal, and Korea (Carter et al. 2002; Desjardins and Proctor 2011; Suga et al. 2008; Lee et al. 2009; Lee et al. 2012; Qiu et al. 2014). Within China, crop rotation systems strongly correlate with pathogen speciation and chemotype prevalence. F. asiaticum with the 3Ac-DON or NIV chemotype is the major species in the Yangtze valley and southwest China where rice and wheat are rotated, while F. graminearum with the 15Ac-DON chemotype is predominant in the Huanghuaihai and northeastern regions under maize– wheat rotation (Gale et al. 2002; Hao et al. 2017; Ji et al. 2006; Zhang, Jia, et al. 2012; Qiu et al. 2014). 

Certain trichothecenes serve as critical virulence determinants in Fusarium–plant interactions. To date, this aspect has been examined primarily in $F .$ graminearum and a limited number of closely related species, with a particular focus on DON and NIV. Pioneering studies established DON as an essential virulence factor for FHB progression in wheat following F. graminearum infection (Proctor et  al.  1995). Genetic disruption of the TRI5 gene, encoding the trichodiene synthase responsible for the initial step of the trichothecene biosynthesis, results in abolished DON production and significantly attenuated aggressiveness in wheat heads (Desjardins et al. 1996). Subsequent investigations demonstrated analogous roles for NIV, where TRI5 gene deletion mutants derived from NIVproducing strains exhibit compromised virulence in wheat spikelets (Maier et al. 2006). Recently, host-selective modulation of Fusarium pathogenicity by trichothecene chemotypes has been increasingly documented. Members of FSAMSC with DON chemotype strains dominate FHB epidemics in wheat, exhibiting superior aggressive than those with the NIV chemotype. In contrast, NIV producers demonstrated enhanced virulence relative to DON producers, particularly in FPB in rice (Dong et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2018). These findings underscore a DON/NIV chemotype-dependent host adaptation strategy within FSAMSC, where trichothecene structural variations (e.g., C-4 hydroxylation in NIV) probably interact with hostselective defence mechanisms to shape infection outcomes. 

The trichothecene biosynthetic pathway in Fusarium species has been thoroughly characterised. In F. graminearum, 15 TRI genes governing this pathway are organised across three discrete chromosomal loci: the core TRI loci consist of a 12- gene locus, a TRI1–TRI16 locus, and a TRI101 locus (Chen et  al.  2019). Chemotype specificity in Fusarium strains is dictated by structural and regulatory polymorphisms within TRI genes. The allelic variation in the TRI13 gene within the core TRI cluster determines the bifurcation between DON and NIV production (Lee et  al.  2002). TRI13 encodes a trichothecene C-4 hydroxylase that is responsible for trichothecene C-4 hydroxylation of the trichothecene skeleton, a prerequisite for NIV biosynthesis (Brown et al. 2002; Lee et al. 2002) (Figure S1A). Consequently, the NIV chemotype strains retain a functional TRI13 allele, while the DON-producing strains harbour a disrupted TRI13 pseudogene (ψTRI13), abolishing C-4 hydroxylation capacity. 

This study aimed to establish conclusive genetic evidence for trichothecene DON and NIV chemotype-mediated host 

selectivity in representative members of FSAMSC, addressing the following three key objectives: (i) comparative transcriptomic profiling: to analyse host-selective expression dynamics of TRI genes in DON- and NIV-producing strains during wheat versus rice infection in F. graminearum and F. asiaticum; (ii) chemotype engineering: to determine the feasibility of redirecting trichothecene biosynthesis between DON and NIV through targeted TRI13 manipulation (deletion/complementation); and (iii) pathogenicity comparison: to evaluate how TRI13-driven trichothecene DON and NIV chemotype shifts alter fungal virulence in wheat and rice. Our findings conclusively demonstrate that trichothecene chemotypes serve as evolutionary determinants of host adaptation in selected species of FSAMSC. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Host Selective Gene Expression of TRI Cluster in DON and NIV Chemotypes in F. graminearum and F. asiaticum

Fusarium graminearum DON chemotype strain FG187 and F. asiaticum NIV chemotype strain FA031 (Table 1) were inoculated into wheat and rice seedlings as indicated in Figure 1A and the transcriptional profiling of the resulting samples was compared. The principal component analysis (PCA) demonstrates that the global gene expression profile of FG187 and FA031 was most dramatically influenced by the host plant (rice vs. wheat), with further significant modulation occurring across the time course of infection (3 days post-inoculation [dpi] vs. 10 dpi) within each host (Figure 1B,C). The heatmap demonstrates distinct host-preference in TRI gene induction: TRI gene expression in the F. graminearum DON chemotype strain FG187 was strongly induced in wheat (Figure  1D), while the F. asiaticum NIV chemotype strain FA031 exhibited a higher level of expression in rice (Figure 1E). Expression of the gene cluster was more activated during the infection stage in the preferred hosts than the hyphal development stage in the cultures. 

# 2.2 | Allelic Variation of TRI13 Governs Chemotype Differentiation in F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale

Previous studies have established that allelic variation in the TRI13 gene within the core TRI cluster determines the bifurcation between DON and NIV production (Lee et al. 2002). In this study, we amplified and sequenced the TRI13 open reading frame (ORF) from seven Chinese strains representing distinct chemotypes: four DON-producing and three NIV-producing strains of F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, or F. meridionale (Table 1). The obtained sequences were subsequently aligned. All three NIVproducing strains of F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, or F. meridionale possessed a functional TRI13 allele. In contrast, the four DON chemotype strains of F. graminearum or F. asiaticum carried a nonfunctional TRI13 pseudogene (ψTRI13) characterised by six deletions (11-, 36-, 61-, 60-, 19-, and 99-bp) within the ORF (Figure S1B,C). 

# 2.3 | DON and NIV Are Key Virulence Factors for F. graminearum and F. asiaticum in Wheat and Rice

To determine whether trichothecene DON and NIV are required for rice infection, we generated TRI5 deletion mutants in both DON chemotype (Fa_3AcDON_SW73) and NIV chemotype (Fa_NIV_RR108) progenitors by transforming them with a TRI5 gene replacement construct (Figure S2A). PCR screening confirmed successful generation of deletion mutants (ΔTri5) and ectopic insertion transformants (Tri5-EC) from each wildtype strain (Figure  S2D,E). Liquid chromatography coupled to tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) analysis revealed complete deficiency of DON/NIV production and their acetylderivatives in ΔTri5 mutants, whereas the wild-type strains and Tri5-EC transformants produced characteristic mycotoxin profiles: DON chemotypes yielded DON (2.8 min) and 3Ac-DON (4.1 min), while NIV chemotypes produced NIV $( 3 . 2 \mathrm { m i n } )$ and 4Ac-NIV (3.6 min) (Figure 2A–G and Table 2). 

We compared the virulence of ΔTri5 mutants and their wildtype strains on wheat and rice heads. Pathogenicity assays revealed that TRI5 deletion significantly reduced disease severity (Figure  2H–K) and area under the disease progress curve (AUDPC) values in wheat and rice heads in both DON and NIV wild-type progenitors (Figure 2L,M). On wheat, ΔTri5 infections remained restricted to the inoculated spikelets (Figure 2N). On rice, spray-inoculated ΔTri5 mutants produced significantly fewer rice panicles with brown-to-black blight symptoms compared to wild-type and Tri5-EC controls (Figure 2O). The 1000-kernel weight of wheat or rice panicles infected with ΔTri5 mutants increased significantly compared to those infected with wild-type strains or Tri-EC transformants (Figure 2P,Q). These results demonstrate that DON and NIV are crucial virulence factors for Fusarium pathogenesis in both wheat and rice. 

# 2.4 | Genetic Modification of TRI13 Alters DON/NIV Chemotypes in F. Graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale

TRI13 was deleted by transforming three NIV producers (Fg_ NIV_SW75, Fa_NIV_SR27, and Fm_NIV_SR5) with a TRI13 gene replacement construct (Figure  S2B). PCR screening confirmed successful generation of deletion mutants (ΔTri13) and ectopic transformants (Tri13-EC) (Figure  S2F–H). When cultured in rice medium, ΔTri13 mutants lost NIV production and instead accumulated DON when cultured in rice medium. LC– MS/MS analysis revealed abolition of NIV and 4Ac-NIV production. The ΔTri13 mutant switched its trichothecene production into a combination of DON, 15Ac-DON and/or trace amounts of 3Ac-DON, while Tri13-EC strains maintained parental NIV/4Ac-NIV profiles (Table 2 and Figure 3).

# 2.5 | Host Preference Mediated by DON/NIV Chemotype in Selective Species of FSAMSC

Pathogenicity assays assessed virulence between TRI13 mutants and wild-type progenitors on wheat and rice. In NIVproducing strains (Fg_NIV_SW75, Fa_NIV_SR27, and Fm_NIV_SR5), TRI13 deletion significantly increased pathogenicity in wheat. ΔTri13 mutants had increased disease severity (Figure 5A–C) and elevated AUDPC values compared to wild-type and Tri13-EC transformants (Figure 5D–F), with infected wheat showing significantly reduced 1000-kernel weight (Figure  5G–I). For rice, however, switch into DON chemotype via TRI13 deletion reduced rice virulence. The disease symptoms developed more slowly in ΔTri13 (Figure 5J–L), with lower AUDPC values than NIV wild-type and Tri13-EC controls (Figure 5D–F). Rice grains infected with ΔTri13 mutants were significantly heavier than those infected with controls (Figure 5G–I). 

Conversely, introducing functional TRI13 into DON chemotypes (Tri13c ) reduced disease severity in wheat heads but resulted in more severe symptom development in rice panicles (Figure 6A–H). AUDPC values confirmed significantly lower virulence in wheat yet higher virulence in rice for all Tri13c strains compared to wild-type controls (Figure 6I–L). Inoculation with Tri13c resulted in significantly decreased wheat grain weight but increased rice panicle weight than that inoculated with their wild-type strains (Figure 6M–P). Collectively, these chemotypeswitching experiments demonstrate that DON chemotypes exhibit more virulence in wheat, whereas the NIV chemotypes show enhanced adaptation to rice.

To date, several secondary metabolites including trichothecene DON, fusaoctaxin A, and fusaristatin A have been identified as contributors to Fusarium virulence in wheat (Khudhair et  al.  2020; Proctor et  al.  1995; Tang et  al.  2021). Previous studies demonstrated that trichothecene DON and NIV influence the virulence of $F .$ . graminearum in a hostspecific manner. Both DON and NIV serve as key virulence factors during wheat infection but are dispensable for virulence on barley (Desjardins et  al.  1996; Maier et  al.  2006). Deletion of the core trichothecene biosynthetic gene TRI5 reduces virulence on wheat heads yet has no significant effects on barley. On maize, TRI5 deletion impairs virulence in NIV chemotypes but not in DON chemotypes (Maier et al. 2006). FSAMSC members have been documented as causal agents of FPB of rice in rice-growing regions (Dong et al. 2020; Lei et al. 2019; Scheidt et al. 2019), but the roles of DON and NIV in rice pathogenesis remain uncharacterised. In this study, we demonstrate that selective species of FSAMSC (F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale) use both DON and NIV to invade the wheat and rice heads, hence confirming their roles as virulence factors. 

Host preference is evident among certain FSAMSC isolates with distinct trichothecene chemotypes. Consistent with this adaptation, Yang et al. (2018) reported that 3Ac-DON producers are prevalent in wheat whereas NIV-producers dominate in rice. This distribution aligns with pathogenicity studies showing that F. asiaticum with the 3Ac-DON chemotype is more virulent on wheat than NIV chemotype isolates, while $F .$ . asiaticum NIV producers exhibit higher virulence on rice than 3Ac-DON 

counterparts (Dong et  al.  2020). The established importance of trichothecenes in Fusarium pathogenicity on cereal crops has driven extensive research into their biosynthetic mechanisms. To provide genetic evidence supporting trichothecene chemotype-mediated host preference within selective species of FSAMSC, we focused on the TRI13 gene. Nucleotide polymorphisms within the trichothecene biosynthetic gene TRI13 determine DON and NIV chemotypes in $F .$ graminearum (Lee et al. 2002). TRI13 functionality is restricted to NIV-producing Fusarium strains, and phylogenetic evidence suggests DON chemotypes probably evolved from NIV progenitors due to the loss of TRI13 function. 

To test this, we artificially altered trichothecene chemotypes by manipulating TRI13. Deletion of the TRI13 gene converted NIV-producing strains into DON producers in F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale, while complementation of TRI13 in DON strains generated hybrid chemotypes in F. graminearum and $F .$ asiaticum. These hybrids displayed distinct toxigenic profiles depending on their parental background (3Ac-DON vs. 15Ac-DON). In particular, we observed significantly higher 3Ac-NIV to 4Ac-NIV ratios in 15Ac-DON_Tri13c than in the 3Ac-DON_Tri13c (Figure  S3A–E). While DON chemotypes often lack a functional Tri7, the introduction of TRI13 can “unmask” latent C-4 acetylation capacity, either through residual Tri7 activities or alternative acetyltransferases present in DON strains (Figure S4), resulting in detectable  4Ac-NIV. The variations in acetylation potential may explain the differing chemotype outcomes found in the two studies. 

Our study demonstrated that deleting TRI13 in NIV-producing strains decreased their virulence on rice but increased it on wheat in F. graminearum, F. asiaticum, and F. meridionale. Conversely, complementing TRI13 in DON producers increased the virulence of the Tri13c mutants on rice but decreased it on wheat in F. graminearum and F. asiaticum. The DON/NIV trichothecene chemotype-mediated host preference in Fusarium species probably reflects differential host sensitivity to these toxins. Trichothecenes are potent phytotoxins (Abbas et al. 2013; Suzuki and Iwahashi 2014), primarily due to their 60S ribosome binding and inhibition of protein synthesis (Pestka 2010). Despite sharing an identical trichothecene backbone differing only by NIV's additional C-4 hydroxyl group, 

DON and NIV exhibit significantly phytotoxicity across hosts. DON exhibits higher phytotoxicity in barley leaves (Bushnell et al. 2010), Arabidopsis thaliana (Masuda et al. 2007), and the alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (Suzuki and Iwahashi  2014; Wipfler et al. 2019). Conversely, NIV shows strong phytotoxicity in Lemna pausicostata (duckweed) plantlets (Abbas et al. 2013) but lower phytotoxicity than DON in detached A. thaliana leaves (Masuda et al. 2007). 

Host plants exhibit differential response to fungal infection. Trichothecenes play crucial roles in the fungus–host plant interaction. Specifically, DON production is triggered by oxidative stress mediated by reactive oxygen species (ROS) bursts within plant host cells (Kachroo et  al.  2003). This mycotoxin subsequently functions as a key fungal defence mechanism, inhibiting plant cell wall thickening and callose deposition at rachis nodes of spikelets, thereby facilitating pathogen spread between florets (Ilgen et  al.  2009; Jansen et  al.  2005). Differences in TRI gene transcriptional levels between the two chemotypes may also contribute to observed virulence variation. In infected wheat, significantly higher TRI expression was detected in 3Ac-DON producing strains compared to 15Ac-DONand NIV-producing strains (Amarasinghe and Fernando 2016). Our study revealed host-specific TRI induction patterns in DON/NIV trichothecene chemotypes in both wheat and rice, aligning with findings by Lee et  al.  (2014), who reported distinct TRI expression levels between DON- and NIV-producing Fusarium strains cultured in a defined liquid medium (Lee et  al.  2014). Chemotypes exhibiting stronger TRI induction 

during infection may confer greater virulence through enhanced toxin accumulation. 

Plants mitigate trichothecene toxicity through glycosylation, a reaction catalysed by uridine diphosphate-glucosyltransferases (UGTs). These enzymes convert trichothecene into less toxic glucoside conjugates. This UGT-mediated detoxification is a key component of Type II FHB resistance, enabling toxin neutralisation or elimination (Lemmens et  al.  2005). For instance, transgenic wheat expressing a barley UGT detoxifies DON and confers strong resistance to F. graminearum (Li et  al.  2015). However, cereal crop UGTs exhibit significant substrate specificity variation toward different trichothecenes. Overexpression of the A. thaliana UGT DOGT1 enhances DON tolerance, but offers no protection against the 

structurally similar NIV (Poppenberger et al. 2003). Similarly, the rice glucosyltransferase OsUGT79 preferentially glycosylates DON over NIV (Michlmayr et  al.  2018; Warth et  al.  2015). Consequently, comparing the virulence of DON producers with their TRI13 complemented mutants (which primarily produce NIV) in OsUGT79-transformed wheat would elucidate how glycosyltransferase substrate specificity influences host preference. 

Overall, our findings indicate that TRI13-mediated DON/ NIV trichothecene chemotype differentiation is closely associated with host preference between wheat and rice, providing mechanistic insight into chemotype-dependent pathogenicity within the tested members of the FSAMSC. Disruption of TRI13 altered chemotype production and correspondingly shifted infection performance on the two hosts, suggesting that chemotype-specific metabolic outputs influence host colonisation. However, TRI13 alone is unlikely to fully account for host adaptation, as additional TRI genes, regulatory networks, and host-derived signals may also contribute. Future studies combining targeted mutagenesis, regulatory analyses, and host–pathogen interaction assays will be necessary to further elucidate the molecular basis of trichothecene chemotype-associated host preference. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Fusarium Strains and Culture Conditions

We used a collection of Fusarium strains with different chemotypes from our previous studies (Table  1) (Dong et al. 2022; Dong et al. 2020; Qiu and Shi 2014), including F. asiaticum Fa_NIV_RR108 (NIV chemotype), F. graminearum Fg_NIV_SW75 (NIV chemotype), F. meridionale $\mathrm { F m \_ N I V _ { - } }$ SR5 (NIV chemotype), F. graminearum Fg_3Ac-DON_SW45 (3Ac-DON chemotype) and Fg_15Ac-DON_SR14 (15Ac-DON chemotype), as well as F. asiaticum Fa_3Ac-DON_SW73 (3Ac-DON chemotype) and Fa_15Ac-DON_SW74 (15Ac-DON chemotype). Mycelial growth was conducted on potato dextrose agar (PDA, $2 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ potato, $2 0 \mathrm { g }$ dextrose, $2 0 \mathrm { g }$ agar, 1 L water) or potato dextrose broth (PDB, $2 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ potato, $2 0 \mathrm { g }$ dextrose, 1 L water). Conidiation was induced in carboxymethyl cellulose medium (CMC, carboxymethyl cellulose $1 5 \mathrm { g }$ , yeast extract 1 g, $\mathrm { M g S O _ { 4 } } 0 . 5 \mathrm { g }$ , $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } 1 \mathrm { g }$ , $\mathrm { K H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } 1 \mathrm { g }$ , 1 L water) (Leslie and Summerell 2007). General culture conditions for Fusarium strains followed previously described methods (Liu et al. 2021). Strains were routinely cultured on solid plates at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a $1 2 \mathrm { h } / 1 2 \mathrm { h }$ light–dark cycle and in liquid medium at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at $1 8 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ , using a $1 2 \mathrm { h } / 1 2 \mathrm { h }$ light–dark cycle for mycelial growth and continuous $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ light for conidiation. For trichothecene production, strains were first grown on PDA plates at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. Subsequently, five mycelial plugs $5 \mathrm { m m }$ diameter) were inoculated into autoclaved rice medium $2 0 \mathrm { g }$ rice soaked in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ water for $4 \mathrm { h }$ , then autoclaved twice in a $1 5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ Erlenmeyer flask). Cultures were incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 21 days (Lee et al. 2016). After incubation, rice cultures were dried, ground into powder in a ventilated hood, and used for trichothecene extraction. 

# 4.2 | DNA Extraction, PCR Assays and Sequencing

Strains representing different trichothecene chemotypes were grown on PDA plates for 4 days at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Fresh mycelia were harvested, and genomic DNA was extracted using an AxyPrep Multisource Genomic DNA Minprep Kit (Axygen) following the manufacturer's protocol. The TRI13 gene ORF was amplified from genomic DNA of seven selected Fusarium strains using the primers listed in Table  S1. Purified PCR products were sequenced by Shanghai Sangon Biotech Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The obtained TRI13 sequences from different chemotypes were aligned and edited using ClustalW (Thompson et al. 1994). 

# 4.3 | RNA Extraction and Gene Expression Analysis (RNA-Seq)

Conidial suspensions were prepared from F. asiaticum strain (NIV chemotype) FA031 or $F .$ . graminearum strain (15Ac-DON chemotype) FG187. Five mycelial plugs (5 mm diameter) were inoculated into $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ liquid CMC medium (Leslie and Summerell  2007) and incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days with shaking (180 rpm) under light. Cultures were filtered through sterile Miracloth (Calbiochem) to harvest conidia. After centrifugation $( 8 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ , 10 min), conidia were resuspended in sterile water and adjusted to $1 0 ^ { 7 }$ conidia/mL. 

Sterile cotton strips $\mathrm { 4 m m } \times 1 \mathrm { c m } ,$ ) were soaked in the conidial suspension. Three-day-old wheat (Triticum aestivum ‘Jimai 22’) and rice (Oryza sativa subsp. japonica ‘Nangeng 9108’) seedlings were inoculated on the coleoptile following a published protocol (Zhang, van der Lee, et  al.  2012). Following inoculation, seedlings were incubated in a growth chamber at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $9 5 \%$ relative humidity. Infection sites on the coleoptiles were harvested at 0, 3 and 10 days post-inoculation (dpi). Hyphae were harvested from a 3-day-PDB culture. Both plant and hyphal tissues were flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and subsequently lyophilised. 

RNA-Seq libraries were prepared using the TruSeq RNA Sample Prep Kit (Illumina Inc.). Following library quantification via qPCR (KAPA kit, KAPA Biosystems), paired-end sequencing (100-bp read length) was performed on an Illumina HiSeq 2500 platform. Clean reads were mapped to the $F .$ graminearum PH-1 genome (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/ assembly/GCF_000240135.3). Transcripts with an expression level $> 3$ tags per million (TPM) were included in subsequent analysis (Jongeneel et al. 2003; Li and Dewey 2011). The frequencies of each sequenced tag were compared in different samples, and the statistical significance (Q-value) was calculated. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using DEGseq (Wang et al. 2009), with a fold change $\geq 2$ and Q-value $\le 0 . 0 0 1$ considered significant. The raw data of RNAsequencing of this study has been deposited in National Center for Biotechnology Information of United States (https://www. ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) under accession numbers SRR26501284- SRR26591295, SRR27895679-SRR27895714 and is publicly accessible online. 

# 4.4 | Mutant Generation and Identification

To generate ΔTri13 mutants in NIV chemotype strains, a deletion cassette was constructed by fusing $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ flanking regions of TRI13 to a selective marker gene via double-joint PCR (Yu et  al.  2004). This cassette was transformed into the wildtype NIV strains (Table  1). For TRI13 complementation, the TRI13 ORF with its native promoter from an NIV chemotype strain was cloned into pYF11-neo (Bruno et  al.  2004) to generate the recombinant plasmid pYF11-neo-Tri13c , which was subsequently transformed into wild-type DON strains (Table 1). Primers for mutant construction and verification are listed in Table S1. Fungal transformation was conducted using a polyethylene glycol (PEG)-mediated protoplast transformation method as previously described (Proctor et  al.  1995). Transformants were screened, subjected to single-spore isolation, and verified by PCR using primers in Table S1. 

# 4.5 | Trichothecene Analysis

Trichothecenes were extracted by soaking Fusarium rice culture in $8 0 \%$ methanol for 4h with shaking, followed by purification with a dispersive extraction cleanup bag (CNW Technologies), (Lu et al. 2021). Each strain was analysed in triplicate, and the entire experiment was repeated twice. Trichothecene quantification was performed using a high-performance liquid chromatographymass spectrometry–mass spectrometry (HPLC–MS/MS) system (Shimadzu 30A LC system coupled to a Triple Quad 6500 plus, Sciex) Mass spectrometric data were acquired in multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode. The electrospray ionisation (ESI) parameters were set as follows: source temperature, $6 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; curtain gas (nitrogen), 55psi; nebulizer gas (nitrogen), 60psi; auxiliary gas (nitrogen), 30psi; spray voltage, 5, 500V (ESI+) and 4, 500V (ESI−); entrance potential, 10V; and cell exit potential, 6V. 

For LC–MS/MS toxin quantification, the following multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) transitions were used: DON (retention time 3.12min, parent ion $2 9 7 . 2 m / z ,$ , daughter ion 249.1 and $2 0 3 . 1 m / z .$ , declustering potential 80V, and collision energy 16eV and 21eV), 3Ac-DON (retention time 3.94min, parent ion $3 3 9 . 1 m / z$ , daughter ion 231.1 and $2 0 3 . 1 m / z$ , declustering potential 60V, and collision energy 17/20eV), 15Ac-DON (retention time 3.94min, parent ion $3 3 9 . 1 m / z$ , daughter ion 137.2 and $2 6 1 . 2 m / z$ , declustering potential 60V, and collision energy 15/16eV), NIV (retention time $2 . 6 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , parent ion $3 5 7 . 0 m / z$ , daughter ion 281.0 and $3 1 1 . 0 m / z ,$ , declustering potential −50V, and collision energy −18/−14eV) and 4Ac-NIV (retention time $3 . 4 8 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , parent ion $3 5 5 . 1 m / z ,$ , daughter ion 229.1 and $2 4 7 . 1 m / z ,$ , declustering potential 70V, and collision energy 17/15eV) (Table S2). 

For determination of trichothecene 4Ac-NIV and 3Ac-NIV, liquid chromatography-time of flight-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-TOF-MS/MS) analysis was carried out using a previously reported method (Wang et al. 2020). LC-TOF-MS/MS analysis was conducted on an AB SCIEX TripleTOF 4600 system (Concord) equipped with an Atlantis T3 column (ODS, $4 . 6 \times 1 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $3 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ). Raw LC–MS data sets were processed using MS-DIAL software (Tsugawa et  al.  2015) for peak detection, baseline calibration, deconvolution, and peak alignment. 

# 4.6 | Plant Infection Assay

The aggressiveness of Fusarium strains on wheat heads was assessed using the point inoculation method (Guenther et al. 2009). At mid-anthesis, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of a conidial suspension $\mathrm { 1 0 ^ { 5 } }$ conidia/mL) was injected into a central spikelet of sensitive wheat (T. aestivum ‘Jimai 22’). Disease severity was evaluated at 8, 12, 14, and 17 dpi by recording the proportion of diseased spikelets per head. For rice infection, aggressiveness of Fusarium strains was determined by uniformly spraying panicles of sensitive rice (Oryza sativa L. subsp. japonica, cultivar ‘Nangeng 9108’) at the flowering stage with a conidial suspension $\mathrm { ( 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } }$ conidia/mL) (Lei et al. 2019). The proportion of diseased panicles per rice head was evaluated at 3, 7, 12, and 21 dpi. Both assays were conducted in a greenhouse at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $8 0 \%$ relative humidity, with a 16-h/8-h light/dark cycle. Each experiment included ten wheat heads or rice plants per strain and was repeated twice. The AUDPC was calculated using the disease severity data for each strain in both hosts as previously described (Nicolli et al. 2018).
