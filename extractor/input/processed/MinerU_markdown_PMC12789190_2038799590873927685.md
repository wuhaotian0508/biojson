# ABSTRACT

Tung wilt disease, caused by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. fordiis (Fof-1), poses a serious threat to tung oil tree (Vernicia fordii) production. Fortunately, another native Vernicia species in China, V. montana (woody tung oil tree) exhibits high resistance ability to the pathogen. The resistant and susceptible tung trees provide material for the investigation on the mechanism underlying the resistance to Fusarium wilt disease. Root xylem extracts of resistant V. montana significantly inhibited Fof-1 growth compared with that of susceptible V. fordii. Metabolomic analysis of V. montana root xylem revealed that 13 types of flavonoids increased after Fof-1 infection. Of the 13 flavonoids, antimicrobial assays showed that catechin, (−)-epicatechin and (−)-epigallocatechin exhibited an obvious inhibitory effect on Fof-1 growth. Transcriptomic analysis revealed that several genes with up-regulated expression patterns were also enriched in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway after Fof-1 infection in V. montana. Among them, the anthocyanidin reductase (ANR) gene is directly involved in the biosynthesis of antimicrobial (−)-epicatechin and (−)-epigallocatechin. Moreover, transgenic V. montana lines overexpressing the VmANR gene elevated eight types of flavonoid concentrations, and silencing VmANR resulted in a substantial reduction in the levels of catechin and myricitrin. The enzyme activity assay in vitro further verified that VmANR catalysed the formation of (−)-epigallocatechin from the substrate cyanidin. This study identifies VmANR as a critical gene to promote biosynthesis of antimicrobial flavonoids in shaping resistance to Fof-1 infection, and offers an effective strategy for breeding Fusariumresistant tung oil trees. 

# 1 | Introduction

The genus Fusarium contains some of the most serious pathogens worldwide and comprises approximately 300 phylogenetically distinct species (Aoki et al. 2014). They infect a wide range of plant hosts and cause various diseases such as Fusarium head blight, branch canker, root rot, Fusarium crown rot and Fusarium wilt (Bai et  al.  2018; Carranza et  al.  2004; Duan et al. 2022; Sun et al. 2019). Fusarium oxysporum is the causal 

agent of Fusarium wilt, a disease among the ‘Top 10’ plant fungal pathogens, which poses a serious threat to agriculture, horticulture, forestry, aquaculture and natural ecosystems (Dean et al. 2012). More than 120 crop species have been reported to be affected by F. oxysporum (Gordon 2017). The chlamydospores of F. oxysporum can survive in the soil for extended periods. Under favourable conditions, chlamydospores germinate and infect plant roots (Warman and Aitken  2018). Despite extensive research into the mechanisms of infection and resistance

to prevent Fusarium wilt disease in plants, no efficient methods have yet been developed to completely control the outbreak and spread of Fusarium wilt. 

Tung oil tree (Vernicia fordii) is an important woody non-edible oil crop in China. Tung oil, extracted from the seeds of V. fordii, is a high-quality oil with widespread applications in industry, agriculture, the armed forces and medicine (Park et al. 2008). Tung wilt disease, caused by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. fordiis (Fof-1), poses a serious threat to $V .$ fordii production and the tung oil industry. Fusarium wilt in V. fordii occurs most severely during the growth stage (May–July), primarily affecting the roots. This leads to the rotting of taproots and lateral roots. As the disease spots expand between the veins, leaves wilt and fade from green to withered, and even the stems soften; ultimately the whole plant gradually dies (Zhang et al. 2021). Since its first discovery in 1939 in Liuzhou city, Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region in China, the disease has spread to eight provinces causing devastating economic losses (Chen et al. 2016). 

Fusarium wilt is extremely challenging to control due to the diversity and high adaptability of F. oxysporum. Its management relies on integrating multiple strategies, such as planting resistant varieties, fungicide applications, crop rotation, and management of water and fertilisation (Cianchetta and Davis 2015). An increasing number of studies have shown that some plant species or varieties exhibit significant resistance to Fusarium wilt disease. These resistant plants are the focus of our research, as they provide valuable insights into preventing and controlling Fusarium disease. Fortunately, Vernicia montana (woody tung oil tree), another native Vernicia species in China, exhibits high resistance to the Fusarium wilt pathogen although the quantity and quality of its oil production are relatively low. The disease can be partly prevented using V. montana as stock and V. fordii as scion (Chen et al. 2016). Therefore, V. montana is recognised as an indispensable material for investigating the resistance mechanism to Fusarium wilt. 

Resistant and susceptible tung trees offer excellent material for studying the mechanisms underlying resistance to Fusarium 

wilt disease. Here, we found that the root xylem extract of resistant V. montana showed an inhibitory effect on the growth of the pathogen Fof-1. To identify the possible metabolites and genes involved in the resistance of the root xylem of the resistant tung tree species, we analysed the responsive metabolites and genes in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway after Fof-1 infection. The data provide new insight into the resistance mechanisms in tung trees. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Root Xylem Extract Shows an Obvious Inhibition Effect on the Growth of Fof-1 in V. montana

By observing the growth of the pathogen Fof-1 on potato dextrose agar (PDA), the inhibitory differences of root xylem extract of V. montana and V. fordii against Fof-1 were determined. We found that the addition of the root xylem extraction from V. montana to PDA strongly inhibited conidial germination and hyphal growth (Figure 1a). Conversely, the root xylem extract from  V. fordii had no inhibitory effect on the growth of Fof-1 when compared to the extract control (ethyl acetate) and blank control (Figure 1a). 

On the 2nd day, the inhibition rate of V. montana reached the highest, $5 9 . 2 \%$ (Figure 1b). To compare the inhibition difference between V. montana and V. fordii, we calculated the average inhibition rate of root xylem extracts during 2–8 days. The average inhibition rate of V. montana and V. fordii was $5 2 . 4 \%$ and $2 1 . 0 \%$ , respectively.

# 2.3 | Catechin, (−)-Epicatechin and (−)-Epigallocatechin Exhibit an Inhibitory Effect on Fof-1 In Vitro

For the 13 types of flavonoids described above in the metabolomic analysis, we evaluated their antimicrobial activity against Fof-1 under using different concentrations in  vitro. Compared with the extract control (dimethyl sulphoxide, DMSO) and blank control, only three flavonoids ((−)-epigallocatechin, catechin and $( - )$ -epicatechin) inhibited the growth of Fof-1, and of these $\left( - \right)$ -epigallocatechin showed the strongest antimicrobial activity (Figure 3a). We further tested the inhibition at various concentrations. As shown in Figure 3b, the inhibitory effect of (−)-epicatechin was the most pronounced at 0.125 and $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m g / }$ mL. Additionally, (−)-epigallocatechin showed the strongest inhibitory effect when the concentration exceeded $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ . When the concentration was $4 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ , the inhibition rate of (−)-epigallocatechin reached its maximum value of $5 4 . 7 \%$ .

# 2.6 | Gene Network Between ANR and Other DEGs and Metabolites in the Flavonoid Biosynthesis

According to FPKM value of 11,837 transcripts and content value of 1921 secondary metabolites in V. montana and V. fordii, we used the weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) approach of the R package to construct the gene and metabolites regulatory network with a weighted cut-off value $> 0 . 4 0$ . ANR (A008279) exhibited extremely high connections with other 39 edge genes and 44 edge metabolites in the pathways of flavonoid biosynthesis, phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, anthocyanin biosynthesis and isoflavonoid biosynthesis (Figure 6a, dark slate-blue module). The co-expression network is presented in Figure 6b using Cytoscape software. Notably, 9 of the 13 types of compounds in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway were identified as edge metabolites and are labelled in red font in Figure  6b. These nine types of flavonoid substances were catechin (n289T142, n289T156), (−)-epicatechin (p291T156), (−)-epigallocatechin (n305T131, n305T54, n305T91), dihydromyricetin (n319T177, p321T177), luteolin (p287T158, p287T214), myricetin (p319T186), myricitrin (n463T186), phlorizin (n435T210) and naringenin (p273T214). We speculate that gene ANR (A008279) might be co-expressed with genes participating in the flavonoid biosynthesis to produce different flavonoid substances to defend against Fusarium wilt. 

# 2.7 | Overexpression of VmANR Increases Flavonoid Production in V. montana

To investigate whether the increased expression level of gene ANR in V. montana (VmANR) may be related to flavonoids content, the open reading frame (ORF) of VmANR was amplified and inserted into overexpression vectors. As a woody plant without an efficient transformation system, the 

Agrobacterium rhizogenes-mediated hairy root generation system was used to examine the function of VmANR in V. montana. Using transgenic hairy roots containing the empty vector as the control, rhizomes of V. montana were agrotransformed with the control or overexpression (OE) vector VmANR (OE-VmANR). The seedlings were then cultured under the same conditions for 40 days, and hairy roots were induced in both control and OE-VmANR. We conducted RT-qPCR verification to screen the transgenic-positive hairy roots of both the control and OE-VmANR. # 2.9 | VmANR Has Enzyme Activity Toward Cyanidin

ANR is a key enzyme in the biosynthesis of proanthocyanidins. The synthesis of proanthocyanidins is part of flavonoid biosynthesis. # 3.1 | Flavonoids Accumulate in Root Xylem of V. montana Against Fof-1 Infection

Xylem vessels of plants are able to accumulate different secondary metabolites such as phytoalexins (e.g., terpenoids, nitrogenous compounds, phenolic compounds) to defend against pathogen invasion (Ahuja et al. 2012). In our study, we compared the inhibitory differences of root

However, there was no obvious inhibition effect by root xylem extract from  V. fordii (Figure  1). To test whether there is a basal level difference of root xylem metabolites that leads to inhibition effect differences, we detected secondary metabolites in root xylem of V. montana and V. fordii over four periods of Fof-1 infection. The metabolomic analysis showed that the content of 13 types of flavonoids exhibited different expression patterns in V. montana and V. fordii. # 3.3 | Overexpressing VmANR Enhances Flavonoid Production

In previous studies, the expression of ANR acts as a regulatory unit to control the biosynthesis of flavonoids and downstream 

products in Gossypium hirsutum (Lv et al. 2023), Lilium (Fan et al. 2022), Trifolium repens (Ma et al. 2022), Litchi chinensis (Zhong et al. 2023) and Punica granatum (Zhao et al. 2023). In our study, we identified the gene A008279, encoding ANR, to be directly involved in the biosynthesis of (−)-epicatechin and (−)-epigallocatechin (Figure  5). Given the potential critical function of ANR in flavonoid biosynthesis, we produced transgenic OE-VmANR in V. montana using hairy roots. The results showed that a higher concentration catechin, (−)-epicatechin, (−)-epigallocatechin, myricitrin, phlorizin, naringenin, dihydromyricetin and luteolin appeared in V. montana hairy roots of OE-VmANR. The fold change of these flavonoids was 6.5 to 63.5 times as compared with the control (Figure  7b). The function of ANR has been studied in other plant species. For example, in cassava (Manihot esculenta), the expression of ANR showed a high positive correlation with most metabolites in the tannin biosynthesis pathway; moreover, overexpressing ANR elevated tannin concentrations and conferred resistance to two-spotted spider mite in transgenic cassava lines (Chen et al. 2022). In strawberry $( F r a g a r i a \times a n a n a s s a )$ $\times$ , the loss-offunction mutation of ANR activated the anthocyanin synthesis pathway, with plants exhibiting increased levels of anthocyanins and flavonols and decreased levels of proanthocyanidins (Xu et al. 2024). Based on the genome and transcriptome data of Phyllostachys edulis, 24 late flavonoid biosynthesis genes were identified, of which PeANR4 has been ectopically expressed in Arabidopsis, and the proanthocyanidin content of transgenic lines was higher than that of wild type and mutant (Xiao et al. 2023). This study is the first attempt to confirm that overexpressing VmANR confers resistance to Fof-1 infection in V. montana. 

# 3.4 | Silencing VmANR Reduces Flavonoid Production

To further validate the requirement for VmANR in flavonoid biosynthesis, we employed the VIGS-based experiments to transiently suppress its expression in V. montana leaves. Successful 

down-regulation of VmANR resulted in a substantial reduction in the levels of catechin and myricitrin (Figure 7d). Some recent studies have demonstrated the utility of TRV-mediated ANR silencing in elucidating the regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis and plant disease resistance. For instance, the knockdown of HcANR by TRV-VIGS caused a significant decrease in proanthocyanidins content in Hibiscus cannabinus (Yang et al. 2023). Transient silencing of ZjLAR and ZjANR in jujube (Ziziphus jujuba) fruits resulted in lower total proanthocyanidins and monomeric catechin (Li et  al.  2025). In cotton cultivar FM9160, partially resistant to Verticillium wilt, silencing of GhANR makes the cotton plants more susceptible (Mustafa et al. 2021). Similarly, silencing of GbANR-47 significantly compromised resistance to F. oxysporum f. sp. vasinfectum, resulting in a marked increase in disease severity index and reduced anthocyanin content compared to controls (Su et al. 2022). More notably, in our study, the other five flavonoids ((−)-epicatechin, dihydromyricetin, $\left( - \right)$ -epigallocatechin, luteolin and myricetin) were not detected in the leaves of either the silenced plants or the control plants. This suggests a potential tissue-specific accumulation of these compounds, possibly in the roots, as supported by our earlier metabolite profiling. These findings imply that VmANR silencing may disrupt a key branch of the flavonoid pathway, preventing the synthesis of essential intermediates required for the production of a broader range of flavonoids. Furthermore, our results robustly complement earlier correlation analyses by demonstrating a causal relationship between VmANR expression and flavonoid biosynthesis, thereby solidifying its central role in the regulation of flavonoid diversity in V. montana. 

# 3.5 | Catalysation of VmANR Induces the Production of $( - )$ -Epigallocatechin From Cyanidin

The ANR enzyme from A. thaliana and M. truncatula produces (−)-epicatechin (along with catechin) from cyanidin and catalyses delphinidin into $( - )$ -gallocatechin and $( - )$ -epigallocatechin in vitro (Xie et al. 2003; Xie et al. 2004). Subsequent studies have revealed functional diversity of ANR among different species. For instance, native Camellia sinensis ANR (CsANR) exclusively produces $( - )$ -epicatechin, whereas recombinant CsANR primarily generates $( + )$ -epicatechin and can catalyse $( + )$ -catechin into (−)-epicatechin in vitro (Qian et al. 2015). In Zea mays, ZmANR1 converts cyanidin into $( + )$ -epicatechin in  vitro, which subsequently forms procyanidin dimers with 2,3-cis-leucocyanidin or $4 \beta$ -(S-cysteinyl)-epicatechin (Lu et al. 2023). Biochemical analyses have further demonstrated that ANRs exhibit dual activity, contributing to both the production of (−)-epicatechin starter unit and the formation of 2,3-cis-leucocyanidin extension unit (Jun et al. 2021). In our study, VmANR uniquely catalysed the formation of $( - )$ -epigallocatechin from cyanidin in  vitro, but the products (−)-epicatechin and catechin were not detected (Figure  8). We speculate that VmANR continuously catalyses cyanidin into (−)-epigallocatechin in vitro, and (−)-epicatechin and catechin as intermediate products are not detected. To our knowledge, there is the first report of the formation of (−)-epigallocatechin from cyanidin. Moreover, the content of $( - )$ -epigallocatechin increased 6.5-fold compared to the control in the OE-VmANR transgenic hairy roots in vivo. 

# 4 | Conclusion

Here, we found that the root xylem extract of resistant V. montana showed a strong inhibitory effect on the growth of Fof-1 compared with that of V. fordii. Through combined transcriptome and metabolome analysis, we screened the increased 13 types of flavonoids and eight genes in the flavonoid biosynthesis that showed an up-regulated pattern after Fof-1 infection in V. montana. Overexpression of VmANR enhanced the content of flavonoids, of which catechin, (−)-epicatechin and $( - )$ -epigallocatechin exhibited an obvious inhibitory effect on Fof-1 growth. The possible mechanism mentioned above is depicted in Figure 9. This study targets VmANR as a key gene to promote the biosynthesis of antimicrobial flavonoids in shaping the resistance to Fof-1 infection, and thus offers a strategy for breeding Fusarium-resistant tung oil trees. 

# 5 | Experimental Procedures

# 5.1 | Plant and Pathogen Materials

Tung trees (V. montana and V. fordii) were used in this study. The seeds of the tung tree come from Tianlin County, Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region, China. The seeds were planted for 3 months in the Research Institute of Subtropical Forestry, Hangzhou City, Zhejiang Province, China. The pathogen Fof-1 was isolated from the stem tissue in $V .$ fordii and cultured in PDA. 

# 5.2 | Root Xylem Extracts and Inhibition Activity

The well-grown root xylem samples of two Vernicia species were collected and rinsed with sterile water, and dried at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ . The dried root xylem samples were ground and stored for backup. A total of $2 0 \mathrm { g }$ of root xylem samples was weighed and successively extracted with $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $9 5 \%$ ethanol for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ and $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 0 0 \%$ ethanol for 1 h by ultrasonic method, then finally filtered with filter paper. We obtained $3 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of solution of root xylem extracts from V. fordii and V. montana each. A rotary eyaporator was used to eyaporate and concentrate the $3 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of solution of root xylem extracts until the solution became a paste. Subsequently, we added $3 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of water, and extracted with the same volume of ethyl acetate. The extracts of ethyl acetate were evaporated, concentrated, and dissolved in ethyl acetate to $4 \mathrm { m L }$ , which was stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for use. We added 1 mL of root xylem extracts of V. fordii and V. montana, or $1 \mathrm { m L }$ of ethyl acetate to each PDA plate. The PDA plate with 1 mL of ethyl acetate and a PDA plate with nothing else added were used as control groups. The PDA plates with 1 mL of root xylem extracts of $V .$ fordii and V. montana were used as comparison groups. Then we placed a $5 \mathrm { m m }$ round colony of Fof-1 in the centre of the above four kinds of PDA plates and cultured at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in an artificial climate incubator. We observed and recorded the colony diameter for 2–9 days. Finally, the inhibition rate $( \% ) =$ [(diameter of control colony − diameter of treated colony)/diameter of controlcolony] $\times 1 0 0$ was calculated,and the inhibition rate differences of the root xylem extracts in V. fordii and V. montana were compared.

# 5.3 | Pathogen Inoculation and Sampling

The Fof-1 colony with a diameter of $3 { - } 5 \mathrm { m m }$ on the PDA was selected and inoculated into the synthetic low-nutrient agar (SNA) and cultured at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days. According to the symptoms of Fusarium wilt, the root xylem of V. montana and V. fordii was harvested at time points 0, 2, 5 and 9 days post-inoculation (dpi), and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . According to the four periods of Fof-1 infection, root xylem samples and their three biological duplications of two tung tree species were prepared and used for the metabolite detection and analysis. # 5.5 | Antimicrobial Activity of 13 Types Flavonoids on Fof-1

The Fof-1 colony with a diameter of $3 { - } 5 \mathrm { m m }$ was inoculated into the PDA containing 13 kinds of flavonoids at different 

concentrations. The plates were placed in an incubator at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days. The diameter of the colony circle was measured by the crossing method, and the average diameter of each treatment was calculated. The inhibition rate $( \% ) =$ [(diameter of control colony − diameter of treated colony)/diameter of control colony] $\times 1 0 0$ was calculated, and the inhibition rate differences of 13 types of flavonoids were compared at different concentrations. 

# 5.6 | RNA Extraction, Construction of cDNA Library, Transcriptome Sequencing and DEGs Analysis

Total RNA was extracted from V. fordii and V. montana in the four periods (M0, M1, M2, M3; F0, F1, F2, F3) by the RN38-EASY Spin Plus Plant RNA kit following the manufacturer's instructions (Aidlab Biotech). The cDNA library was sequenced on the illumina Novaseq 6000 (LC Bio Technology Co. Ltd. Hangzhou, China). The genome data of V. montana was used as a reference for reads mapping when analysing RNA-seq data (Li et al. 2024). As described according to Wang et al. (2023), the DEGs analysis was conducted in V. fordii and V. montana. 

# 5.7 | Integrated Transcriptome and Metabolome Analysis

Using KEGG pathway (KEGG, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes), the screened genes and metabolites of transcriptome and metabolome were functionally annotated and assigned to different hierarchical categories in the flavonoid biosynthesis. For the screened genes and metabolites, we used the MeV4 software package to create a heat map with the normalised value in the four periods of Fof-1 infection. Pearson's correlation test was used to calculate the correlation coefficients between genes and metabolites in the flavonoid biosynthesis. 

The WGCNA of the R package was used to determine the association with weighted cut-off value $> 0 . 4 0$ between genes and metabolites in flavonoid biosynthesis. Subsequently, the coexpression network was viewed using Cytoscape software. 

# 5.8 | RT-qPCR Assay

According to the procedure of the SYBR Premix Ex Taq TM Kit (TaKaRa), the qPCR analysis was performed in a $2 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction volume on ABI7300 Real-Time quantitative instrument (Applied Biosystems). TEF1 (transcription elongation factor 1) was used as an internal control to normalise the gene expression. Specific primers for gene ANR in the flavonoid biosynthesis were designed using Primer Premier 5.0 (Premier Biosoft). Each sample was conducted with three independent biological replicates and three technical triplicates. The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { T } }$ method. Primer sequences are listed in Table S7. 

# 5.9 | The Transgenic Hairy Root Induction of Gene VmANR in V. montana

The full-length coding sequence of gene VmANR was amplified with primers A008279-F $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ATGGAGTACTGTCACACCAAAG TCT-3′) and A008279-R $5 ^ { \prime }$ -TCATGTAATTATATCTCCTAATC GCCTTCCAC-3′) and inserted into the overexpression vector BGV008. As described according to Jia et al. (2022), the resulting construct was introduced into A. rhizogenes K599 and then infiltrated into the stem of V. montana with a 1 mL syringe. The empty vector, as a control, also was infiltrated into the stem of V. montana. After 4 weeks, the injected stem grew hairy roots along the injection lines. The hairy roots were gathered and frozen in liquid nitrogen immediately. These samples were stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for flavonoid detection and RT-qPCR verification. 

# 5.10 | The VmANR Gene Silencing by VIGS in V. montana

The VIGS experiments were performed following the protocol described by Cao et al. (2024). Gene-specific primers of VmANR were designed using Primer-BLAST and the PCR product cloned into the pTRV2 vector. The recombinant plasmid pTRV2- VmANR, along with an empty pTRV2 control, was individually transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101. The Agrobacterium cells containing pTRV1 and the recombinant plasmids (or pTRV2 control) were prepared and cultured according to Cao et al. (2024). The suspensions containing recombinant plasmids (or pTRV2 control) and pTRV1 were mixed in equal volumes and inoculated onto the leaves of V. montana seedlings. Three biological replicates were performed, and functional evaluations were conducted after 2–3 weeks. These samples were stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for flavonoid detection and RT-qPCR verification. 

# 5.11 | Protein Purification and Enzyme Activity Assays

The full-length coding sequence of VmANR was cloned into the expression vector pET-32a to add a 6×His tag at the N 

terminus of VmANR,  and then expressed in $E$ . coli BL21. The compounds cyanidin, delphinidin, $( - )$ -epicatechin, catechin, (−)-gallocatechin, (−)-epigallocatechin used for the enzyme activity assay were purchased from Herb Substance Biotechnology Company (Chendu). The VmANR enzyme activity assay was carried out as described by Xie et  al.  (2004) with modifications. The assay mix $( 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } )$ contained $0 . 2 \mathrm { m g }$ of recombinant VmANR protein, 2 mM NADPH, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrate (cyanidin or delphinidin) and $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ phosphate-buffered saline (pH 6.5). Initial screening for VmANR enzyme activity was carried out in $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.0) at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 \mathrm { h }$ . The enzyme reaction was stopped by adding 1 mL ethyl acetate with vigorous vortexing for 1 min and then centrifuged for 1 min at $9 4 0 0 ~ g$ . The ethyl acetate supernatant phase was transferred to a new $1 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ microcentrifuge tube and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for UPLC-MS analysis. 

# 5.12 | UPLC-MS Analysis

The sample were analysed using an UPLC-ESI-MS/MS system (UPLC, Thermo UltiMate 3000 RS; MS, Thermo Q-Exactive). The analytical conditions were as follows: UPLC: column, Agela Venusil C18 Plus $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , $5 0 \times 2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ ); the mobile phase consisted of solvent A (pure water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid) and solvent B (methanol). Sample measurements were performed with a gradient programme that employed the starting conditions of $9 0 \%$ A and $1 0 \%$ B. A linear gradient to $2 \%$ A $9 8 \%$ B was programmed at 2 min, and a composition of $2 \%$ A, $9 8 \%$ B was kept for $1 . 8 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Subsequently, a composition of $9 0 \%$ A, $1 0 \%$ B was adjusted at 4 min and kept for 1 min. The flow velocity was set to $0 . 3 5 \mathrm { m L }$ per minute; the column oven was set to $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The injection volume was $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The effluent was alternatively connected to an ESI-MS/MS.
