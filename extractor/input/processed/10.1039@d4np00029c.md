# Structural diversity, evolutionary origin, and metabolic engineering of plant specialized benzylisoquinoline alkaloids

Ya Tian,‡ab Lingzhe Kong,‡ab Qi Li,‡ab Yifan Wang,ab Yongmiao Wang,ab Zhoujie An,ab Yuwei Ma,ab Lixia Tian,c Baozhong Duan,d Wei Sun,e Ranran Gao,*f Shilin Chen*g and Zhichao Xu $\textcircled{1}$ *ab

Covering: up to June 2024

Benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIAs) represent a diverse class of plant specialized metabolites derived from Ltyrosine, exhibiting significant pharmacological properties such as anti-microbial, anti-spasmodic, anticancer, cardiovascular protection, and analgesic effects. The industrial production of valuable BIAs relies on extraction from plants; however, challenges concerning their low concentration and efficiency hinder drug development. Hence, alternative approaches, including biosynthesis and chemoenzymatic synthesis, have been explored. Model species like Papaver somniferum and Coptis japonica have played a key role in unraveling the biosynthetic pathways of BIAs; however, many aspects, particularly modified steps like oxidation and methylation, remain unclear. Critical enzymes, e.g., CYP450s and methyltransferases, play a substantial role in BIA backbone formation and modification, which is essential for understanding the origin and adaptive evolution of these plant specialized metabolites. This review comprehensively analyzes the structural diversity of reported BIAs and their distribution in plant lineages. In addition, the progress in understanding biosynthesis, evolution, and catalytic mechanisms underlying BIA biosynthesis is summarized. Finally, we discuss the progress and challenges in metabolic engineering, providing valuable insights into BIA drug development and the sustainable utilization of BIAproducing plants.

# 1. Introduction

Benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIAs) are a diverse group of plant specialized metabolites containing a benzylisoquinoline backbone derived from L-tyrosine (1). The rst BIA compound, morphine (29), was isolated from the opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) by Friedrich Sertürner in 1805,1 and since then, more than 2500 natural BIA molecules have been identied. The cultivation of the opium poppy as a narcotic drug can be traced back to around 3400 BC,2 and the unripe seed capsules of the opium poppy accumulate abundant BIA compounds with different structural backbones, such as tricyclic 1-benzylisoquinoline (e.g., papaverine 18), morphinan (e.g., codeine 28 and morphine 29), and phthalideisoquinoline (e.g., noscapine 60). The industrial production of valuable BIAs relies on chemical extraction from the original plants. However, only a few BIA-producing plants have been cultivated to supply drugs or traditional medicines, such as P. somniferum9 for the extraction of papaverine (18), codeine (28), morphine (29), and noscapine (60); Coptis chinensis for supplying berberine (37);10,11 and Macleaya cordata to produce sanguinarine (48).12 Therefore, the complex components, slight concentration, and low efficiency of the extraction and purication of BIAs from plants largely restrict the development of new drugs.

The chemodiversity of natural products with complex backbones present challenges for large-scale synthesis, leading to the development of alternative strategies such as chemical synthesis, biosynthesis, and chemoenzymatic synthesis. However, the chemical synthesis of many BIAs remains challenging due to the presence of chiral centers, fused ring systems, and the regiochemistry of ring substitutions and oxidations.13 Furthermore, the industrial production of BIAs through chemical synthesis, as well as the release of hazardous substances, poses risks to the environment. Green production of BIAs through microorganisms or plants may address these challenges, with the heterologous expression of epimerase enabling the stereochemical inversion of chiral BIA molecules. P. somniferum and Coptis japonica have served as model species for studying BIA biosynthesis, with the complete biosynthesis pathway of well-known BIAs such as codeine (28), morphine (29), noscapine (60), berberine (37), and sanguinarine (48) being decoded.14–18 However, the biosynthetic pathways of many BIAs, particularly for the modied steps such as oxidation and methylation, remain unclear. Genomic sequencing of BIAproducing plants has led to the identication of novel biosynthetic genes and directed evolution of crucial enzymes, which may accelerate the metabolic engineering of BIAs.11,19–22 Nonetheless, the catalytic promiscuity of modied enzymes to BIA intermediates, metabolic pathway redundancy, and enzyme compatibility in heterologous hosts present challenges for controlling the metabolic ux and efficient production of targeted BIAs.

As sessile organisms, plants synthesize natural products with diverse structures and functions to adapt to specic ecological situations.23 The accumulation of BIAs in response to biotic and abiotic stresses, such as pathogen infection, has been well documented, although the physiological functions of most BIAs remain unclear. For instance, berberine (37) has been shown to induce plant resistance against viruses and pathogens,24,25 while Papaver species increase BIA accumulation under the elicitation of methyl jasmonate and salicylic acid.26,27

Plant-originated BIAs are mainly distributed in early-diverging eudicots, such as Ranunculales species, as well as partial species from Magnoliids.28,29 The distribution of BIAs with different skeletons presents lineage-conserved, family-specic, or genus-specic patterns, such as the unique accumulation of morphinans in Papaver and the universal presence of protoberberines in Ranunculales, suggesting that the BIA biosynthesis underwent multiple independent evolution aer their speciation.11,21,22 The chemodiversity of BIAs in plants to fulll specic physiological needs might arise from the reassembly, recruitment, and functional renement of promiscuous enzymes.30 CYP450s and methyltransferases (e.g. O- and Nmethyltransferases) largely contribute to the backbone formation and modication of complex metabolic traits of BIA molecules.21,31,32 Revealing the evolution and catalytic mechanisms of such enzymes is essential for understanding the origin and adaptive evolution of plant specialized metabolomes.

This review provides a comprehensive analysis of the structural diversity of BIAs and their distribution in specic plant lineages. It summarizes the progress made in understanding the biosynthesis and catalytic mechanisms of crucial enzymes involved in BIA production, as well as the origin and evolution of BIA biosynthetic genes through multi-omics and bioinformatic strategies. Furthermore, the review discusses the metabolic engineering of BIAs and core intermediates in microorganisms and proposes novel green production models, which can help to sustainably utilize BIA-producing plants.

# 2. Chemical diversity, classification, and distribution of BIAs in plants

# 2.1 Structural diversity and classications of natural BIAs

The fundamental skeleton of BIAs is norcoclaurine, which serves as the foundation for the biosynthesis of all BIAs. According to the chemical structure classication, diverse BIA molecules can be divided into nine categories,33–35 namely, 1- benzylisoquinolines (1-BIAs, e.g. papaverine, 18), bisbenzylisoquinolines (bisBIAs, e.g. cepharanthine and tetrandrine), morphinans (morphinans, e.g. codeine 28 and morphine 29), aporphines (Aporphines, e.g. magnoorine 68), protoberberines (BBRs, e.g. berberine 37), phthalideisoquinolines (PQs, e.g. noscapine 60), benzo[c]phenanthridines (BZPs, e.g. sanguinarine 48), protopines (protopines, e.g. protopine 46), and pavines (pavines, e.g. eschscholtzidine) (Fig. 1a and S1†). # 2.2 Distribution of BIAs in plants

Based on plant taxonomy, isolated BIA compounds are primarily distributed in Magnoliids and early-diverging eudicots, such as Piperales,46–48 Laurales,49–51 Magnoliales,52–54 Ranunculales,54–57 and Proteales 58,59 (Fig. 1b and Table ${ \bf S } 2 \dagger { \bf \Sigma } .$ ). Additionally, genera like Zanthoxylum and Phellodendron from Sapindales (Superrosids) have also been reported to accumulate BIAs.60,61 For example, BBRs are abundant in Berberis and Coptis from the order Ranunculales, as well as Phellodendron from the order Sapindales.62 The major members of 1-BIAs represent the fundamental backbones (or their derivatives) of all BIAs, distributed across nearly all BIA-producing plants, except for some plant-specic 1-BIAs, like papaverine (18) in the opium poppy.9 Diverse bisBIAs have been isolated from families including Lauraceae,63 Nelumbonaceae,58 Ranunculaceae,64 Berberidaceae,65 and Menispermaceae,66 which belong to Laurales, Proteales, and Ranunculales. While morphinans have been isolated from various species of Laurales, Magnoliales, and Ranunculales, representative compounds within this category, such as thebaine (25), codeine (28), and morphine (29), are specically distributed in the Papaver species. Aporphines are presently the most widely distributed compounds in BIAproducing plants, spanning Magnoliids and early-diverging eudicots, with over 70 plant genera having yielded natural aporphines. Protopines and BZPs are predominantly found within the Papaveraceae family, notably in genera such as Papaver, Macleaya, and Glaucium. 67–69 PQ and pavine compounds are infrequently reported or isolated and are mainly found in specic species of Ranunculales (Table ${ \bf S } 2 \dagger { \bf \Sigma }$ ). Additionally, pavines are also found in the Cryptocarya species of Lauraceae.70 Furthermore, our analysis of isolated BIAs in various plants revealed that Papaver, Berberis, and Thalictrum accumulate nearly all BIA categories, making them excellent models for studying structural diversity and biosynthesis (Fig. 1b).

# 3. Biosynthetic pathway of diverse BIAs

BIAs are complex and diverse compounds that accumulate differently in various plant species. For instance, opium poppy accumulates morphinan alkaloids, while protoberberine alkaloids are mainly found in Coptis. Therefore, BIA biosynthesis studies require a focus on different plant species. P. somniferum and C. japonica are widely used as premier model plants for the study of BIA biosynthesis. The rst BIA biosynthetic gene, FADdependent berberine bridge enzyme (BBE), was discovered in Berberis beaniana in 1985.71 BBE can convert (S)-reticuline (13) into (S)-scoulerine (34) by stereospecic oxidation and methylene bridge formation. Over 100 enzymes have since been reported to catalyze BIA biosynthesis, including norcoclaurine synthase (NCS) related to Pictet–Spengler reaction,

methyltransferases, and CYP450 monooxygenases (Table ${ \bf { S } } 3 \dagger$ ). The biosynthetic pathways of major compounds such as berberine (37), morphine (29), noscapine (60), and sanguinarine (48) are well understood (Fig. 2–8). However, most BIA biosynthesis pathways remain unclear, especially the modi- cation enzymes that inuence BIA diversity. According to current knowledge, structural diversity and distribution of BIAs are closely related to the species evolution, for example, most BIAs prefer to biosynthesize and accumulate in the earlydiverging eudicots. Therefore, discovering crucial and modi- cation enzymes based on multi-omics and species evolution can help to understand the mechanisms of diverse BIA biosynthesis and nd new genes that can be used functionally in BIA biosynthesis. Herein, we summarize the biosynthetic pathway of well-understood BIAs in eight parts involving skeleton formation and diverse BIA categories of modication: (i) (S)- norcoclaurine (7) biosynthesis (Fig. 2), (ii) (S)-reticuline (13) biosynthesis (Fig. 2), (iii) papaverine (1-BIAs) biosynthesis (Fig. 3), (iv) morphinan biosynthesis (Fig. 4), (v) berberine (BBR) biosynthesis (Fig. 5), (vi) protopine and sanguinarine (BZPs) biosynthesis (Fig. 6), (vii) noscapine (PQs) biosynthesis (Fig. 7), and (viii) bisBIA and aporphine biosynthesis (Fig. 8).

# 3.1 (S)-Norcoclaurine biosynthesis, skeleton formation of all BIA categories

(S)-Norcoclaurine (7) is a crucial intermediate in the biosynthesis of all BIAs found in plants,72 and it can be modied into different classes of BIAs by various enzymes. Dopamine (3) and 4-hydroxyphenylacetaldehyde (4-HPAA, 6), derived from L-tyrosine (1), are the crucial precursor substances of (S)-norcoclaurine (7) formation (Fig. 2). There are two known pathways for dopamine (3) biosynthesis. In the rst pathway, L-tyrosine (1) is decarboxylated into tyramine (2) by the enzyme tyrosine decarboxylase (TYDC). Tyramine (2) is then hydroxylated by either polyphenol oxidases (PPOs) 73 in plants or cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP450) 74 in animals to generate dopamine (3). In the second pathway, L-tyrosine (1) is hydroxylated into L-3,4- dihydroxyphenylalanine (L-DOPA, 4) by the enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase (TH), and then decarboxylated into dopamine (3) by dopa decarboxylase (DODC).75,76 Opium poppy has two known TYDC genes, TYDC1 and TYDC2, which can accept L-tyrosine (1) and L-DOPA (4) as substrates to form decarboxylated tyramine (2) and dopamine (3), respectively.77,78 These two genes exhibit different and organ- and temporal-specic expressions in opium poppy, indicating that specic developmental programs control the specic accumulation of BIAs.75,79 The biosynthesis of 4-HPAA (6) involves two enzymatic reactions. First, the enzyme tyrosine aminotransferase (TyrAT) converts L-tyrosine (1) into 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (5). Then, 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (5) is decarboxylated into 4-HPAA (6). While TyrAT from opium poppy has been shown to catalyze the formation of 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (5), the silencing of TyrAT only moderately reduces BIA accumulation, suggesting that 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (5) may have multiple origins.80

# 3.3 Papaverine biosynthesis

Papaverine (18), an opium alkaloid, has been the subject of much research regarding its biosynthetic pathway in the opium poppy. While the chemical synthesis of papaverine (18) has been achieved and it has been commercially produced for several decades, the natural biosynthetic pathway for this

compound in opium poppy remains incompletely understood. Two possible biosynthetic routes to (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) have been hypothesized, including the N-desmethylated (NH) and $N \cdot$ -methylated $\left( \mathrm { N C H } _ { 3 } \right)$ pathways100–104 (Fig. 3). Furthermore, a avoprotein oxidase with homology to dihydrobenzophenanthridine oxidase (DBOX) could carry out the nal oxidation step from (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) to papaverine (18).105

The NH pathway involves the hydroxylation of (S)-coclaurine (10) to form (S)- $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxycoclaurine (14), followed by three sequential $o$ -methylation steps at the hydroxy group of $\mathrm { { C } ^ { - 4 ^ { \prime } } }$ , C-7, and $\mathrm { { C } } { - } 3 ^ { \prime }$ to yield (S)-norreticuline (15), (S)-norlaudanine (16), and (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17), respectively. The norreticuline 7-O-methyltransferase (N7OMT) enzyme has been isolated from the opium poppy and characterized in this pathway; 106 however, the natural enzymes responsible for $\mathrm { C } { \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime }$ hydroxylation, $\mathrm { C } 4 ^ { \prime }$ -OH $o$ -methylation, and $\mathbf { C } 3 ^ { \prime }$ -OH $o$ -methylation remain unclear. In contrast, the $\mathrm { N C H } _ { 3 }$ pathway involves the Omethylation of (S)-reticuline (13) to form (S)-laudanine (19) by reticuline 7-O-methyltransferase (7OMT),90 which is then further O-methylated and $N \cdot$ -demethylated to form (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) via uncharacterized $3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { O M T }$ and laudanosine N-demethylase (LNdeMT).100 Recent VIGS experiments targeting N7OMT and/or CNMT have shed light on the major biosynthetic route to papaverine (18) in opium poppy. Suppressing CNMT signicantly increased the content of papaverine (18), while reducing the expression of CNMT and N7OMT reduced

papaverine (18) accumulation, suggesting that the main biosynthetic route to papaverine (18) in opium poppy proceeds via N-desmethylated compounds rather than through (S)- reticuline (13).100 However, the complete papaverine (18) biosynthetic pathway still needs further elucidation, and future studies will be needed to identify the enzymes responsible for the missing steps in both the NH and $\mathrm { N C H } _ { 3 }$ pathways.

# 3.4 Morphinan biosynthesis

Morphinan compounds, including thebaine (25), codeine (28), morphine (29), and oripavine (30), are naturally produced only by opium poppy and related species. The complete biosynthesis of morphinan compounds has been elucidated. From the crucial intermediate (S)-reticuline (13), the stereochemical inverted to $\left( R \right)$ -reticuline (21) catalyzed by the unique enzyme reticuline epimerase (REPI or STORR) (Fig. 4). The REPI/STORR is a fusion protein consisting of two modules, a cytochrome P450 domain and an aldo–keto reductase domain, responsible for oxidizing (S)-reticuline (13) to 1,2-dehydroreticuline and reducing 1,2-dehydroreticuline to $( R )$ -reticuline (21), respectively.107,108 The fusion of the two modules possesses conserved direction and 9–13 amino acid linker sequences, and the fusion event occurred only once between 16.8 and 24.1 million years ago.109,110 The discovery of REPI/STORR was a signicant milestone in the complete understanding of morphine (29) biosynthesis. The suppression of REPI/STORR gene expression resulted in a decrease in the content of thebaine (25), codeine (28), and morphine (29) levels but an increase in (S)-reticuline (13) and its $O _ { \ l }$ -methylated derivatives, indicating the essential role of this enzyme in morphinan biosynthesis.107

The salutaridine synthase (SalSyn) encoded by the PsCYP719B1 gene catalyzes the stereo- and regiospecic conversion of (R)-reticuline (21) into salutaridine (22) in opium poppy.111 In addition, an MdCYP80G10 enzyme from Menispermum dauricum could catalyze C–C phenol coupling of (S)- reticuline (13) into sinoacutine (33), the enantiomer of salutaridine (22), with stereospecicity.21 Furthermore, in the opium poppy, salutaridine reductase (SalR) and salutaridinol 7- O-acetyltransferase (SalAT) convert salutaridine (22) into salutaridinol (23) and salutaridinol 7-O-acetate (24), respectively.112,113 Thebaine synthase (THS), a member of the PR10 protein superfamily, catalyzes the initial step in the formation of pentacyclic morphinan compounds.114 The chromosomallevel genome of the opium poppy revealed that the genes involved in thebaine (25) biosynthesis, including REPI/STORR, SalSyn, SalAT, SalR, and THS, are clustered on chromosome 11, along with the genes responsible for noscapine (60) biosynthesis.109 Three Papaver genomes show that a sudden burst of structural variations such as fusions, translocations, and duplications within a span of 7.7 million years has merged nine genes into the morphinan and noscapine (60) biosynthetic cluster, following a punctuated patchwork model.19 The clustering of genes involved in the biosynthesis of thebaine (25) and noscapine (60) suggests selective pressure promoting the coevolution and coordinated gene expression of these specialized metabolites.

Two routes have been proposed for the bifurcation of thebaine (25) to morphine (29). In the rst route, thebaine (25) undergoes O-demethylation at the C6 position catalyzed by thebaine 6-O-demethylase (T6ODM), resulting in the formation of neopinone (26).115 The second route involves the O-demethylation of thebaine (25) at the C3 position to oripavine (30) by codeine O-demethylase (CODM), followed by the conversion of oripavine (30) to neomorphinone (31) by T6ODM. T6ODM and CODM are the only two known examples of oxoglutarate/Fe(II)- dependent dioxygenases that catalyze the O-demethylation reaction. When the expression of T6ODM or CODM was suppressed by virus-induced gene silencing in opium poppy, it efficiently blocked the metabolism at thebaine (25) and codeine (28), respectively.115 Although the isomerization of neopinone (26) and neomorphinone (31) was initially assumed to be spontaneous, the enzymatic catalysis has shown that neopinone (26) and neomorphinone (31) can be interconverted to codeinone (27) and morphinone (32) by neopinone isomerase (NISO), respectively.116 The expression of NISO transcripts is markedly high in the latex of opium poppy, and the VIGS-inhibition of NISO gene expression led to a considerable reduction of morphine (29). The biosynthetic genes responsible for the conversion of thebaine (25) to codeine (28) and morphine (29), such as T6ODM, COR, and CODM, are not located in the thebaine (25) biosynthetic gene cluster. Codeinone (27) and morphinone (32) are subsequently converted to codeine (28) and morphine (29), respectively, by codeinone reductase (COR, an aldo–keto reductase), which is also responsible for the irreversible reduction of neopinone (26) to neopine and neomorphinone (31) to neomorphine.117,118

# 3.5 Berberine (BBR) biosynthesis

The biosynthesis of protoberberine originates from (S)-reticuline (13), with the formation of the protoberberine ring of (S)- scoulerine (34) catalyzed by the berberine bridge enzyme (BBE) (Fig. 5). The rst BBE gene was reported and puried from B. beaniana cell cultures in 1985,71 with the enzyme further identied to catalyze the formation of (S)-scoulerine (34).119 O-Methyltransferase (9OMT) methylates the C-9 hydroxyl group of (S)-scoulerine (34) to form (S)-tetrahydrocolumbamine (35), and then the CYP719A subfamily catalyzes the formation of the methylenedioxy bridge of (S)-tetrahydroberberine ((S)-canadine 36). VIGS-mediated silencing of PsCYP719A21 resulted in signicant upregulation of (S)-tetrahydrocolumbamine (35), with a concomitant decrease in the putative downstream intermediates in opium poppy.120 Lastly, (S)-tetrahydroberberine oxidase (THBO), the nal enzyme in the berberine (37) pathway, catalyzes the dehydrogenation of four hydrogen atoms of (S)-canadine (36) to produce berberine (37). Furthermore, (S)-scoulerine (34) could undergo consecutive methylenedioxy bridging to produce (S)-cheilanthifoline (38) and (S)- stylopine (39),121,122 and (S)-stylopine (39) could be further catalyzed into coptisine (40) via STOX oxidation, similar to the THBO-catalyzed reaction.

BBE and BBE-like (BBEL), THBO and STOX reported to date are subgroups of the FAD-linked oxidase superfamily and

catalyze two- and four-electron oxidation steps in BIA biosynthesis, such as the $\mathbf { C } 2 \mathbf { - C } 2 ^ { \prime }$ bond formation of (S)-reticuline (13) to (S)-scoulerine (34) 119 and the formation of the fully conjugated ring system of berberine (37) from (S)-canadine (36).105 The reduction of BBE in cultured California poppy cells leads to the accumulation of (S)-reticuline (13), indicating its central role in (S)-reticuline (13)-originated BIA biosynthesis.123 Moreover, BBE can also catalyze the six-electron oxidation of (S)- reticuline (13) to dehydroscoulerine (41), which could extend the diversity of oxidized BIA products.124 A species-specic tandem duplicated gene cluster of 25 BBEL genes in Corydalis tomentella genome plays a key role in catalyzing the fourelectron oxidation reaction of (S)-cheilanthifoline (38), (S)-stylopine (39), and cavidine (43) to form dehydrochilanthifoline (42), coptisine (40), and dehydrocavidine (44), respectively,22 leading to the structural diversity of protoberberine alkaloids.

# 3.6 Protopine and sanguinarine biosynthesis

The biosynthesis of protopine (46) and sanguinarine (48) initiates with $N \cdot$ -methylation of (S)-stylopine (39) (Fig. 6). This reaction is facilitated by the enzyme tetrahydroprotoberberine cis-Nmethyltransferase (TNMT). In the opium poppy, TNMT is a promiscuous enzyme that exhibits affinity towards (S)-stylopine (39) and (S)-canadine (36).125 Subsequently, the (S)-cis-Nmethylstylopine 14-hydroxylase (MSH; CYP82N4) hydroxylates the molecule leading to ring opening and production of protopine (46). Further hydroxylation of protopine (46) by protopine 6-hydroxylase (P6H; CYP82N3) results in a spontaneous rearrangement of the ring to form dihydrosanguinarine (47).20,126 The duplication of MSH and P6H occurred before the divergence of Papaveraceae and led to the specic emergence of sanguinarine (48) biosynthesis in Papaveraceae.22 Lastly, DBOX catalyzes a two-electron oxidation process that concludes the ring aromatization, ultimately yielding sanguinarine (48).105 In addition, when (S)-N-methylcanadine (49) is used as a substrate, MSH catalyzes the production of allocryptopinem (50), which then undergoes P6H and DBOX reactions to produce chelerythrine (52).20

# 3.7 Noscapine biosynthesis

The biosynthetic pathway leading to noscapine (60) biosynthesis initiates with a C–C linked cyclization reaction catalyzed by the berberine bridge enzyme (BBE), leading to the formation of (S)-scoulerine (34), which is also a precursor for berberine (37) biosynthesis. Sequential enzymatic transformations, including O-methylation of (S)-scoulerine (34) by 9-O-methyltransferase (PsOMT1), closure of the isoquinoline moiety via methylenedioxy bridge formation by canadine synthase (CAS, CYP719A1), and $N \cdot$ -methylation of (S)-canadine (36) by N-methyltransferase (TNMT), produce (S)-N-methylcanadine (49). Two consecutive hydroxylation reactions at C-1 and C-13 of (S)-Nmethylcanadine (49), catalyzed by PsCY82Y1 and PsCYP82X2, respectively, produce 1,13-dihydroxy-N-methylcanadine (54). Subsequently, C-13 hydroxylated product is acetylated by 13-Oacetyltransferase (PsAT1) to yield 1,8-dihydroxy-13-O-acetyl-Nmethylcanadine (56) under the catalysis of PsCYP82X1. The N7-

C8 bridge of this molecule is then cleaved under spontaneous ring opening to form $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-desmethyl-3-O-acetylpapaveroxine (57). A heterodimer of PsOMT2 and PsOMT3 then catalyzes $4 ^ { \prime }$ -Odesmethyl-3-O-acetylpapaveroxine (57) to 3-O-acetylpapaveroxine (58) with strict substrate specicity.127 Finally, 3-O-acetylpapaveroxine (58) undergoes ester hydrolysis by 3-Oacetylpapaveroxine carboxylesterase (PsCXE1), and the shortchain dehydrogenase/reductase (PsSDR1) encoding noscapine synthase (PsNOS) catalyzes the dehydrogenation of narcotine hemiacetal (59) to produce noscapine $\mathbf { ( 6 0 ) ^ { 1 8 , 1 2 8 } }$ (Fig. 7). The acetylation at C-13 of 1,13-dihydro-N-methylcanadine before C-8 hydroxylation acts as a protective group that is subsequently hydrolyzed by PsCXE1.129 Meanwhile, yeast expression assays have also proposed an alternative pathway for noscapine (60) biosynthesis in which $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-desmethyl-3-O-acetylpapaveroxine (57) is initially acetylated to narcotoline hemiacetal. Bifurcation of narcotoline hemiacetal is further catalyzed into narcotoline or narcotine hemiacetal (59) under the catalysis of PsSDR1 or $\mathbf { P s N 4 ^ { \prime } O M T }$ (PsOMT2: PsOMT3 heterodimer), respectively, which is then catalyzed into noscapine (60).130

Noscapine (60) is a secondary metabolite that is selectively accumulated in the opium poppy, and its production varies among different cultivars.18 The biosynthesis of noscapine (60) from (S)-reticuline (13) requires a total of twelve enzymes, which involve the concerted action of ten biosynthetic genes that are tightly linked and form the noscapine (60) biosynthetic gene cluster on chromosome 11 of the opium poppy genome.18,109 These genes exhibit specic expression patterns in high noscapine (60)-producing poppy cultivars, and targeted gene silencing using virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) results in the accumulation of noscapine (60) pathway intermediates. However, the noscapine (60) cluster does not contain two genes coding BBE and TNMT enzymes that are also involved in sanguinarine (48) biosynthesis. The exclusion of these genes from the cluster is thought to be due to selective pressure resulting from the different spatial biosynthesis of sanguinarine (48, roots) and noscapine (60, aerial organs) in the opium poppy.109 This suggests that the spatial organization of metabolic pathways can inuence the evolution of gene clusters and the biosynthesis of natural products.

# 3.8 bisBIA and aporphine biosynthesis

bisBIAs are produced by coupling two 1-benzylisoquinoline monomers, and many studies reported that CYP450s are responsible for the C–O/C–C phenol coupling. In 1993, the rst CYP450 from Berberis stolonifera, BsCYP80A1, was identied to mediate highly regio- and stereoselective oxidative coupling of two chiral phenols to form bisBIAs.131 The BsCYP80A1 enzyme can oxidize three chiral substrates, (S)-coclaurine (10), (S)-Nmethylcoclaurine (11), and (R)-N-methylcoclaurine (61), resulting in the production of three dimeric products, $( R , S )$ -berbamunine (62), $( R , \enspace S ) – 2 ^ { \prime }$ -norberbamunine (63), and $( R , R ) \cdot$ - guattegaumerine (64) (Fig. 8). In addition, NnCYP80A (also referred to as NnCYP80Q2) from N. nucifera also exhibited regioselective oxidative coupling activities for (R)-N-methylcoclaurine (61), including ${ \bf C 8 - C 3 ^ { \prime } }$ coupling into $( R , \ R ) \cdot$ -

nelumborine (65), and $\mathbf { C } 7 – O – \mathbf { C } 3 ^ { \prime }$ coupling into $( R , R )$ -nelumboferine (66).7 Due to naming irregularities, BsCYP80A1 and NnCYP80A should be classied into different subfamilies as they share less than $5 5 \%$ identity following the CYP450 nomenclature system.7 The biosynthetic enzymes involved in the formation of various bisBIAs, including C–O phenol coupling, methylation, and hydroxylation, remain largely unknown.

The CYP80 family members also catalyze the C–C phenol coupling reactions to directly form the aporphine skeleton. NnCYP80G (also referred to as NnCYP80Q1) from N. nucifera, LsCYP80G from Laurelia sempervirens, and AcCYP80Q8 from Aristolochia contorta have been reported to convert the $\mathbf { { C 8 - C 1 } ^ { \prime } }$ coupling of (S or R)-N-methylcoclaurine (11 or 21) to form the proaporphine skeleton, glaziovine (67).7,132 In addition, CYP80G family members, including AcCYP80G7 (ref. 132) from A. contorta and MdCYP80G8 (ref. 21) from M. dauricum, catalyze the ${ \bf C 8 - C 2 ^ { \prime } }$ coupling of (S)-reticurine (13) to form the aporphine skeleton, (S)-corytuberine (68). Subsequently, the reticuline $N -$ methyltransferase (RNMT) from P. somniferum133 and pavine $N -$ methyltransferase (TfPavNMT) from T. avum134 catalyze the conversion of (S)-corytuberine (68) into magnoorine (69).

# 4. Origin and evolution of BIA biosynthetic genes

The summarization of BIA biosynthetic pathways revealed that ve types of enzymes such as PR10/Betv1 (e.g. NCS), O-methyltransferase (OMT), N-methyltransferase (NMT), FAD-dependent oxidase (e.g. BBE and BBEL), and CYP450 mainly contribute to the formation of BIA complex structures. Given the specic accumulation of BIAs in Magnoliids and early-diverging eudicots, we systematically identied the distributions of these BIA biosynthetic genes in a total of 245 reported plant genomes, including vascular and nonvascular plants (Fig. 9 and Table S4†). According to the results, the genes of the NCS clade that are responsible for norcoclaurine condensation, as well as the CYP450s such as CYP719, CYP80A/B/G/Q, and CYP82N/X/Y that contribute to the formation of methylenedioxy bridge, C–C/C–O phenol coupling, and the hydroxylation of BIAs are specically and widely found in early-diverging eudicots. We also detected the occurrence and expansion of CYP719 and CYP80A/B/G/Q family members in Magnoliids. This is consistent with the accumulation of BIAs in angiosperms. In addition, OMT, NMT, and FAD-dependent oxidase genes are widely distributed in the plant kingdom, suggesting the co-option of these enzymes for BIA biosynthesis with substrate specicity evolved. To better understand the evolution of BIA pathway genes, we constructed the phylogenetic trees of NCS, CYP719, CYP80A/B/G/Q, and CYP82 homologous genes from these plant genomes.

NCS homologous genes can be divided into two main clades: Clade I and Clade II. Clade I is further subdivided into Clade I-I, Clade I-II, and Clade I-III (Fig. 10a and S2†). The functional NCS genes are predominantly found in Clade I-I and Clade I-II. Clade I-I is restricted to Ranunculales species, including the families Papaveraceae, Menispermaceae, Ranunculaceae, and

Berberidaceae. Notably, the NCS homologous genes in Clade I-I have undergone signicant expansion in the Papaveraceae and Menispermaceae families, with whole-genome duplication (WGD) and tandem gene duplication (TGD) events being major contributors to this expansion.8,21 Additionally, the tandem fusion of NCS catalytic domains in Papaveraceae species has been reported,135 possibly as an evolutionary adaptation to enhance the production of (S)-norcoclaurine (7). Clade I-II is primarily found in early-diverging eudicots, including Ranunculales, Proteales, and Trochodendrales. Among the NCS members in Proteales, only NnNCS has been characterized as having NCS catalytic activity.87 Members of Clade I-III have not yet been reported to exhibit any NCS activity. This suggests that NCS evolved specically from the ancestral gene PR10/Bet v1 to acquire the ability to condense dopamine (3) and 4-HPAA (6) to form (S)-norcoclaurine (7) in early-diverging eudicots.

Phylogenetic analysis shows that the CYP719 family members were specically distributed in Magnoliids and early diverging eudicots and were divided into three subfamilies, CYP719A and CYP719B (Fig. 10b and ${ \bf S } 3 \dagger .$ ). Among them, CYP719A and CYP719B members have been reported to be involved in the BIA biosynthesis. The reported CYP719A genes in the Papaveraceae and Ranunculaceae families are involved in methylenedioxy bridge-formation on the A or D rings of BBRs. In addition, NnCYP719A22 from N. nucifera can catalyze the methylenedioxy bridge formation in aporphine biosynthesis.136 The CYP719A members present wide distributions in BIAaccumulation species, indicating the conserved catalytic activity in BIA biosynthesis. However, CYP719B is primarily responsible for the biosynthesis of salutaridine (22), the main skeleton of thebaine (25), codeine (28), and morphine (29), which are lineage-specic distribution in Papaver species. The phylogenetic tree indicated that the CYP719B subfamily only exists in Papaver species, also supporting the independent evolution and origin of these morphinans.

Phylogenetic analysis shows that the distribution of subfamilies CYP80A/B/G/Q and CYP82N/P/X/Y involved in BIA biosynthesis mirrors the distribution of BIAs in the plant kingdom (Fig. 11 and S4†). The CYP80A/B/G/Q members are commonly found in the Magnoliids and early-diverging eudicots, except for Papaveraceae species. This is consistent with the specic distribution of BZPs and PQs in Papaveraceae (Fig. 2 and 4b). # 5.2 Protein structure and catalytic mechanism of MTs

Methylation plays a crucial role in diversifying natural products, altering their chemical properties and biological activities. For example, $o$ -methylated thebaine (25) is much more of a stimulant and less of an effective painkiller compared to fully Odemethylated morphine (29).142 SAM-dependent methyltransferases are classied based on the methyl-accepting group: C-, O-, N- or S-methyltransferases.143 In BIA biosynthesis, OMT and NMT enzymes with independent evolution exhibit different protein conformations and specialize in accepting O- and $N \mathrm { \mathrm { - } }$ atoms, respectively. To date, crystal structures for BIA methyltransferases, including T. avum norcoclaurine 6OMT (Tf6OMT, PDB: 5ICE),91 P. somniferum scoulerine O-methyltransferase (Ps9OMT1, PDB: 6I6K),144 T. avum pavine NMT (TfPavNMT, PDB: 5KOK),134 C. japonica coclaurine NMT

(CjCNMT, PDB: 6GKV),145 and Glaucium avum tetrahydroprotoberberine NMT (GfTNMT, PDB: 6P3O),146 have been elucidated. The protein structures of these OMTs and NMTs showed dimer formation, and the monomers contain the BIA binding domain and SAM/SAH binding domain. However, the conformation between OMTs and NMTs was completely different.

In the ternary complex of BIA OMTs (Tf6OMT and Ps9OMT1), SAH and the key catalytic residues of Tf6OMT (His256, Asp257, and Glu315) and Ps9OMT1 (His296, Asp297, and Glu356) overlap almost completely, and the substrate with nearby locations have different congurations (Fig. 13a). The crystal structures of BIA-OMTs are similar and share a conserved catalytic mechanism with other plant SAMdependent OMTs.91,144 The target hydroxyl group is deprotonated by a general base histidine residue (His256 in Tf6OMT, and His296 in Ps9OMT1). Subsequently, the newly generated phenolate anion carries out nucleophilic attacks on the unstable methyl group of SAM, resulting in the transfer of the SAM methyl group to the target site. In addition, the adjacent residues (Asp257 in Tf6OMT, and Asp297 in Ps9OMT1) and the residues bonded by hydrogen (Glu315 in Tf6OMT, and Glu356 in Ps9OMT1) to the catalytic histidine residues also play a crucial role in the catalytic activity. The results of mutation experiments of BIA NMTs to date clearly show that no essential residue has been identied for $N \cdot$ -methyl transfer to occur.142 Therefore, although partial crystal structures have been resolved, the catalytic mechanism of BIA-NMTs has not been fully elucidated.

# 6. Metabolic engineering of BIAs in microbial hosts

The utilization of synthetic biology strategies enables the production of plant specialized metabolites in microbial or

plant hosts, thereby offering a potential alternative to traditional plant cultivation and chemical synthesis methods.150 Understanding critical BIA biosynthetic pathways such as codeine (28), morphine (29), and noscapine (60) has paved the way for their industrial production through synthetic biology techniques. For over a decade, several teams have conducted groundbreaking research in BIA metabolic engineering using yeast and bacteria as the chassis cells (Fig. 14). Although a few BIAs have been successfully synthesized in microorganisms, challenges arise due to the complex metabolic pathways, low efficiency of catalytic enzymes, and the diverse range of substrates. These complexities result in uncontrolled metabolic ux of the desired BIAs and lower yields during microbial synthesis (Table ${ \bf S } ^ { 1 0 \dagger }$ ). This section provides a comprehensive overview of microbial synthesis studies on BIAs.

# 6.2 Tetrahydropapaverine and papaverine

Although the NH route involving norreticuline (15) and the $\mathrm { N C H } _ { 3 }$ route involving (S)-reticuline (13) for papaverine (18) biosynthesis have been proposed, complete papaverine (18) biosynthesis remains unclear. The de novo microbial biosynthesis of (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) and the semisynthesis of papaverine (18) were performed using a yeast platform. The starting strain for the (S)-reticuline (13) production was engineered to delete PsCNMT to produce the nonnative norreticuline (15). A protein engineering strategy was used to increase the activity of EcNMCH and Tf9OMT towards nonnative substrates, coclaurine (10), and norreticuline (15), respectively, resulting in higher (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) titers. In addition, the MDR transporters were knocked out to improve the metabolic ux. The nal (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) of $1 2 1 \ \mu \mathrm { g } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ was obtained by knocking out multidrug resistance transporters and optimizing the medium and carbon source.103 The study successfully facilitated the microbial production of (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17) and papaverine (18). However, the engineered strain faces challenges in achieving high titers. The low yield of the starting strain for (S)-reticuline (13) production $( 1 . 1 4 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } } )$ ) leads to minimal biosynthesis of (S)-tetrahydropapaverine (17). Additionally, more research is required to identify enzymes capable of performing the nal oxidation step in the production of papaverine (18). Another potential approach is the protein engineering of PsDBOX or a similar

oxidase, which could enable the production of complete biosynthesis of papaverine (18).

# 6.3 Thebaine, codeine, morphine, and hydrocodone

The major bottleneck in morphine (29) biosynthesis was the conversion of enantiomers from (S)-reticuline (13) to (R)- reticuline (21), catalyzed by the STORR fusion genes.107,108 Yeast chassis was chosen to synthesize $( R )$ -reticuline (21) with the introduction of 6OMT, CNMT, and $4 ^ { \prime } 0 \mathbf { M } \mathrm { T }$ and the feeding of $( R ,$ S)-norlaudanosoline (9), and further produced salutaridine (22), an intermediate of morphine alkaloid, at a yield of $2 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 } \nu i a$ the introduction of human CYP2D6 and CPR1.155 A morphine (29) biosynthetic pathway was reconstructed including T6ODM, COR1.3, and CODM in S. cerevisiae to semi-synthesize opioids via feeding thebaine (25). The total morphinan titers were up to $1 3 1 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , including $5 1 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ hydrocodone, $7 0 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ oxycodone, and $1 0 \ \mathrm { m g } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ dihydrocodeine, using the optimization of gene copy number, enhancement of substrate supply, and high-density fermentation.158

The highly efficient de novo synthesis of morphinans faced signicant challenges in microbes. Morphinans were successfully synthesized from sugar for the rst time using 21 and 23 enzyme activities from various sources such as plants, mammals, bacteria, and yeast. The engineered yeast was able to produce $6 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ thebaine (25) and $0 . 3 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ hydrocodone, respectively, showing the potential of yeast as a chassis for complex natural compounds, despite the low yield achieved.14 The de novo production of morphine alkaloids starting from glycerol using engineered E. coli was also investigated. The stepwise culture of four engineered strains, including dopamine (3), $( R , S )$ -tetrahydropapaverine, $( R , S )$ -reticuline (21, 13), and thebaine (25), could synthesize thebaine (25) at yields of $2 . 1 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ , a 300-fold increase from previous yeast systems. Furthermore, $0 . 3 6 \ \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ of hydrocodone was yielded by introducing T6DOM and morB genes.159

# 6.4 Protoberberines, protopine, and benzophenanthridine alkaloids

The crucial stage of the berberine branch involves the oxidative cyclization of the N-methyl group in (S)-reticuline (13) into the formation of berberine bridge carbon of (S)-scoulerine (34), resulting from the catalysis of BBE. The heterologous de novo synthesis of berberine (37) has only been reported in yeast due to the involvement of several membrane proteins, including BBE, CYP719A1, and STOX. PsBBE, Tf9OMT, and TfCYP719A1 with AtATR1 reductase were transferred into a (S)-reticuline (13)-producing yeast strain, and aer feeding norlaudanosoline (9), the (S)-canadine (36), with a yield of $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ .155 In addition, the sanguinarine (48) branch in the BIA biosynthetic pathway of S. cerevisiae has been successfully reconstructed, resulting in the microbial biosynthesis of protoberberines, including $6 7 6 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of (S)-stylopine (39), $5 4 8 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of cis-Nmethylstylopine (45), $2 5 2 \mu \mathrm { g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of protopine (46), and $8 0 \mu \mathrm { g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of sanguinarine (48), demonstrating that protoberberines can be efficiently produced in yeast even in the absence of the associated biosynthetic enzyme.160 Furthermore, the (S)-

canadine (36) pathway was reconstructed and optimized in S. cerevisiae, achieving a (S)-canadine (36) titer of $1 . 8 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ through enzyme variant screening, genetic copy number variation, and culture optimization.161 Berberine (37) was synthesized by introducing PsBBE, PsS9OMT, CjCYP719A1, and CjSTOX into the de novo (S)-reticuline (13) yeast chassis. As the membrane-bound enzyme CjSTOX was inactive in yeast, the spontaneous conversion from (S)-canadine (36) to berberine (37) was adopted. Finally, the de novo yield of berberine (37) increased to $1 . 0 8 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ aer heating the medium.16 In addition, screening and site-mutation of CyCYP719A39 and CyCYP719A42 from Corydalis yanhusuo, participating in (S)- stylopine (39) synthesis, resulted in the de novo production of (S)-stylopine (39) at $3 2 ~ \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ . 162 In the process of synthesizing BIAs, all avin-dependent oxidase BBE-like enzymes that have been identied in plants are found to be inactive in S. cerevisiae. Therefore, the challenge is to modify these membrane-bound enzymes and explore the possibility of alternative enzymes.

# 6.5 Noscapine, bisBIAs, and magnoorine

The biosynthesis of noscapine (60) involves multiple enzymatic steps and the conversion of various types of BIAs. A 14-step biosynthetic pathway by expressing 16 plant-originated enzymes was successfully reconstituted in S. cerevisiae, achieving the production of noscapine (60) using (S)-norlaudanosoline (9) as a substrate, with a yield of $1 . 6 4 \pm 0 . 3 8 \mu \mathbf { M }$ .15 More than 30 biosynthetic enzymes from plants, bacteria, mammals, and yeast were further expressed. Aer optimizing the expression of pathway enzymes, host endogenous metabolic pathways, and fermentation conditions, an over 18 000-fold improvement of noscapine (60) titers to $2 . 2 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ was achieved in S. cerevisiae.130

The biosynthesis of diverse bisBIAs involving chiral transformation, C–O phenol coupling, and various $N / O$ -methylation are not fully elucidated. To date, only $\left( R , R \right)$ -guattegaumerine (64) and $\textstyle ( R , S )$ -berbamunine (62) were de novo synthesized in S. cerevisiae.163 First, they constructed a yeast chassis for the de novo synthesis of (S)-N-methylcoclaurine (11). Then, the responsible enzyme (STORR) for the chiral transformation of (S)-N-methylcoclaurine (11) to (R)-N-methylcoclaurine (61), the optimized phenol coupling enzyme (CYP80A1), and the CPR variants were co-transformed into yeast, leading to the de novo production of $( R , R )$ -guattegaumerine (64) and $\textstyle ( R , S )$ -berbamunine (62), with yields of 108 and $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively.

The biosynthetic pathways of aporphine alkaloids are not yet fully understood due to their complex structure and the signicant differences in methylation and hydroxylation on their backbones. The only reported microbial synthesis of aporphine alkaloids are (S)-corytuberine (68) and magnoorine (69). CYP80G2 enzyme that can catalyze (S)-reticuline (13) to (S)- corytuberine (68) was identied in C. japonica, while (S)-corytuberine (68) can be catalyzed by RNMT to generate magno-orine (69). Therefore, based on the previous synthesis of (S)- reticuline (13) by E. coli, they transferred CYP80G2 and CNMT from C. japonica into S. cerevisiae and used a co-culture system to generate $7 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ magnoorine (69).151

# 7. Conclusion and prospects

BIAs, mainly found in Magnoliids and early-diverging eudicots, are known for their important pharmacological properties. However, extracting BIA sources from specic plants seriously threatens plant diversity and conservation. Moreover, the chemical synthesis of BIAs faces a signicant challenge in preparing them in vitro at a commercial scale, due to their chiral complexity. The scientic community has struggled to use microorganisms or plant chassis to produce specialized plant metabolites using green chemistry strategies. Although progress has been made in the biosynthetic pathway and metabolic engineering of BIAs, the yield still falls short of industrial demands. For example, the rate-limiting enzyme NCS, responsible for condensing dopamine (3) and 4-HPAA (6) into (S)- norcoclaurine (7), can also recognize more extensive aldehydes, leading to the formation of by-products. In addition, NCS has been reported to be toxic in yeast, which limits downstream BIA production.164 MTs display broad substrate recognition and multiple methylation activities, producing many BIA analogs/ by-products through metabolic engineering. Key challenges include: (1) incomplete understanding of enzyme functions. (2) Issues related to uncontrolled metabolic ux and low yields due to insufficient enzyme activity and substrate selectivity in the BIA microbial synthesis pathway. (3) Despite identifying and characterizing plant enzymes involved in BIA synthesis, expressing these plant-derived enzymes in microorganisms is challenging due to the absence of plant-specic organelles in yeast, hindering enzyme function. To address these challenges effectively, the following strategies are proposed (Fig. 14): (1) use high-throughput sequencing platforms along with multiple omics approaches to uncover unknown BIA synthesis pathways. In addition, the machine-learning discovery of unknown BIA biosynthetic branches and enzymes could accelerate the understanding of plant-derived BIA biosynthesis. (2) Improve enzyme catalytic efficiency through enzyme engineering and crystallography. Directed evolution-guided site mutagenesis and regiospecic recognition of key enzymes could control the metabolic ux and enhance the production of BIA metabolic engineering. In addition, the chromosomal modication, promoter libraries, cofactor engineering, and biosensor design could be employed to optimize the metabolic ux and improve the yield. (3) Engineer different enzymes into suitable yeast organelles or employ co-culture systems to optimize the efficiency of BIA synthesis. Furthermore, the employment of plant chassis might be an alternative strategy to synthesize BIA products efficiently.
