# INTRODUCTION

leshy fruits can be categorized into two major groups: climacteric fruits, which show a burst of respiration and ethylene production during fruit ripening, and non‐climacteric (NC) 

fruits, which do not (Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021; Kou et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2022; Perotti et al., 2023). NC fruit ripening is controlled by abscisic acid (ABA) (Gupta et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b). However, increasing evidence suggests that jasmonates, which primarily include jasmonic acid (JA), methyl 

cis‐jasmonate, $( + )$ ‐7‐iso‐jasmonoyl‐L‐isoleucine, and its precursors, are also involved in regulating NC fruit ripening (Concha et al., 2013; Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Yue et al., 2023). This raises the question of why both ABA and JA are required in the regulation of NC fruit ripening, and particularly, what mechanism governs the production of the JA signal during fruit ripening. 

The ripening of fleshy fruits is a complicated process that involves dramatic changes in physiological and biochemical metabolism and determines the development of fruit quality traits. Aroma is a particularly important quality trait because it confers unique flavors to attract consumers (El Hadi et al., 2013; Yan et al., 2018; Sater et al., 2020; George et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024). Fruit aroma arises from various volatile compounds, including terpenes, aldehydes, alcohols, esters, and ketones (El Hadi et al., 2013; Lu et al., 2024). It is well known that fruit ripening and aroma production are closely related. However, it is not known what mechanisms underlie the tight connection between fruit ripening and aroma production. There is evidence that JA may be important for regulating aroma formation (Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). Thus, the coupled processes of fruit ripening and aroma formation are likely determined by the synergistic action of ABA and JA during fruit ripening; however, the underlying mechanisms have not yet been determined. 

The ABA perception and upstream signaling module has three major components: ABA receptors (PYR/PYL/RCARs), type 2C protein phosphatases (PP2Cs) (negative regulators), and SNF1‐related protein kinases 2s (SnRK2s) (positive regulators). ABA signal transduction is triggered by the binding of ABA to PYR/PYL/RCARs to enhance the affinity of ABA receptors for PP2Cs, which results in inhibited PP2C activity; accordingly, PP2Cs are regarded as a coreceptor of ABA (Coego et al., 2021). PP2C inactivation by ABA perception activates SnRK2s. In Arabidopsis, ABA receptors belong to a multigene family (Klingler et al., 2010). ABI1 and SnRK2.6 are the most important components of the ABA signaling pathway, and SnRK2s mediate various responses by phosphorylating target substrates (Hauser et al., 2011). Thus, as the mechanism of ABA perception and upstream signaling is well established, investigations into specific ABA signaling pathways should focus on deciphering the signaling cascade downstream of SnRK2s. In strawberry, while previous work by Han et al. (2015) using transient expression implicated FaSnRK2.6 in ripening regulation in octoploid strawberry (Han et al., 2015), its functional characterization in a stable genetic context and in a diploid model system remains unexplored. 

Jasmonates are perceived by the receptor CORONATINE INSENSITIVE1 (COI1), an F‐box protein that interacts with SKP1‐like1/2, Cullin, and RING‐BOX 1 proteins to form the SCFCOI1 complex (Xie et al., 1998; Xu et al., 2002; Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007; Yan et al., 2009). JASMONATE ZIM‐ DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins are critical repressors of jasmonates signaling, which negatively regulate jasmonates signaling by 

suppressing the function of transcription factors from multiple families (Fernández‐Calvo et al., 2011; Kazan and Manners, 2013; Chini et al., 2016; Mao et al., 2017). Jasmonates binding to COI1 enhances the affinity of COI1 for JAZ proteins, leading to JAZ protein degradation via the SCFCOI1–26S proteasome pathway, thereby triggering JA responses (Thines et al., 2007; Sheard et al., 2010; Yan et al., 2013; Li et al., 2021a; Hu et al., 2023). In previous research, we identified the JAZ member FvJAZ12 in strawberry (Fragaria vesca), which is degraded in a COI1‐independent manner (Wang et al., 2024). Furthermore, we identified a signaling cascade downstream of FvJAZ12, that is, FvJAZ12–MYELOCYTOMATOSIS 2 (FvMYC2)–lipoxygenase 3 (FvLOX3). FvLOX3 plays an important role in diverse biological processes, but particularly in regulating secondary metabolism, which includes biotic responses, flavor formation, and JA biosynthesis. Thus, FvJAZ12 may play a crucial role in regulating strawberry quality traits. 

In this study, we explore the mechanisms governing JA biosynthesis during fruit ripening in strawberry, a model NC fruit. We first demonstrate that FvSnRK2.6 is involved in the ABA signaling pathway. Then, by screening the physical interaction of FvSnRK2.6 with the FvJAZ family, we show that FvJAZ12 is the sole member that can physically interact with and be phosphorylated by FvSnRK2.6. Based on this novel finding, we decipher a signaling cascade from ABA signaling to JA biosynthesis. Moreover, we demonstrate that JA functions as a critical signal to control aroma production during strawberry fruit ripening. Thus, this study has not only revealed the signaling mechanisms governing JA biosynthesis but also elucidated the mechanisms underlying the tight connection between the initiation of fruit ripening and aroma production.

# The effects of ABA on fruit ripening and aroma production depend on jasmonates biosynthesis

Despite ABA being the key signal controlling fruit ripening and quality, evidence suggests that jasmonates also regulate these processes (Jia et al., 2016; Nakajima et al., 2021; Yue et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). To determine whether jasmonates mediate the effects of ABA, we used phenidone, an inhibitor of jasmonates biosynthesis, to examine the promoting effect of ABA on fruit ripening and aroma formation. ABA application substantially promoted fruit ripening, as indicated by changes of pigmentation (Figure 1A), and remarkably enhanced both the expression of key jasmonate biosynthetic genes (Figure S1) and the endogenous levels of JA, MeJA, and JA‐Ile (Figure S2). These results suggest that ABA promotes jasmonates production during strawberry fruit ripening. However, phenidone application inhibited the promoting effect of ABA on pigmentation, implying that ABA‐induced pigmentation is dependent on jasmonates biosynthesis. # FvSnRK2.6 is a component of the ABA signaling pathway and jasmonates biosynthesis

It has been well established that SnRK2s act to mediate ABA signaling. SnRK2s belong to a multigene family. To identify the 

core component of SnRK2s in the ABA signaling pathway, we searched the SnRK2s family in the strawberry genome (Figure S5), which showed that FvSnRK2.6 is the homolog of Arabidopsis OST1 (AtSnRK2.6), the core component of the ABA signaling pathway. Previous studies have established that FvABI1, a PP2C phosphatase, is a key negative regulator of ABA signaling in strawberry (Jia et al., 2013). To determine whether FvSnRK2.6 is also a component of the ABA signaling pathway in strawberry, we examined its physical interaction with FvABI1. A bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay (Figure 2A) and co‐immunoprecipitation (Co‐IP) analysis (Figure 2B) confirmed a physical interaction between them. Furthermore, an immunoprecipitation (IP)‐kinase assay showed that FvSnRK2.6 activation was sensitive to ABA stimulus (Figure 2C), confirming that FvSnRK2.6 is involved in the ABA signaling pathway in strawberry.

Consistent with a role in ABA signaling, the seedling growth of FvSnRK2.6‐OE and FvSnRK2.6‐CE plants displayed enhanced and reduced sensitivity to ABA, respectively (Figure S7). To identify the signaling components downstream of FvSnRK2.6, we used the BiFC technique to screen the FvJAZ family for members that may interact with FvSnRK2.6. Co‐IP (Figure 3B) and luciferase complementation imaging assays (Figure S9) further confirmed the physical 

interaction between FvSnRK2.6 and FvJAZ12. These results demonstrate that FvJAZ12 is the only FvJAZ member that physically interacts with FvSnRK2.6.

Phosphorylation controls the protein stability of FvJAZ12 As transcriptional repressors, JAZs are degraded in response to jasmonates signaling. Because FvJAZ12 is phosphorylated by 

FvSnRK2.6, we examined whether FvSnRK2.6 affects FvJAZ12 stability by conducting a cell‐free assay. Briefly, FvJAZ12‐GST was expressed in and extracted from E. coli. FvSnRK2.6‐GFP was transiently expressed in strawberry fruits, and the total protein containing FvSnRK2.6‐GFP was extracted from the fruits. Subsequently, FvJAZ12‐GST was incubated with the total protein extract for different periods. As shown in Figure 4A, FvJAZ12‐GST degradation was much faster when FvSnRK2.6‐ GFP was expressed in the fruit than when the empty vector carrying only GFP was expressed. The addition of MG132, an inhibitor of ubiquitination, inhibited FvJAZ12‐GST degradation, which implies that FvJAZ12‐GST degradation was a result of ubiquitination. To investigate whether the in vivo degradation of FvJAZ12 responds to ABA signaling, FvSnRK2.6‐GFP or the GFP empty vector was expressed in the fruit of FvJAZ12‐FLAG stable transgenic plants, the fruit was treated with ABA, and the amount of FvJAZ12‐FLAG was detected by an anti‐FLAG blot. As shown in Figure 4B, the FvJAZ12‐FLAG blot signal disappeared when FvSnRK2.6‐GFP was expressed or treated with ABA. These results demonstrate that FvSnRK2.6 promotes the in vivo degradation of FvJAZ12 in response to ABA treatment.

To further explore the mechanism by which phosphorylation controls FvJAZ12 stability, we identified the phosphorylation site of FvJAZ12 by mutating the phosphorylation site to alanine (Ala/A), a non‐phosphorylatable amino acid residue. The protein mass spectrometry assay identified five potential phosphorylation sites: S127, S156, S158, T162, and T173 (Figure S10), whereas the bioinformatics assay identified two potential phosphorylation sites: S142 and S156 (Wang et al., 2013; Mondal et al., 2024). Thus, six phosphorylation sites in the FvJAZ12 protein sequence may be phosphorylated by FvSnRK2.6 (Figure 4C). To confirm the true phosphorylation sites, we examined the effect of the “A” mutation of each of the six amino acid residues on phosphorylation. Notably, substituting S142 with Ala (designated S142A) completely inhibited FvJAZ12 phosphorylation, implying that S142 represents the phosphorylation site (Figure 4C). S142 is located only in the JAS domain (Figure 4D), which controls JAZ protein stability (Chico et al., 2008; Fonseca et al., 2009; Pauwels and Goossens, 2011). After identifying S142 as the phosphorylation site of FvJAZ12, we examined whether it also controls FvJAZ12 degradation. FvJAZ12WT (non‐mutated WT FvJAZ12) and FvJAZ12S142A were fused with firefly luciferase (Fluc), and vectors carrying FvJAZ12WT‐Fluc or FvJAZ12S142A‐Fluc were co‐transformed with FvSnRK2.6‐MYC into tobacco leaves, after which the Fluc signal was detected. As shown in Figure 4E, the Fluc signal was much stronger following transformation of FvJAZ12S142A than following transformation of FvJAZ12WT, indicating that the FvSnRK2.6‐catalyzed phosphorylation of FvJAZ12 at the S142 residue promotes FvJAZ12 degradation. 

# FvSnRK2.6‐catalyzed phosphorylation at the S142 residue plays a key role in aroma production

To demonstrate that the phosphorylation of FvJAZ12 at S142 residue acts to regulate aroma production, we performed complementation of FvJAZ12 in the FvJAZ12‐CE background, that is, transient expression of FvJAZ12 driven by the FvJAZ12 promoter in the fruit of the FvJAZ12‐CE background. In addition to Com-$J A Z \boldsymbol { 1 } \boldsymbol { 2 } ^ { W T }$ complementation (i.e., WT FvJAZ12 gene without mutation), we also performed complementation with $F v J A Z 1 2 ^ { S 1 4 2 A }$ (i.e., the FvJAZ12 gene encoding the protein with S142 mutated to Ala/A) (Figure S11). Volatile metabolites in ripe fruits were analyzed using gas chromatography‐mass spectrometry. Among the volatile compounds, the percentage of ester compounds was $> 1 7 \%$ , indicating that ester compounds are the major components of aroma (Figure 5A). Figure 5B shows the effects of FvJAZ12 complementation on ABA‐induced ester production. More than $6 2 \%$ of ester compounds showed no significant content differences between $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { W T }$ and the WT, whereas more than $60 \%$ of ester compounds showed significant content differences between $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { S 1 4 2 A }$ and the WT, implying that ComJAZ12S142A resulted in much lower sensitivity of ester compound production to ABA treatment. Figure 5C shows the impact of S142 phosphorylation on the formation of several ester compounds upon ABA treatment. Compared to the WT control, complementation with $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { S 1 4 2 A }$ significantly suppressed the ABA‐induced production of multiple primary ester aroma 

compounds. Notably, three key ester compounds (methyl hexanoate, ethyl hexanoate, and methyl caprylate), which are major contributors to strawberry aroma, exhibited significant differences in content among the different lines (Figure 5D). In contrast to the WT, $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { S 1 4 2 A }$ failed to restore ABA‐induced synthesis of these three esters, while $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { W T }$ partially restored it. As expected, the JAZ12‐CE line maintained relatively high ester levels due to the lack of the FvJAZ12 gene. These phenotypic differences were consistent with transcriptional changes: $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { S 1 4 2 A }$ barely rescued the ABA‐induced expression of key ester biosynthetic genes, while $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { W T }$ restored their expression to a level similar to the WT (Figure 5E). Furthermore, endogenous levels of JAs (JA, MeJA, JA‐Ile) (Figure 5F) and the expression of jasmonate biosynthesis genes (Figure 5G) were significantly lower in the $C o m J A Z 1 2 S ^ { 1 4 2 A }$ line compared to the $C o m J A Z 1 2 ^ { W T }$ line. Taken together, these results demonstrate that FvJAZ12 phosphorylation catalyzed by FvSnRK2.6 at the S142 residue regulates aroma production as well as jasmonates biosynthesis. 

# The signaling cascade from ABA signaling to JA biosynthesis

In previous research, we showed that the FvJAZ12–FvMYC2– FvLOX3 signaling pathway mediates flavor formation in strawberries (Wang et al., 2024). In this study, we aimed to elucidate the signaling cascade that mediates ABA‐induced jasmonates biosynthesis. Given that FvSnRK2.6 is activated by ABA and that FvSnRK2.6‐catalyzed FvJAZ12 phosphorylation controls FvJAZ12 stability, we examined whether the interaction between FvJAZ12 and FvMYC2 responds to ABA signaling. The interaction between FvJAZ12 and FvMYC2 was analyzed using luciferase complementation imaging in the presence or absence of ABA. As shown in Figure 6A, ABA treatment significantly inhibited the interaction between FvJAZ12 and FvMYC2, as shown by the much lower fluorescence intensity following ABA treatment. 

To explore the connection between FvMYC2 and jasmonates biosynthesis, we examined whether FvMYC2 modulates the expression of genes encoding key enzymes in the jasmonates biosynthesis pathway. Transient expression of FvMYC2 in strawberries resulted in a significant increase in the transcript levels of all genes encoding key enzymes in this pathway, which included FvAOC3, FvAOS, FvOPR3, and FvLOX3 (Figure 6B). The electrophoretic mobility shift assay indicated that FvMYC2 can bind to the promoters of all four genes (Figure 6C). Additionally, analysis using the effector‐ reporter technique showed that FvMYC2 overexpression greatly promoted $\beta$ ‐glucuronidase (GUS) reporter activity driven by the promoters of the four genes (Figure 6D). Taken together, these results demonstrate that FvMYC2 modulates the expression of genes involved in jasmonates biosynthesis.

# DISCUSSION

Despite abundant research showing that jasmonates play an important role in the fruit defense response (Rehman et al., 

2023; Farooq et al., 2024; Danso Ofori et al., 2025), their roles in fruit ripening are unclear. Several studies have examined changes in jasmonates levels during fruit development and ripening, as well as the pharmacological effect of jasmonates on fruit ripening. However, previous studies on the precise function of jasmonates in fruit ripening have produced conflicting results. For example, Kim et al. (Kim et al., 2019) reported that methyl‐jasmonates levels decrease with 

strawberry fruit development and ripening, and Camarero et al. (Camarero et al., 2023) reported that JA levels decrease during olive fruit development and ripening, implying that jasmonates function as negative signals to suppress fruit ripening (Camarero et al., 2023). Similarly, the application of exogenous jasmonates inhibits fruit ripening. For example, the application of methyl cis‐jasmonate reduces ethylene production and respiration rates in mangoes (Nguyen et al., 2024), and the application of methyl‐jasmonate downregulates some ripening‐related genes, such as 1‐aminocyclopropane‐1‐carboxylic acid oxidase and polygalacturonase, in peaches (Ziosi et al., 2008). In addition, ethylene helps initiate fruit ripening by suppressing JA signaling in blueberries (Wang and Nambeesan, 2024). However, a larger body of research suggests that jasmonates application promotes an array of ripening‐associated metabolisms or gene expression, such as coloration in various fruit species (Liu et al., 2012; Jia et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018; An et al., 2021; Nakajima et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 

2024), ethylene production in apples (Li et al., 2017b), terpene biosynthesis in strawberries (Zhang et al., 2022) and grapes (Wang et al., 2022a), and aroma production in grape berries (Li et al., 2023). These results suggest that jasmonates function as positive signals to promote fruit ripening. In strawberry, exogenous MeJA treatment promoted fruit ripening, as evidenced by the increased levels of aroma compounds (Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024), anthocyanins (Concha et al., 2013; Han et al., 2019), lignin (Concha et al., 2013), soluble sugars (Han et al., 2019), and enhanced fruit firmness (Concha et al., 2013; Han et al., 2019). In this study, we demonstrate that ABA can induce jasmonates biosynthesis, implying that ABA accumulation is accompanied by an increase in jasmonates levels during fruit ripening. Moreover, we demonstrate that jasmonates regulate aroma production during fruit ripening (Figures 1, 5). It should be noted that previously reported reductions in jasmonates levels during fruit development and ripening refer to reductions over the entire duration from fruit set to ripening; jasmonates

Thus, our findings support those of previous research regarding changes in jasmonates levels during fruit ripening. Regardless of whether jasmonates regulate fruit ripening, our results show that ABA‐induced jasmonates production mediates aroma production in strawberries. 

Fruit ripening is a complex process involving dramatic changes in an array of metabolic processes that lead to the formation of various fruit quality traits, such as color, sugar, acid, flavor, aroma, firmness, and texture, which are tightly coupled. Once the fruit becomes pigmented, an increase in sugar and flavor production or a decrease in fruit firmness inevitably occur. Similarly, when fruit firmness begins to decrease, fruit pigmentation and an increase in sugar and flavor also inevitably occur. This coupling of fruit quality traits makes it challenging to improve fruit quality. Accordingly, understanding the mechanisms underlying the coupling of fruit quality traits is especially important for improving fruit quality via molecular breeding. In contrast, the regulation of fruit development and ripening involves multiple hormones, among which ABA is the key hormone initiating fruit ripening (Li et al., 2021b; Fan et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2022). Understanding the synergistic action of these hormones is important for controlling fruit development and ripening. In this study, we deciphered the complete signaling cascade from ABA signaling to jasmonates biosynthesis, demonstrating a tight coupling between ABA and JA biosynthesis. Given that ABA triggers fruit ripening and jasmonates regulate aroma production, these results provide insights into the coupling mechanism controlling the initiation of fruit ripening and aroma production. 

The upstream cascade of ABA signaling comprises three major components: ABA receptors (PYR/PYL/RCARs), PP2Cs, and SnRK2s. SnRK2s belong to a multigene family containing nine members of the octaploid strawberry genome (Han et al., 2015). In Arabidopsis, OST1 acts as a pivotal signaling component that links the upstream cascade with downstream responses in the ABA signaling pathway. In this study, we identified a strawberry homolog of OST1, named FvSnRK2.6, and demonstrated its important role in ABA signaling, as evidenced by its ability to interact with the ABA coreceptor FvABI1 and its activation in response to ABA stimulus (Figure 2). As the mechanism for the upstream signaling cascade from ABA perception to SnRK2s activation has been well established, to uncover the mechanism for ABA‐induced jasmonates production, we aimed to decipher the signaling cascade downstream of FvSnRK2.6, rather than explore the mechanism for ABA receptor perception. JAZ repressors are key components of the jasmonate signaling pathway (Ruan et al., 2019; Griffiths, 2020). In this study, we demonstrate that FvSnRK2.6 interacts with and phosphorylates FvJAZ12, implying that FvJAZ12 is a critical signaling component in the JA signaling pathway as well as the ABA signaling pathway. Thus, our findings shed new light on the mechanism underlying the crosstalk between ABA and JA signaling. 

Previous studies have examined the expression profile of the JAZ family in fruit development and ripening in different 

plant species (Song et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022c; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025), and shown that the transcript levels of most JAZ genes decrease during fruit development and ripening, suggesting that JAZs play an important role in regulating fruit development and ripening. We previously identified eight FvJAZ genes in the diploid strawberry genome (Wang et al., 2024), among which FvJAZ12 mediates flavor formation. Moreover, we demonstrated that FvJAZ12 can be phosphorylated by FvMAPK6 at S179 and T183, thereby modulating its nuclear accumulation in response to both biotic and abiotic stresses. In the current study, by screening the FvJAZ family, we demonstrated that FvJAZ12 is the only member of FvJAZs that can physically interact with FvSnRK2.6 and be phosphorylated by FvSnRK2.6 at S142 to promote its degradation. These results demonstrate that FvJAZ12 functions to regulate different biological processes via different mechanisms. The roles of other FvJAZs in strawberry fruit development and ripening should be investigated in future research. 

In this study, we revealed the signaling cascade from ABA signaling to JA biosynthesis. As ABA is a pivotal signal controlling NC fruit ripening, this signaling cascade likely exists in all NC fruits and is not limited to strawberries. However, the question remains whether this signaling cascade also regulates climacteric fruit ripening. Previous research has shown that exogenous ABA also promotes climacteric fruit ripening and that ABA levels increase during fruit ripening (Zhang et al., 2009; Mou et al., 2016; Tao et al., 2020). Li et al. (Li et al., 2017b) reported that jasmonates induce ethylene biosynthesis in apple fruit, as shown by the fact that MdMYC2, a transcription factor in the JA signaling pathway, binds to the promoters of both MdACS1 and the ACC oxidase gene MdACO1 to enhance their transcription. Assuming that a signaling cascade for ABA‐induced jasmonates production also exists in climacteric fruits, we hypothesize that this signaling cascade involves the coupling of three positive signals, ABA, JA, and ethylene; that is, ABA acts to induce JA production, which then induces ethylene production. However, this hypothesis should be confirmed through future studies. 

In summary, we deciphered a signaling cascade from ABA signaling to jasmonates production (ABA perception $\mathsf { F v S n R K 2 . 6 } \to \mathsf { F v J A Z 1 2 } \to \mathsf { F v M Y C 2 } \to \mathsf { F v L O X 3 } / \mathsf { F v A O C 3 } / \mathsf { F v A O S } / \mathsf { A }$ FvOPR3 jasmonates biosynthesis) and demonstrated that jasmonates are critical signals mediating aroma production during strawberry fruit ripening (Figure 7). This study improves our understanding of the signaling mechanisms governing the jasmonate biosynthesis and provides insights into the mechanism underlying the tight coupling of fruit ripening initiation with aroma production. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant materials and culture conditions

Diploid strawberry plants (Fragaria vesca “Fragola di Bosco”) were used as the primary experimental material, whereas 

octoploid strawberry fruits (Fragaria $\times$ ananassa ‘Duch. Benihoppe) were used exclusively for the transient expression assays. Both strawberry and Nicotiana benthamiana plants were grown under controlled environmental conditions: light/dark photoperiod, $1 6 / 8 \mathsf { h }$ ; temperature, $_ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ ; relative humidity, $70 \%$ . 

# RNA extraction and RT‐qPCR analysis

RNA extraction and RT‐qPCR were performed as previously described (Wang et al., 2021). Briefly, total RNA was extracted from samples using a Plant RNA Kit (Omega, R6827‐01). First‐strand cDNA was synthesized according to the HiScript II 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit protocol (+gDNA wiper) (Vazyme, R212‐01). RT‐qPCR was performed using a QuantStudio 6 Flex Real‐time PCR system with ChamQ SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, Q311‐02). Fvactin was used as the internal reference gene. 

# Prokaryotic protein expression and purification

The coding sequences of FvJAZ12, FvSnRK2.6, and FvMYC2/ bHLH were cloned into a pGEX‐4T2 vector. The FvJAZ12‐GST, FvSnRK2.6‐GST, and FvMYC2/bHLH‐GST constructs were transformed into chemically competent BL21 (DE3) cells. The expression of proteins was induced with the addition of 1 mmol/L isopropyl $\beta$ ‐d‐1‐thiogalactopyranoside and grown for 12–14 h. Proteins were purified according to the manufacturer's protocol. 

# Plant protein extraction and immunoblot analysis

Plant total protein was extracted with the extraction buffer (phosphate buffer pH 7.8, 1 mmol/L ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), $10 \%$ glycerol, $0 . 5 \%$ Triton X‐100, 1 mmol/L dithiothreitol, 1 mmol/L phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, protease inhibitor cocktail $( 1 \times )$ , and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail $( 1 \times ) )$ . Immunoblot analysis was performed as described previously (Wang et al., 2021) with minor revisions. Protein samples were separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate‐ polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, and immunoblot analysis was performed using antibodies. 

# BiFC assays

For complementation assays, FvSnRK2.6 coding sequences were cloned into the pSPYCE vector to generate N‐terminal YFP fusions. The coding sequences of FvABI1 and FvJAZ family members (FvJAZ1, FvJAZ3, FvJAZ4, FvJAZ5, FvJAZ6, FvJAZ8, FvJAZ11, FvJAZ12) were individually cloned into pSPYCE vectors to generate C‐terminal YFP fusions. The resulting constructs were transformed into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Bacterial cultures harboring the respective plasmids were co‐infiltrated into leaves of N. benthamiana using Agrobacterium‐mediated transient expression. Fluorescence signals were visualized $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ post infiltration using a Zeiss LSM 880 confocal microscope. SPYNE and SPYCE were used as negative controls. 

# Co‐IP assays

The coding sequences of FvSnRK2.6 were cloned into the pSuper1300‐MYC vector, and the sequences of 

FvJAZ12 were cloned into the pSuper1300‐GFP vector. The FvSnRK2.6‐MYC and FvJAZ12‐GFP vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens and transiently co‐expressed in strawberries. Total protein was extracted after 3 d. Immunoprecipitation was performed using an anti‐GFP mAb agarose (MBL D153‐8) or an anti‐Myc‐tag mAb agarose (MBL M047‐8). The immunoprecipitated proteins were subsequently analyzed by immunoblotting with anti‐GFP antibody (ABclonal AE012) and anti‐MYC antibodies (MBL M047‐3). 

# Luciferase complementation imaging

For protein interaction studies between FvJAZ12 and FvSnRK2.6 or FvMYC2, the sequences of FvSnRK2.6 and FvMYC2 were cloned into the pCAMBIA‐cLuc vector, and the coding sequences of FvJAZ12 were cloned into the pCAMBIA‐nLuc vector. The vectors were then transformed into Agrobacterium and infiltrated into different areas of N. benthamiana leaves, as previously described (Wang et al., 2024). Luciferase images were obtained using a cold‐charge‐ coupled camera (Nikon‐L936) after 48 h of incubation. nLUC and cLUC were used as negative controls. 

# Cell‐free protein degradation assay

The cell‐free degradation assay was conducted following an established protocol (Li et al., 2017a) with modifications. Total protein was extracted from strawberries using ice‐cold degradation buffer containing $2 5 \mathsf { m M }$ Tris‐HCl (pH 7.5), 10 mM NaCl, $1 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 500 mM sucrose, $1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ EDTA, 5 mM dithiothreitol, and $1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ adenosine triphosphate (ATP), supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail. For degradation reactions, 100 ng of purified recombinant FvJAZ12‐GST fusion protein was incubated with $6 0 0 \mu \ g$ of total protein extract in a 50‐μL reaction system at $_ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for specified durations (0, 15, 30, 45, and 60 min). Following incubation, the samples were immediately placed on ice to terminate the reaction. FvJAZ12‐GST protein levels were analyzed by immunoblotting using a GST‐specific monoclonal antibody (Abclonal, AE001; 1:5,000 dilution). To normalize protein loading, actin was detected using a plant actin antibody (CW0264; 1:5,000 dilution; CwBio). 

# In vitro kinase assay

The in vitro kinase assay was performed according to a published protocol (Li et al., 2017a) with modifications. Briefly, $0 . 2 \mu \mathfrak { g }$ of purified recombinant FvSnRK2.6‐GST (kinase) was incubated with $2 \mu \ g$ of substrate proteins, including WT FvJAZ12‐GST and its six phosphorylation site mutants (S127A, S142A, S156A, S158A, T162A, T173A) in a $3 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction system containing kinase assay buffer. Reactions were initiated by adding 0.1 μL [γ‐ 32P] ATP $( 1 ~ \mu \mathsf { C i } )$ and incubated at ${ } ^ { 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 30 min. To terminate the reactions, $7 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 \times { \mathsf { S D S } }$ loading buffer was added, followed by boiling at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min. Radioactive signals were detected using a Typhoon FLA 9500 Biomolecular Imager (Amersham, USA). Coomassie Brilliant Blue staining was used as a loading control. 

# Immunoprecipitation kinase assay

The IP‐kinase assay was conducted following the protocol established by Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2021). Protein was extracted from fruit samples in which FvSnRK2.6 was overexpressed using a lysis buffer. The mixture was then incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. Subsequently, proteins were immunoprecipitated using anti‐GFP mAb‐agarose (MBL, D153‐8). The samples were thoroughly mixed and incubated with gentle agitation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 45 min. The beads were then washed with a kinase buffer and resuspended in the kinase buffer supplemented with [γ‐ 32P] ATP. In this experiment, the substrate proteins were maltose‐binding protein and E. coli‐expressed FvJAZ12 fused with a GST tag. The resulting gels were visualized using a Typhoon FLA 9500 Biomolecular Imager (Amersham, USA). 

# Phos‐tag assay

Protein samples were separated using an $8 \%$ Phos‐tag polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis system containing $5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l / L }$ $\mathsf { M n C l } _ { 2 }$ and $1 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l / L } }$ Phos‐tag AAL solution (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemical Corporation). Following electrophoresis, the gel was washed with transfer buffer supplemented with $10 \%$ methanol and $1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ EDTA to remove excess $\mathsf { M n } ^ { 2 + }$ . Proteins were transferred from the Phos‐tag gel to a nitrocellulose membrane using a semi‐dry transfer system. The same protein samples were separated on a $10 \%$ sodium dodecyl sulfate‐polyacrylamide gel to verify equal protein loading. Phosphorylated and non‐phosphorylated forms of FvJAZ12 were detected by immunoblotting using an anti‐GFP antibody (ABclonal AE012, 1:5000 dilution). 

# Gas chromatography‐mass spectrometry analysis of volatile metabolites

Strawberry fruits were snap‐frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground into a fine powder for gas chromatography‐mass spectrometry analysis. Metabolites showing |Log2(fold change)| $\geq 1 . 0$ between experimental groups were defined as differentially accumulated metabolites. The identified metabolites were annotated using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes compound database, then mapped to the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes Pathway database. The pathways of the significantly regulated metabolites were then subjected to Metabolite Set Enrichment Analysis, and their significance was determined by a hypergeometric test using $P$ ‐values. 

# Transient fruit transformation

Transient transformation of strawberry fruits was performed as previously described, with minor revisions (Zhao et al., 2019). Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 carrying either target vectors or empty vector controls was cultured to the mid‐log phase $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 6 - 0 . 8 )$ in LB medium. Agrobacterium was individually injected into strawberry fruits. For each construct, three independent biological replicates were analyzed, with each replicate comprising 15 transformed fruits. Protein was extracted from the fruits after 4–6 d of incubation. 

# Stable transformation of diploid strawberry plants

To generate FvSnRK2.6‐OE plants, the full‐length coding sequence of FvSnRK2.6 was cloned into the pSuper1300‐ GFP vector, which contained a C‐terminal GFP tag. For CRISPR/Cas9‐mediated genome editing of FvSnRK2.6 to generate FvSnRK2.6‐CE plants, we designed two specific single‐guide RNAs targeting the exon regions and cloned them into the pYLCRISPR/Cas9 vector following established methods (Zeng et al., 2018). The resulting Agrobacterium tumefaciens cultures were used to transform diploid strawberry leaf disks as previously described (Oosumi et al., 2006). 

# Plant hormone quantification

Endogenous jasmonates were quantified in FvSnRK2.6‐OE, FvSnRK2.6‐CE, and WT strawberries using high‐performance liquid chromatography‐tandem mass spectrometry. Frozen fruit tissues were homogenized in an ice‐cold extraction solvent (isopropanol/water/hydrochloric acid). The contents of endogenous jasmonates, such as JA, methyl cis‐ jasmonate, 12‐oxo‐phytodienoic acid, $( + )$ ‐7‐iso‐jasmonoyl‐L‐ isoleucine, and 2‐hydroxylated JA, were determined in the fruit tissues using an Agilent 1290 high‐performance liquid chromatography instrument connected in series to an AB Sciex QTRAP $6 5 0 0 +$ mass spectrometer. Internal standards were added during the extraction process to correct the detection results. 

# GUS activity assay

A 2,000‐bp DNA fragment spanning the upstream region of the translational start site of FvLOX3, FvAOS, FvAOC3, or FvOPR3 was amplified and cloned into the pCAMBIA1301‐ GUS vector upstream of the GUS reporter gene. The resulting constructs were individually introduced into white‐stage strawberries via Agrobacterium‐mediated transient transformation. GUS activity was quantified following the protocol described by Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2024). For each construct, three independent biological replicates were analyzed, with each replicate comprising 15 transformed fruits. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The binding sites of FvMYC2/bHLH proteins on the promoters of FvLOX3, FvAOS, FvAOC3, FvOPR3 were predicted using the PlantRegMap online tool (Tian et al., 2020). For experimental validation, oligonucleotide probes corresponding to the predicted binding regions were synthesized and end‐labeled with biotin using an EMSA Probe Biotin Labeling Kit (Beyotime, GS008), following the manufacturer's protocol. Electrophoretic mobility shift assays were subsequently performed to assess protein‐DNA interactions using a Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime, GS009).
