# A B S T R A C T

To fend off microbial pathogens and herbivores, plants have evolved a wide range of defense strategies such as physical barriers, or the production of anti-digestive proteins or bioactive specialized metabolites. Accumulation of the latter compounds is often regulated by transcriptional activation of the biosynthesis pathway genes by the phytohormone jasmonate-isoleucine. Here, we used our recently developed flower petal transformation method in the medicinal plant Catharanthus roseus to shed light on the complex regulatory mechanisms steering the jasmonate-modulated biosynthesis of monoterpenoid indole alkaloids (MIAs), to which the anti-cancer compounds vinblastine and vincristine belong. By combinatorial overexpression of the transcriptional activators BIS1, ORCA3 and MYC2a, we provide an unprecedented insight into the modular transcriptional control of MIA biosynthesis. Furthermore, we show that the expression of an engineered de-repressed MYC2a triggers a tremendous reprogramming of the MIA pathway, finally leading to massively increased accumulation of at least 23 MIAs. The current study unveils an innovative approach for future metabolic engineering efforts for the production of valuable bioactive plant compounds in non-model plants.

# 1. Introduction

The medicinal plant Catharanthus roseus (Madagascar periwinkle) produces over 130 monoterpenoid indole alkaloids (MIAs), including the anticancer compounds vinblastine and vincristine (Fig. 1A) (van der Heijden et al., 2004). Today, C. roseus still remains the only source of these compounds, as their total chemical synthesis is economically not viable (Eastman, 2011; van der Heijden et al., 2004). Developing alternatives such as biotechnology-based production methods in hairy roots, suspension cell or yeast cultures has been a long-standing research subject (Facchini and De Luca, 2008; O'Connor, 2015; van der Heijden et al., 2004). In spite of the growing knowledge of the entire MIA pathway, so far only precursors of vinblastine have been successfully produced in heterologous systems, including the MIA precursor strictosidine in yeast and tobacco (Brown et al., 2015; Miettinen et al., 2014) and vindoline in yeast after feeding with the precursor tabersonine (Qu et al., 2015).

MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus is organ and cell-type specific and

compartmentalized at the cellular and subcellular level (Courdavault et al., 2014; Dugé de Bernonville et al., 2015). At the organ level, bisindole alkaloids such as vinblastine and vincristine, as well as vindoline are produced exclusively in the areal parts of the plant, whereas catharanthine accumulates in all organs (Pan et al., 2016; van der Heijden et al., 2004). Other abundant MIAs such as ajmalicine, serpentine and the tabersonine-derived MIAs hörhammericine, lochnericine and 19-hydroxytabersonine are mostly present in roots (Laflamme et al., 2001; Pan et al., 2016; Rodriguez et al., 2003; van der Heijden et al., 2004). At the cellular level, the first seven biosynthesis steps from geranyl diphosphate (GPP) to the iridoid loganic acid take place in internal phloem associated parenchyma (IPAP) cells and involve Geraniol Synthase (GES), 8-Hydroxygeraniol Oxidoreductase (8HGO), Geraniol 8-Oxidase (G8O), Iridoid Synthase (IS), Iridoid Oxidase (IO), 7-Deoxyloganetic Acid Glucosyltransferase (7DLGT) AND 7-Deoxyloganic Acid Hydroxylase (7DLH) (Asada et al., 2013; Geu-Flores et al., 2012; Miettinen et al., 2014; Salim et al., 2014; Simkin et al., 2013). The latter involve Geissoschizine Synthase 1 and 2 (GS1 and 2) that, in conjunction with Geissoschizine Oxidase (GO) and the recently published Redox1 and Redox2, produce the stable intermediate stemmadenine, a precursor of both vindoline and catharanthine (Qu et al., 2018; Tatsis et al., 2017) (Fig. 1A). Next, stemmadenine undergoes acetylation by Stemmadenine-O-Acetyltransferase (SAT) to form O-acetylstemmadenine, which can be converted to catharanthine by $\alpha / \beta$ Hydrolase 1 (HL1) or to tabersonine by $\alpha / \beta$ Hydrolase 2 (HL2) (Qu et al., 2018) (Fig. 1A). Also the conversion of tabersonine to vindoline has been resolved and involves the action of seven enzymes spread over three cell types, i.e. the epidermis, the laticifers and the idioblasts (Besseau et al., 2013; Guirimand et al., 2011; Levac et al., 2008; Liscombe et al., 2010; Qu et al., 2015; St-Pierre et al., 1999) (Fig. 1A). In the presence of the peroxidase Prx1, the monomeric MIAs vindoline and catharanthine can dimerize to form 3’,4’-anhydrovinblastine, the direct precursor of vinblastine and vincristine (Costa et al., 2008; van der Heijden et al., 2004).

Like for many specialized metabolites, MIA biosynthesis is regulated by the plant hormone jasmonate (JA) and serves predominantly a defensive role against insects and pathogens (De Geyter et al., 2012; Dugé de Bernonville et al., 2017; Goossens et al., 2016, 2017; Roepke et al., 2010; Wasternack and Hause, 2013; Zhang et al., 2011; Zhou and Memelink, 2016). In general, in the absence of JA, in particular its bioactive form JA-isoleucine (JA-Ile), the activity of positive regulators of the JA response, such as the transcription factor (TF) MYC2, is physically blocked by the interaction with Jasmonate-Zim Domain (JAZ) proteins (Chini et al., 2016; Pauwels and Goossens, 2011; Wasternack and Strnad, 2018). Insect attack, wounding or JA treatment is followed by a burst of JA-Ile that promotes the interaction between the JAZ repressor proteins and the F-box Coronatine Insensitive 1 (COI1) protein, leading to ubiquitination and consequent proteasomal degradation of the JAZ proteins. Subsequently, TFs such as MYC2 are de-repressed, leading to transcriptional activation of their target genes (Chini et al., 2016; Goossens et al., 2017; Wasternack and Strnad, 2018; Zhou and Memelink, 2016). Recently, it has been shown that specific amino acid changes in the JAZ Interaction Domain (JID) of Arabidopsis thaliana MYC2 (AtMYC2) can prevent the interaction with the JAZ proteins, resulting in a constitutively active (hereafter called ‘de-repressed’) form, such as AtMYC2D105N (Frerigmann et al., 2014; Gasperini et al., 2015; Goossens et al., 2015; Smolen et al., 2002; Zhang et al., 2015).

JA-mediated elicitation of MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus organs involves several other TFs. Expression of the IPAP-specific MIA biosynthesis genes is controlled by the Basic Helix-Loop-Helix Iridoid Synthesis (BIS) 1 and BIS2 TFs (Fig. 1A) (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015). The expression of the epidermis-specific genes steering the following steps leading to strictosidine is controlled by the clade of the Octadecanoid Derivative Responsive Catharanthus Apetala 2-Domain (ORCA) 2, ORCA3, ORCA4, and ORCA5 TFs (Menke et al., 1999; Paul et al., 2017; van der Fits and Memelink, 2000; Zhou and Memelink, 2016). The expression of the BIS TFs as well as the ORCA TFs can be stimulated by JAs (Paul et al., 2017; Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015; Zhang et al., 2011). The mechanism by which the whole MIA pathway and/or the above TFs are controlled by the primary MYC2 JA-signaling module is not well understood yet, because evidence for both indirect and direct actions exist. Overexpression of CrMYC2 in cell suspension and hairy root cultures has been shown to induce the expression of ORCA genes (Paul et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2011) and CrMYC2 and ORCA3 have both been shown to be capable of transactivating the TDC promoter in tobacco protoplasts (Paul et al., 2017).

Recently, we have developed a transient expression system in C. roseus flower petals, thereby creating a rapid and non-labor intensive screening platform for pathway mapping, gene discovery and assessment of engineering tools (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016). Here, we have employed this platform to further unravel the organization of the regulatory TF circuit that drives MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus. Subsequently, we have exploited the gained knowledge to design a combinatorial module of wild-type and engineered, i.e. gain-of-function mutant, versions of three C. roseus TFs that together boost MIA biosynthesis in a coordinated manner across cell types.

# 2. Material and methods

# 2.1. Plant material and transformation

Flower petals of one-year-old Catharanthus roseus var. “Little bright eyes” plants (grown under greenhouse conditions) were infiltrated with Agrobacterium tumefaciens C58C1 harboring the respective constructs for overexpression (see below) as previously described (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016), with slight modifications. One day prior to the infiltration experiment, all open flowers were removed from the plants. The day of infiltration, liquid A. tumefaciens cultures were washed, resuspended and diluted to a final $\mathrm { O D } = 0 . 3$ in infiltration buffer $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone, 10 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 20 mM MES) and left shaking for $^ { 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . Petals of freshly opened flowers were pierced with a needle and then infiltrated with a 1-mL syringe containing the A. tumefaciens solution. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation, petals from five different flowers per sample were cut in two and immediately flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. One sample was used for RNA extraction for gene expression analysis and the other sample was used for metabolite profiling.

# 2.2. Generation of DNA constructs

Constructs were made with the Gateway® Technology (Invitrogen). ENTRY clones containing AtMYC2, AtMYC2D105N, CrMYC2a, BIS1 and ORCA3 had been generated previously (Goossens et al., 2015; Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). For all other transcription factors used in this study, the coding sequences were amplified from C. roseus var. “Little bright eyes” cDNA with $\mathbf { Q } 5 ^ { \circledast }$ High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase (New England BioLabs®) and gene-specific oligonucleotides containing attB Gateway® recombination sites (Table S8). The fragments were gelpurified (GeneJET Gel Extraction Kit, ThermoFisher) and recombined into pDONR™221 using Gateway® BP clonase™ II enzyme mix (ThermoFisher); the resulting ENTRY clones were sequenced. $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N } ,$ , CrMYC2bD60N, $C r M Y C 2 b ^ { D 6 0 N }$ and $C r M Y C 2 c ^ { D 5 3 N }$ were constructed by overlap extension PCR using the ENTRY clones as templates. For expression in flower petals under control of the CaMV35S promoter or promoter

transactivation assays, ENTRY clones were recombined into pK7WG2D or p2GW7 (Karimi et al., 2002), respectively, using LR Clonase™ enzyme mix (ThermoFisher). The promoter fragments of GES, G8O, 8HGO, IS, IO, 7DLGT, 7DLH, LAMT, STR and BIS1 had been cloned previously (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015; Vom Endt et al., 2007). The promoter fragments (including 5′ UTRs) of SLS (1413 bp), TDC (2353 bp) and SGD (1651 bp) were amplified from C. roseus var. “Little bright eyes” genomic DNA and recombined into ENTRY vectors. ENTRY clones containing promoter fragments were recombined into pGWL7 (Karimi et al., 2002) for transient expression assays.

# 2.3. Phylogenetic analysis

Amino acid sequences of full-length proteins of C. roseus and A. thaliana were aligned using ClustalW. A neighbor-joining tree was constructed in MEGA6 (Tamura et al., 2013). Evolutionary distances were calculated using the Jones, Taylor, and Thornton (JTT) model. Bootstrap analyses were performed with 1000 bootstrap replicates. bHLH clades were defined as in Heim et al. (2003).

# 2.4. Protein interaction assays in yeast

Y2H assays were performed as described (Cuéllar Pérez et al., 2013). Bait and prey were fused to the GAL4-AD or GAL4-BD via cloning into pGAD424gate or pGBT9gate, respectively. The Saccharomyces cerevisiae PJ69-4A yeast strain was co-transformed with bait and prey using the polyethylene glycol (PEG)/lithium acetate method. Positive transformants were selected on Synthetic Defined (SD) medium lacking Leu and Trp (− 2) (Clontech). Colonies were grown overnight in liquid cultures $( - 2 )$ at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 10- or 100-fold dilutions were dropped on control medium $( - 2 )$ and selective medium lacking Leu, Trp and His $( - 3 )$ (Clontech).

# 2.5. Transient expression assays in N. tabacum protoplasts

Transactivation of C. roseus gene promoters by C. roseus TFs was assessed in transient expression assays in N. tabacum ‘Bright Yellow-2’ protoplasts as described (Vanden Bossche et al., 2013). Protoplasts were co-transfected with reporter, effector and normalizer plasmids. The reporter plasmid consists of a fusion between the promoter of interest and the Firefly Luciferase (fLUC) gene. The effector plasmid contains the TF of interest driven by the CaMV35S promoter. The normalizer plasmid contains the Renilla Luciferase (rLUC) driven by the CaMV35S promoter. fLUC and rLUC readouts were collected after overnight incubation and lysis using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega). Each assay incorporated four or eight biological repeats. Normalized values of the promoter activities were obtained by dividing the fLUC values by the corresponding rLUC values. The average of the normalized fLUC values was calculated and set out relative to the control fLUC values (i.e. with an effector plasmid carrying the GUS gene instead of a TF gene).

# 2.6. Transcript profiling by qPCR

RNA from agro-infiltrated flower petals was extracted using the RNeasy Plant Mini kit (Qiagen) including an additional DNA on-column digest with RQ1 DNase (Promega). Up to 500 ng of RNA was used as a template for reverse transcription with the iScript™ cDNA synthesis kit (Bio-Rad). qPCR was performed, on a LightCycler® 480 (Roche) using SYBR Green qPCR master Mix (Agilent) and gene-specific oligonucleotides (Table S8) in two technical replicates. The data was normalized to two reference genes, N2227 and SAND (Pollier et al., 2014) using qBase (Hellemans et al., 2007).

# 2.7. Metabolite profiling

For metabolite profiling, infiltrated petals were snap-frozen and ground in liquid nitrogen. After lyophilization, around 5 mg of dry petal tissue was weighed and extracted with 1 mL of methanol containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ caffeine as an internal standard. The samples were extracted at room temperature for 10 min and centrifuged for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $2 0 { , } 8 0 0 \times g .$ . The resulting supernatant was evaporated to dryness under vacuum, and the residue was dissolved in $8 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of water/cyclohexane (1:1, v/ v). The samples were centrifuged (10 min at $2 0 , 8 0 0 \times g )$ , and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the aqueous phase was retained for LC-MS analysis. For LC-MS, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the sample was injected in a ZORBAX RRHD Eclipse XDB-C18 column $( 2 . 1 \times 1 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathrm { , }$ ) mounted on a thermo instrument equipped with an LTQ FT Ultra and an electrospray ionization source. The following gradient was run using acidified ${ \it { 0 . 1 \% } }$ (v/v) formic acid) solvents A (water/acetonitrile (99:1, v/v)) and B (acetonitrile/water; 99:1, $\mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ ): time 0 min, $5 \%$ B; 30 min, $5 5 \%$ B; 35 min, $1 0 0 \%$ B. FT-MS spectra between $m / z ~ 1 2 0 { - } 1 4 0 0$ were recorded at a resolution of 100,000. For identification, full MS spectra were interchanged with a dependent ${ \mathbf { M } } { \mathbf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ scan event in which the most abundant ion in the previous full MS scan was fragmented, two dependent ${ \mathbf { M } } { \mathbf { S } } ^ { 3 }$ scan events in which the two most abundant daughter ions were fragmented and a dependent ${ \mathrm { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 4 }$ scan event in which the most abundant granddaughter ion was fragmented. The collision energy was set at $3 5 \%$ . Peak areas were calculated using the Progenesis QI software and normalized by petal sample mass and the peak area of caffeine. Compounds were identified based on their exact mass, fragmentation and co-elution with authentic standards. Compounds for which no authentic standards are available were tentatively identified based on their exact mass and ${ \bf { M } } { \bf { S } } ^ { n }$ fragmentation.

The production of the standards 16-hydroxytabersonine and 19- hydroxytabersonine was carried out based in yeast on the biotransformation method reported by Qu et al. (2015) with modifications. Briefly, T16H2 or T19H were cloned via Gateway recombination into the yeast vector pAG423GAL-ccdB (Alberti et al., 2007) (AddGene plasmid 14149) and co-transformed in the wild-type yeast strain PA59 (Arendt et al., 2017) (MATa; his3Δ1; leu2Δ0; ura3Δ0; lys2Δ0; trp-1) with the P450 reductase MTR1 (Miettinen et al., 2017) cloned in pAG415GAL-ccdB (Alberti et al., 2007) (AddGene plasmid 14145). Transformed colonies were selected on Synthetic Defined (SD) medium (Clontech) with -His, -Leu (Clontech) amino acid dropouts (DO). For compound production, pre-cultures were grown at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at $2 5 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ in 5 mL of liquid SD medium with -His, -Leu DO. To induce gene expression, the precultures were washed and inoculated into $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of SD Gal/Raf medium with -His, -Leu DO supplements to a starting optical density of 0.25. The cultures were incubated for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ before addition of $1 2 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ of tabersonine (dissolved in ethanol) and further incubated for another $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . The produced 16-hydroxytabersonine or 19-hydroxytabersonine were extracted from the yeast medium using ethyl acetate and analyzed by LC-FT-ICR-MS as described above.

For details on all metabolite identifications, see Supplemental Dataset S1. The PCA was performed with MetaboAnalyst 3.0 with Pareto-scaled mass spectrometry data and standard settings (http:// www.metaboanalyst.ca/) (Xia and Wishart, 2016).

# 3. Results

# 3.1. A protoplast-based screen for transcriptional regulator(s) of BIS1 yields no hits from a collection of wild-type C. roseus TFs

Positive amplification loops are a hallmark of the JA-signaling pathway (Memelink, 2009; Pauwels et al., 2009). As such, the expression of the BIS as well as the ORCA and most MIA pathway genes can be induced by JA treatment (Table S1) (Giddings et al., 2011; Liscombe et al., 2010; Miettinen et al., 2014; Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015). However, thus far we could not establish an auto-amplification loop for the BIS genes (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016). Furthermore, a direct physical interaction between the BIS TFs and the JAZ repressors is unlikely, because the BIS TFs lack a typical JID domain. Nevertheless, we mined the C. roseus transcriptomes for CrJAZ genes, cloned several of them and carried out a yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay to test for potential interaction between BIS TFs and JAZ. As recently also suggested (Patra et al., 2018), BIS TFs failed to interact with all tested JAZ proteins (Figs. S1A and S2A). This suggests that the JA elicitation of BIS expression is mediated by another TF that is repressed by JAZ proteins. Such a regulatory loop has been established for the JA-mediated induction of ORCA genes, and as a consequence the downstream epidermis-specific MIA biosynthesis genes, with MYC2 being the responsible JAZ-interacting regulatory TF (Fig. 1A) (Zhang et al., 2011; Zhou and Memelink, 2016). To investigate whether the same or a similar cascade is driving the expression of BIS genes and consequently that of the IPAP-specific iridoid pathway, we tested the transactivation of the BIS1 promoter (pBIS1) by JA-modulated, potentially JAZ-interacting C. roseus TFs in our well-established tobacco protoplast-based expression system that we previously used to discover the BIS TFs (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015; Vanden Bossche et al., 2013).

Being the most obvious candidate, CrMYC2, as well as the Arabidopsis homolog AtMYC2, both shown to be able to transactivate the ORCA3 promoter (pORCA3) (Montiel et al., 2011), were assessed. In particular AtMYC2 could transactivate pORCA3, in agreement with the previous report, but both MYC2 orthologs failed to transactivate pBIS1 (Fig. 2A), suggesting that CrMYC2 may not be directly regulating BIS1 expression.

The past decade, numerous JAZ-interacting TFs have been identified, mainly in Arabidopsis, including bHLH TFs from the same or other bHLH clades than MYC2, namely clades IIIb (the ‘ICE’ TFs), IIId (the ‘JAM’ TFs), IIIe (the ‘MYC2’ TFs) and IIIf (the ‘GL’ TFs) (Chini et al., 2016; De Geyter et al., 2012; Goossens et al., 2016; Heim et al., 2003; Kazan and Manners, 2012; Pauwels and Goossens, 2011). We therefore screened the available C. roseus transcriptomes for members of these bHLH clades using the Arabidopsis proteins as a bait and cloned them for expression in the protoplast assay. Besides homologs of well-described TFs such as Glabrous 3 (GL3), Jasmonate Associated MYC2- LIKE 1 (JAM1), and Inducer of CBP Expression 1 (ICE1), we also identified two additional C. roseus MYC2 homologs that we named CrMYC2b and CrMYC2c (Fig. S1B). The ‘original’ CrMYC2 was therefore renamed to CrMYC2a here. This nomenclature was chosen to avoid any confusion with previously described bHLH TFs from C. roseus, which are phylogenetically not closely related to CrMYC2a, despite being called CrMYC1, CrMYC3, CrMYC4 and CrMYC5 (Chatel et al., 2003; Zhang et al., 2011) (Fig. S1B). Expression of CrMYC2a, CrMYC5, CrJAM2 and CrJAM3 but not of CrMYC1, CrMYC3, CrMYC4, CrICE1a, CrICE1b and CrGL3 was JA-inducible in plants (Table S1). However, none of the clade III bHLH TFs could transactivate pBIS1 in the protoplast assay (Fig. S3A).

# 3.2. Contrary to wild-type MYC2 versions, de-repressed mutant MYC2 variants can transactivate pBIS1 and pORCA3 in tobacco protoplasts

Because protoplasting is known to trigger JA signaling (Birnbaum et al., 2003), we postulated that endogenous tobacco JAZ proteins may repress the transcriptional activity of bHLH TFs containing JID domains such as CrMYC2a, possibly leading to false negatives. To explore this hypothesis, we tested whether de-repressed MYC2 mutants could

transactivate pBIS1. Indeed, in contrast to wild-type AtMYC2, the available de-repressed AtMYC2D105N mutant (Goossens et al., 2015) was capable to transactivate pBIS1 around eightfold (Fig. 2A), suggesting it is involved in direct regulation of BIS1 expression and that its wild-type activity is limited by interaction with JAZ proteins. Accordingly, transactivation of pORCA3 by AtMYC2D105N was fivefold stronger than by wild-type AtMYC2 (Fig. 2A).

The JAZ/MYC2 regulatory complex is widely conserved among the plant kingdom (Chini et al., 2016; Wasternack and Strnad, 2018). Likewise, the negatively charged aspartate (D) at position 105 of AtMYC2 that causes the de-repression phenotype is strictly conserved among all Arabidopsis and C. roseus clade IIIe bHLH TFs and

corresponds to D126 of CrMYC2a (Fig. S1C). To assess whether the same mutation in CrMYC2a would also trigger de-repression and transactivation of MIA pathway gene promoters, we engineered a derepressed CrMYC2a. As predicted, CrMYC2aD126N could transactivate both pBIS1 and pORCA3 in BY-2 protoplasts (Fig. 2A), supporting a role for CrMYC2a as an indirect regulator of MIA biosynthesis gene expression.

To confirm that the mutation in $\mathrm { C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N } }$ affects the interaction with JAZ proteins, as it does in AtMYC2D105N, we carried out a Y2H assay (Fig. 2B; Fig. S2B). CrMYC2a and AtMYC2 both interacted with all tested CrJAZ proteins. The mutant version of CrMYC2a lost its capacity to interact with CrJAZ3 and CrJAZ8, whereas the interaction with CrJAZ1, 2, 4 and 7 was maintained. In comparison, the mutant version of AtMYC2 did no longer interact with CrJAZ1, 2, 3 and 8, whereas interaction with CrJAZ4 and CrJAZ7 was also maintained. Generally, these Y2H results are in agreement with those published in other studies and that have shown a loss of interaction of AtMYC2D105N with most Arabidopsis JAZ proteins as well as a maintained ability to interact with some of them (Frerigmann et al., 2014; Goossens et al., 2015).

Originally, CrMYC2a was discovered in a yeast-one hybrid (Y1H) screen for activators of the STR promoter (pSTR), but has so far not been shown to transactivate pSTR in any plant system (Chatel et al., 2003; Paul et al., 2017; Pré et al., 2000; Zhang et al., 2011). Here, we corroborate these Y1H data by demonstrating that both the de-repressed AtMYC2D105N and $\mathrm { C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N } }$ can transactivate pSTR in tobacco protoplasts, contrary to the wild-type versions (Fig. 2A). Together, this also supports a role for CrMYC2a as a direct regulator of MIA biosynthesis gene expression. To assess whether this applies to the whole MIA pathway, we tested transactivation of the available promoters of other MIA pathway genes. Indeed, $\mathrm { C r M Y C 2 a } ^ { \mathrm { D 1 2 6 N } }$ , but not CrMYC2a, was capable of transactivating the GES, 8HGO, IS, IO, SLS, TDC and SGD promoters (Fig. 2C).

# 3.3. Transient expression of de-repressed MYC2 variants induces expression of MIA pathway genes in C. roseus flower petals and leads to increased strictosidine accumulation

To assess the potential of the de-repressed MYC2 variants to stimulate MIA biosynthesis gene expression in planta, they were transiently overexpressed in C. roseus flower petals using Agrobacterium tumefaciens infiltration. In accordance with the tobacco protoplast assays, overexpression of de-repressed MYC2 variants resulted in a strong upregulation of MIA pathway genes in comparison to control petals transformed with a pCaMV35S::GUS construct (Fig. 3A; Table S2). Overall, overexpression of $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N }$ and $A t M Y C 2 ^ { D 1 0 5 N }$ caused similar induction patterns, both in terms of the identity of the MIA pathway genes and the fold-induction, although $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N }$ had a particularly stronger effect on the induction of the BIS and ORCA genes than AtMYC2D105N. Both de-repressed MYC2 TFs also strongly induced the expression of genes encoding the NPF iridoid and strictosidine transporters (Fig. 3A; Table S2). Unexpectedly, Tabersonine 19-Hydroxylase (T19H), Minovincinine 19-O-Acetyltransferase (MAT) and the recently published homolog Tabersonine Derivative 19-O-Acetyltransferase (TAT) (Carqueijeiro et al., 2018), normally specifically expressed in roots and involved in the biosynthesis of root-specific MIAs such as hörhammericine (Giddings et al., 2011; Laflamme et al., 2001), were pronouncedly induced by the de-repressed MYC2 TFs (Fig. 3A; Table S2). So far, no positive regulators have been described for these transporter or root-specific MIA biosynthesis genes. Notably, despite the strong upregulation of pathway genes for specific MIAs such as strictosidine, hörhammericine or ajmalicine, the expression of the vindoline pathway genes, i.e., Tabersonine 16-Hydroxylase 2 (T16H2), Tabersonine 16-O-Methyltransferase (T16OMT), Desacetoxyvindoline 4- Hydroxylase (D4H) or Deacetylvindoline O-Acetyltransferase (DAT) was not induced by the overexpression of de-repressed MYC2s (Table S2).

# 3.5. Combinatorial overexpression of CrMYC2aD126N, BIS1 and ORCA3 in flower petals has synergistic effects on MIA pathway gene induction

This and previous studies start to shape (part of) the regulatory network of MIA biosynthesis, and accredit defined roles to CrMYC2a, BIS and ORCA TFs (Fig. 1A) (Montiel et al., 2011; Van Moerkercke et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2011; Zhou and Memelink, 2016). Given the postulated or proven combinatorial action of TFs in JA signaling or the regulation of other specialized metabolic pathways in other plant species (Bemer et al., 2017; Brkljacic and Grotewold, 2017; Colinas and Goossens, 2018; De Geyter et al., 2012), we wondered whether a combination of overexpression of BIS, ORCA and de-repressed MYC2 TFs would further amplify MIA biosynthesis.

First, we assessed the effect on gene expression. Generally, overexpression of either BIS1 or ORCA3 confirmed the previously reported specificities for the MIA pathway branches. Surprisingly, based on MS fragmentation, these compounds were identified as the postulated root-specific MIAs hörhammericine (identity confirmed with an authentic standard) and 16-methoxyhörhammericine, also known as hörhammerinine (Fig. 4B and C; Table S7 and Supplemental Dataset S1) (Abraham and Farnsworth, 1969). Additional novel peaks, potentially corresponding to strictosidine derivatives (including bis(monoterpenoid) indole alkaloids such as strictosidine secologanatoside and

strictosidine secologanoside) and other MIAs, showed a similar marked increase in the triple-TF infiltrated flowers, but their identity could not unambiguously be determined (Table S7).

To further investigate the effects of the single- and triple-TF combinations on the flower metabolome, a PCA was carried out (Fig. 4D). Samples of BIS1-overexpressing flowers largely overlapped with those of the control, but all others formed distinct groups, with the triple-TF infiltrated flowers showing the most divergent distribution pattern. According to the PCA loading plot, distribution of PC1 was mainly explained by variables corresponding to the above-mentioned differentially accumulating MIAs. Conversely, PC2 seemed to be mostly influenced by compounds related to flavonoids such as the tentatively annotated rhamnetin 3-rutinoside, explaining the distribution pattern of the samples infiltrated with the ORCA3 TF.

The observation that combinatorial overexpression of TFs in flowers had no effect on the accumulation of catharanthine and vindoline, but could boost the production of certain MIAs, encouraged us to take a closer look at potential MIA intermediates and final MIA products (Fig. 1A and B). Based on MS fragmentation and standards, we identified and quantified 18 additional MIAs (Table S7 and Supplemental Dataset S1). As recently published, most intermediates between the strictosidine aglycone and tabersonine or catharanthine are unstable, hypothetical or not detectable in planta, which explains why we were not able to detect for instance deshydroxymethylstemmadenine or the critical intermediate stemmadenine (Qu et al., 2018). Nevertheless, we observed an important increase of O-acetylstemmadenine $\mathrm { . } >$ sixfold) and ‘early-branching’ MIAs such as akuammicine $( > 1 4$ -fold), perivine $\cdot >$ sevenfold) and isositsirikine $\mathrm { . } >$ fivefold) in triple-TF infiltrated flowers and to a lesser extent in flowers infiltrated with 35 S::CrMY-$C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N } ;$ , which correlated with the high induction observed for GS1, GS2 and GO (Figs. 1A and 4A; Table S7). Besides the above-mentioned 16-hydroxytabersonine, the subsequent downstream intermediates of the vindoline pathway, 16-methoxytabersonine and 3-hydroxy-16- methoxy-2,3-dihydrotabersonine, accumulated only poorly in control samples, but showed nevertheless slightly increased levels in triple-TF infiltrated flowers (Table S7). The last precursors and derivate compounds in the vindoline pathway, like deacetylvindoline, desacetoxyvindoline and demethoxyvindoline, were relatively highly abundant, but remained unchanged among all samples (Table S7). In contrast to the 16-hydroxytabersonine-derived MIAs that are normally produced in the aerial plant parts, the 19-hydroxytabersonine-derived MIAs, such as hörhammericine, are normally produced in roots (Giddings et al., 2011). Considering the dramatic increase of root-specific MIAs, we screened our data for other MIAs in this specific branch (Figs. 4C and 1B). Similar to hörhammericine, most root-specific MIAs were barely detectable in the control flowers, but displayed a massively increased accumulation in triple-TF infiltrated flowers, including 19-hydroxytabersonine (> 90-fold), 16-hydroxyhörhammericine (not detectable in control flowers), minovincinine $\mathrm { . > }$ 320-fold), vincadifformine $( > 1 7 0$ -fold) and 16-hydroxyvincadifformine (> 220-fold) (Table S7). Surprisingly, although expression of MAT and TAT was strongly increased in triple-TF infiltrated samples, we were not able to detect any previously described acetylated MIAs such as echitovenine, 19-O-acetylhörhammericine or 19-O-acetyltabersonine (Carqueijeiro et al., 2018; Giddings et al., 2011; Laflamme et al., 2001). Instead we detected an over 8,300-fold increased accumulation of an acetylated compound in triple-TF infiltrated samples that we tentatively identified as 16-hydroxy-19-O-acetyltabersonine (Table S7). This acetylated MIA, at the detection limit in all single-TF infiltrated samples, is most likely the result of a concerted reaction involving T16H and the highly expressed T19H and TAT in a yet undiscovered order. 16-methoxy-19-O-acetyltabersonine, a similarly acetylated MIA could be produced in yeast in a previous study, confirming that C-16 hydroxylation and C-19 hydroxylation/acetylation can occur on the same molecule (Carqueijeiro et al., 2018).

Taken together, metabolite profiling reveals that combinatorial

overexpression of BIS1, ORCA3 and $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N }$ has a tremendous effect on the general MIA profile, an effect which cannot be obtained with single TFs, and correlating with the MIA pathway gene induction that they trigger together.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. The blueprint of the regulatory network of MIA biosynthesis involves numerous TFs and reflects the complexity of that of the MIA pathway itself

The availability of a sequenced genome and numerous transcriptomes has been a strong incentive for the C. roseus research community (Góngora-Castillo et al., 2012; Kellner et al., 2015; Van Moerkercke et al., 2013). During the last five years, the whole MIA pathway up to the formation of strictosidine has been resolved, as well as several downstream pathway branches, and previously undescribed TFs and transporters have been characterized. It has become evident that the complexity of the regulatory network is matching that of the biosynthetic pathway itself. For instance, although most regulators have been found to be JA-inducible, they exert cell-specific effects, as exemplified by the BIS TFs that exclusively regulate the IPAP-specific part of the pathway (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016, 2015) and the ORCA TFs that were discovered as regulators of epidermis-specific genes (Menke et al., 1999; Paul et al., 2017; van der Fits and Memelink, 2000).

Recently, we have developed a transient expression platform in C. roseus flower petals using A. tumefaciens infiltration (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016), which we have further optimized for this study and which offers a versatile and complementary platform to the others already existing for C. roseus, including virus-induced gene silencing in shoots and stable transformation of whole plants, hairy roots and suspension cells (Hughes et al., 2002; Liscombe and O'Connor, 2011; van der Fits and Memelink, 1997; Wang et al., 2012). Contrary to the latter transformation platforms, our flower-based platform, in combination with medium-throughput transient expression assays in tobacco protoplasts, particularly allows rapid overexpression and co-expression of numerous genes, such as those encoding TFs and other regulators. This enables to map the architecture of the MIA regulatory network in detail and, simultaneously, to assay combinatorial designs of wild-type and engineered TF cassettes for metabolic engineering programs. Indeed, here we provide a broad view on the hierarchy, range, and cell type- and pathway branch-specificity of and within a specific JA-modulated regulatory module that involves CrMYC2a, BIS and ORCA TFs. This view is visualized in a simplified model (Fig. 5). Elements of the network that have been exposed include: (i) the first direct regulator of SGD expression, namely CrMYC2a, which is particularly valuable because SGD represents the gateway towards all MIAs, (ii) the TFs regulating directly and indirectly the expression of known (seco)iridoid MIA transporters, which is crucial given the spatial distribution of the MIA pathway across cell types and compartments, (iii) the capacity of a combinatorial TF cassette to induce a genuine MIA pathway, thereby overruling previously anticipated organ-specific boundaries such as the unexpected induction of the postulated root-specific T19H, MAT and TAT genes, (iv) the existence of amplification loops or TF cascades, e.g. positioning CrMYC2a as a strong and direct inducer of BIS and ORCA expression, and (v) the existence of negative feedback loops as evidenced by the fact that an engineered, de-repressed CrMYC2a variant needs to be employed to enable its activity in the absence of the JA signal.

Given that more TFs are known to be implicated in the regulation of MIA pathway genes, such as WRKY1, BPF1, the ZCTs, and the very recently identified bHLH factor Repressor of MYC2 Targets 1 (RMT1) (Patra et al., 2018; Pauw et al., 2004; Suttipanta et al., 2011; van der Fits et al., 2000), the view presented here is not comprehensive yet. Likewise, likely many more regulatory TFs need to be discovered still, as exemplified by the fact that expression of the catharanthine (HL1) and vindoline pathway genes is not controlled by the JA-responsive

CrMYC2/BIS/ORCA module. The transient C. roseus flower transformation platform can undoubtedly contribute to the further mapping of the MIA regulatory circuits.

# 4.2. A combinatorial cassette of one engineered and two wild-type TFs boosts MIA production in C. roseus flowers

Increased induction of pathway gene expression does not necessarily lead to an increased flux through the pathway or an increased accumulation of the desired metabolites. This is particularly applicable to the MIA pathway in C. roseus with its numerous branches and high degree of cellular and organellar compartmentalization. This bottleneck is also reflected in our study. For instance, overexpression of BIS1 or CrMYC2a in C. roseus flower petals had no effect on the level of the 30 flower MIAs identified here. Overexpression of ORCA3 and $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N }$ resulted in a statistically significant increase in the levels of six and 14 of these MIAs, respectively. However, a MIA pathwayencompassing effect, leading to a dramatic increase in the levels of 23 MIAs, was only obtained by the triple-TF combination, i.e. when wildtype BIS1 and ORCA3, and the de-repressed $C r M Y C 2 a ^ { D 1 2 6 N }$ were concomitantly transiently overexpressed. This demonstrates that an intelligent design of combinatorial TF cassettes allows to create superior engineering tools, even encompassing transcriptional regulation across multiple cell types in a single engineering event.

Surprisingly, among the most abundant MIAs quantified in the triple-TF infiltrated flowers, we observed particularly high levels of tabersonine-derived 16-hydroxytabersonine and the root-specific metabolites hörhammericine and 16-methoxyhörhammericine, whereas tabersonine itself was undetectable. The increased accumulation of hörhammericine and 16-methoxyhörhammericine could be explained by the highly induced expression levels of T19H in triple-TF infiltrated flowers. Although the T16H1 and T16H2 genes encoding the enzymes catalyzing the C-16 hydroxylation of tabersonine and hörhammericine were not upregulated by the triple-TF overexpression, the relatively high accumulation of the C-16 hydroxylated MIAs could be explained by the naturally high expression level of T16H1 in flowers (Table S1; Table S5) (Besseau et al., 2013; Levac et al., 2008). The fact that an increased 16-hydroxytabersonine accumulation could be observed but was not prolonged downstream, is likely due to the low and non-induced expression of the subsequent vindoline pathway genes, including 16OMT, T3O, T3R, NMT, D4H and DAT. Contrariwise, the conversion of 16-hydroxyhörhammericine to 16-methoxyhörhammericine was not blocked, because this reaction may be catalyzed by a different 16OMT, as already suggested previously (Levac et al., 2008), the corresponding gene of which may be possibly induced in the triple-TF infiltrated flowers. Importantly, the fact that none of the overexpressed TFs or their combinations could induce the catharanthine and vindoline pathway genes points to the existence of yet unknown additional regulators. Identification of these regulators would likely enable to compose the ‘ultimate’ combinatorial TF cassette to engineer vinblastine/ vincristine production in C. roseus organs and thus seems a prime target for future gene discovery programs.

# 4.3. ‘Super-MYC2s’, potent elements of combinatorial TF cassettes for plant metabolic engineering?

Previously, we have shown that the point mutation initially discovered in AtMYC3 in an A. thaliana EMS mutant is reproducible in paralogs within the species, such as AtMYC2, to create a de-repressed TF state because of the loss of the capacity to interact with the JAZ repressors (Goossens et al., 2015; Smolen et al., 2002). Here, we show that this de-repression effect is also translatable to orthologs in other species, such as CrMYC2a in C. roseus, thereby creating a TF that is capable of boosting MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus flowers. Hence, these ‘super-MYC2s’ represent a potent generic tool to boost specialized metabolite pathways in species across the plant kingdom, as postulated

before (Goossens et al., 2015). It is plausible to assume that our triple-TF cassette would work well in other MIA-producing plants, given the conservation of the pathway organization, to stimulate the production of valuable compounds such as reserpine in Rauwolfia serpentina, camptothecin in Camptotheca acuminata or quinine in Cinchona officinalis (Aerts et al., 1994; Deepthi and Satheeshkumar, 2017; Gundlach et al., 1992; Song and Byun, 1998). However, because MYC2 is part of the strongly conserved module of the JA-signaling complex and because it has been implicated in the regulation of the biosynthesis of many specialized metabolites in other species (De Geyter et al., 2012; Goossens et al., 2017; Kazan and Manners, 2013; Wasternack and Strnad, 2018), the applicability of this strategy will undoubtedly go beyond the engineering of the MIA pathway. A scientifically interesting, as well as societally valuable example is artemisinin production in the glandular trichomes of Artemisia annua. Indeed, a role for AaMYC2 as a positive regulator of artemisinin biosynthesis and its value in the engineering of A. annua for increased artemisinin production have already been recognized (Shen et al., 2016). It would be exciting to design a combinatorial cassette composed of a de-repressed AaMYC2 and one or more of the other artemisinin pathway regulators that have been identified recently (Lv et al., 2017) and assess their potential to boost artemisinin production to unprecedented levels in A. annua plants. However, altering MYC2 and JA signaling may modulate plant growth processes and moreover lead to a constant overaccumulation of defense compounds potentially toxic for the plant itself. Stable overexpression of de-repressed MYC2 may thus result in deleterious effects on plant growth and development, which may be circumvented by inducible promoter systems (Goossens et al., 2016, 2015).
