# ABSTRACT

High-temperature (HT) stress causes male sterility in crops, thus decreasing yields. To explore the possible contribution of histone modifications to male fertility under HT conditions, we defined the histone methylation landscape for the marks histone H3 lysine 27 trimethylation (H3K27me3) and histone H3 lysine 4 trimethylation (H3K4me3) by chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP-seq) in two differing upland cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) varieties. We observed a global disruption in H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modifications, especially H3K27me3, in cotton anthers subjected to HT. HT affected the bivalent H3K4me3–H3K27me3 modification more than either monovalent modification. We determined that removal of H3K27me3 at the promoters of jasmonate-related genes increased their expression, maintaining male fertility under HT in the HT-tolerant variety at the anther dehiscence stage. Modulating jasmonate homeostasis or signaling resulted in an anther indehiscence phenotype under HT. Chemical suppression of H3K27me3 deposition increased jasmonic acid contents and maintained male fertility under HT. In summary, our study provides new insights into the regulation of male fertility by histone modifications under HT and suggests a potential strategy for improving cotton HT tolerance.

# INTRODUCTION

Climate change is causing the more frequent occurrence of extreme weather events, including spells of unseasonably high temperature (HT) (Hao et al., 2013). HT stress has a severe negative effect on the growth and yield of crops (Teskey et al., 2015; Ortiz-Bobea et al., 2019). For example, rice (Oryza sativa) yields drop by $10 \%$ for every $\mathsf { 1 } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ increase in average growing temperature (Peng et al., 2004); this drop is mainly due to effects on reproductive development (Sage et al., 2015). Male reproductive organs are especially sensitive to HT stress, which produces abnormal anthers that cannot open (indehiscent phenotype), as well as inviable pollen grains with defects in pollen wall architecture (Hedhly et al., 2009; Zinn et al., 2010; Djanaguiraman et al., 2018). Cotton (Gossypium spp.) is one of the most economically important crops in the world (Wang et al., 2017), and upland cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) is widely cultivated for its natural and spinnable fiber (Wendel and Cronn, 

2003). In China, HT generally occurs in July and August and can involve peak temperatures above $\mathtt { 3 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ or close to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for weeks at a time. Cotton usually flowers during this period, and this HT stress adversely affects cotton anther development, preventing pollen release and thus lowering seed set and fiber yield (Min et al., 2014; Ma et al., 2018). To identify biotechnological, genetic, or chemical solutions to these yield losses, we must understand in detail how cotton male reproductive organs respond to HT stress. 

In epigenetic regulation, DNA methylation, histone modification, noncoding RNAs, and other mechanisms confer heritable changes in gene expression without changes in intrinsic DNA

sequence (Allis and Jenuwein, 2016). Trimethylation of lysine 4 on histone H3 (H3K4me3) and trimethylation of lysine 27 on histone H3 (H3K27me3) are two common and well-studied histone modifications in plants (Deal and Henikoff, 2011; Liu et al., 2014; Qian et al., 2018). Several studies have suggested that histone methylation participates in the transcriptional response to temperature changes (He et al., 2021). For example, memory of HT stress is associated with the accumulation of H3K4me2 and H3K4me3 at specific loci (Lamke et al., 2016). HT conditions promote hypocotyl elongation by increasing auxin biosynthesis and translocation in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) (Gray et al., 1998; He et al., 2021), and H3K4me2 and H3K4me3 levels are critical for the expression of auxin biosynthesis and response genes (Lee et al., 2014; Huai et al., 2018). The Jumonji-C domain–containing protein JMJ30 binds directly to chromatin at the flowering repressor gene FLOWERING LOCUS C (FLC) and removes the repressive mark H3K27me3, preventing precocious flowering caused by HT exposure (Gan et al., 2014; He et al., 2021). Although there is some evidence that histone modifications change in response to HT stress, little is known about how histone modifications regulate downstream events, especially in male organs. 

Plant hormones such as jasmonic acid (JA) shape plant development and are involved in responses to biotic and abiotic stresses such as HT stress (Wasternack and Song, 2017; Griffiths, 2020). Disruption of JA biosynthesis or signaling often leads to male sterility in plants (Park et al., 2002; Song et al., 2011). For example, mutants in allene oxide synthase (AOS), an enzyme that catalyzes the dehydration of hydroperoxide to an unstable allene oxide in the JA biosynthetic pathway, showed severe male sterility due to defects in anther and pollen development in Arabidopsis (Park et al., 2002). More recently, JA has been shown to participate in HT stress in different plant species (Chen et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2020; Khan et al., 2023), with lower JA content under HT conditions generally weakening plant heat tolerance and appropriate concentrations of exogenous methyl jasmonate (MeJA) effectively improving plant heat tolerance (Clarke et al., 2009; Ahmad et al., 2016; Khan et al., 2020). However, most studies on JA responses to HT stress lack in-depth analysis of the underlying molecular mechanisms and typically focus on plant vegetative organs, with very little research on reproductive organs. Our previous work (Min et al., 2014; Khan et al., 2020, 2023) showed that HT causes abnormal expression of JA-related genes in cotton anthers and that mutations in these JA-related genes result in male sterility in response to HT, suggesting that HT affects JA metabolism in cotton male organs. However, how JA modulates anther development in response to HT is not well understood. 

We previously detected changes in the expression levels of several histone genes in cotton anthers in response to HT (Min et al., 2014), but how histone modification is linked to male sterility under HT remains largely unexplored. Here, we used the HT-tolerant cotton line 84021 and the HT-sensitive cotton line H05 to uncover the role of histone methylation in anthers in response to HT stress. We established the H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 landscape in cotton anthers under normal conditions and HT conditions, revealing that H3K27me3 plays a more important role than H3K4me3 in determining the effect of HT stress on cotton anther development. Moreover, HT affected bivalent

# RESULTS

# Genome-wide landscape of H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modification in cotton anthers under HT

We previously identified two cotton lines, 84021 (HT tolerant) and H05 (HT sensitive), that exhibit different sensitivity to HT. Under HT conditions, 84021 shows normal anther dehiscence and produces fertile pollen, whereas H05 anthers remain indehiscent and bear inviable pollen (Figure 1A). We conducted chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by deep sequencing (ChIP-seq) using antibodies against the histone marks H3K4me3 and H3K27me3, with anthers from lines 84021 and H05 collected at the tapetum degradation stage (TDS) and anther dehiscence stage (ADS) under normal temperature (NT) and HT conditions (Supplemental Table 1). The high correlations (over 0.9 on average) between replicates indicated their reproducibility (Supplemental Figure 1). At the chromosome scale, H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modifications were enriched at euchromatic regions across the cotton genome (Figure 1B), consistent with a previous study (He et al., 2010). 

We used MACS2 software to call peaks for each histone modification in each sample, using input DNA as a negative control (Zhang et al., 2008). The number of H3K4me3 peaks declined for 84021 and H05 under HT compared with NT at the TDS and ADS (Figure 1C). Notably, the pattern for H3K27me3 peaks differed between H05 and 84021, with a number of peaks decreasing in 84021 in HT relative to NT, but increasing in H05 in HT relative to NT, at the TDS and ADS (Figure 1C). 

Because cotton is allotetraploid, we investigated the distribution of the two histone marks in the A subgenome (At) and D subgenome (Dt). Strikingly, the two histone marks were more abundant along the Dt subgenome than the At subgenome regardless of genotype, condition, or anther developmental stage (Supplemental Figures 2 and 3). However, changes in H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 over the promoter regions of genes from the At or Dt subgenome were generally similar in all samples under HT, indicating that changes in H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 may affect the At and Dt subgenomes equally (Supplemental Figure 3). We concluded that H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 levels respond to HT exposure, with the cotton lines 84021 and H05 showing similar H3K4me3 profiles but different H3K27me3 profiles. This difference prompted us to speculate that the H3K27me3 mark may play a more prominent role than the H3K4me3 mark in the response of cotton anthers to HT.

# Changes in H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modulate gene expression under HT

In Arabidopsis, H3K4me3 is enriched over proximal promoter regions and transcription start sites (TSSs) (Sequeira-Mendes 

et al., 2014), whereas H3K27me3 is enriched throughout the gene body, with a slightly higher abundance near the TSS (Xiao et al., 2016). We catalogued the positions of H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 peaks separately and observed that H3K4me3 was mainly distributed over promoter regions (at least $73 \%$ of all peaks per sample), defined as the regions spanning from 1 kb upstream to 1 kb downstream of the TSSs, whereas H3K27me3 was distributed mainly over promoters (at least $4 3 \%$ in each sample) and intergenic regions (at least $3 5 \%$ in each sample) (Supplemental Figure 4; Supplemental Table 3), in agreement with a previous report in cotton (Zhang et al., 2021a). 

We next performed a comparative transcriptome analysis, which identified differentially expressed genes (DEGs) at the TDS and ADS in 84021 and H05 under HT compared with NT conditions (Supplemental Figure 5A). We detected more downregulated genes in 84021 under HT relative to NT at both stages and in H05 at the TDS, with more upregulated genes in H05 at the ADS (Supplemental Figure 5A). We detected H3K4me3 peaks over at least $5 5 \%$ (or 38 485) of all promoters and H3K27me3 over $12 \%$ (or 8499) of promoters in each sample (Supplemental Table 5). The trend in the number of promoters that experienced changes in H3K4me3 marks or H3K27me3 marks was consistent with the genome-wide H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 changes seen under HT (Figure 2A). The number of promoters that lost the H3K4me3 modification under HT was more than the number of promoters that gained the H3K4me3 modification in both H05 and 84021 (Figure 2A). Most promoters harboring an H3K27me3 mark in 84021 under NT lost it under HT at the TDS and ADS, with very few promoters gaining an H3K27me3 mark under HT. We observed a different pattern with H05: slightly more promoters lost the H3K27me3 mark at the TDS than gained this histone mark, and three times as many promoters gained the H3K27me3 mark than lost it at the ADS under HT compared with NT conditions (Figure 2A). Looking at the abundance of H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 over promoter regions (Figure 2B) revealed no substantial changes in the relative abundance of H3K4me3 in either cotton line at the TDS, whereas relative H3K4me3 abundance decreased at the ADS under HT in 84021, but not H05, downstream of the TSS (Figure 2B). The relative abundance of H3K27me3 also decreased at the TDS and ADS in 84021, downstream of the TSS (Figure 2B), whereas it decreased at the TDS and increased at the ADS in H05 under HT (Figure 2B). 

H3K4me3 is typically seen as a mark of actively transcribed genes, whereas H3K27me3 tends to be associated with repression of gene expression (You et al., 2017). Consistent with these observations, we found a significant positive association between the presence of H3K4me3 in promoter regions and expression levels in cotton anthers (Figure 2C and 2D). By contrast, H3K27me3 was prevalent in the promoters of repressed genes (Figure 2C and 2D). Furthermore, we observed a significant correlation between promoters with changes in their H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 modification and the corresponding DEGs under HT in each sample (Figures 2E and 2G; Supplemental Figure 6). For example, $23 \%$ (or 1387) of

A similar analysis for H3K27me3 revealed that $23 \%$ (or 880) of all promoters that showed a change in their H3K27me3 modification belonged to DEGs in 84021 at the ADS under HT relative to NT, $6 3 \%$ (or 553) of which showed a change in H3K27me3 modification opposite to that in gene expression (Figure 2G). We performed a similar analysis across all samples (Figure 2F and 2H; Supplemental Figure 6). Again, changes in H3K4me3 tended to follow those in gene expression of the corresponding DEGs across all samples (Figure 2F; Supplemental Figure 6A–6C). By contrast, changes in H3K27me3 levels showed the opposite pattern: an increase in H3K27me3 levels was more often associated with lower expression of the DEGs than higher expression at the ADS (Figure 2H; Supplemental Figure 6D–6F). Our findings suggest a general pattern of H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 dynamics and gene expression under HT conditions. 

To investigate which pathways were individually regulated by the H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 marks under HT, we counted the number of promoters with significant changes in their H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 levels under HT in each sample. Only a few promoters (an average of $1 5 \%$ or $1 1 \%$ of all modified promoters) showed significant changes in H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 level, respectively (Supplemental Figure 7). 

To further investigate the functions of genes with differentially modified promoters (DMPs) by H3K4me3 or H3K27me3, we performed a GO analysis on each set of DMPs. We detected no pathway clearly related to fertility control for the genes associated with H3K4me3-enriched DMPs in H05 or 84021 at the TDS or ADS. Only genes associated with upregulated H3K4me3 DMPs at the ADS in H05 were enriched in pathways related to secondary metabolites, such as alpha-amino acid metabolic process (GO:1901605) and chorismate biosynthetic process (GO:0009423) (Figure 3A; Supplemental Table 6). 

Notably, genes associated with upregulated H3K27me3 DMPs at the ADS in H05 showed enrichment in the JA-mediated signaling pathway (GO:0009867), anther development (GO:0009867), and response to sucrose (GO:0009744) (Figure 3B; Supplemental Table 7). Therefore, we speculate that H3K4me3 plays a weaker role than H3K27me3 in HT-induced male sterility, on the basis of GO results obtained from genes associated with H3K27me3-related DMPs in the HT-sensitive cotton line H05.

# Bivalent H3K4me3–H3K27me3 marks show more dramatic changes under HT

AlthoughH3K27me3profiles differedbetween 84021 and H05 at the ADS under HT (Figures 1C and 2B), H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modifications often coexist on the same nucleosomes to regulate gene expression and chromatin state; nucleosomes carrying both marks are known to have bivalent histone marks (Zeng et al., 2019). To explore whether H3K27me3 acts alone or as a pair with H3K4me3 in a bivalent histone mark to affect anther development in response to HT, we investigated their relative representation in cotton anthers by dividing the genome into four groups: H3K4me3-only regions, H3K27me3-only regions, H3K4me3– H3K27me3 bivalent regions, and unmarked regions (Supplemental Figure 8A). H3K27me3-only and bivalent regions occurred mainly at promoters (Supplemental Figure 8B), with most H3K27me3 marks mapping to bivalent regions (Supplemental Figure 9). For example, $7 1 . 7 \%$ (9147 out of 12 759) of promoters harboring the H3K27me3 mark and $1 9 . 7 \%$ (9147 out of 46 132) of promoters carrying the H3K4me3 mark were in a bivalent state at the ADS in H05 under NT (Figure 3C). H3K27me3-only and bivalent marks repressed gene expression in cotton anthers (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figure 10), similar to results from mammals (Bernstein et al., 2006). Genes whose promoters were marked by H3K27me3 alone appeared to be more strongly repressed than those with a bivalent mark on their promoters, suggesting a stronger effect of H3K27me3 (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figure 10). Interestingly, normalized H3K4me3 abundance was significantly lower over bivalent promoters than that in monovalent H3K4me3- marked promoters (Figure 3E; Supplemental Figure 11A), whereas H3K27me3 abundance in bivalent promoters was significantly higher than that in monovalent H3K27me3-marked promoters (Figure 3F; Supplemental Figure 11B). 

To further investigate the changes in bivalent marks under HT conditions, we analyzed promoters with monovalent or bivalent marks. Taking the ADS of H05 in which H3K4me3 levels decreased and H3K27me3 levels increased under HT (Figures 1C and 2A) as an example, the H3K4me3 levels over bivalent promoters decreased more dramatically under HT than in monovalent H3K4me3-marked promoters, whereas the H3K27me3 levels over bivalent promoters increased more significantly than in monovalent H3K4me3-marked promoters under HT (Figure 3G and 3H). We obtained similar results for most other samples (Supplemental Figure 12). These results indicate that regions harboring bivalent marks change more than those with monovalent marks under HT. 

We looked more closely at promoters with bivalent marks in all samples. Promoters with loss of bivalent status mostly lost the 

H3K4me3 mark at the ADS but mostly lost the H3K27me3 mark at the TDS in H05; in 84021, promoters mostly lost the H3K27me3 mark at both stages. Conversely, promoters that gained bivalent status mainly gained H3K4me3 marks at the TDS in H05 and the ADS in 84021, whereas 84021 at the TDS and H05 at the ADS gained H3K27me3 marks (Figure 3I). We observed that most of these genes were repressed in H05 under HT compared with those in 84021, in agreement with the earlier observation that bivalent and H3K27me3-only marks were associated with repression of gene expression (Supplemental Figure 13A). We performed a GO analysis of genes with bivalent differential modifications in their promoters (DMPs). Surprisingly, bivalent DMPs at the ADS in H05 shared some of the same enriched pathways as H3K27me3-marked DMPs, including JA-mediated signaling pathway (GO:0009867), response to sucrose (GO:0009744), and anther development (GO:0009867) (Figure 3J; Supplemental Table 8). These results indicate that changes in H3K27me3 status are the main driver of anther development at the ADS under HT in H05 (Supplemental Figures 13B–13D), further illustrating the importance of H3K27me3 marks in the HT response. 

# Removal of H3K27me3 increases JA content and maintains male fertility under HT

The JA pathway has been shown to respond to HT stress in cotton anthers (Khan et al., 2020), but how JA responds to HT is not yet clear. At the ADS in H05 under HT, GO analysis revealed enrichment of genes associated with JA-related pathways among genes whose promoters showed significantly lower H3K27me3 levels (Figure 3B and 3J). Thus, we speculated that JA may participate in the response to HT through H3K27me3. To test this hypothesis, we retrieved all JA biosynthesis and signaling genes with H3K27me3 modification at the ADS stage and assessed their H3K27me3 levels under NT and HT conditions. We determined that H3K27me3 levels in genes associated with JA biosynthesis and signal transduction generally decreased in 84021 under HT but increased in H05 at the ADS stage (Figure 4A and 4C; Supplemental Table 9). In particular, H3K27me3 levels of JA biosynthesis genes dropped sharply in 84021 under HT (Figure 4A). We then focused on expressed genes by removing any gene expressed at <1 FPKM in all samples. For the remaining expressed genes, expression and H3K27me3 levels showed opposite patterns: in 84021, genes with lower H3K27me3 levels under HT were more highly expressed than under NT, whereas genes in H05 with stronger H3K27me3 signals under HT had lower expression levels than under NT (Figure 4B and 4D; Supplemental Table 9). This result was consistent with a role for H3K27me3 as a repressive mark.

# Suppression of H3K27me3 deposition restores male fertility under HT

Because H3K27me3 levels decreased in the HT-tolerant line 84021 but increased in the HT-sensitive line H05 under HT (Figure 1C), we sprayed the histone methyltransferase inhibitor GSK343 onto H05 flower buds (tetrad stage [TS] and TDS) to decrease H3K27me3 levels (Figure 6A). Seven days later, we performed ChIP-seq using an anti-H3K27me3 antibody and anthers at the ADS in H05 under HT (Supplemental Figure 14A). We detected a significant decrease in H3K27me3 levels in anthers sprayed with the H3K27me3 inhibitor compared with controls sprayed with water only (Figure 6A and 6B). Compared with mock-treated anthers, H05 anthers sprayed with $5 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ GSK343 had higher anther dehiscence rates and pollen vigor under HT (Figure 6D). Analysis of anther cross sections confirmed the rescue of the anther indehiscence and wrinkled pollen appearance of H05 under HT after treatment with GSK343 (Figure 6E). We collected anthers at the ADS from H05 that had been mock treated or treated with GSK343 and subjected to HT for use in RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) (Supplemental Figure 14B; Supplemental Table 10). 

We identified 1015 DEGs between these two sets of samples, with 400 upregulated genes and 615 downregulated genes in GSK343-treated samples (Figure 6C). These DEGs were enriched in two major GO terms: hormone catabolic process (GO:0042447) and JA metabolic process (GO:0009694) (Figure 6F). We further investigated H3K27me3 levels and expression levels of JA biosynthesis and JA response genes. Compared with mock-treated anthers, GSK343-treated anthers had significantly lower H3K27me3 levels in JA biosynthesis and JA response genes; moreover, JA biosynthesis genes had significantly higher expression than in control samples, whereas JA response genes had significantly lower expression (Figure 6G). Interestingly, H3K27me3 levels of GhAOS and GhAOC2 decreased in treated anthers, whereas expression of these genes increased significantly (Figure 6H–6J). In addition, the JA content was significantly higher in anthers sprayed with GSK343 than in mock-treated anthers (Figure 6K). These results suggest that lower H3K27me3 deposition increased the JA content and restored the male fertility of cotton anthers from line H05 under HT. 

# DISCUSSION

HT stress results in male sterility and reduces yields in rice, wheat (Triticum aestivum), maize (Zea mays), and cotton (Draeger and Moore, 2017; Kan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Sprague et al., 2022). Our previous transcriptome analysis showed the involvement of genes related to glucose metabolism and auxin biosynthesis and signaling in cotton anther responses to HT stress (Min et al., 2014). Changes in the status of DNA methylation and microRNA levels may also regulate male fertility under HT by affecting sugar metabolism, reactive oxygen species homeostasis, and auxin signaling in the early developmental stages, suggesting that epigenetic regulation contributes to anther development under HT (Ding et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2018). However, the specific histone modifications and their regulatory mechanisms in cotton anthers under HT have been unclear until now. 

We performed ChIP-seq analysis on the contrasting cotton lines 84021 (HT tolerant) and H05 (HT sensitive) under NT and HT conditions at two anther developmental stages. This enabled us to systematically analyze the roles of H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 at the TDS and ADS in response to HT, revealing two intriguing patterns. First, H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 were mainly distributed over euchromatin (Figure 1B), although H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 were asymmetrically distributed between the At and Dt subgenomes of cotton, with significantly higher modification levels in Dt than in At (Supplemental Figure 2). The asymmetric distribution of H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 in cotton may modulate the expression of homoeologous genes, as previously reported in other polyploid plants (Shi et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2021a; 2021b). Second, H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 levels and deposition patterns changed substantially under HT; however, only H3K27me3 showed different patterns between 84021 and H05, with lower levels in 84021 but higher levels in H05 (Figures 1C, 2A, and 2B). Histone writers play an important role when plants are subjected to abiotic stress (Wang et al., 2021; Yamaguchi et al., 2021). H3K27me3 demethylases with a JMJ domain are important for heat acclimation of Arabidopsis; jmj mutants do not cope as well as wild-type plants with heat stress, even after acclimation, owing to their inability to establish a memory of heat exposure (Yamaguchi et al., 2021). The H3K9 demethylase JMJ27 positively regulates drought stress responses through its histone demethylase activity. The jmj27 mutant with low JMJ27 transcript levels is hypersensitive to drought (Wang et al., 2021). Thus, we speculate that the changes in H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 levels may have been caused by the observed changes in expression of 

# Plant Communications

corresponding methylases and demethylases in H05 and 84021 under HT, although the specific molecular mechanism remains to be further explored. 

H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 often appear together in a so-called bivalent state (Sequeira-Mendes et al., 2014; You et al., 2017). H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent regions were first described in mouse embryonic stem cells (ESCs) (Azuara et al., 2006; Bernstein et al., 2006). In ESCs, H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent domains appear at the promoters of lineage-specific genes (Bernstein et al., 2006; Voigt et al., 2013), but bivalent marks are lost after ESC differentiation (Bernstein et al., 2006). The expression of some lineage-specific genes that lose H3K27me3 and retain H3K4me3 is activated, whereas that of others that lose H3K4me3 and retain H3K27me3 is repressed (Voigt et al., 2013). In ESCs, the presence of H3K4me3 in silenced genes marked with H3K4me3–H3K27me3 is often thought to facilitate their future transcriptional activation by assisting with the recruitment of RNA polymerase II, whereas H3K27me3 is thought to repress gene expression by preventing RNA polymerase from accumulating at promoters (Vastenhouw and Schier, 2012). Therefore, the bivalent domains are thought to keep genes silenced, but with the potential for later activation. We detected multiple H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent marks over promoters in cotton anthers (Figure 3C; Supplemental Figure 9), and most genes with the bivalent mark were transcriptionally repressed (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figure 10), indicating that the presence and potential function of the H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent mark is conserved in plants and animals. 

We observed that the level of bivalent marks changed more than that of monovalent H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 marks under HT (Figure 3G and 3H; Supplemental Figure 12), similar to previous results concerning bivalent marks and the cold stress response (Zeng et al., 2019); however, a clear mechanism remains to be uncovered. We speculate that HT may increase chromatin accessibility in cotton anthers, with H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent marks representing a unique chromatin environment that becomes more open than monovalent or unmarked regions under HT. Genes associated with H3K4me3–H3K27me3 bivalent DMPs, whose levels changed significantly under HT, showed clear differences between 84021 and H05, especially at the ADS (Figure 3I). Among the promoters that lost their bivalent marks at the ADS under HT, more promoters lost H3K4me3 but retained H3K27me3 in H05 than in 84021. By contrast, among the promoters that reached bivalent status at the ADS under HT, more promoters gained H3K27me3 in H05 than in 84021 (Figure 3I). This result indicates that 84021 is in a more transcriptionally active state than H05 under HT, which may explain why 84021 has enhanced thermotolerance. 

JA plays a very important role in the growth and development of plants and in abiotic stress responses (Song et al., 2011; Sharma and Laxmi, 2015; Khan et al., 2020). We previously showed that JA signaling pathways were affected by HT in cotton, but how the JA pathway was regulated under HT was unclear (Khan et al., 2020). Several studies have revealed that a few genes involved in the biosynthesis, transport, or signaling of plant hormones are marked with H3K27me3 (Lafos et al., 2011; Lu et al., 2011; Ramirez-Prado et al., 2019). JAZ proteins directly interact with several chromatin-associated Polycomb proteins

to mediate H3K27me3 deposition at JA-responsive genes to regulate anther development (Li et al., 2021). Collectively, the results from these earlier studies suggest a strong link between phytohormone signaling and histone methylation. Our results showed that genes that gained an H3K27me3 mark were enriched in JA-related genes at the ADS in H05 under HT (Figure 3B). Some of these genes retained their H3K4me3– H3K27me3 bivalent marks under HT (Figure 3J). Further analysis showed that H3K27me3 levels in genes related to JA biosynthesis and response at the ADS were substantially lower in 84021 than in H05 under HT (Figure 4A and 4C). Intriguingly, expression of the corresponding genes was substantially higher in 84021 than in H05 (Figure 4B and 4D). For instance, the changes in H3K27me3 levels and GhJAZ1 (Ghir_A08G025920) expression differed between H05 and 84021 under HT (Supplemental Figure 13D). H3K27me3 levels at the GhJAZ1 locus were higher in H05 than in 84021, whereas GhJAZ1 expression level was lower in H05 than in 84021 under HT (Supplemental Figure 13D), suggesting that the attenuation of JA signal transduction under HT may cause male sterility. However, JAZ1 RNAi lines (JAZ1-Ri#1 and JAZ1-Ri#2) dehisced normally under NT (Supplemental Figure 15). We speculate that genes homologous to JAZ1 may compensate for the loss of GhJAZ1. Measurements of JA content also showed that JA homeostasis was affected in H05 under HT (Figure 4E). External application of MeJA before exposure to HT restored male fertility (Figure 4F). Furthermore, a moderate decrease in H3K27me3 levels of H05 induced by chemical inhibitor application increased the expression of JA biosynthesis genes, increasing JA content and enhancing thermotolerance of the HT-sensitive line H05 (Figure 6). 

In this study, we describe the first global profile of the H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 landscape in cotton anthers under HT. We showed that H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 marks respond to HT stress, with H3K27me3 playing a more pronounced role than H3K4me3 in HT-induced male sterility. Many H3K27me3 marks in cotton anthers were present in a bivalent state with H3K4me3. Removal of H3K27me3 from JA biosynthesis genes contributed to JA homeostasis, thus allowing heat-tolerant lines to have normal fertility under HT. By contrast, a high level of H3K27me3 marks at JA biosynthesis genes resulted in fairly low expression levels in HT-sensitive lines, leading to a significant decrease in JA content and ultimately to male sterility under HT. Our study thus provides new insights into the regulation of male fertility by epigenetic modification under HT stress and suggests candidates for creating HT-tolerant cultivars by changing the epigenetic state of target promoters (Figure 7).

# ChIP-seq

ChIP was performed as described previously (Wang et al., 2016). Approximately 2 g of cotton anthers were crosslinked by vacuum infiltration with $1 \%$ (w/v) formaldehyde for 30 min. Chromatin was extracted and fragmented to 200–500 bp by sonication (SCIENT2- IID, China). ChIP was performed using an anti-H3K4me3 antibody (Abcam, ab8580) and anti-H3K27me3 antibody (ABclonal, A2363). Antibody validation is available on the manufacturers’ websites. The antibodies were incubated with Dynabeads protein A (Life Technologies; lot no. 165116310) and added to the sonicated samples for immunoprecipitation. For each sample, $2 0 ~ \mu \up$ of supernatant was set aside before incubation with protein A beads as an input control. The immunoprecipitated DNA was reverse crosslinked by addition of 5 M NaCl at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight, then treated with proteinase K (Takara, 9034) and RNase A (Fermentas, EN0531). The treated DNA was precipitated with absolute ethanol, sodium acetate, and glycogen. Finally, the isolated fragments were used for library construction with an Illumina TruSeq Sample Prep Kit (Illumina, FC-404-2002) following the manufacturer’s recommendations. ChIP-seq was performed on an Illumina HiSeq 3000 instrument (paired-end, 150-bp reads). Two biological replicates were prepared for each sample. 

# Histone modification data analysis

Raw sequence data for 84021 and H05 were trimmed using Trimmomatic software (version 0.39) (Bolger et al., 2014). The clean sequencing reads were mapped to the TM-1 reference genome (HAU-

AD1_v1.0) using Bowtie 2 (version 2.4.1) with the parameters -p 10 -N 1 -L 30 (Langmead and Salzberg, 2012; Wang et al., 2019). After trimming of adapters and removal of low-quality reads and PCR duplicates, the signal density was used to estimate mapping depth as normalized reads per kilobase per million mapped reads (RPKM) to evaluate the correlation between replicates of each sample. The two biological replicates were pooled for each stage before performing the downstream analysis. The sets of aligned reads were downsampled to a minimum number of reads for peak calling from the same sequencing depth in all samples used to call histone modification peaks (Jung et al., 2014). Peaks associated with each histone modification were identified with MACS2 2.2.7.1 (default settings for H3K4me3, false discovery rate [FDR] $< ~ 0 . 0 1$ ; broad-peaks mode for H3K27me3, $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 5 )$ (Zhang et al., 2008). The sequencing data from the input DNA were used as a control. Promoter regions (1000 bp upstream of the TSS and 1000 bp downstream of the TSS) that overlapped with H3K4me3 or H3K27me3 peaks over more than 200 bp were considered to be histone-modified promoters. Unique histone-modified promoters under NT or HT conditions, but not both, were considered to show altered histone modification under HT. When a promoter was modified by both H3K27me3 and H3K4me3, the promoter was considered to be modified by the bivalent H3K4me3–H3K27me3 mark. Differential peaks between NT and HT conditions were identified using MAnorm2 (Version 1.2.0) with the parameters adjusted P (P.adj) ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ and ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 }$ (fold change) ${ > } 1$ (Tu et al., 2021). Promoters that overlapped with differential peaks were defined as significant DMPs. Chromatin states were identified and characterized using ChromHMM (version 1.22) (Ernst and Kellis, 2012). The alignment files of H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 modifications were binned into 200-bp bins using the BinarizeBam command in ChromHMM, with the input alignment file as the control. A stringent threshold (fold enrichment greater than two using -f 2 option) was then applied; the HMM was trained with four emission states using a 200-bp resolution and default parameters with the 

# Plant Communications

LearnModel command. Finally, at each sample, four states were defined for genomic regions: H3K4me3-only (without H3K27me3) region, H3K27me3-only (without H3K4me3) region, bivalent region, and unmarked region. 

# RNA-seq data analysis and GO term analysis

RNA-seq data were downloaded from the National Center for Biotechnology Information Sequence Read Archive under accession number PRJNA393079 (Ma et al., 2018). Adapters and low-quality reads were removed using Trimmomatic (Bolger et al., 2014). All remaining reads were mapped to the cotton reference genome (HAU-AD1_v1.0) using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2019). HTSeq (version 0.11.2) was used to count the number of reads that mapped to each gene (Anders et al., 2015). For analysis of DEGs, the R package DESeq2 (version 3.14) was used with the parameters P.adj ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ and ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 }$ (fold change) ${ > } 1$ (Love et al., 2014). The clusterProfiler R package was used for GO analysis to identify the biological processes associated with DMPs or DEGs, and q values ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ were considered to be significant (Wu et al., 2021). To reduce the complexity of the results, redundant child terms based on the GO hierarchy were removed from the reports. 

# Tissue sectioning, staining, and imaging

Tissue dissection, staining, and imaging were performed as described previously (Wu et al., 2022). In brief, anthers were fixed in FAA $( 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m } \mathsf { l }$ of absolute ethanol, $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of $3 7 \%$ [w/v] formaldehyde solution, $5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ o f acetic acid, and water to $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ l) after removal of bracts and petals. Dehydration was performed in a graded ethanol series $( 3 0 \%$ , $50 \%$ , $70 \%$ , $9 5 \%$ , and $100 \%$ , all v/v). Tissue was embedded in epoxy resin and sectioned to a $1 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ thickness. Toluidine blue $( 1 \% \ \mathsf { w } / \mathsf { v } )$ and aniline blue $( 1 \%$ w/v) were used to stain the sections. A Zeiss Axio Scope A1 microscope was used to image the samples under brightfield illumination for toluidine blue staining. To check pollen viability, samples were stained with $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) 2,3,5- triphenyltetrazolium (TTC) in 0.1 M phosphate-buffered saline (PBS; 0.061 M ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf H P } { \sf O } _ { 4 }$ and 0.038 M ${ \sf K H } _ { 2 } { \sf P O } _ { 4 }$ , pH 7) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 40 min and then observed under a microscope. 

# Measurement of JA contents

For JA measurements, fresh anthers $( 5 0 \mathsf { m } 9 )$ were mixed with $6 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ o f $80 \%$ (v/v) cold methanol for homogenization. Samples were covered to protect them from light and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with shaking. The samples were then centrifuged for 5 min at $1 3 \ 5 2 3 \ g$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . JA in the supernatant was quantified with an ABI 4000Q-TRAP HPLC-MS system (Applied Biosystems) with indole-3-acetic-2,2-d2 acid (Sigma-Aldrich; catalog no. 24420-86-8) as the internal standard (Ma et al., 2018). 

# Application of exogenous MeJA and H3K27me3 inhibitor

For exogenous application of MeJA, a solution of $5 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ MeJA in $0 . 0 5 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20 was prepared using the method of Khan et al. (2020). The MeJA solution was sprayed onto early buds at 2-d intervals until the buds opened under HT conditions. As a control, buds at the same stages were sprayed with $0 . 0 5 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20. Anthers and pollen grains were examined and analyzed on the day that the flowers opened. 

The H3K27me3 inhibitor GSK343 (Selleck; catalog no. S7164) was dissolved in DMSO and diluted to $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ with distilled water. Three days before preparing for HT treatment, the inhibitor was sprayed onto buds of plants cultivated under the same conditions, and distilled water was used as a control. After 7 d of HT treatment, samples were collected for analysis. The anthers in the buds from three different biological replicates under HT or $\mathsf { H T } + \mathsf { H 3 } \mathsf { K 2 7 m e 3 }$ inhibitor treatment were collected, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $- 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use.

# CRISPR-Cas9-mediated mutation

To investigate the function of GhAOS, CRISPR-Cas9-mediated genome editing was used to create a Ghaos mutant in Jin668, an easily transformed cotton cultivar (Li et al., 2019). The pRGEB32-GhU6.7-NPT II-GhAOS plasmid was constructed as described previously (Khan et al., 2023). In brief, two single guide RNAs (sgRNA1 targeting a site 60– 82 bp into the GhAOS genomic sequence; sgRNA2 targeting a site 207– 229 bp) were designed using the CRISPR-P tool (http://crispr.hzau.edn. cn/CRISPR2/) (Liu et al., 2017). Fragments containing a tRNA–sgRNA1 (TGGTTTCTCCGGCTTCAGACGGG) fusion and a guide RNA (gRNA)– tRNA–sgRNA2 (TGGTTCGGATTACCGTCGATTGG) fusion were prepared from a template (pGTR) via the primer GhAOS-CRISPR (Supplemental Table 11) and cloned into the pRGEB32-GhU6.7-NPT II vector. The resulting vector was transformed into Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens) strain GV3101, which was used to inoculate cotton hypocotyls to obtain transgenic plants via tissue culture as described previously (Khan et al., 2023). Regenerated transgenic plants were checked for editing efficiency using the Hi-TOM platform (Liu et al., 2019). The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 11. The Ghaoc2 mutant, JAZ1- overexpression lines, and RNAi lines were obtained from previous studies (Li et al., 2014; Khan et al., 2023).
