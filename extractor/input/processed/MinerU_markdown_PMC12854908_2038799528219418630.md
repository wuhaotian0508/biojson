# ABSTRACT

Artemisia capillaris, a traditional Chinese medicinal plant, has displayed favourable effects in the treatment of jaundice, inflammation of the liver and cholecystitis for thousands of years. However, the biosynthesis of scoparone, the major active component in A. capillaris, remains unclear. Here, a full-length transcriptomic and integrated metabolomic dataset of A. capillaris was generated for the first time. A nearly complete map of the biosynthesis pathway of scoparone was subsequently proposed. Notably, a novel O-methyltransferase, AcOMT1, which catalyses the methylation of scopoletin and isoscopoletin as the final step in the biosynthesis of scoparone, was annotated and characterised using a weighted gene correlation network, phylogenetic analysis, subcellular localisation analysis and enzyme assays. Molecular docking and dynamics simulations demonstrated that AcOMT1 has a stronger and more stable affinity for scopoletin than for isoscopoletin, explaining its greater efficiency and preference for the methylation of scopoletin. Ultimately, by coexpressing AcOMT1, along with the crucial genes responsible for scopoletin biosynthesis in Nicotiana benthamiana, we developed a system for scoparone biosynthesis, yielding $3 . 0 3 \mu \mathrm { g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ dry weight. This study provides not only a comprehensive understanding of the biosynthetic pathway of scoparone but also new avenues for scoparone production in A. capillaris via synthetic biology and metabolic engineering. 

# 1 | Introduction

Artemisia capillaris Thunb., known as Yin Chen in Chinese, is a traditional Chinese herb that is frequently used in the management of jaundice (Yang et al. 2023) and chronic liver conditions (Cai et al. 2020). Extensive biological and pharmacological investigations have demonstrated its efficacy in the clinical treatment 

of cholestasis (Cai et  al.  2024), hepatitis C (Hussain  2022), primary biliary cirrhosis (Lee et  al.  2007), liver fibrosis (Feng et al. 2024) and cholestatic diseases (Song et al. 2023). This herb is prevalent in various regions, including China, Korea, Japan, Pakistan, India, Central Europe, Saudi Arabia and Iran (Ding et al. 2021). With more than two millennia of use, A. capillaris holds a significant place in traditional herbal medicine practices

(Yen et al. 2018). More than 100 Yin Chen-related prescription drugs (https://db.yaozh.com/chufang) have been launched. Yin Chen has also become a popular health drink (YinChen tea) because of its multiple medicinal properties. 

Scoparone, a widely recognised polymethoxylated coumarin, is considered the primary and significant active component of A. capillaris (Dai et al. 2021; Ma et al. 2006; Yin et al. 2012). Numerous studies have illustrated the diverse health benefits of scoparone, including its anti-tumour (Shen et  al.  2023), choleretic and liver-protective (Fang et  al.  2016; Liu et  al.  2020), anti-asthmatic (Fang et  al.  2003), anti-allergic (Choi and Yan  2009) and antiinflammatory (Feng et al. 2022) effects. Furthermore, previous research has indicated that scoparone has significant potential as a crop protection agent for the management of widespread and damaging mite pests in agriculture (Wan et al. 2020; Yao et al. 2022; Zhou et al. 2021, 2024). Nevertheless, A. capillaris plants exhibit limited and inconsistent accumulation of the bioactive substance scoparone. Meeting the growing demand for plant-derived scoparone in the pharmaceutical and agricultural sectors poses a significant challenge due to its low production levels. Metabolic engineering offers a potential solution to these issues. Despite the increasing attention given to this minor compound, the biosynthesis of scoparone in A. capillaris, particularly the downstream Omethylation steps, remains poorly understood. Based on a detailed examination of the structure of scoparone (6,7-dimethylesculetin), it has been proposed that scopoletin (6-methylesculetin) or isoscopoletin (7-methylesculetin) is a potential precursor for scoparone biosynthesis and may involve an O-methylation reaction. Omics technology has allowed for the comprehensive analysis of multiomics data to effectively elucidate metabolic pathways in various plant species (Chen et  al.  2023; Jiang et  al.  2021; Polturak et  al.  2018; Wen et  al.  2024), including nonmodel plant species. It is well known that, beginning with phenylalanine, a series of enzymes—including phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL), cinnamate-4-hydroxylase (C4H), coumarate-3-hydroxylase (C3H), caffeoyl-CoA O-methyltransferase (CCoAOMT), 4-coumarate-CoA ligase (4CL), caffeate 3-O-methyltransferase (COMT), coumarin synthase (COSY) and feruloyl-CoA $6 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F6'H1)— drive the biosynthesis of scopoletin in Arabidopsis thaliana (Kai et al. 2008; Vanholme et al. 2019) (Figure S1). Accordingly, the putative scoparone biosynthetic pathway has been predicted (Yang et al. 2024; Zhao et al. 2019); however, very little information concerning the biosynthetic genes, especially O-methyltransferase genes, of this important biosynthetic pathway in this non-model species is available. 

In this study, we utilised the popular medicinal plant A. capillaris to elucidate the biosynthetic pathway of scoparone and identify the missing methyltransferase. Conjoint analysis of the transcriptome and metabolome was used to screen out the genes and metabolites responsible for putative scoparone biosynthesis. Notably, a novel O-methyltransferase, AcOMT1, which catalyses the methylation of scopoletin and isoscopoletin, as the final step in the biosynthesis of scoparone, was identified. By leveraging the identified biosynthetic genes, we subsequently achieved successful de novo heterologous biosynthesis of scoparone in N. benthamiana leaves. Our results provide significant insights into the biosynthesis of scoparone and offer a theoretical foundation for the development of potential biotechnological strategies for its production via synthetic biology. 

# 2 | Materials and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Materials and Chemical Reagents

In September 2021, A. capillaris plants in the late reproductive stage were collected in Xi'an city, Shanxi, China. Fresh plant organs, specifically the roots, stems, leaves and flowers of A. capillaris, were promptly separated, flash frozen in liquid nitrogen and preserved at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for subsequent analysis. The seeds of Nicotiana benthamiana were maintained in storage within our laboratory. N. benthamiana plants were cultivated from seeds that were germinated and grown in potting soil under controlled greenhouse conditions, maintained at a temperature of $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a photoperiod of $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light and 8 h of darkness, until they reached an age of 4–6 weeks, after which they were used for Agrobacterium-mediated transient transformation using standard protocols. 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC)-grade methanol and formic acid were obtained from Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co. Ltd. Chemical reagents, including scoparone, scopoletin, isoscopoletin and S-adenosyl-Lmethionine (SAM), were obtained from Yuanye Biotech Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). 

# 2.2 | Widely Targeted Metabolomics Analysis of A. capillaris

Sample preparation and metabolite extraction, identification and quantification for leaf and flower samples (each in triplicate) were performed at MetWare Biotech Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China) following previously described methods (Zhang et al. 2022, 2020). In brief, freeze-dried samples were ground into powder using a mixer mill (MM 400, Retsch) for 1.5 min at $3 0 \mathrm { H z }$ . Next, $5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of lyophilised powder was dissolved in $1 . 2 \mathrm { m L }$ of $7 0 \%$ methanol extraction solution and vortexed for 30 s every 30 min. This mixing step was repeated six times. Following centrifugation at 12 000 rpm and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min, the extracts were filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microporous membrane. The sample extracts were analysed on an ultraperformance liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC–MS/MS) system. Finally, a quality control sample (mix) was prepared by mixing equal amounts of all the samples to monitor the stability of the analytical conditions for analysis. A UPLC–ESI–MS/MS system was used to detect metabolite profiles. Qualitative analysis of all metabolites was performed via a database constructed by MetWare Biotechnology, Wuhan, China. Mass spectrometry data were analysed and quantified using Analyst software v1.6.3 and MultiQuant software v3.0.2. Orthogonal partial least-squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) and principal component analysis (PCA) were conducted to analyse and verify the differences and reliability of the metabolites in the samples. 

# 2.3 | Determination of Scoparone Content

Quantitative analysis of scoparone in different tissues of A. capillaris and the leaves of transgenic N. benthamiana was performed via HPLC. Different tissues (roots, stems, leaves and flowers) of 

A. capillaris and different leaves of transgenic N. benthamiana were collected and freeze-dried to obtain powders. The powder (1.0 g) of each sample was mixed with $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of methanol by ultrasonication (180 W power, $4 0 \mathrm { k H z }$ frequency) for 60 min and then centrifuged at $1 2 0 0 0 \times \mathrm { g }$ for 30 min. The resulting extracts were dried via a rotary evaporator and dissolved in methanol. The supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ microporous membrane and then subjected to HPLC (Agilent) to determine the content of scoparone. 

Content analysis was performed using an Agilent 1260 Infinity II HPLC system equipped with a C18 column $( 5 \mu \mathrm { m } \times 2 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ ; Phenomenex, USA) at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 4 3 \mathrm { n m }$ . The mobile phase consisted of methanol $(40 \% )$ and $1 \%$ formic acid $( 6 0 \% )$ in isocratic mode, with a flow rate of $1 . 0 \mathrm { { m L } \cdot \mathrm { { m i n } ^ { - 1 } } }$ , and the injection volume was $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . A scoparone standard was used for quantification and qualitative analysis. 

# 2.4 | RNA Extraction, Illumina and PacBio Sequencing and Analyses

Total RNA was extracted using a TRIzol kit (TIANGENT) following the manufacturer's instructions. The concentration, purity, integrity and overall status of the RNA were subsequently assessed to ensure optimal quality. The libraries were sequenced by MetWare Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (https://www.metware. cn/). For Illumina sequencing, libraries from leaf and flower tissues were prepared using an NEBNext Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit (Illumina Inc.) and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 sequencer (Illumina) in paired-end mode (PE150). To construct the library for PacBio sequencing, total RNA from the tissues, including leaves and flowers, was mixed equally. The Iso-Seq library was constructed following the isoform sequencing protocol (Iso-Seq), utilising the Clontech SMARTer PCR cDNA Synthesis Kit protocol as outlined by Pacific Biosciences. The PacBio Sequel sequencing platform was utilised to generate subreads, which were subsequently self-corrected and assembled into circular consensus sequences (CCSs). These CCSs, containing primers at both ends and poly-A sequences at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ ends, were screened for full-length nonchimerics (FLNCs) with no chimeric sequences. Next, self-correction and data correction using second-generation sequencing were conducted. The corrected FLNCs were further processed for redundancy using CD-HIT software with a similarity threshold of $9 5 \%$ . This process yielded high-quality full-length transcripts. The full-length transcripts were functionally annotated through the utilisation of seven databases, namely, the non-redundant protein sequences database (NR), SwissProt, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG), the Protein Family Database (Pfam), Gene Ontology (GO), Clusters of EuKaryotic Orthologous Groups (KOG) and Translation of EMBL (TrEMBL). 

The full-length transcripts were regarded as the reference genome. All the clean reads from the samples were mapped to the reference genome using Bowtie2 (Langmead and Salzberg 2012). The RSEM tool was employed to quantify gene and isoform abundances, and normalisation of mapped reads was performed, followed by the calculation of FPKM (fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads) (Li and Dewey  2011). According to the raw read count values of each 

sample, differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified in pairwise comparisons between groups using DESeq2 software (Love et al. 2014) employing a threshold of $ { \lVert { \mathbf { o g } _ { 2 } } \rVert }$ FoldChange| $\geq 1$ and a false discovery rate (FDR) of $< 0 . 0 5$ (Anders  2010). The clusterProfiler R package was used to carry out GO and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses of the DEGs. 

# 2.5 | Real-Time Quantitative Reverse Transcription-PCR Analysis

The RNA $( 1 \mu \mathrm { g } )$ from each sample was reverse transcribed to cDNA via an Evo M-MLV Plus cDNA Synthesis Kit (AG11615; Accurate Biotechnology, China) in a $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixture. qRT–PCR was performed on a CFX96 system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) using SsoFast EvaGreen Supermix (Bio-Rad, USA). The reactions were performed as follows: an initial denaturation step at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s, followed by 40 cycles of denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 s and annealing at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { s }$ . Melting curve analysis was performed to confirm PCR specificity, with a heat dissociation protocol from $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ following the final PCR cycle. Relative expression levels were normalised to that of the actin gene as an internal control (de Oliveira et al. 2022) and then calculated using the $2 ^ { - \triangle \triangle C t }$ method (Schmittgen and Livak 2008). The qRT–PCR primers used are listed in Table S1. Each assay included three biological replicates. 

# 2.6 | Coexpression Analysis

The WGCNA package (v1.70–3) (Langfelder and Horvath 2008) was used to perform weighted gene correlation network analysis using FPKM values obtained from various tissues. Module eigengene values were calculated within the WGCNA framework and then correlated with the accumulation patterns of potential metabolites in different plant tissues. Cytoscape (v3.9.0) (Shannon et al. 2003) was used for the analysis and visualisation of gene coexpression networks. 

# 2.7 | Bioinformatics Analysis

The amino acid sequences of the AcOMT homologues were obtained from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) and aligned using MAFFT (v.7.310) (Katoh et al. 2019). Phylogenetic tree construction was performed using MEGA X software with the neighbour–joining method and 1000 bootstrap replicates (Kumar et al. 2018). iTOL (https://itol.embl.de/) was utilised for the annotation and visualisation of the phylogenetic trees (Letunic and Bork 2021). Sequence similarity and secondary structure information were analysed using the ESPript 3.0 web server (https:// espript.ibcp.fr/). 

# 2.8 | Protein Expression and Purification

The complete coding sequence (CDS) of AcOMT1-3 (Table S2) was amplified from the A. capillaris cDNA library and subsequently subcloned and inserted into the pET28a $( + )$ vector via 

the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme, China). Following sequence validation, the construct was introduced into the E. coli strain BL21 (DE3) (Takara, China), and protein expression was induced with 1 mM IPTG overnight at 180 rpm and $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The recombinant protein was subsequently purified using Ni-NTA agarose beads (Vazyme, China). Protein concentrations were determined using Bradford reagent (Karimi et al. 2022) and further analysed by $1 0 \%$ SDS–PAGE. 

# 2.9 | In Vitro Assays of AcOMTs

The enzymatic activity assays were conducted in accordance with previously published methods (Zhao et  al.  2024), with slight modifications. Specifically, a $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixture was prepared containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 5 mM SAM, 1 mM DTT, 1 mM substrates and 1 mg of crude recombinant protein. If necessary, $\mathbf { 1 m M M g C l } _ { 2 }$ was included in the mixture. The pET28a $( + )$ empty vector served as a negative control. The assays were carried out at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ , and the resulting mixture was extracted twice with an equal volume of ethyl acetate. The upper organic phase was dried, dissolved in methanol and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter for subsequent analysis by HPLC. 

# 2.10 | Kinetics Measurement of AcOMT1

The biochemical properties of AcOMT1 were investigated using scopoletin or isoscopoletin as the methyl receptor and SAM as the methyl donor. The experimental procedure involved the use of Tris–HCl buffer (pH 6.0, 7.0, 7.5, 8.0 or 9.0) and $4 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of purified AcOMT1, with the reaction conducted at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ to determine the optimal pH. Additionally, the reaction was carried out at temperatures ranging from $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ to determine the optimal temperature. The kinetic parameters $\mathbf { { \cal K } } _ { m }$ and $K _ { c a t } )$ were assessed via reactions with scopoletin or isoscopoletin at concentrations ranging from $0 . 0 6 2 5 { - } 1 . 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , followed by analysis through nonlinear regression curve fitting using Michaelis–Menten bundled in GraphPad Prism 8.3 software (GraphPad, USA). 

# 2.11 | Substrate Binding Analysis

Purified AcOMT1 was subjected to binding titration analysis to determine the $K _ { d }$ values for substrate binding, following a previously described method (Guo et al. 2021) with modifications. A concentration of $1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ AcOMT1 was established as the baseline, followed by the addition of substrate at concentrations ranging from 0 to $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . After 10 min of incubation at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the differences in absorbance between 200 and $5 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ were recorded using a UV–visible 2550 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan), and the data were plotted to determine the binding affinities at $3 4 3 \mathrm { n m }$ . The binding affinities were estimated using GraphPad Prism 8.3 software, as outlined in previous studies (Wang et al. 2023). 

# 2.12 | Subcellular Localisation Assay

Subcellular localisation prediction of AcOMT1 was performed using WoLF PSORT (https://wolfpsort.hgc.jp/). To 

generate the AcOMT1-GFP fusion protein, the CDS of the AcOMT1 gene was cloned and inserted into the pBI121-GFP vector using the primers listed in Table  S1. The constructs were subsequently sequenced and transferred into the A. tumefaciens strain GV3101, which was then co-transformed into 6-week-old N. benthamiana leaves (Li et al. 2019). After 48 h of cultivation, the GFP fluorescence signal was visualised using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM 710 NLO, Oberkochen, Germany). 

# 2.13 | Protein Structure Prediction, Molecular Docking and Molecular Dynamics Analysis

The structural model of AcOMT1 was generated using Alphafold2 (Bryant et  al.  2022). Molecular docking of the protein with ligands was performed using AutoDock 4.2 (Forli et  al.  2016). The resulting models were visualised by AutoDockTools v1.5.6 and PyMOL 2.5 (http://www.pymol. org). Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted to analyse the structures of AcOMT1 docked with scopoletin and isoscopoletin by employing the GROMACS 2019 software package (Abraham et al. 2015). The topology and coordinate files for the compounds were generated using AmberTools18 and subsequently converted into GROMACS-compatible topology and coordinate files using the ACPYPE program (Kagami et al. 2023). The protein topology and coordinate files were generated in accordance with the Amber ff99SB force field using the pdb2gmx tool in GROMACS 2019. The topology and coordinate files of both the protein and ligand were subsequently combined and embedded within a dodecahedral periodic box by employing the SPC216 explicit solvation model. The system was then neutralised by the addition of five $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ ions. Energy minimisation was conducted utilising the steepest descent algorithm, followed by a position-restraint protocol under the NVT and NPT ensembles at $2 9 8 \mathrm { K }$ and 1.0 bar. The MD simulations produced a 100 ns trajectory based on the Leapfrog integrator with a 2 fs time step. Constraints on all bonds involving hydrogen atoms were enforced using the LINCS algorithm. Long-range electrostatic interaction energies were computed via the particle–mesh Ewald (PME) method. Atomic coordinates and energies were recorded every 10 ps for subsequent analysis. The binding free energy of the protein–ligand complex in solvent was determined using the molecular mechanics Poisson–Boltzmann surface area (MM-PBSA) approach, as implemented in the gmx_MMPBSA tool (Valdés-Tresanco et al. 2021). 

# 2.14 | Heterologous Production of Scoparone in N. Benthamiana

With respect to the de novo biosynthesis of scoparone in N. benthamiana, the genes AtF6'H1 (Hoengenaert et  al.  2022), AtCOSY (Vanholme et al. 2019) and AcOMT1 were coexpressed in tobacco leaves. The coding sequences of these genes were connected using a 2A peptide linker from porcine teschovirus (P2A) (Table S2) and then integrated into the pBI121-GFP vector at the BamHI/SacI site to create transient expression vectors. This procedure was carried out by Huada BioTech (Beijing, China). These transient expression vectors were subsequently introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101, which was then used 

to transform tobacco leaves. The plants were then subjected to extended periods of light exposure for 2 days to ensure adequate accumulation of protein. Fluorescence was visualised with a hand-held lamp (LUYOR-3415UR, LUYOR, USA). Leaves were then harvested for further analysis.

# 3 | Results

# 3.1 | Metabolomic Profiling and Scoparone Determination in A. capillaris

Tissues with different levels of secondary metabolites can be used to identify biosynthetic genes and intermediate metabolites. Four organs, namely, the roots, stems, leaves and flowers, were sampled (Figure 1A), and HPLC was used to determine the scoparone content in A. capillaris. The content of scoparone varied among the different tissues (Figure  1B). All four organs analysed contained scoparone, with the highest level in flowers $( 1 0 . 1 \mathrm { m g { \cdot g } ^ { - 1 } }$ DW), a moderate level in leaves $( 3 . 6 0 \mathrm { m g { \cdot g } ^ { - 1 } }$ DW) and the lowest amounts in stems $( 0 . 2 3 6 \mathrm { m g { \cdot } g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW) and roots $( 0 . 2 6 5 \mathrm { m g { \cdot } g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW), indicating that flowers and leaves are the primary sites of scoparone biosynthesis and accumulation in A. capillaris. 

To further understand and elucidate the scoparone biosynthesis pathway, we selected leaves and flowers for broadly targeted metabolomics profiling. Principal component analysis (PCA) and orthogonal partial least-squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) revealed that the leaf and flower groups were significantly separated from each other (Figure 1C,D). Metabolites that may be intermediates for scoparone biosynthesis, such as scopoletin and isoscopoletin, were identified (Table  S3). The relative contents of these compounds (represented as peak areas) are shown in Figure 1E. Scoparone was the major bioactive polymethoxylated coumarin compound, whose abundance was 2.0-fold greater in flowers than in leaves. Two additional methoxylated coumarins with chemical structures similar to scoparone, scopoletin and isoscopoletin also presented relatively high levels in flowers, with 3.0- and 1.5-fold greater accumulation than in leaves, respectively, which was similar to the accumulation trend of scoparone. Moreover, 8 of the 12 possible annotated metabolites associated with scoparone synthesis significantly differed between the leaves and flowers (Figure 1E). Overall, the tissue-specific accumulation of coumarins in leaves and flowers suggests that these tissues may serve as valuable sources for the further identification of genes involved in scoparone biosynthesis. 

# 3.2 | Transcriptome Analysis Based on SMRT Sequencing and NGS in Leaves and Flowers

To further elucidate the scoparone biosynthesis pathway at the transcriptional level in A. capillaris, a hybrid approach 

involving short-read next-generation sequencing (NGS) and long-read single-molecule real-time (SMRT) sequencing was used to obtain complete cDNA sequences. For NGS, six cDNA libraries of two different tissues (leaf and flower, each in triplicate) of A. capillaris were constructed and subsequently sequenced using the Illumina platform. High-quality sequencing data were obtained with a Q30 score $> 9 2 \%$ and an average of $4 7 . 9 0 \mathrm { M }$ raw reads per sample read (Table  S4). The numbers of total clean reads from the leaves and flowers were 141.65 and $1 4 3 . 6 2 \mathrm { M }$ , respectively, which were obtained via the removal of contaminated adapters and lowquality reads. 

To generate the full-length transcriptome of A. capillaris, mixed RNA samples from the leaves and flowers were collected for SMRT sequencing using the PacBio Sequel platform. After the adapter sequences were removed, 28 564 923 subreads were obtained from 760 788 polymerase reads, totaling 54.04 Gb of bases (Tables S5 and S6, Figure S2A,B). These subreads were processed through the PacBio SMRT analysis pipeline, which resulted in 582 104 circular consensus sequences (CCSs) with a total base count of 1 171 578 502 bp and an average length of 2013 bp (Table S7). Among these reads, $8 8 . 6 1 \%$ (515 825) were identified as full-length nonchimeric reads (FLNCs) (Table S8, Figure S2C). The FLNC sequences were analysed using IsoSeq in SMRTLink to cluster similar sequences, correct errors and create consensus transcripts. In total, 53 237 accurate transcripts were identified, with 45 666 non-redundant transcripts remaining after redundancies were removed via CD-HIT $\left( c = 0 . 9 0 \right)$ (Tables S9 and S10). The TransDecoder program was used to predict the coding sequences, and the length distribution is shown in Figure S2D. The constructed set of full-length transcripts was used as a reference dataset for subsequent analyses.

# 3.6 | Identification of AcOMT1-3 as Candidate Genes Responsible for Scoparone Accumulation

To identify OMT genes potentially involved in polymethoxylated coumarin (scoparone) biosynthesis, we performed weighted gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA) and gene coexpression correlation analysis. The transcriptome data from A. capillaris were divided into 13 modules based on their expression patterns (Figure 4A). Analysis of the module–trait relationships revealed that the MEturquoise module was significantly positively correlated with the contents of methoxylated coumarins (scopoletin, isoscopoletin and scoparone) $\textstyle R > 0 . 9 6$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ ; Figure  4B). Therefore, the MEturquoise module was selected for further study to understand the biosynthesis of scoparone in A. capillaris. Within the MEturquoise module, three candidate OMTs (AcOMT1, AcOMT2 and AcOMT3) were screened, along with two F6'Hs, which are core enzymes in the biosynthesis of coumarins (Beesley et al. 2023). Moreover, coexpression and correlation network analyses were conducted to identify the downstream OMTs that were strongly associated with the key genes (F6'Hs) (Figure 4C). These results suggested that AcOMT1–3 play crucial roles in scoparone 

biosynthesis. Therefore, these genes were selected as target genes for subsequent functional analysis.

# 3.9 | AcOMT1 Shows a Greater Substrate Preference for Scopoletin Than for Isoscopoletin

To gain a deeper understanding of the roles of AcOMT1 in the methylation of isoscopoletin and scopoletin, we tested their kinetic parameters using purified AcOMT1 (Figure S7). 

AcOMT1 exhibited maximum activity at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Figure S9A,B) and pH 7.5 (Figure  S9C,D), with apparent $K _ { c a t } / K _ { m }$ values of 900.90 and $1 7 5 9 . 8 8  { \mathrm { M ^ { - 1 } } } .  { \mathbf { s ^ { - 1 } } }$ , respectively (Figure  5E,F). The results demonstrated that although both isoscopoletin and scopoletin can be methylated by AcOMT1, AcOMT1 exhibited superior substrate affinity and catalytic efficiency for scopoletin. To further verify this substrate preference, the binding affinities of AcOMT1 molecules involved in hydrogen bonding to scopoletin or isoscopoletin were assessed through fluorescence-binding assays. As indicated in Figure  5G,H, AcOMT1 had a higher binding affinity for scopoletin $( K _ { \mathrm { d } } = 5 . 8 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) than for isoscopoletin $( K _ { \mathrm { d } } = 7 . 3 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ). These results highlighted that AcOMT1 exhibited a substrate preference for scopoletin. These results suggest that manipulating the biosynthesis of scopoletin could have a substantial effect on the accumulation of scoparone in A. capillaris.

# 4 | Discussion

# 4.1 | High-Throughput Sequencing Facilitates Research on Scoparone-Producing Plants

The extensive availability of nucleotide sequence data, coupled with the accessibility of advanced technologies, has facilitated the development of computational methodologies for identifying genes and pathways associated with plant-specific metabolism (Li et al. 2023; Xu et al. 2023). A key method is to analyse the metabolite levels and gene expression patterns of orthologous genes in tissues that produce specific metabolites (Gao et al. 2024; Xu et al. 2021). 

A. capillaris is widely acknowledged as a medicinal plant utilised in the treatment of jaundice and chronic liver diseases (Yang et al. 2023). The methoxylated coumarins scoparone and scopoletin have been recognised as the principal bioactive constituents (Ding et al. 2021). Through metabolomics analysis, these three methoxylated coumarins were shown to exhibit tissue-specific accumulation (Figure  1). These characteristics make A. capillaris an ideal candidate for investigating scoparone biosynthesis via high-throughput sequencing (Figure  2A). Hence, datasets were generated to predict the genes and metabolites associated 

with scoparone biosynthetic pathways in A. capillaris (Figure 3). These datasets will serve as valuable resources for advancing the understanding of the genes and metabolites implicated in these secondary metabolic pathways, as elaborated upon in other contributions to this special issue. Specifically, the identification of candidate genes involved in specific biosynthetic pathways can be facilitated through a comparative analysis of gene expression and metabolite accumulation, as exemplified by a similar recent study (Wen et al. 2024). 

# 4.2 | AcOMT1 Specifically Catalyses Scopoletin Methylation, the Last Committed Step in Scoparone Biosynthesis

Methyltransferases (MTs) constitute a diverse class of proteins responsible for catalysing methylation reactions, including Cmethyltransferases (CMTs), N-methyltransferases (NMTs) and O-methyltransferases (OMTs) (Tan et al. 2022), which result in changes in the solubility, stability and other characteristics of the substrate, thereby increasing its versatility (Hui et al. 2020; Luo et  al.  2022). Because of their structural properties, OMTs are likely involved in the biosynthesis of scoparone. 

In the present study, a newly discovered multifunctional Omethyltransferase gene was shown to play a crucial role in the final stage of scoparone biosynthesis. This discovery was made through the examination of in vitro enzymatic reactions, transcriptome sequencing and analysis of scoparone accumulation in various tissues of A. capillaris. Notably, the flowers presented relatively high expression levels of AcOMT1 (Figure S8A) and the greatest concentrations of scoparone (Figure  1B), indicating a potential correlation between scoparone accumulation and AcOMT1 gene expression in this particular tissue (Figure  S8B). Usually, genes within the biosynthetic pathway of plant metabolites undergo coregulation, which results in a correlation between the expression of biosynthetic genes and the accumulation of their corresponding end products (Wei et al. 2018). Furthermore, phylogenetic analysis revealed a close relationship between AcOMT1 and the class II OMT group TcOMT, indicating high substrate specificity (Song et al. 2022; Xu et al. 2022; Zhao et al. 2019). Class II OMTs are known to methylate a wide range of secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids, flavonols, stilbenes, phenylpropenes and various volatile phenolics (Hao et al. 2023; Wu et al. 2024). Notably, plant class II OMTs are primarily responsible for para-O-methylation activity (Zhao et al. 2020). AcOMT1, in particular, can methylate scopoletin and is involved in the final step of methylation to produce scoparone (Figure 5B,C). AcOMT1 exhibited a greater affinity for scopoletin than for isoscopoletin, demonstrating increased catalytic efficiency and binding affinity (Figure 5E,F). Consequently, AcOMT1 has emerged as a promising candidate for the regulation of scoparone metabolism. 

# 4.3 | Metabolic Engineering of Scoparone Production in Heterologous Hosts

Scoparone is among the most abundant coumarins identified in A. capillaris and is known to possess rich pharmacological properties, such as anti-inflammatory (Feng et  al.  2022). The 

scoparone content is insufficient to satisfy commercial demand. Consequently, addressing the increasing need for potential plant-derived scoparone benefits to both human health and agriculture presents a considerable challenge. Metabolic engineering offers a promising solution to this issue (De La Peña and Sattely 2021; Martí et al. 2020). For example, the simultaneous introduction of stilbene synthase and O-methyltransferase led to significant heterologous production of pterostilbene $( 5 2 \mu \mathrm { g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ FW) in N. benthamiana (Martinez-Marquez et  al.  2018). Similarly, the transgenic expression of Rhodiola salidroside biosynthetic genes enables the heterologous production of salidroside $( 2 0 \mathrm { m g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW) in N. benthamiana (Torrens-Spence et al. 2018). This study presents the novel induction of scoparone production in a heterologous plant through transient gene expression without the requirement for substrate feeding. This was facilitated by the discovery of the additional scopoletin methylation activity of AcOMT1. The coexpression of AcOMT1, AtF6'H1 and AtCOSY was determined to be adequate for the biosynthesis of scoparone, eliminating the need for external supplementation, albeit still at a very low level $( 3 . 0 3 \mu \mathrm { g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW), indicating that there is substantial room for engineering N. benthamiana for high-yield scoparone production. An adequate supply of endogenous substrates is crucial for the efficient production of products in plants. It would be useful to be able to induce the coumarins biosynthetic pathway more efficiently using transcription factors, as not only are biosynthetic pathway genes induced by Delila and Rosea1, but also upstream genes involved in the biosynthesis of the precursor phenylalanine (Outchkourov et al. 2018). Higher amounts of anthocyanin (C3G: $1 . 2 2 \mathrm { m g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ FW) can be produced by transient coexpression of heterologous pathway genes together with the MYB transcription factors Rosea1 or PAP1 than when only pathway genes are used (Grützner et al. 2023). Furthermore, coexpressing the upstream phenylpropanoid biosynthesis genes with type II GmCHI1B1 markedly increased the yield of liquiritigenin $( 0 . 8 3 8 \mathrm { m g . g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW) (Xie et  al.  2025). In the future, we will develop synergistic engineering strategies to optimise scoparone synthesis in N. benthamiana, including the remodelling of endogenous fluxes for scoparone production with transcription factors (TFs) that upregulate the upstream phenylpropanoid pathway. Nonetheless, this investigation offers a promising approach for enhancing scoparone production via metabolic engineering in nonnative organisms. 

# 5 | Conclusion

In summary, this work presents the first comprehensive transcriptomic and metabolomic analysis of the traditional medicinal plant A. capillaris, offering significant insights into the biosynthesis of scoparone. The resulting gene coexpression networks enabled us to directly connect candidate genes to metabolites at the scoparone pathway level based on correlations between transcript and metabolite abundances. Furthermore, a novel O-methyltransferase, AcOMT1, which is highly correlated with scoparone levels, was functionally characterised via phylogenetic analysis, subcellular localisation analysis, enzyme assays and molecular docking techniques. Finally, we managed to induce scoparone biosynthesis in tobacco. Our findings expand upon those of previous studies by elucidating the complete biosynthetic pathway for scoparone, which involves 

the incorporation of a missing last methylation step essential for its production. These findings provide a theoretical basis for the genetic engineering and molecular breeding of A. capillaris cultivars with increased medicinal value. In addition, the methodology employed in this study is broadly applicable for future investigations of other largely unexplored specialised metabolic pathways in non-model plants.
