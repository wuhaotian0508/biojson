# ABSTRACT

Through co-evolutionary adaptation, phytopathogenic fungi have evolved specialised host preference mechanisms to optimise infection efficacy. Fungi of the Valsa genus infect various Rosaceae fruit trees, with Valsa mali exhibiting a marked host preference for apple trees, while Valsa pyri preferentially colonises pear trees. The divergent secondary metabolite biosynthetic gene clusters (SMBGCs) between these two species may serve as key determinants of their distinct host preferences. In this study, VmPKS5, a polyketide synthase, was identified as a key factor influencing the host preference of V. mali, which is the main pathogen of apple Valsa canker (AVC). Deletion of VmPKS5 greatly reduced the virulence of V. mali in apple trees, but not in pear trees. Deletion of VmPKS5 completely abolished the production of the toxin $p$ -coumaric acid ethyl ester $\cdot p$ -CAEE). Exogenous $p$ -CAEE application partly restored the virulence of ΔVmPKS5 and enhanced the virulence of the wild-type strain of V. mali. Crucially, heterologous expression of VmPKS5 in V. pyri increased its virulence towards apple trees by production of $p$ -CAEE during infection. Notably, V. mali can uptake double-stranded RNA (dsRNA), and exogenous spray of VmPKS5-dsRNA significantly inhibited the infection by V. mali. This study provides new perspectives on host preference mechanisms of fungal pathogens and green disease control of tree disease by dsRNA fungicides. 

# 1 | Introduction

There have been several major epidemics of apple Valsa canker (AVC) caused by fungi of the genus Valsa spp. in China, resulting in the destruction of millions of fruit trees and causing enormous economic losses. Studies have shown that the main pathogens causing AVC in China are V. mali and V. pyri, with V. mali being the predominant pathogenic species (Wang 

et al. 2011, 2014). Evolutionary analysis has revealed a close genetic relationship between these two species, with apple being their common ancestral host (Wang et  al.  2014). Both V. mali and V. pyri can infect apple and pear trees, yet they exhibit significant host preferences, with V. mali preferentially infecting apple trees and V. pyri preferentially infecting pear trees (Wang et al. 2007; Zhou et al. 2014). However, the factors determining the host preference of Valsa spp. remain unclear.

The preference of microorganisms for specific hosts is a ubiquitous phenomenon in nature. Different bacteria can establish specific and preferential associations with particular host plants. For instance, the soil-dwelling bacterium Ensifer meliloti can adapt to the local host genotype within just five generations and proliferate to higher abundances in co-evolved hosts (Batstone et al. 2020; Wippel et al. 2021). Different serotypes of Salmonella enterica exhibit host preferences; S. typhi has a preference for infecting humans, while S. gallinarum preferentially infects poultry (Bäumler and Fang 2013; Langridge et al. 2015). Similarly, fungi also exhibit marked host preferences, particularly evident among plant-pathogenic fungi. While most pathogenic fungi can infect multiple hosts, some are highly specialised to a single host (Borah et al. 2018). Recent studies have indicated that the host preferences of plant-pathogenic fungi may be attributed to a series of complex factors, including avirulence (Avr) genes, horizontal gene transfer (HGT) and secondary metabolites (SMs) (Li et  al. 2020). For instance, two avirulence proteins of Puccinia graminis f. sp. tritici, AvrSr35 and AvrSr50, can be recognised by the resistance proteins Sr35 and Sr50, respectively, triggering immune responses that lead to differential host selection by the pathogen (Chen et  al.  2017; Salcedo et  al.  2017). HGTs in Pyrenophora teres facilitates pathogen infection by interfering with host defence responses and degrading the host cell wall (Sun et al. 2013). Cochliobolus carbonum determines its selective infection of maize by producing the secondary metabolite HCtoxin, which inhibits the activity of maize histone deacetylases and alters the expression of plant defence genes (Walton 2006). 

In fungi, genes involved in secondary metabolite biosynthesis (SMB) are primarily located in regions near subtelomeres on each chromosome, organised into clusters rather than randomly dispersed (Palmer and Keller  2010). Genomic studies have revealed a wealth of SMB gene clusters (SMBGCs) in fungal genomes. For example, fungi belonging to the genus Aspergillus are known to contain an average of approximately 50 SMBGCs per species (Döhren 2009). Compared to other fungi, Valsa spp. possess a greater number of SMBGCs (Yin et al. 2015). Genes in SMBGCs are significantly upregulated during infection, highlighting their importance as virulence factors (Ke et  al.  2014). Collinearity analysis revealed that significant variations occur in SMBGCs of V. mali and V. pyri, with the most prominent variation being gene loss (Yin et al. 2015). Therefore, the SMBGCs that vary between V. mali and V. pyri are likely to determine their host preferences. 

Currently, the control of AVC primarily relies on chemical methods (Jiao et  al.  2015; Wang et  al.  2019). However, issues such as pesticide residues, environmental pollution and increased pathogen resistance resulting from long-term chemical application remain difficult to address effectively. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop new agents that are highly effective, low in toxicity, and environmentally friendly. Previous research has demonstrated that the fungal pathogens Botrytis cinerea and Fusarium graminearum are capable of efficiently absorbing environmental double-stranded RNAs (dsRNAs), subsequently triggering the silencing of pathogen genes that possess complementary sequences (Koch et al. 2016; Wang et al. 2016). These discoveries prompted the development of an innovative crop protection strategy, spray-induced gene silencing (SIGS). SIGS employs the method of exogenous application of dsRNA 

to induce silencing of target genes in pathogens, thereby inhibiting pathogen infection (Song et al. 2018; Wang and Jin 2017). dsRNA, as a biological pesticide, exhibits characteristics such as environmental friendliness and a low tendency to induce resistance. Furthermore, it has been successfully utilised to control some fungal diseases (Wang et  al.  2016; Koch et  al.  2016; McLoughlin et al. 2018). 

In this study, based on the SMBGCs that vary between V. mali and V. pyri, 12 SMBGCs that were lost in $V .$ pyri were identified. Transcriptional profiling using reverse transcriptionquantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) demonstrated that only four core genes from distinct SMBGCs exhibited significant upregulation during the infection process of V. mali. Through targeted gene knockout experiments, we discovered that specifically disrupting VmPKS5 resulted in marked attenuation of $V .$ mali virulence on apple, while maintaining full pathogenicity on pear, thereby establishing VmPKS5 as a determinant of host preference. Mechanistic investigation revealed that VmPKS5 regulates biosynthesis of $p$ -CAEE. Exogenous $p$ -CAEE application partly restored the virulence of ΔVmPKS5 and enhanced the virulence of the wild-type strain of V. mali. Crucially, heterologous expression of VmPKS5 in V. pyri conferred enhanced pathogenicity on apple hosts, with phenotypic complementation strictly dependent on $p$ -CAEE production, providing conclusive evidence that this metabolite mediates host preference. Notably, we established that both mycelia and protoplast forms of V. mali demonstrate efficient dsRNA uptake capacity. Application of dsRNA targeting the VmPKS5 gene significantly weakened the pathogenicity of the pathogen, achieving disease control. These findings provide new insights into understanding the host preferences by pathogenic fungi and offer new ideas and technologies for the green control of AVC. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Four V. mali-Specific SMBGC Core Genes Are Upregulated During Pathogen Infection

Previously, it has been established that there exist significant disparities in SMBGCs between V. mali and its closely related species V. pyri, with 12 such clusters in V. mali exhibiting gene loss in V. pyri (Yin et al. 2015). SMBGCs represent a set of functionally interconnected genes within a genome that encode the biosynthesis of SMs. Among these clusters, core genes serve as pivotal elements, directly participating in the synthesis of the backbone structures of SMs (Brakhage  2013; Keller  2019). To investigate their roles in host infection, we initially examined the expression patterns of core genes within SMBGCs during V. mali. Among these SMBGCs, only the core genes of four clusters, namely VmPKS1, VmPKS5, VmNRPS28 and VmNRPS-PKS33, were found to be significantly upregulated during the infection stage of V. mali (Table S1, Figure 1).

# 2.3 | VmPKS5 Affects the Production of $\pmb { p }$ -CAEE

Given the established role of polyketide synthases in the production of toxins by numerous plant pathogens, fungal toxin isolation and toxicity tests were conducted to assess the potential impacts of VmPKS5 on the generation of toxins. The wild-type 03-8, ΔVmPKS5, and ΔVmPKS5-C strains were cultivated in ABB medium with shaking to obtain the corresponding SMs. Subsequently, the toxicity of these crude SMs was evaluated on apple leaves and twigs. Crude SMs derived from the 03-8, ΔVmPKS5, and ΔVmPKS5-C strains were all capable of inducing necrotic symptoms $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ post-treatment (Figure 3a,b). However, SMs extracted from the ΔVmPKS5 mutant showed markedly diminished toxicity when compared to SMs derived from the wildtype 03-8 and ΔVmPKS5-C (Figure  3c,d). The uninoculated medium control group did not produce any noticeable lesions. The above results reveal a reduction in the toxicity of SMs in the ΔVmPKS5 strain. 

To identify the specific toxin synthesis regulated by VmPKS5, we employed HPLC analysis on the SMs of the wild type, ΔVmPKS5 and ΔVmPKS5-C strains. At 22.2-min, the ΔVmPKS5 exhibited a complete loss of the substance peak compared to the 03-8, whereas the ΔVmPKS5-C strain restored the production of this peak (Figures 3e and S3a–d). The pure substance corresponding to this peak was obtained through rigorous fermentation, isolation, and purification processes (Figure S3e). Electrospray ionisation mass spectrometry (ESI-MS) analysis indicated its molecular formula as ${ \mathrm { C } } _ { 1 1 } { \mathrm { H } } _ { 1 2 } { \mathrm { O } } _ { 3 }$ ([M-H]− m/z 191) (Figure  S4). Furthermore, by combining $^ 1 \mathrm { H }$ -NMR and $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } \cdot$ - NMR detection spectral data (Figures  S5, S6), the detailed analysis results are as follows: $^ 1 \mathrm { H }$ -NMR (MeOD, 400 MHz) $\delta _ { \mathrm { H } }$ : 7.60 (d, $J { = } 1 5 . 9 \mathrm { H z }$ , 1H, H-5), 7.48–7.40 (m, 2H, H-3, H-4), 6.84–6.77 (m, 2H, H-1, H-2), 6.31 (d, $J { = } 1 6 . 0 \mathrm { H z }$ , 1H, H-6), 4.21 (q, $J { = } 7 . 1 \mathrm { H z }$ , 2H, H-7), 1.31 (t, $J { = } 7 . 1 \mathrm { H z }$ , 3H, H-8), its structure was determined as depicted in Figure 3f. The compound, consistent with previous reports, was identified from Camellia pollen and named $p$ -coumaric acid ethyl ester $\cdot ^ { p }$ -CAEE) (Li et  al. 2020). The above results indicated that VmPKS5 is required for the production of $p$ -CAEE.

# 2.5 | Heterologous Expression of VmPKS5 in V. pyri Enhanced the Virulence of the Pathogen Towards Apple

To elucidate the mechanism by which VmPKS5 modulates host preference, VmPKS5 was heterologously expressed in the 

V. pyri wild-type strain 134, resulting in the generation of the OE:Vp-VmPKS5 strain that overexpresses VmPKS5 (Figure S1f). Compared to the wild-type 134 strain, the OE:Vp-VmPKS5 strain exhibited no significant differences in vegetative growth (Figure S7). However, virulence assessments demonstrated that, compared to the wild-type 134 strain, the $O E : V p$ -VmPKS5 strain caused an increase of approximately $7 7 . 3 \%$ in lesion length on apple twigs 3days post-inoculation (Figure 5a,b) and an increase of approximately $1 2 2 . 3 \%$ in lesion area on apple tissue culture seedling leaves 36h post-inoculation (Figure 5d,e). Furthermore, the fungal biomass of the OE:Vp-VmPKS5 strain was significantly greater than that of the control (Figure 5c,f). These results indicate that VmPKS5 enhances the pathogenicity of V. pyri towards apple.

# 2.7 | dsRNA Targeting VmPKS5 Reduces V. mali Infection

It has been established that SIGS technology is effective for the management of a wide range of fungal diseases (Qiao et al. 2021; Ouyang et al. 2024). To assess the potential application of SIGS technology for controlling AVC, we initially synthesised GFPdsRNA labelled with fluorescein (Figure  S9a). The mycelia of V. mali were then treated with this fluorescein-labelled GFPdsRNA. Before microscopic analysis, MNase treatment was administered 30 min beforehand to eliminate any labelled dsRNA located outside the fungal mycelia. Notably, a prominent fluorescence signal was detected in the mycelia of V. mali (Figure 7a). To further confirm that the dsRNA entered the cytoplasm of the fungal cells, the fluorescein-labelled dsRNA was added to the liquid culture of V. mali mycelium, which was subjected to protoplast preparation after culturing for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . Fluorescence signals were clearly observed within V. mali protoplasts after MNase treatment (Figure 7b). 

VmPKS5-dsRNA was designed to target the VmPKS5 gene (Figure S9b). Exogenous application of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of a $5 0 \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ solution of both GFP-dsRNA and VmPKS5-dsRNA was carried out by dripping the solutions onto wounds created on apple twigs or leaves. Following this, wild-type 03-8 mycelial plugs of V. mali were inoculated at the precise locations where the dsRNA had been applied on twigs and leaves (Figure  7c,g). Pathogenicity assessments revealed that co-inoculation of wild-type 03-8 with VmPKS5-dsRNA led to the development of smaller lesion areas compared to co-inoculation with GFP-dsRNA (Figure  7d,h). 

Fungal biomass quantification assays indicated that the biomass of V. mali was significantly reduced after exogenous application of VmPKS5-dsRNA as compared with GFP-dsRNA treatment (Figure  7e,i). Furthermore, RT-qPCR analysis confirmed that the expression level of VmPKS5 was markedly decreased following exogenous application of VmPKS5-dsRNA as compared with GFP-dsRNA (Figure 7f,j). In conclusion, exogenous application of VmPKS5-dsRNA effectively inhibited the expression of VmPKS5, resulting in reduced fungal biomass and attenuated virulence of V. mali.

Conversely, in B. cinerea, a PKS13 deletion mutant demonstrates an increased hyphal growth rate and significantly enhanced pathogenicity towards its host (Zhang et al. 2015). In V. mali, knockout of VmPKS5 significantly reduced the virulence of the pathogen, limiting disease progression primarily to the inoculation site, indicating that VmPKS5 is a crucial virulence factor for V. mali. 

Pathogenic fungi typically secrete large amounts of toxins to rapidly kill host cells and establish colonisation in the early stages of infection (Doehlemann et al. 2017). For example, the metabolite deoxynivalenol produced by F. graminearum (Kazan et  al.  2012), the dihydroxynaphthalene melanin produced by Magnaporthe oryzae (Howard and Valent 1996), the polyketide toxin from the Curvularia sp. strain M5HF2C (Lv and Xue 1996; Liu et  al.  2009), and fusaric acid produced by Fusarium oxysporum (Notz et  al.  2002; Liu et  al.  2019) are all key weapons of plant-pathogenic fungi, typically synthesised by SMBGCs. Electron microscopy observation of apple bark tissues infected with V. mali hyphae revealed severe tissue maceration and cell necrosis, suggesting that toxins may be involved in the early stages of infection in V. mali (Ke et al. 2013). Several toxins of V. mali have been identified (Koganezawa and Sakuma  1982; Natsume et al. 1982; Okuno et al. 1986); however, the biosynthetic pathways of these toxin compounds remain unclear. In this study, deletion of VmPKS5 resulted in a complete loss of $p$ - CAEE synthesis, indicating that VmPKS5 regulates its synthesis. A linear non-ribosomal octapeptide from F. graminearum has been found to promote intercellular invasion in wheat cells (Jia et  al.  2019). Interestingly, it was observed that $p$ -CAEE could partly restore the pathogenicity of the ΔVmPKS5 strain and play a crucial role in the virulence of V. mali during infection. Previous studies have indicated that $p$ -CAEE, identified from Camellia pollen, exhibits inhibitory effects on tyrosinase (Li et  al. 2020; Varela et  al.  2020). However, V. mali also produces this phytotoxic compound. Given that both plants and fungi generate this compound detrimental to plants, it suggests that the presence of $p$ -CAEE may be a result of coevolution between plants and pathogens over an extended evolutionary period. The phytotoxicity of $p$ -CAEE could potentially be exploited by fungi as a virulence factor, while plants may possess the ability to tolerate or detoxify it as part of their resistance strategies. Additionally, $p$ -CAEE may also serve as a medium for niche competition between plants and pathogens. Phytopathogenic toxins function through dual mechanisms of disrupting cellular integrity and interfering with critical physiological processes in host plants. Identifying the precise molecular targets of these phytotoxins within plant systems lays the groundwork for developing targeted countermeasures, including molecular decoy engineering and enzymatic detoxification strategies. In the future, it may be possible to design $p$ -CAEE-specific inhibitors or host-induced gene silencing constructs, providing a new control paradigm for the management of AVC. 

Evolutionary changes at the genomic level, such as chromosomal rearrangements, expansion and contraction of gene families and specific gene expression patterns, determine infection preferences. For example, the human-pathogenic bacteria Clostridium difficile and C. sordellii are both gram-positive bacteria with a broad host range and lethal infectivity. However, comparative genomic analysis reveals that differences in carbon 

and nitrogen source utilisation and adaptation to low pH contribute to C. difficile primarily infecting the gut, while C. sordellii mainly infects soft tissues (Scaria et al. 2015). The PKS5 gene cluster in V. mali comprises two genes, VmPKS5 and VmPKS6. However, the genome of V. pyri lacks a homologue of VmPKS5, indicating significant structural divergence of the PKS5 cluster between V. mali and V. pyri (Yin et al. 2015). Notably, the most significant variation in this gene cluster between V. mali and V. pyri is the absence of a SMBGC core gene. Furthermore, this gene cluster was significantly upregulated during the infection stage of the pathogen. Notably, deletion of VmPKS5 markedly reduced the virulence of V. mali towards apple but not pear, establishing VmPKS5 as a determinant of host preference. Previous studies have shown that the peach tree canker pathogens Leucostoma persoonii and L. cincta can produce small peptide toxins to determine their host preferences (Svircev et al. 1991). In this study, heterologous expression of VmPKS5 in V. pyri activated the pathogen's capacity for $p$ -CAEE biosynthesis, thereby augmenting its virulence against apple trees. This also demonstrates that $p$ -CAEE determines the host preference of V. mali. Fungal species within the genus Valsa biosynthesise diverse phytotoxins. The SM repertoire of V. pyri lacks $p$ -CAEE production but contains targeted metabolites for pear, indicating that the evolutionary divergence of SMBGCs drives host preference. The genus Valsa likely acquired the ability to synthesise specific SMs through the rapid evolution of SMBGCs. These metabolites can exert specific physiological effects on their hosts, thereby enhancing the survival and reproduction capabilities of Valsa species on their hosts. Furthermore, the adaptive evolution of SMBGCs may involve various genetic mechanisms, such as horizontal gene transfer, gene mutations and gene recombination. These mechanisms work together to enable Valsa species to rapidly adjust their metabolite profiles under different environmental and host conditions, allowing them to adapt to new ecological niches and host preferences. 

RNA-based disease management products offer an important ecofriendly alternative to standard chemical pesticides for controlling agricultural losses caused by pests and disease (Taning et al. 2020). SIGS is effective in controlling grey mould disease caused by B. cinerea (Cai et al. 2018; Wang and Jin 2017; Wang et  al.  2016), Fusarium head blight caused by F. graminearum (Koch et al. 2016), and white mould caused by Sclerotinia sclerotiorum (McLoughlin et  al.  2018). SIGS for disease control is dependent on the efficiency of pathogen RNA uptake (Qiao et al. 2021). V. mali hyphae and protoplasts can efficiently uptake dsRNA, indicating that SIGS technology could be applied for the control and prevention of AVC. It has been established that VmPKS5 is a key regulator of host preference in V. mali, holding potential for the development of specific fungicides that inhibit the infection process of V. mali without affecting other organisms. This study also demonstrates that exogenous application of VmPKS5-dsRNA can attenuate the pathogenicity of V. mali and reduce fungal biomass. SIGS is a promising tool for disease control (Wang and Jin 2017). However, naked and unmodified dsRNA exhibits rapid enzymatic degradation in planta, significantly compromising RNA interference (RNAi) efficacy. This instability poses a critical challenge for dsRNA-based applications in plant protection. Future research must focus on developing effective stabilisation strategies, including but not limited to chemical modification, nanocarrier encapsulation, 

or symbiotic microbe-mediated delivery systems, to enhance dsRNA persistence under field conditions. 

Overall, this study reveals that VmPKS5 regulates the synthesis of $p$ -CAEE to mediate the host preference of V. mali, and dsRNA designed against VmPKS5 significantly attenuates the pathogenicity of V. mali. These findings provide new insights into understanding the host preference of V. mali and offer new ideas and technological reserves for the green control of AVC. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Strains, Media, and Culture Conditions

The wild-type strains of V. mali 03-8 and V. pyri 134 strains were obtained from the stock culture of State Key Laboratory for Crop Stress Resistance and High-Efficiency Production, College of Plant Protection, Northwest A&F University, Yangling, Shaanxi, China. All the strains used in this study were grown at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on PDA $2 0 \%$ potato extract, $2 \%$ glucose, and $1 . 5 \%$ agar) for mycelial growth assays. Escherichia coli DH5a, used for plasmid construction, was cultured in lysogeny broth (LB) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . All strains were cultivated in ABB (apple bark broth) medium $( 1 5 \%$ apple bark extract, $0 . 2 \%$ yeast extract powder, $0 . 2 \%$ sucrose) to induce toxin production. 

# 4.2 | RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR Detection

Mycelial plugs (5 mm diameter) of 03-8 were inoculated onto detached twigs of Malus $\times$ domestica ‘Fuji’ as described (Wei et  al.  2010). Samples were collected at specific time points: 3-day-old 03-8 mycelium from axenic culture, and material from the junction of healthy and infected apple bark tissue inoculated with V. mali for 12 and 72 h. The total RNA was extracted using an RNA kit (Tiangen) following the manufacturer's instructions. RNA purity, concentration, and integrity were assessed, and RNA samples were reverse transcribed into cDNA using the Revert Aid First-strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Thermo Scientific). G6PDH from V. mali was used as the internal reference gene. Relative expression levels were determined using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). The experiments were repeated three times. 

# 4.3 | Targeted Gene Deletion, Overexpression, and Complementation

The gene deletion cassettes were assembled using double-joint PCR, incorporating upstream and downstream flanking sequences of the target gene and the NEO gene as the selective marker (Yu et al. 2004). These cassettes were subsequently transformed into protoplasts of 03-8 using a polyethylene glycol (PEG)- mediated transformation method (Gao et  al.  2011). Putative deletion mutants, selected with geneticin, were confirmed through PCR. Gene complementation was conducted by cloning the target genes into plasmid pDL2, using the ClonExpress II One-Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme Biotech), followed by transforming the recombinant plasmids into the respective target gene deletion mutants (Bruno et al. 2004; Zhou et al. 2012). The 

complemented mutants were confirmed via PCR. For the overexpression of VmPKS5, the PKS5 gene sequence was amplified from 03-8 cDNA using Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerase (New England Biolabs) and subsequently introduced into the plasmid pDL2 with the RP27 promoter. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into the 134 strain, and the transformation was confirmed by PCR. A list of all primers used in gene deletion and complementation is provided in Table S1. 

# 4.4 | Virulence Assays and Fungal Biomass Assays

The virulence assays were conducted by inoculating strains onto detached leaves and twigs of apple or pear trees. For SIGS assays, $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 0 \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ GFP-dsRNA and VmPKS5-dsRNA were each applied topically to wounds on apple twigs and leaves. Then, the wild-type 03-8 mycelial plugs were inoculated at the same locations where the dsRNA had been applied to assess the control efficacy of VmPKS5-dsRNA. The inoculated twigs and leaves were then cultivated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days and $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ , respectively. Subsequently, lesion measurements and data analysis were performed (Wei et al. 2010). Each experiment was replicated at least three times and no less than three technical replicates to ensure reliability. 

Fungal biomass was measured using qPCR with V. malispecific G6PDH primers or $V .$ pyri-specific actin primers at 3 days post-inoculation (Xu et al. 2020; Zhu et al. 2024). Genomic DNA was isolated using the Super Plant Genomic DNA Kit (Polysaccharides and Polyphenolics-rich; Tiangen) from $0 . 4 \mathrm { g }$ apple twig bark and leaf tissues, including all infected tissue and additional healthy tissue, following the manufacturer's instructions. The biomass assay was independently performed three times, each time with three technical replicates. 

# 4.5 | Production, Purification, and Characterisation of $\pmb { p }$ -CAEE

Strains were cultivated in ABB medium and cultivated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 110 rpm in a shaking incubator for 10 days. The SMs were extracted three times using ethyl acetate and then evaporated under reduced pressure. The obtained extracts were dissolved in 1 mL of methanol and subsequently analysed using an HPLC system (1260 Infinity II; Agilent) equipped with a Diamonsil C18 column $\cdot 1 5 0 \times 4 . 6 \mathrm { m m }$ , DIKMA, $5 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ). For the analysis of SMs in all strains, a solvent system consisting of acetonitrile (A) and water containing $0 . 1 \%$ (vol/vol) acetic acid (B) was used. The extract was eluted using a linear gradient from $3 \%$ to $6 0 \%$ (vol/vol) A over 25 min and $6 0 \%$ to $3 \%$ (vol/vol) over 7 min, at a flow rate of $0 . 8 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The detection wavelength used was $2 7 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . The raw liquid chromatography data was imported into Origin2021 software for replotting. 

The fermentation broth was adsorbed using XAD-16 macroporous resin and eluted with a mixture of petroleum ether and ethyl acetate in a 1:9 ratio. The crude extract was obtained using an evaporator. This crude sample was dissolved in methanol and further purified by HPLC on a Phenomenex Luna C18 column $( 2 5 0 \times 1 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ) for semipreparative purposes to obtain fractions containing the target compound. The NMR data 

of $p$ -CAEE were recorded on a Bruker Advance III (400 MHz) spectrometer. The molecular weight of $p$ -CAEE was determined by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (Qtrap5500; AB SCIEX). 

# 4.6 | Toxicity Tests

All compounds were dissolved in a $1 0 \%$ methanol solution to a concentration of $\mathrm { 1 m g / m L }$ and tested on apple leaves and twigs using the leaf puncture method. Samples treated with $1 0 \%$ methanol served as controls, and symptoms were carefully examined for $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ . Photographs of the marked areas were taken $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ posttreatment. The area of necrotic lesions was calculated using ImageJ software, with at least three biological replicates of necrotic lesions used in the statistical analysis to ensure accuracy. 

# 4.7 | Transmission Electron Microscopic Observation

The wild-type 03-8 and ΔVmPKS5 strains were separately inoculated onto apple leaves in combination with $1 0 \%$ methanol and $p$ -CAEE. The interfaces between infected and healthy tissues were meticulously collected for subsequent examination using an optical microscope and a transmission electron microscope. At $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation, preparation of semithin sections and ultrathin sections was carried out as described previously (Kang and Buchenauer 2000). 

# 4.8 | In Vitro Synthesis of dsRNA

Primers for transcription templates containing the T7 promoter $5 ^ { \prime }$ -TAATACGACTCACTATAGG-3') were synthesised. Following PCR, gel extraction was performed to obtain transcription template 1 and transcription template 2, which harboured the T7 promoter at their $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ ends, respectively. In  vitro transcription of dsRNA was conducted according to the instructions provided in the T7 RNAi Transcription Kit (Vazyme Biotech Co. Ltd). 

# 4.9 | V. mali Uptake of Fluorescein-Labelled dsRNA In Vitro

Fluorescently labelled dsRNA was synthesised following the guidelines outlined in the Fluorescein RNA Labeling Mix manual (Roche). V. mali mycelium plugs (5 mm diameter) were placed on slides containing PDA and $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 0 \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ fluorescent dsRNA was added to the fungi, and incubated for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ before confocal imaging. All fungal growth slides were cultured in darkness at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Fluorescent dsRNA was added to the YEPD liquid medium $( 1 \%$ yeast extract, $2 \%$ glucose, and $1 \%$ tryptone) containing V. mali mycelia. After being cultured for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , the mycelia were collected and protoplasts were prepared. Mycelium and protoplasts were treated with 75 U micrococcal nuclease (MNase) to degrade the dsRNA in the surface of mycelium and protoplasts before observation at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The fluorescent signals were analysed using an LSM880 confocal microscope. 

# 4.10 | Statistical Analyses

All results are shown as mean values $\pm$ standard deviations from three independent experiments. The statistical significance among different treatments was determined using the Tukey's honest significant difference (HSD) test at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ .
