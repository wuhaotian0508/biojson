# ABSTRACT

The combination of mutation-based genetics and functional genomics has allowed a detailed dissection of the nodulation-induction and autoregulation of nodulation (AON) pathways of soybean. Applicable to all legumes, nodulation is induced by Rhizobium/Bradyrhizobium-produced lipopolysaccharides (Nod factors), perceived by Nod factor receptors (NFR1/NFR5 dimers), leading to cortical and pericycle cell divisions. These induce the production of CLAVATA3-like (CLE) peptides, which travel in the xylem to the shoot, where they are perceived by a receptor complex including a leucine-rich repeat (LRR) receptor kinase, encoded by GmNARK, LjHAR1, MtSUNN and closely related receptors in other legumes like Phaseolus vulgaris (common bean), Pisum sativum (pea), and

Glycine soja. The activated receptor complex negatively regulates by phosphorylation of the constitutive synthesis of miR2111 in the shoot. This is normally is translocated via the phloem to the entire plant body, initiating suppression of a root-expressed Kelch repeat-containing F-box protein "Too Much Love (TML)," which in turn suppresses the nodule initiation cascade. Nodulation is therefore permitted during a developmental window between the induction and progress of the nodulation/cell division/infection cascade during the first few days after inoculation and the functional "readiness" of the AON cascade, delayed by the root-shoot-root loop. Loss-of-function mutations in GmNARK and LjTML result in excessive nodulation (supernodulation/hypernodulation/supernummary nodulation) as well as localized tolerance to externally applied nitrate. Recent analyses have indicated an interaction of the AON with lateral root formation as well as with the autoregulation of mycorrhization (AOM). Further details of the parallel functions of key points in this regulatory loop remain to be elucidated.

Keywords: autoregulation of nodulation, legumes, soybean, symbiotic nodulation

# INTRODUCTION

Things in "Life," and therefore "Science," often develop unexpectedly, but also because of long-term effort

combining ideas, technologies, insights and indeed some luck. One example is the analysis of early legume nodulation leading to agriculturally and environmentally important nitrogen fixation, and its control by both internal and external

mechanisms. One regulatory process is by the common soil nutrient nitrate, which normally makes the essential element nitrogen available for plant growth and productivity; the other involves a complex internal control circuit, called the autoregulation of nodulation (AON; Gresshoff and Delves, 1986; Caetano-Anolles and Gresshoff, 1991a, 1991b), which now has been dissected by a combination of plant mutation genetics, molecular physiology, and functional genomics.

We now have four decades stretching from a simple analysis of that plant physiological response (Carroll and Gresshoff, 1983) to a cutting-edge insight into a complex, interorgan communication involving receptor proteins, transcription factors, peptides and microRNAs (miRNAs) as revealed by functional genomics and molecular physiology (Reid et al., 2011b; Ferguson et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Su et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2024). Our success is due to the continued application of progressively precise technologies, international collaboration, and a decade-long continuum of talented researchers.

# THE PROCESSES TO BE ADDRESS: MULTIPLE LEGUME NODULATION CONTROL

# Nitrate inhibition of nodulation

Nitrate $\left(\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}\right)$ is a common source of the nitrogen (N) element needed for the growth of plants (Ye et al., 2022). Such plant nitrogen is mainly found in polypeptides (i.e., enzymes, peptides, structural, storage and regulatory proteins), nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) and their nucleotide components, and metabolites of various use (Krapp, 2015). Nitrate is actively taken up by plants, before the subsequent reduction by nitrate and nitrite reductases to ammonium and further assimilation into glutamine, which serves as a central source for biologically available nitrogen in all relevant steps of metabolism (Krapp, 2015; Zayed et al., 2023).

In addition to its function as a basic nutrient in plants, nitrate also has developmental signal properties, often at concentrations below those of the normal nutrient level (Courrèges-Clercq and Krouk, 2022). For example, Arabidopsis NIN-like protein 7 (NLP7) transcription factor was shown to act as a nitrate sensor for visualizing nitrate dynamics in plant single cells (Liu et al., 2022). Similarly, legume plants, capable of symbiotic nitrogen fixation via nodule development induced by soil bacteria broadly called "Rhizobium," regulate the symbiosis by external nitrate (Carroll and Gresshoff, 1983; Carroll et al., 1985a). Therefore, nodule induction (reflected in nodule number per plant), nodule growth (measured as nodule dry mass per nodule or per plant), and nodule symbiotic function (quantified by fixing atmospheric nitrogen gas $(\mathsf{N}_2)$ to form ammonia $(\mathsf{NH}_3)$ ) is negatively controlled by the nitrate ion. It has also been reported that nitrate status (endogenous and fixed nitrogen) regulates the ability of legumes to induce flowering (Yun et al., 2023).

Physiological, developmental and anatomical responses to nitrate have been published for a large number of legumes over the last 100 years. It was clear that legumes make a choice for nitrogen acquisition, either by root-nodule symbiosis or uptake of external sources (such as soil or nutrient solution). While nitrate is the most common and stable form of nitrogen-containing molecules in soil and water, urea (CO $(\mathrm{NH}_2)_2$ ), and ammonium $(\mathrm{NH}_4^+)$ are agricultural alternatives as fertilizers. Plants possess uptake systems for direct utilization of these resources, although in most agricultural circumstances, these alternative N sources are converted by soil microbes to nitrate prior to plant uptake.

Nitrate reduction, via the nitrate reductase enzyme complex found in almost all plants, can occur in either root or shoot, or both. The resultant product, nitrite $(\mathrm{NO}_2^-)$ , is quickly converted by the enzyme nitrite reductase to form ammonia. In aqueous solution (such as the plant cytoplasm) this is in the form of the cation ammonium $(\mathrm{NH}_4^+)$ .

The genetic and molecular makeup of these regulatory systems, critical for plant growth and development, has been the subject of research for the last half-century. This article will not go further into any detail of the physiology and biochemistry of nitrate utilization. However, it will focus on (i) the analysis of nitrate effects on nodulation as related to the internal process called "autoregulation of nodulation," and (ii) its molecular physiological and functional genomic dissection, for which the critical components such as receptors, signals, and reaction cascades are now known through mutation and functional genomics studies.

# Autoregulation of nodulation

Legume plants develop root nodules after stimulation from signals derived from bacterial symbionts such as Rhizobium and Bradyrhizobium (and a few other) species. The interaction leads to a nitrogen-fixing symbiosis, which is important globally in both economic and environmental terms. The amounts of nitrogen fixed by soybean can be up to $488\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{Nha}^{-1}$ annum $^{-1}$ (Rochester et al., 2001). An excellent up-to-date analysis of nitrogen inputs by agricultural legumes is given by Herridge et al. (2022).

This symbiosis is regulated by the number and mass of nodules developing after inoculation through a process called "autoregulation of nodulation (AON)" (Caetano-Anollés and Gresshoff, 1991b), also called "feedback regulation of nodulation." It was discovered by Bhuvaneswari et al. (1981), who demonstrated that prior inoculation of soybean roots inhibited further nodulation in lower root portions (i.e., that developed later). Similarly, Kosslak and Bohlool (1984) as well as Olsson et al. (1989) showed that AON in soybean works systemically, so that one side of a physically separated split root system communicates the AON inhibition signal to the other side of the root system. Thus, the shoot is involved!

The inhibition of nodulation by nitrate led Carroll and colleagues (Carroll et al., 1985a, 1985b) to use chemical mutagenesis of soybean cultivar Bragg with ethyl methyl-sulfonate (EMS) to isolate homozygous recessive mutants

with substantially (up to 20-fold) increased nodule number per plant in the presence of potassium nitrate $(5\mathrm{mM})$ . Unexpectedly, the zero nitrate control treatments also had increased nodule number, leading to the concept of "autoregulation of nodulation." These mutants nodulated more in the presence of nitrate, because they nodulated more per se! Increased nodulation in the presence of nitrate came from an absence of a naturally functional regulatory system controlling extended nodulation in root regions normally regulated by AON. This was confirmed in split root systems of soybean (Olsson et al., 1989). Olsson and colleagues showed that, in split roots of wild-type soybean Bragg, prior inoculation of one side reduced nodule number in the other, physically separated, root portion. If the experiment was done with the recessive nts382 (GmNARK; supernodulating) mutant, prior inoculation of one side did not have any effect on nodule number in the second half. Interestingly, when split roots were used with nitrate inoculation, Carroll and Gresshoff (1983) had already found that nitrate inhibited nodulation in a localized fashion, that is, exposure of one root portion to nodule number inhibitory levels of nitrate did not inhibit nodule formation in the other (unexposed) root. Furthermore, they discovered that, although nodule induction and subsequent symbiotic nitrogen fixation are both regulated by external nitrate, their regulation was completely independent.

The chemical mutagenesis of soybean (genotype Bragg) involved the treatment of some 50,000 wild-type seeds, followed by raising the treated M1 survivors, and their single plant harvest (Carroll et al., 1985a). These M2 seeds were screened in pot trials for the "nodulation tolerant to nitrate" phenotype. Separately detected M2 families derived from separate M1 plants, and therefore seeds, meant that independent mutant lines were detected. Multiple supernodulating and hypernodulating mutants were isolated, which turned out to be allelic as studied by complementation analysis (Delves et al., 1988). Parallel studies were done using fast neutron mutagenesis in collaboration with the IAEA research laboratory at Seibersdorf (Austria). The smaller seeded G. soja was used because the treatment chamber was small, allowing a limited sample size. The M2 selection resulted in a stable supernodulation mutant, called FN5, shown to be deleted for the large region around and including GmNARK (Men et al., 2002).

The same chemical mutagenesis also produced soybean non-nodulation mutants (Carroll et al., 1986), which were later defined as being in the two Nod factor receptor genes GmNFR1 (GmNod49) and GmNFR5 (GmNod139) (Indrasumunar et al., 2010, 2011). An interesting aspect of cloning soybean genes was that, if mutated, they act recessively, came from the fact that the allotetraploid soybean genome is largely duplicated (Schmutz et al., 2010). For example, GmSymRK has two nearly identical copies $(96\%)$ in the genome. Therefore a mutant in any one of the genes is very likely to have a phenotype (Indrasumunar et al., 2015). However, the use of RNA interference (RNAi) targeting both copies resulted in clear phenotypes of nodulation and

mycorrhizal associations, confirming the mutational phenotype of SymRK found in L. japonicus and M. truncatula (Endré et al., 2002; Stracke et al., 2002). Thus, duplicate copies of the nodulation-facilitating genes, and also the GmNARK gene controlling AON, were discovered.

Both duplicate genes for GmNFR1 and GmNFR5 were inactive because of internal mutations. The duplicated GmNARK gene was called GmCLV1A, as it has the highest sequence homology to the CIAVATA1 (CLV1) gene of Arabidopsis thaliana (Yamamoto et al., 2000). It is characterized by an 18-nucleotide deletion relative to GmNARK. The phenotype of the GmCLV1A mutant was investigated (Mirzaei et al., 2017) and found that it was altered in the stem, pod and fruiting morphology and patterning. For example, opposing compared with alternate trifoliate leaves and highly abnormal seed pods were observed (Figure 1). Mutations in such genes do not result in supernodulation (as the duplicated GmNARK gene exists in a functionally dominant configuration) but result in severe developmental/structural changes (Mirzaei et al., 2017). It appears that, in soybean, the ancestral CLV1 gene involved in broad architectural symmetry and structure was duplicated to yield two copies, namely GmCLV1A and GmCLV1B, the former maintaining the ancestral architectural function, while the duplicated version became specialized in AON. The question remains as to what had happened in the other legumes, as species like L. japonicus are true diploids and appear to possess only one CLV1 gene copy that, when mutated, resulted in hypernodulation and reduced root growth (the HAR1 phenotype) (Wopereis et al., 2000; Krusell et al., 2002; Nishimura et al., 2002). Indeed, L. japonicus plants grown symbiotically on agar plates were very "crippled," but recovered vegetative growth only when fed nitrate.

Similarly, the Bragg M2 seed population was screened by multiple-well plate assay for alterations in enzymatic activity for constitutive nitrate reductase (Carroll et al., 1987), illustrating that EMS mutation analysis of soybean, although showing a predominantly duplicated and re-arranged genome, is possible for both developmental and biochemical traits.

Extensive growth studies revealed that such AON mutants tended to grow slower than their wild-type segregants or wild-type Bragg (Day et al., 1986). All mutant lines were able to utilize nitrate, were able to fix nitrogen in the developed nodules, grew more slowly (Day et al., 1986) and flowered approximately 4-7 d earlier (Gresshoff, P.M., unpublished results). Cytological analysis of the roots revealed that an early nodule cell division stage (approximately 3 d after inoculation) was negatively regulated by functional AON, but absent or weakened in the mutant lines (Calvert et al., 1984; Mathews et al., 1989). Additionally, it was noted that seedlings of nts382 (7 d after inoculation) showed an increased number of lateral roots, while more mature plants (4 weeks after inoculation) had decreased lateral root numbers. These observations suggest a pleiotropic role for GmNARK in soybean growth and development.

Delves et al. (1986, 1987) demonstrated using reciprocal grafts of nts-mutant and wild-type that the presence of AON required the shoot portion of the graft to be of the non-mutant (wild-type) nature. In other words, the gene mutated to give what was first called supernodulation in soybean or hypernodulation in the model legume L. japonicus (Wopereis et al., 2000; Krusell et al., 2002) or supernummary nodule number in the temperate model legume M. truncatula (Schnabel et al., 2005), which acted in the shoot, receiving some, then unknown, systemic signal from the early stages of nodule development in the root cortex/pericycle. The shoot then transmitted a signal to the entire root system, dictating a cessation of further nodule development. This model was proposed by Gresshoff and Delves (1986), even then including the parallel involvement of the system controlling lateral root growth as the AON circuit was based on a pre-existing analog system involving the root tip and its meristematic primordium.

Major questions arose quickly: (i) What is the mutated gene? (ii) What is its function and regulation? (iii) What are the

"upward" and "downward" signals? and (iv) How does the AON circuit fit developmentally into the known regulation of lateral root growth? Although at present (2025), with billions of DNA data points, sophisticated amplification techniques, and a strong input from the model legume species L. japonicus and M. truncatula, our insight has expanded, indeed "closing the circuit" in broad terms, but leaving critical steps totally unresolved, i.e., How does GmNARK phosphorylation control synthesis of the shoot-to-root signals? What are the intermediate targets (transcription factors)? How is lateral root and nodule formation inversely connected? Is it through genetic action(s), hormonal control (e.g., auxin and cytokinin allocation), or a combination of both?

# FUNCTIONAL GENOMICS AS A TOOL FOR AON MUTANT GENE DISCOVERY

Two questions became clear quickly: (i) What was the gene that was mutated? and (ii) What were the signals exchanged between root and shoot allowing the AON regulation?

Positional cloning, or what was called "map-based cloning" was chosen to attempt the isolation and characterization of the mutated soybean gene. The Shoemaker laboratory in Iowa (USA) (Keim et al., 1990) developed the first molecular map of soybean using RFLP markers. Representative marker clones were provided to our laboratory to determine any possible linkage and therefore the preliminary linkage position. Landau-Ellis et al. (1991) found that RFLP marker pA132 (or more precisely its component region pA132a) was closely linked (0.3 centiMorgans in a small sample segregant population). Further markers were developed by arbitrary primed PCR (DAF; Caetano-Anollés et al., 1991) and DNA silver staining in PAGE gels (Bassam et al., 1991). This allowed the first discovery of an AON-associated gene in soybean (Searle et al., 2003; accepted 2002, but published January 2003), although the work was done concurrently and independently from that of L. japonicus (Krusell et al., 2002). The homologous genes from the model legumes L. japonicus (LjHAR1; Krusell et al., 2002) and M. truncatula (MtSUNN; Schnabel et al., 2005) were isolated and shown to be identical leucine-rich repeat (LRR) receptor kinases with identical function in the shoot and the proposed long-distance circuit. Identical circuitry was found in other legumes such as bean (Ferguson et al., 2014), pea (Sagan and Duc, 1996), and G. soja (Men et al., 2002), and is assumed to exist in all legumes.

Early phenotypic analysis of all mutants in either GmNARK, LjHAR1 or MtSUNN illustrated broadly common properties. First, their nodule number per plant fresh (or dry) weight is higher than the parent's weight; second, they all have reduced root growth (LjHAR1 mutants in its name have the mention of altered root growth), and third, all are somewhat resistant/ tolerant to the inhibitory effect of nitrate on nodule numbers. Mechanistically they are also identical. Namely all are mutated in the same LRR receptor kinase gene, all are

shoot-controlled, and all respond to the bacterial activation and subsequent root-to-shoot translocation of short peptides similar to the Arabidopsis CIAVATA3 (CLV3) peptide (Fletcher et al., 1999). One can suspect that control of root growth (e.g., lateral root primordia and their extension into lateral, then tertiary roots) developed on the ancestral forms of the herestated control of nodule number; hence it is likely that these developmental steps still exist and are seen in alterations of GmNARK and its homologs in seemingly all legumes.

The gene in soybean was identified to be highly structurally similar to an Arabidopsis thaliana gene controlling apical shoot architecture, namely CLV1, a gene encoding a leucine-rich receptor kinase. Multiple mutant alleles were sequenced and found to be mainly non-sense mutations leading to a major truncation of the gene product (Searle et al., 2003). Highly conserved genes and mutant alleles were found in other legumes such as L. japonicus (Wopereis et al., 2000; Krusell et al., 2002), G. soja (Men et al., 2002), Pisum sativum (Sagan and Duc, 1996), Phaseolus vulgaris (Ferguson et al., 2014) and M. truncatula (Schnabel et al., 2005) showing the supernodulation/hypernodulation phenotype. It is concluded that the legume CLV1 gene is commonly used in AON.

# WHAT IS THE ROOT-TO-SHOOT AON SIGNAL IN SOYBEAN?

Given that CLV1 encodes an LRR family protein, it probably serves as a receptor for signal transduction from root to shoot. If this hypothesis is correct, an important question emerges: What ligand interacts with CLV1? In Arabidopsis, CLV1 associates with CIAVATA2 (CLV2) to form a transmembrane receptor complex whose extracellular domain binds the 13-amino-acid CLV3 peptide (Guo et al., 2010). Beyond CLV3, other members of the CLE peptide family also bind directly to LRR-type receptor kinases (Fisher and Turner, 2007; Hirakawa et al., 2008). Therefore it is reasonable to hypothesize that CLE peptides are the probable ligands for GmNARK. Supporting this hypothesis, two independent studies identified CLE peptides specifically induced by rhizobia in L. japonicus (LjCLE-RS1/2; Okamoto et al., 2008) and M. truncatula (MtCLE12/13; Mortier et al., 2010). Overexpression of these rhizobia-induced CLE peptides significantly inhibited nodule formation systemically, indicating their role in AON. Later studies also identified two rhizobia-induced CLE (RIC) peptides in soybean (GmRIC1 and GmRIC2), which were shown to regulate nodulation systemically via GmNARK (Lim et al., 2011; Reid et al., 2011a, 2013). The expression of these CLE genes was antagonistically regulated by transcription factors NINs (Nodule Inceptions) (Soyano et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2019) and NNC1 (Nodule Number Control 1) (Wang et al., 2019). In addition, plant hormone gibberellin (GA) was found to promote the expression of LjCLE-RS1/2 via NIN in L. japonicus

(Akamatsu et al., 2021). Therefore the expression of these rhizobia-induced CLE genes in the roots is controlled by the nodulation signaling pathway in legumes.

Given that GmNARK functions in the shoots and that these CLE peptides are primarily produced in the roots in response to rhizobia, they are strong candidates for root-to-shoot signals in AON. Until 2013, xylem sap collection and nanoLC-MS/MS analysis provided evidence that the CLE peptide LjCLE-RS2 could indeed be transported from the root to the shoot via the xylem, and bind directly to the LjHAR1 receptor kinase (Okamoto et al., 2013). This peptide undergoes tri-arabinosylation, a modification essential for both xylem transport (Okamoto et al., 2013; Imin et al., 2018) and LjHAR1 binding (Okamoto et al., 2013). Given the sequence similarities, one can speculate that GmRIC1 and GmRIC2 may act as root-to-shoot signals, interacting similarly with the LRR domain of GmNARK in soybean.

# WHAT IS THE SHOOT-DERIVED SIGNAL IN SOYBEAN?

As shown above, legumes control their nodule numbers through the AON signaling pathway. Rhizobia infection stimulates the production of root-derived and tri-arabinosylated CLE peptides that are translocated via the xylem to the shoot where they regulate a new signal. Early models suggested the name "Shoot-derived Inhibitor" (SDI), which was incorrect as GmNARK produces a positive signal (phosphorylation; Miyahara et al., 2008), which inhibits an inhibitor (Figure 2). miRNA2111 (miR2111) has been illustrated as a shoot-derived signal in L. japonicus (Tsikou et al., 2018; Okuma et al., 2020), M. truncatula (Gautrat et al., 2020), and soybean (Zhang et al., 2021), which is transported via the phloem to the root, where it targets transcripts of the Too Much Love (TML) gene, a negative regulator of nodulation. Shoot perception of rhizobia-induced CLE peptides GmRIC1 and GmRIC2 suppresses miR2111 expression, resulting in TML accumulation in roots and subsequent inhibition of nodule organogenesis. Feeding synthetic mature miR2111 RNA via the petiole increased nodule numbers per plant (Zhang et al., 2021). Similarly, elevating miR2111 availability by overexpression promoted nodulation, while target mimicry of TML induced the opposite effect on nodule development in wild-type plants and alleviated the supernodulating and stunted root growth phenotypes of AON-defective mutants. Notably, miR2111 expression is upregulated under low nitrate conditions and is regulated by the shoot-localized receptor CRA2 (COMPACT ROOT ARCHITECTURE 2), an LRR-RLK that senses root-derived CEP peptides in M. truncatula (Gautrat et al., 2020). Overall, these findings demonstrated that miR2111 RNA is the critical shoot-to-root factor that positively regulates root nodule development in legumes. Despite great progress in identifying an AON receptor kinase-dependent signal in the shoot, a critical question remains as to how these AON receptor kinases, such as GmNARK, regulate the production of miR2111.

# Journal of Integrative Plant Biology

Plant hormone cytokinin has also been shown to play an important role in the AON pathway. In $L$ japonicus, the cytokinin synthesis gene $LjIPT3$ is activated in shoot in a LjHAR1-dependent manner to promote cytokinin synthesis, and cytokinin synthesized by shoots is subsequently transported over a long distance to the roots, where it inhibits nodulation in $L$ japonicus by suppressing the expression of NSP2 (Sasaki et al., 2014). However, cytokinins may function differently in soybean. A study by Mens et al. (2018) showed that, while high levels of cytokinin treatment reduced the number of nodules, treatment with low levels of cytokinin increased the number of nodules. Moreover, rhizobia induced the expression of the cytokinin biosynthesis gene $GmIPT5$ in shoots, but independent of the AON receptor $GmNARK$ . Further

Figure 2. The autoregulation of nodulation (AON) control circuit of soybean

Flavonoids secreted by legumes attract rhizobia and promote the secretion of their Nod factors (NFs). Subsequently, the Nod receptor complex binds to NFs and initiates the nodulation signaling pathway. NSP1/2 and NIN are key transcription factors that mediate nodulation signaling. GmNIN is Nodule Inception, an essential activator of nodulation signaling, including miR172c. miR172c inhibits the expression of its target gene NNC1, which in turn suppresses the expression of GmENOD40, a gene essential for nodulation. NNC1 can directly inhibit the expression of GmRlC1/2 and also interacts with GmNIN to inhibit the activation of GmRlC1/2 by GmNIN. The CLE peptides (GmRlC1/2) induced by rhizobia are root-to-shoot signals that are sensed by the AON receptor GmNARK located in the shoots. Subsequently, GmNARK, together with its co-receptors, generate signals to regulate nodulation from the shoots to the roots. These include inhibition of miR2111 synthesis, thereby releasing the inhibition of GmTML by shoot-to-root miR2111, and the activation of GmRR11d expression in root through an unknown signal. GmRR11d responds to local cytokinins and inhibits nodulation by repressing the transcriptional activity of GmNSP1 on GmNIN or directly repressing GmNIN expression. High soil nitrate can also induce the expression of GmNIC1/2, which locally inhibit nodulation via GmNARK.

Further evidence emerges of parallel regulation. Nodulation control and cytokinin sensitivity are clearly linked. Yun et al. (2022) showed that the miR156b-GmSPL9d (SQUAMOSA promoter-binding protein-like 9d) module regulates symbiotic nodulation by targeting multiple key regulatory genes in the nodulation signaling pathway of soybean. miR156b is down-regulated by rhizobia and negatively regulates nodulation by mainly targeting GmSPL9d, a positive regulator of soybean nodulation, which binds to the promoters of GmNINa, miR172c and GmENOD40-1, and regulates their expression. However, it is unclear whether miR156b is regulated by GmNARK and whether there is a shoot-derived signal (e.g., cytokinin) that mediates its function.

In summary, there is evidence that microRNAs and phytohormones can act as shoot-to-root signals to regulate nodulation but, to date, it is not clear exactly what SDI is, and different legumes may have their own specific regulatory mechanisms in addition to conserved ones.

# AUTOREGULATION OF NODULATIN-RELATED PROCESS

# Autoregulation of nodulation and nitrate-mediated nodulation inhibition

In addition to rhizobia-induced CLE peptides, a nitrate-induced CLE peptide gene, GmNIC1, has been identified as specifically involved in nitrate-mediated nodulation inhibition through GmNARK in the root (Reid et al., 2013). While GmNARK functions in both AON and nitrate-mediated nodulation pathways, the two pathways remain distinct (Ferguson et al., 2019; Qiao et al., 2024). AON operates systemically, whereas nitrate regulation of nodulation occurs locally. Overexpression of GmNIC1 suppresses nodulation in transgenic roots but does not affect nodulation in nontransgenic roots within the same plant (Reid et al., 2011a). Additionally, high-nitrate levels accelerate nodule senescence and inhibit nitrogenase activity, which has not been shown in AON-related mutants. Recently, transcription factors such as Soybean Nitrogen Associated NAPs (SNAPs) and Fixation Under Nitrate (FUN) have been implicated in the high-nitrate response in legumes (Wang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). Whether these transcription factors primarily regulate nodulation locally or play a role in AON in response to nitrate remains an open question.

# Autoregulation of nodulation and the autoregulation of mycorrhization (AOM)

Legume plants can establish symbiotic relationships with both rhizobia and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) via a common signaling pathway that relies on shared molecular components critical for both nodulation and mycorrhization. Both symbiotic processes are thought to involve autoregulatory pathways; however, while the AON pathway has been relatively well characterized, the AOM remains comparatively less understood. In Medicago truncatula, the AON receptor kinase SUNN has been shown to function in the AOM pathway, in which it

senses CLE peptides MtCLE53 and MtCLE33 induced by AMF or low phosphate (Pi) conditions, respectively (Müller et al., 2019; Karlo et al., 2020). Similar to the CLE peptides in the AON pathway, the root-derived CLE peptides in AOM also require tri-arabinosylation for their function, highlighting a conserved molecular mechanism adopted by AON and AOM.

Given that SUNN serves as a shared receptor in both AON and AOM pathways, an intriguing question arises: How does the plant differentiate between AON and AOM signals to elicit distinct responses? One hypothesis is that specific co-receptors, or interacting proteins, may be involved in helping SUNN distinguish between nodulation and mycorrhization signals. Such co-receptors could modulate SUNN's binding specificity or activate distinct downstream pathways depending on the origin of the CLE signals. Identifying these co-receptors and understanding their roles could provide key insight into how plants fine tune their responses to both symbiotic partners, ensuring an efficient and balanced regulation of nodulation and mycorrhization in response to environmental and physiological cues. Because AON and AOM are likely to be conserved in legumes, further investigation into the mechanisms underlying their crosstalk will enhance our understanding of plant-microbe symbiosis in soybean.

# Autoregulation of nodulation and lateral root development

A recently established developmental model proposes a direct regulatory link between lateral root development and nodule formation, supported by both genetic and molecular evidence. This concept builds upon early observations by Nutman (1948) in red clover, in which correlations between root branching and nodulation patterns were first described at a morphological level.

The symbiotic nodule is a specified lateral organ of the root system, which is composed of the primary root and lateral roots to efficiently acquire water and nutrients. Nitrate regulates the development of both lateral roots and nodules in legumes (Carroll et al., 1985b; Streeter and Wong, 1988; Saito et al., 2014; Nishida and Suzuki, 2018). Recent studies have revealed a direct link between the development of nodules and lateral roots. Upon rhizobia infection, the SHR-SCR (SHORTROOT-SCARECROW) module, which is responsible for cortical cell division for lateral development, activates NIN to recruit lateral organ boundaries (LOB)-domain-containing protein 16 (LBD16) that is involved in lateral development for nodule organogenesis (Schiessl et al., 2019; Soyano et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2021). Subsequently, the transcription factors LSH1 and LSH2 (LIGHT-DEPENDENT SHORT HYPOCOTYLS 1/2) have been identified as key regulators that repress the lateral root developmental program while promoting the differentiation of root cells toward symbiotic nodule formation (Lee et al., 2024). Therefore symbiotic nodulation and lateral root development are intrinsically connected and dynamically controlled in response to nitrate conditions.

AON has also been suggested to play a crucial role in lateral root development as the mutants of har1, sunn and nts1007 exhibit bushy roots (Wopereis et al., 2000; Searle et al., 2003;

Jin et al., 2012; Goh et al., 2019). Recently, it has been shown that HAR1 represses lateral root development locally and systemically under a moderate level of nitrate in L. japonicus (Hayashi-Tsugane and Kawaguchi, 2022). Additionally, in nonnodulating wild-type plants, ectopic expression of miR2111 significantly enhanced lateral root emergence with a decrease in lateral root length and average root diameter (Zhang et al., 2021). In contrast, hairy roots constructively expressing the target mimic construct exhibited reduced lateral root density (Zhang et al., 2021). Moreover, miR2111-TML may be an important module for adaptive shoot control of root architecture in other plants such as L. japonicus and Arabidopsis thaliana (Sexauer et al., 2023). These results suggest a dual role of AON in the systemic regulation of root nodule and lateral root development, thereby regulating the adaptive response of the root system to changing conditions of nitrate. Given the high conservation of AON in legumes, it is conceivable that this AON-mediated systemic coordination is used for lateral root development and root nodule formation in soybean. Indeed, the loss-of-function mutant of GmNARK resulted in supernodulation and an increased number of lateral roots (Searle et al., 2003). It will be interesting to elucidate how two spatially distinct processes, lateral root and nodule formation, are integrated to modulate the root system's plastic development to adapt to the environment in soybean.

# CONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVE

The isolation and characterization of genes involved in the AON in soybean underscore the power of functional genomics. This research also elucidates the mechanism through which external nitrate inhibits nodulation in legumes. Initially, mutants displaying distinct phenotypes were isolated: "supernodulation" mutants (with altered AON function) and nonnodulation mutants (lacking initial reception of bacterial Nod factor). These mutants enabled the use of molecular markers, comprehensive genetic maps, and rapid DNA sequencing methods to identify and analyze the function of mutated genes (Hayashi et al., 2012; Mens et al., 2018; Chu et al., 2022).

Among the key regulators identified, GmNARK has emerged as a critical shoot regulator of AON, yet its direct target within the AON pathway remains unknown. It is known that miR2111 expression is controlled by the AON receptor and can move from shoots to roots, but which transcription factor downstream of the AON receptor is used to activate miR2111 is still unknown. Furthermore, miR2111 is not the SDI that the researchers have been looking for, so it remains an open question as to what the real SDI is. Additionally, TML is recognized as a central regulator of AON within roots. However, TML mutants exhibit a weaker phenotype compared with GmNARK mutants, suggesting the existence of additional, yet-unidentified pathways. Therefore there are still many questions that need to be answered before we can fully understand the regulation of the AON pathway in soybean.

A comprehensive understanding of AON and its regulatory mechanisms will probably benefit from an integrated approach.

Techniques such as map-based cloning, gene expression analysis, epigenetic studies, pan-genome analysis targeting breeding priorities, molecular physiology, mutational studies, and functional genomics will provide critical insight into the complex plant processes controlling growth, adaptation, and productivity. Notably, genetic modifications to enhance the AON pathway have shown promise for increasing soybean yield (Zhong et al., 2024). However, under field conditions, soybean plants are exposed to a variety of biotic and abiotic stressors such as nitrate levels, temperature changes, and pathogens, and the response of the AON pathway to these environmental stimuli is less understood. While indoor studies have identified key components of the AON pathway, further research is required to determine whether these advancements can reliably enhance crop performance to meet the food, feed, and fuel demands of an expanding global population, a pressing challenge for the coming decade.
