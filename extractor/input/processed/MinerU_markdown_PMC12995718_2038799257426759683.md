Phytic acid (PA), the major phosphorus storage compound in cereal grains, chelates essential minerals such as iron and zinc, thereby preventing their absorption in the human intestine and contributing to micronutrient defciencies. Myo-inositol 3-phosphate synthase 1 (INO1) is a key enzyme in PA biosynthesis, catalysing the pathway’s frst step and determining PA contents in cereal grains. Although the genetic modifcation of PA biosynthesis enzymes can reduce PA levels in grains by disrupting enzyme function, it often impairs germination and seedling growth. Here we used a plant chemical biology approach targeting INO1 to reduce PA levels in rice (Oryza sativa L.) and wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) through chemical intervention. From over 1,000 molecules, candidate compounds were identifed on the basis of biophysical or biochemical screening. When applied to developing seeds in panicles or spikes, these selected compounds reduced the PA content in grains of rice and wheat. This study presents a strategy for developing low-PA crops and contributes to global eforts to reduce malnutrition. 

Micronutrient deficiency, also known as hidden hunger, is a global public health issue caused by insufficient intake of essential vitamins or minerals1 . Mineral deficiencies, such as zinc (Zn) deficiency, are estimated to be associated with in excess of 800,000 global deaths annually among children under 5 years of age2 . Myo-inositol hexaphosphate (phytic acid, PA) is a major storage form of phosphorus (P) in cereal grains and accounts for around $70 \%$ of total seed phosphorus3 . PA tightly chelates cations such as iron (Fe) and Zn, forming insoluble salts1,4 that prevent the intestinal absorption of these minerals, leading to micronutrient deficiencies—especially in developing countries where plant-based diets are prevalent1 . Therefore, considerable 

attention has been directed towards reducing PA contents in cereal grains, such as rice, to improve micronutrient bioavailability in crops, contributing to the fight against the global problem of malnutrition1,5 . 

Many studies have examined the mechanism of PA accumulation, including PA biosynthesis genes in crop seeds. PA biosynthesis genes have garnered considerable attention as promising targets for developing low PA (lpa) lines via genetic manipulation. Among the genes, myo-inositol 3-phosphate synthase 1 (INO1), involved in the fundamental step of PA biosynthesis in seeds6 , is an important regulatory gene for PA content in cereals7,8 . A recent study reported that CRISPR–Cas9-targeted mutagenesis of INO1 in rice (Oryza sativa L.)

However, these mutations severely impaired seed germination and seedling growth owing to abnormal development of the embryo, where INO1 is highly expressed during seed development9 . PA in seeds also supplies phosphorus, myo-inositol and other minerals as nutrient resources through the action of endogenous phytase. In addition, PA contributes to stress responses by influencing the activity of mineral-depending superoxide dismutase10. Indeed, numerous studies, aimed at producing lpa crops through mutagenesis of PA biosynthesis genes have reported negative side effects on crop production and seed vitality11,12. 

However, the PA content in rice grains was successfully reduced, without apparent effects on plant growth or crop production, by antisense repression of INO1 regulated by the oleosin promoter, which is temporally and specifically expressed in developing seeds of rice13. This indicates that the conditional inhibition of INO1 is effective in reducing PA content in seeds. Nevertheless, a later study suggested that INO1 silencing affected myo-inositol biosynthesis, resulting in seeds showing increased sensitivity to abscisic acid during germination14. Taken together with these previous reports, a dilemma remains: while grains for food and feed benefit from reduced PA contents, seeds for crop production need PA and a functional PA biosynthesis pathway for normal organ development, such as the embryo, and for supplying nutrients during germination. This presents challenges for the successful development of lpa crops through genetic modification of PA biosynthesis genes. 

As it permits circumventing gene redundancy or lethality, the plant chemical biology approach is considered a valuable tool for understanding plant molecular function, including plant hormonal action15. This strategy can also be effective for improving crop production and resilience16,17. When it comes to the development of lpa crops, this approach is expected to reduce PA content in cereals without the concerns of side effects on seed vitality because it enables a clear distinction between seeds destined for food or feed and those reserved for the next generation of crop production. Therefore, the chemical 

inhibition of PA biosynthesis enzymes, including INO1, is considered a viable approach for addressing challenges in producing lpa cereals. Furthermore, the strategy is expected to aid in reducing PA in cereals in developing countries, as it can be effective for a wide range of crop species with simple treatments, such as spraying. 

We therefore attempted to chemically reduce PA in rice grains, as it is a major staple cereal worldwide and the predominant crop in Asia1,18. In this study, we conducted a chemical screening targeting INO1 on the basis of biophysical and biochemical experiments in combination with computational analysis commonly used in pharmaceutical science19 We validated the effects of the hit compounds on PA levels in rice grains. Furthermore, we demonstrated the effect of the compounds on PA in wheat (Triticum aestivum L.), a polyploid species in which genetic approaches are often challenging. Here, we report that the chemical inhibition of INO1 significantly decreases PA not only in rice but also in wheat. This study provides insights for producing lpa grains contributing to solutions for global food challenges. 

# Results

# Selection of hit compounds on the basis of biophysical and biochemical experiments and computational analysis

Recombinant INO1 (59 kDa) was purified to a high degree as indicated by a single band on SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE) (Fig. 1a). The gel filtration chromatogram showed a sharp single peak, suggesting the sample was free of major contaminants (Fig. 1b). The apparent molecular weight of recombinant INO1 $( 2 4 0 \ k \mathrm { k D } \mathsf { a } )$ , calculated on the basis of its elution volume from gel filtration chromatography, was consistent with the known tetrameric structure of INO1 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Desm.) Meyen20, showing high sequence similarity (Fig. 1b and Supplementary Fig. 1). Furthermore, myo-inositol 3-phosphate synthase activity of the purified INO1 was confirmed (Fig. 1c). These results indicate that the recombinant INO1 was obtained with high purity, maintaining its structural consistency as a tetrameric form and retaining its enzyme activity.

The $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ nuclear magnetic resonance $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR) signal intensity of fragment compounds decreases upon binding to a protein. The fragment screening identified 122 compounds that showed $\geq 4 0 \%$ reduction in 19F NMR signal intensity in the presence of INO1, and these were selected as initial hits (Fig. 2a). These fragments inhibited INO1 activity, with inhibition ratios of up to $8 1 . 0 \%$ (Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 2). However, no strong correlation was observed between the $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR signal decay and the enzyme inhibition ratio, because $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR does not necessarily indicate binding at functionally relevant sites or whether the interaction leads to enzymatic inhibition (Fig. 2c). Therefore, we prioritized fragments on the basis of their inhibition ratios and selected the 21 fragments with inhibition ratios of $70 \%$ or higher (Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 2). 

The characteristics of the hit fragments were validated using the pharmacophore fingerprint (PHFP), which reflects differences in their interaction with proteins. The selected hit fragments were classified into eight clusters (PHFP 18, 61, 64, 153, 230, 248, 251 and others) (Table 1). As candidates for antagonists, one fragment was selected from each cluster on the basis of both the $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR signal decay and the enzyme inhibition ratio (Fig. 2). Ultimately, eight fragments—PK0- 34741, PK0-28011, CK0-02611, PK0-46481, PK0-22791, PK0-35651, PK0- 36481 and PK0-34321—were identified as potential INO1 antagonists. 

# Hit fragments inhibited INO1 through binding to the active site of INO1

Lineweaver–Burk plots indicated that all hit fragments inhibited the INO1 through mixed inhibition with the micromolar inhibition coefficient $( K _ { \mathrm { i } } )$ values (Fig. 3). In addition, alpha values, which give an 

indication on the inhibition mechanism21, suggested that these fragments primarily inhibited enzyme activity by interacting with the active site of the free enzyme. Yet, these fragments may also interact with the enzyme–substrate complex or alter protein conformation by interacting with other binding sites, reflecting the mixed inhibition (Fig. 3). We also analysed the binding activity of hit fragments against INO1 using isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC) (Supplementary Fig. 3). Among the eight fragments, interaction with INO1 was detected for PK0-34321, PK0-34741, CK0-02611 and PK0-28011 with the micromolar binding constant $( K _ { \mathrm { D } } )$ values (Supplementary Fig. 3). 

To investigate the interaction between the hit fragments and INO1, single alanine-substitution mutants of INO1 were prepared. Among the residues that interact with the substrate (glucose 6-phosphate, G6P) or the cofactor (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, NAD) in the active site of yeast INO1, conserved amino acids in rice INO1 were selected and replaced with alanine (A) (Supplementary Fig. 1 and Supplementary Table 1). Only successfully purified mutants were used for consecutive experiments. The mutants affecting substrate recognition completely lost enzyme activity, whereas those related to NAD binding retained low activity (Fig. 4a–c). The interactions between the hit fragments and the INO1 mutants were analysed using $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR (Table 2). The NMR signal intensity of PK0-28011 and PK0-36481 was recovered by the lysine (K)-468A mutation, and PK0-35651 recovered its signal intensity with mutations at threonine (T)-230 and T-233 (Table 2). The remaining hit fragments showed no obvious recovery in NMR signal intensity with any mutations (Table 2). These results suggest where the fragments specifically bind.

# Treatment of rice panicles and wheat spikes with hit fragments significantly reduced PA content in the grains

Next, we confirmed the effect of each hit fragment treatment on PA content in rice grains. PK0-34741 and PK0-28011 significantly decreased PA content in seeds $( \mathsf { m g } \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ of grain weight) compared with the control condition, and PK0-28011 had the greatest effect (Fig. 5a). However, the grain weight, ear weight, grain number and filling ratio were not significantly affected by the chemical treatment, resulting in up to $31 \%$ reduction in PA content per seed (μg per grain) compared with the control (Supplementary Figs. 5 and 6). By contrast, mutation of INO1 through the insertion of retrotransposon Tos17 significantly reduced PA levels in seeds but severely impaired yield-related traits (Supplementary Figs. 6 and 7 and Supplementary Tables 3 and 4). Furthermore, Zn and Fe content in the seeds treated with PK0-28011 was not affected, resulting in a 1.5-fold increase in minerals per PA content (Fig. 5b–e). The germination ratio, protein content, amylose and amylopectin content and the structure of amylopectin were also unaffected by PK0-28011 treatment (Supplementary Figs. 8 and 9). These results demonstrate that the chemical treatment with INO1 antagonist candidates effectively improved the micronutrient bioavailability without any adverse effect on yield-related traits and grain quality, including nutrition and eating quality. 

INO1 is highly conserved across other crops, as shown by the fact that wheat INO1 (ref. 8) shares over $9 5 \%$ amino acid sequence similarity with that of rice (Supplementary Fig. 10). Furthermore, the conserved active residues present both in rice and yeast INO1 are 

fully retained in wheat INO1 (Supplementary Fig. 1). The effects of the three compounds, exhibiting the greatest reduction in PA content in rice, on PA content in wheat were evaluated in the same manner as for rice. PA content $( \mathsf { m g } \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ of grain weight) was significantly lower in grains treated with each fragment, similar to the reduction seen in rice (Fig. 5f). The total PA content in seeds (μg per grain) treated with the three fragments was significantly reduced, with a maximum reduction of $4 7 \%$ observed for PK0-34741 (Supplementary Fig. 5). Grain weight was not significantly affected by PK0-34741 and PK0-28011 treatments (Supplementary Fig. 5). Finally, it was confirmed that the Zn and Fe contents in seeds were not affected, and the ratio of mineral to PA content was doubled by the PK0-34741 treatment (Fig. 5g–j). Therefore, the compounds selected on the basis of their interference with rice INO1 were also effective in another staple crop. 

# Discussion

# Hit fragments are suitable candidates for an INO1 antagonist development

In this study, we selected eight candidate fragments with comparable $K _ { \mathrm { i } }$ values, all of which inhibited INO1 via mixed inhibition. Yet, ITC detected interactions for the four hit fragments with higher alpha values, indicating that they preferentially bind to the free enzyme rather than to the substrate–enzyme complex (Fig. 3 and Supplementary Fig. 3). These results suggest that the remaining hit fragments probably bind to INO1 more weakly because of the non-specific interactions, making them undetectable by ITC. The mutagenesis of the conserved residues in the active site of INO1 between yeast and rice identified key residues for interaction with fragments. The mutagenesis and the docking simulation suggested that PK0-28011 might specifically interact with K-468, a residue proposed to catalyse enolization of the substrate intermediate20. These findings suggest that PK0-28011 inhibits INO1 by disrupting the enolization step, in addition to physically blocking substrate binding.

# Chemical inhibition of PA biosynthesis can improve micronutrient bioavailability in cereals without adverse effects

Numerous studies have examined PA accumulation in crop seeds and attempted to reduce PA levels through genetic approaches. However, developing lpa crops through genetic modification of PA biosynthesis genes is challenging owing to gene lethality. Our study also demonstrated a $3 8 \%$ decrease in PA in rice seed because of the Tos17 insertion in INO1, although this resulted in serious deterioration of yield-related traits. # Drug discovery targeting INO1 sheds light on plant chemical biology

Chemical approaches have previously been used to improve plant functions. For instance, the treatment with the trehalose 6-phosphate 

(T6P) precursor, which significantly increased T6P content in plants, improved yield and drought resilience in wheat16. Although the cellular T6P content was altered approximately twofold through the transgenic approach16,23,24, the increase in T6P content induced by T6P precursor treatment was more than 100-fold higher than the endogenous levels16. Wheat contains more than ten paralogous genes for trehalose 6-phosphate synthase (TPS) and trehalose 6-phosphate phosphatase $( T P P ) ^ { 2 5 , 2 6 }$ , yet the T6P precursor bypasses this redundancy by directly increasing T6P content16. This would explain why the chemical intervention strategy to increase T6P content outperformed the transgenic approach. These studies support the robustness of the chemical approaches for improving crop productivity through simple, temporal and organ-specific treatments. 

Most chemical screens in plant science have been phenotype based, although target-oriented approaches are also used. For instance, several plant hormone inhibitors, such as PEO-IAA, an auxin antagonist, have been developed on the basis of their interaction with the target receptors27,28. It has also been reported that PEO-IAA increases grain yield in rice following application to the aerial parts of the plant, including panicles17. However, such plant hormone antagonists act via receptors that regulate many downstream genes. By contrast, our study targeted the PA biosynthesis enzyme directly, and several hit compounds improved agronomic traits without notable side effects on crop production or grain quality. Previous reports highlight the effectiveness of the chemical intervention approach for improving agronomic traits. 

# A fragment-based drug discovery approach can also be effective for agronomic objectives

Drug discovery typically focuses on pharmaceutically important targets29. Fragment screening enables efficient evaluation of a variety of compounds using a relatively smaller number of compounds (~1,000) and is advantageous for the ease of structural development towards lead compounds19. Many clinical compounds have been developed using fragment screening30. For instance, an inhibitor of KRAS, which is one of the most frequently mutated oncoproteins in cancer, was initially screened using fragments31, designed on the basis of the hit fragments32 and approved for use in 2021 (sotorasib30). However, conducting clinical trials can be challenging owing to high costs and ethical concerns often resulting in a lengthy approval process33,34. To address this, research is generally divided into phases, including screening, in vitro or in vivo evaluation and eventual clinical trials. In this study, we applied these drug discovery techniques to an agronomically important enzyme, and the effect of the hit compounds on the target trait was confirmed in planta. These aspects highlight the originality of the present study and demonstrate that fragment screening is also effective for agronomic applications. 

Taken together, our results propose a chemical intervention strategy that complements conventional genetic approaches by circumventing gene lethality and application across different plant species. This study provides insights into the production of low-PA crops that can potentially contribute to alleviating global malnutrition. 

# Methods

# Heterologous protein expression and purifcation

INO1 is known to be highly conserved, particularly in eukaryotes35–38. High sequence homology between rice and yeast INO1 has been confirmed (Supplementary Fig. 1), with more than $50 \%$ sequence similarity observed between the two enzymes. Expression and purification of recombinant INO1 were performed as previously described in the heterologous expression and purification of yeast $\mathbf { I N O 1 } ^ { 2 0 , 3 6 , 3 9 }$ with modifications outlined in our earlier studies40,41, and the full procedures are described below. A codon-optimized full-length INO1 rice gene was chemically synthesized for expression in Escherichia coli (GenScript Japan). The synthesized INO1 gene was inserted into the pET-28a plasmid using the Nhe I and Xho I restriction sites (see the Supplementary 

Data for the oligonucleotide sequence), after which the construct was introduced into E. coli BL21(DE3). The E. coli strain transformants were cultured at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in Luria–Bertani medium containing $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin until the optical density at 600 nm reached 0.8. Protein expression was induced with 0.1 mM isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thio-galactopyranoside (IPTG), followed by incubation for an additional 3 h at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The cells overexpressing INO1 were collected, resuspended in lysis buffer $( 2 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ Tris-HCl, 300 mM NaCl and pH 8.0) and disrupted by sonication on ice. The cell lysate was centrifuged at 44,000g for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The supernatant containing recombinant INO1 was subjected to four successive chromatographic steps using an ÄKTA Explorer system (Cytiva). First, the crude protein extract was applied to a HisTrap HP 5 ml Ni-affinity column (Cytiva), and bound proteins were eluted with a buffer containing 300 mM imidazole. The eluted INO1 was loaded onto a HiLoad 26/60 Superdex 200 prep-grade gel filtration column (Cytiva) equilibrated with 20 mM Tris-HCl, 150 mM NaCl and a pH of 8.0. Peak fractions were collected and further purified by anion exchange chromatography on an ENrich ${ \boldsymbol { \mathrm { Q 1 0 } } } \times 1 0 0$ column (Bio-Rad), using a linear NaCl gradient from 50 to 300 mM over 150 ml. Finally, the INO1-containing fractions were loaded onto a HiLoad 26/60 Superdex 200 prep-grade column equilibrated with the same buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, 150 mM NaCl and pH 8.0). 

# Site-directed mutagenesis

Given the high sequence homology between rice and yeast INO1, mutation sites were selected on the basis of the active site of yeast INO1, of which the enzyme–substrate complex structure has been established by X-ray crystallography (Protein Data Bank 1RM0) (Supplementary Fig. 1). Among the residues that interact with the substrate or the cofactor in the active site of yeast INO1, conserved amino acids in rice INO1 were selected and replaced with alanine (A) (Supplementary Fig. 1). INO1 single alanine-substitution mutants were amplified by PCR using KOD Plus Neo DNA polymerase (TOYOBO) and primer pairs, synthesized by Eurofins Genomics, as presented in Supplementary Table 1. The plasmid constructs containing each INO1 mutant were used to transform BL21(DE3). Recombinant proteins were expressed under the same conditions as the wild type protein. Purification of each mutant followed by Ni-affinity chromatography and gel filtration procedures were described above for the wild type INO1. 

# Enzymatic activity assay

The enzymatic activity of recombinant INO1 was measured using a colorimetric assay42, and the detailed protocols are given in the following section. The biosynthesized myo-inositol 3-phoshate was quantified by measuring the inorganic phosphate released by periodate oxidation of the product. Purified recombinant INO1 was diluted into a reaction buffer $( 1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), $2 0 \mathrm { m M N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { C l } ,$ , 2 mM dithiothreitol and 1 mM NAD). G6P was added to the desired concentration, and the final reaction volume was adjusted to $1 5 \mu \up$ . The mixture was incubated for 30 min at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and then quenched by adding $5 \mu \mu 2 0 \% \left( \nu / \nu \right)$ trichloroacetic acid. After removing precipitated proteins by centrifugation, $2 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ supernatant was mixed with $2 0 \mu 1 0 . 2 \mathsf { M N a I O } _ { 4 }$ and incubated for 1 h at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } .$ . Excess ${ \mathsf { N a l O } } _ { 4 }$ was removed by adding $2 0 \mu \mu . 0 \ M \ N a _ { 2 } S 0 _ { 3 } ,$ . Inorganic phosphate was quantified at 655 nm using a chromogenic reagent containing $10 \%$ (w/v) ascorbic acid, concentrated ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S } { \sf O } _ { 4 }$ and $5 \%$ (w/v) ammonium molybdate. Reaction mixture without recombinant INO1 served as a blank. Standard curves prepared with $0 . 0 3 \mathrm { M K H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 }$ were used to calculate phosphate concentrations. The enzyme parameters including maximum velocity $( V _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ and the Michaelis constant $( K _ { \mathfrak { m } } )$ were determined using nonlinear regression in GraphPad Prism ver. 8.4.2. 

For the enzymatic inhibition assay, recombinant INO1 was suspended in the same reaction buffer used for the fragments, and 120 of the 122 identified fragments were subjected to the enzymatic inhibition assays. The amount of the biosynthesized myo-inositol 3-phosphate was measured using the procedure described above. The 

Lineweaver–Burk plot and inhibition constant $( K _ { \mathrm { i } } )$ were determined using GraphPad Prism ver. 8.4.2. In these plots, the intersection of fitted lines at the Y axis indicates competitive inhibition, the intersection at the X axis indicates non-competitive inhibition and the intersection between the two axes corresponds to mixed inhibition43. In addition, alpha values, which give an indication of the inhibition mechanism by showing whether the compound preferably binds to the free enzyme or the substrate–enzyme complex in mixed inhibition, were calculated using GraphPad Prism21. An alpha value greater than 1 indicates the compound preferentially binds to the free enzyme21. When it is less than 1, the inhibitor tends to interact with the substrate–enzyme complex21. 

# 19F NMR fragment screening

The $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR experiment was performed as described in previous reports19,41, and the detailed methodological steps are outlined below. The experiment was carried out using a Bruker AV400 spectrometer (Bruker) equipped with a 5-mm broadband fluorine observation probe (BOFF, Bruker). All $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR spectra were acquired at 298 K with a $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ Larmor frequency of 376 MHz. 

The sample mixtures of CF-, ${ \mathrm { C F } } _ { 2 ^ { - } }$ and $\mathrm { C F } _ { 3 }$ -containing fragments were prepared at final concentrations of 50, 25 and $1 7 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively, either in the presence or absence of 10 $\mu \mathrm { M }$ INO1. A total of 1,134 fragments supplied by Kishida Chemical were tested in the 19F $R _ { 2 }$ filter experiment using the Carr–Purcell–Meibom–Gill scheme. Each spectrum represented the average of 1,024 scans. The signal decay was defined as the percentage decrease in signal intensity upon the addition of INO1 relative to the signal intensity obtained in the absence of INO1. To identify potential binders, we set a signal decay cut-off of $40 \%$ , which corresponds to more than twice the background noise level. 

For the $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR assay using INO1 single alanine-substitution mutants, hit fragments were mixed in the reaction solutions divided into two groups of four fragments each. Those solutions were tested by measuring with or without $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ wild type or mutant INO1, under the same conditions as described above. The recovery ratio was defined as the signal intensity recovered upon addition of the INO1 mutant, calculated as the difference between the signal intensities obtained with the mutant and with the wild type INO1, divided by the decrease in signal intensity caused by the wild type INO1. 

# Computational analysis

Fragment library data were processed using Pipeline Pilot v2018 (Dassault)44. Fragments were characterized by calculating their triplet PHFP (PHFP3), a pharmacophore-based fingerprint that indirectly reflects potential interactions with proteins19. Clustering analysis was carried out in R program (v3.4.4) using the single-linkage clustering method (hclust function)45 and distance metric derived from the Tanimoto coefficient applied to PHFP3. The description of the hit fragment identification from the same chemical library, on the basis of the PHFP3, has been reported previously19. 

Docking simulation of PK0-28011 against INO1 was performed with Flare (v9.0.0.) (Cresset)46 using the chain A of the crystal structure of INO1 as the structure of the target protein. The docking grid definition was determined around $5 \mathring { \mathbf { A } }$ of Lys-468 showing specific interaction with PK0-28011 based on $^ { 1 9 } \mathsf { F }$ NMR experiment. Flare was run in the ‘very accurate but slow’ mode, and up to ten poses per ligand were generated. A docking pose was selected according to the lead finder rank score. The score was suitable for comparing alternative docking orientations of the same ligand. 

# Plant material and growth conditions

Seeds of rice (O. sativa) cv. Nipponbare were imbibed in distilled water for 2 days at $3 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark to induce germination. The germinated seedlings were transferred to 1/2,000 Wagner pots (three seeds per pot) filled with nursery soil (Honens Soil, Honenagri) and grown in a greenhouse under 13 h light at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and 11 h dark at ${ 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . 

Wheat (T. aestivum) cv. KOMUGI NOURIN 1 seeds were germinated on filter paper soaked with distilled water for 2 days at $3 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark and transplanted to 1/2,000 Wagner pots (three seeds per pot) filled with a soil mixture of Honens Soil, Kanuma soil and Akadama soil in a 1:1:1 ratio. The wheat plants were grown in a greenhouse with 13 h light at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 11 h dark at $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# Chemical treatment conditions

Each hit fragment was adjusted at $0 . 0 5 \mathsf { m M }$ and sprayed against the panicles and spikes. All spray solutions contained 0.1% (v/v) dimethyl sulfoxide (Nacalai Tesque) and $0 . 0 5 \%$ (v/v) polyoxyethylene sorbitan monolaurate (Tween20; Nacalai Tesque) to facilitate penetration into plant tissues, following the previous report17. 

In rice, PA is known to accumulate predominantly during the early stages of seed development (days after flowering (DAF) 5–15)7 . Data from the Rice Expression Profile Database (RiceXpro) (https://ricexpro. dna.affrc.go.jp) indicate that the expression of INO1 reaches a maximum in seeds around DAF 7–10. Another study, based on in situ RNA hybridization, reported that INO1 expression peaks at 6–7 days after pollination in rice embryos9 . The flowering stage of the rice plants was tracked, and the hit fragments were applied to the panicles in the greenhouse at DAF 6, 8, 10 and 12. A total of 40 days after ear emergence, the panicles were sampled, and only the seeds with known flowering time were used for PA measurements. The treatment of the selected hit fragments against wheat was conducted the same as with rice, as TaMIPS, the wheat ortholog of INO1, exhibits peak expression during seed development at DAF 14 (ref. 8). A total of 35 days after ear emergence, the seeds were sampled for PA measurements as rice. 

# Determination of PA content

PA content was determined following established methods47–49, and the detailed procedures are provided below. PA was hydrolysed by phytase to generate lower myo-inositol phosphate forms and inorganic phosphorus (Pi). After subsequent treatment with alkaline phosphatase, the released Pi was quantified and the PA levels were calculated according to the instructions of the Phytic Acid (Phytase)/Total Phosphorus Assay Kit (K-PHYT, Megazyme International). A single seed was ground in 0.66 M HCl, and the extracts were neutralized with 0.75 M NaOH. The aliquots of the extract were incubated with or without enzymes at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. A chromogenic reagent for Pi detection (see ‘Enzymatic activity assay’ section) was then added, and absorbance was measured at 655 nm. 

# Determination of mineral content

Mineral concentrations were measured using a protocol adapted from the previous report47, with all methodological steps fully provided below. Dehusked rice and wheat seeds were placed in Teflon cases and treated with concentrated ${ \mathsf { H N O } } _ { 3 }$ on a hotplate, with the temperature increased from $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $1 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in 30-min increments. The mineral content in the resulting digests was analysed by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (iCAP RQ, Qtegra, Thermo Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. 

# Statistical analysis

Welch’s two-tailed t-test was used for all two-group comparisons. Normality was not formally tested owing to the small sample size. No statistical methods were used to pre-determine sample sizes; sample sizes were consistent with those used in previous studies19,41,47. Plants and samples were allocated to groups on the basis of growth stage and handled in parallel. Pot positions were alternated and periodically moved to minimize positional bias. No formal randomization protocol was used.
