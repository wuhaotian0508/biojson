# Summary

Chicoric acid, a phenolic compound derived from plants, exhibits a range of pharmacological activities. Light significantly influences the chicoric acid biosynthesis in Taraxacum mongolicum; however, the transcriptional regulatory network governing this process remains unclear. A combined analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome revealed that blue light markedly enhances chicoric acid accumulation compared to red light. The blue light-sensitive transcription factor ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL5 (HY5) is closely associated with multiple core proteins, transcription factors and chicoric acid synthase genes involved in light signalling. Both in vivo and in vitro experiments demonstrated that TmHY5 directly regulates several chicoric acid biosynthetic genes, including TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2. Additionally, TmHY5 promotes the accumulation of luteolin and anthocyanins by increasing the expression of TmCHS2 and TmANS2. The E3 ubiquitin ligase CONSTITUTIVELY PHOTOMORPHOGENIC 1 (COP1) forms a protein complex with TmHY5, significantly inhibiting chicoric acid biosynthesis. Blue light inhibits TmCOP1-TmHY5 complex protein formation while enhancing the expression levels of TmCOP1 through TmHY5. Furthermore, TmHY5 elevates the expression levels of TmbZIP1, which indirectly activates Tm4CL1 expression. In vivo, TmCOP1 directly inhibits the expression of the TmHY5-Tm4CL1 complex. Therefore, we speculate that TmCOP1-TmHY5-mediated blue light signalling effectively activates chicoric acid biosynthesis, providing a foundation for the application of blue light supplementation technology in industrial production. 

# Introduction

Chicoric acid is an important phenolic acid compound found in over 60 plant species, including T. mongolicum, Echinochloa purpurea, Helianthus annuus and Cichorium intybus. These species, which belong to the Asteraceae family, are known for their strong adaptability to light and edibility (China Pharmacopeia Commission, 2020; Fu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). Chicoric acid exhibits various pharmacological activities, including the enhancement of immune function, anti-inflammatory effects, antihyperglycemic properties, inhibition of hyaluronidase and protection of collagen from degradable free radicals (Dandelion, 2021; Ding et al., 2019; Jacqueline et al., 2013). Therefore, in contemporary society, the consumption of foods rich in chicoric acid may contribute to improving human sub-health status and enhancing quality of life (Liu et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2019). Among these plants, a stable cut-dip-budding delivery system has been established for T. mongolicum, allowing for genetic modifications and the application of the CRISPR system (Cao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). T. mongolicum holds significant market value as a wild vegetable (e.g., dandelion root tea, leaf tea 

and flower tea) and as a medicinal plant, thereby providing a robust foundation for research into the biosynthesis of chicoric acid and its transcriptional regulation networks (Liu et al., 2021, 2022). 

Chicoric acid biosynthesis primarily depends on the phenylalanine pathway, which involves a series of multi-step enzymatic reactions, including those catalysed by phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL), cinnamate 4-hydroxylase (C4H) and 4-coumarate coenzyme A ligase (4CL) to produce caffeoyl-CoA. Subsequently, the action of BAHD family members, specifically hydroxycinnamoyl-CoA:quinate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HCT/HQT) and hydroxycinnamoyl-CoA:tartaric acid hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HTT), results in the formation of chlorogenic acid and caffeoyl tartaric acid, respectively (Liu et al., 2018; Sullivan, 2014). However, distinguishing HQT, HCT and HTT proteins based solely on their amino acid sequences—which contain the conserved domains HXXXD and DFGWG—remains a challenge (Liu et al., 2019; Payyavula et al., 2015). In E. purpurea, chicoric acid is biosynthesized by a vacuolar SCPL serine carboxypeptidase-like protein (chicoric acid synthase, CAS) using chlorogenic acid and caffeoyl tartaric acid as substrates; however, 

no highly analogous protein ( $60 \%$ identity) has been identified in T. mongolicum, H. annuus, or C. intybus (Fu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023). The convergent evolution of chicoric acid biosynthesis across different species necessitates further investigation (Fu et al., 2024). 

Light is a critical environmental factor essential for plant survival. It not only serves as a biological energy source through photosynthesis but also plays a significant role in various processes of plant growth and development as an environmental signalling factor (Taulavuori et al., 2015; Wang and Lin, 2020). Different light wavelengths, including blue light (450–435 nm), red light $( 6 4 7 - 7 6 0 \ \mathrm { \ n m } )$ and white light (390–780 nm), have distinct biological functions that can substantially influence the biosynthesis of downstream secondary metabolites (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2016). In Artemisia annua, exposure to blue light enhances the expression of the cytochrome P450 monooxygenase gene (CYP71AV1), leading to increased accumulation of artemisinin (Zhang et al., 2018). In Fagopyrum tataricum, samples treated with blue light exhibited higher chlorogenic acid content compared to those treated with red light (Thwe et al., 2014). In strawberries, blue light stimulates the expression of FvHCT, which upregulates chlorogenic acid biosynthesis (Chen et al., 2020). In red leaf lettuce, blue light increases the contents of quercetin and chicoric acid (Izzo et al., 2021; Taulavuori et al., 2015). Therefore, light quality significantly affects the accumulation of various polyphenolic compounds in plants (Kivim€aenp€a et al., 2022). Further in-depth analysis is necessary to understand how different light conditions influence the accumulation of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds in T. mongolicum. 

The light signalling pathways encompass a vast transcriptional regulatory network that operates under various light conditions, including blue light, red light and UV-B. Among the key players in this network, HY5 is the most well-known transcription factor (TF), which is induced by blue light in many plant species (Hao et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2019; Jing and Lin, 2020; Loyola et al., 2016). MdHY5 can be phosphorylated by the light-induced MdMPK6, leading to the activation of MdCHI and MdUFGT expression, which subsequently affects anthocyanin accumulation in apples (Gangappa and Botto, 2016; Xing et al., 2023). Additionally, MdHY5 is targeted by MdBBX22 through direct binding to its promoter (An et al., 2019). In tomatoes, SlHY5 directly binds to the promoters of CHS1/2 and DFR, thereby regulating anthocyanin accumulation (Liu et al., 2018). Furthermore, B-box proteins, such as SlBBX20, interact with SlHY5 to upregulate flavonoid biosynthetic genes (Shiose et al., 2024). In A. annua, AaBBX21 interacts with AaHY5, leading to increased artemisinin accumulation (Hao et al., 2019; He et al., 2024). Thus, the regulation of polyphenolic and terpenoid compound accumulation by light is dependent on HY5 and its interacting proteins or transcriptional cascade components (Li et al., 2020). The COP1-HY5 module represents a classic component of light signalling, playing a crucial role in phototaxis, apical hook formation in plant roots and the biosynthesis of anthocyanins and terpenes (Han et al., 2020; He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2021). Generally, COP1 functions as a light-inhibitory protein, translocating from the cytoplasm to the nucleus in dark conditions, where it degrades multiple proteins, including HY5. Under light conditions, COP1 is transported back to the cytoplasm, reducing the degradation of proteins such as HY5 through 26S ubiquitination, thereby promoting photomorphogenesis (Jia et al., 2023; Li 

et al., 2021, 2022). Therefore, we hypothesize that the COP1- HY5 module may have similar functions in the biosynthesis of chicoric acid in T. mongolicum. 

In this article, we initially investigated the effects of various light conditions on chicoric acid biosynthesis in T. mongolicum. Through a combined transcriptome and metabolome analysis, we identified that the blue light signalling transcription factor TmHY5, a homolog of the Arabidopsis HY5, significantly promotes chicoric acid accumulation. It has been established that TmHY5 can directly regulate the expression of chicoric acid synthase genes while also enhancing the expression levels of TmbZIP1, thereby indirectly promoting chicoric acid accumulation. Conversely, TmCOP1 acts as a light signal inhibitory factor that directly interacts with TmHY5 and decreases chicoric acid biosynthesis. Blue light inhibits the formation of the TmCOP1- TmHY5 protein complex by reducing TmCOP1 protein accumulation while simultaneously activating the expression level of TmCOP1 via TmHY5 to form feedback regulation. Under dark conditions, TmCOP1 directly inhibits the TmHY5-Tm4CL1 module. Our findings elucidate the molecular mechanism of the TmCOP1-TmHY5-Tm4CL1 module in blue light-mediated chicoric acid biosynthesis in T. mongolicum. 

# Results

# Blue light increases chicoric acid biosynthesis in T. mongolicum

To elucidate the impact of varying light conditions on chicoric acid accumulation in T. mongolicum, we conducted treatments with white light (W), blue light (B) and red light (R) over a period of 20 days, followed by a metabolomics analysis. A total of 2131 metabolites were identified, including 1377 and 754 metabolites quantified in positive and negative ion modes, respectively (Table S1). In positive ion mode, 16, 31 and 18 metabolites were specifically expressed in B-, R- and W-treated plants, respectively. In negative ion mode, 9, 36 and 22 metabolites were specifically expressed in B-, R- and W-treated plants, respectively (Figure S1A). The analysis of differential metabolites (DAMs) using a Venn diagram revealed 812 DAMs, comprising 115 in R vs. W, 81 in R vs. B and 98 in B vs. W, with 88 DAMs common to all comparisons (Figure 1a). KEGG enrichment analysis indicated that phenylpropanoid biosynthesis and metabolic pathways (including phenylalanine, tyrosine, tryptophan biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis) were the most significantly enriched among the DAMs (Figure 1b). In total, 52 polyphenolic compounds (including phenolic acids and flavonoids) were identified. Blue light significantly enhanced the biosynthesis of luteolin, dihydroquercetin and kaempferitrin derivatives (flavonoids), while red light promoted the biosynthesis of puerarin, naringin dihydrochalcone and liquiritigenin (Figure 1c). Furthermore, nearly half of the phenolic acid and flavonoid metabolites exhibited the highest levels under white light, followed by blue light and then red light. Notably, the concentrations of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds, D-tartaric acid and quinic acid, were substantially higher under blue light compared to red light. Additionally, the concentrations of chlorogenic acid, caffeic acid and kaempferol-3-O-rutinoside were greater under red and white light treatments than under blue light, suggesting that red light enhances the biosynthesis of these compounds in T. mongolicum. We conducted a detailed analysis of the transcriptome data of T. mongolicum subjected to various light treatments. The Venn plot analysis revealed that 215, 331 and 1882 genes were specifically expressed under white, blue and red light, respectively (Figure S2A). Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using Transcripts Per Million (TPM) as an indicator; the contribution rates for principal components 1, 2 and 3 were $4 9 . 7 5 \%$ , $2 0 . 5 4 \%$ and $8 . 5 4 \%$ , respectively. Notably, red light 

exhibited significant clustering with blue and white light (Figure S2B). Based on the metabolome analyses, the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the comparison of red light versus blue light warrant further investigation. In comparison to blue light, 2144 DEGs were upregulated under red light, while 1424 DEGs were upregulated under blue light (Figure S2C). Among the 1424 DEGs upregulated by blue light, the three predominant pathways identified were polyphenol biosynthesis (including phenylpropanoid and flavonoid pathways), cysteine and methionine metabolism and arginine and proline metabolism (Figure 1d). 

To further investigate the regulatory effects of blue light on biosynthetic pathways, we present a correlation network map illustrating the relationships between the transcriptome and metabolome in the R_vs_B comparison. Initially, we constructed the interaction network using a metabolic set comprising 52 polyphenolic compounds (Figure 1c) and a gene set consisting of 3568 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) from the R_vs_B comparison. The analysis of the top 500 Pearson correlation coefficients revealed that chicoric acid is associated with only one gene (TbA02G115500), which belongs to the interleukin-1 receptor-associated kinase family (Figure S2D). Subsequently, we utilized a metabolic set alongside a gene set comprising 1424 blue-light-induced DEGs to build another interaction network. In this network, chicoric acid exhibited high correlation coefficients with five genes: TbA02G115500 (receptor-like protein kinase S.2), TbA03G007370 (inactive leucine-rich repeat receptor-like serine/threonine-protein kinase), TbA07G096300 (phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (ATP)-like), TbA08G003330 (uncharacterized protein) and TbA08G034400 (transcription factor bHLH153) (Figure S2E). These highly correlated genes had not been previously investigated. As illustrated in Figure 1f, we identified 41 polyphenol enzyme genes within the transcriptome (Table S2). We then constructed a co-expression network integrating 52 metabolites and 41 biosynthesis pathway genes, as shown in Figure 1e. Our analysis indicated that 4-coumarate-CoA ligase (4CL; TbA03G036550 and TbA03G034060), chalcone synthase (CHS; TbA04G022470), hydroxycinnamic acid:quinate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HQT; TbA03G036790) and hydroxycinnamoyl-CoA:shikimate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HCT; TbA07G106930 and TbA04G071520) were highly correlated with chicoric acid. In contrast, phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL; TbA01G020600), cinnamate-4-hydroxylase (C4H; TbA04G089050), 4-coumarate coenzyme A ligase (4CL; TbA07G106840), hydroxycinnamate:tyramine hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HTT; TbA07G028390), chalcone synthase (CHS; TbA03G110840) and flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3’H; TbA07G102100) showed high correlation with luteolin. Therefore, under varying light conditions, these polyphenolic synthase genes are directly correlated with the accumulation of secondary metabolites such as chicoric acid and luteolin. We randomly selected 10 synthase genes for quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis, which demonstrated a linear relationship of ${ \ge } 0 . 8$ , indicating that the transcriptome results were relatively accurate (Figure S2F). 

We identified 29 light-signal-related genes from transcriptome data and performed heatmap analysis (Figure S2G, Table S3). The expression levels of CRY2, COP1, DET1, SPA1, HY5, Phys and the PIFs were elevated under blue light, whereas those of CRY1, ARFs and SIC were higher under red light. A total of 1699 transcription factors (TFs) belonging to 47 TF families were identified in the transcriptomic data, with 89 members exhibiting differential expression in R_vs_B treatments. The top 50 TFs (with ${ \mathsf { T P M } } > 1$ and fold change $> 2$ ; Table S4) were selected for heatmap analysis, the results of which are presented in Figure S2H. The analysis included the top 50 TFs, 29 light-signalling protein genes and 41 polyphenol synthase genes to construct an interaction protein network based on the top 500 linear relationships (Figure 1g). Notably, TmHY5 (bZIP, TbA01G015620) and TmPIF3 (bHLH, TbA01G141970) exhibited the highest connectivity with other members (Figure S2I). Furthermore, only TmHY5 was simultaneously associated with light signalling, TFs and polyphenol synthase genes responsive to blue light, suggesting its 

potential role in the regulation of polyphenol compounds and warranting further investigation. 

# Altering TmHY5 expressions significantly affected chicoric acid, luteolin and anthocyanin biosynthesis

The full-length TmHY5 gene was cloned and sequenced based on transcriptome data. Cluster analysis revealed that TmHY5 is most closely related to AtbZIP56 and AtbZIP64, both of which belong to the H subfamily (Figure 2a). Multiple sequence alignment demonstrated that TmHY5 contains bZIP domains at the Cterminus, exhibiting over $80 \%$ amino acid homology with other species, including AaHY5, SlHY5 and LsHY5 (Figure S3A, Hao et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018). As illustrated in Figure 2b, GFP was detected in both the cytoplasm and nucleus, while the TmHY5– GFP fusion protein was exclusively localized in the nucleus, consistent with the findings for AaHY5 (Figure 2b, Hao et al., 2019). 

In plants, HY5 not only responds to light signals but also interacts with various signalling pathways, including abscisic acid (ABA) and gibberellin (GA) signalling (Wang et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2023). Through genomic promoter analysis and cloning, a 1093 bp sequence of the TmHY5 promoter was obtained, which contains multiple cis-acting elements such as light-responsive elements (GT1-motif, G-box and TCT-motif), methyl jasmonate (MeJA)-responsiveness (CGTCA-motif, TGACG-motif), ABA hormone signals (ABRE-elements), GA-responsiveness elements (TATC-box), etc. (Table S5). Therefore, qRT-PCR assays were conducted to analyse the effects of different light conditions, ABA hormones, GA, MeJA and NaCl on the expression of the TmHY5 gene. TmHY5 expression is induced by blue light and is highly expressed in flowers (Figure 2c). Following treatment with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ ABA, the expression level of TmHY5 peaked at $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and subsequently decreased gradually over the next $2 4 \mathsf { h } _ { \iota }$ , displaying a similar trend under NaCl treatment. In response to $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ GA treatment, TmHY5 expression initially decreased until 4 h before returning to baseline levels by $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . TmHY5 expression was not significantly affected by $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ MeJA treatment. Based on these findings, we further investigated the effects of different light conditions and ABA hormone combinations on TmHY5 gene expression. Results indicated that TmHY5 expression significantly increased after 2 h of combined light and ABA treatment, reaching levels 8-fold higher than those of the control group exposed to white light. In the short term, blue light and ABA signals exhibit a synergistic effect. Under continuous blue light conditions, ABA treatment also resulted in an initial increase followed by a decrease in TmHY5 expression. In contrast, compared to the white light control group, the expression of TmHY5 was inhibited under continuous blue, red, or dark conditions over a 7-day period. These results indicate that TmHY5 is involved in complex signalling network regulation in T. mongolicum.

The preliminarily screened transgenic T. mongolicum was grown under natural light conditions and further analysed using qRT-HY5F and qRT-HY5R to compare TmHY5 expression levels with those in the wild-type (WT) and empty vector (EV) lines. To further investigate the biological functions of TmHY5, we employed the CRISPR-Cas9 system to generate TmHY5-edited transgenic lines. # TmHY5 regulates the chicoric acid biosynthesis through directly binding to the promoters of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2

In order to further clarify the molecular mechanism by which TmHY5 promotes chicoric acid accumulation, we identified the downstream target genes of TmHY5 through qRT-PCR analysis of potential downstream-regulated enzymes in the TmHY5-OE2 and CR#9 lines (Figure 3f). In the TmHY5-OE2 line, the expression levels of TmPAL1/3/4, TmC4H1, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2 were significantly higher than those in the CR#9 line (Figure 4a; fold change ${ \geq } 2$ ). Consequently, the overexpression of TmHY5 resulted in increased levels of chicoric acid in T. mongolicum. In addition to Tm4CL1, which is significantly influenced by blue light, the downstream target genes of TmHY5 include TmPAL1/3/4, TmC4H1 and TmHQT2. To verify whether TmHY5 positively regulates the biosynthetic genes of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds, we conducted transient expression assays in N. benthamiana leaves. The promoter regions of TmPAL1/3/4, TmC4H1, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2 were utilized to drive the luciferase (LUC) gene as fusion reporters, while TmHY5 was overexpressed under the control of the 35S promoter as an effector. Detection of LUC activity revealed that co-expression with the TmHY5 significantly enhanced the expression of the TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2 reporters compared to controls (Figures 4b and S3). 

To further understand the relationship between TmHY5 and its putative target genes, we investigated whether TmHY5 directly binds to their respective promoter regions. Analysis of the promoter sequences of the target genes revealed that TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2 contained G-box motifs (CACGTG) and predicted AREB-like motifs (e.g., NNACGTNN) (Figure 4c; Table S5). Subsequently, we conducted yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays to assess the ability of TmHY5 to bind to these motifs. After introducing both pGADT7-TmHY5 and pLacZ-motif constructs $3 { \times } 6$ -box and $3 \times$ AREB-like motifs derived from the promoters of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2) into yeast strain Y1H, we observed that TmHY5 interacted with the $3 \times { \sf G }$ -box and AREB-like motifs, resulting in a blue colour on $\mathsf { S D / - T r p / - L e u / + X \mathrm { - } }$ gal (Figure 4c). To confirm that TmHY5 binds directly to the G-box and AREB-like motifs, we performed electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA) using a TmHY5-His fusion protein expressed in Escherichia coli. When the G-box and AREB-like motifs were employed as labelled probes, a DNA-TmHY5 protein complex was prominently detected (Figure 4d,e). However, the presence of cold competing probes (unlabelled G-box and AREB-like elements) diminished the binding of TmHY5 to the labelled probes. These results provide strong evidence that TmHY5 binds to the Gbox and AREB-like elements in the native promoters of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2, which are involved in the biosynthesis of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds. These findings indicate that the upregulation of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2 by TmHY5 enhances the accumulation of chicoric acid and its precursors.

# TmCOP1 interacts with TmHY5, and blue light inhibits the degradation of the TmCOP1-TmHY5 protein complex

The COP1 protein interacts with several light-related proteins, including cryptochromes 1 and 2 (CRY1/2), DE-ETIOLATED 1 (DET1) and HY5, in blue light-induced signalling (Canibano~ et al., 2021; Kerner et al., 2021; Figure S2G). Our findings indicate that the TmCOP1 protein contains three domains: RING (amino acids 74-120), coiled-coil (amino acids 53-324) and WD40-repeat (amino acids 371-575), consistent with the reported structure of COP1 from other species (Han et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021). Using a yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay, we first established that the WD40-repeat domain (amino acids 371-575) directly interacts with TmHY5. Additionally, the TmHY5 fragment encompassing amino acids 1–91, but not the fragment comprising amino acids 92–161, interacts directly with TmCOP1 (Figure 6a). To our knowledge, COP1 has been shown to directly bind to the VP motif of various proteins (Lau et al., 2019). Our analysis revealed that the VP motif is also present in TmHY5 (amino acids 1–91). When we substituted this VP motif with two alanine residues (46AA), TmCOP1 could not bind to the modified TmHY5 (46AA). This indicates that the binding sites for TmCOP1 and TmHY5 are TmCOP1 (amino acids 371–575) and TmHY5 (amino acids 46–51), respectively 

(Figure 6a). LUC complementation imaging (LCI) and additional bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assays confirmed the interaction between TmCOP1 and TmHY5 in tobacco (Figure 6b,c). Furthermore, the in vitro purification of TmHY5-GST and TmCOP1-His-tagged proteins, along with pull-down assays, demonstrated that TmHY5 and TmCOP1 can physically interact with each other (Figure 6d). These results demonstrate that TmCOP1 and TmHY5 proteins can interact both in vivo and in vitro, with binding sites identified as TmCOP1 (amino acids 371–575) and TmHY5 (amino acids 46–51).

The results revealed that the expression pattern of TmCOP1 closely aligns with that of TmHY5, in contrast to TmCRY1/2 (Table S3). Further investigation of TmCOP1 expression in response to light and abscisic acid (ABA) treatment demonstrated that TmCOP1 expression levels were significantly higher within $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ than those in the white light control group; both blue light and ABA promote the expression levels of TmCOP1 (Figure 7a). Conversely, the expression patterns of TmCRY1/2 exhibit inhibition by blue light and promotion by ABA. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that the TmCOP1 may be regulated by the TmHY5. To verify whether TmHY5 positively regulates the expression level of TmCOP1 and itself, we conducted transient expression assays in N. benthamiana leaves. The promoter regions of TmCOP1 and TmHY5 were utilized to drive the luciferase gene (LUC) as fusion reporters, with TmHY5 overexpressed under the control of the 35S promoter as an effector. The detection of LUC activity indicated that coexpression with the TmHY5 enhanced the expression of both TmCOP1 and TmHY5 reporters compared to controls (Figure 7b). 

To gain a deeper understanding of the relationship between TmHY5 and its putative target genes, we investigated whether TmHY5 directly binds to their respective promoter regions. The analysis of the promoter sequences revealed that both TmCOP1 and TmHY5 contain several predicted AREB-like motifs (Figure 7c; Table S5). We subsequently conducted Y1H assays to determine if 

TmHY5 could bind to these AREB-like motifs. Following the introduction of both pGADT7-TmHY5 and pLacZ-AREB-like motifs (39AREB-like from the TmCOP1 and TmHY5 promoters) into the yeast strain Y1H, we observed that TmHY5 interacted with the AREB-like motifs, resulting in a blue colour on SD/ Trp/ Leu/+X-gal (Figure 7c). To further confirm that TmHY5 binds directly to the AREB-like motifs, we performed EMSA assays using TmHY5-His fusion protein expressed in E. coli. Utilizing the AREB-like motifs as labelled probes, we detected a strong DNA-TmHY5 protein complex (Figure 7d). Notably, the introduction of cold competing probes (unlabelled AREB-like elements) reduced the binding of TmHY5 to the labelled probes. These findings provide strong evidence that TmHY5 binds to the AREB-like elements located in the native promoters of TmCOP1 and TmHY5. We propose that TmHY5 enhances the expression levels of TmCOP1 to establish feedback regulation while also promoting its own expression, which may play a role in signal amplification. 

In ABA signalling, we previously reported that the core TF TmbZIP1 (ABI5) directly regulates the expression of Tm4CL1, which is crucial for chicoric acid biosynthesis (Liu et al., 2023). Transcriptomic analysis under ABA treatment revealed similar expression patterns for both TmbZIP1 and TmHY5 (#44, Liu et al., 2023). In the TmHY5 OE#2 transgenic lines, the expression level of TmbZIP1 (TmABI5) was consistently upregulated alongside TmHY5 (Table S7). Therefore, we hypothesize that TmHY5 functions as an upstream regulatory TF for TmbZIP1. Further analysis of the TmbZIP1 promoter region identified three ABREmotif cis-acting elements. Y1H assays confirmed that TmHY5 binds to two of these ABRE motifs, while TmbZIP1 does not interact with the AREB cis-acting elements in the TmHY5 promoter (Figure 7e). EMSA assays further demonstrated that TmHY5 can directly bind to the biotinylated proTmbZIP1 DNA probe in vitro (Figure 7f). Consequently, TmHY5 can also indirectly regulate chicoric acid biosynthesis through the TmbZIP1-Tm4CL1 module. To clarify the effects of TmCOP1 on the TmbZIP1-Tm4CL1 and TmHY5-Tm4CL1 interactions, we conducted a dual-luciferase (LUC) assay. The results indicate that TmHY5 and TmbZIP1 synergistically enhance Tm4CL1 expression levels in tobacco (Figure 7g). Additionally, TmCOP1 inhibits the expression of the TmHY5-Tm4CL1 module but shows no significant effect on the TmbZIP1-Tm4CL1 module (Figure 7g). These findings suggest that TmCOP1 indirectly regulates chicoric acid biosynthesis through the TmHY5-Tm4CL1 pathway rather than the TmbZIP1-Tm4CL1 pathway.

In this study, we conducted a systematic investigation of TmHY5 and identified it as a key transcription factor involved in regulating chicoric acid biosynthesis under blue light (Figures 2 and 3). Further transcriptomic analysis of TmHY5 transgenic lines revealed that phenylalanine, flavonoids and plant hormone signalling are the primary downstream targets of TmHY5 regulation (Figure 3). Both in vivo and in vitro experiments demonstrated that TmHY5 directly influences the expression levels of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1 and TmHQT2, thereby regulating chicoric acid biosynthesis. Additionally, similar to findings from other studies, luteolin and anthocyanin biosynthesis can also be directly regulated through TmHY5-TmCHS2 and TmHY5-TmANS2 interactions (Figures 4 and 5). Thus, our research confirms that TmHY5 positively regulates the biosynthesis of chicoric acid, luteolin and anthocyanins in dandelion under blue light signals. 

HY5, as the smallest bZIP family members, has the largest numbers of binding motifs, including normal G-box (CACGTG) and other ACGT-containing elements with a consensus sequence motif of 50 -(G/A)(G/A)TGACGT(C/G/A)(A/T/G)- $3 ^ { \prime }$ (Song et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2022). In this article, we identified at least 14 motifs such as G-box (including GCACGTGT, CCACGTGA, GCACGTGA, TCACGTGC and ACACGTGT) and ACGTcontaining elements such as CCACGTTA, CGACGTTT, ATACGTCT, AAACGTTT, GTACGTGT, AAACGTGA, CCACGTCA and AGACGTTA that could directly binding to TmHY5 (Figures 4, 5 and 7). There are also other G-boxes and ACGT-motifs present on the promoter of the unfold-changed chicoric acid biosynthase genes such as TmPAL1/2, TmC4H1, TmHCT1/3, TmHQT1 and TmHTT1, which were validated by Y1H assays (Figure S5). The results showed that TmHY5 could also directly bind to G-boxes (TCACGTGA) and ACGT-containing elements (TGACGTGT and 

TCACGTCA) motifs. However, there has not been a systematic study and in-depth comparison of the diversity and universality of HY5 binding elements among different species such as A. thaliana, A. annua, Salvia miltiorrhiza and Solanum lycopersicum (He et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022). More types and sites of downstream genes activated by HY5 needed further exploration. On the other hand, although those chicoric acid biosynthetic enzyme genes (TmPAL1/2, TmC4H1, TmHCT1/3, TmHQT1, TmHTT1) can be bound by TmHY5, their expression levels are not significantly affected in transgenic lines (Figures 4, 5, and 7 and S5). Therefore, under special conditions, we speculate that TmHY5 could form protein complexes with partner proteins BBXs (AaBBX21-AaHY5 promotes the terpenoids biosynthesis; BBX22- HY5 promotes the carotenoid biosynthesis in citrus), regulate the expression of these synthetic enzyme genes and alter downstream metabolic pathways in epigenetics (Fu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024). In addition to directly regulating the biosynthesis of chicoric acid, TmHY5 also targets the expression of TmbZIP1, which mediates ABA signalling and indirectly promotes the expression level of Tm4CL1 (Figure 7; Liu et al., 2023). In summary, TmHY5 can directly and indirectly regulate the biosynthesis of chicoric acid through both blue light and ABA signals. 

As an inhibitory factor of light signals, the COP1 protein accumulates in darkness and degrades in response to light (He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2021). In A. thaliana, A. annua and S. lycopersicum, COP1 interacts directly with HY5 proteins, and it can degrade proteins containing VP domains via the 26S proteasome (Lau et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Ponnu et al., 2019). In this study, we discovered that TmCOP1 can bind to the TmHY5 (Xiong et al., 2023) VP domain. By mutating the VP domain to the “AA” motif, we found that TmHY546AA-TmCOP1 cannot interact in vitro, demonstrating that the TmHY5-TmCOP1 module is conserved across species (Figure 6a–d). In the OE-TmCOP1 transgenic lines, the concentrations of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds exhibited an inverse relationship with TmHY5, indicating that chicoric acid is inhibited by darkness (Figure S4). An intriguing observation was made in yeast, where the binding affinity of TmCOP1-TmHY5 was greater in darkness than in red light, followed by blue light and white light, suggesting that TmCOP1-TmHY5 does not bind under blue or white light conditions (Figure 6e). It is also possible that blue light mediates the transport of COP1 protein from the nucleus to the cytoplasm (An et al., 2019; He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2021). Western blot experiments confirmed that both white and blue light decreased the TmCOP1 protein level in the OE-TmCOP1 transgenic lines when transitioning from darkness to white or blue light conditions, compared with MG132, a 26S proteasome inhibitor (Figure 6f). Further luciferase activity analysis demonstrated that TmCOP1 can directly inhibit the TmHY5-Tm4CL1 module, rather than the TmbZIP1-Tm4CL1 module, indicating that the TmCOP1-TmHY5 interaction is a key component in blue light-mediated chicoric acid biosynthesis (Figure 7g). We speculate that under dark conditions, TmCOP1 degrades TmHY5 through 26S ubiquitination, thereby inhibiting the accumulation of chicoric acid and its precursor compounds. Conversely, the decrease of TmCOP1 protein expression in blue light promotes the accumulation of chicoric acid in T. mongolicum. 

At the transcriptional level, blue light significantly upregulates the expression of TmCOP1, and its expression pattern is almost identical to TmHY5 at 2 h (Figures 2c and 7a). Through in vitro and in vivo experiments such as promoter analysis, Y1H and 

EMSA assays, it was confirmed that TmHY5 can target the G-box on proTmCOP1 and its own gene promoter proTmHY5, forming feedback regulation and its endogenous expression. Dual-LUC results indicated that TmHY5 can directly activate the expression of TmCOP1 and itself. In the previous analysis of light signal protein, transcription factor and synthase genes in this article, TmHY5 formed a transcriptional regulatory network with BBX21, PIFs, bHLHs and multiple transcription factors and light signal proteins (Yang et al., 2022; Figure 1g). Therefore, we need to further clarify the mechanism of action of modules such as TmHY5-BBX21, TmHY5-MYB111, TmHY5-TmPIF3 or TmCOP1- TmHY5-SPAs in the accumulation of chicoric acid (Fu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021). Taken together, we speculate that there is a pattern in the chicoric acid biosynthesis pathway of T. mongolicum: under dark conditions, TmCOP1 protein accumulates and interacts with TmHY5, inhibiting the expression of TmHY5 protein and negatively regulating the chicoric acid biosynthesis; under blue light conditions, TmCOP1 is degraded, while the TmHY5 is significantly increased at both transcriptional and protein levels. On the one hand, TmHY5 can directly promote the chicoric acid biosynthesis, and on the other hand, TmHY5 can promote the chicoric acid accumulation by increasing the expression level of TmbZIP1; meanwhile, blue light indirectly promotes the expression level of TmCOP1 through feedback regulation by promoting the expression level of TmHY5 (Figure 8). 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and treatments

Plants and seeds of T. mongolicum were collected from the Nanjing Botanical Garden, Gem Sun Yat-sen, Jiangsu Province, China. The seeds were planted in black pots measuring $1 0 \times 1 0$ cm and cultivated for six weeks at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ under a light/dark cycle of $1 6 / 8 ~ \mathrm { h }$ in a greenhouse. For 20 days, the plants were exposed to blue, red, or white light. The leaves were subsequently collected, rinsed with sterile water, dried on filter paper, frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for further analysis. T. mongolicum was treated with three phytohormones (GA, ABA and MeJA) at a concentration of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } _ { \ast }$ , along with NaCl at $2 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ , for $2 4 \ h ,$ following the methodology established in a previous study (Liu et al., 2021). Samples were collected at 0, 2, 4, 8 and $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ and then transferred to a $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ environment for qRT-PCR experiments. In the combined treatments involving different light conditions and the ABA hormone, a concentration of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ ABA was applied through both foliar spraying and root irrigation. The treatments were administered continuously for 7 days under various light conditions, with samples collected at 2 h, 8 h, 3 days and 7 days post-treatment. N. benthamiana plants were cultivated in $1 0 \times 1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ pots and maintained in a plant culture room at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ under a photoperiod of 16/8 h for 8 weeks and used for subcellular localization, dual-LUC and LCI analysis.

# Quantitative real-time PCR, gene and promoter cloning analysis

Leaves induced by blue, red and white light, as well as the normal roots, leaves and flowers of T. mongolicum, were analysed using a Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Sangon, Nanjing, China). The extracted RNA served as the template for first-strand cDNA synthesis, utilizing the UnionScript First-strand cDNA Synthesis Mix for quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) (SR512, Genestar, Nanjing, China). To validate the RNA-seq results, ten of the fortyone polyphenol biosynthetic enzyme genes were selected for qRT-PCR, specifically TmPAL1/2, TmC4H1, Tm4CL4, TmHCT1, TmHQT2, TmCHI1, TmF30 H and TmF3H (Table S1). The $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method was employed for analysis, with $\beta$ -actin as the reference gene for calculating relative expression levels (Liu et al., 2023). The means of three replicates were recorded, and reaction specificity was verified through melting curve analysis. 

The dandelion genome (GWH; https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/search/; Lin et al., 2022) served as a reference for cloning full-length TmHY5 and TmCOP1, using T. mongolicum leaf cDNA as the template based on existing transcriptome data. Phanta- Max DNA Polymerase (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) was utilized for cloning and obtaining the promoters of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1, TmHQT2, TmCHS2, TmANS2, TmCOP1, TmHY5 and TmbZIP1 (Table S5). These promoters were cloned using T. mongolicum 

leaf DNA as a template, connected to the pEASY $\textcircled{1}$ -T1 vector and subsequently sequenced to obtain the promoter sequence information. Cis-acting element analysis was conducted using the online software PlantCARE. 

# Plasmid construction and generation of transgenic lines

The full-length coding sequence of TmHY5 was cloned into the pHellsgate8-GFP vector, with the termination codon removed through homologous recombination. The recombinant vector (pHellsgate8-TmHY5-GFP) and the control vector (pHellsgate8- GFP) were individually transferred into Agrobacterium strains GV3101 and K599 using standard methods (Liu et al., 2021). The Agrobacterium strains, which harboured the positive and empty plasmids, were then resuspended to an optical density $\left( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } \right)$ of 0.6–0.8 using a filtration buffer composed of 100 mM AS, 10 mM MES and 10 mM ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ . For subcellular localization analysis, the Agrobacterium GV3101 suspension was injected into the lower epidermis of 8-week-old N. benthamiana leaves using a syringe (without a needle). The plants were subsequently allowed to grow under identical conditions for 48–72 h. A confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM 900, Zeiss, Germany) was employed to observe GFP fluorescence. 

To produce transgenic plants of T. mongolicum, a cut-dipbudding delivery system was utilized, as previously reported before (Cao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). A light source (LUYOR-3415, China) was used for preliminary screening. The kanamycin resistance gene (Kan), the green fluorescent protein gene (GFP ) and the TmHY5-GFP fusion gene were employed to distinguish wild-type (WT), empty vector (EV) and overexpression (OE) transgenic T. mongolicum lines. The digested product of TmHY5 was inserted into the sgRNA-35S-EGFP vector (Biorun, Wuhan, China). The recombinant gene-editing vector pAtU6-26-sgRNA-35S-TmHY5-EGFP-Cas9 was transformed into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens K599 (Biorun, Wuhan, China), resulting in a total of three gene-edited lines (CR#6, CR#9 and CR#19), one of which (CR#9) was homozygous and used for the experiment. Total plant DNA was extracted from specific leaf tissues using a Plant Genomic DNA Kit (Tiangen, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions to detect mutation sites in the target genes and screen for TmHY5 CRISPR-Cas9 knockout lines. 

# Determination of polyphenolic compounds

To quantitatively determine the selected components of T. mongolicum (chicoric acid and its precursor compounds, chlorogenic acid and caftaric acid; luteolin and rutin), high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was performed as previously described (Fu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2021, 2023). A precisely weighed amount of $1 . 0 \ 9$ fresh T. mongolicum sample was sonicated in $80 \%$ methanol for 45 min and subsequently filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ microporous filter membrane into liquidphase vials for analysis. The analysis utilized an Agilent 1260 chromatographic system equipped with a Phenomenex Gemini 5u C18 110A chromatographic column $( 5 ~ { \mu } { \mathsf { m } } \times 4 . 6 ~ { \mathsf { m } } { \mathsf { m } }$ $\times ~ 2 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m } ,$ . The flow rate was maintained at 1.0 mL/min, and the injection volume for each sample was $5 ~ \mu \iota$ . The column temperature was set to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , and the detection wavelength was 327 nm. Mobile phase A consisted of $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in water, while mobile phase B was methanol. The separation gradient for mobile phase B was as follows: $1 3 \%$ to $20 \%$ from 0 to 7 min; $20 \%$ to $30 \%$ from 7 to 18 min; $30 \%$ to $4 1 \%$ from 18 to 28 min; $4 1 \%$ to $45 \%$ from 28 to 35 min; and $45 \%$ back to $1 3 \%$ 

from 35 to 40 min. Each experiment included three replicates. For the analysis of anthocyanins, extraction was conducted as previously described, using commercial cyanidin-3-O-glucoside (Sigma, St. Louis, MO) as a standard for calibration curve construction, with absorbance measured at $5 2 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ (Huang et al., 2019; Xing et al., 2023). All samples were analysed using three biological replicates. 

# Dual-luciferase (LUC) assays

The pHellsgate8-TmHY5-GFP vector was constructed and subsequently transferred into Agrobacterium GV3101 to serve as an effector, with pHellsgate8-GFP utilized as a negative control. The promoters of TmPAL3, Tm4CL1, TmHQT2, TmCHS2, TmANS2, TmCOP1, TmHY5 and TmbZIP1 were cloned and fused with the pGreen0800-rec vector, resulting in the pGreen0800-promoters recombinant vectors. Those vectors were individually fused with pSoup19 (a helper vector) and co-transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. The effector and reporter strains were mixed in a 2 mL proportion, incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and then injected into the 8- week-old leaves of N. benthamiana. After 48 to 72 h of cultivation in the dark, dual-LUC assays were performed as previously described (Liu et al., 2023). The LUC/REN (Firefly luciferase/Renilla luciferase) ratio was measured to assess expression levels, with three replicates conducted for each experimental group. 

# Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays

The coding regions of the TmHY5 and TmbZIP1 were cloned into the pB42AD vector to construct the pB42AD-TmHY5 and pB42AD-TmbZIP1 expression vectors. The ABRE-cis motifs (NNACGTNN) and G-box motifs were present in the promoters of proTmPAL3, proTm4CL1, proTmHQT2, proTmCHS2, proTmANS2, proTmCOP1, proTmHY5 and proTmbZIP1, forming three tandem repeats within the pLacZ vectors (Table S5). The pB42AD-TmHY5 and pB42AD-TmbZIP1 vectors were individually cotransformed into the EGY48 yeast strain along with the recombinant pLacZ-ABREs, pLacZ-G-boxes and the empty pLacZ vectors. Positive clones were screened on a two-deficiency medium (SD/ Ura/ Trp) and subsequently transferred to a chromogenic medium (SD/ Ura/ Trp/+X-gal) for observation (Liu et al., 2021). 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays

TmHY5 was ligated into the pGBKT7 (BD) vector (Clontech Laboratories, Mountain View, CA, USA), while TmCOP1 was ligated into the pGADT7 (AD) vector (Clontech Laboratories, Mountain View, CA, USA). To facilitate segmentation and mutation validation, the TmCOP1 and TmHY5 sequences were reconstructed as vectors following the specifications in Table S6. The BD and AD vectors were then co-transformed into the AH109 yeast strain. Blue-positive clones were screened on SD/ Leu/ Trp medium (DDO) and subsequently transferred to SD/ Leu/ Trp/ His/ Ade $^ +$ X-a-gal (QDO + X-a-gal) medium for growth observation. Under different light conditions, the screened strains were incubated in a light incubator for 3, 5 and 7 days. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) and Luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assays

Both BiFC and LCI assays were conducted on tobacco. TmCOP1 was fused to the pXY106-nYFP vector, while TmHY5 was fused to the pXY104-cYFP vector via homologous recombination. The recombinant nYFP and cYFP vectors were individually 

transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. Agrobacterium containing the nYFP and cYFP vectors were grown to the same concentration and mixed in equal volumes for injection into tobacco leaves for 2–3 days under dark conditions. The BiFC assays were performed as previously reported (Liu et al., 2022). For the LCI assays, TmHY5 was fused to the N-terminal half of firefly luciferase (TmHY5-nLUC), while TmCOP1 was fused to the C-terminal half (TmCOP1-cLUC). The resulting constructs were co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves using Agrobacteriummediated infiltration. LUC activity was visualized two days postinfiltration (An et al., 2019). 

# Protein induction, purification and in vitro pull-down assays

The coding sequence (CDS) of TmHY5 was cloned into the pGEX-4T-1 vector to produce GST-tagged fusion protein, while the CDS of TmCOP1 was cloned into the pET30a vector to generate His-tagged fusion protein. Each vector was individually transformed into the E. coli strain BL21 (TSingke, TSC-E01). Cells were cultured in LB medium containing $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ ampicillin at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with shaking at 200 rpm. When the optical density at 600 nm $\left( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } \right)$ reached 0.4–0.6, isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to the culture to induce the expression of the target proteins. The induction conditions consisted of $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $2 0 { - } 2 4 \mathrm { h }$ with 0.2 mmol IPTG. E. coli BL21 harbouring the recombinant vectors were harvested by centrifugation at $2 4 0 0 \ g$ and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 10 min, then resuspended in Tris-HCl buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H } 7 . 4 \right)$ . Ultrasonic disruption was performed with an ultrasound frequency of 5 s on and 6 seconds off, lasting for a total of 40 min. Finally, the suspension was centrifuged at $9 5 0 0 \ : g$ for 10 min. The His-tagged fusion proteins were purified using Ni-NTA His-tag purification resin (Beyotime). Proteins were first washed with $1 5 \mathrm { m l }$ of column buffer, followed by elution with $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of elution buffer (Tris-HCl buffer containing $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ imidazole), and the protein concentration was determined using the BCA method (Epizyme, Shanghai, China). For the GST fusion proteins, GST-tag purification resin (Beyotime) was utilized, and elution was performed using $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ glutathione elution buffer (Tris-HCl pH 7.4). 

Recombinant pGEX-4T-GST-tagged TmHY5 proteins $1 0 0 ~ \mu \ g )$ were loaded into an affinity column and incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Following a wash with $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of column buffer, pET30a-Histagged TmCOP1 protein $( 1 0 0 ~ \mu \ g )$ was added and incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $4 { \mathrm { - } } 6 \ \mathsf { h }$ . Unbound proteins were removed using a soluble binding buffer consisting of $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Tris-HCl, 500 mM NaCl, $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ imidazole and 10 mM glutathione (pH 7.4). Bound proteins were eluted with a soluble elution buffer containing $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Tris-HCl, 500 mM NaCl and $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ imidazole (pH 7.4). Purified GST $( 5 0 ~ \mu \ g )$ served as a negative control. Immunoblot analysis was conducted using an anti-GST antibody and an anti-HIS antibody (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai, China), following the protocol established by An et al. (2019). Bound proteins were fractionated using $10 \%$ sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and then electroblotted onto PVDF (Millipore, Massachusetts, USA). Protein signals were visualized using SuperKineTM West Pico PLUS Chemiluminescent Substrate (Abbkine, Wuhan, China) and a Tanon imaging system (Tanon, Shanghai, China). 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA)

The CDS of TmHY5 and TmbZIP1 were ligated into the pCold-TF vector. Those recombinant vectors were expressed in the E. coli DE3 (Beijing Tsingke Biotech Co., Ltd.) to induce the production 

of TmHY5-His and TmbZIP1-His fusion proteins, which were subsequently purified as previously described (Liu et al., 2021). Two single-stranded DNA fragments containing a cis-acting element that interacts with the corresponding transcription factors were synthesized based on the promoter fragment and biotin-labelled by Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China), while unlabelled fragments served as competitors. A binding assay was conducted, and the protein–DNA complexes were separated on a $6 \%$ native polyacrylamide gel and electroblotted onto a HyBond nylon membrane (Biosharp, Hefei, China). 

# Western blot analysis

The pHellsgate8-TmCOP1-GFP transgenic T. mongolicum was produced using the aforementioned cut-dip-bud ding delivery system (Cao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). Transgenic seeds were grown in standard field conditions for one month before being transferred to darkness for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Subsequently, the seedlings were exposed to defined light treatments, including transitions from darkness to white light or blue light for specified durations. The treated seedling samples were collected and prepared for protein extraction, ensuring equal protein concentrations using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (C503061; Sangon). The samples were then loaded onto a $12 \%$ (w/v) SDS-PAGE gel, separated and transferred to a nitrocellulose blotting membrane (F619511; BBI) (An et al., 2019). We probed the membrane with an anti-GFP antibody (D191040; 1:3000 dilution; Sangon) and anti-HY5 antibody (PHY0264S; 1:2000 dilution; phytoAB), respectively; stripped the blot; and re-probed it with an anti-ACTIN antibody (D191048; 1:2000 dilution; Sangon) as a loading control.
