# ABSTRACT

Striga hermonthica, an obligate parasitic plant that causes severe agricultural damage, recognizes its hosts by sensing haustorium-inducing factors (HIFs). Perception of HIFs induces the rapid transformation of S. hermonthica radicles into prehaustoria, structures that enable host invasion and mature into haustoria. HIFs consist of various aromatic compounds, including quinones, lignin monomers, and flavonoids. However, the downstream molecular pathways that orchestrate these developmental events are largely unknown. Here, we report that S. hermonthica root-tip cells rapidly deposit lignin, a major cell wall component, in response to HIFs. In addition to enhancing lignin levels, HIFs strongly induce genes involved in lignin monomer biosynthesis and polymerization, including several respiratory burst oxidase homologs (RBOHs) and class III peroxidases. Disruption of lignin monomer biosynthesis compromises prehaustorium formation, whereas HIF-induced class III peroxidases facilitate the process by promoting lignification. Our study demonstrates that cell wall lignification is a converged cellular process downstream of various HIFs that guides root meristematic cells in prehaustorium development. 

Key words: parasitic plant, lignin, haustorium, haustorium-inducing factors

# INTRODUCTION

Lignin is an aromatic polymer and a major component of the secondary cell walls of terrestrial plants. Its recalcitrant nature provides mechanical support and acts as a defensive barrier against pathogens (Mitsuda et al., 2007; Zhao et al., 2013; Mutuku et al., 2019; Sato et al., 2021). In dicotyledonous plants, lignin typically consists of $p$ -hydroxyphenyl (H), guaiacyl (G), and syringyl (S) units, which differ in the number of methoxy groups at the 3- and 5-positions of the aromatic ring. Phenylalanine-derived $p$ -coumaric acid, an initial substrate for lignin biosynthesis, undergoes a series of side-chain modifications and aromatic methoxylations by multiple monolignol biosynthesis enzymes in the cinnamate/monolignol pathway. This process generates H-, G-, and S-type acids, coenzyme A 

(CoA) esters, aldehydes, and ultimately monolignols (e.g., $p$ -coumaryl, coniferyl, and sinapyl alcohols). Some precursors are also converted into non-canonical monolignols, such as caffeoyl alcohol (catechol [C]-type monolignol) and acylated monolignols (Boerjan et al., 2003; Chen et al., 2012; Pollegioni et al., 2015; Li et al., 2016). 

Monolignols are exported to the apoplast, where they undergo oxidative polymerization to form lignin polymers in the cell wall via the peroxidase- $\cdot \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ and/or laccase- ${ \bf \nabla } \cdot { \bf O } _ { 2 }$ oxidative systems (Shigeto and Tsutsumi, 2016; Tobimatsu and Schuetz, 2019). As major sources of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production in plants, the respiratory burst oxidase homolog (RBOH) gene family, which encodes NADPH oxidase enzymes, also contributes to lignin accumulation in some tissues, such as root endodermal Casparian strips 

(Sagi and Fluhr, 2006; Suzuki et al., 2011). Although monolignols and some of their precursors (collectively termed lignin monomeric compounds) are used as building blocks of lignin, they also function as signaling molecules in plant–plant communication (Whittaker and Feeny, 1971; Lynn and Chang, 1990; Xie et al., 2023). 

Parasitic plants develop specialized organs called haustoria, which invade host tissues to establish vascular connections that divert nutrients to the parasite (Yoshida et al., 2016). Haustorium development is triggered by external cues known as haustorium-inducing factors (HIFs) (Goyet et al., 2019). The initial HIF-induced haustorial structures, called prehaustoria or protohaustoria, appear as bulged root tips or hemispherical structures on the sides of roots and are surrounded by hair cells known as haustorial hairs (Furuta et al., 2021; Greifenhagen et al., 2024). The prehaustoria invade host tissues and mature into fully developed haustoria, during which cells differentiate into specialized types that facilitate nutrient uptake (Masumoto et al., 2021). Striga hermonthica is an obligate parasitic plant that severely reduces the yields of major crops, including rice, maize, and sorghum. When S. hermonthica radicles sense HIFs, such as quinones, lignin monomeric compounds, and flavonoids, they immediately halt meristematic activity at the root tip and instead promote radial cell expansion, forming a single terminal prehaustorium at the root tip (Keyes et al., 2000; Masumoto et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2022). Given the limited energy reserves in S. hermonthica seeds, immediate host parasitization through the rapid perception and transduction of HIF signals is crucial for survival. 

2,6-Dimethoxy- $_ { \cdot p }$ -benzoquinone (DMBQ) is a potent and wellknown quinone-type HIF first identified in root extracts from sorghum, a natural host of Striga (Chang and Lynn, 1986). Phenolic compounds analogous to DMBQ, including lignin monomeric compounds such as ferulic acid (a G-type lignin monomeric compound) and syringaldehyde (an S-type lignin monomeric compound), exhibit prehaustorium-inducing activity in many Orobanchaceae parasitic plants, such as Triphysaria versicolor, Phtheirospermum japonicum, Agalinis purpurea, and Striga spp. (Steffens et al., 1982; Albrecht et al., 1999; Cui et al., 2018). A structure–activity relationship study revealed that G- and S-type lignin monomeric compounds, which contain single and double methoxy groups at the 3- and 3/ 5-positions, respectively, act as HIFs, whereas H- and C-type lignin monomeric compounds without methoxy groups do not (Cui et al., 2018). The prehaustorium-inducing activity of quinones and flavonoids also appears to positively correlate with the number of methoxy groups (Steffens et al., 1982; Lynn, 1985; Smith et al., 1996; Cui et al., 2018). In addition, disruption of G- and S-type monolignol biosynthesis in Arabidopsis reduces its ability to induce prehaustorium formation in P. japonicum and S. hermonthica (Cui et al., 2018), which indicates that hostderived G- and S-type lignin monomeric compounds facilitate prehaustorium induction in parasitic plants. However, the mechanisms by which parasitic plants perceive and respond to these lignin monomeric compounds remain unexplored. 

Lignin monomeric compounds have long been postulated to be precursors of quinones, such as DMBQ (Keyes et al., 2001), because syringaldehyde (an S-type lignin precursor) and the 

structurally related syringic acid can be oxidatively converted to DMBQ in vitro by peroxidases (Frick et al., 1996). The Arabidopsis gene CANNOT RESPOND TO DMBQ1 (CARD1), which encodes a leucine-rich repeat receptor-like kinase, has been identified as a quinone sensor (Laohavisit et al., 2020). This suggests that Striga may sense lignin monomeric compounds through the recognition of oxidative quinone products by its CARD1 homologs, termed S. hermonthica CARD1-LIKEs (ShCADLs). However, genes that respond to DMBQ and syringic acid in S. hermonthica differ in both number and expression kinetics, suggesting that phenolic signaling is distinct from quinone signaling in prehaustorium induction (Aoki et al., 2022a, 2022b). 

Multiple transcriptomic analyses suggest the existence of a common or similar molecular pathway downstream of HIFs in Orobanchaceae parasitic plants. The peroxidase gene family, which is associated with lignin polymerization, is consistently upregulated in the haustorial tissue of three Orobanchaceae species: the facultative parasite T. versicolor and the obligate parasites S. hermonthica and Phelipanche aegyptiaca (Yang et al., 2015). Chemical inhibition of peroxidase activity suppresses prehaustorium formation in several species, including S. hermonthica (Smith et al., 1996; Wada et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). Furthermore, genes associated with cell wall modifications and cellular reduction–oxidation (redox) regulation are enriched in prehaustoria induced by DMBQ or syringic acid in T. versicolor, P. japonicum, and S. hermonthica (Matvienko et al., 2001; Ishida et al., 2016; Yoshida et al., 2019; Aoki et al., 2022b), suggesting that cell wall modification occurs during prehaustorium formation. However, the relationship between cell wall modification and HIF signaling remains unclear. 

In this study, we demonstrate that S. hermonthica root tips deposit lignin in response to HIFs and that externally supplied G- and S-type monolignols are simultaneously incorporated into cell wall lignin. HIFs upregulate enzymes involved in monolignol biosynthesis and polymerization, such as class III peroxidases and RBOHs. Disruption of these enzymatic functions reduces prehaustorium formation, suggesting that lignin deposition promotes this process. These observations underscore the diverse functions of lignin monomers in prehaustorium formation, acting both as substrates for lignification at the root tip and as signals that induce root-tip lignin deposition, thereby promoting prehaustorium formation.

Collectively, these results demonstrate that lignin deposition at the prehaustorial epidermis is an endogenous process independent of external monolignol supply. 

We next investigated whether external quinones and anthocyanidins that act as HIFs are incorporated into the cell wall. In vitro peroxidase-catalyzed polymerization of G-type monolignol (coniferyl alcohol) was carried out in the presence of HIFs: DMBQ, syringic acid (a non-monolignol phenolic compound), and malvidin (an anthocyanidin). The resulting synthetic lignin polymers, dehydrogenation polymers (DHPs), were structurally analyzed using two-dimensional (2D) nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. The results showed that syringic acid could be incorporated into the lignin polymers, albeit at a low level, whereas no incorporation was observed for DMBQ or malvidin (Supplemental Figure 4). Taken together, these findings indicate that S. hermonthica begins to deposit lignin at the root apical surface upon exposure to various HIFs and that monolignols, but not other HIFs, serve as lignin precursors incorporated into cell wall lignin. 

Based on the observation that lignin accumulates during prehaustorium formation regardless of external monolignol supply, we investigated whether endogenous monolignol biosynthesis in S. hermonthica contributes to this process. We used piperonylic acid (PA) to inhibit the monolignol biosynthesis pathway and examined its effects on prehaustorium formation. PA specifically targets CINNAMATE 4-HYDROXYLASE (C4H), which catalyzes the conversion of cinnamic acid to $p$ -coumaric acid, an early step in monolignol biosynthesis (Schalk et al., 1998; Naseer et al., 2012). The application of $5 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ or $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ PA along with various HIFs, including a lignin monomer and a related compound (S-DMAC and syringic acid), a quinone (DMBQ), and a flavonoid (peonidin), resulted in significantly reduced prehaustorium formation compared with HIF treatment alone (Figure 3E). Increasing HIF concentrations partially or fully rescued prehaustorium formation in the presence of $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ PA. These results indicate that the synthesis of lignin precursors after HIF exposure contributes to prehaustorium formation in S. hermonthica. 

# Dynamic diminishment of lignin layer during host infection

Given that lignin is a rigid biopolymer resistant to degradation, we investigated whether surface-lignified prehaustoria are still capable of infecting host roots by observing the dynamics of S-DMAC fluorescence during host infection. Prehaustoria in S. hermonthica seedlings were induced with S-DMAC for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then placed near rice roots. We found that fully developed prehaustoria, covered with a lignin layer as indicated by S-DMAC fluorescence, began to deform on the host-facing side, leading to tip expansion and eventual invasion of the host root (Figure 4A). This indicates that surface lignification 

does not restrict the apical regrowth and differentiation of the cells involved in host intrusion in response to host proximity. Notably, the S-DMAC signal was greatly reduced at the protrusion site of the prehaustorial tip 6 h after host placement. The signal subsequently disappeared in the haustorial regions that intruded into the host but remained at a similar intensity in other regions (at 12 and $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ postinfection). 

To investigate whether cell proliferation underlies prehaustorial apical regrowth and host invasion, we used 5-ethynyl- $\cdot 2 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyuridine (EdU) staining to track cell division during prehaustorium development and host invasion (Figure 4B). Prehaustoria were first induced with DMBQ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then placed near rice roots. During DMBQ exposure, root meristematic cells temporarily reduced their proliferation at 3 h. Between 6 and 12 h, the number of dividing cells increased and a bulging prehaustorium structure was formed. By 24 h, only a few dividing cells remained in the prehaustorium, consistent with its morphological establishment at this stage. However, host root proximity reinitiated cell proliferation at the prehaustorial apex, resulting in tip expansion (rightmost panel in Figure 4B), likely caused by the differentiation and proliferation of cells that invade host roots. These reproliferating prehaustorial apex cells spatiotemporally corresponded with diminished S-DMAC signals during invasion (Figure 4A), suggesting that cell proliferation during apical regrowth diluted the pre-existing cell wall lignin. Overall, these results indicate the flexibility of prehaustorial cells in S. hermonthica and the dynamic regulation of cell wall lignification during prehaustorium formation and host invasion in parasitic plants. 

# HIFs induce the expression of genes associated with monolignol biosynthesis and lignin polymerization during prehaustorium formation

Monolignol biosynthesis and polymerization are central to lignin production in plants, and the genes involved are wellcharacterized in Arabidopsis (Vanholme et al., 2019). Given the induction of lignin deposition during prehaustorium formation, we hypothesized that external HIFs transcriptionally activate lignin biosynthesis genes in S. hermonthica. To address this, we analyzed DMBQ-induced expression changes in genes that encode monolignol biosynthesis enzymes in S. hermonthica using reverse transcription–quantitative real-time PCR (RT–qPCR). A BLAST search identified 23 S. hermonthica homologs of Arabidopsis genes that encode monolignol biosynthetic enzymes, including cinnamate 4-hydroxylase (C4H), 4-coumarate:CoA ligase 2 (4CL2), hydroxycinnamoyl-CoA:shikimate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HCT), 4-coumaroyl ester 3-hydroxylase 1 $( \mathsf { C } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { H } 1 )$ , caffeoyl shikimate esterase (CSE), caffeoyl CoA O-methyltransferase 1 (CCoAOMT1), caffeic acid O-methyltransferase 1 (COMT1), cinnamoyl-CoA reductase 1 (CCR1), ferulate 5-hydroxylase 1 (F5H1), and cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase 6 (CAD6) (Figure 5A and Supplemental Table 1). Within 12 h of DMBQ treatment, 16 of the 23 genes were significantly upregulated by a range of 2- to 15-fold (Figure 5B). These findings suggest that lignin deposition during prehaustorium formation is accompanied by the upregulation of monolignol biosynthesis genes.

# Divergent roles of peroxidase activity and RBOHmediated ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation in prehaustorium formation

Although the importance of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production and peroxidase activity in S. hermonthica prehaustorium formation has been suggested previously (Wada et al., 2019), their roles remain unclear. Our results suggest that peroxidases and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ may contribute to prehaustorium formation by facilitating lignification. To test this possibility, we used chemical inhibitors, namely diphenyleneiodonium chloride (DPI, an inhibitor of RBOHs), potassium iodide (KI, an ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ scavenger), and salicylhydroxamic acid (SHAM, an inhibitor of peroxidases), and evaluated S-DMAC accumulation in prehaustoria. As high concentrations of these inhibitors completely block prehaustorium formation (Wada et al., 2019), which would prevent the observation of lignification with S-DMAC, we used lower concentrations $1 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ DPI, 1 mM KI, and $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ SHAM) and observed both S-DMAC signals and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation. Under these conditions, about $30 \%$ of S. hermonthica seedlings formed prehaustoria (Figure 7A). DPI and KI reduced ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation in prehaustoria (Supplemental Figure 9) but had minimal effects on S-DMAC accumulation throughout the root, including the upper root and the prehaustoria (Figure 7B). Conversely, SHAM slightly reduced ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation (Supplemental Figure 9) but greatly reduced S-DMAC accumulation in the prehaustorium (Figure 7B and Supplemental Figure 10). These results highlight the importance of peroxidases in the incorporation of monolignols into cell walls in S. hermonthica. They also demonstrate that reactive oxygen species (ROS) and peroxidase inhibitors suppress prehaustorium formation through different mechanisms. The reduction in ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels caused by DPI and KI likely disrupts cellular redox status, whereas SHAM-mediated interference with peroxidase-mediated monolignol polymerization could disrupt the endogenous lignification processes required for efficient prehaustorium formation (Figure 3E). Thus, ROS accumulation and peroxidase-driven lignification may function as parallel mechanisms downstream of HIFs that mediate prehaustorium formation. 

To further investigate the roles of ROS accumulation and peroxidase-mediated lignification in prehaustorium formation, we treated S. hermonthica seedlings with horseradish peroxidase (HRP) and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , along with low concentrations of several lignin monomers, including both HIFs (coniferaldehyde, coniferyl alcohol, and sinapyl alcohol) and non-HIFs $( p$ -coumaric acid and caffeic acid). For comparison, we also included peonidin and malvidin, flavonoid HIFs that are not HRP substrates in lignin polymerization. As shown in Figure 7C, HRP application markedly enhanced prehaustorium formation with the G-type lignin monomeric compounds, specifically coniferaldehyde and coniferyl alcohol, as well as the S-type lignin monomeric compound sinapyl alcohol (>12-fold increase). HRP had no effect on $p$ -coumaric acid and caffeic acid, which are H- and C-type lignin monomeric com-

pounds, respectively, and have no methoxy groups. In addition, HRP had little to no effect on peonidin or malvidin $( \mathord { \sim } 1 . 7$ -fold increase). Notably, HRP alone induced a substantial increase in prehaustorium formation $\mathrm { ( D M S O ~ + ~ H R P ) }$ , as reported previously (Wada et al., 2019). Interestingly, unlike HRP, exogenous ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ did not noticeably affect any of these experimental conditions and did not synergistically enhance the effects of HRP (Figure 7C), thus supporting our conclusion regarding the distinct roles of peroxidases and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ in the regulation of prehaustorium formation (Figure 7A and 7B; Supplemental Figure 9). Collectively, these results demonstrate that peroxidases substantially enhance prehaustorium formation in the presence of various HIFs, with the strongest preference for monomeric G- and S-type lignin compounds. This suggests that peroxidasemediated monolignol polymerization promotes prehaustorium formation. 

# Class III peroxidases facilitate prehaustorium formation

Our results suggest that peroxidases are important for prehaustorium formation, which prompted us to investigate which members of the class III peroxidase subfamily regulate this process. We cloned three class III peroxidases from S. hermonthica (SHERM_03329, SHERM_15945, and SHERM_17525) that were upregulated by DMBQ, syringic acid, and during host infection (Figure 6E). We fused their C-termini with mCherry and expressed them in the hairy roots of the model parasitic plant P. japonicum. We then quantified the number of prehaustoria in each transgenic root following DMBQ treatment. Based on BLAST searches, we named these genes ShPER52, ShPER2, and ShPER7, because they share the highest amino acid sequence identities with Arabidopsis PER52 (PRX52), PER2 (PRX2), and PER7 (PRX7), respectively. As a control, we used the class III peroxidase SHERM_02279, which is upregulated during host infection but exhibits low expression levels during prehaustorium formation. Given that epidermal cells can recruit monolignols in S. hermonthica (Figure 2), we hypothesized that the root epidermis is important for early HIF recognition. Therefore, we chose the PjABCB4 promoter $( P j A B C B 4 p )$ , which appeared to be active in the root epidermal cell layer and stele (PjABCB4p-mCherry; Supplemental Figure 11), similar to the Arabidopsis ABCB4 (PGP4) promoter (Terasaka et al., 2005; Cho et al., 2007; Wakatake et al., 2018). All peroxidases were expressed in the epidermis and stele under the control of $\mathsf { P i } A B C B 4 p$ (Supplemental Figure 11), and the corresponding transgenic hairy roots did not produce prehaustoria under normal growth conditions without HIFs. After DMBQ treatment, hairy roots expressing PjABCB4p-ShPRX52- mCherry and PjABCB4p-ShPRX2-mCherry exhibited significantly more prehaustoria than the control roots expressing PjABCB4pmCherry (Figure 7D). In contrast, the introduction of ShPRX7 or the host-specific responsive SHERM_02279 did not increase the number of prehaustoria. Overall, our results suggest that HIFs induce several class III peroxidases that facilitate prehaustorium formation.

In the context of plant parasitism, lignin production in host plants affects their interactions with parasitic plants both positively and negatively. For example, the Arabidopsis mutant med5a/5b ref8, which produces only H-type lignin and lacks G- and S-type lignin, and f5h, which lacks S-type lignin, exhibit reduced capacity for S. hermonthica prehaustorium induction (Cui et al., 2018). Certain resistant plants deposit lignin at infection sites to defend against parasitic plants, as reported for tomatoes resisting Cuscuta (dodder) and rice resisting S. hermonthica (Mutuku et al., 2019; Jhu et al., 2022). In this study, we demonstrated that lignin in parasitic plants also contributes to 

host-parasite interactions by promoting prehaustorium formation, thereby revealing additional roles of lignin in the complex interplay between parasitic plants and hosts. 

Actively dividing root apical meristems produce little lignin, except under certain oxidative stresses, such as excess toxic heavy metal exposure, which causes lignin accumulation at the root tip and inhibits root elongation (Sasaki et al., 1996; Kovac et al., 2018). Germinated S. hermonthica seeds exhibit a typical root meristematic structure (Xiao et al., 2022), and like normal plants, they produce little to no lignin at the elongating radicle tips. However, they can rapidly induce local lignin deposition in response to various HIFs, including DMBQ (Figures 1, 2, and 3). These findings demonstrate that lignification is an intricate cellular process tightly coupled with prehaustorium formation. At the cellular level, the root tip epidermis is likely the primary site of HIF-dependent lignin deposition by peroxidases, as shown by our monolignol probe observations and inhibitor assays. By pharmacologically disrupting lignin production through the inhibition of either monolignol biosynthesis or polymerization, we further demonstrated the functional importance of lignin for the transformation of root tips into prehaustoria. Notably, roottip lignification begins within 3 h, which corresponds with a temporary decrease in root meristematic cell division and the initiation of tip swelling, which is associated with cell expansion and redivision (Figures 1E and 4B). Given that lignin is a rigid biopolymer, we hypothesize that its induction at the root tip enhances cell wall stiffness and potentially acts as a secondary signal to promote rapid cell-fate changes in the root meristematic region. Consistent with this hypothesis, mechanical stimuli are important signals that induce cell differentiation and promote prehaustorium formation in the stem parasite Cuscuta (dodder) (Kaga et al., 2020). Lignin may also help stabilize and maintain the prehaustorium structure for later host invasion because prehaustorial cells can revert to root meristematic cells, thereby losing their invasive ability. For example, a Striga radicle tip emerges from the prehaustorium if the seedlings are placed in water after brief exposure to syringic acid or DMBQ (Kim et al., 1998). In summary, continuous lignin deposition at the root apex may serve as both a stimulus and a physical constraint; it supports cell progression and spatial reorganization toward prehaustoria and ensures that Striga seedlings maintain their invasive potential after HIF exposure. 

Lignin is highly resistant to biodegradation, and only a few organisms, such as white-rot fungi and some bacteria, are capable of degrading it using various lignolytic enzymes (Pollegioni et al., 2015). Once deposited, lignin remains in the tissue. However, Ursache et al. (2021) reported that the emergence of lateral roots can facilitate the degradation and subsequent breakage of the Casparian strip, a lignin-based paracellular diffusion barrier in endodermal cells. Similarly, we observed that the lignin layer at the prehaustorial surface locally diminished as the prehaustorial apical cells proliferated, which potentially facilitates protrusion into host roots (Figure 4A). Since the lignin barrier may disturb intimate interactions during host infection, these results highlight the importance of flexible and tightly controlled manipulation of cell wall lignin (both deposition and disruption) via plastic remodeling of Striga haustorial cell lignin during prehaustorium formation and host infection. 

Peroxidases are essential for S. hermonthica prehaustorium formation (Wada et al., 2019) and are highly expressed in the haustoria of various parasitic Orobanchaceae plant species (Yang et al., 2015; Yoshida et al., 2019; Kokla et al., 2022). However, their mechanistic roles in haustorium organogenesis remain unclear. Here, we provide several lines of evidence suggesting that class III peroxidases are crucial mediators of HIF-induced root-tip lignification and prehaustorium formation. A previous study reported that 28 class III peroxidases are upregulated in haustoria invading rice roots (Yoshida et al., 2019). Among them, we found nine that were highly upregulated in HIF-induced prehaustoria (Figure 6E). Notably, no class I peroxidases were upregulated in any prehaustorium-inducing conditions. Class III peroxidases, a large multigene family found only in plants, oxidize phenolic compounds using ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ . They contribute to multiple biological processes, including lignification, stress resistance, redox regulation, and cell elongation (Shigeto and Tsutsumi, 2016; Veljovic Jovanovi  c et al., 2018  ). In contrast, class I peroxidases primarily use ascorbate (Zamocky et al., 2010). We show that ectopic expression of two HIF-responsive class III peroxidases, ShPER52 and ShPER2, in P. japonicum led to an increased number of prehaustoria following DMBQ treatment (Figure 7D). However, ShPER7 did not increase prehaustorium formation relative to the empty-vector control, which may be due to differences in the experimental system, as P. japonicum may lack the downstream signaling machinery for ShPER7-induced prehaustorium formation. Thus, it remains possible that PER7 may facilitate prehaustorium formation in S. hermonthica. The Arabidopsis per52 (Atprx52) and per2 (Atprx2) mutant exhibit reduced levels of lignin in the stem (Shigeto et al., 2013; Fernandez-Perez et al., 2015). These results suggest that some class III peroxidases are involved in root-tip lignification and recruit external monolignols, if present in the environment, to the surface of the prehaustorial cell wall, as shown by the impaired tissue accumulation of the monolignol probe upon treatment with the peroxidase inhibitor SHAM (Figure 7B). Other expected roles of HIF-induced peroxidases include facilitating the oxidative conversion of phenolic compounds to quinones such as DMBQ. Because DMBQ is rarely detected in root exudates (Chang and Lynn, 1986; Wang et al., 2020); Keyes et al., 2001 proposed that S. hermonthica seedlings constitutively secrete ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and peroxidases to facilitate secretion and conversion of host-derived cell wall phenolics, including the lignin precursor sinapic acid, into quinones. Our current data support this model by demonstrating that HRP application greatly enhances prehaustorium formation in S. hermonthica when exposed to lignin precursors and syringic acid (Figure 7C) (Wada et al., 2019). Importantly, HRP application was previously shown to enhance the effects of DMBQ (Wada et al., 2019). We found that genes involved in monolignol biosynthesis, along with class III peroxidases, are elevated by DMBQ. This suggests that the enhanced oxidation of endogenously produced lignin monomeric compounds by peroxidases contributes to prehaustorium formation. Collectively, our results indicate that HIF-induced class III peroxidases may contribute to prehaustorium formation through multiple mechanisms in S. hermonthica, i.e., by polymerizing monolignols into lignin and converting them into quinones. While the former affects cell wall rigidity, the latter might amplify DMBQ signaling by generating more quinones within the tissue. 

Our findings highlight the importance of lignin in parasitic plant organogenesis. We propose a model (Figure 7E) in which Gand S-type lignin monomers are perceived as HIFs, possibly through conversion to quinones and detection by potential quinone sensors such as ShCADLs, or by unknown phenolic sensors (Laohavisit et al., 2020). The HIF signals then activate genes associated with lignin monomer biosynthesis and polymerization, including class III peroxidases, to drive root-tip lignification. Class III peroxidases facilitate not only the polymerization of endogenous monolignols but also the incorporation of external lignin monomers into the prehaustorial cell wall and the conversion of lignin monomers to quinones. These findings underscore the dual roles of external monolignols during prehaustorium formation, acting both as direct prehaustorium-inducing signals and as building blocks for lignin that collectively facilitate prehaustorium formation by driving tissue lignification. However, the precise mechanisms of monolignol perception, including the involvement of ShCADLs or other receptors, remain to be determined in future studies. In addition, investigating the temporal and spatial dynamics of lignin polymerization and degradation during host invasion will provide deeper insights into how parasitic plants regulate lignification to facilitate infection. Unraveling these processes will be crucial for understanding how S. hermonthica dynamically controls cell wall remodeling to optimize parasitism. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

S. hermonthica (Del.) Benth seeds were collected in Sudan and kindly provided by the late Prof. A.G. Babiker (University of Khartoum, Sudan). The seeds were surface-sterilized using $20 \%$ commercial bleach (approximately $6 \%$ sodium hypochlorite; Kao, Tokyo, Japan) and preconditioned in water for 1 week. Germination was then induced with 10 nM strigol (Hirayama and Mori, 1999) for 1 day before prehaustorium induction experiments. The preconditioning and prehaustorium induction were performed at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under dark conditions. 

# Prehaustorium induction assay

Germinated S. hermonthica seedlings were transferred to an aqueous solution containing various chemicals, including HIFs and inhibitors. The preparation of ROS inhibitors and SHAM has been previously described (Wada et al., 2019). Prehaustorium formation was assessed after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ o f treatment using a stereo microscope (Zeiss Stemi 2000-C). All experiments included at least three biological replicates and at least 50 S. hermonthica seedlings. All assays were performed in 96-well plates. 

# Lignin staining

Phloroglucinol staining was performed as previously described (Mutuku et al., 2019). Briefly, S. hermonthica prehaustoria were hand-sectioned longitudinally along the central axis of the radicles, incubated for 5 min in $50 \%$ ethanol (v/v), $1 8 \%$ HCl (v/v), and $1 \%$ phloroglucinol (w/v), and mounted on glass slides. Samples were observed using a Leica DMI3000B microscope. For basic fuchsin staining, S. hermonthica seedlings were treated with DMSO (control) or HIFs for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and rinsed three times with water. Samples were then immersed overnight in $0 . 2 \%$ basic fuchsin dissolved in ClearSee (Kurihara et al., 2015). After staining, the samples were washed 4–5 times with ClearSee, with the final wash lasting at least 1 h, before microscopic observation (Olympus FV3000). To visualize cell outlines, samples were further stained with $0 . 1 \%$ calcofluor white in ClearSee for 15 min, followed by three washes with ClearSee before observation. 

# Observation of fluorescence-tagged monolignols

The DMAC fluorophore and DMAC-tagged monolignols (H-DMAC, G-DMAC, and S-DMAC) were synthesized as previously described (Tobimatsu et al., 2013b). These compounds were dissolved in DMSO to prepare $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ stock solutions, which were then diluted with water to prepare working solutions at specified concentrations. S. hermonthica seedlings were incubated in the working solution for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in darkness at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . Whole seedlings were briefly washed with water, mounted on glass slides, and imaged using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss LSM 700) with excitation/emission wavelengths of 405/415– 476 nm for DMAC and 555/590–660 nm for PI. Maximum-intensity projections were generated by stacking single-plane images taken from the surface to the middle plane of the prehaustorium. For time-lapse imaging, an S. hermonthica seedling was placed in a glass-bottom dish (Matsunami, D910400) containing the S-DMAC solution and immediately subjected to time-lapse imaging (Carl Zeiss LSM 700) with a time interval of 20 min. The fluorescence intensities of S-DMAC and PI were quantified using ImageJ (version 1.47) and displayed in a linear intensity plot. To quantify S-DMAC fluorescence intensity after SHAM treatment, surface images of each prehaustorium were captured using a Carl Zeiss LSM 700 microscope under consistent light intensity across samples. The mean fluorescence intensity was calculated using the ‘‘Histogram’’ function in ZEN 2009 software. 

# ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ staining with carboxyl-H2DFFDA

Following prehaustorium induction, whole seedlings were soaked for 15 min at room temperature in $\uparrow \times$ PBS buffer containing $0 . 1 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ carboxyl-H2DFFDA (Invitrogen), then washed twice with 13 PBS before observation. ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation was observed using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss LSM 710) and a stereo microscope (Olympus SZX16) equipped with a laser suitable for green fluorescence signals. 

# Histochemical staining of peroxidase activity

To assess endogenous peroxidase activity, S. hermonthica seedlings were treated with $1 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ DMBQ for varying durations. The seedlings were then transferred to DAB solution (Sigma-Aldrich; D0426-50SET), incubated for 2.5 min, washed twice with water, and observed under a stereo microscope (Olympus SZX16). The DAB solution was prepared by dissolving one tablet in 5 mL of water according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# EdU staining

Seedlings and prehaustoria induced by DMBQ were subjected to EdU staining and observed using a confocal laser microscope (Olympus FV3000), as previously described (Cui et al., 2020). 

# RT–qPCR analysis

Surface-sterilized S. hermonthica seedlings were incubated in water for 1 week and then treated with 10 nM strigol in a round Petri dish for 1 day to induce germination. Next, $1 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ DMBQ was added, and samples were collected at 0, 3, 6, 12, and $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Total RNA was extracted using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany). First-strand cDNA was synthesized using a ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Kit (Toyobo; FSQ-101). qPCR was performed using a Thunderbird SYBR qPCR Mix Kit (Toyobo; A4251K). The housekeeping gene PP2A was selected from a previous report (Fernandez-Aparicio et al., 2013) and was validated in this study. Primers were designed for S. hermonthica genes homologous to Arabidopsis lignin biosynthesis genes using sequences from Sh14Contig (Yoshida et al., 2019). The corresponding genes in the S. hermonthica genome, along with their SHERM IDs (Qiu et al., 2022), are listed in Supplemental Table 1. The amplification specificity of all primers in this study was validated by electrophoresis after PCR and before RT–PCR. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Data 3. 

# Vector construction

The promoter region of PjABCB4 (2413 bp upstream of the start codon) was amplified by PCR and inserted into a level-1 vector, along with mCherry (CDS module) and HSP (terminator module), using the Golden Gate method (Engler et al., 2014). A SmaI restriction site was included between the PjABCB4 promoter and mCherry. The CDS sequences encoding peroxidases were amplified from S. hermonthica cDNA using the primers listed in Supplemental Data 3 and were inserted into the SmaI site using the in-fusion cloning method. The original PjABCB4-mCherry vector was used as a control. 

# Hairy root transformation in P. japonicum

Agrobacterium rhizogenes strain LBA1334 was transformed with the constructed vectors via heat shock and then used to infect P. japonicum seedlings to induce hairy roots, as previously described (Ishida et al., 2016). Hairy roots that exhibited red fluorescent signals were marked, and the number of prehaustoria was quantified after plants were grown on $0 . 5 \%$ agar medium without HIFs for 7 days. Due to variations in the number of lateral hairy roots among plants, prehaustorium density was calculated by dividing the total number of prehaustoria by the number of root segments in each plant. This value was used to assess the prehaustorium-inducing ability of different transformants. For fluorescence imaging, hairy roots were excised and observed under a microscope (Olympus FV3000) with an RFP filter. 

# Chemical analysis of lignin

S. hermonthica seedlings were freeze-dried, ground with a TissueLyser II (QIAGEN), and extracted with water and $80 \%$ ethanol to obtain cell wall residues. The lignin content and composition of S. hermonthica cell walls were determined using the high-throughput thioglycolic acid method (Suzuki et al., 2009). Synthetic lignins (DHPs) were produced via the HRP-catalyzed co-polymerization of coniferyl alcohol and HIFs (DMBQ, syringic acid, dihydroxy-2,6-dimethoxybenzene [the reduced form of DMBQ], and malvidin), at a coniferyl alcohol:HIFs ratio of 96:4 (mol/mol), as described previously (Lam et al., 2017). The resulting DHPs were acetylated in a 1:1 (v/v) mixture of pyridine and acetic anhydride (Mansfield et al., 2012), dissolved in chloroform-d, and subjected to NMR analysis using an Avance III 800US system (Bruker Biospin) equipped with a cryogenically cooled 5-mm TCI gradient probe. Adiabatic heteronuclear single-quantum coherence (HSQC) NMR experiments were performed using a standard implementation (‘‘hsqcetgpsp.3’’) (Mansfield et al., 2012; Tobimatsu et al., 2013a). Data were processed and analyzed with TopSpin 4.2 software (Bruker Biospin), using chloroform solvent peaks (dC/dH: 77.0/7.26 ppm) as an internal reference. HSQC plots were generated using typical matched Gaussian apodization in F2, squared cosinebell apodization, and one level of linear prediction (16 coefficients) in F1 (Mansfield et al., 2012; Tobimatsu et al., 2013a). 

# Heatmap of peroxidases

Class III and class I peroxidase genes with SHERM gene IDs were identified (Supplemental Figure 5) using the S. hermonthica genome database (Qiu et al., 2022). Expression profiles following DMBQ and syringic acid treatment were obtained from transcriptomic data and expressed as transcripts per million (TPM) (Aoki et al., 2022b). Genes with consistently low expression levels (sum of TPM values less than 4 across all conditions) were excluded. The filtered list of peroxidases, along with associated TPM values, is provided in Supplemental Data 1. Fold changes for each treatment were calculated as the ratio of the mean TPM in treated samples to the mean TPM in mock (water) samples at each time point. Fold changes in class III and class I peroxidases during rice infection were obtained from a previous report, where each gene was assigned an ShContig gene ID (Yoshida et al., 2019). To compare these two datasets, a gene-pairing analysis was conducted to match ShContig IDs with SHERM IDs for each peroxidase using BLAST 

searches (blastn and blastp) (Supplemental Data 1). Most genes with SHERM IDs in the S. hermonthica genome were successfully matched to unique corresponding genes with ShContig IDs. A few genes lacked a corresponding match, possibly due to differences in sequence assembly between RNA sequencing (32 assembled sequences) and genomic sequencing (56 assembled sequences). After unifying the gene IDs, an additional heatmap was generated for the expression profiles of peroxidases from the initial heatmap that were upregulated during rice infection (fold change ${ > } 2$ ). 

# Phylogenetic analysis

P. japonicum and S. hermonthica genes homologous to Arabidopsis RBOHs were identified by BLASTing the full-length amino acid sequences of Arabidopsis RBOHs against the protein databases of P. japonicum (Cui et al., 2020) and S. hermonthica (Yoshida et al., 2019). The Arabidopsis genes FRO1 and FRO2, which encode ferric chelate reductases and share the highest sequence similarity with RBOHs, were used as an outgroup in the initial phylogenetic tree construction to identify RBOH homologs. The SHERM IDs (Qiu et al., 2022) and corresponding ShContig IDs (Yoshida et al., 2019) are provided in Supplemental Data 3. A phylogenetic tree of S. hermonthica peroxidases was constructed by querying the S. hermonthica protein database with 73 class III peroxidases from Arabidopsis to identify both class I and class III peroxidases. Full-length amino acid sequences were aligned using ClustalW, and unrooted phylogenetic trees were constructed using MEGA X (version 10.1.8) with 500 bootstrap replicates.
