# SUMMARY

Domesticated soybean ( ) varieties can be divided into three major groups based on seed coat color: yellow (colorless), bicolored (saddle), and black. In yellow cultivars, the expression of the gene encoding chalcone synthase, essential for flavonoid biosynthesis, is inhibited by post-transcriptional gene silencing (PTGS). In saddle cultivars, PTGS is spatially inhibited in the central region around the hilum, which is black. However, the molecular mechanism of this region-specific inhibition of PTGS remains unclear. This study examined the relationship between Dicer-like 4 (DCL4), essential for PTGS, and flavonoid aglycons, which can inhibit DCL4. In the immature seed coat, DCL4 activity was specifically detected in the region that becomes colorless after seed maturation, but it was not detected in the region that becomes black after seed maturation, although its expression level had no difference. By contrast, phenolic compounds, including flavonoids, accumulated specifically in the region that becomes black. Especially, quercetin accumulated specifically in the central region of immature saddle seed coat and inhibited the dicing activity of DCL4. Furthermore, flavonoids highly accumulated in the funiculi of saddle and black but not yellow cultivars, and immature seeds cultured  had reduced phenolic compounds in their seed coats. These results indicate that flavonoid aglycons including quercetin transported from the maternal tissues via the funiculus, accumulated in the central region of the immature seed coat, and induced bicolor pigmentation in the saddle seed coat by region-specific inhibition of DCL4 (PTGS). 

Keywords: bicolor pigmentation, DCL4, flavonoid, funiculus, PTGS, quercetin, saddle soybean, seed coat. 

# INTRODUCTION

RNA silencing, also known as gene silencing by RNA interference (RNAi), is a widely conserved molecular mechanism in eukaryotes. It protects host cells from parasitic nucleic acids, such as viruses and transposons, and regulates gene expression (Baulcomb, 2004). The regulatory mechanisms of gene expression by RNA silencing can be divided into two types: transcriptional gene silencing (TGS), which suppresses transcription itself through RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM), and post-transcriptional gene silencing (PTGS), which 

suppresses gene expression through mRNA degradation (Borges & Martienssen, 2015). Small interfering RNAs (siRNAs), which induce RNA silencing, are produced from double-stranded RNAs (dsRNAs) by an RNase III-family endonuclease Dicer (Fukudome & Fukuhara, 2017). Most plants, including the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana, have four Dicer-like proteins (DCL1-4), each of which is involved in a specific RNA silencing pathway. DCL3 generates 24-nt siRNAs that function in TGS, while DCL4 produces 21-nt siRNAs that function in PTGS (Fukudome & Fukuhara, 2017). 

One of the most conspicuous examples of traits caused by RNA silencing (PTGS) is the absence of color in plant tissues that results from the inhibition of the expression of genes coding for pigment biosynthetic enzymes. This phenomenon was initially discovered in transgenic petunia plants into which the chalcone synthase (CHS) gene, which plays an important role in anthocyanin biosynthesis upstream of the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway, had been introduced (Napoli et al., 1990; van der Krol et al., 1990). Furthermore, the partial or complete absence of color in plant tissues induced by endogenous PTGS has also been reported in non-transgenic, that is, non-genetically modified (GM), plants. For example, in non-transgenic petunia plants with bicolored petals, such as the Star and Picotee cultivars, PTGS targeting the CHS gene occurs specifically in the colorless (white) regions of the petals (Griesbach et al., 2007; Koseki et al., 2005). In this region-specific PTGS, tandemly duplicated CHS genes generate siRNA in a white tissue-specific manner, resulting in partial loss of color, inducing a bicolored pigmentation pattern in petals (Kasai et al., 2013; Morita et al., 2012). The molecular mechanisms underlying the coexistence of PTGS-induced and non-induced regions in petals with the same genetic backgrounds have to date not been resolved. 

Soybean [Glycine max (L.) Merr.] is another well-known and well-studied plant in which anthocyanin biosynthesis is inhibited in a tissue-specific manner by naturally occurring PTGS (Senda et al., 2004; Tuteja et al., 2004). Soybean is a major legume crop cultivated worldwide for food and feed and is an important source of vegetable oil and protein. Domesticated soybean cultivars can be divided into three main groups based on the distribution of anthocyanins in their seed coats (Kajiya-Kanegae et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020). The first group comprises yellow cultivars with a colorless seed coat in which no anthocyanins accumulate. The second group includes black cultivars with a black seed coat in which anthocyanin pigments accumulate throughout their seed coats. The third group comprises bicolored cultivars in which pigments accumulate only in a limited area around the hilum, where the seed attaches to the seed pod. The distribution of anthocyanins in the soybean seed coat is genetically regulated by the I locus. For example, soybean cultivars containing the I locus have a yellow (colorless) seed coat, cultivars containing the i locus have a black seed coat, and cultivars containing the $\boldsymbol { j } ^ { j }$ locus have a bicolored seed coat with only a pigmented hilum (hilum-pigmented cultivars). The I locus interacts with the $K \eta$ locus to determine the distribution of pigments in the seed coat. For instance, cultivars containing the $i ^ { j }$ locus and the recessive $k \uparrow$ locus have a bicolored seed coat with a saddle pattern, in which pigments spread to a saddle-shaped region around the hilum (Cho et al., 2017). 

An inverted repeat sequence within the CHS gene cluster is found in both the I locus of yellow cultivars and 

the $\boldsymbol { j } ^ { j }$ locus of bicolored cultivars, but not in black cultivars with the i allele (Kajiya-Kanegae et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020). CHS siRNAs accumulate specifically in the yellow region of the yellow and bicolored seed coats (Cho et al., 2013; Tuteja et al., 2009) and cause endogenous PTGS of CHS, inhibiting anthocyanin biosynthesis (Kasai et al., 2007; Kurauchi et al., 2011; Senda et al., 2004; Tuteja et al., 2004). This PTGS occurs specifically in the seed coat (Cho et al., 2017; Tuteja et al., 2009). Therefore, flavonoids and isoflavonoids are similarly produced outside the seed coat in yellow, bicolored, and black cultivars. Furthermore, PTGS is not induced in the black area surrounding the hilum of the saddle seed coat, and anthocyanins accumulate in this region (Cho et al., 2017). 

Flavonoid pigments, that is, anthocyanins and proanthocyanidins, found in the black and saddle seed coat (Todd & Vodkin, 1993), are the major secondary metabolites present in plants. They advantageously function in plant survival by coloring flowers and seed coats, as well as protecting the plant body against pests, herbivores, UV radiation (Li et al., 1993), and oxidative stress (Kytridis & Manetas, 2006). Flavonoids are produced in the cytoplasm as non-glycosides (aglycons) via the phenylpropanoid pathway. Following glycosylation, these compounds are stored in the vacuole as glycosides (Falcone Ferreyra et al., 2012; Grotewold, 2006; Zhao, 2015). 

Flavonoids have a protein-binding capacity (Todd & Vodkin, 1993) and can inhibit the activity of animal digestive enzymes (Li et al., 2023; Pereira et al., 2011). Furthermore, we previously showed that the dicing activity of two A. thaliana Dicers, DCL3 and DCL4, is inhibited by flavonoids in vitro (Tabara et al., 2023). One of these Dicers, DCL4, is essential for PTGS (Fukudome & Fukuhara, 2017). From these previous studies, we hypothesized that the accumulation of flavonoids specific to the central region around the hilum of the saddle seed coat and subsequent region-specific inhibition of DCL4 cause region-specific inhibition of PTGS and recovery of anthocyanin biosynthesis. 

In this study, three soybean cultivars (yellow, saddle, and black) were used to investigate the correlation between flavonoid accumulation and DCL4 activity in the immature seed coat during seed maturation. In the immature saddle seed coat, the central region around the hilum, which turns black during maturation, displayed a high accumulation of flavonoid aglycons and showed potent inhibition of DCL4 activity. Furthermore, flavonoids highly accumulated in the funiculi of saddle and black but not yellow cultivars, and in vitro cultured immature seeds had reduced phenolic compounds in their seed coats. # RESULTS

# PTGS was induced only in the peripheral region (PR) of the immature saddle seed coat but it was suppressed in the central region (CR) around the hilum, where pigmentation occurs as seed maturation

In this study, we used three soybean cultivars, cv. Shinano-Kurakake (SK) with a saddle seed coat, cv. Enrei (En) with a yellow (colorless) seed coat, and cv. Tanbaguro (Tan) with a black seed coat (Figure 1A). Since it was reported that the yellow seed coat accumulates siRNAs derived from the CHS gene at an early stage of seed maturation (Cho et al., 2013), immature seeds were classified into three developmental stages based on the fresh weights (FW) of seeds (DS1, 5–25 mg; DS2, 25–75 mg; DS3, 75–150 mg). These immature seeds lack any visible accumulation of pigment (Figure 1A); it is impossible to visually determine the areas where pigmentation does or 

does not occur in the saddle seed coat of SK. Consequently, the area surrounding the hilum of the seed coat that exhibits pigmentation in mature seeds (the central region [CR] hereafter) and the area distal to the hilum that lacks pigmentation (the peripheral region [PR] hereafter) were dissected for further experiments (Figure 1B). Twenty-one-nt siRNAs derived from the CHS genes were detected in the seed coats of immature En seeds (Figure 1C) and the PR of SK seeds but not in the CR of SK seeds (Figure 1D). Since 21-nt siRNAs are hallmarks of PTGS (Cho et al., 2013), PTGS was induced only in the PR of the saddle soybean seed coat, but it was suppressed in the CR that becomes black after seed maturation as previously reported (Cho et al., 2017). 

# Dicing activity was detected in the PR but not in the CR of the immature seed coat of saddle soybean

The bicolored soybean cultivar SK has a seed coat with a saddle pattern, in which anthocyanins accumulate only in the restricted region (central region) around the hilum (Figure 1A). Although previous studies showed that the 

absence of color of the seed coat in bicolored soybean is due to the inhibition of anthocyanin biosynthesis by endogenous PTGS, acting against the CHS genes (Cho et al., 2017), the molecular mechanism generating this bicolored pattern formation remains unknown. Since we recently reported that the dicing activity of DCL4 was inhibited in soybean leaves, and flavonoid aglycons inhibit DCL4 activity in vitro (Kuriyama et al., 2023; Tabara et al., 2023), we hypothesized that, in the saddle soybean cultivars, DCL4 activity is specifically inhibited in the seed coat around the hilum, where pigmentation develops as the seed matures. To test this hypothesis, we examined dicing activity in vitro using crude extracts prepared from the seed coats, cotyledons, and leaves of SK with a saddle seed coat, En with a yellow (colorless) seed coat, and Tan with a black seed coat (Figure 2). The dicing activities of DCL3 and DCL4 in developing immature seeds were investigated. The strong dicing activities of DCL3 and DCL4, detected as 24-nt and 21-nt signals, respectively, were observed in the cotyledons of the three cultivars throughout seed maturation. However, no dicing activity of either DCL3 or DCL4 was detected in the leaves, as reported previously (Tabara et al., 2023). Notably, the dicing activities of DCL3 and DCL4 were observed in the seed coat of En and in the PR of SK (Figure 2A,B, respectively), whereas no dicing activity was detected in the CR of SK and the seed coat of Tan (Figure 2B,C, respectively) where pigmentation occurs as the seeds mature. The DCL4 activity detected in the PR of SK (Figure 2B) was lower than that detected in the seed coat of En (Figure 2A), and the DCL4 activity decreased as the seeds matured in both cultivars (Figure 2A,B). No significant differences in the transcript levels of DCL3 and DCL4 genes in the seed coats of SK and En were detected between seed maturation stages or between the seed coat regions in the saddle cultivar (Figure S1). These results support the hypothesis that the levels of DCL4 activity are important for regulating anthocyanin accumulation in the soybean seed coat. 

# Crude extracts prepared from the CR of the immature saddle seed coat inhibit dicing activity

No dicing activity was observed in the CR of the immature seed coat in the saddle cultivar SK. We hypothesized that the accumulation of inhibitory substance(s) of DCLs causes the region-specific inhibition of dicing activity in the saddle cultivar SK. To confirm this hypothesis, the dicing assay was performed using crude extracts derived from the CR of the immature SK seed coat (no dicing activity), immature seed coats of En (active dicing activity), and a 1:1 mixture of En extract and CR-SK extract. By mixing the En and the CR-SK extracts, a significant reduction in DCL3 and DCL4 activities was observed compared to the diluted En extract (Figure 3). This result suggests that dicing inhibitor(s) accumulated in the CR of the immature SK seed coat. 

# High accumulation of flavonoids observed in the CR of the immature saddle seed coat

The CHS gene, which is essential to flavonoid biosynthesis, is significantly expressed in the pigmented seed coat of soybean (Wang et al., 1994). Consistently, anthocyanins and colorless or pale-yellow flavonoids, such as catechin and quercetin, accumulate in the black seed coat of soybean cultivars (Zili c et al., 2013). In our previous study, various flavonoids (or phenolic compounds) inhibited the dicing activity of Arabidopsis DCL3 and DCL4 (Tabara et al., 2023). We hypothesized that flavonoids would specifically accumulate in the pigmented region of the soybean seed coat, acting as inhibitors of DCL4. To confirm this hypothesis, total phenolic compounds, including flavonoids, in the seed coats of three soybean cultivars (En [yellow], SK [saddle], and Tan [black]) were determined by the Folin–Ciocalteu method (FAO/IAEA, 2000; Makkar et al., 1993). Since polyvinylpolypyrrolidone (PVPP) can specifically adsorb flavonoids (Bridi et al., 2014), the difference between total phenolic compounds in the untreated extract and those in the extract treated with PVPP was considered as the flavonoid content in this study. 

The immature seed coat of En (yellow) contained low levels of phenolic compounds (Figure 4A). In contrast, high levels of phenolic compounds accumulated in the CR of the immature SK (saddle) seed coat, the majority $( 7 0 - 8 5 \% )$ of which were flavonoids. These results indicate that high concentrations of flavonoids, which are biosynthesized in the phenylpropanoid pathway together with anthocyanins, accumulate in the CR of the immature unpigmented seed coat of SK, which turns black as the seed matures (Figure 4B). A lower level of flavonoids was detected in the PR of the immature SK seed coat. These results indicate that the accumulation of phenolic compounds, mainly flavonoids, is negatively related to levels of the dicing activity in the immature saddle seed coat, suggesting that dicing activity in vivo could be inhibited by flavonoids. 

Tan (black cultivar) has a uniformly black-colored seed coat that produces anthocyanin (black) pigments as the seed matures. It was expected that Tan would accumulate phenolic compounds at a concentration equal to or higher than the levels in the CR of the saddle cultivar SK. Unexpectedly, however, the concentration of the phenolic compounds in the Tan seed coat was lower than that in the CR of SK at all seed maturation stages (compare Figure 4B,C). Therefore, the immature seed coats of Tan were also divided into central and peripheral regions (CR and PR), and the total phenolic content in each region was measured. These results demonstrated that the concentration of phenolic compounds in the CR of Tan (Figure 4C, right panel) was comparable to that in the CR of SK (Figure 4B). # Immature seeds of the saddle cultivar cultured had reduced phenolic compounds in the seed coats

To confirm the transport of flavonoids from the maternal tissues to the seed coat, we compared the content of phenolic compounds in immature seed coats between detached immature seeds cultured in vitro and control seeds grown in seed pods of the saddle soybean cultivar SK (Figure 9). # A model for the bicolored pattern formation in the saddle seed coat

The dicing activity of DCL4 is inhibited by flavonoid aglycons including quercetin transported from maternal tissues to the CR around the hilum of the immature seed coat of saddle soybean cultivars, such as SK. This inhibition suppresses PTGS targeting of the CHS gene in the CR of the seed coat, where de novo anthocyanin/flavonoid biosynthesis occurs. The resulting flavonoid aglycons reinforce the inhibition of DCL4 to maintain anthocyanin biosynthesis in the CR, causing a feedforward regulation of anthocyanin/flavonoid biosynthesis and inhibition of DCL4 (PTGS). In contrast, the dicing activity of DCL4 and PTGS is maintained in the PR of the saddle seed coat, where the inhibition of pigmentation is maintained throughout seed maturation. Consequently, the spatial feedforward regulation consisting of DCL4 and flavonoid aglycons results in the bicolored pattern of seed coat in saddle soybean cultivars (Figure 10). 

# DISCUSSION

While anthocyanins that accumulate in soybean seed coats have undesirable effects on the processing of soybean products (Palmer et al., 2004), in humans, they also have health-promoting effects (Astadi et al., 2009). Therefore, the pigmentation of the seed coat is one of the most important agronomic traits of soybean because it affects the commercial values of this crop. De novo biosynthesis of anthocyanins in seed coats is regulated by naturally occurring endogenous PTGS in both yellow and bicolored cultivars that contain the I and $i ^ { j }$ alleles, respectively (Kajiya-Kanegae et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020). However, the molecular mechanism of spatial regulation of PTGS, which induces bicolored pigmentation in saddle cultivars, remains unknown. Bicolored pigmentation of plant tissues induced by naturally occurring endogenous PTGS occurs not only in soybean but also in other flowering plants such as petunia, dahlia, and gentian (Koseki et al., 2005; Ohno et al., 2018; Ohta et al., 2022). Most PTGS occurring in these bicolored plants target the CHS gene, which functions upstream of the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway.

We hypothesized that flavonoid aglycons, which accumulated in the CR of the immature saddle seed coat, were involved in the region-specific suppression of posttranscriptional CHS gene silencing. In support of this hypothesis, we showed the suppression of DCL4 activity (Figure 2) and the accumulation of dicer inhibitors (Figure 3) and flavonoid aglycons (Figures 4 and 6) in the CR of the immature saddle seed coat, and the recovery of in vitro dicing activity by PVPP treatment to remove flavonoids (Figure 5). One of these flavonoid aglycons, quercetin, inhibited the dicing activity of DCL4 in vitro (Figure 7). Furthermore, we showed that flavonoids were highly accumulated in the funiculi of the saddle and black but not yellow cultivars (Figure 8), while immature saddle seeds cultured in vitro had reduced phenolic compounds in their seed coat (Figure 9), suggesting that flavonoid aglycons supplied from mother plant tissues form a concentration gradient, which causes a differential DCL4 inhibitory gradient from the center around the hilum to the periphery in the immature saddle seed coat. Overall, we propose that 

the local inhibition of DCL4 activity by maternally supplied flavonoid aglycons including quercetin promotes pigmentation in the seed coat around the hilum in saddle soybean cultivars. 

PTGS operates in the cytoplasm, where its components, including DCL4 and target mRNAs, are available (Pumplin et al., 2016). Flavonoids are biosynthesized in the cytoplasm and stored in the vacuole as glycosidic forms (Falcone Ferreyra et al., 2012; Zhao, 2015; Zhao & Dixon, 2009). It can be postulated that quercetin detected in the immature seed coats of the pigmented cultivars in this study likely occurs in the cytoplasm. High accumulation of quercetin in the CR of the immature saddle seed coat (Figure 6) and its inhibitory activity against DCL4 (Figure 7) indicate that quercetin is involved in the inhibition of dicing activity in vivo. Flavonoid glycosides must be stored in the vacuole. In our dicing assay, the vacuoles are likely to be destroyed when the crude extracts (enzyme fractions) are prepared. It is therefore probable that flavonoid glycosides sequestered in the vacuole, as well as cytoplasmic flavonoid aglycons, are present in the enzyme fractions. However, the inhibitory capacity for dicing activity is found in quercetin (Figure 7B) but not in its glycoside rutin (Figure 7C). 

In the peripheral region of the SK seed coat, DCL4 activity, which is responsible for the generation of 21-nt siRNAs and thereby the occurrence of PTGS, was only detected at the D1 stage. Previous studies reported that the level of CHS gene expression in seed coats declined as the seed matures in saddle and black soybean cultivars (Tuteja et al., 2004). Furthermore, it was observed that the CHS siRNAs were abundant in immature seed coats in the early stages of seed maturation in yellow cultivars but decreased significantly as the seed matured (Cho et al., 2013). Those reports, alongside our results, suggest that the occurrence or absence of pigmentation in the mature seed coat is determined by the expression of the CHS gene at an early stage of seed development. Therefore, since DCL4 is responsible for CHS siRNA production (Cho et al., 2013), its dicing activity at this stage greatly influences the pigmentation of mature saddle seeds. The black cultivar Tan, which has the i allele, does not have an inverted repeat sequence of the CHS genes, which is essential for triggering PTGS. Consequently, PTGS targeting the CHS gene does not occur in black cultivars, regardless of their DCL4 activity. 

Quercetin inhibited the activity of not only DCL4 but also DCL3 (Figure 7). This result is consistent with the findings from the dicing assay using immature seed coats, in which neither DCL4 nor DCL3 activity was detected in the CR region of saddle soybean (Figures 2, 3, and 5). These results suggest that, in tissues where flavonoid aglycons such as quercetin accumulate, both PTGS and TGS may be inhibited. 

It has been reported that flavonoid aglycons move a long distance from one organ to another, such as from shoots to roots. In A. thaliana, for example, flavonoid aglycons are synthesized in shoots in a light-dependent manner and are transported to and accumulated in root tips (Buer et al., 2007). Similarly, it was reported that isoflavonoids are transported from maternal tissues to the developing embryo in soybean seeds (Dhaubhadel et al., 2003), and glucosinolates are delivered to seeds after synthesis in other plant organs in Brassica species (Du & Halkier, 1998; Gijzen et al., 1989). Seeds are attached to the seed pod via the funiculus, and the vascular bundles in the seed pod stretch to the seed coats via the funiculi (Thorne, 1981). As there is no vascular bundle connection between the seed coat and the immature cotyledon, the seed coat represents the final point of nutrient transport via the symplastic pathway from maternal tissues to the seeds. Therefore, nutrients that are transported from maternal tissues to the developing embryos could traverse the seed coat. In this study, to elucidate whether flavonoid aglycons are transported from maternal tissues to the seed coat, we focused on the funiculus to quantify phenolic compounds, including flavonoids. We detected a higher content of phenolic compounds in the funiculi of bicolored and black cultivars 

(SK and Tan, respectively) than in the yellow cultivar En (Figure 8). Moreover, the proportion of flavonoids in the total phenolic compounds was also higher in the funiculi of the pigmented cultivars (Figure 8). These results indicate that flavonoids are not only biosynthesized in the seed coat but are also transported from maternal tissues in the saddle and black cultivars, but not in the yellow cultivar (Figures 4 and 8). 

To gain further insight into the transport of flavonoids from maternal tissues to the seed coat, a study was conducted to examine the impact of in vitro culture of immature seeds on the concentration of flavonoids in the seed coat. The results showed a reduction in the total contents of phenolic compounds and flavonoids in the seed coat of immature SK seeds cultured in vitro. This provides clear evidence that the transport of nutrients from mother plants is a factor in the accumulation of flavonoids in soybean seed coats. In particular, immature SK seeds that were cultured during 7–10 DAF exhibited a reduction in flavonoids within the CR of the seed coat relative to the controls (Figure 9A). This result suggests that the transport of flavonoids from the maternal plant to the seed coat commences during an early stage of seed development (DS1). Furthermore, this result aligns with the observation that DCL4 activity within the CR of the immature SK seed coat was already inhibited at the DS1 stage (Figure 2B). Flavonoid aglycons transported from maternal tissues to the CR through the funiculus probably diffuse from the CR to the PR in the immature SK seed coat (Figures 8 and 9). This is consistent with the detection of a certain content of flavonoids in the PR (Figures 4B, 6B, and 9), where de novo flavonoid synthesis is inhibited by PTGS. Furthermore, the diffusion of flavonoids from the CR to the PR can also explain why the decrease in DCL4 activity was observed in the PR of the immature SK seed coat as the seed develops (Figure 2B). 

We propose a hypothetical mechanism for the initiation of PTGS inhibition in the saddle soybean seed coat triggered by the transport of flavonoid aglycons including quercetin from maternal plant tissues and a feedforward enhancement and maintenance of PTGS inhibition by the flavonoid aglycons biosynthesized de novo in the immature seed coat (Figure 10). In yellow soybean cultivars containing the I allele such as En, fewer flavonoids are transported from mother plant tissues to the immature seed coat (Figure 8B), and DCL4 activity is maintained throughout the seed coat (Figure 2A). Thereby, PTGS is maintained throughout the seed coat during seed maturation, and the biosynthesis of flavonoids, including anthocyanins, is completely inhibited throughout the seed coat (Figures 1A and 4A). In contrast, in bicolor saddle cultivars containing the $i ^ { j }$ allele such as SK, large amounts of flavonoids are transported from maternal tissues to the immature seed coat through the funiculus (Figures 8 and 9). The dicing activity of DCL4 is inhibited by flavonoid aglycons, especially quercetin (indicated by red triangles), transported from the maternal tissues to the central region around the hilum of the immature saddle seed coat (e.g., SK). This inhibition suppresses PTGS targeting the CHS gene in the central region of the immature seed coat, where de novo anthocyanin/flavonoid biosynthesis occurs. The resulting flavonoid aglycons, including quercetin, reinforce the inhibition of DCL4 to maintain anthocyanin biosynthesis in the central region, causing a feedforward loop of anthocyanin/flavonoid production and inhibition of PTGS. In contrast, the dicing activity of DCL4 and PTGS is maintained in the peripheral region of the immature saddle seed coat, where the inhibition of pigmentation is maintained during seed maturation. Consequently, the spatial feedforward regulation consisting of DCL4 and flavonoid aglycons causes the bicolored pattern of the seed coat in saddle soybean cultivars.

The transport of flavonoids to the seed coat via the funiculus is consistent with the observation that the accumulation of phenolic compounds (flavonoids) was concentrated in the central region in the black cultivar Tan (Figure 4C). These findings will contribute to the elucidation of the mechanism underlying the formation of bicolored patterns in plant tissues through the spatial regulation of PTGS induction and inhibition by the inter-tissue transport of flavonoids in plants. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant materials

Three cultivars of soybean (G. max) were used in the study: the yellow seed coat cultivar Enrei (En), the bicolored (saddle) seed coat cultivar Shinano-Kurakake (SK), and the black seed coat cultivar Tanbaguro (Tan). Soybean plants were grown in commercially available compost soil (Akagi Horticulture Co., Gunma, Japan) in an outdoor greenhouse under natural light at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / 1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (day 12 h/night 12 h). 

# Detection of small RNAs by Northern hybridization

Total RNA was isolated from the immature seed coats and cotyledons using the Fruit-mate (Takara Bio, Kusatsu, Japan) and Trizol (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Tokyo, Japan) reagents according to the 

manufacturer’s protocols. Approximately $5 ~ { \mu \ g }$ of total RNA was electrophoresed in $1 5 \%$ denaturing polyacrylamide gels containing $\uparrow \times$ TBE buffer (89 mM Tris [pH 8.3], 89 mM boric acid, 2 mM EDTA), and $7 ~ \mathsf { m }$ urea and blotted onto a nylon membrane (Zeta-Probe; Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) by electroblotting $( 1 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { A } / 1 \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at room temperature). DNA fragments of the soybean GmCHS7 gene as probes were amplified from complementary DNAs made from total RNA prepared from the Tan seed coats by PCR, and then, probes for siRNA detection were made using the BcaBEST Labeling Kit (Takara Bio) with $[ x ^ { 3 2 } , 7 ]$ -dCTP (Revvity Health Sciences, Yokohama, Japan). PCR primers are listed in Table S4. Hybridization was carried out in Perfect Hyb Plus hybridization buffer (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) containing $^ { 3 2 } { \mathsf { P } } .$ -labeled DNA probe at $4 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 6 h. Membranes were washed four times in $_ { 2 \times }$ SSC $\left. 1 \times \right.$ SSC, 0.15 M NaCl, 15 mM sodium citrate) with $0 . 5 \%$ SDS at $4 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min and then analyzed by a Typhoon FLA 7000 image analyzer (GE Healthcare, Chicago, IL, USA) (Fukuhara et al., 2011). 

# Preparation of $3 2 >$ -labeled dsRNA

A 50-nt single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) was synthesized by the Japan BioService Corporation (Saitama, Japan). The 50-nt ssRNA was end-labeled using T4 polynucleotide kinase (Takara Bio) and [c-32P]-ATP (Revvity). Equal quantities of $^ { 3 2 } { \mathsf { P } } .$ -labeled 50-nt sense RNA (1.0 pmol) and 52-nt antisense RNA were heated at $\mathfrak { g o c }$ for 5 min in a solution of 10 mM Tris-HCL (pH 7.5) and $1 0 0 \mathrm { \ m u \ N a C l }$ . Following this, the samples were annealed with the heat block turned off for 10 min and incubated at room temperature for 10 min. The nucleotide sequences of ssRNAs are presented in Table S3. 

# Dicing (Dicer) assay

A standard dicing (Dicer) assay was conducted as described in previous studies (Fukudome et al., 2011; Nagano et al., 2014). To prepare cell-free crude extracts as enzyme fractions, seed coats, cotyledons and leaves of soybean were collected and ground in an extraction buffer containing $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 4 mM ${ \sf M g C l } _ { 2 } ,$ 5 mM DTT, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, $1 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mathsf { m } \mathsf { l } ^ { - 1 }$ leupeptin, and $1 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ pepstatin A at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ using a mortar and pestle. To prepare crude extracts depleted of PVPP-bound compounds, the sample was ground in an extraction buffer containing PVPP, to which 1.2 times the FW of plant tissue PVPP was added. PVPP with metabolites bounded was then removed by centrifugation ( $ 1 3 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { \ m i n } )$ , and the supernatant was used as an genzyme fraction. In a standard dicing reaction, $1 5 ~ \mu \iota$ of crude extract was mixed with $4 ~  \mu \mu \mu \mu$ of dicing buffer consisting of $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 250 mM NaCl, 25 mM ${ \sf M g C l } _ { 2 } ,$ 25 mM ATP, and $1 . 0 \ : \mathsf { U } \ : \mu \mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 }$ RNase inhibitor (Takara Bio), and $1 ~ \mu \mu \up$ of $^ { 3 2 } { \mathsf { P } } .$ -labeled 50-nt/52-nt dsRNA (final concentration, ${ \sim } 0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ ) was added and incubated at $\scriptstyle 2 2 ^ { \circ } 0$ for 30 min. In the case of the dicing assay with flavonoids (quercetin and rutin [Fujifilm Wako Pure Chemicals, Osaka, Japan] and epicatechin [Tokyo Chemical Industry Co., Ltd, Tokyo, Japan]), $1 ~ \mu \mu |$ of 0.2, 1.0, or $5 . 0 \mathsf { m m }$ flavonoid solution (dissolved in $50 \%$ dimethyl sulfoxide) was added to the reaction mixture $( 2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l } )$ in which the crude extract prepared from the immature En seed coat (D2 stage, total protein concentration is approximately $5 \mathrm { \ m g \ m l ^ { - 1 } } )$ was used as the enzyme fraction (final concentration of flavonoids, 10, 50, $2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ . Following incubation, the cleaved products were purified using phenol/chloroform and separated by $1 5 \%$ denaturing polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) with $7 ~ \mathsf { m }$ urea. The $^ { 3 2 } \mathsf { P }$ -labeled RNA was detected by autoradiography. The quantity of cleaved RNA product was determined to measure band intensity with a Typhoon FLA 7000 image 

analyzer (GE Healthcare). The dicing activities of DCL3 and DCL4 were quantified as the relative intensity of the cleaved RNA band (24-nt RNA [DCL3] and 21-nt RNA [DCL4]) in relation to the total intensity of all bands (cleaved RNA bands and remaining substrate 50-nt RNA band) in each lane. 

# Quantification of total phenolic compounds, including flavonoids

The total phenolic compounds in plant tissue were quantified using the Folin–Ciocalteu method (FAO/IAEA, 2000; Makkar et al., 1993). The plant tissues were ground in liquid nitrogen using a mortar and pestle and suspended in 1 ml of $70 \%$ acetone. The suspension was then sonicated in an ultrasonic water bath (W-103T; Tokyo Glass Machine, Tokyo, Japan) for 20 min at room temperature. The supernatant obtained after centrifugation at $3 0 0 0 \textbf { \mathfrak { g } }$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ was used as the extract containing total gphenolic compounds. One hundred microliters of the total phenolic compound extract was mixed with an equal volume of a PVPP (Nacalai Tesque, Kyoto, Japan) suspension $( 1 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ m l ^ { - 1 } } )$ . The mixture was then vigorously mixed and allowed to stand at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min. The mixture was centrifuged at $3 0 0 0 \textbf { \mathfrak { g } }$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ g, and then, the supernatant was used as the solution of depleted PVPP-bound compounds. Twenty microliters of total phenolic compound solution was diluted with $2 8 5 . 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \up$ distilled water and $5 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \vert$ phenolic compound solution of depleted PVPPbound compounds was diluted with $2 5 5 . 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ distilled water, and then $6 9 . 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \up$ the Folin–Ciocalteu reagent (Nacalai Tesque) and $6 2 5 \mathrm { - } \mu | \ 7 . 5 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ sodium carbonate solution were added to each of these phenolic compound solutions. These mixtures were allowed to stand at room temperature for 40 min, and then, absorbance at 725 nm was measured. The total amount of phenolic compounds in each sample was determined as tannic acid equivalents (TAE) as per FW of plant tissue. This value was calculated using a calibration curve drawn from the results of five diluted tannic acid standard solutions (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4, and $5 ~ { \mu \ g }$ tannic acid in solution, respectively) prepared at the same time. 

# Flavonoid analysis by LC–MS/MS

Formic acid and chloroform $\left( \mathsf { C H C l } _ { 3 } \right)$ for high-performance LC (HPLC), acetonitrile (ACN), and methanol (MeOH), and of quadrupole time-of-flight MS (QTOF-MS) grade were purchased from FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemical Corp. (Osaka, Japan). ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ used for LC-MS was produced by an ultrapure water production system (Organo, Tokyo, Japan). 

Metabolite extraction was performed according to a previously described protocol (Tsugawa et al., 2019). Freeze-dried samples were homogenized using a multi-bead shocker with a metal cone (YASUI KIKAI, Osaka, Japan) at 2500 rpm for 15 s. Samples (3 mg) were weighed in a $_ { 2 . 0 - \mathsf { m } | }$ microtube (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) and subjected to the Bligh and Dyer method (Bligh & Dyer, 1959). Samples were mixed with $1 0 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of an ice-cold $\mathsf { M e O H } / \mathsf { C H C l } _ { 3 } / \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } 0$ (5:2:2) solvent containing 10 camphor sulfonic acid $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ , 2-naphthalenesulfonic acid $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ , lidocaine $( 0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { { M } } )$ , flavonol-20 -sulfonate $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ , ampicillin $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ , methionine sulfone $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ , and PIPES $( 1 2 . 5 ~ \mu \mu )$ as the internal standards. The samples were shaken at 1200 rpm for 10 min at $\mathfrak { g } \circ \mathfrak { C }$ (ThermoMixer C, Eppendorf) and centrifuged at $1 6 \ : 0 0 0 \ : g$ for 3 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ . Then, $8 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ gof supernatant was transferred into a new tube, and $4 0 0 ~ \mu \updownarrow$ of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ was added. After subsequent centrifugation at $1 6 \ : 0 0 0 \ : g$ for 5 min at 4°C, $3 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of the supernatant gwas collected. After the collected supernatant was dried using a centrifugal evaporator, the dried sample was dissolved by applying $1 5 0 ~ \mu \up$ of $80 \%$ MeOH and centrifuged at $1 6 \ : 0 0 0 \ : g$ for 3 min at 

$4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Then, $1 2 0 ~ \mu \up$ of the supernatant was collected and $3 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ of $80 \%$ MeOH containing 16:0 lysophosphatidylcholine (500 nM) was added as an additional internal standard. The solvent was transferred to an LC-MS vial fitted with an insert (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). 

The experimental procedure was performed according to a previously described protocol (Tsugawa et al., 2019). The liquid chromatography (LC) system comprised an ExonLC system (SCIEX, Framingham, MA, USA). Metabolites were separated on an ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column $( 1 0 0 \times 2 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m m } ; 1 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ (Waters, Milford, MA, USA). The column was maintained at $4 0 \%$ . The mobile phases comprised (A) ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid and (B) acetonitrile (ACN) with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid. A sample volume of $1 ~ \mu \mu |$ was used for the injection. Separation was performed under the following gradient program: 0 min $0 . 5 \%$ (B), 0.1 min $0 . 5 \%$ (B), 10 min $80 \%$ (B), 10.1 min $9 9 . 5 \%$ (B), 12.0 min $9 9 . 5 \%$ (B), 12.1 min $0 . 5 \%$ (B), and 15.0 min $0 . 5 \%$ (B). The flow rate was set as follows: $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 0 min, $0 . 3 ~ \mathsf { m l } ~ \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 10 min, $0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 10.1 min, $0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 14.4 min, and $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for 14.5 min. The temperature of the sample rack was maintained at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . MS detection was performed using QTOF-MS (ZenoTOF 7600; SCIEX). The parameters for the negative ion mode were as follows: MS1 and MS2 mass ranges, m/z 50–1500; MS1 accumulation time, 200 msec; Q1 resolution, units; MS2 accumulation time, 50 msec; maximum candidate ions, 10; ion source temperature, $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ;$ and CAD gas, 7. The following settings were used for the negative ion mode, independently: ion source gas 1, 50 psi; ion source gas 2, 50 psi; curtain gas, 35 psi; spray voltage, $- 4 5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { V } _ { \cdot }$ ; declustering potential, $- 8 0 \ V ;$ and collision energy, $- 3 5 \pm 1 5 \ : \mathrm { e V }$ . 

The LC-MS/MS data were analyzed in MS-DIAL (Takeda et al., 2024) where the default parameter was used for the analysis. Publicly and commercially available mass spectral libraries, containing 384 101 spectra of 26 493 compounds for the negative ion mode, were used for metabolite annotation. The features containing fragment ions related to flavonoid aglycons (cyanidin, delphinidin, catechin, epicatechin, kaempferol, quercetin, genistein, daidzein) and epicatechin dimer (procyanidin) were manually curated. The glycosylated conjugates were further estimated based on the unique neutral loss patterns related to sugar moieties. 

# culture of immature soybean seeds

For the in vitro culture of immature soybean seeds, 7–10 DAF or 12–14 DAF SK seeds were used. Immature seeds were aseptically isolated from seed pods after the pods had been sterilized with $50 \%$ sodium hypochlorite. B5 agar-based medium supplemented with $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m g \ L } ^ { - 1 }$ indole-3-butyric acid (IBA) was used for in vitro culture (Gamborg et al., 1968; Tilton & Russell, 1984). Immature seeds were placed perpendicular to the hypocotyl on the agar medium and incubated for 7 days at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 16 h/8 h = day/night. Immature SK seeds of the same age that had grown in the pods of soybean plants were used as the control. The FW of whole seeds and their seed coats was measured (see Figure S2a,b). The FW of the PR and CR of seed coats was calculated based on the area ratio of each region in the mature seed coat (Figure S2c) and was measured using the ImageJ program (https://imagej. net/ij/).
