# Glycosyltransferases in plant natural product synthesis: characterization of a supergene family

Thomas Vogt and Patrik Jones

Glycosyltransferases of plant secondary metabolism transfer nucleotide-diphosphateactivated sugars to low molecular weight substrates. Until recently, glycosyltransferases were thought to have only limited influence on the basic physiology of the plant. This view has changed. Glycosyltransferases might in fact have an important role in plant defense and stress tolerance. Recent results obtained with several recombinant enzymes indicate that many glycosyltransferases are regioselective or regiospecific rather than highly substrate specific. This might indicate how plants evolve novel secondary products, placing enzymes with broad substrate specificities downstream of the conserved, early, pivotal enzymes of plant secondary metabolism.

lycosylation is a prominent modification reaction and is often the last step in the biosynthesis of natural compounds1 . In coordination with hydroxylation, acylation and methylation reactions, glycosylation contributes to the unsurpassed variety and complexity of plant secondary metabolites. The enzymes leading to glycoside formation, the glycosyltransferases (GTs), transfer nucleotide-diphosphate-activated sugars to low molecular weight substrates. The activated sugar form is usually UDP–glucose but UDP–galactose and UDP–rhamnose are also found. In the case of cardiac glycosides (Fig. 1), more than a dozen different sugar moieties have been identified attached to the respective aglycones. Sugar acceptors include all major classes of secondary metabolites, including phenolics, terpenoids, cyanohydrins (cyanogenic glycoside precursors), thiohydroximates (glucosinolate precursors) and alkaloids (e.g. betalains) (Fig. 1).

The best studied members of the glycoside family are the anthocyanins, which are water soluble pigments located in the plant vacuole. It is important to realize that glycosylation (and, in several cases, subsequent acylation of the corresponding sugars) takes place in the cytosol before these conjugates are transported and stored in the vacuole. Although glycosylation by individual sugars does not contribute directly to color formation of the anthocyanin structure, it is related to two important aspects of anthocyanin formation. Glycosylation is required for stabilization of the flavylium cation and is a prerequisite for further conjugation with acyl groups, leading to highly complex anthocyanin conjugates2 . Inter- and intramolecular stacking of the planar phenolic ring structures to supramolecular complexes, also referred to as interand intramolecular copigmentation of these conjugates, stabilize the anthocyanin chromophore in the vacuole and therefore enhance flower coloration3 .

The importance of glycosylation in the stabilization of secondary metabolites is particularly evident with the glucosinolates and cyanogenic glycosides. In these cases, the attachment of a glucose moiety is an absolute requirement for stability and prevents spontaneous degradation to cyanide, aldehydes or isothiocyanates4 . Therefore, glycosylation enables the storage of potent toxic and deterrent chemicals in high concentrations while simultaneously allowing the plant, upon decompartmentation and exposure to specific glycosidases, to release a battery of highly aggressive chemicals towards a putative pathogen or herbivore. In

addition, various volatile terpenoids and phenolics are stabilized by glycoconjugation and released after cell damage by endogenous glycosidases5 . Prominent examples are the coumarin derived odor of dried grass and the different tastes of the aromatic vanillin and vanillin– $- p$ -hydroxybenzaldehyde compared with their respective bitter glucosides; these aromatics are trapped by conjugation with glucose in the vacuoles and released during the ripening process by endogenous glucosidases in Vanilla planifolia6 .

The diversity of phenolics, glucosinolates, cyanogenic and terpenoid conjugates that are encountered in a plant species is only a small fraction of the whole glycoside pool and the glycoside biosynthetic potential in plants. This raises the question of how many GTs are necessary to create the complexity of secondary product glycosides. Fortunately, secondary metabolite pools are often species specific, suggesting that only a limited number of GTs is present in one plant species. The situation is complicated by a developmental tissue or cell specific presence and regulation of GT transcripts7,8. In these cases, promotor identity and transcriptional control might allow only a transient accumulation of a single GT or a subset of GTs with a limited number of potential target molecules. A well known example is the GTs involved in the biosynthesis of the hydroxamic acids DIBOA and DIMBOA, which are present as defense compounds in young seedlings of many grass species but are not detectable in adult plants9 .

# Purification and biochemical characterization of GTs

# Enzyme purification

In the ‘premolecular’ age, purifying the enzyme and determining its substrate specificity and kinetics were the only method available to characterize and differentiate individual GTs. This proved to be a difficult task because many GTs occur in minute amounts and among a whole set of related enzymes. Accordingly, many data were obtained with partially purified transferases. GTs are generally labile, which renders the purification of GTs as difficult and cumbersome as the purification of other superfamilies of modifying enzymes [e.g. the 300 members of membrane bound cytochrome P450s (Ref. 10)].

GTs involved in secondary plant metabolism are, with a few exceptions, soluble enzymes with a molecular mass between $4 5 ~ \mathrm { k D a }$ and $6 0 ~ \mathrm { k D a }$ under in vitro conditions. Purification has been achieved by a combination of anion exchange, hydrophobic

interaction and dye ligand chromatography. The use of biomimetic dyes such as Reactive Yellow 3 as an affinity matrix has reduced the number of steps necessary to purify and separate rare GTs from similar enzymes present in the extracts11,12. The dye apparently mimics the substrate and fits into the binding fold of the corresponding GT (Ref. 13). The bound enzyme can be eluted by the addition of the substrate or UDP–glucose, or by high salt concentrations, to yield pure enzymes suitable for amino acid sequence analysis.

Substrate specificity tests of these highly purified monomeric proteins should, whenever possible be verified by heterologous expression (e.g. in E. coli or yeast). Of the plant GTs for which this has been done (Table 1), only a few were identified by the classical ‘protein-first’ approach. This includes the GT involved in betacyanin biosynthesis in Dorotheanthus bellidiformis12, the $p$ -hydroxymandelonitrile GT involved in the biosynthesis of the cyanogenic glucoside dhurrin in Sorghum bicolor11, the limonoid GT that glucosylates the bitter terpenoid limonin in the genus Citrus14, the pollen specific, membrane-associated flavonol galactosyltransferase from Petunia hybrida15 and the pathogen-inducible salicylic-acid-modifying GT from tobacco16. The membranebound sterol GT from Avena sativa could be purified and characterized because of its unique solubility and activity in diethyl ether and the reported substrate specificity was later verified after cloning and expression in yeast17.

# Molecular characterization

Molecular techniques define the new ‘cDNA-first’ approach to enzyme characterization by expression in heterologous systems. Several enzymes that glucosylate anthocyanidins and flavonoids from various plant sources18–24 were recently obtained by screening cDNA libraries or differentially expressed cDNAs with existing GT probes (Table 1). These data were obtained within the past two years and show the commitment of researchers to gain more knowledge about these enzymes and the generally increased interest in plant natural product GTs. The major emphasis in these papers,

regardless of which enzyme was purified or characterized, was on acceptor specificity. The results read alike and are puzzling at first glance: these enzymes are not absolutely substrate specific.

However, these data become interesting if we examine a few examples. The primary acceptor target of betanidin 5 GT is the 5-hydroxy group of the orthodihydroxy substituted betanidin structure. The enzyme also accepts the flavonol quercetin with comparable kinetic data, and transfers the glucose moiety to either the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxy or the 7-hydroxy position, with a ratio of 4:1 in favor of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxy group12 (Fig. 1). These few examples illustrate the specificity of GTs for individual hydroxy groups and alert the researcher to be careful in defining the ‘original’ substrate for any GT under investigation.

Conformational changes in the enzyme structure upon substrate binding and in vivo compartmentation should always be considered. Preliminary data might thus have to be verified by heterologous expression with a large set of different substrates. This is particularly evident in the case of the $p$ -hydroxymandelonitrile GT, which was not expected to glucosylate the structurally dissimilar geraniol. The affinity of almost all GTs towards reactive dyes already suggests a broad affinity of these enzymes for a variety of substrates. However, affinity for a substrate is not equivalent to a biochemical reaction. It is therefore necessary to assay a variety of compounds as possible sugar acceptors and, in most cases, this has not been done extensively.

In conclusion, the substrate specificity of GTs is not absolute but GTs exert regioselectivity and regiospecificity for the sugar acceptor. Is there any benefit to the plant in synthesizing enzymes with a broad rather than a narrow acceptor specificity? Enzymes catalysing early steps in plant secondary product formation, such as polyketide synthases, are highly substrate specific26. The broad substrate specificity of all major classes of enzymes late in the biosynthesis of natural products, such as GTs, P450s and methyltransferases27, might ensure that the plant has a certain degree of flexibility. This would enable the plant to respond rapidly to different substrate profiles that are forced upon it by changing environmental conditions or evolutionary tendencies. As the supply and quality of endogenous nucleotides should be fairly constant during evolution, this assumption also explains why high substrate specificity is always observed for the sugar donor.

# Identification of GTs by sequence similarity search

Instead of testing each GT with all putative substrates, it should be worthwhile analysing the molecular sequence of these enzymes in detail and trying to define individual domains and amino acids necessary for the recognition of corresponding substrates. Might it be possible to assign specific, individual characteristics solely based on sequence data (e.g. to discriminate between GTs that glycosylate aglycones and those that transfer the sugar moiety to the hydroxy group of other sugars)? What are the sequence similarities or the significant differences between GTs involved in plant natural product formation and transferases involved in the biosynthesis of complex carbohydrates? In contrast with enzymes that extend existing carbohydrate chains, GTs involved in plant secondary metabolism account for a type of transfer reaction that involves the inversion of the anomeric sugar from an $\alpha$ linkage in UDP–glucose to the $\beta$ configuration in the resultant glycoside28. Is this difference between GT members of the $\alpha$ group (retaining the configuration) and the $\beta$ group (inversion of configuration) apparent in their sequence? What are the similarities between plant, animal and microbial GTs?

The first step in assigning a putative function to each of these GTs is aligning the sequences and focusing on distinct sequence similarities between unequivocally cloned and identified genes and their respective enzymes. The first cDNA sequence of a GT involved in plant secondary metabolism to become available was the bronze-1 gene from maize, which encodes a putative flavonol 3-O-glucosyltransferase; this gene was cloned by transposon tagging in 1984 (Ref. 29). Based on this sequence, several other putative GT genes were cloned by homology in the following years. Mainly based on sequence data from animal UDP–glucuronosyltransferases, a consensus sequence for all GTs was proposed in 1991 (Refs 30,31). With the availability of a set of putative GTs

The two highly conserved amino acids glutamic acid and histidine are proposed to be involved in a nucleophilic S 2 reaction mechanism leading to the inversion of the anomeric sugar configuration28. Both amino acids should therefore be targets for the inactivation of GTs by site-directed mutagenesis.

More than 100 different plant GT sequences can be identified by searching the databases for sequence similarity with the PSPG motif. Roughly half of these putative GT sequences have been obtained by the Arabidopsis sequencing project. The word ‘putative’ describes the disappointing part of the story – none of these genes has yet been functionally characterized. This might be partly explained by the lack of knowledge about this pathway in Arabidopsis, in which the first flavonol glycosides were only recently identified unequivocally33. Most of the remaining sequences from various plant sources are related to flavonoid biosynthesis.

With the exception of the PSPG box, the overall homology of the sequences from the plant GT superfamily obtained from the databases is rather low $( \sim 1 0 \% )$ . A few highly conserved amino acids can be defined and might relate to the structural integrity of all GTs in general. The genomic organization of GTs has not been studied in detail, but preliminary analysis of the Arabidopsis GTs from the database shows that some GTs are organized in tandem arrays of up to three consecutive individual sequences, whereas most other sequences are unclustered on chromosomes two and four. Only a few reports comment on the GT intron–exon structure. One small intron of $\mathord { \sim } 1 0 0$ bp is present in the bronze-1 gene and the corresponding barley gene.

# Individual cluster analysis

Several clusters are apparent in the dendrogram (Fig. 3). GT sequences from Arabidopsis have been included only sparsely, so as not to confuse the reader with a wealth of poorly characterized sequence data. Cluster I is the best characterized group of sequences. It represents either flavonol or anthocyanidin 3 GTs, including a few sequences with only putative flavonoid GT activity from both monocot and dicot sources. The recently cloned and heterologously expressed flavonoid-specific 3 GT from P. hybrida has been included in the dendrogram. This GT could be integrated exactly at the predicted position within cluster I (K. Saito, pers. commun.), whereas the simultaneously expressed anthocyanin 5- GT from P. hybrida is related to cluster II, in agreement with the regiospecificity of similar enzymes from Perilla frutescens. In the case of the phenotypically characterized putative flavonoid 3-GTs from monocots, the bronze-1 gene product and the corresponding

barley GT, the putative similar function and a close taxonomic relationship both contribute to the sequence identity.

Cluster II is characterized by unequivocally defined anthocyanin 5 GTs and separated from the limonoid GT and GTs involved in ester formation, such as the salicylic acid GT from tobacco and the indole acetic acid (IAA) GT from maize. The striking identity of IAA glucosylating GT and the salicylic acid glucosylating GT with the apparently unrelated limonoid GT from citrus might relate to the preference of the corresponding enzymes to glycosylate carboxy groups instead of hydroxy groups (Fig. 1) and might suggest a similar target for the GT from Equisetum. These examples also emphasize the necessity for a detailed search for a putative best substrate for this group of enzymes.

The sequence identity of a group of apparently unrelated sequences is represented in cluster III (Fig. 4). This group of GTs might confirm the observation that regioselectivity rather than substrate specificity is the basis in cluster formation. Functional analysis is only available for the betanidin 5 GT, which also glycosylates flavonols in $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxy and 7-hydroxy groups and the scutellarein 7 GT from Lamiaceae. The stress-inducible Is5a and Is10a homologs from tobacco35 and the wound-induced Twi-1 gene product from tomato36 also probably correspond to flavonol GTs (D. Horvath and D. Bowles, pers. commun.), although the exact enzyme products and therefore the regiospecificity await final elucidation. By contrast, recently detected inducible homologs of Is5a and Is10a in tobacco have been reported to be

involved in the glycosylation of the coumarins scopoletin and esculetin37. Sequence identities among this subfamily are unexpectedly high. The betanidin 5 GT, for example, showed $60 \%$ amino acid identity to the equivalent enzymes from Scutellaria, the Solanaceae and Arabidopsis. Therefore, both Arabidopsis sequences, which have considerable homology to this group, might represent members of the flavonol GT family, probably with regiospecificity for the 7-hydroxy group.

At this point, the task of defining GT specificities according to sequence data can only be preliminary because few of these sequences have yet been thoroughly characterized at the protein level. However, the high sequence identities observed for individual clusters corroborate the observation that acceptor specificity is determined by parts of the protein other than the PSPG box. The N-terminal part of the protein has been suggested to be a steroidbinding domain for the potato solanidine GT (Ref. 38). This agrees with earlier observations that the specificity of human GTs that glycosylate both bilirubin and phenol resides in the N terminus39. Comparing sequences from similar enzymes with closely related regiospecificities such as the anthocyanin 5 GT with the corresponding anthocyanidin 3 GT from Perilla frutescens or Petunia hybrida should facilitate the identification of individual amino acid residues, which determine acceptor and regiospecificities. A crucial point will be the elucidation of the first crystal structure of any GT with a PSPG sequence.

# Glycosyltransferases as biotechnology targets

Several GT-encoding genes are suitable candidates to be inserted into a variety of plants with the aim of improving food or crop quality. A few examples might illustrate the potential use of this approach. In grapevine, the expression of a specific GT transcript is associated with the accumulation of anthocyanins and might distinguish between red and white grape varieties. Once a suitable transformation system for grapevine has been established, this GT will be a potential target for the molecular biologist. The bitterness of pummelo (Citrus maxima) and other Citrus varieties might be modified by the controlled expression of either the rhamnosyltransferase involved in the biosynthesis of the bitter flavanone conjugate naringin40 or the GT for limonoids14. Potential targets of GT manipulation could be concerned with the absolute requirement for many secondary metabolites to be stabilized in the cell by glycosylation (e.g. cyanogenic intermediates, stilbenes, isoflavonoids or flavonols). Improved intestinal uptake of glycosylated antioxidative metabolites might be another major objective41,42.

Detoxification of toxic xenobiotics by recombinant GTs has already been shown by transforming yeast with a glycoalkaloid GT, reducing the toxicity of solanidine and related alkaloids in transformed yeast cells38. Overexpressing specific GTs to glycosylate herbicides or herbicide metabolites [e.g. metribuzin, 2,3,4- trichorophenol (a DDT metabolite) and 6-hydroxybentazone] could increase the detoxifying capacity of such transgenic plants and establish a ‘green liver’ concept for crops or fruits43.

The current activity of enzymes for most xenobiotics is rather low. This might be improved by using directed evolution or sitedirected mutagenesis. The introduction of a new GT with altered, defined regiospecificity into certain crop varieties will provide the opportunity to direct the flow of substrates into new directions by, for example, creating artificial sinks within the vacuole44,45. It might even be possible to modify plant growth using these new GTs, as shown by the regulation of cell division in roots of Pisum sativum46. The potential cellular targets are unknown but plant hormones47, flavonoids and sterols appear to be suitable candidates.

Manipulation of the glycosylation potential or intracellular targeting of the respective enzymes in a plant might be within immediate

reach. Other mechanisms, such as the transport of individual metabolites into the plant vacuoles48, should also be taken into consideration and might require more sophisticated techniques.

# Future of GT research

As well as considering the potential economic benefit of genetically improved crops and fruits with the absence or presence of a recombinant GT, GT research should address a variety of outstanding problems. With sequence data and expression systems readily available, the crystal structure of the monomeric PSPG-box GTs should be of immediate interest. We await the first correlation of threedimensional enzyme structures with acceptor-binding specificity.

However, looking at a single protein in detail does not say a lot about cellular function apart from its substrate specificity. Further immunolocalization studies or GT fusions with a readily detectable marker (e.g. green fluorescent protein) in combination with the yeast two-hybrid system might allow us to study subcellular localization in more detail and identify putative macromolecular complexing with earlier enzymes of individual pathways. There is hardly any knowledge about GTs with a preference for unusual sugars, such as $\beta$ quinovose or $\beta$ digitoxose, which are involved in the biosynthesis of saponins and cardiac glycosides, respectively. A thorough understanding of the subcellular localization and organization of a ‘transferase complex’ might encourage the investigation and identification of GTs involved in the biosynthesis of these pharmacologically important compounds.

Mutant plants using these approaches, or transgenic ‘sense’ and ‘antisense’ approaches, will hopefully verify the biological role of GTs. This should be especially interesting with the various stress inducible GT genes from a variety of sources, whose transcript accumulation has never been followed by a detailed investigation at the protein and substrate level; these might even be correlated with an altered phenotype.
