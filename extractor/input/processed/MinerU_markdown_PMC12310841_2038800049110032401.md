# Summary

Theanine is a non-protein free amino acid accumulated in tea plant leaves and makes a major contribution to the unique umami and sweet taste and a multitude of human health benefits of tea. However, its biosynthesis and transcriptional regulation in tea plant leaves remain to be fully understood. Here, we report that an R2R3-MYB CsMYB213 and a bHLH CsbHLH35 co-regulated two key theanine biosynthesis genes, CsTSI and CsAlaDC, and regulate the theanine biosynthesis in tea plant leaves in response to nitrogen availability. By combining extensive co-expression analyses on a large body of transcriptomic data with validation through yeast one-hybrid, gel shift, dual-luciferase reporter assays as well as antisense inhibition experiments, we demonstrated that CsMYB213 bound to the promoters of CsTSI and CsAlaDC and activated theanine biosynthesis in vitro and in vivo. Although showing no transactivation activity alone, CsbHLH35 could interact with CsMYB213 and enhance the CsMYB213 transactivation of CsTSI and CsAlaDC expression. VIGS experiments revealed that silencing either CsMYB213 or CsbHLH35 in tea leaves significantly repressed CsTSI and CsAlaDC expression. Both CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were up-regulated by application of green manure, nitrogen fertilizer alone or in combination with organic fertilizer, aligning with the enhanced theanine accumulation. The CsMYB213-CsbHLH35 module functions as a nitrogen-responsive transcriptional activator of theanine biosynthesis in tea plants. The study sheds light on the complex regulatory mechanism underlying theanine biosynthesis in tea plants under nitrogen nutrition, offering effective cultivation strategies to enhance theanine content in tea leaves. 

# Introduction

Tea (Camellia sinensis (L.) O. Kuntze) is the most widely consumed beverage in the world, besides water, due to its rich flavours and numerous health benefits (Zhao et al., 2021). As the most abundant free amino acid in tea plants, accounting for more than $6 0 \% { - } 7 0 \%$ of total free amino acids, theanine is among the tea characteristic secondary metabolites responsible for the formation of tea flavours and health functions (Ashihara, 2015). L-Theanine (hereinafter called theanine) contributes to distinctive umami in green tea, and it displays a wide variety of physiological activities such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-tumour, neuroprotective and immunomodulatory effects (Turk€ oz€ u and€ Sanlier, 2017). Therefore, theanine content in green tea usually is regarded as one of the important criteria determining the quality and value of green tea. 

Theanine is synthesized from glutamate and ethylamine by theanine synthase CsTSI (TEA015198) in tea roots and then transported to shoot tips (Liu et al., 2017). CsTSI homologues, cytosolic glutamine synthase (GS), CsGSIa (TEA032217, named as CsGS1.1) and plastidic GS, CsGSIIa (TEA028194, named as 

CsGS2), were also reported to catalyse theanine biosynthesis in tea leaves (Fu et al., 2021; She et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024). Since ethylamine is a crucial precursor for theanine synthesis in tea plants (Cheng et al., 2017), an alanine decarboxylase (CsAlaDC, TEA005658) had been characterized from tea plants to synthesize ethylamine from alanine (Bai et al., 2019). CsTSI and CsAlaDC acted coordinately in determining the high level of theanine in transgenic tobacco leaves (Zhu et al., 2021). Theanine accumulation in tea leaves is subject to regulation by many environmental factors, such as nutrients, climate and hormones (Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2017; Turk€ oz€ u and S€ anlier, 2017; Zhang et al., 2023). Theanine serves as a key nitrogen storage compound in tea plants, and its accumulation is significantly influenced by nitrogen (N) (Jiang and Wen, 2025). Both ammonium and nitrate are widely employed in agriculture, and both the form and concentration of nitrogen impact the theanine content in tea plants (Xin et al., 2024). Tea plants show a preference for ammonium, and theanine content has been reported to be positively correlated with ammonium availability (Xin et al., 2024). Different nitrogen fertilizer sources, including organic fertilizers, chemical fertilizers and green manure, affect 

theanine accumulation (Yang et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024). However, the underlying mechanisms by which nitrogen affects theanine accumulation in tea plants remain to be fully understood. 

Transcriptional regulatory mechanisms by which theanine is synthesized in tea plants under various physiological conditions or environmental stresses have been investigated and several transcription factors (TFs) have been identified to regulate theanine synthesis (Guo et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021). For instance, CsMYB6 directly binds to CsTSI to positively regulate, but CsMYB73 binds to CsGSIb (TEA015580) and CsGSIIa to negatively regulate theanine biosynthesis (Wen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021). CsDOF regulates CsGSIIa in response to the light, CsHHO3, CsMYB40 and CsLBD37 bind to CsAlaDC to co-regulate theanine biosynthesis in response to nitrogen levels (Guo et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2021b). Despite these, many other TFs that have been proposed to regulate theanine biosynthesis based on bioinformatics studies and transcriptome data analyses remain to be verified experimentally, because nitrogen metabolism and theanine biosynthesis are complicatedly related and regulated (Cheng et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022a). 

In this study, we analysed a large body of transcriptome data with varying theanine contents and expression levels of CsTSI and CsAlaDC; CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were identified to be closely related to theanine biosynthesis based on co-expression and metabolite-gene correlation analyses. We have employed various approaches such as Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H), electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA), dual-luciferase reporter assay (DLR) and antisense oligonucleotide (asODN) gene-knockdown technology and showed that CsMYB213 positively regulated CsTSI and CsAlaDC expression and thereby theanine biosynthesis in tea plants. Further, using Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H), bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) and DLR, we proved that CsbHLH35 interacted with CsMYB213 and enhanced the expression of two target genes, CsTSI and CsAlaDC. Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) reveals CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 regulate CsTSI and CsAlaDC in response to nitrogen availability. Both CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 are up-regulated by different forms of N-fertilizer to mediate their promotion of theanine accumulation in tea plants. Our study provides new insights into the transcriptional regulatory mechanisms underlying N-fertilizers induction of theanine biosynthesis in tea plants. 

# Results

# Spatiotemporal patterns of theanine production and related gene expression in tea leaves

To understand the spatiotemporal patterns of theanine accumulation in tea leaves, one bud and two leaves from two varieties, ‘Anjibaicha’ (AJBC) and ‘Huangjincha2’ (HJC2), were picked on March 17, March 27 and April 6 (Figure 1a). The global gene expression patterns were compared to explore the regulatory mechanism of seasonal variations on theanine accumulation among them. Along with the contents of theanine, glutamate and alanine initially increasing and then decreasing over plucking time of both two varieties (Figure 1b, Figure S1a,b), 17 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were found in theanine biosynthesis pathway, among which the expression levels of CsTSI and CsAlaDC were tightly and positively correlated with the 

theanine content (Figure 1c,e). The expression levels of CsTSI and CsAlaDC consistently changed with theanine contents in HJC2 and AJBC leaves over all time period. The expression levels of CsGSIIa and CsGSIc also showed a consistency with theanine variations in HJC2 or AJBC leaves (Figure S1c,d), Our data thus supported that CsTSI and CsAlaDC in tea leaves may determine their accumulation of theanine and indicated that CsTSI and CsAlaDC could serve as key theanine biosynthesis genes targeted by regulatory factors. 

GO and KEGG pathway analyses revealed the significant changes in TFs, sequence-specific DNA binding, and amino acid metabolism pathways (Figures S2 and S3). The correlation analyses on 17 theanine biosynthesis-related DEGs and 10 highly expressed TF families showed that MYB and bHLH TF families were enriched in these TFs tightly associated with theanine variations (Figure 1d). Two TFs that show the highest possibility in regulating theanine biosynthesis, CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35, were chosen from the DEGs for further study. Since CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 expression patterns were highly correlated with theanine contents in different periods of HJC2 and AJBC (Figure 1e), we further verified the functions of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 in the transcriptional regulation of theanine biosynthesis. 

# Characterization of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35

CsMYB213 encodes a polypeptide of 430 amino acids that contains the conserved R2 domain and R3 domain, showing high homology to Oryza sativa OsMYB61 and Arabidopsis AtMYB55 (Figure 2a, Figure S4a,c). CsbHLH35 encodes a polypeptide of 244 amino acids, showing high homology to Arabidopsis AtbHLH27 and AtbHLH35, which are typical MYC-type bHLH TFs containing IPR011598 domain and a coiled coil region (Figure 2b, Figure S4b,d). 

The nucleus-localization of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 was determined under a confocal microscope by transiently expressing CsMYB213-GFP and CsbHLH35-GFP fusions in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Figure 2c). While the free GFP was uniformly distributed throughout the cytoplasm and nucleus, the fluorescence signals of CsMYB213-GFP and CsbHLH35-GFP fusions were only shown in the nucleus (Figure 2c). 

OsMYB61 has been shown to directly regulate nitrogen utilization (Gao et al., 2020). CsMYB213, which shares high sequence homology with OsMYB61, is speculated to be involved in nitrogen regulation in tea plants. Fourteen genes related to nitrogen metabolism and theanine biosynthesis, potentially regulated by CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35, were identified based on their tissue expression patterns $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ (tpdb.shengxin.ren/index.html)(Xia et al., 2019). Hierarchical clustering analysis of these genes revealed that CsTSI clustered with CsbHLH35, and CsMYB213 clustered with CsGSIc and CsGSIb, suggesting a potential role of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 in the regulation of theanine biosynthesis (Figure 2d).

# CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 regulated theanine production under various nitrogen applied

In order to understand the molecular mechanism whether CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 respond to nitrogen applied, we sampled one bud and one leaf in spring in a tea garden with 

long-term fertilization experiments. The base fertilizer was applied into the soil in November and lasted for 15 years with three treatments: no fertilization (NF), chemical fertilizer (CF, $100 \%$ urea), chemical and organic fertilizer (CAOF, Rapeseed cakes with the same nitrogen content as $3 3 . 3 \%$ urea and $6 6 . 7 \%$ urea). Theanine contents in tea leaves with fertilizers, no matter 

CAOF or CF, were significantly higher than that without fertilization. The application of nitrogen fertilizer can significantly increase the expression of CsTSI and CsAlaDC, and the expression of CsTSI increases even more when organic fertilizer is applied. CsMYB213 was significantly highly expressed only when CF was applied, while CsbHLH35 was significantly highly expressed when organic fertilizer was applied (Figure 6b). CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were possibly responsive to nitrogen utilization in tea plants, but the responsiveness to nitrogen source and nitrogen application requires further investigation. 

Green manure intercropping is a soil-sustainable cultivation model and may replace part of chemical fertilizers. We examined the theanine content of one bud and one leaf after green manure intercropping. The green manure intercropping experiment has lasted for 5 years with three treatments: (1) with no leguminous green manure intercropping with $100 \%$ chemical fertilizer (CK); (2) $70 \%$ chemical fertilizer with intercropping of Chamaecrista rotundifolia (GM1); and (3) $70 \%$ chemical fertilizer with intercropping of Vicia villosa Roth. (GM2). Theanine contents both increased after applying green manure Chamaecrista rotundifolia and Vicia villosa Roth., where the highest increment of theanine was up to $3 2 \%$ after applying Chamaecrista rotundifolia. Notably, the highest expression of CsMYB213, CsbHLH35, CsTSI and CsAlaDC was observed in the Chamaecrista rotundifolia applied, followed by Vicia villosa Roth. applied, and no green manure applied was the lowest (Figure 6c). The result further illustrates that CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 exhibited responsiveness to nitrogen regulation. 

# Discussion

# CsMYB213-CsbHLH35 complex promotes theanine biosynthesis

In spring, AJBC and HJC2 were tested for the expression of key theanine biosynthesis genes CsTSI, CsGSIc, CsGSIIa and CsAlaDC. Our results indicate that theanine biosynthesis gene expression is in line with the trend of theanine content, particularly the expression levels of CsTSI and CsAlaDC, which were positively correlated with the theanine contents, but the expression of CsGSIIa showed consistency with the trends of theanine in the AJBC variety (Figure S1d). Thus, CsTSI and CsAlaDC are pivotal for theanine biosynthesis (Zhu et al., 2021). 

MYB TFs in tea plants have been extensively studied for their diverse roles in regulating secondary metabolite biosynthesis, plant development and stress response (Li et al., 2022a). Several MYB TFs are involved in the regulation of theanine biosynthesis. For instance, CsMYB6 directly binds to and activates the promoter of CsTSI, enhancing theanine levels in tea roots (Zhang et al., 2021). CsMYB73 binds to CsGSIb and CsGSIIa, inhibiting theanine in tea shoots (Wen et al., 2020). CsMYB40 regulates CsAlaDC to maintain theanine levels in tea roots in response to nitrogen (Guo et al., 2022). In this study, we showed that CsMYB213 was positively correlated with CsTSI and CsAlaDC in two varieties during growth stages (Figure 2e). Both biochemical data showed that CsMYB213 binds to the CsAlaDC and CsTSI promoters and enhances their expression; also, genetically knocking down CsMYB213 in tea leaves significantly downregulated these genes and reduced theanine levels. CsTSI expression is lower in leaves than in roots (She et al., 2022). The non-significant difference in the down-regulation of CsTSI may be due to the relatively lower expression in leaves (Dong et al., 2020). Interestingly, CsMYB213 was found to have a 

remarkably strong correlation with theanine accumulation in the leaves of tea plants and theanine close relatives in Camellia sinensis var. sinensis (CSS) databases (Table S1) (Xia et al., 2019). 

The module regulation of CsTSI and CsAlaDC expression in tea plant leaves is unknown. The cooperative regulation of CsTSI and CsAlaDC by CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 could further enhance theanine biosynthesis by serving as a paradigm of combining regulation of TFs in plants (Feller et al., 2011). MYB-bHLH interaction complexes in regulating glucosinolate, carotenoid and anthocyanidin/proanthocyanidin biosynthesis, epidermal cell development and differentiation in plants have been well dressed (Frerigmann et al., 2014; James et al., 2017). The MYB-bHLH-WD40 complexes in tea plants are also often observed in regulating flavonoid/carotenoid biosynthesis (Li et al., 2022b). In this study, we showed that a strong interaction between CsbHLH35 and CsMYB213, in which CsbHLH35 acts as an enhancer partner (Figure 5). Induction of CsMYB213 by CsbHLH35 acts as an additional positive feedback loop leading to amplification of theanine regulation, and such regulation has been reported in anthocyanin biosynthesis. Suppression of CsbHLH35 in tea leaves significantly reduced theanine content, further revealing the role of CsbHLH35 in regulating theanine biosynthesis. Whether CsMYB213-CsbHLH35 regulates other theanine metabolism genes and whether CsbHLH35 interacts with other TFs to regulate theanine metabolism genes deserves further exploration. 

# Nitrogen application promotes theanine accumulation via CsbHLH35-CsMYB213 module

The content of theanine is positively correlated with the quality and value of green tea (Yamaguchi and Ninomiya, 2000). Theanine is an N-containing secondary metabolite and its biosynthesis is tightly regulated (Liu et al., 2019, 2020; Wang et al., 2020). Although N-fertilizers have been shown to promote the theanine accumulation, several TFs have been shown to regulate the process; details about them remain elusive (Guo et al., 2022). Oryza sativa OsMYB61 is directly subject to regulation by the known NUE regulator GROWTH-REGULATING FACTOR4, which coordinates cellulosic biomass production and N utilization in rice, and natural variation in the MYB61 locus results in different biomass production and nitrogen utilization between indica and japonica subspecies (Gao et al., 2020). Our observations support the speculation that different N levels affected CsMYB213 expression, as CsMYB213 is an ortholog of Oryza sativa OsMYB61. 

The N responses of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 genes were verified; they are up-regulated under 10 mM ammonium treatment, consistent with the report that theanine content and CsTSI expression also increased significantly under $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ ammonium (She et al., 2022). VIGS experiments confirmed that ammonium-N-regulated CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 could affect the expression of CsTSI and CsAlaDC. The theanine content could not be detected due to the limited amounts of tea leaf samples. N promotes the expression of CsAlaDC and theanine biosynthesis in roots (Wang et al., 2021a). Other MYB TFs reported to regulate theanine biosynthesis, such as CsMYB73, CsMYB42 and CsMYB40 (Guo et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023), showed correlations with different genes involved in N metabolism based on leaf transcriptomes. CsMYB213, CsbHLH35, CsMYB73, CsMYB42 and CsMYB40 may play different roles in leaves under different treatments, and the coordinated regulation among these TFs is worthy of further study.

Although we showed that CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 respond to N, the underlying mechanism by which the two TFs are affected by long-term N application remains elusive. No matter whether CAOF or CF was applied, the theanine content and expression of CsTSI and CsAlaDC in leaves treated with fertilizer was significantly higher than that in leaves not treated with Nfertilizer. This indicates that N has a promoting effect on theanine accumulation in leaves. The expression of CsTSI increases even more when organic fertilizer is applied and CsbHLH35 was expressed significantly highly (Figure 6b). 

Leguminous green manure plants work with rhizobia to fix atmospheric nitrogen and, therefore, enrich the soil with nitrogen at the time of application (Balachandar et al., 2020). Green manure application is effective in boosting total amino acid levels in tea leaves in the spring (Huang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024), with theanine accounting for almost $50 \%$ or more of the total free amino acids. Application of green manure improved theanine contents in spring tea leaves in consistency with the increased expression levels of CsTSI, CsAlaDC, CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 (Figure 6). We posit that CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 are also regulated by N status in tea plants and responsible for the N-induced theanine accumulation in tea plants. The combination of organic fertilizer and chemical fertilizer and green manure application may be a good strategy to promote the theanine accumulation in tea leaves. 

In summary, we defined CsMYB213 as a key TF positively regulating the transcription of theanine biosynthesis genes CsTSI and CsAlaDC and leading to the accumulation of theanine in tea leaves. Moreover, the CsMYB213-CsbHLH35 interaction complex acts as a regulatory module, forming a stronger activation module and positive feedback loop to amplify regulatory effects on CsTSI and CsAlaDC transcription and theanine biosynthesis. Both TFs respond to N, being up-regulated by the application of different 

forms of N-fertilizers and green maturity (Figure 7). The study provides new insights into theanine biosynthesis regulation and lays the foundation for the improvement of tea garden practices for enhanced theanine accumulation in tea plant leaves and better quality. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and treatments

Tea plants (Camellia sinensis L. O. Kuntze cv. (AJBC and HJC2)) were cultivated at the Tea Research Institute of Hunan Province, Hunan, China. One bud and two leaves were randomly picked from the tea plantation during the spring season, 2021. For each sample, three biological replicates were taken. The plant materials were flash-frozen immediately with liquid nitrogen and transported to the laboratory for storage in a $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ freezer. 

The field trial was carried out at the Hunan Provincial Tea Research Institute in Hunan, China, using a randomized complete block design. Three fertilizer treatments were applied with 15 years: (1) no fertilizer; (2) chemical and organic fertilizer (rapeseed cake and $6 6 . 7 \%$ urea); and (3) chemical fertilizer only ( $100 \%$ urea). On April 6, 2022, samples of one bud and one leaf of ‘Fudingdabaicha’ were picked and collected for analysis. Additionally, there were three green manure treatments with 5 years: 1) with no leguminous green manure intercropping with $100 \%$ chemical fertilizer $4 5 0 ~ \mathsf { k g / h a }$ urea, CK); 2) $70 \%$ chemical fertilizer with intercropping of Chamaecrista rotundifolia (GM1); and 3) $70 \%$ chemical fertilizer with intercropping of Vicia villosa Roth. (GM2). On March 27, 2022, samples of one bud and one leaf of HJC2 were picked and collected for analysis (Zhou et al., 2024). Roots of the tea plant seedling grown hydroponically under different N treatment was referred from the literature (She et al., 2022). 

Tobacco (N. benthamiana) plants were grown from seeds in a greenhouse under the same conditions. 

# Determination of the total and free amino acid contents

The samples were vacuum freeze-dried to constant weight for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ (dry weight). Total and free amino acid contents were extracted and detected as previously described with slight modification (Xie et al., 2021). The modification was to mix an aliquot (1.5 g) with $2 2 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of distilled water and extract at $1 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 45 min. 

# RNA extraction, transcriptome analysis and qRT-PCR

Total RNA was isolated from the frozen tea leaf tissue lysates following the manufacturer’s protocol and purified using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). RNA quantity and purity were measured at 260 nm with spectrophotometry, and RNA integrity was verified at $1 . 0 \%$ agarose gel electrophoresis. Transcriptome sequencing was conducted on an Illumina HiSeq2000 platform at Novegene Tech (Beijing, China) and clean reads were aligned to the tea plant genome sequence (Wei et al., 2018). DEGs were Screened by $[ \left| \mathsf { l o g } ^ { 2 } \right.$ (Fold Change) $> 0 ]$ . Total RNA was reverse transcribed into the first strand of cDNA using the manufacturer’s protocol (First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit, Qiagen). Beta-actin ( $\mathrm { \Delta \beta }$ -actin) was employed as the endogenous reference gene. Relative expression levels of the genes were normalized using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T }$ method. All primers used in this study are listed in Table S2. 

# Gene identification, characterization analysis and cloning

The CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 genes were identified in Camellia sinensis var. sinensis (CSS) databases (tpdb.shengxin.ren/index. html) (Xia et al., 2019). Amino acid sequences were translated using the National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) and sequence data of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 have been uploaded in the NCBI under Accession numbers PQ638907 and PQ638908. The characteristics and properties of the amino acids were predicted using ExPASy software. Protein sequences of MYB TFs from other plants were downloaded from NCBI and TAIR (TAIR – Home Page (arabidopsis.org)). The full length of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 coding sequences (CDS) were amplified by PCR with primers. 

# Subcellular localization

The CsMYB213-GFP and CsbHLH35-GFP (fusion proteins of green fluorescent protein (GFP) fused CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35, respectively) vectors were constructed by inserting the coding sequence (CDS) of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 into the pEAQ-GFP vector. All plasmids were constructed by the strategy of homologous recombination. CsMYB213-GFP and CsbHLH35- GFP constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (EHA105) and grown in LB liquid medium supplemented with kanamycin (Kna) at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for approximately 48 h. The bacterial cultures were then used to infiltrate N. benthamiana leaves. GFP expression in leaves was visualized using a Zeiss Axioskop microscope after $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . 

# Transcriptional activation in yeast cells

Transcriptional activity analysis of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 was performed in yeast cells. The CDS of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 was cloned in the pGBKT7 vector (pGBKT7-CsMYB213, pGBKT7- CsbHLH35), respectively, with the pGBKT7 empty vector as a 

negative control and pGBKT7-P53 as a positive control (Clontech). All constructs were transformed into yeast cells (Y2H Gold) and grown on a control medium (SD/-Trp) or selective medium (SD/-Trp/-His/-Ade), respectively. Transcriptional activation was determined by growing transformants on SD/-Trp/-His/-Ade medium for 2 days and quantifying X-a-gal activity. 

# Protein purification and EMSA assay

The CsMYB213-structural domain (from amino acid positions 0–233) was cloned into the pGEX-4T vector (Amersham Biosciences) to create GST-CsMYB213-N, which was then expressed in an E. coli (DE3) strain. The fusion protein was subsequently purified using glutathione sepharose beads. The quality of the recombinant CsMYB213 protein was assessed by SDS-PAGE separation. Biotin-labelled CsTSI and CsAlaDC promoter oligonucleotide probes, along with biotin-labelled primers, were synthesized by PCR. EMSA was conducted using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The probes used in this study are listed in Table S2. 

# DLR assay

Transcriptional activities of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 and their effects on CsTSI and CsAlaDC were analysed using a dualluciferase reporter gene system in N. benthamiana leaves. The CDS of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were ligated into the expression vector pBD (pBD-CsMYB213, pBD-CsbHLH35). The recombinant plasmids and the pBD empty vector (negative control) were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105, respectively, for transcriptional activity analysis. After 2 or 3 days of infiltration, firefly LUC and Renilla LUC were measured using a dual-luciferase assay kit (Promega). Effects between genes and TFs were analysed by the dual-luciferase reporter gene system. The recombinant plasmids pEAQ-CsMYB213 and pEAQ-CsbHLH35, 0800-proCsAlaDC and 0800-proCsTSI were constructed. PEAQ-CsMYB213 and pEAQ-CsMYB213 were used as effectors, and 0800-proCsAlaDC and 0800-proCsTSI were used as reporters transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105, infiltrated for 2 or 3 days and then assayed with the dual-luciferase assay kit (Promega) to analyse firefly luciferase (Fluc) and REN luciferase (Rluc). Subsequent experimental methods are transcriptional activity analysis. 

# Y1H assay

Y1H assay was performed to verify the interaction between CsMYB213 and CsTSI promoter and CsAlaDC promoter. The CDS of CsMYB213 was inserted into the pGADT7 vector to generate recombinant pGADT7-CsMYB213 (AD-CsMYB213) to generate a prey vector. The fragments of the CsTSI (1109-bp) promoter and CsAlaDC promoter (127-bp) were amplified from tea plant genomic DNA and cloned into the pAbAi vector to generate bait vectors. p53-pAbAi was also a bait vector used as a positive control. The bait vector was digested with BbsI, introduced into yeast strain Y1H GOLD and cultured on SD/-Ura to generate a reporter strain. Different concentrations of ABA screened for false positives in appropriate concentration reduction experiments. 

The pAbAi-promoter, AD-CsMYB213 and pAbAi were used as negative controls. Ad-53 and p53-pAbAi were used as positive controls. Plasmids were co-transformed into Y1H Gold yeast and placed on SD-Trp/-Leu/-His medium containing 100–200 ng ml1 Aureobasidin A (AbA) after extensive screening. 

# Y2H and BIFC

For the Y2H assay, the full-length coding sequences of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were cloned into the pGADT7 or pGBKT7 vectors, respectively. After self-activation capacity testing (transcriptional activation in yeast cells), the pGADT7-CsMYB213 and pGBKT7-CsbHLH35 constructs were introduced into the yeast cells (Y2H Gold) for interaction assays. Interaction tests were performed on control media (SD/-Leu/-Trp, SD/-L-T) or selective medium (SD/-Leu/-Trp/-Ade/-His, SD/-L-T-A-H), and interaction was determined by growing transformants on SD/-L-T-A-H medium for 2 days and quantifying X-a-gal activity. 

Full-length coding sequences of CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were respectively cloned into yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) vectors pSPYCE or pSPYNE (YCE-CsMYB213, YCE-CsbHLH35, YNE-CsMYB213 and YNE-CsbHLH35). EHA105 containing all the constructs was co-trans-expressed in the tobacco. The YFP fluorescent photographs were recorded under a fluorescence microscope. 

# Antisense oligonucleotides assay

CsMYB213 and CsbHLH35 were genetically repressed in tea plants using asODN as described previously with slight modifications. Briefly speaking, asODN was designed using SOLIGO software (https://sfold.wadsworth.org/cgi-bin/soligo.pl) (Table S2), one bud and two leaves were picked off and partially inserted into an Eppendorf tube containing $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $2 0 ~ \mathsf { u M }$ asODN solution and a sODN solution of the gene as a control. Samples were harvested after 48 h of treatment for gene expression analysis, and theanine content was determined after 72 h. 

# VIGS experiment

The CsMYB213 (884–1542 bp) and CsbHLH35 (307–606 bp) fragments were designed using the SGN VIGS Tool for VIGS (https://vigs.solgenomics.net/). The fragments were cloned into the pTRV2 vector to construct the pTRV2-CsMYB213 and pTRV2- CsbHLH35 recombinant plasmids. The recombinant plasmids and pTRV1 plasmid were transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105, respectively. VIGS experiments were performed on 100 tea branches from HJC2 and FDDBC using vacuum infection. The detailed transformation method was described by Li et al. (2022b). Branches grown hydroponically under $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ ammonium (10 N) or 5 mM ammonium (5 N) culture were referred from the literature (She et al., 2022). After 35 days of hydroponic culture, one newly emerged bud and two leaves were harvested. All leaves of ‘Fudingdabaicha’ fell off during hydroponics. 5–8 samples of HJC2 with each treatment were collected, quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The silencing efficiency of the target gene and the expression of downstream genes were detected. Each group had at least 5 biological replicates and 3 technical repeats. 

# Statistical analyses

Analysis of variance was performed using SPSS software (v22.0, SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL) to determine the least significant difference between treatments (different letters, $P < 0 . 0 5$ , Duncan’s multiple range test or $^ { * } P < 0 . 0 5$ , $^ { * * } P < 0 . 0 1$ , $* * * P < 0 . 0 0 1$ , two-tailed test) for comparison of means. Means and SDs were calculated from three or six independent biological replicates. GraphPad Prism (v8.0.1) was used to process the data and generate images.
