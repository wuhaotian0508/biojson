# A B S T R A C T

This review summarizes research on the discovery, biosynthesis, accumulation, transport, and metabolism of 3- alkyl-2-methoxypyrazines (MPs) in grape. The MPs are a family of potent volatile compounds distributed throughout biological kingdoms. These compounds impart herbaceous/green/vegetal sensory attributes to certain varieties of wine. Generally, high levels of MPs in wine are derived mainly from the corresponding grapes. Although two pathways for MPs biosynthesis have been proposed, only the final step and the enzymes that catalyze it has been confirmed in grape, and the metabolic intermediates and key enzymes involved in other steps are still unknown. The limited understanding of MPs metabolism has restricted research on these compounds, and some empirical results cannot be explained by the current knowledge of MPs metabolism. This review provides insights into research on MPs biosynthesis and metabolism, and proposes directions for further research on this important class of flavour/odour compounds.

# 1. Introduction

3-Alkyl-2-methoxypyrazines (MPs) are volatile nitrogen-containing heterocyclic compounds that are widely distributed in plants, insects, fungi, and bacteria (Gerber, 1977; Moore, Brown, & Rothschild, 1990; Murray & Whitfield, 1975; Schulz & Dickschat, 2007). They act as an odour signal to deter potential predators, and effectively prevent vegetative tissues or unripe fruit from being eaten (Moore et al., 1990; Rothschild, Moore, & Brown, 1984). Because MPs can be detrimental taste and odour (T&O) compounds, their removal by water has been studied extensively (Wright, Daurie, & Gagnon, 2014). The MPs have been extensively studied in the context of viticulture and enology,

because their herbaceous and vegetal sensory characteristics contribute to the aroma and flavour of wine (Mozzon, Savini, Boselli, & Thorngate, 2016).

In grape berries, MPs exist as volatile free compounds. The MPs concentration in wine is strongly correlated with the final MPs concentration in the berries it is made from (Roujou-de-Boubée, Cumsille, Pons, & Dubordieu, 2002). The vegetative varietal aromas of MPs are acceptable in some white wines, such as Sauvignon blanc, Chardonnay, Semillon, and Riesling (Hashizume & Samuta, 1999; Parr, Green, White, & Sherlock, 2007; van Wyngaard, Brand, Jacobson, & du Toit, 2014). However, their presence is detrimental in some red wine varieties, such as Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Cabernet franc and Carmenere. High

levels of MPs may result in unacceptable green and unripe aromas that negatively affect wine quality, and excessive levels are undesirable for the consumer and the wine industry (Belancic & Agosin, 2007; Hashizume & Umeda, 1996; Roujou-de-Boubée et al., 2002; van Leeuwen, Roby, Alonso-Villaverde, & Gindro, 2013; Ward, Petrie, Johnson, Boss, & Bastian, 2015).

Several recent studies have focussed on the abundance of MPs in grapes and wines in relation to various factors, such as climate and soil, humidity, temperature, light exposure, grape maturation, multi-coloured Asian lady beetles, as well as viticultural and enological practices (Sidhu, Lund, Kotseridis, & Saucier, 2015). The contents of MPs in wines depend mainly on their concentration in the grapes they are made from, and are only marginally affected by winemaking techniques. Only a few studies have focussed on the pathways of MP metabolism.

In general, vines cultivated in cool climates tend to contain higher levels of MPs (Lacey, Allen, Harris, & Brown, 1991; Mendez-Costabel et al., 2013). However, Harris, Ryona, and Sacks (2012) observed the opposite trend; that is, grapes from Central Valley in California contained a higher 3-isobutyl-2-methoxypyrazine (IBMP) concentration than did those cultivated in the Finger Lakes region of New York and Bordeaux, even though Central Valley is considerably warmer than the other two locations. The authors suggested that the growth conditions, particularly high water availability and high temperature, were correlated with higher IBMP accumulation.

The wine industry has explored the use of certain viticultural practices to influence MPs levels in grapes at harvest, and defoliation in the bunch area has become a widely used technique to reduce the MPs content in grapes. It has been suggested that early defoliation in the bunch area can reduce the concentration of MPs in mature grapes. However, in a 3-year study, Gregan and Jordan (2016) found that leaf removal led to a significant reduction in MPs, particularly IBMP, in two years (2010 and 2012), but no change in one year (2011). Analyses of the meteorological data revealed that temperatures were warmer than average in 2011, suggesting that light and temperature affected MPs accumulation after bunch area defoliation. Because the biosynthetic pathway of MPs has not been fully elucidated, these empirical results cannot be interpreted by the patterns of MP biosynthesis and/or metabolism. Thus, there is a need to elucidate the details of MPs metabolism in grape.

Previous studies have focussed on the methods to detect MPs, and the factors influencing their levels in wine and grapes, including viticultural and enological practices. In this review, we summarize research on the discovery, accumulation, and transport of MPs in grape, and discuss recent studies on the mechanisms of MPs metabolism. Finally, we suggest future directions for research on MPs metabolism.

# 2. MPs in grapes and wine

# 2.1. Discovery and classification of MPs in grape

Many different fruits and vegetables contain MPs (Murray & Whitfield, 1975). The compound IBMP was first identified in green capsicum (bell pepper), at concentrations up to $5 0 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { \cdot } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ , and was associated it with the distinct capsicum odour (Buttery, Seifert, Guadagni, & Ling, 1969). Subsequently, 3-sec-butyl-2-methoxypyrazine (SBMP) and 3-isopropyl-2-methoxypyrazine (IPMP) were identified from galbanum oil and green pea, respectively (Murray, Shipton, & Whitfield, 1970). The highest concentration of SBMP was found in beetroot (approximately $5 6 0 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Murray & Whitfield, 1975), and the highest IPMP concentration was found in green peas (approximately $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { n g \cdot l ^ { - 1 } } .$ ). The compound IPMP is commonly described as a ‘sweet pea-like’ and ‘asparagus-like’ odorant (Murray et al., 1970), but it is also associated with the earthy aroma reminiscent of roots (Gerber, 1977). Normally, MPs are present at very low concentrations (low $\mathbf { n } \mathbf { g } \mathbf { \cdot } \mathbf { l } ^ { - 1 }$ levels) in grapes and wines, typically $2 { - } 3 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 }$ (Allen, Lacey, & Boyd, 1994; Allen, Lacey, Harris, & Brown, 1991; Harris, Lacey, Brown, & Allen, 1987; Lacey et al., 1991). The sensory detection threshold of these compounds is also extremely low, as low as $1 { - } 2 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 }$ in water, and $2 { \mathrm { - } } 1 6 \mathrm { n g } { \mathrm { - } } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ in wine (Sidhu et al., 2015). This explains why MPs have such a strong effect on aroma and flavour.

With the increasing sensitivity of detection technology, seven MPs (Fig. 1) have been identified in grapes and wines; 2-methoxypyrazine (2-MP), 3-methyl-2-methoxypyrazine (MEMP), 3-ethyl-2-methoxy-pyrazine (ETMP), IPMP, IBMP, SBMP, and 2,5-dimethyl-3-methoxypyrazine (DMMP) (Sala, Mestres, Marti, Busto, & Guasch, 2000; Seifert, Buttery, Guadagni, Black, & Harris, 1970, 1972; Takken, van der Linde, Boelens, & van Dort, 1975; Botezatu & Pickering, 2012). Among these, IBMP, IPMP, and SBMP are the three most abundant MPs in grapes and wines (Hashizume & Umeda, 1996; Murray & Whitfield, 1975). Other MPs have been identified in wines, but only at very low concentrations that do not affect wine aroma or flavour (Allen et al., 1994).

In 1975, IBMP was identified for the first time in Cabernet Sauvignon grapes (Vitis vinifera L. cv. Cabernet Sauvignon) by Bayonove and Cordonnier. This compound was considered to be responsible for the green pepper aroma of Cabernet Sauvignon grapes. Further studies demonstrated its contribution to the vegetal and green pepper aromas. The concentration of IBMP often significantly exceeds the detection threshold; therefore, it is the main compound responsible for the green pepper aroma in some grape varieties such as Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Sauvignon blanc, and in the wines made from them (Allen, Lacey, Brown, & Harris, 1989; Allen et al., 1991, 1994; Harris et al., 1987; Maga, 1989; Kotseridis, Anocibar, Bertrand, & Doazan, 1998; Roujou-de-Boubée, Van leeuwen, & Dubordieu, 2000).

In general, IPMP is the main odour compound in peas and cucumber, and there are only a few cases of its concentration exceeding the detection threshold and impacting wine flavour. The compound SBMP has rarely been detected in grapes and wines, so it has almost no adverse effect on wine quality (Legrum, Slabizki, & Schmarr, 2015; Murray & Whitfield, 1975).

More studies have focussed on IBMP than on IPMP, and there have been no reports on SBMP affecting wine quality. Therefore, IBMP and IPMP are the two most important compounds in terms of wine aroma

and flavour. Further improvements to the isolation, extraction, purification, detection, and quantification methods for other MPs would be useful for evaluating their effects on the sensory characteristics of grapes and wines.

# 2.2. Accumulation and degradation of MPs in grape

The MPs are present throughout the whole grapevine, including the root, shoot, leaf, and cluster. In grape root, MPs are present mainly in the form of IPMP at concentrations of up to $8 0 0 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { \cdot } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ (4000 times the sensory threshold). In grape, IPMP is sometimes described as having a ‘root-like’ or ‘earthy’ aroma. Above-ground parts of the grapevine contain IBMP, but whether they possibly contain IPMP and SBMP (or not) has not been reported yet. Among the above-ground parts, basal leaves contain the highest IBMP concentration, followed by middle leaves, and then cluster and top leaves and lateral shoots (Roujou-de-Boubée & Botella, 2003).

In grape leaves, the IBMP concentration tends to increase during grapevine growth. For instance, the initial IBMP concentration in basal leaves and middle leaves was found to be $2 4 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ and $5 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { \cdot l } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, but was $4 5 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 }$ and $1 2 0 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } 1 ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, at maturity (Roujou-de-Boubée & Botella, 2003). In grape berries, IBMP was shown to accumulate rapidly between fruit set and preveraison, peak at 2–3 weeks before the onset of veraison, but then decrease markedly as the grapes ripened until harvest (Ryona, Leclerc, & Sacks, 2010). During grape berry maturation, the proportion of IBMP increases in the skins, and decreases in the stems and seeds (Roujou-de-Boubée & Botella, 2003). However, whether this decrease is enzymatic or nonenzymatic is unknown. Although early studies suggested that MPs in grapes may be photodegraded in vivo (Heymann, Noble, & Boulton, 1986), the results of recent reports have not supported this idea (Ryona, Pan, Intrigliolo, Lakso, & Sacks, 2008; Sala et al., 2004). In addition, IBMP accumulates in the basal leaves, which receive as much, if not more, light than the cluster. The metabolism of IBMP may differ between the leaves and the berries, but further research is required to confirm or refute this hypothesis.

Analyses of MPs localization within the grape cluster have shown that the stems contain the largest proportion of IBMP $( 7 9 . 2 \% )$ . In the berries, most of the IBMP is located in the skin $( 7 2 \% )$ and less in the seeds $( 2 3 . 8 \% )$ ). The pulp contains very little IBMP $( 4 . 2 \% )$ (Roujou-de-Boubée & Botella, 2003).

Only a few studies have focussed on the distribution and accumulation of MPs within grapevines. More studies on this topic would be useful to provide the basis for further research on these compounds.

# 2.3. Transport and localization of MPs in grape

There are two different theories about the origins of IBMP in grape. One theory is that IBMP in grape is transported and also synthesized in situ (Fig. 2a). This theory was tested by a stable isotope tracer method, using $1 \mathrm { m g } 1 ^ { - 1 } 2 ( ^ { 2 } \mathrm { H } _ { 3 } )$ methoxy-3-isobutylpyrazine (a deuterated analogue of IBMP) as the tracer. The tracer was deposited on the leaf for 3 days, and then deuterated IBMP was detected in the petiole, stem, and berries. These results demonstrated that IBMP was transported via the phloem from the leaves to the berries, and the large IBMP reserves in leaves supplied the clusters during development. Furthermore, $8 0 \%$ of deuterated IBMP was found in the petioles, and only $1 0 \%$ in growing parts of vine, such as the young leaves and apex, indicating that IBMP had not yet fully migrated towards the other plant organs. It was postulated that the berries were capable of synthesizing MPs, and that the IBMP in the berries was transported from the leaves and synthesized in situ (Roujou-de-Boubée & Botella, 2003).

Another theory is that IBMP in grape is synthesized in situ, and not transferred from the shoot (Fig. 2b). Koch, Doyle, Matthews, Williams, and Ebeler (2010) determined the location of IBMP biosynthesis by grafting Cabernet Sauvignon and Muscat clusters onto each other. The

former could synthesize and accumulate IBMP in whole vine, while the latter could not synthesize IBMP in any part of vine. When the translocation and accumulation of IBMP was monitored after grafting, Cabernet Sauvignon leaves could synthesize and accumulate IBMP, but no IBMP was detected in Muscat berries grafted onto a Cabernet Sauvignon vine. In contrast, Muscat vines could not accumulate IBMP, but IBMP was detected in Cabernet Sauvignon berries grafted onto Muscat vines. These results suggested that IBMP and its precursors originated in the berry and IBMP was not translocated from the shoot to the fruit.

In view of these two theories, further research is needed to identify the exact biosynthetic site of IBMP, and to determine whether the IBMP is synthesized in leaves and translocated to grape berries and, if it is transported, then in what form (intermediate metabolites or end-product).

# 3. Mechanism of MPs metabolism

# 3.1. Hypothesized pathways of MPs biosynthesis

Several researchers have proposed pathways of MPs formation (Hodge, 1953; Hodge, Mills, & Fischer, 1972; Koehler, Mason, & Newell, 1969; Manley, Vallon, & Erikson, 1974; Reineccius, Keeney, & Weiasberger, 1972; Rizzi, 1972, 1988; Shibamoto & Bernhard, 1977). At present, there are two proposed pathways of MPs biosynthesis.

The first pathway was proposed more than 40 years ago (Murray et al., 1970). The initial steps involve amidation of a branched-chain amino acid, followed by condensation with an α, $\beta$ -dicarbonyl compound (Fig. 3a), such as glyoxal or glyoxylic acid. A subsequent methylation reaction leads to methoxy-substituted pyrazines. The amino acids leucine, valine, and isoleucine are the proposed precursors of IBMP, IPMP, and SBMP, respectively. This pathway was not supported by other researchers at the time. It was believed that there was no biochemical precedent for the proposed reactions. First, there was no evidence that amino acids could form α-amino acid amides. Glutamine and asparagine are the only amino acid amides known to occur naturally, and both are amides of the ω- carboxylic group, not the α-carboxylic group. Second, glyoxal has never been detected in plant tissues, despite the presence of glyoxylic acid as a metabolic intermediate in plants that could participate in the condensation reaction (McIver & Reineccius, 1986; Morgan, 1976; Nursten & Sheen, 1974).

In 2003, in line with Murray’s hypothesis, Roujou-de-Boubée and colleagues studied the biosynthesis of IBMP using Cabernet Sauvignon cell cultures. First, they demonstrated that undifferentiated Cabernet Sauvignon callus was capable of synthesizing IBMP. The addition of leucine, the supposed precursor of IBMP, to the culture medium, led to an increase in IBMP production, indicating that leucine was likely the precursor of IBMP. However, in another experiment, the addition of Lleucine- $\mathbf { \cdot d } _ { 1 0 }$ and $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } \mathrm { H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { C l }$ (stable isotope tracers) did not lead to isotopic enrichment of the IBMP produced by the cells, suggesting that leucine was not the precursor of IBMP. This result may have been because they used a stable isotope that was difficult to track accurately. Therefore, these tracer tests should be performed again, using a different stable isotope tracer to determine whether leucine is the precursor of IBMP.

Before another hypothesis pathway was proposed, Gallois, Kergomard, and Adda (1988) proposed that valine and glycine were possible precursors of IPMP in Pseudomonas taetrolens. They conducted tracer tests by adding $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } \mathrm { - L }$ -valine and $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -glycine to the culture medium of P. taetrolens. They obtained IPMP labelled at the C-2 position when $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } \mathrm { - L }$ -valine was added to the culture medium, but $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -glycine (which was supposed to be the second precursor) was not incorporated into IPMP.

In 1991, Cheng et al. used $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -pyruvate as the sole carbon source in the culture medium of Pseudomonas perolens in a tracer test. The results showed that the initial step in pyrazine biosynthesis is the condensation of two amino acids to a cyclic dipeptide, which is then converted into the pyrazine molecule (Fig. 3b), such as the formation of IPMP via the

Whether there are other key enzymes involved in steps before the final one remains unknown.

# 3.3. Intermediate metabolites of MPs biosynthesis

2-Hydroxy-3-alkylpyrazine is the only intermediate metabolite that has been detected in wine grapes so far. This metabolite is associated with the final step of MP biosynthesis, which involves O-methylation of HPs according to the proposed pathways (Murray et al., 1970). Hashizume, Tozawa, Endo, et al. (2001a) detected IBHP and IPHP in immature wine grapes; the levels of HPs were 2- to 20-fold higher than the levels of MPs in eight different grapevine cultivars tested. This result suggested that the enzymatic methylation of HPs to MPs is an important factor determining the MPs level in grape berries (Hashizume, Tozawa, Endo, et al., 2001a). The levels of both IBHP and IBMP in berries increased preveraison, and transcription analyses revealed that VvOMT1 and VvOMT2 were also expressed preveraison, corresponding to the time of maximum IBMP accumulation in grape berries (Dunlevy et al., 2010; Harris et al., 2012). These results were compatible with the hypothesis that IBMP is methylated from IBHP preveraison, and suggested that maximum IBMP accumulation is dependent on IBHP accumulation. However, the maximum IBHP level per berry was detected 2 weeks after the maximum IBMP level, and VvOMT1 expression had decreased during this period (Dunlevy et al., 2010; Harris et al., 2012), correlating with a decrease in IBMP.

On the basis of those results, Harris et al. (2012) hypothesized that MPs are degraded to their corresponding HPs, similar to the way in which IBMP is metabolized into IBHP and IBHP glycosides in rats (Hawksworth & Scheline, 1975). This process may occur during fruit ripening, where the demethylation of IBMP to IBHP effectively reverses the final putative step in IBMP synthesis. Therefore, the demethylation of IBMP is a possible explanation for the increase in IBHP at veraison. If this pathway exists, then there should be an inverse quantitative relationship between MPs and HPs during ripening.

Interestingly, Ryona et al. (2010) observed a significant decline in

IBMP $( 1 2 5 { - } 1 5 \mathrm { n g } { \cdot } \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 }$ and an increase in IBHP (undetectable to $4 2 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) during bell pepper ripening, illustrating an inverse correlation between IBMP and IBHP. In grape, however, all of the tested cultivars (Cabernet Franc, Riesling, and Pinot noir) had comparable IBHP $( 6 4 { - } 8 8 \mathrm { p g { \cdot } m l ^ { - 1 } } .$ ) and IBMP $( 2 5 9 { - } 1 1 \ \mathrm { p g { \cdot } m l } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) concentrations at preveraison, but their concentrations differed by up to two orders of magnitude (undetectable to $2 3 5 \mathrm { p g } \mathrm { \cdot m l } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) at post-veraison among the different varieties. Acid hydrolysis of the grape juice of Cabernet Franc resulted in a significant increase $( 3 3 \% )$ in IBHP, indicating that some IBHP existed as a bound form. Therefore, they suggested that IBMP is demethylated to reform IBHP and then partially glycosylated. Thus, IBHP was proposed to be a key intermediate in both the biosynthesis and degradation of the herbaceous odour compound IBMP in grapes. However, when the juice of 57-day Cabernet Franc was subjected to acid hydrolysis, the IBHP concentration did not increase significantly (186 pg/berry to 210 pg/berry; $p > 0 . 0 5$ ) (Harris et al., 2012). They found that the highest concentrations of IBHP per g of tissue was in Merlot grapes, which also had the highest IBMP concentration. They suggested that differences in IBMP accumulation may be governed at least partially by differences in IBHP accumulation.

In future studies, it will be important to measure IBHP and IBMP concentrations and VvOMTs activity concurrently during the growing season, across a range of growing sites, different varieties, or different viticultural practices, to determine if variations in IBMP accumulation are governed by differences in IBHP accumulation and/or the rates of IBHP methylation.

# 4. Conclusions and future research directions

The pathway of MPs biosynthesis begins with a branched amino acid, and the final step consists of the methylation of HPs to form MPs. However, the initial cyclization step has not been confirmed in plants, and the metabolic intermediates between the initial branched amino acid and the final conversion to HPs are still unknown, as are the key enzymes catalyzing each step. Therefore, there is still much learn about MPs biosynthesis.

Many previous studies on the biosynthetic route of plant secondary products used radioactive isotope tracer techniques. Such methods were used to elucidate the biosynthetic pathways of taxol, morphine, and artemisinin. However, radioactive isotopes are harmful to researchers and cause environmental pollution. After 1960, improvements in stable isotope technology resulted in its widespread use to elucidate metabolic pathways. Stable isotope tracer technology has gradually replaced radioactive tracer technology because it is safer for researchers and the environment.

Isotopic tracers have been used to understand the biochemistry of plants and animals. Such tracers are applied and then incorporated into endogenous compounds in vivo, and can be used to track the dynamic changes of certain substances by detecting the abundance and types of molecules containing the tracer at different time points. One principle of this technique is that the isotope of an atom, or its labelled compound, has the same chemical properties as the endogenous compound, but different physical properties. The organism has no selectivity for the isotope tracer in biochemical reactions, and so the labelled atom can be tracked as it is incorporated into various products that are transported and metabolized in the plant.

There are various means to incorporate tracers into biological systems, including through the root system, injection, as a gas or vapour, and through incisions into the stem or petiole. When krisa, Larronde, and Budzinske (1999a, 1999b) added $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C - L }$ -phenylalanine to grape suspension cell cultures, up to $6 6 \%$ of the $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -phenylalanine was incorporated into resveratrol glucoside, indicating that phenylalanine is a precursor of resveratrol glucoside. In the same year, $5 0 \% { - } 5 7 \%$ of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - }$ phenylalanine was incorporated into anthocyanins, indicating that phenylalanine is also a precursor of anthocyanins. Hayasaka, Baldock, Pardon, Jeffery, and Herderich (2010) used a new stable isotope

labelling method, i.e. soaking berries or leaves in a solution of a stable isotopic tracer. Using this technique, the labelled tracer is incorporated into the vine in situ. Compared with the cell culture system, this grapevine system has the potential to show sites of biosynthesis and metabolism more accurately. The simplicity and efficiency of this method makes it suitable for analyzing many metabolic pathways with polar precursors. Therefore, it provides a powerful tool for exploring specific systems. Researchers, using this method to trace the biosynthesis and degradation of phenolics in grape, have made remarkable progress in recent years (Chassy, Adams, Laurie, & Waterhouse, 2012; Chassy, Adams, & Waterhouse, 2014; Chassy et al., 2015).

The stable isotope tracer technique may be a good choice to study MPs biosynthetic pathways in grape. It can be combined with sensitive and precise gas chromatography coupled to high resolution mass spectrometry (GC-HRMS) measurements to complement genomic and related technologies. Further investigations and experiments with labelled precursors will be useful to fully elucidate the biosynthetic route of this very important class of aroma compounds.
