# Abstract

Celastrol, a quinone‐methide triterpenoid, was extracted from Tripterygium wilfordii Hook. F. in 1936 for the first time. Almost 70 years later, it is considered one of the molecules most likely to be developed into modern drugs, as it exhibits notable bioactivity, including anticancer and anti‐inflammatory activity, and exerts antiobesity effects. In addition, the molecular mechanisms underlying its bioactivity are being widely studied, which offers new avenues for its development as a pharmaceutical reagent. Owing to its potential therapeutic effects and unique chemical structure, celastrol has attracted considerable interest in the fields of organic, biosynthesis, and medicinal chemistry. As several steps in the biosynthesis of celastrol have been revealed, the mechanisms of key enzymes catalyzing the formation and postmodifications of the celastrol scaffold have been gradually elucidated, which lays a good foundation for the future heterogeneous biosynthesis of celastrol. Chemical synthesis is also an effective approach to obtain celastrol. The total synthesis of celastrol was realized for the first time in 2015, which established a new strategy to obtain celastroid natural products. However, owing to the toxic effects and suboptimal pharmacological properties of

celastrol, its clinical applications remain limited. To search for drug‐like derivatives, several structurally modified compounds were synthesized and tested. This review focuses primarily on the latest research progress in the biosynthesis, total synthesis, structural modifications, bioactivity, and mechanism of action of celastrol. We anticipate that this paper will facilitate a more comprehensive understanding of this promising compound and provide constructive references for future research in this field.

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

The bioactive triterpenoid, celastrol, was first extracted from the plant Tripterygium wilfordii Hook. F. (TWHF, family Celastraceae) in 1936. As a traditional Chinese medicinal herb, TWHF has a long history of being used for the treatment of diseases such as arthritis.1 The wild populations of this herb are primarily distributed in eastern and southern China, Korea, and Japan.2 The medicinal usage of TWHF was first recorded in Dian Nan Ben Cao in 1476.3 In the 1960s, TWHF was successfully used for treating patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and inflammatory lesions caused by leprosy.4 The therapeutic effect of TWHF has inspired studies on its active ingredients; nearly, 400 chemical compounds have been isolated and verified, among which terpenoids are considered as the major bioactive constituents of TWHF. Celastrol is a major triterpenoid from this plant; it exerts anticancer5 and anti‐inflammatory,6 as well as antidiabetic7 and antiobesity effects.8 Celastrol is also regarded as a unique chemical ingredient with the quinone‐methide motiety and is present in plants of the families Celastraceae and Hippocrateaceae,2 distinguishing these plants from those of other families. Celastrol accumulates primarily in the roots.9–11

The therapeutic and biological effects of celastrol, along with its unique chemical structure, have attracted the interest of many biologists and chemists. Natural products with excellent therapeutic effects usually have broad prospects for clinical application. However, with the expansion of market demand, wild medicinal plant resources have been decreasing sharply. Moreover, the long production cycles, low extraction efficiency, and high energy consumption involved in their manufacture are formidable challenges in supplying the clinical demand via traditional agricultural production, prompting the formation of a new alternative production model. The biotechnological production of natural products through synthetic biology offers unique advantages, including high yields, supply flexibility, low cost, and sustainability.12 For example, the complete biosynthetic route of the artemisinic acid was successfully determined, and it was reconstituted in yeast, with a yield of $2 5 8 / \mathsf { L }$ via optimized fermentations.13 Opioids are essential medicines for treating severe pain, and researchers engineered yeast to achieve complete biosynthesis of thebaine and hydrocodone.14 Ginsenoside compound K, an active component of Panax ginseng, was successfully produced by microbial fermentation.15 Wang et al.16 systematically optimized yeast chassis cells, and the production of ginsenoside Rh2 reached $2 . 2 5 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ through fed‐batch fermentation. In addition, other natural products such as mogroside V,17 breviscapine,18 and cannabinoids19 were also completely synthesized using highly efficient microbial cell factories. The success of heterologous production of these chemical compounds revealed the

great development potential of synthetic biology and facilitated research on the biosynthesis of celastrol. Researchers have made significant efforts to explore key enzymes in its biosynthesis pathway and characterized sequential synthetic steps catalyzed by nearly 20 different functional enzymes, especially including 2,3‐ oxidosqualene cyclases20 and cytochrome P450s.21 Hansen et al.22 constructed the yeast chassis cells that can produce the celastrol precursor polpunonic acid with a yield of $\pm . 4 \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ . Research is getting closer to the complete biosynthesis of celastrol. However, enzymes that catalyze the downstream steps of celastrol biosynthesis still need further exploration.

In addition, chemical synthesis is also an effective approach to obtain celastrol, and it is necessary to explore the synthetic route and further understand the chemical reaction mechanisms. Ireland et al.23,24 realized the chemical synthesis of friedelin and alnusenone, the compounds closest to celastrol. However, total synthesis of celastrol has always been challenging for chemists owning to a lack of inspiration from biosynthesis. The total synthesis of this molecule was realized in 2015 for the first time.25 However, because of the toxic effects,26 poor water solubility,27 and low oral bioavailability28 of celastrol, its clinical applications are limited, even if it exhibits superior bioactive effects. Therefore, multiple structurally modified celastrol derivatives were synthesized to improve its properties. Studies have revealed that the carboxylic group located at the C‐20 position of celastrol might be crucial for chaperone activity,29 while its quinone‐methide moiety could act a pivotal part in its proapoptotic effect.30

Since celastrol exhibits a diversity of pharmacological effects including anticancer, antidiabetes, and antiobesity effects, it has the potential to be developed as a therapeutic agent for addressing important global public health problems. To accelerate the progress toward clinical translation, scholars have been focused on the specific molecular mechanism of celastrol in addition to the relevant therapeutic targets. Heat shock protein 90 (Hsp90), leptin, nuclear factor‐κB (NF‐κB), and reactive oxygen species (ROS) were identified as molecular targets of celastrol, which act in various signaling pathways.8,31–33 These studies will provide more possibilities for drug development and disease treatment.

In this review, we focused on the past achievements and future perspectives in the biosynthesis, total synthesis, structural modifications, and bioactivity of the natural product celastrol.

# 2 | BIOSYNTHESIS

The critical first step for achieving the complete heterologous biosynthesis of natural products is to elucidate their biosynthetic pathways. Triterpenes are among the most diverse secondary metabolites in nature; however, they are practically all biosynthesized through the mevalonate (MVA) pathway in the cytoplasm.34 Pina et al.,35 using the $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ ‐labeled method, have demonstrated that quinone‐methide triterpenoids are synthesized by the MVA pathway. In addition, sesquiterpenes and sterols are also produced via the MVA pathway. Similarly, the 2‐C‐methyl‐D‐ erythritol 4‐phosphate (MEP) pathway is primarily responsible for the synthesis of monoterpenes and diterpenes, as well as tetraterpenes in plastids.36 It appears that the two pathways separately regulate the biosynthesis of different products; however, there exists a crosstalk between them. Using $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ labeling experiments, it was determined that isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) separately derived from these two pathways can be exchanged, following which they can participate in the subsequent synthesis steps.37

The initial step of the MVA pathway is the condensation of the acetyl‐coenzyme A (CoA) units under the catalysis of acetyl‐CoA C‐acetyltransferase (AACT) to produce acetoacetyl‐CoA (Figure 1). Zhao et al.38 successfully cloned and characterized the gene encoding AACT from suspension cells of T. wilfordii. Subsequently, 3‐ hydroxy‐3‐methylglutaryl‐CoA (HMG‐CoA) synthase converts acetoacetyl‐CoA to HMG‐CoA, which is regarded as the second step.39 HMG‐CoA is subsequently catalyzed into MVA by the NADPH‐dependent HMG‐CoA reductase.40 Next, MVA is metabolized into IPP, the C5‐isoprenoid unit, by a series of enzymes.41 IPP can be reversibly catalyzed into dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP) by IPP isomerase.42 IPP and DMAPP are considered substrates common to the two pathways.43

In contrast, D‐glyceraldehyde‐3‐phosphate (GA‐3‐P) and pyruvate are the important substrates in the MEP pathway.44 The first two rate‐limiting enzymes in this pathway are 1‐deoxy‐D‐xylulose‐5‐phosphate synthase (DXS) and 1‐deoxy‐d‐xylulose‐5‐phosphate reductoisomerase (DXR). GA‐3‐P and pyruvate could be converted into MEP through catalysis by DXS and DXR, sequentially.45,46 In 2015, genes encoding TwDXS and TwDXR were cloned from T. wilfordii, and they were all highly expressed in the roots.47 Then, MEP is converted into 4‐diphosphocytidyl‐2‐C‐methyl‐ D‐erythritol (CDP‐ME) through the action of the CTP‐dependent 2‐C‐methyl‐D‐erythritol 4‐phosphate cytidylyltransferase (MCT).48,49 Subsequently, 4‐(cytidine 5ʹ‐diphospho)‐2‐C‐methyl‐D‐erythritol kinase (CMK), a unique kinase of the MEP pathway, catalyzes CDP‐ME into 4‐diphosphocytidyl‐2‐C‐methyl‐D‐erythritol‐2‐phosphate (CDP‐ME2P).50,51 Next, CDP‐ME2P is converted to IPP and DMAPP through sequential catalysis by different enzymes52,53 (Figure 1).

Isopentenyl diphosphate isomerase catalyzed the interconversion of IPP and DMAPP, which is a rate‐limiting step and is associated with protonation/deprotonation reactions.54,55 As shown in Scheme 1, DMAPP and 1–3 molecules of IPP can respectively be condensed into C‐10 units of geranyl diphosphate (GPP), C‐15 units of farnesyl diphosphate (FPP), and C‐20 units of geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP; C20) by different short‐chain prenyltransferases.56 GPP and GGPP are precursors of monoterpenoids and diterpenoids, respectively.57 The farnesyl pyrophosphate synthase catalyzes DMAPP and IPP to form FPP, which is a key precursor of triterpenoids.58–6 0 Squalene synthase (SQS) plays an essential role in isoprenoid biosynthesis since it converts FPP into squalene.61 The next enzyme, squalene epoxidase (SQE), which is also known as squalene monooxygenase, converts squalene into 2,3‐oxidosqualene with the participation of NADPH‐cytochrome P450 reductase.62 Zhou et al. cloned five SQE genes (TwSEs1‐5) from T. wilfordii, and their functions were identified via complementation in an erg1 mutant yeast constructed using CRISPR/Cas9.63

Cyclization of 2,3‐oxidosqualene into polycyclic alcohols or ketones $( \mathsf { C } _ { 3 0 } \mathsf { H } _ { 5 0 } \mathsf { O } )$ is the initial committed step of triterpenoid biosynthesis.64 It is a complicated process conducted by a single oxidosqualene cyclase (OSC), involving multiple rearrangement steps that form triterpenoids with different skeletons (Scheme 2). Nearly 200 triterpenoid scaffolds produced by this reaction have been reported to date; prominent examples are including $\beta$ ‐amyrin, lupeol, germanicol, and friedelin. Pentacyclic triterpene skeletons are cyclized in the chair–chair–chair conformation via the formation of a dammarenyl cation, whereas the protosteryl cation derived from the chair–boat–chair conformation is an important intermediate of sterols.65 Therefore, the cyclization process of 2,3‐oxidosqualene to form triterpenoids or sterols catalyzed by OSCs is regarded as a branch point of the biosynthetic pathway.66 The cyclization process is primarily divided into four steps: (i) the initial protonation of the substrate, (ii) cyclization via a polyene addition

cascade, (iii) rearrangement process via 1,2‐hydride shifts or methyl shifts, and (iv) deprotonation to yield diverse products in the last step.67 OSCs exhibit product specificity, which may depend on the numbers of high‐energy intermediates stabilized by the enzyme.68 The carbocation shifts from the E ring to the A ring during the rearrangement process, and friedelin is the most rearranged compound among pentacyclic triterpenes.64 It has been speculated that friedelin is first synthesized in the leaves and then transported to the root bark for the production of quinone‐methide triterpenoids.2 Zhou et al.20 confirmed that friedelin is the precursor of celastrol via precursor feeding experiments. Although there have been several studies on OSCs, the crystal structures of the enzymes, their product specificity, and the reaction mechanisms still need further exploration.

In the downstream biosynthetic pathway of celastrol (Figure 1), friedelin is an important precursor and forms the scaffold of quinone‐methide triterpenoids.20 The subsequent steps are primarily oxidative modifications at different positions of the terpenoid skeletons by different cytochrome P450 enzymes (P450s) with diverse functions. Scholars have been screening P450s in the biosynthetic pathway of celastrol; however, the vast number and unknown functions of P450s have rendered this study highly challenging. Bicalho et al.21 reported the P450 CYP712K4, which could catalyze the C‐29 oxidation of friedelin to form polpunonic acid. It is a three‐step oxidation process that proceeds via 29‐hydroxyfriedelin, 3‐oxo‐friedelan‐29‐al, and polpunonic acid. This valuable study

deepened the understanding of P450s and prompted further studies on screening and functional verification of enzymes involved in the following steps. Later, Hansen et al.22 reported another three P450s (CYP712K1–3) from the roots of T. wilfordii; their functions are identical to that of CYP712K4, as described previously. Subsequently, they reconstructed the biosynthetic pathway in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and achieved a yield of $\pm . 4 \ : \mathrm { m g / L }$ polpunonic acid. The construction of chassis cells for the production of polpunonic acid will lay the foundation for the heterologous biosynthesis of celastrol. However, more engineering strategies should be applied to construct high‐ yield strains producing polpunonic acid to provide effective chassis cells with sufficient celastrol precursors for future research. As several P450s that can oxidize the specific sites of other pentacyclic triterpene skeletons have been characterized,69,70 the catalytic specificity of P450s has gradually been elucidated; this data will provide references for the screening of P450 enzymes related to celastrol biosynthesis.

Nevertheless, enzymes in the following biosynthetic pathway still need further investigation. Bicalho et al.21 proposed that the oxidative modifications at the C‐2 of polpunonic acid and $C { - } 5 \beta$ demethylation are important process for the formation of certain groups or structures of celastrol. It is speculated that C‐2 hydroxylation of polpunonic acid is the next catalytic step,20 which could result in wilforic acid C, a metabolite present in the root bark of T. wilfordii.71 Subsequently, a series of intermediates in the biosynthetic pathway of celastrol could probably be produced via catalytic reactions such as oxidation, rearrangement, demethylation, and introduction of double bonds under the action of different P450s. Finally, celastrol could be synthesized through the formation of the quinone‐ methide moiety via multiple steps. Moreover, celastrol might be oxidized into another quinone‐methide triterpenoid, maytenin, and it could be converted to $2 2 \beta$ ‐hydroxy‐maytenin via hydroxylation at C‐22 by further P450s.35

The search for P450s in the downstream biosynthetic pathway of celastrol will remain the focus of future studies. An effective approach is to select candidate genes of interest through homologous BLAST with P450s of known functions, as well as to combine the analysis of genomes, transcriptomes, proteomes, and induced expression profiles. It is additionally useful to mine P450s from related families such as CYP716, CYP71, CYP72, and CYP712, because several P450s that impart structural diversity to triterpenoids have been reported in these families.70,72 However, the catalytic efficiency of wild‐type (WT) P450s is generally low when expressed in heterologous strains, which will cause difficulties in the functional verifications and subsequent research. Different approaches for the engineering of P450 enzymes such as directed evolution and improvement of enzyme‐host compatibility are essential for improving the catalytic capabilities of P450s and enhancing the heterologous biosynthesis of plant‐derived products.73 It is also a promising field to control the chemo‐ and regioselectivity of P450s and change the substrate accessibility to synthesize the desired natural products.74

# 3 | CHEMICAL SYNTHESIS AND STRUCTURAL MODIFICATIONS

# 3.1 | Total synthesis of celastrol

Celastrol belongs to the friedelin family and contains a quinone‐methide moiety. Because of its unique structure and broad pharmacological activity, celastrol has attracted the attention of numerous chemists. Scholars have successfully explored the synthetic routes of various triterpenoids such as lupeol, germanicol, oleanolic acid, and $\beta$ ‐ amyrin by mimicking their biosynthesis. Nevertheless, unlike the biosynthesis of lupeol, the biological polyene cyclization, which generates friedelin, requires a number of complicated energetically unfavorable methyl and hydride transfers; this renders it difficult to synthesize celastroid natural products in the laboratory in analogy to their biosynthesis. Researchers developed polyene cyclization to efficiently synthesize lupeol, germanicol, $\beta$ ‐amyrin, and other related compounds. Ireland et al.23,24 completed the total synthesis of $( \pm )$ ‐alnusenone and $( \pm )$ ‐friedelin, which are the closest natural products to celastrol, using the annulation strategies shown in Scheme 3. The formal synthesis of alnusenone from the enone 1 and ethoxytetralone 2 proceeds in 21 linear steps. The formal synthesis of friedelin proceeds in an analogous fashion in 31 steps. Inspired by Ireland's polyene cyclization approach for the synthesis $( \pm )$ ‐alnusenone, Camelio et al.25 developed a novel strategy to synthesize celastrol and its analogs using a polyolefin cascade. They achieved the total synthesis of celastrol and pristimerin from 2,3‐dimethyl butadiene (Scheme 4).25 First, the key intermediate 12 was synthesized for subsequent generation of the pentacyclic triterpenoid skeleton. Selective hydroboration followed by oxidation with buffered Dess–Martin periodinane was used to obtain Compound 11. This was followed by Stork‐enamine Robinson annulation and ${ \mathsf { L i A l H } } _ { 4 }$ reduction, which generated the allylic alcohol 12 with $8 3 \%$ yield. Polyene cyclization of allylic alcohol 12 yielded the pentacyclic compound 13. Notably, the addition of ferric chloride as a catalyst in the $C H _ { 2 } C l _ { 2 }$ dilute solution resulted in the formation of the pentacyclic compound 13 with a yield of $3 8 \%$ on a Gram scale. After the pentacyclic precursor has been obtained, the subsequent key steps involve the formation of the carboxylic and methyl groups in the E ring, and later, the quinone group in the A ring. The carboxylic and methyl groups were introduced into the E ring of Compound 13 in six reaction steps with a $3 8 \%$ yield to afford the methyl ester 16. There are two possible synthesis routes to obtain celastrol from 16. The initial conjectural route proceeds through wilforic acid A (Scheme 5). The authors first surmised that wilforic acid A can be obtained through a double oxidation process, and then transformed via tautomerization to produce celastrol. Wilforic acid A can be continuously oxidized to form Compound 20; however, 20 cannot be further converted to celastrol. They hypothesized that the Δ8,9 alkene must be installed before oxidation. To install the $\Delta 8 { , } 9$ alkene group, Compound 16 was oxidized first using the Jones reagent, followed by selenoxide elimination, saponification, and deprotection to provide wilforol A. Wilforol A was reduced by benzylation, $\mathsf { L i A I H } _ { 4 }$ reduction, ionic reduction, and subsequent hydrogenation and oxidization to afford celastrol. Celastrol can be transformed into pristimerin via one‐step esterification.

In summary, a platform using the novel polyene cyclization for the production of celastrol was developed by Camelio et al.,25 which opened the door to the chemical synthesis of celastrol and its analogs. However, this synthesis route remains long, requiring 31 steps, and the yield cannot meet the industrial needs. The synthesis and semisynthesis of celastrol need to be further researched to develop a more optimized method to obtain celastrol.

# 4 | BIOACTIVITY AND MECHANISMS OF ACTION OF CELASTROL

Celastrol has great possibility to be developed into new preparations for the clinical treatment of cancer, inflammatory diseases, obesity, neurodegenerative diseases, diabetes, and related disorders, because it exhibits a wide range of bioactivity.8,101–103 As shown in Figure 3, many studies have identified its molecular targets and signaling pathways, for instance, NF‐κB, ROS, and leptin, which contribute to further understanding of the molecular mechanisms of the bioactivity of celastrol.

# 4.1 | Anticancer activity

Celastrol shows a wide range of anticancer activity, including those in breast cancer,104 retinoblastoma,105 lung cancer,106 gastric cancer,107 and melanoma.30 Multiple pharmacological mechanisms underlying the anticancer activity of celastrol have been reported to date, such as inhibition of Hsp90,31 suppression of angiogenesis,108 induction of apoptosis,109 and impairment of DNA damage repair110 (Table 1). However, owing to the complexity of cancers and the diversity of underlying signaling pathways, further effort is needed to further elucidate the anticancer mechanism of celastrol.

# 4.1.1 | Hsp90‐related effects

Hsp90 is regarded as a prospective target for many oncogenic proteins.83 Celastrol was demonstrated to act as an Hsp90 inhibitor that could interfere with the Hsp90–Cdc37 interaction, exhibiting antitumor activity in pancreatic cancer cells.111 It is reported that celastrol can induce the degradation of Bcr–Abl, an Hsp90 client, and restrain the cell growth of chronic myelogenous leukemia despite the T315I‐mutation.112 Furthermore, celastrol can induce cell‐cycle arrest at the G0/G1 phase, which is a novel effect based on thiol‐related Hsp90 inhibition.5,31 Later, Li et al.113 observed that celastrol is an inhibitor of mTOR, which could disturb its association with Hsp90 while conversely enhancing its interaction with Cdc37.

# 4.1.2 | Suppression of angiogenesis, tumor invasion, and migration

# 4.1.4 | Proteasome‐related effects

Celastrol was first reported to predominantly inhibit the 26S proteasome in prostate cancer cells and significantly inhibit tumor growth.114 Wang et al. demonstrated that celastrol might inhibit DNA repair in lung cancer cells through inducing the depletion of Fanconi anemia group D2 (FANCD2) by the proteasome.143 Moreover, celastrol was also reported to exert proteasome‐inhibitory effects in human multiple myeloma (MM) cells, which provides the possibility to develop new anti‐MM drugs.115

# 4.1.5 | Impairment of DNA damage repair

Celastrol was reported to inhibit the DNA double‐strand break repair process in case of damage induced by ionizing radiation, so that tumor growth could be inhibited.116 Degradation of FANCD2 following by celastrol treatment increased the sensitivity of pediatric high‐grade gliomas (pHGG) to DNA damage, thus indicating the potential therapeutic effects of celastrol in pHGG.118 Moreira et al.110 observed that celastrol could induce DNA damage in colon cancer cells through ROS‐related mechanisms.

# 4.1.6 | Effect on tumor‐related transcription factors

Celastrol was reported to decrease the expression of estrogen receptor α, thereby inhibiting the growth of tumor cells.144 Inhibition of the transcription factors NF $\cdot \kappa \mathsf { B }$ and signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) by celastrol could effectively inhibit the proliferation of cancer cells.145 Sha et al.32 further explored the mechanism of celastrol‐induced apoptosis through the transcription factor NF $\cdot \kappa \mathsf { B }$ and concluded that celastrol inhibits its activity by upregulating the expression of miR‐146a. Rajendran et al.123 subsequently observed that STAT3 activation could be suppressed by celastrol, thereby limiting HCC cell growth and inducing apoptosis. Moreover, it was demonstrated that downregulation of transcription factor E2F1 by celastrol could limit the growth of HepG2 cells.125

# 4.1.7 | Other effects

The chemokine receptor CXCR4 is closely associated with tumor metastasis and celastrol was observed to hinder the invasion of tumor cells by downregulating CXCR4 expression.146 Celastrol was found to act as a regulator of G protein signaling 17 (RGS17) inhibitor in prostate and lung cancer cells, leading to cell growth restriction.147 Furthermore, celastrol‐induced upregulation of miR‑33a‑5p resulted in suppression of mTOR signaling, consequently, increasing the sensitivity of lung adenocarcinoma cells to celastrol.126

Inflammatory bowel disease is a nonspecific chronic inflammatory bowel disease involving the ileum, rectum, and colon; it primarily comprises Crohn's disease (CD) and UC. Celastrol also showed anti‐inflammatory actions in mouse models of systemic sclerosis, atherosclerosis, and spinal cord injury. Systemic sclerosis is a connective tissue disease with high mortality that is characterized by vasculitis and fibrosis of the skin and internal organs. Alleviation of skin fibrosis in rats lacking cannabinoid receptor 1 (CB1) and deterioration in CB2‐deficient rats implied a distinct relationship between cannabinoid receptors and fibrosis.170,171 Jiang et al.157 screened CB2 agonists from a library of 366 natural products and discovered that celastrol can be selectively activated. Subsequent studies showed that celastrol reduces inflammation and protects against dermal fibrosis by selectively activating the CB2 signaling pathway.157 Celastrol additionally alleviated renal fibrosis by upregulating CB2.158 Recently, celastrol was found to exert an inhibitory effect against liver fibrosis‐induced inflammation by regulating the adenosine 5ʹ‐monophosphate‐activated protein kinase (AMPK)–SIRT3 signaling pathway. Notably, AMPK was found to regulate SIRT3 rather than SIRT3 activating AMPK.159 Atherosclerosis is a chronic disease and a major cause of coronary heart disease, cerebral infarction, and peripheral vascular disease. Allen et al.6 prepared celastrol micelles using poly (ethylene glycol)‐b‐poly (propylene sulfide) as a carrier. In vivo assays showed that this nanocarrier decreases the number of immune cells within atherosclerotic plaques in a mouse model of atherosclerosis, which demonstrated its potential therapeutic effects on atherosclerosis.6

The NF‐κB and JNK signaling pathways are upregulated in C3A hepatocytes induced by palmitate, which promotes the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines and triggers inflammation. Celastrol reduced inflammation and mitochondrial destruction in palmitate‐induced insulin resistance by inhibiting NF ${ \boldsymbol { \cdot } } { \boldsymbol { \kappa } } { \boldsymbol { \mathsf { B } } }$ and JNK activation and enhancing insulin signaling activity.172 Dai et al.160 reported that celastrol $( 1 \mathrm { m g / k g ) }$ relieved inflammation in a mouse model of spinal cord injury by suppressing microglial pyroptosis.

Additionally, celastrol showed a regulatory effect on inflammation caused by multiple factors, such as transactivator of transcription (HIV‐1 Tat), LPS, and gamma irradiation.173–175 These effects are primarily the result of the regulation of various inflammatory mediators and inhibition of the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines (CXCL10, MCP‐1, TNF $\cdot \alpha$ , and IL‐1β). Kim et al. confirmed that celastrol alleviated LPS‐induced inflammation by decreasing the production of nitric oxide, prostaglandin E2, TNF $\cdot \alpha$ , and IL‐6 by inhibiting the activation of NF‐κB and AP‐1. Xin et al.176 discovered that suppression of the NLRP3 inflammasome and the resulting decrease in IL $\mathtt { 1 \beta }$ secretion also act a part in the treatment of inflammation. Intriguingly, a recent study revealed that celastrol exacerbated organ injury and LPS‐induced inflammation in mice.177 Since studies on the therapeutic effects of

celastrol in LPS‐induced inflammation have focused on in vitro models, the authors argue that the effects of celastrol on LPS‐induced inflammation may be different in vivo and in vitro. However, the reasons for this difference remain unknown. Further experiments can be performed to identify the molecular mechanisms by which celastrol exhibits its therapeutic effect against LPS‐induced inflammation in vivo.

The arachidonic acid (AA) pathway plays a role in inflammation, causing the production of proinflammatory cytokines. Secretory phospholipase A2 group IIA (sPLA2IIA), 5‐lipoxygenase (5‐LOX), and cyclooxygenase‐2 (COX‐2) enzymes participated in the metabolism of AA; they are closely associated with the formation, development, and recession of inflammation. Joshi et al.178 first discovered that celastrol modulates the AA pathway to relieve inflammation. Multiple studies demonstrated that celastrol is a multitarget inhibitor, suppressing the function of sPLA2IIA, 5‐LOX, and COX‐2.178 In addition to acting on inflammatory mediators and cytokines, celastrol promotes the interaction between Nur77 and TRAF2 as well as induces mitochondrial ubiquitination and autophagy to exert its anti‐inflammatory effects.93

# 4.3 | Antiobesity effects

As an NF ${ \boldsymbol { \cdot } } { \boldsymbol { \kappa } } { \boldsymbol { \mathsf { B } } }$ inhibitor, celastrol was reported to exert an effect on obesity in diabetic db/db mice; it decreased the body weight via reduction in food intake.179 Wang et al.180 confirmed that celastrol could induce weight loss by weakening high‐fat emulsion‐mediated oxidative stress via increasing the activity of antioxidant enzymes, while suppressing NADPH oxidase activity.

In 2015, researchers clarified another important mechanism by which celastrol can be used to treat obesity, closely related to the enhancement of leptin sensitivity, leading to the inhibition of food intake and obvious weight loss in hyperleptinemic diet‐induced obese mice instead of leptin‐deficient $( \mathsf { L e p } ^ { \mathsf { o b } } )$ or leptin receptor‐deficient (Lepdb) mice.8 Subsequently, Kyriakou et al.181 demonstrated that protein tyrosine phosphatase (PTP) 1B and T‐cell PTP are negative regulators that are essential for the leptin signaling pathway.181 Pfuhlmann et al.101 further observed that the weight‐reducing effect of celastrol is caused by hypophagia, associated with age, and regulated by functional leptin signaling. Through in‐depth research on the molecular mechanisms of leptin sensitization, interleukin‐1 receptor 1 was identified as a gatekeeper responsible for mediating the metabolic effects of celastrol.182

Furthermore, celastrol was found to upregulate heat shock factor 1 (HSF1) and peroxisome proliferator‐ activated receptor‐γ (PPARγ) coactivator‐1α (PGC‐1α), thereby protecting mice against obesity and metabolic dysfunction; however, it was inefficient in HSF1 knockout mice.183 Celastrol can facilitate the treatment of obesity by decreasing galanin‐induced food intake and activating the expression of P1GC‐1α along with glucose transporter 4 to accelerate glucose consumption.184 Moreover, it is reported that celastrol interferes with the differentiation of adipocytes and enhances lipolysis in 3T3‐L1 adipocytes, thus controlling obesity through the PPARγ2 and CCAAT/ enhancer‐binding protein alpha signaling pathways.185 From another molecular perspective, celastrol acts a pivotal part in lipid metabolism by controlling genes including Lpcat1, Smpd3, Pld1, and Sptc2, which are related to lipid synthesis and catabolism.186 Since celastrol can inhibit lipid synthesis, it effectively alleviates nonalcoholic fatty liver disease in C57BL/6J (WT) male mice via Sirt1.187 Through quantification studies, the central nervous system was speculated to be the target site for the antiobesity effects of celastrol.188

# 4.4 | Therapeutic effect on neurodegenerative diseases

Alzheimer's disease (AD) and Parkinson's disease (PD) are common neurodegenerative diseases in the elderly population.189,190 There is no effective treatment for these neurodegenerative diseases, prompting many scientists to explore drugs to treat AD and PD. AD is a major cause of dementia, which triggers severe memory and cognitive

impairment in the patient.189 The nerve cells are damaged over time, eventually leading to loss of the motor function. The accumulations of beta amyloid (Aβ) and tau protein is believed to be highly relevant to AD.190 Increasing amounts of $\mathsf { A } \mathsf { \beta }$ and tau proteins induce inflammation, neuronal damage, and subsequent atrophy of the brain. Paris et al.103 observed a significant reduction in $\mathsf { A } \mathsf { \beta }$ plaques in a rat AD model expressing human APP695sw and presenilin‐1 M146L mutations overexpressing. Zhao et al.191 demonstrated that celastrol decreases Aβ production induced by LPS in H4 human neuroglioma cells as well as increases the cell viability to a certain extent. The underlying mechanisms are relevant to the increased expression of Hsp70 and Bcl‐2 as well as the suppression of NF $\cdot \kappa \mathsf { B }$ , phosphorylated glycogen synthase kinase $3 \beta$ , and COX‐2 activation.191 In addition, celastrol treatment $( 7 \mu \mathrm { g } / \ k \mathrm { g } )$ resulted in an enhancement of psychomotor memory and learning in a mouse model of AD.192

Studies on the treatment of PD are ongoing. Although its pathogenesis is not yet entirely clear, there is evidence that autophagy, $\alpha \cdot$ ‐synuclein accumulation, oxidative stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction are contributing factors. Autophagy is a cellular self‐digestive process that leads to the recycling of cellular components, which is vital for cell survival and homeostasis. The exact mechanism underlying autophagy regulation in PD is unclear; however, increasing evidence indicates that autophagy is downregulated in PD, suggesting a correlation with disease progression. Investigators observed that celastrol reduced rotenone‐induced damage in human neuroblastoma SH‐SY5Y cells by activating autophagic pathways. In addition, $\alpha$ ‐synuclein levels and ROS production were decreased following celastrol treatment.193 Recently, Lin et al.194 discovered that celastrol suppressed MPP+‐induced neurotoxicity via autophagy and mitophagy in SH‐SY cells. They further proved that celastrol improves motor function and prevents degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in a rat model of PD.194

HSPs regulate the proper folding of proteins and maintain protein function. Misfolding and accumulation of proteins are major features of neurodegenerative diseases; HSPs may prevent intracellular or extracellular proteins from aggregating in and inhibit disease progression.195 Research has shown that celastrol induces Hsp70, Hsp27, and Hsp32 in cerebral cortical cultures.196 Considering the antioxidant and anti‐inflammatory properties, autophagy mediation and neuroprotective HSP‐induction effects of celastrol, its potential for the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases should be studied further.

# 4.5 | Antidiabetes effects

Type 2 diabetes and obesity are considered two major challenges facing global public health,197 and diabetes‐ related mechanisms still need to be elucidated. It has been demonstrated that type 2 diabetes is related to obesity, and celastrol could act as a proteasome inhibitor, which is capable of reducing insulin resistance and strengthening $\beta$ ‐cell function.198 NF‐κB activation in the adipose tissue is regarded as an important element of insulin resistance.199 Zhang et al.200 have demonstrated that celastrol exerts a protective effect on the kidneys of diabetic rats through the MAPK/NF‐κB pathway. Oxidative stress is another important pathogenic factor of diabetes, and celastrol exhibited antioxidant activity in diabetic rats via the AMPK–PGC1α–Sirt3 pathway.201 In addition, researchers found that celastrol could increase glucose uptake via the PI3K/Akt pathway and improve mitochondrial function as well as enhance insulin signaling in antimycin A‐induced cells.202 Han et al.7 reported that celastrol could also downregulate the Toll‐like receptor 4, myeloid differentiation factor 88, and suppress the NF‐κB signaling pathway, demonstrating potential protective effects against diabetic liver injury. These findings suggest that celastrol could potentially be developed into new drugs for treating type 2 diabetes.

# 4.6 | Antiviral effects

According to current research, celastrol also has antiviral properties, which could indicate new approaches for the development of antiviral drugs. The immune system of patients infected with HIV is compromised, seriously

threatening their health and survival. Several anti‐HIV drugs that are used in clinical treatment exhibit serious toxicity and side effects.203 Notably, celastrol was reported to exhibit inhibitory effects against the Tat, a viral transactivating factor, thus suppressing HIV transcription and replication.204 This report implies that celastrol might be applied to new therapies for treating acquired immune deficiency syndrome. Moreover, celastrol can be potentially used for treatment of the severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) using celastrol, since it was reported to act as an inhibitor of the SARS coronavirus protein 3CLpro, which might be attributed to the A ring and E ring of celastrol.205 In addition, celastrol was observed to suppress dengue virus replication by inducing IFN‐ α expression along with the activation of downstream JAK–STAT signaling.206 Tseng et al.207 determined the antiviral activity of celastrol against hepatitis C virus (HCV) using human hepatoma cells, and found that celastrol could upregulate the expression of heme oxygenase 1, thereby hindering HCV replication. These results demonstrate the huge potential of celastrol in antiviral therapy; however, more comprehensive studies on its molecular mechanisms of action and pharmacological activity against other viruses are needed.

# 5 | CONCLUSION AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

Medicinal plants containing abundant active ingredients provide great resources for modern drug development. T. wilfordii contains multiple compounds with notable activity, among which celastrol has attracted the attention of numerous scholars. Nevertheless, there are multiple challenges in the translation of traditional medicinal ingredients into modern clinical practice, with bottlenecks appearing in the isolation, synthesis, identification of molecular mechanisms, and clinical trials.208 The lack of large amounts of celastrol present an obstacle in transforming celastrol into clinical therapeutics. Microbial cell factories, plant tissue culture, and total synthesis are effective strategies for obtaining target compounds,209–211 which provide alternative approaches to obtain celastrol while preserving plant resources. The discovery of genes participating in the biosynthesis of celastrol and the development of synthetic biological strategies indicate the possibility of celastrol biosynthesis in heterologous hosts.20,21,63 The production of precursors of celastrol, friedelin, and polpunonic acid was previously achieved in yeast, although with a low yield. Recently, the high‐quality genome of T. wilfordii has been published, and a network of interactions between genes and metabolites was established by combining the genome, transcriptomes, and metabolic profiles.212 This approach is encouraging because it provides a new strategy and resources for gene‐level studies of celastrol biosynthesis. These studies indicate the possibility of complete biosynthesis of celastrol in a heterologous host, which will correspondingly become the driving force for achieving its industrial production with enormous economic benefits. However, several key problems remain to be solved in the biosynthesis of celastrol, such as the demethylation of C5‐methyl and formation of the quinone‐methide moiety. Therefore, it is required to further mine the genes participating in the downstream biosynthesis pathway, especially those encoding cytochrome P450s. In addition, the reaction mechanisms of the enzymes need further study, because they are conducive to exploring new catalytic reactions and improving the efficiency of enzymes.

Total synthesis of celastrol has been achieved by Camelio et al.,25 paving the way to obtain celastroid natural products by chemical synthesis. However, the synthesis route is long, requiring 31 steps, and the yield is low. Consequently, reducing the number of reaction steps and improving the yield remains an urgent problem for researchers. It is necessary to find more suitable synthetic and semisynthetic methods to obtain celastrol. To identify compounds with good drug‐like properties, as well as to study the SAR, researchers modified the structure of celastrol and synthesized a series of analogs. Some of these derivatives showed higher activity and better properties, and they are worthy of further investigation. The SAR studies revealed certain rules that may help guide the rational design and development of drugs. First, the quinone‐methide moiety is important but not sufficient for anticancer activity, and the C‐3 hydroxyl group is also essential for the antiproliferative activity of celastrol. Second, the acidic proton present in celastrol is not required for most of its activity, and the C‐20 carboxylic group can be replaced by a hydrophilic group to improve water solubility and safety. Third, substitution of the C–C bond

by a C–S bond at the C‐6 position can also enhance the anticancer activity. In addition to structural modification, nanoparticle delivery systems can be used to improve the water solubility and reduce the toxic effects of celastrol. For instance, celastrol–albumin nanoparticles showed better targeting than free celastrol in a mouse model of mesangioproliferative glomerulonephritis. Similarly, celastrol‐loaded poly (ethylene glycol)‐b‐poly (propylene sulfide) nanocarriers suppressed NF‐κB signaling more effectively than free celastrol at low concentrations. 6,213 Finally, combining structural modifications with nanoparticle delivery systems may accelerate the translation of tripterine drugs to clinical use.

Celastrol shows extensive pharmacological activity, such as anticancer, anti‐inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects as well as functions in alleviating metabolic diseases. Its antitumor activity was confirmed in diversified cancer cell lines, and the underlying mechanisms involve the regulation of numerous molecular modulators, including the Hsp90, NF $\cdot \mathtt { x B }$ , VEGF/VEGFR‐2, PTEN/PI3K/Akt, ROS/JNK, and Akt/mTOR signaling pathways. By acting on these molecular targets, celastrol prevents angiogenesis, tumor invasion, and migration as well as induces autophagy and apoptosis. There is now a clearer understanding of the anti‐inflammatory effects and mechanisms of celastrol, such as the activation and inhibition of inflammation mediators, the interference between Nur77 and TRAF2, and regulation of calcium homeostasis. Additionally, investigators observed that celastrol can selectively activate the CB2. Furthermore, celastrol was reported to be combined with the target protein Nur77 through specific noncovalent interactions thus exerting corresponding pharmacological effects.214 For the past few years, the potent antiobesity effect of celastrol has attracted wide attention, and the induction of hypophagia was determined to be one of the mechanisms responsible for this effect. Apart from having well‐known bioactivity, celastrol exhibits antioxidant activity192 and cardioprotective effects.215 Recently, celastrol was first reported to suppress excessive angiotensin II‐induced cardiac dysfunction by binding and suppressing the STAT3.216

All these studies clearly indicate that celastrol can be developed as a candidate drug for the therapy of multiple human diseases, containing cancer, inflammation and obesity. However, further effort is required to fully elucidate the mechanisms underlying the effects of celastrol, and its therapeutic effects in other disease models are also worth investigating. Moreover, few studies have been conducted on the safety of celastrol in conjunction with other drugs or the activity of celastrol derivatives. Considering the uncertainty of in vivo experiments and the complexity of combination therapy, further research is needed in these areas. Furthermore, toxicity assessment of celastrol derivatives is also an essential step to ensure their safety before clinical trials.26 Therefore, toxicity reduction and clinical trials will be essential step in the development of celastrol for modern medicine.
