# ABSTRACT

2-(2-Phenylethyl)chromones (PECs) are the primary constituents of agarwood, a valuable aromatic resin widely used in traditional oriental medicine and incense production, exhibiting diverse pharmacological activities. While we have previously reported a polyketide synthase (AsPECPS) playing a crucial role in the biosynthesis of PECs in agarwood, the intrinsic regulatory mechanisms underlying PEC biosynthesis remain largely elusive. Here, we successfully characterised a new 1R-subtype myeloblastosis (MYB) transcription factor (AsMYB1) from Aquilaria sinensis whose expression is significantly induced under salt treatment. Further overexpression, RNA interference (RNAi) and gene-editing experiments confirmed the positive regulatory role of AsMYB1 in PEC biosynthesis. AsMYB1 specifically bound to cis-elements in the promoter of AsPECPS to enhance its transcription, thereby promoting PEC accumulation under salt stress. A 14-3-3 family protein designated AsGRF1 was characterised to interact with AsMYB1 both in vivo and in vitro, and knockdown or knockout of AsGRF1 led to a dramatic reduction in AsPECPS expression and PECs’ accumulation. Furthermore, AsGRF1 may positively facilitate AsMYB1-mediated activation of PEC production under salt stress by elevating AsPECPS expression through increasing the transcriptional activity, nuclear localisation and stability of AsMYB1. Our findings suggest that the AsGRF1-AsMYB1-AsPECPS module synergistically facilitates salt-induced PEC metabolism, inspiring potential biotechnological strategies to improve agarwood quality through metabolic engineering.

Pharmacopoeia (Editorial Board of the Chinese Pharmacopoeia Commission  2020). However, the wild A. sinensis forests have endured severe destruction due to high economic value and extensive demand for agarwood, leading to its classification as an endangered species under Appendix II of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (Amendments to Appendices Ⅰ and Ⅱ and of CITES 2004). Hence, it is urgently needed to elucidate the agarwood formation mechanism to maintain agarwood industry development sustainably in a healthy manner. Acting as typical active ingredients of agarwood, 2-(2-phenylethyl)chromones (PECs) exhibited anti-inflammatory (Liu, Yu, et  al.  2024), antibacterial (Wang, Mei, et  al.  2018), cytotoxic, neuroprotective (Yoon et  al.  2006), antioxidative (Hashim et  al.  2016) and AchEinhibitory (Li et al. 2019) properties pharmacologically. To date, more than 330 congeners of PECs have been identified in agarwood (Li et al. 2021; Hu et al. 2024; Wei et al. 2024). Therefore, it highlights the necessity of elucidating the biosynthetic pathways and intrinsic regulatory mechanisms related to PECs, which is advantageous for understanding agarwood formation and facilitating the discovery of new therapeutic compounds. We previously reported that the diarylpentanoid-producing polyketide synthase (AsPECPS) in A. sinensis, whose expression abundance correlates with PEC accumulation under salt stress, is a key enzyme responsible for PEC biosynthesis by catalysing the formation of the precursor of PECs with a ${ \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 } – { \mathrm { C } } _ { 5 } – { \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 }$ structural framework (Morita et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2022). We also identified four O-methyltransferases involved in enriching the structural diversity of PECs (Wang et al. 2024), and one $\alpha$ - ketoglutarate-dependent oxygenase responsible for converting PECs into 2-styrychromones in agarwood (Zhang et al. 2025). However, the regulatory networks governing the metabolic pathway of PECs remain poorly understood. 

Transcription factors (TFs) modulate plant physiological and developmental processes by recognising and binding special cisacting elements within target gene promoters to modulate gene expression (Tong et  al.  2024). As one of the largest and most important TF families in plants, the MYB TF family harbours a conserved DNA-binding domain and is divided into four subfamilies according to the type and number of conserved MYB domain repeats: 4R-type MYB (including four R1/R3-like repeats), R1R2R3-type MYB (3R-MYB), R2R3-type MYB and MYB-like (1R-MYB) subfamily (Wu et al. 2024). Among them, R2R3-type MYB TFs have been extensively studied and are crucial for regulating secondary metabolites synthesis, including flavonoids (Jiang et al. 2024), terpenoids (Man et al. 2023) and alkaloids (Sun et al. 2019). A recent report also illustrated that the R2R3-MYB AsMYB054 could negatively modulate PEC biosynthesis (Yang et  al.  2023). Beyond R2R3-MYB TFs, emerging evidence suggests that MYB-like (1R-MYB) TFs or MYB-related proteins also play significant roles in secondary metabolism. Phylogenetically, 1R-MYB TFs can be categorised into five subgroups: CIRCADIAN CLOCK ASSOCIATED 1 (CCA1)-like/R-R-type MYB, TELOMERIC REPEAT BINDING FACTOR (TRF), I-box-binding-like, CAPRICE (CPC)-like and TELOMERIC DNA-BINDING PROTEIN (TBP)-like (Katiyar et  al.  2012). Among them, CCA1-like/R-R-type MYB TFs and CPC-like TFs are associated with the regulation of secondary metabolite formation (Cao et al. 2020). In soybeans, CCA1-like TFs GmMYB176 and GmMYB133 positively affect isoflavonoid 

synthesis by modulating the structural gene expression (Bian et al. 2018; Vadivel et al. 2019). In red pulp pitaya, HuMYB132 encoding an R-R type MYB transcription factor positively facilitates betalain production by promoting the transcription activities of HuDODA1, HuADH1 and HuCYP76AD1-1 (Xie et  al.  2023). However, CPC-like R3-MYBs are small proteins that passively regulate the accumulation of anthocyanin and proanthocyanidin biosynthesis in many plants (Zhu et al. 2009). Arabidopsis CPC and chrysanthemum CmMYB#7 contribute to regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis negatively (Xiang et  al.  2019), whereas CitTRL (TRIPTYCHON-like TF) simultaneously represses the proanthocyanidin and anthocyanin production (He et al. 2022). Despite these advances, the involvement of MYB-like TFs in PEC biosynthesis remains largely unexplored. 

In plants, the regulation of secondary metabolites is invariably mediated by synergistic and hierarchical regulatory networks organised with multi-TFs or the concerted action of several proteins. Specifically, anthocyanin production in various plants is typically promoted by a protein complex (MBW complex) composed of R2R3-MYB TFs, basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) TFs and WD-repeat (WDR) proteins through modulating the expression of structural genes (Xu et al. 2015). The MYB-like TF AceMYBS1 from kiwifruit serves as a positive regulator in fruit Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid (AsA)) accumulation and physically interacts with a bZIP transcription factor AceGBF to additively promote AsA accumulation (Liu et  al.  2022). GmMYB176 interacts with 14-3-3 family protein SGF14 to positively regulate isoflavonoid production in soybean (Li et al. 2012). Consequently, an in-depth investigation of the interaction and hierarchical regulation of PEC biosynthesis will be beneficial to unravelling the regulatory pathway of PEC biosynthesis and agarwood formation. 

Here, we identified AsMYB1, a salt-inducible 1R-MYB TF which exhibited a co-expression pattern relationship with the PECs’ biosynthesized gene AsPECPS, and further explored that AsMYB1 was responsible for the positive regulation of PEC accumulation under salt stress via specifically binding to the promoter of AsPECPS. Additionally, AsGRF1, belonging to the 14-3-3 family, was confirmed to interact with AsMYB1 in vivo and in vitro, thereby positively modulating AsMYB1- mediated PEC biosynthesis. These findings are expected to provide robust evidences for developing biotechnological strategies to manipulate PEC levels and enhance the understanding of plant defence mechanisms, thereby facilitating the improvement of agarwood quality.

# 2.3 | AsMYB1 Specifically Binds to the AsPECPS Promoter and Significantly Activates Its Transcription

To further investigate the AsMYB1 function in regulating PEC production, we cloned the 800 bp region upstream of the start codon of AsPECPS. PlantCARE online analysis indicated the presence of three putative different MYB binding sites (MBSs) (MBS1: CAACTG, MBS2: ATTACCAA, and MBS3: AAAAGAAT) within the AsPECPS promoter fragment, and MBS2 was adjacent to MBS3. The yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays demonstrated that AsMYB1 directly bound to the promoter of AsPECPS (Figure  3a). The dual-luciferase reporter assays indicated that AsMYB1 significantly enhanced the promoter activity of AsPECPS (Figure  3b,c). To explore the binding sites for AsMYB1 in the AsPECPS promoter, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) were conducted. Since MBS3 is consecutive to MBS2, one AsPECPS promoter fragment harbouring MBS1 (probe1) and the other sequence 

harbouring MBS2 and MBS3 (probe2) for the promoter of AsPECPS were amplified and used as probes for EMSAs (Table S4). The results discovered that AsMYB1 protein specifically bound to the two probes, causing mobility shifts, and the binding affinity was attenuated with increasing concentration (100-fold and 200-fold excess) of unlabeled competitor probes and disappeared when the probes were mutated, suggesting that AsMYB1 could bind to the specific MBSs in the promoter of AsPECPS (Figure  3d). Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-PCR assays were utilised to confirm the interaction between AsMYB1 and the AsPECPS promoter in vivo. The results indicated that in AsMYB1-overexpressing suspension cells, two fragments featuring binding sites for AsMYB1 exhibited strong signals (Figure  3e), demonstrating that AsMYB1 directly bound to the AsPECPS promoter through specific association with MBS motifs. Collectively, the data convincingly revealed that AsMYB1 was a vital upstream positive modulator of PEC biosynthesis. 

# 2.4 | AsMYB1 Interacts With AsGRF1 In Vivo and In Vitro

To profoundly understand the molecular mechanism underlying AsMYB1 regulation, putative interacting proteins of AsMYB1 were searched via mass spectrometry affinity purification. We identified 67 N. benthamiana candidate proteins including the 14-3-3 family protein A0A1S4BTV4 that potentially interacted with AsMYB1 (Table  S5). Considering that AsMYB1 was associated with PEC metabolism and AsMYB1 possesses three 14-3-3 family protein binding motifs, we focused our work on the 14-3-3 member for functional investigation, which was based on previous reports establishing 14-3-3 proteins as the key regulator for isoflavonoid and anthocyanin biosynthesis (Li et al. 2012; Ren et al. 2021). We did a Blastp search against the A. sinensis genome (Nong et al. 2020) using A0A1S4BTV4 (a 14-3-3 family protein in N. benthamiana) as a query sequence. The results indicated that there are 15 genes of the 14-3-3 family in the A. sinensis genome, and 8 genes share high amino acid identities with A0A1S4BTV4. We analysed the expression levels of 8 genes of the 14-3-3 family under salt stress and found that 5 genes of the 14-3-3 family were significantly induced (Figure S4a–h), among which AsGRF1 showed the highest correlation with AsPECPS (Pearson's $r { = } 0 . 9 6$ ) and AsMYB1 (Pearson's $r { = } 0 . 9 4 _ { \cdot }$ ) (Table  S2), hinting that AsGRF1 may be a participant in modulating PEC biosynthesis under salt stress. AsGRF1 indicated a complete coding sequence (CDS) measuring 792 bp in length, encoding a protein consisting of 263 amino acids. Multiple sequence alignment revealed that AsGRF1 is composed of nine conserved $\alpha$ -helices (Figure  S5a). Furthermore, phylogenetic analysis demonstrated that six main subgroups could be distinguished and AsGRF1 was assigned to subgroup omega (Figure  S5b). The sublocation analysis indicated that AsGRF1 localised in the nucleus and cytoplasm (Figure S5c).

Given that 14-3-3 family members typically interact with phosphorylated proteins via pST-binding motifs (Huang et al. 2022), we investigated the role of the three predicted 14-3-3 binding motifs in the sequence of AsMYB1. Five AsMYB1 mutants with individual or combined deletions of these motifs were generated (Figure  S6a) and subjected to LCI assays. # 3 | Discussion

PECs are the important indicators of agarwood quality, which are only induced in A. sinensis by exogenous damages (e.g., biotic or abiotic stress) and possess various pharmacological activities. Our previous reports indicated that PECs are involved in scavenging the effects of reactive oxygen species induced by salt stress (Wang, Dong, et  al.  2018). Therefore, the investigation of the regulation of PEC biosynthesis facilitates the understanding of the mechanisms of agarwood formation and plant defence. Emerging evidence highlights the involvement of the 

1R-MYB transcription factor (TF) subgroup in regulating secondary metabolite biosynthesis. However, the specific roles of 1R-MYB genes in PEC biosynthesis and the molecular mechanisms and regulatory networks underlying this process under stress conditions remain poorly understood. This study unveils that a novel 1R-MYB TF AsMYB1 positively regulates PECs' accumulation under salt stress by enhancing the expression of AsPECPS, and the 14-3-3 family protein AsGRF1 interacts with AsMYB1 to additively regulate the PEC production. This discovery provides new insight into strengthening the understanding of the mechanism of agarwood formation and improving the agarwood quality.

Exemplifying this functional class, GmMYB133 and GmMYB176 enhance isoflavonoid biosynthesis through transcriptional activation of pathway genes (Bian et al. 2018; 

Vadivel et al. 2019), while AceMYBS1 regulates ascorbic acid synthesis via AceGGP3 induction (Liu et al. 2022). Our investigation identifies AsMYB1 as a novel CCA1-like MYB family member, exhibiting significant sequence homology with characterised 1R-MYB proteins and retaining the conserved SHAQKYF motif (Figure  1a). Meanwhile, AsMYB1 expression patterns under salt stress strongly correlated with the transcript levels of AsPECPS (Figure S1a,b and Table S2), the pivotal gene controlling PEC biosynthesis. Functional characterisation revealed that transgenic overexpression of AsMYB1 significantly upregulated AsPECPS expression and substantially increased PEC accumulation under 150 mM NaCl treatment (Figures  2a–c and S3a). Conversely, RNAi-mediated suppression and CRISPR/Cas9 mutagenesis of AsMYB1 caused marked reductions in both AsPECPS expression and PEC production (Figures  2d–i and S3b,c). These complementary genetic approaches establish AsMYB1 as a positive regulator of salt-induced PEC biosynthesis. The MYB transcription factor family is one of the largest transcription factor families, regulating various plant biological processes such as secondary metabolite biosynthesis, leaf senescence, trichome formation and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses. For example, SlMYB75 (an R2R3 transcription factor gene) promotes the biosynthesis of anthocyanin, the production of volatile aroma compounds and the formation of trichomes, whereas it suppresses the accumulation of terpenoids (Gong et  al.  2021). Various compounds, including PECs and sesquiterpenes, have been characterised from agarwood. Although salt stress can induce the expression of AsMYB1 and promote PEC accumulation in A. sinensis calli and suspension cells, its impact on sesquiterpene biosynthesis remains unclear. Therefore, further explorations of AsMYB1 on the regulation of other secondary metabolite biosynthesis and response to salt stress should be conducted in subsequent studies. 

CCA1-like/R-R-type TFs can directly associate with the key biosynthetic gene promoters to regulate their expression, thereby modulating secondary metabolite biosynthesis. For instance, in Arabidopsis, REVEILLE8/LHY-CCA1-LIKE5 (RVE8/LCL5) strengthened anthocyanin accumulation by specifically combining with TT18, UGT79B1 and TT4 promoters (Pérez-García et al. 2015). The R-R-type MYB TF HuMYB132 specifically interacted with the conserved MYB recognised elements, including the MRE (CAACCA and ACCTAA) and MBS (T/CAACTG), of HuCYP76AD1-1, HuADH1 and HuDODA1 promoters to increase their expression, thereby enhancing the formation of betalain (Xie et al. 2023). PlantCARE analysis indicated that the promoter of AsPECPS contained three putative MBSs (MBS1-3), and MBS3 was closely adjacent to MBS2. Our results demonstrated that AsMYB1 remarkably enhanced the transcription of AsPECPS promoter and specifically bound to three MBSs in the AsPECPS promoter region (Figure  3a–e). Functionally, AsMYB1 enhanced the biosynthesis of PECs by elevating the expression of AsPECPS. Recent findings demonstrated that AsOMT1, an enzyme belonging to the $\mathrm { M g ^ { 2 + } }$ -dependent caffeoyl-CoA $O$ -methyltransferase subfamily, catalyses non-regioselective methylation at the 6-OH or 7-OH positions of 6,7-dihydroxy-PEC. In contrast, three enzymes from the caffeic acid OMT subfamily (AsOMT2–4) exhibit substrate preference for PECs, producing structurally diverse methylated PEC derivatives (Wang et  al.  2024). 

Further analysis of the expression levels of AsOMT1–4 in AsMYB1-overexpressing suspension cells revealed that overexpression of AsMYB1 significantly enhances the expression levels of AsOMT1–4 (Figure  S11). However, whether the increased expression of these OMTs is directly regulated by AsMYB1 requires additional experimental evidence. 

# 3.2 | AsGRF1 Specifically Interacts With AsMYB1 to Additively Enhance Salt-Induced PEC Synthesis

In plants, elaborate regulatory network pathways have been established to orchestrate the effective biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. MYB TFs can form genetic regulatory complexes by combining with other TFs or proteins in secondary metabolism (Li et  al.  2022). For example, MtWP1 combined with MtTT8 and MtWD40-1 to accelerate carotenoid biosynthesis in Medicago truncatula (Meng et al. 2019). LcR1MYB1– LcNAC13 physically co-regulate anthocyanin metabolism during litchi fruit ripening (Jiang et al. 2019). In soybean hairy roots, co-expression of GmMYB176 and GmbZIP5 enhances the accumulation of multiple isoflavonoid phytoalexins, including isowighteone, glyceollin and a unique O-methylhydroxy isoflavone (Vadivel et  al.  2021). Nonetheless, the specific regulatory module underlying salt-induced PECs’ accumulation remains largely elusive. 

The 14-3-3 family proteins are highly conserved in eukaryotic species and are associated with multiple physiological and biological processes, such as disease and environmental stress responses, cell cycle and primary metabolism (Zhou et al. 2024; Camoni et al. 2018). Recent reports have shown that the 14-3-3 family proteins participate in the modulation of anthocyanin and isoflavonoid biosynthesis. For example, 14-3-3 lambda (14-3-3λ) modulates anthocyanin accumulation under drought stress. MdGRF11 decreased MdBT2 stability, thus increasing MdMYB1 abundance to accelerate the anthocyanin production during a nitrate-deficient environment (Ren et al. 2021). In soybean, SCG14d interacts with GmMYB176 to weaken the influence of GmMYB176-mediated isoflavonoid production (Li et  al.  2012). However, the existing data are insufficient to elucidate the function of the 14-3-3 family proteins in the regulation of PEC biosynthesis Employing mass spectrometry affinity purification technology, we identified a 14-3-3 family protein, AsGRF1, as a potential interacting partner of AsMYB1 (Table S5). 

In many cases, 14-3-3 interacting proteins always contain the canonical internal binding motifs (de Boer et  al.  2013), including mode I (RSXpS/pTXP), mode II (RXF/YXpS/pTXP) and mode III motif $\mathsf { p } ( \mathbf { S } / \mathrm { T } ) \mathbf { X } _ { 1 - 2 }$ -COOH (X denotes any amino acid and pS denotes phosphoserine). Bioinformatic analysis identified three putative 14-3-3 pST-binding motifs in AsMYB1 sequence (Figure  1a), and the expression trend of AsGRF1 is similar to the expression trend of AsPECPS and AsMYB1 (Figure S4 and Table S2), suggesting that AsMYB1 could interact with AsGRF1, and AsGRF1 may respond to the modulation of PEC biosynthesis under salt stress. The results from LCI, BiFC Co-IP and GST pull-down experiments concluded that AsGRF1 could interact with AsMYB1 in vivo and in vitro (Figure 4a–d). Usually, 14-3-3 proteins are 

associated with target proteins by identifying the phosphorylated serine/threonine within the conserved 14-3-3 binding motifs. Intriguingly, deletion mutagenesis of individual or combined 14-3-3 binding motifs failed to disrupt AsMYB1- AsGRF1 complex formation (Figure  S6a–c), suggesting a phosphorylation-independent interaction mechanism. This observation aligns with emerging evidence of atypical 14- 3-3 binding modalities that bypass canonical phosphorylated motif recognition (Aitken 2002; Sribar et al. 2003). Thus, the AsGRF1 binding site on AsMYB1 needs to be further studied in future investigations. 

Since AsMYB1 is a client protein of AsGRF1, AsGRF1 protein may directly or indirectly regulate PEC accumulation under salinity stress. Our study demonstrated that RNAi-mediated knockdown or gene editing of AsGRF1 significantly reduced both AsPECPS expression and PEC accumulation under salt stress (Figure  S7e–l), whereas AsGRF1 overexpression failed to show comparable regulatory effects (Figure S7a–d), suggesting that AsGRF1 indirectly regulates PEC accumulation under salt stress, and AsGRF's role is dependent on AsMYB1. Further analysis indicates that AsGRF1 likely regulates PEC biosynthesis through complex formation with AsMYB1. Coordinated manipulation of both AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 expression demonstrated synergistic effects on PEC production and AsPECPS transcript levels under salt stress (Figures  5a–f, S8, and S9). Such cooperative interaction could explain why AsGRF1 overexpression alone failed to influence AsPECPS expression or PEC accumulation significantly. These findings establish that the AsMYB1–AsGRF1 complex functions cooperatively to amplify stress-induced PEC biosynthesis. 

# 3.3 | AsGRF1 Alters Three Characteristics of AsMYB1 to Mediate PEC Biosynthesis

Generally, 14-3-3 proteins associate with diverse transcription factors or proteins and influence their properties, including transcriptional activity, sublocation, stability, structural configuration, and so on (Sheikh et  al.  2024). Previously, plant 14-3-3 proteins were indicated to interact with TFs to form a DNA-binding complex to control plant biological processes. For example, OsGF14f interacts with OsbZIP33 to form a complex enhancing the DNA binding activity and transcriptional activation of OsbZIP33 to confer osmotic stress (Ma et al. 2023). In barley, interaction with 14-3-3 protein enhanced the transcriptional activity of TF HvABI5 during seed germination (Schoonheim et al. 2007). However, the mechanism of how the 14-3-3 protein participates in these transcription regulation complexes remains elusive. Our study reveals that AsGRF1 critically enhances AsMYB1's transcriptional competence by enabling its full DNAbinding activity at MBS2/3 motifs in the AsPECPS promoter (Figure 6a). Dual-luciferase assays further confirmed AsGRF1's role in amplifying AsMYB1-driven transcriptional activation (Figure 6b). 

The interaction with the 14-3-3 family protein could influence the sublocalisation of the target protein within the plant cells. For example, rice 14-3-3 protein could interact with BRASSINAZOLE-RESISTANT1, an essential activator for the brassinosteroid (BR) signal pathway, and inhibit its nuclear 

localisation, thus mediating BR-controlled rice growth (Bai et  al.  2007). SCG14d binding with GmMYB176 affected the subcellular localisation of GmMYB176, thereby influencing isoflavonoid synthesis (Vadivel et  al.  2019). In contrast to previous investigations, AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 are both located in the nucleus and cytoplasm, and AsGRF1 could increase the nuclear localisation of AsMYB1 (Figures  6c and S10a,b), thereby mediating AsMYB1-controlled PEC biosynthesis. Recent reports have demonstrated that the 14-3-3 family protein could modulate their target protein stability. For example, 14-3-3 protein OsGF14c interacts with and destabilises OsSCL7, thereby mediating rice immunity. However, MdGRF1 negatively modulated BTB protein MdBT2 stability to promote anthocyanin formation. Furthermore, we demonstrate that AsGRF1 stabilises AsMYB1 against proteasomal degradation (Figures 6d and S10c). Therefore, AsGRF1 mediates three characteristics of AsMYB1 to enhance the accumulation of PECs under salt stress. 

In conclusion, the AsMYB1-AsGRF1 module represents a regulatory nexus where the MYB-like TF AsMYB1 directly activates AsPECPS expression, while its partnership with the 14-3-3 protein AsGRF1 synergistically enhances transcriptional output through multifunctional regulation (Figure  7). By potentiating DNA-binding activity, augmenting its nuclear accumulation, and improving stablity of AsMYB1, AsGRF1 ensures robust stress-inducible PEC biosynthesis (Figure  7). The finding of the AsMYB1-AsGRF1 module helps to decipher the biosynthetic regulatory mechanism of PECs and offers a novel target for engineering stress-induced PEC production in A. sinensis.

# 4.3 | Sequence Analysis and Phylogenetic Analysis

The full-length CDSs of AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 were cloned with the specific primers (Table S6) according to the A. sinensis transcriptomic dataset. The promoter sequence of AsPECPS was amplified with the genomic DNA of A. sinensis calli. The cis-acting regulatory elements of the AsPECPS promoter (Table S7) were predicted through the online PlantCARE tool. The amino acid 

alignments were carried out with DNAMAN software, whereas the phylogenetic trees were created with MEGA version 6 using the neighbour-joining method. 

# 4.4 | RNA Extraction and Gene Expression Analysis

Total RNA extraction was completed by the Total RNA Purification Kit (NORGEN, Canada). Genomic DNA was removed using DNase I (TaKaRa, Dalian, China), and then the first-strand cDNA was synthesised with M-MLV (Promega, USA). Quantitative RT-PCR was executed using the Bio-Rad CFX96 Real-Time PCR System along with the PCR Master Mix (Tiangen, China), with AsRPL as the internal control (Gao et  al.  2012). The gene expression was calculated with the $2 ^ { - }$ ΔΔCT method (Giulietti et al. 2001), and Table S6 lists the primer 

sequences. Three independent experiments were carried out for biological statistics. 

# 4.5 | Subcellular Localisation of AsMYB1 and AsGRF1

The coding regions of AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 were inserted into pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP and pCAMBIA1300-35S-mCherry, respectively. To investigate the sublocation of AsMYB1 and AsGRF1, the A. thaliana mesophyll protoplasts were carefully isolated from leaves as previous reports (Wu et al. 2009), and then the constructs of 35S: AsMYB1-GFP, 35S: AsGRF1- GFP and 35S: GFP were co-transferred with a nuclear marker 35S: Osghd7-RFP into A. thaliana protoplasts using the PEGmediated method (Wu et  al.  2009). To verify the function of AsGRF1 on AsMYB1 localisation, the recombinant constructs including pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP-AsMYB1 and pCAMBIA1300-35S-mCherry-AsGRF1 were transformed into the N. benthamiana leaves through the Agrobacterium-mediated method. The GFP fluorescence and RFP signal were captured with an FV10 laser scanning confocal microscope at $4 8 8 \mathrm { n m }$ and $5 8 7 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. 

# 4.6 | Overexpression, RNAi and Gene Editing of Genes in A. sinensis Cells

The overexpression constructs pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP-AsMYB1, pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP-AsGRF1 and the empty vector pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP were transferred into Agrobacterium strain EHA105 and then introduced into A. sinensis suspension cells (Cao et  al.  2022). The suspension cells were cultured in the MS fluid medium containing $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ cefotaxime and $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L }$ hygromycin for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ and then transferred into the MS medium with 150 mM NaCl for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . The suspension cells were collected for RNA extraction and PEC measurement. 

The intron-containing hairpin RNA constructs were yielded after the ligation of 200 bp fragments of AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 introduced into the pBWA(V)HS-RNAi vector. For editing AsMYB1 and AsGRF1, the sequences of guide RNA measuring 23 to 24 bp were synthesised and integrated into the pKSE401 vector using the CRISPR/Cas9 system. The resulting RNAi, CRISPR/Cas9, and empty vectors were transferred into A. sinensis calli as previously described (Tang et al. 2018). The calli were subcultured on the MS plates supplemented with $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ cefotaxime and $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L }$ hygromycin at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 8 days, followed by exposure to 150 mM NaCl for 10 days. 

# 4.7 | Y1H Assays

The ORFs of AsMYB1 were ligated into the pGADT7 vector, resulting in the creation of the prey vectors. The promoter regions of AsPECPS (from $^ { - 1 }$ to $- 8 0 0 \mathsf { b p }$ ) containing three MBSs were inserted into the pAbAi plasmid to generate pAbAi-AsPECPSpro. The primers used for yeast one-hybrid assays are shown in Table  S6. The yeast one-hybrid assay was carried out as 

previously reported. Aureobasidin A (AbA) was used to inhibit the yeast cell growth and acted as a screening marker. The concentration of AbA was $4 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ for the AsPECPS promoter fragment. 

# 4.8 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

AsMYB1 CDS was incorporated into pGreenII 62-SK effector vector, while AsPECPS promoter fragment was recombined into the reporter vector pGreenII0800-LUC. The resulting constructs were introduced separately into A. tumefaciens GV3101 (pSoup) and subsequently transfected into N. benthamiana epidermal cells. The pGreenII0800-LUC reporter vector containing Renilla LUC (REN) acted as an internal control. After 2–3 days, the Dual-luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (Promega, USA) was employed to measure relative LUC activity. 

# 4.9 | EMSAs

AsMYB1 CDS was constructed into the pET28a plasmid and translated into Escherichia coli strain BL21(DE3). The expression of AsMYB1 was induced with $0 . 8 \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl-Dthiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and purified with the His-Tagged protein Purification Kit (Cytiva, Sweden). The 25–27 bp probes containing the MBSs from the AsPECPS promoter were synthesised and labelled with biotin. The same unlabeled probes acted as competitors. EMSA was implemented using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime, China). The primers and probe sequences for EMSAs are listed in Tables S6 and S4, respectively. 

# 4.10 | ChIP-qPCR

The recombinant plasmid pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP-AsMYB1  was transformed into A. sinensis suspension cells using the Agrobacterium-mediated method. The empty vector pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP was introduced as the negative control. Chromatin immunoprecipitation analysis was conducted with an EpiQuik Plant ChIP Kit (Epigentek, the U.S.A.) according to the manufacturer's introduction. Following fixation of $0 . 3 \mathrm { g }$ transgenic suspension cells with $1 \%$ formaldehyde, AsMYB1-bound chromatin was precipitated using an anti-GFP antibody. RT-qPCR detected the enriched DNA fraction in the precipitated chromatin with the negative control (the fragments isolated from the cells transformed with an empty vector). 

# 4.11 | Screening the Potential Interacting Proteins of AsMYB1

The AsMYB1 ORF was constructed into the pCAMBIA1300- 3XFlag vector. Agrobacterium transformed with the fusion constructs and pCAMBIA1300-3XFlag empty vector were introduced into N. benthamiana leaves respectively. Total proteins of N. benthamiana leaves were extracted using lysis buffer as described previously (Zhang et al. 2024). Lysates were incubated 

with anti-Flag magnetic beads (Beyotime, China) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { h }$ and washed three times with 1 mL lysis buffer. Eluted proteins were detected by $1 0 \%$ (w/v) SDS-PAGE, and immunoblot analysis was employed with anti-Flag (ABclonal) antibody. Gel bands were prepared for IP-MS analysis conducted by the researchers at Peking University. The identified proteins interacting with AsMYB1 are listed in Table S5. 

# 4.12 | Determination and Quantification of the Content of PECs

$0 . 1 \mathrm { g }$ treated calli or suspension cells were collected, dried at room temperature and added with $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ methanol for supersonic extraction for 1h. The extracts were centrifuged at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at 13,000rpm for 60min. The supernatants were for testing the content of PECs. The relative content of PECs was quantified using an Agilent Technologies 6460 triple quadrupole mass spectrometer equipped with an Acquity HSS T3 Column $2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ inner diameter $\times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Waters Corporation, Wexford, Ireland); the LC column was maintained at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a constant temperature column chamber. Two elution solutions were used in the gradient program, $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (A) and acetonitrile (B): $0 { - } 2 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , $4 0 \% - 9 0 \%$ (B). The flow rate is $0 . 3 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the injection volume is $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . Qtrap-MS is equipped with an ESI ion source for collection in positive ion mode. Use Analyst 1.6.3 software (SCIEX, USA) for relevant data processing. 1,5-diphenyl-1,3-pentanone $( \mathrm { C } _ { 6 } – \mathrm { C } _ { 5 } – \mathrm { C } _ { 6 } )$ was used as the internal standard. 

# 4.13 | LCI Assays

AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 ORFs were incorporated into pCAMBIA1300-cLUC and pCAMBIA1300-nLUC, respectively. The recombinant constructs were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain EHA105 and co-injected in a 1:1 proportion into 5 week old N. benthamiana leaves. After 36-48 h, the leaves were soaked in 1 mM D-luciferin for 20 min and then visualised with a multi-automatic chemiluminescence image analysis system (Tanon, China). 

# 4.14 | BiFC Assays

The pCAMBIA1300-35S-MCS-HA-YFPC-35S-Hyg vector contained AsMYB1 that had been constructed, whereas the pCAMBIA1300-35S-MCS-Myc-YFPN-35S-Hyg vector contained AsGRF1 that had been cloned. A. tumefaciens strain EHA105 harbouring pCAMBIA1300-35S-MCS-HA-YFPC-35S-Hyg-AsMYB1, pCAMBIA1300-35S-MCS-Myc-YFPN-35S-Hyg-As1GRF1 or empty vectors was co-injected into the leaves of 5 week old N. benthamiana leaf epidermal cells following earlier reported procedures (Li et al. 2024). The YFP fluorescence was observed via an FV10 laser scanning confocal microscope after $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ of infiltration. 

# 4.15 | Co-IP Analysis

The recombinant plasmids pCAMBI1300-35S-GFP-AsMYB1 and pCAMBIA1300-35S-3XFlag-AsGRF1 were introduced into 

A. tumefaciens and then infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. Total proteins were extracted from the injected leaves after 2–3 days, and the supernatant was incubated with Flag beads (Beyotime, China) to capture the epitope-tagged protein. After washing three times with protein extraction buffer, the immunoprecipitated protein complex was detected by Western blotting with anti-Flag and anti-GFP antibodies. 

# 4.16 | GST Pull-Down Assay

AsMYB1 and AsGRF1 full-length sequences were subcloned into pET28a and pGEX-KG vectors, respectively, and then introduced into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells. The His-tagged and GSTtagged proteins were purified with Ni-NTA HisTrapFF (Cytiva, Sweden) and GST-tag Purification Resin (Beyotime, China), respectively. The beads containing GST and GST-AsGRF1 proteins were incubated with AsMYB1-His in a pull-down buffer overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Then the beads were washed, and the proteins boiled in an SDS-PAGE loading buffer for Western blotting utilising antibodies against GST-tag and His-tag. 

# 4.17 | Protein Stability Assay and Cell-Free Degradation Assays

The constructs 35S: AsGRF1-GFP and 35S: GFP were individually introduced into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and then transformed into N. benthamiana leaves. Total protein was extracted from infiltrated leaf segments after 48–72 h as previously described (Zhang et  al.  2024). AsMYB1-His recombinant protein was added to the total protein from leaves infiltrated with 35S: AsGRF1-GFP and 35S: GFP. Protein abundance was detected with immunoblot analysis utilising the antibody against His-tag. Simultaneously, total protein extracts were subjected to $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132 (proteasome suppressor) in the dark for 30 min and then incubated with recombinant AsMYB1 protein. The samples were analysed with immunoblot analysis using anti-His antibody, and ACTIN served as a control.
