# SUMMARY

In response to necrotrophic fungal pathogens, plants often display quantitative disease resistance (QDR), an immune response with complex genetic determinants. Due to their diversity and small phenotypic effect, the genetic bases of QDR are challenging to characterize. Here, we used genome-wide association mapping in Arabidopsis thaliana natural populations to identify novel determinants of QDR against the fungal pathogen Sclerotinia sclerotiorum. We found that presence–absence polymorphism of the AT4TE56270 Copia transposable element (TE) upstream of the cysteine-rich receptor-like kinase 8 (CRK8) gene is associated with QDR. The presence of the TE associates with higher CRK8 expression in healthy and inoculated plants and increased QDR. The constitutive knockdown of CRK8 reduced QDR, hydrogen peroxide production, and the expression of defense genes upon inoculation. Transcriptome analysis revealed altered defense pathways and salt responses in CRK8 mutants, including impaired glutathione and camalexin biosynthesis, likely contributing to disease susceptibility. Mutants in CRK8 showed altered seed germination on salt, and the absence of AT4TE56270 is associated with enhanced seed germination under salt stress in A. thaliana natural populations. These results reveal a trade-off between salt tolerance and defense against S. sclerotiorum associated with presence–absence polymorphism of a TE. 

Keywords: Arabidopsis thaliana, fungal pathogen, plant immunity, receptor-like kinase, salt tolerance, Sclerotinia sclerotiorum, transposable element. 

# INTRODUCTION

Plant pathogens significantly impact agriculture by causing diseases that reduce crop yield and quality, leading to substantial economic losses (Ristaino et al., 2021). They include fungi, bacteria, viruses, and nematodes, each employing unique infection strategies. Among these, fungal pathogens infecting a broad range of hosts are on the rise and expected to expand their geographic range as a consequence of global changes (Bebber et al., 2014). The necrotrophic fungus Sclerotinia sclerotiorum is notorious for its broad host range and devastating effects on various crops (Derbyshire et al., 2022; Navaud et al., 2018). It causes white mold disease, characterized by water-soaked lesions and white, cottony mycelium on infected plant tissues. The fungus produces sclerotia, which are resilient survival structures that can persist in soil for extended periods, making it challenging to manage (Bolton et al., 2006). S. sclerotiorum’s ability to infect a wide array 

of plant species makes it a significant threat to global food security, necessitating robust control measures to mitigate its impact. Effective management of diseases caused by S. sclerotiorum involves integrating cultural practices, chemical control, biological agents, and host resistance (Derbyshire & Denton-Giles, 2016). Cultural practices like crop rotation and proper irrigation, along with judicious use of fungicides, help reduce disease incidence. Biological control agents, such as Trichoderma and Bacillus, antagonize the pathogen. The deployment of genetic sources of resistance is however the preferred route for the durable management of the disease, justifying continuous efforts to identify the molecular determinants of plant immunity against Sclerotinia. 

Plant immunity involves various molecular mechanisms to detect and respond to pathogen attacks. At the molecular level, it can be approximated as a two-tiered immune system (Dodds & Rathjen, 2010): At the cell 

surface, pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) recognize conserved pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), initiating pattern-triggered immunity (PTI); inside plant cells, nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat (NLR) receptors detect pathogen effectors, molecules secreted by pathogens to manipulate plant cells (Lo Presti et al., 2015), leading to a strong and specific immune response designated as effector-triggered immunity (ETI). PTI and ETI are not independent pathogen detection pathways as they share downstream signaling components (Pruitt et al., 2021). These immune responses activate defense genes, produce antimicrobial compounds, and induce localized cell death to limit the spread of pathogens that require living host cells to multiply (biotrophic). The activation of programmed cell death upon pathogen detection may however be beneficial to necrotrophic pathogens that actively kill host cells during infection (Derbyshire & Raffaele, 2023; Govrin & Levine, 2000; Liao et al., 2022). To fend off necrotrophic pathogens such as S. sclerotiorum, plants often display quantitative disease resistance (QDR) that reduces rather than stops pathogen colonization and involves multiple genes with smaller individual effects (Poland et al., 2009; Roux et al., 2014). QDR is characterized by its durability and broad-spectrum effectiveness, making it a valuable trait for developing sustainable crop protection strategies. The molecular basis of QDR involves a complex interplay of genes that contribute to various defense mechanisms, allowing plants to mount a flexible and robust response to a wide range of pathogens. Due to their diversity and small phenotypic effect, only a fraction of the genetic bases of QDR have been characterized to date. 

In S. sclerotiorum virulence strategy, cell walldegrading enzymes, effectors, and phytotoxins play crucial roles in facilitating infection and disease progression (Derbyshire et al., 2022; Mbengue et al., 2016; Xia et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2024). Cell wall-degrading enzymes, such as pectinases and cellulases, break down the plant cell wall, enabling the fungus to penetrate and colonize host tissues while accessing nutrients (Oliveira et al., 2013). Effectors, secreted by the fungus, manipulate the plant’s immune responses, suppressing or evading defense mechanisms to enhance infection success (Derbyshire & Raffaele, 2023; Guyon et al., 2014). Among toxins secreted by S. sclerotiorum, oxalic acid is a major virulence factor which notably manipulates the plant’s redox status by sequestering calcium ions and disrupting cell wall integrity (Cessna et al., 2000; Heller & Witt-Geiges, 2013). This acidification of the plant tissue facilitates the activity of fungal enzymes, such as polygalacturonases (Wei et al., 2022). Additionally, oxalic acid suppresses the plant’s oxidative burst, a critical component of the plant’s defense response, by interfering with reactive oxygen species (ROS) production and signaling (Williams et al., 2011). This manipulation of the plant’s 

redox environment creates a more favorable condition for fungal growth and proliferation, ultimately leading to plant cell death and the development of disease symptoms (Ranjan et al., 2018). 

ROS play a pivotal role in plant immunity by acting as signaling molecules and direct antimicrobial agents (Mittler et al., 2022; Waszczak et al., 2018). During pathogen infection, plants rapidly produce ROS through the oxidative burst, which is primarily mediated by NADPH oxidases and peroxidases. During plant immunity, ROS are primarily synthesized through two major pathways: the plasma membrane-localized NADPH oxidases and apoplastic peroxidases (Daudi et al., 2012; Torres et al., 2002). NADPH oxidases, also known as respiratory burst oxidase homologs (RBOHs), catalyze the production of superoxide $( { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ by transferring electrons from NADPH to molecular oxygen (Mittler et al., 2022). Superoxide can then be rapidly converted to hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ by superoxide dismutases (Smirnoff & Arnaud, 2019). Concurrently, apoplastic peroxidases contribute to ROS generation by catalyzing the production of hydrogen peroxide and other ROS in the apoplast (Daudi et al., 2012). Superoxide and hydrogen peroxide, can directly damage invading pathogens and contribute to the formation of physical barriers such as lignin and callose to restrict pathogen spread. Additionally, ROS act as secondary messengers, activating downstream defense responses, including the expression of defense-related genes and the induction of programmed cell death at the site of infection (Bi et al., 2025; Mittler et al., 2022). Alternative oxidases (AOX) regulate the mitochondrial electron transport chain to reduce the formation of ROS and maintain cellular redox homeostasis during immune responses (Waszczak et al., 2018). During stress conditions, such as pathogen attack or abiotic stresses like salinity, AOX1D is induced to prevent the over-reduction of the ubiquinone pool and subsequent excessive ROS generation. This regulation helps maintain cellular redox balance and limits oxidative stress, supporting plant defense mechanisms without compromising cellular integrity. The absence or insufficiency of AOX1D can lead to increased ROS levels and oxidative damage, highlighting its role in stress acclimation and immune response modulation (Oh et al., 2022; Strodtkotter et al.,€ 2009; Vishwakarma et al., 2015). 

The production of ROS upon pathogen attack is also under the control of receptor-like proteins at the cell surface (Kadota et al., 2014). Cysteine-rich receptor-like kinases (CRKs) play a key role in plant immunity by acting as sensors and regulators of defense responses against various pathogens (Rayapuram et al., 2012; Saintenac et al., 2021; Zeiner et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). These membrane-bound receptors, characterized by their extracellular cysteine-rich domains, might perceive external signals and initiate intracellular signaling cascades that 

activate immune responses. This dual role of CRKs in sensing and signaling makes them essential components of the plant’s immune system. Several studies reported elevated CRK transcript levels following pathogen infection, treatment with PAMPs or with salicylic acid (Bourdais et al., 2015; Wrzaczek et al., 2010; Yadeta et al., 2017). Arabidopsis CRK4, 6, 28, and 36 interact with PAMP receptor complexes and enhance responses to the bacterial PAMP flg22, such as ROS production, callose deposition, defense gene expression and stomatal closure (Chen et al., 2003; Lee et al., 2017; Yadeta et al., 2017; Yeh et al., 2015). AtCRK2 phosphorylates, and thereby activates RBOHD following pathogen perception (Bourdais et al., 2015; Kadota et al., 2014; Kimura et al., 2020), modulating ROS signaling to enhance immune responses without causing excessive oxidative damage (Wrzaczek et al., 2013). Mutations in AtCRK28 enhanced flg22 but not chitin-triggered ROS burst (Yadeta et al., 2017) suggesting functional diversification of CRKs in the organization of defense responses. While overexpression of CRK5, 13, and 28 in Arabidopsis led to enhanced resistance to the hemibiotrophic bacterium Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato (Pst) DC3000 and HR-like cell death (Acharya et al., 2007; Chen et al., 2003; Yadeta et al., 2017), CRK20 loss-of-function increased resistance to Pst DC3000 (Ederli et al., 2011). A screen identified Atcrk2 and Atcrk5 as resistant to the biotrophic fungus Golovinomyces orontii (Bourdais et al., 2015) while AtCRK36 increases resistance to Pst DC3000 but increases susceptibility to the necrotrophic fungus Alternaria brassicicola and the necrotrophic bacterium Erwinia carotovora (Lee et al., 2017). To date, research has mostly focused on the roles of CRKs in defense against bacterial pathogens and the molecular basis of CRK function in response to fungi and how they mediate responses to diverse environmental stimuli remains poorly understood. 

In this study, the presence–absence polymorphism of the AT4TE56270 copia transposable element (TE) upstream of CRK8 was associated with A. thaliana QDR against S. sclerotiorum. The presence of AT4TE56270 was associated with enhanced expression of CRK8 in healthy and fungus-inoculated plants. Constitutive knockdown of CRK8 in crk8.3 mutants reduced QDR against S. sclerotiorum, the biosynthesis of hydrogen peroxide and the expression of defense marker genes upon inoculation. Several of these phenotypes were masked by CRK8 overexpression in healthy crk8.1 plants. Comparative transcriptome analysis of wild-type and crk8 mutants uncovered contrasted regulation on known defense pathways and altered salt responses. CRK8 knockdown notably impaired the biosynthesis of glutathione, a cofactor for glucosinolates biosynthesis. In agreement, the biosynthesis of camalexin and derivatives was reduced in inoculated crk8 mutants, possibly contributing to their disease phenotype. Constitutive knockdown of CRK8 in crk8.3 mutants increased seed 

germination on NaCl and the absence of AT4TE56270 was associated with enhanced seed germination on NaCl in A. thaliana natural accessions. These results point to a tradeoff between response to salt and defense against S. sclerotiorum mediated by a TE presence–absence polymorphism upstream of AtCRK8. 

# RESULTS

# An insertion–deletion polymorphism upstream of CRK8 associates with quantitative resistance to S. Sclerotiorum

To identify genes associated with QDR to S. sclerotiorum in A. thaliana natural populations, we performed a genome-wide association mapping approach in 84 European accessions. For this, we used the average disease severity index (DSI) measured on 6–16 plants per accession as a phenotype (Badet et al., 2017). Association with single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) from A. thaliana 250 K chip was performed with the accelerated mixed model algorithm implemented in GWAPP (Seren et al., 2012), after filtering out SNPs with minor allele frequency lower than 0.1. Among the highest association scores $- 1 0 9 = 5 . 9 7 5$ was a SNP at position 12 131 010 of chromosome IV, located upstream of AT4G23160, coding for the Cysteine-Rich Receptor-like Kinase 8 (CRK8) gene (Figure 1a). To support the association between SNP IV_12 131 010 and QDR against S. sclerotiorum, we analyzed the distribution of QDR phenotypes according to their allele at position IV_12 131 010 (either G or deletion). To the 84 accessions used for GWA mapping, we added Columbia-0 which exhibited a G at position IV_12 131 010, and the susceptible Shahdara accession (Perchepied et al., 2010) which exhibited a deletion at position IV_12 131 010, leading to a total of 41 accessions with G and 45 accessions with deletion at position IV_12 131 010 (Figure 1b, Table S1). Accessions with a G at position IV_12 131 010 had a median DSI of 3.85, while accessions with deletion had a median DSI of 4.86 (Student’s t-test P-value $= 1 . 9 \mathsf { e } ^ { - 0 6 } )$ ). In line with this observation, the resistant accessions Col-0 (average $\mathsf { D S l } = 3 . 1 7$ ) and Rubezhnoe (average ${ \sf D S I } = 2 . 2 9 )$ ) showed a G at position IV_12 131 010, while the susceptible accession Shahdara (average $\mathsf { D S l } = 4 . 1 0 \mathsf { \Omega }$ ) had a deletion at position IV_12 131 010.

# AtCRK8 positively contributes to quantitative disease resistance against S. Sclerotiorum

To assess the role of CRK8 Col-0 allele in resistance against S. sclerotiorum, we analyzed the phenotype of two Col-0 insertion mutant lines. The crk8.1 (SALK_101066C) and crk8.3 (GKA061E11) had a T-DNA insertion in the ${ \mathfrak { H } } ^ { \prime }$ UTR region corresponding to AT4TE56270 and AT4G23160.1 exon 5 encoding the serine/threonine kinase domain respectively (Figure S4a). We validated homozygous T-DNA insertions by PCR and the T-DNA position by PCR sequencing. We collected RNA-seq reads for Col-0, crk8.1 and crk8.3 plants inoculated or not by S. sclerotiorum to analyze CRK8 transcripts accumulation in these plants (Figure 4a, Figure S4c). To estimate the impact of CRK8 expression on CRK8 protein accumulation, we compared read coverage in protein-coding exons of expressed transcripts (exons 2–8 in AT4G23160.1 and exons 4–7 in AT4G23160.2, Table S3). Compared with wild-type, crk8.1 showed increased accumulation of AT4G23160.1 (LFC 1.3, P-value 9E-02) and AT4G23160.2 (LFC 3.84, P-value 4E-03) in healthy leaves, similar accumulation of AT4G23160.2 (LFC 0.76, P-value 0.13) and no detectable expression of AT4G23160.1 upon inoculation. In crk8.3, the accumulation of AT4G23160.1 and AT4G23160.2 was lower than wildtype in healthy (LFC 2.9, P-value 0.053 and LFC 0.4, Pvalue 0.07, respectively) and inoculated leaves (LFC 3.9, P-value 9.6E-03 and LFC 5.0, P-value 4E-04, respectively). Q-RT-PCR with gene-specific primers in the protein-coding region of CRK8 (Table S4) confirmed the upregulation of this gene in healthy crk8.1 plants compared with wild-type, and its silencing in inoculated crk8.1 and in crk8.3 plants (Figure 4b). In summary, crk8.1 is an activation tagging line slightly overexpressing CRK8 in healthy plants but downregulating CRK8 upon inoculation, while crk8.3 is a knockdown line in which the accumulation of CRK8 transcripts is constitutively reduced. 

To test for a role of CRK8 in A. thaliana QDR to S. sclerotiorum, we analyzed the speed of disease lesion growth (Barbacci et al., 2020) in leaves of Col-0, crk8.1, and crk8.3 plants (Figure 4c, Table S5). On average, lesions 

spread similarly in crk8.1 and in wild-type (P-value 0.708), but $1 5 . 5 3 \%$ faster in crk8.3 (P-value 1.2E-04), suggesting that the constitutive knockdown of CRK8 in crk8.3 impaired disease resistance. We hypothesized that the complex misregulation of CRK8 in crk8.1 may mask alterations of immune responses in this line. To test for this hypothesis, we measured the expression of well-known defense marker genes in Col-0, crk8.1, and crk8.3 by Q-RT-PCR (Figure 4d, Table S6). The expression of PR1, PR4 and ABCG40/PDR12 was strongly induced in Col-0 upon S. sclerotiorum inoculation (108-fold, 48-fold, and 5126-fold on average respectively). The expression of PR1 was reduced to $3 9 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.1 (P-value 9.8E-06) and $3 5 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.3 (P-value 7.1E-06) upon inoculation. Similarly, PR4 was reduced to $80 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.1 (P-value 0.01) and $7 4 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.3 (P-value 2.4E-03) upon inoculation. However, upon inoculation ABCG40/PDR12 expression was reduced to $7 1 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.3 (P-value 3.1E-03), but increased to $12 1 \%$ of Col-0 expression in crk8.1 (Pvalue 0.03). These results indicate that some defense responses are altered similarly in crk8.1 and crk8.3, consistent with the knockdown of CRK8 upon inoculation in both lines, but other defense responses show contrasted regulation in crk8.1 and crk8.3, which may reflect contrasted alterations of CRK8 expression in healthy plants. 

Several Arabidopsis CRKs regulate ROS production (Bourdais et al., 2015; Kimura et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2017), prompting us to explore more specifically ROS production in CRK8 mutants. # Camalexin biosynthesis upon inoculation is compromised by CRK8 knockdown

Camalexin biosynthesis, a branch of glucosinolate metabolism, was among ontologies enriched in CRK8- dependent genes upregulated upon S. sclerotiorum inoculation. In addition, MPK3 and WRKY33, whose expression was altered in crk8 mutants positively regulate camalexin biosynthesis (Mao et al., 2011; Zhou et al., 2020). This prompted us to investigate glucosinolate biosynthesis in crk8 plants in more detail. We first focused on the expression of 42 genes with “glucosinolate biosynthesis” ontology differentially expressed in our RNA-seq dataset, among which 39 were upregulated upon inoculation (Figure 6a). One half (Cluster I, 22 genes) of these pathogen-induced genes was more strongly induced in crk8 mutants than in Col-0. This included WRKY33, all genes specifically involved in the biosynthesis of camalexin (GSTU4, PAD3, GSTF6, CYP71A12, CYP71A13), genes involved in the synthesis of isothiocyanates (ICNs) (FOX1, CYP82C2), and several genes involved in the synthesis of glucosinolate precursors (WEI7, TSB2, CYP79B2, CYP79B3) (Figure 6a,b, Table S6). Seven genes (Cluster II) were slightly impaired in their pathogen induction in crk8 mutants, all related to the biosynthesis of indole glucosinolates other than camalexin and ICNs (CYP81F2, GSTF10, AAO1, SUR1, CYP81F3, CYP83B1, UGT74B1). Ten genes (Cluster III) were strongly impaired in their pathogen induction in crk8 mutants involved in the biosynthesis of indole glucosinolates (PEN2, CYP91A2, GSTF9, IGMT1, and 2) and 

in the biosynthesis of the glutathione cofactor (WES1, GSH2, PAD2, TSB3) (Figure 6a,b, Table S6). 

The over-induction of camalexin biosynthesis, glucosinolate precursor biosynthesis and the reduced induction of other indole glucosinolate biosynthetic pathways in crk8 mutants would likely promote camalexin accumulation in crk8 mutants. However, the drastic reduction of glutathione biosynthesis in crk8 mutants could also severely compromise camalexin biosynthesis in these plants. To discriminate between these two scenarios, we quantified camalexin produced by Col-0, crk8.1, and crk8.3 at $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ postinoculation by S. sclerotiorum (Figure 6c, Table S11). We detected an average of $5 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } { 0 } | \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ leaf fresh weight (FW) of camalexin $( 1 1 2 1 \ \mathsf { n g } . \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 } )$ , consistent with quantities produced 24 hpi by Botrytis cinerea in Col-0 (Stefanato et al., 2009). Analysis of variance detected a significant genotype effect on the distribution of camalexin and derivatives (P-value 1.36E-16). Average camalexin production was respectively $2 3 . 8 \%$ and $1 4 . 9 \%$ lower in crk8.1 and crk8.3 (Pvalue 5.3E-04 and 0.017, respectively). S. sclerotiorum is able to detoxify camalexin by conversion into several derivatives, notably glucosylated camalexin (Pedras & Ahiahonu, 2002; Pedras et al., 2004) and hydroxycamalexin. We quantified these byproducts to estimate the overall flux through camalexin metabolism. We detected an average of $2 . 9 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } { \sf o l } ~ \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW of glucosylated camalexin in Col-0, $1 . 9 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } { \sf o l } ~ \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW in crk8.1 $( 3 1 . 5 \%$ reduction, P-value 1.33E-05) and $2 . 1 \ \mu \mathrm { m } { 0 } | \ \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW in crk8.3 (17.6% reduction, P-value 0.24). The production of hydroxycamalexin was in the range of $0 . 0 1 \ \mu \mathrm { m } { \mathsf { o l } } \ { \mathsf { g } } ^ { - 1 }$ FW, making a negligible contribution to the overall pool. Together, camalexin and its derivatives amounted to an average of $8 . 3 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } { \sf o l } ~ \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW in Col-0, a production significantly reduced in crk8.1 $( 2 7 . 5 \%$ reduction, P-value 1.87E-06) and crk8.3 (20.5% reduction, P-value 3.8E-05). These results indicate that CRK8 promotes camalexin production upon S. sclerotiorum inoculation, probably through a positive effect on glutathione biosynthesis rather than directly through the control of glucosinolate biosynthetic pathways.

# DISCUSSION

Understanding stress response trade-offs is crucial for developing strategies to improve plant resilience and productivity. Using GWA mapping, we associated the insertion of a TE upstream of the A. thaliana CRK8 cysteine-rich receptor-like kinase with QDR against the fungal pathogen S. sclerotiorum. Knockdown of CRK8 reduced the synthesis of plant defense compounds such as hydrogen peroxide and camalexin, and impaired resistance to pathogens. On the other hand, low CRK8 expression promoted germination on salt. The TE insertion upstream of CRK8 enhanced the expression of CRK8 and was associated with increased disease resistance but also with a reduced germination on salt, altering a stress response trade-off mediated by CRK8. 

CRKs are a large subgroup of the receptor-like protein kinases (RLKs) with two extracellular DUF26/GnK2 (Domain of unknown function/Gingkobilobin 2) domains harboring conserved cysteine motifs (Zeiner et al., 2023). According to the Pfam database, the DUF26/GnK2 (PF01657) domain is present in proteins involved in the saline stress response (Zhang et al., 2009) and the fungal response (Miyakawa et al., 2014). Interestingly, CRKs identified in A. thaliana are involved in multiple processes including abiotic stress response. information on the role of CRKs in response to fungal pathogens remains scarce. CRK36 was induced upon inoculation with the necrotrophic fungus A. brassicicola (LFC 2.04, P-value 2.44E-04) and negatively regulated disease resistance to this fungus, which was associated with a positive role of CRK36 in regulating cell death (Lee et al., 2017). Although CRK8 was also induced upon necrotrophic fungus inoculation in our study, our analyses of crk8 mutant plants suggest a positive role of CRK8 in disease resistance to S. sclerotiorum. Similarly, root infection with the vascular pathogen Fusarium oxysporum demonstrated that the crk10-A397T gain-of-function allele of CRK10 enhanced resistance to this pathogen (Piovesana et al., 2023). 

Overexpression of AtCRK4, 6, 28, and $^ { 3 6 , }$ enhances flg22-triggered responses, including increased ROS production, callose deposition, elevated defense-related transcripts, and stomatal closure (Chen et al., 2003; Yadeta et al., 2017; Yeh et al., 2015). Some mutants, including Atcrk2 and Atcrk36, display reduced ROS production in response to flg22 (Bourdais et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2017), whereas Atcrk28 increased ROS production (Yadeta et al., 2017). These studies suggest that the perception or regulation of ROS may be a major activity of CRKs during immune responses, in agreement with the early hypothesis that cysteine residues in the extracellular domains of CRKs may act as redox sensors, allowing them to detect and respond to changes in ROS levels (Idanheimo et al.,€ 2014; Wrzaczek et al., 2010, 2013). However, the lectin-like fold of the DUF26 domain and its 

variable surface residues point toward a diverse repertoire of ligands that remain to be identified (Vaattovaara et al., 2019; Zeiner et al., 2023). We did not detect any effect of crk8.1 and crk8.3 alleles on flg22-induced ROS burst, but we found alterations in hydrogen peroxide accumulation following S. sclerotiorum inoculation. ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production correlated with CRK8 constitutive expression suggesting a positive role of CRK8 in this process. To protect itself from oxidative damage, S. sclerotiorum actively detoxifies plant ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 } ,$ notably thanks to catalases (Huang et al., 2021; Williams et al., 2011). ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ may also be detrimental to infection by S. sclerotiorum by prematurely inducing sclerotial formation (Papapostolou & Georgiou, 2010). A positive regulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production may therefore be an important aspect of CRK8 function in plant immunity (Figure 8a). 

Plant cells protect from oxidative damage due to ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ notably by producing glutathione (GSH) which, along with glutathione peroxidase and glutathione S-transferases (GSTs), helps to detoxify ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ by reducing it to water (Hasanuzzaman et al., 2017). The conjugation of GSH with indole-3-acetonitrile and unstable intermediates is a key step in the biosynthesis of camalexin and other indole glucosinolates (Pislewska-Bednarek et al., 2018; Su et al., 2011). These molecules are toxic to bacterial and fungal pathogens, contributing to the plant defense arsenal against S. sclerotiorum (Sotelo et al., 2015; Stotz et al., 2011). We found that CRK8 knockdown impairs the expression of several GSTs upon S. sclerotiorum inoculation, leading to reduced biosynthesis of 

camalexin in spite of the upregulation of multiple camalexin biosynthetic genes. GSH may therefore be a key intermediate in CRK8’s role during QDR through both ROS production and the biosynthesis of antifungal defense metabolites (Figure 8a). 

Several CRKs play a significant role in the response to salt stress in Arabidopsis thaliana. Mutation in CRK2, also known as altered seed germination 6 (ASG6), reduced germination efficiency and root growth on salt (Bassel et al., 2011; Hunter et al., 2019). AtCRK2 interacts with PHOSPHOLIPASEa1 (PLDa1) and CALS1 involved in salt stress response, relocalizes to plasmodesmata-like structure upon NaCl treatment and is required for salt-induced callose deposition (Hunter et al., 2019). CRKs including AtCRK5, 8, 11, 28, 29, 37, and 45 were reported to display delayed germination on salt-supplemented media (Bourdais et al., 2015). Using two mutant alleles, we provide evidence that CRK8 negatively controls germination on salt, with the constitutive knockdown allele crk8.3 conferring increased salt tolerance (Figure 8a). In agreement, natural accessions lacking AT4TE56270 that typically show lower CRK8 expression had higher germination rates on salt. Studies of salt germination in recombinant inbred lines would allow us to further test the role of CRK8 natural alleles in this process. Similarly, the crk41 mutant showed enhanced salt tolerance, whereas CRK41 overexpression resulted in salt sensitivity (Zhou et al., 2023). CRK41 regulates salt stress-induced microtubule depolymerization by interacting with MPK3/MPK6 pathways, which maintain microtubule stability and confer salt stress resistance. CRK8 knockdown enhanced alternative oxidase 1D (AOX1D) expression in our assays, which may protect from proline-induced oxidative stress and facilitate recovery in saline conditions (Oh et al., 2022). CRK8 alleles lacking AT4TE56270 insertion, expressed at low levels, may therefore be beneficial in an environment with high salt concentrations. In Col-0, the salt tolerance phenotype associated with the crk8.3 mutation was relatively weak, and may not result in a major selective advantage. Reduced CRK8 expression may have stronger adaptive potential in other genetic backgrounds such as Bla-1 and Rub, which we found more sensitive to salt stress. 

Retrotransposons are important drivers of gene family expansion in plant genomes (Lee & Kim, 2014; Zhang & Gao, 2017). The CRK family in A. thaliana is organized in four major clusters on chromosomes 1 and 4, which resulted from the retention of duplicates following wholegenome duplication, as well as tandem duplications which may be the result of retrotransposon activity (Vaattovaara et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023). Recent studies have identified that retrotransposons can alter genomic expression in different manners. First, several studies have reported examples of “domesticated” transposons in A. thaliana such as in the R gene RPP7 that include a Copia-R7 

retrotransposon in its first intron, able to recruit H3K9me2 histone marks (Tsuchiya & Eulgem, 2013). Transposon activity is tightly regulated by host epigenetic machinery, being silenced most of the time in order to preserve host genome integrity (Baubec et al., 2013). In stress conditions, transposons have been demonstrated to be more active, meaning less silenced by the host (Negi et al., 2016; Wheeler, 2013). Recent studies also highlighted the genome-wide influence of TE in surrounding gene expression, in relation to DNA methylation (Hollister & Gaut, 2009; Slotkin & Martienssen, 2007; Yan et al., 2014). We can therefore hypothesize that the CRK8 promoter region resulted from transposon domestication to modulate the expression of CRK8 during stress conditions. Second, TEs can directly change the neighboring gene expression during stress conditions by adding cis-regulatory elements. For instance, the ONSEN TE found in A. thaliana (Ito et al., 2011) carried a heat response element and increased the expression of neighboring genes (Ito et al., 2016). Similarly, the LINE-transposable element EPCOT3 carries a CRE element that is bound by the transcription factor WRKY33, enhancing the expression of the CYP82C2 gene and disease resistance (Barco et al., 2019). 

Here, we reported a correlation between the presence of AT4TE56270 in the CRK8 promoter region and higher CRK8 expression in healthy plants and following inoculation by S. sclerotiorum, suggesting that AT4TE56270 might carry a stress-responsive regulatory element. In this line, we identified four W-box (TTGACY where Y represents either a T or a C) motifs located at positions 630 bp, 617 bp, and 539 bp upstream of CRK8 inside the AT4TE56270 sequence, suggesting a potential regulatory role. Especially given that WRKY33 has been reported to preferentially bind to W-box motifs within 500 bp upstream of target genes (Liu et al., 2015), we hypothesize that WRKY33 may be recruited to activate CRK8 transcription. Given the antagonistic role of CRK8 in pathogen response and salt tolerance, modulation of its expression through polymorphism in its cis-regulatory region has the potential to move plant populations along a biotic–abiotic stress response trade-off (Figure 8b). In spite of a slight induction upon fungal inoculation, the expression of CRK8 remained generally low in Col-0 plants. It is then conceivable that AT4TE56270 may have regulatory effects beyond CRK8 expression. In A. thaliana accessions, siRNAs are enriched in TE regions that show presence/absence polymorphism (Wang et al., 2013). Presence–absence polymorphism at AT4TE56270 could therefore lead to changes in siRNA silencing or regulation of DNA methylation with regulatory effects beyond CRK8. Additional work will be needed to identify and validate potential regulatory elements in the CRK8 promoter and determine the precise mechanisms by which the product of CRK8 regulates plant immune responses to fungal pathogens. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant material and growth conditions

Natural accessions used in this work were obtained from the Nottingham Arabidopsis Stock Centre (NASC). Ecotype IDs for natural accessions are listed in Table S1. The Arabidopsis mutant lines crk8.1 (SALK_101066C, stock ID N671790) and crk8.3 (GK-061E11, stock ID N405819) were obtained from the NASC and compared with the Col-0 wild-type (stock ID N1093). Plants were grown in Jiffy pots under controlled conditions at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , with a 9-h light period and a light intensity of $1 2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathsf { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \mathsf { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ 4 weeks prior to infection. To isolate homozygous mutant lines, DNA was extracted using Tris– HCl pH8 200 mM, NaCl 250 mM, EDTA $2 5 \mathrm { \ m M } ,$ SDS $0 . 5 \%$ buffer, and screened by PCR using primers F2753, R4249, and GABI08409 for crk8.3 and primers F1300, R2392, and SALK-LbTK191 for crk8.1 in F1 and F2 generations (Table S4). The position of the T-DNA insertion was confirmed by sequencing PCR fragments obtained from genomic DNA, using T-DNA left border primers (GABI8489 and SALK-LbTK191, Table S4) together with gene-specific primers (Table S4). 

# Genome-wide association mapping

The biallelic SNP database from (Atwell et al., 2010) was used for GWA mapping. About 205 540 genotyped SNPs were considered as polymorphic in at least one of 100 A. thaliana accessions considered for the analysis. Association was calculated for the 163 965 SNPs counting a minimum allele frequency $> 0 . 1$ to limit false-positives (Seren et al., 2012). The analysis was conducted using the accelerated mixed model (AMM) approach implemented in GWAPP (Seren et al., 2012). To account for multiple testing, we calculated genomewide FDR (q-values) using the qvalue R package. Association mapping was performed using the disease score index (Perchepied et al., 2010) for each accession as a phenotypic value (Table S1). 

# Analysis of CRK8 locus diversity

Sequences of AT4TE56270 were retrieved from http://signal.salk. edu/atg1001/ and https://1001genomes.org/ corresponding to chromosome 4 region 12 129 281–12 131 294 (Table S2). The proportion of N’s or gaps in this region was used to determine the extent of deletion. For classification into presence/absence categories, AT4TE56270 was considered absent for $> 7 0 \%$ of gap or missing positions. In case of disagreement between SALK and MPI data for one accession, precedence was granted to the MPI data. 

For dating the AT4TE56270 insertion, divergence from AT1TE86360, the closest full-length paralog, was assessed using pairwise sequence alignment. Out of 5073 aligned positions, there were 2161 aligned sites with 55.53 transitions and 81.44 transversions per Kbp. These metrics yielded a Kimura K2P of 0.1514 (Kimura, 1980). Considering a substitution rate of 1.5E-8 per site per year (Koch et al., 2000), the age of insertion was dated to 5.045 million years. To generate whole-genome SNP phylogeny, .vcf files were downloaded from https://1001genomes. org/data/GMI-MPI/releases/v3.1/intersection_snp_short_indel_vcf/ for each accession and merged using bcftools. In bcftools, indels were normalized and missing positions filled based on the TAIR10 reference genome, alleles with minimum frequency $< 1 5 \%$ filtered out and SNPs in LD with $r ^ { 2 } > 0 . 2 5$ in 1000 Kbp windows pruned out. The combined .vcf file was converted into phylip format using the vcf2phylip-2.8 python script available at https://github. com/edgardomortiz/vcf2phylip/releases. The phylogenetic tree was generated with IQ-tree v2.2.2.6 with automatic substitution model selection by ModelFinder including ascertainment bias correction, using 1000 ultrafast bootstraps with SH-aLRT. 

# RNA extraction and quantitative RT-PCR (Q-RT-PCR) analyses

For RNA extraction of S. sclerotiorum infected A. thaliana leaves, samples were frozen on glass plates in liquid nitrogen and tissue at the border of the infection zone was isolated by cutting the leaves with a sterile razor blade as per (Peyraud et al., 2019). Total RNA extraction was performed with the Macherey-Nagel Nucleospin RNA extraction kit following the manufacturer’s instructions. One $\mu \mathfrak { g }$ of total RNA was used for cDNA synthesis in a $2 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \vert$ reaction according to the Transcriptor Reverse Transcriptase Roche protocol, using $0 . 5 ~ \mu \up$ of SuperScript II reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen), $1 \ \mu \mathfrak { g }$ of oligo(dT), and 10 nmol of dNTP. A 30-min DNase treatment (TURBO DNase, Ambion) was applied following the manufacturer’s instructions to eliminate genomic DNA. 

Quantitative RT-PCR (Q-RT-PCR) was performed using $2 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ of 1:10 diluted cDNAs as template. Amplifications were carried out with gene-specific primers (Table S4) on a LightCycler 480 (Roche Diagnostics) and a CFX OPUS 384 real-time PCR system (Bio-Rad). Quantitative PCR reaction was performed using the Tackyon protocol (5 pmol of each primer and $5 ~ { \mu \mu }$ of RT reaction product in a $7 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ final reaction volume). The PCR conditions were 9 min at $\mathfrak { s } \mathfrak { s } ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ , followed by 40 cycles of 5 sec at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , 30 sec at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , and 20 sec at ${ 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ . Relative expression was calculated based on the expression of the AT2G28390 housekeeping gene (Czechowski et al., 2005). Expression values are given as crossing time (Ct) ratios to gene expression of At2g28390. 

Q-RT-PCR measurements were collected from 2 to 10 technical replicates for each of 4–7 independent biological replicates. Cts ${ \tt s 4 0 }$ were discarded as failed measurements and technical replicates were averaged over each biological replicate to estimate expression value in biological replicates. Differences from the corresponding Col-0 were assessed using Student’s t-test with Benjamini–Hochberg correction. Primers used for Q-RT-PCR are provided in Table S4. 

# CRK8 expression analyses

For CRK8 transcript analyses in wild-type and mutant plants, the minimal read coverage across predicted protein-coding exons (AT4G23160.1 exons 2–8, AT4G23160.2 and AT4G23160.3 exons 4–7) was considered an estimate for the relative transcript accumulation. The specific accumulation of CRK8 transcripts was investigated by Q-RT-PCR with primers designed by (Wrzaczek et al., 2010) and primers designed to target the CRK8 proteincoding region provided in Table S4, as described above. 

# Fungal pathogen inoculation and disease scoring

Inoculations were performed on 4-week-old plant grown in Percival AR-41 L3 growth cabinets at $80 \%$ humidity with a 9-h light period at $1 2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathsf { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \mathsf { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity. S. Sclerotiorum strains were grown on PDA medium at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \complement 4$ days prior infection assays. For inoculations, an agar plug (5 mm in diameter) containing actively growing S. Sclerotiorum mycelium was placed on the adaxial surface of detached leaves and placed in a Navautron system for imaging (Barbacci et al., 2020). Detached leaves were imaged every 10 min to derive the speed of lesion growth following the infest pipeline (Barbacci et al., 2020). 

# ROS experiments

For flg22-induced ROS burst, Leaf discs (4 mm in diameter) were incubated in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ of distilled water overnight in a 96-well plate under 50–100 lmol $\mathfrak { m } ^ { - 2 } \thinspace \mathfrak { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light at $\textstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ . Water was then replaced 

by a reaction solution supplemented with $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ of flg22 or water with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ luminol and $2 0 ~ \mu \ g / \ m \mu$ horseradish peroxidase (SIGMA). Fluorescence was measured using a luminometer (Victor Nivo, PerkinElmer, Shelton, CT, USA) every minute. 

Staining with $_ { 3 - 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) was used to detect the accumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ . A. thaliana leaves were inoculated with a droplet of S. sclerotiorum suspension prepared as described in (Peyraud et al., 2019). 24 h post-inoculation, leaves were immersed in freshly prepared $0 . 1 \%$ (w/v) DAB solution (pH 3.8) and kept at room temperature in the dark overnight. Leaves were transferred into $9 5 \%$ ethanol and bleached at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 2 days to remove chlorophylls. Leaves were rehydrated using water for 10 min before observation and then leaves were scanned using the EPSON 11000XL scanner. Stained areas and intensities were determined using imageJ. ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production was estimated as the product of the stained area and intensity on each leaf, after subtracting the intensity of the leaf background. 

# RNA sequencing experiments and data analysis

Mature leaves of 4-week-old A. thaliana plants were inoculated with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ diameter agar plugs colonized or not by S. sclerotiorum strain 1980. Samples were harvested and RNA extracted as described for Q-RT-PCR experiments. The quality and concentration of RNA preparations were assessed with an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) and the RNA 6000 Nano kit (Agilent Technologies). Library preparation and mRNA sequencing were conducted at the GeT-Plage facility (INRAE, Castanet Tolosan, France) using the Illumina TrueSeq Stranded mRNA kit, $2 \times 1 5 0$ base pair reads sequencing with S4 chemistry on a NovaSeq instrument (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA). Leaf material from three plants was collected and pooled for each sample; samples were collected in three (Col-0 and crk8.1 mock) or two (other treatments) independent biological replicates. The resulting RNA-seq reads were mapped to the Arabidopsis thaliana Columbia-0 reference genome Araport11 using StringTie. 

# Global gene expression analyses

Transcripts were considered low-expressed at $\mathsf { F P K M } \leq 0 . 1$ in two or more samples, leaving 17 350 expressed ranscripts. Transcripts with the highest cumulated FPKM over all samples were considered the most expressed for each of the 14 272 genes expressed and considered in further analyses. Genes dependent upon CRK8 expression, treatment or CRK8 9 treatment interaction were determined using analysis of variance (ANOVA) in R Studio 2023.09.1 Build 494, using the aov function of the dplyr package and the model FPKM ~ CRK8 9 treatment. Considering that AT4G23160.2 was the major transcript detected for CRK8 overall and that AT4G23160.2 exons 4–7 are predicted to be protein-coding, we considered the minimal read coverage over AT4G23160.2 exons 4–7 as an estimate of CRK8 protein abundance in each sample. Samples were classified into the following CRK8 levels: low (minimal coverage in [0;0.5]), medium (minimal coverage in [0.5;1.99]), high (minimal coverage in [2;10]) and very high (minimal coverage above 10). ANOVA P-values were adjusted for multiple testing using Benjamini–Hochberg correction and factors were considered significant for adjusted P-value $< 0 . 0 0 5$ . Gene Ontology enrichment analysis was performed using the BINGO 3.0.5 module in Cytoscape 3.9.1, assessing overrepresentation of GO terms with hypergeometric tests at P-value $< 0 . 0 5$ after Benjamini–Hochberg correction, compared with GO terms in the 14 272 A. thaliana expressed genes detected in this study. Differential expression analysis was performed with DESeq2 v1.42.1 (Love et al., 2014) using the design $= \sim$ replicate $^ +$ treatment formula and contrasts between mock and 

inoculated samples for each genotype separately. Genes with| Log2-fold change $\geq 1$ and adjusted P-value $< 0 . 0 1$ in DESeq2 Wald test were considered significant for differential expression. 

# Quantification of camalexin and its derivatives

Approximately, 100 mg fresh weight (FW) of Arabidopsis leaves inoculated by S. sclerotiorum were harvested at $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ postinoculation, weighed and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. Six replicates were collected for each plant genotype. The sample with the lowest extraction efficiency for each genotype was discarded from further analysis. Samples were ground on a Retsch apparatus and extracted in 1 ml Isopropanol/water/HCl (2/1/0.002) under 30 s vortex mixing and 3 min ultrasonic mixing. About $5 ~ { \mu \mu }$ of $1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ M 3- Indoleacetic acid $^ { 1 3 } { \mathsf { C } }$ (IAA_13C) were added as spike in. About 2 ml dichloromethane were added and mixed, and the hydrophobic phase was collected following 3 min centrifugation at 14000RPM. The samples were then dried on a SpeedVac at 50 mbars, $\mathtt { 3 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ overnight. Dried samples were weighed, dissolved in $1 5 0 ~ \mu \up$ and diluted 100-fold prior to injection of $1 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ on a Sciex Qtrap 5500 UHPLC–MS instrument in positive mode, equipped with a Hypersil Gold C18 (Thermo, $1 0 0 \times 2 . 1$ , $1 . 9 \ \mu \mathsf { m } )$ column. Samples were eluted with $0 . 0 5 \%$ formic acid in water (v/v) (solution A) and $0 . 0 5 \%$ formic acid in ACN (v/v) (solution B) using $5 \%$ of B for the first min and a $5 \text{‰}$ gradient of B at $3 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { l } } ~ { \mathsf { m i n } } ^ { - 1 }$ for min 1–8. Multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode was used on $\mathsf { n } / 2 \ 2 0 1 > 5 9$ , $2 0 1 > 8 9$ , $2 0 1 > 1 1 6$ . Camalexin was detected at 7.6 min. Two standards of $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of camalexin (SML1016; Sigma-Aldrich, Saint-Louis, MO, USA) at $2 5 . 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ M were run. Camalexin and camalexin derivatives quantities were derived by normalizing peak areas with that of the IAA_13C spike in and by FW of each sample, assuming equal extraction for all derivatives in a given sample. Results from different experiments were then pooled together and corrected using the Lsmean package (Rstudio 4.4.2) with the design $=$ ~genotype + experiment to avoid individual experiment bias. Statistical tests were done using ANOVA followed by a post-hoc Tukey test. 

# Germination assays

Germination assays were performed by placing seeds in squared Petri dishes (99 seeds per line, three independent biological replicates) on MS medium containing $1 \%$ sucrose, $1 \%$ agar, buffered to ${ \mathsf { p H } } ~ 5 . 7$ with KOH, with or without $1 2 0 \mathrm { \ m m \ N a C l ; }$ under a 16-hday length $( 5 0 - 1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } .$ , temperature: $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ ). A seed was considered germinated when the two cotyledons were visible. For each line, the germination rate was expressed as the ratio (germination percentage on NaCl plates/germination percentage on control plates). Plates were scanned daily using the EPSON 11000XL scanner (Seiko Epson, Suwa, Japan).
