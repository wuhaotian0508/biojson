# Abstract

Background Diverse diseases are typically associated with perturbed microbiome homeostasis, across ecosystems such as the gut and root habitats. Clubroot, which is caused by the devastating soil-borne pathogen Plasmodiophora brassicae, is a broad-spectrum disease that infects almost all cruciferous vegetables. However, the microbial ecological and metabolic cues underlying pathogen-driven deleterious disruptions of the microbiome remain enigmatic. 

Results In this study, changes in the microbiome and metabolome of the rhizosphere and roots in susceptible (diseased and nondiseased) and resistant pakchoi plants infected with P. brassicae were investigated. Diverse potential benefcial and disease-suppressive microbial families, including Rhizobiaceae and Sphingomonadaceae, were enriched in the healthy group compared with the diseased group. Rhizobiaceae was further characterized as a core driver family between the healthy and diseased groups. Reductionist-based strain validation studies further confrmed that Rhizobium sp. 25F3 showed drastic disease-suppressing activity in soil. The integrated metabolome‒microbiome correlation analysis revealed that phenolic acids were negatively correlated with the relative abundance of Rhizobiaceae. We further confrmed that genes related to phenolic acids were upregulated in diseased roots and that two phenolic acids suppressed benefcial Rhizobiaceae growth and accelerated P. brassicae infection in pakchoi. 

Conclusions Upon P. brassicae infection, signifcant diferences in the microbiome and metabolome were observed between diseased and healthy plants, as well as between resistant and susceptible varieties. Rhizobiaceae is dominant in the root microbiome and acts as a keystone family afected by P. brassicae infection. P. brassicae-induced phenolic acid metabolites selectively inhibit the growth of benefcial Rhizobium sp. 25F3 while promoting P. brassicae bursts in pakchoi. # Background

Accumulating evidences have shown that plants can potentially “cry for help” to enrich benefcial microbes under biotic and abiotic stresses [1–6]. However, successful pathogen invasion will eventually occupy the rhizosphere niche and trigger a shift towards a deleterious microbiome state, characterized by reduced diversity or dysbiosis, as observed in both the gut microbiome and root-associated microbiome [7–12]. Although many studies have focused on the characterization of benefcial metabolites that shape benefcial microbiomes [13–15], the host molecular and metabolic cues associated with deleterious changes in the microbiome during pathogenesis remain largely unstudied in the feld. Our previous studies indicated that plant genetic regulators are potentially involved in orchestrating benefcial microbiome changes under stress [16, 17]. Further understanding how the microbiome becomes “unhealthy” during disease is equally important to understanding how the host maintains a “healthy” microbiome under normal conditions, and will guide novel approaches to decreasing disease incidence and severity. 

Plasmodiophora brassicae (P. brassicae) is a devastating and broad-spectrum obligate protist pathogen that causes clubroot disease in almost all cruciferous plants in more than 80 countries [18]. Clubroot is considered a “cancer”-like disease of Brassica rapa crops including pakchoi, Chinese cabbage, caixin, and diverse leafy vegetables [19]. P. brassicae infects plants via its spores, which colonize and proliferate in the cortex. Tis infection leads to root enlargement, the formation of galls [20, 21], and severe inhibition of plant growth and development [22]. Dormant spores released from mature galls can survive in soil for decades, which causes long-term contamination of agricultural soil and makes this soil-borne disease extremely difficult to control [23]. In Canada， 200.000 hectares of land have been reported to be infected with $P .$ brassicae [24]. Approximately 3.2–4.0 million ha of cruciferous crops are infected with P. brassicae every year in China, causing yield losses of $2 0 { - } 3 0 \%$ [25]. Although $P .$ brassicae is becoming an increasing threat to global leafy vegetable and canola production, how P. brassicae infection reshapes the root microbiome and whether benefcial microbes can be used to antagonize this disease are not fully understood. 

Te plant microbiome acts as a frontline barrier and plays a crucial role in predicting disease incidence and 

severity [26–28]. Identifying changes in keystone taxa related to avoiding disease outbreaks has enormous implications for maintaining a healthy microbiome. For instance, the colonization of benefcial Sphingomonas in rice seeds predetermines the disease outcome and confers cross-generation resistance to seed-borne pathogens [29]. High abundances of Lactobacillus and Leifsonia contribute to induced resistance to false-smut disease in diseased and nondiseased plants [30]. Benefcial microorganisms can suppress disease through direct competition, the activation of induced systemic resistance, or the production of resistance-related metabolites [30–33]. In addition to altering the abundance of benefcial microbes, various pathogens also alter the structure and stability of microbiome co-occurrence networks [2, 34]. A network analysis during disease infection could help identify keystone microbial members essential for the microbiome structure and for potential defense against pathogens [26]. 

Plants secrete approximately $2 0 \%$ of their photosynthetic carbon sources into the rhizosphere as root exudates, a process that renders the root-associated microbiome one of the most diverse and complex microbial communities on Earth [35, 36]. Tus, plant rootsecreted metabolites play essential roles in shaping the root microbiome structure [37, 38]. Numerous researchers have postulated that specifc root metabolites can act as “prebiotics” to reshape the microbiome under diverse stresses, such as nutrient deprivation [39], drought [40], salinity [41], and soil borne diseases [13, 14, 42, 43]. Interestingly, recent studies have also indicated that changes in root exudates from diseased plants can be a double-edged sword that is potentially related to deleterious changes in the microbiome or even promote disease [44, 45]. Although great eforts have been made to identify potential “prebiotic” root metabolites, we know very little about the metabolic cues underlying deleterious changes in the microbiome, such as metabolites that promote disease progression and decrease the abundance of benefcial keystone microbes. 

In this study, we noticed that the susceptible pakchoi variety (Xialing, XL) showed variations in $P .$ brassicae resistance in the same feld, providing a research system for comparing the diferences in the microbiome between diseased and nondiseased plants. We also included an elite commercial pakchoi variety (Shenkang, SK) developed by our laboratory as a resistant control group to 

compare changes in the microbiome and metabolome landscape. We hypothesized that root microbiome differences contribute to variations in disease incidence and that integrated analyses of the microbiome and metabolome could provide critical insights into the shifts in the microbial community and metabolic reprogramming induced by P. brassicae infection. Te aims of this study are to (1) systematically elucidate the changes in the root and rhizosphere microbiome in healthy and diseased pakchoi plants and characterize keystone benefcial microbes with the potential to control disease; (2) reveal metabolic changes in root exudates following P. brassicae infection in resistant or susceptible (diseased and healthy) pakchoi plants; and (3) characterize the potential causal metabolite changes associated with pathogeninduced microbiome dysbiosis. 

# Results

# Defning the physiological and phenotypic changes in resistant and susceptible pakchoi varieties during P. brassicae infection

We selected the susceptible (XL) and resistant (SK) varieties to study changes in the microbiome following $P .$ brassicae infection (Extended Data Fig. 1). We found that compared with the resistant SK plants, most plants from the susceptible variety developed typical disease symptoms (XL_Diseased group, XL_D) including massive substantial accumulation of pathogens, shorter roots, fewer lateral roots, and visible gall formation (Fig. 1a–e). We also measured several critical commercial quality related indices, including vitamin C (VC), total soluble protein, and chlorophyll contents. Te VC content of the XL_D decreased by $2 0 . 9 \%$ compared with that of the SK_Healthy group (SK_H) (Fig. 1f), whereas the total soluble protein and chlorophyll contents did not change signifcantly (Fig.  1g, h). Tese fndings suggest that $P .$ brassicae infection causes systematic damage to the roots and decreases the quality of pakchoi leaves. However, the disease-resistant variety (SK) possessed a stronger ability to resist P. brassicae infection. 

# P. brassicae diferentially afects the root‑associated microbiomes of susceptible and resistant varieties

Interestingly, although we conducted the same infection procedure for all the plants (Extended Data Fig.  2), a small proportion (approximately $8 \%$ ) of the susceptible variety displayed nondiseased phenotypes similar to those of the disease-resistant SK plants without visible gall formation. Considering that subtle diferences in the microbiome can afect or even predetermine the disease outcome [27, 29], we hypothesized that there might be potential changes in the microbiome related to resistance in XL_H and XL_D, and the resistant variety SK. 

We thoroughly investigated changes in root-associated microbiomes following P. brassicae infection by systematically surveying the microbiome compositions of the bulk soil, rhizosphere, and root compartments (Supplementary Table  1). A total of 30,684 bacterial amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) were obtained from 48 samples of the roots and rhizosphere of pakchoi (Supplementary Table 2). Notably, we found that the Chao1 index of the resistant variety SK (SK_H) was signifcantly higher than those of both the diseased (XL_D) and healthy (XL_H) plants from the susceptible variety in the rhizosphere microbiome (Fig. 2a); moreover, the Chao1 index of the root microbiome samples was slightly, but not signifcantly, higher in SK_H. 

We further examined changes in $\beta$ diversity using the Bray‒Curtis distance with principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) (Extended Data Fig.  3). We detected signifcant diferences and drastic separation among the three groups in the rhizosphere microbiome samples but not in the root microbiome compartment (Fig. 2b). Additionally, the distance between SK_H and XL_D was greater than that between XL_H and XL_D in the rhizosphere (Fig.  2b; Supplementary Table  3). Tese results demonstrated that diseased and nondiseased susceptible lines, as well as resistant pakchoi, present diferent microbiome compositions following P. brassicae infection, which potentially contributes to disease incidence and resistance. We analyzed the relative abundances of the top 20 families across diferent samples and compartments. Notably, the relative abundance of Rhizobiaceae was dominant in root microbiome samples, accounting for more than $3 0 \%$ of the total abundance in both the XL_H and SK_H groups and was signifcantly higher than that in the XL_D roots (Fig.  2c). Te relative abundance of Rhizobiaceae in the rhizosphere samples from the XL_H group was also signifcantly higher than that in the XL_D group. Te relative abundance of previously reported, potential disease-suppressing Sphingomonadaceae [29, 42] was also consistently higher in both the root and rhizosphere samples from the SK_H group than in those from the XL_D group (Fig. 2d, e).

# Validation of the disease‑suppressing efects of benefcial Rhizobium strains on P. brassicae

Tese fndings collectively indicate that Rhizobiaceae, particularly Rhizobium in particular, 

represents a potential plant-benefcial microbial genus that was characterized in our study.

# P. brassicae infection triggers metabolome reprogramming associated with changes in the root microbiome

Plants can modulate changes in root exudates under stress, which in turn infuence the surrounding root microbiota [24, 52]. We performed a nontargeted metabolomic analysis of all the rhizosphere soil samples from healthy and diseased pakchoi plants. A total of 2271 compounds were identifed (Supplementary Tables  7–9). Orthogonal partial least squares-discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) revealed that $P .$ brassicae infection signifcantly $( P { < } 0 . 0 1 )$ altered the root exudates of both pakchoi varieties (Fig.  5a). We frst analyzed the metabolic profles of rhizosphere soils from healthy and 

diseased plants, and identifed 1070 diferentially abundant metabolites (DMs) based on variable importance in projection (VIP) scores $( > 1 . 0 )$ and fold changes (FDR adjusted $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ , $| \mathrm { l o g 2 F C } | { > } 0 \rangle$ ) in relative abundance (Supplementary Table 10). We performed K-means clustering on these DMs using their relative abundance profles to explore phenotype- or variety-specifc enrichment patterns, resulting in 6 clusters (Fig.  5b). Tis cluster number was determined by the natural grouping of abundance patterns, which enabled a clear discrimination of metabolite accumulation trends associated with diferent phenotypes or varieties. We found that the DMs in cluster 2, which were enriched in the rhizosphere of nondiseased plants (XL_H and SK_H), were mainly alkaloids and amines (Fig. 5c). However, DMs specifcally enriched in diseased (XL_D) plants (cluster 6) belonged to a wide range of classes such as alcohols, alkaloids, and amines (Fig.  5c). Our results suggest that $P .$ brassicae infection diferentially alters root exudate profles in diseased/nondiseased and resistant plants. 

We further explored the potential metabolites that were signifcantly correlated with the relative abundances of three key driver families that were detected in the Net-Shift analysis (Fig. 3c). Our results revealed that the relative abundance of Rhizobiaceae was negatively correlated with four classes of diferentially abundant metabolites, including phenolic acids, nucleotides and derivatives, free fatty acids, and polyamines (Fig.  5d–g). Moreover, Flavobacteriaceae was positively correlated with the 20 most abundant metabolite classes, such as benzene and substituted derivatives, and organic acids (Extended Data Fig. 8). 

# P. brassicae‑induced phenolic acid biosynthesis inhibits the growth of Rhizobium sp. 25F3 to accelerate clubroot in pakchoi

As strains belonging to the family Rhizobiaceae confer disease-suppressing efects and are key driver taxa in the $P .$ brassicae-infected microbiome, we delved into our metabolome data to characterize potential metabolites afecting Rhizobiaceae abundance. Among those metabolites correlated with Rhizobiaceae abundance (Supplementary Table 11), phenolic acids are well-known major plant allelochemicals secreted by donor plants to suppress the germination and growth of their susceptible neighbors [53, 54]. We utilized Rhizobium sp. 25F3 for a chemical feeding analysis to test whether phenolic acids regulate the relative abundance of Rhizobiaceae because of the drastic disease-suppressing efects of Rhizobium sp. 25F3 on soil. Four phenolic acids signifcantly accumulated in the diseased plants when Rhizobium sp. 25F3 growth medium was added, and the results showed that p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid exhibited dosedependent growth inhibition of the strain (Extended Data Fig. 9). p-Coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid biosynthesis involves the phenylalanine metabolic pathway [55, 56]. Our analysis revealed that the expression of PAL1/2, C4H, and 4CL was signifcantly higher in the susceptible variety (XL_D and XL_H) than in the resistant variety SK_H. In contrast, CCR expression did not difer significantly between in XL_H and SK_H but was noticeably elevated in XL_D (Fig. 6a). We then determined the absolute physiological concentrations of p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid in the rhizosphere soil of XL_D, XL_H, and SK_H plants. As shown in Fig. 6b, the concentrations of p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid were generally higher in the rhizosphere of susceptible plants than in that of resistant plants. Specifcally, in diseased XL_D plants, the levels of p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid reached $0 . 3 4 6 ~ \mathrm { n g / g }$ and $3 . 3 8 6 ~ \mathrm { n g / g } ,$ respectively, both of which signifcantly exceeded the levels in resistant SK_H plants. Moreover, under noninfected conditions, the background total phenolic acid content in susceptible XL 

plants was slightly higher than that in resistant SK plants (Supplementary Table 12; Extended Data Fig. 10). Tese results suggested that p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid may indeed afect the regulatory efect of benefcial Rhizobium on clubroot resistance in pakchoi. We tested this hypothesis by performing validation experiments to evaluate the efects of p-coumaraldehyde, eudesmic acid, and Rhizobium sp. 25F3 on clubroot resistance. Our results showed that Rhizobium sp. 25F3-mediated resistance was almost completely suppressed by physiological concentrations of p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid $( 0 . 3 4 6 ~ \mathrm { n g / g }$ and $3 . 3 8 6 ~ \mathrm { n g / g }$ , respectively). Notably, when the phenolic acid concentration was decreased by $5 0 \%$ relative to physiological levels, the increase in pathogen-induced symptoms mediated by phenolic acid treatment  was signifcantly attenuated (Fig.  6c and d). Collectively, these fndings demonstrate that p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid, which are induced by the clubroot pathogen P. brassicae, can indeed inhibit Rhizobium sp. 25F3-mediated clubroot resistance in pakchoi. 

# Discussion

Te plant-associated microbiome acts as the second genome of plants, enhancing the genetic and metabolic functional potential of plants [57]. Microbiome engineering is considered a promising technology for the second green revolution in agriculture [58, 59]. A thorough understanding of the microbial ecological processes and metabolic cues related to healthy and diseased microbiomes is essential for precisely manipulating microbiome structure for host health [32, 60]. In this work, we conducted comprehensive analyses that integrated rootassociated metabolic dynamics and microbiome differences, particularly in key microbial taxa, following $P .$ brassicae infection in diseased and nondiseased susceptible pakchoi plants, with a resistant variety serving as a control. Our work provides a research paradigm for the use of reductionist-based prediction and characterization of benefcial microbes in the microbiome to efectively control clubroot disease. 

In this work, we integrated microbial relative abundance and network interaction analyses, as well as reductionist-based strain characterization and validation studies. In our previous study, we isolated and sequenced 1479 root-associated microbes from roots grown in natural soil [16] and provided a library for reductionistbased functional validation and mechanistic studies of these strains. We found that the healthy and diseased plants presented substantial diferences in their microbiome composition, network stability, and driver taxa, supporting the signifcant role of the microbiome in regulating disease resistance to P. brassicae. Our data revealed that Rhizobiaceae was among the most keystone 

family during $P .$ brassicae infection for several reasons: (1) Rhizobiaceae was dominant in the root microbiome $( > 3 0 \% )$ ; (2) its relative abundance was signifcantly enriched in the root microbiota of nondiseased plants; (3) it was consistently identifed as the key driver family in several groups; and (4) diverse Rhizobiaceae strains decreased the $P .$ brassicae abundance in roots, as inoculation with the Rhizobium sp. 25F3 strain resulted in $9 4 \%$ healthy rates after P. brassicae infection. Our recent work also suggested that Rhizobiaceae exerts strong protective efects on abiotic stress in the model Cruciferae plant Arabidopsis [16]. Collectively, these fndings suggest that Rhizobiaceae might be a core benefcial family that is critical for plant health under either biotic or abiotic stress, which is also consistent with the claim that root growth promotion is a conserved trait of the commensal Rhizobiales [61]. Extensive work is needed in the future to elucidate the mechanisms underlying the benefcial efects of Rhizobiales on nonleguminous plants. 

Maintaining a healthy microbiome is essential for preventing disease outbreaks. Plant immunity, ROS signaling, certain metabolites, and hormone signaling are required to maintain microbiome homeostasis [37, 62– 64]. However, we know very little about the metabolic mechanism underlying dysbiosis during disease outbreaks. Notably, our data support a clear causal direction: P. brassicae infection acts as the initial trigger for microbiome dysbiosis rather than a shift in the microbiome preceding disease. Tis model is confrmed by the observations that pathogen infection directly induces phenolic acid biosynthesis, which in turn suppresses the growth of benefcial Rhizobiaceae (a keystone family for healthy microbiomes) and creates a positive feedback loop to exacerbate disease (Fig. 6). Upon pathogen challenge, the nondiseased/healthy plants maintain a high Rhizobiaceae abundance and stable microbiome networks (Figs. 2 and 3), further confrming that microbiome shifts are a consequence of pathogen infection. We also propose that Rhizobiaceae is a family driving the network shift when pakchoi is infected with P. brassicae (Fig.  2, Fig.  3, Supplementary Table 4). Tus, elucidating the metabolic cues afecting Rhizobiaceae abundance can provide critical insights into the factors regulating a healthy microbiome status. Our integrated metabolome‒microbiome analysis revealed that the abundance of Rhizobiaceae was negatively correlated with the levels of nucleotides and derivatives phenolic acids in DMs. 

Intriguingly, two phenolic acid metabolites, namely p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid, inhibited Rhizobium sp. 25F3 growth and promoted $P _ { \ast }$ brassicae infection in pakchoi (Fig.  6). Tese fndings indicate that these phenolic acids might be among the triggers for deleterious changes in the microbiome upon P. brassicae 

infection. Tis result raises an interesting question about why metabolites produced by plants are harmful to benefcial microbe colonization but slightly promote disease. One potential explanation is that both biotic and abiotic stresses promote the synthesis of a variety of phenolic compounds (e.g., p-coumaraldehyde) to form defensive lignin [65]. Pathogenic P. brassicae has evolved the ability to tolerate or even metabolize potentially toxic phenolic compounds because they frequently encounter accumulation of phenolic compounds during pathogenesis, while benefcial microbes rarely utilize toxic phenolic compounds under healthy conditions. More interestingly, phenolic acids such as p-coumaric acid and benzoic acids are also known for their function as allelopathic compounds [53, 54], which are usually produced to suppress the growth of neighboring plants, with potential ecological implications [66]. Phenolic acids also include the best studied immune hormone salicylic acid (SA) and its derivatives, which have been reported to inhibit global translation [67] and exhibit an allelopathic function to suppress seed germination. Tus, our fndings reveal a mechanism by which P. brassicae exploits a “loophole” in which host-metabolized phenolic acids disrupt benefcial rhizosphere microbial communities, thereby infecting the plant host (Fig. 7). Considering that the total phenolic acid concentration can be relatively high in plants and thus in agricultural ecosystems [67–69], future studies are needed to evaluate the ecological and environmental efects of phenolic acids (and potentially other allelopathic molecules) on plant‒microbiome interactions in agriculture. 

# Conclusions

Tis study demonstrated substantial diferences in the community composition, network structure, and robustness of the root and rhizosphere microbiomes among diseased and nondiseased/healthy susceptible plants, and a resistant variety (Fig.  7). Rhizobiaceae was dominant in the root microbiome and enriched in the resistant and nondiseased plants. It is also a keystone driver family between healthy and diseased plants, indicating its critical role in maintaining a healthy microbiome and suppressing disease. Trough a reductionist-based approach, we validated the ability of Rhizobiaceae to decrease P. brassicae abundance in soil, and Rhizobium sp. 25F3 showed a strong biocontrol efect on soil with application potential. P. brassicae infection induced the synthesis of phenolic acids (PAs p-coumaraldehyde and eudesmic acid), suppressed benefcial Rhizobium sp. 25F3 growth, and promoted P. brassicae infection in pakchoi. Our work also provided a metabolic explanation for the deleterious changes in the microbiome upon P. brassicae infection. Overall, our study revealed the microbial ecological and 

metabolic cues underlying $P .$ brassicae-induced microbiome dysbiosis and guided the discovery of disease-suppressing keystone benefcial microbes. 

# Methods

# Site selection and experimental description

We chose the Shanghai Shixin vegetable planting base (31° 07′ N, $1 2 1 ^ { \circ } \ 0 3 ^ { ^ { \prime } } \ \mathrm { E }$ ), Shanghai, China, as the study site to study the efect of clubroot disease on green vegetables. Te soil utilized in this study is classifed as yellow‒brown soil, with the following fundamental physicochemical properties: a soil organic matter content of $2 . 1 7 \%$ , total nitrogen content of $0 . 1 3 \%$ , total phosphorus content of $0 . 5 2 ~ \mathrm { g / k g }$ , and total potassium content of $1 0 . 6 5 ~ \mathrm { g / k g }$ . Te diseased nursery has long been aficted by $P .$ brassicae infestation for multiple years, with a spore density of approximately $5 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } P .$ brassicae spores per gram of soil. We chose the same diseased nursery $( 4 \times 4 0 \ \mathrm { m } )$ for the experiment, and the diseased nursery was divided into two equal parts to plant the susceptible variety “Xialing” and the resistant variety “Shenkang” of pakchoi. A schematic diagram of the experimental design is shown in Extended Data Fig. 1. 

# Plant and sample collection

For feld-grown pakchoi, seeds were sown directly in a $P .$ brassicae-infected nursery in November 2022. Te plants were subsequently maintained under standard management practices, including regular watering and fertilization, with harvesting and sampling conducted in January, 2023. We collected composite samples (rhizosphere soil and roots) from each plot, and the sampling diagram is shown in Extended Data Fig. 1. Bulk soil from P. brassicae-noninfected nurseries (healthy) (BS_H) and bulk soil from P. brassicae-infected nurseries (diseased) (BS_D) were used as controls, and the rhizosphere soil and root tissue of susceptible plants (diseased plants, XL_D; healthy plants, XL_H) and resistant plants (SK_H) were collected. Soil and root samples were used for the microbiome analyses, and six replicates were collected from each treatment group. 

Quantifcation of the P. brassicae biomass and physicochemical indices in Pakchoi 

Te root tissue and rhizosphere soil (BS-H, BS-D, XL_D, XL_H, and SK_H) of the pakchoi were ground, after which the DNA was extracted to quantify the biomass of P. brassicae. P. brassicae biomass was determined by performing a quantitative PCR analysis of the P. brassica actin gene, and the pakchoi actin gene was used for normalization [70]. Te primers used for the analysis are shown in Supplementary Table 13. Briefy, pakchoi roots were harvested, rinsed three times with distilled water, and then blotted dry with flter paper. Tree fresh roots 

from each treatment group were randomly selected to measure the length and number of roots. Te vitamin C and protein contents in the samples were determined using commercial kits (Suzhou Comin Biotechnology Co., Suzhou, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Te chlorophyll content was recorded with a SPAD‐502 PLUS chlorophyll meter [71]. 

# Field microbiome sampling and DNA extraction

Rhizosphere soil and root samples from healthy or diseased pakchoi plants following $P .$ brassicae infection, as well as the bulk soil, were harvested for 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing. A Power Soil DNA Isolation Kit (Qiagen, Germany) was used to extract DNA from all microbiome samples according to the manufacturer’s protocol. DNA samples with concentration $\scriptstyle \cdot > 2 0 \ \mathrm { n g } / \mu \mathrm { L }$ (determined using a Qubit 4.0 fuorometer) were selected for further bacterial amplicon sequencing. Te V3–V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene was amplifed using the primers 349 F ( $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ACTCCTACGGGAGGCAGCA- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ ) and 806R $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GGACTACHVGGGTWTCTAAT- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ ). Te library was prepared using the NEBNext® Ultra™ II DNA Library Prep Kit (New England Biolabs, USA). Bar-code amplicon sequencing was performed using the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform (paired-end, 250 bp). 

# Amplicon sequencing analysis

Te raw sequencing reads were initially fltered using fastp v0.14.1 [72]. Te adapter sequences were then removed using Cutadapt v4.0 [73]. Further processing of the sequencing data was performed using QIIME2 v2022.2 [74]. DADA2 was employed to quality flter, denoise, remove chimeras, and generate unique amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) and a feature table [75]. ASVs were taxonomically annotated using a pretrained naive Bayes classifer according to the SILVA database (release 138) [76, 77]. ASVs that were assigned to chloroplasts and mitochondria were considered host contaminants and were discarded. ASVs present in more than 3 samples were retained for the downstream analysis. 

# Analysis of rhizosphere soil metabolomic profles

Rhizosphere soil samples from plants with the XL_D, XL_H, and SK_H phenotypes were collected for metabolite extraction. A total of $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of soil sample was placed into a Falcon tube to which $5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of a $7 0 \%$ methanol solution was added. Te samples were vortexed for 3  min with steel balls and then subjected to ultrasonication in ice water for 10  min. After an incubation at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { \ m i n }$ , the samples were then centrifuged at $1 2 , 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r / m i n }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 5 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . All the supernatant was transferred to a new Falcon tube and 

incubated at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. After centrifugation at $1 2 , 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r / m i n }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , the supernatant was collected for the liquid chromatography‒mass spectrometry (LC‒MS) analysis. 

Te LC‒MS analysis was performed on a Shimadzu LC‒30A system (paired with TripleTOF $6 6 0 0 + { } _ { ; }$ , SCIEX), using an ACQUITY UPLC HSS T3 column $. 1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $2 . 1 \AA ^ { * } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , Waters). Milli-Q water containing $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (solvent A) and acetonitrile containing $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (solvent B) were used as the mobile phases, and all the solvents used were LC‒MS grade. Te column temperature was $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Te fow rate was set at $0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the injection volume was 4 $\mu \mathrm { L }$ . Te capillary temperature was set at $5 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ $( \mathrm { E S I + } )$ or $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (ESI-). Te gradient elution procedure started with $9 5 \%$ A and $5 \%$ B. Within 5  min, a linear gradient to $3 5 \%$ A and $6 5 \%$ B was applied. Within 1  min, a linear gradient to $1 \%$ A and $9 9 \%$ B was programmed and maintained for $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . Te composition of the mobile phase was subsequently adjusted to $9 5 \%$ and $5 . 0 \%$ B within 0.1 min and maintained for $2 . 4 \mathrm { m i n }$ [16]. 

For mass spectrometry (MS) acquisition, the instrument was set to acquire data over the $\mathbf { m } / \mathbf { z }$ range of 50–1250 Da, with an accumulation time of $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m s } }$ for the TOF MS scan. Te product ion scan parameters included a mass range of 50–1250  Da and an accumulation time of $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m s }$ . Te collision energy was set at $3 0 \ \mathrm { V } ,$ and the resolution was set to UNIT. Te ion source parameters were as follows: ion source gas 1 (GAS1), 50 psi; ion source gas 2 (GAS2), 60 psi; curtain gas (CUR), 35 psi; temperature (TEM), $5 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ ; declustering potential (DP), $8 0 \mathrm { ~ V ~ }$ ; and ion spray voltage foating (ISVF), 5500  V. Te mass spectrometry (MS) data were acquired using Analyst® TF 1.17.1 software (Sciex, Concord, ON, Canada) in information-dependent acquisition (IDA) mode. 

Raw MS data were converted into mzXML fles using ProteoWizard software [78]. Peak selection, peak alignment, and retention time correction were performed using the xcms package in R [79]. Peaks with detection rates less than $5 0 \%$ in each group of samples were discarded. Metabolites were identifed and annotated by querying databases, including the self-built database MWDB (compared to local standards), integrated public databases, Metlin (http://metlin.scripps.edu/index. php), HMDB (https://hmdb.ca/), KEGG (https://www. kegg.jp/), Mona (https://mona.fehnlab.ucdavis.edu/), and MassBank (http://www.massbank.jp/). Additionally, AI prediction databases based on competitive fragmentation modeling and metDNA were used [80, 81]. After normalization for total peak area intensity, the feature table of the metabolites was used for the downstream analysis.

# Experiments assessing the inhibition of clubroot disease using specifc microbes

For rhizobial colonization, pakchoi seedlings were frst germinated and subsequently grown on MS medium for 5 days. Teir roots were then soaked in a Rhizobium suspension. Rhizobium was cultured in LB medium for $4 8 \mathrm { h } .$ . After cultivation, the Rhizobium cells were collected by centrifugation and resuspended, and their fnal concentration was adjusted to an $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 1.0 using a PBS solution. After being soaked in the Rhizobium suspension for $4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ , the pakchoi seedlings were transferred to a nutrient substrate (Dayi Agritech Co., Ltd.) that primarily consisted of peat, coconut husk, and lime. For P. brassicae inoculation, $P .$ brassicae spores were extracted and inoculated as described previously [82]. Briefy, diseased roots were homogenized in water using a homogenizer to extract P. brassicae spores, which were subsequently fltered through three layers of cheesecloth. Te concentration of the resulting spore suspension was adjusted to $2 \times 1 0 ^ { 8 }$ spores/mL, which served as the working inoculum suspension. Inoculation with P. brassicae was performed via soil drenching, with each experimental group receiving the inoculum suspension at uniform concentrations and volumes. Each experiment was conducted with three biological replicates.

Te cultures were then incubated on a shaker at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a rotational speed of 150  rpm. After $4 8 \ \mathrm { h }$ of cultivation, the absorbance of the bacterial suspension at $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ was determined to assess the growth status of Rhizobium sp. 25F3.

# Statistical analysis and visualization

Statistical analyses in this study were performed using R v4.4.1 (http://www.r-project.org/). Te vegan package in R was used to calculate the alpha diversity indices and Bray‒Curtis distances (based on rarefed data) of all the root-associated microbial communities. Principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) was performed to visualize the microbiome composition of samples from each plant group. Permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) was conducted using the “adonis” function to assess the efects of diferent factors [83]. Differences in relative abundance at the family level were detected using STAMP (Welch’s t test, Storey’s FDR correction) [84]. Diferentially abundant ASVs were detected using the trans_dif$new function from the microeco package in R, with the “Wilcoxon” (Wilcoxon rank-sum test) method and a false discovery rate (FDR) adjusted $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ [85]. ASVs present in at least two samples and whose relative abundances were greater than $0 . 0 1 \%$ within each group were used to construct the microbial community co-occurrence network. Te Spearman correlation matrix (coefcient $> 0 . 9$ , $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ ) was calculated using the microeco package in R [85]. Te topological parameters of each network were calculated using the igraph package in R [86]. Te driver families between healthy and diseased root-associated microbial communities were identifed based on the NetShift analysis, and the results were visualized on the website https:// web.rniapps.net/netshift. Te UpSetR package in R was used to visualize the driver families that overlapped among each pairwise comparison between healthy and diseased microbial communities [87]. An analysis of the 

diferential abundance of rhizosphere soil metabolites was performed using the R package MetaboAnalystR [88]. Te variable importance in projection $\begin{array} { r } { \left( \mathrm { V I P } > 1 \right) } \end{array}$ ), $P$ -value $( P { < } 0 . 0 5$ , Student’s $t$ test), and fold change $( | \log _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C } | > 0 )$ of each metabolite between each pairwise comparison of healthy and diseased rhizosphere samples were used to identify diferentially abundant metabolites (DMs). An enrichment analysis of the DMs was performed based on their annotations to KEGG pathways. Heatmaps for clustering of metabolome profles in the present study were generated using the ComplexHeatmap package in R [89]. A correlation analysis of the relative abundance of each top abundant metabolite class with the abundance of each core driver family was performed using linear regression models in the stats package in R. Te plots in the present study were generated using the ggplot2 package in R, except for specifcation [90]. For the diferential expression analysis, normality was assessed using the Shapiro‒Wilk test. Group comparisons were conducted with the Kruskal‒Wallis test followed by Dunn’s post hoc test for multiple treatment groups, or Student’s t test when only two treatment groups were compared. Signifcant diferences among treatment groups $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ are indicated by diferent lowercase letters, and asterisks denote diferences relative to the control group $^ { * } P { < } 0 . 0 5$ , ** $P { < } 0 . 0 1$ , and $^ { \ast \ast \ast } P < 0 . 0 0 1$ ).
