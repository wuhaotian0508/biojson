# A B S T R A C T

Triterpenoid saponins are naturally occurring structurally diverse glycosides of triterpenes that are widely distributed among plant species. Great interest has been expressed by pharmaceutical and agriculture industries for the glycosylation of triterpenes. Such modifications alter their taste and bio-absorbability, affect their intra−/extracellular transport and storage in plants, and induce novel biological activities in the human body. Uridine diphosphate (UDP)-glycosyltransferases (UGTs) catalyze glycosylation using UDP sugar donors. These enzymes belong to a multigene family and recognize diverse natural products, including triterpenes, as the acceptor molecules. For this review, we collected and analyzed all of the UGT sequences found in Arabidopsis thaliana as well as 31 other species of triterpene-producing plants. To identify potential UGTs with novel functions in triterpene glycosylation, we screened and classified those candidates based on similarity with UGTs from Panax ginseng, Glycine max, Medicago truncatula, Saponaria vaccaria, and Barbarea vulgaris that are known to function in glycosylate triterpenes. We highlight recent findings on UGT inducibility by methyl jasmonate, tissue-specific expression, and subcellular localization, while also describing their catalytic activity in terms of regioselectivity for potential key UGTs dedicated to triterpene glycosylation in plants. Discovering these new UGTs expands our capacity to manipulate the biological and physicochemical properties of such valuable molecules.

# 1. Introduction

Triterpenoids are chemical compounds of 30 carbon atoms made by six isoprene units $\mathrm { ( C _ { 5 } H _ { 8 } ) }$ . Their biosynthesis (Fig. 1) starts with the condensation of two molecules of isopentenyl diphosphate and one molecule of dimethylallyl diphosphate, which are then catalyzed by farnesyl diphosphate synthase (FPS) to form farnesyl diphosphate (FPP, C15) (Augustin et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2015a). Two molecules of FPP are condensed “head to head” to create a linear C30 molecule of squalene. This reaction is catalyzed by squalene synthase (SQS) (Augustin et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2015a). Squalene is oxidized to

oxidosqualene by squalene monooxygenase (SqMO) (Augustin et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2015a). Oxidosqualene is then cyclized by oxidosqualene cyclases (OSCs) to form various cyclic compounds, including β- amyrin [by $\beta$ -amyrin synthase (β-AS)], dammarenediol [by dammarenediol synthase (DS)], cycloartenol (by cycloartenol synthase), and lanosterol (by lanosterol synthase). Cyclized triterpenes are further oxidized by cytochrome P450-dependent (CYP450) monooxygenases and subsequently glycosylated by uridine diphosphate (UDP)-glucosyltransferases (UGTs) to form various plant triterpenoid glycosides, or saponins (Kim et al., 2015a, 2015b). Oleanane-type saponins, the most abundant, naturally occurring form, are derived from $\beta$ -amyrin, a product of 2,3-oxidosqualene cyclization. This may be due to either the specific functionality that is conferred by the oleanane backbone or the thermodynamic preference of 2,3-oxidosqualene cyclization into $\beta$ -amyrin when compared with other cyclization end products (Augustin et al., 2011). Despite the abundant production of pentacyclic triterpenoids, such as $\beta$ -amyrin, in several genera of Brassicaceae (e.g., Arabidopsis thaliana; hereafter Arabidopsis), only B. vulgaris is capable of triterpenoid saponin biosynthesis within family (Augustin et al., 2011; Nielsen et al., 2010). Therefore, precursors of triterpenoid saponins are widely produced among plants but not the corresponding saponins themselves. This might be correlated with the presence of some downstream enzymes such as UGTs in certain plants.

Of particular value among the triterpenoid saponins are ginsenosides, which are produced exclusively in Panax species as triterpenoid glycosides that are part of their defense mechanisms. Among the 17 species within that genus, P. ginseng (Asian or Korean ginseng) and P. quinquefolius (American ginseng), are the best-selling medicinal herbs, supporting a global market of approximately $\$ 2$ billion USD (Baeg and So, 2013). Each contains the major pharmacological ingredient of ginsenosides. In all, approximately 150 naturally occurring ginsenosides have been found in Panax species (Kim et al., 2015a), with each differing in the number, linkage position, and type of sugar moieties they have. The UGTs play essential roles in synthesizing diverse types of ginsenosides by adding monosaccharides to triterpene

aglycones and triterpenoid glycosides. However, although a few UGT enzymes have been identified to glycosylate triterpene aglycones, the biochemical functions and substrate specificities of the majority of UGTs remain largely unknown.

Previous reviews have described the roles of UGTs in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites (Gachon et al., 2005; Thimmappa et al., 2014; Tiwari et al., 2016; Wang and Hou, 2009). Information is lacking about the role of UGTs in triterpene production due to the difficulties associated with substrate discovery and functional analysis of those enzymes. Here, we describe a system for classification according to substrate specificity among UGT candidates. We also present UGT groupings based on the C-terminus motif involved in sugar donor specificity. Our summary also updates the current knowledge about UGTs involved in triterpene biosynthesis, with particular focus on their tissue-specific expression, physiological functions, and inducibility by methyl jasmonate (MeJA). We also discuss regioselectivity as an important catalytic characteristic of UGTs. Our review will assist in identifying potential UGT proteins involved in the biosynthesis of bioactive glycoconjugates that can lead to improved/desirable catalytic properties. The information presented here can also help support novel approaches for manipulating the production of various types of saponins.

# 2. Overview

There are 106 recognized glucosyltransferase (GT) families, based on amino acid sequence similarities, catalytic mechanisms, and the presence of a conserved sequence motif (http://www.cazy.org/ GlycosylTransferases.html). Members of the GT family 1 share conserved sequences. This large family comprises UGTs and other types of

glycosyltransferases in bacteria, plants, animals, and viruses (Khorolragchaa et al., 2014). The UGTs catalyze the transfer of a glycosyl moiety from UDP sugars to a diverse array of substrates, including secondary metabolites (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2018). These enzymes exist in all living organisms: archaea, viruses, bacteria, fungi, and animals, as well as throughout the plant kingdom.

To determine the putative triterpene-related UGTs, we looked at expressed sequence tags (ESTs) or the amino acid sequences of peptides listed in public genome databases (such as NCBI, https://www.ncbi. nlm.nih.gov/) to identify UGTs with higher similarity to UGTs already known to function in triterpenoid metabolism (Table 1). We then used those data as the query for a local BLAST analysis. Afterward, we gathered UGT amino acid sequences from Arabidopsis that showed higher similarity to UGTs previously recognized as being active in triterpenoid biosynthesis. Those A. thaliana UGTs were used to select false positive UGTs. Applying a local BLAST, we first chose probable UGTs and, from them, we further selected UGTs with at least $3 0 \%$ sequence similarity and aligned lengths of at least 300 amino acids when compared with those previously known UGTs. After selection according to those criteria, we further analyzed their homology via MUSCLE alignment with a maximum 1000 bootstraps (Edgar, 2004a, 2004b). From that analysis, we were able to predict which UGTs could function in triterpenoid biosynthesis.

Examination of the public databases revealed 1983 putative UGTs in triterpene-producing plants. The largest numbers were found in M. truncatula (438), Glycine max (soybean; 434), P. ginseng (416), and Glycyrrhiza uralensis (265). In contrast, Araport11, the Carbohydrate-Active EnZymes (CAZy) database, indicated that Arabidopsis has only 138 UGT genes.

Using sequence analysis, Brazier-Hicks et al. (2018) have identified 120 UGT sequences in Arabidopsis, 107 of which encode apparently functional enzymes. Results from genome analysis of dicot species have indicated that the number of putative UGTs ranges from 56 in Carica papaya (Yonekura-Sakakibara and Hanada, 2011); to 137 in Linum usitatissimum; 178 in Populus trichocarpa: 180, 181, and 182 in Oryza sativa, Vitis vinifera, and soybean, respectively; and 241 in Malus domestica (Barvkar et al., 2012; Jaillon et al., 2007; Schmutz et al., 2010; Tanaka et al., 2008; Tuskan et al., 2006; Velasco et al., 2010). Our own genome-wide search of Panax species (Table 1) has predicted that $>$ 100 unique UGT-encoding complementary DNA (cDNA) sequences exist in P. ginseng, P. notoginseng, and P. quinguefolium. These may possibly be involved in the diversification of ginsenosides (Chen et al., 2011; Li et al., 2013; Luo et al., 2011; Sun et al., 2010).

Among the large number of UGT sequences revealed in the databases, only a few have been characterized in terms of their functions and catalytic mechanisms as potential triterpene-related UGTs. For example, 102 unique GT sequences were found by pyrosequencing of Brassica chinense (Bc). Among them, 49 were screened and full-length cDNAs of seven BcGTs were finally verified through either reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reactions or cloning using ${ } ^ { 5 ^ { \prime } }$ and/or ${ } ^ { 3 ^ { \prime } }$ rapid amplification of cDNA ends. Only three of those BcUGTs were proposed as candidates involved in saikosaponin biosynthesis (Sui et al., 2011). Among the 90 UGT unigenes in Siraitia grosvenorii, only five candidates – Unigene2346 (JL554668), Unigene4016 (JL554669), Unigene8672 (JL554670), Unigene15400 (JL554671), and Unigene37589 (JL554672) – were confirmed as candidates involved in mogrosides biosynthesis (Tang et al., 2011).

Because triterpene-producing plants contain numerous UGTs, we present the following detailed classification to guide further research about their evolution, catalytic activity, and substrate specificity.

# 3. Classification

Glycosylation of hydroxyl and/or carboxyl groups in triterpenoids produces various types of triterpenoids. Therefore, the chemical diversification of plants, along with land life adaptations, is reflected in

the expansion of those large UGT multigene families (Caputi et al., 2012; Yonekura-Sakakibara and Hanada, 2011). For example, triterpene UGTs have been enriched in the genome of P. ginseng during its evolution, compared with other plants (Jiang et al., 2017).

A classification system is essential when determining the possible functions of potential triterpene-related UGTs. As of Year 2003, sequence similarities have been described along with catalytic mechanisms for inversion or retention of the anomeric sugar moiety configuration, and approximately 7200 UGT sequences of Arabidopsis are now grouped into 65 families (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2018). These large UGT families (UGT71–76, UGT78–92) are encoded by approximately 120 UGT genes related to secondary metabolism. The families are subdivided into 14 groups (A–N) based on sequence alignments (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2018). Two new groups, O and P, that are not present in Arabidopsis sequences, have demonstrated massive genome loss in this species during evolution (Caputi et al., 2012).

Khorolragchaa et al. (2014) have selected 12 UGTs as the most likely candidates for triterpenoid synthesis. They are classified into eight families – UGT71, UGT72, UGT75, UGT79, UGT82, UGT93, UGT94, and UGT709 – that belong to six groups (A, E, G, L, N, and O). To date, 26 UGTs within the UGT71, 73, 74, 85, 91, and 94 families have been functionally characterized for triterpene glycosylation, as summarized in Table 2.

# 3.1. Classifications and substrate specificity

Structural analyses of plant UGTs have shown that these enzymes contain two Rossmann folds. Whereas the C-terminus motif is highly conserved and involved in binding with activated sugar donors, the Nterminus region is more variable, which suggests that it participates in binding with sugar acceptors. Using molecular modeling to investigate the structures of UGT families 71, 74, and 94, Jiang et al. (2017) have found a regio-specific conserved motif at the N-terminus that binds specifically to sugar acceptors. This motif contains key residues that may have been subjected to selection pressure during evolution to ensure a particular substrate binding.

Such sugar donor specificities are highly conserved among UGT groups. However, it is still difficult to predict the UGT specificities for a substrate, as the acceptor molecule, possibly because those substrates are much more complex and diverse, and require different UGTs with different topologies of substrate-binding pockets (Wang, 2009). Accordingly, our investigation of UGTs involved in triterpene glycosylation has focused on substrate prediction. Among all of the putative UGT sequences we selected (Table 1), 302 share similarity with triterpenerelated UGTs in soybean, P. ginseng, M. truncatula, Saponaria vaccaria, and B. vulgaris. To understand their phylogenetic positions and gain information about their potential functions, we constructed a phylogenetic tree via the Neighbor-Joining method and established the reliability of each node through bootstrap methods, using MUSCLE (Fig. 2).

Substrate specificity is assigned determined according to previous studies on functionally known triterpene-related UGTs and Arabidopsis UGTs (Fig. 2). For example, those of B. vulgaris (Bv), including BvUGT73C10, BvUGT73C11, BvUGT73C12, and BvUGT73C13, are catalytically functional at different carbon positions. However, due to their activity on common substrates of oleanolic acid, hederagenin, lupane sapogenin, and betulinic acid, they are all classified into the same group. We find it interesting that MtUGT71G1, with a dual function for triterpenoids and flavonoids, is evolutionarily close to UGTs with glycosylation activity on flavonoids. Likewise, GmUGT73F2 and GmUGT73F4 from soybean show glucosylation and xylosylation activity, respectively. These are placed in the same group because they shared the same substrate, saponin A0-αg. In addition, AT5G05860 and AT5G05870 are classified together in the UGT76 family because they encode cytokinin glycosyltransferases for cytokinin homeostasis. The substrate binding pocket of UGTs mainly consists of residues at the N-terminus, with some residues also in the C-terminus, which is adjacent to the sugar donor binding site. Among the UGTs, sequence

lengths and amino acid composition are highly variable at this pocket. Those characteristics affect the flexibility, position, and volume of the pocket, consistent with the capacity for pocket substrate recognition. For example, a loop exists between Nβ3 and Nα3 near Arg89 in MtUGT78G1, and its conformation differs among plant UGTs (Wang,

2009). In MtUGT71G1, this loop is structurally located in a different position, which makes the pocket open and larger for its high promiscuity and recognition of large triterpenes. Engineering the enzyme structure can successfully manipulate UGTs and their glycosylation patterns. Several approaches, e.g., replacement or mutation of residues at the acceptor binding pocket as well as domain-swapping to generate UGT chimeras, can improve their catalytic properties and alter substrate specificity (Wang, 2009).

# 3.2. Groupings and sugar donor specificity

# 3.2.1. Motif for plant secondary product glycosyltransferase

The 44-amino acid consensus sequence, typical of secondary metabolism plant UGTs, is known as the Plant Secondary Product Glycosyltransferase (PSPG) motif. It is found near the C-terminus of all triterpene-related UGTs as it was found by MEME in Fig. 3. Those UGTs are grouped based on their PSPG motif sequences (Fig. S1). The UDP sugar donors, such as UDP-glucose, UDP-galactose, UDP-arabinose, UDP-rhamnose, UDP-xylose, and UDP-glucuronic acid, mainly interact with the residues located at the motif. This motif is considered the site for nucleotide-activated sugar-binding (Wang, 2009). Recognition of UDP-glucose and UDP-galactose can be partially correlated with the last amino acid within the PSPG box, in which glutamine (Q) is necessary

for glucosyl transfer while galactosyl transfer activity requires histidine at this position (Caputi et al., 2012; Ono et al., 2010). Despite having glutamine as the last amino acid residue within the PSPG boxes in both anthocyanidin 3-O-glucoside- $2 ^ { \prime \prime }$ -O-glucosyltransferase (3GGT) from Ipomoea batatas (Ib) and its Arabidopsis (At) ortholog, the two function differently, with Ib3GGT showing glucosyltransferase activity while At3GGT has xylosyltransferase activity. Therefore, the Thr138 of Ib3GGT, with a major role in sugar donor selectivity, is thought to be a key amino acid residue for UDP-glucose recognition (Wang et al., 2018).

With arabinosyltransferase activity, AsAAT1 (from Avena strigosa), GmSSAT1 (soybean), and AtUGT78D3 each have a critical conserved histidine residue for sugar donor specificity, located at the end of the PSPG signature motif. Mutation of this histidine (H404) and P154 converts AsAAT1 from an arabinosyltransferase to a glucosyltransferase (Louveaua et al., 2018). Similarly, replacement of this histidine residue in AtUGT78D3 with glutamine leads to the inability to recognize UDParabinose along with a gain of ability to recognize UDP-xylose and UDP-glucose (Han et al., 2014). However, glutamine residue, as the last amino acid of PSPG, has also been found in glucuronosyltransferases, including UGT94B1 from Bellis perennis, which shows UDP-glucuronic acid:anthocyanin glucuronosyltransferase activity (Sawada et al., 2005; Osmani et al., 2008), as well as bifunctional enzymes (Ono et al., 2010).

Moreover, two amino acids of Arg350 and Ser127 are crucial for the recognition of UDP-glucuronic acid (Wang, 2009).

The PSPG motif is highly conserved at positions 1 (W), 4 (Q), 7 (I/ V), 8 (L), 12 (S/A), 18 (S/T), 19 (H), 21 (G), 23 (N/S/G), 24 (S), 27 (E), 39 (P), 43 (E/D), and 44 (Q) (Fig. 3). Residues at 22 (W), 43(E/D), and 44 (Q) are involved in the formation of hydrogen bonds with sugar groups (Osmani et al., 2009). The PSPG motif residues, which interact with different sugar moieties, may not be conserved and might be expected to vary depending on the type of sugar. All triterpene-related UGTs possess the relatively conserved PSPG motif that can influence the sugar donor specificity of UGTs for triterpene glycosylation. Both UGT activity and sugar specificity can be manipulated via structure-based enzyme engineering around that motif. For that, multiple amino acids are required by UGTs to recognize sugars, although single residues may also have a decisive role (Wang, 2009). Further investigation of UGTs with various sugar preferences is needed if we are to facilitate this manipulation of sugar donor specificity.

# 3.2.2. Groupings

Fig. 2 illustrates grouping of triterpene-related UGT candidates based on the PSPG motif binding with sugar acceptor molecules to get idea about putative substrates. In addition, Fig. S1 presents an evolutionary tree for grouping functionally known triterpene-related UGTs that considers family classifications based on the PSPG motif sequence. Those triterpene-related UGTs belong to Groups A, B, D, E, F, G, H, J, and L, several of which we discuss below.

3.2.2.1. Group A. As part of Group A, UDP-galactose:soyasapogenol B monoglucuronide-galactosyltransferase (GmUGT73P2) and UDPrhamnose:soyasaponin III-rhamnosyltransferase (GmUGT91H4) belong to one of the largest groups (along with UGT79, UGT91, and UGT94), where they are involved in glycosylation at C-3 and in soyasaponin I biosynthesis (Shibuya et al., 2010). They are the first known example of successive additions of sugars to form oligosaccharide chains in a manner similar to that observed in anthocyanin biosynthesis (Sawada et al., 2005). This is in contrast to the preforming “en bloc” transfer of sugar chains (see the review by Thimmappa et al., 2014). Group A also contains PgUGT94Q2, with glucose transfer activity to C-3 of the Rh2 and F2 ginsenosides. We find it interesting that the catalytic activities of both GmUGT91H4 and PgUGT94Q2, as well as GpUGT23 in that group (Kim et al., 2015b), are conserved in successive sugar additions but not in the first sugar attachment. Similarly, their Arabidopsis homolog, AT5G54060, converts cyanidin 3-O-glucoside to cyanidin 3-O-xylosyl $( 1  2 )$ glucoside by xylose addition as the second sugar. However, this group characteristic could be further defined through in vitro analysis that utilizes already-bound sugar substrates.

# 3.2.2.2. Group D

Members of phylogenetic Group D feature broad substrate specificity for UGTs, including those from UGT73 (Fig. 2 and Fig. S1). For example, B. vulgaris can produce feeding-deterrent triterpenoid saponins that confer resistance to the diamondback moth (Plutella xylostella), a crucifer-specific pest. We have predicted 93 UGTs in B. vulgaris, six of which are homologous to other triterpene UGTs, with all belonging to the 73 family. Moreover, UGT73C10 through UGT73C13 are involved in C-3 glycosylation of hederagenin and oleanolic acid (Augustin et al., 2012). Among the 56,044 UGTs from soybean, 39 are homologous to triterpene-related UGTs, which mostly belong to the 73 family. Therefore, that family is the best candidate group for saponin biosynthesis in B. vulgaris and soybean. Sun et al. (2017) have also identified Family 73 UGTs in the triterpene saponin-producing Hedera helix. Although that family also occurs in P. ginseng (Pg) (Jiang et al., 2017), none of its members has been functionally analyzed, possibly because only minor quantities of oleanane-type saponin exist in that species.

3.2.2.3. Group E. The UGT71 and UGT72 families belong to Group E, the largest in plants. Arabidopsis UGTs in that group recognize a wide range of substrates, including terpenoids, flavonoids, and benzoates, as well as abscisic acid (Song et al., 2015). Another example is MtUGT71G1, which has a dual function on triterpenoids and flavonoids. Other members include $\mathrm { U G T P g 1 }$ , $\mathrm { P g U G T P g 1 0 0 }$ , and $\mathrm { P g U G T P g 1 0 1 }$ , which have catalytic activity on C-6 and C-20 of the dammarenediol ginsenosides (Wei et al., 2015; Yan et al., 2014). Wei et al. (2015) have reported on the relaxed substrate specificity of UGTPg1, which can modify both protopanaxadiol (PPD)- and protopanaxatriol (PPT)-type ginsenosides in vitro. Other members of this group may be functional on several substrates, as well. Finally, PgUGTPg101 functions on both C-6 and C-20 of PPT ginsenoside to produce F1 and, through sequential glycosylation, Rg1.

3.2.2.4. Group G. The UGT709 family in Group G is active in the biosynthesis of iridoids, which are the main intermediates in the production of monoterpenoid indole alkaloids, such as those found in Gardenia jasminoides (Nagatoshi et al., 2011). This group also contains UGT85 family members that catalyze the glucosylation of not only monoterpenes but also other volatile compounds, resulting in enhanced water solubility (Yazaki et al., 2017). In Arabidopsis, this family glycosylates the primary hydroxyl groups of terpenoids (Caputi et al., 2008).

Eleutherococcus senticosus, also known as Siberian ginseng, has oleanane, noroleanane, 29-hydroxyoleanan, and lupane types of saponins. Based on their transcriptome analysis, Hwang et al. (2015) have demonstrated that UGT10 and UGT11, in the UGT85A family, are the best candidate genes for saponin biosynthesis in that plant.

3.2.2.5. Group L. Group L includes the UGT 74, 75, and 84 families, whose members can catalyze the formation of glucose ester bonds on the carboxylic groups of a variety of substrates, such as phenylpropanoids, benzoates, and auxin indole-3-acetic acid (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2018). Two members – PgUGT74AE2 and SvUGT74M1 (from Saponaria vaccaria) – catalyze glycosylation of C-3 of PPD and compound K and C-28 of gypsogenic acid, respectively (Jung et al., 2014). Root-derived oleanane-type saponin avenacins with antimicrobial activity are produced in Avena sativa (oats). In particular, UGT74H5 displays activity by N-methylanthranilic acid Oglucosyltransferase enzyme and is involved in the synthesis of avenacin A-1 in oats (Owatworakit et al., 2013).

# 4. Physiological characteristics

# 4.1. Inducibility of saponins by methyl jasmonate

Exogenous treatment with jasmonate derivatives such as MeJA influences the accumulation of saponins (Augustin et al., 2011; Rahimi et al., 2015; Rahimi et al., 2016a, 2016b). Thus, MeJA might be employed to identify putative saponin biosynthetic genes. Such experiments have been developed as a preliminary screening tool for characterizing potential UGTs involved in secondary metabolism, especially saponin biosynthesis (Fig. 4, Table 3). Upregulation of genes in the triterpenoid biosynthetic pathway and activities by their higher enzymes are combined with the function of potential UGTs to enhance saponin biosynthesis in response to MeJA (Hayashi et al., 2003; Shabani et al., 2009).

In P. ginseng, MeJA treatment stimulates the expression of UGTs and the upregulation of upstream terpenoid biosynthetic genes, including those for 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A reductase, FPS, SQS, SqMO, DS, and protopanaxadiol synthase (Kim et al., 2009; Sun et al., 2010; Jung et al., 2014; Kang et al., 2018). Among the 19 MeJA-induced UGT contig groups, only three UGTs – PgUGT71A27 (Group E), PgUGT74AE2 (Group L), and PgUGT94Q2 (Group A) – show biochemical activities toward ginsenosides. Meanwhile, UGT10 and UGT11 (UGT85A subfamily in Group G) have the highest levels of transcription after treatment (Hwang et al., 2015). However, no information is yet available regarding their catalytic activities.

Taken together, the particular impact that MeJA has on the production of certain secondary metabolites suggests that it can affect UGT enzymes with specific involvement in triterpene biosynthesis. This knowledge can be helpful when devising an elicitation strategy to engineer the production of desired triterpenoids (Fig. 5).

# 4.3. Tissue expression and subcellular level

Saponins are distributed to varying degrees among plant organs and tissues. For example, SvUGT74M1 (from Saponaria vaccaria) shows biochemical activity against oleanane-type terpenoids in distinct organs and seeds (Meesapyodsuk et al., 2007). That gene is co-regulated with an upstream β-AS gene (expressed in all tissues) even though the former is expressed only in roots and leaves (Meesapyodsuk et al., 2007). Such findings demonstrate that the downstream pathways for saponin biosynthesis, including glycosylation, are specifically regulated to provide different saponin pools in specific organs.

Tissue expression of UGTs varies among plant species (Fig. 4 , Table 4). Most putative triterpene-related PgUGT genes, such as UGT71 and UGT94 candidates, are expressed in the leaves and roots of P. ginseng (Khorolragchaa et al., 2014) while another Group E member, Gm88E3 from soybean (UDP-glucose; isoflavone-7-O-glucosyltransferase) is expressed in the roots (Noguchi et al., 2007). Candidates for triterpenoid biosynthesis are also highly expressed in the roots of G. uralensis and B. chinense (Li et al., 2010; Sui et al., 2011). Furthermore, levels of transcripts in bacoside triterpene biosynthesis, including genes for $\beta$ -AS and CYP450 monooxygenases, predominate in the roots of Bacopa monnieri when compared with the shoots (Jeena et al., 2017). For saponin biosynthesis in N. sativa, NsUGT74Q1 is expressed in all tissues, with levels peaking in the leaves and capsules, while NsUGT85P1 is expressed in all tissues except the roots (Scholz et al., 2010). These results suggest tight tissue-specific control of UGT genes in each of those species. However, further investigation is needed about the tissue-specific promoter elements or relationship between saponin accumulation and tissue preference if we are to improve our understanding of tissue-specific control of triterpene biosynthesis.

The accumulation of saponins in distinct organs due to UGT expression is also correlated with the physiological function of saponins in particular organs. For example, the higher saponin biosynthesis observed in D. pseudojaponica tubers helps protect those reproductive organs (Augustin et al., 2011). Ginsenoside precursors are transferred from the leaves to the roots of P. ginseng (Schramek et al., 2014). In the roots, ginsenoside contents of Rg1, Re, Rf, Rg2, Rb1, Rc, Rb2, and Rd are significantly higher in the periderm cells than in the cortex and stele (Jiang et al., 2017). Furthermore, ginsenoside types are continuously changing in the cross sections of those roots. This phenomenon can be explained because the accumulation of saponins is influenced by their physiological role, mode of transport, and involvement in growth and development, as well as defense mechanisms and responses to environmental challenges.

# 5. Catalytic mechanism

The CAZy family, one of the largest groups of natural productdecorating enzymes, includes triterpene-related UGTs in more complex plants (Cantarel et al., 2008). Heteroatomic glycosylation of GTs has been reported for oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur, but not for carbon (Chang et al., 2011). A conserved catalysis mechanism of O-glycosylation has been found for GTs, with the OH group being the preferred sugar acceptor. Hydroxyl or carboxyl groups of triterpenoid aglycones

can be glycosylated at one or more positions by UGTs. However, the reaction on carboxyl groups is not specific, and the same enzymes can also catalyze reactions on hydroxyl groups.

As the favored donor, UDP-glucose is greatly utilized by the UGTs characterized to date. As shown in Fig. 1, glucose is usually added as the first sugar at the glycosylation sites. The exception is soybean, a plant system with different types of sugars, including arabinose and glucuronic acid, that are present as the first sugar attached at the glycosylation sites of C-3 and C-22, by unknown UGTs. This might possibly be explained by the thermodynamic preference of glycosylation into glucose-conjugates rather than other glycosylation products.

Three main approaches have been taken to assess UGT functions: mutant isolation, protein purification and cloning, and the screening of cDNA libraries with heterologous probes. To understand UGT catalytic activities, researchers have used E. coli or yeast to measure recombinant protein expression, followed by purification and in vitro UGT enzymatic assays. However, although many UGTs have been cloned and heterologously expressed, only a few have been fully characterized in terms of catalytic activity.

# 5.1. Classification of GTs based on 3D structures and catalytic reactions

The GTs have been classified as GT-A, GT-B, or predicted GT-C, based on 3D structures and type of reaction. The protein databank (Tiwari et al., 2016) contains 108 GT crystal structures, including 40 members of GT-A, 58 of GT-B, two of GT-C, and eight unclassified (Tiwari et al., 2016). Members of GT-A have a single domain fold comprising a seven-stranded beta-sheet core flanked by alpha-helices and a small anti-parallel beta-sheet bridged via a DxD motif (or its variants, e.g., TDD, EDD, DxH, etc.). The GT-B members possess two distinct Rossmann-like domains of several parallel beta-sheets linked to alpha-helices. Their N- and C-terminus domains are connected by a linker region, thereby forming an interdomain cleft and creating the active sites. The C-terminus domain has a kinked alpha helix that extends out to interact with the N-terminus domain (Liang et al., 2015) (Fig. 6A). Finally, the GT-C members feature 8–13 predicted transmembrane segments, as well as a DxD (ExD, DxE, DDx, or DEx) motif in the first extracellular/lumenal loop that may be essential for catalysis.

Mechanisms for glycosylation reactions can be designated as either inverting (e.g., NDP-alpha-sugar-beta-glycoside) or retaining (e.g., NDP-alpha-sugar-alpha-glycoside), based on their stereochemical differences (Liang et al., 2015). Therefore, GTs have been further subdivided into four clans: inverting GT-A fold (Clan I), GT-B fold (Clan II), retaining GT-A fold (Clan III), and GT-B fold (Clan IV) (Tiwari et al., 2016). Family 1 GTs, belonging to Clan II, exhibit an inverting mechanism of catalysis and depict a GT-B structural fold. The GT-B family has a remarkable conservation at the C-terminus that is the nucleotide binding domain (Tiwari et al., 2016). Structural variations occur in the N-terminus domains, involving the helices, loops, and active sites needed to accommodate different acceptors (Breton et al., 2006). The GT-B family members feature a peptide motif, a glutamate residue (e.g., Glu378 from UGT73A5 of Dorotheanthus bellidiformis or Glu81 from SlUGT5 of Solanum lycopersicum) (Hans et al., 2004; Louveau et al., 2011), and glycine-rich loops that interact with the ribose or phosphate moieties of the nucleotide donor (Wrabl and Grishin, 2001).

We find it interesting that plant UGTs can share highly similar structures across species, even if they share relatively low sequence identities. Examples include VvGT1 (from Vitis vinifera), MtUGT78G1, MtUGT85H2, AtUGT72B1, and MtUGT71G1 (Wang, 2009). Several UGTs have resolved crystal structures, including multifunctional triterpene/flavonoid UGT71G1, multifunctional (iso)flavonoid UGT85H2, UGT78G1 from M. truncatula, anthocyanidin 3-O-glucosyltransferase Ct3GT-A from Clitoria ternatea, and flavonoid 3-O -glycosyltransferase VvGT1 from V. vinifera (Shao et al., 2005; Li et al., 2007; Modolo et al., 2009; Hiromoto et al., 2013; Offen et al., 2006). Among them, only the multifunctional triterpene/flavonoid MtUGT71G1 reacts with triterpene as substrate. The crystal structure of M. truncatula UGT71G1, which reacts to flavonoid/triterpene as substrate and binds to UDP or UDP-glucose, reveals the key residues involved in recognizing the donor substrate (Shao et al., 2005). The highly conserved catalytic base His22 is close to the glucose moiety of the sugar donor and the acceptors (Shao et al., 2005). It acts to abstract a proton from the acceptor substrate. Similarly, protein-docking and modeling has indicated that His17, His18, and His23 are the catalytic residues of SlUGT5 (UGT72 family) from Solanum lycopersicum, along with flavonoid GT from Withania somnifera and UGT85B1 from Sorghum bicolor (Hans et al., 2004; Louveau et al., 2011; Tiwari et al., 2016). A nearby conserved aspartate residue, Asp121 in MtUGT71G1 (Asp119 in VvGT1), interacts with the histidine to stabilize it and then balances its charge after de-

protonating the acceptor by forming a hydrogen bond. This results in an acceptor-His-Asp triad. The deprotonated acceptor, as a nucleophilic oxyanion, may attack the C1 carbon center of the UDP-sugar with direct displacement of the UDP moiety and then form a beta-glucosidic linkage product (Shao et al., 2005; Wang, 2009) (Fig. 6B).

In contrast, His15 and Asp125 are not important for catalysis by GmIF7GT from soybean. High catalytic activity has been found in the enzyme lacking the N-terminus 49-residues and His15, and mutants of H15A and D125A remain active (Noguchi et al., 2007). Even residues such as cysteine and arginine/serine/asparagine replace the catalytic histidine and aspartate residues (Shao et al., 2005; Noguchi et al., 2007). This histidine catalytic residue is especially critical for O-glycosyltransferase activity but not for N-glycosylation (Brazier-Hicks et al., 2007). However, further mutation studies should explore the importance of this catalytic residue for S- and C-glycosyltransferase activities.

To demonstrate substrate binding regions of UGTs, clustal X was used with default gap penalties to perform multiple alignments of UGTs (Fig. 6C). A long, narrow channel within the C-terminus domain of the UGTs accommodates UDP-glucose donors to interact with PSPG motif residues (Trp339-Gln382 in MtUGT71G1) (Fig. 6C). The last two residues of the PSPG motif (Glu381 and $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { l n } 3 8 2$ in MtUGT71G1), as well as another highly conserved tryptophan (Trp360 in UGT71G1), are the main residues interacting with the glucose moiety of the sugar donor. This is accomplished through the formation of several hydrogen bonds with its 2-, 3-, and 4-OH. In addition, Thr143 and the catalytic residue His22 in MtUGT71G1, are close to the 4- and 6-OH of glucose. Therefore, sugar recognition and binding can be determined with these residues, and sugar specificity and UGT activity may be changed by manipulating around these regions.

# 5.2. Regioselectivity

A chemical regioselective reaction occurs when the production of one structural isomer is favored over all others. When UGTs transfer sugar from nucleotide diphosphate-activated sugar moieties to small hydrophobic acceptors, they are regioselective. This means that glycosylation happens at the same position as that of acceptors rather than there being selectivity for one or numerous substrates (Lairson et al., 2008; Weadge and Palcic, 2009). Likewise, broad substrate specificity exists for UGTs such as Group D enzymes (Fig. 2), which utilize diverse substrates such as flavonoids, phenolics, betacyanin, steroids, and terpenes. One (or a few) given position(s) on related substrates are specifically glycosylated by UGTs in vitro. For example, MtUGT71G1 can glycosylate all five hydroxyls of quercetin as the acceptor and produce five different products, even though ${ } ^ { 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -O-glucoside is the major product (Wang, 2009). This is due to the critical role of amino acid residues in the binding pocket that dictate glycosylation pattern and substrate recognition. Therefore, the manipulation of residues at this site drastically changes the regioselectivity of UGTs (Wang, 2009).

A typical pattern for triterpenoids calls for the linkage of oligomeric sugar chains of two to five monosaccharides to C-3 and/or C-28. By contrast, the modifications catalyzed by UGTs, e.g., glycosylation at positions C-2, C-4, C-6, C-16, C-20, C-21, C-22, C-23, and/or C-24, are relatively rare among saponins (Augustin et al., 2011) (Fig. 7). Future in vitro biochemical analyses of more UGTs should reveal whether triterpene UGTs are mainly derived from those five regioselectivities of C-3, C-28, C-6, C-20, and C-22.

# 5.2.1. C-3

For B. vulgaris, regioselectivities indicate the specific roles of UGT73C10 and UGT73C11 (both from Group D) at the C-3 hydroxyl group of oleanolic acid and hederagenin. Through the functioning of those two enzymes, that glucose can also be transferred to C-3 of betulinic acid, a lupane-type sapogenin (Seki et al., 2015). Therefore, although diverse substrates have different functions, the regioselectivity of these UGTs is conserved.

In soybean, C-3 and C-22 are involved in glycosylation. However, the UGTs that participate in the addition of glucuronic acid to the C-3 hydroxyl group, or arabinose to the C-22 hydroxyl group, have not yet been identified. These presently unknown enzymes are exceptional UGTs with glycosylation activity, using sugars other than glucose as the first sugar at the glycosylation sites. For example, UGT73P2 (from soybean) in Group D catalyzes the addition of a galactose moiety as the second sugar to the already-bound glucuronic acid of the soyasapogenol B at C-3. Furthermore, UGT91H4 from Group A attaches a rhamnose moiety as the third sugar to the already-bound galactose (Shibuya et al., 2010).

During the ripening phase, fruits of S. grosvenorii produce a cucurbitane-type tetracyclic triterpenoid saponin mogroside from a

mogrol precursor that is catalyzed by UGTs. The number of glucoses attached to the mogrol at C-3 and C-24 significantly affects the mogroside level as well as fruit taste, with glycosylation being continuous while those fruits develop (Liu et al., 2018). Public databases indicate that this species has 10,284 UGTs. There, the strong correlation between the expression patterns of UGT candidates and those of genes involved in saponin biosynthesis, including SqMO and cucurbitadienol synthase (CS), provides evidence that their most likely role is in the conversion of the mogroside backbone into various mogrosides (Tang et al., 2011). Because of improvements in sequencing depth and higher accuracy in detecting expression patterns, researchers have been able to conduct RNA-sequencing and differential gene expression analysis of S. grosvenorii fruits to predict CS and UGT74AC1 (Group L). Enzymatic assays using heterologously expressed UGT74AC1 as a His-tag protein in E. coli have proved the enzyme specificity for C-3 hydroxyl group glycosylation of mogrols that results in the formation of mogroside IE (Dai et al., 2015).

The annual herb N. sativa, a medicinal plant, accumulates monoand bidesmosidic (one- and two-saccharide chains, respectively) triterpene saponins in its seeds. Expression of recombinant NsUGTs in yeast has revealed the glycosylation activity of UGT85P1 (Group G),

which is responsible for the sugar transfer to the hydroxyl group of C-3 in hederagenin (Scholz et al., 2010).

More than 30 different triterpenoid saponins are produced in the model legume M. truncatula, based on at least five triterpene aglycones: soyasapogenols B and E, medicagenic acid, hederagenin, and bayogenin (Huhman et al., 2005). Functional characterization of UGTs from that species has shown the specificity of UGT73K1 (Group D) for hederagenin and soyasapogenols B and E, and the specificity of UGT71G1 (Group E) for medicagenic acid, which are classified into separate substrate groups (Fig. 2). Studies of UGT71G1 have indicated that glycosylation is more efficient for isoflavones and the flavonol quercetin than for triterpenes. Our analysis has also assigned UGT71G1 to the same group of UGTs that utilize flavonoids as substrate for glycosylation (Fig. 2). This is true even though, in elicited cell cultures, UGT71G1 acts on the accumulation of terpenoids but not (iso)flavonoids (Achnine et al., 2005). Moreover, an in silico docking simulation of the MtUGT71G1 crystal structure has shown that UGT71G1 can transfer a glucose to the hydroxyl at the C-3 position (Shao et al., 2005). Based on this, we conclude that the broad substrate specificity of recombinant UGTs in vitro may not provide a comprehensive explanation for their actions in vivo, which could be correlated with substrate availability at the cellular level. Therefore, researchers must still compare catalytic specificity in vitro with the consequences of changing the level of individual enzymes in in vivo.

During the biosynthesis of P. ginseng ginsenosides, PgUGT74AE2 (Group L) catalyzes the glycosylation of C-3 hydroxyl groups in PPD and compound K, thereby synthesizing Rh2 and F2, respectively (Jung et al., 2014). Afterward, PgUGT94Q2 (Group A) glycosylates C-3 of Rh2 and F2 to produce Rg3 and Rd, respectively (Jung et al., 2014). Two other UGTs, Pq3-O-UGT1 and Pq3-O-UGT2, have been identified from P. quinquefolius (Lu et al., 2017a, 2017b). For P. ginseng and P. quinquefolius, sequence similarity is high for Pq3-O-UGT2 and PgUGT94Q2. Furthermore, RNA interference of Pq3-O-UGT2 and PgUGT94Q2 leads to a reduction in the levels of ginsenoside Rd, protopanaxadiol-type, and total ginsenosides, along with the upregulation of protopanaxadiol and protopanaxatriol synthases. In addition, Pq3-O-UGT2 catalyzes the C-3 glycosylation of Rh2 and F2 to produce Rg3 and Rd, similar to PgUGT94Q2 (Lu et al., 2017a). Meanwhile, the deduced amino acid sequence of Pq3-O-UGT1 is highly similar to that of UGT74AE2, and the same catalytic activities associated with C-3 glycosylation and Rh2 production are observed for both Pq3-O-UGT1 and PgUGT74AE2. This then suggests that UGT catalytic activity was evolutionarily conserved prior to species radiation.

Plants of G. glabra accumulate an oleanane-type saponin of glycyrrhizin in their roots and stolons. In the pathway for glycyrrhizin biosynthesis, $\beta$ -amyrin hydroxylations at the C-11 and C-30 positions are followed by two steps of glucuronidation for the hydroxyl group at the C-3 position (Sawai and Saito, 2011). Another glycosyltransferase, GuGT73F17 (Group D), has now been identified that has strict substrate specificity toward the carboxyl group at C-30/C-29 of pentacyclic triterpenoids. We believe that this is the first oleanane C-30 ester-forming glycosyltransferase to be recognized in the literature. This enzyme is also highly promiscuous, using multiple hexose and pentose sugar donors. As such, it is the first reported example of triterpene ester-forming UGTs. Therefore, it could be used as an effective biocatalyst to synthesize glycosyl ester saponins (He et al., 2018).

# 5.2.2. C-28

Two Group D members, UGT73C12 and UGT73C13, form ester bonds between glucose and the C-28 carboxyl group of oleanolic acid and hederagenin in B. vulgaris. That glucose can also be transferred to C-28 of betulinic acid, a lupane-type sapogenin, through the activity of those two enzymes (Seki et al., 2015). As described before, the catalytic function on specific positions is conserved in those UGTs.

Centella asiatica is a perennial medicinal plant that produces the triterpene saponins of centelloside, asiaticoside, madecassoside, and

sceffoleoside (Matsuda et al., 2001; James and Dubery, 2009). The UGTs in that species glycosylate asiatic acid and madecassic acid, and they transfer trisaccharide (rhamnose $^ +$ glucose+glucose) at the C-28 to produce asiaticoside and madecassoside. As a member of Group D, CaUGT73AD1 attaches a glucose at the C-28 carboxyl moiety of asiatic and madecassic acids (De Costa et al., 2017). It can also catalyze glycosylation at the C-28 carboxyl function en route to the asiaticoside triterpene saponins (Kim et al., 2017). Likewise, N. sativa UGT74Q1 (Group L) can esterify a hexose with the C-28 carboxyl group of hederagenin and α-hederin.

Among the 50,376 MtUGTs, 60 are homologous to the triterpenedecorating UGTs from Families 71, 72, 73, 74, 84, 85, 88, and 89. A microsomal UGT from M. truncatula, UGT73F3 (Group D), is active against multiple sapogenins and is known to glycosylate hederagenin at the C-28 position (Naoumkina et al., 2010). As shown in Fig. 2, this UGT is grouped with GmUGT73F2 and GmUGT73F4, which are functional on saponin A0-αg substrates. Therefore, saponin A0-αg is a possible substrate for MtUGT73F3.

Plants of S. vaccaria produce oleanane-type saponins similar to those identified in Quillaja saponaria (Rosaceae) (Meesapyodsuk et al., 2007). Public databases have indicated that the former contains 10,463 UGTs. Of those, three are homologous to triterpene-related UGTs. A full-length cDNA for SvUGT74M1 (Group L) similar to ester-forming glycosyltransferases has been isolated using a developing seed EST collection from that species. In those plants, gypsogenic acid is the most common aglycone, having hydroxy and carboxylate groups at C-3 and C-28, respectively. Enzymatic assays using heterologously expressed SvUGT74M1 have displayed glycosylation activity by oleanane triterpene gypsogenic acid at C-28 in a glycosyl ester linkage (Fig. 7). Taken together, most UGTs with regioselectivity of C-28 will glycosylate carboxyl groups of triterpenoids with an ester linkage.

# 5.2.3. C-6 and C-20

In P. ginseng, UGTs catalyze the addition of monosaccharides to triterpene aglycones, mainly at C-3 and/or C-20 for PPD-type ginsenosides or at C-6 and/or C-20 for PPT-type ginsenosides. Although several UGT enzymes with glycosylating activity on triterpene aglycones have already been identified (Jung et al., 2014; Khorolragchaa et al., 2014; Luo et al., 2011; Xiang et al., 2012; Yan et al., 2014; Yue and Zhong, 2005), a large number of UGTs that function in the diversification of ginsenosides remain unknown (indicated by question marks in Fig. 1).

Within Group E, PgUGTPg100 catalyzes the synthesis of ginsenoside Rh1 from PPT via glycosylation at C-6 (Wei et al., 2015). Furthermore, UGRdGT catalyzes the synthesis of ginsenoside Rb1 from Rd in P. notoginseng by glycosylation at C-20 (Yue and Zhong, 2005). In that same group, $\mathrm { P g U G T P g 1 }$ glycosylates the C-20 positions of dammarenediol, PPD, Rh2, and Rg3 and produces new compounds 20S-O-β-(D-glucosyl)-dammarenediol II (DMG), compound K, F2, and Rd, respectively (Yan et al., 2014). An example of UGTs functioning on multiple positions is $\operatorname { P g U G T P g 1 0 1 }$ (Group E), which has catalytic activity on both C-6 and C-20 of PPT-type ginsenosides. Future studies could use ginsenoside-decorating UGTs (question marks in Fig. 1) to help determine the various pathways for ginsenoside biosynthesis.

Southern ginseng contains 90 dammarane saponins, namely gypenosides or gynosaponins. A patent filed by Kim et al. (2015b) describes a novel UGT protein with glycosyltransferase activity in G. pentaphyllum that can transfer glucose to the C-20 position of PPD- or PPT-type ginsenosides. Within Group A, GpUGT23 converts compounds K, F2, Rd, and F1 into gypenoside LXXV, gypenoside XVII, Rb1, and notoginsenoside U, respectively. Despite its ability to use a broad range of substrates, the regioselectivity of that protein is conserved.

# 5.2.4. C-22

Group A saponins, produced in soybean hypocotyls, contain an acetylated sugar at C-22 (Shimoyamada et al., 1990). Gene mapping

has revealed that their terminal sugar diversity is controlled by Chromosome 7 in that species (Takada et al., 2010). Soybean UGT73F4 and UGT73F2, belonging to Group D, attach different sugars, e.g., xylose and glucose, to the arabinose residue of soyasapogenol A at the C-22 position (Sayama et al., 2012). Nevertheless, because they show preference to the same substrate, they are classified within the same phylogenetic branch (Fig. 2).

Overall, we can conclude that UGTs from different families and groups can have the same regioselectivity. Furthermore, UGTs with diverse substrates can share functionality at the same position in different substrates. Therefore, one can obtain a meaningful classification of triterpene-related UGTs if one considers both their substrate preferences and their regioselectivity.

# 6. Perspectives

The activities of plant UGTs, with their diverse substrate specificities, range from the synthesis of valuable glycoconjugates to the detoxification of pollutants and xenobiotics to the homeostasis of hormones such as cytokinins and auxins. This provides a promising basis for future commercial and economic applications. For example, their significant role in defense responses is highlighted in engineered crops that exhibit enhanced catalytic activity and resistance to pathogen infections. Their actions against exogenous compounds such as insecticides, xenobiotics, pollutants, and herbicides can be harnessed as an efficient way to neutralize and detoxify the detrimental effects of foreign substances in genetically engineered crops. By exploiting the physiological role of UGTs in glycosylation of phytohormones and regulation of hormone metabolism, we can possibly manipulate hormone levels and influence growth and development to create crops with more desirable phenotypic traits.

Identification of UGTs is the key to success when devising a rapid, inexpensive method for de novo biosynthesis of pharmacological saponins in heterologous systems such as microorganisms or plants. This is of great benefit when attempting to provide activated sugar donors for reactions by living cells.

The crucial involvement of glycosylation in saponin biosynthesis has led us to take advantage of numerous novel bioactive substances with evolved biological forms and functions. Such efforts present attractive opportunities for the design of new drugs. To achieve our goal of improving the production of bioactive triterpene glycosides, plant engineering will be necessary if we are to develop new UGTs with greater functional activities or altered regioselectivity and glycosylation patterns. Here, we have described the classification and grouping of triterpene-related UGTs, based on their substrate specificities and catalytic activity. Utilizing the EST collection, in combination with heterologous expression analysis and other experiments, presents an effective basis for uncovering the enzymes that catalyze glycosylation in saponin biosynthesis. Nevertheless, much work remains, including in vivo studies that involve metabolite profile analysis of overexpressing plants and knockouts that lack individual UGTs. Those examinations must also focus on tissue- and subcellular-specificity as well as regulatory promoter elements for saponin accumulations. Such investigations will have to be integrated with in vitro catalytic analysis of recombinant UGTs if we are to gain a comprehensive understanding of their biological roles. Knowledge of UGT crystal structures can then be used as a support for molecular modeling to decode the catalytic functions and substrate specificity of unknown UGTs. Those projects will be beneficial in clarifying the biosynthetic mechanism of different saponins as well as engineering the generation of valuable plant products.

Although numerous plant O-glycosyltransferases have been characterized and their crystal structures found, little has been done toward examining N-, S-, and C-glycosyltransferases. Therefore, we must still determine the difficult chemical reactions if we are to use those novel UGTs for biotechnological tasks. We find it interesting that UGTs can

catalyze both glycosylation and deglycosylation reactions that allow for the exchange of sugars and aglycones (Wang, 2009). However, deglycosylation activity must be explored in triterpene-related UGTs that show reversibility to synthesize activated sugars.

When one considers the diverse applications and pharmacological significance of GT superfamily members in plant secondary metabolism, the isolation and genetic manipulation of UGTs present an interesting foundation for prospective studies of drug design. Therefore, further work is required to elucidate additional enzymes with triterpene glycosylation activity at different positions and in different oligosaccharide chain formations. Those projects are essential if we are to manipulate the biological and physicochemical properties of these molecules.
