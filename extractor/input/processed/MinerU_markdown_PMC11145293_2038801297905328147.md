# A phytoplasma effector destabilizes chloroplastic glutamine synthetase inducing chlorotic leaves that attract leafhopper vectors

Xiao-Feng Zhanga,1, Zhanpeng Lia,1, Hanbin Lina,1, Yu Chenga,1, Huanqin Wanga , Zhoumian Jianga , Zhenxi Jia , Zhejun Huanga , Hongyan Chena , and Taiyun Weia,2 

Edited by Sheng Yang He, Duke University, Durham, NC; received February 18, 2024; accepted April 22, 2024 

Leaf yellowing is a well-known phenotype that attracts phloem-feeding insects. However, it remains unclear how insect-vectored plant pathogens induce host leaf yellowing to facilitate their own transmission by insect vectors. Here, we report that an effector protein secreted by rice orange leaf phytoplasma (ROLP) inhibits chlorophyll biosynthesis and induces leaf yellowing to attract leafhopper vectors, thereby presumably promoting pathogen transmission. This effector, designated secreted ROLP protein 1 (SRP1), first secreted into rice phloem by ROLP, was subsequently translocated to chloroplasts by interacting with the chloroplastic glutamine synthetase (GS2). The direct interaction between SRP1 and GS2 disrupts the decamer formation of the GS2 holoenzyme, attenuating its enzymatic activity, thereby suppressing the synthesis of chlorophyll precursors glutamate and glutamine. Transgenic expression of SRP1 in rice plants decreased GS2 activity and chlorophyll precursor accumulation, finally inducing leaf yellowing. This process is correlated with the previous evidence that the knockout of GS2 expression in rice plants causes a similar yellow chlorosis phenotype. Consistently, these yellowing leaves attracted higher numbers of leafhopper vectors, caused the vectors to probe more frequently, and presumably facilitate more efficient phytoplasma transmission. Together, these results uncover the mechanism used by phytoplasmas to manipulate the leaf color of infected plants for the purpose of enhancing attractiveness to insect vectors.

Many plant pathogens are naturally transmitted by phloem-feeding arthropod vectors, such as leafhoppers, planthoppers, thrips, whiteflies, psyllids, and aphids (1–5). As a result, both the emergence and spread of these pathogens have been shaped by their molecular interactions with the plant hosts and the vectors (6). Conversely, plants infected by diverse arthropod-borne pathogens are known to undergo phenotypic changes that make them more attractive to vectors. We now know that many of the changes are induced by pathogens that modulate specific processes in host plants to promote pathogen colonization, virulence, and transmission (7–12). Arthropod-borne pathogens frequently encode effectors that induce developmental modifications in their plant hosts, ultimately increasing the chance to attract insect vectors (13–18). However, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain poorly understood for most of such effectors. 

Phytoplasmas are plant pathogens known for reprogramming the development of host plants. They are pathogenic bacteria transmitted by piercing–sucking insects such as leafhoppers, planthoppers, and psyllids (19–21). In infected plants, phytoplasmas are restricted in the phloem sieve cells of vasculature, which in normal plants are responsible for transporting nutrients to immature tissues undergoing active growth (21). Phytoplasmas are known to induce significant morphological changes in the infected plants and modulate the behavior of insect vectors (10, 18, 22–28). The three-way interactions among phytoplasmas, plants, and insects constitute excellent systems for dissecting the genetic basis of extended phenotypes programmed by arthropod-borne plant pathogens. 

Phytoplasmas secrete effectors into host cells through the Sec-dependent pathway, where they modulate host defense and morphogenesis (20). These effector proteins commonly carry cleavable signal peptides but lack transmembrane regions. Upon the cleavage of their signal peptide, mature effector proteins enter host cells and interact with target proteins in the cytoplasm or nuclei of plant cells (20). As a result of phytoplasma-mediated developmental reprogramming, most phytoplasma-infected plants develop a characteristic bushy appearance known as witch’s broom due to excessive proliferation of shoots and branches, retrograde development of flowers into leaf-like organs, and leaf yellowing (21, 29). # Significance

Many agricultural pathogens are transmitted to plant phloem by piercing–sucking insect vectors. Leaf yellowing is a well-known phenotype caused by insectvectored plant pathogens; however, how pathogens mediate leaf yellowing to attract insect vectors for promoting their own transmission is still poorly understood. Here, we find that a chloroplast-localized effector secreted ROLP protein 1 (SRP1) secreted from rice orange leaf phytoplasma (ROLP) could bind to glutamine synthetase (GS2) and disrupt the formation of the GS2 holoenzyme decamer, consequently impairing its enzymatic activity in the biosynthesis of chlorophyll precursors. Such a process leads to rice leaf yellowing to attract leafhopper vectors, thereby potentially promoting pathogen transmission. Our research uncovers the molecular mechanism underlying phytoplasma-induced leaf color change to attract insect vectors.

For example, effectors secreted by the Aster Yellows Phytoplasma strain Witch’s Broom (AY-WB; Ca. Nevertheless, the molecular mechanism underlying phytoplasma-induced leaf yellowing is still unknown. It is a well-recognized fact that arthropods are attracted to yellow colors, as yellow sticky cards and light traps are widely used in crop fields to trap piercing–sucking insects such as leafhoppers and psyllids (32–35). However, it is unknown whether phytoplasmas induce leaf yellowing to entice insect vectors that mediate their transmission and whether specific phytoplasma-secreted effectors are needed for such induction. 

Rice orange leaf phytoplasma (ROLP), a member of the genus Ca. Phytoplasma asteris, is the causal agent of the rice orange leaf disease (ROLD). ROLP was first identified in 1960 in Thailand (36). Over the last decade, ROLD has become a serious threat to rice production, particularly in South China, India, Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines (36–38). ROLP is primarily transmitted in a persistent-propagative manner by the rice green leafhopper Recilia dorsalis with high efficiency, although Nephotettix cincticeps and Nephotettix virescens have been identified as newly adapted alternate vectors (37, 39). Typically, rice plants infected 

with ROLP are moderately stunted, with their leaves developing yellow to golden or yellow to deep orange colors before transiting to leaf senescence (37, 40). 

In the current study, we identified an ROLP effector which we call secreted ROLP protein 1 (SRP1). We found that SRP1 was transported from the site of secretion (phloem sieve elements) to adjacent cells, where it localized to chloroplasts. By directly binding to rice glutamine synthetase (OsGS2), SRP1 inhibits the formation of OsGS2 oligomers and attenuates its enzymatic activity, thereby suppressing the synthesis of glutamate (Glu), glutamine (Gln), and 5-aminolevulinic acid (5-ALA), all of which are precursors of chlorophyll. Transgenic rice plants expressing SRP1 exhibited typical leaf yellowing phenotype and attracted higher numbers of leafhopper vectors. Thus, SRP1 secreted by ROLP was alone able to cause leaf yellowing in rice and presumably enhance the plant-to-plant spread of ROLP by enticing the ROLP-transmitting leafhoppers. Our research uncovers the molecular mechanism underlying phytoplasma-induced leaf color change and unveils a target for controlling ROLP in the rice crop.

Then, 50 3rd-instar nymphs or adult leafhoppers were released into the space between the plant-containing plastic boxes. One hour later, leafhopper individuals on the outer wall of each of the two transparent plastic boxes were counted as the ones attracted to the enclosed plants. As expected, ROLP-infected rice plants attracted more insects (nymph $6 8 . 7 \%$ and adult $7 5 \%$ ) than healthy plants (Fig. 1C), suggesting that ROLP infection drove vector preference in a leaf-color-dependent manner. 

Leaf color is generated when the spectrum of the visible light is reflected by plant leaves. Leaf yellowing symptoms of ROLP-infected rice plants can be classified as type I yellow and type II golden (Fig. 1A). We used a spectroradiometer to measure the intensity of sunlight reflected from rice leaves at different stages of ROLP infection. The peak reflectance levels were observed at $5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in healthy control plants, $6 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ in type I, and $6 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ in type II plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). To assess whether the difference in the reflection spectrum of ROLP-infected leaves affected the leafhopper preference, we performed the leafhopper two-choice experiments following the methods outlined by Todd et al. with minor modifications (35). Specifically, we paired LED lights emitting three different wavelengths ( $5 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ green, 610 nm orange, and $6 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ red) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). A total of 50 ROLP-free 3rd instar nymphs or adults were tested every time. The number of leafhoppers attracted to each of the LED lights was countered. The results indicated that LED light with a wavelength of $6 1 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (orange) attracted most leafhoppers (nymphs and adults), whereas that of $6 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ (red) attracted the least (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 C and $D$ ). 

To further determine which of the leaf color types resulting from ROLP infection is more attractive to leafhoppers, we conducted insect color preference experiments following the procedures similar to those described by Todd et al. (35). The leaf colors measured via the Cyan-Magenta-Yellow-Black (CMYK) color printing modes were printed on the $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ glossy coated papers. The papers were then cut into colored cards of identical sizes and used in a color card trap assay that entailed 50 insects. These experiments verified that type I color (yellow) was the most attractive for leafhoppers (SI Appendix, Fig. S1E). Thus, leafhoppers preferred the rice leaves showing modest yellowing, corresponding to mild ROLD. 

We also used an electrical penetration graph (EPG) assay to examine how ROLP infection affected the feeding behavior of leafhoppers on leaves of rice plants. These experiments showed that compared to leafhoppers feeding on healthy control leaves, those on type I ROLD leaves probed more frequently (as evidenced by total probing number) and ingested more actively (as evidenced by waveform N3) (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). Together, these results demonstrated that R. dorsalis individuals were clearly more attracted to rice leaves with type I ROLD symptoms. 

ROLP Infection Inhibits Chlorophyll Biosynthesis in Rice Leaf Cells. The leaf yellowing in ROLP-infected rice plants mirrored a form of chlorosis resulting from the malfunction of chloroplasts. We then used electron microscopy to inspect the chloroplast ultrastructure in the leaf mesophyll cells of ROLP-infected rice plants. In healthy rice leaves, the chloroplasts exhibit the typical structure of well-developed and tightly packed grana thylakoids in the mesophyll cells (Fig.  2 and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S3A). However, the chloroplasts in the mesophyll cells of ROLPinfected leaves exhibited abnormal morphology, with poorly and loosely arranged thylakoid membranes and less dense grana stacks (Fig. 2 and SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). Thus, chloroplasts in ROLPinfected rice plants exhibited markedly accelerated degradation 

compared to those in healthy controls. Consistent with this idea, the total chlorophyll extracted from type I and type II leaves decreased by $6 2 \%$ and $7 5 \%$ , respectively, relative to that of wildtype leaves (SI Appendix, Fig. S3C). Accordingly, photosynthetic parameters, including photosynthetic rate, stomatal conductance, intercellular $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration, and transpiration rate, were significantly reduced in type I ROLD leaves (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 $\bar { D _ { - } G }$ ). Nevertheless, accumulation of the photosynthetic products, including glucose, fructose, and starch, was significantly increased and the content of total amino acid was reduced in type I ROLD leaves (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 3 H { - } K )$ , probably reflecting an even more dramatic reduction of other energy-consuming cellular activities due to the infection of ROLP. Collectively, these results indicate that the leaf yellowing symptom caused by ROLP infection was associated with the reduced chlorophyll biosynthesis. 

The ROLP-Secreted Effector SRP1 Is Translocated from Phloem Tissue to Chloroplasts of Mesophyll Cells. Previous researchers established that phytoplasmas altered plant developmental processes through specific effectors secreted into plant tissues, thereby promoting their own colonization and virulence (10, 18, 22–28). To determine whether ROLP encoded any effectors capable of causing leaf yellowing in the infected leaves, we screened the genome sequence of ROLP to identify candidate effectors with characteristic N-terminal signal peptides (SP) of 20 to 50 amino acids in length. We used the SignalP-3.0 algorithm developed by Bai and colleagues that promised more reliable signal peptide predictions using a deep neural network-based approach (41). A total of 16 candidate effectors were identified that contain the characteristic N-terminal SP (Fig.  3A and SI  Appendix, Table  S1). These 16 candidate effectors were then tested for potential chloroplast localization through transient expression of their GFP-fused forms in rice protoplast cells. Only one of them, designated SRP1, was found in chloroplasts (Fig. 3B). We then used reverse transcription (RT)-qPCR to evaluate SRP1 mRNA levels in the ROLP-infected leaf tissues and used western blotting to assess its protein levels with SRP1 antibody (Fig. 3 C and $D$ ). The specificity of SRP1 antibody was also evaluated via the western blotting assay (SI  Appendix, Fig. S4). Subsequently, we used immunofluorescence microscopy to determine the distribution of SRP1 in ROLP-infected tissues. As a control, the ROLP-encoded immunodominant membrane protein (Imp) was found throughout the phloem (Fig.  3E). By contrast, although SRP1 colocalized with Imp in the phloem, it was also found in leaf tissues outside phloem (Fig. 3E). 

Immunoelectron microscopy confirmed that SRP1 antibody reacted with ROLP bodies in the sieve tube cells within the phloem, as well as with the loosely arranged grana thylakoids of the degraded chloroplasts in the mesophyll cells of ROLD leaves (Fig. 3F). However, SRP1 antibody did not react with the tightly packed grana thylakoids of the normal chloroplasts in the mesophyll of healthy leaves (Fig. 3F). Thus, ROLP-secreted SRP1 was specifically associated with the degraded chloroplasts in ROLD leaves. To further verify the affinity of SRP1 to chloroplasts, we then purified chloroplasts from ROLP-infected rice leaves and used protein extracts of purified chloroplasts to carry out western blotting with the SRP1 antibody. As shown in Fig. $3 G ,$ , western blotting results revealed the presence of SRP1 in purified chloroplast fractions. Together, these results demonstrated that the ROLP-secreted SRP1 effector was translocated from its site of secretion (phloem) to chloroplasts of adjacent mesophyll cells (Fig. 3H).

Next, we attempted to determine the region(s) of OsGS2 responsible for interacting with SRP1, possibly recruiting the latter to the chloroplasts. To this end, several OsGS2 deletion mutants $( \mathrm { O s G S 2 m 1 }$ , $\mathrm { O s G S } 2 \mathrm { m } 2$ , and $\mathrm { O s G S 2 m } 3$ ) were constructed and tested for SRP1 interaction using the yeast two-hybrid assay (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S5A). We next investigated how the interaction of SRP1 with OsGS2 influenced its enzymatic activity. It is well known that in plants, chloroplastic GS functions in the form of oligomers which play a pivotal role in regulating nitrogen metabolism, catalyzing the binding reaction between ammonia and glutamic acid (42). Hence, we hypothesize that the interaction of SRP1 with the OsGS2 monomer may impede the formation of the OsGS2 holoenzyme. To validate this hypothesis, we first confirmed the interaction between OsGS2 monomers through the BiFC assay in N. benthamiana leaves via agroinfiltration (Fig. 5D). The number of punctate structures was significantly decreased with the gradual increase of coexpressed SRP1. whereas the expression of the $\bar { \mathrm { S R P 1 } } _ { \mathrm { E 5 2 A } }$ protein did not affect this interaction (Fig. $5 D _ { \ell }$ ). Similar results were obtained by the dose-dependent affinity-isolation assay, which showed that the content of His-OsGS2 protein bound to GST-OsGS2 protein decreased with increasing levels of Flag-SRP1 protein (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Together, these data suggest that SRP1 disrupts the interaction of OsGS2 monomers.

To further validate whether SRP1 hinders the formation of the OsGS2 holoenzyme, we expressed OsGS2, SRP1, and $\mathrm { S R P l _ { E S 2 A } }$ in N. benthamiana leaves via agroinfiltration. Total proteins were extracted, separated by native-PAGE, and detected through immunoblotting. A band with a molecular weight of approximately 490 kDa was observed when OsGS2 was expressed alone (Fig. $5 E )$ ). Considering the molecular weight of the OsGS2 monomer (47 kDa), this suggests that the OsGS2 holoenzyme likely forms a decamer. When coexpressing OsGS2 and SRP1, the content of the OsGS2 holoenzyme decreased with escalating levels of SRP1 protein, while the accumulation of OsGS2 monomers increased (Fig. 5E). Notably, the expression of $\mathrm { S R P 1 _ { E S 2 A } }$ did not affect the oligomeric state of OsGS2 decamers (Fig. 5E). This indicates that SRP1 alone has the capability to disrupt the formation of the OsGS2 holoenzyme. We also conducted nondenaturing PAGE western blotting to examine the oligomeric state of OsGS2 in rice leaves infected with ROLP. The results revealed that endogenous OsGS2 in rice predominantly existed in the form of decamers (Fig. 5F). Furthermore, in leaves infected with ROLP, the content of OsGS2 decamers decreased when compared to healthy leaves, while the monomeric form of OsGS2 showed an increase (Fig. 5F). 

Therefore, ROLP infection compromises the stability of the OsGS2 holoenzyme. 

We then used the $\gamma$ -glutamyl-transferase assay to assess OsGS2 activity in ROLP-infected rice leaves and found it was significantly decreased (SI Appendix, Fig. S9A). We also evaluated the mRNA and protein levels of OsGS2 with or without ROLP infections. RT-qPCR and western blotting assays showed that ROLP infection decreased the OsGS2 mRNA and protein levels in rice leaves (SI Appendix, Fig. S9 B and C), which may be attributed to chloroplast damage induced by ROLP infection (Fig. 2). Furthermore, we quantified the levels of reaction products of OsGS2 in ROLP infected leaves, including Glu, Gln, and 5-ALA, which were the pivotal precursors in chlorophyll biosynthesis (SI Appendix, Fig. S9D). A comparison of these chlorophyll synthesis-related components in ROLD and healthy leaves showed that the concentration of $\mathrm { N H 4 ^ { + } }$ significantly increased in ROLP-infected tissues, while the concentrations of Glu, Gln, and 5-ALA decreased significantly (SI Appendix, Fig. $S ^ { 9 } E { - } H )$ . However, the concentrations of Glu, Gln, and 5-ALA in rice leaves after ROLP-positive R. dorsalis feeding for $1 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ did not significantly change when compared with ROLP-free R. dorsalis feeding (SI Appendix, Fig. S9 I–K). It seems that the initial feeding of 

ROLP-positive R. dorsalis does not secret enough SRP1 to impact OsGS2 activity. These results indicate that SRP1 secreted during ROLP propagation in the phloem negatively regulates the activity of the OsGS2 holoenzyme within chloroplasts, leading to the inhibition of chlorophyll synthesis and the induction of leaf chlorosis. 

Transgenic Rice Plants Overexpressing SRP1 Exhibit Leaf Yellowing. To further elucidate the role of SRP1 in ROLP-induced leaf chlorosis, we generated transgenic rice plants (ecotype Oryza sativa L. spp. japonica) that constitutively overexpressed SRP1 (with the signal peptide removed). The simultaneously generated transgenic plants overexpressing GUS or $\mathrm { S R P 1 _ { E 5 2 A } }$ were used as controls. As shown in Fig.  6A, the SRP1-expressing rice plants exhibited extensive leaf yellowing, while the expression of either GUS or $\mathrm { S R P l _ { E S 2 A } }$ did not affect leaf color (Fig. 6A). Consistent with the chlorosis phenotype, the total chlorophyll content in SRP1- expressing transgenic rice leaves was at least $6 0 \%$ lower than in GUS-expressing plants or in $S _ { \mathrm { R P l _ { E 5 2 A } } }$ -expressing plants (Fig. 6B). As expected, the peak reflectance of SRP1-expressing transgenic rice leaves was at $6 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (Fig. 6C). Immunoelectron microscopy 

showed that SRP1 was localized in the loosely arranged thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in SRP1-expressing transgenic rice leaves, whereas SRP1 was absent in the fully developed chloroplasts of GUS-expressing transgenic rice leaves (Fig. $6 D$ ). Potentially, SRP1 caused the abnormal chloroplast development. The western blotting assay showed that a substantial proportion of SRP1 was enriched in purified chloroplast fractions isolated from SRP1-expressing plants (Fig. 6E). In contrast, $\mathrm { S R P 1 _ { E 5 2 A } }$ was not detectable in the chloroplast fractions from $\mathrm { S R P 1 _ { E S 2 A } }$ -expressing plants (Fig.  6E). Thus, SPR1 overexpression in the absence of ROLP infection was sufficient to sabotage chloroplasts in leaf cells, causing leaf yellowing phenotype. We also detected the oligomeric state of OsGS2 in SRP1, $S { \mathrm { R P } } 1 _ { \mathrm { E } 5 2 { \mathrm { A } } }$ , or GUS transgenic plants and found that SRP1 expression led to a decrease in OsGS2 decamer content but an increase in OsGS2 monomer accumulation in SRP1 transgenic plants (Fig. 6F). These results validate the role of SRP1 in inducing chlorosis phenomenon in rice leaves, impacting the stability of the chloroplastic OsGS2 holoenzyme.

Transgenic SRP1 Expression in Rice Promotes Host Attractiveness to Leafhopper Vectors and ROLP Transmission. We next examined whether the transgenic rice exhibiting SRP1-induced leaf yellowing attracted more ROLP-transmitting leafhoppers. Insect two-choice experiments indicated that more than ${ \sim } 6 0 \%$ of nymph and adult leafhoppers preferably fed on SRP1-expressing transgenic rice plants, compared with GUS-expressing transgenic rice plants (Fig.  7A). Similar to GUS transgenic rice, SRP1 transgenic rice cultivated in a liquid culture medium with 0.5 $\mathrm { g / L }$ of Gln exhibits a significant reduction in attractiveness to leafhoppers when compared to SRP1 transgenic rice grown in a standard culture medium (Fig. 7 B and C). 

To determine whether the attractiveness of SRP1-expressing transgenic rice to R. dorsalis correlates with enhanced transmission of ROLP, two groups of 50 ROLP-infected R. dorsalis individuals were housed in small cages and arrowed to feed on either SRP1- expressing or GUS-expressing transgenic rice plants. After 6 d of this feeding regime, RT-qPCR and western blot assays were used to show that ROLP loads in SRP1-expressing transgenic rice plants were higher than in the GUS-expressing control (Fig. 7D). This result suggested that SRP1 facilitated ROLP transmission by insect vectors. The feeding behaviors of leafhoppers on transgenic rice plants were further examined. The EPG assay indicated that the mean duration of individual ingestion (waveform N3) and probing number were significantly longer for leafhoppers on SRP1-expressing transgenic rice plants than on those feeding on control plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). This result suggested that SRP1 expression potentially prolonged ingestion probing of leafhoppers to facilitate ROLP transmission into the rice phloem. Overall, our results indicated that the effector SRP1 directly targeted and negatively regulated the expression of OsGS2 and further impaired its activity. Thereby, SRP1 caused chlorophyll reduction and leaf yellowing symptoms, which promoted host attractiveness to insect vectors and potentially facilitated ROLP transmission. 

# Discussion

Our work demonstrates that during ROLP infection in rice plants, the early leaf yellowing phenotype promotes host attractiveness to insect vectors, thereby presumably increasing the number of 

insects that carry and transmit the phytoplasmas. We identify the effector protein SRP1 as a virulence factor from the genome of ROLP, which secretes from ROLP in the infected rice phloem region and then translocates to adjacent chloroplasts via interaction with OsGS2. SRP1 directly binding to the OsGS2 monomer disrupts the formation of the OsGS2 holoenzyme and impairs OsGS2 enzyme activity for the biosynthesis of chlorophyll precursors Glu and Gln, which in turn causes the subsequent leaf yellowing ROLD phenotype, finally attracting more leafhopper vectors and potentially benefitting pathogen transmission (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). Thus, SRP1 mediates a fundamental process in plant development (leaf yellowing) and directly impacts parasite fitness (attraction of further leafhoppers). Our results uncover the underlying mechanism of how a phytoplasma effector modulates leaf color to increase insect attractiveness. 

Leaf yellowing is a highly complex and intricately regulated process at different levels (43, 44). GS2, a critical enzyme in nitrogen assimilation, plays a vital role in incorporating ammonium into Gln, serving as the precursor for the synthesis of amino acids, nucleic acids, and chlorophylls (42, 45, 46). Knockout of OsGS2 expression in rice plants results in a yellow chlorosis phenotype with a significant decline in chlorophyll and Gln/Glu contents (47). A similar chlorosis phenotype is also observed in barley and Arabidopsis, which are deficient in the chloroplastic GS2 gene, suggesting a correlation between leaf color and GS2 activity (48, 49). These results suggest that the suppression of OsGS2 activity and its homologs directly mediates leaf yellowing and plant growth. Consistent with these results, our research reveals the disruption of the OsGS2 holoenzyme in SRP1 transgenic rice. This disruption leads to a reduction in GS2 enzyme activity, resulting in yellowing symptoms in the leaves. Importantly, this phenotype can be alleviated through exogenous supplementation of Gln. In our study, we do not observe a decline in Glu, Gln, and 5-ALA levels in rice leaves following feeding by ROLP-positive R. dorsalis. It suggested that the initial feeding of ROLP-positive R. dorsalis may not secret enough SRP1 to impact OsGS2 activity. Additional experiments are necessary to substantiate this phenomenon. 

The number of monomers in GS2 complexes varies across different plant species, with GSs from soybean and common bean existing as octamers determined by electron microscopy (50, 51). In contrast, GSs from maize and ${ \mathrm { G S 1 } } \beta$ from soybean form decamers as determined by X-ray crystallography (52, 53). Our results provide evidence that both transiently expressed OsGS2 and endogenous GS2 in rice predominantly exist in the form of a decamer. In our investigation, we find that the direct interaction between SRP1 and OsGS2 specifically hinders the enzyme activity of $\mathrm { O s G S 2 }$ involved in the biosynthesis of chlorophyll precursors. This hindrance occurs through the disruption of the OsGS2 holoenzyme, rather than facilitating the degradation of OsGS2 monomers. Interestingly, the inhibition of OsGS2 expression is observed in both ROLP-infected and SRP1-transgenic rice, which potentially is caused by the accumulation of toxic substances such as ammonium ions and the damage of chloroplast, thereby impacting the normal expression of cellular genes. Furthermore, this effect may also promote the occurrence of leaf chlorosis. 

Phytoplasmas infect most vascular plant species and often induce massive changes in their development, mainly including witches’ broom, phyllody, and leaf yellowing (20, 21). It remains debated whether phytoplasma-caused disease symptoms are side effects or adaptive effects that directly or indirectly facilitate phytoplasma transmission by insect vectors (3). The effector SAP54 from AY-WB and phyllogens from onion yellows phytoplasma mediate the transformation of flowers into leaves (24, 27). The effectors SAP05 and SAP11 of AY-WB contribute to witches’ broom-type tissue proliferation (10, 18). These processes lead to a delay in plant senescence and plant death and thus increase the likelihood that plants are visited and colonized by phytoplasma vectors, which increases the number of insects that can transmit the phytoplasmas (3, 21). We find that SRP1 is a typical sec-dependent chloroplast-localized effector protein containing a conserved N-terminal Sec SP without other transmembrane regions. Like conventional phytoplasma effectors, SRP1 migrates beyond the phloem via an unknown mechanism. Moreover, it localized in the chloroplast of mesophyll cells via interaction with chloroplast OsGS2. In this study, we demonstrated that phytoplasma effectors have the capability to induce leaf discoloration. 

In the field, yellow sticky cards and light traps are widely used to attract phytoplasma vectors such as leafhoppers, planthoppers, and psyllids of the order Hemiptera (32–35). Previous studies indicated that phytoplasma pathogens invasion can result in leaf chlorosis, but the impact of chlorosis development on disease infection remains unclear (54–56). Some previous studies suggest that insect-transmitted plant viruses can induce leaf yellowing (7, 8), which potentially influences insect vector behavior. However, the molecular mechanism underlying plant virus-induced leaf yellowing to directly promote host attractiveness to insect vectors and facilitate viral transmission by insect vectors is still unclear. Our results 

demonstrate that the leaf yellowing symptom is an adaptive effect that is beneficial for ROLP infection and could therefore be used to optimize strategies for leafhopper control. Early leaf yellowing or chlorosis, together with a gradual decrease of transpiration caused by the disappearance of chlorophyll is a pathologic hallmark that has been described for many phytoplasmas (54, 57–60). We thus anticipate that other phytoplasmas may have also evolved similar strategies to secret effectors that directly bind to and impair the activity of chloroplast GS2 homologs, finally causing leaf yellowing to enhance insect attraction and colonization.

Please see SI  Appendix, Materials and Methods, for a full description of the methods used. 

Plasmid Construction. All primers used for cloning and experiments are listed in SI Appendix, Table S2. All plasmids constructed in this study are listed in SI Appendix, Table S3. Enzymes from Takara (Cat No. R010A) and enzymes from Genstar (Cat No. A002-10) are used for PCR amplification and verification. Enzymes from Takara (Cat No. 1611, 1605, and 1615) and enzymes from Genstar (Cat No. T197-100) are used for nucleic acid digestion and ligation. 

ROLP, Insects, and Antibodies. ROLP-infected rice plants were collected from rice fields in Zhangzhou, Fujian province, China, and maintained via transmission into rice plants by R. dorsalis under $1 4 \mathsf { h } / 1 0 \mathsf { h }$ (light/dark) photoperiod conditions at $2 5 \pm 3 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in growth chambers. ROLP-free R. dorsalis individuals were also obtained from Fujian province, China, and reared on fresh rice seedlings in an 

air-conditioned room $( 2 5 \pm 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } , 6 5 \%$ relative humidity). The polyclonal antibody against Imp of ROLP has been described previously (40). The polyclonal antibody against SRP1 was prepared by GenScript Biotech Corporation, Nanjing, China, as approved by the Science Technology Department of Jiangsu Province of China. IgGs were isolated from polyclonal antisera, using a protein A-Sepharose affinity column (Pierce Biotechnology, Waltham, MA). 

Biochemical Studies. Details for all biochemical studies including chlorophyll content measure, photosynthetic measurements, total amino acid content analysis, OsGS2 enzyme activity assay, metabolite analysis, spectral analysis, color measurement, LED trap assay, color card trap assay, rice protoplast isolation, immunofluorescence labeling assay, immunoelectron microscopy assay, chloroplast isolation, yeast two-hybrid assays, protein structure prediction, GST pulldown assay, BiFC assay, nondenaturing PAGE western blotting, and Co-IP assay are provided in SI Appendix, Supporting Information Text: Materials and Methods. 

Insect Two-Choice Assay. To assess whether rice plants infected with ROLP or transgenic rice plants overexpressing SRP1 were more attractive to leafhoppers, two rice seedlings of selected genotypes with similar growth conditions were placed in the device shown in Fig. 2B, comprising two closed and transparent plastic boxes (L = 40 cm, W = 35 cm, and $\mathsf { H } = 5 0 \mathsf { c m }$ ) and a small net in the middle $( \mathsf { L } = 4 0 \mathsf { c m } , \mathsf { W } = 1 5 \mathsf { c m } ,$ and $\mathsf { H } = 5 0 \mathsf { c m }$ ) with the same light condition. Approximately 50 ROLP-free nymphs or adults of R. dorsalis were transferred into the middle net. After 30 min, insects that had settled on each side of the transparent plastic boxes were recaptured and counted. The experiments were conducted in three biologically independent replicates. 

EPG Assay. To characterize the probing activities of R. dorsalis individuals on rice leaves, the EPG assay was conducted using an eight-channel DC EPG (Giga-8 DC-EPG) system (Wageningen, The Netherlands). The EPG experiments 

were performed in a temperature-controlled room $( 2 5 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } , 6 0 \pm 5 \%$ relative humidity). The insects were monitored for a maximum of 5 h per recording, which was replicated 10 times using a new insect and a new plant per EPG recording. At least 10 biologically independent replicates for each insect were recorded. More details can be found in SI Appendix, Supporting Information Text: Materials and Methods. 

Statistical Analysis. All data were analyzed for statistical differences using GraphPad Prism version 8. Normally distributed data were then analyzed using two-tailed, paired t tests. Multiple comparisons of the means were conducted using one-way ANOVA, followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison tests for significant differences among different lines or different treatments at the $P < 0 . 0 5$ , significance level.
