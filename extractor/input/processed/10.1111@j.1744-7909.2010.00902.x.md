# Abstract

Plants coordinate their development using long-distance signaling. The vascular system provides a route for long-distance movement, and specifically the xylem for root-to-shoot signaling. Root-to-shoot signals play roles communicating soil conditions, and these signals are important for agricultural water conservation. Using genetic approaches, the Arabidopsis bypass1 (bps1) mutant, which over-produces a root-derived signal, was identified. Although bps1 mutants have both root and shoot defects, the shoot can develop normally if the roots are removed, and the mutant root is sufficient to induce arrest of the wild-type shoot. BYPASS1 encodes a protein with no functionally characterized domains, and BPS1-like genes are found in plant genomes, but not the genomes of animals. Analyses of hormone pathways indicate that the mobile compound that arises in bps1 roots requires carotenoid biosynthesis, but it is

neither abscisic acid nor strigolactone. The current model suggests that BPS1 is required to prevent the synthesis of a novel substance that moves from the root to the shoot, where it modifies shoot growth by interfering with auxin signaling.

Sieburth LE, Lee DK (2010) BYPASS1: How a tiny mutant tells a big story about root-to-shoot signaling. J. Integr. Plant Biol. 52(1), 77-85.

# Introduction

The growth of plants requires conducting tissues that allow for movement of water, nutrients, and energy-carrying molecules. These conducting tissues are collectively referred to as the vascular system, and they are composed of two developmentally and functionally distinct tissues: the xylem and the phloem. Transport through the xylem occurs through aligned files of cells, either tracheary elements or vessels (Figure 1). As these cells develop, they produce heavily reinforced secondary cell walls, and then undergo cell death. Water drawn upwards from the roots carries with it dissolved minerals, which are required throughout the plant for a variety of physiological processes.

Less understood, but of major importance for plant development, physiology, and agriculture, are signaling molecules that are also conducted through the xylem. The present review primarily focuses on these xylem-borne signaling molecules that influence the physiology and development of distant tissues.

Production of foods will require both new areas for farming, and maximizing efficient use of existing farmland, especially efficient water use (Clark et al. 2005). Xylem-borne root-to-shoot signals have been implicated in promoting efficient water use.

To conserve water, agricultural scientists have tested different irrigation methods, and analyzed their effects on yield and water use efficiency (WUE). Three irrigation strategies that aim to reduce water use are deficit irrigation (DI), a strategy that simply cuts back on water; regulated deficit irrigation (RDI), which tailors the periods of water deficit to the plant's developmental requirements; and partial root drying (PRD), which combines watering part of the root while allowing other root regions to experience drought conditions. These studies generally linked reduced water with reduced yield, but also increased efficiency of water use, and especially under PRD conditions (Kang et al. 2001; Stikic et al. 2003; Kirda et al. 2004; Li et al. 2005; Fernandez et al. 2006; Zhang et al. 2006; Fereres and Soriano 2007; Costa et al. 2007).

Increased water use efficiency comes about through tight regulation of stomatal aperture (Sirichandra et al. 2009). Perception of drought conditions by plant roots leads to signaling from the root and culminates in reduced stomatal conductance in the shoot. Irrigation using PRD (Figure 2) appears to be particularly successful at increasing WUE while only causing a modest reduction in productivity (Kang et al. 2001; Kirda et al. 2004). The portion of the root that remains in drought conditions appears to produce a signal that confers drought resistance to the entire shoot, despite the adequate water that is derived from the other half of the roots. This root-derived signal could play a pivotal role in adapting modern agriculture to future climate change.

# Root Influence on Shoot Development: Split Root Experiments

Experimental evidence for how roots influence shoot physiology made progress with the adoption of the split root experimental design (Figure 3). This approach separates the plant root system into two separate containers; half the roots receive one treatment and the other half a different treatment, then the combined influence of the two root treatments on shoot development can be analyzed. Use of this system by Fritz Went in the 1940s led to the hypothesis that a root-derived hormone promoted shoot growth (Went 1943). These experiments exposed portions of the plant root system to air, which induced more rapid growth than control plants. The postulated hormone remains unknown and uncharacterized.

More recently, studies using the split root experimental system have implicated a growth-retarding root-derived hormone that is evoked by drought (Passioura 1988; Saab and Sharp 1989; Gowing et al. 1990; reviewed in Davies and Zhang 1991) (Figure 3). In these experiments, most of the roots are maintained in a water-replete environment, while a portion is exposed to drought conditions. By following leaf water potential, the shoot was shown not to directly experience drought conditions. Nevertheless, stomata closed, consistent with a root-derived signal indicating drought. Moreover, both leaf initiation and leaf expansion rates were reduced under these conditions. The drought-exposed portion of the root was responsible for these growth effects, because when the dried root was excised, the shoot rapidly resumed normal growth rates. Drought-exposed plants produce much smaller plants, and even those grown under PRD irrigation produced somewhat smaller plants (Costa et al. 2007). Thus, it is possible that the same root-derived signal (or signals) underlie both these physiological and agricultural responses.

# Long-Distance Signaling Molecules

Several plant hormones are known to travel long distances, from the root to the shoot, and to affect shoot processes. An excellent example is the control of shoot branching, which is regulated by the root-derived signaling molecule recently identified as strigolactone (Gomez-Roldan et al. 2008; Umehara et al. 2008). Similarly, cytokinin has been implicated as a long-distance signal that communicates the nitrogen status from the root to the shoot (Forde 2001; Takei et al. 2001) (see also Kudo et al. 2010). The signal that communicates drought conditions from the root to the shoot is widely believed to be abscisic acid (ABA) (Zhang and Davies 1989; Bahrun et al. 2002). In part, this is because plants exposed to drought conditions synthesize ABA in their roots (Zhang and Davies 1987) and large increases in ABA can also be detected in the xylem as well as in the shoot. ABA is also an attractive candidate because of its direct role in regulation of stomatal conductance (Mittelheuser and Van Steveninck 1969). However, the relative contributions of root-derived ABA and shoot-synthesized ABA to the increased ABA levels in leaves remains unresolved.

Although ABA in the xylem supports the root as a source of shoot ABA, grafting experiments carried out between wild-type and ABA-deficient tomato mutants suggest that a root source of ABA might not be necessary (Holbrook et al. 2002). Graft chimeras using wild-type and ABA-deficient mutants revealed that wild-type shoots showed normal drought-induced stomatal closure even if the rootstock was unable to synthesize ABA.

This provides strong evidence for the existence of another root-derived signal. The identity of this putative non-ABA root-to-shoot signal is currently unknown.

# BYPASS1 Prevents Synthesis of a Novel Root-to-Shoot Signal

Genetic approaches can be useful for dissection of signaling pathways, and might shed light on the drought-induced root-to-shoot signaling pathway. A genetic dissection of leaf vein patterning led to identification of an Arabidopsis mutant, called bypass1 (bps1) (Van Norman et al. 2004). The roots of this mutant over-produce a substance that functions noncell-autonomously to arrest shoot growth. The remainder of this review will focus on the BPS1 signaling pathway, and its possible link to drought-induced root-to-shoot signaling.

The $bps1$ mutant shows arrested shoot growth, abnormal root development, and it is much smaller than wild-type plants (Figure 4). The $bps1$ rosette leaves initiate, but then undergo developmental arrest, and under most growth conditions they remain small and radialized. In addition to these leaf defects, the $bps1$ primary root also develops abnormally. It ceases elongation within 2 d of germination, and differentiation extends to the root apex. Lateral roots, which appear to initiate normally, also arrest in a manner similar to the primary root. These observations support the hypothesis that $BPS1$ is required for normal organ differentiation.

To test for possible interactions between the bps1 shoot and root defects, a series of grafting and root excision experiments were carried out (Van Norman et al. 2004). Surprisingly, bps1 scion (shoot) grafted to the wild-type (WT) stock (root) allowed the bps1 shoot to transiently resume development (Figure 5). Moreover, removal of the bps1 root also allowed the bps1 shoot to resume normal growth. These experiments support two conclusions: first, the bps1 shoot is capable of normal development, and second, the abnormal development observed in the intact mutant is due to a root-derived signal.

It was possible that the response to the $bps1$ root-derived signal, developmental arrest, only occurred in a mutant shoot. To test this possibility, WT scions were grafted to $bps1$ root stock. These graft chimera showed arrest of both leaf expansion and leaf primordia recruitment (Van Norman et al. 2004) (Figure 5). Thus, the $bps1$ root was both necessary and sufficient to induce shoot arrest. These experiments were used to develop a model for BPS1 action (Figure 6). Because the non-cell-autonomous action of the root required a loss of BPS1, this model specifies that it normally functions in a negative manner, and prevents the synthesis of the xylem-borne signal. A key feature that remains to be elucidated is how a normal plant modulates this BPS1 activity, and the conditions under which $bps1$ signal is produced.

Could this $bps1$ signal be related to the drought-induced root-to-shoot signal discussed above? Temperature shift experiments (allowing shift between weaker and stronger phenotypes) indicated that the $bps1$ signal inhibits both expansion and initiation of leaves (Van Norman et al. 2004). Although this bears striking similarity to responses in the split root/root drying experiments, testing this idea will require chemical identification of the mobile substance and an analysis of its synthesis and action(s) in wild-type plants.

# Molecular Aspects of BPS1

Map-based methods were used to identify the BPS1 gene. BPS1 encodes a 249 amino acid protein with a single large domain that has not been functionally characterized, and which is only present in plant genomes (Van Norman et al. 2004). In the Arabidopsis genome, there are five additional BPS1-

like genes (BPS2-BPS6) and reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) experiments established that BPS1 is expressed in all of the tissues examined. This result might appear to be at odds with the grafting data, which indicated a functional requirement for BPS1 only in the root. One possible explanation is that other members of the BPS gene family prevent ectopic signal synthesis in the shoot. Alternatively, BPS1 expression could be regulated at the post-transcriptional level.

The presence of $BPS1$ -like genes in other plant genomes suggests that the $BPS1$ signaling pathway is conserved. In a nice study by Kang et al. (2008), two $BPS1$ homologs from Nicotiana benthamiana were isolated and studied. These genes, called NbBPS1 and NbBPS2, were both predicted to be Arabidopsis BPS2 homologs. To test their functions, virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) was used to deplete NbBPS1 RNA; this induced abnormal leaf development and ectopic cambium proliferation in the stem. Like the Arabidopsis bps1 mutant, these phenotypes were also partially suppressed by spraying the silenced plants with the carotenoid biosynthesis inhibitor fluridone. These results indicate that BPS genes in other plants function in a similar manner to $BPS1$ , and they support a role for BPS proteins in negative regulation of a mobile signal whose synthesis requires carotenoids.

An additional interesting phenotype noted in the VIGS NbBPS1 lines was an increase in leaf cell ploidy levels (from 2C to 4C-8C) (Kang et al. 2008). In Arabidopsis bps1 mutants, shoot cell division is strongly diminished (L Sieburth, unpubl., data, 2007); this ploidy level change indicates that in tobacco, NbBPS1 is also required for normal cell cycle progression. Based on Arabidopsis bps1 mutant and VIGS NbBPS1 morphologies, BPS genes should be important for both shoot and root development. In contrast to the many parallels between VIGS NbBPS1 and Arabidopsis bps1 mutants, the tobacco VIGS system was not thought to cause silencing in roots (Kang et al. 2008). Because NbBPS1 is an AtBPS2 homolog, rather than a homolog of AtBPS1, this result hints that members of the BPS gene family might have specialized tissue-specific functions.

# The bps1 Signal Targets Auxin Responses in the Shoot

The strong effect of the bps1 root on shoot development led to the question of how development was being modulated. The bps1 mutant was originally isolated because of its leaf vein pattern defects (Van Norman et al. 2004). Vascular patterning is strongly linked to the phytohormone auxin (Sachs 1981, 1989), suggesting that one possible mode of action was crosstalk with the auxin pathway; for example, auxin synthesis, signaling or response. Precedent for this comes from the root-derived

MAX-dependant strigolactone, which regulates branching by modifying auxin transport in shoots (Bennett et al. 2006; Brewer et al. 2009; Ferguson and Beveridge 2009; Hayward et al. 2009). Indeed, tests of auxin signaling in bps1 mutants, using auxin reporters, support that the bps1 signal blocks auxin-induced transcriptional activation (Van Norman et al. 2004). The DR5:GUS reporter uses a synthetic promoter to drive GUS expression in auxin-responsive cells, for example in root apices and developing vascular strands, and expression can be activated in whole plants by addition of the synthetic auxin, 2,4-dichlorophenoxyactic acid (2,4-D) (Ulmasov et al. 1997) (Figure 8). In bps1 leaves, DR5:GUS expression is induced by 2,4-D at the leaf's distal end, in the same pattern as wild-type leaf primordia. In the wild-type, as leaves expand, DR5:GUS expression also expands, and becomes concentrated around the leaf margin and in developing procambial strands. However, the bps1 leaves do not enlarge, and the DR5:GUS expression domain does not expand (Van Norman et al. 2004) (Figure 8). Moreover, the addition of 2,4-D fails to induce expansion

# Concluding Remarks

Long-distance signals, coming from the root, through the xylem, and to the shoot, play important roles in plant development. Manipulating these pathways might be a crucial tool for maximizing agricultural productivity in the face of a changing climate. The BPS1 pathway, which appears to be evolutionarily conserved in the plant kingdom, regulates a mobile signal that could be a component of the root-to-shoot signaling pathway evoked by partial root drying. Linking the severe pleiotropic phenotype of bps1 mutants with long-distance signaling required grafting experiments, a technique that is well established in Arabidopsis (Turnbull et al. 2002). While grafting studies might seem to be a long-shot for most mutant analyses, the ability to identify and clarify signaling roles, and the possibility of finding new signaling pathways, makes this technique a tool of potential use for all investigations of plant development.
