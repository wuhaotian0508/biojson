ABSTRACT: Safflower (Carthamus tinctorius L.) is a multipurpose economic crop that is distributed worldwide. Flavonoid glycosides are the main bioactive components in safflower, but only a few UDP-glycosyltransferases (UGT) have been identified. Three differentially expressed UGT genes related with the accumulation of 9 flavonoid O-glycosides were screened from metabolomics and transcriptome analysis. Safflower corolla protoplasts were used to confirm the glycosylation ability of UGT candidates in vivo for the first time. The astragalin content was significantly increased only when CtUGT3 was overexpressed. CtUGT3 also showed flavonoid 3-OH and 7-OH glycosylation activities in vitro. Molecular modeling and site-directed mutagenesis revealed that G15, T136, S276, and E384 were critical catalytic residues for the glycosylation ability of CtUGT3. These results demonstrate that CtUGT3 has a flavonoid 3-OH glycosylation function and is involved in the biosynthesis of astragalin in safflower. This study provides a reference for flavonoid biosynthesis genes research in nonmodel plants. 

KEYWORDS: Carthamus tinctorius L, flavonoid, O-glycosyltransferase, astragalin, corolla protoplast, molecular characterization 

# INTRODUCTION

Safflower (Carthamus tinctorius L.) is an annual Asteraceae species and the only cultivar of the genus Carthamus. Safflower is a multipurpose economic crop that is distributed worldwide. Its seeds are used for the production of edible oil, and the yellow and red pigments, which are widely used in food, medicine, cosmetics, and other products, are extracted from its flowers.1 In East Asian countries, safflower flowers have a long history of use in traditional medicine. Safflower flowers are used to treat various diseases, such as cerebrovascular, cardiovascular, and gynecological diseases.2 Kaempferol is one of the most common flavonoid sugar acceptors and always binds to a single sugar or multiple sugars at the 3, 6, or 7 position.3,4 In Chinese Pharmacopoeia, the content of kaempferol after acid hydrolysis is regarded as one of the indicator components of safflower medicinal materials. The content of kaempferol after acid hydrolysis reflects the total amount of all kaempferol glycosides in safflower. Various types of kaempferol glycosides are abundant in safflower, and kaempferol 3-O-glucoside (astragalin) is one of the most common glycosides.5 Astragalin is found in many plants with various biological activities, such as antitumor, anti-inflammatory, antiallergic, antioxidant, and antibacterial activities.6 

The basic metabolic pathway of flavonoid biosynthesis is well known, particularly in model plants.7 In safflower flowers, many functional genes involved in flavonoid biosynthesis have been cloned and analyzed, including CHS, CHI, FLS, and F3H.8−12 $F 3 H . ^ { 8 - 1 2 }$ Several studies have been published on flavonoid glycosyltransferases (GTs) in different plants, including O-GTs,13−15 C-GTs, and di-C-GTs.16−20 However, only two GTs have been identified in safflower, and their functions have only 

been confirmed in vitro.21,22 It is necessary to screen and identify new glycosyltransferases in C. tinctorius. 

Glycosylation is an important modification reaction, usually the final stage of natural product biosynthesis, located downstream of the synthesis pathway and coordinated with reactions, such as hydroxylation, acylation, and methylation. Glycosylation contributes to the diversity and complexity of plant secondary metabolite biosynthesis, playing an important role in plant defense and stress resistance. For flavonoids, they usually play an important role in resisting light stress. On the other hand, researchers found that temperature, precipitation, light intensity, and drought stress affected the growth, distribution, and secondary metabolic accumulation of safflower.23−25 Light is an important environmental factor that regulates plant growth and developmental cycles.26 Generally, blue, green, and red light are the major components that positively influence the yield, growth, and nutrient quality of plants. Regarding secondary metabolism in plants, several studies have reported that red, blue, and UV light affect biosynthesis and accumulation.27−30 However, in specific situations, other light sources are also beneficial. Therefore, different light quality treatments offer an effective method for screening genes involved in flavonoid glycosylation.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

Plant Materials. Safflower flowers were cultivated in a phytotron. Prior to the experiment, the plants were grown at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ per day under 15,000 lx white light and at $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark; the relative humidity was set to $6 5 \%$ . Safflower plants of the same growth status were selected and grown in a peat/vermiculite/perlite (3:1:1) mixture. Before flowering, plants were divided into 6 groups of equally sized buds. The flowering process lasted for 2 days. During flowering, 5 groups were illuminated for 12 h per day under different single light that emitted red light $\mathbf { 6 6 4 \ n m } ,$ ), far-red light $( 7 2 4 ~ \mathrm { n m } )$ , blue light $\left( 4 4 7 \mathrm { \ n m } \right)$ , UV-B light $\left( 2 9 0 \mathrm { ~ \ n m } \right)$ , and white light, respectively. The last group was treated in the dark. Then, blooming flowers were collected and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Except for the far-red group, three biological replicates were used for the metabolomic and transcriptome analyses, and each replicate was mixed with 4 individuals. Due to the small amount of corollas in the far-red group, 12 individuals were mixed and detected three times. 

Metabolites Detection and Quantification. Freeze-dried flowers were crushed by using a mixer mill (MM 400, Retsch) with zirconia beads for $1 . 5 \mathrm { \ m i n }$ at $3 0 ~ \mathrm { H z }$ . One hundred milligrams of powder was weighed and extracted overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $7 0 \%$ aqueous methanol. Following centrifugation at $_ { 1 0 , 0 0 0 g }$ for 10 min, the extracts were filtered and analyzed using a UPLC-ESI-MS/ MS system (UPLC, Shim-pack UFLC SHIMADZU CBM30A system; MS, Applied Biosystems 4500 Q TRAP). The analytical conditions were as follows: UPLC column, Waters ACQUITY UPLC HSS T3 C18 (1.8 μm, $2 . 1 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m } ,$ ). The mobile phase comprised solvents A (pure water with $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid) and B (acetonitrile with $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid). Sample measurements were performed using a gradient program with starting conditions of $9 5 \%$ A and $5 \%$ B. Within $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n } _ { \mathrm { : } }$ , a linear gradient of $5 \%$ A and $9 5 \%$ B was programed, and the composition of $5 \%$ A and $9 5 \%$ B was maintained for 1 min. Subsequently, the composition was adjusted to $9 5 \%$ A and $5 . 0 \%$ B within 0.10 min and kept for 2.9 min. The column oven was set to 40 ${ } ^ { \circ } C ,$ and the injection volume was $4 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The effluent was alternately connected to an ESI-triple quadrupole-linear ion trap (QTRAP) mass spectrometer. 

Metabolite identification and quantification were performed as previously described.31 Metabolite identification was based on the primary and secondary spectral data annotated by public databases, namely, MassBank (http://www.massbank.jp/), KNAPSAcK (http:// kanaya.naist.jp/KNApSAcK/), HMDB (http://www.hmdb.ca/), Mo-ToDB (http://www.ab.wur.nl/moto/), and METLIN (http://metlin. scripps.edu/index.php). Metabolite quantification was performed using multiple reaction monitoring. The full data set of the annotated metabolites is listed in Table S8. 

Significantly regulated metabolites between groups were determined by VIP (variable importance in project) $\geq 1$ and absolute Log2FC (fold change) $\geq 1$ . VIP values were extracted from the orthogonal partial least-squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) results, which also contained score plots and permutation plots, and were generated using the R package MetaboAnalystR. The data were log-transformed (log2) and mean-centered prior to OPLS-DA. A permutation test (200 permutations) was performed to avoid overfitting. The identified metabolites were annotated using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) compound database, and the annotated metabolites were mapped onto the KEGG pathway database. Pathways with mapped significantly regulated metabolites were then fed into the metabolite set 

enrichment analysis (MSEA), and their significance was determined using a hypergeometric test’s $p$ -values. 

RNA Sequencing and Annotation. All samples were ground in liquid nitrogen, and total RNA was isolated by the TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, CA, USA). To remove DNA, an aliquot of total RNA was treated with DNase (Takara, Dalian, China) using the standard protocol described by the manufacturer. The purity of the RNA samples according to the A260/A280 ratio was determined using a NanoPhotometer; the A260/A280 ratios of all samples were in the approximate range of 1.9−2.1. The integrity of the RNA samples was assessed using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer, and samples with no signs of degradation were selected for further analysis. 

Sixteen samples, including one mixed sample from the far-red group and three bioreplicates per group from the other five treatments, were used to construct the libraries. mRNA was isolated from total RNA using magnetic beads with oligo (dT) primers; cDNA was synthesized using a cDNA synthesis kit (TaKaRa, Dalian, China), and the sequencing adapter was linked to both ends. Library preparations were sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 4000 platform. After quality control was performed, clean reads were compared with the reference genome (not published). The unigenes were annotated in the following databases: KEGG, NR, SwissProt, GO, KOG, and TrEMBL. The amino acid sequences of the Unigenes were aligned with the Pfam database with HMMER software. The raw data were submitted to NCBI (PRJNA831043). 

Analysis of DEGs. Clean reads were mapped to Unigenes by Bowtie $\dot { 2 } \ \mathrm { v } 2 . 2 . 5 , \textdegree 2$ and gene expression levels were calculated through RSEM v1.2.12.33 Differential expression analysis was performed by DEseq2.34,35 A fold change of ${ \ge } 2 . 0 0$ and an adjusted $P$ value of ${ \le } 0 . 0 5$ were set as the thresholds to establish significantly different expressions. All DEGs were analyzed using KEGG and Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment. 

Real-Time qPCR Analysis. Real-time (RT)-qPCR was performed as previously described.24 Each sample was replicated 3 times. Specific primers were designed with Primer Premier 5 software (Table S9). Gene expression levels under different treatments were measured with the CFX96 RT system (Bio-Rad, USA) using a SYBR Premix Ex TaqII (TaKaRa, Japan). The 25S rRNA gene obtained from C. tinctorius L. was used as an internal reference gene to identify the differences in each cDNA template. The RT-qPCR cycling conditions were as follows: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , followed by 40 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s and $6 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s. The $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T }$ method was employed to analyze relative gene expressions.36 The analysis of variance (ANOVA)- Tukey’s test in SPSS (version 20) was used for data analysis. Two PAL (phenylalanine ammonia-lyase) genes, HCT (shikimate Ohydroxy cinnamoyltransferase) gene, CHS (chalcone synthase) gene, FLS (flavonol synthase) gene, and three UGT (UDP glycosyltransferase) genes related to flavonoid biosynthesis, namely, CtPAL1, CtPAL2, CtHCT, CtCHS, CtFLS, CtUGT1, CtUGT2, and CtUGT3, were successfully amplified and selected to confirm the results. 

Molecular Cloning and Phylogenetic Analysis. A mixed cDNA library containing cDNA from different light treatments was constructed. Total RNA was isolated using TRIzol, after which firststrand cDNA was synthesized from total RNA using a cDNA reverse transcription kit (TaKaRa, Japan). Full-length CDS sequences of the three candidate UGT genes were obtained from third-generation transcriptome sequencing data.37 Specific primer pairs were designed with Primer Premier 5.0 (Table S9). PCR was performed as follows: initial denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 min; 34 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s, 58 $^ \circ \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \ \mathbf { s } ,$ and $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 min; and a final extension at $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 8 min. The PCR products were cloned into pMD19-T vectors (TaKaRa, Japan). Different UGT amino acid sequences were downloaded from NCBI. The UGT phylogenetic tree was constructed using the Geneious Prime software.

# RESULTS

Flavonoid Metabolites and Transcript Analysis of Safflower Samples under Different Light Quality Treatments. The flavonoid metabolites of the four groups of safflower flowers treated with blue light (Bl), far-red light (FR), red light (Re), and UV−B (UV) were compared to those of safflower flowers grown under white light (Wh) and dark condition $\mathrm { ( D r ) }$ , respectively. A total of 114 flavonoid metabolites were detected (Table S1), 32 of which were differentially metabolized (Table S2). Compared with that observed under white light, safflower exhibited the greatest differences in flavonoid metabolites under far-red light, UV−B, and dark conditions. Compared with those grown under dark conditions, the flavonoid metabolites of safflower grown under far-red light and UV−B exhibited the greatest differences (Figure 1A). Thirty-two differentially metabolized flavonoids belonged to 7 different secondary classification components, including flavanol, flavonoid carboglycoside, isoflavone, flavone, flavonol, orange flavonoid, and dihydroflavonoid. Among them, flavonols and flavones had the most differential metabolites with 11 and 9 species, respectively (Figure 1B). In addition, 18 of the 32 flavonoids were glycosides, including 15 O-glycosides and 3 C-glycosides. Most of the aglycones of these glycosides had flavonol or flavone structures. The structures of 32 differentially metabolized flavonoids are listed in Figure S1. 

Through transcript analysis, a total of 121 GB of clean data was obtained. The Q30 value of each sample was greater than $9 1 \%$ . The clean read quality metrics are listed in Table S3. More than $9 5 \%$ of clean reads were mapped to the reference genome (Table S4). Based on gene expression levels, 1269 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified (Table S5). Compared with those in white light or dark conditions, DEGs in the FR group were the most abundant, followed by those in the UV group (Table S6). Eight DEGs were annotated to the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, including two PAL, CHS, HCT, and FLS and three UGTs, named CtPAL1, CtPAL2, CtCHS, CtHCT, CtFLS, CtUGT1, CtUGT2 and CtUGT3 respectively. Eight DEGs were verified by RT-qPCR. The RT-qPCR results were consistent with those of RNA-Seq (Figure S2), indicating that the sequencing data were accurate. Expression of CtUGT1 increased under blue and UV light and decreased under FR light. Expression of CtUGT2 increased under blue and red light and decreased in the dark. Expression of CtUGT3 increased under blue light and decreased under FR and dark conditions (Figure 2). Flavonoid glycosides that were significantly related to the three differentially expressed UGT genes were screened. Finally, the contents of 9 flavonoid glycosides were significantly correlated with the expression of CtUGT1, CtUGT2, and CtUGT3, mainly 3-O glycosides and 7- O glycosides of flavonol (Table 1).

Biochemical Properties and Kinetic Parameter Analysis for CtUGT3. To examine the biochemical properties of CtUGT3, the temperature, reaction time, divalent metal ions, and pH were optimized using 1 as the sugar acceptor and UDP-glucose as the sugar donor (Figure S18). CtUGT3 exhibited the best activity in the range $3 7 - 5 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . In addition to the inhibitory effect of ${ \bar { \mathrm { C o } } } ^ { 2 + }$ , most divalent metal ions had no obvious effect on the activity of CtUGT3. The activity of CtUGT3 was the highest in the $\mathrm { N a _ { 2 } H P O _ { 4 } \mathrm { - N a H _ { 2 } P O _ { 4 } } }$ buffer at pH 8.

Analysis of Active Sites of CtUGT3 Based on Autodocking and Mutagenesis. Ten amino acids, Gly15, His16, Thr136, Ser137, Asn247, Ser276, Gln345, Trp363, Asn364, and Glu384, showed interactions with kaempferol and UDP-glucose in autodocking results (Figure 7A). Among them, Asn247, Ser276, and Gln345 formed hydrogen bonds with kaempferol, while the remaining residues interacted with UDP-glucose at the same time. The three candidate CtUGTs 

and closely evolved UGTs were aligned. A reported 3-O-GT, Sb3GT1, was also compared with candidates, as CtUGT3 showed 3-O-glycosylated ability in vivo. Six amino acids were highly conserved among UGTs, including His16, Ser276, Gln345, Trp363, Asn364, and Glu384 (Figure S20). To explore the key residues of CtUGT3 glycosylation, six mutants were cloned and used to catalyze kaempferol, including G15T, T136P, S137G, N247S, S276T, and E384D. The recombinant proteins of the six purified mutants are shown in Figure S21. The glycosyltransferase activity of G15T was decreased. T136P produced more 7-O-glycosylated products instead of 3-O glycosylation products. S276T and E384D showed a higher 3- O-glycoside content than the wild type (Figure 7B). Kaempferol 7-O-glucoside was then used as a substrate to further confirm the 3-O-glycosylation abilities of CtUGT3 and its mutants. G15T and T136P merely produced a small amount of kaempferol 3,7-di-O-glucosides, while S276T and E384D obtained significantly more di-O-glucoside products than the wild types (Figure 7C). These results confirmed that the 3-O-glycoxylation abilities of S276T and E384D were superior to the others, and G15, T136, S276, and E384 were important residues for the CtUGT3 glycosylation activity.

As the active components of safflower only exist in the flowers, we established the protoplast system of the safflower corolla, providing a reference for further analysis of the biosynthesis of active ingredients in safflower and other medicinal or horticultural plants using flowers. In the protoplast that was successfully transformed into CtUGT3, obvious red fluorescence under DsRed was observed, and the cell color was also deeper. At the same time, the most common chalcones, kaempferol and its glycosides in safflower, such as HSYA, AB, and astragalin, were identified in protoplasts, and the astragalin (kaempferol 3-O-glucoside) content was significantly increased. However, only kaempferol glycosides were detected in the protoplasts, and no glycosides of other flavonols, such as quercetin and isorhamnetin, were identified. It may be due to the fact that kaempferol is the most abundant flavonol in safflower,5 and the protoplasts were prepared from fresh, unflowering corollas, while metabolomics measured blooming flowers, resulting in slight differences in their compositions. On the other hand, although CtUGT3 exhibited 3-OH and 7-OH glycosylation activity in vitro, no significant changes were detected in any component containing 7-Oglycoside in corolla protoplasts. The results proved that CtUGT3 exhibited flavonoid O-glycosylation activities, especially the kaempferol 3-OH glycosylation ability, in safflower flowers. The results also showed that the safflower corolla 

protoplast is an ideal material for verifying the GTs involved in flavonoid biosynthesis in safflower in vivo. 

Most flavonoids in plants exist as their glycosides, and the most abundant flavonoid glycosides in plants are flavone O- or C-glycosides and flavonol O-glycosides, with O-glycosides at positions 3 or 7 being the most common. Sb3GT1 from Scutellaria baicalensis could 3-O glycosylate 17 flavonol aglycones, accepting five different sugar donors (UDP-Glc/- Gal/-GlcNAc/-Xyl/-Ara).49 NpUGT6 has been identified in the embryos of Nelumbo nucifera and catalyzes the 3-OH glycosylation of quercetin.50 7-O-GT is another common enzyme that catalyzes flavonoid glycosylation. A flavonoid 7-Oglycosyltransferase, CsUGT75L12, is identified from Camellia sinensis and was involved in the biosynthesis of bitter compounds.15 7-O-GT from Iris tectorum, UGT73CD1, could glycosylate 7-OH and showed strong catalytic promiscuity for 12 types of flavonoids and 3,4-dichloroaniline to form O- or N-glycosides.14 Most GTs have broad substrate promiscuity, and some glycosylate at different positions for different substrate types. O-GT in Tripterygium wilfordii, TwUGT3, accepts luteolin, quercetin, pinocembrin, and genistein to produce luteolin 7-O-glucoside, quercetin 3-Oglucoside, pinocembrin 7-O-glucoside, and genistein $4 ^ { \prime }$ -Oglucoside, respectively.51 In safflower, UGT73AE1 was the first identified enzyme. It exhibited the capability to glycosylate diverse acceptors to generate N-, O-, or S-glycosides.2 Subsequently, UGT71E5 was found in safflower.47 It can generate N-glycoside from the abundant O-glycoside and glycosylate 4 flavonoids (quercetin, naringenin, genistein, and 

phloretin) to di-O or multi-O-glucosides. However, most GTs in medicinal plants or edible plants were only identified in vitro, and their true effects in vivo may differ from their activity in vitro. 

In this study, CtUGT3 glycosylated the flavone (apigenin), dihydroflavone (naringenin), flavonol (kaempferol, quercetin, and isorhamnetin), and chalcone (naringenin chalcone) structures to their O-glucosides and showed 7-OH and 3- OH glycosylation activities at the same time when flavonols were used as aglycones in vitro. Specifically, CtUGT3 could produce 3,7-di-O-glucoside from kaempferol and kaempferol 7- O-glucoside, but it is difficult to catalyze 3-O-glucoside to produce 3,7-di-O-glucoside. However, when CtUGT3 was overexpressed in corolla cells, only a significant increase in astragalin (kaempferol 3-O-glycoside) was detected, instead of an increase in the content of 7-O-glycoside or 3,7-di-Oglucoside. On the other hand, although the $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ value reflected that the affinity between CtUGT3 and kaempferol was relatively weak, the efficiency of CtUGT3 catalyzing kaempferol was the highest as shown by the $K _ { \mathrm { { c a t } } } / K _ { \mathrm { { m } } }$ value. It was speculated that CtUGT3 might compete with or cooperate with other enzymes in vivo, which is different from the results of enzyme activity determination in vitro. 

To further understand the CtUGT3 catalytic mechanism, autodocking and mutagenesis experiments were performed. T145 and D390 in GgCGT aligned with T136 and E384 in CtUGT3, respectively. Researchers have confirmed that sugar donor selectivity is controlled by hydrogen-bond interactions of sugar hydroxyl groups with D390, T145, and other residues.18 The GgCGT/UDP-glucose structure indicated that the 6-OH, 3-OH, and 4-OH groups of the glucose moiety could form hydrogen bonds with T145 and D390. Similarly, the autodocking results of CtUGT3 showed that T136 and E384 formed hydrogen bonds with the glucose moiety (Figure 7A). Therefore, we speculated that T136 and E384 played an essential role in stabilizing UDP-glucose. Sb3GT1 is a 3-Oglycosyltransferase found in S. baicalensis. T16 and T281 in Sb3GT1 aligned with G15 and S276 in CtUGT3, respectively. Researchers believed that T16 and T281 stabilized the sugar donor.49 In this study, G15 interacted with UDP-glucose, and the glycosylation ability of the G15T mutant was reduced. But S276 interacted with 7-OH of kaempferol instead of the sugar donor through one hydrogen bond. Based on enzyme assays results of CtUGT3 in vitro, we speculated that 7-OH glycosylation might compete with 3-OH glycosylation when CtUGT3 catalyzed kaempferol, resulting in S276T mutant affecting 7-OH glycosylation activity and then obtaining more 3-OH glycosylation products. 

In summary, three differentially expressed UGT genes related to flavonoid 3-O or 7-O-glucosides contents were screened from metabolomic and transcriptome analyses performed using safflower under different light quality treatments. Safflower corolla protoplasts were further used to confirm the flavonoid O-glycosylation ability of UGT candidates in vivo for the first time. CtUGT3 showed the flavonoid 3-OH glycosylation ability, and the astragalin (kaempferol 3-O-glucoside) content was more than doubled when CtUGT3 was overexpressed. CtUGT3 exhibited flavonoid 7-OH and 3-OH glycosylation activities in enzyme assays in vitro. Molecular modeling and site-directed mutagenesis revealed that G15, T136, S276, and E384 were critical catalytic residues for the glycosylation ability of CtUGT3. These results demonstrate that CtUGT3 has a flavonoid 3-OH 

glycosylation function and is involved in the biosynthesis of astragalin in safflower. This study provides insights into the catalytic mechanisms of flavonoid O-glycosyltransferases and makes a reference for flavonoid biosynthesis genes research in nonmodel plants.
