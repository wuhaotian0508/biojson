# ABSTRACT

The plant cuticle and cell wall are pivotal extracellular barriers safeguarding crops against drought, yet their coordinated regulation remains poorly understood. Here, we identify BnaC9.MYB46, an R2R3-MYB transcription factor in the polyploid oilseed Brassica napus, as a dual-function regulatory hub that synchronises cuticle reinforcement and secondary cell wall remodelling to enhance drought resilience. Functional analyses reveal that BnaC9.MYB46 directly activates key genes involved in wax and cutin biosynthesis—including BnaA1.MYB106, BnaLACS3/4/9, BnaKCR1/KCR2, BnaC2.KCS19 and BnaA9.CER1-2—while simultaneously repressing the wax inhibitor BnaC9.DEWAX1. These actions lead to thicker cuticles, enhanced deposition of alkane-rich waxes, modest alterations in cutin-like fatty acids, reduced cuticular permeability and improved water retention. Notably, BnaC2.KCS19 and the BnaC9.DEWAX1–BnaA9.CER1-2 module are validated as critical mediators underpinning wax phenotype and drought tolerance. In parallel, BnaC9.MYB46 promotes the expression of secondary cell wall biosynthesis genes (BnaC9.KOR1, BnaC2.IRX8, BnaC1.F5H), enhancing cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin deposition to stabilise vascular integrity under water deficit. By orchestrating both cuticle fortification and cell wall remodelling, BnaC9.MYB46 establishes dual protective barriers that limit water loss and preserve physiological function. This study redefines MYB46 as a central integrator of extracellular barrier formation and highlights BnaC9.MYB46 as a promising molecular target for breeding drought-resilient rapeseed cultivars through coordinated enhancement of cuticle and cell wall properties. 

# 1 | Introduction

The plant cuticle and cell wall represent two major extracellular barriers that are essential for survival under terrestrial conditions. The cuticle, composed mainly of cutin polyesters 

and cuticular waxes, evolved as a hydrophobic extension of the epidermal cell wall to minimise water loss, regulate gas exchange and protect against abiotic and biotic stresses (Riederer and Schreiber 2001; Yeats and Rose 2013). Beyond desiccation avoidance, the cuticle dynamically mediates plant–environment 

interactions, contributing to UV protection, pathogen resistance, mechanical strength and developmental processes (Bargel et al. 2006; Chassot et al. 2008; Fich et al. 2016; González Moreno et al. 2022; Heredia 2003). 

The functional versatility of the cuticle derives from its bipartite architecture: a cross-linked cutin matrix scaffolded with C16 and $\mathrm { C 1 8 ~ \omega \omega }$ -hydroxy fatty acids, and a hydrophobic wax layer enriched with very long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs, C20– C40) and their derivatives (Yeats and Rose 2013). Recent studies have delineated the biochemical pathways underlying wax biosynthesis and export (Kunst and Samuels  2003; Lee and Suh 2015), as well as cutin monomer synthesis and polymerisation (Kong et al. 2020; Yeats and Rose 2013). Transcription factors (TFs), such as WIN1/SHN1 and its homologues (SHN2 and SHN3), WRI4 and DEWAX1/2 have been shown to positively or negatively regulate cuticular wax or cutin biosynthesis (Aharoni et al. 2004; Al-Abdallat et al. 2014; Go et al. 2014; Kannangara et  al.  2007; Kim et  al.  2018; Park et  al.  2016; Shi et al. 2013). Several MYB family members (e.g., MYB94, MYB96, MYB106, MYB16) have been implicated in promoting wax and cutin production, thereby enhancing drought tolerance (Castorina et al. 2020; Lee et al. 2016; Oshima et al. 2013; Seo et al. 2011). 

Despite these advances, two critical gaps remain. First, although the cuticle is developmentally linked to the epidermal cell wall, it is unclear whether their biosynthetic pathways are co-regulated by shared transcriptional programmes. Notably, overexpression of MYB41 in Arabidopsis disrupts cuticle integrity and increases leaf permeability while simultaneously altering expression of genes involved in cuticle assembly and cell wall biosynthesis, hinting at possible cross-regulation (Cominelli et al. 2008). Second, most mechanistic insights originate from Arabidopsis, whereas key structural and compositional differences exist in crops. In Brassica napus, for instance, C29 alkane, C29 secondary alcohol and C29 ketone dominate the leaf wax profile, with C29 alkane being the most abundant constituent conferring enhanced drought tolerance (Tassone et  al.  2016; Wang et  al.  2020). In contrast, Arabidopsis leaf waxes are primarily composed of C31 alkanes, with negligible ketone or secondary alcohol content (Jenks et al. 1995, 2002). Given that polyploidy in B. napus may confer regulatory diversification, crop-specific investigations into cuticle–cell wall coordination are urgently needed. 

As the world's second-largest oilseed crop, B. napus suffers significant yield losses (up to $6 0 \%$ ) under drought conditions (Batool et al. 2023; Secchi et al. 2023; Tesfamariam et al. 2010; Zhu et al. 2016). Cuticular transpiration, typically accounting for $5 \% - 1 0 \%$ of total water loss under well-watered conditions, can rise dramatically when stomata close during drought (Duursma et  al.  2019; Kerstiens  1996; Riederer and Schreiber  2001; Schönherr 1976). Thus, optimising cuticle properties represents an underexplored avenue for improving drought resilience in rapeseed. 

In this study, we demonstrate that BnaC9.MYB46, a MYB46 ortholog in B. napus, has evolved a dual regulatory role: while retaining its conserved function in activating secondary cell wall biosynthesis, as established in Arabidopsis and other plant 

species (Goicoechea et al. 2005; Zhong et al. , 2007, 2011, 2013) it also enhances cuticle properties by directly promoting cuticular wax biosynthesis and influencing the accumulation of certain cutin-like fatty acids. We uncover two key regulatory modules involving BnaC2.KCS19 and the BnaC9.DEWAX1– BnaA9.CER1-2 axis that underpin alkane-rich wax production. Moreover, we show that BnaC9.MYB46 coordinates these external and internal structural barriers to minimise water loss and maintain physiological function under drought. These findings redefine MYB46 as a central integrator of extracellular barrier formation and provide a promising molecular strategy for breeding drought-resilient B. napus cultivars. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | BnaC9.MYB46 Overexpression Enhances Cuticle Deposition and Drought Tolerance in B. napus

We identified BnaC09G0531900ZS as BnaC9.MYB46, an ortholog of Arabidopsis MYB46 (Figure  S1a) and confirmed its molecular identity as an R2R3-MYB transcription factor. Subcellular localization assays in Nicotiana benthamiana epidermal cells verified that BnaC9.MYB46 predominantly localises to the nucleus (Figure S1b), whereas yeast transactivation assays demonstrated its capacity to drive reporter gene expression (Figure S1c), consistent with its predicted R2 and R3 DNA-binding domains (Figure  S1d). Expression analysis showed that BnaC9.MYB46 was broadly expressed, with higher transcript levels in stem peels and developing siliques (Figure  S2a). Notably, expression was significantly enriched in leaf epidermal peels of high-wax cultivars compared with low-wax lines (Figure S2b), suggesting a role in cuticle formation. Histochemical GUS staining driven by the BnaC9.MYB46 promoter in transgenic Arabidopsis showed strong activity in leaves. Notably, leaf cross sectional analysis revealed GUS signal predominantly in epidermal cells, supporting epidermalspecific promoter activity (Figure S2c). 

To dissect its functional role, we generated transgenic B. napus lines overexpressing BnaC9.MYB46 (BnMYB46-OE3/OE13) and RNA interference (RNAi) lines (BnMYB46-Ri1/Ri10). qRT–PCR confirmed that BnaC9.MYB46 expression was significantly elevated in BnMYB46-OE lines and reduced in BnMYB46-Ri lines compared to wild-type (WT) plants (Figure 1a). Phenotypic analysis revealed that BnMYB46-OE plants exhibited a pronounced glaucous (waxy) appearance on leaves, whereas BnMYB46-Ri plants displayed a glossy phenotype under both growth chamber (Figure  1b) and field conditions (Figure  1c). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) showed that BnMYB46-OE leaves developed larger and denser epicuticular wax crystals compared to WT, whereas BnMYB46-Ri plants displayed slightly sparse wax crystal distribution (Figure  1d). # 2.2 | BnaC9.MYB46 Directly Regulates Genes Involved in Cuticle Biosynthesis

To elucidate the molecular basis of BnaC9.MYB46-mediated cuticle enhancement, we investigated its regulatory impact on key genes associated with cuticular wax and cutin biosynthesis. qRT–PCR analysis revealed that BnaC9.MYB46 overexpression significantly upregulated a suite of genes involved in very long-chain fatty acid (VLCFA) elongation and cuticle lipid metabolism. These included the $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA synthase BnaC2.KCS19, $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA reductases BnaC2.KCR1 and BnaC5.KCR2, aldehyde decarbonylase BnaA9.CER1-2, longchain acyl-CoA synthetases BnaC9.LACS3, BnaC1.LACS4, BnaA7.LACS9, and the MIXTA-like transcription factor BnaA1.MYB106 (Figure 3a). In contrast, expression of these genes was significantly reduced in BnMYB46-Ri lines, consistent with decreased cutin and wax deposition observed in RNAi plants (Figures  1g,h and 3a). Notably, expression of the transcriptional repressor BnaC9.DEWAX1, previously reported to negatively regulate wax biosynthesis (Wang 

et al. 2023), was inversely correlated with BnaC9.MYB46 levels—downregulated in overexpression lines and elevated in RNAi lines (Figure 3a), aligning with corresponding changes in wax composition. 

To determine whether BnaC9.MYB46 directly binds to these gene promoters, we identified secondary wall MYBresponsive elements (SMREs) within the upstream regions of candidate target genes and performed chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assays using BnaC9.MYB46-GFP transgenic lines. Immunoprecipitation with anti-GFP antibodies followed by qPCR analysis showed significant enrichment (3- to 5-fold) of SMRE-containing promoter fragments from BnaC2.KCS19, BnaC2.KCR1, BnaC5.KCR2, BnaC9.LACS3, BnaC1.LACS4, BnaA7.LACS9, BnaA1.MYB106, BnaA9.CER1-2 and BnaC9.DEWAX1 compared with control input (Figure  3b), confirming in  vivo promoter occupancy. # 3 | Discussion

Drought stress is a major constraint to global crop productivity, highlighting the need to understand how plants regulate extracellular structural barriers. Under water deficit, plants reinforce the cuticle to limit non-stomatal water loss and remodel cell walls to balance mechanical support with growth flexibility (Riederer and Schreiber  2001; Tenhaken  2015; Vaahtera 

et al. 2019; Yeats and Rose 2013). Although both the cuticle and the cell wall are essential for maintaining tissue integrity under drought, the transcriptional coordination between these two barriers remains poorly understood in crops. 

Here, we identify BnaC9.MYB46, an R2R3-MYB transcription factor in B. napus, as a dual-function regulator that orchestrates both cuticle fortification and secondary cell wall remodelling to confer drought resilience (Figure  7). Although orthologs of MYB46 are well established as master regulators of secondary wall biosynthesis in Arabidopsis and other species (Goicoechea et al. 2005; Zhong et al. 2007, 2011, 2013), our findings extend the functional repertoire of BnaC9.MYB46 by uncovering its previously unrecognised role in regulating cuticular wax biosynthesis and contributing to water loss control. 

Overexpression of BnaC9.MYB46 in B. napus led to increased cuticle thickness, enhanced accumulation of alkane-rich waxes and moderate changes in cutin-like fatty acid profiles. These anatomical and biochemical changes were associated with reduced cuticular permeability, improving water retention and enhanced drought tolerance in B. napus (Figures 1 and 2). We define two regulatory modules underlying this phenotype. First, BnaC9.MYB46 directly activates BnaC2.KCS19, an epidermisspecific $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA synthase catalysing the first condensation step in VLCFA elongation (Figures  3 and 4b) (Millar and Kunst  1997). Functional analyses demonstrated that overexpression of BnaC2.KCS19 phenocopied the glaucous, high-wax, drought-tolerant traits observed in BnaC9.MYB46-overexpressing plants, whereas RNAi suppression of BnaC2.KCS19 resulted in reduced wax load, increased cuticular permeability, and heightened drought sensitivity. These findings firmly position BnaC2.KCS19 as a key downstream effector mediating BnaC9.MYB46-driven cuticular wax biosynthesis. However, the expected chain-length specificity across all wax classes was not uniformly observed in BnaC2.KCS19 transgenic lines. This could reflect functional redundancy among KCS isoforms or buffering by downstream metabolic networks (Millar and Kunst 1997; Joubès et al. 2008). Importantly, despite its effect on drought tolerance traits, BnaC2.KCS19 manipulation did not alter seed yield under well-watered field conditions (Figure S5), suggesting a context-dependent role for cuticle traits in agronomic performance, as also noted in previous studies that alterations in wax biosynthesis often have neutral effects on growth or yield in the absence of stress (Bi et al. 2018; Bourdenx et al. 2011). 

Second, BnaC9.MYB46 directly represses BnaC9.DEWAX1, an AP2/ERF transcriptional inhibitor of wax biosynthesis (Ju et al. 2017; Wang et al. 2023), whereas concurrently upregulating BnaA9.CER1-2 (Figure 3), a critical aldehyde decarbonylase responsible for alkane production (Bourdenx et al. 2011; Wang et al. 2020). Alkanes are vital cuticle components that form a hydrophobic barrier limiting non-stomatal water loss (Buschhaus and Jetter 2011; Dang and Suh 2025). Notably, C29 alkanes are considered promising targets for improving drought tolerance in B. napus, given their high heritability and strong correlation with stress resilience (Tassone et al. 2016). Overexpression of BnaA9.CER1-2 enhances C29-alkane production and improves drought tolerance in B. napus (Wang et  al.  2020), whereas BnaC9.DEWAX1 restricts this process by suppressing BnaA9.CER1-2 expression (Wang et  al.  2023). Through concurrent repression of BnaC9.DEWAX1 and activation of BnaA9.CER1-2, BnaC9.MYB46 amplifies alkane biosynthesis, thereby reinforcing the hydrophobic cuticular barrier. 

Beyond wax production, BnaC9.MYB46 also activates a broader transcriptional network involved in cuticle-related lipid metabolism. It upregulates additional FAE and cuticle-associated genes, including $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA reductases (BnaC2.KCR1, BnaC5.KCR2), long-chain acyl-CoA synthetases (BnaC9.LACS3, BnaC1.LACS4, BnaA7.LACS9), and the MIXTA-like TF BnaA1.MYB106 (Figure  3). In Arabidopsis, KCR1 facilitates VLCFA elongation for wax and other lipid products (Beaudoin et al. 2009); LACS genes are involved in cuticular lipid metabolism for cutin and wax (Lü et al. 2009; Zhao et al. 2019), and MYB106 regulates cuticle formation by controlling the biosynthesis of both wax and cutin (Oshima et  al.  2013). Although changes in cutin composition were not uniformly significant at the monomer level, the total cutin-like fatty acid content showed moderate increases or decreases in overexpression and RNAi lines, respectively, supporting a regulatory influence on cuticle development (Figure 1g,h). 

Importantly, BnaC9.MYB46 also maintains its conserved role in regulating secondary cell wall biosynthesis. Overexpression 

of BnaC9.MYB46 leads to elevated expression of BnaC9.KOR1, BnaC2.IRX8 and BnaC1.F5H, resulting in enhanced deposition of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin in both stems and leaves (Figure 6). These target genes are well established components of the secondary wall biosynthetic pathway: KOR1 encodes a membrane-bound endo-1,4- $\cdot \beta$ -d-glucanase essential for cellulose microfibril assembly (Vain et  al.  2014; Zuo et  al.  2000), IRX8 functions as a galacturonosyltransferase involved in xylan backbone synthesis (Persson et al. 2007), and F5H catalyses the hydroxylation of coniferaldehyde, a key step in the formation of syringyl lignin monomers (Humphreys et al. 1999; Tetreault et al. 2020). In B. napus, loss-of-function mutations in F5H have been associated with reduced S/G lignin ratios, leading to compromised mechanical strength and impaired vascular development (Cao et  al.  2022). This conserved regulatory framework ensures that BnaC9.MYB46 continues to reinforce cell wall integrity and mechanical resilience—functions particularly vital for maintaining vascular conductivity and structural support under drought conditions. Histological analyses revealed enhanced lignification in stem and vascular tissues of OE lines, indicating BnaC9.MYB46 acts beyond the epidermis. 

Although BnaC9.MYB46 transcript abundance appears low in bulk rosette leaf tissue (Figure  S2a), its promoter activity is highly enriched in the epidermis, as revealed by both epidermal peel qRT–PCR in high-wax cultivars and promoter–GUS staining in Arabidopsis (Figure  S2b,c). This localised expression likely explains its regulatory role in cuticle formation. Meanwhile, strong promoter–GUS activity in stems and petioles suggests a broader spatial function, including subepidermal cell layers such as vascular bundles. These patterns support a model in which BnaC9.MYB46 coordinates structural barrier formation across multiple tissue layers to promote both external and internal drought adaptation. 

Interestingly, RNAi-mediated suppression of BnaC9.MYB46 had only mild effects on cell wall composition and target gene expression (Figure  6). We hypothesise that this may be due to genetic compensation. Indeed, expression of BnaMYB83, a closely related paralog of BnaC9.MYB46, was unchanged in BnMYB46-Ri plants compared to WT (Figure S7), suggesting that its constitutive expression may partially buffer the effects of BnaC9.MYB46 knockdown on cell wall-related pathways. This interpretation is consistent with findings in Arabidopsis, where MYB46 and MYB83 act redundantly as master regulators of secondary wall biosynthesis (Zhong and Ye 2012). 

Our physiological assays further support the contribution of BnaC9.MYB46 to non-stomatal water loss regulation. Although differences in water loss rates among genotypes were clear during the early dehydration phase, later-stage convergence suggests that measurements captured both dynamic and steadystate phases of desiccation. This phenomenon has been previously discussed by Zeisler-Diehl et al. (2017), who highlight the importance of measuring cuticular transpiration under carefully controlled conditions. Although we conducted assays in the dark with detached leaves to minimise stomatal influence, future work including stomatal aperture assays will help refine this interpretation. 

Lastly, whereas BnaC9.MYB46 significantly improved drought tolerance at the seedling stage, its impact on mature plant performance under field drought remains untested. Additional trials in water-limited field conditions are needed to evaluate the full agronomic potential of this regulator. 

In conclusion, our findings establish BnaC9.MYB46 as a central integrator of cuticle and secondary wall biosynthesis, acting in both epidermal and internal tissues to enhance drought adaptation in B. napus. This dual regulatory mechanism not only broadens our understanding of MYB46 family functions but also provides a promising molecular target for engineering drought-resilient Brassica crops. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

Bassica napus cultivars Westar and Zhongshuang11 (ZS11), and Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype Col-0 were used. Plants were grown either under controlled growth chamber conditions $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod) or in the field following standard agronomic practices. 

# 4.2 | Generation of Transgenic Plants

The full-length coding sequences (CDS) of BnaC9.MYB46 and BnaC2.KCS19 were amplified and cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector for constitutive overexpression. RNA interference (RNAi) constructs were generated by cloning CDS fragments into the pCAMBIA1301 vector. Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of B. napus hypocotyls was performed as described previously (Zhang et  al.  2020). Hygromycin-resistant plants were screened by PCR and qRT-PCR analyses. All primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table S2. Homologous $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ generation transgenic lines were selected for experiments. 

# 4.3 | Drought Treatment Assays

For drought stress treatment, 4-week-old soil-grown B. napus plants were subjected to drought by withholding water for 12 days. RWC and survival rates after re-watering and other physiological parameters were assessed. To evaluate drought sensitivity during germination, seeds were germinated on filter paper moistened with $1 5 \%$ polyethylene glycol (PEG 6000) to simulate osmotic stress; germination potential, germination rate, fresh/DW and shoot/root length were evaluated. For drought-induced gene expression analysis, ZS11 seedlings were subjected to water deprivation for 6 h prior to RNA extraction and qRT–PCR. 

# 4.4 | qRT–PCR Analysis

Total RNA was extracted from rosette leaves of 5- to 6-week-old B. napus plants and from various tissues including roots, stems, cauline leaves, flowers, siliques and seeds, using the TRIzol reagent (Transgen, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer's 

protocol. First-strand cDNA was synthesised from $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of DNase I-treated RNA using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit (TaKaRa, Dalian, China). qRT–PCR was carried out using the TB Green Premix Ex Taq II (TaKaRa, Dalian, China) on a Bio-Rad CFX96 Real Time PCR System. Gene-specific primers are listed in Table S2. The expression levels of target genes were normalised to the internal reference gene B. napus ACTIN7. Relative transcript abundance was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \mathrm { t } } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen  2001). All reactions were performed in three technical triplicates with three biological replicates. 

# 4.5 | GUS Histochemical and Fluorometric Assays

Approximately $1 . 5 \mathrm { k b }$ promoter regions of BnaC9.MYB46 and BnaC2.KCS19 were amplified using gene-specific primers (Table  S2) and cloned into the pCAMBIA1305 vector. The constructs were introduced into A. thaliana via A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 using the floral dip method (Zhang et al. 2006). Transgenic lines were selected on $\scriptstyle 1 / _ { 2 }$ MS medium supplemented with $3 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ hygromycin. For histochemical GUS staining, samples were treated with ethanol $( 1 0 \% - 1 0 0 \% )$ ) to remove chlorophyll and visualised under a stereomicroscope (Olympus SEX16). For leaf cross sections, stained tissues were embedded in Spurr's resin, and $2 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ sections were cut using an MT990 microtome. For fluorometric GUS quantification, total proteins were extracted from 3-week-old seedlings following 6 h of water deprivation. Protein concentration was determined via the Bradford assay. GUS activity was assayed using 4-methylumbelliferone (4-MU) as a substrate (excitation: $3 6 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , emission: $4 5 5 \mathrm { n m }$ ) on a BioTek Synergy HT microplate reader and expressed as nmol $4 { \mathrm { - } } \mathrm { M U } / \mathrm { m i n } / \mathrm { m g }$ protein. 

# 4.6 | Subcellular Localization

The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was amplified using primers listed in Table  S2 and cloned into the pBI221 vector to generate a GFP fusion construct. The resulting plasmid was co-infiltrated with the nuclear marker mCherry-D53 into Nicotiana tabacum leaves as previously described (Sparkes et al. 2006). Similarly, the CDS of BnaC2.KCS19 was amplified using the same primer list and cloned into the GFP-containing pAN580 vector. The resulting construct was transiently cotransfected into Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts along with the ER marker ER-mKate via PEG-mediated transformation (Yoo et  al.  2007). After incubation in the dark for 12 or 24 h, GFP fluorescence signals were visualised using a Zeiss LSM 780 confocal laser scanning microscope. 

# 4.7 | Transactivation Assay in Yeast

The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was amplified and inserted into the pGBKT7 vector to generate a fusion with the GAL4 DNA-binding domain. The resulting construct was transformed into the Y190 yeast strain. Transformants were selected on synthetic dropout (SD) medium lacking tryptophan (SD/-Trp) supplemented with $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (3-AT) to inhibit background HIS3 expression. Selected colonies were then cultured in SD/-Trp-His medium containing $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-AT for 

$2 4 \mathrm { h }$ to evaluate transactivation activity. $\beta$ -galactosidase activity was assayed using the Yeast Protocols Handbook (Clontech), following the manufacturer's instructions. Positive transcriptional activation was indicated by HIS3 prototrophy and LacZ reporter activity. 

# 4.8 | Assessment of Cuticular Permeability

Cuticle permeability was evaluated using both inward and outward diffusion assays. To assess inward permeability, toluidine blue (TB) staining was conducted following a previously described method with slight modifications (Tanaka et  al.  2004). Fully expanded leaves from 5- to 6-week-old plants were immersed in $0 . 0 5 \%$ TB solution for 10 or $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , then thoroughly rinsed with distilled water and photographed immediately. For outward permeability, two assays were conducted: water loss rate and chlorophyll leaching. To measure water loss, fully expanded rosette leaves were excised, hydrated in distilled water for $6 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ in the dark, blotted dry, and then weighed at regular intervals (every 15 or 30 min) over a 150-min period under dark conditions. Water loss was expressed as the percentage of weight lost relative to the initial fully hydrated weight. The chlorophyll leaching assay was carried out as described previously (Lolle et al. 1997). Excised rosette leaves were weighed and then immersed in $8 0 \%$ ethanol with gentle shaking at room temperature. At designated time points (every 15 or 30 min), the absorbance of the solution was measured at 647 and $6 6 4 \mathrm { n m }$ using a spectrophotometer to quantify chlorophyll release. 

# 4.9 | Scanning and Transmission Electron Microscope (SEM and TEM)

SEM was employed to examine the crystallisation patterns of epicuticular wax. Leaves from 5- to 6-week-old B. napus seedlings were dried, affixed onto standard aluminium stubs, and gold-coated using a sputter coater (Quorum SC-500). The prepared samples were subsequently analysed using a Hitachi S3000-N scanning electron microscope. To assess the ultrastructure of the cuticle, TEM was performed as described previously with minor modifications (Ukitsu et  al.  2007). Leaf samples were fixed overnight with $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde and $2 \%$ glutaraldehyde in $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium cacodylate buffer (pH 7.4) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The samples were then dehydrated, infiltrated, and embedded in LR White resin. Following embedding, the samples were sectioned and observed under a JEM-1200EX TEM. Cuticle thickness measurements were made from TEM images, with at least 12 measurements taken from different areas per biological replicate and a total of three biological replicates. 

# 4.10 | Cuticular Wax Composition Analysis

Leaves from 5- to 6-week-old B. napus plants were submerged in chloroform for 30 s, and hexadecane was added as an internal standard. The samples were then evaporated under nitrogen. Waxes were derivatized by heating the samples at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min in N, O-bis(trimethylsilyl)trifluoroacetamide (BSTFA) and pyridine $\operatorname { ( 1 : 1 ~ v / v ) }$ ). The silylated samples were analysed by 

gas chromatography (GC) with a flame ionisation detector (FID; GC-2010, Shimadzu) and by GC-mass spectrometry (QP2010; Shimadzu). The GC conditions, including retention times and temperatures, were programmed as described previously (Wang et al. 2020). Wax amounts were quantified as micrograms per square centimetre of leaf area. 

# 4.11 | Cutin Polyester Monomers Analysis

Cutin polyester monomers were quantified after depolymerization of delipidated leaf tissues according to established protocols (Jakobson et  al.  2016). The wax-free rosette leaves were dipped in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of chloroform: methanol (1:1, v/v), with the solvent changed daily until the leaves became colourless. The samples were then dried, weighed and prepared for the depolymerization reaction. Each sample was transferred into a glass vial and spiked with $2 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of methyl heptadecanoate as an internal standard. Depolymerization was conducted in 2mL of methanol containing $1 2 \%$ (v/v) hydrochloric acid at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 h in sealed tubes. After cooling to room temperature, 2mL of saturated NaCl solution and $3 \times 1 \mathrm { m L }$ of hexane were added for liquid–liquid extraction of cutin monomers. Combined hexane layers were dried under nitrogen gas. Dried residues were derivatized by adding BSTFA and pyridine (1:1v/v) and incubating at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The final sample, dissolved in hexane, was analysed by GC–mass spectrometry (QP2010; Shimadzu). Quantification was based on the uncorrected FID peak areas relative to the internal standard methyl heptadecanoate, with results expressed as $\mu \mathrm { g }$ per mg of leaf DW. 

# 4.12 | Histochemical Detection of Lignin Deposition and Quantitative Analysis of Cell Wall Components

Petioles and mid-stem segments from BnaC9.MYB46 transgenic and wild-type (WT) B. napus plants at the early flowering stage were collected for lignin visualisation using histochemical methods. Mäule staining was performed to differentiate lignin monomers, with syringyl (S) units appearing red and guaiacyl (G) units appearing brown, as described by Tu et  al.  (2010). Phloroglucinol-HCl staining, which detects coniferyl aldehyde residues in lignin and yields a red to purple-red coloration in lignified tissues, was conducted according to established protocols (Kim et al. 2014; Zhong et al. 2007). Additionally, autofluorescence imaging under UV excitation was used to visualise lignin accumulation based on its natural fluorescence. 

Quantification of lignin, cellulose, and hemicellulose contents was conducted using commercial assay kits (Geruisi-Bio, Suzhou, China; Cat. Nos.: G0708W48 for lignin, G0715W for cellulose, G0716W for hemicellulose), following the manufacturer's instructions with minor modifications: Lignin content was determined using the acetyl bromide method. Samples were incubated in acetyl bromide solution, followed by spectrophotometric measurement at $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Cellulose content was quantified by anthrone-sulfuric acid colorimetry, with absorbance recorded at $6 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Hemicellulose content was measured via acid hydrolysis using hydrochloric acid, followed by detection of released xylose at $4 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . For each genotype, three biological replicates were analysed, and all values were normalised to sample DW. 

# 4.13 | Measurement of ROS, MDA, and Antioxidant Enzyme Activity

Leaves from BnaC9.MYB46 transgenic and WT plants, grown under normal and drought stress conditions, were collected to measure ROS, MDA content and antioxidant enzyme activities. The activities of catalase (CAT), peroxidase (POD), and superoxide dismutase (SOD), as well as the levels of superoxide anion $( 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ , hydrogen peroxide $( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } )$ and MDA were determined according to the protocols provided in the Solarbio kits. All measurements were carried out using an ultraviolet–visible spectrophotometer (UV-2450). 

# 4.14 | Chromatin Immunoprecipitation (ChIP) Assay

The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was fused in-frame with GFP and cloned into the pTA7002 vector. The resulting construct was transformed into B. napus (cv. Westar) as described above. Four-week-old transgenic plants expressing BnaC9.MYB46-GFP were treated with dexamethasone (DEX) to induce transgene expression prior to sample collection. Approximately 2 g of fresh leaf tissue was harvested and crosslinked in $1 \%$ formaldehyde under vacuum for 10 min, followed by quenching with $1 2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ glycine. Chromatin was extracted, sheared by sonication to an average fragment size of 200–500 bp, and clarified by centrifugation. For immunoprecipitation, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of anti-GFP antibody (Proteintech, 50 430-2-AP) was used to pull down MYB46-bound chromatin. Control samples included non-specific IgG (Abmart, Cat. No. b30010) and input DNA, which represented $1 \%$ of the total chromatin before immunoprecipitation. After immunoprecipitation, crosslinks were reversed by incubation at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight, followed by Proteinase K treatment. Immunoprecipitated DNA was purified and analysed by quantitative PCR (qPCR) using primers targeting promoter regions containing SMRE motifs (Table S2). Enrichment of MYB46 binding was calculated as the ratio of ChIP DNA to input DNA for each target region. 

# 4.15 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA)

DNA fragments containing putative BnaC9.MYB46 binding sites SMRE were synthesised and labelled with biotin to serve as probes. The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was subcloned into the pGEX-4T-1 vector to generate a GST fusion protein. The recombinant GST-BnaC9.MYB46 protein was expressed in Escherichia coli and purified using the GST 4FF Sefinose Resin Kit (Sangon). Binding reactions were performed at room temperature for 30 min using the Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime) according to the manufacturer's instructions. To assess binding specificity, 10- or 30-fold excess of unlabeled probes was added as cold competitors. Reaction mixtures were resolved on a $6 \%$ native polyacrylamide gel, followed by transfer to a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon membrane. After UV crosslinking, biotinlabelled DNA–protein complexes were detected via chemiluminescence using streptavidin–HRP. 

# 4.16 | Yeast One-Hybrid (Y1H) Assay

The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was ligated into the pGADT7 activation domain vector, whereas promoter regions of genes related to cuticular wax, cutin, and cell wall biosynthesis were inserted into the pAbAi bait vector. The bait constructs were integrated into the genome of Y1HGold yeast cells and selected on SD/-Ura medium supplemented with Aureobasidin A (AbA) to determine the minimal inhibitory concentration. The effector construct (pGADT7-BnaC9.MYB46) was then transformed into the bait yeast strains and plated on SD/-Leu/AbA medium to evaluate protein–DNA interactions. Positive clones were cultured, adjusted to equal optical densities and serially diluted for spotting onto SD/-Leu/AbA plates to confirm binding specificity. 

# 4.17 | Dual-Luciferase Transcription Activity Assay

Promoter regions of genes involved in cuticular wax, cutin, and cell wall biosynthesis were cloned into the pGreen-II 0800 reporter vector, upstream of the firefly luciferase (LUC) gene. The full-length CDS of BnaC9.MYB46 was inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK vector to serve as the effector construct. The reporter and effector plasmids were co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves using Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transient expression. After incubation in the dark for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , followed by $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ under standard growth conditions, leaf discs were collected for dual-luciferase activity measurement using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega). Firefly luciferase activity was normalised to Renilla luciferase (REN) activity to control for variation in infiltration efficiency. Relevant primers are provided in Table S2. 

# 4.18 | Agronomic and Yield Trait Measurements

To evaluate the impact of genetic modifications on plant performance, a comprehensive set of agronomic and yield-related traits was assessed. These included: plant height, stem diameter, branching height, main inflorescence length, number of effective branches on the main inflorescence, silique length, number of seeds per silique, number of effective siliques per plant, thousand-seed weight and yield per plant. At least 10 biologically independent plants per genotype were sampled for statistical analysis. 

# 4.19 | Phylogenetic Analysis

A phylogenetic analysis was performed to determine the evolutionary relationship between BnaC9.MYB46 and Arabidopsis MYB transcription factors, including members of the 1R-, R2R3-, 3R- and 4R-MYB subfamilies. An unrooted maximumlikelihood tree was constructed using MEGA version 6.0, with 1000 bootstrap replicates to assess the statistical robustness of each node. 

# 4.20 | Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism version 8.0 or SPSS Statistics version 13.0. For comparisons between two groups, Student's t-test was applied, with significance levels indicated as follows: $^ { * } p < 0 . 0 5$ , $^ { * * } p < 0 . 0 1$ , and $^ { * * * } p < 0 . 0 0 1$ . For comparisons among multiple groups, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey's multiple comparison test was performed. Groups not sharing the same lowercase letter differ significantly at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ . All quantitative data are presented as means $\pm$ standard deviation (SD) from at least three independent biological replicates.
