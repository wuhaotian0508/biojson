# ABSTRACT

Optimization of plant architecture requires precise regulation of internode elongation; however, the posttranslational mechanisms that integrate microRNA and phytohormone signaling remain poorly understood. Here, we describe a hierarchical miR164e–NAC32–DELLA regulatory network that controls stem development in maize. Genetic analyses demonstrate that ZmmiR164e negatively regulates its target gene ZmNAC32, with ZmmiR164e overexpression enhancing internode cell elongation and loss–of–function resulting in dwarfism. Notably, ZmNAC32 physically interacts with and stabilizes the DELLA protein ZmD8, as evidenced by increased ZmD8 protein levels in ZmNAC32-overexpressing plants compared with the wild type. Transcriptome profiling reveals that ZmNAC32-mediated regulation of plant height occurs primarily through post-translational stabilization rather than extensive transcriptional reprogramming, with downstream cell wall biosynthesis genes (EXP, XTH, and LAC) showing GA-responsive suppression. Structural analyses further reveal that ZmNAC32 binding stabilizes ZmD8 by shielding the key interaction residue K399, thereby suppressing its degradation. Together, these results identify a miRNA–NAC–DELLA module that governs post-translational protein stability during stem development and provides strategic targets for precision breeding of plant architecture.

# INTRODUCTION

Lodging is a major constraint limiting maize yield improvement and mechanical harvesting. Excessive elongation of basal internodes leads to longer and narrower internodal segments with reduced mechanical strength and is a primary cause of lodging in maize. Therefore, investigating the regulatory mechanisms governing internode elongation has important theoretical and practical value for improving stem quality and lodging resistance. 

Extensive research has demonstrated that auxin (IAA), gibberellins (GA), and brassinosteroids (BR) promote stem and internode 

elongation, while ethylene (ETH) and abscisic acid (ABA) inhibit this process (Wang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020a). These phytohormones often act interactively to regulate internode elongation (Tong et al., 2014). MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are endogenous small non-coding RNAs that play crucial roles in post-transcriptional gene regulation (Bartel, 2004), and increasing evidence emphasizes their interaction with phytohormones (Dong et al., 2022). MiRNAs coordinate with hormones through diverse regulatory modes and play important roles in plant development. GA is crucial in seed germination, stem elongation, and flowering. In rice, the negative regulators of GA signaling, DELLAs, interact with IDD2 to induce OsmiR396

expression, which in turn inhibits its target genes GRFs, leading to a dwarf phenotype resembling GA deficiency (Lu et al., 2020) . DELLAs can also regulate miRNA abundance through PIF4 degradation, thereby influencing miRNA transcription or stability of the miRNA processing complex (Li et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2018b). Conversely, miRNAs directly regulate GA biosynthesis and signal transduction through modules such as miR155–SPL, miR171–SCL, and miR159–GAMYBs (Yu et al., 2012; Ma et al., 2014; Millar et al., 2019). 

The architecture of maize plants is determined by traits including plant height, leaf number, leaf angle, and tassel branching. miR156 has been identified as an important regulator of maize architecture (Jiao et al., 2010) and plays crucial roles in inflorescence development and sex determination (Chuck et al., 2007). miR156 regulates leaf angle and inflorescence structure by targeting genes such as ZmLG1, ZmSBP2, and ZmUB2/3. Mutations in miR166 result in delayed flowering, reduced plant height, decreased tassel length, and fewer tassel branches (Li et al., 2020b). miR169 and miR172 affect flowering time in maize and are associated with reduced plant height (Lauter et al., 2005; Calvin˜ o and Messing, 2013). miR396 regulates leaf size and plant height by targeting ZmGRF10 (Wu et al., 2014). Additionally, miR528 modulates lignin synthesis in maize stems by targeting ZmLAC3 and ZmLAC5, thereby affecting lodging under high nitrogen conditions (Sun et al., 2018a). Together, these studies highlight the important regulatory roles of miRNAs in maize growth and development. However, despite extensive research on miRNAs controlling maize plant architecture, their roles in internode elongation remain relatively unexplored. Because basal internode length influences lodging resistance, it is necessary to explore the molecular mechanisms by which miRNAs regulate maize internode elongation. 

Differential expression analyses of miRNAs between fixed and elongating internodes in maize have revealed that miR160, miR164, miR168, miR396, and miR398 affect internode elongation by targeting genes associated with cell expansion, cell wall synthesis, and hormone signaling (Peng et al., 2019). Overexpression of miR164a increases rice plant height (Fang et al., 2014); miR164 is also involved in the dwarfing responses of Populus euphratica under drought and salt stress (Lu et al., 2017). In maize, miR164 exhibits high expression levels in stems, roots, and grains, and has been implicated in lateral root development and grain expansion (Li et al., 2012; Zheng et al., 2019b). During leaf maturation, miR164 levels decrease, leading to increased expression of ORE1/AtNAC2 (Kim et al., 2009). Furthermore, EXPB14 and EXPB15, downstream targets of NAC32 and NAC40, respectively, contribute to smaller grain size through miR164-dependent regulation (Zheng et al., 2019b), and miR164 modulates lateral root growth by regulating ZmNAC1 expression (Li et al., 2012). 

Although accumulating evidence implicates miR164 in maize organ development through the regulation of NAC transcription factors, its mechanistic role in basal internode elongation—a key determinant of lodging resistance—remains unclear. Our small RNA sequencing (sRNA-seq) analysis identified members of the miR164 family as differentially expressed during internode elongation, classifying them as internode elongation-responsive miR-NAs (Peng et al., 2019). However, the functional interplay

between miR164-mediated ZmNAC regulation and GA signaling during internode elongation has not been defined. Moreover, whether NAC proteins interact with DELLA proteins to coordinate post-translational regulation of cell elongation remains unknown. Here, we propose that ZmmiR164e fine-tunes basal internode architecture by regulating ZmNAC32, which in turn modulates DELLA protein stability to control GA-responsive cell wall remodeling. By integrating gene expression analysis, protein interaction assays, and targeted mutagenesis, we elucidate a regulatory mechanism by which miR164e orchestrates GA-mediated internode elongation in maize. 

# RESULTS

# ZmmiR164e regulates maize internode elongation

Members of the miR164 family are highly conserved during evolution (Figure S2A). To assess the role of miR164 in maize plant height regulation, we generated miR164e overexpression lines and miR164e loss-of-function lines. RT-qPCR analysis indicated that miR164e abundance varied among the different transgenic lines (Figure S1). Plant height and ear height of the transgenic lines were evaluated at maturity (Figure 1A). The plant height of overexpression lines 1 and 2 was slightly higher than that of the control, increasing by $7 . 0 \%$ and $6 . 5 \%$ , respectively. In contrast, the plant height of the loss-of-function lines was significantly lower compared with the control, with a reduction of $8 . 6 \%$ and $1 0 . 8 \%$ for lines 1 and 2, respectively (Figure 1B). The ear height of the miR164e overexpression lines was modestly increased relative to the control, by $8 . 7 \%$ and $5 . 4 \%$ for lines 1 and 2, respectively. Conversely, the ear height of the loss-of-function lines was significantly lower than that of the control, with reductions of $1 3 . 2 \%$ and $12 . 9 \%$ for lines 1 and 2, respectively (Figure 1C). 

The impact of miR164e abundance on maize internode elongation was further investigated. Compared with the wild type, nearly all above-ground internodes of the miR164e overexpression lines were elongated, with the most pronounced increases observed in internodes 6–10 (Figure 1D). Internode elongation was more prominent in overexpression line 1 than in line 2, consistent with the higher miR164e expression level in line 1 (Figures 1E; S1). In contrast, the above-ground internode lengths of both loss-of-function lines were significantly shorter than those of the wild type, with no significant difference observed between the two lines (Figure 1E). 

# ZmmiR164e participates in cell expansion without altering hormone levels

Maize internode elongation is closely associated with the length of individual cells. Scanning electron microscopy analysis of the eighth internode revealed that miR164e expression levels affected cell length within maize internodes (Figure 2A). Compared with the control, overexpression lines 1 and 2 exhibited increases in cell length of $2 5 . 3 \%$ and $7 . 8 \%$ , respectively, while loss-of-function line 1 showed a $1 5 . 9 \%$ reduction in cell length (Figures 2B and 2C). Correlation analysis between relative cell length and relative internode length revealed a highly significant positive correlation (Figure 2D). These findings suggest that miR164e regulates internode elongation primarily by modulating cell length.

# ZmNAC32 suppresses maize internode elongation

Phenotypic analysis at maturity revealed that ZmNAC32 overexpression significantly reduced both plant height and ear height compared with the wild-type (Figures 4A and 4B). Overexpression lines 1 and 2 exhibited reductions of $1 4 . 2 \%$ and $1 1 . 4 \%$ in plant height, and $1 0 . 6 \%$ and $7 . 2 \%$ in ear height, respectively (Figures 4C and 4D). Despite unchanged internode number (Figure 4E), ZmNAC32 overexpression lines showed reduced internode lengths (Figure 4F), indicating that ZmNAC32 inhibits internode elongation rather than internode initiation. These results demonstrate that ZmNAC32 regulates maize plant height via internode elongation, which is consistent with, and genetically supports, the antagonistic relationship between ZmmiR164e and its target gene, as the dwarf phenotype caused by ZmNAC32 overexpression mirrors the effect of 

reduced ZmmiR164e activity. To further examine the cellular basis of this phenotype, paraffin sections of the basal eighth internode were analyzed. Overexpression of ZmNAC32 inhibited internode cell elongation. Compared with the wild type, the length of subepidermal cells in overexpression lines 1 and 2 decreased by $9 . 2 \%$ and $9 . 4 \%$ , respectively (Figure 4G), whereas the length of parenchyma cells decreased by $3 2 . 8 \%$ and $2 6 . 7 \%$ (Figure 4H). 

To further validate the role of ZmNAC32, CRISPR-Cas9 was used to generate two independent knockout mutants (zmnac32-1 and zmnac32-2) by targeting the first exon (Supplemental Figure 4A). The zmnac32-1 mutant contained a single nucleotide insertion, while zmnac32-2 carried a single nucleotide deletion. Phenotypic observations revealed a modest increase in plant height in both knockout lines compared with the wild type (Supplemental Figure 4B and S4C). Plant height increased by $6 . 2 \%$ in zmnac32-1 and by $6 . 7 \%$ in zmnac32-2. Further investigation showed that this height elevation occurred without changes in internode number and was instead attributable to enhanced internode elongation (Supplemental Figure 4D and S4E). These findings confirm the negative regulatory role of ZmNAC32 in maize internode elongation.

# ZmNAC32 stabilizes ZmD8 by physically shielding its K399 site

Although ZmNAC32 functions as a transcription factor, transcriptomic analysis showed that the transcript abundance of ZmD8 remained unchanged (Figure 5A). Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) and luciferase (LUC) assays were therefore performed to evaluate whether ZmNAC32 directly regulates ZmD8 transcription. The approximately 2k bp promoter region upstream of the ZmD8 transcription start site was subdivided into five fragments. Y1H assays indicated that none of these promoter fragments interacted with ZmNAC32 (Figure S6A), and the luminescence signal generated by co-expression of 62–SKZmNAC32 with the ZmD8 promoter did not differ from that obtained with the 62-SK empty vector control (Figure S6B). To verify whether ZmNAC32 influences ZmD8 protein abundance, we examined ZmD8 protein levels in maize internodes. Internodes from ZmNAC32 overexpression lines exhibited higher ZmD8 protein levels, whereas ZmD8 abundance was substantially reduced in the loss-offunction line (Figure 6A). Conversely, ZmD8 accumulation increased in miR164e-STTM lines but decreased in miR164e overexpression lines (Figure S7), further suggesting that ZmNAC32 might stabilize ZmD8. In cell-free degradation assays, GST–D8 was degraded more slowly in protein extracts derived from ZmNAC32 overexpression lines compared with wild-type extracts (Figure 6B). Taken together, these results confirm that ZmNAC32 enhances ZmD8 protein stability. DELLA proteins constitute a subfamily of plant-specific GRAS transcriptional regulators characterized by a variable N-terminal domain and a conserved C-terminal GRAS domain (Hofmann, 2016). Y2H assays demonstrated that ZmNAC32 interacts specifically with the GRAS domain, but not the DELLA domain, of ZmD8 (Figures 6C and 6D). 

Because poly-ubiquitin-mediated degradation represents a major post-translational regulatory mechanism controlling DELLA proteins, we performed site-directed mutagenesis of two ubiquitination residues (K399 and K463) located within the GRAS domain of ZmD8 (Figure 6C) (Blanco-Tourin˜ a´ n et al., 2020). Structural models of ZmNAC32 and ZmD8 generated by AlphaFold and visualized in PyMOL identified K399, but not K463, as a potential interaction site (Figure 6E). Consistently, LCI assays in N. benthamiana showed that mutation of K399 completely abolished the interaction between ZmNAC32 and ZmD8, whereas mutation of K463 did not affect the interaction (Figure 6F). An in vitro pull-down assay further confirmed that recombinant ZmNAC32–His specifically binds to GST–D8 but fails to interact with GST–D8K399 (Figure 6G). Co-expression of 

FLAG–NAC32 with HA–D8 in protoplasts resulted in a higher accumulation of HA–D8 protein. Notably, HA–D8K399 protein levels were slightly higher than those of HA–D8 even in the absence of NAC32, suggesting that the K399 mutation itself may enhance protein stability (Figure 6H). Overall, these results demonstrate that the miR164e–ZmNAC32 regulatory module governs plant height and internode elongation through the GA signaling pathway in maize. ZmNAC32 directly interacts with ZmD8 and physically shields the critical K399 residue, thereby stabilizing ZmD8 protein and ultimately regulating plant height in maize (Figure 7). 

# DISCUSSION

The miR164 family is highly conserved across plant species. In Arabidopsis, overexpression of miR164a leads to increased plant height (Fang et al., 2014). Additionally, miR164 is involved in the dwarfing process of Populus euphratica under drought and salt stress (Lu et al., 2017). In maize, miR164 shows high expression levels in stems, roots, and grains, and recent studies have demonstrated its involvement in lateral root development and grain expansion (Li et al., 2012; Zheng et al., 2019b). Similarly, overexpression of miR164e in maize in this study also resulted in increased plant height. Comparison of the differences in miRNA expression between fixed and elongating maize internodes revealed that miR164 participates in internode elongation by targeting genes related to cell proliferation and hormone signal transduction (Peng et al., 2019). 

miR164 functions in multiple developmental processes by negatively regulating its target NAC transcription factors. NAC transcription factors play crucial roles in plant cell expansion and developmental regulation. In Arabidopsis, miR164 regulates boundary specification, leaf margin serration, lateral root development, and age-dependent cell death by targeting different NAC genes (Guo et al., 2005; Nikovics et al., 2006; Raman et al., 2008; Kim et al., 2009). In kiwifruit, the interaction between ade-miR164 and AdNAC6/7 regulates fruit ripening (Wang et al., 2020). In strawberry, the miR164–CUC2 module regulates leaf and flower development (Zheng et al., 2019a), whereas in wheat, the miR164 target gene TaNAC21/22 plays a crucial role in resistance to stripe rust (Feng et al., 2014). In rice, osamiR164 targets OsCUC1 and OsCUC3 to regulate meristem and organ boundary specification, and overexpression of osamiR164b results in dwarfism and altered inflorescence architecture (Jiang et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021). In Populus euphratica, peu-miR164 targets PeNAC070, and overexpression of PeNAC070 promotes lateral root development while delaying stem elongation (Lu et al., 2017).

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant material and growth conditions

The miR164 transgenic plants were generated using the maize inbred line 18-599 (red ecological type) as the genetic background and were culti-

vated under natural field conditions in Beijing, China. Transgenic plants overexpressing ZmNAC32 were provided by the Maize Functional Genomics Platform at China Agricultural University, with the maize inbred line ND101 as the genetic background. These ZmNAC32 transgenic plants were grown in a glass greenhouse under controlled conditions of ${ } ^  2 8 ^ { \circ } \subset /$ ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (day/night) with a long-day photoperiod of $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ light and $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ darkness. All growth conditions were maintained to ensure uniform growth and development of the transgenic maize plants. 

# Preparation and observation of internode paraffin sections

The middle region of the eighth internode from ZmNAC32-overexpressing transgenic plants at the tasseling stage was collected. Paraffin sections were prepared following the protocol described by Zhang et al. (2019b). Sections were observed and photographed using an Olympus chargecoupled device (CCD) imaging system (Japan). Cell dimensions were manually measured using ImageJ software (v1.49; NIH, USA). 

# Preparation and CCD observation of internode scanning electron microscopy samples

Fresh samples from the eighth internode were immediately immersed in a fixation solution containing $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde and $5 \%$ glutaraldehyde in $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m o l / L }$ phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) and fixed at room temperature for 1 h. The fixed samples were washed three times for 10 min each with $0 . 1 \mathsf { m o l / L }$ phosphate buffer and then dehydrated through a graded ethanol series $( 3 0 \% - 1 0 0 \% )$ , with 20 min at each concentration. After dehydration, ethanol was replaced with tert-butanol. The substituted samples were placed at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min and subsequently freeze-dried using a freeze dryer. 

The dried samples were mounted on prepared sample holders using conductive adhesive tape, with the observation surface facing upward. The sample holders were placed inside a sputter coater vacuum chamber, which was then sealed, and ion sputter coating was performed. Finally, samples were observed and photographed under a scanning electron microscope (Hitachi S-3400) at an acceleration voltage of $2 0 \mathsf { k V } .$ . Cell dimensions were manually measured using ImageJ software (version 1.49, NIH, USA). 

# Hormone content determination

Fresh samples from the elongation zone were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground into a fine powder, and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Approximately $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of the ground tissue was weighed and mixed with an isotopically labeled internal standard mixture (10 pmol). Hormones were extracted using 1 mL of methanol/water/formic acid (15:4:1, v/v/v). After concentration, the extract was reconstituted in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of $80 \%$ methanol/water and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter for liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (MS) analysis. 

Sample extracts were analyzed using a ultraperformance liquid chromatography-electrospray ionization tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-ESI-MS/MS) system (UPLC, ExionLC AD; MS, Applied Biosystems 6500 Triple Quadrupole, https://sciex.com.cn/). The MWDB (Metware Database) was constructed based on standard compounds for qualitative hormone analysis. Hormone quantification was performed using multiple reaction monitoring mode with a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. Each treatment included three biological replicates.

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The interaction between ZmmiR164e and its target gene was investigated using a dual-luciferase reporter assay. # Yeast one-hybrid

The ZmD8 promoter fragment was cloned into the pAbAi vector to generate a recombinant construct. The plasmid was linearized by digestion with BstBI and transformed into Y1H Gold yeast cells. After growth in liquid SD/− Leu medium for 72 h, the transformants were spotted onto SD/− Leu plates containing the critical AbA concentration for protein–DNA interaction analysis. 

# Subcellular localization observation

Signals were detected using chemiluminescence, and immunoblots were analyzed in grayscale using ImageJ software. The anti-$\beta$ -actin antibody was obtained from Sigma (Sigma-Aldrich, USA). A ZmD8-specific polypeptide (C-GAERTERHETLGQWR-3’) was conjugated to KLH using the Imject Maleimide Activated mcKLH Kit and injected into rabbits as previously described (Suga et al., 2001). # Protein 3D modeling prediction and domain analysis

The 3D structural models of ZmNAC32 and ZmD8 were generated using AlphaFold software. The model with the best Predictive Template Modeling score was selected from the ranked outputs. Structural representations and amino acid substitution sites were visualized using PyMOL (v2.6.0a0) software. 

# Transcriptome resequencing

At the 8-leaf stage, elongation zones of the eighth internode were collected for RNA-seq analysis. Three biological replicates were included for each treatment. Total RNA was extracted using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen), and sequencing libraries were constructed following the Illumina standard protocol (TruSeq Stranded RNA LT Guide). The constructed libraries were quality-checked using an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer and an ABI StepOnePlus Real-Time PCR System. The cDNA libraries were sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 2500 high-throughput sequencing platform to generate raw reads. Low-quality reads, adapter-contaminated reads, and reads with high levels of unknown bases (N) were removed to obtain clean reads. Subsequently, HISAT2 was employed to align the clean reads to a reference genome to predict novel transcripts (Kim et al., 2015). Predicted protein-coding novel transcripts were integrated into the reference gene set to generate a comprehensive reference sequence. Clean 

reads were then aligned to the reference sequence using Bowtie2 to calculate alignment rates (Langmead and Salzberg, 2012). Gene expression levels were quantified using RNA-Seq by expectation-maximization (RSEM), and gene expression level estimation (FPKM) values were generated (Li and Dewey, 2011). Differentially expressed genes were identified using the DEGseq algorithm with screening criteria of |fold change $\geq 2$ and adjusted $p$ value $\leq 0 . 0 0 1$ (Wang et al., 2010). 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted from elongating internode samples using TRIzol reagent. mRNA was reverse transcribed using the TRUEscript First-Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Aialab, Beijing, China). miRNA reverse transcription was performed using stem-loop RT primers and the corresponding kit (Aialab, Beijing, China). Reverse transcription and quantitative analysis were conducted according to previously published protocols (Peng et al., 2024). Primer sequences used for quantitative reversetranscription PCR (RT-qPCR) are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 (GraphPad, San Diego, CA, USA). Differences among multiple groups were evaluated by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison test. $p$ values $< 0 . 0 5$ were considered statistically significant. Data are presented as means $\pm$ standard deviation (SD). Additionally, Pearson correlation coefficient analysis was performed to determine reproducibility among biological replicates.
