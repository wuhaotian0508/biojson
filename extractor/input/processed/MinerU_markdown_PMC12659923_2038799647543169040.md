# INTRODUCTION

Sulfonylurea-based herbicides inhibit acetohydroxyacid synthase (AHAS), also known as acetolactate synthase, and have been in use since 1983 (Ohta & Sada, 2024). AHAS is an enzyme involved in the first step of the biosynthesis of branched-chain amino acids, including leucine, isoleucine, and valine. Some AHAS-targeting herbicides inhibit substrate (pyruvate) binding to the active site through allosteric inhibition of the AHAS protein. In addition, they can induce accumulative inhibition by oxidizing the AHAS cofactor flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) or modifying thiamine diphosphate (ThDP), both of which are essential for enzyme activity (Lonhienne et al., 2016; Zhao et al., 2020). AHAS-targeting herbicides include sulfonylureas, imidazolinones, triazolopyrimidines, pyrimidinyl benzoates, and sulfonylaminocarbonyltriazolinones. 

Extensive research has been conducted on resistance mechanisms to AHAS-targeting herbicides. In the weed 

species Schoenoplectiella juncoides, an increase in the number of mutated AHAS alleles correlates with higher resistance to the sulfonylurea herbicide metsulfuronmethyl (Garcia et al., 2017). Similarly, in Glycine max, individuals with mutations in both GmAHAS1 and GmAHAS4 exhibit greater resistance to halosulfuron-methyl, which belongs to the sulfonylurea family, than those with mutations in only one of these genes (Lawn et al., 2023). Moreover, different mutation patterns also influence the resistance levels of plants to various herbicides. For example, in Arabidopsis thaliana, the P197S substitution in AtA-HAS confers resistance to a sulfonylurea family herbicide, while the W574L substitution provides stronger resistance to imazaquin, and the P197T/L substitution confers resistance to chlorimuron-ethyl (Lonhienne et al., 2022; Walter et al., 2014). Mutations at proline-197 and tryptophan-574 in AHAS are frequently found in weeds resistant to AHAS-targeting herbicides (Li et al., 2019; Lonhienne et al., 2022; Walter et al., 2014). 

In G. max, there are four AHAS genes: GmAHAS1 (GLYMA_04G196100v4), GmAHAS2 (GLYMA_06G169 700v4), GmAHAS3 (GLYMA_13G241000v4), and GmAHAS4 (GLYMA_15G072500v4). CRISPR/Cas9-mediated base editing was used to generate the GmAHAS4 P180S mutant in soybean, conferring resistance to AHAS-targeting herbicides (Wei et al., 2023). Given that herbicide resistance can vary depending on both the chemical family within the same mode of action and the specific mutation pattern, testing a broader range of mutation patterns is necessary (Lonhienne et al., 2022). However, no studies have examined whether mutants with different patterns in multiple genes in soybean exhibit greater resistance to herbicides. 

In this study, we aimed to introduce mutations commonly found in herbicide-resistant weeds into G. max AHAS genes using CRISPR genome editing technology. We employed the nCas9-Petromyzon marinus cytidine deaminase 1 (PmCDA1) system (Nishida et al., 2016) for base editing and successfully obtained soybean lines harboring up to three edited GmAHAS genes, including GmAHAS2, GmAHAS3, and GmAHAS4. In total, six mutant lines with distinct mutation patterns were generated. Each line was evaluated for herbicide resistance using three different herbicide compounds belonging to the sulfonylurea, imidazolinone and pyrimidinyl benzoate families. Our results demonstrated that resistance levels varied depending on both the mutation pattern and the herbicide applied.

# Triple-stacked mutant exhibits superior resistance to pyrazosulfuron-ethyl compared with single and double mutants

The mutant lines containing the proline-to-serine mutation (2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F) were treated with pyrazosulfuron-ethyl (PE) to assess their resistance to this sulfonylurea family herbicide (Figure 2). The herbicide resistance experiment was conducted in a greenhouse maintained at $\mathsf { 2 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ . Nineteen days after seeding, when true leaves were fully developed, PE was treated four times successively, once per week, at a concentration equivalent to $1 0 0 \ g \ h \mathsf { a } ^ { - 1 }$ . The height was measured every week, and dry weight and leaf area were measured 49 days after seeding. Without herbicide treatment, the dry weight and leaf area of the mutant lines and WT were not significantly different (Figure 2D,F). In terms of height, 2S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F lines were slightly taller than WT and the 3S line (Figure 2B). However, when treated with PE, WT plants did not survive (Figure 2A), whereas all mutant lines carrying the proline-to-serine mutation (2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F) exhibited strong resistance, as indicated by their greater height. WT had an average height of 9.13 cm, whereas the mutant lines 2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F exhibited average heights of 27.75, 23.08, 25.62, and 27.99 cm, respectively (Figure 2C). The average dry weight of the mutant lines 2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F was also significantly greater, measuring 1.69, 1.78, 1.91, and $2 . 2 4 \ 9 ,$ , respectively, compared with the average dry weight of WT at $0 . 1 7 ~ 9$ (Figure 2E). Moreover, the average leaf area of the mutant lines: $1 3 0 . 4 9 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ for 2S, $1 2 3 . 2 2 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ for 3S, and $1 2 8 . 9 2 ~ { \mathsf { c m } } ^ { 2 }$ for 2S3S was also larger than that of WT, which measured $1 6 . 7 8 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ (Figure 2G). The average leaf area of the mutant line 2S3S4F was $1 7 3 . 3 9 \ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ , significantly larger than the other mutant lines (Figure 2G), suggesting that the three mutations in GmAHAS2, GmAHAS3, and GmAHAS4 of mutant line 2S3S4F had an additive effect in conferring resistance to the herbicide PE.

# Having both proline-to-serine and proline-to-tyrosine mutation patterns confers resistance to both PE and BS herbicides

We observed that two different mutation patterns conferred varying resistance to two different herbicides. Therefore, we aimed to investigate whether a plant carrying both mutation patterns, each associated with resistance to a different herbicide, could exhibit resistance to both herbicides. The mutant line 2Y3SC has a P182Y mutation in GmAHAS2 and P172S and R174C mutations in GmAHAS3 (Figure 1C). Without herbicide treatment, the height and dry weight of the 2Y and 2Y3SC mutant lines were slightly greater than those of WT (Figure 4B,E), but the leaf area of each line was not significantly different (Figure 4H). Two different herbicides, PE and BS were treated on the mutant line 2Y3SC and 2Y. Intriguingly, 2Y3SC showed significantly higher resistance to both PE and BS than the mutant line 2Y and WT in height (Figure 4A). In PE treatment, the average height of 2Y3SC was 28.67 cm, whereas 2Y and WT measured only 15.57 and 9.13 cm, respectively (Figure 4C). Similarly, the average dry weight of 2Y3SC $( 2 . 0 4 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } )$ was significantly higher than that of WT (0.17 g) and 2Y (0.52 g) (Figure 4F), which shows that 2Y3SC has stronger resistance to PE than 2Y. This trend was also observed in leaf area, with 2Y3SC measuring $1 7 4 . 3 2 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ , compared with $4 2 . 2 1 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ for 2Y and $1 6 . 7 8 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ for WT (Figure 4I). The increased resistance to PE observed in 2Y3SC may result from the P172S mutation, the R174C mutation, or their combined effect. 

Similar to the PE treatment, when treated with BS, the mutant line 2Y3SC exhibited the greatest resistance (Figure 4A). In terms of average height, 2Y3SC measured $4 0 . 5 8 ~ \mathsf { c m }$ , while WT and 2Y measured 21.75 and 30.98 cm, 

respectively (Figure 4D). Similarly, the average dry weight of 2Y3SC was the highest at $2 . 3 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } ,$ , followed by 2Y at $\boldsymbol { 1 . 7 9 \ 9 }$ and WT at $0 . 8 9 5 ~ 9$ (Figure 4G). Finally, the leaf area of 2Y3SC $( 3 3 7 . 1 3 \ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ was ${ \sim } 2 . 9$ times larger than that of WT $. 1 1 6 . 4 5 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ (Figure 4J). These results suggest that the presence of both mutation patterns – proline-to-serine and proline-to-tyrosine – conferring resistance to two different herbicides can lead to the development of a mutant line resistant to both herbicides. 

# mutants retain resistance to imidazolinone G. maxherbicides

By selective breeding, WT G. max Korean cultivar ‘Kwangankong’ has resistance to herbicides in the imidazolinone family which also target the AHAS enzyme. To examine whether mutations at the target site in the mutant lines could lead to the loss of this resistance, we treated them with imazethapyr (IM), an herbicide from the imidazolinone family. IM was treated three times, once a week at a concentration of $7 0 \ g \ h \mathsf { a } ^ { - 1 }$ and after 49 days, the height, dry weight, and leaf area of plants were measured. There was no difference in height among the mutant lines, but WT plants were significantly smaller than mutant lines (Figure 5A). The dry weight and leaf area of WT, 2Y, and 2Y3SC showed no significant differences (Figure 5B,C). However, the average dry weights and leaf areas of 2S, 3S, and 2S3S4F were significantly larger than those of WT (Figure 5B,C). The average leaf areas of 2S, 3S and 2S3S4F were 432.41, 370.37 and $4 0 1 . 3 8 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ , respectively, whereas that of WT was $2 6 3 . 3 3 ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ . These results suggest that the mutations introduced in this study maintained resistance to IM. 

# enzyme assay of AHAS2 when PE was applied

We conducted an enzyme assay to measure the substrate consumption rate of GmAHAS2 from WT, 2Y (P182Y), and 2S (P182S) lines. In the absence of PE, the pyruvate consumption rate was highest in WT, followed by 2S and 2Y (Figure 6A). However, when $1 0 ~ \mu \mu$ of PE was applied, the consumption rate of the GmAHAS2 enzyme in WT was slower than that in the 2S and 2Y lines (Figure 6B). Treatment with $3 0 ~ \mu \mu$ PE further amplified this effect, widening the gap between GmAHAS2 from 2S and 2Y, making the difference statistically significant (Figure 6C). Taken together, GmAHAS2 with the P182S mutation consumed pyruvate more effectively than both the 2Y mutant line and WT when PE was added.

# DISCUSSION

We generated G. max mutants using the CRISPR/Cas9 base editing system with a guide RNA targeting proline-182 of GmAHAS2 (Figure 1). In addition to the mutations at proline-182 in GmAHAS2, we also introduced mutations at the paralogous sites in GmAHAS3 and GmAHAS4 (Figure 1C; Figure S2). Consistent with mismatch-position effects on Cas activity, the absence of edits in GmAHAS1 may result from one of its two mismatches being located proximal to the PAM site, which likely reduced editing efficiency relative to GmAHAS3 and GmAHAS4, each carrying only a single distal mismatch (Figure 1A). We also detected minor, unintended substitutions from C-to-G and C-to-A (Figure S1), which are characteristic byproducts of cytosine base editors. Although C-to-T conversions represent the predominant editing outcome, these minor substitutions can also lead to amino acid replacements with potential functional consequences. As reported by Kang et al. (2019), such noncanonical edits can arise in cellular systems, and our data extend these observations by demonstrating that these rare substitutions can be fixed and transmitted in plants, thereby providing additional routes for generating amino acid-altering mutations beyond canonical C-to-T base editing. 

In our PE herbicide treatments, all four mutant lines – 2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F – exhibited significant resistance to PE. While the 2S3S line showed a similar level of resistance to PE as the 2S and 3S lines, the 2S3S4F line demonstrated stronger resistance than the 2S3S line (Figures 1 and 2). Because the transcript levels of each GmAHAS gene in the mutant lines and WT were comparable (Figure S4), the enhanced herbicide resistance of the 2S3S4F line against PE is unlikely to result from regulatory feedback loops or other forms of transcriptional cross-talk among the gene family members. Instead, this increased resistance may arise from the stacking effect of mutated GmAHAS genes or, alternatively, the stronger contribution of the P180F mutation relative to the proline-to-serine substitutions. Our in silico docking analysis showed that the predicted binding energy of PE to the P180F mutant of GmAHAS4 was higher than that of the WT (Table S2). The docking model also revealed a displacement of the PE binding position, likely caused by the larger phenylalanine residue replacing proline (Figure S6). 

When comparing the resistance of the 2Y and 2S lines to BS herbicides, the 2Y line showed greater resistance to BS than the 2S and WT lines (Figure 3D,G,J). This differential resistance may also be attributed to structural changes affecting herbicide binding; the substitution of proline with a bulkier tyrosine residue (P182Y) could sterically hinder BS from binding to GmAHAS2 (Figure S6), which is reflected in a calculated increase in binding energy from 7.734 to 6.766 kcal/mol (Table S2). This observation is 

consistent with a previous study in rice, which also found that a proline-to-tyrosine mutation conferred stronger resistance to BS than a proline-to-serine mutation (Zhang et al., 2021). To further elucidate the precise mechanism by which the P182Y mutation enhances BS resistance, direct comparative enzyme assays of the GmAHAS2 variants from 2Y and 2S lines in the presence of BS would be highly informative. 

Interestingly, while the 2S, 3S and 2S3S lines demonstrated increased resistance to PE, our static docking simulations did not predict a correspondingly significant change in binding affinity between PE and the mutated GmAHAS enzymes (Table S2). We hypothesize that the P182S mutation may confer resistance through an alternative biochemical mechanism not captured by the simulation, rather than by directly altering binding energy. This hypothesis is supported by in vitro enzyme assays, which showed that the 2S mutant enzyme maintained significantly higher activity in the presence of PE compared with the WT enzyme (Figure 6). There are strong precedents for such complex mechanisms that can explain this inconsistency between in silico binding affinity and in vivo efficacy. For instance, imidazolinone herbicides are known to be highly effective due to strong accumulative inhibition, even with relatively low binding affinities (Garcia et al., 2017). Similarly, Lonhienne et al. (2022) demonstrated that mutations at the P197 residue in AtAHAS abolished accumulative inhibition by the sulfonylurea herbicide chlorimuron-ethyl (CE), but not by bispyribac-sodium (BS). These examples, where static docking is insufficient to predict the final inhibitory outcome, support our hypothesis that a similar alternative biochemical process may be responsible for the herbicide resistance conferred by the proline-to-serine mutations in GmAHAS. 

Several studies have reported the emergence of herbicide-resistant weeds, such as Sinapis alba, Monochoria korsakowii, and Alopecurus aequalis, in natural conditions (Chtourou et al., 2024; Walter et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2024). In addition to weeds, herbicide resistance has also been observed in crops such as rice (Chen et al., 2021), wheat (Zhang et al., 2019), and soybeans (Lawn et al., 2023), most of which have been developed through traditional breeding methods. In this study, we generated six different mutant lines with distinct mutation patterns and varying numbers of mutated GmAHAS genes using CRISPR/Cas-based base editing. Furthermore, we demonstrated that a plant carrying two different mutation patterns can exhibit dual herbicide resistance; the 2Y3SC line displayed stronger resistance to both PE and BS than the 2Y line (Figure 4), while the P182Y and P182S conferred strong resistance to BS and PE, respectively. Our findings suggest a new approach for generating crop varieties resistant to multiple herbicides with different modes of binding using CRISPR genome editing technology. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plasmid construction

Vector construct nCas9-PmCDA1 was used for transformation. AarI restriction enzyme digested the nCas9-PmCDA1 vector and both annealed products of the two oligonucleotides with T4 DNA ligase (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) were used to clone a guide RNA. Then the ligation product was transferred to E. coli DH5a to select the proper construct-containing cells. Guide RNA targeting Pro-182 in G. max in Glyma.06G169700.1 was designed to introduce the SNP found in herbicide-resistant weeds. 

# transformation

Mature soybean seeds of the Korean cultivar ‘Kwangankong’ were used for transformation by following the method described by Kim et al. (2016). 

# Analysis of mutation frequency and patterns

The amplified on-target site from genomic DNA with the targetspecific primers was used for targeted deep sequencing and it was conducted with the Illumina MiniSeq system. With additional PCR, we added read indices for multiplexing of samples and Casanalyzer (www.rgenome.net) was used to calculate substitution and indel frequency. Targeted deep sequencing was done in GmAHAS2 in ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ generation, ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ generation and $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ generation. Sanger sequencing targeting the guide RNA site in GmAHAS2 and potential off-target sites in GmAHAS3, and GmAHAS4 was done in $\mathsf { T } _ { 3 }$ generation (Figure S3). From the $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ generation of line #5-3, we found line #5-3-8, which has three heterozygous mutations in GmAHAS2, GmAHAS3, and GmAHAS4. We harvested seeds and sowed 96 seeds to get homozygous mutants. After genotyping $\mathsf { T } _ { 3 }$ generation of line #5-3-8, we got four lines (2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F) with different homozygous mutation patterns. We obtained the 2Y line from $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ #3-6-26 and the 2Y3SC line from $\intercal _ { 2 }$ #3-6-45 (Figure S2). The mutant lines 2S, 3S, 2S3S, and 2S3S4F were derived from $\intercal _ { 2 }$ #5–3-8 (Figure S2). 

# Plant materials

The soybean Korean cultivar ‘Kwangankong’ was used in this study. The experiment was conducted at Dong-A University from November 21, 2024 to January 9, 2025. Twelve seeds from each soybean line were allocated to the treatment and control group. The seeds were soaked in moistened cotton overnight at room temperature. On the following day, the seeds were sown in soil at a depth of approximately 2 cm in pots (8 cm in height and 9 cm in diameter). Plants were grown in the greenhouse of Dong-A University maintained at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ (16 h:8 h, light: dark photoperiod). 

# Whole-plant resistance experiments

Nineteen days after germination of soybean Korean cultivar ‘Kwangankong’ seeds, the plants in the treatment group were sprayed with pyrazosulfuron-ethyl at a concentration equivalent to $1 0 0 \ g \ h \mathsf { a } ^ { - 1 }$ . Bispyribac-sodium and imazethapyr were treated after 28 days after germination at a concentration equivalent to 36 and $7 0 \ g \ h \mathsf { a } ^ { - 1 }$ respectively. The herbicide solution was prepared by dissolving $3 0 ~ \mathsf { m } \mathsf { g }$ of pyrazosulfuron-ethyl, 2 mg of bispyribacsodium, or 2 mg of imazethapyr in 1 ml of acetone. Three herbicide solutions were added into $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of water at 215.6, 349.3, and $6 7 9 . 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ each. This mixture was applied uniformly across 18 pots. Herbicide applications were conducted once per week for four consecutive weeks for pyrazosulfuron-ethyl and three consecutive 

weeks for bispyribac-sodium and imazethapyr. Control plants were treated with the same volume of water without herbicides. The height of the plants was measured every week. After 49 days of growth, leaf area, and dry weight were measured to assess the effects of herbicide treatment. 

# RNA-extraction and reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR)

At the V1 stage, $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of G. max leaves were collected to extract total RNA using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Subsequently, $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of RNA was used to synthesize cDNA with the RNA to cDNA EcoDry Premix (Takara, Tokyo, Japan). To assess the transcript levels of GmAHAS in mutant and WT plants, the relative transcript abundance of transiently expressed GmAHAS1, GmA-HAS2, GmAHAS3, and GmAHAS4 was quantified using quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) based on the $2 ^ { \Delta \Delta \mathrm { { \dot { c } t } } }$ method, with GmEF1a serving as the reference gene. 

# Enzyme assay

The native coding sequences (CDSs) were inserted into the pET50 vector and subsequently used to transform Escherichia coli Rosetta (DE3) cells. The transformed cells were cultured in LB broth supplemented with $5 0 ~ { \mu \ g } ~ { \mathsf { m l } } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin and $3 0 ~ \mu \ g ~ \mathsf { m l } ^ { - 1 }$ chloramphenicol at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ . Once the optical density at 600 nm (OD600) reached 2.0, protein expression was induced by adding $0 . 5 0 \mathrm { \ m m }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). The cultures were then incubated at $\textstyle 1 8 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for an additional 18–20 h before harvesting the cells. The cell paste was thawed and resuspended in $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris, pH 7.9, $1 0 \mathrm { \ m u \ M g C l _ { 2 } } ,$ 1 mM ThDP, $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ FAD, and $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ imidazole (binding buffer). For lysis, 10 mg lysozyme per gram of cell, $5 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ DNase $( 5 0 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } ~ \mu | ^ { - 1 } )$ , and a capsule of protease inhibitor mixture (Complete, EDTA-free from Roche) were added. The cell suspension was incubated on ice for 15 min and then sonicated in a Branson Sonifier 250 for $1 2 \times 1 0$ sec at constant duty cycle, with rest intervals of 10 sec. To purify the recombinant proteins, the cell debris was removed by centrifugation at 39 100 for 60 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and subsegquently applied to Ni-NTA agarose resin (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The enzyme was then eluted with 50 mM Tris, pH 7.9, 10 mM ${ \sf M g C l } _ { 2 } ,$ 1 mM ThDP, $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ FAD, and $3 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ imidazole (elution buffer). The flow-through was collected, and the fractions containing the protein were pooled and $1 \mathsf { m m }$ DTT was subsequently added to the samples. Dialysis was performed against $5 0 \mathrm { \ m m } \mathsf { K P O } _ { 4 }$ buffer, pH 7.2, 10 mM MgCl2, 1 mM ThDP, $1 0 ~ \mu \mu$ FAD, and 1 mM DTT. The size and purity of the eluted proteins were verified via Coomassie staining. To remove excess imidazole, the proteins were dialyzed against their final storage buffers before being stored at $\mathtt { - 8 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ . For AHAS enzyme assay, the standard buffer contained $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ potassium phosphate, pH 7.2, 1 mM ThDP, $1 0 \mathrm { \ m u \ M g C l _ { 2 } } ,$ and $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ FAD. The concentration of pyruvate was in the range 100–160 mM. The pyruvate content was measured at 333 nm with the microplate spectrophotometer (Infinite 200 PRO; Tecan, Mannedorf, Switzerland) with serial€ detection minute by minute, for a total of 120 min. 

# Protein modeling and molecular docking simulation

To explore the structural basis of altered substrate specificity, the three-dimensional (3D) structures of the wild-type (WT) and mutant GmAHAS proteins were modeled as homodimers using AlphaFold2 (Cramer, 2021), reflecting the location of the substrate binding pocket at the monomer–monomer interface. To prepare the receptor for docking, the predicted GmAHAS homodimer 

structure was aligned to an AtAHAS structure (PDB ID 5WJ1) containing bound cofactors. The coordinates for the cofactors $\mathsf { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ , FAD, and ThDP were then transferred from the template (as HETATM records) into the GmAHAS model to create the final holo-receptor structure. For the docking analysis, ligand PDB files for the herbicides bispyribac-sodium and pyrazosulfuron-ethyl were generated from their respective SMILES strings, obtained from PubChem, using Open Babel (O’Boyle et al., 2011). Both the holo-receptor (GmAHAS + cofactors) and the ligands were then prepared and converted to the PDBQT file format using AutoDock-Tools. Molecular docking simulations to investigate the structural basis of herbicide resistance were subsequently performed using AutoDock Vina (version 1.2.7) (Eberhardt et al., 2021). Docking was performed using AutoDock Vina (version 1.2.7). For each receptor (WT and mutants), a $2 0 \times 2 0 \times 2 0 \mathring { \mathsf { A } }$ grid box was defined, centered on the active site. The specific coordinates used for each docking simulation are detailed in Table S1. The resulting binding affinities are detailed in Table S2.
