ABSTRACT: The firmness of grape berries is a critical factor influencing their commercial feasibility and is highly valued by both breeders and consumers. However, grape berry firmness is a complex quantitative trait governed by multiple genes, and our understanding of its genetic regulatory network remains incomplete. To elucidate the genetic mechanisms underlying grape berry firmness, this study employed genome-wide association studies (GWAS) to identify potential candidate genes associated with fruit firmness and cellulose content and to explore the gene regulatory network that controls their variation. The comprehensive GWAS results identified CesA as a candidate gene potentially regulating fruit firmness through its involvement in cellulose biosynthesis. To validate these findings, whole-genome gene family identification analysis was conducted. Furthermore, the key gene VvCslD5 was selected for functional validation, which included overexpression studies and subcellular localization. This study provides valuable insights into the regulation of biosynthesis and transcriptional signaling pathways that govern the structure of grape cell walls as well as the mechanisms underlying variations in grape firmness. These findings establish a solid foundation for future functional analyses of grape traits and will enhance breeding practices aimed at improving grape quality. 

KEYWORDS: grapes, peel firmness, flesh firmness, GWAS 

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Grapes (Vitis vinifera L.) have been cultivated globally for centuries and hold significant economic importance due to their fresh, crisp berries and nutritional richness.1−3 However, their susceptibility to mechanical damage and pathogen invasion postripening, attributed to low firmness, presents substantial challenges, accelerating fruit decay and reducing economic value.4,5 As a result, enhancing fruit firmness has become a key breeding objective for grape producers.6 

Fruit firmness is a complex quantitative trait influenced by multiple genes and a variety of internal and external factors.7 The composition and structure of the cell wall play a critical role in determining firmness.8 12 During ripening, dynamic changes occur in cell wall components, including cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin, and structural proteins, which collectively impact fruit firmness.13 Among these components, pectin and hemicellulose have been extensively studied in relation to fruit softening. Pectin, a major polysaccharide, influences fruit firmness by degrading through pectinase during ripening. This degradation leads to cell wall loosening and reduced firmness, as observed in grapes, peaches, and plums.14−16 Similarly, hemicellulose, the second most abundant structural polysaccharide in plant cell walls, contributes to the cell wall strength and toughness by forming cross-linked structures with cellulose and pectin. Its degradation is also closely associated with fruit ripening and softening.17−19 

In contrast, the role of cellulose in fruit firmness has received less attention.20,21 Early studies in tomatoes demonstrated that cellulose directly affects fruit firmness, while in grapes, cellulose content is linked to the structural stability of cell walls.22 The 

degradation and reorganization of cellulose during ripening further influence fruit firmness, highlighting its potential as a target for improving fruit quality and extending the shelf life. Future research could explore the interactions between cellulose and other cell wall components to better understand the mechanisms underlying fruit firmness. Additionally, while most studies focus on overall fruit firmness, the distinct anatomical and metabolic differences between grape peel and flesh suggest that these tissues should be examined separately.23,24 The flesh primarily determines firmness and brittleness, whereas the peel contributes to toughness, elasticity, and flavor.25 

Traditional approaches to identifying genes associated with grapefruit firmness, such as quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping, are time-consuming and often yield limited candidate genes.26 However, recent advances in genome-wide association studies (GWAS), coupled with high-throughput sequencing technologies, have significantly improved the accuracy and efficiency of identifying genetic markers, such as singlenucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), associated with fruit firmness.27 By integrating SNP markers from reference genomes, researchers can pinpoint candidate genes and chromosomal regions linked to target traits, facilitating

# 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1. Materials. This experiment utilized leaf samples from 153 grape genotypes (Supporting Table S1) collected from a grape germplasm resource nursery under unified management in Ningbo, China. On the day of collection, the samples were transported to the laboratory. Young leaves free from pests, diseases, and mechanical damage were used for DNA extraction and subsequent whole-genome resequencing. 

From June 2022 to September 2023, a subset of 120 fruit-bearing varieties selected from the 153 grape genotypes was used to evaluate the firmness of the fruit flesh and peel. Fruits free from pests, diseases, and mechanical damage were chosen for the analysis. Grapes of uniform size from the same genotype were selected to ensure consistency in subsequent experiments. 

2.2. Measurement of Fruit Firmness and Peel Firmness. The firmness of the fruit was measured using a GY-4 firmness tester (Lynd, China) with a $2 \mathrm { m m }$ diameter needle probe (P/2) at a testing speed of $1 \ \mathrm { m m } / \mathrm s$ . To assess grape peel firmness, the probe was vertically pressed at a constant speed and the grape peel puncture value was recorded. For flesh firmness, a 1 cm section of the peel was removed, and the fruit was vertically pressed in the direction of grape stem growth at a constant speed. The average peak value from at least three measurements was recorded in Newtons (N) to represent the flesh firmness. For each measurement, 12 grapes of similar size (approximately $2 0 { - } 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ in diameter) were randomly selected for each genotype. 

2.3. Cellulose Content Determination. The cellulose content was determined by the anthrone sulfate colorimetric method.34 Briefly, $_ { 0 . 2 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of dried grape powder was weighed and placed in a beaker, which was then immersed in a cold water bath. Next, $6 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $6 0 \%$ $\% \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ was added to the beaker, and the mixture was digested for 30 min. After digestion, the cellulose solution was transferred to a 100 mL volumetric flask and diluted to the mark with $6 0 \% \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ $6 0 \%$ . The solution was thoroughly mixed and filtered through a Buchner funnel into a separate beaker. A 5 mL aliquot of the filtrate was pipetted into a second $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ volumetric flask, diluted to the mark with distilled water in a cold water bath, and mixed well for further use. 

For cellulose quantification, $2 \ \mathrm { \ m L }$ of the diluted solution was transferred to a stoppered test tube, followed by the addition of 0.5 mL of the $2 \%$ anthrone reagent. Then, $\downarrow \mathrm { m L }$ of concentrated ${ \mathrm { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { S O } } _ { 4 }$ was carefully added along the wall of the test tube. The tube was stoppered, shaken vigorously, and allowed to stand for 12 min. The absorbance of the resulting solution was measured at $6 2 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ by using a Multiskan SkyHigh full-wavelength ELISA reader. Cellulose content was calculated based on the absorbance. 

2.4. DNA Extraction and Sequencing, SNP Calling, and Population Structure. The samples for this study were leaf samples from grape populations provided by BGI Genomics (Shenzhen, China), with three biological replicates for each variety. All DNA samples were extracted from fresh tender leaves using the CTAB 

method (Aidlab Biotechnology, China), and a library was constructed according to standard procedures. The sample DNA library was subjected to genome resequencing by using the Illumina NovaSeq platform. To ensure data quality, the raw sequencing data (raw readings) are filtered and quality-controlled using SOAPNuke (version 2.2.1). The filtering steps include: delete reads containing adapter sequences; filter low-quality sequences and remove sequences with quality values below $1 0 \%$ ; delete reads with quality values below 10 and accounting for over $5 0 \%$ of the total reads. The filtered clean reads are used for genomic information V. vinifera.12X.dna.toplevel-(http://ftp.ensemblgenomes.org/pub/plants/release-54/fasta/vitis_ vinifera/). Compare, generate SAM files, and use SAMtools for sorting processing; Use Qulimap2 (v.2.2.2) to perform quality control on BAM files, estimating features such as coverage, sequencing depth, GC distribution, and insertion fragment length. The genotype data was generated into GVCF files using the HaplotypeCaller tool in GATK (version 4.2.6.1), and the variant data from different samples were merged using the Combine GVCFs tool. Use the following criteria to perform quality filtering on the points: remove the marked “filter” points; Delete sites with a deletion rate greater than 0.05; further filter out sites that meet at least one of the following conditions: $\mathrm { Q D } < 2 . 0$ : Evaluate the ratio of mutation quality to depth. $\mathrm { F S } \ > \ 6 0 . 0$ : Detect false positives due to noise introduced by sequencing processes. $\mathrm { M Q } < 4 0 . 0$ : Filter criteria for mapping quality. MQRankSum $< - 1 2 . 5$ ; Evaluate the difference in mapping quality between variations and references. ReadPosRankSum $< \ - 8 . 0$ : Evaluate the distribution of variant sites in the reading process; Sites with a deletion rate greater than 0.05 are filtered out. Based on the filtered SNP data, genetic structure analysis was performed using STRUCTURE (version 1.3.0) software. By selecting different K values, comparing cross-validation errors, and determining the optimal K value (the K value with the lowest error rate), the proportion of ancestral components in each sample is inferred, and the genetic structure of the sample is analyzed. In addition, we used PopLD Decay (version 3.41) software to identify linkage disequilibrium (LD) in different subgroups. LD is represented by the D value and $r ^ { 2 }$ value, where $r ^ { 2 } = 1$ indicates complete linkage disequilibrium without recombination; If $r ^ { 2 } = 0$ , it means complete balance and random combination. Perform principal component analysis and Kinship analysis on all samples using R package GAPIT3114 (Version 3.2). For $\mathrm { P C A } ,$ we present the clustering results of all samples between the first three levels (PC1, PC2, and PC3); For Kinship analysis, we used the VanRaden algorithm to calculate the kinship matrix between all samples. 

2.5. GWAS. The GAPIT package in R software is used for GWAS, which contains high-quality SNP markers and phenotype data for 120 grape genotypes. Mixed Linear Model (MLM), Generalized Linear Model (GLM), Bayesian Information and Linkage Disequilibrium Iteratively Nested Keyway (BLINK), Compressed Mixed Linear Model (CMLM), and Fixed and Random Model Circulating Probability Unification (FarmCPU) models were used to evaluate the correlation between SNPs and the fruit flesh and peel firmness. GLM is the fundamental model in GWAS, where SNP markers are independent variables and phenotype is the dependent variable in linear regression. The GLM model is simple and computationally efficient but may be affected by population structure and kinship relationships. Principal component analysis (PCA) is used as a covariate for correction, while the MLM model considers fixed and random effects in GWAS analysis and uses kinship matrices for correction. It is mainly used to eliminate false positives caused by samples with close kinship relationships. The Bayesian linear mixture model may combine the characteristics of Bayesian inference and linear mixture models to evaluate the correlation between the SNPs and phenotypes. CMLM is an improvement over MLM aimed at addressing the issue of MLM being too strict during the correction process. FarmCPU is an efficient GWAS analysis method that combines fixed effects with random effects. It adopts a stepwise regression strategy, iteratively selecting the most significant SNP as the fixed effect and considering other SNPs as random effects, thereby improving the accuracy and efficiency of the analysis. 2.6. Gene Annotation. Based on the significant correlated SNPs for each trait across different chromosomes, we extracted the SNP positions of the most significant loci on each chromosome and expanded the genome by a $1 5 ~ \mathrm { k b }$ window from these positions. We then studied the up- and downstream regions of these important SNPs to identify annotated genes. Subsequently, we selected genes within these regions for functional enrichment analysis to identify genes that are potentially associated with the traits. Functional annotation was performed using the UniProt databases (https:// www.uniprot.org/id-mapping) or by downloading existing annotation files to enrich the gene functions in the significant regions. 

2.7. RNA Extraction, Reverse Transcription. Total RNA of grapefruit was extracted using RNAprep pure polysaccharide polyphenol plant total RNA extraction kit (HuiLing, China), and cDNA was synthesized using NovoScript Plus All in One first Strand cDNA synthesis super mixed (gDNA Purge) reverse transcription kit (Novoprotein, China). 

2.8. Identification of Gene Family Members. To identify members of the CesA/Csl gene family in grapes, CesA/Csl protein sequences from Arabidopsis thaliana were collected as query sequences for BLAST analysis and subjected to a BLAST search in the grape database (A. thaliana IDs and proteins are provided in the supplementary table). The blast screening criteria threshold is that the e-value is less than 1e-10, and sequences with amino acid or nucleotide similarity with an alignment rate of less than $3 0 \%$ are removed as preliminary screening results (see Supporting Table S2 for details). The seed file PF03552, which contains CesA/Csl domains, was downloaded from the Pfam database. HMMER3.1 software was 

used to search for CesA/Csl gene family members in grapes with a cutoff value (e-value) of less than $1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ to ensure its reliability. The obtained sequences were merged and redundant sequences were eliminated. NCBI CDD was used to identify deduplicated fragments in the protein sequence (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Structure/ bwrpsb/bwrpsb.cgi), and the SMART database (http://smart.embl. de/smart/batch.pl) was used to perform domain validation and to manually confirm candidate CesA/Csl gene family members. Multiple sequence alignment was performed between grape VvCesA/Csl protein sequences and collected Arabidopsis CesA/Csl protein sequences using MEGE11.0 software, and the neighbor-joining method was used to determine phylogenetic relationships. The bootstrap replicates were set to repeat 1000 times, and other parameters were set to default. ITOL (https://itol.embl.de/) was used to draw the phylogenetic tree. Subcellular localization prediction was performed using the online software WoLF PSORT (https:// wolfpsort.hgc.jp/). MCScanX is used to analyze collinearity and gene replication events. 

2.9. Gene Cloning. Using grape cDNA synthesized via reverse transcription as a template, gene-specific primers were employed to amplify the coding sequence (CDS) region of the VvCslD5 gene through PCR. The amplified fragment was subsequently cloned into the ligation vector pBWA(V)H-1300-GFP. The accuracy of the cloned gene was verified by sequencing. For the PCR amplification, the $2 \times$ Phanta Flash Master Mix (Dye Plus) detection kit was used, with a total reaction volume of $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ . Detailed information regarding the specific primers, reaction components, and PCR conditions is shown in Supporting Tables S3 and S4. 

2.10. Overexpression and Subcellular Localization. The overexpression and subcellular localization vectors of pBWA(V)H-1300-GFP were constructed by using homologous recombination. Primer sequences for the overexpression vectors are listed in Supporting Table S4. The correctly sequenced recombinant plasmid and empty plasmid were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. 3.3. GWAS Analysis of Grape Berry Firmness. To evaluate grape firmness and cellulose content by resequencing data to obtain relevant SNPs (Suppuporting Tables S7 and S8), multiple models were used to analyze firmness and cellulose content, as shown in the Manhattan and QQ plots (Supporting Figures S3 and S4). In the correlation analysis of peel firmness, it was found that the P values observed by the BLINK and CMLM models were lower than the expected $P$ values, indicating a lower accuracy in the fitting process. Their predicted results deviated significantly from the ideal linear model, resulting in higher deviation values, which are not suitable for this analysis. A total of 203 peel firmness variation sites were identified in the remaining models, excluding duplicate values. There are many clustered SNP sites on chromosomes 4, 6, and 14, which explain 9.3, 19.35, and $2 0 . 4 3 \%$ of the variation, respectively. Especially, there is a significant gene cluster on chromosome 14 between 18.08 and 18.42 MB. There are relatively few SNPs related to fruit flesh firmness. Based on the QQ plot, FarmCPU and BLINK models were better suited for gene screening, identifying 15 variant SNPs. There are two SNP markers on chromosomes 2 and 15, namely, markers 3212695 and 3118595, and markers 4205855 and 4069592. The rest were distributed on multiple chromosomes. 

In the study of fruit peel cellulose (Supporting Tables S9 and S10), it was found that the BLINK model and FarmCPU were more suitable for analyzing this data set by comparing the models. 3.6. Overexpression and Subcellular Localization Analysis. Target genes regulating grape firmness through cellulose were identified by combining GWAS, whole gene identification, and collinearity analysis (Figure 7). VvCslD5 was selected for overexpression and subcellular localization analysis. Subcellular localization showed that it was localized on the plasma membrane, consistent with other species. During the gene transformation process, the recombinant plasmid carrying the VvCslD5 gene was used to construct tomato transgenic lines. Based on the observation of the phenotype of transgenic strains, the height of the VvCslD5-OE strain was 2.03 times higher than that of the wild type and there was a significant change in the thickness of the main stem. After tomato fruit was harvested, the peel and flesh firmness were 1.81 and 1.85 times those of the wild-type strain, respectively. The transverse and longitudinal diameters of the fruits are significantly elongated. # 4. DISCUSSION

Fruit firmness is a critical indicator of growth and development in many horticultural crops. While compounds influencing flesh firmness have been identified in various fruits, such as cherry,36 a pple,3 7 lychee,38 and tomato,39 research on grapefruit firmness remains relatively limited. Nevertheless, significant differences in fruit firmness are observed among grape genotypes even under the same growth conditions. Grapes, one of the most globally important fresh fruits, have diversified as both a fruit and wine ingredient alongside the progression of human civilization. Currently, over 11,000 grape varieties are registered worldwide. Grape plants are classified into two subgenera: muscadine grapes (Muscadinia Planch.) and true grapes (Euvitis Planch.).40 Muscadine grapes consist of three species, often used as rootstocks to enhance disease resistance.41 True grapes encompass 79 species, including those cultivated for fruit production and rootstocks. This subgenus is further divided into three geographically distinct populations: Eurasian, East Asian, and North American. The Eurasian population includes only one species, the Eurasian subspecies, while the East Asian and North American populations comprise 50 and 28 species, respectively. Over $8 0 \%$ of global grape varieties have evolved from Eurasian varieties,42 with fresh grape varieties primarily being Eurasian and European-American hybrids, exhibiting close genetic relationships. 

With the introduction and cultivation of numerous varieties in China, the country’s grape industry has expanded significantly. The selection of cultivated varieties directly 

impacts grape yield and quality, making the identification of genes responsible for fruit firmness and the development of hard-fleshed varieties for storage and transport key objectives in breeding programs. Following two consecutive years of grape germplasm resource investigation, cluster analysis revealed that 25 germplasm resources in the fourth category hold significant potential for breeding high peel and flesh firmness varieties. Future efforts should explore the breeding value of these resources based on comprehensive traits, such as sugar content, acidity, and aroma. Quality traits in breeding are typically governed by a few genes with high heritability and early-stage stability, whereas quantitative traits are controlled by multiple genes with lower heritability and stability. For traits with higher heritability, selection occurs in early generations, while for those with lower heritability, selection should be intensified in later generations.43 Therefore, early-generation gene editing is a promising approach for breeding robust and high-quality fruit traits. 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are widely used to analyze complex traits in crops, requiring a minimum population size with diverse genetic backgrounds. However, the grape genus comprises only 82 species, and fresh grape varieties exhibit relatively close genetic relationships, making GWAS less ideal for gene exploration. Traditional QTL mapping is time-consuming, and for grapes�an economically important fruit, GWAS offers a faster approach to gene discovery, accelerating breeding and improving quality and resistance. Therefore, we conducted GWAS analysis based on previous studies.44−46 

This study identified significantly correlated SNPs through GWAS, targeting fruit firmness and cellulose content. Candidate genes within $1 5 \ \mathrm { k b }$ upstream and downstream of these SNPs were screened, revealing that genes associated with fruit firmness are primarily involved in cell wall structure and modification, hormone regulation, and transcriptional activation. These findings align with those of Lin,26 providing valuable insights for subsequent breeding efforts. Changes in flesh and peel firmness are closely linked to cell wall relaxation and elongation. During fruit development, the synthesis, modification, and relaxation of cell walls lead to structural and compositional changes, influencing the mechanical properties of fruit cell walls.47−49 The activity of enzymes involved in cell wall metabolism and changes in cell wall components profoundly affect fruit firmness,50,51 with genes related to cell wall metabolism playing a central role in this process. Recent studies have focused on pectin, a key target during fruit ripening, with its degradation leading to increased water-soluble pectin content and altered flesh firmness.52,53 Genes such as pectin lyase (PME) and pectin esterase (PE) are crucial in this process.54 This study identified a pectin esterase gene (VIT_06s009g02600), which may contribute to pectin dissolution and depolymerization, affecting grape peel mechanical properties and flesh softening, consistent with previous research.16,55 

In addition to pectin,3 changes in other cell wall polysaccharides, such as cellulose and hemicellulose, significantly impact fruit firmness. Cellulose and hemicellulose, as primary cell wall components, provide mechanical strength to the cell wall structure. This study identified genes related to hemicellulose synthesis (XTH, VIT_15s0048g02850) and c e l l u l o s e s y n t h e s i s ( $V I T \_ 0 7 s 0 \bar { 0 } 5 g 0 4 1 1 0$ , VIT_16s0039g02020). XTH enzymes reconstruct the cell wall network, regulate cell wall relaxation, and are directly 

linked to fruit softening. Silencing XTH10 has been shown to maintain grapefruit firmness.56 Additionally, expansin proteins (e.g., Expansin-B15) promote cell wall extensibility by breaking hydrogen bonds in cell wall polysaccharides, indirectly affecting firmness.57 

Transcription factors (TFs) also play a role in regulating fruit firmness by influencing the softening of fleshy fruits.48 TFs are sequence-specific DNA-binding proteins that interact with cis-acting elements in target gene promoter regions to regulate gene expression.58 This study identified several TFs, including MADS-BOX (VIT_03s0088g00550), AP2/ERF (VIT_03s0063g00460), and LOBs (VIT_13s0019g03750), with LOBs being particularly relevant to fruit firmness. Similar findings have been reported in tomatoes, where LOBs regulate fruit firmness by binding to cell wall-related metabolic enzymes or forming regulatory networks with multiple TFs.59−61 

Compared to GWAS for fruit firmness, candidate genes for cellulose content include fewer cell wall modification genes, with notable examples being glucan endo-1,3- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ -D-glucosidase and $\beta$ -galactosidase, which degrade cellulose and reduce cell wall mechanical strength, promoting fruit softening.46 Hormone response-related genes, particularly auxin-responsive proteins, were also identified. Auxins play a crucial role in fruit growth, from flower formation to ripening, and are involved in cell expansion during early fruit development.62 Gibberellinrelated genes were less prominent, although changes in gibberellin regulation are associated with cellulose accumulation and lignification, impacting berry firmness. Additionally, WAT1 proteins, involved in secondary cell wall development and auxin transport, were identified, with potential roles in cellulose synthesis and fruit firmness regulation.63,64 

To further explore key genes involved in cellulose-mediated grapefruit firmness, a combined GWAS analysis of fruit firmness and cellulose content was conducted, identifying 19 gene types. Notably, ABC transporters and Fe2OG dioxygenase domain-containing proteins were abundant.65 ABC transporters alter cellulose and hemicellulose accumulation by transporting UDP-glucose, potentially affecting fruit firmness.66 Cellulose synthase genes, including CesA1, were also identified, with overexpression studies in strawberry and grape demonstrating their influence on fruit firmness.67−69 All identified cellulose synthase proteins contain the Cellulose_- synt domain, and 11 VvCesA members feature the zf-UDP domain, associated with glycosyl transfer, highlighting their critical role in cellulose synthesis.70 Overexpression experiments confirmed that these genes significantly alter the cellulose content, further supporting their role in regulating fruit firmness. Subcellular localization experiments revealed that these cellulose synthase genes are functionally positioned on the plasma membrane, implicating them in cell wall construction during cellulose synthesis.7 

This study suggests that the high firmness of grape berries is influenced by multiple factors. Future research should compare dynamic gene expression patterns during field growth to elucidate the genetic mechanisms underlying berry firmness. Such efforts will enhance our understanding of the complex molecular networks regulating traits like grape shelf life and fruit firmness, ultimately advancing breeding programs for improved grape varieties.
