# ABSTRACT

The timing of flowering is tightly controlled by signals that integrate environmental and endogenous cues. Sugars produced by carbon fixation in the chloroplast are a crucial endogenous cue for floral initiation. Chloroplasts also convey information directly to the nucleus through retrograde signaling to control plant growth and development. Here, we show that mutants defective in chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development flowered early, especially under long-day conditions, although low sugar accumulation was seen in some mutants. Plants treated with the bleaching herbicide norflurazon also flowered early, suggesting that chloroplasts have a role in floral repression. Among retrograde signaling mutants, the golden2-like 1 (glk1) glk2 double mutants showed early flowering under long-day conditions. This early flowering was completely suppressed by constans (co) and flowering locus t (ft) mutations. Leaf vascular-specific knockdown of both GLK1 and GLK2 phenocopied the glk1 glk2 mutants. GLK1 and GLK2 repress flowering by directly activating the expression of B-BOX DOMAIN PROTEIN 14 (BBX14), BBX15, and BBX16 via CCAATC cis-elements in the BBX genes. BBX14/15/16 physically interact with CO in the nucleus, and expression of BBXs hampered CO-mediated FT transcription. Simultaneous knockdown of BBX14/15/16 by artificial miRNA (35S::amiR–BBX14/15/16) caused early flowering with increased FT transcript levels, whereas BBX overexpression caused late flowering. Flowering of glk1/2 and 35S::amiR–BBX14/15/16 plants was insensitive to norflurazon treatment. Taking these observations together, we propose that the GLK1/2–BBX14/15/16 module provides a novel mechanism explaining how the chloroplast represses flowering to balance plant growth and reproductive development.

# INTRODUCTION

Plants modulate the timing of flowering by integrating seasonal cues with internal signals (Andre´ s and Coupland, 2012; Kinoshita and Richter, 2020). Flowering time is controlled by several genetic pathways, including the photoperiod, vernalization, aging, and ambient temperature pathways, which sense internal and external cues and trigger or suppress the floral transition (Bouche´ et al., 2016). The signals from these pathways modulate the expression of the florigen gene FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) and its paralog TWIN SISTER OF FT (TSF) in the leaf, and the floral integrator SUPPRESSOR OF OVEREXPRESSION OF CONSTANS 1 (SOC1) in the shoot apical meristem (SAM) (Kinoshita and Richter, 2020). 

Under a long-day (LD) photoperiod, FT expression is positively regulated by CONSTANS (CO) and nuclear factor Y (NF-Y) transcription factors, which promote chromatin looping at the FT promoter (Cao et al., 2014). CO has an N-terminal B-box domain and a C-terminal CCT (CO, CO-LIKE, and TOC1) domain (Khanna et al., 2009). The CCT domain is essential for forming a trimeric complex with NF-YB and NF-YC. The B-box domain is essential for forming CO multimers to promote high-affinity, specific binding of CO to the FT promoter (Lv et al., 2021). Several B-box

proteins (BBXs) also physically interact with CO through the B-box domain to suppress its transcriptional activity (Song et al., 2020). This CO–FT regulatory module has conserved functions in the photoperiodic control of flowering in other plants (Andre´ s and Coupland, 2012; Kinoshita and Richter, 2020). 

Plant age positively regulates flowering time, and sugars likely act as the signaling molecules in this process (Yang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013). The application of exogenous sugars, such as sucrose, glucose, fructose, and maltose, reduces the accumulation of microRNA156 (miR156), a repressor of flowering time (Yu et al., 2013). Trehalose6-phosphate (T6P) also acts as a signal to promote flowering by preventing MIR156 expression, thus allowing the accumulation the miR156 targets SQUAMOSA PROMOTER BINDING PROTEIN-LIKE 3 (SPL3), SPL4, and SPL5 (Wahl et al., 2013). The levels of T6P are positively correlated with FT levels (Wahl et al., 2013), which are likely regulated by the miR156–SPL3–FT genetic circuitry (Kim et al., 2012). 

The chloroplast produces sugars in photosynthesis and communicates with the nucleus via retrograde signaling (RS), which controls the expression of photosynthesis-associated nuclear genes to regulate chloroplast development in response to various environmental factors (Chan et al., 2016; Herna´ ndez-Verdeja and Strand, 2018). Four major chloroplast RS pathways have been described: (1) signals related to the tetrapyrrole biosynthesis pathway; (2) signals triggered by plastid gene expression; (3) signals related to reactive oxygen species generated by the photosynthetic electron transport chain; and (4) signals from plastid metabolism, such as accumulation of ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ -phosphoadenosine $5 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphate (PAP) and 2-C-methyl-D-erythritol 2,4-cyclodiphosphate (Herna´ ndez-Verdeja and Strand, 2018). 

Multiple genes are involved in chloroplast development, chlorophyll biosynthesis, and RS. In angiosperms, plastid genes are transcribed by the plastid-encoded RNA polymerase (PEP) or the nuclear-encoded RNA polymerase (NEP) (Herna´ ndez-Verdeja and Strand, 2018). PEP activity requires SIGMA FACTOR 2 (SIG2) and SIG6, which encode a subunit of chloroplast RNA polymerase and a general chloroplast sigma factor, respectively (Woodson et al., 2013). SCABRA3 (SCA3) encodes the plastid RpoTp RNA polymerase for plastid transcription (Hricova et al., 2006). PEP and NEP generate the signals responsible for chloroplast RS (Chan et al., 2016; Herna´ ndez-Verdeja and Strand, 2018). The PEP-transcribed tRNAglu is the precursor of the tetrapyrrole biosynthesis pathway, in which GENOMES UNCOUPLED4 (GUN4; an activator of Mgchelatase), GUN5 (the H subunit of Mg-chelatase), and COPPER RESPONSE DEFECT1 (CRD1/CHL27) convert protoporphyrinogen IX into the chlorophyll precursor chlorophyllide a (Chan et al., 2016). Furthermore, SCA1 encodes a plastid-type ribosomal protein that functions in 16S rRNA processing and plastid development (Mateo-Bonmati et al., 2015). GUN1, a plastid-localized protein with homology to pentatricopeptide repeat domain-containing proteins, is a central protein in chloroplast RS, modulating plastid proteostasis, RNA editing, and tetrapyrrole biosynthesis (Tadini et al., 2016; Shimizu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2019). GUN1 also promotes translocation of the N-terminal domain of PHD TYPE TRANSCRIPTION FACTOR WITH 

TRANSMEMBRANE DOMAINS (PTM) from the chloroplast envelope to the nucleus (Feng et al., 2016), although the role of PTM in chloroplast RS is now being scrutinized (Page et al., 2017). 

Chloroplast RS signals modulate nuclear gene expression through several transcription factors, including LONG HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5), GOLDEN2-LIKE 1 (GLK1), and GLK2 (Chan et al., 2016; Herna´ ndez-Verdeja and Strand, 2018; Ortiz-Alcaide et al., 2019). In addition, GATA transcription factors, including GATA, NITRATE-INDUCIBLE, CARBON METABOLISM-INVOLVED (GNC), and CYTOKININ-RESPONSIVE GATA FACTOR 1 (CGA1), are essential for chloroplast development and photosynthetic gene expression (Ruckle et al., 2007; Waters et al., 2009; Zubo et al., 2018). Chloroplast RS also regulates plant stress responses (Estavillo et al., 2011; Feng et al., 2016; Gommers et al., 2020); for instance, PAP accumulation through the regulation of FIERY1 (FRY1) is essential for abiotic stress responses (Estavillo et al., 2011). Although chloroplast RS is known to regulate multiple developmental processes, little is known about the function of chloroplast RS in the regulation of flowering time except for the repression of the floral repressor FLOWERING LOCUS C (FLC) by PTM (Feng et al., 2016). 

Here, we show that several mutants defective in chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development, as well as plants treated with the bleaching herbicide norflurazon (NF), showed accelerated flowering under LD conditions. We identified GLK1 and GLK2 as the main signaling hub that explains the negative role of chloroplasts in regulating flowering time. GLK1 and GLK2 (Waters et al., 2009) directly promote the expression of BBX14, BBX15, and BBX16. BBX14/15/16 interact with CO in the nucleus, hampering the ability of CO to activate FT. Our findings reveal how the chloroplast prevents precocious flowering at early developmental stages to balance plant growth and development. 

# RESULTS

# The chloroplast prevents precocious flowering through GLK1 and GLK2

To investigate the connection between flowering time and chloroplast RS, we measured the flowering time of mutants defective in chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development (crd1, gun4, gun5, sca1, sca3, sig2, and sig6). Under $\scriptstyle 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ LD conditions, crd1, sca1-2, sca3-2, and sig2-2mutants flowered earlier thanwildtype (WT) Col-0 plants (Figure 1A; Supplemental Table 1). Under $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ LD conditions, the gun4, gun5, and sig6-1 mutants, which showed WT-like flowering at $_ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 1A), also flowered early, showing that all tested mutants flowered earlier than WT plants at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . This finding suggested that a defect in chloroplast development affects temperature-responsive flowering. Fructose, sucrose, maltose, and trehalose levels were reduced in sca1-2 and sig2-2 mutants (Supplemental Table 2), which is inconsistent with the notion that sugars generally accelerate flowering (Yang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013). Soluble sugar levels were relatively unperturbed in crd1 mutants, except for an increase in glucose levels. Therefore, these results suggested that the chloroplast negatively regulates flowering via an unknown pathway (Figure 1B), in addition to its central role in sugar production (Wahl et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013).

# GLK1/2 and BBX14/15/16 modulate the enrichment of CO at the FT chromatin

To investigate the role of the BBX–CO interaction in the regulation of FT expression, we performed dual-luciferase effector–reporter assays in Arabidopsis protoplasts. The expression of the pFT::fLUC reporter was enhanced by CO ( ${ \sim } 1 3 . 3$ -fold) but not by BBX14/15/16 (Supplemental Figure 5E). By contrast, the DBbox 

versions of BBXs failed to affect pFT::LUC activity when coexpressed with CO, while the DCCT version showed only a slight reduction ( $1 9 \%$ to $31 \%$ reduction) (Supplemental Figure 5F). 

We tested the effect of mutation of BBXs. We used amiR technology (Schwab et al., 2006) to knockdown BBX14 alone or BBX14/15/16 together. To knock out BBX15 and BBX16, we used CRISPR–Cas12a gene editing technology (Schindele and Puchta, 2020) and generated bbx15-cr and bbx16-cr mutants (Supplemental Figure 6A). Sequencing revealed that both bbx15-cr and bbx16-cr plants have a 5-bp deletion in exon 1 (Supplemental Figure 6B), causing a frameshift mutation and producing a premature stop codon. Knockdown of targeted gene(s) in the 35S::amiR–bbx14 and 35S::amiR–bbx14/15/16 lines was verified (Supplemental Figure 6C), indicating that these lines could be used for subsequent analyses. We also generated BBXoverexpressing plants and verified their overexpression (Supplemental Figure 6D). We observed increased FT mRNA levels (2.4-fold) in 35S::amiR–bbx14/15/16 plants but not in 35S::amiR–bbx14, bbx15-cr, and bbx16-cr plants (Figure 5B). We observed an increase of TSF and SOC1 mRNA levels (2.6- and 1.4-fold, respectively) only in 35S::amiR–bbx14/15/16 seedlings (Supplemental Figure 6E). Consistent with these results, FT mRNA levels were reduced (1.5- to 2.8-fold) in BBX14/15/16-overexpressing plants (Figure 5C), while TSF (1.3- to 1.6-fold) and SOC1 (1.3- to 1.9-fold) mRNA levels were slightly reduced (Supplemental Figure 6F). 

Next, we performed ChIP assays to investigate the enrichment of CO in the $F T$ promoter in glk1/2 mutants and BBX16-overexpressing plants. Consistent with the reduced expression levels of BBX14/15/16 in glk1/2 mutants (Figure 3A), we observed increased CO enrichment $( \sim 3 2 \%$ increase) in the proximal region of the FT promoter in glk1/2 mutants, whereas reduction of CO enrichment $( \sim 4 0 \% )$ was observed in BBX16- overexpressing plants (Figure 5D). CO protein levels were unaltered in glk1/2 mutants and BBX16-overexpressing plants (Supplemental Figure 6G), excluding the possibility that altered CO levels caused the change in CO enrichment. These results suggested that BBX14/15/16 regulated $F T$ transcription by physically interacting with CO and preventing CO binding to the FT promoter. 

# The flowering time of bbx mutants and BBXoverexpressing plants was insensitive to NF treatment

To investigate the role of BBX14/15/16 in chloroplast RSmediated regulation of flowering time, we examined flowering time of bbx mutants and BBX-overexpressing plants with NF treatment. Under $_ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ LD conditions, 35S::amiR–bbx14/15/16 plants flowered early $( 7 . 9 \pm 0 . 9$ leaves; WT: $1 3 . 1 \pm 0 . 8$ leaves), while 35S::amiR–bbx14, bbx15-cr, and bbx16-cr plants showed similar flowering time with WT plants (Figure 6A and Supplemental Figure 6H). By contrast, BBX-overexpressing plants flowered later than WT plants (21–23 leaves; WT: $1 6 . 4 \pm$ 0.8 leaves) (Figure 6B and Supplemental Figure 6I). The observed flowering time changes were consistent with the changes in FT, TSF, and SOC1 mRNA levels (Figure 5B and 5C and Supplemental Figure 6E and 6F). These results suggested that BBX14/15/16 function as floral repressors and act redundantly in flowering time regulation.

# DISCUSSION

In this study, we identified a pathway that regulates flowering time through chloroplast RS. The chloroplast acts as a floral repressor via the GLK1/2 transcription factors, which upregulate BBX14/ 15/16. These BBXs physically interact with CO and impair CO binding to the $F T$ promoter (Figure 6C). Our findings suggest that the chloroplast plays dual roles in regulating flowering time, acting as a positive regulator through the production of sugars (which explains age-dependent flowering time regulation) (Wahl et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2013; Yu et al., 2013) and acting as a negative regulator through the GLK1/2–BBX14/15/16 module. 

The function of chloroplast RS in flowering time is not well understood. A previous study reported that PTM releases its N-terminal 

fragment in response to high light to promote flowering by suppressing FLC expression (Feng et al., 2016). However, our results suggested that PTM is not essential for the floral transition, at least under normal LD conditions (Supplemental Figure 1A). Interestingly, we found that GUN1 likely plays a role as a floral activator (Figure 1C), confirming a previous finding (Wu et al., 2018). By contrast, PAP accumulation in fry1-6 mutants causes late flowering, suggesting that PAP acts as a floral repressor (Figure 1C) (Kim and von Arnim, 2009). Although the function of PAP remains elusive, PAP likely regulates flowering through light and hormonal signaling (Kim and von Arnim, 2009; Phua et al., 2018). Further exploration of the role of chloroplast RS in flowering time regulation is necessary to uncover the complexity of chloroplast function in plant development (Susila et al., 2016). 

We show that GLK1/2 mediate flowering time regulation by the chloroplast. GLKs are members of the GARP family of plant Myb-related transcription factors (Chen et al., 2016) and play a conserved role in regulating chloroplast development across land plants (Fitter et al., 2002; Chen et al., 2016). GLKs are also involved in biotic and abiotic stress responses, photomorphogenesis, stomatal movement, and anthocyanin production (Chen et al., 2016; Martı´n et al., 2016; Nagatoshi et al., 2016; Ahmad et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021). Furthermore, GLKs play pivotal roles in hormonal signaling, including jasmonic acid, salicylic acid, abscisic acid, brassinosteroid, and auxin (Chen et al., 2016; Ahmad et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Alem et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), which are known to affect flowering (Conti, 2017). Therefore, it will be interesting to determine whether chloroplast RS also regulates these signaling processes. 

The exposure of plants to high-light irradiation promotes flowering (Feng et al., 2016; Susila et al., 2016). Interestingly, high light reduces the expression of class III BBX transcription factor genes and GLK1 (Martı´n et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2019). Based on these findings, it is tempting to speculate that chloroplast RS plays a role in the regulation of flowering time mediated by high light through the GLK1/2–BBX14/15/16 module. However, this hypothesis requires further testing. 

An interesting observation is that the flowering times of most chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development mutants that we tested, including glk1/2 mutants, were relatively insensitive to temperature changes (Supplemental Table 1). These results imply that chloroplast RS intersected with the ambient temperature pathway (Susila et al., 2018). The function of GLK1/2 in the ambient temperature pathway is likely independent of the role in the regulation of florigen expression, as the increase in FT mRNA levels in the companion cell did not alter temperature-responsive flowering (Susila et al., 2021b). The transport of FT was restricted at low temperature through the interaction with phosphatidylglycerol, and FT prefers to interact with polyunsaturated phosphatidylglycerol species (Susila et al., 2021b). Considering that GLK1/2 also promotes the synthesis of galactolipids, especially unsaturated fatty acid species (Kobayashi et al., 2014), it will be interesting to determine whether GLK1/2 could alter the composition of phospholipids and thus regulate FT transport. 

In conclusion, our findings reveal the role of the chloroplast in preventing precocious flowering under LD conditions. The 

GLK1/2–BBX14/15/16 transcriptional cascade is integrated into the canonical CO–FT signaling pathway, which is conserved across many flowering plants. The mechanism likely balances plant growth and reproductive development by preventing flowering at early developmental stages, allowing the plant to grow and produce sugars, thereby maximizing reproductive success. These findings thus increase our understanding of how organelles communicate to coordinate plant development.

# Method details

# Chemical treatments and measurement of photosynthetic parameters and flowering time

For chemical treatment, the NF (Sigma-Aldrich) stock solution was prepared in ethanol. For the measurement of flowering time, diluted NF solution was applied to plants by spraying them every two days until the sprayed plants bolted. For short-term treatments to test the effect of NF on gene expression, the NF solution was added to the Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium 24 h before sampling. 

Plants were grown in soil or on standard MS medium at ${ } ^ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under LD conditions (16:8-h light:dark) and at ${ } ^ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under SD conditions (8:16-h light:dark). Chlorophyll a and b levels were measured using ethanol as a solvent (Lichtenthaler, 1987). The photosynthetic parameters Fo, Fm, and Rdf were measured using the FluorPen FP 110 (Photon System Instruments). 

For the measurement of flowering time, total leaf number was counted when the primary inflorescence reached approximately 5 cm. Boxplots were constructed to represent flowering time distribution (Postma and Goedhart, 2019). The data are shown as a boxplot, with the box indicating the interquartile range, the whiskers showing the range of values that are within $1 . 5 \ \times$ interquartile range, and a horizontal line indicating the median. The $P$ values were obtained using Student’s t-test or one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s range test.

The transcript levels were measured by quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) using a QuantStudio5 qPCR instrument (Applied Biosystems). For normalization, two stably expressed genes, PP2AA3 (AT1G13320) and a SAND family protein (AT2G29390), were used (Hong et al., 2010). All qPCR experiments were carried out in three

# Measurement of sugar levels

Gas chromatography/flame ionization detection (FID) was used to measure sugar levels. Sugars were extracted from 12-day-old plants grown at $_ { 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under LD conditions with $80 \%$ ethanol in 50 mM HEPES–KOH (pH 7.5) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. The supernatant was evaporated to dryness under nitrogen and resuspended in pyridine. Samples were derivatized with hexamethyldisilazane and chlorotrimethylsilane at ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 30 min in closed vials. Products of derivatization were analyzed on a gas chromatography-FID system (Agilent Technologies, type 7890A) equipped with HP-1 MS columns $( 3 0 \times 0 . 2 5$ $\times \ : 0 . 5$ ; Agilent Technologies). Helium, hydrogen, and air flow rates were maintained at 1.4, 30, and $4 0 0 \mathrm { { m } } \mathrm { { / m i n } }$ , respectively. The inlet and FID detector temperatures were maintained at $_ { 2 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and ${ } ^ { 2 9 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ , respectively. The temperature program was as follows: $1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (hold 2 min), increase by $6 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } / { \mathsf { m i n } }$ to $_ { 2 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , and increase by $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / \mathsf { m i n }$ to $2 7 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (hold 18 min).
