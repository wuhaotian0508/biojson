# ABSTRACT

Introduction: The postharvest physiological disorder known as ’black spot’ in radish roots (Raphanus sativus) poses a significant challenge to quality maintenance during storage, particularly under summer conditions. The cause of this disorder, however, is poorly understood. 

Objectives: Characterize the underlying causes of ’black spot’ disorder in radish roots and identify strategies to delay its onset. 

Methods: Radish roots were placed in either polyvinyl chloride (PVC) or oriented polypropylene (OPP) packaging and stored for 4 days at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Appearance and physiological parameters were assessed and transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses were conducted to identify the key molecular and biochemical factors contributing to the disorder and strategies for delaying its onset and development. 

Results: OPP packaging effectively delayed the onset of ’black spot’ in radishes, potentially due to changes in phenolic and lipid metabolism. Regarding phenolic metabolism, POD and PPO activity decreased, RsCCR and RsPOD expression was downregulated, genes involved in phenols and flavonoids synthesis were 

upregulated and their content increased, preventing the oxidative browning of phenols and generally enhancing stress tolerance. Regarding lipid metabolism, the level of alpha-linolenic acid increased, and genes regulating cutin and wax synthesis were upregulated. Notably, high flavonoid and low ROS levels collectively inhibited RsPLA2G expression, which reduced the production of arachidonic acid, proinflammatory compounds $\mathrm { { L T A _ { 4 } } }$ and $\mathsf { P G G } _ { 2 }$ ), and ROS, alleviating the inflammatory response and oxidative stress in radish epidermal tissues. 

Conclusion: PVC packaging enhanced the postharvest onset of ‘black spot’ in radishes, while OPP packaging delayed both its onset and development. Our study provides insights into the response of radishes to different packaging materials during storage, and the causes and host responses that either enhance or delay ‘black spot’ disorder onset. Further studies will be conducted to confirm the molecular and biochemical processes responsible for the onset and development of ‘black spot’ in radishes.

# Introduction

Radish (Raphanus sativus L.) is an annual or biennial herbaceous plant in the Brassicaceae family and is native to China. It is extensively cultivated in China, as well as Japan, Korea, the United States, and Europe [1–3]. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) listed the annual worldwide production of radish species (including carrots and turnips) in 2021 to be approximately 42 million tons. China produces about 18 million tons of radishes annually, representing $4 3 \%$ of the world’s total annual production. Radishes are easily cultivated, adaptable, shelf-stable, and readily transportable [2,4]. Radish roots are abundant in nutrients, including folic acid, phenolic acids, anthocyanins, dietary fiber, and essential minerals [5,6]. They are also rich in glucosinolates (GS), which have cancer-preventive properties [7,8], and hydroalcoholic extracts of radishes have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activity [5,9]. Radish roots are eaten as fresh vegetables or processed to make popular food products such as pickles and dried radish roots [8]. 

Our initial observations of stored and marketed radishes indicated that the development of ‘black spot’ is characterized by the appearance of black spots on the epidermal surface of radish roots, which then increase with prolonged storage, and ultimately result in the entire root having a grayish-brown discoloration, severely degrading its appearance. ‘Black spot’ is a physiological disorder and is not caused by a microbial pathogen. It is also concentrated in epidermal tissues of radishes and does not affect internal appearance or quality. ‘Black spot’ is distinctly different from bacterial black spot and black rot [10], and only a few reports have mentioned this disorder. Chandra et al. [10] observed the described symptoms on stored radish roots and called it ’black spot’. We have adopted this terminology to cover the symptoms observed by us and other researchers. Peel color is used by consumers as a major indicator of freshness and quality of radish roots [10]. ’Black spot’ symptoms intensify during the shelf-life of radishes and the disorder is especially prevalent during the high temperatures of summer, severely impacting radish quality and its commercial value. In addition to storage conditions, packaging materials also influence the occurrence of ’black spot’. The utilization of microperforated High-Density Polyethylene film packaging can result in a low incidence of ‘black spot’ in radish roots stored at lowtemperature $( 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ [10]. The application of modified atmosphere packaging on radishes coated with a mixture of $2 \%$ calcium chloride and $1 \%$ starch $+ \ 2 \ \%$ glycerin reduced malondialdehyde content (an indicator of oxidative stress injury) and cell membrane damage in radishes during storage at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ [11]. ‘Black spot’ on radish roots, however, has been recognized as a common problem of room temperature storage in summer months. Notably, the underlying causes of this physiological disorder are poorly understood. Therefore, research on the etiology and causes of this physiological 

disorder are warranted to develop effective and economical preventative treatments. 

Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) has excellent flexibility, self-adhesion, high transparency, and rebound characteristics and is the most widely used plastic material in the world today, especially for food packaging [12]. Oriented polypropylene (OPP) is colorless, tasteless, and non-toxic, with high impact strength and toughness, and products wrapped in OPP can be fully observed due to its high level of transparency [13]. These two materials are the most common packaging materials used in the marketplace for vegetables. PVC is commonly used for packaging of radishes in the Chinese market, however, we observed in our initial observations that PVC packaging induces high levels of ’black spot’ during the postharvest storage of radishes. In contrast, OPP film, a material not typically used in radish root packaging, emerged superior to PVC packaging due to its ability to delay ‘black spot’ symptoms. As such it may represent an important alternative packaging material whose use should be fully explored. 

The combined use of transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses is a valuable approach for investigating postharvest changes in fruit and vegetable quality, providing valuable insights into crucial metabolic pathways and related gene expression that are impacted by storage conditions, packaging, aging, diseases, etc. [14]. Thus, their use could provide deeper insight into the regulation of postharvest deterioration of radish roots [1]. In a recent study, the mechanism underlying blue discoloration during radish preservation was investigated using this approach [15]. Therefore, we utilized transcriptomic and metabolomic investigations to characterize the impact of PVC and OPP packaging materials on radish roots stored for 4 days at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in relation to the causes of postharvest ‘black spot’ disorder. Our results provide a foundation for understanding the impact of storage conditions and packaging materials on the quality and appearance of radish roots, as well as the causes and prevention of ‘black spot’ disorder. 

# Material and Methods

# Plant material and experimental design

Radishes (Raphanus sativus L., ‘Jelia’) used in the experiment were planted in Dahigou Village, Shangyi County, Zhangjiakou City, Hebei Province, China (40.77 E latitude, 114.35 N longitude). Seeds were sown on June 26th, 2022, harvested on August 30th, 2022, and immediately transported to the laboratory. The radish roots were cleaned of surface soil with water and then air dried. Radishes of consistent size and free from pests, diseases, or mechanical damage were chosen for the experiment. Stems and leaves were trimmed from the radishes about $5 \mathrm { m m }$ from the base of the leaves. In our preliminary experiments, we investigated the 

causes of the ’black spot’ phenomenon in radishes under three different packaging conditions: unpackaged, PVC-packaged, and OPPpackaged. During these trials, the unpackaged radishes exhibited severe water loss and extensive physiological changes, leading to a rapid decline in overall quality and commercial value (Fig. S1). These deteriorations rendered it challenging to accurately assess the ’black spot’ issue in the unpackaged radishes. Therefore, we selected PVC and OPP packaging materials for a comprehensive study to explore the underlying causes of the ’black spot’ phenomenon in radishes. Radishes were categorized into two groups, each containing 120 radishes, with one group packaged in polyvinyl chloride film (PVC, $0 . 0 3 ~ \mathrm { { m m } }$ thick, double layer), and the other group packaged in oriented polypropylene bags (OPP, $0 . 0 3 \mathrm { \ m m }$ thick). Each radish root was packaged in a separate bag. The packaged radishes were stored for four days at a constant $3 0 \pm 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $8 5 \sim 9 0 \%$ relative humidity. Peel samples (about 1 mm thick) in each treatment group were taken daily with a sterile paring knife. The tissue samples were then frozen in liquid nitrogen and kept at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ until subsequent processing. The experiment was repeated three times. 

# Assessment of black spot incidence, color, and browning index

Black spot incidence was visually rated using a scale of 0–5, based on the scale used by Chandra et al. [10], where $0 \ : = \ : \mathrm { { n o } }$ , $1 =$ mild, $2 =$ moderate, $3 = { \mathrm { h i g h } }$ , and $4 =$ severe black spot symptoms. L*, a*, $\mathbf { b } ^ { * }$ , and $\Delta \mathsf { E }$ color values in the peel of each radish were determined daily using a CR-400 automatic colorimeter (Japan) in a fixed marked area. The browning index (BI) was calculated according to the method of Plaou et al. [16] using the following formula: $\mathsf { B I } = [ 1 0 0 ^ { * } ( \mathbf { x } { - } 0 . 3 1 ) ] / 0 . 1 7 2$ , x=(a ± 1.75L)/(5.645L + a-3.012b). 

# Quality assessment

Weight loss (WL) in individual radish roots was measured by obtaining the initial weight (IW) and the final weight (FW) of individual radish roots. Percentage fresh weight loss was calculated using the following formula: WL $( \% ) = ( \mathrm { I W } - \mathrm { F W } ) / \mathrm { I W } \times 1 0 0$ . 

The determination of firmness utilized a slight modification of the method reported by Chatkaew and Kim [17]. Radish roots were penetrated to a depth of $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ at a speed of $1 0 0 \mathrm { m m } s ^ { - 1 }$ using a texture analyzer (Food Technology Corporation, U.K.) equipped with a cylindrical probe $0 . 5 \ \mathrm { m m }$ diameter). The maximum force needed to penetrate the tissue was used as a measure of firmness and measured in Newtons (N). 

Total soluble solids (TSS) were quantified with a hand-held refractometer (Atago PAL-1, Tokyo, Japan) and expressed in Brix $( \% )$ . 

The pH values were assessed using a modification of the method reported by Pushkara et al. [18]. Five grams of radish tissue were added to $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of distilled water and left to stand for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The pH of the solution was then determined using a calibrated pH meter (Mettler Toledo Seven Compact, Switzerland). 

Respiration rate and ethylene generation were determined as described by Zhao et al. [19]. The respiration rate of individual radishes was measured with a GHX-3051 respirometer (Jun Fang Li Hua Technology Research Institute of Beijing, Beijing, China). Each radish was sealed in a glass container for one hour prior to the measurement of the respiration rate, which was expressed as mg $\mathbf { C O } _ { 2 } \ \mathbf { k g } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathbf { h } ^ { - 1 }$ . Ethylene levels in individual radish roots were also measured. Each radish root was enclosed in an airtight container for one hour, and then $1 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of headspace gas was withdrawn through a gas port, and introduced into a 7820 A gas chromatograph (Agilent Technologies, Inc., Santa Clara, CA, USA). Ethylene levels were quantified by establishing a linear association 

between peak area and ethylene concentration and reported as $\mu \mathrm { m o l }$ ethylene $\mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

The concentrations of total phenols and flavonoids were quantified using UV spectrophotometry following the methodology reported by Zhao et al. [19]. The concentrations were expressed as milligram equivalents of gallic acid and epicatechin per gram of sample tissue weight $\left( { \mathrm { m g } } / { \mathrm { g } } \right)$ , respectively, based on absorbance measurements at 760 and 490 nm. 

Phenolic acid compounds were identified and quantified using 1260 high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC, Agilent Technologies Co., Ltd., USA) as described in Xu et al. [20]. Samples (5 g) were put into 5 mL methanol, sonicated at $4 0 ~ \mathrm { H z }$ for $4 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and then centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 \times g$ for 20 min. Following filtration using a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter, the HPLC analysis utilized the supernatants. The chromatographic setup utilized an Eclipse Plus C18 column $2 5 0 \ : \mathrm { m m } \times 4 . 6 \ : \mathrm { m m }$ , $5 \mu \mathrm { m } \dot { }$ ) maintained at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , a detection wavelength of $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , and a $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ sample size. The mobile phase was composed of a linear gradient of $1 \%$ formic acid (A) and methanol (B), transitioning from $1 5 \%$ to $3 0 \%$ in 3 min, $3 0 \%$ to $45 \%$ in $3 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , $45 \%$ to $6 5 \%$ in 45 min, and $6 5 \%$ to $1 5 \%$ in 50 min at a flow rate of $0 . 6 \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . Phenolic acids were identified through the retention times and UV spectra of HPLC quality standards. Levels are reported as $\mu \textrm { g } \mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

Relative electrical conductivity, malondialdehyde (MDA) content, and levels of superoxide anion $( 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - } ,$ level and hydrogen peroxide $( H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } )$ ， 

Relative electrical conductivity and MDA content were determined as described by Yan et al. [21]. The procedure for measuring relative electrical conductivity involved an initial and subsequent reading of conductivity with a conductivity meter of a solution containing radish peel before and after boiling. MDA content was assessed by mixing radish peel with trichloroacetic acid, followed by centrifugation, and colorimetric measurement of the reaction of the supernatant with thiobarbituric acid at 450 nm, $5 3 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and $6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ wavelengths. MDA content is expressed as mol $\mathbf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

$0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels were assessed by UV spectroscopy using the methodology of Xu et al. [22]. For the assessment of $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , radish peel (5 g) was homogenized in $5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ phosphate buffer (PBS, $\mathsf { p H } ~ 7 . 8 )$ followed by centrifugation $( 1 2 , 0 0 0 ~ \times ~ g ~ ) ~ \times ~ g ~$ for $2 0 \ \mathrm { \ m i n }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm C$ . Then $1 \textrm { m L }$ of the resulting supernatant was incubated with PBS and $1 \ \mathrm { \ m M }$ hydroxylamine hydrochloride at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ for $^ \mathrm { ~ 1 ~ h ~ }$ , and then reacted with $1 7 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ p-amino benzene sulfonic acid and 7 mM $\pmb { \alpha }$ -naphthylamine for $2 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , after which the absorbance of the reaction solution was measured at $5 3 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . For the measure of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , $_ { 5 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of radish peel was homogenized in 5 mL of pre-cooled acetone and then centrifuged. Subsequently, $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m L }$ of $10 \%$ titanium tetrachloride-hydrochloric acid and $0 . 2 \mathrm { ~ m L }$ of ammonium hydroxide was added to $1 . 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of the resulting supernatant. The mixture was centrifuged again and the resulting precipitate was washed three times with acetone and then dissolved in 2 M sulfuric acid (3 mL). Absorbance of the resulting reaction solution was measured at $4 1 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ . 

# Enzyme activity

UV spectrophotometry was utilized to determine the activity of polyphenol oxidase (PPO), peroxidase (POD), catalase (CAT), and superoxide dismutase (SOD) as described in Xu et al., Shi et al., and Wu et al. [22–24]. Briefly, $2 . 0 \ { \ g }$ of radish peel was extracted three times with $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 1 \textbf { M }$ phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.0) containing $1 \ \mathrm { m M }$ ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), $2 \% ( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { w } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } )$ polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), and $1 \% ( \mathsf { v } / \mathsf { v } )$ Triton X-100. Each extraction mixture was retained and then pooled. The resulting extract was then subjected to centrifugation at $1 3 , 0 0 0 \times \mathbf { g }$ for 

$2 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ to obtain a supernatant. The PPO reaction mixture contained $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of supernatant, $0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 5 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ catechol substrate, and $2 . 1 \textrm { m L }$ of PBS. The POD reaction mixture included $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of supernatant, 1 mL of 0.1 M PBS, $0 . 9 \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 2 \% \mathrm { ( v / v ) }$ guaiacol, and 1 mL of $0 . 3 \ \% ( \mathrm { v / v ) \ H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } }$ . The CAT reaction system contained $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of supernatant $1 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 1 \textbf { M }$ PBS, and $1 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of $0 . 3 \ \% \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } 0 _ { 2 }$ . The SOD reaction system contained $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m L }$ of supernatant, $1 . 7 ~ \mathrm { \ m L }$ of 0.05 M sodium phosphate buffer $\left( \mathrm { p H } ~ 7 . 8 \right)$ $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of 0.13 M methionine, $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $7 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ nitro blue tetrazolium (NBT), $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ riboflavin, and $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ EDTA-Na, which was placed under 4000 LUX fluorescent lamp for $1 5 ~ \mathrm { \ m i n }$ . The assessment of PPO, POD, CAT, and SOD activity involved measuring changes in absorbance at 420 nm, $4 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ $2 4 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , and $5 6 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ over 1 min, respectively. The quantity of enzyme causing a change in absorbance unit at a specific wavelength per minute was defined as one unit (U) of activity. SOD activity unit was defined as the amount of the reaction system that causes a $5 0 \%$ inhibition of NBT photoreduction per gram of samples per minute. 

# Transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses

Transcriptomic sequencing data and metabolomics analyses were conducted on radish root peel samples collected on the initial day (day 0) and different treatment groups on days 1 and 3, comprising a total of five sample groups (Initial-0d, PVC-1d, OPP-1d, PVC-3d, and OPP-3d). Three sequencing libraries were constructed for each sample group $\left( \mathrm { n } = 3 \right) ,$ and biological replicates were used for each sample group in the metabolomic analysis $( \mathtt { n } ) = 6 ]$ ). The transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses, including sample extraction, library preparation, sequencing, peak separation and identification, annotation, etc., were conducted by Beijing Biomarker Technologies (Beijing, China). 

# Rna-seq library preparation, sequencing, and gene functional annotation

The extraction of total RNA from each of the harvested samples was performed using the RNA prep Pure Plant Kit (Tiangen, Beijing, China). The quantity and quality of RNA were assessed using a NanoDrop 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Wilmington, DE). A Hieff NGS Ultima Dual-mode mRNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (Yeasen Biotechnology (Shanghai) Co., Ltd.) was utilized to generate sequencing libraries and to add index sequences, which allowed sequences to be attributed to specific samples. Library quality was evaluated using an Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system. Raw data in fastq format underwent processing to produce clean data that was mapped to the reference genome of radish (Raphanus sativus) (https://www.nodai-genome-d.org/download.html) using Hisat2 software. StringTie software was used to generate transcripts, identified in the Hisat2 alignment results. The quantification of transcript expression levels was conducted using fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments mapped (FPKM). Functional annotation of transcripts was carried out utilizing The National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) nonredundant (NR) protein database, (ftp://ftp.ncbi.nih.gov/blast/db/ FASTA/) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG, https://www. genome.jp/kegg/). 

# Metabolite extraction, quantification, and annotation

Each sample $\mathrm { 5 0 ~ m g ) }$ was combined with $1 0 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ extract (acetonitrile: methanol: water $= 2 { : } 2 { : } 1$ ) and the internal standard (L-2- chlorophenylalanine, $2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } ~ \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ ), and then immersed in an ultrasound cold bath at 35 Hz for 5 min. After centrifuging the mixture for 1 h at 40 C, $2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the supernatant was removed and dried. The dried material was added to $3 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ 50 % acetonitrile and incubated for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Then $1 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sample was injected into 

a Waters Acquity I-Class PLUS UPLC equipped with a Waters Xevo G2-XS QT mass spectrometer, employing a Waters Acquity UPLC HSS T3 column ( $1 . 8 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , $2 . 1 \times 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ ), controlled by MassLynx V4.2 software. Raw data underwent processing using Progenesis QI software for peak extraction, alignment, and additional analyses. Parent ion mass deviation was 100 ppm, while fragment ions remained within 50 ppm. Identification of metabolites and theoretical fragments relied on a self-built database (structured utilizing Progenesis QI software online METLIN database and Biomark’s self-constructed library). The identified compounds were classified and assigned to different pathways utilizing KEGG databases. 

# Identification and analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and differentially abundant metabolites (DAMs)

DESeq2 was used to identify differential gene expression using P-values that were adjusted by the Benjamini-Hochberg method. Genes demonstrating an adjusted $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 1$ and a Fold Change $\geq 2$ were designated as exhibiting differential expression. The KOBAS database and clusterProfiler software were used to identify DEGs that were enriched in KEGG pathways [25,26]. 

A Student’s t-test was employed to determine significant differences in P-values for each metabolite, executed Orthogonal Projections to Latent Structures-Discriminant Analysis (OPLS-DA) modeling, and computed the Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) value of the model. Metabolites with adjusted $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5$ , Fold Change $\geq 1$ , and $\mathrm { V I P } > 1$ were regarded as being differentially abundant. The DAMs for KEGG pathway enrichment significance were assessed using the hypergeometric distribution test. 

# Reverse transcription  quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR)

The relative expression of select genes was determined by RTqPCR to validate the reliability of expression levels (FPKM) of gene expression obtained in the transcriptome data. Ten DEGs were selected for RT-qPCR analysis as described in Zhang et al. [15]. The selected genes and their corresponding primers are listed in Supplementary Table S1. The radish root gene TEF2 was used as a reference gene for normalization of the RT-qPCR data. Relative expression of the selected genes was determined using the $2 ^ { - \Delta C T }$ method. Each of the analyzed samples comprised three biological and three technical replicates. 

# Statistical analyses

Data was subjected to a statistical analysis using SPSS 26 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA). Data obtained for the different experimental variables was subjected to a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), and a significant difference between treatment group means was determined using a Student’s t-test at $\mathrm { ~ P ~ } < 0 . 0 5$ . Data are reported as the mean ± standard deviation (SD).

# Comparison of quality indicators

Percentage fresh weight loss in radishes increased during storage in both packaging materials, with the percentage loss in OPP packaged radishes being significantly higher $( \mathbb { P } \ < \ 0 . 0 5 )$ than radishes in the PVC packaging (Fig. 1H). On day 4, percentage weight loss in OPP packaged radishes was $2 . 8 7 \%$ , which was nearly double the $1 . 6 2 \%$ weight loss of radishes in PVC packaging. Firmness of radishes decreased in both packaging materials, with PVC packaging maintaining a higher radish firmness than OPP packaging (Fig. 1I). No differences in TSS were observed in radishes during storage between the two packaging materials (Fig. 1J). Regarding changes in pH, the pH of PVC packaged radishes generally increased, while the pH of OPP packaged radishes remained essentially stable and was significantly lower on days 2 and 4 than the pH of radishes in the PVC packaging materials (Fig. 1K). OPP packaged radishes had a pH of 6.18 on day 4 of storage, which was $2 . 3 9 \%$ lower than the $\mathsf { p H } 6 . 3 3$ of PVC packaged radishes. 

Radishes in both sample groups (PVC and OPP) exhibited an initial rise in respiration rate followed by a decline (Fig. 1L). The respiration rate in the OPP sample group, however, was considerably lower $( \mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5 )$ ) compared to the PVC sample group throughout storage. The highest respiratory rates were observed on day 1, reaching $4 3 . 6 2 ~ \mathrm { { \ m g } ~ \ k g ^ { - 1 } ~ \ h ^ { - 1 } }$ in the PVC sample group and $3 2 . 8 2 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { k g } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 }$ in the OPP sample group. Ethylene generation rates were also considerably lower $\left( \mathbb { P } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ in the OPP sample group than in the PVC sample group (Fig. 1M). Radishes in the PVC sample group reached a maximum ethylene generation rate of $0 . 4 8 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { L } ~ \ k g ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathsf { h } ^ { - 1 }$ at 2 days, while radishes in the OPP sample group reached a maximum of $0 . 3 7 7 \mu \mathrm { L } \ k g ^ { - 1 } \ h ^ { - 1 }$ at 1 day. 

Although there was a loss of firmness and moisture content compared to PVC, the radishes in the OPP packaging had lower PH value, rates of respiration and ethylene generation than radishes in the PVC packaging. 

# $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ and $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ content, CAT, and SOD activity

The levels of $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ initially rose over the first three days of storage in radishes in both packaging materials, followed by a decrease (Fig. 1N and O). The levels of these oxidative compounds, however, exhibited a statistically significant decrease $\left( \mathsf { P } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ in the OPP sample group from day 2 through day 4 of storage, being $3 1 . 8 6 \%$ and $2 3 . 7 1 \ \%$ lower on day 3 than values in the PVC sample group. 

CAT and SOD activity in the radishes peel initially increased during storage followed by a subsequent drop in both sample groups (Fig. 1P and Q). Nevertheless, the activity of these enzymes showed a noteworthy increase $\mathrm { ( P } < 0 . 0 5 \mathrm { ) }$ in the OPP sample group compared to the PVC sample group from days 2 to 4 of storage, with the largest difference in CAT and SOD activity between the two sample groups occurring on day 2 (2.05 times) and day 3 (1.51 times), respectively. 

# Transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses

# Transcriptomic analyses

After sequencing, alignment, annotation, and determination of FPKM values, the number of DEGs in the following comparisons were determined: Initial-0d vs. PVC-3d, Initial-0d vs. OPP-3d, and 

OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d (Fig. S2A). The analysis identified 4649 DEGs (2228 upregulated, 2421 downregulated) in the Initial-0d vs. PVC-3d comparison, 5019 DEGs (2444 upregulated, 2575 downregulated) in the Initial-0d vs. OPP-3d comparison, and 1615 DEGs (902 upregulated, 731 downregulated) in the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison. A shared set of 344 DEGs was identified across all comparison groups. DEGs that enriched in different metabolic pathways were functionally annotated and classified using KEGG pathway enrichment analysis (Fig. S2B). A KEGG enrichment analysis bubble plot (Fig. S2C) illustrates the top 20 pathways identified with enriched DEGs in the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison. The pathways with enriched DEGs were associated with the biosynthesis of phenylpropanoid, cutin, suberin and wax, and the metabolism of linoleic acid and alpha-linolenic acid. 

# Metabolomic analyses

Functional annotations of metabolites were determined using the KEGG database, focusing on the 20 metabolites with the most annotations listed in the KO pathway level 2 (Fig. S2E). Results of the untargeted metabolomic analysis detected 3,219 metabolites (1,929 in the POS ion mode and 1,290 in the NEG ion mode). Additionally, the analysis identified 1,299 DAMs (639 increased, 660 decreased) in the Initial-0d vs. PVC-3d comparison, 1,080 DAMs (595 increased, 485 decreased) in the Initial-0d vs. OPP-3d comparison, and 1,007 DAMs (386 increased, 621 decreased) in the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison (Fig. S2D and Supplementary Table S2). The top 20 pathways with the most significant DAMs in the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison were illustrated in a KEGG enrichment analysis bubble plot (Fig. S2F). Numerous DAMs in the comparison were associated with phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, as well as arachidonic acid, alpha-linolenic acid, and linoleic acid metabolism. 

# Combined transcriptome and metabolome analysis

Integrating metabolomic and transcriptome data can help address the limitations associated with the use of just one of these approaches and provide information on transcriptional regulation in specific metabolic pathways and their relation to metabolite levels. DEGs and DAMs from the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison were subjected to hierarchical clustering (Fig. S2G), demonstrating a high level of within sample group reproducibility and clear differences between sample groups. Co-enrichment of the top 30 KEGG pathways identified in an integrated-omic analysis of the OPP-3d vs. PVC-3d comparison are visually presented in Fig. S2H. Pathways related to phenolics and lipid metabolism were identified in the transcriptomic, metabolomic, and joint analyses, suggesting that they likely contributed significantly to the observed changes in appearance of radishes in the two different sample groups (PVC and OPP). DEGs and DAMs that were both enriched with the same pertinent KEGG pathways were subjected to further analysis to gain further insight into the specific mechanisms responsible for postharvest changes in the incidence and development of ‘black spot’, as well as the overall quality of radishes in PVC vs. OPP packaging materials.

PPO activity in radish peel tissue increased in both sample groups (PVC and OPP) during storage (Fig. 2A-c), while POD activity gradually increased after 1 day of storage (Fig. 2A-d). # Transcriptomic analysis of lipid metabolism

A total of 30 DEGs enriched into KEGG pathways associated with lipid metabolism included arachidonic acid, linoleic acid, alpha-linolenic acid metabolism, cutin, suberin and wax biosynthesis (Fig. 3C and 4A). # Metabolomic analysis of lipid metabolism

A total of 25 DAMs enriched in lipid metabolism-related pathways were identified, with 13 linked to arachidonic acid metabolism, 5 to linoleic acid metabolism, 5 to alpha-linolenic acid metabolism, and 2 to cutin, suberin, and wax biosynthesis (Fig. 3D and 4A). # Discussion

The postharvest physiological disorder known as ’black spot’ on radishes is primarily observed during storage in summer months, and negatively impacts the appearance and overall quality of radish. The disorder described in the current study is analogous to the physiological disorder reported by and designated as ‘black spot’ by Chandra et al. [10]. As far as we know, this previous report and our current research have been the only studies thus far to document this postharvest physiological disorder in stored radishes. However, the cause underlying the development of ‘black spot’ remains unclear. The findings of the present study indicated that OPP packaging inhibited the occurrence and development of ’black spot’ and helped to maintain the whiteness of radishes during storage and maintain a stable pH in peel tissues. OPP packaging also decreased the rate of respiration and ethylene generation, preserved the appearance quality, and delayed the senescence of radish, relative to PVC packaging (Fig. 1). Although a greater loss in firmness and fresh weight was observed in OPP packaged radishes, compared to PVC packaged radishes, OPP packaging better maintained the overall quality of radishes. The combined metabolomic and transcriptomic analyses conducted in our study suggest that ‘black spot’ in radishes apparently due to changes in the level of phenolic browning, lipid metabolism, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation and scavenging. These processes are adversely and advantageously modulated by the PVC and OPP packaging films, respectively. 

The formation of ‘black spot’ in radish roots is related to the oxidation of phenolic compounds 

Enzymatic browning, a primary contributor to plant tissue browning, involves the oxidation and lignification of phenolic compounds, and the regulation of CCR, POD, and PPO activity, which has been demonstrated in lettuce [27], lily bulbs [28], and lotus roots [29]. However, no direct correlation between the oxidation of phenolic compounds and ‘black spot’ development in radishes has been reported. CCR functions in lignin formation by converting 4CL thioesters into hydroxycinnamaldehydes [30]. POD polymerizes cinnamyl alcohols into lignin, participating in lignin formation and phenolic oxidation process [31]. PPO facilitates the transformation of phenolics into quinones, which polymerize with amino acids and proteins forming black compounds [32]. Notably, PPO and POD collaborate in the oxidation of polyphenols, with POD enhancing PPO-mediated browning reactions [33]. In our study, PVC packaging, relative to OPP packaging, resulted in the upregulation of RsCCR and RsPOD, along with a higher level of POD and PPO activity in radishes during storage. A previous study reported a positive correlation between the level of chlorogenic acid derivatives that serve as PPO substrates and the degree of browning in lettuce, whereas sinapaldehyde derivatives have a negative correlation [34]. Our results indicate that the PVC sample group exhibited a larger concentration of caffeoylquinic acid and a diminished level of sinapoyl alcohol compared to the OPP sample group, resulting in a higher incidence of ‘black spot’ in radishes, which confirms our preliminary findings. These present findings highlight the close relationship between the type of phenolic acids present and their oxidation with the development of ‘black spot’ in stored radishes. 

Phenolic substances, comprising phenolic acids and flavonoids, have potent antioxidant properties, effectively scavenging free radicals and mitigating oxidative stress in vegetables [35,36]. Flavones and isoflavonoids contribute to the maintenance of cell membrane integrity and ameliorate lipid peroxidation [37]. Therefore, enhanced biosynthesis of these compounds is a crucial aspect of plant defense response, with enzymes. such as PAL, 4CL, COMT, CCoAOMT, F5H, DFR, ANS, playing a functional role [30,31,38,39]. For example, flavonoid biosynthesis is positively regulated by the upregulation of DFR and ANS in response to pathogen invasion [40]. Additionally, the resistance of citrus plants is promoted by phenylpropanoid biosynthesis and high levels of total phenols [36]. The treatment of cherry tomatoes with hot air has been reported to induce disease resistance by elevating phenolics and flavonoids levels as evidenced by corresponding gene expression [41]. In our study, the DEGs RsPAL, Rs4CL, RsHCT, RsCSE, RsCOMT, RsCCoAOMT, RsF5H, RsDFR, RsANS, and RsI20 H were found to be more highly expressed in the OPP sample group than the PVC sample group (Fig. 2B). Correspondingly, levels of total phenols and phenolic acids (including gallic acid, caffeic acid, phydroxybenzoic acid, coumarin, ferulic acid, and sinapic acid), flavonoid and related compounds (including quercetin, tricetin, kaempferol and $( + ) .$ -catechin) were also higher in the OPP sample group (Fig. 2C and Supplemental Table S2). However, metabolites associated with isoflavone biosynthesis were lower in the OPP sample group (Fig. 2C), which may be attributed to the interaction between genes regulating the isoflavone and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathways that impact transcript levels. Consequently, the enhanced expression of genes that are involved in phenylpropanoid, flavonoid, and isoflavone biosynthesis, and the increased levels of phenolics and flavonoid, may have enhanced stress resistance in radishes stored in OPP packaging and inhibited the onset and development of ‘black spot’. 

# The onset of ‘black spot’ in stored radish is modulated by ROS production and scavenging

ROS, especially $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , can be excessively generated as a reaction to abiotic and biotic stresses, resulting in oxidative injury to DNA, RNA, proteins, and lipids in plants [42]. The disruption of ROS homeostasis during vegetable storage induces lipid peroxidation and cell membrane damage, rapidly degrading produce quality [43]. Thus, a significant association exists between the generation and elimination of ROS and the postharvest quality of vegetables in storage. MDA and electrolyte leakage levels are strong indicators of lipid peroxidation and the structural integrity of cell membranes [11]. In the present study, significantly lower levels of $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , MDA, and relative electrical conductivity (electrolyte leakage) were observed in radishes in OPP packaging compared to radishes in PVC packaging (Fig. 1I, 1J, 3A, and 3B). These findings indicate that OPP packaging reduces ROS production in 

peel tissues of stored radishes and limits cell membrane damage better than PVC packaging, resulting in a delay in the onset of ‘black spot’ and the inhibition of its further development. Our results were similar to research conducted on fresh-cut lotus root in which the application of melatonin significantly reduced $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , and MDA levels and increased its resistance to browning [44]. 

Phospholipase A2 (PLA2) catalyzes the hydrolysis of phospholipids liberating arachidonic acid (AA), which then serves as a precursor to pro-inflammatory substances, such as prostaglandins (PGs), leukotrienes (LTs), and oxoicosatetraenoic acid (OxoETE) [45]. This process generates large amounts of ROS which can result in oxidative damage to cells [45]. Consequently, PLA2 serves as a limiting factor in the synthesis of AA, pro-inflammatory substances, and free radicals [46], a process that is activated by $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ but inhibited by flavonoids [45,46]. Rapid ROS generation due to AA metabolism has been reported in potato and pepper [47,48]. In the present study, RsPLA2G expression, and the concentration of AA, $\mathsf { P G G } _ { }$ , and $\mathrm { { L T } } \mathsf { A } _ { 4 }$ were lower in radishes in OPP packaging than radishes in PVC packaging (Fig. 3C and D). Excessive ROS accumulation in PVC-packaged radish resulted in the upregulation of RsPLA2G, which enhanced AA release leading to the enhanced synthesis of pro-inflammatory substances and increased levels of ROS generation. Collectively, these events caused high levels of lipid peroxidation. Conversely, higher flavonoids and lower ROS levels in radishes in OPP packaging appear to inhibit RsPLA2G expression, delaying the rapid onset and development of ‘black spot’. Collectively, our data indicate that co-regulation of RsPLA2G in AA metabolism by flavonoids and ROS may modulate ‘black spot’ formation in radish peel. 

Plants have evolved a complex antioxidant defense mechanism comprising enzymatic and non-enzymatic elements that mitigate oxidative stress by neutralizing ROS [49]. Antioxidant enzymes include SOD and CAT, as well as others. SOD facilitates the transformation of $0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ into $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $0 _ { 2 }$ , and CAT functions in breaking down $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ into $_ { \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } }$ and ${ { 0 } _ { 2 } }$ [50]. Non-enzymatic antioxidant compounds include phenolics, glutathione (GSH), ascorbic acid (AsA), and others [49]. Prior studies have indicated that the antioxidant defense system mitigates oxidative stress in radishes. For example, low-oxygen packaging increased AsA and GSH levels, as well as enhanced CAT activity, in radishes resulting in reduced ROS levels and the prevention of blue discoloration [51]. Radish quality was maintained in storage by salicylic acid, which boosted CAT and SOD activity [52]. In our study, radishes in OPP packaging exhibited higher levels of SOD and CAT activity compared to radishes in PVC packaging (Fig. 1K and J). The enhancement of antioxidant enzyme activity reduced ROS-induced cell membrane damage and may have contributed to the delay in the onset of ‘black spot’. 

Based on a comprehensive analysis of the antioxidant system, it appears that OPP packaging may delay the onset and development of ‘black spot’ in stored radishes by increasing ROS scavenging through its ability to enhance the synthesis of antioxidant compounds and antioxidant enzyme activity, as well as inhibiting AA metabolism by downregulating RsPLA2G expression through its enhancement of flavonoids and lower ROS levels. 

# The formation of ‘black spot’ in radish roots is modulated by lipid metabolism

The abiotic stress resistance of harvested fruits and vegetables is closely associated with the levels of linoleic acid and $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic acid, which enhance membrane fluidity and low-temperature tolerance. Previous studies have demonstrated the role of linoleic acid and $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -linolenic acid in enhancing disease resistance in apples, loquat, and citrus [53–55], as well as 

enhancing low-temperature tolerance in tomatoes and peaches [56,57]. Lipoxygenases (LOX) serve as defense signaling molecules during host-pathogen interactions. Enhanced LOX activity and corresponding gene expression have been reported to promote wound healing in fresh-cut potatoes [58] and enhance cold tolerance in peaches [57]. Lu et al. [59] reported that micro-perforated polyethylene film packaging improved the aroma of table grapes by maintaining the level of linoleic acid, boosting LOX activity, elevating VvLOX expression, and downregulating VvADH expression. In the present study, we identified a significant enrichment of DEGs and DAMs related to linolenic acid and alpha-linolenic acid metabolism (Fig. 3C and D). The expression of RsLOX2S increased with 

storage time in radishes in the OPP sample group, while RsADH1 expression decreased, and alpha-linolenic acid content was significantly higher, relative to the PVC sample group. This may have stabilized the cell membrane, contributing to the delayed onset of ‘black spot’ in radishes.

# Conclusions

’Black spot’ development in the peel of radishes in PVC packaging during storage is a common problem that can be effectively eliminated with the use of OPP packaging (Fig. 5). Results of the present study indicate that the formation of ‘black spot’ on the peel of radishes in storage is associated with changes in phenolic and lipid metabolism, as well as ROS generation and scavenging. Relative to PVC-packaged radishes, the pH of OPP-packaged radishes remained relatively stable throughout storage, and exhibited lower rates of respiration and ethylene production, while CAT and SOD activity increased, reducing ROS accumulation. Phenols and flavonoids content increased and the expression of their corresponding genes was up regulated. POD and PPO activity decreased, and RsCCR and RsPOD expression was down regulated. Additionally, the content of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic acid s rose, and the expression of cuticle synthesis genes was up regulated. Notably, co-regulation of RsPLA2G expression by flavonoids and ROS may modulate ‘black spot’ formation. Our data provide a theoretical basis for the impact of packaging on the development of ‘black spot’ and the overall quality of radishes during storage. However, additional studies are needed to further confirm the validity of the processes related to ‘black spot’ development in radishes and the impact of packag-

ing materials on the physiology and biochemistry of radishes, as well as the mechanisms by which packaging materials impact radish quality.
