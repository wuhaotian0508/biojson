# A B S T R A C T

Coenzyme Q (CoQ) is essential for cellular metabolism, however its biosynthesis and function in insects remain largely unexplored. This study investigated decaprenyl diphosphate synthases (DPPSs) in CoQ biosynthesis in the polyphagous leaf beetle Monolepta hieroglyphica, an agricultural pest that acquires CoQ from host plants. Using liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS), CoQ10 $( \mathrm { R T } = 8 . 0 6 \ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) was identified in soybean, and CoQ9 $( \mathrm { R T } = 7 . 1 7 ~ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) in maize. Notably, both CoQ9 and CoQ10 were detectable in adult M. hieroglyphica. Titration revealed significantly decreased JHIII but increased CoQ9 and CoQ10 levels in maize-fed versus soybean-fed beetles at day 11 $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ . Transcriptome analysis revealed that $7 9 . 7 9 ~ \%$ of differentiated expressed genes (DEGs) were upregulated and categorized into five functional groups via KEGG enrichment analysis. Notably, MhieDPPS1 expression in maize-fed insects was significantly higher than in soybean-fed insects $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ , while genes involved in juvenile hormone biosynthesis exhibited significantly reduced expression $( p$ $< 0 . 0 0 1 $ ). Phylogenetic and sequence analysis confirmed that the DPPS1 and DPPS2 of M. hieroglyphica form a distinct clade separate from vertebrate homologous. Kinetic assays demonstrated that recombine MhieDPPS1 catalyzed the synthesis of essential compounds, yielding (E)-GPP at concentrations of $2 8 . 4 2 \pm 0 . 9 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . RNA interference (RNAi) studies indicated that knockdown of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 resulted in a $2 9 . 4 9 \%$ and $3 4 . 0 6 ~ \%$ reduction in expression, respectively, accompanied by a significant increase in juvenile hormone III levels $( 2 7 . 1 3 ~ \%$ upregulation, $p < 0 . 0 5 $ and reductions in CoQ9 and CoQ10 (suppressed by $8 . 9 4 ~ \%$ and $6 . 6 4 ~ \%$ , respectively, $p < 0 . 0 5 )$ and has also been validated through supplementary experiments. Additionally, microbial community analyses revealed significant shifts in gut microbiota composition in transgenic Drosophila melanogaster and M. hieroglyphica, respectively. DPPS manipulation also altered metabolic pathways, lifespan, and gut microbial composition, including increased Wolbachia. Overall, this study elucidates the complex regulatory mechanisms governing isoprenoid metabolism in insects and provides valuable insights into the biosynthetic pathways of coenzymes, potentially informing future research on metabolic functions and evolutionary adaptations.

# 1. Introduction

Coenzyme Q (CoQ) is a lipid-soluble electron carrier essential for mitochondrial ATP synthesis and cellular energy metabolism (Wang and Hekimi, 2016; Crane et al., 1957; Ernster and Dallner, 1995). It plays a crucial role in electron transfer between respiratory complexes and acts as an antioxidant against reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Mohora et al., 1999). Recent studies highlight CoQ’s involvement in neurodegeneration, cardiovascular diseases, and ferroptosis (Renner et al., 2024). While it shows therapeutic promise in reducing oxidative stress in chronic illnesses (McRae, 2023; Lei and Liu, 2017), its lipid solubility limits oral bioavailability, leading to innovative delivery approaches (Uchiyama et al., 2019; Sheykhhasan et al., 2022). CoQ shuttles electrons between Complexes I/II and III in the mitochondrial electron transport chain, driving ATP production (Hirst, 2013). It serves as a potent antioxidant, protecting membranes from oxidative damage. CoQ is ubiquitous in bacteria, animals, and plants, influencing secondary metabolism and stress responses (Mahesh and Rekha, 2009; Choi et al., 2005; Raizner, 2019; Berger et al., 2022), with significant applications in agriculture, particularly in enhancing CoQ10 yields in rice (Takahashi et al., 2009, 2010). Ongoing research continues to uncover promising opportunities for CoQ in health, agriculture, and sustainability.

While CoQ’s role is well-established in model organisms, its functions in non-model insects remain less explored. CoQ is crucial for mitochondrial function, energy metabolism, oxidative stress management, and aging. It serves as an electron carrier in the mitochondrial electron transport chain and possesses antioxidant properties that neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS). Although extensive research on CoQ has been conducted in Drosophila melanogaster, studies in other insects remain limited (Liu et al., 2011; Cheng et al., 2011). Recent findings emphasize the genetic regulation of CoQ synthesis and its dual role in immunity, particularly its complex effects on pathogen resistance (Tasaki et al., 2024). This gap in research hinders the understanding of CoQ’s evolutionary significance and developmental implications across insect taxa, warranting exploration of its adaptive strategies and potential therapeutic applications for mitochondrial diseases. CoQ’s role within coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10) highlights its biochemical and physiological functions, extending beyond the electron transport chain to include immune regulation and effects on lifespan. Although CoQ10 supplementation improved immune responses in Drosophila, it paradoxically increased susceptibility to viruses (Cheng et al., 2011). Tissue-specific and sex-dependent distribution in fruit flies further complicates our understanding of CoQ10 effects (Cirilli et al., 2024). In honeybees and termites, higher CoQ levels correlate with increased lifespan and resilience to age-related decline (Strachecka et al., 2014). Challenges in tissue-specific uptake emphasize the need for optimized supplementation strategies (Strachecka et al., 2014). Moreover, an evolutionary perspective suggests that CoQ plays an adaptive role in social insects, influencing reproductive success and survival (Tasaki et al., 2024). Thus, CoQ’s impact extends from cellular mechanisms to broader systemic regulation, affecting organismal evolution and adaptation.

The biosynthesis of CoQ involves a conserved pathway shared with other essential isoprenoids. CoQ10 biosynthesis shares an initial pathway with other isoprenoids, utilizing dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) and isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) as substrates (Kawamukai, 2002). In mice and humans, decaprenyl diphosphate synthase (DPPS) enzymes (mSPS1/mDLP1 and hDPS1/hDLP1) generate the CoQ9 and CoQ10 precursors (Ogura and Koyama, 1998; Saiki et al., 2005). Bacterial DPPSs, such as those from Sinorhizobium meliloti, can also produce CoQ10 in Escherichia coli (Seo et al., 2006). Insect CoQ10 biosynthesis remains an active area of research, particularly in aphids Aphis gossypii and Megoura viciae. DPPSs are also crucial for synthesizing the prenyl side chain of CoQ10 through co-expression (Zhang and Li, 2013; Song and Li, 2022). Intriguingly, the polyphagous insect Monolepta hieroglyphica can acquire both CoQ9 and CoQ10 from its host plants (maize

and soybean). We hypothesize that M. hieroglyphica’s ability to synthesize or acquire different CoQ contributes to its growth, development, and adaptation to diverse host environments.

To test this hypothesis, we selected the significant maize pest Monolepta hieroglyphica as a model. Monolepta hieroglyphica (Motschulsky, 1858) is a major maize pest in Asia, impacting crop yields and food security (Ramzan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2014). Its primary target is the maize ear, disrupting pollination and grain development (Zheng et al., 2020). Effective pest management strategies require a thorough understanding of M. hieroglyphica’s growth, development, and the factors influencing them. While previous studies have characterized isoprenoid pathway enzymes (MhieFPPS1/2, MhieGGPPS, MhiePFT-β, MhiePF/GGT-α, MhieJHAMT) involved in juvenile hormone biosynthesis and membrane function (Song et al., 2024b, 2025), our research focuses on the CoQ biosynthesis pathway. We identified two decaprenyl diphosphate synthases (MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2) through transcriptomic analysis and molecular cloning. Surprisingly, our findings reveal opposing trends in juvenile hormone and CoQ biosynthesis.

We employed a multi-faceted experimental approach to characterize these key enzymes. Using molecular docking, in vitro enzyme assays, GC-MS, LC-MS, RT-qPCR, transgenic Drosophila melanogaster, and RNAi, we characterized MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2. We demonstrate that both are key enzymes in CoQ biosynthesis, with MhieDPPS1 additionally playing a role in juvenile hormone biosynthesis. These results suggest a link between CoQ biosynthesis and lifespan. To further investigate factors influencing insect growth, development, and lifespan, we conducted differential transcriptome and gut microbiome analyses, along with correlation analyses, on transgenic Drosophila and M. hieroglyphica RNAi lines. Our findings confirm the CoQ biosynthesis pathway in M. hieroglyphica and represent the first functional characterization of insect DPPSs in growth, development, and lifespan. This study reveals the functional plasticity of MhieDPPSs and provides a novel target for developing effective pest management strategies against M. hieroglyphica.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Leaf beetles

Wild-type M. hieroglyphica were collected from a field planted with a combination of soybean and maize in Gongzhuling City, Jilin Province, China. Laboratory colonies were maintained on fresh soybean or maize at the State Key Laboratory for Biology of Plant Diseases and Insect Pests (Institute of Plant Protection, Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences, Beijing) under controlled conditions: $2 5 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $7 5 \% \pm 5 \%$ RH, and a 14L:10D photoperiod in a RXZ-300B growth chamber (Ningbo, China) (Song et al., 2025a).

# 2.2. Metabolites extraction

Ten-gram samples of soybean, maize and adult M. hieroglyphica were collected, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and ground into a fine powder using a cold mortar and pestle. Each sample was then subjected to Soxhlet extraction in a 250 mL round-bottom flask. The extraction was performed with 14 mL of $n$ -hexane as the solvent under reflux at $9 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . After extraction, the mixture was partitioned by adding $2 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of water and mixing vigorously. The n-hexane layer (upper layer) was carefully separated. The solvent was removed by evaporation under reduced pressure (or allowed to evaporate spontaneously) in a small beaker (Zhang and Li, 2013). The resulting residue was reconstituted in 1 mL of ethanol for subsequent analysis by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS). This procedure ensured the efficient extraction and purification of target compounds prior to LC-MS analysis.

# 2.3. MhieDPPS1/2 gene cloning via cDNA synthesis and PCR

Total RNA was extracted from six adult beetles using Invitrogen TRIzol™ reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) and RNA integrity was verified by $1 \ \%$ agarose gel electrophoresis. $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n g } }$ of total RNA from each sample was used for first-strand cDNA synthesis employing the TransScript One-step gDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis SuperMix kit (Transgen Biotech, Nanjing, China). The resulting cDNA samples were immediately stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use.

Primers for amplifying MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 were designed based on previously published transcriptome data (Table S1) (Song et al., 2024a). PCR amplification was performed following established protocols (Song et al., 2024b). Briefly, the PCR cycling conditions included an initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s, $5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \ s ,$ and $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min. A final extension step at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min was also included. The amplified PCR products were ligated into the pEASY®-Blunt Zero Cloning Vector (Transgen Biotech, Nanjing, China) for sequencing.

# 2.4. Differential transcriptome analyses

We quantified the levels of JHIII, CoQ9, and CoQ10 in both the maize-fed (Treatment; T; MF) and soybean-fed (Control; C; SF) strains over an 11-day period, during which tissue samples. Total RNA $( 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } )$ was extracted from control and treatment groups on day 11th using Invitrogen TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Transcriptome data were generated via Illumina Novaseq 6000 sequencing (Allwegene Company, Beijing, China) generated transcriptome data. Clean reads were assembled into unigenes using Trinity (Grabherr et al., 2011). These unigenes were annotated through Blastx searches against the Nr, Nt, Swiss-Prot, GO, and KEGG databases (Camacho et al., 2009). Transcript abundance was quantified as FPKM (Fragments per kilobase million) values (Mortazavi et al., 2008). Differential gene expression analysis was conducted using the DEGseq R package (Robinson et al., 2010; Ma et al., 2015), employing a $p$ -value threshold and stringent criteria for significance: fold change ${ > } 1 . 0$ , $q$ -value ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ , and false discovery rate $\mathbf { ( F D R ) } \leq 0 . 0 5$ (Benjamini and Yekutieli, 2001; Li and Dewey, 2011). Differentially expressed unigenes were functionally annotated and classified using KEGG pathway analysis and hierarchical clustering.

# 2.5. RT-qPCR analysis of gene expression in beetles

Beetles collected from soybean and maize fields were subjected to RT-qPCR to evaluate the gene expression of MhieFPPS1, MhieFPPS2, MhieGGPPS, MhieDPPS1, MhieDPPS2, MhieJHAMT, and MhieJHEH. Using an ABI 7500 system and QuantiNova SYBR Green PCR Master Mix, reactions were conducted with specific cycling conditions. Large subunit ribosomal protein L15e served as the reference gene (Song et al., 2025). Relative expression levels were calculated using the 2-ΔΔCT method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2000). Tissue-specific expression analysis of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 across various adult beetle tissues revealed distinct expression patterns. Each sample included three biological replicates. Primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

# 2.6. Phylogenetic analysis of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2

Homologous DPPS sequences to MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 were obtained from GenBank (Table S2). These sequences, along with those from diverse taxa, were aligned with DNAMAN (Lynnon BioSoft) and subsequently analyzed using IQ-TREE to construct a maximumlikelihood phylogenetic tree (Trifinopoulos et al., 2016). Branch support was assessed using 1000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates. The resulting phylogeny was visualized and refined using the Interactive Tree of Life (iTOL) for enhanced presentation (Letunic and Bork, 2024). The inclusion of diverse taxa enabled a robust assessment of the phylogenetic

placement of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 within the broader DPPS family.

# 2.7. Recombinant protein expression

MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 were cloned into the pTac-His-MBP-PreScission vector (Beyotime, Beijing, China) using BamH I and Xho I, and transformed into BL21(DE3) cells (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China). Cultures (500 mL LB, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ ampicillin) were grown at $3 7 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , 200 rpm to OD600 0.6, then induced with $5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ IPTG and incubated at $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 150 rpm for $^ { 1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Cells were harvested by centrifugation (12,000 g, 30 min), lysed by sonication, and clarified (16,000 g). His-tagged proteins were purified using Ni Sepharose™ 6 Fast Flow (Cytiva, Sweden) with $5 0 \ \mathrm { \ m M }$ imidazole. Purified MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 were dialyzed and stored at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in 25 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 25 mM MgCl2, 1 mM DTT, $5 0 ~ \%$ glycerol (Song et al., 2025). Protein concentrations were determined using the Bradford assay by measuring absorbance at OD595 on a FlexStation 3 (Bradford, 1976).

# 2.8. Enzymatic activity assay

Recombinant MhieDPPS1 $( 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ was assayed in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixtures containing $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 5 mM $\mathrm { { \bf M g C l _ { 2 } } }$ , $5 0 \ \%$ glycerol, and varying substrate combinations: IPP/DMAPP $( 5 0 / 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , IPP/ $( E )$ -GPP $( 5 0 / 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , and IPP/(E)-FPP $( 5 0 / 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ . Reactions were incubated at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , then terminated on ice. Shrimp alkaline phosphatase (20 U, Takara, Beijing, China) was added and incubated for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to hydrolyze products (Song et al., 2024b). After extraction with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ n-hexane, products were analyzed by GC-MS (Shimadzu, GCMS-QP2020, HP-5 column). The GC oven temperature program as follows: $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (1 min), $4 ~ { ^ \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ to $2 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , then at $1 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C / m i n }$ to $2 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (10 min hold). Injector and ion source temperatures were set at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . EI (70 eV) mass spectra $( \mathrm { m / z } 3 5  – 6 5 0 )$ were acquired. $( E )$ -Geraniol, (E)-farnesol, and $( E )$ -geranylgeraniol were quantified by peak area ratios against external standards (Sigma-Aldrich, Oakville, Canada), with three replicates per condition (Song et al., 2024b).

# 2.9. Diphosphate assay and Michaelis-Menten kinetic analysis

MhieDPPS1 kinetics were analyzed using a modified Invitrogen E6645 96-well plate EnzChek Pyrophosphate Assay (Barja and Rodrí- guez-Concepci’on, 2020; Ducker et al., 2023). Reactions $( 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L } )$ contained $5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ MhieDPPS1, varying substrate concentrations [(E)-GPP (40 $\mu \mathrm { M } )$ , IPP $( 1 { - } 8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ; IPP $( 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , DMAPP $( 1 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathbf { M } )$ ; IPP $( 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , $( E )$ -GPP $( 1 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathbf { M } ) ]$ , $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 1 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $5 0 ~ \%$ glycerol, $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ MESG, 0.2 U purine nucleoside phosphorylase, and 0.006 U inorganic diphosphatase. Reactions were incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min, and absorbance at $3 6 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was measured using a FlexStation 3 plate reader. Pyrophosphate $( 1 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ served as a standard. Three biological replicates were performed. $\mathrm { K } _ { \mathrm { m } }$ and $\mathtt { V } _ { \mathtt { m a x } }$ values for IPP (1–80 $\mu \mathrm { M }$ range, $4 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ (E)-GPP), DMAPP, and (E)-GPP $( 1 { - } 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ range, 100 $\mu \mathrm { M }$ IPP) were determined by fitting Michaelis-Menten equations to data using GraphPad Prism 10.0 (Ducker et al., 2023).

# 2.10. Molecular docking

The MhieDPPS1 3D structure was modeled using trRosetta and the COACH server (Du et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2013) and validated with SAVES v6.0 (Colovos and Yeates, 1993). DMAPP and $( E )$ -GPP were prepared in Mol2 format using OpenBabel v3.1.1 (O’Boyle et al., 2011), then converted to PDBQT using AutoDockTools (Trott and Olson, 2010). AutoDock Vina within AutoDockTools v1.5.7, and the results were visualized using PyMOL. Ligand poses were ranked by binding affinity (kcal/mol), and the top-scoring interactions between MhieDPPS1 and ligands were analyzed using PyMOL and LigPlot+ (Aguilar et al., 2017; Laskowski and Swindells, 2011).

# 2.11. Co-expression of DPPS in E. coli and measurement of CoQ

MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 cDNAs were cloned into pUC119 (Xba I/ EcoR I) and pSTV28 (BamH I/Hind III or EcoR I/BamH I), respectively. Four E. coli DH5α groups were evaluated by LC-MS for CoQ9/CoQ10 production: (1) Wide-type E. coli., (2) pUC119-MhieDPPS1, (3) pSTV28- MhieDPPS2, and (4) co-expressing pUC119-MhieDPPS1 and pSTV28- MhieDPPS2. Co-expression strains were selected on LB medium containing $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ ampicillin and chloramphenicol. A prokaryotic expression protocol was adapted: 5 mL of co-expressing E. coli DH5α were inoculated into 400 mL screening medium, grown to an OD600 of 0.8, induced with $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG for $^ { 1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $2 5 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , harvested by centrifugation, and washed with $0 . 9 \%$ NaCl (w/v) (Zhang and Li, 2013). CoQ quantification was performed following the previously described method.

# 2.12. Synthesis of double-stranded RNA and RNAi

Double-stranded RNAs (dsRNAs) targeting MhieDPPS1, MhieDPPS2, and GFP (dsGFP) were synthesized using the T7 RiboMAX™ Express RNAi System (Promega) with primers listed in Table S1. RNA interference (RNAi) experiments were performed on female adult M. hieroglyphica at four time points (days 1, 4, 7, and 11) to evaluate the effects on MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 expression (RT-qPCR) and JH III, CoQ9, and CoQ10 levels (ELISA). Adult beetles received abdominal injections of dsGFP, dsMhieDPPS1, dsMhieDPPS2, or a dsMhieDPPS1+2 mixture $( 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { \ n g / \mu L } } )$ . After injection, beetles were fed a maize diet supplemented with the corresponding dsRNA (500 ng/diet) (Song et al., 2025). A non-injected control group assessed injection-related mortality. Each group comprised three biological replicates. Additionally, we further validated the relationship between JH III, CoQ9 and CoQ10 in leaf beetles through long-term in vitro feeding of ubiquinol.

JH III, CoQ9, and CoQ10 was performed using a commercial ELISA kit (Meimian). Twenty M. hieroglyphica per sample were homogenized in $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ sample solution, centrifuged (12,000 rpm, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 30 min), and 10 μL supernatant was incubated with $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ sample diluent and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ HRP-conjugate reagent $( 3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 60 min). After washing, $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ each of chromogen solutions A and B were added (37 ◦C, 15 min), followed by $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ stop solution. Absorbance was measured at $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ using a SpectraMax I3x (Molecular Devices). Seven biological replicates were used for each treatment, with standards run concurrently. A separate experiment using dsGFP and dsMhieDPPS1+2 injections was conducted to analyze differential gene expression and gut microbiome composition.

# 2.13. MhieDPPS1-2a-MhieDPPS2 in Drosophila transgenic model

The DPPS1-2a-DPPS2 construct was cloned into the pJFRC28- 10XUAS-IVS-GFP-p10 vector (Not I/Xba I sites) and injected into 200 Drosophila embryos (Qidong Fungene Biotechnology). Following injection, P0 adults were crossed with Bc/CyO flies. F1 progeny exhibiting the mini-white eye marker (orange eyes) and balanced with CyO were selected. Integration of UAS-DPPS1-2a-DPPS2 was confirmed by PCR (McGuire et al., 2004). Developmental stages analysis, differential gene expression, and gut microbiome studies were conducted on both wild-type and transgenic flies.

# 2.14. Correlation analysis between differential transcriptome and gut microbiota

Differential transcript expression analysis was conducted on wildtype (WT) and transgenic (TDPPS) Drosophila, as well as on dsGFP (negative control, NC) and dsMhieDPPS1+2 (RNAi treatment, RT) M. hieroglyphica, following previously established protocols. Gut microbiome analysis involved DNA extraction from samples using the CTAB/SDS method. Illumina sequencing libraries were prepared with

the NEB Next®Ultra™DNA Library Prep Kit (NEB, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, incorporating unique index codes (Jiang et al., 2013). Library quality was assessed using a Qubit® 2.0 Fluorometer (Thermo Scientific) and an Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system. Paired-end sequencing (250 bp/300 bp reads) was performed on an Illumina MiSeq platform (Maughan et al., 2012). Sequence analysis, employing MEGAN (http://ab.inf.uni-tuebingen.de/software/megan/) and KEGG (Huson et al., 2011), was conducted by Origingene Company (Shanghai, China). Finally, correlation analysis between significantly altered metabolic pathways and the gut microbiome was visualized using a Sankey plot generated via chiplot.online (https://chiplot.onli ne/sankey_plot.html).

# 2.15. Data analysis

Data analysis was performed using SPSS Statistics 17.0; all values are presented as mean $\pm$ standard error of the mean (SEM). RT-qPCR and RNAi data were analyzed using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s B multiple range test (GraphPad Prism 10.0, GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA) to identify significant differences between groups $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ .

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Host plant-dependent variations in CoQ profiles, JH titers and gene expression in M. hieroglyphica

LC-MS analysis revealed the presence of CoQ10 (Retention time $[ \mathrm { R T } ] = 8 . 0 6 \ \mathrm { m i n } )$ in soybean and CoQ9 $( \mathrm { R T } = 7 . 1 7 ~ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) in maize (Fig. 1A and B). Interestingly, both CoQ9 $( \mathrm { R T } = 7 . 1 6 ~ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) and CoQ10 $( \mathrm { R T } = 8 . 0 6 \ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) were also detected in adult M. hieroglyphica (Fig. 1C), along with an unidentified CoQ $( \mathrm { R T } = 6 . 1 3 \mathrm { m i n }$ ). Standards CoQ9 $\mathbf { \mathrm { R T } } =$ $7 . 2 6 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ ), CoQ10 $( \mathrm { R T } = 8 . 0 7 \ \mathrm { m i n } )$ ) and mass spectrometry data are presented in Fig. 1D–G, respectively. Titration experiments for JHIII, CoQ9, and CoQ10 revealed significant differences in concentrations between the two leaf beetle strains, which had been fed different host plants over an extended period. On the 11th day of feeding, JHIII levels in maize-fed leaf beetle strains were notably lower compared to those in soybean-fed strains $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ ), whereas the opposite pattern was observed for CoQ9 and CoQ10 $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ ) (Fig. 2A). Transcriptome sequencing identified $7 9 . 7 9 \ \%$ up-regulated and $2 0 . 2 1 \ \mathrm { ~ \% ~ }$ downregulated differentially expressed genes (DEGs). KEGG enrichment analysis categorized these DEGs into five functional groups: cellular processes, environmental information processing, genetic information processing, metabolism, and organismal systems (Fig. 2B). Key genes involved in CoQ (MhieGGPPS, MhieDPPS1, MhieDPPS2), juvenile hor mone biosynthesis (MhieFPPS1, MhieFPPS2, MhieJHAMT) and degradation (MhieJHEH) were validated via heatmap analysis and confirmed it with RT-qPCR. Primer specificity and amplification efficiency were confirmed $( \mathrm { R } ^ { 2 }$ values ranging from 0.9644 to 0.9996) (Fig. S1) (Song et al., 2025). Heatmap analysis revealed that MhieDPPS1 and MhieGGPPS expression was elevated in maize-fed insects, whereas the transcript levels of the remaining genes exhibited an inverse pattern (Fig. 2C). Moreover, qRT-PCR analysis revealed significantly higher MhieDPPS1 expression in maize-fed insects compared to soybean $( p <$ 0.05), while MhieGGPPS and MhieDPPS2 showed no significant differences $\left( p \right) \ > \ 0 . 0 5 )$ . # 3.2. Sequence and phylogenetic analysis of two DPPSs

Full-length cDNAs encoding M. hieroglyphica DPPS1 (GenBank acc. no. PQ537095) and DPPS2 (PQ537096) were sequenced. MhieDPPS1’s 1293 bp ORF encodes a 431-amino acid protein (48.16 kDa, pI 8.351), while MhieDPPS2’s 1188 bp ORF encodes a 396-amino acid protein (44.88 kDa, pI 6.995). Interestingly, MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 share only $9 0 . 1 9 \%$ and $6 4 . 4 3 \%$ amino acid identity, respectively. MhieDPPS1 analysis revealed six conserved motifs and three aspartate-rich (DDxxD) motifs (Fig. S2). Phylogenetic analysis (maximum likelihood) showed insect DPPS1 and DPPS2 proteins forming distinct, well-supported clades, separate from a vertebrate DPPS clade. MhieDPPS1 and Drosophila virilis DPPS1 clustered together $8 9 ~ \%$ bootstrap support),

while MhieDPPS2 and Harmonia axyridis DPPS2 showed $7 8 \%$ bootstrap support (Fig. S3). These results indicate that the full-length cDNAs of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 exhibit distinct molecular characteristics and phylogenetic relationships, clustering into separate, well-supported clade alongside their respective insect homologs.

# 3.4. Key binding sites analysis of MhieDPPS1

# 3.7. Effects of knockdown of MhieDPPS1/2 on the contents of CoQ9, CoQ10, JH III and survival rate in the female M. hieroglyphica

To investigate the in vivo functions of MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2, RNA interference (RNAi) experiments were conducted to assess their impact on gene expression, juvenile hormone III (JH III) titers, and CoQ9 and CoQ10 levels. Our study demonstrates that DPPS modulates developmental timing and metabolic pathways in both Drosophila and M. hieroglyphica, as evidenced by transcriptomic changes following genetic manipulation.

# 4. Discussion

Coenzyme Q (CoQ) plays crucial roles in cellular metabolism, antioxidant defense, signaling, and organelle function (Turunen et al., 2004). Studies in animal models, including honeybees, have demonstrated that CoQ10 influences intracellular $\mathrm { { \bar { M } g ^ { 2 + } } }$ and $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ concentrations and calcium channel integrity (Siemieniuk and Skrzydlewska, 2005; Strachecka et al., 2014), enhancing ATP production via the Krebs cycle (Strachecka et al., 2014). Building on this metabolic foundation, our study demonstrates that the polyphagous leaf beetle M. hieroglyphica acquires distinct CoQ isoforms—specifically CoQ9 and CoQ10—directly from its host plants: maize provides only CoQ9, while soybean provides exclusively CoQ10. Notably, the coenzyme diversity in the beetles surpasses that of their plant hosts, as both CoQ types were detected in the insects, suggesting a nutritional reliance on a mixed CoQ profile for optimal growth and development. Consistent with this, beetles feeding on different host plants exhibited CoQ compositions that mirrored their dietary sources. These dietary adaptations were accompanied by significant variations were observed in the levels of juvenile hormone III (JH III), CoQ9, and CoQ10 among beetle populations reared on different plants. To molecularly characterize this key enzyme, we cloned two decaprenyl diphosphate synthase (DPPS) subunits from M. hieroglyphica. Phylogenetic analysis and sequence alignment confirmed their high similarity to known insect homologs, and both proteins possess the characteristic six conserved motifs (I-VI) and three aspartate-rich (DDxxD) domains. Contrary to previous reports indicating that insect DPPS1 and DPPS2 collaboratively synthesize only geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP) (Zhang and Li, 2013), we found that recombinant MhieDPPS1 alone catalyzed the production of $( E )$ -GPP, (E)-FPP, and (E)-GGPP in vitro. These products are essential precursors for monoterpene, sesquiterpene, and diterpene biosynthesis, with FPP also serving as a substrate for juvenile hormone synthesis (Satta et al., 2022). Our functional analysis suggests that MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 act both independently and synergistically. While MhieDPPS2 supports conventional coenzyme synthesis, MhieDPPS1 exhibits broader isoprenoid biosynthesis activity, including the generation of juvenile hormone precursors. This expanded catalytic capability implies a multifunctional role for MhieDPPS1 in regulating both CoQ and hormone metabolism during insect development.

To understand the structural basis of MhieDPPS1’s catalytic promiscuity, we performed molecular modeling and docking studies. The active site contains a $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ binding site located between the second and third aspartate-rich motifs. Key residues Asp195, Asp196, Val197, Ile198, Asp199, Asp322, Asp323, Leu324, Leu325, and Asp326 form this active site, with DMAPP and (E)-GPP binding near Asp322 and Asp195, respectively. Our 3D model shows that the first aspartate-rich motif is spatially distant from the metal site, explaining its frequent omission in previous studies. However, this motif likely contributes to the diversity of isoprene side chains during CoQ biosynthesis.

Despite its independent in vitro activity, further investigation revealed that both MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 are essential for CoQ9 and CoQ10 biosynthesis in M. hieroglyphica; neither subunit exhibits catalytic activity alone. This mirrors findings in aphids, although the observed CoQ diversity suggests species-specific MhieDPPS functionality. Our results demonstrate that insect CoQ biosynthesis requires the co-expression of both DPPS subunits. This expression pattern guided key methodological choices, necessitating the co-expression of DPPS1/2 in transgenic Drosophila via linked constructs and the separate synthesis of dsRNAs for RNAi in leaf beetles, thereby underscoring the functional distinction between the subunits.

To spatially resolve their functional roles, we performed a tissuespecific expression analysis in M. hieroglyphica. Both MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 showed the highest expression in the female abdomen. Whereas pheromone synthesis in bark beetles (e.g., Ips pini, Dendroctonus jeffreyi) typically occurs in midgut cells, which lack dedicated pheromone glands (Gilg et al., 2005; Hall et al., 2002), MhieDPPS1 exhibited significantly higher expression in the female gonad. This suggests a multifunctional role for MhieDPPS1 in insect growth and development. The enrichment of MhieDPPS2 in the female intestine, distinct from the expression pattern of MhieDPPS1, underscores the functional independence between these two subunits. Similar expression patterns were observed for other isopentenyl transferases. Interestingly, contrasting expression profiles were found for MhieFPPS1 (highly expressed in the head) and MhieGGPPS (highly expressed in the male gland), highlighting functional diversification among these homologous genes.

To functionally validate these findings, RNAi-mediated knockdown

of MhieDPPS1+2 was performed, providing evidence for functional support for their roles in M. hieroglyphica lifespan. Depletion of both genes resulted in significantly reduced CoQ9 and CoQ10 levels, coupled with increased juvenile hormone III (JH III) titers. This indicates that MhieDPPSs are not only involved in CoQ biosynthesis, but also exert negative feedback regulation on JH III production. These RNAi results corroborate the in vitro enzyme activity assays and help explain the complementary biosynthesis of CoQ and juvenile hormone observed when leaf beetles feed on diverse plant diets. Ubiquinol supplementation experiments demonstrated an inhibitory effect on juvenile hormones following the increase in the levels of CoQ9 and CoQ10 in leaf beetles. Furthermore, gene expression analysis following MhieDPPS1 and MhieDPPS2 knockdown revealed a lack of compensatory expression, suggesting independent functions for these two genes. Our findings demonstrate that MhieDPPSs influence insect lifespan and participate in insect growth and development.

Expanding to a systemic level, we investigated the effects of DPPS overexpression in Drosophila and DPPS interference in M. hieroglyphica on host gene expression and gut microbiota. DPPS overexpression in Drosophila primarily altered metabolic pathways, including those associated with longevity regulation (e.g., aging pathway) and developmental processes, resulting in increased lifespan. In M. hieroglyphica, DPPS interference also impacted metabolic pathways and significantly altered gut microbiota composition. The microbiota displayed increased abundance and diversity, with Wolbachia as the dominant genus, represented by 30 identified species. Given Wolbachia’s known roles in female wasp parthenogenesis and enhanced Drosophila survival (Perlmutter et al., 2025), its abundance in M. hieroglyphica (which exhibits a high female proportion in the field) suggests a potential link to reproductive strategies. For example, parthenogenesis-inducing Wolbachia bacteria are reproductive parasites that enable infected female wasps to produce daughters asexually, thereby enhancing vertical transmission to subsequent generations (Huigens et al., 2000; Fricke and Lindsey, 2024). Horizontal transfer followed by vertical transmission to the new host’s offspring, has implications for Wolbachia-host co-evolution. Additionally, Wolbachia manipulates arthropod sex by converting potential male offspring into asexually reproducing females, a phenomenon particularly prevalent in haplodiploid species such as parasitoid wasps (Verhulst et al., 2023). Moreover, two unique proteins in parthenogenesis-inducing Wolbachia show structural similarities to key eukaryotic sex-determining factors, suggesting a novel mechanism of bacterial-host interaction that can manipulate sex ratios and promote infection spread (Fricke and Lindsey, 2023). Despite the potential of Wolbachia in reproductive manipulation, studying parthenogenesis induction remains challenging due to the complex sex determination systems of arthropods, warranting further exploration of Wolbachia’s role in host reproduction and evolutionary dynamics (Salunkhe et al., 2014). These studies align with the observation that parthenogenesis represents an adaptive mechanism in insects. Furthermore, our analysis revealed a strong correlation between gut microbiota and FPPS gene expression, emphasizing the role of isopentenyl transferases in insect growth and development. Finally, the involvement of gut microbiota in CoQ synthesis highlights the interplay between host and microbiota in mediating insect adaptation through both endogenous synthesis and exogenous acquisition of this crucial metabolite.

In a broader entomological context, CoQ is integral to regulating lifespan and developmental processes in insects, such as Drosophila melanogaster, honeybees (Apis mellifera), and termites (Reticulitermes speratus). Its crucial roles include acting as an antioxidant and facilitating mitochondrial function, thereby impacting aging and metabolic performance. Studies reveal that CoQ influences lifespan through various mechanisms, including insulin signaling modulation and mitochondrial health, illustrating a complex interplay between genetic and environmental factors. Notably, research highlights paradoxical effects, where CoQ deficiency may extend lifespan in some genetic contexts while impairing it through dietary shortages. Social insects exhibit

unique adaptations, with caste-specific CoQ levels linked to longevity. Understanding these intricacies promises to enhance insights into aging, ecological resilience, and potential therapeutic applications in mitochondrial-related disorders (Partridge, 2010; Palmer and Sackton, 2003; Liu et al., 2011; Strachecka et al., 2014; Tasaki et al., 2024; Grant et al., 2010).

In conclusion, this study elucidates the CoQ biosynthetic pathway in the leaf beetle, M. hieroglyphica, systematically analyzing its crucial role in insect growth, development, and lifespan. Correlation analysis revealed significant effects of key CoQ biosynthetic enzymes on both the insect and its gut microbiota. These findings provide a foundation for future research targeting DPPS localization, the development of growth regulators, and the manipulation of CoQ synthesis and acquisition at different developmental stages. This research holds significant theoretical and practical value for understanding CoQ biosynthesis, growth, development, and ecological regulation in M. hieroglyphica, offering novel strategies for developing environmentally friendly pest management technologies. The insights gained could inform the development of targeted interventions to control leaf beetle populations.
