# The importance of maltose in transitory starch breakdown

YAN LU & THOMAS D. SHARKEY

Department of Botany, University of Wisconsin, Madison, WI 53706, USA

# ABSTRACT

Starch content of leaves responds to environmental stresses in various ways. Understanding these environmental effects on starch metabolism has been difficult in the past because the pathways of transitory starch synthesis and degradation are not completely known. Over the past two years there has been a significant increase in our understanding of transitory starch breakdown. The discovery of a maltose transporter (MEX1) and the studies of a cytosolic disproportionating enzyme (D-enzyme, DPE2) confirmed that maltose is the predominant form of carbon exported from chloroplasts at night. Maltose increases in leaves when starch breakdown is induced during the day under photorespiratory conditions. Maltose metabolism is regulated by a circadian clock, day length and temperature. The expression of maltose-metabolizing genes shows a pronounced circadian rhythm indicating maltose metabolism is clock regulated. Indeed, the maltose level oscillates under continuous light. The transcript of a $\beta$ -amylase gene (BAM3) peaks during the day in long days and peaks at night in short days. This could provide a mechanism for adjusting starch breakdown rates to day length. Under cold-stress conditions, maltose increases and BAM3 expression is induced. We hypothesize that maltose metabolism is a bridge between transitory starch breakdown and the plants' adaptation to changes in environmental conditions.

Key-words: circadian rhythm; day length; maltose metabolism.

# INTRODUCTION

Transitory starch accumulation in photosynthetic tissue is ubiquitous among land plants (Ball & Morell 2003; Sharkey et al. 2004b). Normally, starch builds up through the day and is broken down at night providing (1) an overflow for carbon that is photosynthetically fixed in excess of the leaf's ability to make sucrose (and other exported compounds) and (2) a source of reduced carbon for the plant at night when photosynthesis cannot occur. In healthy, unstressed plants starch degradation is remarkably well coordinated with starch synthesis such that the rate of starch degradation is constant through the night and sufficient to consume approximately all of the starch accumulated the previous

day. The rate of carbon export from leaves at night can be the same as that during the day as a result of transitory starch breakdown (Hendrix & Grange 1991). The rate of export is linearly related to the starch content of leaves at the end of the day and linearly related to leaf respiration at night (Hendrix & Grange 1991).

Leaf starch content can respond to environmental changes in various ways. Starch is broken down almost linearly and plants have a faster rate of starch degradation in long days (short nights) than in short days (long nights) (Gibon et al. 2004; Lu, Gehan & Sharkey 2004, 2005). How plants measure the duration of the day and adjust their rate of starch degradation during the following night will be reviewed here. Endogenous rhythms in starch breakdown have been shown. It has been reported that the expression of starch-mobilizing genes are circadian regulated (Harmer et al. 2000; Smith et al. 2004; Lu et al. 2005). For example, the expression of a gene encoding $\beta$ -amylase (BAM3, At4g17090) is clock regulated and is sensitive to the changes in day length. Under continuous light, the maltose level oscillates with a period length close to $24\mathrm{h}$ (Lu et al. 2005). The physiological significance of this phenomenon is still under investigation.

Starch content of leaves can also respond to environmental stresses. For example, water stress can decrease the partitioning of carbon to starch (Vassey & Sharkey 1989), but it can also lead to accumulation of starch in older leaves of plants adapted to mild water stress (Ackerson 1981). Chilling can lead to an accumulation of starch (Venema et al. 1999), but the effects of an interaction between chilling and the time of day on starch accumulation has been reported (Strand et al. 1999). Freezing tolerance has recently been shown to be related to starch degradation (Yano et al. 2005).

Understanding these environmental effects on starch metabolism has been difficult in the past because the biochemical pathways of synthesis and degradation of transitory starch are not completely known. Over the past two years there has been a significant increase in our understanding of transitory starch breakdown. Enzymes are present that could allow phosphorolytic or hydrolytic starch degradation, and for many years phosphorolytic breakdown was favoured (Stitt, Bulpin & Rees 1978; Stitt & ap Rees 1980; Stitt & Heldt 1981a). One potential role of phosphorolytic starch degradation is to provide carbon for the plastid pentose phosphate pathway (Stitt & Heldt 1981a). Stitt & Heldt (1981a) showed that both pentose phosphate pathway and glycolysis contribute significantly

to spinach (Spinacia oleracea) chloroplast dark metabolism. The presence of maltose during starch degradation had been reported many times (Levi & Gibbs 1976; Herold et al. 1981; Kruger & ap Rees 1983; Neuhaus & Schulte 1996) and had been seen when protoplasts were treated with mannose, which reduces the availability of phosphate (Herold et al. 1981). Nevertheless, the critical role of maltose in starch metabolism has only recently been recognized.

Maltose has the ability to protect proteins, membranes and the photosynthetic electron transport chain at physiologically relevant concentrations under heat stress and freezing stress (Kaplan & Guy 2004). Increased amount of total leaf maltose in Arabidopsis thaliana was observed during severe temperature shock (Kaplan & Guy 2004). The expression of the BAM3 gene is induced under cold (Nielsen Deiting & Stitt 1997; Seki et al. 2001; Kaplan & Guy 2004) and heat stress (Dreier, Schnarrenberger & Borner 1995; Kaplan & Guy 2004). The importance of maltose and BAM3 under temperature stress will also be reviewed here.

# DISCOVERING THE IMPORTANCE OF MALTOSE

With the success of the triose phosphate/phosphate antiporter (TPT) in explaining carbon export from chloroplasts during the day, it was natural to assume that carbon export at night was similar. Maltose as a degradative product of starch was first reported by Levi & Gibbs (1976). Later, it was reported that the principal products of starch breakdown in the presence of phosphate were triose phosphate and 3-phosphoglycerate, and that starch was also converted to maltose and glucose in a phosphate-independent reaction (Heldt et al. 1977; Peavey, Steup & Gibbs 1977; Stitt & ap Rees 1980; Stitt & Heldt 1981b). However, Kruger & ap Rees (1983) reported that the absence of exogenous phosphate substantially reduced starch breakdown and maltose accumulation by isolated pea (Pisum sativum) chloroplasts. Nevertheless, it was concluded that the products of starch breakdown in spinach chloroplasts included triose phosphate, 3-phosphoglycerate, $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ , glucose and maltose (Stitt & Heldt 1981a). Phosphorolytic breakdown of starch was favoured (Stitt et al. 1978; Stitt & ap Rees 1980; Stitt & Heldt 1981a), although it was suggested that some carbon from starch degradation was exported as hexose or maltose (Stitt et al. 1985).

Interest from this lab in starch degradation started with the discovery of a mutant of Flaveria linearis lacking cytosolic fructose bisphosphatase (FBPase). Compared to wild-type plants, these plants partitioned more of their photosynthate into starch and less into sucrose during the day; these plants also did not export carbon from their leaves during the day (Sharkey et al. 1992). However, during the night the rate of starch mobilization in the leaves of the mutant plants was twice the rate in wild-type plants (Sharkey et al. 1992). This indicated that there was a different pathway for exporting carbon from chloroplasts that did not rely on cytosolic FBPase but only operated at night. The

presence of a distinct night-time carbon export pathway was confirmed using plants in which cytosolic FBPase activity was reduced by antisense technology (Zrenner et al. 1996). When the TPT was reduced by antisense, the same reduction in daytime carbon export and an increase in night-time export was observed (Heineke et al. 1994). These works all led to the conclusion that there was a pathway for starch conversion to sucrose that did not involve the TPT or cytosolic FBPase and that could operate at night but not during the day.

The question arose 'is the alternate night-time pathway induced only when the TPT export pathway is blocked or is it the normal night-time pathway?' By feeding deuterium-enriched water to leaves at night, Schleucher, Vanderveer & Sharkey (1998) were able to show that plants which had no genetic modifications also used the alternative pathway for carbon export at night. Deuterium was incorporated at C2, as expected because of interconversion of fructose 6-phosphate (F6P) and glucose 6-phosphate (G6P), but was not incorporated into locations indicative of triose phosphate metabolism. The conclusion of these studies was that there were two independent pathways for carbon export from chloroplasts, one involving triose phosphates that operated during the day and one involving hexoses (or higher order polymers) that normally operated at night. Surprisingly, plants can grow well relying exclusively on either pathway, although optimum growth requires both. If both pathways were blocked, plant growth was severely reduced (Hattenbach et al. 1997).

This conclusion fits with data on cytosolic FBPase. At night, cytosolic FBPase activity is severely reduced by the presence of fructose 2,6-bisphosphate (Herzog, Stitt & Heldt et al. 1984). These authors concluded that 'if the cytosolic FBPase is inactive in the dark, it is unclear how starch can be converted to sucrose by a route involving starch phosphorolysis followed by release of PGA or triose phosphate from the chloroplast, which are then converted to sucrose in the cytosol by a path analogous to that in the light'. Nevertheless, it would be another 20 years before the answer to that conundrum, an alternative pathway of carbon export at night, was widely accepted.

Sucrose-phosphate synthase can be in a less active form at night (Sicher & Kremer 1984; Huber & Huber 1996) but its activity is not zero (Hendrix & Huber 1986), while cytosolic FBPase activity does go to zero. Therefore, a pathway of carbon export that bypassed triose phosphate and cytosolic FBPase could deliver carbon that could be converted to sucrose so that carbon could be exported from leaves at night.

The nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) data proved that the carbon exported at night was never broken down to triose phosphates (Schleucher et al. 1998; Niewiadomski et al. 2005). But what was the primary export compound(s)? Plastids have a hexose phosphate transporter, but this is normally expressed only in heterotrophic tissues (Kammerer et al. 1998) and perhaps in crassulacean acid metabolism (CAM) tissues (Neuhaus & Schulte 1996). In addition to glucose, higher maltodextrins such as maltose

or malotriose might also be exported and provide an energetic advantage over export of glucose (Weise & Sharkey 2004). It was known that the chloroplast is permeable to maltose (but not higher maltodextrins) (Schafer, Heber & Heldt 1977; Rost, Frank & Beck 1996).

Silicon oil centrifugation techniques were used to separate chloroplasts from the incubation medium following starch degradation in the dark. By removing the chloroplasts from the incubation buffer, it was possible to measure what was exported, but not all of the products of starch breakdown. These experiments are difficult because of the need to isolate intact, starch-laden chloroplasts. Nevertheless, these experiments did provide the correct answer, as subsequently confirmed by genetic analysis (below). It was reported by different groups that maltose and glucose are the two major forms of carbon exported from chloroplasts during starch degradation (Servaites & Geiger 2002; Ritte & Raschke 2003; Weise, Weber & Sharkey 2004). Servaites & Geiger (2002), using a maltase assay, concluded that one-third of the carbon could be isomaltose ( $\alpha$ 1,6 linked glucose). Weise et al. (2004) used high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) plus a more specific maltose phosphorylase assay and concluded that no isomaltose nor maltotriose or higher maltodextrin was exported. Between two-thirds and four-fifths of the carbon exported at night was exported as maltose; most of the rest exported as glucose. Maltose was confirmed to be a major product of catabolism of starch in guard cells as well (Ritte & Raschke 2003).

The essential role of maltose was confirmed by finding the maltose transporter in the chloroplast membrane (Niittylä et al. 2004) and the first enzyme that metabolizes maltose in the cytosol, disproportionating enzyme (D-enzyme) 2, DPE2 (Chia et al. 2004; Lu & Sharkey 2004). Plants that

lack either of these proteins accumulate as much as 100 times the normal level of maltose.

# TRANSITORY STARCH BREAKDOWN IN THE CHLOROPLAST

Substantial progress has been made recently in understanding transitory starch breakdown. It is now generally believed that transitory starch is broken down to maltose and glucose in the chloroplast via debranching enzymes (DBEs), glucan water dikinase (GWD, At1g10760), phosphoglucan water dikinase (PWD or AtGWD3, At5g26570), $\beta$ -amylases and D-enzyme (DPE1, At5g64860) (Fig. 1) (Zeeman et al. 1998b; Lao et al. 1999; Yu et al. 2001; Ritte et al. 2002; Scheidig et al. 2002; Baunsgaard et al. 2005; Kotting et al. 2005). $\alpha$ -Amylase (also called endo-amylase) was previously considered to be involved in transitory starch degradation and the starch-excess phenotype in sex4 mutants of Arabidopsis was thought to be caused by the absence of an $\alpha$ -amylase (AtAMY3, At1g69830) (Zeeman et al. 1998a). However, it was recently reported that $\alpha$ -amylase is not required for transitory starch breakdown in Arabidopsis leaves and that the starch-excess phenotype in sex4 mutant is not caused by the deficiency of AtAMY3 protein (Yu et al. 2005). Unpublished data indicated that the $SEX4$ gene encoded a protein phosphatase (Smith, Zeeman & Smith 2005). Mutants of Arabidopsis thought to be involved in transitory starch breakdown are listed in Table 1. Mutants that are involved in transitory starch synthesis and carbohydrate transport are also included (Caspar, Huber & Somerville 1985; Lin et al. 1988a, b; Caspar et al. 1991; Kofler et al. 2000; Yu et al. 2000; Schneider et al. 2002; Niewiadomski et al. 2005; Zhang, Myers & James 2005).

# Recent new steps involving GWDs

Starch degradation has to be initiated by the release of soluble glucans from the semicrystalline starch granule (Lloyd, Kossman & Ritte 2005). Ritte et al. (2002) reasoned

that phosphate levels in starch granules may affect the hydrophilicity of the granule and/or the packing of the amylopectin and thus affect the degradation of starch at night. A follow-up study done by the same group showed that glucans released from starch granules isolated from dark-

ened leaves possesses considerably higher amounts of phosphate than those released from starch granules isolated from leaves in the light (Ritte et al. 2004). Studies in GWD (also known as R1 protein) antisense potato (Solanum tuberosum) plants and in GWD-deficient sex1 mutants of Arabidopsis demonstrated that GWD phosphorylates a small proportion of the glucosyl residues in starch granules and that reduction of GWD activity leads to decreased starch phosphate levels and causes a starch-excess phenotype in leaves (Lorberth et al. 1998; Yu et al. 2001; Ritte et al. 2002). It is concluded that GWD is essential in transitory starch degradation (Fig. 1) (Lorberth et al. 1998; Yu et al. 2001; Ritte et al. 2002). Incubation of purified GWD protein with various phosphate donors and acceptors demonstrated that GWD transfers the $\beta$ -phosphate of ATP to either the C6 or the C3 position of glucosyl residues in amylopectin (Ritte et al. 2002). It was recently discovered that potato GWD is redox regulated and is active in the reduced form (Mikkelsen et al. 2005). Mikkelsen et al. (2005) were able to show that GWD isolated from dark-adapted leaves existed in the inactive, oxidized form. Both reduced thioredoxin (Trx) $f$ and Trx $m$ from spinach leaves could activate oxidized GWD at very low concentrations (Mikkelsen et al. 2005). We speculate that GWD is involved in synthesizing phosphorylated starch granules that are properly degraded during the night (Fig. 1).

A novel protein (PWD or AtGWD3) that has weak homology to GWD was recently identified in Arabidopsis by two different groups (Baunsgaard et al. 2005; Kotting et al. 2005). Both groups discovered that PWD preferentially phosphorylates starch granules that have been prephosphorylated by GWD. By incubating recombinant PWD protein with radiolabelled ATP in the presence or absence of starch granules pre-phosphorylated by GWD, Kotting et al. (2005) found that the $\beta$ -phosphate of ATP is first transferred to a His residue of PWD by the autophosphorylation feature of PWD and is then transferred to the phosphoglucan. Both groups found that PWD primarily phosphorylates the C3 position of glucosyl residues and that repression in PWD leads to a starch-excess phenotype, indicating PWD is important during transitory starch degradation (Fig. 1) (Baunsgaard et al. 2005; Kotting et al. 2005).

Another GWD in Arabidopsis (AtGWD2, At4g24450) is presumably present in the cytosol, and AtGWD2-deficient Arabidopsis mutants have normal levels of leaf sugars and starch, suggesting that AtGWD2 is not essential for transitory starch breakdown (Glaring, Baunsgaard, Henrik, Mikkelsen & Blennow, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of American Society of Plant Biologists (ASPB) in Seattle, WA, USA). AtGWD2 is expressed in the phloem of all green tissues just before the onset of senescence in Arabidopsis, suggesting that AtGWD2 could possibly be involved in degrading starch in sieve elements.

# DBEs

DBEs, also called isoamylases (ISAs), catalyse the hydrolysis of $\alpha(1-6)$ linkages in starch. It is generally assumed that

DBEs play an important role in starch degradation. Because DBEs are also involved in starch synthesis, understanding which DBEs are involved in starch breakdown is complicated. DBEs may act together with branching enzymes to determine the degree of branching of amylopectin. Mutants in AtISA1 or AtISA2 have decreased starch content and increased maltodextrin (also called phyto glycogen) content; the starch granules and the maltodextrin isolated from ISA1- or ISA2-deficient mutants have normal degree of branching, suggesting that ISA1 and ISA2 are important in starch synthesis but may not be essential in starch degradation (Zeeman et al. 1998b; Delatte et al. 2005). Like Arabidopsis, potato also has three ISA isoforms and purification of ISA activity from potato tubers indicated that StISA1 and StISA2 associate with each other to form multimers (Hussain et al. 2003). Hussain et al. (2003) found that StISA3 does not associate with the enzyme complex of StISA1 and StISA2, suggesting that StISA3 may have a distinct role. Recombinant StISA3 protein shows relatively high in vitro activity on $\beta$ -limit dextrin, the remnants of amylopectin digested with $\beta$ -amylase (Hussain et al. 2003). This suggests that ISA3 can have a role in transitory starch breakdown (Fig. 1). It is attractive to speculate that AtISA3 (At4g09020) may act in concert with BAM3 to progressively degrade starch granules to maltose in Arabidopsis (Smith et al. 2005).

# Plastidial $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase

Plastidial $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase (Pho1) catalyses the phosphorolytic cleavage of the glucosyl residues from the non-reducing end of glucan chains, producing glucose 1-phosphate (G1P). In potato and pea, there are two isoforms of Pho1, Pho1a and Pho1b, in a homodimeric (Pho1a-Pho1a) and a heterodimeric (Pho1a-Pho1b) state (Steup & Latzko 1979; Sonnewald et al. 1995; Albrecht et al. 1998, 2001). Pho1 was considered to produce the precursors for triose-phosphate export from chloroplasts at night (Heldt et al. 1977; Peavey et al. 1977; Stitt et al. 1978; Stitt & ap Rees 1980). However, mutations in the gene encoding plastidial $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase (Pho1 or AtPHS1, At3g29320) does not cause a significant change in the total accumulation of starch during the day or its remobilization at night, indicating that Pho1 is not essential for starch degradation in Arabidopsis leaves (Zeeman et al. 2004). Because hexose-phosphate levels in the chloroplast were not measured, we cannot rule out the possibility that Pho1 is involved in providing carbon from starch breakdown for the plastid pentose phosphate pathway.

Based on above results, we shall reconsider the role of Pho1 in chloroplasts. G1P produced by Pho1 can be converted to G6P, an intermediate in pentose phosphate pathway, by plastidial phosphoglucomutase (PGM1). G6P can also be converted to F6P by plastidial phosphoglucoisomerase (PGI1) and then fed into other chloroplast metabolism. Under photorespiratory conditions, G6P and maltose levels in wild-type Arabidopsis plants increased as a result of elevated phosphorolytic and hydrolytic starch

breakdown, respectively (Weise & Sharkey, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA). In Atphs1-2 plants, the maltose level still increased during photorespiration while the level of G6P did not, indicating that AtPHS1 is involved in phosphorolytic starch degradation under artificial photorespiratory conditions (Fig. 1). Weise & Sharkey concluded that in at least spinach, bean (Phaseolus vulgaris) and Arabidopsis, phosphorolytic starch breakdown is used for internal chloroplast metabolism, while hydrolytic starch breakdown is used to export carbon from the chloroplast. However, disrupting phosphorolytic starch degradation has little effect on Arabidopsis, but disrupting maltose export or metabolism has a substantial effect, indicating the importance of maltose metabolism.

# $\beta$ -Amylases

$\beta$ -Amylases cleave maltosyl units only from the nonreducing end of $\alpha(1-4)$ glucan chains. In the Arabidopsis genome, there are nine genes encoding $\beta$ -amylase (Smith et al. 2004). Among the nine isoforms, BAM1 (At3g23920), BAM2 (At4g00490), BAM3 (or CT-BMY, At4g17090) and BAM4 (At5g55700) are probably plastidial (Lao et al. 1999; Smith et al. 2004). An Arabidopsis line lacking BAM1 has a normal level of leaf starch, indicating that BAM1 is not essential for transitory starch breakdown (Chen & Lue, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA). The major isoform of $\beta$ -amylase (BAM5 or RAM1, At4g15210) is located in the phloem (Lin, Spilatro & Preiss et al. 1988c; Monroe, Salminen & Preiss 1991; Wang et al. 1995). The leaves of ram1 plants accumulate wild-type levels of starch, soluble sugars and chlorophyll, suggesting that RAM1 is not required for transitory starch degradation (Laby, Kim & Gibson 2001).

It was reported by Lao et al. (1999) that the BAM3 gene (At4g17090) encodes a functional plastidial $\beta$ -amylase. There are two pieces of evidence supporting the hypothesis that BAM3 is required for transitory starch breakdown: (1) down-regulation of a chloroplast-targeted potato $\beta$ -amylase, whose sequence is very similar to BAM3, led to a starch-excess phenotype (Scheidig et al. 2002); and (2) BAM3 RNAi lines had a starch-excess phenotype and decreased maltose content under cold stress (Kaplan & Guy, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA).

# Plastidial D-enzyme

Plastidial D-enzyme preferentially transfers maltosyl units from one $1,4 - \alpha$ D-glucan to another. By incubating recombinant plastid-targeted D-enzyme from potato with various substrates, Takaha et al. (1993) found that short chain amylose and amylopectin could function as glucosyl donors, although maltooligosaccharides are more effective. The smallest glucosyl donor for this enzyme is maltotriose and the smallest glucosyl acceptor is glucose (Takahata et al. 1993; Takaha & Smith 1999). Critchley et al. (2001) reported that

Arabidopsis also possesses a functional plastid-targeted D-enzyme, DPE1 (At5g64860). DPE1-deficient plants have a starch-excess phenotype and they accumulate high amounts of maltodextrins, especially maltotriose (Critchley et al. 2001). This indicates that DPE1 is important in transitory starch degradation and that the smallest and preferred substrate for DPE1 is maltotriose, which is converted to glucose and maltopentaose by the action of DPE1. DPE1 proteins from Arabidopsis, rice (Oryza sativa) and potato are closely related to each other on phylogenetic trees of protein sequences, suggesting that DPE1 could have a ubiquitous role in transitory starch breakdown among higher plants (Fig. 1) (Lu & Sharkey 2004).

# MALTOSE EXPORT FROM CHLOROPLASTS

The importance of maltose export from chloroplasts is supported by the discovery of a novel maltose transporter (MEX1, At5g17520) on the chloroplast envelope (Niittyla et al. 2004). MEX1-deficient Arabidopsis plants show growth retardation and a starch-excess phenotype, and they accumulate a substantial amount of maltose, indicating that MEX1 is essential in transitory starch breakdown (Fig. 1) (Niittyla et al. 2004). It was reported that maltose exists as an $\alpha$ - or $\beta$ -anomer (Weise et al. 2005). Using a non-aqueous fractionation method, Weise et al. (2005) found that the concentration of $\beta$ -maltose is greater in chloroplasts than in the cytosol by $278\mu \mathrm{M}$ during transitory starch breakdown, suggesting that $\beta$ -maltose is the metabolically active anomer in transitory starch breakdown and that maltose is exported from the chloroplast passively (Weise et al. 2005). Niittyla et al. (2004) reported that mex1/dpe1 double mutants have a much more severe phenotype than either parent, indicating that maltose export and glucose export are two parallel routes for the export of starch degradation products from the chloroplasts at night.

# MALTOSE AND GLUCOSE METABOLISM IN THE CYTOSOL

After maltose and glucose are exported to the cytosol, enzymes must exist to convert them to the precursors for sucrose synthesis. It was proposed that maltose metabolism in the cytosol of the plant cell is similar to that in the cytoplasm of Escherichia coli (Fig.2) (Boos & Shuman 1998; Lu & Sharkey 2004). In the cytoplasm of $E.$ coli, maltose and maltodextrins of up to seven glucosyl residues are metabolized to G1P and G6P by the action of amylo-maltase (MalQ), maltodextrin phosphorylase (MalP) and glucokinase (Boos & Shuman 1998). It was reported by two different groups that cytosolic D-enzyme (DPE2) is involved in the conversion of maltose to sucrose (Chia et al. 2004; Lu & Sharkey 2004). Cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase (Pho2) was also suggested to be involved in maltose metabolism by its interaction with a heteroglycan in the cytosol (Lu & Sharkey 2004). # Cytosolic D-enzyme - activity with maltose?

Cytosolic D-enzyme (DPE2) and cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase (Pho2) were found in the leaves of spinach, pea, Arabidopsis and potato (Fig.1) (Steup & Latzko 1979; Preiss, Okita & Greenberg 1980; Steup, Schachtele & Latzko 1980; Mori, Tanizawa & Fukui 1991; Duwenig et al. 1997; Chia et al. 2004; Lloyd et al. 2004; Lu & Shar

key 2004). These enzymes were considered to be starch-mobilizing enzymes and their presence in the cytosol was puzzling because there is no starch in the cytosol. Mutants of $E$ coli lacking MalQ accumulate maltose just like mutants of Arabidopsis lacking DPE2 (Szmelcman et al. 1976; Lu & Sharkey 2004). Phylogenetic trees based on the protein sequences showed that DPE2 in Arabidopsis and rice is more closely related to MalQ in $E$ coli than to DPE1 in plants (Lu & Sharkey 2004). Therefore, it was suggested that DPE2 is involved in maltose metabolism in the cytosol, analogous to the MalQ in $E$ coli (Fig. 2). We speculate that DPE2 acts on a highly branched heteroglycan in the cytosol and releases glucose from the reducing end, forms a heteroglycan-enzyme complex and transfers the heteroglycan to the non-reducing end of maltose. A similar mechanism in $E$ coli was proposed by Boos & Shuman (1998). Enzymatic activity of DPE2 with maltose was demonstrated by native glycogen gels (Chia et al. 2004). Compared with the migration rate of DPE1 on native glycogen gels, DPE2 has a relatively higher affinity towards oyster glycogen, a polysaccharide with a degree of branching similar to the heteroglycan (Chia et al. 2004). Protein domain prediction with Pfam indicated that DPE2 has a polysaccharide-binding domain located at the C-terminal of the protein sequence (Bateman et al. 2004). The highest activity of recombinant DPE2 protein was recently found with glycogen or heteroglycan together with maltose (Lu, Steichen, Yao & Sharkey, unpublished results).

# Nature of the cytosolic heteroglycan

In the cytosol of spinach, pea, Arabidopsis and potato leaves, there is a highly branched, water-soluble ethanol/ KCl-insoluble heteroglycan (SHG), which has a high affinity towards cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase (Pho2 or AtPHS2) (Yang & Steup 1990; Fettke et al. 2004, 2005a, b; Lu & Sharkey 2004). This heteroglycan mainly contains arabinose, galactose and glucose; glucose in the heteroglycan only accounts for $\approx 5\%$ of total monosaccharide content (Yang & Steup 1990; Fettke et al. 2004, 2005a; Lu & Sharkey 2004). It is plausible to speculate that the glucan residues in the heteroglycan, analogous to maltodextrin in the cytoplasm of $E.$ coli, are both the substrate/product of DPE2 and the substrate for Pho2 in the cytosol (Fig. 1). This heteroglycan may also act as an overflow for carbohydrates formed from starch degradation at night when the production of hexoses exceeds the demand for and transport of sucrose, although the amount of the heteroglycan is substantially lower compared to the amount of starch in the leaf (Lu & Sharkey 2004).

# Cytosolic 'starch' phosphorylase

The involvement of Pho2 in transitory starch degradation is not clear. Antisense inhibition of Pho2 in potato plants has little impact on carbohydrate metabolism and it was proposed that Pho2 is not involved in starch degradation

(Duwenig et al. 1997). Because the maltose level and the hexose phosphate level in the chloroplast and cytosolic compartments of Pho2 RNAi potato plants were not measured (Duwenig et al. 1997), we cannot rule out the possibility that Pho2 is involved in cytosolic glucan metabolism. Lu & Sharkey (2004) proposed that Pho2 phosphorylates glucan residues in heteroglycan and yields G1P, analogous to MalP in $E.$ coli (Fig. 2). The interaction of Pho2 with the cytosolic heteroglycan (SHG) has been reported in spinach, pea and Arabidopsis (Yang & Steup 1990; Fettke et al. 2004, 2005a). In the presence of orthophosphate $(\mathrm{P_i})$ , SHG is used by Pho2 as a glucosyl donor, in the presence of G1P, SHG is used by Pho2 as a glucosyl acceptor (Fettke et al. 2004). This suggests that Pho2 catalyses a bidirectional reaction and that the ratio of $\mathrm{P_i / G1P}$ in the cytosol determines the direction of the reaction. In the dark, cytosolic $\mathrm{P_i}$ concentration is about $700\mathrm{nmol~mg^{-1}}$ Chl in the leaves of bean plants (Sharkey & Vanderveer 1989). During the day, cytosolic G6P concentration is $54\mathrm{nmol~mg^{-1}}$ Chl in the leaves of potato plants (Sharkey & Vassey 1989). Because the G1P level is about one-fifth or less of the G6P level in the cytosol and night-time G1P level is not significantly different from daytime G1P level (Lu et al. 2005), we estimate that there is much more $\mathrm{P_i}$ than G1P in the cytosol at night. This suggests that in vivo, Pho2 converts glucosyl residues in heteroglycan to G1P. A Pho2-deficient mutant of Arabidopsis had relatively higher amount of maltose at night, indicating that Pho2 is indeed involved in the cytosolic metabolism of maltose (Lu, Steichen, Yao & Sharkey, unpublished results).

# Cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucosidase

$\alpha$ -Glucosidase hydrolyses maltose and maltodextrin to glucose. The occurrence and subcellular localization of $\alpha$ -glucosidase in the literature is conflicting. It was reported earlier that $\alpha$ -glucosidase activity was not detected in the leaves of pea and spinach (Okita et al. 1979; Kruger & ap Rees 1983; Kakefuda, Duke & Hostak 1986). Years later, two apoplastic and one chloroplastic isoform were identified in pea seedlings, and the authors suggested inappropriate extraction and assays conditions might have caused non-detectable activity in their earlier studies (Beers, Duke & Henson 1990). In the Arabidopsis genome, there are five genes encoding $\alpha$ -glucosidase-like proteins (Smith et al. 2004). Among the five isozymes, At3g23640 is predicted to be cytosolic. If At3g23640 turns out to be a functional enzyme in the cytosol, the substantial increase in the maltose content in DPE2-deficient mutants indicates that the cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucosidase does not act on maltose. However, we cannot rule out the possibility that the cytosolic $\alpha$ -glucosidase acts on the heteroglycan to yield glucose. Glucose produced by $\alpha$ -glucosidase (or heteroglycan glucosidase 1, HGL1) can be converted to G6P by hexokinase (Fig. 1). Compared with the reaction catalysed by heteroglycan glucosidase, the reaction catalysed by $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase is preferred because hexokinase uses ATP to convert glucose to G6P, while $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase uses free phos

phate to make G1P (Fig. 1). The binding of $\alpha$ -glucan phosphorylase to the SHG may help ensure that the energetically more efficient phosphorolytic path normally predominates.

# STARCH DEGRADATION IN THE PHLOEM

Starch-mobilizing enzymes, such as AtGWD2 (Glaring, Baunsgaard, Henrik, Mikkelsen & Blennow, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA) and RAM1 (Lin et al. 1988c; Monroe et al. 1991; Wang et al. 1995), were recently discovered to be present in the phloem of higher plants. Their presence in the phloem has been puzzling because starch was not thought to be present in the sieve elements. Starch granules are actually widely distributed in the plastids of higher-plant sieve elements (Palevitz & Newcomb 1970; Behnke 2002; Wu & Zheng 2003). Phloem starch is a highly branched molecule with numerous $\alpha(1-6)$ linkages in young sieve elements in bean (Palevitz & Newcomb 1970). It was recently reported by Wu & Zheng (2003) that starch-containing plastids are present during the development of sieve elements in Arabidopsis and are broken down with starch granules left at the end of sieve element differentiation. Starch-like granules are separately sheathed with a layer of membrane in the cytoplasm of mature sieve elements (Wu & Zheng 2003). These starch granules were in proximity to mitochondria and may provide substrates to the mitochondria (Wu & Zheng 2003). The presence of AtGWD2 and RAM1 in the sieve elements is not a coincidence, especially when Arabidopsis stems start to lignify. It is possible that these starch-mobilizing enzymes are recruited to degrade phloem starch in an age-dependent manner (Glaring, Baunsgaard, Henrik, Mikkelsen & Blennow, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA).

# REGULATION OF MALTOSE METABOLISM

# Coordination of maltose metabolism with day length

Accumulation and degradation of starch could be controlled by light, metabolites, day length or circadian rhythms. Decreasing day length leads to stimulation of starch synthesis and inhibition of starch degradation (Stitt et al. 1978; Chatterton & Silvius 1979, 1980; Matt et al. 1998; Lu et al. 2004; Gibon et al. 2004). Lu et al. (2005) reported that starch is broken down almost linearly and plants have a faster rate of starch degradation in long days (short nights) than in short days (long nights). When day length was shortened from 16 to $8\mathrm{h}$ , the starch degradation rate during the first extended night changed from 13.2 to $4.1\mu \mathrm{mol} \, \mathrm{g}^{-1} \, \mathrm{FW} \, \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ (Lu et al. 2005). This suggests that the amount of starch available for consumption and the length of the day could control the rate of degradation during the night. Lu et al. (2005) also reported that the production of maltose is linearly related to the rate of

starch breakdown at different day/night regimes, suggesting that maltose metabolism coordinates with day length.

Several groups have found that transcripts of some enzymes in starch degradation show a rapid accumulation in the light and a coordinated decline in the dark (Harmer et al. 2000; Schaffer et al. 2001; Smith et al. 2004; Lu et al. 2005). The expression of genes encoding maltose metabolism enzymes, such as BAM3, shows a pronounced circadian rhythm (Lu et al. 2005). Transcript levels peak during the day while the enzymes are needed at night. This could provide a mechanism for adjusting starch breakdown rates to day length.

However, protein levels of starch-metabolizing enzymes are relatively stable throughout the day/night cycle in Arabidopsis (Yu et al. 2001; Smith et al. 2004; Lu et al. 2005). In Mesembryanthemum crystallinum, the protein level of SEX1 and the activity of Pho2 were also constant throughout the day/night cycle, unless salt treatment induced CAM (Borland, personal communication). Although in potato leaves, stDPE2 protein level showed diurnal oscillation, increased in amount just prior to sunset and reduced in amount just before sunrise (Lloyd et al. 2004). It is generally accepted that there is a lack of correspondence between transcript levels and protein levels for many enzymes involved in starch degradation in mature leaves regardless of day length (Smith et al. 2004; Lu et al. 2005). This indicates that post-transcriptional and post-translational modifications of key enzymes in starch degradation are important. Recent work by Mikkelsen et al. (2005) provided evidence that phosphorylation of starch by GWD is redox regulated. Recent work from this lab showed that the stroma was in an oxidized state under starch-degrading conditions (photorespiration or night) and the stroma was reduced when starch was not being degraded (photosynthesis) (Weise & Sharkey, unpublished results). This provides additional evidence that starch breakdown may be redox regulated.

It has been reported by different groups that under continuous light, transcript levels of starch-mobilizing genes oscillate with a period length close to $24\mathrm{h}$ , indicating that the expression of starch-mobilizing genes is clock regulated (Harmer et al. 2000; Lu et al. 2005). Under continuous light, the maltose levels, especially $\beta$ -maltose, oscillated with a period length close to $24\mathrm{h}$ (Fig. 3) (Lu et al. 2005). This is surprising because the protein levels of starch-mobilizing genes did not oscillate under continuous light and did not decrease until $48\mathrm{h}$ later (Lu et al. 2005). Lu et al. (2005) reported that under continuous darkness, transcripts of starch-mobilizing genes and carbohydrates decreased substantially during the first $24\mathrm{h}$ and remained at a very low level afterwards. However, the protein levels of starch-mobilizing enzymes did not decrease as much during the first $24\mathrm{h}$ and did not change in amount afterward (Lu et al. 2005). It was concluded that the expression of genes encoding starch-mobilizing enzymes is clock regulated (Lu et al. 2004, 2005). Putative evening element (EE: AAAATATCT), which is necessary for phase-specific transcription, is located in the promoter region of some starch-

# A model for circadian and day-length effects on transitory starch degradation

It was proposed that plants may measure the duration of the day and adjust their rate of starch degradation according to the changes in day length, but the mechanism is still not known (Lu et al. 2004, 2005). The circadian regulation of flowering time at different day lengths has been well-studied (Imaizumi et al. 2003, 2005; Schultz & Kay 2003; Yanovsky & Kay 2003; An et al. 2004). In Arabidopsis, phytochrome A and B, and cryptochrome 1 and 2 contribute to the establishment of period length (McClung 2001). To determine when to flower, plants may sense day length through the degree of coincidence of light with the expression of CONSTANS (CO) (Yanovsky & Kay 2003). As a clock-regulated transcription factor, CO regulates the expression of floral-inductive genes (An et al. 2004; Imaizumi et al. 2005). It is attractive to speculate that plants may adjust their rate of starch degradation according to the changes in day length in a similar manner. However, evidence supporting the involvement of CO in other pathways, such as starch degradation, is still lacking. It is possible that plants may perceive the day length through the degree of coincidence of light with clock-regulated day length-sensitive expression of a gene encoding an essential enzyme in transitory starch breakdown. Lu et al. (2005) reported that the expression of BAM3 was clock regulated and it peaked in the middle of the day in long days but peaked at night in short days. Measuring the starch degradation rate of BAM3 and CO over-expressing lines under different photoperiods may answer the question whether CO and/or BAM3 plays a role in determining starch degradation rate. This model may explain how plants sense the changes in day length and how the transcription of key enzymes in starch degradation is circadian regulated. However, the

oscillation of transcript levels is not reflected in the protein levels, indicating the importance of post-transcriptional and post-translational regulation of key enzymes in starch degradation. Further attention needs to be paid to understand how plants transduce light and day-length signals to modifications of key enzymes in starch degradation.

# Maltose metabolism at low temperature

It was recently reported that total leaf maltose in Arabidopsis increases during cold shock at $5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Kaplan & Guy 2004). In vitro assays indicated that maltose could protect proteins and the photosynthetic electron transport chain at physiological relevant concentrations under freezing stress (Kaplan & Guy 2004). By measuring the activity of electron transport chain of isolated thylakoids, Kaplan & Guy (2004) found that maltose could recover $24\%$ of the activity of the electron transport chain after $20\mathrm{h}$ freezing stress at an estimated physiological concentration $(14\mathrm{mM})$ , and recover $93\%$ of the original activity at $112\mathrm{mM}$ (Kaplan & Guy 2004). The mechanism of how maltose protects proteins and electron transport chain is still not very clear, but Kaplan & Guy (2004) proposed that maltose could act as a compatible solute under freezing stress. Proteins in living organisms are usually surrounded by water molecules and these water molecules form ice and denature proteins under freezing stress. Maltose molecules have hydrophilic groups and replacing water molecules with maltose may alleviate the damage caused by freezing or drying (Bordat et al. 2004; Lerbret et al. 2005). Kaplan & Guy (2004) proposed that this increased amount of maltose under cold stress was located in the chloroplast stroma. Non-aqueous fractionation assays on cold-stressed plants may localize this increased maltose in the plant cell.

It was thought that the increased amount of maltose under cold stress was caused by the increase in the expression of $\beta$ -amylase (Kaplan & Guy 2004). Indeed, the expression of $\beta$ -amylase is induced under osmotic, salt, cold and heat stress (Dreier et al. 1995; Nielsen et al. 1997; Datta et al. 1999; Seki et al. 2001). Kaplan & Guy (2004) reported that the expression of BAM3 was induced by cold treatment. Interestingly, $PtBAMY3$ , a Populus (Populus trichocarpa) ortholog of BAM3 was also strongly induced by cold treatments (Geisler, personal communication). However, the increase in maltose was found to precede the induction of BAM3 expression under cold-stressed conditions: The transcript level of BAM3 peaked after $24\mathrm{h}$ at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , while the maltose level peaked after $4\mathrm{h}$ at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ (Kaplan & Guy, abstract presented at the 2005 annual meeting of ASPB in Seattle, WA, USA). This suggests that the substantial increase in maltose during cold shock is not solely caused by the induction of BAM3 expression. This increase in maltose could be caused by an increase in $\beta$ -amylase activity and/or a decrease in DPE2 or MEX1 activity. Increase in maltose amount and induction of BAM3 expression could be two parallel responses to low-temperature stress. However, the protein levels or the activities of BAM3, DPE2 or MEX1 have not been mea

sured under cold-stressed conditions. Whether the induction of BAM3 expression under cold stress results in an increase in the protein level and/or activity of BAM3 is not known.

# CONCLUSION

Substantial progress has been made recently in understanding the pathway for transitory starch breakdown. Inhibition of maltose export or maltose metabolism significantly slows down the starch degradation rate, supporting the hypothesis that maltose is the predominant form of carbon exported from chloroplasts. Environmental signals such as circadian clock, day length and temperature regulates maltose metabolism. The clock-regulated day length-sensitive expression of a $\beta$ -amylase could provide a mechanism for plants to adjust their rate of starch degradation to the changes in day length. The maltose level increases and the expression of maltose-producing genes is induced under cold-stress conditions. Six areas remain as important areas of research: (1) what enzyme(s) perform the initial attack on starch granules? (2) what is the substrate for SEX4? (3) how is the expression and activity of BAM3 regulated? (4) how does the heteroglycan interact with DPE2 and Pho2 in the cytosol? (5) does $\alpha$ -glucosidase play a role in metabolizing carbohydrates produced in transitory starch breakdown, if it does exist in the cytosol? and (6) what posttranscriptional or post-translational mechanisms regulate starch breakdown?
