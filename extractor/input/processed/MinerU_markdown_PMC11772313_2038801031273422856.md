# Summary

Resistant starch (RS) is a special kind of starch with beneficial effects on obesity, type 2 diabetes and other chronic complications. Breeding high-RS rice varieties is considered a valuable way to improve public health. However, most rice cultivars only contain an RS level lower than $2 \%$ in cooked rice, and cloning of RS genes is critical to improve RS levels in rice. The loss of function of Starch Synthases IIIa (SSIIIa) and SSIIIb, two amylopectin biosynthetic genes, could elevate RS levels up to $10 \%$ . Here, we performed a systematic genetic study of 14 amylopectin biosynthetic genes in the ssIIIa ssIIIb double mutant via genome editing, and investigated their effects on RS formation, the eating quality and grain yield. The results showed that deficiency in SSIIa, SSIVb or ISA2 under the ssIIIa ssIIIb background could each elevate RS content to above $1 4 \%$ , and the quadruple mutants of sbeI sbeIIb ssIIIa ssIIIb and sbeI ssIVb ssIIIa ssIIIb could further increase RS levels to over $1 8 \%$ . Furthermore, the eating quality of cooked rice and grain yield decreased along with the elevated RS contents, showing a trade-off among these traits. In these mutants, ssIIIa ssIIIb showed the balanced performance of RS and grain yield. This study provides insights into RS biosynthesis with a series of RS genes in the amylopectin biosynthesis pathway and practical strategy to breed high-RS rice varieties with balanced performance. 

# Introduction

Starch provides ${ \sim } 5 0 \%$ energy source for human (Miao and Hamaker, 2021). Starch granules with semi-crystalline structure are the primary form of starch, which are composed of two basic glucose polymers: amylose and amylopectin. Amylose is synthesized by granule bound starch synthase (GBSS), whereas amylopectin is synthesized by three classes of enzymes: starch synthases (SS), starch branching enzymes (SBE) and debranching enzymes (DBE) (Huang et al., 2021). For nutritional purposes, starch can be classified as rapidly digestible, slowly digestible and resistant starch (RS). RS is a special kind of starch that cannot be digested in the small intestine and passes to the colon to be fermented by microbiota (Shen et al., 2022). Thus, RS in food is beneficial to type 2 diabetes, weight management and inflammatory bowel disease (Birt et al., 2013; Li et al., 2024). However, most staple food contain a small amount of RS, for example, nearly all rice natural varieties only contain less than $2 \%$ RS, far below the recommended level (Patterson et al., 2020). Therefore, breeding of high-RS rice is of great demand for public health improvement. 

Recently, several high-RS rice mutants have been generated and key functional genes for RS synthesis have been cloned, which are all amylopectin biosynthetic genes. Deficiency in SSIIIa could increase RS contents in cooked rice, which was dependent on the strong allele of Waxy (Wx) encoding GBSSI (Zhou et al., 2016). Deficiency in SBEIIb could also increase RS 

contents (Zhu et al., 2012). Meanwhile, although the single mutant of sbeI or ssIIIb showed no significant effect on RS levels, the double mutant sbeI sbeIIb or ssIIIa ssIIIb could further elevate RS contents of the sbeIIb or ssIIIa single mutant, respectively (Huang et al., 2023; Miura et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2012). Therefore, it is considered that the highly branched amylopectin could be conducive to being catalyzed by $\beta$ -amylases, whereas the amylose with rare branches is hard to be digested and critical to RS formation (Ahuja et al., 2013). However, the function of other amylopectin biosynthetic genes on RS formation remains largely unknown. 

In previous study, we identified a high-RS mutant rs4 with RS levels up to $10 \%$ , which was an ssIIIa ssIIIb double mutant in the indica rice cultivar R7954 background harbouring the strong $W x ^ { a }$ allele (Wang et al., 2023). In this study, we expected that the mutation of amylopectin biosynthetic genes may further increase the RS contents and performed a systematic genetic study of SSs, SBEs and DBEs via genome editing in the rs4 background. We found that the mutation of specific amylopectin biosynthetic gene could elevate RS levels to above $14 \%$ and double mutations of certain amylopectin biosynthetic genes showed increased RS levels over $1 8 \%$ in the ssIIIa ssIIIb background, however, the performance on eating and cooking quality of cooked rice and yield decreased along with the increased RS contents. # Results

Loss of function of specific amylopectin biosynthetic gene elevates RS contents in the ssIIIa ssIIIb background 

We designed sgRNAs targeting 14 amylopectin biosynthetic genes, including 7 SSs of SSI, SSIIa, SSIIb, SSIIc, SSIVa, SSIVb, SSV, 3 SBEs of SBEI, SBEIIa, SBEIIb, and 4 DBEs of isoamylase 1 (ISA1), ISA2, ISA3, and pullulanase (PUL), to generate mutants in the rs4 background. From the genotyping results of $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ plants, we obtained the homozygous frameshift and transgenic-free mutants of 13 genes with at least two independent lines except SSI (Figures S1-S3). For SSI, we only obtained one homozygous in-frame deletion mutant $r s 4 ~ s s I ^ { - 6 b p }$ (Figure S1a), similar to the previous research that failed to obtain homozygous ssI ssIIIa null mutant (Hayashi et al., 2015), indicating that ssI ssIIIa ssIIIb triple mutants may be lethal. The measurement of RS contents in cooked rice showed that the deficiency in SSIIa, SSIVb or ISA2 in rs4 could increase RS levels to over $14 \%$ (Figure 1a), and the genome editing of other seven amylopectin biosynthetic genes could also slightly increase RS levels. Meanwhile, although $r s 4 s s l ^ { - 6 b p }$ was an in-frame mutant, its RS content was also significantly increased to $1 5 \%$ (Figure S4a), indicating that $r s 4 s s I ^ { - 6 b p }$ was a leaky mutant and SSI was a functional RS gene. We then tested 

whether the accumulation of mutations in amylopectin biosynthetic genes could further increase RS contents. We chose SBEI, SBEIIb and SSIVb with divergent functions, and generated all combinations of their double mutants in the $r s 4$ background via CRISPR/Cas9 (Figure S5). Interestingly, the RS contents of homozygous rs4 sbeI sbeIIbcr and rs4 sbeI ssIVbcr mutants were further increased to over $1 8 \%$ (Figure 1b), whereas rs4 ssIVb sbeIIbcr showed a similar RS content as rs4 ${ s s / V b ^ { c r } }$ , demonstrating that double mutations of certain amylopectin biosynthetic genes could further increase RS contents to over $1 8 \%$ in the ssIIIa ssIIIb background. All these results demonstrated that the mutation of specific amylopectin biosynthetic gene could further increase the RS contents in the ssIIIa ssIIIb background (Figure 1c).

We first observed the appearance quality of grains, and found that all examined mutants displayed floury seed (Figure 2; Figure S6). Then, we examined the morphology of starch granule via scanning electron microscope (SEM). In wild type, the starch granules displayed a regular and uniform polyhedral structure, whereas the examined mutants exhibited various morphologies in shape and size. Starch granules of ssIIIa, sbeIIbcr and rs4 were variable in size with rounded surfaces, consistent with the previous reports (Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2012). Similarly, starch granules of $r s 4 \ s s { / / a ^ { c r } }$ were also variable in size. However, $r s 4 \ s s / V b ^ { c r }$ , $r s 4$ sbeI sbeIIbcr and $r s 4 s b e / s s / V b ^ { c r }$ had small starch granules with spherical structures, especially the granule size of $r s 4 \ s s / V b ^ { c r }$ was even smaller (Figure 2; Figure S6). The severely altered starch granule of $r s 4 s s / V b ^ { c r }$ may be due to the crucial role of SSIIIa and SSIVb in determining starch granule morphology and maintaining the amyloplast envelope structure (Toyosawa et al., 2016). The loosely packed structure of starch granule in 

these mutants may lead to abnormal appearance quality and grain filling.

# Thousand-seed weight and grain yield are impaired in high-RS mutants

Furthermore, we grew these mutants in field and measured the yield-related traits (Figure 4a). The grain yield per plant was significantly decreased compared with R7954 and showed strong anti-correlation with RS contents $( \mathsf { P C C } = - 0 . 9 4 )$ in all these mutants (Figure 4b). We then investigated the key attributes 

contributing to this yield loss and found that the reduced 1000-seed weight was the major reason $( \mathsf { P C C } = - 0 . 9 3$ with RS contents) for the yield loss in these mutants (Figure 4c). Different with the strong changes in grain yield per plant and 1000-seed weight, these mutants showed subtle changes in grain number per main panicle, seed setting rate, tiller number, and plant height compared to R7954 (Figure S9). The independent lines with the same mutation combinations showed consistent results in decreased grain yield per plant and 1000-seed weight (Figure S10a,b). Taken together, the increased RS contents was anti-correlated with the reduced 1000-seed weight and yield. 

# ssIIIa ssIIIb showed balanced performance in RS and grain yield

To balance the trade-off between RS levels and grain yield, we calculated the theoretical RS yield per plant through multiplying RS contents with grain yield per plant of individual mutants (Figure 4d). We found that the RS yield per plant were elevated along with the RS content before $10 \%$ , and then the RS yield reached a ceiling level for high-RS mutants with RS content over $10 \%$ . 

Amylose also has valuable industrial application potential in food and non-food usages, such as thicker, stabilizer, biodegraded package, drug delivery and so on (Obadi et al., 2023). Since these RS mutants also exhibited greatly increased AAC levels, we then calculate the AAC yield per plant (Figure 4e). The mutants with RS content less than $10 \%$ showed higher AAC yield per plant than mutants with more than $10 \%$ RS. Among them, $r s 4$ had the highest AAC yield per plant. Taken together, rs4 with $10 \%$ RS contents showed the balanced performance in RS, AAC and grain yields per plant among these RS mutants. 

# Discussion

In previous studies, only a few RS genes have been cloned and all of them functioned in amylopectin biosynthesis pathway. Deficiency in both SBEI and SBEIIb could further elevate the RS contents in the sbeIIb mutant to over $1 5 \%$ (Miura et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2012). The RS levels of SBE family gene multiplex edited heterozygous mutants reached $1 5 \%$ (Biswas et al., 2023). Recently, multiplex genome editing of starch synthetic genes in barley generated sbeIIa sbeIIb and ssIIa sbeIIa sbeIIb mutants which elevated RS contents to $12 \%$ and $1 4 \%$ , respectively (Yang et al., 2024). The function of other amylopectin biosynthetic genes on RS formation remains largely unknown. Here, through systematic study on amylopectin biosynthetic gene, we identified a series of uncovered RS genes. Loss of function of 10 amylopectin biosynthetic genes could each significantly increase RS levels under the $r s 4$ background, among which the deficiency in SSIIa, SSIVb or ISA2 could elevate RS contents to above $14 \%$ (Figure 1a). Furthermore, we found that the accumulation of multiple RS genes could further increase RS levels. The quadruple mutants of sbeI sbeIIb ssIIIa ssIIIb and sbeI ssIVb ssIIIa ssIIIb could each increase RS levels to over $1 8 \%$ (Figure 1b). Meanwhile, with the elevation of RS contents, the 1000-seed weight and grain yield of these mutants were inevitably decreased (Figure 4b,c). To balance the RS content and yield, rs4 with $10 \%$ RS contents showed the optimized performance of RS and grain yield among these mutants. In summary, we propose a model showing the relative levels of RS, AAC, PT, and grain yield among the RS mutants of amylopectin biosynthetic genes (Figure 4f; Table S1). 

According to the physical structure, RS can be divided into five types of RS1–RS5. RS3 and RS5 mainly exist in cooked rice, 

whereas RS2 mainly exists in raw rice (Shen et al., 2022; Yi and Li, 2022). The enzymatic hydrolysis of native rice starch can be affected by amylose content, starch hierarchical structures and physical barriers of starch granules to form RS2 (Li et al., 2020). During gelatinization process, the crystalline structure of starch is destroyed (Wang and Copeland, 2013), and the disordered amylose and amylopectin are aggregated to form a recrystallized gel network with high retrogradation rate to generate RS3 (Wang et al., 2015). Increased gelatinized temperature is associated with increased RS3 content, however, the casual relationship between them is still obscure. Amylose content is considered the main controller in determining the starch digestibility of gelatinized and retrograded rice (Ding et al., 2021; Yi and Li, 2022). In accordance with the previous reports, our results showed that the AAC exhibited a strong linear correlation to RS3 but a weak linear correlation to RS2 (Figure 3a,c). Interestingly, both sbeIIbcr and rs4 ${ s s / V b ^ { c r } }$ exhibited higher RS2 levels than mutants with similar or higher RS3, indicating that SBEIIb and SSIVb may have specific functions in RS2 formation. These mutants could also be used to study the different mechanism in RS2 and RS3 formation and their relationship with other factors such as gelatinized temperature. Moreover, we obtained quadruple mutants with RS contents higher than triple mutants (Figure 1b), which supported the idea that pyramiding more mutations of amylopectin biosynthetic genes could further elevate RS contents (Fujita et al., 2022). In future, multiplex genome editing or cross with existed high RS mutants may allow us to generate quintuple mutants or sextuple mutants that may achieve even higher RS levels. 

Inhibition of the amylopectin biosynthesis is an efficient way to increase RS contents, however, it also causes an avoidable loss of grain yield. Starch biosynthesis genes may also affect starch in leaf, stem and other tissues to influence yield-related traits (Dong et al., 2024; MacNeill et al., 2017). The level of yield loss may vary in different rice varieties, for example, the ssIIIa ssIIIb mutants in japonica background showed lower levels of 1000-seed weight loss compared with the ssIIIa ssIIIb mutants in indica background (Huang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Previous study also found that backcrossing high RS mutant with elite rice cultivars could elevate the seed weight without compromising the RS content (Miura et al., 2022). In future, it is of great importance to identify the genes other than amylopectin biosynthetic genes to increase RS content without yield penalty. In addition, digestion rate of rice may also be affected by other attributes rather than RS, which could be used to optimize the trade-off between eating and cooking quality of cooked rice and digestibility in rice breeding (Buenafe et al., 2021; Parween et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2023). In conclusion, this study identified a series of RS genes and provided new insights into RS biosynthesis and new strategy to breed high-RS rice varieties with balanced overall performance.

# Materials and methods

# Plasmid construction

Single-guiding RNAs (sgRNAs) were designed through the CRISPR-GE tool (http://skl.scau.edu.cn/) (Xie et al., 2017). For single gene editing in R7954 or the mutant rs4, two sgRNAs were designed for each gene targeting coding region. 28 sgRNAs were cloned into the CRISPR/Cas9 vector VK005-1 (Beijing Viewsold Biotech) by enzyme digestion and ligation methods. For multiplex gene editing, three pYLCRISPR/Cas9P -H-based constructs were prepared, each carrying two sgRNAs expression cassettes driven by the OsU3 and OsU6a promoters for coding region of combination of SSIVb, BEIIb and BEI. CRISPR/Cas9 vector pYLCRISPR/Cas9Pubi-H were constructed as previously reported (Ma et al., 2015). Primers of designed sgRNAs were listed in Table S2. 

# Generation of edited plants

Oryza sativa L. ssp. indica cv. R7954 was used as the wild type to generated the sbeIIb edited line. The rs4 mutant was used to generate ssI, ssIIa, ssIIb, ssIIc, ssIVa, ssIVb, ssV, sbeI, sbeIIa, sbeIIb, isa1, isa2, isa3, pul, ssIVb sbeIIb, sbeI sbeIIb, and sbeI ssIVb edited rice lines. The vectors were separately transformed into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105, the strains were mixed with equal amounts for same targeted genes and used to transform the rs4 or R7954 callus. Agrobacterium-mediated transformation was performed as previously described (Hiei et al., 1994; Zhang et al., 2016).

# Genotyping of edited plants

For genotyping of gene-edited plants, genomic DNA was isolated by the CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) method (Murray and Thompson, 1980). PCR was performed to amplify the genomic region surrounding the CRISPR target sites using the corresponding primers (Table S2). The PCR product was sequenced by the Sanger method, and the sequencing chromatograms were decoded by the Degenerate Sequence Decoding method (Liu et al., 2015). 

# Preparation of rice flour

Rice seed sample preparation was conducted as previously described with minor modifications (Zhou et al., 2016). Harvested 

rice grains were air-dried to achieve the moisture content about $12 \%$ for measurements of RS contents and grain quality traits. Rice samples were dehulled by a small rice hulling machine (TOP Cloud-agri, TP-JLG-2018), milled to white rice using a grain polisher (Kett, TP-3000) and subsequently grounded to flour in the ultra-centrifugal mill (Retsch, ZM200) with a $1 . 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ sieve for measuring RS contents and pasting properties. The rice flour was passed through a $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ and $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ sieve for total starch analyses and apparent amylose content (AAC) analyses, respectively. 

# RS, AAC and total starch analysis

RS contents were analysed as previously described using a resistant starch assay kit (Megazyme, K-RSTAR) according to the manufacturer’s assay procedure (Wang et al., 2023). The total starch content was analysed by using a total starch measurement kit (Megazyme, K-TSTA) through RTS-NaOH procedure. AAC was measured by using the method of ISO 6647 (International Organization for Standardization) with standard samples of four levels $1 . 5 \%$ , $1 0 . 4 \%$ , $1 6 . 2 \%$ and $2 6 . 5 \%$ ) provided by China National Rice Research Institute. 

# Starch pasting properties

A total of 3 g rice flour was dispersed in $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ distilled water and characterized using the rapid visco analyser (Perten, RVA4500) according to the methods described previously (Wang et al., 2023). Pasting viscosity, pasting temperature, trough viscosity, breakdown viscosity and final viscosity were recorded. 

# Seed morphology and SEM

Dehulled whole mature seeds and cross-sections were taken by stereomicroscope (OLYMPUS, SZX16). Scanning electron micrographs of starch granules in endosperm cross-section was performed as previously described (Fujita et al., 2007). Rice seeds were fractured across the short axis and adhered to double-sided adhesive tape mounted on an aluminium stub. The surface was sputter-coated with gold of ion sputtering equipment under vacuum (HITACHI, E-1010) and observed by SEM (ZEISS, Crossbeam 340). 

# Phenotypic evaluation

Twenty-four plants of each edited mutants and the wild type were grown in three rows with eight plants per row in the experimental field in the summer in Beijing $( 3 9 ^ { \circ } 5 4 ^ { \prime } \textsf { N } 1 1 6 ^ { \circ } 2 3 ^ { \prime } \textsf { E } )$ , and 10 plants were randomly selected for the phenotypic analysis of the plant heights, tiller numbers, grain yield per plant, grain number per main panicle and seed setting rates. Grain length, grain width and 1000-seed weight were measured using automatic seed counting and analysing instrument (Model SC-G, Wanshen). Mutants and the wild type were grown in the experimental field in the winter in Hainan (19°120 N $1 0 9 ^ { \circ } 4 2 ^ { \prime } \ \mathsf { E }$ ), and three plants were randomly selected for the phenotypic analysis of the grain yield per plant and 1000-seed weight.
