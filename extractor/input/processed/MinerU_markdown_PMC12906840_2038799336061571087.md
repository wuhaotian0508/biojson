# ABSTRACT

Plants balance resource energy allocation between growth and immunity to ensure survival and reproduction under limited availability. This study reveals that rice cultivars with elevated sucrose levels boost resistance to the fungal pathogen Magnaporthe oryzae by accumulating the phytoalexin sakuranetin, regulated by the transcription factor STOREKEEPER (OsSTK). OsSTK binds to the promoter region of OsNOMT (Naringenin-7-O-Methyltransferase) to drive sakuranetin biosynthesis. Rice overexpressing OsSTK exhibits enhanced resistance to M. oryzae and improved growth, with greater plant height and higher grain yield, achieved through optimised resource allocation and carbon flow supporting both primary and secondary metabolite production. Moreover, the identification of OsSTK haplotypes linked to functional divergence in sakuranetin-mediated resistance, particularly in high-sucrose rice cultivars, underscores OsSTK's capacity to overcome the traditional growth-defence trade-off. This work highlights OsSTK as a valuable molecular target for breeding resilient, high-yielding rice varieties capable of withstanding environmental stresses. By advancing our understanding of resource allocation through carbon flow regulation, this study offers a sustainable strategy to improve food security and promote agricultural resilience in the face of climate change. 

# 1 | Introduction

Plants in their natural habitats face constant biotic and abiotic challenges, requiring efficient allocation of limited resources 

between growth and defence to ensure survival and reproduction (Huot et  al.  2014). This growth–defence trade-off, driven by resource limitations, significantly impacts plant fitness and ecological success (Gao et al. 2024; Huot et al. 2014). Enhancing 

overall resource acquisition and utilisation efficiency offers a strategy enabling plants to simultaneously support growth and defence without traditional compromises. 

A primary approach to achieving the enhanced resource utilisation is increasing photosynthetic capacity and optimising carbon allocation, enabling plants to transcend the conventional growth–defence dichotomy. Central to this strategy is sucrose (Suc), the principal end-product of photosynthesis, which serves as a key resource for both growth and defence functions (Choudhary et  al.  2022; Ruan  2012, 2014). Fluctuations in endogenous and exogenous Suc levels influence plant growth dynamics and responses to pathogen attacks, highlighting its central role in growth–defence interactions (Bezrutczyk et al. 2018). For instance, Arabidopsis thaliana jazD mutants show enhanced resistance to herbivores and fungal pathogens but suffer reduced growth (Guo et  al.  2018). Exogenous Suc application can partially restore jazD biomass (Guo et  al.  2018), while introducing the exaggerated growth mutant phyB, which possesses higher endogenous Suc levels, mitigates jazD's growth limitations (Major et al. 2020). Beyond these interactions, Suc has been increasingly recognised for its regulatory role in secondary metabolite biosynthesis (Erb and Kliebenstein  2020), which is essential for plant defence (Piasecka et al. 2015). Studies demonstrate that Suc enhances anthocyanin, glucosinolate and flavonol biosynthesis in a variety of plant species, underscoring its significance in coordinating defence responses (Guo et al. 2011; Miao et al. 2013; Solfanelli et al. 2006; Zhao et al. 2021). 

In response to pathogen or herbivore attacks, plants strategically redirect nutrient resources from growth-related processes toward the synthesis of defence-related secondary metabolites, a trade-off that often occurs at the expense of growth (Erb and Kliebenstein 2020; He et al. 2022). For example, Nicotiana attenuata reallocates nitrogen into defence compounds such as phenolamides and nicotine when under herbivory (Ullmann-Zeunert et  al.  2013). Similarly, barley, rice and Brachypodium distachyon reprogram primary metabolism for secondary metabolite production during fungal colonisation (Parker et al. 2009). This reallocation in metabolic flux enables plants to enhance their resistance mechanisms by producing specialised compounds that deter attackers or mitigate damage. Such metabolic shifts underline the delicate balance between growth and defence in plant resource allocation (He et al. 2022; Koprivova and Kopriva 2022), emphasising the inherent energy and nutrition trade-offs that plants must navigate to survive under biotic stress. 

Here, we explore the function of OsSTK, a transcription factor (TF) from the STOREKEEPER gene family (Zourelidou et al. 2002), in optimising carbon metabolism to enhance both growth and defence in rice simultaneously. Our results reveal that OsSTK improves resistance to the fungal pathogen Magnaporthe oryzae (M. oryzae) while concurrently promoting growth and yield. By boosting photosynthesis and optimising carbon flow, OsSTK supports both primary metabolite biosynthesis for growth and secondary metabolite production, such as the flavonoid phytoalexin sakuranetin, for defence. These findings highlight OsSTK as a promising tool for transcending the traditional growth-defence trade-off, offering a novel approach 

to improving crop resilience and productivity through enhanced carbon assimilation and strategic resource allocation. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Positive Correlation Between Fungal Blast Resistance and Suc Level in Rice

To investigate the relationship between sugar metabolism and blast resistance in rice, we analysed carbohydrate responses in rice plants treated with or without M. oryzae. A total of 25 carbohydrate metabolites were detected (Figure 1 and Table S1). The total sugar content, along with most carbohydrate metabolites, increased in M. oryzae-infected shoots compared to controls at 48 and $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ post inoculation, with the most pronounced changes occurring at $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ (Figure 1A and Table S1). Specifically, 12 out of 25 sugars were unregulated, accounting for $9 8 . 0 8 \%$ of the total sugar content by weight, whereas 5 sugars decreased, making up only $0 . 3 4 \%$ of the total sugar content (Figure 1B). Among the upregulated sugars, Suc comprised the largest portion, contributing $9 0 . 9 8 \%$ of the total sugar (Figure 1B). These results highlight the significant role of sugar in plant defence. To further explore the correlation between sugars and plant resistance, we assessed the content of 25 sugars and their relationship with blast resistance across 27 rice varieties. The patterns of sugar contents and blast resistance were analysed among these varieties, providing additional insights into the role of carbohydrates in disease resistance (Figure 1C,D, Table S2). Correlation analysis revealed a strong negative correlation between the relative growth of M. oryzae and total sugar level, with a Pearson correlation coefficient of $r = - 0 . 7 1 3 2$ ( $\scriptstyle { p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 }$ ; Table S3). Among the detected sugars, Suc, L-fucose (Fuc) and arabinose (Ara) exhibited the strongest negative correlations, with Pearson's $r$ values of $- 0 . 7 3 5 7$ ${ p < 0 . 0 0 1 } )$ , $- 0 . 7 1 1 2$ ${ \scriptstyle \cdot } p { < } 0 . 0 0 1 )$ and $- 0 . 5 8 3 9$ $\scriptstyle { p < 0 . 0 0 1 4 ) }$ , respectively (Figure 1E and Table S3). To confirm the role of Suc in blast resistance, we employed two complementary approaches: foliar Suc application and genetic analysis of sucrose-altered mutants. Foliar Suc application significantly enhanced rice resistance to M. oryzae (Figure 1F–H). Similarly, genetic analysis of three sucrose-altered mutants—ossut2 (defective Suc transport with elevated leaf Suc [Eom et al. 2011]), osnac23 (enhanced Suc content [Li et  al.  2022]) and ostpp1 (reduced Suc levels [Li et  al.  2022])-revealed that ossut2 and osnac23 mutants with higher Suc content demonstrated greater resistance to M. oryzae, whereas the ostpp1 mutant with lower Suc levels showed increased susceptibility compared with their respective controls (Figure S1). These findings establish that elevated Suc levels directly contribute to enhanced blast resistance, suggesting that increasing carbohydrate resource availability could be a viable strategy to strengthen plant defence without compromising growth.

# 2.5 | Over-Expressing OsSTK Enhanced Plant Height and Grain Yield

Interestingly, beyond its role in disease resistance, OsSTK also positively impacts plant growth and yield. The OsSTK-OE 

plants were significantly taller than NIP plants, with longer internodes (Figure  5A–D) and panicles (Figure  5E,F). Moreover, the filled seed number and weight per panicle increased by $2 7 . 4 6 \%$ and $4 1 . 9 5 \%$ , respectively, in OsSTK-OE lines compared to NIP controls (Figure 5G,H). There were no differences in seed width, seed length or 1000-seed weight between OsSTK-OE and NIP plants (Figure  S8). Nevertheless, OsSTK-OE lines yielded $9 . 5 3 \% - 1 4 . 9 \%$ more per $\mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ than the NIP control in paddy fields (Figure  5I). Collectively, these findings show that OsSTK overexpression enhances rice height and grain yield. 

To investigate the OsSTK-regulated genes related to agronomic traits, a high-throughput sequencing approach was employed to compare the expression profiles between control and OsSTKoverexpressing rice. Using the criteria of |log2 (fold change)| $\geq 1$ and $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ , transcriptome analysis identified 3108 DEGs, including 1484 upregulated and 1624 downregulated genes, in OsSTK-OE #4 plants and 1510 DEGs with 458 upregulated and 1052 downregulated in OsSTK-OE #5 plants compared to the wild-type NIP (Figure 5J, Table S6). Venn analysis revealed that 595 genes were altered in both OsSTK-OE lines relative to the NIP control (Figure S9). KEGG analysis of the upregulated genes revealed enrichment in pathways related to sugar metabolism and plant growth, such as Starch and Suc metabolism (dosa00500), Fructose and Mannose metabolism (dosa00051), Carbon metabolism (dosa01200), Pentose and Glucuronate interconversions (dosa00040), Plant hormone signal transduction (dosa04075) (Figure  5K, Table  S7). Additionally, the defence-related pathways were enriched, including Plant– pathogen interaction (dosa04626), MAPK signalling pathway– plant (dosa04016), Phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (dosa00940), Flavonoid biosynthesis (dosa00941) and Flavone and Flavonol biosynthesis (dosa00944) (Figure 5K). As shown in Figure 5L, the RT-qPCR results supported the transcriptome data. Among the identified upregulated genes, OsTPS8 (Os08g0445700) was found to regulate soluble sugar (including Suc) content, plant height and grain yield, while OsPP2C08 (Os01g0656200) is known to promote internode elongation in rice (Song et al. 2023; Vishal et  al.  2018). Additionally, OsPAL4 (Os02g0627100) has been reported to play a positive role in rice blast disease resistance (Tonnessen et al. 2015). Based on these findings, further investigation focused on OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4. 

Bioinformatics analysis revealed that OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4 each possess at least one STKRE (OsSTK recognition element) in their promoters (Figure  5M), suggesting direct OsSTK-mediated regulation. To test this hypothesis, a transient expression assay was conducted using LUC reporters driven by the promoters of OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4. Co-injection of OsTPS8pro-LUC, OsPP2C08pro-LUC or OsPAL4pro-LUC with SK-OsSTK resulted in intense luminescence, while the SK vector displayed only basal luminescence levels (Figure 5M,N). To further validate this, the primers targeting the promoters of OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4 were designed, and ChIPqPCR analysis was performed. Similar to the positive control, significant enrichment of OsSTK-Flag was observed on the promoters of OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4 (Figure 5O). These results indicate that OsSTK directly activates the expression of these genes. Collectively, these findings suggest that OsSTK enhances sugar metabolic flux and promotes plant growth 

via transcriptionally regulating the expression of OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4. 

Furthermore, yield traits showed no difference between OsSTK mutants and NIP plants (Figure 5A–H, Figure S7). In addition, measurements of total protein, total amino acid, free fatty acid, amylose, total flavonoid and soluble sugar in the mature seeds revealed no significant difference between OsSTK-OE plants and wild-type plants (Figure S10). Collectively, our data demonstrated that OsSTK functions as a rare positive regulator of both pathogen resistance and agronomic performance. By simultaneously enhancing disease resistance against M. oryzae while improving plant height and grain yield, OsSTK overcomes the typical growth–defence trade-off that limits many defencerelated genes. This dual functionality positions OsSTK as a particularly valuable target for breeding and biotechnological approaches aimed at developing high-yielding rice varieties with enhanced disease resistance.

# 2.7 | OsSTK34R81M/34H81L Alleles Determine Sakuranetin-Mediated Immunity in High-Suc Rice Cultivars

Given OsSTK's critical role in balancing growth and defence, we next investigated whether this gene has been under selection during rice domestication and improvement. To explore the evolutionary significance of OsSTK across different rice populations, we analysed its genomic diversity using the ECOGEMS database. The corresponding fixation index (Fst) values revealed that OsSTK has played a role in differentiating wild and cultivated rice (Figure 7A). Moreover, nucleotide diversity was observed to be lower in cultivated rice compared to wild rice (Figure 7B), and a distinct peak in the reduction of diversity (ROD) was detected at the OsSTK locus in cultivated rice (Figure 7C). These findings suggest that OsSTK has undergone subspecies-specific positive selection during rice domestication and breeding. This aligns with previous studies indicating that certain genes have been subject to positive selection, contributing to the differentiation and successful adaptation of rice subspecies (Zhai et al. 2022). 

To further investigate the variation at the OsSTK locus in rice populations, we analysed 356 high-quality assembled rice genomes from the RAPDB database. Five major OsSTK haplotypes were identified (Figure 7D). Among the single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) within these haplotypes, three SNPs 

were located in exon regions (positions 101, 241 and 285), while the remaining SNPs were situated in the $3 ^ { \prime }$ UTR, intronic regions and $5 ^ { \prime }$ UTR (Figure 7D). Among these three exon SNPs, one was identified as a nonsense mutation, whereas two were nonsynonymous mutations. Specifically, a G-to-A transition at the 101st nucleotide position results in an amino acid substitution from Arginine (R) to Histidine (H) at the 34th residue, and an A-to-C transversion at the 241st nucleotide position causes a change from Methionine (M) to Leucine (L) at the 81st residue (Figure 7D). The distinct OsSTK haplotypes show specific distributions: OsSTK34R81M is primarily distributed in haplotypes H1 and H5, while OsSTK34H81L is mainly found in haplotypes H2to H4 (Figure 7D). Haplotype frequency analysis revealed that the haplotypes H1 and H5 are more frequent in japonica and tropical japonica (Jap_Int and TRJ) than in wild rice (Figure  7E). Haplotype H1 has a broad geographic distribution, whereas haplotype H5 is predominantly observed in ARU. Haplotype network analysis further revealed that H3 likely represents ancestral haplotypes in rice (Figure 7F). Additionally, we also cloned and sequenced the OsSTK gene from eight cultivated rice varieties. Our analysis revealed that OsSTK34R81M was observed in Zs97, Mxz, Th101 and Xfz, same as NIP, while OsSTK34H81L was found in 9311, Ez5, Mtz and Ywsm (Figure S12). These results suggest that the distinct OsSTK haplotypes are associated with specific rice subspecies and populations, reflecting evolutionary adaptations during rice domestication and breeding.

# 3 | Discussion

The growth-defence tradeoff fundamentally shapes plant survival and reproduction, particularly under resource constraints (He et al. 2022). Plants must allocate limited carbon resources either toward growth or defence, often at the expense of the other. Our study demonstrates how the transcription factor OsSTK enables rice to overcome this tradeoff by optimising carbon allocation between growth and defence pathways (Figure 7K). 

Suc, the primary photosynthetic assimilate and transport sugar, serves as a critical resource for both plant growth and defence responses (Choudhary et al. 2022; Ruan 2012, 2014). This creates an intricate relationship between carbon metabolism and pathogen resistance. In response to pathogen infection, plants reduce the synthesis of storage and structural polysaccharides such as starch while increasing soluble sugar levels to provide sufficient energy for immune activation (Bezrutczyk et al. 2018). This relationship is further complicated by pathogen evolution. As obligate heterotrophs, plant pathogens cannot synthesise sugars independently and must acquire carbon from host plants to establish successful infections (Gao et al. 2007). Consequently, sugar-rich environments typically favour pathogen proliferation and virulence. Many pathogens have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to manipulate host sugar transport systems, such as inducing SWEET transporters to redirect carbon resources from the host (Bezrutczyk et al. 2018; Breia et al. 2021), or secreting effectors that promote polysaccharide degradation into absorbable monosaccharides (Bowyer et al. 2000; Ma et al. 2015). Therefore, plants must balance meeting their own energy requirements for immune activation while preventing pathogen exploitation of these same resources (Bezrutczyk et  al.  2018). Consistently, we observed enhanced carbohydrate metabolites, including Suc, in M. oryzae-infected plants (Figure 1). 

Plants have evolved counterstrategies to maintain defence under high-sugar conditions. These include restricted phloem Suc efflux via SUTs (Pommerrenig et al. 2020) and Suc-induced immunity through anthocyanin accumulation and PR gene activation (Bolouri Moghaddam and Van den Ende 2012). Our study revealed that rice cultivars with higher endogenous Suc levels showed increased resistance to M. oryzae (Figure  1). We observed that ossut2 and osnac23 mutants with higher sucrose content showed enhanced resistance, while ostpp1 with lower sucrose exhibited increased susceptibility (Figure S1). In addition, foliar Suc application enhanced this resistance, consistent with previous findings (Gómez-Ariza et  al. 2007). In addition, critically, the protective effect of Suc was abolished in OsSTK knockout mutants (Figure  4), confirming OsSTK's essential role in Suc-mediated immunity. Most remarkably, OsSTK-overexpressing rice exhibited both enhanced M. oryzae 

resistance and improved biomass and yield (Figures  4 and 6). This dual enhancement occurs through OsSTK's ability to increase photosynthetic capacity while simultaneously directing carbon flux optimally between primary metabolism for growth and secondary metabolism for defence compound production. 

Beyond its role as a carbon source, Suc functions as a signalling molecule that regulates secondary metabolite biosynthesis critical for plant defence (Erb and Kliebenstein  2020). Our analysis revealed a strong positive correlation between endogenous Suc levels and accumulation of sakuranetin—a flavonoid phytoalexin with potent antifungal activity against M. oryzae (Hasegawa et al. 2014; He et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024). Consistently, Suc-altered mutants accumulated corresponsive sakuranetin production (Figure  S1). This correlation extended to enhanced blast resistance (Figures  1 and 2), suggesting Suc levels directly influence the plant's defensive capacity. OsSTK directly promoted sakuranetin biosynthesis by regulating OsNOMT expression through STKRE cis-elements, enhancing blast resistance (Figures  3 and 4). Additionally, RNA-sequencing revealed that OsSTK regulates genes associated with both growth (OsTPS8, OsPP2C08) and defence (OsPAL4) (Figure 5). Beyond transcriptional regulation, metabolomics analysis showed that OsSTK increased photosynthesis and Suc production while diverting carbon resources to both primary metabolites for growth and secondary metabolites, such as flavonoids (sakuranetin), terpenoids (momilactone A and B), and alkaloids for defence (Figure 6). As a result, OsSTK simultaneously enhances both growth and defence by driving OsTPS8 and OsPP2C08 for growth and OsNOMT and OsPAL4 for phytoalexin biosynthesis, enabling OsSTK-OE plants to simultaneously enhance both growth and defence, transcending the traditional growth–defence trade-off paradigm to achieve greater resilience and productivity (Figure  7K). Genetic analysis identified five haplotypes at the OsSTK locus, with two nonsynonymous SNPs (OsSTK34R81M and OsSTK34H81L) affecting OsNOMT expression under pathogen attack (Figure 7A–F). High-Suc rice cultivars associated with OsSTK34H81L displayed higher sakuranetin content and blast resistance, demonstrating the importance of OsSTK in regulating carbon allocation to defence-related secondary metabolites, particularly in high-Suc cultivars (Figure  7G,H, Figure  S13). To understand the molecular basis of this difference, we compared the transcriptional activities of both OsSTK variants. Dual-luciferase assays demonstrate that OsSTK34R81M can activate the promoters of OsNOMT, OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4, while OsSTK34H81L cannot (Figure 7I, Figure S14A). This pattern was confirmed in protoplast experiments using osstk-2 mutant cells, where only the OsSTK34R81M variant could restore expression of these target genes (Figure  7J and Figure  S14B). This consistent loss of transcriptional activity across multiple targets reinforces that the OsSTK34H81L mutation specifically disrupts OsSTK's activation function rather than affecting target gene specificity or DNA binding. The OsSTK34R81M variant maintains normal transcriptional activity across the entire regulon, while OsSTK34H81L acts as a transcriptionally inactive form. The precise molecular mechanism underlying this functional difference warrants further investigation. 

Climate change and food security urgently require crop varieties with both high yields and stress resistance. Rising $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ 

levels affect plant secondary metabolism, crucial for adaptation (Zhang et al. 2022), as seen when elevated $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ increases phenylpropanoids in tobacco, enhancing virus resistance (Matros et al. 2006). We found that OsSTK enhances $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fixation for sakuranetin production, improving rice resistance to M. oryzae while simultaneously boosting photosynthesis, grain yield, biomass and carbon storage (Figures  5 and 6). Sugar-responsive transcription factors like OsSTK represent valuable targets for precision breeding to develop climate-resilient, high-yielding rice varieties. Exploring OsSTK orthologs across crop species could advance sustainable agriculture by optimising both carbon assimilation and stress resilience. 

# 4 | Material and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Material, Growth Conditions and Rice Blast Inoculation

The rice cultivars 1–27 are as follows: Guikang527 (Gk527), Wuyujing7 (Wyj7), Huazhan (Hz), NIP, Jia58, Nanjing46 (Nj46), Zhenshan97B (Zs97), Wuyujing21 (Wyj21), Suxiangjing1 (Sxj1), Nanjing55 (Nj55), AJ1302 (AJ13), Huanghuazha (Hhz), Wushanisimiao (Wssm), Meixiangzhan (Mxz), TianfengB (TfB), Xinfengzhan (Xfz), Zhongkexiwei4 (Zkxw4), Xianliangzhan (Xlz), Tianhuang101 (Th101), Huarun2 (Hr2), Yuewangsimiao (Ywsm), Yueyousimiao (Yysm), shanghaishida (Shsd), Ezhong5 (Ez5), Meitezhan (Mtz), Zhonghua 11 (Zh11) and 9311. OsSTK-OE and ossstk plants were generated in the NIP background, while ossut2 was in shishoubaimiao (SSBM) in this study. The osnomt and its NIP wild-type were as previously described (He et al. 2024). The osnac23 and its NIP wildtype, ostpp1 and its Dongjing (DJ) wild-type were previously described by Li et al. (2022). The germinated plants were transferred to standard rice culture solutions and grown in a greenhouse as described (He et al. 2024; Kong et al. 2021). 

For blast inoculation, the M. oryzae isolate TMC-1 strain was cultured as previously described (Wang et  al.  2023; Zhang et  al.  2024). The 3- to 3.5 leaf stage plants were sprayedinoculated with spores (around $1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } / \mathrm { m L }$ in sterile water with $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween-20) in darkness for the first $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ , followed by a light/dark $( \mathrm { 1 4 h / 1 0 h } )$ cycle at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $8 5 \%$ humidity for 5–7 days. The disease symptoms were evaluated and analysed by ImageJ software. Using the DNA-based quantitative PCR (qPCR), relative fungal growth was analysed by dividing the $C _ { \mathrm { T } }$ value of M. oryzae's 28S rDNA by that of rice genomic ACTIN1 (Zhai et  al.  2022). Two-week-old plants were pre-treated with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ sakuranetin for $0 . 5 \mathrm { h }$ or $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Suc for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , and then inoculated with M. oryzae. 

For mature plants, agronomic traits including plant height, internode length, panicle length, the panicle per plant, seed number per panicle, grain length, grain width, 1000-seed weight and grain yield per plant were recorded. For the yield traits assay, the OsSTK-OE lines and NIP plants were grown in paddy fields in Lingshui, Hainan and Haining, Zhejiang, China. The cultivation period in Lingshui was from December 2023 to April 2024, while in Haining, it was from June to October 2024. The rice seeds were germinated in water at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in darkness for 2 days and then grown in a soil seed bed for 4 weeks before 

transplanting into the paddies. The yield was calculated based on the area $( \mathbf { m } ^ { 2 } )$ in the paddy fields. 

# 4.2 | Sugar Metabolomics Assay and Statistical Analysis

For sugar metabolomics assay, sugar contents were detected and analysed by MetWare (http://www.metware.cn/) using the Agilent 8890-5977B platform. Two-week-old rice cultivars were collected, freeze-dried and then crushed to powder. $2 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of powder was diluted to $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ with methanol: isopropanol: water (3:3:2 V/V/V), vortexed, ultrasound, and then centrifuged at 12 000 rpm under $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The supernatant was further derivatised and analysed by GC/MS. 

For soluble sugar and Suc detection, 2-week-old seedlings and mature seeds of OsSTK-OE, osstk and NIP plants were harvested, and the measurement was performed using the detection kit (D799790 and D799392 Sangon, Shanghai, China) following the manufacturer's instructions. 

# 4.3 | RNA-Seq Library Preparation and Sequencing

The library construction and sequencing were conducted by Novogene (Beijing, China). Two-week-old OsSTK-OE lines and NIP plants treated with or without Scu were collected for total RNA extraction. An RNA sequencing library was prepared and sequenced on an Illumina Novaseq platform and 150 bp pairedend reads were generated. Differential expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 R package (1.20.0). Genes with an adjusted $ { \lVert { \mathbf { o g } _ { 2 } } \rVert }$ (fold change)| $\geq 1$ and $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ found by DESeq2 were assigned as differentially expressed. The cluster Profiler R package was applied to test the statistical enrichment of differential expression genes in KEGG pathways (https://www. genome.jp/kegg/). 

# 4.4 | Total RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR

RT-qPCR was performed to analyse mRNA level changes as described (He et al. 2021). Total RNA was extracted from seedlings and reverse-transcribed to cDNA. RT-qPCR was conducted on a QuantStudio 6 Flex Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, Singapore). RT-qPCR conditions were as follows: $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min; 40 cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s, $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s and $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20 s. OsUBQ5 was used as the internal control, and the transcript level changes were analysed by the comparative $C _ { \mathrm { T } }$ method $( 2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method). At least three biological replicates were used. The primers used in this study are listed in Table S10. 

# 4.5 | Sakuranetin Determination

Measurement of sakuranetin was conducted as previously described (He et  al.  2024). The rice samples were harvested and ground into powder in liquid nitrogen. $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of the powder was infiltrated with 2 mL extraction buffer $\mathrm { ( E t O H { : } H _ { 2 } O { : } M e C N { : } A c O H { = } 7 }$ 9:13.99:7:0.01, v/v/v/v) and incubated for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The mixed buffer was then centrifuged 

at $1 6 0 0 0 g$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and the supernatant was analysed by UPLC-MS/MS (SCIEX, $5 5 0 0 +$ ). Three biological replicates were used, each consisting of at least 10 pooled plants. 

# 4.6 | Vector Construction and Plant Transformation

For OsSTK and OsSUT2 CRISPR/Cas9 mutant generation, the oligo adaptors were annealed from gene-specific sequence pairs (OsSTK: CACCACCCCGCACGATCGCCGGG; OsSUT2: CGGCGGCGGCGCTGGTCCCG-CGG), and inserted into the vector pOs-Cas9 to generate pOs-Cas9-OsSTK and pOs-Cas9-OsSUT2 as described, respectively (He et  al.  2021). For OsSTK-OE plants generation, the OsNOMT ORF was amplified and cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector driven by the 35S promoter fused with a Flag tag (He et al. 2024). The constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and transformed into NIP and SSBM plants, respectively. Positive osstk and ossut2 were confirmed by PCR and sequencing. OsSTK-OE plants were confirmed by RT-qPCR and western blot. The T3 progeny of homozygous lines were used for analysis. The primers used are listed in Table S10. 

# 4.7 | Protein-DNA Binding Assays

For Y1H assay, the OsSTK ORF was inserted into pGADT7-Rec2 to get pAD-OsSTK (prey), and the $0 . 5 { - } 2 ~ \mathrm { k p }$ promoter region of OsNOMT was cloned into the pAbAi vector to get pAbAi-OsNOMTpro (bait) as described (He et al. 2021). The prey and bait were co-transformed into the yeast strain Y1HGold and grown on Leu deficient medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 days. $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ Aureobasidin A (Clontech) was added to select positive clones. The pGADT7-Rec2 empty vector was set as control. Primers are listed in Table 10. 

For the luciferase (LUC) assay, OsSTK was cloned into the pGreenII-62-SK vector to get the effector construct SK-OsSTK, and the promoter region of OsNOMT, OsTPS8, OsPP2C08 and OsPAL4 was inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector to obtain the reporter constructs as described (He et  al.  2021). The reporter and effector were co-transformed into N. benthamiana leaves via A. tumefaciens infiltration. The luminescence was analysed using an imaging system (Lumazone, USA) at $4 4 { - } 4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post infiltration. LUC activities were measured with a Dual Glo Luciferase Reporter Gene Assay Kit using an Infinite M200 PRO microplate reader (Tecan, Austria). The primers are provided in Table S10. 

For TF-centered Y1H assays, we purchased a 7-bp random motif library plasmid from Nanjing Ruiyuan Biotechnology Co. Ltd. The pGADT7-OsSTK+pHIS2-p53, and pGAD53m+pHIS2-p53 (positive control) were transformed into yeast strain 187, respectively. The positive clones were selected on the SD/-His/-Leu/- Trp medium with the concentration series of 3-AT selection media $\left( 0 { - } 1 0 0 \mathrm { m M } \right)$ to identify the concentration to suppress OsSTK self-activation. The motif library plasmid was transformed into the yeast strain harbouring pGADT7-OsSTK, spotted onto SD/-His/-Leu/-Trp medium supplied with 90 mM 3-AT, and then grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 days. Monoclonal colonies were 

selected for sequencing, and the insertion sequences were analysed using PLACE2 and PlantCARE3 to identify known motifs. 

For EMSA, we constructed and transformed the pCold-OsSTK vector in BL21 (DE3) Escherichia coli, then induced and purified the recombinant His-OsSTK proteins using His-beads as previously described (He et al. 2020). The DNA fragment with STKRE was biotin-labelled at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end. The EMSA was conducted with a LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit according to the manufacturer's instructions. The labelled probe (5 pmol) and His-OsSTK protein $( 2 \mu \mathrm { g } )$ were incubated in the binding buffer at room temperature for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Migration of biotin-labelled probes was resolved by $6 \%$ native PAGE, then fluorescence was detected by the ChemiDoc MP imaging system (Bio-Rad). Primers and probe sequences are provided in Table S10. 

For ChIP assay, 2-week-old OsSTKR-OE plants were harvested, pre-treated with formaldehyde and cross-linked as described (He et al. 2021). Anti-Flag (GenScript, A000187) antibody was used for precipitation, with IgG antibody as the negative control. The precipitated cross-linked DNA was purified by an EpiQuik plant ChIP kit, and the ChIP product was analysed by RT-qPCR. The relative fold enrichment was calculated by normalising the Flag-antibody signal to IgG antibody. The promoter of OsUBQ5 was set as a reference. 

# 4.8 | Widely Targeted Metabolomics Assay and Statistical Analysis

To explore the metabolic changes caused by OsSTK overexpression, the widely targeted metabolomics assay was performed as described (He et al. 2024). Two-week-old OsSTK-OE and NIP plants were collected, vacuum freeze-dried and ground to powder for extraction. The detection and analysis of metabolites were performed by Metware (Wuhan, China). Identified metabolites were annotated against the KEGG Compound database (http://www.kegg.jp/kegg/compound/), and annotated metabolites were mapped to the KEGG Pathway database (http://www.kegg.jp/29kegg/pathway.html). 

# 4.9 | Measurement of Photosynthesis

The photosynthesis ability, $P n$ and Ci, was measured in 15-, 30- and 60-day-old OsSTK-OE and NIP plants using an HED-GH40-AB Portable Photosynthesis System according to the manufacturer's instructions. The reference $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration was set to $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l / m o l }$ during the measurements. In addition, the biomass of all seedlings was measured. 

# 4.10 | Subcellular Localization Analysis

The OsSTK ORF was inserted into the pCV-GFP-N vector to create pCV:OsSTK-GFP, as previously outlined (He et al. 2020). The pCV:OsSTK-GFP vector was then introduced into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. The green fluorescence was analysed by the confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica TCS SP5, USA) after infiltration for 40–44 h. 

# 4.11 | Determination of Nutrition and Quality Indicators in Rice Seeds

The measurement of nutrition and quality indicators in rice seeds was conducted using visible spectrophotometry as previously described (Zhao et  al.  2024). Using the commercial kits (Cominbio Co. Ltd., Suzhou, China), the content of total protein (KMSP-2-W), total amino acid (AA-2-W), total flavonoid (HTC-2-W), amylose (ZDF-2-Y) and free fatty acids (FFAZ-2-W) was determined and analysed according to the manufacturer's protocol. The absorbance was measured using a microplate reader (Infinite 200 Pro, Tecan, Switzerland). 

# 4.12 | Population Genetic Analysis

We modified the haplotype classification based on previous reports and made slight adjustments (Zhai et al. 2022). Genomic diversity was analysed using data from the ECOGEMS (https:// venyao.xyz/ECOGEMS/) database. Nucleotide diversity within each 10-kb genomic window (with a 1000 bp sliding step) and the ROD within each 10-kb genomic window (with a 1000 bp sliding step) and the population differentiation parameter FST within a 1-kb window were calculated for the 500-kb region flanking the gene using VCFtools software (v0.1.16). 

To further investigate the natural variation of the OsSTK locus in rice populations, we analysed 356 high-quality assembled rice genomes from the RAPDB (https://rapdb.dna.affrc.go.jp/) database. Haplotypes were classified based on SNPs retrieved from the RAPDB database within the genomic region. Intermediate accessions of Asian cultivated rice were excluded. Haplotypes observed in at least three rice accessions were classified as major haplotypes. A haplotype network was constructed using PopART software. 

# 4.13 | Data Analysis

For differences analysis, the Student's t-test was used when comparing two variables, and ANOVA with Fisher's least significant difference test was applied when comparing three or more conditions. The Pearson analysis was performed by GraphPad Prism 9. A $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ was considered statistically significant.
