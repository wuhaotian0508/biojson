# ABSTRACT

Plants respond to slight increases in ambient temperature by altering their architecture, a phenomenon collectively termed thermomorphogenesis. Thermomorphogenesis helps mitigate the damage caused by potentially harmful high-temperature conditions and is modulated by multiple environmental factors. Among these factors, ultraviolet-B (UV-B) light has been shown to strongly suppress this response. However, the molecular mechanisms by which UV-B light regulates thermomorphogenesis and the physiological roles of the UV-B-mediated suppression remain poorly understood. Here, we show that UV-B light inhibits thermomorphogenesis through the UV RESISTANCE LOCUS8 (UVR8)–CONSTITUTIVE PHOTOMORPHOGENIC1 (COP1)–phytochrome B (phyB)/LONG HYPOCOTYL IN FAR RED 1 (HFR1) signaling pathway. We found that cop1 mutants maintain high levels of active phyB at high temperatures. Extensive genetic analyses revealed that the increased levels of phyB, HFR1, and CRY1 in cop1 mutants redundantly reduce both the level and the activity of PHYTOCHROME INTERACTING FACTOR4 (PIF4), a key positive regulator in thermomorphogenesis, thereby repressing this growth response. In addition, we found that UV-B light inactivates COP1 to enhance phyB stability and increase its photobody number. The UV-B-stabilized active phyB, in concert with HFR1, inhibits thermomorphogenesis by interfering with PIF4 activity. We further demonstrate that increased levels of active phyB enhance UV-B tolerance by promoting flavonoid biosynthesis and inhibiting thermomorphogenic growth. Taken together, our results elucidate that UV-B increases the levels of active phyB and HFR1 by inhibiting COP1 to suppress PIF4- mediated growth responses, which is crucial for plant tolerance to UV-B stress at high temperatures. 

Key words: COP1, high temperature, thermomorphogenesis, phytochrome B, UV-B tolerance, PIF4

# INTRODUCTION

Plants adjust their architecture to adapt to the ever-changing environment. Many dicots, including Arabidopsis thaliana, undergo elongation of the hypocotyl and petiole and exhibit hyponastic leaf growth in response to increases in ambient temperature (Quint et al., 2016; Casal and Balasubramanian, 2019). These morphological changes, collectively termed 

thermomorphogenesis, are thought to enhance leaf cooling capacity, ultimately augmenting plant resilience to heatinduced stress (Crawford et al., 2012).

The basic helix-loop-helix transcription factor PHYTOCHROME INTERACTING FACTOR4 (PIF4) has emerged as a central regulator of thermomorphogenesis (Koini et al., 2009). PIF4 is transcriptionally activated by high temperatures (Koini et al., 2009). The Evening Complex, composed of ELF3, ELF4, and LUX, directly represses PIF4 expression (Nusinow et al., 2011). Hightemperature-mediated phase separation of ELF3 is considered to disrupt Evening Complex formation, thereby inhibiting PIF4 expression (Box et al., 2015; Ezer et al., 2017; Jung et al., 2020). In addition, the stability of the PIF4 protein is known to increase at high temperatures (Kim et al., 2020), leading to the accumulation of PIF4. This accumulation triggers the expression of genes involved in auxin biosynthesis and cell elongation, promoting thermomorphogenesis, which includes the elongation of hypocotyls (Franklin et al., 2011; Sun et al., 2012). While PIF4 plays a primary role in mediating thermomorphogenic responses, a PIF4-independent pathway has also been recently identified (Vu et al., 2021). 

In addition to temperature, several other factors also impact thermomorphogenesis. One such factor is the circadian clock, which influences high-temperature-induced hypocotyl growth through the activity of the TIMING OF CAB EXPRESSION1 (TOC1) protein (Zhu et al., 2016). TOC1 levels peak at the end of the daytime period, directly repressing the transcriptional activity of PIF4 and thus suppressing thermoresponsive growth at dusk (Zhu et al., 2016). Additionally, two growth-promoting hormones, brassinosteroids (BRs) and gibberellic acids (GAs), are essential for thermomorphogenesis (Stavang et al., 2009). The BR-activated transcription factor BZR1 is required for PIF4 to enhance gene expression and hypocotyl elongation, it also positively regulates PIF4 expression (Oh et al., 2012; Ibanez et al., 2018). PIF4 further stimulates BR biosynthesis by directly inducing the expression of BR biosynthetic genes, creating a positive feedback loop that reinforces PIF4 activity in response to rising temperatures (Martinez et al., 2018). Moreover, GA enhances PIF4 activity by promoting the degradation of DELLA proteins, which interact with PIF4 and inhibit its binding to target promoters (de Lucas et al., 2008). Jasmonates (JAs) also play a role in regulating thermomorphogenesis. Elevated temperatures reduce levels of bioactive JAs by triggering JA catabolism, thereby increasing the abundance of JAZ proteins and ultimately promoting growth (Zhu et al., 2021). 

Thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth is also heavily influenced by light conditions. The red/far-red light receptor phytochrome B (phyB) directly interacts with and inhibits PIF4 activity by inducing the degradation of PIF4 protein and preventing its binding to the promoters of target genes (Huq and Quail, 2002; Lorrain et al., 2008; Park et al., 2018). Given that phyB activity is modulated by both temperature and light, it has been proposed to function as a thermosensing molecule in the regulation of thermomorphogenesis (Jung et al., 2016; Legris et al., 2016; Qiu et al., 2019). Consistent with this hypothesis, thermosensitivity of the hypocotyl is reduced in phyB mutants (Jung et al., 2016; Legris et al., 2016). Moreover, blue and ultraviolet-B (UV-B) light strongly suppress thermomorphogenesis (Ma et al., 2016; Hayes et al., 2017). The blue-light receptor CRY1 directly interacts with PIF4 in a blue-light-dependent manner to attenuate its transcriptional activity (Ma et al., 2016). UV-B-activated UV RESISTANCE LOCUS8 (UVR8) represses 

PIF4 mRNA expression and also prevents PIF4 from binding to target genes by stabilizing LONG HYPOCOTYL IN FAR RED 1 (HFR1) (Hornitschek et al., 2009; Hayes et al., 2017). 

A light-signaling component, CONSTITUTIVE PHOTOMORPHO-GENIC1 (COP1), is essential for thermomorphogenesis, as the loss of COP1 function impairs thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth (Delker et al., 2014; Park et al., 2017; Nieto et al., 2022). COP1 is a RING-finger-containing E3 ubiquitin ligase protein previously identified as a repressor of light responses (Deng et al., 1992). One of the targets of COP1 for 26S proteasomemediated degradation is the basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL5 (HY5) (Osterlund et al., 2000). It has been suggested that HY5 is responsible for the impaired thermomorphogenesis observed in cop1 mutants due to its direct binding to the PIF4 promoter, leading to repression of PIF4 expression. Additionally, HY5 interferes with PIF4 by competitively binding to the PIF4 target genes (Delker et al., 2014; Gangappa and Kumar, 2017). 

It was recently reported that COP1 is involved in destabilizing DELLA proteins, and the growth rate of cop1 mutants at high temperatures is partially restored by treatment with GA or della mutations (Blanco-Tourinan et al., 2020). These findings suggest that DELLA proteins contribute to the impaired high-temperature responses in cop1 mutants, likely by inactivating PIF4. In addition, COP1 activity is required for the stabilization of the PIF4 protein (Gangappa and Kumar, 2017), indicating that COP1 promotes thermomorphogenesis by stabilizing PIF4. However, the precise molecular mechanisms by which COP1 stabilizes PIF4 remain unclear. 

As COP1 functions as an E3 ligase protein, it is anticipated to regulate thermomorphogenesis by facilitating the proteolysis of its target proteins. In this study, we investigated the role of multiple COP1 target proteins in high-temperature responses in cop1 mutants through extensive genetic analyses. We found that the high levels of phyB, HFR1, and CRY1 in cop1 mutants redundantly suppress thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth by reducing both the PIF4 protein level and its activity. Furthermore, our study reveals that UV-B light enhances the stability of phyB and increases the number of phyB photobodies (PBs) through the inactivation of COP1. This increased level of active phyB is responsible for the destabilization of PIF4 caused by UV-B. In addition, we demonstrate that the suppression of thermomorphogenesis by UV-B-induced active phyB and HFR1 is crucial for enhancing UV-B stress tolerance. Thus, our work demonstrates that UV-B-mediated disruption of the proteolysis of phyB and HFR1 attenuates thermomorphogenic growth, thereby enhancing UV-B stress tolerance.

PIF4 is a key regulator of thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth and gene expression. Previous studies have shown that both PIF4 mRNA levels and protein stability are reduced in cop1 mutants (Delker et al., 2014; Gangappa and Kumar, 2017). Consistent with these studies, we observed lower endogenous levels of PIF4 protein in cop1-6 mutants compared to the wild type at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ and $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Figure 1C). These observations strongly suggest that decreased PIF4 protein levels are likely responsible for the defects in high-temperatureinduced hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants. 

PIF4 levels are known to be positively regulated by BRs, both transcriptionally and post-translationally (Bernardo-Garcia et al., 2014; Ibanez et al., 2018). However, the exogenous application of brassinolide, an active BR, did not significantly restore PIF4 levels in cop1-6 mutants (Supplemental Figure 1A). Furthermore, even with brassinolide treatment, the hypocotyl growth of cop1-6 mutants remained almost insensitive to high temperatures, although exogenously supplied brassinolide significantly promoted hypocotyl growth at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Supplemental Figure 1B). Additionally, the defect in thermosensitive growth in cop1-6 mutants was not restored by treatment with bikinin, a chemical activator of BR signaling (Supplemental Figure 1C) (De Rybel et al., 2009). These findings strongly suggest that the reduction in PIF4 protein levels in cop1 mutants is not due to attenuated BR signal transduction. 

Both HY5 and ELF3, as target proteins of COP1, are known to inhibit thermomorphogenesis partially by repressing PIF4 expression (Delker et al., 2014; Box et al., 2015; Raschke et al., 2015; Ezer et al., 2017). In our study, the defect in hightemperature-dependent hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants was not significantly restored by the elf3 mutation and was only marginally restored by the hy5 mutation (Figure 1D; Supplemental Figure 2). In addition, the expression of YUC8 and IAA29 was not increased by high temperatures in either cop1-6 or cop1-6;elf3-1 mutants (Figure 1E). These observations are consistent with previous studies reporting thermosensitive hypocotyl growth in cop1-4;hy5 and cop1- 4;elf3-8 mutants (Gangappa and Kumar, 2017; Nieto et al., 2022), implicating the involvement of additional factors beyond HY5 and ELF3 in suppressing high-temperature-induced hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants. 

Interestingly, we observed higher endogenous PIF4 levels in cop1-6;elf3-1 compared to cop1-6 (Figure 1F). However, the absence of thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1-6;elf3- 1 mutants, despite elevated PIF4 levels (Figure 1D and 1F), suggests that COP1 may influence not only PIF4 levels but also its activity. To further investigate the impact of COP1 on PIF4 activity, we generated and analyzed cop1-6;PIF4-OX plants, where the PIF4-MYC transgene is overexpressed under the 35S promoter in the cop1-6 mutant background. Surprisingly, while PIF4 overexpression promoted hypocotyl growth in the wildtype background at both temperatures, it failed to induce hypocotyl growth in the cop1 mutant background (Figure 1G). Moreover, the expression of PIF4 target and high-temperatureactivated genes (YUC8, IAA19, and IAA29) was not induced by high temperatures in cop1-6 and cop1-6;PIF4-OX plants (Figure 1H). Given that previous studies have established that PIF4 protein stability is dependent on COP1 (Gangappa and Kumar, 2017), we measured the levels of PIF4-MYC protein. Our findings indicate that PIF4-MYC protein levels were lower in cop1-6;PIF4-OX plants than in PIF4-OX plants at both $_ { 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ and ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , aligning with previous findings (Figure 1I). However, in cop1-6;PIF4-OX plants, PIF4-MYC protein levels were significantly elevated by high temperature (Figure 1I), despite the observed insensitivity of hypocotyl growth and gene expression to temperature changes (Figure 1G and 1H). These results suggest that COP1 not only influences PIF4 levels but also modulates its activity. 

# phyB and HFR1 redundantly suppress thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants

Both PIF4 activity and levels are shown to be negatively regulated by phyB (Lorrain et al., 2008; Park et al., 2018). A previous study has demonstrated that COP1 directly interacts with phyB to induce its degradation via the 26S proteasome (Jang et al., 2010). In addition, phyB activity and stability are highly temperature-dependent (Jung et al., 2016; Legris et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2020), suggesting that phyB could be a critical factor in suppressing thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants. To test this hypothesis, we measured phyB levels in both the wild type and cop1-6 mutants grown at two different temperatures $( 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Consistent with previous reports, we observed a reduction in phyB levels at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ compared to $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in the wild type (Figure 2A). Similarly, phyB 

levels were also reduced by high temperatures in cop1-6 mutants (Figure 2A), suggesting that high temperatures can destabilize phyB independently of COP1. Notably, basal levels of phyB were significantly higher in cop1-6 mutants compared to the wild type, resulting in significantly higher phyB levels at $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in cop1-6 mutants than at $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the wild type (Figure 2A). 

In response to light irradiation, phyB interacts with various lightsignaling components to form subnuclear compartments known as PBs, which are crucial for phyB-mediated light responses (Chen et al., 2003; Kim et al., 2023). The number and size of PBs are dynamically regulated by light and temperature conditions and generally represent the amount of the active Pfr form of phyB (Chen et al., 2003; Hahm et al., 2020). To determine whether the cop1 mutation affects the levels of active phyB, we quantified the number of PBs in the epidermalcell nuclei of phyB-GFP and phyB-GFP;cop1-6 plants. The overall patterns of phyB-GFP levels in these plants were similar to those of endogenous phyB levels in the wild type (Supplemental Figure 3). Notably, although the number of PBs was reduced in both phyB-GFP and phyB-GFP;cop1-6 in response to elevated temperatures, the overall PB number was significantly higher in the cop1-6 background compared to the wild type at both $_ { 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ and $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Figure 2B). These results suggest that COP1 reduces the number of phyB PBs. 

Given that phyB destabilizes PIF4 and prevents its binding to target promoters (Lorrain et al., 2008; Park et al., 2018), the impaired thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants is likely due to increased phyB PBs at high temperatures. Supporting this hypothesis, overexpression of phyB substantially suppressed high-temperature-induced hypocotyl growth, mirroring the cop1 mutation effect (Figure 2C). To genetically confirm this hypothesis, we examined whether the high-temperature response of hypocotyl growth in cop1-6 mutants could be restored by the inactivation of phyB. The levels of PIF4 protein increased with the phyB mutation at $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 2D), indicating that phyB contributes to the reduced levels of PIF4 protein in cop1 mutants. However, despite having a higher amount of PIF4 protein than cop1-6 mutants at $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , cop1-6;phyB-9 mutants still displayed no hypocotyl elongation response at the elevated temperature, suggesting the involvement of an additional component in this process (Supplemental Figure 4). 

HFR1, an atypical helix-loop-helix protein that forms a non-DNAbinding heterodimer with PIF4, (Hornitschek et al., 2009), has been reported to suppress thermomorphogenesis under blue and UV-B light (Foreman et al., 2011; Hayes et al., 2017). Since HFR1 is also a target of COP1-mediated degradation (Jang et al., 2005), it is possible that HFR1 contributes to the suppression of thermomorphogenesis in cop1 mutants. However, the hfr1 mutation (hfr1-201) alone did not restore thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1-6 mutants (Supplemental Figure 4). This led us to hypothesize that phyB and HFR1 may act redundantly to suppress thermomorphogenesis in cop1 mutants. # CRY1, in conjunction with phyB and HFR1, suppresses thermoresponsive growth in cop1 mutants

Although restoration of hypocotyl growth at high temperatures was observed in cop1-6;phyB-CR;hfr1-201 (cbh) triple mutants, the hypocotyl lengths of these mutants remained substantially shorter than those of the wild type at $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Figure 2E). This suggests that additional COP1-regulated factors may further suppress thermomorphogenesis in cbh mutants. CRY1, a bluelight receptor, interacts with PIF4 and inhibits its transcriptional activation activity, thereby suppressing thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth (Ma et al., 2016). Moreover, CRY1 negatively regulates PIF4 expression (Boccaccini et al., 2020). Recent findings indicate that COP1 destabilizes CRY1 under blue light conditions (Miao et al., 2022). Our data show elevated CRY1 levels in cop1-6 mutants compared to the wild type at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ and $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Figure 2J), suggesting that increased CRY1 levels may contribute to the defects in thermomorphogenesis in cop1 mutants. To test this hypothesis, we generated and analyzed cop1-6;phyB-CR;hfr1-201;cry1-CR (cbhc) quadruple mutants by employing CRISPR-Cas9 to target the CRY1 gene. This intervention introduced a single nucleotide insertion in the third exon of CRY1, leading to a premature stop codon (Supplemental Figure 6), likely eliminating the C-terminal fragment of CRY1 in the cry1-CR mutant. The cry1 mutation significantly enhanced thermoresponsive growth in cbh mutants (Figure 2E), and consistent with this, the expression of PIF4 target genes (YUC8, IAA29, and ATHB2) was more pronounced in cbhc mutants than in cbh mutants under high temperatures (Figure 2F– 2H). In addition, PIF4 protein levels were higher in cbhc mutants compared to cbh mutants at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ and ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 2K). The cry1 mutation also increased PIF4 transcript levels in cbh mutants (Figure 2I), suggesting that the increase in PIF4 protein levels in cbhc mutants is partially due to enhanced PIF4 transcription. Taken together, these results indicate that CRY1, along with phyB and HFR1, contributes to the suppression of thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants. 

# phyB and HFR1 are responsible for UV-B-mediated suppression of thermomorphogenesis

It has been previously reported that UV-B light inhibits thermomorphogenesis by reducing PIF4 mRNA expression and destabi-

lizing the PIF4 protein (Hayes et al., 2017), although the exact mechanisms remain unclear. Consistent with these findings, high-temperature-induced hypocotyl growth and PIF4 target gene expression (YUC8 and IAA19) were substantially suppressed under UV-B light in wild-type plants (Figure 3A; Supplemental Figure 7). Notably, this UV-B-mediated suppression of thermomorphogenesis was compromised in uvr8-6 mutants (Figure 3A). In addition, the high-temperature-induced expression of YUC8 and IAA19 was significantly less affected by UV-B in uvr8-6 mutants compared to the wild type (Supplemental Figure 7), indicating that these UV-B responses are partially dependent on the UV-B receptor UVR8 (Hayes et al., 2017). UVR8 has been shown to induce various UV-B responses through COP1 (Favory et al., 2009). Upon exposure to UV-B, UVR8 becomes monomerized and interacts with COP1, which then induces UV-B responses by preventing COP1 from binding to its target proteins (Favory et al., 2009; Rizzini et al., 2011). Supporting this mechanism, the expression of HY5 and flavonoid biosynthetic genes (CHI and CHS) was insensitive to UV-B in both cop1-4 and cop1-6 mutants, similarly to uvr8-6 mutants (Supplemental Figure 8). Furthermore, the hypocotyl growth of the cop1 mutants at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ was not affected by UV-B (Figure 3B), suggesting that UV-B suppresses thermomorphogenesis through the UVR8-COP1 signaling pathway. 

Based on our findings that phyB and HFR1 accumulate and redundantly suppress thermomorphogenesis in the absence of COP1 activity, we hypothesized that UV-B inhibits thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth by interfering with the COP1-mediated degradation of phyB and HFR1. Previous studies have reported that UV-B stabilizes HFR1 (Hayes et al., 2017). Consistent with this, we observed that UV-B increased HFR1 levels at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Figure 3C). To examine whether UV-B also stabilizes phyB, we investigated changes in phyB levels in response to UV-B. Similar to HFR1, phyB levels increased upon exposure to UV-B at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ and $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 3D). However, this UV-Bmediated stabilization of phyB was not observed in either cop1-4 or cop1-6 mutants (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figure 9). Furthermore, phyB levels were not significantly affected by UV-B in uvr8-6 mutants (Figure 3E), suggesting that UV-B-activated UVR8 might stabilize phyB by preventing COP1 from binding to it. Given COP1’s impact on phyB PB number, we also examined changes in PBs under UV-B. Consistent with our hypothesis, PB number significantly increased under UV-B exposure in wild-type plants at both $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 3F), whereas no change was observed in cop1 mutants (Figure 3F). These results corroborate that UV-B enhances the number of phyB PBs through a mechanism dependent on COP1 activity, thereby influencing the regulatory pathway of thermomorphogenesis.

# UV-B-mediated suppression of thermomorphogenesis enhances UV-B tolerance

The physiological function of UV-B-induced suppression of thermomorphogenesis in contributing to UV-B tolerance has yet to be fully explored. We investigated whether the UV-B-mediated accumulation of active phyB is involved in UV-B acclimation and thereby contributes to tolerance against damaging UV-B levels. We first determined the expression levels of genes involved in flavonoid biosynthesis (CHALCONE ISOMERASE [CHI], CHALCONE SYNTHASE [CHS], and FLAVONOL SYN-THASE1 [FLS1]) and DNA damage repair (PHOTOLYASE1 [PHR1]), which are crucial for UV-B tolerance. The expression of these genes was significantly less induced by UV-B in phyB-9 mutants compared to the wild type at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Figure 5A– 5D). Additionally, we noted that HY5 protein levels, which regulate the expression of these UV-B protective genes, were less elevated by UV-B in phyB-9 mutants than in the wild type (Figure 5E). This suggests that the reduced gene expression in phyB-9 mutants is partially due to decreased HY5 levels. Consistent with the gene expression pattern, UV-B-induced anthocyanin accumulation was also compromised in phyB-9 mutants (Figure 5F). 

We further examined the role of phyB in UV-B acclimation and tolerance by exposing plants that were grown under either weak narrowband UV-B or no UV-B for 4 days at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ to damaging broadband UV-B light. Exposure of wild-type plants to narrowband UV-B (non-damaging UV-B) for 4 days dramatically increased their tolerance to damaging UV-B (Figure 5G). However, the survival rate of phyB-9 mutants showed less improvement through pretreatment with non-damaging UV-B compared to the wild type, consistent with the observed lower accumulation of flavonoids (Figure 5F and 5G). These results indicate that the accumulation of active phyB enhances tolerance against the damaging effects of UV-B radiation at high temperatures, partly by promoting flavonoid biosynthesis. 

Interestingly, phyB-9;hfr1-201 and phyB-9;hfr1-201;cry1 mutants were more susceptible to UV-B stress compared to phyB-9 mutants (Figure 5G). Given the higher PIF4 activity and enhanced thermomorphogenic growth in these mutants compared to phyB-9 mutants, these results suggest a negative impact of PIF4 on UV-B acclimation and tolerance. Supporting this notion, PIF4-OX plants exhibited severe susceptibility to UV-B stress even after pretreatment with non-damaging UV-B (Figure 5G). Furthermore, the reduced UV-B tolerance of phyB-9 mutants was significantly restored by pif4 and pif5 mutations (Figure 5G). 

# UV-B increases phyB to suppress thermomorphogenesis

These findings suggest that the suppression of PIF4 and thermomorphogenesis at high temperatures is crucial for UV-B acclimation, thereby enhancing UV-B stress tolerance. Despite these findings, mutations in hfr1, cry1, or pif did not significantly affect anthocyanin accumulation in phyB-9 mutants under UV-B (Figure 5H). In addition, the constitutive activation of PIF4 did not reduce the UV-B-induced expression of flavonoid biosynthetic genes (CHS and FLS1) or anthocyanin accumulation (Figure 5H–5J). Thus, the negative effects of PIF4 on UV-B tolerance appear to be independent of flavonoid biosynthesis. 

Previous studies have shown that PIF4 promotes auxin biosynthesis and signaling, which are crucial for thermomorphogenesis (Franklin et al., 2011; Sun et al., 2012; Oh et al., 2014). In this study, we observed that elongation of hypocotyls in PIF4-OX plants under UV-B was inhibited by $\pmb { \alpha }$ -(phenylethyl-2-oxo)-IAA (PEO-IAA), an inhibitor of auxin perception (Hayashi et al., 2012) (Figure 5K). Interestingly, defects in UV-B tolerance in PIF4-OX plants were significantly restored in the presence of PEO-IAA (Figure 5L). Consistent with these findings, hypocotyl growth of PIF4-OX plants was inhibited in axr2-1 mutants, where a stabilized form of the AUX/IAA protein constitutively represses auxin signaling (Figure 5M), (Nakamura et al., 2006). Furthermore, PIF4-OX;axr2-1 plants exhibited significantly enhanced UV-B tolerance compared to PIF4-OX plants without an increase in anthocyanin accumulation (Figure 5N; Supplemental Figure 13). Together, these results suggest that PIF4-mediated activation of auxin signaling and its associated thermomorphogenic growth negatively affect UV-B tolerance. 

# DISCUSSION

Previous studies have demonstrated that COP1 positively regulates thermomorphogenesis through PIF4 (Delker et al., 2014; Park et al., 2017). Given that COP1 targets multiple proteins antagonistic to PIF4 for 26S proteasome-mediated degradation, it is likely that these COP1 target proteins suppress thermomorphogenesis in cop1 mutants. However, the specific COP1 targets responsible for this suppression have not been genetically demonstrated until now. In our study, extensive genetic analyses revealed that in the absence of COP1 activity, phyB, HFR1, and CRY1 accumulate substantially, destabilizing and inactivating PIF4, thereby suppressing thermomorphogenesis (Figure 6). Additionally, our evidence supports that UV-B suppresses thermomorphogenesis through the UVR8–COP1–phyB/HFR1 signaling module, playing a critical role in enabling plants to withstand the harmful effects of UV-B at high temperatures (Figure 6).

COP1 directly interacts with PIF3, a protein homologous to PIF4, and this interaction interferes with BRASSINO STEROID-INSENSITIVE2 (BIN2)-mediated PIF3 phosphorylation and degradation in darkness (Ling et al., 2017). While direct interactions between COP1 and PIF4 have not been confirmed, it is known that PIF4, similar to PIF3, undergoes phosphorylation and destabilization by BIN2 (Bernardo-Garcia et al., 2014). This suggests that COP1 might directly interact with and stabilize PIF4, potentially shielding it from BIN2. Previous studies have indicated that COP1 is involved in the degradation of phyB (Jang et al., 2010). In line with this, we observed that phyB protein levels were higher in cop1 mutants compared to the wild type, even at high temperatures. However, a previous study reported that phyB PB formation is not significantly affected in lrb1;lrb2 double mutants, where phyB levels are markedly higher compared to the wild type (Christians et al., 2012). Supporting this hypothesis, we observed that the reduced levels of PIF4 in cop1 mutants were partially restored in cop1-6;phyB-9 mutants and almost completely restored in cbh mutants. Despite these changes, PIF4 mRNA levels at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ remained lower in cbh mutants compared to the wild type, indicating that the increased PIF4 abundance in cbh mutants is partly due to enhanced PIF4 protein stability. Therefore, COP1 appears to stabilize PIF4 protein directly through the proteolysis of phyB and HFR1. Although it has not been previously reported that PIF4 stability is influenced by HFR1, evidence suggests that two homologous proteins of PIF4, PIF1 and PIF5, are degraded via the 26S proteasome pathway following heterodimerization with HFR1 (Shi et al., 2013). Given that HFR1 can also form a heterodimer with PIF4 (Hornitschek et al., 2009), it is plausible that HFR1 may induce the degradation of PIF4 through a similar mechanism that mediates the degradation of PIF1 and PIF5. 

Our results provide genetic evidence that COP1 enhances both the activity and levels of PIF4. Notably, increased PIF4 levels, whether due to the elf3 mutation or PIF4 overexpression, failed to restore thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in cop1 mutants. Consistent with our results, a recent study has shown that the thermomorphogenesis defects of cop1-4 mutants could not be restored by an elf3 mutation (elf3-8) (Nieto et al., 2022). phyB, HFR1, and CRY1, which are target proteins of COP1, directly interact with and inactivate PIF4 through different mechanisms. Specifically, both phyB and HFR1 prevent PIF4 from binding to the promoters of its target genes (Hornitschek et al., 2009; Park et al., 2018), whereas CRY1 represses the transcriptional activation activity of PIF4 (Ma et al., 2016). suggesting that these proteins are responsible for the reduced PIF4 activity in the absence of COP1. 

The conversion of phyB from its active to inactive form, known as ‘‘thermal reversion’’, is temperature-dependent and accelerates at high temperatures, leading to decreased phyB activity (Jung et al., 2016; Legris et al., 2016). Interestingly, we also observed a decrease in phyB levels at high temperatures, suggesting that high temperatures inhibit phyB through both thermal reversion and destabilization. Previous studies indicate that hightemperature-mediated destabilization of phyB is markedly reduced in spaQ mutants, where all four SUPPRESSOR OF PHYA-105 (SPA) proteins are mutated (Lee et al., 2020). These SPAs interact with COP1 and are required for its E3 ligase activity (Hoecker, 2017). Given the observed increase in nuclear COP1 levels at high temperatures (Park et al., 2017; Blanco-Tourinan et al., 2020), COP1, in collaboration with SPAs, likely plays a role in the destabilization of phyB under such conditions. However, similar reductions in phyB levels at high temperatures in both the wild type and two cop1 mutants challenge this hypothesis. This discrepancy could be due to residual COP1 activity in cop1-4 and cop1-6 mutants. Further experiments are required to elucidate the mechanistic basis of the high-temperature-mediated destabilization of phyB. 

UV-B radiation perceived by UVR8 suppresses thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth by inhibiting PIF4 at multiple levels (Hayes et al., 2017). We observed that under UV-B, the stability of phyB and HFR1 increases. UVR8 and COP1 appear to mediate the UV-Binduced stabilization of phyB, as UV-B had no noticeable effect on phyB levels in either uvr8 or cop1 mutants. Additionally, UV-B increased the number of phyB PBs in a COP1-dependent manner, suggesting that UV-B indirectly augments the amount of active phyB through COP1. UV-B substantially impaired thermoresponsive hypocotyl growth in the wild type, and in phyB-9 and hfr1-201 mutants. Notably, this impairment was significantly mitigated in phyB-9;hfr1-201 double mutants. Taken together, our findings suggest that UV-B suppresses thermomorphogenesis via the UVR8–COP1–phyB/HFR1 signaling module. However, the thermosensitivity of hypocotyl growth under UV-B was not fully restored by phyB and hfr1 double mutations, implying that other COP1 target proteins, such as HY5, or COP1-independent factors, likely contribute redundantly to the suppression of thermomorphogenesis under UV-B. 

UV-B has been reported to degrade PIF4 through COP1 (Hayes et al., 2017; Tavridou et al., 2020) but the underlying mechanisms remain poorly understood. We found that PIF4 protein degradation by UV-B is severely attenuated in phyB-9 mutants. Given that phyB directly interacts with and degrades PIF4 (Huq and Quail, 2002; Lorrain et al., 2008; Park et al., 2018), our results suggest that phyB contributes to UV-Bmediated destabilization of PIF4. Similar to PIF4, PIF5 also undergoes COP1-mediated destabilization under UV-B (Sharma et al., 2019; Tavridou et al., 2020). The degradation of PIF5 is also induced by interaction with phyB (Lorrain et al., 2008), suggesting that phyB is also required for UV-B-mediated destabilization of PIF5. 

Plants enhance their UV-B tolerance by inducing the expression of genes involved in flavonoid biosynthesis and DNA damage 

repair. Our results show that UV-B-mediated increases in active phyB levels contribute to flavonoid accumulation at high temperatures, enhancing tolerance against damaging UV-B. Furthermore, we found that UV-B tolerance is negatively correlated with PIF4 activity during UV-B acclimation. The restoration of UV-B tolerance in PIF4-OX plants by PEO-IAA treatment or the axr2-1 mutation suggests that PIF4-mediated auxin activation, and the consequent thermomorphogenic growth, counteract UV-B acclimation, thereby reducing UV-B tolerance. It appears that suppressing thermomorphogenesis is an adaptive mechanism employed by plants to enhance their tolerance to UV-B at high temperatures. Interestingly, this growth-suppression-mediated UV-B tolerance seems to be independent of flavonoid accumulation, as UV-B-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis was not significantly altered by changes in PIF4 activity. Future research will focus on elucidating how the auxin-induced morphological changes compromise UV-B tolerance. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials

For general growth and seed harvesting, A. thaliana plants were grown in a greenhouse under 16 h light/8 h dark cycles at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ All A. thaliana plants used in this study were of the Col-0 ecotype background, except for PHYB-OX, which is in a Ler background (Jeong et al., 2016). The mutant lines cop1-4, cop1-6, phyB-9, hfr1-201, elf3-1, hy5 (SALK_056405), uvr8-6, axr2-1, phyB-9;pif4;pif5, cry1-104, and PIF4-OX have been previously described (McNellis et al., 1994; Hicks et al., 1996; Soh et al., 2000; Nakamura et al., 2006; Sung et al., 2007; Lorrain et al., 2008; Favory et al., 2009; Zhu et al., 2016). The cbh triple mutants were generated by transforming the CRISPR-Cas9 construct pHEE401-UBQ10-PHYB (modified from pHEE401; Wang et al., 2015) into cop1-6;hfr1-201 mutants (Hwang et al., 2021). The cbhc quadruple mutants were produced by targeting the CRY1 locus with the CRISPR-Cas9 construct pHEE401- UBQ10-CRY1 in cbh mutants. Gene-specific primers used for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Growth conditions and light treatment

Seedlings were grown under a light source from cool-white fluorescent tubes with a photon irradiance of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } { \cdot } \sigma ^ { - 1 }$ . UV-B light was supplied by narrowband UV-B lamps (Philips PL-L 36W/01) at a photon irradiance of $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m W } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ . 

# Hypocotyl length measurements

For the analysis of hypocotyl length, seeds were sterilized using $70 \%$ (v/v) ethanol and $0 . 0 1 \%$ (v/v) Triton X-100 and plated on Murashige and Skoog medium (Duchefa Biochemie) supplemented with $0 . 7 5 \%$ phytoagar. Seedlings were grown under specific light and temperature conditions. Hypocotyl lengths were measured using NIH ImageJ software, available at http://rsb.info.nih.gov/nih-image/. 

# Protein extraction and western blot analysis

Total proteins were extracted using a buffer containing 8 M urea, 100 mM Tris-Cl (pH 6.8), $5 \%$ SDS, 1 mM EDTA, $2 \%$ b-mercaptoethanol, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, and $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail. Western blot analysis was performed using specific antibodies: anti-PIF4 antibody (homemade, used at a dilution of 1:1000), anti-MYC antibody (Cell Signaling, 9B11, used at a dilution of 1:5000), anti-phyB antibody (phytoAB, PHY1733, used at a dilution of 1:5000), anti-HY5 antibody (Abiocode, R1245-2, used at a dilution of 1:1000), and anti-CRY1 antibody (used at a dilution of 1:2000) (Song et al., 2014). These antibodies were used to detect the endogenous levels of PIF4, phyB, CRY1, HY5, and PIF4-MYC. 

# Quantitative real-time PCR

Seedlings grown under specified conditions were used to extract total RNA using the MiniBEST Plant RNA Extraction Kit (Takara) following the manufacturer’s instructions. cDNA synthesis was performed using M-MLV Reverse Transcriptase (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Quantitative real-time PCR was conducted on a CFX96 Real-Time PCR detection system (Bio-Rad) using EvaGreen master mix (Solgent). Transcript levels of each gene were normalized to the PP2A gene. The quantitative realtime PCR experiments were carried out in three to five biological replicates, each consisting of over 40 seedlings. Gene-specific primers are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Confocal laser scanning microscopy

Seedlings were fixed in $1 \%$ paraformaldehyde in PBS for 12 min, washed three times with PBS for 5 min each, and stained with a $1 \mu \ g / \mathsf { m l }$ solution of 40 ,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) for 15 min at room temperature. After staining, seedlings were washed once with PBS for 5 min and then mounted on slides, and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark. Measurements of the number of PBs were conducted within 4 days after fixation. Each nucleus of the seedling’s cotyledon epidermal cells was imaged using a Zeiss LSM 700 confocal microscope equipped with a Plan-Apochromat $1 0 0 \times / 1 . 4$ oil immersion objective. Images were captured with a $_ { 3 \times }$ scan zoom, using the following laser filter settings: GFP (excitation at $4 8 8 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , emission from 488 to $5 5 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ ) and DAPI (excitation at $4 0 5 \ \mathsf { n m }$ , emission from 420 to 505 nm). 

# UV-B sensitivity assays

Seedlings were initially incubated at $_ { 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ under continuous white light $( 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \cdot \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \cdot \sigma ^ { - 1 } )$ for 3 days. They were then transferred to a chamber equipped with narrowband UV-B lamps (Philips PL-L 36W/01; $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m w } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ ) and incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 4 days under continuous light. Subsequently, the seedlings were exposed to broadband UV-B light (Philips TL20W/12RS; $0 . 9 \mathrm { \ m w } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ for $1 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Survival rates were measured 5 days after returning the seedlings to white light conditions. 

# Anthocyanin measurement

For anthocyanin extraction, 30 seedlings were immersed in $6 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of $1 \%$ HCl in methanol (v/v) and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark with shaking. Post incubation, $4 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of water and $4 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of chloroform were added to each sample and mixed thoroughly. After centrifugation, the absorbance of the supernatant was measured at $5 3 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ and 657 nm. Anthocyanin concentration was calculated using the formula (A530 – $[ 0 . 2 5 \times \mathsf { A } 6 5 7 ]$ )/fresh weight. 

# Statistical analysis

The exact number of individual plants used for hypocotyl length measurements is detailed in the figure legends. For gene expression analyses via quantitative real-time PCR and anthocyanin measurements, approximately 40–50 individual plants were pooled as one biological replicate, with three to six biological replicates analyzed per experiment. Statistical analyses of the quantitative data were performed using a two-tailed Student’s t-test or one-way ANOVA, conducted using an Excel statistical package.
