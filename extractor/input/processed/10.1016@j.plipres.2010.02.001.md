# a b s t r a c t

The attachment of a sugar moiety to the 3-hydroxy group of a sterol drastically increases the size of the hydrophilic part of the lipid. It is obvious that the glycosylation of a considerable fraction of membranebound free sterols alters the biophysical properties of the membrane. However, the consequences of such changes in the proportions of free sterols and steryl glycosides on the biological functions of a membrane are still unclear. This is the main hurdle in understanding the biological functions of steryl glycosides on a molecular level. The recent cloning of sterol glycosyltransferase genes from plants, fungi and bacteria has enabled genetic approaches to analyze steryl glycoside functions. Down regulation of phytosteryl $\beta$ -glycoside biosynthesis in Arabidopsis thaliana causes several dysfunctions in seed development. Ergosteryl $\beta \cdot$ - glycoside depleted mutants of the yeast Pichia pastoris lose their ability to degrade their peroxisomes by an autophagic mechanism called micropexophagy. In the plant-pathogenic fungus Colletotrichum orbiculare the same defect impairs invasion of the cucumber host plants. Helicobacter pylori, a bacterium colonizing the human stomach, is unable to modulate the host’s immune response when the cholesteryl aglycoside biosynthesis of the bacterium is mutated. These mutants with manipulated steryl glycoside metabolism will inspire further studies with cell biological, biophysical and other methods that will provide us with a mechanistic understanding of steryl glycoside functions.

# 1. Introduction

Most plants and fungi, some animals and a few bacteria synthesize glycosylated steryl derivatives. Surprisingly, the attachment of a glycosyl headgroup to the 3-hydroxy group of the sterol turns this world-famous and interesting molecule into the insignificant steryl glycosides: Are they just some rare and exotic glycolipids with obscure and speculative functions? Indeed, very limited data were available on the biological functions of steryl glycosides. These glycolipids were last reviewed in 1999 (structural aspects, [1]), 1996 (structures and analysis, [2]), 1993 (biosynthesis, [3]) and 1991 (comprehensive review, [4]). In contrast to the regularly reviewed fields of glycerophospholipids, sphingolipids and cholesterol, where scientific progress is rapid, updates are rare in the field of steryl glycosides.

Our motivation to write this review (and maybe your intention to read it) is that the knowledge in this field has recently proceeded extensively. The identification and cloning of several sterol glycosyltransferase genes have opened up the use of genetic approaches to investigate the biological functions of steryl glycosides. Indeed, transgenic organisms with manipulated steryl glycoside metabolism are powerful tools to study their functions and some unexpected phenotypes have already shown up.

In the following, we will review the structural diversity and occurrence of steryl glycosides (but not of steroid glycosides), their biosynthesis by nucleotide sugar-depending sterol glycosyltransferases, their enigmatic intracellular localization and their functions with focus on genetically manipulated organisms: the plant Arabidopsis thaliana, the yeast Pichia pastoris and the bacterium Helicobacter pylori.

We will not review technical aspects regarding extraction, purification and analysis of steryl glycosides. This has been done before [2] and techniques are developing too rapidly. In the near future, the analysis of steryl glycosides should be covered by comprehensive and quantitatively reliable lipidomics technologies.

# 2. What are steryl glycosides and which organisms synthesize them?

# 2.1. Steryl glycosides in vascular plants and in algae

In 1908 a hydrophobic compound, ipuranol, was isolated from the bark of olive tree and from the plant Ipomoea purpurea [5]. Five

years later this compound was identified as sitosteryl D-glucoside by showing that its properties were identical to chemically synthesized sitosteryl D-glucoside (Fig. 1) [6,7]. Another 50 years later an esterified form of phytosteryl glucoside was found in lipid extracts of soybean seeds and potato tubers: steryl 60 -O-acyl-glucoside with palmitic acid (16:0) and linoleic acid (18:2) as the most frequent acyl chains [8]. The majority of the publications on steryl glycosides reports the occurrence of these glycolipids in various plants, describes their structural analysis, and provides data on the enzymes involved in their biosynthesis. All these studies have shown that steryl glycosides (SGs) and acylated steryl glycosides (ASGs) are ubiquitously present in vascular plants. In addition, SGs are frequent in fungi and algae and have been found in some animals and bacteria (Table 1). A profound knowledge of structures builds the basis for the understanding of biological systems. It is therefore justified that we discuss the diversity of SG molecules more thoroughly.

SGs are derivatives of a typical membrane-bound sterol molecule. The sugar moiety is attached to the $3 \beta$ -hydroxy group at the C3-atom of a sterol characterized by a planar sterol backbone made up of four condensed aliphatic rings and a hydrocarbon side chain at C17 (Fig. 1). In addition, there is a double bond between C5 and C6 $\Delta ^ { 5 }$ -sterol) and/or between C7 and C8 ( $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -sterol) in ring B. The C4-atom does not carry methyl groups (4-desmethylsterol). 4,4-Dimethyl- and 4-monomethylsterols, intermediates of sterol biosynthesis, are poor substrates for the plant sterol glycosyltransferases [3,9,10]. Thus, mainly sterol end products and a few intermediates of sterol biosynthesis have been found in glycosylated form in plants [4,11].

Steroid glycosides carry one or more sugar residues at the 3-hydroxy group, at other hydroxy groups in the rings, or at the side chain of a modified sterol backbone. These various modifications result in additional hydroxy groups or N-free/N-containing aliphatic rings, leading to the biosynthesis of saponins, avenacosides or glycoalkaloids just to mention a few out of the legions of steroid glycoside compounds [12].

At present it is not quite clear whether it is justified to distinguish between two pairs of corresponding lipids: free sterols/steryl glycosides, both of which fulfill their functions in biomembranes and steroids/steroid glycosides, both of which fulfill functions as secondary metabolites. Below, we present some hints that this is indeed the case. The multiplicity of SG structures have extensively been reviewed by Wojciechowski [4], Heinz [2] and Kovganko and Kashkan [1]. The latter listed 43 different SGs of plant origin. Therefore, we will only summarize their collection of data.

(1) Sterol heterogeneity: plants contain a variety of end products of sterol biosynthesis. These phytosterols usually only differ in the presence of methyl or ethyl groups at C24, and in the presence and position of double bonds in the rings (mainly ring B) and at the side chain [11]. Usually, the composition of sterols in SG reflects the free sterol composition of the plant [4].   
(2) The sugar and its linkage to the sterol: the most common sugar moiety in plant steryl monoglycosides is the pyranose form of D-glucose attached to the 3-hydroxy group of the sterol in $\beta$ -configuration. Besides this ubiquitous sugar, other sugars do also appear in lower abundance in SGs:

$\beta \cdot$ -D-galactopyranose, $\beta$ -D-glucopyranuronic acid, a-Lrhamnopyranose, $\pmb { \alpha }$ -D-ribofuranose, $\beta$ -D-xylopyranose, and $\pmb { \alpha }$ -D-xylofuranuronic acid.

(3) Number of sugar moieties: as mentioned above, the steryl $\beta$ - D-monoglucopyranoside is the most abundant SG in plants. In addition, diglycosyl or oligoglycosyl sterols have been found in different configurations [1,2,4]: in adzuki beans (Vigna angularis) and rice (Oryza sativa) up to four or five glucosyl moieties linked in gentio- (b-1,6) and cello- (b-1,4) configuration are attached to the sterol backbone, respectively [13,14].   
(4) Acylation of the sugar moiety: SGs may be acylated, usually at the $C 6 ^ { \prime }$ -atom of the sugar moiety with palmitic-, oleicand less frequently with stearic-, linoleic-, and linolenic acid [1,2,4].

In the next section, we will mainly discuss the functions of steryl $\beta$ -D-monoglucopyranoside and its acylated form. However, it is not known whether all molecular SG- and ASG-species present in plants fulfill common functions as a lipid class or whether particular molecular species have specific functions depending on their individual structural details.

SGs and ASGs have been found in many algae as well, although in lower proportions compared to higher plants (Table 1). Structural analyses have been performed in only a few cases reporting either gluco- or galactopyranosyl residues in $\beta \cdot$ -linkage to the sterol backbone.

# 2.2. Steryl glycosides in fungi and some lower eukaryotes

Concerning fungi, the baker0 s yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most intensively studied organism with regard to several aspects of biology. There are rare reports on the presence of SG in this yeast [15,16] and also only few determinations of in vitro sterol glycosyltransferase activity in cell extracts of S. cerevisiae [17– 20]. The amounts of the isolated SG were far too limited for further structural analyses of this glycolipid. Later, a gene encoding an ergosterol- $\cdot \beta$ -glucosyltransferase was identified in S. cerevisiae by its functional expression in Escherichia coli and subsequent determination of in vitro activity with radiolabeled substrates [21]. These results show that S. cerevisiae possesses the capability to synthesize SGs, although the expression of the gene, the activity of the sterol glucosyltransferase and the amount of SG are very low in the cells. Therefore, it is difficult to study the functions of SGs in S. cerevisiae.

However, SGs have been found in many fungi in larger proportions compared to baker0 s yeast (Table 1). Usually a single glucopyranose is attached in $\beta$ -linkage to the fungal sterol (Fig. 1). Differing sugars and ASGs have been found occasionally as well [22,23].

SGs are also present in the two slime moulds Dictyostelium discoideum and Physarum polycephalum (Table 1). In D. discoideum the content of SG varies with the developmental stage: $1 . 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { S G }$ per gram of total lipids were found in 1-day old fruiting bodies and less in other stages. Thus, SG is a quantitatively minor lipid in D. discoideum [24]. SG could not be detected in haploid amoeboid cells of P. polycephalum, but increased to 200 nmoles per gram wet weight in diploid plasmodia [25]. Interestingly, amoeboid cells subjected to heat shock $( 2 4 \to 4 0 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf C } )$ also synthesize amounts of SG in the same range as diploid plasmodia [26]. Since SG was detectable one minute after launching the heat shock, the increase in sterol glucosyltransferase activity was due rather to activation than to increased biosynthesis of the enzyme.

Furthermore, two oomycete species, Phytophthora infestans and Pythium sylvaticum synthesize SG and ASG, respectively (Table 1).

# 2.3. Steryl glycosides in animals

In contrast to the ubiquitous occurrence of steryl glycosides in plants and fungi, there are only few reports on steryl glycosides in animals (Table 1). Cholesteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucoside and its acylated form have been found in the skin of snakes and birds [27,28]. In mammals, cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside has been found in heat shocked cultivated fibroblasts [29,30]. Cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucopyranuronic acid has been isolated from human liver [31–33]. Radiolabeling of lipids in rat brain with $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -mevalonic acid revealed a compound with chromatographic properties similar to cholesteryl galactoside, but the evidence for its structural identity was rather limited [34]. It is not known whether different cholesterol glycosyltransferase activities in mammals are represented by a single or by different enzymes, since no sterol glycosyltransferase from animals has yet been purified nor has a gene encoding such an enzyme from animals been cloned.

# 2.4. Steryl glycosides in bacteria

Sterol biosynthesis is a typical eukaryotic property and only a few bacteria synthesize sterols [35]. However, steryl glycosides and acyl steryl glycosides have been found in some saprophytic or parasitic bacterial species which do not synthesize sterols, but absorb the sterol from their substrate or from cells of their host organisms (Table 1). These bacteria contain UDP-sugar-dependent glycosyltransferases which attach the sugar to the sterol (see Section 3.1). Detailed structural analyses of bacterial SGs have been performed for Acholeplasma axanthum [36], different species of Helicobacter [37–39] and Borrelia [40–42]. Whereas three Borrelia species (B. afzelii, B. burgdorferi, and B. garinii) contain cholesteryl $\beta$ -galactopyranoside and its acylated form (Fig. 1), B. hermsii synthesizes cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucopyranoside and the acylated form. Interestingly, A. axanthum and various Helicobacter strains contain SGs with a glucopyranose attached to cholesterol in the unusual $\pmb { \alpha }$ -configuration [36,39]. Steryl $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -glycoside has been observed only in these two genera so far. A. axanthum also produces an acylated diglucoside, $\pmb { \alpha }$ -Glcp- $\cdot 1  3 ^ { \cdot }$ )-(60 -O-acyl)- $\pmb { \alpha }$ -Glcp- $( 1  3 )$ )-O- $\cdot \beta$ -cholesterol, whereas H. pylori contains the acylated SG and the unique phosphatidyl SG (Fig. 1).

In the following sections we will focus on the ubiquitous phytosteryl $\beta$ -monoglucopyranosides and their acylated forms in

plants, the ergosteryl $\beta$ -monoglucopyranosides of fungi, and the cholesteryl $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -monoglucosylpyranoside derivatives of H. pylori. This is only a small selection of the diverse mono- and oligoglycosylated steryl glycosides present in different organisms, but we regard these SG molecules as a good foundation to start the investigations of their functions.

# 3. Enzymes of steryl glycoside biosynthesis

The first step in the biosynthesis of SG and its derivatives is the conversion of membrane-bound free sterols into SG by NDP-sugardependent glycosyltranferases (Fig. 2). Via three alternative pathways, the SG can be decorated with a fatty acid to form ASG, with additional sugar residues to form steryl di- and oligoglycosides, or with a phosphatidyl residue to form phosphatidyl steryl glycoside. Only little is known about the degradation and turnover of SG and their derivatives. The sparse research shows a rapid turnover of these glycolipids in plants [43,44], and enzymatic activities involved in the hydrolysis of SG or SG derivatives have been reported (Section 3.3).

# 3.1. UDP-sugar:sterol glycopyranosyltransferases

The first studies on the sterol glucosyltransferase (SGT) of plants were published in 1967 and 1968 describing the determination of UDP- ${ \boldsymbol { \mathrm { I } } } ^ { 1 4 } { \boldsymbol { \mathrm { C } } }$ ]glucose:phytosterol $[ ^ { 1 4 } \mathsf { C }$ ]glucosyltransferase activity in homogenates of lettuce leaves and immature soybean seeds, respectively [45–48]. In the following years, many reports on this activity in plants contributed to the characterization of the plant sterol glucosyltransferase and showed that it is a ubiquitous enzyme present in several tissues of all plant species investigated so far. We will not discuss the properties of this glucosyltransferase in detail, since this has been done extensively in former reviews and recent papers [3,4,9]. Therefore, a short summary based on these articles is given.

The plant sterol glucosyltransferase is membrane-bound and can be activated and solubilized by non-ionic detergents such as Triton X-100. The sterol glucosyltransferases from most plants show a strict substrate specificity regarding the sugar nucleotide, with UDP-glucose as the preferred sugar donor. All the cited studies on sterol glycosyltransferases of vascular plants and other organisms in the reviews and in Table 1 are of value for our understanding of steryl glycoside biosynthesis. But they all suffer from a common disadvantage: these studies were performed with crude homogenates, purified membranes, or partially purified enzyme preparations. Such studies are required to understand the biological functions of steryl glycosides in various tissues and different membrane systems of eukaryotic organisms.

Within the last decade the main progress in sterol glycosyltransferase research resulted from the identification of sterol glycosyltransferase genes in eukaryotes as well as in prokaryotes. The breakthrough was achieved in 1994 by the 12,500-fold purification of a membrane-bound sterol- $\cdot \beta \cdot$ -D-glucopyranosyltransferase from etiolated oat seedlings [49]. The pivotal purification step was the extraction of the SGT from a solubilized microsomal fraction with diethyl ether (Fig. 3).

The purified enzyme allowed N-terminal and internal amino acid sequencing and PCR amplification of cDNA fragments, which were used as probes to isolate corresponding full-length cDNAs from libraries of oat and A. thaliana [50]. These sequences were expressed in E. coli and the SGT activities of the recombinant polypeptides were demonstrated by in vitro assays using cell-free extracts of the transgenic E. coli cells supplied with radiolabeled UDP-glucose and cholesterol (Table 2) [50]. The amino acid sequences of the deduced polypeptides showed similarity to some bacterial glycosyltransferases which as far as is known do not use sterols as sugar acceptors. There was also similarity to a putative polypeptide from S. cerevisiae (Ylr189c protein). Alignment of the plant and the baker0 s yeast sequences enabled the PCR-based cloning of additional putative sterol $\beta$ -glucosyltransferase sequences from genomic or cDNA libraries of plants, fungi and of the slime mould D. discoideum [21]. So far, 15 eukaryotic SGTs have been identified by heterologous expression in E. coli or P. pastoris, and for eight of them mutants with inactivated sterol glucosyltransferase genes have been generated (Table 2).

The recent sequencing of the complete genomes of many organisms revealed the existence of several putative sterol glycosyltransferase genes in various eukaryotes. A multiple sequence alignment shows that known and putative SGTs represent a protein family within the glycosyltransferase superfamily 1 (GT1) (Fig. 4). GT1 is also named ‘‘UGT superfamily” (UDP-glycosyltransferase family) and is described in the ‘‘Carbohydrate-Active en-Zymes database” (http://www.cazy.org/) and on the ‘‘UDP Glucuronosyltransferase Homepage” (http://som.flinders.edu.au/ FUSA/ClinPharm/UGT/) [51–53].

The genome of A. thaliana contains two sterol $\beta$ -glucosyltransferase genes in the subfamily Ugt80 that have already been subjected to functional identification (see Table 2 and Section 5.4). This plant also contains one more distantly related sterol glucosyltransferase-like sequence of unknown function (AT5G24750, not shown). Rice (Oryza sativa) has three putative SGTs and the moss Physcomitrella patens has five of the Ugt80 type. A BLAST search [54] at the National Center for Biotechnology Information in 84 genomes of ascomycetes and basidiomycetes revealed the presence of at least one copy of Ugt51 in 78 different fungal species. Thus, SGTs are ubiquitous in fungi, and the absence of SGT genes in the genomes of a few species may be ascribed to the loss of these genes (Malassezia globosa and M. restricta, Candida glabrata and Schizosaccharomyces pombe, S. japonicus and S. octosporus). Interestingly, basidiomycetes and pezizomycotina (filamentous ascomycetes) contain additional SGT sequences of the Ugt53 and Ugt54 subfamilies, respectively. Putative SGTs have also been found in algae, zygomycetes and oomycetes. Homo sapiens, for example, possesses 31 UGT sequences in the families Ugt1, 2, 3 and 8 (http://som.flinders.edu.au/FUSA/ ClinPharm/UGT/), but these sequences show only poor similarity to the SGTs. # 3.2. NDP-sugar:steryl glycoside glycosyltransferases

As mentioned above, plants and A. axanthum synthesize di- and oligoglycosyl sterols, respectively. It seems unlikely that the known sterol glucosyltransferases are responsible for these reactions. # 3.3. Steryl glycoside hydrolases

The presence of a specific SG hydrolase has been demonstrated in mustard (Sinapis alba) seedlings. The enzyme was stimulated by neutral lipids and inhibited by detergents and anionic PGL [64–66]. Another interesting SG hydrolase activity has been found in bean hypocotyls. SG at the apoplastic side of the plasma membrane

# 3.4. Steryl glycoside acyltransferases

# 4. The intracellular location of steryl glycoside and sterol glycosyltransferases is not clear yet

In comparison to free sterols and phosphoglycerolipids, SGs have probably been regarded as less important lipids because of their low amounts in lipid extracts of different organisms. Indeed, it is unlikely that a minor lipid component fulfills bulk functions in a membrane e.g. contributing to the biophysical properties of extended membrane systems. It should, however, be kept in mind that putative specific functions, such as interactions of SG with other lipids or with proteins would not require large amounts of this lipid. Low proportions of SG in total lipid extracts do not rule out that larger proportions of SG, sufficient to fulfill bulk functions, could be present at locally confined membrane sites. Analyses of lipids extracted from whole cells are not adequate to test this hypothesis. Instead, analyses of purified membranes, of microdomains within certain membranes, and of bilayer halves will solve this problem. However, the methods to determine the lipid composition of these subcellular compartments are technically challenging and they still have pitfalls. SG and ASG have been quantified in purified membrane preparations from leaves, roots and protoplasts. The two glycolipids were found mainly in the plasma membrane (PM) and the tonoplast, but also in the endoplasmic reticulum [80]. Interestingly, the proportions of SG and ASG in these membranes differ extremely depending on the plant species and the growth conditions. Uemura et al. [110,219] showed that the PM of A. thaliana leaves contains only $8 \%$ SG plus ASG, $7 \%$ Glc-Cer and large proportions of PGL $(4 7 \% )$ and free sterols (FS) $( 3 8 \% )$ (Fig. 6). In contrast, spring oat (Avena sativa) leaf PM contains only $2 9 \%$ PGL, low amounts of FS $( 8 \% )$ and huge proportions of glycolipids: GlcCer $2 7 \%$ , SG $6 \%$ and ASG $2 7 \%$ . Even more SG and ASG is present in the PM of potato (Solanum tuberosum) leaves, whereas the level of GlcCer is much lower $( 7 \% )$ . Since the biophysical properties of a PM of cells in a certain tissue should be similar in different plant species, how plants meet this demand with such explicitly divergent membrane lipid compositions is still a mystery. On the other hand, this may be a naïve question based merely on our limited understanding of the contribution of each molecular lipid species to the membrane properties and their interplay with membrane proteins.

Two recent studies on detergent resistant membranes (DRM) prepared from plasma membrane of Medicago truncatula roots as well as from PM and Golgi membranes of Allium porrum and A. thaliana showed that SG and ASG are enriched 1.6–3.4-fold in DRM (Fig. 7) [81,82]. These data suggest that SG and ASG may be present

in laterally separated lipid microdomains within these membranes (see also Section 5.1). No data are available on the distribution of SG and ASG between the two lipid monolayers of a biomembrane. Since an asymmetric distribution would not be a surprise, it could be concluded that regardless of the minor proportions of SG and ASG in total lipid extracts, it seems very likely that the proportion of these glycolipids in certain membrane microdomains clearly exceeds those of PGL. Thus, bulk functions as well as specific functions should be considered in further studies on the biological functions of these membrane glycolipids. Since intracellular localization is obviously crucial for the function of steryl glycosides, more detailed quantitative analyses are required concerning purified membranes, membrane domains and membrane monolayers of plants, fungi and bacteria.

The data on the intracellular localization of SG and ASG are in agreement with the studies on the localization of the SGT in plant membranes. However, as mentioned above, these data cannot be assigned to each of the two or three SGT isozymes present in plants. Preliminary experiments with yellow fluorescent protein labeled SGTs of A. thaliana showed that they localize to the ER (Ugt80B1) and the cytoplasm (Ugt80A2) [83]. For S. cerevisiae, two independent global analyses of protein localization revealed cytoplasmic localization of the sterol glucosyltransferase [84,85]. Localization of an SGT in the ER is coherent with the experiments on the subcellular location of SGT activity mentioned above. In contrast, localization of the other SGTs in the cytoplasm is surprising, since one of the two substrates of the enzyme, the hydrophobic sterol, is located in membranes. This presumes at least temporary contact of the active site of the enzyme with the membrane. The transfer of the sugar residue from the soluble UDP-glucose to the 3-hydroxy group of the sterol at the surface of the membrane may require only superficial contact of the enzyme with the membrane. Since the SGT recognizes at least some parts of the planar sterol backbone, the enzyme probably dips into the membrane to some extent.

It is obvious that more detailed studies on the localization of SGTs are necessary which could be supported by previous in silico analyses of their amino acid sequences. Therefore, a comparison of the sequences of the five Ugt subfamilies has been performed from which at least one member has been identified as a sterol glycosyltransferase: Ugt51, Ugt52, Ugt53, Ugt54, Ugt80 (Fig. 8). Interestingly, none of the SGTs contains any predicted transmembrane domains. A second surprise was that the lengths and the domain organization differ significantly between the various subfamilies: about 500–600 amino acids for Ugt80 (plants), about 1200–1600 amino acids for Ugt51 and Ugt53 (all fungi and basidiomycetes, respectively), about 1700 amino acids for Ugt52 (D. discoideum) and about 800–1400 amino acids for Ugt54 (filamentous ascomycetes). These differences are significant, since the catalytic domain of all the sequences has a length of about 400 amino acids. The plant SGTs (Ugt80) and the fungal SGTs (Ugt51) contain short or long N-terminal extensions (about 150 and 700 amino acids, respectively) which are dispensable for the catalytic activity [21,50]. # 5. Biological functions of steryl glycosides

Prior to the availability of genetic approaches, mainly physiological studies were performed to elucidate the biological functions

of steryl glycosides. Most of these studies show a correlation between alterations in physiological or morphological parameters and changes in the amount/proportion of steryl glycosides in the respective organism. Despite careful execution, no causal connection could be drawn between SGs and any biological phenomenon on a molecular level. Nevertheless, the hypotheses on SG functions derived from these analyses will be discussed in the Sections 5.1– 5.3, since they pointed out interesting directions for future investigations. The last three Sections 5.4–5.6 will be dedicated to sterol glycosyltransferase gene inactivation studies. These genetic approaches do not unambiguously elucidate a causal link between SG and any biological phenomenon either, but the interesting phenotypes that have been found provide a basis for further studies on causal linkages. Such analyses will combine genetic, biochemical, cell biological, biophysical and other methods to describe the functions of SGs on a molecular level and to prove or disprove the hypotheses outlined on the next pages.

# 5.1. Biophysical properties of steryl glycosides differ from those of free sterols

It is obvious that the attachment of a sugar moiety to the 3-hydroxy group of a sterol increases the size of the hydrophilic headgroup of the lipid and thus changes its biophysical properties. In order to elucidate the biological functions of SGs, it therefore seems self-evident to compare the impact of free sterols and their corresponding glycosides on the properties of biomembranes or artificial lipid bilayers. There are, however, only very few studies about this issue (Table 3).

By observing the ethanol-induced leakage of electrolytes from barley roots, one study found that exogenously added cholesterol and campesterol reduced the permeability of biomembranes, while cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside, sitosterol, and stigmasterol did not. This suggests that the presence of a sugar moiety at C3 and a bulky ethyl branch at C24 may nullify the effect that cholesterol would otherwise have [104].

Cholesterol and sitosterol eliminate the thermotropic transition of a lamellar gel phase to a lamellar liquid-crystalline phase $( \mathrm { L } _ { \beta }  \mathrm { L } _ { \alpha } )$ in PGL bilayers resulting in rather constant membrane properties over a large temperature range. However, this effect depends on chain length and unsaturation of the PGL (and additional components in more complex systems). In dipalmitoyl lecithin bilayers, the efficacy of ASG in eliminating the phase transition is similar to that of sitosterol, whereas SG only reduces the peak height of endothermal transition [105]. In bilayers composed of palmitoyl oleoyl phosphatidyl choline (POPC), sphingomyelin (PSM) and sterols, cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside and cholesteryl $\beta \cdot$ -galactoside show higher propensity than free sterols to eliminate the phase transition at a low temperature $( 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ [106].

Both cholesterol and SG reduce bilayer fluidity at temperatures above the $\mathrm { L _ { \mathrm { \{ \} } } } _ { } .$ -to-La phase transition (condensing effect), although free sterols are much more efficient than SG [107]. Both increase fluidity at temperatures below the $\mathrm { L _ { \beta } }$ ${ \mathrm { L } } _ { \mathrm { \scriptsize { \beta } } } { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { t } } 0 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { L } } _ { \alpha }$ phase transition. There are, however, differences depending on the lipid composition of the bilayer [106]. Whereas the effects of free sterol and SG are similar in POPC bilayers, they differ significantly in sphingolipid bilayers (PSM or PSM:galactosylceramide). Together with data on the lateral phase separation, the data were interpreted as follows: free sterols interact with both POPC with disordered hydrocarbon chains and sphingolipids with tightly packed carbon chains. SGs with their larger headgroups compared to free sterols, are also able to integrate into the POPC bilayer which has a moderate lateral packing density. In contrast, SG shows a lower tendency than free sterols to interact with a sphingolipid bilayer that has a high lateral packing density. There are no indications for sugar–sugar interactions between SG and GalCer [106]. With regard to these results,

it is surprising that both the sphingolipid GlcCer and SG are enriched in DRMs (Fig. 7). This discrepancy shows the difficulties in interpretations of such experiments with regard to the role of SG in biological membranes.

A study on the lyotropic and thermotropic transition of a lamellar liquid-crystalline to an inverted hexagonal $( \mathrm { L } _ { \mathsf { \Phi } }  \mathrm { H } _ { \mathrm { I I } } )$ phase showed that ASG more efficiently promoted this phase transition compared to free sterol [108]. The impact of this effect on freezing resistance of plants will be discussed below.

Physico-chemical properties of liposomes containing SG have been investigated to explore their use for drug delivery, reviewed by Maitani et al. [109], and will not be repeated here.

Although the impact of SG on the biophysical properties of artificial bilayers is expected to be of particular importance for the biological functions of SG, few conclusions have been drawn from the experiments described above to support the interpretation of physiological phenomena which correlate with SG (see below). The main obstacle is that there are still basic technical problems in biophysical methods when analyzing natural biomembranes with a complex lipid mixture and inserted membrane proteins.

# 5.2. Steryl glycosides and stress

# 5.2.1. Freezing stress and cold acclimation

Many plants suffer from temperatures below $0 ^ { \circ } C$ and several of the freeze-induced lesions, as well as mechanisms of acclimation to avoid these damages, are realized at the PM. Both the damage and the acclimation are very complex phenomena which cannot be covered in this review. We will focus on one single component involved in freezing stress: ASG. After formation of extracellular ice, which has a very low water potential, one major threat for the life of the cell is the severe dehydration of the protoplast (e.g. to $2 0 \%$ of its volume at $- 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Thus, PM and subtending endomembranes are brought into close apposition resulting in the formation of inverted hexagonal $\left( \operatorname { H } _ { \operatorname { I I } } \right)$ phase. The incidence of $\mathrm { H } _ { \mathrm { I I } }$ phase correlates with lethal injury of the protoplast, probably due to leakage of intracellular contents. It was found that lipids with low head group hydration, such as free sterols, GlcCer and ASG promote the approach of membranes and the formation of $\mathrm { H } _ { \mathrm { I I } }$ phase [108]. Interestingly, ASG is much more effective than free sterols in inducing the phase transition. This data may explain why winter rye leaves, which contain a low proportion of ASG in the PM of their cells, are much more resistant to freeze-induced lesions than spring oat leaves which contain a large proportion of ASG in the PM. Both species increase their resistance to freezing stress in a 4 weeks acclimation process at low temperatures above zero degrees, but the high proportion of ASG in spring oat only slightly decrease. Instead, acclimation in oat induces different lipid alterations in the PM that decrease the propensity of the bilayer to undergo the $\mathrm { L } _ { \mathfrak { X } }$ to $\mathrm { H } _ { \mathrm { I I } }$ phase transition. However, acclimated oat does not reach the freezing resistance of acclimated rye [108,110,111]. A similar observation relating to differing freezing resistances was made with Solanum species. A freezing-tolerant, cold-acclimating wild potato species (Solanum commersonii) and a freezing-sensitive, nonacclimating cultivated species (Solanum tuberosum) both contain large proportions of ASG in the PM of leaves. Thus, properties of other lipids in the PM compensate for the high proportion of ASG at least in S. commersonii [112]. Although ASG represents a risk factor at subzero temperatures, its proportion is not reduced in the course of cold acclimation in three species with extraordinarily high ASG content. The physiological advantages provided by high proportions of ASG in the PM are still unknown.

# 5.2.2. Heat shock

Cells from some organisms subjected to heat shock show a rapid increase of SG biosynthesis: the slime mould Physarum polycephalum [26], the yeast P. pastoris [114], and cultured human fibroblasts [29]. In addition, cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside exogenously added to human fibroblasts activate the heat shock transcription factor 1 to bind to a heat shock element in front of the heat shock protein 70 gene, inducing its transcription [30]. It would be interesting to identify the enzymes that are responsible for SG biosynthesis in P. polycephalum and humans and the corresponding genes for further studies on the role of SG in heat stress response.

# 5.3. Effects of exogenously applied steryl glycosides in animals and humans

# 5.3.1. Immune function

In 1996, Bouic and colleagues already suggested an effect of sitosterolin (a mixture of sitosterol/sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside) on the behaviour of T-helper cells [115]. By measuring $^ 3 \mathrm { H \cdot }$ -thymidine incorporation after $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ of phytohemagglutinin stimulation, they found that T cell proliferation was increased. More importantly, the concentrations of secreted TH1 cytokines IL-2 and IFNc also increased by $2 0 \%$ and $3 8 \%$ , respectively. T-helper 1 $\left( \mathrm { T _ { H } } 1 \right)$ cells produce these cytokines in response to antigen presenting cells, (APC)-derived cytokines IL-12 and IL-18, and by direct cell-to-cell contact with APCs [116–118]. The effect of the TH1 cytokines is an activation of macrophages, which play an important role during intracellular infections. Besides TH1, there are also T-helper 2 $( \mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { H } } 2 )$ cells, T-helper 17 $( \mathrm { T _ { H } } 1 7 )$ cells and $\mathrm { C D } 4 + / \mathrm { C D } 2 5 + +$ and Foxp3 + regulatory T cells, which all have their own role in host defense. $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { H } } 2$ cells produce the cytokines IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, which activate mast cells and eosinophils, and stimulate B cells to produce IgE and are therefore often related to hypersensitivity reactions. Normally, $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ and $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { H } } 2$ cell activity is well balanced, whereas a skewed response in either direction seems associated with disease. That is, $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ over-activation is associated with autoimmune diseases and inflammatory diseases, whilst $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { H } } 2$ over-activation seems causally related to allergies and asthma [116]. A similar condition is observed, for example, in human immunodeficiency virus patients in whom the decreased activity of $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ cells, also resulting in a shift towards a $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 2$ dominant immune response, leads to an impaired host defense [119]. Regarding the $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1 / \mathrm { T _ { H } } 2$ balance, evidence has accumulated that the regulatory T cells play a major role in maintaining this balance. A highly relevant but yet unsolved question is how to explain the effects of the sitosterolin mixture in the abovementioned experiments. It should be acknowledged that the physiological serum concentration of sitosterol in healthy subjects is approximately $6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [120], whereas the amount of sitosterol supplied to the cells was only 2.4 nM. Therefore, a more likely explanation is that the sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside present in the mixture was responsible for modulating the immune response. This can also be deduced from the study by Lee et al. [121]. They showed that mice receiving $4 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside intraperitoneally twice during a three day interval before introducing a Candida albicans infection had a $6 0 \%$ survival rate, whereas all control mice died. Moreover, splenic lymphocytes from sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucosidetreated mice that were stimulated with mouse anti-CD3 MAb for $4 8 \mathrm { { h } }$ ex vivo produced five times more $\operatorname { I F N } \gamma$ and four times more IL-2 than lymphocytes from control mice not receiving sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucoside. Additional experiments showed that administering an anti-mouse $\operatorname { I F N } \gamma$ antibody to the sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside-treated mice completely blunted the survival after the C. albicans infection, suggesting that indeed the sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucoside-induced $\mathrm { I F N } \gamma$

production contributed to the observed protection. This clearly demonstrated that sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucosides might induce a $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ shift in the in vivo situation, at least in animal models.

In a human study of 23 people with pulmonary tuberculosis, daily consumption of capsules containing $2 0 \mathrm { m g }$ sitosterol and $0 . 2 \mathrm { m g }$ sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside three times a day as add-on treatment to regular treatment elevated lymphocyte proliferation and promoted general recovery [122]. A 12 weeks consumption of the sitosterol/sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside mixture showed beneficial effects in 24 atopic subjects, both in terms of reduced IgE concentrations as well as symptom scores via questionnaires [123]. From these in vivo studies, it is difficult to assign responsibility of the effects to either the sitosterol or the sitosteryl $\beta$ -glucoside. However, considering the fact that the average daily intake of plant sterols in western countries is 300–400 mg/day [124], it seems unlikely that an addition of $6 0 \mathrm { m g }$ sitosterol was responsible. Whether this indicates that the effects are mainly caused by the $0 . 6 \mathrm { m g }$ sitosteryl $\beta$ - glucoside the subjects received daily is debatable, but certainly needs further study.

In this respect, it is important to consider bioavailability of the steryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides. In terms of digestion, Moreau and Hicks [125] reported that acyl steryl $\beta$ -glycosides are hydrolyzed to steryl glycosides by mammalian digestive enzymes, but that the glycoside linkage is not further hydrolyzed. This might suggest that the steryl glycosides, if any, should be absorbed intact. However, feeding rats $[ { } ^ { 1 4 } \mathsf { C } ]$ ]sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucoside for 4 weeks clearly indicated that absorption is negligible since there was only a small amount of radioactivity from the parent molecule in the stomach and intestine tissues, and no radioactivity at all in other organs. Most of the radioactive label was found in the feces, of which a part was present in the metabolite coprositostanol [126]. Similarly, other studies reported that the uptake of sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucoside in rats is approximately $1 - 2 . 5 \%$ [127]. Data from a completely different research area, i.e., development of new drug delivery systems, confirms the low absorption of steryl $\beta$ -glucosides. For example, peptide drugs as part of steryl $\beta$ -glucoside-containing particles can be targeted towards the colon, where the steryl $\beta$ -glucosides act as local absorption enhancers [109]. This effect is due to the physicochemical properties of the steryl glucosides among which low intestinal absorption is important. In contrast, Seki et al. described absorption of sitosteryl $\beta$ -D-glucopyranoside from the intestinal tract of rats via incorporation into chylomicrons, hereby entering lipoprotein metabolism [128]. Data describing uptake of sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides in humans is lacking.

Altogether these results suggest that sitosteryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides can shift the $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1 / \mathrm { T _ { H } } 2$ balance towards a more $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ dominant response. This may explain the beneficial immune modulating properties of these compounds in men and mice. However, exploring a possible mechanism to explain these findings as well as the aspect of low intestinal absorption warrants further study.

# 5.3.2. Neurodegeneration

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis-parkinsonism dementia complex (ALS-PDC) is a neurological disorder with features of parkinsonism. It has been suggested that there might be a link between ALS-PDC and the consumption of cycad (the seed of Cycas circinalis) a traditional staple of the indigenous diet in Guam [129]. In this seed, three steryl $\beta$ -glucosides have been identified in vitro as potential neurotoxins that may play a role in neurodegenerative disorders in vivo [129,130]. Indeed, feeding washed cycad flour results in symptoms that mimic ALS-PDC [131]. In addition, certain bacteria that are associated with some forms of neurodegenerative disorders have the capacity to synthesize steryl glycosides [130]. Further work in this direction resulted in speculations that cholesteryl $\pmb { \alpha }$ -glucosides present in H. pylori are responsible for the link between H. pylori infection and parkinsonism [132].

However, the structural differences between steryl $\beta \cdot$ - and $\pmb { \alpha }$ -glucosides may also influence their physiological effects. Wunder et al. showed in a series of experiments that cholesteryl $\pmb { \alpha }$ -glucoside protects H. pylori from phagocytosis by macrophages whereas cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside, just like cholesterol itself, promotes phagocytosis of H. pylori by macrophages [133]. By exploiting the capacity to form $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -glucosides from cholesterol, H. pylori protects itself from being phagocytosed and presented by APCs, hereby avoiding consequent T cell responses. Although the effects of aversus $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides on T cell activation were not compared, it is very likely that the $\beta$ -glucosides do not induce avoidance of T cell responses whereas the $\pmb { \alpha }$ -glucosides do. Therefore, assumptions that effects of a- and $\beta$ -glycosides are exchangeable is not correct and needs to be explored for each component individually. The observation that the $\beta$ -glucosides enhanced phagocytosis of H. pylori is in line with the $\mathrm { T _ { H } } 1$ immune stimulating effects described by Bouic and colleagues [115]. Despite the fact that effects of steryl glycosides on T cells seem to be partly understood, the mechanism underlying neurodegeneration is still largely unknown. Ly et al. [134] have, however, shown that cholesteryl $\beta$ -glucoside treatment stimulated the survival of protein kinase B in motor neuron-derived cells (NSC34 cells) in terms of a 2-fold increase in phosphorylation at the Ser-473 activation site, which also suggests toxicity. Despite the low or negligible absorption of SGs, there are different indications suggesting neurotoxicity of these compounds.

# 5.3.3. Intestinal absorption of cholesterol

Plant sterols are cholesterol-like compounds that are well known for their effects on cholesterol metabolism, and as such, are incorporated into a wide range of functional foods with the aim to improve lipoprotein profiles of consumers. The mechanism underlying the beneficial effects on serum low density lipoprotein cholesterol concentrations is a reduction of intestinal cholesterol absorption. Lin et al. now showed that a mixture of plant steryl glycosides isolated from soy lecithin lowers intestinal cholesterol absorption [135]. Eleven healthy volunteers each consumed a breakfast enriched with plant steryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides $( 3 0 0 \mathrm { m g } )$ , plant sterols $\left( 3 0 0 \mathrm { m g } \right)$ or control in a random order. Both plant sterols as well as plant steryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides significantly lowered intestinal cholesterol absorption compared to the control, as measured by plasma enrichment of $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -cholesterol, which was included in the test meals. These findings are in line with data already presented in 1994 by Tateo et al. [136], showing that diets enriched with steryl $\beta \cdot$ -glucosides from soybean oil fed for 3 weeks suppressed intestinal cholesterol absorption in male Wistar rats. In contrast to effects of SG on immunefunction and their possible role in neurodegeneration, in which the possible negligible absorption is a relevant point of debate, this is not relevant in explaining effects of SG on intestinal cholesterol absorption. Lowering absorption of cholesterol from the intestinal lumen into the circulation does not require the active component itself to be absorbed into the circulation. Effects of these compounds are most likely localized within the intestinal lumen or even within the enterocytes, however, not necessarily in the circulation.

# 5.4. Impairment of sterol $\beta$ -glucosyltransferase activity results in developmental defects of A. thaliana seeds

Although SGs are abundant glycolipids of plants, very little was known about the biological functions of these lipids in plant membranes until recently. Thus, it was an exciting observation that SGs serve as precursors for cellulose biosynthesis in cotton (Gossypium arboreum) fiber [63]. In cell-free membrane preparations of cotton fiber the biosynthesis of SG and steryl cellosides with 2–4 glucosyl residues correlated with the biosynthesis of cellulose. Expression of a putative subunit of cellulose synthase, CesA-1 from cotton,

in yeast enabled the in vitro synthesis of steryl cellosides from SG and UDP-glucose by yeast membrane preparations. Mixing of these yeast membranes with cotton fiber membranes enhanced the in vitro cellulose synthesis. These results and additional experiments lead to the hypothesis that SG synthesized in the PM of cotton fiber cells serves as a substrate for further glucosylation steps by CesA-1 leading to steryl cellosides with up to four sugar residues in $\beta 1  4$ linkage. These steryl cellosides must ‘‘flop” to the outer face of the PM by an unknown mechanism or supported by an unknown transporter protein. Then steryl cellosides get cleaved probably by Korrigan cellulase and the released glycan chain serves as a primer for cellulose biosynthesis by elongating subunits of the cellulase complex [63,137].

This hypothesis was tested using A. thaliana mutants impaired in SG biosynthesis [113]. Two homozygous T-DNA insertion lines with either inactivated UGT80A2 or UGT80B1 SGT genes were isolated and a double mutant generated by crossing of the single mutants. The single mutants and the double mutant showed severely reduced amounts of SG and ASG in all investigated tissues (leaf, stem, inflorescence/siliques). There was, however, a small residual amount of SG present in all tissues. Three possible activities may be responsible for this surprising result: first, a so far unknown UDP-glucose-dependent SGT with low sequence similarity to UGT80A2 or UGT80B and second, the plant UDP-glucose-dependent glucosylceramide synthase, which is also capable of synthesizing SG in vitro. The latter has been demonstrated with the gene from cotton expressed in P. pastoris [138]. Until now, a homozygous A. thaliana T-DNA mutant deficient in the glucosylceramide synthase gene has not been generated, which hints to an essential function of GlcCer in Arabidopsis. The third possibility is an enzyme located at the apoplastic side of the plasma membrane from bean hypocotyls that synthesizes glucosylceramide in vitro using SG as the glucosyl donor [67]. It could be possible that this enzyme also catalyzes the reverse reaction. Whether the SG-dependent or the UDP-glucose-dependent glucosylceramide synthases contribute to SG biosynthesis in vivo needs to be investigated.

The Arabidopsis double mutant with the reduced content of SG and ASG did not show a phenotype that one would expect from a plant with impaired cellulose biosynthesis [113]. No significant alterations were found regarding the cellulose-content of different tissues and sugar composition of cell wall polysaccharides. These unexpected results may be explained by four different hypotheses: SG is not involved in cellulose biosynthesis in A. thaliana; residual amounts of SG are sufficient to enable cellulose formation; alternative molecules can compensate for SG as primers of cellulose biosynthesis; or SG acts as the predominant primer only in some plant species or in tissues with high rates of cellulose formation, such as developing cotton fiber.

Detailed analyses of the Arabidopsis mutants revealed defects in seed development [113]. This phenotype was more pronounced for the ugt80b1 mutant (and the double mutant) than for ugt80a2, suggesting that the functions of the two isozymes are different, but partially overlapping. This assumption is consistent with the distinct expression of UGT80A2 and UGT80B1 determined by promoter studies using the GUS reporter gene. Further experiments with the double mutant also showed major defects in seed morphology, seed coat integrity and embryogenesis (Table 4). The interpretation of these analyses leads to the hypothesis that SG or ASG are required for efficient intracellular trafficking of lipid polyester precursors or for their export into the apoplast in Arabidopsis seeds. Impairment of these functions results in the accumulation of material in the cytoplasm (probably suberin, wax, cutin), lack of the cuticle layer that covers the seed coat in wild type plants, reduction of suberization at the hilum region of the seed, and reduction of various monomers of lipid polyesters (determined after hydrolysis of the polymers) (Fig. 10 and Table 4). These defects in the for-

mation of lipid polyesters may explain the reduced pigmentation of the seed coat, the transparent testa phenotype and in particular the increased permeability of the seeds for tetrazolium salt. It is not clear yet whether these defects cause the further dysfunctions in seed development that have been detected. The cell elongation in different stages of cotyledon development is effected and sinapate derivative accumulation around the hydatode at the tip of the cotyledons is unusual in the mutants. As mentioned above, these defects do not block the development of the seedlings which look similar to wild type plants in later stages.

The impairment of transport of lipid polyester precursors in the differentiating integuments of the seed coat seems to be the primary phenotype of the double mutant. Interestingly, the authors do not report any deficiencies regarding deposition of extracellular lipids in other plant tissues such as leaf epidermis or the casparian strip of the endodermis. This may point to the prevalence of different mechanisms for lipid export in various tissues. Previous studies showed that ABC transporters and other proteins are involved in the translocation of lipids across the PM. The ABC protein Cer5 (At1g51500) supports the export of cuticular wax in epidermal cells of leaves and the stem of A. thaliana [139]. Different proteins are required for the export of glycolipids in heterocyst development in the cyanobacterium Anabaena [140,141]. However, a putative interplay of membrane lipids and proteins in the transport of lipids across the PM is still enigmatic. Thus, the deficiency in SG and ASG may directly affect lipid trafficking or an interaction with proteins that are involved in the export of the lipid polyester precursors. Since different hydrophobic, extracellular layers are crucial for plant life, further studies will focus on the interplay between lipids and proteins in transport and export of lipids and lipid precursors.

It should be mentioned that the transparent testa phenotype has also been observed in mutant plants defective in a PM $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ -ATPase [142]. Interestingly, SG stimulated and ASG reduced the activity of a tonoplast $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ -ATPase in cultured rice cells [143,144].

The Arabidopsis mutants impaired in SG biosynthesis will support new efforts for detailed studies of the biological functions of the two SGT genes and of SG and ASG in plants.

These degradative organelles with strong hydrolytic activity are vacuoles in yeasts and lysosomes in mammalian cells [146].

5.5.1. Ugt51B1 is essential for micropexophagy in the yeast P. pastoris

P. pastoris is able to grow on methanol as the sole carbon and energy source [149]. In a culture medium with methanol, this methylotrophic yeast increases the number and the size of the peroxisomes, which produce the enzyme alcohol oxidase (Aox) required for methanol catabolism [150]. When glucose is added to the medium, the yeast switches to glucose metabolism and large peroxisomes are no longer necessary and degraded by micropexophagy [151,152] (Fig. 11B).

Micropexophagy is characterized by the invagination of the vacuolar membrane and formation of vacuolar lobes which engulf peroxisomal clusters. A newly formed membrane system, the micropexophagic apparatus (MIPA), is required to completely envelop the peroxisomes. Finally, they get lyzed through the activity of hydrolytic enzymes in the vacuole (Fig. 11B). In this context it should be mentioned that the membrane of the peroxisome-containing vesicle, which is subjected to degradation in the vacuole, emerged from the vacuolar membrane. Therefore, it is surprising that the vesicle, but not the vacuolar membrane become lyzed. This contradiction could be solved by the postulate that the vesicle membrane differs from the vacuolar membrane. This difference could be due either to lateral segregation of lipids and/or proteins during the formation of vacuolar lobes, to the introduction of new lipids and/or proteins by the fusion of the lobes with the MIPA, or by a yet unknown mechanism. These lipids and/or proteins may support the lysis of the vesicle inside the vacuole.

In the ugt51b1 knockout mutant, the complete envelopment of the peroxisomes was arrested in phase 1b (Fig. 11B) probably due to a defective MIPA or MIPA precursor structure [92,93]. Expression of a GFP-tagged copy of the UGT51B1 gene in the mutant restored steryl $\beta$ -glucoside biosynthesis and the ability to degrade peroxisomes (Fig. 12B) [92]. The Ugt51b1 enzyme was localized by fluorescence microscopy in a membrane compartment that was interpreted as a preautophagosomal structure representing a MIPA precursor membrane [93]. So far, the biogenesis of preautophagosomal structures of non-selective and of selective autophagy is still not known [153].

Additional complementation studies (Fig. 12B) showed that expression of a SGT copy lacking the PH-G domain recovered ergosteryl glucoside biosynthesis, but not the ability to perform micropexophagy. The enzyme showed an intracellular localization differing from that of the GFP-tagged native enzyme. Expression of an enzyme lacking the catalytic domain resulted in a localization similar to that of the GFP-tagged native enzyme, but neither micropexophagy nor SG biosynthesis was recovered.

These data can be interpreted as follows: Ugt51B1 is recruited to a cytosolic lipid-protein-nucleation complex that contains PI(4)P via binding of the PH-G domain to the phosphoinositide.

SG biosynthesis at this particular place is essential for the elongation and maturation of this MIPA precursor structure leading to a MIPA that supports proper micropexophagy [92,93].

In this context it should be mentioned that P. pastoris wild type strain also shows SG synthesis under conditions that do not induce peroxisome degradation e.g. growth in minimal or complete media supplemented with glucose. Thus, SG synthesis is obviously not restricted to the precursor of the MIPA, and additional membrane contacts of Ugt51 can be expected.

Interestingly, methanol-induced peroxisomes in P. pastoris are very large, whereas smaller peroxisomes are formed after induction by oleic acid or amines [154,155]. It turns out that Ugt51B1 is essential for the degradation of the large methanol-induced peroxisomes, but it contributes in a non-essential manner to the degradation of small peroxisomes. The same applies to non-selective autophagic processes. In the yeasts Yarrowia lipolytica and S. cerevisiae, Ugt51 is not required for any autophagic process investigated so far [94,156].

# 5.6. Cholesteryl a-glucoside protects H. pylori from the immune response of the host

H. pylori is a motile, Gram-negative, microaerophilic, spiral bacterium, which was isolated more than 25 years ago from gastric mucosa of patients with chronic gastritis [170]. Subsequently, the bacterium was identified as a causative agent of stomach diseases [171,172]. H. pylori infections are usually acquired in childhood,

probably by gastric–oral or oral–oral spread and persist, if left untreated, for years or even for the entire lifetime. Over $5 0 \%$ of the worldwide population is infected, whereas the prevalence is lower in young people of industrialized countries due to good hygiene conditions [173–175]. The bacterium has developed several mechanisms to persistently colonize the human stomach, e.g. it adheres to gastric epithelial cells and it is able to buffer the gastric acid in its close proximity by urease activity, which produces $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ and ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ from endogenous urea [176]. Most of the infected individuals are asymptomatic, but a few percent develop chronic gastritis, peptic ulcer and gastric cancer [175].

A publication from 1995 reported for the first time that H. pylori and some other Helicobacter species, when grown on cholesterol-supplemented media, contain three types of cholesteryl derivatives: cholesteryl $\pmb { \alpha }$ -glucopyranoside (aCG), cholesteryl $6 ^ { \prime }$ - O-acyl- $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathtt { Q } \cdot$ -glucopyranoside (aCAG), and cholesteryl $6 ^ { \prime }$ -O-phosphatidyl- $\mathbf { \alpha } _ { \cdot \propto }$ -glucopyranoside (aCPG) [37–39]. Later it was shown that the bacterial cells are able to take up cholesterol from the medium or from epithelial cells of the stomach and that a bacterial glucosyltransferase, Hp0421, attaches a glucose moiety to the sterol backbone [61,133]. As shown in Fig. 2, enzymes yet to be identified can transfer either an acyl or a phosphatidyl moiety to the C6 atom of the glucose residue of cholesteryl glucoside. Although aCG is biosynthesized by the UDP-glucose-dependent glucosyltransferase Hp0421 probably at the inner face of the inner bacterial membrane, all three glycolipids, aCG, aCAG, and aCPG, were found to be enriched in the outer membrane [177]. This finding suggests a transport of aCG from the inner to the outer membrane of H. pylori.

Interestingly, the proportions of the three different cholesterol derivatives vary depending on morphological alterations of H. pylori cells (Fig. 13). Stressful culture conditions, such as excessive oxygen, alkaline pH, and long-term culture, result in a morphological change from spiral to coccoid cell shape. This alteration is accompanied by significant changes in membrane lipid composition [178]. The proportion of aCAG and $\mathsf { \Omega } \mathsf { \Omega } \mathsf { \Omega } \mathsf { z } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { P } \mathsf { G }$ increase at the expense of aCG. The proportions of phosphatidyl ethanolamine (PE) and phosphatidyl glycerol (PG) decrease, whereas those of cardiolipin (CL), phosphatidyl serine (PS) and phosphatidyl choline (PC) increase (PC is probably not synthesized by H. pylori, but is taken up from the complex culture medium).

Another alteration in colony variants is also accompanied by drastic differences in lipid composition. On agar plates H. pylori forms either large, shiny colonies, the HpL form, or smaller, opaque colonies, the HpS form [179]. The HpS form appears spontaneously in low frequency or is induced at high frequency by acidic growth conditions (pH 6). The HpS form shows high phospholipase A2 activity, resulting in the hydrolysis of PE and CL to lyso-PE and lyso-CL [78] (Fig. 13). The biosynthesis of lyso-PGL correlates with an increase in the proportion of aCAG [78,180]. However, this increase is quantitatively not sufficient to assume a direct and efficient transfer of fatty acyl moieties from PGL to aCAG. Such a transacylation could be a side activity, but the majority of PGL

seems to be hydrolyzed to lyso-PGL and free fatty acids. The strong phospholipase A2 activity of the HpS form may explain its haemolytic activity on blood agar plates and the release of VacA toxin and urease, due to a destabilization of the bacterial membranes [179]. VacA toxin represents a pathogenicity factor, which affects T cell responses to the infection. Indeed, H. pylori isolates with a high content of lyso-PGL are associated with patients suffering from ulcer disease [180,181]. In this context one should bear in mind that most individuals infected with H. pylori are asymptomatic.

The exact role of the phospholipase A2 for the pathogenicity of H. pylori is not clear yet. It seems likely that the enzyme hydrolyzes both the PGL of the stomach epithelial cells and of the H. pylori membranes. The autolysis destabilizes the membranes of the bacterial cells by increasing the content of lyso-PGL and free fatty acids. This destabilization may be a negative side effect of affecting the plasma membranes of host cells, but on the other hand it could support the release of VacA toxin and urease. Thus, PGL hydrolysis supports pathogenicity of H. pylori, but at the same time threatens the life of the bacterial cells. The biosynthesis of aCAG from aCG and fatty acids released from PGL may be a mechanism to restabilize the membrane despite a high proportion of lyso-PGL to achieve both release of pathogenicity factors and survival of the cell. These speculations show that further studies are required to understand the role of lipid alterations in morphological and physiological

changes of H. pylori cells and their relevance for host pathogen interactions.

The biological roles of aCG were further disclosed by the finding that the biosynthesis of these lipids influences the interaction of H. pylori with cells of the human immune system [133]. On the one hand, the uptake of cholesterol by H. pylori supports the growth of the cells. On the other hand, bacterial cells with high cholesterol proportions are prone to phagocytosis by macrophages (or other APC), which induces subsequent T cell activation and finally eradication by the immune system of mice (Fig. 14). However, only a few hours after feeding H. pylori cells cholesterol, the bacteria transformed large proportions of the ingested lipid into aCG. The presence of these lipids in H. pylori significantly reduced the degree of phagocytosis and T cell activation. Thus, a H. pylori strain deficient in aCG biosynthesis induced a strong inflammatory response and was not able to colonize the stomach of mice [133]. Interestingly the feeding of the mutant bacterium with aCG, but not with $\beta C G$ , protected the cells from phagocytosis [133]. Thus, a specific mechanism depending on the anomeric configuration of the sugar headgroup seems to control the extent of phagocytosis.

The details of this mechanism are not known yet, but by envisaging the precise molecular function of aCG in immune escape of H. pylori, we propose a dual hypothesis: a cis and a trans mechanism with regard to the pathogen’s location to the host cell membrane. First, a cis (pre-uptake) mechanism, where aCG of extracellular pathogens interfere with receptor-mediated engulfment by phagocytes. This could be due to varying sterical properties of $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -glucosides compared to cholesterol, for example through different spacing mediated by the acyl chain of aCAG. Second, a trans (post-uptake) mechanism, where a low number of bacteria is engulfed by phagocytes for subsequent degradation. Thus, aCG is incorporated into the total cholesterol pool inside the host cell and interferes with ongoing phagocytosis of H. pylori cells or downstream signaling of membrane receptors.

# 6. Conclusion and future perspectives

Recently, several biological functions of SGs were elucidated using gene inactivation mutants of bacteria, fungi and plants. In Arabidopsis and filamentous fungi the impairment of one sterol glucosyltransferase gene results in physiological alterations although other isozymes still produce SG in the mutants. This suggests that different sterol glucosyltransferase isozymes and different intracellular SG pools do not compensate for each other. Therefore, additional functions of SG will be elucidated by careful investigation of the phenotypes of further mutants with the other isozymes impaired.

So far, the biological functions of SG were not determined in any mutant on a molecular level. The observed phenotypes suggest roles of SG in the assembly and function of biomembranes and in transport or membrane translocation of cargo molecules such as lipid precursors of cell wall polymers. More detailed studies on these functions require accurate intracellular localization of sterol glycosyltransferases and SGs depending on growth conditions, developmental stage and other parameters. In addition, the influence of SG on membrane properties and possible interactions of SG with other lipids and with proteins demands further attention.

The deletion of sterol glycosyltransferase genes resulted in reduced amounts or disappearance of both SG and its acylated or phosphorylated derivatives. We are, therefore, not able to distinguish between the functions of SG and ASG or CPG. The cloning of the still elusive SG acyltransferases and the SG phosphatidyltransferase will be helpful in identifying the function of each of these glycolipids.
