# REVIEW ARTICLE

# On the significance of an alternate pathway of melatonin synthesis via 5-methoxytryptamine: comparisons across species

Abstract: Melatonin is a phylogenetically ancient molecule. It is ubiquitously present in almost all organisms from primitive photosynthetic bacteria to humans. Its original primary function is presumable to be that of an antioxidant with other functions of this molecule having been acquired during evolution. The synthetic pathway of melatonin in vertebrates has been extensively studied. It is common knowledge that serotonin is acetylated to form N-acetylserotonin by arylalkylamine N-acetyltransferase (AANAT) or arylamine N-acetyltransferase (SNAT or NAT) and N-acetylserotonin is, subsequently, methylated to melatonin by N-acetylserotonin O-methyltransferase (ASMT; also known as hydroxyindole-O-methyltransferase, HIOMT). This is referred to as a classic melatonin synthetic pathway. Based on new evidence, we feel that this classic melatonin pathway is not generally the prevailing route of melatonin production. An alternate pathway is known to exist, in which serotonin is first O-methylated to 5-methoxytryptamine (5-MT) and, thereafter, 5-MT is N-acetylated to melatonin. Here, we hypothesize that the alternate melatonin synthetic pathway may be more important in certain organisms and under certain conditions. Evidence strongly supports that this alternate pathway prevails in some plants, bacteria, and, perhaps, yeast and may also occur in animals.

# Introduction

Melatonin is a small molecule with a molecular weight of 232 Daltons. It is a derivative of an essential amino acid, tryptophan. After its isolation from the bovine pineal gland [1], it was initially classified as a neurohormone [2]. Subsequently, it was found that many peripheral tissues and organs in animals synthesize melatonin. Some of these include retina, Harderian gland, gut, ovary, testis, bone marrow, lymphocytes, and skin [3-11]. Also, the gut and skin likely produce more melatonin than does the pineal gland [6, 12]. Thus, the concept of melatonin as a neurohormone has been challenged [13].

Melatonin was also portrayed as an exclusive animal product until it was identified in the unicellular organism, the dinoflagellate Lingulodinium polyedrum (syn. Gonyaulax polyedra), in 1991 [14]. This important observation has greatly expanded the base of melatonin research from that of being unique in the animals to the other clades. As then, melatonin has been found in almost all life forms including bacteria [15, 16], fungi [17-19], nematode (Caenorhabditis elegans) [20], and plants [21-25]; it is yet

to be identified in the domain of archaea [26]. These new discoveries led to a consideration on the origin of melatonin. Its origin may be far older than previously expected. Melatonin is already present in the primitive photosynthetic aliphaproteobacteria, Rhodospirillum rubrum, and Erythrobacter longus [15, 16] and also widely distributed in cyanobacteria [27, 28]. With regard to the early occurrence in these groups of organisms, melatonin can be assumed to have been present in their ancestors and may be traced back to more than 2.5 billion years ago. This was the period in which the majority of the organisms began the transition from an anaerobic metabolism to an aerobic form [29]. Aerobic metabolism inevitably produces reactive oxygen species (ROS) which are toxic when they are excessively generated. ROS destroy DNA, proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates, which lead to cell injury that may result in death. The damage is referred as oxidative stress [30]. To reduce oxidative stress, organisms developed a variety of defense strategies. The evolution of antioxidants is one of the defense mechanisms that appeared [31].

Melatonin was first reported as a naturally occurring free radical scavenger and an antioxidant in 1993 [32]. It

was speculated that the initiated primary function of melatonin is as an antioxidant for protection from the oxidative stress [33, 34]. Indeed, accumulated evidence has strongly supported this hypothesis [35, 36]. Research has confirmed that melatonin is a potent direct free radical scavenger and antioxidant, which provides strong protection for unicellular organisms, plants, and animals against a variety of oxidative insults [37-44]. The other functions of melatonin, including photoperiodic signaling, immune enhancement, sleep promotion and, reproductive regulation, were acquired later during evolution [45]. These developments should be specific for the respective clades, but may have also occurred polyphyletically. For example, it was assumed that the activity of melatonin as a sleep promotor may have evolved in a marine annelid Platynereis dumerilii (Polychaeta), hundreds of millions of years ago [46, 47].

Due to the increased importance of melatonin to human health [48-50], its potential utility in agriculture and in food and beverage industries [51-53], research on melatonin has significantly increased in the recent years. As a result, additional novel aspects of melatonin have emerged. For example, naturally occurring melatonin isomers have been identified in wine and in other fermented foodstuffs [19, 54, 55]. These isomers were presumably synthesized by bacteria and fungi during the fermentation process. Cyclic melatonin and $\beta$ -hydroxylated melatonin have been found in plants [56]. A recent striking discovery is that the levels of 2-hydroxymelatonin in plants are several 100-fold higher than those of melatonin [57, 58]. Thus, in some species, melatonin is not the end product of this pathway, but it is an intermediate metabolite. Currently, it is unknown whether this process is limited to plants or also exists in other organisms. In mammalian skin, 2-hydroxymelatonin is formed intramitochondrially [59], but not by a homolog of the plant enzyme, the 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase M2H (melatonin 2-hydroxylase), and does not attain high levels. These new findings imply that the melatonin synthetic pathway(s) is (are) not always as previously proposed.

Herein, we hypothesize a predominant melatonin synthetic pathway, which is different from the classic route. Before discussion of the details on this melatonin synthetic pathway, the nomenclature of several melatonin synthetic enzymes should be clarified. These nomenclatures are frequently used in following text, and they will be easily confused by readers. These include arylalkylamine N-acetyltransferase (AANAT); arylamine N-acetyltransferase, if rather selective for indole-ethylamines (SNAT or serotonin N-acetyltransferase); arylamine N-acetyltransferase, if not selective for indoles (NAT); and N-acetylserotonin O-methyltransferase (ASMT), that is, also known as hydroxyindole-O-methyltransferase (HIOMT). AANAT had previously been called SNAT; however, they are not just synonyms and they differ in substrate specificity. AANAT, SNAT, and NAT all can catalyze the acetylation of indolamines at different rates depending on the substrate specificity and their concentrations. We believe that in some organisms, particularly, in plants and bacteria, O-methylation of serotonin can prevail over N-acetylation, with the consequence that, in this

case, the final step of melatonin formation will be N-acetylation of 5-MT. In this context, it may be of relevance that the reactions are not generally catalyzed by enzymes identical with those studied in vertebrate pineal glands. In alphaproteobacteria, N-acetylating enzymes with some homology to AANAT may be involved, whereas in plants as well as in cyanobacteria, the serotonin N-acetyltransferase (SNAT) is devoid of substantial homology to AANAT and has to be classified as a paralog [60]. Additional N-acetyltransferases may be also involved, even in vertebrates, such as in murine skin including NAT1 and 2 [61]. Concerning the O-methylating enzymes, an additional diversity exists, which comprises, for example, in plants, at least three ASMT subforms and, species-dependently, a side activity of caffeic acid O-methyltransferase (COMT) [60]. On the basis of the divergence in both O-methylating and N-acetylating enzymes in the different organisms, the prevailing sequence of steps in melatonin synthesis has to be re-addressed, as will be performed below in terms of a hypothesis.

# Classic melatonin synthetic pathway in animals

The classic melatonin synthetic pathway in animals was first deduced by Axelrod's group in 1960 [62, 63]. Briefly, the essential amino acid, tryptophan, is used as the initial precursor of melatonin with this precursor being converted to melatonin in four consecutive enzymatic steps. Tryptophan is first hydroxylated to form 5-hydroxytryptophan (5HTryp) by tryptophan-5-hydroxylase. This product is subsequently decarboxylated to 5-hydroxytryptamine (serotonin or 5-HT) under the catalytic action of aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (AADC). Serotonin is widely present in cells, tissues, and organs of animals. Its synthetic pathway is well known and widely accepted. The final two steps in the synthesis of melatonin, however, seem less clear. This begins with serotonin and ends with melatonin. Weissbach et al. [62] found that serotonin was acetylated to form N-acetyl-5-hydroxytryptamine (N-acetylserotonin) only in liver or pineal gland of rats by animal AANAT (EC 2.3.1.87). This process was more active and stable in liver than that in pineal gland. However, the other enzyme involved, ASMT (at that time, called HIOMT, EC 2.1.1.4), was only detected in the pineal gland. Thus, it was apparent that melatonin could only be synthesized in pineal gland and apparently not in liver. Meanwhile, even this statement has to be modified, as melatonin is also produced in hepatic cholangiocytes [9] and additional, extrapineal source of melatonin [9, 11] had not been considered at that time. In an additional study, Axelrod and Weissbach [64] observed that the enzyme activity of the HIOMT reaction with N-acetylserotonin was approximately 14 times greater than when it reacted with serotonin. Thus, N-acetylserotonin is the preferable substrate of HIOMT rather than serotonin. Based on these observations, they claimed that serotonin was first acetylated to form N-acetylserotonin by AANAT and, finally, N-acetylserotonin was methylated to melatonin by ASMT. This classic melatonin biosynthetic pathway is illustrated in Fig. 1.

Subsequently, the mammalian Aanat and Asmt genes were cloned and their structures were also identified [65- 68]. AANAT, concluded to be the penultimate enzyme, had also been classified as the rate-limiting enzyme for melatonin synthesis [69, 70]. This may not be the case under all conditions, and ASMT may become rate-limiting at highest synthetic rates at the nocturnal maximum [71]. Nevertheless, the consideration of AANAT as the ratelimiting enzyme is widely accepted.

There are good reasons for this conclusion, at least, as far as pineal gland and retina are concerned. The arguments are not only restricted to relative substrate affinities, but also extend to regulatory mechanisms. In this regard, AANAT exhibits exceptional dynamics, in both its circadian upregulation and the mechanisms of photic turnover [72, 73]. In the rodent pineal gland, Aanat mRNA exhibits a circadian cycle with an extremely high amplitude, in which maximal levels are 100- to 150-fold higher than those of the minimum. In primates and artiodactylans, regulation is mainly post-translational, widely PKA-dependent, partially PKC-dependent, and based on formation and decay of a phospho-AANAT/14-3-3 complex.

Under conditions of photic runoff, the sudden decrease of cAMP causes an immediate and rapid drop of both AANAT activity and melatonin concentration. A comparably high dynamic is not known from ASMT, although this enzyme also undergoes circadian rhythmicity. It is now recognized that AANAT and ASMT are not only present in pineal gland, and they are also widely

distributed in numerous cells, tissues, and organs. Their distributions explain the ubiquitous presence of extrapineal melatonin in mammals [9, 74, 75]. Nevertheless, there may be some exceptions. In murine skin, N-acetylation was shown to be possible by another NAT, presumably NAT-1, and, in a strain defective in pineal melatonin formation, to be decisive for cutaneous melatonin synthesis [61]. Melatonin synthesis had been also reported to occur in anucleate cells such as thrombocytes and even erythrocytes, findings that had been interpreted in terms of actions of long-lived unspecific N-acetyl and O-methyltransferases [76]. Generally, this classic melatonin biosynthetic pathway has been expanded in vertebrates and also been applied to other organisms including insects, algae, and fungi, with some deviations concerning especially additional functions of AANAT homologs [77].

Melatonin research in plants is a rapidly developing field [41, 78, 79]. Melatonin has been identified in many plants and plant products [80-82]. Indeed, melatonin may be a universal molecule in plants. The levels of melatonin vary strongly from almost undetectable to high concentrations, orders of magnitude higher than in vertebrates [79, 83]. In many species, concentrations in the nanomolar range can be found, which would be in line with conventional signaling mechanisms via high-affinity binding sites [79]; in many others, they exceed by far those found in the majority of animals. This was speculated to represent a defense strategy of plants to cope with their survival under harsh environmental conditions, as they have only limited possibilities for physically avoiding damage by extreme external influences, for example, by leaf movements or changes in stomatal aperture, and they cannot escape from hostile ambient conditions, contrary to animals [81]. In addition, under unfavorable conditions, for example, hot or cold temperatures, exposure to soil pollutants or bacterial infection, plants have the capacity to upregulate melatonin production to help their ability to survive and to thrive [39, 52, 84-89]. To understand the mechanisms of this elevated melatonin synthesis, the synthetic pathway of melatonin in plants has been extensively investigated recently [24, 90-95]. The deduced melatonin synthetic pathway in plants is illustrated in Fig. 2.

A decisive difference between melatonin synthesis in animals and plants concerns the availability of the precursor tryptophan. While this amino acid is an essential nutrient in animals and, as it is the amino acid which is present at lowest concentration, it limits the biosynthetic capacity for melatonin. This is entirely different in organisms that possess the shikimic acid pathway, which allows plants and various microorganisms to synthesize tryptophan according to requirements. Such a lack of limitation by tryptophan had also been observed in the dinoflagellate, $L$ polyedrum, which was shown to transiently increase its melatonin concentration within a very few hours to almost millimolar levels [96, 97] in response to a moderately decreased temperature. Another major difference consists in a reverse sequence of steps from tryptophan to serotonin, compared to animals, that is, tryptophan is first decarboxylated to tryptamine by tryptophan decarboxylase (TDC) and then tryptamine is hydroxylated at the five position to form serotonin by tryptamine-5-hydroxylase

There are few studies on the melatonin synthetic pathway in microorganisms. In the dinoflagellate, $L$ . polyedrum, inhibition of tryptophan 5-hydroxylase strongly decreased melatonin and 5-MT levels, and a 5-MT-dependent circadian maximum of bioluminescence was restored by addition of either 5-hydroxytryptophan, serotonin, N-acetylserotonin, or melatonin [98]. These depletion/restoration experiments already show that melatonin is formed, in this organism, via tryptophan hydroxylation as in animals and not from tryptamine as in plants. Substrate affinity studies in this dinoflagellate revealed a higher affinity of SNAT to serotonin than to 5-MT and of ASMT to N-acetylserotonin than to serotonin [99], again resembling the findings previously obtained in animals. The pathway was deduced to be the same as in the vertebrate pineal gland. In baker's yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), depletion/restoration experiments were also performed, in this case, by applying a starvation period in salt medium and, thereafter, adding potential precursors of melatonin [100]. Under the depletion conditions, melatonin dropped to below detectability, but it recovered after addition of tryptophan, serotonin or N-acetylserotonin. Notably, melatonin levels were much more strongly elevated if 5-MT was added instead. In a recent study, an S. cerevisiae strain was used, which is capable of forming tryptophan via a shikimic acid pathway that is fueled by glucose [101]. Again, this shows that high amounts of tryptophan can be generated in yeast and may be used for melatonin

synthesis. However, in that investigation, the steps from tryptophan to melatonin were catalyzed by enzymes coded by transgenes of different origin.

To date, many researchers still believe that, in all organisms, the last two steps of melatonin's biosynthesis are as described by the classic pathway. Based on newly available data and our current observations, we realized that the classic melatonin synthetic pathway cannot be of general validity. Based on the finding that melatonin can be formed from 5-MT in yeast, an alternate pathway had already been suggested, in which serotonin is first O-methylated and the resulting 5-MT is N-acetylated [100]. However, this alternative route was only regarded as a possibility existing under experimental conditions, but not necessarily of physiological significance. Herein, we now hypothesize that the alternate pathway may be of greater importance than previously believed. Depending on organisms and conditions, it may substantially contribute to overall melatonin formation or even prevail over the classic pathway. This idea is supported by data from several plants and invertebrates. It may also apply to bacteria and fungi and, perhaps, even to some extrapineal sources of melatonin in vertebrates.

# A potentially predominant melatonin synthetic pathway in plants, yeast, and bacteria

The classic melatonin synthetic pathway has been accepted for more than five decades. Few challenges have been raised as to its reliability. A major question as to the validity of this pathway comes from recent results in plant melatonin research. That melatonin is produced in plants is no longer questioned. Interestingly, the homologs of animal AANAT and ASMT have not been identified in plants [26]. It is believed that plant type SNATs belong to the GCN5-related N-acetyltransferase (GNAT) superfamily [102], which can catalyze the transfer of the acetyl group from acetyl coenzyme A to the primary amine of a wide variety of substrates. This family is present in all life kingdoms. There is little information regarding the phylogenetic traits of plant type ASMT. In mammals, only single genes encoding AANAT and ASMT are usually expressed, notwithstanding the fact that splice variants have been sometimes detected and that the reaction known for AANAT can be also catalyzed by arylamine N-acetyltransferases such as NAT-1 or NAT-2 [61]. However, in plants, many isoforms of SNAT and ASMT have been reported [91, 93, 102–105]. It appears that the different isoforms of these enzymes are responsive to different stimulators of melatonin synthesis [104]. The kinetic studies of plant type SNAT and ASMT have yielded unexpected results. They indicate that the preferred substrate for plant type SNATs is not serotonin, but rather it is 5-MT. Previously, 5-MT was not considered an important candidate involved in the melatonin synthetic pathway. However, in Arabidopsis thaliana, the affinity $(K_{\mathrm{m}})$ of SNAT (atSNAT) with 5-MT is approximately sixfold higher than that of serotonin; the maximal reaction speed $(V_{\mathrm{max}})$ of atSNAT with 5-MT is approximately fourfold faster than that with serotonin [103] (Table 1).

To test the substrate specificity of ASMT in a plant, the recombinant Asmt Arabidopsis thaliana (atASMT) was used. The result also implied that serotonin might be a more preferable substrate for plant type ASMT than N-acetylserotonin. atASMT catalyzed the conversion of N-acetylserotonin to melatonin and serotonin to 5-MT with $V_{\mathrm{max}}$ values of 0.11 and 0.29 pkat/mg protein, respectively [105]. Interestingly, in Arabidopsis, caffeic acid methyltransferase (atCOMT) also converts serotonin to 5-MT [103]. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that the predominant melatonin synthetic pathway, at least, in plants, is from serotonin to 5-MT and subsequently 5-MT acetylation to melatonin. Hence, the last enzyme for melatonin synthesis in plant would be SNAT rather than ASMT. It appears that the last two steps are reversed compared to the classic pathway of melatonin synthesis in animals (Fig. 3). However, some caution is due concerning generalizations to all plants, especially with regard to multiple isoforms of ASMTs and SNATs, which might have different properties. Also, there have been limited interspecies comparisons; thus, more studies are needed.

It was hypothesized that chloroplasts may be the major sites of melatonin synthesis in plants [28], in accordance with the demonstrated localization of the plant type SNAT in chloroplasts [106]. Chloroplasts are believed to be derived from the cyanobacteria during evolution [107]. Genetic analysis has revealed that the cyanobacterial Snat-like gene (cbSnat) codes for a relatively high amino acid homology with plants, for example, $56\%$ with rice [27]. The melatonin synthetic pathway in plants was, at least partially, inherited from cyanobacteria. Therefore, the

hypothesized predominant melatonin synthetic pathway should also be present in the cyanobacteria. To answer this question, the recombinant Snat of cyanobacteria (Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803) (cbSNAT) was selected to test its substrate specificity as cyanobacteria have the capacity to synthesize melatonin. The results showed that the $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $V_{\mathrm{max}}$ of the cbSNAT reaction with 5-MT and serotonin were $647~\mu \mathrm{M}$ , $17.4~\mathrm{nmol / min / mg}$ protein and $823~\mu \mathrm{M}$ , $1.60~\mathrm{nmol / min / mg}$ protein, respectively (Table 1). The catalytic efficiency of cbSNAT reaction with 5-MT is 14.2-fold greater than when it reacts with serotonin. The data clearly indicate that the alternate melatonin synthetic pathway in plants also exists and seems to prevail in cyanobacteria.

Does the alternative melatonin synthetic pathway apply to other organisms, for example, yeasts, a fungal clade of eukaryotic microorganisms? It was reported more than a decade ago that baker's yeast, S. cerevisiae, had the capacity to synthesize melatonin [100]. In that study, the alternate pathway had already been explicitly mentioned, because 5-MT was shown to cause rises in melatonin. However, a physiological relevance was not attributed to this route. Meanwhile, it is believed that melatonin present in wine, beer and other fermented beverages, and foodstuffs is mainly produced by yeasts [18, 19, 108-110]. Currently, genetic modified yeasts with melatonin synthetic and cofactor-providing enzymes of mammalian, trematodal, and bacterial origin were reported to generate high quantities of melatonin and that this technology was predicted to replace the chemical synthetic process of producing 'biosynthetic melatonin' on an industrial scale [101].

To understand the molecular mechanisms of yeast AANAT (scAANAT), scAANAT was cloned and its structure was systematically analyzed [111]. The kinetic studies of scAANAT on its substrate specificity have generated surprising results. The $K_{\mathrm{ms}}$ of scAANAT with 5-MT and serotonin were $2.7 \pm 0.6$ and $5.1 \pm 0.4 \mathrm{mm}$ respectively. The results clearly indicate that the prevailing substrate of scAANAT is, again, 5-MT rather than serotonin. As yeasts have the capacity to produce high levels of 5-MT under certain conditions [100], it appears that the predominant pathway for melatonin synthesis in yeasts is likely similar to that of cyanobacteria or plants, that is, scSNAT is the ultimate enzyme for melatonin production.

# The alternate melatonin synthetic pathway in animals?

As mentioned previously, the melatonin synthetic pathway was deduced by Axelrod and Weissbach [62, 63]. In their study, it was observed that animal (rat) AANAT could convert serotonin to N-acetylserotonin. At that time, it was known that AANAT could also acetylate other amines in addition to serotonin [112]. In their 1960 study, the authors did not perform enzymatic kinetics to compare the substrate specificities of AANAT to other potential substrates, that is, serotonin and 5-MT. Thus, the results obtained from their study do not provide definitive proof of the exact synthetic sequence from serotonin to melatonin. To counter this shortcoming, the authors cited two references to support their deduced melatonin synthetic

pathway. One is that of McIsaac and Page [113], which was published in 1959, and another is from the authors cited as an unpublished observation that was eventually published in 1961 [64]. McIsaac and Page [113] failed to detect the methoxyl metabolites after addition of serotonin. In Axelrod and Weissbach [64] publication, the authors reported that the enzyme activity of ASMT is approximately 14 times greater when it reacted with N-acetylserotonin than when it reacted with serotonin. Based on these observations, the authors concluded that the last step of melatonin formation was the methylation of acetylserotonin.

When these two supporting references are evaluated in detail, it becomes apparent that, in the McIsaac and Page [113] study, no standard for methoxylated indoles, for example, 5-MT, was used. Thus, it was not surprising that they failed to detect the methoxylated metabolites of serotonin. Actually, serotonin metabolism can form methoxyl metabolites; this has been verified in many studies [114-116]. Thus, the results of McIsaac and Page [113] are potentially inaccurate.

In the publication on Axelrod and Weissbach [64] in 1961, there was an obvious weakness. The kinetic analyses of ASMT reaction with N-acetylserotonin and serotonin were performed using different methods. The method for detection of N-acetylserotonin methylation was based on radiolabeling plus thin-layer chromatography; however, the means for detection of serotonin methylation were restricted to conventional thin-layer chromatography. The detection sensitivity of the radioactive method is likely much higher than that of the regular thin-layer chromatography. Thus, an additional confirmation of the relative substrate specificities of AANAT was missing.

It is not our intention, however, to criticize imperfect approaches of pioneering studies, which had been

performed under experimental conditions of that time, including the limited availability of radiolabeled compounds. With regard to pineal melatonin formation, much stronger indications for the prevalence of the classic pathway have emerged and concern the above-mentioned dynamics of circadian and photic regulation, in which the considerably more pronounced width and velocity of regulation of AANAT relative to ASMT largely speaks for a predominant role of the classic pathway, especially as a rapidly catabolized compound like 5-MT seems less suitable as a precursor of the strongly accumulating melatonin than the more stable N-acetylserotonin, which is a poor MAO substrate.

With regard to extrapineal sites of melatonin synthesis, such a conclusion may not be certain. Because of similarities to the pineal gland, including regulatory dynamics, the classic pathway may well prevail in the retina. However, this may be entirely different at other extrapineal sites. Unfortunately, the alternatives have never been studied systematically. However, one should take into consideration that the circadian amplitudes of melatonin and, thus, the dynamics of its biosynthetic enzymes are considerably smaller in some extrapineal sites of formation, such as the gastrointestinal tract and the rodent Harderian gland [11, 117]. Moreover, it will be necessary to identify the biosynthetic enzymes of melatonin formation in all extrapineal sites. Except for brain regions such as the cerebellum in which AANAT has been demonstrated [118], the involvement of the classic melatonin-forming enzymes is not generally certain. The example of replacement of AANAT by NAT-1 in the murine skin [61] may indicate relatively low rates of N-acetylation, which could favor higher rates of O-methylation.

Studies on some AANATs from invertebrates revealed substrate specificities reminiscent of bacterial or yeast AANATs with preferences for 5-MT rather than serotonin. For example, in the American cockroach, Periplaneta americana, the substrate specificity of AANAT for 5-MT $(0.05\mathrm{mm})$ at alkaline pH was approximately 43 times higher than that for serotonin and, also at acidic pH, the ratio was twice as high [119]. In the fruit fly, Drosophila melanogaster, the catalytic efficiencies of AANAT1a with 5-MT and serotonin were $6.4\pm 0.7\times$ $10^{5}$ and $2.7\pm 0.2\times 10^{5} / \mathrm{M / s}$ , respectively [120]. However, in Drosophila, these findings cannot be taken as an argument for the inverse pathway. Apart from the fact that AANAT subforms exist in this species, the most convincing argument against the alternate route results from the concentration differences between N-acetylserotonin and melatonin of about 1000:1 [121], which indicates a functional loss of ASMT activity. Although AANAT subforms from insects also serve functions other than melatonin formation, for example, in sclerotization and neurotransmitter inactivation [120, 122], the findings on substrate specificity of Drosophila AANAT may be taken as a reason of searching for similar properties in insects that produce higher levels of melatonin. Some data may indicate similar substrate specificities for indoles for even mammalian AANATs. The $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ of human AANAT (hAANAT) reaction with 5-MT is 20-fold lower than that with serotonin and the catalytic efficiency of sheep (ovine) AANAT

acting on 5-MT was 13.1-fold higher than that with serotonin (Table 1). However, it must be noted that these data were obtained at $\mathrm{pH}6.8$ not at the typical $\mathsf{pH}$ optimum of mammalian AANATs which is about $\mathsf{pH}8$ .

Molecular analyses indicated that in a range of entirely different species tested, such as bacteria, yeast, and vertebrates, AANATs are present that contain conserved, centrally located catalytic core structures, whereas differences mainly concern the presence or absence of regulatory N- and C-terminal flanking regions. These flanking regions were conserved in all vertebrate AANATs but were absent in bacteria and fungi [123]. No flanking regions that mediate circadian control have been found in D. melanogaster AANATs (dmAANATs) [124, 125]. However, the homology of these AANATs to the respective mammalian or other vertebrate enzymes was rather low. The dmAANAT1 gene, which encodes two differently expressed proteins, dmAANAT1a and dmAANAT1b, was even considered to represent another N-acetyltransferase subfamily [125]. The homology between AANAT1 and AANAT2 was also rather limited, and, additionally, data were obtained indicating the existence of further AANAT candidates in Drosophila [124]. Therefore, no clear picture is discernible concerning AANAT homology in animals. As mentioned above, plant SNATs are also devoid of substantial homology to AANATs sensu stricto, as present in vertebrates. As discussed in the context of dynamics, the vertebrate AANATs exhibit more complicated operational mechanisms than those of bacteria and yeasts. This indicates that more precise and accurate regulatory responses to photoperiodic alterations are available in vertebrate AANATs. However, their conserved catalytic core structures indicate that the AANATs of these different species should exhibit similar catalytic properties. This may open the possibility of finding further AANAT forms that prefer 5-MT rather than serotonin as a substrate (cf. Table 1).

5-MT was first detected in human urine in 1963 [126]. It is formed by melatonin deacetylation, 5-methoxytryptophan decarboxylation, and serotonin methylation [17, 127-129]. In the older literature, 5-MT has been also considered as a neurohormone by some investigators [115, 130]. A major final metabolite of 5-MT is 5-methoxyindole-3-acetic acid [131]. Based on the influences of some pteridines and light wavelengths on pineal methoxyindole synthesis in rats, Balemans et al. [132] suggested an alternative indolic metabolic pathway in which 5-hydroxytryptophan is methylated by ASMT to 5-methoxytryptophan, which is decarboxylated to 5-MT that might be finally acetylated to melatonin by AANAT. This was probably the first suggestion which assumed 5-MT to participate in melatonin synthesis. Due to the lack of supporting evidence, this alternative pathway has failed to draw any attention by investigators.

As already discussed above, an alternate route of melatonin synthesis is unlikely to be the prevailing pathway in the pineal gland, for example, because of considerations of regulation dynamics, which does not yet exclude a contribution to the overall rate of synthesis. However, this may be different in extrapineal melatonin synthesis and, also, in invertebrate animals. This possibility would also require

convincing data on the substrate specificities of the various animal ASMTs (aASMTs), by comparing serotonin and N-acetylserotonin. To date, there has been little direct evidence that allows deciding whether N-acetylserotonin or serotonin is the preferred substrate of aASMTs, as few studies have systematically investigated the kinetics of the purified enzymes. Several investigations have compared the enzymatic activities of unpurified aASMT with either N-acetylserotonin or serotonin. The results are listed in Table 2.

These results are rather divergent. Some reports show that the enzymatic activity of aASMT is higher when it reacts with N-acetylserotonin than with serotonin, but others show the opposite results. An interesting observation was made by Galzin et al. [133] which might explain the inconsistent results listed in Table 2. In previous studies, only trace amounts of 5-MT were detected in pineal gland of many species; Galzin et al. [133] thought this might be a result of the rapid degradation of 5-MT by monoamine oxidase A (MAO A). In their study, they pretreated golden hamsters with a MAO inhibitor and a robust 5-MT rhythm was detected in the pineal gland of this animal. Similar data were obtained in another study on circadian rhythmicity of pineal 5-MT using the MAO inhibitor pargyline [134]. On the one hand, the relatively low enzyme activities of aASMT with serotonin observed in some of these studies (cf, examples in Table 2) might reflect under determinations. The real activity of aASMT with serotonin may have been masked by the rapid degradation of 5-MT due to the presence of MAO A. In contrast to melatonin, 5-MT is a readily metabolized substrate of MAO A and the resulting oxidation product, 5-methoxyindole-3-acetaldehyde, is rapidly converted to 5-methoxyindole-3-acetic acid by aldehyde dehydrogenase or to 5-methoxytryptophol by alcohol dehydrogenase. MAO A is expressed in most mammalian tissues including pineal gland and eyes [135-138]. In the majority of studies summarized in Table 2, the homogenates of pineal gland or eye but not purified ASMT were used. Moreover, MAO inhibitors were also not included.

If 5-MT is the immediate precursor of melatonin, the elevated levels of 5-MT due to inhibition of MAO A would increase melatonin production. Many studies have reported that application of MAO inhibitors indeed significantly enhanced melatonin synthesis in animals as well as in the cultured pineal gland [139-144]. However, the involvement of 5-MT has to be judged with caution. The rhythm of pineal 5-MT measured in the presence of pargyline largely paralleled that of serotonin, with a diurnal maximum [134], rather than that of the nocturnally peaking melatonin. It could also be assumed that during the day the activity of the AANAT in pineal gland is silent; however, the significantly higher nocturnal activity of AANAT might also allow 5-MT conversion to melatonin at a high rate, provided that 5-MT formed from serotonin by ASMT is sufficiently available. 5-MT depletion by melatonin formation might likewise lead to the observed parallel nocturnal drop in serotonin and 5-MT while melatonin is peaking. # Summary

The classic melatonin synthetic pathway was deduced by Axelrod and Weissbach [62, 63] based on several pioneering studies that did not sufficiently consider alternatives from a methodological point of view. As then, this pathway has been accepted by the majority of researchers. Even though the synthetic pathway of melatonin in vertebrates has been extensively studied in many different aspects, few studies have systematically examined their liability of this pathway. Balemans et al. [132] suggested an alternative melatonin synthetic pathway. This suggestion failed to draw any attention from investigators as there was no solid evidence supporting their opinion. Ho et al. [150] observed that the melatonin levels in rat serum after isoproterenol injection were positively associated with the pineal AANAT activity, but a corresponding association was absent between N-acetylserotonin and AANAT. If serotonin is the substrate of AANAT as the classic pathway claimed, this association should have been expected. Therefore, those authors suggested that the serum NAS level was not regulated by pineal AANAT activity. However, serum levels of an intermediate may strongly differ from those in the synthesizing organ. Tan et al. [34, 151] also proposed a parallel melatonin synthetic pathway

complementary to the classic one. Similar conclusions on the existence of the alternate pathway were drawn by Sprenger et al. [100] in yeast and by Hardeland in 2016 in some plants [79]. Based on the new evidence including the current analysis, we hypothesize that the alternate pathway should contribute to melatonin biosynthesis and even may be the predominant melatonin synthetic route in a number of organisms and, perhaps, in some extrapineal sites of vertebrates. In several cases, we observed that the preferred AANAT/SNAT substrate was 5-MT but not serotonin. In such cases, this might imply that the last enzyme of melatonin synthesis can be AANAT/SNAT but not ASMT. This conclusion would still be compatible with a role of AANAT or SNAT as a rate-limiting enzyme of melatonin synthesis.

The contribution of the alternate pathway to pineal melatonin rhythms should still be seen with caution. However, it may play a predominant role in plants and be of relevance to bacteria and yeast. In plants, the role of 5-MT degradation may be considerably lower than in animals. Plants do possess MAO enzymes, but not the typical A and B subforms. Their specificities differ from those of animals; they oxidize catecholamines and polyamines, and numerous plants are sources of inhibitors of mammalian MAOs. The stability of 5-MT in plants would be worthy future investigation. The eventual role of the alternate pathway in invertebrate animals remains to be investigated in detail. These considerations should also include the possibility of N-acetylation by paralogs of AANAT. To confirm our hypothesis, more sophisticated studies related to the enzyme kinetics of purified ASMT and AANAT or SNAT, including their subforms, are required in the different species.
