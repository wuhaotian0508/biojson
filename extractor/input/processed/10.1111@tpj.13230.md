# SUMMARY

Monoterpenoid indole alkaloids (MIAs) are produced as plant defence compounds. In the medicinal plant Catharanthus roseus, they comprise the anticancer compounds vinblastine and vincristine. The iridoid (monoterpenoid) pathway forms one of the two branches that feed MIA biosynthesis and its activation is regulated by the transcription factor (TF) basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) iridoid synthesis 1 (BIS1). Here, we describe the identification and characterisation of BIS2, a jasmonate (JA)-responsive bHLH TF expressed preferentially in internal phloem-associated parenchyma cells, which transactivates promoters of iridoid biosynthesis genes and can homodimerise or form heterodimers with BIS1. Stable overexpression of BIS2 in C. roseus suspension cells and transient ectopic expression of BIS2 in C. roseus petal limbs resulted in increased transcript accumulation of methylerythritol-4-phosphate and iridoid pathway genes, but not of other MIA genes or triterpenoid genes. Transcript profiling also indicated that BIS2 expression is part of an amplification loop, as it is induced by overexpression of either BIS1 or BIS2. Accordingly, silencing of BIS2 in C. roseus suspension cells completely abolished the JA-induced upregulation of the iridoid pathway genes and subsequent MIA accumulation, despite the presence of induced BIS1, indicating that BIS2 is essential for MIA production in C. roseus.

Keywords: basic helix-loop-helix, Catharanthus roseus, Madagascar periwinkle, jasmonate, terpenoid, iridoid, monoterpenoid, alkaloid.

# INTRODUCTION

Catharanthus roseus (Madagascar periwinkle) is an important medicinal plant, as it is the only natural source of the anticancer monoterpenoid indole alkaloid (MIA) compounds vinblastine and vincristine. MIAs are derived from strictosidine, which is synthesised from the monoterpenoid (seco-iridoid) precursor secologanin and the indole compound tryptamine, which in turn are products of the primary metabolic methylerylthritol-4-phosphate (MEP) and shikimate/tryptophan pathways, respectively. Production of MIAs in C. roseus leaves, stems and flower petals involves multiple cell types (Courdavault et al., 2014; De Luca et al., 2014), and the involvement of a MIA membrane transporter has been shown in leaves (Yu and De Luca, 2013), illustrating the complexity of MIA production. In healthy non-stressed plants, iridoid genes, encoding the

enzymes that catalyse the seven-step conversion of the MEP pathway product geranyl-diphosphate (GPP) to the monoterpenoid loganic acid, are expressed in internal phloem-associated parenchyma (IPAP) cells, whereas the seco-iridoid genes downstream of loganic acid, i.e. loganic acid-O-methyltransferase (LAMT) and secologanin synthase (SLS), the indole pathway gene tryptophan decarboxylase (TDC) and those involved in early alkaloid biosynthesis, like strictosidine synthase (STR) and strictosidine- $\beta$ -glucosidase (SGD), are expressed in epidermal cells. Production of dimeric MIAs occurs in specialised laticifer/idioblast cells (St-Pierre et al., 1999; Courdavault et al., 2014). Reflecting their putative function in defence against insect herbivores and pathogens, MIA genes are induced by jasmonates (JAs; Memelink et al., 2001) that

act as signalling molecules for the production of valuable specialised compounds in many (medicinal) plants (Pauwels et al., 2009). Such coordinate gene expression is orchestrated by JA-responsive transcription factors (TFs; De Geyter et al., 2012). The central regulator of the JA-response is the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) TF MYC2, which in C. roseus regulates the expression of ORCA2 and ORCA3, encoding the AP2/ERF domain TFs Octadecanoid derivative-Responsive Catharanthus APETALA2-domain 2 and 3 (Zhang et al., 2011). ORCA2 and ORCA3 regulate several indole and downstream MIA biosynthesis genes, but not iridoid biosynthesis genes (Menke et al., 1999; van der Fits and Memelink, 2000; Li et al., 2013; Miettinen et al., 2014). The latter were recently shown to be regulated by bHLH iridoid synthesis (BIS) 1, a bHLH TF from clade IVa, thus distinct from MYC2 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). Ultimately, coordinate regulation of the complete set of MIA biosynthesis genes will require a more complex combination of transcriptional activators and repressors. Indeed, several other TFs have been reported as positive or negative regulators of MIA genes, including G-box-binding factors (GBFs), the MYB TF box P-binding factor-1 (BPF1), WRKY1, AT-hook TFs and EAR-domain containing zinc finger TFs (van der Fits et al., 2000; Sibéril et al., 2001; Pauw et al., 2004; Vom Endt et al., 2007; Suttipanta et al., 2011; Li et al., 2015).

Here, we describe the identification and functional analysis of a second JA-responsive regulator of the iridoid pathway branch, called BIS2 because of its homology with BIS1, and show its essential role in activating iridoid biosynthesis gene expression in C. roseus. We also present evidence for a positive regulatory loop involving both BIS proteins.

# RESULTS

# Identification of the clade IVa bHLH transcription factor BIS2

Mining of C. roseus transcriptome databases (Gongora-Castillo et al., 2012; Van Moerkercke et al., 2013; Miettinen et al., 2014) to identify regulators of the iridoid pathway has previously resulted in the discovery and characterisation of the bHLH TF BIS1 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). Further mining of these databases has led to the identification of a closely related gene encoding the bHLH TF BIS2 (Caros006385.1), which is tightly co-expressed with BIS1 and MEP and iridoid pathway genes, but not with ORCA-dependent and/or further downstream MIA genes (Figure 1a). Expression of BIS2 is JA-responsive in suspension cells, hairy roots and seedlings of C. roseus (Figure 1a; Table S1). Interestingly, compared with control transgenic lines, BIS2 is twofold upregulated in C. roseus hairy root lines stably overexpressing BIS1, whereas it remains unaffected in ORCA3-overexpressing suspension cells

(Figure 1a; Table S2). We have previously shown IPAP-enrichment of BIS1 transcripts (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), coinciding with IPAP-localised iridoid biosynthesis gene transcripts (Burlat et al., 2004; Miettinen et al., 2014). Here, we show a strong enrichment in IPAP-containing tissue for BIS2 as well (Figure 1b). Together, these transcriptomic data indicate a potential role for BIS2 in the regulation of iridoid biosynthesis genes, in analogy with BIS1.

The BIS1 and BIS2 proteins share $49\%$ and $67\%$ overall amino acid identity and similarity, respectively. Phylogenetic analysis indicates that they belong to clade IVa of the bHLH TF family, which comprises four closely related members in Arabidopsis (Heim et al., 2003) and the recently discovered Medicago truncatula Triterpene Saponin biosynthesis Activating Regulator (TSAR) 1 and 2 (Mertens et al., 2016; Figure S1). They contain a regulatory ACT domain that is utilised for dimerisation (Feller et al., 2006; Kong et al., 2012) in addition to the conserved bHLH domain (Figure 1c).

# BIS2 activates iridoid pathway gene promoters

To assess whether BIS2 can activate iridoid biosynthesis genes, we performed promoter transactivation assays with BIS2 in Nicotiana tabacum (tobacco) protoplasts (Figure 2) and C. roseus suspension cells (Figure S2). In tobacco protoplasts, BIS2 could transactivate the promoters of geraniol synthase (GES), geraniol-8-hydroxylase (G8O), 8-hydroxygeraniol oxidoreductase (8HGO), iridoid synthase (IS/P5βR5), 7-deoxyloganetic acid glucosyltransferase (7DLGT) and 7-deoxyloganic acid hydroxylase (7DLH). These results have been corroborated in C. roseus cells as well (Figure S2). We observed a synergistic effect for some iridoid biosynthesis gene promoters when combining BIS1 and BIS2 in tobacco protoplasts, but did not observe this in C. roseus suspension cells.

# BIS1 and BIS2 form homo- and heterodimers

Based on the fact that bHLH TFs are known to form dimers (Feller et al., 2006), that both BIS genes are coexpressed, and that both BIS proteins transactivate the same set of promoters (Figure 2), we hypothesised that in vivo dimerisation of the BIS proteins could occur. To assess this, we performed split-luciferase (split-LUC) assays in agro-infiltrated Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. We additionally performed yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) experiments to corroborate these results. # Overexpression of BIS2 specifically activates the genes of the iridoid pathway and results in (seco-)iridoid and MIA accumulation

To further explore the role of BIS2, we set out to create BIS2-overexpressing MP183L C. roseus cell lines by co-bombarding a pCaMV35S-driven BIS2 construct with a plasmid carrying the hygromycin resistance gene. Among 20 independent hygromycin-resistant cell lines we only found one line that successfully overexpressed BIS2. This line showed a dramatic upregulation of all iridoid pathway genes, but not the ORCA-regulated seco-iridoid gene SLS (Figure 4a). Targeted metabolite profiling (Table 1) indicated that, without methyl jasmonate (MeJA) elicitation and/or precursor feeding, seco-iridoid and MIA compounds in this line accumulated to levels comparable to those previously reported for suspension cells overexpressing BIS1 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), suggesting a similar role and action range for BIS2.

To corroborate these results, we ectopically expressed BIS2 using agro-infiltration in petal limbs of C. roseus flowers (Figure 5), which was shown to be a useful platform for transgene expression in other species (Shang et al., 2007; Verweij et al., 2008; Long et al., 2009; Van Moerkercke et al., 2009; Ratanasut et al., 2015). Compared with flowers infiltrated with an Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain harbouring a pCaMV35S:GUS control vector, BIS2 transcripts accumulated to high levels in petals agro-infiltrated with pCaMV35S:BIS2 (Figure 5). In these flowers, iridoid genes were strongly upregulated as well. In comparison, transcript accumulation levels of the MEP pathway gene 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate reductoisomerase (DXR) were moderately, but also significantly, induced. Overexpression of BIS2 did not upregulate the MIA genes encoding SLS, LAMT, SGD and TDC, and had a minor (though significant) effect on STR expression (Figure 5). In addition, transcript accumulation of the MVA pathway gene mevalonate kinase (MVK) and the carotenoid gene carotenoid isomerase (CRTISO) was not affected by BIS2 (Figure 5). Together, this indicates that BIS2 has a similar, iridoid-specific target gene set as BIS1 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015).

# Both BIS proteins regulate BIS2 expression

A remarkable observation was that the transformed hairy root lines constitutively expressing $BIS1$ , generated previously (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), showed a twofold induction of $BIS2$ mRNA levels compared with control lines (Figure 1; Table S2). Conversely, in hairy root lines down-regulated for $BIS1$ expression (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), no effect on $BIS2$ transcript accumulation could be observed (Figure S3).

# BIS2 is essential for iridoid production in Catharanthus roseus suspension cells

To evaluate the importance of BIS2 in MeJA-induced MIA production, we created two stably transformed C. roseus MP183L lines with reduced BIS2 transcript levels by RNAi (irBIS2) and monitored MeJA-induced gene expression in these lines (Figure 4b). Compared with control transgenic lines, in which iridoid biosynthesis genes were strongly upregulated as the result of MeJA application, induction of iridoid biosynthesis genes was completely abolished in the irBIS2 lines, whereas induction of the seco-iridoid gene SLS remained unaffected (Figure 4b). Silencing of BIS2 did not interfere with JA-induced BIS1 expression either (Figure 4b).

# DISCUSSION

# Biosynthesis of the terpenoid moiety of MIAs is regulated by two JA-inducible clade IVa bHLH TFs with overlapping functions

Plants accumulate terpenoid compounds as protection against pathogens or herbivore attack. Which specific terpenoid pathway is active varies between species, and individual species can accumulate different classes of terpenoids, which may be constitutively produced or JA-induced. For instance, leaves of C. roseus accumulate both monoterpenoid-derived alkaloids as well as triterpenoids, but only biosynthesis of the former can be elicited by JA, implying distinct regulatory mechanisms (Van Moerkercke et al., 2013, 2015). Conversely, M. truncatula organs mainly accumulate triterpene saponins and cannot produce MIAs. Notably, in this legume species, the biosynthesis of triterpenoid compounds is JA inducible (Suzuki et al., 2005; Pollier et al., 2013).

Like BIS1 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), BIS2 is a clade IVa bHLH TF that specifically regulates the expression of JA-inducible iridoid pathway genes but not of other terpenoid pathway genes in C. roseus. Interestingly, two homologues of BIS that were recently discovered in M. truncatula, i.e. TSAR1 and 2, do not regulate monoterpenoid biosynthesis genes but instead activate precursor and downstream genes of the JA-inducible triterpene saponin pathway (Mertens et al., 2016). The G8O promoter contains a G-box (CACGTG) element (Suttipanta et al., 2007), known to be bound by bHLH TFs. In silico analysis indicates that among the iridoid biosynthesis genes the 7DLH promoter is the only other promoter that contains this element (Table S3), suggesting the involvement of several or distinct cis-elements for coordinate regulation of iridoid genes by BIS. Indeed, the available promoter sequences of the GES, 8HGO, IS, IO and 7DLGT genes, but not of the pG8O and p7DLH genes, contain a related element, called the N-box (CACGAG; Table S3; Sasai et al., 1992; Tietze et al., 1992; Takebayashi et al., 1994), that was recently identified in plant terpenoid biosynthesis gene promoters as a target element of clade IVa bHLH TFs (Yamamura et al., 2015; Mertens et al., 2016). The available promoter sequence of the BIS2 gene does not contain either of these elements, which could explain its lack of transactivation by the BIS proteins, whereas the BIS1 gene promoter contains a G-box. Additional studies will need to be carried out to determine whether these or other cis-elements in the iridoid biosynthesis and/or BIS gene promoters can account for their coordinate transactivation by BIS.

# Regulation of the iridoid pathway branch in Catharanthus roseus involves amplification of BIS2 expression

Silencing of BIS2 expression in C. roseus cells abolishes the JA-induced upregulation of the iridoid biosynthesis genes and MIA production despite upregulation of BIS1 expression, further illustrating the critical role for BIS2. Possibly reflecting this, in stably transformed C. roseus

hairy root lines (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015) and transiently transformed C. roseus flower petals (this study), constitutive expression of both BIS1 and BIS2 induced the expression of BIS2. Positive amplification and negative regulatory feedback loops are a hallmark of the JA-signalling pathway (Kazan, 2006; Memelink, 2009; Pauwels et al., 2009; Pauwels and Goossens, 2011). Specifically for the C. roseus MIA pathway, ERF-associated amphiphilic repressor (EAR)-domain containing zinc finger proteins negatively act on the ORCA-regulated promoters of the TDC and STR genes (Pauw et al., 2004) and on the promoter of the MEP pathway gene $HDS$ (Chebbi et al., 2014), and G-box-binding proteins also act as repressors of STR promoter activity (Siberil et al., 2001). Because these negative regulators are induced by JAs as well, they probably represent a negative feedback loop to fine-tune target gene expression and/or to allow arresting the costly production of specialised metabolites at the appropriate time. Such feedback regulation of the iridoid pathway branch is likely to occur as well, but has not been shown before. Autoregulation of TFs in general is widely used, and often involves amplification of the initial response and subsequent attenuation to prevent overextending the initial signal (Bateman, 1998). In plants, the Arabidopsis bHLH TFs MYC2, GLABRA3 (GL3) and TRANSPARENT TESTA 8 (TT8), all involved in the regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis, also regulate their own expression (Baudry et al., 2006; Dombrecht et al., 2007; Morohashi et al., 2007). This feature seems to be evolutionarily conserved, because the TT8 homologues in petunia (AN1) and grape (VvMYC1), both anthocyanin regulators as well, also activate themselves (Spelt et al., 2000; Hichri et al., 2010). Although direct binding and transactivation of the BIS2 gene promoter by the BIS proteins remains to be determined, our results indicate that in C. roseus both BIS proteins amplify the elicitation signal by stimulating BIS2 expression, thereby ensuring iridoid precursor supply for MIA production after the initial perception of JA.

# EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# Plant material and transformation

Catharanthus roseus cell line MP183L and C. roseus cv. sandstorm (Syngenta) were used. Transgenic C. roseus cell suspension lines were created as described by van der Fits and Memelink (1997), and as detailed in Van Moerkercke et al. (2015). N. benthamiana leaves were infiltrated as described by Van Moerkercke et al. (2011). C. roseus flower petals were infiltrated using a method by Verweij et al. (2008) with modifications according to Van Moerkercke et al. (2009).

# Construct design

Creation of the BIS1pDONR207, MYC2pDONR207 and BIS1pK7 WG2D vectors, promoter isolation and cloning of reporter (in pGWL7) and effector (in p2GW7) constructs have been described

elsewhere (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). All constructs were made using the Gateway Technology (Invitrogen, Waltham, MA, USA). A list of primers is included in Table S4. The coding sequence of BIS2 was isolated using primers P1/P2 and recombined into pDONR207. We isolated the ACT domains of BIS1 and BIS2 using primers P3/P9 and P2/P4, respectively, and the bHLH domains of BIS1 and BIS2 using primers P5/P7 and P6/P8, and cloned them into pDONR207. The $5^{\prime}$ -UTR and $3^{\prime}$ -UTR were isolated introducing an Ncol site using primers P10/P13. Both fragments were ligated and FL attB sites were introduced by PCR using primers P14/15, and subsequently cloned into pDONR207. The CDS of JAZ1 was isolated using primers P16/P17 and recombined into pDONR207. A 685-bp fragment of the promoter of BIS2 (pBIS2) and a 1750-bp fragment of the pBIS1 was isolated based on the recently reported genome sequence (Kellner et al., 2015) using primers P32/P33 and P34/P35, respectively. The fragments were then cloned into pDONR221. All Entry clones were sequenced and LR recombined into the appropriate destination vectors (p2GW7 and pK7WG2D for BIS2; pGADT7 for BIS1, BIS2 and MYC2; pGBKT7 for BIS1-ACT, BIS2-ACT, BIS1-bHLH, BIS2-bHLH and JAZ1; pMK7WG-cL-2 and pMK7WG-nL-2 for BIS1 and BIS2; pK7GWIWG2D for irBIS2; and pGWL7 for pBIS1 and pBIS2).

# Gene expression and metabolite analyses

RNA extractions, gel blot hybridisations, cDNA synthesis and qRT-PCR were performed as previously described (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). Primers for qRT-PCR have been described elsewhere (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), or are shown in Table S4 in the case of CRTISO (P18/P19), MVK (P20/P21), LAMT (P22/P23), BIS2-CDS (P24/P25), BIS2-UTR (P26/P27), BIS1-UTR (P28/P29) and SGD (P30/P31). Metabolites were measured as described in Van Moerkercke et al. (2015).

# Protein-protein interaction studies using Y2H and splitLUC assays

Yeast two-hybrid assays were performed as described by Cuellar Pérez et al. (2013). For split-LUC analyses, A. tumefaciens harbouring combinations of the partner split-LUC vectors were combined together with the pCaMV35:rLUC plasmid in a 1:1:1 ratio, and independent infiltration was randomised between different plants and leaves. Five infiltrations per combination were performed and, for each replica, four leaf discs were sampled for subsequent analysis. Proteins were extracted using cell culture lysis reagent (Promega, Madison, WI, USA), and luciferase activity was measured with the Dual-Luciferase® Reporter Assay System (Promega).

# Transient promoter analyses in tobacco protoplasts and Catharanthus roseus cells

Transient activation assays were performed as in Van Moerkercke et al. (2015), but for the tobacco protoplasts four biological replicas were used here.

# Hierarchical clustering, protein alignment and phylogenetic analysis

For protein alignment, the amino acid sequences of the bHLH and ACT domains were used as assigned by ProSite, aligned using CLUSTALW in default settings and processed using Jalview. For hierarchical clustering, the average linkage hierarchical clustering with Pearson correlation was performed using the Multi Experiment Viewer (MeV) software. FPKM values of control samples were used to normalise and all values were subsequently LOG

(base 10) transformed before clustering. Phylogenetic analysis and tree construction were performed as described by Mertens et al. (2016).
