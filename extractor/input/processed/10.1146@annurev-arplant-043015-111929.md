# Abstract

Cutin, a polyester composed mostly of oxygenated fatty acids, serves as the framework of the plant cuticle. The same types of cutin monomers occur across most plant lineages, although some evolutionary trends are evident. Additionally, cutins from some species have monomer profiles that are characteristic of the related polymer suberin. Compositional differences likely have profound structural consequences, but little is known about cutin's molecular organization and architectural heterogeneity. Its biological importance is suggested by the wide variety of associated mutants and gene-silencing lines that show a disruption of cuticular integrity, giving rise to numerous physiological and developmental abnormalities. Mapping and characterization of these mutants, along with suppression of gene paralogs through RNA interference, have revealed much of the biosynthetic pathway and several regulatory factors; however, the mechanisms of cutin polymerization and its interactions with other cuticle and cell wall components are only now beginning to be resolved.

# INTRODUCTION

Approximately 450 million years ago, plants began to colonize desiccating habitats, a process of terrestrialization that profoundly affected the biosphere and represents one of the most important evolutionary events in the history of life on Earth (4, 55). The biggest challenge for early land plants would have been desiccation, and so arguably the most critical evolutionary innovation to allow land colonization was the development of a specialized epidermal cell wall in the form of a hydrophobic skin, or cuticle, to limit transpirational water loss. Consequently, cuticles are present on the outer surfaces of almost all land plant aerial organs except for the stems of woody plants, which instead have a periderm (bark) containing the hydrophobic polymer suberin (59).

Most cuticles are composed largely of cutin, an insoluble polyester of primarily long-chain hydroxy fatty acids. This lipophilic cutin framework is associated with hydrophobic compounds collectively referred to as waxes, which can be embedded within the cutin matrix in the form of intracuticular waxes or can accumulate on the outer surface of the epidermis as an epicuticular film, or crystals (103). The cuticle is also thought to contain varying amounts of intermingled cell wall polysaccharides and sometimes also an insoluble, saponification-resistant fraction termed cutan (90). The composition of cutan appears to be broadly similar to that of cutin (124, 129), but the nature of the linkages between the constituent monomers remains controversial (24, 90, 129). This review focuses on cutin, with other cuticle components discussed only in the context of cutin associations. For broader overviews of cuticle structure and functions, we refer readers to other recent reviews (80, 141).

Cutin is chemically stable and resistant to decay, possibly even more so than lignin (41), and indeed cuticle samples have been recovered and analyzed from fossilized ancient plants dating back as far as 400 million years (30, 89, 123) (Figure 1a). The cuticles of extant plants can be readily visualized using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) or lipophilic stains (Figure 1b,c). Together with biochemical studies, these techniques have demonstrated that cutin is present in species from across the plant kingdom (52), with the amounts ranging from $\sim 0.5$ to $1,500~\mu \mathrm{g} / \mathrm{cm}^2$ (33, 46). # COMPOSITION AND STRUCTURE

Analyses of cutin monomer composition typically involve ester depolymerization, which allows an assessment of the types and quantities of the constituent molecules using gas chromatography and mass spectrometry. This inherently destructive approach has yielded a wealth of data regarding the variation in cutin composition among species and organs. Other techniques have provided additional valuable information regarding the nature and abundance of specific chemical linkages within the cutin polymer; however, little is known about the macromolecular architecture and spatial heterogeneity, a critical issue that remains experimentally challenging.

# Monomer Composition

Almost all cutins analyzed to date consist predominantly of oxygenated long-chain (16- or 18-carbon) fatty acids. These monomers typically bear a terminal hydroxyl $(\omega -\mathrm{OH})$ as well as one or more midchain oxygenations, most commonly hydroxy or epoxy groups, although oxo groups are prevalent in some cutins (47) (Figure 2). The 18C fatty acids may contain one or two unsaturations. Several related monomers can also be present, usually as minor constituents: unsubstituted fatty acids, fatty aldehydes, dicarboxylic acids, and primary alcohols (47). In addition, cutin often contains a relatively small proportion of phenylpropanoids, such as ferulic acid and coumaric acid, and in some cases may contain glycerol.

Although there are distinct variations in cutin monomer profiles across species, organs, and developmental stages, the classes of monomers are generally conserved across land plant lineages, suggesting the existence of evolutionary constraints. However, a broad trend is evident in terms of monomer size. Although 18C acids are a common, and often dominant, component of angiosperm cutins, they are only minor constituents in gymnosperms (if present at all) and appear in at most trace amounts in seedless plants (37, 47). Furthermore, many seedless vascular plants have a 14C acid as one of their main cutin monomers, but this is typically less abundant in gymnosperm cutin and absent in angiosperms (13, 15, 37, 47, 48). Thus, monomer length appears to generally increase in later-diverging plant groups.

In addition to this broad evolutionary trend, unusual cutin monomers have also been associated with specific plant groups. For example, although only two liverwort species have been analyzed to date, they both lack or have only trace amounts of $\omega$ -OH acids, instead containing 15-OH 16C acid (15), a compound that has not yet been identified in any other plant taxa. In addition, the psilophytes (of which also only two species have been analyzed) are apparently unique in containing moderate amounts of 16C triols (16). However, because so few seedless plant species have been investigated, it is unclear whether these variants are ubiquitous within a specific lineage, also occur

in other lineages, or are unique to these few species. More extensive compositional analyses of cutin from early-diverging land plant taxa would undoubtedly be an interesting area of future research to better understand cutin evolution and diversity.

Another important evolutionary question related to the diversity of cutin composition and structure is the origin of the related substance suberin, a hydrophobic polymer that accumulates in the apoplastic regions of many non-cutinized boundary cell layers, such as the periderm, endodermis, and root exodermis (130). Suberin is also a polyester of predominantly 16C and 18C acids but is typically distinguished from cutin by its much higher abundance of phenylpropanoids, diacids, and primary alcohols, as well as the presence of glycerol and monomers longer than 18C (96). Importantly, however, there are examples of cutin with supposedly suberin-like features. For example, the cutin of Arabidopsis thaliana leaves and stems is rich in diacids, accounting for $40\%$ of the cutin mass (33). Franke et al. (33) reported that glycerol is also a constituent of A. thaliana cutin but did not quantify its abundance; however, Graça et al. (39) measured glycerol levels in a variety of plant cuticles and found that it accounts for up to $14\%$ of the cutin mass. How current models of cutin polymer structure can accommodate such large amounts of glycerol in cases where diacids are absent is unclear, as discussed below. Lastly, another distinctive suberin characteristic, high phenolic content, has been found in cutin from some early- and later-diverging land plants (11, 13, 15).

Given the compositional similarity of cutin and suberin as well as their associated biosynthetic and regulatory genes, as discussed below, it seems likely that they have a common origin. Indeed, the only truly consistent distinguishing trait is that, whereas cutin is deposited on the outside of the polysaccharide cell wall of epidermal cells, suberin accumulates on the inner face of the cell wall of internal cell layers (6). We speculate that suberin arose as a result of ectopic expression of a master regulator of cutin biosynthesis that proved to be beneficial. Furthermore, we propose that although suberin and cutin are generally referred to as distinct polymers, it would not be surprising if studies of a more extensive taxonomic range of plant species showed that the distinction between the two is artificial, and that they in fact exist on a single compositional continuum.

# The Structure of the Cutin Polymer

Interpreting the significance of monomer profile differences between cutins is difficult without understanding the polymer structure and how it is affected by composition. Examination of cuticles or isolated cutin polymers using a variety of methods has revealed some information about macrostructural properties. Analysis of cutin using X-ray diffraction showed that it is amorphous (59, 79), although the cuticle clearly has some degree of spatial organization, because differences have been observed between the inner and outer sides based on staining patterns (14), electrical behavior in impedance spectroscopy experiments (7, 27), and nanomechanical performance probed with atomic force microscopy (R.E. Stark, personal communication). However, this asymmetry may well be explained by other cuticle constituents, and indeed, Benavente et al. (7) reported that removing the polysaccharide fraction results in homogeneous electrical conductivity properties across the remaining isolated cutin. TEM can often show more detailed structural features, which has resulted in the designation of three distinct cuticle ultrastructures—amorphous, lamellate, and reticulate—with some cuticles having two of these in adjacent layers (52). What determines these ultrastructures—e.g., whether they arise from a compositional feature of cutin, some aspect of the polymer assembly, or association with other cuticular components—is unknown. A common assertion is that the reticulate appearance occurs where there is intermingling of polysaccharides with the cutin, but this has not been demonstrated experimentally.

That cutin is composed of ester-linked monomers is well established, but whether there is any ordered organization in the polymer as a whole or in specific regions of the polymer is unknown.

The chemical structures of different cutin monomers allow for certain configurations in the mature polyester (96). A fatty acid with only one hydroxyl group can only contribute to elongating a linear chain, whereas incorporation of an acid lacking hydroxyls terminates a chain (Figure 3a). By contrast, the presence of dihydroxy acids allows branching to occur, which could lead to the formation of a dendritic cutin polymer (Figure 3b). However, ester cross-linking between different chains/dendrimers would require the presence of dicarboxylic acids (Figure 3c). Such diacids are also necessary for the integration of glycerol into the polymer (Figure 3c), because a glycerol ester of a monocarboxyl fatty acid would not have a free carboxyl group to allow further growth of the polymer. It is therefore surprising that Graça et al. (39) found low to moderate

levels of glycerol in the depolymerization products of cutins from several species, along with the presence of monoacylglycerols, considering that diacids were not detected in most cases. One explanation is that the glycerol and monoacylglycerols are not covalently linked into the polymer, but rather are trapped within the cutin matrix and released only after the polymer network has broken apart in vitro. However, a cutin polymer that contains both diacids and glycerol has a multitude of potential structural arrangements, with branching theoretically possible from the hydroxyl groups of the glycerol component as well as from the fatty acids (96) (Figure 3c).

To directly study the connectivity of monomers in intact cutin, researchers have used several variations of a solid-state nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) approach [magic-angle spinning NMR (MAS NMR)], each of which has specific benefits and limitations. Direct-polarization $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ MAS NMR can give quantitative measures of the different carbon moieties (113), unlike the more commonly used cross-polarization technique; however, both techniques are limited by the occurrence of overlapping signals with similar magnetic environments, which makes it difficult to accurately distinguish the proportions of free or ester-linked hydroxyls (R.E. Stark, personal communication). By contrast, two-dimensional high-resolution MAS NMR provides higher spectral resolution that can clearly distinguish between these groups and establish covalent connections between monomer units (e.g., 25), but this procedure relies on swelling the sample with solvent, which may not occur as effectively for highly branched cutin sites, thereby biasing the analysis (31, 113). Solid-state NMR analyses can also suggest the extent of connectivity within the polymer based on the motional constraints of cross-linked or branched carbon moieties (144).

Another analytical strategy is to partially depolymerize the cutin and use mass spectrometry and/or solution-state NMR to analyze the resulting soluble oligomers (e.g., 31, 38). However, this method may have inherent bias because the mild depolymerization process may favor the cleavage of certain bonds.

Arguably the most informative approach used to date involves labeling free hydroxyl groups before complete depolymerization. Such studies of cutin from several species have consistently indicated that almost all of the primary hydroxyls are esterified, and in most cases a large proportion of the monomers also have midchain linkages, which suggests a highly branched structure (1, 22, 58). The one known exception is the cutin of lemon fruit ( $Citrus \times limon$ ), which had relatively few midchain linkages, mostly because the majority of the monomers lacked a midchain hydroxyl group (22). Further studies with this type of technique would be of great benefit in understanding the diversity of possible molecular arrangements.

Although these analyses can provide a general sense of how the monomers may be linked, the large-scale native architecture of cutin remains mysterious. It may be determined not only by the monomer composition, but also by the actions of the polymerizing enzymes, the microenvironment at the polymerization site, and interactions with other cuticle and cell wall polysaccharide constituents.

# Interactions with Other Cuticle Components

Current evidence suggests that the cutin matrix is not a dense, airtight mass, but rather has a more open branching/network structure. Computational modeling of cutin molecular dynamics has suggested the presence of cavities within the polymer and predicted a spacing of $0.4 - 0.5\mathrm{nm}$ between cutin oligomer chains (83), whereas X-ray diffraction data have suggested values of 0.45 and $1.0\mathrm{nm}$ (79). These cavities represent areas that may potentially be occupied by other cuticle components, and indeed a diverse set of mostly hydrophobic compounds, collectively referred to as intracuticular waxes, can be extracted from within the cutin framework using nonaqueous solvents. NMR studies indicate that these waxes form hydrophobic interactions with the cutin (34).

Cutin also has some hydration and ion exchange capacity (17, 109), likely through adsorption of water molecules and ions to its free hydroxyl groups. Furthermore, it has a binding preference for calcium over sodium (109), and its polymer structure changes depending on the type of ions with which it is saturated (79). Thus, the ionic environment in the outer apoplast may have an important influence on cutin microstructure and function.

Another cutin interaction of potential significance is with the cell wall polysaccharides that underlie and extend within it. Such associations likely anchor the cuticle to the epidermal cell surface and may also play a role in maintaining the integrity of the cutin network in the absence of cross-linking diacids. Microscopic analyses of chemically stained cuticles suggest the presence of a layer rich in pectins at the interface between the cuticle and the polysaccharide wall (52), and a report that pectinase treatment released cuticles from the polysaccharide wall in the leaves of several plant species supports a role for pectins in cementing the cuticle in place (92). However, in another study, cuticle isolation from conifer needles required the application of both pectinases and cellulases (69), implying cutin interactions with both pectin and cellulose or with other cellulase-susceptible wall glycans. Penetration of polysaccharides into the cutin matrix has also been suggested by chemical stains (91) and infrared spectroscopic analyses of isolated cuticles (77, 128). In other studies, removal of the cutin fraction from the isolated cuticles of several species left behind continuous polysaccharide films (43, 77), and TEM imaging indicated that these films from two Eucalyptus species are fibrillar and quite similar in appearance to the polysaccharide wall (43). To study the distribution of polysaccharides in situ, Guzmán et al. (42) used gold-conjugated pectinases and cellulases to label their respective substrates in the cuticles of three species and found that their densities were highest on the inner face but extended almost entirely across the cuticle membrane. Interestingly, the labeling did not appear to correlate with the fibrillar reticulum structures observed using TEM, which suggests that these features may not be polysaccharide based.

Whether and (if so) which polysaccharides are linked to cutin remains an open question. Pollard et al. (96) postulated that suberin is bound to the cell wall via its aromatic monomers, but the relative scarcity of such compounds in most cutins implies that other types of linkages are involved. Pectins are an attractive candidate because free carboxyl groups on the constituent galacturonic acid moieties, a common feature of pectin backbones, provide potential sites for ester linkages (Figure 4). In this regard, it is noteworthy that overexpression of a pectin methylesterase inhibitor in A. thaliana results in cuticular defects (86). However, the hydroxyl groups from any type of sugar can potentially be esterified to cutin through a fatty acid carboxyl terminus (Figure 4a) or through an ether bond to any of the free hydroxyls (Figure 4b). Indeed, evidence was reported for covalent linkages between cutin and polysaccharide monomers in studies that analyzed oligomers derived from mild depolymerization of cutin from lime fruit ( $Citrus \times aurantifolia$ ), some of which contained both cutin acids and sugars (31, 125). Furthermore, NMR analysis of one such oligomer showed that this linkage is an ether bond (125).

An important consideration is which end of the cutin polymer attaches to the polysaccharide, as this would likely have a profound effect on cutin architecture. # Monomer Synthesis and Export

Cutin monomers are synthesized by the modification of plastid-derived 16C and 18C fatty acids in the endoplasmic reticulum, yielding variously oxygenated fatty acid-glycerol esters referred to as monoacylglycerols. This process involves activating the fatty acids by conjugation to coenzyme A (CoA), oxidizing the terminal and/or midchain carbons, and transferring the fatty acid from CoA to glycerol. The timing of the oxidation step in relation to the other two steps has been a longstanding question, although accumulating evidence suggests that it occurs after CoA conjugation and before the acyl transfer to glycerol. In rare cases, some monomers may also undergo one or more rounds of fatty acid elongation, increasing their chain lengths beyond 18C. This elongation process has been reviewed by Li-Beisson et al. (73) and is not discussed here.

The probable first step in this synthesis pathway is the esterification of CoA to a fatty acid, producing acyl-CoA, a reaction carried out by long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase (LACS) proteins. The A. thaliana genome contains nine LACS genes, two of which (LACS1 and LACS2) have a semi-redundant function in the production of cutin and waxes (9, 78, 108). LACS3 may also contribute to cuticle formation, as its transcripts are significantly enriched in epidermal cells (118), but this has yet to be validated experimentally. There are several pieces of evidence that suggest that these enzymes act prior to the oxidation reactions. First, an $\omega$ -hydroxylase enzyme from Petunia × hybrida that is in the same subfamily as the cutin $\omega$ -hydroxylases has a much higher activity in vitro with acyl-CoA substrates than with free fatty acids (44). Second, the presence in A. thaliana of a series of 2-hydroxy fatty acids with lengths ranging from 16 to 28 carbons (33) implies that the 2-hydroxylation reaction occurs after fatty acid elongation, which uses acyl-CoA as the substrate. Lastly, expressing A. thaliana LACS1, -2, or -3 in yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) promotes transmembrane fatty acid uptake (97), which suggests that they contribute to the import of fatty acids into the endoplasmic reticulum in planta.

Many cutin monomer oxidation reactions involve cytochrome P450 enzymes: Specifically, terminal carbon reactions involve members of the CYP86A family, and midchain reactions involve a member of the CYP77A family (Figure 6). A. thaliana cyp86a2, -4, and -8 mutants have altered cutin composition (72, 85, 131, 136), and cyp86a2 and -8 mutants have abnormal cuticle structures (131, 136). CYP86A7 may also be related to cutin biosynthesis based on its sequence similarity and the fact that its expression is induced by SHINE 1 (SHN1), a transcription factor known to regulate cuticle formation (54). The proteins encoded by these four genes, together with the paralog CYP86A1, which is involved in suberin monomer synthesis (130), all catalyze the $\omega$ -hydroxylation of fatty acids in heterologous expression systems (29, 101, 133). Further oxidation of the hydroxylated $\omega$ carbon would lead to the production of an aldehyde group and then a second carboxyl group. The A. thaliana GPAT4 and GPAT8 genes have largely redundant functions in leaf and stem cutin formation (70), whereas GPAT6 contributes to petal cutin synthesis (72). The three corresponding enzymes show a considerably stronger preference for fatty acids with a terminal hydroxyl or carboxyl group compared with those that are unsubstituted (138), suggesting that they act after the oxidation reactions. To be incorporated into the cutin matrix, the cutin monomers must be exported from the endoplasmic reticulum, transported across the plasma membrane, and trafficked across the polysaccharide cell wall to the site of polymerization. There is no evidence that the canonical secretory pathway is involved in this export process; Samuels & McFarlane (104) have proposed that the lipids are trafficked directly from the endoplasmic reticulum to the plasma membrane at sites of close contact, but this has yet to be demonstrated. Studies of $A$ . thaliana have shown, though, that monoacylglycerol monomers exit the cell via specific ABC transporters in the plasma membrane. The half transporters ABCG11 and ABCG13 (10, 95) and the full transporter ABCG32 (8) have been implicated in cutin deposition.

The least understood aspect of the export process is how the largely hydrophobic cutin monomers traffic through the hydrophilic polysaccharide wall to reach the site of cuticle synthesis. Lipid transfer proteins (LTPs) may act as transporters, and LTPG1 and LTPG2 indeed play a role in depositing cuticular waxes (23, 56, 67); however, a specific role for LTPs in cutin transport has yet to be demonstrated. Given the abundance of cutin at the cell surface, particularly in organs such as fleshy fruit (80), it seems unlikely that cutin transport is mediated by secreted proteins traveling between the plasma membrane and cuticle. A simpler and less energetically expensive mechanism might involve self-aggregation and phase separation, whereby the cutin monomers diffuse throughout the apoplast and their biophysical attributes favor them partitioning into the more hydrophobic microenvironment of the growing cuticle. However, this explanation does not account for some complexities, such as the deposition of cutin in the anticlinal walls of some fruits (80) and how the mechanism could differ for suberin, which accumulates instead on the inner face of the polysaccharide cell wall. Thus, essentially nothing is known about this critically important process, which represents a key area for future investigation.

# Polymerization

The mechanism by which the cutin monomers polymerize upon arrival at the growing cuticle has been a long-standing question. Two recent studies of cutin formation in tomato fruit independently identified a member of the GDSL-motif lipase/esterase (GDSL) superfamily as functioning as a cutin synthase (36, 140).

One of these studies involved characterizing a tomato null mutant, cutin deficient 1 (cd1), the fruit of which exhibits a severe decrease in levels of polymeric cutin ( $>95\%$ ) and accumulates the cutin monomer 2-mono(10,16-dihydroxyhexadecanoyl)glycerol (abbreviated as 2-MHG). Incubating recombinant CD1 with synthetic 2-MHG in vitro resulted in the formation of a series of linear cutin oligomers composed of terminal ester-linked fatty acids (139, 140). This result led to a model in which CD1 transfers the hydroxyacyl group from one molecule of 2-MHG to the terminus of the growing polymer chain, releasing glycerol (140). In the other study, Girard et al. (36) used RNA interference (RNAi) to suppress the same gene (referred to as $GDSL1$ ), leading to reductions in cuticle thickness and cutin monomer content and the appearance of nanopores in the cutin matrix. Further analysis of these RNAi lines and of a new knockout mutant utilizing chemical labeling of the free hydroxyl groups before cutin depolymerization revealed an increase in the proportion of free midchain hydroxyls but not $\omega$ -hydroxyls (B. Bakan, personal communication). This suggests that, in contrast to the in vitro results, CD1/GDSL1 may preferentially catalyze midchain esterification in vivo.

Phylogenetic analysis revealed that $CD1$ aligns within a distinct clade of GDSL genes, which includes representatives from a taxonomically broad range of land plants (139). Further analysis

additionally showed that members of this clade from the moss Physcomitrella patens and $A$ . thaliana also exhibited 2-MHG polymerizing activity in vitro, whereas a more divergent $A$ . thaliana GDSL enzyme with known cutinase activity (119) did not. This clade of homologous genes has now been described as encoding an ancient and conserved family of cutin synthase (CUS) proteins that act as polyester synthases, with negligible hydrolytic activity, and CD1 was accordingly renamed CUS1 (139). The various CUS enzymes evaluated in this study showed large differences in how efficiently they use 2-MHG as a substrate, which may indicate that they act on other native cutin monomer substrates. Moreover, although the authors reported $K_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $K_{\mathrm{cat}}$ values for the reactions, allowing a relative comparison, they also noted that the reaction rates in the hydrophobic environment of the cuticle may be different.

Although one of the enzymes responsible for polymerizing the cutin matrix has now been identified, many questions remain regarding the mechanistic details and other aspects of cutin polymerization. For example, despite the null mutation in $CD1 / CUS1$ , the fruit of the $cd1$ mutant still accumulates appreciable amounts of polymeric cutin (140), which suggests either the presence of additional CUS proteins or the use of other polymerization mechanisms. Since $CUS$ genes appear to be present as families in all land plants surveyed to date (J.K.C. Rose, T.H. Yeats, E.A. Fich & N.A. Segerson, unpublished results), the simplest explanation is that another CUS protein is responsible for forming the remaining cutin polymer in the $cd1$ mutant fruit. Furthermore, CUS1 has only been shown to polymerize 2-MHG; whether CUS1, another CUS family protein, or a different class of enzymes is responsible for polymerizing other types of cutin monomers is unknown.

In addition to CUS proteins of the GDSL superfamily, two other enzymes have been hypothesized to contribute to cutin polymerization, although clear biochemical evidence is lacking in each case. The first is BODYGUARD (BDG), an $\alpha/\beta$ -hydrolase family protein that localizes to the outermost portion of the epidermal cell wall (63). An A. thaliana bdg mutant has a highly disorganized cuticle that is intermingled with the polysaccharide cell wall but shows a significant increase, rather than reduction, in the amount of cutin polymer (63), suggesting a function other than polymerization. The second enzyme is the acyltransferase DEFECTIVE IN CUTICULAR RIDGES (DCR), a mutant of which has a near-complete loss of dihydroxy 16C fatty acids in its cutin (94). However, this enzyme is located in the cytosol (94) and has diacylglycerol acyltransferase activity in vitro (98), which is not consistent with a role in cutin production. In addition, Dominguez et al. (28) proposed that cutin monomers may spontaneously polymerize through a non-enzymatic mechanism in which partially polymerized cutin monomers form nanoparticles, termed cutinsomes, which self-assemble. Whether any of these processes contribute to cutin polymerization in planta remains to be determined.

Although these proposed mechanisms all seek to explain the polymerization of aliphatic cutin, they do not address how the phenolic monomers are incorporated. The gene DEFICIENT IN CUTIN FERULATE (DCF) plays such a role for ferulate by catalyzing the transfer of ferulic acid from CoA to the terminal hydroxyl of an $\omega$ -hydroxy fatty acid in the cytosol (99). However, the pathway by which these alkyl ferulates are exported and whether they require a unique enzyme to incorporate them into the cutin polymer are unknown.

Cuticle deposition must be carefully coordinated with organ growth and epidermal cell differentiation to ensure continuous surface coverage and maintenance of structural integrity. For example, the transcription factor TOMATO AGAMOUS-LIKE 1 (TAGL1), a key regulator of tomato fruit development, affects cuticle production, including cutin, waxes, and polysaccharides, in part by regulating the transcription factor genes SHN1 and SHN3 (35). The SHN genes (SHN1- SHN3), a set of three largely redundant APETALA 2 (AP2) family transcription factors from A. thaliana, are regulators of floral cutin and epidermal cell morphology (115), and homologs play similar roles in leaves and fruits from other species (114, 132, 143). SHN1 is regulated by the MYB family transcription factor MYB106, which along with its paralog MYB16 controls many aspects of cuticle and epidermis formation in A. thaliana (93). In addition, studies have shown that class IV homeodomain-leucine zipper (HD-ZIP IV) family transcription factors [tomato CD2, A. thaliana ANTHOCYANINLESS 2 (ANL2) and HOMEODOMAIN GLABROUS 1 (HDG1), and maize OUTER CELL LAYER 1 (OCL1)] regulate cuticle synthesis as well as other aspects of epidermal cell identity (50, 51, 88, 135). The activity of HDG1 is affected by an interaction with the WW-domain protein CURLY FLAG LEAF 1 (CFL1), which acts as a suppressor of cuticle development (135).

For most of these transcription factor genes, experimental results so far have not definitely indicated whether they directly regulate cutin biosynthesis or whether the cuticle structural and compositional consequences that typically result from altering their expression are a secondary effect of changes in the growth and morphology of the epidermal cells. However, studies have demonstrated that SHN1 binds to the promoters of cutin biosynthesis genes from $A$ . thaliana and rice (Oryza sativa) (54, 132), and SHN1 can thus be considered a direct, if not necessarily specific, regulator of cutin biosynthesis.

In addition, Kosma et al. (60) have reported that cuticle formation is responsive to environmental conditions; however, few genes involved in mediating these responses have been identified, and those that have been found generally mediate a broad range of stress responses. Two such examples are MYB41 and MYB96, although there is no evidence that either of them affects cutin levels (61, 112). A mutation of the $A$ . thaliana gene NFX1-LIKE 2 (NFXL2), an apparent repressor of abiotic stress responses, resulted in increased expression of SHN1, -2, and -3 and BDG (74). Although the SHN genes themselves have not been implicated in $A$ . thaliana stress responses, the rice SHN1 homolog WAX SYNTHESIS REGULATOR 1 (OsWR1) is induced by high salinity, drought, and the stress-related hormone abscisic acid (132). There are almost certainly additional factors involved in this regulatory network, and future research will elucidate the interactions between transcription factor-mediated regulation of cutin deposition, developmental programs, and responses to environmental signals.

# DEGRADATION BY CUTINASES

The cuticle represents the first line of defense against invasion by pathogens seeking to colonize aerial organs, and certain fungal and bacterial phytopathogens are known to secrete cutinases that are capable of depolymerizing cutin (20, 87, 127). Skamnioti & Gurr (117) showed that a mutation in the cutinase gene CUT2 of the fungal pathogen Magnaporthe grisea reduced its ability to infect rice and barley (Hordeum vulgare), demonstrating that cutinases play a role in its pathogenicity. The microbial cutinases studied to date belong to the large $\alpha/\beta$ -hydrolase family of lipases/esterases and are considered to form a structurally distinct class owing to the lack of a lid covering the active site (75). # AN INDUSTRIAL ROLE FOR CUTINASES

The industrial enzyme market had an estimated worldwide value of $1.5 billion in 2000 (84). With the rise of industrial biotechnology, this value was expected to grow to$ 4.4 billion by 2015 (105). A recent report identified lipases as the group of enzymes with the second-highest potential for growth (5), although cutinases may provide another possible area of development. The wide substrate specificity of cutinases has made them targets of interest in several industrial settings (2, 32, 142); however, their widespread use has been limited, in part because of their low thermostability (19). To this end, there has been some success in using protein engineering to identify residues in cutinases that, when mutagenized, result in improved stability and efficiency (12, 49). Future efforts to identify and characterize new cutinases, as well as the use of protein engineering, may lead to the development of cutinases that are better able to meet industrial needs and the identification of novel applications.

# BIOLOGICAL FUNCTIONS

# Water Barrier

A critical function of the cuticle is to restrict transpirational water loss, but the relative contribution of cutin is unclear. Cuticular waxes are generally thought to provide the key component of the water barrier because cuticle permeability increases 100-1,000-fold following wax extraction, depending on the species (110). By contrast, significant reductions in cutin abundance can have a minimal effect on the cuticular transpiration rate, as was observed in a cutin deficient 2 (cd2) tomato mutant, the fruits of which have $\sim 2\%$ of wild-type cutin levels (50). Interestingly, in the same study, the fruits of the cd1 mutant, which have a marginally less severe reduction in cutin, showed a significantly increased transpiration rate. Indeed, most cutin-related mutants analyzed to date show increased cuticular permeability, usually in conjunction with an abnormal cuticle ultrastructure. However, comparative analyses of different varieties of pepper (Capsicum annuum) and of tomato fruit-ripening stages revealed no correlation between the amount or composition of cutin and its contribution to cuticular water resistance (57, 68). These results suggest that the amount of cutin does not determine the effectiveness of the cuticle as a barrier to limit water loss, but a properly formed and structured cutin matrix is critical. In this regard, cutin likely provides a hydrophobic scaffolding to hold the waxes (Figure 9a) and maintains the continuity of the cuticle across the plant surface.

# Organogenesis and Biomechanical Contribution

It has been proposed, based on several lines of evidence, that the growth of plant organs is constrained by their epidermal cell layer (66, 107). This epidermal-growth-control theory posits that the skin is under tension, resisting the expansion of the interior tissues. Indeed, when the skin is isolated, it contracts, whereas the remaining internal tissue expands (65). The cuticle, as part of the epidermis, is also under tension and similarly contracts when isolated (27). As the outermost layer, the cuticle would experience the strongest forces (27) and thus would require significant rigidity and strength. In two studies of tomato fruit, the cuticle was reported to account for either $\sim 25 - 50\%$ (3) or essentially all (82) of the mechanical strength of the peel. However, to accommodate external forces and deformation, the cuticle must also be capable of an elastic response, and plastic deformation along with the intercalation of new materials would be important to allow organ growth without cuticle rupture.

At low levels of biomechanical stress, the cuticle behaves elastically but becomes viscoelastic as stress increases (27), and several studies have investigated which cuticle component contributes to these properties. A comparison of isolated cuticles with purified cutin, from which embedded waxes and polysaccharides were removed, revealed that cutin is viscoelastic and contributes only a small amount to the overall stiffness and strength of the cuticle (77). In support of this conclusion, comparisons of the cuticles of Sonneratia alba leaves at various developmental stages (120) and of the fruits of 27 varieties of Japanese persimmon (Diospyros kaki) (126) showed that cutin amount correlates with cuticle viscoelasticity and lower stiffness, respectively. Taken together, these results indicate that cutin contributes plasticity but not strength to the cuticle, allowing it to be stretched during organ growth (Figure 9b).

Another significant role of the cuticle is to allow organs to fully separate from each other during organogenesis. Indeed, organ fusion is a typical characteristic of cuticle mutants, and such a phenotype occurs in mutants and suppression lines of almost every class of cutin biosynthetic gene (10, 62, 63, 72, 94, 115, 133, 134) and some cutin biosynthesis regulatory genes (115). By contrast, it has rarely been reported to result from wax-specific mutations. In addition, in a screen for mutants with disrupted cuticles, almost all mutants analyzed had some degree of organ fusion (121). Furthermore, ectopic expression of a pollen cutinase or fungal cutinase in A. thaliana, both of which severely disrupted the cuticle structure, also resulted in organ fusion (116, 119). The above studies suggest that the structural integrity of the cuticle, provided by cutin, is necessary to prevent organ fusion.

# Pathogen Barrier

The cuticle can play a major role in plant defenses against potential pathogens, and several studies have provided correlative evidence to suggest that the cutin component is critical in this regard. As an example, three cutin-deficient tomato mutants, $cd1$ , $cd2$ , and $cd3$ , all have fruit with a substantially increased susceptibility to infection by the fungus Botrytis cinerea (50). Saladié et al. (102) reported another correlation between cutin levels and resistance to $B$ . cinerea infection following an analysis of fruit from the delayed fruit deterioration (dfd) tomato genotype. However, more direct evidence that cutin is key to limiting pathogen infection comes from a study in which inhibiting a cutinase from a fungus substantially suppressed its ability to infect papaya (Carica papaya) fruit, whereas treatment of the fruit with the functional cutinase facilitated infection by what is normally only a wound pathogen (26).

Surprisingly, and in direct contrast to these studies with fruits, almost all $A$ . tbaliana cutin mutants tested to date have shown significantly higher $B$ . cinerea resistance (e.g., 8, 9, 122, 131).

Moreover, expression of a fungal cutinase in $A$ . thaliana conferred complete immunity against B. cinerea (18). Extracts that diffused from the leaf surface (known as diffusates) of the lacs2 cutin biosynthetic mutant and the cutinase-expressing line inhibited fungal development in vitro (9, 18), suggesting that the defective cuticles of these lines cause, or allow for, the release of antifungal compounds. The resistance of these lines could also be because their permeable cuticles facilitate the entry of defense elicitor compounds from the environment (9). In addition, Schweizer et al. (111) showed that rice and barley respond to the application of the products of cutin hydrolysis generated in vitro by inducing the expression of resistance genes. Both studies indicate that plants have evolved mechanisms to elicit defense responses upon perturbation of the cutin matrix. However, the defensive measures do not inhibit all microbes, as both lacs2 and the cutinase-expressing line have normal susceptibility to, and ineffective diffusates against, certain other fungal species (9, 18, 122). Furthermore, both the lacs2 and cyp86a2 mutants showed increased susceptibility to the bacterial pathogen Pseudomonas syringae (122, 136), implying that the leaf cuticle does have a defensive function in this interaction.

Based on these results, we propose that fleshy fruits and leaves have evolved distinct strategies to limit initial pathogen attack. Fruits produce substantial amounts of cutin, which serves as an effective physical barrier to prevent entry by most pathogens (Figure 9c). The typically much thinner cuticles of leaves (at least in $A$ . thaliana) can inhibit the entry of certain bacteria, such as $P$ . syringae, but are an insignificant obstacle to many pathogenic fungi. Leaves therefore employ a system in which degradation of the cutin network triggers defense responses and results in the release of antifungal compounds (Figure 9d). However, this idea is based on data from a limited variety of plants, and similar analyses should be performed with other species to test the hypothesis.

# SUMMARY POINTS

1. Cutin monomeric composition shows broad conservation across plant lineages, although slightly longer chain lengths are more typical of later-diverging plants.   
2. In the few species for which cutin structural information is available, it appears that terminal $(\omega -)$ esterification is nearly ubiquitous, although a considerable amount of midchain branching also occurs.   
3. Cutin monomers are synthesized from fatty acids through three successive sets of reactions: CoA conjugation, oxidation, and acyl transfer to glycerol.   
4. Specific GDSL enzymes, termed cutin synthases, polymerize cutin in vitro and in vivo.   
5. Cutin is a highly viscoelastic polymer with low tensile strength that provides structural continuity to the cuticle and a hydrophobic framework for the deposition of cuticular waxes.

# FUTURE ISSUES

1. Biochemical analysis of a greater diversity of early-diverging land plants and closely related algal relatives will help clarify the evolutionary origin of cutin and cuticles.   
2. Elucidation of the structure(s) of the cutin polymer will represent a major milestone. To what extent does cross-linking occur, and what effect does this have on cutin architecture and properties?

3. What is cutan, and how are its compositional and structural properties distinct from those of cutin? Such information will likely help elucidate its functional and evolutionary significance.   
4. How does cutin associate with cell wall polysaccharides? Can it form linkages to only one specific type of sugar or polysaccharide, or is the interaction more complex?   
5. What roles do the $BDG$ and $DCR$ genes play in the biosynthetic process?   
6. Do the paralogs of the identified CUS genes also contribute to cutin polymerization? If so, do they have different functional specificities? Are terminal and midchain esterification performed by distinct enzymes?   
7. Are hydrophobic cutin monomers actively transported through aqueous environments (namely, the cytosol and the cell wall) or do such processes involve stochastic diffusion?   
8. Which genes and pathways control alterations in cutin in response to environmental stresses?
