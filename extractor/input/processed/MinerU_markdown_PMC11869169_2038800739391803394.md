# Summary

Tomato fruit ripening is a complex developmental process that is important for fruit quality and shelf life. Many factors, including ethylene and several key transcription factors, have been shown to play important roles in the regulation of tomato fruit ripening. However, our understanding of the regulation of tomato fruit ripening is still limited. Here, we describe mut26, an EMS-induced tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) mutant that exhibits chlorophyll-deficient phenotypes in various organs, including fruits. Genetic mapping and functional analyses revealed that a single-nucleotide substitution and a corresponding Pro398–>Ser mis-sense mutation in SlChlH (GENOMES UNCOUPLED 5, GUN5), which encodes the H subunit of magnesium chelatase, are responsible for the defects in the mut26 strain. Transcript analyses towards the expression of many SlPhANGs revealed that mut26 is defective in plastid retrograde signalling during tomato fruit ripening initiation, namely the transition from mature green to breaker stage. mut26 exhibits delayed progression of fruit ripening characterized by reduced fruit ethylene emission, increased fruit firmness, reduced carotenoid content and delayed plastid conversion from chloroplast to chromoplast. Given that fruit ripening requires signalling from plastids to nucleus, these data support the hypothesis that GUN5-mediated plastid retrograde signalling promotes tomato fruit ripening. We further showed that the delayed fruit ripening of mut26 is not likely caused by reduced chlorophyll content. Taken together, we identified a new function of SlChlH in the promotion of tomato fruit ripening and ethylene biosynthesis, suggesting that GUN5-mediated plastid retrograde signalling plays a promotive role in tomato fruit ripening. 

# Introduction

Chloroplasts act as environmental sensors that communicate with the nucleus to regulate the expression of large numbers of nuclear genes via a mechanism known as retrograde signalling (Chan et al., 2016; Chi et al., 2013; de Souza et al., 2017; Nott et al., 2006; Richter et al., 2023; Wu and Bock, 2021). The process conveys information on the metabolic status of the plastid to the nucleus via multiple pathways, including tetrapyrrole biosynthesis (TPB), plastid gene expression, plastid redox state, reactive oxygen species (ROS) and plastid protein homeostasis. Norflurazon (Nf), a noncompetitive inhibitor of carotenoid biosynthesis that blocks thylakoid biogenesis without inducing photo-oxidative stress, is commonly utilized to investigate the retrograde signalling derived from TPB pathway (Kim and Apel, 2013). Through genetic screens based on Nf treatment, six GENOMES UNCOUPLED (GUN ) loci (GUN1–GUN6) have been identified in Arabidopsis (Koussevitzky et al., 2007; Larkin et al., 2003; Mochizuki et al., 2001; Susek et al., 1993; Woodson et al., 2011). These gun mutants are strongly inhibited by Nf in the early stages of seedling development. Moreover, 

expression of Photosynthesis-Associated Nuclear Genes (PhANGs) is efficiently repressed by Nf in wild-type (WT) plants, but partially derepressed in gun mutants. GUN1 encodes a chloroplast pentatricopeptide repeat (PPR) protein, but its mechanism of action remains elusive (Koussevitzky et al., 2007; Pesaresi and Kim, 2019; Wu et al., 2019). GUN2–GUN6 all participate in the TPB pathway (Wu and Bock, 2021). GUN2, GUN3 and GUN6 code for heme oxygenase, phytochromobilin synthase and ferrochelatase 1 (FC1), respectively (Mochizuki et al., 2001; Woodson et al., 2011). GUN4 encodes an activator of magnesium chelatase (MgCh) (Larkin et al., 2003), and GUN5 encodes the H subunit (ChlH) of MgCh (Mochizuki et al., 2001). In addition to regulate retrograde signalling, GUN4 and GUN5 are also important for chlorophyll biosynthesis in plants (Larkin et al., 2003; Mochizuki et al., 2001). The functions of these GUN proteins have been investigated mainly at the seedling stage in Arabidopsis, but their functions in crops, particularly during reproductive growth stages, have not been explored extensively. 

Tomato is an important model for studies of the development and ripening of fleshy fruits (Azzi et al., 2015; Klee and 

Giovannoni, 2011), which can in turn be divided into climacteric (e.g. tomato) and non-climacteric (e.g. grape) fruits. Initiation of ripening in climacteric fruits is associated with increased respiration and biosynthesis of the phytohormone ethylene. Conversely, non-climacteric fruits show no such increase in these processes at the onset of ripening. Defects in ethylene biosynthesis, perception and signalling significantly delay fruit ripening in tomato (Chen et al., 2004; Giovannoni, 2004; Huang et al., 2022; Wilkinson et al., 1995; Yang et al., 2010), highlighting the important function of the gas in tomato ripening. The initiation of ripening process is accompanied by extensive structural modifications that trigger the conversion of chloroplasts into chromoplasts. These alterations involve changes in the density and size of the organelle, the breakdown of chlorophyll, disruption of the thylakoid membrane and aggregation of carotenoids into crystals (Egea et al., 2010). The transformation also requires bi-directional communication between plastids and the nucleus to coordinate the expression of nuclear and plastid genomes. Previous reports suggest the association of plastid retrograde signalling with tomato fruit ripening. The tomato plastid ubiquitin E3 ligase SlSP1 and its homologue SlSPL2 both accelerate fruit maturation (Ling et al., 2021). Arabidopsis SP1 regulates chloroplast protein import by selectively targeting subunits/components of the translocon in the outer envelope of the chloroplast for degradation (Ling et al., 2012), and defects in components of the plastid protein-import apparatus lead to reduced expression of PhANGs (Kakizaki et al., 2009). It is speculated that tomato SlSP1 and SlSPL2 contribute to the regulation of a wide range of ripening-related genes through plastid protein homeostasis-mediated retrograde signalling (Ling et al., 2021). A possible role for plastid retrograde signalling in the regulation of fruit ripening in tomato has been proposed by several other groups (Pesaresi et al., 2014; Wu and Bock, 2021). Thus, it would be interesting to analyse whether fruit ripening is affected in tomato mutants defective in plastid retrograde signalling. 

We screened a population of tomatoes that had been mutagenized with ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) and identified the pale-green mutant mut26, which carries a mis-sense mutation in the gene SlChlH. mut26 exhibits typical gun phenotypes at seedling stages when treated with Nf, as well as during fruit ripening initiation. The mut26 exhibits delayed fruit ripening associated with reduced ethylene production, increased fruit firmness and delayed plastid conversion, all of which are genetically complemented by the genomic fragment of SlChlH. Our work revealed a novel function of SlChlH in the regulation of tomato fruit ripening. The defects of plastid retrograde signalling coincides with delayed fruit ripening in mut26, supporting the hypothesis that plastid retrograde signalling promotes tomato fruit ripening.

# Isolation of a chlorophyll-deficient tomato mutant

The EMS-induced tomato (cv. Ailsa Craig, AC) mutant mut26, which exhibits a yellow-green leaf phenotype, was grown under natural light and temperature conditions during the spring season in Shanghai (Figure 1a). Morphological analysis of the chloroplast ultrastructure of the yellowish mut26 leaves revealed collapsed thylakoid grana stacks and immature starch granules (Figure 1b). The yellow–green leaves of mut26 displayed reduced levels of 

chlorophyll fluorescence compared with that of WT (Figure S1a), which became more pronounced at low temperature $( 1 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ than at room temperature $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ (Figure 1c). Moreover, mut26 developed yellow–green stigma, floral style and ovary (Figure S1b). At the mature green (MG) stage, mut26 fruits developed a pale-green shoulder, while the base remained unpigmented (Figure 1d) and was associated with undeveloped chloroplasts (Figure S1c,d). 

# Genetic mapping of the chlorophyll-deficient tomato mutant

To identify the genetic locus responsible for the phenotypes observed in mut26, we crossed the mutant strain with a wild tomato species, LA1589 (Solanum pimpinellifolium), for bulked-segregant analysis (BSA) mapping, and we also backcrossed mut26 with AC for cleaved amplified polymorphic sequence (CAPS) analysis. The F1 individuals from both crosses did not show any of the growth defects seen in mut26 (Table S1). In each of the two F2 populations, the segregation ratio between WT and mutated individuals was approximately 3:1 (Table S1). This indicates that the chlorophyll-deficient phenotypes in different organs of mut26 are controlled by a single recessive locus. We chose the F2 population derived from the $m u t 2 6 \times \mathsf { L A } 1 5 8 9$ cross for BSA mapping. To facilitate the mapping, libraries were also constructed for both parental strains. Allele frequencies of two extreme trait pools of the F2 generation and their differences were calculated for each of the 12 chromosomes to form a Manhattan plot (Figure S2a). A confidence threshold exceeding $9 9 \%$ was located on chromosome 4 and was divided into three regions: 4.9–8.6 Mb, 9.1–51.5 Mb and 52–56 Mb (Figure 1e). 

To pinpoint the candidate locus, we prepared WT (AC) cultivar for library construction and sequencing. In total, 30 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and 10 insertion–deletion length polymorphisms (InDels) fulfilled the selection criteria, as listed in Figure S2b (Tables S2 and S3). Of these, only one SNP site (SL2.50ch04_6038590) was located within a coding region (Table S2). This SNP was found in SlChlH (Solyc04g015750) and leads to a single-nucleotide substitution and a corresponding Pro398–>Ser mis-sense mutation (Figure 1f). Since the nucleotide substitution in mut26 disrupts a HaeIII restriction-enzyme site, we employed a CAPS marker to further validate the mapping result with two independent F2 populations derived from $\mathsf { A C } \times m u t { 2 6 }$ and mut269LA1589 crosses (Figure S3). The SNP in SlChlH strictly co-segregated with the mutant phenotypes, including pale-green leaves, pale-green stigmas and pale-green fruits. The homozygous-to-heterozygous ratios in WT pools were 3:7 (mut269LA1589) and 7:12 (AC9mut26), each of which corresponds to a segregation ratio of approximately 1:2 (Figure S3). Hence, the BSA and CAPS analyses indicated that the SNP in SlChlH is likely responsible for the observed phenotypes in mut26. 

Notably, Pro398 in SlChlH is highly conserved in photosynthetic organisms ranging from cyanobacteria to flowering plants (Figure S4), indicating that it is important for the enzyme’s function. To investigate the effect of the SlChlHP398S mutation on the protein’s structure and function, we performed a structural analysis based on the published crystal structures of ChlH in the cyanobacterium Synechocystis (Adams et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2015). # Tetrapyrrole synthesis is reduced in

In Arabidopsis ChlH plays important roles in TPB, since it serves as the catalytic and substrate-binding subunit of MgCh (Figure S8a). Therefore, we examined the levels of tetrapyrrole intermediates in mut26 with HPLC. The total chlorophyll content of mut26 amounted to only ${ \sim } 4 3 \%$ of the WT level in leaves (Figure 3a), which is consistent with its known biochemical function in chlorophyll biosynthesis. Interestingly, the rate of 5-aminolevulinic acid (ALA) synthesis in mut26 amounts to ${ \sim } 4 7 \%$ of the WT (Figure 3b), which is likely attributable to the feedback regulation resulting from the low chlorophyll content. Moreover, the steady-state levels of all tetrapyrrole intermediates in mut26, including Mg protoporphyrin, Mg-protoporphyrin monomethylester, protochlorophyllide and chlorophyllide were reduced compared with both WT and the complemented mut26 line (Figure 3c). Interestingly, in contrast to the higher accumulation 

of protoporphyrin IX (Proto IX) expected as the result of perturbed catalytic substrate conversion, Proto IX levels in mut26 were comparable to those in WT (Figure 3d). This finding is consistent with previous work based on sense and antisense lines of ChlI in tobacco (Papenbrock et al., 2000) and reflects the yellow-green leaf phenotype, which contrasts with the necrosis of leaves owing to the photo-oxidative effects of porphyrin accumulation (Papenbrock and Grimm, 2001). In contrast, rates of accumulation of non-covalently bound heme were not markedly altered in mut26 (Figure 3e). 

In addition to SlChlH, the steady-state levels of TPB-related proteins were examined. These proteins encompass several key enzymes and regulators in the TPB pathway. # Fruit ripening is delayed in

The level of SlChlH transcripts increased from 35 days post-anthesis (dpa), reached their peak at 41 dpa and then declined (Figure 4a). During this period, the levels of SlChlH followed a pattern similar to that of the SlChlH transcript level (Figure 4b). This expression profile prompted us to analyse whether SlChlH itself regulates fruit ripening. 

Surprisingly, although chlorophyll content is reduced in mut26 (Figure S7c,f), the maximum quantum efficiency of PSII in leaves of mut26 was comparable to that in WT (Figure S10). Notably, the flowering time of mut26 was similar to that of the WT (Figure S11), indicating that the transition from vegetative to reproductive growth is not affected in mut26. Similar to WT, mut26 fruits reached the MG and BR stages at 35 dpa and 38 dpa, respectively (Figure 4c). The onset of the BR stage is considered as the initiation of tomato fruit ripening. Apart from the reduction in green pigmentation, the development of mut26 fruits up to the MG stage was similar to that of WT fruits, while the subsequent ripening process was delayed by about 3 days in mut26 with respect to the WT (Figure 4c). Ethylene emission increased rapidly from 35 to 38 dpa in WT fruits (Figure 4d; Figure S12). In contrast, ethylene emission was only moderately increased from 35 to 38 dpa in mut26 fruits and peaked at 41 dpa (Figure 4d). Thus, peak ethylene emission in mut26 is delayed by about 3 days relative to WT. Interestingly, complementation line #6 initiated ethylene emission earlier than the WT (Figure 4d), which is consistent with its early-ripening phenotype. It should be noted that mut26 fruits can eventually reach a comparable level of red ripeness as WT fruits (Figure S13), which indicates that SlChlH is not essential for fruit ripening. The delayed ripening of mut26 was associated with increased fruit firmness at 44 dpa, and indeed, this trait is genetically complemented to a WT level (Figure S14). 

Ripening of tomato fruits is associated with decreased chlorophyll levels and increased carotenoid contents. We 

measured total chlorophyll content in the pericarp of the fruit from 32 to 44 dpa. The total chlorophyll content in mut26 was lower than that in the WT prior to 38 dpa (Figure S15). At later stages, no obvious differences were detected (Figure S15). We analysed carotenoids in 44-dpa fruits with the aid of ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC). The levels of $\beta$ -carotene and lycopene were significantly lower in mut26 than in the WT line, while those of both carotenoids returned to the WT level in complementation line #6 (Figure 4e; Figure S16). Only trace amounts of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -carotene were detected in the WT, and these were undetectable in mut26 (Figure S16). We also examined the plastid ultrastructure of the fruit shoulder using transmission electron microscopy. At 35 dpa, mut26 had developed thinner thylakoid grana stacks, which was consistent with its low chlorophyll content (Figure 4f). At 38 dpa, the thylakoid structure of the WT plastid was degraded and was replaced by long, linear thylakoid membrane structures (LTH), whereas mut26 contained a relatively intact thylakoid network (Figure 4f). At 41 dpa, WT plastids differentiated into typical mature chromoplasts, characterized by the appearance of carotenoid crystalloids (CCR) and the loss of thylakoid structure, whereas mut26 plastids only formed LTH (Figure 4f). Plastoglobuli (PGs) can bind and store carotenoids, and the number of PGs normally increases during fruit ripening. However, we found that the number of PGs was lower in mut26 than in the WT at both 38 and 41 dpa, and that this trait was complemented in complementation line #6 (Figure 4g). 

We measured several agronomic characteristics of red ripen fruits. The cracking rate of mut26 fruits was lower than that seen in WT (Figure S17a,b). Average fruit weight and total soluble solids were lower in mut26 than in the WT (Figure S17b). The vertical diameter and transverse diameter of mut26 fruits were on average similar to those of WT (Figure S17b). Thus, mut26 fruits exhibited various phenotypes characteristic of delayed ripening, including low ethylene emission, low carotenoid content, high firmness and slow plastid conversion, highlighting a new function of SlChlH in tomato fruit ripening.

The protein HY5 has been proposed to act in the GUN5-mediated retrograde signalling pathway in Arabidopsis (Kindgren et al., 2012). To explore the genetic relationship between GUN5 and SlHY5, we analysed the impact of the latter protein on the GUN5-mediated plastid retrograde signalling pathway in the mut26 Slhy5 double mutant.

# Discussion

Two major functions of ChlH have been reported in plants. ChlH plays essential roles in chlorophyll biosynthesis, as it serves as the catalytic and substrate-binding subunit of MgCh (Karger et al., 2001; Willows and Beale, 1998). Secondly, some mutations in ChlH lead to gun phenotypes in Arabidopsis and also in rice (Kindgren et al., 2012; Mochizuki et al., 2001; Wang et al., 2023). Previous work in Arabidopsis demonstrated that the ChlH-mediated plastid retrograde signalling pathways are important for early chloroplast biogenesis and environmental adaptation (Chen et al., 2017; Kindgren et al., 2012). Here, we reported a new function of ChlH 

in the regulation of tomato fruit ripening. The EMS-mutant mut26 carries a single amino acid substitution in SlChlH, SlChlHP398S, leads to delayed ethylene emission and several other features characteristic of delayed fruit ripening. Notably, the mut26 mutant only showed moderately delayed fruit ripening. It is quite conceivable that a stronger, but still viable tomato gun5 mutant would have a more pronounced phenotype during fruit ripening. 

SlChlH regulates tomato fruit ripening either via plastid retrograde signalling or via metabolic defects associated with reduced chlorophyll accumulation as observed in mut26. Although no direct evidence provided, previous independent reports indicate that plastid signals are important for plastid conversion and tomato fruit ripening. Proteomic studies of tomato during the chloroplast-to-chromoplast transition have revealed a decreased abundance of photosynthesis-related proteins (Barsan et al., 2012; Szymanski et al., 2017) whose coding genes are targets of plastid-derived retrograde signals. Previous studies have identified several tomato mutants that exhibit delayed fruit ripening phenotypes due to deficiencies in plastid proteins. Among these, the lutescent1 (l1, LA3717) and l2 (LA3581) mutants display a range of chlorophyll-deficient phenotypes in leaves and fruits. The REDUCED CHLOROPHYLL MUTANT 1 (SlRCM1) has been proposed as a potential causal gene underlying the chlorophyll-deficient phenotypes observed in the $\boldsymbol { { I } } \boldsymbol { { \mathcal { I } } }$ mutant (Liu et al., 2021). SlRCM1 is homologous to the Arabidopsis gene BALANCE OF CHLOROPHYLL METABOLISM 

(BCM1), which functions as a MgCh regulator, contributing to the stability of MgCh. BCM1 has also been proposed to support the interaction with GUN4 and Mg transport (Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020). The L2 gene encodes the tomato homologue of the Arabidopsis ETHYLENE-DEPENDENT GRAVITROPISM DEFI-CIENT AND YELLOW-GREEN1 (EGY1), which is a chloroplasttargeted zinc metalloprotease belonging to the M50 family (Barry et al., 2012). In l1 and $_ { I 2 }$ mutants, the onset of fruit ripening is delayed by approximately 7 days; however, once the ripening is initiated, these mutants proceed to ripen at a normal rate (Barry et al., 2012). Notably, the late fruit ripening phenotypes of $\boldsymbol { { I } } \boldsymbol { { \mathcal { I } } }$ and l2 mutants are distinct from those of mut26. The mut26 mutant does not affect the timing of the onset of ripening but specifically influences the ripening degree from the MG stage onwards (Figure 4c–g). However, whether the $1 1$ and $_ { I 2 }$ are gun mutants need further investigation. Here, we provide further evidence that the tomato gun mutant mut26 exhibits delayed fruit ripening. Overall, evidences from different angles all support an important role of plastid retrograde signalling in tomato fruit ripening. 

However, it cannot be excluded that the reduced chlorophyll content and its secondary effects in mut26 also contribute to the delayed fruit ripening. Fruit ripening is regulated by several phytohormones, including ethylene and ABA. We observed that the content of carotenoids, which serve as precursors for ABA biosynthesis, are reduced in mut26. Moreover, silencing of ABA receptors leads to reduced ethylene biosynthesis and delayed tomato fruit ripening (Zou et al., 2022). ABA signalling appears to be not affected in mut26 fruits, since the expression pattern of ABA signalling genes is similar in mut26 and WT during tomato fruit ripening initiation (Figure S22; Dataset S5). It should be noted that the reduced chlorophyll content in mut26 did not affect overall plant development before fruit ripening initiation. Importantly, Slhy5 has reduced chlorophyll content (Zhang et al., 2022) and decreased carotenoid levels in fruits (Figure 7b). However, Slhy5 exhibits WT levels of ethylene emission and normal expression of ripening-related genes at both MG and BR stages (Figure 7c,d). Thus, it appears that the reduction in the levels of chlorophyll and carotenoid is not likely the major cause of the phenotypes associated with decreased ethylene emission and delayed fruit ripening phenotype in mut26. Whether the reduced carotenoid contents in mut26 affects ABA and other phytohormones, that ultimately affects fruit ripening is an important issue for future investigation. 

HY5 has been proposed to regulate the expression of PhANGs downstream of GUN5 in Arabidopsis (Kindgren et al., 2012). However, direct evidence for an epistatic relationship between GUN5 and HY5 in the retrograde signalling pathway has been lacking, since gun5 hy5 double mutant is not available. In tomato, these genes are located on different chromosomes, which enabled us to generate a mut26 Slhy5 double mutant. Transcript analysis of SlPhANGs in Nf-treated seedlings revealed that SlHY5 is epistatic to GUN5 in plastid retrograde signalling. Interestingly, Slhy5 itself is not a gun mutant under Nf treatment. This is consistent with the previous finding that Arabidopsis hy5 is a weak gun mutant in the presence of lincomycin, an inhibitor of protein translation in plastids (Ruckle et al., 2007). It is conceivable that GUN5-mediated plastid retrograde signalling impedes the positive regulatory effect of SlHY5 on SlPhANG expression, at least in seedlings. However, GUN5 is insufficient to inhibit the transcription of SlPhANGs through the suppression of SlHY5 function; it is also necessary to activate negative regulators to inhibit the expression of SlPhANGs. It has been proposed that 

the activity of cytoplasmic HEAT SHOCK PROTEIN 90 (HSP90) protein is positively correlated with the expression of PhANGs (Kindgren et al., 2012; Wu et al., 2019). We propose that GUN5 represses the transcription of SlPhANGs by inhibiting the function of SlHY5, in conjunction with the suppression of HSP90 activity and potentially other unknown components. 

We attempt to analyse whether GUN5 regulates fruit ripening via SlHY5. Unfortunately, only one to two fruits can be harvested from a given mut26 Slhy5 double mutant plant, it is not practically possible to analyse its fruit ripening phenotype. We turn to analyse Slhy5 single mutant for fruit ripening. It was reported that the expression of RIN, CNR and FUL1 was shown to be down-regulated in Slhy5 lines relative to WT (Wang et al., 2021). However, we found that the expression levels of these ripening-related genes in Slhy5 remain at WT levels during both MG and BR stages (Figure 7d). Moreover, we found that both ethylene emission and the progression of ripening in Slhy5 fruits follow a timetable, which is similar to that seen in WT (Figure 7a,c). This discrepancy is likely attributable to the different sampling approaches employed in these two studies. In the previous report, fruits were sampled based on their age, that is dpa (Wang et al., 2021). Fruits of the same age (39 dpa) harvested from WT and Slhy5 were directly compared for ethylene emission and gene expression patterns (Wang et al., 2021). However, we observed that Slhy5 mutant fruits exhibit about a 3-day delay in reaching the MG stage. Thus, 39 dpa WT fruits are probably at the BR stage, whereas 39 dpa Slhy5 fruits are still at the MG stage (Wang et al., 2021). With these distinguishable development stages of the fruit material, we found that SlHY5 is dispensable for fruit ethylene emission and enhances expression of key ripening-related genes (Figure 7c,d). Consistently, it is recently reported that ethylene emission in Slhy5 mutant is of WT levels in red ripening fruits (Jia et al., 2024). 

Intriguingly, SlHY5 has nevertheless been shown to bind to the promoters of these ripening-related genes (Wang et al., 2021). Further work is needed to clarify the biological relevance of this. Thus, except for the control of SlPhANGs, open questions remain with respect to how GUN5 contributes to the control of the expression of ripening-related genes. Similar to Arabidopsis HY5, SlHY5 requires cofactors to regulate target-gene expression (Yang et al., 2022). Considering that expression of SlHY5 is GUN5- dependently induced during fruit ripening (Figure S20), GUN5 suppresses the positive regulatory role of SlHY5 on the transcription of SlPhANGs during this process by inhibiting its cofactors. Based on these findings and previous reports, we propose a working model (Figure 7e) in which GUN5 impinges on the transcriptional control of nuclear gene expression during fruit ripening in two ways: (1) GUN5 activates SlHY5 expression. SlHY5 acts together with an unknown factor Y to promote the expression of SlPhANGs, and Y is inhibited by signals originating from the chloroplasts through GUN5. The inhibition of cofactor Y is sufficient to repress SlHY5’s ability to positively regulate SlPhANGs. Meanwhile, GUN5 may inhibits the expression of SlPhANGs through HSP90 or other factors. (2) Transcription of GUN5 is induced during fruit ripening, and GUN5 promotes the expression of ripening-related genes via unknown transcription factors. 

In summary, fruit ripening in tomato is a complex metabolic process that is regulated by diverse internal and external factors. We have reported here that impaired SlChlH function leads to delayed ethylene emission and fruit ripening associated with reduced plastid retrograde signalling. Our work reports the first tomato gun5 mutant and will serve as a platform for further 

investigations of the regulation of tomato fruit ripening by plastid retrograde signalling.

# Bulk Segregant analysis

For BSA mapping, the mut26 was crossed with the wild ecotype LA1589, and the resulting F1 progeny were allowed to selfpollinate and produce F2 seeds. For whole-genome sequencing, DNA samples were extracted from tomato leaves using a Plant DNA Extraction Kit (TIANGEN, CHN). Five bulked DNA sample pools, each consisting of 30 individuals with WT or mutant-type phenotype drawn from the isolated F2 population, and six individuals from each parent were prepared for library construction. Libraries were sequenced using the Illumina NovaSeq platform by Personalbio Biotechnology Co., Ltd (CHN). The sequencing depth of each library is shown in Table S7. The generated reads were mapped and assembled based on the reference genome (Heinz1706, SL2.50, https://solgenomics. net). Like AC and LA1589, Heinz1706 exhibited normal chlorophyll phenotypes. Qualified SNP sites were chosen in conformity with the following standards: (i) they must be homozygous and differ between mut26 and LA1589 pools; (ii) their sequencing depth must exceed $5 \times$ in all pools; and (iii) the SNP index must be greater than 0.3 in all pools. 

# Carotenoid measurements

Carotenoid content was determined with the Waters Acquity Ultra-performance Convergence Chromatography (UPC2 ) system (Waters Corp., Milford, MA, USA) as described in a previous report (Zhao et al., 2021). 

# Ethylene measurement

Freshly detached fruits were weighed and kept at room temperature for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ to eliminate the ethylene triggered by harvesting stress, and then sealed in airtight gas collectors for $3 \textmd { h }$ . Then, $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of the collected gas was injected into a GC-2010 gas chromatograph (Shimadzu Co., Ltd., Japan). The gas 

standard used contained 1 ppm ethylene (Weichuang, CHN), and ethylene emission was calculated according to a recent report (Zhao et al., 2021). 

# Chlorophyll quantification and chlorophyll fluorescence imaging

Plant tissues were ground into powders with liquid nitrogen, extracted with $80 \%$ acetone (v/v) solution and kept in darkness for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . After centrifugation at $1 0 \ 0 0 0 \ { \pmb g }$ for 5 min, the gabsorption of 1 mL of the clear supernatant was measured at 663 nm and 646 nm with a spectrophotometer (UV1800, CHN). Formulas for chlorophyll content calculation are as follows: Chlorophyll $a = 1 2 . 2 1 \times A _ { 6 6 3 } – 2 . 8 1 \times A _ { 6 4 6 } ;$ ; Chlorophyll $b = 2 0 . 1 3 \times A _ { 6 4 6 } – 5 . 0 3 \times A _ { 6 6 3 }$ . Chlorophyll fluorescence was observed using a Leica SP8 confocal microscope (GER). The excitation and emission wavelengths of chlorophyll fluorescence are 488 nm and 650–750 nm, respectively. 

# Protein extraction and western blot analysis

Levels of TPB-related proteins were assessed in total leaf extracts. The fresh leaf material was homogenized in liquid nitrogen and dissolved in $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L / m g }$ protein extraction buffer (56 mM ${ \mathsf { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { C O } } _ { 3 } ,$ $2 \%$ (w/v) SDS, $12 \%$ (w/v) sucrose and 2 mM EDTA). Protein extracts were fractionated by $10 \%$ or $12 \%$ SDS-PAGE, followed by transfer onto nitrocellulose membranes. Antibodies against GluTR, GSAT, FC1, GUN4, POR, CHLM and FLU were generated in the Grimm laboratory. The CHL27 antibody (AS06 122) was purchased from Agrisera (V€ann€as). Furthermore, antibodies against ChlI and ChlD were kindly donated by Prof. Meizhong Luo. Anti-ChlH and anti-ACTIN antibodies obtained from Orizymes (PAB09012) and Sigma (A0480), respectively. Immunoblotting signals visualized by ClarityTM Western ECL (Bio-Rad) were detected with a CCD camera (Intas Biopharmaceuticals). 

# Isolation and HPLC analysis of tetrapyrrole intermediates

Pigments and tetrapyrrole intermediates were isolated from fresh leaf material $( \sim 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m g } )$ of 33-day-old plants with $0 . 3 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of basic acetone (acetone: 0.2 M $N H _ { 4 } O H$ ; 9:1) for the data shown in Figure 3. Samples were incubated for 20 min at $- 2 0 \textdegree C$ and centrifuged for 30 min (4°C, 16100 ). The clear supernatant gobtained after centrifugation was used for quantification of the pigments, including chlorophyll, the tetrapyrrole intermediates Proto IX, MgP, MgPMME, Pchlide and Chlide, which were analysed by HPLC using the Agilent 1100, 1260 or 1290 HPLC system equipped with a diode array and a fluorescence detector (Agilent Technologies). The non-covalently bound heme was extracted from the pellet by incubation in acetone:hydrochloric acid:dimethylsulfoxide (10:0.5:2, v/v/v) for 20 min at room 

temperature. Identification and quantification of pigments and tetrapyrrole intermediates were carried out with the aid of authentic standards. 

# Analysis of plastid ultrastructure

The samples from leaves and fruit pericarps were prepared according to a recent report (Zhao et al., 2021). The ultrastructure of plastids was observed by TEM (Tecnai G2 Spirit Biotwin, USA).

ALA-synthesizing capacity was measured as described previously (Hou et al., 2021).
