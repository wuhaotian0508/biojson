# Foliar dewdroplet–induced redox cascades promote early flowering in Brassicaceae plants

Atmospheric water inputs such as dew are often overlooked in plant biology. We show that foliar dewdrops act as biochemical microreactors that trigger flowering in Arabidopsis thaliana, a small plant from the mustard family (Brassicaceae). These droplets generate reactive oxygen species that induce intracellular nitric oxide accumulation, initiating a redox cascade that suppresses the biosynthesis of abscisic acid, which contributes to the regulation of flowering time. This occurs via S-nitrosylation-mediated activation of histone deacetylase 19, which silences AAO3 and ABA2. Analysis of over 12 million field records across the Brassicaceae family reveals a global correlation between dew point temperature and flowering time. These findings identify dewdrop chemistry as an unrecognized environmental cue that regulates developmental timing in plants.

Flowering is a critical phenological event in the plant life cycle, governed by complex interactions among environmental cues such as photoperiod and temperature (1–4). While increasing evidence shows that global warming is shifting plant phenology by days or even weeks (5, 6), these changes cannot be fully attributed to photoperiod, which remains seasonally fixed, or to direct thermal stress, given that global temperatures have risen only about $0 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ over the past 30 y (7). A more fundamental yet often overlooked consequence of global warming lies in its effect on atmospheric moisture dynamics (8). According to the Clausius–Clapeyron relationship, the water-holding capacity of near-surface air increases by roughly $7 \%$ per Kelvin, altering the formation and distribution of nonrainfall water sources such as dew (9, 10). Dew is a key pathway for atmospheric water to return to ecosystems. In early-leafing plants, microdroplets preferentially condense on leaf surfaces, particularly on trichomes, during spring, when diurnal temperature fluctuations are more pronounced (11). 

Recent studies reveal that both microdroplet condensation on solid surfaces and water– solid contact can spontaneously generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) (12–17), which are well-known regulators of plant development through signaling and epigenetic mechanisms (18–20). This raises the intriguing possibility that dewdrops may influence phenological transitions such as flowering. However, the specific chemical reactions occurring within these leaf-associated microdroplets, and their broader implications for plant metabolism and development, remain largely unexplored. Here, we investigate the chemical nature of dewdrops on Arabidopsis leaves and their potential role in triggering flowering. 

# Results and Discussion

Observing Dew-Driven Flowering. Flowering and meteorological data of Arabidopsis thaliana in the wild from March to May in 2021 and 2022 in the Utrecht region of the Netherlands were collected and analyzed as shown in Fig. 1A. We operationally defined dew events as periods when ground surface temperature dropped below the dew point, indicating the presence of dew condensation. Flowering consistently followed such dew condensation events in March and May 2021 and again in May 2022, although the specific dates of floral transition varied between years. Importantly, by restricting observations to the March–May window, we averaged the relatively constant photoperiod to uncover additional factors influencing flowering behavior that are often masked by its dominant seasonal effect. While average air temperatures fluctuated slightly across the observation periods, temperature alone was unlikely to account for the observed flowering dynamics. To assess whether this association extended beyond local conditions and individual years, we expanded our analysis to include nationwide records of Arabidopsis thaliana flowering phenology and meteorological data spanning 2004 to 2023 (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). # Significance

Water microdroplets are regarded as microreactors capable of generating various reactive species, including reactive oxygen species (ROS), at their interfaces. However, our understanding of the impact of microdroplet chemistry on the natural world remains limited. This study reveals that during dew formation on Arabidopsis thaliana leaves, radical cascade reactions occur within the microdewdrops on leaf trichomes, influencing the synthesis of abscisic acid and thereby promoting early flowering. Globally, changes in dew point temperature affect the flowering events of Brassicaceae plants. Against the backdrop of global warming, these findings enhance our comprehension of the advanced timing of plant flowering. More importantly, this research presents a case in which a microscopic interfacial chemical reaction within droplets influences macroscopic events in the natural world.

NO Generation in Foliar Dewdroplet. To investigate the chemical processes occurring within microdewdrops on A. thaliana leaves, we developed an experimental model that mimics natural condensation. Given that microdroplets preferentially form at the surface of trichomes during dew formation, we manually placed microdroplets on these structures to replicate the spatial distribution of natural dew (Fig.  2 A and $B$ and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S2). We introduced a water-soluble ROS-sensitive probe, 10-acetyl-3,7-dihydroxyphenoxazine into the microdroplets. As shown in Fig. $2 C$ and $S I$ Appendix, Fig. S3, strong fluorescence signals indicated spontaneous ROS generation within the microdewdrop condensates found on trichomes. Additionally, SI  Appendix, Fig.  S4 indicates that the microdewdrops on the trichomes will disappear along with the evaporation process of water, and the related ROS fluorescence signal also disappears accordingly. To determine the source of the fluorescence signals, we placed plant leaves in an environmental simulation chamber, controlled the leaf temperature below the dew point, collected the condensate from the leaf surfaces, and allowed it to react with free radical trapping agents to indirectly analyze the types of free 

radicals in the microdewdrops. As shown in SI Appendix, Fig. S5, the fluorescence signals that we observed from the microdewdrops originate from hydroxyl radicals. Since ROS are essential for plant growth, the ROS observed in microdewdrops may act as a signaling molecule for growth regulation (21). 

To explore downstream chemical transformations, we detected the presence of nitric oxide (NO), a key signaling molecule in plants (21), using a NO-specific fluorescent probe (3-amino, 4-aminomethyl- $\cdot 2 ^ { \prime } , 7 ^ { \prime }$ -difluorescein, diacetate, DAF-FM DA (A nonfluorescent compound that can react with NO to produce a fluorescent product). As shown in Fig. $2 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S6, NO generation was observed in microdroplets on the leaf surface. This NO may be generated from reactions between ROS and amine, amide, or nitrite groups present on the surface of trichomes (shown in SI Appendix, Fig. S7) (22, 23). These findings suggest that the condensation of atmospheric water can initiate a cascade of redox chemistry on the leaf surface, including the formation of NO, with potential implications for signal transduction in plants. This observation prompts a fundamental question: can these extracellular chemical processes, triggered by dew condensation, influence intracellular biochemical processes?

Under these conditions, the leaf surface temperature stabilized at $2 5 . 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S8), below the dew point, resulting in persistent condensation on the leaf surface.

After $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ of dew exposure, we analyzed NO accumulation in guard cells using DAF-FM DA, which can cross the cell membrane and react with intracellular NO. As shown in SI  Appendix, Fig.  S9, the fluorescence intensity of NO in the guard cells of dew-exposed leaves was much higher than that in leaves without dewdrops. We suggest that NO likely enters the leaf primarily via diffusion through stomata, while the cuticular wax layer presents a significant barrier to its penetration. Consistent with this localized observation, we detected a significant increase in seedling NO content following dew exposure (Fig. 2E), whereas the activity of nitric oxide synthase (NOS) remained unchanged between dewtreated and untreated control samples (Fig. 2F). It should be noted that NOS activity was measured using a commercial kit based on the L-arginine reaction to generate NO and L-citrulline. These results indicate that microdewdrops on the leaf surface chemically elevate NO levels in leaf cells. 

It is well established that NO-related signaling pathways involve S-nitrosylation of proteins of a wide range of physiological activities in plants (24–26), including the histone deacetylase HDA19 that is involved in the regulation of flowering time (27–30). HDA19 is present in both cytoplasm and nucleus and appears in two different protein complexes in native nonreducing gels (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Interestingly, dew exposure increases molecular masses (MM) of the HDA19 complexes in both cytoplasm and nucleus (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Analysis of the HDA19 protein complexes 

by mass spectrometry identified several peptides of the glutaredoxin GRXS17 (SI Appendix, Fig. S11), suggesting that the complexes comprise GRXS17. Coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) assays confirmed that HDA 19 interacts with GRXS17 (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). 

GRXS17 is also a NO target (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). At the cellular level, dew exposure led to an increase in NO levels in the cell (Fig. 3A). Similarly, the interaction between HDA19 and GRXS17 in the nucleus was highly enhanced after dew exposure. To investigate the relationship between NO and the proteins, we generated the complementation plants by transforming tagged GRXS17 and HDA19 into their respective loss-of-function mutants (which display early flowering) (28, 29). As shown in Fig. 3B and SI Appendix, Fig. S14, the tagged HDA19 and the GRXS17 proteins exhibit enhanced S-nitrosylation in complementation lines under dew exposure. In GRXS17 mutant background, S-nitrosylation of HDA19 was undetectable. By contrast, HDA19 mutation has no effect on S-nitrosylation of GRXS17. Furthermore, GRXS17 displays a higher NO-binding rate than HDA19 (SI Appendix, Fig. S15A) and can mediate S-nitrosylation of HDA19 in vitro (SI Appendix, Fig. S15 B and C). These findings suggest that GRXS17 may serve as a transnitrosylation mediator, transferring NO to HDA19. This is supported by yeast two-hybrid assays and computer modeling showing specific interactions between the 3 glutaredoxin domains of GRXS17 and the catalytic domain of HDA19 of which several Cys residues are S-nitrosylated (SI Appendix, Fig. S16). Moreover, we assessed the deacetylase activity of fully complemented lines with wild type HDA19 (W) alongside partially complemented plants with mutant HDA19 carrying Cys 137 to Ala point mutations (M). The fully complemented HDA19 lines exhibit significantly elevated deacetylase activity under dew conditions (Fig. 3C), consistent with previous reports that S-nitrosylation enhances HDA19 activity (30). Although the point-mutation lines also show increased deacetylase activity following dew exposure, this enhancement is markedly reduced compared to that in the fully complemented plants. This partial increase in deacetylase activity of the point mutation lines under dew may be explained by the presence of other S-nitrosylation sites in the HDA19 protein (SI Appendix, Fig. S17). Together, these results suggest that NO generated by extracellular microdewdrops can modulate histone deacetylase activity through GRXS17-mediated S-nitrosylation to control plant growth (31). 

ChIP-seq profiling revealed that dew exposure enhances HDA19 binding across the genome in leaf tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S18 and Dataset S1). We observed altered expression of $F T$ (FLOWERING LOCUS T) under dew conditions, supporting the involvement of the NO-HDA19 pathway in flowering regulation (SI Appendix, Fig. S20A). However, the biochemical network underlying this process appears complex. Integrated epigenomic analysis identified 21 direct HDA19 target sites showing both increased HDA19 occupancy and reduced promoter H3K14ac levels under dew conditions (SI Appendix, Fig. S19 and Dataset S2). These loci are involved in signaling and metabolic pathways activated by NO, which accumulates in response to dew. Coexpression network analysis further identified six gene modules enriched for signaling and metabolic processes that correlated with both NO accumulation and FT expression. Convergent enrichment analysis revealed three key GO terms central to the dew-NO-HDA19-FT axis (SI Appendix, Figs. $S 2 0 B$ and S21). Transcriptome profiling revealed 29 dew-inhibited genes with significant lower expression in the fully complemented lines relative to point-mutation lines (SI Appendix, Fig. S22 A and B). For evaluating the impact of microdewdrops on ABA formation in leaves, we cultivated A. thaliana under standard growth conditions (16 h light/8 h dark) with and without microdroplet spray treatments. As shown in Fig. $4 D$ , ABA levels in the control plants remained relatively stable $\mathrm { \tilde { ( } { \sim } 6 ~ n g / g }$ fresh weight) from 7 to 15 d post two-leaf stage. In contrast, microdroplet-treated plants exhibited a progressive decline in ABA concentration, decreasing from ${ \sim } 5 \mathrm { n g / g }$ at day 7 to ${ < } 2 \mathrm { n g } / \mathrm { g }$ by day 15. Phenotypically, these plants displayed premature bolting and flowering within 1 wk of treatment, whereas control plants remained in the vegetative phase (Fig. 4E and SI Appendix, Fig. S23). Genetic analysis of flowering time in hda19 and grxs17 loss-of-function mutants is confounded by their inherent early-flowering phenotypes, which masks any dew-specific effects (28–29, 31). To directly test the role of S-nitrosylation in this pathway, we employed a targeted approach using an HDA19c137a point-mutant, which specifically abrogates this redox-sensitive modification while preserving the protein’s canonical function (30). In contrast to lines fully complemented with wild-type HDA19, the HDA19c137a lines showed no significant dew-induced decrease in ABA content and no corresponding alteration in flowering time (SI Appendix, Fig. S24). The decreased ABA synthesis may enhance gibberellic acid accumulation and signaling leading to premature flowering. Alternatively, HDA19 may repress other negative regulators of flowering. 

Regardless, the results provide compelling evidence that the extracellular chemical processes that occurred in microdewdrops on leaves can influence intracellular biochemical processes and the flowering timing of A. thaliana . It is important to emphasize that dewdropmediated NO generation occurs after leaf unfolding, thereby influencing plant development at a later stage. This contrasts with earlier studies focusing on endogenous NO, which examined its role throughout the entire plant life cycle, including processes such as root development and flowering initiation (33). Furthermore, plant flowering is regulated by a complex network of biochemical reactions. Previous studies have shown that mechanical signals mediated by processes such as rainfall can promote flowering by activating the JA/MYC2 signaling pathway (34–35). We hypothesize that after rainfall, the microdroplets remaining on the leaf surface may also trigger chemical processes similar to those occurring in microdewdrops, thereby influencing the flowering process in plants. However, considering that raindrops may contain more compounds compared to dewdroplets, the corresponding redox reactions are likely to be more complex and require further investigation. 

Global Dew Point Impact on Flowering. As a globally distributed family of broad-leaved angiosperms, Brassicaceae predominantly inhabit temperate regions, where dew condensation frequently occurs on leaf surfaces. To broaden our investigation beyond species-specific boundaries and better understand the potential impact of in  vitro physicochemical processes occurring within microdewdrops, we extended our analysis to the whole Brassicaceae family. To assess the influence of environmental factors on flowering, we assembled a meteorological dataset derived from 670 weather stations located closest to the respective flowering observation sites. This dataset spans the same 34-y period and includes 11 daily meteorological variables: temperature, dew point temperature, mean sea level pressure, station pressure, wind speed, atmospheric visibility, maximum temperature, minimum temperature, precipitation, and relative humidity. We employed generalized linear mixed-effect models (GLMMs) to evaluate the probabilistic contribution of these variables to flowering dynamics (36), using flowering counts as the response variable (SI Appendix, Fig. S25) and meteorological factors as predictors (SI Appendix, Fig. S26). Since photoperiod is a biological construct not directly captured by meteorological measurements, we statistically controlled for its effect by incorporating geographic coordinates (latitude, longitude) and the date of observation (year, day-of-year) as foundational covariates in all models. The effects of proximate meteorological variables (e.g., dew point depression, visibility) and their interactions with the spatiotemporal baseline are then systematically evaluated through model comparisons (SI Appendix, Figs. S27–S30 and Datasets S7 and S8). Additionally, we hypothesize that under light conditions, the concentration of ROS in microdewdrops on leaf surfaces and the related redox reactions may undergo changes, potentially creating a coupling effect between dew and photoperiod on the flowering process of plants. This possible foliar chemical process warrants further investigation. 

As shown in Fig. 5B and Datasets S7 and S8, dew point temperature emerged as the most significant positive predictor of flowering frequency across the Brassicaceae family (contribution $= 0 . 1 1 8$ , $P =$ $1 . 1 \dot { 8 } \times \dot { 1 0 } ^ { - 7 } \dot { }$ ). The interaction between dew point temperature and visibility also had a positive effect $( P { = } 0 . 0 1 1 )$ ), suggesting that sunlight acting on dewdrops may contribute to foliar redox cascades involved in the regulation of flowering. In contrast, several variables showed significant negative associations with flowering frequency, including precipitation (contribution $= - 0 . 0 2 9$ , $ { P = 2 . 9 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } } )$ ), mean sea level pressure (contribution $= - 0 . 0 1 5$ , $P = 1 . 5 5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 } )$ , and atmospheric visibility alone (contribution $= - 0 . 0 0 8$ , $P = 8 . 9 4 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } .$ ). These findings support the hypothesis that dew formation may act as a 

global-scale promoter of flowering in the Brassicaceae family. We suspect that these findings can be generalized to other plant types, but this remains to be established. Importantly, this effect does not negate the well-established roles of photoperiod and temperature in floral regulation. Instead, we propose that leaf-surface microdewdrops serve as a supplementary modulator of flowering timing. In the context of global climate change, these results underscore the importance of considering fluctuations in nonrainfall water inputs, such as dew, in shaping plant phenology and reproductive strategies. Moreover, given the foundational role of plants as primary producers, shifts in atmospheric hydrology may have far-reaching implications for ecosystem dynamics and the geographic boundaries of environmentally sensitive zones. 

# Conclusions

Our findings uncover a previously unrecognized role for microdewdrops on plant surfaces as transient yet potent biochemical microreactors that can trigger systemic developmental transitions. In Arabidopsis , we show that dew condensation initiates redox cascades involving reactive oxygen species (ROS) and nitric oxide (NO), modulating intracellular signaling, altering gene expression, and accelerating flowering. This condensation-driven mechanism operates alongside classical photoperiodic and thermal cues, adding a dimension to our understanding of how plants integrate environmental signals. While demonstrated here in Arabidopsis , this mechanism may represent a more general strategy by which plants sense and respond to atmospheric moisture. As climate change alters atmospheric moisture dynamics, our results suggest that microdroplet chemistry may influence plant developmental timing at ecosystem scales, with broad implications for biodiversity, agriculture, and biosphere–atmosphere interactions. These findings redefine the ecological significance of water–plant interfaces and highlight how microscale physicochemical processes can shape macroscopic biological outcomes. 

# Materials and Methods

Plant Materials and Treatments. Arabidopsis thaliana wild-type (Col-0), hda19 and grxs17 mutants, and complementation lines (HDA19-HA, HDA19C137A-HA, GRXS17-MYC) were grown on 1/2 MS medium for 7 d. Dew treatment was performed at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C / 9 0 \%$ RH for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ (control: $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / 6 5 \%$ RH). 

Molecular Assays. Protoplast isolation and transfection followed established standard protocols. NO content was measured using DAF-FM DA fluorescence and Griess reagent. NOS activity was determined via citrulline production. Protein extraction used nonreducing buffers for S-nitrosylation preservation. 

Protein Analyses. Co-IP was performed with anti-HA/anti-MYC antibodies. S-nitrosylation was detected by TMT switch assay. HDAC activity was measured spectrophotometrically. FRET probes (Clover-mRuby2 for GRXS17, CFP-Venus for HDA19) monitored SNO transfer kinetics. 

Omics Analyses. RNA-seq and ChIP-seq (anti-H3K14ac/anti-HA) libraries were prepared following Illumina protocols and sequenced on HiSeq 2000. Data were processed using FastQC, Bowtie2, MACS2, and DESeq2. WGCNA identified coexpression modules $( \beta = 2 5 , \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } > 0 . 8 5$ ). 

Structural Studies. Homology modeling (MODELLER) and protein docking (ZDOCK/PyROSETTA) predicted GRXS17-HDA19 interactions, validated by BiFC and Y2H assays. 

Statistical Information. Each test was performed with at least three independent biological replicates, ensuring robustness against biological variability. Data normality and homogeneity of variance were confirmed using Shapiro– Wilk $( P > 0 . 0 5 )$ ) and Levene’s tests $( P > 0 . 0 5 )$ , respectively. Only when One-way ANOVA indicated a significant overall effect $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ , Fisher’s Least Significant Difference (LSD) test was applied for post hoc pairwise comparisons. Groups with 

mean differences $\geq \mathsf { L S D }$ were considered statistically significant $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ ). Analyses used R and SPSS, with data reported as mean $\pm \mathsf { S D }$ .
