# Summary

Leaf morphogenesis is a crucial process in plants that governs essential physiological functions such as photosynthesis and transpiration. Despite significant advances in understanding leaf development, the mechanism of intricate cellular patterning remains elusive. We characterize the OsLC1 mutant, which displays a curly leaf phenotype alongside reductions in plant height and tiller number, which are indicative of multiple morphological abnormalities. Through map-based cloning, we identified OsLC1 as encoding a transaldolase (TA) protein, whose genetic variations in $O s L C 1$ lead to the disruptions of cell patterning across the vasculature, bundle sheath cells, mesophyll, stomata, bulliform cells and sclerenchyma cells. OsLC1 exhibited TA activity and modulated metabolic flux to the shikimic pathway, thereby affecting phenylpropanoid metabolism. This regulation influenced lignin and flavonoid biosynthesis, ultimately modulating cellular pattern formation through perturbations to flavonoid-mediated auxin or lignin homeostasis. Notably, loss of OsLC1 function led to a reduction in leaf water status, which, along with abnormal cellular patterns in oslc1, caused leaf curling. Overall, our findings provide insights into the regulatory mechanisms underlying cell patterning in the leaf and offer valuable perspectives on leaf morphogenesis in rice. 

# Introduction

Leaf morphogenesis, characterized by the intricate patterning of cells, is essential to plant functioning, influencing vital processes such as photosynthesis and water transport through stomata (Buckley, 2005; Izawa, 2022; Jang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2015). The precise arrangement of vascular tissues, stomata, bundle sheath cells (BS), mesophyll cells, bulliform cells (BCs) and sclerenchyma cells is essential for the regulation of rice architecture and adaptation to varying environmental conditions (Rinne and Schoot, 1998; Zou et al., 2014). In rice, numerous mutants affecting cell patterning in the leaf have been identified, highlighting the complexity of this process, which calls for further investigation. For instance, the waf1 mutant exhibited increased and enlarged BS cells compared to the wild-type (Abe et al., 2010), while mutations in TWI1 led to abnormal cell patterning around the vascular system and inhibited differentiation of sclerenchyma cells (Cui et al., 2018). Similarly, mutations in genes such as NRL1 resulted in defective development of BCs and the formation of aberrant longitudinal veins on the adaxial side of the leaf (Hu et al., 2010). These mutants collectively underscore the complexity of the mechanism of leaf cellular patterning, with each gene acting independently in multiple signalling pathways, 

suggesting that the mechanism of cellular patterning in leaf remains unclear. 

The pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), consisting of independent oxidative and non-oxidative phases, plays a pivotal role in plant growth and development by providing the reducing agents in the form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) for various metabolic processes (Huang et al., 2003; Lansing et al., 2020; Stincone et al., 2014, 2015). The non-oxidative phase of PPP contributes to the synthesis of phenylalanine, a precursor for phenylpropanoid metabolism (Maeda and Dudareva, 2012; Parthasarathy et al., 2018). The activation of this metabolic pathway diverts carbon flux from the primary to the secondary metabolism, generating over 8000 metabolites, which play crucial roles in plant development and plant–environmental interaction (de Vries et al., 2018; Fraser and Chapple, 2011; Herrmann and Weaver, 1999; Moulia, 1994; Vogt, 2010; Zhang and Liu, 2015; Zheng et al., 2020). For example, phenylpropanoid-based polymers like lignin are required for mechanical support for the plant and facilitate the upward transport of water to the vessels (Dong and Lin, 2021; Eudes et al., 2014; Miedes et al., 2014; Weng and Chapple, 2010). Flavonoid, another group of secondary metabolites, play important roles in protecting plants from UV radiation, conferring 

defence against pathogens and pests, pollen reproduction, microbial signalling and regulation of auxin transport (Dong and Lin, 2021; Nakayama et al., 2019; Shih et al., 2008; Stobiecki and Kachlicki, 2006; Tohge et al., 2013; Winkel-Shirley, 2001). Nevertheless, little is known about the role of flavonoids and lignins in regulating leaf cellular patterning and morphogenesis. 

Transaldolase (TA), a crucial enzyme in the non-oxidative phase of the PPP, catalyses the reverse conversion of sedoheptulose-7-phosphate (S7P) and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P) to form erythrose-4-phosphate (E4P) and fructose 6-phosphate (F6P) (Caillau and Paul, 2005; Moehs et al., 1996). In humans, TA is implicated in oxidative stress and apoptosis, multiple sclerosis, liver disease and cancer cell proliferation (Perl et al., 2011; Samland and Sprenger, 2009). In Caenorhabditis elegans, the inhibition of TA activity impairs mitochondrial respiration and induces a starvation-like longevity response (Murphy et al., 2017). Despite its importance, the functions of TA in land plants remain poorly understood. In Arabidopsis, TRA2 encoding a TA protein, loss function of TRA2 disrupts secondary metabolite biosynthesis (Caillau and Paul, 2005; de Vries et al., 2018; Vanholme et al., 2012). Similarly, the knockdown of TAL expression in rice alters the number and pattern of vascular bundles (Yang et al., 2014). However, the precise mechanisms by which TA regulates plant growth and development are yet to be elucidated. 

Here, we characterized the rice curled-leaf mutant oslc1, which encodes a transaldolase. We observed that mutation in OsLC1 led to defects in cell patterning within the leaf vasculature, mesophyll cells, stomata, bulliform cells and sclerenchyma cells, which is mediated by perturbations in flavonoid-mediated auxin or lignin homeostasis. OsLC1 exhibited TA activity and impacted lignin and flavonoid metabolism by modulating the metabolic flux in the shikimic pathway. Collectively, by shedding light on the role of OsLC1 in leaf cellular patterning and its association with secondary metabolite biosynthesis, our study provides new insights into rice leaf morphogenesis. 

# Results

# oslc1, a rice mutant exhibits adaxially rolled leaves

To gain insight into the molecular mechanisms that underlie the formation of rice cellular patterns, we generated a leaf-curling mutant (oslc1) which was derived from a rice population by ethyl methane sulfonate (EMS) mutagenesis. Under field conditions, oslc1 leaves displayed adaxial rolling, with a leaf-rolling index (LRI) accounting for only $4 1 \%$ of the normal value throughout the plant growth and development period (Figure 1a,d; Figure S1). With the goal to reduce water loss, plants typically roll their leaves, a process that is frequently linked to variations in the leaf’s water potential, water loss rate (WLR) and relative water content (RWC) (Dingkuhn et al., 1989; O’Toole and Cruz, 1980; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020). Therefore, we assessed the water status of flag leaves of oslc1 and observed significantly lower leaf water potential and RWC in oslc1 compared to those in wild-type (WT) (Figure 1e; Figure S2), indicating that altered water status may be the mechanism underlying leaf curling. To investigate further, oslc1 mutants were grown at high relative humidity $( \geq 9 0 \%$ RH), and the water potential and water content were found to be significantly increased in oslc1 leaves. Consistent with this finding, the leaves of oslc1 became flat without curling, but compared to WT, the leaves of oslc1 appear 

softer and drooping due to insufficient support (Figure 1h–j; Figure S3). However, although the leaves are no longer curled, the WLR of oslc1 leaves is still significantly higher than that of WT, and the detached leaves quickly curl (Figure S3), suggesting a change in the formation of leaf cell patterns. 

Additionally, under full sunlight, the photosynthesis and transpiration rates were considerably lower in oslc1 compared to WT throughout the growth period (Figure 1f,g), indicating a correlation between the reductions in physiological functions in oslc1 and the degree of leaf rolling (Figure 1; Figure S2). 

In addition to adaxially rolled leaves, the oslc1 exhibited additional morphological features at both the seedling and maturity stages, including significant decreases in plant height, tiller number, panicle length, seed setting rate and 1000-grain weight (Table S2). These findings underscore the modification of physiological and yield characteristics in the oslc1 mutant, and OsLC1 is necessary for normal rice growth and yield. 

# The oslc1 leaves show abnormal development of stomatal guard cells, bulliform cells, small vascular bundles, sclerenchyma cells and mesophyll cells

To investigate whether leaf curling in oslc1 mutants is attributed to alterations of cell patterning in the leaf, we initially examined the leaf surface structures of WT and oslc1 plants using scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Our analysis revealed that the width of the S-ST-BC unit in oslc1 leaves was reduced by $2 5 \%$ compared to that in WT (Figure 2a–c). Moreover, the width of the stomatal (ST), bulliform cell (BC) and silica–phellem (S) bands decreased correspondingly compared to that of WT. Notably, the BC band on the upper leaf epidermis of WT displayed an even distribution, while in oslc1, it exhibited significant wrinkling (Figure 2a), indicating potential alterations of bulliform cells (BCs). Similarly, subsequent analysis demonstrated a noticeable reduction in the stomatal density but an increase in the stomatal size in oslc1 leaves compared to those in WT (Figure 2a,d,e), suggesting disruptions of guard cell patterning. Furthermore, stomatal aperture was evaluated in three different groups categorized based on the proportion of stomata: complete open (CO), partial open (PO) and complete close (CC) (Figure 2f). Along with the higher water loss rate in oslc1, under field conditions, the proportion of CC stomata was significantly lower in oslc1 than in the WT, whereas the proportions of PO and CO stomata were significantly higher in oslc1 than in the WT (Figure 2g). 

Histological studies were further carried out to determine whether cell fate specification was aberrant in oslc1 leaves. Examination of cross sections of young leaves indicated several alterations, including paraxial sclerenchyma and undeveloped small vascular bundle (SVB) and BCs (Figure S4). Typically, the number of BCs adjacent to small vascular bundles (SVBs) was dramatically reduced in oslc1 compared to WT, as well as the total area and the relative depth of BCs adjacent to both large vascular bundles (LVBs) and SVBs, were dramatically reduced in oslc1 compared to that in WT (Figure 3f–h). Interestingly, under high humidity conditions $( \geq 9 0 \%$ RH), the BCs of the oslc1 mutant expand and become larger (Figure S16). These results imply that adaxial leaf curling is related to aberrant BC formation in oslc1. 

Compared to WT, the size of LVB is smaller, and no visible changes were observed in the number and shape of LVBs in the mutant plants (Figure 3a,b,d; Figure S4). # OsLC1 encodes a transaldolase protein and exhibits TA activity

To identify the causal gene for $O s L C 1$ , we performed a genetic analysis of two F2 populations produced from the intercross between oslc1 and indica cultivar (9311). The segregation ratio of normal leaf to rolling leaf plants was observed to be approximately the expected 3:1 (Table S1), indicating that the oslc1 phenotype is governed by a single recessive allele. To validate the role of OsLC1 in producing the oslc1 phenotype, we performed a complementation assay by introducing a 4-kb genomic DNA fragment of OsLC1 into the oslc1 mutant. Transgenic rice plants exhibited leaf morphology identical to that of WT plants (Figure 4; Figure S17). Additionally, two OsLC1 mutants in the background of Nipponbare (oslc1-1 and oslc1-2) were generated using CRISPR/Cas9 technology. The oslc1-1 had a T insertion into the third exon of $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ , resulting in the premature termination of protein at 219 amino acids, whereas oslc1-2 had an A insertion into the fifth exon, causing the protein truncation at the 341 amino acids (Figure 4b). Similar to the oslc1, both knockout transgenic rice plants exhibited rolled leaf morphology and disrupted (Figure 4c–h; Figure S17), confirming that Os01g0926300 corresponds to $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ . 

According to sequence alignment and bioinformatics analysis, OsLC1 was found to belong to the transaldolase-like (TAL, 

cd00955) superfamily and shared motifs and the transaldolase domain like other plants (Figures S5–S7), indicating that the OsLC1 encodes a putative TA and has catalytic activity. To ascertain the TA catalytic activity of $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ , we expressed and purified the OsLC1-His-tag fusion protein from Escherichia coli. Enzyme activity assays demonstrated that the OsLC1 protein exhibited TA activity (Figure 6a). Additionally, we determined the TA activity in transgenic plants and found substantial decreases in TA activity in the mutants oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 compared to that in WT (Figure 6b). These results, which reveal that the protein encoded by the OsLC1 gene possesses TA activity, corroborate those of previous studies, indicating the catalytic activity of TAL-type TA (Caillau and Paul, 2005).

# OsLC1 affects lignin and flavonoid synthesis

TA catalyses the reversible transfer of a dihydroxyacetone group from F6P to E4P, yielding S7P and G3P (Caillau and Paul, 2005; de Vries et al., 2018; Parthasarathy et al., 2018; Perl, 2007; Zheng et al., 2020). Previous research has indicated that TA functions as the rate-limiting enzyme responsible for converting R5P to G6P (Perl, 2007; Zheng et al., 2020), which expands our knowledge of how oslc1 deficiency affects metabolic flow. By measuring the contents of S7P and E4P in the oslc1 mutant and WT, it was found that the latter content in the mutant was significantly reduced, whereas the former significantly increased (Figure 6c,d), indicating that the deficiency of OsLC1 mainly leads to the accumulation of S7P in the forward reaction of TA, rather than that of E4P in the reverse of reaction TA, which corresponds with the outcomes for animals (Perl et al., 2006). Given the involvement of E4P in the shikimic pathway as an initial precursor, we were further interested in investigating whether the reduction of E4P, catalysed by OsLC1, would affect downstream metabolic pathways, including the biosynthetic 

pathways of secondary metabolites (Caillau and Paul, 2005; de Vries et al., 2018; Maeda and Dudareva, 2012; Parthasarathy et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2020). To address this hypothesis, we first conducted autofluorescence and histochemical localization and quantitative analysis of lignin, which was observed to be deposited in large quantities in vascular bundles and sclerenchyma cells to provide structural support for the plant (Fukuda, 2000; Zhong and Ye, 2007). At maturity stage, the lignin contents in oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 were significantly lower than that found in WT (Figure 6e). Additionally, autofluorescence analysis of lignin showed that oslc1 mutants had weaker fluorescence signals in the vascular bundles and sclerenchyma cells than in the WT (Figure 6g,h). The lignin on the cross sections of leaves was further detected using the phloroglucinol-HCl (Wiesner) reaction, and both oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 showed a lighter magenta stain than WT (Figure 6i,j). These results imply that $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ is required for the biosynthesis of lignin biosynthesis. Similarly, the content of flavonoids in oslc1 was notably reduced compared with that in WT (Figure 7a), indicating that $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ also contributes to flavonoid biosynthesis. Collectively, the aforementioned findings demonstrated that the syntheses of lignin and flavonoids are affected by OsLC1 deficiency. # OsLC1 regulates auxin homeostasis

Flavonoids are known to play a crucial role in regulating the cellular efflux of auxin and its subsequent polar auxin transport, thereby influencing its distribution in plants (Brown et al., 2001; Jacobs and Rubery, 1988; Murphy et al., 2000; Peer et al., 2004). Given the involvement of $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ in cellular pattern formation and the regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis, we investigated whether local auxin biosynthesis or degradation is disrupted in oslc1. Our findings revealed a significant decrease in the endogenous auxin content in oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 mutants compared to that in WT (Figure 7c), suggesting a reduction in auxin biosynthesis in the absence of functional OsLC1. To explore its underlying mechanism, we examined the expression patterns of the OsYUCCA gene family, which is responsible for auxin biosynthesis. The expression levels of most OsYUCCA genes were markedly downregulated in oslc1-1 compared to those in WT (Figure S10a), suggesting a possible mechanism for the reduction in auxin biosynthesis observed in this mutant. To elucidate the physiological roles of OsLC1 genes in response to exogenous auxin, we performed an assay in the WT and oslc1-1 mutant under the IAA or 2,4-D treatment. Seedlings were grown for 7 days in 1/2 MS medium containing different concentrations of IAA, there was no significant increase in the length of the shoot and root of oslc1-1 (Figure S13a–f). Interestingly, following 2,4-D treatment for 7 days, shoot elongation in WT was quickly suppressed compared to that of the oslc1 (Figure S13). 

To further investigate the role of OsLC1 in auxin signalling, we employed 35S:DR5-GUS and 35S:DR5-GFP reporter lines to monitor the auxin response (Benkova et al., 2003; Geldner et al., 2004; Sabatini et al., 1999; Shi et al., 2015; Ulmasov et al., 1997). Our results showed a decrease in GFP fluorescence intensity in oslc1 mutant roots compared to WT, both in the absence and presence of exogenous IAA (Figure 7d–g). Similarly, the GFP fluorescence signal in the leaves of DR5-GFP/oslc1 was also reduced compared to DR5-GFP/WT (Figure 7h–j). This suggests an impaired auxin signalling and distribution in oslc1 mutants. Additionally, the uneven distribution of GFP fluorescence in DR5-GFP/oslc1 roots and leaves (compare regions indicated by the red arrows in Figure 7) was observed, which further supports the notion of disrupted auxin signalling in oslc1 (Figure 7d–h). In addition, consistent with this observation, the GUS staining in the roots was decreased in DR5-GUS/oslc1 plants compared to the DR5-GUS/WT plants (Figure S12d). 

Furthermore, we examined the expression of OsPIN genes, which are involved in auxin transport (Bureau et al., 2010). Most OsPIN genes exhibited significant downregulation in the oslc1-1 mutant compared to WT (Figure S10b), suggesting a potential role of OsLC1 in modulating polar auxin transport. To further assess the functional consequences of OsLC1-mediated polar auxin transport, we conducted gravitropic response assays in the oslc1 mutant (Rashotte et al., 2000), which showed a delayed gravitropic response compared to WT, indicating disturbed auxin-mediated root bending (Figure S12b,c). In summary, our results demonstrated that OsLC1 plays a crucial role in regulating auxin homeostasis in rice by influencing its auxin biosynthesis, distribution and polar transport. 

# RNA-seq analysis of the oslc1 mutant

To further explore the underlying mechanism by which OsLC1 regulates cellular pattern formation, 7-day-old seedlings of oslc1 and WT plants were collected, and transcript profiles were 

analysed by RNA-seq. Comparing WT to the oslc1 mutant, we identified 644 differentially expressed genes (DEGs), of which 381 genes were downregulated but 263 genes were upregulated (Figure S11a). The DEGs were further investigated using Gene Ontology (GO), protein–protein interaction (PPI) networks and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG). GO results showed that the DEGs were enriched in 44 GO functional groups, including 15 cellular components, 17 biological processes and 12 molecular functions (Figure S11c). The top two terms in each functional group were ‘cell and cell part’, ‘metabolic process and cellular process’ and ‘binding and catalytic activity’, respectively, which corresponded exactly to the functions of OsLC1. Especially, several downregulated DEGs associated with cellular pattern formation were identified (Table S3). Among them, the epidermal patterning factor proteins OsEPFL1 (Pawel et al., 2019) and OsEPFL8 (Guo et al., 2023) are involved in regulating the development of stomatal complexes and leaf initiation (Table S3), which is consistent with the role of OsLC1 in the regulation of guard and leaf cell patterning (Figures 2, 3). Additionally, four genes involved in leaf morphology have been identified, namely OsGIF1/MKB3, OsGRF4, DL and OsC-SID4/NRL1. The loss-of-function mutant of OsGIF1/MKB3 exhibits pleiotropic growth defects including narrowed and rolled-leaf phenotype (Lu et al., 2020; Shimano et al., 2018). OsGRF4 predominantly promotes cell expansion but also increases cell proliferation (Duan et al., 2015). Coincidently, AtGRF2 increased cell expansion in leaves (Kim et al., 2003), while several Arabidopsis AtGRFs promoted cell proliferation during leaf development (Horiguchi et al., 2005; Vercruyssen et al., 2015). The Drooping Leaf (DL) mutant is linked to a twisted and folded leaf base in rice that results from a lack of midrib development (Kang et al., 2022). OsCSID4/NRL1 mutant showed phenotypes of reduced leaf width and semi-rolled leaves, and microscopic analysis indicated that the mutant had fewer longitudinal veins and smaller adaxial bulliform cells compared with the wild-type (Hu et al., 2010). Furthermore, the co-expression network analysis of DEGs revealed several major genes (nodes in the network), such as CDC20.1, CDC20.2, CYCA3-1 and CYCA3-2 (Figure S11d, Table S3), that are associated with cell division or cell cycle (Iwata et al., 2011; Willems et al., 2020). The above results further support the involvement of OsLC1 in regulating cellular pattern formation and leaf morphology. 

According to the KEGG annotation, 199 DEGs were assigned to 79 pathways (Figure S11b). Convincingly, a large number of DEGs were referred to the secondary biosynthesis, including phenylalanine metabolism (ko00360), flavonoid biosynthesis (ko00941), isoflavonoid biosynthesis (ko00943) and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (ko00940). This aligns with the role of TA in regulating phenylpropanoid metabolic pathways in plants. Furthermore, DEGs enriched in plant hormone signal transduction pathway, particularly auxin signalling, were identified, with a downregulation observed for genes encoding an auxin response factor (OsARF12) and auxin efflux carrier (OsPIN3b) (Figure S12a). To validate the results from RNA-seq, the expression levels of OsARF12 and OsPIN3b in WT and oslc1 were analysed by RT-PCR, and the experimental results were consistent with them, both of which were downregulated (Figure S12e,f). This suggests the influence of OsLC1 on local auxin homeostasis. Surprisingly, DEGs were not enriched in auxin biosynthesis, in contrast to the reduced IAA levels in oslc1 and changes in the gene expression associated with auxin synthesis observed by RT-qPCR (Figure 7c; Figures S10 and S11b). Additionally, DEGs enriched in plant– 

pathogen interaction (ko04626) and the MAPK signalling (ko04016) pathways were noted, implicating the role of OsLC1 in plant defence responses (Figure S11b). Considering the effect of OsLC1 on lignin biosynthesis (Figure 6), which is essential for an efficient plant defence (Caillau and Paul, 2005), these findings underscore its involvement in the plant–pathogen interaction and the MAPK signalling pathways by mediating the regulation of lignin biosynthesis. In summary, our RNA-seq analysis highlights the involvement of OsLC1 in the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway, downstream lignin and flavonoid pathways and plant hormone signal transduction, shedding light on its role in secondary metabolite synthesis, auxin signalling and activating plant defence mechanisms. 

# Discussion

The cooperation between various types of cells in an intricate network plays a pivotal role in determining leaf shape and physiological functions in plants (Cui et al., 2018; Itoh et al., 2005; Zou et al., 2014). Cell specification and pattern formation in the leaf are essential for processes such as photosynthesis and the transfer of fixed carbon through the vascular system (Cui et al., 2018; Kadioglu et al., 2012; Li et al., 2017). Additionally, plants exhibit remarkable adaptability to different environmental conditions by modifying the specification and patterning of their leaf cells (Rinne and Schoot, 1998; Zhang et al., 2020; Zou et al., 2014). 

# OsLC1 affected secondary metabolism by mediating metabolic flux in the shikimic pathway

The two subfamilies of the plant TA gene family, TAL and TalAB, derived from an ancient horizontal gene transfer (HGT) event that originated in Actinobacteria. Additional data indicate that only TAL-type TA possesses catalytic activity (Caillau and Paul, 2005; Yang et al., 2014). Our results indicated that OsLC1, belonging to the TAL (cd00955) superfamily (Figure S5), possesses TA catalytic activity (Figure 6a), indicating its role in catalysing the reversible transfer of a dihydroxyacetone group from F6P to E4P, S7P and G3P in the non-oxidative phase of the PPP (Caillau and Paul, 2005; Perl, 2007; Zheng et al., 2020). Furthermore, our research revealed that deficiency of OsLC1 resulted in alterations in metabolic flux, as evidenced by changes in the levels of key metabolites such as S7P and E4P (Figure 6c,d). These findings are consistent with those of previous studies conducted on animals (Perl, 2007). Importantly, the reduction in E4P content in OsLC1 mutants affected downstream metabolic pathways, resulting in the decreased synthesis of secondary metabolites including lignin and flavonoid (Figures 6 and 7). This observation is supported by a notable downregulation of the expression levels of crucial genes involved in the flavonoid and lignin biosynthetic pathways in the mutant plants (Figures 6 and 7). Additionally, RNA-seq analysis further highlighted the significant enrichment of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) involved in secondary metabolite biosynthetic pathways (Figure S11), including phenylalanine metabolism (ko00360), flavonoid biosynthesis (ko00941), isoflavonoid biosynthesis (ko00943) and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (ko00940), underscoring the regulatory role of $O s L C 1$ in carbon fluxes and plant development. Overall, our study elucidates the pivotal role of OsLC1 in mediating metabolic flux in the shikimic pathway and its downstream implications for plant secondary metabolism. 

Moreover, the current study raises intriguing scientific questions regarding the molecular mechanisms underlying metabolic flux modulation by OsLC1 and its propagation through wider metabolic networks involving pathways beyond the PPP. Addressing these questions will not only deepen our understanding of OsLC1 functions but also elucidate the influence of metabolic flux on plant growth, development and stress responses. 

# OsLC1 regulates cell patterning and leaf morphology by mediating secondary metabolism

A complex interaction between genetic and environmental factors, including cell development, differentiation, specification and axis determination, controls leaf cellular patterning (Itoh et al., 2005; Zou et al., 2014). Many genes linked to leaf shape in rice have been found and functionally defined; these genes regulate various components of the cell including BCs, VBs and sclerenchyma cells via signalling pathways mediated by phytohormones, transcription factors and miRNAs (Kadioglu et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2009). 

Earlier research has demonstrated the involvement of the lignin pathway in leaf cellular patterning. For instance, mutation of NRL2 led to reduced lignin content and altered phenylpropanoid metabolism in plants, resulting in defective cell differentiation (Zhao et al., 2016). Similarly, TWI1, a key regulator of cell pattern formation in rice leaves, is associated with decreased lignin content and transcriptional levels of the key genes involved in lignin synthesis (Cui et al., 2018). Lignin regulates cellular patterning including structural support and cell wall integrity (Kang et al., 2019), signalling molecules (Campobenedetto et al., 2020) and crosstalks with hormonal signalings (Shah et al., 2023). Our findings revealed that OsLC1 is likely involved in cell patterning and regulation of leaf morphogenesis by mediating lignin homeostasis. KEGG and GO analyses indicate that OsLC1 is involved in the phenylpropanoid metabolism pathway including lignin synthesis (Figure S11). The oslc1 mutants exhibited abnormal development of stomatal guard, BC, SVB, sclerenchyma and mesophyll cells (Figures 2 and 3). The absence of OsLC1 led to the downregulation of key genes in the lignin biosynthetic pathway and a reduced lignin content (Figure 6). However, the specific pathways through which OsLC1 regulates cell patterning, such as signalling molecules or cell wall development, require further analysis. 

Plant hormone auxin is extensively researched and is involved in many aspects of plant growth, such as controlling apical dominance, lateral root formation, meristem maintenance, geotropism, cell division and elongation (Teale et al., 2006). However, its underlying mechanisms remain unclear. Recent evidence suggests that flavonoids interfere with auxin signalling (Dong and Lin, 2021). Flavonols aglycones, which are structurally similar to the auxin transport inhibitor 1-naphthylphthalamic acid (NPA), are proposed as the endogenous regulators of auxin transport (Taylor and Grotewold, 2005). In Arabidopsis, auxin transport was improved in the tt4 mutant that lacked flavonoids, whereas it was reduced in the tt3 and tt7 mutants that accumulated too much flavonol glycoside (Peer et al., 2004). In rice, GSA1 controls grain size by influencing cell division and proliferation, which are controlled by flavonoid-mediated auxin levels and related gene expression (Dong et al., 2020). In our studies, we found that the IAA content (Figure 7c) and the expression levels of most auxin-related genes, including the gene involved in auxin biosynthesis (OsYUCCAs) and auxin transport 

(OsPINs) gene, were notably reduced in the oslc1 mutant (Figure S10). Consistently, the expression levels of 35S:DR5-GUS and 35S:DR5-GFP were significantly reduced in this mutant (Figure 7d; Figure S12d). Similarly, flavonoid content and expression of key genes involved in the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway were significantly reduced in the oslc1 mutants (Figure 7a,b), implicating a link between flavonoid biosynthesis and auxin signals and indicating that the regulation of cell patterning by OsLC1 is likely associated with flavonoid-mediated auxin homeostasis. Collectively, our findings underscore the involvement of the intricate regulatory network of OsLC1, lignin, flavonoid and auxin in orchestrating leaf cellular patterning. However, further evidence is needed to determine whether lignin and flavonoids regulate cellular patterning separately or interactively. 

# OsLC1 modulates leaf curling by mediating cell patterning and altering the water potential

A very complicated agronomic trait controlled by environment and genetics is leaf rolling, and it also serves as a defence mechanism to keep plants safe from photodamage (Kadioglu et al., 2012; Li et al., 2017). Thus far, it has been demonstrated that the majority of leaf rolling or curling phenotypes are caused by aberrant BC’s shape, quantity, size or distribution (Fang et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2010; Li et al., 2010; Xiang et al., 2012; Xu et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2011). Studies on curling or rolling leaf mutants obtained from other cell types in rice leaves, however, remain limited. SLL1, SRL2 and OsAGO1b were observed to be involved in the formation of sclerenchymatous cells as well as the regulation of leaf rolling (Li et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2009). Rice leaves rolled outwards as a result of an OsARVL4 mutation that reduced the amount of mesophyll cells and vascular bundles (Wang et al., 2015). Despite no change in the appearance of BCs, the twi1 mutant showed notably rolled leaves, which may have resulted from disturbed development and disordered patterning of sclerenchyma, bundle sheath and mesophyll cells (Cui et al., 2018). Interestingly, in this study, we identified a curled-leaf mutant, oslc1, with multiple morphological defects in leaves, whose variation led to defects in cellular patterns in the vasculature, mesophyll cells, stomata, bulliform cells and sclerenchyma cells (Figure 3). Hence, we concluded that other cell types can work along with bulliform cells to regulate leaf rolling or curling in rice. 

Maintenance of water homeostasis is one of the most essential functions of a plant, which has a direct impact on its ability to grow, develop and eventually survive (Chen et al., 2005; Sharp et al., 2004; Woo et al., 2007). Previous studies have shown that rolling leaf mutants, such as psl1 and oscow1, have caused significant changes in water homeostasis in addition to histological changes in specific cell types (Woo et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2020). Consistent with these findings, our investigation revealed drastic decreases in water potential and transpiration rate, a faster leaf water loss and a lower water content in the oslc1 mutant (Figure 1; Figure S2). Interestingly, when seedlings of the oslc1-1 mutant were grown at high relative humidity, the leaf shape did not differ from that of the WT (Figure 1). Correspondingly, increased water potential and water content were observed in oslc1 leaves, under high humidity conditions, suggesting that cell water status is a fundamental mechanism underlying leaf rolling. 

Moreover, the identification of a drought-responsive element in the OsLC1 promoter (Figure S8) implies its involvement in drought stress tolerance. Subsequent osmotic stress treatment of oslc1 exacerbated its stress response (Figure S9), suggesting that OsLC1 might contribute to drought tolerance. Overall, our findings suggest that OsLC1 regulates leaf shape by modulating patterning and influencing the water potential of leaf cells, providing insights into strategies to improve crop yield and abiotic stress tolerance. 

Here, we propose a working model for the regulation of leaf morphogenesis and cell patterning by OsLC1. Under normal conditions, OsLC1, which acts as a key enzyme participates in the non-oxidative phase of PPP and contributes to lignin and flavonoid biosynthesis, which are required for plant growth and development. In contrast, the decreased TA activity of the oslc1 mutant resulted in the subsequent repression of lignin and flavonoid biosynthesis and the inhibition of the biosynthesis, transport and downstream signalling of auxin, leading to the disruption of cell pattern formation, consequently inducing leaf curling (Figure 8). Therefore, this discovery not only establishes the regulatory mechanism of rice leaf curling but also reveals strategies to improve rice drought stress tolerance. OsLC1 is a potential genetic resource that could coordinately improve crop yield and enhance abiotic stress tolerance by regulating leaf development, thereby developing strategies for molecular design breeding and food security and sustainability. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The oslc1 mutant was isolated from a mutant population of Oryza sativa L. ssp. japonica cultivar Nipponbare generated using $1 \%$ ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) solution, and Nipponbare was used as the wild-type (WT). The oslc1 mutant was crossed with indica rice variety 9311 to generate the F1 population, and the F2 population was derived by selffertilization of the F1 population. Rice plants were grown in an experimental field under natural growth conditions in Changsha (Hunan province, China). To maintain high relative humidity $( \mathsf { R H } \geq 9 0 \%$ ) under natural conditions, plants were wrapped with a transparent cling film; in the greenhouse, a temperature set of $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C : 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ (day : night), $90 \%$ RH, and a light : a dark period of $1 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ : ~ } 1 0 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ were used to culture rice. 

# Measurement of leaf photosynthetic parameters

The photosynthesis rate and transpiration rate were evaluated with a portable system (Li-6400; LiCor Inc., Lincoln, NE, USA), the middle region of the flag leaf from WT and OsLC1 mutant plants, at the tilling stage, was used to measure. All measurements were carried out at $1 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l / p h o t o n s / m } ^ { 2 } / s$ , with a cuvette ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentration of $4 5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } }$ at $5 0 0 \ m \mathrm { m o l / s }$ flow speeds at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Follow the manufacturer’s instructions for other steps. 

# Estimation of leaf water potential, LRI

The flag leaves from the WT and OsLC1 mutant plants in the mature stage were removed at 11:00 am and put immediately into a digital pressure bomb (Skye Instruments, Llandrindnod Wells, UK) to measure the leaf water potential (Wan et al., 2001). The leaf rolling index (LRI) is calculated using the mature flag leaves, and its formula is as follows: LRI $( \% ) = ( \mathsf { L } \mathsf { w } - \mathsf { L } \mathsf { n } ) / \mathsf { L } \mathsf { w } ^ { \ast } 1 0 0$ .

# SEM and analysis of paraffin section

For the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis, the samples were fixed in pre-cooled 0.1 M phosphate buffer containing $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde for 8 h, washed with $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ phosphate buffer, and dehydrated with graded ethanol series. The tissue samples were then transferred to isoamyl acetate, dried, coated with gold and then visualized by SEM (SU8010, Hitachi, Japan). 

For the tissue paraffin section analysis, the samples were fixed in FAA solution and then dehydrated it in a graded ethanol series. Finally, the samples were embedded in paraffin and sliced using a rotary slicer (Leica RM2015) (Li et al., 2015). For lignin staining, the sliced samples were stained with $1 \%$ p-phenylene triazole dissolved in $12 \%$ HCl for 5 min, and finally, visualized under an optical microscope (Leica DM2500 LED) as described earlier (Gui et al., 2019). For auto-fluorescence analysis, the sections were examined and photographed under a fluorescence microscope (Nikon; Ti-E, Japan) with an excitation/emission of 488/530 nm (Baldacci-Cresp et al., 2020). 

# Map-based cloning, complementation assay and genome editing

Selected 898 plants with extremely curly leaf phenotype from the F2 population and used them to map OsLC1. Gene mapping using simple sequence repeat (SSR) markers obtained from the publicly available rice databases, Gramene (http://www.gramene.org) and the Rice Genomic Research Program (http://rgp.dna.affrc.go. jp/E/publicdata/caps/index.html). Insertion/deletion markers were developed by comparing the genome sequences of Nipponbare and 9311. 

For functional complementation testing, the 4-kb genomic DNA fragment (containing the 2.0-kb wild-type coding region, plus 1.2-kb upstream and 0.8-kb downstream sequences) of OsLC1 was cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector. Use Agrobacterium-mediated (EHA105) transformation method to transform the construct into oslc1 mutant plants (Huang et al., 2018). The transgenic seedlings were detected by hygromycin primers and specific primers. 

Targeting the OsLC1 gene in Nipponbare using RNA-guided genome editing technology (CRISPR/cas9). The OsLC1 coding 

sequence was selected to guide RNA design. Twenty nucleotide target sites were selected based on the criteria described by Jin (Miao et al., 2013). The two sgRNAs were designed to target two sites, respectively, in OsLC1 genes. The completed CRISPR-Cas9-OsLC1 constructs were then transformed into Nipponbare by Agrobacterium-mediated transfection. The remaining steps refer to previous research (Huang et al., 2018). 

All primers used in this study are listed in Table S4. 

# TA activity assay

For prokaryotic expression of protease activity detection, firstly, using homologous recombination, clone the OsLC1 open reading frame (ORF) fragment onto the prokaryotic expression vector pET28a to fuse a His tag. The expression and purification of protein were carried out according to previously described methods (Zhao et al., 2023) with small modifications. The OsLC1-pET28a recombinant vector was transferred into Escherichia coli competent cell BL21 and then Luria-Bertani (LB) media was used to cultivate the cells until an optical density of 0.8 was attained at $6 0 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ (OD600). Next, cells were transferred to $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 100 rpm and isopropyl-beta-D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to a final concentration of $0 . 0 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ to induce protein expression 16 h. TA activity assay was performed as described before (Caillau and Paul, 2005; Zheng et al., 2020). 

Further analysis of the TA activity in transgenic plants was conducted. Protein samples from 7-day-old seedlings of the WT, oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 on 1/2 MS medium were extracted and desalted by ultrafiltration centrifugal tube and then measure TA activity according to the previous experimental steps (Zheng et al., 2020). 

# The measurement of S7P, E4P, lignin, flavonoids and IAA levels

The sedoheptulose-7-phosphate (S7P) and erythrose-4-phosphate (E4P) were detected by HPLC as described by Perl et al. (2006). Approximately $0 . 2 \ 9$ seedling stage WT and oslc1 plant samples were weighed and frozen with liquid nitrogen, respectively. Then, the samples and 1 mL of $50 \%$ methanol were added to a precooled mortar, ground to a homogenate and centrifuged (12 500 , 20 min) at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , next, the supernatant was isolated for HPLC g(Waters 2695–2489) analysis (Perl et al., 2006). 

For lignin measurement, 1 g of the maturity stage sample should be weighed and dried at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to a consistent weight. Grind it and strain it through a sieve with a 30–50 mesh size. Fill a 1.5 mL EP tube with around 5 mg of weight, then the Lignin content was determined using a Lignin Content Assay Kit (Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China; Cat. No.D799081) following the manufacturer’s protocol. After acetylation of phenolic hydroxyl groups in lignin, there is a characteristic absorption peak at 280 nm, and the absorbance value at $2 8 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ is positively correlated with lignin content. 

The determination of flavonoid concentrations was tested using Plant Flavonoids Assay Kit (Jiangsu Meimian Industrial Co., Ltd., Jiangsu, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Seven-day-old WT, oslc1-1 and oslc1-2 grown on 1/2 MS medium were used as samples. Plant flavonoids level in the sample use purified plant flavonoids antibody to coat microtitre plate wells, make solid-phase antibody and then add Flavonoids to wells, combined flavonoids antibody which with HRP labelled, become antibody–antigen–enzyme–antibody complex. After washing completely, add TMB substrate solution, TMB substrate becomes blue colour at HRP enzyme-catalyzed, the reaction is terminated by the addition of a sulphuric acid solution, and the 

colour change is measured spectrophotometrically at a wavelength of 450 nm. The concentration of Plant Flavonoids in the samples is then determined by comparing the OD of the samples to the standard curve. 

Collect 7-day-old young leaves of seedlings (WT and mutant materials) grown on 1/2MS medium for IAA content analysis. IAA extraction and measurement were performed according to previous reports (Liu et al., 2012). 

# DR5-GFP and DR5-GUS analysis

The construction of the DR5-GUS vector was carried out according to previously described methods with slight modifications (Ottenschl€ager et al., 2003; Ulmasov et al., 1997). The auxin-response promoter DR5 consists of nine inverted repeats of the 11-bp sequence $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CCTTTTGTCTC-30 , a 46-bp CaMV35S minimal promoter element and a TMV leader sequence. GFP sequences were generated by the pCUbi1390-GFP vector and then 35Smini-DR5::GFP fusion in a pCambia1300 vector background. The vector carrying the DR5-GFP reporter gene was transformed into WT and oslc1 plants by using Agrobacteriummediated (EHA105) method (Huang et al., 2018). DR5-GFP/WT and DR5-GFP/oslc1 seedlings grown for 7 days, with roots and leaves sliced manually, and the GFP signals were detected under confocal microscopy. The DR5-GUS transgenic line with WT background was a gift from Li et al. (2019), and the DR5-GUS line in oslc1 background was obtained by hybridization between oslc1 mutant and DR5-GUS-containing WT. The GUS signal was obtained by staining. 

# Subcellular localization of OsLC1 and GUS staining

The full-length coding region of OsLC1 without the stop codon was amplified from Nipponbare and cloned into the pCUbi1390- GFP vector driven by Ubiquitin promoter to generate the pCUbi1390-OsLC1-GFP (green fluorescent protein) fusion protein. Both pCUbi1390-OsLC1-GFP and empty pCUbi1390-GFP plasmids were introduced into rice protoplasts and Arabidopsis protoplasts as described previously, respectively (Chen et al., 2006; Meng et al., 2014). After cultivation at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight, the GFP signal was detected by using a confocal laser scanning microscope with 488 nm excitation laser (Nikon; Ti-E, Japan). For tobacco leaf epidermal cell transformation, A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 containing the recombinant plasmid pCUbi1390-GFP and pCUbi1390-OsLC1-GFP was infiltrated into leaves of tobacco plant (N. benthamiana) (Zhao et al., 2023). After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of cultivation, tobacco leaf epidermal cells were observed using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Nikon; Ti-E, Japan) with a 488 nm excitation laser. 

In order to investigate the tissue-specific expression of $O s L C \mathcal { 1 }$ , a putative $2 . 3 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ promoter genomic fragment (upstream of the ATG start codon) was amplified by PCR, and the PCR product was cloned into the binary vector pCambia1301 to drive the reporter gene $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) (Hu et al., 2024). The $O s L C 1 _ { P r o } .$ ::GUS construct was transformed into rice, and the $0 s L C 1 _ { P r o } .$ :: GUS transgenic plants (T2) were used for histochemical staining analysis. GUS histochemical assays were performed as described previously (Hao et al., 2018).
