# A B S T R A C T

It took several millennia to fully understand the scientific intricacies of the process through which grape juice is turned into wine. This yeast-driven fermentation process is still being perfected and advanced today. Motivated by ever-changing consumer preferences and the belief that the ‘best’ wine is yet to be made, numerous approaches are being pursued to improve the process of yeast fermentation and the quality of wine. Central to recent enhancements in winemaking processes and wine quality is the development of Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast strains with improved robustness, fermentation efficiencies and sensory properties. The emerging science of Synthetic Biology – including genome engineering and DNA editing technologies – is taking yeast strain development into a totally new realm of possibility. The first example of how future wine strain development might be impacted by these new ‘history-making’ Synthetic Biology technologies, is the de novo production of the raspberry ketone aroma compound, 4-[4-hydroxyphenyl]butan-2-one, in a wine yeast containing a synthetic DNA cassette. This article explores how this breakthrough and the imminent outcome of the international Yeast 2.0 (or Sc2.0) project, aimed at the synthesis of the entire genome of a laboratory strain of S. cerevisiae, might accelerate the design of improved wine yeasts.

# 1. History in the making

# 1.1. Making wine-history through continuous cycles of innovation

Continuous innovation is the corkscrew which allows grapegrowers, winemakers and scientists to open the bottled mystique of wine quality and consumer satisfaction. With every twist and turn the underpinning sciences uncork novel ideas of how to improve the quality of wine while minimizing resource inputs, production costs and environmental impact. For $> 7 0 0 0$ years there has been a continuing evolution in grape and wine production. Working symbiotically, grapegrowers, winemakers and scientists have raised fresh questions and revealed new possibilities regarding old problems, ingeniously overcoming emerging challenges regarding the way grapes are grown, how wine is made, and how consumers respond to their products.

The Human race was imbibing alcohol long before writing was even invented. Fermented beverages were not a mere byproduct of civilization – they were central to it. Since at least 9000 years ago, the hedonic properties of alcoholic beverages sparked the creativity of our ancient ancestors that led to the development of language, the arts and religion. In fact, there is a link to alcohol to nearly all great transitions in human history from the origin of farming to the origin of writing.

The oldest evidence of an alcoholic beverage comes from Jiahu in China where farmers were fermenting a cocktail of rice, hawthorn berries, wild grapes and honey in clay jars by 7000 BCE (McGovern et al., 2004). Evidence has also been found that, in the Caucasus Mountains of modern-day Georgia and the Zagros Mountains of Iran, grapes were one of the earliest fruits to be domesticated. We can trace the evolution of grape-derived wine from its earliest archaeological origins in Ancient Persia, the Caspian Sea shores (ca. 6000 BCE), and ancient Egypt (ca. 5000 BCE) (Fig. 1). For an overview of the history of winemaking and timelines of developments in the wine industry, see the following articles and references therein: Borneman et al., 2013b; Curry, 2017; Chambers and Pretorius, 2010; McGovern et al., 2004; Pretorius, 2000, 2010, 2016; Pretorius et al., 2015). The following paragraphs represent a synthesis of information drawn from the latter publications and references therein.

Based on how wine was written into the literature of the day, portrayed through the art, and from fossilized grape seeds and skins found in the remains of long-gone buildings and cities, we know that by approximately 4000 BCE, wine was fully embedded in the diet and culture of the ancient Greeks. 1000 years later, by 500–100 BCE, wine was also produced in China and India, coinciding with the development of large earthenware pots for storage and transport by Roman potters. The Romans went on to develop barrels (around 3 CE) following their contact with the Gauls, who had occupied much of western Europe and were experienced at fashioning barrels from wood.

Admittedly, ancient Persian and Pharaonic wine might well be considered too historically remote to be usefully compared with winemaking today; however, it does offer valuable insights of how the ancients founded a tradition of imaginative ingenuity, inventiveness and innovation.

The decline of the Roman Empire was followed by a ‘dark’ period of economic and cultural decay that aligned with little innovation in wine production practices. Advances in production methods (such as vine cultivation, pottery production and winemaking practices) peaked around 200–400 CE and was followed by a period of 1200 to 1400 years during which progress in wine technology slowed and was generally restricted to monastic religious orders in western Europe. But then things started to change. Spanish explorers and conquistadors took vines to the New World in the 1500s, planting Vitis vinifera varieties in Mexico and South America. Halfway through the 1600s, Dutch settlers planted French vine cuttings in South Africa, followed by plantings in California and, by the mid-1800s, vineyards were also plentiful in Australia and New Zealand.

Geographic exploration was accompanied by a search for scientific knowledge. The Age of the Enlightenment – reflected in European art, culture and science through the 17th and 18th centuries – was also evident in wine research as early scientists sought to understand the chemical composition of wine. In 1768, for example, the French chemist Antoine Baumé published his hydrometer scale, allowing the concentration of sugar in grapes to be accurately measured for the first time. Today, winemakers still refer to Baumé, preferring this measurement term to ‘sweetness’ or ‘sugar’.

Yeast cells were observed for the first time under a microscope in 1680 by the Dutchman Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, and by 1789 another French chemist, Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier, had analyzed the process of fermentation, and showed that through fermentation sugar produces carbon dioxide and ethanol. Knowledge of grape composition took another step forward, as researchers isolated sugar as well as tartaric acid from grapes. Wine was no longer a mysterious ferment, produced from haphazard vines. It was now ripe for development backed by scientific research.

The pursuit of technological innovation that transformed industry, agriculture and daily life throughout Europe and North America in the 19th and early 20th centuries also heralded dramatic change in the world's fledgling wine industry. The discovery and adoption of pasteurization facilitated storage and transport. This was not the only significant contribution famed French biochemist, Louis Pasteur, made for the wine industry; he also proved that yeast are living cells, responsible for wine fermentation. Pasteur also went on to recognize the importance of wine phenolics and how oxygen played a pivotal role in the development of color and in the production of red wine. The term ‘enzyme’ was used for the first time by the German pathologist Wilhelm Kühne in the late 1800s. These discoveries spurred researchers to further investigate the chemical composition of wine, with a view to understanding its creation and production in more detail. Chemists, biochemists and microbiologists analyzed and quantified sugars, acids, alcohol, sulfur dioxide and color in wine and reached a deeper understanding of fermentation enzymes, wine yeast and malolactic bacteria. The concept of inoculating grape juice with pure Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast cultures was introduced in 1890; a practice that would only catch on more than half a century later.

In the vineyard, plant pathologists confronted fungal diseases such as powdery and downy mildew. By the end of the 1800s, it had become widely accepted to graft V. vinifera noble variety scions onto Phylloxeraresistant rootstocks. By the 1920s and 1930s, plant geneticists had

turned their attention to vine breeding programs and improved production practices, with a view to increasing yields and bottling enough wines to meet the world's growing demand: a productionfocused trend that would continue until the end of the last century.

Dramatic changes in the way that grapes were grown, harvested and turned into wine were evident through the contribution of sound science and rigorous research to production efficiency and longevity: by the mid-1900s, wine production flourished in Old and New World countries. However, the biggest step-change occurred in the 20th century when the wine industry saw the introduction of innovative production methods backed by new technology. The production cycle of vineyards and wineries around the world were changed forever through the contribution of computers and high technology: from the chemical analysis of intriguing aromas and ferments in the laboratory through to the mechanization of harvesting.

There was a deluge of new discoveries and opportunities. The first twist-off bottle closure was developed in 1965, for example, marking the beginning of revolutionary changes to closure technologies. New analytical techniques were developed to isolate tannins along with a range of other critical components. There were dramatic improvements in mechanization complemented by the adoption of precision viticulture techniques. Machines saved time and money by taking over pruning, crop thinning and harvesting. New ways to water and trellis vines took hold in the 1970s and 1980s along with a greater understanding of fundamental grapevine biology.

Grape and wine researchers, too, found a new way to fast-track their discoveries. The late 1900s saw an increase in collaborative, crossdisciplinary research, as researchers refused to be limited by the historical categories assigned to their work. The cross-cultivation of botanists, chemists, geneticists, informaticians, microbiologists, physicists and engineers began to deliver an integrated research context of transdisciplinarity: they saw themselves as collaborative, multi-disciplinary researchers seeking progress in pursuit of sustainable grape and wine production.

The last 20 years have seen unprecedented change. In the late 1990s, email was in its infancy; surfing was only done in the ocean (Google was unknown before 1996); and ‘computer illiteracy’ was still regarded as acceptable in some wine enterprises. Today's web-based reality, reaffirmed through the ubiquitous nature of handheld smart devices, cloud-computing and social media, has changed the way industry practitioners and wine consumers think, act and communicate.

While the 20th century was dominated by a focus on production and increasing yield, business conditions had changed over the last 20 or so years, and the wine industry needed to adapt. The route-to-market became more complex, as did the consumers. Today's market knows what it wants, when, why and how. It communicates globally and uses websites, blogs, Facebook, Twitter and other instantaneous communication channels to its advantage. For wine producers, the imperative is to ensure their wine has market appeal, despite changing consumer preferences and despite changing environmental conditions. Having greater control over grape and wine composition, to align with preferences in target market segments, is one way researchers are assisting producers to remain viable. Researchers from around the world have advanced the scientific knowledge of tannin, for example, which assists winemakers with greater understanding and ultimately control over the effect of tannin on wine style and quality. There were also ground-breaking discoveries in the analysis of DNA in the mid-2000s, leading to the cracking of the full genome codes of a Pinot Noir grapevine cultivar (Jaillon et al., 2007) and Saccharomyces wine yeast (Borneman et al., 2008) for the first time. This breathed new life into grape breeding and yeast strain development programs focused on the crafting of specific wines and styles anticipated in consumer markets of the 21st Century (Pretorius et al., 2012).

# 1.2. Making yeast-innovation a new tipping point in the history of Synthetic Biology

A tipping point is often described as that ‘unexpected defining moment of magical change’ in history. It also applies to an evolving situation when a new idea crosses a threshold and suddenly tips the balance to cause a significant change, shifting a paradigm and setting a new trend in a totally new and irreversible direction. Such is the case with the emergence of Synthetic Biology – considered as the most exciting 21st Century interdisciplinary science on the scene of, amongst other things, yeast biotechnology and strain development (Pretorius, 2016, 2017; Steensels et al., 2014). Over the past seven or so years, researchers developed DNA technologies that enabled them to chemi-

cally synthesize long stretches of DNA sequences and to edit any DNA sequence with laser-sharp precision (David and Siewers, 2015). These newest breakthrough-technologies toppled the balance between DNA sequencing and DNA synthesis approaches, thereby shifting the ‘DNA reading’ (sequencing) paradigm of the late 1990s to today's ‘DNA writing’ (synthesis) paradigm.

This paradigm-shift marks a historical turning point in the course of biotechnological progress in agriculture, bio-energy and biomedicine (Cameron et al., 2014; Khalil and Collins, 2010; Kelly et al., 2014). The approach to yeast research, including wine yeast strain development, was forever changed the moment when ‘genetic engineering’ upgraded to ‘genome engineering’ (Annaluru et al., 2014; Dymond et al., 2011; Dymond and Boeke, 2012; Gaj et al., 2013; Gibson, 2014; Gibson and

Together with the bacterium Escherichia coli, the yeast S. cerevisiae has played a pivotal role in the development of biomolecular sciences as fields of academic research (Adams, 2016). It is therefore not surprising that Synthetic Biology – as the newest addition to the suite of biomolecular sciences – was built upon these two model organisms over the past 15 years. As a eukaryotic model organism for genetics, molecular and systems biology, and genetic engineering, S. cerevisiae has been central in an impressive list of breakthrough discoveries in the laboratory and ‘world-first’ applications in industry (Fig. 6) (Pretorius, 2017). This simple single-celled fungus (Fig. 7) has now become Synthetic Biology's legacy eukaryotic ‘chassis’, as a result of this interdisciplinary field of research growing out of the biomolecular and computational sciences, informational technologies and engineering, and genetic engineering morphing into genome engineering (Williams et al., 2016, 2017).

The term ‘chassis’ is often used by engineers to describe the framework that supports other physical components for an engineered

structure (Adams, 2016). Synthetic biologists borrowed this term from the engineers to refer to the organism that serves as a framework to physically accommodate new biological parts (genes), devices (gene networks) and modules (biosynthetic pathways) when the biological systems (cells and organisms) are being (re)designed.

Over the past five years, yeast cell ‘chasses’ have been used in the context of both fundamental and applied research. For example, in 2014, a laboratory strain of S. cerevisiae became the first eukaryotic cell to be equipped with a fully functional synthetic chromosome for the purpose of advancing the frontiers of Synthetic Biology and fundamental understanding of the inner workings of yeast cells (Annaluru et al., 2014). While this work was being conducted, S. cerevisiae was used as a ‘cell factory’ for the production of the first commercial product derived from Synthetic Biology, i.e. artemisinic acid, a precursor of the potent anti-malarial compound called artemisinin (Paddon et al., 2013). The latter successful commercial application of S. cerevisiae as a cell factory was quickly followed by the semisynthetic production of other high-value compounds which included food ingredients, such as vanillin (Brochado et al., 2010; Brochado and Patil, 2013; Gallage and Møller, 2015; Strucko et al., 2015), resveratrol (Li et al., 2015), raspberry ketone (Lee et al., 2016), saffron, stevia and nootkatoon (see www.evolva.com).

# 2. Synthetic genomes in the making

# 2.1. Making synthetic DNA cost-effectively and time-efficiently

The journey of emerging fields of scientific endeavor normally commences with a slow pace of small incremental steps, due to costprohibitive, underdeveloped technologies, before the pace of progress starts to accelerate exponentially. This truism was captured in 1965 with Intel co-founder Gordon Moore's iconic observation that computing power tends to double every two years with a concomitant halving of its price. Over the past 50 years, Moore's law stood the test of time with only minor revision in many technological advances, including DNA sequencing.

Nowhere was the breathtaking acceleration of progress with a technology more evident than with the sequencing of the complete genome of the S288c laboratory strain of S. cerevisiae (Goffeau et al., 1996; Oliver, 1996). The announcement of the first full genome sequence of a eukaryotic cell on 24 April 1996 was a milestone achievement of a well-coordinated international effort of many laboratories over a period of $> 1 0$ years: $5 5 \%$ was completed by a consortium of $> 7 0$ European laboratories with significant contributions from the Sanger Centre in the UK, Washington University in St. Louis $( 1 5 \% )$ , Stanford University in California $( 7 \% )$ , McGill University in Canada $( 4 \% )$ , and RIKEN University in Japan $( 2 \% )$ . This project not only uncovered answers to a wide range of fundamental biological questions, it also drove significant advances in sequencing technology – advances that made genome sequencing faster, cheaper and more accurate.

The successes achieved with the yeast genome sequencing project, boosted the efforts aimed at the sequencing of the human genome [Human Genome Project (HGP)], which was conceptualized in 1984 and launched in 1 October 1990. Due to the incredibly powerful improvements in DNA sequencing technologies, the first draft of the human genome was announced in June 2000, and a more complete version in April 2003. In part driven by the National Human Genome Research Institute's (NHGRI) Advanced DNA Sequencing Technology Development program, the cost of sequencing a human genome fell a million-fold from $\$ 3$ billion to less than a $\$ 1000$ in just over a decade (Boeke et al., 2016).

As the field of Synthetic Biology is maturing into a ‘game-changing’ science, the world is yet again teetering on the ‘tipping point’ of Moore's steep downward cost graph driven by an equally steep upward curve of technological innovations. Sequencing, analyzing, synthesizing and editing DNA continue to advance at breakneck speed. The swift

improvement of foundational tools to enable the synthesis of longer and longer DNA segments at fast-falling prices, together with the rapid development of more powerful software analytics and high-throughput robotics in genome foundries, will undoubtedly transform the scale and velocity in which the ‘rewriting’ of the genetic scripts of life-forms can occur.

It is in our nature to take advantage of more cost-effective and timeefficient approaches to uncover and utilize the ‘sheer possibility’ offered by Synthetic Biology. As was the case with improvements in DNA sequencing technologies, faster, cheaper and more accurate DNA synthesis technologies will help us advance the frontiers of our knowledge of the world we live in. However, with improved DNA synthesis and associated technologies we can go one step further to overcome a hurdle described by Richard Feynman as “What I cannot create, I do not understand”. Put differently in the context of this article, there is now a growing excitement amongst researchers with the prospect of being able to make DNA, chromosomes and genomes in search of the missing answers in the As, Gs, Cs and Ts of cells and organisms, and of being able to fully understand the deepest intricacies of life's genetic blueprints and regulatory networks.

# 2.2. Making it big with synthetic chromosomes

The first glimpse of how the enabling DNA synthesis technologies were put to work in the laboratory was the synthesis of a DNA copy of the 7458-nucleotide poliovirus' RNA genome in 2002 (Cello et al., 2002) and the 5386-bp genome of the bacteriophage Phi-X174 in 2003 (Smith et al., 2003). Since 2003, only a limited number of other virus and transposon genomes have been synthesized in their entirety. These synthesized DNA sequences of viruses were tiny in comparison to what was to come with the synthesis of two bacterial chromosomes.

Thanks to the reduced cost and improved accuracy of DNA synthesis techniques, the 583-kb ‘chromosome’ of Mycoplasma genitalium was chemically synthesized in full in 2008 (Gibson et al., 2008). This was followed by the synthesis of the 1.1-Mb chromosomal DNA of Mycoplasma mycoides in 2010; the result nearly identical to M. mycoides' natural genome (Gibson et al., 2010). When this synthetically-made circular chromosome was successfully used to replace the chromosomal DNA of Mycoplasma capricolum, the world witnessed the creation of the first SSO. Since then, a reduced (minimal) version (531 kb carrying 473 genes) of the M. mycoides genome was synthesized as a versatile platform for further investigations into whole-genome design (Hutchison et al., 2016).

These achievements with bacteria was taken one step further with the alteration of the genetic code throughout the ~4.7-Mb E. coli genome (Isaacs et al., 2011; Lajoie et al., 2013). The recoded genome conferred viral resistance to a laboratory strain of E. coli. The ‘immunity’ to viruses was later extended to biocontainment strains, known as E. coli 2.0, fully dependent on a 21st amino acid (Chan et al., 2016; Mandell et al., 2015; Rovner et al., 2015).

Following the successful synthesis of two viral and bacterial genomes, the next logical step was to synthesize a chromosome of a eukaryote; and the obvious choice was a yeast chromosome. The sequenced genome of S. cerevisiae S288c varies between ~12 Mb (non-redundant) and ${ \sim } 1 4 \mathrm { M b }$ (total) in length. This genome is distributed along 16 linear chromosomes with sizes ranging between $\sim 2 0 0$ and $\sim 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { k b }$ . Together, these chromosomes carry $\sim 6 0 0 0$ genes of which $\sim 5 0 0 0$ are individually non-essential (Goffeau et al., 1996; Oliver, 1996).

The first step towards synthesizing a yeast chromosome was taken in 2011 with the synthesis of the two arms of Chromosome 3 – the third smallest S. cerevisiae chromosome (Dymond et al., 2011). This paved the way for the synthesis of Chromosome 3 in full. In 2014, a 272,871- bp synthetic version (syn3) was used to replace the native Chromosome 3 of 316,617 bp (Annaluru et al., 2014). In addition to the fact that syn3 was $1 4 \%$ shorter than its native counterpart, syn3 was also designed to

contain $> 5 0 { , } 0 0 0$ deliberate alterations. These ‘edits’ in syn3 included the deletion of dispensable DNA sequences; the incorporation of uniquely-recognizable sequences that could be used to differentiate between the native and synthetic DNA; the replacement of particular stop codons with other codons that terminate gene transcription equally well; and the flanking of non-essential genes with DNA sequences designed to catalyze their deletion on a specific signal. More specifically, subtelomeric regions, introns, transfer RNA (tRNA) genes, transposons (Ty elements) and silent mating-type loci (HML and HMR) were deleted; TAG stop codons were recoded to TAA stop codons; and loxPsym sites were added to enable genome scrambling when needed. These changes in syn3 did not, however, make the transformed strain less fit than the parent strain containing the native form of Chromosome 3 (Gibson and Venter, 2014). This successful building of the world's first synthetic functional chromosome of a complex cell, represented a major step forward in Synthetic Biology, and confirmed the versatility of S. cerevisiae as the leading experimental eukaryotic model organism. This pioneering work also posed the obvious question to the research community of how to move this research from building a synthetic yeast chromosome to the design and building of a synthetic yeast genome.

# 2.3. Making it to the top with a synthetic yeast genome

Making it their priority to design and build the world's first synthetic eukaryotic genome, a consortium of a dozen research laboratories in five countries (USA, UK, China, Australia and Singapore) initiated the Synthetic Yeast Genome (Sc2.0) Project to synthesize all 16 chromosomes of S. cerevisiae S288c. The stated goals of this large collaborative project include answering “a wide variety of profound questions about fundamental properties of chromosomes, genome organization, gene content, function of RNA splicing, the extent to which small RNAs play a role in yeast biology, the distinction between prokaryotes and eukaryotes, and questions relating to genome structure and evolution” (see www.syntheticyeast.org).

Less than four years into this ambitious project, about $7 5 \%$ of the yeast genome has been synthesized. To date, the design, synthesis and three-dimensional organization of six of the 16 chromosomes (i.e. Chromosomes 2, 3, 5, 6 10 and 12) have been published (Mercy et al., 2017; Mitchell et al., 2017; Richardson et al., 2017; Shen et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2017; Y. Zhang et al., 2017). The Sc2.0 project is on track to be completed within the next year or so, and to answer some basic and intriguing biological questions: What would be the effect on cell fitness if all introns and Ty elements were to be removed from the yeast genome?; Would the genetic processes and protein synthesis machinery be disadvantaged if all tRNA genes were to be transferred from the cell's 16 chromosomes to an additional ‘neochromosome’?; and Would it be possible to reduce the total number of chromosomes by fusing some of the 16 chromosomes? These are just a few examples of fundamental questions that are being answered as the $s \mathrm { c } 2 . 0$ project progresses.

It is hoped that the availability of a fully synthetic S. cerevisiae genome will also advance the development of novel strains tailored for various industries, including the food and wine industries.

# 3. Synthetic bioproducts in the making

# 3.1. Making waves with food ingredients from semisynthetic yeast cell factories

Make no mistake, different people see very different shades and tones of red when they think of food ingredients produced by a GMO or SSO. In a physical context, red is a number of similar color tones of the longest wavelengths of light discernible by the human eye; however, in the context of food ingredients derived from GMOs or SSOs, red is the color range associated with a wide spectrum of emotions – from love to

rage – that such foods evoke. For example, whereas most scientists might visualize a food industry that rolls out the ‘red carpet’ for appropriately-tested GMO- and SSO-derived food products, some anti-GMO and anti-SSO advocacy groups might raise ‘red herrings’ with baseless safety claims and, as a result, regulators might envision more ‘red tape’ to be rolled out. In this red-hot debate, many people tend to be ‘color-blind’ confusing which hue of red correlates with factually correct information about food ingredients produced by a GMO or SSO.

Nowhere is this ‘color-blindness’ more visible than when one watches how the world is currently accepting the yeast-derived semisynthetic artemisinic acid ingredient of the commercialized antimalarial compound, artemisinin, versus the diverse range of public responses when they are confronted with semisynthetic food ingredients, such as resveratrol, saffron, stevia, vanillin, nootkatoon and raspberry ketone (Lee et al., 2016; Li et al., 2015; Strucko et al., 2015; www.evolva.com). Why would GMO/SSO-derived medicines be safer to inject into one's veins than to swallow GMO/SSO-derived food products? Why are SSO-produced food ingredients making immensely bigger waves than GMO/SSO-generated medicines in this ongoing debate? The fact of the matter is that it does not matter whether scientific studies have demonstrated that a particular GMO/SSOderived food product is safe if people refuse to eat or drink it. This scientific and cultural quagmire is a ‘red flag’ that needs to be dealt with in a very balanced and rational way.

One solution might be that Synthetic Biology tools, such as CRISPR DNA editing technologies, would provide us with the ability to redesign specific genes and gene networks without having to introduce DNA from other organisms. The first glimpses of hope come from countries, such as Argentina, Germany and Sweden, where regulators have already made a distinction between GMOs and organisms whose DNA has been edited with CRISPR-cas9 technologies. It also seems that the US Food and Drug Administration might follow suit (Goold et al., 2017). This could make CRISPR-designed food products more readily available and easily regulated than any other form of bioengineered food product. It is, however, too early to speculate whether food regulators and authorities as well as consumers would roll-up the ‘red tape’ where necessary and roll out the ‘red carpet’ for safe bioproducts with value-adding properties that involves organisms whose DNA was edited by the CRISPR-cas9 ‘red pen’.

# 3.2. Making it happen in winemaking with a semisynthetic ‘raspberry’ yeast

Red wines made from grape cultivars, such as Gamay, Nebbiolo, Pinot Noir and Tempranillo, are often associated with ‘red fruit’ flavours as opposed to ‘dark fruit’ flavours. One of these ‘red fruit’ flavours is raspberry. Of course, it is uncommon to detect raspberry in the aroma of a white wine. To demonstrate Synthetic Biology's potential power for step-change innovation in the wine industry, an experimental raspberry-tasting Chardonnay wine was made by a semisynthetic wine yeast strain (Lee et al., 2016).

This SSO was equipped with a biosynthetic pathway, consisting of four separate enzymatic activities (for the detailed construction of the pathway see Figure 20 in Pretorius, 2017). These bioengineered activities complemented the native activities of the host wine yeast strain (AWRI1631), thereby enabling it to produce the de novo synthesis of the highly-desirable raspberry ketone, 4-[4-hydroxyphenyl]butan-2-one. The phenylpropanoid pathway leading to the production of 4-[4-hydroxyphenyl]butan-2-one commences with the conversion of phenylalanine to $p$ -coumaric acid via cinnamate or directly from tyrosine to $p$ -coumaric acid. Conversion of $p$ -coumaric acid to 4-[4- hydroxyphenyl]butan-2-one requires three additional enzymatic steps including a condensation reaction between coumaroyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA.

# 4. A brighter future in the making

History tells us that the wine industry and wine consumers have benefitted from human ingenuity, inventions and scientific discoveries for more than seven millennia. It is, therefore, not far-fetched to predict that innovation will continue to be the ‘oxygen-carrying red blood cells’ of a healthy wine industry to future-proof its sustainability. This is not the first time that the wine industry has had to grapple with new futureshaping technologies and disruptive innovations. Our contention is that there will be robust debates as to whether the wine industry should make room for Synthetic Biology in their toolkit of technologies to support their relentless pursuit of making ‘the best wine ever’.

These debates about the potential application and impact of SSOs in winemaking will no doubt be emotive, inflamed and heated at times. Hopefully, this review article will help bring a calm historical perspective to the debate of future wine innovation based on synthetic genomics and will facilitate crucially-important well-informed dialogue

about Synthetic Biology in the context of the wine industry. The wine industry's long history of being reliant upon continuous innovation installs the necessary confidence that it has all the makings to be, once again, up for the challenges of the future.
