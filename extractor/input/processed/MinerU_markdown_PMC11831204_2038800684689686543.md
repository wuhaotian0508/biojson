# Ethylene-independent modulation of root development by ACC via downregulation of WOX5 and group I CLE peptide expression

Wangshu Moua,b,c,d,1 , Ria Kharee,1 , Joanna K. Polkoe , Isaiah Taylorf , Juan Xug , Dawei Xuea , Philip Benfeyf , Bram Van de Poelb,c,2 , Caren Changd,2 , and Joseph J. Kiebere,2 

Edited by Mark Estelle, University of California at San Diego, La Jolla, CA; received August 30, 2024; accepted December 30, 2024 

In seed plants, the canonical role of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) is to serve as the precursor in the biosynthesis of the phytohormone ethylene, and indeed, ACC treatment is often used as a proxy for ethylene treatment. Increasing evidence suggests that ACC can also act independently of ethylene to regulate various aspects of plant growth and development. Here, we explore the effects of ACC on Arabidopsis thaliana root growth and the mechanisms by which it acts. ACC inhibits growth of the primary root in Arabidopsis seedlings when ethylene signaling is blocked, which becomes evident after $3 6 \mathbf { h }$ of treatment with ACC. This reduced root growth is in part the result of suppressed cell proliferation in the root meristem resulting from altered expression of a key regulator of stem cell niche activity, WOX5. ACC also promotes lateral root (LR) development, in contrast to ethylene, which inhibits LR formation. Transcriptomic analysis of roots revealed no significant changes in gene expression after $4 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ or 4 h of ACC treatment, but longer treatment times revealed a large number of differentially expressed genes, including the downregulation of the expression of a small group of phylogenetically related CLE peptides. Reduced expression of these group 1 CLEs in response to ACC leads to the activation of a transcription factor, LBD18, which promotes LR development. These results suggest that ACC acts to modulate multiple aspects of Arabidopsis root growth independently of ethylene via distinct transcriptional effects in the root meristem and LR precursor cells.

In seed plants, the first committed step in the biosynthesis of the plant hormone ethylene is the production of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) from S-adenosylmethionine catalyzed by the enzyme ACC SYNTHASE (ACS), which is followed by the conversion of ACC to ethylene by the enzyme ACC OXIDASE (ACO) ( 1 ). There is accumulating evidence that ACC also functions independently of ethylene to regulate multiple biological processes, including root cell wall extensibility ( 2 , 3 ), early vegetative growth ( 4 ), stomatal development ( 5 ), and pollen tube attraction ( 6 ). The mechanistic underpinnings of these roles of ACC have not been elucidated. 

Root architecture plays a critical role in providing structural support for plant anchorage and optimizing water and nutrient absorption ( 7 ). One aspect of root architecture is primary root length, which is determined by cell proliferation and expansion that occurs in three continuous developmental zones: the meristem, elongation, and differentiation zones ( 8 ). Root cells in the meristem zone undergo repeated rounds of cell division, then exit the cell cycle to rapidly expand in the elongation zone, and finally differentiate to maturity in the differentiation zone ( 8 ). The quiescent center (QC) along with the surrounding initial cells constitutes the stem cell niche, whose activity influences cell division in the meristem zone ( 9 , 10 ). A number of factors have been identified that regulate stem cell niche activity, including WUSCHEL RELATED HOMEOBOX5 (WOX5), a transcription factor specifically expressed in the QC that plays a pivotal role in balancing cell proliferation and differentiation in the root apical meristem ( 11 – 14 ). Ethylene has been reported to inhibit primary root elongation by affecting cell expansion, acting in part by upregulating auxin biosynthesis and basipetal auxin transport ( 15 – 17 ) and by negatively regulating meristematic cell division via crosstalk with cytokinin ( 18 ). 

A second aspect of root architecture is the density and length of lateral roots (LRs). The growth and development of LRs is a complex process: LR primordia (LRP) are initiated from pericycle cells, which then undergo multiple rounds of division and expansion to pass through overlaying cell layers, and eventually penetrate the epidermis to emerge as mature LRs ( 19 ). Auxin plays a key role in the formation of LRs, acting as a positive regulator of the asymmetric divisions of the pericycle founder cells ( 20 ). In contrast, ethylene inhibits LR formation, at least in part by modulating auxin transport ( 21 , 22 ). 

# Significance

1-Aminocyclopropane-1- carboxylic acid (ACC) has long been known as the precursor of the plant hormone ethylene. Studies have suggested that ACC may act independently of ethylene to modulate various aspects of plant growth and development, but its mechanism of action has not been characterized. The work presented here begins to illuminate how ACC acts to modulate root growth in Arabidopsis .

Multiple other inputs regulate LR formation, including several transcription factors (LBDs and GATA23) and small signaling peptides ( 23 ). 

One group of peptides regulating LR formation are the CLAVATA3 (CLV3)/EMBRYO SURROUNDING REGION (ESR)-related (CLE) family, one of the largest groups of small signaling peptides in plants ( 24 ). A specific subclade of CLE peptides (CLE1–CLE7) has been shown to negatively regulate LR formation ( 25 ). These group I CLEs are perceived by the receptor-like kinases CLV1 and BAM1 and suppress LR initiation by modulating the expression of several LATERAL ORGAN BOUNDARIES DOMAIN/ASYMMETRIC LEAVES2-LIKE (LBD/ASL ) genes ( 25 ), which encode transcription factors that promote LR formation ( 26 ). Further, the expression of most of these group I CLEs is affected by nutrients in the environment (N, P, K, or S), and thus link LR formation to the abundance of these nutrients ( 27 – 29 ). Additionally, CLE peptides from a distinct phylogenetic clade (CLE16/17) promote LR emergence via activating the expression of HAESA /HAESA-LIKE2 (HAE /HSL2 ) ( 30 ). 

Several studies indicate an ethylene-independent role for ACC in regulating root development ( 31 , 32 ). Inhibition of ACC, but not inhibition of ethylene perception, suppresses the short root phenotype of the fei1/fei2 mutant, which reduces root anisotropic growth via decreased cellulose biosynthesis ( 2 ). Similarly, treatment with exogenous ACC, but not ethylene, reduces root cell elongation in response to cell wall damage stress ( 3 ). Further, exogenous ACC suppresses primary root elongation in Arabidopsis independent of ethylene ( 4 ). These results suggest that ACC acts independently of ethylene to modulate root development. 

Here, we demonstrate that ACC, independent of its role in ethylene biosynthesis, inhibits primary root elongation in Arabidopsis and that this inhibition is not evident until after $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ of ACC treatment. ACC acts by suppressing cell division in the meristematic zone via altered WOX5 expression as well as by reducing cell expansion in the differentiation zone. ACC also promotes LR development by reducing the expression of multiple group I CLE s, leading to the activation of LBD18 . 

# Results

ACC Inhibits Primary Root Length Independently from Its Role as the Ethylene Precursor. We used an ethylene-insensitive mutant, ein2-5 (33), to query the effects of ACC on Arabidopsis primary root growth independent of ethylene. We found that exogenous ACC at $\leq 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ did not have a substantial effect on primary root elongation, but at 10 to $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , there was significant inhibition of root elongation (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). This inhibition was only evident after 36 h of ACC treatment (Fig. 1A). Such inhibition of primary root elongation was also observed in the ET-free mutant, which is unable to convert ACC to ethylene due to the disruption of all five ACO genes (34) (Fig. 1 B and C, SI Appendix, Fig.  S1A), and in wild-type (WT) seedlings in the presence of a strong inhibitor of ethylene signaling [1-methylcyclopropene; 1-MCP (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 B and $C )$ ], at a concentration that completely blocks ethylene responses (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). We confirmed that ACC is stable under the growth conditions used and is not detectably converted to ethylene (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). Taken together, these results indicate that ACC reduces primary root growth independently of ethylene signaling. 

ACC Suppresses Cell Division in the Primary Root Meristem. We examined the cellular basis underlying the inhibitory effect of ACC on primary root growth. With ACC treatment, the length of the root apical meristem (RAM) in 7-d-old ein2-5 and ET-free seedlings was ${ \sim } 2 0 \%$ shorter compared to that in 

untreated seedlings (Fig. 1 D and $E _ { c }$ ). Consistent with this, RAM size was also reduced $( \sim 2 2 \% )$ in ACC-treated WT seedlings in the presence of 1-MCP (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 $D$ and $E$ ). These results suggest that the ethylene-independent effect of ACC on primary root elongation is at least in part due to a smaller RAM. Interestingly, the response of the RAM to ACC was somewhat attenuated in WT seedlings when ethylene signaling was not blocked (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 A and $B$ ), suggesting that ethylene may counteract the effects of ACC on the RAM. Consistent with this, treatment with exogenous ethylene blocked the effect of ACC on RAM size in the ET-free mutant, but not in ein2-5 (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 C and D). 

ACC reduced the number of meristematic cells (as measured in cortical cell files) in both ein2-5 and ET-free mutants ( Fig. 1F ) without affecting the length of these cells ( Fig. 1G ). Similarly, WT roots treated with ACC in the presence of 1-MCP also had fewer cells in the RAM, while cell size was not significantly affected (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 F and G ). These findings suggest that ACC reduces RAM size via reduced cell proliferation rather than inhibition of cell expansion. To assess whether this results from a defect in cell cycle progression, we incubated the seedlings with 5-ethynyl- $\cdot 2 ^ { \prime }$ ′-deoxyuridine (EdU), a thymidine analog that incorporates into nascent DNA during S-phase ( 35 ). We found that the fluorescence intensity of actively dividing cells labeled by EdU was significantly lower in both ACC-treated ein2-5 and ET-free roots ( Fig. $1 H )$ ), suggesting that ACC suppresses mitotic activity of root meristem cells. 

Several transcriptional factors, including SCARECROW (SCR)/SHORT ROOT (SHR), PLETHORA1/2 (PLT1/2), and WUSCHEL-RELATED HOMEOBOX5 (WOX5), play crucial roles in maintaining the stem cell niche activity in the RAM ( 36 ). We examined whether ACC affected the expression of transcription factors by examining the expression of various GFP reporter transgenes. The proSHR:SHR-NLS-GFP , proSCR:SCR-NLS-GFP , proPLT1:PLT1-YFP, and proPLT2:PLT2-YFP reporters were not obviously influenced by ACC treatment in the presence of 1-MCP (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 5 A { - } H )$ ). In contrast, there was a significant decrease in WOX5 expression in the QC in response to ACC ( Fig. 1 I and $J ,$ red arrow in Fig. 1I ). Further, in approximately half of the seedlings, ACC treatment caused an expansion of proWOX5:GFP expression to a more proximal meristem position and to the lower columella tiers (yellow arrow in Fig. 1I ). As WOX5 is critical for the differentiation of CSCs ( 11 ), we examined whether ACC affects CSC development. We observed that ${ \sim } 5 8 \%$ of WT roots treated with ACC (in the presence of 1-MCP) exhibited an additional tier of CSC ( Fig. 1 K and L ) and the same was seen in ${ \sim } 5 0 \%$ of ACC-treated ein2-5 and ET-free mutant roots (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 ). These results indicated that ACC reduces WOX5 expression and alters its spatial expression pattern, resulting in a delay in the differentiation of CSC daughters into differentiated CCs. 

Given that auxin signaling may function downstream of ACC in reducing root elongation in response to cell wall damage ( 3 ), we examined whether ACC alters auxin responses in roots using an auxin-responsive reporter, proDR5:GUS . The expression pattern of proDR5:GUS was not significantly affected by ACC treatment in the root when ethylene was blocked, indicating that the effect of ACC on primary root length is likely not mediated by changes in auxin (SI Appendix, Fig. S5I ). 

We next examined the effects of ACC on the root differentiation zone. In response to ACC treatment, we observed a significant reduction in the size of cortical cells in the differentiation zone of WT, ein2-5 , and ET-free seedlings (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ), which is similar to the effects of ethylene on cell expansion in WT roots ( 37 ).

ACC Positively Regulates LR Density Independently of Ethylene Function. Consistent with prior studies (21, 22, 38), we find that treatment of WT roots with ACC, which causes elevated ethylene biosynthesis, reduced the total number of emerged LRs as well as the LR density (Fig. 2 A–C). In the ein2-5 and ET-free mutants, however, we found that ACC treatment significantly increased the density of LRs (measured as the number of LRs per cm of the primary root or per 100 cortical cells) (Fig. 2 A–C). We analyzed the frequency of LRPs at specific developmental stages [classified as Stage I–IV, VI–VII, and emerged LRs (19)]. In 5-d-old ein2-5 seedlings, there was a significant increase in the density of emerged 

LRs in response to ACC, but there was no effect on the density of preemergent LRs (Fig. 2 $D$ and $E$ ). In 7- and 9-d-old seedlings, there was also increased density of emerged LRs in response to ACC, but surprisingly fewer LRPs at earlier stages (Stage I–IV) of LRPs (Fig. 2 $D$ and E), suggesting that ACC accelerates the growth and/or emergence of LRs. Consistent with this, the length of LRs is increased in response to ACC (SI Appendix, Fig. S8A). ACC also significantly promoted LR branching, causing an increase in the number of second-order LRs (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 B and C).

The acs octuple mutant had a shorter primary root and a significantly lower density of LRs as compared to the WT when ethylene perception was blocked with 1-MCP, and this difference was suppressed by exogenous ACC ( Fig. 2 F and $G$ ). We quantified the time course of LR development in WT and acs octuple mutant seedlings ( Fig. 2 H and I ). The density of emerged LRs was decreased in the acs octuple mutant at day 7 and 9, and earlier stages of LRPs (I–IV) were significantly decreased at days 5 and 7, suggesting a delay in LR formation when ACC synthesis is disrupted ( Fig. $2 H ,$ ). The length of the LRs in the acs octuple is significantly shorter than that of the WT (SI Appendix, Fig. S8D ). Taken together, these results suggest that ethylene and ACC have opposing effects on LR development, with ACC positively regulating LR development. 

# ACC Promotes LR Formation By Suppressing CLE Gene Expression.

To further elucidate the molecular mechanisms underpinning the response to ACC, we examined changes in the transcriptome in response to exogenous ACC in the ein2-5 mutant using RNA-seq. There were no significant differentially expressed genes (DEGs) after 45 min in response to ACC treatment, and only 3 DEGs after $^ { 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ (two up-regulated and one down-regulated, all three of which were expressed at very low levels (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S9 A and B). We re-examined the expression of these three potential DEGs in roots in response to exogenous ACC using qRT-PCR. One gene showed no detectable expression $\left( { \mathrm { A t } } 3 \mathrm { g } 4 4 9 7 0 \right)$ ) and the other two (At3g46780 and $\mathrm { A t } 2 \mathrm { g } 0 4 0 5 0 $ ) were not significantly differentially expressed in response to ACC between 3 and $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S9C). In contrast, after $4 0 \mathrm { { h } }$ of treatment, there were 359 DEGs $P$ -value $\leq 0 . 0 0 1$ ; $\mathrm { F D R } = 0 . 0 5$ ; $\log _ { 2 }$ fold-change $\geq 1$ ) in response to ACC (Fig. 3A and Dataset S1). These results are consistent with the observation that ein2-5 roots exhibited significantly reduced elongation in response to ACC only after $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ of exposure (Fig. 1A). The 147 upregulated genes were enriched in multiple gene ontology (GO) terms, including “response to hypoxia” (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S9D). We examined the kinetics of one upregulated gene, HRG2 $( H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ Response Gene), and found that it was induced in response to ACC after $3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ of treatment, but not after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ or less of treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S9E). The downregulated genes were predominantly enriched in GO terms related to root development (SI Appendix, Fig. S9F). Notably, the down-regulated genes included multiple genes encoding CLE peptides, all of which are within the same phylogenetic clade (Group I) (Fig. 3 B and C). No other CLE genes apart from this clade were found to be significantly regulated by ACC (Fig. 3B). We examined the kinetics of the regulation of a subset of these group I CLE genes and found that there was significant downregulation in response to ACC in both ein2-5 and ET-free lines only after $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ (CLE7) or 48 h (CLE5 and CLE6) of treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S10 A and B). Consistent with exogenous ACC treatment, the acs octuple mutant showed enhanced expression levels of CLE5 and CLE7 (Fig. 3D). 

To examine the effect of ACC on the spatial pattern of CLE expression, we analyzed the proCLE:GUS fusion lines for CLE5 , CLE6, and CLE7 ( 25 ). ACC treatment (in the presence of 1-MCP) decreased the promoter activity of CLE5 , CLE6, and CLE7 , primarily early in LR development ( Fig. 3E ). The expression of these CLE genes at this stage was mostly in the cells surrounding the LRP ( Fig. 3E ), consistent with ACC likely affecting early LR development or emergence ( Fig. $2 D$ ). 

Group I CLEs (CLE1–CLE7) have been previously reported to negatively regulate LR formation ( 25 ). Thus, we examined LR density with and without ACC (in the presence of 1-MCP) and/ or exogenous CLE5/6 peptide in WT, a mutant disrupted for all 

seven group $\mathrm { ~ I ~ } C L E$ genes (cle septuple) ( 25 ), and the acs octuple ( Fig. 4 A and B ). As above, WT and the acs octuple mutant seedlings displayed an increased number of LRs in response to the ACC, and this increase was slightly reduced by exogenous CLE5/6 peptide ( Fig. 4 A and B ). The cle septuple mutant constitutively displayed an increased number of LRs compared to the WT, consistent with previous observations ( 25 ), and this increase was partially suppressed by treatment with exogenous CLE peptide ( Fig. 4 $A$ and B ). ACC treatment caused a slight increase in LR density in the cle septuple mutant as compared to control cle septuple seedlings, but the fold-change in response to ACC relative to the control was substantially less than was observed in WT and acs octuple seedlings (1.9-fold increase in cle vs. 4.4- and 17-fold in WT and acs oct, respectively). The partial response to ACC in the cle septuple mutants suggests the cle septuple is not a complete null and/or that ACC acts partially through a mechanism independent of these group-1 CLEs. Overall, these data suggest that ACC promotes LR formation at least in part through downregulation of group I CLE expression. 

A subset of LATERAL ORGAN BOUNDARIES DOMAIN (LBD) transcription factors (LBD16, LBD18, and LBD29) are key regulators of LR formation, and prior studies have shown that they function downstream of group I CLE peptides to mediate LR development ( 25 ). Thus, we examined the transcript levels of LBD16 , 18, and 29 in ein2-5 roots treated with ACC. There was no significant change in expression of LBD16 or LBD29 in response to ACC, but LBD18 was induced 3.4-fold compared to the control ( Fig. 4C ). We examined the timing and spatial pattern of LBD18 expression in response to ACC using a proLBD18:GUS reporter line ( Fig. 4D ). There was an increase in expression of LBD18 most notably early in LR formation (Stage I–III), in both the early LRP cells and some surrounding cells, suggesting that the ACC may promote early LR development by activating LBD18 expression. Consistent with this, the lbd18 mutant exhibited reduced sensitivity to ACC treatment compared to the WT in terms of increased density of LRs ( Fig. 4 $E$ and $F _ { . }$ ). 

# Discussion

Several studies suggest that ACC functions as a signal independently of its role as the ethylene precursor in multiple biological processes (reviewed in ref. 32 ), including inhibition of primary root elongation in Arabidopsis ( 4 ), but the mechanisms by which it acts are not understood. Surprisingly, we found that the effects of ACC on root elongation are substantially delayed $\left( > 3 6 \mathrm { ~ h } \right)$ ) compared to most other signals in plants, including the response to ethylene gas, which acts with a lag time of <15 min ( 40 ). This delayed response is not a result of delayed ACC uptake as ACC treatment of WT seedlings results in a rapid decrease in root elongation ( Fig. 1A ). One explanation for this delayed response is that ACC acts via a slow-acting signaling pathway, similarly to the pathway underlying the induction of flowering time ( 41 ). Alternatively, ACC may need to be metabolized into a distinct, active component, and the conversion to this hypothetical active molecule may be slow. Finally, ACC may act by modulating the metabolome in some way to ultimately alter root elongation and LR formation, and the delay in response time may reflect the time it takes to accumulate a target metabolite(s) to sufficient levels. Other responses to ACC may act with distinct kinetics. For example, ACC’s role in the production of signals emanating from the ovule required for pollen tube attraction likely occurs within $^ { 4 \mathrm { h } }$ as shown by semi-in vitro assays, and an increase in calcium levels in isolated ovules in response to ACC was noted within 2 min of ACC treatment ( 6 ).

Prior studies have linked ACC to multiple processes, including stomatal development ( 5 ), pollen tube attraction ( 6 ), and most relevant to this work, root elongation. For the latter, ACC modulated cell wall function and hence root elongation in the context of the fei1 fei2 mutant or in response to various cell stresses such as isoxaben or $\mathrm { { f g } } 2 2$ treatment ( 2 , 3 ). Similar to the results presented here, Vanderstraeten et al. ( 4 ) showed that ACC inhibited root elongation independent of ethylene, though these authors do not report any effects on LR development. 

Intriguingly, ACC and ethylene have opposing or distinct effects on some aspects of root development. For example, ethylene inhibits ( 21 ), but ACC promotes LR formation in Arabidopsis seedlings. Consistent with the increased number of LRs observed in the ein2-5 mutant ( Fig. 2B ), Negi et al. ( 21 ) also observed an obvious increase in LR density (per cm of root length) of ethyleneinsensitive mutants in response to ACC treatment. They interpreted this result as ethylene negatively regulating LR formation in an ETR1- or EIN2-dependent manner, but in light of the results presented here, we suggest that ACC itself mediates LR 

development. Likewise, ACC and ethylene have distinct effects on RAM growth; ethylene has been reported to stimulate QC cell divisions with the formation of extra cells with QC identity ( 9 ), but we found that ACC suppressed meristematic cell proliferation and caused supernumerary CSCs. Additionally, we observed an attenuated response to ACC in the RAM of WT seedlings as compared to that observed in ein2 or ET-free mutants, implying ethylene signaling may counteract the ACC-specific inhibitory effect on root meristem growth. 

The conversion of ACC to ethylene is blocked in anaerobic conditions as this reaction requires oxygen ( 1 ), but ethylene actually accumulates in flooded tissues due to impaired gas diffusion in an aqueous environment ( 42 , 43 ) and elevated ethylene signaling enables plants to anticipate impending hypoxia ( 44 ). Thus, the relative levels of ethylene and ACC likely change as roots experience low oxygen, and, interestingly, among the GO terms enriched in the genes induced in response to ACC are response to hypoxia, suggesting a potential role of ACC in the response to hypoxia (SI Appendix, Fig. S9D ).

Our results indicate that ethylene and ACC can both inhibit root cell elongation, though likely via distinct mechanisms. In the case of ethylene, this inhibition is mediated via increased auxin biosynthesis and transport toward the elongation zone, leading to the activation of local auxin responses ( 15 – 17 ). However, we do not observe significant changes in the pattern of proDR5:GUS expression in response to ACC when ethylene function is blocked (SI Appendix, Fig. S5I ). This suggests that changes in auxin likely do not play a role in mediating the effects of ACC on root growth and development. Consistent with this, the effects of group 1 CLEs on LR formation are also independent of changes in auxin distribution ( 25 ). 

The regulatory role of ACC in primary root elongation is complex. We observed that the primary root of the acs oct , ein2 mutant is shorter than that of the ein2-5 mutant (SI Appendix, Fig. S11A ). Consistent with this effect of the acs oct mutant, we found that treatment with a low concentration of ACC $( 0 . 0 1 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) actually slightly increases primary root length in ethylene-insensitive mutants (SI Appendix, Fig. S11 $B$ and $C$ ). These results suggest a biphasic effect of ACC on primary root development, similar to that of other plant hormones. For example, exogenous cytokinin inhibits primary root elongation, but higher-order cytokinin receptor mutants have shorter roots than the WT ( 45 ). 

Our results suggest that ACC promotes LR development by downregulating the expression of several group 1 CLE genes as their expression was repressed in response to exogenous ACC and up-regulated in the acs octuple mutant. The acs octuple mutant exhibited a temporally regressed LRP profile compared to the WT, suggesting that ACC may act by regulating the timing of LRP development and/or emergence. Group I CLE peptides (CLE1-CLE7) have been implicated in negatively regulating LR initiation and emergence ( 25 , 28 ), as well as the timing of LRP initiation ( 25 ), consistent with the effects of ACC on LRP. 

ACC treatment suppressed the decreased LR density in the acs octuple mutant, suggesting that the decreased LR density in the acs octuple mutant is the result of decreased endogenous ACC levels. However, application of exogenous CLE5/6 peptide did not fully restore LR density to control levels in WT or acs octuple mutant seedlings in the presence of ACC. This suggests that unmodified CLE peptides may not be fully functional or that there are specific functions for individual CLE family members, but the observation that exogenous CLE5/6 peptide nearly fully suppressed the LR phenotype of the cle1-cle7 septuple mutant indicates that this is not the case. An alternative explanation for the lack of complete suppression of the effect of exogenous ACC by exogenous CLE5/6 is that there are additional pathways by which ACC affects LR formation that do not involve the CLE peptides. 

LBD16, LBD18, and LBD29 are pivotal regulators of LR development ( 46 , 47 ), and CLE1-CLE7 negatively regulate the expression of these three LBD genes ( 25 ). Here, we found that ACC significantly induces the transcript level of LBD18 , specifically increasing its expression in the pericycle cells and LRP, especially during earlier LR developmental stages (Stage I–III). LBD18 functions particularly during LR initiation and emergence ( 47 ), suggesting that these stages are targets of ACC in LR development. LBD18 directly induces the expression of EXPANSIN14 (EXP14 ), which encodes a cell wall-loosening factor that likely facilitates LR emergence ( 48 ). Interestingly, among the ACC downregulated genes, GO terms related to “hemicellulose metabolic process,” “cell wall development and biosynthesis,” and “cell wall biogenesis” were significantly enriched, consistent with previous studies linked ACC to cell wall function ( 2 , 3 ). The enrichment of these terms may reflect a role for ACC in reshaping the cell wall during LR emergence and early growth (SI Appendix, Fig. S9F ). The expression of the group 1 CLEs and LBD18 only partially overlap, though the CLEs are mobile signals that can act in a non-cell-autonomous manner ( 49 ). 

In conclusion, our study has dissected ethylene-independent ACC-specific responses in controlling primary root elongation and LR development and uncovered a potential antagonistic interaction between ACC and ethylene in regulating aspects of root growth and development. We propose a model in which ACC negatively regulates the expression of WOX5 and a subset of group 1 CLE genes to regulate primary root growth and LR development, respectively ( Fig. 4G ). Ethylene in general opposes these effects of ACC, and thus, plants may fine-tune root architecture in response to different environmental conditions by balancing the relative levels of ACC and ethylene. Our findings provide a basis for future studies on how ACC directs root development independently from ethylene, including deciphering the mechanism by which ACC modulates CLE and WOX5 expression. 

# Materials and Methods

Plant Material, Growth Conditions, and Chemical Treatments. We used Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype Columbia (Col-0) as the WT. The aco quintuple ET-free mutant (34) and the cle septuple (cle1-7) mutant (25) were kindly provided by Drs. 

Lijia Qu (Peking University, China) and Shinichiro Sawa (Kumamoto University, Japan), respectively. The acs octuple;ein2 mutant was previously described (6). The CLE5/6/7 promoter-driven GUS reporter lines (#CS66273, #CS66274, and #CS66276), lbd18 mutant (SALK_038125), and the acs octuple mutant (acs2-1, acs4-1, acs5-2, acs6-1, acs7-1, acs9-1, amiRACS8ACS11; #CS16651) were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (ABRC). 

Seeds were surface sterilized, stratified in the dark at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ fo ${ 3 \mathord { \mathrm { d } } } ,$ and sown on media containing 1X Murashige and Skoog (MS) salts (PhytoTech Labs) solidified with $0 . 8 \%$ Gelzan (Sigma-Aldrich). The plates were sealed with micropore tape (3M) and then incubated vertically (except where noted) in a growth chamber with cycles of 16-h light $( 7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } ) / 8$ -h dark at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ and $5 0 \%$ relative humidity. To treat the plants with ACC, we prepared a 100 mM stock of ACC (Sigma-Aldrich) dissolved in water, which was supplemented in the MS agar medium. All ACC treatments were at $1 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } _ { \iota }$ , unless otherwise indicated. For 1-MCP treatment, plates were transferred to a 36 L airtight plexiglass chamber under the same light condition as described above. MCP (Aladdin) was placed in a 50 mL beaker inside the chamber, and $3 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of Milli-Q water was added to dissolve the 1-MCP powder and the chamber immediately sealed, yielding a final conc. of 1 ppm. For ethylene gas treatment, plates were transferred to the same type of airtight chamber, and ethylene gas injected through a septum. For treatment with the exogenous peptide, CLE5/6p (RVSPGGPDPQHH) peptide $( \geq 9 0 \%$ purity) was commercially synthesized (https://www.biomatik.com) and applied as described previously (50). 

Root Growth Analysis. To measure primary root length, seedlings were grown for the indicated times and then photographed with a digital camera (Nikon D3400). Primary root length was measured based on the images using ImageJ (https://rsbweb.nih.gov/ij/). For the time course of root elongation, seedlings were first grown vertically on MS agar medium for $5 ~ 0$ and then transferred to medium with or without ACC $( 1 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ and grown vertically for four more days. Arabidopsis plates were imaged using a version of a time-lapse robotic phenotyping system as described (51). Briefly, the imaging robot was constructed inside a reach-in growth chamber set to $2 2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ under 7,000 lx LED illumination. The robot was controlled by an Arduino microcontroller driving a single axle gantry system powered by a stepper motor, driving a mounted Flea3 camera. The robot was capable of imaging six plates simultaneously. Imaging of each plate was performed every 30 min. 

To determine LR density, the total number of emerged LRs was divided by the primary root length for each seedling (LR/cm). In addition, we estimated the LR initiation events on a cellular basis by quantifying the total number of both LRP and emerged LRs corresponding to 100 cortical cells in a file, thereby normalizing for differences in root cell length (52). 

Confocal Microscopy Imaging. Roots of 7-d-old light-grown seedlings were stained with $1 0 ~ \mu \ j / \mu \nu \updownarrow$ PI for $3 0 \ s ,$ and then mounted on slides in water for imaging under a confocal microscope (Nikon, C2 Si) with a 20X objective using an excitation wavelength of 561 nm and emission wavelength of 575 to 615 nm. We visualized the cells in the differentiation zone as described using calcofluor white (CFW) staining (53). Specifically, we fixed 7-d-old seedlings in $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde (PFA) at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $2 \hslash ,$ , washed twice with 1X PBS, then incubated in ClearSee solution overnight. The samples were then stained with $0 . 1 \%$ CFW (dissolved in ClearSee) for 30 min and imaged with an excitation wavelength of 405 nm and an emission wavelength of 425 to 475 nm. To image YFP or GFP fusions in transgenic lines, we observed the samples using confocal microscopy with an excitation wavelength of 488 nm and emission wavelength of 520 to 560 nm. To quantify the fluorescence intensity, each single channel (GFP or YFP) was converted to 8-bit format, and a fixed threshold was set to delineate the fluorescent region (WOX5:120-255, SCR:70-255, SHR: 40-255, PLT1:60-255, and PLT2:50-255). For WOX5, we specifically selected the QC position as the Region of Interest for quantifying the fluorescence intensity. The gray value of the selected region was measured as integrated intensity by ImageJ. 

EdU Incorporation Assay. EdU staining was performed by using the Yefluor 488 EdU Assay kit (Yeasen 40278ES25). Six-day-old seedlings were incubated in 10 mM EdU (dissolved in 1X MS liquid medium) for 45 min and then washed twice with washing buffer $3 \%$ BSA in PBS). Next, the samples were fixed in $4 \%$ PFA in PBS for 15 min in the dark and then washed twice with washing buffer and then immersed in $0 . 5 \%$ Triton X-100 in PBS for 30 min for permeabilization. The 

samples were then washed twice with washing buffer and finally incubated in Click-iT EdU reaction mixture solution (provided with the kit) (54) for 30 min in the dark and analyzed using confocal microscopy with an excitation wavelength of 495 nm and emission wavelength at 520 nm. The fluorescence intensity, indicating cell proliferation activity, was quantified by ImageJ software. After converting the images to an 8-bit format, a fixed threshold (45 to 255) was set to outline the fluorescent region, of which the gray value was measured as integrated intensity. 

Modified Pseudo-Schiff PI (mPS-PI) Staining. A mPS-PI staining method was used to visualize starch granules in the columella as described (55) with slight modifications. Seven-day-old seedlings were fixed in a solution of $5 0 \%$ methanol and $1 0 \%$ acetic acid a $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ overnight and then washed with water. Subsequently, the samples were incubated in $1 \%$ periodic acid at room temperature for $4 0 \mathsf { m i n } ,$ followed by another water wash. Then the samples were incubated in Schiff’s solution with PI $1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ sodium metabisulfite and 0.15 N HCl with PI freshly added to a final concentration of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ for 2 h. Afterward, the samples were cleared using a chloral hydrate solution $( 4 9$ chloral hydrate, 1 mL glycerol, and 2 mL water) overnight at room temp. Finally, the samples were transferred to slides, mounted in Hoyer’s solution (30 g gum arabic, $2 0 0 { \mathfrak { g } }$ chloral hydrate $2 0 { \mathfrak { g } }$ glycerol, and 50 mL water), and imaged using confocal microscopy (Nikon, C2 Si).

The samples were incubated in GUS staining solution (5 mM potassium ferrocyanide, 5 mM potassium ferricyanide, 50 mM NaPO , pH 7.0, 1 mg/mL 5-bromo-4-chloro -3-indolyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-glucuronic acid) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in the dark for 45 min. for proLBD18:GUS and overnight for the proDR5:GUS and proCLEs:GUS reporter lines. The roots were visualized using DIC microscopy (Nikon Eclipse 80i). 

RT-qPCR Analysis. For transcriptomics, 5-d-old ein2-5 seedlings grown on 1X MS agar were transferred to a hydroponic growth setup as described (56). The magenta were boxes half-filled with 1X MS liquid medium with and without ACC. Root tissue was harvested and snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. To study the time-based expression of specific up- and down-regulated genes, ein2-5 seeds were grown vertically on 1X MS agar plates for 6 to 7 d and then transferred to 1 MS agar plates with and without ACC for 3, 6, 12, 24, 36, and 48 h. Roots were harvested after each time interval along with no ACC controls and snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was isolated using RNAZol RT as described by the manufacturer (Sigma). Total RNA (500 ng) was used for cDNA synthesis using the Protoscript II cDNA synthesis kit (New England Biolabs). Real-time reverse transcription PCR was performed using the Applied Biosystems QuantStudio 6 Flex system and the PowerUp SYBR Green Master Mix (Life Technologies). Relative mRNA values were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C I } }$ method (57) with GAPDH (At1g16300) as an internal reference gene. The primers used are listed in SI Appendix, Table S1. 

RNA-Seq Analysis. Total RNA samples with three biological replicates were analyzed by BGI Genomics (San Jose, CA). The cDNA library was generated (Eukaryotic Strand-specific mRNA) and sequenced (paired-end reads of $2 0 0 { \mathsf { b } } { \mathsf { p } } ,$ , 30 M clean reads per sample) using the DNA NanoBall Technology (DNB-Seq™). After sequencing, raw reads were filtered to reach a Phed $+ 3 3$ FASTQ quality score. DEGs were identified using DESeq2 analysis tool on Partek® Flow® data analysis tool, software access provided by UNC Center for Bioinformatics. Initially, raw sequence data FASTA files were uploaded on Partek and then a pipeline was designed to align the raw reads to TAIR 10 genome using STAR aligner (58) which were then processed to fetch DEGs in ACC-treated samples with respect to Control. Significant DEGs were filtered based on P-value $\le 0 . 0 0 1$ 1, $\mathsf { F D R } = 0 . 0 5 ;$ ${ \mathsf { I } } 0 { \mathsf { g } } _ { 2 }$ foldchange $\geq 1 . 6 0$ analysis was performed using the AmiGO2 PANTHER overrepresentation tool with binomial test and false discovery rate (FDR) correction (59). 

Ethylene and ACC Measurements in the Absence of Plants. Sealed 5-mL vials containing $5 0 0 \mu \updownarrow 1 \times$ MS liquid or agar medium supplemented with 10 $\mu \mathsf { M A C C }$ (without any plants) were incubated under plant growth conditions (16 h light/8 h dark) a $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 10 d. Ethylene concentration in the vial headspace was assayed at different time points (0, 5, and 10 d) by gas chromatography (Shimadzu, GC-2014C). The ACC level in the vials was measured using the Lizada and Yang method (60), with a 5-min reaction using $2 0 \mu \updownarrow 2 5 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { H g C l } _ { 2 }$ and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } 5 \%$ NaClO–NaOH (2:1), followed by measuring the resulting ethylene (produced by chemical conversion of ACC) using a gas chromatograph (Shimadzu, GC-2014C).

Box plots shown in the manuscript were generated by the online tool BoxPlotR (61) (https://shiny. chemgrid.org/boxplotr/). All other graphs were created using Prism (8.0.1, GraphPad).
