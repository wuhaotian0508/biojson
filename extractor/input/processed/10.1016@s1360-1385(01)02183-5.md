# Sucrose to starch: a transition in molecular plant physiology

Alisdair R. Fernie, Lothar Willmitzer and Richard N. Trethewey

The major flux in potato tuber carbon metabolism is the conversion of sucrose through hexose phosphates to starch. The enzymes that mediate this pathway are well characterized, the genes that encode them have been cloned and transgenic plants have been generated. These transgenic studies have confirmed hypotheses based on more indirect methods, but they have also generated new challenges by highlighting the enormous flexibility and complexity inherent in plant metabolism. The investigation of the sucrose-to-starch transition in potato tubers is an excellent example of how the discipline of molecular plant physiology is evolving at both the scientific and technical levels.

Since the advent and widespread adoption of transgenesis approaches 15 years ago, giving rise to the discipline of molecular plant physiology, much information has been obtained about the regulation of plant metabolism. In the case of the potato (Solanum tuberosum) tuber, nearly all the genes believed to be directly involved in the sucrose-to-starch transition have been cloned, with much progress being made in the past couple of years [1–3]. An impressive variety of transgenic lines have been generated in which the activities of most of the individual genes have been modulated, alone or in combination [4–8]. In addition, a large range of transgenic potato lines has been created in which deregulated alternatives to endogenous enzymes have been introduced. These studies have confirmed many longstanding hypotheses that were previously based upon indirect methods.

However, some crucial questions, such as the precise steps that are important for potato tuber yield or the source of the pyrophosphate necessary for sucrose mobilization, have only been partially answered. Experimentation in the emerging science of molecular plant physiology has highlighted the enormous flexibility and complexity inherent in plant metabolism. The need to perform sophisticated physiological, cell biological and metabolic analyses to understand a transgenic event completely has driven the significant progress in the development of new methods. Of particular note has been the development and application of metabolic profiling protocols using gas chromatography coupled to mass spectrometry. In this article, we summarize these developments with a focus on recent findings, and highlight some older scientific questions that remain to be addressed by new technologies.

Supply of sucrose to the cytosol of potato tubers The pathway of phloem unloading in the tuber has been the subject of much debate. Two possible mechanisms are in contention: apoplastic unloading,

in which solutes are transferred via a cell-wallmediated step, and symplastic unloading, in which solutes are transferred between cells via cytosolic strands (plasmodesmata). It has been clear for some time that there are plasmodesmatal connections between the phloem and the surrounding parenchyma cells in the tuber [9], and that plasmolysis of growing tubers has an inhibitory effect on the flux of sucrose into the tuber [10]. This suggests that unloading occurs via a symplastic mechanism. However, isolated tuber discs display a substantial capacity to take up sucrose supplied to the surrounding medium [11,12]. Furthermore, transgenic production of a yeast-derived invertase in the apoplast under the control of the tuber-specific B33-patatin promoter significantly altered tuber yield [4], indicating the importance of apoplastic sucrose. A recent study using a combination of confocal microscopy, autoradiography and biochemical analyses has provided definitive evidence that unloading in the potato tuber is predominantly apoplastic during stolon elongation, and becomes primarily symplastic during the initial phases of tuberization [13].

Given that sucrose unloading is essentially symplastic in the developing tuber, the effect on tuber morphology of a heterologous invertase in the apoplast at this developmental stage [4,14] is intriguing. However, in spite of the large morphological changes, apoplastic production of invertase has no effect on the levels of cellular metabolites [14] and the role of sugars in the apoplastic space remains unknown. Interestingly, when the rate of glucose consumption in these transgenic lines is increased by the cytosolic production of a bacterial glucokinase, the total hexose content of the tuber is reduced, implying that apoplastic hexose is somehow able to enter cytosolic metabolism [5]. Comparison of transgenic plants exhibiting apoplastic expression with those exhibiting cytosolic expression of the invertase reveals that a completely different phenotype is produced depending upon the compartment to which the enzyme is targeted. This observation, together with results from biochemical studies, suggests that the route of entry of hexoses into metabolism differs according to whether they are generated in the cytosol or in the apoplast. Production of hexose phosphates in the cytosol The circular pathway operating between sucrose and hexose phosphates is well established and has been intensively investigated (Fig. 1). During the past decade, a battery of transgenic plants expressing sense or antisense constructs targeted against most of the genes involved in this pathway have been generated, and proteins specifically directed at many of the metabolic intermediates have been ectopically expressed. Moreover, during the past two years, the genes for hexokinases [3,17], cytosolic [2] and plastidial [1] phosphoglucomutase, and sucrose phosphate phosphatase [18] have been cloned, suggesting that the jigsaw puzzle of genes is nearing completion.

Sucrose delivered to the tuber can be cleaved in three ways: by invertase in the apoplast or by either invertase or sucrose synthase in the cytosol. The primary route of sucrose cleavage mirrors the mechanism of unloading, with invertase activities being high during the early stages of tuber initiation, whereas sucrose synthase dominates in the developing tuber [19]. The products of sucrose

cleavage enter into metabolism by the concerted action of fructokinase and UDP–glucose pyrophosphorylase (Box 1) or of fructokinase and hexokinases [3,17] in the sucrose synthase and invertase pathways [8], respectively. The hexose phosphates produced by these reactions are equilibrated by the action of cytosolic isoforms of phosphoglucose isomerase and phosphoglucomutase [2]. Transgenic studies have underlined the relative importance of these routes for sucrose cleavage in the developing tuber: potato plants with repressed hexokinase [3,17] and invertase activities [8] exhibit little difference from the wild type, whereas those repressed in sucrose synthase look dramatically different [7].

Although the net flux in the tuber is towards sucrose degradation, the rate of sucrose (re)synthesis within this tissue is considerable [20–22]. This process can also proceed via two different pathways: the reverse of the sucrose synthase degradative pathway or the reactions catalysed by sucrose phosphate synthase and sucrose phosphate phosphatase [21]. There is clear evidence from feeding experiments with labelled sugars that both pathways contribute to sucrose (re)synthesis within the developing tuber [21]. It is thought that the combined operation of these pathways with the degradative pathway allow the cell to respond sensitively to both variations in sucrose supply and the cellular demand for carbon for biosynthetic processes [22]. The importance of sucrose synthase was shown by antisense repression of this enzyme, which resulted in a reduced starch content coupled to a reduced tuber dry weight and a reduction in storage proteins. Furthermore, when sucrose synthase is bypassed by the introduction of either a yeast invertase [4,23] or a bacterial sucrose phosphorylase [24], there are similar dramatic repercussions on metabolism.

By contrast, sucrose synthesis via sucrose phosphate synthase is relatively well defined [22]. Although the reaction catalysed by sucrose phosphate synthase is not irreversible, the efficient removal of its product by sucrose phosphate phosphatase means that sucrose phosphate synthase is the first committed reaction in the sucrose synthesis pathway. In photosynthetic tissue, sucrose phosphate synthase is regulated at a variety of levels, including allosteric activation by glucose 6-phosphate and inhibition by inorganic phosphate (which allows sucrose synthesis to proceed at times when substrate is plentiful), and deactivated by protein phosphorylation. There is a growing body of correlative evidence that the potato tuber enzyme is regulated in an analogous manner to the leaf enzyme [21,25,26]. The complex regulation of this enzyme suggests that it plays an important metabolic role, a view that is supported by antisense studies, which reveal only a minor influence on starch metabolism but a major role in sucrose synthesis in response to water stress [27]. # Box 1. Pyrophosphate

Pyrophosphate is an essential cofactor in starch-storing tubers because, after the developmental shift from invertase- to sucrose-synthase-dependent sucrose cleavage, sucrose mobilization includes the reaction catalysed by the pyrophosphate-dependent UDP–glucose pyrophosphorylase [a]. Because one molecule of pyrophosphate is required for each sucrose cleaved by the sucrose-synthase-dependent pathway and most of the incoming sucrose is converted to starch, there are only two possible sources for the necessary pyrophosphate to fuel this reaction. It is either recycled across the amyloplast membrane from the starch-biosynthesis pathway or it is produced by a cycling process involving pyrophosphate–fructose-6-phosphate 1-phosphotransferase (PFP) or the tonoplast pyrophosphatase [b].

Correlative evidence for pyrophosphate recycled across the amyloplast membrane includes the observation that pyrophosphatase activity does not increase during the developmental switch from elongating stolon to growing tuber [c], the description of a pyrophosphate transporter in chloroplasts [d] and measurements of pyrophosphate contents and the rates of sucrose degradation and starch synthesis in a range of transgenic lines exhibiting altered levels of pyrophosphate [b]. Furthermore, analysis of metabolite levels in transgenic lines exhibiting reduced levels of PFP suggest that this enzyme is operating in the glycolytic direction and thus consuming pyrophosphate in vivo [e], and therefore is incapable of supporting sucrose degradation. Moreover, there was no difference in the rate of sucrose degradation (or resynthesis) in heterotrophic transgenic tobacco cells exhibiting increased levels of fructose 2,6-bisphosphate and therefore elevated in vivo activity of PFP [f]. Taken together, these data indicate that it is unlikely that PFP supplies pyrophosphate for sucrose degradation, and the pyrophosphate level might provide an important link between the catabolic reactions of the cytosol and the anabolic reactions of the plastid.

Direct evidence to support this model is unfortunately lacking. When pyrophosphate levels were depressed by the expression of a bacterial pyrophosphatase, initial experiments revealed an inhibition of sucrose breakdown and a reduction in

starch accumulation [g], whereas subsequent experimentation carried out with plants at a different developmental stage showed the opposite [h]. A further discrepancy in the behaviour of plants exhibiting low levels of pyrophosphate is that they have been variously reported to be characterized by accelerated [i] and delayed [j] sprouting. However, different, independent transgenic lines were used in these two studies and it is possible that different pyrophosphate levels trigger different effects. In spite of these discrepancies, the combination of results to date suggest that pyrophosphate is capable of affecting a variety of processes and therefore has a central, if somewhat enigmatic, role in the regulation of the sucrose-to-starch transition in potato tubers.

# Uptake of carbon into amyloplasts

The form in which carbon crosses the amyloplast membrane and enters starch biosynthesis has been the subject of considerable debate. Categorical evidence

that carbon enters the potato amyloplast in the form of hexose monophosphates (or nucleosides) rather than triose phosphates was provided by determining the degree of randomization of radiolabel in glucose units isolated from starch following incubation of potato tuber discs with glucose labelled at the C1 or C6 positions [31]. These data are in agreement with the observation that potato tubers lack plastidial fructose 1,6-bisphosphatase activity [32].

There has been considerable debate as to whether it is glucose 1-phosphate [33] or glucose 6-phosphate [34] that is taken up into the plastid. The cloning of a hexose monophosphate transporter from potato and the finding that the cauliflower homologue is highly specific for glucose 6-phosphate provides strong support for the glucose 6-phosphate theory [35]. In light of recent findings of extraplastidial isoforms of ADP–glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase) [36], we should not overlook the possibility that ADP–glucose is produced by a cytosolic enzyme and subsequently transported into the plastid to supplement starch synthesis. However, this seems unlikely because, after nonaqueous fractionation of potato tuber tissue, AGPase activity always localizes with pyrophosphatase activity, which is known to be located exclusively in the plastid [37]. In addition, production of a bacterial AGPase in the cytosol of potato tubers does not result in an altered starch content [38].

# Synthesis of starch

Following uptake of carbon into the tuber amyloplast, starch synthesis proceeds via the concerted action of the plastidial isoform of phosphoglucomutase [1,39], AGPase and the polymerization reactions that are catalysed by starch synthases and branching enzymes (Fig. 2) [40]. The genes for all these reactions are well known and many transgenic lines have been generated that are characterized by a reduction in one or more of the corresponding enzymes [1,40–42]. These approaches have been the subject of excellent recent reviews [41,42], so we concentrate here on the control resident in the various enzymes constituting this pathway. Recent studies of 14-3-3 proteins within starch granules of Arabidopsis chloroplasts [43] and of an ADP–glucose pyrophosphatase from barley leaves [44] indicate that enzymes other than those classically considered to participate in the starch synthesis pathway might also contribute to this process. However, given that there is currently no

evidence for a physiological role for either of these proteins within the potato tuber, we restrict discussion here to the classical pathway.

Most attention to this pathway has been focused on AGPase, which is activated by 3-phosphoglycerate (3PGA) and inhibited by inorganic phosphate in vitro. Furthermore, changes in 3PGA concentration correlate with changes in the rate of starch synthesis under a wide range of conditions [22,45] and plants exhibiting a dramatic reduction in AGPase activity also have a significantly reduced starch content [46]. These plants have been used to estimate flux control coefficients for starch synthesis of between 0.30 and 0.55 for AGPase [46]. In addition, when the activity of the amyloplastidial ATP translocator was genetically manipulated, the control coefficient of this transporter was estimated to be 0.78 [47]. This translocator provides ATP, one of the substrates of AGPase, to the plastid, which is further support for a pivotal role of AGPase in the starch biosynthesis pathway. Further evidence for the importance of nucleotides has come from the demonstration that manipulation of uridine nucleotide levels following the supply of precursor molecules to incubating tuber discs stimulates the rates of starch synthesis [48]. However, it is probable that these changes result from a moderate stimulation of sucrose degradation and it remains to be seen whether similar results are obtained following genetic manipulation of uridine nucleotide metabolism.

Although the later polymerization reactions of starch synthesis do not appear to play a role in the control of starch accumulation, they are crucial in determining the structure of starch [40–42].

# What are the prospects for engineering a more efficient tuber?

In tandem with the study of the regulation of the endogenous pathway of potato tuber carbohydrate metabolism, there has been considerable interest in increasing the efficiency of the sucrose-to-starch transition and thereby increasing tuber starch accumulation. Both conventional plant breeding [49] and genetic manipulation [4,23,24,38] strategies have been adopted. Conventional breeding strategies have been dominated by quantitative trait analyses [49], whereas transgenic approaches have focused primarily on the modulation of sucrose metabolism [4,23,24] or on the manipulation of the plastidial starch synthesis pathway [38,47]. To date, the most successful approaches have been those targeted at the plastidially localized reactions with plants overproducing either an unregulated bacterial AGPase [38] or the Arabidopsis amyloplastidial ATP–ADP translocator [47], both of which led to a marked increase (up to $1 5 0 \%$ ) in starch accumulation. However, when the unregulated bacterial AGPase was overexpressed in a second potato cultivar, no increase in starch accumulation was observed, indicating that these results might be highly context dependent [50].

# Box 2. Do sugars regulate their own metabolism?

Because attempts to increase the yield of starch by manipulating cytosolic metabolism have not been successful, attention has focused on the potential role of sugars as regulatory signals [a,b]. Driven by compelling evidence from yeast, the primary candidate for a signal is glucose, acting via sensing mechanisms involving either hexokinase or SNF1. In potato tubers, the expression of invertase in the cytosol results in an accumulation of glucose and a resultant shift in partitioning from starch toward glycolysis [c]. A similar metabolic shift was observed following the incubation of wild-type tuber discs in high glucose concentrations [d]. However, the expression of a bacterial sucrose phosphorylase and the supertransformation of invertase-expressing tubers with a bacterial glucokinase [c,e] displayed essentially the same metabolic phenotype, even though they were not characterized by an increased glucose concentration. Furthermore, modulation of the activities of either isoform of potato tuber hexokinase had no major effect on either tuber morphology or tuber metabolism [f,g], militating strongly against a role for glucose-dependent signalling processes in the tuber.

That said, the subject of sugar sensing remains highly controversial and there is reasonable evidence that sugar carriers or other factors at the plasma membrane might play a role in the regulation of tuber metabolism [a,h–j]. First, potato tubers expressing invertase in the apoplast are characterized by an increased rate of cell division and display a different metabolism to those expressing the invertase at a cytosolic location [h], even though the hexoses released at both sites are able to enter metabolism [i]. Second, the rates of sucrose degradation and starch synthesis are stimulated when the unmetabolizable sucrose analogue palatinose is supplied to isolated tuber discs, even though the uptake of palatinose into the tuber parenchyma

is negligible [j]. Although these studies enable us to speculate that these responses are mediated by a factor(s) localized in the plasma membrane [a], much work will be needed using a variety of approaches before the precise nature and role of sugar sensing in the potato tuber can be defined.

Attempts have also been made to improve starch yield by producing a more efficient pathway of sucrose degradation, consisting of either a yeast invertase and a bacterial glucokinase [4,23] or a bacterial sucrose phosphorylase [24]. Even though, in both approaches, the transgenic tubers exhibited higher levels of hexose phosphates and 3PGA than wild-type tubers, these attempts led to reduced starch accumulation. This is intriguing because hexose phosphates and 3PGA are the immediate precursors and the activator of the AGPase reaction, respectively. Detailed biochemical analyses of these tubers using standard spectrophotometric and infrared gas analysis techniques revealed that both transgenics were characterized by an induction of glycolysis and a massive partitioning of carbon into respiration. The reason for this dramatic change in partitioning remains mysterious but it probably relates to a signal that is generated as a consequence of the changes in cytosolic metabolism (Box 2). These studies highlight that, in spite of our structural understanding of the reactions that mediate the sucrose-to-starch transition, we lack a fundamental understanding of the complex interactions that occur in the regulation of this pathway.

What are the emerging technologies for analysing tuber physiology?

Although the cloning of the genes involved in the sucrose-to-starch transition and the generation of transgenic plants with altered enzyme activities have confirmed many hypotheses based on correlative data, they have also highlighted the inherent complexity of metabolic regulation. To advance our understanding of this pathway, we will need to enhance the quality of experimental data by gaining higher spatial and temporal resolution. Furthermore, given the poor success rate of transgenesis-driven metabolic engineering strategies, we need to enhance metabolic analyses to encompass as many metabolites as possible to understand the coordination of interlocking pathways.

Within the past few years, the first genomic potato analyses have been published. These are based on cDNA–AFLP (amplified fragment length polymorphism) strategies and have focused on tuber development rather than metabolism [51]. There are now ${ > } 4 8 0 0 0$ sequences from potato and ~133 000 sequences from the highly homologous tomato available in GenBank, so wide expression profiling in tubers, including the use of microarrays, is likely to become prevalent in the near future.

A new method has been developed that is based on gas chromatography linked to mass spectrometry, which enables the qualitative and quantitative multi parallel detection of metabolites within a single methanol extract [52,53]. This method allows detection of ${ > } 1 5 0$ tuber metabolites including sugars, sugar alcohols, dimeric and trimeric saccharides, amines, amino acids and organic acids. It is also rapid and robust, with recoveries of metabolites similar to those of standard spectrophotometric analyses. Furthermore, when this metabolic profiling method is applied in tandem with data-mining tools, such as hierarchical cluster analysis and principal component analysis, it is possible to phenotype potato tubers on the basis of their metabolic complements and also to identify metabolic phenocopies rapidly [54,55].

Improvements in spatial awareness of metabolism at the subcellular and single cell levels have also been achieved recently [6,37,56]. The first of these approaches was the targeting of sucrose-metabolizing proteins to the plastid, which has provided conclusive proof of the occurrence of sucrose in the plastid. The recent optimization of non-aqueous fractionation techniques for the quantification of compartmented metabolite levels in the potato tuber and its subsequent coupling to the gas chromatography and mass spectrometry protocol introduced above have led to unprecedented data about subcellular pool sizes in the wild-type tuber [37]. Recent advances in single cell

analyses have allowed the identification and sampling of a wide variety of cell types and the evaluation of local patterns of gene expression and of protein and solute abundance [56]. When all of these data types are available for transgenic lines, it is likely to permit a greater understanding of tuber metabolism and to provide the necessary insights to enable a more rational design of metabolic engineering strategies.

# Conclusion

Molecular plant physiology has emerged as a powerful new scientific discipline that has used and driven the development of modern techniques in molecular biology and biochemistry. Carbon metabolism in tubers has been subject to particularly intense exploration, not least given the commercial importance of this crop. In many cases, hypotheses about individual steps in the sucrose-to-starch transition have been confirmed by molecular approaches. However, transgenic experiments have also revealed how little we understand the complex regulatory mechanisms that govern the operation of a relatively straightforward metabolic network. Further success in deepening our understanding of the biology of tuber metabolism will require systems approaches that both provide a wide breadth of experimental measurement (e.g. RNA, proteins, enzyme activities and metabolites) and offer spatial and temporal resolution.

# Complex regulation of ABA biosynthesis in plants

Mitsunori Seo and Tomokazu Koshiba

Abscisic acid (ABA) is a plant hormone that plays important roles during many phases of the plant life cycle, including seed development and dormancy, and in plant responses to various environmental stresses. Because many of these physiological processes are correlated with endogenous ABA levels, the regulation of ABA biosynthesis is a key element facilitating the elucidation of these physiological characteristics. Recent studies on the identification of genes encoding enzymes involved in ABA biosynthesis have revealed details of the main ABA biosynthetic pathway. At the same time, the presence of gene families and their respective organ-specific expression are indicative of the complex mechanisms governing the regulation of ABA biosynthesis in response to plant organ and/or environmental conditions. There have been recent advances in the study of ABA biosynthesis and new insights into the regulation of ABA biosynthesis in relation to physiological phenomena.

Abscisic acid (ABA) is a plant hormone involved in various physiological processes of plants [1]. In developing seeds, ABA is necessary for inducing the synthesis of reserve proteins and lipids as well as for the onset of seed dormancy and the acquisition

of desiccation tolerance. Endogenous ABA levels peak during seed maturation and the onset of primary dormancy. ABA also plays important roles in vegetative development in response to various environmental stresses such as drought and highsalinity conditions. Moreover, ABA is known to control the expression of many genes related to these phenomena. Thus, an understanding of the mechanisms underlying the regulation of plant ABA levels is a crucial part of determining ABA action in plant growth and physiological responses, which is correlated to endogenous ABA levels. Because the rates of synthesis and the rates of breakdown determine ABA levels in situ, both mechanisms should be studied in detail, although the rate of breakdown remains largely unknown [2].
