# Abstract

An unusual $\beta \mathrm { . }$ -amylase7 (BAM7) gene in some angiosperms, including grasses such as maize (Zea mays), appears to encode 2 functionally distinct proteins: a nuclear-localized transcription factor (BAM7) and a plastid-localized starch hydrolase (BAM2). In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), these 2 proteins are encoded by separate genes on different chromosomes but their physiological functions are not well established. Using the maize BAM7 gene as a model, we detected 2 populations of transcripts by $5 ^ { \prime }$ - RACE which encode the predicted proteins. The 2 transcripts are apparently synthesized independently using separate core promoters about 1 kb apart, the second of which is located in the first intron of the full-length gene. The N-terminus of the shorter protein, ZmBAM7-S, begins near the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end of the first intron of ZmBAM7-L and starts with a predicted chloroplast transit peptide. We previously showed that ZmBAM7-S is catalytically active with properties like those of AtBAM2. Here, we report that ZmBAM7-S targets green fluorescent protein to plastids. The transcript encoding the longer protein, ZmBAM7-L, encodes an additional DNA-binding domain containing a functional nuclear localization signal. This putative dual-function gene originated at least 400 Mya, prior to the emergence of ferns, and has persisted in some angiosperms that lack a separate BAM2 gene. It appears to have been duplicated and subfunctionalized in at least 4 lineages of land plants, resulting in 2 genes resembling Arabidopsis BAM2 and BAM7. Targeting of 2 products from a single gene to different subcellular locations is not uncommon in plants, but it is unusual when they are predicted to serve completely different functions in the 2 locations. 

# Introduction

Gene fusion and duplication followed by subfunctionalization have contributed to the remarkable diversification of eukaryotic genomes (Orgel 1977; Force et al. 1999; Jones and Begun 2005). Evidence for these processes can be gleaned by examining homologous genes in phylogenetically diverse taxa, but like the relative rarity of transitional fossils that are useful for understanding organismal evolution, transitional genes that shed light on gene evolution are also rare. In the course of our investigation of the $\beta$ -amylase (BAM) 

gene family in plants, we noticed an unusual gene in some angiosperms that, based on their sequences, we hypothesized to encode 2 functionally distinct proteins, one resembling a nuclear-localized transcription factor and the other resembling a plastid-localized enzyme. We suspected that this putative dual-function gene could be transitional in the evolution of 2 structurally and functionally different BAM genes currently found in most angiosperms. 

The $\beta$ -amylase gene family in flowering plants comprises about 10 unique members (Monroe and Storm 2018; Thalmann et al. 2019). In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), 

in which the BAM genes are best characterized, the family includes 4 plastid-localized starch hydrolases capable of generating maltose from the nonreducing ends of $\mathfrak { a }$ -1,4-linked glucans: AtBAM1, -2, -3, and -6 (Lao et al. 1999; Fulton et al. 2008; Monroe et al. 2017), an additional catalytically active cytosolic enzyme of unknown function: AtBAM5 (Monroe and Preiss 1990; Wang et al. 1995; Laby et al. 2001), 2 catalytically inactive, plastid-localized proteins that appear to regulate starch metabolism: AtBAM4 and -9 (Fulton et al. 2008; Li et al. 2009; David et al. 2022), and 2 catalytically inactive, nuclear-localized transcription factors: AtBAM7 and -8 (Reinhold et al. 2011; Soyk et al. 2014). Some angiosperms excluding Arabidopsis also encode a different presumably catalytically inactive, plastid-localized protein referred to as BAM10 (Thalmann et al. 2019). Using conservation of intron positions and phylogenetic analysis of BAM genes in charophyte algae and land plants, we proposed that there were 2 ancestral BAM genes in the first land plants, one gene that was ancestral to BAM1, -3 and later BAM9, and the other gene that is most like BAM2 and later gave rise to all of the other BAMs including BAM7 (Monroe et al. 2017; Monroe and Storm 2018). 

Compared with the other catalytically active BAMs, AtBAM2 is unique in that it is tetrameric with a secondary starch-binding site in a groove between pairs of subunits (Monroe et al. 2017, 2018; Chandrasekharan et al. 2020). AtBAM2 activity is enhanced when starch is bound in this groove, and the enzyme exhibits sigmoidal kinetics. Other catalytically active BAMs are typically monomeric with hyperbolic kinetics (Monroe and Preiss 1990; Monroe et al. 2017; Chandrasekharan et al. 2020). Why AtBAM2 has these unusual properties is currently unknown; a mutant lacking AtBAM2 has no apparent phenotype (Fulton et al. 2008; Monroe 2020). The Arabidopsis BAM with the highest amino acid sequence identity to BAM2 is BAM7, which, in addition to the BAM domain, also contains an N-terminal BRASSINAZOLE-RESISTANT1 (BZR1)-like DNA-binding domain that binds to BZR1-BAM Responsive Elements (BBRE) and, with potential heterodimerization with BAM8, regulates the expression of other genes (Reinhold et al. 2011; Soyk et al. 2014). Given that starch is only found in plastids, it is not surprising that the BAM domain of this nuclear-localized protein has accumulated mutations that render it catalytically inactive (Reinhold et al. 2011; Soyk et al. 2014). 

Unlike BAM2, which is found in all lineages of land plants (Monroe et al. 2017), BAM7 is found only in pteridophytes, gymnosperms, and angiosperms, suggesting that BAM7 likely originated about 400 Mya by the fusion of a BZR1-like gene to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end of BAM2 prior to the origin of ferns (Thalmann et al. 2019). In some angiosperms, however, we identified a type of BAM7 gene that has all of the residues necessary for a functional BAM7 and a catalytically active BAM2 (Ravenburg et al. 2022). Importantly, these genomes lack a canonical BAM2 gene. The best studied example of this unusual gene is from maize (Zea mays) named BRI1-ETHYLMETHANESULFONE-SUPRESSOR/ 

BRASSINAZOLE-RESISTANT1-4 (ZmBES/BZR1-4). This gene was shown to encode a nuclear-targeted protein expressed throughout maize tissues that was transcriptionally induced by abscisic acid treatment (Yu et al. 2018). Its sequence suggests it is orthologous to Arabidopsis BAM7, but its function as a transcription factor has yet to be tested. Suspecting that the BAM domain of ZmBES/BZR1-4 might have catalytic properties like that of Arabidopsis BAM2, we expressed just the BAM domain in Escherichia coli and found that it was tetrameric and catalytically active with sigmoidal kinetics, like AtBAM2 and unlike all other catalytically active BAM enzymes (Ravenburg et al. 2022). We hypothesized that this gene has a cryptic chloroplast transit peptide (cTP) starting in the first intron and that it might encode 2 different proteins starting from alternative transcriptional start sites (Ravenburg et al. 2022). 

Here, we describe the structure of ZmBES/BZR1-4 (hereafter referred to as ZmBAM7), provide evidence that it indeed generates 2 different transcripts, and characterize the cellular localization of the 2 predicted gene products. We also report evidence for at least 4 independent duplication events of the ancestral BAM7 gene: in pteridophytes, gymnosperms, and twice in angiosperms, in which duplication and subfunctionalization led to the formation of separate BAM7 and BAM2 genes, the latter of which resembles the ancestral form. 

# Results

Using BLASTp, we identified BAM2 and BAM7 sequences in 61 diverse species of Viridiplantae ranging from charophyte algae to eudicots, especially those with well-annotated RefSeq entries (see accession numbers in Supplemental Table S1). We then identified the “ERDF” motif marking the most N-terminal conserved sequence in the BAM domain in all BAM2 sequences (Monroe et al. 2018) and divided the BAM7 sequences into the BZR1 and BAM domains. Trees were then generated from amino acid sequence alignments using just the BZR1 domains of BAM7 sequences or BAM domains of BAM7 and BAM2 sequences (Supplemental Figs. S1 and S2). The tree generated from the BZR1 domains closely resembles the evolutionary tree of land plants, suggesting that there was likely a single BAM7 origin and that the function of the BZR1 domains in BAM7 has not changed substantially since that origin $\sim 4 0 0$ Mya (Supplemental Fig. S1). 

In contrast to the BZR1 tree, the tree generated from an alignment of just the BAM domains of these proteins suggests a more complex evolutionary history. In the pteridophyte, gymnosperm, and angiosperm clades, the BAM domains of their BAM2 (yellow or orange shading) and BAM7 sequences (blue or dark blue shading) are more closely related to each other than they are to BAM domains in the other taxa represented in the tree. This suggests that the BAM2 and BAM7 sequences in these clades diverged from each other after the separation of these taxa from their ancestors. Importantly, all of the BAM domains in BAM7 

proteins from angiosperms that also contain a BAM2 gene (blue shading of eudicot BAM7 sequences) appear to be more divergent from all other BAM domains, suggesting that they may have diverged functionally. Also, BAM domains of BAM7 proteins in angiosperms that lack a BAM2 gene (green shading) are more closely related to the BAM2 sequences (yellow shading of eudicot BAM2 sequences, Supplemental Fig. S2). We refer to these green-shaded sequences as “dual-function BAM7” (DF-BAM7) genes. 

We then examined individual residues in angiosperm BAM7 and BAM2 sequences that are known to have specific functions in BAM7 and BAM2. For this analysis, we used 15 genomes that contain a BAM7 gene but lack a BAM2 gene (DF-BAM7 genes), and 14 genomes that contain both BAM2 and BAM7 genes (Fig. 1; Supplemental Table S2). Within the BZR1 domain of BAM7 and DF-BAM7 sequences is a putative nuclear localization signal (NSL) identical or nearly identical to that of Arabidopsis BAM7 (Reinhold et al. 2011) and confirmed in the full-length maize BAM7 (ZmBAM7-L) (Yu et al. 2018). All BAM7 and DF-BAM7 sequences also contained a glutamic residue (E83) identified through mutagenesis as critical for DNA binding (Soyk et al. 2014). Turning to the BAM domain, 15 residues in the active site of soybean BAM5 that bind starch (Laederach et al. 1999) are nearly all perfectly conserved in BAM2 and in the DF-BAM7 sequences (Fig. 1). In contrast, only one of the canonical BAM7 sequences examined had perfect conservation of these residues in the active site, suggesting these proteins have not been selected for catalytic activity. Residues identified as critical for starch binding to the AtBAM2 secondary binding site (SBS) and in the subunit interfaces involved in tetramerization (Monroe et al. 2017, 2018) were also nearly perfectly conserved in BAM2 and in DF-BAM7 genes (Fig. 1; Ravenburg et al. 2022). Altogether, conserved residues in the DF-BAM7 sequences known to be important for both BAM7 and BAM2 function suggest that these genes might encode both proteins, a longer DF-BAM7-L that is nuclear localized and functional as BAM7 and a shorter DF-BAM7-S that we hypothesize might function as a plastid-localized BAM2, assuming that it contains an N-terminal cTP. To test this hypothesis, we examined the maize BAM7 gene (NP_001337631; GRMZ M2G446515). 

The maize BAM7 gene contains 10 exons, the first of which contains the entire BZR1-like DNA-binding domain with its predicted NLS, and encodes a protein of $7 5 . 7 \mathrm { k D a }$ (Fig. 2A). The remaining 9 exons encode the BAM domain, probably derived from an ancestral BAM2 gene, that would have had an N-terminal cTP. However, after fusion with the BZR1 gene, this cTP would not have been functional in a BAM7-like protein because of its internal location (Bruce 2000). Interestingly, most of the DF-BAM7 genes contain an in-frame AUG start codon just upstream from the start of Exon 2 (Fig. 2A). In one exception, there is a AUG codon 5 codons into Exon 2. Each of the predicted shorter proteins starting with the Met residue in Intron 1 or Exon 2 is 

predicted to be chloroplast-targeted by at least 2 of the 3 programs used, Localizer (Sperschneider et al. 2017), TargetP (Armenteros et al. 2019), and principal component logistic regression (PCLR) (Schein 2001) (Fig. 2A). For a transcript to be used for translating DF-BAM7-S, its $5 ^ { \prime }$ end is likely within the first intron, and thus, we predicted that the DF-BAM7 genes should contain 2 transcriptional start sites (Fig. 2B). 

To determine whether the maize BAM7 gene yields 2 different transcripts differing in length by about 500 bp at their $5 ^ { \prime }$ ends and encoding the predicted ZmBAM7-L and ZmBAM7-S proteins, we conducted $5 ^ { \prime }$ -RACE experiments. RNA was extracted from growing maize leaf tissue and used for cDNA synthesis. After trying several gene-specific primers (GSPs) that should bind to both predicted gene products, one that was located in Exon 3 successfully generated several populations of products in the expected 400 to 900 bp range (Fig. 2C). We are unable to explain why the patterns of bands after 25 and 35 rounds of amplification were not identical despite being derived from the same sample of cDNA. Four regions of DNA products (A to D in Fig. 2C) from the 2 PCR runs were purified, cloned, and analyzed for the presence of DNA derived from ZmBAM7. Clones from regions B and C contained sequences from other genes and were discarded. Sequences of all 8 clones from region A matched the sequence of predicted ZmBAM7-L, and sequences of all 15 clones from region D matched the sequence of predicted ZmBAM7-S (Fig. 2B; Supplemental Table S3). The $5 ^ { \prime }$ ends of each set of clones were somewhat variable, as expected (Mejía-Guerra et al. 2015), but the 2 populations of transcripts shared no $5 ^ { \prime }$ sequence in common, which would be expected if they were derived from alternative splicing. In support of these observations, the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database contains 2 maize ESTs that align with the predicted ZmBAM7-L transcript and 5 maize ESTs that align with the predicted ZmBAM7-S transcript (Fig. 2B). In addition, 2 full-length maize cDNAs: NM_001350702.1 and EU956905.1, also match the expected sequence of ZmBAM7-L and -S, respectively. 

Insight into whether the ZmBAM7 gene yields 1 or 2 transcripts can also be gleaned from RNA-Seq data sets maintained by NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gene) by monitoring the ratio of intron-spanning reads between Intron 1 and Intron 2 for a given BAM7 gene. If the gene yields a single transcript, the ratio should be approximately 1:1 because both introns should be spanned equally in each fulllength transcript, but if a shorter transcript is also produced from the gene, the ratio of intron-spanning reads across Intron 1 should be less than those spanning Intron 2 if intron 2 is $3 ^ { \prime }$ to an exon used in both transcripts. Analysis of 25 canonical BAM7 genes indicated that the median ratio of intron-spanning RNA-Seq reads between Intron 1 and Intron 2 was about 1 (Fig. 3; Supplemental Table S4). However, the same ratio from 12 DF-BAM7 genes from which these numbers could be obtained was significantly less than 1 $( P \mathrm { ~ < ~ } 0 . 0 0 1 ) ,$ ). Yu et al. (2018) used transient expression of a full-length ZmBAM7 fused to GFP in onion (Allium cepa) cells to show that this protein was targeted to nuclei. To confirm that the nuclear localization signal (NLS) for ZmBAM7-L is in the same DNA-binding domain as it is in AtBAM7, and to determine if ZmBAM7-S is targeted to chloroplasts, we generated fusion proteins of just the DNA-binding domain of ZmBAM7-L, which includes the putative NLS, and the full-length predicted ZmBAM7-S including its putative cTP fused to GFP in the vector pEZT-NL and used them to generate stable transgenic Arabidopsis plants through Agrobacterium transformation. Protoplasts from these plants were then examined using fluorescence microscopy. Wild type (WT) protoplasts contained very low fluorescence in 

the GFP channel, likely from chlorophyll autofluorescence (Fig. 4A). As expected, protoplasts expressing the DNA-binding domain of ZmBAM7-L fused to GFP contained GFP fluorescence that co-localized with Hoechst 33342 stain, indicating that the protein was located in nuclei. This result was observed in several independent transgenic lines. In contrast, protoplasts expressing full-length ZmBAM7-S fused to GFP contained a strong GFP signal that co-localized with chlorophyll, indicating the protein was located in chloroplasts (Fig. 4A). Similar results were observed in several independent transgenic lines. Closer examination of these chloroplasts revealed several punctate GFP signals from each chloroplast indicative of association of ZmBAM7-S or GFP with a subchloroplast structure, possibly starch (Fig. 4B). However, Arabidopsis PROTEIN TARGETING TO STARCH 2 (PTST2) was also seen associated with subchloroplast structures, even in a starchless mutant (Seung et al. 2017), so the identity of the subchloroplast structure is unknown. 

To investigate the evolution of the dual-function BAM7 gene, we mapped the presence of canonical BAM2 and BAM7 genes, and/or DF-BAM7 genes from the 61 diverse species of Viridiplantae aligned above (Supplemental Figs. S1 and S2) onto a phylogenic tree generated using timetree.org (Kumar et al. 2017) (Fig. 5; Supplemental Table S1). A BAM2-like gene was found in the charophyte alga Klebsormidium flaccidum and in the earliest land plants, whereas the first evidence of a BAM7-like gene was found in ferns, suggesting that the fusion event joining an ancestral BZR1 gene to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end of a BAM2-like gene occurred at least 400 Mya. The 15 species containing putative DF-BAM7 genes comprise several basal angiosperms, basal eudicots, and some monocots (Fig. 5). Duplication and subfunctionalization of the original BAM7 gene likely occurred in 4 independent lineages including pteridophytes, gymnosperms, some monocots, and some eudicots. In most eudicot genomes, the BAM2 gene is immediately upstream from the BAM7 gene, suggesting that a tandem duplication likely occurred prior to the separation of asterids and rosids (Fig. 5). In 3 taxa of eudicots: Solanaceae, Cucurbitales, and Fabales, the BAM2 gene has apparently been lost, and in 2 orders, Lamiales and some members of the Brassicales, including Arabidopsis, the 2 genes now reside on separate chromosomes.

The 2 transcriptional start sites are approximately 1 kb apart, so it is unlikely that a single core promoter could drive the expression of both transcripts (Schmitz et al. 2022), or, because there was no overlapping $5 ^ { \prime }$ sequence between the 2 populations of transcripts, that the 2 populations of transcripts could be derived from alternative splicing. Using intron-spanning RNA-Seq data from 12 putative DF-BAM7 genes, we deduced that the shorter transcript from each gene was expressed on average about 5 times more strongly than the longer transcript (Fig. 3). Based on this observation, we can predict that in genomes with separate BAM2 and BAM7 genes, transcripts from BAM2 may be more abundant than those from BAM7 across all tissues. 

The longer ZmBAM7-L transcript encodes a protein with an N-terminal DNA-binding domain containing a putative bipartite NLS. A similar NLS was shown to target the AtBAM7 protein to Arabidopsis nuclei (Reinhold et al. 2011). Moreover, a fulllength ZmBAM7 protein was shown to be located in nuclei using transient expression in onion cells (Yu et al. 2018). We confirmed that the first exon of ZmBAM7-L contains an NLS 

by stable expression of a GFP fusion protein in Arabidopsis (Fig. 4A). Based on its sequence, domain structure, and localization, it is likely that ZmBAM7-L is a transcription factor similar to AtBAM7 (Reinhold et al. 2011; Soyk et al. 2014), but confirmation of this function awaits further experimentation. 

To support the hypothesis that the shorter ZmBAM7-S transcript encodes a BAM2-like protein, we previously expressed the protein in E. coli and showed that unlike the BAM domain of AtBAM7, ZmBAM7-S is catalytically active, albeit less so than AtBAM2, but like AtBAM2, the ZmBAM7-S protein was tetrameric and had sigmoidal kinetics (Ravenburg et al. 2022). Both of these properties are unique to BAM2 and different from other characterized catalytically-active BAMs (Monroe et al. 2017, 2018). Importantly, the ZmBAM7-S protein fused to GFP was targeted to chloroplasts where starch is located (Fig. 4A). Targeting of 2 products from a single gene to different subcellular locations is not uncommon in plants (Carrie and Whelan 2013). Moreover, alternative transcription start sites also seem to be common in plant genes generating proteins differing in their N-terminal sequences, and this can lead to proteins being targeted to different locations (Ushijima et al. 2017). By interrogating sequenced genomes from all of the major groups of land plants, we identified 15 species of angiosperms including economically important members of the Poales (maize, wheat, and rice), the basal angiosperms Amborella 

trichopoda and Nymphaea colorata, and well as 4 species of basal eudicots, including Aquilegia coerulea, Nelumbo nucifera, Telopea speciosissima, and Macadamia integrifolia, that contain a gene resembling the dual-function BAM7 gene in maize. Analysis of the presence and structure of BAM7 genes in land plant genomes allows us to draw several conclusions. First, because of the common domain structure and sequence conservation among all BAM7 genes, the fusion event between BAM2 and a BZR1-like gene likely occurred just once. Second, BAM7-like genes are not found in in the Charophyta, Bryophyta, and Lycopodiopsida. The ferns Azolla microphylla and Ceratopteris richardii contain BAM7 genes, so the fusion event happened prior to the divergence of pteridophytes about 400 Mya. 

Because of the presence of the dual-function BAM7 genes in several angiosperms, we can deduce that the genome in which the fusion event occurred must have contained just a single BAM2 gene. If that genome contained 2 BAM2 genes, one would have continued to be selected for BAM2 function and the other would have rapidly evolved into a BAM7-like transcription factor with properties of Arabidopsis BAM7, notably the lack of catalytic activity. All of the descendants of that original species would have contained only functionally unique BAM2 and BAM7 genes. In such a scenario, there would be no persistent dual-function BAM7 genes, suggesting that the original fusion event occurred in a genome with a single BAM2 gene. Interestingly, not all of the descendants from the fusion event retained the dual-function BAM7 gene. Separate BAM2 and BAM7 genes structurally resembling those in Arabidopsis occur in 4 different lineages: pteridophytes, gymnosperms, monocots, and eudicots (Thalmann et al. 2019). The most parsimonious explanation for this pattern is that duplication of the dual-function gene occurred independently at least 4 times followed by parallel subfunctionalization resulting in distinct BAM7 and BAM2 genes (Fig. 5). This hypothesis is also supported by the phylogeny of BAM domains described above (Supplemental Fig. S2). Examination of BAM2 and BAM7 sequences in these 4 clades could be valuable for testing hypotheses of evolutionary predictability (Lobkovsky and Koonin 2009). 

In 2 monocot genomes, Dendrobium catenatum and Asparagus officinalis, separate BAM2 and BAM7 genes occur on separate chromosomes. The presence of a dual-function BAM7 in 4 species of basal angiosperms, Aquilegia coerulea, Macleaya cordata, Nelumbo nucifera, and Macadamia integrifolia, suggests that a duplication event occurred in the Asparagales order after it split from the Poales (Fig. 5). Of the 34 genomes from the 2 major clades of angiosperms that we examined, rosids and asterids, $6 2 \%$ contain BAM2 immediately upstream from BAM7, with both genes encoded on the same DNA strand. Because the adjacent BAM2 and BAM7 genes are equally distributed among the 2 clades, they probably arose from a single tandem duplication event that occurred about 120 Mya. Among the descendants from this event are 2 lineages in which the 2 genes became separated to different chromosomes, the Brassicales, including 

Arabidopsis, and the Lamiales (Fig. 5), probably from differential gene loss after whole-genome duplication events that are known to have been widespread in angiosperms (Ren et al. 2018). Interestingly, in 3 different taxa including the Solanaceae, Cucurbitales, and Fabales, the BAM2 gene was apparently lost. Once a function for BAM2 is hypothesized, it will be interesting to test that hypothesis in these natural deletion mutants. 

Most of the BAM7 genes in the eudicot species with BAM2 genes contain mutations that should render the proteins catalytically inactive. However, because these mutations will continue to accumulate at different rates, some of these proteins may have lingering secondary functions longer than others. For example, BAM7 in Vitis vinifera contains all of the active site conserved residues identified as functioning in starch binding in an active BAM enzyme, whereas all of the others have accumulated mutations in some of these residues (Fig. 1). However, none of these BAM7 genes contain an in-frame AUG start codon and a predicted cTP starting near the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end of the first intron, so they are unlikely to also encode BAM2-like enzymes. 

Ohno (1970) described gene duplication as a primary source of new genes. However, immediately after the duplication, 1 of the 2 genes would be under no selective pressure and mutations rendering one of the duplicates nonfunctional would be far more frequent than mutations leading to a novel function (neofunctionalization), a conundrum referred to as Ohno’s dilemma (Bergthorsson et al. 2007). One resolution to this problem, called the Duplication-Degeneration-Complementation or DDC model, posits that when an ancestral gene with multiple functions becomes duplicated, different mutations in the daughter genes partition the ancestral functions between them so that both genes are retained (Hughes 1994; Force et al. 1999). The structural features of the dual-function BAM7 genes described here clearly fit the DCC model of gene evolution. A later model called Escape from Adaptive Conflict (EAC) states that after a multifunction gene is duplicated and subfunctionalized, the daughter genes are free to evolve toward optimization of their respective functions devoid of conflicting selective pressure for the other function (Hittinger and Carroll 2007). Comparing AtBAM2 with ZmBAM7-S revealed that the former is about 40-fold more efficient catalytically, supporting the EAC model (Ravenburg et al. 2022). The dual-function BAM7 genes appear to be an example of an ancestral, multifunctional gene that has undergone repeated duplication and subfunctionalization events leading to 2 genes with different functions. The DF-BAM7 genes are thus transitional relics in the evolution of BAM7 and BAM2. These genes should be valuable for rigorous testing of models for gene evolution. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant growth

Maize (Zea mays), ecotype B73, and Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), ecotype Columbia-0, were grown at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of illumination $\mathbf { \langle } 1 3 0 \ \mu \mathbf { m o l } \ \mathbf { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \leq ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) on a growth cart 

(Growers Supply Co., Dexter, MI, USA). Nutrients for maize growth were supplied within the potting mix (Miracle-Gro). The growth medium for Arabidopsis was ProMix BX (Premier Tech Horticulture, Quakertown, PA, USA) supplemented with macronutrients and micronutrients as described by Somerville and Ogren (1982). 

# Gene sequence analysis

A BLASTp search using the maize BAM7 protein sequence (NP_001337631; GRMZM2G446515) was conducted to identify similar genes in the NCBI RefSeq database (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/), the China National GeneBank (https://db.cngb.org/blast/blast/blastp/), the Klebsormidium nitens NIES-2285 genome project (http:// www.plantmorphogenesis.bio.titech.ac.jp/), the Genome Database for Marchantia polymorpha (https://marchantia. info/), and fernbase (https://fernbase.org). Sequences were aligned using Clustal Omega (Madeira et al. 2019) and were further analyzed manually. Subcellular location predictions were done using the programs: Localizer (https:// localizer.csiro.au/) (Sperschneider et al. 2017), TargetP (https://services.healthtech.dtu.dk/service.php?TargetP-2.0) (Armenteros et al. 2019), and PCLR (http://www. andrewschein.com/cgi-bin/pclr/pclr.cgi) (Schein 2001). A phylogenetic tree of selected land plants was generated using TimeTree (Kumar et al. 2017). RNA-Seq intron-spanning reads from selected genes were obtained from NCBI gene pages (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gene), and ratios were compared using a Wilcoxon rank–sum test. RNA-seq data on expression of ZmBAM7 was obtained from JBrowse (https://jbrowse.maizegdb.org/). 

# RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis, and $5 ^ { \prime }$ RACE

RNA was extracted from 6-wk-old maize leaf tissue using a Plant/Fungi Total RNA Purification Kit (Norgen Biotek Corp., Thorold, ON, Canada). RNA quality and concentration were assessed using a NANODROP 2000 Spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). cDNA was synthesized from the total RNA using a Takara Bio SMARTer $5 ^ { \prime }$ -RACE cDNA Synthesis kit (Takara Bio USA, Inc., Mountain View, CA, USA). $5 ^ { \prime }$ -RACE utilized the GSP: $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GATTACGCCAAGCTTATGACGTAGACCGGCACGTAG-GGCGTGC- $3 ^ { \prime }$ with cycling parameters $9 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, 5 cycles of $9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 0 \ s , 7 2 ^ { \circ } C$ for 2 min, then 5 cycles of $9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 0 \ s , 7 0 ^ { \circ } C$ for $3 0 \ s , 7 2 ^ { \circ } C$ for 2 min, then 25 or 35 cycles of $9 4 ^ { \circ }$ C for $3 0 \ s , 6 8 ^ { \circ } C$ for $3 0 \ s , 7 2 ^ { \circ } C$ for 2 min, then $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min. PCR products were extracted from agarose gels using the Macherey–Nagel NucleoSpin Gel and PCR Clean-up kit, and ligated to linearized pRACE vector using the In-Fusion kit and then transformed into Stellar Competent Cells (Takara). Resulting colonies were analyzed by restriction with EcoRI and HinDIII for inserts and BamHI/PstI and MluI/PstI for the identity of sequences derived from ZmBAM7. Plasmids were then sequenced using the M13F primer (Eurofins Scientific).
