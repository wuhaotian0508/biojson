# Summary

As a prominent external feature of watermelon, the stripe pattern exhibits remarkable phenotypic diversity, directly impacting commercial value through consumer preference. However, the genetic and molecular mechanisms underlying this important agronomic trait in watermelon remain poorly understood. In this study, we discovered that the total chlorophyll content in dark green stripes (DGS) was significantly higher than that in light green stripes (LGS) or reticular green stripes (RGS). Moreover, the number and size of chloroplasts were significantly increased in the DGS. Genetic analysis identified the KNOX TF ClSP as the most likely candidate for regulating watermelon dark green stripe formation, whose functional disruption substantially impaired chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development, converting dark green stripes into reticulate stripes. Through transcriptome analysis, we identified approximately 94 differently expressed genes (DEGs) that contain the KNOX TF binding cis-element ‘TGAC’ in their promoters. Among these genes, the expression pattern of ARABIDOPSIS PSEUDO RESPONSE REGULATOR 2-LIKE (APRR2-like) TF ClAPRR2 closely mirrored that of ClSP, displaying significantly down-regulated transcriptional expression in LGS compared to DGS. Utilizing Y1H, GUS activity, DLR and EMSA assays, we confirmed that ClSP activates the transcriptional activity of ClAPRR2 through promoter binding. Collectively, we propose a potential working model for the ClSP-ClAPRR2 module, which regulates the chlorophyll synthesis and chloroplast development in watermelon fruits, providing new insights into the mechanisms underlying stripe pattern formation. 

# Introduction

As an important indicator of fruit quality, the external appearance of watermelon (Citrullus lanatus L.) significantly influences consumer preference. The green rind adorned with dark green stripes is a recognizable morphological trait of watermelon, which has been depicted in Egyptian tomb murals and the Papyrus de Kamara (Vergauwen and De Smet, 2019). In nature, watermelon fruits exhibit diverse variations in background rind colour and foreground stripe pattern. Morphologically, stripes can be characterized by their margin (well-defined, medium and diffuse), width (very narrow to very broad), colour intensity (unicolored, bicoloured, marbled or only vein) and conspicuousness (inconspicuous to very strong) (Kim et al., 2015; Yue et al., 2021). In plants, the stripe phenotype is closely associated with the content and composition of pigments such as anthocyanin, carotenoid and chlorophyll. For example, the formations of red and green stripes in apple and pear are caused by different levels of anthocyanin (Qian et al., 2014; Telias et al., 2011). In contrast, the green stripes and green shoulder in tomato fleshy fruits are highly correlated with chlorophyll content (Liu et al., 2020b; Meng et al., 2018; Nadakuduti et al., 2014). 

Extensive research has established the pivotal roles of transcription factors (TFs) in regulating stripe formation on plant fruit surfaces. For example, the distinct green and red stripe patterns on apple and pear fruit peel are highly linked to the methylation levels within the MYB10 promoter region (Qian et al., 2014; Telias et al., 2011). Analogously, promoter methylation of the MADS-box TF TAGL1 epigenetically represses its expression, leading to the formation of green stripes in tomato (Liu et al., 2020b). In melon (Cucumis melo), the MYC2-like TF MELO3C003412 is considered as the most likely candidate responsible for the striped fruit phenotype (Liu et al., 2019). Chlorophyll accumulation and chloroplast development are orchestrated through complex interactions between environmental cues and transcriptional regulators. The knotted1-like homeobox (KNOX) TFs and BEL1-like homeodomain (BEL) proteins have increasingly been recognized for their roles in modulating these processes (Ezura et al., 2024; Keren-Keiserman et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b). In tomato, KNOX members SlTKN2/SlTKN4 and BEL-like factors SlBEL4/SlBEL5/SlBEL7/SlBEL11 exhibit fruit-specific regulation of chloroplast development (Meng et al., 2018; Nadakuduti et al., 2014). Mechanistically, KNOX-BEL protein complexes bind the conserved tandem ‘TGAC’ 

cis-element to coordinately regulate downstream targets (Ezura et al., 2022; Ezura et al., 2024; Hay and Tsiantis, 2010; Meng et al., 2018). For instance, the expression levels of chlorophyll biosynthesis-related genes, such as the protochlorophyllide reductase (POR), magnesium chelatase H subunit (CHLH) and chlorophyllide a oxygenase (CAO), as well as genes related to chloroplast development, such as those encoding chlorophyll a/b binding protein (CAB), are effected in KNOX or BEL-like TFs mutants (Ezura et al., 2024; Nadakuduti et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2020). Beyond KONX and BEL proteins, GOLDEN 2-LIKE (GLK2) and ARABIDOPSIS PSEUDO RESPONSE REGULATOR 2-LIKE (APRR2-like) TFs have emerged as critical regulators of chloroplast formation and chlorophyll accumulation (Ezura et al., 2024; Nadakuduti et al., 2014). Notably, the potential functions of APPR2 genes in peel pigmentation have been raised in many cucurbit crops, such as cucumber (C. sativus) (Liu et al., 2015), zucchini (Cucurbita pepo) (Gebretsadik et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2022), wax gourd (Benincasa hispida) (Ma et al., 2021; Nong et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2024), bottle gourd (Lagenaria siceraria) (Huo et al., 2023) and bitter gourd (Momordica charantia) (Guo et al., 2024). In watermelon, APRR2 expression quantitatively correlates with fruit rind pigmentation intensity, which is congruent with the predicted biological function in melon (Oren et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2024). However, the potential interaction between KNOX and APRR2 TFs in regulating stripe pattern remains to be systematically investigated. 

Forward genetic approaches have systematically advanced our understanding of genetic determinants controlling stripe pattern formation in watermelon. For instance, the locus S responsible for the foreground stripe pattern has been mapped on chromosome 6 in watermelon (Park et al., 2016). Additionally, a single dominant candidate gene associated with the wide-stripe trait has been delimited between markers W09-180.3 and W09-180 on chromosome 8 (Zhang et al., 2018). Genome-wide association studies further identified Cla97C06G126710, encoding a WD40-repeat protein, as the primary candidate regulating rind striation (Guo et al., 2019). Complementary investigations resolved two tightly linked genes (Cla97C09G175170 and Cla97C09G175150) on chromosome 9 that coordinately regulate stripe patterning and inter-stripe pigmentation (Maragal et al., 2022). Recently, the stripe pattern gene ClGS, a member of the MoRC family, was fine-mapped to a 107 Kb region on chromosome 6 (Wang et al., 2022a). The polygalacturonase-1 non-catalytic subunit beta gene Cla97C06G126560 has also been implicated as a candidate for regulating watermelon stripes (Liang et al., 2022). Intriguingly, promoter-region SNPs in the homeobox TF ClIS were shown to causally underlie interrupted stripe phenotype in watermelon (Wang et al., 2023). Despite successful identification of these valuable candidate genes for this typical agronomic trait, their functions in stripe development have not yet been verified. 

In our previous research, we successfully mapped the stripe-regulating gene ClSP (Citrullus lannatus Stripe Pattern) to chromosome 6 and pinpointed the KNOX TF No4_5 as the most promising candidate (Yue et al., 2021). In this study, the functional role of No4_5 (ClSP ) in watermelon dark green stripe formation has been convincingly validated through the CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing system. To the best of our knowledge, ClSP is the first well-characterized gene demonstrated to regulate watermelon stripe development. Furthermore, we predicted a series of potential downstream targets of ClSP and verified that 

ClAPRR2 is directly regulated by ClSP, thereby influencing the chlorophyll synthesis and chloroplast development. Ultimately, we proposed a potential model for the ClSP-ClAPRR2 module in watermelon stripe pattern formation. 

# Results

# The chloroplast number and chlorophyll content contribute to variations of stripe patterns

As previously reported (Yue et al., 2021), distinct stripe pattern phenotypes were observed in the watermelon germplasms ‘W006’ (striped fruit) and $' { \mathsf { C } } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ (reticular-striped fruit) at 8 DAP. Given that colour differences in plant organs are primarily attributed to cytochrome content, we meticulously sampled rind tissues from 8 DAP fruits of $' { \mathsf { C } } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ and $" { \mathsf { W } } 0 0 6 ^ { \prime }$ , categorized as RGS (reticular green stripe peel from line ‘C118’), DGS (dark green stripes from line ‘W006’), and LGS (light green stripes from line ‘W006’), respectively (Figure 1a). Quantitative pigment analysis revealed that significantly higher of chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b and total chlorophyll in DGS compared to both LGS and RGS tissues, with RGS showing intermediate chlorophyll content between DGS and LGS (Figure 1b). While carotenoid levels showed no significant difference between LGS and RGS, both contained significantly lower carotenoid content than DGS. 

To further investigate the morphological variations in different fruit peel stripes, we carefully collected RGS samples from line $' { \mathsf { C } } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ , as well as DGS and LGS peels from the striped line ‘W006’. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) observations revealed no discernible differences in chloroplast morphology among the three peel tissues (Figure 1c). However, the thylakoid thickness within chloroplasts in DGS was significantly greater than that in LGS and NGS (Figure 1d). Furthermore, DGS contained a higher number of chloroplasts compared to LGS and NGS (Figure 1e), despite slight variations in chloroplast size among the three tissues (Figure 1f). These findings suggest that the number of chloroplasts and the chlorophyll content may be the primary factors contributing to the differences in peel stripe morphology. 

# The best candidate for ClSP is a KNOX transcription factor

Genetic inheritance analysis established that the stripe phenotype in watermelon is governed by a single dominant gene ClSP (Yue et al., 2021). Through integrated sequence and expression profiling, we identified the transcription factor No4_5 as the best likely candidate, due to the hypothesis that loss-of-function mutation leads to reticular stripe formation while differential expression levels correlate with distinct DGS and LGS patterns (Yue et al., 2021). According to genome annotation, No4_5 is identified as a KNOX transcription factor containing three conserved motifs: MEINOX, ELK, and TALE-HD (Figure 2a). Subcellular localization assays confirmed the nuclear targeting of ClSP through GFP fluorescence (Figure 2b). 

To explore the dynamic expression pattern of No4_5 during the early developmental stages of stripe formation, we assessed its transcriptional levels in fruit peel at various time points after pollination (Figure 2c). # Mutagenesis of No4_5 converts dark green stripes to reticular stripes

To functionally validate No4_5, we designed two sgRNAs targeting its second and fourth exons (Figure 3a) and generated CRISPR/Cas9-mediated mutations in the striped germplasm ‘TC’ (wild-type, WT) exhibiting medium-width dark green stripes. Sequencing confirmed two heterozygous mutant lines (Clsp_1 and Clsp_2), both carrying frame-shift mutations: Clsp_1 contained 2-bp/4-bp deletions at target site 1 and 4-bp/5-bp deletions at target site 2, while Clsp_2 showed 1-bp/4-bp deletions at target site 1 and 6-bp/0-bp deletions at target site 2 (Figure 3a). Critically, all deletions (1/2/4-bp) at target site 1 

generated premature termination codons through frame-shifts (Figure S2), confirming effective gene disruption. 

Phenotypic observations showed no significant differences in ovaries before pollination due to fruit hairs (Figure S3). However, at 9 DAP, edited plants ${ C I S p \_ 1 }$ and Clsp_2 exhibited discernible stripe phenotype changes in fruit rinds compared to WT (Figure 3b). At 28 DAP, WT maintained medium-width dark green stripes, whereas Clsp_1 and Clsp_2 developed reticular stripes in dark green backgrounds (Figure 3b). Interestingly, we observed that fruits developing under shaded conditions exhibited a significantly lighter pericarp coloration compared to those exposed to sunlight (Figure 3b; Figure S3), indicating environmental modulation on the rind coloration. Consequently, we confirmed that the KNOX transcription factor No4_5 functions as the watermelon stripe regulatory gene ClSP.

# Natural variations in ClSP correlate with stripe pattern divergence

To investigate the role of ClSP in natural stripe pattern variation, we analysed re-sequencing data from 10 watermelon accessions: 5 striped (‘JX-2’, ‘JXF’, ‘RZ-901’, ‘Sugarlee’, and ‘PI482326’) and 5 reticulate-striped (‘XHBFGM’, ‘Calhoun Grey’, ‘RZ-900’, ‘PI249010’, and ‘PI595203’) (Figure S4A). Comparative analysis revealed complete CDS conservation of ClSP in all striped lines 

(including the reference genome accession ‘97103’), which consistently produce medium-width dark green stripes. Conversely, four conserved SNPs were identified in the CDS of all reticulate-striped accessions (Figure S4B), mirroring the mutations previously reported in netted-striped varieties $' { \mathsf { C } } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘N21’ (Yue et al., 2021). These variants were further validated in thirteen additional gemplasms (6 striped and 7 netted-striped accessions), showing strict correlation between ClSP haplotypes and pericarp patterning (Figure S5). Based on these findings, we postulate that ClSP likely serves as a key regulator for the stripe patterns observed in at least some, if not all, watermelon germplasms.

# ClSP activates the transcriptional expression of ClAPPR2

To verify the transcriptional regulation of ClAPRR2 by ClSP, we conducted a yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay (Figure 6a). All combinations grew normally on the SD/ Leu medium, indicating system viability. However, in the medium containing Aureobasidin A (AbA), the AD and proClAPRR2-pAbAi combination showed growth inhibition with increasing dilution, while ClSP and proClAPRR2-pAbAi maintained robust growth, demonstrating the binding capacity of ClSP to the ClAPRR2 promoter. Supporting this observation, the GUS assay also indicated a physical interaction between ClSP and the ClAPRR2 promoter, with notably higher GUS activity in 35S:ClSP cells compared to cells with the empty vector (Figure S8). Dual-luciferase reporter (DLR) assays further confirmed transcriptional activation (Figure 6b). Fluorescence imaging showed the ClSP with proClAPRR2 combination had a much stronger fluorescence signal than the negative control (Figure 6c). Enzyme activity assays demonstrated the LUC/REN value of ClSP/proClAPRR2 was significantly higher than that of the negative control, indicating ClSP promotes ClAPRR2 transcription (Figure 6d). # Discussion

The rind stripe pattern, a crucial foreground trait of watermelon fruits, significantly impacts consumer purchasing preferences (Kim et al., 2015; Park et al., 2016; Yue et al., 2021). To date, several candidate genes for stripe pattern formation have been identified, including the S locus (Park et al., 2016), ClGS (Wang et al., 2022a), and ClIS (Wang et al., 2023). In our prior study, the stripe pattern regulatory gene ClSP was mapped to chromosome 6, with the KNOX TF No4_5 identified as the most promising candidate (Yue et al., 2021). However, functional verification and molecular mechanisms of these genes remain largely unexplored. 

# ClSP regulates dark green stripe formation by increasing chloroplast number and chlorophyll accumulation

Variations in the peel colour mainly depend on anthocyanin, carotenoid and chlorophyll levels. For example, anthocyanin 

differences cause red and green stripes in apple and pear (Qian et al., 2014; Telias et al., 2011). In tomato, green shoulder and stripes are highly associated with the chlorophyll content (Liu et al., 2020b; Meng et al., 2018; Nadakuduti et al., 2014). In watermelon, dark green stripes and rind are closely linked to both chlorophyll content and chloroplast number. For instance, the size and number of chloroplasts were obviously greater in the striped line ‘WT2’ than in the non-striped line ‘WM204’ (Wang et al., 2022a). Similarly, the DGS peel has more chloroplasts and thicker thylakoids than the LGS tissue, with higher chlorophyll content (Figure 1), which is consistent with the pigment distribution in watermelon rind (Li et al., 2019). As a major pigment component, chlorophyll accumulates more in dark green peel than in yellowgreen- or white-skinned cucumber mutants (Hao et al., 2018; Jiao et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2015). Additionally, the pericarp pigment is also highly correlated with chlorophyll content and chloroplast number in bottle gourd and wax gourd (Huo et al., 2023; Nong et al., 2023). In summary, variations in chloroplast number, size, and chlorophyll content primarily account for the diverse phenotypes of green peel fruits.

# ClSP modulates chloroplast development through regulating ClAPRR2

KNOX genes play pivotal roles in diverse aspects of plant development and are phylogenetically classified into class I and II (Ezura et al., 2024; Hay and Tsiantis, 2009; Hay and Tsiantis, 2010). In Arabidopsis, class I includes four KNOX members: SHOOT MERISTEMLESS (STM ), BREVIPEDICELLUS (BP ), KNAT2, and KNAT6 (Hay and Tsiantis, 2009). STM and $B P$ are required for both vegetative and reproductive development, including the maintenance of shoot apical meristem (SAM), ovule morphogenesis, and the architecture of inflorescence and compound leaves (Ezura et al., 2022; Hay and Tsiantis, 2009; Kumar et al., 2007). For example, BP modulates inflorescence patterning by 

suppressing KNAT2 and KNAT6 transcription (Zhao et al., 2015), while the AS1-AS2 protein complex represses $B P$ and KNAT6 activity during leaf development (Hung et al., 2023). Although ClSP shares high sequence homology with KNAT2/- KNAT6 (Figure 2d), it uniquely regulates chloroplast development in watermelon rind (Figure 4). This functional divergence parallels observations in tomato, where four class I KNOX genes (SlTKN1- 4) exhibit distinct roles: SlTKN2 and SlTKN4 enhance chlorophyll accumulation in immature fruits via transcriptional activation of SlGLK2 and SlAPRR2-like (Ezura et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2018; Nadakuduti et al., 2014), while the class II KNOX gene SlKN5 influences chloroplast biogenesis and leaf complexity (Ezura et al., 2024). 

In plants, APRR2-like genes have been identified as key regulators of chlorophyll accumulation in fruits. For instance, loss-of-function mutations in CaAPRR2 reduce chlorophyll content in pepper fruits (Pan et al., 2013; Song et al., 2022), while a SNP deletion in SmAPRR2 converts eggplant peel colour from green to white (Fang et al., 2023). Similar mechanisms underlie chlorophyll regulation in cucumber (CsAPRR2) (Liu et al., 2015), where transposon-mediated activation of CmAPRR2 generates chlorophyll-rich ‘Shooting Star’ phenotypes on melon white rinds (Zhang et al., 2024). # A putative working model for ClSP-mediated stripe formation in watermelon rind

Based on the aforementioned analysis, we propose a working model to elucidate the regulatory role of ClSP in watermelon stripe formation (Figure 7). In germplasms with dark green stripes, ClSP displays significantly higher accumulation in dark green rind regions compared to adjacent light green areas (Yue et al., 2021). Given that ClSP can transcriptionally activate ClAPPR2, a gene associated with chloroplast development and chlorophyll accumulation, this differential accumulation further affects the distribution of ClAPRR2, ultimately resulting the regular alternation of dark and light green stripes on fruit rind. The loss of ClSP function disrupts dark green stripe formation, leading to a reticulate stripe phenotype (Figure 3b). The normal development of green rind in edited lines implies that ClAPRR2 expression is not completely inhibited and may be regulated by other unidentified proteins. Interestingly, ${ \mathsf { C } } I { \mathsf { S } } P$ mutants show no obvious change in leaf coloration, possibly due to 

distinct regulatory pathways governing chloroplast development in leaves versus fruits (Nadakuduti et al., 2014). Notably, while ClSP modulation alters foreground striping patterns, background rind coloration exhibits pronounced plasticity contingent upon environmental conditions. For instance, shade-grown fruits of gene-edited lines develop lighter rind pigmentation, whereas sun-exposed counterparts display significantly darker pigmentation (Figure S3). This phenotypic difference suggests the existence of an environmental-genetic interplay in rind colour determination, wherein extrinsic factors (e.g. light) may exert regulatory effects parallel to intrinsic genetic pathways. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials and growth conditions

As described in our previous research (Yue et al., 2021), the high generation inbred lines with dark green stripes and reticular stripes were designated as ‘W006’ and ‘C118’, respectively. All seedlings were grown in greenhouses at the farms of Northwest A&F University (Yangling, Shaaxi, China). Fruits were harvested at approximately 0, 1, 2, 4, and 8 days after pollination (DAP) for photograph and sample collection. Moreover, tissues including the root, stem, leaf, tendril, male flower, and female flower were sampled from the ‘W006’ genotype and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent tissue-specific analysis. 

# Identification of the best candidate for ClSP

Genetic inheritance analysis revealed that watermelon stripe patterning is governed by a single dominant locus, designated ClSP (Citrullus lanatus Stripe Pattern), through segregation studies in a $^ { \prime } \mathsf { W } 0 0 6 ^ { \prime } \times ^ { \prime } \mathsf { C } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population (Yue et al., 2021). Map-based cloning delimited ClSP to a 611.78-Kb interval on chromosome 6 containing eight candidate genes. Sequence analysis revealed that the third SNP in the CDS of candidate No4_5 resulted in a premature stop codon (TGA) instead of the 247th amino acid glutamine (Q) in $' \mathsf { C } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ . Moreover, the expression level of No4_5 in dark green stripes is significantly higher than that in light green stripes in ‘W006’. Taken together, we inferred that No4_5 is the best candidate for ClSP. The loss of function in No4_5 possibly resulted in the change from the dark green stripe phenotype to the reticular stripe pattern, while differential expression levels lead the formation of dark and light green stripes on the fruit peel. Detailed information of sequence and expression analysis can be found in our previously published research (Yue et al., 2021). 

# Pigment quantification and chloroplast ultrastructure analysis

Pigment extraction was performed on precisely dissected exocarp tissues from 8 DAP fruits: dark green stripes (DGS) and light green stripes (LGS) of ‘W006’, alongside reticular green stripes (RGS) of $' { \mathsf { C } } 1 1 8 ^ { \prime }$ . Following established protocols (Liu et al., 2020a; Yin et al., 2023), approximately 1 g tissue samples of DGS, LGS and RGS were independently extracted using a 1:1 mixture of acetone and alcohol. After complete extraction, absorbance spectra (470, 649, 665 nm) were measured using a UV-2800A spectrophotometer (UNICO, Shanghai, China) with three biological replicates per sample. Chlorophyll a/b and carotenoid concentrations were subsequently calculated based on the described method (Harmut, 1987). 

For transmission electron microscopy (TEM), stripe tissues from DGS, LGS, and RGS were independently fixed in $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) glutaraldehyde for $4 \mathfrak { h }$ , post-fixed with $1 \%$ osmium tetroxide and dehydrated through an ethanol series. White resin-embedded samples were sectioned and imaged using a transmission electron microscopy system (Hitachi, Japan). The number of chloroplasts was counted and compared across ten cells per sample. Additionally, the size of the chloroplasts, as well as the thylakoid thickness, was measured using the software Image J (Schneider et al., 2012). 

# Comparative sequence and phylogenetic analysis

Gene structures of ClSP and ClAPPR2 were annotated using the watermelon reference genome $^ { \prime } 9 7 1 0 3 ^ { \prime }$ (V2.0) (Yu et al., 2023). Re-sequencing data from 10 watermelon accessions (SRA052158, SRA052198 and SRA052519) (Guo et al., 2013), as well as four experimental lines (‘W006’, ‘M08’, ‘C118’, ‘N21’) (Yue et al., 2021) and 13 additional germplasms, were aligned to the reference genome using the BWA software (Li and Durbin, 2009). Sequence polymorphisms were visualized using the IGV software (Thorvaldsdottir et al., 2013). 

For phylogenetic analysis, closest homologues of ClSP and ClAPPR2 were identified in Arabidopsis and tomato genomes using BlastP. Multiple sequence alignments were performed using Muscle (Edgar, 2004), and phylogenetic trees were subsequently constructed using the neighbour-joining method in MEGA X with 1000 bootstrap replicates (https://www.megasoftware.net/). 

# RNA extraction and qRT-PCR analysis

For expression analysis, total RNA was extracted using the RNA Simple Total RNA Kit (DP432, Tiangen, China), followed by cDNA synthesis using the Fast King RT Kit (KR116, Tiangen, China). Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was conducted with SYBR Green PCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems®, Inc., Foster, USA) on a Step-One Plus RealTime PCR system (Applied Biosystems®). The housekeeping gene actin was used as an internal control for normalization (Yue et al., 2023). The relative transcriptional levels of mRNAs were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \intercal } }$ method (Yue et al., 2023). Gene-specific primers were listed in Table S1. 

# Subcellular localization analysis

The coding sequence (CDS) of ClSP and ClAPPR2 (excluding the stop codon) were individually amplified from the ‘W006’ line and subsequently inserted into the pGreenII 35S-GFP vector, respectively. A nuclear marker was created by fusing the 35S-driven AtH2B gene with the mCherry protein. Similarly, the 35S-driven gene AtCBL fusing with the mCherry protein was utilized as the 

plasma membrane marker. Subsequently, these constructs were transiently transformed into tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaves via Agrobacterium tumefaciens infiltration (Zhang et al., 2021). After a 48-h incubation period, the epidermis of the tobacco leaves was visualized using a Leica TCS-SP8 SR fluorescence microscope (Germany). Primers for vector construction are listed in Table S1. 

# Vector construction and watermelon transformation

For the knockout of ClSP, two target sequences were designed and selected using CRISPR-P V2.0 (Lei et al., 2014). The guide RNA sequences were inserted into the CRISPR/Cas9 vector pBSE402. Subsequently, the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105, harbouring the recombinant vector pBSE402-gRNA, was utilized for watermelon transformation (Zhang et al., 2021). The watermelon germplasm ‘TC’ with dark green stripes was served as the transgenic material (Feng et al., 2021). Genomic DNA was extracted from young leaves for editing detection. The individuals from ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ generation of stable transgenic lines were cultivated in greenhouses at the farms of Northwest A&F University (Yangling, Shaaxi, China). After pollination, phenotypes of watermelon fruits were continuously documented through photography. Fruits at 10 DAP from wild type (WT) and gene-editing lines were sampled for the comparative pigment quantification and chloroplast ultrastructure analysis. 

# Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays

The CDS of ClSP and the promoter sequence of ClAPPR2 were amplified the ‘W006’ line, which were subsequently inserted into the prey vector pGADT7 and bait vector pAbAi, respectively. The recombinant plasmids were then co-transformed into the Y1H Gold yeast strain and selected on SD/ Leu/-Ura/AbA media containing $1 2 5 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ aureobasidin A (AbA) at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Protein-DNA interactions were validated through three biological replicates. Primers for Y1H assay are listed in Table S1. 

# Histochemical GUS activity analysis

The CDS of ClSP and the promoter of ClAPPR2 were amplified from the $' \mathsf { W } 0 0 6 ^ { \prime }$ line and subsequently ligated into the effector vector pGreenII-35S-GFP and the reporter vector pBI121-GUS, respectively. These constructs were then introduced into A. tumefaciens (strain GV3101-pSoup) and subsequently cotransformed into tobacco leaves. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ incubation, leaves were subjected to vacuum-infiltrated with staining buffer containing $5 \sf { m m } \sf { K } _ { 3 } [ F e ( C N ) _ { 6 } ]$ , $5 \mathsf { m m K } _ { 4 } [ \mathsf { F e } ( \mathsf { C N } ) _ { 6 } ]$ , 1 mM 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-beta-D-glucuronide acid, 100 mM phosphate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H } ~ 7 . 0 \right)$ , and $1 0 \mathsf { m m }$ EDTA. Samples were incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $1 6 \mathsf { h }$ in darkness, then decolorized with $70 \%$ (v/v) ethanol. After rinsing in a series of ethanol solutions, the bleached leaves were prepared for photography. Primers for GUS assay are listed in Table S1. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter (DLR) assays

The CDS of ${ \mathsf { C } } I { \mathsf { S } } P$ was amplified from the ‘W006’ line and inserted into pGreenII 62-SK as the effector, while the ClAPPR2 promoter from the same line was cloned into pGreen II 0800-LUC as the reporter. Constructs were co-delivered into tobacco leaves via Agrobacterium infiltration (GV3101-pSoup, effector: reporte $= 9 : 1$ ). To verify the interaction between ClSP and the promoter of ClAPPR2, three independent experiments with three replicates were performed. The activities of Firefly luciferase (LUC) and Renilla luciferase (REN) were assayed by a Dual-Luciferase 

Reporter Gene Assay Kit (Yeasen, Shanghai, China) on an Infinite M200 Pro Microplate Reader (Tecan, Switzerland). Primers for vector construction are listed in Table S1. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The ClSP coding sequence from the ‘W006’ line was cloned into the vector $\mathsf { p E T } - 3 2 \mathsf { a } ( + )$ , to generate His-tag fusion proteins. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into Escherichia coli strain BL21 for protein expression. The promoter sequence of ClAPPR2 containing the targeted motifs was synthesized and labelled with biotin oligonucleotides. Additionally, unlabeled probes served as competitors, and mutant probes confirmed specificity. EMSAs were conducted using a Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (GS009, Beyotime, China) according to the manufacture’s protocols. Primers for vector construction are listed in Table S1. 

# Statistics analysis

Data was analysed using the software SPSS 25, followed by Duncan HSD’s multiple range tests at $P < 0 . 0 5$ or Student’s t-test at $P < 0 . 0 1$ .
