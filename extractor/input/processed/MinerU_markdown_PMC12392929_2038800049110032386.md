# Summary

Schizonepeta tenuifolia Briq. (S. tenuifolia) is a traditional Chinese medicinal plant widely used to treat colds, fever and respiratory diseases. Volatile oils including the monoterpenoids limonene, pulegone and menthone are major constituents of S. tenuifolia that determine its medicinal value and consumer popularity. A better understanding of the molecular regulatory mechanisms underlying terpenoid accumulation is needed to improve the quality and market value of S. tenuifolia. Here, we demonstrate that limonene and pulegone accumulate in S. tenuifolia under high-light conditions, whereas menthone accumulates in the dark. Transcriptome and proteome analyses revealed that light up-regulated both the transcriptional and protein levels of limonene synthase (LS), whereas darkness elevated pulegone reductase (PR) expression and protein abundance. An ${ \mathsf { N } } ^ { 6 }$ -methyladenosine $( \mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } )$ dot blot assay revealed that levels of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification are higher in the dark than in the light. Silencing of individual $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes resulted in higher levels of monoterpenoids such as menthone, pulegone and limonene in S. tenuifolia. We further determined that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modulates the content of monoterpenoids in S. tenuifolia by affecting the stability of transcripts encoding PR and LS. This study reveals a vital role for post-transcriptional regulation mediated by the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification in light intensity-dependent terpenoid biosynthesis in S. tenuifolia and provides a strategy for genetically improving the quality of this valuable plant. 

# Introduction

Schizonepeta tenuifolia Briq. (S. tenuifolia), an herbaceous plant belonging to the Lamiaceae family, is a well-known traditional Chinese medicinal herb that has been widely used for thousands of years. The aerial parts and spikes of this plant are commonly prescribed to treat colds, fevers and respiratory diseases. S. tenuifolia can also be eaten as a vegetable in salads or cooked dishes (Fung and Lau, 2002; Shan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024). The main chemical constituents in S. tenuifolia include volatile oils, flavonoids, polysaccharides and others. The main pharmacologically active components in its volatile oils are menthone, pulegone and limonene, which have multiple pharmacological effects such as anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, antiviral, analgesic and antitumor activities (Shan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024; Zhao and Zhou, 2022). 

Menthone, pulegone and limonene are biologically active monoterpenoid compounds (Liu et al., 2023; Shan et al., 2021). Their biosynthetic pathway begins with pyruvate and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P) as substrates, proceeding through the methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway located in plastids to produce the monoterpenoid precursor geranyl pyrophosphate (GPP). Subsequently, limonene is produced via catalysis by limonene synthase (LS). Pulegone is then produced 

via catalysis by limonene-3-hydroxylase (L3OH), isopiperitenol dehydrogenase (IPD), isopiperitenone reductase (IPR) and isopulegone isomerase (IPI). Finally, menthone is produced via pulegone reductase (PR) (Liu et al., 2023). 

Light is an environmental cue required for plant growth that also serves as an important signal to regulate the accumulation of plant secondary metabolites (Zhang et al., 2021). Although the transcription factor StWRKY1 is known to be involved in monoterpenoid biosynthesis in response to different light intensities in S. tenuifolia (Wang et al., 2024), the underlying post-transcriptional regulatory mechanisms remain unclear. 

${ \mathsf { N } } ^ { 6 }$ -methyladenosine $( \mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } )$ , one of the most prevalent internal post-transcriptional modifications of messenger RNAs (mRNAs) in most eukaryotes, widely regulates RNA metabolism, including the splicing, localization, stability and translation of mRNA (Frye et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2018). The deposition and decoding of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification involve three key components: writers, erasers and readers. In plants, the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification is deposited (or written) by methyltransferase complexes that include the components METHYLTRANSFERASE A (MTA), MTB, FKBP12 INTERACTING PROTEIN 37 (FIP37), VIRILIZER (VIR) and HAKAI (Bodi et al., 2012; Ruzicka et al., 2017; Shen et al., 2016; Zhong et al., 2008) and is dynamically and reversibly erased by the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ demethylases ALKBH10B and ALKBH9B (Duan et al., 2017; 

Martınez-Perez et al., 2017). Subsequently, the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification is recognized and decoded by readers such as EVOLUTIONARILY CONSERVED C-TERMINAL REGION 2 (ECT2), ECT3, ECT4 and CLEAVAGE AND POLYADENYLATION SPECIFICITY FACTOR 30 (CPSF30) (Arribas-Hernandez et al., 2018; Scutenaire et al., 2018; Song et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2018), which mediate downstream post-transcriptional regulation, thereby realizing the biological functions of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification. 

The $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification regulates numerous aspects of plant growth and development, including embryo development, flowering time and fruit ripening; it also participates in various stress responses (Zhou et al., 2022). Manipulating the methylation levels of mRNAs in plants is an important strategy for enhancing yield, quality and stress resistance; for instance, this approach has been used to improve the yield and biomass production of rice and potato and to enhance disease resistance in apple (Guo et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021). In recent years, the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification has been shown to affect the accumulation of metabolites (Hou et al., 2022; Su et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023). However, the role of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ in light intensity-dependent terpenoid biosynthesis and the underlying regulatory mechanisms are unclear. 

In this study, we determined that light intensity significantly influences the levels of major pharmacologically active monoterpenoids in S. tenuifolia and affects its overall $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification levels. We demonstrate that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ plays a crucial role in the biosynthesis of terpenoid compounds in this plant and that lower levels of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification lead to significantly higher accumulations of menthone, pulegone and limonene. Furthermore, we unveiled a mechanism by which light intensitydependent deposition of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification regulates the biosynthesis of terpenoid compounds in S. tenuifolia by modulating the post-transcriptional fate of transcripts encoding terpenoid biosynthesis enzymes. Our findings provide theoretical support for developing new strategies to enhance the accumulation of active ingredients in medicinal plants. 

# Results

# Light intensity regulates the growth and development of S. tenuifolia

To explore the influence of light intensity on S. tenuifolia growth and development, we subjected plants grown under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod and a light intensity of $1 4 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathrm { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \mathsf { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ to one of three treatments for 7 days. We transferred one group to constant darkness (Dark; $0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ and the second group to high light (HL; $1 0 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ , while the third group remained under the same growth light intensity (GL; $1 4 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ . We measured the height, fresh weight, chlorophyll content, carotenoid content and photosynthetic parameters of all plants in all three groups. Both dark and HL treatments affected the height of S. tenuifolia plants. Compared to GL conditions, the plants were shorter by $5 7 . 7 \%$ and $2 5 . 1 \%$ under dark and HL treatments, respectively (Figure 1a). The fresh weight (Figure 1b) of the aboveground parts of S. tenuifolia plants largely followed the same pattern. After dark treatment, the fresh weight of S. tenuifolia (mean $5 0 1 \ \mathrm { m g } ,$ ) was less than half that of GL plants (mean 1131 mg). However, the HL group (mean 1060 mg) showed no significant difference in fresh weight compared to the GL plants. Similarly, the chlorophyll a (Figure 1c), chlorophyll b (Figure 1d) and carotenoid contents 

(Figure 1e) were all lower under dark and HL treatments compared to GL conditions. 

We analysed the effects of different light intensities on the photosynthetic efficiency of S. tenuifolia using a chlorophyll fluorescence video imaging system. The photosynthetic efficiency of S. tenuifolia subjected to dark and HL treatments was lower than that under GL conditions, as evidenced by their lower $F _ { v } / F _ { m }$ values (Figure 1f, g). To assess ${ \mathsf { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ (superoxide anion) levels in S. tenuifolia plants exposed to each treatment, we stained their leaves with nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT), which forms a dark blue precipitate in the presence of ${ \mathsf { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ . We observed only minimal ${ \mathsf { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ production in the leaves of GL plants, with staining present only in the vasculature (Figure 1h–j). After 7 days of dark treatment, certain areas of S. tenuifolia leaves were stained blue (Figure 1h), while the leaves of HL-treated plants were by far the darkest, with the largest stained areas (Figure 1j). We conclude that light intensity affects the growth and development of S. tenuifolia plants, with constant darkness having a greater effect on biomass accumulation than HL treatment, although the latter results in more pronounced oxidative stress. 

# Light intensity affects the production of active compounds in S. tenuifolia

To evaluate the effects of light intensity on the content of pharmaceutically active compounds in S. tenuifolia, we subjected plants exposed to dark, HL or GL conditions to metabolite profiling via gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC–MS) (Figure S1). We observed higher limonene and pulegone contents in S. tenuifolia in a light intensity-dependent manner, with HL treatment significantly enhancing the accumulation of these monoterpenes. Indeed, compared to GL conditions, HL treatment elevated the limonene content by $1 7 0 \%$ and the pulegone content by $6 2 \%$ (Figure 2a–d). 

The pattern of menthone accumulation differed from that of limonene and pulegone (Figure 2e, f). Dark-treated plants accumulated approximately 40 times more menthone than GL and HL plants (Figure 2f). These findings shed light on the varied influences of light conditions on the accumulation of specific terpenoids in S. tenuifolia, emphasizing the intricate interplay between environmental cues and secondary metabolite production in this species.

# Combined proteome and transcriptome analyses uncover the light-mediated post-transcriptional regulation of the MBP

To expand our understanding of light-mediated changes in metabolite content in S. tenuifolia, we performed a proteome analysis of the same samples used for transcriptome profiling. In total, we identified 4367 proteins, including 3532 comparable proteins with quantifiable abundance (Figure S3a–c), and robustly quantified their abundance (relative standard deviation [RSD] $< 1 5 \%$ ; Figure S3d). Most differentially abundant proteins (DAPs) detected in the Dark vs GL and HL vs GL comparisons (Tables S5 and S6) were predicted to localize to chloroplasts, followed by the cytoplasm and nucleus (Figure S3e). Among all enriched GO terms in the Dark vs GL comparison, the up-regulated DAPs were significantly enriched in ‘fatty acid beta-oxidation’, whereas the down-regulated DAPs were significantly enriched in ‘mRNA metabolic process’ (Figure 4a). KEGG pathway enrichment analysis of up-regulated DAPs in the Dark vs GL comparison revealed significant enrichment of ‘photosynthesis-antenna proteins’ (Figure S4a), while this term was enrichment in the down-regulated DAPs in the HL vs GL comparison. We also performed a clustering analysis to identify similarities and differences in the functions of DAPs among the comparisons, and in summary, dark treatment significantly increased the abundance of proteins in photosynthesis-related pathways while simultaneously downregulating the abundance of proteins involved in mRNA metabolism in S. tenuifolia (Figure S4b). 

We focused on the abundance of proteins in both the MEP pathway and the MBP. In the Dark and GL groups, the abundances of most key proteins in the MEP pathway and MBP diminished following dark treatment (Figure 4b, c). Notably, the abundance of PR (Figure 4b, c; Dark vs GL) rose significantly under dark treatment, mirroring the elevated menthone content detected under this condition (Figure 2e, f). By contrast, LS abundance dropped significantly under dark treatment but was higher following HL treatment compared to that in GL samples (Figure 4b, c; HL vs GL), mirroring the increase in limonene content observed under HL treatments (Figure 2a, b). 

Comparative transcriptomic and proteomic analyses revealed partial concordance between the expression profiles of genes in the MEP pathway and MBP, as well as the abundance of their encoded proteins (Figure 4c). However, notable discrepancies emerged for specific transcripts (Figure S5a, b, Table S7), suggesting that post-transcriptional regulation such as RNA processing and/or modification may mediate metabolite accumulation in S. tenuifolia (Figure S4c). We therefore summarized all changes in the transcriptome and proteome for GO enrichment analysis using all DETs and DAPs. In the Dark vs GL comparison, the abundance of proteins associated with the GO term ‘mRNA metabolic process’ was down-regulated, while the levels of their encoding transcripts remained unchanged in the transcriptome (Figure S5c, Dark vs GL). Similarly, in the HL vs GL comparison, the abundance of proteins related to ‘monoterpene biosynthetic process’ was down-regulated but their corresponding transcript abundance was not significantly different between these two conditions (Figure S5c, HL vs GL). 

# Dark treatment up-regulated the overall $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification level in S. tenuifolia

We hypothesized that the discrepancy between the proteome and transcriptome data of genes associated with the pathways 

‘mRNA metabolic process’ and ‘monoterpene biosynthetic process’ resulted from $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ -mediated post-transcriptional regulation (Figure S5c), as $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ is the most abundant modification of mRNA (Sun et al., 2023). To investigate the possible relationship between $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification and light intensity, we measured the total $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels in the mRNA of S. tenuifolia plants under GL, HL and dark treatments. Total $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels were higher in the dark-treated plants, as evidenced by a dot blot assay (Figure 5a). Using liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS), we quantified the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels in mRNA. This analysis confirmed that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels were significantly higher (by about $20 \%$ ) in the Dark samples compared to the GL and HL samples (Figure 5b), suggesting that darkness induces the upregulation of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels in mRNA. 

To analyse the expression of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } .$ -related genes in S. tenuifolia under different light conditions, we looked for genes homologous to genes involved in $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification from Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) in the S. tenuifolia unigene dataset. The expression of these genes in S. tenuifolia was differentially regulated by varying light intensities (Figure 5c). In particular, the expression of genes encoding $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writers (Tang et al., 2023) such as MTA was positively associated with light intensity. By contrast, MTB, FIP37, HAKAI, ECT2 and VIR were expressed at significantly higher levels in the Dark samples compared to the GL and HL samples. 

To assess the involvement of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ in regulating the transcript abundances of genes from the MEP pathway and MBP, we performed $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ immunoprecipitation (IP) using fragmented polyA+ RNA, followed by RT-qPCR to examine the abundance of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ on the transcripts of key genes. For the MEP pathway, transcripts of genes encoding 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate reductoisomerase (DXR), 2-C-methyl-D-erythritol-2,4-cyclodipho sphate synthase (MDS) and isopentenyl-diphosphate delta isomerase (IDI) were predominantly enriched in the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ immunoprecipitates from the Dark samples compared to the GL and HL samples (Figure 5d). For the MBP, the transcripts of LS, which encodes the key enzyme in limonene biosynthesis, were markedly enriched in the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ immunoprecipitates of Dark samples (Figure 5d). However, limonene content, as well as LS transcript and LS protein abundances, rose with higher light intensity; however, these components were all present at low levels under dark treatment (Figure 2a, b; Figure 3f, h; Figure 4b; Figure S4c), suggesting that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels might negatively regulate LS transcript and LS protein abundance, as well as limonene production. Contrary to LS mRNA, the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ of PR mRNA was highly enriched in the GL and HL samples (Figure 5d). Nevertheless, menthone content, PR transcript levels and PR protein abundances were significantly higher in the Dark samples than in the GL or HL samples (Figures 2e, f, 3h and 4b), suggesting that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels might also negatively regulate the accumulation of these components. In summary, our findings suggest that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification potentially has a negative regulatory function in S. tenuifolia, notably affecting LS and PR expression (Figures 3f,h, 4b and 5d; Figure S4c).

# The $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification influences the biogenesis of pulegone reductase in S. tenuifolia

Since PR is the key enzyme catalysing the reduction of pulegone to menthone (Croteau et al., 2005), understanding its $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } -$ mediated post-transcriptional regulation is essential for enhancing menthone yields. Based on the results of RT-qPCR (Figure 5c) and the analysis of the contents of active compounds and the transcript levels of genes encoding key enzymes following the silencing of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes (Figure 6), we hypothesized that VIR may be one of critical factors affecting $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ enrichment, menthone accumulation and PR mRNA levels. To determine the effects of VIR on the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification of PR transcripts, we performed $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ -IP-qPCR assays. Silencing VIR markedly lowered the enrichment of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification on PR transcripts (Figure 7a), indicating that the depletion of VIR results in a loss of this modification. Since the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification can influence the stability of mRNAs in both mammals and plants, affecting their degradation and 

overall half-lives (Kramer et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2014), we measured the level of PR mRNA, as its accumulation was elevated after silencing individual $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes (Figure 6d). PR mRNA was degraded more rapidly in the EV control samples than in VIGS plants silenced for individual $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writers (Figure 7b), suggesting that higher levels of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification promote mRNA degradation. Moreover, the degradation mechanism of LS caused by the dysfunction of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes is similar to that of PR (Figure S6b). 

The $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification stabilizes mRNAs by affecting local ribonucleolytic cleavage (Anderson et al., 2018). We therefore investigated whether the lower mRNA abundance and accelerated degradation in the EV control were due to greater local cleavage. We analysed the predicted $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ sites in the PR transcript using deepSRAMP (Figure S7a) and identified two RRACH motifs in the centre of the coding sequence (Figure 7c, Figure S7b, c). We compared cleavage levels around these motifs between VIGS plants targeting individual $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writers and EV samples using RT-qPCR. The relative mRNA levels of PR in the pTRV2:VIR plants containing the cleavage site (RRACH motif) were $50 \%$ higher than those of PR mRNA in the EV plants (Figure 7c, d), suggesting that fewer PR mRNAs containing the RRACH motif are cleaved in pTRV2:VIR plants compared to those in the EV control. 

Finally, to further assess the roles of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification in protein accumulation, we constructed expression vectors for PR containing point mutations that replace adenine residues in the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ motifs with guanine (A-to-G transition mutations). We generated an $\mathsf { A } ^ { 8 2 6 }$ -to-G mutant that disrupts a selected $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ motif $( \mathsf { A G A } ^ { 8 2 6 } \mathsf { C A }$ to AGGCA) (Figure S7d). We transfected Arabidopsis protoplasts with equal amounts of plasmid expressing either intact or mutant PR from S. tenuifolia. Immunoblotting with anti-FLAG antibodies revealed that PR protein abundance was approximately $50 \%$ higher in protoplasts expressing the $\mathsf { A } ^ { 8 2 6 }$ -to-G mutant version of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ motif $\mathsf { A G A } ^ { 8 2 6 } \mathsf { C A }$ compared to those expressing the intact PR transcript (Figure 7e, f). 

In conclusion, we propose a model illustrated in Figure 7g, whereby MTA, HAKAI and VIR markedly influence menthone biosynthesis in S. tenuifolia in the dark, with the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification playing a crucial role in controlling the biogenesis of pulegone reductase.

# LS transcript levels and LS abundance are enhanced in the light, whereas PR transcript levels and PR abundance are enhanced in the dark

LS and PR play pivotal roles in menthone biosynthesis in S. tenuifolia. LS is responsible for the conversion of GPP into 

limonene, which is the first committed step in the MBP. The activity of this enzyme is crucial, as it sets the stage for subsequent transformations that lead to the formation of menthone. By contrast, PR catalyses the stereoselective reduction of pulegone to menthone, a key step that determines the stereochemistry of the final product (Liu et al., 2022). The stereoselectivity of this reduction step is essential, as it influences the biological activity of the resulting menthone isomers. The coordinated action of LS and PR is therefore critical for the efficient production of menthone in S. tenuifolia, highlighting their importance in the metabolic pathway that leads to the production of this valuable monoterpene. 

When performing RT-qPCR validation of the transcriptome of S. tenuifolia under varying light intensities (Figure 3g, h), we focused on changes in the expression of numerous genes in the MBP, as well as genes encoding enzymes in the MEP pathway, which is the source of the monoterpene precursors for the MBP. In the Dark vs GL comparison, we observed significant downregulation of all genes in the MEP, as well as significant downregulation of StLS and StL3OH in the MBP, whereas StIPD, StIPR and StPR were strongly up-regulated. These results suggest that these changes are key variables regulating the menthone 

content of S. tenuifolia in the dark. However, in the HL vs GL comparison (Figure 3g, h), the expression of genes encoding enzymes in both the MEP pathway and the MBP did not change as significantly as in the dark (we detected subtle pervasive fluctuations in the expression levels of genes associated with the MEP pathway, along with StLS and StL3OH ). However, the yields of active components, such as limonene and pulegone, rose substantially, indicating that high-light-induced regulation of active products in S. tenuifolia might be closely related to posttranscriptional modifications. 

Based on these findings, we conducted proteome profiling and discovered additional clues that support our hypothesis, particularly regarding the fewer differentially abundant proteins (DAPs) involved in RNA processing and modification in the HL vs GL comparison compared to the Dark vs GL comparison (Figure S4c). However, lower enrichment in modification-related biological processes is not directly linked to fewer modification sites. Therefore, more comprehensive analyses should be performed in future studies to elucidate the intricate interplay between the regulation of gene expression and cellular functions, particularly in the context of the two major types of RNA modifications (ranked by abundance in mRNAs) (Sun et al., 2023): $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ and pseudouridine $( \psi )$ . 

# $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ negatively regulates LS and PR expression posttranscriptionally via mRNA degradation specifically in the dark

The MEP pathway, limonene synthase and pulegone reductase are intricately linked in the biosynthesis of menthone in S. tenuifolia (Figure 3f), with post-transcriptional $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ enrichment playing an intricate role in regulating menthone production under varying light conditions (Figure 5). 

The MEP pathway in plastids (Perez-Gil et al., 2024) is responsible for the production of the isoprenoid precursors IPP and DMAPP (Figure 7g). These precursors are essential for the biosynthesis of monoterpenes, which are the primary constituents of essential oils in S. tenuifolia. DXR, a key enzyme in the MEP pathway, catalyses the conversion of DXP to MEP, a reaction that requires NADPH and a divalent metal cation, typically ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ (Kesharwani and Sundriyal, 2021). This step is crucial for the production of isoprenoids, which are the backbones of menthone biosynthesis. Notably, among all the MEP pathway genes investigated in our study, the enrichment of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification on DXR mRNA was particularly pronounced under dark treatment, suggesting that the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification has a strong selective effect on specific DXR transcript (Figure 5d). We also observed marked $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ enrichment on LS mRNA under dark conditions (Figure 5d). By silencing key genes involved in $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ deposition (MTA, HAKAI and VIR), we confirmed that high $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels can affect the stability of LS mRNA and promote its degradation (Figure S6b; Figure 7g). By contrast, $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ enrichment on PR mRNA was markedly lower than that on LS mRNA under dark treatment (Figure 5d), potentially diminishing the local ribonucleolytic cleavage of the mRNA. Indeed, the low $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification level of PR mRNA under dark treatment enhanced PR mRNA abundance (Figures 6d and 7g) and attenuated its degradation (Figure 7b,g), which in turn led to a higher conversion of pulegone to menthone. Unexpectedly, in the light, the relative mRNA levels of LS were consistently lower following the silencing of MTA, HAKAI or VIR (Figure 6h), suggesting that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ might positively regulate LS mRNA levels in the light by inhibiting local ribonucleolytic cleavage to stabilize mRNAs 

(Anderson et al., 2018). By contrast, PR mRNA levels showed no significant changes across the same samples (Figure 6d), suggesting that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ does not significantly regulate PR mRNA levels in the light. In summary, our findings reveal the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } \cdot$ mediated regulatory mechanisms of LS and PR expression in the dark, while the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ regulatory mechanisms in the light require further in-depth investigation. 

# Genetic engineering of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ -mediated menthone biosynthesis by controlling LS and PR biogenesis

The intricate interplay between environmental cues and posttranscriptional modifications, such as $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } ,$ represents a fascinating area of study (Zhang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022). The current findings underscore the dynamic nature of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ enrichment and its influence on the MBP, highlighting the potential for precisely controlling metabolism in response to light and darkness. This regulatory mechanism offers insights into the adaptability of plants to their environment while paving the way for biotechnological applications aimed at enhancing the production of valuable terpenoids. 

The differential modulation of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ on LS and PR mRNA under varying light conditions reveals a sophisticated layer of gene expression control that could be harnessed to optimize metabolic flux towards menthone (Figure 7g). The observation that $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes affect IPD and L3OH expression (suggesting a considerable degree of plasticity in $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification, Figure 6f, g) further emphasizes the complexity of this regulatory network and the potential for fine-tuning enzyme activities and metabolic intermediates. 

Despite our focus on certain genes in this study, the broader implications of the roles of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ in the MEP pathway and MBP are clear. Unexplored $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ writer genes (Tang et al., 2023) such as MTB, ECT2 and ALKBH9B may hold the key to further understanding and manipulating menthone biosynthesis. In the future, the integration of advanced genetic tools, such as clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR-associated nuclease 9 (Cas9)-mediated gene editing (Shi et al., 2024) and miCLIP- $m ^ { 6 } A$ $\mathrm { { ( m ^ { 6 } A } }$ individualnucleotide-resolution cross-linking and immunoprecipitation) (Linder et al., 2015), could enable the targeted manipulation of these genes or individual $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ sites, leading to a more comprehensive understanding of the regulatory landscape of the $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification. 

The proposed strategies for enhancing menthone production by transferring genes encoding key enzymes into high-biomass model plants (such as Nicotiana benthamiana) (Hasunuma et al., 2008) and employing chloroplast engineering (Malhotra et al., 2016) could represent a significant step forward in synthetic biology and metabolic engineering. These approaches could help bypass the problem of the limited biomass of the native menthone-producing plant (S. tenuifolia) while providing a scalable platform for the production of other valuable plant secondary metabolites. 

In conclusion, by genetically engineering the synergistic effects of LS, PR and $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ and fine-tuning their functionality, we propose a promising strategy for enhancing monoterpenoid biosynthesis. The integration of post-transcriptional regulation with genetic engineering offers a powerful toolkit for manipulating plant metabolic pathways, setting the stage for future breakthroughs in the production of pharmaceuticals, fragrances and other high-value compounds derived from plant secondary metabolism.

# Materials and methods

# Sources and processing of plant materials

Plant specimens and seeds of Schizonepeta tenuifolia Briq. were obtained from Shijiazhuang City, Hebei Province, China. DNA barcode sequencing of the internal transcribed spacer 2 (ITS2) and psbA-trnH sequences confirmed the identity of these samples as S. tenuifolia (Figure S8) (http://59.108.50.222/china/). S. tenuifolia seeds were soaked in sterile water and incubated in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 days to promote even germination. The seeds were sown in peat moss soil (Jiffy) in a controlled environment with stable relative humidity $( 6 0 - 7 0 \% )$ ). The light intensity was set to $1 4 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathrm { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \varsigma } ^ { - 1 }$ , under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod and a temperature of $2 3 - 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ . When the seedlings developed two cotyledons, they were transplanted into $1 0 \times 1 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ black square pots containing peat soil. The time of treatment was calculated from the day of seedling transfer. At 24 days post transfer, S. tenuifolia plants were divided into three groups: Dark (transferred to constant darkness), HL (high light at $1 0 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity) and GL (growth light; control group, $1 4 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ ). Each group of plants was maintained under its respective conditions for 7 days at a temperature of $2 3 - 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ , under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod. 

# Measurements of physiological parameters and extraction of active compounds

To measure plant height, the distance from the base of the stem to the tip of the shoot was measured with a tape measure, with six or more biological replicates per group. The fresh weight of the aboveground parts of S. tenuifolia plants was determined using a Sartorius 1/10000 balance with three or more biological replicates per group. 

To measure chlorophyll content, the middle part of the second pair of true leaves (counted from the base of the plant) was harvested and frozen in liquid nitrogen. Twenty milligrams of tissue was ground in a pre-cooled tissue grinder at 60 W for 30 s (Scientz, SB25-12DTS). The ground powder was resuspended in $80 \%$ (v/v) acetone and mixed thoroughly. Each tube was wrapped in aluminium foil to avoid light exposure and incubated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ while shaking at 55 rpm overnight until the ground powder turned white. Each sample was centrifuged at 10000 g for 10 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ using a Sigma 1–16 microfuge. The $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ supernatant was transferred to a new centrifuge tube and diluted in 1.8 mL $80 \%$ (v/v) acetone. The absorbance of each diluted supernatant was determined on a spectrophotometer at 645 nm and 663 nm; chlorophyll concentration was calculated according to the formulas Chl $a = 1 2 . 7 2 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 6 3 } - 2 . 5 8 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 4 5 } ;$ Chl $b = 2 2 . 8 7 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 4 5 } - 4 . 6 7 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 6 3 }$ . To measure carotenoid 

content, the extraction was performed overnight by placing 200 mg of fresh samples in ethyl acetate. Following centrifugation as described above, the $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ supernatant was transferred to a new centrifuge tube and diluted in $1 . 8 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ethyl acetate. Absorbance was measured at $4 8 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , 645 nm and $6 6 3 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ to calculate the carotenoid content according to the formula $\left( \mathsf { A } _ { 4 8 0 } - \right.$ $0 . 6 3 8 3 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 4 5 } + 0 . 1 1 4 3 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 6 3 } / 0 . 2 1 8$ . 

To measure chlorophyll fluorescence $( F _ { v } / F _ { m } )$ , plants from each of the three groups were dark-adapted for 20 min, after which the second pair of true leaves (counting from the base of the plant) was excised and used to measure $F _ { v } / F _ { m }$ under a saturating pulse of $2 8 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ using a chlorophyll fluorescence system (Heinz-Walz Instruments). For each group of plants, the leaves of three or more plants were collected separately, and at least, three biological replicates were performed. 

The relative abundance of reactive oxygen species was determined by staining with nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT). The second pair of true leaves was excised and quickly placed in a tube with NBT staining solution (diluted from a $2 0 \times$ stock to a $\boldsymbol { { 1 } } \times$ working solution in $1 \times$ phosphate buffered saline [PBS] in pure water; the stock solution comprised 20 mg/mL NBT in $1 \times \mathsf { P B S }$ ), wrapped in aluminium foil, and vacuum-infiltrated for $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { h }$ in the dark. Following incubation for $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { h }$ at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , each sample was destained in a mixture of ethanol, glacial acetic acid and glycerol (3:1:1, v/v/v) and heated at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in a water bath for 10 min. The destained leaves were mounted in fresh destaining solution and photographed with a Canon camera. 

For metabolite profiling, the leaves of S. tenuifolia plants from each of the three groups were cut and immediately placed in liquid nitrogen. Each sample was ground in liquid nitrogen, and $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of the ground tissue was transferred to a brown glass bottle to which 1.5 mL of $40 \%$ (v/v) hexane was added, followed by ultrasonic extraction at $2 5 ~ \mathsf { k H z }$ for 1 h (Scientz, SB25-12DTS). Each extract was then transferred to a centrifuge tube and centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 \ 9 $ for 10 min at room temperature. The supernatant was transferred to a new centrifuge tube, and an appropriate amount of anhydrous sodium sulphate was added to remove excess moisture. An aliquot was drawn using a syringe and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter to purify the sample. Subsequently, gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) was performed to detect metabolites in the samples, as detailed below. 

# Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) of active components

GC–MS was conducted using an Agilent HP-5MS UI chromatography column $( 3 0 ~ \mathsf { m } \times 0 . 2 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m } \times 0 . 2 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { m } )$ ). Helium (He) was used as the carrier gas at a flow rate of 1 mL/min with a split ratio of 10:1. The injection volume was $1 ~ \mu \iota$ . The temperature 

program for the experiment was as follows: the initial temperature was set to $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and held for 3 min. The temperature was increased at a rate of $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ per min to $1 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ , where it was held for 3 min. Subsequently, the temperature was increased at the same rate to $2 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and held for 3 min. Finally, the temperature was increased to $2 3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ at a rate of $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ per min and held for 3 min. A mass selective detector (MSD) was used, with the electron impact ionization (EI) source set to an ionization voltage of $7 0 ~ \mathsf { V }$ . The ion source temperature was maintained at $2 3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and the quadrupole temperature was set to $1 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The MSD data acquisition mode covered a mass-to-charge ratio $( m / z )$ range of 40 to 500. 

# Library preparation for transcriptome sequencing

Total RNA $( 1 ~ \mu \mathsf { g } )$ was extracted from each sample using TRIzol reagent (Solarbio) and was used as input material to prepare transcriptome libraries. mRNA was purified from the total RNA using oligo(dT) primers attached to magnetic beads using a DynabeadsTM mRNA Purification Kit (Thermo Fisher, USA). Fragmentation was carried out using divalent cations at elevated temperatures in NEBNext First Strand Synthesis Reaction Buffer (5X). First-strand cDNA was synthesized using random hexamers and M-MLV reverse transcriptase. Second-strand cDNA synthesis was performed using DNA Polymerase I in the presence of RNase H. The remaining overhangs were converted into blunt ends via exonuclease/polymerase activities. Following the adenylation of the $3 ^ { \prime }$ ends of the DNA fragments, NEBNext adaptors with a hairpin loop structure were ligated to prepare for hybridization. cDNA fragments (approximately 200 bp in length) were purified using AMPure XP Beads (Beckman Coulter, USA). The sizeselected, adaptor-ligated cDNAs were incubated in $3 ~ \mu \iota$ USER Enzyme (NEB, USA) at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 15 min, followed by a 5-min incubation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , prior to PCR. PCR was performed using Phusion High-Fidelity DNA polymerase, Universal PCR primers, and Index (X) Primer. 

# Transcriptome sequencing and data analysis

After library preparation for transcriptome, the samples were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq platform as paired-end reads. 

The sequences were processed using the bioinformatics pipeline tool BMKCloud (www.biocloud.net). Raw data (raw reads) in FASTQ format were processed using in-house Perl scripts. In this step, clean data (clean reads) were obtained by removing reads that contained adapters, reads with poly-N sequences and low-quality reads from the raw data. At the same time, the Q20, Q30, GC content and sequence duplication levels of the clean data were calculated. All downstream analyses were performed using high-quality clean data. The read1 files from all sample libraries were pooled into one large left.fq file, while the read2 files were pooled into one large right.fq file. De novo transcriptome assembly was performed with the left.fq and right.fq files using Trinity with the parameters ‘-- min_contig_length 200 --group_pairs_distance $5 0 0 ^ { \prime }$ , while all other parameters were set to default. The sequencing reads were aligned to the resulting unigenes using Bowtie. Gene expression levels were estimated by RSEM for each sample: (1) Clean data were mapped back onto the assembled unigene library; (2) Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads (FPKMs) and read counts for each unigene were obtained from the mapping results. Biological reproducibility was assessed by calculating Pearson’s correlation coefficients between pairs of replicates. 

The unigene sequences were aligned to the NR, Swiss-Prot, COG, KOG, eggNOG and KEGG databases using DIAMOND software. The KEGG Orthology results for unigenes in the KEGG database were obtained using KOBAS, while the GO Orthology results for the new genes were analysed with InterProScan, using the databases integrated by InterPro. After predicting the amino acid sequences (using TransDecoder) of the proteins encoded by the unigenes, HMMER software was employed to align the sequences with those in the Pfam database to acquire annotation information (E-value $\leq 1 \mathsf { e } - 1 0 )$ for the unigenes. 

Differential gene expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 R package. Genes with a false discovery rate (FDR) $< 0 . 0 1$ and |log2(fold change)| > 1, as identified by DESeq2, were classified as differentially expressed. 

# Protein extraction and 4D labelling for proteomics analysis

Tryptic peptides were dissolved in solvent A $( 0 . 1 \%$ [v/v] formic acid, $2 \%$ [v/v] acetonitrile, in water) and directly loaded onto an in-house-made reversed-phase analytical column (25-cm length, $7 5 / 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ i.d.). Peptides were separated along a gradient from $6 \%$ (v/v) to $2 4 \%$ (v/v) of solvent B $0 . 1 \%$ [v/v] formic acid in acetonitrile) over 70 min, from $2 4 \%$ (v/v) to $3 5 \%$ (v/v) over 14 min, increasing to $80 \%$ (v/v) in 3 min, and then held at $80 \%$ (v/v) for the last 3 min, all at a constant flow rate of 450 nL/min on a Nano Elute UHPLC system (Bruker Daltonics). The peptides were subjected to capillary source followed by mass spectrometry using a timsTOF Pro (Bruker Daltonics). The electrospray voltage was set to $1 . 6 0 ~ \mathsf { k V }$ . Precursors and fragments were analysed in the TOF detector, with an MS/MS scan range from 100 to $1 7 0 0 ~ m / z$ . The timsTOF Pro instrument was operated in parallel accumulation serial fragmentation (PASEF) mode. Precursors with charge states ranging from 0 to 5 were selected for fragmentation, and 10 PASEF–MS/MS scans were acquired per cycle. The dynamic exclusion was set to 30 s. 

The resulting MS/MS data were processed using the MaxQuant search engine (v.1.6.15.0). Tandem mass spectra were searched against the human Swiss-Prot database (20 422 entries) concatenated with a reverse decoy database. Trypsin/P was specified as the cleavage enzyme, allowing up to 2 missing cleavages. The mass tolerance for precursor ions was set to 20 ppm in the first search and 5 ppm in the main search, while the mass tolerance for fragment ions was set to 0.02 Da. Carbamidomethyl on Cys was specified as the fixed modification, while acetylation at the protein N-terminal ends and oxidation of Met were specified as variable modifications. The FDR was adjusted to ${ < } 1 \%$ . 

Differential abundance analysis was performed using a custom R script. Proteins with a $P$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ and a fold change $> 1 . 5$ or $P$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ and a fold change $< 1 / 1 . 5$ were designated as differentially abundant. 

# Dot blot analysis of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels

Poly(A) RNA was purified from total RNA using a Dynabeads mRNA Purification Kit (Thermo Fisher, USA) for 3 min at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The purified mRNA was spotted onto an Amersham Hybond- $\mathsf { N } +$ membrane and cross-linked using UV light $1 2 0 0 \times 1 0 0 ~ \mu )$ . After cross-linking, the membrane was blocked in Tris-buffered saline (TBS) with $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20 (TBST) containing $5 \%$ (w/v) non-fat dry milk and incubated overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with an anti-$\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ antibody (1:500 dilution; Synaptic Systems). After incubation with a secondary anti-rabbit antibody, the membrane was 

visualized using SuperSignal West Pico chemiluminescent substrate (Thermo Scientific). 

# Quantification of $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels in mRNA by LC–MS/MS

To quantify $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels in mRNA, 150 ng of mRNA extracted from Dark, GL and HL plants was incubated overnight at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with nuclease P1 (NEB) and alkaline phosphatase (NEB) in a $5 0 \mathrm { - \mu L }$ reaction. The samples were then diluted to $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } ,$ after which $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of each sample was injected into the LC–MS/MS system. The system comprised a high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) equipped unit with a C18-T column (Weltech) and a Triple Quad 4500 mass spectrometer (AB SCIEX). Multiple reaction monitoring was performed in positive ion mode, monitoring and recording mass transitions for $1 3 6 . 0 { - } 2 6 8 . 0 ~ m / z$ (A), 113.1–245.0 m/z (U), 112.1–244.0 m/z (C), 152.0–284.0 m/z (G) and 150.1–282.0 m/z $( \mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } )$ . Nucleoside concentrations were quantified based on a standard curve generated using pure commercial nucleosides (MCE, China; catalogue numbers HY-N0086 and HY-B0228). 

# RT-qPCR analysis

A BLAST search was performed using TBtools to identify genes involved in the MEP pathway, MBP and $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ modification (from S. tenuifolia unigene sequences), using Arabidopsis genes as queries. From the alignments, unigene sequences with smaller e-values and larger scores were selected to predict the presence of conserved domains in their encoding proteins. Total RNA was extracted from the leaves of S. tenuifolia plants exposed to one of three treatments and collected as three biological replicates using TRIzol reagent (Solarbio). Subsequently, an Evo M-MLV Reverse Transcription Kit II (including gDNA removal reagent for qPCR) (Accurate Biology) was used for first-strand cDNA synthesis. RT-PCR was carried out using SYBR Premix ExTaq reagent (Takara), and real-time amplification was monitored on a LightCycler 480 system (Roche). Primer sequences are listed in Table S8. 

# m6 A-IP-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from the leaves of S. tenuifolia plants exposed to one of three treatments and collected as three biological replicates using TRIzol reagent (Solarbio). mRNA was purified from 1 mg total RNA samples using a Dynabeads mRNA Purification Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The poly(A) RNA was randomly fragmented into ~200-nt fragments using RNA fragmentation reagents (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The fragmented RNA was incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A } .$ -specific antibody (No. 202003, Synaptic Systems, Germany) in IP buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.5, 750 mM NaCl and $0 . 5 \%$ [v/v] IGEPAL CA-630). The mixture was then incubated with protein A beads (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) that had been pre-blocked with BSA and eluted with elution buffer (6.7 mM N6-methyladenosine [Sigma-Aldrich] in IP buffer). The input RNA and immunoprecipitated RNA were reverse-transcribed using M-MLV Reverse Transcriptase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The $\mathsf { m } ^ { 6 } \mathsf { A }$ levels of specific mRNA fragments were determined by qRT-PCR, first normalized to TUA levels, and then, the ratio of the abundance of the IP sample against the input sample was calculated. 

# Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS)

S. tenuifolia plants used for virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) were grown as follows: seeds were sown in peat moss soil (Jiffy) and maintained under controlled conditions with stable relative humidity $( 6 0 - 7 0 \% )$ ). The growth parameters included a 

photosynthetic photon flux density of $1 4 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ , a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod and a temperature maintained at $2 3 - 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ . When the seedlings developed two cotyledons, they were transplanted into $1 0 \times 1 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ black square pots containing peat soil. VIGS inoculation was performed 20 days after transplantation. 

The VIGS constructs were obtained by amplifying DNA fragments specific to each of the S. tenuifolia MTA, HAKAI and VIR genes using Primer Star Max DNA Polymerase (Takara) and first-strand cDNA from leaves as the template. The gene-specific fragments were 350 bp (MTA) or 500 bp (HAKAI, VIR) in length. Each PCR amplicon was cloned into the pTRV2 vector at the BamHI and KpnI or EcoRI and BamHI sites using a ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme). The resulting constructs were confirmed by Sanger sequencing using pTRV2 vector-specific primers (Table S8) and were introduced individually into Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens) strain GV3101. Positive Agrobacterium colonies carrying pTRV1 or pTRV2-based constructs were cultured separately and the cells were resuspended in infiltration buffer (10 mM ${ \sf M g } { \sf C l } _ { 2 }$ , $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ MES, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone) to an OD600 of 1.0. Agrobacterium cell suspensions carrying pTRV1 or pTRV2-based constructs were mixed at a 1:1 (v/v) ratio and incubated in the dark at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The mixed Agrobacterium suspension was infiltrated into the leaves of S. tenuifolia plants. After infiltration, the plants were incubated in the dark for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ , followed by two different growth conditions: GL conditions $( 1 4 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ , 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 7 days) or dark conditions (constant darkness at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 7 days). Control experiments were also performed, consisting of infiltrating pairs of Agrobacterium strains each carrying the empty pTRV1 or pTRV2 vector. Leaf samples were collected 7 days after infiltration to detect gene expression and for subsequent experiments. 

For metabolite extraction, $0 . 2 \ 9$ of fresh infiltrated leaf tissue was ground in liquid nitrogen and mixed with 2 mL of n-hexane in a brown sample bottle. The samples were extracted by sonication at 25 W for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ (Scientz, SB25-12DTS). The supernatant was transferred to a 2-mL centrifuge tube and centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min at room temperature. The resulting supernatant was transferred to a new centrifuge tube to which an appropriate amount of anhydrous sodium sulphate was added. An aliquot was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane into a sample bottle. Metabolite content was analysed using an Agilent HP-5MS UI chromatography column $3 0 \textrm { m } \times$ $0 . 2 5 0 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 0 . 2 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ with a GC–MS detector. For the VIGS experiment, at least three biological replicates were performed.
