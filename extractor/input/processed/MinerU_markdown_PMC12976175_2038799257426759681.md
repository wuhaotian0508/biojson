# Abstract

BACKGROUND: The efficacy of entomopathogenic nematodes (EPNs) in the biological control of insect pests can be influenced by the host's chemical defenses. Phyllotreta flea beetles, among the most destructive pests of Brassica crops, deploy highly reactive glucosinolate hydrolysis products as a defense against natural enemies. Here, we investigate the susceptibility of EPNs and their symbiotic bacteria to glucosinolate hydrolysis products and assess how this defense shapes the interaction between the horseradish flea beetle, Phyllotreta armoraciae, and EPNs. 

RESULTS: Glucosinolate hydrolysis products were detected in uninjured P. armoraciae larvae but not in adults, and their levels were unaffected by EPN infection. EPNs and their bacterial symbionts were susceptible to glucosinolate hydrolysis products in vitro, with EPN immotility rates ranging from $35 \%$ to $96 \%$ and bacterial growth suppression from $20 \%$ to $85 \%$ at biologically relevant concentrations. However, reducing the levels of glucosinolate hydrolysis products in larvae, either by silencing myrosinase gene expression or by feeding on different Arabidopsis genotypes, did not make them more susceptible to EPNs. Nevertheless, the food plant influenced larval susceptibility to EPNs and the relative abundance of EPN bacterial symbionts in infected larvae. 

CONCLUSION: Although glucosinolate hydrolysis products are toxic to EPNs and their symbiotic bacteria, they did not protect P. armoraciae larvae from EPN infection. However, the larval food plant influenced EPN susceptibility and bacterial community composition, highlighting the role of host plant traits in shaping insect–EPN interactions. These findings provide new insights into the limitations of EPN-based biocontrol against glucosinolate-sequestering pests.

# 1 INTRODUCTION

Phyllotreta flea beetles are among the most destructive insect pests of Brassica crops worldwide.1–4 The two economically most important species, Phyllotreta striolata and Phyllotreta cruciferae, are estimated to cause management costs of approximately 300 million USD per year in canola (Brassica napus) production in the northern Great Plains in North America alone.2,5 Adult beetles feed on aboveground plant tissues, producing a characteristic shot-hole damage that can delay growth, reduce yield, or even kill seedlings.3,6 Phyllotreta larvae typically feed on roots, but their damage is considered less severe than that caused by adults.3 To reduce pesticide use, which currently represents the main means of controlling flea beetles in Brassica crops, it is important to develop alternative management strategies.4,7 Within the

framework of integrated pest management, the use of biological control agents offers a promising approach by exploiting natural enemies to suppress pest populations. 

One important group of biological control agents against insect pests are entomopathogenic nematodes (EPNs) of the genera Steinernema and Heterorhabditis.8,9 Their mode of action involves entering the host through body openings and releasing symbiotic bacteria, which rapidly kill the host through toxemia and septicemia, thereby enabling nematode reproduction.8,10 Several studies have investigated the potential of EPNs as biocontrol agents against Phyllotreta flea beetles and the closely related cabbage stem flea beetle, Psylliodes chrysocephala, another major European flea beetle pest of winter oilseed rape, under laboratory and field conditions.11,12 EPNs were able to kill larvae and adults of both Phyllotreta and Psylliodes in laboratory assays, although flea beetle susceptibility varied considerably among EPN species and strains.12–15 EPN performance also varied under field conditions and in some cases failed to reduce pest pressure.13,16–19 

The efficacy of EPNs is strongly influenced by biotic and abiotic factors, including temperature, soil characteristics, as well as nematode and host ecology.8,20,21 Plant defense compounds are increasingly recognized as important mediators of interactions between herbivorous insects and EPNs.21–25 For instance, Steinernema riobrave showed reduced virulence and reproduction when infecting tobacco-fed corn earworm larvae (Helicoverpa zea) compared to larvae fed on tomato or eggplant.25 Although the underlying mechanism was not fully elucidated, defense metabolites from tobacco were suggested to play a role. Many herbivorous insects are able to sequester plant defense compounds for their own protection,26,27 which can also influence susceptibility to EPNs. A well-known example are western corn rootworm larvae (Diabrotica virgifera virgifera), which gain protection against EPNs by sequestering and metabolizing benzoxazinoids from their maize host plants.22 In contrast, sequestered cucurbitacins failed to protect banded cucumber beetle larvae (Diabrotica balteata) against several tested Heterorhabditis species and strains.28 This was surprising because cucurbitacin D reduced growth of several bacterial symbionts of EPNs in vitro.29 

Several Phyllotreta species were shown to sequester glucosinolates (GSLs) from their brassicaceous host plants.30,31 Together with plant myrosinase enzymes, GSLs form the characteristic activated defense of Brassicales,32–34 producing highly reactive isothiocyanates and other hydrolysis products upon tissue damage. These metabolites confer resistance against non-adapted herbivores and pathogens,35,36 and, when applied as biofumigants, suppressed plant parasitic nematodes and pathogens in the soil.32,33 However, isothiocyanates can negatively affect infectivity of EPNs and may thus interfere with biological pest control. 37,38 

Phyllotreta flea beetles not only sequester GSLs, they also possess endogenous myrosinase activity, allowing them to hydrolyze GSLs into reactive isothiocyanates.30,31 In the horseradish flea beetle, Phyllotreta armoraciae, sequestered GSLs are present in all life stages, while myrosinase activity occurs mainly in larvae and adults.31,39 Although the GSL-myrosinase system protects P. armoraciae larvae against generalist predators,31,39 its efficacy against EPNs is unknown. Here, we investigated the susceptibility of EPNs and their bacterial symbionts to GSL hydrolysis products produced by P. armoraciae in vitro and in vivo. We show that while GSL hydrolysis products negatively affect EPNs, the GSLmyrosinase system does not protect larvae from EPN infection. However, sequestered GSLs reduced the relative abundance of bacterial symbionts, which may influence EPN reproduction. 

# 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

# 2.1 Biological resources

Phyllotreta armoracia beetles were reared on 3- to 4-week-old potted Brassica juncea cv. ‘Bau-Sin’ plants (Known-You Seeds, Kaohsiung, Taiwan) in a controlled-environment chamber at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ $60 \%$ relative humidity, and a 16 h:8 h light/dark cycle. Adults were provided with new plants once a week for feeding and oviposition. Plants with eggs were kept in separate cages for larval development under the same conditions. Any remaining leaf material was removed 3 weeks later, and the soil with pupae was kept in plastic containers (9 L volume; Lock & Lock storage container). Newly emerged adults were collected every 2–3 days. 

Entomopathogenic nematodes Heterorhabditis bacteriophora (nematop®), Steinernema feltiae (nemaplus®), and Steinernema carpocapsae (nemastar®) were purchased from e-nema GmbH (Schwentinental, Germany) and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until they were used for experiments. For P. armoraciae survival assays (described in Section 2.5), H. bacteriophora were reared on Galleria mellonella larvae (Aquaexot, Jena, Germany) as previously described and used within 2 weeks of emergence.40 

Symbiotic bacteria Photorhabdus laumondii, Xenorhabdus bovienii, and Xenorhabdus nematophila were directly isolated from H. bacteriophora, S. feltiae, and S. carpocapsae, respectively, by plating surface-sterilized and homogenized EPNs on nutrient bromothymol blue triphenyltetrazolium agar (NBTA) plates. The identities of isolated bacteria were confirmed by Sanger sequencing of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) products obtained with universal 16S rRNA primers (sequences provided in Table S1).41 

Arabidopsis thaliana Col-0 and $t g g 1 \times t g g 2$ (tgg) double knockout mutant plants lacking myrosinase activity in leaves were cultivated at $2 1 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ $6 0 \%$ relative humidity, and a $1 0 \mathsf { h } { : } 1 4 \mathsf { h }$ light/dark cycle in a controlled environment chamber.42 

# 2.2 Quantification of GLS hydrolysis products in EPNinfected and uninfected larvae and adults

To quantify the levels of 2-propenyl GSL-derived hydrolysis products in non-infected and EPN-infected beetles, we exposed P. armoraciae third-instar larvae and 6- to 8-day old, unsexed adults to H. bacteriophora. Groups of five larvae or five adults were exposed to 5000 EPNs in $4 8 0 ~ \mu \ L$ tap water in Petri dishes (3 cm diameter) lined with filter paper at top and bottom $( N = 1 1 - 1 8$ for adults, $N = 1 2$ for larvae, five individuals per sample). Beetles were incubated for $1 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ at ${ 2 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ in the dark. Larvae and adults kept under the same conditions without EPNs served as control groups. For quantification of GSL hydrolysis products, larvae and adults were weighed, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until extraction. To verify infection, we transferred a subset of larvae and adults of inoculated and control groups to new Petri dishes without EPNs and monitored survival after $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ $( N = 5  – 1 0$ for adults, $N = 5$ for larvae). 

GSL hydrolysis products were extracted from homogenized samples in ice-cold octane containing benzonitrile as an internal standard. Products were identified by gas chromatography– mass spectrometry (GC–MS) by comparing retention times and mass spectra to authentic standards, and quantified by gas chromatography-flame ionization detection (GC-FID; Table S2). Details of the extraction and analytical parameters are provided in Supporting Information Method S1. Concentrations of 2-propenyl isothiocyanate were compared between treatment groups with Mann–Whitney rank sum test in SigmaPlot 14.0. 

# 2.3 Susceptibility of EPNs to GSL hydrolysis products

To analyze the effects of GSL hydrolysis products on H. bacteriophora, S. feltiae, and S. carpocapsae, we exposed EPNs to 2-propenyl isothiocyanate and 3-butenenitrile over a concentration range of 0.1 to $1 0 0 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ in 10-fold dilutions. Assays were carried out in 48-well plates with 150 EPNs in $2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of tap water containing the test compounds $( N = 6$ per compound and concentration, divided over two experimental days). Pure tap water was used as a control. After $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ incubation at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ we assessed the status of 50 randomly selected EPNs: normal motility and immotile (elongated) per assay. 

To assess the effects of GSL hydrolysis products on EPN motility, we fitted generalized linear mixed-effects models with a binomial error distribution and logit link function using the glmer() function from the lme4 package in R v4.3.1.43,44 Analyses were conducted separately for each EPN species. Fixed effects included compound, their concentration (treated as ordinal variable), and their interactions. 

Well position, experimental day and the interaction of both were considered as random intercepts, and the best model was selected via Akaike information criterion (AIC) comparison. Model selection to test for the influence of the fixed effects selection followed a backward sequential approach based on likelihood ratio tests. Terms were retained only if their removal significantly reduced model fit. Model assumptions were checked using the DHARMa package.45 Post hoc comparisons of estimated marginal means were performed using emmeans,46 with Tukey adjustment for multiple testing. Due to known batch effects, we did not directly compare the immotility rates between the different EPN species. The stability of the GLS hydrolysis products was estimated using proton nuclear magnetic resonance $\mathsf { \Pi } ^ { 1 } \mathsf { H }$ NMR) spectroscopy prior to conducting the biological experiments (see Supporting Information Method S2 and Fig. S1). 

# 2.4 Susceptibility of bacterial symbionts to GSL hydrolysis products

To analyze the effects of GSL hydrolysis products on the bacterial symbionts P. laumondii, X. bovienii, and X. nematophila, we exposed liquid bacterial cultures to 2-propenyl isothiocyanate and 3-butenenitrile over a concentration range of 4.8 to $2 5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ in 3.5-fold dilutions. Assays were conducted in 96-well plates by adding $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of an overnight culture grown in Müller–Hinton (MH) medium diluted in pure MH medium to an optical density measured at 600 nm $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 \mathsf { n m } } ) = 0 . 1$ to $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of MH medium containing the test compound $( N = 6$ per bacterial strain and concentration). Pure MH medium was used as a control. The plate was sealed with adhesive foil and incubated at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $3 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ with shaking in a plate reader (Tecan, Männedorf, Switzerland). ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 { \mathrm { n m } } }$ values were recorded every 20 min. 

Data analysis was conducted using R v4.3.1.43 The growth observed in each well was quantified as the area under the growth curve (AUC). To compensate for different growth of individual bacterial strains, the AUCs for each bacterium-compound combination were normalized on a scale from 0 (mean AUC of wells only containing media without bacteria) to 1 (mean AUC of wells with media and bacteria but without GSL hydrolysis products). A dose–response log-logistic model was fitted to the relationship between AUC and concentration using the drc package.47 The $5 0 \%$ inhibitory concentrations $( \mathsf { I C } _ { 5 0 } )$ were calculated based on this model. 

# 2.5 Impact of Phyllotreta armoraciae larval defense on susceptibility to EPNs

The influence of the beetle's chemical defense on the interaction with the EPN H. bacteriophora was assessed in two experiments. In Experiment 1, we reduced the levels of myrosinase activity by injecting second instar larvae with double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) targeting PaMyr2 or a lepidopteran metalloproteinase inhibitor from G. mellonella (IMPI; AY330624.1) as control as previously described.39 Injected larvae were provided with B. juncea petioles and kept in the laboratory under ambient conditions. Due to limited availability of beetle larvae, we determined the impact of PaMyr2 silencing on GSL hydrolysis product levels and on the susceptibility in two separate RNA-interference (RNAi) experiments. To first determine whether PaMyr2 silencing reduces GSL hydrolysis product levels in uninjured larvae, we sampled third-instar larvae 6 days after dsRNA injection for myrosinase assays $( N = 8$ per treatment, two larvae per sample) and GSL hydrolysis product analysis $( N = 1 2 – 1 3$ per treatment, five larvae per sample). Myrosinase activity was measured in crude protein extracts and compared between treatments using Welch's t-test in SigmaPlot 14.0.39 GSL hydrolysis products were analyzed as described in Section 2.2, and compared between treatments using generalized least squares models implemented via the gls() function from the nlme package in R v4.3.1.43,48 Treatment and hydrolysis product (2-propenyl isothiocyanate and 3-butenenitrile) served as explanatory factors. To account for heteroscedasticity between treatment groups and hydrolysis products, we specified a group specific variance structure using the varIdent() function for both factors. Model significance was assessed via model simplification and subsequent likelihood ratio tests comparing models as recommended by Zuur.49 Post hoc multiple comparisons of estimated marginal means were conducted using Tukey contrasts (glht() function, multcomp package).50 

After confirming that PaMyr2 silencing reduces GSL hydrolysis product levels in larvae, we performed survival assays with dsPa-Myr2- and dsIMPI-injected larvae. Six days post-injection, groups of five larvae were exposed to 100 EPNs in $4 8 0 ~ \mu \ L$ tap water in Petri dishes (3 cm diameter) lined with filter paper at top and bottom $N = 1 0$ per treatment, five larvae per replicate). Larvae kept under the same conditions without EPNs served as control groups $( N = 3$ per treatment, five larvae per replicate). Larval mortality was monitored after $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ exposure at ${ 2 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ in the dark. No mortality was observed in controls without EPNs. Dead larvae were transferred to new Petri dishes without EPNs to visually determine the red coloration rate (not red or red) after $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ and EPN reproduction after $9 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Larval survival and red coloration rates were compared between treatments using Chi-squared test with Yates's correction for continuity in ${ \sf R } \mathsf { v } 4 . 3 . 1$ .43 To correlate red coloration of cadavers with EPN reproduction, we collected emerging EPNs using White traps and counted them.51 

In Experiment 2, we manipulated the levels of sequestered GSLs by feeding P. armoraciae first-instar larvae with two different A. thaliana genotypes for 8 days: Col-0 wild-type and myrosinase deficient tgg mutant plants. We previously showed that larvae can sequester GSLs from leaves of the tgg mutant, but not from leaves of the corresponding Col-0 wild-type.52 As controls, we sampled a subset of Col-0 and tgg-fed larvae for GSL analysis $( N = 8$ per treatment, three larvae per sample) and for quantification of GSL hydrolysis products $( N = 8 – 1 0$ per treatment, four larvae per sample). The weights of Col-0 and tgg-fed larvae sampled for GSL and GSL hydrolysis product analyses were compared using twosample t-tests or Welch's test, respectively, in SigmaPlot 14.0. 

GLS were extracted and analyzed by high-performance liquid chromatography with a diode array detector (HPLC-DAD) as previously described.30 GSL hydrolysis products were extracted from larval samples using ice-cold dichloromethane containing benzonitrile as an internal standard. Following homogenization and centrifugation, the products were quantified by GC–MS and GC-FID. Details of the extraction and analytical parameters are provided in the Supporting Information Method S3. Total concentrations of GSLs and GSL hydrolysis products were compared between treatment groups with Mann–Whitney rank sum test in SigmaPlot 14.0. 

Five larvae were exposed to 200 EPNs in $4 8 0 ~ \mu \ L$ tap water in Petri dishes (3 cm diameter) lined with filter paper at top and bottom $( N = 1 2 – 1 4$ per treatment). Larvae kept under the same conditions without EPNs were used to determine the background mortality $( N = 3$ per treatment). Larval mortality was monitored after $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ exposure at $2 1 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark. No mortality was observed in controls without EPNs. Dead and alive larvae were separated and transferred to new Petri dishes without EPNs and incubated for another $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ to determine the red coloration rate of dead larvae. Larval survival and red coloration rates were compared between treatments using Chi-squared test with Yates's correction for continuity and Fisher's Exact test, respectively, in R v4.3.1.43 

# 2.6 Impact of the chemical defense of Phyllotreta armoraciae larvae on bacterial symbionts

Because EPN reproduction in cadavers varied strongly in Experiment 1, we instead aimed to quantify P. laumondii abundance in EPN-exposed larvae in Experiment 2. Therefore, all larvae (dead and alive) of the same biological replicate were pooled, weighed, frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until DNA extraction using the Quick-DNA Tissue/Insect Microprep Kit (Zymo Research, Freiburg, Germany). Because several attempts to design P. laumondii-specific primers were unsuccessful (sequences provided in Table S1), we estimated total bacterial 16S gene copy numbers using universal BactQuant primers (Table S1).5 3 Total bacterial abundance in DNA extracts were estimated by quantitative PCR (qPCR) in optical 96-well plates on a CFX Connect Real-Time-System (Bio-Rad, Feldkirchen, Germany) using the Biozym Blue S'Green qPCR Kit Separate ROX Kit (Biozym Scientific, Hessisch Oldendorf, Germany). Quantification was done using a serial dilution of P. laumondii 16S rDNA. The PCR program was as follows: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min, 59 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $1 5 \ s , 6 0 ^ { \circ } C$ for 30 s and $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 s. Afterwards, the melting curve was determined from $5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in $0 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ increments. 

To estimate the relative abundance of bacterial symbionts, we performed 16S amplicon sequencing of the V3–V4 region $( N = 1 1 - 1 2 )$ ). Raw reads were processed using a pipeline incorporating DADA2 and phyloseq to generate an amplicon sequence variant (ASV) table and assign taxonomy against the SILVA database.54–57 Detailed information on the 16S amplicon sequencing data analysis and the complete R script are provided in the Supporting Information Method S4 and Data S2. 

The relative abundance of P. laumondii in Col-0 and tgg-fed larvae was compared using a two-sample t-test. Only samples in which P. laumondii was detected were included and data were square-root transformed prior to analysis. The total abundance of P. laumondii in larvae was estimated by multiplying the relative abundance of Photorhabdus determined by 16S sequencing with the total bacterial abundance determined by qPCR and compared 

between Col-0- and tgg-fed larvae using a two-sample t-test. To test whether the food plant and relative abundance of P. laumondii predict the total bacterial abundance in beetle samples, we fitted a linear model. Model selection followed a forward sequential approach based on F-tests using analysis of variance (ANOVA) and AIC. All analyses were performed using R v4.3.2.43 

# 3 RESULTS

# 3.1 GSL hydrolysis products are present in Phyllotreta armoraciae larvae but not adults

To determine whether EPNs are exposed to toxic GSL hydrolysis products during infection of P. armoraciae, we quantified the levels of 2-propenyl isothiocyanate and 3-butenenitrile in EPNinfected and uninfected larvae ( $100 \%$ infection rate) and adults $2 0 \%$ infection rate). In larvae, we primarily detected 2-propenyl isothiocyanate, whereas 3-butenenitrile was only present in trace amounts in nematode-infected individuals $( 3 . 4 6 \pm 8 . 2 7$ pmol mg fresh weight $( \mathsf { F W } ) ^ { - 1 } )$ . Concentrations of 2-propenyl isothiocyanate did not differ between EPN-infected and uninfected larvae (Mann–Whitney rank sum test, $N = 1 2$ , $U = 5 2 , P = 0 . 2 6 0 )$ . Assuming that GSL hydrolysis products are predominantly localized in the hemolymph, the detected isothiocyanate levels correspond to average hemolymph concentrations $( \pm$ standard deviation (SD)) of $1 0 6 . 8 \pm 5 6 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ in uninfected larvae and $8 7 . 5 \pm 7 5 . 3 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ in EPN-infected larvae (Fig. 1). In adults, only traces of 2-propenyl isothiocyanate were detected in nematode-infected individuals in two out of 18 samples $( 0 . 5 5 \pm 1 . 6 1$ pmol mg FW−1 ).

# 4 DISCUSSION

The sequestration of plant defense metabolites by herbivorous insects is a widespread defense strategy against predators, but 

its impact on other natural enemies such as entomopathogens remains poorly understood. EPNs are an important group of biocontrol agents against crop pests, yet EPN efficacy against GSLsequestering Phyllotreta flea beetle pests of Brassica crops showed considerable variation under both laboratory and field conditions.13–15,17 Here we demonstrate that EPNs and their bacterial symbionts are susceptible to GSL hydrolysis products produced in P. armoraciae larvae, but that GSL hydrolysis product levels do not correlate with larval susceptibility to EPNs. 

To protect Phyllotreta flea beetles against EPN infection, sequestered GSL must be hydrolyzed by endogenous myrosinases to form toxic isothiocyanates. Such activation of sequestered pro-toxins upon entomopathogen attack has been documented in other insects, for example in GLS-sequestering cabbage aphids (Brevicoryne brassicae) infected with the entomopathogenic fungus Beauveria bassiana, and in western corn rootworm larvae, which hydrolyze sequestered 2-hydroxy-4,7-dimethoxy-1,4-benzoxazin-3-one-O-glucoside to 6-methoxy-2-benzoxazolinone upon EPN infection.22,61 In P. armoraciae, EPN infection did not increase levels of GSL hydrolysis products in larvae or adults (Fig. 1), suggesting that EPNs and their bacterial symbionts either do not activate the defense or actively suppress myrosinase activity in the host. Although GSL hydrolysis was not induced upon EPN infection, we still detected 2-propenyl isothiocyanate and traces of the corresponding nitrile in both uninjured and infected larvae (Fig. 1). In contrast, no 2-propenyl GSLderived hydrolysis products were detected in adults, despite previous reports of 4-methylsulfinylbutyl GSL hydrolysis products in adults that had fed on Arabidopsis leaves.52 The differences between larvae and adults are likely explained by different biochemical properties of myrosinase enzymes in larvae and adults, as well as higher levels of myrosinase activity in larvae compared with adults.31,39 The detection of hydrolysis products derived from 4-methylsulfinylbutyl GSL but not 2-propenyl GSL in adults furthermore suggests that hydrolysis products derived from different GSLs are metabolized at different rates. 

Due to their electrophilic properties, isothiocyanates are toxic to a broad range of organisms, including bacteria, nematodes, and insects, some of which have evolved counter-adaptations to overcome isothiocyanate-based defenses.32,62–65 Other GSL hydrolysis products, such as nitriles, are generally considered less toxic66–69; however, their biological effects have been investigated far less extensively. EPNs and their symbiotic bacteria infecting P. armoraciae larvae primarily encounter 2-propenyl isothiocyanate but also small amounts of 3-butenenitrile (Fig. 1). Both compounds caused similar immotility rates in a dose-dependent manner for all three EPN species, reaching up to $100 \%$ at the highest concentration $( \sim 1 \textrm { m } )$ in our in vitro assays (Fig. 2(A)–(C)). The sensitivity of the three EPNs to 2-propenyl isothiocyanate is comparable to that of the root knot nematodes Meloidogyne incognita and Meloidogyne hapla, 70,71 whereas Heterodera spp. appear to be less susceptible to this isothiocyanate.72,73 The susceptibility of EPNs to 3-butenenitrile was unexpected, and to our knowledge, the effects of GSL-derived nitriles on nematodes have not yet been investigated. These findings raise the question of whether nitriles may also affect plant-parasitic nematodes and thus contribute to direct plant defense. 

In the EPNs' bacterial symbionts, 2-propenyl isothiocyanate suppressed growth in a dose-dependent manner, with complete inhibition at ${ \sim } 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ (Fig. 2(D)–(F)). This concentration is markedly lower than the minimum inhibitory concentration of 2-propenyl 

isothiocyanate (between 0.5 and $2 \mathsf { m m }$ ) determined for several other bacterial isolates.74,75 By contrast, 3-butenenitrile had minimal impact on P. laumondii and X. nematophila growth, and a dose–response relationship was only observed in assays with X. bovienii, showing species-specific sensitivity of bacterial symbionts to different GSL hydrolysis products, including nitriles. The minor effects of 3-butenenitrile on bacterial growth are consistent with earlier studies using disc diffusion assays,63 and support the hypothesis that nitriles are less toxic to bacteria than isothiocyanates. The susceptibility of EPNs and their bacterial symbionts to 2-propenyl isothiocyanate likely explains the negative effects of mustard biofumigation on the biocontrol potential and infectivity of EPNs in glasshouse and field conditions,37,38 and highlights the potential for selecting or engineering increased isothiocyanate resistance to improve EPN performance in Brassica crops. 

Although biologically relevant levels of 2-propenyl isothiocyanate killed EPNs and inhibited growth of their bacterial symbionts in vitro (Fig. 2), 2-propenyl GSL hydrolysis product levels did not correlate with larval susceptibility to H. bacteriophora (Fig. 3(B), (C)). Since residual isothiocyanate levels in PaMyr2-silenced larvae might still have affected interactions with EPNs, we increased differences in hydrolysis product levels by feeding larvae with Arabidopsis Col-0 wild-type or tgg leaves. Despite a $9 9 \%$ reduction in GSL hydrolysis products in Col-0-fed larvae relative to tgg-fed larvae, Col-0-fed larvae survived even better than tgg-fed larvae (Fig. 3(E)), suggesting that feeding on Col-0 alters physiological traits that influence larval susceptibility to EPNs. One possible explanation could be that GSL sequestration and hydrolysis in tgg-fed larvae incurs physiological costs that influence the ability to prevent EPN infection. Indeed, we found that Col-0-fed larvae sampled for GSL analysis and DNA extraction were heavier than the corresponding tgg-fed larvae (GSLs, t-test, $N = 8$ , $t = 2 . 5 6 9$ $P = 0 . 0 2 1$ ; DNA extraction, t-test, $N = 1 2 – 1 4$ , $t = 3 . 0 0 8$ $P = 0 . 0 0 6 )$ ), but weights of Col-0- and tgg-fed larvae sampled for GSL hydrolysis product analyses did not differ in weight (Welch's t-test, N = 8–10, t = 0.486, $\textstyle P = 0 . 6 3 5$ ). Further research is needed to understand the physiological costs of GSL sequestration and both plant and insect myrosinase activity in larvae. 

Insects killed by P. laumondii usually turn red due to the bacterial biosynthesis of anthraquinones, which accumulate in the cadaver. Although the biological function of anthraquinone biosynthesis is not well-understood,76–78 red coloration of cadavers is widely used as a visual marker for bacterial growth. The red coloration rate did not differ between larvae with high and low levels of myrosinase activity and 2-propenyl isothiocyanate, but was higher in Col-0-fed larvae, which are largely devoid of GSL hydrolysis products, than in tgg-fed larvae (Fig. 3(F)). This suggests that Arabidopsis GSL hydrolysis products suppressed the growth of P. laumondii in larvae. However, quantification of P. laumondii revealed no difference in absolute abundance between Col-0- and tgg-fed larvae, although relative abundance was higher in Col-0-fed larvae, indicating a potential competitive advantage when the host is not chemically defended (Figs 4 and S3). The apparent discrepancy between red coloration and bacterial abundance may reflect reduced metabolic activity of P. laumondii in tgg-fed larvae, potentially due to GSL hydrolysis products or other host physiological factors.

We propose that this apparent contradiction can be explained by a mechanism of competitive release, where the host's chemical defense changes the competitive landscape in favor of the adapted resident microbiota. In Col-0-fed larvae without GSLs, P. laumondii likely acts as a classic pathogen, using its own antimicrobial compounds to suppress competing bacteria and establish dominance.58 In this scenario, when P. laumondii replaces the existing community, it leads to a modest increase in the total bacterial load. By contrast, the native P. armoraciae bacteria proliferate in tgg-fed with GSLs, as they have probably co-evolved and adapted to these chemical defense mechanisms. When H. bacteriophora EPNs introduce P. laumondii, the GSL hydrolysis products may inhibit the non-adapted P. laumondii more strongly, giving the adapted native bacteria a competitive advantage. This allows the native bacteria to proliferate alongside P. laumondii in the host, resulting in a steeper increase in the total bacterial load for a given increase in Photorhabdus relative abundance (Figs S3 and S4). 

In conclusion, our study reveals that GSL hydrolysis products, despite their strong toxicity toward EPNs and their bacterial symbionts in vitro, do not confer direct protection to P. armoraciae larvae in vivo. Instead, the host's chemical defense appears to influence the post-infection microbial environment in a way that alters pathogen performance. We suggest that isothiocyanatemediated interference with P. laumondii growth may give an advantage to resident, chemically adapted microbiota, thereby limiting nematode reproduction. This highlights that biocontrol efficacy against chemically defended herbivores cannot be understood solely through toxin–pathogen interactions, but must also consider the competitive dynamics within the host microbiome. Based on our findings, we suggest that a deeper understanding 

of interactions between EPN bacterial symbionts and host microbiota, together with the selection of isothiocyanate-tolerant bacterial strains, represents a promising route to improving the reliability of EPN-based biocontrol of Phyllotreta flea beetle pests.
