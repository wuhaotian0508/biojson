# Summary

The stem nematode (Ditylenchus destructor Thorne) is a pervasive and destructive plant-parasitic nematode worldwide, inflicting severe agricultural and economic losses in a wide range of crops. Despite its global impact, the molecular mechanisms underlying plant resistance to this pathogen remain poorly understood. Our previous studies in sweet potato (Ipomoea batatas L.) identified IbPIF1 as one of the most strongly induced genes following stem nematode infection, positioning it as a key candidate for unravelling host defence strategies. In this study, we revealed that overexpression of IbPIF1 not only activates systemic immunity and strengthens physical barrier functions, but also drives the accumulation of phytoalexins, significantly enhancing nematode resistance in transgenic plants. Furthermore, IbPIF1 enhances the accumulation of defensive compounds, including lignin, callose and terpenoids, which are vital for the resilience of plants against stem nematode infection. Intriguingly, genetic and molecular analysis shows that IbMVD, a pivotal gene in the regulation of terpenoid synthesis, operates downstream of IbPIF1 in this defence network. Specifically, IbPIF1 directly binds to the promoter of IbMVD, inducing its expression and thereby modulating terpenoid-mediated resistance. These results suggest that IbPIF1 plays a pivotal regulatory role in a complex transcriptional network controlling the stem nematode response. This work advances our understanding of plant-nematode interactions and opens avenues for engineering nematode-resistant crops through genetic manipulation. 

# Introduction

Plant diseases and insect pests seriously affect plant growth, yield and quality (Bao et al., 2021). Globally, crop losses due to diseases exceed $2 5 \%$ , with pest damage accounting for $1 0 . 8 \%$ of this total (Kreuze et al., 2020). Plant-parasitic nematodes attack almost all plant species, posing a huge food safety threat worldwide (Jones et al., 2013). Sweet potato is one of the most important food crop. Due to the presence of pathogenic microorganisms, the production of sweet potato faces significant challenges (Liao et al., 2020). Stem nematode (Ditylenchus destructor Thorne) is an important plant-parasitic nematode and widely distributed in warmer regions; it can harm a variety of plants and cause serious damage to the storage root of important food crops (Pan et al., 2021). Stem nematode disease typically results in a reduction of sweet potato yield by $2 0 \% - 5 0 \%$ . In severe cases, it can lead to a complete loss of yield in the field due to the infection of the plant by the nematode (Chen et al., 2022b). The breeding of sweet potato varieties resistant to stem nematodes has become increasingly important (Fuller et al., 2008). The increased resistance of plants is generally at the expense of yield (Tian et al., 2003). This correlation poses a limitation on the improvement of these crucial traits for sweet potato through conventional hybridization methods. Gene engineering has been shown to have the potential for improving resistance to stem nematodes in sweet potato. The OC1 gene from rice has been demonstrated to enhance stem nematode 

resistance in transgenic sweet potato (Gao et al., 2011a, 2011b). Similarly, overexpression of IbMIPS1 improves stem nematode resistance in sweet potato (Zhai et al., 2016). 

Stem nematode is a migratory endoparasite that can enter plants through stoma or wounds. It releases enzymes that soften cell walls and facilitate feeding on the parenchymatous cells of the cortex (Jones et al., 2013). The nematode feeds on the cells of stems and roots, resulting in extensive lesions and cavities along the root system of the plant (Huang et al., 2019), which in turn impairs growth and plant development (Mathew and Opperman, 2020). In addition, stem nematodes can also interact with a variety of fungi, causing great harm to plants (Chen et al., 2022a). The in-depth investigation of the plant–nematode interactions at the cellular and molecular level could lead to the development of more rational and efficient control strategies (Eves-van den Akker, 2021). The production of toxic, herbivore-deterrent or -repellent secondary metabolites, which is typical of many plant defence systems, has been investigated. Histochemical and ultrastructural investigations of lesions caused by Radopholus similis revealed the accumulation of phenolic compounds in response to infection (Valette et al., 1998). However, a considerable number of studies have been based only on histological observation, with the chemical structure of the corresponding nematicidal secondary metabolites remaining undetermined (Beckman, 2000; Chitwood, 2002). 

Terpenoids play an important role in plant–insect, plant– pathogen, plant–stress and plant–plant interactions (Nagegowda 

and Gupta, 2020). The largest and most diversified group of natural terpenoids is sesquiterpenes (Holopainen and Gershenzon, 2010). In plants, sesquiterpenes are synthesized from isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) of the mevalonate (MVA) pathway (Vranova et al., 2013; Abbas et al., 2017). Mevalonate diphosphate decarboxylase (MVD; EC 4.1.1.33) is a key enzyme in the MVA pathway that catalyses the ATP-dependent decarboxylation of mevalonate diphosphate (MDP) to yield IPP (Shi et al., 2012). The genes encoding MVD have been cloned and characterized from a number of different species, including human, yeast, Arabidopsis thaliana, Candida albicans, Ganoderma lucidum, Bacopa monniera and Hevea brasiliensis (Abbassi et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2017). The overexpression of MVD has been demonstrated to significantly increase triterpene content in Ganoderma lucidum and Panax ginseng hairy roots (Kim et al., 2014). Despite the isolation and characterization of MVD genes, their roles in the regulation of resistance to biotic stresses remain unreported. 

The PIF1 transcription factor is highly sensitive to light quality and light intensity. Like other phytochrome-interacting factors (PIFs), PIF1 can interact with both phyA and phyB. With the strongest affinity, PIFs play an important role in regulating cell elongation, division and differentiation (Leivar and Monte, 2014). Overexpression of ZmPIF1 in rice not only enhances the crop’s capacity to conserve water and withstand drought conditions, but it also increases the overall yield (Gao et al., 2018). However, little research has been done on biotic stress. 

In this study, overexpression of the IbPIF1 gene leads to an increase in the content of lignin, callose, and terpenoids. This, in turn, has been found to enhance resistance to stem nematode disease. Furthermore, IbPIF1 was observed to bind to the IbMVD promoter, resulting in its activation and promotion of terpenoid synthesis. 

# Results

# Expression of IbPIF1 differs between stem nematode-resistant and -susceptible varieties

IbPIF1 has been identified as one of the most highly induced genes in response to Fusarium wilt and stem nematode infection, highlighting its potential role in plant defence against pathogenic microorganisms (Nie et al., 2023). To further investigate the expression pattern of IbPIF1, reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was conducted in two sweet potato cultivars: the stem nematode-resistant cultivar Lushu 3 and the susceptible cultivar Lizixiang. The results revealed significantly higher expression levels of IbPIF1 in Lushu 3 compared to Lizixiang following stem nematode infection, suggesting that IbPIF1 plays a critical role in the plant’s response to nematode infection (Figure 1a). 

The IbPIF1 gene features a 1605 bp open reading frame (ORF), encoding a 534-amino acid polypeptide with a predicted molecular mass of $5 8 . 1 4 ~ \mathsf { k D a }$ and an isoelectric point (pI) of 6.22. Domain analysis using the InterProScan program identified the presence of a bHLH domain, an APA domain and an APB domain in the IbPIF1 protein (Figure 1b; Figure S1). A BLASTP search demonstrated that IbPIF1 shares $4 7 \%$ sequence identity with ItbPIF1, as supported by protein modelling software. Additionally, the genomic sequence is 5165 bp and comprises seven exons and six introns (Figure 1c). 

# IbPIF1 is a nuclear localization protein and has self-activation in yeast cells

To investigate the transcriptional activation activity of IbPIF1, we inserted three fragments encoding the full-length protein, the N-terminal 200 amino acid residues $( \mathsf { I b P I F 1 N } ^ { 2 0 0 } )$ and the C-terminal 334 amino acid residues (IbPIF1C334) of IbPIF1 into the GAL4 pGBKT7 vector, respectively. The resulting fusion constructs were separately transformed into the Y2H Gold strain (Figure 1d). We observed that yeast transformations harboring BD-IbPIF1 and BD-IbPIF1C334 grew well and turned blue on SD/-Trp/-His/-Ade/X-a-gal medium. An in vivo plate assay confirmed that IbPIF1 exhibited transcriptional activation activity, with the activation domain located within the N-terminal 200 amino acid residues of the protein. These findings confirm that IbPIF1 functions as a transcriptional activator (Figure 1d). 

To determine the subcellular localization of IbPIF1, we generated an IbPIF1-GFP fusion protein by fusing the ORF of IbPIF1 with GFP under the control of the 35S promoter. The IbPIF1-GFP fusion protein was transiently expressed in rice protoplasts using a polyethylene glycol-calcium-mediated transformation. Confocal scanning microscopy images of rice protoplasts revealed the green fluorescence emitted by IbPIF1-GFP, indicating that IbPIF1 is localized to the nucleus (Figure 1e). 

# IbPIF1 positively regulates sweet potato resistance against stem nematodes

To investigate whether IbPIF1 confers resistance to stem nematodes, the overexpression and RNA interference (RNAi) vectors of IbPIF1 were constructed, respectively. IbPIF1 was then introduced into the stem nematode-susceptible sweet potato cultivars Lizixiang and Shangshu 19 by Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation (Figure S2). Compared to the wild-type (WT) plants, no significant differences in growth or morphological phenotypes were observed in the overexpression lines (IbPIF1-OE) and RNAi lines (IbPIF1-Ri). After examining the levels of IbPIF1 mRNA in these transgenic lines, we selected two overexpression lines (OE-L1 and OE-L2) and two RNAi lines (Ri-1 and Ri-2) in the Lizixiang background for stem nematode disease resistance analysis. Similarly, two transgenic lines in the Shangshu 19 background overexpressing IbPIF1 (OE-S1 and OE-S11) were also analysed for their resistance to stem nematode disease. Six weeks after inoculation with stem nematodes, the storage roots of WT and IbPIF1-Ri lines were almost completely rotten, whereas the stem nematodes only slightly proliferated around the inoculation hole, at a distance of $1 - 2 c m$ , without further expansion in the storage roots of the IbPIF1-OE lines (Figure 2a and Figure S3a). After 6 weeks, there was a sixfold difference in infected area/total and a twofold difference in weight loss between the IbPIF1-OE lines and the WT plants (Figure $2 \mathsf { b } , \mathsf { c } ;$ Figure S3b). 

To assess the extent of stem nematode damage to the storage roots, we performed histological analyses on WT and transgenic plants infected with stem nematodes for 0, 5 and 6 weeks (Figure 2d; Figure S3c). The results showed that at 5 weeks after stem nematode infection, the cell arrangement in the storage roots of both WT and IbPIF1-Ri plants was loose and the integrity of the root tissue was destroyed. In contrast, the cell arrangement in the storage roots of IbPIF1-OE plants was tight, and the root tissue structure was intact. To further validate the data in the transcriptome, we determined the expression of both ROS scavenging-related and resistance-related genes by RT-qPCR. The results demonstrated that the expression levels of IbSOD, IbCAT, IbAPX, IbCPI, IbHRGP and IbPAL were significantly higher in IbPIF1-OE plants compared with both WT and IbPIF1-Ri plants before and after stem nematode infection (Figure $4 \mathsf { a }$ ; Figure S6a), consistent with the results of the transcriptome analysis. Subsequently, the results of the determination of relevant substance contents showed that the levels of malondialdehyde (MDA) and hydrogen peroxide $( H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 } )$ were significantly lower in the IbPIF1-OE lines than in the WT plants, and the activity of SOD and CAT, as well as the levels of lignin and callose, were significantly higher in the IbPIF1-OE lines than in the WT after stem nematode infection. However, their levels showed an opposite trend in the IbPIF1-Ri lines (Figure 4b; Figure S6b). In the absence of infection, there were no significant differences in the levels of these substances between the WT and transgenic lines. 

After stem nematode infection, the JA-responsive related genes IbLOX, IbAOS and IbOPR3 were up-regulated in IbPIF1-OE plants but down-regulated in IbPIF1-Ri plants compared to the WT (Figure 4c). Next, we quantified Methyl jasmonate (MeJA) levels in IbPIF1-OE plants compared with the WT and IbPIF1-Ri plants before and after infection with stem nematodes (Figure S5). Whatever at 5 weeks or 6 weeks, the MeJA was much more abundant in IbPIF1-OE plants than in WT and IbPIF1- Ri plants. These results indicate that overexpression of IbPIF1 increases the level of JA and might enhance the resistance of the transgenic sweet potato to stem nematodes. 

# Accumulation of terpenoids reduces stem nematode activity

RT-qPCR analysis indicated that after stem nematode infection, the overexpression of IbPIF1 enhanced the expression levels of terpenoids synthesis-related genes IbFPS, IbTPS, IbGDS and IbMVD in the transgenic sweet potato plants compared to the WT plants (Figure 5a,b; Figure S7), and the results were consistent with the previous transcriptomic analysis (Figure 3c; Figure S4c). So we further investigated the role of terpenoids in stem nematode disease resistance. The total terpenoids content was significantly higher in the IbPIF1-OE lines but lower in the IbPIF1-Ri lines compared to the WT after stem nematode infection (Figure 5d). 

Further, the results of the comprehensive metabolomics analysis showed that 141 different terpenoids, including monoterpenes, sesquiterpenes, diterpenes, triterpenes, tetraterpenes and polyprenyl side chains, were detected in the transgenic lines (OE-L1 and Ri-1) after stem nematode infection for 5 weeks, 

compared to the WT (Figure S8). Among them, 95 terpenic compounds accumulated after stem nematode infection. The concentration of geranyl acetate, a monoterpene, increased significantly by more than 600-fold after stem nematode infection. The concentrations of 12 sesquiterpenes, santalol (NMW0306, NMW0299), cedrenol (KMW0636), farnesal (NMW0302, NMW0319), lcevomenol (KMW0645), $\beta$ -bisabolol (KMW0664), zerumbone (XMW1501), curcumenol (NMW0318), caryophyllene oxide (KMW0643), aromadendrene (KMW0644) and spathulenol (XMW1035) also increased significantly (Figure 5c). Subsequently, the sand column experiment showed that stem nematode activity was significantly inhibited with increasing curcumenol concentration (Figure 5e). However, different concentrations of farnesal and curcumol did not have a significant effect on nematode activity (Figure S9). This result indicated that terpenoids, such as curcumenol, have the ability to significantly inhibit the activity of stem nematode. 

# IbPIF1 binds to the IbMVD promoter to up-regulate IbMVD expression

Through the analysis of the promoter sequences of terpenoids synthesis-related genes, it was discovered that the promoter of the IbMVD gene contains the G-box motif, which may be bound and regulated by the IbPIF1 gene. 

Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays indicated that IbPIF1 directly bound to the promoter of IbMVD in yeast cells (Figure 5f), and transient dual-luciferase assays demonstrated that IbPIF1 significantly activated IbMVDpro-LUC expression in sweet potato protoplasts (Figure 5g). To further confirm the binding of IbPIF1 to the promoter of IbMVD, we conducted electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs). In the EMSA, $6 \times$ His-tagged IbPIF1 bound to a 30-bp promoter fragment of IbMVD ( $\sim 4 4 4$ bp upstream of the ATG start codon) in vitro (Figure 5h). These results indicate that the G-boxes are crucial for mediating IbPIF1 binding to the IbMVD promoter.

# Discussion

An increasing body of evidence indicates that the defensive response of plants is not a single, uniform process, but rather a multifaceted and dynamic phenomenon comprising a network of interconnected defence strategies (Hu, 2022; Toyota and Betsuyaku, 2022). These include the regulation of hormonal signalling, morphological and structural characteristics, and compound synthesis (Isah, 2019). In order to overcome various pathogenic microorganisms, plants constantly perceive each stress, integrate and transmit various information within the plant, and ultimately activate local and systemic resistance responses. Previous studies have shown that plant cell walls are the first physical and defensive barrier against pathogens. The plant cell wall plays a role in preventing pathogen infection by working in conjunction with the immune response (Wan et al., 2021). Some secondary metabolites and volatile organic compounds are capable of forming robust chemical barriers (Mostafa et al., 2022). In addition, jasmonic acid and salicylic acid are pivotal hormones in the plant immune response (Du et al., 2017). The expression of SlJIG was markedly enhanced by the application of MeJA. It is not only involved in JA-mediated resistance, but also involved in terpenoids biosynthesis to improve resistance to cotton bollworm and Botrytis cinerea (Cao et al., 2022). In this study, we found that overexpressing IbPIF1 in transgenic sweet potato plants increased the expression of JAresponsive related genes IbLOX, IbAOS and IbOPR3, and elevated MeJA content (Figures 3c, 4c; Figures S4c, S5). These findings suggest that the increased resistance against stem nematodes 

may be attributed to JA-dependent reaction mechanisms. The presence of stem nematodes induces systemic resistance in the plants which is regulated by the JA hormone signal transduction pathway. This pathway subsequently activates the systemic defence in transgenic sweet potato (Figure 7). 

In plants, biotic and abiotic stresses cause the formation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), such as hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide anion $( ^ { \bullet } { \sf O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ . Accumulation of $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ could directly inhibit the growth of bacteria, fungi and nematodes (Mehdy et al., 1996; Zebelo and Maffei, 2015; Zhai et al., 2016). The active oxygen scavenging system, in which SOD, CAT and APX are important enzymes, can reduce the content of active oxygen and reduce the damage of active oxygen to plant cells (Liu et al., 2014). In this study, the ROS-scavenging system genes (IbSOD, IbCAT and IbAPX ) were up-regulated, the activities of related enzymes were increased, and then the content of $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ was decreased, thus reducing the damage of $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ to transgenic sweet potato plants (Figures 3c, 4a,b; Figures S4, S6). 

Secondary metabolites provide protection against microbial and pest attacks through a variety of pathways, including the use of anti-feedants, inhibitors, toxic compounds, precursors of physical defence mechanisms and antioxidants (Piasecka et al., 2015). Callose deposition represents one of the earliest responses of plants as secondary metabolites to invasive nematodes (Hofmann et al., 2010; Luna et al., 2011). Cyst nematodes gain entry to the root and migrate intracellularly through the root cortex in search of the vascular cylinder. Upon reaching the vascular bundle, they establish a specialized feeding site known as a syncytium. The syncytium becomes the sole source of nutrition for the nematodes (Grundler et al., 1997). The overexpression of RAP2.6 has been demonstrated to enhance callose deposition at syncytia, which impedes the import of nutrients into the syncytia and results in enhanced resistance against nematodes (Ali et al., 2013). Lignin also plays a significant role in the resistance of plants to pathogen infection by enhancing physical reinforcement of the cell wall (Sato et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023). The differential expression of key genes in the secondary metabolic pathway is consistent with the results of our cell biology observations, thereby providing new evidence for the study of secondary metabolites in sweet potato against stem nematode infection. In the IbPIF1-Ri, the nematodes easily broke through the obstacles and infected more tissues, resulting in greater lesions. It can thus be concluded that IbPIF1 effectively resists the infection of stem nematodes by regulating the biosynthesis of lignin and callose. 

In contrast to the role of callose and lignin in resisting pathogenic microorganisms, another secondary metabolite, terpenoids, a large and structurally diverse class of molecules, represents an important "chemical weapon" against microorganisms and pests. For example, cotton plants accumulate a substantial quantity of sesquiterpene aldehydes, which serve as plant antitoxins, enabling them to resist pathogens and phytophagous insects (Tian et al., 2016). Maize has been found to contain more than 30 terpenoids synthases, which have been demonstrated to play a direct and indirect role in the terpenoids defence system of maize (Block et al., 2019). In the event of the resistant Pinus massoniana being invaded by the pine wood nematode, a substantial quantity of oleoresin terpenoids is secreted, thereby forming a defensive front that enables the plant to survive (Liu et al., 2022). In this process, terpenoids can play a defensive role in a number of ways, including as an antifeedant, inhibitor, toxic compound, precursor of a physical defence mechanism and antioxidant. A number of studies have demonstrated that a range of natural compounds, including carvacrol, thymol, nerolidol, a-terpinene, geraniol, citronellol, farnesol, limonene, pseudoionone and eugenol, possess pronounced nematicidal properties (Abdel-Rahman et al., 2013). 

Phenylphenalenones, a terpenoid present in Musa, play a role in the plant’s defence system, significantly enhancing the banana’s resistance to nematodes (Holscher€ et al., 2014). The accumulation of tannin-like deposits in root cells may be a key factor in the resistance of Pratylenchus penetrans nematodes (Vieira et al., 2019). The inhibition of pine wood nematode reproduction was shown to be effectively achieved by the application of low concentrations of $\beta$ -pinene, while the promotion of reproduction was observed at high concentrations (Li et al., 2020). The results of the RNA-Seq and metabolomics experiments conducted before and after stem nematode infection showed that terpenoids play a pivotal role in the plant’s resistance to this pathogen (Figures 3c, 5a–d, Figures S7, S8). Many studies have confirmed that Curcuma longa has strong antifungal activity, in addition to insecticidal, antibacterial, antimalarial, antiviral and antioxidant effects (Amalraj et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Gupta et al., 2015). Its effective components, curdione and curcumenol, have been demonstrated to exert a significant inhibitory effect on the mycelial growth of Fusarium graminearum (Chen et al., 2018). In the study of Curcuma longa inhibited the growth of fungi, it was found that it could potentially destroy the synthesis of key proteins and enzymes. The findings of this study indicate that curcumenol significantly inhibits the activity of stem nematodes (Figure 5e). However, further investigation is required to elucidate the toxicological mechanisms underlying the interaction between curcumenol and stem nematode. 

It has been reported that specific genes are involved in the synthesis of terpenoids, which regulate disease resistance. The fungus Arthrobotrys oligospora has been observed to produce a class of sesquiterpene cyclohexene metabolites with the ability to capture nematodes (Chen et al., 2020). Among these, the AOL_s00215g278 gene is not only involved in the biosynthesis of sesquiterpenyl epoxy-cyclohexenoids for the capture of nematodes but is also involved in the nematicidal activity of the fungi (Teng et al., 2020). PmGPPS1 plays an active role in the metabolism and defence of oleoresin terpenes in response to pine wood nematode attack. By promoting the synthesis of monoterpenes, limonene reduces the survival rate of the pine wood nematode in vitro (Liu et al., 2022). It has been demonstrated that EREB58 can directly bind to the TPS10 promoter. Overexpression of EREB58 can promote the transcription of TPS10, thereby increasing the content of the sesquiterpenes (E)- $\beta$ -farnesene and (E)-a-bergamotene, and attracting the natural enemies of lepidopteran larvae in maize (Li et al., 2015). In this study, we explored the potential regulatory genes of IbPIF1 by analysing the promoters of key genes involved in terpenoid synthesis in transcription. This research also presents an opportunity to comprehend the resistance sources conferred by polygenes. IbPIF1 plays a crucial role in inhibiting nematode invasion by regulating terpenoid synthesis through the control of IbMVD expression (Figure 5f-h). The cloning of IbPIF1 and verification of its interaction with IbMVD have provided valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms of resistance genes. The information obtained from the study of IbPIF1 provides valuable insights into the field of plant interactions and plant defence responses. In the future, IbPIF1 will cooperate with a variety of terpenoids to form a complex defence layer, and its identity, regulation and precise functions will continue to be elucidated. 

This study provides the first report of the regulatory relationship between IbPIF1 and IbMVD, as well as their co-regulation of 

stem nematode resistance in sweet potato. Our findings demonstrate that JA, lignin, callose, and terpenoids collectively inhibit nematode feeding and invasion behaviors through coordinated chemical and structural defences, thereby enhancing transgenic plant resistance to stem nematodes. This study not only provides new insights into the molecular mechanisms of stem nematode resistance in sweet potato but also offers potential strategies for breeding resistant varieties. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The stem nematode-resistant sweet potato cultivar Lushu 3 and the stem nematodesusceptible cultivar Lizixiang were used for expression analysis. In vitro-grown transgenic sweet potato plants (Shangshu 19 cultivar and Lizixiang cultivar) were grown on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium. Sweet potato plants were grown in the field, greenhouse, or growth chamber on the campus of China Agricultural University in Beijing. 

# Cloning and sequence analysis of IbPIF1 and IbMVD

Total RNA was extracted from cultivar Lushu 3 using the RNAprep Pure Kit (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Subsequently, the extracted RNA samples were reverse-transcribed following the instructions provided with the Quantscript Reverse Transcriptase Kit (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing, China). Rapid amplification of cDNA ends (RACE) procedure was employed to amplify the $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ ends of the coding region using the GeneRaceTM Kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). Based on the sequence of expressed sequence tags (EST), primers were designed using the Primer 3 program (http://frodo. wi.mit.edu/primer3) listed in Table S1. 

The full-length cDNA of IbPIF1 was analysed by online BLAST at the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) website (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). For multiple sequence alignment analysis, the amino acid sequence of IbPIF1 and other PIF1 homologues from different plant species obtained from NCBI were aligned using the ClustalX (1.8.1) software (Conway Institute UCD, Dublin, Ireland). Phylogenetic analysis was conducted using the MEGA4 software (http://www.megasoftware.net/). 

Genomic DNA was extracted from the cultivar Lushu 3 using the EasyPure Plant Genomic DNA Kit (TransGen, Beijing, China). The corresponding fragment was amplified using the primer pairs that are listed in Table S1. An exon/intron analysis of the IbPIF1 gene was conducted using the Splign programme (https://www. ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sutils/splign/splign.cgi). 

# Expression analysis of IbPIF1

Storage roots of cultivar Lushu 3 were inoculated with stem nematodes according to the method proposed by Gao et al. (2011a). Samples were taken at 0 h, 6 h, 1 d, 2 d, 4 d, 6 d, 8 d and 10 d after treatment for analysis of IbPIF1 expression. RT-${ \mathsf { q P C R } }$ was performed as described by Liu et al. A 169 bp fragment of the sweet potato $\beta$ -actin gene (Genbank AY905538) was used as an internal control. Gene expression was quantified by the comparative CT method. The corresponding RT-qPCR primer pairs are listed in Table S1. 

# Subcellular localization of IbPIF1

The complete IbPIF1 coding region, excluding the stop codon, was inserted into the pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP vector via PCR 

using the respective primers (1300-PIF1-F/R). The construct was confirmed by sequencing. The construct and control were transfected into rice protoplasts using a polyethylene glycol-calcium mediated method, followed by an 18-h incubation period to allow for transient expression. 

Isolation of rice protoplasts and transfection of the vectors into the protoplasts were performed according to the method of Yoo et al. (2007). Subsequently, the green and red fluorescent protein (GFP and RFP, respectively) signals were collected using confocal laser scanning microscopy (LSM880; Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). 

# Production of transgenic plants

The coding region of IbPIF1 was amplified from the cultivar Lushu 3 using a pair of specific primers and subsequently inserted into pBI121 to replace the glucuronidase gene (Table S1). The 35S-IbPIF1 expression cassette was excised from the pBI121-IbPIF1 vector and ligated between the identical cleavage sites in pCAMBIA1300, thereby generating the overexpression vector pCAMBIA1300-35S-IbPIF1. To construct the RNAi plasmid, the non-conserved sequence of IbPIF1 was identified using the InterProScan sequence search tool (http://www.ebi.ac. uk/Tools/pfa/iprscan/) and the RNAi Target Sequence Selector tool (http://bioinfo.clontech.com/rnaidesigner/frontpage.jsp) was used to provide suggestions for designing siRNA for the nonconserved sequence. The unique 299 bp sequence of IbPIF1 was cloned into the RNAi vector pFGC5941 in the sense and antisense orientations. The two recombinant plasmids for overexpression and RNAi were each transfected into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105. The procedure of transformation and subsequent plant regeneration was conducted using embryogenic suspension cultures of Lizixiang and Shangshu 19, as previously described by Zhai et al. (2016). 

The transgenic sweet potato plants were identified by PCR analysis (Table S1). Subsequently, the transgenic and the WT plants were transferred to soils in a greenhouse and then to a field for their evaluation. The vine cuttings, measuring approximately $2 5 ~ \mathsf { c m }$ in length, were used for further functional analysis as described by Zhai et al. (2016). 

# Assay for stem nematode resistance

The transgenic plants were identified for stem nematode resistance through a stem nematode inoculation test, according to the method proposed by Gao et al. (2011b). The MDA content and activities of SOD, GPX, CAT, APX and POD were measured according to the methods of Zhai et al. (2016). The concentrations of furano sesquiterpenes, callose and lignin in the storage roots of transgenic and WT plants infected with stem nematodes were measured before and after infection according to the methods of Zhai et al. (2016). The alterations in hydrogen peroxide levels within the storage roots following various periods of infection were examined in accordance with the methodology outlined by lexieva et al. (2001). The sand column assay was employed to assess the movement of the stem nematodes, with the methodology derived from the approach outlined by Twomey et al. (2002). In brief, 300 stem nematodes were treated with sand (low iron, $4 0 { - } 1 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ particle size) in each plastic tube. The sand was soaked in solutions of 0, 150, 2100, 3200 and $3 3 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ curcumenol. After $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , the number of stem nematodes that had migrated through the sand columns into the dishes was counted. 

# Transactivation activity assay

To construct the serial vectors, the full-length IbPIF1 coding sequence and the sequences encoding the N- and C-terminal truncation products were amplified and cloned into the pGBKT7 vector, which was fused with the GAL4 DNA binding domain. These constructs were transformed into yeast strain Y2H Gold using the. The transformed yeast was streaked on SD/-Trp and SD/-Trp/-His/-Ade/X-a-Gal plates to observe yeast growth at $3 0 \textdegree$ for 2–3 days. 

# Transcriptome analysis

Total RNA was extracted from the storage roots of transgenic and WT plants infected with stem nematodes for 5 weeks using the Trizol method (Invitrogen, CA). Ten micrograms of RNA was used for Illumina RNA-Seq. Transcriptome sequencing, de novo transcriptome assembly and evaluation were conducted by Beijing Biomarker Technologies. The differentially expressed transcripts ${ \tt ( \geq } 2 0 0$ bp) with more than twofold changes in the transgenic plants compared to the WT were submitted for homology and annotation searches using the Blast2GO software, version2.4.4. For BLASTX against the non-redundant (NR) database, the threshold was set to E-value $P < 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ . The sequencing data are available in the National Genomics Data Center (NGDC) with the project ID PRJCA036058. 

# Metabolome analysis

A total of 18 samples were selected and divided into six groups for metabolic profiling. The samples, stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , were homogenized using liquid nitrogen grinding and vortexed to ensure uniformity. Approximately $5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of each sample (or 1 mL for liquid samples) was weighed and transferred into a headspace vial, followed by the addition of 1 mL of saturated NaCl solution and $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of internal standard solution $( 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ . Metabolite extraction was performed using fully automated headspace solid-phase microextraction, and the extracted metabolites were analysed using a gas chromatography–mass spectrometry platform. 

# Expression analysis of the related genes

The storage roots of transgenic plants and WT infected with stem nematodes for 4 weeks were used to analyse the expression of the genes related to peroxisome regulation, resistanceassociated, JA-responsive and terpenoid metabolism as described above. The primers used for RT-qPCR were listed in Table S1. 

# Histochemical staining of storage roots

The storage roots of transgenic and WT plants infected with stem nematodes for 4 weeks were used for histological observation according to the method of Sun et al. (2023). Samples were taken from blocks adjacent to disease spots. Toluidine blue $( 0 . 0 5 \% )$ ) was used to stain. The images of these sections were observed using an Echo Revolve light microscope (ECHO, San Diego, CA). 

# Y1H assay

For the Y1H assays, the coding sequence of the IbPIF1 gene was inserted into the pB42AD vector (Clontech), while the promoter fragment of IbMVD was cloned into the ${ \mathsf { p L a c Z i 2 } } \mu$ vector. The primers utilized for the cloning of these constructs are detailed in Table S1. The vectors and the empty vector were transformed into yeast strain EGY48 by the PEG/LiAc method, and X-Gal was 

used for stringent screening of possible interactions according to the protocol of the Matchmaker One-Hybrid system (Clontech). 

# Dual-luciferase assay

The coding sequence of the IbPIF1 gene was cloned into the pGreenII 62-SK vector, which was used as the effector. The empty vector was used as a negative control. Promoter sequence of IbMVD were inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector, which was used as the reporter. Primers are listed in Table S1. Protoplasts were isolated from sweet potato petioles and used for the dual-luciferase assays, as previously described by Huang et al. (2018). Firefly LUC and Renilla luciferase (REN) activities were measured using the dual luciferase reporter assay system (Promega, USA). The LUC activity was normalized to REN activity. Three biological replicates were performed for this analysis. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The pET-28a-IbPIF1 vector was introduced into competent E. coli strain Transetta (DE3) cells to produce the recombinant $6 ~ \times$ His-IbPIF1 protein. Purification of the recombinant proteins was carried out following the protocol described by Zhang et al. (2020). The oligonucleotide probes used for the EMSAs were synthesized by Tsingke. Binding probes and mutant probes were labelled with biotin at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end, while competitors were unlabeled. Sequences of primers and probes are listed in Table S1. EMSAs were conducted using a LightShift chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Scientic, Waltham, MA, USA) following the instructions provided by the manufacturer. 

# Statistical analysis

The experiments were repeated three times and the resulting data, presented as the means $\pm ~ \mathsf { S E }$ , were analysed by Student’s t-test in a two-tailed analysis. A value of $P < 0 . 0 5$ or $P < 0 . 0 1$ was considered to be statistically significant. These statistical methods were used for the gene expression and resistance-related indicators of stem nematode disease.
