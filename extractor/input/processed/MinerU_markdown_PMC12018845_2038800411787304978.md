# Summary

The snow lotus species Saussurea involucrata (Kar. & Kir.) Sch.Bip., an endangered traditional Chinese herb, belongs to a genus of the Asteraceae family. Syringin present in S. involucrata stands as one of the predominant bioactive compounds. However, the biosynthetic pathway of syringin remains largely elusive. Here, S. involucrata suspension cell culture was subjected to methyl jasmonate (MeJA) treatment, which stimulated the synthesis of syringin, increasing its content by up to 3.9-fold. Comparative transcriptome analysis revealed that genes involved in syringin biosynthesis were generally upregulated in response to MeJA. Furthermore, two candidate UDP-glycosyltransferase genes, SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1, were identified through phylogenetic tree and expression profiling analyses. Overexpression of SiUGT72BZ2 (BZ2_OE) and SiUGT72CY1 (CY1_OE) in S. involucrata suspension cell cultures led to 15.2- and 5.9-fold higher syringin levels than empty vector control cultures, respectively. Notably, upregulation of SiUGT72BZ2 enhanced the biosynthesis of coniferin as well. In contrast, only trace amounts of coniferin were present in control and CY1_OE cell cultures. Subsequent antiinflammatory assays using lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-stimulated RAW264.7 cells demonstrated that the extracts from these cell cultures possessed remarkable anti-inflammatory properties. Most strikingly, the BZ2_OE cultures exhibited superior anti-inflammatory effects compared to the control and CY1_OE. In conclusion, our research has not only identified the key enzymes in syringin synthesis but also, through genetic engineering, has generated novel cell culture resources enriched with syringin and coniferin, and enhanced anti-inflammatory activities, highlighting the potential of S. involucrata cell culture as an alternative for wild snow lotus resources. 

# Introduction

Saussurea involucrata (Kar. & Kir.) Sch.Bip. is a perennial alpine medicinal plant belonging to the Saussurea genus of the Asteraceae family, mainly distributed in the Tianshan Mountains of China. To adapt high-altitude environments characterized by intense ultraviolet radiation, low temperatures and thin air, S. involucrata has evolved a unique array of secondary metabolites, endowing it with notable therapeutic benefits in treating rheumatoid arthritis, tumour diseases and high-altitude sicknesses (Chik et al., 2015). Due to the extreme growing conditions and excessive exploitation by humans, the wild populations of S. involucrata are on the brink of extinction, leading to their classification as national Class-II protected and Class-III endangered species by the Chinese government (Fu and Jin, 1991; Guo et al., 2013). Consequently, the exploitation and utilization of wild S. involucrata resources are severely limited in China. 

In recent decades, the continuous progress in biotechnology has resulted in the widespread use of plant tissue and cell culture technologies, which have proven to be crucial for the preservation and advancement of medicinal plant resources. These endeavours, especially those focused on utilizing rare medicinal plants, have primarily involved the application of tissue-cultured seedlings, hairy roots and calli (Yao et al., 2022). For instance, tissue-cultured Dendrobium seedlings were found to have the capacity to replace wild resources in pharmaceutical manufacturing (Silva et al., 2015). Hairy roots of Panax ginseng were employed in the production of ginsenosides (Meng et al., 2022). Callus cultures of Rhodiola imbricata were frequently used to generate salidroside, effectively eliminating the need for direct plant extraction (Kapoor et al., 2018). Regarding the snow lotus, hairy root, callus/suspension cell culture systems have been developed as well (Fan et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2005, 2006). Significantly, cell cultures of S. involucrata have emerged as 

promising ingredients for cosmetic and food products (Gong et al., 2020a). In 2010, the Ministry of Health of the People’s Republic of China approved these cell cultures as a new food resource, with the expectation that they will ultimately serve as alternatives to wild S. involucrata in the future. 

Genetic engineering has emerged as a pivotal strategy for the development of novel plant resources. The advent of synthetic biology, building upon the principles of genetic engineering, has been gradually facilitating the study of secondary metabolism in medicinal plants (Liu et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2022). For example, the co-overexpression of the geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthase gene (GGPPS ) and the 1-deoxy-D-xylulose-5-phosphate synthase gene (DXSII ) into hairy roots of Salvia miltiorrhiza can significantly increase tanshinone levels, surpassing the yields observed in both control groups and those with single-gene overexpression (Shi et al., 2016). Paul et al. (2017) reported that the octadecanoid-derivative responsive Catharanthus AP2-domain (ORCA) gene cluster plays a vital role in the biosynthesis of indole alkaloids in Catharanthus roseus hairy roots, facilitated by genetic engineering techniques. Similar studies have been conducted on ginsenoside synthesis in the hairy roots of P. ginseng, camptothecin in the cell cultures of Camptotheca acuminata and tropane alkaloids in the cell cultures of Atropa belladonna (Guillon et al., 2006; Wink et al., 2005). Despite the nascent stage of synthetic biology research on snow lotus, noteworthy progress has been made in the development of genetic transformation technologies, such as the use of leaf tissues, roots or seed-derived calli as explants for the Agrobacterium-mediated transformation (Fu et al., 2005; Li et al., 2011; Qiu et al., 2013; Yao et al., 2022). However, due to the unique growth environment of the snow lotus, the cultivation of tissue-cultured seedlings has always been a challenge (Kuo et al., 2015), making it difficult to achieve the requirements for production. Efforts have also been made in the suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata (Fan et al., 2021), but many issues require further research, such as the regulation of cell growth cycles and the synthesis of secondary metabolites, which remain unknown. 

Syringin (sinapyl alcohol 4-O-glucoside), also known as eleutheroside B, is a bioactive monolignol glucoside synthesized de novo from phenylalanine through a series of enzymatic reactions to form the precursor sinapyl alcohol, followed by glycosylation modification (Anterola and Lewis, 2002). In Arabidopsis, UGT72E members have been identified as participating in this glycosyl transfer reaction, ultimately leading to enhanced syringin and coniferin biosynthesis through semi-rational design modifications of AtUGT72E2 and AtUGT72E3 (Chu et al., 2014). Furthermore, recent pharmacological studies have demonstrated syringin’s multifaceted therapeutic potential, including antiinflammatory, anti-osteoporosis, anti-nociceptive, anti-cancer, immune-modulation and anti-diabetic effects (Aventurado et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2021). Previous chemical analyses of S. involucrata have indicated an abundance of syringin in both wild plants, tissue culture seedlings, hairy roots and callus cultures as well (Fu et al., 2006; Kuo et al., 2015). However, further research is still needed to elucidate the specific contribution of syringin to the medicinal properties of S. involucrata and the potential for regulating syringin content to improve snow lotus quality. 

This study focused on screening and functional identification of glycosylation enzymes crucial for the final step of syringin biosynthesis in S. involucrata. The research also aimed to generate 

novel suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata enriched in syringin and evaluate their anti-inflammatory capacities. Through the application of methyl jasmonate (MeJA) induction treatment and comparative transcriptome analysis, two S. involucrata UDP-glycosyltransferase genes, SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1, were identified as potentially involved in the glycosylation modification of syringin. Furthermore, by directly utilizing suspension calli as infection explants, the transgenic suspension cell lines with overexpression of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1, rich in syringin, were successfully generated. However, only the UGT72BZ2 overexpressing cell cultures exhibited enhanced antiinflammatory properties compared to control lines when assessed using lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced RAW264.7 macrophage cell models. Finally, our findings provide theoretical and resourceful support for the innovation of S. involucrata cell cultures and their potential application in the field of pharmaceuticals and functional foods. 

# Results

# Syringin accumulation in calli after MeJA treatment

The calli of wild type S. involucrata were induced from the seedling-derived leaf explants (Figure 1a–d). The friable calli were transferred into the liquid medium, in which the large callus pieces were eliminated, and only small cell aggregates were subcultured. The suspension cell cultures of wild type S. involucrata were successfully established after five consecutive subcultures (Figure 1e). We initially examined the composition of soluble phenolics in the extracts from suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata using HPLC and liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) analysis. The findings showed that syringin, chlorogenic acid and 1, 5-dicaffeoylquinic acid were the three predominant phenylpropanoid-derived metabolites, with syringin being the most abundant (Figures 1f,g and S1). Subsequent monitoring of the biomass yield of the cell cultures at seven-day intervals demonstrated a gradual increase in biomass as the cultivation period progressed, culminating in a peak yield of $1 1 2 . 0 3 \pm 4 . 0 6 \ : \mathrm { g / L }$ of fresh weight on the 28th day (Figure 1h). Notably, by the 21st day, the calli cells had begun to brown, and growth halted. However, a significant accumulation of syringin was observed after the 21st day, with a leap from $3 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 1 1 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ on the 21st day to $4 . 5 0 \pm 0 . 2 9 \mathrm { { \ m g / g } }$ by the 28th day (Figure 1i). This observation suggested that the biosynthesis of syringin is temporally lagging behind the proliferation of suspension cell aggregates under shake flask conditions. In an effort to explore the factors that rapidly initiate the biosynthesis of syringin in the suspension cell aggregates, we attempted to treat them with varying concentrations (0, 5, 50 and $5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } )$ of MeJA within 3 days. The results exhibited that the accumulation of syringin showed no significant variation in the untreated groups. In contrast, MeJA led to a significant increase in syringin content, and the highest syringin level (i.e. a relative 3.9-fold increase compared to the untreated groups) was achieved after treatment with $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ of MeJA for $7 2 \ \mathrm { h }$ (Figure 1j). These findings suggested that MeJA is involved in the short-term induction of syringin biosynthesis in suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata.

# SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 were identified as potential UGTs involved in syringin synthesis

To identify the UGTs involved in syringin biosynthesis, a phylogenetic tree was constructed encompassing 48 deduced amino acid sequences of UGTs upregulated in suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata treated with MeJA and representative Arabidopsis UGTs. Among them, five S. involucrata UGTs (EVM0014810, EVM0011052, EVM0014378, EVM0000862 and EVM0015515) were clustered within Group E, closely associated with AtUGT72Es, and independently divided into two separate branches (Figure 3a). Notably, EVM0014378 was distinguished by the absence of an intact substrate-binding domain compared to the other four SiUGTs (Figure S3a). EVM0014810 and EVM0011052 within one branch exhibited a $9 2 \%$ similarity, while EVM0000862 and EVM0015515 in the other branch displayed $100 \%$ similarity. However, the similarity between the members of the two branches was no more than $5 5 \%$ (Figure S3b). These sequence structure properties suggested potential divergent roles despite their close phylogenetic relationship. To select objects for further study, EVM0000862 and EVM0014810 were chosen for amplification from S. involucrata as representative candidates from the two branches, and the quantitative real-time RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis revealed upregulated expression levels of EVM0000862 and EVM0014810 in suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata treated by MeJA as well (Figure 3b,c). Therefore, the two SiUGT sequences were submitted to the UGT Nomenclature Committee, and then were named as SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1, respectively. 

Subsequently, the recombinant proteins of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 were induced to express in vitro to perform enzyme activity assays to verify their capability to catalyse syringin synthesis. The results showed that SiUGT72CY1 directly catalysed the conversion of sinapyl alcohol and coniferyl alcohol to syringin and coniferin, respectively; however, SiUGT72BZ2 failed to yield soluble protein for the subsequent enzymatic assay (Figure 3d,e). 

# Generation of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 overexpressing transgenic S. involucrata suspension cell cultures with high syringin contents

SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 were also selected for in vivo investigation into their roles in syringin synthesis within S. involucrata. Agrobacterium-mediated transformation technology was employed to introduce the overexpression vector pEarly-Gate100 containing the two genes into the calli of S. involucrata (Figure 4a). The green fluorescent protein, fused in the vector as a visual marker for precise identification of positive transformants, facilitated the successful isolation of seven and six positive SiUGT72BZ2 overexpressing (BZ2_OE) and SiUGT72CY1 overexpressing (CY1_OE) seedling lines, respectively. The plants transformed with the empty pEarlyGate100 vector (EV) were employed as the control, which exhibited similar phenotypes to wild type plants. The suspension cell cultures were established from the corresponding callus lines, which were re-induced from the above independent BZ2_OE, CY1_OE and EV transgenic seedlings. The relative expression levels of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 in these transgenic lines were detected by qRT-PCR. The results indicated a substantial increase in the expression levels of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 in BZ2_OE and CY1_OE compared to EV, peaking at 154.3- and 6.2-fold, respectively (Figure 4b,c). Six suspension cell cultures derived from BZ2_OE (Lines 1, 2 and 6) and CY1_OE (Lines 1, 4 and 6) were selected for further investigation, which exhibited high SiUGT72BZ2 (sum of exo- and endo-SiUGT72BZ2 transcript versions) and SiUGT72CY1 (sum of exo- and endo-SiUGT72CY1 transcript versions) expression levels (Figure 4b,c). In addition, the endo-SiUGT72CY1 expression levels in these CY1_OE suspension cell culture lines were comparable with those in EV. In contrast, the BZ2_OE lines exhibited a dramatic decrease in endo-SiUGT72BZ2 expression levels compared with EV (Figure S4). Therefore, the substantial increase in SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 transcript abundances was mainly resulted from the overexpression of exo-transgenes in suspension cell cultures. 

The syringin contents in these BZ2_OE and CY1_OE suspension cell lines were quantified, revealing 15.2- and 5.9-fold increases over EV (Figure 5a). Notably, high levels of coniferin were observed in the BZ2_OE suspension cell lines. In contrast, only trace amounts of coniferin were detected in the EV and CY1_OE cell cultures (Figure 5b). Due to these superior attributes, some species have been extensively exploited to the brink of extinction and are legally protected, making them difficult to obtain for sustainable utilization. Snow lotus, a rare and endangered precious species, holds substantial promise for development and application within the realms of health and food. The scarcity of wild resources, however, inevitably necessitates the exploration of new approaches for raw material supply. S. involucrata is a broadly used endangered herb in traditional Uyghur, Mongolian and Kazakhstan medicine, which is named as Tianshan snow lotus as well. Previous research has primarily focused on hairy roots, with limited attention given to callus cultures (Fu et al., 2005; Li et al., 2006). Here, we concentrated on analysing the growth and bioactive ingredients of S. involucrata suspension cell 

cultures. By employing technologies such as omics analysis and genetic engineering, we developed novel materials enriched in syringin and preliminarily confirmed their superiority in antiinflammatory capacities. 

In medicinal plants, the abundance of secondary metabolites is a limiting factor in the artificial fermentation process. A prevailing consensus among previous studies indicates that the initial phase of the fermentation cycle is characterized by the preferential allocation of abundant substrates and energy resources to facilitate optimal cellular proliferation. As cell growth approaches the plateau phase, the reallocation of resources may begin to favour secondary metabolism, leading to the synthesis of specific secondary metabolites (White et al., 2022). As anticipated, in our study, the life history of S. involucrata suspension cell cultures in shake flasks also adhered to this consensus. Throughout the initial cultivation phase, which extended to approximately the 14th day, there was a notable increase in cell biomass, contrasted with a sluggish accumulation of syringin. After the 21st day, the cells began to exhibit signs of browning, coinciding with a marked surge in syringin content (Figure 1h,i). This discoordination between growth rate and the accumulation of secondary metabolites may significantly impact the yield and quality of fermentation products. 

Synthetic biology modification based on genetic engineering has gradually emerged as an effective approach to overcome this contradiction, encompassing targeted single-gene edits, sophisticated metabolic pathway engineering and even advancing to the synthesis of artificial chromosomes (Jiang et al., 2023, 2024; Mitiouchkina et al., 2020). To identify UGTs involved in syringin biosynthesis, various concentrations of MeJA were applied to treat suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata to induce the synthesis of syringin over a short period. The results revealed a significant accumulation of syringin following a three-day exposure to $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ of MeJA, coinciding with a phase where the growth variation of the suspension cell cultures was not markedly altered (Figure 1j). Similar phenomena of inducing the synthesis of phenylpropanoid compounds, such as chlorogenic acid, using MeJA have also been reported in Carthamus tinctorius and Malus domestica (Demiwal et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), suggesting a common regulatory mechanism underlying MeJA-induced phenylpropanoid secondary metabolism. Transcriptome analysis indicated that DEGs were predominantly enriched in the phenylpropanoid metabolic network, particularly along the pathway from de novo synthesis of phenylalanine to the formation of sinapyl alcohol (Figure 2), indicating that MeJA has a notable effect on the transcriptional regulation of syringin synthesis. The glycosylation reaction is a key step in the production of syringin, and most glycosylation modifications of secondary metabolites in plants are catalysed by UGTs (Wu et al., 2022). Previous research in Arabidopsis has indicated the potential of UGT72E members to play pivotal roles in the glycosylation of syringin; however, despite sequence similarity exceeding $90 \%$ between AtUGT72E2 and AtUGT72E3, the two enzymes exhibited markedly distinct catalytic properties (Chu et al., 2014). In this study, a total of 48 UGTs were upregulated by MeJA in suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata and were randomly distributed across different phylogenetic groups (Figure 3a). Five members were allocated to group E together with Arabidopsis UGT72E members and were phylogenetically divided into two branches (Figure 3a). Among them, SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 were selected from the two distinct branches, with a sequence similarity of just $5 5 \%$ , suggesting potential functional 

divergence. In vitro enzyme assays were initially employed to verify the catalytic ability to synthesize syringin and coniferin from sinapyl alcohol and coniferyl alcohol, respectively. SiUGT72CY1 was demonstrated to possess substantial catalytic activity, whereas the recombinant protein of SiUGT72BZ2 was found to be largely enriched in inclusion bodies, which prevented its in vitro functional validation. Nevertheless, in vivo overexpression of either SiUGT72BZ2 or SiUGT72CY1 resulted in a significant accumulation of syringin in the transgenic suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata (Figure 5a). Moreover, compared to the CY1_OE lines, all of the BZ2_OE lines not only exhibited a dramatic increase in its transcript abundance but also yielded significantly higher contents of syringin and coniferin (Figures 4 and 5a), although its recombinant proteins in vitro were not obtained. Additionally, the different levels of transgene expression between the BZ2_OE and CY1_OE lines also suggest that the transcript abundance of UGT72CY1 was strictly controlled against random fluctuations by some post-transcriptional regulators in S. involucrata suspension cell cultures (Figure 4b,c). Previous studies have shown that small RNAs are involved in the establishment of a threshold for the expression of their target genes (Meins Jr., 2000). Therefore, it is of great interests for deciphering the post-transcriptional regulatory mechanism of UGT72CY1 in S. involucrata suspension cell cultures in future. Taken together, our results suggest that a large amount of syringin and coniferin can be obtained in S. involucrata suspension cell cultures by sustainably elevating the expression of the relevant UGTs. 

Plant cell and tissue cultures have a long history of application, and with the progress of biotechnology, their use has increased in industries such as pharmaceuticals and functional foods (Eibl et al., 2018). Research has demonstrated that cell cultures can fully or partially possess medicinal properties comparable to those of wild plants (Wawrosch and Zotchev, 2021). Anti-inflammatory properties are a crucial characteristic of medicinal plants used as pharmaceutical and food ingredients. In this study, LPS-induced RAW264.7 cell models were employed to assess the antiinflammatory efficacies of the UGT-overexpression and EV suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata. We found that extracts from all cell cultures, BZ2_OE, CY1_OE and EV, exhibited inhibitory effects on induced inflammatory responses, aligning with previous research using extracts from wild S. involucrata plants (Gong et al., 2020b). Notably, the overexpression of SiUGT72BZ2 resulted in a substantial increase in syringin and coniferin contents, approximately 15.2- and 21.0-fold higher than EV, which correlated with a marked enhancement in antiinflammatory effects. Conversely, extracts from the CY1_OE cell cultures, only containing 5.9-fold higher syringin content than EV, showed no significant difference in anti-inflammatory efficacy (Figures 5 and 6). These results suggest that the antiinflammatory efficacy of extracts from suspension cell cultures of S. involucrata is partially dependent on syringin and coniferin contents, but the efficacy appears to be dosage-dependent. The variability of substrates processed by UGT enzymes, however, might lead to fluctuations in other secondary metabolites, potentially affecting overall anti-inflammatory activity (Wu et al., 2022). Therefore, the effects of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 on other metabolic pathways and antiinflammatory activities of cell cultures require further investigation in the future. 

It is important to recognize that the use of medicinal plants often involves the synergistic application of various species, where the overall effectiveness arises from the complex interactions 

among numerous components. These factors may pose challenges in the practical implementation of S. involucrata cell cultures. The results of this study not only deepen our comprehension of the biosynthetic pathway of syringin in S. involucrata but also establish a basis for the efficient generation of superior-quality suspension cell cultures. This holds great significance for the preservation and utilization of scarce and threatened medicinal resources in traditional Chinese medicine. 

# Materials and methods

# Preparation and cultivation of S. involucrata calli

Seeds of S. involucrata were collected from the Germplasm Bank of Wild Species, Yunnan, China (http://www.genobank.org). The robust seeds were selected for sterilization and subsequently germinated on Murashige and Skoog (MS) solid medium under a photoperiod of $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ darkness and $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of light at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 month. The leaves of sterile seedlings were chosen and placed onto a callus induction medium supplemented with $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA) and $1 . 0 \ m { \mathsf { g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ N6-benzyladenine (6-BA), then cultured in the dark at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 month to facilitate calli initiation. Subsequently, two grams of fresh and vigorous callus tissues were transferred into 100 mL of MS liquid medium containing $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ NAA and 1.0 mg/L 6-BA, followed by suspension culture in the dark at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with a shaking speed of 120 rpm, and subculturing was conducted every 3 weeks. The suspension cell culture of S. involucrata was established according to a previously described method (Yu et al., 2006). Moreover, considering the growth status of the callus cells, a period of 21 days was determined as the optimal suspension culture cycle, as prolonged cultivation beyond this duration frequently led to browning and ultimately progresses to necrosis. 

# MeJA treatment and transcriptome analysis

During the rapid growth phase (around the 14th day of suspension culture), 2 g of cell aggregates were weighed and transferred into $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of fresh suspension culture medium supplemented with gradient concentrations of MeJA: 0, 5, 50 and $5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ . The experiment was conducted in triplicate. Samples were collected at 12-hour intervals, freeze-dried and ground into powders. The metabolites composition and content were analysed using by LC time-of-flight MS/MS (LC-TOF-MS/MS) (Bruke Maxis UHR-TOF, USA) following extraction with a 1:1 methanol–water mixture, as described by Wu et al. (2018). MS was performed under negative ion mode (ESI-) scanning, with the ion source temperature set at $3 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the capillary voltage set at $4 0 0 0 \vee ,$ , and the scanning range set between m/z 100–800. Syringin, coniferin, 1, 5-dicaffeoylquinic acid and chlorogenic acid standard compounds were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, Merck KGaA (Darmstadt, Germany). 

For RNA sequencing and analysis, total RNA was extracted from three independent samples harvested from both MeJA treatment and control S. involucrata suspension cell cultures using Trizol reagent according to the manufacturer’s instructions (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China). RNA sequencing was performed using an Illumina NovaseqTM 6000 (LC Sciences, Houston, USA). Transcripts were assembled using HISAT2 software and quantified using StringTie (https://ccb.jhu.edu/software/stringtie/ gffcompare.shtml). Gffcompare software was used to construct a comprehensive transcriptome. The differential expression genes (DEGs) between the two groups were identified using a $p$ -value threshold of 0.01 and a fold change exceeding 2.0. Gene 

Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analyses were performed following the methods described by Su et al. (2021). 

# Phylogenetic analysis of UGTs

Differentially expressed UGTs in MeJA-treated S. involucrata suspension cell cultures, as determined by the transcriptome analysis, were selected, and their deduced amino acid sequences were used to perform a phylogenetic analysis, together with some classic well-characterized UGTs from Arabidopsis, including AtUGT91C1 from group A, AtUGT73B1/B3/B4 from group D, AtUGT72B1 and AtUGT72E1-E3 from group E, AtUGT85A3 from group G, AtUGT76B1 from group H and AtUGT84A1 from group L (Li et al., 2001). All of the UGTs were then manually confirmed and subjected to a multiple sequence alignment, and an unrooted neighbour-joining (NJ) phylogenetic tree was ultimately constructed using MEGA X software suite (Mega Limited, Auckland, New Zealand). 

# In Vitro enzymatic assays of recombinant SiUGTs

Total RNAs were extracted from cell cultures of S. involucrata using the TRIzol Kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China) and subsequently reverse transcribed into cDNA using a PrimeScriptTM RT Kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China) following DNA Eraser treatment (Takara, Dalian, China) to eliminate genomic DNA contamination (Cvetkovska et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). Full-length coding sequences were amplified and cloned into the pGEX-4 T-1 vector after digestion with EcoRI. The primers used in this study are listed in Table S3. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) strains for the expression of recombinant SiUGT proteins, which were purified using a glutathione S-transferase (GST) tag and subjected to enzymatic assays as previously reported (Fu et al., 2022; Su et al., 2024). Sinapyl alcohol and coniferyl alcohol purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, Merck KGaA (Darmstadt, Germany) were used as the substrates and syringin and coniferin standards served as positive controls for the enzymatic product, while boiled proteins acted as a negative control for the reaction. The enzymatic reactions were monitored by reverse-phase HPLC, with absorbance measured at $2 7 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ to identify the products. 

# Generation of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 overexpression transgenic lines

Full-length coding sequences of SiUGT72BZ2 and SiUGT72CY1 were, respectively, integrated into the overexpressing vector of pEarleyGate100 fused with green fluorescent protein (GFP) via attL-attR recombinant reaction, and then transformed into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 (Bai et al., 2023). The fusion plasmids were introduced into the calli of S. involucrata by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, which was optimized based on previous reports (Qiu et al., 2013). Following selection with $1 . 0 \ m { \mathsf { g } } / \mathsf { L }$ phosphinothricin (PPT) and GFP-based visualization screening, the positive calli were transferred onto shoot regeneration medium, formulated by supplementing MS base medium with $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m g / L }$ NAA and 3.0 mg/L 6-BA. Upon reaching a height of approximately $5 ~ { \mathsf { c m } }$ , the seedlings were assessed for expression levels using qRT-PCR with primers listed in Table S3. The ABI PRISM 7900 HT system (Applied Biosystems, California, USA) was utilized to determine cycle threshold values, and the data obtained were normalized against the expression levels of GAPDH transcripts (Qiu et al., 2013). Subsequently, the 

transgenic lines with highly expressed SiUGTs were induced to initiate calli formation on the solid MS medium containing $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ NAA and 1.0 mg/L 6-BA, ultimately resulting in the acquisition of positive callus lines. The friable calli were taken and transferred into 100 mL of MS liquid medium containing $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ NAA and 1.0 mg/L 6-BA, followed by suspension culture in the dark at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with a shaking speed of 120 rpm, and subculturing was conducted every 3 weeks. Finally, the transgenic suspension cell cultures were established by following the protocol described by Yu et al. (2006). The composition and content of soluble phenolics in the extracts of these SiUGTs highly overexpressed lines were examined using LC-TOF-MS/MS analysis (Bruke Maxis UHR-TOF, USA). 

# Assessment of interleukin-6 (IL-6) content in RAW264.7 cells

One hundred milligrams of lyophilized powders of BZ2_OE, CY1_OE and EV cell cultures were subjected to $1 \mathrm { \ m L } 5 0 \%$ methanol extraction assisted by sonication for 30 min, and the mixtures were subsequently centrifuged at 25 000 , $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for g15 min. Fifteen and thirty millilitres of supernatants were collected for further anti-inflammatory activity assay, respectively. The RAW264.7 macrophage cells, obtained from the Cell Bank of Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China), were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle medium (DMEM) supplemented with $1 \%$ penicillin, $1 \%$ streptomycin and $10 \%$ Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) (v/v) at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in a $5 \%$ $C 0 _ { 2 }$ incubator. Three millilitres of RAW264.7 cell suspensions were transferred into 96-well plates $( 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } / \mathrm { m L } )$ and incubated for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The experimental design of Figure 6a included three groups: a blank control group, a model group treated with LPS $1 \ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ , the final concentration) and an experimental group treated with $0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } ,$ , $1 . 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ and $4 . 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ syringin, respectively. The experimental design of Figure 6b consisted of three groups: a blank control group, a model group treated with LPS $( 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L }$ , the final concentration), and an experimental group treated with $5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ LPS plus $1 5 ~ \mu \iota$ and $3 0 ~ \mu \iota$ extracts from BZ2_OE, CY1_OE and EV cell cultures, respectively. Following a 24-hour incubation period, the IL-6 content was measured using an enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) Kit (CUSABIO, Wuhan, China), adhering to the protocol outlined by Tie et al. (2022). Each group of experimental samples was subjected to five independent replicates. 

# Statistical analysis

Each sample included at least three biological replicates. Statistically significant $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ differences were determined by Student’s t-test or one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). In figures, statistically significant differences among groups are represented by different letters. Standard errors (SE) are provided in all tables and figures as appropriate. All statistical analyses were performed using the SPSS package (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA).
