# Abstract

Sugar content is a key determinant of peach (Prunus persica) fruit quality, influencing taste, consumer preferences, and market value. However, the roles of Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) transporters in sugar metabolism and regulation remain largely unexplored. This study employed a combination of spatial metabolomics, quantitative genetics, transcriptomics, comparative genomics, and functional genomics to investigate the role of 67 MFS members in balancing sugar metabolism during peach fruit development. Spatial metabolomics revealed dynamic sugar distribution patterns, with ERD6-like transporters (PpERDL16-1) and tonoplastic sugar transporters 1 (PpTST1) promoting sucrose accumulation and polyol/monosaccharide transporters 5 (PpPMT5-1) and sucrose transporters 4 (PpSUT4) reducing sucrose transport during fruit ripening. Functional studies confirmed these roles: PpERDL16-1 overexpression enhanced sucrose transport, and PpPMT5-1 or PpSUT4 silencing reduced sugar levels in peach fruit. Quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping identified a major locus on chromosome 5, upstream of PpTST1, forming distinct haplotypes (Hap1 and Hap2). Hap1 was associated with lower PpTST1 expression and higher sugar and soluble solids content (SSC), while Hap2 was linked to higher PpTST1 expression and lower sugar content. This inverse relationship suggests that upstream genetic variants fine-tune PpTST1 expression in a context-dependent manner, potentially through interactions with transcription factors or epigenetic modifiers. Notably, PpTST1 overexpression increased sugar content but did not alter SSC, indicating compensatory mechanisms such as changes in organic acid metabolism or water content. These results illuminate the molecular mechanisms regulating sugar homeostasis in peach fruits, providing valuable targets for the genetic improvement of fruit quality through breeding programs. 

# Introduction

Fruit flavor in peaches (Prunus persica), a complex trait influenced by the balance of sugars and acids, is a critical determinant of fruit quality and consumer preference (Predieri et al. 2006). Sweetness, often quantified by soluble sugar content (SSC), primarily reflects the accumulation of sugars such as sucrose, glucose, and fructose, as well as their relative balance (Kroger et al. 2006; Cirilli et al. 2016). Previous studies have linked SSC and sugar metabolism to multiple chromosomes in peaches, underscoring its polygenic nature (Quilot et al. 2004; Salazar et al. 2014; Zanon et al. 2015; Cirilli et al. 2016; Tian et al. 2017; Shi et al. 2020; Peng et al. 2020b; Yu et al. 2021). Despite substantial advances, the molecular mechanisms driving sugar transport and accumulation during fruit development remain poorly understood. 

Peach fruit development follows a double-sigmoid growth curve, with distinct stages marked by dynamic shifts in sugar composition, where sucrose becomes the dominant sugar during maturation (Falchi et al. 2013). In peaches, as in other Rosaceae fruit trees such as apples and pears, sorbitol is the dominant sugar transported, unlike other plants where sucrose is primarily used for transport (Zhang and Turgeon 2018). Several key sugar transporter families mediate sugar movement across membranes, including Sugar Will Eventually be Exported Transporters (SWEET) proteins (Ren et al. 2020; Singh et al. 2023), and members of the Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS), such as sugar porters (SP/ MST) and sucrose transporters (SUT/SUC) (Niño-González et al. 2019). These transporters regulate sugar partitioning within cells and between tissues, playing crucial roles in plant development 

and fruit ripening (Zhu et al. 2022). Specifically, MFS transporters facilitate sugar loading into the phloem, transport through the sieve element-companion cell complex, and subsequent unloading into sink tissues (Andersen et al. 2023). 

In sink tissues such as fruits, sugar unloading occurs via 2 distinct pathways: the symplastic pathway, where sugars move passively through plasmodesmata, and the apoplastic pathway, which involves active sugar transport across membranes (Paniagua et al. 2021). Unlike pome fruits such as apples, which predominantly rely on apoplastic unloading throughout development, stone fruits like peaches exhibit remarkable developmental plasticity in sugar unloading, switching between symplastic and apoplastic pathways depending on developmental stage, metabolic demands, and environmental conditions (Zanon et al. 2015). For instance, in the Japanese plum, both pathways coexist and alternate based on time of day and environmental cues, allowing flexible regulation of osmotic potential and sugar accumulation in sink tissues (Corelli Grappadelli et al. 2019). This unique plasticity highlights the need for a deeper understanding of sugar transport mechanisms in peach fruit, as these processes are tightly regulated by MFS transporters that mediate sugar allocation during development (Tan et al. 2022). 

Sugar transporter proteins (STPs), or hexose transporters (HTs), function as proton/sugar symporters for various monosaccharides, including glucose and fructose (Büttner 2007; Johnson and Thomas 2007; Wang et al. 2020). Polyol/monosaccharide transporters (PMTs/PLTs) and sugar facilitator proteins (SFPs) include plasma membrane symporters for sugar alcohols and vacuolar hexose exporters contributing to osmotic regulation (Noiraud et al. 2001; Juchaux-Cachau et al. 2007; Klepek et al. 2010; Wang et al. 2016; Tian et al. 2017). Tonoplastic sugar transporters (TSTs/TMTs) (Wormit et al. 2006; Cheng et al. 2018a, 2018b; Braun 2022; Li et al. 2022), and vacuolar glucose transporters (VGTs) (Aluri and Büttner 2007) mediate vacuolar sugar transport. Inositol transporters (INTs/ITRs) import inositol, a regulatory molecule precursor (Schneider et al. 2006, 2008; Sambe et al. 2015; Schneider 2015; Strobl et al. 2018). ERD6-like transporters are vacuolar membrane transporters (Yu et al. 2021; Zhu et al. 2021), while plastidic glucose translocators (pGlcTs) export starch degradation products (Cho et al. 2011; Valifard et al. 2023). Sucrose transporters (SUTs/SUCs) enable proton/sucrose symport across the vacuolar membrane, playing a crucial role in phloem transport (Peng et al. 2020a; Braun 2022; Zhang et al. 2023). 

Leveraging Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption/Ionization Mass Spectrometry Imaging (MALDI-MSI), which allows high-resolution visualization of metabolite distribution during fruit ripening (Lu et al. 2023; Mao et al. 2024), this study aims to investigate the roles of MFS transporters in regulating sugar metabolism and distribution. Spatial and temporal regulation of these genes is essential for sugar partitioning and accumulation, as observed in other fruit crops like melons and strawberries, where specific MFS transporters enhance sugar content (Cheng et al. 2018a; Liu et al. 2020). 

Despite advances in identifying MFS in fruit crops, the mechanisms regulating sugar accumulation in peaches remain poorly understood. While studies on apples (Zhu et al. 2021, 2023), grapes (Liu et al. 2024), strawberries (Liu et al. 2020; Milosavljevic et al. 2023), and melons (Cheng et al. 2018a; Lu et al. 2020) have provided valuable insights, the genetic and molecular basis of sugar metabolism in peaches is less explored. By integrating spatial metabolomics, transcriptomics, quantitative genetics, and functional genomics, we seek to uncover the genetic and molecular basis of sugar accumulation. Understanding these processes will not only provide insights into the regulatory networks governing 

sugar transport but also offer valuable targets for genetic improvement of peach fruit sweetness in breeding programs.

To explore the roles of MFS members in sugar metabolism and transport during peach fruit development, we employed a combination of multiomics approaches, as outlined in Supplementary Fig. S1. 

# Comparative genomic studies of 67 MFS members in peach

We used the Rosaceae Genome Database (GDR) and the diploid peach genome Lovell 2.0 (Verde et al. 2017) to identify and characterize 67 MFS genes involved in sugar transport. Bioinformatics analysis, supported by functional annotation from SwissProt, TrEMBL, NCBI, and TAIR databases, revealed 8 distinct MFS subfamilies: STP/HT, PMT/PLT/SFP, TST/TMT, INT/ITR, ERD6-like, pGlcT, VGT, and SUT. Phylogenetic analysis demonstrated that the SUT/SUC subfamily formed a separate branch, while the SP subfamily clustered into a coherent group (Fig. 1A, Supplementary Table S1), consistent with previous findings (Niño-González et al. 2019). 

Expression analysis of MFS genes in the fruit flesh of 3 peach varieties—“Daguo Heitao” (DG), “Zheng Bai 5-2” (ZB), and “Babygold 5” (BG5)—revealed a set of highly expressed genes during fruit maturation. Genes with expression levels exceeding ${ > } 2$ fragments per kilobase per million mapped reads (FPKM) in at least 2 developmental stages were classified as “highly expressed,” based on established thresholds for peach fruit transcriptomics. Genes such as Prupe.8G101200 (PpPMT5-2), Prupe.1G144800 (PpERDL16-1), Prupe.5G006300 (PpTST1), Prupe.3G066300 (suppressor of G protein beta1, PpSGB1) (Wang et al. 2006), Prupe.8G101500 (PpPMT5-1), Prupe.8G180600 (PpSTS2), Prupe.1G378500 (PpERDL6), Prupe.7G234100 (suppressor of G protein beta2, PpSGB2), Prupe.3G144600 (PppGlcT), and Prupe.1G542000 (PpSUT4) were clustered together and exhibited differential expression across fruit developmental stages (Fig. 1B, Supplementary Table S2). 

To elucidate the relationship between MFS genes and sugar metabolism, we analyzed sugar content dynamics during fruit development in the variety “Babygold 5” (BG5), which is not classified as a high-sugar variety by modern standards. Fructose content remained relatively stable throughout development, while glucose content showed a slight increase during the second rapid growth phase (BG5_3—BG5_4). In contrast, sucrose accumulation was substantially more pronounced, peaking during the same period, which highlights the second rapid growth phase as a critical stage for sugar accumulation (Fig. 2A, Supplementary Table S3). Statistically validated correlation analysis of candidate MFS genes with sucrose, glucose, and fructose levels throughout the developmental stages (BG5_1—BG5_4) revealed significant positive correlations $( P \leq 0 . 0 5 )$ between sucrose content and genes such as PpERDL6, PpSGB1, PpSGB2, and PpTST1, while PpPMT5-2 and PppGlcT exhibited significant negative correlations with sucrose content (Supplementary Fig. S2). Further differential expression analysis during the premature (BG5_3) and mature (BG5_4) stages revealed highly significant positive correlations $( P \leq 0 . 0 1 )$ for PpERDL16-1, PpERDL6, PpSGB1, PpSTS2, and PpTST1 with sucrose content, while PpPMT5-2, PpPMT5-1, PpSUT4, and PppGlcT exhibited highly significant negative correlations during these stages (Fig. 2B, Supplementary Table S4).

# Targeted spatial metabolomics and transcriptomics of peach fruit

MALDI-MSI offers high spatial resolution, enabling the visualization of metabolite distribution across different tissue regions (e.g. inner, middle, and outer flesh), while HPLC provides precise quantification of metabolite concentrations. Sucrose showed a marked increase in intensity from the premature to mature stages, indicating progressive accumulation during ripening. Meanwhile, sorbitol exhibited a decreasing trend. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) analysis provided key insights into the molecular dynamics of peach ripening, highlighting significant transcriptional regulation influenced by both spatial tissue variability and developmental stage (Fig. 3, G and H, Supplementary Fig. S4, and Supplementary Table S6). In the premature stage, 12 genes, including PpERDL6, were commonly upregulated across the inner (“in1”), middle (“mid1”), and outer (“out1”) regions (Fig. 3G), while 37 genes were downregulated (Supplementary Fig. S4D). During the mature stage, the number of commonly upregulated genes across these tissue regions (“in2,” “mid2,” “out2”) decreased to 4, with 24 genes being downregulated, including PpSUT4 (Supplementary Fig. S4, E and F). When only developmental time was considered (without spatial variability), genes such as PpERDL16-1 and PpTST1 were significantly upregulated, while PpPMT5-1 and PpSUT4 were significantly downregulated (Fig. 3I, Supplementary Fig. S5, Supplementary Tables S7 and S8). 

# Impacts of MFS transporters on SSC and sugar in peach fruits

Our previous research demonstrated that structural variation in PpTST1 affects SSC and sugar accumulation in peach fruits (Peng et al. 2020b; Wang et al. 2022). Building on this, we identified several DEGs for functional validation, including PpERDL16-1, PpERDL6, PpPMT5-1, and PpSUT4. Notably, PpPMT5-2 and PpPMT5-1 belong to the same gene family and are located adjacently on chromosome 8. To investigate the functional roles of MFS transporters in sugar metabolism, we performed transient overexpression (OE) and virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) experiments in peach fruits using Agrobacterium-mediated injection (Supplementary Tables S9 and S10). Peach fruit at the premature stage was transiently transformed via injection, and samples were collected 10 days post-transformation. Browning at the injection site, referred to as “inject,” distinguished these tissues from surrounding “uninject” regions that showed no visible color change (Fig. 4, A and B). RT-qPCR analysis revealed significantly higher expression levels of MFS genes in “inject” regions compared with “uninject” and control samples, demonstrating the effectiveness of transient transformation (Supplementary Fig. S6 and Supplementary Table S11). 

HPLC analysis quantified fructose, glucose, sorbitol, and sucrose in OE, silencing, and empty vector transformation samples, and “uninject” regions (Supplementary Fig. S7). Sucrose levels in “inject” regions were generally depleted, although slight variations were observed in certain genes. Notably, significant differences in sucrose levels for the same gene across different regions 

underscore the complexity of sugar transport evaluation in peach flesh (Fig. 4C, Supplementary Table S12). The analysis demonstrated that MFS genes play a significant role in transporting fructose, glucose, sorbitol, and sucrose between the “inject” and “uninject” regions (Fig. 4D, Supplementary Table S13). The transport direction was determined by sugar level differences: lower sugar levels in the “Experimental” group indicated transport from “inject” to “uninject,” while higher levels indicated transport from “uninject” to “inject.” For (OE/VIGS) PpPMT5-2, all sugars were transported from “inject” to “uninject.” Conversely, (OE) PpPMT5-1 transported all sugars from “uninject” to “inject,” while (VIGS) PpPMT5-1 showed mixed transport, with sucrose moving from “uninject” to “inject” and other sugars in the opposite direction. Similarly, (OE) PpSUT4 transported all sugars from “inject” to “uninject,” but (VIGS) PpSUT4 reversed the direction for sucrose and sorbitol while maintaining “inject” to “uninject” transport for fructose and glucose. For (OE/VIGS) PpERDL16-1, all sugars were transported from “uninject” to “inject,” while (OE) PpERDL6 moved all sugars from “inject” to “uninject.” Interestingly, in (VIGS) PpERDL6, sucrose transport reversed to “uninject” to “inject,” while other sugars moved in the opposite direction. Overall, PpPMT5-1 and PpERDL16-1 promoted sucrose accumulation in the “inject” region, while PpPMT5-2, PpSUT4, and PpERDL6 reduced it. These findings highlight the specialized roles of MFS transporters in sugar transport pathways and their complex regulatory mechanisms. 

SSC reflects total dissolved solids, including sugars, organic acids, and minerals. Here, we focused on sugar components (sucrose, glucose, fructose, and sorbitol) quantified by HPLC, while SSC was measured as a complementary trait for breeding relevance. In the control group, SSC and sugar levels were lower in the “inject” regions, likely due to sucrose consumption by injected Agrobacterium (Figs. 4C and 5H, Supplementary Fig. S8A, Supplementary Table S14. Among the tested genes, only (OE) PpPMT5-1 significantly increased SSC levels $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ , while no significant changes were observed for others (Supplementary Fig. S8B and Supplementary Table S15). These results suggest that MFS transporters not only regulate sugar accumulation but also influence overall fruit quality through their effects on SSC. 

# Genome-wide quantitative trait locus mapping and functional studies of PpTST1 in balancing sugar contents and SSC in peach

As shown in Fig. 1B, PpTST1 expression increases significantly during different developmental stages across 3 peach varieties, highlighting the need to explore the mechanisms regulating its expression. # Discussion

Fruit sweetness in peaches is regulated by a complex network of sugar transport and metabolism, mediated by the coordinated activity of multiple transporter families (Cirilli et al. 2016). This study highlights the critical role of MFS transporters in sugar transport and storage, offering insights into their function during peach fruit development, as previously studied in Arabidopsis (Niño-González et al. 2019). By integrating spatial metabolomics, transcriptomics, quantitative genetics, and functional genomics, we identified and characterized 67 MFS genes in the peach genome (Fig. 1A). Among these, PpERDL16-1 and PpTST1 were positively correlated with sucrose accumulation during ripening, while PpPMT5-1, PpPMT5-2, and PpSUT4 exhibited negative correlations, suggesting their involvement in sugar efflux or catabolism during early fruit development (Figs. 1B and 3). Functional studies confirmed these roles, with OE of PpERDL16-1 enhancing sucrose 

transport and storage, while silencing PpPMT5-1 and PpERDL6 reduced sugar levels, underscoring their roles in sugar partitioning and compartmentalization (Fig. 4). Notably, sucrose transport pathways differed from those of glucose and fructose, indicating independent regulatory mechanisms. 

# Spatiotemporal regulation of sugar partitioning by MFS transporters

Our multiomics approach uncovered a spatiotemporal regulatory network in which MFS transporters dynamically coordinate sugar partitioning. For instance, PpERDL16-1 and PpTST1 dominate sucrose accumulation in the outer flesh during ripening (Fig. 3, D and I), while PpPMT5-1 and PpSUT4 may limit sugar influx into vacuoles at early stages (Zanon et al. 2015). This stage-specific specialization aligns with findings in apples, where MdTST1/2 expression peaks during fruit maturation (Zhu et al. 2021), but contrasts with grapes, where VvERDL6l13 is stress-inducible (Breia et al. 2020). These parallels suggest that MFS transporters play conserved yet adaptable roles in sugar homeostasis across species. By integrating spatial metabolomics and transcriptomics, we revealed how different MFS transporters contribute to sugar accumulation at distinct developmental stages and tissue regions, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the regulatory networks governing fruit sweetness. 

# PpTST1 as a key regulator of sugar accumulation

Genome-wide QTL mapping identified PpTST1 as a major regulator of sugar content, located on chromosome 5 (Salazar et al. 2014; Cirilli et al. 2016; Shi et al. 2020; Yu et al. 2021) within the D locus, a critical region where structural variations in Prunus species influence traits like titratable acidity, pH, and sugar content (Yang et al. 2024). The increase in PpTST1 expression from the preripening to the ripening stage aligns with the fruit’s need for enhanced sugar transport and storage as it matures (Wang et al. 2022). However, the negative correlation between high preripening PpTST1 expression and low SSC at ripening in some varieties suggests that the timing and level of PpTST1 expression are crucial for optimal sugar accumulation (Fig. 5, F and G). This developmental regulation may involve interactions with other metabolic pathways or transcription factors. 

Interestingly, while transient OE of PpTST1 increased sugar content and reduced organic acid levels in peach fruits (Wang et al. 2022), it did not significantly alter SSC in tree-based assays (Fig. 5, H and I). This discrepancy highlights the complexity of SSC as a composite trait influenced by multiple factors, including sugars, organic acids, and other soluble compounds (Kroger et al. 2006; Cirilli et al. 2016). The lack of SSC change despite increased sucrose levels suggests that PpTST1 OE may trigger compensatory mechanisms, such as altered organic acid metabolism or changes in water content, to maintain SSC homeostasis. This finding underscores the importance of considering broader metabolic networks when interpreting the effects of gene manipulation on fruit quality traits. 

# Upstream genetic variants fine-tune PpTST1 expression

We identified an upstream genetic variant associated with distinct haplotypes (Hap1 and Hap2) providing insights into the regulatory mechanisms controlling PpTST1 expression (Fig. 5, D and E). Hap1, linked to lower PpTST1 expression during the preripening stage and higher fructose and glucose levels, likely contains a repressive regulatory element. In contrast, Hap2, 

associated with higher PpTST1 expression, may enhance transcriptional activity. These findings suggest that natural variation in the PpTST1 promoter region contributes to phenotypic diversity in sugar accumulation among peach varieties. 

Haplotype analysis revealed that Hap1, associated with lower PpTST1 expression, correlates with higher SSC, while Hap2, linked to higher PpTST1 expression, results in lower SSC (Fig. 5, F and G). This inverse relationship highlights the regulatory role of upstream genetic variants in modulating PpTST1 expression and sugar accumulation. The apparent contradiction between our findings and those of previous studies (Peng et al. 2020b; Wang et al. 2022) may arise from the differential effects of upstream genetic variants (Hap1 and Hap2) on PpTST1 expression. We hypothesize that the upstream SNP locus (Chr5:1,093,132) may act as a regulatory element (e.g. an enhancer or repressor) that finetunes PpTST1 expression in a context-dependent manner. For instance, Hap1 may enhance PpTST1 expression during early fruit development, promoting sugar import into vacuoles, while Hap2 may suppress PpTST1 expression during ripening, facilitating sugar export. This regulatory mechanism could explain the stagespecific roles of PpTST1 in sugar homeostasis. 

# Implications for fruit quality and breeding

The functional characterization of PpTST1 (Peng et al. 2020b; Wang et al. 2022) and its upstream regulatory variants provides valuable targets for genetic improvement of peach fruit quality (Fig. 5, D and E). By manipulating PpTST1 expression or selecting favorable haplotypes, breeders can optimize sugar accumulation and metabolic balance in peach fruits. Additionally, our findings highlight the importance of considering developmental timing and genetic background when designing strategies to enhance fruit quality. 

Some limitations should be acknowledged (Wang et al. 2022). For instance, the transient nature of our OE and silencing assays may not fully capture the long-term effects of PpTST1 manipulation (Fig. 5, H and I). Future studies should employ stable transgenic approaches to validate these findings. Additionally, the integration of multi-omics data (e.g. transcriptomics, metabolomics, and epigenomics) will be essential to unravel the complex regulatory networks governing PpTST1 expression and function. 

The upstream regulatory region may interact with transcription factors or epigenetic modifiers to modulate PpTST1 expression in response to developmental or environmental cues. For example, ABA signaling, which is known to influence sugar accumulation (Falchi et al. 2013; Zhu et al. 2021, 2023), may interact with the upstream regulatory region to fine-tune PpTST1 expression during fruit ripening. Future studies should explore the molecular mechanisms underlying these regulatory interactions and their impact on sugar metabolism. 

# Conclusion

This study underscores the critical roles of MFS transporters, particularly PpTST1, in regulating sugar transport and accumulation during peach fruit development. By leveraging multi-omics approaches, we have provided a robust framework for understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying sugar accumulation. These findings not only deepen our understanding of fruit sweetness regulation but also offer practical targets for breeding programs aimed at enhancing peach fruit sweetness and quality. Future studies should explore the mechanisms underlying PpTST1’s stage-specific functions, particularly its interactions with hormonal signals (e.g. ABA) and environmental factors, as well as the potential regulatory roles of transcription factors and 

epigenetic modifications in mediating the effects of Hap1 and Hap2 on PpTST1 expression. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and sample collection

This study analyzed 3 peach varieties: ZB, DG, and BG5, all 14 years old, to investigate changes in sugar composition at different fruit developmental stages. These cultivars were selected based on their contrasting sugar accumulation patterns and unique metabolic profiles, providing a representative range of phenotypes for investigating sugar metabolism in peach fruit. Specifically, DG is characterized by moderate sugar content $12 \%$ soluble solids) and a distinct acidic flavor, making it a valuable model for studying the balance between sugar and acid accumulation. ZB exhibits high sugar content $1 3 \%$ soluble solids) and a sweet flavor, ideal for exploring mechanisms of sugar accumulation and partitioning in high-sugar peaches. BG5, with a soluble solids content of $1 0 \%$ , is not classified as a high-sugar variety by modern standards but exhibits unique sugar metabolism traits, particularly in sucrose accumulation, making it useful for comparative studies. 

Fruit development was divided into 4 stages: S1 (rapid growth), S2 (pit hardening), S3 (second rapid growth), and S4 (fruit maturity). Stage S4 was further categorized into S4-1 (fruit reaches final size) and S4-2 (ethylene-dependent ripening) (Borsani et al. 2009). Samples from each stage were taken from at least 9 fruits, with 3 fruits per biological replicate. Additionally, tissue samples from the cultivar “KX_1729_93” were collected at premature and mature stages, categorized as “in”, “mid”, and “out” based on proximity to the fruit core. “KX_1729_93” has an average SSC of $9 . 2 9 \%$ at maturity, providing a contrasting phenotype to the other cultivars studied. For MALDI-MSI analysis, 1 mm thick tissue slices were prepared, covering developmental stages from premature to mature fruit (Fig. 3, C and D, Supplementary Fig. S3). For transcriptome analysis, the 6 sub-tissue samples were pooled, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and used for RNA extraction (Fig. 3, E to I). Five to 6 fruits from each of the 23 natural population peach accessions were harvested 15 days before maturity, in 2016 (Cao et al. 2021), and their mesocarps were sampled to perform SSC and RNA-seq analysis (Fig. 5, F and G). The accessions exhibited an average SSC of $1 0 . 9 9 7 \%$ at maturity in 2016. To account for environmental variation, multi-year normalized SSC values were used as phenotypic data, calculated by adjusting for year-specific effects. 

# Evolutionary analysis

To explore the evolutionary relationships of MFS genes, the diploid peach genome (Lovell 2.0) (Verde et al. 2017) was referenced from the GDR to comprehensively identify MFS genes. Functional characterization and bioinformatics analysis of these genes were conducted using databases including SwissProt, TrEMBL, NCBI, and TAIR. We performed sequence alignment of 129 MFS amino acid sequences from Arabidopsis thaliana and peach using CLUSTAL W. A phylogenetic tree was constructed with MEGA 7 software using the neighbor-joining method, applying the p-distance model, 1,000 bootstrap replicates for statistical confidence, and pairwise deletion to handle missing data (Fig. 1A). 

# Transcriptome sequencing and expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted using a kit (Waryong, Beijing, China), treated with RNase-free DNase, and reverse-transcribed using 

the Supremo III RT kit (BioTeKe, Beijing, China). RNA concentration and purity were assessed using the Agilent Bioanalyzer 2,100 and NanoDrop 2000. mRNA was enriched using Oligo(dT) magnetic beads, randomly fragmented, and first-strand cDNA was synthesized with random hexamers, followed by secondstrand cDNA synthesis. The constructed library was sequenced on the HiSeq 2,500 system (Illumina) with 3 biological replicates for all peach flesh samples. Quality control of RNA sequence data included the removal of adapters, low-quality reads, and reads containing N. Clean reads were aligned to the peach reference genome (Lovell 2.0) (Verde et al. 2017) using TopHat2 (Kim et al. 2013), and gene expression levels were quantified both as raw counts and as fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads (FPKM) for correlation studies. Raw counts were normalized using VST or rlog for correlation analysis with sugar content, while FPKM values were also used for similar analyses. Pearson correlation coefficients were computed, and their statistical significance was evaluated using t-tests $( \operatorname { d f } = n - 2 )$ , with results visualized as a correlation matrix using the corrplot package in R (Fig. 2B).DEGs were identified from raw counts using DESeq2 $( \log 2 \mathrm { F C } \geq 1$ or $\leq - 1$ , padj $< 0 . 0 5$ ) (Fig. 3, G and H). 

# Phenotypic analysis for fruit flavor

Phenotypic data for fruit flavor were collected from hybrid peach populations in 2021. A single plant was sampled multiple times, each time collecting 8 to 10 commercially mature fruits. Fruit juice was obtained, filtered, and stored for subsequent analysis of sugar components. SSC was measured with a hand refractometer (Frett et al. 2012), and sugar contents (sucrose, fructose, glucose, and sorbitol) were quantified using HPLC, following methods from previous studies (Figs. 4 and 5). 

# Targeted spatial metabolomics

Spatial metabolomics analysis was conducted using tissue samples from the “KX_1729_93” cultivar. Frozen samples were equilibrated at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h to ensure temperature uniformity before slicing. Using a Leica CM1950 cryostat, the tissues were sectioned to a thickness of $1 4 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and mounted onto indium tin oxide-coated conductive glass slides. The mounted sections were dried in a vacuum desiccator for 30 min to remove residual moisture. MSI was performed on a rapifleX MALDI-TOF/TOF MS system in positive reflectron mode. Data were acquired over a mass range of $m / z$ 200 to 600 Da with a spatial resolution of $5 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , ensuring detailed mapping of metabolites. Each spectrum was averaged over 100 laser shots, and the resulting signal intensities were normalized using total ion current for consistent data analysis. To confirm metabolite identities, MS/MS fragmentation was conducted in LIFT mode, enabling precise structural validation of the targeted metabolites. This approach provided detailed spatial localization and profiling of key metabolites within the tissue samples (Fig. 3, C and D). 

# Transient OE and VIGS functional validation

Transient OE and VIGS were used to validate the target gene’s function. The target gene was amplified using specific primers and cDNA from ZB and “Tianjin Shuimi.” PCR products were purified and cloned into the pSAK778 vector for OE or the pTRV2 vector for VIGS. The plasmids were transformed into E. coli DH5α, and positive clones were confirmed by colony PCR and sequencing (Supplementary Table S9). Recombinant plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101. For OE, Agrobacterium carrying pSAK778_MFS was used, and for VIGS, pTRV2_MFS was used. 

Cultures $( \mathrm { O D } 6 0 0 = 0 . 8 $ ) were infiltrated into peach fruits on the tree. After 7 to 10 days, peach flesh was sampled for sugar measurement and RNA extraction for RT-qPCR analysis (Supplementary Fig. S6, and Tables S10 and S11). 

Peach flesh was homogenized into juice, and SSC, fructose, glucose, sorbitol, and sucrose concentrations were quantified in both OE and virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) experiments (Supplementary Tables S12 and S14). Concentration discrepancies between transformed and non-transformed regions were calculated as: ΔConcentration $=$ Inject − Uninject (Supplementary Tables S13 and S15). 

# RT-qPCR expression analysis

Total RNA was reverse-transcribed to cDNA, and real-time qPCR was performed using the SYBR Premix ExTaq Green PCR Kit on a QuantStudio 7 Flex system. PpActin was used as the reference gene, and relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method. Expression levels were normalized against the control, and technical replicates were used for each gene (Supplementary Fig. S6C, and Table S12). 

# Plant materials of populations and SNP calling

This study included 1,059 peach (P. persica) accessions, comprising 400 natural populations and 4 biparental populations. The natural populations were collected from China, the USA, Japan, South Africa, Russia, France, Italy, Canada, and Korea, consisting of wild relatives and local or breeding cultivars (Yu et al. 2018; Cao et al. 2019; Li et al. 2019; Guo et al. 2020; Guan et al. 2021). Seven high soluble solid content (SSC) parents were selected for hybridization, generating 4 biparental populations (653 plants) at the Xinxiang Comprehensive Experimental Base, Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences (CAAS) (Supplementary Table S17). Illumina resequencing data were processed using fastp and SNPs were identified through BWA-MEM (Jung and Han 2022) alignment and GATK (McKenna et al. 2010) filtering. 

# Genetic QTL mapping

SNPs from 160 individuals in the “wen_07-2-30” × “wen_07-2-47” population (160) were used for QTL mapping. The parental lines exhibited contrasting sugar accumulation: “wen_07-2-30” had an SSC of $1 0 \%$ , while “wen_07-2-47” had an SSC of $1 7 \%$ . The hybrid progeny showed a broad SSC range, averaging $1 2 \%$ in 2020 $( 7 . 7 \%$ to $1 7 \%$ ) and $1 2 . 1 \%$ in 2021 $8 . 4 \%$ to $1 9 . 6 \%$ ). Multiyear normalized SSC values, adjusted for year-specific effects, were used as phenotypic data to account for environmental variation. 

SNPs were filtered using VCFtools (Danecek et al. 2011), and PLINK (Purcell et al. 2007), with common SNPs having a minor allele frequency $\ge 0 . 0 5$ . Genetic maps were constructed using Lep-MAP3 (Rastas 2017), and QTLs were mapped using qtl.R and qtlPerm.R, with the female parent map containing 8 linkage groups (Fig. 5A).
