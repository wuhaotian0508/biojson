# Progress in the dissection and manipulation of vitamin E synthesis

Dean DellaPenna

Department of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology, Michigan State University, East Lansing, MI 48824-1319, USA

Tocochromanols are a group of four tocopherols and four tocotrienols that collectively constitute vitamin E, an essential nutrient in the human diet. Tocochromanols are only synthesized in oxygenic photosynthetic organisms. Although they have similar antioxidant activities in vitro, the individual tocochromanols vary widely in their in vivo vitamin E activities. During the past several years, mutant and transgenic approaches in model organisms, particularly in Arabidopsis thaliana and Synechocystis sp. PCC6803, have allowed the full complement of core pathway genes to be isolated and studied. Pathway engineering with these genes has provided significant insights into the molecular genetic and biochemical control of tocochromanol biosynthesis in plants. The resulting knowledge base is driving efforts to manipulate the levels of this essential nutrient in staple crops for populations in the developed and developing world.

# Introduction

Tocochromanols are amphipathic molecules – the general structure is shown in Figure 1. Tocochromanols are comprised of two groups, tocopherols and tocotrienols, which differ from each other in the degree of saturation of their hydrophobic tails. Tocopherols having a fully saturated tail derived from phytyl diphosphate (PDP) and tocotrienols having an unsaturated tail derived from geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGDP). a-, $\beta \cdot$ -, g- and d-tocochromanols differ only in the number and position of methyl substituents on the aromatic ring. As a group, the eight tocochromanols have similar lipid-soluble antioxidant activities in vitro but the in vivo vitamin E activity of individual tocochromanols differ by orders of magnitude, with $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol having the highest vitamin E activity. This difference is not because of the preferential absorption of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol during digestion, all tocochromanols are absorbed equally, but because of the preferential retention of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol by a hepatic $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol transfer protein $\scriptstyle { \alpha }$ -TTP), which shows a marked preference for $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol. The importance of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -TTP binding affinity is reflected in the vitamin E activity of each tocochromanol (Figure 1) [1] and the severe phenotype of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -TTP null mice [2,3].

Plant tissues have tremendous natural variation in their tocochromanol content and composition and, hence, in their vitamin E activities. Photosynthetic tissues

contain $1 0 { - } 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g }$ tocochromanols/g fresh weight, with the predominant form being $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol, whereas seeds range from 300 to $2 0 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } }$ tocochromanols/g oil, with $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol often only a minor component [4]. In spite of the relatively low $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol content, seed oils still represent the major source of vitamin E in the American diet because of the high level of intake of vegetable oils.

The plant tocochromanol biosynthetic pathway uses cytosolic aromatic amino metabolism for head group synthesis and the plastidic deoxyxylulose 5-phosphate pathway for hydrophobic tail synthesis (Figure 2). The committed step in head group synthesis is the production of homogentisic acid (HGA) from $p$ -hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid (HPP) by $p$ -hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid dioxygenase (HPPD) [5–7]. HGA is prenylated with either PDP or GGDP to yield the first committed intermediates in tocopherol and tocotrienol synthesis, 2-methyl-6-phytylplastoquinol (MPBQ) and 2-methyl-6-geranylgeranylplastoquinol (MGGBQ), respectively. MPBQ and MGGBQ are substrates for either tocopherol cyclase or MPBQ methyltransferase (MPBQ MT). MPBQ MT adds a second methyl group to MPBQ to form 2,3-dimethyl-5-phytyl-1,4-benzoquinone (DMPBQ) or to MGGBQ to form 2,3-dimethyl-5- geranylgeranyl-1,4-benzoquinone (DMGGBQ). Tocopherol cyclase converts MPBQ and DMPBQ to d- and $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -tocopherols, respectively, and corresponding geranylgeranylated intermediates to d- and $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -tocotrienols. Finally, $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -tocopherol methyltransferase (g-TMT) adds a methyl group to C-6 of the chromanol ring, converting d- and $\Upsilon$ -tocopherols and tocotrienols to $\beta \cdot$ and $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherols and tocotrienols, respectively. This review will focus on progress in our molecular and genetic understanding of the tocochromanol biosynthetic pathway in recent years, primarily in the model photosynthetic organisms Arabidopsis thaliana and Synechocystis sp. PCC6803. When appropriate, highlights of ongoing efforts to apply this knowledge to manipulate levels of this essential nutrient in food crops will be discussed.

# Synthesis of homogentisic acid, the tocochromanol aromatic headgroup

The first step of the plant tocochromanol biosynthetic pathway to be cloned was $p$ -hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid dioxygenase (HPPD), the product of the PDS1 locus in Arabidopsis (At1g06590). HPPD catalyzes synthesis of the aromatic compound HGA from HPP, which in turn is produced in plants from tyrosine by the activity of tyrosine amino transferase (TAT) (Figure 2a). Mutation of the Arabidopsis PDS1 locus conclusively demonstrated that HPPD activity was essential for both tocochromanol and plastoquinone biosynthesis in plants [5,6]. Given the key location of HPPD in the tocochromanol pathway, it seems logical that it might be an important activity regulating pathway flux. To test this hypothesis, HPPD was overexpressed in Arabidopsis, which resulted in a ${ } > 2 0$ -fold increase in protein and activity but only a $1 5 \%$ and $30 \%$ increase in seed and leaf tocopherols, respectively [23]. Similarly modest increases were obtained for overexpression of HPPD targeted to either the cytosol or plastid of tobacco [24,25]. Inducing TAT expression in Arabidopsis also had little effect on total leaf tocopherols [26]. These combined data indicated that although required, HPPD activity alone is unlikely to be a significant limitation for tocopherol flux in Arabidopsis and tobacco.

A different approach can be used to increase flux to HGA: bypassing the feedback inhibition of arogenate dehydrogenase by its product tyrosine yielded surprising results [27,28]. A Saccharomyces cerevisiae or Erwinia herbicola feedback-insensitive, bifunctional prephenate dehydrogenase (TyrA) that catalyzes production of HPP directly from prephenate was engineered for expression in plants (Figure 2a). TyrA expression alone had minor effects on tocochromanol levels but when combined with overexpression of Arabidopsis HPPD resulted in an eightfold increase in total tocochromanol levels in tobacco leaves [28] and two- and threefold tocochromanol increases in Arabidopsis and soybean seed, respectively [27]. The simultaneous overexpression of TyrA and HPPD are required for large increases in tocochromanols, which suggests that flux to HGA (i.e. HPPD) is indeed limiting but also requires increased flux to HPP. In all cases, the increased tocochromanols in TyrACHPPD overexpressers were almost entirely because of tocotrienols, which are normally produced in tobacco seed but not in tobacco leaves, they are absent from Arabidopsis tissues and are present in trace components in soybean seed. The available literature suggests that tocotrienol accumulation in tissues that normally accumulate tocopherols is because of induction of a homogentisate geranylgeranyl transferase (HGGT), but not homogentisate phytyltransferase (HPT), by TyrACHPPD co-overexpression, or that the high level of HGA in TyrACHPPD overexpressers somehow alters the substrate specificity of HPT such that it is able to use GGDP as a cosubstrate [9,11,14,17,18,20,27–30]. It is also important to note that Arabidopsis and soybean seed overexpressing both TyrA and HPPD were black as a result of oxidative

polymerization of the extremely high HGA levels (60- and 800-fold higher, respectively, than the wild-type seed) and germination was impaired as a result [27].

# Prenylation of homogentisic acid in tocochromanol synthesis

Homogentisate prenyl transferases catalyze the first committed step in tocochromanol synthesis: tocopherols if PDP is a co-substrate, tocotrienols if GGDP is a cosubstrate. Approaches using Synechocystis and Arabidopsis whole genome sequence information were crucial for the initial isolation and characterization of HPTs by various groups [9,11,14,20]. It was hypothesized that HPTs would show some primary sequence similarity to related prenyltransferases that use similar prenyl-diphosphates as substrates. Chlorophyll synthase (ChlG) is one such enzyme that attaches PDP or GGDP to chlorophyllide during chlorophyll synthesis [31,32]. Querying the Synechocystis genome database with Synechocystis ChlG identified several related polyprenyltransferases, including slr1736, which has $\sim 2 0 \%$ identity to ChlG. Disruption of the slr1736 locus by homologous recombination resulted in deficiency of all tocopherols and prenylated pathway intermediates. An Arabidopsis ortholog was subsequently isolated (VTE2, At2g18950) and found to only use PDP as a co-substrate whereas SLR1736 could use both PDP and GGDP, an intriguing result given that Synechocystis does not accumulate tocotrienols. A null mutation at the Arabidopsis VTE2 locus resulted in complete tocopherol deficiency in both leaves and seeds, indicating that VTE2 is the only activity in wild-type Arabidopsis responsible for the synthesis of tocopherols [18]. Overexpression of VTE2 increased total tocopherol levels up to fivefold and twofold in leaves and seeds, respectively, indicating that it is a limiting activity for tocochromanol synthesis in these tissues [11,14,29]. The enzyme activity was also found to be limiting for leaf tocopherol synthesis in response to combined high light and nutrient stress [30]. Co-overexpression of VTE2 and VTE4 [g-tocopherol methyl transferase $\cdot \Upsilon$ -TMT), described below] were additive traits, that is, one can increase total tocopherols in seed by increasing VTE2 activity and simultaneously convert virtually all the $\Upsilon$ -tocopherol to $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol by increasing $\Upsilon$ -TMT, resulting in a nearly 12-fold increase in the vitamin E activity [30]. Increases in the content and types of tocochromanols can also be engineered in agricultural crops using these genes [17,27].

# Methyltransferases of tocochromanol synthesis

The two methyltransferases of the tocochromanol pathway, MPBQ MT and $\Upsilon$ -TMT, do not appear to be key activities regulating total pathway flux but their activities and substrate specificities are essential for determining the types of tocochromanols made and, hence, the vitamin E activity of a given tissue. Arabidopsis HPPD provided a genomic stepping-stone for the cloning and functional analysis of $\Upsilon$ -TMT from both Synechocystis and Arabidopsis [15]. In brief, a Synechocystis HPPD ortholog (slr0090) was identified in the Synechocystis genome

database using the Arabidopsis HPPD sequence as query. $\mathbf { s } \mathbf { l r } 0 0 9 0$ is in a putative ten-gene operon and it was hypothesized that other tocopherol biosynthetic enzymes might also be present in the operon. Disruption of the adjacent $\mathbf { s } \mathbf { l r } 0 0 8 9$ gene by homologous recombination resulted in loss of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol and accumulation of $\Upsilon$ -tocopherol, the anticipated phenotype for loss of $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -TMT activity. Subsequent expression of SLR0089 and the Arabidopsis $\Upsilon$ -TMT ortholog (VTE4, At1g64970) in E. coli conclusively demonstrated the activities and substrate specificities of the enzymes. Arabidopsis seed contains $\sim 9 5 \%$ g-tocopherol; seed-specific overexpression of VTE4 resulted in the near-complete conversion of $\Upsilon$ -tocopherol to $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol (an 80-fold increase in $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol) and a ninefold increase in vitamin E activity [15]. These results have since been extended to soybean seed where seed-specific overexpression of Arabidopsis $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -TMT and MPBQ MT (described below) resulted in nearcomplete conversion of b, $\Upsilon$ and d-tocopherols to $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol, with a nearly fivefold increase in the vitamin E activity of soybean oil [17]. These combined experiments provide a graphic example of the relevance of studies in model systems to agricultural crops.

MPBQ MT was the last enzyme of the tocopherol pathway to be cloned from Synechocystis sp. PCC6803 (sll0418) and Arabidopsis (VTE3). The Synechocystis gene was identified based on sequence similarity to $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -TMT and enzymatic analysis of E. coli expressed protein [10,16]. Unlike all other steps of the tocochromanol pathway, the cyanobacterial sequence was not useful in identifying a plant ortholog. Instead, two research groups used mapbased cloning approaches to isolate partial loss-of-function alleles for MPBQ MT (VTE3, At3g63410) from ethyl methane sulfonate-mutagenized Arabidopsis [10,17]. Although VTE3 was found to have less than $20 \%$

amino acid identity to SLL0418, both had similar activities [10] – a clear case of convergent evolution for this step of the pathway. Why two evolutionarily unrelated genes catalyze this reaction, whereas the rest of genes in the tocopherol biosynthetic pathway are conserved in cyanobacteria and plants, remains an open question.

# Tocopherol cyclase enzyme: an intriguing enzyme in tocochromanol synthesis

The tocopherol cyclase was identified in Arabidopsis based on mutations that eliminated the synthesis of tocopherols but resulted in accumulation of the pathway intermediate DMPBQ in leaves and seeds (Figure 2b) [19,21]. The mutated gene (At4g32770, the VTE1 locus) was subsequently isolated by chromosome walking and characterized by assay of protein expressed in E. coli. A Synechocystis ortholog, slr1737, was identified in a two open-readingframe operon with the HPT gene (slr1736). Even more surprising, a maize tocopherol cyclase ortholog had been cloned and studied in detail more than two years before the isolation of Arabidopsis VTE1 [33]. This maize gene, originally designated SXD1 for sucrose export defective 1, disrupts carbohydrate translocation in maize seedlings when mutated owing to the formation of aberrant plasmodesmata between bundle sheath and vascular parenchyma cells in mature leaf tissues. It is only retrospectively that SXD1 has been shown to have tocopherol cyclase activity [19]. These data are intriguing and suggest tocopherol cyclase activity is somehow required for regulating carbon translocation from source to sink tissues in maize. Whether the pleiotropic sxd1 phenotype is due to the accumulation of the pathway intermediate DMPBQ, the absence of tocopherols or the production of other molecules because of the absence of tocopherols in sxd1 remains an open question. RNAi inhibition of potato (a dicot) tocopherol cyclase expression [34] resulted in a phenotype similar to maize sxd1 (a monocot), but the phenotype was not observed in Arabidopsis vte1 or vte2 under standard growth conditions [19] and it remains unclear whether or not the maize sxd1 phenotype defines a tocopherol cyclase function conserved in plants.

Overexpression of VTE1 in Arabidopsis leaves yielded surprising results: leaf tocochromanols were increased sevenfold solely as a result of $\Upsilon$ -tocopherol, rather than $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol, the major tocochromanol normally found in Arabidopsis leaves [35] (Figure 3). This led to the conclusion that $\Upsilon$ -TMT is limiting in VTE1-overexpressing leaves [35], which is puzzling because previous work overexpressing HPT and $\Upsilon$ -TMT singly and in combination clearly demonstrated that $\Upsilon$ -TMT activity was not limiting in Arabidopsis leaves, except during severe abiotic stress [15,29,30] (Figure 3). An alternative explanation that VTE1 overexpression is perceived as or causes stress was not considered despite the levels of two major water-soluble antioxidants, ascorbate and glutathione, being reduced $60 \%$ and $40 \%$ , respectively, in VTE1 overexpressing leaves relative to the wild type [35]. This reduction is particularly significant because of the 100- and 1000-fold molar excesses of leaf ascorbate and glutathione, respectively, relative to tocopherols.

# Open questions in tocopherol research

Isolation and characterization of the full set of core tocochromanol biosynthetic genes during the past decade has contributed much to our fundamental understanding of genetic, molecular and biochemical aspects of tocochromanol synthesis in photosynthetic organisms. Although much has been learned, many open questions regarding tocochromanol synthesis and function in plants remain. Why is it possible to engineer large fluxes to tocotrienols in seeds but relatively difficult to do the same for tocopherols, particularly $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tocopherol, the most active form of Vitamin E? How and why does TyrACHPPD co-overexpression result in elevated tocotrienols, even in organisms and tissues that normally do not produce tocotrienols? Why are tocopherols not produced as well? How and why does overexpression of tocopherol cyclase specifically increase $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -tocopherol levels and decrease ascorbate and glutathione levels in leaves?

With regard to tocochromanol function(s), there is even more to do. In animal systems, tocopherols have well defined antioxidant and non-antioxidant functions. The non-antioxidant functions include alterations in the synthesis of lipid-derived signaling molecules, membrane-associated signaling pathways and gene expression (reviewed in Refs [36,37]). One can propose a multitude of analogous non-antioxidant tocopherol functions in plants [38–41] but experimental evidence to support such speculation is nonexistent. The only experimentally defined plant tocochromanol function to date is in protecting storage lipids from oxidation during dormancy and germination in Arabidopsis [18]. vte2 mutants, but not vte1 mutants, exhibit massive oxidation of storage lipids during germination and severely reduced fitness. The DMPBQ accumulated by vte1 can compensate for the lack of tocopherols in this regard. Tocochromanol biosynthetic mutants will be crucial for functional studies in photosynthetic organisms and are already providing interesting and unanticipated results. Tocopherol-deficient Synechocystis mutants show surprisingly robust phenotypes compared with the wild type [11,20,22,42]. Similarly, mature vte4, vte2 and vte1 plants are indistinguishable from wild type and have thus far shown little or no phenotypic response to selected abiotic stresses, including high light and no obvious increase in lipid peroxidation [18,19,21,35,43]. This lack of obvious phenotypes runs counter to what one would predict from the published literature on tocochromanols and plant stress and suggests that although some tocochromanol functions are shared between plants and animals, many aspects might differ, and further studies of tocochromanol function in photosynthetic organisms might yield some surprises.

# Thoughts for the future

The past several years have seen tremendous progress in our understanding of genetic, molecular and biochemical aspects of tocopherol synthesis in photosynthetic organisms. Studies in Arabidopsis thaliana and Synechocystis sp. PCC68033 have been particularly important in this regard and provide an example of how basic research on plant metabolism in model organisms can have a direct impact on traits important for agricultural crops and

human health. Extending such work to include the many tens of thousands of plant compounds in the diet that also impact human health holds great promise for advancing our fundamental understanding of the interface of plant metabolism and human biology. In the near future, the availability of the full suite of tocochromanol biosynthetic genes should provide ongoing opportunities to engineer or breed for crops with specific tocopherol and/or tocotrienol content and composition for agriculture, industry or human nutritional applications. One of the greatest potential uses of this research will be to address vitamin E deficiencies in diets in the developing world through the food supply.
