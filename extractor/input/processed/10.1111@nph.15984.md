# Summary

Conifers have evolved complex oleoresin terpene defenses against herbivores and pathogens. In co-evolved bark beetles, conifer terpenes also serve chemo-ecological functions as pheromone precursors, chemical barcodes for host identification, or nutrients for insect-associated microbiomes. We highlight the genomic, molecular and biochemical underpinnings of the large chemical space of conifer oleoresin terpenes and volatiles. Conifer terpenes are predominantly the products of the conifer terpene synthase (TPS) gene family. Terpene diversity is increased by cytochromes P450 of the CYP720B class. Many conifer TPS are multiproduct enzymes. Multisubstrate CYP720B enzymes catalyse multistep oxidations. We summarise known terpenoid gene functions in various different conifer species with reference to the annotated terpenoid gene space in a spruce genome. Overall, biosynthesis of terpene diversity in conifers is achieved through a system of biochemical radiation and metabolic grids. Expression of TPS and CYP720B genes can be specific to individual cell types of constitutive or traumatic resin duct systems. Induced terpenoid transcriptomes in resin duct cells lead to dynamic changes of terpene composition and quantity to fend off herbivores and pathogens. While terpenoid defenses have contributed much to the evolutionary success of conifers, under new conditions of climate change, these defences may become inconsequential against range-expanding forest pests.

# I. General introduction

Conifers dominate many of the world's forest ecosystems, some of which are experiencing increased pressure from the related effects of forest pests and climate change. To cope with herbivores and pathogens, conifers have evolved complex biochemical defense systems. Herbivores that affect conifers range from small insects such as bark beetles, weevils, sawflies, aphids and budworms to large ungulates such as moose, deer and elk. A signature component of the conifer defense system is the terpene-rich oleoresin (Langenheim, 1994; Phillips & Croteau, 1999; Keeling & Bohlmann, 2006a,b; Zulak & Bohlmann, 2010). Oleoresin terpenes accumulate in specialised anatomical structures such as resin ducts, resin vesicles, resin blisters, resin glands or resin cells (Fahn, 1988; Hudgins et al., 2004; Franceschi et al., 2005) (Fig. 1). In members of the Pinaceae, resin ducts are abundantly present in most parts of the tree. Damaging these structures as a result of feeding or oviposition exposes herbivores to the viscous oleoresin. Conifer oleoresin consists mostly of complex mixtures of dozens or hundreds of different monoterpenes, sesquiterpenes, and diterpenes (Fig. 2). In addition to chemical defense, oleoresin acts as a physical force against small herbivores. Beyond defense, terpenes have diverse roles in other chemo-ecological interactions of conifers with various co-evolved insects. For example, in bark beetles conifer terpenes serve as signal molecules in host identification; they play roles in the biosynthesis of insect pheromones; they affect insect behavior and activities such as feeding, aggregation and reproduction; they can directly or indirectly affect insect mortality as toxins, feeding barriers, or as attractants of parasitoids and predators (Seybold et al., 2006; Raffa, 2014).

Our knowledge of oleoresin terpene biosynthesis in conifers has been substantially expanded in recent years, in part based on the genome and transcriptome sequencing and annotation of several conifer species. For example, annotation of the white spruce (Picea glauca), interior spruce $(P.$ glauca $\times$ engelmannii $x$ sitchensis) and Sitka spruce $(P.$ sitchensis) transcriptomes, identified genes that cover much of the terpene biosynthetic system in spruce (Ralph et al., 2008; Warren et al., 2015; Celedon et al., 2017). Additional annotations of terpene biosynthetic systems was accomplished using the genome assemblies for white spruce, interior spruce, Norway spruce (Picea abies), loblolly pine (Pinus taeda) and sugar pine (Pinus lambertiana) (Birol et al., 2013; Nystedt et al., 2013; Neale et al., 2014; Wegrzyn et al., 2014; Zimin et al., 2014, 2017; Warren et al., 2015; Stevens et al., 2016). Annotations of terpene biosynthesis include the large terpene synthase gene family (TPS) and cytochrome P450 (P450) genes, whose functions contribute much of the chemical diversity of conifer terpenes (Hamberger et al., 2011; Keeling et al., 2011b; Warren et al., 2015; Geisler et al., 2016; Celedon et al., 2017). Beyond the Pinaceae, transcriptome analysis of yew (Taxus spp., Taxaceae), ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba, Ginkgoaceae) and western redcedar (Thuja plicata, Cupressaceae) provided additional insights into terpene biosynthesis across taxonomically diverse gymnosperms (Hao et al., 2011; Wu et al., 2011; Li et al., 2012; Sun et al., 2013; Shalev et al., 2018).

# II. Scope of this review

In this review, we provide an update on terpene defenses in conifers with an emphasis on the molecular and biochemical origins of terpene diversity. This diversity is explained with recent information from genomic and biochemical studies into functions and evolution of the conifer TPS and P450 gene families. This review builds, in part, on an earlier Tansley review (Keeling & Bohlmann, 2006a) that preceded the sequencing of a conifer genome. Over the past decade, biochemical functions of many conifer TPS and P450s

have been elucidated, highlighting how conifers produce a diversity of oleoresin terpenes under constitutive or biotic stress conditions. This review will focus on oleoresin terpene biosynthesis in Pinaceae, with select additional references to other conifers (e.g. Cupressaceae) and nonresin terpenes (e.g. hemiterpenes, taxol) for comparison. This review does not attempt to provide a complete catalog of the many papers published on the phytochemistry of conifer terpenes. We will only highlight a few selected studies on the chemical ecology of terpenes in conifers, a topic that has been covered in excellent previous reviews (e.g. Mumm & Hilker, 2006; Raffa, 2014).

Oleoresin terpene defense systems have evolved in conifers over hundreds of millions of years to protect the tree against most herbivores. However, these defenses can be overcome by co-evolved pests such as bark beetles or weevils. As an example, we will discuss recent discoveries of P450 genes and enzymes in the mountain pine beetle (Dendroctonus ponderosae) that allows the insect to cope with and co-opt terpene defenses of the host. Recent studies in the mountain pine beetle system also showed that the effectiveness of terpene defenses is limited when bark beetle populations increase from endemic to epidemic outbreak dimensions (Boone et al., 2011; Raffa et al., 2013; Burke & Carroll, 2016). Under changing climatic conditions, and with global transport of plant disease agents, conifer forest are also becoming exposed to new pests. The effectiveness of terpene defenses against range-expanding, invasive or newly introduced pests is often unknown. Conversely, conifer species whose oleoresin defenses may be of limited effect against co-evolved pests in their native environment are being planted in other parts of the world, where they are temporarily free from attack. An example is the commercial forestry with Sitka spruce in the UK, using genetic material that is highly susceptible to the spruce weevil (Pissodes strobi) in the native range of the tree in the Pacific Northwest of North America (Whitehill et al., 2019).

# III. Formation of isoprenyl diphosphates for oleoresin terpene biosynthesis

The biosynthesis of the hundreds of different terpenes of conifer oleoresin is rooted in the two isoprenoid molecules isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) (Fig. 2). IPP and DMAPP originate from the methylylthritol phosphate (MEP) and mevalonic acid (MEV) pathways (Fig. 3). Two or more of these five-carbon $(\mathrm{C}_5)$ metabolites are conjugated by isoprenyl diphosphate synthases (IDS) to produce the longer-chain $(\mathrm{C}_{10},\mathrm{C}_{15},\mathrm{C}_{20})$ substrates for terpene synthases (TPS). MEP and MEV pathway genes and their functions are widely conserved (Vranova et al., 2013), and several genes of these pathways have been described in conifers and other gymnosperms. Genes for four out of seven steps in the MEP pathway, DXS, MDS, HDS and HDR, have been characterised in ginkgo (Kim et al., 2005, 2006, 2008; Kim & Kim, 2010). In Pinus densiflora, DXS and HDR genes have been functionally characterised and shown to be methyl jasmonate (MeJA) inducible (Kim et al., 2009). In Norway spruce, functions of three different DXS genes are known (Phillips et al., 2007). Two different DXS type-II genes and DXR and HDR genes were MeJA inducible in Norway spruce cell cultures (Phillips et al.,

The MEP pathway supports predominantly monoterpene and diterpene biosynthesis, which are the major components of the conifer oleoresin, while the MEV pathway provides precursors for sesquiterpenes which are less abundant in the oleoresin. However, work in angiosperms showed that the plastid MEP and cytosolic MEV pathways are not strictly separated (Lange et al., 2000; Tholl, 2015). Recent work in Arabidopsis thaliana also discovered an isopentenyl phosphate kinase (AtIPK) to recover IPP for terpene biosynthesis from cytosolic isopentenyl phosphate (IP), which is produced by Nudix superfamily hydrolase activity (Henry et al., 2015, 2018). The white spruce genome contains a gene with $65\%$ predicted protein sequence identity to AtIPK.

Different IDS catalyse the elongation of DMAPP with one, two or three IPP units into, respectively, 10-carbon $(\mathrm{C}_{10})$ , 15-carbon $(\mathrm{C}_{15})$ and 20-carbon $(\mathrm{C}_{20})$ isoprenyl diphosphates. Geranyl diphosphate (GPP, $\mathrm{C}_{10}$ ), farnesyl diphosphate (FPP, $\mathrm{C}_{15}$ ), and geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP, $\mathrm{C}_{20}$ ) are the common intermediates in the biosynthesis of oleoresin monoterpenes, sesquiterpenes and diterpenes, respectively (Figs 2, 3). Gymnosperm genes for GPP synthase (GPPS), FPP synthase (FPPS) and GGPP synthase (GGPPS) were first characterised in yew and grand fir (Hefner et al., 1998; Tholl et al., 2001; Burke & Croteau, 2002). Annotation of the white spruce (PG29) genome and transcriptomes identified 12 IDS genes, including one putative GPPS, two putative FPPS and nine putative GGPPS, but their functions have not yet been experimentally confirmed (Warren et al., 2015). The enzymes encoded by several IDS genes, namely $PaIDS1$ (bifunctional GGPPS/GPPS activity), $PaIDS2$ (GPPS activity), $PaIDS4$ (FPPS activity), $PaIDS5$ (GGPPS activity), and $PaIDS6$ (GGPPS), were characterised in Norway spruce (Schmidt & Gershenzon, 2007, 2008; Schmidt et al., 2010, 2011). $PaIDS$ transcripts were induced by MeJA (Schmidt & Gershenzon, 2007, 2008; Schmidt et al., 2010, 2011). $PaIDS2$ and $PaIDS5$ were expressed in bark and xylem with patterns suggesting roles in oleoresin biosynthesis (Schmidt & Gershenzon, 2007, 2008). $PaIDS1$ is a bi-functional and homodimeric IDS that produces GPP and GGPP precursors for monoterpene and diterpene biosynthesis, respectively (Schmidt et al., 2010). $PaIDS1$ transcript levels were strongly increased in bark and xylem after MeJA treatment, and immunolabeling of xylem tissues localised $PaIDS1$ in the epithelial cells of traumatic resin ducts (TRDs) (Schmidt et al., 2010).

# IV. Conifer terpene synthases

TPS generate the many different cyclic or acyclic core structures of conifer terpenes, and these enzymes contribute the most to the structural diversity of oleoresin terpenes (Keeling & Bohlmann, 2006b; Keeling et al., 2008, 2010, 2011a,b; Hall et al., 2011, 2013a,b; Zerbe et al., 2012a,b; Roach et al., 2014; Zerbe & Bohlmann, 2014, 2015). Conifer TPS include hemiterpene synthases (hemi-TPS), monoterpene synthases (mono-TPS), sesquiterpene synthases (sesqui-TPS) and diterpene synthases (diTPS), which use DMAPP, GPP, FPP and GGPP, respectively, as substrates (Figs 2, 4). Conifer TPS genes are members of large gene families that have been a topic of previous review articles and conifer genome analyses (Bohlmann et al., 1998b; Martin et al.,

2004; Keeling & Bohlmann, 2006b; Chen et al., 2011; Keeling et al., 2011b; Warren et al., 2015; Shalev et al., 2018). Within the plant TPS family, the conifer TPS of specialised (i.e. secondary) metabolism belong to the gymnosperm-specific TPS-d subfamily. Conifer diterpene synthases of general metabolism (i.e. gibberellin phytohormone biosynthesis) are members of the TPS-c and TPS-e subfamilies. Conifer TPS share a conserved three dimensional structure with either one (class-I TPS and class-II TPS) or two active sites (class-I/II TPS) (Chen et al., 2011; Zhou et al., 2012). Variations of the TPS structure appear to have evolved from an ancestral class-I/II TPS composed of three domains, namely the $\alpha$ , $\beta$ and $\gamma$ domains (Fig. 5).

Genome- and transcriptome-wide annotation of conifer TPS is currently most advanced in white spruce (Warren et al., 2015; Celedon et al., 2017). The white spruce (PG29, which is a genetic admix of $P.$ glauca, $P.$ engelmannii and $P.$ sitchensis) $PgesTPS$ family contains at least 55 different genes, plus 28 apparent pseudogenes. Specific biochemical functions of seven $PgesTPS$ and over 30 TPS in several other spruce species, including Norway spruce and Sitka spruce, have been characterised and their contributions to the oleoresin metabolome described (Fig. 4; Supporting Information Table S1). Functions of at least 112 TPS are known across all conifers (Table S1). These numbers only include TPS genes for which functions have been experimentally validated. As TPS functions cannot be accurately predicted based on sequence analyses alone, annotations that are only based on sequence comparisons are not included.

# 1. Hemiterpenes and hemiterpene synthases

Conifer hemi-TPS are composed of $\alpha$ - and $\beta$ -domains with a class-I active site that converts DMAPP into the linear hemiterpenes $(\mathrm{C}_5)$ isoprene and 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol (MBO). Homology modelling of Pinus sabiniana MBO synthase in comparison with the closely related Norway spruce linalool synthase, a mono-TPS, showed differences in the active site that control substrate preferences for DMAPP $(\mathrm{C}_5)$ and GPP $(\mathrm{C}_{10})$ , respectively (Gray et al., 2011). Conifer hemi-TPS appear to have evolved from conifer mono-TPS of the TPS-d1 clade and presumably arose independently of angiosperm hemi-TPS (Sharkey et al., 2013) (Gray et al., 2011). Annotations of the white spruce genome and transcriptomes identified only one putative hemi-TPS (Warren et al., 2015).

Hemiterpenes are not a significant component of conifer oleoresin but, along with other volatile terpenes, are emitted from conifer needles. Hemiterpenes serve physiological and ecological roles in protection against abiotic stress, and emission of hemiterpenes and other volatiles from conifers play a considerable role in global carbon cycles and atmospheric processes (Niinemets et al., 2004; Loreto & Schnitzler, 2010; Sharkey et al., 2013). Although it is not known which cell type of conifer needles contribute to hemiterpene biosynthesis, formation in mesophyll cells and release through stomata seems plausible (Harley et al., 2014). # 2. Monoterpene and monoterpene synthases

Conifer mono-TPS also contain the $\alpha$ - and $\beta$ -domain structure and a class-I active site that converts GPP into cyclic or acyclic monoterpenes (Figs 2, 4a). Monoterpenes accumulate as major components of the oleoresin and are also emitted as volatiles. The abundance and diversity of dozens of different monoterpenes in

conifers, and their roles in conifer herbivore interactions, prompted the cloning and characterisation of mono-TPS in several conifer species (Table S1). For reference, the white spruce genome annotation identified 24 mono-TPS genes (Warren et al., 2015), but only four have been functionally characterised including 1,8-cineole synthase, $(-)$ -linalool synthase, $(-)$ - $\alpha$ -pinene synthase, and $(-)$ - $\beta$ -pinene synthase (Keeling et al., 2011b).

Characterisation of conifer mono-TPS genes began in the 1990s in grand fir (Bohlmann et al., 1997, 1999), followed by systematic analysis of mono-TPS in Norway spruce (Faldt et al., 2003; Martin

et al., 2004; Zulak et al., 2009) and Sitka spruce (Byun-McKay et al., 2006; Hall et al., 2011; Keeling et al., 2011b) in parallel with the characterisation of select mono-TPS in other conifer species (Table S1). More recent work identified mono-TPS genes and their functions in the mountain pine beetle hosts lodgepole pine (Pinus contorta) and jack pine (Pinus banksiana) (Hall et al., 2013a,b). In grand fir, Norway spruce and Sitka spruce, mono-TPS have been characterised in the context of constitutive and induced oleoresin biosynthesis and volatile emissions. In these species, transcripts and enzyme activities of mono-TPS are upregulated in stems or needles in response to mechanical wounding, MeJA treatment or insect feeding (Steele et al., 1998b; Byun-McKay et al., 2003; Faldt et al., 2003; Miller et al., 2005; Byun-McKay et al., 2006; Zulak et al., 2009; Abbott et al., 2010; Hall et al., 2011). In general, these studies revealed that the majority of conifer mono-TPS are multiproduct and stereospecific enzymes, which are induced by real or simulated insect feeding or oviposition. In Norway spruce and Sitka spruce, as well as in lodgepole pine and jack pine, the products of cloned and characterised mono-TPS account for many of the major and minor oleoresin monoterpenes of these four species.

Research on the role of mono-TPS in Sitka spruce resistance to the spruce weevil (Pissodes strobi) revealed intraspecific copy-number and functional variation among three different $(+)$ -3-carene synthase (PsTPS-3car) genes and a closely related $(-)$ -sabinene synthase (PsTPS-sab) gene (Hall et al., 2011; Roach et al., 2014). The bicyclic monoterpene $(+)$ -3-carene is associated with weevil resistance in Sitka spruce (Robert et al., 2010). The three mono-TPS genes PsTPS-3car1, -2 and -3 differed with regard to their presence/absence in the genomes, transcriptomes and proteomes of a weevil-resistant and a weevil-susceptible Sitka spruce genotype. The expressed enzymes varied in their catalytic efficiencies of $(+)$ -3-carene formation. The presence and activity of PsTPS-3car2 in the weevil-resistant Sitka spruce genotype accounted for the relatively high levels of $(+)$ -3-carene, while its absence explained the very low levels of $(+)$ -3-carene in the susceptible genotype (Hall et al., 2011). Structure/function analysis revealed that variations in one or a few amino acid positions played a major role in defining the formation of $(+)$ -3-carene vs $(-)$ -sabinene. Minor sequence variations also affected the catalytic efficiency of the different PsTPS-3car and PsTPS-sab enzymes (Roach et al., 2014). These studies highlighted, as an example with just four genes, the plasticity of multiproduct TPS enzymes and their contribution to monoterpene variations in conifer oleoresin and resistance to an insect pest.

Characterisation of 17 different mono-TPS and monoterpene profiles in different parts of lodgepole pine and jack pine identified orthologous sets of enzymes that contribute to the biosynthesis of oleoresin monoterpenes such as $(+)$ - $\alpha$ -pinene, $(-)$ - $\alpha$ -pinene, $(-)$ - $\beta$ -pinene, $(+)$ -3-carene, and $(-)$ - $\beta$ -phellandrene (Hall et al., 2013a). The monoterpene $(-)$ - $\beta$ -phellandrene is typically the most abundant in the oleoresin of lodgepole pine, but is less abundant in jack pine.

Among the Cupressaceae, western redcedar (Thuja plicata) has been established as a system to study the variation of monoterpene biosynthesis and accumulation in foliage and their roles in deterring ungulate browsing. The oxidised monoterpene $\alpha$ -thujone dominates the terpene profile of western redcedar foliage and has been

associated with herbivore deterrence (Vourc'h et al., 2002). Thujones are biosynthesised from sabinene, and a western reedcedar mono-TPS encoding $(+)$ -sabinene synthase has been cloned and characterised with highly preferential expression in the epithelial cells of foliage resin glands (Foster et al., 2013). A recently produced reference transcriptome for western reedcedar contains 33 full-length TPS sequences, the functions of all but one of which remain to be discovered (Shalev et al., 2018).

# 3. Sesquiterpenes and sesquiterpene synthases

Conifer sesqui-TPS can be classified in two types according to their domain structure as $\beta \alpha$ - or $\gamma \beta \alpha$ -sesqui-TPS. Both types have a class-I active site that catalyses the formation of cyclic or acyclic sesquiterpenes (Fig. 2). Sesquiterpenes are generally of low abundance in the oleoresin relative to mono- and diterpenes, but are prominent in the volatiles emitted from spruce needles upon MeJA treatment or herbivory (Martin et al., 2003; Miller et al., 2005). Specific roles of sesquiterpenes in conifer defense remain mostly unknown. The sesquiterpene $(E)$ - $\alpha$ -bisabolene can act as precursors for insect juvenile hormone III analogues (Bohlmann et al., 1998a). Conifer sesquiterpenes may also serve as signals to attract insect parasitoids in tri-trophic interactions (Köpke et al., 2008, 2010).

As with mono-TPS, the first conifer sesqui-TPS were cloned and characterised from grand fir (Bohlmann et al., 1998b; Steele et al., 1998a,b), followed by discovery and characterisation of sesqui-TPS genes in species of spruce, pine, Douglas fir, ginkgo and Alaska cedar (Callitropsis nootkatensis) (Table S1). The characterisation of the grand fir sesqui-TPS $\gamma$ -humulene synthase and $\delta$ -selinene synthase (Steele et al., 1998a,b) highlighted the multiproduct nature of TPS enzymes, which was later recognised to be a common feature of many plant TPS (Chen et al., 2011).

Annotation of sesqui-TPS in the white spruce genome and transcriptome revealed 22 genes (Warren et al., 2015), of which only one, $\alpha$ -humulene synthase, has been functionally characterised (Keeling et al., 2011b) (Fig. 4b). Characterised sesqui-TPS genes in other spruce species include $(E,E)$ - $\alpha$ -farnesene synthase and longifolene synthase from interior spruce and a longipinene synthase from Sitka spruce (Keeling et al., 2011b). MeJA treatment of Sitka spruce and Norway spruce resulted in increased emissions of sesquiterpenes from needles, with $(E,E)$ - $\alpha$ -farnesene dominating the induced terpene volatiles in Sitka spruce (Miller et al., 2005). Weevil feeding on Sitka spruce stems induced accumulation of the sesquiterpenes $(E)$ -caryophyllene and aromadendrene in needle resin and longifolene and $\alpha$ -longipinene in the bark resin (Miller et al., 2005).

Four sesqui-TPS genes have been characterised in Scotts pine (P. sylvestris), a host species of the pine sawfly (Diprion pini) (Kopke et al., 2008, 2010). They produce $(E)$ - $\beta$ -caryophyllene and $\alpha$ -humulene (PsyTPS1), 1(10), 5-germacradiene-4-ol (PsyTPS2), longifolene and $\alpha$ -longipinene (PsyTPS3), and $(E)$ - $\beta$ -farnesene (PsyTPS5) (Kopke et al., 2008, 2010). Oviposition by pine sawfly induces increased sesqui-TPS transcripts (Kopke et al., 2008, 2010) and sesquiterpene emission from Scotts pine, which attracts the egg parasitoid Chrysonotomia rufoorum (Hilker et al., 2002, 2005). Sesquiterpenes such as bisabolene and farnesene have been studied for their potential use as precursors for biofuels (Peralta

Yahya et al., 2011). Engineered microbial systems expressing conifer sesquiterpene synthases have been explored as a platform for biofuels production (for review see Peralta-Yahya et al., 2012).

In Alaska cedar, a heartwood specific (+)-valence synthase is involved in the biosynthesis of (+)-nootkatone, which is used in the flavor and fragrance industry and in nature may confer resistance against fungal heartwood decay (Beekwilder et al., 2014).

# 4. Diterpenes and diterpene synthases

Diterpenes, mostly in the form of diterpene resin acids (DRAs), are major constituents of conifer oleoresin. Due to their high viscosity and polymerisation when exposed to air, they contribute to the trapping and encrusting of insects, and they can be toxic to insects and pathogens. Di-TPS typically have a conserved three-domain $\gamma \beta \alpha$ structure (Gao et al., 2012; Zhou et al., 2012) (Fig. 5). Conifer di-TPS exist as bifunctional enzymes with functional class-I and class-II active sites, or as monofunctional enzymes with either class-I or class-II active sites. The class-II active site is located at the interface of the $\beta$ and $\gamma$ domains and is characterised by the conserved DxD motif. The class-I active site is buried in the $\alpha$ -domain and is characterised by a DDxxD and NSE/DTE motifs that coordinate $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ ions. In a bifunctional di-TPS, the reaction cascade starts at the class-II active site with the protonation-initiated cyclisation of GGPP into bicyclic (+)-copyl diphosphate (CPP), followed by ionisation-dependent cyclisation and rearrangement reactions to generate the final diterpene product at the class-I active site (Fig. 5).

The first cloning and characterisation of a conifer di-TPS, a bifunctional class-I/II abietadiene synthase (later referred to as levopimaradiene/abietadiene synthase) from grand fir (AgLAS), served as the foundation for much of the current knowledge of the structure and function of di-TPS both in gymnosperms and angiosperms (Stofer Vogel et al., 1996; Peters et al., 2000; Peters & Croteau, 2002; Zhou et al., 2012). Most other cloned conifer di-TPS are also bifunctional (Table S1), including LAS and isopimaradiene synthases (Iso) from Norway spruce and Sitka spruce (Martin et al., 2004; Keeling et al., 2011b), loblolly pine (Ro & Bohlmann, 2006), and balsam fir (Abies balsamea) (Zerbe et al., 2012b). These di-TPS produce the diterpene scaffolds that serve as precursor for the most abundant DRAs in each species (Figs 2, 4c).

Initially, LAS was thought to be a single-product abietadiene synthase (Stofer Vogel et al., 1996) and later described as a multiproduct enzyme that produces the diterpene olefins $(-)$ -abietadiene, $(-)$ -levopimaradiene, and $(-)$ -neoabetadiene (Peters et al., 2000). However, in a detailed mechanistic study, Keeling et al. (2011a) discovered the actual product of LAS to be the diterpene alcohol 13-hydroxy-8(14)-abetene (Fig. 5). This unstable allylic alcohol can undergo dehydration resulting in the formation of $(-)$ -abetadiene, $(-)$ -levopimaradiene, $(-)$ -neoabetadiene, and $(-)$ -palustradiene (Keeling et al., 2011a). Both the diterpene alcohol 13-hydroxy-8(14)-abetene, as well as the four olefins $(-)$ -abetadiene, $(-)$ -levopimaradiene, $(-)$ -neoabetadiene, and $(-)$ -palustradiene, can serve as substrates for conifer P450s to produce a large set of diterpene alcohols, aldehydes and resin acids (Figs 2, 4c) (Hamberger et al., 2011; Geisler et al., 2016). In a comparative structure/function analysis of the Norway spruce LAS and Iso enzymes, Keeling et al.

(2008) found that just four amino acid positions are critical for the different products of these two closely related enzymes. Reciprocally changing these four amino acids converts multiproduct LAS into a single-product enzyme, and single-product Iso into a multiproduct enzyme (Keeling et al., 2008). These results demonstrated the capacity of conifers to evolve functionally diverse TPS genes that generate diverse terpene products through gene duplications and minor sequence divergence.

Until recently, the only functionally characterised monofunctional di-TPS in gymnosperms was taxadiene synthases from yew species (Taxusspp.) (Wildung & Croteau, 1996; Wang et al., 2002). This enzyme catalyses the first pathway specific step in the taxol biosynthetic system. Recent work in lodgepole pine and jack pine identified additional monofunctional di-TPSs that contained a functional class-I active site and a nonfunctional class-II active site (Hall et al., 2013b). These monofunctional di-TPS were characterised as isopimaradiene synthases and pimaradiene synthases, which contribute some of the lesser abundant compounds of the diterpene profile of lodgepole pine and jack pine (Hall et al., 2013b). Given the lack of a functional class-II active site, these monofunctional di-TPS require access to $(+)$ -CPP produced at the class-II active site of other di-TPS (Hall et al., 2013b). To date, no conifer di-TPS enzyme has been characterised as a monofunctional $(+)$ -CPP synthase, although the white spruce genome contains a putative CPP synthase (CPS) that lacks the DDxxD motif (Warren et al., 2015). The western redcedar transcriptome (Shalev et al., 2018) also revealed a putative monofunctional di-TPS. Phylogenetic analysis of pine monofunctional di-TPS of specialised metabolism suggest they evolved independently from the di-TPS genes of primary metabolism and independently from taxadiene synthase (Hall et al., 2013b).

Annotation of the white spruce genome and transcriptomes identified five, as of yet uncharacterised, di-TPS genes that resemble bifunctional di-TPS of oleoresin biosynthesis in other conifers, two di-TPS that have been characterised as ent-CPS and ent-kaurene synthase (KS) of gibberellin biosynthesis (Keeling et al., 2010), plus an additional gene putatively annotated as CPS-like (Warren et al., 2015). Sequence alignment of the spruce CPS and KS revealed shorter N-terminal sequences compared with the corresponding angiosperm enzymes, and the conifer CPS and KS lacked features of predicted plastid transit peptides.

Beyond oleoresin and gibberellin biosynthesis, products of various conifer di-TPSs and their derivatives are of interest as high-value bioproducts, including pharmaceuticals (Zerbe et al., 2013). Example are the anticancer drug taxol found in yew (Croteau et al., 2006), the antitumor compound pseudolaric acid B produced in the roots of Pseudolarix amabilis (Mafu et al., 2017), and cis-abienol as a molecule of interest in the fragrance industry produced in balsam fir (Zerbe et al., 2012b).

# V. Sequence phylogeny and structural relatedness of conifer terpene synthases

Based on sequence relatedness, all currently known conifer TPS of the TPS-d family appear to have originated from an ancestral bifunctional di-TPS through multiple events of gene duplications and neo- or subfunctionalisation (Keeling et al., 2010; Chen et al.,

2011) (Fig. 6). The elucidation of the structures of three conifer TPS provided additional information for our understanding of the evolution of conifer TPS (Köksal et al., 2011; McAndrew et al., 2011; Zhou et al., 2012). The structures of the two di-TPS, AgLAS from grand fir (Fig. 5) and taxadiene synthase from $T.$ brevifolia, and the sesqui-TPS $(E)$ - $\alpha$ -bisabolene synthase (AgBIS) from grand fir, suggested that the $\gamma\beta\alpha$ AgBIS may represent an evolutionary link between the three-domain $\gamma\beta\alpha$ di-TPS and the two-domain $\beta\alpha$ sesqui-TPSs (McAndrew et al., 2011) (Fig. 7). Other studies have shown that changes in the size of the class-I active site can affect substrate preference (Kampranis et al., 2007; Gray et al., 2011). To incorporate structure-function relationships in a reconstruction of the TPS-d family evolution, we performed a phylogenetic analysis with conifer TPS of known functions and annotated each sequence with the estimated size of its class-I active site and their predicted subcellular localisation (Fig. 6). The overall topology of the conifer TPS family was similar to phylogenies reported earlier (Keeling & Bohrmann, 2006a; Warren et al., 2015). In addition, we found a pattern of smaller class-I active sites for $\beta\alpha$ sesqui-TPS, mono-TPSs and hemi-TPS enzymes which use, respectively, the $C_{15}$ FPP, $C_{10}$ GPP and $C_5$ DMAPP as preferred substrates, compared with the larger class-I active sites of $\gamma\beta\alpha$ sesqui-TPS and $\gamma\beta\alpha$ di-TPS that use $C_{20}$ GGPP as substrate (Fig. 6). The nonrandom distribution of class-I active site size across the TPS-d phylogeny supports a model according to which reduction of the size of the class-I active site played a role in the evolution from $\gamma\beta\alpha$ di-TPS to $\beta\alpha$ sesqui-TPS, with intermediate $\gamma\beta\alpha$ sesqui-TPS. In agreement with a model of conifer TPS gene evolution proposed by Trapp & Croteau (2001) and by Peters and co-workers (Gao et al., 2012), the evolution of $\alpha\beta$ sesqui-TPS involved loss of the plastid targeting sequence, loss of the $\gamma$ -domain and reduction of the active site volume (Fig. 7).

We also assessed predicted subcellular localisation across the TPS-d family phylogeny (Fig. 6). Localisation of TPS in different subcellular compartments may effect access to different isoprenyl diphosphate substrates. It is commonly thought that mono-TPS and di-TPS are located in plastids and sesqui-TPS in the cytosol (Chen et al., 2011). Experimental evidence for conifer TPS localisation exists for $P$ taeda abietadiene synthase, which was shown to localise to plastids (Ro & Bohlmann, 2006). Sequence-based predictions (Sperschneider et al., 2017) indicated that among the characterised conifer TPS, several mono- and sesqui-TPS may also be targeted to mitochondria (Fig. 6); however, these predictions need to be validated with actual localisation studies. Three $\gamma \beta \alpha$ sesqui-TPS and one $\beta \alpha$ sesqui-TPS were predicted to have plastid targeting signals (Fig. 6). Currently, there is a lack of knowledge on subcellular localisation of conifer TPS, and a gap between general assumption and in silico predictions of subcellular localisation of conifer TPS. Closing these gaps will be important to understand the evolution of TPS substrate preference and its role in the evolution of terpene product diversity.

# VI. Cytochrome P450s, terpene functionalising enzymes

The diversity of terpenes produced by TPS is increased through oxygenation catalysed by cytochrome P450 monooxygenases

(P450s) (Figs 2, 4c). Annotation of the white spruce genome and transcriptome identified over 300 different P450 genes with large expansions in the CYP736 subfamily and in the apparently gymnosperm-specific CYP76AA and CYP750 subfamilies (Warren et al., 2015; Celedon et al., 2017). Functionally characterised P450s of oleoresin diterpene biosynthesis are members of the conifer-specific CYP720B subfamily, which has at least eight different genes in white spruce. In total, 15 different CYP720B genes have been identified in different spruce and pine genomes and transcriptomes (Geisler et al., 2016). The known CYP720B sequences group into four distinct clades I-IV (Hamberger et al., 2011; Warren et al., 2015; Geisler et al., 2016). Of this set of known spruce and pine CYP720B genes, four have been functionally characterised, two in clade I (CYP720B2, CYP720B12) and two in clade III (CYP720B1, CYP720B4) (Ro et al., 2005; Hamberger et al., 2011; Geisler et al., 2016). The clade III enzymes are multisubstrate, multifunctional P450s that catalyse the sequential three-step oxidation of multiple diterpene olefins to the corresponding C18 diterpene alcohols, aldehydes and DRAs (Fig. 2). By contrast, clade I enzymes were inactive with diterpene olefins. Instead, they converted the 13-hydroxy-8(14)-abietene produced by LAS-type di-TPS into multiple DRAs (Geisler et al., 2016) (Fig. 4c). Through a matrix-like biochemical system, CYP720B enzymes of clades I and III produce some of the same DRAs (abietic, neoabietic, levopimaric, and palustric acid), while clade III enzymes also produce pimaric, isopimaric, and sandaracopimaric acid (Geisler et al., 2016). In addition, Sitka spruce CYP720B4 produces dehydroabietic acid (Hamberger et al., 2011), a DRA associated with weevil resistance (Robert et al., 2010). Together, the characterisation of sets of conifer di-TPS and CYP720B P450s highlighted the modularity of diterpene metabolism in oleoresin biosynthesis (Zerbe & Bohlmann, 2015). In this metabolic system, variable combinations of di-TPS and CYP720B enzymes gives rise to an array of different diterpene alcohols, aldehydes and DRA defense metabolites.

Two P450s for terpene oxidation have recently been discovered in members of the Cupressaceae. In western redcedar, a member of the conifer-specific CYP750 family, CYP750B1 was characterised as a $(+)$ -sabinene 3-hydroxylase (Gesell et al., 2015). This enzymes catalyses the second step, after sabinene synthase, in the biosynthesis of $\alpha$ -thujone in resin glands of western redcedar foliage. The discovery of this P450 was enabled by the differential gene expression analysis in western redcedar genotypes with or without foliar resin glands. High levels of thujone, and other monoterpenes, that accumulate in foliage resin glands act as a deterrent for deer browsing (Vourc'h et al., 2002). In Alaska cedar, CYP706M1 catalyses the two-step oxidation of $(+)$ -valencene to $(+)$ -nootkatone, which is a sesquiterpene proposed to confer resistance to heartwood rot caused by fungi and a compound of interest in the fragrance industry (Cankar et al., 2014).

Several P450s are involved in the formation of taxol in Pacific yew, a biosynthetic system that has been pioneered by Rodney Croteau (Croteau et al., 2006), and was recently reviewed by (Bathe & Tissier, 2019). # VII. Induced oleoresin terpene biosynthesis

In response to biotic stress, conifers can massively increase oleoresin terpene biosynthesis and accumulation together with changes in terpene composition. Natural causes for induced oleoresin defenses are feeding or oviposition on conifer stems by insects such as bark beetles or weevils, fungal disease, or mechanical damage (Nagy et al., 2000; Franceschi et al., 2005; Miller et al., 2005; Boone et al., 2011). Similar responses can be induced by exposure of trees to MeJA (Franceschi et al., 2002; Martin et al., 2002; Zulak & Bohlmann, 2010). Induced terpene defenses have been shown to protect Norway spruce against the bark beetle Ips typographus and its associated fungi (Erbilgin et al., 2006; Zeneli et al., 2006; Schiebe et al., 2012). In Sitka spruce, induced terpene accumulation acts synergistically with stone cells in the resistance against the spruce weevil (Whitehill et al., 2019).

The induced oleoresin response in spruce involves upregulation of terpenoid transcriptomes in cells of the CRD system, as well as reprogramming of gene expression in cambium meristems that leads to the de novo formation of TRD (Miller et al., 2005; Abbott et al., 2010; Zulak et al., 2010; Schmidt et al., 2011; Celedon et al., 2017) (Fig. 8). Changes of terpene

composition in the oleoresin metabolome can be attributed to the differential expression of specific TPS and P450 transcripts and proteins (Zulak et al., 2009; Hall et al., 2011; Hamberger et al., 2011). Among the different MeJA-induced TPS and P450 transcripts in Sitka spruce, PsLAS and PsCYP720B4 involved in DRA biosynthesis are preferentially expressed in CRD as shown with spatially refined transcript analysis using laser microdissection (Hamberger et al., 2011). Induced upregulation of transcript and protein expression for terpene biosynthesis occurs within hours of MeJA treatment and can last for over $30\mathrm{d}$ , leading to both rapid and long-lasting induction of terpenoids in the bark and xylem of spruce stems, along with induced emissions of terpene volatiles from needles that last for several days (Miller et al., 2005; Zulak et al., 2009).

Laser microdissection and RNA-seq based transcriptome analysis also revealed the complete constitutive and MeJA-induced transcriptomes of CRD and other cell types of the white spruce bark (Celedon et al., 2017). The cell-type and tissue-specific transcriptome analyses showed large-scale spatial and temporal dynamics of the MeJA-induced transcriptome response of the white spruce bark, which was metaphorically described as a 'kaleidoscopic' response (Celedon et al., 2017). The de novo formation of xylem TRD is a hallmark of the induced terpenoid defense response in spruce and to some variable degree in other conifers (Nagy et al., 2000; Franceschi et al., 2002; Martin et al., 2002; Hudgins et al., 2004; Zulak & Bohlmann, 2010). At the cellular level, stress-induced TRD formation involves redirection of the normal developmental program of the stem cambium zone (Fig. 8). Upon a stress signal, which may involve octadecanoids and ethylene (Hudgins & Franceschi, 2004; Miller et al., 2005; Hudgins et al., 2006; Ralph et al., 2007), the normal formation of xylem elements is transiently shut down in parts of the stem cambium, and instead, cells derived from the cambium zone differentiate into terpenoid producing TRD cells. Formation of TRD increases the capacity of the spruce stem to produce and accumulate oleoresin terpenes (Martin et al., 2002; Miller et al., 2005; Zeneli et al., 2006). While increased transcript levels of terpenoid biosynthesis have been described as part of the process of TRD formation (Miller et al., 2005; Abbott et al., 2010; Schmidt et al., 2011), genes involved in the developmental reprogramming of the cambium zone for TRD formation or transcription factors that lead to the de novo expression of terpene biosynthesis in TRD are unknown. To this end, new work is now underway that is exploring the transcriptome of laser microdissected cambium zone and TRD forming cells of MeJA-induced white

spruce for transcription factors that activate the promoters of inducible terpenoid pathway genes (J. M. Celedon & J. Bohrmann, unpublished).

# VIII. The mountain pine beetle system: example for co-evolution of herbivores with conifer terpene defenses and limitations under conditions of climate change

Oleoresin terpene defenses are effective in protecting conifers against most herbivores and may have contributed to the survival of conifers throughout evolutionary times. However, some specialised insect herbivores have evolved strategies that allow them to cope with conifer terpene defenses and in some cases even use them to their advantage in colonising a conifer host. Bark beetles (Scolytidae) are some of the best studied forest pests to have co-evolved with the conifer oleoresin defense system (Seybold et al., 2006; Raffa, 2014). Bark beetles and conifer hosts co-exist on the landscape and, under normal conditions, the insect mostly infests weakened and poorly defended hosts. However, long-term anthropogenic impacts on forests such as lack of diversity due to fire suppression as well as climate change, have contributed to enhanced landscape level forest disturbance by bark beetles. This is documented, for example, with the recent epidemic outbreak of the mountain pine beetle (MPB, Dendroctonus ponderosae) in western North America (Safranyik & Carroll, 2006; Aukema et al., 2008; Kurz et al., 2008; Raffa et al., 2008, 2015; Anderegg et al., 2015; Sambaraju et al., 2019). Related to the topic of oleoresin terpene defense, it was noted that terpene defenses that are effective in protecting lodgepole pine $(P.$ contorta) against MPB at low levels of infestations during the endemic phase, may become ineffective under epidemic conditions (Boone et al., 2011). While MPB prefer hosts with less oleoresin under endemic conditions, beetles in the epidemic phase of an outbreak will successfully mass-colonise and kill well

In the current MPB epidemic, which has affected over 47 million hectares of pine forests in western North America (Raffa et al., 2013), lodgepole pine has been the major host based on area affected. However, a larger suite of different pine species can serve as MPB hosts. The climate-driven impact of MPB on pines that are keystone species of fragile ecosystems, such as whitebark pine (P. albicaulis) in the high-elevation Rocky Mountains, is of particular concern (Raffa et al., 2013, 2017; Buotte et al., 2016). Another concerning issue is the recent climate-driven geographic range expansion of MPB and its uncertain impact on naïve pine hosts that have not historically co-evolved with MPB. A prominent example in the current epidemic is the host and range expansion of MPB into jack pine (P. banksiana) east of the Rocky Mountains, whose defense systems have not co-evolved with MPB (Cullingham et al., 2011). Lodgepole pine and jack pine have overlapping oleoresin terpene profiles, but individual monoterpenes and DRAs show substantial differences in abundance between these two species (Hall et al., 2013a,b; Clark et al., 2014; Erbilgin et al., 2014). As individual monoterpenes differ in their toxicity towards MPB (Chiu et al., 2017), differences in the oleoresin may affect susceptibility of different pine species to MPB.

As a forest pest that has co-evolved with conifer oleoresin defenses, MPB and its associated fungal and bacterial microbionts have a variety of mechanisms to cope with host terpenes: (1) Successful mass-colonisation of a host tree requires coordinated behavior of a large number of insects which is achieved by insect pheromones. Mass attack allows the MPB to physically drain some of the host oleoresin defenses. (2) In addition, MBP are associated with a complex fungal and bacterial microbiome, and some of these fungi (Wang et al., 2013, 2014) and bacteria (Adams et al., 2013; Boone et al., 2013) can tolerate and metabolise host terpene defenses to the degree of using host terpenes as a nutrient source. For example, the genome of the MPB associated fungus Grosmanni clavigera contains a terpene inducible ABC transporter GcABC-G1 (Diguistini et al., 2010), which makes the fungus tolerant of (+)-limonene. This compound is one of the more toxic monoterpenes towards MPB (Chiu et al., 2017), but in the presence of GcABC-G it can be metabolised and used as a carbon source by the MPB symbiont G. clavigera (Wang et al., 2013). (3) MPB also has the metabolic capacity to convert host monoterpenes and DRAs, which involves several different MPB P450s that were discovered in the MPB genome and several of which have been functionally characterised for their role in terpene modification (Keeling et al., 2013; Chiu et al., 2019a,b,c). One of the oxidised monoterpenes produced by a MPB P450 is the aggregation pheromone $(-)$ -transverbenol. $(-)$ -trans-Verbenol is derived from the pine oleoresin monoterpene $(-)$ - $\alpha$ -pinene and accumulates in the phloemfeeding MPB larvae in the form of monoterpenyl ester (Chiu et al., 2018, 2019c2018, 2019b). These esters then serve as reservoir for the release of aggregation pheromone in the adult MPB.

Taken together, a large volume of studies on the MPB, of which we could only cover a selected subset here, highlighted that this co

evolved conifer pest has genomic, molecular, behavioral and ecological mechanisms to cope with the massive and chemically diverse oleoresin terpene defenses of its hosts. While MPB coexists with pines under normal environmental conditions of a healthy forest ecosystem, disturbance of the equilibrium driven by climate change, can lead to massive range and host expansion.

# IX. Conclusions

Based on the characterisation of conifer TPS and P450s as reviewed above, the following genomic and biochemical features of conifer terpene biosynthesis contribute to the large and diverse chemical space of oleoresin defenses: (1) multigene TPS families; (2) multigene CYP720B P450 families; (3) multiproduct TPS; (4) multifunctional CYP720B enzymes that act on multiple substrates; (5) the combination of the above 1-4, resulting in (6) radiating and matrix-type (as opposed to linear) pathway systems; and (7) inducible variation of terpenoid biosynthesis in response to biotic stress. These biochemical and molecular features are expressed in cells of conifers that are highly specialised for the biosynthesis of terpenes, such as epithelial cells of resin ducts. While many fundamental aspects and details of conifer terpenoid defense systems are now known, the reference genome annotations also highlight that many genes remain to be functionally characterised in one given conifer system. While the diversity, variability and abundance of terpene oleoresin is thought to have contributed much to the evolutionary success of conifers, climate-driven range and host expansion of conifer pests and disease may challenge the effectiveness of these defense systems.
