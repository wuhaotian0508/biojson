# ABSTRACT

SUMO PROTEASE RELATED TO FERTILITY 1 (SPF1) and SPF2 are responsible for deSUMOylation of SUMO-conjugated protein substrates and for maintaining protein SUMOylation homeostasis. To date, the role of SUMO proteases in fatty acid biosynthesis and lipid accumulation remains unclear. Here, we demonstrate that the Arabidopsis thaliana mutants spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 exhibit increased seed size and elevated seed oil content (SOC). We further show that SPF1 and SPF2 interact with WRINKLED1 (WRI1), a master regulator of the transcriptional control of plant oil synthesis. Genetic analyses indicate that the spf1-1 wri1-3 and spf2-1 wri1-3 double mutants, as well as the spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 triple mutant, phenocopy wri1-3 and display severe seed shriveling, indicating that SPF1 and SPF2 act upstream of WRI1. WRI1 was identified as a SUMO1 substrate with two conserved SUMOylation sites, lysine 257 (K257) and K266, in cruciferous plants, with K257 acting as the dominant site required for seed oil synthesis. SUMOylation enhances WRI1 stability, whereas SPF1- and SPF2- mediated deSUMOylation promotes WRI1 degradation. In spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants, the abundance of SUMOylated WRI1 increases during seed development and correlates with elevated seed oil accumulation. Together, these results indicate that SPF1 and SPF2 negatively regulate oil synthesis by deSUMOylating WRI1, establishing a dynamic SUMOylation and deSUMOylation switch centered on the SPF1/SPF2–WRI1 module that fine-tunes seed development and oil synthesis.

# INTRODUCTION

SUMO (small ubiquitin-like modifier) is a small protein of approximately 100–115 amino acids that is highly conserved among eukaryotic organisms (Hanania et al., 1999). The functions of SUMO proteases include SUMO maturation and the removal of SUMO from target proteins. The SUMOylation process depends on SUMO proteases to remove C-terminal amino acids from SUMO precursors, followed by ATP-dependent activation via the SUMO-activating enzyme complex SAE1a/SAE1b– SAE2 (Kurepa et al., 2003), conjugation by the SUMO E2 

enzyme SCE1 (Castano-Miquel et al., 2017), and substrate selection mediated by E3 ligases, including SAP and Miz1 (SIZ1), methyl methanesulfonate sensitivity protein 21 (MMS21)/ HIGH PLOIDY 2 (HPY2), and protein inhibitor of activated STAT-like 1/2 (PIAL1/2) (Miura et al., 2005; Huang et al., 2009; Streich and Lima, 2016). Through this enzymatic cascade, SUMO is ultimately transferred to lysine residues of target proteins. Genetic analyses have revealed that Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) mutants lacking core components of the SUMOylation machinery (including sae2, sce1, sae1a sae1b, and sumo1 sumo2), as well as the E3 double mutant siz1-2 

hpy2-1, exhibit embryonic lethality, whereas siz1-2 and hpy2-1 single mutants display pleiotropic defects in plant growth (Saracco et al., 2007; Ishida et al., 2012). In Arabidopsis, SIZ1 is the most well-characterized transcription factor (TF) SUMOylation regulator and functions in diverse biological processes (Roy and Sadanandom, 2021). SIZ1 mediates cold adaptation by SUMOylating ICE1 (inducer of CBF/DREB1 expression) at K393, thereby enhancing freezing tolerance (Miura et al., 2007). The phosphate starvation response regulator PHR1 is SUMOylated by SIZ1 at K261 and K372, which stabilizes the protein and increases its binding affinity for P1BS motifs (Miura et al., 2005). SUMOylation of ABA INSENSITIVE 5 (ABI5) at K391 by SIZ1 negatively regulates ABA signaling (Miura et al., 2009). In contrast, SUMOylation of MYB30 at K283 by SIZ1 prevents its degradation and enhances protein stability in plants (Zheng et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2018). More recently, SIZ1 was reported to promote PROPEP1 processing by strengthening the interaction between PROPEP1 and MC4, thereby increasing plant tolerance to cell wall damage (Zhang et al., 2025). 

SUMOylation is a reversible post-translational modification that is removed by SUMO proteases through cleavage of SUMO moieties from target proteins (Augustine et al., 2016). These proteases are essential for regulating SUMOylation and deSU-MOylation processes and thereby affect multiple cellular functions, including protein stability, subcellular localization, and protein–protein interactions (Lois et al., 2003; Dohmen, 2004; Ishida et al., 2012; Castano-Miquel et al., 2013; Augustine and Vierstra, 2018). SUMO PROTEASE RELATED TO FERTILITY 1 (SPF1) and SPF2, members of the SPF-type protease subgroup, exhibit distinct roles in modulating SUMO conjugation and consequently influence stress responses and developmental pathways (Castro et al., 2018). Our previous work indicated that SPF1 and SPF2 regulate the development of male and female gametes as well as embryos, and show partial functional redundancy in controlling reproductive growth (Liu et al., 2017). ASP1/SPF1 also positively regulates flowering time and fine-tunes far-red light signaling via an FHY- and phyA-dependent pathway (Kong et al., 2017; Qu et al., 2020). In addition, SPF1 and SPF2 act as key regulators of photomorphogenesis, and exposure to blue light promotes their degradation, leading to increased SUMOylation of MYC2 (Srivastava et al., 2022). Although SPF1 and SPF2 have been shown to contribute to overall plant growth, it remains unclear whether they regulate seed oil synthesis. 

WRINKLED1 (WRI1) plays an essential role in regulating seed oil synthesis. WRI1 was identified as a member of the APETALA2 (AP2)/EREBP (ethylene-responsive element-binding protein) TF family (Cernac and Benning, 2004; Masaki et al., 2005; To et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2020a; Yang et al., 2022). The Arabidopsis WRI1 knockout mutant (wri1-1) displays an approximately $80 \%$ reduction in seed oil content (SOC) compared with the wild type (WT) (Focks and Benning, 1998). Subsequent studies demonstrated that WRI1 controls gene expression during late glycolysis and fatty acid (FA) biosynthesis (Ruuska et al., 2002; Baud et al., 2007; Maeo et al., 2009). For example, WRI1 directly modulates the transcription of genes associated with glucose metabolism and FA synthesis, including sucrose synthase 2 (SUS2), plastidic pyruvate kinase β1 (PKP-β1), biotin 

carboxyl carrier protein 2 (BCCP2), ketoacyl–acyl carrier protein synthase I (KASI), and acyl carrier protein 1 (ACP1) (Ruuska et al., 2002; Baud et al., 2007; Maeo et al., 2009). Posttranslational modifications (PTMs) of WRI1 play an important role in regulating its function. The WRI1 protein contains two AP2 domains and three intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs), with the IDR3 region located near the C terminus and featuring a phosphorylated PEST motif. Deletion of the PEST motif enhances WRI1 stability (Ma et al., 2015). 14-3-3 proteins physically interact with WRI1 and mediate both its transcriptional activity and WRI1-dependent oil production (Ma et al., 2016; Kong and Ma, 2018). Mutation of the phosphorylation sites Thr-70 and Ser-166 to Ala results in loss of SNF1 KINASE HOMOLOG 10 (KIN10)-dependent phosphorylation and significantly inhibits WRI1 degradation (Zhai et al., 2017). Trehalose 6-phosphate stabilizes WRI1 and increases FA production by inhibiting KIN10 activity (Zhai et al., 2018, 2021). Furthermore, KIN10 physically interacts with CDK8, leading to its phosphorylation and degradation. Once activated, KIN10 downregulates WRI1 expression and suppresses lipid synthesis by promoting CDK8 degradation (Zhai et al., 2023). A Cullin3-based E3 ligase adaptor interacts with the WRI1 BPM (MATH-BTB/POZ) domain and mediates its degradation via the 26S proteasome (Chen et al., 2013). Recent studies have identified additional transcriptional regulators that modulate WRI1 at multiple levels. For example, TEOSINTE BRANCHED1/CYCLOIDEA/PROLIFERATING CELL FACTOR 4 (TCP4) directly binds the WRI1 promoter and represses its transcription, thereby restricting oil accumulation (Kong et al., 2020b). The WRI1–BLI–CLF–SWI3B module mediates seed maturation and FA biosynthesis by altering Arabidopsis chromatin dynamics (Huang et al., 2022). Conversely, the basic leucine zipper (bZIP) protein bZIP52 physically interacts with WRI1, repressing its transcriptional activation of downstream FA biosynthesis genes and reducing oil accumulation (Yang et al., 2023). WRI1 also coordinates with LEAF COTYLEDON 1 (LEC1), LEC2, and FUSCA3 (FUS3) to promote auxin biosynthesis and lipid accumulation during somatic embryo induction by enhancing the activation of YUCCA4 (YUC4) and OLEOSIN3 (OLE3) (Zhang et al., 2024). In addition, PIF4 (PHYTOCHROME-INTERACTING FACTOR 4) and PIF5 promote WRI1-dependent expression of FA biosynthesis genes (Liao et al., 2025). Together, these interactions underscore the complexity of lipid metabolism regulation and suggest multiple entry points for enhancing seed oil production. Previous studies have shown that PTMs strongly influence WRI1 stability, yet whether SUMOylation affects seed oil synthesis, and how this regulation influences oil accumulation, remains unresolved. 

Here, we observed that the spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants exhibit increased SOC, identifying SPF1 and SPF2 as key negative regulators of oil synthesis. We further identified the master lipid regulator WRI1 as a direct target of SPF1 and SPF2 and pinpointed two functional SUMOylation sites, Lys257 (K257) and Lys266 (K266), with K257 acting as the dominant site in oil synthesis. SPF1- and SPF2-mediated deSUMOylation reduces WRI1 stability and promotes its degradation. By contrast, in spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants, accumulation of SUMOylated WRI1 activates FA biosynthesis genes, thereby elevating SOC. # RESULTS

# SPF1 and SPF2 negatively regulate seed size and seed oil content (SOC) in Arabidopsis

To investigate the roles of SPF1 and SPF2 in seed development, loss-of-function Arabidopsis mutants spf1-1 and spf2-1, as well as 35S:SPF1 and 35S:SPF2 transgenic plants, were analyzed. Mature seeds were imaged using a stereomicroscope, followed by phenotypic analyses. The spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants exhibited significantly larger seed areas than WT, whereas seed area was markedly reduced in 35S:SPF1 and 35S:SPF2 transgenic plants (Figure 1A–1C). SOC was subsequently quantified by gas chromatography–flame ionization detector (GC–FID) analysis. Specifically, the SOC of WT seeds was $3 0 . 0 7 \% \pm 0 . 2 5 \%$ , whereas the spf1-1 mutant showed a significant increase to $3 4 . 3 6 \% \pm 0 . 3 8 \%$ , corresponding to a 4.29 percentage point increase. Similarly, the spf2-1 mutant exhibited an SOC of $3 2 . 4 8 \% \pm 0 . 2 1 \%$ , representing a 2.41 percentage point increase. Notably, the spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant displayed an additive effect, with SOC further increasing to $3 6 . 5 4 \% \pm 0 . 5 0 \%$ , corresponding to a 6.46 percentage point increase (Figure 1D). By contrast, SOC was significantly reduced in both 35S:SPF1 and 35S:SPF2 transgenic lines: three independent 35S:SPF1 lines showed SOC values of $2 6 . 3 2 \% \pm$ $0 . 4 5 \%$ , $2 7 . 7 8 \% \pm 0 . 1 5 \%$ , and $2 5 . 1 7 \% \pm 0 . 6 7 \%$ , whereas three independent 35S:SPF2 lines exhibited SOC values of $2 7 . 8 9 \% \pm$ $0 . 2 8 \%$ , $2 7 . 2 7 \% \pm 0 . 2 8 \%$ , and $2 6 . 6 8 \% \pm 0 . 1 3 \%$ , respectively (Figure 1D). Together, these results indicate that SPF1 and SPF2 act as negative regulators of seed size and SOC in Arabidopsis.

KEGG pathway enrichment analysis indicated that up-regulated DEGs at both 10 DAF and 14 DAF were enriched in starch and sucrose metabolism, glycolysis, FA metabolism, FA biosynthesis, and the FA elongation pathway (Supplemental Figure 2). # WRI1 functions downstream of SPF1 and SPF2

To establish the genetic relationship among SPF1, SPF2, and WRI1, we crossed the spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants with wri1-3 to generate spf1-1 wri1-3, spf2-1 wri1-3, and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 lines. Compared with the WT, seed number per silique at 10 DAF was significantly reduced in the spf1-1 and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants. By contrast, no significant differences were observed in the wri1-3, spf1-1 wri1-3, spf2-1 wri1-3, or spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 plants (Supplemental Figure 3). Despite extensive multigenerational crossing and PCR-based genotyping, we were unable to obtain homozygous spf1-1 wri1-3 plants; only plants homozygous for spf1-1 and heterozygous for wri1-3 (spf1- $1 ^ { - / - }$ wri1- $\cdot 3 ^ { + / - ) }$ were identified. By contrast, we successfully obtained homozygous spf2- $\pmb { 1 } ^ { - / - }$ wri1- $\cdot 3 ^ { - / - }$ double mutants as well as heterozygous triple mutants $s p t 1 ^ { + / - } \ s p t 2 ^ { - / - }$ wri1- $3 ^ { - / - }$ , which are heterozygous for spf1-1 and homozygous for spf2-1 and wri1-3. These results reveal a critical functional distinction between SPF1 and SPF2 (Supplemental Figure 4). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) further confirmed that the spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants exhibited significantly larger seed areas than the WT, primarily owing to increased singlecell size (Figure 3A and 3B). In contrast, seeds from spf1-1 wri1-3, spf2-1 wri1-3, and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 mutants phenocopied wri1-3, displaying wrinkled seed surfaces and no significant difference in single-cell area compared with wri1-3 while being significantly smaller than WT seeds (Figure 3A and 3B). Quantitative stereomicroscopic analysis further revealed that spf2-1 wri1-3 and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 seeds exhibited pronounced wrinkling and a significant reduction in seed area relative to WT (Figure 3F–3I). The spf1-1 wri1-3 seeds also showed shrinkage; however, their seed areas did not differ significantly from WT (Figure 3D and 3E), suggesting that SPF1- and 

SPF2-mediated regulation of cell expansion is antagonized by WRI1 deficiency. 

Because wri1-3 was heterozygous in the spf1-1 wri1-3 background, we used homozygous spf2-1 wri1-3 double mutants to examine the expression of downstream WRI1 target genes. Expression analysis showed that, in the spf2-1 mutant, genes involved in FA synthesis precursor supply, including BCCP2, CAC2, and PKP-β1, as well as FA carbon chain elongation genes KASI and KASIII, were significantly upregulated. By contrast, the lipid assembly-related gene PDAT1 showed no difference in expression compared with WT. In the spf2-1 wri1-3 double mutant, expression of these genes was suppressed, resulting in a transcriptional profile resembling that of the wri1-3 single mutant (Supplemental Figure 5). Consistent with these expression patterns, SOC in both spf2-1 wri1-3 and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 mutants was significantly reduced relative to WT and was indistinguishable from that of wri1-3. This apparent rescue of SOC is likely attributable to the heterozygous wri1-3 allele, which partially restored the expression of downstream target genes. Collectively, these findings indicate that WRI1 functions downstream of SPF1 and SPF2 within a shared regulatory pathway. Although SPF1 and SPF2 redundantly regulate seed size and exhibit partial independence from WRI1, their combined loss is insufficient to bypass the oil synthesis defect caused by WRI1 deficiency. These results position SPF1 and SPF2 as key regulatory nodes linking SUMO proteostasis to seed oil synthesis through WRI1-dependent regulation. 

# WRI1 is a SUMO substrate protein

# SPF1 mediates WRI1 deSUMOylation

Our results show that WRI1 undergoes SUMOylation in vivo and that physical interactions occur specifically between WRI1 and the SUMO proteases SPF1 and SPF2. To determine whether SPF1 and SPF2 function as deSUMOylases of WRI1, we performed in vitro deSUMOylation assays using total protein extracts from 35S:SPF1 transgenic plants at 10 DAF. Protein extracts were incubated with WRI1-Myc and collected at different time points (0, 1, 2, and 4 h), followed by immunoblot detection using anti-WRI1 and anti-actin antibodies. When extracts from WT plants were coincubated with WRI1-Myc, WRI1 abundance remained relatively stable between 0 and 4 h. By contrast, co-incubation of 35S: SPF1 extracts with WRI1-Myc resulted in enhanced WRI1 degradation, with levels decreasing to $45 \% - 6 7 \%$ of the initial amount at 4 h. Consistent results were obtained from two independent 35S:SPF1 lines (#1 and #2) (Figure 6A; Supplemental Figure 9A). 

To directly assess deSUMOylation, 35S:SPF1 and WRI1-Myc proteins were incubated with anti-Myc beads for 0, 2, and 4 h and subsequently detected using anti-SUMO1 antibodies. Input controls detected with anti-Myc antibodies confirmed comparable protein loading across samples. A pronounced reduction in SUMOylated WRI1 was observed at 2 h, decreasing to approximately $3 3 \%$ of initial levels. Notably, addition of the cysteine protease inhibitor N-ethylmaleimide (NEM) reversed this reduction. NEM effectively suppressed SPF1 deSUMOylation activity, leading to increased accumulation of SUMOylated WRI1 at both $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , approximately $5 3 \%$ of initial levels, and $4 \mathsf { h }$ , approximately $3 5 \%$ of initial levels. These data demonstrate that SPF1 mediates WRI1 deSUMOylation in a time- and protease activity-dependent manner (Figure 6B; Supplemental Figure 9B). 

# SPF1 and SPF2 negatively regulate WRI1 stability

Previous studies have shown that SUMOylation can influence substrate protein stability. To examine how SUMOylation affects WRI1 stability, we performed a transient dual LUC assay in 

Nicotiana benthamiana leaves by co-expressing WRI1 with SPF1 or SPF2. Relative LUC activity, calculated as Firefly-to-Renilla ratios, was significantly reduced when WRI1 was coexpressed with SPF1, showing a 0.83-fold decrease $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ , or with SPF2, showing a 0.93-fold decrease $( p ~ < ~ 0 . 0 1$ ; Supplemental Figure 10). These results indicate that both SPF1 and SPF2 negatively affect WRI1 stability, with SPF1 exerting a stronger effect. To further confirm these observations, we analyzed WRI1 protein abundance in three genetic backgrounds: 35S:SPF1 plants, in which line #1 exhibited a higher SPF1 expression level than line #2 (Supplemental Figure 11A); WT plants with endogenous SPF1 and SPF2 expression; and the spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant. Immunoblot analysis revealed a strong inverse correlation between SPF1/ SPF2 activity and WRI1 abundance. The 35S:SPF1 line #1 showed the lowest WRI1 protein levels, approximately 0.92-fold of WT, consistent with enhanced deSUMOylation-associated degradation. WT plants displayed baseline WRI1 abundance, whereas spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants accumulated the highest levels of SUMOylated WRI1, reaching 3.51-fold of WT, reflecting stabilization due to impaired deSUMOylation (Figure 6C; Supplemental Figure 11B). To further examine this relationship, we generated 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 lines (homozygous for both mutations) by crossing 35S:WRI1 transgenic plants with the spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant. Immunoblot analysis of total protein extracts from WT, 35S:WRI1, and 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 plants revealed a stepwise increase in WRI1 abundance, with levels of 1.2-fold in 35S:WRI1 and 1.39-fold in 35S:WRI1/ spf1-1 spf2-1 relative to WT (Figure 6D). We next performed a cell-free degradation assay to directly assess WRI1 stability in vivo. Following treatment of 12-day-old seedlings with $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ CHX, degradation of WRI1 was markedly delayed in spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants compared with WT (Supplemental Figure 12). These results provide direct evidence that SPF1 and SPF2 negatively regulate WRI1 stability at the post-translational level. 

In addition, increased WRI1 abundance in spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1- 1 spf2-1 mutants at 6, 10, and 14 DAF correlated with elevated SOC, indicating that SPF1 and SPF2 function as negative regulators of seed oil synthesis (Figure 6E). Statistical analysis of seed size and SOC among WT, 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1, and 35S:WRI1 genotypes showed that seed area was significantly increased in 35S:WRI1 lines, whereas no significant difference was observed in 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 lines compared with WT (Figure 6F and 6G). Interestingly, SOC was markedly increased in 35S:WRI1/ spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants despite their WT-like seed morphology (Figure 6H). These results further confirm that SPF1 and SPF2 suppress oil synthesis by negatively regulating WRI1 stability. 

By integrating evidence from WRI1 protein stabilization analyses (Supplemental Figure 10; Figure 6C and 6D), phenotypic observations, and SOC measurements (Figure 6F–6H), we conclude that the synergistic elevation of WRI1 abundance in spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants is the primary driver of enhanced oil synthesis. This dosage-dependent relationship directly links SPF1- and SPF2-mediated deSUMOylation to WRI1 proteostasis. Overall, these findings establish SPF1- and SPF2-catalyzed deSUMOylation of WRI1 as a regulatory mechanism that finetunes seed development and oil synthesis and define a critical regulatory node in lipid metabolism.

# DISCUSSION

The genetic regulation of seed size and SOC represents a fundamental question in seed developmental biology, yet mechanistic insights into these interrelated traits remain under-studied. Our results reveal that two redundant SUMO proteases, SPF1 and SPF2, exert a broad influence on seed development, as loss-of-function mutants produced significantly larger seeds with elevated SOC (Figure 1). SPF1 and SPF2 have previously been implicated in multiple aspects of plant development, including flowering time, red and far-red light signaling, and ABA signaling (Kong et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018; Qu et al., 2020). Mutations in SPF1 and SPF2 have also been shown to affect developmental traits such as chlorophyll content, carotenoid levels, and anthocyanin accumulation (Castro et al., 2018). However, their roles in seed development, particularly in oil synthesis, have remained largely unexplored. Based on our findings, we propose that SPF1 and SPF2 act in a partially redundant manner to regulate seed size and SOC. 

To elucidate the mechanism by which SPF1 and SPF2 regulate SOC, we combined RNA sequencing with protein interaction as-

says, which identified WRI1 as a central downstream substrate. WRI1 is a well-established master regulator of FA synthesis and exhibits conserved functions across plant species, including Arabidopsis, maize, rapeseed, and soybean (Shen et al., 2010; Wu et al., 2014; Kong et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2020). However, the impact of SUMOylation on WRI1 function has remained unclear. In this study, we demonstrate that WRI1 is a SUMOylation substrate and directly interacts with the SUMO proteases SPF1 and SPF2 (Figure 2B–2F). Transcriptomic profiling further revealed that the expression of WRI1 and FA biosynthesis genes differed significantly at both 10 and 14 DAF, with peak expression at 10 DAF followed by a decline at 14 DAF in the mutants (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 13). Key FA biosynthesis genes typically display a bell-shaped expression pattern between 4 and 10 DAP, exhibiting a 2–13-fold increase during the maturation phase before rapidly declining at 10 DAP (Baud and Lepiniec, 2009). Elevated WRI1 expression at 10 DAF is therefore well timed to drive carbon flux into de novo FA biosynthesis, supplying precursors for subsequent triacylglycerol (TAG) assembly. By 14 DAF, metabolic priorities shift toward FA packaging into TAG and oil body formation, reducing the requirement for WRI1 and 

resulting in decreased transcript levels (Schmid et al., 2005). As a master regulator, WRI1 is transcriptionally activated upstream by LAFL-controlled developmental programs (To et al., 2006), and extensive mutual and self-activation occurs within the LAFL network (Gazzarrini and Song, 2024). Consistent with this, LEC1, L1L, LEC2, and FUS3 transcript levels were significantly elevated at both 10 and 14 DAF in the mutants and closely correlated with increased oil accumulation (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 13). In contrast, ABI3 upregulation was specific to the spf2-1 mutant, suggesting distinct regulatory roles for SPF1 and SPF2, potentially reflecting the high endogenous expression of ABI3 during seed maturation (Supplemental Figure 13). These observations suggest that SUMOylation-mediated fine-tuning of WRI1 at the protein level may indirectly influence the activity of the broader LAFL–WRI1 regulatory network. Genetic analyses further supported functional distinctions between SPF1 and SPF2 and their synergistic influence on SOC through the WRI1 pathway. In the spf2-1 wri1-3 double mutant, expression of downstream WRI1 target genes was strongly suppressed. Although only a semi-heterozygous spf1-1 wri1-3 mutant could be obtained, the homozygous spf2-1 wri1-3 double mutant phenocopied wri1-3, displaying wrinkled seeds. SOC in spf2-1 wri1-3 closely resembled that of wri1-3, whereas spf1-1 wri1-3 exhibited recovery of SOC to WT levels, likely due to the heterozygous wri1-3 background (Figure 3C–3F). These transcriptional patterns were reflected and amplified at the protein level. WRI1 protein abundance was elevated in spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants at 10 DAF but slightly reduced at 14 DAF (Figure 6E). The strong correspondence between transcript and protein levels indicates that SPF1 and SPF2 play a critical role in regulating oil synthesis during the key 10-DAF developmental window. Whether LAFL proteins themselves are SUMOylated and subsequently deSUMOylated by SPF1 and SPF2 remains an open question. 

To understand how SUMOylation and deSUMOylation influence WRI1 stability, immunoblot analyses confirmed that WRI1 undergoes SUMOylation both in vivo and in vitro, and that increased levels of SUMOylated WRI1 are associated with enhanced protein stability (Figure 4C, 4D, and 5A). Structurally, the AP2 domain and the C-terminal IDR3 motif are critical for WRI1 stability, and WRI1 is dynamically regulated by KIN10-mediated phosphorylation and E3 ligase-dependent ubiquitination, which directly affect oil biosynthesis (Ma et al., 2016; Zhai et al., 2017). Consistent with this, mutation of the SUMOylation site at K257 partially rescued seed area in pWRI1:WRI1K257R wri1-3 lines and significantly reduced SOC, whereas mutation at K266 did not produce similar effects (Figure 5E–5H), indicating that K257 is a key regulatory site. DeSUMOylation is essential for protein homeostasis and depends on specific proteases (Chosed et al., 2006). Notably, our results identify SPF1 and SPF2 as dedicated deSUMOylation enzymes for WRI1 (Figures 2B–2F, 6A, and 6B). Reduced WRI1 abundance in 35S:SPF1 lines and increased accumulation in the spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant demonstrate that SPF1- and SPF2-mediated deSUMOylation decreases WRI1 stability (Figure 6C). Interestingly, WRI1 abundance was highest in 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 plants, yet seed size did not differ from WT. One possible explanation is that extremely high WRI1 levels, resulting from both overexpression and stabilization, may trigger strong feedback inhibition or compensatory mechanisms that constrain further 

increases in seed growth, potentially due to metabolic bottlenecks or toxicity. Alternatively, seed size may have reached an upper physiological limit. These observations suggest that the SUMOylation and deSUMOylation dynamics of WRI1, finely regulated by SPF1 and SPF2, are essential for balancing carbon allocation into storage oil. 

Based on our findings, we propose a model in which SPF1 and SPF2 regulate SOC through WRI1. SUMO precursors are matured by SUMO proteases and conjugated to WRI1 through the sequential actions of E1, E2, and E3 enzymes, resulting in enhanced WRI1 stability. In WT plants, SPF1 and SPF2 counteract this stabilization by removing SUMO moieties from WRI1, thereby promoting protein turnover and limiting activation of downstream lipid biosynthesis genes. In spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants, impaired deSUMOylation leads to hyperaccumulation of SUMOylated WRI1, ultimately increasing SOC (Figure 7). Previous studies have shown that SPF1 and SPF2 interact with multiple SUMO isoforms and process both shared and distinct substrates (Liu et al., 2017). We therefore propose that SPF1 and SPF2 may also regulate additional substrates involved in FA biosynthesis, lipid metabolism, and oil accumulation pathways, which represents an important avenue for future research. Overall, our study provides mechanistic insight into SOC regulation and highlights the central role of SUMOylation and deSUMOylation within the SPF1/SPF2–WRI1 regulatory module. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The Arabidopsis T-DNA mutants spf1-1 (SALK_040576), spf2-1 (SALK_023493), and wri1-3 (SALK_085693) are in the Col-0 background. The homozygous spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant, generated by genetic crossing, has been described previously (Liu et al., 2017). The spf1-1 wri1-3, spf2-1 wri1-3, and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 mutants were generated by genetic crossing and confirmed by PCR-based genotyping. The 35S: WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 lines, homozygous for both mutations, were generated by crossing 35S:WRI1 transgenic plants with the spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutant. 

Arabidopsis seeds were surface-sterilized and sown on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium containing half-strength MS salts, $1 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / \mathsf { L }$ sucrose, and $7 . 5 { - } 8 ~ \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { L }$ agar, adjusted to pH 5.8–6.0. Seeds were transferred to a growth chamber under a 12-h light and 12-h dark photoperiod at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ $50 \%$ relative humidity, and a light intensity of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

# Plasmid construction

The full-length CDSs of SPF1, truncated SPF11− 648 (amino acids 1 to 648 starting from ATG), SPF2, WRI1, SUMO1, other SUMO isoforms (SUMO2, SUMO3, and SUMO5), and known SUMO proteases (OTS1, OTS2, ESD4, and ELS1) were first cloned into the Gateway entry vector pGWC, which contains attL1 and attL2 recombination sites, using standard restrictionligation methods (Liu et al., 2017). Subsequent LR recombination using the Gateway cloning system (Invitrogen) was performed to transfer these entry clones into their corresponding expression vectors. 

To generate the 35S:SPF1, 35S:SPF2, 35S:WRI1-Myc, 35S:WRI1K257R-Myc, and 35S:WRI1K266R-Myc constructs for transgenic plant production, the vectors pGWC-SPF1, pGWC-SPF2, pGWC-WRI1, pGWC-WRI1K257R, and pGWC-WRI1K266R were recombined into the Gateway vector 35S: GENE-Myc. Point mutations in WRI1, in which lysine residues were replaced by arginine, were introduced by site-directed mutagenesis.

SUMO precursors are converted into mature SUMO by SUMO-specific proteases. The transcription factor WRI1 is modified via ATP-dependent SUMO conjugation through the E1–E2–E3 enzymatic cascade, which enhances WRI1 stability. In WT plants, SPF1- and SPF2-mediated deSUMOylation dynamically removes SUMO from WRI1, thereby reducing its stability. In spf1-1 spf2-1 double mutants, impaired deSUMOylation disrupts this equilibrium. Failure to remove SUMO from WRI1 results in the accumulation of SUMOylated WRI1 in seeds. Stabilized WRI1 subsequently promotes transcriptional activation of downstream oil synthesis networks, ultimately resulting in increased SOC in spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 mutants. 

To generate the 35S:maSUMO1-WRI1-Myc construct for transgenic plant generation, pGWC-WRI1 was recombined into the Gateway vector 35S: maSUMO1-GENE-Myc. 

For native promoter-driven GFP fusion constructs pWRI1:WRI1-GFP, pWRI1:WRI1K257R-GFP, and pWRI1:WRI1K266R-GFP for the recovery assays, the 2,580 bp WRI1 promoter region was used to replace the 35S promoter, and pGWC-WRI1, pGWC-WRI1K266R, and pGWC-WRI1K257R were recombined into the Gateway vector pWRI1:GENE-GFP. 

To generate the 35S:WRI1-GFP, 35S:WRI1K257R-GFP, and 35S: WRI1K266R-GFP constructs for subcellular localization assays, pGWC-WRI1, pGWC-WRI1K257R, and pGWC-WRI1K266R were recombined into the Gateway vector 35S:GENE-GFP. 

For yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays, the AD-WRI1, BD-SPF11− 648, BD-SPF2, BD-SIZ1, BD-ESD4, BD-ELS1, BD-OTS1, BD-OTS2, BD-SUMO2, BD-SUMO3, BD-SUMO5, and BD-Myc constructs were generated. Specifically, pGWC-WRI1 was recombined into pGADT7 (Clontech), while pGWC-SUMO1, pGWC-SPF11− 648, pGWC-SPF2, pGWC-SIZ1, pGWC-ESD4, pGWC-ELS1, pGWC-OTS1, pGWC-OTS2, pGWC-SUMO2, pGWC-SUMO3, pGWC-SUMO5, and pGWC-Myc were recombined into pGBKT7 (Clontech).

For SFLC assays, constructs SPF1-cLUC, SPF2-cLUC, SUMO1-cLUC, and WRI1-nLUC were generated by recombining pGWC-SPF1, pGWC-SPF2, and pGWC-SUMO1 into the 35S:GENE-cLUC vector and pGWC-WRI1 into the Gateway vector 35S:GENE-nLUC. 

To generate the 35S:SPF1, 35S:SPF2, 35S:WRI1-LUC, and 35S:Myc constructs for dual-LUC reporter assays,pGWC-SPF1 and pGWC-SPF2 were recombined into the Gateway vector 35S:GENE, and pGWC-WRI1 was recombined into the Gateway vector 35S:GENE-rLUC. 

For in vitro SUMOylation assays, the pCDFDuet-WRI1-Flag, WRI1K266R-Flag, WRI1K257R-Flag, and WRI12KR-Flag constructs were generated by inserting the corresponding CDSs of WRI1, WRI1K266R, WRI1K257R, and WRI12KR into the pCDFDuet-Flag vector.

# RNA extraction and reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RTqPCR)

Total RNA was isolated from Arabidopsis leaves and 10 DAF developing siliques using an RNAprep Pure kit (Tiangen, China, DP441), followed by complementary DNA synthesis using TransScript One-Step gDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis (TransGen, China, AT311-02). RT-qPCR was performed on a CFX Connect™ Real-Time PCR system (Bio-Rad, USA) using SYBR Premix TransStart® Green qPCR (TransGen, China, AQ211-01) under the following cycling conditions: one cycle at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 2 min, followed by 40 cycles at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 10 s, ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 15 s, and ${ 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 5 min. The Arabidopsis Actin gene was used as the internal control. All experiments were conducted with three biological replicates and three technical replicates. Primer sequences used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Microscopy phenotypic analysis

Seed morphology and developing seeds from siliques at 10 DAF of Arabidopsis thaliana mutants spf1-1, spf2-1, spf1-1 spf2-1, wri1-3, spf1-1 wri1-3, spf2-1 wri1-3, and spf1-1 spf2-1 wri1-3 were examined using a Leica DM2500 stereomicroscope, as previously described (Liu et al., 2017). 

At least 10 siliques were imaged for each genotype. Mature seeds from mutant lines, complementation lines pAtWRI1:WRI1 wri1-3, pAtWRI1: WRI1K266R wri1-3, and pAtWRI1:WRI1K257R wri1-3, overexpression lines 35S:WRI1, 35S:WRI1K266R, 35S:WRI1K257R, 35S:SPF1, and 35S:SPF2, as well as 35S:WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1 lines, were documented using a stereomicroscope. At least 10 images were acquired for each line. Surface cell morphology of WT and mutant seeds was analyzed using a Hitachi S-3000N microscope, with seeds sputter-coated with platinum prior to imaging (Huang et al., 2021). At least three representative images were collected per genotype. ImageJ software was used to quantify seed area and single-cell area. 

# Seed oil content (SOC) measurement

Fatty acids extracted from dried seeds were analyzed using the GC–FID method, as described previously (Lu et al., 2016). Fatty acid content was calculated as nmol%. Heptadecanoic acid (C17:0) was used as the internal standard, and SOC was calculated as a percentage of seed dry weight. Three biological replicates were analyzed for each mutant line and for each of three independent transgenic plants. 

# RNA-seq analysis

Siliques containing seeds were collected at 10 and 14 DAF from WT, spf1-1, spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 plants and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA extraction, cDNA library construction, and transcriptome sequencing using a HiSeq 2500 platform in 150-bp paired-end mode (Illumina, USA) were performed by Frasergen Biotech Co., Ltd., Wuhan, China. Clean reads were aligned and analyzed, and transcripts per kilobase of exon model per million mapped reads (TPM) values were used to normalize relative transcript abundance. Genes with an average TPM value below 1 in each tissue were considered not expressed. DEGs were identified using the R package edgeR (Nikolayeva and Robinson, 2014) with thresholds of absolute log2 fold change greater than 1.5 and $p < 0 . 0 5$ . Gene Ontology enrichment and KEGG pathway analyses were performed using the R package clusterProfiler (Wu et al., 2021). Three biological replicates were analyzed for WT and each mutant line. 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay

Yeast transformation was performed using the LiAc/DNA/PEG method (Schiestl and Gietz, 1989). Pairs of plasmids were co-transformed into the yeast strain Y2HGold. Transformants were selected on synthetic drop-out medium lacking leucine and tryptophan (SD/–Leu–Trp). Protein interactions were assessed on synthetic drop-out medium lacking adenine, histidine, leucine, and tryptophan (SD/–Leu–Trp–His–Ade) and incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Each experiment was performed independently at least three times with consistent results. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay

The 35S:WRI1-nYFP, 35S:SPF1-cYFP, 35S:SPF2-cYFP, and 35S: SUMO1-cYFP constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens. Bacterial strains carrying nYFP or cYFP constructs were collected and resuspended in infiltration buffer containing $1 0 \ m M$ MES $( \mathsf { p H } 5 . 7 )$ , $1 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , and $1 5 0 \mathrm { \ m g / m L }$ acetosyringone. Mixed bacterial suspensions were infiltrated into the leaves of 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana plants and incubated for 48 h (Liu et al., 2017). Infiltrated leaf regions were excised and examined using a Zeiss LSM 980 confocal microscope with a 514-nm excitation laser and a 535–550-nm emission filter to detect YFP fluorescence. 

# Split firefly luciferase complementation (SFLC) assay

Firefly LUC was split into N-terminal and C-terminal fragments to generate nLUC and cLUC constructs, including SPF1-cLUC, SPF2-cLUC, SUMO1- cLUC, and WRI1-nLUC. These constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 and co-infiltrated with P19 into 4- week-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves at four positions per leaf. After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ , firefly luciferase activity was measured in the presence of $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ 

D-luciferin, following a previously described protocol (Huang et al., 2021). Leaves were incubated for 5 min before imaging using a cooled CCD system (Berthold, LB985). More than five leaves were analyzed for each experiment. 

# Subcellular localization assay

Subcellular localization assays were conducted as described previously (Liu et al., 2017). The 35S:WRI1-GFP, 35S:WRI1K257R-GFP, and 35S:WRI1K266R-GFP constructs were co-infiltrated with P19 into leaves of 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana plants and incubated under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod for 48 h. GFP fluorescence was visualized using a Zeiss LSM 980 confocal microscope with a 488-nm excitation laser and a 510–550-nm emission filter. 

# Dual-LUC reporter assay

The combinations of 35S:SPF1 with 35S:WRI1-rLUC and 35S:SPF2 with 35S:WRI1-rLUC were co-infiltrated with P19 into the leaves of 4-weekold Nicotiana benthamiana plants and incubated in the dark at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 12 h. Firefly and Renilla (REN) LUC signals were subsequently detected using a dual-LUC reporter assay kit (Promega, E1910), as described previously (Yang et al., 2023). Relative luciferase activity was calculated by normalizing firefly luciferase activity to REN activity across three independent biological replicates. 

# Plant protein extraction and western blotting

Immunoblot analysis was performed as described previously (Zhang et al., 2020). Proteins were extracted using lysis buffer containing $5 0 ~ { \mathsf { m M } }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA (pH 8.0), $0 . 5 \%$ (v/v) Triton X-100, $10 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, and $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche, #04693159001). Input controls were detected using anti-Myc (Abmart, M20002L, 1:5,000) and anti-actin (Abmart, M20009L, 1:2,000) antibodies.

# In vitro SUMOylation assay

The in vitro SUMOylation assay was performed as described previously (Huang et al., 2023). The CDSs of WRI1, WRI1K266R, WRI1K257R, and WRI12KR were inserted into the pCDFDuet-FLAG vector. The pCDFDuet-WRI1-FLAG, WRI1K266R-FLAG, WRI1K257R-FLAG and WRI12KR-FLAG constructs were generated in Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) containing pET28-AtSAE1a-His-SAE2, pACYCDuet-AtSUMO1GG (-E2), or pACYCDuet-1-His-AtSCE1-Myc-AtSUMO1GG $( + \mathsf { E } 2 )$ , respectively. Bacterial cultures were induced with 0.5 mM IPTG at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 6 h. Proteins were harvested and quantified using a Bradford Protein Assay kit to normalize protein input across samples. SUMOylation was analyzed by immunoblotting using an anti-FLAG antibody (Abmart, M20018M, 1:2,000). 

# In vitro deSUMOylation assay

The in vitro deSUMOylation assay was conducted as described previously (Qu et al., 2020). Total proteins were extracted from 35S:SPF1 transgenic seeds at 10 DAF and incubated with total protein lysates from WT plants or from the WRI1-Myc transgenic line. Mixed protein samples were collected at 0, 1, 2, and 4 h, and WRI1 abundance was analyzed by immunoblotting using anti-WRI1 (PhytoAB, PHY1793A, 1:5,000) and anti-actin (Abmart, M20009L, 1:2,000) antibodies. In parallel, total protein extracts from 35S:SPF1 seeds were incubated with WRI1-Myc protein and Myc agarose beads for 0, 2, and 4 h. NEM (20 mM) was added to the 2 h and 4 h samples as a negative control. DeSUMOylation of WRI1 was assessed by immunoblotting using an anti-SUMO1 antibody (Abcam, Ab5316, 1:5,000). 

# WRI1 stability assay

WRI1 stability assays were performed as described previously (Huang et al., 2021). Total proteins were extracted from WT, 35S:WRI1, 35S: WRI1/spf1-1 spf2-1, and spf1-1 spf2-1 plants and analyzed by immunoblotting using an anti-WRI1 antibody (PhytoAB, PHY1793A, 1:5,000).
