# AtMYB4: a transcription factor general in the battle against UV

Matthew R. Hemm, Klaus M. Herrmann and Clint Chapple

Mounting evidence indicates that members of the large family of plant MYB proteins are involved in the transcriptional regulation of an array of metabolic and developmental processes. Recently, the Arabidopsis thaliana MYB, AtMYB4, was shown to regulate the accumulation of the UV-protectant compound sinapoylmalate by repressing the transcription of the gene encoding the phenylpropanoid enzyme cinnamate 4- hydroxylase. AtMYB4 is thus a key regulator of phenylpropanoid pathway gene expression, and is the first example of a MYB protein that functions as a transcriptional repressor.

Plants have diverse biochemical pathways to help cope with their everchanging environment. The decline of the stratospheric ozone layer has raised concerns about increases in UV radiation, a cause of heritable DNA damage in many organisms, including plants such as Arabidopsis and tobacco1. To protect themselves from UV damage, plants produce UV-absorbing phenolic compounds such as flavonoids and hydroxycinnamate esters2–4. Most of the structural genes involved in phenylpropanoid biosynthesis have been characterized, but little is known about how the constitutive and UV-inducible synthesis of these compounds is regulated.

Recently, Hailing Jin and colleagues5 reported the identification of a transcriptional repressor of the gene encoding cinnamate 4-hydroxylase (C4H), the second enzyme in the pathway leading to multiple UV-protective secondary metabolites. An Arabidopsis mutant in which this transcription factor gene is disrupted displays elevated levels of C4H mRNA, and increased accumulation of UV sunscreens. Of particular note is that the C4H repressor is a member of the MYB family of DNAbinding proteins, a group of proteins previously known only to act as activators of gene expression.

MYB proteins are DNA-binding transcriptional regulators

The animal proto-oncogene c-myb was the first gene identified to encode a MYB protein6. MYB proteins contain up to three 52-amino acid residue repeats, the R1, R2 and R3 MYB domains, which bind DNA in a sequence-specific manner. The c-myb protein contains three of these DNAbinding domains and, like the other two animal MYB proteins, is involved in cell cycle control and cellular differentiation6. R1R2R3 MYB proteins have now been identified in plants and slime molds, suggesting that these proteins are fundamental regulators of a basic cellular process that evolved before the divergence of plants and animals7.

The first plant gene identified as MYB-like was the Zea mays C1 gene, which is required for anthocyanin biosynthesis in kernels8. Since that initial discovery, it has been found that plants contain large MYB gene families. Plant MYB proteins can contain one, two or three MYB domains, but the majority are of the R2R3 type. R2R3 MYBs are involved in the regulation of morphogenesis, disease resistance, cell division, hormone signaling and phenylpropanoid metabolism9. Although few R2R3 MYB proteins have been tested rigorously for their DNA-binding ability, they are thought to act as transcriptional activators of suites of genes involved in these various plant processes10,11. Although MYB genes represent one of the largest gene families in Arabidopsis, the processes that each of these proteins regulate are largely unknown.

MYB proteins transcriptionally regulate the phenylpropanoid pathway Much of what is known to date about the role of R2R3 MYB proteins in transcriptional regulation comes from work with the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway in maize and snapdragon (Antirrhinum majus)9. This metabolic sequence channels products of the general phenylpropanoid pathway toward the

production of a variety of flavonoid derivatives such as anthocyanins, phlobaphenes and flavonols. The synthesis of each of these groups of flavonoids is controlled by R2R3 MYB transcription factors that regulate different subsets of genes in the pathway10,12. In contrast with flavonoid biosynthesis, relatively little is known about the developmental and tissuespecific cellular regulation of the general phenylpropanoid pathway9. Phenylpropanoid derivatives are involved in vascular tissue formation, plant defense mechanisms, floral pigmentation and UV protection13,14. The transcript levels of specific subsets of phenylpropanoid genes are induced in a coordinated fashion in response to wounding, fungal attack and other environmental stresses13. The promoters of these genes contain potential MYB protein recognition motifs that are required for their response-specific and tissue-specific transcriptional induction; however, until recently, the identity of the trans-acting factors that interact with these putative cis-elements remained elusive.

AtMYB4 regulates sinapate ester biosynthesis in Arabidopsis One of the first proteins shown to regulate genes in the general phenylpropanoid pathway was the A. majus R2R3 MYB308 (AmMYB308). AmMYB308 represses the expression of several phenylpropanoid pathway genes when over-expressed in transgenic tobacco15. Because interpretation of overexpression experiments of this type can be problematic, Jin and colleagues5 identified and characterized an Arabidopsis insertional mutant defective in the ortholog of AmMYB308, AtMYB4.

In addition, although Arabidopsis leaves also contain flavonoids, sinapoylmalate has been suggested to play an important role in UV protection2,3. Consistent with this hypothesis, AtMYB4 expression was down-regulated in wild-type plants exposed to UV-B, and the atmyb4 mutant exhibited increased resistance to UV light. Quantitative RT-PCR analysis of phenylpropanoid gene expression in atmyb4 plants revealed that the increase in sinapoylmalate accumulation in the mutant appears to be the result of increased expression of the C4H gene. AtMYB4 is the first MYB protein identified that functions as a transcriptional repressor at the physiological level. It is also one of the first proteins shown to transcriptionally regulate a gene of the general phenylpropanoid pathway, thereby extending the role of R2R3 MYB proteins in secondary metabolism beyond flavonoid biosynthesis.

Dynamics of MYB regulation Multiple MYBs are thought to regulate expression of a gene encoding phenylalanine ammonia lyase as well as several downstream flavonoid genes in Antirrhinum flowers14. Biochemical analysis has shown that other MYB proteins, such as PcMYB1 in Petroselinum crispum and PhMYB3 in

Petunia hybrida, can regulate flavonoid biosynthesis by transcriptional regulation of the gene encoding chalcone synthase, the enzyme that catalyzes the first committed step in flavonoid biosynthesis (Fig. 1)17,18. By contrast, AtMYB4 regulates C4H, but does not affect flavonoid biosynthesis. These few examples suggest that several MYB proteins can work in concert to regulate phenylpropanoid metabolism, and the levels of the various end-products of the pathway accumulated in specific cells, tissues and organs might represent the balanced expression of several interacting MYB proteins. The levels of these MYBs are probably influenced by internal and external cues, including UV-B radiation, as shown for AtMYB4 (Ref. 5). Further understanding of this dynamic regulation is necessary before we have a clear understanding of how these proteins mediate plants’ responses to their biotic and abiotic environment. An important step towards this understanding requires the identification of a complete library of Arabidopsis MYB insertional mutants, coupled with phenotypic characterization of the mutants and microarray analysis of changes in their gene expression. These experiments promise to reveal a host of MYB-mediated regulatory circuits, MYB co-regulated genes, and possibly even new mechanisms by which plant MYB proteins act to regulate transcription.
