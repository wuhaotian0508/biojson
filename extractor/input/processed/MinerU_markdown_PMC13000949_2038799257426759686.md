# Introduction

Increasing crop productivity potential is essential to satisfying the dietary demands of the growing world population [1]. As a globally important food crop, enhancing the yield potential of rice has become a key goal for breeders [2]. In recent years, the regulating of photosynthetic potential and tillering number has become a key factor in determining rice yield, while abiotic stresses such as drought severely affect its stable production [3,4]. Mining genes linked to enhanced photosynthetic efficiency, stress tolerance, and tiller development, as well as breeding rice varieties exhibiting these desirable traits, have emerged as important strategies for ensuring food security and sustainable agricultural development [5,6]. Therefore, exploring genes related to high photosynthetic efficiency and tiller, and further understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying these processes, will be key to breeding rice varieties with high-yield potential. 

Photosynthesis, a fundamental process in plants, is driven by a set of intricate membrane protein complexes embedded within the thylakoid membrane of the chloroplast [7,8]. These complexes, namely Photosystem II (PSII), cytochrome b6f (Cyt b6f), Photosystem I (PSI), and F-ATPase, orchestrate the conversion of light energy into chemical energy [7–9]. Over the past two decades, PSII has emerged as the primary reaction center of photosynthesis, composed primarily of two homologous proteins, D1 and D2, along with two closely associated chlorophyll (chl) coordinating proteins, CP43 and CP47, and the cytochrome b complex [10–12]. A conserved model of the PSII repair cycle holds a crucial position in photosynthetic organisms [13]. This repair mechanism involves the synchronized action of various proteases, notably FtsH (filamentation temperature-sensitive H) and Deg (responsible for the degradation of periplasmic proteins) [13,14]. The FtsHs play a crucial role in the photoprotection of the PSII complex, in which FtsH2/3 can enter the damaged D1, which is then rapidly degraded and replaced with newly synthesized D1 proteins [14–16]. The gene Osftsh2, which encodes FtsH, was significantly affected in mutants and has profound effects on various biological processes, including photosynthesis-related pathways and plant hormone signal transduction in rice [17]. 

Carotenoids are one of the important photosynthetic pigments, engaging not just in the capture and transduction of light energy, but also in shielding the photosynthetic machinery from photoxidative damage [18–20]. Over the past few decades, the genes encoding key enzymes that catalyze core reactions in the biosynthetic pathway of carotenoids have been characterized. Among the genes encoding phytoene synthase, OsPSY1, and OsPSY2 participate in photosynthesis through photo responsive elements, while OsPSY3 is independent of illumination and only responds to abscisic acid (ABA) through the ABA element [20,21]. OsPDS, OsZDS, and OsCRTISO have a crucial influence on the synthesis of ABA and strigolactone (SL) carotenoid precursors, and OsZDS can also trigger oxidative damage in 

photosystem PSII [22,23]. Studies have shown that carotenoids participate in light energy capture and serve as precursors for ABA (abscisic acid) and SL (strigolactone) to modulate stress responses and tillering. However, the genetic mechanism underlying carotenoid biosynthesis and tillering regulation in rice remains unclear. 

The cleavage of carotenoids leads to the generation of biologically active compounds, including plant hormones such as ABA and SL [19,24,25]. ABA is derived from $\beta$ -carotene through a process that initiates with the hydroxylation of all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene to produce zeaxanthin, which is subsequently oxidized to violaxanthin by the zeaxanthin epoxidase enzyme, encoded by OsABA1 in rice [26–29]. Neoxanthin synthase, encoded by ABA4, plays a vital role in the conversion of all-trans-violaxanthin to all-transneoxanthin, although the detailed mechanism remains unclear in Arabidopsis [5,30]. OsVDE, a gene related to ABA biosynthesis, can confer sensitivity to abiotic stress in rice plants [31]. Derived from $\beta$ -carotene, SL is a class of hormones that inhibit branching in plants [32]. Its biosynthesis is influenced by various enzymes. The initial committed step in this process is the isomerization of all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene to 9-cis- $\cdot \beta \cdot$ -carotene, catalyzed by $\beta$ -carotene isomerase, encoded in rice by D27 (OsDWARF27) [33]. This is followed by cleavage via carotenoid cleavage dioxygenase 7 (CCD7), encoded by HTD1/D17, and CCD8, encoded by D10, ultimately leading to the formation of caprolactone [5,34]. Despite SLs and ABA originating from $\beta$ -carotene, our understanding of how SLs and ABA coordinate to influence tiller number in rice remains limited. 

In the present study, we developed and characterized a mutant, named few tillers (ft), which exhibits zebra leaf and few tillers. The gene OsFT, identified in this mutant, encodes $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase and was found to represent a novel allele of OsZDS, which is involved in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway. The $f t$ rice mutant showed reduced functional efficiency of the photosynthetic system and impairment in the carotenoid metabolic pathway. Although the involvement of OsFT in preharvest sprouting regulation has been reported, the precise molecular mechanisms through which it modulates the photosynthetic system and carotenoid metabolism remain incompletely understood. Our results implicate OsFT in maintaining the functional integrity of the PSII complex by interacting with OsFTSH2. This interaction regulates the levels of photosynthetic proteins, particularly under bright light conditions. Meanwhile, we observed varying degrees of disruption in endogenous SL and ABA levels in ft, pgl13 and ft/pgl13. These results led us to uncover a crosstalk between the biosynthetic pathways of SL and ABA. Specifically, we found that SL stimulates the production of ABA, which subsequently suppresses SL biosynthesis. This crosstalk mechanism ultimately determines the tiller number in rice. This study not only elucidates the role of OsFT in modulating photosynthetic efficiency and carotenoid biosynthesis but also reveals the intricate interplay between SL and ABA biosynthetic pathways. Our findings provide valuable insights for improving drought tolerance and enhancing rice yield. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The japonica rice cultivar Chunjiang06 served as the primary wild-type (WT) for most of the analyses. Within the EMSinduced mutant population derived from the japonica cultivar, we identified the ft mutant The ft mutant, identified in the japonica cultivar Chunjiang06, is an allele analogous to phs2-1 identified in the Nipponbare (another japonica cultivar) genetic background [23]. According to the previous research methods of drought treatment, the soil water content in the treated group was maintained at $6 0 \%$ of that in the control group, both 15 days before and 15 days after the tillering stage [35]. All plants were cultivated in experimental fields under natural growing conditions at the China National Rice Research Institute (Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province, China, and Lingshui, Hainan Province, China). 

# Map-based cloning of OsFT

To map the OsFT gene, we conducted primary gene mapping using 118 homozygous few-tillered plants with zebra leaves from the $\mathrm { F } _ { 1 }$ population. This mapping was aided by four molecular markers flanking the target region. For fine-mapping the OsFT locus, we utilized approximately 568 individual plants exhibiting phenotypes of zebra leaf and few tillers. These plants were selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ populations resulting from a cross between ft and 9311 (indica) varieties. Through this approach, we narrowed down the OsFT locus to a 78-kb segment on chromosome 7, bounded by markers L4 and L5. We then sequenced and compared the candidate OsFT genes from both the wild-type and ft genomic DNA. Details of the primers utilized in this study are provided in the Supplemental Information. 

# Vector construction and plant transformation

To assess the functionality of OsFT (LOC_Os07g10490), we devised a range of constructs for this gene. we amplified an 8.2- kb genomic DNA fragment from the wild-type (WT), encompassing a 2.2-kb upstream segment, the complete OsFT gene, and a 0.8-kb downstream segment. This amplified sequence was subsequently cloned into pCAMBIA1300, creating the complementation vector. Additionally, the full-length coding sequences of OsFT (spanning 1737 bp) were inserted into the pCAMBIA1300 binary vector, regulated by the Ubi promoter, to forge overexpression constructs. Furthermore, we cloned specific 20-bp genomic sequences from OsFtsh2 into the CRISPR-Cas9 vector, forming a fused Case9- OsFtsh2 vector. All these constructs were then transformed into both WT and ft plants utilizing Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105-mediated transformation [36]. The primers employed in this study are detailed in the Supplemental Information. 

# GUS activity assay

To investigate the tissue-specific expression patterns of the OsFT, we cloned the 2000 base pair upstream sequence from the start codon (ATG) into the pCAMBIA1305 vector, generating the pCAMBIA1305-PRO OsFT::GUS construct (abbreviated as PRO OsFT::GUS). This recombinant vector was subsequently introduced into WT calli to produce transgenic plants. For GUS histochemical staining, we collected various tissues from the PRO OsFT::GUS transgenic plants and incubated them in GUS staining buffer at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for a duration of $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Following staining, the samples underwent dehydration in $7 5 \ \% \ ( \mathrm { v / v } )$ ethanol to eliminate chlorophyll and were then scanned by a Microtek Scan Maker i800 plus scan-

ner [36]. The primers utilized in this study are provided in the Supplemental Information. 

# Transmission electron microscopy

Take leaves from the same location on WT, $f t ,$ , and $f t$ complementation lines (com1 and com2) at the tillering stage. Remove the leaf veins, cut the leaves into $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ sections, and promptly place them in a $2 \ \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tube containing $2 . 5 \ \%$ glutaraldehyde solution. Apply vacuum to ensure the samples sink to the bottom of the tube and fix them at $4 ~ \mathrm { { ^ \circ C } }$ overnight. Following fixation, dehydrate the samples gradually using 50 %, 70 %, $80 \%$ , $9 5 \ \%$ , and $1 0 0 \%$ ethanol and acetone solutions. Embed the samples in epoxy resin, section them, and stain them with uranium acetate for observation as described in [37]. Observations were carried out under a transmission electron microscope at the Electron Microscope Centre of Zhejiang University. 

# RNA extraction and Quantitative Real Time PCR (qRT PCR)

Total RNA was extracted from different tissues utilizing the Total RNA Miniprep kit (Axygen, Hangzhou, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. cDNA synthesis was carried out using the ReverTra Ace qPCR-RT kit (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan). Subsequently, RT-PCR was executed with SYBR Premix Ex Taq (Takara, Kusatsu, Japan) and primers specific to the genes under investigation, employing a CFX96 Touch Real-time PCR Detection System. To ensure the reliability of our findings, all experiments were replicated three times using different biological samples. For all our analyses, Rice ACTIN1 served as an internal control, as described in [38]. The primers employed in this study are detailed in the Supplemental Information. 

# RNA-seq analysis

Total RNA was extracted from the leaves of WT and ft plants during the tillering and heading stages, using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) and purified with the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen). We collected three biological replicates, each composed of pooled leaves, and immediately froze them in liquid nitrogen. From these samples, we constructed twelve libraries, which were then sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq platform. Utilizing the Python framework for high-throughput sequencing data analysis, HTSeq, we estimated initial gene expression levels [36,39]. DEGs were identified based on a P-value of less than 0.05 and an expression difference represented by |log2FoldChange| greater than 1. Furthermore, we performed a gene ontology (GO) analysis and a Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) enrichment analysis for the identified DEGs through this website software (https:// www.genescloud.cn/home).

# Yeast two-hybrid assay (Y2H)

# Results

# Cloning of OsFT and complementation testing

To achieve a deeper comprehension of the genetic networks regulating tiller development, we identified a zebra leaf and fewtiller mutant, named ft, from our rice mutant library. This library was created using ethyl methane sulfonate (EMS) mutagenesis in the Chunjiang06 (CJ06) genetic background. The ft mutant displayed a zebra leaf phenotype at the seedling stage (Fig. S1A), which lasts until the tillering stage, after which it exhibited a phenotype of few tillers (Fig. 1A). At the full heading stage, the zebra leaves revert to green, but the phenotype of few tillers remains unchanged (Fig. S1B). Agronomic traits of WT (CJ06) and ft showed that during the tillering stage, ft exhibited significantly lower plant height, reduced tiller number, and lower pigment content compared to the WT (Fig. 1B). 

We conducted map-based cloning using an $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ mapping population derived from crossing ft with 9311. Initially, we delimited the OsFT locus to a region on chromosome 7, bounded by molecular markers A2 and A3. To refine the location of the OsFT locus, we selected 568 plants exhibiting the ft phenotypes from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population. Through this approach, we narrowed down the OsFT locus to a 78 kb region defined by markers L4 and L5. Utilizing data from the Rice Genome Annotation Project (RGAP), we identified eleven potential open reading frames (ORFs) within this region. Upon sequencing the genomic DNA, we discovered a single base substitution from G to A in the seventh exon of LOC_Os07g10490 (Fig. 1C). This finding led us to hypothesize that LOC_Os07g10490 could be the candidate gene for OsFT. Previous research has established that the OsFT is a new allele of the OsZDS gene, which regulates preharvest sprouting and photo-oxidation in rice, encoding $\zeta \cdot$ carotene desaturase [5,23]. To validate our hypothesis, we performed transgenic complementation tests. As anticipated, the transgenic positive complementary lines (com1 and com2) demonstrated a recovery in plant height, tiller count, relative gene expression, and pigment content to levels comparable to the wild type during the tillering stage (Fig. 1D and E). To gain further insights into the reversal of the zebra leaf phenotype in ft, we employed transmission electron microscopy to observe chloroplast development during the tillering stage. Our microscopy results indicated that the chloroplast structure in the leaves of com1 and com2 appeared normal, with an elliptical shape and a clearly arranged, dense granular lamella structure, closely resembling the wild type (Fig. 1F). To examine the expression of OsFT in different tissues, we constructed the OsFT-GUS vector and transformed it into the wild type to generate positive transgenic plants. Histochemical staining results revealed GUS signals in stem, leaf, sheath and panicle (Fig. S2A). Consistent with the GUS staining results, RT–PCR analy-

sis revealed that OsFT was highly expressed in stem, leaf and sheath, with lower expression in panicle and root (Fig. S2B). Taken together, these findings suggest that the OsFT is responsible for both the zebra leaf and the reduced tillering phenotypes observed. 

# Impairment of photosynthetic system and carotenoid metabolism in ft mutant

To further elucidate the potential molecular mechanisms by which OsFT is involved in the transcriptional regulation of zebra leaf and a few tillers, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of global expression profiles in both WT and ft plants during the tillering and heading stages. Our findings revealed distinct patterns of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) dependent on OsFT. Specifically, we identified 122 DEGs during the tillering stage and a significantly higher number, 818 DEGs, during the heading stage. The analysis of Gene ontology (GO) classifications further disclosed that the top-enriched GO terms were associated with oxidoreductase activity-related genes, photosynthetic system-related genes, and genes involved in signal transduction pathways (Fig. 2A). Meanwhile, The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis exhibited significant differences in MAPK signaling pathway, Zeatin biosynthesis, Photosynthesis, Plant hormone signal transduction, and other related pathways (Fig. 2B). To further verify the effects of the OsFT genes on photosynthesis, we used western blot to detect the levels of photosystem complex subunits in WT and ft leaf total protein extracts. Specifically, we measured the levels of Photosystem I (PSI) proteins, including Lhca1, Lhca2, PsaA, PsaB, PsaD, and PsaE, as well as Photosystem II (PSII) proteins, including PsbA, PsbD, PsbB, PsbC, Lhcb2, Lhcb4, Lhcb5, and Lhcb6. The results revealed there is a significant decrease in the levels of PSI and PSII-related proteins in the ft leaves as compared to the WT leaves (Fig. S3A). This indicates that the mutation in the OsFT gene appears to downregulate the production of proteins associated with photosynthesis, thereby indicating a crucial role of OsFT in regulating photosynthetic protein expression. Additionally, the expression analysis results showed that most antioxidant-related genes and carotenoid pathway-related genes were significantly downregulated. However, the antioxidant-related genes AOX1a and AOX1b, as well as the carotenoid-related genes OSbOHase1, OsRIGOase3, and OsZE, were significantly upregulated (Fig. 2C and D). fcarotene desaturase, encoded by the OsFT gene, indirectly affects the synthesis and accumulation of lutein by catalyzing a key step in the carotenoid synthesis pathway, so we examined the contents of zeaxanthin, antheraxanthin, and violaxanthin in the lutein cycle in both WT and ft. Our findings revealed significant reductions in the levels of violaxanthin, antheraxanthin, and zeaxanthin (key components of the lutein cycle) in the ft plants at the tillering stage compared to the WT (Fig. 2E). These results showed that the mutation in the OsFT gene could potentially induce specific impairments in both the photosynthetic system and the carotenoid metabolic pathway.

# Overexpression of OsFT improves light energy utilization efficiency

# OsFT regulates tillering by influencing the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway

In our previous studies, PGL13 was also involved in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway, encoding a carotenoid isomerase (CRTISO). However, the pgl13 mutant exhibited an increased tiller number [38]. To explore the potential mechanisms by which the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway affects tillering in rice, we 

crossed two japonica rice mutants, pgl13 and ft, with identical genetic backgrounds, obtaining the double mutant ft/pgl13 (Fig. 5A). The tiller number statistics at the tillering stage showed that, compared with the WT, the tiller number of ft was significantly reduced, while that of pgl13 was significantly increased (Fig. 5B). The measurement results of pigment content showed that compared with the WT, the pigment content in both the ft and pgl13 mutants was significantly reduced (Fig. 5C). Overexpression of OsFT enhances drought stress tolerance and yield in rice 

Studies have shown that carotenoid isomerase has a crucial role in photoprotection and stress responses in plants, especially in the processes of ABA synthesis and response. Meanwhile, the hormpne ABA regulates plant responses to drought stress. Since FT-OE1 and PGL13-OE1 accumulated more ABA, we speculate that the OsFT gene may also be associated with drought stress. We also have found that overexpression of OsFT enhances light energy utilization efficiency, which is closely related to yield-related traits. Therefore, we conducted drought treatment on WT, ft, OE1, and OE2 plants 

and measured yield-related traits under both normal and drought conditions. 

Under normal conditions, $f t$ plants exhibited significantly reduced plant height, tiller count, and yield per plant compared to the WT. However, they showed a notable increase in thousand-grain weight, grain length, and grain width (Fig. 6A–D). In contrast, the plant height, tiller number, and yield per plant of OE1 and OE2 were significantly higher than those of the wild type, while the thousand-grain weight, grain length, and grain width were significantly lower (Fig. 6A–D). The trends in plant height, tiller number, and grain traits of WT, ft, OE1, and OE2 plants under drought stress were completely consistent with those under normal condition (Fig. 6A–G). In addition, under drought conditions, the plant height, tiller number, and yield per plant of OE1 and OE2 were significantly higher in comparison to the WT, but slightly lower than under normal conditions (Fig. 6A–G). These results 

indicate that overexpression of OsFT enhances plant adaptability to drought stress and increases yield. 

# Discussion

Carotenoids, a crucial group of terpenoid pigments, confer photoprotection to the photosynthetic machinery while also serving as vital precursors in the biosynthesis of SL and ABA [5,19]. This dual role profoundly affects photosynthesis and plant structure [19,24]. Over the past few decades, numerous genes involved in the biosynthesis pathway of carotenoids, from GGDP to all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene, have been identified in higher plants, primarily including OsPSY1, OsPDS, OsZDS, OsCRTISO, among others [20,23]. Previous studies have demonstrated that OsZDS plays a role in pre-harvest sprouting and photo-oxidation in rice [23]. However, it remains unclear whether the genetic mechanism associated with the biosynthesis pathway of carotenoids impacts photosynthesis and tiller development in rice. In this study, we found that the ft/phs2 mutant not only exhibits a zebra leaf phenotype but also produces fewer tillers (Fig. 1A and B). Further research revealed that OsFT, a novel allele of OsZDS encoding $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase, plays a key role in regulating photosynthesis and tiller development in rice (Fig. 1C–F). While genetic sequence analysis confirmed the allelic relationship between OsFT and OsZDS, our comprehensive molecular and physiological investigations significantly enhance the understanding of the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway. Notably, we demonstrate novel roles for OsFT in maintaining photosystem stability, orchestrating carotenoid metabolism, regulating tiller development through the SL/ABA signalling pathway and evaluating potential in responding to climate change and drought stress. These findings not only deepen our understanding of OsZDS/OsFT functional diversity but also provide a novel perspective for cultivating new rice varieties with higher photosynthetic efficiency, stronger stress resistance and higher yield. 

Carotenoids are crucial for the structural integrity and stability of membranes, performing vital functions in photosynthesis [6,18]. Therefore, mutants characterized by diminished carotenoid production typically exhibit abnormal chloroplast growth and compromised photosynthetic activity [10,23,38]. In the current study, compared with the WT, ft exhibited lower pigment contents (Fig. 1E), abnormal chloroplast development (Fig. 1F), along reduced expression of genes associated with photosynthesis (Fig. 2A–D), indicating that ft was in a decrease in the rate of photosynthesis. The decline in photosynthetic rate is often accompanied by PSII damage [12,15,16]. In this study, compared to the WT, the PSII proteins in the ft have incurred various degrees of damage (Fig. S3A), resulting in significant decreases in photosynthesis-related parameters (Fig. 4C). FtsHs occupy a crucial position in a conserved model of the PSII repair cycle [13,14,45]. FtsH2 is critical for maintaining the functionality of the PSII complex during exposure to high-light conditions [8,11,17]. In our study, we observed that OsFT and OsFtsh2 interact with each other and exhibit an additive effect in photosynthesis (Fig. 3A–F), and ft showed significant decreased in Y(II), ETR and NPQ, compared to the WT under high light conditions during the seedling stage (Fig. 4F, G, Fig. S4A). Collectively, these results indicated that OsFT maintains the function of the PSII complex by interacting with OsFtsh2, thereby playing a major role in regulating photosynthesis. 

The carotenoid synthesis pathway not only generates pigments critical for photosynthesis, providing energy for rice tillering but also protects the photosynthetic machinery from damage through the lutein cycle [25,38,46]. Compared to the WT, ft exhibited significantly reduced levels of violaxanthin, antheraxanthin, and zeaxanthin—key components of the lutein cycle—during the tillering stage (Fig. 2E). The synthesis of carotenoids begins with GGDP and gradually yields 

all trans-lycopene and then all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene through a sequence of enzymatic reactions in rice [5,22]. The loss of function of DN/ PGL13, encoding carotenoid isomerase (CRTISO), disrupts all-translycopene accumulation and blocks carotenoid biosynthesis [22,38]. Consistently, our results revealed significantly decreased all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ - carotene levels in ft, pgl13, and ft/pgl13 relative to WT during tillering (Fig. 5D). Importantly, intermediate products of the carotenoid synthesis pathway serve as precursors for ABA and SL biosynthesis [19]. These phytohormones coordinate rice architecture through synergistic regulation of tiller bud outgrowth, number, and angle, with profound implications for yield potential [4,5,23,34]. Previous studies have elucidated a reciprocal regulatory relationship between ABA and SL, with SL acting as a major regulatory factor of this hormonal feedback network in regulating tillering [5,22]. In this study, pgl13 exhibits a significant increase in tiller number, accompanied by decreased contents of SL and ABA (Fig. 5B, E), which is consistent with dn [22]. Conversely, ft displays a reduction in tiller numbers and a significant increase in SL content, indicating that endogenous SL constitute the primary determinant underlying differential tillering phenotypes between ft and pgl13 mutants (Fig. 5A, B and E). Notably, increased tillering in OE1 lines correlates with ABA overaccumulation, reduced SL levels, and upregulated ABA signalling pathway genes (Fig. 4A, Fig. S5A, B). We proposed that the elevated ABA accumulation in OE1 may strengthen the inhibitory effects on SL, thereby indirectly relieving SL-mediated suppression of tiller initiation and ultimately resulting in increased tiller numbers. Such genetic evidence reinforces the central regulatory role of SLs within the hormonal feedback architecture, particularly through antagonistic interactions with ABA signalling [5,22,32,34]. The ft/pgl13 exhibited a partially rescued tillering phenotype, with tiller numbers significantly increased compared to the ft but still reduced relative to pgl13, representing an intermediate phenotype between WT and single mutant plants (Fig. 5A, B). These results indicate functional synergy between OsFT and PGL13 in tiller regulation, with OsFT activity being at least partially dependent on PGL13 function. While feedback regulation constitutes the mechanisms between ABA and SL, the phenotypic recovery of ft/pgl13 implies involvement of parallel compensatory genetic pathways beyond feedback regulation [32,34,38]. In this study, while carotenoid biosynthesis is disrupted in ft/pgl13, both SL and ABA levels increase significantly (Fig. 5D, E), suggesting that OsFT may directly modulate key enzymatic in ABA biosynthesis. This regulatory rewiring could alleviate carotenoid pathway constraints, enabling hormonal signal integration critical for tiller development [22]. The loss of OsFT function may disrupt interactions with one or more critical enzymes in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway, leading to enhanced activity of key enzymes in the ABA biosynthesis pathway and subsequent modulation of tillering through direct or indirect mechanisms. Collectively, we concluded that rice tillering regulation operates through a highly integrated regulatory network that orchestrates interactions among carotenoid metabolism, ABA-SL hormonal feedback regulation, and genetic compensation pathways. Our findings highlight the SL and ABA as the primary determinant of tillering plasticity. Elucidating these mechanisms will require comprehensive interactome screening and targeted metabolomic analyses to characterize the functional contributions of carotenoid pathway genes to tiller architecture and yield traits in the future. 

Carotenoids, serving not only as crucial components of photosynthetic pigments, also play a pivotal role in regulating the levels of plant hormones such as ABA and SLs through key enzymes like DUONIE (DN) in their biosynthetic pathway [22]. These hormones not only modulate plant growth and developmental processes, including tillering, but also profoundly influence the plant’s responsiveness to environmental stresses, particularly drought. ABA serves as a crucial regulator in plant responses to drought stress, stimulating local ABA production in various plant organs 

to mediate stress signaling and physiological adjustments that optimize water use and enhance drought resistance [47]. In the current study, OE1 exhibited elevated endogenous ABA accumulation under normal conditions at the tillering stage (Fig. S5A). Concurrently, key photosynthetic parameters including net photosynthetic rate, intercellular ${ \mathrm { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentration, transpiration rate, and stomatal conductance were significantly elevated in OE1 compared to both WT and ft at the tillering stage (Fig. 4C). Furthermore, the expression level of antioxidant-related genes was significantly downregulated in ft mutants relative to WT, suggesting OsFT functions as a positive regulator of antioxidant defence mechanisms (Fig. 2C). As a positive regulator of ABA signalling pathway, OsPYL5 also serves as a critical activator of abiotic stress-responsive gene expression, thereby enhancing drought tolerance in rice through the promotion of stomatal closure [43]. In this study, a significant increase in OsPYL5 expression level was observed in OE1 lines, providing a potential molecular mechanism underlying the enhanced drought tolerance (Fig. S6B). Collectively, these findings support a mechanism where OsFT may prepare plants for drought stress by elevating ABA content to optimize stomatal dynamics, thereby improving water use efficiency and enhancing antioxidant capacity. Notably, OE1 exhibited significantly higher plant height, increased tiller number, enhanced yield performance specifically under drought stress, providing further evidence for the significant contribution of OsFT to drought tolerance (Fig. 6A–G). As a result, we may reasonably come to the conclusion that overexpression of OsFT enhances plant adaptability to drought stress and increases plant yield by regulating ABAmediated stress responses to drought. Our findings initially elucidated the molecular mechanisms underlying OsFT-mediated drought response, identifying a novel regulatory node within the rice stress response network. Notably, these findings not only establish the groundwork for dissecting the regulatory architecture of drought responses but also position OsFT as a valuable genetic resource for developing drought-resistant crops. Future researches should employ multi-omics integrated analyses combining transcriptomic and metabolomic profiling to systematically decipher the pleiotropic regulatory mechanisms through which OsFT modulates drought adaptation. 

# Conclusions

We cloned the OsFT from the new zebra-leaf phenotypic rice with reduced tiller number. OsFT encodes rice $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase, which is involved in carotenoid biosynthesis. The study found that the disruption of the OsFT gene function affects the degradation of photosynthetic proteins, thereby impacting the stability of the photosynthetic system. Additionally, OsFT regulates the levels of ABA and SL hormones by participating in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway, thus controlling tillering in rice. Overexpression of OsFT enhances the efficiency of light energy utilization, and the overexpressing plants exhibit stronger drought tolerance and higher yield. Taken together, this research enriches our understanding of the molecular mechanisms of genes related to the biosynthesis pathway of rice carotenoids regulating tillering.
