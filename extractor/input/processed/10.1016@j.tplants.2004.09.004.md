# Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate: a traffic signal in plant metabolism

Tom Hamborg Nielsen, Jesper Henrik Rung and Dorthe Villadsen

Plant Biochemistry Laboratory, Center for Molecular Plant Physiology, Department of Plant Biology, Royal Veterinary and Agricultural University, Thorvaldsensvej 40, DK-1871 Frederiksberg, Denmark

Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate $( \mathsf { F r u } - 2 , 6 - \mathsf { P } _ { 2 } )$ regulates key reactions of the primary carbohydrate metabolism in all eukaryotes. In plants, $F r u - 2 , 6 \mathrm { - } P _ { 2 }$ coordinates the photosynthetic carbon flux into sucrose and starch biosynthesis. The use of transgenic plants has allowed the regulatory models to be tested by modifying the Fru-2,6-P2 levels and the enzymes regulated by Fru-2,6-P2. Genes for the bifunctional plant enzyme that synthesizes and degrades Fru-2,6-P2 have been isolated and molecular characterization has provided new insight into structure and molecular regulation of the enzyme. Advances in Fru-2,6-P2 physiology and molecular biology are discussed. These advances have not only enlightened in vivo operation of $F r u - 2 , 6 \mathrm { - } P _ { 2 }$ $\cdot \mathsf { P } _ { 2 }$ but also revealed that the Fru-2,6-P2 regulatory system is highly complex and interacts with other regulatory mechanisms.

Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate $( \mathrm { F r u { - } } 2 , 6 { - } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } } )$ is a cellular signaling molecule found in all eukaryotes [1]. $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ regulates a single step in cytosolic metabolism: the interconversion of fructose-6-phosphate (Fru-6-P) and fructose-1,6-bisphosphate $( \mathrm { F r u - 1 } , 6 \mathrm { - } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } } )$ . This is a key junction in the highways of cellular carbon flow. Molecular data suggest that this regulatory system evolved in an ancestor common to all eukaryotic life forms. Because $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ has been retained for more than a billion years, $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ must have continuously provided a metabolic advantage to plants and other organisms. Fru-2,6-P2 was discovered in 1980 [2] as an activator of mammalian liver phosphofructokinase (PFK, EC 2.7.1.11) and was reported in higher plants in 1981 [3].

In non-plant organisms, $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ enhances glycolysis by activating PFK and inhibiting cytosolic fructose-1,6- bisphosphatase (cyt-FBPase, EC 3.1.3.11) [1]. In mammals, extensive research has revealed a complex system with tissue-specific isoforms of the enzyme that forms and degrades $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ , where some isoforms are regulated by protein phosphorylation [4]. The physiological relevance is emphasized by studies of transgenic mice with increased levels of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in the liver, resulting in decreased blood sugar levels [5].

In plants, the regulatory system possesses some fundamentally different features: $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ activates pyrophosphate-dependent PFK (PFP, EC 2.7.1.90), and plant PFKs are insensitive to $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ [6,7]. The role of

$\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in plant metabolism, based on characterization of physiology, biochemical analyses and enzymology, has been reviewed previously [6,7], providing a model for the regulation of photosynthetic metabolism. Since then, the model has been validated and refined (Figure 1) as molecular biology has allowed new approaches. This review summarizes recent advances in $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ physiology and molecular biology covering the structure and function of the bifunctional plant enzyme that synthesizes and degrades $\mathrm { F r u { - } } 2 , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } }$ , studies of transgenic plants, and the enzymes regulated by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ .

# Enzymes making and degrading $F r u { \bf - } 2 , 6 { \bf - } P _ { 2 }$

The synthesis and degradation of Fru-2,6-P2 is catalyzed by a unique bifunctional enzyme. The polypeptide harbors the fructose-6-phosphate,2-kinase (F6P,2K) (EC 2.7.1.105) and the fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase (F26BPase) (EC 3.1.3.46) activities, which phosphorylate and dephosphorylate the C-2 position of Fru-6-P, respectively. The mammalian enzymes (called PFK-2/FBPase-2) have been characterized in detail, including the structure of the crystallized enzyme [4]. Relatively limited information about the plant enzyme, which we call F2KP, was available until recently. Characterizations of F2KP activities in partially purified preparations, and of an enzyme purified to near homogeneity [8], established that plant F2KP enzymes share several basic allosteric properties with those of mammalian enzymes [7]. More recently, molecular biology approaches have enabled further characterization of the plant enzyme. Definitive proof of the bifunctionality of F2KP was first obtained by expressing potato (Solanum tuberosum) F2KP in E. coli [9]. cDNA clones encoding F2KP have now been isolated from several species (Figure 2) and expressed as active bifunctional enzymes.

# Structure and function of F2KPs

The amino acid sequences deduced from plant F2KP cDNAs predict a subunit size of $\sim 8 3 \mathrm { k D a }$ . F2KP has been purified from plant tissue as a tetramer [8], which has been corroborated by heterologous expression [10,11]. By contrast, the mammalian PFK-2/FBPase-2s assemble into homodimers of the $4 5 { \mathrm { - } } 5 0 \ { \mathrm { k D a } }$ subunits, which are arranged head to head with the two kinase regions facing each other [12].

# Isoforms of F2KP

A range of tissue-specific isoforms have been identified in mammals [4], whereas in plants, molecular data suggest that only one gene encodes the bifunctional enzyme. For example, in Arabidopsis, F2KP is encoded by a 6 kbp gene (At1g07110) comprising 23 exons [14]. Southern blot analyses indicate that there is also only one F2KP gene in several other plant species [9,10,14,15]. In Arabidopsis a predicted hypothetical protein (At3g30200) is homologous to a fragment of the AtF2KP N-terminus (V139–K248). However, other regions of this sequence resemble transposome-related proteins and it does not appear to be a functional gene.

Chromatographic separation of the F6P,2K and F26BPase activities in various plant species [7] has supported the presence of monofunctional enzymes in plants, as has the purification of a monofunctional F26BPase from spinach (Spinacia oleracea), with size and kinetic properties that are different from the bifunctional enzyme [16]. However, proteolytic degradation can result in active fragments of the phosphatase region.

Furthermore, transgenic Arabidopsis plants with strongly reduced levels of F2KP have no detectable F26BPase activity, indicating that a monofunctional enzyme is not present in Arabidopsis [17]. Conclusive proof for the existence of monofunctional enzymes in other plant species is also missing. This implies that regulation of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ levels depends entirely on the bifunctional enzyme, and cannot be modified by the differential expression of isozymes, as for example observed in yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), which has three monofunctional isoforms, two F6P,2Ks and one F26BPase [2].

# Regulation and modification of F2KP activity

F2KP activities are subject to complex allosteric regulation by metabolites representing intermediates of primary metabolism. Early studies of purified enzyme preparations demonstrated that triose-phosphates (triose-P), 3-phoshoglycerate (3-PGA), orthophosphate $( \mathbf { P } _ { i } )$ and Fru-6-P are key regulators of F2KP [7]. Recently, a range of potential regulators was tested on Arabidopsis and spinach F2KP [10,11]. Pyrophosphate $( \mathrm { P P } _ { i } )$ and phosphenolpyruvate (PEP) strongly inhibited F6P,2K activity, suggesting that these regulators have a prominent physiological role. For AtF2KP, several organic acids, particularly pyruvate, activate F6P,2K and inhibit F26BPase. Furthermore, 6-P-gluconate and $\mathrm { F r u – 1 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ inhibit F26BPase activity [11]. These complex properties might reflect that the regulatory system also operates under conditions other than photosynthetic metabolism. In future studies, it will be important to address the physiological impact of these properties in vivo.

Specific isoforms of the mammalian PFK-2/FBPase-2 are regulated by reversible protein-phosphorylation of N- or C-terminal extensions. Phosphorylation affects the kinase:phosphatase activity ratio, providing a powerful mechanism to regulate metabolism [4]. There is considerable

circumstantial evidence that reversible modification of F2KP occurs in plants. For example, changes in the kinase:phosphatase ratio have been observed in response to drought [18,19] (D. Villadsen et al. unpublished), salt stress [15] and shifting light conditions [20–22]. In addition, incubation of partially purified spinach F2KP with ATP induces a new form of the enzyme [23,24] and differentially phosphorylated forms occur in Arabidopsis leaves in developmental and diurnal patterns [25]. Recently two phosphorylation sites were identified in the N-terminus of AtF2KP (Ser220 and Ser303), which also binds 14-3-3 proteins in a phosphorylation-dependent manner [13]. This is corroborated by data showing that human cardiac PFK-2/FBPase-2 binds 14-3-3 proteins, resulting in altered cellular $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ content in human HeLa cells [26]. Phosphorylation and binding of 14-3-3 proteins regulates the activity of enzymes such as nitrate reductase and sucrose-phosphate synthase [27]. It is reasonable to assume that a similar mechanism exists for AtF2KP, thereby linking the regulation of key metabolic enzymes. Binding of 14-3-3s might also serve other functions such as enzyme localization [28] and targeting for or protecting against proteolytic degradation [27]. Elucidating the effects of phosphorylation and 14-3-3 binding on F2KP activity in planta will be important for further clarifying the role of the plant N-terminus.

# Fru-2,6-P2 – in planta function

The current model proposes that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ coordinates sucrose synthesis with starch formation and photosynthetic activity (Figure 1). This relies on the ability of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ to inhibit cyt-FBPase, whereas the activation of PFP appears less important for photosynthetic metabolism [29,30]. The amount of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in plant tissues typically ranges from $0 . 0 5 { - } 1 . 0 0 \mathrm { n m o l g F W } ^ { - 1 }$ . Even the lowest F6P,2-K activities reported can form these amounts of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ within minutes and there is no general correlation between the level of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ and F2KP activity in different plant tissues. Compared with sink tissues (tissues that import assimilates), F2KP activity and F2KP expression is typically higher in the mature leaves [9,29,31], which probably reflects the need for a fast regulatory system to follow the ever-changing photosynthetic metabolism. In sink tissues, the generally high levels of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ suggest a role in activating sink metabolism. However, there is not a single unifying model that can describe the function of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in the many types of sink tissues.

# Plants with modified Fru-2,6-P2 levels

Studies of transgenic and mutant plants have enabled a closer evaluation of the in vivo function of Fru- ${ \cdot 2 , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } } }$ . Two approaches have been applied to change $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ levels in vivo. One approach was the expression of modified ratliver enzymes to increase selectively either the F6P,2-K or the F26BPase activity in the plant tissues [32,33]. In the second approach, the activities of the native plant enzyme were reduced by antisense and co-suppression [17,34].

These transgenic studies have mainly focused on leaf metabolism. Tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) [32,35] and Kalancho¨e daigremontiana [33] leaves with increased

levels of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ partitioned less carbon into sucrose. Correspondingly, Arabidopsis [17], potato [34] and tobacco [35] plants with decreased levels of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ changed carbon partitioning in favor of sucrose formation, increasing the sucrose:starch $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ -labeling ratio by at least twofold [17,34]. In Arabidopsis, this resulted in a $2 0 { - } 3 0 \%$ higher level of sucrose and a delay in diurnal starch accumulation [17]. However, in tobacco [35] and potato [34], the extra sucrose was mainly cleaved and hexoses accumulated in the leaves. All these studies prove that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ contributes significantly to the regulation of leaf carbon partitioning during photosynthesis.

During starch mobilization, hexose or maltose units are exported from the chloroplasts rather than triose-P [36,37]. This implies that cyt-FBPase is not required for sucrose synthesis during the night, which has been verified for potato and Arabidopsis plants with decreased cyt-FBPase [38,39]. Consequently, the regulation of sucrose synthesis by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ is abolished during starch remobilization. Accordingly, metabolite levels are independent of $\mathrm { F r u { - } } 2 , 6 { - } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } }$ levels in Arabidopsis leaves during the night period [17]. By contrast, Peter Scott and Nicholas J. Kruger [40] observed changed levels of metabolites and less starch mobilization during darkness in tobacco plants with increased $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ levels. These authors suggested that the increased ${ \mathrm { 3 - P G A : P } } _ { i }$ ratio activated re-synthesis of starch and that the increased 3-PGA:Pi ratio could result from an activation of PFP by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ . PFP leaf activity is much higher in tobacco than it is in Arabidopsis, which might contribute to these reported differences.

Serving as a model for sink tissue, heterotrophic tobacco cells with increased $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ show more rapid cycling of carbon between triose-P and hexose-P, presumably because of PFP activation [41,42]. The information on sink tissues is still limited, and it appears to be difficult to obtain efficient reduction of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in sink tissue [34] and therefore it would be valuable to isolate knockout mutants without F2KP activity for such studies.

# Fru-2,6-P2 and stress responses

Even though $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ affects carbon partitioning no distinct phenotype has been reported. And yet, this regulatory system has been preserved in plants. Presumably Fru-2,6-P2 provides adaptive abilities that are advantageous under suboptimal growth conditions. We will now briefly consider osmotic stress, hypoxia and phosphate deficiency.

During drought, many plants accumulate soluble sugars, presumably as osmotica to prevent further desiccation. Both the level of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ and the kinase: phosphatase activity ratio of F2KP increase considerably in leaf tissue during drought or osmotic stress [15,18,19] (D. Villadsen et al. unpublished), suggesting that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ could have an important role in osmotic adaptation by regulating sucrose synthesis.

During hypoxia $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ levels increase in rice (Oryza sativa) seedlings [43], carrots (Daucus carota) [44] and Euglena gracilis [45]. This is likely to activate PFP to adapt the glycolytic metabolism to low oxygen concentrations. PFP, which uses $\mathrm { P P } _ { i }$ , could substitute for

PFK and thereby save ATP. Alternatively, PFP activity could provide $\mathrm { P P } _ { i }$ for sucrose synthase-dependent sucrose breakdown and vacuolar proton pumping [46,47]. Conversely, slicing potato tubers increases Fru- ${ \mathrm { 2 , 6 { - } P _ { 2 } } }$ levels [48,34] and PFP activity [49], presumably in response to the enhanced supply of oxygen, which can be restricted in bulky tissues [50]. This might activate PFP to provide substrate for the increased respiration. These seemingly contrasting results reflect differences in experimental systems and underline the flexibility in the primary metabolism of plants.

Fru-2,6-P2 might also serve to adapt metabolism during phosphate starvation. P-starved and P-sufficient Brassica cells and seedlings contain different isoforms of PFP [51,52]. Only the isoforms present during P-starvation are activated by $\mathrm { F r u { - } } 2 , 6 { \mathrm { - } } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } }$ , and activation of PFP might provide a P-saving glycolytic bypass [53]. Transgenic tobacco plants with modified $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ levels or PFP activity have not provided evidence that either PFP [30] or $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ [42] are required to adapt metabolism to P-starvation. Therefore, adapting the metabolism to P-starvation seems to depend on plant species and might be related to the different abilities of plants to form mycorrhizal symbiosis, as proposed by Vanessa R. Murley et al. [54].

# Enzyme activities and regulation by Fru-2,6-P2 Cyt-FBPase

Cyt-FBPase catalyzes a key step in photosynthetic sucrose biosynthesis; studies of plants with reduced cyt-FBPase [38,39,55] have verified that the enzyme is required for photosynthetic sucrose synthesis. Flaveria linearis [55] and Arabidopsis [39] plants with reduced cyt-FBPase have reduced growth rates. Potato lines with low cyt-FBPase have reduced photosynthesis but normal growth rate [38]. During photosynthesis, the level of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ is reduced in Arabidopsis [39] and potato leaves (J.H. Rung, et al. unpublished) with low cyt-FBPase activity. This might counteract the low cyt-FBPase by releasing it from inhibition by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ .

Cyt-FBPase is a homotetrameric protein – cDNAdeduced amino acid sequences [56,57] resemble the mammalian counterpart rather than the chloroplast FBPase. Cyt-FBPase is inhibited by nano-to-micromolar concentrations of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ , by AMP and by high levels of $\mathrm { F r u – 1 } , 6 { - } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ [7,56,58]. Animal FBPase binds $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ at the active site [59], which explains the competitive in vitro inhibition – this was also observed for a recombinant plant enzyme [58]. Interestingly, castor bean cyt-FBPase binds to aldolase [60] suggesting channeling of Fru-1,6-P2, which might affect binding of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in vivo.

# PFK

Cytosolic PFK, (cyt-PFK) catalyzes the entry to glycolytic metabolism in heterotrophic tissues. Purified plant cyt-PFK is regulated by $\mathrm { M g ^ { 2 + } }$ , PEP and $\mathrm { P } _ { i }$ [53]. Remarkably, $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ does not activate plant cyt-PFK [6], which contrasts with other eukaryotic organisms where PFK activation by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ is a cornerstone of the regulation of glycolysis. It has been proposed that rabbit muscle PFK has two repeated domains that fold into structurally

related sites creating a substrate binding site and a $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ binding site [61]. It would be interesting to compare this structure with the plant cyt-PFK to evaluate what defines the unique properties of the plant enzymes. However, plant genes encoding cyt-PFK still need to be identified. The Arabidopsis genome contains seven genes with homology to bacterial PFK (Figure 3). These genes only have one PFK-like domain, and this suggests a structure that is different from the mammalian enzyme. Establishing the precise identities of these genes to discover those encoding plant PFKs remains an important task.

# PFP

Plant PFP is activated by nanomolar levels of $\mathrm { F r u - 2 , 6 \mathrm { - } P _ { 2 } } ,$ but in vivo activation presumably requires higher concentrations of $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ [7,62,63]. Fru-1,6-P2 also activates PFP [64,65]; assays measuring formation of Fru-6-P include sufficient Fru- ${ \cdot } 1 { , } 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P _ { 2 } }$ to mask activation by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ . Occasionally, this has lead to the misconception that $\mathrm { F r u - 2 , 6 { - } P _ { 2 } }$ preferentially activates in the glycolytic direction. However, $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ clearly activates in both directions [7,64,65]. In vivo, PFP operates near equilibrium; it has been discussed whether PFP primarily serves a role in glycolysis, gluconeogenesis or in $\mathrm { P P } _ { i }$ formation or removal [7]. Experiments using transgenic plants with reduced PFP activity have confirmed that PFP is involved in glycolytic metabolism [29,30,66] but showed remarkably small changes in carbon flux: a physiological role remains enigmatic [46]. It is intriguing that PFP does not shortcut the regulation exerted over cyt-FBPase.

Plant PFP contains two related types of subunits, $\pmb { \alpha }$ and $\beta$ [67,68] (Figure 3). The $\beta$ -subunit is suggested to be catalytic, whereas the $\pmb { \alpha }$ -subunit might serve a regulatory function [67,51]. Conclusive proof for this is still needed, and heterologous expression would be valuable to establish the function of the subunits.

PFP is also found in some protists [69] and bacteria [70] as homomeric proteins, which usually are not activated by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ . PFP has regions of similarity to PFK, and based on modeling of PFP over a bacterial PFK structure it was hypothesized that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ binds at the interface between the $\beta$ and $\pmb { \alpha }$ subunits [71]. However, analogy to eukaryotic PFKs suggests that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ binds at a modified catalytic site – perhaps in the $\pmb { \alpha }$ subunits. Recently, the structure of a bacterial (Borrelia burgdorferi) PFP was resolved [72]. This enzyme is closely related to plant PFP [69] and has presumably been acquired from plants by lateral gene transfer, as suggested for Chlamydia trachomatis PFP [73]. The core structure of the Borrelia enzyme resembles bacterial PFK [72], whereas the sequence similarity to plant PFP extends throughout the enzyme. This should enable modeling of the plant enzyme to be improved.

# Concluding remarks

Recent research has revealed the molecular basis for regulation of metabolism by $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ in plants. The gene encoding F2KP has been cloned and the enzyme characterized. F2KP is reversibly phosphorylated and binds 14-3-3 proteins. Experiments using transgenic plants have verified that $\mathrm { F r u – 2 } , 6 { \cdot } \mathrm { P } _ { 2 }$ regulates photosynthetic sucrose
