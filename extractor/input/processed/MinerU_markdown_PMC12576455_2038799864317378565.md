# ABSTRACT

Soil salinisation poses a significant threat to alfalfa (Medicago sativa L.) growth and development, limiting its productivity and hindering its widespread cultivation. Hydrogen sulphide $\mathrm { ( H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S ) }$ serves as an important gaseous signalling molecule in plants, mediating a myriad of physiological processes like salt tolerance. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ accumulation and its regulation under salinity stress in alfalfa are still not fully elucidated. In this study, we demonstrated that MsNAC2a, a NAC transcription factor, is a negative modulator of salt stress resistance in alfalfa. Constitutive overexpression of MsNAC2a downregulated the expression of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ biosynthesis-related genes, such as $L$ -CYSTEINE DESULFHYDRASE1 (MsLCD1), and upregulated the O-ACETYLSERINE(THIOL)LYASE ISOFORM A1 (MsOASA1) gene, a key gene involved in $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ metabolism, while also suppressing the expression of reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging genes like MsCOX11, leading to a reduction in hydrogen sulphide levels and an increase in ROS accumulation, ultimately impairing the plant's salt tolerance. Furthermore, the AP2/EREBP-type transcription factor MsEREBP1 physically interacts with MsNAC2a both in vivo and in vitro, influencing its transcriptional activity and modulating salt stress responses in alfalfa. Conversely, silencing MsNAC2a enhanced salt stress resistance without affecting plant growth or yield. Collectively, our study highlights that MsNAC2a precisely regulates the homeostasis of salt stress responses and provides new insights into the mechanisms by which the cooperative interaction between MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1 fine-tunes the homeostasis of endogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ levels, thereby influencing alfalfa's salt tolerance and offering valuable strategies for improving crop resilience under saline stress. 

Alfalfa (Medicago sativa L.), often referred to as the ‘Queen of Forages’, is a crucial forage crop appreciated worldwide for its high nutritional value and its ability to enhance soil fertility through nitrogen fixation. Due to its rich nutritional composition and high economic value, it has become an essential forage crop, contributing significantly to the sustainability of agricultural systems around the world (Ye et al. 2025; Shi, Liu, et al. 2024; Annicchiarico et al. 2015; Gou et al. 2018). However, alfalfa's growth and productivity are increasingly threatened by abiotic stresses, especially soil salinity. Salt stress adversely affects alfalfa's germination, root development, water uptake, and photosynthetic efficiency, resulting in reduced biomass yield and forage quality (Gou et al. 2018; Ma et al. 2023). As global land salinisation continues to worsen due to climate change, irrigation practices, and soil degradation, the need for developing salt-tolerant alfalfa varieties has become increasingly urgent (Morton et  al.  2019; Kim et  al.  2007; Zhan et  al.  2021). While improving salt tolerance in alfalfa is critical to ensure sustainable forage production in saline-affected regions, the underlying key genes and regulatory mechanisms responsible for salt tolerance in alfalfa remain largely unknown. Although genomic research has made significant progress, the molecular pathways involved in alfalfa's response to salt stress remain poorly understood, emphasising the importance of further studies to enable the development of salt-tolerant, resilient alfalfa varieties (Lu et al. 2024; Cheng et al. 2021; Zhu 2016; Liu, Zhang, et al. 2024; Bao et al. 2022). 

Plants, as sessile organisms, have developed multifaceted strategies at molecular, biochemical, physiological and developmental levels to counteract salt stress, activating pathways that regulate calcium $( \mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } )$ signalling, ion homeostasis, ROS levels and antioxidant responses to increase resilience (Lu et al. 2025; Sun et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2022; Li, Zhou, et al. 2023; Liang et al. 2024; Bernstein and Hayward 1958). Salt tolerance in plants is mediated by a complex signalling network involving the $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ -dependent Salt Overly Sensitive (SOS) pathway, comprising SOS1, SOS2 and SOS3, in addition to other factors like CALCINEURIN B-LIKE PROTEIN 10 (CBL10), HIGH-AFFINITY $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ TRANSPORTERS (HKTs) and $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / H ^ { + } }$ EXCHANGERS (NHXs), which also contribute to ion transport and stress adaptation (Wei et  al.  2025; Kim et  al.  2007; Lin et  al.  2009; Li, Zhao, et  al.  2023; Ren et  al.  2005; Zhou et  al.  2024; Ramakrishna et  al.  2025; van Zelm et al. 2020). Transcription factors (TFs) play pivotal roles in mediating plant responses to various abiotic stresses, particularly salt stress. Among these regulatory proteins, several major families have been extensively studied, including AP2/ ERF (APETALA2/ethylene responsive factor), MYB, NAC, WRKY and bZIP (basic leucine zipper) (Zhu  2016; Zhang et al. 2022; Chaudhry et al. 2021; Xiong et al. 2025). Recent studies have demonstrated that plants initiate comprehensive transcriptomic reprogramming when exposed to salt stress, with numerous transcription factors serving as crucial regulators of stress-responsive genes. These TFs orchestrate multiple physiological processes essential for salt tolerance, particularly through the regulation of ion homeostasis and antioxidant defence systems (Wei et al. 2025; Shi, Wang, et al. 2024; Song et al. 2025; Waadt et al. 2022; Zhang, Long, et al. 2020). 

Despite significant progress in understanding TF-mediated salt stress responses in model plants, research focusing on alfalfa (M. sativa) remains relatively limited. Specifically, the functional characterisation of transcription factors in regulating alfalfa's salt response mechanisms and their potential applications in enhancing salinity resistance warrant further investigation (Su et  al.  2025; Guo et  al.  2024; Liu, Elzenga, et al. 2024; Liu, Zhang, et al. 2024). 

The NAC (NAM, ATAF1/2, CUC2) family represents one of the largest families of plant-specific transcription factors. These proteins are typically defined by a conserved N-terminal DNAbinding domain, a nuclear localisation signal, and a divergent C-terminal domain. NAC transcription factors are known to play a crucial role in regulating plant growth, development, and responses to environmental stresses. Their involvement extends across a variety of processes, from phytohormone signalling and cell differentiation to flowering time regulation and organogenesis (Han et  al.  2023; Mao et  al.  2017, 2018; Liang et al. 2014; Zhu et al. 2015; Xie et al. 2002; Xi et al. 2019; Olsen et al. 2005; Chen et al. 2015). Particularly in the case of abiotic stresses such as salt stress, NACs help plants adapt by regulating stress-responsive genes and signalling pathways, thus protecting them from the detrimental effects of high salinity, including ionic imbalance and osmotic stress (Mao et al. 2018; Hu et al. 2006; Han et al. 2023). For example, studies have shown that ATAFA1 and AtJUB1 are essential for the salt stress response in Arabidopsis. Overexpression of ATAF1 in Arabidopsis under salt stress conditions significantly enhances plant sensitivity to both oxidative and salt stress, indicating that the ATAF1-mediated signalling pathway plays a crucial role in regulating ROS homeostasis during salt stress responses (Wu et al. 2009). Meanwhile, AtJUB1, a NAC-type transcription factor, enhances resilience to salt stress by upregulating osmoprotectant biosynthesis (Alshareef et al. 2019). In rice, OsNAC2, OsNAC5, OsNAC6, and ONAC022 are known to play important roles in salt stress responses (Mao et al. 2018; Takasaki et al. 2010; Hong et al. 2016). OsNAC2 negatively regulates salt tolerance by promoting ROS accumulation and ion imbalance. Overexpression induces programmed cell death, characterised by DNA fragmentation and increased caspaselike activity. This is associated with the regulation of stressresponsive genes like OsCOX11 (ROS scavenger), OsAP37 (caspase-like protease), and $\mathrm { K ^ { + } }$ efflux channels (OsGORK and OsSKOR), resulting in disrupted cellular homeostasis and reduced salt tolerance (Mao et  al.  2018). Additionally, overexpressing ONAC022 in rice enhances salt tolerance by upregulating ABA biosynthesis genes, such as OsNCEDs and OsPSY, thereby promoting ABA synthesis and contributing to the improved salt resistance (Hong et al. 2016). The interaction between OsNAC5 and OsNAC6 strengthens salt stress tolerance by synergistically increasing the expression of OsLEA3, a gene related to late embryogenesis abundance (Takasaki et al. 2010). Recent findings emphasise the critical role of NAC transcription factors in conferring salt tolerance. In particular, NAC genes like SlNAC10 in halophyte Suaeda liaotungensis K. (Du et al. 2022), GmNAC109 and GmSIN1 in soybean (Li et  al.  2019; Yang et  al.  2019), IbNAC3 in sweet potato (Meng et al. 2023), TaSIP1 in wheat (Wang et al. 2022), and BpNAC012 in Betula platyphylla (Hu et al. 2019) are key players in enhancing salt stress resistance. Although research 

on NAC transcription factors and their role in salt tolerance has been increasing, studies specifically focusing on NACs involved in salt stress regulation in alfalfa are limited. In particular, the molecular mechanisms underlying NAC-mediated salt tolerance in alfalfa remain scarcely explored and require further investigation. 

The AP2/ERF (Apetala2/ethylene responsive factor) transcription factor family, specific to plants, plays a crucial role in regulating plant responses to environmental stimuli as well as growth and development. The function of these factors is determined by the presence of one or two highly conserved 60-amino acid AP2 domains in the protein (Nakano et al. 2006; Sharoni et al. 2011; Cui et al. 2025; Jin et al. 2019; Xu et al. 2020). In rice, the AP2/EREBP transcription factor OsEREBP1 has been shown to enhance resistance to both biotic and abiotic stresses. It plays a crucial role as a key component in the pathogen defence signalling cascade as well as in the plant's response to environmental stress (Jisha et al. 2015). Overexpression of OsEREBP1 led to the upregulation of genes involved in lipid metabolism, jasmonate and abscisic acid biosynthesis, resulting in increased jasmonate and abscisic acid levels that enhanced drought and submergence resistance while also reducing susceptibility to Xoo infections, a process mediated by the Xa21-binding protein OsXb22a (Jisha et al. 2015). In recent investigations, it was found that OsNAC2 competitively binds to OsEREBP1 in the cytoplasm, inducing OsEREBP1 instability and disrupting its interaction with the Xa21-binding protein OsXb22a, a crucial element for pathogen defence, resulting in weakened resistance to bacterial blight (Zhong et  al.  2024). However, the involvement of OsEREBP1 and its homologues in the salt stress response remains unclear, and its functional role in alfalfa has yet to be fully elucidated, necessitating further investigation. 

$\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ , traditionally regarded as a toxic gas characterised by its distinctive rotten egg-like odour, has recently been identified as the third endogenous gasotransmitter in plants, alongside nitric oxide (NO) and carbon monoxide (CO), based on emerging research (Sun et al. 2022; Fang et al. 2017). $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ has been shown to be pivotal in several key plant processes, including root development, nodule symbiosis, seedling growth, stomatal regulation, flowering, leaf senescence, fruit ripening, and the adaptation to abiotic stresses such as drought, salt, low temperatures, and osmotic stress. Specifically, when NaHS, a donor of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ , is applied exogenously, it has been shown to enhance salt stress tolerance in plants, demonstrating that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ functions as a beneficial signalling molecule during salt stress (Sun et al. 2022; Fang et al. 2017; Zhang, Cai, et al. 2020; Matamoros et al. 2024; Liu, Si, et al. 2024; Xiang et al. 2023; Zhang et  al.  2021; Wang et  al.  2024). In alfalfa, the application of exogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ affects the $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / H ^ { + } }$ antiporter, nonselective cation channels (NSCCs), and SOS1 pathways, which contribute to the regulation of $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ concentrations and the reduction of the $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / K ^ { + } }$ ratio, thus enhancing salt tolerance (Lai et al. 2014; Jiang et al. 2019). Likewise, research in crops like cucumber and wheat demonstrates that exogenous hydrogen sulphide improves salt tolerance by regulating $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ and $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ levels in the roots (Deng et al. 2016). Recent studies have increasingly highlighted the role of hydrogen sulphide in mitigating oxidative damage induced by salt stress in a variety of 

plant species. For instance, in strawberries, exogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ application maintains high levels of ascorbic acid and glutathione, helping plants cope with salt stress (Christou et al. 2013). Similarly, in soybean and alfalfa, pretreatment with $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ preserves elevated levels of homoglutathione (hGSH), counteracting the damage caused by NaCl (Cui et al. 2014; Lai et al. 2014). In cucumber and pepper plants, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ donors have been found to enhance the activity of antioxidant enzymes, including superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), ascorbate peroxidase (APX), and glutathione reductase (GR), which reduces reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels and prevents lipid peroxidation (Lisjak et al. 2013; Yu et al. 2013). Recent research in Arabidopsis has shown that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ can alleviate salt stress by promoting root growth and influencing auxin signalling, demonstrating its potential in improving plant resilience to saline environments (Yang et al. 2023). To uncover the underlying mechanisms of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ action, researchers have investigated its ability to modify specific proteins through persulfidation, offering deeper insights into how this modification influences plant stress responses. For example, in Arabidopsis, persulfidation of SnRK2.6 and ABI4, key components of the ABA signalling network, alters their activity, contributing to the fine-tuned regulation of ABA responses (Zhou et  al.  2021; Chen et  al.  2020). Additionally, hydrogen sulphide, through the persulfidation of cytokinin dehydrogenase CKX2, antagonises cytokinin signalling, thereby modulating root system architecture (Wang et  al.  2024). In Chinese cabbage, pear, and tomato, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ influences flowering time and fruit ripening by persulfidating key transcription factors, such as BraFLCs, PyMYB10, SlBRG3, and SlWRKY6, which are crucial for regulating these developmental processes, highlighting the importance of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ in controlling plant development (Ma et al. 2021; Sun et al. 2023; Yao et al. 2023; Zhang et al. 2024). It is well established that $\mathrm { L }$ -CYSTEINE DESULFHYDRASE (LCD) is essential for producing $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ from $\mathrm { L }$ -cysteine in plants, while O-ACETYLSERINE(THIOL)LYASE ISOFORM A1(OASA1) plays a crucial role in detoxifying $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ , thereby influencing its overall balance within the plant (Li et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2024). Despite the importance of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ in plant stress responses, the transcriptional mechanisms governing $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ biosynthesis under salinity stress and their contribution to salt tolerance in alfalfa are still not well understood. 

In this study, we demonstrate that MsNAC2a is a pivotal regulator in alfalfa's response to salt stress. Under NaCl treatment, the expression of MsNAC2a was significantly upregulated. Functional analyses revealed that overexpression of MsNAC2a impaired the plant's salt stress tolerance, whereas its silencing enhanced the ability to withstand salt stress. Mechanistically, MsNAC2a disrupted $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ homeostasis by downregulating $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ biosynthesis-related genes, such as -CYSTEINE DESULFHYDRASE1 (MsLCD1), and upregulating O-ACETYLSERINE(THIOL)LYASE ISOFORM A1 (MsOASA1), a key gene in $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ metabolism. Additionally, it inhibited the expression of ROS-scavenging genes, such as MsCOX11, leading to an increase in ROS accumulation. Furthermore, the AP2/ EREBP-type transcription factor MsEREBP1 physically interacts with MsNAC2a both in  vivo and in  vitro, influencing its transcriptional activity and modulating salt stress responses in alfalfa. The identification of the MsNAC2a–MsEREBP1– $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ signalling pathway unveils a novel regulatory mechanism 

underlying $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ -mediated salt tolerance, shedding light on the role of NAC transcription factors in plant stress adaptation and offering potential targets for improving crop resilience to salinity. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Cloning and Characterisation of MsNAC2a as a Salt-Responsive Factor

The transcription factor NAC2 plays a crucial role in regulating plant growth, development, and stress responses. While extensive research has been conducted on its function in Arabidopsis and rice (Chen et al. 2015; Mao et al. 2017, 2018, 2020; Xi et al. 2019; Zhong et al. 2024; He et al. 2005; Balazadeh et al. 2010), its roles in other plant species (e.g., alfalfa), particularly in mediating salt tolerance mechanisms, remain largely unexplored. Previous transcriptome and quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT-PCR) data demonstrate that MsNAC2a is significantly induced under salt stress (Luo et  al.  2019), suggesting its important involvement in plant salt stress tolerance responses. Conversely, MsNAC2b, a homologue of MsNAC2a, displayed no significant response to salt stress. As a result, we prioritised MsNAC2a for further exploration in this study. 

To examine the evolutionary relationship of MsNAC2a and MsNAC2b with their homologous proteins in other higher plants, we constructed a phylogenetic tree using the protein database from the National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) and the Neighbour-Joining (NJ) method. By aligning the amino acid sequences of MsNAC2a and MsNAC2b with 10 homologous proteins from other plant species, we identified conserved motifs and key structural features. As depicted in Figure 1A, MsNAC2a encodes a 303- amino acid protein with a molecular weight of $3 4 . 5 6 \mathrm { k D a }$ (kilodaltons). The sequence of MsNAC2a demonstrates significant identity with several NAC2 transcription factors, including OsNAC2 (from rice, Oryza sativa) and TaNAC2 (from wheat, Triticum aestivum), and exhibits the highest similarity to AtNAC2 from Arabidopsis thaliana (Figure  1A). This result suggests that MsNAC2a, a homologue of AtNAC2 and OsNAC2, belongs to the NAC family of transcription factors, and it is speculated to play a crucial role in the molecular response to salt tolerance in alfalfa. 

As mentioned earlier, transcriptome data indicate that MsNAC2a is significantly induced under salt stress. To further validate the expression profile of MsNAC2a, qRT-PCR was performed on different alfalfa tissues and under a range of stress conditions. Upon exposure to 200 mM NaCl, the expression levels of MsNAC2a began to increase after 1 h, peaking at 6 h with a 40-fold rise compared to control plants, and then slowly decreased (Figure  1B). Additionally, the expression of MsNAC2b followed a similar trend, but the peak was approximately seven times higher than that of the control group (Figure  1B). The temporal and spatial expression of MsNAC2a in alfalfa showed that the MsNAC2a transcript was detected in all the tissues (root, stem, leaf and flower), with the highest expression observed in leaf and the lowest levels 

found in flowers (Figure 1C). Under salt stress, MsNAC2a expression was greatly upregulated, reaching its peak at 6 h in alfalfa roots and $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ in leaves (Figure 1D). NaHS $\mathrm { ~ H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ donor) treatment led to a more than two-fold increase in MsNAC2a expression at 6 h in roots and $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ in leaves (Figure  1E). Similarly, ABA also significantly upregulated the expression of MsNAC2a (Figure S1A). However, under drought and lowtemperature stress conditions, MsNAC2a did not show a significant upregulation (Figure  S1B,C), suggesting a specific response to salt stress and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ signalling. In order to study the salt stress response of MsNAC2a, its promoter was linked to the GUS reporter gene. Histochemical staining and GUS activity analysis revealed that the MsNAC2a promoter drove elevated GUS activity under salt stress, in contrast to the control under normal conditions (Figure 1F,G), in accordance with its expression profile under salt stress. Therefore, these findings indicate that MsNAC2a plays a significant role in responding to abiotic stresses, including salinity, and that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ may be involved in this process. 

The proper subcellular localisation of a protein is vital for its function (Tang et  al.  2014). Hence, we investigated the subcellular localisation of the MsNAC2a protein by conducting a green fluorescent protein (GFP)-tagging assay. To achieve constitutive expression, the coding region of MsNAC2a was linked to the N-terminus of the GFP reporter gene, driven by the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. Agrobacterium-mediated transformation was used to introduce the 35S-GFP and 35S-MsNAC2a-GFP constructs into the leaf epidermal cells of tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana). Fluorescent observations confirmed that MsNAC2a-GFP is predominantly localised in the nucleus, and its fluorescence signals coincide with those of the nuclear marker BES1nmCherry (Figure  1H). By comparison, the GFP empty vector control was distributed throughout the cells without any specific localisation (Figure 1H). The result suggests that the MsNAC2a protein is exclusively localised to the nucleus. In addition, to determine whether MsNAC2a has transactivation activity, the full-length coding sequence of MsNAC2a was fused to the GAL4 DNA-binding domain in the pGBKT7 vector and introduced into the yeast strain. Yeast transformants harbouring the BD-MsNAC2a fusion construct were able to grow on SD/-Trp/-His medium, while those carrying the empty pGBKT7 vector failed to grow under the same conditions, indicating that MsNAC2a possesses transcriptional activation activity in yeast (Figure S1D). Based on the above observations, it can be inferred that MsNAC2a is a stressinduced NAC transcription factor that may be involved in regulating salt tolerance in alfalfa.

To better understand the biological function of MsNAC2a in plant response to salt stress, we constructed two constructs: the 35S:MsNAC2a overexpression vector (OE) and the MsNAC2a-RNAi cassette (Ri). Using the Agrobacterium-mediated transformation method, we successfully generated stable transgenic alfalfa plants, which included 30 lines of MsNAC2a-OE plants and 20 lines of MsNAC2a-RNAi plants. Based on the qRT-PCR analysis, which revealed significant differential gene expression levels, we selected two independent overexpression lines (OE6 and OE13) and two RNAi-silenced lines (Ri3 and Ri15) for functional characterisation (Figure S2B). These transgenic lines were subsequently employed to investigate their specific roles in salt stress response mechanisms and to evaluate their effects on plant growth parameters and stress tolerance capacity. 

For salt stress tolerance evaluation, 4-week-old soil-cultivated seedlings of WT and transgenic plants were treated with $5 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl for 5 days to examine their growth performance and stress adaptation under salinity stress. Conversely, the MsNAC2a-RNAi lines displayed reduced symptoms of salinity stress, with fewer signs of wilting and leaf curling, which indicates better salt tolerance (Figure 2F). To validate these observations, fresh weight, Fv/Fm ratio, and electrolyte leakage were measured in transgenic lines and WT plants after NaCl treatment. Upon salt stress exposure, compared to the WT plants, MsNAC2a-OE plants exhibited significantly lower fresh weight and Fv/Fm ratio, along with a significant increase in electrolyte leakage. On the other hand, the RNAi lines displayed elevated fresh weight and Fv/Fm ratio and a reduction in electrolyte leakage (Figure 2G–I). Taken together, 

these results indicate that MsNAC2a could play a key role as a negative regulator in the salinity stress response in alfalfa.

# 2.5 | MsNAC2a Regulates the Transcript Levels of MsOASA1, MsLCD1 and MsCOX11 by Directly Binding to Their Promoters

As demonstrated in the findings above (Figure  5), MsNAC2a might modulate the expression levels of some genes, including MsSOD1, MsSOD3, MsCAT, MsAPX1, MsAPX2, MsCOX11, MsLCD1, MsLCD2, and MsOASA1, under salt stress conditions. To gain deeper insight into how MsNAC2a modulates plant responses to salt stress, we evaluated its capacity to directly control the expression of these genes. We conducted a comprehensive bioinformatics analysis of the promoter sequences of the aforementioned genes. The analysis revealed that only a subset of these gene promoters contained multiple TAGC cis-elements (putative binding sites for NAC), suggesting that these conserved motifs may serve as potential binding sites and play a crucial role in the transcriptional regulation of these target genes by MsNAC2a (Figure 6A). Through chromatin immunoprecipitation quantitative PCR (ChIP-qPCR) analysis, we observed that MsNAC2a specifically interacts with the TAGC elements in the promoters of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11 (Figure  6A), but it did not appear to bind to the promoters of MsLCD2 and MsDCD1 (Figure  S4). In order to further explore if MsNAC2a could bind to the promoters of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11, we carried out a yeast one-hybrid assay to assess this hypothesis. Therefore, the promoter regions of the three target genes were cloned into pHIS vectors, and recombinant pGADT7- MsNAC2a plasmids were subsequently constructed. These constructs were then transformed into the Y1H Gold yeast strain for further analysis. According to the Y1H assay, MsNAC2a could directly bind to the promoter regions of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11 (Figure  6B). Moreover, in order to gain deeper insights into the interactions discussed earlier, we performed 

dual-luciferase promoter activation assays using the N. benthamiana system. The promoter regions of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11 were cloned into pGreenII0800-LUC, while the ORF of MsNAC2a was integrated into the pGreenII62-SK vector. As shown in Figure 6C, these findings of the dual-luciferase reporter assays indicate that the LUC/REN ratio was elevated compared to the control when pGreenII62-SK-MsNAC2a was co-infiltrated with pGreenII0800-LUC-MsOASA1pro, whereas the ratio was reduced when pGreenII62-SK-MsNAC2a was co-infiltrated with pGreenII0800-LUC-MsLCD1pro and pGreenII0800-LUC-MsCOX11pro, respectively. Also, we performed an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) to assess the interaction between MsNAC2a and promoter probes of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11. As revealed in Figure 6D–F, these results suggest that the purified recombinant protein His-MsNAC2a showed strong binding to specific regions of the target promoters of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11 in  vitro (Figure 6D–F). It is noteworthy that excess unlabelled wild-type probes obviously decreased the binding affinity of MsNAC2a, while mutated probes showed no effect on binding. Altogether, these findings imply that MsNAC2a might modulate the transcript levels of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11 by directly binding to their promoters, thus modulating salt stress tolerance in alfalfa.

# 2.7 | MsEREBP1 Physically Binds to MsNAC2a to Influence Its Transcriptional Activity

Recent studies have shown that EREBP1 is involved in modulating plant resilience to biotic and abiotic stresses, with the interaction between OsEREBP1 and OsNAC2 notably modulating disease resistance in transgenic rice (Zhong et al. 2024; Jisha et al. 2015). Given the homology between MsNAC2a and OsNAC2 and its significant role in alfalfa's response to salt stress as previously mentioned, we analysed the transcript levels of MsEREBP1 under salt stress conditions. As shown in Figure  S7A, MsEREBP1 expression increases in response to salt stress, suggesting its involvement in the alfalfa's salt stress response. Furthermore, we investigated if MsEREBP1 and MsNAC2a interact to fine-tune the plant's response to salt stress. To validate the hypothesis, the pot-to-pot Y2H assay was carried out by co-transforming the yeast strain with MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1. Consistent with our expectations, only the yeast cells containing both MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1 were able to grow on selective media (Figure 8A), suggesting a strong interaction between the two proteins. To further validate the interaction, Co-IP assays were conducted, and the results showed that 6xMYC-tagged MsEREBP1 was coimmunoprecipitated with GFP-tagged MsNAC2a (Figure 8B), confirming the interaction between the two proteins. Furthermore, BiFC assays were performed, and it was found that the interaction of MsNAC2a-nYFP with MsEREBP1-cYFP produced strong nuclear fluorescence in tobacco leaf epidermal cells, which overlapped with the BES1n-mCherry nuclear marker (Figure 8C). Additionally, considering the interaction between MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1, we sought to investigate whether this interaction modulates the transcriptional activity of MsNAC2a. To answer this query, we conducted dual-luciferase promoter activation assays using the N. benthamiana system. In this assay, pGreenII62-SK-MsNAC2a, either individually or together with pGreenII62-SK-MsEREBP1, was co-transfected with pGreenII0800-LUC-MsOASA1pro, pGreenII0800-LUC-MsLCD1pro, and pGreenII0800-LUC-MsCOX11pro into tobacco leaves. The results of the assays suggest that MsEREBP1 attenuated the suppression of MsNAC2a on the expression of MsLCD1 and MsCOX11, as well as diminishing the transcriptional activation capacity of MsNAC2a in regulating the expression of MsOASA1 (Figure  8D). To further investigate whether the interaction between MsEREBP1 and MsNAC2a affects MsNAC2a's binding to the promoters of downstream target genes, ChIP-PCR was performed using the same transiently expressed tobacco leaves described above. The results showed that MsEREBP1 has no effect on MsNAC2a's binding to the promoters of MsOASA1, MsLCD1, and MsCOX11(Figure  S7B). Thus, MsEREBP1 is unlikely to regulate MsNAC2a via modulation of its DNA-binding activity. Furthermore, we obtained the MsNAC2a-OE/TRV-MsEREBP1 lines and MsNAC2a-OE/TRV-Control lines based on the MsNAC2a-OE lines through VIGS technology. When exposed to high salinity, the MsNAC2a-OE/TRV-MsEREBP1 lines displayed obviously increased sensitivity to salt stress, characterised by a more intense wilting phenotype compared 

to control plants (Figure  9A). Similarly, after salt treatment, the MsNAC2a-OE/TRV-MsEREBP1 lines exhibited significantly higher relative electrolyte leakage and MDA concentration, alongside a notable decrease in fresh weight and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ content compared to control plants (Figure 9B–E). These findings provide further genetic evidence for the interaction between MsEREBP1 and MsNAC2a and imply that MsEREBP1 functions as a positive regulator of salt tolerance in alfalfa. Taken together, these observations reveal that the MsEREBP1- MsNAC2a protein complex mediates the transcriptional regulation of downstream target genes, particularly genes associated with hydrogen sulphide synthesis and metabolic processes, orchestrating a coordinated plant response to salt stress in alfalfa. 

# 3 | Discussion

Alfalfa (M. sativa L.), known as the ‘Queen of Forages’, is a widely cultivated forage crop appreciated for its high nutritional value and its essential role in nitrogen fixation, which enhances soil productivity (Annicchiarico et  al.  2015; Gou et al. 2018; Ye et al. 2025). Global land salinisation presents a growing challenge to alfalfa production, adversely impacting key physiological and metabolic functions. Salt stress interferes with germination, root growth, water balance, and photosynthetic activity, ultimately compromising biomass output and forage value (Gou et al. 2018; Ma et al. 2023). The adaptive mechanisms plants employ to cope with salt stress are highly complex, encompassing osmotic regulation, redox modulation, and intricate ion and hormone signalling networks (Zhu 2016; Deinlein et al. 2014; Zhang et al. 2022). The limited genetic resources for breeding salt-tolerant alfalfa present a major challenge, restricting the development of resilient varieties. While enhancing salt tolerance is crucial for ensuring sustainable forage production in saline-affected regions, the key genes and regulatory mechanisms governing salt stress responses remain largely unknown. Despite advances in genomics, the molecular pathways underlying salt tolerance in alfalfa are still poorly understood, emphasising the urgent need for further research to facilitate the development of salt-tolerant and resilient cultivars (Ye et al. 2025; Zhu 2016; Bao et al. 2022; Lu et al. 2024). It is widely recognised that transcription factors (TFs) are essential in mediating plant adaptation to a range of abiotic stresses, with salt stress being one of the most studied. Many significant families of these regulatory proteins have received considerable attention in research, such as NAC, AP2/ ERF (APETALA2/ethylene responsive factor), MYB, WRKY, and bZIP (basic leucine zipper) (Zhu 2016; Zhang et al. 2022; Sun et al. 2022; Lu et al. 2024). While significant progress has been made in understanding transcription factor-mediated salt stress responses in model plants, research on alfalfa (M. sativa) is still limited. Further studies are needed to explore the role of transcription factors in alfalfa's salt stress mechanisms and their potential for enhancing salinity tolerance. 

As one of the largest plant-specific transcription factor families, the NAC family (NAM, ATAF1/2, CUC2) plays a pivotal role in controlling plant growth, development, and stress responses. In rice, research has shown that OsNAC2 overexpression leads to salt-sensitive phenotypes, with significant growth retardation under 150 mM NaCl conditions. Molecular analysis further showed that OsNAC2 modulates abiotic stress tolerance through direct binding to the promoter regions of the OsLEA3 and OsSAPK1 (Shen et al. 2017). Subsequent studies reported that overexpression of OsNAC2 triggers programmed cell death, marked by DNA fragmentation and increased caspaselike activity. This is closely associated with the regulation of stress-related genes, including OsCOX11 (a ROS scavenger), OsAP37 (a caspase-like protease), and $\mathrm { K } ^ { + }$ efflux channels such as OsGORK and OsSKOR, which together contribute to the disruption of cellular homeostasis and a reduction in salt tolerance (Mao et al. 2018). This finding suggests that MsNAC2a might play a role in other $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S } \cdot$ - regulated physiological processes, aside from its involvement in salt stress response. Consequently, further studies are necessary to investigate the biological functions of MsNAC2a within the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ signalling network. 

It has been well established that osmotic and ion stress induced by salinity disturbs ROS homeostasis, promoting the excessive accumulation of ROS, such as ${ \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , thereby intensifying oxidative stress and increasing cellular damage in plants (Zhou et al. 2024). Maintaining ROS homeostasis is crucial for plant survival, necessitating the presence of efficient mechanisms to rapidly eliminate excess ROS, which are essential 

for ensuring optimal growth and facilitating stress adaptation (Yang and Guo 2018). Recent research on alfalfa has revealed that the transcription factors MsbZIP53 and MsMYBH play significant roles in enhancing the plant's resistance to salt stress and drought (Guo et al. 2024; Shi, Liu, et al. 2024). By upregulating the expression of ROS detoxification genes such as MsSOD, MsPOD, and MsCAT, MsbZIP53 and MsMYBH enhance the plant's ability to neutralise ROS and mitigate oxidative stress. Another study showed that MsWRKY33 positively regulates genes involved in ROS scavengers under salt stress conditions (Ma et al. 2023). These help to preserve cellular functions and protect the plant from the damaging effects of environmental stress, ultimately boosting its survival and growth in saline and drought environments. In this study, our findings show that upon exposure to salinity treatment, MsNAC2a-OE plants exhibit a significant downregulation of MsSOD1, MsSOD3, MsCAT, MsAPX1, and MsCOX11 expression compared to WT plants, whereas MsNAC2a-RNAi plants display higher transcript levels of these genes (Figure  5). Additionally, the ChIP-qPCR, EMSA, and Dual-LUC assays provided evidence that MsNAC2a directly regulates the expression of MsCOX11 (an ROS scavenger) through binding to its promoter regions (Figure 6). Further research should focus on identifying transcriptional regulators that directly target the promoters of these genes (MsSOD1, MsSOD3, MsCAT, and MsAPX1) and suppress their expression in response to salt stress in alfalfa. 

Growing research findings support the notion that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ is crucial in modulating plant responses to environmental challenges, such as drought, salt stress, cold stress, and osmotic stress (Zhang et al. 2021; Chen et al. 2020; Sun et al. 2022). The application of NaHS, an $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ donor, has been shown to boost plant salt tolerance, indicating that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ plays an essential regulatory role in the adaptation to saline environments. However, little is known about the molecular basis of endogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ function in improving alfalfa salt tolerance. In this study, upon exposure to salinity treatment, the expression levels of MsLCD1 (an $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ synthase gene) in MsNAC2a-OE plants are significantly lower compared to those in WT, while MsNAC2a-RNAi plants show a marked increase in the transcript levels of MsLCD1. Conversely, the expression pattern of MsOASA1 (an $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ metabolic enzyme gene) showed an inverse correlation with that of MsLCD1. Furthermore, through ChIP-qPCR, EMSA, and Dual-LUC assays, we validated the regulatory effect of MsNAC2a on these $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ -related genes (e.g., MsLCD1 and MsOASA1). As illustrated in Figure  7, MsNAC2a promotes the expression of MsOASA1 by binding directly to its promoter, in contrast to inhibiting the expression of MsLCD1. Recent research increasingly supports that plant transcriptional regulation is orchestrated by the precise cooperation of transcription factors, enhanceosomes, holoenzymes, the Pol II initiation complex, and activators (Singh 1998; Mao et al. 2020). Moreover, most transcription factors exhibit dual regulatory roles, functioning not solely as activators or repressors, but rather exerting context-dependent effects on gene expression (Mahendrawada et al. 2025). For example, AtWRKY75 has been shown to promote salicylic acid (SA) biosynthesis and inhibit ROS scavenging, leading to accelerated leaf senescence in Arabidopsis thaliana (Guo et al. 2017). Similarly, OsNAC2 in rice has been reported to possess dual transcriptional activity—functioning as both an activator and a repressor—thereby contributing to the regulation of diverse 

physiological and biochemical pathways (Mao et al. 2018, 2020; Chen et al. 2015). Further exploration is needed to understand the mechanism by which the MsNAC2a transcription factor specifically recognises and binds to the promoter regions of MsLCD1 and MsOASA1, as well as the potential involvement of co-regulatory factors or auxiliary proteins under salt stress conditions. Moreover, we established MsLCD1-OE transgenic lines and conducted a comprehensive analysis of their phenotypic traits and physiological responses under saline conditions. These results suggest that plants overexpressing MsLCD1 exhibit enhanced salt stress tolerance due to the accumulation of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ (Figure 7). Furthermore, MsNAC2a-RNAi TRV-MsLCD1 plants display heightened susceptibility to salt stress relative to control plants (Figure S6). Our results demonstrate that MsNAC2a contributes to reduced salt stress tolerance in alfalfa, in part by modulating MsLCD1 to decrease $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ levels. It would be interesting to explore how the persulfidation sites of salt stressresponsive genes like MsNAC2a influence their function in salt stress signalling in plants and to investigate the transcriptional regulatory mechanisms underlying the synthesis of endogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ in response to salt stress in alfalfa. 

It is well known that the AP2/ERF family, found only in plants, plays a pivotal role in coordinating growth, development, and the plant's reaction to environmental stimuli (Cui et al. 2025). In rice, OsEREBP1, a member of the AP2/ERF transcription factor family, has been found to play a crucial role in boosting plant resistance against both biotic and abiotic stresses (Jisha et al. 2015). Recent investigations have revealed that OsNAC2 competitively interacts with OsEREBP1 in the cytoplasmic compartment, leading to destabilisation of OsEREBP1 and subsequent disruption of its interaction with OsXb22a, an Xa21-binding protein essential for pathogen defence. This molecular interference ultimately compromises the plant's resistance to bacterial blight (Zhong et  al.  2024). In this work, our findings demonstrate that MsEREBP1 physically interacts with MsNAC2a to modulate salt stress responses in alfalfa. The marked induction of MsEREBP1 expression under salt stress further supports its role in stress-responsive regulatory networks (Figure  S7A). Future studies could focus on identifying additional transcription factors or regulatory proteins that interact with MsNAC2a and/or MsEREBP1 to orchestrate diverse aspects of plant physiological processes. 

Taken together, we propose a simplified model for the MsNAC2a-MsEREBP1-H S/ROS pathway during salt stress responses. MsNAC2a functions as a key negative modulator of salt tolerance in alfalfa, downregulating MsLCD1 and upregulating MsOASA1, which repress the accumulation of endogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ . Additionally, MsNAC2a interacts with MsEREBP1, influencing the transcriptional activity of MsNAC2a (Figure  10). The complex interaction between MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1 reveals a sophisticated regulatory mechanism that enables plants to precisely modulate their responses to environmental stress, thereby facilitating a delicate balance between stress resilience and normal developmental progression. This study deepens our understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying stress tolerance, providing valuable insights for future molecular breeding approaches aimed at developing crops capable of thriving under harsh environmental conditions, thereby enhancing agricultural productivity and sustainability.

# 4 | Conclusions

In this study, we demonstrate that MsNAC2a is a pivotal regulator in alfalfa's response to salt stress. Under NaCl treatment, the expression of MsNAC2a was significantly upregulated. Functional analyses revealed that overexpression of MsNAC2a impaired the plant's salt stress tolerance, whereas its silencing enhanced the ability to withstand salt stress. Mechanistically, MsNAC2a disrupted $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ homeostasis by downregulating $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ biosynthesisrelated genes, such as MsLCD1, and upregulating MsOASA1, a key gene in $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ metabolism. Additionally, it inhibited the expression of ROS-scavenging genes, such as MsCOX11, leading to an increase in ROS accumulation. Furthermore, the AP2/EREBP-type transcription factor MsEREBP1 physically interacts with MsNAC2a both in  vivo and in  vitro, influencing its transcriptional activity and modulating salt stress responses in alfalfa. The identification of the MsNAC2a–MsEREBP1- $\cdot \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ signalling pathway unveils a novel regulatory mechanism underlying $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ -mediated salt tolerance, shedding light on the role of NAC transcription factors in plant stress adaptation and offering potential targets for improving crop resilience to salinity. 

# 5 | Materials and Methods

# 5.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

All experimental alfalfa plants utilised in this study were based on the M. sativa L. cv. Regen-SY4D genetic background. The model plant, for performing transient transformation assays, was 

determined to be a genotype of the tobacco varieties Nicotiana benthamiana. To obtain transgenic plants, the recombinant plasmids were conducted as described previously (Lu et al. 2024). In brief, for the overexpression lines of MsNAC2a, its CDS sequence was amplified from the cDNA of SY4D and then inserted into the linearized pCAMBIA2301-S2 vector using a recommended cloning kit (Vazyme, China), following the manufacturer's protocol. For the generation of the RNAi vector targeting MsNAC2a, its 300-bp gene fragment was inserted flanking a PDK gene intron with the pCAMBIA2301 vector, driven by the CaMV 35S promoter and terminated by an OCS terminator. The resulting recombinant constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 and subsequently delivered into the leaves of alfalfa plants through Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Finally, these candidate transgenic seedlings were identified via DNA sequencing and quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR). In addition, to conduct virus-induced gene silencing experiments (VIGS), a specific segment of MsNAC2a was incorporated into the pTRV2 backbone for the generation of the pTRV2-MsNAC2a recombinant construct. The abovementioned plasmid was intended to induce the silence of MsNAC2a expression in plants using the VIGS technique (Luo et al. 2025; Yan et al. 2012). 

These seedlings of different genotype plants were repotted into plastic pots filled with vermiculite upon attaining a size of $6 { - } 8 \mathrm { c m }$ and grown for a duration of 4 weeks. At multiple growth stages, tissues from leaves, roots, stems and others were obtained for the expression profiling of MsNAC2a. The leaves from 5-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana plants were chosen to perform transient transformation experiments. The aforementioned 

plants were cultivated in a greenhouse set at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16/8 h light/dark cycle. 

# 5.2 | Phylogenetic Analysis of MsNAC2a

To conduct evolutionary comparison, a diverse collection of 12 protein sequences, including MsNAC2a and 11 other related proteins, was utilised for phylogenetic analysis. The construction of a rootless phylogenetic tree was carried out utilising MEGA (Molecular Evolutionary Genetic Analysis) 7 software after aligning multiple sequences with a maximum likelihood (ML) approach. The bootstrap analysis involved 1000 replicates, and any gap or missing data were dealt with through complete elimination. 

# 5.3 | RNA Extraction and Quantitative Real-Time PCR Analysis

Total RNA was isolated from alfalfa cultivar SY4D using the M5 Total RNA Extraction Reagent Kit (Mei5bio, Beijing, China), in accordance with the manufacturer's specified instructions. The reverse transcription process was performed with the aid of HiScript III RT SuperMix for qPCR from Vazyme (Nanjing, China), coupled with gDNA wiper to boost its efficiency. Briefly, a sample containing $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA was treated with $4 \times \mathrm { g D N A }$ wiper mix from the provided kit to remove any traces of genomic DNA, subsequently allowing for the synthesis of first-strand cDNA via $5 \times$ HiScript III qRT SuperMix. The Thermal Cycler Dice Real-Time System III (TaKaRa, Kusatsu, Japan) was utilised to perform qRT-PCR experiments, employing SYBR Green Master Mix, in compliance with the previously described methodology (Mao et  al.  2017). The MsActin was chosen as the internal control gene, and the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method was used to assess expression levels of the genes. The primers utilised for qRT-PCR assays are listed in Table S1. 

# 5.4 | Subcellular Localization of MsNAC2a

For the investigation of the subcellular localisation of MsNAC2a protein, the CDS sequence was amplified and integrated into a linearised 2301-S2-GFP vector to build a 2301-S2-MsNAC2a-GFP recombinant plasmid driven by the 35S promoter, subsequently introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101. The fusion construct of 2301-S2-MsNAC2a-GFP, along with the nucleus marker, was coinfiltrated into the 1-month-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. The fluorescence signals were captured with a laser confocal microscope (LEICA TCS SP5II, Wetzlar, Germany) in the plants, which were exposed to a 24-h period of darkness followed by a 2-day long-day regimen (16 h light/8 h dark). 

# 5.5 | Phenotype Analysis of Tolerance to Salt Stress

For the phenotype assessment under salt treatment conditions, three T0 transgenic alfalfa lines were selected in an independent manner, and vegetative propagation of shoots from both transgenic and wild-type (WT) plants was performed, following 

the protocols established by Su et al. (2025). Subsequently, these abovementioned plants were cultivated in plastic pots filled with vermiculite, which were watered with 1/2 Hoagland's solution $\mathrm { ( p H = } 5 . 8 $ ) for 1 month. To guarantee uniform growth and size, these plants needed to be trimmed every 4-week period until they were fully grown. For salt tolerance experiments, generally speaking, after reaching a plant height of approximately $_ { 1 0 \mathrm { c m } }$ , the plants were subjected to salt treatment by administering 1/2 Hoagland's solution $\mathrm { ( p H } = 5 . 8 $ ), supplemented with or without 500 mM NaCl every 2 days over a week period. Following that, the phenotypic characteristics were documented, and the shoots were collected for further study. 

# 5.6 | Physiological and Biochemical Measurements

The Fv/Fm was assessed with a Hansatech m-pea fluorescence spectrometer. For the evaluation of MDA level, leaf tissues were exposed to the TBA assay, involving the sectioning of leaves and their subsequent combination with a solution composed of $0 . 6 \%$ TBA and $1 0 \%$ TCA. After being incubated at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for a duration of $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , the mixture underwent cooling. Subsequently, spectrophotometric readings were obtained at wavelengths of 532, 600 and $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ for the determination of MDA levels. To assess ion leakage rates, leaves were first immersed in deionised water and then exposed to a vacuum pump for 30 min. A measurement for conductivity was conducted, which was then noted down as E1. Following that, the leaves underwent boiling for 10 min and subsequent cooling to room temperature, and conductivity was measured again, labelled as E2. Finally, the calculation of the ion leakage rate $( \% )$ was conducted by applying the formula: ${ \mathrm { E 1 } } / { \mathrm { E 2 } } \times 1 0 0$ . The determination of hydrogen peroxide $( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide anion radical $( \mathrm { O } ^ { 2 - } )$ levels adhered to the methods outlined by Alexieva et al. (2001) and Hou et al. (2022), respectively. The levels of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathrm { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ in alfalfa leaves were visualised and quantified through $_ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) and nitro blue tetrazolium (NBT) staining, respectively. Briefly, the leaves, separated from the indicated plants, were exposed to a 40-min incubation with DAB or a 2-h incubation with NBT, followed by immersion in $9 5 \%$ ethanol for decolourisation and subsequent shift to $6 0 \%$ glycerol for photography. To determine the contents of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ , the leaf samples were gathered and processed following the methods described by Sun et al. (2022). In short, the homogenisation of plant samples was carried out utilising 1 mL phosphate buffer solution $\mathrm { ( p H = 7 . 0 ) }$ ) consisting of $_ { 0 . 2 \mathbf { M } }$ AsA and $0 . 1  { \mathrm { M } }$ EDTA, with a $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ concentration. Subsequently, 1 mL of 1 M HCl was added to the supernatant to promote the liberation of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ . After $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ was mixed with a trap, which comprised $1 \%$ (w/v) zinc acetate $\left( 0 . 5 \mathrm { m L } \right)$ ), and cultivated for 30 min, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of a mixture of $5 \mathrm { m M N }$ , N-dimethylp-phenylenediamine and 3.5 mM $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 }$ was added to the resulting solution. The $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ level was ascertained by measuring the absorbance at the specified wavelength of $6 7 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . 

# 5.7 | GUS Staining

After being subjected to GUS staining solution at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for a duration of $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , the samples detached from the designated plants were decolourised with $7 5 \%$ ethanol for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ and ultimately 

preserved in $5 0 \%$ glycerin for imaging purposes. The composition of the GUS staining solution $( 2 0 0 \mathrm { m L } )$ contained $5 0 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ ${ \mathrm { K } } _ { 4 } [ \mathrm { F e }$ $\left( \mathrm { C N } \right) _ { 6 } ]$ 2 mL, $5 0 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ K [Fe $\left( \mathrm { C N } \right) _ { 6 } ]$ 2 mL, X-Gluc 100 mg, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m o l / L }$ EDTA $\mathrm { ( p H = 8 . 0 }$ ) 4 mL, Triton $\mathrm { X - 1 0 0 } ~ 0 . 2 \mathrm { m L }$ , methanol 5 mL, and the volume was topped up to $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ with $5 0 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ phosphate buffer (Lu et al. 2024). 

# 5.8 | Yeast One-Hybrid (Y1H) and Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H) Analysis

The Y1H experiments were executed according to the protocol detailed previously (Lu et  al.  2024). Generally speaking, the CDS sequence of MsNAC2a was ligated into the pGADT7 vector as effectors, while the promoter fragments were integrated into the $p H I S 2 . I$ vector as reporters. Subsequently, the Y1HGold yeast cells were transfected with both the recombinant plasmids and an empty vector control, followed by cultivation on SD/-Leu-Trp plates and SD/-His-Leu-Trp containing 60 mM 3-AT. The capability of MsNAC2a to bind to DNA was evaluated by monitoring the proliferation of yeast cells for a duration of $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ . 

For the Y2H assays, the CDS sequence of MsNAC2a was integrated into the vector pGBKT7, and the CDS sequence of MsEREBP1 was cloned into the vector pGADT7, leading to the production of pGBKT7-MsNAC2a and pGADT7-MsEREBP1 constructs. The fusion constructs were co-introduced into Y2HGold yeast strains and then cultivated on SD/-Leu-Trp-Ade-His. The primers used for Y1H and Y2H experiments are listed in Table S1. 

# 5.9 | ChIP Assay

To elucidate the relationship between MsNAC2a and alfalfa genomic DNA, the EpiQuik Plant ChIP Kit (Epigentek, NY, USA) was used to carry out chromatin immunoprecipitation, with subsequent quantitative PCR analysis, in accordance with the manufacturer's protocols. The 4-week-old seedlings from the designated plants, after being subjected to a 300 mmol/L NaCl solution for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , were harvested for crosslinking of protein to DNA using $1 \%$ formaldehyde. With cell lysis and DNA cleavage completed, the chromatin-protein complexes were immunoprecipitated, employing either an anti-GFP antibody (Abmart, Shanghai, China) or immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibody serving as a negative control for comparison. Finally, the binding affinity of MsNAC2a to DNA was determined with quantitative PCR. The primers used for ChIP assay are listed in Table S1. 

# 5.10 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

Amplification of the promoter sequence of the targeted genes was conducted through PCR utilising alfalfa DNA as a template, followed by ligation into the vector pGreenII0800-LUC, resulting in a reporter construct. Simultaneously, the CDS sequence of MsNAC2a was integrated with the vector pGreenII 62-SK to generate an effector construct. The recombinant plasmids mentioned above were introduced into the leaves of 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana plants using Agrobacterium-mediated 

transformation. The LUC/REN ratio was calculated with the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System Kit (Promega, Madison, WI, USA), following the manufacturer's procedure. 

# 5.11 | EMSA

To execute the EMSA assays, the entire CDS sequence of MsNAC2a was cloned into the pET-28a vector, resulting in the production of the His-MsNAC2a fusion protein. The recombinant protein mentioned above was purified through the Ni Sepharose High Performance (GE Healthcare) according to the manufacturer's protocols, while the EMSA assay was carried out utilising a chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Beyotime, China). The biotin-labelled probes were provided by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China), and the primers used in this study are listed in Table S1. 

# 5.12 | BiFC Assay

To verify the interaction between MsNAC2a and MsEREBP1, a BiFC experiment was executed as described previously (Lu et al. 2024).In short, the cYFP vector was fused with the CDS segment of MsNAC2a, whereas the nYFP vector was combined with the CDS sequence of MsEREBP1. The above-mentioned fusion constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium strain GV3101, followed by co-infiltration into the leaves of Nicotiana benthamiana plants by means of Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Ultimately, the visualisation of YFP fluorescence signals was achieved by a laser confocal microscope (LEICA TCS SP5II, Wetzlar, Germany). 

# 5.13 | Western Blot Analysis

For protein extraction, 4-week-old seedlings from the specified plants were selected and subjected to RIPA buffer (Coolaber, China), which comprised 1 mM PMSF as a supplement. Following that, the proteins were isolated through $1 0 \%$ SDS-PAGE and subsequently transferred onto PVDF membranes (Millipore, USA). To eliminate nonspecific binding, those membranes mentioned earlier were exposed to primary inhibition by incubating them in a solution consisting of $5 \%$ milk and $1 \times$ TBST buffer (supplemented with $0 . 0 2 \%$ Tween) at room temperature for 2 h. Afterwards, the membranes underwent incubation with the initial antibodies at a temperature of $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for a period of overnight, followed by a rigorous washing process via $1 \times \mathrm { T B S T }$ buffer. Ultimately, those membranes, as previously stated, were subjected to incubation with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibodies for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at room temperature, and detection was then conducted via BeyoECL plus (Mei5bio, Beijing, China).

Student's t-test was used to evaluate the significance of the treatments. All experimental procedures in this article were performed in three independent replicates.
