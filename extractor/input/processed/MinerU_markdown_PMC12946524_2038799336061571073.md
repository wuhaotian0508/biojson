# ABSTRACT

Sinapine (O-sinapoyl choline) is the major phenolic metabolite typically found in the oil-rich seeds of Brassicaceae such as Camelina sativa and Brassica napus. It imparts a bitter taste to the seeds as a defence mechanism against herbivores, but it also renders them less palatable to livestock. To improve Camelina flour for human consumption or as animal feed, we reduced sinapine content through CRISPR/Cas9-based genome editing of REF1 (REDUCED EPIDERMAL FLUORESCENCE1), which encodes the NADP+-dependent coniferaldehyde/sinapaldehyde dehydrogenase (CALDH/SALDH), a key enzyme for sinapine biosynthesis in Arabidopsis thaliana and B. napus. Inactivation of all three homoeologues found in C. sativa lowered the sinapine content in seeds by an overall $5 6 \%$ in two cultivars indicating the presence of a REF1-independent pathway for sinapine biosynthesis. Most importantly however, crucial agronomic seed traits such as total lipid or protein content of the seeds, seed weight or germination were not affected. Hence, the ref1 mutant lines produced here provide a valuable trait, that can be combined with other traits through gene stacking to obtain crops with significantly improved product quality. Furthermore, metabolite fingerprinting by ultra-performance liquid chromatography-electrospray ionisation-quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry of ref1 mutant lines revealed a contrasting phenylpropanoid profile in seeds and leaves, indicating that REF1 oxidises sinapaldehyde to sinapate in seeds and coniferyl aldehyde to ferulate in leaves. In contrast to Arabidopsis however, Camelina accumulates no comparable levels of sinapoyl malate, but substantial amounts of chlorogenic acid, of which an additional chlorogenic acid isomer distinguishes the two different Camelina cultivars as a metabolite marker. 

# 1 | Introduction

Camelina sativa is described as an ‘Old World’ oilseed crop, which is believed to have been domesticated in prehistoric (Bronze Age) times (Bouby 1998; Brock et al. 2018; Iskandarov et al. 2014). Nowadays, the size of the global Camelina oil market has the potential to grow significantly since major agricultural companies have begun to incorporate Camelina into their 

feedstock portfolio (Berti et al. 2025). The Camelina genus belongs to the mustard family (Brassicaceae or Cruciferae), which typically accumulates substantial $( 2 0 \% - 4 0 \% )$ oil in their seeds, as observed in oilseed rape (OSR, Brassica napus), white mustard (Sinapis alba) and the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana. Major traits of commercial interest in Camelina seeds are oil quality, oil yield, seed size and seed protein content (Haslam et al. 2025). Ranging from $3 5 \% - 4 5 \%$ , Camelina seed oil content 

is similar to that of the major oil crops soybean $( 1 8 \% - 2 2 \% )$ ), OSR $( 4 0 \% - 4 4 \% )$ and sunflower $( 3 9 \% - 4 9 \% )$ (Moser 2010). Like OSR, Camelina varieties comprise both spring and winter cultivars, which produce seed yields in a similar range (Berti et al. 2025; Zhang et al. 2021) and have similar agronomic output to spring OSR (Anderson et al. 2022). 

Camelina features several more remarkable qualities: Its enhanced resistance to typical Brassicaceae diseases, drought tolerance and minimal nutritional requirements enable cultivation on marginal land (Zanetti et al. 2024). Because of its short vegetation period of 3–4 months and the availability of both summer and winter varieties, it is highly suitable for cultivation as an inter- or cover crop (Keadle et al. 2023). The nutritive value of Camelina oil for either human or animal consumption lies in its particularly high content $( 3 5 \% - 3 9 \% )$ of $\propto$ -linolenic acid (18:3), an essential ω-3 fatty acid useful in both the food industry and as animal feed. Its recent rediscovery has also been driven by the possibility of producing customised, high-quality oil in its seeds through genetic enhancement and metabolic engineering (Bansal et al. 2018; Han et al. 2022; Iven et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2025; Neumann et al. 2021; Yu et al. 2018). Lastly, $1 0 \% { - } 1 4 \%$ of the oil remains in the so-called ‘seed cake’, the solid leftover from pressing seeds for oil extraction. This by-product is also rich in protein (up to $3 0 \% - 3 5 \%$ ), making it a viable additive or alternative to soybean meal for use as animal feed (Luise et al. 2024). 

Camelina protects its seeds against herbivores and pathogens by synthesising bitter-tasting and toxic metabolites like sinapine and glucosinolates. Unfortunately, these accumulate in the seed cake and restrict its use as animal feed; reducing these antinutritive compounds is a major objective in breeding efforts (Halkier and Gershenzon  2006; Nour-Eldin et  al.  2017; Russo and Reggiani 2012). Glucosinolates are sulphur- and nitrogencontaining metabolites found in all organs in Camelina and constitute the precursors of pungent mustard oils. Excessive glucosinolate content in livestock feed leads to growth retardation, reduced performance and kidney and liver damage (Hölzl et al. 2023). Sinapine is the major phenolic metabolite in seeds of Brassicaceae, where it imparts a bitter taste as a pest deterrent, and has also been suggested to render seeds difficult to digest and less palatable to livestock (Ismail et  al.  1981; Qiao et  al.  2008; Shahidi and Naczk  1992). Sinapine has also been held responsible for a fishy taste in eggs when present in chicken feed (Butler et al. 1982; Pearson et al. 1980; Ward et al. 2009). The sinapine content of defatted flours from different Camelina accessions has been reported to range from $0 . 1 \%$ to $0 . 5 \%$ (Russo and Reggiani  2017), and from 1.7 to $4 . 2 \mathrm { m g / g }$ in the camelina oil seed cake (Matthäus and Zubr 2000), but even lower levels would be desirable for Camelina flour used for animal feed or human consumption (Russo and Reggiani 2017). 

# 1.1 | Biosynthesis of Sinapine and Other Phenylpropanoids in Brassicaceae

The biosynthesis of sinapate-derived metabolites is part of phenylpropanoid metabolism and is linked to that of many other bioactive and specialised metabolites including lignans, lignins and flavonoids, which are often involved in plant defence, survival or 

structural support (Fraser and Chapple 2011). Sinapate biosynthesis derives from aromatic amino acids produced via the shikimate pathway in plastids, which are then transported into the cytosol where they are converted via several intermediate metabolites into coniferyl aldehyde (Figure 1). Coniferyl aldehyde is a major branch point serving as a substrate for several reactions. It is hydroxylated to 5-hydroxy coniferyl aldehyde by ferulic acid 5-hydroxylase (F5H) and then methylated to sinapaldehyde by caffeic acid/5-hydroxy ferulic acid:o-methyltranserase (COMT). Sinapaldehyde is then oxidised by the NADP+-dependent coniferyl aldehyde/sinapaldehyde dehydrogenase (CALDH/ SALDH or reduced epidermal fluorescence (REF1)) to sinapate. Finally, sinapate is esterified first to sinapoyl $\beta$ -glucose through SGT (UDP-glucose:sinapate glucosyltransferase) and then to either sinapoyl malate via sinapoyl glucose-malate:o-sinapoyl transferase (SMT) or to sinapine via SCT (sinapoyl glucose:choline sinapoyl transferase) (Figure 1; Milkowski and Strack 2010; Nair et al. 2004). In Arabidopsis, the two most abundant sinapate esters are sinapine and sinapoyl malate, of which the latter accumulates predominantly in the mature leaf epidermis to protect plants from UV-B radiation $( 2 8 0 - 3 1 5 \mathrm { n m } )$ (Dean et al. 2014). Sinapine is synthesised in the embryos during seed development and is hydrolysed by SCE (sinapin esterase) to choline and sinapate during germination. While sinapate feeds back into sinapoyl malate biosynthesis in the cotyledons, it has been suggested that the liberated choline moieties fuel phosphatidyl choline (PC) biosynthesis (Strack 1981). Coniferyl aldehyde can also be oxidised by REF1 to produce ferulic acid. Ferulic acid esters accumulate in cell walls and form ether or C–C bonds to cross-link polysaccharides and lignin, thereby increasing cell wall physical and chemical resilience (Missihoun et  al.  2016). Alternatively, the reduction of both coniferyl aldehyde and sinapaldehyde to alcohols by cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) constitutes the last step of monolignol biosynthesis before oxidative polymerisation in the cell wall during lignin biosynthesis (Tronchet et al. 2010). In addition to coniferyl alcohol, various types of lignans are derived from sinapyl alcohol. Lignans are of pharmaceutical interest as they possess antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antiviral and antitumor properties (Teponno et al. 2016). The function of lignans in planta is not completely understood, but they have been predicted to protect against pathogens and herbivores (Markulin et al. 2019). 

# 1.2 | Phenylpropanoid Metabolism in Camelina sativa

Due to their manifold functions, it is not surprising that phenylpropanoids are found in all land plants (de Vries et al. 2021). Yet, their distribution and occurrence are highly diverse, which constitutes a hallmark feature of secondary metabolism. Even closely related members within the same species may differ significantly in their selection of secondary metabolites, which has been observed in Papaver somniferum (poppy) or Vitis vinifera (grape) (Hong et al. 2022; Šikuten et al. 2025). Studies on secondary metabolites including derivatives of phenylpropanoid metabolism in C. sativa primarily focus on seeds (Alberghini et  al.  2022; Boutet et  al.  2022); very few reports have investigated vegetative tissues (Karamać et  al.  2020; Onyilagha et al. 2003; Zhang et al. 2020). # 1.3 | Reduction of Sinapine Content in Seeds Through Genetic Engineering

When we initiated this study, several genes had been targeted to reduce or eliminate sinapine in seeds of different plants (mostly Arabidopsis and B. napus) with varying effects on metabolite levels and plant viability. F5H (also annotated as FAH, ferulic acid 5-hydroxylase) encodes the coniferyl aldehyde 5-hydroxylase activity which is located further upstream in the biosynthesis of sinapate esters and was originally discovered in a forward genetic screen for mutants with reduced fluorescence upon UV illumination (Chapple et  al.  1992). Arabidopsis fah1 mutants are not only totally devoid of sinapate esters but also 

impaired in the production of sinapaldehyde-derived syringyl (S)-lignin. Morphologically they are similar to wild type (WT) (Anderson et al. 2015) but have been shown to be more susceptible to UV-B, Verticillium longisporum, white mould and drought stress (Huang et al. 2009; König et al. 2014; Landry et al. 1995). Seed-specific silencing of FAH in B. napus resulted in a $4 0 \%$ reduction of sinapine (Nair et al. 2000). A similar approach was pursued for SCT, which catalyses the exchange of glucose for choline to produce sinapine (Figure  1). In Arabidopsis, this abolished total sinapine completely $( < 1 \% )$ (Huang et  al.  2008; Shirley et  al.  2001). However, it caused hyperaccumulation of sinapoy $\beta$ -glucose which counteracted the sinapine reduction so that no net change was achieved in total sinapoyl ester and thus antinutritional metabolites content. RNA interference of SCT in Brassica napus seeds led to a $5 2 \%$ reduction of sinapine (Bhinu et al. 2009; Milkowski and Strack 2010) which was enhanced to a $9 0 \%$ reduction when combined with downregulation of FAH (Bhinu et al. 2009). During germination, SCE hydrolyses sinapine to choline and sinapate, and seed-specific overexpression of SCE reduced the sinapine content in rapeseed to less than $5 \%$ of the WT and increased choline content. This was accompanied by some pleiotropic changes including differences in both central and specialised metabolism as revealed by metabolic profiling of the transgenic seeds, but without severe effects on agronomically important traits (Clauß et al. 2011). 

The sinapaldehyde/coniferaldehyde dehydrogenase (REF1) is a key enzyme for sinapine biosynthesis (Figure 1). Arabidopsis contains a single REF1 gene (At3g24503), and REF1 loss-of-function 

mutant alleles exhibited a reduction of sinapine levels to $2 5 \%$ of WT in seeds, a reduction of sinapoyl malate to $1 0 \%$ of WT levels in leaves, and a ${ \sim } 5 0 \%$ reduction of cell wall-linked ferulate esters (Milkowski and Strack 2010; Nair et al. 2004; Ruegger and Chapple 2001). However, lignin biosynthesis was not affected in Arabidopsis ref1 mutant lines. OSR lines with either decreased REF1 expression or EMS-induced nonsense mutations in both BnREF1 paralogs also exhibited significantly reduced sinapine levels, down to $5 8 \%$ and $2 9 \%$ of WT, respectively (Emrani et al. 2015; Mittasch et al. 2013). Taken together, we concluded that the REF1 gene constituted a promising target for the development of commercially attractive Camelina lines with low seed sinapine levels. 

Conventional breeding methods are difficult and lengthy in polyploid varieties like Camelina (Hutcheon et  al.  2010; Weeks  2017). However, like Arabidopsis, Camelina can easily be genetically modified by Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transgenesis, and the CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene-editing approach has been increasingly successful in dealing with multiple target genes and gene redundancy (Haslam et  al.  2025; Hölzl et al. 2023; Ozseyhan et al. 2018). Therefore, we generated Camelina ref1 loss-of-function mutants via gene editing through CRISPR/Cas9, followed by targeted evaluation of sinapine content and commercially valuable seed traits. The lines were subsequently subjected to metabolite fingerprinting analyses to detect possible pleiotropic alterations (Feussner et al. 2023) and elucidate the phenylpropanoid pathway in both seeds and leaves of Camelina. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Identification of Camelina sativa REF1 Homoeologues on Chromosomes 1, 15 and 19

Camelina is a hexaploid organism, where most genes are encoded by three homoeologues (Hutcheon et  al.  2010). A database search (https://plants.ensembl.org/index.html) with the Arabidopsis REF1 sequence revealed only two of the expected three REF1 homoeologues, REF1 on chromosome 15 (REF1_Chr15): LOC104746994, and REF1 on Chromosome 19 (REF1_Chr19): LOC104767838. Examination of neighbouring genes revealed that both REF1 homoeologues are flanked by a preserved gene sequence. A predicted ‘DNA mismatch repair protein’ and ‘multiprotein bridging factor’ are found upstream of both REF1_Chr15 and REF1_Chr19 homoeologues (chromosome 15: LOC104746992, LOC10474699; chromosome 19: LOC104767837, LOC104766378, respectively), and a ‘podocalexin-like protein’ and ‘putative F-box/FBD/ LRR-repeat protein’, are found downstream of both REF1_ Chr15 and REF1_Chr19 (chromosome 15: LOC104766379 and LOC104767839; chromosome 19: LOC104766379 and LOC104767839). A search for homoeologues of these neighbouring genes identified a region on Chromosome 1, in which the arrangement of these loci is preserved. The third homoeologue of REF1 would be expected here, but instead contained a stretch with no available sequence information. By homoeologue-independent, overlapping PCR and subsequent cloning and sequencing, the complete sequence of the third REF1 homoeologue on Chromosome 1 (REF1_Chr1) was 

obtained. The full-length genomic sequences were determined for all three homoeologues in two cultivars used for this study, namely IPK139 and Ligena. 

Between the three IPK139 homoeologues, REF1_Chr 1 and REF1_Chr19 display the highest sequence identity to each other $( 9 8 \% )$ , and $9 1 \%$ to REF1_Chr 15. The similarity between inferred protein sequences is even more conserved (Figure S1): REF1_Chr1 differs from REF1_Chr19 in one amino acid only (V162I), and REF1_Chr15 differs from both REF1_Chr1 and REF1_Chr19 in seven of a total of 501 residues $9 8 . 4 \%$ and $9 8 . 6 \%$ , respectively). Comparison with corresponding Arabidopsis and B. napus protein sequences (all of which are 501 amino acids long) revealed the highest identities between Arabidopsis and Camelina orthologs $( 9 3 \% - 9 4 \%$ , Figure  S1). Arabidopsis REF1 displays a total of 33 substitutions compared with all three Camelina homoeologues, three of which are conserved in REF1_Chr 15, but not in REF1_Chr1 or REF1_Chr19 (L22I, I29V and A373P). Identities between Arabidopsis and B. napus or Camelina and B. napus are $8 8 \%$ and $8 6 \%$ , respectively. 

# 2.2 | REF1 Homoeologues Are Expressed in Leaves and in Developing Seeds

Based on the genomic sequences of IPK139, specific primers were designed for both homoeologue-independent and homoeologue-specific analysis of REF1_Chr15 and REF1_Chr19 gene expression. Cloning of gene products revealed that REF1_Chr19 specific primers also amplify REF1_Chr1, due to the high sequence similarity between both homoeologues (data not shown). Gene expression of different organs and developmental stages (flowers, leaves, young pods (silicles), developing seeds (embryos) and associated seed coats) was determined in IPK139 by quantitative real-time (qRT) PCR and normalised to ACTIN8 as reference (Figure  2). For all primer combinations, the strongest REF1 expression was observed in 3-week-old developing seeds (embryos). Equally strong REF1 expression was observed in leaves, while it was much weaker in flowers and young pods. Overall, transcript abundance of REF1_Chr15 transcript was lower than the combined levels of REF1_Chr1 and REF1_Chr19 transcripts.

# 2.4 | Selection of Highly Conserved Target Regions in REF1 Homoeologues for CRISPR/Cas9—Based Multiple Genome Editing

The following web tools were used: CRISPR-P (http://cbi.hzau.edu.cn/cgi-bin/CRISPR), CRISPRko (http://portals.broadinstitute.org/gpp/public/analy sis-tools/sgrna-design) and CRISPRdirect (https://crispr. dbcls.jp/). Four individual 20 nucleotide (N)-long target sequences $\cdot 5 ^ { \prime }$ -N20NGG-3′) were selected within the exons of the genomic DNA of all three homoeologues, namely gRNA_521, and gRNA_662, located on exon three and four, respectively, as well as gRNA_789 and gRNA_820, both located on exon five (Figure S3). These four target sequences are present in all three homoeologues; they are $1 0 0 \%$ identical in both IPK139 and Ligena cultivars, and their specificity was checked using ‘off-target finder’ algorithms (data not shown). To maximise our chances of obtaining complete loss-of-function mutants, the gRNAs were cloned in pairs into two distinct CRISPR/ 

Cas9 constructs, construct ‘a’: pHEE6_521 662 and construct ‘b’: pHEE6_789 820 (Figure S3). 

# 2.5 | Camelina Transformation and Isolation of ref1 Mutants

Both constructs (‘a’ and ‘b’), each expressing two different gRNAs targeting all three CsREF1 homoeologues, were transformed into two Camelina cultivars, IPK139 and Ligena. T1 candidate seeds were selected by red fluorescence upon illumination and T1 plants were subsequently subjected to amplification and homoeologue-specific sequencing of at least one of the three target sequences. Promising candidates were propagated and subjected to further screening. Eventually, seven independent, homozygous ref1 mutant alleles were isolated (ref1-1 – ref1-7) (Table 1). ref1-1 and ref1-2 represent triple ko mutants in IPK139, while ref1-3 and ref1-4 are triple ko mutants in Ligena. Here, the term ‘triple’ is used to indicate the presence of ko mutations in all three REF1 homoeologues. ref1-1 and ref1-3 originate from gene editing with construct ‘a’, and ref1-2 and ref1-4 originate from gene editing with construct ‘b’. ref1-5 and ref1-6 are single Ligena mutants with polymorphisms only in REF1_Chr15, introduced by constructs ‘a’ and ‘b’, respectively. Because preliminary analysis of the leaf metabolome indicated that ref1-3 has a mixed genetic background (possibly IPK139/Ligena, elucidated further below), ref1-7 was isolated as an additional Ligena triple ko mutant, which also originates from transformation with construct ‘a’. Table 1 provides the exact polymorphisms detected at each target sequence of the mutant alleles. Mutations were identified at the anticipated regions, and sequencing results implied homozygosity, as deduced from the sequencing results of two offspring of each mutant allele. Representative traces for each mutant are provided in Figure  S4. Observed polymorphisms were most often insertions or deletions of single or a small number of nucleotides, leading to frameshifts and early stop codons producing truncated proteins. In a few cases, mutations at both target sites gave rise to 47 different residues between the targets, in an otherwise full-length WT sequence. Fragment loss between the two targets was observed in ref1-3 in REF1_15. All these events are expected to result in a complete loss of function of the REF1 gene product. Only ref1-3 exhibited the loss of two single amino acids in the REF1_1 protein sequence (N169 and G216) with unpredictable consequences on activity. Inferred protein sequences for each homoeologue and mutant line are provided as Figure  S5. Taken together, all four gRNAs were effective in producing mutations. A rough estimation of the mutation rates observed in the first two generations revealed a higher percentage of construct ‘a’ (approx. $1 5 \%$ ) than construct ‘b’ (approx. $5 \%$ , data not shown). However, as not all homoeologues of the candidates were sequenced in the T1 and T2 generations, these rates are imperfect approximations of CRISPR guide efficiency.

# 2.9 | Distinctions in Phenylpropanoid Metabolism Were Observed Both Among Brassicaceae Species and Among Camelina Cultivars

Remarkably, we did not detect any significant levels of sinapoyl malate in seeds, leaves or germinating seeds (data not shown), despite its major role in phenylpropanoid metabolism in Arabidopsis and B. napus. Furthermore, hierarchical cluster analysis revealed that some of the most prominent differences within the leaf metabolome were not caused by the genetic inactivation of REF1, but could be attributed to alterations in the accumulation of chlorogenic acid isomers between the 

two Camelina cultivars IPK139 and Ligena (Figure 6). Countwise, the most abundant feature in leaves (LN1), represents a chlorogenic acid isomer present in all samples with little variation, likely hitting detector saturation due to its high abundance (Figure S11a). # 3 | Discussion

# 3.1 | REF1 Is a Valid Candidate to Reduce Sinapine Levels in Seeds With Potential to Increase the Economic Value of Gene-Edited Camelina Lines

Camelina is an alternative production platform for plant oils with potential for sustainably meeting the increasing demands for bioenergy, especially considering diminishing reserves of fossil fuels and the ‘food or fuel’ debate (Haslam et  al.  2025). The protein-rich seedcake is a valuable by-product of oil extraction and is used as cattle or poultry feed, thereby providing the growers with additional income, but it is rich in antinutritional compounds such as glucosinolates and sinapine. By (i) obtaining the sequence information for the missing (expected) third REF1 allele on chromosome 1 and (ii) generating a Camelina-adapted germline (egg cell) specific CRISPR/Cas9 vector, we successfully isolated several novel ref1 mutant lines in two cultivars, in which sinapine content was reduced by $5 6 \%$ over two independent growth experiments. Most importantly, the changes did not affect crucial agronomic seed traits such as fatty acid composition, lipid and protein content, and germination. Likewise, no adverse effects were observed in transgenic Camelina lines, in which a $9 5 \%$ depletion of sinapine was achieved through seed-specific expression of a bacterial phenolic acid decarboxylase (PAD) from B. pumilus. This enzyme had been engineered to also accept sinapic acid as a substrate and resulted in the synthesis of mostly glycosides of 4-vinyl phenols for industrial purposes (Menard et al. 2022; Morley et al. 2013). The performance of these lines in field experiments was not negatively impacted by the loss of sinapine in the seeds. In a more recent study, the CRISPR/Cas9 approach was also used to simultaneously mutate all homoeologues of both F5H and of FAD2 ( fatty acid desaturase 2) (van Belle et al. 2025). Mutant plants with up to $1 0 0 \%$ reduction of sinapine and less PUFAs (polyunsaturated fatty acids) were obtained, thereby improving both seed meal and oil quality. As observed in Arabidopsis (Anderson et  al.  2015) (and despite its central role in lignin biosynthesis), the loss of F5H did not affect plant growth, but caused the expected depletion of syringyl-derived metabolites. This shift towards guaiacyl units in the lignin composition (Figure  6), renders it more hydrolysable for biofuel or feed production. Interestingly, not all fad2 mutants exhibited the previously reported dwarf phenotype (Lee et  al.  2021). Glucosinolates constitute another prominent group of antinutritional, even toxic compounds in cruciferous seeds, for which there are regulatory limits in animal feeds. By CRISPR/ 

Cas9-mediated inactivation of transcription factors regulating either biosynthesis or transporters involved in the import of glucosinolates into the seeds, Hölzl and coworkers generated lines both strongly depleted and even totally devoid of seed glucosinolates (Hölzl et al. 2023). Stacking both traits (i.e., depletion of sinapine and glucosinolates) to render the seed meal more palatable would increase the financial return of such lines even further. However, future studies should consider that these (and related) compounds are part of the plants' defence system and that a comprehensive reduction of antinutritional compounds may lead to a greater vulnerability in the field. This may also be the case for Camelina fah1 mutants, since Arabidopsis fah1 mutants exhibit an altered regulation of selected defence genes as well as changes in cell wall composition (Gallego-Giraldo et al. 2018) and display the previously mentioned susceptibility towards abiotic and biotic stresses (Huang et  al.  2009; König et al. 2014; Landry et al. 1995). It remains to be demonstrated, to what extent the loss of REF1 activity affects resistance. Metabolite fingerprinting revealed, that Camelina accumulates significant amounts of chlorogenic acid, another powerful antioxidant, but no sinapoyl malate in its leaves, seeds or germinating seedlings. Therefore, it is tempting to speculate, that UV stress may not pose an increased risk to the aerial parts of ref1 or fah1 Camelina mutants, since chlorogenic acid biosynthesis occurs upstream of both activities (Figure 6) (Alcázar Magaña et al. 2021). Further investigations are necessary to determine if sinapoyl malate and chlorogenic acid(s) indeed fulfil similar antioxidative roles and which factors regulate the accumulation of either as observed in different plant species. Our metabolome analyses also revealed that REF1 oxidises sinapaldehyde to sinapate in seeds and coniferyl aldehyde to ferulate in leaves, leading to contrasting phenylpropanoid metabolomes. Overall, few changes were observed in the leaves of the ref1 mutants with so far unknown impact on resistance towards herbivores and pathogens. It cannot yet be concluded whether the presence of the residual sinapine (as well as of respective precursors or derivatives) contributes to fitness in a positive or negative way. 

# 3.2 | Residual Sinapine Levels in ref1 Mutants Support the Presence of an Alternative Pathway for Sinapine Biosynthesis

Mutation of REF1 resulted in a significant loss of sinapine content in the seeds, but it did not completely block its biosynthesis. This is in accordance with other studies targeting REF1 in Arabidopsis and OSR, in which EMS-induced mutagenesis resulted in reduced sinapine levels of $2 5 \%$ and $2 9 \%$ , respectively (Emrani et al. 2015; Nair et al. 2004; Ruegger and Chapple 2001). Based on the mutant lesions it is safe to assume that at least four out of the five Camelina triple mutants investigated here constitute true kos, and that the residual amount of sinapine derives from an additional activity present in at least these three Brassicaceae, as already previously postulated (Nair et al. 2004). In Arabidopsis, REF1 activity is thought to be encoded by one gene only; nevertheless, it cannot be ruled out that other aldehyde dehydrogenases (ALDHs) also contribute to sinapine biosynthesis. The Arabidopsis genome comprises three Family 2 ALDHs, of which ALDH2C4 constitutes REF1, and ALDH2B and ALDH2B7, which are localised to mitochondria, have been implicated in the detoxification of acetaldehyde and synthesis 

of acetic acid during ethanol fermentation (Wei et  al.  2009). Acetic acid has been reported to contribute to drought responses (Kim et al. 2017), since an enhanced drought tolerance occurs in response to chemical priming via the exogenous application of ethanol, which was lost in Arabidopsis aldh triple mutants (Bashir et al. 2022). 

Complete loss of sinapate esters including sinapine has been observed in fah1 mutants both in Arabidopsis as well as Camelina, indicating that F5H-mediated hydroxylation is essential for all sinapine synthesis (Ruegger and Chapple 2001; van Belle et  al.  2025). During lignin biosynthesis F5H predominantly converts coniferyl aldehyde to 5-OH coniferyl aldehyde and coniferyl alcohol to 5-OH coniferyl alcohol (Figure 6) without corresponding hydroxylation of ferulate to 5OH ferulate as originally believed (Grand 1984; Higuchi and Brown  1963; Humphreys et  al.  1999). Also, the biosynthesis of syringyl moieties is completely dependent on caffeic acid O-methyltransferase (COMT) activity, which catalyses the O-methylation reaction at the C5 position of 5-OH coniferyl aldehyde and 5-OH coniferyl alcohol (Figure 6). Arabidopsis plants defective in COMT1 (At5g54160) display not only a near complete loss of syringyl lignin units, but also reduced sinapoyl malate levels in leaves and stems as well as decreased sinapine levels in seeds (Do et  al.  2007; Goujon et  al.  2003; Weng et al. 2010). Methylation of 5-OH ferulate to sinapate was concluded not to contribute to lignin biosynthesis, since no isoform of a 4-coumarate:CoA ligase with significant activity with sinapate was identified (Hamberger and Hahlbrock 2004; Humphreys et al. 1999; Li et al. 2015). Nevertheless, COMT1- mediated methylation at the acid level (of both caffeic acid to ferulic acid as well as 5-OH ferulate to sinapate) may indeed represent a key step in the REF1-independent biosynthesis of sinapine in the seeds (Figure  6). Similar to COMT, caffeoyl-CoA:3-O-methyltransferase (CCoAOMT) represents another O-methyltransferase implicated in the initial $O$ -methylation at the C3 position during lignin biosynthesis (Do et al. 2007). A respective ccomt1 Arabidopsis mutant (At4g34050) displayed only a relatively mild lignin phenotype, whereas a comt1 ccomt1 double mutant exhibited a significantly reduced lignin content and severe growth arrest 4 days after germination (Do et al. 2007). Remarkably however, even the residual sinapoyl malate found in the Atcomt1 mutant was abolished in the double mutant plantlets, implying that both activities have redundant functions, (i.e., able to O-methylate both C3 and C5 positions of multiple substrates) and contribute to phenylpropanoid biosynthesis beyond lignification. 

In conclusion, by mutating REF1 homoeologues, we were able to isolate stable Camelina lines with reduced sinapine content in their seeds in two different cultivars without adverse effects on seed quality. Investigation into both seed and leaf metabolomes provided deeper insights into the phenylpropanoid pathway in Camelina. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Primers

All primers are listed in Table S3. 

# 4.2 | Cloning

Cloning of the pHEE401E-derived CRISPR/Cas9 vectors pHEE4, pHEE6 and of the reporter constructs is described in Methods S1. 

# 4.3 | Cloning of CRISPR/Cas9-Based Constructs for Genome Editing of CsREF1 in Camelina

4 different putative target sequences (gRNAs_521, _662, _789 and _820, Figure  S3) with $1 0 0 \%$ sequence identity in all 3 CsREF1 homoeologues of both cultivars were identified using the web tools CRISPR-P (http://cbi.hzau.edu.cn/cgibin/CRISPR), CRISPRko (http://portals.broadinstitute.org/ gpp/public/analysis-tools/sgrna-design) and CRISPRdirect (https://crispr.dbcls.jp/). These served as templates for the generation of two different pHEE6-based CRISPR/Cas9 constructs, each harbouring two individual gRNAs (construct ‘a’: pHEE6_REF1_521 662 and construct ‘b’: pHEE6_ REF1789 829) following the Golden Gate-based cloning protocol described in (Wang et al. 2015). 

# 4.4 | Plant Material and Growth Conditions

Camelina sativa cvs. IPK139 and Ligena (Deutsche Saatveredelung, Lippstadt-Bremen, Germany) were germinated on soil without prior stratification and grown to seed in climate chambers under long day conditions (16 h light/8 h darkness) at $2 0 0 { - } 3 5 0  { \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { s } }$ , $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $6 0 \%$ humidity. Plants were fertilised once a week. 

# 4.5 | Plant Transformation and Mutant Isolation

Constructs ‘a’ and ‘b’ were each transformed into Camelina sativa cvs. IPK139 and Ligena by Agrobacterium tumefaciens following the vacuum infiltration protocol of flowers as described in (Marmon et  al.  2017). PositiveT1 transformants were selected by screening for red fluorescent seeds upon illumination. Genomic DNA preparations were isolated using a CTAB-based method from T1 plants and subsequent progenies as templates for amplification of target regions using different combinations of allele-dependent and allele-independent primers and allele-specific PCR programs (Tables S4 and S5), followed by allele-dependent sequencing of respective PCR products. 

# 4.6 | RNA Extraction and Quantitative Real-Time PCR

Total RNA was extracted from Camelina leaves, flowers, immature silicles, mature seeds as well as developing embryos and surrounding seed pods of different stages of developing seeds using the Spectrum Plant Total RNA Kit (Sigma-Aldrich). cDNA was produced using the RevertAid H Minus First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Quantitative real-time PCR was performed using Takyo No Rox SYBR MasterMix dTTP Blue (Eurogentec) following the manufacturer's instructions. 

Respective PCR primers are listed in Table  S3. The 2-ΔΔCT method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001) was used to estimate relative gene expression, which was normalised to the expression of ACTIN8 as the reference gene (Marmon et al. 2017). 

# 4.7 | Quantification of Sinapine in Camelina Seeds by HPLC-DAD

Per seed batch (progeny of a single plant, i.e., one biological replicate), 15 Camelina seeds were weighed and transferred to 2 mL Eppendorf tubes, deep-frozen in liquid nitrogen and homogenised using a ball mill (Retsch ball mill MM 400, Retsch GmbH) for 90 s at $2 0 \times / \mathrm { s }$ . Frozen material was resuspended in $8 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $7 0 \%$ (v/v) methanol and $1 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ sanguinarine (Sanguinarinchloride, PhytoLab GmbH & Co. KG, Vestenbergsgreuth, Germany, $\mathrm { 1 m g / m L } )$ was added to each sample as an internal standard. Samples were mixed vigorously and subjected to three rounds of sonification à 10 min each with vigorous mixing in between. Samples were centrifuged at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $1 4 0 0 0 { \mathrm { g } }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ to remove debris, and supernatants were stored in glass vials at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . On the day of measurement, $2 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the supernatant were evaporated and resuspended in $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of HPLC solvent B (90:10 (v/v) $0 . 1 2 5 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile: $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ammonium formiate pH 3). After a final round of vigorous mixing, sonification for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and centrifugation (4°C, 20 817 g, 10 min), $8 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the supernatant were transferred to HPLC vials with glass inserts. HPLC analyses were performed on an HPLC-DAD (Agilent Technologies) equipped with a CORTECS UPLC HILIC column $( 2 . 1 \times 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 6 \mu \mathrm { m }$ particle size, Waters Corporation). Column temperature was set at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a gradient between HPLC solvent A (50:50 (v/v) $0 . 1 2 5 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile:10 mM ammonium formiate pH 3) and HPLC solvent B was created as follows: 0 to 1 min: $9 9 . 9 \%$ solvent B, 1 to 20 min: $9 9 . 9 \%$ to $0 . 1 \%$ solvent B, 20 to 22 min: $0 . 1 \%$ to $9 9 . 9 \%$ solvent B, 22 to 30 min: $9 9 . 9 \%$ solvent B, all with a flow rate of $0 . 4 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The absorbance was recorded between 210 and $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and absorbance at $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ was used for the quantification of sinapine. 

# 4.8 | Protein and FA Measurements of Camelina Seeds

Per seed batch, three seeds were homogenised and total protein content was quantified using the Lowry DC protein assay (Bio-Rad) as described (Hölzl and Dörmann  2021). For fatty acid profiling and quantification, 15 seeds per seed batch were incubated with $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of tripentadecanoin as an internal standard in $3 \mathrm { m L }$ FAME solution (methanol/toluene (2:1, v/v), $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) sulfuric acid, $2 \%$ (v/v) dimethoxypropane) in 7 mL glass tubes with PTFE-coated screw caps at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . After cooling down, $1 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of each saturated NaCl and n-hexane were added, samples were mixed vigorously, centrifuged at $8 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ for 5 min and supernatants were transferred to fresh glass tubes. Samples were evaporated, resuspended in $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ acetonitrile and transferred to GC vials with glass inserts. GC-FID analyses were performed on an Agilent 6890 N Network Gas Chromatograph (Agilent Technologies) equipped with a DB-23 GC column $( 3 0 \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ , film thickness: $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , Agilent Technologies). Helium gas was used with a constant flow of $1 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ and the 

oven temperature was held at $1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , then raised to $2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ and to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ , and finally held at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for another 6 min. FAMEs were identified by comparing their retention times with standards. 

# 4.9 | Metabolite Profiling of Seeds and Leaves

Non-targeted metabolite fingerprinting analyses by UPLC-ESI-QTOF-MS were conducted in both positive and negative mode as described (Feussner et  al.  2023). Metabolite profiling of seeds was performed on the same samples as used for sinapine quantification in HPLC analyses. For metabolite extraction from leaves, samples from 5-week-old, climate chamber-grown plants were deep-frozen and ground in liquid nitrogen. $3 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of leaf material were extracted with $9 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $8 0 \%$ methanol $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ and subjected to two rounds of vigorous mixing each followed by 15 min of sonication. Samples were centrifuged at $2 0 8 1 7 \mathrm { g }$ and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Supernatants were stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the day of measurement.
