ABSTRACT: The various grass-induced epichloecyclins ̈ of the Epichloë spp. are ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs), produced as small, secreted cyclopeptides from a single gene, gigA. Here, four clustered and coregulated genes (gigA, gigB, gigC, and kexB) with predicted roles in epichloecyclin ̈ production in Epichloë festucae were evaluated through gene disruption. Subsequent chemical analysis indicates that GigB is a DUF3328 domain-containing protein associated with cyclization of epichloecyclins; ̈ GigC is a methyltransferase enzyme responsible for N-methylation of desmethylepichloecyclins; ̈ and KexB is a subtilisin-like enzyme, partly responsible for the propeptide cleavage of epichloecyclin ̈ intermediates. Symbiotic effects on the host phenotype were not observed for gigA, gigC, or kexB mutants, although ΔgigB infection correlated with increased host tiller height and biomass, while only ΔkexB exhibited an effect on endophyte morphology. Disrupting epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthesis showed negligible influence on the biosynthesis of E. festucae-associated alkaloids. Epichloecyclins ̈ may perform other secondary metabolism functions in Epichloë and other fungi. 

KEYWORDS: cyclic peptides, endophyte, secondary metabolism 

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Fungi produce a variety of secondary metabolites among which polyketides, terpenes, alkaloids, and peptides are the most abundant, with many of them having bioactive and medicinal properties (e.g., penicillin, cyclosporine, and lovastatin).1,2 Small peptides are the second most extremely diverse class of natural products found in fungi.1 They can be synthesized either non-ribosomally via large enzymes called non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) or via a ribosomal-derived route. Of the latter, ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs) from fungi are an underexplored class of natural products that constitute a biochemically/ structurally diverse range of natural products with numerous possibilities for bioactivities, including antimicrobials (antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral, and virulence activity), and therapeutics.3 Epichloë spp. are endophytic fungi that form symbiotic relationships with cool season grasses of the Poaceae family.4 Epichloë spp. confer a range of benefits to the host, especially protection against mammalian and insect herbivores through the production of alkaloids.5 

RiPPs are usually synthesized from precursor peptides and then processed by peptidases.6 Precursor propeptides contain a signal peptide at the N-terminal followed by perfect or imperfect repeats of core peptide sequences.7 Individual enzymes are usually responsible for the various modifications of a core peptide, which are encoded by genes clustered around the coding gene for the precursor peptide.8 In eukaryotes, site-specific proteolytic cleavage is a common feature in protein maturation and plays an important role in 

the activation of a large number of enzymes.9 Kexin, a member of the subtilisin-like serine proteinase subfamily, is a critical enzyme in the endoproteolytic process in the Golgi compartment, with most fungi containing only a single gene coding for kexin,10 such as KexA from Kluyveromyces lactis11 and KexB fromCandida albicans andCandida glabrata.12 Peptides derived from Kex2-processed repeat proteins are widely distributed with highly diverse sequences and roles in the Fungi kingdom from fungal mating factors to toxins.13 

By comparison to bacteria, few RiPPs have been functionally characterized in filamentous fungi, including the ustiloxins discovered in Ustilaginoidea virens and Aspergillus flavus, 14,15 victorin found in Cochliobolus victoriae, 16 and hexapeptide mycotoxins produced by Phomopsis leptostromiformis.17 In Aspergillus flavus, a biosynthetic gene cluster that involves 17 genes has been identified for synthesizing ustiloxin B. The precursor propeptides are cleaved by Golgi protease KexB that is outside the ustiloxin B gene cluster.18 Two genes, ustYa and ustYb, located near candidate genes for ribosomal peptide precursor proteins containing signal peptides and repeated peptides, are involved in the cyclization of ustiloxin B.1 4

Until recently, four families of RiPPs have been found in fungi, including amatoxins/phallotoxins, borosins, dikaritins, and epichloecyclins ̈ as described in Vogt and Künzler.19 The RiPPs produced by ascomycetes are classified as dikaritins which include the ustiloxins, asperipins, and phomopsins,20 although there is a question as to whether the epichloecyclins ̈ should be grouped, by virtue of belonging to the Dikarya subkingdom and some inferred similar characters to other members, into dikaritins or should remain a separate RiPP family until chemical structural analyses to determine the chemical nature of the cyclizing bridge are completed.19,21,22 Johnson et al.23 hypothesized that the cyclization is through amination of tyrosine in the epichloecyclins,̈ while Kessler and Chooi24 proposed that the cyclization was formed through an ether bond incorporating the hydroxyl group of the tyrosine side chain. However, other types of cyclization modes cannot be ruled out as further investigation is still required. The five epichloecyclins̈ (A, B, C, D, and E) of the Fl1 strain differ slightly in the amino acid composition of the seven-amino-acid cyclic ring structure and also in the presence of the amino acid(s) that are outside of the cyclic ring binding to tyrosine (C-terminus threads).23 

Previously, Johnson et al.23 discovered that epichloecyclins ̈ are produced from Epichloë species. They identified GigA (grass-induced gene) as a small, secreted protein that is cleaved by Kexin to liberate epichloëcyclins. These macrocyclic heptameric RiPPs are all produced from a single precursor peptide via gigA, which is exceptionally high in the plantaexpressed gene. Strain-specific variant combinations and quantities of the 8−9-mer epichloecyclins ̈ (epichloecyclins ̈ A−N) can result due to a variable number of imperfect repeats of a 27 amino acid sequence encoded in the gigA gene. For example, Epichloë festucae strain Fl1 synthesizes five epichloe-̈ cyclins (A−E) from four repeats, with epichloecyclins ̈ B and D derived from (identical) repeats 1 and 2, epichloecyclins ̈ C and D from repeat 3, and epichloecyclin ̈ A from repeat 4. Methylated epichloecyclins ̈ were discovered to contain both double- and monomethylated lysine residues,23 thus it was postulated that the activity of a lysine-methyltransferase enzyme was involved. The post-translational modifications to produce the final product were predicted to involve kexin processing, cyclization, methylation, and truncation. However, these mechanisms have not been confirmed for epichloecy- ̈ clins. Deletion of gigA, while abolishing the normally widely dispersed in planta production of the epichloecyclins, ̈ was not essential for ryegrass-Epichloë symbiosis suggesting a bioactive role for these compounds. In the Epichloë genome, the gigA gene is clustered with three other genes that are coregulated,25 including a gene-encoding kexB, one of two kexin subfamily proteins found in Epichloë. 26 Multiple roles have been suggested for the epichloecyclins, ̈ including as pheromones or signaling molecules in host-fungal interaction (effectors), yet the function of epichloecyclins̈ remains unconfirmed. 

Continuing the work of Johnson et al.,23 this study has characterized the three coexpressed genes adjacent to gigA to determine their functions in epichloecyclin̈ biosynthesis in E. festucae strain Fl1, which produces five epichloecyclin ̈ variants A−E. We also provide further insights into the potential roles for RiPPs synthesized in the symbiosis between perennial ryegrass and Epichloë endophytes.

2.1. Culture Conditions and Growth Assays. E. festucae strain Fl1 (ex-cultivar SR3000) was initially isolated from perennial ryegrass and is the wild-type (WT) strain for this study.27,28 E. festucae WT and derivatives (ΔgigA, ΔgigA/gigA, ΔgigB, ΔgigB/gigB, ΔgigC, ΔgigC/ gigC, ΔkexB, ΔkexB/kexB) generated from this study were maintained on $3 . 9 \%$ potato dextrose agar (PDA; Oxoid Ltd., UK) at a temperature of $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in an 8 h light/16 h dark cycle. Immunoblotting of tissue prints was utilized to confirm endophyte infection.2 Ryegrass plants infected with endophytes were cultivated in a glasshouse in 1 L pots filled with potting mix and irrigated as needed.

# 3. RESULTS

3.1. Orthologous Analysis on gigA Cluster Genes. The coexpression of three adjacent genes with gigA, including kexB, supports the hypothesis for an epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthetic gene cluster.25 These four genes, designated gigA, gigB, gigC, and kexB, colocate on a ${ \sim } 7 . 7 ~ \mathrm { k b }$ region of E. festucae strain Fl1 chromosome 4 (accession CP031388.1: bases 800,376− 808,466), as shown in Figure 1. The gene boundaries were determined using available transcriptome sequences, and the predicted proteins were inferred from protein prediction information, with manual correction for translation of the optimal protein sequences. BlastP analyses using amino acid sequences of the putative open reading frames of E. festucae strain Fl1 GigA, GigB, GigC, and KexB proteins as queries revealed the possible presence of similar proteins in other fungi. A summary of results for the top non-Epichloë hits is given in Table 1. All matches returned were annotated as uncharacterized or hypothetical proteins. Searches for conserved domains using BlastP/CDD and/or Hmmscan (HMMER) did not return any results for GigA, as previously reported.23 However, conserved domains were detected for GigB, a domain of unknown function DUF3328; for GigC, a SET domain; and for KexB, a peptidase S8 domain. # 4. DISCUSSION

In Epichloë species, epichloecyclins ̈ are proposed to arise via the expression of the conserved and coexpressed gigA gene cluster. In this study, we have confirmed, through functional characterization, the predicted roles for the gigA cluster genes (gigA, gigB, gigC, and kexB) in epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthesis based on the foundation work.23,48 Gene disruptions followed by chemical analyses of plants infected with the various mutants proved the hypotheses as follows. Consistent with Johnson et al.,23 disrupting gigA eliminated epichloecyclin ̈ production and the gigA gene complementation partially restored their production. Loss of gigB was expected to prevent maturation of epichloecyclins ̈ due to homology to DUF3328-type proteins that are typically involved in RiPP cyclization. Accordingly, no cyclic peptides were detected in ryegrass plants infected with ΔgigB but were present when infected with the ΔgigB/gigB strain. The absence of methylated epichloecyclins ̈ in ryegrass plants infected with ΔgigC and their reoccurrence in those infected with the ΔgigC/gigC strain supports the hypothesis that gigC encodes the lysinemethyltransferase responsible for mono- and dimethylation of epichloecyclins. ̈ Finally, the observation that epichloecyclins ̈ B to E (but not epichloecyclin ̈ A) were still detected in ryegrass plants infected with ΔkexB suggested the involvement of another protease in the cleavage of epichloecyclins ̈ (e.g., kexA). We have found that epichloecyclins ̈ appear to be synthesized in a pathway similar to that of other fungal RiPPs, in which a multicore preprotein featuring a leader, several cores, and follower peptides is post-translationally modified, in this case by cleavage, tyrosine cyclization, methylation, and unknown truncation. Despite different resulting RiPP products, epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthesis resembles the early steps in the pathway leading to ustiloxin B, a mycotoxin which was initially found in rice false smut caused by the pathogenic fungus Ustilaginoidea virens. 49 An equivalent biosynthetic gene cluster has been elucidated for Aspergillus flavus. 18,50 In this proposed pathway, UstA, a multicore RiPP precursor protein, is converted to ustiloxin F, first via digestion into 16 trideca-/ tetradecapeptides by Kex2 proteinases.13 A combination of three oxygenases, including a tyrosinase (UstQ) and two domains of unknown function DUF3328 cyclic oxidases ustYa and ustYb, forms via meta-oxidation of the tyrosine aromatic ring, a stable macrocyclic intermediate desmethylustiloxin F, which is further methylated by UstM to produce ustiloxin F.50 Similarly (Figure 7A), we speculate that in Epichloe,̈ GigA is first digested by KexB (and other proteases), followed closely by cyclization by the DUF3328 domain-containing GigB through amination of the conserved tyrosine linking to the first position amino acid of the RiPP, as confirmed for a first position proline residue [W. Mace, unpublished nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) results]. The preliminary NMR analysis also showed that this cyclization mode differed from the ether bond cyclization as previously proposed24 and was via the meta position of the tyrosine aromatic ring, which was potentially unique among fungal RiPPs (Figure 8). However, more data are needed to accurately uncover the chemical structure of epichloecyclins. ̈ Finally, N6, N6 dimethylation of the conserved RiPP lysine residue is performed by GigC since the presence of desmethylepichloëcyclins indicates that 

methylation can occur last in the order of biosynthesis of epichloecyclins. ̈ The conservation of the core lysine among fungi producing epichloëcyclin-like RiPPs, regardless of whether a methyltransferase-encoding gene was present in the biosynthetic cluster, is interesting and suggests that methylated mature peptides are dispensable for some fungi. It could also be that other methyltransferase enzyme(s) encoded elsewhere in the genome may provide this function. 

Whereas kexin B likely cleaves the leader peptide sequence, the protease responsible for digesting the follower peptide remains unknown. In contrast to ustiloxin B production in A. flavus, tyrosinase involvement in epichloecyclin ̈ cyclization was not expected to be required as amination of tyrosine is predicted, and this enzyme is not encoded by any of the genes neighboring the gig gene cluster. However, two candidate tyrosinase-encoding genes (EfM3.076380 and EfM3.019440) that are present elsewhere in the E. festucae Fl1 genome and have very low homology to UstQ $. < 2 2 \%$ identity to USTQ_ASPFN) may be involved in epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthesis. Ideally, the detection of linear epichloecyclin-likë peptide products would have strengthened the evidence for cyclization by GigB; however, none were found using multiple high-resolution peptidomic approaches. The most likely explanation is that the intermediary peptides are unstable and susceptible to proteolysis and have limited ability to penetrate cells when compared to the cyclopeptide form.51 The lack of stable linear epichloecyclin ̈ intermediates is not uncommon as linear intermediates were not discovered in the biosynthesis of ustiloxin B.50 It may be that cleavage and cyclization are closely coupled to reduce epichloecyclin ̈ loss. Removal of the follower peptide may involve GigB as the coincidence of the A. take predicted GigB protein with Cterminal truncation, together with the two gigA genes, each encoding for a single epichloecyclin ̈ might suggest that the GigB C-terminal region could be required for processing from a multiple repeat preprotein. This could implicate the GigB Nterminal region in the epichloecyclin ̈ cyclization reaction.

The partial redundancy of function observed for the ΔkexB strains may be explained by the fact that E. festucae unusually contains two copies (kexA and kexB) rather than the one copy of kexin-like genes found in most microorganisms.9,26 Why epichloecyclin̈ A is not present in ΔkexB mutant-derived samples (while the other epichloecyclins̈ are) could not be 

addressed in this study but may be dependent on processing location or specificity of the kexin enzyme. For example, the production of epichloecyclins ̈ B to E which is predicted to occur in the Golgi apparatus52,53 c ould be performed by KexA or other proteases with overlapping but differing specificities for the kexin cleavage site. An example of the latter is the preference for basic residues surrounding cleavage sites of Kex2 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and54 Aspergillus niger55 and for furin, a protease enzyme found in humans and animals, which has specific requirements at P1’ relative to the cleavage site.56 Basic residues surrounding the Kexin cleavage sites may specify epichloecyclin ̈ A as a KexB target. Therefore, we hypothesize that the Kexin enzymes in Epichloë have unique cleavage sites and/or a requirement for appropriate cellular localization for Kexin B function, which awaits further validation, including a comparison of function with that of Kexin A on the epichloecyclins ̈ of multiple Epichloë strains. GigB should not be excluded as a candidate for leader peptide removal as no unprocessed or processed derivatives were detected in peptide analyses (Figure 7B). Proof for this function would require mutagenesis studies of GigB and search for intermediate cyclized products in the absence of KexB. 

Previously, gigA was reported as a novel endophyte gene,23 but our homology searches on gigA cluster proteins discovered orthologous proteins in Ophiocordyceps sinensis and seven Aspergillus species (Figure 2). The top hit organism, O. sinensis, is a high-altitude entomophagous ascomycetous fungus, infecting and feeding on the larvae of Hepialidae family moths including ghost moths (Thitarodes spp.). It has been classified as a medicinal mushroom57 with over 30 bioactivities, including immunomodulatory, anticancer, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects.58 Three types of peptides have been found in O. sinensis culture extracts, including cyclodipeptides, cordymin, and myriocin,58 but none of these share sequence similarity with E. festucae GigA. Given epichloecyclin-like ̈ peptides have not been reported in these culture extracts, we conclude that the peptides are likely produced only under specific conditions, e.g., symbiotic conditions.59 Interestingly, of the next two top matching organisms, A. versicolor and A. bombycis, the former strain was isolated from silkworm frass,60 while the latter was isolated in “rotted algae” but has also been isolated from the gut of Aspongopus chinensis (stink bug).61 A. leporis (NRRL 3126) is known to produce leporins, a family of 2-pyridone metabolites with anti-insectan/antifeedant properties and have been shown to influence conidial and sclerotial development.62 Interestingly, an interdependent relationship between Epichloë and Botanophila fly life cycles exists, whereby the flies are vectors for Epichloë sexual spore dissemination to the stroma of new hosts leading to new infections, while laying eggs in and grazing fungal tissues supports the proliferation of the fly progeny. Moreover, the grass symbionts of the Clavicipitaceae family are thought to have arisen from an interkingdom host jump from animals to plants, with the closest related subclades including Hypocrella and Metarhizium genera of arthropod pathogens,63 although we did not find epichloecyclin-like ̈ peptides in the genomic resources for these genera. The curious coincidence of insect-gut associated fungi with the presence of a respective gig-like gene clusters suggests a possible function related to insects, maybe as attractants or deterrents.64 The requirement for in planta evaluation of epichloëcyclin production provided an opportunity to determine whether the genes within the gigA cluster had any roles in symbiotic maintenance. Our findings that the 

disruption of any of the genes caused no gross phenotypical changes to the ryegrass plants implies that the epichloecyclins̈ have no or little effect on host development (Figures 5 and 6) and is consistent with previous observations for ΔgigA. 23 Endophyte phenotypic changes were only detected in leaf sheaths when infected with ΔkexB. Considering no effects were observed for the other gig mutants, the atypical growth may arise from pleitrophic effects owing to the loss of kexin B activity in the cleavage-based maturation of other compound(s), which may be required for the normal growth of E. festucae. Consistent with the findings of this research, which revealed highly branching epiphyllous hyphae in the leaf sheath tissues of ryegrass plants when infected with E. festucae ΔkexB strain, the Aspergillus niger ΔkexB strain exhibited increased branching and also thicker cell walls,65 and a potential function for KexB in surface attachment processes such as biofilm formation has been suggested. Therefore, the negligible effects of epichloecyclins ̈ on the symbiosis under standard glasshouse conditions suggest that these secondary metabolites are not essential for the growth, development, and reproduction of Epichloë but likely act as bioactives against other organisms such as bacteria, fungi, amoebae, plants, insects, and viruses or as attractants or deterrents to insects.66,67 

Furthermore, having established that the function of GigB is cyclization of epichloecyclins, ̈ our additional findings of only mutant $( \Delta g i g B )$ -infected plants with significantly more tillers and plant biomass (than WT-infected plants) was not expected. However, putting this together with the disappearance of expected linear peptide intermediates in ΔgigB-infected hosts, and observations that gig cluster mutants produce either cyclic peptides or no epichloecyclins ̈ at all, brings us to the hypotheses that the comparatively unstable linear peptides may act as some type of plant growth promoters or simply may be absorbed as nutrients by the host plant.51 

In summary, this study discovered the biosynthetic functions for genes in the gig gene cluster which were shown to be essential to produce epichloëcyclins. The full structural determination of the epichloecyclins̈ is still to be completed, although the evidence produced here, with the several similar characters to dikaritins, suggests that the epichloecyclins ̈ should indeed belong to the dikaritin family of RiPPs. The study also confirmed that epichloecyclins̈ are not involved in maintaining the symbiotic relationship between Epichloë and its host ryegrass plants. As a result, any ecological roles of epichloecyclins ̈ remain unknown. Our research is currently constrained by epichloecyclin ̈ biosynthesis only occurring in planta, making their isolation difficult. A future opportunity might be investigating how to activate the gigA cluster genes under culture conditions for epichloecyclin̈ purification, which could then be used for subsequent antagonistic tests and insect feeding assays. Moreover, with a number of antifungal and antiviral cyclic peptides discovered in other fungi,68,69 further research might investigate the effects of epichloecyclins ̈ on the microbial diversity of ryegrass, e.g., as antibiotics, toxins, pheromones, and/or enzyme inhibitors.
