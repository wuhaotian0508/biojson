# ABSTRACT

Root rot disease in Panax notoginseng, primarily caused by the pathogenic fungus Fusarium solani, significantly impacts the growth and production of this medicinal herb. To elucidate the defence mechanisms of P. notoginseng against root rot, we employed proteomics and gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS)-based metabolomics analyses. These analyses revealed significant accumulations of metabolites involved in phenylpropanoid, terpenoid and steroid biosynthesis pathways in F. solani-infected P. notoginseng roots. This accumulation correlated with the up-regulation of synthetases in these pathways as indicated by proteomics data. Focusing on stigmasterol, a representative steroid with differential accumulation levels, and its biosynthesis gene PnCYP710A, we investigated the role of stigmasterol metabolism in the defence response against root rot. Stigmasterol exhibited significant inhibitory effects on spore germination and hyphal growth of $F .$ . solani. Furthermore, PnCYP710A was up-regulated upon F. solani infection and induced by hormonal signals such as methyl jasmonate (MeJA). Overexpression of PnCYP710A in tobacco enhanced resistance to F. solani, up-regulated expression of JA biosynthesis/signalling pathway-related genes, increased accumulation of stigmasterol/lignin/callus, and maintained reactive oxygen species homeostasis during F. solani infection. Conversely, RNA interference (RNAi) of PnCYP710A in P. notoginseng yielded opposite effects. Additionally, PnWRKY4 positively regulated the transcription level of PnCYP710A by binding to its promoter. In summary, this study not only identifies volatile metabolites and proteins involved in the defence response of P. notoginseng against root rot but also discovers that PnWRKY4 activates stigmasterol biosynthesis to resist root rot pathogen infection. 

# 1 | Introduction

Plants face a myriad of biotic and abiotic stresses throughout their growth and development, with pathogen-induced diseases posing a significant threat (Wilkinson et al. 2019; Waadt et al. 2022; Du et al. 2024). To counteract these threats, plants have evolved 

sophisticated defence mechanisms. Surface-localised pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) recognise pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), initiating pattern-triggered immunity (PTI) to impede pathogen invasion (Bigeard et al. 2015; Wang, Song, et al. 2023; Yang et al. 2023). detect effector proteins, activating effector-triggered immunity (ETI) (Ngou et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2023). However, this qualitative resistance, which relies on recognising specific pathogen effectors, is often race-specific and limited. Recent research has highlighted quantitative resistance (QTR), governed by multiple genes, as a more robust resistance mechanism in plants (Gou et al. 2023; Yang et al. 2024). 

The integration of multi-omics approaches has become a cornerstone in studying plant QTR mechanisms. For instance, comprehensive multi-omics analysis revealed the pivotal role of phenylpropanoid metabolism in defending lilies against Fusarium oxysporum infection, with resistant lilies accumulating higher levels of flavonoids and hydroxycinnamic acids compared to susceptible varieties (Deng et al. 2024). Similarly, transcriptomic and metabolomic comparisons between susceptible watermelon and resistant wild watermelon infected with cucumber green mottle mosaic virus (CGMMV) showed significant enrichment of flavonoid biosynthesis genes and metabolites in the susceptible variety. Exogenous salicylic acid (SA) pretreatment enhanced flavonoid synthesis and effectively inhibited CGMMV infection (Liu et al. 2023). Jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis pathway key enzyme gene FtCYP94C1 exhibited differential expression upon Rhizoctonia solani infection and JA treatment, with overexpression enhancing plant disease resistance and increasing flavonoid compound rutin content (He et al. 2023). 

Secondary metabolites are integral to plant stress responses and defence mechanisms (Hammerbacher et  al.  2019; Pang et al. 2021). Based on volatility, they are categorised into volatile and non-volatile compounds. Plant volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are further classified into volatile terpenoids (VTPs), volatile phenylpropanoids (VPBs) and volatile alcohol aldehydes (VAAs). Steroids, a class of triterpenoid compounds with cyclic structures, are essential components of cell membranes and include cholesterol, vitamin D, brassinosteroids, stigmasterol and $\beta$ -sitosterol (Miras-Moreno et  al.  2016). Stigmasterol possesses diverse pharmacological properties, including antiinflammatory, immunoregulatory, antifungal, antibacterial and antioxidant effects (Bakrim et al. 2022; Jie et al. 2022; Goswami et al. 2023). Plants enhance their defence against pathogens by accumulating secondary metabolites upon pathogen colonisation. For example, stigmasterol content in Solanum tuberosum seedlings increased by 1.5 times after 14 days of culture in soil containing Beauveria bassiana, correlating with enhanced plant growth and defence mechanisms (Tyurin et al. 2023). 

Panax notoginseng is a perennial herbaceous plant known for its pharmacological effects, including promoting blood circulation, reducing inflammation and relieving pain (Wan et  al.  2007; Kim 2012). Cultivation of P. notoginseng is primarily in Wenshan Prefecture, Yunnan Province, China. The medicinal parts are the main root and rhizome, but root rot caused by pathogens like Fusarium solani can severely reduce yield and quality (Fan et al. 2016). Identifying proteins and metabolites accumulated during the incompatible interaction between P. notoginseng and pathogens could not only deepen our understanding of its defence mechanisms but could also aid in developing environmentally friendly control methods for root rot. This study integrates metabolomics and proteomics to analyse changes in volatile 

metabolites and proteins in P. notoginseng during F. solani infection, focusing on stigmasterol, a secondary metabolite that accumulates upon infection. Additionally, we characterised a sterol C-22 desaturase gene (PnCYP710A), encoding the synthetase for stigmasterol, to elucidate its role in the defence response against F. solani. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Volatile Metabolite Profiling in P. notoginseng Responding to F. solani Infection

The metabolome of P. notoginseng inoculated with F. solani was analysed using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS), which detected 280 volatile metabolites. These volatile metabolites were categorised into 10 groups, including organic oxygen compounds, organic acids and their derivatives, lipids and lipid-like molecules (Figure  S1). To gain deeper insights into the effects of $F .$ solani infection on volatile metabolites in P. notoginseng, differentially accumulated metabolites (DAMs) were identified based on fold change $\mathrm { F C } > 1 . 1$ or FC $< 0 . 9 1 \AA \AA ,$ ). Subsequently, the volatile metabolites were categorised using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database. A total of 114 primary DAMs and 24 secondary DAMs were identified at 2 days post-inoculation (dpi), while 116 primary DAMs and 23 secondary DAMs were detected at 8 dpi (Figure  1A,B). Among primary metabolites, certain amino acids, sugars, alcohols, organic acids, nucleotides, and lipids were up-regulated at 2 dpi, while the content of 2-aminobutyric acid and xylose acid decreased at 8 dpi (Figure 1A). Regarding secondary metabolites, some alkaloids, terpenes, and phenylpropanoids were up-regulated at 2 dpi (Figure  1B). Moreover, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis of DAMs demonstrated that DAMs at 2 dpi were associated with carbon metabolism, phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, D-amino acid metabolism, plant hormone biosynthesis, terpenoid and steroid biosynthesis (Figure 1C). At 8 dpi, DAMs were related to amino acid biosynthesis, cofactor biosynthesis, D-amino acid metabolism, plant hormone biosynthesis, terpenoid biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Figure 1D). In summary, most secondary metabolites differentially accumulated in metabolic pathways related to plant defence, such as phenylpropanoid, terpenoid and steroid biosynthesis pathways, and hormones. This suggests that DAMs involved in phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, terpenoid compounds and steroid biosynthesis pathways may be related to F. solani stress and actively participate in plant defence responses against pathogens.

# 2.4 | Stigmasterol Inhibited the Mycelial Growth and Spore Germination of F. solani

The antifungal activity of stigmasterol against F. solani was evaluated. Adding stigmasterol (10 and $2 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) to liquid culture medium significantly inhibited F. solani conidial germination in a 

concentration-dependent manner. The inhibition rates of stigmasterol at concentrations of 10 and $2 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ on spore germination were $6 2 . 5 \%$ and $7 8 . 7 \%$ , respectively (Figure  4A). Furthermore, plate assays indicated that stigmasterol significantly inhibited $F .$ . solani hyphal growth, with the inhibitory effect increasing with compound concentration (Figure  4B). Notably, $1 6 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of stigmasterol exhibited the strongest inhibitory effect, resulting in a growth inhibition area of $9 9 . 7 \mathrm { m m } ^ { 2 }$ . Even at a low concentration $( 4 0 \mu \mathrm { g } )$ , stigmasterol inhibited F. solani mycelial growth, with a growth inhibition area of $2 2 . 5 \mathrm { m m } ^ { 2 }$ (Figure 4C). 

According to HPLC detection results, stigmasterol accumulation in P. notoginseng roots significantly increased under MeJA treatment, reaching 2.4 times that before treatment (Figure 4D). Additionally, stigmasterol content in P. notoginseng increased after inoculation with F. solani, reaching 2.3 times that of the control group at 6 dpi (Figure 4D). In summary, the stigmasterol biosynthesis pathway in P. notoginseng is induced by both MeJA treatment and F. solani infection. 

# 2.5 | PnCYP710A Is a Cytoplasmic Protein and Induced by Multiple Stressors and Phytohormones

Based on the combined analysis of proteomics and metabolomics, the key gene in the downstream steroid synthesis pathway, PnCYP710A, was selected for functional analysis. The full-length cDNA of PnCYP710A was 1518bp, with an open reading frame (ORF) of 963bp encoding 320 amino acids and possessing a conserved C-22 sterol desaturase domain (amino acids 1–306 aa) (Figure S4A). PnCYP710A shares homology with the cytochrome P450 family 710 subfamily A, with identities of $8 5 . 2 \%$ , $8 3 . 6 \%$ , $8 2 . 8 \%$ , $8 2 . 7 \%$ and $8 2 . 5 \%$ with PnCYP710A from Heracleum sosnowskyi, Daucus carota, Helianthus annuus, Silybum marianum and Vitis vinifera, respectively (Figure S4B). Bioinformatics analysis of subcellular localisation predicted that PnCYP710A is a cytoplasmic protein. In onion epidermal cells expressing PnCYP710A-GFP, fluorescence was detected in the cytoplasm, whereas the empty GFP vector showed uniform distribution throughout the cell (Figure 4E). Thus, PnCYP710A is a cytoplasmic protein. 

According to reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) data, PnCYP710A was expressed in various organs of P. notoginseng, with dominant expression in stems and roots and minimal expression in flowers (Figure 4F). Additionally, PnCYP710A expression in P. notoginseng was induced by F. solani and plant signalling molecules such as MeJA (Figure  4G). These results indicate that PnCYP710A is a defence-related gene involved in P. notoginseng's response to $F .$ . solani infection. 

To further study the expression pattern of PnCYP710A, its promoter (PPnCYP710A) was cloned. The 1463 bp PPnCYP710A contains several cis-acting elements responsive to hormones (abscisic acid [ABA]/salicylic acid [SA]/ethephon [ETH]/jasmonic acid [JA]/gibberellin 3 [GA3]) and abiotic and biotic stresses. It also includes binding sites for MYC, MYB, and WRKY transcription factors, with three WRKY binding sites in PPnCYP710A (Table  S3). The pBI121-PPnCYP710A-GUS construct was transformed into tobacco to assess PPnCYP710A activity. The $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) activity data indicated that 

PPnCYP710A was activated by infection of root rot pathogens including F. solani and F. oxysporum, with GUS activity 1.73 times and 1.65 times higher than the control group, respectively (Figure S5A). Additionally, five hormones regulated the activity of PPnCYP710A to varying degrees. MeJA, GA3, and ETH induced the GUS activity of PPnCYP710A-GUS transgenic tobacco to reach 3.84, 3.14 and 2.21 times that of the control group; however, SA and ABA treatments inhibited the GUS activity of transgenic tobacco (Figure S5A). In terms of abiotic stress impact, PPnCYP710A exhibited varying degrees of response to abiotic stress. Drought, cold and injury stress induced the transcriptional activity of PPnCYP710A, while $\mathrm { A l C l } _ { 3 }$ and NaCl had inhibitory effects (Figure  S5B). These results suggest that the transcription level of PnCYP710A can be induced by various phytohormones and stresses, highlighting its important role in defence response. 

# 2.6 | Down-Regulation of PnCYP710A Expression via RNAi Enhanced the Susceptibility of P. notoginseng to F. solani and Reduced the Expression Levels of JA Signalling Pathway Genes

Due to the lack of a stable genetic transformation system for P. notoginseng, the PnCYP710A RNAi construct was transiently expressed in P. notoginseng leaves to functionally characterise PnCYP710A. Following inoculation with F. solani, PnCYP710A RNAi leaves and control leaves (empty RNAi vector) exhibited varying degrees of decay (Figure  5A). Specifically, the damage area on PnCYP710A RNAi leaves was approximately five times larger than that of the control group (Figure  5B). The results of Coomassie Brilliant Blue R-250 staining showed that more fungal hyphae propagated in PnCYP710A RNAi leaves than in the control group (Figure 5C). According to RT-qPCR analysis, the expression level of PnCYP710A in RNAi leaves was significantly lower than that in control leaves (Figure 5D). HPLC analysis revealed that the content of stigmasterol in PnCYP710A RNAi leaves was only $6 6 \%$ of the control group, and after inoculation with F. solani, the content of stigmasterol in PnCYP710A RNAi leaves was only $6 0 \%$ of the control group (Figure 5E). Therefore, down-regulation of PnCYP710A in the leaves increases the susceptibility of P. notoginseng to F. solani. 

Through RT-qPCR analysis, it was found that the expression levels of JA biosynthesis genes decreased in P. notoginseng undergoing PnCYP710A RNAi (Figure 5F). In the leaves of PnCYP710A RNAi, the expression of PnLOX, PnAOC, PnACX, PnAOS, PnOPR, PnMYC and PnJMT were significantly suppressed compared with that in control P. notoginseng after inoculation with F. solani. In particular, the expression level of PnLOX was only one-tenth of the control group.

# 2.8 | Overexpression of PnCYP710A in Tobacco Enhanced Resistance to F. solani and Up-Regulated the Expression of JA Signalling Pathway-Related Genes

To further understand the function of PnCYP710A, T2 PnCYP710A-OE tobacco lines were generated. There was no significant difference in growth and morphology between the $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ tobacco lines and wild-type (WT) plants. RT-qPCR analysis confirmed that PnCYP710A was highly expressed in all examined tobacco lines, although the expression levels varied (Figure S6). To evaluate the resistance of PnCYP710A-OE transgenic tobacco lines to F. solani, young leaves from lines A1, A6, A8, A11 and WT were inoculated with F. solani. Seven days post-inoculation, the inoculation site on WT tobacco leaves turned yellow and decayed, while the leaves of the four PnCYP710A-OE tobacco lines showed only mild symptoms around the inoculation site (Figure 6A). Moreover, the lesion area on the leaves of WT tobacco was 6, 11.9, 15.2 and 20.1 times larger than those on the leaves of lines A1, A6, A8, and A11, respectively (Figure 6B). Staining of F. solani mycelium with Coomassie Brilliant Blue showed that more mycelium propagated in WT leaves than in the leaves of the four transgenic lines (Figure 6C). In summary, the ectopic expression of PnCYP710A significantly enhanced the resistance of tobacco to F. solani. 

HPLC data indicated that the accumulation of stigmasterol was the lowest in WT, while the stigmasterol content significantly increased in PnCYP710A-OE tobacco, with the highest level being 1.53 times that of the WT (Figure 6D). Therefore, overexpression of PnCYP710A promoted stigmasterol biosynthesis in tobacco. Additionally, RT-qPCR analysis revealed that the expression levels of JA biosynthesis- and signalling-related genes were significantly up-regulated in PnCYP710A-OE tobacco. In particular, the relative expression levels of NtLOX, NtACX, NtMYC and NtJMT significantly increased at 72 hpi (Figure 6E). 

# 2.9 | Overexpression of PnCYP710A in Tobacco Promoted Lignin/Callus Deposition and ROS Scavenging Ability

To further validate the role of PnCYP710A in defence response, the deposition of lignin/callus and ROS scavenging ability in tobacco overexpressing PnCYP710A was measured. The content of lignin in PnCYP710A-OE tobacco was higher than that in WT tobacco before and after inoculation with F. solani, and the lignin accumulation was up-regulated by 2 times in PnCYP710A-OE tobacco compared with WT (Figure 6F). The ROS level in tobacco roots was measured using a fluorescent probe, and after inoculation with F. solani for 5days, the ROS content (i.e., green fluorescence) in tobacco root tips significantly increased, but the accumulation of ROS in PnCYP710A-OE tobacco was evidently lower than that in WT, which was supported by the quantification of ROS content in extracellular cells (Figure 6G). In addition, the detection of fluorescence from callus showed that overexpression of PnCYP710A in tobacco resulted in a significant increase in callus fluorescence intensity, which reached 2.79 times that of WT after inoculation with F. solani (Figure  6H); thus, overexpression of PnCYP710A boosted the callus deposition in tobacco. Therefore, PnCYP710A overexpression significantly increased ROS scavenging ability in tobacco regardless of inoculation with F. solani. In brief, overexpression of PnCYP710A in tobacco promoted lignin/callus deposition and ROS scavenging ability.

# 3 | Discussion

The absence of superior germplasm resources with high disease resistance levels has hindered the genetic breeding research of P. notoginseng, lagging behind the rapid development of the entire industry. Moreover, root rot in P. notoginseng poses a serious threat to the yield and quality of medicinal herbs. Plant secondary metabolism plays a crucial role in defence responses against pathogens, yet little is understood about the metabolic profile changes during the molecular interactions between P. notoginseng and root rot pathogens. Investigating the differential metabolites produced during defence responses is significant for comprehending plant disease resistance mechanisms (Zhao and Rhee 2022). Numerous volatile metabolites, such as terpenes, phenols, and fatty acids, are well-known for their antifungal properties. To explore the role of volatile metabolites in P. notoginseng's defence against F. solani, metabolomics analysis based on LC–MS data revealed up-regulation of amino acids, polysaccharides, phenylpropanoids, and terpenes (Figure 1A,B). Most secondary metabolites were differentially accumulated in 

phenylpropanoid/terpene/steroid biosynthesis pathways and several hormone biosynthesis pathways (Figure  1C,D), indicating their involvement in P. notoginseng's defence against $F .$ solani. 

Integrated multi-omics analysis is an effective approach to uncovering plant defence mechanisms against pathogens. Combined transcriptomics, proteomics and metabolomics analyses revealed that phenylpropanoid metabolism plays a vital role in the defence response of Lilium regale against Fusarium wilt, with LrERF4 positively regulating resistance to F. oxysporum by modulating phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Deng et  al.  2024). Integrated full-length transcriptome and metabolome analysis identified eriodictyol and oxalic acid as potential marker metabolites for gummy stem blight (GSB) resistance in melon (Wang, Wei, et al. 2024). In this study, comprehensive analysis of volatile metabolomics and proteomics showed that the steroid biosynthesis pathway was significantly up-regulated in P. notoginseng roots after inoculation with F. solani (Figure 3). Stigmasterol, an essential component of plant membranes, maintains membrane integrity and aids in resisting external invasions. It boasts various biological effects, including anti-inflammatory, antifungal, antibacterial, and antioxidant properties (Goswami et al. 2023). Stigmasterol is considered a promising green and environmentally friendly antimicrobial metabolite during plant growth (Bakrim et  al.  2022). Stigmasterol from Caloconoba glauca roots has shown inhibitory effect on Candida krusei (Ndoumbe Tamba et al. 2025). This study demonstrated that stigmasterol can directly inhibit hyphal growth and spore germination of F. solani, thereby effectively reducing root rot development (Figure  4A–C). These findings highlight the potential of stigmasterol as a natural defence compound and its role in enhancing plant resistance to pathogens. 

The role of CYP710A, a sterol C22 desaturase within the plant cytochrome P450 monooxygenase family, has been increasingly recognised in plant defence mechanisms. CYP710A is responsible for converting $\beta$ -sitosterol to stigmasterol, a process that has been linked to enhanced plant resistance against various pathogens. The function of CYP710A in converting $\beta$ -sitosterol to stigmasterol is well documented (Nelson  2018), and its role in plant disease resistance has been explored through reverse genetics approaches. In transgenic Arabidopsis thaliana overexpressing GhCYP710A1 or AtCYP710A, the increase in stigmasterol content was positively correlated with the expression level of GhCYP710A1 and AtCYP710A, and this was associated with enhanced resistance to Verticillium dahliae and Pseudomonas syringae (Huang et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2012). VqWRKY56 and VqbZIPC22 in grapevine regulate the biosynthesis of SA and anthocyanins to mediate defence against powdery mildew (Wang, Wang, et al. 2023). In rice, the OsMKK10-2-OsMPK3/6- OsWRKY31 module is involved in camalexin biosynthesis for defence and growth regulation (Wang, Ha, et al. 2023). The interaction between VvMYB44-1 and VvWDR2 in grapevine competitively inhibits the formation of the MYB-bHLH-WD40 activation complex, affecting anthocyanin accumulation (Leng et al. 2024). In P. notoginseng, two WRKY transcription factors, PnWRKY9 and PnWRKY15, have been identified as important regulators of defence response to root rot (Su et al. 2023; Zheng et al. 2022). This study observed that PnWRKY2/4/5/9/12/15/27 can up-regulate the transcription of PnCYP710A, with PnWRKY4 showing the strongest effect (Figure  7A). This study revealed significant accumulations of metabolites involved in phenylpropanoid, terpenoid, and steroid biosynthesis pathways in $F .$ solani-infected P. notoginseng roots through proteomics and GC–MS based metabolomics analyses. In addition, our study revealed the importance of steroid biosynthesis in P. notoginseng's defence against root rot. Stigmasterol inhibited the mycelial growth and spore germination of F. solani; F. solani infection promoted PnCYP710A expression in P. notoginseng, thereby promoting stigmasterol accumulation, lignin/callus deposition and ROS scavenging ability; up-regulation of PnCYP710A expression in P. notoginseng induced the expression of JA biosynthesis and signalling pathway genes, thus activating the immune response against F. solani infection; PnWRKY4 positively modulated the PnCYP710A expression in P. notoginseng besides F. solani infection. These processes collectively strengthen the defence response of P. notoginseng against F. solani. The findings of 

this study provide valuable insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying P. notoginseng's defence against root rot and highlight potential targets for breeding disease-resistant cultivars. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant and Fungal Materials

Two-year-old P. notoginseng plants were cultivated in a greenhouse under a double layer of shade netting to facilitate gene/ promoter isolation, gene expression profiling, and metabolite content determination. In this study, two types of tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum and Nicotiana benthamiana) were used for genetic transformation and dual luciferase reporter assay, respectively. Wild-type (WT) tobacco seeds were surfacesterilised with $7 5 \%$ ethanol and $0 . 1 \%$ mercuric chloride solution before being sown on $1 / 2 \times$ Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium. Two-month-old tobacco seedlings were grown in an incubator at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16-h light/8-h dark cycle. F. solani was isolated, identified, and maintained in our laboratory (Liu et  al.  2019). Prior to use, the fungus was inoculated onto potato dextrose agar (PDA) and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 week. 

# 4.2 | Fungal Inoculation and Hormone Treatment

For fungal inoculation, the roots of healthy 2-year-old P. notoginseng plants were immersed in a F. solani conidial suspension $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ conidia $\mathrm { m } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ), with sterile water serving as a control. Following a 2-h inoculation period, the plants were transferred to sterile soil, and root samples were collected at 12 h, 1, 2, 3 and 8 days post-inoculation (hpi/dpi). Roots collected at 2 and 8 dpi were subjected to proteomics and metabolomics analysis with three biological replicates. # 4.4 | Protein Extraction, Annotation and Proteome Analysis

Proteins were extracted from P. notoginseng roots, and protein concentration was determined using the BCA assay kit (Beyotime) (sample collection details are provided in the Section 4.2). The protein samples were treated with $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ dithiothreitol (DTT) and $4 0 \mathrm { m M }$ iodoacetamide (IAM). Following precipitation and acetone washing, peptides were generated by trypsin digestion. Peptides were labelled with TMTpro 16plex Label Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and pre-separated using an LC-20AD system (Shimadzu). Labelled peptides were analysed using a liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometer (LC–MS/MS). For bioinformatics analysis, the Proteome Discoverer software was used to identify proteins, which were then annotated using the KEGG database. Differentially expressed proteins (DEPs), defined by a fold change relative to control levels $> 1 . 2$ or $< 0 . 8 3$ and $p \leq 0 . 0 5$ , were mapped to KEGG pathways using KEGG Mapper. Enrichment of DEP pathways was determined using a two-tailed Fisher's exact test. 

# 4.5 | Identification of DETFs and Correlation Analysis

The putative TFs of P. notoginseng were identified from the proteomic data using the PlantTFDB database (http://plant tfdb.gao-lab.org/). The TFs with fold change relative to control level $> 1 . 2$ or $< 0 . 8 3$ and $p \leq 0 . 0 5$ were defined as DETFs. The OmicStudio tool (https://www.omicstudio.cn/tool/62) was used to analyse the correlation between DETFs and differentially accumulated steroids. The Pearson correlation coefficient (ρ) of $> 0 . 6 5$ or $< - 0 . 6 5$ with $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ was identified as a significant relationship. 

# 4.6 | Antifungal Activity and HPLC Determination of Stigmasterol

The antifungal activity of stigmasterol was assessed using the PDA plate method as described by Deng et al. (2024). F. solani was incubated with stigmasterol at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days, and fungal growth was documented using a digital camera (Nikon). 

The effect of stigmasterol on fungal growth was analysed with Photoshop 7.0. Additionally, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $F .$ solani conidial suspension was mixed with $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of stigmasterol solution (initial concentrations of 0.5 and $1 \mathrm { n g } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). Spore suspensions were also mixed with $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sterile water and $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of methanol as controls. Germination of $F .$ . solani conidia was observed after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ using an inverted microscope (Leica). Plate and spore germination assays were performed in triplicate. 

For stigmasterol content determination, P. notoginseng and tobacco samples were dried in an oven, ground, and $0 . 3 \mathrm { g }$ of powder was extracted with 3 mL of $9 0 \%$ ethanol in a $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ water bath with ultrasonic treatment for 50 min. The extract was centrifuged and concentrated using a rotary evaporator (N-1300, Eyela). The concentrated solution was analysed by HPLC using an LC-40A system (Shimadzu) equipped with a Utry C18 column. This assay included three biological replicates. 

# 4.7 | Cloning of PnCYP710A cDNA and Promoter

Based on P. notoginseng transcriptome data, the cDNA sequence of the stigmasterol synthetase gene, PnCYP710A (Data S1), was isolated by PCR using a differentially expressed gene (DEG) as a target (Table S4). Plasmids of pGEM-T-PnCYP710A were confirmed by sequencing. Conserved domain analysis of PnCYP710A was performed using the NCBI online tool (https://www.ncbi. nlm.nih.gov/Structure/cdd/wrpsb.cgi). SignalP v. 5.0 (https:// services.healthtech.dtu.dk/services/SignalP-5.0/) was used to analyse whether PnCYP710A contains a signal peptide, and PlantmPLoc v. 2.0 (http://www.csbio.sjtu.edu.cn/bioinf/plant-multi/) was used to predict the subcellular localisation of PnCYP710A. Multiple sequence alignment of PnCYP710A and five highly homologous CYP710A genes was completed using ClustalX v. 2.1. 

The genomic sequence of PnCYP710A was obtained by retrieving P. notoginseng genome data (Yang et al. 2021), and the promoter sequence of PnCYP710A (PPnCYP710A) (Data  S1) was amplified from genomic DNA using gene-specific primers (Table  S4). PlantCARE (https://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/ webtools/plantcare/html/) was employed to predict possible cisacting elements in PPnCYP710A. 

# 4.8 | RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from P. notoginseng roots using the TRIGene kit (GeneStar), followed by cDNA synthesis using the Eastep RT Master Mix kit (Promega). The qPCR reaction mix was prepared according to the Go Taq qPCR Master Mix Kit (Promega) protocol. The P. notoginseng actin gene (PnACT2, KF815706) served as an internal reference gene, and relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ method. The qPCR assay was performed in triplicate, with gene-specific primers listed in Table S5. 

# 4.9 | Subcellular Localisation

To determine the subcellular localization of PnCYP710A, a fusion construct of PnCYP710A and green fluorescent protein 

(GFP) was generated. The PnCYP710A ORF was cloned into the pBINm-gfp5 vector. Both the recombinant pBINm-gfp5- PnCYP710A vector and the control vector (pBINm-gfp5) were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 cells using the $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ freeze–thaw method (Holsters et  al.  1978). Positive clones were identified by PCR and used to infect onion (Allium cepa) epidermal cells. After $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of dark cultivation on Murashige and Skoog medium, the green fluorescence of PnCYP710A-GFP was visualised using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Nikon). Additionally, the epidermal cells were treated with a $1 2 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ sucrose solution to induce plasmolysis. 

# 4.10 | Transient Expression of PnCYP710A RNAi Construct in P. notoginseng Leaves

PnCYP710A-specific primers with attB adapters (Table  S4) were designed for RNAi vector construction. The pHellsgate 2-PnCYP710A recombinant plasmids were obtained using Gateway BP Clonase (Invitrogen). Both the recombinant and empty vector plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens EHA105 cells. Healthy 2-year-old P. notoginseng leaves were collected for RNAi assays. A wound was created at the same position on each leaf, followed by infection with EHA105 bacterial solutions containing either pHellsgate 2 empty or pHellsgate 2-PnCYP710A vectors. After $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of cultivation in a $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ incubator (2000 Lx, 16h of light/8h of darkness, $6 0 \%$ relative humidity), F. solani fungal blocks were inoculated at the wound sites. The plants were then incubated for an additional $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ . Disease symptoms on the leaves were recorded using a digital camera (Nikon), and Photoshop software was used to measure lesion sizes. Some leaves were also collected for RT-qPCR analysis. The RNAi assay included three biological replicates. Coomassie Brilliant Blue tissue staining was used to observe F. solani colonisation in P. notoginseng leaves, following protocols described by Su et al. (2024). 

# 4.11 | Generation and Resistance Analysis of PnCYP710A Transgenic Tobacco Lines

A plant overexpression (OE) vector, pCAMBIA230S-PnCYP710A, was constructed to generate transgenic tobacco lines for further investigation of the biological function of PnCYP710A in response to F. solani infection. Recombinant plasmids of pCAMBIA230S-PnCYP710A were transformed into A. tumefaciens LBA4404 cells, and positive clones were identified by PCR for transformation into tobacco discs. PnCYP710A-OE tobacco lines were confirmed by PCR, and $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation transgenic lines were cultivated through self-pollination in a greenhouse. For subsequent experiments comparing wild-type (WT) and $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ PnCYP710A-OE tobacco lines, three biological replicates were included. 

Total RNA was extracted from the leaves of $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation transgenic tobacco, and PnCYP710A expression levels were analysed by RT-qPCR (gene-specific primers listed in Table  S4). Four transgenic tobacco lines with high PnCYP710A expression levels were selected for further assays. F. solani was inoculated on the tobacco leaves using the previously described method to evaluate the resistance of PnCYP710A-OE tobacco. After 7 days of cultivation in a light incubator $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 2000 Lx, $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light: 

$^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ of darkness cycle), disease symptoms and damage were recorded. Coomassie Brilliant Blue staining was performed to visualise F. solani infection in tobacco leaves. 

# 4.12 | Determination of Lignin Accumulation, ROS Level and Callus Deposition

Lignin content was determined using the method described by Deng et al. (2024). For ROS observation, leaves were soaked in fluorescent probe ${ } ^ { 2 ^ { \prime } , 7 ^ { \prime } }$ -dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (Beyotime Biotechnology) and incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20 min. Following washing with phosphate buffer $\mathrm { ( p H = 7 . 4 ) }$ ), fluorescence microscopy was used to observe ROS accumulation in the leaves of P. notoginseng and transgenic tobacco. To analyse callus deposition, leaves were soaked in a mixture of $7 5 \%$ ethanol and $2 5 \%$ glacial acetic acid for $1 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ , then washed twice with distilled water for decolourisation. After staining with $1 \%$ aniline blue solution for 3 h in the dark, callus deposition was observed using fluorescence microscopy (Leica). 

# 4.13 | Activity Analysis of PnCYP710A Promoter

The $\beta$ -glucuronidase gene (GUS) was selected as a reporter gene to analyse the promoter activity of PnCYP710A. Specific PCR primers (Table  S4) were used to amplify the PPnCYP710A, which was then inserted into the pBI121-GUS vector to construct the recombinant vector pBI121-PPnCYP710A-GUS. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens LBA4404 cells for production of transgenic tobacco. To detect the activity of PPnCYP710A, leaf discs from PPnCYP710A-GUS transgenic tobacco were subjected to plant hormones and various biotic/abiotic stresses following the procedures described by Wang, Hai, et al. (2024). 

In our previous study, a total of 30 WRKY transcription factors were isolated from P. notoginseng based on transcriptome data (Zheng et  al.  2022). Given that PPnCYP710A contains three binding sites for WRKY transcription factors, the transient interactions between PPnCYP710A and PnWRKY2/4/5/9/12/15/27 were analysed to determine whether PnCYP710A is regulated by WRKY transcription factors using methods described by Wang, Hai, et  al. (2024). Additionally, the WRKY transcription factor with the most significant impact on PPnCYP710A activity was selected and transformed into PPnCYP710A-GUS transgenic tobacco mediated by A. tumefaciens. Finally, tobacco co-expressing PPnCYP710A-GUS/PnWRKY was developed, and GUS activity was detected using PPnCYP710A-GUS transgenic tobacco as a control. All GUS activity assays consisted of three biological replicates. 

# 4.14 | Y1H Assay

The Y1H assay was employed to further validate the interaction between PnWRKY4 and PnCYP710A. The PPnCYP710A and PnWRKY4 were subcloned into the pAbAi yeast bait vector (Clontech) and pGADT7 yeast prey vector (Clontech), respectively. Following the Y1H assay protocols, Y1HGold yeast cells carrying pAbAi-p53/pGADT7-p53 (positive control), 

pAbAi-PPnCYP710A/pGADT7-AD (negative control) and pAbAi-PPnCYP710A/pGADT7-PnWRKY4 were plated onto SD/−Leu/aureobasidin A (AbA) medium supplemented with $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ AbA. After 3 days of incubation at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , yeast cell growth was observed and documented using a digital camera. 

# 4.15 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

For the dual-luciferase assay, the PPnCYP710A was ligated into pGreenII-0080-LUC to generate the reporter construct PPnCYP710A:LUC. The PnWRKY4 ORF was subcloned into pGreenII 62-SK to generate the effector construct PnWRKY4-SK. Specific primers can be found in Table  S4. The effector and reporter constructs were transformed separately into A. tumefaciens GV3101 (Weidi Biotechnology) for co-infection of 4-week-old N. benthamiana leaves. Leaves infiltrated with the PnWRKY4-SK and PPnCYP710A:LUC were used as controls. The infected plants were subsequently cultured in the dark at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 h, followed by culture in light for 48–72 h. The leaves were treated with sodium D-fluorescein (Sangon Biotech), and fluorescence was detected using a CCD camera (NEWTON 7.0 Bio, Vilber Biotech). The dual luciferase reporter gene detection kit (Yisheng Biotechnology Co. Ltd.) was used to evaluate the activities of firefly luciferase (LUC) and Renilla luciferase (REN). 

# 4.16 | Statistical Analysis

Physiological and biochemical parameters, gene expression levels, lesion size, fungal growth inhibition zone, and GUS activity were calculated and presented as means $\pm$ standard deviations. Statistical analyses, including Student's $t$ test (* and ** indicating significant differences at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ and $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ levels, respectively) and Duncan's multiple range test (with different letters indicating significant differences at $p { < } 0 . 0 5 $ ), were performed using SPSS software (v. 17.0) to determine significant differences between treatment/inoculation groups and control groups.
