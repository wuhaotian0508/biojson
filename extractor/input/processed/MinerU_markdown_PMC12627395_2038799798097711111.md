# Introduction

Sweetpotato [Ipomoea batatas (L.) Lam.] is recognized for its global importance in food security [1]. Among its varieties, PFSP stands out for its high anthocyanin content, which exhibits potent antioxidant properties [2]. This characteristic has drawn the attention of researchers, leading to a surge in molecular studies aimed at elucidating the underlying biosynthetic pathways, primarily focusing on above-ground plant organs like flowers and leaves. 

Advances in omics technologies have provided insights into the molecular complexities of anthocyanin biosynthesis in PFSP. Recent studies [3–5] have demonstrated that the upregulation of key genes within the anthocyanin metabolic network is essential for the high anthocyanin levels in these varieties. These insights have been instrumental in elucidating the complex genetic architecture responsible for the unique pigmentation of these tuberous roots. 

Anthocyanin biosynthesis is a well-characterized pathway that originates with phenylalanine [6]. This biochemical pathway is composed of sequential enzymatic reactions facilitated by key enzymes, including phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL), chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavanone 3- hydroxylase (F3H), dihydroflavonol reductase (DFR), anthocyanin synthase (ANS), among others. Furthermore, transcription factors, notably the MYB-bHLH-WD40 (MBW) complex, consisting of MYB, bHLH, and WD40 proteins, plays a pivotal role in regulating these enzyme genes by interacting with their promoters [7]. 

Further complexity in the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway arises from the participation of microRNAs (miRNAs), which serve as critical regulators of gene expression in plants, including genes associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis. For example, in Arabidopsis and Populus, miR156 enhances anthocyanin biosynthesis by targeting SPL genes [8,9]. MiR858 acts as an inhibitor of this process in tomato and kiwifruit by suppressing SlMYB7 and AaMYBC1, respectively [10,11]. Interestingly, induced mdm-miR858 expression under light stress leads to enhanced anthocyanin biosynthesis in apple peel [12]. These discoveries underscore the dual roles of miRNA in modulating anthocyanin biosynthesis in the aboveground organs of plants. 

Current research on miR2111 primarily explores its role in nodulation and nitrogen fixation in legumes, as well as its regulatory function in plant responses to abiotic stress [13–16]. IbmiR2111 was initially identified in Arabidopsis under phosphate deficiency, where it targets a Kelch repeat F-box protein gene, AtKFB (AT3G27150) [16]. KFB is part of the SCF (Skp1-Cullin-Fbox) complex within the E3 ubiquitin ligase family, crucial in plant growth, development, and response to environmental challenges [17–22]. 

In this study, we delve into the specific role of Ib-miR2111 in anthocyanin biosynthesis in the underground tuberous roots of sweetpotato, a hexaploid crop with a complex genetic background. 

Our investigation involved a thorough analysis of Ib-miR2111 sequence characterization, expression profiling, followed by IbKFB target validation of Ib-miR2111 through degradome sequencing and transient expression studies in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana). Manipulations of Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB through overexpression strategies and CRISPR/Cas9 dual-target technology elucidated their pivotal function in modulating anthocyanin levels in sweetpotato. Furthermore, regulatory mechanisms underlying IbKFB were explored by utilizing a range of protein interaction methods including sweetpotato yeast library screening, yeast two-hybrid (Y2H), bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC), GST pull-down, co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP), and ubiquitination assays. Moreover, in vitro cell assays were conducted to assess the anti-inflammatory effects of anthocyanin extracts from the transgenic sweetpotatoes, with modified Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB expression, on inflammatory cell morphology, cytokine expression patterns, cell proliferation rates. These studies collectively provide novel insights into the post-transcriptional and post-translational regulatory mechanisms of anthocyanin biosynthesis in sweetpotato, highlighting the critical roles of Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB. Our findings not only advance the understanding of miRNAmediated gene regulation in sweetpotato, but also have implications for developing biofortified varieties with enhanced healthpromoting properties in other crops. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

This study utilized fresh tuberous roots from six sweetpotato cultivars categorized by flesh color: white-fleshed (‘SL-1’, ‘XS-18’), yellow-fleshed (‘SS-8’, ‘LZX’), and purple-fleshed (‘NZS-4’, ‘XZS-8’). These cultivars were obtained from the Xuzhou Sweetpotato Research Institute of the Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences and maintained at the College of Life Sciences, Shanxi Agricultural University. Stem segments with buds were grown in Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium maintained at a temperature of $2 2 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16-hour light/8-hour dark regime with $6 0 \%$ relative humidity. One-month-old tissue-cultured plantlets were transplanted to the experimental field of Shanxi Agricultural University. Tuberous roots were harvested 90 days post-planting for anthocyanin quantification and RNA extraction. Tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) and Arabidopsis (Col-0) were grown under a 16- hour light/8-hour dark regime at $2 5 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

Sequence and expression analysis of Ib-miR2111 and its target IbKFB in sweetpotato 

The sequences of the precursor (Ib-pre-miR2111) and the primary transcript (Ib-pri-MIR2111) were identified using a BLAST 

search with the sweetpotato genome database (https://public-genomes-ngs.molgen.mpg.de/SweetPotato/DOWNLOADS/). Secondary structural of hairpin formations by Ib-pre-miR2111 sequences was evaluated using the RNAfold web server (https://rna.tbi.univie.ac.at/cgi-bin/RNAWebSuite/RNAfold.cgi). The conservation of Ib-miR2111 was examined through comparative analysis with mature miR2111 sequences from various plant species including Vitis vinifera, Arabidopsis, Brassica napus, Brassica rapa, Lotus japonicus, Medicago truncatula, Theobroma cacao, and Vigna unguiculata, accessed through the PMRD plant miRNA database [23]. 

The following analyses of Ib-miR2111 and Ib-pri-MIR2111 refer specifically to Ib-miR2111a and Ib-pri-MIR2111a, respectively. Cleavage site analysis between Ib-miR2111 and its target gene was conducted using the psRNATarget online software [24] and degradome sequencing [25]. Genomic DNA and total RNA were extracted using DNA (Solarbio, Beijing, China) and RNA extraction kit (TAKARA, Dalian, China), respectively. Ib-pri-MIR2111 and IbKFB were amplified using specific primers listed in Dataset S1. 

Conserved domains in KFBs were identified through PfamScan database [26]. Phylogenetic analysis of KFBs was conducted using the Maximum-Likelihood method in IQTree with 1,000 bootstrap iterations. Arabidopsis KFB protein sequences were sourced from the TAIR10 database (https://www.Arabidopsis.org/download/index-auto.jsp?dir=%2Fdownload_files%2FProteins%2FTAIR10_protein_lists) (accessed on June 5, 2021). Multiple sequence alignment was conducted using DNAMAN software v9.0.1.116. Homologous protein sequences to IbKFB from Ipomoea triloba and Ipomoea trifida were obtained from the Sweetpotato Genomics Resource (https://sweetpotato.uga.edu/gt4sp_download.shtml) (accessed on April 17, 2021) [27]. Protein sequences of IaKFB from Ipomoea aquatica, StKFB from Solanum tuberosum, OsKFB from Oryza sativa, were obtained from the National Genomics Data Center (https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/search/?dbId=gwh&q=PRJCA002216& page $^ { \prime = 1 }$ ), Spud DB Potato Genomics Resources (https://spuddb. uga.edu/), Rice Genome Annotation Project database (https:// rice.plantbiology.msu.edu/) (accessed on April 17, 2021), respectively, as noted in Dataset S2. 

# Generation of Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB overexpression and knockout constructs

The overexpression vectors for Ib-miR2111 (pC2300-pOT2-Ibpri-MIR2111) and IbKFB (pC1300-IbKFB-GFP) were prepared according to the following method. A cloning vector, designated as pOT2-Ib-pri-MIR2111, was constructed utilizing restriction enzyme sites of HindIII and EcoRI. Subsequently, the pOT2-Ib-pri-MIR2111 was transferred into a modified pCAMBIA2300 binary vector to generate the overexpression vectors for Ib-miR2111, termed pC2300-pOT2-Ib-pri-MIR2111, using the PacI restriction enzyme site. The IbKFB gene, excluding the stop codon, was introduced into the vector pC1300-GFP, which carries a GFP fusion protein, using SacI and SalI. To inhibit miR2111, a small tandem target mimic (STTM-At-miR2111) vector was constructed using a previously reported method [28]. Additionally, Arabidopsis (Col-0) mutant Atkfb (SALK_044075) was acquired from Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (ABRC https://abrc.osu.edu/). 

For CRISPR/Cas9 mediated editing of Ib-pri-MIR2111 and IbKFB, two gRNAs were designed using the CRISPOR tool [29]. These gRNAs were cloned into the pCBC-DT1T2 backbone vector, which was subsequently assembled with the pHSE401 vector to create CRISPR-Ib-miR2111 and CRISPR-IbKFB constructs [30]. The CRISPR constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium (A.) tumefaciens strain EHA105 for transformation. Detailed primers and single-

guide RNA information is provided in Dataset S1 and Fig. S1, respectively. 

# Interaction validation of Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB with transient expression system

To validate interactions between Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB, the leaves of 4-week-old tobacco plants were infiltrated with A. tumefaciens strains carrying pC2300-pOT2-Ib-pri-MIR2111 and pC1300- IbKFB-GFP constructs. The fluorescent signal was observed using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica Microsystems CMS GmbH, Germany). 

# Subcellular localization analysis of IbKFB

Transformed strains containing pC1300-IbKFB-GFP and pC1300- GFP were delivered into onion epidermal cells via the gene gun method for transient expression. The signal was detected with a Leica DM6B fluorescence microscope (Leica Microsystems, Germany). 

# Sweetpotato transformation

Sweetpotato embryogenic suspension cultures of the cultivar XS-18 were transformed using these plasmid constructs through an Agrobacterium-mediated transformation protocol based on a previously established method [31], with specific modifications to enhance efficiency. The transformation process is shown in Fig. S2. These modifications included extending the infection and co-cultivation period with A. tumefaciens strain EHA105 to 30 min and 4 d, increasing acetosyringone concentration to $3 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ to enhance gene transfer efficiency, using a combination of antibiotics with $2 5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ cephalosporin (Cef) and $1 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ hygromycin (Hyg) to eliminate A. tumefaciens and select transgenic cells. The resulting transformants were designated as Ib-miR2111-OE, CRISPR-Ib-miR2111, IbKFB-OE, and CRISPR-IbKFB. 

# Detection of gene editing sites using high-throughput tracking of mutations (HI-TOM)

To verify positive transgenic sweetpotato lines for CRISPR-IbmiR2111 and CRISPR-IbKFB, PCR screening was conducted using U626-IDF and U629-t-IDR primers (Dataset S1). HI-TOM technology was employed to identify genotypic alterations in these transgenic lines [32]. Following the instructions for gene editing mutation sequencing in High Throughput Mutation Analysis Website (https://www.hi-tom.net/hi-tom/index-CH.php), primers were designed to amplify regions flanking 100 base pairs (bp) upstream and downstream of the gRNA1 and gRNA2 target sites (Dataset S1). PCR amplification was performed on DNA extracted from both gene-edited and wild type (WT) sweetpotato leaves. The resulting PCR products were sequenced using the HI-TOM method, enabling precise determination of mutation sites. 

# Gene expression analysis using quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNA extraction, RNA integrity assessment, RNA concentration measurement, cDNA synthesis, and quantitative real-time PCR were carried out following an established method [25,33]. The specific primer sequences are listed in Dataset S1. In tobacco experiments, NtActin was used as an internal reference gene. AtU6 and AtActin were used as reference genes for normalizing miRNA and mRNA expression levels in Arabidopsis, while IbU6 

and IbActin served as internal reference genes in sweetpotato. Each sample was analyzed with three independent biological replicates, each comprising three technical replicates. Expression levels were quantified with the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method [34]. 

# Northern blotting

Northern blotting was performed following a previously established protocol [35]. Total RNA was extracted from the tuberous roots of XS-18, Ib-miR2111-OE and CRISPR-Ib-miR2111 sweetpotato using Trizol reagent (Solarbio, Beijing, China). Approximately $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g }$ of small RNA from each sample was resolved using $1 5 \%$ polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) with $^ { \mathrm { ~ 8 ~ M ~ } }$ urea. After transferring onto a Hybond $\mathsf { N } +$ membrane, the RNA was crosslinked by UV irradiation for 5 min and hybridized with biotin-labeled oligonucleotides at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . U6 was used as a loading control to normalize results. Three biological replicates were performed. Probe sequences are listed in Dataset S1. 

# Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H) assay

The coding sequence (CDS) of IbKFB was inserted into the pGBKT7 vector using the NdeI and BamHI restriction sites to create pGBKT7-IbKFB. Concurrently, cDNA from the tuberous roots was inserted into the pGADT7 vector to generate a comprehensive cDNA yeast library, constructed by OE Biotech Company, Qingdao, China. A mating approach was used to facilitate interactions between the bait (pGBKT7-IbKFB) and prey (cDNA library in pGADT7) constructs. Different combinations of recombinant and empty vectors were co-transformed into the Y2H Gold yeast strain. The transformed yeast cells were plated on synthetic dropout (SD) medium devoid of leucine and tryptophan (SD/-Leu/-Trp, DDO) for selection. Interaction assays were further refined on quadruple dropout medium lacking leucine, tryptophan, histidine, and adenine (SD/-Leu/-Trp/-His/-Ade) supplemented with X-a-Gal. DNA from positive clones exhibiting growth on selective medium was sequenced, and the resulting sequences were subjected to functional annotation using NCBI BLAST (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.- gov/Blast.cgi) and UniProt (https://www.uniprot.org). Specifically, interactions between IbKFB and key proteins involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis such as IbPAL1, IbSAUR50, and IbGAPCp1 were verified using Y2H assay. 

# BiFC assays

BiFC assays were conducted to validate interactions of IbKFB with IbPAL1, IbSAUR50, and IbGAPCp1. The CDS of IbKFB was fused into the pXY-106-YFP-N vector, while the coding sequences of IbPAL1, IbSAUR50, and IbGAPCp1 were introduced into the pXY-104-YFP-C vector, respectively. Specific primers used for these constructs are presented in Dataset S1. The resulting constructs (IbKFB-nYFP, IbPAL1-cYFP, IbSAUR50-cYFP, and IbGAPCp1-cYFP) were introduced into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Mixtures of A. tumefaciens containing different construct pairs were infiltrated together into 4-week-old tobacco leaves. After injection, the tobacco plants were exposed to darkness for a duration of $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , followed by cultivation at approximately $2 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Subsequently, yellow fluorescence signals were observed using a Leica DM6B fluorescence microscope. Three independent biological replicates were carried out. 

# Molecular docking analysis

Protein data for docking analysis were sourced from the Alpha-Fold Protein Structure Database [36], selecting the highest confidence models produced by AlphaFold. Initial docking simulations 

were conducted using the web-based GRAMM-X platform [37]. The 3D structures (pdb files) obtained were subsequently analyzed for molecular docking using AutoDockTools 1.5.6 [38], and refined models were subjected to docking simulations using AutoDock 4.2.6. Interaction patterns were visualized using LigPlot+ 2.2.4 [39] for the 2D model and PyMOL (https://www.pymol.org/) for the 3D model, respectively. 

# Co-IP and ubiquitination assay

The coding sequences of IbPAL1 and IbGAPCp1 were cloned into the pCAMBIA-35S-4 Myc-CMV-3 FLAG vector, respectively, while IbKFB was inserted into the pBinGFP2 plant expression vector. These recombinant constructs were introduced into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 and subsequently infiltrated into the 4-weekold tobacco leaves. Following a 48-hour incubation, proteins were isolated from the infiltrated leaves using Co-IP buffer comprising $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 100 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, $1 \%$ Triton X-100, 1 mM DTT, 1 mM PMSF, supplemented with a $1 ~ \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail. These extracts were then incubated with Protein $\mathsf { A } + \mathsf { G }$ Agarose beads (P2012, Beyotime) in conjunction with a GFP antibody and incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ to pull down the protein complexes. The immunoprecipitated proteins were then visualized by immunoblotting employing anti-GFP (1:5,000, M20003, Abmart) and anti-FLAG (1:5,000, M20008, Abmart) antibodies. IRDye 800CW Goat anti-Mouse secondary antibodies (1:20,000, 31450, LI-COR) were used. 

For ubiquitination assay, following $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of incubation posttransfection, each leaf was treated with infiltration buffer, with or without $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132 (a proteasome inhibitor), and incubated for an additional $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Leaf tissues (1 g per sample) were homogenized in protein extraction buffer $1 0 \%$ glycerol, $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl with a pH of 7.5, 5 mM $\mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ and 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM DTT, $1 ~ \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail, $0 . 2 \%$ Nonidet P-40 Substitute). The resulting raw protein extracts underwent incubation with BeyoMagTM Anti-Myc Magnetic Beads (P2118, Beyotime) to isolate ubiquitylated forms of IbPAL1-Myc and IbGAPCp1-Myc. The eluted proteins were treated with SDS sample buffer, heated at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 10 min, and separated by SDS-PAGE gel. Proteins were blotted onto a PVDF membrane, blocked in TBST buffer containing $3 \%$ skim milk for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ with antibodies targeting GFP (1:5,000, M20003, Abmart), Myc (1:5,000, M20002, Abmart), and ubiquitin (1:5,000, ab19247, Abmart). Goat Anti-Mouse IgG HR (1:5,000, M21001, Abmart) was used as the secondary antibodies. Protein signals were detected using Pierce ECL Western Blotting Substrate (Thermo Fisher Scientific), followed by imaging with a VILBER FUSION FX5 imaging system (France). 

# GST pull-down assay

The coding sequences of IbGAPCp1 and IbKFB were inserted into the pGEX-4T-1 and pET-28a plasmids, fused with GST and His tags, respectively. These recombinant plasmids were introduced into E. coli BL21 (DE3) strains. Protein expression was induced by the addition of $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG followed by incubation at $2 0 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ for a duration of 4 h. Cells were subsequently lysed in specific buffer $( 1 \times \mathrm { P B S }$ buffer, 1 mM PMSF) and sonicated, followed by centrifugation to collect supernatants. GST-tagged proteins were isolated using GST magnetic beads (P2258, Beyotime) and then eluted with GST elution buffer (150 mM NaCl, 50 mM Tris-HCl with a pH of 8.0, $2 5 \ \mathrm { \ m M }$ Reduced Glutathione). After three times washes, these beads were mixed with the His-tagged IbKFB supernatants to allow protein interactions. After $^ { 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ incubation, beads were washed to remove non-specifically bound proteins with GST-washing buffer (150 mM NaCl, $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl with a pH of 7.5, 2 mM EDTA). The bound proteins were eluted with GST elution buffer. Interac-

tions were analyzed through immunoblotting employing anti-His (1:5,000, M20001, Abmart) and anti-GST (1:5,000, M20007, Abmart) antibodies. 

# Anthocyanin content detection

Sweetpotato tuberous roots were thoroughly cleaned, sliced, freeze-dried and ground into a fine powder. One gram of each powdered sample was immersed in $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 \%$ HCl-methanol solution $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ and kept at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in darkness for a period of $2 4 \ \mathrm { h } .$ After incubation, the mixture was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the supernatant was analyzed at $5 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $6 5 7 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ using a UV-2450 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan). The anthocyanin concentration was determined using the equation: QAnthocyanins $= \left( \mathrm { A } _ { 5 3 0 } - 0 . 2 5 ^ { \ast } \mathrm { A } _ { 6 5 7 } \right)$ / M, where QAnthocyanins indicates the anthocyanin content; $\mathsf { A } _ { 5 3 0 }$ and $\mathsf { A } _ { 6 5 7 }$ represent the absorbance at $5 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ and $6 5 7 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , respectively; M is the sample mass in grams. Each sample was subjected to triplicate measurements across three separate biological experiments. 

# Extraction and purification of anthocyanin

To extract and purify anthocyanin from sweetpotato root powder for anti-inflammatory experiments, $_ { 1 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of sweetpotato root powder was immersed in $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ ethanol containing $1 0 . 5 \%$ citric acid at a 4:6 vol ratio. This mixture was maintained at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 3 \ \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , followed by centrifugation at a speed of 12,000 rpm for 10 min at room temperature, after which the supernatant enriched in anthocyanins was collected. The collected $6 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ anthocyaninrich supernatant was re-extracted by mixing it with a solution of n-butanol and $0 . 0 1 \%$ hydrochloric acid $( \boldsymbol { \mathbf { v } } / \boldsymbol { \mathbf { v } } = 9 5 { : } 5 )$ along with $0 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of $2 \%$ ammonium ferric sulfate solution (Sangon Biotech Shanghai, China), then boiled for 40 min. The concentration of purified anthocyanin extracts was determined. 

# Anthocyanin treatment and confocal microscope imaging

Human rheumatoid arthritis synovial fibroblasts (MH7A) were obtained from Qingqi Biotechnology Development Co., Shanghai, China. The cells cultured in DMEM (Thermo Fisher, USA) with $1 0 \%$ FBS under $5 \%$ CO2 at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ were divided into groups during their exponential growth phase to test anthocyanin extracts from different sweetpotato sources, including WT, Ib-miR2111-OE, CRISPR-Ib-miR2111, IbKFB-OE, and CRISPR-IbKFB. Each group received $1 . 9 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ DMEM mixed with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ anthocyanin extract. After $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , cells were fixed in $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde, rinsed with PBS, and then permeabilized with $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100. 

To visualize cellular structures, cells were stained with Actin-Tracker Green-488 for actin filaments and DAPI for nuclei, respectively. Confocal microscopy was performed using an Olympus FV3000 confocal microscope. Images captured at wavelengths suitable for Actin Tracker Green and DAPI provided detailed views of cellular morphology and cytoskeletal arrangements. 

# Cytokine detection by ELISA assay

To quantify inflammatory cytokines in MH7A after anthocyanin treatment, an ELISA assay was conducted. Cells were plated in 96- well dishes and maintained in DMEM supplements with fetal bovine serum and Penicillin-Streptomycin antibiotics. After $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ for cell attachment, the medium was replaced with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of anthocyanin extracts at varying concentrations. Following another 24-hour incubation, supernatants were collected for cytokine measurement, specifically interleukin-1 alpha (IL-1a), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and interleukin-18 (IL-18), using ELISA kits (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai). The assay utilized capture and detection antibodies 

with a reporter enzyme (Tecan, Shanghai), and absorbance was quantified at $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . Cytokine levels were derived from standard curves, with data collected in triplicate. 

# Cell apoptosis and cycle analysis

To evaluate cell apoptosis and cell cycle progression, MH7A cells were used. Cell apoptosis degree was evaluated utilizing the Annexin V FITC/Propidium Iodide (PI) Apoptosis Detection Kit supplied by BD Science & Technologies, Shanghai, China. Following the manufacturer’s guidelines, the cells were labeled with Annexin V FITC and PI, then analyzed through flow cytometry utilizing a FACS Canto II system (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA). Cells were categorized into viable, early apoptotic, late apoptotic, or necrotic groups based on staining patterns. This procedure was performed in triplicate to ensure reliability and reproducibility. 

For the analysis of cell cycle, cells treated with anthocyanin were fixed in $7 0 \%$ ethanol at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ overnight, and then labeled with PI to evaluate DNA content across cell cycle phases. The fluorescence from FITC and PI was measured in the green and red channels, respectively, using the same FACS Canto II flow cytometer. Data acquisition and analysis for both apoptosis and cell cycle phases were facilitated by BD FACSDiva software (BD Biosciences, San Jose, USA). The software enables detailed profiling of the cell cycle stages through DNA content histograms and quantifies the proportion of cell populations in sub-G1, G1, S, and G2/M phases, providing insights into the effects of anthocyanin treatment on cell proliferation and cell death mechanisms.

# Interaction between Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB in sweetpotato

Degradome sequencing showed Target plot with a single prominent peak on chromosome 5 (position: 13713008–13717959) of the sweetpotato genome, located 564 bp from the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end, highlighting the cleavage site of Ib-miR2111 (Fig. 2A). The alignment of the sequence (chr5: 13713008–13717959) with the sweetpotato genome showed it encoding a KFB protein, subsequently naming it IbKFB. This site was analyzed using the psRNATarget tool, which aligned well with the degradome results (Fig. 2B). Quantitative real-time PCR using sweetpotato tuberous roots of XS-18 and XZS-3 varieties showed an inverse expression pattern between IbKFB and Ib-miR2111, with significantly higher level of IbKFB in XS-18 (Fig. 2C). To further confirm this interaction, the Ib-pri-MIR2111 and IbKFB were isolated from the sweetpotato variety XS-18 (Fig. S3 and Fig. S4). Then, the recombinant vectors pC2300-pOT2-Ib-pri-MIR2111 and pC1300-IbKFB-GFP were constructed and transiently transfected into tobacco plants for expression. Observations from transient expression experiments 

demonstrated that the green fluorescence of IbKFB was reduced when co-expressed with Ib-miR2111, suggesting posttranscriptional regulation (Fig. 2D). Quantitative real-time PCR confirmed a reduction in IbKFB expression in the presence of IbmiR2111 in tobacco leaves, supporting the targeted suppression by this miRNA (Fig. 2E). 

# Characterization of IbKFB

Phylogenetic analysis indicated a close evolutionary relationship between IbKFB and AtKFB47 (AT3G27150) in Arabidopsis, suggesting functional similarities between these proteins (Fig. S5A). The protein structure of IbKFB includes an F-box domain and two Kelch motifs, features commonly found in KFB proteins across various plant species (Fig. S5B). Subcellular localization studies, conducted with a pC1300-IbKFB-GFP fusion protein transiently expressed in onion epidermal cells, showed that IbKFB is primarily localized in the nucleus, underscoring its potential role in nuclear functions (Fig. S6).

IbKFB represses anthocyanin biosynthesis by interacting with and degrading key enzymes in sweetpotato 

To delineate the function of IbKFB in modulating the production of anthocyanin in sweetpotato, we employed Y2H system to screen a sweetpotato yeast library and identified 21 clones that potentially interact with IbKFB. Functional annotation of these clones revealed involvement in various biological processes, including growth, glucose metabolism, protein kinase activity, ubiquitination and degradation, cell transport, and specifically, anthocyanin biosynthesis (Fig. 5A). We focused on validating interactions of IbKFB with three key proteins related to anthocyanin biosynthesis: IbPAL1, small auxin up RNA 50 (IbSAUR50), and glyceraldehyde-3- phosphate dehydrogenase 1 (IbGAPCp1). Yeast cells co-expressing IbKFB with these proteins exhibited robust growth and distinct blue coloration on SD/-Leu/-Trp/-His/-Ade/X-a-Gal medium, indicating effective interaction, unlike the negative controls (Fig. 5B). Supporting these findings, molecular docking and BiFC assays experiments validated the physical interactions between IbKFB and the selected proteins (Fig. 5C and D). Additionally, Co-IP and GST pull-down assays provided further evidence of the physical association between IbKFB and IbGAPCp1, reinforcing the specificity of these interactions (Fig. 5E and F). Ubiquitination assays confirmed that IbPAL1 and IbGAPCp1 are substrates for degrada-

tion mediated by IbKFB through the ubiquitination-26S proteasome pathway, illustrating the role of IbKFB in post-translational modification processes that impact phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Fig. 5G and H). These comprehensive in vitro and in vivo biochemical assays systematically demonstrate that IbKFB interacts with and modulates key proteins involved in anthocyanin and broader phenylpropanoid biosynthetic pathways, underscoring its significant regulatory role within these crucial metabolic networks.

Crucial roles of Ib-miR2111 in enhancing anthocyanin biosynthesis in the under-ground tuberous roots of sweetpotato 

Sweetpotatoes, particularly PFSP varieties, have gained attention for their rich anthocyanin content, which provides notable nutritional and antioxidant benefits, contributing to global food security and human health [40,41]. Anthocyanin in PFSP has been associated with a lower risk of cardiovascular disease, improved visual and cognitive function, and anti-cancer properties, underscoring the importance of enhancing anthocyanin levels in these tuberous roots. 

MiRNAs have emerged as pivotal post-transcriptional regulators [42,43]. Several recent studies illustrate their involvement in anthocyanin biosynthetic pathways. For example, miR828 and 

miR858 have been shown to target transcription factors in the MYB family, which modulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis and other plants [10–12,44]. 

Our study demonstrates that Ib-miR2111 displays notably elevated expression levels in PFSP compared to WFSP, suggesting its critical role in enhancing anthocyanin accumulation. To validate the functional role of Ib-miR2111 in anthocyanin biosynthesis, we manipulated its expression in Arabidopsis and sweetpotato (Fig. S7 and Fig. 4). Overexpression of Ib-miR2111 in Arabidopsis and sweetpotato resulted in pronounced purple pigmentation, indicating enhanced anthocyanin accumulation, while CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated downregulation of Ib-miR2111 in sweetpotato reduced anthocyanin levels. These findings emphasize the pivotal role of miRNAs in regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis, consistent with previous study where overexpression of VcMIR156a in blueberry increased anthocyanin level [45]. The broader functional spectrum of Ib-miR2111 and potential agricultural applications 

MiR2111 has been recognized as a multifunctional regulator in response to various stresses, including phosphate deficiency, high nitrogen, salt, wounding, and topping [15,16,46–48]. In legumes, miR2111 has a novel role in nodule formation, where it undergoes long-distance transport from the shoot to the root to regulate this process. High nitrogen levels or ectopic expression of CLE35 (CLAVATA3/ESR-related) suppresses systemic accumulation of miR2111, which in turn increases the transcript accumulation of its target gene, TML (Too Much Love), thereby inhibiting root nodulation [13–15]. This role of miR2111 in legumes suggests that miR-NAs can orchestrate complex developmental and physiological responses in plants. 

In our study, we extended our investigation to include additional PFSP and WFSP varieties, confirming that higher IbmiR2111 expression correlates with increased anthocyanin level, accompanied by upregulated expression of key anthocyanin biosynthetic enzyme genes, including IbCHS, IbCHI, IbDFR, IbANS (Fig. 3). This correlation is consistent with findings in other species, where miRNAs modulate secondary metabolite pathways by targeting genes directly involved in biosynthesis. For instance, miR156 has been reported to regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis by targeting SPL genes, which in turn affect MYB transcription factors implicated in the pathway [8]. The functional 

conservation across species highlights the evolutionary significance of miRNAs in controlling secondary metabolite production. 

Additionally, in order to identify regulatory elements associated with anthocyanin pigmentation, long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) were analyzed in PFSP (XZS-3) and WFSP (XS-18). We identified 14,939 novel lncRNA transcripts through full-length transcriptome sequencing (Fig. S14A). Among them, 12,592 lncRNAs were present in both varieties, while 1,233 and 1,114 lncRNAs were specifically expressed in XZS-3 and XS-18, respectively. Furthermore, we conducted an in-depth analysis of the differential expression of lncRNAs, applying the criteria of |log2(fold change) $\mid \geq 1$ and an adjusted $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ . Compared to XS-18, 1,331 lncRNAs were upregulated, while 1,316 were downregulated in XZS-3 (Fig. S14B). To verify the accuracy of the sequencing data, eight differentially expressed lncRNAs were randomly selected for quantitative real-time PCR assays (Fig. S14C). The primers are presented in Dataset S1. The findings revealed that the expression trends of these eight lncRNAs were consistent with the sequencing data, demonstrating the reliability of the sequencing results. Furthermore, 64 lncRNAs with potential interactions with IbmiR2111 were identified utilizing psRNATarget [24] (Dataset S4), and nine of these candidates showed inverse expression patterns relative to Ib-miR211 (Fig. S14D). Based on these findings, we hypothesize that these lncRNAs may regulate the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway by modulating Ib-miR2111. Future studies will focus on elucidating the specific interaction mechanisms between these lncRNAs and miR2111, as well as their detailed roles in anthocyanin biosynthesis.

Ib-miR2111-modulated IbKFB acts as a negative regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis 

KFB proteins are crucial for plant growth, development, circadian rhythms, secondary metabolism, and response to environmental stimuli across diverse species [17–22,49]. For instance, specific KFB members such as CFK1 in Arabidopsis are involved in light-mediated growth processes, like hypocotyl elongation in response to light signals [19]. In rice, OsFBK12 modulates ethylene levels, affecting key physiological responses such as seed germination and leaf senescence [20]. Additionally, AFR in Arabidopsis facilitates the degradation of light signal inhibitors, enhancing the 

plant’s response to dawn light signals and illustrating the versatility of KFB proteins in environmental adaptations [20]. In this study, we identified IbKFB as a target gene of Ib-miR2111, a relationship supported by bioinformatics predictions, degradome sequencing, expression analysis, and tobacco transient transformation experiments (Fig. 2). Our data revealed that there was a notable increase in IbKFB expression in anthocyanin-poor sweetpotato varieties, presenting an inverse expression pattern relative to Ib-miR2111 (Fig. 3). Functional manipulation of IbKFB in both Arabidopsis and sweetpotato demonstrated that silencing of IbKFB led to increased anthocyanin accumulation, coinciding with elevated expression of key anthocyanin biosynthesis enzyme genes, including IbCHS, IbCHI, IbDFR, and IbANS. Conversely, overexpression of IbKFB was associated with reduced anthocyanin level, underscoring its role as an inhibitor of anthocyanin production in these species (Fig. S7 and Fig. 4). These findings align with studies in other plants where KFB proteins act as negative regulators in secondary metabolite pathways, such as in Arabidopsis where KFB50 has been shown to regulate flavonoid biosynthesis by targeting flavonoid biosynthetic enzymes for degradation [49]. 

# IbKFB modulates the stability of IbPAL, a crucial enzyme for anthocyanin synthesis

A notable interaction partner of IbKFB identified in our study is IbPAL1 (Fig. 5B, C and D). PAL initiates the phenylpropanoid biosynthetic pathway, directing carbon flow toward the synthesis of various aromatic compounds, including anthocyanins [50–52]. This interaction mirrors findings in other species, such as in Arabidopsis where KFBs mediate the ubiquitination and degradation of PAL, modulating phenylpropanoid accumulation in response to environmental cues [49–54]. In our study, ubiquitination assays confirmed that IbKFB facilitates the ubiquitination and subsequent degradation of IbPAL1 through the 26S proteasome pathway (Fig. 5G). This regulatory mechanism underscores the role of IbKFB in anthocyanin biosynthesis by affecting the stability of PAL, a crucial enzyme for phenylpropanoid and anthocyanin synthesis. Our findings are in line with studies in other crops, such as grape and apple, where miRNA-mediated regulation of ubiquitin ligases has been shown to modulate anthocyanin biosynthesis under various environmental conditions [55,56]. The conservation of this regulatory mechanism across species emphasizes the importance of miRNA and KFB proteins as modulators of secondary metabolism, providing potential targets for genetic manipulation in crop improvement. 

# Broader interactions of IbKFB in sweetpotato metabolic pathways

Beyond anthocyanin biosynthesis, IbKFB interacts with other proteins in sweetpotato, which were identified through Y2H assays. Key proteins among these are IbSAUR50 and IbGAPCp1 (Fig. 5B, C and D). SAUR proteins have been recognized for their significant contributions to plant growth, development processes, and stress adaptations [57–60]. For instance, OsSAUR39 has been implicated in enhancing anthocyanin accumulation in rice [58], suggesting a conserved role of SAUR proteins in modulating secondary metabolism. Similarly, AtSAUR32 in Arabidopsis participates in the drought stress response regulated by ABA, highlighting SAUR proteins’ roles in both growth and environmental adaptation [59]. The interaction between IbKFB and IbSAUR50 suggests that IbKFB may influence anthocyanin biosynthesis indirectly through growth regulation and stress response pathways, potentially impacting anthocyanin accumulation under specific conditions. IbGAPCp1, another interaction partner of IbKFB, is a key enzyme in glycolysis and is crucial for energy metabolism and stress adaptation in plants [61,62]. GAPCp1 provides interme-

diates that are essential for various biosynthetic pathways, including the phenylpropanoid biosynthetic pathway. The confirmed interactions between IbKFB and GAPCp1 through pull-down, Co-IP, and ubiquitination assays (Fig. 5E, Fig. 6F and H) suggests that IbKFB may regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis by influencing the availability of energy or metabolic intermediates. 

# Anti-inflammatory effects of high-anthocyanin sweetpotato extracts: Disruption of cell morphology, suppression of cytokine expression and cell proliferation, and induction of apoptosis

Anthocyanins are increasingly recognized for their diverse health-promoting effects, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer properties, with growing evidence supporting their potential in disease prevention and management [63,64]. Inflammation protects against pathogens and injury but becomes harmful in its chronic form. Chronic inflammation can lead to diseases like rheumatoid arthritis (RA), causing joint damage and disability [64,65]. In this context, the anti-inflammatory properties of anthocyanins are particularly relevant, as they can mitigate inflammatory responses by modulating cytokine production, transcription factors, and other key mediators involved in inflammation. This study examines the effects of anthocyanin extracts from transgenic sweetpotatoes with altered Ib-miR2111 and IbKFB expression on inflammatory pathways in MH7A cells, which are a model for RA-related inflammation. Treatment with these anthocyanin-rich extracts induced pronounced morphological changes in MH7A cells, disrupting their normal spindle shape and causing cell contraction and nuclear material leakage. These effects were prominent in cells treated with extracts from the IbmiR2111-OE sweetpotato lines, which had the highest anthocyanin concentrations, suggesting dose-dependent relationship between anthocyanin level and cellular disruption (Fig. 6B). 

Anthocyanin is known to modulate inflammation by inhibiting the production and release of inflammatory cytokines, which are key mediators in chronic inflammatory diseases [66]. Our results support this mechanism, as extracts with higher anthocyanin content, particularly from the Ib-miR2111-OE line, significantly reduced the expression of inflammatory cytokines IL-1a, IL-6, and IL-18 in MH7A cells compared to extracts from WT (Fig. 6C, D and E). These results are consistent with previous findings where anthocyanin from other sources, such as blueberries and black rice, have been shown to reduce cytokine levels and attenuate inflammatory responses in cellular and animal models of inflammation [67,68]. This study extends these findings to genetically enhanced sweetpotato lines, illustrating their potential as a functional food source for anti-inflammatory compounds. 

In addition to cytokine suppression, studies have indicated that anthocyanin possesses anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting cell proliferation and inducing apoptosis in inflammatory and cancer cells [69,70]. Consistent with these findings, our study demonstrates that anthocyanin extracts from Ib-miR2111-OE and CRISPR-IbKFB transgenic lines effectively inhibit MH7A cell proliferation and DNA synthesis by inducing apoptosis and causing cell cycle arrest, A significant proportion of cells treated with these extracts were arrested in the G2/M phase, preventing further division and proliferation (Figs. 7 and 9). These observations suggest that anthocyanin-rich sweetpotato extracts could be useful not only in RA treatment but potentially in managing other proliferative diseases. These findings underscore the therapeutic potential of anthocyanin-rich sweetpotato extracts, which can disrupt inflammatory cell morphology, suppress cytokine expression, and inhibit cellular proliferation through mechanisms involving apoptosis and cell cycle arrest. # Conclusion

This study identified Ib-miR2111 and its target gene, IbKFB, as central regulators of anthocyanin biosynthesis in sweetpotato. Our results revealed that Ib-miR2111 is predominantly expressed in purple-fleshed sweetpotatoes, while IbKFB is more abundant in white-fleshed varieties. By overexpressing Ib-miR2111 and using CRISPR/Cas9-edited IbKFB in sweetpotato, we achieved significantly increased anthocyanin accumulation. Conversely, CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated knockout of Ib-miR2111 and overexpression of IbKFB led to reduced anthocyanin levels, highlighting the antagonistic roles of these molecules in anthocyanin regulation. Beyond anthocyanin production, our research delved into the regulatory network involving IbKFB, uncovering interactions with key proteins such as IbPAL1 and IbGAPCp1, which were shown to undergo ubiquitination and degradation mediated by IbKFB, adding layers to the regulatory mechanis m. 

In vitro assays further demonstrated the anti-inflammatory and apoptotic effects of anthocyanin-enriched extracts from sweetpotatoes overexpressing Ib-miR2111 or with IbKFB knockout. These extracts disrupted inflammatory cell morphology, 

suppressed cytokine expression, and inhibited cell proliferation through apoptosis induction and cell cycle arrest, highlighting the health-promoting properties of anthocyanin-rich sweetpotatoes. 

In summary, this research provides insights into the posttranscriptional and post-translational regulatory roles of IbmiR2111 and IbKFB in anthocyanin biosynthesis. The findings pave the way for developing sweetpotato varieties enriched in anthocyanin with enhanced health-promoting properties, offering promising applications in agricultural biotechnology and contributing to global public health outcomes through biofortified, nutrient-rich crops.
