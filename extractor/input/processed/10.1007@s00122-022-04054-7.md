# Abstract

# Key message The mutation of ClZISO identifed in EMS-induced watermelon leads to photosensitive fesh in watermelon.

Abstract Watermelon (Citrullus lanatus) has a colorful fesh that attracts consumers and benefts human health. We developed an ethyl-methanesulfonate mutation library in red-feshed line ‘302’ to create new fesh color lines and found a yellowfeshed mutant which accumulated $\zeta$ -carotene. The initial yellow color of this mutant can be photobleached within 10 min under intense sunlight. A long-term light-emitting diode (LED) light treatment turned fesh color from yellow to pink. We identifed this unique variation as photosensitive fesh mutant $( \overleftarrow { p s f } )$ . Using bulked segregant analysis, we fne-mapped an EMS-induced G-A transversion in ‘psf’ which leads to a premature stop codon in 15-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene isomerase (ClZISO) gene. We detected that wild-type ClZISO is expressed in chromoplasts to catalyze the conversion of 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene to 9,9’-di-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene. The truncated $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ protein in psf lost this catalytic function. Light treatment can partially compensate $\mathrm { C l Z I S O } _ { \mathrm { m u } }$ isomerase activity via photoisomerization in vitro and in vivo. Transcriptome analysis showed that most carotenoid biosynthesis genes in psf were downregulated. The dramatic increase of ABA content in fesh with fruit development was blocked in psf. This study explores the molecular mechanism of carotenoid biosynthesis in watermelon and provides a theoretical and technical basis for breeding diferent fesh color lines in watermelon.

# Introduction

The watermelon fesh accumulates a large number of carotenoids that have fundamental contributions to human health as a source of retinoids (including vitamin A) and other health-related biologically active metabolites (Fraser and Bramley 2004; Baker and Gunther 2004). The coloration of watermelon fesh is attributable to its carotenoid composition and content (Bang et al. 2010). The traditional red watermelon fesh is largely pigmented by lycopene (red) with lower amounts of $\beta$ -carotene (orange). The canary yellow and yellow fesh lines accumulate small amounts of

violaxanthin, neoxanthin and neochrome (Bang et al. 2010), with orange fesh resulting from $\beta$ -carotene enrichment (Sandra et al. 2017). White and pale yellow fesh accumulate little violaxanthin and neoxanthin, and light yellow $\zeta$ -carotene in white-feshed watermelon was also reported (Kang et al. 2010). In China, watermelon is one of the major sources of dietary carotenoids (Zhang et al. 2017; Yuan et al. 2021). To meet market demands for novel functional foods and for natural sources of carotenoids, we used routine crosses and EMS mutagenesis to create new varieties of fesh color watermelon fruits.

Carotenoids are $\mathrm { C } _ { 4 0 }$ essential compounds and synthesized within plastids through the deoxy-xylulose phosphate isoprenoid pathway in plants (Hirschberg 2001; Walter and Strack 2011; Yuan et al. 2015). Carotenoids play an important role in plant photosystems and are crucial for their assembly, light harvesting and photoprotection (Ruiz-Sola and Rodríguez-Concepción 2012). In addition, carotenoids are precursors of plant hormones ABA and strigolactones (Rodríguez-Concepción et al. 2018). The carotenoid biosynthesis pathway has been proposed in watermelon (Grassi et al. 2013). The frst carotenoid produced in the pathway is the colorless

phytoene, which is catalyzed by phytoene synthase (PSY) through a head condensation of two $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 0 }$ molecules, geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP). Then, two double bonds are introduced into phytoene to form $\zeta .$ -carotene by phytoene desaturase (PDS). $\zeta$ -Carotene isomerase (ZISO) isomerizes the $1 5 { - } 1 5 ^ { \prime }$ cis double bond in tri-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene. $\zeta .$ -Carotene desaturase (ZDS) introduces another two double bonds to the tetra-cis-lycopene (prolycopene). Another isomerase, carotenoid cis–trans isomerase (CRTISO), produces alltrans-lycopene which is the red pigment accumulated in watermelons (Edwards et al. 2003; Li and Yuan 2013). Then, lycopene is cyclized to form $\beta$ -carotene with lycopene $\beta$ -cyclase (LCYb). Further oxygenations give rise to various xanthophylls. Diferent fesh colors in watermelons are primarily determined by the main structural carotenoid biosynthetic gene variations. ClPSY1 plays a dominant role in carotenoid biosynthesis during watermelon fruit ripening, in which transcript levels increase rapidly with fesh color development (Fang et al. 2020). Natural missense mutations in ClLCYB infuence ClLCYB protein abundance and contribute to red fesh color formation in watermelon. The decreased ClLCYB protein abundance in red-feshed lines is the reason for substrate lycopene accumulation (Zhang et al. 2020). Orange watermelons, such as ‘Orangeglo,’ have a carotenoid profle resembling that of the CrtISO gene mutation in tomato (Lewinsohn et al. 2005).

Compared to other well-known enzymes in carotenogenesis, ZISO is one of the most recent components of the core carotenoid biosynthetic pathway (Li et al. 2007; Chen et al. 2010). The $^ { 9 , 1 5 , 9 ^ { \prime } }$ -tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene generated by PDS should be isomerized to 9,9’-di-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene which is reported to occur spontaneously and enhanced by exposure to light (Bartley et al. 1999). However, the downstream products in the carotenoid pathway can be detected in dark tissues such as the roots, fesh and calli, which suggests that there must be other components involved. With analysis of the maize $y 9$ mutant, a genetic locus required for isomerization of 9,15,9′-tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene to 9, $9 ^ { \prime }$ -tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene was found to encode a $\zeta$ -carotene isomerase (ZISO) gene (Janick-Buckner et al. 2001; Li et al. 2007). Then, the Arabidopsis zic1 mutant and sweet orange mutant ‘Pinalate’ were reported as accumulating the $\zeta$ -carotene due to the ZISO mutations (Chen et al. 2010; Rodrigo et al. 2019). ZISO was clarifed as a bona fde enzyme and integral membrane protein. It independently catalyzes the cis–trans isomerization of the $1 5 { - } 1 5 ^ { \prime }$ carbon–carbon double bond in 9,15,9′-cis- $\zeta$ - carotene (Beltrán et al. 2015). In the maize $y 9$ mutant, loss of function of ZISO showed a pale yellow endosperm and the conversion of $\zeta .$ -carotene to prolycopene in maize endosperm was interrupted. But almost all leaves in $y 9$ developed normal green, where the isomerase activity would be light complemented (Li et al. 2007).

Light is one of the most important environmental factors infuencing fruit pigmentation (Llorente et al. 2016). Lightdependent regulations of carotenoid biosynthesis have been reported in many studies (Harding and Shropshire 1980; Humbeck and Krupinska 1993; Pizarro and Stange 2009). The efects of light can be detected on carotenoid profle change, gene transcription regulation and enzymatic activation levels (Von Lintig et al. 1997; Welsch et al. 2000). It has been reported that light activates carotenoid accumulation by direct induction of PSY transcription in tomato and sweet orange (Schofeld and Paliyath 2005; Lado et al. 2019). As important enzymes in carotenogenesis, CRTISO and ZISO activities can be partially compensated by photoisomerization (Park et al. 2002; Chai et al. 2010; Rodrigo et al. 2019). In tomato CRTISO mutant, yellow color could be detected in newly developed leaves and inhibition of lutein biosynthesis could be compensated by light treatment (Isaacson et al. 2002). Light growing conditions can afect carotenoid profle in the sweet orange ZISO mutant (Rodrigo et al. 2019).

In this study, we isolated a novel photosensitive fesh color mutant ‘psf’ from a red-feshed EMS-mutagenized watermelon population. The fesh color of this psf mutant can change with light treatment. This special photosensitive fesh was not previously reported in watermelon. The nonsense mutation of ClZISO was isolated in psf, which generates a non-functional truncated $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ protein in the chromoplast. We clarifed that ClZISO isomerization function to catalyze the 15–15’ cis to trans $\mathrm { C } = \mathrm { C }$ could be partially compensated by photoisomerization. We also detected that the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway was altered, and early upstream carotenes were accumulated in psf. All of these results revealed the crucial role of ClZISO in watermelon carotenogenesis and normal fesh color development.

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

The red-feshed cultivated watermelon line $\cdot 3 0 2 ^ { \cdot }$ (C. lanatus) was used to generate the mutant library. Seeds of 302 were treated with the mutagen $1 . 5 \%$ EMS (ethyl methanesulfonate) for $^ \mathrm { 1 0 h }$ in attempt to induce mutations in watermelon. M1 plants originating from the treated seeds were self-pollinated to produce the M2 generation. Fifteen plants of each of 1012 M2 families were grown in the feld and visually screened. Among these M2 families, one family had an original yellow-feshed color. Plants carrying such fruit were self-pollinated, and their progeny were stabilized for this mutation, which was named the photosensitive fesh (‘psf’) mutant. A cross between 302 (wild type) and the psf mutant was constructed. F1 and F2 populations were generated by controlled pollinations in the greenhouse. Briefy,

12 F1 plants were self-pollinated to obtain F2 seeds. Three hundred and seventy-one F2 individuals were genotyped. The cultivated yellow-feshed line JLM was obtained from the China National Engineering Research Center for Vegetables (NERCV), Beijing, China (Zhang et al. 2020). Seeds of each watermelon line were sown at the same time and grown under natural sunlight under conventional conditions in the feld and in the greenhouse. Surface-sterilized watermelon seeds were sown on basic MS solid medium supplemented with $3 \%$ sucrose for 3 days. Then, cotyledons without embryo were cut into $1 . 5 9 1 . 5 \mathrm { - m m }$ pieces and put on the above medium containing $1 . 5 \mathrm { m g } / 1$ 6-BA to produce cotyledon calli. All fruits for evaluation were harvested at full maturity and cut longitudinally, photographed and categorized into diferent fesh color groups.

# Carotenoid and chlorophyll analysis

Carotenoids were extracted from $5 \mathrm { \textrm { g } }$ of watermelon fesh and fower. Samples were freeze-dried, and then, the dried samples were homogenized and powdered. Fifty milligrams of dried powder was extracted with a hexane/acetone/ ethanol (50:25:25, v/v/v) mixture, and $\mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ [13C10]- $\beta$ - carotene was added as an internal standard to calibrate and eliminate errors. The sample extracts were analyzed using an UPLC–APCI–MS/MS system (UPLC ExionLC AD, https://sciex.com/cr/products/hplc-products/exionlc, Applied Biosystems 6500 Triple Quadrupole, https://sclex.com.cn/ ). The analytical conditions were as follows: LC: column, YMC C30 $3 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 2 . 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ i.d.); solvent system, methanol/acetonitrile (1:3, v/v) with $0 . 0 1 \%$ BHT and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (A); methyl tertbutyl ether with $0 . 0 1 \%$ BHT (B); gradient program: started at $0 \%$ B (0–3 min), increased to $7 0 \%$ B (3–5 min), then increased to $9 5 \%$ B (5–9 min) and fnally ramped back to $0 \%$ B (11–12 min); mobile-phase fow rate: $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ ; temperature: $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; and injection volume: $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . An API6500 + QTRAP LC/MS/MS system, equipped with an APCI Turbolon-Spray interface, was used to verify the compounds. Multiple-reaction monitoring (MRM) and Analyst software 1.6.3 were used for quantifcation. Quantifcation of the relative concentration of individual carotenoids was achieved by comparison of the individual peak areas with a calibration curve constructed from authentic standards (15-cis-phytoene, 15,9’-tri-cis-phytofuene and $\alpha$ -carotene from BOCSCI Inc., lycopene, $\beta$ -carotene, lutein, zeaxanthin, violaxanthin and neoxanthin standards from Sigma–Aldrich). Identifcation of $\zeta$ -carotene isomers without authentic standards was performed using a Nexera highperformance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system (Shimadzu) in accordance with the method described by Li et al. (2007). $\zeta$ -Carotene isomers standards were produced with expression of pAC-ZETA (Sandmann 1991; Li et al. 2007). Photosynthetic pigments were extracted by homogenizing of

$1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of fresh leaf tissue in $8 0 \%$ acetone solution $( 1 0 \mathrm { m L } )$ ). The samples were stored at $4 ~ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for night and then centrifuged at ${ 3 , 0 0 0 } \mathrm { g }$ for 20 min. The supernatants were measured by spectrophotometry at 452.5, 645 and $6 6 3 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ for carotenoid, chlorophyll a and chlorophyll b, respectively, following the method described by Arnon (1949).

# Light treatments

Mature fruits at 34 d after pollination (DAP) of 302, psf mutant and the cultivated yellow-feshed line JLM were crosscutted and put under intense sunlight of summer. The average sunlight intensity reached $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ Lux. For the longterm LED light treatment, 34 DAP fruits from 302, psf mutant and JLM were treated under white LED lamps placed at $8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a plant incubator. The white LED lights were placed in the blackbody track and presented daylight spectral characteristics with an intensity of $2 9 3 9 . 7 \pm 1 5 5 . 6 \mathrm { \ l x }$ . Its maximum emission overlapped with the spectra of the red, green and blue light at 430–460 and 500–640 nm. The illumination intensity of the LED lamps was monitored by a portable light meter (TES-1339R Data Logger, TES Electrical Electronic Corp., Taipei, China).

# Bulked segregant analysis of the psf locus

The 371 plants of the F2 mapping population from the cross between 302 and psf were used to clarify the yellow fesh controlling gene. The fesh color phenotypes of the F2 population were investigated at 34 DAP. The CTAB method was used for genomic DNA extraction from each F2 watermelon. In this F2 population, 32 yellow-feshed lines and 34 redfeshed lines were selected to generate two bulked progeny groups. Two bulked DNA samples were prepared by mixing an equal ratio of DNA extracted from individual lines.

Equal amounts of DNA were bulked and subjected to 150-bp paired-end sequencing on an Illumina X-10 instrument (https://en.novogene.com). Approximately 10 Gb of high-quality sequence, corresponding to $\sim 1 5 \times$ coverage of the whole genome, was obtained for each bulked $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population. The data from the two $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ populations were frst analyzed separately using our highly efcient pipeline, EMS mutation, in the CyVerse Discovery App (Merchant et al., 2016). Only EMS-induced GC to AT single-nucleotide changes supported by 5–100 reads were retained for downstream fltering. Based on the high accuracy of phenotyping, the SNP ratio (number of reads supporting the mutation allele/number of all reads mapped to the loci) cut-of was set to 1. SNPs with large efects on genes (missense, nonsense, splice_site_acceptor, splice_site_donor) predicted by SNPef (Cingolani et al., 2012) were retained as candidate causal mutations. The candidate mutations

from two bulked DNA samples were then compared to determine the overlap.

# Quantitative RT‑PCR (qRT‑PCR) analysis

Total RNA was extracted using the Quick RNA isolation kit (Huayueyang Biotechnologies Co., Ltd). cDNA was synthesized from 1 mg of the total RNA using SuperScript III transcriptase (Invitrogen). Each total RNA was reversetranscribed with an anchor-tagged switching template and oligo-dT primer by using PowerScript reverse transcriptase to prepare the frst cDNA strand mixture. Then, a PCR assay was carried out to amplify the cDNA fragments for ClZISO with degenerate primers. For quantitative RT-PCR, SYBR Green I was added to the reaction system and run on a Roche 480 according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Three repeats were carried out for each gene. The watermelon actin gene was used as an internal control in the analysis. The primer sequences used for the above studies are listed in Table S7 online.

# Functional analysis of watermelon ZISO in E. coli

Functional analysis of ClZISO and $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ in E. coli was followed as previously described (Beltrán et al. 2015) with slight modifcations. The full-length cDNA of ClZISO and $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ was isolated from fruit cDNA libraries by using the primers ZISO-F, ZISO-R and $\mathrm { Z I S O } _ { \mathrm { m u } ^ { - } } \mathrm { R }$ . The verifed PCR products were cloned into the expression vector pET30a, and the resulting plasmids were pET30a-ClZISO and $\mathrm { p E T } 3 0 \mathrm { a } - C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ . The plasmid pAC-ZETA (Cunningham et al. 1994) carrying bacterial crtE, crtB and crtP genes for $\zeta .$ -carotene formation was kindly provided by Dr. Li (University of Cornell). Plasmid pAC-ZETA was transformed or cotransformed with plasmid pET30a-ClZISO into $E$ . coli BL21. The double transformants were plated onto LB agar supplemented with ampicillin $( 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m l } )$ ) and chloramphenicol $( 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m l } )$ . A 50-ml culture of $E$ . coli double transformant was grown in LB media at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $\mathrm { O D } 6 0 0 = 0 . 8$ . Protein expression was induced with the addition of isopropyl- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \beta }$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) to the growth media to a fnal concentration of $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ . The bacterial culture was grown at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for an additional $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ under dark- or light-grown conditions. The $E .$ . coli cells of diferent color development were harvested by centrifugation, and the pellet was extracted in acetone until loss of color. Analysis of the reaction products was performed using a Nexera high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system (Shimadzu) in accordance with the method described by Li et al. (2007).

# Subcellular localization analysis

To determine the subcellular localization of ClZISO proteins, ClZISO and $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ were inserted into the pYBA1138-mCherry vector with mCherry at the C-terminus, driven by the constitutive 35S promoter. The sequences were PCR amplifed using primers ClZISO-BamH1/Xho1 and $\mathrm { C l Z I S O } _ { \mathrm { m u } }$ -BamH1/Xho1 listed in Table S7. The PCR fragments were inserted in to BamHI and XhoI sites of pYBA1138. The final constructs were named ClZISOmCherry and $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ -mCherry. Plasmids were purifed and transformed into watermelon fruit protoplasts according to a previously reported method (Yoo et al. 2007). The mCherry fuorescence and chromoplast autofuorescence were analyzed using an LSM700 microscope at wavelengths of 587 and $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ for excitation and 610 and $5 0 9 \mathrm { n m }$ for emission, respectively.

# Comparative transcriptome analysis

Fruit fesh from 302 and psf mutants were collected at four stages (10 DAP, 18 DAP, 26 DAP and 34 DAP). Three fruits from each line were bulked to construct each biological repeat. Three biological replicates were sampled. Total RNA extraction and RNA-seq library construction and sequencing were performed according to the methods of Guo et al. 2015. Briefy, the quality and quantity of total RNA were assessed via a NanoDrop2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientifc Inc., USA), and RNA integrity was assessed on a $1 . 5 \%$ agarose gel. Strand-specifc RNA-Seq libraries were prepared and sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 2000 system using single-end, 100-bp mode by Novogene Corporation (Beijing, China). The data analysis was performed according to the method of Zhang et al. (2017) with modifcations. The watermelon 97103 v2 reference genome was used for annotation (Guo et al. 2013 and 2019), and DEGs were defned as genes with a false discovery rate $( \mathrm { F D R } ) { < } 0 . 0 1$ and an average fragments per kilobase of exon per million mapped fragments (FPKM) fold change larger than 2. GO enrichment and GO slim analysis of DEG subsets were analyzed using the Gene Ontology and pathway modules of Cucurbit Genomics Database (CuGenDB), and adjusted p values were less than 0.05.

# Free ABA quantifcation

Determination of ABA was conducted by the ESI–HPLC–MS system in the mass spectrometry (MS) laboratory of the College of Biological Sciences, China Agricultural University. The retention time and mass spectrometry information of ABA were determined with standard substance ( $( \pm )$ -abscisic acid, A1049, Sigma, St Louis, MO, USA). Free ABA was measured following the method

by Wang et al. (2017). Each sample had three biological replicates.

# Results

# Identifcation of an EMS‑mutagenized yellow‑feshed mutant

We used the chemical mutagen ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) to create new genetic variations in watermelon. A distinct yellow-feshed mutant was identifed in the M2 population derived from the EMS-mutagenized red-feshed cultivar ‘302.’ This EMS-induced yellow-feshed mutant was self-pollinated, and their progeny were stabilized for the fesh color mutation. Compared with red-feshed wildtype 302 (WT), the preliminary fesh color of this mutant (mu) was yellow, as shown in Fig. 1a. We detected the carotenoid composition content in these two fresh-cut fruits and found that some upstream products in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathways, such 15-cis-phytoene, 9,15,9’-tri-cis-$\zeta .$ -carotene and 9,9’-di-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene, accumulated in the mutant (Table S1). But the total carotenoids content was decreased in the mutant compared with the wild type. In addition to fruit fesh, the coloration of seedlings, fowers, hypocotyls and calli were also infuenced in this mutant. The hypocotyl of the WT seedlings turned green quickly after sprouting, while in the mutant, this process was delayed (Fig. 1b). However, no obvious diference was detected in mature leaf chlorophyll content in mu compared with WT (Table S1). Compared with the bright yellow flower in

WT, mu had a light yellow fower, especially the stamen, as shown in Fig. 1c. We detected higher levels of phytoene and $\gamma$ -carotene in mu, but higher levels of lutein and violaxanthin and antheraxanthin in the WT fowers (Table S1). Under dark conditions, the cotyledon callus color of mu was lighter than that of the WT (Fig. 1d).

# Light‑induced photosensitive efect was detected in the fesh

The primary fesh color of this mutant was yellow. However, when fresh-cut fruit was placed under direct and hot sunlight, the original yellow fesh faded quickly. Its phenotype was not reported in watermelon before. To clarify this phenotype, we exposed the fruits of the mu mutant and a traditional bright yellow-feshed JLM, together with wildtype red-feshed 302 to sunlight. Fresh cross sections of watermelon fruits were put under intense sunlight treatment $( 1 0 ^ { 5 } 1 \mathbf { x } )$ , half of which were kept in dark (covered with tin foil). As shown in Fig. 2a, the fesh color of mu turned white within $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ , but the fesh color of 302 and JLM was not infuenced.

To dissect the function of light treatment on fesh color formation, we used controlled LED light in a plant incubator for analysis. The cross sections of watermelon fruits were placed at $8 \mathrm { ^ \circ C }$ under LED $( 3 0 0 0 \pm 1 5 0 \ \mathrm { L u x } )$ light, half of which were kept in the dark. Surprisingly, a light pinkish fesh color emerged in mu with time extension. As shown in Fig. 2b, the light treatment turned the fesh color from yellow to pale yellow within $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and then, the pink color appeared slowly. # Inheritance and location of the psf locus

To study the inheritance and identify the candidate psf controlling gene, fesh color segregation populations were derived from a cross between red-feshed 302 and psf. All fruits of the F1 generation had red fesh, and the total of 371 plants of the F2 population segregated into 278 plants with red fesh and 93 plants with yellow fesh. # Chromoplast‑located $\mathbf { C l Z l S O } _ { \mathbf { m u } }$ lost catalytic function which can be partially compensated by photoisomerization

To clarify the $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ function in psf fesh, we frst analyzed the expression pattern of the ClZISO and $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ in the WT and psf. The gene expression levels of ClZISO and $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ were analyzed by qRT-PCR. The results showed that ClZISO was ubiquitously expressed in the examined watermelon organs, including roots, stems, leaves, fowers and fruits, in both the WT and psf mutant (Fig. 4a). Increased ClZISO expression was detected in the WT fesh with fruit development, while the expression of $C l Z I S O _ { m u }$ remained relatively stable in the psf mutant. Then, we examined the subcellular localization of ClZISO and $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ proteins in watermelon fruit protoplasts. ClZISO-mCherry fusion proteins and free mCherry vectors (controls) were transiently transformed in watermelon fesh protoplasts. The fuorescence signals of the two mCherry-fused proteins precisely coincided with the chromoplast autofuorescence (green), in contrast to the ubiquitous distribution of free mCherry (Fig. 4b). These results imply that ClZISO was located in the chromoplast, and the truncated mutation $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ in psf did not infuence the subcellular localization.

It had been reported that ZISO can catalyze the cis–trans isomerization of central double bond (15–15’) of the 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene to 9,9’-di-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene in E. coli (Chen et al. 2010; Beltrán et al. 2015; Rodrigo et al. 2019). To detect the catalytic function of ClZISO and $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ and distinguish the $\zeta \cdot$ -carotene isomers appearing in psf fesh tissues, $\zeta$ -carotene isomers were extracted from E. coli cells containing pAC-ZETA for use as HPLC standards (Cunningham et al. 1994). This pAC-ZETA plastid carries bacterial crtE, crtB and CrtP and can produce $\zeta$ -carotene. Meanwhile, we constructed the pET30a-ClZISO and pET30a-ClZISOmu plasmids to express these two ClZISO proteins in a substrate-producing E. coli strain expressing the pAC-ZETA plastid under dark-grown conditions. We used HPLC to analyze carotenoids extracted from the bacterial cells under dark- or light-grown conditions to clarify the products. Following HPLC conditions reported earlier (Isaacson et al. 2004), we were able to replicate separation and spectral identifcation of the tri-cis and di-cis $\zeta$ -carotene isomers. It has been previously shown that the expression of a plant PDS resulted in the accumulation of 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene, which can be photoisomerized to 9,9’-di-cis- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene

(Bartley et al. 1999; Isaacson et al. 2004; Breitenbach and Sandmann 2005). As shown in Fig. 4c, the strains containing vector control/pAC-ZETA grown in the dark contained two main peaks, whose retention time and absorbance spectrum corresponded to 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene in peak 1 and 9,9’-di-cis- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene in peak 2, which was consistent with earlier reports. Under light treatment, with an obvious increase in peak 2, the ratio of peaks 1 to 2 changed from 1:1 (Fig. 4c) to almost 1:3 (Fig. 4d). This result showed that light treatment can catalyze the isomerization of 9,15,9′-tricis- $\zeta$ -carotene to $^ { 9 , 9 ^ { \prime } }$ -tri-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene. In Fig.  4e, the ratio of peaks 1 to 2 changed from 1:1 (Fig. 4c) to almost 1:4 in extracts from pET30a-ClZISO/pAC-ZETA cells, which showed that mainly 9,9’-di-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene accumulated. The HPLC peaks of pET30a-ClZISO /pAC-ZETA cells in Fig. 4f were similar to those in Fig. 4c, with the same amount of 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotenein peaks 1 and 9,9’-di-cis- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene in peaks 2. These results showed that ClZISO catalyzes the conversion of 9,15,9’-tri-cis-$\zeta$ -carotene to 9,9’-di-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway, and this conversion can also be induced by light treatment. $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ lost this enzymatic activity which was responsible for upstream substrate $^ { 9 , 1 5 , 9 ^ { \prime } }$ -tricis- $\zeta$ -carotene accumulation. Together with the downstream products accumulated in psf fesh after long-term light treatment (Fig. 2b), which was also reported in tomato and sweet orange as in vivo photoisomerization (Fantini et al. 2013; Rodrigo et al. 2019), these phenotypes strongly suggest that $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { m u } }$ loss of function in psf would be partially compensated by light treatment in vitro and in vivo.

# Discussion

The fesh color of watermelon is one of the most important quality traits, which is an indicator of nutritional benefts and important for consumer preference. Watermelon exhibits a wide range of fesh colors, such as white, salmon yellow, canary yellow, orange and red (Henderson et al. 1998). The varied fesh colors of watermelon are attributed to diferent carotenoid compositions and concentrations (Bang et al. 2010). Considering the nutritional and health-related properties of carotenoids.research on carotenoid metabolism in watermelon fruit has been an active feld (Edwards et al. 2003; Li and Yuan 2013). Through EMS mutagenesis, we created a new type of photosensitive fesh watermelon psf which accumulates $\zeta .$ -carotene based on the mutation of the 15-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene isomerase gene—ClZISO. The psf mutant was frst regarded as a yellow-feshed line, but the initial yellow color changed with the light treatments. This distinct photosensitive fesh phenotype draws our attention.

Signifcant progress has been achieved in our understanding of carotenoid biosynthesis in plants (Cazzonelli and Pogson 2010; Nisar et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2015). Our understanding of carotenoid metabolism, regulation and roles of carotenoid derivatives is still developing. ZISO was newly found to be an integral membrane isomerase that responds to the redox state in performing a key step in carotenoid biosynthesis (Beltrán et  al. 2015). ZISO gene mutation alters the carotenoid content and composition in fruit and leads to upstream $\zeta$ -carotenes accumulation, while the content of downstream carotenoids is signifcantly decreased (Fantini et al. 2013; Rodrigo et al. 2019). Silencing ZISO in tomato caused the accumulation of phytoene, phytofuene and $\zeta$ -carotene. The ratio of 9,15,9′-tri-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene to $^ { 9 , 9 ^ { \prime } }$ -di-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene is approximately 1:2 in mature fruits (Fantini et al. 2013). ZISO-mutated sweet orange contained more upstream carotenes. Phytoene was the predominant carotenoid representing more than $5 0 \%$ of the total content, followed by phytofuene and $\zeta$ -carotene $\mathrm { 1 . 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / g } }$ of 9,15,9’tri-cis and $3 . 7 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of 9,9’di-cis isomers) which were

barely detectable in the wild-type fruit (Rodrigo et al. 2019). In psf, we also found the accumulation of phytoene, but we did not detect the phytofuene. Meanwhile, the $^ { 9 , 1 5 , 9 ^ { \prime } }$ -tricis- $\zeta$ -carotene to 9,9′-di-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene is approximately 1:2 in mature watermelon fruits, which is consistent with the previous report in other fruits. Mutants accumulating prolycopene have been reported in watermelon fruits (Lewinsohn et al. 2005); however, this is the frst report of the accumulation of $\zeta$ -carotene in watermelon fruits.

It has been reported that light treatment can partially compensate for the absence of the ZISO gene to mediate this 15–15’ cis to trans $\mathrm { C } { = } \mathrm { C }$ isomerization reaction as photoisomerization function (Li et al. 2007; Chen et al. 2010; Giuliano et al. 2002). We showed that the $^ { 9 , 1 5 , 9 }$ -tri-cis-$\zeta$ -carotene isomer accumulates in the psf mutant flesh. We demonstrate that ClZISO catalyzes the conversion of 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene to 9,9’-di-cis- $\cdot \zeta$ -carotene and this isomerase activity can be partially compensated by light via photoisomerization. When expressed with pAC-ZETA (Escherichia coli expressing bacterial crtE, crtB and crtP), ClZISO results in the isomerization of the 15-cis bond of phytoene, which was also identifed by exposure of ‘pAC-ZETA’ bacterial extracts to light. These results suggest that it is an isomerase that can be replaced by light in vitro. The fesh of psf fruit directly exposed to light developed light-red pigmentation in which lycopene increased, which suggests that light treatment can compensate the loss of function of $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { m u } }$ in vivo. However, this process required more than $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , which indicated that the product accumulation progress required more time than photobleaching. In addition to the fruit fesh, endosperm and other tissues not exposed to light, the colors were thinner in psf, which indicates that the biosynthesis of carotenoids is dependent on ClZISO. Without ClZISO, carotenoid biosynthesis is blocked. However, in photosynthetic tissues, the diference between psf and WT was not obvious. All these fndings mean that light could mediate the nonenzymatic photoisomerization of phytoene and produce downstream products for plant development.

In addition to imparting color, carotenoids play essential roles in photosynthesis and photoprotection (Domonkos et al. 2013; Hashimoto et al. 2016). Light is required for photosynthesis, but excessive light may cause the inactivation of photosynthesis. The main function of carotenoids in plants is protection against high light stress and reactive oxygen species (Gruszecki et al. 2005). Because carotenoids are exposed to strong light in the photosynthetic apparatus, they face the risk of being photobleached at high light intensities (Andreeva et al. 2007). However, the photobleaching phenotype is difcult to detect in fresh-cut fruit. The main carotenoids contained in fruit fesh, such as lycopene, $\beta$ -carotene, violaxanthin and neoxanthin, are more stable, and bleaching of their molecules can be negligible (Andreeva et al. 2007). The photodegradation of lycopene, $\beta$ -carotene and

$\alpha$ -carotene carotenoids followed frst-order kinetics with half-lives ranging from 2 to 11 days (Mortensen and Skibsted 1999). The quick photobleaching found in psf may be due to the high photosensitivity of the upstream products in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway which was undetected in our research, or some unknown mechanism infuenced the carotenoids photodegradation which needs further research.

The truncated $\mathrm { C l Z I S O _ { \mathrm { m u } } }$ in psf lost its catalytic function, and total carotenoids were substantially reduced in the fruit. Meanwhile, we detected generalized reduced transcription of most genes involved in the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway in psf. For example, a dramatic increase in the transcript levels of PSY with fruit development was detected in wild-type 302. In parental fruit tissues, this gene expression upregulation was smoothed in psf. CHYB gene expression was also blocked in psf. Carotenoid biosynthesis is modulated by developmental, phytohormone, highlight, salt, drought, temperature, photoperiod and epigenetic mechanisms, including pre- and posttranscriptional feedback regulation (Cazzonelli and Pogson 2010). This regulation could be explained by the fact that fewer downstream products, such as ABA, a carotenoid derivative, may be implicated in transcriptional regulation of biosynthesis gene expression.

Carotenoids are regarded as the precursors of phytohormones abscisic acid (ABA) strigolactones and other signaling molecules (Enfss et al. 2017). Mutation in ClZISO in psf mutates leads to a block in the carotenoid pathway and decreases the ABA content in the fruit. In the previous study, ABA was found to be an important factor that promotes watermelon fruit development and ripening (Wang et al. 2017). Watermelon, as a non-climacteric fruit, together with cucumber, citrus, grape, and sweet cherry, showed dramatically increased ABA content in the mature fruit, with the peak level reached at the onset of ripening (Wang et al. 2017). However, in psf, ABA accumulation was not as dramatic as that in WT. Therefore, the ABA content in psf might be enough to support fruit development.

Overall, we identifed and created a new type of photosensitive fesh watermelon, which accumulates $\zeta$ -carotene based on the mutation of the 15-cis- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene isomerase gene—ClZISO. The fesh color of this mutant can change under light treatment. As has been shown before for ZISO enzymes from other plants, we demonstrate that ClZISO catalyzes the conversion of 9,15,9’-tri-cis- $\boldsymbol { \zeta }$ -carotene to $^ { 9 , 9 }$ -di-cis- $\zeta$ -carotene and that this isomerase activity can be partially compensated by light treatment.
