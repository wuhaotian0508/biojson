# Summary

A complete and genetically stable male sterile line with high outcrossing rate is a prerequisite for the development of commercial hybrid soybean. It was reported in the last century that the soybean male sterile ms2 mutant has the highest record with seed set. Here we report the cloning and characterization of the MS2 gene in soybean, which encodes a protein that is specifically expressed in the anther. MS2 functions in the tapetum and microspore by directly regulating genes involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites and the lipid metabolism, which is essential for the formation of microspore cell wall. Through comparison of the field performance with the widely used male sterile mutants in the same genetic background, we demonstrated that the ms2 mutant conducts the best in outcrossing rate and makes it an ideal tool in building a cost-effective hybrid system for soybean. 

# Introduction

Soybean (Glycine max) serves as a major source of protein and oilseed worldwide; its production urgently needs to be increased to feed the growing world population with limited agricultural land (Ainsworth et al., 2012; Hickey et al., 2019). The ‘Green Revolution’ contributed greatly to a dramatic increase in the production of food grains, especially in rice and wheat (Hickey et al., 2019). However, little improvements have been made for soybean over the past few decades (Liu et al., 2020a). The big success for rice and wheat was mainly due to the adoption of semi-dwarf varieties, as dwarfism enhances lodging resistance and allows increased planting density (Peng et al., 1999; Pingali, 2012). Soybean yield is based on both node number and the number of pods per node; therefore, the yield increase cannot be achieved in soybean by simply reduplicating the principles from rice and wheat (Liu et al., 2020a). 

Hybrid breeding has proven to be a practical strategy in increasing crop yield (Kim and Zhang, 2018; Perez-Prat and Van Lookeren Campagne, 2002; Tester and Langridge, 2010). An earlier comprehensive heterosis test program demonstrated that significant yield increase could be achieved in soybean; the heterozygous $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants yielded $20 \%$ more than their parental lines (Palmer et al., 2001; Sun et al., 1999). Unfortunately, the hybrid autogamous soybean for commercialization is still a limited sector compared with autogamous rice and wheat. One of the reasons is the low seed set on male sterile plants resulting in high effort and low reward in hybrid seed production (Palmer et al., 2001). The soybean ms1 mutant was the first reported genic male sterile mutant and has been widely used to generate 

recurrent selection populations (Lewers and Palmer, 1997), and the development of the female gametophyte was also severely affected in the mutant leading to reduced female fertility (Cutter and Bingham, 1977; Kennell and Horner, 1985; Oliver Chen et al., 1985). The rapid progress in the identification and characterization of soybean male fertility genes has been made in recent with the efforts in generating high-quality de novoassembled soybean genomes (Chan et al., 2012; Li et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020b). To date, soybean ms4 (Thu et al., 2019), ms1 (Fang et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2021; Nadeem et al., 2021), ms6 (Yu et al., 2021) and ms3 (Hou et al., 2022) have been cloned in succession. Mutations affecting male function but leaving female reproductive capacity unaffected will contribute significantly to hybrid soybean. 

The soybean ms2 mutant arose spontaneously in a breeding population in 1971 (Graybosch et al., 1984). It has been recorded that the female fertility of the ms2 mutant was nearly as high in male-sterile plants as in male-fertile plants (Carter et al., 1983; Ortiz-Perez et al., 2008). In this study, we cloned the MS2 gene by bulked segregant analysis (BSA) combining with traditional positional cloning approach. MS2 encodes a bHLH transcriptional factor, and both the gene and protein express exclusively in the anther. Integrated RNA-seq and ChIP-seq data reveal that MS2 initiates the formation of microspore cell wall by directly regulating biosynthesis of secondary metabolites and the lipid metabolism. Comparison of outcrossing rate with ms1, ms2 and ms6 mutant in the same genetic background showed that the ms2 mutant has the highest average seed setting rate, making it a perfect tool in hybrid soybean seed production. The cloned MS2 not only elucidated mechanisms of male sterility but also 

demonstrated its potential application in hybrid breeding of lowyield crops. 

# Results

# Completely male sterility of ms2 mutant

To determine the candidate gene of the male sterile locus ms2, we constructed a ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ population, which was developed by crossing the female parent ms2 mutant with the male parent Jilin16 (JL16) and then backcrossing the male sterile progeny five times with JL16 to develop a secondary $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ $( \mathsf { B C } _ { 5 } \mathsf { F } _ { 2 } )$ population (Figure S1a). The fertile and sterile lines originated from the same ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 1 }$ were regarded as near-isogenic lines (NILs), named as NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16, respectively. No obvious differences in vegetative growth characters, such as plant height, mainstem node number and branch number, were observed between NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16 (Figure S2). However, the NIL-ms2/JL16 plant stayed green with undeveloped tiny pods when compared with the NIL-MS2/JL16 plant-bearing seeds at maturity stage (Figure 1a,b). 

Gross anther morphology appeared normal except that the NIL-ms2/JL16 produced pale white stamen rather than 

the NIL-MS2/JL16 line with stamen appeared yellow in colour (Figure 1c,d). The male fertile and male sterile plants could be distinguished before pollination, in contrast with the male fertile plant, male sterile plant fails to produce pollen grains (Figure 1e, f). To figure out the effects of ms2 mutant on anther development, a cross-sectional analysis with light microscope observation was performed. No difference in stamen development was observed up to the uninuclear stage of microsporogenesis between NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16. During anther development from stages 9 to 12 (stage is defined according to the anther development in Arabidopsis) (Ma, 2005; Sanders et al., 1999), the tapetum underwent programmed cell death and microspore cell wall formed in fertile anther, the microspores release from the tetrad and to begin the post-meiotic development (Figure 1g). # MS2 was delimited to a 19-kb region

The ms2 locus was formerly mapped to the molecular linkage group O defined by the molecular markers Sat_190 and Scaa001, with a distance of 8.5 and 16.2 cM, respectively (Cervantes-Martinez et al., 2007). In our study, we first mapped the ms2 locus to a 3.9 Mb genomic region on chromosome 10 with bulk segregant analysis sequencing (BSA-seq) using male 

fertile and sterile plants segregated from the progeny of heterozygous NIL-MS2/JL16 plants (Figure 2a and Figure S1b), which covered the region of the ms2 locus previously reported (Cervantes-Martinez et al., 2007). Then, we have set out to fine map the gene responsible for male sterility. Eventually, we located ms2 to a 19-kb interval between markers M3 and M5 using 389 individuals from the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ segregating populations derived from ms2 and Jilin16 (Figure 2b and Table S1).

# Confirmation of MS2 identity by CRISPR/Cas9 technology

To confirm that the male-sterile phenotype was caused by Glyma.10G281800 mutation, we mutated Glyma.10G281800 by CRISPR/Cas9 (clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats/CRISPR-associated protein 9)-mediated genome-editing technique in Williams 82 (Wm82) (Figure 3a). Two null ms2 alleles were obtained, named ms2cr-1 and ms2cr-2 with a 2 bp deletion and 1 bp insertion on exon 4 and 5, respectively (Figure 3b). Both these two mutations cause a frameshift and lead to premature termination of translation ahead of the bHLH domain (Figure 3c and Figure S3). The expression level of MS2 significantly decreased in the two mutants (Figure S4c). Both the two mutants did not produce any pollen grains and only have undeveloped tiny pods, which were quite similar to that of ms2 phenotypes (Figure 3d,e). These results confirmed that the gene Glyma.10G281800 does indeed cause the male-sterile phenotype in ms2 mutant. A recent study also demonstrated that the 

Glyma.10G281800 knocking out mutant display the similar phenotypes as of the ms2 mutant in terms of male fertility (Chen et al., 2021). 

Molecular evolutionary analysis revealed that Glyma.10G281800 and its close paralog Glyma.20G107500 are two homologues of Arabidopsis AMS (ABORTED MICROSPORES) gene (Sorensen et al., 2003) (Figure S5), and the amino acid similarity between Glyma.10G281800 and Arabidopsis AMS is about $70 \%$ . To distinguish these two genes in soybean, Glyma.10G281800 and Glyma.20G107500 were named MS2/ GmAMS1 and GmAMS2 hereafter, respectively. 

GmAMS2 is preferentially expressed in anther (Figure S6) and shares $7 8 . 3 \%$ amino acid identity compared with MS2/GmAMS1, whether it participate in regulating soybean male fertility need to be explored. Here, we constructed two Gmams2 knockout mutants with CRISPR/Cas9 technique (Figure S7). Both of the two independent mutants showed no visible effect on male fertility and could produce seeds (Figure S7). These results indicated that GmAMS2 could have evolved new functions in soybean development that need to be further explored other than the functions in controlling the male fertility.

# MS2 functions in the initiation and development of microspore walls in soybean

To reveal the role of MS2 in soybean anther development, we first carried out RNA-seq analysis with anthers from NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16 plants. A total of 8042 genes were identified as differentially expressed genes (DEGs), with 4979 up-regulated and 3063 down-regulated (Figure S8a,b and Table S3). KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) analysis revealed 

that metabolism and especially lipid metabolism pathway were altered in NIL-ms2/JL16 plants, mainly in fatty acid biosynthesis and elongation and cutin, suberin and wax biosynthesis (Figure S8). To thoroughly explore genes under the transcriptional control of MS2, we performed a genome-wide ChIP-seq assay with the customized anti-MS2 monoclonal antibody. The potential targets were mainly composed of genes in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites and lipid metabolism (ether lipid metabolism), which is highly consistent with those identified from the RNA-seq (Figure S9 and Table S4). 

To integrate the ChIP-seq and RNA-seq data analysis, the 611 up-regulated and 279 down-regulated genes were identified as most likely directly regulated by MS2 protein (Figure 5a). The binding ability was also supported by using the promoter fragment containing the Gbox element from one of the target genes (Figure S10). # Discussion

So far, an economical way of producing $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid seed either for research or commercial use is still a major challenge in soybean breeding (Chandra et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019). It is becoming increasingly apparent that outcrossing rate is one of the key components for successful development of commercial hybrid 

soybean. Our study together with previous field evaluation data provided strong evidence for researchers and breeders that the ms2 mutation could give the highest average seed setting rate for male sterile plants among all the characterized male sterile mutants in soybean at least up till now (Ortiz-Perez et al., 2008). The reason for ms2 mutation becoming the best choice for generation male sterile lines for hybrid soybean is its exclusive expression pattern in anther, making the mutation only affect the male fertility and without any effect on female productivity and other growth habits. All kinds of efforts have been made in building a cost-effective pollination control system for soybean in the past few decades, including looking for high-efficient pollination insect vectors and soybean flowers that are more attractive to pollination insects (Beard and Knowles, 1971; Garibaldi et al., 2021; Koelling et al., 1981; Palmer et al., 1983). The identification of MS2 will contribute greatly to expedite the development and application of biotechnologybased male-sterility (BMS) systems for hybrid soybean breeding (Wan et al., 2019, 2021). 

The limited information on anther and pollen development in soybean largely restricted their application in hybrid breeding and agricultural improvement (Gomez et al., 2015; Rutley and Twell, 2015). Although some conserved developmental pathways or gene networks are observed in anther and pollen development between monocot model rice and dicot model Arabidopsis, it could not simply translate this knowledge to soybean, as an ancient tetraploid, which underwent two whole genome duplications and more than $7 5 \%$ genes are paralogous genes with multiple copies (Hackenberg and Twell, 2019; Liu et al., 2020b). Without functional characterization, it is hard to determine the genes in regulating male fertility and figure out the ideal mutation that can lead to complete male sterile. From this perspective, the cloning and characterization of the natural complete male sterile mutants are still an efficient way to identify the male fertility genes. 

The soybean MS2/GmAMS1 seems to function more earlier in anther development than their homologues in Arabidopsis AMS and rice TDR (Tapetum Degeneration Retardation) (Li et al., 2006; Sorensen et al., 2003; Xu et al., 2010). The pollen degeneration occurs at tetrad stage and no microspores release from the soybean ms2 mutant; however, in Arabidopsis and rice the young microspores could release from the tetra and the degeneration occurs during the pollen development in the mutants (Li et al., 2006; Sorensen et al., 2003). Both the tapetum and microspore contribute to pollen wall formation, and we concluded that the MS2/GmAMS1 functions in both places based on our ChIP-seq data (Figure 6). The direct targets genes of MS2/ GmAMS1 perform their functions starting from the callose wall formation and involve in the sporopollenin biosynthesis and transport, tryphine formation and pollen exine and intine formation (Ariizumi and Toriyama, 2011; Shi et al., 2015a; Wan et al., 2020). Studies, mainly in the model plant Arabidopsis have demonstrated that homologues of the direct target genes play essential roles in anther and pollen development, e.g., cals5 (Callose synthase) or gsl5 (Glucan synthase-like) mutants displayed defect in callose deposition and exine wall formation; the ABC subfamily G (ABCG) transporters responsible for the transport of pollen wall component precursors (Choi et al., 2011; Dong et al., 2005; Enns et al., 2005; Shi et al., 2015b). Another interesting finding is that MS2 could directly bind to COI1 gene promoter (Figure S10), implying its function in the stamen filament, which is consistent with 

its expression pattern (Jewell and Browse, 2016). However, no obvious difference was observed in the filament in both the NIL-ms2/JL16 plants and the two null alleles generated by CRISPR/ Cas9 technology, one explanation may be that GmAMS2 play a redundant role in the filament development. 

In summary, we reported the cloning and characterization of a male fertility gene in soybean. Our work has made it feasible to increase the outcrossing rate at an economical level and speed up the commercialization of hybrid soybean. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Soybean cultivars Williams 82 (Wm82), Jilin 16 (JL16) and two near-isogenic lines (NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16); Arabidopsis thaliana wild type Columbia-0 (Col-0), ams mutant (SALK_152147) (Xu et al., 2010) and transgenic lines $( A t A M S _ { p r o }$ : AtAMS-6HA/ams and $A t A M S _ { p r o } . M S 2 - 6 H A / a m s )$ ; and tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) were used in this study. The Arabidopsis and tobacco were grown in growth chamber conditions with 16 h light/8 h dark and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The soybean was kept in a $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ incubator under long-day (14 h light/10 h dark) conditions or grown in the field in Guangzhou $\overline { { 2 3 ^ { \circ } 0 6 ^ { \prime } } }$ N, 113°150 E) or Changchun $\langle 4 3 ^ { \circ } 3 1 ^ { \prime } \mid$ N, $1 2 4 ^ { \circ } 4 9 ^ { \prime }$ E), China. 

# Outcrossing rate evaluation

To compare the outcrossing rate of soybean nuclear male sterile lines, three knockout mutants, crms1, crms2 and crms6, deriving from the same background (Wm82) were used. To minimize the environmental impact, the evaluation was conducted in the net house covered with nylon net (pore size: $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ ). The homozygote mutant seeds were identified through genotyping the mature seeds with the first generation of sequencing. Then, the seeds were planted and separated by one row of male parent in between; Wm82 was used as the male parent. The interplant spacing is $2 0 \ { \mathsf { c m } } \times 6 0 \ { \mathsf { c m } }$ (row distance). Three biological replicates were performed in three independent net houses. One honey bee hive with about 800 honey bees was placed in the net house for each test. This evaluation was conducted in the summer of 2022 in Changchun $( 4 3 ^ { \circ } 3 1 ^ { \prime } \Lambda$ , $1 2 4 ^ { \circ } 4 9 ^ { \prime }$ E), China. 

# BSA sequencing and data analysis

In the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ segregating population, 20 representative individuals were selected for constructing the fertile and sterile pools, respectively. The DNA from each individual plant was extracted according to the manufacturer’s instructions (CW0531M, CWBIO), and then, the same amount of DNA was mixed to generate the sequencing pool. The DNA was sheared by sonification, and the adaptors were added on both ends of each fragment for generating the sequencing library. Subsequently, we performed the whole genome re-sequencing of the male parent JL16 and two mixed pools though the Illumina X-Ten platform by Gene Denovo Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Guangzhou, China). The process of data analysis has been described (Fang et al., 2021). 

# Fine mapping of MS2

The ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ segregation population including 389 individuals were used for fine mapping of MS2 gene. The SNP and indel variations from BSA sequencing were used to design molecular markers. Twenty-six molecular markers were applied to identify the genotype first. Then, 66 fertile lines were selected for 

phenotypic confirmation in the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 3 }$ generation. Subsequently, six markers (Table S1) were used for recombinant screening and the MS2 locus was delimited to a 19-kb (between 50 289 and 50 270 kb, W82.a2.v1) region on the chromosome 10. The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# Knockout vector construction and plant transformation

Two editing targets for MS2 (GmAMS1), GmAMS2 and MS6 (Glyma.13G066600), respectively, were designed with the CRISPR/Cas genome editing online tool MMEJ-KO (http://skl. scau.edu.cn/mmejko/) (Xie et al., 2021). The editing targets of MS2 under the control of GmU6 promoter were inserted into the CRISPR/Cas9 BGK041 vector (Biogle Genome Editing Center, Changzhou, China). The editing targets of GmAMS2 and MS6 were under the control of U3 and U6 promoters, respectively. Then, these two cassettes were cloned into the pYLCRISPR/Cas9- DB vector (Ma et al., 2015). The constructed vectors were introduced into the Agrobacterium strain EHA101 and were transformed into the soybean cultivar Wm82. The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# MS2 monoclonal antibody production

To ensure the antigen recognition specificity, the peptide sequences QQFNYNQHNRMKSDAAFSEEYQAGNSFLHDE (224– 254 amino acid residues) of MS2 was used to produce monoclonal antibody in mice. The peptide synthesis and immunization, and antibody purification were conducted by Beijing Protein Innovation Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). 

# RNA isolation and qRT-PCR

The root, stem and unifoliolate leaf (UL) were harvested at the vegetative cotyledon stage (VC). The shoot apical meristem (SAM) and the fourth trifoliate leaf (TL) were harvested at the vegetative stage 5 (V5). The flower bud or tissue was harvested at the reproductive stage 2 (R2). Total RNA was isolated with the Ultrapure RNA kit (CW0581M, CWBIO). Then $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of total RNA was reversetranscribed with the PrimeScriptTM RT regent kit (RR047B, TaKaRa). The qRT-PCR reaction was performed using LightCycler 480 SYBR Green I Master (04887352001, Roche) on a LightCycle480 II system (Roche, Germany). In each assay, each gene was run in triplicate with cDNAs synthesized from three biological replicates. F-box gene (Glyma.12G05110) was used as the reference gene for the normalization of gene expression (Fang et al., 2013). The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# RNA-seq and data analysis

The samples of soybean anther were collected from the NIL-MS2/ JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16 of ${ \mathsf { B F } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 3 }$ plants from the early to before pollination stage. The method of total RNA isolation is described above. The process of library preparation, sequencing and data analysis have been described in our previous study (Fang et al., 2021). 

# ChIP-seq and data analysis

The flower samples including petal, stamen and pistil were harvested from Wm82 plants from the early to before pollination stage. Totally, about $5 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ sample was used for each biological experiment. After grounded in liquid nitrogen, the sample was cross-linked by $1 \%$ formaldehyde for 10 min and quenched by 125 mM glycine for 5 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Soluble chromatin was collected after sonication with a Bioruptor Plus sonication system (Diagenode, Belgium). The supernatant was incubated with 

$3 0 ~ \mu \ g$ MS2 monoclonal antibody (described in MS2 monoclonal antibody production) for 2 h. Then, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ Protein G SureBeads (1614833, Bio-Rad) was added and incubated for another 1 h at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with rotation. Subsequently, the associated DNA was purified followed by decrosslinking. 

For Input sample library construction, 20 ng purified DNA was used. After evaluating by Qubit fluorometer and agarose gel electrophoresis analysis, the DNA fragments undergone endrepaired, dA-tailed, and ligated to sequencing adaptor and amplified to generate the library. For IP sample library construction, 5 ng purified DNA was used. The library was prepared using NEBNext Ultra II DNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (E7645S, NEB) and NEBNext Multiplex Oligos for Illumina (E7335S, NEB) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The ChIP-seq libraries were prepared and sequenced with the Illumina HiSeq1500 platform by Novogene Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Tianjin, China). 

After mapping reads to the reference genome Williams 82.a2.v1, MACS2 v2.1.7.1 was used to call peaks with $q$ -value threshold of 0.05 (Model-based Analysis of ChIP-Seq). Then, we used Homer v4.11 to identify the de novo sequence motif and the matched known motifs (Heinz et al., 2010). Subsequently, ChIPseeker was used to annotate peaks to the nearest genes (Yu et al., 2015). The data were analysed by Novogene Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Tianjin, China). 

# Subcellular localization

The vector with nuclear localization marker NLS-RB (NLS-mCherry) was reconstructed based on ER-RB vector (Nelson et al., 2007). The nuclear localization signal peptide PKKKRKV was fused to the N terminal of mCherry. The CDS of MS2 without stop codon was amplified with the cDNA from soybean cultivar Williams 82 and was inserted into the PTF101-GFP vector (Chen et al., 2020). The two plasmids were co-transformed into the Arabidopsis protoplasts. We also used tobacco leaves to further confirm the localization pattern. The plasmids were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101 first, and then, these two stains were co-transformed into the tobacco leaves. The GFP and mCherry signals were visualized using an LSM-800 confocal microspore (Zeiss, Germany). The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The MS2 coding sequence was amplified and cloned into the pET28a vector (Novagen Merck, Germany). The recombinant protein was purified by Ni-NTA Agarose beads (30210, Qiagen). Single-strand oligonucleotides with biotin at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end were synthesized by Invitrogen (Guangzhou,China) andsubsequently annealedtodsDNA probes. The in vitro binding assays were carried out with the Light Shift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (20148, Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacture’s instruction. In addition, we confirmed the result with the similar binding assay. The purified protein and dsDNA probes (without biotin) were incubated in the buffer containing 10 mM Tris–HCl and 100 mM NaCl (pH 8.0) on ice for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ . The samples were assessed by $8 \%$ native PAGE gel. The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# Transient transcription dual-luciferase (Dual-LUC) assay

The MS2 and ms2 coding sequence were amplified and cloned into the pCAMBIA1306-3xFLAG binary vector to generate the effectors. The 5 X G-box fused with 35S mini sequences were inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC to produce the reporter. Agrobacterium GV3101 carrying the effector and reporter were mixed in a 1:1 ratio and infiltrated into tobacco leaves. The 

luciferase activity was measured using the Dual-Luciferase Assay Kit (E1910, Promega). The primer sequences are listed in Table S1 in Appendix S1. 

# Protein extraction and Western blot analysis

The samples used for Western blot analysis were collected at the same stage with that of RNA samples for qRT-PCR assay. Total proteins were extracted with the extraction buffer containing 100 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5), 100 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA $\left( \mathsf { p H 8 . 0 } \right)$ , $5 \%$ SDS, $20 \%$ Glycerol, $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ DTT, 40 mM $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol, $2 \mathsf { m m }$ PMSF, $\boldsymbol { { 1 } } \times$ Roche protease inhibitor cocktail, $8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ MG132, $8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ MG115, 10 mM NEM and $1 \%$ (V/V) Phosphatase inhibitor cocktail 3. Membranes were incubated with anti-MS2 (described above) and anti-Tubulin (M0267-1a, Abiocode) antibodies, respectively. 

# Cytological analysis

The method of transverse section has been described (Fang et al., 2021). For scanning electron microscopy observation, flower buds of NIL-MS2/JL16 and NIL-ms2/JL16 were harvested at R2 stage and fixed with FAA solution ( $10 \%$ formaldehyde, $50 \%$ ethanol and $5 \%$ acetic acid) and stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . After dehydrating and air-drying, the samples were sputter-coated with gold. 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical significance was determined using Student’s t-test or one-way ANOVA. All statistical analyses were performed with SPSS v2.6 (SPSS Inc).
