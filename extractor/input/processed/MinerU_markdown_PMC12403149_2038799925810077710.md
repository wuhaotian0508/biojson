# Aspartic acid residues in BBE-like enzymes from Morus alba promote a function shift from oxidative cyclization to dehydrogenation

Berberine bridge enzyme (BBE)-like enzymes catalyze various oxidative cyclization and dehydrogenation reactions in natural product biosynthesis, but the molecular mechanism underlying the selectivity remains unknown. Here, we elucidated the catalytic mechanism of BBE-like oxidases from Morus alba involved in the oxidative cyclization and dehydrogenation of moracin C. X-ray crystal structures of a functionally promiscuous flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)–bound oxidase, MaDS1, with and without an oxidative dehydrogenation product were determined at 2.03 Å and 2.21 Å resolution, respectively. Structure-guided mutagenesis and sequence analysis have identified a conserved aspartic acid that directs the reaction toward the oxidative dehydrogenation pathway. A combination of density functional theory (DFT) calculations and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations has revealed that aspartic acid acts as the catalytic base to deprotonate the carbon-cation intermediate to generate the dehydrogenated product, which otherwise undergoes a spontaneous $6 \pi$ electrocyclization in the oxidative cyclization pathway to furnish the 2H-benzopyran product.

Berberine bridge enzyme (BBE)-like enzymes belong to the superfamily of flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)-linked oxidases, characterized by the bicovalent attachment of the FAD cofactor and a typical fold ( 1 , 2 ). BBE-like enzymes are commonly found in the biosynthetic pathways of plant natural products ( 1 ), catalyzing various oxidation reactions to produce structurally diverse natural products. These transformations involve oxidative cyclization catalyzed by EcBBE from Eschscholzia californica ( 3 ), tetrahydrocannabinolic acid synthase (THCAS) from Cannabis sativa ( 4 ), and daurichromenic acid synthase (DCAS) from Rhododendron dauricum ( 5 ) and oxidative dehydrogenation catalyzed by At BBE13/15 from Arabidopsis thaliana ( 6 ), two carbohydrate oxidases (Ha CHOX and Ls CHOX) from Heliantus annuus and Lativa sativa ( 7 ), and (S )-tetrahydroprotoberberine oxidase (STOX) from Berberis wilsoniae ( 8 ) ( Fig. 1A ). 

Moraceae plants are a rich source of Diels–Alder (D-A) type natural products and their structurally related benzopyran-type natural products (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 ) ( 9 – 13 ). In our previous study, we found that BBE-like oxidocyclases from Morus alba (MaOCs) catalyze oxidative cyclization of moracin C (1 ) to yield benzopyran-type natural product moracin D (2 ) while the BBE-like diene synthases (MaDSs) catalyze the oxidative dehydrogenation of moracin C (1 ) to yield diene (3 ), which further undergoes an enzymatic intermolecular Diels–Alder (D-A) reaction with morachalcone A (4 ) to yield the D-A type natural product chalcomoracin (5 ) in the presence of BBE-like Diels–Alderases (MaDAs) ( Fig. 1B ) ( 14 – 16 ). Interestingly, several BBE-like enzymes in M. alba , such as MaDS1 and MaDS6, were found to catalyze both oxidative dehydrogenation and oxidative cyclization, and this functional promiscuity is not commonly observed in BBE-like enzymes ( 16 ). Although the evolutionary mechanism of MaDAs from MaOCs has recently been elucidated ( 16 ), the molecular mechanism underlying the precise control of the oxidative outcomes for MaDSs and MaOCs remains unknown. 

Here, we used site-directed mutagenesis, X-ray crystallography, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, and density functional theory (DFT) calculations to elucidate the molecular mechanisms of BBE-like enzyme-catalyzed oxidative dehydrogenation and cyclization reactions. # Significance

Berberine bridge enzyme (BBE)-like enzymes, a class of enzymes that mainly catalyze oxidative cyclization or oxidative dehydrogenation, play a crucial role in natural product biosynthesis. However, the mechanism governing the selectivity between oxidative cyclization and oxidative dehydrogenation still remains poorly understood. In this work, we demonstrate that an aspartic acid in a BBE-like enzyme acts as a catalytic base, enabling deprotonation to form diene products, while its absence leads to spontaneous cyclization. These findings provide critical insights into the evolutionary adaptation of BBE-like enzymes and offer a foundation for engineering these oxidases to synthesize a diverse range of natural product-like compounds.

# Results

As previously described, MaDS1 is a functionally promiscuous oxidase that catalyzes moracin D and diene 3 formation from moracin C (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 ). To investigate the molecular mechanisms of the Morus alba BBE-like oxidase-catalyzed oxidative dehydrogenation and cyclization, we solved the crystal structure of this functionally promiscuous BBE-like enzyme, MaDS1, at a resolution of 2.21 Å. The overall topology of MaDS1 (Protein Data Bank (PDB) no. 8ZLS) is similar to that of THCAS and MaDAs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A ) and comprises 17 α-helices and $1 5 \beta$ -sheets ( Fig. 2 A and B ). The electron density reveals that the FAD cofactor covalently binds in the FAD binding pocket through the characteristic 6-S-cysteinyl-,8α-N1-histidyl-FAD linkage like other typical BBE-like oxidases (2) . 

Despite considerable attempts, we failed to obtain the cocrystal structure of MaDS1 with moracin C (1 ), possibly due to the high reactivity of this substrate. To get a suitable probe to reveal the interactions between MaDS1 and its substrate, several substrate mimics with weaker enzymatic activity were synthesized (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ) and cocrystallized with MaDS1. After optimizing the conditions, we successfully obtained a cocrystal structure of MaDS1 bound to the oxidative dehydrogenation product 14 from precursor 9 (PDB no. 8ZLT) at a resolution of 2.03 Å 

(SI Appendix, Fig. S3B ). The overall MaDS1-FAD- 14 structure is very similar to the ligand-free structure, with a RMSD of $0 . 3 7 4 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S3C ). The electron density indicated the binding pose of product 14 in the substrate binding pocket composed of 6 $\beta$ -sheets (β9-11 and β13-15), one α-helix (α11), and one loop (loop 356-363), as well as the adjacent FAD (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 3 D$ ). Further analysis of the cocrystal structure revealed that 14 is stabilized by hydrogen-bonding interactions between its hydroxyl groups and residues R314, R292, and Y116. Additionally, hydrophobic interactions involving bulky residues (F482, F412, Y290, Y190, N437, L414, F383, and Y479) likely contribute to substrate binding by engaging with the hydrophobic side chain of 14 (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 3 D$ ). Based on this cocrystal structure of MaDS1, molecular docking was performed to get a reasonable binding model for moracin C (1 ) ( Fig. $2 C$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S3E ). In contrast to this binding model with cocrystal structure, moracin C (1 ) lacks this extended hydrophobic side chain, explaining the absence of hydrophobic interactions with Y190, N437, L414, F383, and Y479 in its binding mode. We found that the substrate binding is likely facilitated by Y116, R292, and D410, which can form hydrogen-bonding interactions with the phenol groups of the substrate ( Fig. 2C ). In addition, hydrophobic interactions between the aromatic residues (i.e., Y290, F412, H439, and F482) and the substrate can contribute to the binding of the substrate ( Fig. $2 D$ ). Despite no direct interaction between R314 and the substrate, R314 forms a hydrogen bonding network with D410 and D175. Due to these substrate–protein interactions, the benzylic position of the substrate is located close (2.8 Å) to FAD, thus enabling ProS hydride transfer to occur from the substrate to FAD ( 19 ). 

To demonstrate the interactions between these residues and the substrate, we performed site-directed mutagenesis on each of these 

residues and compared their catalytic activities on moracin C (1 ) with wild-type (WT) MaDS1 ( Fig. 2E ). The variant Y116F exhibited a complete loss of both oxidative cyclization activity to yield moracin D and oxidative dehydrogenation activity to yield diene 3 , indicating the critical role of the hydroxyl group of Y116 in maintaining substrate binding. Variant D175A displayed a complete loss of oxidative dehydrogenation activity without any loss of oxidative cyclization activity ( Fig. $2 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ), indicating the crucial role of D175 in oxidative dehydrogenation. Although no direct interaction between R314 and the substrate can be observed, this residue was necessary for the diene formation, probably due to its indirect influence via forming a hydrogen bonding interaction with D175 ( Fig. 2E ). Notably, the variant D410A displayed a loss of more than $7 0 \%$ in oxidative dehydrogenation activity, consistently revealing that the hydrogen bond within D175/R314/D410 is crucial for promoting the dehydrogenation process. 

To gain additional insight into how the BBE-like enzymes control the selectivity between oxidative cyclization and oxidative dehydrogenation reactions, we used bioinformatics to analyze the sequence features of DSs and OCs in M. alba (SI Appendix, Table S1 ). Y116 is well conserved across all the oxidases, indicating its essential role in substrate recognition ( Fig. 3A ). D175 is evolutionarily conserved in the DSs, while all the OCs contain hydrophobic amino acids like V or L ( Fig. 3A ). R314 is also evolutionarily conserved in DSs, while all OCs contain an S, indicating the coevolutionary relationship between R314 and D175 in the DSs. Due to the hydrogen bonding interaction between R314 and D175, the carboxyl group of D175 is positioned close (3.6 Å) to the methyl group of the isopentenyl moiety and thus may serve as a catalytic base to deprotonate the methyl group, yielding diene 3 ( Fig. 2C ). To test this hypothesis, site-directed mutagenesis was conducted to mutate this D to E or N in MaDS1 ( Fig. 3B and SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). However, the D175N mutation entirely abolished the dehydrogenation activity, converting MaDS1 into a monofunctional oxidocyclase with increased catalytic efficiency ( Fig. 3B and SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). Due to the absence of the catalytic residue D175 in the D175N variant, its oxidative dehydrogenation activity was completely abolished and thus shifted to the D175-independent oxidative cyclization activity, resulting in a significant increase in the $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ values of D175N over WT $( k _ { \mathrm { c a t } } ^ { \sim } = 1 3 . 6 8 s ^ { - 1 }$ vs $1 . 1 9 s ^ { - 1 }$ ) (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). By contrast, substituting the corresponding aspartic acid with V in monofunctional DSs, MaMO and MaDS5, converted them into monofunctional oxidocyclases ( Fig. 3 C , i–vi ). The ancDADS, the ancestor of MaDSs ( 16 ) could acquire the oxidative dehydrogenation activity via this V to D single mutation ( Fig. $3 ~ C _ { \mathrm { { s } } }$ vii–ix ), demonstrating the critical role of this substitution during the evolution of DSs from OCs. Together, these results demonstrated the important role of the carboxyl group of D175 in promoting the oxidative dehydrogenation reactions catalyzed by BBE-like enzymes from M. alba . 

To provide further insight into the mechanism of the selective oxidative cyclization of moracin C catalyzed by enzyme MaDS1, DFT calculations were performed. The results are summarized in Fig. 4 , and the structure of the transition states is shown in SI Appendix, Fig. S6 . The reaction is first initiated by a hydride transfer from M-1 to FAD via M-TS2 with a barrier of $2 3 . { \dot { 0 } } \mathrm { k c a l } / $ mol. This leads to an allylic carbocation intermediate M-3 . M-3 can then be readily deprotonated without barrier by D410 to give a quinone intermediate M-4 (see more details in SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). Another alternative pathway is shown in red in Fig. 4 , where the substrate M-1 is first deprotonated and then oxidized via M-TS1 . However, the calculated barrier of this pathway is relatively higher $\left( 2 6 . 4 \mathrm { k c a l } / \mathrm { m o l } \right)$ ), so it should not be favored. The 

quinone intermediate M-4 then undergoes a 6π electrocyclization, closing the ring to yield the benzopyran product M-5 . To consider different catalytic mechanisms of this step, two possible electrocyclization transition states were calculated in our calculation: M-TS4 and M-TS5 . The Gibbs free energy is high up to $3 2 . 5 \mathrm { k c a l } / $ mol for M-TS5 using the same theozyme model. But it can be significantly lowered by allowing the reaction to occur outside the cavity $( 2 2 . 0 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ , M-TS4 ), which is consistent with our MD simulation results that M-TS4 was not able to be stabilized within the substrate pocket (SI Appendix, Fig. S8A ). Meanwhile, we found that MaDS1 oxidized substrates 10 and 12 without any stereoselectivity, which also supports the M-TS4 pathway (SI Appendix, Figs. S10 and S11 ). The other observed product, diene M-6 , is proposed to be generated by the deprotonation of cationic intermediate M-3 by the nearby D175 $( { \sim } 3 . 0 \mathrm { ~ \AA } )$ ) via M-TS6 in the orange pathway, with a barrier of $2 2 . 3 \mathrm { \ k c a l / m o l }$ ( Fig. 4 and SI Appendix, Fig. S8B ). The barrier of oxidative cyclization (22.0 kcal/mol, M-TS4 ) is close to that of deprotonation $( 2 2 . 3 \mathrm { \ k c a l / }$ mol, M-TS6 ), which explains the low selectivity of MaDS1 toward moracin D compared to diene 3. Considering the importance of D175 in selectivity control, we investigated the effect of D175 on key transition states (M-TS4 and M-TS5 form cyclic product, and M-TS6 forms diene product). In wild-type MaDS1, the production of cyclic and diene products via M-TS4 and M-TS6 has similar barriers ( Fig. 5A ). If D175 is protonated in the reaction ( Fig. 5B ), M-TS5-COOH is more inclined to form the cyclic product. When D175 is mutated to asparagine, M-TS5-ASN is more favored to produce the cyclic product. 

# Discussion

In summary, we have elucidated the molecular mechanisms of the oxidative dehydrogenation and cyclization reactions catalyzed by BBE-like enzymes through the use of X-ray crystallography, sitedirected mutagenesis, sequence analysis, MD simulations, and DFT calculations. The FAD fragment abstracts hydride from the substrate to yield the carbocation intermediate. A conserved aspartic acid in MaDSs can then act as the catalytic base to deprotonate the methyl group of the intermediate and direct the reaction pathway to yield diene. In MaOCs, a spontaneous $6 \pi$ -electron electrocyclization occurs to generate the benzopyran product due to the absence of such a basic catalytic residue. Our findings reveal that D175 serves as a gatekeeper residue, strictly controlling the oxidative dehydrogenation activity. This represents a remarkable example of how a single amino acid can dictate the catalytic outcome of a biosynthetic enzyme. While previous studies have documented cases where a single mutation alters substrate specificity ( 20 ), product specificity ( 21 ), or stereoselectivity ( 22 ) through laboratory evolution, our study uncovers a distinct mechanistic paradigm. Here, a neofunctionalization event mediated by a single point mutation redirects the enzymatic reaction into an alternative biosynthetic pathway. The resulting diene product subsequently serves as a substrate for a Diels–Alder reaction, ultimately generating a natural product with a structurally distinct scaffold that may function in stress response ( 16 ). These findings reveal the evolutionary mechanism of the BBE-like diene synthases and provide more mechanistic insights into the functional roles of the structurally diverse natural products in Morus plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 ). This mechanistic understanding of the oxidative dehydrogenation and cyclization processes catalyzed by BBE-like enzymes will significantly enhance our ability to rationally engineer these oxidases and to customize their functionality for the synthesis of a diverse array of natural product-like compounds.

# Materials and Methods

Site-Directed Mutagenesis. The variants of MaDS1, MaMO, MaDS5, and anc-DADS were constructed by Megawhop PCR using primers in the supplementary table. DNA fragments containing site-directed mutations were amplified with the pI-secSUMOstar plasmid with wild-type oxidases as templates and oxidase forward primer with corresponding reverse primers or oxidase reverse primer with corresponding forward primers (SI Appendix, Table S2) using FastPfu DNA polymerase (TransGen Biotech, China). The DNA fragment with site-directed mutation was used as megaprimer and pI-secSUMOstar plasmid with wild-type oxidases as templates to perform whole-plasmid PCR followed by Dpn I [New England Biolabs (NEB, Ipswich, MA)] digestion at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $5 \mathfrak { h }$ to obtain the vectors with mutations. All the constructs were confirmed by Sanger sequencing. 

The variants of oxidases were expressed using the Bac-to-Bac system and purified according to the literature (14). Recombinant bacmid was generated from E. coli DH10bac competent cells, and the baculoviruses were generated by infecting Sf9 insect cells cultured in SIM Sf-900™ II SFM medium (Gibco) for 4 d, followed by amplification in Sf9 cells maintained in SF medium (Sino Biological Inc.) for an additional 4 d. DFT Studies. The theozyme model is constructed based on the experimental cocrystal structure of MaDS1 and compound 14 (PDB ID 8ZLT), shown in SI Appendix, Fig. S3. The substrate in the crystal structure is positioned near the aromatic portion of FAD. The aromatic part of FAD, shown in SI Appendix, Fig. $\mathsf { S } 3 B ,$ is believed to capture hydride. Then, combined with the cocrystal structure and the mutation experiment of MaDS1 in Fig. 2E, the mutation Y116F is crucial to the reactivity of the reaction. The mutation of D175 and R314 has a significant effect on the selective generation of oxidative cyclization, while the mutation of D410 results in the diene product being the major product. Considering these mutagenesis results and the close proximity of these residues to the substrate in the cocrystal structure, the side-chains of Y116, D175, R314, and D410 are included in the theozyme model for DFT calculation. 

The Gaussian 16 program was used for all the DFT calculations. All structures were optimized in water using the SMD solvation model at the B3LYP-D3BJ//6- 31G(d) (24–27) level of theory. Vibrational frequency calculations were performed at the same level to obtain thermal energy correction and to confirm that the stationary point is an energy minimum or a transition state. The single-point energies in water were calculated using the SMD solvation model at the M06-2X (27) and $6 { - } 3 1 1 { + } { + } 6 ( 0 , 0 )$ basis set level of theory. Intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) (28, 29) calculations were performed to confirm the connection between the transition state and its reactant and product.

The charges were calculated according to the Merz–Singh–Kollman scheme (43, 44) using Gaussian 16 program package (45). Long-range electrostatic effects were modeled using the particle mesh Ewald method with periodic boundary conditions (46). An 8 Å cutoff was applied to Lennard-Jones and electrostatic interactions.
