# a b s t r a c t

Palmitoleate (cis-D9-16:1) shows numerous health benefits such as increased cell membrane fluidity, reduced inflammation, protection of the cardiovascular system, and inhibition of oncogenesis. Plant oils containing this unusual fatty acid can also be sustainable feedstocks for producing industrially important and high-demand 1-octene. Vegetable oils rich in palmitoleate are the ideal candidates for biodiesel production. Several wild plants are known that can synthesize high levels of palmitoleate in seeds. However, low yields and poor agronomic characteristics of these plants limit their commercialization. Metabolic engineering has been developed to create oilseed crops that accumulate high levels of palmitoleate or other unusual fatty acids, and significant advances have been made recently in this field, particularly using the model plant Arabidopsis as the host. The engineered targets for enhancing palmitoleate synthesis include overexpression of D9 desaturase from mammals, yeast, fungi, and plants, down-regulating KASII, coexpression of an ACP-D9 desaturase in plastids and CoA-D9 desaturase in endoplasmic reticulum (ER), and optimizing the metabolic flux into triacylglycerols (TAGs). This review will mainly describe the recent progress towards producing palmitoleate in transgenic plants by metabolic engineering along with our current understanding of palmitoleate biosynthesis and its regulation, as well as highlighting the bottlenecks that require additional investigation by combining lipidomics, transgenics and other ‘‘- omics’’ tools. A brief review of reported health benefits and non-food uses of palmitoleate will also be covered.

# 1. Introduction

Plant seed oils are an important component of human diets. The most commonly consumed vegetable oils are derived from palm (Elaeis guineensis), soybean (Glycine max), rapeseed/canola (Brassica napus), sunflower (Helianthus annuus) and peanut (Arachis hypogaea). These common oil crops contain only five main fatty acids, the saturated palmitic (C16:0) and stearic (C18:0) acids, monounsaturated oleic acid (C18:1), and the polyunsaturated linoleic (C18:2) and $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic (C18:3) acids. In addition, some 300 additional fatty acids have been found in plants. A number of unusual fatty acids, synthesized in various plants but not in common oilseeds, have been identified to have high value for human nutrition and/or industrial uses. Palmitoleic acid (C16:1D9) is one such important unusual fatty acid that can contribute greatly to human health and is also a valuable renewable source for industrial chemicals and biodiesel.

The ester of palmitoleic acid, palmitoleate can accumulate in triacylglycerol (TAG), phospholipids or other glycerolipids in some plant tissues. By definition palmitoleate or its free acid is cis-D9- hexadecenoate (x7) or cis-D9-16:1 (Fig. 1). This will simply be referred to as 16:1 in this review and other forms of 16:1 or hexadecenoate will be delineated.

Palmitoleate has unusual characteristics such as low titer (0.5– $1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ melting point) [1] while also having good oxidative stability, which will benefit its use in food production. Recently, there has been considerable interest in including 16:1 in foods and nutraceuticals for health purposes [2–4]. Accumulating studies indicate that palmitoleate can increase cell membrane fluidity, inhibit oncogenesis, reduce inflammation associated with diabetes, heart disease and other health problems [5–10]. Moreover, palmitoleate is recognized as a good renewable source of 1-octene, a high-demand feedstock used as a co-monomer in the expanding production of linear low density polyethylene [11–13]. Since natural sources of 16:1 cannot be produced in high amounts at low cost there is interest in metabolic engineering of oilseed crops for 16:1 production [13–21]. In this review current progress in examining the health benefits of 16:1, non-food uses, understanding the biosynthesis of 16:1 in plant seeds, producing 16:1 in engineered plants and discussing bottlenecks that require additional investigation are summarized.

# 2. Health benefits of palmitoleate

There is a growing body of evidence that 16:1 has notable health properties and particularly, it is now considered as a lipokine or lipid-derived hormone. This new lipokine apparently has several actions that promote health including increasing muscle response to insulin.

Insulin resistance is a key factor of a collection of metabolic disorders including Type II diabetes, atherosclerosis, fatty liver disease, hypertension and dyslipidemia. Insulin regulates numerous cellular responses including stimulation of glucose uptake, glycogen synthesis and lipogenesis in key target tissues, such as skeletal muscle and white fat, while simultaneously suppressing hepatic gluconeogenesis. This action of insulin is mediated by its binding to a receptor. Impaired insulin responsiveness (i.e. insulin resistance) of the above-mentioned tissues leads to dysregulation in the hormonal control of these important physiological responses and contributes to the development of Type 2 diabetes mellitus. Non-esterified fatty acids (NEFAs) have been implicated strongly in diabetes development in tissues such as skeletal muscle [21,22]. Insulin sensitivity was evaluated in a number of experiments by Bajaj et al. [23] and Stefan et al. [24] and it was found that 16:1 can prevent insulin resistance in mice and predict insulin sensitivity in humans. These studies indicate that circulating 16:1 positively correlates with insulin sensitivity, independently of age, sex, and adiposity. High baseline 16:1 is associated with a larger increase in insulin sensitivity. There is growing evidence that 16:1 plays an important role in the pathophysiology of insulin resistance in humans [23–25]. It can counteract the palmitate-induced impairment of insulin-response [26].

Dysregulation of lipid metabolism in individual tissues leads to systemic disruption of insulin action and glucose metabolism. Cao et al. [27] explored how metabolic alterations in adipose tissue are linked to whole-body metabolism through lipid signals. Utilizing quantitative lipidomic analyses and mice deficient in adipose tissue lipid chaperones aP2 and mal1, they found that 16:1 worked as an adipose tissue-derived lipid hormone that strongly stimulates muscle insulin action and suppresses hepatosteatosis. The data reveal a lipid-mediated endocrine network and demonstrate that adipose tissue uses lipokines such as 16:1 to communicate with distant organs and regulate systemic metabolic homeostasis [27]. Fabbrini et al. [28] report that decreased palmitoleate in plasma and in VLDL is not associated with insulin resistance in skeletal muscle, liver, or adipose tissue in obese humans. Experiments by Mozaffarian et al. [29] indicate that circulating 16:1 is strongly associated with multiple metabolic risk factors in humans but in mixed directions possibly due to lifestyle determinants or whether endogenous synthesis is in liver or adipose tissue.

The effects of 16:1 and palmitate 16:0 on insulin action and glucose utilization in rat L6 skeletal muscle cells were examined by Dimopoulos et al. [30]. Basal glucose uptake was enhanced approximately 2-fold following treatment of cells with palmitoleate. A 16:1-induced increase in glucose transport led to an associated rise in glucose oxidation and glycogen synthesis, which could not be attributed to activation of signaling proteins normally modulated by stimuli such as insulin, nutrients or cell stress.

Palmitoleic acid is a candidate anti-melanogenic agent, and it might be useful in ameliorating hyperpigmentation disorders. Melanogenesis is a well-known physiological response of human skin that may occur because of exposure to ultraviolet light, for genetic reasons, or due to other causes. Studies by Yoon [31] show that 16:1 can inhibit melanogenic factors in human cells at levels with no cytotoxicity. 16:1 can regulate liver function in rats and humans. Studies by Kuda et al. [32] indicate that antidiabetic thiazolidinediones (TZDS) such as rosiglitazone up regulate a stearoyl-

CoA desaturase 1 (SCD 1) gene in adipose tissue and increase plasma levels of the SCD I product 16:1. Furthermore, a number of studies indicate that 16:1 can reduce cell death and ER stress in human and mouse cells [23,33–35]. By contrast, its counterpart, palmitic acid 16:0, induces cell death and ER stresses. Additional functions of palmitoleate for human health may be identified.

It is noteworthy to point out that the nutritional and biological functions of palmitoleate are complex, and our understanding is still limited. Elegant genetic studies in animal models showed the significance of circulating palmitoleic acid levels as a positive correlate of insulin sensitivity [25,27]. However, investigations performed in human subjects have exhibited some mixed results [28,29]. As such it is difficult to assign a defined role (determinant of metabolic risk vs. predictor of insulin sensitivity) to plasma levels of palmitoleic acid [36]. Also there may be different effects of palmitoleic acid derived from the diet from that endogenously produced. Therefore, further comprehensive studies are needed to fully understand the biological significance of palmitoleic acid on human health.

# 3. Industrial uses of palmitoleate

In addition to the biological functions described above, palmitoleate also has anti-oxidant, anti-microbial, and anti-aging properties, which makes it attractive in direct non-food uses in the skin care industry. Palmitoleate is reported to impart skin protection from wounds, scratches, burns, and the sun’s harmful UV rays (http://www.prosperorganics.com/tag/palmitoleic-acid). Omega-7 Plus, a skin cosmetic mainly consisting of palmitoleic acid also is purported to nourish skin, and increase healing, rejuvenation and moisture replenishment (http://www.bronsonvitamins.com/ 548A/omega-7-plus).

Biodiesel, defined as the fatty acid monoalkyl esters of vegetable oils or animal fats or waste oils, is a technically competitive and environmentally friendly alternative to conventional petrodiesel. While biodiesel possesses a number of advantages such as biodegradable, renewable, and nontoxic features as well as inherent lubricity, technical problems facing biodiesel include poor cold flow and oxidative stability. Palmitoleate is considered to be an ideal candidate for improving biodiesel properties [1]. Methyl palmitoleate shows a lower melting point (approximately $- 3 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ and an acceptable oxidative stability simultaneously while not compromising properties such as cetane number and kinematic viscosity [1]. Low temperature problems of biodiesel in northern US have been major issue. This trait of palimitoleate oil may also allow dual use of future crops for food and/or fuel which offers advantages in meeting shifting market demands and prices.

However, common oil crops (e.g. soybean and rapeseed/canola) contain little palmitoleate in their seed oils. Inherent genetic modification of the seed fatty acid profiles in oil crops, particularly increasing palmitoleate level, offers the best possibility of addressing several or all fuel property issues simultaneously. Nguyen et al. (2010) [13] of John Shanklin’s group describe major progress on engineering 16:1 and other omega-7 production in seed oil. Their study reports $4 3 \%$ 16:1 and $6 7 \%$ total omega-7 in the model plant Arabidopsis. Similar results have recently been achieved in Camelina sativa, an alternative oil crop, showing that the product can potentially be produced in a commercial oilseed [37,38].

Another major industrial use of palmitoleate is that vegetable oils enriched in this fatty acid can be sustainable feedstocks for producing high-demand 1-octene, an organic compound with a formula $\mathrm { C H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C H C } _ { 6 } \mathrm { H } _ { 1 3 }$ . 1-Octene is one of the important linear aolefins in industry with a global consumption of over half a million tons per year. The main use of 1-octene is as a comonomer in production of polyethylene. Annually, $7 5 \%$ of 1-octene in the world is

used for linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE), $1 2 \%$ for plastizizers, and $6 \%$ for lubricants.

Commercially, 1-octene is mainly synthesized from petroleumderived ethylene by oligomerization to yield a complex mixture of $\pmb { \alpha }$ -olefines or from coal-derived syngas [11]. Among five commercial processes that oligomerize ethylene to 1-octene, olefin metathesis has emerged as a powerful tool for synthetic organic as well as polymer chemistry. Particularly, recent development in olefin metathesis using plant-oil-derived fatty acids make it more efficient to split long-chain monoene fatty acids into the corresponding short-chain $\pmb { \alpha }$ -olefines and x-alkenoic acids [11,12]. Therefore, ethenolytic metathesis of x-7 fatty acids including palmitoleic (16:1D9), and its elongation products, cis-vaccenic (18:1D11) and cis-eicosenoic $\left( 2 0 { : } 1 \Delta 1 3 \right)$ acids can efficiently produce 1-octene and 9-decenoate by this technology. Plant oils rich in x-7 fatty acids can be highly-valuable renewable feedstocks for industrial chemicals.

# 4. Natural sources of palmitoleate

Low but readily detectable 16:1 levels are reported in some animal tissues such as in phosphatidylcholine (PC) of aveolar surfactants isolated from lungs [39] (Table 1).

Palmitoleate is rare in plants but a small number of plants are known to accumulate 16:1 to moderate to high levels in seeds (Table 2). Among these Kermadecia sinuate has the most 16:1 followed by cat’s claw. Macadamia nuts $30 \%$ 16:1) are the only significant plant food source of palmitoleate to date. Cat’s claw seeds are very small (15–18 mg/seed fresh weight) and relatively low in oil $( \sim 2 9 \% )$ . Macadamia nuts on the other hand are large and very high in oil.

Another high accumulator of 16:1 is sea buckthorn whose total lipids contain ${ \sim } 4 3 \%$ 16:1 [40]. Unlike other high accumulators of 16:1 in seed oil, sea buckthorn seeds only contain $4 . 4 \%$ of 16:1 (total seed oil content is $1 5 \%$ ) while the pulp accumulated $2 4 . 9 \%$ of 16:1(total pulp oil content is $34 \%$ ) suitable for commercial extraction. Fractionation of sea buckthorn pulp oil into liquid (LF) and solid (SF) fractions yielded about $2 0 \%$ of LF in which 16:1 represented $5 3 \%$ of total lipids [41]. The SF fraction was mainly 16:0. Notably, 16:1 is detected up to $1 5 \%$ in the pomace from sea buckthorn juice production, which is generally considered waste. Klaas and Meurer [42] developed a process increasing the 16:1 product levels from the pomace to $8 2 \%$ palmitoleic acid methyl ester, indicating that sea buckthorn pomace is a good raw material for 16:1 production.

# 5. Biosynthesis and accumulation of palmitoleate in plants

Plant lipid biosynthesis and accumulation in seeds involve several organelle and multiple enzymatic reactions. The key steps of the synthesis and accumulation of standard plant fatty acids and 16:1 are outlined in Fig. 2. This diagram is a simplified version and only covers part of FA biosynthesis. Similar to other fatty acids, there are three major biosynthetic events that occur during the production of 16:1 in plant seeds. The first involves the synthesis of 16:0-ACP and/or 16:1-ACP in plastids and their export to cytosol. The second involves the formation of 16:0-CoA and /or 16:1- CoA and their transfers to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) where 16:0-CoA can be further converted to 16:1-CoA. The third involves

the packaging of the nascent 16:1 into triacylglycerols (TAGs), which subsequently accumulate in oil bodies that pinch off from ER into the cytosol.

During seed development, photosynthate is imported and subsequently converted into precursors of fatty acid biosynthesis. Triose phosphates (TPs) derived from sucrose can be reduced to glycerol-3-P (3PGA) that provides the glycerol backbone for membrane lipids and TAGs. The pyruvate (Pyr) produced by glycolysis is converted to acetyl-CoA (2C) by the plastidic pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH). The first committed step of fatty acid biosynthesis is the conversion of acetyl-CoA into malonyl-CoA (3C) by the highly regulated plastidic acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) and then to malonyl-ACP (acyl carrier protein) by a transacylase. In most tissues of most eukaryotic organisms including plants malonyl-ACP is elongated in eight cycles, two carbon units at a time, via the fatty acid synthase (FAS) complex, to palmitoyl (16:0)-ACP (or -CoA). The fatty acid synthase complex involves four different enzymatic reactions with each cycle starting with a condensation, followed by a reduction, a dehydration and a second reduction [46]. The condensation reactions are catalyzed by enzymes known as 3-ketoacyl-ACP synthases or KASs. The 1st condensation reaction going from acetyl-CoA to 3-ketobutyrate is catalyzed by KAS III, the reaction from butyryl-ACP (C4) to palmitoyl-ACP (C16:0) by KAS I and from palmitoyl-ACP to stearoyl-ACP (C18:0) by KAS II. The reaction stops at C16 and C18 fatty acids not only by virtue of the specificity of the KAS enzymes but also by the action of ACP thioesterases (TEs) which hydrolyze the acyl-S-ACP thioester bonds. The first desaturation step in plastids is catalyzed by a D9-desaturase (D9D) converting 16:0-ACP to 16:1D9-ACP. This enzyme can also desaturate 18:0-ACP to 18:1D9-ACP. 16:1D9-ACP can be further elongated to 18:1D11-ACP by KASII in plastids and to 20:1D13 by extra-plastidial processes [13]. Two main types of TEs exist in plants [47]. The FatA class preferentially cleaves unsaturated 18:1D9 or 18:1D11 from ACP, whereas the FatB class is more specific for saturated acyl-ACPs (e.g. 16:0, 18:0), and in some species (e.g. coconut), for shorter-chain-length acyl ACPs (C8-C14) [48,49]. Regarding hexose-6-phosphates, PEP and pyruvate discussed here, more studies are needed to ascertain whether they are most important for providing hydrocarbon for fatty acid biosynthesis in chloroplasts in green tissues and proplastids (or leucoplasts) in non-green tissues.

After release from ACP, the free fatty acids including 16:0 and 16:1D9 are exported from plastids and converted to acyl-CoAs through the activity of acyl-CoA synthetases (ACS) located on the outer membrane. Plants have multiple ACSs that participate in lipid metabolism [50,51]. ACS enzymes encoded by different genes can have differential specificities for particular fatty acids [50,52]. Possiblly, different ACSs may have different substrate preferences such as in castor where the ACS 2 enzyme displayed approximately a 3-fold preference for incorporating the ricinoleate into acyl-CoA while the ACS 4 enzyme showed greater than 2-fold preference for oleate [53].

In the ER, acyl-CoAs are transferred to phospholipids such as phosphatidycholine (PC). 16:0-PC can be converted into 16:1D9- PC by a CoA-dependent D9D, and can then be elongated to 18:1D11 and further elongated to 20:1D13 in some plants such as Arabidopsis [13]. 18:0-PC can be desaturated to 18:1D9-PC by an ER D9 desaturase and then desaturated into 18:2D9,12-PC by D12-desaturase (D12D or FAD2) and 18:3D9,12,15-PC by x-3 desaturase (x3D, FAD3, D15D) sequentially.

Once modified by desaturases in the ER/microsome, nascent fatty acids including 16:1D9 and 18:1D11 can be shuttled by a series of acyltransferase reactions from the phospholipid fraction to TAG, known as the Kennedy pathway. First, fatty acyl moieties are added to the sn-1 position of glycerol-3-phosphate (G3P) by glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase (GPAT) to produce lysophosphatidic acid (LPA). Second, LPAs are acylated by lysophosphatidic acid acyl transferase (LPAT) to form phosphatidic acid (PA). The PA formed is then de-phosphorylated by phosphatidate phosphatase (PPA) to yield diacylglycerol (DAG). DAG can be used to form TAGs, or serve as a substrate for membrane lipid biosynthesis. Diacyl-

glycerol acyltransferase (DGAT), the last enzyme for TAG synthesis, uses acyl-CoA as an acyl donor to add a third acyl chain to the sn-3 position of DAG and yields TAGs. Alternatively, phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase (PDAT) are able to transfer the sn-2 acyl chain from PC to DAG and produce TAG and lyso-PC [54]. Moreover, PDAT and DGAT have overlapping functions for TAG synthesis in both Arabidopsis seed and pollen [55]. Finally, TAGs accumulate in oil bodies.

A number of intermediates occur between the biosynthesis of 16:1 and its selective accumulation in TAG in 16:1 oil seeds. This intermediate pool could play an important role in the high accumulation of 16:1. PC has been identified as a key intermediate in oil biosynthesis and plays a central role in polyunsaturated fatty acid production by serving as a substrate for D6, D9, D12 and D15 desaturases [56]. In addition, acyl-CoA:lysoPC acyltransferase (LPCAT) can provide a mechanism to enrich the acyl-CoA pool with polyunsaturated fatty acids synthesized on PC [57]. Similarly phosphatidylcholine: diacylglycerol cholinephosphotransferase (PDCT) can supply moieties to the DAG pool for TAG synthesis from PC [58]. The substrate specificity of PDCT directs 18:1-containing glycerolipids toward further desaturation and 18:2- and 18:3-containing DAG toward TAG biosynthesis, thereby playing a role in modulating the FA composition of TAG. The discovery of this PDCT enzyme now helps explain the considerable flux from PC back into DAG required for TAG synthesis that may complement a reversibility of cholinephosphotransferase (CPT) activity [59]. PLA (phospholipase A) also catalyzes acyl-PCs to yield LPC and a free FA intermediate that then is activated to CoA, showing a role in acyl group exchanges among phospholipid molecular species with an intervening acyl-CoA pool.

It is noted that each of the major metabolic steps in seed oil biosynthesis occurs in a highly integrated, cooperative fashion, resulting in a massive flux of carbon into storage oil. Disruption of any of these pathways can lead to decreased metabolic flux of fatty acids into oil, increased degradation of fatty acids, and/or reduced yield of desired fatty acids in storage oil. Regulation of oil synthesis also occurs at multiple levels [60]. For instance, some transcription factors such as WRINKED 1(WRI1) are identified to control oil biosynthesis [61–63]. Allosteric enzymes are found to be involved in the regulation, as examplified by plastid pyruvate kinase [64]. In regard of 16:1 synthesis and regulation, the underlying mechanistic details are not well understood yet, particularly in high accumulators of 16:1.

# 6. Metabolic engineering of 16:1 pathway in plants

There is increasing interest in enhancing plant oil for human nutrition and using plant oils as renewable sources of industrial chemical feedstocks [65–68]. With multiple health benefits and a new sustainable feedstock for 1-octene production as well as other industrial uses, palmitoleate is an important target fatty acid to be engineered for increased levels in plant oil for commercial production. To meet this goal, various approaches have been designed with some promising results achieved. In this section, we briefly describe the state of art in terms of the ability to produce this important lipid in plants and the need for a better understanding of the molecular and cellular events that will underpin rational metabolic engineering designs.

# 6.1. Breeding oil crop mutant lines accumulating high level of palmitoleate

Most species accumulating palmitoleic acid are not grown commercially because of limiting agronomic traits such as low yields and other difficulties in large scale economic production including limited geographical growing areas. Producing oil crop lines with high 16:1 is of great interest.

Sunflower (Helianthus annuus L.), a field oil crop, normally contains ${ < } 1 \%$ 16:1. A new high x7 monounsaturated fatty acid sunflower mutant was reported by [64,69], which was bred by chemical mutagenesis and backcrossing (Fernández-Martínez et al., 1997). This new line contains up to $1 2 \%$ 16:1D9, $5 \%$ 16:2D9, $1 2 \%$ and $6 \% 1 8 { : } 1 \Delta 1 1$ . The high-16:1 phenotype is associated with reductions in fatty acid synthase II activity and an increase of stearoyl-ACP desaturase activity. This mutant accumulated significant quantities of x7 fatty acids in the polar lipid fraction, especially in phosphatidylcholine [70]. However, the total polar lipid content of these lines was not affected. In contrast x7 lipid accumulation was associated with decreased oil yield.

Soybean is an important oil source that is high in linoleic (18:2) and palmitic (16:0) fatty acids ${ \sim } 5 5 \%$ and $1 5 \%$ , respectively). Conversion of all or part of these 16:0 into palmitoleic acid (16:1) would be a great benefit for health. Genetic loci that harbor mutations leading to altered palmitic acid phenotypes are designated Fap. A22 with $6 . 8 \%$ 16:0, has a single recessive fap3 allele [71]. A mutant soybean line with elevated 16:0 containing the fap2 allele was found to have a single base-pair substitution in codon 299 of the GmKASIIA gene with TGG ? TAG converting a trp to a premature stop codon [72]. KASII encodes the keto-acyl-ACP synthase that catalyzes the condensation reaction of the FAS complex involved in elongation of 16:0-ACP to 18:0-ACP. It is likely that reduced elongation of 16:0-ACP to 18:0-ACP led to the 16:0 enhancements in the seeds of the fap2 allele genotype (C1727). Possibly, this mutant can be used as a transgenic host to provide more 16:0 substrates for introduced specificD9D to form more 16:1D9 than regular soybeans.

# 6.2. Transgenic overexpression of the 16:0-specific D9D to increase palmitoleate synthesis in plants

As shown on Fig. 1, D9D enzyme catalyzes desaturation of 16:0 to form 16:1D9. Most D9D enzymes also work for conversion of 18:0 to 18:1D9. It is believed that increasing D9D activity can enhance 16:1D9 biosynthesis. Numerous D9D genes from various organisms have been cloned and characterized so far [73–77]. There are two classes of D9D enzymes in plants. One is acyl-ACP dependent (soluble), and the other is acyl-CoA dependent (membrane-bonded). A number of studies have been reported on expression of $\Delta 9$ desaturases capable of converting 16:0 to 16:1 in transgenic plants including tobacco, petunia, tomato, eggplant, Arabidopsis and soybeans. Heterogeneous expression of D9D alone results in $\leqslant 1 0 \%$ 16:1 levels.

Graburn et al. [78] reported significant conversion of 16:0 to 16:1 in transgenic Nicotiana tobacum tissues expressing a rat liver ER 18:0/16:0-CoA desaturase. Polashock et al. [17] also report on the introduction of the protein coding sequence of the endoplasmic reticulum cytochrome b5-dependent, D9 fatty acid desaturase gene from Saccharomyces cerevisiae into Nicotiana tabacum via Agrobacterium transformation. All transformed plants expressing the yeast gene exhibited at a 10-fold increase in the levels of palmitoleic acid (16:1D9) in leaf tissue. This is above the low levels (less than $2 \%$ ) of 16:1D7 seen in wild-type plants as 16:1D9 and 16:1D7 are not always easily separated. Positional analysis shows that the cytoplasmically produced 16:1 is found primarily in the sn-2 position of phosphatidylcholine, phosphatidylethanolamine, monogalactosyldiacylglycerol and digalactosyldiacylglycerol. These results indicate that mammalian and yeast desaturases can function in plants, apparently by using a leaf ER cytochrome b5- mediated electron transport system. The 16:1 is found to be incorporated into most of the major polar lipid classes, including the cytoplasmically produced ‘‘eukaryotic’’ fraction of the chloroplast galactolipids. 16:1D9 is not found, however, in phosphatidyl glycerol, which is considered to be produced almost exclusively in chloroplasts. Despite these changes in membrane lipid composition, no obvious phenotypic differences were apparent in the transformed plants. The positional data suggest that sn-2 acyltransferases responsible for the ‘‘eukaryotic’’ arrangement of 16- and 18-carbon fatty acids in glycerolipids are selective for unsaturated fatty acids rather than chain length [17]. Petunia plants were also transformed with a D9 fatty acid desaturase gene from Saccharomyces cerevisiae using Agrobacterium tumefaciens. An increase in the level of monounsaturated 16:1 and 18:1 fatty acids was found in transformed leaves [16]. Despite the changes in lipid composition, other phenotypic differences were not apparent.

Tomato plants (Solanum lycopersicum Mill.) transformed with the yeast D9 desaturase gene were found to display enhanced resistance to powdery mildew (Erysiphe polygoni) and to possess higher levels of 16:1, 16:2 and 18:1 fatty acids. Lipid peroxides also increased [16,19]. The improved resistance of the transgenic plants was associated with the increases in 16:1 and 16:2 fatty acids. Expression of the yeast D9 desaturase gene in eggplant (Solanum melongena L. var. hibush) also increased levels of 16:1, 18:1 and 16:3 fatty acids and enhanced resistance to Verticillium dahliae. Eggplants responded to V. dahliae inoculation with transitory upsurges in phospholipase $\mathsf { A } _ { 2 }$ and lipoxygenase activities, as well as increased levels of free fatty acids and lipid peroxides. The increase in certain fatty acids, particularly 16:1 and 16:3, were more pronounced in the transgenic plants [16,20]. The 16:1D9 directly inhibited growth of V. dahliae suggesting that increasing 16:1 in plants could be an approach to enhance their resistance to fungal diseases.

Various tissues of Nicotiana tabacum plants transformed with a rat stearyl-CoA desaturase gene were thoroughly analyzed for alterations of individual lipids in different subcellular sites. Western blot analysis indicated that the animal desaturase was targeted to the

microsomes, i.e. ER [15]. The 16:1D9 was incorporated into both the sn-1 and sn-2 positions of all the major membrane lipids tested, indicating that the endoplasmic reticulum acyltransferases do not exclude unsaturated C-16 fatty acids from the sn-2 position but instead discriminate against saturated fatty acids. In addition to increases in monounsaturated and decreases in saturated fatty acids, accumulation of 16:1 was accompanied by a reduction in 18:3 in all the lipids tested except phosphatidylglycerol and increases in 18:2 in phospholipids. Total C-16 fatty acid content in the galactolipids of the CoA desaturase expressing plants was significantly higher than that in controls, but those in the phospholipids were unchanged. The 18:1D11 was detected in the sn-1 position of the lipids tested except phosphatidylinositol and phosphatidylserine. The 16:1D9 not 16:1D7 was also largely synthesized in the transgenic plants expressing a mammalian or yeast D9D in tobacco or tomato [18,79]. Introduction of the animal desaturase, controlled by a seed-specific phaseolin promoter, into soybean somatic embryo resulted in a significant reduction in saturated fatty acids. Such effects were greater in cotyledons than hypocotyl-radicles [80,81]. Nearly $5 0 \%$ of the palmitic acid of soybean somatic embryos was converted into palmitoleic acid with good expression of a D9-CoA desaturase [80]. The transformed embryos had 16:1 levels from $0 \%$ to over $1 0 \%$ of total fatty acids, while the levels of 16:0 dropped from $2 5 \%$ to approximately $5 \%$ of total fatty acids. This study demonstrated that the animal desaturase can be used to decrease the levels of saturated fatty acids in a crop plant.

D9 Desaturase genes isolated from 16:1-high accumulators were also used in the transgenic plants to increase 16:1D9 synthesis. Cahoon et al. (1997 [69] and 1998 [82]) isolated a cDNA clone encoding a 16:0-specific D9-ACP desaturase from developing seeds of cat’s claw (Dolichandra unguis-cati (L.) L.G. Lohmann) and milkweed (Asclepias syriaca). In prior literature cat’s claw, Dolichandra unguis-cati, has been classified as Batocydia unguis, Batocydia unguis-cati, Bignonia unguis-cati, Doxantha unguis-cati L. and Macfadyena unguis-cati (http://www.itis.gov/servlet/SingleRpt/SingleRpt?search_topic= TSN&search_value=832955; http://www.theplantlist.org/tpl/record/kew-320930). Heterologous expression of the milkweed desaturase in Arabidopsis failed to produce detectable x-7 fatty acids (16:1D9 plus 18:1D11). However, cat’s claw desaturase expression led to x-7 fatty acids synthesis in Arabidopsis and Brassica napus although the FA content was only ${ \sim } 9 \%$ [14], indicating variant desaturases from divergent species works differently in the transgenic host plants. In general, transformation of Arabidopsis resulted in a greater change in the FA composition of the seed oil than for B. napus [14]. An increase 16:1 was obtained in transgenic lines along with other effects on lipid metabolism. In Arabidopsis, there was a large increase in the proportions of 18:1D11 and cis-D13- eicosenoic acid (20:1D13), likely generated through elongation of 16:1D9. Elongation of 18:1D11 to 20:1D13, however, was not observed in B. napus indicating that certain aspects of lipid metabolism in the model plant, Arabidopsis, may not apply to B. napus. The appearance of 18:1D11 was accompanied by a decrease in oleic acid (18:1D9). Although the introduced ACP-desaturase resulted in synthesis of some unsaturated FAs, the overall saturated FA content was maintained at similar levels to the control or was enhanced. Increased levels of saturation were mainly associated with an increase in stearic acid, which unlike 16:0, is considered non-atherogenic. The results suggest that a mechanism exists further downstream in oil biosynthesis to counteract the decrease in saturation brought about by 16:0-ACP-desaturase action.

# 6.3. Systematic engineering for increasing palmitoleate biosynthesis in plant seeds

The information described above suggests that various D9Ds have different activities for 16:1D9 synthesis. The expression of

D9D alone, either from animals, yeast or 16:1-high accumulators, generally only produce 16:1D9 of only $\leqslant 1 0 \%$ far below that of high accumulating source plants. Additional metabolic modifications are required for increasing $1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ accumulation in the host oil plants. Enhancing 16:0 substrate may benefit D9D to form more 16:1D9. Mutants with higher 16:0 can be obtained by blocking the reaction of 16:0 to 18:0 in normal fatty acid synthesis pathway. Naturally, 16:0-ACP can either be converted to 16:1D9-ACP or directly exported out of plastid. Coexpression of ACP- and CoA-desaturases respectively in plastids and cytosol may convert most of 16:0 into $1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ . For high accumulation of $1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ in engineered plant seeds, systematic engineering strategies to modify multiple steps involved in the fatty acid biosynthesis pathway are needed.

Nyguyen et al. [13] combined modifications on several targets to optimize 16:1D9 biosynthesis in model plant Arabidopsis seeds. They used an enzyme evolution approach to develop a castor 18:0D9-ACP desaturase (namely Com25) with the highest 16:0- ACP desaturase activity. Seed-specific expression of this engineered enzyme in Arabidopsis results in $1 4 \%$ x-7 FA $( 2 \% 1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ and $1 2 \%$ of its elongation products, 18:1D11 and 20:1D13), $4 \%$ higher than in the cats claw 16:0D9-ACP desaturase-expressing plants. Then, they selected an elevated 16:0 mutant (fab1) in which KASII (elongating 16:0-ACP to 18:0-ACP) is reduced, and 16:0 level $( 2 0 \% )$ is double that in wild-type seeds. Expression of Com25 in such lines with reduced elongation of 16:0 led to $3 9 \%$ x-7 FA synthesis $( 2 3 \% 1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ and $1 6 \% 1 8 { : } 1 \Delta 1 1$ ). FAE1 is responsible for the elongation of 18:1D11 to 20:1D13 in cytoplasm. This mutant (fae1) also accumulates high 16:0. They combined these two mutants to obtain a double mutant which contained more 16:0 $( 2 6 \% )$ . Expression of Com25 in this double mutant yielded $\sim 5 0 \%$ x-7 FA synthesis $( 2 6 \% 1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ $2 6 \%$ and $23 \%$ 18:1D11). To convert 16:0-CoA to $1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ in the cytoplasm, they further co-expressed two fungal acyl-CoA D9 desaturases in the ER along with Com25 in plastids in elevated 16:0 double mutants. Notably, x-7 FAs accumulated up to $67 \%$ ( ${ \sim } 4 3 \%$ 16:1D9 and $\sim 2 3 \%$ 18:1D11) on average in the transgenic plants, but 16:0 still remained ${ \sim } 1 1 \%$ . These approaches developed in Arabidopsis are also being used for engineering Camelina sativa, and similar results are reported for this oilseed that can be grown commercially [37,38], representing significant progress in the field of 16:1 metabolic engineering. Other oilseeds such as soybeans can be similarly metabolically engineered.

Emerging data evidence indicates that the packaging of FA into TAG plays important roles in high accumulation of unusual fatty acids in plant seeds [83–87]. DGATs (particularly DGAT2) and other acyltransferases (e.g. LPA) may be further targets to be modified for optimizing the accumulation of 16:1D9 in common oil crops so that the engineered oil crops can be used for commercial production of palmitoleate. As noted, it might be necessary to replace the competing endogenous machinery that is likely specific for the endogenous PC, DAG and acyl-CoA species for maximum accumulation of unusual FAs in TAG in heterologous systems. ACP thioesterase could be another target for 16:1 metabolic engineering since it was found that some FatAs have high specificity with 16:1D9. For example, macadamia FatA (MtFatA) displayed stronger specificity with 16:1D9 and lower specificity with 16:0-ACP than MtFatB [88]. Similarly, castor FatA (RcFatA) exhibited high specificity to palmitoleoyl-ACP (16:1D9-ACP) and oleoyl-ACP(18:1D9ACP) while RcFatB showed high specificity to palmitoyl-ACP(16:0-ACP) and oleoyl-ACP(18:1D9ACP) [89].

# 7. Conclusions and perspectives

Over the past decade, substantial advances have been made in the understanding of plant oil biosynthesis and the metabolic engineering for producing high-value fatty acids using transgenic

plants. In case of palmitoleate, an important health contributor and new sustainable source of oleochemical-derived products of 1-octene, promising progress has been achieved in seed oil of a model system plant that may be applicable to commercial oilseeds.

As described above, the major target for metabolic engineering for 16:1 biosynthesis has been D9 desaturase. However, expression of this enzyme alone only results in moderate accumulation of 16:1 D9 in the transgenic plants. A combination of expression of D9D in both plastids and ER together with high 16:0 levels by reducing KASII lead to high accumulation of $1 6 { : } 1 \Delta 9$ in model plant Arabidopsis and a potential oil crop Camelina. No such results have been obtained in common oil crops.

Further optimization of the process will be facilitated by a better understanding of 16:1 biosynthesis in plant seeds, which includes not only the biochemistry of the individual enzymes and subcellular organization of those enzymes, but also the regulatory network throughout seed development. Findings from seeds engineered for other unusual fatty acids suggest that specialized enzymes have evolved for the metabolism of unusual fatty acids from their site of synthesis on phosphatidlcholine to their storage in TAGs [90,91]. These enzymes are presumably absent from seeds of commercial oil crops such as soybean that do not normally produce unusual fatty acids. These enzymes may include specialized phospholipases, acyltransferases, and enzymes associated with the removal or transfer of phospholipid head groups.

Tung tree DGATs were found to work in distinct ER domains [92], indicating acyltransferases and associated proteins might need to be targeted to special domains dedicated to the synthesis of membrane or storage lipids. Similarly, confocal microscopy studies in HeLa cells have also recently shown co-localization of stearoyl-CoA desaturase and an acyl-CoA-dependent DGAT [93], showing the possibility of substrate channeling into the oil droplet. Apparently, the correct assembly of the related enzymes in ER special domains may be important for high unusual fatty acid accumulation in oil bodies. It is not known how significant these observations are in the context of 16:1 D9 biosynthesis. Increasing attention should be paid to understanding the enzymatic steps and substrate specificities that such organisms use to achieve high synthesis and accumulation of unusual fatty acids, and to cloning genes that encode the enzymes involved.

To gain a global perspective of lipid metabolism in high natural accumulators of 16:1 such as in Macadamia and cat’s claw seeds, a combination of lipidomics and transcriptomics analysis could provide a good experimental platform to identify candidate genes that are associated with the synthesis of 16:1 and the efficient metabolism of 16:1 from its synthesis on PC to storage in TAG. Moreover, comparative mining the publicly available genome data bases such as castor, soybean and Arabidopsis could generate useful information for studies in this regard. Taken together, these further approaches will establish a more complete knowledge base for the rational metabolic engineering of the sustainable production of palmitoleate in oilseed crops. This could lead to economical large scale production of palmitoleate for health and renewable chemical applications.
