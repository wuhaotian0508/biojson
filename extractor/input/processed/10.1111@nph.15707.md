# Summary

New Phytologist (2019) 223: 113-117  
doi: 10.1111/nph.15707

Key words: fatty acid, jasmonate, lipid signaling, oxylipin, phospholipid, salicylate, sphingolipid, sterol.

Plant lipid signals are crucial developmental modulators and stress response mediators. A family of acyl-CoA-binding proteins (ACBPs) participates in the lipid trafficking of these signals. Isoform-specific functions can arise from differences in their subcellular distribution, tissue-specificity, stress-responsiveness, and ligand selectivity. In lipid-mediated cell signaling, plant ACBPs are not merely transporters but are also important regulators via their interaction with lipid-metabolic enzymes and precursor lipids. In this Insight, the regulatory roles of plant ACBPs in the synthesis of various signaling lipids, including phosphatidic acid, sterols, oxylipins, and sphingolipids, are reviewed. We focus on the functional significance of these lipid signals in plant development and stress responses with an overview of recent work using reverse genetics and transgenic Arabidopsis.

# I. Introduction

Lipids function not only as cellular membrane constituents and carbon reserves but are also mobile messengers in signal transduction. Plants generate a variety of signaling lipids, including phospholipids (Heilmann, 2016; Hong et al., 2016; Nakamura, 2017), sphingolipids (Sánchez-Rangel et al., 2015; Michaelson et al., 2016), fatty acids (FAs; Lim et al., 2017), oxylipins (Dave & Graham, 2012; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018), and sterols (Valitova et al., 2016). Lipid signals are biosynthesized or derived from precursors (e.g. membrane lipids, FAs) in response to stimuli (e.g. hormones, growth factors, external cues) by many

lipid-metabolic enzymes during plant growth, development, and adaptation (Chen et al., 2014; Hong et al., 2016; Michaelson et al., 2016; Valitova et al., 2016; Xue et al., 2016; Lim et al., 2017; Nakamura, 2017; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018). Numerous branches of the acyl-lipid metabolic network involve multiple organelles, necessitating metabolite exchange via intracellular trafficking. As an acyl source for lipid biosynthesis, free FAs are transported and metabolized in the form of acyl-CoA esters (Du et al., 2016; Lung & Chye, 2016a).

In all eukaryotic cells and certain pathogenic eubacteria, a cytosolic acyl-CoA pool is maintained by members of the acyl-CoA-binding proteins (ACBPs; Du et al., 2016; Lung & Chye,

2016a). Besides housekeeping members, plant ACBPs can be expressed in different tissues, respond to abiotic and biotic stresses, and are sorted to various subcellular locations (Du et al., 2016; Lung & Chye, 2016b). Together with their differential ligand selectivity as governed by protein structure (Guo et al., 2017), ACBP isoforms possess different functions (Lung & Chye, 2016c). In the past two decades, their roles have been elucidated in plant lipid metabolism. These are related to lipid storage (e.g. lipid droplets in seeds and pollen) and structures (e.g. cuticle and pollen exine) (Xue et al., 2014; Hsiao et al., 2015; Ye et al., 2017). Reverse genetics and studies using transgenic Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) have also revealed the multifaceted functions of ACBPs in signal transduction. Interestingly, ACBPs are not simply transporters but also act as signaling regulators. Their protein-protein interaction with lipid-metabolic enzymes has recently emerged to constitute a core component in the regulatory mechanism (Fig. 1). In other instances, plant ACBPs bind precursor lipids to regulate substrate availability for the generation of downstream signals. In this Insight, we review our current knowledge of the regulatory roles of plant ACBPs in the synthesis of signaling lipids, including phosphatidic acid (PA), sterols, oxylipins and sphingolipids.

# II. Overview of Arabidopsis ACBPs

The characteristics and functions of Arabidopsis ACBPs have been recently reviewed (Du et al., 2016; Lung & Chye, 2016a,b,c). Briefly, they are grouped by the size and domain architecture into Class I small ACBP (AtACBP6), Class II ankyrin-repeat-containing ACBPs (AtACBP1 and AtACBP2), Class III large ACBP (AtACBP3) and Class IV kelch-ACBPs (AtACBP4 and AtACBP5). AtACBP6 is the smallest $(10\mathrm{kDa})$ prototype, in contrast to the other five members $(38 - 73\mathrm{kDa})$ comprising sorting signals and/or protein regions accompanying the acyl-CoA-binding domain (Meng et al., 2011). Arabidopsis ACBPs are located at the endoplasmic reticulum and plasma membrane (AtACBP1 and AtACBP2), apoplast (AtACBP3), and cytosol (AtACBP4–AtACBP6) (Lung & Chye, 2016b). By measurement of binding kinetics using isothermal titration calorimetry, all isoforms were shown to bind long-chain (LC) acyl-CoA esters, albeit at varying affinities, whereas AtACBP1 and AtACBP3 exhibited high affinity to very-long-chain (VLC) species, and AtACBP3–AtACBP6 to medium-chain species (Lung & Chye, 2016c; Chen et al., 2018). In addition, all members bind phosphatidylcholine (PC), and AtACBP1, AtACBP2, and AtACBP3 also bind PA, lysoPC, and phosphatidylethanolamine, respectively (Lung & Chye, 2016c). The ankyrin repeats of Class II ACBPs have been shown, and kelch motifs of Class IV ACBPs have been proposed to interact with protein partners, ranging from heavy-metal-binding protein and transcription factors to phospholipid hydrolases and sterol biosynthetic enzymes (Lung & Chye, 2016c; Lung et al., 2017, 2018; Chen et al., 2018).

# III. PA signaling

PA is a second messenger in stress and hormonal responses, and its accumulation is triggered by abscisic acid (ABA) (Pokotylo et al.,

2018). Signaling PA, which promotes ABA responses, can be generated via several pathways, including phosphorylation of diacylglycerol by diacylglycerol kinase, hydrolysis of phosphoinositide by phosphoinositide-specific phospholipase C, and degradation of structural phospholipids by phospholipase D (PLD; Heilmann, 2016; Pokotylo et al., 2018). AtACBP1 facilitates the latter reaction via interaction with PLDα1 and its catalytic product, PA (Fig. 1; Du et al., 2013a). Whereas ABA or drought treatment upregulated AtACBP1, PA accumulation in AtACBP1 overexpressors rendered freshly harvested seeds more dormant, and afterripened seeds showed greater ABA sensitivity, consistent with the upregulation of PLDα1 and other ABA/stress-responsive genes in seedlings (Du et al., 2013a). The reciprocal atacbp1 phenotypes further substantiated the regulatory role of AtACBP1 in the synthesis of signaling PA at germinative and seedling stages (Du et al., 2013a). Its homologue, AtACBP2, is also involved in ABAMediated drought responses (Du et al., 2013b). ABA signaling genes were misregulated in AtACBP2 overexpressors, and the accumulation of ABA-mediated reactive oxygen species promoted stomatal closure leading to reduced transpiration loss and higher drought tolerance (Du et al., 2013b).

AtACBP1 is also associated with ABA-mediated stress responses. Under salinity and osmotic stresses, AtACBP1-overexpressing seeds were less germinable in contrast to atacbp1 seeds, which adapted better than the wild-type (Chen et al., 2018). These phenotypes were rescued by the application of an ABA biosynthesis inhibitor, fluridone (Chen et al., 2018). In the AtACBP1 overexpressors, $ABA$ -RESPONSIVE ELEMENT BINDING PROTEIN1 (AREB1) and its downstream targets were upregulated (Chen et al., 2018). Intriguingly, AtACBP1 overexpression appeared to enable more membrane-associated docking sites for tethering AREB1 via protein-protein interaction, and its release into the nucleus under stresses will elicit a stronger ABA-mediated response (Fig. 1; Chen et al., 2018). By analogy, AtACBP1 and AtACBP2 bind a group VII ethylene response factor, RELATED TO APETALA2.12 (RAP2.12), such that hypoxia triggers its release from the plasma membrane into the nucleus for responsive gene activation (Fig. 1; Licausi et al., 2011). AtACBP2 also interacts with another homologue, RAP2.3 (Li & Chye, 2004). Whether such RAP2.12 and RAP2.3 interactions mediate other functions of AtACBP1 and AtACBP2 in lipid signaling remains to be explored. Although AtACBP1-facilitated synthesis of PA during cold acclimation is beneficial to membrane stabilization from a structural perspective (Du et al., 2010), an alternative explanation in respect to signal transduction awaits better characterization of PA in cold stress signaling.

# IV. Sterol-mediated signaling

Whereas animals and fungi produce one principal sterol, a diversity of plant sterols forms the basis for more specific functions as signaling molecules (Valitova et al., 2016). The sterol C4-demethylation complex also produces sterol biosynthetic intermediates that negatively regulate polar auxin transport (Mialoundama et al., 2013). The misregulation of sterol synthesis is detrimental, because the atacbp1smo1-1 double mutant was arrested during early embryogenesis, and SMO1-2 silencing in acbp1 led to proembryo abortion and gametophytic defects (Lung et al., 2017, 2018). The hemizygous atacbp1 mutant produced high-oil, low-mucilage seeds upon SMO1-1 or SMO1-2 knockdown, and AtACBP1 mutation and/or SMO1-2 silencing also affected trichome initiation and branching (Lung et al., 2017, 2018). zipper (HD-ZIP) IV transcription factors, GL2 possesses the StAR-related lipid transfer (START) domain that putatively binds sterols and phospholipids (Schrick et al., 2014). As knockdown of SMO1-1, SMO1-2, and/or AtACBP1 misregulated the genes encoding GL2, its downstream targets, and other HD-ZIP IV members, AtACBP1-SMO1 interaction may regulate the synthesis of unknown lipid signal(s) that serve as modulators of downstream effectors such as HD-ZIP IV proteins (Lung et al., 2017, 2018). A better understanding of plant START domain ligands and other HD-ZIP IV members will provide critical insights into the regulatory role of AtACBP1 in sterol-mediated signaling.

# V. Oxylipin signaling

Oxylipins comprise a multitude of oxygenated FA derivatives, including jasmonic acid and related metabolites (e.g. methyl jasmonate and jasmonate-soleucine), which act as stress and developmental signals (Dave & Graham, 2012; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018). Their synthesis begins within the plastids with the oxidation of plastidial linolenic acid to form cis-(+)-12-oxo

phytodienoic acid (cis-OPDA) (Dave & Graham, 2012), which is a jasmonate intermediate and signaling molecule (Taki et al., 2005). COMATOSE (CTS) transports OPDA in its free acid or CoA-activated form into the peroxisome (Schaller et al., 2008), where it is reduced and converted into jasmonates via three rounds of $\beta$ -oxidation (Dave & Graham, 2012).

AtACBP3 and AtACBP6 are linked to jasmonate synthesis (Ye et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2018). Ye et al. (2016) demonstrated that the phloem exudates of atacbp6 contained lower linoleic acid, linolenic acid and jasmonates but higher cis-OPDA. The downregulation of CTS in atacbp6 rosettes implicates that AtACBP6 may facilitate CTS-mediated OPDA transport, although the binding of AtACBP6 with OPDA-CoA has not been verified (Ye et al., 2016). While jasmonate synthesis is inducible by wound signals from a distal site (Dave & Graham, 2012; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018), AtACBP6 promoter activity was also upregulated systemically by wounding (Ye et al., 2016), corroborating the tight correlation of AtACBP6 and oxylipin signaling. The role of AtACBP3 in the jasmonate-mediated defense response has been better substantiated (Hu et al., 2018). Jasmonate-responsive marker genes were induced less significantly in locally wounded and distal leaves of atacbp3 and RNA interference (RNAi) lines than the wild-type (Hu et al., 2018). The phloem exudates of atacbp3 and AtACBP3-RNAi contained lower methyl jasmonate and precursor FAs, and micrografting indicated that AtACBP3 is phloem-mobile (Hu et al., 2018). Thus, it appears that cytosolic AtACBP6 and extracellularly localized AtACBP3 cooperate to regulate jasmonate synthesis by influencing FA and oxylipin composition in the phloem via symplastic and apoplastic transport, respectively (Ye et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2018).

# VI. Sphingolipid signaling

Sphingolipids are a class of complex lipids derived from ceramides that feature the linkage of a sphingoid base to an LC- or VLC-acyl moiety (Sanchez-Rangel et al., 2015; Michaelson et al., 2016). VLCFA-enriched sphingolipid synthesis is modulated by AtACBP3, which binds VLC acyl-CoA esters (Xie et al., 2015). Under submergence, VLCFA-containing glycosyl inositol phosphoceramides are elevated in wild-type Arabidopsis and more notably in atacbp3 and AtACBP3-RNAi, contrasting their decline in AtACBP3 overexpressors (Xie et al., 2015). AtACBP3 overexpressors were hypersensitive to submergence and ethanolic stresses, whereas atacbp3 and AtACBP3-RNAi were more tolerant than the wild-type, suggesting a regulatory role of AtACBP3 in VLC-sphingolipid biosynthesis during hypoxia signaling (Xie et al., 2015). Recent studies indicated that sphingolipid metabolism is linked to salicylate signaling, as several sphingolipid intermediates trigger salicylate accumulation and influence disease resistance (Sanchez-Rangel et al., 2015). Consistently, AtACBP3-mediated regulation of hypoxic response correlates with salicylate signaling given that the hypoxia-sensitive phenotype of AtACBP3 overexpressors could be rescued in a salicylate nonresponsive mutant, npr1-5 (Xie et al., 2015). Although atacbp3 failed to generate signal (s) for inducing systemic acquired resistance (Xia et al., 2012), the resistance of AtACBP3 overexpressors to the bacterial pathogen

Pseudomonas syringae was dependent on NPR1-mediated signaling (Xiao & Chye, 2011). Interestingly, the accelerated leaf senescence phenotype of AtACBP3 overexpressors was invariably dependent on salicylate signaling but not jasmonate signaling, despite alteration in oxylipin composition (Xiao et al., 2010). Thus, it is plausible that AtACBP3 interaction with LC- (Hu et al., 2018) and VLC-acyl-CoA esters (Xie et al., 2015) is pertinent to jasmonate, sphingolipid and salicylate signaling in a ligand-specific manner.

# VII. Conclusions and future perspectives

Evidence is accumulating that discrete members of the Arabidopsis ACBP family exert isoform-specific and cooperative functions to modulate lipid signal synthesis. In some cases, the regulatory roles are mediated by protein-protein interaction of ACBPs with lipid-metabolic enzymes. Previously, site-directed mutagenesis demonstrated that the ligand-binding activity of AtACBP1 is crucial for its interaction with protein partners (Lung et al., 2017, 2018). Accordingly, a lipid-sensing function is proposed for plant ACBPs, such that their ligand-binding status reflects the abundance of certain acyl-CoA or phospholipid species in plant cells. This cellular cue is conveyed to downstream lipid signal generators when liganded ACBPs modulate the activity of interacting enzymes. Possibly, ACBPs not only act as signal transducers but also represent potential branch points in the complex lipid signaling network. For instance, AtACBP1 may regulate the generation of phospholipid and sterol signals at the same time via its interaction with PLDα1 (Du et al., 2013a) and SMO1 (Lung et al., 2017, 2018), respectively. This working model is to be validated in future research on the protein-protein and protein-lipid interactions from a structural perspective. In other instances, plant ACBPs influence the availability of acyl-CoA precursors for downstream lipid signal synthesis. Given the complexity of lipid signaling that involves numerous crosstalk between proteins, hormones, metabolites, and other forms of molecular signals (Valitova et al., 2016; Nolan et al., 2017; Pokotylo et al., 2018), the versatility of plant ACBPs in phospholipid, sterol, oxylipin, and sphingolipid signaling implicates their importance for serving as critical regulatory nodes in the signaling network.
