# Summary

High light stress is an important factor limiting crop yield. Light receptors play an important role in the response to high light stress, but their mechanisms are still poorly understood. Here, we found that the abundance of GmPLP1, a positive blue light receptor protein, was significantly inhibited by high light stress and mainly responded to high blue light. GmPLP1 RNA-interference soybean lines exhibited higher light energy utilization ability and less light damage and reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation in leaves under high light stress, while the phenotype of GmPLP1:GmPLP1-Flag overexpression soybean showed the opposite characteristics. Then, we identified a protein–protein interaction between GmPLP1 and GmVTC2, and the intensity of this interaction was primarily affected by sensing the intensity of blue light. More importantly, overexpression of GmVTC2b improved soybean tolerance to high light stress by enhancing the ROS scavenging capability through increasing the biosynthesis of ascorbic acid. This regulation was significantly enhanced after interfering with a GmPLP1-interference fragment in GmVTC2box soybean leaves, but was weakened when GmPLP1 was transiently overexpressed. These findings demonstrate that GmPLP1 regulates the photosynthetic capacity and ROS accumulation of soybean to adapt to changes in light intensity by sensing blue light. In summary, this study discovered a new mechanism through which GmPLP1 participates in high light stress in soybean, which has great significance for improving soybean yield and the adaptability of soybean to high light. 

# Introduction

Light is one of the most important environmental factors affecting plant growth (Kami et al., 2010), development (El-Assal et al., 2001) and abiotic stress resistance (Kleine et al., 2007). Plants sense day length, light quality and light spectra through light receptors. The light information ranging from UV-A to far-red is perceived primarily by five photoreceptor families: UV-B receptor (UVR8) (Rizzini et al., 2011), cryptochromes (CRYs) (Cashmore et al., 1999), phototropins (PHOTs) (Briggs et al., 2001), ZEITLUPE family proteins (ZTLs) (Somers et al., 2000) and phytochromes (PHYs) (Quail et al., 1995) in Arabidopsis thaliana. 

Ogura et al. (2008) first found that the Per-ARNT-Sim (PAS)/ light, oxygen or voltage (LOV) protein (PLP) may be a novel blue light receptor protein in plants. Then, the expression of natural and mutated PLP proteins in Escherichia coli showed that both LOV domains could bind FMN. All four LOV domains of PLP1 and PLP2 show typical LOV domain characteristics, including active cysteine. A novel LOV-containing domain with typical biochemical and photochemical characteristics similar to phototropins in plants was discovered (Kasahara et al., 2010). Two recent 

advances have confirmed this: GUS activity was up-regulated under darkness and blue light and was driven by the GmPLP1 promoter in proGmPLP1:GUS transgenic soybean and Nicotiana benthamiana (Luo et al., 2013). Furthermore, GmPLP1 expression was mainly induced by blue light, and the overexpression of GmPLP1 in A. thaliana significantly inhibited hypocotyl elongation under blue light (Li et al., 2018). 

Under normal light, plants convert light energy into chemical energy to provide material and energy for growth and development. When the light intensity exceeds the requirements of plants, high light stress occurs. High light stress causes photooxidative cell damage and photoinhibition, which greatly restrict plant growth and development. The plant response to high light stress mainly includes: the improvement of photosynthetic capacity (Demmig-Adams and Adams, 1992), chloroplast light-avoidance movement (Sakai et al., 2001), the heat dissipation pathway (Niyogi, 1999), REDOX regulation (Escoubas et al., 1995) and the reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging system. Superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), peroxidase (POD) and some small molecules, including ascorbic acid (AsA), glutathione, tocopherol (vitamin E), flavonoids and carotenoids, 

are important in the scavenging of ROS produced by high light stress. 

Ascorbic acid is an important antioxidant that helps control the redox state and antioxidant activity of cells (Shi et al., 2022). Plants use three main pathways for ASA biosynthesis, the Lgalactose pathway (Wheeler et al., 1998), D-galacturonic acid pathway (Isherwood et al., 1953) and inositol pathway (Valpuesta and Botella, 2004). The L-galactose pathway is considered to be the main pathway for AsA accumulation in plants (Wheeler et al., 1998). This pathway converts D-fructose 6-phosphate to AsA through eight enzymes, including phosphomannose isomerase (PMI), phosphor mannose mutase (PMM), GDP-D-Man pyrophosphorylase (GMP), GDP-D-Man-30 ,50 -epimerase (GME), GDP-L-Gal phosphorylase/L-Gal guanylyl transferase (GGP), L-Gal-1-phosphate phosphatase (GPP), L-Gal dehydrogenase (L-GalDH) and L-GalL dehydrogenase (L-GalLDH). Many studies have shown that GGP is an important regulatory factor in AsA biosynthesis (Bulley et al., 2009; Mellidou et al., 2012). 

L-Gal guanylyl transferase (VTC2/5) encodes a GDP-L-galactose phosphatase, a key enzyme in the sixth step of the L-galactose pathway, which catalyses the conversion of GDP-L-galactose to Lgalactose L-phosphate. Phenotypic analysis showed that GGP was the first enzyme to be fully committed to AsA synthesis, as growth arrest occurred in vtc2/vtc5 double mutants, and downstream mutants were rescued by AsA supplementation (Dowdle et al., 2007). Transient expression of single or a combination of Smirnoff-Wheeler pathway enzymes in N. benthamiana increased the AsA concentration only with the inclusion of GGP (Fenech et al., 2021). Studies have demonstrated that the AsA concentration in A. thaliana leaves is correlated with the expression of the GGP gene under different illumination conditions (Laing et al., 2017). The GGP gene exhibits obvious diurnal variation, with its expression being higher in the morning and decreasing thereafter, which is consistent with the variation trend of AsA content in A. thaliana (Bulley, 2016; Dowdle et al., 2007). Moreover, the translation of VTC2 was feedback-inhibited in the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -Untranslated Region through a conservative upstream open reading framework (uORF) (Laing et al., 2015). 

Previous studies have found that photoreceptors such as PHOT2 (Christie, 2007; Jarillo et al., 2001), CRY2 (Kleine et al., 2007) and UVR8 (Favory et al., 2009; Morales et al., 2013) participate in high light stress, while PLP has not been reported. Herein, we demonstrated that GmPLP1 regulates the photosynthetic capacity and ROS accumulation of soybean to adapt to changes in light intensity through sensing blue light. These findings have great significance for improving the adaptability of soybean to regions characterized by light stress. 

# Results

# Analysis of GmPLP1 protein expression patterns

In order to verify whether GmPLP1 had the same function as other photoreceptors (Christie, 2007; Favory et al., 2009; Jarillo et al., 2001; Kleine et al., 2007; Morales et al., 2013) involved in the response to high light stress, this study first analysed the expression pattern of the GmPLP1 gene under high light stress. The mRNA expression abundance of GmPLP1 significantly decreased under high white light (HWL) compared with low white light (LWL), reaching the lowest point at 8 h (Figure 1A). Then, this work further explored the expression pattern at the 

protein level by constructing an overexpressed soybean model (GmPLP1:GmPLP1-Flag) of the GmPLP1 self-promoter. We characterized three independent ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ homozygous overexpression (GmPLP1-ox) transgenic lines, as confirmed by RT-qPCR and immunoblot analysis (Figure S1). Immunoassay showed that the expression of the GmPLP1 protein increased at first and then decreased rapidly after $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ under HWL (Figure 1B). Past studies have found that blue light receptors fine-regulate many physiological responses of plants in a light intensity-dependent and quality-dependent manner (Lin et al., 1998; Sakai et al., 2001). This work analysed GmPLP1 protein expression under different intensities of blue and red light. The expression abundance of the GmPLP1 protein was significantly increased under low blue light (LBL), but significantly decreased under high blue light (HBL), and could hardly be detected at $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ (Figure 1C). The expression abundance of the GmPLP1 protein was not induced by low red light (LRL), whereas it was significantly decreased under high red light (HRL), but not as significantly as under HBL (Figure 1C). The above results suggest that GmPLP1 may be involved in the response to high light stress and mainly responds to HBL. 

# GmPLP1 participates in the response to high light stress through regulating the photosynthetic capacity and ROS accumulation in soybean

To test our hypothesis, the GmPLP1-RNAi (GmPLP1-i) transgenic soybean seedlings were also subjected to phenotypic observation. The GmPLP1-i soybean was identified by PCR, RT-qPCR and bar test strips (Figure S2). Previous studies have found that high light stress can lead to light inhibition and then affect the ability of plant leaves to use light energy. This study first measured the light response curves of the WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i transgenic soybean plants under $0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathsf { s }$ light intensity. The results showed that when the light intensity reached the low light zone $( 0 { - } 5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { \ m o l / m } ^ { 2 } / \mathsf { s } )$ , there was no significant difference in the net photosynthetic rate (Pn) among all plants. The Pn continued to increase with the light intensity, and the Pn and light saturation point of GmPLP1-i plants were significantly higher than those of WT plants, while the Pn and light saturation point in the overexpression plants decreased significantly (Figure 2A), suggesting that GmPLP1-i plants had higher utilization of light energy. When the light energy absorbed by plant leaves exceeds their needs, the excess light energy will cause the photochemical efficiency rate of PS II to decrease (expressed by the maximum fluorescence $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ and produce ROS (Kok, 1956; Nielsen, 1952). Therefore, this work further measured the $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ and ROS content in WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i plants. The $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values in the leaves of the GmPLP1-ox plants were significantly decreased compared with WT, demonstrating obvious light damage, while the leaves of GmPLP1-i exhibited no significant change under high light stress after $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Most of the leaves of the GmPLP1-ox plants suffered severe light damage compared with WT, while the GmPLP1-i plants suffered only slight light damage under high light stress after 6 h (Figure 2B,C). Further, $3 , 3 ^ { \prime }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) staining, nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT) staining and $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ detection in the WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i plants under high light stress for 6 h showed that the content of ROS in GmPLP1-ox soybean was significantly higher than that in WT, while that in the GmPLP1-i plants was significantly lower than that in WT (Figure 2D,E).

# GmVTC2b improves soybean tolerance to high light stress by reducing ROS accumulation

Previous studies have found that VTC2 responds to high light stress by regulating AsA biosynthesis in Arabidopsis (Dowdle et al., 2007; Yabuta et al., 2007). To verify whether GmVTC2b was involved in the response to high light stress, the overexpression of proGmVTC2b:GmVTC2b-FlagFlag (- GmVTC2b-ox) soybean was achieved and confirmed by RTqPCR and immunoblot analysis (Figure S6). Immunological analysis showed that the protein expression abundance was low under darkness and LWL. Under HWL, the protein expression showed a trend of continuous accumulation. The expression of the GmVTC2b protein was induced significantly under HWL (Figure 5A). This study further distinguished the expression pattern of GmVTC2b protein under different intensities of blue and red light. Immunoassay showed that the expression abundance of the GmVTC2b protein was not induced by LBL and LRL, whereas it was significantly increased under HBL and HRL. The above results suggest that GmVTC2b may be involved in the response to high light stress, and it responds to both HRL and HBL. Then, this work measured the total AsA content of GmVTC2b-ox and WT under LWL and HWL. The results showed that the content of the total AsA in GmVTC2b-ox transgenic soybean was not significantly different from that in WT under LWL, but the difference was more significant under HWL (Figure 5B). 

Furthermore, we observed the phenotype of the GmVTC2b-ox transgenic and WT soybean under high light stress. # GmPLP1 negatively regulates AsA biosynthesis by inhibiting GGP enzyme activity

To verify our conjecture, the total AsA content was first measured under LWL and HWL in the WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i transgenic seedlings. The results showed that overexpression of GmPLP1 had little effect on the endogenous total AsA content under LWL (Figure 6A), but under HWL, the total AsA content was significantly decreased compared with WT (Figure 6A). The GmPLP1-i plants increased the content of the total AsA under both LWL and HWL conditions compared with WT (Figure 6A). This showed that GmPLP1 negatively regulated AsA synthesis in response to different light intensities. Then, we overexpressed the GmVTC2b gene in the leaves of WT and GmPLP1-i soybean and further verified the relationship between GmPLP1 and GmVTC2b using the method described above (Wang et al., 2021). Immunoblot analysis revealed that pCAMBIA3300-GmVTC2b-GFP (GmVTC2b-ox) was expressed in WT and GmPLP1-i transgenic soybean (Figure S7C). Compared with EV, it was found that the leaves with overexpressed GmVTC2b exhibited significantly increased resistance to high light stress in both WT and GmPLP1-i soybean, but the ratio of AsA content in GmVTC2b leaves to EV in GmPLP1-i was significantly higher than that in WT (Figure 6B). Finally, GGP enzyme activity was analysed in WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i transgenic soybean under HWL. Compared to WT, the GGP enzyme activity was significantly inhibited in GmPLP1-ox transgenic soybean, and GmPLP1-i activity was significantly increased (Figure 6C). These results confirm that GmPLP1 inhibits the biosynthesis of AsA by inhibiting the activity of GGP and then leads to the negative regulation of high light resistance in soybean. In addition, we also analysed the expression level of GmVTC2b in the WT, GmPLP1-ox and GmPLP1-i transgenic plants under LWL and HWL. There was no significant difference in the expression of GmVTC2b under LWL, while GmVTC2b expression was significantly increased in GmPLP1-ox and significantly decreased in GmPLP1-i under HWL compared with WT (Figure S8). compared with GmPLP1-ox (Figure 6E). # Discussion

The growth and development of plants are influenced by various environmental factors, and the photosynthetic capacity plays a vital role in the accumulation of biomass and the formation of yield. Although light is the energy source of green plants, excessive light absorption beyond their utilization capacity can lead to the accumulation of excess excitation energy within the photosynthetic system, which will lead to the burst of ROS and the inhibition of photosynthesis. Drought, saline-alkali, high temperature and other common environmental stresses during crop growth will cause the decrease of photosynthesis, mainly manifested in stomatal closure, the burst of ROS, the decline of electron transport capacity and so on. When these stresses coincide with high light stress, it will further aggravate the damage caused by stress, resulting in photoinhibition, photooxidation and light damage. Ultimately, these combined stressors can cause irreversible damage to the cellular structure and function of plants, potentially leading to plant mortality (Allakhverdiev et al., 2002; Cavusoglu, 2007; Porcel et al., 2015; Salvucci and Crafts-Brandner, 2004; Santos, 2004; Yamamoto, 2016; Yue et al., 2023). In addition, fluctuating light is another mild but persistent type of light stress in nature. In the natural environment, the daily variation in photosynthetic photon flux density (PFD) can span 2–3 orders of magnitude, Particularly, when plants experiencing long-term low light conditions (continuous cloudy days, thick cloud cover, mutual shading among crops, etc.) suddenly encounter strong light, the rapid changes in light intensity can lead to imbalances in excitation PSI and PSII, resulting in reduced photochemical efficiency of photosynthesis, leading to photoinhibition or even photodamage (Hou et al., 2015). 

The rapid response mechanism of plants to high light stress and light fluctuation is crucial for their adaptation to changing environments (Gjindali et al., 2021; Morales and Kaiser, 2020; Shi et al., 2022). Previous studies have indicated that the light saturation point of soybean is typically between 40 000 and 60 000 Lux (Wang et al., 2023; Yang and Miao, 1983). Stresses such as fluctuating light and high light can significantly decrease the light saturation point and photosynthetic capacity of leaves, 

especially when multiple stresses occur simultaneously, it will seriously damage the growth and yield of soybean. In our previous studies, we demonstrated that the expression level of

This indicated that GmPLP1 suppressed the interaction between GmPLP1 and GmVTC2b through the blue light perception of the LOV domain and also suggested that the Cys and Arg sites were very important for the complete functioning of the LOV domain. Previous studies have found that FKF1 interacts with CO through the LOV domain, and blue light enhances their interaction in A. thaliana (Song et al., 2012). The interaction of ZTL-GI was significantly and deliberately enhanced in A. thaliana through the LOV domain under blue light (Kim et al., 2007). We found that the interaction of GmPLP1- GmVTC2b was inhibited under blue light. Sequence alignment of the LOV domain indicated that the LOV domains of FKF1 and ZTL were relatively conserved in soybean and Arabidopsis, but quite different from that of PLP (Figure S5). We speculated that this might be due to functional differences caused by differences in LOV domain sequences. The specific mechanism remains to be further studied. 

Increasing experimental evidence shows that GGP (VTC2) is a key rate-limiting step in the AsA biosynthesis pathway (Dowdle et al., 2007; Gao et al., 2011). In three independent studies, Escherichia coli-expressed recombinant Arabidopsis VTC2 (Dowdle et al., 2007; Linster et al., 2007) or a kiwifruit homologue thereof (Laing et al., 2007) were found to specifically convert GDP-L-galactose to L-galactose-1-P. Interestingly, VTC2 has no absolute specificity for its nucleotide sugar substrate, and the catalytic efficiency of GDP-L- galactose is similar to that of GDP-D- glucose (Linster et al., 2007). GDP-D-mannose and GDP-L-galactose are not only used for L-ascorbate formation but are also involved in the synthesis of cell wall polysaccharides and/or protein glycosylation (Reuhs et al., 2004; Smirnoff and Wheeler, 2000). Therefore, if the transcription, translation or enzyme activity of VTC2 are not regulated or restricted, it will consume a large amount of substrates and have a significant impact on plant growth and development. Although AsA is generally considered beneficial, extremely high concentrations can also have harmful effects (Deslous et al., 2021). Previous studies have found that GGP has a negative feedback regulation model at the level of protein translation, and the translation of VTC2 was feedbackinhibited in the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -Untranslated Region through a conservative upstream open reading framework (uORF) (Laing et al., 2015). However, the rate limit of its enzyme activity has not been reported. In this study, we proposed a new working model for 

PLP1-VTC2 protein pairs; that is, PLP1 limits the rate of GGP in terms of enzyme activity. Previous studies have found that the light saturation point of soybean is generally between 40 000 and 60 000 Lux (Wang et al., 2023; Yang and Miao, 1983). When the plant leaves are exposed to high light at noon (10:00–15:00 h), the level of PLP1 protein decreases rapidly and weakens the interaction with VTC2, thus relieving the inhibition of GGP activity to ensure sufficient AsA synthesis to protect photosynthetic organs. When the leaves were exposed to low light in the morning and evening, the level of PLP1 protein was relatively high and the interaction between VTC2 and GGP was closely and dynamically regulated to ensure that it would not lose control and produce too much AsA to affect plant growth and development.

The soybean variety ‘DongNong $5 0 ^ { \prime }$ (DN50) was used as the WT and the background plant for genetic transformation. # Plasmid construction and transformation of soybean

For construction of the recombinant vector proGmPLP1:GmPLP1- FlagFlag, 2000-bp promoter regions were amplified from the genomic DNA of ‘DN50’, and 1170-bp coding sequences (CDSs) of GmPLP1 were amplified from the cDNA of ‘DN50’ by overlapping PCR, and the Flag tag was amplified from a vector sequence synthesized by Rui Biotech (Harbin, China). Then, the three PCR products were ligated to the linear vector pCAM-BIA3300 using a homologous recombination system (ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit, Vazyme, Nanjing, China). For construction of the recombinant vector proGmVTC2b:GmVTC2b-Flag, 2174-bp promoter regions were amplified from the genomic DNA of $' \mathsf { D N } 5 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and 1311-bp CDSs of GmPLP1 were amplified from the cDNA of ‘DN50’ by overlapping PCR, and the Flag tag was amplified from a vector sequence synthesized by Rui Biotech. The construction of the proGmVTC2b:GmVTC2b-Flag vector was mentioned earlier. 

For the construction of GmPLP1-RNAi, the specific sequences of 498-bp sense and antisense fragments of the GmPLP1 gene were cloned into the RNA interference plant expression vector pFGC5941 through homologous recombination. The 

recombinant plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium rhizogenes EHA105 according to a method described previously (Zhao et al., 2018), and transgenic soybean was obtained. 

# Immunoblot analysis

The methods were described previously (Chen et al., 2021). The Flag tag protein was detected using a horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated anti-Flag antibody (A8592, Sigma, Saint Louis, USA). The GAPDH protein was detected by the HRP-conjugated anti-GAPDH antibody (HRP-60004, Proteintech, Wuhan, China). All experiments were performed at least three times with independent biological replicates. 

# Measurements of chlorophyll fluorescence

The maximum efficiency of Photosystem II (PSII) photochemistry $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ ratio) was measured using a chlorophyll fluorometer (Max-PAM, Heinz Walz, Ef-feltrich, Germany). Plants were darkadapted for 15 min before measurements were made. The chlorophyll fluorescence parameters were calculated as follows: $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } = ( F _ { \mathrm { m } } - F _ { \mathrm { o } } ) / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ (Yamamoto, 2016). All measurements were recorded from three biological replicates.

# Y2H assay

The CDSs of GmVTC2a, GmVTC2b and GmVTC2c were cloned into the pGADT7 vector using the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme). The full CDS of GmPLP1 was cloned into the BD plasmid. Bait (BD-PLP1) and prey (AD-VTC2a/VTC2b/ VTC2c) plasmids were co-transformed into yeast strain Y2H Gold competent cells and then grown for 2 days in SD-Leu-Trp culture plates at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Then, the transformants grown from SD-Leu-Trp were transferred into SD-Leu-Trp-His-Ade culture plate at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3 days. 

# Pull-down assay

The full-length CDSs of the GmPLP1 and GmVTC2a, and GmVTC2b and GmVTC2c cDNAs were cloned into pGEX-TX-1 and pET-29b, respectively. All plasmids were introduced into Rosetta (DE3) Chemically Competent Cell (Weidi Biotech, EC1010, Shanghai, China). GST, GST-GmVTC2a, GST-GmVT2b, GST-GmVTC2c and GmPLP1-His protein expression were induced by $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ isopropyl- $\beta$ -thiogalactopyranoside (Coolaber, 367-93- 1, Beijing, China). Soluble GST, GST-GmVTC2a, GST-GmVT2b and GST-GmVTC2c fusion proteins were extracted and immobilized to glutathione affinity resin (Sangon Biotech, C600327- 0001, Shanghai, China). Soluble GmPLP1-His fusion proteins were extracted using His-Tag Protein purification Kit I (Coolaber, PTE006-10T). The pull-down assays were conducted according to a method described previously (Sawa et al., 2007). The interaction was determined by western blot using an anti-His antibody (Abclonal, AE028, Wuhan, China) and anti-GST antibody (Abclonal, AE027). 

# BiFC assay

The full-length CDS of GmPLP1 was inserted into the pSPYCEcYFP vector. The GmVTC2a, GmVTC2b and GmVTC2c coding sequences were cloned into the pSPYNE-nYFP vector. The new fusion plasmid was introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 (pSoup-p19) and transformed into the leaves of N. benthamiana (Chen et al., 2021). Infected tissues were analysed $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ after infiltration using a confocal microscope (TCS SP5; Leica Microsystems, Deerfield, USA). 

# Luciferase complementation assay

GmPLP1 was fused to the N-terminus of luciferase (pCAMBIA1300- nLuc), and GmVTC2a, GmVTC2b and GmVTC2c were fused to the C-terminus of luciferase (pCAMBIA1300-cLuc). The GmPLP1 (C290A) mutants were generated by seamlessly connecting two fragments of 1–855 bp and 855–1173 bp of the GmPLP1 coding sequence, with 15-bp overlaps, using an infusion cloning method. The GmPLP1 (R290D) mutants were generated with two fragments of 1–858 bp and 858–1173 bp of the GmPLP1 coding sequence using the same method used to construct GmPLP1 (C290A). GmPLP1 (C290A) and GmPLP1 (R290D) were fused to the Nterminus of luciferase by in-fusion. The new fusion plasmid was introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 (pSoup-p19) and transformed into the leaves of N. benthamiana according to a method described previously (Chen et al., 2021) and then photographed using chemiluminescence imaging (Amersham Imager 600, General Electric, Pittsburgh, USA). 

# Measurement of the total AsA

The measurement of the total AsA was performed as previously described (Huang et al., 2018). 

# GDP-L-galactose phosphorylase assays

Leaf extracts were prepared as described previously (Dowdle et al., 2007). The GDP-L-Gal used in the assays was synthesized by XI’AN QIYUE BIOLOGY (Xian, China), and the GDP was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (43139-22-6, Shanghai, China). Hence, the GGP activity was assayed by the measurement of reduction in GDP-L-galactose as the substrate and the formation of GDP as the product following (Dowdle et al., 2007). Phosphate-dependent production of GDP from GDP-L-Gal was measured by high-performance liquid chromatography separation of the reaction products according to Wagner (1985). GDP-L-Gal and GDP were detected based on absorbance at 260 nm. The GDP and GDP-L-galactose concentrations were calculated by comparing the integrated peak areas with those of standard GDP or GDP-D-glucose solutions. 

# Injection of soybean leaves

The coding sequences of GmPLP1 and GmVTC2b were cloned into pCAMBIA3300 (with a GFP tag), and the vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101 (pSoup-p19) cells for transient expression. 

The method was previously described (Wang et al., 2021) and modified to reduce the damage degree of ‘DN50’ soybean leaves. Soybean seedlings were grown in a growth room at $2 6 /$ $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 days under 16-h light/8-h dark conditions, and then the lower epidermis of soybean leaves was wiped with a brush. The transformed Agrobacterium cells were adjusted to $\mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 5$ with infiltration buffer [10 mM ${ \sf M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ MES, pH 5.6, 50 mg L-ascorbic acid (Coolaber, CA2261), $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { { M } }$ acetosyringone (Sigma) and $0 . 0 2 \%$ Silwet L-77 (Coolaber, CS9791)], and the leaves were quickly immersed in heavy suspension, followed by vacuum pumping to $0 . 1 \mathsf { M P a }$ (two to four times) until each leaf was completely wet. The transformed soybean seedlings were left to recover in continuous darkness for 1 day and low light for 1 day, then grew under normal conditions for 3 days. 

# Transcriptomic analysis

For transcriptomic analysis, the plants grew for 3 weeks under LD $( 2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathrm { s } )$ , and after $1 5 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of transfer to high light $( 1 8 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l / m } ^ { 2 } / \mathsf { s } )$ , trifoliate leaves were collected and frozen in liquid nitrogen. cDNA library preparation, RNA-seq sequencing and assembly were performed on the Illumina Nova 6000 sequencing platform. For the identification of differentially expressed genes, $\log _ { 2 }$ ratios were calculated with the reads per kilobase of exon model per million mapped reads (RPKM) value. Each gene with a $P .$ -value ${ \le } 0 . 0 5$ and a fold change ${ \geq } 2$ was divided into up-regulated and down-regulated transcripts. GO annotation analyses were completed using BLAST2GO software (https:// www.blast2go.com/), an automated tool for the assignment of GO terms. 

# Statistical analysis

At least three biological replicates were included in the data, and all data were analysed using Student’s t-test or analysis of variance (ANOVA) for the determination of significant differences with SPSS 25.0 (IBM, Armonk, NY). All data were analysed using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA) for calculating the mean and standard error of the mean.
