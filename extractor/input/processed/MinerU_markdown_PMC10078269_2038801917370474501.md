# Abstract

Melatonin (Mel) is a multifunctional biomolecule found in both animals and plants. In plants, the biosynthesis of Mel from tryptophan (Trp) has been delineated to comprise of four consecutive reactions. However, while the genes encoding these enzymes in rice are well characterized no systematic evaluation of the overall pathway has, as yet, been published for wheat. In the current study, the relative contents of six Mel‐pathway‐intermediates including Trp, tryptamine (Trm), serotonin (Ser), 5‐methoxy tryptamine (5M‐Trm), N‐acetyl serotonin (NAS) and Mel, were determined in 24 independent tissues spanning the lifetime of wheat. These studies indicated that Trp was the most abundant among the six metabolites, followed by Trm and Ser. Next, the candidate genes expressing key enzymes involved in the Mel pathway were explored by means of metabolite‐based genome‐wide association study (mGWAS), wherein two TDC genes, a T5H gene and one SNAT gene were identified as being important for the accumulation of Mel pathway metabolites. Moreover, a 463‐bp insertion within the T5H gene was discovered that may be responsible for variation in Ser content. Finally, a ASMT gene was found via sequence alignment against its rice homolog. # 1 | INTRODUCTION

Melatonin (N‐acetyl‐5‐methoxytryptamine, abbreviated as Mel), a metabolite derived from tryptophan (Trp), has been found in almost all living organisms.1 It has been demonstrated that Mel impacts numerous aspects of human health such as circadian rhythms, sleep, mood, and the immunological system.2–5 Meanwhile, the regulatory role of Mel in plants has also been extensively reviewed.6–8 For example, it has been demonstrated to hinder the growth of several fungal pathogens.8 The implication that Mel contributes to plant pathogen defense was further supported by the finding of attenuated fungal infection in potato9 and cotton,10 as well as enhanced bacterial disease resistance of Arabidopsis and tobacco,11,12 and reduced plant viral infections.13 Mel biosynthesis in plants is essentially similar to that in animals, with the exception that while the first committed step in animals is catalyzed by tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH) which produces 5‐hydroxy tryptophan (5OH‐Trp),14 that in plants is catalyzed by tryptophan decarboxylase (TDC) which promotes the conversion of Trp to tryptamine (Trm).15 In brief, the Mel pathway in plants is believed to encompass four enzymatic steps catalyzed by enzymes including TDC, tryptamine 5‐hydroxylase (T5H), serotonin N‐ acetyltransferase (SNAT) and N‐acetylserotonin methyltransferase (ASMT)/caffeic acid O‐methyltransferase (COMT).15 This pathway is additionally comprised of other branching enzymatic steps. For instance, the N‐acetylserotonin deacetylase (ASDAC) could catalyze the conversion of $N \cdot$ ‐acetylserotonin (NAS) to serotonin (Ser), and as such represents the reverse of the reaction catalyzed by SNAT which yields NAS from Ser.16 Moreover, the Mel catabolic enzymes, melatonin 3‐hydroxylase (M3H)17 and melatonin 2‐hydroxylase (M2H),18 were discovered to exclusively exist in plants.19 Since the Mel pathway in plants was mainly delineated in rice,20 functional gene identification in other crops have most frequently been achieved via sequence alignment against the respective rice orthologs. For instance, the first plant ASMT and SNAT genes that 

catalyze the final two steps in the Mel pathway were cloned from rice,21,22 with their homologous sequences subsequently being cloned and validated in several other plant species including Arabidopsis, apple and loblolly pine.23–26 

Wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) is a leading cereal crop which is consumed worldwide. Alterations in Mel levels have been indicated to be an important component within several wheat stress responses including those to cold,27 low nutrient availability,28,29 heavy metals,30–32 and drought,33,34 which has been similarly suggested in other plant species.35–38 However, the Mel pathway metabolites and underlying genes have not yet been systematically studied in wheat. The currently substantiated wheat Mel pathway genes, to the best of our knowledge, merely include a COMT gene34 and a TDC gene.39 The deficient molecular resources for the Mel pathway in wheat have hindered an efficient large‐scale exploration on the relationships between Mel‐pathway‐ intermediates and physiological traits that may be impacted by involving metabolites or genes. Therefore, metabolic profiling and candidate gene identification towards the Mel pathway are imperative in wheat. Although homology‐based approaches have been successfully utilized to reveal functional genes of the Mel pathway in rice and other plants,20 this reverse‐genetics strategy may cause an unintended labor increase for hexaploid wheat given that it has a genome size of approximately 16 Gb.40 Indeed, a previous work conducting a stringent sequence alignment of Mel pathway genes suggested a total of 33 TDC genes, six T5H genes, nine SNAT genes, and 43 ASMT genes in wheat.41 Alternatively, the forward‐genetics approach which combines metabolic profiling from population collections has been proved promisingly effective to dissect several metabolic pathways in wheat.42,43 

In the current study, we first quantified the relative contents of Mel pathway metabolites including Trp, Trm, Ser, 5‐methoxy tryptamine (5M‐Trm), NAS and Mel, in 24 tissue types spanning the entire growth period from each of three wheat accessions (Chinese Spring, Aikang58, and Yannong19). The overall glimpse on 

content distributions of these Mel‐pathway‐intermediates in wheat would likely direct some future investigations toward the tissue‐specific roles of designated metabolites. Following this, the core enzyme coding genes were cloned using both forward‐genetics approaches and reverse‐genetics methods, which provided potential pivotal genes for surveying or monitoring related metabolites. In light of the results obtained, we postulated that a 463‐bp insertion within the T5H gene was responsible for the Ser content variation among wheat accessions. Involvement of these candidate genes in the Mel pathway was confirmed via in vitro enzymatic assays, as well as ectopic expression and transgenic approaches. Our work thus represents the first comprehensive exploration of the Mel pathway in common wheat, and the identified genic resources will contribute to future wheat investigations associated with the Mel pathway. 

# 2 | MATERIALS AND METHODS

# 2.1 | Plant materials

A total of 407 wheat accessions (Supporting Information: Table S1) including 111 CIMMYT resources provided by Prof. Caixia Lan, 17 cultivars generated from Prof. Fa Cui, 31 cultivars obtained from Prof. Zhonghu He,42 and another collection constituted cultivars and landraces from Prof. Jizeng Jia, were planted in a greenhouse at $2 5 / 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ 16/8 h. When the seedlings reached two‐leaf‐stage, leaves were collected, snap‐frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored in a $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ freezer until subsequent metabolite extraction. Two biological replicates were gathered from each wheat accession. Meanwhile, another three wheat accessions, including Chinese Spring (abbreviated as CS), Aikang58 (AK), and Yannong19 (YN) were also planted in Wuhan, Hubei province ( $\mathrm { ~ E ~ } 3 0 ^ { \circ } 5 8 ^ { \prime }$ , $\mathrm { ~ N ~ } 1 1 4 ^ { \circ } 3 0 ^ { \prime }$ ) for sample collection, which were subjected to local normal field management. A total of 24 tissues spanning the entire growth period44 from each of the three wheat accessions were collected (Supporting Information: Table S2). 

# 2.2 | Chemicals

The commercial standards of Trp, Trm, tyramine (Tym), 5OH‐Trp, Ser, 5M‐Trm, NAS, N‐acetyltyramine (Acy‐Tym), pyridoxal $. 5 ^ { \prime }$ ‐phosphate (PLP), acetyl‐CoA, S‐adenosyl‐methionine (SAM) and Mel were purchased from Sigma (www.sigmaaldrich.cn/CN/zh) and Macklin (www.macklin.cn). They were dissolved either in DMSO or methanol. 

# 2.3 | Metabolite detection

Mel metabolites were extracted from plant tissue samples as previously described.42 Briefly, freeze‐dried samples were ground into fine power, extracted by adding $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of $7 0 \%$ methanol (v/v, with pre‐added acyclovir and roxithromycin at final concentration of $0 . 1 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ as inner control) to $0 . 0 5 { \mathrm { g } }$ of powder. The mixture was vortexed, and ultra‐sonicated for 30 min at room temperature. The supernatant was generated by centrifuging the mixture at $_ { 1 0 , 0 0 0 g }$ for 6 min followed by filtering ${ \mathrm { ( } } 0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size; ANPEL). Following this, Mel pathway metabolites were quantified using the multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) method,45 with parameters listed in Supporting Information: Table S3. The metabolic profiling of Mel metabolites was performed on the Thermo Fisher TSQ Altis platform, the analytical conditions were as follows, UPLC: column, Waters ACQUITY UPLC HSS T3 C18 ( $\mathrm { 1 . 8 } \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $2 . 1 \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m } )$ ; column temperature, $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; flow rate, $0 . 3 5 \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ ; injection volume, $2 \mu \mathrm { l }$ ; solvent system, water $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid): acetonitrile $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid); gradient program, $9 8 { : } 2 \mathrm { V / V }$ at 0 min, $2 { : } 9 8 \mathrm { V / V }$ at 6.0 min, 2:98 V/V at 8.0 min, 98:2 V/V at 8.1 min, $9 8 { : } 2 \mathrm { V / V }$ at 10.0 min. Samples from candidate gene validation were subjected to the API 4000 Q TRAP LC‐MS/MS system as previously described,45 parameters are listed in Supporting Information: Table S3. 

# 2.4 | Quantification of metabolites in wheat tissues

Standard linear curves for each of the six Mel pathway metabolites (i.e., Trp, Trm, Ser, NAS, 5M‐Trm, and Mel) were generated by calibrating a coefficient value between peak areas and corresponding metabolite dilutions (Supporting Information: Table S4). In these equations, the intercept was set to zero. Afterward, concentrations of each metabolite were calculated based on established equations, molecular masses of respective chemicals, plant sample weights, and buffer amounts during extraction. 

# 2.5 | Metabolite genome‐wide association study (mGWAS)

After genotyping the 407 wheat accessions using unpublished re‐sequencing data (data not shown), a total of 12,803,125 polymorphic SNPs with minor allele frequencies $( \mathrm { M A F s } ) > 0 . 0 5$ and missing data $< 2 0 \%$ were retained and mapped to the wheat reference genome,46 wherein a total of 2,010,806 effective SNPs were generated based on a previously reported method,47 to investigate the associations between metabolite contents 

and SNPs. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) was estimated48 and generated49 as previously described,42 relative contents of metabolites were $\log _ { 2 }$ ‐transformed to fit the normal distribution. Subsequently, the mGWAS was implemented by a linear mixed model (LMM) using the FaST‐LMM program50–52 at the threshold of $p = 1 /$ $2 0 1 0 8 0 6 = 4 . 9 7 \times 1 0 ^ { - 7 }$ after Bonferroni correction, as carried out in previous wheat studies.42,53 

# 2.6 | Sequence amplified from wheat accessions

Primers used in the current study, as listed in Supporting Information: Table S5, were designed using the oligo 7 software54 version 7.60 combined with the PrimerServer online tool (https://202.194.139.32/PrimerServer/). PCR procedures were conducted using high‐fidelity enzyme (TransStart FastPfu Fly DNA Polymerase: TransGen) according to manufacturer's protocol. The amplicons were directly cloned using the pEASY‐Blunt Zero Cloning Kit (TransGen), colonies were randomly selected for sequencing. 

# 2.7 | Enzymatic validation of candidate genes

PCR products containing the coding sequences of designated genes were directionally cloned into the $p G E X – 6 p – 1$ expression vector (Novagen). The error‐ free ligates were transformed into the BL21 (DE3) competent cells (Transgen) and cultured on an LB plate. Standard in vitro enzyme assays were inherited from previous reports,55–58 wherein the reaction components for tryptophan decarboxylase $\mathrm { 0 . 1 } \mathrm { m M }$ Trp or other substrates, 50 ppm PLP), acetyl transferase (0.1 mM Ser or other substrates, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ acetyl‐ CoA) and methyl transferase (0.1 mM NAS or other substrates, 0.5 mM SAM) were respectively mixed in the Tris‐HCl buffer $\left( \mathrm { p H 8 . 0 } \right)$ . For ectopic expression in tobacco, candidate genes were subcloned into the $p C X S N$ vector (Novagen) and transformed into the GV3101 (pSoup‐p19) competent cells (Weidi Biotech). The transformed Agrobacterium cells culture and injection, tobacco leaves collection, and extraction followed previous report.43 

# 2.8 | Enzymatic kinetics

After validating the enzymatic activity for TraesCS 2D01G518200/TraesCS2D01G518300 (TDC), TraesCS 

1B01G312700 (SNAT), and TraesCS1D01G016700 (ASMT), kinetic constants for them were further determined. In brief, varied substrates (Trp for TDC, both of Ser and 5M‐Trm for each of SNAT and ASMT) were supplied into the reaction mixture. After incubation at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (TDC and ASMT) or $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (SNAT), amounts of generated products were calculated based on the standard curves for respective chemicals. Kinetic parameters were calculated using a Michaelis–Menten model (SigmaPlot, version 14.0). All the reactions were run in duplicate, and each experiment was repeated twice. 

# 2.9 | Wheat transformation

Coding sequences of TraesCS2D01G518300 and TraesCS1B01G312700 were cloned, constructed and transformed into wheat variety Fielder as previously described.59 Afterward, positive lines were screened by PCR using the primers listed in Supporting Information: Table S5. 

# 2.10 | Expression analyses

Total RNA was extracted from young leaves of T2 (TraesCS2D01G518300 overexpression) or T1 (TraesCS1B01G312700 overexpression) progenies using an RNA extraction kit (TRIzol reagent; Invitrogen). Reverse transcription and quantification were conducted as previously described.55 The relative expression quantities were calculated60 using Actin as an endogenous control. Primers for qRT‐PCR were listed in Supporting Information: Table S5. 

# 2.11 | Phylogenetic analysis

Wheat genes and their homologs from rice and other plant species were generated. Neighbor‐joining phylogenetic trees were constructed from the amino acid sequences alignment of respective gene sets using version 7.0.26 of MEGA software.61 A bootstrap of 1000 replicates was applied to these phylogenetic trees. 

# 2.12 | Statistical analysis

Data were presented by averages with standard deviations. Significance between different data sets was determined by Student's t‐test using the Excel software.

# 3.2 | Candidate genes identified via the metabolite‐based genome‐wide association study

In view of the fact that wheat seedlings at the two‐leaf‐ stage (stage code 111, Supporting Information: Table S2) possess relatively high contents of both Trp and Mel (Figure 1G), we postulated that the Mel pathway may be more active at this stage than at other stages, and therefore leaves from 407 wheat accessions were collected at this stage for metabolite genome‐wide association study (mGWAS). For this purpose, the relative contents of the six metabolites involved in the Mel pathway were profiled (Supporting Information: Table S6). Subsequently, the mGWAS was performed based on the 2,010,806 SNPs (see Section 2), which resulted in a total of 29 SNP‐metabolite associations (Supporting Information: Table S7) being detected under the threshold of $4 . 9 7 \times 1 0 ^ { - 7 }$ , within which we have designated four genes as candidates for respective loci. Specifically, two TDC genes, TraesCS2D01G518200 and TraesCS2D01G518300 (608.56 Mb on chromosome 2D), were assigned as candidates for the association $( p = 2 . 4 3 \times 1 0 ^ { - 8 } )$ between Trm and SNP AX‐03551850 (606.02 Mb on chromosome 2D), since the candidates locate within the LD range of leading SNP (Figure 2A) and share high sequence similarity with reported rice TDC genes (Figure 2B). Preliminary experiments indicated that the coding products of TraesCS2D01G518200 

and TraesCS2D01G518300 exhibited similar enzymatic properties. Specifically, purified TraesCS2D01G518200 (TraesCS2D01G518300) could catalyze the conversion of Trp to Trm (Figure 2C), with $K _ { \mathrm { m } } = 1 4 8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $( 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ and $K _ { \mathrm { c a t } } = 6 . 0 8 / \mathrm { s }$ (4.47/s). It can also convert 5OH‐ Trp into Ser (Supporting Information: Figure S1A), which resembled TDCs from other plant species.58,66 Meanwhile, an elevated Trm was detected in the TraesCS2 D01G518300‐overexpressing wheat leaves (Figure 2D), further validating the functional roles of these two candidates. 

For the second committed step in the Mel pathway, a T5H coding gene, TraesCS4B01G396300 (670.80 Mb on chromosome 4B), was designated to be the candidate for the association between Ser and the leading SNP AX‐ 07256520 (668.88 Mb, $p = 2 . 1 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 } ,$ ), given that it resides close to the leading SNP (Figure 3A,B) and shares high sequence similarity with the rice T5H gene (Supporting Information: Figure S2). However, enzymatic validation, similar to that previously reported,67 via the Chinese Spring reference coding sequence expressed in Escherichia coli indicated no hydroxylation activity was detected upon use of either Trp or Trm as substrates (data not shown). Subsequently, the wheat (TraesCS4B01G396300) and rice (LOC_Os12g16720) T5H genes were respectively cloned and coexpressed with the above‐validated wheat TDC (TraesCS2D01G518200, Figure 2C) in one half of a tobacco leaf. By comparison with the corresponding counterpart half‐ leaves that solely expressed TraesCS2D01G518200 as a control, we concluded that the wheat T5H could catalyze the conversion of Trm into Ser (Figure 3C), albeit at a lower enzymatic rate than the rice T5H (Figure 3D). # 3.3 | Constructing the Mel pathway in wheat

We have now assigned and validated the candidate genes that are responsible for three consecutive enzymatic steps (i.e., TDC, T5H, and SNAT) in the Mel pathway through the forward genetics approach. However, unfortunately no COMT/ASMT genes were obtained from the above mGWAS outputs. # 4 | DISCUSSION

# 4.1 | Candidate gene identification through mGWAS in wheat

Mel is a ubiquitous metabolite of plants and animals,1 which has been reported to play pleiotropic roles in plants since its first discovery.68–72 However, the molecular mechanisms regarding how this particular metabolite is biosynthesized or catabolized in plants are much less well characterized than in animals. Recently, the functional genes responsible for the Mel pathway have been revealed in rice following a homologous sequence alignment strategy.20,22,57,73 Similarly, some candidate genes have been functionally validated in various plant species,24,26,74–76 indicating that the reverse‐genetics scheme is effective. For instance, a total of 29 SNAT candidates were expressed in and subsequently purified from E. coli, only one of them were validated to possess the SNAT activity.22 The number of SNAT candidates through homologous alignment raised to 52 in tetraploid cotton,74 w hich will likely be further augmented for hexaploid wheat. However, we have not discovered any reliable sequence variations resided within the coding regions of TDC and SNAT genes that may associate with the respective content variations of the corresponding metabolites (Figures 2 and 4). Nonetheless, it is important to note that this forward‐genetics approach is not an iron‐clad assurance for candidate gene identification, and the ASMT gene was obtained by the reverse‐genetics manner (Figure 5). That said to our knowledge this is the first report that systematically unveils the Mel pathway candidate genes largely through a forward‐genetics approach. 

# 4.2 | Novel hints postulated from current metabolic profiling data

In the current study, some of the outputs have consolidated previous findings from other plant species. For instance, Trp is the most abundant metabolite (Figure 1B–D) that initiates this pathway.20 Accordingly, TDC harbors the highest activity among the four (i.e., TDC, T5H, SNAT, and ASMT) pathway enzymes, overexpressing the TDC genes will not only elevate the contents of its direct enzymatic product Trm, but also the next metabolite Ser.20 However, in transgenic wheat progenies overexpressing TraesCS2D01G518300 (Figure 2D), either slightly augmented or unchanged Ser contents were observed (data not shown), this may potentially be ascribed to the hexaploid nature of common wheat, since it harbors multiple functionally redundant TDCs (personal communication with other members of our group, who has enzymatically validated several TDCs in wheat). Similarly accredited, NAS contents were elevated in SNAT‐overexpressed wheat descendants (Figure 4E), however, no trace of Mel elevations were detected (data not shown). 

The Mel pathway metabolites have previously been documented as being involved in multiple biological processes.20 Hints to this were also provided by our data. For instance, the specific enrichment of 5M‐Trm in wheat pistils (Figure 1E) led us to assume it plays some pistil‐specific roles. Although it was reported that auxin controlled the cell proliferation of pistil77 and the auxin pathway could be regulated by Mel,78–80 no reports (to the best of our knowledge) have directly linked this particular metabolite to any specific biological roles in the pistil. Ser has been widely reported to be associated with senescence,81–85 but it seems to be not the case in current example since Ser levels peaked at around 3–4 weeks postanthesis (stages 831 and 871) during wheat seed development and subsequently decreased (Figure 1F). In conclusion, our systematic profile of metabolites within the Mel pathway in wheat not only confirmed some expected opinions, but also raised several points that require further investigations. 

# 4.3 | Future perspectives on the Mel pathway surveys in wheat

The metabolic profiling methodology combined with forward genetics design have been proved promisingly effective to swiftly identify candidate genes in hexaploid wheat.42,43 However, the significantly longer LD range in common wheat than other crops such as rice and maize86 requires additional efforts of thorough explorations upon 

the responsible sequence variations, which may demand extensive evaluations into a larger genomic segment surrounding the candidate genes in subsequent assessments. Meanwhile, it is important to mention that the reverse‐genetics manner through homology alignment is an indispensable supplement to swiftly sift potential candidate genes, especially in exploiting the orthologs of some constantly functioned genes or newly discovered candidates. 

Parallel to replenishing basic knowledge of the Mel pathway in wheat, downstream investigations could also be enlightened from currently unveiled data. For instance, what on earth are the biological roles for the specifically enriched or trended metabolites (Figure 1) in designated wheat tissues? Are the candidate genes identified in the current study useful for future Mel investigations or wheat breeding programs? If so intended, possible genetic factors and superior haplotypes should be further documented in premise. Finally, currently we have merely focused on the structural genes involved in the Mel pathway, some extensive candidate genes like the Mel receptors87 may be identified via this forward‐genetics manner (Supporting Information: Table S6) or reverse‐genetics approaches. 

All in all, our current study represents a very first report, to the best of our knowledge, that systematically profiled the Mel metabolites and delineated candidate genes through forward‐genetics approaches in wheat. This study provides a pivotal initiation for following investigations, such efforts will further contribute to the Mel research community.
