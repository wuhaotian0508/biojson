# The bHLH transcription factor PIL1 orchestrates starch synthesis in key cereal crops

Received: 4 November 2024 

Accepted: 24 November 2025 

Published online: 10 December 2025 

Check for updates 

Yanjun Meng, Jiaxing Cui, Wenbo Zeng, Xiaopei Li, Changqing Du, Pengfei Wang, Gezi Li, Qiang Ge , Tiancai Guo & Guozhang Kang 

Key cereal crops, including wheat, rice and maize, supply nearly $50 \%$ of the world’s caloric intake, with starch constituting over $70 \%$ of the grain weight. However, the regulatory mechanisms underlying starch synthesis are poorly understood. In this study, we identify a transcription factor TaPIL1-5D, which binds to the promoters of ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase small subunit 1 (TaAGPS1a−7A), granule-bound starch synthase I (TaGBSSI−4A), and branching enzyme II b (TaBEIIb-2D). Similarly, its homologous OsPIL11 and ZmPIL1 also activate the expression of the AGPS1, BEIIb, and GBSSI, and positively regulates grain starch synthesis. TaPIL1-5D and ZmPIL1 improve grain yields in two successive years. Nine alleles of TaPIL1-5B form three distinct haplotypes (Hap1-3), while TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 is repressed due to its binding by the transcriptional repressor TaBZR1.1. Additionally, TaPIL1-5B interacts with TaPIL1-5D in the nucleus, enhancing its transcriptional activation. Collectively, our study enriches the starch regulatory network and provides a promising tool for improving starch content. 

By 2050, with a global population approaching 10 billion, crop production will need to increase by $50 \mathrm { - } 7 0 \%$ to meet demand1 . Bread wheat (Triticum aestivum L.), a globally cultivated cereal crop, supplies over $20 \%$ of the calories in the human diet2 . In recent decades, studies have shown that thousand kernel weight (TKW) plays a crucial role in boosting wheat yields per unit area, particularly in China3 . Starch, which makes up about $70 \%$ of wheat grain, has a significant impact on both TKW and overall yield4 . 

Starch is the primary component in wheat endosperm, originating from photosynthetic products that serve as precursors. These precursors are subsequently catalyzed by various starch synthesis-related enzymes to form starch. Recent research has identified several key enzymes involved in starch synthesis, including ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase), granule-bound starch synthase I (GBSSI), starch synthase (SS), starch branching enzyme (BE) and debranching enzyme $( \mathsf { D B E } ) ^ { 5 - 7 }$ . AGPase, a central enzyme in this process, is composed of two small subunits (AGPS) and two large subunits (AGPL)8 . 

GBSSI is essential for amylose synthesis in grains, while SS, SBE, and DBE collaborate to synthesize the more complex amylopectin9 . Since starch is made up of both amylose and amylopectin, it is closely linked to grain weight. Overexpression of the AGPase cytosolic large subunit gene has been shown to increase both starch content and TKW in wheat10. Additionally, the BRITTLE1 (BT1) gene, responsible for transporting ADP-glucose, plays a positive role in regulating starch synthesis and enhancing TKW4 . 

In the past decade, some transcription factors (TFs) have been identified as key players in starch synthesis, impacting grain weight. In maize, the endosperm-specific transcription factor opaque2 (O2), a bZIP factor, works alongside various partner proteins to influence starch synthesis pathways11,12. TFs such as NAC (NAM, ATAF, and CUC) 128 and $1 3 0 ^ { 1 3 }$ , bZIP9114, ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE 19 (ABI19), and prolamine-box binding factor15 have all been shown to regulate starch synthesis in maize seeds. In rice (Oraza sativa L.), several TFs— including NF-YB116, NF-YB917, bHLH14418, AP2/EREBP family member

Rice Starch Regulator 1(RSR1)19, OsbZIP5820, and Brassinazole-Resistant 1(BZR1)21 play significant roles in regulating starch synthesis during endosperm development. However, in wheat, only a limited number of TFs, such as $\mathsf { N A C O 1 9 ^ { 2 2 } }$ , NAC-A1823, $\mathsf { b } Z \mathsf { I P } 2 8 ^ { 2 4 }$ , $\mathsf { M A D S } 2 9 ^ { 2 5 }$ , and TabHLH9526, have been associated with starch synthesis in the endosperm. As a result, much remains to be understood about the transcriptional regulation of starch synthesis in wheat. 

In plants, the phytochrome-interacting factor (PIF) family, part of the bHLH TF group, interacts with phytochromes and has been reported to regulate light, temperature, and photoperiod responses in Arabidopsis27,28. PIFs directly bind to G-box (CACGTG) and/or E-box (CANNTG) motifs within downstream gene sequences to regulate their expression29. To date, five PIFs (PIF1–PIF5) have been shown to participate in photomorphogenesis during the seedling stage and in starch degradation in Arabidopsis30. In rice, six genes homologous to AtPIF3, known as phytochrome-interacting 3-like (OsPIL11–OsPIL16)31, have been identified. Specifically, OsPIL12/OsBP-5 interacts with OsEBP-89 to form a heterodimer, regulating transcription of the Wx gene32. OsPIL1/OsPIL13 influences internode elongation33, leaf senescence34, chlorophyll biosynthesis and biomass yield35,36. Additionally, OsPIL15 binds directly to the promoter of the purine permease gene OsPUP7 to regulate cytokinin transport, impacting grain size37. In the hexaploid wheat genome, five PILs (TaPIL1–TaPIL5) were identified, each with three homoeologs across the wheat sub-genomes. TaPIL1 interacts with squamosa promoter binding protein-like (SPL)3/17 to activate teosinte branched1 (TB1) expression, which regulates tiller number in wheat38. However, little is known about TaPILs function in starch synthesis. 

In this study, we characterized a bHLH TF TaPIL1-5D, which directly binds to the promoters of TaAGPS1a-7A, TaGBSSI-4A, and TaBEIIb-2D, thereby activating their expression and positively regulating starch accumulation in wheat grain. Similarly, its homologous OsPIL11 in rice and ZmPIL1 in maize exhibit conserved functions by activating expression of the starch synthesis genes AGPS1, GBSSI, and BEIIb, also promoting starch synthesis and increasing TKW. Natural variations in the promoter region influence TaPIL1-5B expression by affecting TaBZR1.1 binding affinity, potentially shaping selection during Chinese breeding programs. Furthermore, TaPIL-5B forms a heterodimer with TaPIL1-5D to enhance transcriptional activity. Overall, our findings underscore the critical role of TaPIL1-5D in regulating starch synthesis within the wheat endosperm. 

# Results

# TaPIL1-5D is predominantly expressed in the grain of wheat and recognizes the motif of E-box

To identify TFs regulating starch synthesis, the promoter of TaAGPS1a, a key enzyme initiating starch synthesis, was cloned into the bait vector pAbAi. This vector was used in a yeast one-hybrid assay to screen a wheat cDNA expression library. Among the 21 positive clones, one gene encoded a TF classified as PIF-like 13 (bHLH39, TraesCS5D02G386500), a member of the bHLH family in wheat. In previous studies, TabHLH39 has shown to improve tolerance to various abiotic stresses when ectopically expressed in Arabidopsis. It has also been referred to as TaPIL1, noted for repressing tillering/branch in plants38,39. However, its role in starch synthesis remains unclear. As an allohexaploid species, wheat consistently exhibits homologous genes across all three subgenomes40. Specifically, TraesCS5D02G386500 shows high sequence similarity to TraesCS5B02G380200 and TraesCS5A02G376500 respectively (Supplementary Fig. 1). In constructing a polygenetic tree using the TaPIL1-5D protein sequence along with its homologs, it was observed that OsPIL11, ZmPIL1, and TaPIL1 clustered within the same clade (Fig. 1a). Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis revealed that TaPIL1−5D had a higher expression level in developing endosperm compared to TaPIL1−5A or TaPIL1−5B (Fig. 1b), 

suggesting a potential significant role for TaPIL1−5D in endosperm development. 

The bHLH TF family recognizes E-box (CACATG) motifs to regulate gene expression41. To examine transcriptional activity, the coding region of TaPIL1-5D was fused with the GAL4-binding domain (BD) vector and transformed into yeast cells along with both positive (BDp53) and negative (BD-Lam) controls. Yeast strains containing TaPIL1- 5D-BD or BD-p53 exhibited a blue coloration in SD/-Trp medium with X-α-gal whereas BD-Lam did not, indicating that TaPIL1-5D has transcriptional activity (Supplementary Fig. 2). In yeast one-hybrid assays, TaPIL1-5D bound to a probe from the promoter region of TaAGPS1a-7A containing the E-box motif but not to a probe with a mutated E-box motif (Fig. 1c). Similarly, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA) demonstrated that the recombinant His-TaPIL1-5D protein bound to the probe with the E-box sequence, but did not recognize the probe containing a mutant E-box sequence (Fig. 1d). To further confirm the transcriptional activation function of TaPIL1-5D on the E-box motif, we conducted dual-LUC reporter assays in N. benthamiana leaves. The results demonstrated that TaPIL1-5D specifically activated expression of the E-box motif but not the mutant E-box motif (Fig. 1e). Overall, these findings suggest that TaPIL1-5D is predominantly expressed in developing grains and specifically recognizes and activates E-box motif expression. 

# TaPIL1-5D regulates starch accumulation to enhance TKW

To clarify the role of TaPIL1-5D, we overexpressed TaPIL1-5D under the control of a ubiquitin promoter in the wheat cultivar Zhengmai7698, producing four independent transgenic lines (Fig. 2a and Supplementary Fig. 3). Compared to wild-type (WT) plants, the overexpression lines showed significantly higher contents of amylose, amylopectin, and total starch in field plot trail (Fig. 2b–e). Furthermore, the transgenic lines exhibited notable increases in grain length, grain width (GW) and TKW (Fig. 2f–h). 

To deepen our understanding of TaPIL1 function, we generated knock-out mutants using CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing in the same wheat cultivar Zhengmai7698. Based on the conserved sequence across all three TaPIL1 homoeologs, a single guide RNA (sgRNA) was designed to target the conserved region within the first intron of TaPIL1 copies on chromosomes 5A, 5B, and 5D (Supplementary Fig. 4). This approach yielded three triple mutant TaPIL1 lines (aabbdd-1, aabbdd-3, and aabbdd-7) with frameshift mutations (Supplementary Fig. 5). Compared to WT plants, the content of amylose, amylopectin and total starch in the grains of the tapil1 mutants was also notably lower than in WT (Fig. 2b–e). Additionally, grains from the tapil1 mutants were smaller than those of WT plants, and both grain length, GW and TKW were modestly reduced in the mutants (Fig. 2f–h). TaPIL1-5D OE lines also showed improved grain yields (Fig. 2i). Overall, these findings indicate that TaPIL1-5D plays a critical role in regulating grain size and weight, and starch accumulation.

# TaPIL1-5D shares the conserved function in rice and maize to regulate starch accumulation

In cereal crops, homologous genes often share conserved functions. Promoter sequence analysis of OsAGPS1, OsBEIIb, and OsGBSSI (within 2000 bp upstream of the ATG start codon), revealed that these genes’ promoters also contain 20, 7 and 6 E-box motifs respectively (Supplementary Fig. 8). In a yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay, the colonies containing both OsPIL11 and the promoters of OsAGPS1, OsBEIIb, or OsGBSSI turned blue on the culture plate, whereas those with the empty pGAG424 vector did not, suggesting that OsPIL11 bound to these promoters (Fig. 4a). Previous studies have shown that OsPIL11 has transactivation capabilities38. To investigate whether OsPIL11 

activates OsAGPS1, OsBEIIb, or OsGBSSI, we performed a dual luciferase reporter assay using a system comprising a reporter and an effector vector (Fig. 4b). Co-expression of OsPIL11 with the promoters of OsAGPS1, OsBEIIb, or OsGBSSI significantly increased LUC activity, as opposed to the control co-expressing only the promoters and OsPIL11 (Fig. 4b). Compared to WT, the pUbi::OsPIL11 transgenic plants in ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ homozygous generation displayed slight increases in grain length, GW and TKW in rice (Fig. 4c, d and Supplementary Fig. 9a–d). Amylose, amylopectin and total starch were higher in OE lines compared to WT (Fig. 4e and Supplementary Fig. 9e, f). These findings suggest that OsPIL11 directly binds to the promoters of OsAGPS1, OsBEIIb, or OsGBSSI and activates their expression, facilitating starch synthesis.

# The allelic variation sites of TaPIL1-5B promoter are significantly associated with the starch content and TKW

To further investigate allelic variation sites in TaPIL1, we sequenced the coding and promoter regions (−2000 bp upstream) of its three homeologs in a natural population of 301 diverse wheat cultivars, which included 46 landraces and 255 elite modern cultivars (Supplementary Data 2). The analysis revealed that three haplotypes (Hap1-3) of TaPIL1-5B were characterized by nine SNPs—three in the intron with no amino acid changes and six in the promoter region— and four InDels in the promoter (Fig. 5a). In contrast, TaPIL1-5A and TaPIL1-5D exhibited no polymorphism among wheat cultivars. We hypothesized that variations in the TaPIL1-5B promoter might influence its transcriptional activity. To test this, we fused the TaPIL1-5B-Hap1/2/3 promoters with GUS or LUC reporter genes and introduced them into tobacco leaves to assess promoter-driven activity. Results indicated that the Hap2/3 promoters displayed higher transcriptional activity than Hap1 (Fig. 5b, c). Analysis of ten diverse wheat accessions across the three TaPIL1-5B haplotypes revealed that in 14 DAP grains, accessions with Hap2/3 exhibited higher expression levels of TaPIL1-5B, TaAGPS1a-7A, TaBEIIb-2D, and TaGBSSI-4A compared to Hap1 (Supplementary Data 3 and Supplementary Fig. 11). Moreover, Hap2/3 accessions consistently showed higher levels of amylose, amylopectin, total starch content, and TKW than Hap1 across six environments (Fig. 5d–g). Among 301 diverse wheat accessions grown in these environments, those carrying Hap2/3 consistently displayed higher TKW than Hap1 (Fig. 5h). 

Our genetic and functional analyses of TaPIL1-5B indicate that TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 serves as an elite allele for starch synthesis and TKW in bread wheat. We then traced its origin by sequencing the TaPIL1-5B promoters of 17 Aegilops speltoides collections and 33 tetraploid accessions. Results showed that TaPIL1-5B-Hap2 was present in 15 diploid and 31 tetraploid accessions, while TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 was found in only 2 diploid and 2 tetraploid accessions, with no presence of TaPIL1−5B-Hap3 (Fig. 5i). In 46 hexaploidy landrace accessions, there were 23 Hap1, 21 Hap2, and 2 Hap3. Among 255 modern cultivars, the numbers shifted to 33 Hap1, 165 Hap2, and 57 Hap3 (Fig. 5i). These findings suggest a rise in Hap2/3 frequency from the landraces to modern cultivars. The elite Hap2/3 alleles increased consistently from $5 2 . 7 \%$ in 1980 to $9 0 . 7 \%$ in 2020 among 301 hexaploid wheat cultivars, indicating selective breeding for TaPIL1-5B (Fig. 5j). To distinguish Hap1/2 and Hap3, we developed a cleaved amplified polymorphic site marker based on the −1809 SNP within the GGGCCC restriction site of Apa I. Additionally, a 275 bp InDels at −1489 allowed differentiation between Hap1 and Hap2 (Supplementary Fig. 12). Overall, these results indicate that TaPIL1-5B is associated with TKW, and that the elite TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 alleles have undergone positive selection throughout wheat breeding.

# TaBZR1.1 binds to the promoter of TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 and inhibits its expression

To compare the contributions of TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 and TaPIL1-5B-Hap3 to starch synthesis and TKW, we designed an InDel marker to identify recombinant inbred lines (RILs) derived from the parent varieties Zhengnong17 (TaPIL1-5B-Hap3) and Yangbaimai (TaPIL1-5B-Hap1) (Supplementary Fig. 13). From the RILs, we randomly selected 15 plants each containing either Hap1 or Hap3 and measured their amylose, amylopectin, and total starch contents. Results indicated that plants with TaPIL1-5B-Hap3 exhibited higher levels of amylose, amylopectin, total starch, and TKW than those with TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 (Fig. 6a–e), reinforcing that TaPIL1-5B-Hap3 is an advantageous allelic variant associated with increased starch content and TKW compared to TaPIL1-5B-Hap1. 

Among the three TaPIL1-5B haplotypes, the main sequence differences were two insertions/deletions (InDels): InDels-1212 (12bp) and InDel-1489 (275bp). Analysis of the 275 bp insertion sequence using the PlantPAN3.0 database43, identified a brassinosteroid-response element (BRRE) CGTGCG (Supplementary Fig. 14). Previous studies have shown that OsBZR1 recognizes BRRE elements, playing a role in grain development21. To explore the relationship between TaPIL1-5B and BZR1 family genes in wheat, we selected TaBZR1.1 (TraesC-S2A02G187800), which shares high homology with OsBZR1, for further study44. To test TaBZR1.1’s ability to bind to BRRE element, we expressed and purified the His-TaBZR1.1 recombinant protein in E. coli. We then synthesized a probe containing the BRRE element (from the 275 bp InDel) and a mutated probe with a BRRE sequence altered to AAAAAA for EMSA assay. The results showed that TaPIL1-5B bound specifically to the BRRE-containing probe but not to the mutant probe (Fig. 6f). In the Y1H assay, TaBZR1.1 was found to directly bind to the promoter of TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 but not to the promoters of TaPIL1-5B-Hap2 or TaPIL1-5B-Hap 3, confirming that TaBZR1.1 specifically recognizes the BRRE element in the TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 promoter (Fig. 6g). To further investigate whether TaBZR1.1 regulated the transcriptional activity of TaPIL1-5B-Hap1, we performed a LUC assay in tobacco leaves. The results showed that luciferase activity significantly decreased when TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 was co-transformed with TaBZR1.1 compared to TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 alone. In contrast, luciferase activity showed no significant change between TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 and TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 co-transformed with TaBZR1.1 (Fig. 6h). These findings suggest that TaBZR1.1 binds to the BRRE element in the TaPIL1-5B-Hap1 promoter region, inhibiting its expression and influencing starch synthesis.

# Discussion

# TaPIL1-5D acts as a positive regulator in grain starch synthesis and TKW

In cereal crops, several bHLH TFs have been reported to regulate grain development and starch synthesis23,24,47. For instance, Wheat Positive Regulator of Grain Size 1 (TaPGS1), a bHLH TF specifically expressed in developing grain, directly binds to the E-box in the promoter regions 

of target genes, thereby positively regulating starch synthesis in wheat42. Similarly, TabHLH95 binds directly to the promoters of AGPL1- 1D/-1B, AGPL2-5D, and ISA1-7D to activate their expression, contributing to starch synthesis26. In our study, we found that TaPIL1-5D, another bHLH TF, specifically recognizes the E-box motif and activates its expression (Fig. 1c–e). In TaPIL1-5D overexpression plants, both starch content and TKW significantly increased (Fig. 2), whereas its CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated knout-out mutants exhibited the opposite results (Supplementary Fig. 4), proving its important role in wheat starch synthesis. We analyzed the expression of 17 SSREs in 14 DAP grains from WT, OE6 and tapil1-aabbdd-1 plants. Results showed that SSREs had higher expression levels in OE6 and lower expression levels in tapil1-aabbdd-1 compared to WT (Fig. 3a). For further study, we selected AGPS1a-7A, BEIIb-2D, and GBSSI-4A, which contain 11, 17, and 11 E-box motifs in their promoter regions, respectively, and had higher expression levels than their homoeologs (Fig. 3b). A bHLH TF, ZmICE1, has been reported to recognize the E-box motif in the ZmSSIIa promoter to regulate starch biosynthesis48. Similarly, TaPIL1-5D was found to directly bind to the E-box motif within the promoters of AGPS1a-7A, BEIIb-2D, and GBSSI-4A, activating their expression in both in vivo and in vitro assays (Fig. 3c–e). Overall, these findings indicate that TaPIL1- 5D recognizes the E-box element of AGPS1a-7A, BEIIb-2D, and GBSSI-4A and activates their expression, contributing to the synthesis of amylose, amylopectin and total starch in wheat grain.

GBSSI, encoded by the Waxy (Wx) gene, is responsible for amylose synthesis49–5 1, while BEIIb is essential for amylopectin synthesis52–54. Homologous TFs involved in starch synthesis in cereals can also share conserved functions. For example, the basic leucine zipper (bZIP) TF Opaque2 (O2) in maize, along with its orthologs OsbZIP58 in rice and Storage protein activator (TaSPA) in wheat, directly binds to SSREs promoters to regulate starch accumulation in grains13,20,55. In our study, OsPIL11 in rice and ZmPIL1 in maize exhibited the following key characteristics: (i) they clustered within the same subclade as TaPIL1 in a phylogenetic analysis (Fig. 1a); (ii) they bound directly to the promoters of AGPS1, BEIIb and GBSSI, activating their expression (Fig. 4a, b, f, g); (iii) they positively regulated starch content and TKW in grains, as demonstrated in transgenic plants (Fig. 4d, e, i, j and Supplementary Figs. 9 and 10). Together, these findings underscore the conserved functionality of PIL1 in cereals, all of which participate in starch synthesis within grains. 

# TaPIL1-5B is a potential target for starch content and TKW breeding

Identifying superior allelic variations of target genes involved in starch synthesis presents an effective strategy for enhancing grain weight and improving wheat yield through breeding efforts. Several genes with allelic variations have been implicated in starch synthesis and grain weight regulation, including TaGW2-6A, TaNAC100, and TaNAC-A18 and TabHLH48923,47,56,57. Our research showed that knocking out TaPIL1 reduces both starch content and TKW in wheat grains (Fig. 2), indicating a correlation between TaPIL1 expression levels and starch content and grain weight. Genetic analysis of TaPIL1-5A/B/D across a natural population of 301 wheat accessions comprising 46 landraces and 255 modern cultivars, classified TaPIL1-5B into three haplotypes (Hap1-3), primarily based on genomic polymorphisms in the promoter region (Fig. 5a). TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 were identified to be elite alleles and underwent positive selection in wheat breading by measuring activities of LUC and GUS (Fig. 5b, c), TKW, contents of amylose, amylopectin and total starch in a natural population and a RIL group (Fig. 6a–e and Supplementary Fig. 13), and expression levels of TaPIL1- 5B, TaAGPS1a-7A, TaBEIIb-2D, and TaGBSSI-4A in the developing grains (Supplementary Fig. 11). The primary differences between Hap2/3 compared to Hap1 were two Indels located at positions −1212 and −1489 (Fig. 5a). Upon analyzing the diversity of the InDel sequences in the promoter region of TaPIL1-5B, we discovered a BR-response element (BRRE, CGTGC/TG), at −1688 bp in Hap1, which is known to be recognized by a transcriptional repressor brassinazole resistant 1 (BZR1)58,59. Recent studies have shown that BZR1 directly binds to the promoters of several genes involved in grain development in both rice and wheat21,47,60. Our results indicate that TaBZR1.1 specifically binds to the promoter of TaPIL1-5B-Hap1, suppressing its expression in comparison to Hap2 and Hap3 (Fig. 6f–h and Supplementary Fig. 14). Taken together, these findings suggest that TaPIL1-5B-Hap2/3 could be a potential target for starch contents and TKW breeding, and TaBZR1.1 does not bind to its promoter, thereby enhancing its expression and starch synthesis. 

# TaPIL1-5B forms the heterodimer with TaPIL1-5D to enhance its transcriptional activity

The basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) TFs are characterized by a conserved bHLH domain comprising 60 amino acids, which consists of a basic region and a helix-loop-helix (HLH) region61. The basic region is responsible for binding to the E-box (CANNTG) motif located at the N-terminus of the bHLH domain, while the HLH region, found at the Cterminus, facilitates the formation of homodimers or heterodimer with other bHLH proteins62. For instance, in Arabidopsis, bHLH121 interacts with clade IVc bHLH TFs to participate in iron uptake63. Similarly, OsbHLH107 has been shown to form homodimers that regulate grain size development64. In our study, we demonstrated that TaPIL1-5B 

forms a heterodimer with TaPIL1-5D both in vivo and in vitro (Fig. 7a–c). The formation of heterodimers can enhance its transcriptional activity, as seen with ICE1, a bHLH TF that interacts with IDD14 to boost its transcriptional capacity during pollen germination and viability65. In our LUC assay, TaPIL1-5B was shown to enhance the transcriptional activity of TaPIL1-5D towards target genes TaAGPS1a-7A, TaBEIIb-2D, and TaGBSSI-4A (Fig. 7d). These results clarify that the TaPIL1-5B-TaPIL1-5D module plays a crucial role in the regulation of starch synthesis and TKW. 

In summary, our study identified the conserved TF PIL1 in wheat, rice and maize, which activates the expression of AGPS1, BEIIb, and GBSSI to facilitate starch synthesis in grain. These findings not only elucidated the regulatory mechanisms of starch synthesis in major cereal crops but also provided potential genetic resources for enhancing starch content through molecular breeding. 

# Methods

# Plant materials

All the names and geographical origins of wheat cultivars, including modern cultivars, landraces and Triticum turgidum (AABB), used in this study are listed in Supplementary Data 2. These wheat cultivars were grown in the experimental field of Henan Agriculture University in Yuanyang $( 3 5 . 1 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } ,$ , $1 1 3 . 9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E } )$ , Mengjin $( 3 4 . 8 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } ,$ , $1 1 2 . 4 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { E } )$ ), and Wenxian $( 3 4 . 9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } ,$ , $\underline { { 1 1 3 . 0 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { E } } } )$ ) in China for at least two cropping seasons (2021-2023), which were collected to estimate TKW. To clarify the gene expression, root, stem and leaf tissues were harvested from field plants at the flowering stage, and whole seeds at the 7, 14, 21, and 28 DAP during the grain filling stage. For each sample, there were harvested from three different plants, to represent as one biological replicate. All samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The RIL population was generated from Zhengnong 17 and Yangbaimai with selfing for eight generations66. 

The OsPIL overexpression lines were grown in the experimental field of Henan Agriculture University in Yuanyang $( 3 5 . 1 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N }$ , 113.95°E) during the 2024 growing season. Its maize transgenic lines were grown in Zhengzhou $( 3 4 . 7 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 1 3 . 8 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { E }$ ) during the 2024 growing season and Xishuangbanna $( 2 2 . 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 0 0 . 5 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ) during the 2025 growing season. 

Tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) seedlings were cultured in the incubator under 16 h: 8 h, light: dark with a light intensity of 3000 lux for 30 days, which were used to perform the luciferase (LUC) activity assay. 

# Generation of transgenic plants

The open reading frame (ORF) of the TaPIL1-5D sequence from Chinese spring was cloned into the pUbi-CAMBIA1301. To generate TaPIL1 knock mutants, the website CRISPRdirect (http://crispr.dbcls.jp/) was used to design the sgRNA, which was cloned into pWMBX110-SpCas9 vector driven by TaU3 promoter67. All vector constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105, while TaPIL1-5D vectors were transformed into Zhengmai7698 cultivar68. 

To investigate whether PIL proteins in rice and maize also participated in starch synthesis, we generated transgenic rice and maize plants overexpressing OsPIL11 (pUbi::OsPIL11) and ZmPIL1 (pUbi::ZmPIL1), respectively. To generate OsPIL11 and ZmPIL1 overexpression plants, the ORF sequence of OsPIL11 and ZmPIL1were cloned into pUbi-CAMBIA1301 and pCAMBIA3300 vector, respectively, which was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 to transform into the rice cultivar Zhonghua11 and the maize KN5585 inbred line by the Agrobacterium-mediated method69,70. 

# Yeast one-hybrid screening and assay

For yeast-one hybrid screening, a cDNA expression library derived from various pollination day wheat seeds mixture (7 DAP, 14 DAP, 21 DAP) was considered as prey in the pGADT7 vector. The promoter 

fragment of TaAGPS1a (2000 bp upstream of ATG) was cloned into the pAbAi vector, which was linearized by BstB I enzyme, and then transformed into the Y1HGold yeast strain. According to the manufacturer’s instructions (Takara, 630491), Y1H screening was performed. 

To detect the specific TaPIL1-5D-binding E-box motif, the 59 bp sequence from the promoter of TaAGPS1a containing the E-box motif and the corresponding mutated sequence were cloned into pLacZi vector separately. The CDS fragment of TaPIL1-5D was inserted into pGAD424 vector, and then co-transformed with pLacZi vector into YM4271 yeast strain. Subsequently, the transformants was dropped on the synthetic dropout –Ura and –Leu medium containing $2 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L }$ X-gal. Besides, the 2000 bp promoter regions of TaAGPS1a-7A, TaGBSSI-4A, and TaBEIIb-2D were amplified and cloned into the pLacZi vector. The recombinant pLacZi vector, along with the pGAD424 vector containing the full TaPIL1-5D coding region, was cotransformed into yeast for YM4271 yeast strain. Then, the yeast strain was cultured on a medium lacking Ura and Leu containing X-gal, which was used to identify the positive strain. 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR

Total RNA from different samples was extracted with the Plant RNA Kit (Tiangen, DP432), and first-strand cDNA was synthesized from $2 \mu \ g$ total RNA using FastKing cDNA Kit (Tiangen, KR116) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. For qRT-PCR, the synthesized cDNA was diluted to $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ , and $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the diluted cDNA was mixed with $7 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ SYBR Green Mix (Tiangen, FP201), $\mathbf { 0 . } 2 \mathbf { m M }$ primers to form $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ Amplication (Thermofisher, QuantStudio5). Actin was used as an internal control, and all samples were performed three biological replicates. All the primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplementary Data 4. 

# Starch content analysis

After harvest, wheat, maize, and rice grains were milled into flour to ground with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ mesh sieve. A simple was used to assess moisture content, while the remainder was taken to measure the total starch, amylose and amylopectin with Megazyme Total Starch Assay Kit (Megazyme, KTSTA-50A, Ireland). Each sample was performed three times. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The full-length CDSs of TaPIL1-5D and TaBZR1.1 were inserted into pColdTF expression vector (Takara, 3365), which were transformed into E. coli (BL21 DE3), respectively, to produce a His fusion protein. The recombinant protein was purified using the His-tag protein purification kit (Beyotime, P2226-1). To test the binding of TaPIL1-5D to these promoters, we used a His-TaPIL1-5D recombinant protein and synthesized 59 bp probes containing the E-box motif from the promoters of TaAGPS1a-7A, TaGBSSI-4A, and TaBEIIb-2D for the EMSA assay. The probes were synthesized and labeled with FAM at the ${ \bar { \mathsf { S } } } ^ { \prime }$ and ${ } ^ { 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -end, and annealed from complementary oligonucleotides by cooling from 95 to $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to form double-stranded probes. EMSA assays were performed using Gel-Shift Kit (Beyotime, GS002) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and detected the fluorescence by a Starion FLA-9000 instrument (FujiFilm, Japan). All probe sequences are listed in Supplementary Data 1. 

# Dual luciferase transcriptional activity assay

As previous reports58, LUC assays were performed in N. benthamiana leaves. The full-length CDSs of TaPIL1-5B/D and TaBZR1.1 were cloned into pBI121 vector to serve as effectors, while the promoters of AGPS1a-7A, BEIIb-2D, and GBSSI-4A were separately inserted into pGreenII-0080-LUC vector to consider as the reporter constructs. Then, the vectors and constructs were separately transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101, which were infected into N. benthamiana leaves. 

LUC signal was captured by the CCD imaging apparatus (Tanon, 5200), and each experiment was used at least five independent leaves. The activities of LUC (Firefly luciferase) and REN (Renilla luciferase) were detected by a dual-luciferase reporter assay system (Beyotime, RG029). The relative luciferase activity was analyzed using LUC/REN ratios. 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

The coding sequences of TaPIL1-5B and TaPIL1-5D were cloned into pGADT7 and pGBKT7 vector, respectively. According to the manufacturer’s instructions (Clontech, 630489), the plasmids were transformed into Y2H Gold yeast cell, which were plated on the minimal medium. Then, the cells containing plasmids were cultured on the Dropout Medium/-Tryptophan-Histone-Leucine-Adenine containing $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ Aureobasidin A (AbA) with or without X-α-gal. The p53 and Lam were used as positive and negative controls, respectively. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assays

For preform BiFC assay, the full-length CDS of TaPIL1-5B and TaPIL1- 5D were cloned into pSCY-nGFP (N-terminal fusion to green fluorescent protein) and pSCY-cGFP (C-terminal fusion to green fluorescent protein), respectively. Then, the recombinant plasmids were transformed into wheat protoplasts by PEG-mediated transformation methods. The transformed protoplasts, containing various plastids, were cultured at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under darkness for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , which were obtained GFP signals on a FluoViewTM FV300 confocal microscope (Olympus, Japan). 

# Luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assay

To perform the LCI assay, the coding sequences of TaPIL1-5B and TaPIL1-5D were inserted into pCAMBIA1300-35S-cLUC-RBS or pCAM-BIA1300-35S-HA-nLUC-RBS vectors, respectively, which were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV310171,72. Then, various combinations were co-transfected into N. benthamiana leaves. After 3 days culturation, the infiltrated leaves were sprayed with 1 mM luciferin (Sigma, 2591-17-5), and the LUC signal was captured using Tanon-5200 chemiluminescent imaging system. 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses of all bar graphs were performed using GraphPad Prism (Version 8.0.2) (https://www.graphpad.com/scientific-softwareprism/), Origin Pro (Version 2024b) (https://www.originlab.com/) or Microsoft Excel (2016). Values were represented a mean ± SD, $p$ values are made by two-tailed Student’s t test or one-way ANOVA with Tukey’s multiple comparisons test. In this study, the biological replicates of experiments presented are indicated in the respective figure legends. No data were excluded from the analysis, and no statistical method was used to predetermine sample size. The experiments were not randomized, and the investigators were not blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment.
