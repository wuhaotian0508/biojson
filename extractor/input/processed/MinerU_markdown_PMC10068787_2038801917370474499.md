# The OsBDR1-MPK3 module negatively regulates blast resistance by suppressing the jasmonate signaling and terpenoid biosynthesis pathway

Lanlan Wanga,1 , Guojuan Xua,b,1 , Lihua Lia , Meiying Ruanc , Anne Benniond , Guo-Liang Wange , Ran Lif,2 , and Shaohong Qua,2 

Edited by Zuhua He, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shanghai, China; received June 28, 2022; accepted February 17, 2023 by Editorial Board Member Qifa Zhang 

Receptor-like kinases (RLKs) may initiate signaling pathways by perceiving and transmitting environmental signals to cellular machinery and play diverse roles in plant development and stress responses. The rice genome encodes more than one thousand RLKs, but only a small number have been characterized as receptors for phytohormones, polypeptides, elicitors, and effectors. Here, we screened the function of 11 RLKs in rice resistance to the blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae (M. oryzae) and identified a negative regulator named BDR1 (Blast Disease Resistance 1). The expression of BDR1 was rapidly increased under M. oryzae infection, while silencing or knockout of BDR1 significantly enhanced M. oryzae resistance in two rice varieties. Protein interaction and kinase activity assays indicated that BDR1 directly interacted with and phosphorylated mitogen-activated kinase 3 (MPK3). Knockout of BDR1 compromised M. oryzae-induced MPK3 phosphorylation levels. Moreover, transcriptome analysis revealed that M. oryzae-elicited jasmonate (JA) signaling and terpenoid biosynthesis pathway were negatively regulated by BDR1 and MPK3. Mutation of JA biosynthetic (allene oxide cyclase (AOC)/signaling (MYC2) genes decreased rice resistance to M. oryzae. Besides diterpenoid, the monoterpene linalool and the sesquiterpene caryophyllene were identified as unique defensive compounds against M. oryzae, and their biosynthesis genes (TPS3 and TPS29) were transcriptionally regulated by JA signaling and suppressed by BDR1 and MPK3. These findings demonstrate the existence of a BDR1-MPK3 cascade that negatively mediates rice blast resistance by affecting JA-related defense responses.

Rice blast, caused by the fungus Magnaporthe oryzae (syn. Pyricularia oryzae), is a consistent threat to global food security. The most effective and environmentally friendly approach for disease control is breeding and planting resistant crop cultivars (1, 2). Recent studies have characterized dozens of genes contributing to rice blast resistance and provided potential solutions for controlling diseases in rice. Nevertheless, breeding for durable resistance to this fungus is difficult, as most resistance genes break down in a few years due to the alteration of their race specificity and the rapid change in the pathogenicity of the blast fungus (3). Therefore, it is critically required to improve rice immunity against M. oryzae by manipulating potential gene targets that can provide more durable resistance to rice blast disease. 

Rice has evolved sophisticated innate immune systems to recognize and respond to pathogen infections at different layers. The first layer of innate immune response is activated to detect conserved pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), resulting in PAMP-triggered immunity (PTI). However, pathogens have evolved strategies to overcome PTI by secreting effectors in the infection site. Meanwhile, plants form a second layer of innate immune responses which recognize pathogen-secreted effectors by host disease resistance $( R )$ gene-encoding proteins, leading to race-specific effector-triggered immunity (2). Many receptor kinases are documented to recognize PAMPs and effectors secreted by pathogens. For instance, the rice chitin elicitor receptor kinase 1 (CERK1) can recognize fungal chitins and induce downstream defense responses (4–6). The rice genome encodes 1,131 Receptor-like kinases (RLKs) genes (7). Knowledge in understanding the biological function of these RLKs (e.g., response to blast fungus) is still limited. Identifying more RLKs that directly link the extracellular and intracellular compartments will help in understanding plant defense mechanisms.

# Significance

Rice blast, caused by Magnaporthe oryzae fungus, is a consistent threat to global rice production and food security. Despite decades of study, the molecular basis of rice immunity to the fungus is still limited. Here, we report that the rice receptorlike kinase BDR1 interacts with mitogen-activated kinase 3 and this module negatively regulates blast resistance by suppressing the jasmonate and terpenoid pathways. We further showed that terpenoid compounds significantly suppressed the blast fungus. Since the osbdr1 mutants were resistant to M. oryzae without significant abnormality in agronomic phenotypes, BDR1 can be utilized for the gene-editing breeding of rice resistance. Our results enrich the understanding of plant–fungus interactions and provide both gene and chemical resources for controlling this destructive pathogen.

Biochemical or genetic evidence has suggested several complete MAPK cascades involved in rice blast fungus resistance responses (10, 11). MPK3 plays a negative role in rice resistance to M. oryzae (10), whereas MPK6 is involved in the biosynthesis of chitin-induced diterpenoid phytoalexins, which act as toxins to defend against M. oryzae infection (11). A recent study revealed that OsMAPKKKε-silenced rice lines exhibited suppressed chitin-based MPK3/6 activation and reduced resistance to M. oryzae (12). Increasing evidence suggests that the phytohormone jasmonate (JA) is positively involved in rice response to blast fungus by mediating the biosynthesis of antimicrobial compounds (13–15). Mutation or overexpression of JA biosynthesis genes altered rice resistance to M. oryzae (14, 16, 17). Additionally, overexpression of the JA-responsive MYB transcription factors (TFs) OsMYB in rice conferred a significant increase in resistance to M. oryzae (18). MAPKs have been implicated in regulating JA biosynthesis and signaling pathways in tobacco and Arabidopsis (19). For instance, MPK6 was recently reported as an important regulator of the JA signaling pathway in Arabidopsis (20). In tobacco, WIPK and SIPK were reported to be required for wounding- and herbivore-induced JA production (21, 22). However, the interaction between MAPK and JA signaling in mediating rice defense against blast disease is elusive. 

Plant secondary metabolites play important roles in the interaction between plant and biotic stresses (23). Rice blast fungus infection can also elicit many secondary metabolites, such as phytoalexin, flavonoids, and terpenoids (24–28). The flavonoid sakuranetin accumulated in blast-infected tissues and exhibited antifungal activity against M. oryzae (29, 30). Many diterpenoids which have been identified to have antifungal activities are also elicited by blast fungus infection (31, 32). Mutation of the diterpene biosynthetic gene, copalyl diphosphate synthase 2 (CPS2), decreased rice resistance to M. oryzae Guy11 strain (33), while overexpression of a diterpene modification gene, CYP71Z18, enhanced blast fungus resistance (26). Given the enrichment of secondary metabolites, the blast resistance-related chemicals and their regulation mechanisms are worthy of being investigated. 

Here, we screened 11 blast fungus-induced rice RLKs genes and found that only one RLK-silenced plant showed altered resistance to the M. oryzae TMC-1 strain. This RLK was named Blast Disease Resistance 1 (BDR1). We next confirmed the function of BDR1 related to blast resistance by genome editing and gene overexpression. Using protein interaction and phosphorylation assays, we then showed that BDR1 is directly linked to MAPK signaling. The regulatory mechanism of BDR1 was then explored by transcriptome analysis. Finally, we explored the regulatory mechanism of BDR1 through transcriptome analysis, phytohormone analysis, and resistance bioassays and found that BDR1 regulates JA-mediated terpenoid biosynthesis. Our results demonstrate that BDR1 interacts with and phosphorylates MPK3, which subsequently negatively regulates rice blast resistance by modulating JA and terpenoid biosynthesis in rice. 

# Results

BDR1 Is Rapidly Up-Regulated under M. oryzae Infection and Is Involved in Rice Blast Resistance. Our previous transcriptome analysis identified many up-regulated RLK genes in M. oryzaetreated rice samples, suggesting that these RLKs may play a role in rice and M. oryzae interaction (34). To test this hypothesis, gene silencing lines of 11 RLKs were constructed by RNAi (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S1). The transcription levels of these RLKs 

were significantly decreased in RNAi lines (ir-RLKs) compared with wild-type (WT) plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). Next, rice blast inoculation of RLK-silenced rice lines was performed. Only the rice line silencing the candidate gene Os07g38810 exhibited enhanced resistance to rice blast fungus (Fig. 1A). This RLK was named BDR1. To further confirm this phenotype, four additional independent silencing lines were used. The BDR1 mRNA level was reduced by 65 to $8 0 \%$ in ir-BDR1 lines compared with that of WT plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S1C). All ir-BDR1 lines exhibited enhanced resistance to the rice blast fungus after 7 d of TMC-1 treatment. Lesion areas and disease indexes of ir-BDR1 lines were significantly lower than those in WT plants (Fig. 1 B–D). These results suggested that BDR1 is a negative regulator of rice immunity against blast fungus. 

Rice blast fungus-induced BDR1 expression was confirmed by RT-qPCR. The BDR1 transcripts increased from 12 h TMC-1 treatment and steadily increased during treatment (Fig. 1E). BDR1 gene was universally expressed in all rice tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). The expression of BDR1 gene was also determined in the BDR1 promoter:GUS reporter line (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B). GUS activity could be observed in all rice tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S2C), while it increased in rice leaves after blast fungus treatment (Fig. 1E, Inset). To determine the subcellular localization of BDR1 protein, GFP-tagged BDR1 was transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves. The results showed that BDR1 was localized in the plasma membrane (SI Appendix, Fig. S2D). 

BDR1 Negatively Regulates Rice Blast Fungus Resistance. To further evaluate the function of BDR1 in rice blast resistance, two independent BDR1 knockout mutants and two overexpression (OE) lines were generated. The two bdr1 mutants had either 1- or 5-bp deletion in the target site (Fig. 2A). The two OE lines were confirmed by the identification of BDR1 transcription levels (Fig. $2 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S3). Mutation of BDR1 did not affect rice growth and development (grain length and width, grain number per plant, tiller number per plant, and 1,000 grain weight, SI Appendix, Fig. S4 A and $B$ ), while BDR1-OE plants showed growth retardation with lower grain number per plant and lower 1,000 grain weight (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 C and $D$ ). Blast resistance in different plants was then evaluated. Lesion areas and disease indexes were used as indicators. Relative to WT plants, the bdr1 mutant showed enhanced resistance to the M. oryzae strain TMC-1 (Fig. $2 B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S5) with smaller lesion areas (Fig.  2C) and lower disease indexes (Fig. 2D), whereas BDR1-OE lines showed reduced resistance to TMC-1 (Fig. 2F) with larger lesion areas (Fig. $2 G ,$ ) and higher disease indexes (Fig. $2 H$ ). To corroborate these results, anothe $M .$ oryzae-compatible strain Guy11 was also used for the bioassays. Consistently, bdr1 mutants showed enhanced resistance to Guy11 (SI Appendix, Fig. S6), suggesting that BDR1 conferred broadspectrum resistance to rice blast resistance. Taken together, these results indicated that BDR1 is a negative regulator of rice blast resistance.

To elucidate whether rice MPK3 or MPK6 is phosphorylated by BDR1, we performed in vitro phosphorylation assay using anti-phospho-MAPK. The results showed that His-MBP-BDR1 could phosphorylate rice GST-MPK3/6, and the phosphorylation effect could be disrupted by calf intestinal alkaline phosphatase (CIP) treatment (Fig. $3 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S8C). To confirm whether BDR1 phosphorylates rice MPK3/MPK6 in vivo, the phosphorylation levels of MPK3/6 were examined in bdr1 mutants and WT plants. M. oryzae infection enhanced the phosphorylated MPK3/6 protein levels in WT plants after 24 h treatment (Fig. $3 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S8D). However, the phosphorylation levels of MPK3 were dramatically lower in the bdr1 mutant than those in WT plants, while the levels of phosphorylated MPK6 did not differ (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 B and C). Consistently, the phosphorylated MPK3 levels were much higher in BDR1-OE lines than those in WT plants $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ after blast fungus treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). These data indicated that BDR1 regulates M. oryzae-elicited MPK3 activity. To determine whether MPK3 also acts as a negative regulator in blast resistance like BDR1, the resistance phenotype of two mpk3 mutants was evaluated. Rice blast-elicited MPK3 phosphorylation could not be detected in either mpk3 mutant (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Consistent with bdr1, both mpk3 mutants exhibited enhanced resistance to the rice blast fungus compared to WT plants (Fig. $3 F$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S11). Lesion areas (Fig. 3G) and disease indexes (Fig. $3 H )$ in leaves of mpk3 mutants were significantly lower than those in WT plants after fungal infection. 

Mutation of BDR1 Enhanced the Expression of M. oryzae-Elicited JA and Terpenoid Pathway Genes. To investigate the signaling downstream of the BDR1-MPK3 cascade, a transcriptome analysis of rice blast-treated bdr1 and WT plants was performed. Different genotypes were treated by TMC-1 at four time points, with three replicates for each time point. A total of 24 samples were RNA sequenced. PCA of all genes showed that the three replicates of each treatment clustered together, suggesting good biological replicability (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). Moreover, different genotypes were clearly separated at each time point, suggesting significant transcriptional changes brought by BDR1 mutation. Rice genes whose transcript abundance showed a fold change (FC) $> 2$ and adjusted $P < 0 . 0 \bar { 5 }$ were defined as differentially expressed genes (DEGs). In total, 2,366 DEGs were up-regulated in bdr1 mutant, and the numbers were highest in the $\bar { 2 4 } \mathrm { h }$ -treated samples (Fig.  4A). Only 52 genes were up-regulated in all treatments, suggesting that BDR1 regulates downstream genes at different fungal infection stages. The GO analysis was then conducted to predict the function of these up-regulated DEGs. Six GO groups were enriched based on biological process (Fig.  4B). Of them, one group included “cell surface receptor signaling” and “signaling transduction,” which were highly correlated with the potential function of BDR1. Interestingly, the terms “JA-mediated defense response” and “terpenoid biosynthetic and metabolic process” were most abundant. The core JA pathway genes and terpenoid biosynthesis genes also appeared in the up-regulated DEGs. The JA module included many biosynthetic genes (e.g., AOS2), signaling genes (e.g., $J A Z )$ , and JA-responsive TFs (e.g., JAMYB) (Fig. 4C), some of which were reported to be involved in rice blast resistance (16, 18). Diterpenes have been broadly investigated to play roles in rice blast resistance (24, 26, 27, 33, 35). As expected, many diterpene biosynthetic genes were found in the up-regulated DEGs (Fig.  4D). In addition, some monoterpene and sesquiterpene synthases were also identified in the terpenoid module, such as linalool and caryophyllene biosynthetic genes. Together, these transcriptome data suggested that JA and the terpenoid pathway are involved in BDR1-mediated rice defense against blast disease. 

BDR1-MPK3 Cascade Suppresses M. oryzae-Elicited JA Signaling. To test whether M. oryzae-elicited JA signaling is regulated by BDR1, the expression of JA pathway genes and JA content were quantified. The transcription levels of the JA biosynthetic genes AOC, AOS1, AOS2, and JAR1 significantly increased in bdr1 mutant compared to WT plants after blast fungus treatments (Fig. 5 A and B and SI Appendix, Fig.  S13 A and B). Furthermore, the JA signaling-related genes, MYC2 and JAZ8, were also up-regulated in bdr1 mutant (Fig. $5 C$ and $D \}$ ). Consistent with gene expression, levels of JA and JA-Ile were also significantly increased in bdr1 mutant after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , and 72 h M. oryzae treatments (Fig. $5 E$ and F). To confirm the role of JA in rice blast resistance, two JA deficient lines were applied. The rice genome only encodes one copy of AOC and MYC2 genes. Thus, aoc-2 and myc2-5 mutants and their background variety, XS11, were used (36). Both mutants were more susceptible to the M. oryzae strain TMC-1 than XS11 plants (Fig. 5G). The phenotype was consistent with observations that lesion areas (Fig.  5H) and disease indexes (Fig. 5I) increased in the two mutants compared with XS11 plants. Since BDR1 could directly regulate MPK3 phosphorylation levels, whether MPK3 also regulates the M. oryzae-elicited JA pathway was investigated. The transcription levels of JA biosynthetic genes (AOC, AOS1, AOS2, and JAR1) and signaling genes (JAZ8 and MYC2) were all elevated in mpk3 mutant compared to WT plants under M. oryzae treatment (Fig. 5 J–O). Taken together, these results suggest that the BDR1-MPK3 cascade negatively regulates rice JA-mediated defense against blast disease. 

TPS3 and TPS29 Are Involved in Rice Resistance to M. oryzae and Regulated by the BDR1-MPK3-JA Module. Emerging evidence has revealed the role of volatile monoterpene (e.g., limonene) in rice resistance to blast fungus (37). From the RNA-seq data, two known volatile terpene biosynthetic genes, TPS3 and TPS29, were up-regulated in bdr1 mutant after blast fungus treatment, suggesting their potential roles in blast resistance. The expression of TPS3 and TPS29 was first validated by RT-qPCR (Fig. 6 A and B). TPS3 and TPS29 transcripts indeed accumulated at higher levels in bdr1 mutant than in WT plants under blast fungus treatments. To evaluate the biological function of TPS3 and TPS29, mutants of each gene were constructed. Target sequences of TPS3 and TPS29 were edited by CRISPR/Cas9, respectively, and two different mutation types were obtained (Fig. 6 C and $D$ ). The tps3 and tps29 mutants exhibited increased susceptibility to $M .$ . oryzae TMC-1 strain infection compared to WT plants (Fig. 6 $E$ and $H$ and  SI  Appendix, Fig.  S14). Lesion areas and disease indexes of tps3 or tps29 mutants were higher than those on WT plants (Fig. 6 F, G, I, and J). TPS3 and TPS29 are responsible for linalool and caryophyllene biosynthesis, respectively (38, 39). To further confirm the role of both genes, the in vitro antifungal activity of linalool and caryophyllene was performed. Linalool strongly repressed the growth of M. oryzae (strain TMC-1) in complete medium at concentrations of 10, 20, or $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , and few conidia were produced 7 d after treatment (SI  Appendix, Fig. $S 1 5 A$ and B). The growth inhibition effect of caryophyllene on M. oryzae was also observed at $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S15 $\dot { D }$ and E). Meanwhile, appressorium formation decreased with different concentrations of linalool or caryophyllene treatments (SI Appendix, Fig. S15 C and F), implying that linalool and caryophyllene may have a direct role in the suppression of fungal infection. Collectively, these results indicated that the monoterpene and sesquiterpene synthase, TPS3 and TP29, are positively involved in blast resistance like diterpenes.

# Discussion

The RLK gene family is two times larger in rice than in Arabidopsis, which is likely due to the fact that many are involved in innate immunity and have apparently undergone duplication events (40). To date, a few RLKs conferring rice disease resistance have been characterized, while the function of numerous RLKs is still unknown (6, 41, 42). Here, we screened and identified a membrane-localized 

RLK gene, BDR1, that negatively regulates rice resistance to blast fungus. An enhanced and broad-spectrum blast disease resistance was found in the bdr1 mutant lines, whereas BDR1 overexpression plants were more susceptible to this fungus. By using a series of molecular, genetic, and chemical tools, we revealed the mechanism of BDR1 regulation of rice blast resistance. First, BDR1 directly interacted with and phosphorylated MPK3. Altering the expression of BDR1 affected blast fungus-elicited MPK3 phosphorylation levels. Consistent with bdr1 mutants, mpk3 knockout lines exhibited enhanced blast fungus resistance. Second, the BDR1-MPK3 cascade could function through the JA pathway. The blast fungus-elicited JA signaling was activated in bdr1 and mpk3 mutants. Third, linalool and caryophyllene were identified as defensive chemicals against blast fungus, and their biosynthesis was regulated by JA signaling and suppressed by blast fungus-elicited BDR1-MPK3 cascade. The MAPKs, MPK3/4/6, and their orthologs play key roles in signaling plant development and defense responses (19, 44). MPKs may be regulated not only by upstream MKKs but also by some other kinases (45–48). We found that BDR1 mediated rice blast resistance mainly through interacting with and phosphorylating MPK3. This is consistent and similar with a previous study showing that MPK3 was activated by an upstream kinase CPK18 which negatively regulated rice blast resistance (48). Although the interactions between BDR1 and MPK6 were also observed by the in vitro and in vivo assays, the blast fungus-elicited phosphorylation levels of MPK6 were not affected by BDR1. We inferred that BDR1-MPK6 cascade might be involved in other biotic and abiotic stresses (44). The specificity of MPK3/6 regulation was also observed in other studies. For instance, the receptor kinase SIT1 or MKK9 targets both MPK3 and MPK6 but functions mainly through MPK6 in salt tolerance (49, 50). 

Upon rice blast fungal infection, the biosynthesis of JA and related signaling pathways are activated in rice. JA regulates blast fungus resistance through its ability to promote the synthesis and accumulation of antimicrobial compounds (13–15). It has been reported that cpm2 and hebiba mutants lacking JA biosynthetic gene AOC reduced the levels of both endogenous JA and M. oryzae-inducible phytoalexins and displayed increased vulnerability to M. oryzae (14), which is consistent with our results of genome-edited aoc mutants on rice blast resistance (Fig. 5G). Similarly, transgenic rice plants overexpressing OsAOS2 (allene oxide synthase), a JA biosynthetic gene, exhibited increased resistance to M. oryzae (16). Other JA-deficient lines were also reported to be hypersusceptible to M. oryzae (17). In bdr1 mutant lines, many JA biosynthetic genes were up-regulated under blast fungal infection, resulting in enhanced production of jasmonic acid and JA-Ile (Fig. 5 E and $F _ { . }$ ). Given that JA biosynthetic and signaling genes were also up-regulated in mpk3 mutants under M. oryzae infection, MPK3 is likely to function upstream of the M. oryzae-elicited JA pathway. Previous studies also supported this argument: Silencing of MPK3 increased M. oryzae-elicited expression of PR10 and Chitinase genes, both of which were known to be regulated by JA pathway in rice (10, 48). Several studies have described that the basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) and WRKY TFs are the substrates of MPK3/6 phosphorylation (51, 52). Hence, it is possible that MPK3 negatively regulates M. oryzae-elicited JA by targeting TFs. We proposed two mechanisms of regulation. First, MPK3 may directly phosphorylate some key JA components, such as MYC2. Phosphorylation may impair the protein stability of MYC2 as other bHLH TFs to suppress JA signaling (51). Second, some WRKY TFs that function as repressors of JA signaling may be phosphorylated by MPK3, such as WRKY62 and WRKY76, which are a negative regulator of rice blast resistance (53). Moreover, silencing of both genes enhances the biosynthesis of JAs and some defensive compounds, including the transcription levels of TPS3 (54). Phosphorylation of WRKYs is known to increase their transcriptional activities (52, 55). Further studies are required to investigate the potential role of these TFs in BDR1-MPK3 cascades and JA-mediated blast resistance. 

The transcriptome analysis suggested that the terpenoid pathway might be involved in BDR1-mediated rice defense against blast disease (Fig. 4D). Among them, the diterpenoids have been broadly characterized (24–28). Here, a special attention is paid to some monoterpene and sesquiterpene. The linalool and caryophyllene biosynthetic genes, TPS3 and TPS29, were found 

responsive to M. oryzae infection, respectively (Fig. 6). Exogenous application of linalool and caryophyllene inhibited the growth of M. oryzae, which was consistent with the disease evaluation of linalool and caryophyllene synthase gene knockout lines (Fig. 6 and SI Appendix, Fig. S15). Linalool and caryophyllene were previously identified in herbivore-induced plant volatiles, which function as indirect defensive compounds to attract natural enemies of herbivores in rice and maize (38, 56). Emerging evidence also showed the possible antimicrobial role of terpenes in numerous plant species. For instance, caryophyllene is the major volatile compound in Arabidopsis flowers and plays a role in flower defense against bacterial Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato DC3000 (57). In addition, the monoterpene 1,8-cineole in roots is involved in resistance to the fungus Verticillium longisporum (58). E-nerolidol and $\gamma$ -terpinene function as antibacterial compounds against Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae (Xoo) (59, 60), while limonene and $\beta$ -elemene have antifungal activity against M. oryzae (15, 37). The expressions of TPS3 and TPS29 were suggested to be regulated by JA pathway by examining the transcription levels of both genes in aoc and myc2 mutants (Fig. 6 M–P). Consistently, JA-induced accumulation of linalool is also reported to be regulated by OsJAZ8, a rice JA ZIM-domain protein involved in JA signaling (15, 61). Given that MYC2 transcription factor could regulate the transcription of TPS genes by binding a G-box element in the promoter region of TPSs in Arabidopsis (62), we inferred that TPS3 and TPS29 might also be directly regulated by rice MYC2. Further studies using chromatin immunoprecipitation assays are required to test this hypothesis. 

M. oryzae is a model species for studying the interaction between pathogenic fungi and host species. Salicylic acid (SA) and JA are important hormones regulating disease resistance in higher plants. SA and JA signaling often acts antagonistically in both dicot and monocot species (63). The defense signaling in Arabidopsis follows a binary model: SA and JA control the resistance to biotrophic and necrotrophic pathogens, respectively. However, in rice, both SA and JA signaling contributes to resistance against hemibiotrophic pathogens. In this study, we identified the BDR1-MPK3 module that regulates JA signaling in rice. The components of JA and SA signaling in the BDR1-MPK3 genotypes of rice can be further investigated to enrich our understanding of the crosstalk between JA and SA hormones in higher plants. 

In conclusion, M. oryzae infection enhances intracellular JA biosynthesis, resulting in the activation of JA-responsive TFs, such as MYC2. These TFs then regulate the biosynthesis of monoterpenes (e.g., linalool), sesquiterpenes (e.g., caryophyllene), and diterpenes, which function as antifungal compounds against $M .$ oryzae. Meanwhile, M. oryzae infection rapidly activates the expression of BDR1 gene in the plasma membrane. BDR1 protein could physically interact with and phosphorylate MPK3. The increased phosphorylation levels of MPK3 suppressed JA signaling and terpenoid biosynthesis via some TFs to facilitate the infection of M. oryzae (Fig. 7). Thus, the activation of BDR1 might be a strategy of blast fungus to counter plant immune responses. Given that BDR1 is localized in the cell membrane, we hypothesized that BDR1 could be the target of M. oryzae-secreted effectors or PAMPs. Further study will focus on this interesting question by characterizing the interaction partner of BDR1. Our results enrich the understanding of rice and blast fungus interactions and provide gene and chemical resources for controlling blast disease. # Materials and Methods

Rice Blast Fungal Inoculation and Plant Materials. Rice blast fungal inoculation was performed as described previously (64). For spray inoculation, the disease symptoms were scored 7 d after infection by measuring the lesion areas and disease indexes. Disease indexes were evaluated based on 10 classes from 0 (no disease lesions) to 9 (more than $7 5 \%$ disease lesions) as described (65). The lesion area $( \% )$ was quantified by calculating the pixels under the lesion and healthy areas of diseased leaf blades using the Histogram command of Adobe Photoshop (66). For punch inoculation, disease severity was evaluated 10 d after inoculation by measuring lesion length and relative fungal expression (10, 66, 67). The fungal inoculation experiment was repeated more than three times. For details, see SI Appendix. 

Coimmunoprecipitation Assay. Coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) was performed as described by Li et al. (68). The resulting immunoprecipitant was separated by SDS-PAGE and detected by immunoblotting using an anti-MYC antibody. See SI Appendix for details on Co-IP assay. 

In Vitro Pull-Down Assay and Kinase Assay. For in vitro pull-down and kinase assays, purification of the recombinant proteins GST-MPK3/6 and His-MBP-BDR1 was performed. For in vivo kinase assay, rice seedlings of bdr1 mutants and WT plants were treated with the TMC-1 strain at the indicated time points. The phosphorylation levels of MPK3/6 were detected using anti-phospho-p44/42 antibodies. Details of purification of the recombinant protein, pull-down assay, and kinase assay are shown in SI Appendix. 

Transcriptome Analysis and RT-qPCR. Total RNA of rice samples was extracted using the MiniNEST Plant RNA Extraction Kit (Takara) for transcriptome analysis and RT-qPCR. RNA sequencing was conducted by Novogene. The transcripts were analyzed as described previously (69). For details, see SI Appendix. Some other details of experimental methods are shown in SI Appendix. 

Data, Materials, and Software Availability.  Raw sequence data, data have been deposited in Beijing Institute of Genomics Data Center (https://bigd.big. ac.cn/gsa/browse/CRA007058) (70).
