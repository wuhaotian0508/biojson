# Sesterterpenoids: chemistry, biology, and biosynthesis†

Keke Li $\textcircled{1}$ a and Kirk R. Gustafson $\textcircled{1}$ *b

Covering: July 2012 to December 2019

Over the last seven years, expanding research efforts focused on sesterterpenoids has led to the isolation, identification, and characterization of numerous structurally novel and biologically active sesterterpenoids. These newly reported sesterterpenoids provide diverse structures that often incorporate unprecedented ring systems and new carbon skeletons, as well as unusual functional group arrays. Biological activities of potential biomedical importance including suppression of cancer cell growth, inhibition of enzymatic activity, and modulation of receptor signaling, as well as ecologically important functions such as antimicrobial effects and deterrence of herbivorous insects have been associated with a variety of sesterterpenoids. There has also been a rapid growth in our knowledge of the genomics, enzymology, and specific pathways associated with sesterterpene biosynthesis. This has opened up new opportunities for future sesterterpene discovery and diversification through the expression of new cryptic metabolites and the engineered manipulation of associated biosynthetic machinery and processes. In this paper we reviewed 498 new sesterterpenoids, including their structures, source organisms, country of origin, relevant bioactivities, and biosynthesis.

# 1 Introduction

This review provides comprehensive coverage of 498 new sesterterpenoids that were reported in the literature from mid 2012 through 2019, and is a complement to the previous reviews of sesterterpenoids that have appeared in Natural Product Reports.1–5 Similar to these prior reviews, the format focuses on the discovery of new sesterterpenoids from natural sources and the associated biological properties of these metabolites. Due to the large number of new sesterterpenoids that were described during the current review period, only the structures of select new compounds appear in the text and they are indicated in standard fashion by bold numbers. All of the other new sesterterpenoids are indicated with bold, italicized numbers and a complete compilation of all numbered structures is available in the ESI. $^ { \dagger }$ In addition to more traditional natural product discovery efforts, recent studies of the genomics, biosynthesis, enzymology, and heterologous expression of sesterterpenoids are also covered in this review. New structures derived from these biosynthetic studies are described in the biosynthesis section. There have been numerous synthetic efforts towards sesterterpene end products and they are generally not described in this review, unless they produced new sesterterpenoid structures for SAR or other biological studies. While the biological activities of many novel compounds are detailed throughout the review, papers that solely describe biological activity proling of previously known sesterterpenoids are not included.

Sesterterpenoids are pentaprenyl terpenoids whose oen complex polycyclic structures are derived from the linear precursor geranylfarnesyl diphosphate. Although sesterterpenoids are a relatively small group of terpenoids, their sources are widespread as they have been isolated from fungi, bacteria, lichens, higher plants, insects, and various marine invertebrate organisms, especially sponges. A driving force behind sesterterpenoid research is the diverse biological properties they oen exhibit, which includes cytotoxicity toward human cancer cells and inhibition of pathogenic microbes, as well as anti-inammatory, antiprotozoal, antitubercular, and antifeedant activities. Some compounds have also been found to selectively inhibit a broad array of enzymatic activities, modulate specic receptor functions and signal transduction

pathways, alter transcriptional activity, or reduce cellular levels of specic proteins.

During the period covered in this review, there have been several more specialized reviews of sesterterpenoids including “A new family of sesterterpenoids isolated around the Pacic Rim”, 6 “Sesterterpenoids with anticancer activity”, 7 “Targeting cancer with sesterterpenoids: the new potential antitumor drugs”,8 “Synthetic approaches toward sesterterpenoids”, 9 and “Total synthesis of sesterterpenoids”. 10 In this review, we provide the structures and bioactivities of new sesterterpenoids recently isolated from diverse natural sources, those derived as synthetic intermediates, and new sesterterpenoid metabolites generated through genetic and biosynthetic studies.

# 2 Structure, occurrence, and biological activity of sesterterpenoids

# 2.1. Linear sesterterpenoids

This class of sesterterpenes lack carbocyclic rings, but they frequently contain heterocyclic moieties such as furan, pyran, or pyrrolidine rings. While linear sesterterpenoids are oen structurally less complex than other groups of sesterterpenoids, congurational assignment of their acyclic stereogenic centers can be challenging due to a high degree of conformational mobility in these molecules. The majority of new sesterterpenoids described in this section were obtained from marine sponges. Four balibaloside derivatives 1–4 which are characterized by different acetyl substitutions on a trisaccharide residue linked to a common sesterterpenoid aglycone core were isolated from the Mediterranean sponge Oscarella balibaloi. None of them displayed biological activity in either cytotoxicity or antimicrobial tests.11 Coleifolides A 5 and B 6, sesterterpenoids with a $\beta$ -methyl- $^ { \cdot \alpha , \beta }$ - unsaturated- -lactone moiety that are related to the manoalide family of marine metabolites, were isolated from aerial parts of the perennial herb Scutellaria coleifolia, and both compounds showed cytotoxicity against several cancer cell lines with $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ values ranging from 13.6 to $3 7 . 6 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g } } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ .12 Granuloside 7 was isolated from the Antarctic nudibranch Charcotia granulosa, and its structure was assigned following extensive spectroscopic analysis of the $o$ -methyl derivatives 8 and 9.13

Sulawesins A–C 10–12 were isolated from the Indonesian marine sponge Psammocinia sp. Compound 12 had a dimeric structure that contained two ircinin-1 sesterterpene subunits, and it represents the rst dimer in this structural class of sesterterpenoids. Compounds 10 and 11, which have an imbedded carbocyclic ring in the polyprenyl chain, are included in this family of $\gamma \cdot$ - lactones due to close structural similarities with the other members, and they inhibited the ubiquitin specic protease USP7 with $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ values of 2.8 and $4 . 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ .14 (+)-Hippolide E 13 and (3R,4R)- m14,18-secoluffariolide C 14 were isolated from the South China Sea sponge Cacospongia sp., but they were not inhibitory when tested against K562, HCT116, Hep3B, A-549, and Jurkat human tumor cell lines.15

Cacolides A 15 and B 16, cacolic acid A 17, and cacolides C–L 18–27 were isolated from a Southern Australian Cacospongia sp. marine sponge.16 The cacolides all have terminal butenolide moieties and compounds 23–27 also have terminal -lactam ggroups. Sarcotragin C 28, a new norsesterterpenoid containing a leucine-derived $\gamma$ -lactam was isolated from the sponge Sarcotragus sp. collected from Chuja Island, Korea. It exhibited cytotoxicity against K562 and A549 cancer cell lines with $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ values of 18.8 and $1 3 . 1 ~ \mu \mathbf { M } ,$ , respectively.17 Chemical investigation of the marine sponge Ircinia oros (G¨okçeada, Turkey) yielded ircinialactams E 29 and F 30, which showed moderate leishmanicidal, trypanocidal, and antiplasmodial activities.18 Additional ircinialactams B 31, G 32, H 33, and I 34, along with 8-hydroxyircinialactams C 35 and G 36, and ircinialactone A 37 were isolated from two Australian sponges, Psammocinia sp. (CMB-01018) and Sarcotragus sp. (CMB-01012).19 The sponge Hippospongia lachne collected around Yongxing Island in the South China Sea provided two new

nitrogenous sesterterpenoids 38 and 39. Both compounds had inhibitory activity against the phosphatase enzyme PTP1B, with $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ values of 5.2 and $8 . 7 \ \mu \mathbf { M }$ , respectively.20

mA furanosesterterpenoic acid 40 was obtained from the sponge Luffariella variabilis and its structure was characterized by spectroscopic analyses of the methyl ester derivative 41. Compound 40 showed very modest cytotoxic activity, while the methyl ester was inactive.21 An interesting diketo, dilactone named erectusolide A 42 was isolated from the sponge Hyrtios erectus collected from Similan Island in Thailand.22 Two hybrid sesterterpenes ether-linked to coumarin moieties, 43 and 44 were isolated from a methanolic extract of roots from the Asian tree Aegle marmelos (L.) Corr. (Amroha, India).23 Rhopaloic acid H 45, a new norsesterterpene peroxide was isolated from the sponge Hippospongia sp. collected off the coast of Pingtung County, Taiwan.24 An acylated sesterterpenoid 46 was isolated from the ethyl acetate extract of rice straw from Oryza sativa. 25

# 2.2. Monocarbocyclic sesterterpenoids

A variety of structurally diverse monocarbocyclic sesterterpenoids were reported during the review period and most of these metabolites were also derived from marine sponges. The biological properties exhibited by this group of sesterterpenoids included cancer cell growth inhibition, cell line specic cytotoxicity, and the selective targeting of various molecular targets and signaling pathways. A family of spirocyclic sesterterpenoids, phorbaketals D–K 47–54, were isolated from a collection of the Korean marine sponge Monanchora sp. All of the compounds were evaluated for cytotoxic activity against four human cancer cell lines (renal cancer, A498 and ACHN; pancreatic cancer, MIApaca and PANC-1) and while 51 and 52 showed modest cytotoxicity against A498 cells, the other compounds were inactive against all four cell lines.26 Additional phorbaketals L–N 55–57 were isolated from a Phorbas sp. marine sponge (Gageo Island, Korea), and epoxide 57 showed cytotoxicity against human pancreatic cancer cells with an $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of $1 1 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ .27 A related spiroketal 58 was isolated from a Monanchora sp. sponge collected from deep waters off the Aleutian Islands of Alaska. Newly described bicarbocyclic sesterterpenoids provided a diverse ensemble of structural scaffolds and functional group arrays. These compounds were reported from terrestrial plants and marine sources, and they exhibited biological activities relevant to both chemical ecology and biomedical applications. Structural elucidation challenges associated with this group of metabolites were addressed using a variety of contemporary spectroscopic and computational methods. # 3. Biosynthesis of sesterterpenoids

There has been tremendous recent progress in elucidating the fundamental mechanisms as well as many of the ner details of sesterterpene biosynthesis. While much of the earlier work on biosynthetic pathways of sesterterpenes was carried out using methodologies such as isotopic labeling, the 2012–2019 period of this review coincides with the initiation and rapid development of detailed genomic and enzymatic analysis of sesterterpene biosynthesis. Identication of the rst fungal sesterterpene synthase in 2013 facilitated the discovery of other synthase genes through the application of genome mining,126 and heterologous expression in suitable host systems has provided numerous new sesterterpenoids, many with unprecedented carbon skeletons. This is proving to be a very effective means to access cryptic sesterterpene metabolites and thus unlock tremendous new chemical diversity.

The sesterterpene synthases in fungi are bifunctional (chimeric) with characteristic domain organization of a Cterminal prenyl transferase domain and an N-terminal terpene cyclase domain. The prenyl transferase domain generates the linear substrate geranyl farnesyl pyrophosphate (GFPP) from dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP) and isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP), and the terpene cyclase domain performs the subsequent cyclizations (Fig. 1). While GFPP is the preferred substrate of sesterterpene synthases, these enzymes exhibit a high degree of substrate plasticity as they can act on various sized isoprene diphosphate substrates $\mathrm { ( C _ { 1 5 } - }$ $\mathbf { C } _ { 3 5 } ]$ ). In addition to promiscuity in substrate utilization, a single enzyme can also produce multiple sesterterpene products, oen with different carbon skeletons.

Sesterterpene biosynthesis proceeds from the achiral and linear GFPP precursor into complex polycyclic chiral products with multiple stereocenters that are all dened in a single step. The overall process involves a reaction cascade via cationic intermediates that makes use of the initial conformation and inherent reactivity of the GFPP substrate. These cyclization cascades involve diverse carbocation chemistry including proton shis, hydride migrations, and rearrangements, as well as cation stabilization and selective termination of the cascade. Carbocation initiated cyclization events are generally the rst committed step in sesterterpenoid biosynthesis and yield terpene hydrocarbons or alcohols. These cascades result in complex molecular architectures with multiple rings and chiral centers that are constructed in a single step. Once the carbon scaffold is assembled by the sesterterpene synthase, structural diversication is largely accomplished with a variety of associated tailoring enzymes to generate the nal sesterterpenoid products. Unlike the bifunctional sesterterpene synthases found in fungi, genes encoding sesterterpene synthases recently discovered from bacteria and plants occur in clusters, with genes encoding an independent prenyl transferase and

a terpene cyclase co-located within a cluster. These enzyme pairs are also capable of generating diverse sesterterpenoid products. The prenyl transferase provides the GFPP substrate, which the associated cyclase then converts to complex products via similar carbocation mediated reaction cascades.

In addition to the individual studies that are cited in the following sections, there were three more general articles directly relevant to sesterterpene biosynthesis that have appeared recently. Chen, et al. provide a discussion of multidomain terpenoid synthases, including bifunctional sesterterpene synthases and how this type of enzyme architecture provides an evolutionary means for biosynthetic control and compound diversication.127 A paper by Yan, et al. describes methodologies for genome sequencing, in silico identication and analysis of terpene synthases, genetic manipulations, and the in vitro verication of recombinant protein function. Practical applications for detecting terpenes using HPLC and GC-MS were also detailed.128 In addition, there was an insightful and comprehensive review of cyclopentane-forming di- and sesterterpene synthases from fungi, bacteria, and plants.129

# 3.1 Biosynthesis in fungi

The rst sesterterpene synthase (AcOS), described in 2013 by the Oikawa laboratory, was actually discovered while screening the genome of Aspergillus clavatus for diterpene synthase genes.126 They isolated, identied, and functionally characterized the gene responsible for the biosynthesis of ophiobolin F 421 and designated it A. clavatus ophiobolin synthase (AcOS). Transformation of this gene into an A. oryzae expression system provided ophiobolin F, a well-known 5/8/5 tricyclic sesterterpene alcohol, as well as two new tricyclic $\mathbf { C } _ { 2 5 }$ hydrocarbons 422 and 423. The structure of 423 was conrmed by dehydration of ophiobolin F 421 to give the exo olen 423. A new 9/4 bicyclic sesterterpene hydrocarbon named clavaphyllene 424 was also obtained and characterized. This was the rst example of

a sesterterpene synthase that can generate two completely different carbon skeletons. Recombinant production of the AcOS protein in Escherichia coli revealed it was a bifunctional synthase with C-terminal prenyl transferase and N-terminal terpene cyclase domains. AcOS preferentially generated sesterterpene products, but a small amount of diterpenoid hydrocarbons were also produced by this enzyme.

The Oikawa group later identied and heterologously expressed four genes $\left( o b l A _ { A c } , o b l B _ { A c } , o b l C _ { A c } , o b l D _ { A c } \right)$ from ophiobolin biosynthetic gene clusters in A. clavatus that process and oxidize the initial ophiobolin cyclization products into the nal ophiobolin natural products.130 Similar gene clusters were also found in Bipolaris maydes and Emericella variecolor. The ophiobolin genes encode the bifunctional sesterterpene synthase OblA (also named AcOS), OblB a cytochrome P450 monooxygenase, OblC a avin-dependent oxygenase, and a transporter protein OblD. Transformation of A. oryzae with OblA lead to the production of ophiobolin F 421, while transformation with OblA and OblB, resulted in production of a series of oxidized intermediates including the new compounds 425–428. Mixing genes from the different fungi provided additional insight into the biosynthetic pathway of ophiobolins. Transformation with OblA from A. clavatus and OblB from B. maydes provided no new metabolites, but when the transporter OblD from B. maydes was added, then ophiobolin C 429 and several artifacts were produced. Transformation with OblA and OblC from A. clavatus along with OblB and OblD from B. maydes resulted in the production of another new compound 430.130

# 3.2 Biosynthesis in bacteria

Sesterterpenes are not common metabolites of cultured bacteria and are rarely reported from bacterial sources. However, a number of bacterial terpene synthases have demonstrated a broad spectrum of substrate tolerances as well as the ability to generate sesterterpene products. Two bacterial terpene synthases that can produce both $\mathbf { C } _ { 2 5 }$ and $\mathbf { C } _ { 3 0 }$ products were rst reported in 2013. The Masuda laboratory described a bacterial triterpene synthase named squalene-hopene cyclase (SHC) from Alicyclobacillus acidocaldarius that could convert the sesterterpene precursor all-$E$ -geranylfarnesol into ve cyclized sesterterpene alcohols 460–464. 148 It also converted 2Z-geranylfarnesol into 465, which is epimeric at C-18 with compound 460. All of the cyclized sesterterpene products were new structures except for the tricyclic compound 463. Later studies with SHC revealed it can also convert geranyfarnesol into new bicyclic 466, tricyclic 467, and tetracyclic products 468. 149 When geranyfarnesyl acetate was the substrate, SHC converted it into two tricyclic metabolites 469 and 470 and one tetracyclic compound 471. 149 Sato and co-workers reported a new sesterterpene/triterpene synthase (Bcl-TS) from Bacillus clausii that appears to lack a cyclization domain.150 They found it is a promiscuous enzyme that can modify $\mathbf { C } _ { 2 5 }$ or $\mathbf { C } _ { 3 0 }$ isoprene diphosphate substrates to produce $\beta$ -geranylfarnesene or $\beta$ -hexaprene hydrocarbons, respectively. It bwas later shown that Bcl-TS is a broad spectrum multifunctional terpene synthase that can catalyze the production of $\mathbf { C } _ { 2 5 }$ (sesterterpene), ${ \bf C } _ { 3 0 }$ (triterpene), and $\mathbf { C } _ { 3 5 }$ (sesquarterpene) products from geranylfarnesyl diphosphate (GFPP), heptaprenyl diphosphate (HexPP), and heptaprenyl (HepPP) substrates, respectively.151

Further evidence of substrate promiscuity in bacterial synthases was seen with spata-13,17-diene synthase (SpS), a terpene synthase isolated from the marine bacterium Streptomyces xinghaiensis.152 It demonstrated sesqui-, di-, and sesterterpene synthase activity when expressed in E. coli. When GFPP was the enzyme substrate, two new tricyclic sesterterpene hydrocarbons 472 and 473 were produced. Similar tricyclic diterpenoids lacking one isoprene subunit in the sidechain were produced when GGPP was the substrate. Isotope labeling studies supported a proposed cyclization pathway to generate the 5/4/5 and 4/5/5 tricyclic cores of 472 and 473, respectively.

A new sesterterpene synthase (StsC) was obtained from the bacterium Streptomyces somaliensis, characterized as a member of the UbiA superfamily of terpene synthases, and heterologously expressed in E. coli to provide somaliensenes A 474 and B 475, which are new sesterterpene hydrocarbons.153 Building upon biosynthetic studies of diterpenoids, Dickschat and co-workers proposed a theoretical terpene cyclization mechanism similar to that deduced for spinodiene diterpene synthase from Saccharopolyspora spinosa, but starting with GFPP as the substrate to generate two articial sesterterpene alcohols 476 and 477. 154 These two

In an effort to mitigate the fact that most bacterial biosynthetic gene clusters are not expressed under laboratory conditions, a system was developed by the Brady laboratory to activate the gene clusters with a series of new promoters.155 They

developed a scalable method to generate libraries of refactored biosynthetic gene clusters from bacteria by disassembling them using CRISPR techniques, adding synthetic promotors, and then reassembling the gene cluster in yeast using a transformation assisted recombination (TAR) strategy. This provides an efficient means for parallel, combinatorial refactoring of biosynthetic gene clusters with multiple different promotors. Using this approach, two new bacterial sesterterpene alkaloids atolypene A 478 and B 479 were produced by activation of the atolypene (ato) gene cluster. This type of promoter-enhanced gene cluster activation provides a means to potentially activate and express silent gene clusters, which represents a very promising approach for generating new, cryptic metabolites.

# 3.3 Biosynthesis in plants

Sesterterpenoids are widely distributed in plants and some of the initial studies of their biosynthesis focused on identication of biosynthetic intermediates. Research on metabolic pathways in the Chinese plant Leucosceptrum canum provided seven new biosynthetic intermediates that were isolated and identied as norleucosceptroids D–H 480–484, and leucosceptroids P 485, and Q 486. The $\mathbf { C } _ { 2 0 }$ 482–484 and $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 1 } 4 8 0$ , 481 compounds were clearly established as sesterterpene-derived metabolites. Further oxidative degradation studies supported the presence of two parallel metabolic pathways that are involved in leucosceptroid biosynthesis. Leucosceptroids are defensive metabolites and antifeedant activity for 480–482 against the cotton bollworm Helicoverpa armigera was observed with $\mathrm { E C } _ { 5 0 }$ values of 3.8, 10.9, and $7 . 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 2 }$ , respectively, suggesting that these leucosceptroid derived products are also involved in plant defense against herbivorous insects.156

Fortunately, plants have physically colocalized prenyl transferase (PT) and terpene synthase (TPS) gene pairs, which facilitates genome mining and subsequent analysis. # 4 Conclusions and outlook

The rst review on sesterterpenoids was published by J. R. Hanson in 1986 and it introduced 121 new sesterterpenoids.148 Since then, 846 additional sesterterpenoids were summarized in the reviews covering 1985 to 2012.1,2,149,150 Including the 498 sesterterpenoids discussed in this review, more than 1400 sesterterpenoids have been isolated and identied through 2019. New metabolites with interesting biological properties, such as alotaketals C–E 59–61, asperophiobolin D 163, cybastacine B 342, phorone B 363, and asperterpenoid A 393, illustrate the broad range of activity and potency oen associated with sesterterpenoids. While many of the reported biological activities reported for sesterterpenoids are related to cellular toxicity, this probably correlates more with the availability of cytotoxicity-

based assay systems than an inherent biological property of this class of metabolites. When sesterterpenoids are evaluated in other types of assays, a more diverse range of activities are oen uncovered such as enzyme inhibition, receptor modulation, HIV activation, and insect antifeedant properties. In addition to important bioactivities, many new sesterterpenoids provided novel and truly fascinating structural scaffolds such as involudispirones A 253 and B 254, dibritannilactone A 375, bipolarolides A–D 376–379, and asperterpenacids A–C 395–397, which have attracted widespread attention from pharmacologists and synthetic chemists. Efforts to discover, characterize, and develop new sesterterpenoids with important biological activities constitute a vibrant and expanding research area in the eld of natural products chemistry. The increasing interest in bioactive sesterterpenoid discovery from natural sources, along with the application of genomics, biosynthetic analysis, and synthetic biology for identifying and diversifying sesterterpene structures should facilitate signicant progress in nding and advancing new lead compounds for potential biomedical or biotechnological applications. Genome mining to identify sesterterpene synthases, coupled with domain swapping and modication, and increasingly effective means of heterologous expression, provide powerful new approaches to expand and accelerate sesterterpenoid discovery. It is anticipated that when these recently developed methodologies for producing and modifying the sesterterpene carbon skeletons can be effectively combined with the associated oxidative and other tailoring enzymes, the diversity of sesterterpenoid products will expand dramatically. Previous research on sesterterpenoids has been highly productive, and future efforts that incorporate and advance the evolving chemical, biological, and computational technologies should prove to be even more so.
