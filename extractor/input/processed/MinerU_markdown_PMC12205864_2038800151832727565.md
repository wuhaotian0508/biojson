# Summary

Plant viruses often suppress jasmonic acid (JA)-mediated defences through disturbing JA signalling or biosynthesis pathways to benefit their own infection. Few studies have examined how the precursors of JA biosynthesis are regulated by viral infection. In this study, we demonstrate that rice black-streaked dwarf virus (RBSDV) infection inhibits the production of $\pmb { \mathrm { \xleftarrow } }$ -linolenic acid (C18:3), a key JA biosynthesis precursor that is catalysed by a set of fatty acid desaturases (FADs). The viral capsid protein P10 directly interacts with OsFAD7, an x-3 fatty acid desaturase, and promotes its autophagic degradation through an ATG8-interaction motif (AIM). This disrupts JA production and weakens antiviral defence against RBSDV infection. Genetic analysis reveals that overexpression of OsFAD7 enhances JA levels and resistance to virus. But OsFAD7-mediated antiviral resistance is attenuated if OsCOI1a, a JA receptor, is silenced, indicating that the enhancement of resistance to RBSDV infection conferred by OsFAD7 depends on the JA pathway. Our findings reveal a novel viral strategy that suppresses JA biosynthesis at its metabolic source, providing insights for developing viral protection strategies and virus-resistant crops. 

# Introduction

Autophagy, a highly conserved mechanism in eukaryotes, primarily serves to degrade misfolded, unwanted and dysfunctional intracellular components. In plants, this process plays a crucial role in maintaining cellular homeostasis and responding to external stresses (Liu et al., 2005; Pohl and Dikic, 2019). During the autophagy process, multiple autophagy-related proteins (ATGs) cooperate to assemble autophagosomes (Mizushima et al., 2011). Among these ATGs, ATG8 is a key component involved in autophagosome assembly and targets specific cargo for degradation by interacting with various autophagy adapters and receptors (Michaeli et al., 2016). Proteins that interact with ATG8 typically possess an ATG8-interaction motif (AIM), recognized by the conserved sequence W/F/Y-X-X-L/I/V (Birgisdottir et al., 2013; Johansen and Lamark, 2020; Noda et al., 2008). Increasing evidence has suggested that autophagy plays a significant role in plant–virus interactions; however, these effects appear to be bidirectional (Yang et al., 2020a). On the one hand, the pathogenic proteins of various viruses or viral particles are degraded by the host autophagy pathway, resulting in interruption of the infection process. For example, the non-assembled cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV), a double-stranded DNA pararetrovirus and its particle-forming capsid proteins can be targeted 

by the autophagy cargo receptor NEIGHBOUR OF BRCA1 (NBR1) and degraded via autophagy, thereby restricting the establishment of CaMV infection (Hafren et al., 2017). Conversely, many viruses can manipulate the autophagy process to counteract antiviral defence, thereby promoting viral infection (Baumberger et al., 2007; Derrien et al., 2012; Li et al., 2019b; Michaeli et al., 2019). Certain viruses also utilize the autophagy pathway for their own replication and viral assembly. For instance, turnip mosaic virus (TuMV) hijacks the NBR1-mediated selective autophagy through the NIb-NbNBR1-NbATG8f interaction complex to target the NIb-containing VRC to the tonoplast for viral replication and virion assembly (Li et al., 2020). Therefore, the role of autophagy in the arms race between plants and viruses is complex and varies depending on the plant–virus pathogenic system, highlighting the need for further investigation. 

Jasmonic acid (JA), a key plant defence hormone, has been extensively studied for its pivotal role in plants immune responses against various invaders, including insects, fungi, bacteria, nematodes and particularly viruses (He et al., 2017, 2020b; Kachroo and Kachroo, 2009; Xie et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). In most plants, JA is synthesized through the octadecane pathway, with linolenic acid (C18:3) serving as the precursor (Liu et al., 2015). In the chloroplast, 13-lipoxygenases (13-LOXs), allene oxide synthase (AOS) and allene oxide cyclase 

(AOC) sequentially catalyse the conversion of C18:3 to 12-oxophytodienoic acid (OPDA). Subsequently, OPDA is converted to JA-Ile in the peroxisome by the enzymes OPR3 and ACX1, which are essential for JA perception and signalling (Delker et al., 2006; Fonseca et al., 2009; Wasternack and Song, 2017). Once JA-Ile is synthesized, it binds to the JA receptor coronatine insensitive 1 (COI1), a component of the SCF E3 ubiquitin ligase complex. This interaction specifically targets jasmonate-zim domain protein (JAZ) for ubiquitination and subsequent degradation via the 26S proteasome system (Kazan and Manners, 2012; Pauwels and Goossens, 2011; Thines et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2015). This process allows the released MYC2 to recruit MED25, a subunit of the mediator transcriptional coactivator complex, to activate the transcription of JA-responsive genes (Liu et al., 2019; Zhai et al., 2020). Numerous studies have highlighted the positive role of JA in enhancing plant resistance to viral infections (He et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2020b). To successfully infect their hosts, viruses often employ effector proteins to manipulate both the JA signalling pathway and JA biosynthesis pathway. For example, various RNA viruses in rice commonly target SLR1, a crucial negative regulator in the gibberellin (GA) signalling pathway, to directly suppress the broad-spectrum antiviral pathway mediated by JA via regulating SLR1 homeostasis in plants. This manipulation promotes viral infection and transmission (Li et al., 2022). The C2 protein of tomato yellow leaf curl virus and the P5-1 protein of rice black-streaked dwarf virus (RBSDV) inhibit the ubiquitination activity of SCF E3 ligases, thereby affecting gene transcription in the JA signalling pathway (He et al., 2020a; Li et al., 2019a). Furthermore, recent studies have shown that rice stripe virus P2 directly interferes with the physical association between rice histone deacetylase (OsHDA706) and OsLOX14, disrupting the deacetylation of OsLOX14 and consequently reducing JA accumulation, which facilitates viral infection (Yang et al., 2024). However, the regulatory mechanisms by which plant viruses affect JA-mediated host immunity are not fully understood, and it remains unclear whether the virus regulates the metabolic process of JA precursor. 

As mentioned, linolenic acid, the most abundant unsaturated fatty acid (UFA) in plants, serves as the precursor for JA biosynthesis. The relative abundance of different UFAs and their derivatives in plants is mainly regulated by fatty acid desaturases (FADs) and related enzymes (Kachroo and Kachroo, 2009). x-FADs are crucial for the desaturation of plant fatty acids and can be categorized into x-3 FADs (FAD3, FAD7 and FAD8) and x-6 FADs (FAD2 and FAD6) based on their catalytic functions. Specifically, x-3 FADs are responsible for synthesizing C18:3 from C18:2, while x-6 FADs facilitate the synthesis of C18:2 (Jusoh et al., 2015). Although FAD7 and FAD8, which are located in the chloroplast, have high homology and similar functions, they exhibit different responses to stress (Nair et al., 2009). In Arabidopsis, AtFAD7 is the dominant x-3 FAD in leaves and exhibits a strong specificity for biological and defence responses. Despite the similar subcellular localization of FAD7 and FAD8, their functions may not be redundant (Roman et al., 2015). Furthermore, Arabidopsis mutants deficient in FAD3, FAD7 and FAD8 demonstrate heightened sensitivity to oomycete infection (Browse, 2009). The levels of C18:3 and reactive oxygen species (ROS) in the fad7fad8 double mutant are significantly reduced, which increases susceptibility to certain bacterial pathogens (Yaeno et al., 2004). Silencing of FAD7 in tobacco increases 

susceptibility to bacterial, oomycete and viral infections (Im et al., 2004). These observations suggest that FAD-mediated FA desaturation plays a crucial role in plant–pathogen interactions. Disruption of this pathway often results in heightened host susceptibility, yet the molecular mechanisms by which pathogens regulate FAD-associated FA desaturation remain largely mysterious. 

RBSDV is a double-stranded RNA virus that belongs to the family Reoviridae and the genus Fijivirus. It is transmitted by the small brown planthopper (SBPH, Laodelphax striatellus) and poses a significant threat to major food crops such as rice, wheat and maize, causing rice black-streaked dwarf disease, wheat green dwarf disease and maize rough dwarf disease, respectively, thereby threatening food security (Wu et al., 2020). Genetic analyses have shown that the JA-insensitive rice mutants, such as coi1 and myc3, exhibit increased susceptibility to rice-infecting viruses including RBSDV. This indicates that JA is involved in the host resistance to RBSDV infection (He et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2020b). Meanwhile, RBSDV can suppress the JA signalling pathway through distinct viral proteins such as P5-1, P7-1 and P8 to benefit its own infection (Li et al., 2024). However, the effect of viral infection on the accumulation of $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -linolenic acid (C18:3), the key JA precursor, remains unknown. In this study, we uncover that the level of C18:3 unsaturated fatty acid is significantly reduced in RBSDV-infected rice plants. Further investigation reveals that the RBSDV capsid protein P10 interacts with OsATG8 and OsFAD7 to promote the degradation of OsFAD7 via the autophagy pathway. Consequently, the levels of C18:3 and JA are positively correlated with the expression level of OsFAD7 in rice plants. Genetic analysis demonstrates that the overexpression of OsFAD7 enhances resistance to RBSDV infection, while mutations in OsFAD7 lead to hypersensitivity to the virus. Our findings elucidate a combined mechanism involving the autophagy pathway, fatty acid desaturation and the JA-mediated antiviral response, which could be leveraged for future crop breeding strategies. 

# Results

# RBSDV infection reduces the level of unsaturated fatty acid C18:3 in rice

To investigate the impact of RBSDV infection on rice fatty acid metabolism, a targeted fatty acids metabolome analysis was carried out using liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS) on rice leaves at various time points following exposure to viruliferous SBPHs. At 7 days post-inoculation (dpi) with RBSDV, the levels of total saturated fatty acids (SFAs) and UFAs in rice showed no significant change compared with mock-inoculated controls. However, at the late infection stage (60 dpi), both SFAs and UFAs significantly declined: SFAs by twofold and UFAs by approximately fivefold (Figure 1a). Further analysis focused on the accumulation of C18 UFAs. At 7 dpi, RBSDV infection slightly elevated C18:1 and C18:2 levels but reduced C18:3 levels (Figure 1b), decreasing its proportion among all UFAs (Figure 1c). By 60 dpi, the levels of C18:1, C18:2 and C18:3 were significantly reduced by threefold, threefold and fivefold, respectively (Figure 1b), and the proportion of C18:3 in the total UFAs also significantly decreased (Figure 1c). These results suggest that the reduction in UFAs content during the early and late stages of RBSDV infection is primarily attributed to the decreased levels of C18:3.

Previous studies have demonstrated that changes in the ratios of different UFAs can reflect the activity of the corresponding FADs (Harwood, 1996; Shi et al., 2018). Therefore, the ratios of the three UFAs were further compared. The results showed that the C18:1/C18:0 ratio increased at both 7 dpi and 60 dpi, while the C18:2/C18:1 ratio remained unchanged, and the C18:3/C18:2 ratio decreased (Figure 1d–f). These findings suggest that the conversion of C18:2 to C18:3 is inhibited during RBSDV infection. In conclusion, RBSDV infection reduces UFAs content, particularly the level of C18:3, and may also affect the activity of FADs, which are key enzymes in UFA synthesis. 

# RBSDV P10 interacts with rice fatty acid desaturase OsFAD7

As mentioned, plant FAD7/8 is crucial for the catalytic synthesis of C18:3. Sequence analysis revealed that the amino acid sequences of FAD7/8 are highly conserved among plant species (Figure S1). The expression patterns of rice FAD7 (OsFAD7) and FAD8 (OsFAD8) were examined through the Rice Expression Profile Database (ricexpro.dna.affrc.go.jp/) and confirmed using GUS reporter transgenic lines. The results indicated that OsFAD7 may 

play a dominant role in rice, as it was highly expressed in roots, stems and especially in the leaves during the rice vegetative stage (Figure S2). By contrast, OsFAD8 was barely expressed during all growth stages of rice, suggesting that OsFAD7 is a primary fatty acid desaturase catalysing the production of C18:3. Consequently, OsFAD7 was used as the bait in a yeast two-hybrid assay to investigate the interactions between RBSDV-encoding proteins and OsFAD7. Yeast growth on selective medium (SD/-Trp-Leu-Ade-His, SD/-T-L-A-H) was observed when transformed with both OsFAD7 and the capsid protein P10, indicating a positive interaction between OsFAD7 and RBSDV P10 (Figure 2a). Bimolecular fluorescence complementation experiments further support this interaction, and the interaction occurs on chloroplasts (Figure 2c). These findings provide evidence of the interaction between OsFAD7 and P10 in vivo.

# P10 degrades OsFAD7 through the autophagy pathway

Based on the above, to determine whether P10 and even RBSDV affect the protein stability of FAD7, transgenic rice lines overexpressing Flag-tagged OsFAD7 (designated as OsFAD7 OE) were generated and fed by RBSDV viruliferous SBPH. As expected, OsFAD7 accumulation significantly decreased following RBSDV inoculation (Figure 3a). To further verify the degradation of OsFAD7 by RBSDV P10, protein stability assays were conducted in N. benthamiana. The results showed that increasing concentrations of Flag-tagged P10 (Flag-P10) led to a gradual decrease in the levels of GFP-tagged OsFAD7, indicating that P10 facilitates the degradation of OsFAD7 in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 3b, left panel). By contrast, when GFP-OsFAD7 was co-expressed with Flag-RFP, the protein level of OsFAD7 remained stable (Figure 3b, right panel). This confirms that RBSDV P10 promotes the degradation of OsFAD7. 

To investigate the degradation mechanism of OsFAD7, protein stability assays in N. benthamiana using E64d, an inhibitor targeting the autophagy pathway, and MG132, which inhibits the 26S proteasome pathway, were conducted. Results demonstrated that the levels of GFP-OsFAD7 significantly increased following E64d treatment when co-expressed with Flag-P10 (Figure 3c). By contrast, OsFAD7 expression continued to decrease following MG132 treatment. However, when co-expressed with Flag-RFP, the protein levels of GFP-OsFAD7 remained unchanged regardless of treatment with E64d, MG132 or absence of treatment (Figure 3d). 

To monitor the autophagic process in plant cells, typical autophagosomes were observed in epidermis cells of N. benthamiana expressing GFP-OsFAD7, as evidenced by the co-localization of GFP-labelled OsFAD7 and RFP-labelled NbATG8e, a known autophagosome marker (Figure 3e). 

Interestingly, in the presence of P10, more and larger autophagosomes formed, and the accumulation of GFP-labelled OsFAD7 was further reduced (Figure 3f,g). To confirm the occurrence of autophagy in virus-infecting rice plants, a transgenic line that overexpresses GFP-fused OsATG8b (GFP-OsATG8b OE) was employed. Compared with rice plants that were either mockinoculated or inoculated with a virus-free vector, the GFP-OsATG8b OE lines infected with RBSDV exhibited a significantly increased formation of autophagosomes (Figure 3h,i). Additionally, RBSDV infection markedly enhanced ATG8-PE accumulation (Figure 3j), indicating autophagy induction in rice upon RBSDV infection (Figure 3j). To verify whether RBSDV P10 induces autophagy, P10 was transiently expressed in N. benthamiana epidermal cells, and the number of GFP-labelled puncta was significantly increased (Figure 3k). These findings suggest that OsFAD7 may be degraded by P10 through the autophagy pathway during RBSDV infection. 

# P10 promotes OsATG8-mediated autophagic degradation of OsFAD7

During autophagy, ATG8 is essential as it directly identifies specific AIMs within target proteins or facilitates selective autophagy through dedicated cargo receptors to recognize substrates for degradation. After analysing the amino acid sequence of OsFAD7 through the online iLIR Autophagy Database (https://ilir.warwick.ac.uk/index.php), results discovered the presence of two AIM sequences, indicating that OsFAD7 might be a direct substrate for ATG8. These motifs, termed as AIM1 (226- ESWQPL-231) and AIM2 (350-KEWSYL-355) (Figure 4a), are likely involved in mediating the interaction between OsFAD7 and OsATG8s, thereby facilitating the degradation of OsFAD7. 

To examine the importance of these AIMs in the degradation of OsFAD7, mutations were introduced into the critical residues of AIMs within the full-length OsFAD7 protein (OsFAD7-mAIM1: W228A L231A and OsFAD7-mAIM2: W352A L355A) for further interaction investigations. Structure prediction through Alphafold2 illustrated that the structure of OsFAD7-mAIM1 differed from that of wild-type OsFAD7 (Figure 4a). In rice, six ATG8 homologues have been identified, including OsATG8a-f. To investigate whether OsFAD7 is targeted by these OsATG8s, OsFAD7 was used as a bait in Y2H assays to screen four isolated OsATG8 proteins from rice, specifically OsATG8a-d. Our results reveal that OsFAD7 interacts with OsATG8a, OsATG8b, OsATG8c and OsATG8d (Figure 4b). By contrast, mutations in AIM1 disrupted the interaction between OsFAD7 and the OsATG8 proteins, while mutations of AIM2 did not affect this interaction. Their interactions were further confirmed by BiFC assays (Figure 4c). These findings highlight the significance of the Nterminal AIM domain in the interactions between OsFAD7 and OsATG8s. 

To further investigate the role of OsATG8 in the degradation of OsFAD7, protein stability assays were conducted using Flagtagged OsATG8b (Flag-OsATG8b) in N. benthamiana. The results demonstrated that OsATG8b mediates OsFAD7 degradation in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 4d, left). When either GFP-fused OsFAD7-mAIM1 (GFP-OsFAD7-mAIM1) or GFP-fused OsFAD7- mAIM2 (GFP-OsFAD7-mAIM2) was co-expressed with Flag-OsATG8b, the abundance of OsFAD7-mAIM1 remained unchanged (Figure 4d, middle). However, the degradation of OsFAD7-mAIM2 continued to occur (Figure 4d, right). This suggests that the AIM1 motif in OsFAD7 is crucial for targeting by OsATG8b. 

To assess whether these AIMs are required for P10 targeting, Flag-P10 was co-expressed with either GFP-OsFAD7-mAIM1 or GFP-OsFAD7-mAIM2. The results revealed that when AIM1 was mutated, degradation did not take place (Figure 4e, left), while 

mutations in AIM2 allowed P10 to still promote the degradation of OsFAD7 (Figure 4e, right).

# OsFAD7 elevates C18:3 unsaturated fatty acid and enhances JA biosynthesis and signalling pathway

Given that OsFAD7 is the primary enzyme responsible for the synthesis of chloroplast C18:3, a precursor for JA biosynthesis (Figure 5a), the potential relationship between OsFAD7 and JA accumulation was examined. A stable transgenic rice line overexpressing OsFAD7 (OsFAD7 OE-4#) (Figure S5) and a gene-edicting-based mutant rice line (osfad7 KO-10#) (Figure S6) were selected for further analysis. The levels of C18:3, OPDA (an intermediate product of JA biosynthesis), JA and 

JA-Ile in wild-type rice plants and the corresponding transgenic rice lines were compared using LC–MS. The results indicated that compared with ZH11, the OsFAD7 overexpression lines exhibited elevated levels of C18:3, OPDA, JA and JA-Ile, while these compounds were significantly decreased in the osfad7 knockout lines (Figure 5b). These findings indicate that the overexpression of OsFAD7 increases the accumulation of C18:3 and promotes the biosynthesis of defence hormone JA. 

To evaluate the sensitivity of rice seedlings to C18:3 and JA, a series of physiological experiments with wild-type rice seedlings were conducted. The results indicated a significant reduction in shoot length among wild-type seedlings treated with C18:3 compared with the control group (Figure S7a,b). Additionally, the expression levels of JA-responsive genes such as OsPR1a and OsWRKY28 were significantly increased in wild-type seedlings treated with C18:3 (Figure S7c). Notably, rice plants treated with C18:3 displayed similar shoot length to the control but showed a significantly reduced level of P10 RNA, indicating a positive role of C18:3 in antiviral defence (Figure S7d–f). Consistent with previous studies, treating wild-type rice seedlings with MeJA resulted in a significant decrease in shoot length, whereas seedlings treated with diethyldithiocarbamic acid (DIECA), an inhibitor of JA biosynthesis, displayed no change in growth (Figure S8a,b). Treatment with MeJA enhanced the resistance of wild-type rice seedlings against RBSDV infection, as demonstrated by the mild disease symptoms (Figure S8c,d), and decreased accumulation of P10 RNA compared to treatments with DIECA and Tween 20 (Figure S8e). Two JA-responsive genes, OsPR1a and OsMYB61, were significantly up-regulated by MeJA treatment but were slightly down-regulated by DIECA treatment (Figure S8f). These findings imply that C18:3 unsaturated fatty acids exert effects on plant growth that are comparable to those of the defence hormone JA.

# OsFAD7 enhances resistance to RBSDV through the JA pathway

To assess the role of OsFAD7 in the plant’s response to RBSDV infection, virus inoculation experiments using OsFAD7 overexpression and knockout lines were conducted. Compared with ZH11, the OsFAD7 OE lines exhibited milder symptoms, specifically showing reduced dwarfing and fewer black streaks on their leaves (Figure 6a). Furthermore, both mRNA and protein levels of RBSDV P10 were significantly suppressed in the OsFAD7 overexpression lines (Figure 6b,c). By contrast, the osfad7 knockout lines displayed more severe symptoms than ZH11, characterized by decreased height and more pronounced black streaks on the leaves (Figure 6a), along with a notable increase in the accumulation of RBSDV P10 RNA and protein (Figure 6b,c). These observations indicate that OsFAD7 positively regulates rice resistance to RBSDV. 

To verify whether OsFAD7-mediated resistance to RBSDV is dependent on the JA pathway, RBSDV-inoculated OsFAD7 overexpression lines with DIECA and RBSDV-inoculated osfad7 knockout lines were treated with MeJA. Virus accumulation was then assessed by evaluating the P10 RNA and protein levels. The shoot length in the seedlings of ZH11, E.V., OsFAD7 overexpression and knockout lines before DIECA and MeJA treatments and RBSDV inoculation were comparable (Figure 6d, e). Following treatment, DIECA-treated OsFAD7 overexpression lines showed a slight increase in shoot length, whereas the shoot length of osfad7 knockout lines treated with MeJA decreased significantly (Figure 6d,f). Importantly, P10 RNA and protein levels in the DIECA-treated OsFAD7 overexpression lines remained similar to those in the control group, whereas the MeJA-treated osfad7 knockout lines exhibited reduced levels of P10 RNA and protein (Figure 6g,h). 

To genetically illustrate the importance of the JA pathway in OsFAD7-mediated antiviral defence, we generated knockdown lines of OsCOI1a, a key JA receptor, in the ZH11 background and in OsFAD7 overexpression lines (designated OsCOI1a IR and OsCOI1a IR/OsFAD7 OE-4#), followed by inoculation with RBSDV. OsCOI1a knockdown lines (OsCOI1a IR and OsCOI1a IR/OsFAD7 OE-4#) exhibit more severe disease symptoms compared to those with OsFAD7 overexpression (Figure 7a). Additionally, the levels of RBSDV P10 RNA and protein were significantly elevated in OsCOI1a knockdown lines compared to those with only OsFAD7 overexpression (Figure 7b,c). These results suggest that inhibiting JA biosynthesis through DIECA treatment or suppressing JA signalling by silencing OsCOI1a expression in the OsFAD7 overexpression lines impairs OsFAD7-mediated resistance to RBSDV. MeJA treatment assays were also performed to assess their sensitivity to JA. The results indicated that the inhibition of shoot length in OsFAD7 overexpression rice seedlings by MeJA was alleviated following the silencing of OsCOI1a expression (Figure 7d,e). The expression of JA-responsive genes in these rice lines inoculated with RBSDV was also measured. The results showed that the expression levels of OsPR1a, OsPR5 and OsPR10 in OsCOI1a knockout lines were significantly lower than those observed in ZH11 and OsFAD7 OE-4# line (Figure 7f). Overall, 

these findings demonstrate that an active JA pathway is essential for OsFAD7-mediated defence against RBSDV in rice. 

# Discussion

There is no doubt that infections caused by plant viruses can reprogram the lipid metabolome of the host. Plant viruses, as obligate intracellular parasites lacking their own cellular structures, rely entirely on the lipid-rich endomembrane systems of their hosts for rapid genome replication, virion assembly and systemic infection. However, research on the regulation of lipid metabolism in rice plants infected by different viruses is very limited. Present studies found that RBSDV infection significantly reduces the levels of UFAs, particularly C18:3, in diseased rice plants. Our findings demonstrate that the viral protein P10 promotes the degradation of OsFAD7 through autophagy via direct interaction. Genetic analysis confirms the essential role of OsFAD7 not only in JA biosynthesis but also in enhancing resistance to RBSDV infection. Overall, our study demonstrates that RBSDV infection significantly reduces the accumulation of UFAs, particularly linolenic acid (C18:3), by degrading OsFAD7 through autophagy. This leads to a suppression of JA biosynthesis and compromises antiviral defence (Figure 7g). Given the taxonomic similarity between southern rice black-streaked dwarf virus (SRBSDV) and RBSDV (both belonging to the genus Fijivirus, family Reoviridae; Zhou et al., 2013), we extended our investigation to examine whether SRBSDV SP10 exhibits similar effects on OsFAD7. Both BiFC and GST pull-down assays confirmed a direct physical interaction between SP10 and OsFAD7 (Figure S9a,b). When Flag-tagged SP10 and GFP-tagged OsFAD7 were co-expressed in N. benthamiana, we observed a progressive decrease in OsFAD7 protein levels corresponding with increasing SP10 concentration (Figure S9c), indicating SP10- mediated degradation of OsFAD7 occurs in a concentrationdependent manner. These findings suggest that the proposed degradation mechanism, wherein viral P10 proteins target OsFAD7 for autophagic degradation, represents a conserved strategy employed by multiple fijiviruses, including both RBSDV and SRBSDV. 

JA signalling pathway represents a critical battlefield in the ongoing arms race between plants and viruses. Upon sensing a virus invasion, plants rapidly synthesize JA, which is then recognized by COI1 to form a COI1-JA complex. The complex recruits jasmonate-zim domain protein (JAZ) proteins for polyubiquitination and degradation via the 26S proteasome pathway, thereby activating the expression of downstream genes crucial for plant defence responses. Conversely, viruses have evolved numerous strategies to inhibit JA signalling and counteract the plant defence system (Li et al., 2024). While there are a limited number of studies addressing how viruses suppress JA biosynthesis, our previous work indicated that rice ragged stunt virus (RRSV) induces the post-transcriptional silencing of OsTCP21, resulting in elevated levels of miR319 and decreased JA levels, ultimately rendering the plant more susceptible to the virus (Zhang et al., 2016). Similarly, the turnip mosaic virus P1 protein interacts with the chloroplast protein cpSRP54, leading to cpSRP54 degradation, and thereby inhibiting JA biosynthesis by disrupting the transfer of allene oxide cyclase (AOC) to the thylakoid membrane (Ji et al., 2021). The 126 kDa protein from pepper mild mottle virus and the p25 protein from potato virus X can also trigger the degradation of cpSRP54, promoting viral infection (Ji et al., 2021). FAD7 function has been shown to enhance tomato defences against aphids in a salicylate-dependent manner (Avila et al., 2012). A recent study illustrated the critical role of NbFAD4 in TuMV replication and suppression of immunity-associated phytohormone signalling in N. benthamiana (Fang et al., 2024). Despite numerous studies highlighting the involvement of FADs in plant defence mechanisms, the molecular processes that underpin these defences, especially in the context of plant viruses, are not fully understood (Kachroo and Kachroo, 2009). Our study showed that overexpressing OsFAD7 significantly improves RBSDV resistance in rice. While previous research has noted crosstalk between OsFAD7 and JA, their relationship in viral resistance remains undetermined. Importantly, our data indicate that the enhancement of RBSDV resistance conferred by OsFAD7 is reliant on an active JA pathway, as evidenced by the attenuation of antiviral resistance observed in the OsFAD7- overexpressed rice plants with OsCOI1a knockdown. These findings fill in the gap in how viruses regulate fatty acid metabolism to counteract JA-mediated antiviral defence. 

Emerging studies have highlighted the crucial role of the autophagy pathway in interactions between plants and pathogens. In particular, the autophagy pathway displays a dual role in plant resistance. As an antiviral strategy, autophagy can specifically target essential viral components or viral particles for degradation, thereby enhancing plant protection against the virus (Hafren et al., 2017; Haxim et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018). In turn, viruses can manipulate the autophagy pathway to inhibit the antiviral defence pathways including antiviral RNAi, phytohormone and other immune systems, facilitating successful infection (Baumberger et al., 2007; Cheng and Wang, 2016; Derrien et al., 2012; Li et al., 2019b; Michaeli et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2018). This interplay varies depending on the plant–host interaction systems. Additionally, autophagy’s role in lipid metabolism and storage has been detailed across various species (Elander et al., 2018; Jaishy and Abel, 2016; Shatz et al., 2016; Wang, 2016; Zechner et al., 2017). However, when plants are challenged by viruses, how the autophagy pathway regulates fatty acid metabolism and its effects on viruses have remained unknown. Our study reveals that RBSDV P10 promotes the autophagic degradation of OsFAD7 through direct interaction with OsATG8, thereby impairing downstream JA biosynthesis. AlphaFold and BiFC assays identified distinct interaction domains on P10 for OsFAD7 and OsATG8, explaining how P10 hijacks autophagy to degrade OsFAD7 and disrupt JA signalling (Figure S10). These results imply that P10 utilizes different domains for its interactions with OsATG8 and OsFAD7, thereby promoting the ATG8-mediated autophagic degradation of OsFAD7. Taken together, our study not only expands our understanding of the linkage of lipid metabolism and JAmediated defence response but also reveals a novel viral pathogenesis strategy that couples autophagy-dependent protein degradation with lipid signalling regulation. These insights will be valuable for breeding virus-resistant crops and devising new strategies to boost plant immunity. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials, growth conditions and virus inoculation

The wild-type rice cultivar ZH11 was used in this study. osfad7 knockout lines were generated using the CRISPR/Cas9 system, and the OsFAD7 transgenic lines were obtained via the agrobacterium-mediated transformation method (Hiei et al., 

1994). OsATG8b-GFP overexpression transgenic lines were generously provided by Professor Mingyong Zhang from the Center of Economic Botany, Core Botanical Gardens, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Guangzhou, China. All rice lines were grown in a growth chamber under a 14-h light $( 3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C ) / 1 0 { \cdot } { } h$ dark $( 2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ photoperiod or in an experimental field in Zhengzhou, China. Nicotiana benthamiana plants were cultivated in a greenhouse with a 16-h light $( 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ and 8-h dark $( 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ photoperiod for transient expression assays. 

RBSDV-infected rice plants were maintained in the laboratory. Virus inoculation was carried out using the small brown planthopper (SBPH, Laodelphax striatellus). Briefly, first- or second-instar SBPH nymphs were allowed to feed on RBSDVinfected rice plants for 3 days before being transferred to wildtype rice plants for an additional 12 days. RBSDV viruliferous SBPHs were then moved to two-week-old rice seedlings (three nymphs per plant) and allowed to feed for 3 days. After removing the insects, the inoculated rice plants were grown inside a glasshouse or in the experimental field for symptom observation. RBSDV infection in the inoculated plants was verified by RT-PCR using RBSDV-specific primers and western blotting with an RBSDV P10-specific antibody. 

# RNA extraction and quantitative real-time PCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted from rice samples using Trizol reagent (Bioconnet, Beijing, China) and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until further use. The first-strand cDNA was synthesized from $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of total RNA using the HiScript- II Q RT SuperMix kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ , following the manufacturer’s instructions. Next, $3 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the diluted cDNA was used for RTqPCR analysis in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ with ChamQ SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme) on a PCR machine (LC96, Roche, Basel, Switzerland). Rice Actin 1 gene was used as the internal reference. The primer sequences used in quantitative real-time PCR are listed in Table S1. 

# Protein extraction and western blotting

Total proteins were isolated from plant tissues using a lysis buffer (50 mM Tris, pH 7.5; 300 mM NaCl; 1 mM PMSF; $1 \%$ Cocktail protease inhibitor). The proteins were then separated by SDSpolyacrylamide gel electrophoresis. Membrane transfer, blocking, antibody incubation and ECL chemiluminescence detection were performed according to the manufacturer protocols (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). The primary antibodies used in this study were: GST (1:5000, Transgen, Beijing, China), MBP (1:8000, Transgen), Flag (1:5000, Transgen), GFP (1:5000, Abmart, Shanghai, China) and Actin (1:10000, Abmart). The secondary antibodies used were horseradish peroxidase-conjugated Anti-HRP (1:8000, Abmart). The blots were detected using the ECL Substrate Kit (Herui, Fujian). 

# BiFC assays

The coding sequences of OsFAD7, OsATG8a, OsATG8b, OsATG8c and OsATG8d from ZH11, as well as P10 from RBSDV-infected rice, were amplified by RT-PCR. These sequences were then cloned into pMDC43-nYFP and pMDC43-cYFP vectors to create fusion constructs: P10-nYFP, OsFAD7-cYFP, OsFAD7- nYFP, P10-cYFP, OsATG8a-nYFP, OsATG8b-nYFP, OsATG8cnYFP, OsATG8d-nYFP. Each plasmid was transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101 strain using the freeze/thaw method, and the positive clones were cultivated overnight at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in LB medium containing $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ kanamycin and $5 0 ~ \mu \gamma / \mu \nu L$ 

rifampicin. Subsequently, the cultures were suspended and incubated for $3 \textrm { h }$ in an infiltrating buffer (10 mM ${ \sf M g } { \sf C l } _ { 2 }$ $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone and 10 mM 2-[N-Morpholino] ethane sulfonic acid) at room temperature. The BiFC assays were performed on 4-week-old N. benthamiana leaves that were infiltrated with a combination of Agrobacterium cultures harbouring the respective plasmid pairs. Interaction signals of YFP were observed using a Zeiss LSM510 META laser scanning microscope at $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ post-infiltration. YFP was excited at 514 nm and detected between 565 and 585 nm. Confocal images were then captured and analysed. To ensure accuracy, at least three independent experiments were performed. 

# Co-IP assays

Full-length OsFAD7 was cloned into the pMDC43 vector, which includes a CaMV35S promoter and a green fluorescent protein (GFP) tag at the N-terminus of the fused protein, using Gateway technology (Invitrogen) to generate the plasmid pMDC43- OsFAD7. Free GFP was used as a negative control. Flag-tagged P10 was co-expressed with GFP-tagged OsFAD7 or free GFP in four-week-old N. benthamiana leaves. Total protein was extracted from 1 g agro-infiltrated N. benthamiana leaf tissue. After centrifugation at $1 3 \ 6 8 0 \ 9$ and $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 15 min, the supernatant was incubated with $4 ~ \mu \ L$ anti-GFP at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ Subsequently, $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of magnetic beads (Abmart, Shanghai) was added and incubated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The beads were washed five times, and the protein samples were separated by SDS-PAGE gels. Target proteins were detected by Western blotting using either GFP or Flag antibodies. 

# Y2H assays

The full-length OsFAD7 and P10 sequences were cloned into the pPR3-N and pBT3-STE vectors to generate plasmids pPR3-N-OsFAD7, pPR3-N-P10, pBT3-STE-OsFAD7 and pBT3-STE-P10, respectively. Yeast strain NMY51 was transformed with pairs of these plasmids and cultured on selective medium lacking Tryptophan (T) and leucine (L) (synthetic dextrose (SD)/-T-L) for 3 days. Co-transformants were then transferred and cultured on selective medium lacking T, L, adenine (A) and histidine (H) (SD/- T-L-A-H) and SD/-T-L-A-H with X-a-gal for 4 days to assess potential interactions. 

# Recombinant protein expression assays

To produce MBP-P10 fusion protein, the full-length P10 coding sequence was amplified by PCR and cloned into the pMAL-c2X vector. To generate GST-OsFAD7-N and GST-OsFAD7-C fusion proteins, OsFAD7-N (N-terminal region of OsFAD7, 1–151 aa) and OsFAD7-C (C-terminal region of OsFAD7, 152–458 aa) sequences were PCR-amplified and cloned into the pGEX-4T-3 vector. All recombinant and empty plasmids (GST-tag) were transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells and induced with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG, Sigma, 092M4001V, Darmstadt, Germany). The MBP-tagged and GSTtagged fusion proteins were expressed and subsequently purified using amylose resin (NEB, E8032LVIAL, Ipswich, MA, USA) and GST Bind Resin (Millipore, EM70541-5, MA, USA), respectively. 

# In vitro pull-down assays

For the GST pull-down assay, $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of GST, GST-OsFAD7-N or GST-OsFAD-C was incubated with the supernatant containing MBP-P10 using $4 0 ~ \mu \ L$ GST beads at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight with continuous slow shaking. After incubation, the GST resin was washed three times 

with detergent to remove any nonspecific proteins. The proteins bound to the GST resin were eluted, separated by SDS-PAGE and analysed by immunoblotting using an anti-MBP antibody. Input proteins were also separated by SDS-PAGE and assessed by immunoblotting to confirm their presence in the assays. 

# Subcellular localization assays

Plasmid pMDC43-OsFAD7 and the chloroplast membrane marker were separately transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The Agrobacterium cultures were suspended and adjusted to an ${ \mathsf { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.5 with the infiltrating buffer prior to infiltration into the fully expanded leaves of N. benthamiana. The localization pattern of GFP-OsFAD7 protein was observed and imaged using a Zeiss LSM510 META laser scanning microscope $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ postinfiltration. GFP was excited at 488 nm, and the emitted signal was captured at 505–530 nm. DsRed was excited at 543 nm, and its signal was captured at 590–630 nm. Each experiment was independently repeated three times. 

# In vivo protein degradation assays

To investigate the effect of RBSDV P10 on the stability of OsFAD7 protein, GFP-OsFAD7 and Flag-P10 were co-expressed in fully expanded leaves of N. benthamiana. The protein levels of Flag-P10 or Flag-tagged OsATG8b were adjusted by controlling the concentration of infiltrating fluid. At 48 h post-infiltration, the transformed N. benthamiana leaves were collected for Western blotting analysis. For control experiments, Agrobacterium cultures containing GFP-OsFAD7 and Flag-RFP were coinfiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. 

To assay the inhibitory effects on protein degradation, infiltration buffer containing dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), DMSO with $1 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ E64d (an autophagy inhibitor; GLPBIO, Montclair, CA, USA) or DMSO with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ MG132 (a proteasome inhibitor; GLPBIO-Montclair, CA, USA) was infiltrated into the leaves $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ before collecting samples for Western blotting analysis. 

# Autophagy activation experiments

For the autophagy activation experiment, 10-day-old GFP-fused OsATG8b transgenic seedlings and free GFP transgenic seedlings were fed by SBPH with or without RBSDV. At 7 days postinoculation, the roots were excised from the rice seedlings, washed with sterile water, and the epidermal cells in the root elongation zone were observed under a confocal microscope. The number of autophagosomes was compared between different treatments. 

# Fatty acid quantification

Methods to perform targeted FAs metabolomics followed the procedure described previously. An ultra-high performance liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (UHPLC–MS/MS) system (ExionLCTM AD UHPLC-QTRAP $6 5 0 0 +$ ; AB SCIEX Corp., Boston, MA) was used to quantify FAs by Novogene Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). Separation was achieved using a Waters ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column $( 2 . 1 \times 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ maintained at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The mobile phase consisted of $0 . 0 5 \%$ formic acid in water (solvent A) and Isopropanol/acetonitrile (1:1) (solvent B) and was delivered at a flow rate of 0.30 mL/min. The solvent gradient was set as follows: initia $30 \%$ B, 1 min; $3 0 { \mathrm { - } } 6 5 \% \ 8 , 2$ min; $6 5 { - } 1 0 0 \%$ B, 11 min; $100 \%$ B, 13.5 min; $100 { - } 3 0 \%$ B, 14 min; $30 \%$ B, 15 min. The mass spectrometer was operated in negative multiple reaction mode (MRM) mode. To ensure data accuracy, at least four biological replicates were performed for each sample. Fatty acid content measurements are presented in Table S2. 

# JA, JA-Ile and OPDA quantification

Approximately 100 mg of completely ground sample powder was weighed and placed into a 2 mL centrifuge tube. Subsequently, $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of extraction liquid (methanol:water $= 8 0 { : } 2 0$ , V/V) and 10 ng of the internal standard of each hormone isotope to be measured were added. After rotating mixing, ultrasonic extraction was carried out for 30 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 5 min, the supernatant was transferred to a new centrifuge tube. The residue was mixed with 1 mL methanol, ultrasonically extracted for 30 min and centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 5 min. The resulting supernatant was combined twice, concentrated to dryness using a vacuum concentrator, redissolved in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of methanol, filtered through a $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ organic filter membrane and finally transferred to a vial for LC–MS/MS detection. This process was conducted with three biological replicates. 

# GUS staining

The promoter sequences of OsFAD7 and OsFAD8 were cloned from total rice DNA and, respectively, inserted into the pMDC163 vector. The plasmids were transformed into rice plants using the Agrobacterium-mediated transformation method. The leaf, root and stem of the resulting rice plants were subjected to GUS histochemical staining at 7, 20 and 60 days after germination, respectively. The GUS staining involved infiltrating the samples with $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sodium phosphate (pH 7.0), $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ EDTA and 0.5 mg/mL X-gluc (Apollo Scientific, UK). The staining was followed by incubation overnight at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark. Prior to photographing, the samples were destained using $70 \%$ ethanol. 

# Hormone and fatty acid treatments

Methyl jasmonate (MeJA; MACKLIN, shanghai, China) with $9 8 \%$ purity was dissolved in a small amount of $100 \%$ ethanol and then diluted with sterile distilled water to prepare a $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ solution immediately before use. A stock solution of $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ DIECA was prepared by dissolving DIECA in sterile water to obtain a $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ solution. MeJA and DIECA were further diluted in nutrient solutions to yield 1 and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ solutions, respectively, which were used to treat 10-day-old wild-type rice seedlings. 

To investigate the effect of the JA pathway on OsFAD7- mediated resistance to RBSDV infection, transgenic seedlings were inoculated with RBSDV using viruliferous SBPHs for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Following this, OsFAD7 overexpressing and osfad7 mutants were sprayed with $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ DIECA or $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ MeJA solution, respectively. For the sensitivity test, overexpressing transgenic plants and mutant plants were grown on rice 1/2MS medium containing MeJA at concentrations of 0, 0.25 or $1 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ for 10 days, and the shoot and root lengths of the 10-day-old seedlings were measured. Negative controls included nutrient solutions or sterile water with $0 . 0 2 \%$ (v/v) Tween20. Each treatment involved at least 30 rice seedlings. 

For the fatty acid treatments, C18:3 (MACKLINshanghai, China) was dissolved in appropriate $G C \ge 9 9 . 8$ dimethyl sulfoxide and then added to autoclaved nutrient solutions to achieve a final concentration of $5 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ . For negative controls, nutrient solution with $0 . 0 5 \%$ (v/v) dimethyl sulfoxide was used.
