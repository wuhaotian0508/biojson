# ABSTRACT

Nitrogen and auxin uptake play pivotal roles in seed germination and development. Gastrodia elata, a fully mycoheterotrophic plant, depends entirely on its symbiotic association with Mycena for early growth and seed germination. The process by which Mycena supplies nitrogen nutrients and auxin, which are deficient in G. elata, remains poorly understood. In this study, genome-scale analysis of G. elata revealed the loss of genes associated with nitrogen utilization and indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) biosynthesis, which are retained in Mycena. Further analysis of the dynamic transcriptomic interactions between G. elata seeds and Mycena across different symbiotic stages revealed that genes involved in nitrogen- and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis were significantly upregulated in Mycena during the symbiotic germination of G. elata seeds. Concurrently, G. elata seeds exhibited increased expression of genes involved in the hormone signal transduction pathway and the starch and sucrose metabolism pathway. Functional disruption of nitrite reductase (MyNir, EVM0012344) and amidase (MyAmid, EVM0010270), key enzymes in nitrogen assimilation and IAA biosynthesis in Mycena, significantly impaired the symbiotic germination of G. elata seeds. This disruption interfered with energy supply, caused cellular restructuring, and altered hormonal signaling crosstalk. In conclusion, our findings provide novel insights into the mutualistic symbiotic relationship between Mycena and G. elata. Specifically, the fungus Mycena compensates for the incomplete nitrogen metabolism of its plant partner, G. elata, and supplies IAA, thereby promoting seed germination. These results shed light on plant-fungal symbiotic associations from the perspective of nitrogen utilization.

# INTRODUCTION

Symbiotic associations between microbiota and plants influenced the evolutionary trajectory of terrestrial plants during their transition 

from marine environments over the past 450 million years (Heckman et al., 2001; Brundrett and Tedersoo, 2018; Yuan et al., 2018). The primary function of these symbiotic relationships and interactions is integrated nutrient exchange 

and transmission, enabling plants to overcome nutrient acquisition challenges in soil ecosystems (van der Heijden et al., 2015; Martin et al., 2016; Delaux, 2017; Genre et al., 2020; Miyauchi et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2025; Qiu et al., 2025). Orchids exhibit some of the most specialized and diverse symbiotic relationships with mycorrhizal fungi, as demonstrated by their varied and complex nutritional strategies. Based on how adult orchids assimilate carbon (C), they are categorized as photoautotrophic, mixotrophic, or fully mycoheterotrophic (Leake, 1994; Julou et al., 2005; Mennes et al., 2013; Li et al., 2022). Multiple orchid species exhibit mixotrophy, with young plants deriving C from fungi prior to the development of photosynthetic capabilities while simultaneously obtaining nitrogen (N) through root interactions (Leake 2004; Yuan et al., 2018; Ho et al., 2021). The most extreme form, full mycoheterotrophy, occurs in achlorophyllous plants whose seeds lack nutrient storage tissues such as endosperm and cotyledons, rendering them nutritionally dependent on fungi throughout their life cycle (Yuan et al., 2018; Jacquemyn and Merckx, 2019). All mycoheterotrophic orchids rely on their fungal partners for seed germination and nutrient absorption (Cameron et al., 2006; Bidartondo and Read, 2008; Kinoshita et al., 2016; Hua et al., 2023). 

Gastrodia elata is a fully mycoheterotrophic species widely used in traditional Chinese medicine. It has highly degenerative leaves and bracts and is often described as a leafless, rootless plant by field guides and systematists (Yuan et al., 2018). Its seeds consist of a tiny spherical proembryo encased in a thin seed coat, but unlike most seed plants, they lack essential storage tissues such as an endosperm and cotyledons. As in other mycoheterotrophic species, its proembryo contains only minimal nutrient reserves, insufficient to support germination or early development, necessitating reliance on fungal partners for essential nutrients (Ogura-Tsujita et al., 2009; Hsieh et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). A unique feature of G. elata is its requirement for at least two fungal partners during its life cycle: Mycena spp. for seed germination and Armillaria mellea for subsequent growth (Genre et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2020). 

N is a key macronutrient essential for the biosynthesis of amino acids, nucleotides, hormones, and chlorophyll, and it plays a fundamental role in plant metabolism, growth, and development (Kemppainen and Pardo, 2013; Zhang et al., 2024). Orchids are highly dependent on their fungal partners for N supply during seed germination and development, especially during early developmental stages (Bougoure et al., 2010; Fochi et al., 2017; Zahn et al., 2023). Stable isotope labeling has demonstrated that Rhizanthella gardneri acquires up to $2 2 . 5 \%$ of N supplied as labeled glycine from the soil via its fungal partner within 240 h (Bougoure et al., 2010). Previous research has shown that N acquisition in orchids is strongly influenced by the N form preference of their fungal partners (Fochi et al., 2017; Zahn et al., 2023). Genetic information for Tulasnella calospora has revealed that this fungal partner preferentially utilizes ammonium $( N H _ { 4 ^ { + } } { - } N )$ and organic N over nitrate $( \mathsf { N O } _ { 3 ^ { - } } { - } \mathsf { N } )$ (Fochi et al., 2017). Stable isotope signatures suggest that both specialized and unspecialized Rhizoctonia-associated orchids selectively transfer protein-derived N into orchids (Zahn et al., 2023). Additionally, orchid growth, persistence, and seed germination are sensitive to nitrate availability, which influences 

their distribution and habitat preferences (Figura et al., 2020). N also serves as a precursor for hormone biosynthesis (e.g., indole-3-acetic acid [IAA] synthesized from tryptophan and cytokinin derived from adenine, an N-containing purine), which regulates physiological and biochemical processes in plants and participates in the initiation and regulation of seed germination. However, it remains unclear whether the fungal partner Mycena assimilates nitrate to provide N and N-related hormones to meet the requirements of G. elata seed germination. 

Genetic information is essential for elucidating the symbiotic relationships between fungi and plants. Previous research has shown that extensive loss of photoautotrophic genes renders G. elata incapable of fixing C. As a result, G. elata must establish symbiotic relationships with Mycena and Armillaria, which decompose woody material to facilitate C acquisition by G. elata (Yuan et al., 2018). The genome of the orchid fungal partner T. calospora encodes two functional ammonium transporters and several amino acid transporters but lacks a nitrate assimilation system—features that determine the N absorption preferences of its symbiotic plant partner (Fochi et al., 2017). However, the genetic basis of nitrate assimilation by Mycena and its role in supplying N to G. elata remain elusive. 

In this study, we use genome sequencing analysis to elucidate the genetic basis of N assimilation and N-derived hormone biosynthesis in Mycena, processes critical for the germination of G. elata seeds. Interactive transcriptomic analysis revealed changes in genes associated with N source utilization, as well as hormone synthesis and signaling in Mycena and G. elata seeds throughout the symbiotic process. Homologous knockout techniques were applied to comprehensively clarify the functions of genes involved in N assimilation and IAA synthesis in Mycena, given its contribution to the symbiotic germination of G. elata seeds. Our findings demonstrate that Mycena compensates for the N-utilization deficiencies of G. elata. This compensatory action promotes seed germination and provides novel insights into the mechanisms of plant-fungal symbiosis, particularly regarding N supply.

# Nitrogen utilization and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis genes are lost in G. elata but retained in Mycena

As G. elata lacks nutrient storage and absorption tissues such as endosperm and roots, genes annotated with ‘‘N utilization’’ and ‘‘N-derived phytohormone’’ functions were strongly represented among the missing genes in its genome and transcriptome (Figure 2B). To further investigate the putative functions of these missing genes, we examined those related to N metabolism (map00910) and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis (map00380). The G. elata genome contained only 13 nuclear genes encoding N metabolism proteins (including glnA, GLT1, and GLUD1_2), and these were involved in transforming ammonia into glutamine and glutamate. Genes required for nitrate/nitrite assimilation to ammonia and for organic N absorption (e.g., nitroalkane, formamide, and nitrile) were absent (Figure 2B; Supplementary Table 10). However, one ammonia transporter gene and 15 aquaporin genes involved in ammonia transport were present in G. elata. Only 21 nuclear genes coding for tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis were detected in the G. elata genome, including 3 of 6 IAA biosynthetic apparatus proteins (indole pyruvate, indole-3-acetaldehyde, and indole-3-acetamide) (Figure 2C; Supplementary Table 11). PCR confirmed the absence of conserved domains for these genes in G. elata (Supplemental Figure 3B). Additionally, IAA was not detected in either G. elata seeds or protocorms (Figure 2E). The absence of genes involved in N assimilation and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis suggests impaired N utilization in G. elata, preventing it from independently acquiring N. 

The Mycena genome and transcriptome analyses revealed 41 nuclear genes encoding a complete set of N metabolism proteins, including those required for inorganic and organic N absorption and assimilation (Figure 2B; Supplementary Table 8). In addition, 64 nuclear genes encoding tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthetic enzymes were present in the Mycena genome, including 3 of the 6 IAA biosynthetic apparatus proteins relevant to G. elata seed germination (indole acetonitrile, indole-3-acetaldehyde, and indole-3-acetamide) (Figure 2C; 

Supplementary Table 9). PCR detection of the conserved domains of these genes confirmed their presence in Mycena (Supplemental Figure 3A). Furthermore, three plant hormones (IAA, zeatin, and abscisic acid [ABA]) were highly detectable in both the cultures and hyphae of Mycena (Figure 2D). The presence of a complete N assimilation system and tryptophandependent IAA biosynthesis pathway in Mycena suggests its ability to assimilate and metabolize inorganic and organic N and to produce IAA, thereby supplying essential N nutrients and IAA to facilitate G. elata seed germination. 

# Nitrogen utilization and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis genes in Mycena contribute to the symbiotic germination of G. elata seeds

To investigate the roles of mycelia in N utilization and IAA biosynthesis during G. elata seed germination, we conducted transcriptome profiling across symbiotic stages. Substantial changes in gene expression were observed, with 1,504, 1,087, 2,178, 2,289, 2,330, and 3,010 upregulated genes and 1,021, 1,981, 2,766, 2, 236, 2,209, and 2,769 downregulated genes at 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 dac, respectively (Supplemental Figure 3A). Functional enrichment analysis revealed that the N metabolism pathway (map00910) was significantly enriched at 10 and 20 dac, while the tryptophan metabolism pathway (map00380) was prominently enriched at 30, 40, and 50 dac (Supplemental Figure 4A). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) further confirmed the significant enrichment of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in these pathways (Supplemental Figure 4B and 4C). Cluster and correlation analyses of N metabolism DEGs revealed upregulation of genes encoding 2-nitropropane dioxygenase (EVM0026874 and EVM0004286), acetamidase/ formamidase (EVM0019604 and EVM0004291), glutamate dehydrogenase (EVM0012300), nitrate reductase (EVM0025330), nitrate/nitrite transporter (EVM0010776), and NAD(P)H nitrite reductase (EVM0012344) at 10, 20, and 30 dac (Supplemental Figure 5A and 5B). Among these, 2-nitropropane dioxygenase (MyNpd, EVM0026874), formamidase (EVM0004291), glutamate dehydrogenase (MyGdhA, EVM0012300), nitrate reductase (MyNr, EVM0025330), nitrate/nitrite transporter (MyNrt, EVM00 10776), and ${ \mathsf { N A D } } ( { \mathsf { P } } ) { \mathsf { H } }$ nitrite reductase (MyNir, EVM0012344) were positively correlated with the seed germination rate (Pearson’s $\mathsf { r } > 0 . 6 )$ (Supplemental Figure 5C and 5D), indicating the involvement of the N pathway in G. elata seed germination. Cluster and correlation analyses of DEGs related to tryptophan metabolism showed that amidase (EVM0010270, EVM0024263, and EVM0006733) and fatty acid hydroxylase (EVM0017510) were upregulated during symbiosis (Supplemental Figure 6A and 6B). Eleven IAA biosynthesis genes, particularly amidase (MyAmid, EVM0024263, EVM0006733, and EVM0010270) and aldehyde dehydrogenase (MyALDH, EVM0023058 and EVM0014104), were positively correlated with the seed width-tolength ratio (Pearson’s $\mathsf { r } > 0 . 7$ ) (Supplemental Figure 6C and 6D), reflecting the involvement of IAA biosynthesis in G. elata seed germination. As representative key enzymes, MyNir and MyAmid were selected for functional validation. 

Plant hormone signal transduction and starch and sucrose metabolism pathways in G. elata seeds respond to Mycena during symbiotic germination 

Genome-wide transcriptome profiling of G. elata protocorms symbiotic with Mycena was performed at 5–50 dac to further examine how G. elata seeds respond to Mycena. A total of 2,104, 3,400, 3, 279, 3,161, 3,020, and 169 genes were upregulated, while 2,144, 3, 092, 2,675, 2,346, 2,394, and 10,232 genes were downregulated at 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 dac, respectively (Supplemental Figure 3B). Functional enrichment analysis showed significant enrichment of the plant hormone signal transduction pathway and the starch and sucrose metabolism pathway at 5, 10, 20, 30, and 40 dac (Supplemental Figure 7A). GSEA further confirmed the significant enrichment of DEGs in these pathways, which were correlated with the enrichment factor (Supplemental Figure 7B and 7C). Cluster analysis showed that 22 auxin response genes, seven cytokinin response genes, three gibberellin (GA) response genes, and six brassinosteroid response genes (involved in cell division, elongation, and germination) were upregulated at 5–40 dac (Supplemental Figure 8A). In contrast, nine ABA response genes, five ethylene response genes, and three jasmonic acid response genes (involved in seed dormancy and senescence) were downregulated during symbiosis (Supplemental Figure 8B). Genes involved in polysaccharide (starch and cellulose) biosynthesis were downregulated (Supplemental Figure 8C), whereas those related to polysaccharide degradation were upregulated during symbiosis with Mycena (Supplemental Figure 8D). 

Disruption of genes involved in N utilization and tryptophan-dependent IAA biosynthesis in Mycena blocks symbiotic seed germination in G. elata 

Deletion mutants were generated for MyAmid and MyNir genes to elucidate their function in symbiotic G. elata germination (Supplemental Figure 9). Growth assays showed that the wild type (WT) displayed slightly weaker growth on MM- $\cdot N H _ { 4 } ^ { + 1 }$ , MM-${ \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - 1 }$ , and MM- ${ \sf N H _ { 4 } N O _ { 3 } }$ than on MM. By contrast, the ΔMyNir mutant exhibited significantly impaired growth on all four medium types, particularly on MM- $\cdot N H _ { 4 } ^ { + 1 }$ and MM- ${ \cdot } { \mathsf { N H } } _ { 4 } { \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 }$ , compared with WT and ΔMyAmid (Figure 3A and 3B). Liquid culture assays showed similar results (Supplemental Figure 10). Compared to WT, ΔMyAmid and ΔMyNir displayed a significantly reduced ability to invade and expand on sawdust cornmeal (SCM) medium (Figure 3C and 3D). Expression of MyAmid and MyNir was significantly downregulated in their respective mutants (Figure 3E and 3F), and IAA biosynthesis was reduced by $6 1 . 3 3 \substack { - 6 9 . 2 8 \% }$ in ΔMyAmid and $3 7 . 2 2 \substack { - 3 8 . 5 7 \% }$ in ΔMyNir (Figure 3G). Additionally, tryptophan and tryptamine contents were significantly reduced in ΔMyNir (Figure 3H and 3I), confirming that impaired N assimilation limits tryptophan biosynthesis. 

The symbiotic germination assay revealed significantly reduced seed germination rate and width-to-length ratio in ΔMyAmid and ΔMyNir compared to WT (Figure 4A–4C). The expression of auxin-responsive genes and starch, glycogen, and cellulose degradation genes in G. elata was significantly reduced during mutant symbiosis, while biosynthesis genes were significantly upregulated (Figure 4D). Ultrastructural observation revealed 

markedly reduced infection capability of mutant hyphae in suspensor cells, with irregular, disrupted mycelia and thicker apical meristem cell walls compared to WT (Figure 4E). 

Principal component analysis (PCA) of the metabolome data of G. elata protocorms obtained via ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry showed that WT was clearly separated from the mutants during symbiosis (Figure 5A). There were 370, 514, 475, and 427 upregulated compounds and 628, 429, 357, and 469 downregulated compounds in ΔMyAmid #2, ΔMyAmid #3, ΔMyNir #1, and ΔMyNir #14 symbioses, respectively (Figure 5B; Supplemental Figure 11). The levels of single sugars (e.g., L-arabinose and UDP-galactose), starch/glycogen/cellulose degradation intermediates (e.g., cellobiose and lactose 6-phosphate), glucose derivatives (e.g., (E)-2-O-cinnamoyl- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranose and 1-O-transcinnamoyl- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranose), and $\alpha$ -amino acids/derivatives were significantly reduced in mutant symbioses (Figure 5C and 5D). Additionally, GA and zeatin contents decreased, whereas the levels of ABA and its derivatives increased (Figure 5E–5L). These results indicate that the disruption of N assimilation and IAA biosynthesis in Mycena blocks G. elata seed germination by impairing the conversion of polysaccharides to monosaccharides and by altering hormone crosstalk. 

# DISCUSSION

Roots are typically the primary tissues for N acquisition. Although G. elata is rootless, N acquisition remains essential for its growth. In rootless orchids, full mycoheterotrophy has evolved through extensive gene loss and accelerated substitution rates in the genome (Zhang et al., 2024). Genome and transcriptome analyses revealed that G. elata has lost most N absorption/ assimilation genes, retaining only ammonia transporter genes (one GeAMT and 15 aquaporins; Figure 2B). Genes involved in the conversion of ammonia into amino acids via the GS/ glutamine–2-oxoglutarate aminotransferase cycle are still present, indicating that G. elata cannot utilize nitrate $( \mathsf { N O } _ { 3 } , \mathsf { \bar { \Pi } } - \mathsf { N } )$ or certain forms of organic N (e.g., nitroalkane, formamide, and nitrile). The enhanced ${ \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ uptake capacity of Rhizophagus irregularis does not involve the upregulation of plant nitrate transporter genes (Wu et al., 2020), suggesting that the mycorrhizal pathway represses direct root uptake—a process that may have contributed to N gene loss in G. elata. Because nitrate uptake consumes more energy than ammonium uptake, G. elata likely evolved to rely on passive, low-energy ammonium uptake to cope with its inability to fix C. Similar to arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, Mycena possesses a complete set of N absorption/assimilation genes (Figure 2B), allowing it to reduce nitrate to ammonium for G. elata germination. MyNir deletion impaired nitrate utilization and reduced G. elata germination (Figure 4A–4C), confirming that Mycena compensates for N deficiencies in G. elata. 

Symbiotic interactions also dynamically regulate the N utilization of mycorrhizal fungi. Previous studies have shown that genes involved in nitrate utilization are upregulated fungal partners during plant-fungal symbiosis (Kemppainen and Pardo, 2013). Mycena genes encoding nitrite/nitrate transporters, nitrate reductase, and nitrite reductase were significantly induced during symbiosis, peaking at 20 dac (Supplemental Figure 4). Hormone crosstalk governs the transition between dormancy and germination (Shu et al., 2016). Although ABA and GAs are the primary regulators of this process, auxin also plays a critical role by stimulating ABA signaling to modulate germination (Shu et al., 2016; Mei et al., 2023). In G. elata, hormone signal transduction pathways were strongly induced during symbiosis with Mycena (Supplemental Figure 7), with auxin, cytokinin, GA, and brassinosteroid pathways upregulated and ABA, ethylene, and jasmonic acid pathways downregulated (Supplemental Figure 8A). Expression of ABF, a negative regulator of seed germination, was significantly repressed (Supplemental Figure 8B), consistent with previous findings that loss of function of such regulators promotes germination (Albertos et al., 2015). Disruption of MyNir or MyAmid reduced the expression of auxin-responsive genes (SAUR and SAUR50) in G. elata and inhibited germination (Figure 4A and 4D), confirming that fungal-derived auxin modulates ABA signaling to drive germination. 

Seed germination is an energy-intensive process that involves extensive cellular restructuring. Sugars are critical for this process as both primary energy sources and signaling molecules (Gomez et al., 2009; Tsai and Gazzarrini, 2012). Seed starch degradation provides carbohydrates and energy for germination and early growth (Peng and Harberd, 2002), and $\alpha$ -amylase activity involved in starch degradation is significantly higher in the loss-of-function mutant of a negative regulator of seed germination (Fu et al., 2023). Consistently, our transcriptomic data revealed that G. elata upregulated genes involved in starch, glycogen, and cellulose degradation while downregulating biosynthesis genes during symbiosis (Supplemental Figure 8C), a metabolic shift also observed in germinating seeds of other plant species, such as Cinnamomum migao (Huang et al., 2021). Disruption of MyNir or MyAmid impaired G. elata seed germination (Figure 4A) and reversed this metabolic balance: auxin-responsive and polysaccharide degradation genes were downregulated and biosynthesis genes were upregulated (Figure 4D). In addition, seed germination involves embryo cell elongation—an energyconsuming process associated with cell wall loosening and remodeling (Xue et al., 2021). Ultrastructural analysis further showed that protocorms associated with mutant fungi developed thicker apical meristem cell walls (Figure 4E), resembling germination defects caused by impaired cell wall remodeling (Yang et al., 2021). These observations indicate that 

disruption of fungal IAA biosynthesis blocks auxin signaling, energy mobilization, and cellular restructuring, collectively inhibiting germination. 

This study elucidates the genetic and physiological basis of the Mycena–G. elata symbiosis (Figure 6). Genomic analysis revealed that G. elata lacks key genes for N utilization and IAA biosynthesis, which are retained in Mycena. Transcriptomic and functional validation demonstrated that N assimilation (via MyNir) and IAA biosynthesis (via MyAmid) in Mycena drive G. elata seed germination by coordinating hormonal crosstalk, energy metabolism, and cellular restructuring. These findings provide a new framework for understanding how fungal symbionts regulate orchid germination, extending beyond nutrient supply to encompass hormone regulation and metabolic coordination. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Strains and plants

Mycena purpureofusca strains SHXG and YSWM were obtained from Guizhou Wumengteng Fungi Industry Co., Ltd. (Bijie, China). Gastrodia elata seeds were obtained from the same company. Mycelia were grown on potato dextrose agar (PDA) plates and maintained in SCM flasks at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in the dark. 

# Symbiotic seed germination of G. elata

To determine the germination-promoting ability of Mycena on G. elata seeds, the mycelium of Mycena strain YSWM was inoculated at the center of a sterilized dialysis cellulose membrane sheet (8030-32, Cellu Sep T-Series, Seguin, TX, USA) placed in an 8-cm SCM flask and cultured at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in the dark. When the mycelium had fully covered the SCM flask after 14 days of cultivation, disinfected G. elata seeds were evenly and uniformly spread in the flask. Disinfected seeds without Mycena served as controls. Germination of G. elata seeds was observed at 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 dac under an Olympus SZX7 stereo microscope (Hachioji-shi, Tokyo, Japan). The germination rate was calculated, and the width-to-length ratio (swelling index) of seeds or protocorms was measured using ImageJ software (ver. 1.54k) to quantify progressive morphological changes during early development, namely baseline water uptake in non-germinated seeds (S group) and developmental enlargement during the transition to protocorms (MS group). The increase in seed germination rate was calculated using the following formula: seed germination increase rate ${ ( \mathrm { i } ) } ^ { = }$ (number of germinated seeds at $\mathsf { s t a g e } _ { \mathrm { ( i ) } } -$ number of germinated seeds at stage(i− 1))/total number of seeds. Moreover, G. elata seeds or protocorms in symbiosis with Mycena were collected at different stages of co-culture and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for interactive transcriptome sequencing.

# Disruption of MyNir and MyAmid genes and GFP tag expression in Mycena

Disruption of MyNir (EVM0012344) and MyAmid (EVM0010270) was achieved by homologous recombination using a disruption vector (Supplemental Figure 6) containing 0.7–1.2 kb upstream 

and downstream homologous arms of the target gene, a hygromycin resistance cassette (hph, $5 0 \ \mu \ g \ \mathsf { m } \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ selection), and a GFP reporter gene. Agrobacterium tumefaciensmediated transformation was used to introduce the vector into Mycena strain YSWM (Song et al., 2013, 2016). Transformants were selected on PDA containing $5 0 ~ { \mu \ g } ~ \mathsf { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ hygromycin (14 days, $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , and dark conditions) and subcultured twice to stabilize resistance. Positive mutants were further confirmed by GFP fluorescence (Olympus BX53, 488-nm excitation) and quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (real-time qPCR) (Supplementary Table 12), showing reduced MyNir/MyAmid expression. Genotypes and molecular characterization of all generated Mycena mutant strains are detailed in Supplemental Figure 6.

High-performance liquid chromatography was performed on an Agilent 1260 Infinity II platform (Agilent) to detect the supernatant. Authentic reference standards of IAA, ABA, and zeatin (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) were used for quantitative analysis.

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR

Samples of Mycena mycelium and G. elata protocorms, as described above, were used for RNA extraction. Total RNA was extracted with a TransZol Up Plus RNA Kit (TransGen Biotech, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. In a $2 0 \mathrm { - \mu L }$ reaction system, $1 \ \mu \mathfrak { g }$ of RNA was used to synthesize first-strand cDNA with the All-in-One First-Strand Synthesis MasterMix Kit (Aladdin Scientific, Riverside, CA, USA), following the manufacturer’s instructions. RTqPCR was performed with the Taq SYBR Green qPCR Premix Ex II Kit (Takara Bio, Dalian, Liaoning, China) using $1 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 1:20 diluted cDNA as the template on a Bio-Rad iQ2 PCR System (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) (Yuan et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023).
