# ABSTRACT

In cereal crops, the endosperm synthesizes large quantities of proteins, including storage proteins and functional factors essential for the accumulation of storage reserves. The unfolded protein response (UPR) monitors the folding of nascent polypeptides in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) to alleviate cellular stress. However, the molecular mechanisms linking the UPR to endosperm development in plants remain poorly understood. In this study, we isolated and characterized a rice (Oryza sativa L.) mutant with defective endosperm development, designated as floury endosperm27 (flo27). Molecular cloning revealed that FLO27 encodes RISBZ1/bZIP58, an endosperm-specific transcription factor co-expressed with seed storage protein (SSP) genes and starch biosynthesis-related genes in rice. The flo27 mutant exhibited severe ER stress, along with upregulation of UPR-related genes. Notably, RISBZ1 interacts with bZIP50 and bZIP60 to antagonistically suppress downstream UPR genes. These interactions simultaneously downregulate SSP and starch biosynthesis-related genes, ultimately leading to reduced dry matter accumulation. In conclusion, these findings demonstrate that RISBZ1 functions as a ‘‘brake signal’’ to mitigate ER stress, thus enhancing our understanding of the trade-off between grain filling and adaptation to adverse environmental conditions in rice.

# INTRODUCTION

In eukaryotic cells, the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) serves as the site for the synthesis and folding of membrane and secretory proteins, which represent a large proportion of total protein output (Lin et al., 2008). These proteins are properly folded and modified within the ER lumen before transport to their functional destinations. Under biotic and abiotic stresses (e.g., pathogen infection, heat stress) or during developmental stages that require increased secretory protein synthesis (e.g., cell expansion), the ER’s biosynthetic capacity can become overwhelmed. ER stress is a potentially lethal condition that arises when misfolded and 

unfolded proteins accumulate in the ER (Ron and Walter, 2007; Vitale and Boston, 2008; Liu and Howell, 2010b). To alleviate ER stress, cells activate a sophisticated signaling cascade known as the unfolded protein response (UPR). The UPR restores ER homeostasis by modulating gene expression to enhance proteinfolding capacity and eliminate aberrant proteins through ER quality control and the ER-associated degradation (ERAD) pathway (Harding et al., 1999). 

Recently, molecular genetics studies have started to elucidate the regulatory mechanisms of the UPR in plants. Two major UPR signaling branches have been identified: one mediated by 

the inositol-requiring enzyme 1 (IRE1)-bZIP60 module, the plant counterpart of the animal IRE1-XBP1 pathway, and another mediated by site-1/site-2 proteases (S1P/S2P)-bZIP17/bZIP28, which corresponds to the animal activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6) pathway (Liu et al., 2007a; 2007b; Gao et al., 2008; Liu and Howell, 2010a; Deng et al., 2011; Nagashima et al., 2011). In Arabidopsis thaliana, bZIP60 (AtbZIP60) is a transcription factor normally localized to the ER membrane (Iwata and Koizumi, 2005). Under ER stress, IRE1 mediates unconventional splicing of AtbZIP60 mRNA, producing a transcript with a frameshift that encodes a protein without a transmembrane domain. The resulting AtbZIP60 protein relocates to the nucleus, where it activates downstream genes (Deng et al., 2011). Although AtbZIP60 is regulated by cytoplasmic splicing similarly to HAC1 and XBP1, its functional properties substantially differ from those of HAC1 and XBP1 (Iwata and Koizumi, 2012). Notably, AtbZIP60 possesses a transactivation domain at its N-terminus (Iwata et al., 2009), in contrast to yeast HAC1 and animal XBP1, whose transactivation domains are located at the C-terminus only after splicing. In animals, the two IRE1 homologs also exhibit distinct functions (Bertolotti et al., 2001; Iwawaki et al., 2001). In Arabidopsis, AtIRE1A and AtIRE1B share functional redundancy in the ER stress response, whereas AtIRE1C represents an unconventional isoform that lacks a luminal domain and participates in the regulation of gametogenesis (Nakamura et al., 2011; Pu et al., 2019). Similar to ATF6, AtbZIP28 is a type II transmembrane protein; its N-terminal bZIP domain is oriented toward the cytoplasm, and its C-terminal region resides in the ER lumen. Normally, AtbZIP28 is localized to the ER. Under ER stress, however, AtbZIP28 is transported to the Golgi apparatus, where it is cleaved by the Golgi-resident proteases AtS1P and AtS2P; the processed form then translocates to the nucleus to regulate downstream gene expression (Howell, 2013). AtbZIP17 and AtbZIP28 share similar protein structures, domains, and S1P proteolytic cleavage sites. The UPR pathways are well conserved in rice (Oryza sativa) (Zhou et al., 2022). OsbZIP50, the rice ortholog of AtbZIP60, is activated by OsIRE1-dependent unconventional mRNA splicing (Hayashi et al., 2012; Lu et al., 2012). OsbZIP60, the ortholog of AtbZIP28 in rice, is activated presumably through proteolysis (Hayashi et al., 2013). 

Given the importance of the UPR in maintaining cellular homeostasis, its magnitude and duration must be tightly regulated. The mechanisms that modulate the UPR are critical for plant survival. Although most studies have focused on activation of the UPR, less is known about how the response is attenuated to preserve cell viability. Several UPR ‘‘brake’’ genes have been identified, including yeast Snf1 (Mizuno et al., 2015); metazoan Wolfram syndrome 1 (WFS1) (Fonseca et al., 2010); NON-EXPRESSOR OF PATHOGENESIS-RELATED GENES1 (NPR1) (Lai et al., 2018), BLISTER (BLI) (Hong et al., 2019), CONSTITUTIVE EXPRESSION OF PATHOGENESIS-RELATED GENES5 (CPR5) (Meng et al., 2017), and ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) (Nawkar et al., 2017) in Arabidopsis. Despite this progress, the mechanisms underlying UPR regulation remain largely unknown, especially in rice, a staple crop for more than half of the global population. 

Rice seeds accumulate large quantities of starch and storage proteins during grain filling. In addition to starch biosynthesis-

related enzymes and seed storage proteins (SSPs), multiple transcription factors are essential for this process, including RICE SEED bZIP1 (RISBZ1), Rice Prolamin-box Binding Factor (RPBF), and OsNAC20/26 (Onodera et al., 2001; Kawakatsu et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2020). The rice basic leucine zipper transcription factor RISBZ1 (also known as OsbZIP58) regulates the biosynthesis of SSPs and starch by directly binding to GCN4 and ACGT motifs, respectively, in the promoters of storagerelated genes (Onodera et al., 2001; Wang et al., 2013). Similarly, the maize ortholog of RISBZ1, Opaque2 (O2), is a key transcription factor required for storage reserve accumulation during seed development (Qiao et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016; Deng et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023). To support the synthesis of these storage reserves, endosperm cells must maintain sufficient levels of molecular chaperones to ensure proper protein folding. However, during ER stress—such as under the high temperatures frequently encountered during grain filling—these chaperones may become overwhelmed in the endosperm. Therefore, the mechanism by which rice endosperm coordinates dry matter accumulation with precise control of the UPR remains unresolved. 

In this study, we identified and characterized the role of RISBZ1/ OsbZIP58 in UPR signaling during rice endosperm development. Mutation of RISBZ1 led to substantial upregulation of downstream UPR genes. Under ER stress conditions, RISBZ1 indirectly regulates these genes by interacting with bZIP50 and bZIP60 to repress their DNA-binding activities. These interactions suppress the expression of SSP and starch biosynthesis-related genes, reducing dry matter accumulation. Taken together, our results highlight the central role of RISBZ1 in alleviating ER stress and provide new insights into the coordination between ER stress responses and storage reserve accumulation during rice seed development.

# Reduced reserve accumulation in flo27 grain

To identify key regulators of endosperm development in rice, we screened for mutants with altered endosperm characteristics. The floury endosperm27 (flo27) mutant was isolated from a transfer DNA insertional mutant pool generated from rice (Oryza sativa ssp. japonica cv. Dongjin). During the vegetative stage, flo27 plants exhibited growth and development comparable to those of Dongjin. At the heading stage, flo27 plants were slightly shorter, although tiller number and panicle length remained similar to the wild type (Dongjin) (Supplemental Figures 1A–1D). During grain development, flo27 showed a significantly lower grain-filling rate than Dongjin, leading to a partially opaque mature endosperm with a shrunken, floury-white region extending from the center to the ventral side of the grain (Figures 1A– 1D; Supplemental Figure 1E). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) showed that starch granules in the mature endosperm of flo27 were round and loosely packed in the floury-white region, in contrast to the densely packed, crystalline granules observed in Dongjin (Figures 1E–1H).

# RISBZ1 mutation induces severe ER stress in developing endosperm

ORF13 encodes the bZIP transcription factor RISBZ1/bZIP58, an ortholog of maize O2. To identify the molecular pathways disrupted in flo27 that contribute to abnormal endosperm development, we conducted RNA sequencing analysis on developing endosperm collected at 14 DAF from both flo27 and Dongjin. Relative to Dongjin, flo27 showed upregulation of 384 genes ( ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 }$ fold change ${ > } 1$ ) and downregulation of 314 genes ( ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 }$ fold change $< - 1 \}$ ), with a false discovery rate $< 0 . 0 5$ (Supplemental Figure 4). In line with the impaired accumulation of starch and storage proteins in flo27, many differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were related to the biosynthesis of these storage reserves (Supplemental Dataset 1). Intriguingly, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes and Gene Ontology enrichment analyses indicated that many DEGs were significantly enriched in pathways related to protein processing in the ER, unfolded protein binding, and heat shock protein binding. These findings suggest a role for RISBZ1 in regulating the UPR during endosperm development (Figures 2A and 2B; Supplemental Dataset 1). Subsequent RT–qPCR analysis confirmed elevated expression of multiple UPR-related genes in the flo27 endosperm. These included genes encoding protein disulfide isomerase-like enzymes (PDILs), binding proteins (BiPs), heat shock proteins (HSPs), calreticulin (CRT), calnexin (CNX), the ER-localized DnaJ family member 3 (ERDJ3), nucleotide exchange factor (NEF), UDP-glucose transferase 4 (UGT4), membraneassociated bZIP transcription factors (bZIP50, bZIP39, and bZIP60), the ERAD-related protein Derlin, and the programmed cell death (PCD) marker NPR1 (Figures 2C–2F). Immunoblot analysis also revealed increased accumulation of ER stressrelated proteins BiP1 and PDIL1-1 in flo27 endosperm at 14 DAF (Figure 2G). Notably, BiP1 and PDIL1-1 protein levels in the complemented lines (Com) were restored to those observed in the wild type (Supplemental Figure 5). Moreover, unconventional splicing of bZIP50 mRNA (bZIP50s) was obviously elevated in endosperm 

cells of flo27 but was absent in Dongjin (Supplemental Figure 6), further supporting the involvement of RISBZ1 in regulating the UPR during rice endosperm development. 

O2, the counterpart of RISBZ1 in maize, is also a key transcription factor for storage reserve accumulation during seed development (Qiao et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016; Deng et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023). O2 has also been reported to affect the expression of HSP family genes (Guo et al., 2012), which are generally associated with heat stress and the UPR (Yu et al., 2015). To determine whether O2 plays a similar role in regulating the UPR in maize, we performed RT–qPCR analysis of developing maize grains from wild-type and opaque2 (o2) mutant plants. Only a few ER stress-related genes showed slight upregulation in the o2 mutant, whereas most were unchanged or significantly downregulated (Supplemental Figure 7). These findings indicate that the expression profiles of UPR-related genes in maize substantially differ from those in rice, implying functional divergence between O2 and RISBZ1, or speciesspecific differences in endosperm development between rice and maize. 

# RISBZ1 physically interacts with bZIP50 and bZIP60 during ER stress

Rice bZIP50 and bZIP60, orthologs of AtbZIP60 and AtbZIP28, respectively, are key bZIP transcription factors that function as transducers of ER stress signaling (Hayashi et al., 2012; Cao et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022). RT–qPCR analysis showed that both bZIP50 and bZIP60 were significantly upregulated in flo27 (Figure 2E). Given the involvement of RISBZ1 in ER stress regulation and its classification as a bZIP transcription factor, we investigated potential interactions between RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60. 

We first examined their subcellular localization by transient expression in rice leaf sheath protoplasts under normal and ER stress conditions induced by $1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ dithiothreitol (DTT), a known ER stress inducer. RISBZ1 localized to the nucleus under both conditions (Supplemental Figure 8). Under non-stress conditions, bZIP50-green fluorescent protein (GFP) and GFPbZIP60 fluorescence colocalized with the red fluorescence of the mCherry-KDEL ER marker (Figure 3A). However, after DTT treatment, both bZIP50 and bZIP60 translocated to the nucleus (Figure 3A), implying ER-to-nucleus relocation in response to ER stress. Consistent with these results, bZIP50 and bZIP60 showed typical ER localization in protoplasts from Dongjin, whereas, in flo27, both proteins mainly localized to the nucleus, reflecting the presence of intrinsic ER stress (Figure 3A; Supplemental Figure 9). Therefore, we hypothesized that RISBZ1 interacts with bZIP50 and bZIP60 in the nucleus during ER stress.

# bZIP50 and bZIP60 directly regulate UPR downstream genes

To determine whether bZIP50 and bZIP60 directly contribute to the UPR, knockout mutants for both genes were generated using the CRISPR–Cas9 system (Supplemental Figures 10A and 10B).

# RISBZ1 negatively regulates the UPR during ER stress

We found that RISBZ1 expression was significantly upregulated in developing grains of bzip60 (Figure 6A), suggesting that RISBZ1 is responsive to ER stress at the transcriptional level. To further investigate the biological significance of RISBZ1 under ER stress, we overexpressed RISBZ1 in isolated rice protoplasts treated with 10 mM DTT to assess its regulatory role in vivo. Our analysis revealed significant downregulation of all tested UPR-related genes in response to RISBZ1 overexpression (Figure 6B). We also attempted to overexpress the RISBZ1 CDS using CaMV35S and Ubiquitin promoters in the Dongjin background; however, none of the transgenic lines showed a substantial increase in RISBZ1 expression for unknown reasons. As an alternative approach, we generated double-knockout mutants of bZIP50 and RISBZ1 (bzip50 risbz1), as well as bZIP60 and RISBZ1 (bzip60 risbz1) (Figures 6C and 6D). Phenotypic analysis demonstrated that grains of both double mutants exhibited a more severe floury phenotype, significantly increased chalkiness, and reduced grain filling and weight compared with the respective bzip50 or bzip60 single mutants (Figures 6C–6H; Supplemental Figure 12). Moreover, immunoblot analysis revealed enhanced accumulation of ER stress-responsive proteins BiP1 and PDIL1-1 in both double mutants, indicating an exacerbated UPR in the endosperm (Figures 6I and 6J).

# RlSBZ1 was selected during japonica and indica rice domestication

To determine whether RISBZ1 underwent selection during rice domestication, we analyzed SNP variants within the RISBZ1 genomic region using data from 115 wild rice accessions (Oryza rufipogon and Oryza nivara), 673 landraces, and 995 modern cultivars. SNPs located within the 2-kb upstream region and missense variants in exons were selected for haplotype analysis. Seven RISBZ1 haplotypes were identified among wild rice accessions; RISBZ1-Hap1 and RISBZ1-Hap5 were the most common. These findings suggest that RISBZ1 was a target of selection during rice domestication.

In this study, we found that the flo27 mutant displayed a floury mature endosperm with abnormal starch and storage protein deposition (Figure 1), confirming an essential role for RISBZ1 in dry-matter accumulation during rice endosperm development. Additionally, we observed significant upregulation of UPR downstream genes in flo27 (Figure 2), 

suggesting that severe ER stress was induced by loss of RISBZ1 function. This stress likely contributes to the defective endosperm phenotype, given that ER stress and UPR activation have been implicated in promoting grain chalkiness in rice (Bertolotti et al., 2001; Oono et al., 2010; Guo et al., 2012; Han et al., 2012). 

As a transcription factor, the induction of numerous UPR-related downstream genes in the flo27 mutant suggests that RISBZ1 functions as a negative regulator of the UPR signaling pathway. Under ER stress conditions, RISBZ1 forms complexes with bZIP50 and bZIP60 in the nucleus and suppresses their DNAbinding affinities for downstream UPR gene promoters (Figure 5). Additionally, overexpression of RISBZ1 in isolated rice protoplasts treated with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ DTT led to the downregulation of UPR-related genes (Figure 6B). These results strongly support the idea that RISBZ1 functions as a negative regulator of the UPR under ER stress conditions. As expected, grains of the bzip50 risbz1 and bzip60 risbz1 double mutants displayed more severe floury phenotypes than those of the bzip50 and bzip60 single mutants, along with greater accumulation of BiP1 and PDIL1-1 proteins (Figures 6C–6J). In contrast, expression of bZIP60 or bZIP50 was highly induced in the bzip50 and bzip60 single mutants, respectively (Figures 4A and 4C), suggesting the existence of a compensatory mechanism between the two branches of the UPR signaling pathway. One possible explanation is that ER homeostasis is disrupted in the bzip60 mutant due to the accumulation of unfolded proteins, which may activate IRE1 and subsequently induce bZIP50 expression, thus reinitiating the UPR. This model may explain why many UPR-related genes in the single-knockout lines were upregulated rather than downregulated (Figures 4A and 4C). 

A previous study showed that a bZIP50 knockout mutant exhibited significantly elevated grain chalkiness (Yang et al., 2022); in our study, grains of bzip50-1/-2 exhibited only mild chalkiness (Supplemental Figure 10C). Additionally, the grain phenotype of the bZIP60 knockout mutant in the prior study appeared more severe than that of bzip60-1/-2 in our study (Supplemental Figure 10D; Yang et al., 2022). This discrepancy may be due to the different environments in which the plants were grown. The temperature during grain filling in Wuhan (latitude $2 9 . 6 ^ { \circ } - 3 1 . 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } )$ , where ZH11 was cultivated, is considerably higher than that in Beijing (latitude $3 9 . 4 ^ { \circ } { - } 4 1 . 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { N } )$ , where our study was conducted. These observations imply that RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 play critical roles in regulating ER stress and UPR activation under high-temperature conditions in rice. Supporting this hypothesis, we found that grain development in bzip50 risbz1 and bzip60 risbz1 double mutants was more sensitive to high-temperature treatment, displaying severe floury and shrunken grains (Supplemental Figure 13). Besides, the grain phenotype of bzip60 was more severe than that of bzip50 (Supplemental Figure 10; Yang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022), indicating that bZIP60 plays a more prominent role than bZIP50 in the UPR pathway in rice. Together with previous findings, our results demonstrate that both loss-of-function mutations and overexpression of bZIP50 or bZIP60 disrupt ER homeostasis, leading to UPR activation and varying degrees of grain chalkiness (Figures 4A–4D; Supplemental Figure 10; Yang 

et al., 2022). These findings highlight the importance of maintaining appropriate expression levels of bZIP50 and bZIP60 to fine-tune the UPR and ensure normal endosperm development and the production of low-chalky grains. 

Our haplotype analysis indicates that RISBZ1-Hap2 and RISBZ1- Hap6 have undergone positive selection in the japonica and indica subpopulations, respectively; they show a conspicuous tendency for geographic distribution favoring northward and southward expansion (Supplemental Figure 14). These patterns suggest that RISBZ1-Hap2 and RISBZ1-Hap6 contribute to rice adaptation to cold and heat environments, respectively, although further validation is required. Future studies should explore and utilize optimal haplotype combinations of bZIP50, bZIP60, and RISBZ1 with appropriate expression levels to develop high-quality rice cultivars with improved resistance to ER and heat stress. 

Previous studies, together with our new findings, demonstrate that RISBZ1 has pleiotropic roles during rice grain filling, especially under ER stress conditions (Figure 7). Under normal conditions, nuclear-localized RISBZ1 predominantly regulates the expression of SSP and starch biosynthesis-related genes, promoting rapid accumulation of dry matter in the developing endosperm. During this phase, bZIP50 and bZIP60 proteins remain anchored to the ER membrane, limiting their translocation to the nucleus and maintaining appropriate chaperone levels for normal cellular function. Under ER stress, bZIP50 and bZIP60 translocate from the ER to the nucleus, where they activate the expression of UPR downstream genes. In the nucleus, RISBZ1 interacts with bZIP50 and bZIP60, repressing UPR signaling by inhibiting their DNA-binding affinities to the promoters of UPR genes. Interestingly, this interaction also interferes with RISBZ1-mediated activation of SSP and starch biosynthesisrelated genes, which may lead to reduced accumulation of dry matter. The mutual inhibition of gene expression resulting from the RISBZ1–bZIP50/60 interaction likely serves to mitigate ER stress, although it carries the expense of diminished dry-matter accumulation under adverse conditions, such as high temperature during grain filling. Our results also provide a plausible explanation for the observed downregulation of seed storage-related genes under high-temperature conditions (Supplemental Figure 15; Zhang et al., 2018), aside from alternative splicing events that involve RISBZ1 (Xu et al., 2020). In the absence of RISBZ1, as in the flo27 mutant, the suppression of bZIP50 and bZIP60 is released, leading to severe ER stress, considerably reduced dry-matter accumulation, and the development of floury endosperm. Therefore, RISBZ1 functions cooperatively with bZIP50 and bZIP60 to balance grain filling and the ER stress response in rice. 

In conclusion, this study reveals an unexpected function of RISBZ1 in regulating rice grain filling. The establishment of RISBZ1 as a negative regulator of the UPR signaling pathway provides new insight into the cellular mechanisms that enable rice grains to cope with ER stress. The discovery of RISBZ1-mediated crosstalk between stress response pathways and grain filling has important implications for improving crop yield and quality, especially in the context of global population growth and the challenges posed by global warming.

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The flo27 mutant was isolated from a transfer DNA mutant population generated from Oryza sativa ssp. japonica cv. Dongjin. All plants were cultivated in paddy fields at the Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences (Beijing, China) and Nanjing Agricultural University (Nanjing, China) under natural environmental conditions and conventional agronomic practices. 

For high-temperature treatment, wild-type, bzip50, bzip60, bzip50 risbz1, and bzip60 risbz1 plants at the flowering stage were transferred to controlled growth chambers maintained under either normal temperature $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 12 h light / ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ , 12 h dark) or high temperature $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 12 h light / $\boldsymbol { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , 12 h dark). Mature grains from each treatment group were harvested and assessed for phenotypic characteristics. 

# Measurement of total starch, amylose, and protein contents

Dehulled rice grains were ground into fine flour using a laboratory mill. Total starch, amylose, and protein contents were measured according to the protocol outlined in a previous study (Lei et al., 2022). 

# Protein extraction and immunoblot analysis

Protein extraction was performed as described by Takemoto et al. (2002), followed by SDS–PAGE and immunoblotting as described by Wang et al. (2016). Synthetic peptides corresponding to AGPS2a, AGPL2, BEI, BEIIb, SSI, ISA1, GBSSI, GluA, GluB, and Glb were used to generate specific polyclonal antibodies in rabbits at Yingji Biotech (http://www. immunogen.com.cn/), as previously described (Long et al., 2018). Anti-EF-1α antibodies were purchased from Agrisera (AS10934). 

# Microscopy

SEM was performed as previously described (Peng et al., 2014). Transverse sections of mature seeds were examined using a Hitachi S-3400N scanning electron microscope. To observe amyloplast morphology, transverse sections ( $1 { - } 2 \mathsf { m m }$ thick) of developing endosperm from wild-type and flo27 seeds were fixed overnight in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) containing $2 \%$ (v/v) glutaraldehyde and $2 \%$ (w/v) paraformaldehyde. Samples were embedded and sectioned as previously described (Kang et al., 2005). Semithin sections $( 1 ~ \mu \mathsf { m }$ thick) were stained with 0.5% I -KI and observed under a Nikon 80i light 

microscope. For ultrastructural analysis, developing seeds (12 DAF) were fixed for at least $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde buffered with 0.2 M sodium phosphate (pH 7.2), sectioned using an ultramicrotome (Power Tome-XL, RMC), and examined with a Hitachi H-7650 transmission electron microscope. 

# Map-based cloning of RISBZ1

To map the FLO27 locus, an $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population was generated from a cross between the flo27 mutant and the indica cultivar N22. In total, 1464 recessive individuals were used for fine mapping of FLO27. Primer sequences are provided in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Subcellular localization and BiFC assays

For subcellular localization analysis, the full-length CDSs of RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 were fused to GFP and placed under the control of a double CaMV35S promoter in the pAN580-GFP transient expression vector. Rice protoplast isolation and transformation were performed as previously described (Chen et al., 2006). D53 (LOC_Os11g01330) (Zhou et al., 2013) and mCherry-KDEL were used as nuclear and ER localization markers, respectively. To induce ER stress, protoplasts were treated with 10 mM DTT for 16 h. For BiFC assays, the CDS of RISBZ1 was cloned into the pYN vector, whereas the CDSs of bZIP50 and bZIP60 were cloned into the pYC vectors. Nicotiana benthamiana leaf transformation was conducted as previously described (Kerppola, 2008). ER stress was induced by treating the leaves with 2 mM DTT for 16 h after transfection. Fluorescence signals were observed using a Zeiss LSM 780 confocal laser-scanning microscope. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Firefly LUC complementation assays

The CDSs of bZIP60 and RISBZ1 were cloned into pCAMBIA-nLUC, and those of bZIP50 and RISBZ1 were cloned downstream of C-Luc in pCAMBIA-cLUC. The resulting constructs were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves. ER stress was induced by applying 2 mM DTT for 16 h between 24 and 48 h post-transfection. The assays were performed as previously described (Chen et al., 2008). Primer sequences are provided in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Y1H and yeast two-hybrid assays

For Y1H assays, promoter fragments from PDIL1-4, PDIL2-1, PDIL2-3, CRT, NEF, ERDJ3, and BiP1 were amplified and cloned into the pLacZi58 

Y1H vector. Yeast strains were cultured on -SD/-Ura/-Trp media and X-Gal indicator plates. For yeast two-hybrid assays, the CDSs of bZIP50 and bZIP60 were cloned into the prey vector pGADT7, and the CDS of RISBZ1 was cloned into the bait vector pGBKT7. Constructs were co-transformed into yeast strain AH109 in accordance with the Matchmaker Gold Yeast Two-Hybrid System User Manual (Clontech Laboratories). Transformed strains were grown on -SD/-Leu/-Trp and -SD/- Leu/-Trp/-Ade/-His selection media supplemented with 10 mM 3-AT (Sigma-Aldrich) to identify positive protein–protein interactions. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# EMSA

The full-length CDSs of RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 were amplified and cloned into the pMAL-c2X vector. Maltose-binding protein (MBP) and MBP-RISBZ1 fusion proteins were expressed in Escherichia coli strain DE3 and purified using amylose resin (NEB, E8021S) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Biotin-labeled DNA probes were synthesized by Invitrogen. DNA gel-shift assays were conducted using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Pierce, 20148X), in accordance with the manufacturer’s protocol. The relative intensities of shifted bands were quantified using ImageJ software. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# ChIP assay

A synthetic peptide of bZIP60 (NYGEKGSGNQGKEE) was used to immunize rabbits and generate polyclonal antibodies at ABclonal Tech (Wuhan, China). The specificity of the anti-OsbZIP60 polyclonal antibodies was confirmed by western blot analysis using 12-DAF Dongjin endosperm. 

The ChIP assay was performed as previously described (Duan et al., 2019). Briefly, ${ \sim } 2 \ 9$ of developing endosperm (12 DAF) from Dongjin and flo27 was ground into a fine powder and crosslinked with formaldehyde. Nuclei were isolated, and chromatin was sheared to 200– 500 bp fragments by sonication. Chromatin fragments were immunoprecipitated using the anti-bZIP60 polyclonal antibodies. Recovered DNA was used as the template for ChIP–qPCR. Fold enrichment was calculated as the ratio of immunoprecipitated DNA to input DNA. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# LUC activity assays

Promoter sequences $( \sim 2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k b } )$ of PDIL1-1, BiP1, NEF, PDIL1-4, GluA2, and AGPL3 were amplified and cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector to generate reporter constructs. Full-length CDSs of RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 were cloned into the pCAMBIA1305 vector to generate effector constructs. Reporter and effector plasmids were co-expressed transiently in N. benthamiana leaves via Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation. LUC activity was measured using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega, E2920). ER stress was induced as previously described. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Pull-down assays

For in vitro pull-down assays, the CDSs of RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 were cloned into pGEX4T-1 or pMAL-c2X vectors to generate RISBZ1- GST, bZIP50-MBP, and bZIP60-MBP fusion proteins. GST- or RISBZ1- GST-conjugated beads were used to capture the bZIP50-MBP and bZIP60-MBP proteins. Pull-down assays were performed as previously described (Duan et al., 2019). Detection was carried out using anti-MBP (NEB, E8038S) and anti-GST (MBL, PM013-7) antibodies at a 1:5000 dilution. Primer sequences are provided in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Generation of transgenic plants

For complementation of the flo27 phenotype, a 2-kb promoter region and the coding sequence of RISBZ1 were cloned into the pCUbi1390 vector and introduced into flo27 to generate RISBZ1 complementation lines. 

To generate RISBZ1, bZIP50, and bZIP60 knockout lines, gene-specific 20-bp target sequences were cloned into a CRISPR–Cas9 expression vector (Lei et al., 2014) and introduced into calli of the Dongjin cultivar. Transgenic individuals were identified by sequencing. All transformation procedures were performed via Agrobacterium-mediated transfection. 

# RNA extraction and RT–qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from various rice tissues using the RNA Prep Pure Kit (Zymo Research, D7003). Reverse transcription was carried out with the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit (QIAGEN, 205314) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RT–qPCR was performed using an ABI 7500 system and SYBR Premix Ex Taq (TAKARA, RR041A). Gene expression was analyzed across three biological replicates using the relative quantification method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Haplotype, selection, and evolutionary analysis

Resequencing data for RISBZ1 were obtained from in-house datasets and publicly available wild rice data (Jing et al., 2023). Haplotype classification was performed using SNP variants from these datasets. Geographic distributions of RISBZ1 haplotypes were visualized using the Maps R package (version 4.3.2) under the GPL-2 license. 

For selection analysis, sequences spanning the RISBZ1 coding region and $\pm 4 \cdot$ -Mb flanking regions were analyzed using publicly available wild rice datasets (Jing et al., 2023). Nucleotide diversity (π values) for various subpopulations and fixation index $( F _ { S } \eta$ values were calculated using a sliding-window approach (window size: 200 kb; step size: 20 kb) implemented in Vcftools (Danecek et al., 2011). 

# Quantification and statistical analysis

All plant materials used for phenotypic evaluation, immunoblotting, and RT–qPCR were grown simultaneously under uniform environmental conditions and management practices. Data are presented as means $\pm$ standard deviation (SD). Statistical significance between control and experimental groups was assessed using a two-sided Student’s t-test $( ^ { \star } \mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5$ ; $\mathsf { \Pi } ^ { \star \star } \mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1 ,$ ) in GraphPad Prism 8. Details regarding sample sizes, control group definitions, quantification methods, and statistical procedures are provided in the figure legends. No data were excluded from the analyses.
