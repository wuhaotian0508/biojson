# Summary

Apple (Malus domestica Borkh.) is one of the most economically valuable fruit crops globally and a key dietary source for various nutrients. However, the levels of ascorbic acid (AsA) and anthocyanin, essential micronutrients for human health, are extremely low in the pulp of commonly cultivated apple varieties. In the present study, the second-generation hybrid strain of Xinjiang red-fleshed apple (‘Zihong No. $1 ^ { \prime } \times ^ { \prime } { \sf G a l a ^ { \prime } } )$ ) was used as the test material. The results revealed that AsA content was significantly higher in red-fleshed apple pulp than in non-redfleshed varieties, and the expression of MdGLDH, a key gene in the D-mannose/L-galactose pathway, correlated strongly with AsA levels. Using the promoter of MdGLDH as bait, an R3-type MYB transcription factor (TF), MdCPC-like, was identified through yeast one-hybrid screening. Further analysis revealed that the overexpression of MdCPCL increased the AsA and anthocyanin levels in both callus and fruits, whereas MdCPCL knockdown led to a reduction in their levels. Moreover, the interaction between MdCPCL and the bHLH TF MdILR3-like was confirmed, forming the MdCPCL-MdILR3L complex. This complex significantly enhanced the transcription of downstream target genes MdGLDH and MdANS, promoting the synthesis of AsA and anthocyanins. This study contributes to further enrich the anabolic pathways of AsA and anthocyanin in apples and provides a theoretical foundation for the quality breeding of redfleshed apple varieties. 

# Introduction

Apple (Malus domestica Borkh.) is among the most widely cultivated and economically crucial fruit crops in temperate regions worldwide (Duan et al., 2017). A traditional Welsh proverb from 1866 states, ‘An apple a day keeps the doctor away’. Apples are highly nutritious and enriched in polyphenols that are easily absorbed by the human body. They also exhibit antioxidant and antitumor properties (Kris-Etherton et al., 2002; Ma et al., 2019). In addition, apples have been reported to delay ageing and prevent cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases (Bondonnoa et al., 2012; Hyson, 2011), as well as protect the liver (Zou et al., 2020). Despite the high antioxidant potential of apples, their ascorbic acid (AsA) content, a key broad-spectrum antioxidant, remains relatively low (Li et al., 2008). Fruits such as kiwi (Liu et al., 2022), Rosa roxburghii (Ding et al., 2023) and jujube (Lu et al., 2022) contain approximately 1200 mg, 1200 mg and 500 mg of AsA per 100 g of fresh weight (FW), respectively, whereas apples contain less than $1 0 \mathrm { \ m g } { \cdot } 1 0 0 \mathrm { \ g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW (Davey et al., 2000). AsA levels not only vary between species and cultivars but also across different tissues of the same plant. For example, apple leaves contain over $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } { \cdot } 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW of AsA, whereas the pulp of ‘Gala’ apples has less than 5 mg 100 g1 FW of AsA (Li et al., 2008). 

AsA (vitamin C, Vc) is a crucial antioxidant that plays a key role in plant growth, development, signal transduction and stress defence (Broad et al., 2020). In addition to its importance in 

plants, AsA is essential for human health, contributing to the prevention of diseases such as cardiovascular conditions and cancer as well as delaying ageing (Li and Schellhorn, 2007). Because humans cannot synthesize AsA, they must obtain this vital nutrient through diet, primarily from fruits and vegetables (Li and Schellhorn, 2007). Maintaining optimal AsA levels through diet remains a challenge in both developed and developing nations (Troesch et al., 2012). Four major pathways for AsA biosynthesis are the D-mannose/L-galactose, L-gulose, D-galacturonic acid and inositol pathways (Broad et al., 2020). Among these, the D-Man/L-Gal pathway is the most prominent (Ishikawa and Shigeoka, 2008; Lorence et al., 2004; Wheeler et al., 1998). Several key genes involved in this pathway have been identified, including those encoding GDP-D-mannose pyrophosphorylase (GMP), GDP-D-mannose-30 ,50 -epimerase, GDP-L-galactose phosphorylase (GGP), L-galactose phosphorylase (GPP), L-galactose dehydrogenase (GDH) and L-galactono-1,4- lactone dehydrogenase (GLDH). In plant tissues, AsA synthesis and degradation are affected by both internal and external factors, such as developmental stage (Bulley et al., 2009), light conditions (Lin et al., 2008) and oxidative stress (Yoshimura et al., 2014) (Bulley and Laing, 2016). Over the past two decades, research on the physiological and molecular mechanisms of AsA biosynthesis and regulation in higher plants has primarily focused on model species, such as Arabidopsis thaliana (Conklin et al., 2000), tobacco (Badejo et al., 2009) and tomato fruits (Ioannidi et al., 2009). In recent years, increasing attention has 

been paid to AsA research on perennial fruit trees. Liu et al. (2022) demonstrated that the transcription factors (TFs) AceMYBS1 and AceGBF3 play critical roles in AsA biosynthesis in kiwifruit. Similarly, Su et al. (2024) identified key TFs involved in AsA synthesis during R. roxburghii fruit development, and Ma et al. (2021b) showed that the F-box protein MdAMR1L1 inhibited AsA biosynthesis in apple leaves. However, research on the biosynthesis and regulation of AsA in apple pulp remains limited. 

Fruit colour is primarily determined by the presence of chlorophyll, carotenoids and anthocyanins (Gonzali et al., 2009; Purugganan, 2019). Among these, anthocyanins are crucial natural pigments that impart vivid red, purple and blue hues to fruits and are commonly found in many plant species (Jaakola, 2013; Winkel-Shirley, 2001). For most horticultural crops, particularly those with fleshy fruits, fruit colour has a profound effect on the fruits’ market value and consumer preference (Ballester et al., 2010; Che and Zhang, 2019; Wang et al., 2018a). Beyond the attractive appearance of the peel, the presence of pigments in the pulp is gaining increasing attention because consumers prioritize health and quality of life (Bartley and Scolnik, 1995; Butelli et al., 2008; He and Giusti, 2010). Currently, most commercially valuable apple varieties have white or pale-coloured pulp, with anthocyanin levels close to zero (Wang et al., 2024b). Therefore, red-fleshed apples have attracted more and more attention because of their bright red colour and high anthocyanin content. As a key subclass of plant flavonoids, anthocyanin is primarily produced through the phenylalanine metabolism pathway (Williams and Grayer, 2004) and is synthesized via the catalytic activity of several enzymes encoded by structural genes. The expression of these genes is regulated by MYB domain proteins (MYB), basic helix–loop–helix proteins (bHLH), WD repeat proteins (WDR), ethylene response factors (ERFs) and NAC domain proteins, among others (Allan et al., 2008; Zhou et al., 2015; An et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2021a). Of these, the MYB-bHLH-WDR (MBW) ternary protein complex is the most extensively studied regulatory mechanism for anthocyanin synthesis (Xu et al., 2015). 

Among higher plants, MYB and bHLH represent the largest group of TFs (Feller et al., 2011; Pireyre and Burow, 2015). Complexes can be widely formed between MYB and bHLH TFs and different MYB-bHLH complexes regulate different physiological processes, including organ development, metabolic pathways and biotic and abiotic stress reactions (Millard et al., 2019; Toledo-Ortiz et al., 2003). Based on the number of adjacent repeat sequences (R1, R2 and R3), MYB TFs are classified into four subtypes: 1R-MYB, R2R3-MYB, 3R-MYB and 4R-MYB (Dubos et al., 2010). Of these, R2R3-MYB is considered the key TF in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway (Allan et al., 2008). Previous studies have shown that MdMYB1 participates in anthocyanin synthesis in apple peel (Takos et al., 2006), whereas MdMYB6 was reported to inhibit this process (Xu et al., 2020). MdMYB10, an allele of MdMYB1, promotes anthocyanin synthesis in red-fleshed apples (Espley et al., 2007). In addition, R3-type MYB may have evolved from R2R3-MYB, playing roles in cell morphology regulation and secondary metabolic pathways (Pesch and Hulskamp, 2009; Simon et al., 2007). bHLH TFs are defined by about 60 conserved amino acids, which include a basic DNA-binding domain and a dimerization domain (Heim et al., 2003). The ternary protein complex (MBW) composed of bHLH, MYB and WDR proteins not only regulates the synthesis of anthocyanin but also controls the development of plant 

epidermal and root hairs (Kurata et al., 2005; Wada et al., 2002). Despite extensive research on the function of MYB-bHLH complexes across various aspects of plant growth and development, a regulatory module that simultaneously mediates the synthesis of both AsA and anthocyanins has not been reported yet. 

In this study, we used the second-generation hybrid strain of Xinjiang red-fleshed apple (Malus sieversii f. niedzwetzkyana) (‘Zihong No. $1 ^ { \prime } \times ^ { \prime } { \sf G a l a ^ { \prime } } )$ (Wang et al., 2018b) constructed by our research group as materials. An R3-type MYB TF, MdCPC-like, was identified through yeast one-hybrid screening. Further studies revealed that the overexpression of MdCPCL led to an increase in both AsA and anthocyanin levels in the callus and fruits, whereas the knockdown of MdCPCL resulted in a decrease in the levels of these compounds. Moreover, we confirmed that MdCPCL interacts with the bHLH TF MdILR3-like (MdILR3L), forming an MdCPCL-MdILR3L complex. This complex significantly enhances the transcription of the downstream target genes MdGLDH and MdANS, thereby promoting the synthesis of both AsA and anthocyanin. The findings of this study provide insights into the biosynthesis pathways of AsA and anthocyanins in apples, laying a theoretical foundation for the quality breeding of red-fleshed apple varieties. 

# Results

# Changes in AsA content and analysis of key genes expression in the pulp of different strains from the population of hybrid offspring

In the D-Man/L-Gal pathway, AsA is synthesized from D-mannose-1-phosphate through intermediates such as GDP-Dmannose, GDP-L-galactose, L-galactose and L-galactono-1,4- lactone (Figure 1a). From the second-generation population of Xinjiang red-fleshed apple hybrids (‘Zihong No. $1 ^ { \prime } \times ^ { \prime } { \sf G a l a ^ { \prime } } )$ developed by our research group, we randomly selected one redfleshed and one non-red-fleshed strain (Figure 1b). We analysed AsA content in the flesh and monitored the expression of key genes involved in the synthesis pathway throughout development. The AsA content in both strains demonstrated a consistent trend $\left( r = 0 . 9 5 \right)$ ), peaking at 60 days after full bloom (DAFB), followed by a gradual decrease (Figure 1c). However, the AsA content in redfleshed apples remained significantly higher than that in non-redfleshed apples at all time points $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ ). Gene expression analysis of the key genes involved in the synthesis pathway revealed that MdGMP, MdGME, MdGGP, MdGPP and MdGDH displayed no clear patterns in either strain during development, with a low correlation between their expression levels (Figure 1d–h). By contrast, the expression of MdGLDH showed a strong correlation between the two strains and was closely aligned with changes in the AsA content $( r = 0 . 9 0$ ; Figure 1i). Therefore, we believe that MdGLDH may play an important role in the synthesis of AsA in the pulp of hybrid offspring.

# Overexpression of MdCPCL promotes the accumulation of AsA and anthocyanin in apple

Because MdCPCL binds to the promoter of MdGLDH and enhances its expression, we hypothesized that MdCPCL may play a role in AsA synthesis. To test this, we obtained three independent transgenic ‘orin’ callus with stable overexpression and obtained three independent transgenic red callus knockdown by CRISPR/Cas9 (Figure 4a). The presence of the transgene was confirmed through PCR amplification (Figure 4b), western blot analysis (Figure 4c) and sequence identification (Figures 4d and S4). Compared with the control callus, the MdCPCL overexpression callus exhibited significantly increased AsA and anthocyanin contents $\prime P < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 4e–g). Real-time fluorescence quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) revealed a significant increase in the relative expressions of MdCPCL and MdGLDH $\cdot P < 0 . 0 5$ , Figure 4h–k). By contrast, MdCPCL knockdown resulted in the opposite effect (Figure 4e–k). In addition, we analysed the relative expression level of other key genes in the L-galactose synthesis pathway in the MdCPCL-overexpression callus and found that the relative expression of MdGGP and MdGDH also increased significantly (Figure S5a). Given that MdCPCL overexpression also promoted anthocyanin accumulation, we analysed the expression of key genes involved in the anthocyanin synthesis pathway (Figure S6) in callus by using RT-qPCR. The results showed a general upregulation of these key genes, particularly MdANS (ANS: anthocyanidin synthase) and MdUFGT (UFGT: UGP glucoseflavonoid 3-O-glycosyltransferase), in the callus of overexpression lines $( P < 0 . 0 5$ , Figure S5c). Besides, compared with the red callus, we found that the relative expression levels of MdGGP, MdGDH, MdANS and MdUFGT in MdCPCL-knockdown red callus were significantly lower $( P < 0 . 0 5$ , Figure S5b,d), which corresponded to the comparison results between MdCPCL-overexpression ‘Orin’ callus and ‘Orin’ callus. 

To verify the biological function of MdCPCL in apple fruit, we conducted transient expression experiments involving gene silencing and overexpression. Compared with the TRV control, silencing MdCPCL reduced the transcription levels of MdGLDH, MdANS and MdUFGT (Figure 4q), leading to a decrease in the AsA and anthocyanin contents around the infiltration site (Figure 4p) and a reduction in fruit redness (Figure 4m). By contrast, the overexpression of MdCPCL increased the transcription levels of MdGLDH, MdANS and MdUFGT (Figure 4o), 

resulting in the accumulation of AsA and anthocyanin around the infiltration site (Figure 4n) and an enhancement in fruit redness (Figure 4l). These results indicated that MdCPCL positively regulates the accumulation of AsA and anthocyanin in apple fruits. 

# MdCPCL directly binds to the promoter of the structural gene MdANS in the anthocyanin synthesis pathway

Given the substantial increase in MdANS and MdUFGT expression in the MdCPCL-overexpression callus, we speculated that MdCPCL interacts with the promoters of MdANS and MdUFGT. The Y1H assay (Figure 5a) demonstrated that Y187 competent cells co-transformed with MdCPCL-AD and proMdANS-pHIS2 could grow on -T-H-L selective medium containing 80 mM 3-AT. However, no interaction was detected between MdCPCL-AD and proMdUFGT-pHIS2 (Figure 5b). Further analysis of the MdANS promoter revealed the presence of two MYB TF binding elements (MBS and MRE; Figures 5c and S7). To confirm the interaction between the MdCPCL and the promoter of MdANS, we conducted an EMSA. The results revealed that MdCPCL binds to the MBS cis-element (Figure 5d), and this binding ability decreased as the concentration of the competitive probe increased (Figure 5e, lanes 3–5). When the labelled probe was replaced with a mutant probe, no binding was detected (Figure 5e, lane 6). Moreover, LUC assay confirmed that MdCPCL significantly activates the MdANS promoter (Figure 5f,g). The results of ChIP assay also proved the combination of the two, and the promoter fragment of MdANS containing MBS cis-element was enriched in the transgenic ‘Orin’ callus of MdCPCL-GFP (Figure 5h–j).

# MdCPCL-MdILR3L complex promotes the synthesis of AsA and anthocyanin in apple

Given that MdCPCL and MdILR3L form heterodimers, we further investigated whether this complex affects AsA and anthocyanin synthesis. We co-transformed MdCPCL and MdILR3L into ‘orin’ callus and obtained three independent, stable transgenic lines (Figure 7a). The presence of the transgenes was confirmed using

These results indicated that MdCPCL interacts with MdILR3L to promote the transcription of downstream genes and increase the accumulation of AsA and anthocyanin.

# Discussion

AsA (Vc) is an essential micronutrient for humans, primarily obtained from fruits and vegetables. Adults are recommended to have a daily intake of at least 90 mg of AsA (Liu et al., 2022). Although medicinal Vc tablets are an effective source of AsA, excessive consumption may lead to DNA damage and promote cancer cell formation (Lee and Blair, 2001). By contrast, because of the interaction between natural Vc in fruits and vegetables and other phytochemicals, excessive consumption not only has no negative impact on human health but also the absorption efficiency of AsA by human body is significantly higher than that of medicinal Vc tablets (DellaPenna, 1999). In China, fruits such as kiwi, R. roxburghii and jujube are rich sources of dietary AsA, whereas apples have a relatively low AsA content. In this study, we used the second-generation hybrid population of Xinjiang red-fleshed apples previously developed by our research group as the test material. Among 30 randomly selected strains, we observed that the AsA content was significantly higher (16-fold) in the pulp of red-fleshed apples than in the pulp of non-redfleshed apples $\langle P < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 2b). We guessed that the reason for this extreme difference was due to some differences in the biosynthetic pathway of AsA in the pulp of hybrid offspring. 

Previous studies have demonstrated that the expression of AceGGP3 in kiwifruit is closely linked to the AsA content (Liu et al., 2022), whereas RrGGP and RrGalUR are key genes in the AsA biosynthesis pathway of R. roxburghii (Su et al., 2024). Similarly, MdGMP1 plays a crucial role in AsA synthesis in apple leaves (Ma et al., 2021b). In the present study, we found that the expression level of MdGLDH in pulp had a high correlation during the development of two kinds of apples (red fleshed and non-red fieshed) (Figure 1i). Further RT-qPCR analysis of MdGLDH in the mature pulp revealed a high degree of correlation between MdGLDH expression and AsA content (Figure 2f). This finding suggests that MdGLDH may be a key factor in the synthesis of AsA in the pulp of hybrid offspring. Although the relative expression level of MdGDH is consistent with the overall change trend of AsA content during fruit development, but there is no significant correlation between the expression level of MdGDH and AsA content in mature pulp of hybrid offspring $( r = 0 . 4 1$ ) (Figure S11a,b). Li et al. (2011) analysed the AsA content and MdGLDH expression in the pulp, peel and leaves of ‘Gala’ apples, finding that both AsA content and MdGLDH expression were significantly higher in the peel than in the pulp. Interestingly, although the AsA content was much higher in leaves than in the pulp, MdGLDH expression in leaves was only half that of the pulp. This finding suggests the presence of differences in AsA synthesis pathways between apple leaves and fruits or among different 

apple organs. Whether or how MdGLDH participates in AsA synthesis in apple leaves requires further investigation. 

Anthocyanin and AsA are both vital components of plant secondary metabolites, with anthocyanin being a major class of flavonoids (Allan et al., 2008). The content of free flavonoids in apples is higher than that in other fruits, which leads to more flavonoids eventually being absorbed by human blood. Due to its high flavonoid levels, apples are considered one of the primary dietary sources of flavonoids in many countries (Hertog et al., 1997; Vinson et al., 2001; Zamora-Ros et al., 2010). However, the genetic diversity and nutritional quality of modern apple varieties have decreased through domestication, and anthocyanin content in the edible parts of most apples remains limited. Thus, breeding red-fleshed apples has become a key focus in global apple breeding programs (Wang et al., 2018a). In this study, we found that the pulp of red-fleshed apples in hybrid offspring contain high levels of anthocyanin, reaching up to 150.15 mg/kg FW, significantly higher than in non-red-fleshed apples $( P < 0 . 0 1 )$ ) (Figures 2g,h and S11c,d). These findings are consistent with those of a previous study (Li et al., 2023). In addition, in the two randomly selected hybrid progeny strains, the anthocyanin content in the pulp of red-fleshed apple gradually decreased during fruit development, while the anthocyanin content in the pulp of non-red-fleshed apple did not change obviously, which may be related to the fact that the anthocyanin content in the pulp of non-red-fleshed apple was almost zero (Figure S11c). Correlation analysis further showed that the anthocyanin content in the fruit development period of the two strains was positively correlated with the AsA content $( r = 0 . 9 7$ , Figure S11e). 

The accumulation of anthocyanin is regulated by the coordinated expression of genes encoding enzymes in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway. Most plant MYB genes encode R2R3-MYB proteins, which are considered key factors in anthocyanin biosynthesis (Allan et al., 2008). Studies on R3-type MYB TFs have mainly focused on their roles in the development and regulation of trichomes and root hairs in A. thaliana. however, limited studies have investigated their role in secondary metabolic pathways. Previous studies have shown that AtCPC (Zhu et al., 2009) and AtMYBL2 (Matsui et al., 2008) negatively regulate anthocyanin synthesis in A. thaliana, and research on the involvement of R3-type MYB factors in AsA biosynthesis is lacking to date. In this study, we found that the R3-type MYB TF MdCPCL participates in the biosynthesis of both anthocyanins and AsA. Y1H, EMSA and luciferase assays demonstrated that MdCPCL specifically binds to the promoters of MdGLDH and MdANS, activating their transcription (Figures 3 and 5). Further research revealed that the overexpression of MdCPCL in the callus and fruits significantly increased the expression of MdGLDH and 

MdANS, thereby promoting the accumulation of both AsA and anthocyanin (Figure 4). By contrast, knocking down or silencing MdCPCL in the callus and fruits reduced the AsA and anthocyanin contents (Figure 4). In addition, RT-qPCR analysis found that the expression level of MdGGP, another key gene in AsA synthesis pathway, in the MdCPCL-overexpression ‘Orin’ callus, increased significantly. We speculated that MdCPCL might directly combine with the promoter of MdGGP to promote the synthesis of AsA. Although MdCPCL positively regulates the accumulation of AsA and anthocyanin, the current results are not enough to prove the perfect positive correlation between AsA and anthocyanin content, and the complex regulatory mechanism between them needs to be further explored. 

It is an important way of plant biosynthesis that many transcription factors cooperate to participate in transcription regulation. In the AsA biosynthesis pathway, the interaction between AceMYBS1-like and AceGBF3 promotes AsA synthesis in kiwifruit (Liu et al., 2022). However, aside from this example, there have been no other reports of MYB TFs working synergistically with other TFs in AsA biosynthesis. It is worth noting that extensive research has been conducted on MYB– bHLH complexes in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway, such as the interaction between the MYB TF ZM1 and bHLH TF ZMB in corn (Chandler et al., 1989), MYB AN2 and bHLH JAF13 in petunia (Quattrocchio et al., 2006), AtMYB123 and AtbHLH042 in A. thaliana (Baudry et al., 2004), VvMYBA1 and VvMYC1 in grapes (Hichri et al., 2010) and FaMYB9/FaMYB11 and FabHLH3 in strawberries (Schaart et al., 2013). In apples, the interaction between MdMYB10 and MdbHLH3/33 has also been identified. Beyond anthocyanin biosynthesis, MYB-bHLH 

complexes are involved in plant organ development (Wang et al., 2022) and epidermal cell formation (Song et al., 2011). However, a MYB-bHLH regulatory module that mediates both AsA and anthocyanin synthesis simultaneously has not yet been reported. 

In this study, we identified a bHLH TF, MdILR3L. Using Y2H, pull-down, LCI and BiFC assays, we confirmed that MdILR3L interacts with MdCPCL both in vitro and in vivo (Figure 6). In addition, the interaction between MdCPCL and MdILR3L led to the further increased expression of MdGLDH and MdANS compared with the overexpression of MdCPCL alone, resulting in further accumulation of AsA and anthocyanins in apple callus. Interestingly, the expression of MdCPCL in co-transformed callus was significantly higher than that in MdCPCL-overexpression callus. We speculated that this may be because MdILR3L combined with some element in the promoter of MdCPCL, which further promoted the transcription of MdCPCL. It is well known that bHLH proteins bind to E-box (CANNTG) elements (Hichri et al., 2010), the analysis of the promoter of MdCPCL showed that there are eight E-box cis-elements (Figure S12), and EMSA assay showed that MdILR3L may bind to three of them (Figure S13a). These findings suggest that MdILR3L can not only indirectly promotes by forming a complex with MdCPCL but also promotes AsA and anthocyanin synthesis by binding to the promoter of MdCPCL. However, Y1H assay (Figure S13b,c) showed that MdILR3L can’t directly bind the promoters of MdGLDH and MdANS, therefore, MdILR3L directly contributes to the synthesis of AsA and anthocyanin requires further investigation. # Materials and methods

# Plant material

The second-generation strain of the Xinjiang red-fleshed apple hybrid (‘Zihong $\mathsf { N o } . 1 ^ { \prime } \times \mathsf { \Omega } ^ { \prime } \mathsf { G a l a } ^ { \prime } )$ used in this experiment was collected from the fruit tree breeding base in Liaocheng City, Shandong Province, China. This population was planted in 2014 and produced its first fruit in 2017. The ‘orin’ callus and red callus used for genetic transformation were sterile cultures maintained on Murashige and Skoog solid medium, with subculturing performed every 20 days. The hormone content and culture conditions adhered to the methods described by Wang et al. (2024b). For the transient transformation experiments with apple fruit, ‘Gala’ apples were collected from the fruit tree breeding base in Tai’an City, Shandong Province, China. On July 21st, the ‘Gala’ apple which was about to mature but had not taken off its fruit bag was sampled. 

# Extraction and RT-qPCR analysis of total RNA from plants

The Fast Pure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (RC 411, Vazyme, China) was used to extract total RNA from the pulp, peel, and callus of the tested apples. The extracted RNA was then reverse transcribed into cDNA using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit with gDNA Eraser (Takara, Japan). RT-qPCR was performed using the iCycler iQ5 system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) with ChamQ SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, China). RT-qPCR primers were designed using Beacon Designer 7 software, and the primer sequences are listed in Table S1. Gene expression was normalized to the endogenous reference gene MdActin, and relative transcription levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). 

# Determination of AsA and anthocyanin content

The contents of AsA and anthocyanin were measured using kits provided by Suzhou Grace Biotechnology Co., Ltd. AsA content $\left( \mathsf { m g } { \cdot } 1 0 0 \ \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 } \right.$ FW) was determined using an ascorbic acid content test kit (G0201F), whereas anthocyanin content (mg/kg FW) was measured using a plant anthocyanin test kit (G0126F). Both assays were conducted using visible spectrophotometry. After completing the reactions according to the kit instructions, the absorbance was measured with a UV2450 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Kyoto) at the specified wavelengths. 

# Screening of Y1H library and assay

An apple cDNA library for the yeast system was constructed by our research group in collaboration with Takara Biotechnology Co., Ltd. The promoter sequence of MdGLDH was integrated into the pAbAi vector, and the recombinant plasmid was linearized using the Bstb1 endonuclease. The linearized plasmid was then transformed into Y1H yeast cells (Clontech) and cultured on 

uracil-deficient (-Ura) medium. To determine the optimal concentration for inhibiting self-activation, different concentrations of Aureobasidin A (AbA) were added to the culture medium. After introducing the cDNA library, positive clones were selected on leucine-deficient (-Leu) solid medium containing the ideal AbA concentration. Recombinant plasmids MdCPCL-PGADT7, ProMdGLDH-pHIS2 and ProMdANS-pHIS2 were constructed, and MdCPCL-PGADT7 was cotransfected with either ProMdGLDHpHIS2 or ProMdANS-pHIS2 into Y187 yeast cells (Clontech). To inhibit self-activation of the pHIS2 plasmid, 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (3-AT) was used. The interaction between the constructs was detected by monitoring yeast growth on a medium lacking tryptophan, histidine and leucine (-T/-H/-L) but containing 3-AT. An empty pGADT7 plasmid served as the control. The primers used to construct the vectors are listed in Table S2. 

# Bioinformatics analysis and subcellular localization

Sequences with high homology to MdCPCL and MdILR3L in different species were identified using the National Biotechnology Information Center database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). The amino acid sequences of various species were compared using DNAMAN software, and phylogenetic trees for R3-type MYB and bHLH family members were constructed using MEGA 5.1 software. Conserved domains of MdCPCL and MdILR3L were analysed using the online MEME Suite program (https://memesuite.org/meme/tools/meme). The related amino acid sequences can be found in Table S3. 

The coding sequences (CDS) of MdCPCL and MdILR3L, excluding stop codons, were inserted into the pRI101-AN vector with a GFP tag. The resulting recombinant plasmid was transformed into the Agrobacterium strain LBA4404. Subcellular localization was performed following the method described by Zhang et al. (2023). The transformed LBA4404 strain was resuspended in infection buffer and injected into 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of co-infiltration, GFP signals were observed using a fluorescence microscope (NIKON ECLIPSE Ni-E, Tokyo, Japan) at an excitation wavelength of 488 nm. Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are detailed in Table S2. 

# EMSA

The CDS of MdCPCL was cloned into the pET-32a vector containing a HIS tag sequence to construct the recombinant plasmid MdCPCL-HIS. This plasmid was then transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells by using a prokaryotic expression system, and the MdCPCL-HIS fusion protein was subsequently purified. Biotin-labelled probes were synthesized by Shenggong Bioengineering (Shanghai) Co., Ltd. Mutant probes containing different nucleotide alterations in the cis-acting elements were also designed. Unlabeled probes were used as competitive probes. EMSA was performed using the chemiluminescence EMSA kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Table S2 provides detailed information on the probes used in these experiments. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The CDS of MdCPCL (without the stop codon) was inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK vector to create the effector plasmid, whereas the promoter fragments of MdGLDH and MdANS were inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector as reporter plasmids. Following the method described by Zhao et al. (2024), the 

recombinant plasmids were co-transformed into 4-week-old tobacco leaves via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation (GV3101). After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of co-infiltration in the dark, the activity and luminescence intensity of LUC were measured using an in vivo imaging analysis system (Night Owl II LB983, Berthold, Germany). The primers used for vector construction are detailed in Table S2. 

# ChIP assay

The transgenic ‘Orin’ callus stably overexpressing MdCPCL fused to a GFP tag were prepared for the ChIP assay. It was consistent with the previous method (Wang et al., 2020), the ChIP experiment was carried out by using EZ-ChIP 244 Chromatin Immunoprecipitation kit (Upstate, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and the ‘Orin’ callus expressing an empty GFP tag were used as a negative control, and the corresponding antibody was used for detection. Primers were designed for the regions containing MBS cis-elements on the MdGLDH and MdANS promoters. ChIP-qPCR analysis was performed with primers listed in Table S2, and specific DNA fragments were amplified by PCR and monitored on agarose gel. 

# Genetic transformation of apple callus

The CDS of MdCPCL (without the stop codon) was inserted into the pPI101-AN vector, driven by the CaMV 35S promoter and tagged with GFP. Similarly, the CDS of MdILR3L (without the stop codon) was inserted into the pCB302 vector, also driven by the CaMV 35S promoter and tagged with HA. The recombinant vectors were introduced into the Agrobacterium strain LBA4404, and infection was induced followed the protocol described by Wang et al. (2017). After 20–30 min of infection, the apple callus was transferred to the MS solid medium and cultured in the dark at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Subsequently, the apple callus containing the recombinant pRI101-AN and pCB302 vectors was transferred to kanamycin and glufosinate screening media, respectively. Positive transgenic calli were screened and verified through PCR amplification, western blotting and RT-qPCR. Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are listed in Table S2. 

A CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing toolkit was used to obtain apple callus with MdCPCL knockdown (Xing et al., 2014). Based on PAM sequences, two gRNAs were generated from the MdCPCL genome sequence using an online tool (http://crispr.hzau.edu. cn/CRISPR2/). The designed gRNAs were recombined into the pHSE401 vector and then introduced into the Agrobacterium strain LBA4404. After infection, the apple callus containing the recombinant pHSE401 vector was transferred to the kanamycin screening medium. Positive transgenic callus was screened by extracting genomic DNA, followed by PCR amplification with gene-specific primers. The PCR products were sequenced and compared to identify mutant transgenic lines for further analysis. The gRNAs and primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are detailed in Table S2. 

# Transient transformation of apple fruits

The coding sequence of MdCPCL was inserted into pHB-GFP and pTRV2 vectors respectively to construct recombinant plasmids of CPCL-PHB and CPCL-pTRV2. The overexpression vector of MdCPCL-PHB was integrated into Agrobacterium GV3101 cells. At the same time, pTRV1 was used as an auxiliary plasmid, which was integrated with the recombinant plasmid of MdCPCL-pTRV2 into Agrobacterium GV3101 cells, resuspended in osmotic buffer and then injected into ‘Gala’ apple peel according to the required 

experimental combination. The buffer preparation method and instantaneous transformation method were consistent with the previous methods (Wang et al., 2024b) The infected fruit was cultured in the dark for 1 day, and then in a constant light $( 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \mu m o l / m } ^ { 2 } / s )$ incubator at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 days. The tissues around the injection site were collected, and the contents of AsA and anthocyanin and the relative expression of related genes were determined. Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are listed in Table S2. 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

The full-length CDS of MdCPCL was cloned into the pGBKT7 (BD) vector to construct a recombinant plasmid. An apple cDNA library for the yeast system was constructed by our research group in collaboration with Takara Biotechnology Co., Ltd., and the interaction between MdCPCL and MdILR3L proteins was confirmed through screening. The CDS of MdCPCL and MdILR3L were cloned into BD and pGADT7 vectors, respectively. These recombinant plasmids were then introduced into the Y2HGold yeast strain (Clontech, China) and cultured on the corresponding selective medium by using the protocol described by Wang et al. (2024b). According to previous research methods (Wang et al., 2024a), 3-AT was used to inhibit the self-activation of MdILR3L-BD. Different concentrations of 3-AT were added to the culture medium (SD/-Trp/-His) to determine the optimal concentration for inhibiting self-activation. The primers used for vector construction are listed in Table S2. 

# Pull-down assay

The CDS of MdCPCL and MdILR3L (without stop codons) were inserted into the pET-32a vector containing a His tag sequence and the pGEX-4T-1 vector containing a GST tag sequence, respectively. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells, where protein expression was induced. The fusion proteins were then purified using the corresponding affinity purification columns. The pull-down assay was performed using a His-labelled protein purification kit (Clontech), following the method described by Wang et al. (2020). Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are detailed in Table S2. 

# Luciferase complementary imaging (LCI) assay

The CDS of MdCPCL and MdILR3L (without stop codons) were inserted into pCAMBIA1300-35S-cLUC and pCAMBIA1300-35SnLUC vectors, respectively. The resulting recombinant plasmids, MdCPCL-cLUC and MdILR3L-nLUC, were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The transformed GV3101 strains containing MdCPCL-cLUC and MdILR3L-nLUC were mixed and infiltrated into 4-week-old tobacco leaves. After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of coinfiltration in the dark, LUC activity and luminescence intensity were measured using an in vivo imaging analysis system (Night Owl II LB983, Berthold, Germany). Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are detailed in Table S2. 

# BiFC assay

The CDS of MdCPCL and MdILR3L (without stop codons) were inserted into pSPYCE-35S and pSPYNE-35S vectors, respectively. The resulting recombinant plasmids, MdCPCL-SPYCE and MdILR3L-SPYNE, were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Following the method described by Wang et al. (2020), the transformed GV3101 strains containing MdCPCL-SPYCE and MdILR3L-SPYNE were mixed and infiltrated into 4-week-old tobacco leaves. After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of co-infiltration, the YFP signal was 

detected using a fluorescence microscope (NIKON ECLIPSE Ni-E, Tokyo, Japan) at an excitation wavelength of 488 nm. Primers used for gene cloning and vector construction are listed in Table S2. 

# Data analysis

GraphPad Prism 8.0 and Microsoft Excel 2016 were used for data analysis and figure generation. All experimental data are presented as the mean $\pm$ standard deviation from at least three independent biological replicates. One-way ANOVA was used to analyse the data, and the Tukey–Kramer multivariate range test was performed using GraphPad Prism 8.0 or DPS software. A P value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant.
