# Goldilocks zone of lignin: Two extremes of valve lignification lead to silique indehiscence in Brassicaceae

Justin B. Nichola,1, Logan A. Skoria,b,1, Muhammad Jamsheda , Neil Hickersona , Mendel Perkinsa,b , and Marcus A. Samuela,2 

Edited by Sean Cutler, University of California Riverside, Riverside, CA; received May 25, 2025; accepted November 5, 2025 

The spring-loaded spontaneous seed dispersal mechanism known as dehiscence, has been a critical plant feature for the successful colonization of land by angiosperms. Although advantageous for seed dispersal, spontaneous dehiscence is largely an unfavorable agronomic trait which historically was selected against during selective breeding of crops to increase seed retention. In canola (Brassica napus), a major global oil seed crop, spontaneous or harsh weather-induced fruit shattering at maturity could lead to yield losses from 3 to $5 0 \%$ . Here, we show an extraembryonic role for the ABA-responsive transcription factor, ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE-3 (ABI3) in controlling seed dispersal through mediating lignification of the endocarp b (enb) layer and the lignified layer (LL) of the valves. The resistance created by these lignified layers is critical for valve opening at maturity as the tensile forces generated during silique drying converge on these fortified cell layers to trigger shatter. We further show that ABI3 functions independent of the patterning genes and functions through transcriptional regulation of NAC-domain transcription factors, NST1 and NST3, to mediate lignin biosynthesis. Our results show that both excessive and complete absence of lignification could prevent the tensile drying forces from breaking open the pod, leading to fruit indehiscence. As a proof-of-concept, we show that BnABI3 overexpression in canola results in highly lignified, robust siliques that are shatter tolerant. Besides uncovering an extraembryonic role for ABI3, this study has identified spatial distribution and abundance of lignin in the silique valve tissue as the key determinants for silique dehiscence.

Many angiosperms, over the course of evolution have developed effective mechanisms for seed dispersal through a process known as dehiscence, where the mature, dry fruit breaks open to release the seeds. Unfortunately, this great innate plant strategy is a detriment following domestication where the prevention of seed loss is a more preferred trait. Arabidopsis thaliana, which has spontaneously dehiscing siliques, has been extensively used as a model to identify candidate genes to develop shatter-tolerant traits in Brassicaceae crops such as canola. 

In Arabidopsis , through precise and coordinated functioning of cell identity and tissue patterning genes, two crescent-shaped, symmetric cell tissues referred to as valves fuse together at a central seam known as the replum ( 1 ). This conjunction of the valves and replum creates a dehiscence zone (DZ), with a well-defined lignified layer (LL) and a separation layer (SL) where the valves and replum meet ( Fig. 1 ). The valve itself consists of three types of cell layers, the exocarp (epidermis), mesocarp, and endocarp a/b that make up the pericarp of the silique. Lignification is strictly restricted to the mature endocarp $b$ cell layer and LL, conferring the rigidity to these layers to provide the resistance for the tensile forces from silique drying to converge on this layer during silique maturity. During dehiscence, the SL breaks down following the enzymatic degradation mediated by cell wall processing enzymes, which primes the valves for dehiscence. The tensile forces from the drying of the mesocarp layers eventually converge on the rigid enb and LL separating the valve from the replum along the SL in a spring-loaded fashion. Lignin in the LL and enb layers is known to play an important role during this silique spring-loaded dehiscence process ( 2 , 3 ). 

A suite of genes precisely regulates silique patterning in Arabidopsis ( 4 – 10 ). Of these several genes, FRUITFULL (FUL) , SHATTERPROOF1 and 2 (SHP1/2), AlCATRAZ (ALC), and INDEHISCENT (IND) are the key players responsible for patterning of the DZ. FUL is a member of the MADS-box gene family required for normal valve differentiation and development along with controlling the extension of the silique ( 5 , 11 ). SHP1 and 2 are MADS-box gene family members downstream of FUL, involved in the differentiation of the DZ, required for lignification of the LL, and for promoting formation of the valve margin. # Significance

The canola industry significantly contributes $( \mathsf { C$ 43.7 } )$ ) billion to the Canadian economy. However, a major challenge persists with pod shattering at maturity, leading to an average annual seed loss of $3 \%$ $\lceil C \$ 1.31$ billion to the economy) due to spontaneous pod shatter. This loss can surge to $5 0 \%$ under harsh weather conditions. While shatter-tolerant canola varieties are available, their high seed costs often burden farmers. Our research offers a promising solution by exploiting canola pod lignification-a process of secondary cell wall deposition that reinforces the pod structure-to develop strong, shatter-tolerant canola lines. This innovation not only mitigates seed loss in canola but also holds potential for application in other agricultural crops, such as soybean and field peas.

IND gene encodes an atypical bHLH responsible for the differentiation of the valve, replum, and valve margin ( 13 – 15 ). The ind-1 mutant harbors major defects within the valve margin with no distinctive SL, with reduced lignification within the LL. Given that IND controls cell patterning, depending on the allelic variation utilized, there is the likelihood of development of complete indehiscence (valves fused to replum) due to either lack of or improper formation of the valve margin required for spring-loaded breakage ( 16 ). An alternate approach to this would be to enhance the strength of the siliques by preventing breakdown of the SL which is a prerequisite for valve opening. The coordinated, enzymatic breakdown of the SL is initiated by degrading the middle lamella and the cell wall material of the cells in the SL by enzymes such as ARABIDOPSIS DEHISCENCE ZONE POLYGALACTURONASE1 (ADPG1), CELLULASE6 (CEL6) and MANNANASE7 (MAN7) ( 17 , 18 ). Defects in all these genes can manifest as reduced shattering phenotypes in Arabidopsis ( 17 , 18 ). 

For the spring-loaded mechanism to function, it is crucial for lignification to occur in the LL and enb layers. The NAC 

SECONDARY WALL THICKENING PROMOTING FACTOR 1 and 3 (NST1/3) are required and function as master regulators of this process ( 3 , 19 , 20 ). NST1 and SND1/NST3 genes create a transcriptional regulatory network for secondary cell wall (SCW) deposition, as nst1/3 siliques display a complete lack of lignification within the LL, and endocarp $\bar { b }$ cell layer, leading to silique indehiscence ( 3 ). In addition, absence of functional NST1/3 also led to severely reduced lignification in the interfascicular and xylary fibers resulting in a pendent phenotype failing to stand upright ( 21 ). Such a severe compromise in growth precludes translation of the shatter tolerance observed in Arabidopsis to crop species such as canola. However, identifying genes that regulate silique-specific expression of NST1/3 could allow development of strategies to create stronger siliques via alterations to valve-specific lignin deposition, without affecting the overall silique or plant architecture. 

Abscisic acid insensitive 3 (ABI3) is a B3 domain transcription factor which has been demonstrated to be the master regulator of transcriptional responses during seed maturation and acquisition of desiccation ( 22 – 24 ). While it predominantly functions in the seed tissue, expression has been documented in seedlings, cauline leaves, abscission zones (leaf, pedicel), and root primordia ( 22 , 25 , 26 ), although its functional relevance in these tissues is poorly understood. In seed tissue, ABI3 has been shown to promote the accumulation of seed storage proteins (SSPs) and other protective proteins (HSPs), which play important roles in establishing seed dormancy and desiccation tolerance ( 23 , 24 ). In this study, we 

identify ABI3 as a bona fide regulator of NST1/3 that functions independent or downstream of the patterning genes to control lignification of the enb layer and the replum. Through exploiting this finding, we have developed a shatter-tolerant canola germplasm with stronger siliques resulting from enhanced lignification of valve tissue. 

# Results

ABI3 Is Required and Sufficient to Enhance Lignification of the Endocarp b (enb) of the Silique Wall. ABI3 is an evolutionarily conserved B3 domain transcription factor known to be involved in drought adaptation in mosses and has been repurposed in angiosperms to be exclusive for embryo desiccation and acquisition of dormancy. We had previously demonstrated the requirement of ABI3 for embryo degreening through regulating Mendel’s I locus (23). While investigating nonfemale gametophyte related functions of ABI3, we observed that siliques lacking ABI3 harbored severely decreased lignification in the valve tissue, particularly in the endocarp b (enb) layer, the innermost layer of the silique wall (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S1A). While fresh handsections with phloroglucinol staining showed almost an absence of lignin in abi3-6 (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A), fixed, resin-embedded toluidine blue O-stained sections detected a marked reduction in enb lignin in abi3-6 mutant compared to Arabidopsis wild-type Col-0 at 12 DAP (Days After Pollination) (Fig. 2A). In contrast, overexpression of ABI3 under the 35S promoter was sufficient to drive enhanced lignification in these layers of the valve suggesting the sufficiency of ABI3 to cause remarkable alteration of valve lignin deposition (Fig.  2 A and $B$ and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S1B). When the secondary cell wall thickening was quantified relative to the total cross-sectional area of the cell, a significant reduction in valve $\operatorname { e n } \theta$ lignification was observed in abi3-6, relative to Col-0, while ABI3-OX valves exhibited a significant increase relative to Col-0 valves at 12 DAP (Fig. $2 C$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S1C). 

To eliminate the possibility of an indirect effect of lack of ABI3 in the embryonic tissue on silique wall lignification, we assessed ABI3 promoter activity in the valve tissue using $\mathit { p A B I 3 ! : Y F P }$ in Col-0 background. Strong YFP signal could be observed within the mesocarp and endocarp a (ena ) cells in fresh histological cross-sections examined at 6 DAP when silique lignification was beginning to occur ( Fig. $2 D$ , Top ). Sections taken at 9 DAP showed strong YFP signal within the endocarp $b$ cells ( Fig. 2 D , Bottom ). This result confirms the presence of ABI3 expression within the endocarp $b$ cells where the lignification phenotype was observed, suggesting ABI3 has a self-autonomous function within this layer. 

To further examine whether ABI3- mediated transcriptional network included genes involved in secondary cell wall biosynthesis, siliques devoid of seeds collected from Col-0 and abi3-6 plants at 6, 9, and 12 DAP were subjected to RNA sequencing. When GO term accession for the lignin biosynthetic process (GO:0009809) was analyzed in the differentially expressed genes, majority of the genes within the lignin biosynthetic process were downregulated in abi3-6 siliques (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A ). This observation suggests that ABI3 is required for transcriptional control of genes involved in lignin biosynthesis. 

ABI3-Overexpression Results in Shatter Tolerance in Arabidopsis. The key to developing shatter-tolerant traits in crops is to either modify cell patterning that leads to nonshattering siliques or create siliques that maintain their identity of cellular architecture through forming the dehiscence zone but are harder to open due to stronger silique walls. Using a spontaneously shattering model plant such as Arabidopsis, both approaches could be effectively assessed 

by determining improvement from the baseline of spontaneous shattering phenotype. However, extreme care must be taken to avoid any physical perturbations that would induce premature dehiscence while observing for shatter-tolerant phenotypes. When silique shattering was assessed in the highly $\operatorname { e n } \theta$ lignified ABI3-OX lines compared to Col-0 and abi3-6, ABI3 overexpression resulted in a silique indehiscent phenotype with a significantly increased percentage of intact siliques (Fig. $2 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S2 B–D). This suggests that enhanced lignification and increased strength to the silique wall can lead to stronger siliques that are less shatter prone. 

ABI3-OX Rescues Reduced enb Lignification of the Indehiscent Mutants ind-1 and shp1/2. We next wanted to investigate whether the increase in lignification observed in ABI3-OX had an effect on the classical indehiscent silique cell identity mutants ind-1 and shp1/2, which lack or have reduced lignification in the layer normally designated for valve margin formation. Histological preparations of ind-1 siliques showed the lack of valve margin specification due to the absence of a distinct nonlignified separation layer. When assessed for LL and enb lignification, ind-1 displayed a general reduction in lignified enb cells in valve tissue compared to Col-0 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). When ABI3-OX was placed within background lines of ind-1, although ABI3-OX did not complement tissue identify defect, it was able to enhance the lignification of enb cells in ind1 siliques (depicted by red arrowheads) (SI Appendix, Fig.  S3) suggesting that ABI3 functioned at or downstream of IND in the enb lignification pathway. 

Histological sections at the base of the siliques of shp1/2 mutant showed reduced lignification in enb cells and complete absence in the valve margin region (indicated by red and black arrowheads) (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). When siliques from shp1/2/ABI3-OX lines were assessed, although the tissue patterning defect (lack of a dehiscence zone) was not complemented, enhanced lignification of enb cells were observed (indicated by red arrowheads) (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ), suggesting that ABI3 functioned at or downstream of SHP1/2 to mediate enb lignification. 

The previously characterized ful-1 (CS3759) mutant has been shown to have ectopically lignified cells within valve tissue ( 5 , 11 ). The ful-1 mutant displays severe developmental phenotypes, which include shorter siliques that fail to restrict the valve margin identity to dehiscent zone 5, 11). To evaluate the impact of ABI3 on valve lignification in the ful-1 mutant, both transgenic ABI3-OX /ful-1 and double mutant abi3-6/ful-1 were developed and assessed for lignification patterns. As previously described, the cells in the valve region of ful-1 failed to expand compared to valve mesocarp cells in Col-0 (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). In abi3-6/ful-1 double mutant, although lack of ABI3 resulted in visually (based on TBO staining) less ectopically lignified cells in the valve area when compared to the ful-1 mutant, this observation was not statistically significant (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 A and B ). ABI3 overexpression in the ful-1 background did not affect the developmental defects in replum and valve tissue when compared to the ful-1 mutant. Additionally, ABI3-OX did not result in enhanced lignification in the valve region in ABI3-OX/ful1 plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). Observations from the digenic analyses suggest that ABI3 modulates LL and endocarp $b$ lignification at or downstream of the valve margin identity genes, IND, and SHP1/2 .

Given the reduction in lignification within the abi3-6 mutant and increase in lignification when ABI3 is overexpressed, and detection of ABI3 expression within lignifying enb cells (Fig. 2), we hypothesized that ABI3 should be acting through transcriptional regulation of genes involved in secondary cell wall biosynthesis.

ABA Levels and ABA Signaling Are Required for enb Lignification of the Silique Valves. ABI3 being an ABA-dependent transcription factor, we next sought to determine whether upstream ABA biosynthesis and signaling were required for the observed enb lignification phenotype in the abi3-6 mutant. We utilized the aba 2-2 ABSCISIC ACID DEFICIENT 2 (ABA2) mutant with severely reduced ABA levels and the SUCROSE NONFERMENTING 1 (SNF1)-RELATED PROTEIN KINASE 2 (SNRK2) triple mutant, snrk $2 . 2 / 2 . 3 / 2 . 6$ to assess the lignification in both the silique and stem tissue. Given the desiccation intolerance of these lines and their sensitivity to low humidity, we maintained these lines with the appropriate controls at high humidity $( > 8 0 \% )$ ) conditions. A complete silique histological profile for each mutant was analyzed to create a complete picture of silique lignification. Within the aba 2-2, a slight reduction in silique lignification was observed within the replum, LL, and endocarp $b ,$ when compared to Col-0 (Fig. 4A). This reduction in lignification is not as drastic as is observed within abi3-6 lines, suggesting a marginal ABA level is sufficient to drive lignification (Fig. 4A). In contrast, the snrk $2 . 2 / 2 . 3 / 2 . 6$ triple mutant harbored a drastic reduction or sporadic absence of lignification in the cells within the LL and endocarp a/b joint (Fig. 4A). This lack of lignin phenotype was profound within the enb cell layer where most of the cells within this region contained no lignification (Fig. 4A). Interestingly, the absence of intense lignification in the enb layer resulted in a lack of rigidity of this layer causing a wavy pattern of the cells similar to nst1/3 (Fig. 4A). 

Two Extremes of Lignification Result In Silique Indehiscence. In our study, we identified that enhancing enb lignification in ABI3- OX siliques (Fig. 4A) resulted in a shatter-tolerant phenotype, while more dehiscent (more cracked siliques) phenotype was observed in the abi3-6 siliques. However, the siliques of nst1/3 double mutant that completely lacked lignification in the LL and the enb layer remained indehiscent (Fig. 4B) (3). This observation was quite intriguing as either extremely high lignification or complete lack of lignification of the valve layers resulted in indehiscent phenotypes, providing insights into how the tensile forces during silique drying require the appropriate resistance from the lignified silique layers to break open the siliques. 

Given that ABI3 could be mediating lignification through the NST1/3 circuit, we tested whether overexpression of NST1 or 3 could confer a similar shatter tolerance phenotype ( Fig. 4A ). Overexpression of 35S::NST1 or NST3 in Col-0 background resulted in strong enb lignification phenotype similar to the ABI3-OX lines and a shatter-tolerant phenotype ( Fig. 4 A and $B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). We next examined whether the altered LL and enb lignification of ABA biosynthetic and signaling mutants resulted in an alteration in shatter tolerance. Since silique dehiscence within Arabidopsis is spontaneous under low ambient humidity conditions, siliques were classified as either intact, cracked, or shattered (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B ). The aba2-2 and abi3-6 siliques with reduced enb lignin relative to Col-0, phenotypically mimicked the spontaneous shatter phenotype of Col-0 ( Fig. 4 B –D ). The snr $\cdot 2 . 2 / 2 . 3 / 2 . 6$ siliques with severely reduced or absent LL and enb lignification had no shattered siliques with majority of the siliques remaining intact $0 \%$ shattered and $8 8 . 3 \%$ intact) very similar to nst1/3 $( 0 \%$ shattered and $9 3 . 3 \%$ intact) ( Fig. 4 B –D ). These data further validate our observation that while a reduction of valve layer lignin (abi3-6 ) is insufficient to block dehiscence, almost complete absence of valve lignin would result in indehiscent siliques ( Fig. 4 B –D and SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ). Compared to the no lignin phenotypes of nst1/3 and snrk2.2/2.3/2.6 siliques, overexpression of ABI3, NST1, and NST3 resulted in a similar silique indehiscence phenotype ( Fig. 4 B –D and SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ). These statistically significant observations further validate our hypothesis that silique indehiscence can be achieved through both complete loss of lignification and an increase in lignification, while a reduction in lignin is not capable of achieving an indehiscent phenotype (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ).

# Discussion

The coordinated communication between the maturing embryos/ seeds and the developing fruit is crucial in angiosperms for precise silique maturation synchronously with seed development for timely silique dehiscence and seed dispersal to occur. Despite our understanding of the seed maturation program, our knowledge of how this synchrony is maintained is still lacking. Our study has uncovered an extraembryonic role for ABI3 in an ABA signaling-dependent 

regulation of valve lignification, a key programing step for tensile forces from drying silique layers to trigger silique dehiscence ( 2 , 3 , 27 ). It is important to note that contrary to NST1/3 , which have a general role in mediating lignification throughout the plant conferring the erect phenotype, ABI3’s control of lignification is primarily restricted to the valve tissue. Lack of ABI3 did not affect interfascicular lignification unlike the pendent nst1/3 mutant (SI Appendix, Fig. S11 ) or other ABA signaling mutants, and the plants looked normal except for the lack of ABI3 associated embryonic phenotypes such as absence of seed degreening, dormancy, and desiccation tolerance. 

Our study has demonstrated a role of both ABA and $\mathrm { S n R K } 2 . 2 / 2 . \dot { 3 } / 2 . 6$ kinases for lignification of the valve tissue suggesting that ABA-dependent signal could converge on the ABI3- NST1/3 network to mediate secondary cell wall biosynthesis. 

$\mathrm { S n R K } 2 . 2 / 2 . 3 / 2 . 6$ kinases have been previously shown to directly phosphorylate NST1 to regulate its transcriptional activity during lignification in the stem interfascicular fibers and Xylary fibers ( 21 ). Although, whether the SnRKs could directly phosphorylate ABI3 similar to NST1 remains to be seen in its extra embryonic role in the valve tissue, we cannot preclude this possibility given the similar phenotypes the loss-of-function mutants of these genes confer in the seeds. With the ind-1 and shp1/2 mutants, ABI3 misexpression was not sufficient to drive lignification in the valve margin zones that were missing cell identity in the dehiscence zone suggesting that correct tissue patterning may be required for proper deposition of lignin in the valve margin. The maintenance of proper tissue patterning in the nst1/3 mutant despite the lack of 

lignification in the valve margin and enb layer supports this theory ( 3 ). 

Typically, during dehiscence, the process of drying results in shrinkage of the thin walled mesocarp and exocarp cells while the lignified enb and LL are inelastic and resist shrinking. This differential shrinking causes immense tensile stress across the valve that eventually converges on the weakest point near the dehiscence zone causing the valves to break open. The extent of lignification within the enb and mesocarp layers could determine whether a phenotype is ultimately shattering or shatter tolerant ( Fig. $4 A { - } D )$ ). Both aba2-2 and abi3-6 that have reduced but do not completely abolish lignin within the enb layer have a shattering phenotype similar to Col-0 ( Fig. 4 A –D ). Mutants that contain no lignin within the enb layer (nst1/ 3) or an enb layer that contains disrupted zones with no lignin $( s n r k 2 . 2 / 2 . 3 / 2 . { \overset { . } { 6 } } )$ harbor a pod inde hiscent phenotype ( Fig. 4 A –D ). Contrastingly, we observed shattertolerant phenotype when excessive lignification was detected within the enb layer, as observed in ABI3-OX, NST1-OX, and NST3-OX lines ( Fig. 4 A –D ). We propose two hypotheses to explain the contrasting lignin levels resulting in silique indehiscence. 1. In the complete absence of lignin (nst1/3, snrk2.2/2.3/2.6) , the enb cells are more elastic and continue to shrink without any resistance, resulting in inability to generate sufficient tensile forces across the system to break the valves open 2. In the case of excessive lignification (ABI3-OX, NST1/3-OX ), the enhanced fortification of either enb layer or layers of the valve margin close to the DZ, could prevent the tensile forces from reaching the DZ resulting in an indehiscent phenotype. Based on these findings, it appears that lignification could serve as a double-edged sword with requirement of goldilocks amount of lignin for shattering to occur 

that could be further explored to develop strategies to mitigate silique shatter losses. 

In Canola, the yield loss associated with silique shatter ranges from 3 to $5 \%$ under favorable harvesting conditions ( 29 ) with extreme weather conditions (wind, rain, snow) during harvest resulting in ${ > } 5 0 \%$ shatter related loss ( 29 ). Based on 2024 Canola production values, a modest $5 \%$ yield loss would result in economic losses exceeding $\sim \mathrm { C S 6 0 0 }$ million (The Canola Council of Canada, 2024). In addition to yield loss, silique shatter can increase the population of volunteer canola, which require more frequent and highly concentrated applications of herbicide to control competition in successive crops ( 30 ). If not managed properly these could lead to volunteer canola seeds with resistance to broad spectrum herbicides including glyphosate and glufosinate ammonium ( 31 ). Through identification of ABI3 as a master regulator of lignification exclusively in the valve tissue, we were able to develop shatter-tolerant canola lines by overexpressing ABI3. These canola lines developed stronger siliques harboring thicker and wider valve walls with more silique biomass when compared to unmodified parental line (SI Appendix, Fig. S10 ). The overall mass of canola siliques lacking seed tissue has been shown to have a positive correlation to shatter tolerance ( 32 ). Our histological analysis indicates that extensive lignification in the valve-margin region and valve mesocarp cells is likely conferring the shatter tolerance trait. We speculate that the increased lignin in the valve margin may promote the rigidity of tissues adjacent to the separation layer, which could potentially prevent any drying-related flexion of the silique wall. This may reduce the effect of tensile forces acting on the separation layer required for silique shatter to occur ( Fig. 6 ).

We have previously shown that these ABI3-OX lines manifested enhanced degreening capabilities under challenging freezing conditions ( 33 ). Demonstrating shatter tolerance through enhanced silique lignification is an added agronomic trait that would bolster the resiliency of this germplasm to unfavorable environmental conditions. It is important to note that in contrast to targeting the cell identity genes that could completely abrogate silique identity resulting in nonshattering siliques, the ABI3-OX approach results in stronger siliques that have no defect in cell or tissue patterning. B. napus (Canola) ABI3 Overexpression Construct. The BnABI3-OX plasmid was previously cloned in the Samuel lab by Mendel Perkins. Westar) was harvested from 30 DAP seed and used for RNA extraction. Seed RNA was then used for cDNA synthesis. The cDNA was used as the DNA template to PCR amplify the CDS region of the BnABI3 gene. Regenerated plants following transformation were screened on hygromycin antibiotic.

Following successful sequencing of the genetic construct, the AtABI3-overexpression cassette was transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Seeds produced through floral dip transformation were screened and selected on plates containing hygromycin.

Canola seeds were subjected to $4 0 \%$ bleach sterilization, followed by triple rinse with autoclaved deionized water. Plates were covered in foil and allowed to stratify at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Random Impact Shatter Test. The random impact shatter test was adapted from previously published works (Wang et al., 2007) (35). Hand-pollinated canola flowers developed into siliques which were grown until maturity (~50 DAP) and manually harvested to ensure minimal disturbance to silique integrity. Siliques were allowed to acclimate in $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \complement$ incubator for $\boldsymbol { { 1 0 \mathrm { h } } }$ overnight to ensure similar relative humidity. Ten canola siliques were placed in a flatbottom $9 . 5 ~ \mathrm { c m } \times 1 5$ cm cardboard box with 10 steel ball bearings (9 mm diameter). The box was placed in bacterial orbital shaker and ran at 300 rpm intervals for 2 min. After each 2-min interval, siliques were removed and photographed for shatter analysis. Shatter testing was completed over a 4-min time span. All siliques broken and intact were placed into the box after each time increment. To score the shatter tolerance, each full silique received a score of one and a silique half intact received 0.5. The scores across the two intervals were totaled, with the maximum possible score of 20 (10 full intact siliques $\times$ two intervals). The total score was then divided by 20 to give us a number that represented the shattering index. Two biological replicates with six plants per replicate were grown for analysis. Five siliques from each plant were collected and pooled for shatter analysis. In total, six replicates consisting of 10 siliques each were analyzed for silique shatter. 

Dried B. napus Silique Lignin Content Percentage. Dried canola siliques were used to determine lignin content in valve tissues. Approximately 8 g of dried valves were ground using an electric coffee grinder and shipped to SGS Canada for lignin quantification. Siliques were collected from three biological replicates consisting of three plants each and pooled for analysis, due to the large amount of tissue required for analysis. Four replicates consisting of two grams each, were sent for lignin quantification. The primary methodology to determine lignin content was through proprietary acid detergent lignin quantification.

The total endocarp b cell surface area was determined. The inner surface

A total of 50 enb cells were measured per genotype. For a visual representation of the measurement, refer to figure 17g.

A. thaliana and B. napus Promoter Analysis. A. thaliana promoter sequences were analyzed using Plant Pan 3.0 platform. Gene accession numbers were used to identify the gene promoter of interest. The analysis was conducted $+ 1 . 0 0 0$ base pairs upstream of the ATG translational start site. Specifically, the promoters were analyzed for the core RY motif (CATGCA) and full-length RY motif (CATGCATG). Canola promoters were manually assessed for the core and full-length RY motif. Sequences of the canola promoters were obtained off NCBI database. The promoter sequence $+ 1 . 0 0 0$ base pairs upstream of the ATG translational start site were assessed. 

Chromatin Immunoprecipitation PCR (ChIP-PCR). The chromatin immunoprecipitation protocol used in this investigation was recommended by Yuhai Cui from Agriculture Agri-Food Canada (40). I had the opportunity to meet Cui and learn the ChIP-PCR technique with his research scientists during a trip to London, Ontario. The ChIP procedure was conducted using the ChIP Assay Kit from EMD Millipore (17-295 Sigma-Aldrich). Combined seed and silique tissues were harvested from two independently transformed aip2 ABI3-OX Arabidopsis (L1, L2) lines. One gram was harvested from each line and pooled to form a 2-g sample, which was cross-linked using fresh formaldehyde. The following stages were conducted in accordance with the published literature utilizing the buffers and reagents specified in the kit (40). Following isolation of nuclear protein and preclearing samples with protein A-agarose beads, ABI3 was immunoprecipitated using a gene specific polyclonal antibody developed and manufactured in rabbit by GenScript. Prior to ChIP analysis, the ABI3 antibody was tested to ensure adequate detection of ABI3 protein in seed and silique samples. 

The antibody was added to sample tubes using a 1:200 dilution and allowed to incubate at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ overnight. Beads were recovered the following day and immunoprecipitated complexes were eluted using a series of premade buffers. Cross linking was reversed following addition of 5 M NaCl, and overnight incubation at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ . After cross linking, protein in the eluted sample was degraded following a 1-h proteinase K digestion (0.5 M EDTA, $\mathbb { 1 } \mu \iota$ of $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ proteinase K, 1 M Tris-HCl pH 5.5). Immunoprecipitated DNA was recovered using phenol/ chloroform followed by ethanol-sodium acetate precipitation. The eluted DNA was subsequently used fo ${ \mathsf { q } } ^ { \mathsf { P } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { R } }$ analysis to detect enriched promoter sequences following ABI3 immunoprecipitation. The qPCR was used to determine CT threshold which could then be utilized to determine fold enrichment which normalized immunoprecipitated samples to no-antibody controls. 

RNA-Seq Heat Maps and Venn Diagrams. RNA-sequencing and differential expression analysis were performed by Novogene (Sacramento, CA, USA). Differential expression was assessed using Log2Foldchange $( | | 2 \mathsf { F C } | > 1$ ) to identify significantly regulated genes, with all samples standardized to Col-0. Venn diagrams for coexpression profiling were created by identifying differentially expressed genes, compiling a master list of unique accessions, and classifying genes as up- or down-regulated based on their Log2Foldchange. For heat maps, gene ontology terms from AmiGO2 (https://amigo.geneontology.org/amigo) were used to create a master list, which was uploaded to the Heatmapper web server (http://www.heatmapper.ca/expression/).
