# Abstract

Cold and drought stresses severely limit crop production and can occur simultaneously. Although some transcription factors and hormones have been characterized in plants subjected each stress, the role of metabolites, especially volatiles, in response to cold and drought stress exposure is rarely studied due to lack of suitable models. Here, we established a model for studying the role of volatiles in tea (Camellia sinensis) plants experiencing cold and drought stresses simultaneously. Using this model, we showed that volatiles induced by cold stress promote drought tolerance in tea plants by mediating reactive oxygen species and stomatal conductance. Needle trap microextraction combined with GC-MS identified the volatiles involved in the crosstalk and showed that cold-induced (Z)-3-hexenol improved the drought tolerance of tea plants. In addition, silencing C. sinensis alcohol dehydrogenase 2 (CsADH2) led to reduced (Z)-3-hexenol production and significantly reduced drought tolerance in response to simultaneous cold and drought stress. Transcriptome and metabolite analyses, together with plant hormone comparison and abscisic acid (ABA) biosynthesis pathway inhibition experiments, further confirmed the roles of ABA in (Z)-3-hexenol–induced drought tolerance of tea plants. (Z)-3-Hexenol application and gene silencing results supported the hypothesis that (Z)-3-hexenol plays a role in the integration of cold and drought tolerance by stimulating the dual-function glucosyltransferase UGT85A53, thereby altering ABA homeostasis in tea plants. Overall, we present a model for studying the roles of metabolites in plants under multiple stresses and reveal the roles of volatiles in integrating cold and drought stresses in plants. 

# Introduction

Plants are constantly exposed to harsh and changing environmental conditions like drought, salinity, heat, cold, chilling, freezing, nutrient scarcity, and high light intensity. To cope with these adverse environmental factors, plants have adapted to undergo cellular reprogramming by triggering a wide-ranging signaling network that compromises their development and reproduction (Suzuki et al. 2014). 

Mechanisms of plant response to abiotic stresses that could drastically impact crop growth and production have been extensively studied (Wang et al. 2003; Nakashima and Yamaguchi-shinozaki 2006; Munns and Tester 2008; Mittler and Blumwald 2010; Shi et al. 2018). Nevertheless, under field conditions, plants and crops are regularly subjected to 2 or more abiotic stresses simultaneously. The response of plants and crops to combined stresses is substantially more 

complex than to a single stress due to the crosstalk between signal pathways and the interactions among different metabolic pathways (Suzuki et al. 2014). Efforts to examine plant response and acclimation to multiple stresses may also inform strategies for manipulating plants to improve stress tolerance and productivity (Prasch and Sonnewald 2015). 

The major abiotic stresses that affect plants and crops in the field are being extensively studied in the laboratory on an individual basis, but the study of stress combinations has only been attempted in recent years (Suzuki et al. 2014). In experiments using microarray analysis, ${ \sim } 2 5 \%$ of the transcripts showed similar responses to different individual stresses but showed a different response when the stresses were combined (Rasmussen et al. 2013). This suggests that each stress requires a unique response and that a combination of stresses may require a response that is also unique (Mittler et al. 2004). Systems biology analyses highlighted the complexity of signaling pathway integration under multiple stresses, and both negative and positive impacts of plants to different combinations of stresses were found (Suzuki et al. 2014). Metabolic and signaling pathways, including transcription factors, photosynthesis, antioxidant mechanisms, and hormones, have been studied in plants simultaneously subjected to 2 or more distinct stresses (Prasch and Sonnewald 2013; Rasmussen et al. 2013). 

The tea plant (Camellia sinensis (L.) O. Kuntze) is 1 of the most important economic crops in China, Japan, India, and Kenya and prefers warm and humid conditions (Hu et al. 2020). Cold and drought are 2 major environmental factors that affect the geographic distribution and growth of tea plants during the winter, especially in northwestern and eastern regions of China (Hu et al. 2020; Lu et al. 2021; Zhao et al. 2022). A separate study focusing on maize (Zea mays) response to a combination of drought and cold stress demonstrated that plants subjected to cold stress alone suffered irreversible damage to photosynthetic capacity and chloroplast structure, while plants subjected to combined drought and cold stress experienced a decline in photosynthetic capacity but subsequently recovered following removal from stress (Guo et al. 2021). 

Given that both cold and drought stresses lead to cellular dehydration, it is unsurprising that the signaling pathways overlap when plants are exposed to combined drought and cold stress. Both drought and cold stresses increase the endogenous level of abscisic acid (ABA), a key and universal plant stress signaling hormone that plays a critical role in the closure of stomata by regulating guard cell ion fluxes in response to osmotic stress (Liu et al. 2013). In plants, the ABA level is triggered in response to various stress signals, mainly due to the induction of genes encoding enzymes responsible for ABA biosynthesis. Among them, 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase (NCED) is considered to catalyze the rate-limiting step in ABA biosynthesis (Tan et al. 1997). The signaling pathways involved in drought stress are orchestrated by both ABA-dependent and ABA-independent pathways, whereas plant responses to 

cold stress are thought to be less dependent on ABA compared with the response to drought stress. Despite the importance of the crosstalk between ABA-dependent and ABA-independent pathways, knowledge of how the 2 signaling pathways regulate each other has been limited until recently. 

Because of our limited knowledge of stress signaling intermediates, crosstalk between different stresses is poorly understood in plants. In addition to the hormones and sugars mentioned above, secondary metabolites may also play a role in integrating drought and cold stress pathways. Plants produce volatile organic compounds (VOCs) constitutively in response to external stimuli (Takabayashi et al. 1994; Loreto and Schnitzler 2010). VOCs mediated that plant–plant communication under biotic stress has been observed in more than 30 species (Heil and Karban 2010). For instance, in insects, (Z)-3-hexenol can activate ${ \mathsf { C a } } ^ { 2 + }$ -related genes or MAPK (mitogen-activated protein kinase) pathways, or it can be absorbed and converted into glycosides to improve insect resistance of plants (Xin et al. 2019; Jing et al. 2019a).Very recently, our studies also confirmed the role of VOCs under abiotic stress, such as cold-induced nerolidol, geraniol, linalool, and methyl salicylate that play key role in plant–plant communication and cold stress tolerance of tea plants (Zhao et al. 2020). In addition, the volatile eugenol can be taken up and metabolized into glycosides and thus enhance cold and drought tolerance of tea plants (Zhao et al. 2022). 

Both cold and drought stresses have been extensively studied, and based on these stress tolerance mechanisms, it is logical to expect that signal transduction pathways may crosstalk at various steps under multiple stresses. In contrast to Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), tea plants can produce and emit abundant VOCs during growth and development, which is relevant for tea plant performance and product quality (Song et al. 2018). In this study, a research model was built to investigate the role of VOCs between signaling pathways in plants exposed to multiple stresses. In our model, tea plants were exposed to the volatile compounds produced by cold-stressed tea seedlings and then subjected to drought stress. We found that tea plants that had been exposed to cold-induced volatile compounds (VOCs) were more drought resistant. Cold-induced (Z)-3-hexenol was found to play a key role in the droughtstressed tea plants by stimulating the expression of C. sinensis UDP—glycosyltransferase 85A53 (CsUGT85A53), which catalyzed UDP-glucose and ABA to form $\beta$ -D-glucopyranosyl abscisate (ABA-GE), in turn promoting soluble sugar and ABA accumulation and improving drought resistance.

# Cold stress–induced volatile cues enhance drought tolerance of their neighbors

Interestingly, we found that the degree of wilting in tea leaves in the T group, which had been exposed to cold-stressed emitters, was lower than in the CK group (Fig. 1, B and C). The tea leaves of the CK group also lost noticeably more water than those of the T group throughout the study (Fig. 1D). It is well-known that peroxidase (POD) and superoxide dismutase (SOD) are 2 crucial antioxidant enzymes responsible for reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging (Choudhury et al. 2017). To assess whether POD and SOD were involved in inducing drought tolerance in the T group plants, their activities were subsequently measured, and the results showed that their activities were much higher in the T group than in the control plants (Fig. 1, E and F). 

ROS accumulation and subsequent oxidative stress are common phenomena that occur when a plant is challenged with abiotic stressors. The levels of 2 major ROS, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ , were examined based on histochemical staining using $^ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) and nitro blue tetrazolium (NBT), respectively. Upon exposure to drought stress, the tea leaves of the control displayed a deeper and more extensive staining by both DAB and NBT relative to the T group (Fig. 1G). In situ accumulation of ROS analyzed by quantitative measurements of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ (measured by anti- $\cdot 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ capacity, which is negatively proportional to $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ ) clearly showed that tea leaves in the T group displayed lower ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels and greater anti- $\cdot 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ capacity than the CK group (Fig. 1, H and I). In addition, the tea leaves in the T group had dramatically smaller stomatal apertures compared to the CK group (Fig. 1, J and K). These results suggested that cold-stressed induced volatile cues can enhance the drought tolerance of their neighbors by affecting water loss rate, ROS accumulation, and stomatal apertures. 

# Cold-induced volatiles enhance drought tolerance by affecting the soluble sugar and ABA accumulation of their neighbors

To gain insight into the mechanism underlying these observed effects, the gene expression of tea leaves from plants exposed to cold stressed and control plants was compared by RNA-Seq. A total of 260,052,408 $( 9 7 . 3 7 \% )$ ) clean reads were retained from 6 cDNA libraries constituting 3 biological replicates (Supplemental Table S1). The Q20 values were $1 0 0 \%$ and the GC percentages ranged from $4 4 . 6 \%$ to $4 8 . 5 \%$ . Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were screened with a threshold value of $P < 0 . 0 5$ and d $\| \mathsf { o g } _ { 2 } \mathsf { F C } \| > 1$ . In total, 922 DEGs were screened between T 

and CK group samples, including 589 up- and 333 downregulated genes (Supplemental Fig. S1). Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis revealed that many DEGs were involved in starch and sucrose metabolism and plant hormone signal transduction pathways (Supplemental Fig. S2), suggesting that sucrose and plant hormones played key roles in VOC-mediated integration of 2 stresses. 

To further determine which plant hormones can integrate different stresses, the expression of genes involved in the biosynthesis of plant hormones, including ABA, auxin, jasmonic acid (JA), and salicylic acid (SA), was analyzed, and the results showed that most of the genes involved in ABA biosynthesis were upregulated (Supplemental Fig. S3). Plants synthesize ABA using the carotenoid pathway initiated from $\beta$ -carotene. Conversion of $9 ^ { \prime }$ -cis-neoxanthin and $9 ^ { \prime }$ -cis-violaxanthin to xanthoxin by NCED is a rate-limiting step in ABA biosynthesis. In the cytoplasm, xanthoxin dehydrogenase (ABA2) and abscisic-aldehyde oxidase (AAO3) convert xanthoxin into ABA (Fig. 2A). In this study, we observed that the expression of NCED and ABA2 was upregulated when focal plants were exposed to cold-stressed plants (Fig. 2A). Moreover, the content of ABA and ABA-GE in tea leaves was also increased after exposure compared to control plants (Fig. 2, B and C). 

Starch is degraded at night into glucose or maltose for export to the cytoplasm. Since many genes of starch and sucrose metabolism were enriched in T plants, we further analyzed the transcript changes of genes involved in sugar-mediated signaling pathways (Fig. 2D). Glucose may be phosphorylated by hexokinase to generate Glc-6-phosphate (Glc-6-P) and then converted to fructose-6-phosphate (Fru-6-P). In addition, glucose can be further converted to UDP-glucose, catalyzed by UTP-glucose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase (UGP). The starch degradation–related genes α/β-amylase (AMY) were partly upregulated, and UGP was upregulated (Fig. 2D). Genes involved in sucrose synthesis, including sucrosephosphate synthase (SPS) and sucrose-phosphate phosphatase (SPP), were upregulated (Fig. 2D). Sucrose content in the T group was also dramatically higher than in the control group (Fig. 2F). Trehalose-6-phosphate (T6P), a recently discovered signal metabolite, is a product of T6P synthase (TPS) using UDP-glucose and Glc-6-P as substrates. After exposure, most of TPSs were upregulated while most of TPPs were downregulated. Meanwhile, trehalase (TL) showed a downward trend, and most sucrose degradation–related genes, including invertase (INV), showed an upward trend (Fig. 2D). As expected, the total soluble sugar content in the T group was higher than in the control plants (Fig. 2, E and G). Taken together, our current results suggest that cold-induced volatiles might enhance the drought tolerance of their neighbors by affecting soluble sugar and ABA accumulation.

# Cold-induced (Z)-3-hexenol improved the drought tolerance of tea plants

To investigate which VOCs released from the cold-stressed emitters can integrate cold and drought stress, exogenous volatile exposure experiments were performed. Tea plants were preexposed to (Z)-3-hexenol, (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate, hexyl acetate, linalool, and trans-linalool oxide in 5-L glass cases with a concentration equal to the amounts released by tea plants under cold stress for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Then, drought tolerance was measured. Tea plants exposed to the same content of DMSO were used as control. 

No remarkable effect was observed for tea plants after exposed to hexyl acetate, trans-linalool oxide, (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate, or linalool compared to the controls under drought stress according to the phenotype and relative moisture (Fig. 4, A to D). In addition, the (Z)-3-hexenol–exposed seedlings had dramatically smaller stomatal apertures compared to control plants (Fig. 4K). # (Z )-3-Hexenol integrates cold and drought stresses dependent on ABA homeostasis in tea plants

To further confirm the role of ABA in (Z)-3-hexenol–induced drought tolerance in tea plants, we blocked the biosynthesis of ABA using the ABA inhibitor fluridone (FLD) (Gamble and Mullet 1986), which has been used to determine the role of ABA in wound suberization (Lulai et al. 2008; Tao et al. 2016). Using FLD, the biosynthesis pathway of ABA was successfully inhibited, as evidenced by the dramatically decreased content of ABA and ABA-GE in FLD-treated tea plants (Fig. 6, A and B). Under drought stress, FLD-treated plants showed 

more severe wilting (Fig. 6H) compared to control plants. Consistent with that phenotype, FLD-treated tea plants also had a larger leaf angle, lower moisture content, and accumulated more ROS and MDA (malondialdehyde) compared to control plants (black pillar in Fig. 6, C to G). The severe wilting phenotype and corresponding indicators in FLD-pretreated tea plants could not be rescued by (Z )-3-hexenol exposure (Fig. 6, C to H), indicating that (Z)-3-hexenol exposure promoted drought tolerance of plants dependent on the ABA biosynthesis pathway. 

ABA-responsive element-binding factors (ABFs) have been found to act at the core of the ABA signaling transduction pathway. CsABF2, CsABF8, and CsABF11 were found to play a key role in regulating drought tolerance in tea cultivars (Lu et al. 2021). CsSnRK2.5 is a positive regulator of ABA-regulated drought stress responses (Zhang et al. 2020). Expression of CsSnRK2.5, CsABF2, and CsABF8 was remarkably upregulated in (Z)-3-hexenol–exposed tea plants under drought stress compared to control plants, suggesting that (Z)-3-hexenol integrates cold and drought tolerance through the ABA signaling pathway (Fig. 6, I to K). 

# (Z)-3-Hexenol integrates cold and drought stresses and is associated with UGT85A53-mediated ABA glycosylation in tea plants

We have presented evidence that airborne (Z)-3-hexenol affects the biosynthesis of ABA, and the next question is how (Z)-3-hexenol regulates the biosynthesis of ABA in drought stress. The evidence that airborne (Z)-3-hexenol can be taken up and further modified by glycosylation or acetylation inspired us to explore the underlying mechanism for the regulation of ABA formation by (Z )-3-hexenol. In tea plants, insect-induced (Z)-3-hexenol can be absorbed by neighboring tea plants and converted to (Z)-3-hexenol glycosides by CsUGT85A53 (Jing et al. 2019a) (Fig. 7L), which is also involved in the glucosylation of ABA in tea plants (Jing et al. 2020). In addition, (Z)-3-hexenol can also be converted to (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate by a still unknown acetyl-CoA (Z )-3-hexenol acetyltransferase (CHAT) (Fig. 7A) in tea plants. To determine which genes are involved in (Z)-3-hexenol acetylation, 1 CsCHAT that was remarkably upregulated after exposed to (Z)-3-hexenol was selected, and the enzymatic activity of its encoded protein was shown to promote the formation of (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate in tea plants (Fig. 7, B and C).

# Discussion

The capacity to perceive and respond to environmental cues is essential to all life on Earth (Erb 2018). Under stress, plants synthesize and release volatile compounds that act as attractants and repellents (Arimura et al. 2009; Huang et al. 2012; Jing et al. 2019a). Our previous studies revealed the role of volatiles in the communication of tea plants under cold stress (Zhao et al. 2020) and drought stress (Jin et al. 2021). Specifically, airborne eugenol can be taken up by tea plants and metabolized into glycosides, thus enhancing cold and drought tolerance of recipient tea plants (Zhao et al. 2022). Although it is clear that volatiles play a role in plant–plant interaction under each single stress, it is still unclear whether 

volatiles are involved in the crosstalk between cold and drought stress. 

Low temperatures and drought are the most critical environmental factors that limit the growth and geographic distribution of tea plants (Wang et al. 2013; Zheng et al. 2016). Different stresses may lead to the same physiological condition; for example, drought and cold stress all lead to osmotic instability, and thus, plants elicit a common response for such developments. It is therefore logical to expect plants to have crosstalk among the signaling networks of different stresses. In Arabidopsis, heptahelical protein 1 (HHP1) is a negative regulator in ABA and osmotic signaling, and the hhp1-1 mutant grown in soil showed hypersensitivity to cold stress with limited watering, suggesting that HHP1 may function in the crosstalk between cold and osmotic signaling (Chen et al. 2010). In maize, endogenous ABA activated by drought pretreatment plays an important role in acclimation of the plants to cold (Guo et al. 2021). It has also been shown that insect-induced (Z)-3-hexenol can affect the insect resistance of neighboring tea (Jing et al. 2019a). To investigate which VOCs are involved in these interactions, quantitative analysis of VOCs (Figs. 3, B to F, and S4) and exogenous volatile exposure experiments (Fig. 4) were conducted, and the results confirmed the role of (Z )-3-hexenol in the integration of cold and drought stress of tea plants. 

Under drought stress, the change in ABA concentration is one of the most drastic changes observed thus far of a plant hormone responding to an environmental stimulus (Zeevaart 1980). In this study, the expression of genes involved in plant hormone biosynthesis, including ABA, auxin, JA, and SA, was analyzed, and the expression of most of the genes involved in ABA biosynthesis was increased (Supplemental Fig. S3). For example, expression of NCED and ABA2 was upregulated when plants were exposed to cold-stressed plants (Fig. 2A). Moreover, the content of ABA and ABA-GE in tea leaves was also increased after exposure (Fig. 2, B and C), suggesting that (Z )-3-hexenol integrates cold and drought stresses, likely via ABA. The role of ABA in (Z)-3-hexenol–induced drought tolerance in tea plants was confirmed using an ABA inhibitor. In addition, sugars are an integral component of plants, serving many key biochemical and structural roles. Drought triggers 

sugar accumulation in plants and resets the source–sink relationship (Kaur et al. 2021), and it also facilitates the breakdown of storage sugars into soluble sugars (Camisón et al. 2020). 

Our previous research has found that the content of (Z)-3-hexenol in tea leaves decreased under mild drought stress (Jin et al. 2021). However, in this study, the production of (Z)-3-hexenol can be induced when tea plants are experiencing cold and drought stress simultaneously (Supplemental Fig. S5); moreover, VOC detection from tea plants in different temperatures showed that a wide temperature range can induce the production of (Z)-3-hexenol (Supplemental Fig. S6). 

Glycosylation regulates the solubility, stability, and biological activity of small molecules, some of which have been reported to influence plant abiotic and biotic stress responses (Lim and Bowles 2004; Tognetti et al. 2010; von Saint Paul et al. 2011; Liu et al. 2015; Li et al. 2017). The inducible regulation of UGTs is controlled largely by substrate levels through ligand-dependent transcription factors (Meech et al. 2019), and the upregulation of UGTs can serve to maintain the balance of intracellular glycosylation pathways. Besides, our previous research in tea plants found that most of UGTs can be induced with the increase of corresponding substrate concentration (Zhao et al. 2020; Hu et al. 2022). UGT85A53 is a multifunctional enzyme which can catalyze the glycosylation of (Z)-3-hexenol and ABA. As its substrate, the accumulated (Z)-3-hexenol can induce the expression of UGT85A53, and then, ABA was promoted 

to form ABA-GE. The upregulation of CsUGT85A53 lead to increased expression levels of ABA biosynthesis–related gene (NCED) via feedback activation of the structural genes, which is consistent with the observed that alteration of the expression of UGTs by silencing or overexpression would result in a parallel change in the expression of biosynthesis genes related to substrate formation in tea and the other plants (Yin et al. 2012; Li et al. 2017; Hu et al. 2022). 

These results suggest that UGT85A53-mediated ABA glycosylation plays a key role in (Z)-3-hexenol–induced drought tolerance of tea plants. Taken all together, our results demonstrate that cold stress–induced (Z)-3-hexenol can induce the expression of CsUGT85A53, affect ABA homeostasis, and promote ABA accumulation, ultimately promoting the drought tolerance of tea plants (Fig. 8). 

# Materials and methods

# Plant material, growth, and stress conditions

Healthy 1-yr-old tea seedlings of tea (C. sinensis var. sinensis cv. Shuchazao) were purchased from the Dechang Seedling Company and transferred into water for immediate use. Drought stress was induced by applying $1 5 \% ( w / \nu )$ polyethylene glycol (PEG)6000 according to Frank Van Breusegem (Tognetti et al. 2010). Cold stress was induced by exposing tea plants to $- 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , as described (Zhao et al. 2020). One bud and 2 leaves from C. sinensis plants were sampled for each treatment, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until further analysis. 

# Chemicals and reagents

PEG6000, (Z)-3-hexenol $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate $( \geq 9 9 \% )$ , linalool $( \geq 9 7 \% )$ ), hexyl acetate $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , SA $( \geq 9 8 \% ) .$ , JA $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , ABA $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , and FLD were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Shanghai, China). trans-linalool oxide $( \geq 9 5 \% )$ was purchased from Yuanye Bio-Technology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). 

# Plant–plant interaction experimental design

The emitter tea plants were treated at $- 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then moved to room temperature for VOC priming experiments. The cold-stressed and control emitters were placed upwind of the unstressed neighboring plants (receivers), which were kept in a 5-L glass chamber for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ , and the VOC flow rate from the emitter’s chamber to the receiver chamber was 20 mL/min. Then, the receivers were treated with 15% (w/v) PEG6000 for $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ to assess physiological responses to drought. The receivers that were exposed to cold-stressed emitter plants were designated as treatment group (T). A separate group of receiver plants were exposed to unstressed emitter plants as a control (CK). The experimental design and results are presented in Fig. 1A. At least 6 independent biological replicates were performed. 

To further confirm the function of (Z)-3-hexenol, the CsADH2-silenced tea plants were treated at $- 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ 

and set as emitters, the neighboring plants were primed in the same way as described above, and the drought tolerance of neighbors of CsADH2-silenced tea plants (T2) and neighbors of normal tea plants (CK2) was detected. 

# Relative moisture, $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ value, and osmotic potential detection

To determine leaf relative moisture content, the third leaf of at least 3 tea plants in each treatment was collected, immediately weighted (fresh weight), fixed at ${ 1 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C }$ for 20 min, and subsequently oven-dried for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (dry weight), as described (Upadhyaya et al. 2008). The maximum efficiency of photosystem II photochemistry $( F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ was measured using a pulse-modulated fluorimeter Imaging-PAM (Walz, Effeltrich, Germany) after 30 min of dark adaptation. $F _ { \mathrm { v } } / F _ { \mathrm { m } }$ was quantified using Imaging WinGegE software. The angle between the first leaf and the stem of each tea seedling was measured using ImageJ software. 

# ROS and MDA content detection

Histochemical staining of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ was performed by placing the leaves in fresh solutions of $1 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ DAB or NBT in darkness for $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , followed by decolorization in $80 \%$ absolute ethyl alcohol at $9 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ . For analysis of MDA, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 } ,$ and anti- $\cdot 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ capacity, $_ { 0 . 2 \ \& }$ of leaf samples was homogenized in $2 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ cold extraction buffer $\mathbf { \ } ^ { \prime } 0 . 1 \mathbf { \Omega } _ { M }$ phosphate buffer, pH 7.0). After centrifugation at 8,000 rpm for 10 min, the supernatants were analyzed using test kits (Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute, Nanjing, China) (Zhao et al. 2020). 

# Stomatal aperture bioassays

For scanning electron microscopy (SEM), the first leaves of all tea plants were collected and incubated in the fixative solution according to a previous method (Arnon 1949). Samples were dehydrated using graded ethanol solution in addition of isoamyl acetate to pure isoamyl acetate solution $\left( 1 ; 1 ~ \nu / \nu \right)$ . Then, samples were vacuum-dried by using HCP-2 in the presence of liquid $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } ,$ and images were taken using Hitachi Regulus 8100 (Hitachi Le). The width of the stomatal aperture was measured using ImageJ software. Three plants were used for each experiment, and 10 randomly selected apertures were examined for each leaf. 

# Soluble sugar content detection

Plant soluble sugar content Test Kit (A145-1-1), Sucrose Measurement Kit (A099-1-1), and Glucose Assay Kit (glucose oxidase method; A154-1-1) were used for soluble sugar, sucrose, and glucose content analyses, respectively. All 3 kits were purchased from Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute (Nanjing, China). $_ { 0 . 0 5 ~ \mathrm { g } }$ of leaf samples was homogenized in $0 . 4 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ extraction buffer $( 0 . 1 ~ m$ phosphate buffer, pH 7.0). After centrifugation at 8,000 rpm for $1 0 \mathrm { \ m i n }$ , the supernatants were analyzed using test kits. 

# RNA-Seq and bioinformatics analysis

Total RNA was isolated from leaves of C. sinensis var. sinensis cv. Shuchazao using RNAiso Mate for Plant Tissue (Vazyme Biotech Co., Ltd) and RNAiso Plus (Vazyme Biotech Co., ltd) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Next, cDNA was synthesized from total RNA by reverse transcription using PrimeScript RT Master Mix (Yeasen Biotech Co., Ltd, Shanghai, China). 

cDNA fragments were purified with a QIAQuick PCR Purification Kit, end-repaired, and ligated to Illumina sequencing adapters. Fragments were selected by gel electrophoresis, amplified via PCR, and paired-end sequenced with an Illumina HiSeqTM 2000 (Gene Denovo Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Guangzhou, China) instrument. 

After sequencing, the raw reads were filtered by removing low-quality reads, defined as those containing ${ > } 5 0 \%$ lowquality $( Q \leq 1 0 )$ bases, to obtain high-quality clean reads. The rRNA-removed reads were assembled de novo using Trinity (v2.1.1), and the length distribution of the assembled unigenes was obtained. Unigene expression was calculated and normalized to reads per kilobase per million reads (RPKM). To annotate the unigenes, BLASTX $( \mathsf { v } 2 . 2 . 2 9 + )$ was used to search the unigenes against the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) nonredundant protein (Nr), SwissProt, KEGG, and Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COG) databases, with an E-value threshold of 1e − 5. 

# Detection of cold-induced VOCs

To detect volatiles induced by cold stress, tea seedlings were placed in a large glass vessel (5 L) and kept at $- 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ After that, the glass vessel containing cold-stressed plants was moved to room temperature for VOC analysis, and the conditions were as follows: illumination of 10,000 lux (16 h light and $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ dark), room temperature of $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C \pm 1$ , and air humidity of $45 \%$ to $50 \%$ . A total of 1 L of gas was pumped out through a fiber using gas collection equipment for NTME (PAS Technology, Deutschland GmbH). 

GC–MS analysis was performed using a GC column (DA-5, $6 0 ~ \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m } ,$ , $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ film thickness, Folsom, USA) and the following temperature program: hold at $4 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min, ramp to $1 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ at $2 \ ^ { \circ } C / \mathsf { m i n } ,$ , hold at 1 min, increase to $2 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ at $2 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / \mathsf { m i n } _ { \ d }$ , and finally hold at 3 min. The column effluent was ionized by electron impact ionization $( 8 0 ~ \mathsf { e V } )$ , and mass scanning was conducted from 50 to $6 0 0 ~ m / z$ . Compounds were identified based on NIST and standards when possible, and quantification of compounds was based on the peak area and standard curve (Supplemental Fig. S4).

# Volatile exposure experiment

To test the effects of single volatile compounds on tea plants, (Z)-3-hexenol, (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate, linalool, trans-linalool oxide, and hexyl acetate were dissolved in DMSO. Solutions of volatiles were added to pieces of cotton, which were separately placed in glass cases (5 L) containing the tea plants for 12 h. For the pot-cultured tea seedlings, we exposed the plants 2 times (the 1st day and 7th day), during the 12-d drought stress period and each time the plants were exposed for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . After 12-d water-deficient treatment, the drought tolerance of (Z)-3-hexenol–exposed tea seedlings was measured. The content of these 5 volatiles was set based on the expected physiological concentration released by cold-stressed tea plants: $1 0 \mu \ g$ for (Z )-3-hexenol, $8 \mu \mathrm { g }$ for (Z)-3-hexenyl acetate, 5 μg for hexyl acetate, $1 . 5 \mu \mathbf { g }$ for linalool, and $0 . 4 \mu \mathrm { g }$ for linalool oxide. Neat DMSO was used as the control. The exposed tea seedlings were then treated with $1 5 \%$ (w/v) PEG6000 for physiological measurements of drought response. 

# Treatment of FLD

To investigate the role of ABA in (Z)-3-hexenol–induced drought tolerance in tea plants, tea leaves were sprayed with water or with 50 μM FLD (ABA biosynthesis inhibitor) to block the biosynthesis of ABA according to the method (Gamble and Mullet 1986). After $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of pretreatment, the tea plants were used for 3-h (Z)-3-hexenol exposure and 12-h drought stress.

The full-length CDS (coding sequences) of CsCHAT was cloned and ligated into the PGEX-4T-1 vector. The CDS sequence and gene ID are listed in Supplemental Table S2. CsCHAT protein activity was measured according to Dudareva et al. (1998) and modified as follows: $2 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 0.05 M acetyl-CoA and 5 μL of $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m } { } M$ (Z)-3-hexenol substrate were dissolved in DMSO, and 100 ng purified CHAT enzyme and $_ { 0 . 1 ~ M }$ of $M \mathbf { g } \mathsf { C l } _ { 2 }$ were dissolved in 100 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5) to bring the assay volume to $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L } .$ . The assays were carried out at $3 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min, and then $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $2 \mathsf { m } { \boldsymbol { M } }$ of $5 , 5 ^ { \prime }$ -dithio-bis-(2-nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB) was added, and the reaction was allowed to proceed at room temperature for 10 min. Absorbance values were measured at 412 nm. The enzyme activity unit (U) is defined as the amount of absorbance increase per minute. 

To detect products, the enzymatic reaction was scaled up to 1 mL final volume of the above solution. The reaction was carried out for $6 \textup { h }$ , and product was extracted with 1 mL hexane, concentrated, and injected into the GC–MS system (Dudareva et al. 1998; Pichersky et al. 2007). 

# Gene suppression in tea plants

Candidate AsODNs were selected using Soligo software (Dinç et al. 2011; Liu et al. 2018) with CsUGT85A53, CsCHAT, and CsADH as the input sequence, respectively. AsODNs were synthesized by General Biological system Co., Ltd (Anhui, China). One milliliter of 20 μM AsODN solution was injected into tea leaves for gene silencing. Tea leaves injected with sense oligonucleotides (sODN) were used as controls. After 24-h incubation, the leaves were exposed to (Z )-3-hexenol for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then subjected to drought stress for drought tolerance analysis. 

# Reverse transcription quantitative PCR analysis

Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was performed according to published protocols with gene-specific 

primers designed by Primer3 online. The glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) gene was used as an internal reference gene, and the relative expression was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta C T }$ method. Each reaction contained $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ Hieff SYBRGreen Master Mix (Yeasen Biotech Co., Ltd, Shanghai, China), 2 μL of cDNA, and 1 μL of 10 μM genespecific primers in a final volume of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . All reactions were performed on the CFX96 Real-Time System (Bio-Rad, USA) using a 2-step program: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 40 cycles at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 10 s and $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \thinspace s .$ All of primers used are listed in Supplemental Table S3. 

# Data analysis

Data were analyzed using SPSS Statistics 20.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA) and presented as mean $\pm \mathsf { S D }$ of at least triplicate biological replicates, and each measurement was carried out 3 times. Significance was determined at $P < 0 . 0 5$ by ANOVA followed by Tukey’s HSD test.
