# ABSTRACT

Salvianolic acid is the main active component of Salvia miltiorrhiza and holds significant value in the clinical treatment of myocardial ischemia and hypoxia. Previous studies have shown that salicylic acid (SA) can significantly promote the accumulation of salvianolic acid, but its molecular mechanism remains incompletely understood. To reveal the molecular mechanism by which SA regulates salvianolic acid, this study treated transgenic hairy roots overexpressing mitogen-activated protein kinase 3 (SmMAPK3) with SA and demonstrated that SmMAPK3 is essential for SA-induced salvianolic acid biosynthesis, with this function relying on the kinase activity of SmMAPK3. Through yeast two-hybrid screening, Rosmarinic Acid Synthase 1 (SmRAS1) was identified as a direct interacting protein of SmMAPK3. In vitro kinase assays showed that SmMAPK3 can catalyse phosphorylation of SmRAS1 at the Ser178 site, a process critical for salvianolic acid biosynthesis. Further studies confirmed that SA promotes the interaction between SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1, activates the kinase activity of SmMAPK3, and enhances the phosphorylation level and protein stability of SmRAS1. Additionally, SA treatment significantly promoted salvianolic acid accumulation in transgenic hairy roots coexpressing SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3. This study elucidates the mechanism by which the SA-SmMAPK3-SmRAS1 signalling module regulates salvianolic acid biosynthesis through post-translational modification, providing new theoretical targets for metabolic engineering and yield improvement of S. miltiorrhiza. 

# 1 | Introduction

Salvia miltiorrhiza is a traditional Chinese herbal medicine. Its dried roots and rhizomes are used as medicinal ingredients and serve as a main component in formulations such as Danshen Dripping Pills, Danshen Tablets, and Danhong Injection. Notably, salvianolic acids derived from S. miltiorrhiza exhibit protective effects against myocardial ischemia and hypoxia (Li et al. 2018; Xiao et al. 2020). Because of the relatively low content of phenolic acids in artificially cultivated resource plants and the long growth cycle, the production of phenolic 

acids cannot meet the rapidly growing market demand (Fang et al. 2014; Shao et al. 2025). Consequently, enhancing the salvianolic acid content in S. miltiorrhiza represents the most pressing problem facing the S. miltiorrhiza industry at present. 

Various biotechnological methods including suspension cells, elicitation treatment, transgenic plants, endophytic fungi, metabolic engi, neering, and so forth, have been utilised to improve the production of phenolic acids (Shi et al. 2019). Among them, treatment with salicylic acid (SA) as an elicitor is the main method for promoting the accumulation of salvianolic

acids (Dong et al. 2010; Guo et al. 2015). The nonexpressor of pathogenesis-related genes (NPR) protein, serving as a receptor for SA, will be activated when the plant detects the SA signal. The NPR protein then transmits the signal downstream, thus mediating the synthesis of secondary metabolites. A previous study found that SmNPR1, a positive regulator of the SA signalling pathway, interacts with the downstream transcription factor SmTGA2 of the SA signalling pathway and activates the expression of the key enzyme SmCYP98A14 for salvianolic acid biosynthesis, thereby playing a positive regulatory role in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids (Ding, Xie, et al. 2023). In addition, SmNPR4, a negative regulator of the SA signalling pathway, interacts with the transcription factor SmTGA5. Inhibition of the transcriptional activity of SmTGA5 suppresses the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids. Treatment with SA relieves the inhibitory effect of SmNPR4 on SmTGA5 (Ding, Zhang, et  al.  2023). In addition to the classical pathway of SA signalling, there are also some other transcription factors that respond to the SA signal and can regulate the accumulation of salvianolic acids. For example, SmMYB111 is induced by SA and interacts with SmTTG1 and SmbHLH51 to form the ternary transcription complex SmTTG1-SmMYB111- SmbHLH51, which is involved in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids (Li et al. 2018). 

In addition to transcriptional regulation, the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signalling cascade is crucial for the accumulation of secondary metabolites. The MAPK signalling pathway has been extensively documented in regulating the accumulation of phenylpropanoids, flavonoids, terpenoids and alkaloids in plants. Specifically, in Populus and Oryza sativa, MAPK regulates the synthesis of phenylpropanoid lignin by phosphorylating the LTF1 transcription factor (Gui et al. 2019; Zhu et al. 2023); in Arabidopsis thaliana, AtMPK3/4/6/11 promotes flavonoid biosynthesis through the phosphorylation of the Serine (Ser) at position 131 of the MYB transcription factor AtMYB75 (Li, Wang, et  al.  2016). Upon light activation, MdMPK4-06G in Malus × domestica phosphorylates the transcription factor MdMYB1, thereby enhancing the expression of anthocyanin biosynthesis genes MdCHS and MdUFGT, which results in increased anthocyanin production (Yang et  al.  2021). In Gossypium hirsutum, subgroup IIc WRKY transcription factors promote GhMKK2-mediated flavonoid biosynthesis (Wang et  al.  2021); JA activates the CsMPK6- CsMYC2 module in Citrus, thereby regulating the biosynthesis of terpenoid $\beta$ -citronellol (Yue et al. 2023). The AP2/ERF transcription factor gene cluster CrORCA3/4/5 regulates the synthesis of terpenoid indole alkaloids downstream of the safflower MAPK kinase cascade (Paul et al. 2016; Susheel Kumar Raina et al. 2012). Low temperature-induced phosphorylation of chalcone synthase FvCHS1 by FvMAPK3 enhances proteasomal degradation, leading to a decrease in anthocyanin synthesis (Mao et  al.  2022). The above results indicate that after the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) senses and is activated by exogenous signal stimuli, it will phosphorylate transcription factors and key enzymes involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, thereby regulating the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. As a crucial hub in the signal transduction pathway, mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) is involved in the signal transduction process of SA. It has been found in studies that in S. miltiorrhiza, SA can 

significantly induce the expression of the SmMAPK3 gene. When SmMAPK3 is overexpressed, it increases the expression levels of genes related to phenolic acid biosynthesis and promotes the accumulation of salvianolic acids (Xie et al. 2022). The above results indicate that SmMAPK3 may play a role in the biosynthetic pathway of salvianolic acids regulated by SA. Whether its mechanism of action is consistent with that in Citrus and strawberries, where SA activates SmMAPK3 and phosphorylates downstream targets, thereby leading to the accumulation of salvianolic acids, still remains to be further explored. 

In this study, we clarified that SmMAPK3 is essential for the SA-mediated biosynthesis of salvianolic acids. Moreover, SmMAPK3 interacts with SmRAS1 and phosphorylates it, which enhances the protein stability of SmRAS1 and promotes the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids. Treatment with SA enhances this process. This study clarifies the molecular mechanism of the SmMAPK3-SmRAS1 module in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids mediated by SA, laying a theoretical foundation for precisely promoting the synthesis of salvianolic acids through targeted regulation of key enzymes. 

# 2 | Materials and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Material and SA Treatment

S. miltiorrhiza sterile seedlings were maintained on $^ { 1 / 2 - }$ strength Murashige and Skoog (½MS) solid medium under a $1 6 \mathrm { { h } ; 8 \mathrm { { h } } }$ light–dark cycle at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and were subcultured every 2 months (Li, Guo, et al. 2016; Li et al. 2021). 

The wild-type hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza were induced and maintained in our laboratory. We took $0 . 3 \mathrm { g }$ of S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots, inoculated them aseptically into $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ Erlenmeyer flasks containing $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of 6,7-V liquid medium, and incubated them at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and protected from light, with shaking at $_ { 1 2 0 \mathrm { ~ r ~ } }$ $\mathrm { m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ , and subcultured every 21 days (Li, Guo, et  al.  2016; Li et al. 2021). 

SA was (Sigma-Aldrich) dissolved in ethanol. The hairy roots were cultured for 18 days, and then SA was added. The final concentration of SA was $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . After treatment for $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ , the samples were harvested (Ding, Zhang, et al. 2023). 

# 2.2 | Generation of Transgenic S. miltiorrhiza Hair Roots

The SmRAS1 gene was cloned into the pCsGFP-BT vector to generate the SmRAS1-GFP recombinant. For the knockout vector, we designed the sgRNA according to the principle of 5-G-19 bp-NGG and constructed the sgRNA together with Cas9 into the pHSbdcas9 vector. Additionally, SmMAPK3, constitutively active mutants CA-SmMAPK3 (D196E, G200A), and the loss-of-autophosphorylation mutant SmMAPK3K70R (K70R) were cloned into the pCAMBIA1300-myc vector to produce the SmMAPK3-myc, CA-SmMAPK3-myc, and SmMAPK3K70R-myc recombinant vectors (Liu, Yu, et al. 2022; Xie et  al.  2022; Lv et  al.  2025). SmMAPK3 was cloned into 

the pK7GWIWG2R RNAi vector to form a SmMAPK3-RNAi construct. Transgenic hairy roots and transgenic plants were obtained by using Agrobacterium ATCC15834 harbouring corresponding vectors to infect the explants of aseptic S. miltiorrhiza (Li et al. 2021). Genomic DNA extraction was performed using the CTAB method, and positive clones were identified using primers listed in Table S1. 

# 2.3 | RNA Extraction and Quantitative Reverse Transcriptional Polymerase Chain Reaction Analysis

Using S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots as the material, all total RNA extraction were performed by referring to the instructions of the AG Extraction Kit (AG, AG21022; China). The extracted total RNA served as the template for reverse transcription, following the guidelines of the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit (AG, AG11728; China), to synthesize complementary DNA (cDNA) for qRT-PCR. We conducted quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction using a Bio-RAD CFX96 real-time PCR system (CA, USA) and the Taq SYBR Green qPCR Premix Kit from AG (AG, AG11701; China) (Ding, Zhang, et al. 2023). The quantitative polymerase chain reaction primers are provided in Table S1. 

# 2.4 | HPLC Analysis of Salvianolic Acids

Hairy roots were cultured in flasks containing $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of liquid 6.7 V medium for 21 days, after which they were removed and dried in an oven at $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The contents of $2 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of dried powder were extracted in $2 \mathrm { m L }$ of a methanol–water mixture $( 7 { : } 3 , \mathrm { v } / \mathrm { v } )$ for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ in darkness. We treated them for 45 min in an ultrasonic bath and then centrifuged the mixture at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The supernatant solution was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Millipore filter and analysed by HPLC. The solvent gradient used in this study was prepared by mixing solvent A (acetonitrile) and solvent B $0 . 0 2 \%$ phosphoric acid-water, $\mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ in the following elution program: $0 { - } 8 \ \operatorname* { m i n }$ , $5 \% - 5 0 \%$ A $\mathbf { \Pi } ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ ; 8–15 min, $5 0 \% - 8 0 \%$ A $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ ; 15–16 min, $8 0 \% - 5 \%$ A (v/v); 16–20 min, $5 \%$ A $\mathbf { \Pi } ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ . The solvent flow rate was kept constant at $1 . 0 \mathrm { { m L } \mathrm { { m i n ^ { - 1 } } } }$ . Samples $( 1 0 \mu \mathrm { L } )$ were detected with a UV detector at a wavelength of $2 8 6 \mathrm { n m }$ (Ding, Zhang, et al. 2023). 

# 2.5 | Subcellular Localization Analysis

The open reading frame (ORF) of SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 was cloned into the pCsGFP-BT vector to generate the SmRAS1- GFP and SmMAPK3-GFP recombinant (primers are shown in Table  S1). The N. benthamiana leaves were infested with Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 carrying the recombinant plasmid. The pCsGFP-BT vector was utilised as a control in this experiment to determine the nuclear localization. We selected the flowering gene OsFTL1, which is localised in the cytoplasm and nucleus of rice, and fused it with the mCherry fluorescent tag (OsFTL1-mCherry) to serve as a localization marker protein (Wei et al. 2025). After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation, detection of GFP fluorescence in tobacco epidermal cells was performed using a 

confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica, Germany) (Liu, Yin, et al. 2022). 

# 2.6 | Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H)

The pGBKT7-53 and pGADT7-T were used as positive controls, whereas the pGBKT7-lam and pGADT7-T were used as negative controls. The pGBKT7-SmMAPK3 and pGADT7-SmRAS1 served as the experimental group. They were transformed into Y2H Gold, plated onto DDO (SD-Trp-Leu-His-Ade) medium, and incubated at a constant temperature of $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 to 3days (Ma et  al.  2022). After selection of monoclonal strains on DDO medium, their growth was assessed on QDO+X-α-Gal $^ +$ AbA (X-α-Gal, 5-Bromo-4-chloro-3-indoxyl $\propto$ -D-galactopyranoside; AbA, Aureobasidin A) medium. Three biological replicates were selected to minimise false positives at high concentrations. The growth status of the bacterial plaques was subsequently observed. 

# 2.7 | In Vitro Pull Down Assay

The SmRAS1-GST and SmMAPK3-His proteins were prepared as previously described (Ding, Zhang, et al. 2023). The GST-fused SmRAS1 protein was purified on GST purification resin (Beyotime, P2250; China). The His-fused SmMAPK3 protein was purified on His purification resin (Beyotime, P2221; China) and incubated for $4 \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Following three washes with the wash buffer (50 mM ${ \mathrm { N a H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { P O } } _ { 4 }$ , $3 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, 2 mM imidazole), the SmRAS1-GST purification protein was combined with SmMAPK3-His for $4 \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The proteins were then eluted with elution buffer $( 5 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { N a H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { P O } _ { 4 } ;$ 300 mM NaCl, $8 0 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole) and subjected to SDS-PAGE and immunoblotting. 

# 2.8 | Coimmunoprecipitation Assay (CoIP)

SmRAS1-GFP and SmMAPK3-myc were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana by Agrobacterium infiltration. Proteins were extracted and resuspended in immunoprecipitation (IP) buffer, supplemented with protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche, 04693132001; Switzerland), and PhosSTOPTM (Roche, 4 906 845 001; Switzerland). # 2.9 | Split NanoLuc Luciferase Complementation Assay (LCI)

The LCI assays were based on those previously described. In summary, we generated SmMAPK3/CA-SmMAPK3/ SmMAPK $3 ^ { \mathrm { K 7 0 R } }$ -nLUC and cLUC-SmRAS1 recombinant constructs, which were subsequently transformed into GV3101. # 2.10 | Bimolecular Fluorescence Complementation Assays (BiFC)

The genomic sequence of SmRAS1 and the coding sequence of SmMAPK3 were individually cloned into binary yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) BiFC vectors, resulting in the generation of SmRAS1-cYFP and SmMAPK3-nYFP constructs. Agrobacterium carrying the constructs was coinfiltrated into leaves of N. benthamiana. The plants were subsequently kept in the dark for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ prior to the detection of YFP fluorescence using a Leica confocal laser scanning microscope (DMi8, Leica, Germany) (Liu et al. 2024).

The dissociation constant $( K _ { \mathrm { d } } )$ values were calculated by averaging the triplicate F norm measurements at each concentration and fitting the data to the binding curve using the MO-Affinity Analysis software (NanoTemper Technologies GmbH, Germany).

# 2.15 | Docking Studies

The interaction of SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 was studied by protein–protein docking tools using the Z dock module in DS. In the docking process, we set an angular step size for the rotational sampling of ligand orientations to 6 degrees, generating 54 000 poses. In filtering poses, a distance cutoff of $1 2 . 0 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ was employed. No restriction was set in the receptor/ligand blocked residue or receptor/ligand binding site residue. In the clustering parameter, the RMSD cutoff was set to $1 0 \mathrm { \AA }$ , and the interface cutoff was set to $1 0 \mathrm { \AA }$ . After docking, 2000 top poses were generated and clustered with a maximum number of 100. The FFT algorithm ranked all possible binding modes between the receptor and ligands on the basis of shape, desolvation energy, and electrostatics. The screening result model showing the top 1 ZDOCK score was used to analyse the binding sites, indicating that the residues Ser178, Pro179, Arg283, and Lys284 of SmRAS1 could interact with the 

residues Glu123, Leu124, Ala334, and Asp335 of SmMAPK3 (Sudhakar et al. 2018).

# 3 | Results

# 3.1 | SmMAPK3 Is Essential for SA-Induced Biosynthesis of Salvianolic Acid

We generated multiple hairy root lines: SmMAPK3-RNAi (#10, #13, #28), SmMAPK3-OE (#1, #5, #16) (Figure 1a,b; Figures S7 and S8). Compared with the control group (WT), the levels of rosmarinic acid (RA) and salvianolic acid B (SalB) in the SmMAPK3-OE lines increased significantly. Notably, in sample #16, the levels of RA and SalB increased by 3.87 times and 3.02 times (Figure  1c). The contents of RA and SalB in the SmMAPK3-RNAi lines decreased significantly. Among them, the decreases in sample #18 were approximately $3 8 \%$ and $4 5 \%$ , respectively (Figure  1d). To investigate whether SmMAPK3 is involved in the accumulation of RA and SalB induced by SA, the hairy roots of SmMAPK3-OE5 and SmMAPK3-RNAi18 were treated with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ SA. Following exogenous SA treatment, the contents of RA and SalB in the hairy roots of SmMAPK3-OE5 were significantly upregulated (Figure  1e), whereas no significant changes were observed in the hairy roots of SmMAPK3- RNAi18 (Figure 1f). The above results indicate that SmMAPK3 is essential for SA-induced salvianolic acid biosynthesis. 

# 3.2 | The Kinase Activity of SmMAPK3 Is Critically Relied Upon for the SA-Induced Biosynthesis of Salvianolic Acids

In order to explore whether the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids induced by SA mediated by SmMAPK3 depends on its kinase activity, we obtained multiple hairy root lines, constitutively active hairy roots: CA-SmMAPK3-OE (#13, #14, and #28) (Figure  1g; Figures  S7, S8) and loss-of-autophosphorylation activity hairy roots: SmMAPK3K70R-OE (#8, #9, and #16) (Figure 1h, Figures S1, S2). The CA-SmMAPK3-OE lines also showed significant decreases in RA and SalB. Especially in sample #14, the levels of RA and SalB decreased by approximately $8 5 \%$ and $8 9 \%$ , respectively (Figure 1i). The continuous activation of CA-SmMAPK3 led to hypersensitive reactions in plants, premature senescence of hairy roots, and a substantial reduction in the content of RA and SalB. Compared with the control group, the contents of RA and SalB in the SmMAPK3K70R-OE lines showed significant decreases (Figure 1j). Treatment with SA on the transgenic materials of SmMAPK3K70R and SmMAPK3-RNAi showed no significant 

change in the content of salvianolic acids (Figure 1k), indicating that the kinase activity of SmMAPK3 is essential for SA to regulate the biosynthesis of salvianolic acid. 

# 3.3 | SmMAPK3 Interacts With SmRAS1

In order to further elucidate the mechanism by which SmMAPK3 mediates the regulation of the accumulation of RA and SalB by SA, we screened the yeast cDNA library. After completing the sequencing and analysis, 41 interacting proteins were screened out, including transcription factors such as WRKY, MYB, bHLH, AP2/ERF, and AP9/EREBP, as well as protein kinases, synthases, and so forth (Table  S2). Among them, SmRAS1, which serves as a key enzyme in the biosynthesis process of salvianolic acids (Figure S3), has attracted much attention. On the basis of previous studies, we speculated that SmMAPK3 might interact with SmRAS1 to regulate the biosynthetic pathway of salvianolic acids in a more direct manner. Subsequently, Y2H was conducted to verify this interaction. The positive controls pGBKT7-53 and pGADT7-T, along with SmMAPK3-pGBKT7 and SmRAS1-pGADT7, were able to grow on $\mathrm { Q D O + X - } \alpha \cdot$ - gal $^ +$ AbA, whereas the negative controls pGBKT7-lam and pGADT7-T failed to grow under these conditions, confirming the interaction between SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 (Figure 2a). 

In vitro pull-down assay results revealed that SmMAPK3-His fused protein was captured by SmRAS1-GST fused protein but not by GST protein (Figure 2b). The results of the CoIP experiment indicated that the SmMAPK3-myc protein could specifically capture the SmRAS1-GFP protein, but not the GFP protein (Figure  2c). LCI analysis demonstrated a significant fluorescence signal was generated between SmMAPK3-nluc and SmRAS1-cluc; however, no analogous signal was observed between cluc and nluc, SmRAS1-cluc and nluc, or cluc and SmMAPK3-nluc (Figure 2d). BiFC analysis further confirmed the interaction between SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 in the cytoplasm and nucleus (Figure 2e), consistent with the subcellular localization of SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 (Figure S4). Microscale thermophoresis (MST) analysis revealed that the dissociation constant $( K d )$ for the binding of SmRAS1 to SmMAPK3 was $0 . 1 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Figure  2f). In conclusion, the results indicate that SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 have direct interactions both In vitro and In vivo. 

# 3.4 | SmMAPK3 Phosphorylates SmRAS1 at Serine 178

Since the SA-mediated biosynthesis of salvianolic acids is reliant on the kinase activity of SmMAPK3, we consider that SmRAS1 serves as a substrate for SmMAPK3. To validate this proposal, In vitro kinase experiments will be conducted. The results showed that neither SmMAPK3-His nor SmMAPK3K70R-His exhibited autophosphorylation activity, whereas CA-SmMAPK3-His demonstrated robust autophosphorylation activity, as evidenced by band migration. Furthermore, CA-SmMAPK3-His significantly increased the phosphorylation level of SmRAS1-GST In vitro (Figure 3a). To verify the autophosphorylation of SmMAPK3 in N. benthamiana, we transiently expressed SmMAPK3-myc, CA-SmMAPK3-myc, and 

SmMAPK3K70R-myc in N. benthamiana. Phos-tag acrylamide gel electrophoresis analysis showed that there was a band migration in the CA-SmMAPK3-myc lane, indicating a phosphorylation phenomenon (Figure 3b). In addition, in N. benthamiana, CA-SmMAPK3-myc increased the phosphorylation level of 

SmRAS1-GFP (Figure  3c). Treatment with λPPase confirmed that the retarded migration of SmRAS1-GFP in the Phos-tag acrylamide gel was due to its phosphorylation (Figure 3d). In summary, these findings demonstrate that SmMAPK3 is capable of phosphorylating SmRAS1 both In vitro and In Vivo.

In order to explore the phosphorylation site of SmRAS1 phosphorylated by SmMAPK3, molecular docking prediction was carried out. SmMAPK3 interacts with A (Arg283) and K (Lys284) in the D site (RAKLRL) of SmRAS1 through A (Ala334) and D (Asp335) in the CD domain (LHDVADEPVC). In addition, Glu (E123) and Leu (L124) of SmMAPK3 interact with Ser (S178) and Pro (P179) of SmRAS1 (Figure  3e). The S178 site of SmRAS1 is consistent with the phosphorylation site predicted by NetPhos-3.1 (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/ services/NetPhos) and is confirmed by previous literature (Table S3). This indicates that S178 of SmRAS1 may serve as a phosphorylation site for SmMAPK3 (Figure 3e). Site-directed mutagenesis was performed on SmRAS1S178 (mutating S to A to mimic the non-phosphorylated state and mutating S to D to mimic the continuously phosphorylated state). The phosphorylation status of SmRAS1-GST, SmRAS1S178D-GST, and SmRAS1S178A-GST by CA-SmMAPK3-His was detected using Phos-tag polyacrylamide. The results showed that SmRAS1- GST and SmRAS1S178D-GST were phosphorylated, whereas SmRAS1S178A-GST was not (Figure  3f). The above results demonstrate that Serine at position 178 of SmRAS1 is the phosphorylation site of SmMAPK3. 

# 3.5 | Phosphorylation of S178 Promotes the Accumulation of Salvianolic Acids

The hairy roots of SmRAS1 gene overexpression (SmRAS1-OE) (#1, #2, #10) (Figure 4a), knockout (SmRAS1-Cas9) (#1, #4, #9) (Figure  4b), overexpressing hairy roots of SmRAS1S178D(#2, #5, and #9) (Figure  4c) and $S m R A S I ^ { \mathrm { S 1 7 8 A } }$ (#3, #10, and #11) (Figure  4d) lines in S. miltiorrhiza were successfully generated (Figures  S7, S9). Compared with the control (RA: $7 . 8 7 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW; SalB: $9 . 1 3 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ DW), a significant increase in RA and SalB content was detected in the SmRAS1-OE plants, particularly in line #1, where RA increased by approximately 1.35 times and SalB by 1.52 times (Figure 4e). A significant reduction in RA and SalB content was observed in the SmRAS1-Cas9 lines, with decreases of approximately $9 6 \%$ for both compounds (Figure 4f). Compared with the control, $S m R A S I ^ { \mathrm { S 1 7 8 D } }$ promoted the accumulation of salvianolic acids in the hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza, particularly in line #2, where RA increased by 1.84 times and SalB by 2.88 times (Figure 4g). Compared with the SmRAS1-OE1, RA increased by 1.18 times and SalB by 1.37 times. Compared with the control, SmRAS1S178A had no significant effect on the accumulation of salvianolic acids in the hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza (Figure 4h). The above results indicate that SmMAPK3 phosphorylates SmRAS1 on Ser178 residues to promote the accumulation of salvianolic acids. 

# 3.6 | The Stability of the Phosphorylated SmRAS1 Protein Is Enhanced

The experimental results indicate that SA facilitates the phosphorylation of SmRAS1. To investigate whether this phosphorylation influences protein stability, we conducted a cell-free assay. Specifically, SmRAS1-GFP and SmRAS1-GFP/CA-SmMAPK3- myc proteins were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana. Total protein extracts were prepared and incubated with ATP at designated time points (0, 10, 20, 30, 60, and 120 min). The levels of SmRAS1 were detected using an anti-GFP antibody. The results indicated that SA, MG132, and CA-SmMAPK3-myc significantly enhanced the stability of both SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 proteins (Figure  5a,b). To further explore this phenomenon, we stably expressed SmRAS1-GFP-OE2, SmMAPK3-myc-OE5, SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE1 (SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1- GFP-OE1), and SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE8 (SmMAPK3- myc/SmRAS1-GFP-8) in the hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza. Proteins were extracted after SA treatment for 0, 10, 20, 30, 60, and $1 2 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ . The expression level of SmMAPK3-myc in the transgenic hairy roots was evaluated using an anti-MYC antibody, and the protein level of SmRAS1-GFP was assessed using an anti-GFP antibody. The results showed that SA treatment enhanced the stability of the SmRAS1-GFP protein in SmMAPK3- OE/SmRAS1-OE8 (Figure 5c–e). The above results demonstrate that the phosphorylation of SmRAS1 enhances the stability of the protein.

# 3.8 | SA Promotes the Accumulation of Salvianolic Acids in the Double-Transgenic Hairy Roots of SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1

The SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 mutants were coexpressed in the hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza, and the double transgenic hairy root lines of SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE, SmMAPK3K70Rmyc/SmRAS1-OE, and SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE were obtained (Figures  S7, S10). A coexpression line with a relatively high expression level of SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3 was obtained, including SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE (#5, #8, and #13) (Figure  7a,b) and SmMAPK3K70R-myc/SmRAS1-OE (#3, #6, and #7) (Figure 7c,d). A coexpression line with a relatively high expression level of SmRAS1 and a relatively low expression level of SmMAPK3 was obtained, including SmMAPK3- RNAi/SmRAS1-OE (#1, #7, #8) (Figure 7e,f). Compared to the control (WT) (the content of RA is $7 . 8 7 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ , the content of SalB is $9 . 1 3 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } } ,$ ), the average content of RA in the SmMAPK3- OE/SmRAS1-OE (#5, #8, and #13) lines is $2 3 . 3 0 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ , and the average content of SalB is $2 4 . 4 5 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . The contents of both RA and SalB are significantly upregulated, with an approximate 2.96-fold increase in the content of RA, and an increase in the content of SalB close to 2.79-fold. These values are higher than those in the lines with SmRAS1-OE. In the SmRAS1-OE lines, the average content of RA is $1 1 . 2 9 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ and the content of SalB is $1 8 . 4 6 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . In the SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE lines, the increase in the content of RA is close to 2.06-fold compared to the SmRAS1-OE lines, and the increase in the content of SalB is close to 1.43-fold (Figure  7g). In the SmMAPK3K70R-OE/SmRAS1-OE lines, the average content of RA remains $1 5 . 4 4 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . The average content of SalB remains $9 . 8 8 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . Compared to the WT, the increase in RA is close to 1.96-fold, and the increase in SalB is close to 1.08-fold. The decrease in RA is close to 1.36-fold compared to the SmRAS1-OE, and the decrease in SalB is close to $4 6 \%$ (Figure 7h). In the SmMAPK3- RNAi/SmRAS1-OE lines, the average content of RA remains $1 0 . 9 3 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . The average content of SalB remains $7 . 8 7 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ . The decrease in RA is close to $3 8 \%$ compared to the WT, and the decrease in SalB is close to $1 4 \%$ . The decrease in RA is close to $3 \%$ compared to the SmRAS1-OE, and the decrease 

in SalB is close to $5 7 \%$ (Figure  7i). When the SmMAPK3- OE/SmRAS1-OE8, SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE1, and SmMAPK3K70R-myc/SmRAS1-OE3 were treated with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ SA, the contents of SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE8 RA and SalB were up-regulated to varying degrees. However, the changes in the contents of RA and SalB in SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE-1 and SmMAPK3K70R-myc/SmRAS1-OE-3 were not significant (Figure 7j–l). 

These results indicate that the SA treatment promotes the accumulation of RA and SalB in the hairy roots with the coexpression of SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1. This suggests that the SA-SmMAPK3-SmRAS1 module has a superimposed effect in regulating the synthesis of salvianolic acids in S. miltiorrhiza. 

# 4 | Discussion

SA is a crucial defence hormone in plants. When invaded by pathogens, SA accumulates and triggers systemic acquired resistance (SAR) (Janda et al. 2020; Malamy et al. 1990; Rivas-San Vicente and Plasencia 2011; Rong et al. 2016). In addition, SA plays a vital role in regulating the synthesis of secondary metabolites. Previous studies have shown that SA can promote the accumulation of salvianic acids in the hairy roots of S. miltiorrhiza (Dong et al. 2010; Guo et al. 2014). However, the molecular mechanism by which SA induces the biosynthesis of salvianic acids has not been fully elucidated. Early research mainly focused on transcriptional regulation. SA can activate transcription factors, thereby regulating the expression of genes involved in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids and ultimately regulating the accumulation of secondary metabolites. Among these transcriptional regulations, a key target in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids, SmRAS1, functions as the first step that directs the phenylpropanoid pathway and the tyrosine pathway towards the synthesis of salvianic acids (Dong et al. 2010; Guo et al. 2015; Di et al. 2013; Fu et al. 2020; Zhou et al. 2024, 2021). Gene knockout of SmRAS1 showed that the content of salvianolic acids decreased sharply (Figure  5b), indicating that SmRAS1 is a key enzyme in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids in S. miltiorrhiza

Clarifying the molecular mechanism by which SA regulates the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids with SmRAS1 as the target, on the one hand, can not only supplement the research on the molecular mechanism by which SA regulates the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids but also expand the functions of SA in the regulation of other phenolic compounds. On the other hand, it can also be used as a target for genetic improvement to increase the accumulation of secondary metabolites in S. miltiorrhiza, providing some ideas for solving the problems in the S. miltiorrhiza industry. 

In this study, we elucidated the crucial role of SmMAPK3 in the SA-induced biosynthesis of salvianic acids in S. miltiorrhiza (Figure 1). Through Y2H screening, we discovered an interaction between SmRAS1 and SmMAPK3. Members of the MAPK family play important roles in plant immunity, plant growth regulation, and the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites (Chen et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2021). Numerous studies have shown that MAPK regulates the synthesis of secondary metabolites by phosphorylating key enzymes in metabolic pathways or transcription factors such as the WRKY, MYB and bHLH families (Mao et al. 2022; Yang et al. 2021; Yue et al. 2023; Zhu et al. 2023). This study demonstrated that SmMAPK3 acts as a positive regulator of the biosynthesis of salvianic acids, and the promoting effect of SA on the biosynthesis of salvianic acids is dependent on the kinase activity of SmMAPK3 (Figure 1). To elucidate the molecular mechanism by which the SmMAPK3-SmRAS1 module mediates the regulation of SA-induced salvianic acid biosynthesis, In  vitro and In  Vivo phosphorylation experiments confirmed that SmMAPK3 phosphorylates SmRAS1, and this process is induced by SA (Figures  2–5). Phosphoproteomics provides valuable insights into physiological processes mediated by phosphorylation and the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. This approach has been widely studied in species such as rice and A. thaliana, identifying many proteins involved in tissue development, signal transduction and stress responses (He et al. 2024; Li et al. 2024; Qu et al. 2023; Shi et al. 2022). In S. miltiorrhiza, phosphoproteomics has been applied to study drought stress (Zhang et al. 2022). To further explore the potential targets of SmMAPK3 in the regulation of salvianic acid biosynthesis, we conducted phosphoproteomics analysis using the empty vector and the SmMAPK3 gene RNAi lines. 

Phosphorylation usually affects the stability of substrate proteins and their interactions with other proteins, DNA and RNA (Bigeard and Hirt  2018; Han et  al.  2020; Wei et  al.  2023). In this study, we constructed a constitutively active mutant (CA-SmMAPK3) and a mutant that lost its autophosphorylation activity (SmMAPK3K70R) to investigate their interactions with 

SmRAS1 using the LCI assay. The interaction between CA-SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 was enhanced, whereas the interaction between SmMAPK3K70R and SmRAS1 was weakened. These results indicate that the phosphorylation state of SmRAS1 affects its interaction with SmMAPK3. To determine whether SA has an effect on the interaction between SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1, treatment with exogenous SA promoted the interaction between SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1. To elucidate whether this phenomenon is due to the enhanced kinase activity of SmMAPK3 induced by SA, which in turn phosphorylates SmRAS1, we demonstrated in the hairy roots of N. benthamiana and S. miltiorrhiza that SA enhanced the phosphorylation of SmRAS1 by SmMAPK3. This phosphorylation event enhanced the stability of the SmRAS1 protein and inhibited the ubiquitination and degradation of SmRAS1. Under normal conditions, SmMAPK3 is capable of partially phosphorylating SmRAS1, which in turn enhances the stability of the SmRAS1 protein. Meanwhile, a specific portion of SmRAS1 undergoes degradation through the process of ubiquitination. Consequently, delving into the question of which occurs first, ubiquitination or phosphorylation, and determining the ratio between the phosphorylated and ubiquitinated proteins represents a highly intriguing area of inquiry (Figure 8). 

To investigate the effect of SA-induced phosphorylation of SmRAS1 via SmMAPK3 on the accumulation of secondary metabolites, we constructed double-transgenic hairy roots overexpressing SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 (SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE), as well as hairy roots with SmMAPK3 RNA interference and SmRAS1 overexpressed (SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE). Subsequently, the contents of secondary metabolites were analysed. Compared with the individual SmMAPK3-OE or SmRAS1-OE lines, the contents of RA and SalB in the SmMAPK3- OE/SmRAS1-OE line were significantly increased. In contrast, compared with the WT, there were no significant changes in the levels of RA and SalB in the SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE line, indicating that SmMAPK3 plays a crucial role in the biosynthesis of salvianolic acids. When treated with exogenous SA, the accumulation of RA and SalB in the SmMAPK3-OE/SmRAS1-OE line increased significantly, whereas no significant changes were observed after SA treatment in the SmMAPK3-RNAi/SmRAS1-OE line. These results indicate that SmMAPK3 enhances the accumulation of RA and SalB by phosphorylating SmRAS1. In this study, we also created transgenic materials overexpressing the constitutively activated SmMAPK3 (CA-SmMAPK3-OE) and those co-overexpressing CA-SmMAPK3 and SmRAS1 (CA-SmMAPK3- OE/SmRAS1-OE) (Figure  S7). However, the continuous activation of MAPK may trigger the hypersensitive response (HR) in plants, which limits further experiments on these constructs (Cui et al. 2015; Hirt et al. 2013; Wang et al. 2023).
