# SUMMARY

L-lysine catabolic routes in plants include the saccharopine pathway to $\alpha$ -aminoadipate and decarboxylation of lysine to cadaverine. The current review will cover a third L-lysine metabolic pathway having a major role in plant systemic acquired resistance (SAR) to pathogen infection that was recently discovered in Arabidopsis thaliana. In this pathway, the aminotransferase AGD2-like defense response protein (ALD1) $\alpha$ -transaminates L-lysine and generates cyclic dehydropipecolic (DP) intermediates that are subsequently reduced to pipecolic acid (Pip) by the reductase SAR-deficient 4 (SARD4). L-pipecolic acid, which occurs ubiquitously in the plant kingdom, is further N-hydroxylated to the systemic acquired resistance (SAR)-activating metabolite N-hydroxypypecolic acid (NHP) by flavin-dependent monooxygenase1 (FMO1). N-hydroxypypecolic acid induces the expression of a set of major plant immune genes to enhance defense readiness, amplifies resistance responses, acts synergistically with the defense hormone salicylic acid, promotes the hypersensitive cell death response and primes plants for effective immune mobilization in cases of future pathogen challenge. This pathogen-inducible NHP biosynthetic pathway is activated at the transcriptional level and involves feedback amplification. Apart from FMO1, some cytochrome P450 monooxygenases involved in secondary metabolism catalyze N-hydroxylation reactions in plants. In specific taxa, pipecolic acid might also serve as a precursor in the biosynthesis of specialized natural products, leading to C-hydroxylated and otherwise modified piperidine derivatives, including indolizidine alkaloids. Finally, we show that NHP is glycosylated in Arabidopsis to form a hexose-conjugate, and then discuss open questions in Pip/NHP-related research.

Keywords: lysine metabolism, pipecolic acid, $N$ -hydroxypypecolic acid, NHP, systemic acquired resistance, saccharopine pathway, flavin-containing monooxygenase, $N$ -oxygenation, $N$ -hydroxylation, Arabidopsis thaliana.

# INTRODUCTION

Amino acids are common precursors for the biosynthesis of plant natural products. The basic amino acid lysine, which is synthesized in plants from aspartate by a multistep plastidial reaction sequence (Jander and Joshi, 2009; Zeier, 2013), can enter several metabolic routes. In some plant species, including members of the Fabaceae, Poaceae or Solanaceae families, lysine decarboxylase catalyzes the conversion of lysine to the diamine cadaverine (Tomar

et al., 2013; Jancewicz et al., 2016). As part of the specialized metabolism in these species, cadaverine might subsequently be converted to piperidine, quinolizidine or lycopodium alkaloids (Figure 1) (Bunsupa et al., 2017). A lysine catabolic pathway ubiquitously present in plants, and also occurring in animals, is the saccharopine pathway, by which L-lysine is converted to the non-protein dicarboxylic amino acid $\alpha$ -aminoadipic acid (Aad). Subsequent

metabolism of Aad can lead to the generation of acetyl-CoA and glutamate (Galili, 2002). The present review will focus on the biochemistry and function of another common plant L-lysine catabolic pathway that has been biochemically elucidated in the last couple of years, primarily but not exclusively in Arabidopsis thaliana (Arabidopsis). This pathway is activated in plants upon microbial pathogen attack and has a central role in plant immunity. It comprises the two-step conversion of L-lysine to the cyclic non-protein amino acid pipecolic acid (Pip) and a subsequent, flavin-containing monooxygenase-catalyzed $N$ -hydroxylation reaction that results in the biosynthesis of $N$ -hydroxypyvecolic acid (NHP), a recently discovered defense-regulatory metabolite that mediates plant systemic acquired resistance (SAR) to pathogen infection (Figure 1; Návarova et al., 2012; Bernsdorff et al., 2016; Ding et al., 2016; Hartmann et al., 2017, 2018; Chen et al., 2018). In addition, we will give summary comparisons of hitherto characterized $N$ -oxygenation reactions in plants and further aspects of Pip-related metabolism (Figure 2).

# Bullet point summary (box)

- The pathogen-inducible biosynthesis of pipecolic acid from lysine in plants consists of a two-step biochemical conversion via the 'KAC pathway': an ALD1-catalyzed $\alpha$ -transamination of L-lysine to DP intermediates followed by a reduction step generating L-Pip, in which SARD4 acts as a major reductase.   
- The $N$ -hydroxylation of Pip in response to pathogen attack in plants is catalyzed by the clade I flavin-containing monooxygenase FMO1 and results in the systemic accumulation of $N$ -hydroxypyvecolic acid in the foliage, which induces SAR and thereby protects plants from subsequent infections.   
- The SAR-inducing capacity of NHP is exerted by the induction of a whole set of immune-regulatory genes to enhance defense readiness, by the amplification of defense responses such as accumulation of SA and defense-gene expression, by the promotion of HR cell death and by enhancing the competence of plants to react rapidly and effectively towards pathogen infection (defense priming).   
- The NHP biosynthetic pathway is promoted by feedback amplification and regulated by the EDS1/PAD4 node as well as by transcription factors of the TGA family and SARD1/CBP60g.   
- Other $N$ -hydroxylated compounds from plants are known to be generated by cytochrome P450 monooxygenases, including $\alpha-N$ -hydroxylated L-tyrosine and benzoxazinoids as protective secondary metabolites from Poaceae.

# THE OCCURRENCE OF PIPECOLIC ACID IN PLANTS

The first records describing the occurrence of Pip in plants date back to the early 1950s. Unidentified non-protein amino acids with the subsequently confirmed characteristics of Pip were detected after two-dimensional paper chromatography of potato tuber extracts and other plant sources (Dent et al., 1947; Steward and Thompson, 1950). In the following years, Pip was unequivocally identified as a natural product in leaves of Trifolium repens (white clover), in the pods of Phaseolus vulgaris (green bean), in young Malus domestica (apple) fruits and in several other plant species (Hulme and Arthington, 1952; Zacharius et al., 1952, 1954; Morrison, 1953; Broquist, 1991). Evidence for the presence of a pathway for biosynthesis of Pip in plants has since been presented for a rapidly growing list of monocot and dicot species, including the model crucifer Arabidopsis (Návarova et al., 2012) and economically and nutritionally important crops such as potato (Solanum tuberosum; Zacharius et al., 1954; Pálfi and Dészi, 1968), soybean (Glycine max; Aliferis et al., 2014), barley (Hordeum vulgare; Møller, 1974), maize (Zea mays; Kiyota et al., 2015), rice (Oryza sativa; Pálfi and Dészi, 1968; Jung et al., 2016), and wheat (Triticum sp.; Garcia-Seco et al., 2017). Significantly, many studies showed that Pip strongly accumulates in response to phytopathogens in the leaves of different plants, for example in rice infected with the blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae, in virus-infected tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum), potato and soybean (Pálfi and Dészi, 1968; Ádám et al., 2018), in Arabidopsis and tobacco inoculated with the bacterium Pseudomonas syringae (Návarova et al., 2012; Vogel-Adghough et al., 2013) and in soybean infected with the fungi Rhizoctonia solani and Fusarium virguliforme (Aliferis et al., 2014; Abeysekara et al., 2016).

# Catabolism of L-lysine to Pip, and conversion of Pip to N-hydroxypyvecolic acid

The biochemical origin of Pip has been a central aspect of various studies, and early evidence of its metabolic provenance from lysine was provided by Rothstein and Miller (1954). By administering various isotope-labeled compounds in rats, such as L-lysine-6- ${}^{14}\mathrm{C}$ , it was shown that the resulting radiolabeled Pip is a major product of L-lysine catabolism in animals. At about the same time, incorporation of the radioactivity of L-lysine-6- ${}^{14}\mathrm{C}$ into Pip in developing green bean (P. vulgaris) was observed, establishing that in plants Pip is also metabolically derived from L-lysine (Grobbelaar and Steward, 1953).

Earlier studies proposed that the biosynthesis of Pip from L-lysine involves a sequence of transamination, cyclization and reduction steps (Weissman and Schoenheimer, 1941; Schütte and Seelig, 1967; Gupta and Spenser, 1969). In principle, as L-lysine possesses amino groups at both the

# Step 1 of Pip biosynthesis: ALD1-mediated $\alpha$ -transamination of L-lysine to dehydropipecolic acid (the 'KAC pathway')

Pipecolic acid accumulates in Arabidopsis in response to leaf infection by the bacterial pathogen $P$ syringae; this enabled the identification of the aminotransferase required for the supposed transamination step in the biosynthesis of

Pip (Návaróva et al., 2012). In Arabidopsis, 44 genes are annotated to code for aminotransferases (Liepman and Olsen, 2004). Among them is Arabidopsis aberrant growth and death2 (AGD2) which catalyzes the final transamination step in L-lysine biosynthesis – the conversion from L-tetrahydrodipicolinate to L-diaminopimelate (Hudson et al., 2006). The closest Arabidopsis homolog of AGD2, AGD2-like defense response protein1 (ALD1), exhibits $62\%$ protein sequence identity to AGD2 (Song et al., 2004a), and expression of the ALD1 gene is strongly systemically induced in the plant foliage in response to pathogen attack (Song et al., 2004b). Aminotransferase activity assays indicated that ALD1 protein preferentially accepts L-lysine as amino acid substrate in vitro (Song et al., 2004a). Later, Návaróva et al. (2012) demonstrated that ald1 knockout plants are unable to generate Pip upon bacterial infection, indicating that ALD1 is a key Pip biosynthetic gene. This strongly suggested that ALD1 catalyzes the required transamination step in the biosynthesis of L-lysine-derived Pip (Zeier, 2013).

The exact enzymatic function of Arabidopsis ALD1 was recently elucidated in detail by two parallel studies through biochemical characterization of heterologously expressed ALD1 proteins and in planta analyses (Ding et al., 2016; Hartmann et al., 2017). Comparative gas chromatography (GC)-mass spectrometry (MS)-based metabolite analyses of extracts from $P.$ syringae-inoculated leaves of wild-type and ald1 knockout plants identified the ALD1-dependent generation of a dehydropipecolic acid (DP) isomer in plants that was also produced in vitro by purified, recombinant ALD1 in the presence of L-lysine as the amino acid substrate, an acceptor oxoacid and pyridoxal phosphate as a cofactor (Hartmann et al., 2017). Combined mass spectrometric and Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopic analyses indicated that the in planta detectable product of the ALD1-catalyzed reaction is the enamine 2,3-dehydropipecolic acid (2,3-DP) (Figure 1), which moderately accumulated upon bacterial treatment in the pathogen-inoculated and distant leaves. Use of isotope-labeled $\varepsilon$ - $^{15}$ N-L-lysine and $\alpha$ - $^{15}$ N-L-lysine variants as substrates in both the in planta and the in vitro studies showed that the $\alpha$ - $\mathrm{NH}_2$ group of L-lysine is transferred to acceptor oxoacids such as pyruvate and that the $\varepsilon$ - $\mathrm{NH}_2$ group is retained in the DP product (Hartmann et al., 2017). In-cell biochemical assays by Ding and colleagues with Escherichia coli cultures expressing Arabidopsis ALD1 protein also identified the function of ALD1 as L-lysine- $\alpha$ -aminotransferase. Using liquid chromatography (LC)-MS/MS-based analysis, this work proposed the ketimine 1,2-DP as the reaction product (Ding et al., 2016), although mass spectral analysis alone might not allow the 1,2- and 2,3-DP isomers to be distinguished. However, ketimine/enamine tautomer pairs such as 1,2-DP and 2,3-DP can exist in a reversible chemical equilibrium (Nardini et al., 1988). Together, the studies of Ding et al. (2016) and Hartmann et al. (2017) demonstrate that the first

enzymatic step in the biosynthesis of Pip from L-lysine in Arabidopsis involves the ALD1-catalyzed transfer of the $\alpha$ - $\mathrm{NH}_2$ group of L-lysine to acceptor oxoacids. The resulting ketoacid KAC is not detected in plant extracts, presumably because of spontaneous dehydrative cyclization to the cyclic ketimine 1,2-DP, which can be in chemical equilibrium with the enaminic 2,3-DP tautomer (Figure 1). In Arabidopsis biosynthesis of Pip therefore proceeds via the KAC route but not via the AAS pathway. More recently, biochemical analyses of ALD1 from the fir clubmoss Hyperzia serrata showed that Pip is also produced in lycopods via the KAC pathway and the corresponding cyclic DP intermediates (Xu et al., 2018). This might imply a conserved mechanism of Pip biosynthesis in vascular plants.

Although L-lysine is the preferred amino acid substrate of ALD1, the aminotransferase exhibits significant in vitro transaminase activity to several other amino acids (Song et al., 2004a). For instance, purified ALD1 protein is able to transaminate L-methionine, L-leucine, L-ornithine or diaminopimelate to corresponding ketoacids or derived cyclized products in vitro (Hartmann et al., 2017). However, none of the transamination products obtained in vitro, except 2,3-DP, were detected in planta, indicating that the in vivo function of ALD1 is restricted to the conversion of L-lysine to DP products associated with Pip biosynthesis (Hartmann et al., 2017). Following the ALD1-catalyzed transamination reaction, a reduction step is required to convert DP intermediates into Pip (Gupta and Spenser, 1969; Zeier, 2013). The intrinsically Pip-deficient ald1 mutant is able to accumulate Pip provided that 2,3-DP is exogenously supplied to plants concomitantly with bacterial infection. Thus, ald1 possesses pathogen-inducible activity to reduce DP intermediates to Pip (Hartmann et al., 2017).

# Step 2 of Pip biosynthesis: reduction of DP intermediates to Pip

In mammals, the protein $\mu$ -crystallin (CRYM) catalyzes the reduction of the imine bond of 1,2-DP and structurally similar ketimins by using NADH or NADPH as reduction equivalents (Hallen et al., 2011). The Arabidopsis gene with closest similarity to CRYM was annotated as ornithine cyclodeaminase/ $\mu$ -crystallin (ORNCD1) and later renamed SARD4, because a forward genetic screen for SAR-attenuated mutants identified, among others, plants with mutated sard4 alleles (Sharma et al., 2013; Zeier, 2013; Ding et al., 2016). The sequences of both CRYM and SARD4 possess a predicted Rossman NAD-binding domain (Sharma et al., 2013). As ALD1, the SARD4 gene is systemically induced in the foliage of Arabidopsis upon bacterial attack (Hartmann et al., 2017). Coupled in vitro assays showed that combinations of purified ALD1 and SARD4 proteins or ALD1 and CRYM were able to convert L-lysine, in the presence of pyruvate, pyridoxal phosphate and NAD(P)H, to Pip, whereas SARD4 or CRYM alone were not (Hartmann et al., 2017).

Similarly, when Arabidopsis ALD1 and SARD4 were simultaneously expressed in $E.$ coli, in-cell activity assays indicated that Pip was generated from endogenous lysine (Ding et al., 2016). Moreover, $P.$ syringae-treated sard4 knockout plants showed significantly attenuated levels of Pip in inoculated and distal leaves, while 2,3-DP was over-accumulated (Ding et al., 2016; Hartmann et al., 2017). Thus, SARD4 functions as a dehydropicolic acid reductase and catalyzes the NAD(P)H-dependent reduction of ALD1-generated DP intermediates to Pip (Figure 1). However, sard4 knockout plants still accumulate considerable amounts of Pip at later stages of bacterial infection, suggesting the presence of further DP reductase activities beyond SARD4 in Arabidopsis (Hartmann et al., 2017). Combining in vitro assays with the ALD1 and SARD4 homologs from $H.$ serrata with use of deuterium-labeled L-lysine indicated that SARD4 reduces the ketiminic C=N bond of 1,2-DP rather than the C=C bond of the 2,3-DP enamine (Xu et al., 2018). This is in line with the reported reduction of ketimine substrates by mammalian CRYM (Hallen et al., 2015). SARD4-mediated reductive consumption of 1,2-DP might shift the ketimine/enamine equilibrium towards the ketimine form and thus constantly reestablish reducible 1,2-DP from the chemically more stable enamine 2,3-DP. The detour over 2,3-DP could minimize hydrolytic ring opening of ketiminic 1,2-DP to KAC which would dismantle the piperidine ring structure and thus adversely affect Pip formation.

Coupled in vitro assays also showed that concerted action of ALD1 and SARD4 can convert L-ornithine via the five-membered DP homologue pyrroline-2-carboxylate (Pyr2C) into proline. However, it is unlikely that a pathogen-inducible L-ornithine to proline conversion via ALD1 and SARD4 is relevant in planta, because Pyr2C was not detected in Arabidopsis extracts and proline levels did not increase upon bacterial infection (Hartmann et al., 2017). The biological function of ALD1 and SARD4 therefore relies on the pathogen-triggered, two-step enzymatic conversion of L-lysine to Pip (Figure 1). Bioassays in the context of the induction of flowering in Lemna and SAR in Arabidopsis (see below) demonstrate that the L- but not the D-enantiomer of Pip is bioactive in plants (Fujioka et al., 1987; Návaróva et al., 2012), strongly suggesting that plants biosynthesize L-Pip. Subcellular localization studies in Arabidopsis or rice with ALD1 and SARD4 proteins fused to reporter sequences indicate that biosynthesis of L-Pip proceeds in plastids (Sharma et al., 2013; Cecchini et al., 2015; Jung et al., 2016), in which the precursor amino acid L-lysine is also synthesized (Mazelis et al., 1976).

# Flavin monooxygenase-catalyzed conversion of Pip to $N$ -hydroxypyvecolic acid

A recent study demonstrated that L-Pip is further metabolized in Arabidopsis by flavin-dependent-monooxygenase1 (FMO1) (Hartmann et al., 2018). FMO1 belongs to the flavin-

containing monooxygenase (FMO) gene family which consists of 29 members in Arabidopsis (Olszak et al., 2006; Schlaich, 2007). Flavin-containing monooxygenases are present in all kingdoms of life and possess a tightly bound flavin-adenine dinucleotide (FAD) prosthetic group. They commonly use NAD(P)H and $\mathrm{O}_2$ to monooxygenate or otherwise oxidize lower-molecular-weight, mostly heteroatom-containing substrates (Rossner et al., 2017). As for ALD1 and SARD4, the expression of FMO1 is strongly induced systemically in the plant foliage in response to pathogens (Mishina and Zeier, 2006). Using comparative GC-MS analyses, Hartmann et al. (2018) spotted a metabolite that accumulated in the pathogen-inoculated wild type but was absent in ald1 or fmo1 mutant plants. Mass spectrometric and infrared (IR) spectroscopic analyses identified the compound as NHP, a previously undescribed plant metabolite. Purified recombinant FMO1 protein catalyzed the conversion of L-Pip to NHP in the presence of FAD and NADH as cofactors. Feeding experiments in Arabidopsis with deuterated $\mathrm{D}_9$ -Pip resulted in the generation of $\mathrm{D}_9$ -labeled NHP, confirming that NHP is directly derived from Pip in plants. Similar experiments with isotope-labeled L-lysine variants showed the in planta derivation of NHP from L-lysine and the involvement of the ALD1-mediated L-lysine $\alpha$ -transamination step in the biochemical sequence leading to NHP biosynthesis (Hartmann et al., 2018). The function of FMO1 as an NHP synthase was confirmed in a very recent independent study. When FMO1 was transiently expressed in Nicotiana benthamiana and plants were concomitantly supplied with exogenous Pip, the formation of NHP was observed. Moreover, FMO1 variants with mutations in predicted FAD- or NADPH-binding domains did not exhibit NHP synthase activity (Chen et al., 2018). Taken together, the biosynthesis of NHP from L-lysine consists of three enzymatic steps: ALD1-mediated $\alpha$ -transamination of L-lysine to DP intermediates via the KAC pathway, a reduction step to L-Pip that includes SARD4, and $N$ -hydroxylation of L-Pip to NHP by FMO1 (Figure 1).

# N-OXYGENATION REACTIONS IN PLANTS AND OTHER ORGANISMS

Phylogenetic analyses based on protein sequences have divided plant FMOs into three clades (Olszak et al., 2006; Schlaich, 2007; Yoshimoto et al., 2015). Arabidopsis FMO1 falls into clade I; as described above it monooxygenates the N-H group in the piperidine ring of Pip to generate the $N$ -hydroxylated amino acid NHP (Figure 2a). By contrast, hitherto characterized proteins of clades II and III catalyze oxidative decarboxylation of indole-3-pyruvate to indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) and S-oxygenation reactions in distinct plant natural product biosynthetic pathways, respectively (Hansen et al., 2007; Li et al., 2008; Mashiguchi et al., 2011; Stepanova et al., 2011; Dai et al., 2013; Yoshimoto et al., 2015).

Besides NHP, a limited number of other $N$ -hydroxylated compounds have been described as plant natural products.

$N$ -hydroxytyrosine, which is formed by cytochrome P450 monooxygenase-mediated $N$ -hydroxylation of L-Tyr in Sorghum bicolor (great millet) as the first intermediate in the biosynthesis of the cyanogenic glycoside dhurrin, was the first $\alpha-N$ -hydroxyamino acid detected in plants (Møller and Conn, 1979; Halkier et al., 1991). The CYP79A1-catalyzed reaction involves a subsequent second $N$ -hydroxylation followed by dehydration and decarboxylation steps to the final reaction product $p$ -hydroxyphenylacetaldoxime (Figure 2b). Amino acid-derived oximes contain hydroxylated $\mathfrak{sp}^2$ -hybridized nitrogens and have been identified from tyrosine, isoleucine, leucine, methionine and tryptophan (Sørensen et al., 2018). They are common intermediates in the biosyntheses of cyanogenic glucosides and glucosinolates, which are classes of plant secondary metabolites with important roles in the protection of plants against herbivorous insects and microbial phytopathogens (Gleadow and Møller, 2014; Burow and Halkier, 2017). In Arabidopsis, CYP79B isoforms catalyze the conversion of tryptophan to indole-3-acetaldoxime, an intermediate in the biosyntheses of indolic glucosinolates, IAA and the phytoalexin camalexin (Glawischnig et al., 2004; Sugawara et al., 2009). The CYP79-mediated formation of oximes in these biosynthetic reactions might generally involve the above-described $N$ , $N$ -dihydroxylation mechanism (Sørensen et al., 2018).

Further, several members of the Poaceae family, including the cereal crops maize, wheat and rye, as well as a few species from four dicot families synthesize indole-derived $N$ -hydroxylated benzoxazinoids such as 2,4-dihydroxy-1,4-benzoxazin-3-one (DIBOA) and 2,4-dihydroxy-7-methoxy-1,4-benzoxazin-3-one (DIMBOA) (Figure 2c). They act as defensive phytochemicals with allelopathic, antimicrobial and antiherbivore properties and are stored as inactive glycosides in the vacuole. The benzoxazinoid biosynthetic pathway has been fully elucidated in maize. Here also, a cytochrome P450 monooxygenase - in this case from the CYP71C subfamily - rather than a FMO introduces the $N$ -hydroxyl group into the benzoxazine ring (Bailey and Larson, 1991; Frey et al., 1997).

The FMO1-mediated oxidation of the secondary amino group of Pip in NHP biosynthesis is reminiscent of the reaction catalyzed by fungal and bacterial $N$ -hydroxylating flavoprotein monooxygenases (NMOs) (Olucha and Lamb, 2011). The NMOs mediate $\mathrm{O}_{2}^{-}$ and NADPH-dependent hydroxylation of primary amino groups in the side chains of L-ornithine or L-lysine, or monooxygenate amino groups from aliphatic diamines to produce primary hydroxylamines (Binda et al., 2015). For example, ornithine hydroxylase from Pseudomonas aeruginosa catalyzes the formation of $\mathsf{N}^5$ -hydroxy-ornithine (Figure 2d), which is subsequently formylated and incorporated into the iron-chelating siderophore pyoverdin (Meneely et al., 2009). Some FMOs also convert substrates with tertiary amino groups into the corresponding $N$ -oxides. Humans possess

five FMOs that participate in the oxidative metabolism of a wide range of xenobiotics (Krueger and Williams, 2005). For example, human FMO3 (hFMO3) $N$ - or $S$ -oxygenates different drugs, which often results in compound detoxification or bioactivation. An important substrate of hFMO3 is trimethylamine (TMA) produced by gut microbes from dietary components (Fennema et al., 2016). In the liver, TMA is oxygenated by hFMO3 to TMA $N$ -oxide (Figure 2e). Genetic variants of hFMO3 with compromised enzyme activity give rise to the inherited disorder trimethylaminuria ('fish-odor syndrome'), because of inefficient conversion of odorous TMA to the odor-free $N$ -oxide (Dolphin et al., 1997). Moreover, larvae of the monophagous cinnabar moth Tyria jacobaeae use the flavin monooxygenase senecionine $N$ -oxygenase (SNO) to oxygenate the pro-toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloid senecionine, derived from their host plant tansy ragwort (Senecio jacobaea), to the non-toxic $N$ -oxide (Figure 2f). This biochemical detoxification mechanism not only allows $T$ . jacobaeae to feed on alkaloid-rich S. jacobaea plants but also to sequester and accumulate the plant-derived pyrrolizidine alkaloids in the hemolymph for its own chemical defense against predatory attack (Naumann et al., 2002). The $N$ -oxide is also the storage form of senecionine in plants, indicating the existence of an endogenous $N$ -oxygenating enzyme in S. jacobaea.

# THE N-HYDROXYPIECOLIC ACID BIOSYNTHETIC PATHWAY IS CRITICAL FOR SAR

Plants are equipped with a multilayered defense system with which they counteract microbial pathogen attack (Thordal-Christensen, 2003). This includes a basal immune program that is activated upon recognition of pathogen- or damage-associated molecular patterns. To cause disease, however, virulent phytopathogens can overcome constitutive defense layers of their host plants and weaken inducible basal defense mechanisms (Boller and Felix, 2009). Systemic acquired resistance is a central trait of the immune repertoire of plants. It is triggered by microbial inoculation of one or a few leaves and confers resistance within the whole foliage to subsequent attack by otherwise virulent pathogens (Shah and Zeier, 2013). Both plant basal immunity and SAR are regulated by signal-active metabolites (Berens et al., 2017; Adam et al., 2018; Shine et al., 2018). Salicylic acid (SA) is a well-documented defense metabolite required for the effective induction of plant immunity. It is biosynthesized after pathogen inoculation from the shikimate pathway intermediate chorismate by isochorismate synthase, and signals immune responses via the transcriptional co-regulator and SA receptor non-expresser of PR genes1 (NPR1) (Wildermuth et al., 2001; Mou et al., 2003; Wu et al., 2012).

More than a decade before their biochemical roles were elucidated, a strict requirement of intact ALD1 and FMO1 genes for SAR was demonstrated. Ald1 and fmo1 knockout

mutants were unable to systemically enhance pathogen resistance after bacterial inoculation, and both mutants failed to activate responses such as SA accumulation and pathogenesis-related (PR) gene expression in the distal – but not in the inoculated – tissue (Song et al., 2004b; Mishina and Zeier, 2006). The central function of both genes for SAR was later confirmed by numerous independent studies (e.g. Jung et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2011; Chaturvedi et al., 2012; Návaróva et al., 2012), and by an unbiased screen for SAR-defective Arabidopsis mutants (Jing et al., 2011). Návaróva et al. (2012) then provided evidence for the essential function of ALD1 in Pip biosynthesis and showed that physiological doses of exogenously applied Pip override the defect of the Pip-deficient ald1 mutant in acquired resistance. This demonstrated that the accumulation of Pip in plants is necessary for SAR. Moreover, exogenous L-Pip but not the D-enantiomer triggered SAR-like resistance in Arabidopsis, supporting the notion that plants biosynthesize L-Pip in response to pathogen inoculation and that the L-form is active in biological SAR (Návaróva et al., 2012). Resistance assays with naive Arabidopsis ald1 and fmo1 plants also indicate that the Pip/NHP pathway contributes to local resistance against incompatible and compatible isolates of P. syringae and the oomycete Hyaloperonospora arabidopsidis (Hpa), which causes downy mildew (Song et al., 2004b; Bartsch et al., 2006; Koch et al., 2006; Hartmann et al., 2018).

Notably, Pip was unable to induce SAR in the fmo1 mutant, indicating that FMO1 possesses a critical role in inducible resistance downstream of Pip accumulation (Návarova et al., 2012; Bernsdorff et al., 2016). Consistent with the critical function of FMO1 in biological SAR (Mishina and Zeier, 2006), overexpression of FMO1 in Arabidopsis enhanced resistance of plants to infection by $P.$ syringae and by the oomycete Hpa (Bartsch et al., 2006; Koch et al., 2006). The observation that mutations in the second Pip biosynthetic gene, SARD4, compromise the enhanced resistance phenotype of FMO1-overexpressing plants further suggested that FMO1 requires the accumulation of Pip for its immune function, corroborating the proposed action of FMO1 downstream of Pip (Ding et al., 2016). The subsequent characterization of FMO1 as an NHP-synthesizing pipecolate N-hydroxylase directly demonstrated the interrelation between Pip and FMO1 at the biochemical level (Chen et al., 2018; Hartmann et al., 2018). Alongside Pip and SA, NHP accumulated in both locally inoculated and in distal systemic leaves upon $P.$ syringae treatment in dependence of ALD1, SARD4 and FMO1. In contrast to exogenously applied Pip, which complemented the immune defects of ald1 but not those of fmo1, exogenous NHP was able to override the defects of both ald1 and fmo1 in acquired resistance to $P.$ syringae and Hpa infection (Hartmann et al., 2018). This demonstrated that Pip-derived NHP is the actual metabolic

regulator of SAR within the Pip/NHP resistance pathway (Figure 1).

So far, SAR-like effects of exogenous NHP and Pip in Arabidopsis have been observed against the hemibiotrophic bacterium $P_{\cdot}$ syringae and the biotrophic oomycete Hpa (Navarova et al., 2012; Hartmann et al., 2018). Further, the application of Pip also induced resistance of tobacco plants to infection by $P_{\cdot}$ syringae and tobacco mosaic virus (Vogel-Adghough et al., 2013; Adam et al., 2018). In addition, overexpression of a rice homolog of Arabidopsis ALD1 in transgenic rice plants increased immunity to the rice blast fungus M. oryzae (Jung et al., 2016). Therefore, the Pip/NHP resistance pathway is conserved between species belonging to very different angiosperm orders and, typical for its involvement in SAR, is active against (hemi) biotrophic pathogens with inherently different modes of plant infection (Sticher et al., 1997).

# HOW DOES NHP EXERT ITS RESISTANCE-ENHANCING ACTIVITY?

In Arabidopsis, the SAR state induced biologically upon $P$ . syringae inoculation is associated with the enhanced systemic expression of more than 3000 genes ( $\mathrm{SAR}^+$ genes), and concomitantly about the same number of genes are downregulated ( $\mathrm{SAR}^-$ genes). $\mathrm{SAR}^+$ genes comprise pathogenesis-related genes and genes associated with elicitor recognition, defense signaling and genes encoding stress-related (WRKY and NAC) transcription factors, whereas $\mathrm{SAR}^-$ genes include many photosynthesis- and growth-related genes (Bernsdorff et al., 2016). Thus, plants with biologically activated SAR exhibit increased competence and readiness for pathogen defense at the cost of photosynthetic capacity and growth. The ald1 and fmo1 mutants lack this transcriptional SAR response (Gruner et al., 2013; Bernsdorff et al., 2016), indicating that accumulation of NHP is required for systemic transcriptional reprogramming in SAR. Interestingly, treatment with Pip is sufficient to enhance the transcription of about 700 genes which almost completely fall into the $\mathrm{SAR}^+$ category, whereas gene downregulation by Pip is modest. Similar to plants exhibiting biologically induced SAR, plants treated with Pip show increased expression of a whole battery of immuneregulatory genes. The transcriptional response to Pip fully depended on FMO1, suggesting that NHP induces the immune-related genes upregulated by exogenous Pip (Hartmann et al., 2018). Consistently, exogenous treatment with NHP triggered enhanced transcription of defense genes in leaves (Chen et al., 2018). Therefore, a first important function of NHP is to activate immune-associated gene expression, and thus to increase the defense readiness of plants.

We know that SAR-activated plants respond more rapidly and effectively to pathogen attack than naïve, unprepared plants (Jung et al., 2009; Návaróva et al., 2012). This competence is commonly designated as

defense priming (Conrath et al., 2015). Primed responses in SAR-induced Arabidopsis include defense-related gene expression, SA biosynthesis and accumulation of the phytoalexin camalexin (Návarova et al., 2012). Notably, the systemic activation of a primed state by locally inoculating plants with $P.$ syringae depended on both ALD1 and FMO1. Further, exogenous Pip promotes Arabidopsis into a primed state that conditions plants for similar responses than biologically induced SAR. Priming of SA accumulation was also observed in tobacco pre-treated with Pip (Vogel-Adghough et al., 2013). Priming in the Arabidopsis wild type and in ald1 is induced by Pip, but not in fmo1 (Návarova et al., 2012; Bernsdorff et al., 2016). In addition, the treatment of Arabidopsis plants with azelaic acid, a dicarboxylic acid linked with defense priming, induces resistance to bacterial infection in dependence on ALD1 and FMO1 (Jung et al., 2009). These findings indicate that a second important function of NHP is to prime plants for effective defense activation in response to pathogen or elicitor stimuli. Future experiments investigating the direct effect of exogenous NHP on the activation of priming in plants would further corroborate this function.

Elevated leaf SA levels following pathogen inoculation or supply of exogenous SA induce expression of a battery of defense-related genes and confer increased resistance to plants against infection by hemibiotrophic and biotrophic pathogens (Delaney et al., 1995; Nawrath and Métraux, 1999; Thibaud-Nissen et al., 2006). The SA-induced expression of the marker gene pathogenesis-related gene1 (PR1) was strongly potentiated when wild-type Arabidopsis was pre-treated with Pip, and this potentiation was absent in fmo1 (Bernsdorff et al., 2016). Induction of resistance by exogenous Pip and NHP was also significantly lower in sid2 plants defective in SA biosynthesis than in wild-type plants (Hartmann et al., 2018). This suggests that a third important function of NHP is to amplify SA-inducible resistance responses. Thus, a positive interplay of the NHP and SA pathways towards resistance induction against (hemi)biotrophic pathogen infection seems to exist, which is reminiscent of the synergism of jasmonate and ethylene signaling in plant defense against necrotrophs (Memelink, 2009). However, the NHP pathway also bears an SA-independent branch because an NHP- and Pip-inducible resistance effect is observable in SA-deficient plants, both in exogenous feeding and biological SAR assays (Bernsdorff et al., 2016; Hartmann et al., 2018). The partly independent character of NHP- and SA-signaling also exists in basal immunity, since the SA- and Pip/NHP-deficient sid2ald1 double mutant shows a more compromised basal resistance to bacterial infection than each of the respective single mutants (Bernsdorff et al., 2016).

The hypersensitive response (HR) is a genetically controlled program that culminates in the death of plant cells attacked by microbial pathogens (Coll et al., 2011). It is

thought that HR-associated cell death prevents pathogen invasion in incompatible plant-pathogen interactions and therefore promotes disease resistance. Infection of Arabidopsis leaves with compatible isolates of the downy mildew pathogen $Hpa$ is associated with the entry of penetration hyphae between junctions of leaf epidermal cells and widespread invasive growth of intercellular hyphae in leaf mesophyll tissue (Slusarenko and Schlaich, 2003). Pre-treatment of plants with NHP protects Arabidopsis against invasion by otherwise compatible $Hpa$ and promotes the development of localized HR cell death lesions on inoculated leaves, presumably at sites of attempted penetration. Further, leaves of NHP-treated plants respond to infrequently occurring oomycete entry events with a strong trailing necrotic reaction that halts intercellular hyphal growth at very early stages (Hartmann et al., 2018). In addition, incompatible $Hpa$ isolates eliciting HR lesions in resistant wild-type Arabidopsis fail to do so in $fmo1$ and establish invasive growth in that mutant (Bartsch et al., 2006). Moreover, NHP pre-treatment of Arabidopsis accelerated HR cell death triggered by the inoculation of leaves with the incompatible bacterial strain $P$ . syringae pv. tomato expressing the avrRpt2 avirulence gene (Chen et al., 2018). Together, these findings suggest that a fourth function of NHP is to promote plant cell death at sites of pathogen inoculation. This function of NHP is consistent with the finding that cell death-inducing stimuli such as avirulent pathogen inoculation, treatment with the fungal toxin fumonisin B1 or superoxide generation trigger FMO1 expression (Olszak et al., 2006).

# REGULATION OF THE NHP BIOSYNTHETIC PATHWAY AND DOWNSSTREAM SIGNALING

The NHP biosynthetic pathway is activated in pathogeninoculated and in distant leaves, which is reflected by the systemic upregulation of the ALD1, SARD4 and FMO1 biosynthetic genes and by the induced systemic accumulation of 2,3-DP, Pip and NHP (Figure 1) (Mishina and Zeier, 2006; Návarova et al., 2012; Hartmann et al., 2017, 2018). The basal levels of the metabolites are either low (Pip) or hardly detectable (2,3-DP, NHP). Further, the levels of the precursor amino acid lysine increase in response to pathogens (Návarova et al., 2012). Although the mechanisms of pathway regulation are not yet sufficiently understood, some interesting information exists. First, Pip induces expression of ALD1, SARD4 and FMO1 in an FMO1-dependent manner (Hartmann et al., 2018). This indicates that the end product NHP exerts positive feedback on the pathway, which might be the basis of the signal amplification mechanisms required for the establishment of SAR (Shah and Zeier, 2013). Consistently, direct application of NHP proved sufficient to trigger enhanced expression of the three NHP pathway genes (Chen et al., 2018). Second, the accumulation of Pip and NHP is strongly promoted by the

interacting defense signaling proteins enhanced disease susceptibility1 (EDS1) and phytoalexin-deficient4 (PAD4) (Hartmann et al., 2018). These proteins constitute the main control units of plant basal immunity and regulate expression of a battery of defense-related genes (Feys et al., 2001; Wang et al., 2008). Consistent with a function of PAD4 and EDS1 upstream of NHP production, previous studies have shown that they promote the expression of the biosynthetic genes ALD1 and FMO1 (Song et al., 2004b; Bartsch et al., 2006). Both PAD4 and EDS1 are necessary for an effective SAR response and positively affect expression of the SA biosynthesis gene isochorismate synthase 1 (ICS1) and SA accumulation (Zhou et al., 1998; Mishina and Zeier, 2006; Jing et al., 2011; Rietz et al., 2011; Breitenbach et al., 2014). Conversely, elevated levels of Pip trigger the expression of ICS1, PAD4, EDS1 and SAG101 (Hartmann et al., 2018), the latter encoding for another EDS1-interacting immune component (Wagner et al., 2013). Moreover, PAD4 is necessary for Pip-inducible resistance to bacterial infection (Návaróva et al., 2012). This corroborates the assumption that a defense amplification loop, which is put in motion when Pip and NHP levels begin to rise, is involved in the induction of SAR.

Transcription factors involved in the expression of NHP biosynthetic genes have also been identified. SAR-deficient1 (SARD1) and calmodulin-binding protein 60g (CBP60g) are members of the plant-specific transcription factor family ACBP60 that activate ICS1 transcription and in this way SA biosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2011). Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) sequencing revealed that SARD1 and CBP60g not only target the ICS1 promoter but also the promoters of several other defense genes including ALD1, FMO1, EDS1 and PAD4. In addition, a sard1 cbp60g double mutant exhibits attenuated expression of ALD1 and FMO1 in response to bacterial inoculation and reduced Pip accumulation at early infection stages (Sun et al., 2015, 2018). Further, the basic leucine zipper transcription factors TGACG-binding factor 1 (TGA1) and TGA4 promote the expression of SARD1 and CBP60g, and analyses by ChIP-polymerase chain reaction indicated that TGA1 binds to the promoter region of SARD1. A tga1tga4 double mutant also accumulated reduced levels of Pip during early phases of bacterial infection (Sun et al., 2018). Thus, concerted action of TGA1/4 and SARD1/CBP60g appears to contribute to the regulation of the NHP biosynthetic pathway. In line with these findings, sequential waves of transcription factor activities were proposed to drive the induction of plant immunity (Moore et al., 2011). It is conceivable that WRKY factors, which are prominently expressed during SAR and are inducible by exogenous Pip (Bernsdorff et al., 2016; Hartmann et al., 2018), also contribute to such transcriptional waves in NHP biosynthesis.

Interestingly, NHP was strongly overproduced in the leaves of sid2 plants after bacterial inoculation, resulting in

10-fold higher NHP levels in the SA-deficient mutant than in the wild type at later infection stages (Hartmann et al., 2018). This indicates that while SA and NHP cooperate in the induction of disease resistance (see above), the pathogen-induced biosynthesis of SA modulates the accumulation of an excess of NHP (Figure 1). Further, the SA and Pip/NHP signaling pathways appear to converge at NPR1, because $npr1$ mutant plants are compromised in both SA and Pip-triggered resistance responses (Delaney et al., 1995; Navarova et al., 2012; Bernsdorff et al., 2016). In the course of SAR induction, cellular redox changes induce an oligomer-to-monomer transition of NPR1 protein which is initially localized in the cytosol (Mou et al., 2003). Moreover, SA-independent signaling processes activate expression of the serine/threonine kinase SNF1-related protein kinase 2.8, which phosphorylates NPR1 (Lee et al., 2015). Both events are required for the translocation of NPR1 into the nucleus, where it interacts in an SA-dependent manner with transcription factors of the TGA family to induce PR gene expression and resistance (Després et al., 2003). In doing so, NPR1 physically binds SA through a coordinative copper complex in which conserved cysteine residues are involved. A structural feature proposed as necessary for ligand binding to NPR1 is a hydroxyl group in the ortho position to a free carboxylate group on the aromatic ring of SA (Wu et al., 2012). NHP is not aromatic but it contains OH- and COOH-functionalities in a similar constitutional arrangement as SA (Figure 1). Whether NHP can bind to NPR1 and/or a functional relationship between NPR1 monomerization and NHP accumulation exist remains to be determined. Another interesting parallel of SA and Pip/NHP downstream signaling relates to Arabidopsis mildew resistance locus O2 (MLO2). MLO2 is required for biologically induced SAR and proper resistance induction by exogenous SA and Pip, but not for SAR-related metabolite accumulation or defense gene expression. This indicates that MLO2 functions at a very downstream position of both SA and Pip/NHP signaling during SAR (Gruner et al., 2018).

# OTHER ASPECTS OF PIP METABOLISM

In the first step of the saccharopine pathway to Aad biosynthesis, L-lysine is converted into AAS by the bifunctional enzyme lysine-ketoglutarate reductase/saccharopine dehydrogenase (LKR/SDH) (Galili, 2002; Zeier, 2013; Kiyota et al., 2015). As the spontaneous cyclization product of AAS, 1,6-DP could theoretically be converted to Pip via reduction of its double bond (Figure 1). An enzyme capable of reducing 1,6-DP to Pip under in vitro conditions, pyrroline-5-carboxylate reductase (P5CR), was described for E. coli (Fujii et al., 2002). Orthologous P5CR enzymes have been identified in mammals and in several plant species, including Arabidopsis (Struys and Jakobs, 2010; Giberti et al., 2014). Expression of the Arabidopsis LKR/SDH gene is induced by the plant stress hormone abscisic acid

(Stepansky et al., 2005), and the enzymatic activity of LKR/ SDH rises under osmotic stress conditions in rapeseed leaves concomitantly with the levels of Pip (Moulin et al., 2006). This indicates the existence of a metabolic branch from the saccharopine pathway towards Pip biosynthesis induced by osmotic stress (Figure 1), consistent with a role for Pip as an osmoprotectant in certain bacteria (Neshich et al., 2013). The same metabolic branch was also proposed to occur in the developing endosperm of maize seed (Kiyota et al., 2015). However, direct genetic evidence for such an alternative, osmotic stress-induced route for Pip biosynthesis is still lacking. For the pathogen-inducible biosynthesis of Pip, a route via LKR/SDH can be ruled out, because $Ikr/sdh$ mutants accumulated Pip to similar levels as the wild type in response to $P_{\cdot}$ syringae inoculation and, conversely, ald1 mutants were completely defective in Pip accumulation (Návaróva et al., 2012).

In animals, Pip is catabolized through its conversion to 1,6-DP by peroxisomal pipecolate oxidase (PIPOX), which also functions as sarcosine oxidase (SOX) (Reuber et al., 1997). The closest Arabidopsis SOX/PIPOX homolog, AtSOX, is able to convert Pip to 1,6-DP in vitro (Goyer et al., 2004). Suppression of the expression of AtSOX by RNA interference (RNAi) resulted in a significant accumulation of Pip as well as in strongly reduced levels of Aad (Goyer et al., 2004). This suggests that in physiological stress scenarios, such as pathogen attack, the metabolic flux from L-lysine towards Pip might be modulated at the level of AtSOX (Figure 1). Consistently, exogenous application of Pip led to elevated levels of Aad in Arabidopsis (Návarova et al., 2012). Moreover, microarray data indicate a downregulation of AtSOX in response to P. syringae inoculation, which might contribute to the massive accumulation of Pip under SAR-inducing conditions (Zeier, 2013). Just like its five-membered ring analog proline, Pip protected mammalian cells against oxidative stress (Krishnan et al., 2008). The protective effect of proline is mediated by the enzyme proline dehydrogenase (PRODH), which oxidizes proline to $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylate in mitochondria (Natarajan et al., 2012), and new studies indicate that PIPOX might play a similar role in the mechanism by which Pip enhances cell survival (Natarajan et al., 2017). Whether this mechanism is relevant in plants, however, has yet to be determined.

As part of specialized metabolism in plants, Pip can serve as a biosynthetic precursor for different derivatives. In some species of the Fabaceae, Moraceae or Apocynaceae families several hydroxylated or otherwise substituted Pip derivatives exist as plant secondary metabolites (Figure 2g) (e.g. Schenk and Schutte, 1963; Kristensen et al., 1974; Hatanaka and Kaneko, 1977; Romeo et al., 1983; Kite et al., 2015). For example, trans-4-hydroxypyvecolic acid was detected in Acacia species, and radiolabeling experiments suggest that the compound is derived

from the direct hydroxylation of Pip (Fowden, 1960; Kunii et al., 1996). While the biochemical bases of $C$ -hydroxylation reactions within the piperidine ring of Pip in plants are not clear yet, fungal 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases with regio- and stereoselective L-Pip 4-hydroxylase activity have been characterized (Hibi et al., 2016). As another $N$ -substituted Pip derivative besides NHP, pipecolic acid betaine (homostachydrine) has been described from Medicago and Citrus species (Figure 2h) (Wood et al., 1991; Servillo et al., 2012). The biosynthesis of this compound is thought to occur via a putative $N$ -methylated Pip intermediate and might be catalyzed by the same set of $N$ -methyltransferases responsible for the biosynthesis of the better characterized five-ring homologues, $N$ -methyl-proline (hygric acid) and proline betaine (stachydrine), both of which are suggested to function in osmoregulation and ion homeostasis in response to stress situations (Nolte et al., 1997). Evidence for the natural occurrence of $N$ -methylpipecolic acid in plants has been provided by Sargenti et al. (1993) from various organs of Erythrochiton brasiliensis. It is also assumed that Pip is the biosynthetic precursor of indolizidine alkaloids that are formed in species of some plant families such as the Fabaceae, Asclepiadaceae, Convolvulaceae, Moraceae and Orchidaceae (Figure 2i) (Bunsupa et al., 2017).

# POTENTIAL INVOLVEMENT OF THE NHP PATHWAY IN OTHER BIOLOGICAL PROCESSES

Oviposition of butterfly eggs on leaves triggers defense responses in Arabidopsis similar to those observed upon bacterial inoculation (Little et al., 2007). Egg deposition on leaves also induced Pip accumulation and systemic resistance dependent on both ALD1 and FMO1 (Hilfiker et al., 2014), indicating that NHP functions as an inducer for this insect egg-triggered systemic plant response. Marked accumulation of Pip also occurred in aphid-infested soybean and switchgrass (Panicum virgatum) leaves, pointing to a possible role for the Pip/NHP pathway in plant-aphid interactions (Klein et al., 2015; Donze-Reiner et al., 2017). In addition, roots of the model legume Lotus japonicus infected with the symbiotic rhizobium Mesorhizobium loti exhibited enhanced expression of the L. japonicus ALD1 (LjALD1) gene, and silencing of LjALD1 by RNAi resulted in a reduction of the size of developing nodules (Chen et al., 2014), consistent with a potential influence of Pip on nodule formation. The Pip/NHP pathway might also affect plant developmental processes. Earlier studies from Fujioka and colleagues strongly suggested a role for Pip in flower induction in duckweed species (Lemna spp.). # FINAL THOUGHTS AND OUTLOOK

The three enzymatic steps leading to the pathogen-inducible formation of the SAR regulator NHP from lysine in Arabidopsis consist of L-lysine- $\alpha$ -transamination by ALD1, reduction of the arising DP products by SARD4 and further reductase activities to L-Pip, and $N$ -hydroxylation of L-Pip to NHP by FMO1 (Figure 1). Is NHP further metabolized in plants? The immune regulator SA can be glycosylated to both SA- $\beta$ -glucoside (SAG) and SA-glucoseester (SGE), methylated to volatile methyl salicylate, hydroxylated to 2,3- and 2,5-dihydroxybenzoic acid, and conjugated to aspartate (Chen et al., 2003; Dean and Delaney, 2008; Attaran et al., 2009; Bartsch et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2013, 2017). Glycosylation of SA is thought to inactivate signal-active free SA and generate the storage forms SAG and SGE (Dean and Delaney, 2008). In fact, GCMS analyses in our laboratory have identified an NHP-hexose conjugate in extracts from P. syringae-inoculated plants that accumulated in leaves depending on ALD1, SARD4 and FMO1 (Figure 3a). Mass spectral fragmentation patterns suggest a possible NHP-hexose ester as the substance, but also cannot rule out a $\beta$ -glucosidic hexose linkage (Figure 3b,d-f). Thus, further spectroscopic information is required for an exact structural assignment. Moreover, a LC-MS analysis in the recent work of Chen et al. (2018) also identified the FMO1-dependent accumulation of a molecular species with mass spectral properties consistent with a not yet fully elucidated NHP-hexose structure. Isotope-labeling studies from our laboratory clearly demonstrate the biosynthetic derivation of the NHP-hexose conjugate from L-lysine and L-Pip (Figure 3c). Elucidation of the exact chemical nature of the NHP-hexose conjugate, the genetics and the enzymology behind its generation and the biological function of the conjugate will be intriguing future tasks.

Although the SAR-regulating function of NHP is well established, it is still ambiguous whether NHP pathway metabolites contribute to long-distance communication

between pathogen-inoculated and distant leaves during SAR. Some observations support the notion that Pip might move from infection sites to distant leaves after pathogen attack. For example, among more than 20 different amino acids analyzed in petiole exudates collected from Arabidopsis leaves, Pip was the only one that was found enriched in the exudates in the course of $P$ syringae infection (Návaróva et al., 2012). Additionally, Pip was detected in the phloem sap of cucumber (Hu et al., 2016), and its levels significantly increased in the xylem sap of soybean plants infected with the fungus $F$ virguliforme (Abeysekara et al., 2016), indicating its potential to move through the plant vasculature. Also, NHP has been directly implicated in long-distance transport, because infiltration of lower rosette leaves of Arabidopsis wild type or fmo1 with NHP was sufficient to induce resistance responses in the upper leaves (Chen et al., 2018). However, pressure infiltration might flush the leaf apoplast with unnaturally high concentrations of inducer and not adequately reflect the spatial situation in biological SAR. Future approaches in which the endogenous production of NHP pathway metabolites are activated in a spatially defined manner might clarify their involvement in long-distance transport in SAR.

While the site of Pip biosynthesis in the plastid is established (Sharma et al., 2013; Cecchini et al., 2015; Jung et al., 2016), the subcellular localization of the $N$ -hydroxylating enzyme FMO1 is unclear. Localization studies on the Arabidopsis clade III $\mathrm{FMO}_{\mathrm{GS - OX1}}$ involved in glucosinolate S-oxygenation suggest a cytosolic localization for this protein (Li et al., 2010). Therefore, subcellular aspects of NHP biosynthesis and the characterization of transport proteins involved in the movement of Pip, and possibly NHP, over membranes are interesting issues to address in future studies. Moreover, further information about the regulation of the NHP biosynthetic pathway, and particularly about the downstream mechanisms underlying the resistance-enhancing action of NHP, which might involve receptor-ligand

interactions, is required. This also concerns the molecular basis of the positive interplay between SA and NHP in resistance induction.

# Open questions (box)

- What is the exact chemical nature, biochemistry of generation and biological function of the described NHP-hexose conjugate, and are there additional NHP-derived metabolites?   
- Do Pip and (or) NHP ensure informational long-distance transfer from inoculated to distant leaves during SAR, and if yes, what are the underlying physiological principles?   
- How is NHP biosynthesis organized at the subcellular level and how do pathway metabolites cross biological membranes?   
- What are the downstream principles of NHP signal transduction and does a specific NHP receptor exist?   
- What is the molecular basis of the synergistic interrelation between SA and NHP and is there additional interplay between NHP and other hormone signaling pathways?
