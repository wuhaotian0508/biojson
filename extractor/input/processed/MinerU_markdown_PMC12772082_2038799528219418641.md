# Synthetic bacterium-facilitated colonization of nitrogen-fxing bacteria for remodeling the rhizosphere microbiome and improving plant yield

# Abstract

Background Nitrogen-fxing bacteria (NFBs) play a critical role in biological nitrogen fxation for supplying essential nitrogen nutrients to plants in agriculture and natural ecosystems. Especially, these bacteria and Leguminosae plants form symbiosis to improve plant growth and soil fertility. Theoretically, the inoculation of NFBs into soils increases biological nitrogen fxation, but the efciency of NFBs is frequently compromised by the low capacity of NFB root colonization. In this study, we introduced the synthetic bacterium EcCMC, which was genetically engineered to express the surface-displayed artifcial polysaccharide (PS)-recognizing protein Cmc, to test if it can improve NFBs root colonization in representative Leguminosae plants, including Astragalus sinicus and Medicago sativa. Rhizosphere microbiomes, biochemical indicators, and plant yields were evaluated after 28 days in the three treatments, i.e., the control group without addition of any exogenous bacterium, the NFBs plus EcM (bacteria only expressing mCherry rather than Cmc) group, and the NFBs plus EcCMC group $( n = 3 )$ ). 

Results Owing to its polysaccharide-binding capacity, EcCMC strongly bound to the surface of A. sinicus roots. This binding was followed by the increased recruitment of the exogenous NFBs, Sinorhizobium meliloti and Sphingomonas endophytica, on the roots. As revealed by amplicon sequencing of the 16S rRNA gene, a combined inoculation of EcCMC and the NFBs increased the relative abundance of both Rhizobiales and Sphingomonadales, two important bacterial groups involved in nitrogen fxation. Consistently, metabolomic analysis showed that the metabolites involved in nitrogen fxation remarkably accumulated in the rhizosphere soils inoculated with NFBs plus EcCMC. Moreover, inoculation of NFBs plus EcCMC increased the activity of nitrogenase from $1 0 . 8 \sim 1 1 . 3 $ to 16.2 nmol/min/g (signifcant diference, $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ , t-test), together with the total soil nitrogen levels from $2 1 7 \sim 2 5 8$ to $4 1 4 \mathrm { m g / k g }$ (signifcant diference, $p < 0 . 0 5 ,$ ), and the soil organic matter levels from $1 9 . 5 \sim 2 0 . 8 $ to $2 3 . 6 \mathrm { m g / k g }$ (signifcant diference, $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ . Consequently, the yield of A. sinicus was remarkably improved by the inoculation of NFBs plus EcCMC. Similar results were observed in the experiments using Medicago sativa. 

Conclusions This study sheds a novel light on a synthetic biology-assisted regulation of rhizosphere microbiomes for enhanced nitrogen fxation and soil fertility in Leguminous plants. The designed polysaccharide-binding protein may be used as a universal tool to promote plant growth and enhance crop resilience in the future. 

Keywords Rhizosphere microbiome, Nitrogen-fxing bacteria, Synthetic bacterium, Colonization, Green manure

# Introduction

Rhizosphere microbiomes include microbial communities closely associated with the plant roots [1–3] and infuenced by the root metabolites [4–6]. Tese microbiomes can have positive or negative efects on plant performance, including growth and defence. Te benefcial rhizosphere microbiomes, such as the plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR), are indispensable for plant growth. Tese microbes may produce growth-promoting agents and facilitate the release of bioavailable nutrient elements from insoluble minerals [7–9]. Consequently, rational regulation of rhizosphere microbes is of great importance for phytoremediation, soil fertility promotion, and crop production [10–13]. With the advances of synthetic biology, more and more eforts have been devoted to remodel natural microbiomes with rhizosphere inoculants [14–16]. For example, synthetic microbial communities involved in nutrient utilization have been designed and applied to assist the rhizosphere microbiomes in improving plant growth or plant disease resistance [17, 18]. Previously, we also constructed a polysaccharide (PS)-recognizing synthetic bacterium, which enhanced phytoremediation by strengthening the physical contact between natural PS-producing microbes and plant roots [19]. In addition, to avoid the potential ecological risk of artifcial DNA, an engineered protein was further designed to improve the interaction between functional microbes and plant roots [20]. 

Nitrogen-containing compounds are critical nutrients for plant growth in agriculture. Traditionally, the 

well-known chemical nitrogen fertilizers, e.g., ammonium and nitrate, have been widely used to provide inorganic forms of nitrogen for plant productivity. However, their wide application in agriculture leads to their abundant leaching in the underground water and severe ecological pollution problems [21–23]. Terefore, there is a growing need to develop sustainable strategies for reducing the use of chemical nitrogen fertilizers. As such, biological nitrogen fxation is a green and economic way for the conversion of $\Nu _ { 2 }$ to bioavailable nitrogen forms [24–26]. Te nitrogen-fxing bacteria (NFBs), commonly functioning as PGPR, provide about 200 million tons of nitrogen to the agricultural lands per year [27–30]. 

Leguminous plants serve as nutrient suppliers in economic crops to improve soil fertility and crop yield. Some of these manures, e.g., Astragalus sinicus and Medicago sativa, are also used as food for animals [31, 32]. Owing to the symbiosis between Leguminous plants and rhizosphere NFBs, these plants import considerable organic nitrogen compounds into the soil during their growth [33, 34]. Teoretically, inoculation of exogenous NFBs could increase the degree of nitrogen fxation. However, the activity of these NFBs is frequently compromised by their low root colonization efciency, leading to rapid replacement of these NFBs by indigenous rhizosphere microbes (i.e., the microbes inhabiting the soil before the exogenous NFBs are introduced) [35, 36]. Up to now, it remains a great challenge to enhance the root colonization and persistence of these functional bacteria. 

Te development of synthetic biology techniques is strongly promoting rational regulation of the rhizosphere microbiome [19, 20]. To enhance the root colonization of exogenous NFBs in legumes, this study introduced the synthetic bacterium (i.e., EcCMC), which was genetically engineered to express the surface-displayed artifcial polysaccharide (PS)-recognizing protein Cmc. Our previous studies have revealed that this bacterium may bind to the roots of bioremediation plants via Cmc, leading to the remodeling of the rhizosphere microbiome [19]. Herein, we further investigated the efect of this synthetic bacterium on NFB colonization, rhizosphere microbiome remodeling, and the growth of two typical Leguminous plants (i.e., Astragalus sinicus and Medicago sativa). Tis study aimed to develop a facile synthetic biology-based strategy for promoting the growth of legumes during soil fertility improvement. 

# Materials and methods

# Strains

Te synthetic E. coli strains EcM and EcCMC were constructed and described in our previous study [19]. Briefy, the E. coli strain BL21 at the competence state was transformed using the pET-28a-based plasmids pET-28a-CMC and pET-28a-M, obtaining the strains EcCMC and EcM, respectively. Te synthetic E. coli strain EcCMC expresses the Cmc protein containing the glucan-binding domain of Clostridium cellulolyticum CipC $\mathrm { ( C B M _ { \mathrm { C i p C } } ) }$ , mCherry, and another $\mathrm { C B M } _ { \mathrm { C i p C } }$ domain. To realize the expression of Cmc, the gene CMC was de novo synthesized using the optimized codon of E. coli and cloned into pET-28a, generating pET-28a-CMC for further transformation. Te control E. coli strain EcM expresses the fuorescence protein mCherry not containing the glucanbinding domain. Te NFB strains, including Sinorhizobium medicae NKN632 and Sphingomonas endophytica NKN643, were isolated from the rhizosphere soil of Vicia dasycarpa, Tianjin, China. Both strains are free-living nitrogen-fxers and can promote plant growth under low levels of nitrogen fertilizers. Te solid BNN medium (glucose 5  g, $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ 0.15  g, $\mathrm { M g S O _ { 4 } }$ 0.1  g, $\mathrm { F e S O _ { 4 } { \cdot } 7 H _ { 2 } O }$ $\boldsymbol { 0 . 0 4 \ \mathrm { g } }$ , $\mathrm { N a M o O _ { 4 } } 0 . 0 0 5 \ \mathrm { g }$ , $\mathrm { K _ { 2 } H P O _ { 4 } } 0 . 8 \ \mathrm { g }$ , $\mathrm { \ d H _ { 2 } O }$ 1000 mL, agar $1 5 ~ \mathrm { g } , ~ \mathrm { p H } ~ 6 . 8 \sim 7 . 0 )$ was used for this isolation. Te exogenous control bacteria not producing extracellular polysaccharides, i.e., Pseudomonas soli NK1431 and Achromobacter denitrifcans NK531, were isolated from the rhizosphere soils of Amygdalus persica plants, Shandong Province, China. Te LB medium (yeast extract ${ \mathfrak { 5 } } { \mathfrak { g } } ,$ tryptone 10 g, NaCl 10 g, $\mathrm { { d H } _ { 2 } O }$ 1000 mL, agar $1 5 { \mathrm { ~ g } }$ ) was used to isolate the two strains. Both NFBs and the control strains were cultured at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \sim 4 8 \mathrm { h }$ for further use.

# Rhizosphere microbiome analysis

Te plants were cultured in fowerpots as described in Sect.  2.2. at Biomarker Technologies Corporation, China, and analyzed with the online platform (http://www.biocloud. net). # Results

Exogenous NFB fails to efciently colonize rhizosphere soil Initially, two nitrogen-fxing bacteria (i.e., Sinorhizobium medicae NKN632 and Sphingomonas endophytica NKN643), which were isolated from the rhizosphere soil of Vicia dasycarpa, were inoculated in the rhizosphere soil of Astragalus sinicus to explore the growthpromoting efects of these exogenous nitrogen-fxing bacteria. As shown in Fig. S1a, the CFU numbers of the control group without inoculation of exogenous bacteria increased with the increase in culturing time, which may be attributed to the slow proliferation of local bacteria inhabiting the Astragalus sinicus roots. Upon inoculation of NKN632, NKN643, or both strains together (NFBs), the number of nitrogen-fxing bacteria in rhizosphere soil increased signifcantly. However, after 14 or 28 days, the number of nitrogen-fxing bacteria between the inoculation treatment and the control did not show a signifcant diference. Although inoculation of functional bacteria into the rhizosphere soil is an ideal strategy in sustainable agriculture [38–41], we did not obtain ideal results by using the two exogenous bacteria. 

Nitrogenase activity and total nitrogen (TN) contents were further measured. Interestingly, the inoculation of NKN632 or NKN643 alone had no impact on the nitrogenase activity and total nitrogen (TN) contents in the rhizosphere soil, and the inoculation of both strains (NFBs) only slightly increased both indicators (Fig. S1b, S1c). For example, the NFBs only led to a slight increase in the nitrogenase activity from 11.0 to $1 1 . 5 \mathrm { \ \mu m o l / ( h { \cdot } g }$ soil) (Fig. S1b) and to an increase in TN contents from 214.8 to $2 4 2 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / k g }$ after 28  days (Fig. S1c). Similarly, after 28 days of inoculation, the mixed NFBs, or NKN632 and NKN643 alone, had only a mild impact on plant fresh weight (FW, Fig. S1d). Tis indicated that the exogenous NFBs, when they were solely inoculated, failed to efciently colonize the rhizosphere. Tese results suggest that other strategies are needed to improve the colonization and duration of NFBs in the rhizosphere soil for a 

signifcant improvement of nitrogen fxation and plant yield. Terefore, in the following experiments, we used synthetic biology techniques to improve root binding and colonization of NFBs. 

# The synthetic bacterium EcCMC strongly binds polysaccharide‑exposed NFBs

Previously, we found that the introduction of polysaccharide-binding synthetic bacteria (EcCMC) enhanced the interaction between plant roots and functional bacteria, which further improved the accumulation of heavy metal cadmium, leading to enhanced phytoremediation [19]. Moreover, the introduced EcCMC was not detectable after 14 days of culturing, suggesting that the potential ecological risk caused by the synthetic bacteria is very small, and its application can be further expanded. 

To investigate whether these proteins could also function during binding and colonization of NFBs on roots, the distribution of polysaccharides on NFBs and its binding to the synthetic bacterium exposing polysaccharidebinding proteins (i.e., EcCMC) were observed by using confocal microscopy. After staining of the bacterial cells by FITC-ConA, which specifcally binds polysaccharides, the nitrogen-fxing bacteria NKN632 and NKN643 alone or in combination (NFBs) were strongly stained by FITC-ConA, while the control strains NK1431 and NK531 did not bind the dye (Fig. S2a, Fig. S3). Terefore, the used NFBs exposed polysaccharides on their cell surfaces, while the control strains did not expose these macromolecules. 

Based on these results, we further introduced the engineering bacterium EcCMC. It has a strong capacity to recognize polysaccharides commonly distributed on the root surface, owing to the high-degree surface exposure of the polysaccharide-binding protein Cmc on EcCMC [19]. As revealed by confocal images, EcCMC exhibited strong green fuorescence after its incubation with FITC-labeled polysaccharides extracted from the NFB cell surface (FITC-NFB PS, Fig. S2c & S2d). Hence, the synthetic EcCMC cells, rather than the control EcM cells, could efciently recognize and bind the polysaccharides produced by NFBs.

# EcCMC and exogenous NFB drastically remodel rhizosphere microbiome

Te microbial diversity of the rhizosphere microbiome was analyzed by bacterial 16S rDNA and fungal ITS sequencing. Te rarefaction curves in each sample fattened out with the increase of sequencing amount, suggesting that the sample sequences were sufcient for analysis (Fig.  S6). Te $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ treatment had no signifcant impact on the bacterial Simpson index, but increased the fungal Simpon index (Fig.  2a), indicating the higher rhizosphere fungal diversity of the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group. Abundance analysis of bacterial community further revealed that $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ led to the increase in the relative abundance of several bacterial groups (Fig. 2b). Especially, the relative abundance of Rhizobiales, Sphingomonadales and Xanthomonadales in the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group increased by 0.494-fold (0.240 versus 0.154), 0.345-fold (0.074 versus 0.055), and 0.676- fold (0.062 versus 0.037), respectively, as compared to that in the Control group. 

It is reported that many soil bacteria adhered to the root surfaces and were organized to form biofilms 

[42]. The formation of biofilm on the root surface further assists bacterial survival and stability near the rhizosphere [43]. The relative abundance of biofilms in each group was then tested. As shown in Fig.  2c, the abundance of biofilm-formed bacteria in the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group was significantly higher than that in the control and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups, indicating that more bacteria could be colonized on the root surface to form biofilms. The microbial diversity of the bacterial orders forming biofilms was further analyzed. As shown in Fig. 2d, the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group had the highest relative abundance of Rhizobiales, Sphingomonadales and Xanthomonadales. The Xanthomonadales order was responsible for early colonization and community establishment [44], while due to the bridging role of EcCMC, much more PGPR strains, such as Rhizobiales and Sphingomonadales tended to colonize on the root surfaces. Consistently, as revealed by CFU assays, the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group had the highest amount of NFBs as compared with the control and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ group (Fig. 2e), confirming the enhanced root colonization of NFBs by EcCMC. 

ITS sequencing was further used to detect the fungal abundance in rhizosphere soil. As shown in Fig.  2f, the relative abundance of Pleosporales, which was associated with nitrogen fixation [45], increased by 1.87-fold (0.43 versus 0.15) in the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group compared with the control group. In addition, fungal polysaccharide-binding assays revealed that the EcCMC cells could adsorb much higher levels of the polysaccharides (Fig. S7), suggesting that the binding of EcCMC to fungal polysaccharides was involved in the change in fungal abundance. Taken together, the introduction of NFBs plus EcCMC strongly remodeled the rhizosphere microbiome of A. sinicus. 

Metabolomic analysis of the control, $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ , and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups was then performed. Principal component analysis (PCA) showed the remarkable difference between the groups and sample distribution in each group. As shown in Fig. 3, the control and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ groups had significantly different metabolites as the PCA dots were completely separated (Fig.  3a), while the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ and the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups exhibited less difference (Fig. 3b). A higher level of group differentiation was obtained by using the orthogonal partial least-squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA). Moreover, all samples in the same treatment were gathered in the same area (negative or positive), and all the extrapolated intercept values of $Q ^ { 2 }$ were less than 0.05 (Fig. S8), indicating the models in this study have good predictability [46]. 

# EcCMC and exogenous NFBs infuence rhizosphere metabolism of A. sinicus

Diferential metabolite analysis was further performed between the control and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ groups as well as the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups. Te volcano map in Fig. 3c showed that 438 metabolites of the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group were increased compared with that of the control group, including Tragopogonsaponin B, Avermectin B1a, Adonixanthin, Desoxoglabrolide, Cynarasaponin F, etc. Moreover, 82 metabolites of the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group were increased compared with the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups (Fig. 3d). 

Te pathway enrichment analysis of diferent metabolites in the three groups was conducted with the KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) database. Tere was a signifcant diference between the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ and control groups in six metabolic pathways, including alpha-linolenic acid metabolism, infammatory mediator regulation of TRP channels, histidine metabolism, purine metabolism, ascorbate and aldarate metabolism, and serotonergic synapse (Fig.  3e). On the contrary, the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ and $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ groups had diferent metabolites in eight metabolic pathways, including cutin, suberine and wax biosynthesis, sulfur metabolism, biosynthesis of alkaloids derived from ornithine, lysine and nicotinic acid, cAMP signaling pathway, glucagon signaling pathway, and serotonergic synapse (Fig. 3f ). Together, these results revealed that NFBs plus EcCMC had an impact on rhizosphere metabolism of $A$ . sinicus, which may be related to the remodeled rhizosphere microbiome.

# Discussion

Leguminous plants are efective plants to improve farmland, increase soil organic matter, reduce the use of chemical fertilizers, and increase crop yield. Owing to the symbiosis between these plants and rhizosphere 

nitrogen-fxing bacteria, Leguminosae plants provided considerable organic nitrogen compounds into the soil during their growth. With the increasing awareness of green environmental protection, the utilization of Leguminous plants and biological nitrogen fxation resources has attracted high attention. 

In theory, inoculating exogenous nitrogen-fxing bacteria can enhance the growth of leguminous green manure, but how to colonize nitrogen-fxing bacteria quickly and efectively in plant roots is an urgent problem to be solved. Previously, our group constructed a synthetic bacterium (i.e., EcCMC) exposing the artifcial polysaccharide-recognizing protein Cmc, which strongly bound to polysaccharides and recruited natural polysaccharideproducing bacterial and fungal cells [19]. However, the specifc receptor recognition pathway mediating the binding of EcCMC and the roots remains to be investigated. Te synthetic bacteria were demonstrated to increase the microbial biomass on the roots and strongly remodel the rhizosphere microbiome, improving plant growth and oxidative stress resistance in water. To evaluate the regulation ability of rhizosphere colonies in terrestrial plants, in this study, EcCMC was used to enhance the colonization of exogenous NFBs on the plants of legumes for improved yield. Tis enhancement may be attributed to the function of the synthetic bacterium as a bridge, supporting the physical binding of the functional NFBs to plant roots via exposed polysaccharides. Consequently, the colonization and bioflm formation of the NFBs were strengthened. Te colonized NFBs further supplied more nitrogen nutrients to the plant roots for the promotion of plant growth as compared to free-living NFBs. 

Microbiome analysis revealed that the introduction of EcCMC and NFBs increased the relative abundance of PGPR. Several bacterial phyla (Rhizobiales, Sphingomonadales, and Xanthomonadales) and fungi phyla (Pleosporales) associated with N fxation were signifcantly upregulated. Rhizobiales are the bacteria that enter the plant cells to form symbiosomes in legumes and perform the biological $\Nu _ { 2 }$ fxation [51], while Sphingomonadales bacteria are important participants in free-living $\Nu _ { 2 }$ fxation. Te increase of benefcial phyla in the rhizosphere might promote plant growth [52, 53]. Since the two kinds of exogenous NFBs, i.e., Sinorhizobium medicae NKN632 and Sphingomonas endophytica NKN643, were added in both the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ group and the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group, the result that the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group had higher abundance in both Rhizobiales and Sphingomonadales than the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c M }$ group (Fig.  2a) indicated the enhanced root colonization of exogenous NFBs by EcCMC. Moreover, the bioflm amount of the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group was higher than other groups, 

indicating that more bacteria could be colonized on the root surface. Moreover, the amount of nitrogen-fxing microbes in the rhizosphere soil of the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group was obviously upregulated compared with other groups. Tese results implied that EcCMC facilitated both bacterial and fungal adsorption and growth on the root surfaces, making PGPR strains fully exert their functions to promote plant growth. Consistently, in plant experiments, the parameters for plant growth (including plant height, fresh weight, and levels of leaf chlorophyll a and chlorophyll b) and soil fertility (enzymatic activity of rhizosphere soils, total nitrogen, and soil organic matter) in the $\mathrm { N F B s + E c C M C }$ group were signifcantly higher than other groups. Tese results indicate that the introduction of EcCMC can recruit functional microorganisms to the rhizosphere, fully exerting their promoting efect on plant growth. 

Microbiome analysis revealed that the above results and our previous fndings indicate that EcCMC can promote the colonization of nitrogen-fxing bacteria, remodel the rhizosphere microbiome, and further improve plant yield. Trough screening, more functional microorganisms can be introduced into the plant rhizosphere using this method, achieving efects such as promoting plant growth, soil fertilization, and soil remediation. 

# Conclusion

In summary, this study developed a synthetic bacterium-assisted colonization of exogenous nitrogen fxation bacteria techniques for enhanced nitrogen fxation and soil fertility in the application of Leguminous plants. Owing to its polysaccharide-binding capacity, the synthetic EcCMC could act as a “bridge” to improve the colonization of nitrogen-fxing bacteria on the roots. Te remodeled microbiome caused by EcCMC further promoted soil fertility and plant growth. With the positive efect of the synthetic bacteria as well as nitrogen fxation bacteria on green manure, this study provides a strategy of engineered bacteria-based regulation of rhizosphere microbiome to improve biological nitrogen fxation as well as soil fertility.
