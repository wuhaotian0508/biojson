# SUMMARY

Mineral nutrient uptake and deposition profoundly influence plant development, stress resilience, and productivity. Silicon (Si), though classified as a non-essential element, significantly influences a plant’s physiology, particularly in fortifying defense responses and mitigating stress. While the genetic and molecular mechanisms of Si uptake and transport are well studied in monocots, particularly rice, their role in dicot species, such as soybean, remains unclear at the cellular and molecular levels. In this study, we utilized single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) to dissect cellular responses to Si accumulation in soybean leaves. We identified distinct cellular populations, including a unique Si-induced or Si-associated cell cluster within vascular cells, suggesting a specialized mechanism of Si distribution. Si treatment notably induced the expression of defense-related genes, with a pronounced enrichment in vascular cells, underscoring their pivotal role in activating plant defense mechanisms. Moreover, Si modulated the expression of genes involved in phytoalexin biosynthesis, salicylic acid, and immune receptor signaling, suggesting transcriptional priming of genes involved in defense responses. Further investigation of Si transporters revealed precise expression of an Si efflux gene in epidermal cells in response to Si treatment. We also validated the role of efflux Si transporters using a Xenopus oocyte assay and CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing of composite soybean plant roots. This study provides critical insights into the biotic stress regulatory networks influenced by Si treatment in soybean leaves at the single-cell level, thus laying the foundation for enhancing stress tolerance through optimized mineral nutrient uptake. 

Keywords: Glycine max, silicon, single-cell genomics, transcriptional response. 

# INTRODUCTION

Mineral nutrients are essential elements that plants obtain from the soil and use for various physiological processes, such as growth, development, and overall function (Dhingra et al., 2024). Silicon (Si) is a lesser-known mineral nutrient but is highly beneficial to plant health, 

environmental resilience, and agricultural productivity (Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). Si is not classified as an essential nutrient for plants but has been recognized as quasi-essential for its beneficial effects on plant growth and stress tolerance (Coskun et al., 2019; Thakral et al., 2024). Although Si ranks as the second most 

abundant element in the Earth’s crust, the role of Si in plant development, abiotic stress tolerance, and disease resistance was unstudied until the discovery of Si transporter genes in rice (Ma et al., 2006). Si is absorbed through the plant root system as silicic acid (Si $( O H ) _ { 4 } )$ and transported to aerial tissues. Si is deposited in developing tissues and forms a complex with organic compounds, which is thought to create an apoplastic barrier that provides a barrier against invading pathogens (Coskun et al., 2019). Recent evidence based on comparative genomics, physiological, and molecular studies showed that Si uptake improves resistance against several fungal diseases, such as powdery mildew (Wang et al., 2020), rice-blast (Sathe et al., 2021), stem rust (Li et al., 2022), root-rot (Abbai et al., 2019), frog-eye disease (Telles et al., 2014), and Phytophthora (Rasoolizadeh et al., 2018) in various economically important crops. Si also plays a beneficial role in abiotic stresses, including salt and osmotic stress tolerance in crops such as cucumber and sorghum, and reducing heavy metal toxicity in rice, wheat, and maize (Thakral et al., 2021; Thakral et al., 2024). 

Accumulation of Si in the leaf epidermal layer acts as a modulator influencing the timing and extent of the plant’s defense response. For example, it obstructs pathogenesis-related events, interferes with signaling, and prevents penetration of pathogenic fungal hyphae into the plant cells (Coskun et al., 2019). Similarly, the Si-generated apoplastic barrier restricts evapotranspiration, positively decreasing water stress (Thorne et al., 2020). In planta, Si content varies from 0.1 to $10 \%$ on a dry weight basis (Hodson et al., 2005). In monocots, Si deposition occurs in specialized dumbbell-shaped cells called silica cells. This mechanism has been extensively studied in rice and sorghum (Kumar et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2006), while in the case of dicots (e.g., soybean and cucumber), Si is deposited in unorganized patches on the leaf surface between the cuticle layer and epidermal cells (Arsenault-Labrecque et al., 2012; Dhingra et al., 2024; Samuels et al., 1991). Si transport in monocot species such as rice is mediated by two types of Si transporters, consisting of influx (Lsi1) and efflux (Lsi2, Lsi3, and Siet4). These transporters further show different polar localization in the exodermis and endodermis of root tissues (Ma et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2007; Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). However, the molecular mechanisms involved in Si uptake, transport, deposition, and their effects on molecular cascades in dicot species, including soybean, have not been well studied. Moreover, previous studies have primarily relied on bulk transcriptomics, which fail to account for the cellular heterogeneity inherent in plant tissues and do not consider the ability of specific cell types to influence, or be influenced by, nutrient uptake and deposition (Hao, Shi, et al., 2021). Single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) techniques have enabled researchers to identify cell-specific gene expression in 

several organisms (Cervantes-Perez et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021; Nobori et al., 2023; Shahan et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Tenorio Berrıo et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2022). In the present study, we employed an snRNA-seq approach to identify genetic and cellular heterogeneity in the soybean leaf upon Si treatment. We have characterized the efflux Si transporter in soybean and discussed the transformative impact of snRNA-seq on understanding cellular and molecular networks and biological processes, highlighting the role of Si on plant immunity and uncovering comprehensive transcriptomes of 12 distinct cell types upon Si treatment. The analysis identified specific gene expression patterns related to defense response, salicylic acid (SA) metabolism, and other induced processes. Additionally, our study discovered a new subset of cells present in higher fractions in Si-treated plants, revealing the dynamic response of plants to Si treatment. Moreover, we functionally validated efflux Si transporters by determining their subcellular localization using gene editing and Xenopus oocyte assays.

# Discerning the cellular responses to Si treatment

Previous studies have investigated the effects of Si treatment on the expression of genes involved in both abiotic and biotic stress tolerance (Kurabachew et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2017). However, the precise mechanism by which Si treatment regulates gene expression across various cell types remains to be explored. Comparing snRNA-seq data from both untreated and Si-treated samples, 4327 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) across all cell types in response to Si treatment were identified (Table S1). To discern the impact of $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment on specific cell types, we conducted a comparative analysis of UMI (Figure 3a) and revealed a notable increase in UMI counts for Si-induced xylem cells, phloem parenchyma, and epidermal cells in $\mathsf { S i + }$ treated plants, indicating a comparative increase in gene expression activity. In contrast, a decrease in UMI values was observed for companion, guard, and mesophyll cells (Figure 3a). We identified DEGs between Si– and $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatments and performed GO biological process term interaction analysis (Figure 3b, Figure S5–S7). The number of DEGs was significantly increased upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment, wherein vascular (Si-induced cells) and epidermal cells were the most affected cell types (Figure 3c), consistent with the fact that 

Si is transported from vascular tissues and deposited on the epidermal cell layers. 

In response to Si treatment, vascular cells, specifically Si-induced cells in cluster no. 5, exhibited a significant transcriptional change in GO terms associated with immune response, including NUCLEOTIDE-BINDING SITE LEUCINE-RICH REPEAT (NBS-LRR) pathway genes and WRKY family transcription factors (TFs), as well as defense responses against oomycetes and viruses (Figure 3b). Additionally, the GO term “RNAi-mediated antiviral response” showed prominent enrichment in these cells (Figure S5). Two other vascular cell types (clusters no. 10 and 2) displayed enrichment for “response to biotic stimulus”, “SA signaling”, and “innate immune response” upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure S5). Notably, epidermal cells under $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment exhibited significant genes associated with phytoalexin biosynthesis and response to chitin, both known to trigger immune responses (Ahuja et al., 2012; Pusztahelyi, 2018; Sanchez-Vallet et al., 2015) (Figure S6). Overall, the GO term enrichment analysis suggests a coordinated transcriptional priming response to Si treatment aimed at enhancing biotic stress tolerance in plants (Figure 3b). To gain further insights into cell-type-specific gene expression under Si treatment, heatmaps for three key cell types: epidermis (cluster no. 1), xylem parenchyma cells (cluster no. 10), and Si-induced cells (cluster no. 5) (Figure 3c) were constructed. Differential upregulation of 97 genes in epidermal cells, 68 genes in Si-induced cells, and 100 genes in xylem cells following $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment was observed (Figure 3c). A predominant portion of the upregulated genes in xylem parenchyma and epidermal cell clusters upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment was associated with defense and immune response.

# Perturbations of defense-responsive genes upon Si treatment

In our snRNA-seq dataset, we explored cell-type-specific expression of vital extracellular and intracellular immune receptor genes such as receptor-like proteins (RLPs) and NLRs (NBS-LRR; nucleotide-binding domain leucine-rich repeat). Six RLP genes were identified as highly expressed in vascular cells following $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 5a,b and Table S1). Among them, GmRLP9, GmRLP13a, and GmRLP15 exhibited notably increased expression in both xylem and Si-induced cells after $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 5c). The NLRs (intracellular immune receptors) are categorized into toll/interleukin-1 receptor/resistance protein NLR (TIR-NLR/TNL), coiled-coil-NLR (CNL/CC-NLR), and RPW8-like helper NLR (RNL) (Kourelis et al., 2021). Of 319 NLR genes reported in soybean (Kang et al., 2012), we identified 46 (27 TNLs, 15 CNLs, and 4 RNLs) as highly expressed in $\mathsf { S i } ( + ) .$ - treated plants and confined mainly to epidermal and vascular cell clusters (no. 1, 2, 5, 9, 10, and 11) (Figure 5a,b and Table S1). The TNL class genes, GmTNL1, GmTNL2, and GmDSC1.1, exhibited higher expression in epidermal, vascular, and Si-induced cells (Figure 5d), whereas GmRPM1.1 (CNL) and GmNRG1.2 (RNL) were enriched in xylemassociated cells (Figure 5d), indicating activation of NLRand RLP-mediated immune pathways under $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment. 

Salicylic acid is a signaling molecule (plant hormone) involved in systemic acquired resistance (SAR) and functions by triggering the plant immune system and activating pathogenesis-related genes (Rossi et al., 2023; Stroud et al., 2022). In our snRNA-seq data, we observed cellspecific expression of SA biosynthesis and signaling genes (Figure S14a). In soybeans, both phenylalanine (PAL) and isochorismate (ICS) pathways contribute equally to SA biosynthesis (Huang et al., 2020; Klambt,€ 1962; Shine 

et al., 2016; Wildermuth et al., 2001). However, upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment, we observed no induction of PAL or ICS genes, which were only expressed under Si– conditions (Figure S14a,b). The MES (methyl esterase) gene (Vlot et al., 2008), responsible for converting inactive methyl-SA to active SA, was expressed in mesophyll cells under both treatments. Notably, SA signaling genes, such as GmSARD1, GmPAD4, and GmWRKY40 were upregulated in xylem, Si-induced, and guard cells under $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure S14). Downstream SAR-related genes, such as GmNPR3, GmTGAs, and GmWRKY70 were enriched in vascular and epidermal cells, while GmPR1 genes were active in vascular and mesophyll cells under $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment. 

# Functional validation of Si transporters

Silicon transport occurs through influx (passive, gradient) and efflux (active) type transporters in plants, and has been very well characterized in monocots (Huang & Ma, 2024; Ma et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2007; Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). To understand the cell-type-specific expression patterns of Si transporters in soybean leaves, we explored the expression of known putative Si influx aquaporin family transporters (Deshmukh et al., 2013) and recently identified Si accumulation QTL genes on Chr. 16 (Dhingra et al., 2024) in our snRNA-seq dataset. Among several putative Si transporters in soybean (Table S1), the two influx Lsi1-type Si transporters NOD26-LIKE INTRIN-SIC PROTEIN 2;1 / 2;2 (GmNIP2-1 and GmNIP2-2) were found to be expressed in our datasets (Figure 6a). The UMAP plots showed that the expression of GmNIP2-1 and GmNIP2-2 was notably higher in Si– (control) leaves, particularly in the epidermal cells, whereas their expression decreased following $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 6a). On the other hand, among the three Si efflux transporters identified in the genome-wide association study (GWAS) (Dhingra et al., 2024), only GmHiSil2c (soybean ortholog of rice Siet4) was highly expressed in our snRNA-seq dataset. GmHiSil2c was exclusively expressed in leaf epidermal cells upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment. In an independent experiment, we validated the expression of these genes using qRT-PCR analysis, and their expression pattern aligned with the snRNA-seq data (Figure S15). 

To determine the subcellular localization of these Si transporters, the coding sequences (CDS) of GmNIP2-1, GmNIP2-2, and GmHiSil2c were fused with the green fluorescent protein (GFP) at the C-terminal end and expressed under the control of a constitutive promoter (CmYLCV ) followed by transfection in N. benthamiana protoplasts. Confocal microscopy analysis confirmed their role as transmembrane proteins as these transporter-GFP fusions were precisely localized to the cell membrane (Figure 6b). To determine the Si transport activity of putative efflux Si transporter genes GmHiSil2b and GmHiSil2c for $S i ( O H ) _ { 4 }$ using $G e ( O H ) _ { 4 }$ as an analog (Chen et al., 2017; Ma 

et al., 2006; Nikolic et al., 2007) both genes were heterologously expressed in Xenopus laevis oocytes. GmHiSil2c exhibited a stronger Ge efflux activity with an $\sim 7 0 \%$ reduction in oocytes injected with Ge $( \mathsf { O H } ) _ { 4 } ,$ whereas GmHiSil2b showed moderate Ge efflux activity with an $\sim 2 0 \%$ reduction of Ge in oocytes (Figure 6c). This confirmed that GmHiSil2c is a comparatively stronger Si efflux transporter than GmHiSil2b.

# DISCUSSION

# Understanding cellular heterogeneity upon Si accumulation in leaves

Numerous previous studies substantiate the importance of mineral nutrients on plant development, disease resistance, abiotic stress tolerance, seed quality, and net productivity (Dhingra et al., 2024). The availability of mineral nutrients to plants depends on several interacting factors. These factors encompass inherent genetic diversity, soil composition, physical and chemical properties, and other environmental variables. Among major and minor nutrients, the role of Si in plants has often been overlooked. Although Si is not classified as an essential plant nutrient, it plays a beneficial role in plant development (Pavlovic et al., 2021), abiotic stress tolerance (Gao et al., 2005; Hamayun et al., 2010; Wang, Wang, et al., 2021), and biotic stress alleviation (Fauteux et al., 2005; Fauteux et al., 2006; Rasoolizadeh et al., 2018; Thakral et al., 2021). The Si uptake mechanism is well-studied in rice but remains poorly understood in dicot crops like soybean. While a major Si uptake QTL was recently identified, a detailed molecular and cellular understanding of its mechanism is lacking. Therefore, to comprehensively elucidate the cellular and molecular mechanism of Si deposition in soybean leaves and its consequential effects on gene networks, we employed snRNA-seq technology. This approach allowed us to gain comprehensive insights into the complexities of Si-mediated biological processes in soybean. Utilizing this technology, we identified unique Si-induced cells (cluster no. 5) that were highly induced in $\mathsf { S i + }$ treated soybean plants and were closely associated with xylem cells (Figure 4a,b). Moreover, $\mathsf { S i } ( + )$ -treated plants exhibited altered patterning and arrangement of xylem cells, which were denser, continuous, and showed less distance between the xylem vessels (Figure 4c and Figure S10).

Previous studies have shown that Si uptake impacts cell size, shape, differentiation, and overall arrangements in leaf and root tissue (Motomura et al., 2006; Yamaji et al., 2015). For instance, both high Si-accumulating (rice and maize) and low Si-accumulating (onion) plants develop new Casparian band cells in roots upon Si treatment (Fleck et al., 2015). Similarly, the addition of Si led to changes in vascular tissue in Glycyrrhiza uralensis, improving water transport efficiency (Zhang et al., 2020), altering leaf anatomy (thickness of epidermis and mesophyll) in banana, and increasing metaxylem and vascular bundle length in wheat (Asmar et al., 2013; Taha et al., 2021). Based on these reports, we hypothesized that in $\mathsf { S i } ( + )$ - treated soybean plants, preexisting vascular cells may undergo differentiation and/or distinct transcriptome regulation leading to xylem cell patterning and altered arrangements in leaves (Figure 4a–c). Nevertheless, this hypothesis necessitates dedicated experimentation to validate and further explore the underlying mechanism. 

# Si treatment transcriptionally primes the vascular cells for plant defense

It is well established that Si plays a significant role in enhancing plant resilience against biotic stress through multiple mechanisms (Fauteux et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2017). These mechanisms include the formation of physical barriers or apoplastic obstructions (Coskun et al., 2019), enhancement of defense-related metabolites, hormones, and antimicrobial compounds (such as phytoalexins), and the activation of defense-responsive molecular pathways (Fauteux et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2017). However, a few studies that used bulk RNA sequencing have suggested that Si alone has no impact on a plant’s transcriptome and metabolism in a controlled or unstressed condition (Cai et al., 2008; Coskun et al., 2019). This inference could be partly due to the presumption that Si is an inert substance. However, a recent study suggested that Si is not inert and can cause substantial oxidation of molecules (Li et al., 2023). Our study supports this new understanding, demonstrating that Si treatment could change the cell-specific expression and prime the plant to induce defense-responsive genes, even in the absence of pathogenic stress. Through detailed characterization of distinct cell types, we uncovered complex yet highly informative gene expression patterns across various cell populations (Figures 1–3). Notably, a subset of genes associated with “defense response” and “plant immunity” were predominantly enriched in $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 3). This finding also aligns with prior studies, showing that Si supplementation can change the plant transcriptome with or without pathogen or abiotic stressors (Brunings et al., 2009). For instance, in wheat, Si treatment has been reported to upregulate a significant number of genes, particularly those associated with MYB TFs (Hao, Shi, 

et al., 2021). Similarly, in rice, Si treatment under unstressed conditions was found to decrease phosphorus (P) uptake by modulating the transcriptome, including the downregulation of P transporter genes, independent of any biotic or abiotic stress (Hu et al., 2018). Nevertheless, it cannot be ruled out that the transcriptional priming may be temporal and cell/tissue-specific and therefore was not captured in previous studies utilizing bulk RNA sequencing. 

Identification of cell-specific expression of defenseresponsive genes that are confined to vascular cell types represents a significant advancement beyond prior knowledge, emphasizing the distinct and specialized role of Si treatment in coordinating molecular responses related to biotic stress (Figures 3 and 4). Moreover, the Si-induced cluster exhibited a unique gene expression pattern, significantly enriched for plant–pathogen interaction, including phytoalexin, RNAi silencing, immune response, and SA signaling. Genes involved in the phytoalexin biosynthesis process (GmWRKY33a, GmWRKY33b, GmWRKY33c, and CALRETICULIN-3a; (GmCRT3a)) were significantly enriched in epidermal, vascular and guard cells after $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 3e; Figure S8). Several functional studies have demonstrated the role of CRT3a and WRKY33a genes in mediating the production of phytoalexins and eventually conferring resistance against fungal diseases (Ahmed & Kovinich, 2021; Mao et al., 2011; Matsukawa et al., 2013; Tao et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2020). In soybean, glyceollin is a major phytoalexin and plays a crucial role in providing defense against Phytophthora sp. (Cheng et al., 2015; Fawe et al., 1998; Rodrigues et al., 2004; Schmidt et al., 1992). Among other prominent biological processes, the RNAi silencing pathway is activated in response to pathogen attack, preventing the spread of infection (Deleris et al., 2006; Mukherjee et al., 2013; Qin et al., 2017). We identified a significantly higher transcript abundance of RNA silencing machinery genes, such as GmDCL1, GmDCL4, and GmAGO5, in Siinduced and other cell types (Figure S13). 

In plants, including soybean and Arabidopsis, it has been shown that the NLR genes are involved in fungal, bacterial, and viral defense (Boyes et al., 1998; Castel et al., 2019; Peart et al., 2005; Pedley et al., 2019; Wang, Chen, et al., 2021). These genes were highly upregulated in vasculature, Si-induced, and epidermal cells in our dataset (Figure 5a,b). Similarly, NLR genes play a pivotal role in recognizing and initiating defense mechanism through the hypersensitive response (HR) and SAR (DeYoung & Innes, 2006). A recent study utilizing single-cell RNA-seq revealed cell-type-specific responses to 

fungal infection in Arabidopsis leaves (Tang et al., 2023). Consistent with our findings, they reported the enrichment of NLRs in vascular cells (Tang et al., 2023). In our study, snRNA-seq provided a nuanced perspective, enabling the detection of distinct expression patterns of NLR immune response genes (Figure 5d).

# Unveiling Si deposition in soybean leaves

Processes, such as Si uptake, transport, and deposition vary among plant species. Si is deposited in specialized silica cells of monocot species (Kumar et al., 2017; Kumar et al., 2020). Conversely, in dicots such as Cucurbitaceae (pumpkin, watermelon) (Abe, 2019), sunflower (Van der 

Ent et al., 2020), soybean (Rasoolizadeh et al., 2018), tomato (Zexer et al., 2023), and broad-leaf trees (Ge et al., 2020), Si has been shown to be deposited in patches at the trichome base, subsequently spreading to epidermal cell layers (Figure 1a and Figure S2). The reason for Si deposition in dicots as patches rather than a uniform pattern remains unclear. However, several factors may contribute to this phenomenon, including parallel venation in monocots compared with net-like (reticulate) veins in dicots, which may affect Si unloading in leaves. Additionally, the transpiration rate, dynamics of xylem flow, or preferential unloading of Si in dicot leaves could influence Si deposition. Previous studies have demonstrated that Si can modify root anatomical features, including Casparian bands, suberin lamellae, and enlarged vascular tissue organization in maize and rice (Bokor et al., 2019; Kubo et al., 2005; Ma & Yamaji, 2015; Vaculık et al., 2012). Here, we observed altered spacing between xylem vessels under $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment compared with Si– controls, indicating that Si uptake may influence the organization of vascular tissues. The development of protoxylem and metaxylem is known to occur early during tissue growth and is tightly regulated by hormonal cues and TFs (Kubo et al., 2005). Our findings, together with prior work, suggest that Si may not act by altering the initial onset of protoxylem differentiation but rather by modulating broader developmental pathways that govern xylem patterning (Kubo et al., 2005; Vaculık et al., 2012). However, future research focused on the spatiotemporal regulation of xylem development in response to Si, including transcriptomic and hormonal profiling, will be essential to unravel the underlying developmental processes influenced by Si treatment. 

Si uptake in plants occurs primarily through the root epidermal cells, facilitated by specific transporters. The influx transporter Lsi1 (aquaporin family) mediates Si uptake from the soil, while the efflux transporter Lsi2 (anion transport superfamily) facilitates Si efflux from the root cells into the xylem. These transporters have been extensively 

characterized in rice and maize (Coskun et al., 2021; Deshmukh & Belanger, 2016; Ma, 2010; Ma et al., 2006; Ma & Yamaji, 2015; Mitani et al., 2008; Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). A previously published GWAS (Dhingra et al., 2024) identified a major QTL on Chr. 16 that controls Si accumulation in soybean leaves. This significant QTL region harbors three efflux Si transporter genes (GmHiSil-2a, -2b, and -2c). To understand the expression patterns of putative Si influx and efflux transporters in soybean, the snRNA-seq data were explored and genes were characterized using subcellular localization, gene editing, and a Xenopus oocyte-based efflux activity of transporters assay. All putative Si transporters identified in previous genome-wide search (Deshmukh et al., 2013) and GWAS (Dhingra et al., 2024) were investigated. Relatively, higher expression of influx Si transporters GmNIP2-1 and GmNIP2-2 was observed in epidermal and vascular cells of control (Si–) leaves compared with those subjected to $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment (Figure 6a and Figure S15). The reduced expression of Lsi1 influx transporters (- GmNIP2-1 and GmNIP2-2) in $\mathsf { S i + }$ -treated samples suggests a potential gradient-based transport mechanism, similar to observations in rice seedling tissues (Mitani et al., 2008). Additionally, it has been observed that following Si accumulation in the leaves, Si does not move to other tissues (Mitani et al., 2008), and therefore, the active supply is halted by downregulation of both Lsi1 and Lsi2 genes in rice (Mitani et al., 2008; Mitani-Ueno et al., 2016). Recently, the new leaf-specific Si efflux transporter (OsSiet4) has been identified as a regulator of Si distribution in rice (Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). The knockout mutant of OsSiet4 exhibited severe wilting and lethality in the presence of $\mathsf { S i + }$ due to disturbed and abnormal Si accumulation in rice leaf mesophyll cells (Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). Although the mechanism of Si efflux transporters has not been previously studied in dicots, we found that GmHiSil2c (homolog of rice OsSiet4) was exclusively expressed upon $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment in soybean leaf epidermal cells (Figure 6a), and its protein was localized to the cell membrane (Figure 6b). 

To functionally validate the role of GmHiSil2c, we used CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene knockout via both whole-plant and composite hairy root transformation. While we successfully recovered T0 plants carrying targeted mutations in GmHiSil2c, we were unable to detect transmission of these mutations to the next generation. This outcome suggests a potential biological constraint associated with the gene’s function and its role in Si homeostasis, particularly under Sisupplemented conditions (Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). However, the precise cause of transmission failure remains unclear. To fully understand this, additional experiments such as phenotypic assessment of edited lines under Sideficient conditions, detailed reproductive and histological analyses, and generation of conditional knockouts or tissuespecific knockdown lines are needed. Nevertheless, the composite hairy root system was successfully used to assess the 

role of these Si transporter genes (Figure 6d,e). As predicted based on tissue-specific (leaf) expression (Figure 6a and Figure S15b), the composite hairy root CRISPR mutants of GmHiSil2c (Figure 6e) showed no difference in Si accumulation in leaves compared with EV (Figure 6f). Whereas the CRISPR knockout mutant in roots of GmHisil2b, identified as a root-specific transporter (Figure S15b) (Dhingra et al., 2024), exhibited a significant reduction in Si accumulation in leaf tissue. In correlation with expression analysis, this outcome was expected because the gene knockouts were performed in root tissues, whereas the shoots remained wild-type. Since GmHiSil2c is primarily expressed in leaves and not in roots (Figure 6a and Figure S15b), knocking it out in roots had little impact on overall Si transport. However, GmHiSil2b functions as an active efflux Si transporter in roots, and knocking it out disrupted Si translocation, uptake, and subsequent transport to aerial tissues. In rice, multiple Si transporters, such as OsLsi1, OsLsi2, and OsSIET4 play tissue-specific roles in Si uptake, transport, and export in the leaf tissue, suggesting that a coordinated network of transporters is essential for efficient Si mobilization and accumulation (Huang & Ma, 2024; Ma et al., 2006; Ma et al., 2007; Mitani-Ueno et al., 2023). Furthermore, to validate the Si transport activity of GmHiSil2b and GmHiSil2c, we employed the Xenopus laevis oocyte expression system, a well-established heterologous system widely used for functional characterization of plant transporters, including Si transporters (Chen et al., 2017; Deshmukh et al., 2013; Ma et al., 2006). This system provides a controlled environment for assessing transporter activity by enabling the expression of candidate genes in oocytes, followed by functional assays to quantify their transport capacity, including efflux activity. Our findings from the oocyte assays (Figure 6c) provide direct evidence of Si efflux activity for GmHiSil2c. These findings substantiate that GmHisil2c functions as a leafspecific efflux Si transporter. 

In conclusion, this study illuminates the significant impact of Si uptake on soybean physiology, particularly transcriptional priming in enhancing defense responses. Utilizing snRNA-seq, we identified distinct cellular populations and novel Si-induced clusters within vascular cells, indicating a specialized mechanism for Si distribution in dicot species. Si treatment notably induced the expression of defense-related genes, with a pronounced effect in vascular cells, underscoring their crucial role in plant defense. Key biological processes involved in phytoalexin biosynthesis, SA and immune receptor signaling substantiated a transcriptional priming of defense pathways. The differential expression patterns of putative Si transporters highlight an active and gradient-based Si transport mechanism. Additionally, functional validation using targeted gene editing, subcellular localization, and a Xenopus oocyte assay confirmed the leaf and root-specific role of efflux Si transporters. These findings provide a deeper 

understanding of the biotic stress regulatory networks modulated by Si in soybean, offering pragmatic solutions for enhancing crop stress tolerance and productivity through strategic Si utilization. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant material, growth conditions, hydroponic system, and silicon treatment

The soybean cultivar (cv) Hikmok (PI 372415) was used in this study. Seeds were sterilized using $\mathsf { C l } _ { 2 }$ gas $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m }$ bleach, $5 \mathrm { \ m l }$ 37% HCl in a closed seal-proof chamber in a chemical hood) overnight and grown on plates containing sterile germination paper soaked with ½ MS media containing $1 \%$ sucrose and a ${ \mathsf { p H } } 5 . 7$ for 5 days. Subsequently, seedlings were transplanted onto hydroponic trays containing a solution with half-strength Hoagland media (Hoagland & Arnon, 1950) at a pH of ~5.7. The hydroponic system was continuously aerated using an air pump to prevent oxygen deficiency. The hydroponic setup was established in a growth chamber with a photoperiod of 16/8 light/dark, providing 350 parabolic aluminized reflector (PAR) light, a relative humidity of $6 5 \%$ , and a day/night temperature regime of $2 6 / 2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The nutrient solution in the hydroponic system was replenished once every 5 days. After 2 weeks of seedling growth, the Si treatment was performed (Rasoolizadeh et al., 2020). We used potassium silicate $( \mathsf { K } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S i O } _ { 3 } ,$ trade name Kasil 6, PQ Corp) as the Si source at a concentration of $3 7 7 . 4 { \mathrm { ~ m g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ , thus providing 100 ppm $\mathsf { S i O } _ { 2 }$ (1.7 mM Si) and $1 . 0 1 \mathrm { \ m M \ K ^ { + } }$ (Chiba et al., 2009; Ma & Yamaji, 2008). The same amount of potassium $( 1 . 0 1 ~ \mathsf { m M } )$ was supplied to the Si (control) samples using 75.6 mg/L KCl. We detected similar content of $\mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ in the Si ( )- and $\mathsf { S i } ( + )$ -treated plant leaf samples grown in KCl and ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf S i O } _ { 3 }$ supplemented media (Figure S1), indicating the functionality and accuracy of the treatments. The concentration of 1.7 mM Si was determined based on our previous studies, which showed that higher accumulation of Si is observed, followed by polymerization, and Si availability is reduced at higher concentrations (Arsenault-Labrecque et al., 2012; Deshmukh et al., 2020; Thakral et al., 2024). 

# Nuclei extraction, snRNA-seq library preparation, and sequencing

Nuclei isolation was performed from $\mathsf { S i } ( - ) \cdot$ and $\mathsf { S i } ( + )$ -treated soybean leaf tissues as previously described (D’Agostino et al., 2023). Briefly, the third leaf from three different soybean plants were collected from hydroponically grown plants treated with $\mathsf { S i } { + } ( 1 . 7 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { K } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S i } 0 _ { 4 }$ ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf S i O } _ { 4 }$ for 7 days) and Si (1.7 mM KCl as a control for 7 days) (see Materials and Methods section for details). Leaf samples were then immediately ground in a nuclei isolation buffer and incubated briefly $( 2 { - } 3 \mathsf { m i n } )$ ) on a shaker at 60 rpm. After incubation, the nuclei suspension was passed through 100, 70, 30, and $1 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ cell strainers. The suspension was spun at $\boldsymbol { 1 0 0 0 } \ \mathfrak { g }$ for 5 min. The nuclei pellet was resuspended in wash buffer and spun again at $\boldsymbol { 1 0 0 0 } \ \mathfrak { g }$ for 2 min. The nuclei were stained with propidium iodide and counted with the Luna-FL cell counter (Logos Biosystems, Annandale, VA, USA). The nuclei were then loaded onto the chip as per the manufacturer’s recommendations $1 0 \times$ Genomics, Pleasanton, CA, USA). Library construction for Illumina sequencing was performed with the ChromiumTM Single Cell ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ Library & Gel Bead Kit v3.1 protocol $1 0 \times$ Genomics). The single-indexed sequencing and paired-end libraries were carried out on an IlluminaTM NovaSeq 6000 platform according to the $1 0 \times$ Genomics manual. 

# SnRNA-seq data preprocessing, integration, and clustering

Sequencing data were processed through Cell Ranger (v7.2.0) to obtain cell feature counts for analysis. The data were aligned to the Glycine max Wm82.a4.v1 genome. Count data were further analyzed within the R package Seurat v4 (Hao, Hao, et al., 2021). Nuclei were filtered according to the following criteria: RNA gene count ${ > } 2 0 0$ , UMI count ${ \tt s 4 0 0 }$ and $< 1 0 \ 0 0 0$ , mitochondrial reads $< 5 \%$ per cell, and chloroplast reads $< 3 0 \%$ per cell. Doublets were removed with scDblFinder (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/ articles/PMC9204188/). The data were then normalized by SCTransform and the samples were integrated using 3000 features to reduce batch effect and identify conserved cell types. Dimension reduction of the integrated data was performed by PCA with RunPCA and the UMAP plots were generated using PCs 1–40. Leiden clustering was applied (resolution 0.8) to identify the potential cell types. Ubiquitous/non-descript clusters were removed manually and the UMAP and clustering were re-run according to previous specifications. 

# Annotation of cell types by utilizing ortholog gene markers and gene ontology (GO) analysis

Annotation of cell clusters was performed through orthologous expression of Arabidopsis genes and GO term analysis. Markers for each cluster were generated with “FindAllMarkers” at the following specifications: min.pct $= 0 . 2 5$ , logfc.threshold $= 0 . 2 5$ min.diff.pct $= 0 . 1 5$ . Arabidopsis orthologs were then compared with (Kim et al., 2021) and the Leaf SC atlas (Tenorio Berrıo et al., 2022) (https://www.psb.ugent.be/sc-leaf-yieldlab/) to identify cell types. ClusterProfiler (Yu et al., 2011) was used to perform GO term enrichment analysis with default parameters. 

# Differential gene expression and gene ontology analysis

DEGs between control (Si–) and the $\mathsf { S i + }$ treatment were found with FindMarkers with the following parameters: min.pct $= 0 . 2 5$ , logfc.- threshold $= 0 . 2 5$ , min.diff.pct = 0.15, group.by $=$ sample, subset.ident $=$ cluster no. GO analysis for treatment induced differentially enriched GO terms was performed with ClusterProfiler. 

# RNA extraction and gene expression analysis

Total RNA extraction and purification were carried out from leaves and roots of $\mathsf { S i } ( + )$ and $\mathsf { S i } ( - ) \cdot$ - treated soybean plants after 1- and 12-day in hydroponics (as explained in the above section) using Trizol reagent, followed by the utilization of the Pure Link RNA Mini Kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). For each treatment (Si– and $\mathsf { S i } { + } \mathsf { I }$ , four biological replicates were obtained from the same hydroponic experiment, with each biological replicate containing leaf tissue pooled from three individual plants. Subsequently, cDNA synthesis and qRT-PCR procedures were conducted following previously established protocols (Devkar et al., 2020; Le et al., 2012). The PCRs were executed using the C1000 Touch PCR with the CFX96 C1000 touch Real-Time Optics Module thermal cycler qRT-PCR System (BIO-RAD, USA). The qRT-PCR primers were designed using the QuantPrime online tool. GmElf1b (Glyma.02 g276600) served as the internal control for gene expression analysis (Le et al., 2012). All the qRT-PCR primers are listed in Table S1. Expression data were normalized by subtracting the mean CT value of the reference gene from the mean CT value of the gene of interest (DCT). Relative expression was calculated as: Relative expression $= 2 ^ { - \Delta \mathsf { C } \mathsf { t } }$ . The determination of statistical significance was carried out using the student’s t-test. 

# Silicon estimation by SEM-EDS

Silicon content analysis in soybean leaves was conducted through scanning electron microscopy–energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDS) as described (Dhingra et al., 2024). The analysis was conducted at an accelerating voltage of $2 0 ~ \mathsf { k V }$ and $1 4 0 \ \mathsf { P a }$ under the variable pressure mode using a Hitachi S-3400 microscope (Tokyo, Japan) equipped with an Oxford EDS system. 

# Silicon estimation by XRF

The plant tissues were subjected to a week-long drying process at $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , after which they were finely pulverized using the Tissue Lyser II (Qiagen, Germantown, MD, USA, Cat. No. /ID: 85300). Subsequently, ${ \sim } 3 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ pellets were prepared from the dried and finely ground samples using a 15 T hydraulic press with a $1 3 \mathsf { m m }$ diameter (Athena Technology, Mumbai, MS, India). Portable X-ray fluorescence (pXRF) analyzer (Bantam Axon; Olympus, Center Valley, PA, USA) was used to quantify mineral element accumulation in each sample as previously described (Otaka et al., 2014). Briefly, the pXRF was set on the analysis mode (Vegetation Analysis) specifically optimized for plant samples by the manufacturer (Bantam Axon; Olympus). The NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) samples, including Apple Leaves (NIST1515), Peach Leaves (NIST1547), and Pine Needles (NIST-1575) with known mineral concentrations were used to generate user factors for each element and as standard control to check the reproducibility of the XRF analysis. 

# Leaf anatomy, staining, and imaging

To capture the effects of Si treatment on leaf vasculature, especially xylem, soybean (cv. Hikmok) plants were grown as described above in $\mathsf { S i + }$ and Si– media for a week. After 1 week, the leaf midribs were sectioned with a sharp razor blade using a dissection microscope. For each treatment, four biological replicates (i.e., four different plants) were used, and eight midrib sections from two different leaves (eight technical replicates/ biological replicate) were collected per biological replicate. Sections were stained with $0 . 1 \%$ (w/v) berberine hemisulfate (Sigma, C.I. no. 75160) to stain lignin in xylem cell walls as previously described by Brundrett et al. (Brundrett et al., 1988). Finally, leaf midrib sections were transferred to $50 \%$ (v/v) glycerol for microscopic observation. Midrib leaf sections were imaged under $\mathsf { E V O S } ^ { \mathsf { T M } }$ M5000 Imaging System at $4 8 8 \ \mathsf { n m }$ excitation and $5 2 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ emission wavelengths. The distance between xylem vessels was calculated using Fiji ImageJ software. 

# Generation of constructs and genotyping

The Gateway recombination compatible C-terminal GFP fusion destination modular vector pMod_A218-pCmYLCV:R1-CCDB-R2-GFP: HspT was made in the laboratory. Briefly, the CmYLCV (Cestrum yellow leaf curling virus) promoter amplicon from the pMod_B2103 vector, an R1-CCDB-R2 amplicon from the pEarleyGate 100 vector, and a GFP-HSPTerminator amplicon from the pMod_C3003 vector were amplified by PCR and assembled by using NEB HiFi assembly master mix (E2621S, NEB, Ipswich, MA, USA). Later, by employing Gateway cloning (www.lifetechnologies.com), GmNIP2-1, GmNIP2- 2, GmHiSil2c genes (genes synthesized from Twist Bioscience, San Francisco, CA, USA) were cloned into the Pd221 entry vector by using the BP clonase enzyme. Next, entry clones were moved to a GFP fusion destination modular vector pMod_A218 by using the LR clonase enzyme (Invitrogen, Waltham, MA, USA). 

For genome editing by CRISPR/Cas9, Golden Gate construct modules, such as A0521 (AtCas9), B2103b (cloning multiple 

gRNAs), C3001 (GFP marker) and pTRANS230d (binary vector) were used (Cerm ak et al., 2017). Arabidopsis codon optimized Cas9 was expressed under the control of a strong soybean constitutive promoter GmUbi. Multiple guide RNAs (gRNAs), each containing a gRNA scaffold, are expressed under the control of the CmYLCV promoter and are separated by the Csy4 RNA processing system. GFP was expressed under the enhanced CaMV 35S promoter, respectively. To knockout GmHisil2b and GmHiSil2c, gRNAs were designed by the CRISPR-P tool (Liu et al., 2017). High-ranking gRNAs were chosen with no possible off-targets (Liu et al., 2017). Two gRNAs were designed for each gene to induce larger deletions. For GmHiSil2b, the two gRNAs were spaced 156 base pairs (bp) apart, while for GmHiSil2c, they were separated by 398 bp. We used a multiplex gRNA cloning protocol and vector B2103b to assemble dual gRNAs for GmHiSil2b and GmHiSil2c, as previously described (Cerm ak et al., 2017). All vector cassettes (A0521, B2103b and C3001) were assembled together in binary vector pTRANS230d by employing Golden Gate cloning method utilizing type II restriction enzyme AarI as previously described by Cermak et al. (Cerm ak et al., 2017). Amplicons of CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing target genes (GmHiSil2b and GmHiSil2c) were amplified and cloned into the pJET1.2 vector by using the Clone-JET PCR Cloning Kit (ThermoFisher, Waltham, MA, USA) and different clones were sequenced. For Xenopus oocyte assay of Si transporters, we synthesized GmHiSil2b and GmHiSil2c genes were synthesized from Twist Bioscience, USA and sub-cloned into vector pd221 as an entry clone using BP clonase enzyme. Later, these transporter genes were mobilized into Gateway compatible oocyte-specific expression vector pOO2-GW (Addgene plasmid no. 135097) destination vector using LR clonase. The SP6 promoter drives the expression of transporter genes in oocytes. All the primers used for cloning and genotyping are listed in Table S1. All the constructs were confirmed by sequencing whole plasmids by Oxford Nanopore Technology from Plasmidsaurus (Eugene, OR, USA). 

# Generation of composite hairy roots and Si treatment

Composite soybean plants with GmHiSil2b- and GmHiSil2c-edited roots were generated using Agrobacterium rhizogenes (strain, K599) mediated transformation according to the established method (Fan et al., 2020). After K599 inoculation, the plants were sealed and placed in a growth chamber maintained at $2 6 \%$ with a 16-h light $( 3 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } / \mathsf { s } ^ { 1 } ) / 8 \cdot \mathsf { h }$ dark photoperiod for generation of hairy roots. Once the roots developed, the plants were moved to the greenhouse. Under fluorescent light, composite plants with transformed hairy roots were selected, and any untransformed roots were removed. Finally, plants with uniform roots were transplanted into small pots. After 1 week of seedling growth, Si treatments were performed by applying $1 . 7 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ of ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf S i O } _ { 4 }$ (silicon treatment; $\mathsf { S i + }$ for 5 days, while $1 . 7 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ of KCl (control, Si–) was used as a potassium control. Later, Si content was measured in leaves by XRF. 

# Protoplast preparation and transformation

Tobacco protoplasts were isolated from the leaves of Nicotiana benthamiana and transiently transformed as described by (Nwoko et al., 2023; Yoo et al., 2007). We used $2 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ protoplasts per transfection reaction. An equal concentration $( 1 5 ~ \mu 9 )$ of plasmids was used for the localization experiments. 

# Laser scanning confocal microscopy

Protoplasts, expressing fluorescent constructs transiently, were observed using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Olympus 

IX83) within their culture medium $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after transformation. GFP fluorescence was detected utilizing a 488 nm argon ion laser line, with emission recorded using a 500–540 nm filter set. The chlorophyll autofluorescence was detected using the fluorophore set at 647 nm and emission recorded at 650–750 nm filter set. Confocal microscope images were processed in Fiji ImageJ software (Schindelin et al., 2012). 

# cRNA synthesis and efflux assay of Si transporters in Xenopus laevis oocytes

The cRNA synthesis and oocyte injections were performed as previously described (Chen et al., 2017). Briefly, the capped mRNA (cRNA) was synthesized in vitro using SP6 RNA polymerase and the mMESSAGE mMACHINE kit (Ambion, Inc., Austin, TX, USA), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Healthy oocytes at stage V or VI were chosen for injection with 50 nl of cRNA $( 1 \ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mu | ^ { - 1 } )$ and equal volume of RNase-free water injected as a negative control. The oocytes were kept in Modified Barth’s Saline solution (MBS, pH 7.4) at $\textstyle 1 8 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 2 days until the implementation of the assays. After 2 days of cRNA injection, 50 nl of $1 \ \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { G e } ( \mathsf { O H } ) _ { 4 }$ (as the analog of $S i ( \mathrm { O H } ) _ { 4 } ;$ Nikolic et al., 2007, Chen et al., 2017) was injected into each oocyte, respectively, and then incubated further in MBS (pH 7.4) for 6 h, then washed thoroughly with MBS and digested with ${ \mathsf { H N O } } _ { 3 }$ at $\mathfrak { g o c }$ for 60 min. Later, the Ge left in oocytes was measured by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS).
