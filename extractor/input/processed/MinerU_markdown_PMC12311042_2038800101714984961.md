Yu Zheng1,2,4, Chun Bao1,2,4, Jingxian Fang2,4, Bolei Chen1,3, Xiaoxi Yang3 , Jianbo Shi 3 , Meiling Su2 , Zhenting Li2 , Yun Yin2 , Changli Zeng2 & Yong Liang 1 

Bisphenol A (BPA) is a widely used chemical that is commonly found in soils and surface waters worldwide and acts as an oxidative stressor on plants. Our understanding of how BPA exposure affects cellular redox homeostasis remains limited. We discovered that the epigenetic reprogramming of melatonin biosynthesis helps defend against oxidative stress induced by BPA in Arabidopsis. Five key BPA-responsive genes were identified, with AT1G26220 (encoding SNAT2) showing exceptional specificity to BPA and its analogs in various tissues. BPA binding to consecutive M1-M2 elements in the AT1G26220 promoter led to decreased H3K9ac and H3K14ac levels, subsequently significantly reducing AT1G26220’s expression. It alters the melatonin biosynthesis pathway in order to maintain the redox homeostasis in plants by increasing the cellular antioxidative capacity and alleviating specific BPA-induced cellular damage. This epigenetic reprogramming changes the melatonin biosynthesis pathway, enhancing the dynamic redox homeostasis in plants. Additionally, this specific oxidative response in plants reduces the harmful effects of BPA to some extent. Our study finds a sophisticated and structure-specific epigenetic response to BPA in plants, providing a solid molecular mechanism that enhances our understanding of plant resistance to BPA at the cellular level and contributes to assessing BPA risks globally. 

Bisphenol A (BPA) is widely used as a monomer to produce polycarbonate plastics and epoxy resins and as additive in various materials. Its release and accumulation in the environment are escalating due to increased demand for BPA-containing materials and improper disposal worldwide1 . BPA has been globally detected in soils, surface waters, air, and flora exposed to effluent and sludge from sewage treatment2 . BPA has been shown to exert toxic effects on plant growth and development, including reproductive toxicity, reduced mineral nutrient acquisition, inhibited absorptive capacity, impaired photosynthesis and growth, and disrupted hormone synthesis3 . Contrast to animals, low doses of BPA may benefit certain plants4,5 , while high doses of BPA can also be harmful to plants6,7 . Recently, more and more studies suggested that the diverse effect of BPA, ranging from stimulating 

growth to inhibiting development in the short term or survival in the long term, depends on the plant species7 . Although plants' complex and varied responses have prompted numerous studies on understanding of BPA uptake, biochemical distribution, biotransformation and physiological processes in plants8 , the inner molecular mechanism and cellular targets of BPA in plants remain relatively understudied7 . Therefore, parsing the cellular responsive network to BPA in detail and identifying BPA-specific conserved biomarkers are essential for evaluating the risk of environmental pollution to plant populations and agricultural production9,10. 

As well known, BPA contamination disrupts the dynamic redox homeostasis and cellular functions both in plants and animals11. In plants, BPA exposure stimulates ROS production12,13, reducing protein content due

Additionally, BPA affects endogenous melatonin levels, a compound known to mitigate abiotic stress and BPA phytotoxicity in plants17. Melatonin biosynthesis in plants exhibits remarkable plasticity, employing distinct constitutive and stress-adapted pathways. Under homeostasis, chloroplast-localized enzymes synthesize baseline melatonin to regulate circadian rhythms and growth. During oxidative stress, plants activate alternative isoforms that redirect synthesis toward mitochondria and cytoplasm, producing specialized melatonin derivatives with 3-fold higher antioxidant activity. This metabolic switching —regulated epigenetically through promoter acetylation—enables tissuespecific detoxification, as observed in roots where BPA triggers 10-fold melatonin surges compared to shoots18,19. These findings suggest that plants have evolved sophisticated protective mechanisms against BPA-induced stress. The plant antioxidant system regulates cellular functions through multiple pathways, including modulating enzyme activity, instigating epigenetic modifications, changing transcription factor targets and orchestrating gene expression 20,21 . To maintain redox homeostasis under various environmental stresses, plants synthesize different antioxidants or produce the same antioxidant through alternative pathways. This adaptability results in species- and developmental-specific responses to stressors like BPA22. 

Plant diversity complicates the identification of key pathways involved in responses to global emerging chemical pollutant like BPA and its analogues16,23. This study investigates the role of epigenetic changes in the redox signaling pathway on plant responses to BPA. Using an integrative approach developed in this study, five important BPA-responsive genes were identified, with AT1G26220 (encoding SNAT2) showing high sensitivity to BPA. The binding of BPA to consecutive M1-M2 assemblies inhibited promoter acetylation and subsequently transcription of AT1G26220, leading to a shift in melatonin biosynthesis. The specific high sensitivities of AT1G26220 to BPA and its analogous were determined through an BPA biomonitoring system based on M1-M2-AT1G26220. The consistent depression of AT1G26220’s expression in protoplasts and leaves suggested switching melatonin production to a more efficient pathway is a systemic antioxidant defense to BPA in Arabidopsis thaliana. This epigenetic reprogramming in redox metabolism found in this study enhances our understanding of how plants resist negative affect of global emerging chemical pollutants (BPA) on their development and growth24, thereby improving crop productivity in challenging environments to advancing environmental monitoring techniques25. 

# Results Exhumation of five hub BPA-responsive genes via topological evaluation on RNA-seq data at cellular level

A comprehensive network analysis of RNA-seq data from BPA-treated and control Arabidopsis thaliana protoplasts revealed a BPA-responsive network comprising 2,238 nodes and 52,590 edges (Supplementary 1-2, Supplementary. Fig. 1a). Topological analysis indicated a well-connected network with efficient information sharing capabilities, characterized by short inter-node distances and low average distance (Supplementary Fig. 2a, 2c). Five hub nodes (AT3G19450, AT1G01300, AT1G19670, AT1G26220, and AT1G27730) that exceeded Q3 thresholds were identified as crucial in the plant’s BPA response (Fig. 1a). 

To elucidate underlying pathways, we simplified the network using a Graph Neural Network model26–28, resulting in a subnetwork of 96 indispensable GO terms classified into 26 functional groups (Supplementary 3, Supplementary Fig. 2c). This simplified network maintained the original structural characteristics while improving clarity and efficiency (Supplementary Fig. 2c). Topological analysis of this subnetwork 29–3 4 revealed strong associations between AT1G26220 and AT1G27730 at the network’s center (Fig. 1b). Gene expression analysis showed that BPA exposure downregulated AT1G26220, AT1G19670, and AT1G01300, while upregulating AT1G27730 and AT3G19450 (Fig. 1d). Additionally based on upstream and 

downstream relevance of biological functions (Supplementary 4), the 26 GO groups were further clustered into 5 functional clusters, with gene expression and response to oxidative stress forming a central cluster interconnected with others (Fig. 1c). Three clusters (warm colors) indicated processes conserved between animals and plants, while two (cold colors) displayed plant-specific characteristics. We validated the subnetwork using a plant detection system comprising protoplasts and BPA-sensitive vectors containing the five hub genes. Fluorescence changes upon BPA exposure were quantified in over 100 plant cells (Fig. 1e). Five distinct protoplast systems, each utilizing a single BPA-sensitive hub gene, demonstrated varying responses to increasing BPA concentrations. Quantitative analysis revealed more dramatic changes $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ in green fluorescence for AT1G26220, AT1G19670, and AT1G01300 systems compared to red fluorescence in AT1G27730 and AT3G19450 systems (Fig. 1f and Supplementary Fig. 2a). The AT3G19540 system demonstrated the strongest response, whereas the AT1G27730 system lost all red fluorescence at high BPA levels. 

These findings demonstrate that our GNN-derived subnetwork effectively illustrates the cascading signal transduction pathways in Arabidopsis thaliana’s response to BPA, encompassing both conserved and plantspecific reactions from cell surface perception to intracellular gene expression. 

# Identification and characterization of crucial motifs in BPA responsive pathway in plant

To study the slight difference among hub genes’ sensitivity (Fig. 1f, Supplementary Fig. 3b), the promoter regions of the top 100 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were analyzed, identifying two prevalent BPAresponsive motifs: TCNCA (M1, $N { = } \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { T }$ ) and CAAAAN (M2, $N { = } C / G )$ . Examination of 5 hub BPA-sensitive genes revealed that all contained at least two M1 motifs, with AT1G27730 possessing four, AT1G19670 exclusively featured M1 motifs, while others displayed varying combinations of M1 and M2 (Fig. 2a). Systems containing only M1 or M2 were evaluated for BPA detection. The M2 system showed minimal fluorescence change across BPA concentrations, while the M1 system exhibited a slight but significant decrease $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ in fluorescence following BPA exposure. However, neither system effectively differentiated between 10 and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ BPA (Fig. 2b). GO analysis indicated M1 motif associated with various biochemical reactions and processes, including transcriptional regulation, circadian rhythms, protein phosphorylation, and response to auxin stimulus, while M2 motifs were primarily linked to membrane-localized protein and signal transduction functions (Supplementary Fig. 3a). Six novel systems incorporating M1 or M2 into green-BPA-sensitive vectors were developed. These systems demonstrated reduced green fluorescence under BPA stress and improved differentiation between $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ BPA. Quantitative analysis revealed a significant, concentration-dependent decrease in fluorescence, with M1-containing systems showing greater sensitivity than M2-containing systems (Fig. 2c, Supplementary Fig. 3b). In conclusion, integrating M1 or M2 motifs with AT1G01300, AT1G26220, and AT1G19670 systems significantly enhanced BPA sensitivity. However, neither motif alone detected environmental BPA as effectively as hub BPAsensitive genes.

# M1-M2 assembly plays a critical role in sensitive response to BPA stress through epigenetic modification

ChIP-qPCR analysis revealed BPA-induced epigenetic modifications in plant genes, mirroring effects observed in animals35–37. H3K9ac marks were present in AT1G26220 and AT1G01300 promoters under BPA-free conditions, with AT1G26220 also showing H3K14ac marks. BPA exposure significantly reduced acetylation levels $\cdot p < 0 . 0 5$ , foldchange ${ > } 2$ ) in these promoters but not in AT1G19670, which lacks the M2 motif (Fig. 4a–c). Consistent M1-M2 motif combinations were identified in AT1G26220 and AT1G01300 promoters, following an M1-M2-ATG sequence within 350 bp. AT1G26220 contained two such assemblies, showing BPA-induced 

suppression of both H3K9ac and H3K14ac, while AT1G01300 had one assembly, exhibiting only H3K9ac suppression (Fig. 4a). Three green systems containing M1-M2 assemblies under varying BPA concentrations were evaluated. All systems showed decreased EGFP signals with increasing BPA $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ , confirming M1’s BPA affinity (Fig. 3). The M1-M2- AT1G26220 system demonstrated the highest sensitivity, followed by AT1G01300 and AT1G19670 (Fig. 4d). This suggests that plants possess specific M1-M2 assembly and downstream gene combinations for selective BPA response. 

To assess the M1-M2-AT1G26220 combination’s specificity, a vector simulating plant cell BPA response was constructed, incorporating a 35S promoter, BPA binding “catcher” (M1), response “amplifier” (M2), “responder” (AT1G26220), and EGFP “reporter” (Fig. 4e). Tests confirmed the system’s specificity to low concentrations of BPA and analogues (e.g., TBBPA), but not to other aromatic compounds like phenol or melatonin. These findings demonstrate that the M1-M2-AT1G26220 combination in plants can sensitively distinguish BPA from other common aromatic molecules, highlighting its potential as an effective BPA sensor. 

# Shift of melatonin biosynthesis as an early preventive mechanism for BPA stress in plants

To validate the dose-dependent induction of melatonin by BPA and its potentiation by exogenous melatonin, we conducted additional experiments across a wider concentration range. The results showed that BPA exposure increased endogenous melatonin content in both protoplasts and leaves, with this effect enhanced by exogenous melatonin application. Moever, the results of MDA level, SOD activity and GSH/GSSG ratio indicated a dose-dependent increase in BPA-induced oxidative stress in both protoplasts and leaves. Melatonin enhances SOD activity and GSH/GSSG ratio, while reducing MDA in these tissues across all tested concentrations (Fig. 5B–D) suggested the antioxidant capacity of melatonin in mitigating the BPA-induced oxidative stress in protoplasts and leaves. Although there were no statistically significant differences, the above results consistently showed that protoplasts exhibited higher sensitivity to BPA and melatonin compared to leaves, particularly at elevated BPA concentrations, which was clearly observed in DCF-DA signal and DAB staining analysis (Fig. 5E). Building upon this foundation, we leveraged Arabidopsis thaliana protoplasts and an ACFVA41 system to elucidate the early response mechanisms of plant cells to BPA exposure. Our approach, utilizing RNA sequencing of protoplasts, revealed several putative stress perception sensors associated with early BPA detection in membrane microdomains42. Notably, the significant induction of AT1G34590, a hub gene in the CDPcholine pathway, aligns with previous findings in animal studies43,44, suggesting that protoplasts serve as a robust model for investigating BPA’s impact across species45. We employed a GNN model for bioinformatic analysis, which offered distinct advantages over traditional methods40. As successfully as in the Alzheimer’s study41, our GNN model can effectively capture graph dependencies through inter-node message passing while incorporating deep topological information. This advanced method created a refined subnetwork identifying 5 hub genes and 26 key GO terms grouped into five functional categories. Within this network, gene expression and oxidative stress emerged as central nodes, intimately connected to all other biological processes in BPA-stressed cells (Fig. 1c). The prominence of oxidative stress suggests that ROS-mediated signaling, known to be primary in animals’ response to BPA17, also serves as the initial signaling cascade in plants’ cellular response to BPA exposure. It was further supported by the downregulation of AT1G01300 (Fig. 1d, f), a negative regulator of cGMPdependent methionine oxidation42, indicating an elevated redox state in BPA-stressed plant cells. 

While these findings position oxidative stress as a central orchestrator of plant BPA responses, but raise critical questions about its evolutionary conservation and spatiotemporal prioritization compared to prior studies. Previous research in whole-plant systems revealed species-specific adaptations4,6,7,46: chronic BPA exposure (14–28 days) suppressed ethylene biosynthesis and delayed flowering in Arabidopsis thaliana47, while in Oryza sativa, mitochondrial Complex I dysfunction caused irreversible $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation after $7 2 \mathrm { h } ^ { 4 8 }$ . Dose-dependent divergence was also evident— soybean activated GST-mediated detoxification ${ \le } 3 \mathrm { m g / L }$ but succumbed to lipid peroxidation ${ \geq } 6 \mathrm { m g / L } ^ { 7 }$ , whereas mung bean upregulated genistein biosynthesis as a compensatory barrier16, a mechanism absent in Arabidopsis thaliana. However, three fundamental limitations obscured mechanistic unification: (1) bulk tissue analyses masked cell-type-specific signaling hierarchies; (2) low temporal resolution $\mathord { \geq } 2 4 \mathrm { h }$ sampling) missed early molecular triggers such as dynamic epigenetic regulation; (3) plantanimal conservation of BPA sensing remained speculative39,49–51. 

Our single-cell protoplast model, coupled with 2h-resolution RNA-Seq/GNN analysis, directly addresses these gaps. First, we identified AT1G34590—a CDP-choline pathway hub gene homologous to mammalian CDS2 —as a sensor for membrane microdomain perturbation, thereby unifying BPA perception mechanisms across kingdoms52. Second, oxidative stress emerged as the dominant hub within $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ of exposure (Fig. 1c), 

preceding previously observed hormonal changes by ${ > } 1 2$ hours and mechanistically explaining dose-dependent defense prioritization (enzymatic SOD/CAT versus non-enzymatic lignin/phenolics). Crucially, GNNbased deconvolution revealed that cGMP-mediated methionine oxidation (via AT1G01300 downregulation) serves as the unifying motif linking ROS signaling in plants to endocrine disruption in animals. This systems-level insight reconciles species-specific disparities: for instance, genistein compensation likely requires slower, tissue-level ROS integration, whereas our protoplasts prioritize rapid, ubiquitous oxidative stress—a dichotomy resolvable only through single-cell temporal mapping. 

Further, while BPA-induced ROS generation has been observed across various plant species, our single-cell temporal map redefines the mechanistic hierarchy of antioxidant responses. The magnitude of ROS accumulation, antioxidant system responsiveness, and antioxidant component profiles exhibit striking spatiotemporal heterogeneity (Fig. 5). Tissuespecific ROS sources dominate this divergence: mitochondrial Complex III dysfunction drives ROS bursts in root meristems, whereas chloroplast PSII photoinhibition (Fv / Fm decline) prevails in mesophyll cells4,6,7 , developmental stage further modulates outcomes—seedlings prioritize enzymatic antioxidants (e.g., SOD, CAT), while mature plants enhance non-enzymatic defenses (e.g., lignin polymerization)21,42. Consistent with these patterns, our analysis predicted the early activation of several antioxidant pathways to maintain cellular redox homeostasis, including the tetrapyrrole pathway, indole-containing compound metabolism, amide biosynthesis, and cellular aromatic compound metabolism21,53. These predictions are corroborated by observed BPA concentration-dependent transcriptional changes in hub genes (Figs. 1e, 2c, and 4d). Intriguingly, three of these pathways converge on melatonin biosynthesis, with AT1G26220 (SNAT2), a mitochondrial membrane-localized rate-limiting enzyme in melatonin synthesis44, occupying a central position within the subnetwork. SNAT2’s centrality reflects its dual roles: direct quenching of mitochondrial ROS via melatonin’s methoxy-indole scaffold, and indirect amplification of antioxidant enzyme expression through MAPK-Nrf2/ARE signaling45. This mechanism mirrors animal models where BPA impair oxidative stress responses46, providing compelling evidence for evolutionary conservation in early cellular response to BPA exposure across kingdoms.

In our study, we uncovered a remarkable adaptive mechanism in plants that rapidly modify melatonin biosynthesis in response to BPA stress. This process serves dual critical functions: maintaining cellular redox homeostasis and preventing oxidative DNA damage through epigenetic modulation59,61,62. Under normal conditions, melatonin synthesis in plants follows a well-established pathway involving serotonin acetylation catalyzed 

by SNAT2 (encoded by AT1G26220), followed by methylation54. The transcription of AT1G26220 is tightly regulated by histone acetylation levels (H3K9ac and H3K14ac) in its promoter’s M1-M2 region. This epigenetic change results in decreased AT1G26220 transcription and appears to redirect melatonin synthesis toward an alternative, more efficient pathway. This shift in melatonin biosynthesis serves to mitigate the detrimental effects of BPA on cells (Fig. 6), highlighting the adaptability of plant cellular responses to environmental stressors53.

# Methods

# Plant materials and vector construction

The Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype Columbia (Col-0) was used as plant material for protoplast isolation. After surface sterilization in $3 0 \%$ bleach, Arabidopsis thaliana seeds were kept at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ before sowing. The 

seeds were grown in vitro on half-strength Murashige Skoog with $0 . 5 \%$ sucrose media $( \mathsf { p H } 5 . 7 , 1 . 2 \%$ agar) in a growth chamber at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under white light $( 1 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ photons) in $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ light/day photoperiods for 10 days and then collected for protoplast isolation. The full-length cDNA of hub genes was amplified and cloned into a p1301-35S-EGFP vector (Supplementary Fig. 3b) using primers listed in the Supplementary data 5. 

# Arabidopsis thaliana Protoplast Isolation, Transfection Assays, and BPA Treatment

Arabidopsis thaliana fully expanded leaves (rosette leaves of 4-week-old) were cut into small pieces ( $0 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ width; $2 \mathrm { c m }$ length) and submerged in a digestion enzyme buffer $1 . 5 \%$ Cellulase R10, $0 . 4 \%$ Macerozyme R10, 0.4 M Mannitol, 20 mM KCl, 20 mM MES, pH 5.7), $1 0 \mathrm { m M C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , and $0 . 1 \%$ BSA for 6 hours in the dark. After protoplasts were released, sufficient W5 solution (154 mM NaCl, 125 mM $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5 mM KCl, 2 mM MES, pH 5.7) was added to stop the digestion reaction. The protoplasts were centrifuged at $1 0 0 \times { \bf g }$ for 10 minutes, washed twice with $2 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of pre-chilled W5 solution, and incubated on ice for 30 minutes. They were then centrifuged and resuspended in MMG solution (0.2 M mannitol, $1 5 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , and 4 mM MES, pH 5.7) to a final concentration of $2 { - } 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ cells/ mL. # DNA Docking with BPA Molecule

The structure file of the small molecule ligand (BPA) was downloaded from the PubChem database and imported into Chem 3D v20.0 software for adjustment to a 3D structure and small molecule energy minimization using 

the MM2 force field. The output was saved as a “.pdb” format file for further analysis. The DNA double-stranded structure was constructed as a macromolecular receptor using Discovery Studio 2019. The small molecule ligand and DNA 3D structure were imported into AutoDock Tools v1.5.7 software for hydrogenation, charge number calculation, and other processes. The file was then converted into pdbqt format. Using the center of the DNA 3D structure as the docking box center, a docking box with dimensions $8 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } \times 8 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } \times 8 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } }$ was set to encompass the entire DNA. Molecular docking was performed using AutoDock Vina v1.1.2, and the results were visualized using PyMOL v2.5.4. For every complex, nine independent simulation analyses were performed (Supplementary Fig. 5a). 

MD simulations employed the Nosé-Hoover thermostat $\mathit { \Omega } _ { \overline { { \tau } } = 0 . 1 }$ ps) to maintain $3 1 0 \mathrm { K }$ with separate temperature coupling groups for DNA_BPA and Water_and_ions (V-rescale algorithm, ${ \tau } = 0 . 5 \mathrm { p s }$ and 0.1 ps, respectively), and the Parrinello-Rahman barostat $( \tau _ { - \mathrm { P } } = 2 . 0 \mathrm { p s }$ ) for isotropic pressure control (1 atm with C-rescale coupling, $\tau _ { \mathrm { - P } } = 5 . 0 \mathrm { p s }$ ). The Velocity Verlet integrator (md-vv) was used with a 2-fs time step. All trajectories were processed to eliminate periodic boundary artifacts and align the DNA molecule. First, the gmx trjconv -pbc nojump -center command was used to center the DNA backbone in the simulation box. Subsequently, rotational alignment (-fit rot+trans) was performed relative to the initial structure. Long-range electrostatics were treated via the Particle Mesh Ewald (PME) method with a Fourier grid spacing of $0 . 1 4 \mathrm { n m }$ and 4th-order interpolation, and a $1 . 2 \mathrm { n m }$ cutoff, while van der Waals interactions used a force-switch modifier for the Lennard-Jones potential from 1.0 to 1.2 nm. Hydrogen bonds were constrained using the LINCS algorithm (6th order expansion, 2 iterations) to enable stable integration. The system was solvated in a TIP3P water box extending $1 . 2 \mathrm { n m }$ beyond the solute. The overall translation and rotation of the DNA molecule are controlled through the removal of linear momentum (comm-mode=Linear) and bond length constraints (constraint $\mathrm { \Pi = H }$ -bonds). For every complex, three independent simulation analyses were performed (Supplementary Fig. 5b). Multiple comparisons were conducted to compare significant differences among samples using LSD $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ . Production runs (500 ns each) were performed with trajectory snapshots saved every 10 ns for analysis. For related parameter settings, refer to the complete mdp file in Source Data1 (including pressure coupling compressibility $= 4 . 5 e { - } 5 \ b a r ^ { - 1 }$ , PME settings, and temperature group definitions). AmberTools-2024 and GROMACS 2024.1 were used for MD simulations. 

# BPA Affinity Analysis In Vitro

Epoxy-activated Sepharose 6B resin (GE Healthcare Bio-Sciences Corp.) was utilized to immobilize BPA via ether linkages to hydroxyl groups63. The epoxy-activated resin was mixed with 20 mM BPA in a coupling buffer $5 0 \%$ dimethylformamide, pH 13.0). The BPA-resin coupling process took place overnight. Subsequently, the coupled resin was washed, and any unoccupied hydroxyl groups were blocked. A non-BPA resin was prepared using the same process but without BPA. The amount of BPA bound to the resin was estimated by measuring the unbound BPA using UV spectrometry (absorption at $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ). For the preparation of aptamers, motif sequences were synthesized and labeled with a TaqMAN probe by Biologics to create M1/M2 aptamer templates, serving as positive controls. The BPA-coupled resin and non-BPA resin were washed with a binding buffer (25 mM Tris-HCl, 100 mM NaCl, 25 mM KCl, $1 0 \mathrm { m M M g C l _ { 2 } }$ , and $5 \%$ DMSO, pH 8.0). Subsequently, 1 mM aptamers dissolved in binding buffer were incubated with both BPA-coupled and non-BPA resins for 1 h. The first eluted fluids, containing unbound DNA, were used as the negative control. Bound DNA was eluted by washing the resin with the binding buffer. Binding affinities were quantified using real-time PCR and calculated by the $2 ^ { \Delta } \mathrm { C t }$ method, with the normalized relative quantity of the positive control as 1. Various motifs were synthesized to obtain M1 and M2 dsDNA or ssDNA fragments (Supplementary data 5). These fragments were incubated with different concentrations of BPA in a binding buffer overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The mixture containing 100 ng of DNA was separated by agarose electrophoresis on a $3 \%$ agarose gel at $1 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ in $0 . 5 \times \mathrm { T A E }$ buffer. 

# RNA-Seq and RT-qPCR Experiments

Total RNA was extracted from Arabidopsis thaliana protoplasts using Trizol reagents for RNA-Seq analysis. For RNA-seq library construction, $2 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA was used for mRNA purification and cDNA synthesis. The mRNA was enriched using oligo (dT) magnetic beads and fragmented into short fragments $( \sim 2 0 0 \mathrm { b p } )$ with a fragmentation buffer. The first strand of cDNA was synthesized using a random hexamer primer. Buffer, dNTPs, RNase H, and DNA polymerase I were added to synthesize the second strand. The double-stranded cDNA was purified using magnetic beads. End reparation and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -end single nucleotide A (adenine) addition were performed, and sequencing adaptors were ligated to the fragments, which were then enriched by PCR amplification. The library products were sequenced using the Illumina HiSeq 2000 platform. And the RNA-seq data were deposited in the GSA database (GSA: subCRA025822). For quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) assays, cDNA was synthesized from $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA using the First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Transgene Biotech). Transcript levels were normalized against the average of the reference genes tubulin and actin (AT5G62690 and AT3G18780). Ct values and real-time PCR efficiencies were obtained using LinRegPCR (12.X) software. Normalized relative quantities and standard errors for each sample were calculated using qBaseplus software. The relative fold differences in gene expression among samples were determined based on normalized relative quantities, with the control as 1. Three biological replicates (protoplasts derived from independently grown plant batches) were analyzed for RNA-Seq, and three biological replicates (each with three technical repetitions) were included for RT-qPCR to ensure statistical robustness. Negative controls using double distilled water as templates were included to assess the specificity of each real-time PCR reaction. The primers were listed in Supplementary data 5. 

# ChIP-PCR Experiments

Arabidopsis thaliana protoplasts under different treatments were crosslinked in $1 \%$ formaldehyde $2 0 \mathrm { m i n } ,$ ), quenched with $1 2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ glycine, and pelleted $( 5 0 0 \times \mathrm { g }$ for 5 min). Nuclei were extracted using lysis buffer (10 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 10 mM KCl, $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { M }$ sucrose, $0 . 5 \%$ Triton X-100), and chromatin was fragmented via sonication (5 cycles of 10-sec pulses at $1 5 \%$ amplitude in $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ HEPES pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, $0 . 1 \%$ SDS) to achieve 200–500 bp fragments. Chromatin was pre-cleared with Protein A/G magnetic beads, then immunoprecipitated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with antibodies against H3K14ac (Millipore, 2686926, 1:2000) and H3K9ac (Invitrogen, PA5-117740, 1:2000). IgG (Abcam, ab171870, 1:5000) was used as a negative control. Beads were washed sequentially with low-salt, high-salt, LiCl, and TE buffers. DNA was eluted $1 \%$ SDS, 0.1 M NaHCO₃), reverse-crosslinked overnight with Proteinase K at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and purified via spin columns. The amounts of immunoprecipitated chromatin fragments relative to input chromatin were determined by qPCR analysis using primer pairs specific to target genes and calculated according to the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta } \mathrm { C T }$ method. Each test was performed with three biological replicates. The primers were listed in Supplementary data 5. 

# Bioinformatic analysis of high-throughput sequence

For bioinformatics analysis of the RNA-seq data, clean reads were obtained by removing reads containing adapters, reads containing poly-N, and lowquality reads from the raw data. Clean reads were then aligned to the Arabidopsis thaliana genome (The Arabidopsis thaliana Information Resource, http://www.Arabidopsis.org/index.jsp) using FastQC64, Subread65, featureCounts66, and Deseq67 software were subsequently employed for RNA-seq data analysis. Genes with an $\mathrm { F D R } \leq 0 . 0 1$ and Fold Change $\geq 4$ were identified as DEGs. GO enrichment analysis was performed using R (R Core Team, 2021) with the clusterProfiler package68. The p-value was calculated using a two-sided minimum-likelihood test (equivalent to Fisher’s exact test) for hypergeometric distribution. The GO database of Arabidopsis thaliana (levels 1–15) was used to construct a 

network of DEGs and GO terms as nodes via igraph69 Edges were defined by relationships between GO terms and q-values linking DEGs to GO terms. 

The GCN analysis was carried out as follows: 

1. Libraries including PyTorch70, NetworkX71, igraph, and scikit-learn72 for $\mathrm { \ t - S N E } ^ { 7 3 }$ dimensionality reduction were imported. 

2. An igraph network was created using DEGs and GO terms from the training subset, then converted to a NetworkX graph for compatibility with PyTorch Geometric 

3. The GCN model architecture comprised three GCN layers with a hidden dimension of 64, following established practices for biological network analysis74. Node features (p-values) and categories were initialized, with random features generated for demonstration. 

4. The NetworkX graph was converted to PyTorch Geometric format. Training was optimized with a learning rate of 0.001 and weight decay of 1e-4, parameters validated for stability in similar biological datasets. The loss function and training loop followed PyTorch Geometric documentation. 

5. t-SNE was applied to reduce model outputs for 2D visualization of node distributions. 

6. Use Kappa Score 0.4 for GO term fusion in the sub-network based on data from GO, KEGG, and STRING databases. Choose the GO term with the smallest p-value as the model GO term. 

7. Conduct function relation analysis of GO groups on public data experimental evidence and associated genes in model GO terms. Two GO terms are part of a cluster if the Kappa Score between them was more than 0.6. 

For bioinformatics analysis of promoters, the promoter regions of the top 100 up- or down-regulated DEGs were downloaded from the Entrez database. Rentrez75 and Biostrings76 were used for further analysis. 

# Statistics and reproducibility

Each test was performed with three independent biological replicates, ensuring robustness against biological variability. Data normality and homogeneity of variance were confirmed using Shapiro-Wilk $\left( p > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ and Levene’s tests $\left( p > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ , respectively. Only when One-way ANOVA indicated a significant overall effect $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ , Fisher’s Least Significant Difference (LSD) test was applied for post hoc pairwise comparisons. Groups with mean differences $\geq \mathrm { L S D }$ were considered statistically significant $\left( p < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . Analyses used R and SPSS, with data reported as mean $\pm \mathrm { S D }$ .
