# ABSTRACT

After germination in the dark, plants produce a shoot apical hook and closed cotyledons to protect the quiescent shoot apical meristem (SAM), which is critical for seedling survival during skotomorphogenesis. The factors that coordinate these processes, particularly SAM repression, remain enigmatic. Plant cuticles, multilayered structures of lipid components on the outermost surface of the aerial epidermis of all land plants, provide protection against desiccation and external environmental stresses. Whether and how cuticles regulate plant development are still unclear. Here, we demonstrate that mutants of BODYGUARD1 (BDG1) and long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase2 (LACS2), key genes involved in cutin biosynthesis, produce a short hypocotyl with an opened apical hook and cotyledons in which the SAM is activated during skotomorphogenesis. Light signaling represses expression of BDG1 and LACS2, as well as cutin biosynthesis. Transcriptome analysis revealed that cuticles are critical for skotomorphogenesis, particularly for the development and function of chloroplasts. Genetic and molecular analyses showed that decreased HOOK-LESS1 expression results in apical hook opening in the mutants. When hypoxia-induced expression of LITTLE ZIPPER2 at the SAM promotes organ initiation in the mutants, the de-repressed expression of cell-cycle genes and the cytokinin response induce the growth of true leaves. Our results reveal previously unrecognized developmental functions of the plant cuticle during skotomorphogenesis and demonstrate a mechanism by which light initiates photomorphogenesis through dynamic regulation of cuticle synthesis to induce coordinated and systemic changes in organ development and growth during the skotomorphogenesis-to-photomorphogenesis transition. 

Key words: plant cuticles, SAM quiescence, organ initiation, skotomorphogenesis, photomorphogenesis

# INTRODUCTION

Plants undergo skotomorphogenesis or etiolated growth when germinating under dark conditions, as in soil. This phase is characterized by an elongated hypocotyl with an apical hook and tightly closed cotyledons that contain undifferentiated chloroplast precursors; these structures protect the quiescent shoot apical meristem 

(SAM) as the seedling pushes through the soil (Arsovski et al., 2012). Upon reaching the soil surface and being exposed to light, the

seedling undergoes photomorphogenesis or de-etiolated growth, characterized by repression of hypocotyl elongation, opening of the apical hook, and unfolding and expansion of the cotyledons with maturation of the chloroplasts (Arsovski et al., 2012). Etiolated growth is conserved across many land plants, including most dicots and monocots, providing a pattern of dark development and growth. These processes are accomplished mainly by promoting the activity of the basic-helix-loophelix phytochrome-interacting factor (PIF) transcription factors and by degrading transcription factors such as ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL5 (HY5) via the ubiquitin-proteasome system mediated by constitutively photomorphogenic/de-etiolated/fused in sarcoma (FUSCA) proteins (COP/DET/FUS) (Li et al., 2011; Pham et al., 2018). Consistent with this scenario, the cop1 mutant displayed constitutive photomorphogenesis in darkness (Deng et al., 1992). The phytohormones auxin, gibberellic acid, brassinosteroids, and ethylene (ET) are also key regulators of etiolated growth, tightly controlled by dark/light signaling components like PIFs, COP1, and HY5 (Leivar and Monte, 2014). 

Soon after germination, the apical part of the hypocotyl undergoes asymmetric cell expansion and division caused by an auxin gradient between the inner and outer cells, resulting in formation of an apical hook (Raz and Koornneef, 2001; Za ´ dnı´kova´ et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2019). Acetyltransferases, like HOOKLESS1 (HLS1), act as hubs integrating various external and internal signals (Zhang et al., 2018) and are essential for apical hook formation (Lehman et al., 1996; Lyu et al., 2019). Mutation of HLS1 completely abolishes hook formation with normal hypocotyls and root elongation, showing its indispensable role in hook formation (Lehman et al., 1996). Plants monitor light from UV-B to the near infrared (far red) using several families of photoreceptors, such as phytochromes (phyA–phyE in Arabidopsis). Upon light exposure, light-activated photoreceptors cause PIF degradation and COP1 inactivation, leading to reduced hormone levels and the release of growth-suppressing proteins such as HY5 to induce photomorphogenesis. Light-regulated translocation of photoreceptors from the cytoplasm to the nucleus is a key event in the phytochrome signaling cascade (Li et al., 2011). However, mutations of a conserved GAF-domain (a domain in some of the proteins it is found in: cGMP-specific phosphodiesterases, adenylyl cyclases and FhlA) Tyr residue $( \mathsf { Y } ^ { \mathsf { G A F } } )$ in both phyA and phyB $( P H Y B ^ { Y 2 7 6 1 }$ and PHYAY242H) result in light-independent activation of gene expression and photomorphogenesis (Su and Lagarias, 2007). 

All aboveground post-embryonic organogenesis occurs through the primary SAM that is established during embryogenesis. Activation of a quiescent SAM is the key sign of success in photomorphogenesis (Arsovski et al., 2012). The SAM is dynamically maintained by a balance between stem cell renewal and organ primordium generation outcomes, in which meristem maintenance and organ generation are tightly regulated by phytohormones (Hepworth and Pautot, 2015). High levels of auxin activity are distributed in the peripheral zone, where auxin promotes cell differentiation and induces the production of organ primordia (Vernoux et al., 2010). Cytokinin (CK) activity is concentrated in the meristem region and is required for cell proliferation and maintenance of stem-cell activity (Gordon et al., 2009; Pfeiffer et al., 2016; Meng et al., 2017). The crosstalk between auxin and CK is mediated by auxin response factors (ARFs) and type A/B Arabidopsis 

response regulators (ARRs) (Zhao et al., 2010; Su et al., 2011; Schaller et al., 2015). A recent study also demonstrated that the SAM develops embedded in a low-oxygen niche and that this hypoxic condition is required for regulation of new organ production. In this process, LITTLE ZIPPER 2 (ZPR2), an N-degron pathway substrate, is protected from proteolysis, resulting in inhibition of the class III homeodomain-leucine zipper transcription factors and consequent organogenesis (Weits et al., 2019). Although research related to skotomorphogenesis and photomorphogenesis is always an active area, less attention has been paid to the mechanisms that underlie repression and activation of the SAM under dark/light switching. 

Plant cuticles, including cutin polyester and cuticular wax, are present on the outer surfaces of almost all land-plant aerial organs, serving as the first barrier to protect the plant body from environmental stresses (Wang et al., 2011; Ingram and Nawrath, 2017) (Supplemental Figure 1A). The lipophilic cutin framework is associated with hydrophobic compounds referred to as waxes, which are embedded within the cutin matrix. Cutin is a typically insoluble polyester of primarily oxygenated long-chain (16- or 18- carbon) fatty acids, with lesser amounts of glycerol, phenylpropanoids, and dicarboxylic acids. Although there are distinct variations in cutin monomer profiles across species, organs, and developmental stages, the 18C acids are a common, and often dominant, component of angiosperm cutins (Fich et al., 2016). Several important genes for cutin biosynthesis have been identified by genetic analysis (Yeats and Rose, 2013; Fich et al., 2016). Cutin monomers are synthesized by modification of plastid-derived 16C and 18C fatty acids in the endoplasmic reticulum, yielding variously oxygenated fatty acid–glycerol esters referred to as monoacylglycerols (Fich et al., 2016) (Supplemental Figure 1A). Long-chain acyl-coenzyme A (CoA) synthetase (LACS), encoded by LACS1 and LACS2, catalyzes the esterification of CoA to a fatty acid, producing acyl-CoA in the probable first step of the cutin and wax synthesis pathway (Schnurr et al., 2004; Bessire et al., 2007). BODYGUARD1 (BDG1), an ${ \alpha } / { \beta }$ -hydrolase-family protein that localizes to the outermost portion of the epidermal cell wall, is required for the last step of cutin polymerization and normal plant development (Kurdyukov et al., 2006; Jakobson et al., 2016). DEFECTIVE IN CUTICULAR RIDGES (DCR), a member of the BAHD (benzylalcohol O-acetyltransferase, anthocyanin O-hydroxycinnamoyltransferase, anthranilate N-hydroxycinnamoyl/benzoyltransferase, deacetylvindoline 4-O-acetyltransferase) family of acyltransferases, also functions in cutin polymerization using oxygenated acyl-CoA as a substrate (Panikashvili et al., 2009; Dominguez et al., 2015; Berhin et al., 2019) (Supplemental Figure 1A). The critical functions of the cuticle are to restrict transpirational water loss and epidermal cell organogenesis and to act as a pathogen barrier (Bessire et al., 2007; Panikashvili et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2011). A recent study revealed that a cuticle-like cell wall structure on plant root caps is produced shortly after seed germination, protecting the sensitive root meristem from abiotic stress and contributing to proper lateral root emergence (Berhin et al., 2019). However, the roles of cuticles in regulation of aerial organogenesis and plant development during skotomorphogenesis remain unclear.

# RESULTS

Plant cuticle-biosynthesis genes repress organ initiation and development during skotomorphogenesis 

To identify key factors involved in repression of SAM activity under dark conditions, we created and screened an ethyl 

# Plant Communications

methanesulfonate mutagenesis library of Arabidopsis. We isolated two mutants that showed a similar impaired skotomorphogenesis phenotype when grown in darkness for 4 days after germination. The mutant seedlings had an opened apical hook and cotyledons with a true leaf growing inside, as well as a slightly shorter hypocotyl but longer root. By contrast, the WT plants displayed normal etiolated growth (Figure 1A–1C). When grown for 7 days in darkness, the mutants had dramatically shorter hypocotyls than the WT, and the SAMs of the mutants were activated to produce more true leaves (Supplemental Figure 1B and 1C). These results indicated that the mutations occurred in genes whose products maintain proper skotomorphogenesis, particularly the repression of SAM activity under dark conditions. 

By backcrossing the mutants with the WT and performing bulk segregant analysis sequencing (BSA-seq), we cloned the mutated sites in both mutants. One G-to-A transition resulted in a G417D mutation at the LACS2 locus, and one C-to-T transition led to a V315M mutation at the BDG1 locus (Supplemental Figure 1D). The G417 residue of LACS2, located in the AMP-binding domain of long-chain fatty acid CoA synthetase, is conserved among species, and the V315 site is a conserved amino acid in the active-site Ser regions of various ${ \mathfrak { o } } / { \beta }$ -hydrolases (Supplemental Figure 1E) (Kurdyukov et al., 2006), indicating that lacs2 and bdg1 (called lacs2-4 and bdg1-4 hereafter) are loss-of-function mutants of BDG1 and LACS2. Both LACS2 and BDG1 are involved in plant cutin biosynthesis. We therefore hypothesized that if cutin functions in proper skotomorphogenesis, then mutants of other cutin biosynthesis genes should also exhibit phenotypes similar to those of lacs2-4 and bdg1-4. Indeed, the transfer DNA knockout mutant dcr-4 also produced seedlings with a shorter hypocotyl, opened apical hook and cotyledons, and an active SAM that produced true leaves under dark conditions, as reported previously (Wu et al., 2015) (Figure 1A and 1B and Supplemental Figure 1B, 1C, and 1F), indicating that plant cutin plays important roles in maintaining proper etiolated growth. To confirm that the mutations in BDG1 and LACS2 are responsible for the mutant phenotype, we performed allelic analysis by crossing transfer DNA insertion mutants of BDG1 (bdg1-202) and LACS2 (lacs2- 760) with bdg1-4 and lacs2-4, respectively (Supplemental Figure 1F). The F1 plants of bdg1-4/bdg1-202 and lacs2-4/lacs2- 760 also displayed the de-etiolated growth phenotype observed in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (compare Supplemental Figure 1G with Figure 1A and Supplemental Figure 1B), indicating that the identified mutation sites in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 are responsible for the developmental defects of the mutants. 

To determine whether plant cuticle biosynthesis was impaired in the mutants, we first measured cuticle permeability by toluidine blue (TB) staining. Cuticular integrity was assessed by observing inward permeability to the aqueous TB stain (Jakobson et al., 2016). When etiolated seedlings were immersed in TB stain buffer, lacs2-4 and bdg1-4 displayed a staining phenotype characteristic of an impaired cuticle (Supplemental Figure 1H). We then inspected the outermost cell wall of the SAM epidermis of etiolated seedlings by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). In electron micrographs, the cuticles of the WT SAM were seen as a continuous and regular layered structure in which an outer electron-dense membrane corresponding to the cuticle proper was deposited over a less dense layer corresponding to the cuticle layer (Figure 1D). By contrast, the SAM cuticle structure 

of the mutants was abnormal, characterized by a series of irregular layers in which the electron-dense cuticle proper was intermingled with empty spaces (indicated by arrowheads in Figure 1D), indicative of disruption and/or absence of the cuticle proper, consistent with the altered cuticle permeability and with previous reports (Panikashvili et al., 2009; Jakobson et al., 2016). Finally, we analyzed the cutin monomer content in 4-day-old etiolated seedlings of bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and the WT, excluding hypocotyls and roots. We found that the content of C18:1, C18:2, and C18:3 dicarboxylic acids (DCAs) was lower in etiolated seedlings of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 than in those of the WT, whereas accumulation of C18:0 and C16:0 DCAs was unchanged (Figure 1E), consistent with a previous report showing that BDG1 functions mainly in the synthesis of C18 unsaturated monomers (Jakobson et al., 2016). These results demonstrated that cuticle synthesis was indeed impaired in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4. 

# Light signaling represses cuticle biosynthesis

To investigate the functions of the plant cuticle in skotomorphogenesis, we first examined the expression of LACS2, BDG1, and DCR in seedlings of the dark-grown WT and found that their expression gradually increased after germination in the dark (Figure 2A). Because light inhibits skotomorphogenesis and plant cuticles maintain normal etiolated growth, we hypothesized that light represses cuticle biosynthesis. We grew WT seedlings under dark conditions for 4 days, transferred them to light conditions at different time points as shown in Figure 2B, and examined gene expression and cutin biosynthesis using cotyledons and the SAM, including the apical hook. Compared with seedlings under continuous light conditions, seedlings grown in the dark showed much higher LACS2, BDG1, and DCR expression. However, under light conditions, the expression of LACS2, BDG1, and DCR was dramatically and quickly repressed, indicating that light indeed represses the expression of genes related to cutin biosynthesis (Figure 2B). The light signal is perceived by photoreceptors such as phyA–phyE and mediated by COP/DET/FUS proteins (Li et al., 2011). The activated versions of PHYBY276 H and PHYAY242H induce photomorphogenesis in darkness (Su and Lagarias 2007). We therefore examined gene expression in PHYBY276H, PHYAY242H, cop1-4, and WT plants grown in the dark for 4 days. Expression of LACS2, BDG1, and DCR was significantly lower in PHYBY276H, PHYAY242H, and cop1-4 compared with the WT (Figure 2C). By contrast, their expression was higher in phyA-201 and phyB-5 but unchanged in cop1-4 compared with the WT under continuous light conditions (Figure 2D). These results indicate that light signaling represses LACS2, BDG1, and DCR expression. We also grew WT seedlings under 6-day dark conditions and 4-day dark plus 2-day light conditions, then analyzed cutin synthesis using cotyledons and the SAM, including the apical hook. Consistent with our earlier results, lower contents of C18:1, C18:2, and C18:3 DCAs were detected in WT seedlings grown in the light, similar to those of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 seedlings grown in darkness, indicating that cutin biosynthesis is repressed during photomorphogenesis (Figure 2E).

# Plant cuticles regulate HLS1 expression and auxin activity in the apical hook and hypocotyl development

The apical hook is formed shortly after seed gemination, and HLS1, responding to auxin, plays an important role (Lehman et al., 1996; Abbas et al., 2013). To understand why the apical hook was opened in bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and dcr-4 seedlings, we first examined apical hook formation during skotomorphogenesis. WT seedlings formed and maintained an apical hook 30 h after germination. By contrast, the apical hook of bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and dcr-4 formed at 36–42 h but opened 72 h after germination (Supplemental Figure 3A), showing that formation and maintenance of the apical hook were impaired in bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and dcr-4. We noted that expression of HLS1 was decreased in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Supplemental Table 1), and we confirmed this finding by RT– qPCR and B-glucuronidase (GUS) staining using HLS1::GUS reporter lines (Supplemental Figure 3B–3D). Genetic analysis showed that overexpression of HLS1, which enhanced apical hook formation as reported previously (Lehman et al., 1996), could rescue the opened apical hook of bdg1-4, indicating that decreased HLS1 expression was responsible for the impaired hook development of bdg1-4 (Supplemental Figure 3E). Given that asymmetric cell expansion and division due to asymmetric distribution of auxin activity are required for apical hook formation (Raz and Koornneef 2001; Za ´ dnı´kova´ et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2019), we examined the local auxin output at the apical hook region using the DR5::GUS reporter gene. In the WT, the auxin response was higher at the inner cell layer than at the outer cell layer of the hook, whereas no obvious auxin activity was detected at the hook region of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Supplemental Figure 3F). Surprisingly, we detected higher auxin activity in the SAM of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 than in that of the WT (Supplemental Figure 3F). RT–qPCR verification using tissue from the hook region only showed specific and increased GUS expression in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 compared with the WT (Supplemental Figure 3G).

# Plant cuticles repress CK activity and cell division during skotomorphogenesis

Auxin and CK antagonistically regulate cell differentiation and proliferation in the SAM. We found that the auxin response was increased in the SAMs of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Supplemental Figure 3F and 3G). To examine whether induction of auxin at the SAM promotes leaf production and growth, we treated bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and the WT with different concentrations of the auxin biosynthesis inhibitors auxinole and yucacin. Both treatments had little effect on the production and growth of true leaves in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Supplemental Figure 5A and 5B), although they did effect hypocotyl elongation (Supplemental Figures 4E, 4F, 5C, and 5D), indicating that the altered leaf initiation and growth of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 were auxin independent. 

At the same time, we noticed that expression levels of key genes in CK biosynthesis, signaling, and degradation were altered in the mutants compared with the WT (Figure 4A; Supplemental Table 4). We verified the expression of these genes in the mutants and WT by RT–qPCR. Expression levels of the two CK biosynthesis genes ISOPENTENYLTRANSFERASE5/7 (IPT5/7) and the CK receptor ARABIDOPSIS HISTIDINE KINASE4 were significantly enhanced in both bdg1-4 and lacs2-4. The A-type ARRs (ARR3/4/5/6/7/8/9/ 15/16/17) negatively regulate the CK response, whereas the B-type ARRs (ARR1/2/10/11/12/13/14/18/19/20/21) promote it (Kieber and Schaller, 2014). Here, we detected decreased expression of ARR8/9 and increased expression of ARR10/11/14 (Figure 4B), which were indicative of increased CK response in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4. These results indicate that plant cuticles also systemically regulate the CK response during skotomorphogenesis. Because IPT5/7 expression was dramatically increased in the 

mutants, we next treated bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and the WT with the CK biosynthesis inhibitor lovastatin (Figure 4A and 4B). Lovastatin treatment largely rescued the premature leaf initiation and growth defects of lacs2-4 and bdg1-4 (Figure 4C and 4D). We also performed genetic analysis by crossing bdg1-4 with ipt3/5/7 and found that mutations in IPT3/5/7 could also complement the altered leaf initiation and growth of bdg1-4 (Figure 4E and 4F), indicating that de-repressed CK activity contributes to the leaf initiation and growth of bdg1-4. 

Organ initiation and growth are the results of cell division, which is controlled by cell-cycle genes (Dante et al., 2014). RNA-seq analysis and RT–qPCR verification revealed that expression of most key cell-cycle genes was activated in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Figure 5A and 5B; Supplemental Table 5), in line with the activated leaf initiation and growth in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4. Consistent with these results, the SAM and root apical meristem (RAM) of CYCB1;1::GUS bdg1-4 and CYCB1;1::GUS lacs2-4 displayed strong GUS staining compared with CYCB1;1::GUS under dark conditions (Figure 5C), showing active cell division in the SAM and RAM of bdg1-4 and lacs2-4. The D-type cell cycle genes, including CYCD3;1 and CYCD3;3, are critical for the G1/S transition during the cell cycle (Zhang et al., 2022). We introduced the cycd3;3 mutation into bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 by crossing and found that the defects in leaf initiation and growth were greatly rescued in cycd3;3 bdg1-4 and cycd3;3 lacs2-4 compared with cycd3;3 and the WT (Figure 5D and 5E). Given that CK activity was derepressed in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Figure 4B) and that CKs promote cell division in a wide variety of plant cells (Kieber and Schaller 2014), we treated bdg1-4, lacs2-4, and the WT with lovastatin and examined the expression of cell-cycle genes. Although repression of CK synthesis did not affect the expression of CYCD3;3 in bdg1-4 and lacs2-4, the expression of CYCBs and CDKBs, which are key genes in the G2/M transition (De Veylder et al., 2003), was reduced in lovastatin-treated bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Figure 5F), indicating that derepressed cell cycle expression was partially dependent on CK in these mutants.

# DISCUSSION

All aboveground post-embryonic organogenesis originates from stem cells in the central zone of the SAM (Groß-Hardt and Laux, 2003). Unlike animals, which complete most organogenesis during embryogenesis before birth, plants generate basic organs or tissues, such as the cotyledons, SAM, and RAM, during embryogenesis (Capron et al., 2009). When germinating in the darkness of the soil, plants must undergo skotomorphogenesis to reach the light for photomorphogenesis 

before exhausting their seed reserves (Arsovski et al., 2012). The primary SAM remains in a quiescent state after germination, protected and maintained by a tightly closed cotyledon during skotomorphogenesis. Activation of the SAM to produce true leaves marks successful photomorphogenesis and the heterotrophic-to-photoautotrophic transition. Several studies have revealed how the quiescent SAM is activated for organ initiation and growth. Light can activate WUS production independent of photosynthesis, mediated by target of rapamycin (TOR) kinase. In addition, CK relays information from cells that 

detect light to stem cells. Metabolic signals, such as sucrose, also activate the SAM through TOR (Pfeiffer et al., 2016; Mohammed et al., 2018). Another study found that glucose and light-induced auxin are essential for TOR activation and consequent SAM activation (Li et al., 2017). These findings suggest that activation of the SAM is an active process in which light, energy, and plant hormones coordinately activate the SAM to start organ initiation and growth. However, our findings here show that maintenance of SAM quiescence is also a repression process. After embryogenesis, SAM activity is repressed by the plant cuticle and other factors. Impaired cuticle biosynthesis derepresses the quiescent SAM, resulting in organ initiation and growth (Figure 1). During photomorphogenesis, light signaling represses cuticle biosynthesis to reactivate the SAM for normal plant development (Figure 2). Consistent with this notion, the SAM was de-repressed and organogenesis was initiated in dark-grown cop1 mutants with a sucrose supplement (Pfeiffer et al., 2016). Because WUS expression is detected in the four subepidermal cells of the 16-cell embryo and the embryo thereafter (Mayer et al., 1998), SAM activity is more likely fine-tuned rather than quiescent during skotomorphogenesis. More repressors of SAM activity await isolation and investigation. 

Auxin and CK antagonistically regulate plant skotomorphogenesis, with auxin playing essential roles in hypocotyl elongation and apical hook formation by regulating cell expansion and cell division (Oh et al., 2014; Za ´ dnı´kova´ et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2019) and CK promoting photomorphogenesis in the dark through Arabidopsis histidine kinases and B-type ARRs (Argyros et al., 2008; Cortleven et al., 2019). Consistent with this scenario, light initiates rapid hormonal responses in the shoot apex, including transient repression of auxin and ET action and activation of CK and gibberellic acid action (Lopez-Juez et al., 2008). However, the underlying mechanisms by which light regulates phytohormone action are unclear. Here, we discovered that impaired cuticle synthesis due to mutations in cutin biosynthesis genes caused a systemic change in phytohormone response, including decreased auxin and ET responses and increased CK response (Figure 3). In particular, the entire metabolic and signaling pathways of auxin and CK, including biosynthesis, signaling, and degradation, were altered (Figure 4 and Supplemental Figure 4). Previous studies have revealed that highlevel auxin activity is required for cell differentiation and organ initiation (Blilou et al., 2005; Shani et al., 2006; Cheng et al., 2007). However, although we detected increased auxin responses at the SAM in mutants (Supplemental Figure 3F and 3G), results of chemical treatment indicated that activation of the SAM in mutant seedlings during etiolated growth was independent of increased auxin (Supplemental Figure 5). It remains unclear how and why auxin was increased at the SAM in the mutants. On the other hand, light repressed the expression of cutin biosynthesis genes and consequent cuticle synthesis through the light-signaling pathway during photomorphogenesis (Figure 2). Thus, light may dynamically regulate phytohormone action by influencing cuticle synthesis. How cuticle synthesis or structure affects phytohormone action requires further investigation. 

Plant cuticles, composed of the polyester cutin that is impregnated by waxes of very-long-chain fatty acids and their derivatives, cover almost all aerial surfaces of higher plants (Yeats and Rose, 2013; Fich et al., 2016). The most important role of cuticles is protection 

against water loss and other environmental stresses and aggressions (Panikashvili et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2011). Several studies have also revealed that cuticles play roles in plant developmental processes such as organ formation, seed germination, and viability, as well as seedling establishment and lateral root formation (Kurdyukov et al., 2006; Panikashvili et al., 2009; De Giorgi et al., 2015; Berhin et al., 2019). However, the precise mechanisms underlying these processes remain unclear. Here, we found that expression of LACS2, BDG1, and DCR gradually increased after germination in the dark (Figure 2A), indicating that increased cuticle synthesis is required for skotomorphogenesis. Consistent with this finding, cutin biosynthesis was impaired in lacs2-4 and bdg1-4 mutants, which displayed de-etiolated growth in the dark, including a short hypocotyl, opened apical hook and cotyledons, and activated SAM (Figure 1), indicating that plant cuticles are critical for proper skotomorphogenesis. Indeed, analysis of global gene expression profiles revealed that lacs2-4 and bdg1-4 mutants undergo photomorphogenesis in the dark, with most DEGs (around $60 \%$ of total DEGs) in mutants compared with the WT showing light-stimulated expression patterns (Figure 3). In particular, the genes involved in protein synthesis, such as enriched in ‘‘ribosome’’ and ‘‘structural constituent ribosome’’, as well as ‘‘heme binding’’ and ‘‘iron ion binding’’, were significantly induced expression in the mutants (Figure 3). Protein synthesis is one of the most energy-consuming processes in the cell (Lindqvist et al., 2018). Heme, one of the tetrapyrroles, is exclusively synthesized with insertion of $\mathsf { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ in the plastids, but heme proteins are found throughout the cell, where they protect the cell from cytotoxic effects or function as carriers, transporters, or regulators of the remaining free heme pool (Mochizuki et al., 2010). Plant cuticles are required for repression of these processes. On the other hand, 16C/18C fatty acids (FAs), substrates of LACS1/2 for cutin synthesis, are synthesized in the chloroplast (Supplemental Figure 1A). A chloroplast is generated in the cotyledon during embryogenesis and develops during photomorphogenesis. We found that chloroplast development and function were very likely impaired in lacs2-4 and bdg1-4, given the reduced expression of genes related to the chloroplast thylakoid, photosynthesis, and responses to different light pathways (Figure 3). These results indicate that impaired cuticle synthesis results in disturbance of chloroplast development and function for upcoming photomorphogenesis. Moreover, the markedly hypoxic environment detected in both bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 (Figure 6) indicates that, besides acting as a physical boundary, plant cuticles may also function in maintenance of the tissue microenvironment, which is critical for proper organ initiation and growth. During skotomorphogenesis, synthesis of plant cuticles is thus required for chloroplast development and repression of protein synthesis to balance energy consumption for upcoming photomorphogenesis. Cuticles are also required for repression of cell division, CK activity, and hypoxic conditions that control organ initiation and growth and for the expression of HLS1 and PIFs, which regulate apical hook development and hypocotyl elongation. During photomorphogenesis, light signaling represses cuticle synthesis to reverse these processes for proper organ initiation and development (Figure 7). Therefore, our findings reveal one strategy by which light initiates photomorphogenesis through dynamic regulation of cuticle synthesis, inducing coordinated and systemic changes in phytohormone action, protein synthesis, and cell division during the skotomorphogenesis-to-photomorphogenesis transition.

# METHODS

# Plant materials and culture conditions

The main mutant lines used in this study were bdg1-202 (CS847959), lacs2-760 (CS65776), dcr-4 (SALK_128228), and zpr2 (CS868360) in the Arabidopsis thaliana ecotype Col-0 background, which were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center. The cycd3;3 mutant line was generated in our laboratory by gene editing (Zhang et al., 2022). Other lines used in the study include cop1-4 (Yu et al., 2008), $P H Y A ^ { Y 2 4 2 H }$ , $P H Y B ^ { Y 2 7 6 H }$ , phyA-201, phyB-5 (Su and Lagarias, 2007), DR5::GUS (Chen et al., 2013), CYCB1;1::GUS (Zhang et al., 2022), 35S::HLS1 (Shen et al., 2016), ipt3/5/7, and TCSn::GFP (Zhang et al., 2018), which have been described in previous studies. Seeds were surface sterilized with $10 \%$ sodium hypochlorite and stratified at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ o n 1/2 Murashige and Skoog medium supplemented with $5 \ : { \mathfrak { g } } / 1$ sucrose for 2–3 days, followed by vertical cultivation under dark conditions at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 4, 7, or 10 days for phenotypic observation. Reagent treatments were added to the culture medium and included auxinole (Sigma-Aldrich, SML3231), yucasin (Sigma-Aldrich, 573760), and lovastatin (Sigma-Aldrich, PHR1285). The HRPE::GUS line used for GUS staining was grown for 4 days in darkness and for 25 days in light. 

# BSA-seq analysis

To clone the candidate genes, both mutants were backcrossed with Col four times. For each mutant, 30 individuals with the opened apical hook phenotype in the BC4F2 population were selected for BSA-seq. DNA was extracted using the DNeasy Plant Mini Kit (QIAGEN, 69104), and DNA content was measured using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. Equal amounts of DNA were mixed and used for library construction. Sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 sequencer. After sequencing, clean reads were aligned to the Arabidopsis reference 

genome (TAIR10) using Bowtie 2 (v.2.3.4.1) (Langmead and Salzberg, 2012) with default parameters. SNPs were called using SAMtools (v.1.8) (Li et al., 2009), annotated with Annovar (v.2018-04-16) (Wang et al., 2010), and visualized with Integrative Genomics Viewer (IGV) Browser (v.2.4.16) (Robinson et al., 2011). 

# Total RNA extraction and RT–qPCR

One hundred milligrams of Arabidopsis samples (the cotyledons and SAM, including the apical hook) were ground in TRIzol reagent for total RNA extraction. The extracted RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA. For RT–qPCR, SYBR Green Real-time PCR Super Mix (AQ141, Beijing Quanshi Jin Biological Technology) was thoroughly thawed and mixed before use and used according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# Puromycin treatment and western blotting

Seedlings of the WT and mutants were grown in darkness for 4 days and then treated with puromycin $( 2 1 . 2 1 \mu \mathsf { M }$ , Thermo Fisher Scientific, A1113802) for 2 h. Proteins were extracted from the aboveground parts (excluding hypocotyls) and detected using anti-puromycin (1:2500, Sigma-Aldrich, MABE343), with $\beta$ -actin (1:5000, LABLEAD, BP0101) as the reference protein. 

# TEM sample preparation and imaging

Fresh stem-tip tissue containing the SAM tissue only was carefully collected within 1–3 min, minimizing mechanical damage such as stretching, bruising, and squeezing. The tissue blocks were immediately placed in a specialized culture dish for electron microscopy and cut into small blocks measuring 1 $\mathsf { m m } ^ { 3 }$ using a surgical blade. The cut tissue blocks weretransferred to eppendorf (EP) tubes containing fresh electron microscopy fixative. A vacuum was applied until the tissue blocks settled, and they were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2 h after room-temperature incubation. The tissue blocks were rinsed 3 times

for 15min each in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (PB; pH 7.4). The blocks were fixed in $1 \%$ osmium tetroxide prepared in 0.1 M PB (pH 7.4) under light-protected conditions for 7 h at room temperature, then rinsed 3 times for 15 min each in 0.1 M PB (pH 7.4). Dehydration was performed by sequential immersion in $30 \%$ , $50 \%$ , $70 \%$ , $80 \%$ , $9 5 \%$ , and $100 \%$ ethanol for 1 h each. The tissue blocks were dehydrated using a series of ethanol:acetone mixtures (3:1, 1:1, and 1:3) for $0 . 5 \mathsf { h }$ each, followed by pure acetone for 1 h. Embedding was carried out by incubating the tissue blocks in a mixture of acetone and epoxy resin (3:1) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 2 - 4 \mathsf { h } }$ , followed by acetone and epoxy resin (1:1) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight, acetone and epoxy resin (1:3) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 2 - 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , and pure epoxy resin at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $5 { \mathord { - } } 8 \mathsf { h }$ . The pure epoxy resin was poured into embedding molds, and the samples were inserted into the molds and placed in a $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ oven overnight. The embedding molds were then transferred to a $\mathtt { 6 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ oven for $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ for polymerization. After polymerization, the resin blocks were ready for sectioning. Ultra-thin sections of 60- to 80-nm thickness were cut using an ultramicrotome and picked up onto 150-mesh copper grids coated with formvar film. The copper grids were stained in $2 \%$ uranyl acetate-saturated alcohol solution for 8 min under light protection. They were rinsed 3 times in $70 \%$ ethanol and 3 times in ultrapure water, then stained with $2 . 6 \%$ lead citrate solution for 8 min and rinsed 3 times in ultrapure water. Excess water was gently removed with filter paper, and the copper grid sections were allowed to air dry overnight in a copper grid box at room temperature. The sections were observed and imaged using a TEM for further analysis. 

# Time-course of apical hook development

The analysis and statistical assessment of apical hook development were initiated $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after germination, and data were recorded every $^ { 6 \mathfrak { h } }$ for a 6- day period under dark conditions at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . Angles between the hypocotyl axis and cotyledons (hook curvatures) were measured with ImageJ software. The apical hook angles of 15 synchronized germinating seedlings were quantitatively processed using ImageJ (NIH; http://rsb.info.nih.gov/ij). 

# Preparation of staining solutions

GUS staining solution was prepared as reported previously (Zhang et al., 2022). Two hundred milligrams 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-beta-Dglucuronic acid cyclohexylammonium salt (X-Gluc) were dissolved in 10 ml DMF to which $5 - 1 0 \ \mathsf { m l }$ buffer (50 mM ${ \mathsf { N a H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { P O } } _ { 4 }$ , 50 mM ${ \mathsf { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { H P O } } _ { 4 }$ , 10 mM EDTA$2Na, $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, 0.5 mM potassium ferricyanide, and $0 . 5 \mathrm { \ m M }$ potassium ferrocyanide) was added. The materials were placed in the staining solution for $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and images were obtained using a Leica two component signalling (TCS) SP8 laser confocal microscope (Leica Application Suite [LAS] v.4.10). 

TB staining was performed according to a method reported previously (Jakobson et al., 2016). Seedlings were collected into a 5-ml centrifuge tube, and TB staining solution was added until the samples were fully immersed. The tubes were immersed in boiling water for 2 min, then cooled to room temperature. The boiling water step was repeated once, and the tubes were left at room temperature overnight. The staining solution was discarded, an appropriate amount of $2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { m l }$ chloral hydrate was added, and multiple decolorization steps were performed. Afterward, $20 \%$ glycerol or distilled water was added, and tissues were observed and photographed. 

# RNA-seq data analysis

Total RNA was extracted for library construction using RNAiso Plus (TakaRa, 9109). Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. After sequencing, clean reads were aligned to the Arabidopsis reference genome (TAIR10) using TopHat (v.2.1.1) (Kim et al., 2013) with default parameters to remove any impurities. The uniquely mapped reads were counted using HTSeq (v.0.11.2) (Anders et al., 2015). Counts per million were calculated, and DEGs were identified using edgeR (Robinson et al., 2010); genes with a normalized expression level greater than or equal to 1 count per million in at least one sample were considered to be expressed genes. DEGs were determined using a false discovery 

rate threshold of less than 0.05. GO enrichment analysis was performed using clusterProfiler (v.3.6.0) (Yu et al., 2012). Heatmaps and Venn diagrams were generated using R software packages. 

# Vector construction

To construct the pHRPE::GUS vector (Weits et al., 2019), 500 bp of HRPE was cloned into pENTR/D-TOPO using the topoisomerase (TOPO) cloning method. The resulting plasmid was sequenced to confirm the DNA sequence. The plasmid was then linearized and recombined with pGWB3 using Gateway LR Clonase II Enzyme Mix (Invitrogen, 56484). The confirmed plasmid was transformed into Col-0 plants, which were crossed with bdg1-4 and lacs2-4 mutants. A general overview of the transcriptomic data is given in Supplemental Tables 1–5. The primers used for RT–qPCR and mutant genotyping are listed in Supplemental Table 6. 

# Targeted metabolomics

Metabolite extraction and mass spectrometry (MS) detection were performed using an ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography–MS system at Biomarker Technologies (Beijing, China). The liquid chromatography–MS system for metabolomics analysis consisted of a Waters Acquity I-Class PLUS ultra-high performance liquid chromatography system in tandem with a high-resolution Waters Xevo G2-XS QTof mass spectrometer. The Waters Xevo G2-XS QTof mass spectrometer can collect primary and secondary mass spectrometry data in MSe mode under the control of MassLynx v.4.2 acquisition software (Waters). Raw data collected with MassLynx v4.2 was processed with Progenesis QI software for peak extraction, peak alignment, and other data processing operations. The online Progenesis QI software’s database and Biomark’s proprietary library were used for the data identification, and the theoretical fragment identification was conducted with a mass deviation of within 100 ppm for precursor ions. 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis of hypocotyl length, true-leaf number, and root length involved 15 seedlings per treatment with 3 biological replicates. Each experiment was repeated at least three times to ensure biological reproducibility. Statistical significance was determined using Student’s t-test, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), and Tukey’s test.
