# Summary

 Monoterpene indole alkaloids (MIA) are a diverse class of plant natural products produced by a subset of lineages within the Asterid clade of eudicots. The diversity of MIAs provides a unique opportunity to study not only the evolution of biosynthetic genes but also their regulation. 

 In this study, we investigate the cell type specificity of biosynthetic genes and coexpressed transcription factors (TFs) in two MIA-producing Asterid species, Catharanthus roseus, a well-studied MIA-producing species, and Camptotheca acuminata, which belongs to an early-diverging lineage of the Asterid clade. We generated single-cell RNA-seq data from the C. acuminata stem, the primary site of camptothecin biosynthesis. 

 We found that MIA biosynthetic genes in C. acuminata are specific to exceptionally rare cell populations. We discovered MYB and bHLH TFs coexpressed in the same cell types as MIA biosynthetic genes in the C. acuminata stem. Interestingly, the C. roseus orthologs of these TFs are idioblast-specific and activate MIA biosynthetic genes in C. roseus upon overexpression. We constructed an extended gene regulatory network for the idioblast metabolic regulon in C. roseus, which contains both feedback and feedforward activation loops. 

 This study demonstrates co-option of the same clades of TFs for regulation of cell-typespecific MIA biosynthesis across two Asterid species separated by c. 115 million years of evolution. Investigating cell-type-specific TFs that are coexpressed with biosynthetic genes across multiple species is a powerful strategy to increase the power for the discovery of plant metabolic regulators. 

# Introduction

Plant specialized metabolites, or plant natural products, are restricted to specific plant lineages (Ji et al., 2024). Lineagespecific specialized metabolism evolves rapidly, giving rise to immensely diverse natural products across the plant kingdom. For example, the monoterpene indole alkaloids (MIA) are a group of plant-derived natural products, of which $> 3 0 0 0$ different compounds have been identified (Pan et al., 2016). MIA biosynthesis can be delimited into at least three different stages (O’Connor & Maresh, 2006). The first stage is the methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway that produces the building blocks for the monoterpene moiety of MIAs. In the second stage, the iridoid pathway produces a group of monoterpenes in the 

general form of cyclopentanopyran, which can be condensed with a tryptamine to produce MIAs. In the third stage, the MIAs are further elaborated by biosynthetic enzymes that are specific to families, genera, or even individual species to produce diverse MIAs, many with potent biological or pharmaceutical activities. While the MEP pathway is conserved in all plants (Zeng & Dehesh, 2021), iridoids emerged within the Asterid clade of eudicots (Dinda et al., 2007), and MIAs are further restricted to a subset of lineages within the Asterids, including Nyssaceae, Apocynaceae, Loganiaceae, Gelsemiaceae, and Rubiaceae families (Ramos et al., 2019). 

Gene and genome duplications followed by neo- and subfunctionalization fueled the evolution of biosynthetic genes (Ober, 2005). Newly evolved biosynthetic genes are assembled 

into gene regulatory networks, as genes within the same biosynthetic pathway tend to be coexpressed (Wisecaver et al., 2017). The expression of biosynthetic pathways is generally also under strict spatiotemporal control, such that these pathways are expressed in specific organs, tissues, and even cell types (Weng et al., 2021). Therefore, regulatory mechanisms, such as transcription factors (TFs), have a crucial role in defining the expression patterns of biosynthetic pathways, thereby connecting the chemistry of natural products to their ecological functions at the organismal level. The diversity of MIAs provides an excellent framework to understand not only the evolution of biosynthetic genes but also the extent of conservation and/or innovation in their regulation. 

Catharanthus roseus G. Don (Madagascar Periwinkle) produces MIAs with clinical relevance, including vinblastine and vincristine (also known as vinca alkaloids) that are used to treat various cancers in the clinic (O’Connor & Maresh, 2006). While C. roseus is primarily known as a medicinal plant, in recent years, it has become a model for studying how specialized metabolism is restricted to specific cell types, as the MIA biosynthetic pathway in C. roseus exhibits strong cell type specificity (Li et al., 2023b; Vu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025). The early steps of the pathway, including the MEP and iridoid biosynthetic genes, are specifically expressed in the internal phloem-associated parenchyma (IPAP) (Burlat et al., 2004; Simkin et al., 2013; Miettinen et al., 2014). Subsequent biosynthetic steps occur in the epidermis (Burlat et al., 2004; Guirimand et al., 2011; Miettinen et al., 2014). The final known steps of the pathway are restricted to a rare and specialized cell type termed idioblast (Guirimand et al., 2011; Li et al., 2023b), which is interspersed among mesophyll cells and can be recognized by blue autofluorescence under UV excitation due to the presence of the blue fluorescent MIA serpentine (Yamamoto et al., 2016; Yamamoto et al., 2019). The cell type specificity of this pathway is, in part, controlled by cell-typespecific TFs. For instance, a group of basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) TFs termed ‘bHLH Iridoid Synthesis’ (BIS ) (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015, 2016; Singh et al., 2021) regulates the IPAP-expressed biosynthetic genes in C. roseus (Li et al., 2025). Furthermore, an R2-R3 MYB TF Idioblast MYB1 (CrIDM1) was recently reported to be idioblast specific and activates late-stage vinca alkaloid biosynthetic genes (Li et al., 2025). While the above-mentioned TFs regulate cell-type-specific expression of biosynthetic genes, it is unclear whether they are also involved in the differentiation of the underlying cell types. Other than the above-mentioned TFs, factors contributing to cell-type-specific biosynthesis in $C .$ roseus remain undiscovered. It is also unclear whether conserved regulatory components exist across species to control the expression of MIA biosynthetic genes in specific cell types. In Arabidopsis, a collection of MYB and bHLH TFs is involved in cell-type-specific accumulation of flavonoids (Baudry et al., 2006; Li et al., 2009). However, it is unclear whether homologous TFs are involved in the cell type specificity of MIAs. 

Camptotheca acuminata Decne, known as the ‘happy tree’, belongs to the Nyssaceae family within the Cornales order of the 

Asterids (Fig. 1a). The Cornales order is an early diverging lineage within the Asterids, sister to all other lineages within this clade (The Angiosperm Phylogeny Group et al., 2016). While distantly related to other MIA-producing species such as C. roseus, C. acuminata produces the MIA-derived compound camptothecin. First extracted from the stems of C. acuminata in 1966 (Wall et al., 1966), camptothecin has been used since then as a starting material to semi-synthesize many leading anticancer drugs such as irinotecan (Camptosar®) and topotecan (Hycam-$\mathrm { t i n } ^ { \widetilde { \mathbb { B } } } ,$ ) (Lorence & Nessler, 2004). Camptothecin and its derivatives are potent inhibitors of DNA topoisomerase I and are widely used for the treatment of lung, cervical, ovarian, and colon cancers (Giovanella et al., 1989). Owing to the conserved nature of MEP and iridoid pathways, the early stages of camptothecin biosynthesis have been characterized in C. acuminata, while the downstream, lineage-specific biosynthetic steps remain largely unidentified. It is also unclear if camptothecin biosynthesis is cell-type specific and if there are conserved TFs that govern the cell-type specificity of camptothecin biosynthetic genes. Highquality, chromosome-scale genome assemblies and annotations have recently become available for C. acuminata (Kang et al., 2021; V.-H. Bui et al., 2025), which facilitates omics analyses such as single-cell genomics. 

Our understanding of plant metabolic pathways in plants has, until very recently, relied on whole tissue or whole organ-derived metabolome and transcriptome datasets. However, biosynthetic pathways of natural products are localized not only to distinct organs but also to distinct cell types within these organs. The emergence of single-cell omics technologies has demonstrated enormous potential to revolutionize metabolic pathway gene discovery in plants (Zhan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Vu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; McClune et al., 2025). Single-cell omics technologies have also empowered the detection of cell-type-specific gene regulatory networks, which have been recognized to be integral for the regulation of specialized metabolism (Ntelkis et al., 2024). In this study, we showed that by using a pair of medicinal plant species that produce structurally related MIAs, it is possible to leverage regulatory relationships in one species to inform gene regulation in the other species, or vice versa, through phylogenetic analyses.

# Curation of MIA biosynthetic genes in C. roseus and C. acuminata

The biosynthetic pathways (gene names, gene symbols, and structures of key intermediates) (Supporting Information Figs S1, S2; Tables S1, S2) of C. roseus and C. acuminata were taken from the literature (Li et al., 2023b; Kang et al., 2021; H. V. 

Bui et al., 2025) by referencing the gene IDs, names of the enzymes, and structures of biosynthetic intermediates. 

# Comparative genomics analyses

Comparative genomics analyses were performed with GENE-SPACE (Lovell et al., 2022) using the genome annotations of 

C. acuminata (V.-H. Bui et al., 2025), C. roseus (Li et al., 2023b), M. speciosa (Brose et al., 2021), and $G$ . sempervirens (Franke et al., 2019) using Arabidopsis (Lamesch et al., 2012) as an outgroup. The pangenes.txt.gz output was parsed to produce Fig. 1b. 

# LC-MS analysis

Three biological replicates of young $C .$ acuminata stems from the second to fourth nodes below the shoot apex were collected and snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen. Tissues were ground to a fine powder with a mortar and pestle, and metabolites were extracted using $8 5 \%$ methanol, $1 5 \%$ water, and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid at a ratio of $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } }$ tissue to $1 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ solvent. Extracts were filtered through $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ PTFE syringe filters, then diluted 100-fold with extraction buffer containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { n M }$ catharanthine as an internal standard. For the C. roseus leaf, sample collection (three biological replicates) and extraction were performed as described previously (Vu et al., 2024). UHPLC–HRMS analysis was performed by injecting $2 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of diluted extract into a Vanquish UHPLC system coupled to a Q-Exactive Plus Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific), following methods described previously (Vu et al., 2024). Metabolites were separated on a Waters ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column $( 1 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $1 \ \mathrm { m m } \ \times$ $5 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ ) at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , using a binary mobile phase of solvent A (Milli-Q water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid) and solvent B (acetonitrile) under a linear gradient. Chromatographic peak areas were extracted from the corresponding extracted ion chromatograms (Figs S3, S4; Table S3) using MZmine 3 (Schmid et al., 2023). 

# Stem protoplast isolation for single cell metabolomics and single cell RNA-seq

Young stems from the second to fourth nodes below the shoot apex were sampled in ice-cold water in a $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ tube. Two biological replicates were collected from the same tree and processed at the same time. Using a sterile razor blade, an incision was made longitudinally along the stem segments, and the outer stem layers were carefully peeled away. Both outer and inner stem tissues were immediately transferred into $2 0 \ \mathrm { m l }$ of protoplast enzyme cocktail. The cocktail contained $0 . 4 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ sorbitol, $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ KCl, $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ MES, $2 \%$ (w/v) Cellulase Onozuka R-10, $0 . 3 \%$ (w/v) Macerozyme R-10, $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) pectinase, 1 mM $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 2 \%$ (v/v) 2-mercaptoethanol, and $0 . 1 \%$ (w/v) bovine serum albumin, adjusted to $\mathrm { p H } 5 . 7$ and heated at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ before cooling to room temperature. A protoplast resting solution was made, which contained the same composition as the enzyme solution except for the enzymes. Vacuum infiltration of stem segments in the protoplast cocktail was performed for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ at $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m B a r } }$ . Following vacuum infiltration, the mixture was shaken at $8 0 \ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and $4 5 { - } 7 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ at room temperature. Subsequently, the mixture was briefly shaken at $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { r p m }$ for 5 min to facilitate protoplast release. The suspension was then filtered through sequential nylon sieves of $1 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ and $7 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ mesh sizes to remove debris. Filtrate was centrifuged at ${ 2 0 0 \ g }$ for 5 min at 

$4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ after which the protoplast pellet was gently resuspended in $2 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of resting solution. For protoplast purification via OptiPrep density gradient, a working solution (WS) was prepared by dissolving $6 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { K C l }$ per $1 \ \mathrm { m l }$ of OptiPrep density medium. Three density layers (A, B, and C) were created. Layer A was produced by mixing 500 ll WS with $2 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ protoplast suspension by gentle pipetting. WS $( 2 6 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { l } )$ was combined with $1 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ resting solution to produce Layer B, and $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of resting solution was used as Layer C alone. Layers were sequentially and carefully layered into a $5 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ tube (A, followed by B and C) without disturbing interfaces. The gradient was centrifuged at $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { g }$ for $5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ , allowing viable protoplasts to float to the top while debris settled at the bottom. The uppermost $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ fraction containing purified protoplasts was collected into a new tube. Protoplast viability and concentration were determined using fluorescein diacetate staining and hemocytometer counting. The final protoplast concentration was adjusted to $1 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ protoplasts per ml. 

# PIP-seq library construction and data processing

Stem protoplasts were isolated and used as input into the PIPseq $\mathrm { \ v } 4 . 0$ Plus kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) to generate scRNA-seq libraries per manufacturer recommendations. The libraries were sequenced on an AVITI instrument (Element Biosciences, San Diego, CA, USA). The subsequent reads were processed using PIPSEEKER (v.3.2.0) (Illumina), which trimmed read two, generated a barcode whitelist and prepared the reads for downstream analysis. Gene-barcode matrices were produced using STARsolo (v.2.7.10b) (Kaminow et al., 2021) using the following options: --runThreadN 24 --alignIntronMax 5000 --soloBarcodeReadLength 0 --soloUMIlen 12 --soloCellFilter EmptyDrops_CR --soloFeatures GeneFull --soloMultiMappers EM, aligning to the C. acuminata v.4 reference genome (V.-H. Bui et al., 2025). Gene-barcode matrices are analyzed with SEURAT (v.4) (Hao et al., 2021) for downstream analysis. Removal of low-quality cells and suspected multiplets was performed using the distributions of UMI counts and detected genes (Fig. S5; Tables S4, S5). 

# Single-cell RNA-seq analyses using PIP-seq data

Biological replicates were integrated using the ‘IntegrateData()’ function in Seurat using the top 3000 variable genes. Uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) were performed after a principal component analysis using the following parameters: dims $= 1 : 3 0$ , min.dist $= 0 . 0 0 1$ , repulsion.strength $= 1$ , n.neighbors $= 3 0$ , spread $= 1$ . Clustering of cells was performed with a resolution of 0.5. Expression of previously studied biosynthetic genes was plotted (Tables S3, S4) across each cell cluster. For cell type classification, we used a manually curated marker gene list (Table S5), using previously established marker genes from a poplar stem single cell RNA-seq dataset (Conde et al., 2022) (Fig. S6). An expression matrix (logCPM values, Dataset S1) was generated at the cell cluster level, where each row is a gene, and each column is a cell cluster. 

# Gene coexpression analyses

Gene coexpression analysis by graph-based clustering was performed as previously described (Li et al., 2023a). Graph-based clustering was performed using the Leiden algorithm (Traag et al., 2019), which was implemented in R (v.4.1.1) by the IGRAPH package (v.1.4.2) (Csardi & Nepusz, 2006). Expression of coexpressed TFs was visualized. 

# Phylogenetic analysis of MYB and bHLH domain TFs

We used ‘MYB annotator’ (Pucker, 2022) and ‘bHLH annotator’ (Thoben & Pucker, 2023) to automatically identify MYB domain proteins in C. acuminata and C. roseus (Datasets S2, S3). We aligned the sequences of MYB domains or bHLH domains using MAFFT (v.7.505) (Katoh, 2002) using the following parameters: --anysymbol --maxiterate 1000 -localpair. The alignment file generated by MAFFT was used to generate maximum likelihood phylogenetic trees using RAxML (v.8.2.12) (Stamatakis, 2014) with the following parameters: -f a -m PROTGAMMAAUTO -x 666 -N 250 -p 666 -T 4, with 250 iterations for bootstrapping. We used ggtree (Yu et al., 2017) to perform visualization of phylogenies (Figs 3b–d, S7a,b). The number of orthologs for TF clades across species (Fig. S7c) was visualized using the pangenes.txt.gz output of GENESPACE (Lovell et al., 2022). Expression patterns of C. roseus across single-cell datasets (Fig. 3e,f) were visualized like Fig. 2b. Expression data of methyl jasmonate treatment experiments (Van Moerkercke et al., 2013), sorted idioblast, and sorted mesophyll (Guedes et al., 2024) were trimmed using CUTADAPT (v.3.5) (Martin, 2011) and pseudo-aligned and quantified using KALLISTO (v.0.48) (Bray et al., 2016), with the --plaintext option turned on (Figs 3g, S8a). ATAC-seq signal across cell types at CrIDB1 and CrIDM4 loci in leaf (Fig. S8b) was visualized using the CoveragePlot() function of Signac in R. 

# Overexpression assays

Coding sequences of TFs of interest were cloned between the 35S promoter and 35S terminator and transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Transient expression experiments were performed on C. roseus (cultivar ‘Little Bright Eyes’) petals. Three biological replicates (one flower per replicate) were used for each overexpression treatment. Infiltration was done as previously described (Colinas & Goossens, 2022). Two days before the infiltration, all open flowers were removed. We performed infiltrations at 0.4 optical density (OD), which is the highest OD that can be used without resulting in wilting of the petals after infiltration. Two days after the infiltration, infiltrated petals were harvested and stored in a $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ freezer until RNA extraction. RNA extraction was performed with a NEB Monarch Total RNA Miniprep Kit. RNA-seq libraries were made using the PerkinElmer NEXTFLEX Rapid Directional RNA-Seq Kit 2.0 and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq X Plus instrument in 150 nt paired-end mode. 

# RNA-seq analysis for overexpression samples

Sequencing adapters were trimmed from petal RNA-seq libraries using CUTADAPT (v.3.5) (Martin, 2011). Adapter-trimmed libraries were pseudo-aligned and quantified using KALLISTO (v.0.48) (Bray et al., 2016), with the --plaintext option turned on. Differential gene expression analyses were performed using DESeq2 (v.1.34.0) (Love et al., 2014), using the AmCyan-only treatment (Treatment M) as the control. Genes with adjusted $P$ values $< 0 . 0 5$ were taken as differentially expressed genes (Fig. 4). Target genes of TFs (Figs S8) were defined as genes differentially upregulated and having the highest expression in the idioblast. A gene expression matrix was also reported as Dataset S4. 

# Reporter transactivation assays

Reporter transactivation assays were performed in a twocomponent format: a reporter component and an overexpression component. Genetic parts used in reporter assays were amplified from a vector tool kit for plant molecular biology (Chamness et al., 2023). The accessible chromatin regions immediately upstream of IDB1 were cloned upstream of a 35S minimal promoter, which controls the expression of the DsRed reporter. A GFP internal control driven by the Arabidopsis UBQ1 promoter was included on the same plasmid. The overexpression component was an Agrobacterium GV3101 strain carrying UBQ10: IDB1 and 35S:IDM1 on the same plasmid. Constructs were infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves using a syringe. Three biological replicates (one infiltration per replicate per treatment) were performed for each treatment. Two days after the infiltration, N. benthamiana leaves were imaged using a fluorescent microscope. Pixel intensity was quantified using IMAGEJ (Schneider et al., 2012).

# Cell-type-specific expression of MIA biosynthetic genes

We performed single-cell RNA-seq using stems of C. acuminata to investigate the cell type specificity of the biosynthetic pathway, as well as to identify potential regulators involved in organ or cell type specificity, since the stem is the primary site of camptothecin biosynthesis (H. V. Bui et al., 2025). We performed protoplast-based single-cell RNA-seq using the Particletemplated Instant Partition-seq platform (PIP-seq) (Clark et al., 2023). We obtained gene expression profiles of 11 321 cells and detected 29 002 expressed genes (Tables S4, S5; Fig. S5; Dataset S1). With unsupervised clustering, we detected 23 cell clusters and identified cell types using previously established cell type marker genes for stem (Figs 2a, S6; Table S6) (Conde et al., 2022). We found that the MEP pathway and iridoid biosynthetic genes (Tables S1, S2) are highly specific to a rare cell population (Fig. 2b) that accounts for only $0 . 6 8 \%$ of all stem protoplasts, which we termed ‘iridoid cells’. In $C .$ roseus leaves, MEP and iridoid biosynthetic genes are specific to IPAP (Miettinen et al., 2014). The iridoid cells we detected in C. acuminata stems could be homologous to IPAP in C. roseus leaves. Similar to C. roseus, the rest of the biosynthetic pathway was expressed in different cell types (Clusters 14 and 17; Fig. 2b) (Li et al., 2023b). These results suggest that the product of 7DLH, loganic acid, may be synthesized in one cell type and subsequently transported to another cell type for downstream biosynthetic steps (SLAS and TDC ). # An extended idioblast regulon based on overexpression, cell-type-specific coexpression, and orthology

When we compared the target genes of CaTT8 or CrIDB1 in combination with CrIDM1, in which a target gene was defined as upregulated by overexpression and coexpressed in the idioblast, we found that the majority of target genes were shared between these combinations $( P < 2 . 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 6 }$ , Fisher’s Exact test) (Fig. 5a). In addition, we found that genes activated by either the $C r I D M I + \ C r I D B I$ combination or by CrIDM4 alone are highly enriched for idioblast expression (Fig. 5c,d). Specifically, among all differentially upregulated genes in the $C r I D M I + C r I D B I$ combination treatment, 147 out of 796 $( 1 8 \% )$ genes were most highly expressed in the idioblast, representing a 4.5-fold enrichment over the expected background ratio $( P \overset { ^ { \circ } } { < } 2 . 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 6 }$ , chi-squared test) (Fig. 5c). The enrichment was even stronger for CrIDM4, since 43 out of 144 $( 3 0 \% )$ differentially upregulated genes were most highly expressed in the idioblast, representing a 7.5-fold enrichment 

over the expected background ratio $( P < 2 . 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 6 }$ , chi-squared test) (Fig. 5d). Among the target genes were putative biosynthetic genes encoding alcohol dehydrogenase, FAD/NAD (P)-binding oxidoreductase, and cytochrome P450 family enzymes (Fig. S9b,c), which also included previously characterized idioblast-specific enzymes CrTHAS1 (01G033230), CrADH32 (06G024590) (Li et al., 2023b), and Serpentine Synthase (03G003540) (Yamamoto et al., 2021). 

# CrIDB1 activates its own expression via CrIDM1

Since the combination of CaTT8 and CrIDM1 activated the expression of CrIDB1 (Fig. 4d), we performed promoter transactivation assays in Nicotiana benthamiana (Fig. 6a) to test if the combination of CrIDB1 and CrIDM1 could activate the CrIDB1 promoter (pIDB1 hereafter). Using existing single-cell ATACseq data (Li et al., 2025), we located an accessible chromatin region c. 400-bp in length immediately upstream of the CrIDB1 locus, which was most accessible in the idioblast (Fig. 6b), the cell type where CrIDB1 was expressed (Li et al., 2025). We cloned this promoter element upstream of a minimal 35S promoter driving DsRed. On the same construct, an internal GFP control driven by a constitutive promoter is included (Fig. 6a). Consistent with RNA-seq results obtained from overexpression experiments (Fig. 4d), the combination of CrIDB1 and CrIDM1 activated the DsRed reporter (Fig. 6c,d), but not the minimal 35S promoter lacking the pIDB1 fragment (Fig. 6e,f). The pIDB1 reporter alone could not drive the expression of DsRed (Fig. 6g,h). These results were validated by red/green pixel intensity ratio quantifications (Fig. 6i). 

# Gene regulatory network for late-stage vinca alkaloid biosynthesis

Using overexpression and single-cell omics datasets, combined with phylogenetic analyses and previously reported TF-promoter transactivation assays (Li et al., 2025), we identified TF candidates from C. accuminata and produced an extended gene regulatory network for late-stage vinca alkaloid biosynthetic genes in $C .$ roseus (Fig. 6j). The extension of such a network is facilitated by the orthologous relationships between C. acuminata and $C .$ roseus. The network contained 15 edges, which were decorated by the type(s) of supporting evidence: (1) activated by overexpression, (2) coexpressed across single cells, (3) orthologous between C. acuminata and C. roseus, and (4) promoter activation in a transactivation assay. Notably, we discovered both feedback and feedforward activation loops. CrIDB1 could activate its own expression when CrIDM1 was also expressed. While CrIDM1 and CrIDB1 in combination activate late-stage biosynthetic genes, they also activate CrIDM4, which in turn activates the same late-stage biosynthetic genes targeted by CrIDM1 and CrIDB1 (Fig. 6j). These results showcased that by investigating TFs coexpressed with biosynthetic genes in a different species (e.g. CaTT8 and CaMYB5), we gain power for discovering celltype-specific metabolic regulators, in this case, CrIDB1 and CrIDM4. 

# Discussion

Both C. acuminata and C. roseus, separated by c. 115 Myr of evolution (Fig. 1a) (Kumar et al., 2017), produce MIAs, with camptothecin and vinblastine being the respective key natural products of interest to human health. Among plant lineages that produce MIAs, the MEP and iridoid pathways are largely conserved (Fig. 1). Cell type specificity of plant specialized metabolism has been well documented (Weng et al., 2021), including MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus. In this study, we showed that several camptothecin biosynthetic genes are also cell type specific in the stem of $C .$ acuminata, with MEP and iridoid biosynthetic gene expression restricted to an exceptionally rare cell population (Fig. 2). 

We discovered TFs, CaTT8 and CaMYB5, coexpressed with MIA biosynthetic genes in the C. acuminata stem (Fig. 3), whose phylogenies include previously reported TFs that regulate MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus, such as CrIDM1 and CrBIS1. Among these newly described TFs, CaTT8 and CaMYB5 were able to activate similar sets of target genes as their $C .$ roseus orthologs (Fig. 4) when heterologously expressed, either in combination with CrIDM1 (in the case of CaTT8) or on their own (in the case of CaMYB5). Surprisingly, CrIDB1 (CaTT8 ortholog) and CrIDM4 (CaMYB5 ortholog) were specific to the idioblast in $C .$ . roseus, suggesting they play a regulatory role in controlling biosynthetic genes that are also idioblast-specific. We demonstrated that CrIDM1, in combination with CrIDB1, occupies a higher position within the hierarchy of idioblast-specific TFs, where the combination of CrIDM1 and CrIDB1 not only activates biosynthetic genes but also activates other TFs, including CrIDM4, which in turn activates biosynthetic genes (Fig. 5), forming a feedforward activation loop. We also showed that CrIDB1 reinforces its own expression via CrIDM1 (Fig. 6), reminiscent of how AtTT8, the Arabidopsis ortholog, activates its own transcription in a MYB-dependent manner as a feedback activation loop contributing to strong and cell-type-specific accumulation of flavonoids in Arabidopsis (Baudry et al., 2006). CrIDM1, CrIDB1, and CrIDM4 activate the idioblast metabolic regulon, which includes all currently known idioblast-specific enzymes (Figs 6j, S9). The lack of a convenient transient expression platform for $C .$ . acuminata makes similar in planta experiments for C. acuminata very difficult, but it is likely that CaTT8 and CaMYB5 play a role in regulating calprotectin biosynthesis as well. 

Our experiments established CrIDM1, CrIDB1, and CrIDM4 as idioblast metabolic activators that activate late-stage vinca alkaloid biosynthetic genes. Additional mechanisms, such as chromatin accessibility (in the case of T16H2 and 16OMT ), might be involved in restricting biosynthetic gene expression to specific cell types. In C. roseus leaves, T16H2 and 16OMT are specific to the epidermis, not the idioblast (Li et al., 2025). The lack of T16H2 and 16OMT expression in the idioblast can be explained by the fact that the promoter regions of these biosynthetic genes are only accessible in the epidermis (Li et al., 2025), such that CrIDM1 and CrIDB1 cannot activate T16H2 and 16OMT transcription in the idioblast. 

Lastly, the same clade of TFs (e.g. the TT8 clade and the MYB5 clade) might have been co-opted to regulate biosynthetic 

genes in a cell type-specific manner across multiple species. Investigating cell type-specific TFs that are coexpressed with biosynthetic genes across multiple species is a powerful strategy for discovering metabolic activators. A common strategy for identifying TFs relevant to a biosynthetic pathway is coexpression analysis, either across organs and/or stress conditions, and more recently, across cell types (Li et al., 2025). However, such analyses often yield an unwieldy number of candidate TFs, making downstream functional characterization challenging. Taking advantage of phylogenetic analyses, we demonstrated that if a clade of TF includes orthologs that are cell type-specific and coexpressed with biosynthetic genes across multiple species, other members of the same clade are also likely to be metabolic regulators, as shown in the case of CrIDB1 and CrIDM4 (Fig. 6j).
