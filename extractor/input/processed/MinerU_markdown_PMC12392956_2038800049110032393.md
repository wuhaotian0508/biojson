# Summary

Pseudocydonia sinensis, commonly known as Chinese quince, belongs to the Rosaceae family and is closely related to apple and pear. Despite its botanical significance, genomic resources for this species remain limited. We present a high-quality, chromosome-scale, and haplotype-resolved genome assembly of P. sinensis, characterized by high BUSCO completeness and QV scores. The genome sizes of Haplotypes A and B are 581.29 Mb and 561.80 Mb, respectively, each spanning 17 chromosomes with a contig N50 of 15.76 Mb. Comparative genomic analyses place P. sinensis within the Maleae tribe, closely related to Malus domestica and Pyrus communis, both of which have undergone an additional whole-genome duplication event. Gene family and metabolomic analyses show that the genome has an expansion of 1603 gene families, with an accumulation of secondary metabolites such as flavonoids and phenolic acids (e.g. rutin and caffeoylquinic acid). This duplication has contributed to the expansion of gene families involved in secondary metabolite biosynthesis, particularly in the phenylpropanoid and flavonoid pathways. Integrative transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses further revealed the MYB transcription factor ODORANT1 (ODO1) is a key regulator of phenylpropanoid metabolism. Functional assays show that PsMYBODO1 directly binds to and activates the promoters of key genes in this pathway, including PsRT3, PsPAL, PsCAD and PsCOMT. This high-quality reference genome provides a valuable resource for functional genomic studies and breeding programmes aimed at enhancing the medicinal properties of P. sinensis. 

# Introduction

Pseudocydonia sinensis, commonly known as Chinese quince, is a distinctive tree in the Rosaceae family and the only species within the Pseudocydonia genus. Also referred to as Chaenomeles sinensis, its fruit is notable for its pronounced acidity, astringency and firm flesh, which are utilized in both culinary and medicinal applications. The medicinal value of the Chinese quince has long been recognized, leading to its extensive use in traditional folk medicine (Najman et al., 2023). Rich in polyphenols, it exhibits potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that help protect both cells and the nervous system (Grygorieva et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2017). Additionally, compounds derived from Chinese quince have shown the ability to inhibit cancer cells and the influenza virus, highlighting its potential for anti-cancer and antiviral applications (Gao et al., 2003; Kostecka-Gugala, 2024; Sawai-Kuroda et al., 2013). The fruit also contains glycosidase inhibitors that aid in regulating blood glucose levels, making it a promising natural option for managing diabetes. Furthermore, it plays an important role in treating respiratory conditions and supporting liver and gastrointestinal health (Watychowicz et al., 2017; 

Zhang et al., 2018). In addition to its medicinal uses, Chinese quince can be processed into flavourful jams and jellies, further demonstrating its commercial potential. These findings underscore the remarkable therapeutic and economic value of this unique plant. 

Phenylpropanoid metabolism is a crucial secondary metabolic pathway in plants, playing a key role in growth, development and interactions with the environment. This pathway produces a variety of metabolites, including flavonoids and lignin. In P. sinensis, flavonoids are particularly abundant, with approximately 37 distinct compounds identified in its fruits (Dong and Lin, 2021; Li et al., 2013; Shao et al., 2021; Watychowicz et al., 2017). These flavonoids, in conjunction with lignin, which contributes to the fruit’s high lignification, are essential for regulating plant developmental processes and signalling networks (Ambawat et al., 2013). Despite extensive research on the physical, chemical and medicinal properties of P. sinensis, studies investigating the genetic mechanisms influencing its nutrient content have been limited due to the absence of comprehensive genomic resources. Therefore, establishing a detailed genomic dataset is crucial for a deeper comprehension of the phenylpropanoid pathway and its effects on the characteristics of P. sinensis. 

The transcriptional regulation of phenylpropanoid metabolism, particularly through the control exerted by MYB transcription factors (TFs) on the structural genes responsible for lignin and flavonoid biosynthesis, is a crucial area of study (Geng et al., 2020; Ma and Constabel, 2019). Among the MYB TFs, the R2R3-MYB family stands out as the largest group in plants, playing pivotal roles in a wide range of biological processes, including plant growth, development, metabolism, hormone signal transduction, cellular proliferation, apoptosis and responses to both disease and abiotic stresses (Li et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2024; Martin and Paz-Ares, 1997; Mehrtens et al., 2005). Notably, the R2R3-MYB family is involved in regulating specialized metabolites such as flavonoids (Hichri et al., 2011; Yu et al., 2020), anthocyanins (Goicoechea et al., 2005; Mehrtens et al., 2005) and lignin (Bedon et al., 2007; Su et al., 2021). Their regulatory influence on these compounds highlights their critical role in shaping plant secondary metabolism and the development of important traits. 

In this study, we successfully assembled the 34 chromosomes of the two haplotypes of P. sinensis and investigated the expansion of gene families involved in the synthesis of bioactive compounds. By exploring the phylogenetic relationships within the Maleae tribe and conducting a comparative analysis of ancient whole genome duplication events in the Rosaceae family, we gained insights into the evolutionary processes shaping this plant group. Through the integration of transcriptomic and metabolomic data, we identified key regulatory genes and network modules involved in the biosynthesis pathways of bioactive substances. These findings not only enrich the genomic database for evolutionary studies but also provide valuable genetic resources for functional genomics research in P. sinensis. Overall, this research advances our understanding of the genetic basis and regulatory mechanisms underlying the unique traits of this species. 

# Results

# Genome sequencing and assembly

The genome of Pseudocydonia sinensis was sequenced and assembled, with initial $k$ -mer analysis $( k = 2 1$ ) estimating the genome size at approximately 576 Mb, characterized by high heterozygosity $( 0 . 7 3 8 \% )$ (Figure S1 and Table S1). To address this complexity, we generated 28 Gb of high-fidelity PacBio long-read data (HiFi), yielding reads with an N50 of $2 0 . 7 2 \mathrm { k b }$ and an average length of 20.54 kb (Table S2). Together with the Hi-C data, we scaffolded the assembly into 34 pseudochromosomes corresponding to the two haplotypes (Figures S2 and S3; Table S3). The finalized genome assembly was 1.09 Gb, with Haplotype A of 581.56 Mb and Haplotype B of 557.01 Mb; scaffold N50 is 32.98 Mb (Table S4).

# Whole genome duplication inference and ancestral karyotype reconstruction of the Maleae clade

A single peak at approximately $K S = 1 . 6$ in the distributions of G. trifoliata, P. persica and R. chinensis genomes indicated the occurrence of the γ triplication events. The adjusted Ks distributions of orthologous pairs between P. sinensis and other species were used to infer speciation events, revealing that $P .$ sinensis diverged first from R. chinensis, followed by P. persica, G. trifoliata and M. domestica, consistent with the phylogenetic reconstruction (Figure 3b). 

The Ks density curves of paralogous pairs within P. sinensis and other Maleae species displayed two distinct peaks at approximately 0.2 and 1.6. The younger Ks peak corresponds to the whole genome duplication event shared among the Maleae species (Figure 3b). 

To further investigate the evolutionary relationships, we compared Haplotypes A and B of P. sinensis with those of M. domestica and G. trifoliata. The analysis revealed a 2:1 syntenic relationship between P. sinensis and G. trifoliata, and a 1:1 syntenic relationship with M. domestica (Figure S8). This suggests that both P. sinensis and M. domestica underwent the same WGD event, in addition to the $\gamma$ triplication event shared by core eudicot species. Together, the Ks density curves and synteny analysis provide robust evidence supporting this WGD. 

Using Multiple Genome Rearrangements and Ancestors (MGRA), we reconstructed the hypothetical ancestral chromosomes of Rosaceae and traced their evolutionary trajectory towards extant karyotypes. Our analysis identified nine independent protochromosomes representing the ancestral Rosaceae karyotype (ARK), excluding minor fragments affected by chromosomal inversions and deletions (Figure 3c,d). The results indicate that Gillenia and Maleae shared a reciprocal translocation event, involving two fissions and two fusions, which gave rise to chromosomes 1 and 9 in G. trifoliata. Subsequently, the MRCA of Maleae experienced more extensive rearrangements, including seven fissions and eight chromosome fusions. A key event was the fusion of chromosome 3 and chromosome 6, reducing the Maleae chromosome number from 18 to 17. Subsequently, no interchromosomal rearrangements occurred in M. domestica, P. communis or P. sinensis, whereas E. japonica and C. pinnatifida diverged via species-specific rearrangements: eight fissions and seven fusions in the former, and two fissions and two fusions in the latter. 

These findings reaffirm the closer phylogenetic relationship of P. sinensis to M. domestica and P. communis, validating the 

accuracy of the phylogenetic construction. Collectively, the results elucidate the WGD events in Rosaceae and the karyotype evolution of Maleae, providing critical insights into the genomic dynamics shaping this plant family. 

# Integrated analysis of the metabolome and transcriptome during fruit development

The fruit of P. sinensis contains a diverse array of secondary metabolites that contribute to its antioxidant capacity and medicinal value. However, the biosynthetic mechanisms and metabolic variations underlying these bioactive compounds remain poorly understood. To address this gap, we conducted a comprehensive study using widely targeted metabolomics based on extensive LC–MS/MS data interpretation to analyse P. sinensis fruit metabolites across four developmental stages (S1–S4). 

A total of 1179 metabolites were identified, with flavonoids representing the most abundant class, accounting for $4 6 . 3 5 \%$ of all detected metabolites (Figure 4a). Differential analysis identified key metabolites with significant changes, including increased levels of flavonoids and phenolic acids, such as rutin and caffeoylquinic acid, and a decrease in L-Glutamic acid, N2- Acetyl-L-lysine and Isocitrate. Notably, the accumulation of differentially accumulated metabolites peaked during the S4 stage, with rutin levels reaching the maximum at this phase (Figure $4 \mathsf { b } , \mathsf { c } )$ . Flavonoids and caffeoylquinic acids, specialized bioactive metabolites derived from the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway, play pivotal roles in P. sinensis. Rutin, a key flavonoid, exhibits antibacterial, anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties, highlighting its potential health benefits. The coordinated interplay among rutin, lignin, and the antioxidant capacity of P. sinensis suggests a functionally significant relationship (Alcazar Magana et al., 2021; Battat et al., 2019). 

Allele-specific expression (ASE) analysis of the P. sinensis genome revealed distinct functional roles for its two haplotypes in biological processes. Expressed Haplotype A genes were significantly enriched in KEGG pathways such as ‘starch and sucrose metabolism’ and ‘ubiquitin-mediated proteolysis’, while expressed Haplotype B genes showed significant enrichment in pathways including ‘plant-pathogen interaction’ and ‘flavonoid biosynthesis’ (Figure S9; Table S12).

# Transcriptional landscape and TFs associated with phenylpropanoid pathway in P. sinensis

TFs play a pivotal role in regulating the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway, orchestrating key biosynthetic processes. To delve deeper into this regulatory network, we conducted a comprehensive TF prediction analysis within the yellow module of WGCNA, focusing on genes involved in flavonoid and lignin biosynthesis in P. sinensis. 

Building on WGCNA results, we constructed a comprehensive co-expression network from the yellow module comprising 21 enzyme genes and 117 TFs, all functionally linked to phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Figure 5a; Table S14). These TFs, predominantly belonged to the MYB, bHLH, NAC, WRKY, HD-zip, AP2/ERF and bZIP families (Figure 5b; Table S15), suggesting their crucial roles in phenylpropanoid synthesis. 

Notably, these TFs exhibited significantly elevated expression during the S4 stage, displaying coordinated expression patterns with structural genes and differential metabolite accumulations. This synchronization strongly implicates their regulatory function in rutin and lignin biosynthesis. Among these, the MYB TF PsinChr01HBG004760 (TPM $> 1 0 0$ in S4) was particularly prominent, annotated as ODORANT (ODO1) (Figure 5c; Table S16). Given the established role of ODO1 as a master regulator of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis across species, we further investigated its functions in P. sinensis. Phylogenetic analysis revealed strong evolutionary conservation between PsinChr01HBG004760.1 and ODO orthologues from other species, highlighting its functional significance (Figures 5d and S12). 

Further investigation of the PsinChr01HBG004760 regulatory network revealed its extensive interactions with critical structural genes (CAD8, COMT1, PAL2, RT3 and C4H1) and TFs (including 

bZIP and bHLH family members) (Figure 5e). These molecular connections suggest that PsinChr01HBG004760 may serve as a central regulator, potentially modulating the biosynthesis of both lignin and rutin through direct or indirect mechanisms. We further validated the expression of PsMYBODO1 and the structural genes by RT-qPCR (Figure S13). The observed network architecture positions this MYB TF as a key determinant of phenylpropanoid metabolic flux in P. sinensis, potentially coordinating the production of these biologically and economically important secondary metabolites. 

# PsMYBODO1: A transcriptional activator of flavonoid and lignin biosynthesis in P. sinensis fruits

Our identification of PsMYBODO1, a MYB TF in P. sinensis, reveals its crucial role in regulating flavonoid and lignin biosynthesis during fruit development. Quantitative RT-PCR analysis demonstrated tissue-specific expression patterns, with PsMYBODO1 showing significantly higher transcript levels in fruits compared to flowers and leaves. This expression profile correlated strongly with key structural genes in the phenylpropanoid pathway, particularly lignin-related PsCAD and flavonoid-associated PsPAL (Figure S13). 

Subcellular localization studies indicated that PsMYBODO1 is present in the cell membrane and the nucleus (Figure 6a), consistent with its predicted function as a transcriptional regulator. Transient overexpression experiments, verified by fluorescence imaging (Figure 6b), resulted in significant metabolic changes in rutin and lignin contents (Figure 6c,d). These changes were accompanied by coordinated upregulation of pathway genes such as PsRT3, PsPAL, PsCAD, PsC4H1 and PsCOMT (Figure 6e). 

Yeast One-Hybrid (Y1H) assays demonstrated PsMYBODO1’s direct binding to promoters of PsRT3, PsPAL, PsCAD and PsCOMT (Figure 6f). Notably, the concentration of 3-AT suggested that PsMYBODO1 exerts a strong regulatory effect on PsPAL and PsCOMT (Figure 6f). These findings highlight PsMYBODO1 as a master regulator coordinating the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway, simultaneously enhancing both flavonoid and lignin biosynthesis in P. sinensis fruits. 

# Discussion

Pseudocydonia sinensis has long been valued in traditional Chinese medicine due to its rich diversity of secondary metabolites that confer anti-tumour, hepatoprotective and immunomodulatory properties. However, progress in understanding the biosynthetic pathways of these bioactive compounds has been limited by the lack of high-quality genomic resources. Our study addresses this gap by presenting the first chromosomelevel, haplotype-resolved genome assembly for P. sinensis, generated using PacBio HiFi reads and Hi-C scaffolding. This genomic advance not only clarifies phylogenetic relationships within the Maleae tribe but also provides a foundation for comparative genomic studies to facilitate gene functional studies and genetic improvement of this medicinally important species. 

Our phylogenetic analysis confirms the close evolutionary relationship between P. sinensis and other Maleae species, aligning with previous findings (Xiang et al., 2017). Furthermore, Ks and synteny analyses reveal evidence of an additional WGD event specific to the Maleae lineage. The potential adaptive benefits of WGD events during periods of environmental stress are well documented (Fawcett et al., 2009; Song et al., 2020; 

Zhang et al., 2020a). Gene redundancy resulting from WGD can enhance stress resilience, as demonstrated by the preferential retention of cold resistance genes following the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event (Wu et al., 2020). Additionally, gene duplication can lead to the emergence of new functions. Furthermore, the neo-functionalization of duplicated genes can enable novel adaptations, as seen in mangrove species that evolved specialized salt tolerance mechanisms through modifications of MADS-box TFs (Feng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2017). Our GO enrichment analysis on 511 significantly expanded gene families at the Maleae-G. trifoliata divergence node provides additional support for this evolutionary pattern, showing strong enrichment for stress-response functions including responses to wounding, UV radiation, light intensity, hypoxia, flooding and cold stress (Figure S7). Therefore, the results suggest that the Maleae-specific WGD event provided crucial genetic material for environmental adaptation during a period of global climatic instability. 

Our karyotype reconstruction provides important insights into the dynamic chromosomal changes that have occurred during Rosaceae evolution. Following the two WGD events, the P. sinensis genome underwent extensive genome rearrangements shared with other Maleae species, ultimately leading to changes in chromosome number. These substantial karyotype variations likely played a significant role in shaping the species’ distinctive traits. While determining the precise functional consequences of karyotype changes remains methodologically challenging, enrichment analysis offers a productive approach for investigating these relationships. Previous studies in other systems have successfully linked karyotypic changes to phenotypic traits through such approaches. In Gayal, researchers identified significant enrichment of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) associated with structural variations in function categories related to muscle development (Li et al., 2023). Similarly, work in Muntiacus species revealed that karyotype changes in gradually established novel chromatin interactions at fusion sites, with nearby genes enriched for pathways involved in lipid metabolism, bone morphogenesis and melanin production (Yin et al., 2021). Applying this approach to P. sinensis, we focused on eight chromosomal fusion regions that emerged during its evolutionary divergence from ancestral lineages (Table S17; Figure S14). Enrichment analysis of genes located within 50 kb of these rearrangement breakpoints revealed significant overrepresentation in several biologically relevant KEGG pathways, including phenylalanine/tyrosine/ tryptophan biosynthesis, flavone/flavonol biosynthesis and general metabolic pathways. These findings strongly suggest that genes near karyotype rearrangement sites contribute substantially to P. sinensis’s distinctive secondary metabolite profile and flavour characteristics. 

Our multi-omics investigation revealed a significant accumulation of rutin and lignin-related metabolites during the S4 developmental stage of P. sinensis fruits, closely associated with the activation of the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway. Transcriptomic analysis demonstrated coordinated upregulation of key biosynthetic genes during this stage, mirroring the metabolic changes. Through integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic network analysis, we established robust correlations between metabolic shifts and gene expression patterns in phenylpropanoid metabolism. 

Central to these findings was the identification of PsMYBODO1 (PsinChr01HBG004760.1), which emerged as a key regulator of multiple branches within the phenylpropanoid pathway.

Functional characterization confirmed PsMYBODO1’s ability to activate transcription of critical structural genes (PsRT3, PsPAL, PsCAD and PsCOMT ) through direct promoter binding, providing mechanistic insight into its role in modulating flavonoid and lignin biosynthesis. These findings align with established roles of ODO1-like regulators in other species, where they govern 

phenylpropanoid metabolism and volatile compound production (Dudareva and Pichersky, 2008). In petunia, ODO1 regulates floral fragrance by controlling phenylalanine-derived volatiles (Van Moerkercke et al., 2011), while lily LhODO1 directs biosynthesis of aromatic benzene/phenylpropane compounds (Yoshida et al., 2018). Notably, our metabolomic data identified 49 volatile

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

Tender leaves were obtained from P. sinensis growing naturally on the campus of the Economic Arboretum of Northwest A&F University in Yangling, Shaanxi, China $( 3 4 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime } \ N ,$ , $1 0 8 ^ { \circ } 2 4 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { E }$ ) for de novo sequencing. Fruit samples of $P .$ . sinensis were collected for transcriptome and metabolome analyses from the same arboretum. Flowering initiation was recorded on 15 April 2022 (designated as 0 Days After Anthesis, DAA), with fruit samples collected at 100 DAA (S1), 120 DAA (S2), 140 DAA (S3) and 160 DAA (S4). For each stage, three independent biological replicates were used. 

# Genome survey and assembly

Genome characteristics were initially assessed through k-mer analysis $( k = 2 1 )$ ) using Jellyfish v2.2.10 (Marcais and Kingsford, 2011), with genome size and heterozygosity estimated using GenomeScope 2.0 (Vurture et al., 2017) with parameters -k $2 1 - 1 = - 1 - 1$ . For chromosome-level assembly, we employed an integrated approach combining PacBio HiFi reads and Hi-C data. The Hifiasm assembler (v0.16.1-r375) (Cheng et al., 2021) generated haplotype-resolved contigs (Haplotypes A and B) using default parameters. ALLHiC (v. 0.9.8) (Zhang et al., 2019) was used for contig scaffolding. The final scaffold correction was performed through manual curation. Assembly quality was evaluated using multiple metrics: BUSCO completeness (v5.4.5, embryophyta_odb10 dataset) (Simao et al., 2015), LAI (Ou and Jiang, 2018), and Merqury $( k = 2 0 )$ (Rhie et al., 2020). Telomeric regions were identified by screening for the canonical plant telomere repeat sequence ‘AAACCCT’. 

# Genome annotation

Repetitive elements were identified using Extensive de novo TE Annotator (EDTA) pipeline (v2.0.0) (Ou et al., 2019). Protein-coding gene structures were predicted through a combination strategy involving ab initio prediction, transcriptomes and homology-based gene prediction via the MAKER2 pipeline (Holt and Yandell, 2011). Iso-seq RNA sequencing data was used for gene model prediction. For homology-based prediction, we used GeMoMa (Keilwagen et al., 2019) with protein sequences from three sequenced plants as homologous protein evidence, including Prunus persica, Rosa chinensis and 

Arabidopsis thaliana. For ab initio prediction, predicted gene models were integrated using SNAP (v1.0) (Korf, 2004) and AUGUSTUS (v3.5.0) (Stanke et al., 2006). The consensus gene set was derived from all the aforementioned data and predictions. EVidenceModeler (EVM) (Haas et al., 2008) was used to integrate the gene model predictions from various sources. The completeness of gene annotation was evaluated using BUSCO (v5.4.5, embryophyta_odb10 dataset) (Simao et al., 2015). 

# Comparative genomic and phylogenetic reconstruction

We conducted a comparative genome analysis involving the genomes of Malus domestica, Pyrus communis, Eriobotrya japonica, Chaenomeles pinnatifida, Gleditsia trifoliata, Prunus persica, Prunus avium, Rubus chingii, Fragaria vesca, Rosa chinensis, Dichroa drummondii and Pseudocydonia sinensis. For phylogenetic inference, we employed both the concatenationbased approach and the coalescent-based tree summary approach, as used in a former study (Zhao et al., 2021). 

CSSC were utilized as whole-genome derived gene markers for this analysis. We performed searches for all potential inter- and intra-species homologous gene pairs using DIAMOND $( { \mathsf e } < 1 { \mathsf e } - 1 0$ and top five matches). Synteny blocks were detected using MCScanX (Wang et al., 2012) with recommended parameters (A5G25). In the resulting synteny network, nodes represented collinear genes within collinear blocks, while edges connected collinear gene pairs. To identify synteny clusters, we applied the Infomap algorithm (v.1.4.1) (Rosvall and Bergstrom, 2008) within the map equation framework, utilizing a two-level partitioning mode with 10 trials (--clu -N 10 2). These clusters were transformed into a binary data matrix, where rows represented species and columns represented clusters. Clusters containing a single gene per species were selected as CSSCs. 

For the concatenation approach, multiple sequence alignments of each CSSC were performed using MAFFT (v7.515) (Katoh and Standley, 2013). These alignments were trimmed with trimAl (v1.4. rev15) (Capella-Gutierrez et al., 2009) using the automatic heuristic method ( automated1). Concatenated gene sequence alignments, encompassing 12 species and totalling 269 486 amino acids, were analysed using RAxML (v8.2.12) (Stamatakis, 2014) under the ‘PROTGAMMAAUTO’ model, with 1000 bootstrap replicates. For the coalescent approach, we constructed phylogenetic trees of 512 CSSC alignments using IQ-TREE (v2.2.0.3) (Nguyen et al., 2015), which served as the input for ASTRAL-Pro2 (Zhang and Mirarab, 2022) to infer the species tree. 

# Divergence time and gene family dynamics

To evaluate divergence times among the species, we employed MCMCTree from the PAML (v4.9) package (Yang, 2007), utilizing five fossil calibration points (Table S18). We referenced the TimeTree database (http://www.timetree.org/) (Kumar et al., 2022) to ensure temporal accuracy. Gene family expansion and contraction patterns were analysed using CAFE (v4.1) (De Bie et al., 2006), excluding gene families with ${ > } 2 0 0$ total members and families with zero genes in any species. 

# Whole-genome duplication inference

To detect whole-genome duplication (WGD) events in P. sinensis, we estimated the synonymous substitution rate $( K S )$ . The process began by self-aligning the protein sequences of P. sinensis, Malus domestica, Gleditsia trifoliata, Rosa chinensis and Prunus persica using DIAMOND (Buchfink et al., 2021) with an E-value threshold of $1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ . The top 10 BLAST matches from each alignment 

were used as input for the WGDI tool (Sun et al., 2022) to identify collinear blocks, requiring a minimum of five anchor points and allowing a maximum gap size of 25 genes between anchors. 

For all detected collinear gene pairs, Ks values were calculated using the Nei–Gojobori method (Nei and Gojobori, 1986) with Jukes-Cantor correction, implemented through Codeml in PAML (Yang, 2007). To identify potential WGD events, we analysed the median Ks values of intra- and inter-specific collinear blocks using Gaussian kernel density estimation ( pf parameter in WGDI). This approach allowed us to distinguish between shared and lineagespecific WGD events within Maleae species by comparing Ks distributions across the analysed genomes. The integration of these methods provided robust evidence for both recent and ancient WGD events in P. sinensis while controlling for potential artefacts such as local duplications or assembly errors. 

# Ancestral karyotype construction

To infer the number of ancestral chromosomes and the evolutionary trajectory of chromosomes in the Rosaceae family, we employed the telomere chromosome recombination model (Wang et al., 2015). Ancestral protochromosomes were inferred through comparative analysis of shared chromosome-like synteny blocks (CLSBs). For pairs of chromosomes that exhibited complete homology, we designated those that contained extensive synteny anchor genes as the ancestral chromosomes. Initially, by comparing Maleae species with their sister species G. trifoliata, we inferred nine protochromosomes constituting the ancestral Gillenia and Maleae karyotype (AGMK). Subsequently, comparative analysis between AGMK and Prunus $( n = 7 )$ ), as well as Rubus, Fragaria and Rosa (each $n = 8 ,$ ), enabled us to reconstruct nine protochromosomes representing the ARK. Finally, we verified the accuracy of ARK by comparing it to the Ancestral Eukaryotic Karyotype (AEK) constructed by previous studies (Wang et al., 2022). This validation process ensured that our karyotype inference was robust and aligned with established evolutionary models. 

# Transcriptomic analysis

Total RNA was extracted from the peel and flesh of P. sinensis fruit at four developmental stages, with three biological replicates for each tissue type (except the flesh sample from stage S1). The extracted RNA underwent cDNA library construction and sequencing processes. Briefly, mRNA with a polyA tail was purified using oligo(dT) magnetic beads, fragmented, and used as a template for synthesizing the first and second strands of cDNA. Following cDNA purification, specific sequencing adapters were added, and PCR amplification enriched the library fragments. Quality testing was conducted using Qubit 2.0 and the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer before sequencing the cDNA libraries on the NovaSeq 6000 platform. 

Subsequently, the genome of P. sinensis was indexed using the Kallisto software (v0.50.0) (Bray et al., 2016), and clean reads were quantified to obtain expression levels in Transcripts Per Kilobase of exon model per Million mapped reads (TPM). Differential expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 package (Love et al., 2014), with |log2 fold-change $( \mathsf { F C } ) \mathsf { I } \geq 1$ and $p$ -value ${ \le } 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Allele identification and specific expression

To identify allelic genes, we first assessed the synteny between the two haplotypes of P. sinensis using WGDI (Sun et al., 2022). Subsequently, we filtered syntenic gene pairs based on the 

median Ks value of syntenic block $( K S < 0 . 1 )$ ). Orthologous syntenic genes retained from both haplotypes were considered as allelic genes. All haplotype sequences are simultaneously quantified by Kallisto and alleles with haplotype-specific SNPs exhibit differential read assignment (Bray et al., 2016). Transcriptome data from different stages were analysed separately with the DESeq2 package to identify DEGs, applying a fold change threshold of $^ { < 2 }$ and a corrected $P .$ -value of $- 0 . 0 1$ (Love et al., 2014). DEGs exhibiting consistent expression trends across various tissues and stages were designated as exhibiting ASE. 

# Co-expression and regulatory networks analysis

We utilized the R package WGCNA (Langfelder and Horvath, 2008) to analyse DEGs at various developmental stages of P. sinensis, aiming to identify gene modules with distinct expression patterns. The soft power was set to 16, the minimum module size (minModuleSize) was established at 100 and the module merge threshold (mergeCutHeight) was set to 0.30. To assess the correlation between module eigengenes and metabolite content, we employed the R package psych (https://github. com/cran/psych) (Revelle, 2016). 

For predicting gene regulatory networks, we used the R package GENIE3 (https://github.com/vahuynh/GENIE3/tree/ master/GENIE3_R) (Huynh-Thu et al., 2010). The resulting gene regulatory networks were then visualized using Cytoscape (Shannon et al., 2003). 

# TF analysis

We employed iTAK (Zheng et al., 2016) and PlantTFDB (v3.0) (Jin et al., 2017) to identify TFs from protein sequences. Coexpression networks linking TFs and genes involved in phenylpropanoid synthesis were constructed using the R package WGCNA (Langfelder and Horvath, 2008). The network connections were visualized using Cytoscape (Shannon et al., 2003). To identify cis-acting regulatory elements, we defined the 2000-bp sequences upstream of key genes as the promoter regions and analysed these regions using PlanRegMap (Tian et al., 2020). 

# Phylogenetic analysis

Sequence alignments were generated with MAFFT (Katoh and Standley, 2013) using default parameters. Phylogenetic trees were obtained using IQ-TREE (v2.2.0.3) (Nguyen et al., 2015), and also with default parameters. The resulting phylogenetic trees were visualized and coloured using the iTOL website (v6) (Letunic and Bork, 2021). 

# Enrichment analysis of KEGG and GO

GO term and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses were carried out using the KOBAS 3.0 web server (Bu et al., 2021). The statistical test method was Fisher’s Exact Test and the false discovery rate (FDR) correction method was Benjamini and Hochberg. GO terms and KEGG pathways with an FDR (qvalue) $< 0 . 0 5$ were regarded as significantly enriched. 

# Subcellular localization assay

The cDNA sequences of PsMYBODO1 were inserted into the pSuper1300-GFP vector. The resulting recombinant plasmid was introduced into GV3101. Subsequently, Agrobacterium was resuspended to an OD value of 0.8, and the recombinant plasmid was transferred into the tobacco leaves by injection. The injected area was incubated for 8 h in the dark and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ under low-light conditions, and then was observed using a confocal laser 

scanning microscopy with the 460, 488, and 532-nm laser (LEICA TCS SP8). 

# Transient expression assays

To overexpress PsMYBODO1, the open reading frame (ORF) of MYBODO1 was cloned into the PC1300 vector, resulting in the construct 35S:MYBODO1. This expression vector was then introduced into the Agrobacterium strain EH105. Positive Agrobacterium strains were subsequently injected into the fruit peels. The fruits were incubated in an artificial climate chamber for $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ under a $1 6 \mathsf { h } / 8 \mathsf { h }$ (light/dark) photoperiod $( 7 5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Following this incubation period, fruit peels at the injection sites were collected for gene expression analysis and related experiments. 

# RT–PCR validation

Samples from $P .$ . sinensis flowers, leaves, fruits and fruits overexpressing the PsMYBODO1 were collected for validation. Total RNA was extracted from the samples using the RNA Plant Plus kit (Tiangen, Beijing, China). Single-stranded cDNA was synthesized through reverse transcription using the PrimeScript™ RT kit, following the manufacturer’s protocol. Quantitative reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was performed with three biological replicates to ensure reliability. The primers used for qPCR analysis are listed in Table S19, with amplification performed under the following conditions: initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 3 0 5 , }$ followed by 40 cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 s and $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $3 0 s$ . The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C t }$ method with P. sinensis actin as the internal reference gene. This approach ensured accurate quantification of PsMYBODO1 expression patterns across different tissues and transgenic materials.
