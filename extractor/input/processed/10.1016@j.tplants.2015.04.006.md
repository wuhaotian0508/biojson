# Transport of defense compounds from source to sink: lessons learned from glucosinolates

Morten Egevang Jørgensen, Hussam Hassan Nour-Eldin, and Barbara Ann Halkier

DynaMo, DNRF Center of Excellence, Department of Plant and Environmental Sciences, Faculty of Science, University of Copenhagen, 1871 Frederiksberg C, Denmark

Plants synthesize a plethora of defense compounds crucial for their survival in a challenging and changing environment. Transport processes are important for shaping the distribution pattern of defense compounds, albeit focus hitherto has been mostly on their biosynthetic pathways. A recent identification of two glucosinolate transporters represents a breakthrough in our understanding of glucosinolate transport in Arabidopsis and has advanced knowledge in transport of defense compounds. In this review, we discuss the role of the glucosinolate transporters in establishing dynamic glucosinolate distribution patterns and source–sink relations. We focus on lessons learned from glucosinolate transport that may apply to transport of other defense compounds and discuss future avenues in the emerging field of defense compound transport.

# Transport of defense compounds

Plants synthesize thousands of specialized metabolites that are crucial for their survival [1]. As stated in the optimal defense theory, defense compounds (a major category of specialized metabolites) accumulate to high levels in tissues of highest fitness value [2]. Status as a tissue of highest fitness value is dynamic and changes in response to developmental cues (e.g., transition from vegetative to reproductive tissues) [3] and upon biotic and abiotic stresses [4,5]. As a consequence, defense compounds are continuously (re)distributed and/or de novo synthesized in different parts of the plant. Accordingly, a given distribution pattern reflects a highly coordinated (re)programming of synthesis and transport. However, we are only beginning to understand the mechanisms that determine how defense compounds are transported because only a few transporters of defense compounds have been identified. This includes nicotine transporters in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) [6–8] and berberine transporters in Coptis japonica [9–11]. However, the alkaloid-producing species C. japonica and N. tabacum do not provide optimal model systems for studying transport of defense compounds due to the

current shortcoming of community tools (e.g., comprehensive mutant collections and readily available ‘omics databases). This complicates in planta characterization upon transporter identification, and currently no experimental evidence (e.g., changed alkaloid distribution pattern in mutants) shows whether the hitherto identified alkaloid transporters have a role in long-distance transport. Here, we use transport of glucosinolates as a case study and discuss, in a general defense compound context, the lessons learned from transport of glucosinolates in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana). This review is intended as a starting point to aid other researchers in the identification and characterization of specialized metabolite transporters.

# Glucosinolates in Arabidopsis: a model system for transport of defense compounds

It is inherently difficult to identify transporters of defense compounds. Classical functional complementation of yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) auxotrophic mutants has identified numerous plant transporters of primary metabolites [12–18]. However, this method is ill suited for identifying transporters of defense compounds (and specialized metabolites in general) because these are often not essential for yeast growth. Instead, transporters of defense compounds have been identified by substrate-induced expression analysis and based on co-expression with biosynthesis genes [7–10].

Recently, a functional genomics approach identified two Arabidopsis transporters, AtNPF2.10 (AtGTR1) and AtNPF2.11 (AtGTR2), which transport the glucosinolate defense compounds (Box 1) [19,20]. A library of Arabidopsis transporters was expressed in Xenopus laevis oocytes and screened for glucosinolate uptake [19,20]. This novel approach is likely to be applicable for identifying transporters of any given metabolite.

In leaves, AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 were shown to transport glucosinolates across the plasma membrane into phloem companion cells, with AtGTR1 fulfilling an additional role in glucosinolate transport into mesophyll cells in rosette leaves [19]. In roots, AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 were found to predominantly localize to cortex cells and root vasculature [21]. As a consequence, this suggests a role of glucosinolates in phloem loading (Figure 1). # Box 1. Glucosinolates are amino acid-derived defense compounds

Arabidopsis primarily synthesizes methionine-derived aliphatic and tryptophan-derived indole glucosinolates. The biosynthetic pathway includes: (i) chain elongation of amino acid side chains; (ii) core structure formation; and (iii) modification of amino acid side chains [62–64]. Aliphatic glucosinolates with three to five methylene groups are called short-chained glucosinolates and those with six and higher methylene groups are called long-chained glucosinolates. The glucosinolate side chain often undergoes modification. A frequent modification of aliphatic glucosinolates is the S-oxygenation of methylthioalkyl glucosinolates by flavin-monooxygenases to form methylsulfinylalkyl glucosinolates (Figure IB) [65,66]. For indole glucosinolates, the indole ring can be hydroxylated and methoxylated by P450 monooxygenases and methyltransferases (Figure IC) [67]. Upon tissue disruption by pathogens or herbivores, glucosinolates come into contact with myrosinases (thioglucoside glucohydrolase), resulting primarily in the formation of the biologically active isothiocyanates and nitriles [62].

In Arabidopsis Col-0, methylthioalkyl glucosinolates predominantly accumulate in the seed, whereas methylsulfinylalkyl glucosinolates are the major form in all other tissues [3]. The underlying biological significance of this distribution pattern is not clear, but the methylthioalkyl and methylsulfinylalkyl glucosinolates have been proposed to show different biological potency against herbivores $( { \mathsf { B o x } } 2 )$ [41,42]. For indole glucosinolates, methoxylation of the indole side chain is induced upon fungal attack, suggesting a specific role in defense against fungi [68,69].

# Source and sink tissues for defense compounds

Partitioning of synthesis and storage of defense compounds into separate organs and/or cells appears to be a common characteristic in plants: nicotine in tobacco (synthesized in roots and transported to the shoot via the xylem) [24]; berberine in C. japonica (synthesized in root and transported to starch-rich cells of the rhizome) [9,10]; cyanogenic glucosides in cassava (Manihot esculenta; synthesized in leaves and transported to roots via the phloem) [25,26]; and glucosinolates in brassicas (synthesized in maternal tissues and transported to seeds) [22,23,27,28]. From a strategic point of view, separation of site of storage (e.g., laticifer cells; Box 3) from site of synthesis may protect the metabolically highly active biosynthetic cells from potential cytotoxic effects of defense compounds [29], similar to keeping poison depots. It is also conceivable that the spatial separation is a consequence of resource economization with centrally localized biosynthetic machinery that delivers to several destinations.

Several maternal tissues have been suggested as source tissue for the glucosinolates that accumulate in seeds, including siliques [22,30] and the rosette [27,28,31]. Overaccumulation of glucosinolates in both rosette and siliques of atgtr1 atgtr2 suggested that both tissues were potential sources [19]. However, so far, there is no experimental evidence to conclude whether siliques and/or the rosette are source tissue(s) for seed glucosinolates. Reciprocal grafting of stem with the rosette of three genotypes [wild type, the biosynthetic null mutant (myb28 myb29 cyp79B2 cyp79B3 [32]), and atgtr1 atgtr2] may determine whether GTRs mediate long-distance transport of glucosinolates from rosette to reproductive organs.

Noticeably, the atgtr1 atgtr2 leaves specifically overaccumulate long-chained, aliphatic glucosinolates that normally reside in roots [21]. This suggests that roots are sink tissue for long-chained, aliphatic glucosinolates produced in the rosette, which had been generally assumed based on reports that roots have no de novo synthesis [33]. It has been proposed that the overaccumulation in rosette leaves reflects blockage of phloem loading in the absence of the GTRs. Much to our surprise, this is not the case, as evidenced by micrografting experiments (see below).

# Box 3. S-cells accumulate high concentrations of glucosinolates

Sequestration of defense compounds into laticifer cells is a wellknown means for storage of defense compounds in more than 20 plant families, such as opium poppy (Papaver somniferum), rubber trees (Hevea brasiliensis), and Madagascar periwinkle (Catharantus roseus) [29]. Only a few examples exist of laticifer cells with biosynthetic capacity, such as morphine biosynthesis in opium poppy, where (only) the last steps in the biosynthetic pathway occur in the laticifer cells [71]. This suggests that transport processes are required for the sequestration of defense compounds into their storage cells. In Arabidopsis, a special type of glucosinolate-accumulating, laticifer-like cells, the so-called ‘S-cells’, shield the vascular tissue along the flower stalk [39,40]. The thin-walled Scells are extremely large, with a length of up to $1 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and a diameter of $3 0 \mu \mathsf { m } _ { i }$ , and they are located around the phloem zone of each vascular bundle. In Arabidopsis Col-0 accession, the estimated glucosinolate concentration of these cells was more than $1 3 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ total glucosinolates [40]. When punctured by herbivores, the S-cell works as a high-pressure mustard bomb. At the site of attack, the large glucosinolate content is released and mixed with the hydrolyzing myrosinase to produce the deterring hydrolysis products [39]. Proteomics on cytoplasm from S-cells detected no glucosinolate biosynthetic enzymes, suggesting that transport processes are necessary for accumulation of glucosinolates in Scells [72]. # Plasticity of source and sink tissues

For primary metabolites, source tissues are fixed in the sense that, for example, roots are not capable of photosynthesis, whereas sink tissue status changes dynamically upon developmental and environmental cues. For defense compounds, feeding studies in Arabidopsis showed that sink tissue for leaf-fed glucosinolates changes during development from roots of 3-week-old plants to the inflorescence of bolting 5-week-old plants concomitant with the transition of the plants from vegetative to reproductive growth stage. In the absence of the two GTRs, glucosinolates did not load onto the phloem for long-distance transport, and the source leaves were subsequently turned into permanent sinks in atgtr1 atgtr2 [35]. This suggests that the expression pattern of transport proteins is a major factor in determining source or sink status of a given tissue, and that transporter mutants contribute to identify source and sink tissues for defense compounds.

For defense compounds, source tissues appear to be plastic. For example, in cassava, where cyanogenic glucosides synthesized in the leaves are transported to the tubers, which normally have no de novo synthesis, RNAi lines were designed to target specifically in leaves the first committed step in cyanogenic glucoside biosynthesis. The leaves accumulate less than $1 \%$ cyanogenic glucosides; however, in several of these lines, tubers accumulate wild type levels of cyanogenic glucosides [25]. This shows that the site of biosynthesis of cyanogenic glucosides can change based on the source–sink status of other tissues. In Arabidopsis atgtr1 atgtr2 mutants, aliphatic glucosinolates were both redistributed from root to shoot, and reached levels in the whole plant that were above wild type levels [21]. This suggests that a regulatory feedback mechanism senses the reduction in long-chained, aliphatic glucosinolates in the root of the atgtr1 atgtr2, leading to an increase in glucosinolate biosynthesis. This exemplifies the plastic nature of glucosinolate source tissues and shows that transporters can influence the biosynthetic activity of a source tissue.

# Transport of defense compounds within a leaf

Evolutionarily, plants have developed different non-uniform distribution patterns of defense compounds within leaves to optimize resistance against herbivory [4,36]. Examples include accumulation of cyanogenic glucosides in epidermal cells of Sorghum bicolor [37], salvinorin (a psychoactive diterpene) in glandular trichomes of Salvia divinorum, and opium in laticifer cells of Papaver somniferum [38]. Currently, little is known about how such distinct distribution patterns are established. In an Arabidopsis leaf, glucosinolates accumulate in non-uniform patterns with high concentrations in large longitudinal cells along the margin and in the so-called sulfur-rich, laticifer-like S-cells along the vasculature (Box 3) [39–41]. Moreover, in vivo mass

spectrometry imaging shows that the epidermis accumulates $1 - 5 \%$ of total leaf glucosinolates with an abaxial:adaxial concentration ratio of 15–20 [42]. The higher levels of glucosinolates on the more vulnerable guard cell-dense abaxial side of the leaf underline the strategic importance of a non-uniform distribution of defense compounds (Box 2). Importantly, the intra-leaf distribution of glucosinolates appears to encompass a range of patterns also seen for other defense compounds.

The expression patterns of glucosinolate biosynthetic genes locate them along the vasculature, with no apparent expression along the leaf margin or in the epidermis cells [43–48]. Thus, accumulation of glucosinolates in these cells (which do not de novo synthesize glucosinolates) suggests that transport processes are essential for distribution of glucosinolates to the defensive outer perimeter tissues. Detailed analyses of glucosinolate content within the leaf (i.e., in dissected leaf parts, stripped epidermal layers, apoplastic fluids, and guttation drops) showed that atgtr1 atgtr2 mutants overaccumulate glucosinolates in the leaf margin and in the abaxial epidermis compared with wild type [49]. This was unexpected because we thought (intuitively and in hindsight naively) that, without transporters, the glucosinolates would not be moved. The overall increased accumulation was substantially skewed towards the tip of the leaf and was accompanied by an increase in glucosinolate content in guttation drops and apoplastic fluids. From this, we learned that the activity of the glucosinolate importers AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 is required for proper intra-leaf distribution of glucosinolates both along the vasculature and to the outer leaf perimeters (i.e., margin, abaxial epidermis, and base of the leaf). A model for establishing glucosinolate distribution patterns in leaves has been proposed that includes a GTR-dependent import of glucosinolates into symplasmic domains. According to this model, glucosinolates are moved via connecting plasmodesmata to storage cells in the outer defensive perimeters along glucosinolate gradients created by vacuolar importers. Thus, the proposed role of the two plasma membrane-localized glucosinolate importers in leaves includes import into not only phloem for long-distance transport to other organs, but also symplasmic domains for intra-leaf transport (Figure 1) [49].

# The rhizosphere: a sink for defense compounds

Rhizosphere deposition of defense compounds is important in plant defense against root herbivory and in shaping the microbial community of the rhizosphere [50–52]. Examples include exudation of the phytoanticipin rhizathalene from Arabidopsis roots [53] and the phytoalexin momilactone from rice (Oryza sativa) roots [54]. Arabidopsis exudes a range of metabolites, including glucosinolate breakdown products [55–57], which are suggested to affect the microbial community in the rhizosphere [58,59]. Interestingly, glucosinolates from turnip roots (Brassica rapa ssp. rapa) are exuded into hydroponic growth media in an inducible manner [60], suggesting that plants actively regulate glucosinolate exudation. This further suggests that transport processes are crucial for glucosinolate release to the rhizosphere, such as directly by export or by accumulation of

glucosinolates in the outermost cells that are released by mechanical shearing.

A large-scale metabolomics study of five root cell types reported that aliphatic glucosinolates predominantly accumulate in the cortex layer, whereas indole glucosinolates predominantly localize to the columella with less in cortex cells [33]. Interestingly, the distribution pattern of aliphatic glucosinolates correlated with the apparent expression pattern of AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 in root cortex [21], indicating that the GTRs also have a role in intraroot glucosinolate distribution (Figure 1). In addition to the redistribution of long-chained, aliphatic glucosinolates from the root to the shoot, the entire atgtr1 atgtr2 plant overaccumulated approximately $30 \%$ more total glucosinolates per plant compared with wild type [21]. We speculate that the $30 \%$ increase corresponds to glucosinolates that in wild type plants would be exuded from the roots, but in the transporter mutants are translocated to the inflorescence.

Germinating seeds (that do not produce glucosinolates) lose $30 \%$ of their glucosinolate content between day 2 and day 8 after germination. This observation was interpreted as evidence for catabolism of glucosinolates [3]. Alternatively, the loss of glucosinolates could be the result of exudation to the rhizosphere. The latter is supported by the detection of isothiocyanates (glucosinolate hydrolysis products; Box 1) in root exudates of Brassica plants. Further studies are needed to unravel the mechanisms for exudation of defense compounds and other specialized metabolites to the rhizosphere, and to learn whether exudation is a means to cultivate the bacterial population surrounding the growing seedling.

# Concluding remarks

Our understanding of defense compound transport processes and their role in maintaining an optimal defense compound distribution has been hampered by a low number of characterized transporters of defense compounds. Identification of the glucosinolate transporters AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 in Arabidopsis provided an important first molecular tool to investigate the contribution of transport processes in shaping glucosinolate distribution patterns. The major lesson learned so far is that we had to carefully consider the tissue context when investigating the role of transporters that we initially thought would only be longdistance transporters of glucosinolates from maternal tissues to seeds. Surprisingly, the same transporters also have an essential role in retaining glucosinolates in roots and in establishing proper intra-leaf distribution by loading glucosinolates into symplasmic domains. In addition, we were surprised that, although belonging to the same class of defense compounds, even subtle differences in glucosinolate molecular structure (e.g., short- versus long-chained, aliphatic glucosinolates) resulted in different source–sink relationship in Arabidopsis. This indicates that the distribution of defense compounds in plants is tightly regulated with a high degree of specificity. Finally, synthesis, transport, and storage appear to be ‘cross-talking’ as evidenced by the dynamic source–sink relation between organs. # Box 4. A possible link between phytohormone and defense compound transport

Biosynthesis of glucosinolates is regulated by several phytohormones, including jasmonates, ethylene, and salicylic acid [73]. Several studies have shown a direct link between jasmonic acid (JA) signaling and glucosinolate biosynthesis [74–76]. Expression of AtGTR1 is induced by JA treatment [19], which suggests an involvement in plant response to biotic stress. Interestingly, a recent publication proposed that AtGTR1 is multifunctional and may be involved in hormone transport in Arabidopsis [77]. Uptake studies with JA-isoleucine (JA-Ile) and gibberellin GA3 (which is not found in plants) in a 24-h in vitro uptake assay in Xenopus (Xenopus laevis) oocytes showed GTR1-dependent import of phytohormones. However, this transport activity was strongly competed by glucosinolates [77], which are abundant in reproductive tissues. Moreover, atgtr1 mutants were severely impaired in filament elongation and anther dehiscence resulting in reduced fertility when grown in continuous light conditions. Such phenotypes were not observed under normal day/light conditions [19].

The strong glucosinolate-free seed phenotype of the atgtr1 atgtr2 mutant clearly shows that AtGTR1 has a primary role in glucosinolate transport. Thus, it remains to be proven whether the observed phenotypes are due to a role for AtGTR1 in phytohormone transport or reflect an effect of altered glucosinolate distribution in atgtr1.

We are only beginning to unravel the intricate dynamics of glucosinolate transport in Arabidopsis. As an example of outstanding questions, we highlight the proposed vacuolar storage of glucosinolates that is likely to require transport into and export out of the vacuole during storage and remobilization, respectively. Similarly, export across the plasma membrane (of mesophyll and storage cells) and into the apoplast is expected to be a prerequisite step before phloem loading by AtGTR1 and AtGTR2 (Figure 1). It is currently unknown how many of these transport processes are carried out by transporters or by vesicle-mediated processes, as has been suggested for flavonoids (Figure 1) [61]. Accordingly, more knowledge is needed to fully understand glucosinolate transport processes in Arabidopsis.

It is interesting to note that defense compounds from various species show profound similarities with regards to separation of biosynthesis and storage (e.g., laticifer-like storage cells along the veins and at the outer perimeter) that necessitate transport. This suggests that the lessons learnt from glucosinolate transport processes in Arabidopsis are transferable to other species and, thus, will inspire the study of transport of defense compounds, as well as other specialized metabolites (Box 4).
