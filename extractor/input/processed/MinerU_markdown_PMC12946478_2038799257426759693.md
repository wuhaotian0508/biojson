# ABSTRACT

The curd is a unique indeterminate inflorescence structure, which is the most important edible organ of broccoli. Premature bolting of the curd is a major factor that limits the yield and marketability of broccoli. However, the formation and development of broccoli curd, especially the bolting of curd, remain poorly understood. In this study, BolAG, a homologous gene of the floral development gene AtAG, was highly expressed in curd and floral organ of broccoli. Overexpression of BolAG in broccoli significantly promoted curd development, leading to early bolting. Correspondingly, RNA interference (RNAi) of BolAG resulted in arrested curd development, leading to delayed or even absent bolting. Physiological analyses indicated that BolAG-overexpressing broccoli exhibited higher starch and soluble sugar contents (SSC) compared to the wild-type (WT). RNA-Seq analysis also identified significant enrichment of genes related to starch metabolism. Among them, the starch hydrolysis-related gene BolBAM4 was confirmed to be the target gene of BolAG. Additionally, BolAG was confirmed to interact with ribose-5-phosphate isomerase 2 (BolRPI2), a protein involved in the pentose phosphate pathway and their interaction enhanced the transcriptional promotion of BolAG on BolBAM4, accelerating starch degradation and thereby leading to the accumulation of soluble sugars. While BolRPI2 was demonstrated to have a positive effect on chloroplast function and promote starch synthesis, BolAG appeared to promote starch synthesis in a BolRPI2- independent manner. These results confirm that the BolAG-BolRPI2-BolBAM4 module plays a crucial role in regulating starch metabolism, providing a theoretical foundation for addressing industrial losses due to premature bolting. 

# 1 | Introduction

Broccoli (Brassica oleracea var. italica) is a significant vegetable crop in the cruciferous family with high nutritional and health value. The edible organ of broccoli, known as the curd, is composed 

of numerous short branches forming an indeterminate inflorescence. The developmental pattern of the curd structure and the factors influencing curd production have been the subjects of extensive research, primarily in cauliflower, a close relative of broccoli (Kieffer et al. 1998; Cao et al. 2020). Early studies identified 

that BobAP1 and BobCAL are the dominant regulatory factors responsible for the cessation of curd development in cauliflower (Goslin et  al.  2017). Genomic comparisons between cauliflower and cabbage have highlighted several structural variants crucial for the distinct cauliflower phenotype, implicating potential negative regulatory genes in the floral meristem, such as BobAGL14, BobSVP and BobCCE1 (Guo et  al.  2021). For the specific spiral structure observed in cauliflower, a SALT network (SAX, AP1/ CAL, LFY and TFL1) has been constructed to explain the formation and development of curd (Azpeitia et al. 2021). Research on structural variations (SVs) of flowering-related genes in the whole genome of cabbage has revealed that BoFLC2 plays a critical role in vernalization and flowering differentiation, while 39 variations in the first intron of BoFLC3 participate in the fine-tuning of the cauliflower flowering process (Guo et al. 2024). In addition, a highdensity genetic map consisting of 2741 SNPs has been constructed to investigate the cessation of floral development, revealing that cauliflower curd formation and development are regulated by multiple genes through a complex regulatory mechanism (Irwin et al. 2016). Nevertheless, the molecular mechanism of curd formation and development in broccoli as well as in cauliflower is still mysterious. 

Bolting is the phenomenon of elongation of the flower stem before flowering in some plant species, which is caused by autonomous developmental regulation and influenced by environmental factors such as temperature, photoperiod and plant hormones (Meng et  al.  2022). High temperature accelerates the bolting of lettuce, leading to bitter leaves and reduced marketability (Han et al. 2021; Simonne et al. 2002). Knockdown of LsFT and LsSOC1 resulted in a significant delay in lettuce bolting time and insensitivity to high temperature (Chen, Han, et al. 2018; Chen, Zhao, et al. 2018; Yoo et al. 2005; Blümel et al. 2015). In Angelica sinensis, premature bolting leads to nutrient transfer from the roots to the reproductive organs, causing root lignification and a loss of medicinal value (Yang et al. 2022; Li et al. 2022). Bolting time in Chinese cabbage and turnips of the Brassica genus also seriously affects the yield and quality of harvested products. Studies identified candidate genes such as FLC and FT related to bolting time through the construction of a linkage map and analysis of QTL (Nishioka et  al.  2005; Kakizaki et  al.  2011). Additionally, plant hormones such as gibberellins (Wang et  al.  2022; Schomburg et  al.  2003), auxins (Schenck et al. 2010) and abscisic acid (Re et al. 2011) have been identified as important regulators of bolting and flowering. In broccoli, the bolting time of curd affects the maturity, harvest time and compactness of the curd. Early bolting is one of the most destructive factors that directly impairs the quality and economic value of broccoli. However, the regulatory mechanisms underlying bolting in broccoli remain limited (Rosen et al. 2018). 

MADS-box transcription factors (TFs) play crucial roles in determining floral organ identity (Theißen et  al.  2016), as well as in the development of fruits, seeds, leaves and roots (Baowen et  al.  2017; Alvarez-Buylla et  al.  2019). The class C MADS-box gene AGAMOUS (AG) is involved in floral organ identity, floral meristem determinacy and fruit development (Kramer et  al.  2004). In Arabidopsis, the TFs LEAFY (LFY) and WUSCHEL (WUS) bind to the second intron of AG, activating its expression (Lohmann et al. 2001). AG recruits CURLY LEAF (CLF) to methylate and inactivate WUS, while activating the expression of KNUCKLES (KNU), which suppresses 

WUS expression during stage 6 of floral development (Sun et  al.  2009), ultimately terminating the proliferation of undifferentiated cells. During this stage, the AG-WUS-KNU module precisely controls the maintenance of floral meristem stem cells (Liu et al. 2011; Mayer et al. 1998; Lenhard et al. 2001; Payne et al. 2004). Although the function of AG has been extensively studied in model plants like Arabidopsis, its role in broccoli remains unclear. 

Plants coordinate growth and development through nutrient supply. Sugars produced by photosynthesis regulate various biological processes, including flowering time, the onset of senescence, embryogenesis, seed germination, seedling development and tuber formation (Eveland and Jackson 2012). In flowering crops, bolting time directly determines plant morphology and yield, with sugars playing a crucial role in the flowering process (Turnbull 2011). Studies have shown that sugars not only serve as energy sources to regulate the growth and flowering transition in apple (Xing et al. 2015), grape (Caspari et al. 1998) and citrus (Shalom et al. 2014), but also function as signalling molecules mediating flowering induction (Wahl et al. 2013). Furthermore, sugar signals generated from the ‘source’ leaves are transported to the shoot apical meristem (SAM) and integrated into the flowering pathway, where the TF AtIDD8 regulates flowering time by modulating sugar transport and metabolism in Arabidopsis (Bernier and Périlleux 2005; Seo et al. 2011). Additionally, the key starch synthase gene Granule-Bound Starch Synthase 1 (GBSS1), which influences starch deposition, promotes flowering by regulating the expression of the CONSTANS (CO) (Serrano-Bueno et  al.  2009). Starch, the primary carbohydrate energy reserve in plants, is synthesised during the day and degraded at night (Streb and Zeeman 2012). Starch degradation provides essential energy for maintaining plant growth and development (Horrer et al. 2016; Flütsch et al. 2020), influencing plant structure, morphology and environmental adaptation (Thalmann et al. 2016; Thalmann and Santelia  2017). However, the impact of starch metabolism on broccoli curd development remains limited. 

In this study, to elucidate the internal regulatory mechanisms underlying curd development in broccoli, we investigated  the role of BolAG, a homologue of Arabidopsis AtAG, previously reported to function in floral organ development. The downstream target genes of BolAG were identified, and the proteins that interact with BolAG were also discovered. Notably, a novel gene module centred on BolAG that regulates starch metabolism and plays a critical role in curd development, particularly in bolting, was revealed. These findings provide novel insights into the regulatory mechanisms governing curd development in broccoli.

# 2.2 | BolAG Plays a Key Role in Regulating the Bolting Process of Broccoli Curd

To investigate the function of BolAG, transgenic broccoli plants with BolAG overexpression and RNA interference (RNAi) silencing were generated. Quantitative analysis revealed that BolAG transcript levels in overexpression lines exceeded wild-type (WT) expression by over 5-fold, whereas RNAi-mediated silencing lines exhibited $a > 8 0 \%$ reduction in BolAG expression compared to WT controls (Figure 4d,f). Following field transplantation, WT broccoli displayed normal curd development throughout maturation. However, RNAi-BolAG lines showed significant developmental arrest at earlier stages, resulting in smaller curds compared to WT plants. Conversely, BolAG-overexpressing broccoli exhibited premature bolting phenotypes (Figure  2a). The average bolting time for BolAG-overexpressing plants was 18days earlier than WT, while RNAi-BolAG plants bolted 13days later than WT (Figure 2b). Additionally, the length of the primary lateral flower stem of BolAG-overexpressing lines was approximately twice 

as long as  that of WT, whereas the primary lateral flower stem of RNAi-BolAG lines was shorter than that of WT (Figure  2c). Furthermore, transcriptional expression levels of BolAG in various broccoli varieties with different bolting times revealed that early-bolting (EB) varieties exhibited significantly higher BolAG expression levels compared to late-bolting varieties (Figure  2d). These results indicate that BolAG is a key regulator in regulating curd development and bolting in broccoli.

# 2.4 | BolAG Positively Regulates BolBAM4, a Key Gene Functioning in Starch Degradation

Among the 214 DEGs involved in starch and sucrose metabolism, the expression of BolBAM4 was significantly upregulated in BolAG-overexpressing plants compared to WT (Figure  4a,b). BolBAM4 exhibited high expression levels in curds, flowers and seeds (Figure 4c). Expression analysis revealed that BolAG overexpression increased the transcription of BolBAM4, while RNAi-silenced lines showed decreased BolBAM4 expression (Figure 4d–g). To verify whether BolAG directly regulates BolBAM4, we analysed the BolBAM4 promoter and identified a CArG motif, which has been widely reported as a target of MADS-box domains. Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays confirmed that BolAG could directly bind to the promoter region of BolBAM4 (Figure 4h). Furthermore, a vector in which the BolBAM4 promoter was fused with the reporter gene luciferase $( \mathrm { P } _ { B o l B A M 4 } { : : } L U C )$ was constructed and co-infiltrated with the 35Spro::BolAG (SK-BolAG) expression vector to uncover the activation of BolAG on BolBAM4 (Figure  4i). The results indicated that the relative luciferase activities of $\mathrm { P } _ { B o l B A M 4 }$ ::LUC co-expressed with SK-BolAG were 

significantly higher than that of the control (Figure  4j,k). These results demonstrate that BolBAM4 is a downstream target gene of BolAG. BolAG directly binds to the promoter of BolBAM4 and positively regulates the transcriptional expression of BolBAM4.

# 2.6 | BolRPI2 Promotes Starch Synthesis and Influences Chloroplast Function

To further explore the function of BolRPI2, we generated BolRPI2- overexpressing and RNAi-silenced broccoli (Figure  6a). Leaf yellowing assay indicated that the leaves of the WT exhibited partial loss of green colour on the 21st day, whereas BolRPI2- overexpressing plants maintained most of their green colour. In contrast, RNAi-BolRPI2 plants displayed severe chlorophyll loss (Figure 6b). Consistent with these observations, chlorophyll content in BolRPI2-overexpressing leaves was significantly higher than in WT, while RNAi-BolRPI2 plants showed significantly reduced chlorophyll levels (Figure 6c). Furthermore, the transcriptional expression levels of chlorophyll biosynthesis genes BolHEM1 and BolGSA1 were significantly upregulated in BolRPI2-overexpressing plants (Figure 6d), while their expression was significantly downregulated in RNAi-BolRPI2 broccoli (Figure  6e). These results demonstrate that BolRPI2 may enhance chlorophyll content by regulating chlorophyll synthesis genes. TEM observations further revealed that chloroplasts in the leaves of BolRPI2-overexpressing plants were both larger (Figure 6f) and more numerous (Figure 6g) compared to the WT, while RNAi-BolRPI2 plants contained smaller and fewer chloroplasts. Additionally, metabolic analysis showed that BolRPI2- overexpressing plants accumulated significantly more starch compared to both WT and RNAi-BolRPI2 plants (Figure  6f, Figure S5a,b). BolRPI2 overexpression also exhibited increased 

SSC (Figure S5c). These results demonstrate that BolRPI2 plays crucial roles in carbohydrate metabolism, maintaining chlorophyll content and chloroplast development. 

# 2.7 | BolAG Negatively Regulates the Transcription of BolRPI2

To investigate whether BolAG affects the transcription of BolRPI2, expression levels of BolRPI2 in BolAG-overexpressing and RNAi-BolAG lines were measured. The results indicated that BolRPI2 expression was downregulated in BolAGoverexpressing plants but upregulated in RNAi-BolAG lines (Figure  7a,b). Y1H assay results confirmed that BolAG can directly bind to the BolRPI2 promoter (Figure 7c). Supporting this finding, GUS reporter assay demonstrated that BolAG has a transcriptional inhibitory effect on BolRPI2 (Figure 7d). Further validation through dual luciferase assays confirmed that co-expression of SK-BolAG with $\mathrm { P } _ { B o l R P I 2 } { \therefore } L U C$ led to significantly lower luminescence intensity and luminescence signal of $\mathrm { P } _ { B o l R P I 2 } { \therefore } L U C$ compared to the control (Figure 7e–g). These results confirmed that BolAG not only interacts with BolRPI2 but also negatively regulates the transcription of BolRPI2. 

# 2.8 | Co-Expression of BolAG and BolRPI2 Enhances BolBAM4 Expression

To explore the synergistic effects of BolAG and BolRPI2 on BolBAM4, dual-luciferase assays were performed. The results indicated that the relative luciferase activity in tobacco leaves with co-expressing BolAG and BolRPI2 was 1.69-fold higher than that in the control (Figure 7h,i). Meanwhile, the luminescence signal produced by $\mathrm { P } _ { B o l B A M 4 } { : : } L U C$ in the co-expression of BolAG and BolRPI2 was significantly higher than that of the control group (Figure 7j). These results indicate that the interaction between BolAG and BolRPI2 synergistically enhances the transcriptional expression of BolBAM4.

Notably, under short-day conditions, ag mutants display partial revision of floral meristems to inflorescence meristems, whereas overexpression of the AG converts both primary and secondary inflorescence meristems into terminal flowers (Mizukami and Ma  1992). In broccoli, the inflorescence loses apical dominance, forming a unique multi-level inflorescence structure. Overexpression of BolAG in broccoli leads to premature bolting in some inflorescences and accelerates the development of the curd, demonstrating functional conservation with AG's role in Arabidopsis. In contrast, RNAi lines exhibit delayed curd development due to inhibited differentiation of inflorescence meristems, which consequently results in delayed floral meristem development. 

During floral organ development, overexpression of AG in Arabidopsis induces the transformation of sepals into carpels and petals into stamens (Bowman and Meyerowitz 1991), whereas ag mutations result in petaloid stamens and indeterminate floral meristems that produce reiterating flowers in place of carpels (Bowman et al. 1991). Similarly, in broccoli, BolAG overexpression leads to progressive degeneration of petals and sepals while maintaining normal carpel development. Conversely, RNAi-mediated suppression of BolAG causes elongation of bracts surrounding the inflorescence and disrupts curd formation (Figure S6). These findings demonstrate that BolAG plays a crucial regulatory role in both bolting and floral organ development during curd formation in broccoli. Given that previous studies have mainly investigated AG function in raceme-type inflorescences (Mizukami and Ma 1992; Kater et al. 1998; Hui et al. 2021), further exploration of how BolAG governs the unique developmental program of broccoli curd presents an intriguing research direction. Here, BolAG overexpression significantly enhanced both starch synthesis and degradation, leading to increased accumulation of soluble sugars in broccoli (Figure  3). Transcriptome analysis revealed that BolAG overexpression substantially modulates the expression of genes associated with membrane integrity (GO:0110165, GO:0016020), carbohydrate metabolism (GO:0005975) and photosynthetic apparatus (GO:0009521, GO:0009523) (Figure  S4a). The enrichment of membrane-related genes suggests that BolAG may modulate cellular structure or signalling during curd development, potentially affecting cell-to-cell communication or hormone transport—processes critical for phase transition (Gill et  al.  2021). Notably, GO enrichment analysis further supported BolAG's strong association with carbohydrate metabolic processes (Figure S4). Given that sugar signalling is widely recognised as a critical regulator of flowering time across plant species, these findings suggest a mechanistic link between BolAG and carbohydrate metabolism in broccoli bolting. In Arabidopsis, for 

instance, FLOWERING LOCUS T (FT) promotes flowering by activating the sugar transporter SWEET10 (Andrés et al. 2020), and exogenous sucrose application has been shown to modulate flowering time and floral meristem development via trehalose-6-phosphate synthase activity (Gibson  2005). These findings strongly suggest that BolAG's regulatory role in broccoli bolting is closely associated with carbohydrate metabolism. 

Overexpression of BolAG upregulates the expression of the starch degradation gene BolBAM4, while BolAG RNAi-silenced broccoli downregulates its expression (Figure 4d–g). $\beta$ -Amylase (BAM) is a key enzyme in starch degradation (Streb and Zeeman 2012). The bam4 single mutant displays starch excess (Fulton et  al.  2008; David et  al.  2022), suggesting that BAM4 plays a functional role in starch degradation. Our early work on broccoli curd development revealed elevated BolBAM4 expression during curd maturation, suggesting its importance in providing energy for floral meristem differentiation through carbohydrate metabolism (Zhu et al. 2024). In this study, we found that BolAG promotes starch degradation by directly regulating BolBAM4, thereby increasing the SSC. Sugars play a vital role in numerous biological processes of growth and development, such as flowering time, onset of senescence, embryogenesis, seed germination, seedling development and tuber formation (Eveland and Jackson 2012; Li and Sheen 2016; Wingler 2018; Ohto et al. 2001). Specifically, sugars not only serve as energy substances to regulate flowering transition but also act as signal molecules mediating flowering induction (Halford and Paul 2003; Griffiths et al. 2016). Thus, the sugars accumulated through BolAG overexpression likely serve dual roles: providing energy and acting as signalling molecules to trigger the bolting transition. 

RPI2 catalyses the reversible interconversion of ribulose-5- phosphate and ribose-5-phosphate in the non-oxidative stage of the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway (oxPPP) (Xiong et  al.  2009). AtRPI2 T-DNA knockout mutants in Arabidopsis lead to impaired chloroplast function, premature cell death and altered morphology (Xiong et  al.  2009). In this study, we identified the BolRPI2 protein that interacts with BolAG. Overexpression of BolRPI2 in broccoli increased chloroplast size and number, and promoted starch synthesis and accumulation of soluble sugars (Figure  6, Figure  S5). Chloroplast function directly affects photosynthesis and thus the metabolism of sugars (Baroja-Fernández et al. 2004). Compared to WT broccoli, BolRPI2-overexpressing lines exhibited accelerated curd development with precocious curd formation and subsequent premature elongation of the primary inflorescence meristem during late developmental stages, suggesting early initiation of bolting.

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

The KRJ-012 homozygous broccoli seeds were used in this study. Transgenic broccoli seedlings were obtained using the method of Agrobacterium-mediated infection of the hypocotyl callus. These seedlings were screened and cultured in a greenhouse (16h light/8h dark, with temperatures ranging from $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). 

After 20days of culture on the medium, seedlings were transferred to pots for further cultivation (16 h light/8 h dark, with temperatures ranging from $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). After 30days of greenhouse cultivation, seedlings were transplanted to an experimental field. The KRJ-012 homozygous broccoli seeds, and early-bolting (EB) and late-bolting (LB) broccoli varieties used in this study were provided by Dr. Hanmin Jiang (Tianjin Kernel Vegetable Research Institute, Tianjin, China) (Table S3). 

# 4.2 | Plasmid Construction and Genetic Transformation

To construct the overexpression vectors of BolAG and BolRPI2, coding sequences (CDS) were amplified from KRJ-012 homozygous broccoli cDNA pools and cloned into the pCAMBIA3301 vector. To construct RNAi vectors for BolAG and BolRPI2, the CDS of BolAG and BolRPI2 were analysed using the SGN VIGS Tool (https://vigs.solgenomics.net) to identify highly efficient and specific silencing target fragments of approximately 300 bp. The purified PCR products were then ligated into the pNC-Cam3304-RNAi vector using the NC cloning method (Yan et al. 2020). For the promoter-driven GUS reporter gene, a 2 kb genomic sequence upstream of the start codon was amplified from the DNA of broccoli and cloned into the pCAMBIA3301 vector after removing the 35S promoter. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into the Agrobacterium strain EHA105 (Zoman, Beijing, China). # 4.3 | RNA Extraction and qRT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from samples using the RNeasy Kit (Promega, Wisconsin, USA), followed by mRNA enrichment using oligo (dT). The enriched mRNA was subjected to fragmentation, reverse transcription and PCR amplification for RNA-seq libraries preparation. Gene-specific primers were designed for target gene detection (Table  S2). The Bolactin gene from broccoli was used as an internal reference. Faststart Universal SYBR Green Master (Roche, Frankfurt, Germany) was used in all experiments. Relative expression levels of genes were calculated by the comparative $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method based on the manufacturer's protocol. Three batches of RNA independently isolated from each sample were used, and three technical replicates were performed to ensure the reliability of the quantitative analysis. 

# 4.4 | RNA-Seq Analysis and Identification of DEGs

RNA-seq libraries in this study were sequenced on the BGISEQ-500 platform (Genomics Institute, Beijing, China), with three biological replicates for analysis. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified based on the criteria $| \mathrm { l o g } _ { 2 } ( \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { B } ) | > 1$ and $q$ value $< 0 . 0 1$ , where A and B represent FPKM values of two groups, respectively. GO analysis of DEGs was performed using the agriGO platform (http:// bioinfo.cau.edu.cn/agriGO/), with a hypergeometric test performed to identify significantly enriched GO terms (corrected $p$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ ). To further visualise statistically significant overexpressed GO terms, GO and KEGG enrichment analysis of DEGs was performed using the enrichGO and enrichKEGG functions in the clusterProfiler package (Wu et  al.  2021). The clean reads of transcriptome generated and analyzed during the current study are available in the NCBI database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov), and the BioProject ID is PRJNA1236524. 

# 4.5 | Transmission Electron Microscope

Fresh broccoli leaves were cut into small blocks of approximately $1 \mathrm { m m } ^ { 3 }$ and fixed with $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde $\left( \mathrm { p H } 7 . 0 - 7 . 5 \right)$ . The samples were rinsed three times with 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ each time. Dehydration was performed using a series of ethanol concentrations, with each step lasting 1 h. After infiltration, the samples were placed in embedding medium and baked overnight at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The embedding medium was then polymerised in an oven at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , after which the resin blocks were removed for storage. The resin blocks were sectioned into ultrathin slices $\left( 6 0 - 8 0 \mathrm { n m } \right)$ and collected on 150 

mesh copper grids. The grids were stained in the dark with a saturated solution of $2 \%$ uranyl acetate in ethanol for 8 min, followed by three washes with $7 0 \%$ ethanol and three washes with ultrapure water. Then, the grids were stained with $2 . 6 \%$ lead citrate solution in the absence of carbon dioxide for 8 min, followed by three washes with ultrapure water and gentle blotting with filter paper. The copper grid sections were placed in a grid box and allowed to dry at room temperature overnight. Observations were made using a transmission electron microscope (HT7800; HITACHI, Tokyo, Japan), and images were collected for analysis. Three biological replicates of both transgenic and wild-type (WT) broccoli plants were analyzed to ensure experimental reproducibility. 

# 4.6 | Determination of Starch and Soluble Sugar Content

Starch and soluble sugar contents were determined using the Starch Content Assay Kit and Plant Soluble Sugar Content Detection Kit (Solarbio, Beijing, China), following the manufacturer's protocols. The experiment included three biological replicates, each with technical replicates, to ensure data reliability. 

# 4.7 | Chlorophyll Content Determination

Weighed $1 . 0 \mathrm { g }$ of the broccoli leaf sample and placed it in a mortar. A small amount of quartz sand, calcium carbonate powder and $2 \mathrm { m L }$ of $8 0 \%$ acetone solution were added, and the mixture was ground into a uniform paste. Then, $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $8 0 \%$ acetone solution was added, and the grinding continued until the tissue turned white. The mixture was left to stand for 5 min for extraction. A piece of filter paper was folded and placed in a funnel, then wetted with $8 0 \%$ acetone. The extract was poured into the funnel along a glass rod and filtered into a $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ brown volumetric flask. The mortar, pestle and residue were rinsed several times with acetone solution until colourless. The extract was then diluted to $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ with $8 0 \%$ acetone solution and shaken well. The $8 0 \%$ acetone solution was used as a blank reference for spectrophotometer calibration. Absorbance at wavelengths of 663 and $6 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ was measured. Three biological replicates and technical replicates were performed for the experiment. 

# 4.8 | Potassium Iodide Staining

Fresh broccoli leaves were bleached in boiling ethanol $8 0 \%$ v/v) and then left to cool to room temperature. The leaves were removed and stained with Lugol's solution $( 2 \mathbf { m } \mathbf { M } \mathbf { \Lambda } ] _ { 2 }$ $\mathrm { I } _ { 2 }$ and 6mM KI) in a petri dish for 2 min. After briefly rinsing with distilled water, the leaves were observed and photographed. Three biological replicates and technical replicates were performed for the experiment. 

# 4.9 | Subcellular Localization and Bimolecular Fluorescence Complementation (BiFC) Assay

The constructed pBI121-EGFP fusion vector was transformed into the GV3101 Agrobacterium strain and injected into the 

lower epidermis of N. benthamiana leaves. For the BiFC assay, the pNC-BiFC-Enn-BolAG and pNC-BiFC-Ecn-BolRPI2 fusion vectors were co-transformed into GV3101 Agrobacterium strains and injected into the lower epidermis of N. benthamiana leaves at a 1:1 ratio. The tobacco plants were cultivated for 8 h in the dark and then for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. DAPI dye was used to mark nuclear localization. Three biological replicates were performed for the experiment. The cells were observed and scanned using a laser scanning confocal microscope Leica TCSSP5 (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). 

# 4.10 | Yeast 1-Hybrid (Y1H) Assay

In the Y1H assay, the full-length CDS of BolAG was cloned into the pGADT7 vector as the prey vector, while the promoter of the target gene was cloned into the pAbAi vector as the bait vector. The bait vector was transformed into the Y1HGold chemically competent cell (Zoman, Beijing, China). The constructed bait strain was spotted on SD/-Ura medium containing a gradient of AbA concentrations to determine the minimal AbA concentration required to suppress the background expression of the reporter gene in the bait strain. The prey vector and the empty pGADT7 vector were then transformed into the bait strain. The transformed strains were resuspended in $0 . 9 \%$ NaCl solution to an $\mathrm { { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.2, followed by serial dilutions (10-fold, 100-fold and 1000-fold). The diluted cell suspensions were spotted onto SD/-Ura-Leu plates containing the predetermined AbA concentration. The plates were incubated upside down at $2 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days, and colony growth was observed to assess the interaction between the prey protein and the bait DNA fragment. 

# 4.11 | Protein Interaction Model Prediction

Protein structure predictions for BolAG and BolRPI2 were performed using the monomer_casp14 model in AlphaFold v2.3.2 (Jumper et al. 2021). The multimer model in AlphaFold v2.3.2 was used to predict the heterodimer structure of BolAG and BolRPI2 (Evans et al. 2022). Default parameters were used for all analyses. The dimer structure was visualised using PyMOL v2.5.0 (Student 2010). The predicted multimer structure was analysed using PRODIGY v2.0, which provided the binding affinity and dissociation constant (Li et al. 2016). 

# 4.12 | Yeast 2-Hybrid (Y2H) Assay

For the Y2H screening, the pGBKT7-BolAG bait strain was mixed with an Arabidopsis cDNA library and incubated. After microscopic confirmation of zygote formation, the culture was plated on SD/-Trp-Leu-Ade solid medium. Positive colonies were inoculated into SD/-Trp-Leu-Ade liquid medium until turbid, then spotted onto SD/-Trp-Leu-His-Ade plates containing X-α-gal. Colonies exhibiting normal growth and blue coloration were selected for further validation in SD/-Trp-Leu-His-Ade liquid culture. 

Putative interactors were identified through PCR amplification of yeast colonies, followed by sequencing. The obtained sequences were aligned against the Arabidopsis genome to identify candidate interacting proteins. Corresponding CDS were 

retrieved and aligned against the broccoli genome in the BRAD database (http://brassicadb.cn/#/) to obtain reference CDS. The pGADT7-BolAG and pGBKT7-bait (interacting protein) vectors were co-transformed into the Y2H Gold yeast strain for reverse hybridization validation. 

To verify the interaction between BolAG truncated protein and BolRPI2 protein, the full-length BolAG and various truncated BolAG (truncation according to MIKC domain) were fused with GAL4 BD as bait, while the full-length BolRPI2 CDS was fused with GAL4 AD as the prey. The bait and prey vectors were cotransformed into Y2HGold chemically competent cell (Zoman, Beijing, China) and grown on SD/Trp-Leu-His-Ade + x-α-gal medium. 

# 4.13 | Histochemical GUS Assay

The GUS reporter gene fusion vector, driven by the target gene promoter, was transformed into GV3101 Agrobacterium strain and injected into the lower epidermis of N. benthamiana leaves. The tobacco plants were cultivated for 8 h in the dark and then for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. Staining was performed using the GUS Staining Kit (Solarbio, Beijing, China) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , followed by decolorization with $9 5 \%$ ethanol. Four biological replicates were included in this experiment to ensure data reproducibility. 

# 4.14 | Dual-luciferase reporter (DLR) Assay

The CDS of the BolAG and BolRPI2 were cloned into the pNC-Green-SK vector driven by the CaMV 35S promoter. The promoter regions of the target genes were cloned into the pNC-Green-LUC vector, just upstream of the LUC coding sequence. The effector and reporter constructs were mixed at a 4:1 ratio (V:V) and transiently transformed into N. benthamiana leaf epidermal cells. The tobacco plants were cultivated for 8h in the dark and then for 72h under a 16h light/8h dark cycle. Firefly and Renilla luciferases were measured using a dual-luciferase reporter system (Promega, Wisconsin, USA), and fluorescence imaging was performed using Beetle Luciferin, Potassium Salt (Promega, Wisconsin, USA). In the DLR assay, both relative luciferase activities and fluorescence imaging were performed with three independent biological replicates to ensure experimental reproducibility.
