# ABSTRACT

Drought priming is a critical agronomic strategy for enhancing plant drought tolerance, yet the optimal priming intensity and transcriptional regulatory mechanisms underlying subsequent drought responses in the tea plant (Camellia sinensis) remain poorly characterised. In this study, we systematically evaluated tea plants exposed to recurrent drought stress under varying priming intensities. Results demonstrated that moderate drought priming specifically conferred superior drought tolerance compared to non-primed controls. Integrated metabolomic and transcriptomic profiling identified flavonoid biosynthesis as the key pathway associated with priming-induced drought resilience. Exogenous flavonoid application and overexpression of six biosynthesis genes (CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR) functionally validated flavonoids' role in drought adaptation. Notably, transcriptional regulators CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were identified as hub transcription factors demonstrating transcriptional activation potential towards flavonoid biosynthesis. Combinatorial transient overexpression and silencing assays revealed that both CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 coordinately upregulate flavonoid biosynthesis genes, redirecting metabolic flux towards flavonoid accumulation to enhance drought tolerance. Multimodal validation through yeast one-hybrid assays, dualluciferase reporter systems and electrophoretic mobility shift assays, as well as molecular docking, confirmed or simulated direct binding of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 to promoter regions of flavonoid biosynthesis genes for transcriptional activation. These findings establish a synergistic regulatory model where CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 cooperatively enhance flavonoid accumulation through transcriptional reprogramming, thereby conferring acquired drought tolerance. This study provides mechanistic insights for developing adaptive cultivation practices and advances molecular breeding strategies for drought-resilient tea cultivars. 

Drought represents a critical environmental stressor that significantly impairs plant growth, development and productivity (Sato et al. 2024). Under current climate change scenarios, rising global temperatures and altered precipitation patterns are triggering more frequent, intense and prolonged drought events globally (Sharma et al. 2022). This poses particular challenges for tea plants (Camellia sinensis), an economically vital crop predominantly cultivated in tropical and subtropical regions (Wei et  al.  2018). Projections indicate substantial increases in drought frequency and severity across tea-growing areas, significantly elevating the risk of recurrent short-term drought episodes. These conditions present formidable obstacles to tea plant growth and sustainable tea production. So far, research on drought stress in tea plants has mainly focused on the physiological response patterns under single drought stress and the identification and biological function analysis of related genes. Without comparing drought-tolerant and nondrought-tolerant tea plants, it is difficult to explore the key genes and regulatory mechanisms to enhance drought tolerance. Thus, clarifying the drought tolerance mechanism of the tea plant and developing a new, effective strategy for cultivating and breeding tea plants is crucial for the tea plant to withstand the current and future climate models. 

Drought priming (also termed hardening, conditioning or training) has emerged as a crucial adaptive strategy for plants to enhance drought tolerance (Hilker et  al.  2016). This process involves pre-exposure to moderate stress that induces physiological, metabolic and genetic modifications, creating a ‘stress memory’ mechanism (Lämke and Bäurle  2017; Oberkofler et al. 2021), enabling plants to mount more rapid and robust responses to subsequent drought events while enhancing both immediate adaptation and transgenerational resilience (Galviz et al. 2020; Sharma et al. 2022). For tea plants, elucidating the regulatory mechanisms of priming-acquired drought tolerance is critical to developing effective strategies for enhancing drought resilience. Substantial evidence indicated that plants possess drought stress memory, a phenomenon mediated through transcriptional reprogramming and sustained alterations in transcription factor activity. These molecular adjustments regulate key protective proteins and signalling metabolites, enabling enhanced responsiveness to subsequent drought events. This adaptive mechanism facilitates more robust and rapid stress responses, ultimately mitigating drought-induced damage (Ganie et  al.  2024; Sharma et  al.  2022). Among these, the flavonoids, a diverse class of over 6000 phenylpropanoid-derived compounds, play particularly vital roles as potent antioxidants. They effectively scavenge reactive oxygen species (ROS), thereby reducing oxidative damage induced by drought stress (Dong and Lin 2021). For example, the $\beta$ -glucosidase 17 contributed to flavonoid accumulation to enhance Medicago truncatula drought and salt tolerance (Du et  al.  2024). Additionally, the accumulation of flavonoids promoted the drought tolerance of apples and Populus ussuriensis (Li et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2020). In tea plants, the tea polyphenol was found to mediate CsMYB77 regulation of CsPOD44 to enhance root drought resistance (Xu et al. 2025). Although studies have shown that flavonoids are the key metabolites of plants to resist drought stress, the 

function and transcription regulation of flavonoids in tea plants have not been well clarified under drought stress, especially under drought priming. 

Generally, the biosynthesis of flavonoids in plants is regulated by encoding different key enzyme genes, and these genes are also regulated by many transcription factors (TF). The YABBY TF is plant-specific and is a small family of TFs that consists of an N-terminal C2C2 zinc-finger domain and a C-terminal helix–loop–helix domain (YABBY domain) (Bowman and Smyth 1999). YABBY was first thought to be limited to regulation on leaf polarity establishment and flower organ development in plants (Han et al. 2024). However, some studies have reported the function of YABBY in secondary metabolism, especially in flavonoid biosynthesis and response to drought stress (Cui et  al.  2024; Kayani et  al.  2022; Xiong et  al.  2023; Zhao et  al.  2017). Additionally, the R2R3-MYB TFs are the central factors that have been systematically documented as direct activators or repressors in flavonoid biosynthesis. For example, the MYB114 belonging to Subgroup 6 (SG6) R2R3- MYB family can directly bind to the promoter regions of flavonoid biosynthesis genes and activate or repress their expression to regulate flavonoid biosynthesis in numerous species (Li et al. 2020; Stracke et al. 2007; Tirumalai et al. 2019). Due to the species-dependent functional exertion of TFs, the expression of the same TFs varies in different species. Although the function of YABBY and MYB114 in flavonoid biosynthesis and drought stress response has been studied in other plants, the relevance of YABBY and MYB114 to tea plant flavonoid biosynthesis and acquired drought-tolerance formation remains obscure. 

In this study, we set the different degrees of drought priming of tea plants and filtered the moderate drought priming that could endow tea plants with more tolerance to drought stress. The multiomics were deployed to investigate the internal precise mechanism of acquired drought tolerance of tea plants formed by drought priming. Flavonoids were identified as the key active metabolites in enhancing acquired drought tolerance. Moreover, the regulatory network of flavonoid synthesis governing the drought tolerance in tea plants was constructed. These results provide an important theoretical basis for the breeding and genetic improvement of drought-tolerant tea cultivars.

# 2.3 | High Expression Levels of Six Flavonoid Biosynthetic Genes Resulted in the Accumulation of Flavonoids in Drought-Primed Tea Plants Under Drought Stress

To investigate the molecular mechanisms of moderate droughtpriming enhanced drought tolerance in tea plants, we collected the transcriptome profiles of tea plants from R-CK, C2-T1, C2-T2, CK-CK, CK-T1 and CK-T2 (Table  S5). The results of PCA show that the biologically repeated samples are clustered together, while the samples between groups are scattered (Figure S4a), which shows that the data is reliable and can be used for subsequent analysis. A total of 7760 DEGs were identified (Figure  S4b). KEGG enrichment analysis of DEGs in CK-CK versus R-CK, CK-T1 versus C2-T1 and CK-T2 versus C2- T2 revealed significant enrichment in flavonoid biosynthesis pathways, particularly in the CK-T2 versus C2-T2 comparison (Figure S4c). These results aligned with the metabolic profiles, indicating that the enhanced drought tolerance in primed tea plants primarily stems from transcriptional regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis. A total of 125 DEGs were annotated as phenylpropanoid biosynthesis-related genes in both droughtprimed and control tea plants (Figure S4d). Among these, 119 DEGs were associated with flavonoid biosynthesis. Notably, 84 genes exhibited higher expression in drought-primed plants

# 2.4 | Identification of a Regulatory Network Involved in the Maintenance of Acquired Drought Tolerance of Tea Plants

To investigate the regulation mechanisms and establish the regulatory network related to flavonoid biosynthesis during the tea plant's drought memory generation, the co-expression network of six flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes using 18 transcriptome profiles of tea plants at different drought treatments was constructed via weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA). The 10 modules (blue, tan, green, magenta, greenyellow, red, cyan, turquoise, salmon and yellow) were classified by hierarchical clustering and dynamic branch cutting, with gene numbers in each module ranging from 30 to 559 (Figure  4a,b). Further analysis found that genes encoding TFs that preferentially correlated with CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR were primarily gathered in MEcyan, MEturquoise and MEsalmon modules (Pearson $> 0 . 8$ ; Figure 4b). The 35 TFs were identified as positively correlated with six flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes in the MEcyan, MEturquoise and MEsalmon modules, respectively (Table S6). These TFs belong to 21 families (Figure 4c). Additionally, the PlantRegMap (https://plantregmap.gao-lab. org/binding_site_prediction.php) was used to map the TFbinding site of six flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes, and Arabidopsis thaliana was used as a reference species (Table S7). Our analysis revealed that the promoters of CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR contain binding sites for at least 13 TFs, excluding LIM, NF-YC and TUB. Notably, drought-primed plants exhibited significantly higher expression of four key TFs (CsbHLH [CsTGY01G0002824], CsYABBY [CsTGY03G0002716] and two CsMYBs [CsTGY15G0000202, CsTGY08G0001209]), particularly during the second drought cycle (C2-T2) (Figure  4d). These findings suggest that these hub TFs actively regulate flavonoid pathway gene expression to maintain acquired drought tolerance in tea plants. 

Subsequently, we validated the expression patterns of those four TFs using qRT-PCR and found that the expression of the four TF genes was higher in the plants undergoing moderate drought-priming than in the control (Figure 4e). An analysis of the expression levels of these four TF genes in various tissues of the tea plant found that CsbHLH, CsYABBY and two CsMYBs had high expression in tea shoots (bud and young leaf) (Figure  S7a,b). Therefore, we focused on CsbHLH, CsYABBY and two CsMYBs and indicated that these four TFs potentially perform the hub roles in regulating the expression of six flavonoid biosynthesis genes in the maintenance of acquired drought tolerance of tea plants. 

The full-length cDNA sequences of CsbHLH, CsYABBY and two CsMYBs contain 1125, 648, 582 and 885 bp open reading frames (ORFs), respectively. # 2.5 | Overexpression of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 in Tea Plants Enhances Drought Tolerance and ROS Scavenging Ability by Controlling Flavonoid Biosynthesis

To verify the functionalities of four TFs in the maintenance of acquired drought tolerance in tea plants, the CsbHLH18, CsYABBY1, CsMYB114 and CsMYB91 were overexpressed in tea plants, and their transgenic status was confirmed using RT-PCR (Figure  5a). The expression levels of CsbHLH18, CsYABBY1, CsMYB114 and CsMYB91 in the CsbHLH18-OE, CsYABBY1-OE, CsMYB114-OE and CsMYB91-OE tea plants were significantly higher than those in the control (Figure 5b). Subsequently, the flavonoid contents of the four TFs-OE tea plants were detected. The flavonoid content was extremely significantly accumulated in CsbHLH18-OE, and the CsYABBY1-OE and CsMYB114-OE tea plants to an extremely significant level $\cdot p < 0 . 0 1 )$ ). However, although the flavonoid content in CsMYB91-OE leaves of tea plants was increased, it was not at a substantial level (Figure 5c). Thus, considering the function of flavonoids in tea drought tolerance, CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were chosen to investigate further the function of TFs in regulating flavonoid biosynthesis for the maintenance of the tea plant's acquired drought tolerance.

Under drought stress, the CsYABBY1-OE and CsMYB114-OE leaves of tea plants had significantly lower MDA, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - }$ content than the control (Figure 5g–i). # 2.6 | CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 Could Promote the Expression of CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR

Y1H assay was employed to investigate the binding of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 to these targets (Figure  6a). When pGADT7- YABBY1 was cotransformed with CsCHSp, CsCHIp, CsFLSp, CsANSp and CsANRp-pAbAi constructs, the yeast cells grew normally on SD/−Leu medium with aureobasidin A (AbA), while the control and those cotransformed with CsDFRp showed no signs of growth. Previous studies have shown that the YABBY TFs recognise the consensus binding sequences defined as $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ATTNATAA- ${ \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime }$ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AATNATTA- ${ \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime }$ elements of their target genes (Kayani et al. 2022). To further comprehensively scan the CsYABBY recognition site in the promoter sequences of six flavonoid biosynthesis genes, MEME suite v5.5.7 was used to scan the putative YABBY binding sites from these genes' promoters. The result showed that six flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes' promoter regions were identified with at least two YABBY binding sites (Table S8). More MYB binding sites existed in the promoter regions of flavonoid synthesis genes (Table  S7). Furthermore, the core YABBY and MYB binding cis-elements with the top or second-lowest $q$ value in flavonoid biosynthesis gene promoters were selected to perform molecular docking (Table S9). Solvation energy effect $( \Delta ^ { \mathrm { i } } G )$ was used for the molecular docking to evaluate the intermolecular binding efficiency. Negative $\Delta ^ { \mathrm { i } } G$ corresponds to positive protein affinity between receptors and ligands. The molecular docking results showed that DNA-binding sites in five or six flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes' promoter regions could effectively bind to the CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 receptor protein, respectively (Figure  S9). Their $\Delta ^ { \mathrm { i } } G$ value ranged from −11.5 to $- 1 9 . 6 \mathrm { k c a l } / $ mol and −15.6 to $- 3 0 . 3 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ , respectively (Table  S9), suggesting that the CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 could bind to the promoter region of five (CsCHS/CsCHI/CsFLS/CsANS/CsANR) and six (CsCHS/CsCHI/CsFLS/CsDFR/CsANS/CsANR) flavonoid biosynthesis genes to regulate their expression. Then, these TFbinding sites in the promoter regions of the flavonoid biosynthesis genes were selected for the EMSA assay. The EMSA assay results showed that CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114, respectively, bound to the core cis-elements of CsCHS/CsCHI/CsFLS/CsANS/CsANR (Figure 6f) or CsCHS/CsCHI/CsFLS/CsDFR/CsANS/CsANR promoters (Figure  6g). These results collectively demonstrated that CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 positively regulated the expression of core structural genes involved in flavonoid biosynthesis.

# 3 | Discussion

# 3.1 | Drought Priming Enhances the Acquired Drought Tolerance of Tea Plants by Eliciting Multilevel Responses

Drought stress is one of the most devastating environmental factors that seriously limits plant yield and quality (Sato et  al.  2024). Lots of research has investigated the intricate adaptation and coordinated drought stress response mechanisms in multiple species to attempt to elucidate the mechanisms by which plants respond to drought stress to establish more feasible strategies for plant improvement (Dey and Bhattacharjee 2020; Dong et  al.  2021; Jin et  al.  2023; Kong et  al.  2023; Li, Ren, et al. 2024; Li, Zhou, et al. 2024; Shao et al. 2023). Recently, advances in elucidating the mechanism of the plants' pre-exposure to drought events (drought priming) provide new strategies for enhancing drought tolerance (Backhaus et  al.  2014; Ding et  al.  2012; Schulze et  al.  2021). In this study, we exposed tea 

plants to different levels of drought stress in advance and subjected them to simultaneous drought treatments with tea plants that did not experience drought stress and found that tea plants that experienced drought priming showed stronger drought tolerance (Figure 1). These results were consistent with the findings that drought priming enhances drought tolerance in coffee (Menezes-Silva et al. 2017), potato (Solanum tuberosum) (Banik et al. 2016) and tea plant (Shao et al. 2023). 

The capacity of the acquired drought tolerance conferred by drought priming is derived from the plant's interconnected and elaborate regulatory mechanisms involved at multiple levels, including modifications at morphological, physiological, metabolic, transcriptional, translational and even epigenetic levels (Ganie et al. 2024). Our research supports this conclusion. After experiencing drought priming, the tea plant's phenotype and related physiological and biochemical indices involved in the self-protection mechanism of the tea plant were enhanced and improved when the tea plant was exposed to repeated drought stress compared with the control (Figure  1), although the related indices did not seem to be fully restored to a perfect state during the stage of restoration of water supply to the tea plant after drought priming. Notably, the drought tolerance obtained from drought priming presents more excellently in the tea plants undergoing moderate drought priming than mild drought priming, especially when tea plants were again subjected to moderate drought stress (Figure  1), suggesting that compared to the mild drought priming, the moderate drought priming left a profound imprint on the tea plant, which enabled it to retrieve the imprinted information quickly and respond faster when it was exposed to drought stress again. 

Additionally, the available imprinting information tends to initiate faster recruitment and efficient production of salubrious metabolites, triggering the changes in the plant metabolic profile and allowing it to behave more tolerantly in the face of stress (Jacques et  al.  2021; Liu et  al.  2022). In tea plants, we found significant changes in the metabolic profiles of tea plants that had undergone moderate drought priming under different levels of drought stress, and some metabolites represented by flavonoid compounds were highly accumulated in tea plants that had undergone drought priming (Table S3 and Figure 2a–e). Generally, the changes in physiological and metabolic processes formed by stress priming are governed by transcriptional and epigenetic mechanisms (Avramova 2015; Ganie et  al.  2024). Evidence shows that plants possess transcriptional stress memory, with memory genes activated upon recurrent drought stress (Ding et al. 2012, 2013, 2014). # 3.2 | Flavonoids Are the Key Imprinted Metabolites to Enhance the Acquired Drought Tolerance of Tea Plants

Flavonoids are characterised as critical antioxidants that play an important role in plant defence against drought stress by reducing oxidative damage induced by a burst of ROS (Xu et  al.  2015; Yildiztugay et  al.  2020; Zhan et  al.  2019). It is worth noting that the flavonoids in plants that have been exposed to stress in advance have significant potential in coping with subsequent stress. When pre-exposed to pulsed UV-B, although the total duration was the same, the plants accumulated more UV-B protective flavonols (Höll et  al.  2018). The same phenomenon was observed in the present study. In the face of post-drought stress, tea plants that experienced drought priming accumulated more flavonoids, especially under moderate drought stress (Figure  2d,e). Exogenous spraying of these flavonoids significantly alleviated droughtinduced damage, especially in ROS scavenging (Figure 3f–l). In Arabidopsis, the flavonoid structural genes such as F3H and DFR have been proven to increase flavonoids to reduce the accumulation of ROS and enhance drought tolerance (Nakabayashi et al. 2013). To elucidate the function of flavonoid structural genes in flavonoid biosynthesis to enhance drought tolerance, the CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR were overexpressed in the tea plants. Consistent with previous studies, the overexpression of six flavonoid structural genes significantly increased the flavonoid content in tea plants, alleviating oxidative damage caused by ROS accumulation and thus enhancing the drought tolerance of tea 

plants (Figure  3). Therefore, flavonoids are considered one of the core metabolites in the formation of drought tolerance memory. This is achieved because tea plants retain information from early exposure to drought stress, allowing for rapid retrieval when exposed to drought and the rapid initiation of flavonoid synthesis-related genes, which leads to enhanced flavonoid synthesis and drought tolerance in tea plants.

The development of acquired drought tolerance in plants involves a complex regulatory network, in which transcriptional regulation by TFs plays a crucial role (Ding et  al.  2013; Liu et al. 2016). Previous studies have shown that many TFs regulated flavonoid biosynthesis, including MYB, WRKY and CCCH. (Li et  al.  2020, 2022). Our study revealed significant correlations between the expression levels of numerous TFs and six key flavonoid biosynthesis genes (CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR) under drought stress (Table S6 and Figure 4a–d), and CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were identified as key TFs involved in the formation of acquired drought tolerance of tea plants, regulating the formation of flavonoids by manipulating the expression of six hub genes in the flavonoid synthesis pathway (Figure  4). The CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were confirmed to be able to directly bind the promoter and activate the expression of CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR (Figure 6). Overexpressed CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 could significantly improve flavonoid content and the expression of flavonoid biosynthesis genes (Figure 5a–c,k,l). In our study, CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were proven to be transcriptional activators, which can activate the expression of downstream genes (Figure  S8). Therefore, we reasoned that CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 promote flavonoid biosynthesis by activating the flavonoid biosynthesis genes. Secondary metabolites are crucial for plant viability, coordination and regulation in environmental stress (Kutchan 2001). Previous studies have revealed that plant YABBY and R2R3-MYB participate in the stress response of plants (An et al. 2018; Zhao et al. 2017). In summary, we clarified that moderate drought priming could significantly improve the drought tolerance of tea plants. The metabolome and transcriptome have been united in revealing that drought priming can confer acquired drought tolerance to tea plants, a trait that is largely attributed to the retention of some information, mainly related to flavonoid biosynthesis, upon early exposure to drought stress. Additionally, we excavated CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 as potential regulatory factors that play a crucial role in the acquired drought tolerance of tea plants. of the formation of acquired drought tolerance of tea plants. However, the establishment of acquired drought tolerance in tea plants is also regulated by epigenetic inheritance, and the relevant regulatory mechanisms need to be clarified in the future. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials

Well-grown and consistent 1-year-old ‘Tieguanyin’ (C. sinensis) tea plants grown in a greenhouse of Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University, Fuzhou, Fujian Province, China $\mathrm { ~ E 1 1 9 ~ } ^ { \circ } 1 4 ^ { \prime }$ ′, $\mathrm { ~ N ~ } 2 6 ^ { \circ } 0 5 ^ { \prime }$ ) were used as the experimental materials. The average temperature and humidity of the greenhouse during the treatment period were $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \pm 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $8 0 \% \pm 5 \%$ , respectively. The cultivated Allium cepa, a kind of Chinese onion cultivar with red skin purchased from Yonghui Superstores Co. Ltd. (Fuzhou, China), was used for subcellular localisation. All N. benthamiana used in this study were grown in the greenhouse of Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University. The Nicotiana benthamiana first germinated on soil, then was transplanted and grown under $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod for use in transient expression analysis. 

# 4.2 | Chemicals and Reagents

The epicatechin $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , epicatechin gallate $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ , and saponarin $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ were purchased from Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Polyethylene glycol (PEG) 6000 and Astragalin $( \geq 9 8 \% )$ were purchased from Solarbio Science and Technology Co. Ltd. (Beijing, China) and Shanghai Canspec Scientific Instruments Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China), respectively. 

# 4.3 | Experimental Design of Tea Plant Drought Priming Treatment

For drought treatments, tea plants were divided into control and treatment groups (Figure  S1). In the first round of drought stress (Cycle 1), the tea plants in the control group were supplied with normal water (CK) to maintain a SRWC of field capacity of $8 0 \% \pm 5 \%$ until further treatment. Tea plants were withheld of water according to the previous method, with minor modifications (Guo et  al.  2017; Tian et  al.  2022) in the treatment group, as described in Methods S1. For the subsequent second round of drought treatment (Cycle 2), the tea plants in the control group were subjected to well-watered, mild, and moderate drought stress (CK-CK, CK-T1, and CK-T2) as controls in the second round of drought treatment; R-T1 and R-T2, in which the tea plants recovered with water in the treatment group were regarded as experiencing drought priming, were subjected to well-watered, mild and moderate drought stress (R-T1-CK/T1/T2 and R-T2-CK/T1/T2), respectively. Each test treatment consisted of 30 pots. The tea shoots, including one bud and three leaves, were collected for further analysis. 

# 4.4 | Measurement of Drought-Related Indicators

The second-third leaf of tea plants in the control and treatment groups was harvested for measurement of drought-related indicators. Detailed protocols are described in Methods S2. 

# 4.5 | Leaf Anatomical Structure and Stomatal Bioassay

The leaf anatomical structure of tea leaves in the first round of drought stress and re-watered tea plants was observed, and the anatomical analysis of those samples was performed, as described in Methods S3. The tea leaves of the experienced drought-primed and control tea plants under drought stress (Cycle 2) were collected for the stomatal bioassay, as described in Methods S3. 

# 4.6 | Metabolomic Analysis

According to the results of drought priming degree screening, the tea shoots (one bud and three leaves) of R-T2 (renamed as R-CK), R-T2-T1 (renamed as C2-T1) and R-T2-T2 (renamed as C2- T2) in the treatment group experienced drought priming, and 

CK-CK, CK-T1 and CK-T2 in the control group under drought stress were collected for metabolomic analysis, as described in Methods S4. 

# 4.7 | Flavonoid Exposure Experiment

To test the effects of flavonoids on tea plants under drought stress, the epicatechin, epicatechin gallate, saponarin and astragalin were randomly selected and dissolved in pure water. Pure water was used as the control. Subsequently, the tea plants were treated as described in Methods S5. 

# 4.8 | Transcriptome Sequencing and Analyses

The samples mentioned above, including R-CK, C2-T1, C2-T2, CK-CK, CK-T1 and CK-T2, were collected for total RNA extraction, detection, sequencing and analysis, as described in Methods S6. 

# 4.9 | RNA Extraction, Genomic DNA Extraction and qRT-PCR Analysis

RNA and genomic DNA extraction, first-strand cDNA synthesis and reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction were performed as previously reported (Tian et al. 2024). The quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) was performed using SYBR fluorescence with gene-specific primers. Unless otherwise specified, all primers used in this study are listed in Table S2. The $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \mathrm { T } } }$ formula was used to calculate relative expression levels, and $\beta$ -actin was used as an internal reference gene. All analyses consisted of three biological replicates. 

# 4.10 | Genes Overexpression in Tea Plants

The CDS fragments of six flavonoid biosynthesis genes (CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR) and four TFs (CsbHLH18, CsYABBY1, CsMYB114 and CsMYB91) were inserted into the pCAMBI1301 vector. The details of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 overexpression in tea plants are described in Methods S7. 

# 4.11 | Phylogenetic Tree Construction

The amino acid sequences of CsbHLH, CsYABBY and two CsMYBs were used to construct phylogenetic trees with AtbHLH, AtYABBY and AtMYBs of Arabidopsis thaliana, respectively, based on the NJ (Neighbour-joining) method in the software MEGA 10.0 and a bootstrap test with 1000 iterations. 

# 4.12 | Transcriptional Activation Analysis

The subcellular localisation in A. cepa epidermis and transcriptional activity in yeast cells and tobacco leaves of CsbHLH18, 

CsYABBY1, CsMYB114 and CsMYB91 were examined. Detailed protocols are described in Methods S8. 

# 4.13 | Molecular Docking

For the molecular docking, the CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were used as receptors, and the DNA-binding sites of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 in six flavonoid biosynthesis genes were regarded as ligands, as described in Methods S9. 

# 4.14 | Protein-DNA Interaction Assays

The yeast one-hybrid assays (Y1H), dual-LUC reporter assay, and electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) were used to test the binding of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 to the CsCHS, CsCHI, CsFLS, CsDFR, CsANS and CsANR promoters. Y1H was performed as described in Methods S10. Dual-LUC reporter assay and EMSA were performed as described in Methods S11 and S12, respectively. 

# 4.15 | Silencing of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 in Tea Plants Based on AsODN

The expression of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 was repressed based on antisense oligonucleotides (AsODNs)–mediated gene suppression. The CDS sequences of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 were used to design the probe using $\mathrm { S _ { O L I G O } }$ online software (http://sfold.wadsworth.org/cgi-bin/soligo.pl) and operated as previously reported (Tian et al. 2024) to silence the expression of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 in tea plants. The details of CsYABBY1 and CsMYB114 expression suppression in tea plants are described in Methods S13. 

# 4.16 | Data Statistics and Analysis

Every test was performed with at least three separate biological replicates, and the data were represented as mean ± standard deviation (SD), analysed by GraphPad Prism 9.50. Different letters or asterisks represent statistical significance detected by analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Duncan's multiple range test using IBM SPSS Statistics 25.0 software (SPSS Inc., Chicago, USA). Lowercase letters and * represent $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ , and uppercase letters and ** represent $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ .
