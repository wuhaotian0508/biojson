# SUMMARY

The initiation of starch granule formation and the mechanism controlling the number of granules per plastid have been some of the most elusive aspects of starch metabolism. This review covers the advances made in the study of these processes. The analyses presented herein depict a scenario in which starch synthase isoform 4 (SS4) provides the elongating activity necessary for the initiation of starch granule formation. However, this protein does not act alone; other polypeptides are required for the initiation of an appropriate number of starch granules per chloroplast. The functions of this group of polypeptides include providing suitable substrates (maltooligosaccharides) to SS4, the localization of the starch initiation machinery to the thylakoid membranes, and facilitating the correct folding of SS4. The number of starch granules per chloroplast is tightly regulated and depends on the developmental stage of the leaves and their metabolic status. Plastidial phosphorylase (PHS1) and other enzymes play an essential role in this process since they are necessary for the synthesis of the substrates used by the initiation machinery. The mechanism of starch granule formation initiation in Arabidopsis seems to be generalizable to other plants and also to the synthesis of long-term storage starch. The latter, however, shows specific features due to the presence of more isoforms, the absence of constantly recurring starch synthesis and degradation, and the metabolic characteristics of the storage sink organs.

Keywords: starch granules, starch metabolism, starch granule initiation, starch granule number per chloroplast, starch morphology, Arabidopsis thaliana.

# INITIATION OF THE STARCH GRANULE

After cellulose, starch is the most abundant polymer in the biosphere. It plays an essential role in those organisms that accumulate it (photosynthetic eukaryotes and some cyanobacteria) (Cenci et al., 2014). Starch is water-insoluble, in contrast to glycogen, which is the storage carbohydrate of nearly all other organisms.

The harvested parts of our staple crop plants are starch-storing organs. Starch is one of the main contributors to the human diet in terms of calories, and it is often used as animal feed. Furthermore, there is also an increasing demand from non-food industries for starch as a renewable material (Zeeman et al., 2010). The synthesis and degradation of starch has therefore been the subject of much research efforts, all with the final aim of obtaining more productive crops or modified starches for industrial applications (Zeeman et al., 2010).

In plants, starch appears in the form of granules in the plastids (mainly in chloroplasts of photosynthetic tissues or amyloplasts of storage organs such as tubers, endosperm, or roots) (Ball and Morell, 2003). The size, number, and morphology of these granules vary greatly depending on the species and even the organ analyzed (Tetlow and Bertoft, 2020). Starch granules are composed of two different polymers: amylose and amylopectin. In both of these glucans, thousands of glucosyl residues possessing the $\alpha$ -configuration are linked together. In starch (and in glycogen as well), there are essentially only two types of glycosidic linkages that interconnect these glucosyl residues: linear chains of $\alpha$ -1,4-linked residues and $\alpha$ -1,6-linked branches. Amylopectin is the main component of starch, and has a large number of branches ordered in clusters that confer a semi-crystalline structure to the granule. In contrast, amylose, the minor component, has a

small number of branches and no known structure (Tetlow and Bertoft, 2020). The enzymatic activities involved in the formation of starch are relatively simple: ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase, EC 2.7.7.27) provides ADP-glucose, which is one substrate used by starch synthases (SSs, ADP-Glc:α-1,4-glucan α-4-glucosyl transferases, EC 2.4.1.21) to elongate the glucan polymer chains. Starch-branching enzyme (SBE, α-1,4-glucan branching enzyme, EC 2.4.1.18) establishes the branches, and starch-debranching enzyme (DBE or isoamylase, EC 3.2.1.68) eliminates excess branches, allowing the crystallization of clusters (Ball et al., 1996; Brust et al., 2013). This scenario becomes more complex as a consequence of the presence of different isoenzymes for each activity and the discovery of new proteins involved in the synthesis of the granule. These isoenzymes show different substrate affinities, and they form different complexes with other enzymes involved in starch synthesis and degradation, modulating their activities (Ahmed et al., 2015; Ball et al., 2011; Kotting et al., 2010; Mehrpouyan et al., 2021). Moreover, for some enzymes, for example, plastidial phosphorylase (PHS1, EC 2.4.4.1), their participation in starch synthesis, degradation, or both is still a matter of discussion.

Overall, the concerted action of all these polypeptides is necessary to form the starch granule. The initiation of starch synthesis remains obscure, in contrast to that of glycogen. For the latter, its synthesis initiation is known to depend on a self-glycosylating protein - glycogenin (EC 2.4.1.186; Lomako et al., 1988) - that generates short $\alpha$ -glucan chains, which are further elongated to form the entire glycogen molecule. No functional glycogenin or equivalent has been reported for higher plants.

However, together with advances in the understanding of the different steps necessary for the synthesis and degradation of the starch polymers, relevant progress has been made in the research on how starch granule formation is initiated. Most of the available data refer to transitory starch, and this focuses on that of the Arabidopsis thaliana leaf starch. Therefore, the present review will focus on this example.

# THE CENTRAL ROLE OF SS4-DEPENDENT INITIATION OF STARCH GRANULE FORMATION IN ARABIDOPSIS CHLOROPLASTS

The chloroplasts of Arabidopsis Columbia-0 usually contain four to seven starch granules of lenticular shape, although the granule number per chloroplast varies slightly between different accessions (Crumpton-Taylor et al., 2012; Malinova et al., 2014). In the wild type (WT), the number seems to be correlated to chloroplast volume (Crumpton-Taylor et al., 2012). This restricted number seems to be linked to the coordinated initiation of starch granule synthesis and therefore to an existing regulation network.

Mutants lacking SS isoform 4 (SS4) have just one or two starch granules per chloroplast. These are considerably larger than those in WT and show a rounded shape (Roldán

et al., 2007). The amylopectin chain-length profiles, reflecting alterations to the inner granule structure, of ss4 mutants are similar to those of the WT. No changes in the activity of the main enzymes involved in the synthesis or degradation of starch were observed, except for an increase in the cytosolic and plastidial phosphorylase activities (see below). It was proposed that SS4 is involved in the priming of starch granule formation (Roldan et al., 2007).

SS4 is a GT-B-fold glycosyltransferase classified within the GT5 family in the Carbohydrate Active Enzyme (CAZy) database (Lombard et al., 2013). It has starch catalytic (GT5) and glycosyltransferase 1 (GT1) domains in its C-terminal region. These domains are highly conserved among the SS4 homologs of different species and among the different isoforms of SSs (Leterrier et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2015). In addition, SS4 has a long N-terminal region with some features exclusive to this isoform. This region is less phylogenetically conserved, although all SS4 analyzed have long coiled-coil domains. A domain of approximately 50 amino acids, highly conserved among all the SS4 enzymes analyzed to date, separates the coiled-coil and glycosyltransferase regions (Raynaud et al., 2016). SS4 is not found soluble throughout the stroma. On the contrary, localization experiments involving translational fusion with green fluorescence protein (GFP) have indicated SS4 to be associated with specific areas of the thylakoid membranes known as plastoglobules (Gámez-Arjona et al., 2014). The N-terminal part of the protein is responsible for its localization pattern (Raynaud et al., 2016), and the interaction of this region with the plastoglobule-associated proteins fibrillins 1a and 1b has been shown, although the elimination of these fibrillins does not alter the initiation of starch granule formation (Gámez-Arjona et al., 2014). Using translational fusions of the N-terminal part of SS4 with Agrobacterium glycogen synthase (GS), Lu et al. (2018) showed that this region is also somehow involved in altering the shape of starch granules, which may establish a link between the localization of SS4 and the shape of granules.

SS4 has an active catalytic site and shows SS activity using amylopectin, glycogen, or some maltooligosaccharides (MOS) as substrates when the polypeptide is expressed in E. coli (Brust et al., 2013; Szydlowski et al., 2009). The active form of SS4 seems to be a homodimer, and the conserved region localized between the coiled-coil and glycosyltransferase regions is necessary for its formation (Raynaud et al., 2016).

SS4 was the first element described to be involved in the initiation of starch granule formation, but this complex process requires the participation of other players. Some of these elements have been recently described. Seung et al. (2017) showed that Protein Targeting To Starch 2 (PTST2) interacts with SS4, and its elimination leads to a phenotype similar to that of ss4 mutants. PTST2 contains,

as does its homolog PTST1, a carbohydrate-binding domain (Lohmeier-Vogel et al., 2008; Seung et al., 2017). It has been proposed that PTST2 facilitates granule initiation by delivering suitable substrates to SS4 (Seung et al., 2017). Moreover, PTST2 has been shown to interact with two other plastidial polypeptides: MAR-BINDING FILAMENT-LIKE PROTEIN1 (MFP1) and MYOSIN-RESEMBLING CHLOROPLAST PROTEIN (MRC) (Seung et al., 2018). MRC is also known as PROTEIN INVOLVED IN STARCH INITIATION (PII1) (Vandromme et al., 2019). The elimination of MFP1 or PII1 reduces the number of starch granules per chloroplast, but the granules, although bigger than WT, maintain their lenticular shape. Unlike ss4 mutants, in which the distribution of starch granules is not homogeneous in the plant (indeed they are almost absent in young leaves), the distribution of starch granules in mfp1 and pii1 mutants is homogeneous throughout the rosette leaves (Seung et al., 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). MFP1 was identified in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) by its ability to bind to a matrix attachment region of DNA (Meier et al., 1996). MFP1 is a large coiled-coil protein with a C-terminal DNA-binding domain and a predicted N-terminal transmembrane domain (Gindullis and Meier, 1999). More recently, it has been shown to be associated with the thylakoid membranes, with its C-terminal DNA-binding domain oriented towards the stroma. It is associated in vivo with nucleoids, suggesting a function for MFP1 at the interface between chloroplast nucleoids and the developing thylakoid membrane system (Jeong et al., 2003). In addition to these functions, it has been suggested that MFP1 is responsible for the localization of PTST2 associated with specific points of the thylakoid membranes (Seung et al., 2018). PII1 interacts with SS4 and shows the same dot-like localization pattern, suggesting PII1 is also associated with specific points of the thylakoid membranes (Vandromme et al., 2019). However, although these two polypeptides seem to interact in vivo, analyses of the localization of PII1 in ss4 mutants and SS4 in pii1 mutants indicate that their localization patterns do not depend on each other (Vandromme et al., 2019). The significance of the interaction between SS4 and PII1 is unclear; unlike PTST2, PII1 does not contain a carbohydrate-binding module, which suggests that it does not provide an adequate substrate to SS4. As commented above, the localization of SS4 associated with specific points of the thylakoid membranes does not depend on PII1. It has been suggested that PII1 might be required for the correct folding of SS4 or its interaction with some other factor needed by the starch-priming machinery (Vandromme et al., 2019). PTST2 has another homolog in Arabidopsis, PTST3. This gene is not phylogenetically well conserved, and several plant species, including grasses, appear to have lost it (Seung et al., 2017). The elimination of PTST3 in Arabidopsis leads to a reduction in the number of starch granules in leaves, but

the change is minor compared to that seen in ptst2 mutants. Unlike PTST2, there is no evidence that PTST3 interacts with SS4. Although PTST3 seems to be involved in the initiation of starch granules in Arabidopsis, its function remains unclear.

# OTHER STARCH SYNTHASES INVOLVED IN INITIATION OF STARCH GRANULE FORMATION

It has recently been described that the elimination of SS isoform 5 (SS5) in Arabidopsis reduces the number of starch granules synthesized per chloroplast, indicating that it may also be involved in the initiation of their formation (Abt et al., 2020). This protein is found in all green plants and is phylogenetically related to SS4, and it has been suggested that both result from a gene duplication event that may have occurred before the appearance of algae (Liu et al., 2015). Abt et al. (2020) showed that SS5 is a noncanonical SS isoform that lacks catalytic glycosyltransferase activity. Mutants lacking SS5 have fewer starch granules per chloroplast, although the phenotype is less severe than that of ss4 mutant plants. Like SS4, SS5 forms a homodimer in vivo, interacts with PII1, and localizes to specific points on the thylakoid membranes (Abt et al., 2020). The pii1 mutation seems to be epistatic over that of SS5, suggesting that SS5 exerts its function through its interaction with PII1. In contrast, the ss4 and ss5 phenotypes are additive rather than epistatic, suggesting that SS5 does not exert its function through SS4 (Abt et al., 2020). However, the function of SS5 in the initiation of starch granule formation remains unclear.

Thus, SS4 remains the main protein with enzymatic activity among the different elements that make up the starch granule initiation machinery described to date, and as such should have ultimate responsibility for the elongation of the primer that gives rise to the starch granule. Nevertheless, Arabidopsis plants lacking SS4 still synthesize starch granules, indicating that another elongating activity takes over the function of SS4. Analyses of double and triple SS mutants indicate that SS isoform 3 (SS3) may be responsible for the synthesis of starch in the absence of SS4. Indeed, most of the chloroplasts in ss3 ss4 double mutants lack starch granules (Szydlowski et al., 2009). SS3 has the GT5 and GT1 domains characteristic of the rest of the SSs. It also has a long N-terminal region containing three carbohydrate-binding domains necessary for its activity and the binding of the enzyme to glucan molecules (Busi et al., 2007; Wayllace et al., 2010). These features enable this enzyme to bind and elongate MOS present in the chloroplast and to initiate starch granule formation (see below). However, it is not clear whether SS3 carries out this function by interacting with the rest of the initiation machinery. No interaction of SS3 with other elements that interact with SS4, such as Pll1 or PTST2, has been described, and SS3 seems to be localized mainly

surrounding the surface of the starch granule and not associated with specific points on the thylakoid membranes, as most of the elements involved in the initiation of granule formation (Gámez-Arjona et al., 2014). These points suggest that the SS3-mediated initiation of starch granule formation is different from the canonical initiation described above. Notwithstanding, Arabidopsis is still able to synthesize starch even without SS4 and SS3, and some chloroplasts with large, spherical starch granules characteristic of SS4-lacking mutants are seen in the ss3 ss4 double mutant (D'Hulst and Mérida, 2012; Figure 1). This indicates that yet other elongating activities can replace the function of SS4 and SS3, although they do so inefficiently since just a few chloroplasts in such mutants contain starch (Figure 1). The elongating activities present in chloroplasts of ss3 ss4 mutants are provided by GBSS, SS1, SS2, and PHS1. None of these enzymes has a noncatalytic starch-binding domain, which may explain their very low efficiency in terms of elongating MOS and initiating starch granule formation.

# OTHER PROTEINS INVOLVED IN GRANULE INITIATION

In addition to SS4 (and partially SS3 and SS5) and its interacting partners, other proteins, which so far have been not directly linked with SS4, influence the starch granule number significantly. PHS1 is one of these. A lack of PHS1 has minor effects on starch metabolism, and causes no alteration in starch granule formation initiation (Malinova et al., 2014). However, the additional lack of either MEX1, a maltose exporter responsible for maltose release from the chloroplast at night during starch degradation (Niittylä et al., 2004), or disproportionating enzyme 2 (DPE2, EC 2.4.1.25), involved in maltose metabolism in the cytosol (Chia et al., 2004; Fettke et al., 2006; Lu and Sharkey, 2004), results in a massive reduction in starch granule number. dpe2 phs1 double mutants show the strongest reduction with one starch granule per chloroplast, whereas in mex1 phs1 double mutants mostly two or three granules per chloroplast are seen. In both double mutants, the amount of SS4 is not reduced. Interestingly, the reduction in starch granule number is only observed when a dark phase exists. Under continuous light the starch granule number increases to WT values (Malinova et al., 2014a, 2014b, 2017, 2018). Since PHS1 is putatively involved in plastidial maltodextrin metabolism, a connection between MOS and the initiation of starch granule formation is plausible.

However, two scenarios have to be envisaged: the generation of MOS (i) when starch synthesis and degradation are already underway and (ii) when starch synthesis is not yet underway. For Arabidopsis SSs, maltose was shown to be the shortest acceptor; glucose and ADP-glucose alone cannot be used as a starting point for producing longer MOS (Brust et al., 2013). Arabidopsis phosphorylases also require a glucan acceptor (at least of degree of polymerization [DP] 4; Fettke et al., 2005, 2006). However, reports exist of rapid maltose production in the light (e.g., Szecowka et al., 2013). A

possible explanation for this is the export of glucose phosphates out of the chloroplast during photosynthesis. Glucose 6-phosphate as well as glucose 1-phosphate transporters in the chloroplast have been described (Fettke and Fernie, 2015; Fettke et al., 2011; Kunz et al., 2010; Malinova et al., 2019). These phosphorylated monosaccharides are also connected via the existing phosphoglucomutase (EC 5.4.2.2) activity inside and outside of the chloroplast (Caspar et al., 1985; Egli et al., 2010; Malinova et al., 2014). Furthermore, the interconversion of fructose 6-phosphate (an intermediate of the Calvin-Benson cycle) via phosphoglucoisomerase (EC 5.3.1.9) to glucose 6-phosphate is possible (Kunz et al., 2014; Yu et al., 2000). In the cytosol, the phosphorylase PHS2 can transfer the glucosyl residue of glucose 1-phosphate to cytosolic-soluble heteroglycans, even if their origin is unclear (Fettke et al., 2005; Fettke and Fernie, 2015; Ruzanski et al., 2013). Thus, heteroglycans with longer glucan chains can be formed, which eventually allow the release of maltose via the action of DPE2 (Fettke et al., 2006; Fettke and Fernie, 2015) or cytosolic $\beta$ -amylase (Monroe and Storm, 2018). This maltose can be transported into the chloroplast via MEX1 (this transport has been shown to be bidirectional) (Lu et al., 2006), where it functions as a primer for elongation to longer MOS via the various SS isoforms, especially SS4. Moreover, DPE1 in the chloroplast is also able to generate longer MOS using maltose, although it does so relatively slowly (EC 2.4.1.25; Kartal et al., 2011).

The generation of MOS by already existing starch turnover is much simpler. During both the synthesis and the degradation of starch, MOS are easily produced. During starch synthesis, MOS are produced by the trimming action of DBEs (isoamylase 1 [ISA1] and ISA2) (Delatte et al., 2005; Myers et al., 2000; Wattebled et al., 2005). Starch degradation generates a huge amount of maltose and short MOS by the action of $\alpha$ - and $\beta$ -amylases, ISA3, and limited dextrinase (Fettke et al., 2012). MOS longer than DP 4 can be elongated by PHS1. This, plus evidence from $dpe2$ phs1 double mutants (see below), may indicate the involvement of PHS1 in starch initiation.

In summary, several alternative pathways exist for MOS generation. They may well substitute for one another, since phs1, dpe2, mex1, and dpe1 mutants and isa1 isa2 double mutants show no obvious alteration in starch granule formation initiation (Chia et al., 2004; Critchley et al., 2001; Delatte et al., 2005; Malinova et al., 2014; Niittyla et al., 2004). Further evidence of this redundancy is provided by mex1 phs1 and dpe2 phs1 double mutants; in both, the maltose production described above is blocked and therefore MOS generation is too. In principle, this allows the reduction in starch granule formation initiation to be explained. However, under continuous light, no reduction in starch granule number occurs. Repetitive switching between different photoregimes shows that the

number of granules per chloroplast in these mutants is highly flexible, since they reflect the current rather than the previous light regime (Malinova et al., 2017). Furthermore, the mutants show increased amounts of a wide range of MOS compared with WT (Malinova et al., 2014).

It was recently shown that the elimination of the amylolytic enzyme $\alpha$ -amylase 3 (AMY3) partially suppresses the growth and morphological phenotypes of ss4 mutants and ss3 ss4 double mutants (Seung et al., 2016). In the amy3 ss3 ss4 triple mutant, most of the chloroplasts have no starch, but those that do contain many granules (Seung et al., 2016). It has been suggested that the amylolytic activity of AMY3 eliminates the MOS that could be used by elongating enzymes to synthesize a starch granule (Seung et al., 2016). The involvement of amylolytic activities in the control of the starch granule number had previously been shown in other plant species. The reduction of isoamylase activity in transgenic potato (Solanum tuberosum) tubers and mutant barley (Hordeum vulgare) seeds lead to the accumulation of large numbers of tiny starch granules (Burton et al., 2002; Bustos et al., 2004). In this respect, it is worth noting that ss3 ss4 double mutant plants have more starch in the cotyledons (P. Ragel, 2012, PhD Thesis, Figure 1). Thus, it would be interesting to see if the amylolytic activity is lower in these organs so that 2021 the formation of a starch granule is more probable in the cotyledons of this mutant.

# DEVELOPMENTAL AND METABOLIC IMPLICATIONS

The phenotype of ss4 plants depends on the developmental stage of the leaves. Thus, most of the chloroplasts in mature leaves contain one large starch granule, whereas in young leaves they are devoid of starch (Crumpton-Taylor et al., 2013). dpe2 phs1 double mutants have different numbers of starch granules in mature and young leaves, but the effect is opposite to that seen in ss4 plants (Malinova et al., 2014). In contrast, ss5 plants have starch in both young and mature leaves (Abt et al., 2020). Similar results have been reported for mfp1 and pii1 plants (Seung et al., 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). Furthermore, dpe2 phs1 double mutant plants show the abovementioned alteration in starch granule formation initiation under different light/dark regimes. It is likely that differences in starch granule number will be seen for other mutants that lack the proteins involved in starch granule formation initiation. These examples indicate that both the developmental and the metabolic state of the leaf influence the initiation of starch granule formation.

The starch degradation rate is adjusted to the amount of starch accumulated at the beginning of the dark phase. This control avoids the complete degradation of starch during the night and prevents periods of starvation that will be deleterious for the growth of the plant (Graf et al., 2010; Scialdone et al., 2013). This indicates a connection

between the degradation and the synthesis of starch, and hence with the initiation of starch granule formation. dpe2 phs1 double mutant plants illustrate this idea on starch granule formation initiation. In these mutants, the lack of a dark phase (continuous light) results in a nearly normal number of starch granules per chloroplast, indicating that the dark phase per se or starch degradation in the dark may be responsible for the reduction of the starch granule number. Blockage of starch degradation in the dpe2 phs1 background by an additional mutation in the gene coding for the $\alpha$ -glucan, water dikinase (GWD, EC 2.7.9.4; Ritte et al., 2006), a key enzyme in the phosphorylation/dephosphorylation cycle at the starch granule surface, which initiates starch degradation (Fettke et al., 2012), results in an increase in starch granule number, indicating that starch breakdown is likely involved in its regulation (Malinova and Fettke, 2017). This idea is supported by the increase of the number of starch granules per chloroplast in the gwd single mutant with respect to that found in WT (Mahlow et al., 2014). Moreover, it was reported that phosphorylation of starch by GWD also occurs during starch synthesis (Hejazi et al., 2014).

For metabolic implications, not only restrictions on substrates and products can be considered. In ss4, the substrates for starch synthesis, ADP-glucose and MOS, are available for the remaining SSs (Malinova et al., 2017; Ragel et al., 2013). The high accumulation of ADP-glucose (with an approximately 100-fold increase compared with WT) was thought to reflect that SS4 acts upstream of all other SSs, which are limited in starch formation when SS4 is lacking. However, dpe2 phs1 double mutants show only a very moderate accumulation of ADP-glucose, although the number of starch granules is strongly reduced. MOS are also accumulated in this double mutant. Furthermore, ss4 mutants, dpe2 phs1 double mutants, and dpe2 phs1 ss4 triple mutants show an increase in the number of starch granules per chloroplast when the light phase is elongated, which is not accompanied by a reduction in the intracellular levels of ADP-glucose. Overall, these data suggest a precise regulation of the number of starch granules that is not strictly dependent on the availability of substrates and relevant enzymes. The regulatory mechanisms are as yet largely unknown. The elucidation of these mechanisms is hindered by massive alterations in the metabolism produced by changes in starch turnover in those mutants (Malinova et al., 2014). However, these observed comprehensive metabolic alterations reflect the central significance of starch turnover for plant metabolism. In addition, the regulation seems to be tissue- and organ-specific. Leaf guard cells show fewer starch granules per chloroplast than mesophyll cells, and are differently affected by mutations in the abovementioned proteins (Santelia and Lunn, 2017). Similarly, mutations in these proteins differentially affect the columella cells in root tips (Seung et al.,

2020). Environmental conditions also affect the regulation of the number of starch granules. Maize (Zea mays) and potato plants grown at low temperature changed the number of starch granules, likely by alterations of plastidial phosphorylase activity (Orawetz et al., 2016; Satoh et al., 2008).

# IMPACT OF ALTERED STARCH GRANULE NUMBER ON OTHER STARCH PARAMETERS

Arabidopsis WT starch is typically thin and discoid, while ss4 and dpe2 pts plants have bigger and more spherical granules (Malinova et al., 2017). ss5 mutant granules are typically discoid, but with irregular margins (Abt et al., 2020). Other mutants in starch metabolism, although not involved in the initiation starch granule formation, such as gwd, starch excess 4 (sex4), or like starch excess four 1 (lsf1) (Comparot-Moss et al., 2010; Kotting et al., 2009; Mahlow et al., 2014), also show changes in granule morphology, which point to an impact of metabolism on granule morphology (Figure 2). The molecular basis of the relation between starch granule morphology and metabolism is still completely unknown. dpe2 pts1 ss4 triple mutants and dpe2 ss4 double mutants have perfectly spherical, large or even very large granules, a distinct phenotype that may be the result of simultaneous alterations in both starch synthesis and degradation. Reports of a dominant function of SS4 in normal starch granule morphology, especially of its N-terminal domain (Lu et al., 2018), are unlikely, since dpe2 pts1, mex1 pts1, and dpe2 mex1 double mutants express SS4 but show more spherical starch granules (Malinova et al., 2017), suggesting that starch granule morphology is a feature that is ultimately determined by the balance between different factors, such as the localization of granule formation initiation and the metabolic state of the chloroplast.

The idea that metabolism is of central importance for starch granule parameters is further supported by the fact that several mutants that reveal alterations in size and/or morphology also show differences in the chain length distribution pattern, and thus, at least in part, in the inner structure of starch granules or in the starch granule surface properties (Mahlow et al., 2014).

Overall, a direct link between granule number per chloroplast, granule size, and granule morphology is demonstrated. Unfortunately, there is still a huge gap in our understanding of the various parameters, which in part is conditioned by the time and resolution limitations of the available methods and techniques (see also Compart et al., 2021).

The research performed to date indicates that many of the elements involved in the starch formation initiation machinery in Arabidopsis are conserved in other plants (other elements are missing in some groups, such as PTST3 in grasses; Seung et al., 2017), where they carry out a similar function to those described in Arabidopsis. For example, SS4 is required for normal starch granule formation initiation in wheat endosperm amyloplasts (Hawkins et al., 2021). However, the absence of daily turnover in long-term starch storage and the different metabolic environment in storage organs means that the impact of the different elements of the initiation machinery on the synthesis of starch is different to that described in Arabidopsis chloroplasts. For instance, the elimination of SS3a and SS4b in rice (Oryza sativa) leads to a change in granule morphology from polyhedral to spherical granules and to a reduction in the starch content in grains (Toyosawa et al., 2016), although this phenotype is not as severe as that of the Arabidopsis ss3 ss4 double mutant, probably because of the presence of other isoforms of SS3 and SS4. Another difference between

chloroplasts and amyloplasts, in which transitory and long-term starch is accumulated, respectively, is the absence of thylakoid membranes in the latter. Many of the elements involved in the initiation of starch granule formation in chloroplasts are associated to the thylakoid membranes, likely providing specific points for the initiation of granule formation. Further studies are necessary to understand how these points are established in plastids lacking thylakoid membranes.

Most of the newly identified proteins of the starch initiation machinery are absent in green algae (Seung et al., 2017, 2018), suggesting the presence of a core set, which includes SS4 and PHS1, that is shared by green algae and land plants. The necessity and advantage of the potentially newly established initiation mechanism is unclear, but it could be related to the increase in granule number per chloroplast frequently observed in land plants compared to algae. Evolutionary studies of starch granule formation initiation in different groups of land plants and green algae are needed to define the core elements necessary for the initiation of starch granule formation and to understand the selective pressure that might explain the differences between these organisms.

# CONCLUSION

Figure 3 summarizes the current model of starch granule formation initiation in the Arabidopsis chloroplast. The initiation of starch synthesis requires at least SS4, PTST2, PTST3, PII1, and MFP1. In addition, SS3 and SS5 influence/substrate for other enzymes (at least when SS4 is missing) probably by a different mechanism. PHS1 also seems to be involved, maybe by providing the necessary MOS substrate. A direct protein-protein interaction is reported for SS4 and PHS1 (Malinova et al., 2018), and thus the latter might be involved in both SS4-dependent and SS4-independent initiation mechanisms. Most of the

newly identified proteins involved in starch granule formation initiation in land plants are not found in algae (Seung et al., 2017, 2018). Therefore, SS4 and PHS1 may represent proteins of the original initiation mechanism that is still used in algae and which has been adapted to the needs of land plants.
