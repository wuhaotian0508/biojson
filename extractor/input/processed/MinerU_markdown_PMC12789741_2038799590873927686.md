GP and GS reduce adiposity in HFDfed mice by targeting gut microbiota. 

GP and GS favour specific CAZymes and thereby selectively stimulate the growth of gut bacteria. 

The microbiota shaped by GP and GS encode distinct functional genes for the metabolism of SCFAs and IGN. 

GP and GS differently regulate gut microbiota to activate SCFA-GLP-1/ PYY signaling and IGN to ameliorate obesity phenotypes. 

GP and GS are the crucial chemical components responsible for the antiobesity effect of ginseng.

# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Ginseng demonstrates therapeutic potential in treating obesity, with both experimental and clinical studies suggesting its anti-obesity effects are mediated by gut microbiota. Nonetheless, the specific chemical components responsible for this effect remain largely unidentified. 

Objectives: This study aims to investigate the anti-obesity effects and mechanisms of ginseng polysaccharides (GP) and ginsenosides (GS), the primary chemical components of ginseng, with a focus on their impact on gut microbiota. 

Methods: The impact of GP and GS on high-fat diet (HFD)-induced obesity was assessed using a mouse model. Molecular mechanisms were explored through a combination of chemical analysis, metagenomics, RT-qPCR, ELISA, and biochemical assays. 

Results: GP or GS administration effectively prevented adiposity in HFD-fed mice, and both effects were mediated by gut microbiota. Chemical analysis revealed diverse glycosyl groups in GP and GS. Metagenomics data suggested that GP-enriched species, e.g., Bacteroides stercorirosoris and Clostridiales bacterium encoded carbohydrate-active enzymes GH35, GH43 and PL9_1, while GS-enriched 

Sulfurospirillum halorespirans encoded GH16_5. These enzymes facilitated the utilization of glycosyl groups in GP and GS, selectively stimulating bacterial growth and reshaping the gut microbiota. Furthermore, bacterial species enriched by GP or GS encoded specific functional genes involved in short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) synthesis (K00625 and K00925 for GP; K18118, K00100, and K18122 for GS) and intestinal gluconeogenesis (IGN) (K01678, K00024, and K01596 for GP; K18118 and K00278 for GS). Consequently, the SCFA-GLP-1/PYY signaling and IGN were activated by both GP and GS to ameliorate obesity phenotypes. 

Conclusion: GP and GS, containing diverse glycosyl groups, selectively stimulate specific gut bacteria, triggering mechanisms involved in SCFA-GLP-1/PYY signaling and IGN activation to reduce adiposity in HFD-fed mice. The study enhances understanding of the chemical components crucial for the gut microbiota-mediated anti-obesity effect of ginseng. The mechanistic understanding provides valuable insights for developing ginseng-based drugs or health products to combat obesity.

# Introduction

Obesity, characterized by an excess of fat accumulation, is a complex chronic condition that is closely associated with metabolic disorders, including insulin resistance, and low-grade inflammation. In modern society, obesity has emerged as a leading health concern because it not only compromises the quality of life but also promotes the incidence of conditions such as type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, stroke, cancer and depression [1]. Thus far, obesity prevention and treatment strategies have not been successful in the long term, in which lifestyle and behavioral interventions have limited effectiveness, while anti-obesity medications and bariatric surgery may cause side effects and/or unpredicted risk [1,2]. These circumstances define a necessary agenda for developing more effective, safer treatment strategies for obesity. 

In recent decades, it has been increasingly recognized that gut microbiota dysbiosis acts as a pathogen-like entity in the onset and progression of obesity [3–5]. Furthermore, the current knowledge obtained from cause-and-effect research has indicated that various members in the gut microbial community play different mechanistic roles in preventing, causing, or treating obesity [6– 9]. For example, various bacteria, such as Bacteroides spp., and Bifidobacterium spp., are proficient at producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), which can mitigate obesity-associated insulin resistance and inflammation through G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) signaling [6,10]. Conversely, Proteobacteria have been shown to release lipopolysaccharides (LPS), a major component of the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria, into the enterohepatic circulation. This release activates Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) signaling, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and resulting in metabolic inflammation, insulin resistance, and obesity [8]. The evidence allows gut microbiota to be promising targets for developing anti-obese therapies [11]. 

Ginseng, one of the most famed herbal medicines worldwide, shows therapeutic potential for obesity [12,13]. Moreover, both experimental and clinical studies have suggested that gut microbiota mediates the anti-obesity effect of ginseng [14–21]. However, the chemical components in ginseng accountable for this gut microbiota-mediated anti-obesity effect are still largely unknown. Ginseng comprises multiple components, with ginseng polysaccharides (GP) and ginsenosides (GS) being the most prominent [22]. While previous research has not examined the anti-obesity effects of GP, the in vivo anti-obesity effects of GS have been investigated; however, the role of gut microbiota in mediating these effects remains largely unclear [17,18]. Both GP and GS contain abundant glycosyl groups, but they are structurally diverse. GP are characterized by high-degree glycan polymers composed of various monosaccharide residues linked by different glycosidic bonds, forming long chains or branches [23]. In contrast, glycosyls in GS are generally found as monomers or oligomers [22]. Accumu-

lating studies have shown that GP and GS orally administered are fermented by gut microbiota with a large number of carbohydrateactive enzymes (CAZymes) encoded in their genomes [24–28]. In this process, the glycosyls in GP and GS serve as nutrients to a subset of the microbes, thereby facilitating their growth. Moreover, CAZymes residing in different gut bacteria are compositionally specific. For example, Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes are known to encode a higher number of polysaccharide-degrading enzymes compared to Proteobacteria, which possess a more limited repertoire of CAZymes that primarily enable the utilization of monosaccharides and disaccharide s [26,29,30]. These differences in specificity may lead to structurally distinct preferences among microbes in utilizing GP and GS. 

Thus, in this study, we aim to elucidate if and how GP and GS play crucial roles in the gut microbiota-mediated anti-obesity effect of ginseng. To address this, we hypothesized that GP and GS, with their structurally diverse glycosyl groups, favor the specific CAZymes encoded in different obesity-related gut bacteria. The preferences lead to the selective stimulation of these bacteria’s growth, ultimately triggering particular molecular and cellular mechanisms for suppressing obesity. To test the hypothesis, we investigated the effects of GP and GS on high-fat diet (HFD)- induced obesity in a mouse model, in which we highlighted how the structural specificity of GP and GS drove different interactions with gut microbiota to achieve their anti-obesity effects. The research outcomes will elucidate the gut microbiota-mediated effects and mechanisms of GP and GS in suppressing HFDinduced obesity. This mechanistic understanding is expected to shed new light on the development of ginseng-based drugs or health products for the prophylactic and remedial management of obesity.

# Preparation of GP and GS

Dried ginseng slices $( 5 0 0 \mathrm { g } )$ were boiled with 7 L distilled water for $1 . 5 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ twice. The extracts were centrifuged at 5500 rpm for $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the supernatants were collected and combined. The extracted solutions were precipitated by adding ethanol to make a final concentration of $8 0 \%$ and kept at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ . Extracts were centrifuged at 5500 rpm for $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . The precipitate was washed with ethanol three times and dried in a water bath at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to remove residual ethanol. The generated precipitate was washed with Sevag reagent (isoamyl alcohol and chloroform in 1:4 ratio). After that, the precipitate was dissolved in water and then dialyzed with molecular cut-off $3 . 5 \mathrm { \ k D a }$ for $^ { 4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The dialyzate was lyophilized, yielding GP (172.6 g) [24]. The supernatants from the ethanol precipitation were concentrated till dry. The concentrates were diluted in $2 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m L }$ water, followed by extracting with watersaturated n-butanol $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m L } \times 5$ times). The extracts were then combined and freeze-dried to achieve GS (17.58 g) [24]. 

# Structural characterization of GP and GS

The physicochemical and structural properties of GP were characterized as described in our previous studies [31,32]. In brief, the phenol–sulfuric acid method was performed to determine the content of total sugar in GP by using D-galactose as standard. Uronic acid content in GP was determined by m-hydroxyldiphenyl assay using D-galacturonic acid as standard. The neutral polysaccharides (NP) and acidic polysaccharides (AP) fractions in GP were separated using DEAE-cellulose column chromatography (Waters Corp., MA, USA). The molecular weight distributions of NP and AP were determined by high performance gel permeation chromatography (HPGPC) on an Agilent 1200 system (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) equipped with a refractive index detector using dextran standards (MW 2000, 670, 410, 270, 150, 80, 50, 12, 5, and 1 kDa) for the construction of the molecular weightretention time calibration curve. The Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) spectra of NP and AP were determined using a Nicolet 380 FT-IR spectrophotometer (Nicolet, Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) with a scan range from 4000 to $4 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ Monosaccharide compositions of NP and AP were analyzed by high performance liquid chromatography with a diode-array detector (HPLC-DAD, Agilent 1260, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). Methylation analysis was performed to determine the glycosidic linkages present in NP and AP through gas chromatographymass spectrometry (GC–MS, Agilent GCMS-5975, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). Typical GS was qualitatively and quantitatively determined by using ultra-performance liquid chromatography coupled with quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (UPLC-QTOF-MS/MS, Waters Acquity UPLC core system coupled with Waters Synapt G2 QTOF mass spectrometer, Waters Corp., MA, USA) and ultra-performance liquid chromatographytriple quadrupole mass spectrometry (UPLC-QqQ-MS, Agilent 

1290 UPLC system coupled with a 6460 QqQ mass spectrometer, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA), as we established before [33–35]. 

# Animals and treatments

Eight-week-old male C57BL/6J mice (18–22 g) were obtained from the Chinese University of Hong Kong (Hong Kong SAR, China). One week before the experiment, the animals were kept in standard living conditions (room temperature $2 2 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ 12 h light/dark normal cycle, and constant humidity) with free access to food and water at the animal laboratory of the School of Chinese Medicine, Hong Kong Baptist University. Mice were randomly distributed into four groups (10 mice per group) and fed with either a standard chow diet $1 0 \%$ kcal fat) or an HFD $6 0 \%$ kcal fat) plus daily administration of either saline or GP, GS by intragastric gavage (i.g.) for 7 weeks (Fig. S1) to evaluate the effects of GP and GS on preventing HFD-induced obesity. The dosages of GP and GS were $4 6 6 ~ \mathrm { m g / k g }$ and 47.5 mg/kg, respectively. These were calculated with reference to the amounts of GP and GS in the clinical dosage of ginseng $_ { 9 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ ginseng herbal material/day, recommended by Chinese Pharmacopoeia, 2020 edition) and converted into mouse dosage by the standard human equivalent dose conversion equation [36]. The body weight and energy intake of the animals were measured every week during the study. After 7 weeks of treatment, whole blood was collected from the ophthalmic venous plexus, and serum was separated by centrifuging at ${ 2 , 0 0 0 } \ g$ for 15 min. After the trial, mice were euthanized by cervical spondylolisthesis under anaesthesia, and then the adipose tissue, ileum tissue, and cecal contents were collected for subsequent analysis. The inguinal fat, epididymal fat, and brown adipose tissue (BAT) were weighed immediately after the euthanasia. The mice were then properly disposed of following the animal care guidelines of the Hong Kong Baptist University Environmental Health and Safety Committee. 

# Fecal microbiota transplantation

Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) experiment was performed based on an established protocol [37]. In brief, the mice from the GP and GS groups served as FMT donors, and their feces were collected daily from the beginning of 4th week to the end of 6th week each group separately (Fig. S1). The feces were frozen for storage at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until required for use. Eight-week-old male C57BL/6J mice (18–22 g) served as the recipient mice, and they were fed with HFD and treated daily with fresh transplant material from either GP- or GS-treated mice for 4 weeks (10 mice per group). To prepare fecal microbiota, donors’ fecal pellets $\mathbf { \nabla } _ { 1 0 0 } \mathrm { m g } )$ were homogenized in 1 mL sterile PBS. After centrifugation at 2000 rpm at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ for 1 min, bacteria-enriched supernatants were collected and orally administered $( 0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m L } / 1 0 \ \mathrm { g } )$ as soon as possible to HFD-fed recipient mice.

# Histological analysis of adipose tissue and intestines

Ileum and inguinal adipose tissues were collected. Ileum and inguinal adipose tissues were fixed in $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , dehydrated with 70 %, $8 0 \%$ , $9 0 \%$ , $1 0 0 \%$ of ethanol and xylene, 

successively, and then embedded in paraffin wax. Then, the tissues were cut into $5 ~  { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thickness section, and de-paraffinized in xylene, followed by H&E staining. The stained tissue sections were examined under a light microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany). 

# Oral glucose tolerance test

Oral Glucose tolerance test (OGTT) was performed on mice after 6 h fast with free access to water. Mice were intragastrically gavaged (i.g.) with $2 ~ \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { k g }$ glucose. Blood glucose was measured in tail vein blood at 0, 15, 30, 60, 90, 120 min after the glucose gavage. Area under the curve (AUC) was calculated to quantify the OGTT results. 

# Real-time qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from ileum tissue or BAT using RNAiso Plus according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA concentrations were equalized and converted to cDNA using PrimeScriptTM RT reagent Kit. qPCR was performed using iTaq Universal SYBR Green Supermix with primers listed in Table S2. Relative expression was normalized to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) and calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method. 

# Metagenomics and data analysis

Total fecal genomic DNA samples were extracted using the OMEGA Soil DNA Kit, following the manufacturer’s instructions. The quantity and quality of extracted DNAs were measured by a NanoDrop NC2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, MA, USA) and agarose gel electrophoresis (Bio-Rad, California, USA), respectively. The extracted microbial DNA was processed to construct metagenome shotgun sequencing libraries with insert sizes of 400 bp using Illumina TruSeq Nano DNA LT Library Preparation Kit (Illumina, California, USA). Each library was sequenced by Illumina HiSeq X-ten platform (Illumina, California, USA) with PE150 strategy at Personal Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Raw sequencing reads were processed to obtain qualityfiltered reads for further analysis according to the manufacturer’s instructions: First, sequencing adapters were removed from sequencing reads using Cutadapt (v1.2.1); Secondly, low quality reads were trimmed using a sliding-window algorithm in fastp (v0.20.0). Thirdly, reads were aligned to the host genome of C57BL/6J mice using BMTagger to remove host contamination. Once quality-filtered reads were obtained, taxonomical classifications of metagenomics sequencing reads from each sample were performed using Kaiju [38] with greedy-5 mode against an nrderived database, which included proteins from archaea, bacteria, viruses, fungi and microbial eukaryotes. Megahit (v1.1.2) was used to assemble for each sample using the meta-large presetted parameters. The generated contigs (longer than 200 bp) were then pooled and clustered using mmseqs2 with ‘‘easy-linclust” mode; sequence identity threshold was set to 0.95 and covered residues of the shorter contig to $9 0 \%$ . The lowest common ancestor taxonomy of the non-redundant contigs was obtained by aligning them against the NCBI-nt database by mmseqs2 with ‘‘taxonomy” mode, and contigs assigned to Viridiplantae or Metazoa were dropped in the following analysis. MetaGeneMark was used to predict the genes in the contigs. CDS sequences of all samples were clustered by mmseqs2 with ‘‘easy-cluster” mode, setting protein sequence identity threshold to 0.90 and covered residuse of the shorter contig to $9 0 \ \%$ . To assess the abundances of these genes, the high-quality reads from each sample were mapped onto the predicted gene sequences using salmon in the quasi-mapping-based mode with ‘‘--meta--minScoreFraction $= 0 . 5 5 '$ , and the CPM (copy per kilobase 

per million mapped reads) was used to normalize abundance values in metagenomes. The functionality of the non-redundant genes was annotated using mmseqs2 with the ‘‘search” mode against the protein databases of KEGG and CAZy databases. The above experiment and data analysis were performed at Shanghai Personal Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). 

# Serum LPS, GLP-1 and PYY measurements

Serum lipopolysaccharide (LPS) was quantified using a commercial LAL Chromogenic Endpoint Assay according to manufacturer’s instructions. Serum GLP-1 and PYY were quantified using commercial mouse GLP-1 and mouse PYY ELISA kits. 

# SCFAs analysis

The levels of acetate, propionate, butyrate, and succinate in feces were determined by UPLC-QqQ-MS after derivatization [39]. For sample preparation, $2 0 \ \mathrm { \ m g }$ of lyophilized feces was homogenized with $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 0 \%$ aqueous acetonitrile, and then centrifuged at $1 4 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min. For derivatization, $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ supernatants were mixed with $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 200 mM 3- nitrophenylhydrazine (3NPH) HCl in $5 0 \ \%$ aqueous acetonitrile and $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 120 mM N-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)-N0-ethylcarbo diimide (EDC) HCl-6 % pyridine solution in the same solvent. The mixture was reacted at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. After reaction, this solution was diluted to 2 mL with $1 0 \%$ aqueous acetonitrile. To prepare internal standard (IS) solution, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 1 mM 2-methylvaleric acid were mixed with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 200 mM 3NPH HCl and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ EDC HCl- $6 \%$ pyridine solution, the mixture was reacted at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ . After reaction, this solution was diluted to 10 mL with $1 0 \%$ aqueous acetonitrile. A $9 5 0 – \mu \mathrm { L }$ derivatized sample solution was mixed with $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the IS solution, and a $2 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of supernatant was injected into UPLC-QqQ-MS for quantitative analysis. Chromatographic separation was performed on an Agilent 1290 UPLC (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) with a Waters BEH C18 column $2 . 1 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m } ,$ , $1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } \cdot$ ). The mobile phase consists of $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in water $\left( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } \right)$ (A) and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ (B), with a gradient elution as follows: 0–2 min, $1 5 \% \ \mathrm { B }$ ; 2–10 min, $1 5 \mathrm { - } 3 0 \% \ \mathrm { B }$ ; 10–18 min, $3 0 \mathrm { - } 4 0 \% 1$ B; 18– 23 min, $4 0 - 5 5 \%$ B; 23–24 min, $5 5 \mathrm { - } 1 0 0 \% \mathrm { B }$ ; 24–27 min, $1 0 0 \%$ ; 27– 28 min, $1 0 0 { - } 1 5 \% \mathrm { ~ B ~ }$ ; 28–30 min, $1 5 \% \ \mathrm { B }$ . The column flow rate was $0 . 3 5 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the column temperature was $4 0 ~ \mathrm { ^ { \circ } C }$ . A multiple reaction monitoring mass acquisition method was modified based on previous established method [39]. 

# Biochemical assays

PEPCK and G6Pase activities in cecal contents were determined using PEPCK and G6Pase activity kits, respectively. The levels of G6P and glucose in the cecal contents were determined using G6P and glucose assay kits. These assays were performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# Statistical analysis

The results of biological assay are presented as means ± SD/SE M. The differences between the two groups were analyzed by Student’s t test. Datasets that involved more than two groups were assessed by one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison’s test. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.5 (GraphPad Software, Inc., Boston, USA). PLS-DA was performed in SIMCA 14.1. Spearman correlation analysis and co-occurrence network analysis were performed using the OmicStudio tools (https://www.omicstudio.cn), and networks were explored and visualized with the interactive 

platform Gephi 0.10.1. The top 100 most abundant differential species altered by GP or GS treatment (compared with the HFD group) were included for co-occurrence network analysis. The Spearman’s correlations at $\Gamma > 0 . 6$ and $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ were used for network construction. All results were considered statistically significant at $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Results

# GP and GS comprised structurally diverse glycosyl groups

GP and GS were acquired from the water extract of ginseng, and then were structurally characterized by hyphenated chromatography, mass spectrometry and spectroscopy. The total sugar content in GP was $9 2 . 3 8 \ \%$ , of which uronic acids accounted for $8 . 9 3 \%$ . Further chromatographic separation showed that GP contained neutral and acidic polysaccharide fractions (Fig. 1A). The neutral polysaccharides (NP) presented a narrower molecular weight distribution (around $1 2 . 1 3 \mathrm { ~ k D a } )$ ) than the acidic polysaccharides (AP) (1.87–82.49 kDa) (Fig. 1Aa). The FT-IR spectra showed typical peak at around $1 7 3 0 \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 1 }$ was the asymmetrical COO– stretching vibration, which proved the presence of carboxyl groups in AP but not NP and indicated the property of acidic polysaccharides (Fig. 1Ab). Monosaccharide composition analysis revealed that NP consisted almost entirely of glucose (Glc), whereas AP was composed of additional residues such as galacturonic acid (GalA), galactose (Gal), arabinose (Ara), and rhamnose (Rha), with high molar ratios (Fig. 1Ac). The molar ratio of monosaccharides in GP is as follows: Glc:Gal:GalA:Ara:Rha = 87.08:5.28:3.05:3.23:1.36. Glycosyl linkage analysis revealed that NP consisted of mainly glucopyranosyl (glc) linked by linear or branched $( 1  4 / 6 )$ -glycosidic linkages (t-glc, 1,4-glc and 1,4,6-glc); and the AP were heterosaccharides containing much more complex glycosyl residues, including terminally and $1  4$ linked galacturonopyranosyl (t-galA and 

1,4-galA), terminally and $1 \to 3 / 5$ linked arabinofuranosyl (t-araf, 1,5-araf, 1,3,5-araf), terminally linked rhamnopyranosyl (t-rha), terminally and $1  4$ linked glc (t-glc and 1,4-glc), and terminally and $1  3 / 4 / 6$ linked galactopyranosyl (t-gal, 1,3-gal, 1,4-gal, and 1,3,6-gal) (Fig. 1Ad). These are consistent with previous reports that GP consist of starch-like glucans $1 0 0 \ \% \ g l c )$ and heterogeneous pectins (galA, gal, ara and rha occur with high molar ratios) [23]. In addition, GS were found to contain various monoglycosyl and diglycosyl groups, typically ginsenosides Rg1 (glc), Re (glc and $g l c ^ { 2 }  { ^ { 1 } r h a ) }$ , $\mathsf { R b } _ { 1 }$ $( g l c ^ { 2 } \to ^ { 1 } g l c$ and $g l c ^ { 6 }  { ^ 1 g l c } )$ , Rc $( g l c ^ { 2 } \to ^ { 1 } g l c$ and $g l c ^ { 6 }  { ^ 1 a r a f } )$ , $\mathsf { R b } _ { 2 }$ $( g l c ^ { 2 } \to ^ { 1 } g l c$ and $g l c ^ { 6 }  { ^ 1 a r a p } $ ), $\mathsf { R b } _ { 3 }$ $( g l c ^ { 2 } \to ^ { 1 } g l c$ and $g l c ^ { 6 } \to ^ { 1 } x y l )$ (xyl denotes xylopyranosyl, arap denotes arabinopyranosyl) (Fig. 1B & Table S1). The results indicate that both GP and GS comprised abundant glycosyl groups, but their structural properties, in terms of the types and amounts of compositional monosaccharides and glycosidic linkages, are different. 

# GP and GS reduced adiposity in HFD-fed mice

We then evaluated the effects of GP and GS on HFD-induced obesity. Treating HFD-fed mice with GP or GS for 7 weeks significantly reduced body weight gain and suppressed energy intake compared with HFD feeding alone $( \mathbf { p } \ < \ 0 . 0 0 1 ^ { \cdot }$ ), and the energy intake suppression by GS was stronger than that by GP $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ ) (Fig. 2A). Both GP and GS treatments led to significant decreases at distinct levels in the fat accumulation compared with the HFD group $\left( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ (Fig. 2B, C). GP or GS protected the mice from HFD-induced glucose intolerance, as evidenced by the significantly decreased blood glucose level and the AUC of oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) $\cdot \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 2D). Gut microbiota mediated the suppression of GP and GS on HFDinduced obesity 

FMT experiments were then performed to determine the role of gut microbiota in the anti-obesity effects, in which the fecal microbiota of GP- or GS-treated HFD-fed mice were transferred to HFDfed mice, followed by an examination of the obesity-related traits. The results showed that the horizontal fecal microbiota from both donors significantly reduced the body weight gain and energy intake compared with the HFD feeding $( \mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 0 0 1 $ ) (Fig. 3A). In addition, these fecal transplant recipient mice demonstrated a trend towards reduced fat accumulation $\cdot \mathrm { p } < 0 . 0 6$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 3B, C), a significant enhancement in glucose tolerance $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 0 0 1$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 3D), and a notable decrease in certain inflammatory indicators, namely LPS and IL- $\cdot 1 \beta$ $\cdot \mathrm { p } < 0 . 0 5$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 3E) compared with the cohort of HFD-fed mice. The results demonstrated that gut microbiota mediated the suppressive effects of GP and GS on HFD-induced obesity. However, UCP-1, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-gamma coactivator (PGC)-1a, and PR domain containing 16 (PRDM 16) were 

not found to be upregulated in both FMT groups, whereas the expression of PGC-1a, an important activator to induce the high expression of UCP-1 in BAT [42], was even downregulated by the FMT $\cdot \mathrm { p } < 0 . 0 5$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 3F).

GP and GS enriched the abundance of specific CAZymes encoded in gut microbiota 

As aforementioned, both GP and GS have been found to be fermented by gut microbiota as the dominant in vivo process after oral administration. Specifically, the microbes digest the glycosyls in GP and GS by encoded CAZymes. Here we have ascertained that both GP and GS contain abundant but structurally diverse glycosyl groups. Therefore, with the aim of understanding the different impacts of GP and GS on the diversity of gut microbiota in HFDfed mice, we annotated and compared the CAZymes in all microbial samples using CAZy database (https://www.cazy.org/) based on metagenome sequencing data. The results showed that gut microbes in GP and GS treatment groups harbored different CAZymes profiles as PLS-DA showed a distinct separation between the score plots of the two groups (Fig. 5A). In detail, the CAZymes families enriched by GP were GH49, GH5_9, GH97, PL9_1, GH35, GH30, GH43, GH43_29, GH43_33, GT34 and GH103, whereas GS treatment enriched the abundance of CAZymes families GH16_5, GT41, GT45, CBM70, and CBM82 (Fig. 5B). The relationship between the structures of GP and GS and the activities of these CAZymes families was based on CAZy (https://www.cazy.org/), CAZypedia (https://www.cazypedia.org/) database and literature research. Among these CAZyme families, GH49 and GH97 target $\alpha \cdot$ -glucosidic linkages to digest glucan residues in GP with glucosidase. While PL9_1 includes pectate lyase activity that cleaves the pectins in GP. Additionally, GH35, GH30, and GH43 degrade gal 

residues in heterogeneous pectins with various galactosidases and galactanase, while GH43 contains various arabinanases and arabinofuranosidases that digest ara residues in these pectins. The glycoside hydrolases GH16_5 includes $\beta$ -glycosidase activity [44], and should therefore be capable of degrading the glycosidic linkages between the aglycone and glycosyls in GS (Fig. 5B). We thus assume that the distinctive CAZyme profiles observed in the two treatment groups are a result of the CAZymes’ specificity towards different glycosyls in GP and GS. In agreement with this, some of these CAZymes, including PL9_1, GH35, GH43 and GH16_5, showed significant differences between GP and GS groups $\left( \mathtt { p } < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ (Fig. 5C). In addition, GH126 and GH30_3 were found to be enriched in the microbiome of both groups $\cdot \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 5$ or $\mathfrak { p } < 0 . 1$ vs HFD group) (Fig. 5B), which might be due to the versatile metabolic functions of the CAZymes. For example, GH30_3 consists of $\beta \cdot$ - glucosidase, $\beta$ -xylosidase, $\beta$ -glucuronidase, and it could be involved in the degradation of both GP and GS by targeting specific sites, i.e., glc residues in GP and glc/glc ? glc, glc ? xyl, glcA ? glc bonds in GS (Fig. 5B). 

The relationship between CAZymes and bacterial species was then investigated by gene prediction and annotation based on metagenomic data (Fig. 5D). It was found that the CAZymes and bacterial species enriched in the same group were closely related. For example, B. stercorirosoris, B. oleiciplenus, B. uniformis, Clostridiales bacterium, and Eubacterium sp. 14–2, which increased in the GP group, were predicted to encode GH49, GH5_9, GH97, PL9_1, GH35, GH43, GH43_29, GH43_33, and GT34 that were accordingly upregulated in the GP group (Fig. 5D). Similarly, Candidatus Gastranaerophilales bacterium HUM_1, Verrucomicrobia bacterium, Sulfurospirillum halorespirans and their encoded GH16_5 and GT41 were enriched in the GS group (Fig. 5D). We further analyzed the microbes that encode certain CAZymes from CAZy database (https://www.cazy.org/) [45]. It was found that bacterial strains in Bacteroides cellulosilyticus, B. uniformis, encode GH97, GH35, GH43 (including GH43_29, GH43_33), GH30_3 (Fig. 5E). The results facilitated the understanding of why GP and GS differently regulated the gut microbiota composition in the HFD-fed mice. However, some findings were unexpected. For example, GPenriched species (such as B. oleiciplenus, B. stercorirosoris, Clostridiales bacterium) contain gene fragments that encode GT41 that were enriched in the GS group, while GS-enriched Pseudoflavonifractor sp. Marseille-P3106 contains gene fragments that encode GH49, GH5_9, GH97, and GH35 that were enriched in the GP group. The reason may be that a single bacterial species can encode a variety of CAZymes, but the expression of these enzymes relies on the environment [29,46].

# Discussion

In this study, we found that the oral administration of GP or GS, the two major types of components of ginseng, target gut microbiota to suppress HFD-induced obesity. To explore the mechanisms, we first characterized the structural properties of glycosyl groups in GP and GS, and the results were consistent with previous studies [22,23,51]. It has been reported that GP can be structurally categorized into two major groups: starch-like glucans, such as 1,4-glucans and 6-branched-1,4-glucans, and heterogeneous pectins, including several structural domains, such as rhamnogalacturonan-I, homogalacturonan and arabinogalactan, in which 1,4-linked glaA residues, 1,3/1,4/1,3,6-linked gal residues and 1,5/1,3,5-linked ara residues occur with high molar ratios [23]. GS are structurally elucidated to be triterpene saponins that are mostly composed of a dammarane skeleton with various monoglycosyls and oligoglycosyls attached to the C-3 and C-20 positions [51]. Glycosyl degradation by versatile CAZymes encoded in gut microbiota has been demonstrated to be the major intestinal metabolic pathway of GP and GS after oral administration [24–28]. We therefore investigated the CAZymes expressed in the fecal microbial genomes in the GP and GS groups to understand the molecular mechanisms underlying their different interactions with gut microbiota. The results showed that the CAZymes upregulated in the GP and GS groups were highly specific to degrade their glycosyls. GP treatment led to a significant increase in the abundance of CAZymes (GH97, PL9_1, GH35, and GH43) consisting of glucanase, glucosidase, pectate lyase, galactanase, galactosidase, arabinanase, and arabinofuranosidase (Fig. 5B). These enzymes are known to selectively target and degrade the glycosyl groups present in glucans and pectins found in GP. Among them, the starch-like glucans present in GP is presumed to be hydrolyzed through the action of host-derived amylases, thereby heterogeneous pectins as the primary substrate amenable to utilization by the gut microbiota. This presumption is supported by our observations, which indicate that the preponderance of GH30, PL9_1, GH35, and GH43 are implicated in pectin degradation. Notably, GH49 and GH97, specialized in the 

degradation of glucans, were also found to be enriched in GP, indicating that gut microbiota may have the capacity to digest a portion of the starch-like glucan in GP. GS treatment resulted in a selective increase in the abundance of GH16_5, a glycosidase enzyme capable of breaking down the glycosidic linkages between the aglycone and glycosyls within GS. We then analyzed the correlation between the profiles of gut microbes and CAZymes to further understand how the upregulated expression of CAZymes is associated with the growth of specific bacteria. It was found that various species encode the CAZymes that can digest GP or GS (Fig. 5D, E). Notably, a great number of bacteria encoding GP-degrading CAZymes belong to the phylum Bacteroidetes, which aligns with existing literature indicating that Bacteroidetes possess a broad range of polysaccharide-degrading enzymes [26]. This is likely the underlying reason for the notable increase of Bacteroidetes and Bacteroides in response to GP rather than GS. In contrast, bacteria encoding GS-degrading CAZymes primarily stem from Proteobacteria, which have been reported to utilize a limited range of carbohydrates, specifically monosaccharides and disaccharides that are abundant in GS [30]. These facts suggested that GP and GS, with their structurally diverse glycosyl groups, favor the specific CAZymes encoded in distinct gut bacteria, which thereby selectively stimulate the growth of these bacteria. Hence, we propose that certain bacterial species that are differently enriched by GP and GS are driven by this mechanism (Fig. 5D, E). 

The growth of these microbes may further trigger chain reactions to decrease or increase other commensal bacteria within the same or between different bacterial families due to colonization competition or dependence [52,53]. For example, B. stercorirosoris, B. oleiciplenus, B. uniformis, may be able to promote the growth of B. cellulosilyticus in the same genus, since there was a significantly positive correlation between the two sets of bacteria (Fig. S3). Similar positive correlation could also be observed in the GS group (e.g., B. sartorii, and B. clarus) (Fig. S3). Some cross-feeding studies have also found that polysaccharides can be utilized by Bacteroides spp., and generate polysaccharide breakdown products, thereby stimulating the growth of non-Bacteroides species, for example, Bifidobacterium spp. and species in Firmicutes and Proteobacteria [29]. These non-Bacteroides species mainly import the mono- and oligosaccharides for intracellular processing [30,54]. In this study, several species, such as B. pseudocatenulatum from Actinobacteria, Clostridiales bacterium from Firmicutes, and Azospirillum sp. 51_20 from Proteobacteria, were enriched in the GP group. We speculated that they may also utilize GP in cross-feeding ways with Bacteroides spp., as they also showed positive correlations with certain species in Bacteroides spp. (Fig. S3), which was supported by stable co-occurrence network between species from Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria, Firmicutes and Proteobacteria (Fig. 4D, E). We then explored if and how the different impacts of GP and GS on gut microbiota triggered specific molecular mechanisms for combating HFD-induced obesity. It was found that the bacterial species enriched by GP or GS encode specific functional genes that participate in the synthesis of acetyl-CoA and SCFAs, thereby resulting in different levels of SCFAs, i.e., more acetyl-CoA and butyrate in the GP group, whereas more succinate in the GS group. Given that acetyl-CoA is the principal precursor for acetate and butyrate production, the significant increase of acetyl-CoA levels likely plays an important role in the higher concentrations of these two SCFAs in the GP group in comparison to the GS group, albeit without statistical significance [6]. The discrepancy in the rise of acetyl-CoA compared to the downstream SCFAs can be ascribed to the multifaceted functions of acetyl-CoA in cellular metabolism. It serves as a pivotal building block, not only participating in the synthesis of fatty acids and isoprenoids but also fueling the tricarboxylic acid cycle [55]. In addition, GP or GS treatment also enriched distinct reported SCFA-producers or potential SCFAproducers. For example, acetate producer A. ruminis [56], propionate producer A. neopropionicum [57] and butyrate producer P. capillosus [58] as well as propionate and butyrate producer C. catus [59], were enriched by GP treatment (Fig. 4C). Furthermore, previous studies reported that the monosaccharide composition of the carbohydrate greatly influenced the production of SCFAs in bacterial fermentation [60]. Specifically, galactose and galacturonic acid served as the main components contributing to acetate formation; arabinose and glucose primarily produce propionate; the synthesis of butyrate results predominantly from galacturonic acid [60]. These monosaccharides were abundant in GP but not GS, which could explain why GP treatment led to more generation of acetate, propionate, and butyrate than GS at nonsignificant levels (Fig. 6E). As important metabolites from carbohydrates produced by gut microbiota fermentation, SCFAs exert their metabolic and appetite-related functions by binding to the G-protein-coupled receptors in various tissues and organs, including GPR43 and GPR41, and then stimulating enteroendocrine cells to release anorexigenic hormones, such as GLP-1 and PYY [6,48]. In this study, the expression of GPR43/41 exhibited an upregulated trend in both treatment groups, with the effects of GP appearing to be more pronounced than GS. This may be correlated with the significant increase of butyrate by GP, as it acts as a ligand for GPCRs and can trigger GPR43/41 activation [6]. In addition, while there was a rising tendency in the level of GLP-1 within the GS group, PYY was specifically upregulated by GP treatment. Although GP and GS work differently in terms of generating SCFA, sensing GPCRs, and releasing anorexia hormones, they are both capable of inhibiting energy intake in HFD-fed mice. In addition, different SCFAs have been associated with distinct metabolic benefits through specific mechanisms. For example, acetate can cross the blood–brain barrier, where it is taken up and activates hypothalamic neurons driving satiety [61]. Moreover, butyrate can also act on the gut-brain 

neural circuit to enhance fat oxidation by activating BAT thermogenesis [62] or to restrain inflammation by inhibiting histone deacetylases [63]. Succinate drives UCP1-dependent thermogenesis by brown adipose tissue in vivo, which stimulates robust protection against diet-induced obesity and improves glucose tolerance [64]. Succinate can additionally bind to succinate receptor 1, and not only activates an anti-inflammatory program in macrophages that modulates the metabolic response to obesity but also mediates the nutrient-sensitive dynamics of leptin crucial for the maintenance of whole-body homeostasis [65,66]. Therefore, given that treatments with GP and GS resulted in the production of varying SCFA levels, these additional mechanisms may be specifically responsible for the observed effects of GP and GS on reducing inflammation, activating BAT thermogenesis, and maintaining energy balance. 

IGN is a recently identified function influencing energy homeostasis. It has been linked to many metabolic benefits, such as decreased energy intake, decreased fat storage and body weight, and improved whole-body glucose metabolism and insulin action [67,68]. Mechanistically, IGN-released glucose can be detected by portal vein glucose sensor, which then transmits the signal to the brain through the peripheral nervous system to elicit favorable effects on energy intake suppression and glucose metabolism [49]. In this study, we found that both GP and GS activated IGN by impacting different nodes in the process. In addition, the activities of two important rate-limiting enzymes in the gluconeogenic process [68], G6Pase and PEPCK, were increased by both treatments, but GP treatment caused a stronger PEPCK activity than GS ${ \bf \tilde { p } } < 0 . 0 5 $ ), which should be at least partially due to the fact that GP and GS targeted specific microbial genes related to the IGN process. Another reason could be that more butyrate may lead to higher PEPCK gene expression since butyrate-enriched diet can enrich cAMP, which is a known activator of PEPCK gene expression [50]. In addition, GP treatment also induced a higher level of the end product of IGN, glucose, than GS treatment (Fig. 7E). The higher PEPCK activity may be one of the reasons. Several SCFAs have been demonstrated as the substrates of IGN process. For example, propionate activates IGN gene expression via a gutbrain neural circuit involving the fatty acid receptor GPR41; butyrate activates IGN gene expression through a cAMP-dependent mechanism [50]; succinate serves as an agonist of fructose-1,6- bisphosphatase in the IGN pathway [69]. Thus, the different amounts of these SCFAs in the GP and GS groups may also contribute to the different levels of IGN end product. The IGN process activated in the GP- and GS-treated mice, even with different mechanisms, should be causatively related to the improvement of obesity phenotypes, including feeding suppression, the decrease of fat storage and body weight, and the amelioration of glucose intolerance. 

Lastly, we would like to outline our perspectives on future research directions following this study. First, while our current focus has been on the individual effects of GP and GS, it would be intriguing to investigate the potential anti-obesity effects and underlying mechanisms of their combination. # Conclusion

In summary, as summarized in Fig. 8, here we found that both GP and GS prevented HFD-induced obesity by targeting gut micro-

biota. Interestingly, they differently regulated the composition of gut microbiota, notably, B. stercorirosoris, B. oleiciplenus, B. uniformis, and Clostridiales bacterium enriched in GP-treaed group, but S. halorespirans enriched in GS-treated group. These microbes were shown to encode the specific CAZymes that facilitate the degradation of GP and GS. For example, PL9_1 encoded in Clostridiales bacterium and GH35 encoded in B. stercorirosoris were involved in the degradation of pectins in GP, while GH16_5 encoded in S. halorespirans targeted the glycosidic linkages in GS. Beyond the CAZyme-mediated mechanisms, cross-feeding was also proposed to contribute to the modulation of gut microbiota by GP and GS. It was further found that the bacterial species enriched by GP or GS encoded specific functional genes that participate in the synthesis of SCFAs (K00625 and K00925 by GP, K18118, K00100, and K18122 by GS), as well as the distinct nodes in the IGN process (K01678, K00024, and K01596 by GP, K18118 and K00278 by GS). This ultimately triggered SCFA-GLP-1/PYY signaling and activates IGN to combat HFD-induced obesity. The study contributes to a broader understanding of the chemical constituents that play a crucial role in the gut microbiota-mediated anti-obesity effect of ginseng, thereby providing a solid scientific basis for the development of drugs or health products derived from ginseng for obesity management.
