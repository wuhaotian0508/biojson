# ABSTRACT

Maintaining a stable basal level of salicylic acid (SA) is crucial for plant growth, development, and stress response, although basal levels of SA vary significantly among plant species. However, the molecular mechanisms by which basal SA regulates plant growth and stress response remain to be clarified. In this study, we performed a genetic screen to identify suppressors of the root growth defect in Osaim1, a rice mutant deficient in basal SA biosynthesis. We found that mutation of the E3 ligase OsPRT6, a key component of the Arg/N-degron pathway, can rescue the root growth defect of Osaim1. Further analysis revealed that OsWRKY62 and OsWRKY76 act as substrates of the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway to modulate root growth. We demonstrated that reducing the basal SA level activates the PRT6 N-degron pathway and that basal SA modulates the stress response in part through the PRT6 N-degron pathway. Importantly, the effects of basal SA levels on the PRT6 N-degron pathway are conserved across plant species. Taken together, these findings reveal a novel regulatory mechanism by which basal SA represses the PRT6 N-degron pathway to modulate root growth and abiotic stress response in rice.

# INTRODUCTION

Salicylic acid (SA) plays a pivotal role in plant growth, development, and responses to abiotic and biotic stresses (Rivas-San Vicente and Plasencia, 2011). Under normal growth conditions, SA can maintain normal plant growth and development, and under stress conditions, SA-mediated signaling contributes to the amelioration of abiotic and biotic stresses (Rossi et al., 2023). 

Nonexpresser of pathogenesis-related (NPR) genes have been identified as core receptors of the SA signaling pathway in plants (Kesarwani et al., 2007), but there are also NPR-independent SA pathways, such as those involving SA-binding proteins (Pokotylo et al., 2019). Recent studies have found that SA can regulate root growth through both NPR-dependent and NPR-independent pathways. SA regulates quiescent centre (QC) cell division and stem cell maintenance by promoting reactive oxygen species 

(ROS) production through NPR1- and NPR3/NPR4-dependent pathways in Arabidopsis (Wang et al., 2021a). SA can also shape plant growth through noncanonical SA signaling pathways in plants. For example, SA directly binds to a subunit of protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) to inhibit its phosphatase activity, thereby regulating the polarity of the auxin efflux carrier PIN2 and inhibiting auxin transport, leading to attenuated root growth and development (Tan et al., 2020). SA also regulates root growth by an NPR1/WRKY45-independent pathway in rice (Jia et al., 2024). A previous study showed that SA is biosynthesized mainly through OsAIM1-dependent $\beta$ -oxidation in rice (Xu et al., 2017). OsAIM1-dependent SA biosynthesis is required for the maintenance of root meristem activity. SA represses the expression of redox- and ROS-scavenging-related genes to maintain ROS accumulation for root meristem activity, in part through activity of the transcriptional repressors OsWRKY62 and OsWRKY76 (Xu et al., 2017). SA also acts as a multifaceted phytohormone in the mitigation of abiotic stresses.

A high basal level of SA is important for tolerance to abiotic stresses in rice, including salt, drought, and high light intensity (Jia et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Nonetheless, how SA regulates root growth and abiotic stress response in rice remains largely unknown. 

The N-degron pathway regulates the degradation of targeted substrates by recognizing their N-terminal residue (N-degron) via the ubiquitin 26S proteasome system (Zhang et al., 2024). The N-degron pathway mediates a broad range of growth, developmental, and stress-associated processes (Dissmeyer, 2019; Holdsworth et al., 2020). Among them, the PROTEOLYSIS6 (PRT6) Arg/N-degron branch has been well studied in plants. Plant PRT6 was first identified in Arabidopsis in 2007 (Garzo´ n et al., 2007) Since then, this pathway has been extensively explored. In the PRT6 N-degron pathway, the N-terminal methionine (Met) residue is first cleaved by nonprocessive endopeptidases or Met aminopeptidase (MetAP). N-degrons that contain primary destabilizing N-terminal amino acids can also be obtained by conversion of pre-N-degron tertiary or secondary destabilizing residues (Nguyen et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2024). Tertiary destabilizing residues can be converted to secondary ones through the action of amino-terminal aminohydrolases (NTAN1 or NTAQ1) or the modification of cysteine (Cys) by plant Cys oxidases (PCOs) (Wang et al., 2009; White et al., 2017). Secondary destabilizing residues can then be Nt-arginylated by arginyl-tRNA arginyltransferases (ATEs) to generate a primary destabilizing Nt-Arg (Holman et al., 2009). Finally, proteins with Nt-Arg are recognized by the E3 ligase PRT6 and undergo polyubiquitination and degradation via the ubiquitin 26S proteasome system (Garzo´ n et al., 2007; Gibbs et al., 2011; Licausi et al., 2011). 

The PRT6 N-degron pathway has been reported to participate in many aspects of plant growth, development, and stress response (Graciet et al., 2009; Weits et al., 2014; Holdsworth et al., 2020). Three types of substrates are known to be physiologically mediated by the PRT6 N-degron pathway. The Group VII ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR (ERFVII) transcription factors function as key regulators of plant oxygen sensing for the hypoxic response in Arabidopsis and enhance tolerance to submergence in rice (Gibbs et al., 2011; Licausi et al., 2011; Lin et al., 2019). The Polycomb Repressive Complex 2 component VERNALIZATION 2 (VRN2) was also found to enhance seedling tolerance to hypoxia and waterlogging, and the LITTLE ZIPPER 2 (ZPR2) transcription factor was shown to be specifically stable in the shoot apical meristem to maintain meristem activity (Gibbs et al., 2018; Weits et al., 2019). The PRT6 N-degron pathway also has key roles in the regulation of plant–pathogen interactions, as impaired PRT6 activity led to enhanced resistance to Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato (Pst) in Arabidopsis and P. syringae pv. japonica in barley (Vicente et al., 2019). The PRT6 N-degron pathway also coordinates with phytohormone signaling pathways to regulate plant growth and stress response. For example, ethylene enhanced the stability of ERFVIIs to overcome hypoxia by inducing the expression of the NO-scavenging protein PHYTOGLOBIN 1 (PGB1) for removal of cellular NO (Hartman et al., 2019). The PRT6 N-degron pathway inhibited Abscisic Acid (ABA)signaling to promote seed germination, and disturbance of this pathway might abolish ethylene-stimulated seed germination (Holman et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2018; Zhang

et al., 2018). In addition, Arabidopsis AtATE1 and AtATE2 regulate various aspects of leaf and shoot development (Graciet et al., 2009). 

Here, we performed a suppressor screen of the Osaim1 mutant. We identified several suppressors of Osaim1 based on their ability to develop longer roots, one of which was designated Ossra1-1 (Suppressor of root growth defect of Osaim1-1). Genetic mapping showed that Ossra1-1 encodes an E3 ligase, OsPRT6, which is a key component of the Arg/N-degron pathway and can partially rescue the short-root defect of Osaim1 by restoring root ROS accumulation. We found that the basal level of SA can inhibit the PRT6 N-degron pathway, which is conserved across green algae and land plants. Furthermore, we also showed that the basal level of SA regulates the stress response in part through the PRT6 N-degron pathway in rice. Taken together, these findings reveal a novel mechanism by which the basal level of SA represses the PRT6 N-degron pathway to modulate root growth and abiotic stress response in rice. 

# RESULTS

# Identification of Osaim1 suppressors

Our previous study showed that OsAIM1-dependent SA biosynthesis is required to maintain root meristem activity and abiotic stress response in rice (Xu et al., 2017, 2023). To identify new players in SA-regulated root growth in rice, we performed a suppressor screen of the Osaim1 mutant. We identified several Osaim1 suppressors from an ethyl methanesulfonate-treated M2 population of Osaim1 on the basis of their ability to develop longer roots. We designated one of these suppressors Ossra1-1. The short-root phenotype of Osaim1 was significantly suppressed in Ossra1-1 plants, although the root length of this mutant was still shorter than that of the wild type (WT) (Figure 1A and 1B). Thus, the reduced meristem length and cell length observed in Osaim1 were partially rescued in Ossra1-1 (Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B). We also found that the shoot length of Ossra1-1 was shorter than that of the WT (Supplemental Figure 2). To reveal the molecular mechanism by which Ossra1-1 suppressed the root growth defect of Osaim1, we first measured SA levels in WT, Osaim1, and Ossra1-1 plants, because the root growth defect of Osaim1 was caused by a reduction in SA biosynthesis in the rice root. However, the SA level of the Ossra1-1 plants was comparable to that of Osaim1 plants (Figure 1C), indicating that rescue of the short-root phenotype in the Ossra1-1 plants was not caused by recovery of SA biosynthesis. 

Our previous study showed that reducing the basal level of SA altered root ROS accumulation and caused a short-root phenotype (Xu et al., 2017); we therefore supposed that Ossra1-1 suppressed the root growth defect of Osaim1 through recovery of ROS scavenging. We first performed an RNA sequencing analysis of WT, Osaim1, and Ossra1-1 root tips to examine global changes in the transcriptome of Ossra1-1. Analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) showed that 1515 genes and 735 genes were significantly upregulated and downregulated (fold change $\geq 1 . 5$ , adjusted $p \leq 0 . 0 1$ ), respectively, in Osaim1 compared with the WT. # OsSRA1-1 encodes PRT6

To determine the molecular identity of the suppressor, we used the MutMap approach (Abe et al., 2012) to identify the mutated gene. The Ossra1-1 mutation was mapped to a locus on chromosome 1. A single C-to-T mutation was identified in the coding region of LOC_Os01g05500, which introduced a premature stop codon (Q to *) into the putative coding region (Figure 2A). LOC_Os01g05500 was annotated as encoding an E3 ligase with high similarity to AtPRT6 in Arabidopsis. AtPRT6 is an E3 ligase in the N-degron pathway and regulates plant growth, development, and stress-associated processes (Holdsworth et al., 2020). Hence, we named the rice homolog LOC_Os01g05500 OsPRT6. 

Expression analysis of OsPRT6 by qPCR showed that OsPRT6 was ubiquitously expressed in various organs, including roots, shoot bases, leaves, leaf sheaths, and panicles (Supplemental Figure 3A). The tissue-specific expression patterns of OsPRT6 were further examined using a ProOsPRT6: GUS transgenic reporter line, which indicated that OsPRT6 was predominantly

expressed in actively dividing cells of tissues such as root tips, young leaves, and lateral root primordia (Supplemental Figure 3B). These results indicate that OsPRT6 may be essential for the maintenance of meristem viability. 

To confirm that this mutation was responsible for suppressing the short-root phenotype of Osaim1, we performed a cosegregation test. Phenotypic and genotypic analyses revealed that homozygous Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutants had longer roots (Supplemental Figure 4A and 4B). We then transformed the genomic fragment of OsPRT6 into the Ossra1-1 background. Unfortunately, we failed to obtain any positive transgenic plants. Alternatively, we created Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutants using CRISPR-Cas9 in the Osaim1 background. Similar to Ossra1-1 plants, the Osprt6Cas9 Osaim1 double mutant plants also had longer roots than Osaim1 (Figure 2C and 2D), suggesting that the mutation in OsPRT6 is responsible for suppressing the root growth defect of the Osaim1 mutant. To obtain an Osprt6 single mutant, the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutants were crossed with the WT (SSBM), and the Osprt6 single mutant was segregated from the progeny. The root length of the Osprt6 single mutant was similar to that of the WT (Supplemental Figure 5A and 5B). 

To investigate the presence of PRT6 in other plant species, we used a similarity search combined with a phylogenetic approach to identify PRT6 orthologs in 17 representative species with wellassembled genomes. Most of the representative plant species, including rice, contained only one homolog of PRT6 (Figure 2B). Multiple sequence alignment of the PRT6 proteins showed that the mutated amino acid in the Ossra1-1 mutant is conserved among different plant species (Supplemental Figure 6). These results indicate that PRT6 is conserved in plants and may be important for maintenance of normal plant growth and stress response.

To test this possibility, we designed a bi-fluorescence complementation (BiFC) imaging assay to examine the interaction between OsPRT6 and its substrates in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaves. We first tried to express the full length of OsPRT6 in vivo, but we did not detect fluorescence signals in tobacco leaves or rice root protoplasts. Furthermore, when we substituted the second Cys (C) of OsERF66 with Ala (A), an amino acid not recognized by OsPRT6, we could not detect a fluorescence signal (Figure 3B). # The basal level of SA represses the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway

As described above, the root growth defect of Osaim1 was restored by loss of OsPRT6 function. We therefore speculated that the function of OsPRT6 was enhanced in Osaim1. To test this possibility, we first analyzed OsPRT6 expression level in the WT and Osaim1 and found that OsPRT6 expression was

increased in Osaim1. We next asked whether the same effect was observed for the expression levels of other N-degron-pathway genes in Osaim1. Interestingly, the expression of OsMetAps and OsATE was also upregulated in Osaim1 (Figure 4A). This increased expression of OsPRT6 N-degron-pathway genes indicated that activity of the N-degron pathway might be enhanced in Osaim1. We then selectively stained the free N termini of native soluble proteins isolated from WT and Osaim1 plants. The amount of free N termini was significantly increased in Osaim1, consistent with the increased expression of OsMetAps (Figure 4B). To further test whether disruption of the OsPRT6 N-degron affects the accumulation of free N termini, we examined the global amounts of free N termini in WT and Osprt6 plants and found that the amount of free N termini was significantly increased in the Osprt6 mutant. These results suggest that mutation of OsPRT6 blocks the protein turnover of free N termini (Supplemental Figure 9). 

To further confirm that the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway was enhanced in Osaim1, we tested the stability of OsWRKY62 in Osaim1. A cell-free degradation assay showed that the degradation rate of OsWRKY62-His protein was faster in Osaim1 extracts than in WT extracts. We also found that the OsWRKY62-D2A-His protein degraded more slowly than the OsWRKY62-His protein in the Osaim1 extracts (Figure 3D). The degradation of OsWRKY62-His protein was also slower in the Osprt6 Osaim1 extracts than in Osaim1 extracts (Figure 3D). To confirm this possibility, we analyzed the effect of seedling treatment with 2-aminoindan-2-phosphonic acid (AIP), an SA biosynthesis inhibitor that inhibits phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) activity (Liu et al., 2024). Upon AIP treatment, we observed reduced SA levels and increased expression levels of N-degron-pathway genes, accompanied by accumulation of free N termini (Figure 4C and 4D; Supplemental Figure 10). Taken together, our results indicate that the basal level of SA suppresses the PRT6 N-degron pathway to regulate rice root growth. 

OsNPR1 and OsWRKY45 are well-known central regulators of SA signaling in rice (Shimono et al., 2007); we therefore asked whether OsNPR1 and OsWRKY45 were involved in regulation of the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway by the basal level of SA. # The basal level of SA regulates abiotic stress response in part through the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway

Both SA and N-degron pathways have been reported to play vital roles in plant abiotic stress response, and plant prt6 mutants show enhanced tolerance to several abiotic stresses (Mendiondo et al., 2016; Vicente et al., 2017). Given that the expression of OsPRT6 was higher in the SA-defective plants, we wondered whether OsPRT6 was involved in SA-mediated abiotic stress response. To investigate this possibility, we exposed seedlings of the WT, the Osaim1 and Osprt6 single mutants, and the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant to NaCl stress. We observed that the defect caused by NaCl treatment in the Osaim1 mutant was partially rescued in the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant. Compared with WT leaves, the leaves of Osaim1 became wilted after a 5-day NaCl treatment, but this phenotype was largely rescued in the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant (Figure 5A and 5B). After NaCl treatment, the Osaim1 mutant showed lower fresh weight and higher malondialdehyde (MDA) content than the WT. Moreover, compared with detached WT leaves, detached leaves of the Osaim1 mutant lost water more quickly and those of the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant lost water more slowly (Figure 5C–5E). These results suggest that increased OsPRT6 expression in Osaim1 impaired its stress resistance. Consistent with this notion, the Osprt6 mutant exhibited higher resistance to salt stress and a lower rate of leaf water loss than the WT. These results indicate that the basal level of SA may facilitate the abiotic stress response of rice in part through the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway. 

Taking these results together, we proposed a novel mechanism linking the basal level of SA to the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway, which controls plant growth and stress response (Figure 5F). In WT plants, the basal level of SA represses the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway, thereby stabilizing downstream targets to promote normal root growth and stress response. In the Osaim1 mutant, the reduced basal level of SA activates the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway, impairing root growth and stress response.

Given that both SA and the N-degron pathway are widely present in green plants and have crucial roles in plant defense and growth, it seems likely that the regulation of the PRT6 N-degron pathway by the basal SA level was established in the ancestor of green plants. 

We also analyzed the effects of reduced basal SA levels on the PRT6 N-degron pathway in the model eudicot Arabidopsis thaliana and the model green alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii. The expression levels of N-degron-pathway genes and free N termini were increased in Arabidopsis NahG plants (Figure 6B and 6C), which showed a dramatic decrease in plant SA content. Previously, we reported that the basal SA level was markedly lower in the Craim1 mutant than in WT Chlamydomonas (CC-4533) (Jia et al., 2023). Similar to observations in higher plants, the expression levels of N-degron-pathway genes and the levels of free N termini were significantly increased in the Craim1 mutant, suggesting that regulation of the PRT6 N-degron pathway by the basal SA level is conserved in plants and green algae (Figure 6D and 6E). 

# DISCUSSION

In addition to its crucial role in plant biotic stress response, the basal level of SA plays a vital role in plant growth and response to abiotic stresses. SA acts with other signaling molecules, such as jasmonic acid, auxin, and ethylene, to facilitate growth and development un-

der conditions of abiotic stress (Yu et al., 2020). In this study, we revealed crosstalk between SA and the PRT6 N-degron pathway 

in the coordinated regulation of growth and abiotic stress adaptation. In a genetic screen for suppressors of the short-root phenotype of Osaim1, we found that a mutation in OsPRT6 partially rescued the root growth defect of Osaim1. We showed that the reduced basal level of SA in the Osaim1 mutant activated the PRT6 N-degron pathway. The expression levels of OsMetAps, OsATE, and OsPRT6 were increased in Osaim1, and the amount of free N termini was significantly increased. Furthermore, treatment with the SA biosynthesis inhibitor AIP also increased the expression of genes in the N-degron pathway and the accumulation of free N termini. We then demonstrated that OsWRKY62/76 act as substrates of the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway. A cell-free degradation analysis indicated that the OsWRKY62 protein degraded faster in extracts of Osaim1 than in those of the WT. These results suggest that the basal level of SA represses the PRT6 N-degron pathway in rice. We observed a similar phenomenon in Arabidopsis NahG transgenic plants and C. reinhardtii Craim1 mutants, indicating that the repressive effect of the basal SA level on the PRT6 N-degron pathway is conserved in plants. 

MetAPs have been reported to cleave the initiating Met from target proteins whose N termini start with Met-Cys, Met-Asn, or Met-Gln (Nguyen et al., 2019; Holdsworth et al., 2020). Interestingly, our results showed that OsWRKY62/76, with an Asp at position 2, can physically interact with OsPRT6 in the presence of OsMetAP1A. Cell-free degradation analysis showed that OsWRKY62/76 were substrates of the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway. These results indicated that OsMetAP1A could cleave the initiating Met of targets with Met-Asp. However, its activity toward Met-Asp may be low, as we could detect an interaction between OsPRT6 and OsWRKY76 only when OsMetAP1A was constitutively expressed in tobacco leaves. Otherwise, both 

OsWRKY62 and OsWRKY62-D2A proteins were still degraded in Osprt6 extracts, indicating that other systems, such as other E3 ligases or autophagy, may mediate the degradation of OsWRKY62/76 (Zhang et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2023). 

We also found that the effect of SA on the PRT6 N-degron pathway was independent of the canonical OsWRKY45- and OsNPR1-mediated signaling pathway in rice. A previous study found that SA could also regulate root growth through noncanonical SA signaling pathways in Arabidopsis (Tan et al., 2020). A recent study showed that SA could regulate gene expression through epigenetic regulation (Powers et al., 2024). TGACGbinding transcription factors acted as main activators to mediate SA signaling (Liu et al., 2020). TGACG-binding transcription factors have been reported to regulate root growth and nitrate response in an NPR1-independent manner (Alvarez et al., 2014; Contreras-Lo´ pez et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022). Plants have evolved complex SA signaling networks to regulate their growth and stress response. Additional molecular mechanisms by which the basal SA level regulates the PRT6 N-degron pathway in plants need to be clarified. 

Although crosstalk between the PRT6 N-degron pathway and SA has not been extensively demonstrated, a few studies suggest a link between the PRT6 N-degron pathway and the SA signaling pathway. SA biosynthesis and signaling contribute to plant submergence and hypoxia tolerance (Chen et al., 2015). Cellproliferation-induced hypoxic niches limit the regeneration capacity of the plant callus. Hypoxia-inducible RAP2.12 binds directly to the promoter of the SA biosynthetic gene SID2 and enhances SA accumulation, which lowers the accumulation of PLT3, PLT5, and PLT7, thus limiting plant regeneration (Koo et al., 2024). However, in the present study, we found that SA content was unchanged in the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant compared with the Osaim1 single mutant, suggesting that the loss of OsPRT6 function might suppress the root growth defect of Osaim1 by modulating SA signaling. Consistent with this notion, most of the DEGs in the Osaim1 mutant were restored in the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant. Further experiments showed that the loss of OsPRT6 function repressed the increased expression levels of ROS-scavenging-related genes and the ROS-accumulation defect observed in the Osaim1 mutant. Therefore, these results indicate that the loss of OsPRT6 function suppresses the root growth defect of Osaim1 by regulating SA signaling. Given that the effects of the PRT6 N-degron on SA biosynthesis and signaling were complex, the mechanisms by which these two pathways interact require more study. 

The PRT6 N-degron pathway has been reported to participate in abiotic stress response. The prt6 mutant shows enhanced tolerance to several abiotic stresses. We found that the impaired abiotic stress resistance in the Osaim1 mutant was partially restored in the Osprt6 Osaim1 double mutant, suggesting that elevation of the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway in the Osaim1 mutant impaired its stress resistance. Our results indicate that the OsPRT6 N-degron pathway may act as a new player downstream of the basal SA level to regulate the stress response in rice. In the future, more investigation of the crosstalk between the PRT6 N-degron and the SA signaling pathway will help us to understand their roles in plant growth and stress response.

Hydroponic experiments were performed using a modified rice culture solution containing 0.74 mM $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } \mathsf { C l }$ , 0.2 mM ${ \mathsf { N a H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { P O } } _ { 4 }$ , 0.09 mM ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf S } { \sf O } _ { 4 }$ , 0.37 mM $\mathsf { C a } ( \mathsf { N O } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ , 0.55 mM ${ \sf M g S O _ { 4 } }$ , 0.18 mM ${ \mathsf { K N O } } _ { 3 }$ , $0 . 0 0 9 \mathrm { \ m M \ M n C l _ { 2 } }$ $\mathsf { M n C l } _ { 2 }$ , 0.075 mM $( \mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ) _ { 6 } \mathsf { M o } _ { 7 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 4 }$ , 0.019 mM $\mathsf { H } _ { 3 } \mathsf { B O } _ { 3 }$ , 0.155 mM $\mathtt { C u S O _ { 4 } }$ , and 0.152 mM $Z _ { \mathsf { n S O } _ { 4 } }$ with 0.125 mM EDTA–Fe. The pH of the solution was adjusted to 5.8. A. thaliana Col-0 and NahG plants were grown under long-day conditions $( 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 16 h light/8 h dark) and approximately $80 \%$ humidity. The C. reinhardtii WT strain CC-4533 and Craim1 (LMJ.RY0402.141054) mutants were obtained from the Chlamydomonas Resource Center (Li et al., 2019). Cells were cultured in standard Tris– acetate–phosphate medium at $\mathsf { p H 7 . 0 }$ under continuous illumination on a rotating platform (150 rpm) at ${ } ^ { 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ . For NaCl treatment, 14-day-old WT, Osaim1, Osprt6, and Osprt6 Osaim1 seedlings were treated with 100 mM NaCl for 5 days. To eliminate mutations other than Osprt6 in Ossra1-1, Ossra1-1 was backcrossed with Osaim1 for three generations. A co-segregation test was performed with phenotypic investigation and PCR identification to generate the Ossra1-1 mutants. 

# Plasmid construction and plant transformation

Sequence information for the primers used in vector construction is given in Supplemental Table 2. The CRISPR-OsPRT6 (LOC_Os01g05500) vector was constructed according to the instructions of Ma et al. (2015). The constructs were transformed into mature embryos developed from seeds of the WT (‘Shishoubaimao’) or the Osaim1 mutant by Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain EHA105)-mediated transformation. 

# Histochemical localization of GUS expression

For $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) staining, tissues were incubated in a solution containing $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.0), 5 mM ${ \sf K } _ { 3 } \mathsf { F e } ( \mathsf { C N } ) _ { 6 } ,$ , 5 mM ${ \sf K } _ { 4 } { \sf F e } ( { \sf C } { \sf N } ) _ { 6 }$ , $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, and 1 mM X-Gluc at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Sections $( 4 0 \mathsf { m m } )$ of various plant tissues were obtained using a vibratome (Leica VT 1000 S, Leica Biosystems, Nussloch, Germany). Images were acquired under a microscope (Nikon Eclipse Ni, Tokyo, Japan). 

# Quantitative RT–PCR

Reverse transcription was performed using $2 \mu \ g$ of total RNA and M-MuLV reverse transcriptase (NEB) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. qPCR was performed using Vazyme Taq Pro Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix on a LightCycler 480 instrument (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Three biological replicates were performed for each gene. The rice Actin gene was used as an internal control. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 2. 

# NBT and HPF staining

Rice roots were stained for 30 min in a solution of 2 mM NBT or $5 \mu \mathsf { M }$ HPF in $2 0 \mathsf { m M }$ phosphate buffer (pH 6.1). The reaction was stopped by transferring the seedlings to distilled water. Root tips were imaged under bright-field illumination for NBT staining or the GFP channel for HPF staining on a Nikon microscope. The intensity of NBT and HPF staining was quantified using ImageJ software. 

# BiFC assays

The Coding sequences (CDSs) of OsERF66, OsERF66-C2A, OsWRKY76, OsWRKY76-D2A, and OsPRT6D (1–1164) were cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300-35S vector in frame with either the C-terminal or N-terminal fragment of YFP. The CDS of OsMetAP1A was cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300-RFP vector to generate 35-OsMetAP1A-RFP. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 2. The constructs were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves by Agrobacterium-mediated infiltration (strain EHA105) as described previously (Walter et al., 2004). 

# Plant Communications

YFP fluorescence in N. benthamiana leaves was imaged 2 days after infiltration using a Zeiss LSM 710 NLO confocal laser scanning microscope. 

# Co-IP assays

The CDSs of OsWRKY76 and OsWRKY76-D2A were cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300-GFP vector to generate 35S-OsWRKY76-GFP and 35S-OsWRKY76-D2A-GFP. The CDS of OsPRT6D (1–1164) was cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300-3xFLAG vector to generate 35- OsPRT6D-FLAG. The CDS of OsMetAP1A was cloned into a modified pCAMBIA1300-RFP vector to generate 35-OsMetAP1A-RFP. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 2. Different combinations of vectors were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves via Agrobacterium. The co-IP assay was performed as described by Feng et al. (2008). The protein complexes were immunoprecipitated using anti-FLAG magnetic beads (Sigma-Aldrich) and detected with anti-GFP (Sigma-Aldrich) and anti-FLAG (Sigma-Aldrich) antibodies. 

# Recombinant protein purification

OsWRKY62-His and OsWRKY62-D2A-His proteins were expressed and purified from E. coli BL21(DE3) cell cultures using standard protocols. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 2. 

# Cell-free degradation assays

The cell-free degradation assays were performed as described previously (Lv et al., 2014). In brief, seedlings (WT, Osaim1, Osprt6, and Osprt6 Osaim1) grown under normal conditions for 5 days were harvested and frozen in liquid nitrogen for total protein extraction. Total proteins were extracted in degradation buffer (25 mM Tris–HCl [pH 7.5], 10 mM NaCl, 10 mM ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , 4 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, 5 mM dithiothreitol, and 10 mM ATP) and adjusted to equal concentrations in degradation buffer for each assay. The purified OsWRKY62-His and OsWRKY62- D2A-His proteins were incubated with various protein extracts at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ for 0, 10, 30, 60 min and 60 min with $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ MG132. The His-tagged proteins were detected using an anti-His antibody (Proteintech) diluted 1:7500. 

# Western blot analysis

Antibodies used for western blotting were anti-GFP-HRP (G1544, Sigma, 1:10 000), anti-FLAG-HRP (M2, Sigma, 1:7500), goat anti-rabbit-IgG-HRP (Sigma, 1:10 000), and goat anti-mouse-IgG-HRP (Sigma, 1:10 000). The proteins were detected using an ECL reagent (Millipore) and the ChemDoc XRS system (Bio-Rad) under standard conditions. 

# Quantification of free N termini

To determine the amount of free N termini in the WT and the Osaim1 mutant, soluble proteins were extracted from roots (50 mM sodium citrate buffer [pH 7.0], 1 mM EDTA). Protein extracts were gel filtered using PD SpinTrap G-25 columns (GE Healthcare) to remove free amino acids. Free N termini were labeled using $2 . 5 \mathsf { m M }$ extracted protein and $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ NBD–Cl (Bernal-Perez et al., 2012) in 50 mM sodium citrate buffer (pH 7.0) supplemented with 1 mM EDTA. After $1 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of incubation at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , the fluorescence intensity was quantified using a FLUOstar Omega plate reader (BMG Labtech; excitation, $4 7 0 \pm 1 0 \mathsf { n m }$ ; emission, 520 nm) (Gong et al., 2022). 

# AIP treatment

Germinated WT seeds were grown on mock medium with or without 10 $\mu \mathsf { M }$ AIP (BIOBOMEI, Hefei, China; AT6923) for 7 days. The AIP stock solution was prepared using distilled water. 

# Measurements of water loss

Water loss rates of detached leaves from 14-day-old plants were measured by monitoring the loss of fresh weight at the indicated time points at a constant temperature $( 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ and humidity $( 6 0 \% )$ . The percentage weight loss was calculated on the basis of the initial plant weights.

# MDA content assay

MDA contents were detected with the Malondialdehyde (MDA) Content Assay Kit (Solarbio, cat. no. BC0025) using a microplate reader. 

# Metabolite measurements

For metabolite measurements, seedlings were grown in a culture solution for 2 weeks. The aerial parts and roots were collected separately and freeze-dried. The freeze-dried tissues were used for metabolite measurement following previously published methods (Chen et al., 2013). The sample extracts were analyzed using an liquid chromatography–ESI–tandem mass spectrometry system (HPLC, Shimpack UFLC Shimadzu CBM20A system; MS, Applied Biosystems 4000 QTRAP). 

# RNA sequencing and data analysis

For rice samples, we harvested primary root tips (about $2 . 5 \mathsf { m m }$ ) of 4-dayold WT (SSBM), Osaim1, and Ossra1-1 seedlings. Total RNA was extracted from three independent biological replicates of each line using an RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen). Library construction and sequencing were carried out on the NovaSeq 6000 platform to obtain paired-end reads $( 2 \times 1 5 0$ bp). 

Transcriptome data were prepared as described in our previous study (Wang et al., 2023). In brief, paired-end reads for each individual were mapped to the rice reference genome (IRGSP1.0) using HISAT2 (version 2.1.0) (Kim et al., 2015). Transcripts per million values were calculated using StringTie (version 1.3.4b) with default parameters (Pertea et al., 2015). Differentially expressed genes were identified using DESeq2 (version 1.32.0) (Love et al., 2014) with a significance threshold of adjusted $p < 0 . 0 1$ and log2(fold change) values of ${ > } 1 . 5$ and $< - 1 . 5$ indicating upregulation and downregulation, respectively. GO analysis was performed using agriGO v.2.0 (Tian et al., 2017), and significantly enriched GO terms were identified using a threshold of $p < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Identification of core elements in the Arg/N-degron pathway

Genome and gene-annotation data for all 47 selected plant species were obtained from the Phytozome database (Goodstein et al., 2012), NCBI Assembly, PLAZA (Van Bel et al., 2018), FernBase (Li et al., 2018), and published data (Li et al., 2020). Sequences encoding core SA-related elements were identified using pipelines described in our previous study (Wang et al., 2021b). In brief, these core proteins were identified using InterProScan 5 (Jones et al., 2014) and validated by BLASTP with an e-value cutoff of 1e 10. The protein sequences carrying their featured domains were selected and extracted using in-house scripts (Supplemental Table 1).
