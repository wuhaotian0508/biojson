# Summary

Yellow-seed is widely accepted as a good-quality trait in Brassica crops. Previous studies have shown that the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway is essential for the development of seed colour, but its function in Brassica napus, an important oil crop, is poorly understood. To systematically explore the gene functions of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway in rapeseed, several 

representative TRANSPARENT TESTA $m$ genes, including three structural genes (BnaTT7, 

BnaTT18, BnaTT10), two regulatory genes (BnaTT1, BnaTT2) and a transporter (BnaTT12), were selected for targeted mutation by CRISPR/Cas9 in the present study. Seed coat colour, lignin content, seed quality and yield-related traits were investigated in these Bnatt mutants together with Bnatt8 generated previously. These Bnatt mutants produced seeds with an elevated seed oil content and decreased pigment and lignin accumulation in the seed coat without any serious defects in the yield-related traits. In addition, the fatty acid (FA) composition was also altered to different degrees, i.e., decreased oleic acid and increased linoleic acid and $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -linolenic acid, in all Bnatt mutants except Bnatt18. Furthermore, gene expression analysis revealed that most of 

BnaTT mutations resulted in the down-regulation of key genes related to flavonoid and lignin synthesis, and the up-regulation of key genes related to lipid synthesis and oil body formation, which may contribute to the phenotype. Collectively, our study generated valuable resources for breeding programs, and more importantly demonstrated the functional divergence and overlap of flavonoid biosynthesis pathway genes in seed coat colour, oil content and FA composition of rapeseed. 

# Introduction

Brassica napus L. (AACC, $2 n = 3 8 )$ is the largest oil seed crop in China, providing approximately $5 5 \%$ of domestic vegetable oil and contributing significantly to animal feed and biofuel. Therefore, increasing rapeseed yield and oil content while improving oil quality have been the main objectives for rapeseed breeding (Abbadi and Leckband, 2011). Yellow-seeded B. napus has fewer proanthocyanidins (PAs), phenolic compounds and tannins than the black-seeded type (Chao et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020), which helps simplify oil processing with less nutrient loss (Akhov et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2017). In addition, yellow seeds are generally associated with a low-lignin content and high-oil content compared to those of black-seeded B. napus (Chao et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022). 

The seed coat develops from the inner and outer integuments of the ovule, and pigment mainly exists in the inner integument and palisade cell layer of the seed coat (Mo€ıse et al., 2005; Wan et al., 2002; Zhang et al., 2022). Many studies have shown that the flavonoid pathway is involved in seed coat colour formation in Brassica species (Akhov et al., 2009; Auger et al., 2009; Li et al., 2010; Lian et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). The metabolites of this pathway, flavonols and PAs, are the main pigments for seed coat colouring and are oxidized by polyphenol 

oxidase to produce brown substances during seed ripening, causing the seed coat to turn black (Lepiniec et al., 2006; Pourcel et al., 2005; Routaboul et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2013). 

In Arabidopsis, seed coat colour is mainly regulated by flavonoid pathway genes, which include structural genes (TRANSPARENT TESTA 3 (TT3), TT4, TT5, TT6, TT7, TT10, TT18, Flavonol synthase (FLS), BANYULS (BAN)), transporters (TT12, TT19, auto-inhibited H+ -ATPase isoform 10 (AHA10/TT13)) and regulatory genes (TT1, TT2, TT8, TT16, TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 1 (TTG1), TTG2, PRODUCTION OF ANTHOCYANIN PIGMENTS 1 (PAP1), GLABRA 3 (GL3), ANTHOCYANINLESS 2 (ANL2)) (Appelhagen et al., 2014; Marinova et al., 2007; Xu et al., 2014; Yu, 2013). There are two groups of structural proteins: early and late biosynthetic genes (EBGs and LBGs, respectively). TT4, TT5, TT6, TT7 and FLS are EBGs and catalyse the early biosynthetic steps, encoding naringenin-chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), naringenin 3- dioxygenase (F3H), flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase $( \mathsf { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { H } )$ and flavonol synthase (FLS), respectively. TT3, TT10, TT18, BAN are LBGs and catalyse the late steps of the pathway, encoding dihydrokaempferol 4-reductase (DFR), leucocyanidin oxygenase (LDOX) and anthocyanidin oxidoreductase (BAN), respectively. TT10 (laccase 15 or polyphenol oxidase, PPO) can polymerize flavonols in the last step of PAs biosynthesis (Pourcel et al., 2005). In addition, numerous studies have indicated that the expression of the 

biosynthetic genes involved in the flavonoid pathway are controlled by regulatory genes (Appelhagen et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2014; Yu, 2013). R2R3-MYB and R/B-like basic helix–loop– helix (bHLH) transcription factors, together with TTG1 (WD repeat protein) form a well-known ternary MBW complex, and directly regulate the expression of EBGs and LBGs, in which the TT2-TT8- TTG1 complex is critical for PA metabolism in the seeds of Arabidopsis thaliana (Appelhagen et al., 2011a; Baudry et al., 2004; Xu et al., 2014, 2015). TT1/WIP1 (WIP zinc finger) may act synergistically with the TT2-TT8-TTG1 complex to regulate the expression of EBGs, such as TT4 (Appelhagen et al., 2011a, b). 

Both flavonoid metabolism and lignin anabolism are branches of the general phenylalanine pathway, in which three main enzymes phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL), cinnamic acid 4- hydroxylase (C4H) and 4-coumaric acid: CoA ligase(4CL) are involved (Fraser and Chapple, 2011; Zhai et al., 2020). The first step of phenylalanine metabolism catalysed by PAL is the formation of $\beta$ -coumaroyl CoA through C4H and 4CL. Then, bcoumaroyl CoA enters the lignin or flavonoid metabolic pathway. Lignin is a complex biomacromolecule polymer that forms the polysaccharide component of plant cell walls and provides adequate compressive strength to the cells (Peng et al., 2010; Wei et al., 2017). The caffeoyl CoA 3-O-methyltransferase (CCoAOMT), cinnamoyl CoA reductase (CCR), cinnamoyl CoA reductase-like (CCRL) and caffeate/5-hydroxyferulate 3-Omethyltransferase (COMT) genes play key roles in lignin biosynthesis (Chabannes et al., 2001; Goujon et al., 2003; Guo et al., 2001; Zhang et al., 2022). 

In the plastid, fatty acid (FA) synthesis begins with the same substrate (malonyl-CoA) as flavonoid synthesis, which is catalysed by ACCase by acetyl-CoA from the glycolytic pathway (Adhikari et al., 2016; Andre et al., 2007). Then, the key cofactor, acylcarrier protein (ACP), uses a series of FA synthases to produce long-chain FA-CoA and transport it to the endoplasmic reticulum for triacylglycerol (TAG) biosynthesis (Bates, 2016; Pollard and Shachar-Hill, 2022). The final products of FA synthesis, lipids, are stored as TAGs in spherical compartments referred to as oil bodies (Bates, 2016; Pollard and Shachar-Hill, 2022). Some studies have shown that oil body proteins may play a signalling function related to the synthesis or degradation of oil bodies, which is crucial for in the stability of seed oil bodies through electronegative repulsion and steric hindrance (Siloto et al., 2006; Shimada et al., 2008; van der Schoot et al., 2011). In addition, transcription factors involved in seed development are also involved in FA synthesis (Yamamoto et al., 2010; Wang and Perry, 2013; Wang et al., 2014; Correa et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022). 

Previous studies have shown that mutations in flavonoid biosynthesis and regulatory genes related to the flavonoid pathway can make the seed coat colour lighter in Arabidopsis (Appelhagen et al., 2014). However, in the allotetraploid B. napus, the functions of most genes in the flavonoid pathway are unknown, except BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). In this study, we used CRISPR/Cas9 technology to create the targeted mutants of seven representative genes in the flavonoid pathway, including three structural genes (BnaTT7, BnaTT18, BnaTT10), three regulatory genes (BnaTT1, BnaTT2, BnaTT8) and a transporter (BnaTT12). Seed coat colour as well as other important seedrelated traits was investigated in different mutants. Moreover, 

the gene expression of phenylpropanoid metabolism and FA synthesis in Bnatt mutants was analysed, and the molecular mechanisms of genes in the flavonoid pathway regulating not only pigment accumulation in seed coat but also oil content and FA composition in rapeseed were discussed. Functional analysis of key genes involved in flavonoid pathway in rapeseed will be helpful for understanding the genetic basis of yellow seed traits and provide theoretical guidance for yellow seed breeding. 

# Results

# BnaTT genes display distinct functional domains with different expression patterns

To systematically investigate the gene functions of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway in relation to seed colour formation, we selected seven representative BnaTT genes and isolated the genomic DNA as well as the coding sequences from RNA from the B. napus pure line J9707. There are two homologous copies of BnaTT1, BnaTT2, BnaTT7 and BnaTT12, three homologous copies of BnaTT10 and four homologous copies of BnaTT18 (Table S1; Figure S1). These homologous copies of each gene have similar gene structures (Figure S1). 

Amino acid sequence analysis showed that all homologous copies of these BnaTT genes except BnaTT10 share the same conserved domain as the Arabidopsis homologues (Figure 1a; Figures S2–S8). BnaTT7 contains a cytochrome P450 domain, encoding structural protein $\digamma 3 ^ { \prime } H$ and flavonol synthase (Schoenbohm et al., 2000). BnaTT18 contains the N-terminal DIOX_N and the C-terminal 2OG-FeII_Oxy domains. It encodes the structural protein LDOX, which synthesizes anthocyanins and PAs (Abrahams et al., 2003). BnaTT12 contains two conserved MatE domains and transports PAs into the vacuole (Zhao et al., 2010). Two homologous copies of BnaTT10, BnaTT10.A06/ BnaTT10.C02, contain three conserved copper-binding domains similar to those of Arabidopsis TT10 (Figure 1a; Figure S6); but the predicted BnaTT10.A02 lacked the copper-binding domains, which are essential for TT10 function (Figure S6). BnaTT1, BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 are transcription factors that regulate the transcription of PA pathway-specific genes (Yu, 2013). BnaTT1 contains a SUF4-like domain, which contains a zinc finger domain; BnaTT2 contains two typical Myb domains; and the functional copies of BnaTT8 contain the N-terminal MYB interaction region (bHLH-MYC_N) and the HLH domain in the C-terminal region (Figure 1a; Figures S6–S8). 

To understand the expression profile of BnaTTs, the expression data of different tissues in ZS11 (a reference genome of rapeseed) were collected from a recent public RNA-seq database BnTIR (http://yanglab.hzau.edu.cn/BnTIR). All of these BnaTT genes except BnaTT7 were mainly expressed in developing seeds. The expression of the two functional copies of BnaTT10, BnaTT10.A06/BnaTT10.C02, gradually increased during the early stage of seed development and peaked at 35 days after flowering (DAF). The expression pattern of other BnaTTs during seed development was similar to that of BnaTT10, but peaked earlier at approximately 22–28 DAF (Figure 1b). Obviously different from that of other BnaTT genes, the expression of BnaTT7 was widespread in different tissues, with the highest expression in developing seeds (Figure 1b). # Creation of CRISPR/Cas9-targeted mutations in BnaTT genes

To generate Cas9-induced knockout mutations in all of the functional copies of each selected BnaTT gene, a binary construct carrying two to four sgRNAs with Cas9 driven by the $2 \times 3 5 5$ promoter was generated as previously described (Figures S1 and S9; Ma et al., 2015). All of these sgRNAs were designed to target the first exon or conserved domain of each BnaTT gene and ensure the success rate of gene disruptions altering the reading frame (Figure 1a; Figures S1 and S9). 

The resulting six constructs (named PSH36-PSH39 and PSH41- PSH42) were independently transformed into J9707 using Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and generated 60 to 

233 independent plants for each construct, of which $5 0 . 0 0 \%$ to $9 1 . 4 2 \%$ were positive transformants (Table S2). The targeted mutations of each BnaTT were identified by Hi-TOM sequencing analysis of the target sites, and the editing efficiency ranged from $2 2 . 2 4 \%$ to $6 2 . 4 1 \%$ in the ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ generation (Table S2). 

To obtain stable lines with targeted mutations, these ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ edited plants were self-pollinated for several generations. The targeted mutations of progenies from ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ plants were verified by Hi-TOM sequencing analysis of the target sites. Variable numbers and types of homozygous mutants with different allelic combinations in each selected BnaTT gene were detected (Table S3). The homozygous mutant lines for each BnaTT gene with different frameshift mutations in all functional copies, which were predicted to result in nonfunctional proteins, were chosen for subsequent phenotypic characterization and gene expression analysis (Figure S10). 

To ascertain whether off-targeting occurred in the present study, we searched the B. napus genome for putative off-target sites with high homology to these target sites according to the CRISPR-P2.0 program (http://crispr.hzau.edu.cn/CRISPR2/). A total of 96 putative off-target sites were identified for 15 sgRNAs, and no off-target editing was detected in ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ edited plants by gene-specific primers amplification and Sanger sequencing (Table S4). 

# Bnatt mutants exhibit different colours and PA accumulation patterns in the seed coat

Compared to the dark colour of mature seeds in the wild type (WT), all the selected knockout mutants together with the Bnatt8 mutant generated previously produced visible seed colour alterations to different degrees (Figure 2a). Among these mutants, the yellow seed phenotype of the Bnatt10 mutant was the most obvious (pure yellow), followed by those of the Bnatt8, Bnatt2, Bnatt18, Bnatt7 and Bnatt1 mutants, which had a the darker colour in turn, and the seed coat colour of the Bnatt12 mutants was very close to that of the WT. When the yellowseeded degrees (YSDs) of the WT and Bnatt8 were set to $0 \%$ and $100 \%$ , respectively, the average YSDs of Bnatt10, Bnatt2, Bnatt7, Bnatt18, Bnatt1 and Bnatt12 were $1 3 1 . 6 6 \%$ , $8 5 . 9 7 \%$ , $6 2 . 4 7 \%$ , $5 6 . 9 3 \%$ , $4 3 . 7 8 \%$ and $2 2 . 3 2 \%$ , respectively (Figure 2b). Interestingly, the seed coat colour of the Bnatt10 mutant gradually became darker during storage (Figure S11), which was similar to 

the pattern observed in the tt10 mutant in Arabidopsis (Pourcel et al., 2005). 

Dissecting the seed coat and embryo of the WT and Bnatt mutants, it clearly showed that the seed colour difference was mainly determined by the seed coat, not by the embryo (Figure 2c), which was consistent with our previous reports (Zhai et al., 2020). To investigate the inheritance of the yellow seed trait in rapeseed, reciprocal crosses between the Bnatt10 mutant and WT were conducted. The seed coat colour of $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants from the reciprocal crosses exhibited the phenotype of the maternal parent, indicating its maternal inheritance (Figure 2d). 

The dissected seed coats during various stages of seeds of the WT and Bnatt mutants were used for the specific detection of PA accumulation by vanillin and DMACA staining (Figure 3a; Figure S12). In the WT, PAs in the seed coat showed a gradual increase starting at 21 DAF and peaked at 42 DAF and then decreased at later stages (Figure S12). Since targeted mutation of Bnatts altered the colour and PA accumulation in the seed coat, the expression of several key enzymes involved in the general phenylpropanoid pathway which is upstream of flavonoid and lignin anabolic metabolism, including PAL1, PAL2, 4CL and C4H (Fraser and Chapple, 2011), and key genes involved in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, was then compared between Bnatt mutants and the corresponding WT during seed development. Although there were different degrees of changes in these key enzymes involved in the general phenylpropanoid pathway, BnaPAL1 and BnaPAL2 showed similar expression trends that were significantly down-regulated in Bnatts except for the Bnatt12 in seeds at 35 DAF compared with the WT seeds (Figure 3b). As BnaPAL1 and BnaPAL2 

catalysed the first step of phenylalanine metabolism, the downregulation of these two genes in Bnatts indicated that the substrate available for flavonoid and lignin anabolism was reduced. 

In relation to flavonoid biosynthesis, most of the genes were down-regulated in Bnatt mutants (Figure 3b). Interestingly, the targeted gene itself was one of the most drastically downregulated genes at all stages in each Bnatt mutant, which further confirmed the loss-of-function of the targeted BnaTT gene and the reliable expression data utilized in the present study (Figure 3b). Regarding to the three transcription factors BnaTT1, BnaTT2 and BnaTT8, the down-regulation of flavonoid metabolism genes in Bnatt2 and Bnatt8 mutants showed similar trends and was more drastic than that in other Bnatts. These findings indicated that BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 acted synergistically and 

further supported the fact that BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 together with TTG1 form a complex to regulate flavonoid biosynthesis (Appelhagen et al., 2011a). The expression of the flavonoid transporter BnaTT12 was significantly down-regulated in Bnatt2 and Bnatt8, demonstrating the transportation defects in those two mutants. The expression of BnaTT2, BnaTT4, BnaTT7, BnaTT8 and BnaTT18 in 35 DAF seeds of the structural gene mutants Bnatt7 and Bnatt18 was down-regulated to varying degrees. However, the Bnatt10 mutant which displayed most prominent yellow seed coats, showed little differential expression except for Bnatt10 itself, probably because Bnatt10 is in the downstream of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway. Thus, the above results suggest that Bnatts affect the expression of genes involved in the flavonoid pathway during seed development. 

# Bnatt mutants reduced the seed coat thickness and the lignin content in the seed coat

Microscopy of paraffin staining transverse sections showed that the seed coat of the mature WT rapeseed consists of an outer integument (oi) and an inner integument (ii; Figure 4a). The oi consists of an epidermal layer (oi3) formed by one layer of cells, two to three layers of parenchyma (oi2), and a palisade layer (oi1) formed by one layer of cells (Figure 4a). The $j j$ consists of ii2, which consists of multiple parenchymatous cell layers, and ii1, which is the innermost layer of the seed coat (Figure 4a). In the WT, the ii2 parenchyma layer decreased gradually in number and disappeared during 35–42 DAF, while the oi2 parenchyma layer did not decrease in number until 49 DAF (Figure 4a), which is consistent with the findings of Nesi et al. (2009). The red-stained layers (ii1 and oi1) showed the lignification of seed coat cells. 

All the Bnatt mutants had less a red-stained structure during different developmental stages compared with the WT, which was further verified by the comparison of lignin content in seeds and seed coats (Figure 4a,b). Additionally, the seed coat thickness of all the Bnatt mutants, especially the palisade layer (oi1), was significantly reduced to different degrees compared with that of the WT (Figure 4a,cc), which was consistent with the alteration of the seed coat content (Figure 4d). The reduction in seed coat thickness/content showed a significant positive correlation with the lignin content of the seed coat (Figure 4e). Together, these findings indicate that the disruption of BnaTTs affected seed development and hindered lignin deposition in the seed coat. The reduction in seed coat thickness/content showed a similar trend to the alteration in seed coat colour except in the mutants of Bnatt10 (Figures 2a and 3a). 

Considering the reduced lignin deposition in the seed coat of the Bnatts, the expression of two key enzymes involved in the lignin biosynthesis pathway, BnaCCoAOMT and BnaCOMT1 (Chabannes et al., 2001; Goujon et al., 2003; Guo et al., 2001), was then compared between Bnatt mutants and the corresponding WT during seed development. Relative to WT, the expression of BnaCCoAOMT and BnaCOMT1 was downregulated in Bnatt mutants to varying degrees (Figure 3b). Combined with the expression levels of BnaPAL1, BnaPAL2, Bna4CL and BnaC4H, these results suggested that BnaTTs also play an important role in the regulation of lignin biosynthesis. 

# Bnatt mutants affect oil content and FA composition in seeds

To characterize the effect of the BnaTTs targeted mutations on seed quality traits, the oil and protein contents and FA composition were measured. As expected, the oil content of all 

the Bnatt mutants was significantly increased compared with that of the WT (Figure 5a; Table S5). Among the mutants, Bnatt12 had the highest oil content which was $8 . 2 5 \%$ higher than that of the WT, followed by Bnatt2 and Bnatt8 (approximately $5 \%$ increase), and the increases in Bnatt1, Bnatt7, Bnatt10 and Bnatt18 were relatively low $( 2 \%$ to $3 \%$ ). The protein contents of most Bnatt mutants showed no significant change, except for Bnatt10, which showed a significant decrease (Figure 5b). 

Examination of FA profiles in the mature seed and its two components (embryo and seed coat), showed a consistent alteration in oleic acid (C18:1), linoleic acid (C18:2) and alinolenic acid (C18:3) in these mutants (Figure 5c–e, Table S6). Most Bnatt mutants showed a decreasing trend in the C18:1 content, but an increasing trend in the C18:2 and C18:3 contents (Figure 5c–e). Among them, Bnatt8 showed the most pronounced changes in FA composition. The change trends of these FA compositions in seeds and their two components (embryo and seed coat) were similar, suggesting that the BnaTT gene had the same mode of action in the seed coat and embryo (Figure 5c–e). Notably, the oil content of the Bnatt18 mutant was significantly increased without significant alteration of the FA composition (Figures 2 and 5); therefore, this mutant could serve as an excellent starting material for rapeseed breeding. We also examined the yield traits of Bnatt mutants. Although several lines showed differences in the silique number of main inflorescences, silique length, seed number per silique and the thousand seed weight, the yield per plant did not change significantly compared with that of the WT (Table S7). In addition, for freshly harvested seeds, the germination behaviour of the Bnatt mutants at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ was not different from those of the WT, and after 1 year of storage, the germination speed was even faster than that of the WT and the germination percentage on the 6th day after germination was higher than that of the WT (Figure S13). Together, these findings indicate that targeted mutation of different BnaTTs conferred a high oil yield potential with different degrees of changes in FA composition in seeds without significant negative effects on plant growth and seed development. 

To further characterize the oil and protein accumulation pattern in Bnatt mutants, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed on the ectocotyledon of seeds at the mature stage (Figure 6a,b). All the Bnatts displayed a remarkable increase in the number of oil bodies (OBs; Figure 6c), while the sizes of the large OBs and small OBs of the mutants were smaller than those of the WT, except for the large OBs of Bnatt1 (Figure 6d,e). The number of protein bodies (PBs) in most Bnatts showed a significant increase, while PB size significantly decreased for all Bnatts except for Bnatt8 (Figure 6f,g).

# BnaTT genes exhibit functional differentiation in pigment accumulation in the seed coat

In a previous study, we showed that disruption of the BnaTT8 gene completely blocked the PA-specific deposition in the seed coat and resulted in seeds with a visible yellow seed coat phenotype (Zhai et al., 2020). In the present study, targeted mutants of the other six representative genes in the flavonoid pathway, including three structural genes (BnaTT7, BnaTT18, BnaTT10), three regulatory genes (BnaTT1 and BnaTT2) and a transporter (BnaTT12) were successfully generated. The homozygous knockout mutants of these genes could produce seeds with extensive variations in seed coat colour, in which the yellow-seed phenotype of the Bnatt10 mutant was more obvious than that of the Bnatt8 mutant. The yellow seed colour of the Bnatt2 mutants was very close to that of the Bnatt8 mutant but more obvious than that of the Bnatt18, Bnatt7 and Bnatt1 mutants. Only the Bnatt12 mutant exhibited no visible change in seed coat colour. The changes in seed colour in these Bnatts were similar but not identical to those in Arabidopsis tt mutants (Appelhagen et al., 2014). For example, the yellow seed coat colours of tt1, tt2, tt7 and tt18 in Arabidopsis were very similar, but the colour was almost the same, but it was obviously different in rapeseed (Figure 2a; Appelhagen et al., 2014). Thus, these BnaTTs functionally diverged from their orthologs in Arabidopsis. These results suggest that the regulatory network of flavonoids in allotetraploid rapeseed is different or more complex than that in Arabidopsis. 

In Brassicaceae, PAs are the prominent pigments that accumulate especially in the seed coat, where they are oxidized to a dark colour by polyphenol oxidase during seed maturation, resulting in dark brown seeds (Lepiniec et al., 2006; Pourcel et al., 2005). The specific detection of PA accumulation by chemical staining and histological analysis indicated that all Bnatt mutants in the present study showed different degrees of changes in PA accumulation, which agreed well with the alteration of seed coat colour except in the mutants of Bnatt10 and Bnatt12 (Figures 2a and 3a). For the Bnatt10 mutant, the yellow colour of mature seeds was the most obvious among the mutants even though more PAs accumulated in the seed coat (Figures 3a; Figure S12), which was similar to the result for the Arabidopsis mutant tt10-2 (Pourcel et al., 2005). A possible reason for this might be that BnaTT10 encodes laccase 15 (AtLAC15) and is involved in PA oxidation and polymerization in the last step of PA biosynthesis (Pourcel et al., 2005; Zhang et al., 2013). For the Bnatt12 mutant, PA accumulation in the seed coat decreased significantly, whereas its seed coat colour was very close to that of the WT (Figure 2a). Considering that the function of TT12 in Arabidopsis is to transport PAs into the vacuole for oxidation (Zhao et al., 2010), we hypothesized that the lack of BnaTT12 affected PA transportation but not PA oxidation. Thus, vanillin and DMACA staining methods may not be suitable for the early identification of all types of yellow seed materials. 

According to gene expression analysis, most flavonoid synthesis-related genes were significantly down-regulated to varying degrees in Bnatt mutants, especially the most drastic changes of the targeted gene itself at all stages (Figure 3b). Although there were different degrees of changes in these 

flavonoid synthesis-related genes in different Bnatt mutants, the expression changes in the pathway were consistent with the phenotypic variations in seed colour and PA accumulation; i.e., the more obvious changes in the phenotype were the more drastic down-regulation of genes in the pathway (Figure 3b). For the mutants of three transcription factors (BnaTT1, BnaTT2 and BnaTT8), the structural genes including LBGs (BnaTT4, BnaTT7) and EBGs (BnaTT10, BnaTT18, BnaBAN and BnaTT12) were all downregulated during seed development, which was consistent with previous reports (Appelhagen et al., 2011b; Debeaujon et al., 2003; Lian et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). In addition, it has been reported previously that TT1 can interact with TT2 and MYB75/PAP1 to regulate the correct expression of EBGs (CHS/TT4) and LBGs (DFR/TT3, LDOX/TT18, ANR/BAN and TT12) (Appelhagen et al., 2011b). TT1 can increase the activity of AtBAN synergistically with the MBW complex (Appelhagen et al., 2010). These results indicated that BnaTT1, BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 had similar functions and synergistically regulated the appropriate expression of upstream and downstream genes of flavonoids. The expression of structural genes was slightly downregulated in Bnatt1 compared with Bnatt2 and Bnatt8, possibly due to the partial restoration of TT1 function by WIP family members (WIP2, WIP3, WIP5, and WIP6) (Appelhagen et al., 2010). In the mutants of several structural genes (Bnatt7, Bnatt10, Bnatt12 and Bnatt18), the expression of the transcription factors BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 was also significantly downregulated. In Arabidopsis, TT12 and TT18 were directly regulated by the MBW complex; when TT2 or TT8 was mutated, both of them were significantly down-regulated (Xu et al., 2014). Thus, the alteration of structural gene expression may also activate a feedback regulation mechanism to regulate the expression of regulatory genes. 

# BnaTTs affected embryonic FA synthesis by regulating key genes involved in FA metabolism

In Brassica oil crops, yellow seed is widely accepted as a goodquality trait accompanied by a reduced percentage of pigment and hull, and a higher oil content (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). In B. napus, studies have shown that the functions of two transcription factors, BnaTT8 or BnaTT2, are essential for not only seed coat colour but also seed oil accumulation (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). Consistent with these findings, the seed oil contents of all the Bnatt mutants created in the present study were significantly increased compared with those of the WT, in which Bnatt12 showed the most drastic increase of more than $8 \%$ relative to the corresponding WT, followed by Bnatt2 and Bnatt8 with approximately $5 \%$ increases (Figure 5a; Table S5). Assessment of the yield-related traits and germination traits revealed that these mutants did not cause any severe defects (Table S7), which was consistent with previous reports that most yellow-seeded materials appear morphologically normal (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). Since a $1 \%$ increase in oil content is equivalent to a $2 . 3 \% - 2 . 5 \%$ increase in seed yield of rapeseed (Wang, 2004), the Bnatt mutants generated in the present study might provide excellent starting materials for promoting high oil production breeding in rapeseed. 

In Arabidopsis, TT2 regulates embryonic FA biosynthesis by targeting FUS3 during the early seed developmental stage, whereas TT8 inhibits FA accumulation by targeting several seed developmental regulators, such as LEC1, LEC2 and FUS3 (Chen et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2014). FUS3, LEC1, LEC2, and WRI1 are 

critical TFs during seed maturation that positively regulate FA synthesis in developing seeds (Correa et al., 2020). In this study, we found that both the BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 genes exhibited lower expression in Bnatts than in the WT at 21–35 DAF, which was the most important stage for the activity of TT2 and TT8 (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). The expression of the critical TFs, BnaLEC1, BnaLEC2, and BnaFUS3 regulated by BnaTT2 and/or BnaTT8, was significantly up-regulated during seed development in the Bnatt mutants, in which BnaLEC1 showed the most drastic increase (Figure 6h). Thus, it is possible that the down-regulation of BnaTT2 and BnaTT8 resulted in the activation of BnaLEC1, BnaLEC2 and BnaFUS3, which in turn increased the oil content in the Bnatt mutants. In addition, it is well known that the flavonoid and FA synthesis pathways share the same substrate malonyl-CoA (Adhikari et al., 2016; Andre et al., 2007). Thus, it is possible that a lack of flavonoids could result in a reduced substrate requirement and allow more substrates to enter the FA synthesis pathway in the Bnatt mutants. Alternatively, less flavonoids in the seed coat could enter the embryo and participate in the inhibition of FA biosynthesis, as previously reported by Zhang and Rock (2004). Indeed, we observed the up-regulated expression of FA synthesis pathway genes (BnaMCMT, BnaENR, BnaFATA, BnaGPAT9, BnaLPCAT, BnaDGAT2, BnaCPT) in the Bnatt mutants (Figure 6h). Thus, the lack of flavonoids in the seed coat could be another factor affecting oil content in the Bnatt mutants. 

In this study, we found that the targeted mutation of all Bnatts except Bnatt18 resulted in consistent modification of FA composition, including increases in C18:2 and C18:3 and decreases in C18:1 relative to those in WT seeds (Figure 5c–e). It is well known that several key enzymes, including FAE1, which catalyses the chain elongation process of FAs, and FAD2 and FAD3, which are involved in FA desaturation, determine the FA composition in seeds (Huang et al., 2020; Los and Murata, 1998; Ozseyhan et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2012). Our expression analysis of these genes showed that BnaFAD2 and BnaFAD3 were significantly up-regulated at the early stage of seed development (21 DAF) in these Bnatt mutants, with the most up-regulated expression in Bnatt12 and the least up-regulated expression in Bnatt18 (Figure 6h). Similar results were found in Arabidopsis tt2 and tt8 (Chen et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2014) and rapeseed Bnatt2 and Bnatt8 (Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020). Recently, it was reported that the knockout of GmPDCT1/2 caused increased C18:1 and decreased C18:2 and C18:3 in soybean (Li et al., 2023). Thus, the up-regulation of FAD2/3 and BnaPDCT in the Bnatt mutants are likely to be one of the reasons for the alteration of FA components. In conclusion, the accumulation of oil and changes in FA composition in Bnatt mutants are caused by various factors and need more investigation. 

# The regulatory network of TT genes in B. napus has functionally diverged from its orthologs in Arabidopsis

Previous studies have shown that yellow seed generally corresponds to a high oil content in Arabidopsis as well as in rapeseed (Chen et al., 2012; Chao et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). However, the changes related to FA composition were not always consistent between these two species. In Arabidopsis, the C18:1 content increased significantly in tt2 and tt8 (Chen et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2014). In contrast, the trend was the opposite in rapeseed with C18:1 significantly decreasing in Bnatt2 and Bnatt8 (Figure 5), indicating functional divergence of these genes from their orthologs in Arabidopsis. 

OBs are specific lipid storage organelles of seeds. Previous studies indicated that abnormally large OBs during the maturation of seeds were highly correlated with a low oil content in B. napus (Hu et al., 2009). Consistent with this finding, the Bnatt mutants with a higher oil content showed a marked increase in the number and decrease in the size (both large and small OBs) of OBs at the cytological level (Figure 6a–e). In contrast, the tt2 and tt8 mutants in Arabidopsis showed marked decreases in the number and increases in the size of OBs, which resulted in a higher oil content (Chen et al., 2012, 2014). In B. napus, it was also reported that some genes, such as pyruvate transporter gene BnaBASS2 and phosphoenolpyruvate/phosphate translocator BnaPPT, could increase the oil content with a decreased number of OBs and increased size of OBs (Tang et al., 2022a, b). Thus, the molecular mechanism for seed oil accumulation in rapeseed is complex, and BnaBASS2 and BnaPPT could both be involved as transporters and translocators, while BnaTTs could affect FA synthesis and OB formation. The underlying mechanism needs to be further investigated.

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

Rapeseed (Brassica napus L) cultivar ‘J9707’ was used as the transformation recipient, and the seeds were obtained from the National Engineering Research Center of Rapeseed, Wuhan, China (30.48 N, 114.28 E). 

# Vector construction and agrobacterium-mediated rapeseed transformation

The binary pYLCRISPR/Cas9P35S-H multiplex genome targeting vector system was utilized for gene editing in this study, which was provided by Prof. Yaoguang Liu (South China Agriculture University) (Ma et al., 2015). The sequence-specific sgRNAs in the target gene was designed by the CRISPR-P 2.0 design tool (http:// cbi.hzau.edu.cn/CRISPR2/) (Lei et al., 2014) (Table S8). In each construction, from the first to the fourth sgRNA is driven by AtU3b, AtU3d, AtU6-1 and AtU6-29 promoter, respectively. The resulting construct is described in detail in Figure S9. Genome editing of ‘J9707’ rapeseed plants were performed using Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated (GV3101) transformation, according to the method previously described (Zhou et al., 2002). Regenerated seedlings were selected according to the hygromycin resistance, and transferred to a field during the rapeseed growing season. 

# Identification of CRISPR-edited plants and off-target mutation analysis

Firstly, PB-L/PB-R primers were used to identify the T-DNA (Table S8), to confirm that the regenerated seedlings successfully gained the CRISPR system. To further explore whether the sequences of targeted genes were modified, genomic fragments containing the targeted sites of each BnaTT was confirmed by the high-throughput tracking of mutations (Hi-TOM) platform (Liu et al., 2019). The sequencing data was then decoded using a corresponding online tool to track the mutations of the target sites (http://www.hi-tom.net/hi-tom/). 

The potential off-target sites were identified using CRISPR-P2.0 (http://crispr.hzau.edu.cn/CRISPR2/; Lei et al., 2014). All the potential off-target sites for each BnaTT are listed in Table S4. 

# Seed colour measurement and trait analysis

The same number of seeds from Bnatts was scanned in real colour at 600 dpi. The YSD was calculated and used to evaluate the seed colour phenotype according to the method described by Fu et al. (2007). 

# RNA extraction and Quantitative Real-Time PCR (qRT– PCR)

Total RNA was extracted from seeds (21 DAF, 28 DAF, 35 DAF) using Trizol plant (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China), and cDNA was synthesized using the Transcript RT Kit (TransGen Biotech). qRT–PCR was performed with the TransStart Top Green qPCR SuperMix Kit (TransGen Biotech) on a CFX384 Real-Time System 

(Bio-Rad). Relative expression was calculated automatically by the software of qRT–PCR machine based on the $2 ^ { - \mathsf { a } \mathsf { a } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { t } }$ method. BnaACTIN7 was used as a reference gene. 

# Vanillin and p-dimethylaminocinnamaldehyde (DMACA) staining

To observe the seed pigment, we used vanillin and pdimethylaminocinnamaldehyde (DMACA) for PA staining in the developing seed coat (14 to 42 DAF) of B. napus line J9707 and Bnatts knockout mutants. The staining of dissected seed coats was done essentially as previously described (Hong et al., 2017). 

# Determination of total PAs

The total PAs was determined by vanillin-hydrochloric acid method. $4 ~ 9$ vanillin was dissolved in 96 g methanol to prepare $4 \%$ (w/w) vanillin-methanol solution. 1 mg of seed coat was dissolved in 2 mL distilled water, and $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of seed coat leaching solution was taken under the condition of light protection, and added into 2 mL of $4 \%$ vanillin–methanol solution, and then $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of concentrated hydrochloric acid was added to mix thoroughly, reaction at room temperature for 15 min, and then light absorption value was measured at $5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ . The above operations must be performed away from light. 

# Light microscopy

To observe the seed coat structure, developing seeds of Bnatt mutations and WT at 28 to 42 DAF were harvested for microscopic analysis. Tissue fixation, embedding, sectioning, and Safranin O and Fast Green staining were performed essentially as described by Li et al. (2012). Images were obtained using a Nikon ECLIPSE 80i compound microscope. 

# Determination of lignin content

Mature seeds and seed coat lignin was quantified followed the manufacturer’s instructions of the Lignin Content Assay Kit (Beijing Boxbio Science & Technology, AKSU010U), as described in Yao et al. (2023). 

# Field experiments and phenotyping

The ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ and its generation mutants were all grown in transgenic field of Huazhong Agriculture University, Wuhan, China (30.48 N, 114.28 E). The field experiment followed a randomized complete block design with three replications. Each replicates had three rows with 11–12 plants per row, with a distance of $2 1 \ \mathrm { c m }$ between plants within each row and $3 0 ~ \mathsf { c m }$ between rows. The field management was performed in line with standard breeding practice. 

The developing seeds were used for seed coat staining, seed coat sectioning, and RNA sampling. Mature plants were used for quality traits, including oil content, protein content and FA composition. The FA profiles were determined using gas chromatography (GC) system with a Model 6890 GC analyser (Agilent Technologies, Inc., Wilmington, DE) according to the method previously described by Yang et al. (2012). The protein and oil contents were examined using a Foss NIRSystems 5000 near-infrared reflectance spectroscopy (NIRS) using the parameters described by Gan et al. (2003), and the content was expressed as a percentage of the total seed dry weight. 

In total,15–30 plants growing uniformly in each plot were chosen for trait evaluation. The mean of each trait calculated from the 15–30 sampled plants in one plot was used to determine 

plot performance. The yield traits included yield per plant, pod number of main inflorescence, pod length, pod number per pod and 1000-grain weight. 

# Seed germination experiment

In total, 49 intact rapeseeds were put into a germination box $( 1 2 \times 1 2 \times 5 ~ \mathrm { c m } )$ containing two layers of germination paper ( $1 1 . 5 \times 1 1 . 5 ( \mathrm { m } )$ with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ${ \sf d H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { \sf \tau }$ , and the lid was covered and put into an climate chamber (temperature: $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ ; light $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light/8 h dark; humidity $70 \%$ ) The germination percentage was recorded once every $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The radicle protrusion throughout the seed coat was as germination. The freshly harvested seeds in 2023 and the seeds stored for 1 year in room temperature were simultaneously germinated. The germination was scored with three biological replicates. 

# Transmission electron microscope

The seed coat was removed, and the inner cotyledons were collected. The cotyledons were cut into $1 \times 1 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ pieces and fixed in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde solution overnight. The samples were prepared, sectioned and observed using a Huazhong Agricultural University Electron Microscope platform. A transmission electron microscope (TEM; H-7650; HITACHI, Tokyo, Japan) was used in the research. 

# Statistics of the number and size of oil bodies and protein bodies

The number and size of OBs and PBs in WT and mutants were counted and measured by Image J (Tang et al., 2022a). The number of proteomes in the entire visual field was counted in the 2000-fold magnification image. The number of oil bodies was counted in the whole field of view in the 3000 times magnification image; in the calculation of oil body area, large oil bodies and small oil bodies were distinguished. All the oil bodies in the image were measured for their diameters and the average diameters of the 30 largest oil bodies were used to calculated large OB size while the average diameter of at least 80 smallest oil bodies were used to calculated small OB size, according to the elliptic area. Each statistic was repeated 4–8 times.
