# Perspectives Article:

# Molecular Controls of Proanthocyanidin Synthesis and Structure: Prospects for Genetic Engineering in Crop Plants

C. Peter Constabel

Centre for Forest Biology and Department of Biology

University of Victoria

# Abstract

The proanthocyanidins (PAs) are widespread oligomeric and polymeric flavan-3-ols with significant benefits to human and animal health. As products of the general flavonoid pathway, the biosynthesis of the flavan-3-ols is well understood and the major enzyme-encoding genes that determine proanthocyanidin structure have been identified. However, the mechanism of PA polymerization remains unknown. The most important transcription factors regulating PA biosynthesis are the MYB factors, potent tools for enhancing PA biosynthesis in plants. In some species, simple overexpression of these transcription factors has led to spectacular successes in upregulating PA synthesis. However, targeted metabolic engineering of PA structure has not yet been achieved.

# Introduction

The proanthocyanidins (PAs), also called the condensed tannins, have gone from being considered obscure secondary plant metabolites to becoming fascinating subjects for research in plant and food science. They are present in many crop plants, in particular in whole foods such as fruits, grains, and nuts1. They can be astringent and act as feeding deterrents, but at moderate concentrations they are important flavour constituents of wine and beer. A diet rich in PAs is associated with a reduced risk for cardiovascular disease, neurodegenerative conditions and metabolic syndromes, and many so-called superfoods are high in PAs1,2 . In cattle and other ruminants, a moderate level of PAs in forage reduces rumen foaming and risk of bloat, improves nitrogen use efficiency, and reduces methane emissions. These effects result from the protein-binding and antibacterial activities of PAs, which slow protein breakdown in the rumen3. The importance of the PAs on human and animal health are a thus major driver of research in this field.

Our knowledge of the PAs has been dramatically advanced by improved methods of PA analysis, in particular those based on mass spectrometry. These methods facilitate efficient PA quantification and can also capture the structural variation of PAs in plants1. In parallel, unprecedented access to plant genomic data via next-generation DNA sequencing technology has facilitated rapid discovery of new regulatory and biosynthetic genes required for PA biosynthesis. Many new MYB transcription factor genes regulating the flavonoid and PA pathway have recently been described, establishing the basis for future manipulation of the PAs.

This review will provide a brief overview of the recent advances in the molecular biology of PA biosynthesis in plants. It will give describe the key enzymes and genes important for defining PA structure, as well as outline major regulatory genes that control overall flux into the pathway. Many of these genes have been studied in transgenic plants, and the potential of these genes for metabolic engineering of PAs will be discussed throughout. Finally, the outstanding challenges in the field of PA biosynthesis and metabolic engineering will be summarized.

# PROANTHOCYANIDIN STRUCTURE AND BIOSYNTHESIS

The proanthocyanidins (PAs) are oligomeric and polymeric flavan-3-ols that can range in size from 2 to 30 or more subunits (Figure 1). They occur in plants as a heterogeneous mixture of sizes; the mean polymer length is characteristic for a given species or tissue and is referred to as the mean degree of polymerization (mDP). The subunits are derived from 2, 3-cis-flavan-3-ols (i.e., (-)-epicatechin and (-)- epigallocatechin), as well as from 2, 3-trans-flavan-3-ols (i.e., $( + )$ -catechin and $( + )$ -gallocatechin), and are most commonly linked via 4ß->8 C-C bonds. Other linkages are also found, for example, the additional $2 1 3 { \mathrm { - } } { > } \mathrm { O } 7$ bond in the A-type PAs (Figure 1). The PAs from some species such as persimmon and grape are further decorated with gallic acid or other phenylpropanoids.

Proanthocyanidins are the most widespread and abundant of all secondary plant metabolites. They are typically found at high levels in woody plants, where they occur in most tissues including bark, leaves, and roots, as well as flowers and fruit4. Many herbaceous plants also accumulate PAs, but here they are more restricted in distribution and in many species found only in the seed coat. In plant foods, PAs commonly occur in whole grains, pulses, and fruit, as well as in plant products such as chocolate and spices1. Significant dietary sources of PAs in the North American diet are apple, blueberry, and nuts; they are also found at high levels in cranberry, blackberry, and in wild forest berries1. Astringent varieties of persimmon fruit have a very high PA content, with the fruit flesh containing more than $1 \%$ PA by weight3.

The PAs are products of the flavonoid pathway, which is the origin of a broad and diverse set of secondary plant metabolites (Figure 1). The major enzymes of the flavonoid and PA pathway were first characterized at the molecular level in genetic models such as Antirrhinum and Arabidopsis thaliana. Flower colour mutants helped to establish the basic flavonoid pathway, while Arabidopsis mutants lacking PAs in the seed coat (transparent testa, tt) were essential in defining PA-specific steps. An important discovery was the discovery that the banyuls gene encodes new PA enzyme, anthocyanidin

reductase (ANR), which provided a biosynthetic route to the 2,3-cis-flavan-3-ols from anthocyanidins4. Prior to this, only the biosynthetic route to the 2,3-trans-flavan-3-ols via leucoanthocyanidin reductase (LAR) was known. Together, LAR and ANR provide the cis and trans flavan-3-ol precursors needed for PA synthesis.

In living tissues, PAs accumulate exclusively in the vacuole. Translocation into the vacuole is believed to occur via specific transporters of the multi-drug detoxification and extrusion (MATE) factor family. Such MATE transporters have been characterized in A. thaliana and Medicago trunculata, and shown to preferentially transport epicatechin-3'-O-glucoside across membranes in yeast assay systems5. Flavan-3-ol precursors coupling is thus assumed to occur in the vacuole, but the mechanism is unknown. Oxidative polymerization by phenol oxidases has been proposed4, but nonenzymatic radical-based reactions are also possible6. The Arabidopsis PA mutant (tt-10) was found to be deficient in a laccase-type phenol oxidase, lending support to an enzymatic route; however, no corresponding enzyme activity has so far been characterized. Recently, Liu et al.7 discovered that the Medicago LAR enzyme has the ability to cleave S-cysteinyl-epicatechin, a proposed intermediate of the PA polymerization reaction, resulting in release of free catechin; loss of this enzyme leads to increased PA polymerization and reduced extractability of the polymer. Thus one function of LAR is proposed to be the regulation of PA polymer length7.

The naturally occurring range of mDP of PAs is broad and depends on the plant species1. Altering PA polymer length in food plants could be of great interest, since the absorption across the gut lining is limited to trimeric and dimeric PAs. It is interesting that PA mDP is highly variable, even among closely related plants: for example, Populus angustifolia accumulated primarily PA dimers, whereas $P _ { - }$ . fremontii PAs are polymers with a mDP of 22. These species provide an interesting contrast that could be exploited to obtain further insight into the genes that control PA polymerization.

# ENZYMES THAT DETERMINE PROANTHOCYANIDIN STRUCTURE

Flavonoid B-ring hydroxylation. In addition to polymer size, a key feature of PA structure is the extent of hydroxylation (Figure 1). Flavan-3-ols with a triply hydroxylated B-ring (i.e., gallocatechin and epigallocatechin) are commonly incorporated into PAs, and give rise to prodelphinidin-type (PD) subunits, whereas catechin and epicatechin give rise to procyanidin-type (PC) subunits. Most PAs contain both types and the PD: PC ratio is usually consistent within a given plant and tissue. It can vary widely between species, but in general PC-type PAs appear to be more common1. The extent of PA hydroxylation is important for its biological activity, such as insect herbivore resistance and modulation of nutrient cycling in soils8, as well as for taste perception. .For example, Laaksonen et al.9 found that higher prodelphinidin content of blackberry juices gives rise to greater astringency, whereas high procyanidin content produces more of a 'puckering' sensation in the mouth. Food plants with exceptionally high PD content include persimmon, grapeseed, and salal berry.

Flavonoid B-ring hydroxylation occurs early in the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway (Figure 1). The trihydroxylated B-ring is the result of flavonoid 3',5'-hydroxylase (F3'5'H), a cytochrome P450 enzyme which has been studied for its role in producing the blue and purple hues in flower anthocyanins. This enzyme also determines PA hydroxylation, since both pathways share the early biosynthetic steps. High PD content in grapeberry skin PAs is correlated to strong expression of F3'5'H, as well high expression of cytochrome b5, its required coenzyme. By contrast, in grape seed, the F3'5'H gene is not highly expressed, and grape seed PA contains no PD subunits10 . A correlation of PAs low in PD content and low $\mathrm { F } 3 7 \mathrm { H }$ expression was also observed in blueberry fruit11 .

Since F3'5'H and cytochrome b5 expression determine flavonoid B-ring hydroxylation, manipulating these genes provides a strategy for enhancing PD content of PAs. Transgenic overexpressing only the F3'5'H gene in poplar led to no detectable enhancement of the PD content, perhaps due to low cytochrome b5 expression (H. Tang and C. P. Constabel, unpublished). However, a strongly elevated PD content was unexpectedly observed in high-PA transgenic poplars, and this correlated with greatly enhanced expression of both F3'5'H and cytochrome ${ \mathsf { b } } { \mathsf { 5 } } ^ { 1 2 }$ . Therefore, it appears

overexpression of both these genes is needed to manipulate the extent of PA hydroxylation. In addition, substrate specificities of downstream enzymes such as DFR need to be taken into consideration, as these are known to discriminate between number of hydroxyls on substrates. Manipulation of both DFR and F3'5'H was successfully carried out in rose petals in order to generate delphinidins and blue hues3.

ii) Stereochemistry and coupling of flavan-3-ol subunits. As mentioned earlier, most PA are comprised of a mix of both epicatechin-type (2, 3-cis-flavan-3-ol) and catechin-type (2, 3-trans-flavan-3-ol) subunits1,2 . However, epicatechin is typically the prevalent extender subunit of the polymer, whereas terminal subunits are less constrained and both subunit types are commonly observed3. For example, blueberry fruit PA uses both terminal subunits in equal proportions, but the extenders are more than $80 \%$ epicatechin11 . How the ratio of subunits is regulated is unclear, but differences in expression and activities of ANR and LAR enzymes could impact availability of each respective type of subunit. Overexpression of LAR would thus in principle be the basis of engineering of new PA polymers in planta. However, this approach has not resulted in the predicted PA structure, suggesting further complexities of the flavonoid pathway. As mentioned above, LAR is able to regenerate epicatechin from cysteinyl-epicatechin, and ANS has been shown to additionally catalyse the conversion catechin to cyanidin (discussed in Liu et al.7). Therefore, further work is needed to understand how both types of flavan-3-ols are integrated into the larger PAs, and how this is controlled.

The structural diversity of PAs can be illustrated by the procyanidins, a series of dimeric flavan-3-ols classified by their subunit composition and linkages (see Figure 1 for examples). The common procyanidin B1 dimer $( + )$ -epicatechin $4 \beta { \cdot } > 8$ (-)-catechin) is composed of a catechin terminal and an epicatechin extender unit2. It is the most common procyanidin in blueberry11 . The B2 dimer is composed of two $4 \beta { \cdot } > 8$ linked epicatechin subunits, while procyanidin B3, the most abundant procyanidin in barley, is composed of two $4 \mathrm { { d } } \mathrm { { - } } \mathrm { { > } } ~ 8$ linked catechin subunits. Other important dimeric

PAs are the prodelphinidins, which contain at least one gallocatechin or epigallocatechin subunit. The strong bias of a given plant to make specific procyanidins or prodelphinidins would suggest that flavan-3-ol coupling and procyanidin polymerization is enzyme-mediated. Metabolic engineering of PAs with precision will therefore first require characterization of these enzymes.

A number of plants produce A-type PAs, in which the flavan-3-ol subunits are linked by a second bond (Figure 1). The mechanism for formation of this C-O bond has not been described, although Porter6 again discussed possible non-enzymatic mechanisms. If the enzyme responsible for Atype PAs could be identified, it would be of great interest for metabolic engineering. A-linked PAs interfere with bacterial adhesion, a process important for bacterial invasion; the occurrence of high concentrations of A-type PAs in cranberry juice is thus believed to underlie its efficacy in fighting urinary tract infections1.

iii) Decoration of PAs with gallate and other acyl groups. Flavan-3-ols and PA polymers can be esterified with gallate1,2 , and other acyl groups such as $p$ -coumarate may also be added. Galloylation has been correlated with increased astringency and potentially greater health benefits2: this may explain why grapeseed and green tea PAs are believed to be particularly beneficial for human health. The enzyme responsible for galloyl addition has been identified in persimmon as epicatechin:1-O-galloyl-D-glucose $O$ -galloyltransferase, and belongs to the family of serine carboxypeptidase-like (SCPL) acyl transferases13 . The corresponding gene has been cloned, and would provide a direct pathway for metabolic engineering of galloylated PAs. The enzyme responsible for synthesizing galloyl glucose, which is needed as a co-substrate and galloyl donor, would likely also need to be transgenically expressed.

TRANSCRIPTIONAL REGULATORS OF THE PROANTHOCYANIDIN PATHWAY: POTENT GENES FOR METABOLIC ENGINEERING

Biosynthesis of PAs is regulated primarily at the gene expression level, i.e. by regulating transcription of genes encoding PA biosynthetic enzymes14, 15 . These genes are turned on and off by specific transcription factors, which can simultaneously control several genes within the pathway. They thus act as 'master switches' for the pathway, and are desirable targets for metabolic engineering. Among major groups of plant transcription factors, the MYB factor proteins are most important for the flavonoid pathway2. MYB proteins in plants are encoded by large gene families, with more than 100 members in most plant genomes. By far the greatest number of MYBs belong to the R2R3-MYB subgroup, although R3 MYBs can also play important roles16 . The first MYB PA regulator to be identified was Arabidopsis TT2 (MYB123). This gene led to the identification of other MYB PA regulators from crop species16 . In addition to Arabidopsis MYB123, PA-specific MYBs from Medicago trunculata, grapevine and poplar have been extensively investigated3, 12, 17 .

Transcriptional activation requires MYB factor binding to specific DNA regulatory elements in the promoter regions of target genes. The PA- and anthocyanin-specific MYBs, unlike many other MYBs, additionally need to partner with bHLH and WD-40 proteins, forming the so-called 'MBW' complex to promote transcription15, 18 . Both the MYB and the bHLH protein bind specific nucleotide sequences within the flavonoid promoter. The two most prevalent MYB binding sites for PA and flavonoid MYBs are the MYBCORE element and the AC-element. There are many variants, however, and for most PA genes the precise MYB binding sites have not been demonstrated. The MYB and bHLH proteins also interact with each other, with the WD40 protein stabilizing their interaction. Of the proteins in the MBW complex, the MYB factor ultimately determines the specificity of binding and the genes to be activated, since the bHLH and WD40 proteins can also participate in the regulation of other pathways. Thus the Arabidopsis MYB123 protein interacts with a specific bHLH (TT8) and WD40 protein (TTG1) to determine PA synthesis in the seed coat. In cells that produce anthocyanins, however, the same TTG1 and WD40 partner with MYB75 (PAP1) to activate the anthocyanin pathway14 .

Despite the requirement for co-factors, transgenic overexpression of the MYB alone is often sufficient to upregulate PA synthesis. This is assumed to be due to constitutive expression of the bHLH and WD-40 cofactor genes. In poplar and grape, it could be shown that MBW complex also upregulates the bHLH co-factor gene required for the complex, leading to a positive feedback loop. This likely causes the dramatic induction of PAs12, 17 observed, making these MYBs potent tools for metabolic engineering.

i) MYB activators of proanthocyanidins in fruit and vegetative tissues. The strong interest in food and wine PAs has stimulated many studies of PA regulation in fruit such as grape, apple, and persimmon3, 16 (Table 1). In grapevine, the major MYB PA regulators are VvMYBPA1 and VvMYBPA2, active in grape seed and skin, respectively17, 19 . Overexpression of either gene in grapevine hairy roots led to strongly enhanced PA levels17 . VvMYBPA2 is the ortholog of Arabidopsis MYB123, while VvMYBPA1 defines a new subgroup of PA-MYBs. This second type of PA-MYB is also present in other species, such as cacao and poplar12 . In apple, only PA MYB regulators of the MYBPA2-type have been identified20, 21 . By contrast, in persimmon, the major fruit PA regulator DkMYB4 belongs to the MYBPA1 group. Suppressing the DkMYB4 gene in transgenic persimmon callus resulted in reduced PA synthesis, but its overexpression led to an induction of PAs. These ectopic PAs had toxic effects, however, as the calli stopped growing and turned black22 . The same effect was observed in transgenic kiwifruit callus, where overexpression of DkMYB4 resulted in PA synthesis but also prevented shoot regeneration22 . However, the latter experiment illustrated that the persimmon PA MYB functions in a heterologous species with the endogenous bHLH and WD40 cofactors. Work with the apple PA regulator MdMYB9 demonstrated that it functions in Arabidopsis cells and can interact with poplar $\mathrm { b H L H } ^ { 2 0 }$ ; this cross-species flexibility of MYBs for co-factors greatly facilitates metabolic engineering.

The same type of MYB factors and MBW complexes found in seeds and fruit are responsible for PA synthesis in vegetative tissues. In forage legumes, the search for PA MYBs active in leaves led to the identification of TaMYB14, a TT2-like MYB which regulates PAs in black clover (Trifolium arvense)23 . When overexpressed, it stimulates PA accumulation in transgenic alfalfa (Medicago sativa) to $1 \%$ DW, and in white clover (T. repens) to as high as $1 . 8 \%$ DW. Neither of these plants normally synthesize significant amounts of PAs in leaves. Additional PA-acting MYB genes from forage legumes include MtMYB14, MtMYB5 and MtPAR from M. trunculata; these genes contribute to regulation of seed PAs, and can induce substantial levels of PAs when ectopically expressed in M. sativa roots. However, they are much less active in leaves24, 25 . At present, TaMYB14 is a top candidate for metabolic engineering of legumes for higher PA content. Alfalfa is the most important forage crop, and engineering sufficient PAs for optimal ruminant health would be a significant advance.

In forest trees, leaves and other vegetative tissues can contain substantial quantities of PA, and the underlying molecular biology has been extensively studied in aspen and poplar trees (Populus spp). In P. tremuloides, both MYBPA1- and MYBPA2-type regulators (MYB115 and MYB134, respectively) are broadly expressed and control PA accumulation in leaves. They appear to have overlapping functions; interestingly, they were found to be able to regulate each other's promoters12 . This mutual regulation leads to a potential feed-forward loop, and may explain why overexpression of either MYB134 or MYB115 leads to a very strong upregulation of the PA pathway: in greenhousegrown MYB134- and MYB115-overexpressing transgenic poplars, the PA content is 40- to 50-fold greater than controls12 . These poplar MYBs could be useful genes for the engineering of PA levels in other species.

ii) Additional genes that contribute to PA regulation. Since the bHLH and WD40 co-factors of the MBW complex are less specific for PAs than the MYB factors, they have received proportionally less attention. In grapevine, the bHLH partner of the PA-MBW complex, VvMYC1, showed it to be

functionally similar to the Arabidopsis bHLH, TT826 . VvMYC1 can interact with either the PA-specific VvMYBPA1 or the anthocyanin regulator VvMYBA1 to form a functional complex for either pathway. Likewise, in M. trunculata and poplar, the endogenous TT8-like bHLHs interact with both the PAspecific and anthocyanin-specific MYBs20, 27 . Transgenic overexpression of flavonoid bHLH factor genes alone can demonstrate their function in regulating the PA pathway, but only generate modest increases in PAs26, 27 . In combination with MYB gene overexpression, however, the bHLH cofactors are useful tools for metabolic engineering (see below).

Repressor-like MYBs were originally discovered in studies relating to general phenylpropanoid metabolism and anthocyanin synthesis. The involvement of R2R3 MYB repressors in PA metabolism was revealed by the identification of VvMYBC2-L1 in grape, which, when overexpressed in hairy root cultures, downregulated PAs and other phenylpropanoids28 . A similar repressor from alfalfa, MYB2, negatively regulates seed PA accumulation, and was suggested to restrict the spatial distribution of PAs in the seed coat. In poplar, the MYB182 repressor also acts on the PA pathway in leaves, and may serve to slow down PA accumulation during stress-induced PA synthesis29 . Overexpression of MYB182 in transgenic poplar led to a reduction of both PAs and anthocyanins; this broader target range compared to the MYB activators appears to be typical for the MYB repressors. Three additional poplar repressor MYBs with inhibitory activity on the PA genes and phenylpropanoid pathway have now been characterized29 , suggesting that negative regulators could play a more important role in fine control of the PA pathway that previously thought. Mechanistically, MYB repressor proteins act by binding promoter regions as well as the bHLH co-factor of the MBW complex. Their potential for metabolic engineering has not yet been extensively explored, but early work with the FaMYB1 strawberry anthocyanin repressor showed it could also be used to reduce PA contents in Lotus corniculatus. The recently characterized MYB repressors may thus provide additional tools for genetic engineering. In principle, it should be possible to downregulate MYB repressors to enhance flux into

PAs. Alternatively, MYB repressors may find application in downregulating undesirable pathways that compete with PA synthesis.

# PROSPECTS AND CHALLENGES FOR METABOLIC ENGINEERING OF PROANTHOCYANIDINS

The identification of the regulatory and enzyme-encoding genes that determine PA content and structure provide opportunities for engineering of PAs in crops. As outlined above, transgenic manipulation of genes are common approaches to testing gene function. These have provided the proof of concept for PA enhancement, and further suggest more targeted modifications. The ability of the black clover TaMYB14 gene to alter PA levels in other forage crops has been demonstrated23 and is poised for application in the field. PA-enhanced alfalfa would provide clear benefits for both animal and environmental health. Biofortification of food plants for human consumption can also be envisaged. Apple can be readily transformed, and the overexpression of PA MYBs with an appropriate promoter could boost overall PA content in apple. This could have positive benefits for health, and also impact juice and cider properties. The biofortification of tomato with anthocyanins has been successfully demonstrated30 ; the engineering of PAs should therefore be possible using similar strategies. However, more research in various biological systems will be needed in order to produce optimal PA contents in the target tissues.

To obtain sufficient levels of PA, the coexpression of several regulatory genes is a useful strategy. Extremely high anthocyanin content in tomato flesh and peel was achieved using simultaneous MYB and bHLH overexpression30 . Likewise, a combination of three MYB and bHLH transcription factors stimulated PA synthesis in Arabidopsis leaves, which normally accumulate do not accumulate PAs. In this case there was some toxicity, which led to early plant death3. Combining MYB repressors with the activators might also be used to improve precision of metabolic engineering. Tissue-specific promoters can direct expression to desired cell types and minimize toxicity. The general

cross-species functionality of MYBs and bHLHs allows for the use of previously characterized genes from other plants, with the caveat that effects will need to be tested for each target species.

The directed modification of PA structure has not yet been reported, but is becoming feasible using some of the enzymes and genes outlined above. Shifts towards greater PA hydroxylation have been observed as unintentional consequences of other genetic manipulations12, which demonstrate a general tolerance for alterations in PA structure within the plant. In food plants, enhancing hydroxylation via expression of F3'5'H, or to generate galloylated PAs with a galloyl transferase, would facilitate modulation of taste, astringency, and improve the health-promoting activity of PAs. It would also allow for direct testing of hydroxylation pattern on susceptibility of foods to oxidation, stability in wines, or haze formation in beers. A significant modification of PAs structure would be to reduce mDP, since only smaller PAs can be absorbed by the gut. Engineering this trait will have to await elucidation of the mechanism of polymerization, however.

Several challenges still limit our ability to modify PAs in crops. For many plant species or varieties, genetic transformation is not yet possible. Typically, regeneration of shoots from tissue culture or single cells is the limiting factor: even though cells have taken up exogenous DNA, transgenic plants cannot be recovered. Many soft fruit such as blueberries have not yet been genetically transformed, but this should be possible if a concerted effort is undertaken. Another limitation to enhancement of PAs in non-PA producing plants is a potential lack of phenylpropanoid precursors. Successful enhancement has been most easily achieved in tissues that already have a low-level capacity to make PAs. For example, the constitutive overexpression of poplar MYB134 had a strong impact in boosting PA accumulation in transgenic poplar, but had no effect in tobacco. A final challenge for the practical application of PA modification is the interconnectivity of phenolic metabolism in plants. Work in poplar has shown how strong enhancement of the PA pathway leads to an unexpected reduction in other phenolics. This inadvertently increased the palatability of these transgenics to insect

323 pests. Regardless of the strategy for metabolic engineering of PAs, impacts on other phenolics need to 324 be monitored carefully.
