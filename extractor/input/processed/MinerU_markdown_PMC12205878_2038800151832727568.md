# Summary

Rice (Oryza sativa L.) is one of the most important food crops. Starch is the main substance of rice endosperm and largely determines the grain quality and yield. Starch biosynthesis in endosperm is very complex, requiring a series of enzymes which are also regulated by many transcription factors (TFs). But until now, the large-scale regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis has not been established. Here, we constructed a rice endosperm starch biosynthesis regulatory network comprised of 277 TFs and 15 starch synthesis enzyme-encoding genes (SSEGs) using DNA affinity chromatography/pull-down combined with liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (DNA pull-down and LC–MS). In this regulatory network, each SSEG is directly regulated by 7–46 TFs. Based on this network, we found a new pathway ‘ABA-OsABI5-OsERF44-SSEGs’ that regulates rice endosperm starch biosynthesis. We also knocked out five TFs targeting the key amylose synthesis enzyme gene OsGBSSI in japonica rice ‘Nipponbare’ background and found that all mutants had moderately decreased amylose content (AC) in endosperm and improved eating and cooking quality (ECQ). Notably, the knockout of OsSPL7 and OsB3 improves the ECQ without compromising the rice appearance quality, which was further validated in the indica rice ‘Zhongjiazao17’ background. In summary, this gene regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis established here will provide important theoretical and practical guidance for the genetic improvement of rice quality. 

# Introduction

With the improvement of living standards, people have higher requirements for rice quality. Starch accounts for more than $80 \%$ of the rice grain weight (Zeeman et al., 2010), which is composed of linear amylose and highly branched amylopectin, and their composition and fine structure largely determine the diversity of the appearance, eating and cooking quality (ECQ) of rice (Huang et al., 2021a; Okpala et al., 2022). 

Starch biosynthesis in endosperm is a complex system that requires the precise collaboration of a series of key enzymes (Pfister and Zeeman, 2016), and among them, ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGP) is responsible for the synthesis of ADPglucose, which is the major substrate for starch synthesis (Figueroa et al., 2022). Granule-bound starch synthase I (GBSSI) is responsible for the synthesis of amylose and the extralong-chain fraction of amylopectin (Wang et al., 2015a). Amylopectin synthesis requires the precise cooperation of soluble starch synthases (SSS or SS), starch branching enzymes (SBE), and starch debranching enzymes (DBE). In this process, SSs are responsible for amylopectin chain elongation, SBEs introduce new branches, and DBEs remove improper branches (Figure 1a; Crofts et al., 2015). Each type of starch synthesis-related enzyme contains multiple homologous isoenzymes. For instance, SSs 

include SSI, SSII, SSIII, and SSIV; SBEs consist of SBEI and SBEII; and DBEs comprise Isoamylase1 (ISA1), ISA2, ISA3, and Pullulanase (PUL) (Ball and Morell, 2003; Nakamura, 2002; Wang et al., 2014). These starch synthesis enzyme-encoding genes (SSEGs) can be grouped into two types based on their tissuespecific expression: type I (expressed in sink tissues, grains) and type II (expressed in source tissues, vegetative tissue). Type I includes 15 SSEGs: OsAGPL1, OsAGPL2, OsAGPS1, OsAGPS2, OsGBSSI, OsBEI, OsBEIIb, OsISA1, OsISA2, OsISA3, OsPHOL, OsPUL, OsSSI, OsSSIIa, and OsSSIIIa. These genes are preferentially expressed in the developing endosperm (Fu and Xue, 2010; Table S1; Figure S1) and are crucial for endosperm starch biosynthesis and rice quality formation. Loss-of-function mutations in these SSEGs will lead to abnormal endosperm development and significant changes in the appearance and cooking quality (ECQ) of rice (Kubo et al., 1999; Lee et al., 2007; Tanaka et al., 2004; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2011). 

Meanwhile, the expression levels of these SSEGs are finely regulated by a series of transcription factors (TFs). Mutation in these TFs can also lead to abnormal starch synthesis in the endosperm, resulting in a decline in the appearance and ECQ of rice. NF-YB1 directly binds to the ‘G-box’ domain of OsGBSSI promoter and forms a heterotrimer complex with NF-YC12 and bHLH144, thereby activating the expression of OsGBSSI and regulating amylose biosynthesis and grain quality (Bello et al., 2019). OsbZIP58 directly binds to the promoters of six SSEGs (OsAGPL3, OsGBSSI, OsSSIIa, SBE1, OsBEIIb, and ISA2), and the osbzip58 mutant displayed a white belly and decreased total starch and ACs (Wang et al., 2013). However, to date, a large-scale regulatory network for starch biosynthesis in rice endosperm has not been established, which makes it difficult to accurately improve rice quality by manipulating the content and fine structure of starch components. 

Traditional methods to identify TFs that regulate starch biosynthesis usually include positional cloning based on mutant screening, reverse genetics, and yeast one hybridization (Y1H) screening. However, these methods are inefficient and difficult to fully understand the complex regulatory network of starch biosynthesis within a short timeframe. In contrast, DNA affinity chromatography/pull-down combined with liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (DNA pull-down and LC–MS) is an efficient technique for pinpointing the candidate TFs that bind to gene promoters. This method has been effectively employed in wheat (Xie et al., 2023), mulberry (Liu et al., 2019) and bacteria (Chaparian et al., 2016; Jutras et al., 2012). In this study, we specifically utilized DNA pull-down and LC–MS to identify TFs that bind to the promoters of rice type I SSEGs. We successfully constructed a rice endosperm starch biosynthesis regulatory network, which comprises 277 TFs and 15 SSEGs. Based on this network, we preliminarily identified a new pathway ‘ABA-OsABI5-OsERF44-SSEGs’ that regulates rice endosperm starch biosynthesis. Moreover, by knocking out some TFs, we refined the endosperm AC, thereby rapidly enhancing rice quality. In summary, this large-scale gene regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis will provide significant theoretical and practical guidance for the genetic improvement of rice quality. 

# Results

# Construction of a large-scale gene regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis

We employed a high-throughput DNA pull-down and LC–MS method to identify the TFs that bind to the promoters of 15 rice type I SSEGs preferentially expressed in the developing endosperm (Fu and Xue, 2010; Table S1; Figure S1). Briefly, the promoter sequences of the SSEGs were attached to beads and incubated with nuclear protein extracts from rice-developing endosperm. Non-specifically bound DNA-binding proteins were removed using a buffer, and the promoter-binding proteins were subsequently eluted and visualized via silver-stained polyacrylamide gel. Finally, the proteins were identified by LC–MS (Figure 1b,c). For each SSEG, 5–7 overlapping DNA probes (~300–400 bp in length) covered the approximately 2.0 kb promoter region (Figure 1b; Table S2). Through this DNA 

pull-down and LC–MS analysis, more than 2100 DNA-binding proteins were identified (Dataset S1), which can be classified into various groups, such as TFs, kinases, metabolic enzymes, ribosomal proteins, and others, based on the UniProt database (http://www.uniprot.org/) and the Rice Genome Annotation Project (RGAP, Os-Nipponbare-reference-IRGSP-1.0) (Kawahara et al., 2013). Among these DNA-binding proteins, a total of 277 typical TFs were identified (Dataset S2). 

These 277 TFs are distributed across 12 rice chromosomes (Figure S2) and represent over 40 TF families, with the MYB, bZIP, bHLH, ERF, WRKY, GRAS, FAR, NAC, and MADS families having the most members (Figure 2). Our analysis captured numerous known TF-DNA interactions, including the binding of OsbZIP58, OsbZIP60, RPBF (OsDof3), RSR1, SERF1, OsDOF18, and OsNAC24 to their respective SSEGs (Figure 1d; Dataset S2). These TFs have been reported to play crucial roles in starch synthesis in rice endosperm (Cao et al., 2022; Fu and Xue, 2010; Jin et al., 2023; Kawakatsu et al., 2009; Schmidt et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2013; Zhu et al., 2024). To further validate these interactions, we conducted a Y1H assay, confirming 20 out of 24 TF-DNA interactions with an accuracy of approximately $8 3 \%$ (Figure S3). Consequently, we constructed a large-scale regulatory network for starch biosynthesis in rice endosperm, comprising 277 TFs and 15 SSEGs (Figure 1d). Within this regulatory network, each SSEG is directly regulated by 7–46 TFs; for instance, OsISA2, OsAGPL2, OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsBEI, and $O S S S I$ are bound by as many as 46, 45, 39, 38, 37, and 30 TFs, respectively, while OsISA1, OsAGPL1, and OsAGPS2 are bound by only 7, 9, and 10 TFs, respectively (Figures 1d and 2b). Among the identified TFs, 217 only directly regulate a single SSEG, whereas the remaining 60 act as nodal regulators, simultaneously regulating 2–6 SSEGs (Figure 1d; Dataset S2). These findings indicate that starch biosynthesis in the rice endosperm is a very complex process and is finely tuned by multiple TFs. 

# A node TF OsERF44 binding to five SSEGs was identified based on the regulatory network

In the aforementioned regulatory network, these node TFs are likely to play crucial roles in rice endosperm starch biosynthesis. For example, OsbZIP58 and OsbZIP60 have been demonstrated to activate the expression of several SSEGs, and in their mutants, endosperm starch synthesis is seriously insufficient, resulting in a significant decline in grain appearance, ECQ, and grain weight (Cao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2013). Here, we found that a novel AP2/ERF family TF, OsERF44 (encoding by LOC_Os08g45110) as another node TF can bind to the promoters of five SSEGs (OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI ) (Figures 1d and 3a). The involvement of OsERF44 in endosperm starch biosynthesis and rice quality has not been previously reported. 

To confirm that OsERF44 can directly bind to the promoters of these five SSEGs, we first conducted a Y1H assay. The promoters of five SSEGs were individually fused into the ‘bait’ vector pLacZi, and the coding sequence of OsERF44 was inserted into the ‘prey’ vectors pB42AD. Compared to the empty pLacZi vector, the co-transformation of the five Promoter-pLacZi constructs with OsERF44-pB42AD induced high expression levels of $\beta \mathrm { \cdot }$ -galactosidase activity in yeast (Figure 3b). Moreover, a dual-luciferase (LUC) reporter assay in rice protoplasts also showed that OsERF44 significantly enhanced the signal strength of the LUC reporter driven by these five SSEGs’ promoters (Figure 3c). # ‘ABA-OsABI5-OsERF44-SSEGs’ pathway regulating the rice endosperm starch biosynthesis

To elucidate the detailed regulatory pathway of OsERF44, we analysed its promoter and found that there are several ABRE-box motifs in its promoter (Figure S6b). It has been reported that ABRE-box is the binding site of TF OsABI5 (Zou et al., 2008). We, therefore, investigated the genetic relationship between OsABI5 and OsERF44. Firstly, the Y1H and EMSA showed that OsABI5 can bind to the promoter of OsERF44 via the ABRE-box motif (Figures 3e,g and S4c). Secondly, a dual-LUC reporter assay showed that OsABI5 can activate OsERF44 expression, and this activation is enhanced by the presence of ABA (Figure 3f), because OsABI5 is a key TF in ABA signalling transduction. Furthermore, a dual-LUC reporter assay showed that OsABI5 significantly enhanced the transactivation of OsGBSSI and OsSSI when OsERF44 was present, with the highest activity observed in the presence of both ABA and OsABI5 together with OsERF44 (Figure 3h,i). Interestingly, protoplasts from the oserf44-1 mutant showed that OsABI5 could still activate OsGBSSI transcription in the presence of ABA, indicating that OsERF44 is not absolutely required for this process (Figure S5a). Meanwhile, the Y1H assay showed that ABI5 could not directly bind to the promoter of OsGBSSI (Figure S5b). Collectively, these findings suggest an ABA signalling pathway where OsABI5 activates the expression of OsERF44, and OsERF44 subsequently positively regulates the expression of five SSEGs, although ABA-ABI5 signalling can also regulate SSEGs expression via other TFs. 

To further investigate the biological functions of OsERF44 and OsABI5 in rice endosperm starch biosynthesis, we obtained oserf44 and osabi5 mutants in the Nipponbare (NIP) genetic background (Figure S6a–c). qRT-PCR and Western blot analyses revealed that the expression levels and protein abundances of OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI in mutants were significantly lower than those in the wild type (Figure 4a–c). The expression of OsERF44 was also significantly lower in osabi5 mutants (Figure 4b). Compared with the wild type, the contents of total starch, amylose, and amylopectin were significantly decreased, and the starch granules were irregularly rounded and loosely packed in the endosperm of oserf44 and osabi5 mutants, resulting in a poor grain appearance quality (chalkiness increased significantly) and significant reductions in grain thickness and grain weight (Figures 4d–i and S6d–j). The alkali spreading values (ASVs) of oserf44 and osabi5 endosperm were significantly higher than those of the wild type (Figure 4j), while the total protein content and gel consistency did not differ significantly (Figure S6k,l). In brief, based on the aforementioned regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis, we preliminarily identified an important pathway, ‘ABA-OsABI5-OsERF44-SSEGs’, that regulates endosperm starch biosynthesis, rice quality, and grain yield (Figure 4k). 

# Knockout TFs targeting the Waxy gene can qualitatively create new soft rice germplasm with improved ECQ and appearance under medium amylose content background

AC is a key determinant of rice ECQ. Endosperm amylose is synthesized by GBSSI, which is encoded by the Waxy (Wx, OsGBSSI ) gene (Huang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). Soft rice with low AC $( 8 \% - 1 3 \% )$ is highly favoured in domestic markets due to its desirable taste (Wang et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022a). However, traditional soft rice varieties often exhibit poor appearance quality, characterized by irregular chalkiness and reduced transparency (Xu et al., 2021). Our constructed regulatory network for rice endosperm starch biosynthesis identified 39 potential TFs that bind to the OsGBSSI promoter and regulate its expression (Figure 1d; Dataset S2). If some TFs that activate OsGBSSI expression are knocked out, can the expression level of OsGBSSI be reduced, resulting in the reduction of AC in the endosperm, thereby creating new soft rice germplasm with better ECQ? To confirm this possibility, we used the CRISPR/cas9 gene editing system to knock out five randomly selected OsGBSSI-targeting TFs (OsMADS91, OsSPL7, OsC2H2, OsMYB1, and OsB3) in a japonica rice ‘NIP’ background $( \sim 1 6 . 4 \%$ AC with $W X ^ { b }$ allele) (Figures 5a and S8a–e). These TFs exhibited differential expression patterns across various rice tissues, with OsMADS91, OsSPL7, OsC2H2, and OsB3 being highly expressed in the developing endosperm (Figure S7). There are no previous reports indicating that these five TFs directly bind to the promoters of OsGBSSI. 

Initially, we found that the expression levels and protein of OsGBSSI in all mutants were significantly lower than those in wild type NIP (Figure 5b). We then measured the main ECQ properties of the mutants, wild type, and a well-known soft rice variety, Nangeng46 (NG46). The AC in the mature endosperm of mutants decreased significantly from $1 6 . 4 0 \%$ in NIP to moderate levels of $9 . 8 0 \% - 1 4 . 7 4 \%$ , while the AC of NG46 was $8 . 7 0 \%$ (Figure 5c). The ASV was significantly higher in most mutants, indicating easier gelatinization compared to the wild type (Figure 5d). The hardness and chewiness of the cooked rice of mutants of 5 TFs were significantly decreased, while adhesiveness was significantly increased compared to the wild type, which suggested that the mutants of the 5 TFs have better ECQ, and some indicators even surpassed those of NG46 (Figures 5e,f and S8f–i). Furthermore, the chalky grain rate and chalkiness degree were significantly higher in mutants osmads91, osc2h2, and osmyb1 compared to the wild type, while osspl7 and osb3 mutants showed no significant differences (Figures 5a,g and S8c,j). In addition, the plant morphology between mutants and the wild type was largely indistinguishable, except for a slight decrease in 1000-grain weight (Figure S8b,d,e,k). These results suggested that osspl7 and osb3 mutants are promising soft rice germplasms. Therefore, knocking out TFs targeting OsGBSSI (e.g., OsSPL7 and OsB3) can effectively down-regulate AC and improve ECQ, offering a rapid method to develop new soft rice germplasm with desirable ECQ and appearance quality under medium AC backgrounds with $W x ^ { b }$ allele.

# Discussion

Starch biosynthesis in rice endosperm is a highly complex biological process that has been extensively studied, especially as the enzymes involved in this pathway have been largely identified (Jin et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Thitisaksakul et al., 2012). The expression levels of SSEGs are finely regulated by a series of TFs. However, the transcriptional regulation of SSEGs remains largely unexplored, and large-scale identification of TFs that regulate rice endosperm starch biosynthesis is still lacking. Traditional methods are inefficient and difficult to fully understand the intricate regulatory network of starch biosynthesis in a short period. In contrast, DNA pull-down and LC–MS is a highly efficient tool to capture TFs that bind to promoters of target genes. In the regulatory network described above, several node TFs appear to play crucial roles in rice endosperm starch biosynthesis. For example, OsbZIP58 and OsbZIP60 have been proven to activate the expression of several SSEGs and are important for regulating starch biosynthesis and rice quality (Cao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2013). In this study, we identified a novel AP2/ERF family TF, OsERF44, which binds to the promoters of five SSEGs (OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI ). The role of OsERF44 in endosperm starch biosynthesis and rice quality has not been previously reported. Therefore, we hypothesised that OsERF44 may play a key role as a regulator of rice endosperm starch biosynthesis and quality formation. Our results from Y1H, 

EMSA, and dual-LUC reporter assays demonstrated that OsERF44 can bind to and activate the expression of OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI (Figure 3a–d). Furthermore, we found that OsABI5, as a key TF in ABA signalling, can bind to the OsERF44 promoter via the ABRE-box motifs and activate its expression (Figure 3e–g). A dual-LUC reporter assay showed that ABA and/or OsABI5 can enhance the transactivation activities of OsGBSSI and OsSSI mediated by OsERF44 (Figure 3h,i). In mutants of oserf44 and osabi5, the expression levels and protein abundances of OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI were significantly lower than those in the wild type (Figure 4a–c). The expression levels of OsERF44 were also significantly reduced in osabi5 mutants (Figure 4b). Loss-of-function of OsERF44 or OsABI5 led to insufficient endosperm starch synthesis, reduced grain weight, and increased chalky grain rate. Notably, oserf44 exhibited more severe defects in grain weight and rice quality, such as lower thickness and grain weight, as well as higher chalkiness compared to osaibi5 (Figures 4d–f and S6i,j). These observations suggested that OsERF44 acts downstream of OsABI5 in the regulation of rice endosperm starch biosynthesis. Thus, our findings indicate an ABA signalling pathway in which OsABI5 activates OsERF44 expression, which subsequently 

regulates the expression of OsGBSSI, OsISA3, OsISA2, OsBEIIb, and OsSSI, thereby controlling starch synthesis in the rice endosperm (Figure 4k). Interestingly, we also found that in the absence of OsERF44, OsABI5 can still activate OsGBSSI transcription in the presence of ABA (Figure S5a). Additionally, ABI5 cannot directly bind to the OsGBSSI promoter (Figure S5b), suggesting that ABA-OsABI5 signalling can regulate SSEGs expression through other TFs, such as OsbZIP58. We confirmed that OsABI5 can bind to and activate the transcription of OsbZIP58, which has been shown to directly activate the expression of several SSEGs, including OsGBSSI (Figure S5c–e; Wang et al., 2013). In conclusion, these results further elucidate the complexity of the molecular mechanisms of ABA regulation of rice quality and yield. 

The large-scale regulatory network we constructed not only enhances our understanding of the molecular mechanism underlying starch biosynthesis in rice endosperm but also offers practical applications for improving rice quality. We all know that starch is the major component of rice, and its composition and physicochemical properties determine rice ECQ quality (Huang et al., 2021a). AC is the major factor that determines ECQ quality (Wang et al., 2024). Endosperm amylose is synthesized by GBSSI, which is encoded by the Waxy (Wx, OsGBSSI ) gene (Huang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). Variations in Waxy have led to regional differences in rice AC and influenced consumer preferences (Huang et al., 2020, 2021b; Zhang et al., 2021). For instance, glutinous rice, which has an AC of less than $5 \%$ , results from a loss-of-function mutation in the Waxy gene. Editing the Waxy coding region via CRISPR/Cas9 can easily lead to its loss of function to produce glutinous rice (Ma et al., 2015). Soft rice with low AC $( 8 \% - 1 3 \% )$ is highly favoured in the domestic market for its good taste (Wang et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022a). Recently, studies have shown that editing the Waxy promoter can fine-tune AC and rice quality (Huang et al., 2020). In our regulatory network, we identified 39 potential TFs that bind to the OsGBSSI promoter and regulate its expression (Figure 1d); we hypothesised that knocking out these TFs could moderately reduce AC and quickly obtain soft rice germplasm with improved quality. 

We used the CRISPR/cas9 system to knock out five randomly selected OsGBSSI-targeting TFs in a japonica rice ‘NIP’ background $( \sim 1 6 . 4 \%$ AC with $W x ^ { b }$ allele). The AC in the endosperm of these mutants decreased from $1 6 . 4 0 \%$ in NIP to $9 . 8 0 \% -$ $1 4 . 7 4 \%$ , indicating that gene editing of these TFs can slightly reduce rice AC. The mutants exhibited improved ECQ, with some indicators even surpassing those of the well-known soft rice variety NG46. Notably, the mutants osspl7 and osb3 showed no significant differences in chalky grain rate and chalkiness degree compared to the wild type, suggesting they are promising candidates for soft rice development (Figure 5). We further validated this approach in the high AC indica rice ‘YK17’ $( \sim 2 8 \%$ AC with $W x ^ { a }$ allele) by knocking out OsSPL7 and OsB3. The mutants exhibited significantly reduced AC, chalky grain rate, and chalkiness degree, with partial improvement in ECQ (Figure 6). These results demonstrate that knocking out TFs targeting OsGBSSI, such as OsSPL7 and OsB3, can slightly down-regulate AC and improve ECQ in both japonica and indica rice backgrounds, and new soft rice germplasm with good ECQ and appearance quality can be quickly created under medium AC varieties with $W x ^ { b }$ allele by this method. 

In conclusion, we have established a preliminary large-scale starch biosynthesis regulatory network in rice endosperm. This 

network provides important theoretical and practical guidance for the genetic improvement of rice quality. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

NIP (Oryza sativa, ssp. japonica), YK17 (Oryza sativa, ssp. indica) and all the transgenic plants used in this study were grown in the experimental field of China National Rice Research Institute. The thousand-grain weight, seed length, width, thickness, and chalkiness of wild type and mutant lines were examined by a seed phenotyping system (Wansheng, Hangzhou, China). 

# Measurement of grain physicochemical properties

The total starch and AC were measured using a starch assay kit (Megazyme, Ireland, UK). The content is expressed as the percentage of total sample weight on an oven-dry basis. The total protein content in the grain was measured according to the method described by Kang et al. (2005). 

The ASV was assessed to measure the degree of spreading of milled rice in a $1 . 7 \%$ KOH solution after $2 3 \textrm { h }$ according to Cruz and Khush (2000). ASV scores are generally categorized into four groups: (1) high $\mathsf { G T } = \mathsf { A S V }$ score 1–2, (2) high intermediate GT = ASV score 3, (3) intermediate $\mathsf { G T } = \mathsf { A S V }$ score 4–5, and (4) low $\mathsf { G T } = \mathsf { A S V }$ score 6–7 (Armstrong et al., 2022). Textural analysis of cooked rice was performed with a texture analyser (Food Technology Corporation). 

The starch granules of endosperm were observed using the mature brown rice grains by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (TM-1000; Hitachi, Japan). The mature rice grains were transversely broken and coated with gold to the ruptured transverse surface, observing the surface by SEM. 

# DNA pull-down and LC–MS

The developing grains at 5, 10, and 15 days after flowering (DAF) of NIP were harvested for nuclear protein extraction following the user manual of the Nuclear and Cytoplasm Protein Extract kit (Cat#P0028, Beyotime, Beijing). The nuclear proteins of 5, 10, and 15 DAF were mixed in a 1:1:1 ratio for DNA pull-down experiments. Double-strand probes for DNA pull-down were synthesized and labelled with biotin tags at Sunya Biotech (Hangzhou). The probe sequences are listed in Table S2. DNA pull-down assays were performed according to Jutras et al. (2012). To facilitate the DNA pull-down for the promoter of 15 SSEGs, we took a strategic approach in which the 2-kb long promoter was split into 5–7 ~350-bp long partially overlapping probes (Table S2; Figure 1b). The finally captured proteins were separated by sodium dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and visualized by silver staining. The proteins binding to the probe were identified by LC–MS at OE Biotech (Shanghai). 

# RNA extraction and reverse transcription quantitative PCR

Total RNA was extracted by TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) and treated with DNase I (TaKaRa, Japan), and first-strand cDNA was synthesised by a ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Kit (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan). Quantitative RT-PCR was performed with specific primers (Table S3) in the reaction system of ABI PRISM 7900HT (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) using SYBR Green Master Mix (Toyobo Co. Ltd, Japan). Rice UBIQUITIN gene (LOC_Os03g13170) was used as an internal control. 

# Mutant lines obtained by CRISPR/Cas9

The mutants of TFs were generated using a CRISPR/Cas9 Kit (BGK03, Biogle, China). Annealed double-strand oligos of the gDNA sequences were ligated into the constructed into the BGK03 vector. gDNA sequences are shown in Table S3. The japonica rice cultivar ‘Nipponbare’ and indica rice cultivar ‘Zhongjiazao17’ (YK17) were used as the recipient. The obtained vectors were transformed into callus by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. 

# Yeast-one-hybrid assay

The ClontechTM one-Hybrid System (Clontech, Dalian, China) was used in this study. The CDS of potential TFs was fused with the GAL4 AD domain in the pB42AD vector, and the promoter region of SSEGs was cloned into the pLacZi vector. Yeast strain EGY48 was co-transformed with the indicated constructs and grew on SD/-Ura/-Trp plates, and then struck on SD/-Ura/-Trp plates containing $2 \%$ galactose, $1 \%$ raffinose, $1 \times \mathsf { B U }$ salts, and 100 mg/L X-Gal. The interaction was confirmed by the visualization of blue colonies on the medium. The empty vectors pLacZi and pB42AD were used as negative controls. 

# Luciferase transient transcriptional activity assay

The CDS of TFs were inserted into ‘None’ vector as effectors and the promoter region of SSEGs was cloned into 190LUC vector as a reporter. Protoplast preparation and transformation were conducted according to the method of Cao et al. (2022). Luciferase $\textcircled{8}$ Reporter Assay System (Promega, Madison, WI) was used to measure the luciferase activity on TD-20/20 Luminometer (GloMax20/20; Promega). The primers used in the study are listed in Table S3. 

# Electrophoresis mobility shift assay

The CDS of OsERF44 was amplified from cDNA of NIP and inserted into the pGEX6P-1 vector to obtain the GST-OsERF44 fusion protein. The CDS of OsABI5 was amplified from cDNA of NIP and inserted into the pET-28a vector to obtain the OsABI5-His fusion protein. Single-strand probes for DNA pull-down were synthesised and labelled with biotin tags at Sunya Biotech (Hangzhou, China). Double-strand probes were prepared by annealing complementary single-strand probes. DNA gel-shift assays were performed using an EMSA Kit according to manufacturer instructions (GS009; Beyotime, Shanghai, China). 

# Immunoblot analysis

Fresh rice grains were used to extract total protein with an extraction buffer, followed by separation on $10 \%$ SDS-PAGE gels and immunoblotting with specific antibodies. The specific bands were visualized using an enhanced chemiluminescence detection kit from Thermo Fisher Scientific.
