# A dual role of Arabidopsis PAL nuclear localization in fine tuning flavonoid biosynthesis

Linhui $\mathsf { S u n } ^ { 1 , 2 } \dag _ { \mathrm { \Pi } }$ , Zifeng Yang1 †, Xiaotong Shan1,3 †, Nan Wang1 , Zile Liu1,2 , Xiang Gao1 , Yangnan ${ \tt G u } ^ { 4 }$ , Qiao Zhao1 * 

The phenylpropanoid pathway is essential for plant development and environmental adaptation, producing metabolites such as flavonoids, lignin, and salicylic acid. Phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) catalyzes the first committed step in this pathway and has long been viewed as a cytoplasmic enzyme. Here, we reveal that PAL responds to elevated flavonoid levels in Arabidopsis by undergoing phosphorylation and relocating to the nucleus. Nuclear sequestration of PAL reduces its cytoplasmic abundance and enzymatic activity, resulting in rapid suppression of phenylpropanoid metabolic flux. In parallel, nuclear-localized PAL interacts with the transcription factor TT8, disrupting MBW complex formation and down-regulating flavonoid biosynthetic genes. This dual mechanism enables PAL to act as a metabolic feedback regulator that dynamically adjusts flavonoid production in response to internal metabolic states. Our findings redefine PAL as both a biosynthetic enzyme and a responsive integrator of cellular metabolic status, establishing a previously unknown paradigm for feedback control in plant specialized metabolism.

# INTRODUCTION

The phenylpropanoid pathway is a fundamental specialized metabolic pathway in plants, producing lignin, flavonoids, isoflavonoids, stilbenes, and salicylic acid, which play essential roles in plant growth, development, and stress responses (1–3). Phenylpropanoid derivatives are also active components in various medicinal herbs, underscoring their importance for human health (4–6). Phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) initiates this pathway by catalyzing the conversion of phenylalanine to trans-cinnamic acid, linking primary metabolism to specialized metabolism (7). This bridging role is essential for regulating the flow of metabolites into secondary pathways, positioning PAL as a central focus in studies of plant metabolic regulation. 

Given the central role of PAL in metabolic pathways, the activity of PAL is known to respond to multiple environmental stimuli (8– 10). For instance, in Arabidopsis thaliana, PAL1 and PAL2 are coexpressed under low-temperature stress, resulting in the accumulation of soluble phenylpropanoids, sinapate esters, and flavonoids to improve cold tolerance (11, 12). PAL is also induced by ultraviolet (UV) exposure, with PAL1 activating the phenylpropanoid pathway to synthesize anthocyanins, protecting plants from UV damage (13). Mutants deficient in PAL exhibit reduced synthesis of essential specialized metabolites, affecting plant growth and stress response. For example, the pal1pal2 double mutant in Arabidopsis has reduced flavonoid levels and heightened UV sensitivity, while the pal1pal2pal3pal4 quadruple mutant shows a significant decline in salicylic acid content, compromising resistance to pathogens (14). 

In addition to transcriptional induction by environmental factors, PAL is also regulated at the posttranslational level (15–17). Certain intermediates in the phenylpropanoid pathway, such as cinnamic acid, $\boldsymbol { p }$ -coumaric acid, caffeic acid, and coniferyl alcohol, can rapidly induce PAL degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway (18, 19). In Arabidopsis, an F-BOX protein with a Kelch repeat domain (KFB) has been shown to ubiquitinate and degrade PAL (20). This study also observed that the PAL-GFP (green fluorescent protein) fusion protein exhibited extracytoplasmic localization, including network-like distribution and nuclear peripheral localization (20). These findings suggest that PAL may have previously unrecognized functions in plants. 

Flavonoids represent a major class of PAL-dependent metabolites, derived from the conversion of $\boldsymbol { p }$ -coumaroyl-CoA through a series of enzymatic reactions (21–23). Key enzymes involved in this pathway include chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), and flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H), which catalyze the formation of dihydrokaempferol. Dihydrokaempferol can then be further converted into flavonols or anthocyanins via flavonol synthase (FLS) and dihydroflavonol reductase (DFR), respectively (24, 25). Transcriptional regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis largely relies on the MBW transcriptional complex, composed of MYB, basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH), and WD40 proteins (26–29). The function of the MBW complex can be modulated through interactions with other transcriptional regulators. For instance, A. thaliana MYB4, which we previously identified, interacts with transparent testa 8 (TT8) within the MBW complex, thereby attenuating its binding affinity and reducing its transcriptional activity (30). Similarly, Camellia sinensis CsJAZ6 and Chrysanthemum CmBBX28, which have been discovered in recent years, also act as MBW repressors (31, 32). These proteins inhibit the formation of the MBW complex and its regulation of downstream target genes by interacting with a component of the MBW complex. Moreover, recent studies have shown that sucrose nonfermenting related kinase 1 (SnRK1) can phosphorylate MBW complex components, promoting complex dissociation and further modulating flavonoid synthesis (33).

However, accumulating evidence has revealed nontraditional nuclear localizations for several metabolic enzymes, implying potential previously unidentified functions beyond their established catalytic roles. For example, in Arabidopsis, CHS and CHI, the initial enzymes in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, are localized to both the cytoplasm and nucleus, although their specific function in the nucleus remains unclear (35). Similarly, in the shikimate pathway, 5-enolpyruvylshikimate 3-phosphate synthase (EPSP) generally operates in the chloroplast but has been shown to localize to the nucleus in poplar, where it functions as a transcriptional repressor (36). These examples suggest that, like other enzymes, PAL may also have a nuclear role in addition to its established cytoplasmic function. 

In this study, we demonstrate that Arabidopsis PAL localizes not only in the cytoplasm but also in the nucleus. Its nuclear accumulation is dynamically regulated and depends on phosphorylation. Under conditions that stimulate flavonoid biosynthesis, such as metabolic induction by endogenous cues, phosphorylated PAL translocates into the nucleus. There, it interacts with TT8, a core bHLH component of the MYB-bHLH-WD40 (MBW) complex, disrupting MBW formation and repressing flavonoid biosynthetic gene expression. In parallel, nuclear sequestration of PAL reduces its cytoplasmic abundance and enzymatic activity, thereby limiting the metabolic flux into the flavonoid pathway. Together, these findings uncover a previously unrecognized nuclear function of PAL and reveal a metabolite-responsive feedback mechanism that fine-tunes flavonoid production through dynamic control of enzyme localization and activity. 

# RESULTS Subcellular localization and functional implications of PAL in Arabidopsis

PAL has traditionally been regarded as a cytosolic enzyme. However, previous studies have reported that PAL-GFP fusion proteins exhibit extracytoplasmic localization (20). To further investigate PAL’s subcellular localization and potential function, we transiently expressed PAL-GFP fusions in Arabidopsis protoplasts. Confocal microscopy revealed that these isoforms were predominantly cytoplasmic and exhibited distinct nuclear localization (Fig. 1A). 

To further validate these findings, we generated a series of transgenic plants expressing stable PAL-GFP fusions in Arabidopsis wildtype Columbia-0 (Col-0). Consistent with our transient expression data, nuclear accumulation of PAL-GFP was prominently observed in root meristem cells, but no significant nuclear localization was observed in the elongation regions (fig. S1). We hypothesized that the degradation of PAL could potentially obscure its visibility in the nucleus. Previous studies have suggested that KFB proteins mediate the degradation of the PAL isozymes (20, 37). To mitigate the effects of PAL degradation on localization observations, we expressed PAL-GFP fusions in Arabidopsis kfb4m (kfb1/20/39/50) mutants. Notably, nuclear localization was significantly enhanced in the root elongation zone cells of 35S:PAL1-GFP/kfb4m, 35S:PAL2-GFP/kfb4m and 35S:PAL4-GFP/kfb4m plants, while substantially less nuclear accumulation was observed for PAL3 in the same cell type of 35S:PAL3- GFP/kfb4m plant (fig. S1B). This differential localization suggests that PAL3 was different from other PAL isoforms and may have distinct, yet undefined, biological functions (14, 34, 38). 

To further corroborate these observations, we performed subcellular fractionation followed by immunoblot analysis using specific endogenous PAL antibodies (19). Both Col-0 and kfb4m seedlings exhibited detectable PAL protein in nuclear fractions, confirming its nuclear presence (Fig. 1B). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that PAL can accumulate in the nucleus and may perform noncanonical, nucleus-associated functions beyond its established metabolic role. 

# Metabolic induction of flavonoids triggers nuclear localization of PAL

Although we have confirmed the nuclear localization of PAL, it remains unclear whether this localization is dynamic, regulated, or functionally relevant. Given that several studies have revealed that metabolites can regulate the protein localization of metabolic enzymes through feedback mechanisms (39–41), we hypothesized that the subcellular localization of PAL may itself be dynamically regulated in response to metabolic cues. 

Flavonoids are one of the key metabolites downstream of PAL in the phenylpropanoid pathway. Previous studies have shown that UV-B induces the accumulation of plant flavonoids, including flavonols, anthocyanins, and proanthocyanidins (42). To test whether enhanced flavonoid biosynthesis influences PAL nuclear localization, we treated Arabidopsis 35S:PAL1-GFP/kfb4m seedlings with UV-B and monitored PAL distribution. PAL1 exhibited nuclear accumulation after 24-hour treatment (Fig. 2, A to D), correlating with increased anthocyanin levels (Fig. 2, E and F). We further investigated the reversibility of this process by conducting recovery experiments after UV-B exposure, both in the presence and absence of the protein synthesis inhibitor cycloheximide (CHX). During a 36-hour recovery period, PAL1 returned to the cytoplasm, and this nuclear depletion occurred even when protein synthesis was inhibited by CHX (Fig. 2, A to D). This indicates that the change in PAL subcellular localization is due to reversible relocalization of existing PAL protein rather than degradation and resynthesis of cytoplasmic PAL. In addition, Western blot analysis showed no significant change in total PAL protein levels during UV-B treatment and recovery (fig. S4D), further supporting that the dynamic nuclear-cytoplasmic shuttling of PAL is independent of de novo protein synthesis. We further extended this observation by examining the subcellular localization of other PAL isoforms through nuclear-cytoplasmic fractionation assays. Consistent with the behavior of PAL1, both PAL2 and PAL4 also displayed significant UVinduced nuclear accumulation, while PAL3 showed a distinct response pattern (fig. S2). Similarly, high-sucrose treatment, another wellestablished inducer of flavonoid biosynthesis, also promoted PAL nuclear translocation (Fig. 2, G to J, and fig. S3). Together, these results demonstrate that elevated flavonoid levels promote PAL nuclear import and stabilization, likely as part of a negative feedback loop. Nuclear-localized PAL may serve as an inactive reservoir that can be reactivated by reexporting it to the cytoplasm when phenylpropanoid flux needs to resume. 

To further test whether flavonoid metabolites directly regulate PAL localization, we applied exogenous naringenin (NAR)—a stable upstream intermediate of the flavonoid pathway known to modulate metabolic and signaling processes (22, 24, 43). This allowed us to assess the impact of flavonoid accumulation in a controlled and metabolite-specific manner.

# Nuclear-localized PAL negatively regulates flavonoid biosynthesis gene expression and flavonoid accumulation

Flavonoid accumulation triggers PAL phosphorylation and nuclear import, implying PAL’s potential involvement in feedback control of flavonoid biosynthesis. This nuclear localization suggests transcriptional regulatory functions analogous to the EPSP synthase’s dual role as both a metabolic enzyme and a nuclear transcriptional regulator (36). Building on our finding that elevated flavonoid levels promote PAL nuclear import (Figs. 2 and 3), we hypothesized that nuclearlocalized PAL influences the transcription of flavonoid biosynthetic genes. To test this nuclear function, we overexpressed a nuclearlocalized version of PAL containing a nuclear localization signal (NLS) (fig. S1B) and compared the transcription levels of flavonoid biosynthetic genes between 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 and control (35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/Col-0) plants. Quantitative analysis revealed significantly reduced steady-state mRNA levels of CHS, CHI, F3H, FLS, and DFR in 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 plants compared to 35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/ Col-0 controls (Fig. 5, A to E). Furthermore, dual-luciferase reporter assays revealed that coexpression of PAL1-GFP-NLS with flavonoid biosynthetic gene promoters (ProCHS:LUC, ProCHI:LUC, ProF3H:LUC, ProFLS:LUC, or ProDFR:LUC) significantly reduced luciferase activity compared to GUS-GFP-NLS controls (Fig. 5, F to J). Furthermore, to directly test the repressive effect of PAL nuclear import on flavonoid gene transcription, we analyzed transcript levels of these genes after NAR-induced PAL nuclear translocation. Notably, in 35S:PAL1- GFP/kfb4m plant, treatment with NAR led to down-regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis genes (Fig. 5, K to O). These findings support a role for nuclear PAL1 in modulating the steady-state mRNA levels of flavonoid biosynthetic genes, as evidenced by decreased mRNA accumulation; however, whether this occurs through direct transcriptional repression remains to be further investigated. 

We next analyzed flavonoid accumulation in these transgenic lines, focusing on the two major flavonoid classes, anthocyanins and flavonols. The total anthocyanin content was determined by a colorimetric 

method, and the results showed that under sucrose induction, the anthocyanin content of 35S: PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 seedlings at 17 days old was significantly reduced (Fig. 5P). Regarding flavonol composition, these compounds predominantly accumulate in glycosylated forms rather than as free forms (e.g., NAR, kaempferol, or quercetin). In Arabidopsis, the flavonol profile is primarily composed of glycosylated derivatives of quercetin and kaempferol (47), with characteristic glucose or rhamnose substitutions at the 3- and 7-hydroxyl positions (48, 49). We selected several glycosylated products of flavonols for liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS) detection, including kaempferol 3-O-rhamnoside (k-3-O-rha), kaempferol 3-O-rhamnoside-7-O-rhmanoside (k-3-O-rha-7-O-rha), kaempferol 3-O-rhamnosyl $_ { ( 1 - > 2 ) }$ -glucoside-7-O-rhamnoside (k-3-O-[rha (1->2 glu)]-7-O-rha), and quercetin 3-O-glucoside-7-O-rhmanoside (q-3-O-glu-7-O-rha). LC-MS quantification of these selected flavonol glycosides showed significantly lower levels in 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 plants versus controls (Fig. 5P). 

To rule out the possibility that the reduced flavonol glycoside levels were due to impaired glycosylation, we examined the steadystate mRNA levels of key flavonol glycosyltransferase genes (UGT89C1, UGT78D1, and UGT78D2), which are not direct targets of the MBW complex. No significant changes were observed in PAL1-NLS transgenic plants compared to controls (fig. S6). This finding demonstrates that the observed reduction in flavonol glycoside accumulation in 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 plants resulted from decreased flavonol biosynthesis rather than impaired glycosylation. Together, these results demonstrated that PAL accumulation in the nucleus affects the steady-state level of flavonoid gene mRNA and accumulation of flavonoids. 

# Nuclear-localized PAL inhibits MBW complex formation and transcriptional activity

As a metabolic enzyme, PAL likely lacks the ability to directly bind to and regulate gene transcription. Following nuclear import, it may require nuclear protein partners to exert transcriptional regulatory functions. Given that the transcription of plant flavonoid genes is typically governed by the MBW transcription complex (22), we sought to determine whether PAL regulates flavonoid gene transcription through interaction with the MBW complex. To validate this, we cloned known transcription factors associated with PAL-derived metabolic pathways and conducted a bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay. These experiments revealed a specific and robust interaction between PAL and TT8, a core component of the MBW transcriptional complex that regulates flavonoid biosynthesis (Fig. 6A). In contrast, no interactions were observed with other tested transcription factors, TTG1 and MYB75 (fig. S7, A and B), a finding further corroborated by yeast two-hybrid assays (fig. S8, B and C). Furthermore, lignin synthesis is also one of the important metabolic pathways mediated by PAL. Our results showed that no detectable interaction was observed between PAL and key lignin biosynthesis regulators MYB43, MYB58, and MYB85, suggesting that PAL nuclear translocation does not modulate lignin biosynthesis (figs. S7, C to E, and S8, D to F). While the TT8-PAL interaction was not observed in the yeast system (fig. S8A), this discrepancy likely reflects the importance of plant-specific posttranslational modifications or cellular context for this interaction, as demonstrated by the positive BiFC results in planta.

# Nuclear translocation of PAL reduces its enzymatic activity in Arabidopsis

Although we demonstrated that NAR induces PAL phosphorylation, promotes its nuclear entry, and enables its function as a transcriptional repressor, the PAL’s primary role remains its enzymatic activity in metabolism. Given that nuclear sequestration of PAL would reduce its cytoplasmic availability, we hypothesized that nuclear translocation attenuates PAL’s enzymatic activity. To test this, we first validated that PAL protein abundance correlates with enzymatic activity. Previous studies have shown that PAL1 and PAL2 are the primary PAL enzymes in Arabidopsis, and the pal1/2 double mutant exhibits nearly no PAL protein expression (14, 38). In contrast, the kfb01/20/39/50 quadruple mutant (kfb4m) accumulates excessive endogenous PAL due to impaired PAL degradation (20, 37). Therefore, we verified that the PAL enzymatic activity in the wild-type (Col-0), pal1/2 double mutant, and kfb4m Arabidopsis seedlings correlates with their PAL protein abundance (Fig. 8, A to C). We next tested the effect of NAR-induced nuclear translocation on PAL activity by treating Col-0 and pal1/2 mutant seedlings with NAR. Compared to the mock control, NAR-treated Col-0 seedlings exhibited a significant reduction in PAL enzymatic activity (Fig. 8D). This reduction likely results from the NAR-induced nuclear entry of PAL1 (Fig. 4D) and the consequent decrease in cytoplasmic PAL protein. In contrast, pal1/2 mutants, which have negligible PAL expression, showed no significant change in activity upon NAR treatment (Fig. 8, A and D). Collectively, these results support the hypothesis that nuclear translocation diminishes PAL’s catalytic output.

# DISCUSSION

Our study uncovers an unexpected nuclear role for A. thaliana PAL, challenging the conventional view of PAL as solely a cytoplasmic enzyme (14, 34). Traditionally, PAL catalyzes the initial step in the phenylpropanoid pathway, localizing within the cytoplasm where it contributes to a range of specialized metabolites, such as flavonoids and lignin (18, 21). Here, however, we provide evidence through GFP localization and nuclear-cytoplasmic fractionation via Western blotting that PAL can also localize within the nucleus (Fig. 1 and fig. S1). This nuclear localization suggests a previously unknown regulatory function, adding a previously unidentified layer to the understanding of phenylpropanoid pathway control. 

PAL’s nuclear localization is a dynamically changing process, which is induced by enhancing flavonoid biosynthesis under UV-B and high-sucrose treatment (Fig. 2). While this result supports a link between flavonoid accumulation and PAL nuclear localization, we acknowledge that further genetic validation—such as experiments in the tt4 (CHS) mutant—would be required to definitively establish causality. We found the flavonoid compound NAR regulates PAL localization (Fig. 3). While our data identify NAR as a representative metabolite capable of promoting PAL nuclear localization, this effect is not necessarily NAR specific. It should be noted that NAR is used here as an indicator of elevated flavonoid metabolism rather than as a proven direct signal for PAL nuclear import. Other flavonoids or their derivatives that accumulate under UV or high-sucrose conditions may exert similar regulatory influences. This finding implies that metabolite-induced nuclear entry dynamically links metabolic status to regulatory decisions (19, 50). Supporting this notion, our 

previous work demonstrated that the lignin monomer coniferyl alcohol functions as a signaling molecule that rapidly up-regulates KFB transcription and promotes PAL protein degradation in Arabidopsis (19), implying a feedback loop between metabolic intermediates and enzyme stability. Understanding how plants sense these metabolite signals and integrate them into regulatory networks is crucial. This will reveal how metabolic signaling coordinates with enzyme dynamics to maintain homeostasis. 

In addition, PAL undergoes phosphorylation following NAR induction, and this posttranslational modification is essential for its nuclear translocation (Fig. 4A), implicating a yet unidentified kinase in this regulatory process. Identifying the kinase(s) responsible for PAL phosphorylation is therefore a key future direction for understanding how PAL localization is regulated. A precedent for kinase-mediated nucleocytoplasmic trafficking exists: SnRK1 promotes the nuclear localization of NLP7 via phosphorylation, demonstrating its role in regulating protein subcellular distribution (44). However, while our previous work confirmed that enhanced SnRK1 kinase activity reduces PAL protein levels, further analysis revealed that PAL is not a direct substrate of SnRK1 (51). This finding indicates that an alternative kinase pathway must mediate the NAR-induced phosphorylation of PAL, linking flavonoid perception to its nuclear translocation. Thus, further work incorporating proteomic approaches and kinase activity assays will be necessary to elucidate the upstream regulatory factors controlling PAL phosphorylation and its nuclear translocation.

have been previously documented (35, 52); for instance, EPSP synthase, an enzyme primarily involved in the shikimate pathway in chloroplasts, was found to function as a transcriptional repressor within the nucleus in poplar (36). This reinforces the idea that enzymes can adopt additional regulatory roles within the nucleus, expanding the complexity of plant metabolic control (35, 36). Our findings reveal that nuclear PAL interacts with TT8 (Fig. 6), a central component of the MBW transcriptional complex, which controls flavonoid biosynthesis (53–55). By interacting with TT8, PAL indirectly inhibits the transcription of flavonoid biosynthetic genes (Fig. 7). Further evidence supporting this regulatory function was obtained by overexpressing NLS-tagged PAL, forcing PAL to localize entirely within the nucleus (fig. S1B). This manipulation led to a down-regulation of flavonoid biosynthetic genes and a consequent reduction in flavonoid levels, affirming the PAL’s role in nuclear regulation (Fig. 5). 

Although our findings demonstrate that NAR treatment induces PAL phosphorylation and promotes its nuclear localization (Fig. 3), it is unlikely that PAL directly binds to NAR or other flavonoids. Instead, these results suggest the existence of an upstream flavonoidresponsive kinase that senses changes in metabolic status and transduces this signal through PAL modification. Identifying such a sensor-kinase module would be critical to elucidating how plants couple specialized metabolite accumulation to regulatory feedback. To this end, chemical proteomic approaches such as flavonoid-affinity pull-down assays, thermal proteome profiling, or kinase activity screening may be used to uncover candidate flavonoid-binding proteins and associated signaling components. Notably, despite the central role of flavonoids 

in plant development and stress responses, no bona fide sensor for flavonoid metabolites has been reported to date. Discovery of a flavonoid sensor would not only fill a major gap in our understanding of phenylpropanoid pathway regulation but also provide a broader framework for exploring metabolite-driven signaling in plant metabolism. 

While our study highlights the nuclear localization of PAL, it also raises questions regarding its fate once it enters the nucleus. Our findings demonstrate that PAL undergoes dynamic nucleocytoplasmic shuttling, with its nuclear localization serving not only to influence mRNA steady-state levels of flavonoid biosynthetic gene (Fig. 5) but also reduces the enzyme activity of PAL, thereby inhibiting flavonoid overaccumulation through dual transcriptional and posttranslational regulation (Fig. 8). This spatial regulation likely represents an adaptive mechanism that allows plants to rapidly modulate phenylpropanoid flux by temporarily sequestering PAL in the nucleus until metabolic demands require its remobilization to the cytoplasm. The factors influencing PAL’s retention or exit from the nucleus are not yet understood but could be tied to phosphorylation events or other posttranslational modifications. In addition, it is also possible that KFB contributes to eliminating mislocalized nuclear PALs, as suggested by previous studies on PAL turnover. Although we did not directly compare nuclear PAL levels between WT and kfb mutants, this remains an intriguing hypothesis worthy of future investigation. These modifications might control PAL’s interaction with nuclear import/export machinery, influencing whether it remains in the nucleus or is recycled back to the cytoplasm for continued involvement in specialized metabolism. This spatial regulation strategy allows plants to rapidly toggle between metabolic production and transcriptional repression without resorting to irreversible enzyme degradation. 

In conclusion, our study reveals a dual localization and regulatory function for PAL in Arabidopsis, enhancing our understanding of phenylpropanoid pathway regulation. By demonstrating that flavonoidinduced nuclear localization of PAL both reduces its enzymatic activity and disrupts the MBW complex (Figs. 7 and 8D), our findings establish a dynamic “metabolic checkpoint” that fine-tunes phenylpropanoid flux in response to environmental cues. PAL’s ability to influence gene expression in the nucleus provides previously unidentified perspectives on how metabolic feedback and enzyme localization control specialized metabolism. Moreover, the observed correlation between PAL phosphorylation and its nucleocytoplasmic distribution (Fig. 4) opens avenues to decipher how plants decode metabolite signals. This highlights an intricate network of metabolic and transcriptional regulation in plants (Fig. 9). 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

A. thaliana mutants used in this study were in the Col-0 background. The kfb01/20/39/50 quadruple mutant was described previously (19), and pal1/2 double mutant was generated by crossing pal1 and pal2. The pal1 (SALK_096474) and pal2 (Wiscseq_DsLoxHs159_03B) A. thaliana mutants were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center, and all overexpression lines were generated in this study. Arabidopsis seeds were surface-sterilized with $1 0 \%$ sodium hypochlorite and cultured on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (1/2 MS) medium containing $1 \%$ (w/v) sucrose and $0 . 3 5 \%$ Phytagel (pH 5.8) at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16-hour light/8-hour dark cycle. The N. benthamiana plants used in this study were cultivated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16-hour light/8-hour dark photoperiod.

Fig. 9. Model for dynamic regulation of flavonoid biosynthesis by PAL. Under normal conditions, PAL is predominantly localized in the cytoplasm, catalyzing the key step in flavonoid biosynthesis; meanwhile, the MBW complex continuously activates the transcription of flavonoid biosynthesis-related genes in the nucleus, collectively maintaining the basal activity of the pathway. When signals such as UV or high sucrose induce flavonoid accumulation, excessive flavonoids trigger the phosphorylation of PAL by an unknown kinase. This phosphorylation promotes PAL nuclear localization: On one hand, it reduces the effective concentration of functional PAL in the cytoplasm, directly inhibiting flavonoid biosynthetic reactions; on the other hand, nuclear PAL interacts with TT 8, disrupting the structural integrity of the MBW complex and thereby suppressing the transcription of flavonoid biosynthesis genes. This mechanism exerts a dual inhibitory effect on excessive flavonoid accumulation through “spatial restriction of enzymatic activity” and “transcriptional repression,” ultimately forming a negative feedback regulatory loop in flavonoid biosynthesis. In the figure, NAR is used as a representative of flavonoid pathway metabolites, and other flavonoids may exert similar effects.


For plant transformation, 5- to 6-week-old Arabidopsis plants (Col-0 or kfb01/20/39/50 mutant) were grown in a growth chamber under a 16-hour light/8-hour dark cycle at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Transformed plants were selected on 1/2 MS medium containing hygromycin B ( $2 5 \mathrm { m g / }$ liter) and timentin (100 mg/liter). Hygromycin-resistant T1 lines showing GFP fluorescence were selected, and homozygous T3 progeny were used for further experiments. Leaves from 3- to 4-week-old wild-type (Col-0) plants were used for protoplast isolation. 

# Vector construction

For transient protoplast assays, the coding sequences (CDS) of PAL1, PAL2, PAL3, and PAL4 (without the stop codon) were subcloned into the Nde I– and Cla I–digested 35S:FhMYB27-GFP vector (56), yielding 35S:PALs-GFP. For plant transformation, the coding regions of PALs-GFP, PALs-GFP-NLS, PAL1S152DT153DS163D-GFP, and PAL1S152AT153AS163A-GFP were cloned into the pH2GW7 vector and transformed into Col-0 or kfb01/20/39/50 mutants to generate overexpression lines. 

For yeast two-hybrid assays, the CDS of MBW (MYB75, TT8, and TTG1) and three transcription factors related to lignin synthesis (MYB43, MYB58, and MYB85) were individually inserted into the pGADT7 vector, and the PAL1 CDS was inserted into the pGBKT7 vector. For the BiFC assay, the CDS of MYB75, TT8, TTG1, MYB43, MYB58, and MYB85 (without the stop codon) were cloned into the pCNHP-CYFP vector to fuse with the C-terminal fragment of YFP (cYFP), and the PAL1 CDS without the stop codon was cloned into 

the pCNHP-NYFP vector to fuse with the N-terminal fragment of YFP (nYFP). For the Co-IP assay, the CDS of TT8 was cloned into the UBQ10pro-3HA and pCAMBIA1300-35S-EGFP vector, yielding TT8-HA and TT8-GFP, and then TT8-GFP was transformed into Col-0. For the dual luciferase reporter assay, ~1 kb of CHS, CHI, F3H, FLS, and DFR promoters was cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector to generate P-LUC reporter constructs. The primers used for vector construction are listed in table S2. 

# Transient protoplast assay

Protoplasts were isolated from 4-week-old rosette leaves using the protocol described by Yoo et al. (57). The isolated protoplasts were filtered through Miracloth (EMD Millipore, USA), pelleted by centrifugation, and transformed with endotoxin-free plasmids using PEG 3350. After a 20- to 22-hour incubation in the dark, protoplasts were harvested for further analysis. 

For subcellular localization, protoplasts were observed with a confocal microscope (Nikon). GFP fluorescence was detected using excitation/emission wavelengths of 488/493 to $5 5 6 \mathrm { n m }$ , while RFP constructs were detected using 561/590 to 628 nm. 

# DAPI staining

Ten-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings grown on 1/2 MS solid medium were immersed in $1 \times$ phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) for 10 min. The seedlings were then stained with DAPI $^ { ( 4 ^ { \prime } , 6 }$ -diamidino-2-phenylindole) solution $( 0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m l } )$ , ensuring full coverage of the roots, and incubated 

at room temperature in the dark for 25 to $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , with time adjusted as needed. After staining, the seedlings were washed three times with $1 \times$ PBS for 3 min each. Last, the samples were mounted in water and imaged using a confocal microscope (Nikon). 

# Subcellular localization analyses by confocal laser scanning microscopy

The localization of PAL1, PAL1(3A), and PAL1(3D) was investigated in roots of 35S:PAL1-GFP/Col-0, 35S:PAL1(3A)-GFP/Col-0, and 35S:PAL1(3D)-GFP/Col-0 seedlings grown on 1/2 MS plates for 7 days. For NAR treatment, 10-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings were transferred to liquid 1/2 MS and treated with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ NAR ( $0 . 1 \%$ dimethyl sulfoxide as control) for 6 hours. For UV treatment, 7-day-old seedlings were irradiated with a UV-B FS20 fluorescent lamp (Westinghouse) at an intensity of $3 . 9 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } s ^ { - 1 }$ for 6, 12, 18, and 24 hours, or for 24 hours followed by 36-hour recovery with or without CHX. For sucrose treatment, 4-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings were transferred to liquid 1/2 MS supplemented with either $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ mannitol (control) or $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sucrose and incubated for an additional 4 days. Following treatment, roots were stained with propidium iodide (PI; $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m l }$ in aqueous solution) for 2 min to visualize cell walls. Imaging was performed using a Nikon confocal laser scanning microscope. GFP fluorescence was detected using excitation/emission wavelengths of $4 8 8 \mathrm { n m } / 4 9 3$ to $5 5 6 \mathrm { n m }$ . PI fluorescence was detected using $5 6 1 \mathrm { n m } /$ 600 to 660 nm. 

The nuclear/cytoplasmic localization of PAL was assessed using confocal laser scanning microscopy and ImageJ software, calculating the N/C ratio $( \mathrm { N } / \mathrm { C } =$ mean nuclear fluorescence intensity/mean cytoplasmic fluorescence intensity, with mean fluorescence intensity being the ratio between the total fluorescence intensity measured and the number of pixels measured). The area of the nucleus was determined by analyzing the bright field acquired through the transmitted light mode, while the area of the cytoplasm was selected using the PI signal as a reference. Quantification of mean cellular fluorescence and nucleus-to-cytosol ratios was done from five root tips, each consisting of the average of five meristematic epidermal cells. 

# Cell fractionation and protein gel blotting

For nuclear-cytoplasmic fractionation, 10-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings were ground in liquid nitrogen, and the powder was resuspended in precooled lysis buffer $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ tris-HCl $\mathrm { ( p H } 7 . 4 \mathrm { ) }$ , $2 5 \%$ glycerol, 20 mM KCl, 2 mM EDTA, 2.5 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sucrose, 5 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), and 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF)]. The extract was filtered through Miracloth to remove debris. Nuclei retained on the Miracloth were washed to increase yield. After centrifugation at $1 5 0 0 g$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , the supernatant was removed to a new tube and centrifuged at $1 3 { , } 0 0 0 g$ for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to collect the nonnuclear fraction. Nuclear pellets were washed four times with Triton buffer $[ 2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ tris-HCl (pH 7.4), $2 5 \%$ glycerol, $2 . 5 \ : \mathrm { m M } \ : \mathrm { M g C l _ { 2 } }$ , and $0 . 2 \%$ Triton X-100] and resuspended in extraction buffer II $\mathrm { . 1 0 m M }$ tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 2 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 1.7 M sucrose, $0 . 1 5 \%$ Triton X-100, $0 . 0 3 5 \%$ $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol, and 1 mM PMSF]. The resuspension was layered onto an equal volume of extraction buffer III [10 mM tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 2 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $2 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sucrose, $1 \%$ Triton X-100, $0 . 0 3 5 \% \ \beta$ - mercaptoethanol, and 1 mM PMSF] and centrifuged at $1 3 { , } 0 0 0 g$ for $4 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to collect nuclear fractions. 

Proteins from nuclear and cytoplasmic fractions were separated by SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes (Millipore). 

Anti-PAL (rabbit polyclonal antibody provided by ABclonal), antihistone H3 (Abcam, ab1791), and anti-UGPase (UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase) (Agrisera, AS05086) were used as primary antibodies. Goat anti-rabbit immunoglobulin G (CoWin Biosciences , CW0103S) was used as the secondary antibody. 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

For the yeast two-hybrid assay, the yeast strain AH109 was transformed with different combinations of bait and prey vectors and initially selected on synthetic complete medium lacking leucine and tryptophan (−Leu/−Trp). The positive clones were assayed on SD/- Ade/-His/-Leu/-Trp medium to determine the interaction between the two expressed proteins. The empty pGADT7/pGBKT7 vector (named AD/BD) and AD-PAL1 were used as a negative and positive control, respectively. 

# BiFC analysis

For BiFC analysis, the MYB75-cYFP, TT8-cYFP, TTG1-cYFP, MYB43- cYFP, MYB58-cYFP, MYB85-cYFP, and PAL1-nYFP vector constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. The empty pCNHP-CYFP/pCNHP-NYFP vector (named cYFP/nYFP) was used as the negative control. Different combinations of Agrobacterium strains were resuspended in infiltration buffer [10 mM 2-(N-morpholine) ethanesulfonic acid, 10 mM $\begin{array} { r } { \mathbf { M } \mathbf { g } \mathbf { C l } _ { 2 } . } \end{array}$ , and $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ acetosyringone] and cotransformed into leaves of 3- to 4-week-old N. benthamiana. The mCherry-VirD2NLS (58) was cotransformed with each combination as a nuclear protein marker. To determine whether PAL1 proteins can influence the interaction of TT8 with MYB75 by BiFC assays, GUS and PAL1 were cloned into the pH2GW7 vector and were coinfiltrated into the same N. benthamiana leaf with TT8-cYFP/MYB75-nYFP. The YFP and mCherry fluorescence were detected using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Nikon), 2 days after infiltration. 

# Co-IP assay

Co-IP experiments were conducted as described previously (59). To test the interaction between TT8 and PAL1, the TT8-HA and PAL1- GFP-NLS were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 and cotransformed into N. benthamiana leaves. Total proteins were extracted from the N. benthamiana leaves after a 3-day incubation and then incubated with GFP-Nanoab-Magnetic Beads (LABLEAD, GNM-25-1000) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 hour. The input proteins and immunoprecipitates were detected by immunoblotting with either anti-HA (Roche, 12013819001) or anti-GFP (Sigma-Aldrich, 11814460001) antibodies. 

For Co-IP assay in Arabidopsis, 10-day-old seedlings of 35S:TT8- GFP/Col-0 and 35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/Col-0 were subjected to crude protein extraction with protein extract buffer [50 mM tris-HCl (pH 7.4), $1 0 \%$ glycerol, 150 mM NaCl, $0 . 1 \%$ NP-40, 5 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5 mM DTT, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132, and 1 mM PMSF]. The samples were incubated with GFP-Nanoab-Magnetic Beads (LABLEAD, GNM-25-1000) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 hour. Then, the input and immunoprecipitation were visualized by immunoblot analysis using anti-GFP and anti-PAL antibodies. 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis

For quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) quantification of gene expression in 35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/Col-0 and 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0, the seeds were sown on normal 1/2 MS plates for 10 days. For the NAR assay, the seeds were sown on normal 1/2 MS plates for 10 days and then transferred to 1/2 MS liquid with or without 0.1 mM NAR for another 6 hours. The FastPure Universal 

Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme, RC411-01) was used to extract RNA from Arabidopsis seedlings, following the manufacturer’s instructions. For each sample, $1 \mu \ g$ of total RNA was used for synthesizing the first-strand cDNA using Superscript III Reverse Transcriptase (Invitrogen, 18080044). The qRT-PCR was performed with Taq Pro Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, Q712-02). The GAPDH (AT1G16300) gene was used as a reference gene in the qRT-PCR. The relative quantification of each gene was performed using three biological replicates, and each biological replicate was detected in duplicate. All primers used in this experiment are listed in table S2. 

# Dual luciferase reporter assay

To test the transcriptional repression of PAL1-NLS, the dual-luciferase reporter assay was performed in N. benthamiana leaves. Each promoter in pGreenII 0800-LUC was transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 containing the helper plasmid pSoup-P19. These five A. tumefaciens strains and GUS-GFP-NLS, PAL1-GFP-NLS, used in transgenic plants, were cultured overnight. After collecting the A. tumefaciens, they were resuspended in infiltration buffer $\mathrm { { 1 0 ~ m M } }$ $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 10 mM MES, and $0 . 1 \mathrm { m M }$ acetosyringone) to an $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ (optical density at $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ ) of 0.6 and incubated at room temperature for 2 hours. The reporter:effector ratio was 1:9. Different combinations of A. tumefaciens suspension were infiltrated onto N. benthamiana leaves. After 48 hours, leaf samples were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen for the dual-luciferase assay. Luciferase activities were detected using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega). Each sample contained four biological repeats. 

# Metabolite analyses

For targeted metabolite analysis of flavonoids, the seeds of 35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/Col-0 and 35S:PAL1-GFP-NLS/Col-0 were sown on standard 1/2 MS plates for 14 days. The seedlings were then collected, ground in liquid nitrogen, and lyophilized for 2 days. For extraction, 10-mg samples were treated with $8 0 \%$ (v/v) methanol containing 1/5000 formic acid, followed by a 20-min ultrasound bath to ensure complete dissolution. After centrifugation at $1 2 , 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , the supernatant was collected and placed in $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight to degrade chlorophylls. All samples were filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ Millipore membrane before measurement. 

Metabolite analyses using ultra-performance liquid chromatography with triple quadrupole mass spectrometric (UPLC-QQQ-MS/MS) on an Agilent 1290 LC system were conducted according to the previously developed method (48, 60). Flavonoids were resolved using an Agilent ZORBAX Eclipse Plus C18 column (2.1 mm by $1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m } $ ) with a mobile phase of $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) aqueous formic acid (A) and acetonitrile (B), at a flow rate of $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ . The 9-min linear gradient with the following ratios of solvent B was used: 0 to 2 min, $1 5 \%$ B (v/v); 2 to 8 min, 15 to $6 0 \%$ B (v/v); 8 to 9 min, 60 to $1 5 \%$ B (v/v). The optimum operating electrospray ionization conditions were as follows: gas temperature, $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ gas flow rate, 7 liters/min; nebulizer pressure, 45 psi (310.26 Kpa); sheath gas temperature, $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ and sheath gas flow, 11 liters/min. The capillary voltages were optimized to $4 0 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ in positive mode or $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ in negative mode with equal nozzle voltages (500 V). Quantification of target metabolites was carried out using multiple reaction monitoring modes, and detailed conditions are provided in table S1. The flavonoid content of 35S:GUS-GFP-NLS/Col-0 was set to 1.0. 

Anthocyanins were extracted and quantified as described previously (33). Briefly, 10-day-old seedlings were harvested, weighed, 

and incubated in $1 \mathrm { m l }$ of extraction buffer [acidic methanol, $1 \%$ HCl (w/v)] for 24 hours at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. Absorbance of the supernatants was measured at 530 and $6 5 7 \mathrm { n m }$ . Relative anthocyanin levels were quantified as $( \mathrm { A } 5 3 0 \mathrm { n m } - 1 / 4 \mathrm { A } 6 5 7 \mathrm { n m } )$ ) per gram fresh weight. 

# Phosphorylation protein analysis in plants using Phos-tag BTL-bound streptavidin-conjugated HRP

The PVDF membrane with blotted protein was used to detect phosphorylated PAL1 protein and total PAL1 protein by incubation with Phos-tag BTL-bound streptavidinconjugated HRP and anti-GFP antibody, respectively. 

# PAL activity assay

The enzymatic assays were performed following the protocol described previously (61). Briefly, to measure the PAL enzyme activity of the wild-type (Col-0), pal1/2 double mutant, and kfb01/20/39/50 quadruple mutant $( k f b 4 m )$ , the 10-day-old seedlings were ground in liquid nitrogen, and the powder was resuspended in precooled extraction buffer [0.1 M borate $( \mathrm { N a } ^ { + } )$ buffer $\mathrm { ( p H 8 . 7 ) }$ ]. After centrifugation at $4 0 0 0 g$ for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , the supernatant was taken as enzyme solution and reserved. Twenty microliters of enzyme solution and $4 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Phe were pipetted in a 96-well enzyme-labeled plate, the absorbance was detected at $2 9 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ (A1), and then the absorbance at $2 9 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ was measured again after the reaction at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ (A2). Relative PAL activity levels were quantified as (A2 − A1) per gram fresh weight.
