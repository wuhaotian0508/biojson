# ABSTRACT

Anthocyanin biosynthesis in non-climacteric grape berries is regulated through coordinated phytohormonal signalling networks. However, the precise mechanisms by which abscisic acid (ABA), brassinosteroids (BRs) and ethylene (ET) synergistically modulate this process remain unclear. In this study, we identified the ET-responsive transcription factor VvERF045 as a critical integrator mediating the crosstalk among ET, BR and ABA pathways, thereby regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis. Mechanistically, VvERF045 physically interacts with its R2R3-MYB partner, VvMYB62, to co-activate the structural genes (SGs) involved in anthocyanin, BR and ABA biosynthesis pathways, including VvUFGT, VvDFR, VvDWF4, VvCPD, VvZEP/ABA1, thus collaboratively redirecting metabolic resources toward the production of anthocyanin, BR and ABA, respectively. Notably, ET significantly enhances the activity of VvERF045, suggesting a potential regulatory hierarchy wherein both BR-dependent and ABA-dependent berry pigmentation may be contingent on upstream ET perception. Collectively, our findings provide insights into how non-climacteric grape fruits temporally coordinate phytohormone pathways for colouration, revealing VvERF045 as a central regulator that bridges ET sensing to BR-mediated and ABA-mediated anthocyanin accumulation. 

# 1 | Introduction

Grape (Vitis vinifera L.), domesticated in the Caucasus region, has become a critical component of global horticulture owing to its rich nutritional metabolites and significant economic value (Ramos-Madrigal et al. 2019; Bouby et al. 2021). Berry colouration, regulated by anthocyanins—a class of flavonoid-derived pigments with potent reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging capabilities—serves as a key determinant of fruit quality (Enaru et al. 2021; Li et al. 2024). These 

glycosylated derivatives of cyanidin, delphinidin and malvidin (Liang et al. 2008) are biosynthesised via enzymatic processes within the phenylpropanoid pathway. This pathway is organised into three functional tiers: the phenylpropanoid core (PAL, C4H, 4CL), the flavonoid backbone (CHS, CHI, F3H) and anthocyanin-specific modifications (DFR, F3'H, LDOX, UFGT), collectively driving anthocyanin production (Zhang et  al.  2014; Sunil and Shetty  2022). The MBW complex (MYB-bHLH-WD40), particularly R2R3-MYB transcription factors (TFs), orchestrates this metabolic cascade by 

activating genes such as VvMYBA1/A2 and repressing others like VvMYBC2-L1 (Naing and Kim 2018; Rinaldo et al. 2015; Koyama et al. 2014). 

Anthocyanin accumulation is governed by genetic programmes and environmental inputs, including light, temperature, water status, phytohormones and mechanical stress (Araguirang and Richter 2022; Enaru et al. 2021; Kaur et al. 2023; Li and Ahammed 2023; Wang et al. 2023). Among these regulators, phytohormones, particularly ABA and BR, play pivotal roles in the pigmentation of non-climacteric grapes by activating MYB TFs and stabilising the MBW complex. Mechanistically, ABA promotes anthocyanin accumulation by activating MYB TFs and counteracting the inhibition of MBW complex assembly mediated by VvSBP8/13 (Guo et al. 2024). BR synergises with light perception to co-activate anthocyanin and proanthocyanidin biosynthesis in “Cabernet Sauvignon” grapes (Zhou et al. 2018), while directly upregulating VvMYBA2/PA1 via BZR1-dependent transcriptional activation (Li et al. 2022). Although ET primarily governs climacteric fruit ripening (Pech et al. 2008), functional ET biosynthesis persists in grape berries and contributes to pre-veraison development (Chervin et al. 2004). Notably, ethephon (ETH) application is widely applied to accelerate grape colouration (Wang, Ge, et al. 2022), yet the molecular integration of ET with ABA/BR pathways in non-climacteric pigmentation remains a critical knowledge gap. The ET signalling cascade involves receptor-mediated inactivation of constitutive triple response1 (CTR1) (via ethylene response1 (ETR1)/ethylene responsive sensor1 (ERS1)/ ethylene-insensitive4 (EIN4)), stabilisation of ethyleneinsensitive2 (EIN2) and subsequent ethylene-insensitive3/ EIN3-like1 (EIN3/EIL1)-dependent activation of ERF TFs (Alonso and Stepanova 2004; Chen et al. 2005; Binder 2020). Multiple ERF TFs central to phytohormone crosstalk exhibit context-dependent roles in the regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis. In apple, MdERF38 stabilises MdMYB1 to maintain pigmentation under drought stress (An et al. 2020), whereas in grape, VvERF23 directly activates VvDFR (Lu et al. 2023). Conversely, VvERF4 suppresses colouration by repressing VvMYB5a (Jia et  al.  2023), highlighting the intricate regulatory complexity of ERF-MYB networks. This positions ERFs as underexplored hubs for the crosstalk among ET, ABA, and BR pathways. 

Phytohormonal regulation operates through interactive networks rather than isolated pathways, orchestrating plant development from growth to senescence (Depuydt and Hardtke 2011; Iqbal et al. 2017). Accumulating evidence highlights the intricate crosstalk among ABA, BR and ET, with species-specific dynamics. In climacteric plant systems, ABA stimulates ET biosynthesis via the upregulation of ACS/ACO genes (Zhu et al. 2015; Mou et al. 2016). Conversely, in nonclimacteric fruits, a reciprocal regulatory mechanism is observed, wherein ET signalling promotes ABA accumulation in strawberries and grapes, thereby facilitating their ripening process (Tosetti et al. 2020; Wang, Yu, et al. 2022). Molecular dissection has demonstrated that the ABA-PYL-PP2C-SnRK2 components function as critical mediators of the synergistic interaction between ET and ABA via AREB/ABF-ERF transcriptional complexes (Bai et al. 2021). Additionally, the interactions between BR and ET display functional plasticity. BR 

facilitates banana ripening via MaBZR1/2-mediated activation of MaACS/MaACO (Guo et al. 2019), while it suppresses ET signalling during Arabidopsis hook development (Smet et al. 2014). Nevertheless, the crosstalk between ET and ABA/ BR during grape ripening remains poorly understood. The developmental dynamics of phytohormones in grape berries are governed by hierarchical regulation. Specifically, auxin (IAA), cytokinins (CKs) and gibberellins (GAs) dominate during early fruit set, followed by ABA-mediated initiation of veraison, with BR subsequently acting as critical coordinators during the transition from veraison to ripening (Perotti et al. 2023). This phased regulatory mechanism establishes an evolutionarily conserved framework for investigating phytohormonal coordination in grapevine. However, several unresolved mechanistic ambiguities remain, particularly regarding the molecular determinants underlying ET-ABA/BR crosstalk in non-climacteric pigmentation processes and the principles of integration that govern this trihormonal network. 

Our study elucidates an integrated ET-mediated regulatory cascade that enhances grape berry pigmentation by coordinating BR and ABA biosynthesis pathways. In this work, we identified VvERF045, an ET-responsive ERF transcription factor previously associated with ripening (Leida et al. 2015), as a pivotal regulatory hub governing pigment accumulation. Functional analysis demonstrates that VvERF045 directly binds to the promoters of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (VvDFR, VvUFGT), BR biosynthetic genes (VvDWF4, VvCPD) and ABA biosynthetic genes (VvZEP/ABA1). Transcriptional activation was confirmed through yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) and dual-luciferase (Dual-LUC) assays. Notably, VvERF045 interacts with VvMYB62 to form binary complexes, which synergistically enhance anthocyanin accumulation and BR/ABA biosynthesis. Mechanistic profiling reveals a sequential activation cascade wherein ET-triggered phytohormone biosynthesis primes pigment deposition, thereby establishing a self-amplifying cycle that is further intensified by prolonged ETH exposure. This phased regulatory architecture designates VvERF045 as the central integrator, responsible for translating ET signals into metabolically synchronised outputs, with particular emphasis on its role in grape anthocyanin accumulation. Collectively, our findings suggest a precision agriculture strategy wherein controlled ETH application enhances grape colouration via multi-phytohormone coordination networks. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | ETH Treatment Coordinately Enhances Anthocyanins Accumulation and the Biosynthesis of Endogenous BR and ABA in Grapevine

While ET is widely recognised as pivotal for climacteric fruit post-ripening, emerging evidence highlights its potential regulatory role in non-climacteric fruit maturation. To comparatively evaluate the roles of ET and ABA in grape ripening, we first assessed the effects of exogenous ETH treatment on berry colouration. Preliminary trials demonstrated a significant enhancement of skin pigmentation in “Muscat Hamburg” grapes during the developmental period from 47 to 63 days after 

anthesis (DAA) following ETH treatment (Figure  S1a). This pattern was subsequently validated in “Ruidu Hongyu” grapes, that demonstrated a significant acceleration in colour development from DAA 40 to DAA 60 (Figure  1a). Concurrently, ETH-treated berries exhibited an increase in total soluble solids (TSS) content, particularly at DAA 40 (Figure 1b) and demonstrated elevated anthocyanin accumulation throughout the veraison to ripening developmental stage (Figure 1c), with anthocyanin content serving as a direct chromatic indicator of ripening. 

Given the established roles of ABA and BR in non-climacteric fruit pigmentation, we monitored their endogenous levels under ETH treatment. BR content demonstrated a biphasic response, characterised by an initial decrease followed by recovery. Notably, significant increases were observed during the veraison to maturity stage (DAA 40) in comparison to the control group (Figure 1d). Conversely, ABA levels peaked earlier and subsequently declined, with significant elevations observed at the veraison stage (DAA 40–DAA 60) (Figure  1e). Transcriptional analysis of key biosynthetic pathways revealed that anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (VvDFR and VvUFGT) and the BR biosynthetic gene VvCPD displayed transient upregulation, with ETH-enhanced expression at the maturity stage (DAA 50–DAA 60) (Figure  1f). Progressive maturation led to the downregulation of VvDWF4 (a BR biosynthetic gene) and VvZEP/ABA1 (an ABA biosynthetic gene), effects that were attenuated by ETH treatment at DAA 60 (Figure 1f). The expression of VvVDE (an ABA biosynthetic gene) remained stable and was unaffected by ETH treatment (Figure  1f). These findings suggest that ET may coordinate with ABA and BR biosynthesis to enhance anthocyanin accumulation and colour development in grape berries. Subsequently, we replicated exogenous ETH treatment experiments in “Gamay” grape calli, and the results indicated that ETH treatment demonstrated the highest efficacy for intensified colouration and anthocyanin accumulation (Figure  1g,h). Meanwhile, these treatments also elevated BR and ABA levels (Figure 1i) and significantly upregulated the expressions of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (VvUFGT, VvDFR), BR biosynthetic genes (VvDWF4, VvCPD) and ABA biosynthetic genes (VvZEP/ABA1) (Figure 1j). 

# 2.2 | ET-Inducible VvERF045 Is Involved in Coordinating Anthocyanin Accumulation With BR/ABA Homeostasis During Grape Berry Ripening

To explore the ET-mediated regulatory network governing BR and ABA biosynthesis in anthocyanin accumulation, we conducted transcriptomic sequencing (RNA-Seq) analysis of exogenously ETH-treated grape berries at three time points: 10 days after treatment (DAA 40), 20 days after treatment (DAA 50) and 30 days after treatment (DAA 60). Untreated controls were analysed in parallel as a reference (Figure S1b). RNA-Seq revealed a total of 24,065 transcripts, including 4129 differentially expressed genes (DEGs), with 1050 upregulated and 3079 downregulated across developmental stages (Figure  S1c, Tables  S1 and S2). Quantification of stage-specific DEGs demonstrated dynamic expression patterns: 505 upregulated and 1545 downregulated at DAA 40 (ETH-2 vs. CK-2), 369 upregulated and 859 downregulated at DAA 50 (ETH-3 vs. CK-3) and 176 upregulated and 

675 downregulated at DAA 60 (ETH-4 vs. CK-4). Intersection analysis identified 65 conserved DEGs across all stages, which were enriched in metabolic processes as determined by Gene Ontology (GO) analysis (Figure S1d). 

Stage-specific pathway enrichment analysis revealed distinct temporal regulatory shifts: cyanidin 3-O-glucoside biosynthesis was predominant at DAA 40 (Figure  S2a), xyloglucan metabolism at DAA 50 (Figure  S2b) and fruit ripening/ET signalling at DAA 60 (Figure S2c). Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis indicated that anthocyanin biosynthesis was significantly enriched during the early stages (DAA 40–50; Figure  S3a,b), BR biosynthesis at DAA 50 and phytohormone signalling at DAA 60 (Figure S3c). Furthermore, ETH treatment significantly upregulated 19 genes associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis, particularly downstream regulators including VvUFGT, VvDFR, VvF3H, VvF3'H, VvF3H.2 and VvF3′5′H (Figure S4a). In contrast, it downregulated the majority of genes associated with BR biosynthesis and metabolism (10 genes) as well as ABA biosynthesis and metabolism (11 genes). Notably, this effect did not apply to VvCPD and VvDET2 (which are related to BR biosynthesis) or VvAAO3.2 and VvCYP707A1 (which are linked to ABA biosynthesis and metabolism), as shown in Figure  S4c,e. Finally, quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) validation confirmed the ETH-induced upregulation of VvUFGT, VvDFR, VvCPD, VvDWF4 and VvZEP/ABA1 during fruit ripening at DAA 60 (Figure  S4b,d,f). The integration of transcriptomic and quantitative data indicates that these genes may functionally associate with the biosynthesis pathways of anthocyanin, BR and ABA, acting as key regulatory nodes. These findings highlight their importance as prioritised targets for elucidating the underlying transcriptional regulatory mechanisms. 

Weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) revealed the clustering of key biosynthetic genes: VvUFGT/VvDFR/VvCPD within the palevioletred3 module and VvDWF4/VvZEP/ABA1 in the dark-orange module (Figure 1k). Notably, VvERF045, which was identified as a DEG in the CK-4 vs. CK-1 comparison group, was characterised as a hub gene in the cyan module and exhibited strong correlations with both anthocyanin and BR biosynthesis pathways (Figure  1k, Tables  S3 and S4). This hub gene was notably distinguished from other DEGs belonging to the ERF TFs family within the same transcriptome and was further validated through qRT-PCR analysis (Figure 1l, Figure S4g). Phylogenetic analysis classified VvERF045 into ERF subgroup IV (Figure S5a,b), which is characterised by the presence of a conserved AP2 domain (Figure S5c). This ERF family TF contains an AP2/ERF DNAbinding domain (amino acids 88–151) and three functional motifs (Figure  S5d,e). Notably, unlike most other members of subgroup IV, VvERF045 demonstrated consistent and sustained ET-responsive upregulation across all developmental stages (Figure  S5f) and exhibited maturation-associated expression specificity in grape berries (Figure S5g). The nuclear localisation of the VvERF045-YFP fusion protein was verified in Nicotiana benthamiana (N. benthamiana) epidermal cells (Figure  S5h). Dual-LUC assays confirmed that ETH activated the VvERF045 promoter (Figure 1m,n), with a concurrent rapid accumulation of the corresponding protein observed within 20–40 min post-ETH treatment (Figure 1o). Collectively, these findings indicate VvERF045 as a critical ET-responsive transcriptional regulator

# 2.3 | VvERF045 Functions as a Central Regulatory Hub Integrating Anthocyanin Biosynthesis With BR/ABA Metabolic Circuitry

To systematically elucidate the functional mechanism of VvERF045 in regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis and phytohormone metabolism, we conducted gain- and loss-of-function assays using an optimised Agrobacterium-mediated grape berry injection. Transient overexpression (OE) of VvERF045 via grape berry injection significantly enhanced pigmentation (Figure 2a), which was associated with increased accumulation of anthocyanins (Figure 2b), BR (Figure 2c) and ABA (Figure 2d). Conversely, silencing of VvERF045 induced reciprocal effects. Transcriptlevel analysis revealed that the overexpression of VvERF045 led to the co-activation of genes involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis (VvC4H, VvCHI, VvF3'H, VvDFR, VvUFGT), BR biosynthesis (VvDWF4, VvCPD, VvDET2, VvROT3, VvBR6OX1) and ABA biosynthesis (VvZEP/ABA1, VvNCED2, VvAAO3.3). Concurrently, genes implicated in BR catabolism (VvBAS1) and ABA catabolism (VvCYP707A1) were downregulated. In contrast, silencing of VvERF045 reversed these gain or inhibitory effects, respectively (Figure 2e; Figure S6a–c). Subsequent functional validation experiments employing grape stem-mediated agroinfiltration for transient overexpression and silencing unequivocally confirmed the pivotal regulatory role of VvERF045 in modulating pericarp pigmentation dynamics (Figure  2f,g). This phenotypic progression was temporally synchronised with significant fluctuations in phytohormonal levels (BR and ABA) and anthocyanin accumulation patterns (Figure  2h–j). 

Meanwhile, validation through qRT-PCR revealed coordinated transcriptional activation cascades in VvERF045-OE grape tissues. These cascades included key gene clusters associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis (VvDFR, VvUFGT, VvPAL, VvC4H, VvCHI and VvLAR), BR biosynthesis (VvDWF4, VvROT3, VvBR6OX1 and VvCPD), and ABA biosynthesis (VvZEP/ABA1, VvNCED2 and VvAAO3.3). Conversely, phytohormone catabolic regulators such as VvBAS1 and VvCYP707A1/2 were significantly downregulated. Notably, reciprocal expression patterns were observed in VvERF045-silenced samples, indicating transcriptional inversion of these metabolic pathways (Figure  2k; Figure S7a–c). These findings were highly consistent with those derived from parallel grape berry injection assays, thereby reinforcing the mechanistic coherence of VvERF045-mediated grape pigmentation regulation. 

Consistent regulatory patterns were observed in both “Vitis heyneana Roem. et Schult” leaves (Figure S8a) and “Thompson Seedless” seedling leaves (Figure  S8e). Overexpression of VvERF045 was found to increase the levels of BR and ABA (Figure S8b,c,f,g), while concurrently activating orthologous target genes involved in anthocyanin, BR and ABA biosynthesis, including VqUFGT/VvUFGT, VqDFR/VvDFR, VqDWF4/VvDWF4, VqCPD/VvCPD and VqZEP/ABA1/VvZEP/ABA1. Conversely, silencing of VvERF045 reversed these effects, resulting in a significant reduction in BR and ABA levels, as well as downregulation of the target gene expressions (Figure S8d,h). 

While transient genetic manipulation provides initial functional insights, the development of stable transgenic systems remains critical for rigorous mechanistic validation. # 2.6 | Heterodimeric VvERF045-VvMYB62 Cooperatively Enhances Anthocyanin Accumulation and Phytohormone Biosynthesis

Subsequently, our investigation into the molecular interplay between VvERF045 and VvMYB62 has uncovered three key mechanistic insights. The initial domain characterisation of the confirmed interactors (Figure S17a) demonstrated that VvMYB62 contains dual SANT domains, a hallmark feature of R2R3-MYB TFs. This conserved architectural feature suggests a synergistic functional interaction, possibly enabling cooperative regulation of downstream targets via heterodimer formation with VvERF045 (Figure S17b). Functional interrogation via transient overexpression in “Gamay” grape calli elucidated the regulatory capacity of VvMYB62 in secondary metabolism. Notably, the overexpression of VvMYB62 resulted in the coordinated upregulation of key anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (VvDFR, VvUFGT, VvANR) and phenylpropanoid pathway components (VvPAL1, Vv4CL, VvCHI, VvC4H), thereby confirming its role as a multilevel metabolic activator (Figure  S17c–e). Furthermore, this regulatory spectrum extends beyond secondary metabolism to encompass phytohormone biosynthesis, as evidenced by the significant induction of BR-related VvDWF4 and ABA-associated VvZEP/ ABA1 (Figure  S17f). The paradox stemming from VvMYB62's inability to intrinsically activate transcription in yeast systems (Figure S17g), coupled with the lack of binding to target promoters as observed in Y1H assays (Figure S17h), indicates that this MYB transcription factor may regulate downstream phenotypes via non-autonomous mechanisms. Specifically, we hypothesise 

that VvMYB62 achieves functional specificity through cooperative interactions with auxiliary transcriptional regulators, such as VvERF045, potentially forming binary complexes to enhance its regulatory influence on target gene networks. 

Next, through combinatorial analysis utilising Dual-LUC assays in N. benthamiana, we elucidated a synergistic coactivation mechanism. The VvERF045-VvMYB62 binary complex significantly enhanced the transcriptional activation of both anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (VvDFR, VvUFGT) and phytohormone pathway biosynthetic components (VvCPD, VvDWF4, VvZEP/ABA1), as evidenced by a marked increase in luminescence compared to individual effector controls (Figure 6f–p). This cooperative enhancement was phenocopied in homologous plants through the co-injection of VvERF045 and VvMYB62 in grape berry. Notably, the coordinated overexpression substantially elevated the transcript levels of target SGs, including VvUFGT and VvZEP/ABA1 (Figure S18a–h). 

Collectively, these findings suggest that VvERF045 serves as a central regulator coordinating anthocyanin accumulation with BR/ABA biosynthesis through dynamic interactions with VvMYB62. The elucidated heterodimer-mediated co-activation mechanism offers a novel regulatory framework for comprehending metabolic crosstalk between specialised metabolites and phytohormone signalling pathways. 

# 2.7 | VvERF045 Integrates ET Perception Through the Coordination of BR and ABA Pathways to Regulate Berry Pigmentation

Our systematic dissection of phytohormonal crosstalk downstream of VvERF045-mediated regulation revealed a 

sophisticated temporal coordination network governing anthocyanin metabolism. Building on the established role of VvERF045 as a central regulator of anthocyanin/BR/ABA biosynthesis, we first demonstrated that exogenous treatments with 24-epibrassinolide (EBL) and ABA at DAA 40 synergistically enhanced berry pigmentation in “Muscat Hamburg” grapes from DAA 47 to DAA 75, with the EBL + ABA combination exhibiting significant synergistic potentiation (Figure S19a). Targeted metabolic profiling identified the concerted upregulation of key anthocyanin derivatives, including malvidin-3-O-glycoside, peonidin-3-O-glycoside, delphinidin-3-O-glycoside, cyanidin-3-O-glycoside, and petunidin-3-O-glycoside, along with their respective −3-O-rutinoside forms, at both DAA 47 and DAA 75. The highest accumulation levels were detected under combinatorial treatment conditions (Figure  S19b–k; Table  S5). Intriguingly, the profiling of phytohormones across different developmental stages revealed distinct temporal dynamics and patterns. Endogenous BR levels displayed progressive accumulation associated with ripening, while ABA levels peaked at mid-veraison (DAA 55) before declining. Both phytohormones exhibited treatment-induced elevation at DAA 47, DAA 55 and DAA 75 (Figure S19l–o). Transcriptional network analysis identified stage-specific regulatory patterns. Specifically, EBL and ABA were found to upregulate VvERF045 and its direct targets (VvUFGT, VvCPD, VvZEP/ABA1) at DAA 47, while downregulating VvDFR and VvDWF4 at DAA 75 (Figure  S19p,q). Furthermore, integrated RNA-Seq and qRT-PCR analyses demonstrated the activation of the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway at DAA 55, as evidenced by the upregulation of genes such as VvCHI, VvLDOX and VvCHS3. Concurrently, these analyses demonstrated the repression of BR metabolism, as evidenced by the downregulation of VvBAS1 at DAA 55, and ABA catabolism, as indicated by the suppression of VvVDE at DAA 47 (Figure S19r–u). These results suggest that although exogenous ABA and BR treatments successfully enhance the endogenous levels of their respective phytohormones, they may also exert inhibitory effects on the expression of key metabolic genes within these pathways. 

Moreover, a developmental time-course analysis (DAA 20– 90) revealed sequential ET, ABA and BR activation in nonclimacteric grapes, with ET peaking first (DAA 40), followed by ABA (DAA 60) and BR (DAA 80) (Figure  S20a–d). This temporal hierarchy implies that ET may serve as a priming signal to coordinate the downstream biosynthesis of BR and ABA, potentially initiating anthocyanin biosynthesis. These findings are consistent with emerging evidence positioning ET as a crucial regulatory factor in the ripening of non-climacteric fruits (Perotti et  al.  2023). Subsequently, experimental validation using “Gamay” calli treated exogenously with EBL, ABA and ETH further supports the presence of this regulatory sequence. Notably, the ETH treatment demonstrated superior efficacy compared to EBL/ABA in enhancing pigmentation and promoting anthocyanin accumulation (Figure  S21a,b). Additionally, all three treatments significantly enhanced BR and ABA levels, with ETH demonstrating a relatively more pronounced effect (Figure  S21c). Mechanistically, the three phytohormones synergistically upregulate VvERF045 and its interacting partner VvMYB62 while coordinately activating biosynthetic genes involved in anthocyanin (VvUFGT, VvDFR), BR (VvDWF4, VvCPD) and ABA (VvZEP/ABA1) pathways (Figure  S21d,e). 

Notably, extensive evidence supports ET's role as a critical regulator that induces broad-spectrum upregulation of genes associated with BR biosynthesis and metabolism (VvROT3, VvDET2, VvBAS1), ABA biosynthetic and metabolic pathways (VvVDE, VvAAO3.2, VvABAH4, VvCYP707A2) and anthocyanin biosynthetic enzymes (VvC4H, Vv4CL, VvF3H, VvF3'H, VvLDOX) (Figure S22a–c). Collectively, these findings delineate a hierarchical regulatory network wherein ET-initiated signalling orchestrates subsequent BR and ABA activation through VvERF045-mediated transcriptional regulation, thereby synergistically promoting anthocyanin accumulation. 

Next, we performed a comprehensive mechanistic dissection utilising chemical genetic perturbation methods to elucidate the critical regulatory function of VvERF045 in the integration of metabolic and phytohormonal processes. Targeted inhibition of BR and ABA biosynthesis pathways via brassinazole (BRz) and fluridone in VvERF045-OE calli led to pronounced phenotypic attenuation. This was evidenced by a marked reduction in pigmentation intensity (Figure  7a,b) and comprehensive suppression of anthocyanin, BR and ABA contents (Figure  7c–f). Transcriptional profiling revealed substantial downregulation of key biosynthetic components, including VvERF045 itself, as well as VvUFGT and VvDFR (involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis), VvDWF4 and VvCPD (associated with BR biosynthesis) and VvZEP/ABA1 (pertinent to ABA production) (Figure 7g–r). Collectively, these findings provide preliminary evidence that VvERF045 relies on ABA and BR for coordinating ET-induced anthocyanin accumulation. 

Furthermore, the analysis of transcriptional dynamics revealed a temporally ordered transcriptional cascade among target genes following ETH treatment. Specifically, VvERF045 exhibited a pronounced peak in expression at 1 h post-induction, which preceded the sequential activation of phytohormone biosynthetic genes, such as VvCPD (at 2 h), VvZEP/ABA1 (at 2 h), and VvDWF4 (at 3 h). Subsequently, key genes involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, including VvDFR and VvUFGT, exhibited significant upregulation at 7 h post-treatment (Figure  S23a,b). Concurrently, this hierarchical activation pattern was functionally recapitulated at the phenotypic level, as ETH treatment elicited increases in pigmentation and anthocyanin accumulation, as well as BR and ABA biosynthesis in both wild-type (WT) and VvERF045-OE calli (Figures  S23c–f and S24a–c). Simultaneously, these phenotypic changes were observed to coincide with the transcriptional upregulation of VvMYB62 (an interacting partner gene of VvERF045), as well as the target genes involved in the anthocyanin, BR and ABA pathways, including VvUFGT, VvDFR, VvDWF4, VvCPD and VvZEP/ ABA1 (Figures S23g,h and S24d,e). In contrast, treatment with 1-methylcyclopropene (1-MCP), an inhibitor of ET signalling, completely abolished the effects induced by ETH, significantly reducing the expression levels of both primary target genes and additional regulators such as Vv4CL and VvLDOX involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, VvAAO3.2 in ABA biosynthesis, and VvDET2 and VvROT3 in BR homeostasis regulation (Figures S23g–j and S24d–g). Collectively, the integrated analysis of transcriptional and metabolic networks demonstrates that ET regulates anthocyanin biosynthesis and further modulates the role of VvERF045 in anthocyanin production with the biosynthesis of phytohormones, specifically BR and ABA.

# 3 | Discussion

Grape berry quality enhancement represents a significant challenge in horticultural production (Alston and Sambucci  2019) and is substantially influenced by phytohormonal regulation. Although ABA primarily governs ripening processes in 

non-climacteric grapes (Pilati et  al.  2017), recent studies have underscored the importance of BRs as critical regulators of anthocyanin biosynthesis (Leng et al. 2018). Pre-veraison applications of ABA significantly enhance anthocyanin accumulation via transcriptional activation of key SGs, including VvUFGT and VvDFR, as well as ABA biosynthetic genes (VvNCED1/2) and regulatory factors (VvMYBA1/2) (Wang, Brouard, et  al.  2022; Li et al. 2021). Similarly, BR signalling upregulates genes such as VvCHI1, VvF3′5′H and VvMYB5a/b (Zhou et al. 2018), with both pathways converging on light-responsive factors (VvHY5, VvCOP1) and BR-associated TFs (VvBZR1, VvBIN2) to accelerate colouration (Yang et  al.  2023; Kondo et  al.  2024). Our findings further validate the essential role of ABA/BR-induced upregulation of VvUFGT and VvDFR in promoting anthocyanin enrichment (Figure  S19). Despite these advancements, the exact mechanistic interaction between ABA and BR in the context of pigmentation remains unclear and warrants further investigation. 

Intriguingly, ET, traditionally associated with climacteric fruit ripening, significantly promotes grape pigmentation by activating SGs such as VvUFGT and VvDFR (El-Kereamy et al. 2003; Liu et  al.  2016; Wang, Ge, et  al.  2022). The AP2/ERF superfamily,

While prior studies have highlighted the roles of ERFs in disease resistance (e.g., VvERF16; Zhu et  al.  2022) and sugar metabolism (e.g., VvERF105; Lu et al. 2025), our findings demonstrate that VvERF045 serves as a pivotal regulator integrating ABA and BR biosynthesis into ETresponsive anthocyanin accumulation. Notably, our comparative functional profiling reveals the distinct functional specialisation of VvERF045 in relation to its paralogue VvERF003, a previously characterised activator of the monoterpene biosynthetic pathway (Wang, Wei, et  al.  2024), the stress-responsive homologue PndERF045 (Chen et al. 2019) and the sugar accumulation regulator CitSAR (Liu et al. 2025). In this study, our comprehensive and multi-dimensional characterisation identifies VvERF045 as a dual-functional regulatory hub that orchestrates anthocyanin biosynthesis through evolutionarily conserved mechanisms (Figures 2–7). This function is distinct from that of VvERF4, which has been shown to suppress pigmentation (Jia et al. 2023), thereby providing further evidence supporting the functional diversification within the ERF transcription factor family. Specifically, first, it directly activates the promoters of anthocyanin SGs (VvUFGT, VvDFR), BR biosynthetic genes (VvDWF4, VvCPD) and ABA 

pathway components (VvZEP/ABA1) via dual GCC-box/DRE core motif binding (Figure  5). Functionally, transgenic models exhibit dose-dependent activation of the target genes involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis and phytohormonal pathways, particularly BR and ABA (Figures 2–4). Notably, in heterologous tomato VvERF045-OE transgenic lines, BR-mediated developmental phenotypes such as dwarfism and fruit enlargement were observed, indicating the integrative role of VvERF045 in modulating the BR pathway to enhance anthocyanin accumulation (Figures S10 and S11). Second, VvERF045 recruits VvMYB62 to assemble synergistic transcriptional modules (Figure  6), thereby mirroring ERF-MYB partnerships observed in anthocyanin biosynthesis and stress responses in citrus and pear (Wang, Li, et al. 2024; Ni et al. 2021), yet uniquely integrating anthocyanin regulation with phytohormone biosynthesis. This innovative discovery extends the canonical ERF-MYB functional paradigm beyond anthocyanin biosynthesis and stress regulation, incorporating phytohormone biosynthesis and thereby unveiling a novel mechanism for systemic metabolic coordination. 

Phytohormones coordinate plant physiological processes through dynamic crosstalk networks rather than isolated actions (Munné-Bosch and Müller 2013). In fleshy fruits, synergistic interactions among CTKs, IAA, GAs, BRs, jasmonates 

(JA), ABA and ET govern developmental processes and quality traits (Fuentes et al. 2019; Tipu and Sherif 2024). Strikingly divergent patterns are observed in ET signalling when it interacts with ABA and BR across climacteric and non-climacteric fruit systems. In non-climacteric strawberry fruits, a reciprocal transcriptional regulation between ABA and ET occurs via crosstalk involving the AREB/ABF-ACS/ACO pathways, as reported by Bai et  al.  (2021). By contrast, in climacteric tomato fruits, BR and ET exhibit synergistic interactions through the coordinated upregulation of ET biosynthesis genes induced by exogenous EBR treatment (LeACS, LeACO and LePSY1; Zhu et  al.  2015). These species-specific signalling architectures likely represent evolutionary adaptations of phytohormonal regulation to optimise divergent ripening strategies. However, fundamental questions remain regarding the integration mechanisms governing anthocyanin regulation through crosstalk among ET, ABA and BR, particularly in non-climacteric fruit systems. To address this knowledge gap, our study indicates ET's critical role as the primary initiator of grape pigmentation via VvERF045-mediated synchronisation of ABA and BR pathways. We further delineate a three-step regulatory cascade wherein ET sequentially activates ABA and BR biosynthesis to enhance grape anthocyanin production. Functional validation using chemical genetics demonstrates complete suppression of pigmentation and target gene expression in VvERF045-OE plant materials treated with ABA and BR biosynthesis inhibitors (Figure 7). Conversely, the blockade of ET perception using 1-MCP abolishes both pigment accumulation and the biosynthesis of ABA and BR, suggesting that ET may act as the apex regulator of this trihormonal network (Figures S23 and S24). 

In summary, our study elucidates VvERF045 as the central coordinator that integrates ET perception with ABA and BR biosynthesis, thereby precisely modulating grape berry pigmentation (Figure 8). This mechanistic framework significantly advances the understanding of non-climacteric fruit biology by offering four key insights: First, ET orchestrates the ABA and BR pathways in a hierarchical manner through sequential activation. Second, the phased induction of phytohormone biosynthesis ensures an adequate supply of precursors prior to anthocyanin biosynthesis. Third, VvERF045 evolutionarily links phytohormonal coordination with the regulation of fruit quality traits. Finally, the exogenous application of ETH can leverage the VvERF045 regulatory network to precisely modulate anthocyanin accumulation. This approach offers a targeted alternative to broadspectrum hormone treatments, potentially enhancing pigment uniformity and thereby addressing consumer-oriented demands for improved colouration in table grapes. To further substantiate this regulatory hierarchy, future research should prioritise the genetic manipulation of key ET signalling components, such as VvERF045, VvEIN3 and VvEBF1, to establish a robust mechanistic basis for innovative grape cultivation strategies.

# 4.2 | Exogenous ETH, ABA, EBL and Plant Growth Regulators (PGRs) Treatments

Eight-year-old “Ruidu Hongyu” (early-maturing) and three-yearold “Muscat Hamburg” (late-maturing) grapevines under archshed cultivation were sprayed with $5 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ethephon (ETH; Sigma) or $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ (control) one week pre-veraison. Berries were sampled at pre-treatment (DAA 30 (E-L 33) and DAA 40 (E-L 33) for “Ruidu Hongyu” and “Muscat Hamburg”, respectively), 10/7days post-treatment (DAA 40 (E-L 35)/DAA 47 (E-L 35)), 20/15days post-treatment (DAA 50 (E-L 36)/DAA 55 (E-L 36)) and 30/23days post-treatment (DAA 60 (E-L 37)/DAA 63 (E-L 37)) (Coombe 1995). Three biological replicates, each consisting of 100 berries, were immediately flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent RNA-seq analysis and physiological assays. “Muscat Hamburg” grapevines (five-year-old) were treated with the following solutions: $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ epibrassinolide (EBL; Sigma), $5 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ABA (Sigma), and a combined solution of $\mathrm { E B L + A B A }$ . All solutions were prepared by dissolving the respective compounds in ethanol. Grape berries were harvested at DAA 40 (pre-treatment, E-L 33), DAA 47 (7days post-treatment, E-L 34), DAA 55 (15days post-treatment, E-L 35) and DAA 75 (35days post-treatment, E-L 38). For each replicate, five berries per replicate (totalling 100 berries per treatment) were photographed prior to being frozen at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for further analysis. “Gamay” WT calli were infiltrated with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ETH, EBL, or ABA, respectively. VvERF045-OE and WT calli were subjected to treatments with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ BRz, fluridone, or 1-MCP. All compounds were dissolved in an infiltration buffer containing 10mM MES (pH5.2) and 10mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , using either ethanol or $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ as the solvent. Following treatments, the calli were cultured on B5 medium under controlled conditions $( 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , dark) for 10days prior to harvest. Three independent biological replicates were conducted. 

# 4.3 | Illumina Sequencing and Transcriptome Data Analysis

Transcriptomes of “Ruidu Hongyu” berries from ETH-treated and control groups were sequenced at four developmental stages (DAA 30, DAA 40, DAA 50 and DAA 60). Three biological 

replicates were prepared for each group: the ETH-treated group (designated as ETH-2 to ETH-4) and the control group (designated as CK-1 to CK-4). Additionally, transcriptomes of “Muscat Hamburg” berries under EBL, ABA, and combined EBL+ABA treatments were sequenced at four developmental stages (DAA 40, 47, 55 and 75). Three biological replicates were included for each treatment group: EBL group (B1 at DAA 47, B2 at DAA 55, B3 at DAA 75), the ABA group (A1 at DAA 47, A2 at DAA 55, A3 at DAA 75) and the control group (C0 at DAA 40, C1 at DAA 47, C2 at DAA 55, C3 at DAA 75). RNA integrity was assessed using NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometry and the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer. RNA sequencing was conducted in accordance with standardised protocols by OEbiotech. DEGs were identified based on stringent criteria $\mathrm { | l o g } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C | } > 1$ , false discovery rate [FDR] $< 0 . 0 5 \AA ,$ . Functional enrichment analysis included GO annotation, KEGG pathway mapping and heatmap visualisation. The RNA-seq data involved in this study have been deposited in the NCBI BioProject database under the following accession numbers: PRJNA1271993 and PRJNA1275011. 

# 4.4 | RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR Analysis

Total RNA was extracted using the RNA Prep Pure Plant Plus Kit (TaKaRa, Dalian, China). The quality of the RNA was assessed using a NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). cDNA synthesis was carried out using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit with gDNA Eraser (TaKaRa, Dalian, China). Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) was performed on the CFX Connect System (Bio-Rad, CA, USA) under the following cycling conditions: initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20s, followed by 39cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15s, $5 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15s and $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15s. Gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method. Primers were designed with the assistance of the qPrimerDB Database (https://qprimerdb.biodb.org/) and are listed in Table S6.

# 4.15 | GST Pull-Down Assay

GST-VvMYB62 and His-VvERF045 were heterologously expressed in BL21(DE3) cells after induction with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 8 \mathrm { h }$ . The recombinant proteins were subsequently purified using GST/His resins (Beyotime, SHN) and concentrated to appropriate concentrations. GST pull-down assays were performed according to the manufacturer's instructions provided with the GST Pull-down Kit (ACE, SHN). Specifically, GST-VvMYB62 immobilised on glutathione beads was incubated with the lysate containing His-VvERF045 under the following conditions: $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.4), 150 mM NaCl, $0 . 5 \%$ NP-40, and protease inhibitors at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 h. The beads were then sequentially washed with lysis buffer, 500 mM NaCl solution and PBS to remove non-specifically bound proteins. Finally, the samples were subjected to SDS-PAGE separation followed by immunoblotting analysis using anti-His and anti-GST antibodies (Beyotime, SHN). The molecular weights of the proteins are summarised in Table S8. 

# 4.16 | Overexpressed Plant Construction

Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 $( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8 )$ , carrying the PHB-VvERF045-YFP or PHB-YFP construct, was introduced into the following plant materials: “Muscat Hamburg” berries (DAA 40–50, corresponding to E-L stage 33–34) via direct injection or stem-mediated agroinfiltration; leaves of Vitis heyneana Roem. et Schult; and “Thompson Seedless” tissue culture through vacuum infiltration. For tomato transgenic experiments, “Alisa Craig” leaf explants were transformed with PHB-VvERF045-YFP or PHB-YFP constructs. Transformed tissues were selected on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium supplemented with $3 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ timentin, $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ hygromycin, and appropriate phytohormones. Regenerated T0 and T2 transgenic lines were subsequently characterised. Additionally, “Gamay” calli were co-cultured with A. tumefaciens GV3101 harbouring pRI101-VvERF045- eGFP. Selection of transformed calli was performed on B5 medium containing $3 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ cefotaxime, $3 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ carbenicillin and $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin. 

In the VIGS experiment, the pTRV2-VvERF045 plasmid was introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101 (pSoup19) via transformation. The resulting suspension, containing pTRV2-VvERF045 and pTRV1 (1:1), was infiltrated into grapevine stems, leaves, tissue-cultured seedlings and calli, and injected into berry epidermis. To emphasise the inhibitory effect of VvERF045 silencing on anthocyanin accumulation, berries for silencing were at an advanced maturity stage (47–52 days post-anthesis), compared to those for overexpression (40–45 days post-anthesis). Silenced plant materials were ground in liquid nitrogen for biochemical analysis. 

# 4.18 | ET Emission Measurement and BR/ABA Content Quantification

ET emission determination followed the methodology established in previous studies (Kaur et al. 2024). Ten grams of berries were placed in $3 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { m L }$ sealed vials. After an incubation period of 8 h, 5 mL of headspace gas was analysed using gas chromatography (GC) with the following conditions: Agilent 8890 system, PEG-20 M column at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , flame ionisation detector (FID) at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and carrier gas flow rate of $7 . 6 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ . The ET emission rate was calculated as nL/(kg·h) fresh weight (FW). Each assay included three biological replicates. The ABA concentration was determined by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) using a C18 column, following established protocols (Li et  al.  2021). The results were expressed as $\mathrm { n g / g }$ FW. Each assay included three biological replicates. All reagents used for quantification were of chromatographic grade (ANPEL, China). BR concentrations were quantified using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), according to the manufacturer's instructions for a commercially available ELISA kit provided by Shanghai Yanqi Biotechnology Co. Ltd., China. The results were expressed as pg/g FW. Each assay included three technical replicates. 

# 4.19 | Anthocyanin Content Measurement

The pH differential method was employed to determine the total anthocyanin content in the skins of peeled grapes (Li et al. 2021). The absorbance measurements were taken at wavelengths of 530 and 657 nm using an HD-UV90A UV–Vis spectrophotometer. The calculation was conducted as $( \mathrm { A } 5 3 0 { \cdot } \mathrm { A } 6 5 7 ) / \mathrm { g }$ FW. Each treatment involved three biological replicates. Anthocyanin monomer quantification was carried out by LC– MS/MS (QTRAP 6500) according to the methodology outlined by Vega et al. (2025), with compound identification presented in Table S5. This analysis also included three biological replicates. 

# 4.20 | Bioinformatics Analysis

Hierarchical clustering of candidate SGs and TFs under exogenous ETH treatment was performed using TBtools (Chen et  al.  2023). Weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) correlated RNA-Seq profiles with metabolite 

contents across treatments. Co-expression networks for VvERFs-SGs and VvMYBs-SGs were constructed on the OE cloud platform (https://cloud.oebiotech.com/#/home), with visualisation and hub gene identification in Cytoscape (v3.9.1). From the 80 Vitis vinifera L. ERF family proteins identified in PlantTFDB, multiple sequence alignments were performed using MEGA 5.0 (ClustalW), and a maximum-likelihood phylogenetic tree was constructed with 1000 bootstrap replicates. Motif analysis used the MEME Suite, and domain characterisation used the SMART Suite. cis-Elements were predicted via PlantCARE and visualised with TBtools. Molecular docking utilised AlphaFold3-predicted structures with PyMOL visualisation.
