# ABSTRACT

Plant diseases cause enormous economic losses in agriculture and threaten global food security, and application of agrochemicals is an important method of crop disease control. Exploration of disease-resistance mechanisms and synthesis of highly bioactive agrochemicals are thus important research objectives. Here, we show that propranolol, a phosphatidate phosphatase (Pah) inhibitor, effectively suppresses fungal growth, sporulation, sexual reproduction, and infection of diverse plants. The MoPah1 enzyme activity of the rice blast fungus   is inhibited by propranolol. Alterations in lipid metabolism Magnaporthe oryzaeare associated with inhibited hyphal growth and appressorium formation caused by propranolol in . Propranolol inhibits a broad spectrum of 12 plant pathogens, effectively inhibiting infection of M. oryzaebarley, wheat, maize, tomato, and pear. To improve antifungal capacity, we synthesized a series of propranolol derivatives, one of which shows a 16-fold increase in antifungal ability and binds directly to MoPah1. Propranolol and its derivatives can also reduce the severity of rice blast and Fusarium head blight of wheat in the field. Taken together, our results demonstrate that propranolol suppresses fungal development and infection through mechanisms involved in lipid metabolism. Propranolol and its derivatives may therefore be promising candidates for fungicide development.

# INTRODUCTION

Plant diseases cause severe economic losses in crops, including rice, wheat, and maize (Meng et al., 2009; Pennisi, 2010). The global annual losses of crops from diseases are about $\$ 220$ billion, and the losses are often more serious in developing countries than in developed countries (Tampakaki et al., 2009). 

The fungal pathogen Magnaporthe oryzae causes rice blast, the most destructive disease of cultivated rice, resulting in annual

losses that are sufficient to feed about 60 million people worldwide (Fisher et al., 2012; Fernandez and Orth, 2018; Sha et al., 2023). Fusarium graminearum, the causal agent of Fusarium head blight (FHB) in wheat, not only causes serious yield losses but also contaminates the grain with mycotoxins that are harmful to humans and animals (Lofgren et al., 2018). The fungal pathogens Bipolaris maydis and Botrytis cinerea cause tan and rectangular lesions on maize and fungal rot on fruit, respectively (Williamson et al., 2007; Dean et al., 2012). Devastating crop diseases including those mentioned above seriously threaten global food security, and fungicide use is one of the important measures for control of these diseases (Steinberg and Gurr, 2020). Targeting virulence factors based on studies of pathogenesis is an important strategy for development of new fungicides, helping to mitigate fungicide resistance in pathogens (Leroux et al., 2002; Vielba-Fernandez et al., 2020). Thus, it is necessary to identify more fungicide candidates to better manage plant diseases, but whether the M. oryzae virulence factor MoPah1 can be used as a target for new fungicides is largely unknown. 

The appressorium of M. oryzae is a specialized infection structure that penetrates the plant cell wall by generating enormous turgor pressure (Xu and Hamer, 1996). During appressorium formation, glycerol and melanin are important for turgor pressure accumulation and full virulence (Foster et al., 2017). Tricyclazole, iprobenfos, and edifenphos are fungicides used to control rice blast by targeting melanin or choline biosynthesis (Uesugi, 2001; Kunova et al., 2013). Phosphatidic acid (PA) is an important phospholipid and can be converted into diacylglycerol (DAG) by phosphatidate phosphatase 1 (Pah1), which is highly conserved in eukaryotes and participates in fungal infection processes (Khondker et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022). Our previous results showed that deletion of MoPAH1 in M. oryzae causes accumulation of PA as well as defects in development and pathogenicity (Zhao et al., 2022). Propranolol, an inhibitor of Pah1, suppresses its enzyme activity by about $70 \%$ at $1 \mathsf { m M }$ in Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Han and Carman, 2010). In the human fungal pathogen Candida albicans, propranolol interrupts the yeast-to-hyphae transition by inhibiting the cAMP-EFG1 pathway (Baker et al., 2002). In medicine, propranolol has been used to treat Fusarium keratitis, a fungal infection of the cornea (Bao et al., 2021). However, whether propranolol can inhibit plant pathogens has not been investigated. 

Rationally designed chemical modification improves biological activity and systematically increases the antifungal activity of fungicides (Jesu´ s et al., 2018). In a previous study, a novel fungicidal candidate was synthesized using chemical modification based on natural quinine alkaloids and displayed significant fungicidal activities against four phytopathogenic fungi (Yang et al., 2019). Cinnamic acid synthesized with the 1,3,4-oxadiazole moiety has shown improved antibacterial activity against rice bacterial leaf streak and leaf blight (Wang et al., 2021b). Thus, chemical modification is a critical strategy for improving antifungal activity. 

In this study, propranolol was found to effectively inhibit multiple plant pathogens. We demonstrated that propranolol suppresses lipid metabolism by inhibiting MoPah1. Moreover, structural

In summary, propranolol and its derivatives represent potential broadspectrum fungicides for defense against a wide array of crop diseases. 

# RESULTS

# Propranolol suppresses development and plant infection of

To investigate the inhibitory effect of propranolol on development and infection of M. oryzae, we cultured four M. oryzae strains (P131, Guy11, 70-15, and ZB25) on plates supplemented with propranolol. As the concentration of propranolol increased, the colony diameter of the four strains gradually decreased (Figure 1A and 1B). Propranolol inhibited mycelial growth of the P131 strain by $76 \%$ at $2 . 0 \ : \ : \mathsf { m M }$ . Conidiation, conidial germination, and appressorium formation were effectively suppressed by propranolol at $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ , and appressorium formation was more sensitive to propranolol than conidial germination or hyphal growth (Figure 1C–1F). To observe the effect of propranolol on the infection process, we inoculated conidial suspensions with or without propranolol onto barley leaves. Invasive hyphae had spread extensively in epidermal cells in the control at 36 h post inoculation (hpi) (Figure 1G). By contrast, conidial suspensions supplemented with 0.1 mM propranolol showed limited infectious growth. Infection assays on susceptible rice seedlings demonstrated that lesion number was substantially reduced as propranolol concentration increased, and typical blast lesions were significantly reduced in the $1 . 0 \mathrm { ~ m M }$ propranolol treatment (Figure 1H and 1I). In addition, we investigated the effects of propranolol applications on plants 24 h before or after M. oryzae inoculation with conidial suspensions. The number of disease lesions was reduced when propranolol and compound treatments were applied before M. oryzae inoculation. However, disease control was less effective when these chemicals were applied after M. oryzae inoculation (Supplemental Figure 1). These results demonstrate that propranolol effectively inhibits mycelial growth, conidiation, conidial germination, appressorium formation, and plant infection of M. oryzae.

Propranolol effectively inhibits growth and infection of 12 plant pathogens 

We next evaluated the inhibitory effect of propranolol on different plant pathogens. Phylogenetic analysis and protein predictions showed that Pah1 proteins of 12 plant pathogens are conserved and contain the two important domains Lipin_N and LNS2 (Figure 4A; Supplemental Figure 3). The Pah1 proteins of Phytophthora infestans, Phytophthora sojae, and Rhizoctonia solani, like PAH1 from Homo sapiens, have an additional domain, Lipin_mid. We subsequently evaluated the inhibitory effects of propranolol on these plant pathogens. When different pathogens were grown on plates supplemented with $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ propranolol, their colony diameters were reduced by $8 0 \% - 1 0 0 \%$ . However, the inhibition rate of propranolol on P. infestans, P. sojae, and R. solani ranged from $5 0 \% { - } 7 0 \%$ , much lower than that on other pathogens (Figure 4B). Conidial germination of B. cinerea and F. graminearum was completely suppressed by propranolol at $1 . 5 \ \mathsf { m M }$ (Figure 4C). Sexual reproduction of F. graminearum, R. solani, and Sclerotinia sclerotiorum was also significantly suppressed by propranolol (Figure 4D and 4E). Infection assays with conidia or mycelial blocks on barley, wheat, maize, tomato, and pear supplemented with propranolol showed that propranolol effectively suppressed fungal infection on various hosts (Figure 4F and 4G). No lesions were observed on barley, wheat, and maize leaves when the inoculum was supplemented with propranolol at $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ , and there were limited lesions on tomato and pear leaves, demonstrating the broad-spectrum antifungal ability of propranolol toward diverse plant pathogens. 

Improvement of the inhibitory capacity of propranolol through molecular design and systematic chemical modifications 

Despite the potential utility of propranolol as a broad-spectrum antifungal agrochemical, its biological activity is below the standard required for commercial fungicides. To improve its bioactivity, we designed a series of propranolol derivatives according to computational design strategy and chemical modification theory. In total, 19 new compounds derived from propranolol containing various substituents were systematically synthesized (Figure 5A). We first changed the amino units with different primary amines and found that I-13, bearing a para-fluorobenzyl group, exhibited the best antifungal activity, effectively suppressing hyphal growth of M. oryzae at $0 . 1 \mathsf { m M }$ . Encouraged by this result, we then investigated the effect of phenol substituents, and the inhibitory capacity was further improved (Figure 5B and 5C). The concentrations for $50 \%$ of maximal effect $( \mathsf { E C } _ { 5 0 } )$ of propranolol, I-13, and II-8 were 982, 67, and $6 2 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ , respectively (Supplemental Figure 4). The improvement was supported by analyses of molecular van der Waals surface electrostatic potential (ESP) and total energy, in which compound II-8 displayed lower ESP than propranolol and compound I-13 (Figure 5D; Supplemental Figure 5). In addition, plant infection assays showed that compounds I-13 and II-8 effectively inhibited disease lesion formation on rice leaves and wheat stem bases (Figure 5E and 5F). 

Because compound II-8 displayed the best results, we used it for additional detailed assays. On plates, compound II-8 effectively inhibited vegetative growth of eight plant pathogens at $6 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ (Figure 6A and 6B). Propranolol and its derivatives reduce disease severity of rice blast and wheat head blight in the field 

Given that propranolol and its derivatives effectively suppressed plant infection in the greenhouse, their effectiveness was next 

evaluated under field conditions. We planted susceptible rice seedlings in a blast field nursery and treated them with propranolol $( 2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M } )$ ), compound II-8 (0.1 mM), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) $( 0 . 2 \% )$ , or water. The seedlings treated with water and DMSO displayed abundant typical blast lesions, but seedlings treated with propranolol and compound II-8 developed fewer lesions on leaves (Figure 7A and 7B). The rice blast disease indices of the groups treated with propranolol and compound II-8 were reduced by $1 6 \%$ and $2 2 \%$ , respectively, compared with that of the control group. We applied propranolol and compound II-8 to wheat twice to determine their effects on FHB. Under propranolol and compound II-8 treatments, the FHB disease indices were reduced by $7 9 \%$ and $84 \%$ , respectively (Figure 7C–7E), comparable to or even better than those achieved with commercial fungicides. Taken together, these results indicated that propranolol and compound II-8 could reduce the disease severity of rice blast and FHB under field conditions.

# DISCUSSION

Pah1, a member of the phosphatidic acid phosphatases, is essential for lipid homeostasis (Pascual and Carman, 2013; Khondker et al., 2022). Our previous study showed that MoPah1 is required for pathogenicity in M. oryzae (Zhao et al., 2022). Here, we sought to evaluate the antifungal ability of propranolol, a Pah1 inhibitor, and we observed that propranolol suppressed the enzyme activity of Pah1 and inhibited infection by 12 plant pathogens. 

Propranolol efficiently suppresses hyphal growth, conidial germination, and appressorium formation of M. oryzae (Figure 1A–1F), and appressorium formation is the most sensitive process. Similar results have been observed for the fungicides tricyclazole and isoprothiolane, which target the biosynthesis of melanin and lipids, respectively, both of which are critical for appressorium formation in M. oryzae (Li et al., 2012; Foster et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018; Koehler et al., 2021). The metabolism of lipid droplets and glycogen was blocked by propranolol in germinated conidia during appressorium formation (Figure 3A and 3B). Consistent with this result, many DEGs downregulated in the propranolol treatment were enriched in lipid metabolism, including DAK1, DGA1, and TGL1 genes that are involved in glycerolipid metabolism (Figure 3E and 3F) (Molin et al., 2003; Koffel et al.,€ 2005; Kamisaka et al., 2007). Similar results were observed in another lipid metabolism mutant, Mosnf1, which shows delayed lipid droplet and glycogen degradation and attenuated virulence (Zeng et al., 2014). Thus, our study, together with others, shows that propranolol inhibits infection processes through mechanisms involved in lipid metabolism. 

MoPah1 catalyzes the conversion of PA into DAG, and propranolol significantly inhibits MoPah1 activity, resulting in increased PA levels (Figure 3E) reminiscent of those in the Mopah1 mutant. These changes caused by propranolol in M. oryzae are also consistent with findings in other fungi and mammalian cells (Balboa et al., 1998; Fang et al., 2014; Montoya et al., 2019; Kwiatek et al., 2020). In addition, exogenous PA inhibits hyphal growth and conidiation (Supplemental Figure 8), indicating that PA accumulation caused by propranolol or mutation of MoPAH1 is one of the critical factors for inhibiting development of M. oryzae. We therefore conclude that propranolol alters lipid metabolism and inhibits fungal development by inhibiting the enzyme activity of MoPah1. 

Propranolol has broad-spectrum antifungal activity against 12 pathogens, including M. oryzae (Figure 4). Propranolol also inhibits the yeast-to-hyphae transition of C. albicans and the virulence of Fusarium (Baker et al., 2002; Derengowski Lda et al., 2009; Bao et al., 2021). The above results are consistent with phylogenetic analyses showing that Pah1 proteins are highly conserved in many plant pathogens. The Lipin_N and LNS2 

domains of Pah1, which are critical for its enzyme activity, are targeted by propranolol, potentially via residues Ser24 and Gly479 (Figure 2A and 2B). Therefore, the highly conserved Pah1 proteins and the similar infection processes of different pathogens support the broad-spectrum inhibitory activity of propranolol. 

Our results showed that the inhibitory activity of compound II-8 was 16-fold higher than that of propranolol, and II-8 displayed greater inhibition of plant pathogen growth than the commercial fungicide boscalid (Figure 5B and 5C; Supplemental Figure 10). Compound II-8 contains two modified moieties, 4-fluorobenzylamine and 2,4-dichlorophenol, making it more stable, a possible factor in its increased inhibitory efficiency (Figure 5A and 5D; Supplemental Figure 9). Similar results have also been observed in other chemicals, including agrochemicals, modified with 4- fluorobenzylamine and 2,4-dichlorophenol and have been attributed to the high electronegativity and small atomic radius of fluorine atoms (Swallow, 2015). Results of the chemical modifications of propranolol also provide a potential target on the benzene ring for further modifications for commercial uses. 

There are multiple strategies to minimize the safety concerns associated with agricultural use of propranolol, which is already used in medicine. First, human and plant PAH1 homologs have an additional protein domain, Lipin_mid, which may make propranolol less effective and minimize its possible side effects on these organisms. This is evidenced by the fact that inhibition by propranolol is weaker for pathogens whose Pah1 homologs contain the Lipin_mid domain than for those whose Pah1 homologs lack it (Figure 4B; Supplemental Figure 3). Second, we used chemical modifications to synthesize new compounds in the hope of further minimizing possible side effects of propranolol. This strategy has been used for the human medicine tetrandrine, which was chemically modified to reduce its side effects (Mo et al., 2022). Third, the rapid development of novel nanomaterials also enables efficient and smart delivery of fungicides into plants to minimize possible environmental contamination (Liang W et al., 2021). For example, an enzyme-responsive lignin nanocarrier has been used to control grapevine diseases with a minimal amount of fungicide and few side effects, because the fungicide in the nanocarrier is released only when the nanocarrier is degraded by enzymes secreted by the pathogen (Fischer et al., 2019). Finally, appropriate application concentrations can be a practical strategy to reduce safety concerns. The combination of two or more fungicides is an efficient and commonly used strategy to reduce the concentration of each fungicide in controlling crop diseases (Persaud et al., 2021). For example, the combination of fenoxanil and isoprothiolane is more effective against rice blast than tricyclazole alone (Singh et al., 2011). Taken together, chemical modification, novel nanomaterials, and appropriate application practices are good strategies to address possible safety concerns.

Nevertheless, there are some limitations to this study. Possible off-target effects of the fungicide candidate should be investigated further. Although our results demonstrated that compound II-8 binds to MoPah1 and that propranolol and compound II-8 show attenuated inhibition on the Mopah1 mutant, whether compound II-8 can bind to other proteins is unknown. The propranolol-interacting residues of MoPah1 require additional investigation, which will facilitate rational modifications of propranolol. In addition, before launching propranolol and its derivatives into the market, more field trials are needed, and this is the next focus of our research work. 

In conclusion, the phosphatidate phosphatase inhibitor propranolol can effectively control plant diseases and also serve as a precursor for the improvement of antifungal compounds by chemical modification. Given the common occurrence of chemical resistance to fungicides among plant pathogens, the design and development of new environmentally friendly chemicals would be beneficial. Synthesizing propranolol and experimentally evaluating the effects of its derivatives are time-consuming and expensive tasks, and computer-aided molecular design can 

greatly facilitate this process and reduce its cost (Chai et al., 2022). This approach can be further aided by the deep-learning tools AlphaFold 2 and RoseTTAFold, which predict high-quality protein structures (Senior et al., 2020; Baek et al., 2021; Jumper et al., 2021). Integrated use of these cutting-edge tools and application practices will direct future rational modification of propranolol to reach potential commercial values and reduce possible safety concerns.

# Plant infection assays

Six-day-old barley seedlings of cultivar E9 and 2-week-old rice seedlings of cultivar CO39 were used for plant infection assays as described previously (Qi et al., 2016). In brief, conidia of M. oryzae strain Guy11 were harvested from 10-day-old OMA plates. Conidial suspensions ( $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml) were drop-inoculated or sprayed evenly onto plants with or without different concentrations of propranolol. Infected plants were maintained in a humidity chamber at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ . Lesions on rice and barley leaves were examined at 7 and 5 dpi, respectively. The GFP-tagged M. oryzae strain was used for infection assays on barley leaves, and invasive hyphae were observed under a confocal microscope (Leica TCS SP8, Leica Microsystems, Germany). 

Infection assays for other fungal pathogens were performed as described previously. In brief, conidial suspensions of B. cinerea $( 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ ml), B. maydis ( $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml), B. sorokiniana $( 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml), and F. graminearum ( $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml) were inoculated with or without 1.5 mM propranolol on 30-day-old maize (B73), 5-day-old wheat (Huamai 8) leaves and stem bases, and mature tomato fruit at $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathtt { C }$ in a humidity chamber. Symptoms were examined at 5 dpi. 

# Protein structure prediction and molecular docking

AlphaFold 2 was used to predict the three-dimensional structure of Mo-Pah1 (David et al., 2022). The chemical structure of propranolol was retrieved from the PubChem Compound database. The propranolol ligand and the MoPah1 receptor were removed from water molecules and hydrogenated, and molecular docking was performed with Autodock and PyMOL as described previously (Seeliger and de Groot, 2010). 

# Protein purification and enzyme activity assays

Glutathione S-transferase (GST)-tagged protein purification was performed as described previously (Schafer et al., 2015€ ). In brief, full-length MoPAH1 was cloned into the pGEX-6p-1 plasmid with the One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme, China), and the resultant construct was transformed into Escherichia coli BL21(DE3). Expression of the MoPah1-GST fusion protein was induced with $1 . 0 \ \mathsf { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-thiogalactoside (IPTG) in Luria broth medium at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 12 h. E. coli cells were centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 10 min, and the supernatant was discarded. The cell pellet was suspended and ultrasonically disrupted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) buffer. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 10 min, the supernatant was subjected to purification with GST NUPharose 4B (Nuptec, China). The MoPah1 protein was washed with PBS buffer and eluted with elution buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl, 10 mM glutathione, pH 8.0). 

MoPah1 enzyme activity was measured as described previously with minor modifications (Kimura et al., 2022). Purified MoPah1 proteins were incubated with the substrate 1-oleoyl-2-{12-[(7-nitro-2-1,3-benzoxadiazol-4-yl) amino] dodecanoyl}-sn-glycero-3-phosphate (NBD-PA, $5 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ (Avanti Polar Lipids, USA) in 1 mL of assay buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl [pH 7.5], 1 mM ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 2 \%$ Triton X-100) at $_ { 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 90 min. To extract total lipids, 15 mL of chloroform/methanol (2:1, v/v) was added to the sample and vortexed for 5 min. Then 2 mL of water was added to the sample and vortexed for 5 min. After centrifugation at 1000 rpm for 5 min, the upper phase was discarded, and the lower phase was dried under nitrogen gas. The lipids were dissolved in $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of chloroform and analyzed on a 4000 QTRAP LC‒MS/MS System (SCIEX, USA). 

# Staining assays

The staining assays were performed as described previously (Chen et al., 2022). ${ \sf K I } / { \sf I } _ { 2 }$ staining solution and Nile Red solution (Sigma-Aldrich, Germany) were used to stain conidia and appressoria to observe glycogen 

and lipid droplets, respectively. Conidial suspensions ( $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml) were inoculated onto hydrophobic coverslips and observed at different time points under an epifluorescence microscope. 

# Lipidomics analysis

Mycelia were cultured in liquid minimal medium (MM) supplemented with propranolol at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ with shaking (120 rpm) for 4 days. Lipids were extracted and separated by 2D–TLC as described. Samples were incubated with isopropanol containing $0 . 0 5 \%$ (v/v) butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT) (Sigma-Aldrich, Saint Louis, MI, USA) at $7 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min. We used chloroform/methanol (2:1) with $0 . 0 1 \%$ BHT to extract total lipids; 1 M KCl and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ were added to the sample for centrifugation, and the upper phase was discarded. The solvent was evaporated under $\Nu _ { 2 }$ gas and redissolved in chloroform $( 5 ~ \mathrm { \ m g / m L } )$ . Chloroform/ethanol/ammonium hydroxide (65:25:2, v/v/v) and chloroform/ethanol/acetic acid/water (85:15:10:3, v/v/v/v) were used for 2D–TLC (Macherey Nagel, Duren, Nordrhein-€ Westfalen, Germany) (Li et al., 2015). 

Lipidomics analysis was performed using an Agilent HPLC system coupled with an Applied Biosystems Triple Quadrupole/Ion Trap 4000 QTrap mass spectrometer (Applied Biosystems, USA) (Zhao et al., 2022). 

# RNA sequencing and qRT–PCR analysis

RNA samples were isolated from mycelia of M. oryzae strains cultured in liquid MM supplemented with or without propranolol at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 3 days. The FastPure Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme, China) was used to extract total RNA from samples. RNA samples were sequenced on the BGISEQ-500 sequencer (BGI, China). Data were quality-controlled and mapped to the M. oryzae genome (http://fungi.ensembl.org/index.html). Gene expression and differential expression analyses were performed using featureCounts and DESeq2, respectively. The criteria for DEGs were $\mathsf { p } \leq 0 . 0 5$ and log2FC $\geq 1$ (FC, fold change). 

For qRT–PCR assays, we used the 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Vazyme, China) to generate complementary DNA (cDNA). TransStart Tip Green qPCR SuperMix (TransGen Biotech, China) was used for qRT–PCR assays on the CFX Connect Real-time PCR System (Bio-Rad, USA). The $\beta$ -tubulin gene (MGG_00604) was used as the internal control to calculate gene expression by the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C } t }$ method. Each treatment was repeated at least three times with independent samples. The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Phylogenetic analysis

Protein sequences of Pah1 homologs were obtained from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database and aligned with ClustalW. MEGA 7.0 was used to construct a multispecies neighborjoining phylogenetic tree with the p-distance model and 1000 bootstrap replicates. 

# Sexual reproduction assays

The sexual reproduction of R. solani and S. sclerotiorum was assessed on PDA plates with/without propranolol, and the sclerotia were counted and quantified at 14 dpi. The sexual reproduction of F. graminearum was assessed on carrot agar plates with/without propranolol, and the perithecia were counted and quantified at 14 dpi under a microscope (Zhu et al., 2022). 

# Chemical modifications of propranolol

Commercially available reagents were used without further purification. Solvents were purified according to standard methods prior to use. All reactions were performed under nitrogen in flame-dried glassware, and products were purified by recrystallization or column chromatography. High-resolution mass spectra were recorded on an LTQ-Orbitrap Elite mass spectrometer with $C H _ { 3 } C N / H _ { 2 } O$ as the mobile phase for measurement.

Molecular electrostatic potential calculation 

Propranolol and compounds I-13 and II-8 were selected for molecular ESP calculation. First, in Chem3D 20.0 software, the molecular structure was pre-optimized under the MM2 molecular force field (Allinger, 1977). Then, the obtained structure was imported into Gaussian 16W for geometry optimization at the B3LYP-D3(BJ)/6-31G(d) level, and high-precision single-point energy calculations were performed at the B3LYP-D3 (BJ)/6- $3 1 1 + 6$ (d, p) level (Stephens et al., 1994; Rassolov et al., 2001; Krishnan et al., 2008; Grimme et al., 2010; Frisch et al., 2016). All calculations took into account the compound in water under the SMD solvent model (Marenich et al., 2009). On the basis of the calculated molecular wave function, the molecular electrostatic potential was obtained using Multiwfn 3.8 software, and the ESP diagram was drawn using VMD software (Humphrey et al., 1996; Lu and Chen, 2012a; 2012b; Manzetti and Lu, 2013). 

Surface plasmon resonance 

The interaction between compound II-8 and MoPah1 was tested as described previously using SPR on a Biacore T20 (Cytiva, USA) at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (Li et al., 2023). Purified MoPah1 protein with the GST tag was immobilized on the CM5 sensor chip surface via amine coupling. The SPR assay was performed using II-8 at concentrations of 1.5625, 3.125, 6.25, 12.5, and $2 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ with PBS running buffer $( \mathsf { p H 7 . 4 } ) + 1 \%$ DMSO. Data were analyzed with the Biacore software Biacore Insight by fitting to a 1:1 steady-state affinity model. 

Phytotoxicity assays 

Phytotoxicity assays for propranolol and compounds I-13 and II-8 were performed on 4-week-old rice (CO39), 1-week-old wheat (Huamai 8) seedlings, and wheat plants at the flowering stage. Propranolol and related compounds were dissolved in water and DMSO for the assays. Propranolol and compounds I-13 and II-8 with Tween 20 $( 0 . 0 2 5 \% )$ were sprayed evenly onto leaves twice. The fresh weight of rice seedlings was examined at 7 and 20 dpi. For wheat seedlings, the fresh weight of each plant was examined at 5 dpi; for wheat heads, the chemical was applied to the flower, and the effect was examined at 20 dpi by measuring the dry weight of each head. 

Field trials 

For propranolol field trials on rice, we assessed inhibitory concentrations in the blast nursery in March 2022 at Yangjiang $( 2 1 ^ { \circ } 8 7 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N }$ , 111980 W) as described previously (Kimura et al., 2022). One-month-old rice 

seedlings of cultivar CO-39 were transplanted to the field plot, in which 600 plants about 10 cm apart were grown in a $3 0 - { \mathsf { m } } ^ { 2 }$ plot. After 30 days, propranolol (2.5 mM), II-8 (0.1 mM), and DMSO were sprayed twice on rice seedlings, and the rice blast disease index was examined at 16 dpi. 

For wheat, we used F. graminearum to assess the inhibitory concentration of propranolol on flowering wheat heads of Xiaoyan 22 as described previously. Conidia of F. graminearum PH-1 were harvested from 4-day-old cultures on carboxymethyl cellulose medium and adjusted to $\mathsf { 1 0 ^ { 5 } }$ conidia/ml with sterile distilled water. About $1 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of conidial suspension with or without propranolol was injected into a floret in the middle of the wheat head, and each treatment included 20 replicates. The disease index was assessed at 15 dpi.
