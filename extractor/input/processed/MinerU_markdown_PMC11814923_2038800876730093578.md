# ABSTRACT

Starch biosynthesis is a critical factor in wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) quality and yield. However, the full scope of its regulation is not fully understood. Here we report that TaDL interacts with TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 to synergistically regulate starch biosynthesis and quality in wheat. Genome‐edited tadl mutant lines had smaller and lighter grains with lower total starch and amylose contents compared to wild type (WT). Correspondingly, the transcript 

levels of starch biosynthesis‐related genes, including TaSUS1, TaSUS2, TaAGPL2, TaSBEIIa, TaGBSSII, and TaSWEET2a, were markedly lower at 15 d after flowering (DAF) in tadl mutants. TaDL physically interacted with TaB3 and TaNF‐ YB1 and activated the transcription of TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 through direct binding to their promoter regions. A null mutant of TaB3 also affected grain filling, with phenotypes similar to those of tadl mutants, whereas overexpression of TaNF‐YB1 promoted grain filling. Our study demonstrated that TaDL plays an essential role in starch biosynthesis and identified an elite allele (TaDL‐BI) associated with starch content, providing insights into the underlying molecular mechanism of wheat grain filling, which may be useful in breeding of high‐yielding wheat and quality improvement.

# INTRODUCTION

Wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) is an important food crop worldwide, feeding more than $3 5 \%$ of the global population (Li et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2024b). As living standards have improved, people have paid greater attention to the quality of end‐use products of wheat. Starch is the major component of wheat grains, representing about $70 \%$ of total dry grain weight, and therefore plays an essential role in determining grain weight and quality (James et al., 2003; Jeon et al., 2010). Moreover, the 

composition, structure, and accumulation of starch in grains are major factors affecting the physiochemical properties of starch and the quality of wheat flour (Toyokawa et al., 1989; Konik et al., 1994; Crosbie et al., 1999; He et al., 2004). Thus, revealing the molecular mechanisms underlying the regulation of starch biosynthesis is vital for improving wheat grain quality. 

Starch molecules are deposited in starch granules as semicrystalline structures (Tang et al., 2006). Starch granules can be classified into two types, A‐type (diameter $> 1 0 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and B‐type (diameter $\leq 1 0 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ ) (Peng et al., 1999). A‐type 

starch granules are formed during early grain development (starting at 4 d after flowering (DAF)), whereas the formation of B‐type starch granules is initiated during middle grain development (starting at 14 DAF) (Peng et al., 2000). A‐type granules show a typical disc‐like structure, whereas B‐type granules have spherical or irregular morphologies (Kim and Huber, 2010). Starch has two major components: amylose, composed of $\alpha \cdot$ ‐1,4‐glucosidic chains, and amylopectin, composed of $\alpha$ ‐1,4‐glucosidic linkages with α‐1,6 branches (Jeon et al., 2010). A‐granule starch has a higher amylose content than B‐granule starch (Bertolini et al., 2003). Wheat grains with low amylose content are used to produce noodles of superior texture. By contrast, products prepared from high‐amylose wheat grains are digested more slowly by humans and thus have a low glycemic index, making them more suitable for the diet of patients with diabetes or weight concerns (Hoshino et al., 1996; Mishra et al., 2016). 

Sucrose is the main carbohydrate produced in photosynthetic tissues and is translocated to other tissues, known as sink tissues (Schleucher et al., 1998). Sucrose synthase (SUS) is responsible for the hydrolysis of sucrose into UDP‐ glucose and fructose, the first step in its conversion to starch (Baroja‐Fernández et al., 2003; Keeling and Myers, 2010). Starch biosynthesis in the endosperm involves a complex and coordinated series of enzymatic reactions (Huang et al., 2021). Amylose production is catalyzed by ADP‐glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase) and granule‐ bound starch synthase (GBSS, also reported as Waxy), and amylopectin is synthesized by AGPase, starch branching enzyme (SBE), starch synthase (SS), and starch debranching enzyme (DBE) (Jeon et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2014). AGPase catalyzes the first key step of starch biosynthesis in cereal endosperm. In bread wheat, overexpression of TaAGPS1a significantly increased starch content (Yang et al., 2017). By contrast, the granule size, morphology, and structure of starch in wheat endosperm were affected by suppressed expression of TaSSI via RNA interference (McMaugh et al., 2014). 

In addition to enzymes involved in starch biosynthesis, many transcriptional regulators related to starch synthesis have been identified in cereals. For example, OsNF‐YB1 regulates grain filling in rice (Oryza sativa) through activating the expression of the sucrose transporter genes SUT1, SUT3, and SUT4 (Bai et al., 2016). OsMADS14 directly binds to the CArG‐box in the promoter regions of OsAGPL2 and Waxy and promotes their transcription by interacting with OsNF‐ YB1 (Feng et al., 2022). The mutual regulation of OsNAC25 and OsNAC20/26 forms a positive regulatory loop that stimulates the expression of starch synthesis‐related genes to meet the great demand for starch accumulation during the grain filling stage. Simultaneously, a negative regulatory loop forms among these three proteins to avoid the excessive expression of starch synthesis‐related genes (Wang et al., 2024). In maize (Zea mays), ZmNAC128 and ZmNAC130 regulate the rate‐limiting step in starch synthesis of maize 

endosperm by decreasing the Bt2 protein abundance (Zhang et al., 2019). ZmABI19 directly regulates multiple key grain filling transcription factors (TFs) including O2, Prolamine‐box binding factor 1, ZmbZIP22, NAC130, and Opaque11 in the endosperm and Viviparous1 in the embryo (Yang et al., 2021). In bread wheat, some TFs that regulate wheat starch synthesis have been isolated, including TaRSR1 (Liu et al., 2016), TabHLH39 (Dong et al., 2020), TaSPA‐B (Guo et al., 2020), TaNAC019‐A1 (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021), TabZIP28 (Song et al., 2020), TaNAC100 (Li et al., 2021), TaB3‐2A1 (Xie et al., 2023), TaJAZ1 (Yuan et al., 2023), TaABI3‐A1 (Zhao et al., 2024), and TaCAMTA2 (Zhang et al., 2024). Among them, TaRSR1 negatively regulates the expression of starch synthesis‐related enzyme genes, leading to markedly increased 1,000‐grain weight and grain starch content in the plants silenced for TaRSR1 (Liu et al., 2016). Overexpression of TabZIP28 increased the transcript levels of the gene encoding cytosolic AGPase, thus elevating AGPase activity and total starch content (Song et al., 2020). Knockout or overexpression of TaNAC019 resulted in smaller grain with decreased starch content (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021). TaABI3‐A1 negatively regulate grain size primarily by inhibiting the transcription of starch biosynthesis genes, while promoting storage protein accumulation through the activation of SSP‐related genes (Zhao et al., 2024). Although some of the TFs that regulate starch synthesis have been identified, the complete regulatory mechanisms behind starch synthesis need further study. 

The YABBY transcription factor family contains two conserved structural domains—an N‐terminal zinc‐finger domain (C2C2) and a C‐terminal YABBY domain (Bowman and Smyth, 1999). YABBY proteins play pivotal roles in multiple plant biological processes. For example, OsYABBY4 interacts with SLR1 to modulate gibberellic acid (GA) signaling during plant growth and development (Yang et al., 2016). Expressing TaYABBY1 or TaYABBY2 heterologously in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) promoted the abaxialization of the adaxial epidermis in leaves and arrested development of the shoot apical meristem (Zhao et al., 2006, 2012). A member of the YABBY family OsDL regulates carpel specification and midrib development in rice (Yamaguchi et al., 2004). The wheat genome encodes 20 YABBY members (Hao et al., 2022). A previous study showed that a YABBY domain‐containing protein and homolog of OsDL, TaDL, regulates pistil specification (Murai, 2013), and knockout of TaDL significantly decreased awn elongation in both top and central spikelets compared to the wild type (WT) (Du et al., 2021). However, the function of TaDL in starch synthesis during grain development is unknown. 

In the present study, we demonstrate that knockout of TaDL in bread wheat diminished starch content and lowered 1,000‐grain weight. TaDL directly activates the expression of genes involved in starch synthesis via binding to motifs in their promoter regions. In addition, TaDL interacts with TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 to synergistically regulate starch synthesis. The tab3 mutants exhibited a phenotype similar to that of tadl 

mutants, whereas overexpression of TaNF‐YB1 promoted grain starch synthesis. This study revealed a previously unrecognized function for YABBY and B3 TFs in starch synthesis during wheat grain filling and identified an elite allele (TaDL‐BI) associated with starch content that may contribute to the genetic improvement of high‐yielding wheat. 

# RESULTS

# TaDL is highly expressed in developing grains and its protein localized in the nucleus

In our previous study, we identified 96 TF genes that were co‐ expressed with the starch biosynthesis genes TaAGPL1, TaGBSSI, TaSBEIIa, TaSSI, and TaSSII (Liu et al., 2023). Among them, we noticed a member of the YABBY family that was highly expressed in developing grains. Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) searches and phylogenetic analysis indicated that the protein encoded by this gene is homologous to OsDL (Figure S1A), and we therefore designated it as TaDL (Du et al., 2021). The wheat genome harbors three TaDL homoeologs based on a query of the wheat genome databases (International Wheat Genome Sequencing Consortium; Appels et al., 2018), named here TaDL‐4A (TraesCS4A02G058800) and TaDL‐4B (TraesCS4B02G245900), and TaDL‐4D (TraesCS4D02G245300). These three homoeologs are highly similar $( 9 7 . 3 \% )$ . The coding sequences of three homoeologs are 603, 597, and 603 bp in length, encoding putative proteins of 200, 199, and 200 amino acids, respectively (Figure S1B, C). 

To characterize the expression of these different TaDL homoeologs, we used gene/isoform‐specifc primers for quantitative real‐time polymerase chain reaction (RT‐qPCR). Each homoeolog had a similar expression pattern, with peak expression in developing grains, and very low expression levels in vegetative organs (roots, stems, and leaves) (Figure 1A). This result suggests that TaDL genes may play a role in grain development. To investigate the subcellular localization of TaDLs, we infiltrated Nicotiana benthamiana leaves and transfected wheat protoplast cells with a construct encoding a fusion between TaDL‐4B, as a representative of the TaDL homoeologs, and green fluorescent protein (TaDL‐GFP). Examination by confocal laser microscopy showed that TaDL‐GFP is specifically localized in the nucleus of N. benthamiana leaf cells and wheat protoplasts (Figures 1B, S2). We conclude that TaDL is a nuclear protein, which is consistent with its putative function as a TF. 

# Knockout of TaDL inhibits starch synthesis in wheat grain

To explore the function of TaDL in grain development, we generated knockout lines in the wheat cultivar Fielder via clustered regularly interspaced small palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR‐associated nuclease 9 (Cas9) mediated gene editing. We identified insertion/deletions at the target site of each homoeolog, resulting in frameshift mutations and severely affecting the expression levels of each mutated genes 

(Figures S3, S4). Considering the barren state of the tadl triple mutants, we focused on two double mutants, the tadl‐aabb and tadl‐bbdd (tadl mutants #1 and #2), to further study how TaDL regulates wheat grain development (Du et al., 2021). 

The plant height, spike length, number of spikelets per panicle, and grain length did not differ significantly among the WT and the two tadl double mutants, but the grain width and 1,000‐grain weight of the mutants were significantly lower in the mutants relative to WT (Figures 1C–E, S5A–G). In addition, only tadl‐a single mutant had slightly decreased grain width and 1,000‐grain weight, suggesting functional redundancy among TaDL homeologs and a dosage effect in regulating grain development (Figure S6). Moreover, no significant differences for the total root length, lateral root number, primary root length and number of primary lateral roots of seedings were observed between WT and tadl mutants (Figure S7). In a short, these results indicate that TaDLs positively regulate kernel size and weight in bread wheat. 

TaDL‐overexpressing (Myc‐tagged) transgenic lines (TaDL‐OE, #8 and #9) were also generated by using spring wheat Fielder. The overexpression of TaDL and the accumulation of generated TaDL‐Myc proteins in these transgenic lines were separately confirmed by qRT‐PCR and immunoblotting assays (Figure S8A, B). As shown in Figure S8C–F, TaDL‐OE lines exhibited higher grain width and 1,000‐grain weight compared to that of WT plants, suggesting a positive regulatory role of TaDL in facilitating wheat grain development. 

Starch is the major component of wheat endosperm (McMaugh et al., 2014). The decreased kernel size and weight of the two tadl double mutants suggested that storage starch might be affected by the loss of two TaDL members. To test this hypothesis, we measured the total starch and amylose contents of grains from WT and the two tadl double mutants. Indeed, the total contents of starch, amylose and amylopectin, as well as the ratio of amylose to amylopectin, were significantly lower in the two tadl double mutants compared to WT, especially the amount of starch per grain, which decreased by $13 \% - 1 6 \%$ (Figures 2A–D, S5H). At the same time, the contents of soluble sugars and total proteins were higher in the two tadl double mutants relative to WT (Figure 2E, F). 

To examine the morphology of starch granules, we prepared paraffin sections from developing grains at 8 DAF. Periodic acid‐Schiff (PAS) staining of starch in these sections indicated that the sizes of both endosperm cells and A‐type starch granules in the two tadl double mutants were smaller than to those of the WT. We verified this result by scanning electron microscope (SEM; Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) analysis (Figure 2G–J). We also measured the solubility of starch from the grains of WT and the two tadl double mutants when mixed with various concentrations of urea. Although the starch in grain powder prepared from WT and either tadl double mutant did exhibit increased solubility with increasing concentrations of urea, detectable as gelatinization, the starch of the two tadl double mutants gelatinized to a lesser extent than that from the WT (Figure 2K, L). # TaDLs regulate the expression of starch synthesis genes in wheat grains

Starch in wheat endosperm is produced by a series of starch synthesis enzymes (Jeon et al., 2010). The markedly lower total starch content in the two tadl mutants suggested that TaDL may regulate the expression of starch synthesis genes during grain development. To explore the underlying mechanism, we performed transcriptome sequencing analysis (RNA‐seq) of grains from WT and the tadl‐aabb double mutant at 15 DAF. In total, we identified 5,241 differentially 

expressed genes (DEGs), consisting of 2,196 downregulated and 3,045 upregulated genes, in the tadl‐aabbDD mutant compared to WT (Dataset S1). A Gene Ontology (GO) term enrichment analysis revealed that the genes downregulated in tadl‐aabbDD are highly enriched for sucrose metabolic process, sucrose synthase activity, 1,4‐alpha‐glucan branching enzyme activity, and ADP‐glucose‐starch glucosyltransferase activity (Figure 3A; Dataset S2). These results were consistent with the accumulation of starch in grains and the 1,000‐grain weight and grain quality of the tadl double mutants. An RT‐qPCR analysis confirmed the downregulation of transcript levels of six core genes related to sucrose and starch metabolism: TaSUS1, TaSUS2, TaAGPL2, TaSBEIIa, TaGBSSII, and TaSWEET2a (Figure 3B). These results indicate that the regulation of starch accumulation is associated with the expression of starch synthesis genes in wheat grains and is affected by the loss of TaDL function.

During starch biosynthesis in wheat grains, the first step is catalyzed by SUS, and AGPase is the key rate‐limiting enzyme in starch synthesis (Keeling and Myers, 2010). We asked whether TaDLs may activate the expression of TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 by conducting a dual‐luciferase transcriptional transactivation assay. To this end, we placed the firefly luciferase (LUC) reporter gene under the control of the TaSUS2 or TaAGPL2 promoter; as effector construct, we overexpressed TaDL‐4B from the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. When co‐infiltrated with the TaSUS2pro: LUC or TaAGPL2pro: LUC reporter construct in N. benthamiana leaves, TaDL significantly enhanced the transcriptional output from the TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 promoters, as evidenced by the upregulated LUC activity relative to that of Renilla luciferase (REN) (Figure 3C–E). In an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) with recombinant purified His‐tagged TaDL, we established that TaDL binds to biotin‐labeled probes derived from the TaAGPL2 and TaSUS2 promoters containing a CCCCAC or CCCTCACC motif, respectively (Figure 3F, G). In a chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by quantitative PCR (ChIP‐qPCR) assay using a TaDL‐Myc transgenic line and an anti‐Myc antibody, we observed an enrichment for TaAGPL2 and TaSUS2 promoter fragments in the chromatin co‐precipitated with TaDL‐ Myc (Figure 3H, I). # TaDLs physically interact with the B3 family TF TaB3 and NF‐YB1 for the transcriptional activation of its target genes

To better understand the molecular basis of TaDL‐mediated transcriptional regulation, we performed a yeast two‐hybrid (Y2H) screen of a complementary DNA (cDNA) library prepared from total RNA extracted from 5‐DAF to 25‐DAF grains to identify the TaDL‐interacting proteins. We obtained 20 proteins as putative interactors of TaDL (Dataset S3). Among these candidates, a B3 family TF (TaB3) and TaNF‐YB1 were of particular interest, because TaB3 is highly expressed in developing grains (Figure S10A), and TaNF‐YB1 was previously shown to regulate wheat grain development (Liu et al., 2023). In addition, subcellular localization analysis showed that TaB3 also localizes in the nucleus of both N. benthamiana leaf epidermal cells and wheat leaf protoplasts, consistent with its predicted function as a TF (Figure S10B). Therefore, we chose TaB3 (TraesCS4A02G053000, TraesCS4B02G251500, and TraesCS4D02G251500) and TaNF‐YB1 (TraesCS6A02G28- 7500, TraesCS6B02G316800, and TraesCS6D02G268200) for subsequent analysis. 

To confirm the interaction between TaDL and TaB3 or TaNF‐YB1, we conducted a Co‐IP assay using soluble protein 

extracts prepared from N. benthamiana leaves co‐infiltrated with the constructs TaDL‐Myc and TaB3‐GFP or TaNF‐YB1‐ GFP, using the GFP empty vector as control. Following IP with anti‐GFP beads, we detected TaDL‐Myc among the proteins co‐precipitated with TaB3‐GFP and TaNF‐YB1‐GFP, but not with GFP (Figure 4A). # TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 positively regulate starch synthesis in bread wheat

To decipher the roles of TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 in starch synthesis during grain development, we obtained tab3 knockout (KO) lines (tab3 #3, #4 and #5) by inactivating all three homoeologs (in the A, B, and D subgenomes) using a CRISPR/ Cas9 system (Figure S15A) and then generated transgenic lines overexpressing TaNF‐YB1 with an in‐frame Flag tag (TaNF‐YB1‐OE, #6 and #7). 

Compared to the tadl double mutants, the three tab3‐aabbdd triple mutants (tab3 thereafter) exhibited similar phenotypes. The grain width and 1,000‐grain weight of the tab3 triple mutants were significantly lower than those of the WT, whereas the grain length did not differ significantly among the tab3 mutants and WT (Figures 5A–C, S15B, C). These results indicate that TaB3 contributes to grain size and weight. In addition, the contents of total starch, amylose, and amylopectin, as well as the ratio of amylose to amylopectin and the starch weight per grain, were also markedly decreased in the grains of tab3 triple mutants compared to WT (Figures 5D–G, S15D). By contrast, the contents of soluble sugars and total proteins were significantly increased in the tab3 triple mutants relative to WT (Figure 5H, I). Scanning electron microscopy observations and analysis revealed that A‐type starch granules in the tab3 triple mutants are smaller than those in WT (Figure 5J, K). Much as for the two tadl double mutants, the peak viscosity and final viscosity of starch from grains of the tab3 triple mutants were significantly lower than those of starch from WT grains (Figure 5L). These results indicate that the physicochemical properties of starch are altered by the loss of TaB3 function. 

A previous study showed that knockout of TaNF‐YB1 significantly decreased grain length, grain width, 1,000‐grain weight, and total starch content compared to those of WT grains (Liu et al., 2023). In the present study, we investigated the microstructure of grains (Figure 6A (20 DAF developing grains), C (35 DAF mature kernels)) from tanf‐yb1 triple mutants. The upper regions of cross sections from tanf‐yb1 mutant grains were thinner than those of the WT (Figure 6A), which was accompanied by significantly smaller endosperm cells in the tanf‐yb1 mutants (Figure 6B). Notably, the size distribution of A‐type starch granules peaked at ${ \sim } 2 1 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ in WT, but at $\sim 1 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ in tanf‐yb1 mutants (Figure 6C, D). We confirmed that TaNF‐YB1 transcript levels are higher in the TaNF‐YB1‐OE lines by RT‐qPCR; we also detected TaNF‐YB1‐Flag in these transgenic lines by immunoblotting (Figure 6E, F). The TaNF‐YB1‐OE lines and WT had grains of similar length (Figure 6G, H), while the TaNF‐YB1‐OE lines had wider and heavier grains, with greater total starch content (Figure 6G, I–K), compared to the WT. 

In addition, the starch biosynthesis genes TaSUS2, TaAGPL2, and TaSWEET2a also exhibited increased transcript levels in the 15‐DAF grains of TaNF‐YB1‐OE lines 

(Figure 6L). By contrast, the expression of TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 were significantly decreased in tab3 and tanf‐yb1 mutants (Figure S16), suggesting that TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 have positive regulatory roles in facilitating wheat grain filling. 

Together, our findings support the notion that TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 are interdependently required for grain filling. 

# TaDL‐4B is significantly associated with starch content

Considering the function of TaDL in starch biosynthesis, we hypothesized that TaDL might be a key factor in determining the starch content in natural populations. To identify natural variants of TaDL‐4B in wheat collections, we analyzed nucleotide polymorphisms over a region covering the exons, introns, the 2‐kb sequence upstream of the translation start codon, and the 2‐kb sequence downstream of the translation termination codon using Wheat‐SnpHub‐Portal (Wang et al., 2020). As a result, we identified two single‐nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), a T‐to‐C and a G‐to‐T change, in the proximal sequence downstream of the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ untranslated region $( 3 ^ { \prime }$ UTR) (Figure 7A). Accordingly, we designed two semi‐ thermal asymmetric reverse PCR (STARP) markers (STARP1 and STARP2) based on these two SNPs (Figure S17; Dataset S4). We then genotyped a natural population of 465 bread wheat accessions, comprising modern cultivars, landraces, and breeding lines collected worldwide, especially from China (Dataset S5), with these two markers. The natural population contained three main haplotypes, which we named TaDL‐B‐Hap1, TaDL‐B‐Hap2, and TaDL‐B‐Hap3 (Figure 7A). Among them, Hap1 was the most frequent $( n = 2 8 5 , 6 1 . 3 \% )$ ) and Hap3 the least frequent $n = 5 6$ , $1 2 . 0 \%$ ). Association analysis between the three haplotypes and starch content indicated that the mean starch contents of accessions harboring Hap1 and Hap2 were not significantly different, but were higher than that of accessions carrying Hap3 (Figures 7B, S18). These results indicate that the STARP1 marker is associated with starch content, with Hap1 and Hap2 defining elite alleles. Thus, we defined Hap1 and Hap2 as HapI, and defined Hap3 as HapII (Figures 7B, S18). We genotyped another panel of 250 global wheat accessions with STARP1, revealing that the frequency of HapI $( n = 2 2 9$ , $9 1 . 6 \%$ ) is much higher than that of HapII $n = 2 1$ , $8 . 4 \%$ ) (Figure 7C; Dataset S6). 

The SNPs are located in the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ UTR, possibly affecting messenger RNA (mRNA) levels. Based on the above haplotype analysis, we chose 20 wheat varieties with HapI and 20 varieties with HapII and performed RT‐qPCR on 15‐DAF grains (Dataset S5). Relative TaDL expression levels in HapI accessions were significantly higher than those for accessions with HapII (Figure 7D), indicating that the SNPs located in the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ UTR may affect the transcript levels of TaDL. 

Taken together, these results indicate that TaDL‐B is significantly associated with starch content, and the elite allele HapI is present in most wheat accessions both domestically and abroad.

# DISCUSSION

# TaDL is pivotal to starch synthesis and grain properties in wheat

YABBY‐type TFs are involved in various aspects of plant growth and development. For example, in rice, the YABBY TF, ObSH3, is indispensable for the development of the seed abscission layer, and deletion of the SH3‐containing genomic region caused loss of seed shattering in African cultivated rice (Lv et al., 2018). OsDL contributes to leaf midrib development, carpel specification, and flower meristem determinacy (Yamaguchi et al., 2004; Ohmori et al., 2011). DROOPING LEAF1 (DRL1) and DRL2 are responsible for floret development and floral meristem determinacy in maize (Strable and Vollbrecht, 2019). In bread wheat, tadl double mutants showed significantly lower awn elongation at the top and center spikelet than in the WT (Du et al., 2021). In the present study, we demonstrated that TaDL regulates grain development and affects starch biosynthesis in the grains of the double mutants tadl‐aabb and tadl‐bbdd (Figure 2A–C). These data indicate that the transcription factor TaDL plays an important role in wheat growth and development, leading to pleiotropic phenotypes when it is knocked out. Although several YABBY family members are thought to regulate plant growth and development, a role in starch biosynthesis had yet to be reported. 

At present, the regulations of starch biosynthesis have seldom been reported in bread wheat. Since TaDL was co‐expressed with starch biosynthesis‐related genes, we hypothesized that starch biosynthesis was regulated by its protein product. In the present study, we investigated the role of TaDL in wheat grain starch biosynthesis, with a focus on grain development and quality. Notably, 1,000‐grain weight and starch content were significantly lower, with the amylose content decreasing more than that of amylopectin and with altered starch pasting properties, in the tadl‐aabb and tadl‐bbdd double mutants compared to the WT Fielder (Figures 1E, 2A–D, K–M). Consistent with these observations, the expression levels of some key starch biosynthesis genes were markedly decreased (Figure 3A, B). In addition, dual‐ luciferase reporter assays, EMSAs, and ChIP‐qPCR assays revealed that TaDL activates the transcription of starch biosynthesis genes by directly binding to their promoter regions (Figure 3C–I). These results indicate that TaDL is a positive regulator of starch accumulation and formation. Collectively, our data indicate that the YABBY TF TaDL modulates grain filing and quality in bread wheat. Therefore, we speculate that related YABBY TFs may regulate grain starch biosynthesis in other cereal crops. 

In wheat breeding, quality improvements are a major objective (Kong et al., 2013). Starch gelatinization parameters are highly correlated with the quality of wheat‐based products like bread and noodles (Yun et al., 1996; Ragaee and Abdel‐Aal, 2006). Previous studies have reported that peak viscosity increases with larger starch granules, which affects swelling and viscosity profiles during pasting (Kaur et al., 

2007). Consistent with those findings, the size of A‐type starch granules and the peak viscosity decreased in tadl double mutants (Figure 2J, M). In addition, the final viscosity was mainly caused by the aggregation of amylose molecules, indicating a decreasing tendency for starch paste stability and gel hardness (Sun et al., 2014). Furthermore, the ratio of amylose to amylopectin influences the physicochemical properties of starch used as raw materials (Zhu and Wang, 2014). In the present study, we observed marked decreases to both the amylose content and the amylose/amylopectin ratio in the tadl double mutants (Figure 2B, D), suggesting changes in the eating quality of their starch. Taken together, these results indicate that TaDL plays a major role in regulating the eating quality of products prepared from wheat flour, including noodles and breads. 

# TaDL interacts with TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 to directly promote TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 expression and regulate starch synthesis

The physical interaction among TFs that regulate the same downstream targets may be advantageous for enhancing their regulatory efficiency (Zaret and Carroll, 2011). B3 domain‐containing proteins are plant‐specific and play essential roles in plant growth and development. For instance, the B3‐domain TF VAL1 modulates floral transition by repressing FT expression in Arabidopsis (Jing et al., 2019). The B3 domain‐containing TF ZmABI19 affects starch and sucrose accumulation by directly regulating the expression of several genes encoding key grain filling TFs during maize seed development (Yang et al., 2021). In rice, OsNF‐YB1 activates the expression of SUT1, SUT3, and SUT4 through direct binding to the CCAAT boxes in their promoter regions, with knockout of NF‐YB1 leading to defective grain filling with fewer and smaller starch granules (Bai et al., 2016). TaB3 was previously shown to be highly expressed in developing wheat grains (Liu et al., 2020), which we confirmed in the present study (Figure S10A). Furthermore, TaB3 physically interacts with TaNAC019‐A1 and may participate in starch biosynthesis in wheat grains (Liu et al., 2020). In the present study, we determined that TaDL interacted with TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 in a yeast two‐hybrid screen and confirmed these interactions through several in vivo and vitro assays (Figure 4A–D). Knockout of all three homoeologs of TaB3 led to significantly smaller and lighter grains containing less starch (Figure 5A–D), a phenotype similar to that of the tadl double mutants. By contrast, TaNF‐YB1‐OE lines exhibited wider and heavier grains with more total starch content to the WT (Figure 6G, I–K). In summary, TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 share overlapping expression patterns in developing grains, and their encoded proteins have similar functions in the biosynthesis of grain storage starch in wheat. These results indicate that TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 may regulate wheat grain starch biosynthesis through a similar pathway.

During grain filling, sucrose is converted to glucose‐1‐phosphate (G1P) by invertase, sucrose synthase (SuS), UDPG pyrophosphorylase (UGP), and hexokinase (Bahaji et al., 2014). TaNAC019 was reported to regulate starch accumulation through directly binding to the promoter of and activating the transcription of starch biosynthesis genes, including TaSUS1 and TaSUS2 (Gao et al., 2021). TaSUS2 is expressed specifically in the endosperm, and two of its haplotypes are significantly associated with 1,000‐grain weight (Maraña et al., 1990; Jiang et al., 2011). In plants, AGPase catalyzes the conversion of G1P to ADP‐glucose, and is the key enzyme in starch biosynthesis (Jeon et al., 2010). The contents of total starch, amylose, and amylopectin are significantly lower in osagpl2 mutants (Wei et al., 2017). In the present study, the expression levels of some key starch biosynthesis genes were coordinately decreased in the tadl double mutants, including TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 (Figure 3A, B). Further analysis revealed that these two genes were activated by TaDL via directly binding to their promoter regions (Figure 3C–I). Moreover, the presence of TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 significantly enhanced the transcriptional activation activity of TaDL toward its target genes (Figures 4E, S13). These findings suggest additive effects among TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 in manipulating the expression of their downstream genes. Previous studies showed that different TFs interact with each other to form large protein complexes that regulate the expression of common downstream target genes (Zaret and Carroll, 2011). For example, in rice NF‐YB1, NF‐YC12, and bHLH144 form a heterotrimeric complex, in which NF‐YB1 directly binds to the “G‐box” motif in the Wx promoter and activates Wx transcription, hence regulating rice grain quality. In agreement with this notion, knockout of any of the complex components alters grain qualities (Bello et al., 2019). Similarly, OsHAPL1, Hd1, and DTH8 form a complex, which acts as a transcriptional regulator of flowering time by interacting with the HAP complex and GTFs (Zhu et al., 2017). In bread wheat, the specific nuclear factor‐Y (NF‐Y) trimeric complex consisting of TaNF‐YA3‐D, TaNF‐YB7‐B, and TaNF‐YC6‐B were identified, which dynamically orchestrates starch and seed storage protein biosynthesis by repressing TaNAC019, TaGli‐γ‐700, and TaLMW‐400 (Chen et al., 2024a). In summary, TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 may form a complex to additively activate the transcription of genes involved in starch biosynthesis via direct binding to their promoter regions, thereby facilitating the filling of developing wheat grains (Figure 7E). Our results supply a theoretical basis for the investigation of starch biosynthesis in wheat endosperm and provide new insights into the molecular mechanism of YABBY and B3 TFs in facilitating wheat grain filling, which may be useful for the development of new wheat varieties with high yield potential. 

# Potential application of the TaDL‐B molecular marker for selection of wheat with high starch content

Marker‐assisted selection (MAS) is an efficient approach to molecular breeding. Many functional markers have been used in 

MAS for important agronomic traits, including plant height, 1,000 grain weight, heading date, and disease resistance (Ma et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020; Chai et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022). However, to date, only a few molecular markers associated with starch content have been developed (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021). In the present study, we developed two STARP markers to distinguish between the three haplotypes at TaDL‐B (Dataset S4). In addition, we discovered that HapI was significantly associated with high starch content in a natural population (Figures 7B, S18). The geographic distribution of TaDL‐B haplotypes indicated that TaDL‐B‐HapI is present in the majority of global wheat germplasms (Figure 7C). Notably, the portion of HapII is higher in Africa than in other continents. Many studies have demonstrated there are often interactions between genotypic and environmental effects, which means that differences in performance among wheat varieties are partly dependent on the growing environment. Therefore, different geographical regions have different preferences for selection in breeding (Fradgley et al., 2023). The higher proportion of HapII in Africa may be due to the special driving force of selection associate to the local climate. 

The ${ \sf 3 ^ { \prime } }$ UTRs are the noncoding parts of mRNAs and regulate the diverse fates of mRNAs, including their degradation, translation, and localization (Jackson, 1993; Kuersten and Goodwin, 2003). Previous study showed that fusion of the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ UTR of seed storage protein genes to the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ ends of DNA sequences encoding recombinant proteins enables massive accumulation of recombinant proteins with enzymatic activity in Arabidopsis seeds (Kanai et al., 2023). In rice, a C‐type SNP in the ${ \sf 3 ^ { \prime } }$ UTR decreased the transcript levels of OsTb2, confers the adaptive fewer tillers in upland rice and was fixed by artificial selection during dryland adaptation (Lyu et al., 2020). In our study, TaDL transcript levels in HapI wheat accessions were significantly higher than those in HapII accessions, suggesting that SNPs located in the ${ \sf 3 ^ { \prime } }$ UTR may affect the abundance of TaDL mRNA (Figure 7D). However, such a conclusion needs genetic testing and should be elucidated by future studies. Altogether, our study may provide a functional marker for starch content to facilitate the genetic improvement of wheat. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Generation of constructs and plant transformation

Two single guide RNAs (sgRNAs) were designed for TaB3 using the E‐CRISPR design website (http://www.e-crisp.org/ E-CRISP/designcrispr.html). To prevent off‐target effects, potential off‐target sites for each sgRNA designed above were predicted using Cas‐OFFinder (http://www.rgenome. net/cas-offinder/), returning no targets other than TaB3. Moreover, the target sites were queried by BLAST against Ensembl Plants (http://plants.ensembl.org/index.html), also identifying only one gene, TaB3. The sgRNAs were cloned into the pBUE411 vector. The resulting CRISPR/Cas9 construct was transformed into the wheat cultivar “Fielder” using 

Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens)‐mediated (strain EHA105) transformation (Ishida et al., 2015). The tadl and tanf‐yb1 mutant lines were previously reported and were also generated by CRISPR/Cas9‐mediated gene editing (Du et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). To generate the overexpression construct, the full‐length coding sequence of TaNF‐YB1 cloned in‐frame and upstream of the sequence encoding a Flag tag was inserted into the pMWB122 vector, the full‐length coding sequence of TaDL cloned in‐frame and upstream of the sequence encoding a Myc tag was inserted into the pMWB110 vector, and then were transformed into the wheat cultivar Fielder using Agrobacterium‐mediated (strain EHA105) transformation (Ishida et al., 2015). 

# Plant material and phenotypic evaluation

For the tissue expression analysis of TaDL and TaB3, the wheat cultivar Fielder was grown in a greenhouse under ${ } ^ { 2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ (day) $\iota 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (night) cycles and a 16‐h light/8‐h dark photoperiod with a relative humidity of $7 5 \%$ . The tadl, tab3, and tanf‐yb1 mutant lines and the TaNF‐YB1 overexpression lines were grown in an isolated experimental field at China Agricultural University, Beijing (E116°, N40°). Tissue samples were collected from three individual plants per genotype at each developmental stage. All samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and then stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ . 

Grain length, grain width, and 1,000‐grain weight were assessed using mature seeds and a Wanshen SC‐G seed detector (Wanshen Detection Technology, Hangzhou, China). The total root length, lateral root number, primary root length and number of primary lateral roots of 15 d after germination (DAG) seedings were measured with a root scanner (Epson, Suwa, Japan) and analyzed using Wanshen LA‐S root detector (Wanshen Detection Technology, Hangzhou, China). Total starch content was determined using a starch assay kit (Megazyme Ltd., Wicklow, Ireland) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Amylose content was measured using a kit (Megazyme Ltd., Wicklow, Ireland). Soluble sugar content was measured according to a previously reported method (Wang et al., 2013). The gelatinization of grain starch in urea solution was measured according to a previous report (Nishi et al., 2001). The viscous properties of starch was measured with a Rapid Visco Analyzer according to a previous report (Newport Scientific, Warriewood, Australia) (Tian et al., 2022). 

# RNA‐seq and data analysis

Grains from the WT and tadl double mutant grains were harvested at 15 DAF. Three independent biological replicates were collected. Sequencing libraries were prepared with Poly‐ A Purification TruSeq library reagents and sequenced on an Illumina HiSeqTM 2500 platform (Illumina, San Diego, USA). After screening and trimming, the clean reads were mapped to the wheat reference genome sequence IWGSCv1.1. DEGs were identified based on the criteria of P‐ value $< 0 . 0 5$ and | Log (Fold Change) $| > 1$ . GO term enrichment analysis was performed using Triticeae‐Gene Tribe (http://wheat.cau.edu. cn/TGT/m5/?navbar=GOEnrichment) (Chen et al., 2020). 

# RNA extraction and qRT‐PCR

Total RNA was extracted from various tissue samples collected at different developmental stages using RNAiso Plus (Takara, Dalian, China). First‐strand cDNA was synthesized using a reverse transcription kit (Takara, Dalian, China). Quantitative PCR was performed on a CFX 96 Real‐Time System RT‐PCR system (Bio‐Rad, Hercules, USA) using TB Green Premix (Takara, Dalian, China). Gene expression levels were calculated using the ΔCT method with wheat Actin (GenBank accession no. AB181991) as the reference transcript. All primers used are listed in Dataset S4. 

# Subcellular localization

To determine the subcellular localization of TaDL and TaB3, the full‐length coding sequences of TaDL‐4B and TaB3‐4B without the termination codon were individually amplified from Fielder cDNA and cloned in‐frame and upstream of the GFP sequence to generate the TaDL‐GFP and TaB3‐GFP constructs, respectively. The resulting constructs and the GFP control were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Agrobacterium cultures harboring each construct were infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves and the GFP fluorescence signal was observed 48 h later. The TaDL‐GFP, TaB3‐GFP plasmids and the GFP control plasmid were also transfected into wheat protoplasts. OsSPL16 fused to red fluorescent protein (RFP) was used as a marker for nuclear localization (Gao et al., 2021). After an incubation of 16 h in the dark, the GFP fluorescence signal was observed under a confocal laser scanning microscope (Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) with excitation using a 488‐nm laser. Images were acquired at a resolution of $1 , 0 2 4 \times 1 , 0 2 4$ pixels, 4 bits/pixel, using a $2 0 \times$ objective, with bidirectional scanning and as eight stacks $1 \mu \mathsf { m }$ apart. 

# Paraffin sections

Developing grains at 8 DAF from the WT and tadl mutants, as well as developing grains at 20 DAF from WT and tanf‐yb1 mutants, were collected and fixed in formaldehyde alcohol acetic acid solution for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The samples were dehydrated through a graded ethanol series, embedded in paraffin (Sigma Aldrich, St Louis, USA), and sectioned to a thickness of $8 - 1 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ with a microtome (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). Tissue sections were stained using PAS reagent. 

# Scanning electronic microscopy

Mature grains were dried at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 d. The seeds of the mutants and WT plants were broken transversely, and the cross sections were coated with gold. The samples were observed using a Hitachi S‐3400N SEM (Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan). 

# Yeast two‐hybrid screen

Wheat grains were collected at 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25 DAF for cDNA library construction, which was performed by Shanghai Ouyi Biotech Company. Y2H screens were performed according to the Yeast Protocols Handbook (Clontech, Kyoto, Japan). The transcriptional activation assay of TaDL was carried out following the same protocols. The full‐length coding 

sequence of TaDL‐4B was cloned into the pGBKT7 vector; the resulting construct was transformed into yeast strain Y2H Gold. Transformants were selected on synthetic dropout (SD) medium lacking Trp (SD/−Trp). The pGBKT7‐p53 and pGADT7‐T plasmid pair was co‐transformed into Y2HGold as positive control, and the pGBKT7‐lam and pGADT7‐T plasmid pair was used as negative control. Transformants were selected on DDO medium (SD/−Leu/−Trp) and protein–protein interactions tested on QDO medium (SD/−Leu/−Trp/−Ade/−His). 

# Luciferase complementation imaging assay

The full‐length coding sequences of TaDL, TaB3 and TaNF‐YB1 were individually cloned in‐frame with the sequence encoding the C‐ terminal or N‐terminal half of LUC, respectively. The resulting plasmids were confirmed by sequencing and transformed individually into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Agrobacterium culture harboring each construct were infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. At 48 h post infiltration, LUC activities were observed using a NightSHADE LB 985 plant imaging system (Berthold, Bad Wildbad, Germany). Three biological replicates were performed, each yielding similar results. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay

The full‐length coding sequences of TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 were amplified by PCR and cloned into the nYFP, cYFP, and cYFP vectors, respectively. The resulting plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and then co‐infiltrated as appropriate pairs in N. benthamiana leaves. The fluorescence signal of YFP was observed under a confocal laser scanning microscope (Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany). 

# Co‐immunoprecipitation assay

The full‐length coding sequences of TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1 were cloned into vectors harboring the sequence for a GFP or Myc tag and then transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 for co‐infiltration into N. benthamiana leaves. After 2 d, total proteins were extracted in protein lysis buffer (50 mmol/L Tris‐HCl pH 7.5, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 5 mmol/L ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid pH 8.0, $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) Triton X‐100, $0 . 2 \%$ (v/v) NP‐40) with 10 mmol/L phenylmethanesulfonyl fluoride, and $1 \times$ protease inhibitor (Roche, Rotkreuz, Switzerland). Anti‐GFP beads (MBL, Nagoya, Japan) were used for immunoprecipitation. For immunoblotting, a monoclonal anti‐ Myc antibody (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China) was used for the detection of Myc‐tagged proteins. 

# Pull‐down assays

The production of recombinant TaDL‐His, GST‐TaB3, GST‐TaNF‐YB1, and maltose‐binding protein (MBP)‐TaB3 was induced in Escherichia coli strain BL21. The recombinant proteins were purified with His or GST or MBP beads. Recombinant TaDL‐His was incubated with GST‐TaB3 or GST‐TaNF‐YB1 or MBP‐TaB3 and $3 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of GST or His beads (Millipore, Billerica, USA) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 3 \mathfrak { h } }$ . The beads were washed six times with washing buffer (50 mmol/L Tris‐HCl pH 8.0, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 10 mmol/L MgCl ). The proteins on the 

beads were eluted by boiling in $4 \times$ sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) loading buffer, separated by SDS – polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, and detected by immunoblotting with anti‐His antibodies (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China). 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The full‐length coding sequences of TaDL and TaB3 were cloned into the expression vector pET32a and pGEX‐6P‐1 and transformed into E. coli strain BL21 (DE3). The recombinant proteins were purified with Ni‐NTA resin (Qiagen, Valencia, USA) and GST Bind Resin (Millipore, Billerica, USA). The ${ } ^ { 5 ^ { \prime } }$ end‐labeled probes with biotin were used as binding probes, while unlabeled probes were used as competitors. EMSAs were performed using a LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo, Waltham, USA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Primer and probe sequences are listed in Dataset S4. 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation‐‐qPCR

The ChIP assay was performed as previously described (Zhang et al., 2024). Wheat grains (10 DAF) of the TaDL‐Myc transgenic lines were collected and crosslinked with $1 \%$ (v/v) formaldehyde for 10 min under a vacuum. Chromatin was extracted and fragmented to 200–500 bp by sonication (20 min sonication, 30/30 s on/off, at high‐energy level) with a Bioruptor Plus System (Qsonica, Newtown, USA). The protein–DNA complex was immunoprecipitated with anti‐Myc (Abcam, Cambridge, USA) and Magna ChIP™ protein $\mathsf { A } + \mathsf { G }$ magnetic beads (Millipore, Billerica, USA). DNA precipitates were recovered with a QIAquick PCR purification kit (Qiagen, Valencia, USA) and quantified by qPCR. Equal amounts of seed tissue and ChIP products were used for each reaction. Primers used for qPCR are listed in Dataset S4. 

# Dual‐luciferase reporter assay

A 2 kb promoter fragment of TaSUS2 and TaAGPL2 was inserted into the reporter vector pGreen II‐0800‐LUC, and the effector vector PHB was used to express TaDL, TaB3, and TaNF‐YB1. The reporter and effector constructs were individually transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 for co‐infiltration of N. benthamiana leaves. LUC and REN activities were measured using a Dual‐Luciferase Reporter Assay kit (Promega, Madison, USA). 

# Semi‐thermal asymmetric reverse PCR marker development

Two STARP markers were developed according to a previous publication (Chai et al., 2018). Primer sequences are listed in Dataset S4.
