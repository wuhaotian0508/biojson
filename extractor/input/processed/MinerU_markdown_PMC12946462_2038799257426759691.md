# ABSTRACT

Plants are promising bioreactor for the sustainable and scalable production of high-value natural bioactive compounds, because they can synthesise phytochemicals from $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , light, water and minerals through their innate photosynthetic carbon assimilation machinery. However, metabolic engineering in multicellular plants via stable transgene is challenged by the low accumulation of heterologous compounds. Forskolin is a labdane-type diterpenoid that accumulates in the roots of medicinal plant Coleus forskohlii. It activates the cyclic adenosine $^ { 3 ^ { \prime } , 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -monophosphate (cAMP) signalling pathway and is used for the treatment of heart complications, respiratory disorders, high blood pressure, obesity and asthma. Here, we report the successful production of forskolin in transgenic tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum). Constitutive expression of six biosynthetic genes, including diterpene synthases (CfTPS2 and CfTPS3), cytochrome P450s (CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16) and acetyltransferase (CfACT1-8), results in low-level production of forskolin throughout the plant. In contrast, glandular trichome-specific expression of these genes leads to a significant increase in forskolin accumulation in the aerial parts of the plant. Further optimisation through CRISPR/ Cas9 disruption of the biosynthetic pathway of endogenous diterpenoids $\alpha / \beta$ -cembratriene-diols and redirection of precursor flux towards forskolin biosynthesis improves its yield, reaching up to $2 1 . 2 6 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ fresh weight $( 2 0 6 . 3 3 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ dry weight) in the leaves of transgenic tobacco plants, surpassing the content in C. forskohlii roots. Our work not only provides a scalable and sustainable plant-based approach for forskolin production, but also represents a novel strategy of tissue- or organ-specific engineering and metabolic flux redirection to boost the heterologous production of high-value natural bioactive compounds in plants. 

# 1 | Introduction

Plant produces specialised metabolites that play crucial roles in environmental adaptation, such as attracting pollinators and defending against herbivores and pathogens (Weng et al. 2021). 

Many of these metabolites also exhibit special bioactivities like antifungal, antiviral, anticarcinogenic and antioxidative, making them of particular value as food additives, fragrance ingredients, or pharmaceuticals (Acquaviva et al. 2021; Subramaniam et al. 2019). However, the complex chemical structures of these 

compounds make chemical synthesis difficult and inefficient, while direct extraction from plant materials is resourceintensive and may lead to environmental and ecological damage due to their low abundances in plant tissues (Barnum et al. 2021; Nogueira et al. 2018). In recent years, engineering heterologous hosts for the production of bioactive compounds has been proved to be a promising alternative, with dramatic successes achieved in microbial systems like Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Liu et al. 2022; Navale et al. 2021). However, this approach faces significant challenges, such as the complexity of plant metabolic pathways, enzyme promiscuity and poor activity, intermediate cytotoxicity, competing native metabolic processes and high costs of fermentation (Brooks and Alper 2021; Gallup et al. 2021; Kulagina et al. 2021). As a result, large-scale industrial applications remain limited, and there is a need to explore alternative eco-friendly and sustainable methods for producing high-value natural bioactive compounds. 

Plants have long served as natural producers of diverse bioactive compounds and are now regarded as ideal chassis for metabolic engineering, as they utilise their inherent photosynthetic machinery to generate diverse metabolites from $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ , light, water and soil minerals. This autonomy eliminates the need for supplied carbon substrates and special fermentation equipment. The complex subcellular compartments in plants (e.g., plastid, mitochondria, peroxisome and vacuole) enable the spatial organisation of metabolites and enzymes, with active molecular trafficking in diverse microenvironments (ions, co-factors, pH and oxidation–reduction potential) (Yao et  al.  2023). Particularly, approximately $3 0 \%$ of vascular plant species possess glandular trichomes (GTs) on their surfaces (Huchelmann et  al.  2017). These multicellular architectures have an extraordinary capacity to synthesise, secrete, and store large quantities of specialised metabolites, such as terpenoids, phenylpropanoids, flavonoids and acyl sugars (Deschamps and Simon 2010; Fan et al. 2019; Feng et  al.  2023; Hao et  al.  2024; Kim et  al.  2012; Lange and Turner 2013). As discrete structures comprising only a few cells, GTs not only support highly active specialised metabolism but also sequester cytotoxic compounds, thereby minimising adverse effects on plant growth and development. These attributes make GTs promising cell factories for producing compounds with high industrial and pharmacological value. Thus, plants offer distinct advantages over microbial hosts in precursor supply, membrane protein expression, and product tolerance, positioning them as promising bioreactors for producing highvalue natural bioactive compounds (Wang and Demirer  2023; Yao et  al.  2023; Zhu et  al.  2020). However, despite being easy to scale up and having low cultivation costs, engineering stable transgenic plants for the production of high-value bioactive compounds remains challenging (Rizzo et  al.  2023). This is partly due to the long time required and low efficiency of genetic manipulations (e.g., organisation, integration, and coordination of multiple transgenes in the plant genome), as well as the typically low yields compared to their native source plants or transient expression systems like Nicotiana benthamiana (Breitel et al. 2021; Farhi et al. 2011; Liao et al. 2023). 

Forskolin is a labdane-type diterpenoid primarily found in the roots of Coleus forskohlii, a medicinal plant of the Lamiaceae family that has been traditionally used across Nepal, India and Southeast Asia (Le Cabec et al. 2024; Mitra et al. 2020). As an 

activator of adenylate cyclase, forskolin elevates cyclic adenosine $^ { 3 ^ { \prime } , 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -monophosphate (cAMP) levels, triggering downstream signalling pathways and cellular processes (Sapio et  al.  2017; Seamon and Daly  1981). This imposes forskolin broad pharmacological activities, including anti-HIV, antihypertensive, positive inotropic and platelet aggregation inhibitory properties (Amen et  al.  2024; de Souza et  al.  1983; Sapio et  al.  2017). Its derivative, NKH477, has been approved in Japan as an orally active, water-soluble bronchodilator and inodilator for treatment of heart failure, especially in the severe stage with $\beta$ -adrenoceptor downregulation (Hosono et  al.  1992; Iida et  al.  1995). Recent studies have uncovered more promising applications, such as reprogramming human somatic cells into induced neurons (Wang, Zhang, et  al.  2024), reversing motor symptoms, loss of hind limb strength, neurodegeneration of midbrain dopamine neurons in Parkinsonian rats (Dagda et al. 2022; Vazquez-Mayorga et al. 2022), and the treatment of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (Wang, Xue, et  al.  2024). However, the total chemical synthesis of this therapeutically valuable compound is hindered by its stereochemical complexity, requiring 24 steps to produce a mixture of enantiomers (Hylse et al. 2017). Consequently, commercial production of forskolin relies exclusively on extraction from C. forskohlii roots, where it constitutes approximately $0 . 0 1 3 \%$ of the dry weight $( 1 3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ DW) (Srivastava et  al.  2017). This reliance has led to unsustainable cultivation and overharvesting, making C. forskohlii endangered (Srivastava et al. 2017). 

The biosynthetic pathway of forskolin in C. forskohlii has been elucidated (Figure  1a): the diterpene synthases CfTPS2 and CfTPS3 catalyse the cyclization of geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP, $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 0 } )$ to 13R-manoyl oxide (Pateraki et  al.  2014), which is further modified by cytochrome P450s (CYP76AH8, CYP76AH9, CYP76AH10, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH15, CYP76AH16, CYP76AH17) and acetyltransferases (CfACT1-6 and CfACT108), forming forskolin and related derivatives (Pateraki et  al.  2017). This has enabled heterologous production in microbial hosts, with initial production of 13R-manoyl oxide by expressing CfTPS2 and CfTPS3 in S. cerevisiae, E. coli and Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 (Andersen-Ranberg et al. 2016; Englund et al. 2015; Nielsen et al. 2014; Zhang et al. 2019), followed by forskolin production $\left( 4 0 \mathrm { m g / L } \right)$ upon the additional expression of CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16 and CfACT1-8 (Pateraki et al. 2017). The yield was further improved to $2 1 . 4 7 \mathrm { m g / L }$ in shake flasks and $7 9 . 3 3 \mathrm { m g / L }$ in a 5 L bioreactor by optimising the adaptations amongst P450s, regulating gene copy numbers, expanding the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) area, and enhancing cofactor metabolism (Ju et al. 2022). Recently, by co-expressing a specific cytochrome b5 (CfCytB5A), the forskolin yield was improved to $1 . 8 1 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ in yeast (Forman et al. 2024). Despite these advances, the yield and cost of microbial forskolin production remain insufficient for commercial viability. 

Tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) is a fast-growing crop cultivated worldwide, with a lifecycle of about four months and a biomass yield of up to 170 tons/ha (Barla and Kumar 2019). As a model plant, its allotetraploid genome (comprising S- and T-subgenomes), along with the genomes of its diploid ancestors, Nicotiana sylvestris and Nicotiana tomentosiformis, has been sequenced (Sierro et  al.  2024, 2014). Robust protocols for propagation, crossing, genetic transformation, and genome editing have been established (Niedbala et al. 2021; Sierro et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2023). 

Notably, on the aerial surface of tobacco, there is a high density of GTs, which synthesise and accumulate substantial amounts (up to $0 . 1 \%$ of dry weight) of the diterpenoid $\alpha / \beta$ -cembratriene-diols (CBT-diols, Figure 1a) (Baraka et al. 2011; Xu et al. 2022; Zhang et  al.  2024). The biosynthetic pathway of CBT-diols has been

This indicates that tobacco GTs are highly active in terpenoid metabolism

In this study, we accomplished high-level heterologous production of forskolin in tobacco by GT-specific engineering and redirecting the metabolic flux of GGPP via blocking endogenous CBT-diols biosynthesis. We first reconstituted and validated the forskolin biosynthetic pathway through transient expression of its biosynthetic genes in N. benthamiana. We then generated stable transgenic tobacco plants constitutively expressing these genes, which resulted in low-level forskolin accumulation throughout the plant. By using the GT-specific promoter to control gene expression and genome editing of the CBTS gene family to disrupt CBT-diols biosynthesis, we increased forskolin yield by 34.8-fold totally, reaching $2 0 6 . 3 3  { \mu \mathrm { g / g } }$ DW in tobacco leaves, surpassing the content in the roots of its native producer, C. forskohlii $( 1 3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ DW). Our work not only establishes a scalable and sustainable plant-based platform for forskolin production, but also represents a paradigm of organ-specific engineering and metabolic flux redirection in multicellular plants, enabling the molecular farming of high-value natural bioactive compounds. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Reconstitution and Validation of Forskolin Biosynthetic Pathway by Transient Expression in N. Benthamiana

Previous studies demonstrated that co-expression of diterpene synthases (CfTPS2 and CfTPS3), cytochrome P450s (CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16) and the acetyltransferase (CfACT1-8), along with the plant cytochrome P450 reductase (CPR) can reconstitute forskolin biosynthesis in yeast (Forman et  al.  2024; Ju et  al.  2022; Pateraki et  al.  2017). To validate the feasibility of forskolin production in plants, we first assessed the functionality of these genes through transient expression in N. benthamiana. As plant cells provide appropriate subcellular compartments and cofactors for enzyme localization and activity, including plastids for diterpene synthases, the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) for P450s, and endogenous CPRs to support electron transfer for P450s, we therefore synthesised the full-length C. forskohlii genes of CfTPS2, CfTPS3, CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16 and CfACT1-8, retaining their organelle-targeting signals to ensure proper localizations. Each gene was assembled with the Cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter (35Spro) and Agrobacterium tumefaciens octopine synthase terminator (OCSter), forming individual transcriptional units (Figure  S1). 

These constructs were introduced into A. tumefaciens separately, and the bacterial cultures were mixed and co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves (Figure S1). 

At 5 days post infiltration (DPI), forskolin $\mathrm { ( [ M + N H _ { 4 } ^ { \ + } ] }$ , $\mathrm { m / z } = 4 2 8 . 2 6 4 2$ ) was detected in the leaf samples, exhibiting the same retention time and mass spectrum as the authentic standard (Figure  1b,c). These results suggest that co-expression of CfTPS2, CfTPS3, CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16 and CfACT1-8 is sufficient to support the heterologous biosynthesis of forskolin in plants. 

# 2.2 | Constitutive Expression of Biosynthetic Genes Results in Low-Level Forskolin Production

Based on the success of transient expression, we generated stable transgenic tobacco plants for forskolin production. All transcription units, in which each biosynthetic gene was controlled independently by 35Spro and OCSter, were assembled into a single plant expression vector, generating the construct pForskolin-35S for constitutive expression (Figure 2a). This construct was introduced into tobacco via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. 

Of 18 independent transgenic lines (pForskolin-35S/WT, $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation), only three lines accumulated detectable levels of forskolin in their leaves, with content ranging from $0 . 2 1 { \pm } 0 . 0 6 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ fresh weight (FW, #29) to $0 . 6 1 { \pm } 0 . 0 7 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ FW (#20, Figure 2b). The content of forskolin varied across different plant organs: it was high in the roots and leaves, but low in the stems and flowers (Figure  2c, Table  1). Interestingly, moderate levels of forskolin $( 0 . 0 4 \pm 0 . 0 2 { \sim } 0 . 3 6 \pm 0 . 0 9 \mu \mathrm { g / g } \mathrm { F W } )$ was detected in GTs collected from the leaves (Figure 2c, Table 1). These results indicate that the expression of biosynthetic genes can lead to the production of forskolin in stable transgenic tobacco plants, with accumulation levels varying amongst different transgenic lines and across different organs. The moderate levels of forskolin detected in GTs suggest that these structures may contribute to forskolin accumulation, making them potentially useful for further optimization.

We supposed that targeting the forskolin biosynthetic pathway specifically to GTs could increase GGPP availability, thereby increasing forskolin production. To test this, we employed the GTspecific CBTS2b promoter (CBTSpro) to control the expression of biosynthetic genes (Ennajdaoui et al. 2010), and generated the plant expression construct pForskolin-CBTS for GT-specific engineering (Figure 2d). 

Analysis of transgenic plants (pForskolin-CBTS/WT, $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation) revealed significant improvement of forskolin accumulation. Amongst 12 of the 18 independent lines, forskolin levels in the leaves ranged from $0 . 3 2 { \pm } 0 . 1 7 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ FW (#8) to $3 . 5 0 { \pm } 0 . 2 7 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ FW $( \# 4 7 )$ , representing a 5.74-fold increase over the highest level observed in the constitutive pForskolin-35S/WT leaves (Figure  2e, Table  1). However, unlike the accumulation profile of constitutive expression, forskolin was detected in leaves, stems and flowers, but not in roots, of the pForskolin-CBTS/WT plants (Figure  2f, Table  1). This spatial pattern aligns with the distribution of GTs on the surface of aerial tissues. Importantly, in GTs collected from the leaves of pForskolin-CBTS/WT plants, the forskolin content reached up to $2 3 . 3 6 \pm 3 . 6 1 \mu \mathrm { g / g F }$ W, representing a 64.89-fold increase compared to the content in pForskolin-35S/WT GTs $( 0 . 3 6 \pm 0 . 0 9 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ FW, Figure 2c,f, Table 1). These results demonstrate that using 

the GT-specific promoter (CBTSpro) to drive biosynthetic gene expression can significantly enhances forskolin production, with higher accumulation observed in aerial tissues. By optimising the supply of the precursor GGPP, this targeted approach may offer a more efficient method for forskolin production. 

# 2.4 | Disruption of Endogenous CBT-Diols Biosynthesis by Genome Editing of the CBTS Gene Family

Inspired by the observation of increase in forskolin production in GTs, we deduced that disrupting endogenous CBTdiols biosynthesis through genome editing of CBTS could further increase GGPP supply and redirect the metabolic flux of the MEP pathway towards engineered heterologous diterpenoid biosynthetic pathway, such as forskolin (Figure  1a). Previous studies have identified three full-length CBTS genes (NsylCBTS2a, HM241151.1; NsylCBTS2b, HM241152.1; and NsylCBTS3, HM241153.1) and one pseudogene (NsylCBTS4, HM241154.1) in the genome of diploid ancestor N. sylvestris, sharing ${ \sim } 9 9 \%$ nucleotide sequence identities (Ennajdaoui et  al.  2010). We used the nucleotide sequences of the fulllength $N s C B T S 2 a / 2 b / 3$ as queries to search the genome of the 

other diploid ancestor N. tomentosiformis, which revealed only one single ortholog (NtomCBTS, XM_009596254.4) with ${ \sim } 9 4 \%$ nucleotide identity to NsylCBTSs. Given the absence of CBTdiols in N. tomentosiformis (Figure S2), we inferred that this gene was dysfunctional. 

Subsequent analysis of the tobacco genome initially identified only one NsylCBTSs ortholog (NM_001325450.1) and one NtomCBTS ortholog (XM_016603386.1), likely due to the challenge in assembling highly identical sequences in the genome. To facilitate the design of guide RNAs (gRNAs) for CRISPR/ Cas9 genome editing, we performed PCR amplification of the first exon and intron of CBTS genes from the tobacco genomic DNA using degenerate primers (Table S1), followed by Sanger sequencing of the resulting colonies. Sequence alignment revealed at least three NsylCBTS orthologs (designated NtabCBTSs1/2/3) and one NtomCBTS ortholog (designated NtabCBTSt) in the tobacco genome (Figure S3). Based on these, two guide RNAs (gRNAs) targeting to the conserved regions $1 0 0 \%$ identity) within the first exon of the NtabCBTSs1/2/3 were selected to make the construct for the simultaneous editing of these genes in tobacco (Figure 3a). Notably, NtabCBTSt was not involved in the design, as we deduced that it is dysfunctional, similar to its ortholog NtomCBTS in N. tomentosiformis. 

By continuous screening of $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ to $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ generation transgenic plants, a homozygous mutant plant that was devoid of both CBT-diols and T-DNA insertion was identified (designated cbts, Figure  3b,c). Cloning and sequencing of the targeted genomic region confirmed mutations in NtabCBTSs1/2/3 genes (including nucleotide insertions in NtabCBTSs1 and NtabCBTSs2, and a 48-bp deletion in NtabCBTSs3). The homologous region of NtabCBTSt, however, remained unchanged due to the absence of the protospacer-adjacent motif (PAM) sequence adjacent to the designed target sites (Figure 3a, Figure S4). When compared to the wide-type (WT) plants, the cbts mutant did not show any significant alterations in morphology and GT density (Figure 3d,e). 

# 2.5 | Minor Impact on Terpenoid Precursor Biosynthesis in the Cbts Mutant

To assess potential pleiotropic effects of CBTS knockout on tobacco metabolism, we performed RNA-seq analysis on leaves and GTs from the WT plant and the cbts mutant. Principal component analysis (PCA) of all transcript abundances revealed clear separation between the leaf and GT samples, confirming their distinct transcriptional profiles. Importantly, within each group, individual WT and cbts samples clustered closely, indicating minimal differences (Figure 4a). Consistent with this, only a few differentially expressed genes (DEGs, $\mathrm { | L o g } _ { 2 } ( \mathrm { F o l d C h a n g e } ) | > 1$ $\mathrm { { l L o g } } _ { 2 }$ $| > 1$ and q-value $< 0 . 0 5$ ) were identified in leaves (276 down, 183 up) and GTs (424 down, 333 up, Figure  4b). Kyoto Encyclopaedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis of DEGs revealed that knocking out of CBTSs increased phenylpropanoid, amino acid, and fatty acid metabolism in both tissues. Conversely, starch and sucrose metabolism decreased in GTs, while pyrimidine metabolism decreased in the leaves (Figure S5). Analysis of the transcript abundances of genes encoding enzymes of the terpenoid precursor biosynthetic pathways, including the methylerythritol 4-phosphate pathway 

(MEP pathway) and the mevalonate pathway (MVA pathway) revealed almost unchanged expression profiles between WT and cbts mutant in both leaves and GTs (Figure 4c,d). Measurement of key MEP pathway products, including GGPP and geranyl diphosphate (GPP, $\mathrm { C } _ { 1 0 } )$ , showed that GGPP was highly abundant in GTs and increased dramatically (4.8-fold) in the cbts mutant compared to WT plant (Figure 4e). In contrast, GPP content did not change significantly in roots, leaves, or GTs of the cbts mutant (Figure S6). Moreover, the content of farnesyl diphosphate (FPP, $\mathrm { C } _ { 1 5 } )$ , the key product of the MVA pathway, remained unchanged in the cbts mutant (Figure 4f). These results indicate that the disruption of CBT-diols biosynthesis by knocking out CBTSs leads to increased GGPP accumulation and causes only subtle perturbations on other metabolic processes, thus could support the heterologous biosynthesis of terpenoids. 

# 2.6 | Boosting Forskolin Production in the Cbts Mutant

To evaluate the effect of disrupting endogenous CBT-diol biosynthesis on the heterologous forskolin production, we first introduced the constitutive construct pForskolin-35S into the cbts mutant. The resulting pForskolin-35S/cbts plants $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation) showed dramatic increase in forskolin accumulation: $1 . 1 5 { \pm } 0 . 2 9 { \sim } 4 . 2 1 { \pm } 0 . 4 2 { \mu } \mathbf { g } / \mathbf { g }$ FW in leaves (6.9-fold increase compared to pForskolin-35S/WT plants, Figure  5a), and $1 . 1 8 \pm 0 . 3 3 { \sim } 2 . 2 5 \pm 0 . 4 8 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ FW in GTs (6.25-fold increase, Figure 5b). Further measurement of forskolin in dry leaves revealed contents ranging from $9 . 8 6 \pm 2 . 2 6$ to $3 7 . 3 7 { \pm } 1 1 . 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ DW in pForskolin-35S/cbts plants (Figure S7a, Table 1). 

To further assess the impact of blocking CBT-diols on GT-specific forskolin production, the construct pForskolin-CBTS was introduced into the cbts mutant. The resulting pForskolin-CBTS/cbts plants $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation) accumulated $4 . 2 6 { \pm } 0 . 6 2 { \sim } 2 1 . 2 6 { \pm } 3 . 4 3 { \mu } \mathrm { g / g }$ FW of forskolin in leaves, representing a 34.85-fold increase over pForskolin-35S/WT leaves and a 6.08-fold increase over pForskolin-CBTS/WT leaves (Figure 5c). In GTs, forskolin content reached $6 4 . 9 7 \pm 6 . 1 6 { \sim } 1 2 0 . 4 1 \pm 1 3 . 0 8 { \mu } \mathrm { g / g }$ FW, representing 334.5- and 5.15-fold increase compared to those of pForskolin-35S/WT and pForskolin-CBTS/WT GTs, respectively (Figure 5d, Table 1). Moreover, in dry leaves of pForskolin-CBTS/cbts plants, forskolin content reached up to $3 8 . 5 5 \pm 8 . 0 7 { \sim } 2 0 6 . 3 3 \pm 2 8 . 1 8 \mu \mathrm { g }$ /g DW (lines #7, #11 and #15, Figure S7b, Table 1), which surpasses the content in the roots of C. forskohlii plant $( 1 3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ DW) (Asada et al. 2012; Pateraki et al. 2017).

Interestingly, when the biosynthetic genes were driven by the constitutive 35S promoter (pForskolin-35S/WT and pForskolin-35S/cbts plants), forskolin accumulation was higher in the whole leaves than in the isolated GTs (Figures 2, 5 and Table  1). We deduce that this is associated with the transcriptional profile of the 35S promoter. Although the 35S promoter has been used extensively for constitutive expression of transgenes in plants, its activity can vary considerably amongst plant species, tissues, and environmental conditions (Amack and Antunes 2020). In tobacco GTs, the 35S promoter activity is restricted to the basal region, rather than the glandular head cells, where terpenoid biosynthesis actively occurs (Choi et  al.  2012). Moreover, during GT collection, most of the head and stalk cells were brushed off the leaf surface and collected, whereas the basal cells (where the 35S promoter is active) largely remained attached to the leaf epidermis. Together, these factors likely account for the lower forskolin content in GTs than in the whole leaves of the pForskolin-35S transgenic plants. 

In contrast, forskolin production in the leaves of pForskolin-CBTS transgenic plants was substantially higher than in those of pForskolin-35S plants (Figures  2, 5 and Table  1). Tobacco GTs are highly active in diterpenoid biosynthesis, producing CBT-diols that can reach up to $0 . 1 \%$ of leaf dry weight (Baraka et al. 2011; Kandra and Wagner 1988; Xu et al. 2022). This not only reflects an abundant supply of the precursor GGPP via the MEP pathway in GTs (Figure  4e), but also indicates that the CBTS promoter, which drives the expression of key enzymes in CBT-diols biosynthesis, possesses strong and specific transcriptional activity (Ennajdaoui et al. 2010). The combination of high GT-specific transcriptional activity and a favourable metabolic context accounts for the superior forskolin accumulation in pForskolin-CBTS transgenic plants. 

Previously we proposed that life cycle, biomass, genetic manipulation protocols, endogenous metabolism, and environmental adaptability are key criteria for selecting a plant host 

for metabolic engineering (Yao et al. 2023). According to these criteria, tobacco emerges as a particularly attractive chassis. In this study, we further improved tobacco by genome editing of the CBTS gene family, which eliminates the accumulation of endogenous CBT-diols. Compared to WT plants, the cbts mutant showed no significant alterations in GT morphology or density, and only minor changes in the transcriptome. Importantly, the content of GGPP, the common precursor for diterpenoid biosynthesis, increased by approximately 5-fold. This not only enhances the precursor supply for the engineered heterologous metabolic pathway but also helps to reduce interference from endogenous diterpenoids during the extraction and purification of target compounds. Taking this advantage, similar strategies can be applied to improve other traits. For example, disrupting the nicotine biosynthetic pathway by editing berberine bridge enzyme-like proteins (BBLs) genes (Vollheyde et al. 2023) and eliminating acyl-sucrose in tobacco GTs by knocking out NtASAT1 or NtASAT2 (Chang et al. 2022). Further combination of these modifications would generate a tobacco variety that is deficient in CBT-diols, acylsucrose, and nicotine, creating a versatile platform for the sustainable production of diverse high-value natural bioactive compounds. 

One of the challenges in plant metabolic engineering via stable transgene is the relatively low yield of the target metabolite. Although GTs constitute a small portion of the total biomass, they function as metabolic cell factories with the capacity to produce large quantities of specialised metabolites, such as artemisinin (Artermisia annua), menthol (Mentha spp.) and cannabinoids (Cannabis sativa) (Fuchs et  al.  2022; Xie et  al.  2023; Yin et  al.  2025). This indicates that massive metabolic fluxes are likely to occur within GTs, making them highly attractive target for metabolic engineering. Despite this potential, few cases of GT-specific metabolic engineering have been reported: in tobacco, the constitutive expression of biosynthetic genes produced $0 . 2 { \sim } 0 . 4 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ FW thymol and $0 . 1 { \sim } 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ FW carvacrol, whereas GT-specific engineering increased their yields by 5~20 fold, up to $2 . 1 { \sim } 7 . 6 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ FW and $1 . 9 { \sim } 4 . 2 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ FW, respectively (Jiang et  al.  2024). # 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

Tobacco (wide-type cultivar ‘K326’, cbts mutant and all transgenic plants) seeds were germinated on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium (WT and cbts mutant, or containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin for selection of transgenic plants). The 14-day-old seedlings were transferred to soil and grown in a greenhouse under a photoperiod of 16h light/8h dark at a constant temperature of $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ throughout their life cycle. Samples of Leaf, flower, stem and root were collected from 8-week-old plants for metabolite analysis. 

To collect GTs, leaves were frozen in liquid nitrogen, and GTs were brushed off the leaf surface with a paintbrush that had been dipped in liquid nitrogen. The collected GTs were used for RNA extraction or metabolite analysis immediately. 

N. benthamiana seeds were germinated on MS medium, and the 7-day-old seedlings were transferred to individual pots and grown in a growth chamber at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $7 0 \%$ humidity and under a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h dark. Four-week-old plants were used for syringe infiltration and transient expression assay. 

# 4.2 | Plasmid Construction

Constructs for genome editing and plant expression were produced using the MECA system (Jiang et al. 2024). The Golden Gate Assembly (GGA) was performed as described (Engler and Marillonnet  2014). Briefly, a $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixture was prepared by mixing $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ 10× T4 DNA ligase buffer $2 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris– HCl, pH 7.6, 50 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5mM ATP and $5 \mathrm { m M }$ DTT), $1 \mu \mathrm { L T 4 }$ DNA ligase (New England Biolabs), $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ Esp3I (New England Biolabs) or BsaI (New England Biolabs), or BpiI (Thermo Fisher Scientific), 20 fmol of each plasmid or PCR product and adequate amount of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ . The reaction mixture was incubated in a thermocycler for $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ 5 min and $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ 10 min for 15–30 cycles. The reaction mixture was transformed immediately into $E$ . coli strain DH5α. 

For transient expression in N. benthamiana, genes of CfTPS2, CfTPS3, CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16, CfACT1-8 were amplified by PCR, and assembled with CaMV 35S promoter (35Spro) and OCS terminator (OCSter) directly into plant expression vector pBI121-GG. To produce the plant expression construct pForskolin-35S, the 35Spro, OCSter and biosynthetic genes (CfTPS2, CfTPS3, CYP76AH15, CYP76AH11, CYP76AH16, CfACT1-8) were amplified by PCR. The 35Spro and OCSter were assembled with biosynthetic genes separately into pUC19-GG by Esp3I-based GGA reaction, forming individual expression cassettes of 35S::CfTPS2::OCS, 35S::CfTPS3::OCS, 35S::CYP76AH15::OCS, 35S::CYP76AH11::OCS, 35S::CYP76AH16::OCS and 35S::CfACT1-8::OCS. These plasmids were mixed and assembled into pBI121-GG by BsaI-based GGA reaction, generating the plant expression construct pForskolin-35S. Similarly, to produce the pForskolin-CBTS plasmid, the CBTS promoter (CBTSpro) and OCSter were assembled with biosynthetic genes separately via Esp3I-based GGA reaction, and the resultant expression cassette plasmids were assembled into pBI121-GG by BsaI-based GGA reaction. 

To produce construct for genome editing, the 35Spro, SpCas9 and OCSter were amplified by PCR and assembled into pUC19-GG by Esp3I-based GGA reaction, producing the expression cassette of 35S::SpCas9::OCS. The Arabidopsis U6-26 promoter and sgRNA backbone were amplified and assembled into pUC19-GG, generating the AtU6::sgRNA1 and AtU6::sgRNA2 modules. The plasmids of 35S::SpCas9::OCS, AtU6::sgRNA1 and AtU6::sgRNA2 were assembled into pBI121-GG by BsaI-based GGA reaction, generating the plasmid for genome editing. Primers used for making constructs are listed in Table S1. 

# 4.3 | Transient Expression in N. Benthamiana

The plasmids for transient expression were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 by the freeze–thaw method (Hofgen and Willmitzer 1988). The transformed A. tumefaciens colonies were inoculated into 2 mL of LB medium supplemented with $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ rifampicin, $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ gentamicin and $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin and grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . The culture was centrifuged at $8 0 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ for 5 min at room temperature, and the cell pellet was resuspended in infiltration buffer containing $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 10 mM 2-N-morpholino-ethanesulfonic acid (MES) and $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ acetosyringone (pH 5.6) to a final $\mathrm { { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.2. Equal volumes of bacterial suspensions were mixed and infiltrated into N. benthamiana (4-week-old) leaves using a syringe without a needle. After infiltration, the plants were grown for 5 additional days in the growth chamber. Samples from the infiltrated leaves were analysed for forskolin accumulation. The experiment was conducted three times and the results are shown from representative experiments. 

# 4.4 | Tobacco Transformation and Characterisation

Tobacco (WT and cbts mutant) seeds were surface sterilised in $7 0 \%$ ethanol for 1 min, in $2 \%$ (v/v) sodium hypochlorite solution for $5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , rinsed with sterilised water four times and then germinated on MS medium containing $1 5 \mathrm { g / L }$ sucrose and $8 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ agar at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. Leaves from 2-month-old plants were used for transformation. 

Plasmids (pForskolin-35S and pForskolin-CBTS) were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain LBA4404 via the freeze–thaw method. Overnight cultures of A. tumefaciens were pelleted and resuspended in MS liquid medium to $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 6$ . The leaf discs were immersed in the bacterial suspension for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , blotted dry on sterilised filter paper, and incubated for 2 days at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. The explants were then transferred onto callus induction medium (MS medium, $3 0 \mathrm { g / L }$ sucrose, $8 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ agar, $2 \mathrm { m g / L }$ 6-benzyladenine (6-BA), $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin and $2 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ cefotaxime, pH 5.8) and sub-cultured every 4 weeks. Shoots were cut and transferred to rooting medium (MS medium, $3 0 \mathrm { g / L }$ sucrose, $8 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ agar, $\mathrm { 1 0 0 m g / L }$ kanamycin, pH 5.8). Plants with developed roots were transferred to soil and grown in the greenhouse for further analysis. 

Genomic DNA was extracted from WT and transgenic tobacco plants using DNeasy Plant mini kits (Qiagen, Cat. No. 69204). Incorporation of T-DNA into the tobacco genome was confirmed by PCR amplification of kanamycin resistance gene (NPTII) using primers listed in Table S1. 

# 4.5 | Transcriptome Analysis

Total RNA was extracted from the leaves and GTs of 16-week-old plants $( n = 7 )$ , frozen immediately in liquid nitrogen, and ground into fine powder. The total RNA was extracted using the TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, 15 596 018) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, TRIzol reagent (1 mL) was added to the sample $( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } )$ ), mixed vigorously and incubated at room temperature for 5 min. Then, chloroform $( 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } )$ was added, mixed vigorously and incubated at room temperature for 2 min, and centrifuged at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ for $1 5 \mathrm { m i n }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The aqueous phase (about $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } ,$ ) was mixed with equal volume isopropanol and centrifuged at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The RNA pellet was washed with $7 5 \%$ ethanol (1 mL), vacuum-dried, and dissolved in $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of RNase-free $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ . The RNA integrity was evaluated using the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, USA), and the RNA concentration was determined with the NanoDrop2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, USA). The cDNA libraries were constructed using VAHTS Universal V6 RNA-seq Library Prep Kit according to the manufacturer's instructions, and sequenced on the lllumina Novaseq 6000 platform (150 bp paired-end). Low quality reads were removed using Fastp program (Chen et al. 2018). The clean reads were mapped to the tobacco genome (Edwards et  al.  2017) using HISAT2 with default parameters (Kim et  al.  2019). Transcript abundance was quantified using HTSeq 2.0 (Putri et al. 2022). Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed to compare the FPKM values of expressed genes using the PRCOMP function in R. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified 

with criteria of $| \log _ { 2 } ( { \mathrm { F o l d C h a n g e } } ) | > 1$ and $\mathsf { q }$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ using the DESeq2 package (Love et al. 2014). Kyoto Encyclopaedia of Gene and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis was performed using ClusterProfiler (Wu et al. 2021). 

# 4.6 | Quantitative Real-Time PCR (qRT-PCR)

Tobacco leaf and GT samples collected from WT and cbts plants were frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground into fine powder. Total RNA was extracted using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen, Germany). One microgram of total RNA was treated with DNase I and reverse transcribed using the SuperScript III First-Strand Synthesis System for RT-PCR (Life Technologies, USA). The qRT-PCR reactions were performed using Power SYBR Green PCR MASTER MIX (Applied Biosystems, USA) on the 7500 Fast Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, USA). The relative transcript level was calculated with the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method using the built-in 7500 software (v2.0). Tobacco elongation factor 1α (EF-1α, AF120093) was used as the reference gene (Schmidt and Delaney 2010). Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. 

# 4.7 | Forskolin Extraction and Analysis

Leaves from N. benthamiana transient expression and all samples collected from tobacco plants were frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground into fine powder. Methanol (1mL) was added to the sample $\mathrm { ( 5 0 m g ) }$ , mixed vigorously and extracted at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3h. After centrifugation at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ for 15min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the supernatant was dried, dissolved in 1mL methanol: $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ solution (80:20), and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter. The extracts were analysed using the Q Exactive Plus Orbitrap LC–MS/MS System (Thermo Scientific). 

Chromatography separation was performed on a Phenomenex Kinetex C18 $3 \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $2 . 6 \mu \mathrm { m }$ particle size). The injection volume of sample was $5 . 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ with a flow rate of $0 . 4 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the column temperature was maintained at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .Themobile phase composed of solution A (aqueous solution containing $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid and $1 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ ammonium acetate) and solution B (acetonitrile). The 19-min gradient method was as follows: $1 0 \%$ B from 0.0 to $0 . 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , linear increasing to $9 8 \%$ B at $1 1 . 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , holding until 15 min, followed by a return to $1 0 \%$ B and holding from 15.1 to 19 min. 

Quantitative analysis of forskolin was performed in positive and negative ionisation modes. The MS parameters were: spray voltage $+ 3 . 5 0 \mathrm { k V }$ (positive) and $- 3 . 5 0 \mathrm { k V }$ (negative); capillary temperature $2 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; sheath gas flow rate 40 Arb; aux gas flow rate 10 Arb; aux gas heater temperature $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Data were acquired under the Full MS/dd-MS2 (TopN) scanning mode (scanning range m/z 100–1200, full mass resolution 70 000; dd-MS2 resolution 17 500). Data were proceeded by Xcalibur 4.1 software (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). 

# 4.8 | Analysis of GGPP, GPP and FPP

The measurement of GGPP, GPP and FPP was performed as described (Chhonker et al. 2018; Zita et al. 2023). Briefly, tobacco 

root, leaf and GT samples $\left( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } \right)$ were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen, and extracted with 1 mL acetonitrile: water $( 8 0 { : } 2 0 , \mathrm { v / v ) }$ at room temperature for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . After centrifugation at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the supernatant was collected for LC–MS/MS analysis on the equipment described above. The mobile phase consisted of $0 . 0 5 \%$ $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { O H }$ (phase A) and acetonitrile containing $0 . 0 5 \%$ $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { O H }$ (phase B). The flow rate was $0 . 4 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The gradient started at $5 \%$ phase B and increased linearly to $5 0 \%$ B in $5 . 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , then to $1 0 0 \%$ B in $0 . 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and kept for $2 . 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) transitions for GGPP, GGP and FPP were $\mathrm { m / z 4 4 9 . 2 > 7 9 . 1 }$ , $3 1 3 . 1 > 7 9 . 1$ and $2 8 1 . 2 > 7 9 . 1$ , respectively. 

# 4.9 | Analysis of CBT-Diols

Tobacco leaf and flower samples $( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } )$ ) were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen, and extracted with 1 mL $n$ -hexane containing $2 \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ nonyl acetate (Sigma-Aldrich, S364258) at room temperature for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . After centrifugation at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ for 5 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the supernatant was collected, and analysed using an Agilent 7890B gas chromatography coupled with an Agilent 5977 quadrupole mass analyser. The chromatographic separation was performed on a DB-5MS fused-silica capillary column $( 3 0 \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m } \times 0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , Agilent, CA, USA) with helium as the carrier gas at a flow rate of $1 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The injector temperature was maintained at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The oven temperature was ramped from $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (held for 3 min) to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (at $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n } )$ ), and held at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min. The MS quadrupole and ion source temperatures were set to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The electron impact (EI) collision energy was $7 0 \mathrm { e V } .$ . Mass data were acquired in full-scan mode $\mathrm { ( m / z ~ 5 0 - 5 0 0 ) }$ . Metabolites were identified by comparing the mass spectra with the NIST Mass Spectral Library (NIST14).
