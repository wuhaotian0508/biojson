# Summary

Variation in anthocyanin biosynthesis in pear fruit provides genetic germplasm resources for breeding, while dwarfing is an important agronomic trait, which is beneficial to reduce the management costs and allow for the implementation of high-density cultivation. Here, we combined bulked segregant analysis (BSA), quantitative trait loci (QTL), and structural variation (SV) analysis to identify a 14-bp deletion which caused a frame shift mutation and resulted in the premature translation termination of a B-box (BBX) family of zinc transcription factor, PyBBX24, and its allelic variation termed PyBBX24DN14. PyBBX24DN14 $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ overexpression promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis in pear, strawberry, Arabidopsis, tobacco, and tomato, while that of PyBBX24 did not. PyBBX24DN14 directly activates the transcription of PyUFGT and PyMYB10 through interaction with PyHY5. Moreover, stable overexpression of $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ exhibits a dwarfing phenotype in Arabidopsis, tobacco, and tomato plants. $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ can activate the expression of $P y G A 2 0 x 8$ via directly binding to its promoter, thereby deactivating bioactive GAs and reducing the plant height. However, the nuclear localization signal (NLS) and Valine-Proline (VP) motifs in the C-terminus of PyBBX24 reverse these effects. Interestingly, mutations leading to premature termination of PyBBX24 were also identified in red sports of un-related European pear varieties. We conclude that mutations in $P y B B { \times } 2 4$ gene link both an increase in pigmentation and a decrease in plant height. 

# Introduction

Pear (Pyrus spp.) fruits typically can be classified into green, yellow, russet brown, and red based on their peel colour (Wu et al., 2013; Xue et al., 2017), of which red cultivars account for a smaller proportion and with limited available germplasm, especially in Asian pears (Feng et al., 2010; Wu et al., 2018). The red colour phenotype is due to the accumulation of anthocyanin, which have diverse physiological functions, including protection against ultraviolet radiation, defence against phytopathogens, and the capture of reactive oxygen species (Albert et al., 2014; Winkel-Shirley, 2001). These components also determine fruit maturity and quality for consumers, as well as providing an excellent source of antioxidants with benefits to human health (Allan et al., 2008; Einbond et al., 2004). 

Two major classes of genes responsible for anthocyanin biosynthesis have been extensively characterized in many species (Holton and Cornish, 1995; Winkel-Shirley, 2001). One encodes biosynthetic enzymes such as phenylalanine ammonialyase (PAL), cinnamate 4-hydroxylase (C4H), p-coumaroyl coenzyme A ligase (4CL), chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H), dihydroflavonol 4-reductase (DFR), anthocyanidin synthase (ANS), and UDP-glucose:flavonoid 

3-O-glucosyltranferase (UFGT), and another covers various regulatory factors controlling transcription of these anthocyanin biosynthetic genes. The MYB-bHLH-WDR (MBW) complex is a well-known conserved regulatory network containing MYB transcription factors (TFs), basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) TFs, and WD repeat proteins. This complex is considered as the core regulatory mechanism for controlling anthocyanin biosynthesis (Albert et al., 2014; Gonzalez et al., 2008; Hichri et al., 2011; Zhao et al., 2023). To date, a number of TFs that mediate anthocyanin accumulation and contribute to fruit coloration in association with the MBW complex have been identified, including NAC (Zhou et al., 2015; Zou et al., 2023), WRKY (An et al., 2019b; Li et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021), ERF (Ni et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2017), and HY5 (An et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020). HY5 acts upstream of MYB proteins and anthocyanin structural enzymes and regulates their expression by directly binding to G-box motifs within their promoters (Bai et al., 2019b; Wang et al., 2020). In addition, several BBX proteins were identified as HY5 co-factors and antagonistically regulate light-induced fruit coloration (An et al., 2019a; Bai et al., 2019a,b; Fang et al., 2019). 

In pear, some varieties have gained or lost the ability to synthesize anthocyanins in fruits, including ‘Red Zaosu’, ‘Max Red 

Bartlett’, and ‘Red D’Anjou’. In these natural mutants, anthocyanin content was elevated, providing genetic resources for red pear breeding. Natural variations in coloration can be explained by altered transcriptional patterns in many species, often resulting from point mutations, insertions, deletions, and transposable elements. Studies on flower colour polymorphisms have revealed that the mutations in coding or promoter regions of biosynthetic genes CHS (Habu et al., 1998), DFR (Inagaki et al., 1994), ANS (Hisatomi et al., 1997), and UF3GT (Kroon et al., 1994) could alter flower pigmentation. Similarly, mutations in the MYB genes in grape (Vitis vinifera) (Kobayashi et al., 2004), apple (Malus 9 domestica) (Espley et al., 2009), citrus (Citrus sinensis) (Butelli et al., 2012), and strawberry (Fragaria spp.) (Castillejo et al., 2020) cause gain or loss of fruit anthocyanin. Additionally, mutations of the bHLH gene, ANTHOCYANIN1 (AN1), in petunia lead to a white flower phenotype (Spelt et al., 2002), and mutations in the bHLH genes in pea (Hellens et al., 2010) and in Antirrhinum majus have been linked to anthocyanin accumulation (Albert et al., 2021). These characterized mutations in anthocyanin-related genes result in a loss or a restricted distribution of anthocyanins. 

Dwarfing and vigour control are essential traits for reducing labour demand and management costs, so allowing for the implementation of high-density cultivation (El-Sharkawy et al., 2012; Hollender et al., 2016). Plant dwarfism is often associated with mutations in phytohormone pathways, particularly gibberellins (GAs) and brassinolides (BRs). GAs are a major class of growth regulators controlling cell division and elongation throughout plant developmental processes (De Lucas et al., 2008). Changes in GA levels, or the expression of the genes involved in GA metabolism and perception, have a strong effect on plant height. For example, overexpression of GA 2-oxidase 5 (GA2ox5) induces a strong dwarfing phenotype due to a decline in bioactive GA levels in poplar (Gou et al., 2011), while the dwarfing phenotype of the stumpy mutant is due to GA deficiency (Busov et al., 2003). Many TFs also participate in the regulation of plant height. In apple, MdWRKY9 overexpression induces a dwarfing phenotype through directly suppressing the transcription of MdDWF4, thereby regulating BR-mediated dwarfism in apple rootstock ‘M26’ (Zheng et al., 2018). A recent report showed that the pear TFs PcBZR1 and PcBZR2 could suppress the transcription of an arabinogalactan protein 7-like gene, PcAGP7-1, thereby inhibiting stem elongation (Zheng et al., 2022). 

In fruit trees, several dwarf (Dw) loci have been identified, such as Dw in peach, caused by a non-sense recessive mutation in GID1c on chromosome (Chr) 6 (Hollender et al., 2016). In the apple rootstock ‘M9’, two major QTLs, Dw1 on linkage group (LG) 5 and Dw2 on LG11, primarily control dwarfism (Foster et al., 2015). The Dw of P. communis is determined by a single dominant gene mapped on LG16 (Wang et al., 2011a). In rootstock-induced dwarfing pear, the genetic control of Dw1 is located on LG5 and LG6, syntenic to the apple Dw1 loci (Foster et al., 2015; Kn€abel et al., 2015). Despite several gene candidates and QTLs being associated with plant dwarfism in fruit crops, little is known about the underlying mechanisms. 

In this study, we showed that two traits, red-peel colour and dwarfism, co-segregate in a pear $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ population derived from a cross between pear ‘Red Zaosu’ and ‘Cuiyu’. Using multi-omics analysis including bulked segregant analysis (BSA), quantitative trait loci (QTL), and whole-genome sequencing, we mapped the QTLs associated with both traits onto LG4 and identified a 

candidate gene, of the B-box (BBX) family of zinc transcription factors, $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ . Functional validation revealed that $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ promoted anthocyanin biosynthesis and reduced plant height. Additionally, the nucleus localization signal (NLS) and Valine-Proline (VP) motifs in the C-terminus of PyBBX24 may have inhibitory activity suppressing its own transcriptional activity. Further work showed that variations of PyBBX24 also occur in other un-related red sports of European pear (P. communis), confirming that this gene is a major determinant for red-peeled pear varieties. These findings reveal a link between red coloration and plant dwarfism and show functional diversity of BBX TFs, to provide a precious genetic resource for molecular-assisted breeding. 

# Results

# Red coloration is genetically linked with dwarf plants in an $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ population of ‘Red Zaosu’ pear

The Chinese pear ‘Zaosu’ is a hybrid of ‘Pingguoli’ $\times$ ‘Mishirazu’, and its red sport called ‘Red Zaosu’ which produces red foliage and red-peeled fruits (Qian et al., 2014). Given the differential accumulation of anthocyanin in fruits between ‘Zaosu’ and ‘Red Zaosu’, a cross of ‘Red Zaosu’ $\times$ ‘Cuiyu’ (green-peeled pear) was examined for the genetic basis of red coloration formation (Figure 1a,b). This study used a total of $3 0 7 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ individuals comprising plants with red foliage (red plants) and green foliage (green plants), which gave a segregation ratio of 163:144 (Figure S1). These colour phenotypes were evident in the stems, leaves, and fruit peel (Figure 1c). Anthocyanin content was significantly higher in young stems, leaves, and fruit peel of red progeny as compared to those of green progeny (Figure 1d). This segregation ratio indicated that the red phenotype is controlled by a monogenic, dominant gene that affects both vegetative and reproductive tissues. 

A further observation was made that the growth of the red plants appeared obviously slower than the green plants (Figure 1e). Hence, we measured the plant height for all of the individuals of the population at nine time points throughout the course of an entire season. This revealed dwarfism in the red plants, with a proportional maximum and minimum height of $7 6 . 1 \%$ and $4 3 . 1 \%$ compared to the green progeny (Figure 1f). This observation led us to term the two progeny groups as ‘reddwarf’ and ‘green-standard’, respectively, and highlights the genetic linkage between red coloration and dwarfism.

Next, we constructed a high-density genetic map with 307 individuals to fine map red coloration on the LG4 (Figure 2b,c), and a steep peak overlapped with the genomic regions (Chr4: 68 548–19 055 928). # $\mathsf { P y B B } \mathsf { X } 2 4 ^ { \Delta \mathsf { N } 1 4 }$ induces anthocyanin biosynthesis in pear, strawberry, and tobacco

To investigate the potential anthocyanin-related functions of $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ , we stably overexpressed both variants of PyBBX24 in pear calli, and with transient overexpression in pear and strawberry fruits. Red coloration was seen in pear calli, pear fruits, and strawberry fruits carrying $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ , but not PyBBX24, wild-type (WT), and empty vector (EV) control (Figure 3a–c). Quantification of anthocyanin content in pear calli, pear fruits, and strawberry fruits correlated with the observed phenotypes (Figure 3d). Moreover, CRISPR/Cas9-mediated mutagenesis of PyBBX24 was performed in pear calli. The single nucleotide or small deletions or single nucleotide insertions at different frequencies were detected in three transgenic lines of pear calli, which caused frame-shift mutations in exon 3 of PyBBX24 (Figure S4a–c). There was significant anthocyanin accumulation in calli harbouring these edits (Figure S4d,e). A significant rise in transcript abundance for the key biosynthetic and regulatory genes including PyDFR, PyANS, and PyUFGT in transgenic pear calli overexpressed $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ (Figure 3e) and knocked out PyBBX24 (Figure S4f) were shown by qPCR. Similar pattern was seen in the transgenic strawberry fruits (Figure S5). These results show that PyBBX24DN14 promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis. 

To explore the differences in anthocyanin biosynthesis induced by PyBBX24 and PyBBX24DN14 in pear. A dual-luciferase reporter assay in N. benthamiana leaves was used to test whether PyBBX24DN14 could activate the key the anthocyanin-related genes PyDFR, PyANS, and PyUFGT and for the anthocyaninrelated transcription factors, PyMYB10 and PyMYB114. Infiltration of $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ significantly activated the promoters of PyUFGT and PyMYB10, but no significant activation was seen of the promoters of PyDFR, PyANS, or PyMYB114 (Figure 3f). Furthermore, when $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ was co-infiltrated with three truncated promoter sequences of PyUFGT (P1, P2, and P3), respectively, it was able to activate P2 and P3 sequences, but not P1 sequence. When $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ was co-infiltrated with three truncated promoter sequences of PyMYB10 (P1, P2, and P3), respectively, all three promoter sequences could be significantly 

activated (Figure S6). In contrast, no significant activation by full-length PyBBX24 was seen for all the examined promoter sequences. These results indicate that $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ specifically activates the transcription of PyUFGT and PyMYB10 through specific target sites in their promoters. 

Analysis was conducted of cis-acting elements in the PyUFGT and PyMYB10 promoter regions using PlantCARE (http:// bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html). This predicted the presence of G-box elements that are known target motifs for the HY5 and BBX proteins (Abbas et al., 2014; Xiong et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2016). An electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) was designed, using biotin-labelled probes synthesized corresponding to two G-box elements ( 322 and 461 bp) in the promoter of PyUFGT, and one G-box element ( 206 bp) in the PyMYB10 promoter to test whether PyBBX24DN14 can directly bind these promoters. EMSA showed that no binding bands were detected when the fused $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ -MBP and PyBBX24-MBP proteins were incubated with the probes, respectively (Figure S7), suggesting that PyBBX24DN14 and PyBBX24 might not directly target the promoters of PyUFGT and PyMYB10.

# $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ contributes to dwarfism in Arabidopsis, tobacco, and tomato

Since $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N / 4 }$ was also associated with the plant height trait, mapping to the same region of LG4, we sought to test whether $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ was involved in dwarfism. We performed stable transformation in Arabidopsis, tobacco, and tomato. The 35S::PyBBX24DN14-GFP construct was introduced into wild-type (WT) Arabidopsis Col-0, and three transgenic lines of T2 generation displayed shorter hypocotyls and a dwarf phenotype compared to WT (Figure 5a,b). Similar results were seen from Arabidopsis bbx24 mutant seedlings. Then, the 35S::PyBBX24- GFP construct was introduced into the Atbbx24 mutant; the short hypocotyl phenotype of Atbbx24 mutant could be complemented to normal levels, indicating that PyBBX24 promotes plant growth. 

The transgenic tobacco lines overexpressing $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ showed a severe dwarf phenotype with dark-green, compact leaves and severe shorter internodes as compared with both transgenic PyBBX24 and EV tobaccos (Figure 5c,d). Moreover, transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tomatoes exhibited reduced average height comparing with the transgenic PyBBX24 tomatoes (Figure 5e,f), suggesting that $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ reduces plant height. High constitutive expression levels of PyBBX24 and PyBBX24DN14 in the 6-day-old seedlings of T2 generation transgenic Arabidopsis, T0 generation of transgenic tobacco and tomato plants was verified by RT-qPCR (Figure S9a–c). Notably, overexpression of $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ resulted in higher anthocyanin content in Arabidopsis seedlings, tobacco flowers, and tomato leaves, flowers, and roots (Figure 5g–i). A moderate increase in expression of some anthocyanin biosynthetic genes was consistent with their 

red coloration phenotypes in the transgenic PyBBX24DN14 Arabidopsis (Figure S9d), indicating that $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ mediates both red coloration and dwarf phenotypes. 

# Dwarf phenotypes of $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ plants are due to a deficiency in active GAs

GAs play a critical role in stem elongation by expanding the cell size and number and has been extensively applied to rescue plant dwarfing (Magome et al., 2004; Otani et al., 2013; Schomburg et al., 2003; Wei et al., 2019). We thus performed $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment to test whether dwarfism in both $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ pear seedlingsand transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobaccos could be rescued by exogenous $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ . A total of 15 1-month-old red-dwarf pear seedlings of the $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ pear population were sprayed with $1 0 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { G A } _ { 3 } ,$ while the control, 15 1-month-old red-dwarf and green-standard pear seedlings, were sprayed with water, respectively. After treatment, the height of red-dwarf seedlings increased by $6 5 . 8 \%$ compared to the red-dwarf control, and increased by $2 4 . 5 \%$ compared to the green-standard control (Figure 6a,b). We also grew six transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobacco plants in the glasshouse, three of which were sprayed with $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 } ,$ , while the other three were treated with water and used as the control. After treatment, plant height of transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobaccos increased by 1.9-fold as compared to the control (Figure 6c,d). Similar results were observed in $5 ~ \mathsf { m g } \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ GA-treated transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobacco shoots sub-cultured in an MS medium, with a six-fold height increase compared to the untreated control (Figures 6e and S10). These findings demonstrate GA effectively reverses dwarf phenotypes in $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ pear populations and transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobacco plants. 

We further analysed the active GAs content in the red-dwarf and green-standard progenies and found that active GA content in the stems of red-dwarf seedlings was $4 6 \%$ of that observed in green-standard seedlings (Figure 6f). In transgenic tobaccos, there were no difference in bioactive GA content between the transgenic $P y B B { \times } 2 4$ tobaccos and EV control, but a significant reduction was found in the transgenic $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ tobaccos (Figure 6g). Therefore, the dwarfism phenotype is associated with a deficiency in bioactive GA content.

# Association between NLS and VP motifs and the repressive activity of PyBBX24

To understand the differential effects of PyBBX24 and $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ on anthocyanin biosynthesis and plant height, we focused on the two motifs in the C-terminal region of PyBBX24. The first motif is a putative NLS, mediating the transport of proteins into the nuclei (Cokol et al., 2000), while the second is a VP motif, important for interactions with the WD40 repeat domain of COP1 (Lau et al., 2019). As PyBBX24DN14 does not contain either NLS and VP motifs, we tested their importance by designing three mutated versions: PyBBX24DNLSDVP (lacking both NLS and VP), PyBBX24DVP (lacking VP), and $\mathsf { P y B B } \mathsf { X } 2 4 ^ { \Delta N L S }$ (lacking NLS) (Figure 7a). The transient expression in strawberry fruits of all three mutated versions produced varying degrees of red pigmentation (Figure 7b), with PyBBX24DNLSDVP showing highest intensity and anthocyanin content compared to $\mathsf { P y B B } \mathsf { X } 2 4 ^ { \Delta \mathsf { V P } }$ and $\mathsf { P y B B } \mathsf { X } 2 4 ^ { \Delta N L S }$ (Figure 7c). In tobacco leaves, the co-infiltration of the three mutated versions with PyMYB10 and PybHLH3 led to higher anthocyanin content compared to controls (Figure 7d,e). Dual-luciferase assays showed that all three mutants activated the transcription of PyUFGT, with the strongest activation observed in ${ \mathsf { P y B B } } { \mathsf { X } } 2 4 ^ { \Delta { \mathsf { N L S } } \Delta { \mathsf { V P } } }$ (Figure 7f), suggesting that both motifs repress PyBBX24 activity. 

To fully explore the biological role of these two motifs, PyBBX24DNLSDVP, $P y B B { \times } 2 4 ^ { 4 V P } ,$ , and $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N L S }$ were stably overexpressed in tobacco, respectively. Compared with the transgenic PyBBX24 tobacco, the transgenic $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N L S A V P }$ tobacco displayed a severely dwarf phenotype, the transgenic 

$P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { V P }$ tobacco displayed a moderately dwarf phenotype, while the transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N L S }$ tobacco showed no significant difference in height (Figure 7g,h). In addition, PyBBX24DNLSDVP and PyBBX24DVP can significantly activate the PyGA2ox8 promoter, while PyBBX24DNLS showed no ability compared with PyBBX24 (Figure 7i). This data suggests that this region, particularly the VP motif, represses PyBBX24 activity. 

# Allelic variation in PyBBX24 is associated with increases in anthocyanin in other pears

Previous reports show that the red-peel trait of the un-related European pear ‘Max Red Bartlett’ was on LG4 (Dondini et al., 2008; Kumar et al., 2019). We found that PyBBX24 was located in the genetic region between two SNPs (S161_15221 and S39_462595) (Kumar et al., 2019) through mapping the SNPs to the ‘Cuiguan’ pear genome (Gao et al., 2021) (Figure S13). We further tested if allelic differences could be detected in the coding region of PyBBX24 in ‘Max Red Bartlett’. Sequence analysis revealed that a single-nucleotide mutation (C to T) at the position of base 544 was identified in ‘Max Red Bartlett’, but not in ‘Bartlett’ (Figure S14). This mutation altered the amino acid Gln (Q) to a stop codon and resulted in a loss of NLS and VP motifs in the C-terminus of PyBBX24, namely $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \mathsf { C 5 4 4 7 } }$ . Function validation showed that PyBBX24C544T can induce anthocyanin accumulation in pear calli, pear and strawberry fruits, while significantly enhancing the transcriptional activation of PyUFGT and PyMYB10 (Figure S15). Moreover, stable transformation of $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { C 5 4 4 T }$ caused a dwarf phenotype in tobacco compared with the EV control (Figure S16), and the dual-luciferase assay revealed that ${ \mathsf { P y B B } } { \times } 2 4 ^ { \mathsf { C 5 4 4 T } }$ was able to activate the PyGA2ox8 promoter. These observations demonstrate that $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \mathsf { C 5 4 4 7 } }$ has a similar impact as $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ to regulate red coloration and dwarf phenotypes due to the lack of the NLS and VP motifs. 

A 14-bp deletion or a stop codon/point mutation in PyBBX24 induced a gain-of-function mutation in ‘Red Zaosu’ and ‘Max Red Bartlett’, suggesting independent colour selections derived from distinct mutations of PyBBX24. Through sequencing the coding region of PyBBX24 from a collection of germplasm accessions, including six European cultivars (‘Red Ruby Bay Blush’, ‘Red Sensation’, ‘Doyenne du Comice’, ‘Crimson Gem’, ‘Anjou’, ‘Red D’Anjou’) and eight Asian cultivars (‘Cuiyu’, ‘Fojianxi’, ‘Miantianhong’, ‘Yunhong NO.1’, ‘Huobali’, ‘Kuerlexiangli’, ‘Hongxiangsu’, ‘Hongsucui’) (Figure 8a), we found that all three red sports contained mutations for PyBBX24, but no such mutations were found in varieties with green- and red-blushed peels (Figure 8b and S14). Specifically, the 14-bp deletion was identified in ‘Red Ruby Bay Blush’, ‘Crimson Gem’, and ‘Red D’Anjou’, respectively, and both the 14-bp deletion and the stop codon point mutation at base 544 (C to T) were found in ‘Crimson Gem’. Interestingly, a novel single base mutation at the position of base 556 (G to T) was discovered in ‘Red D’Anjou’, which was predicted to lead to a stop codon instead of Glu (E) residue, resulting in loss of both NLS and VP motifs. There were no other allelic variations in PyBBX24 in the Asian pear cultivars. These observations provide further evidence that mutations in PyBBX24 cosegregate with increases in anthocyanins in different pear accessions and that these mutations have been selected for independently on several occasions.

# Multi-omics analysis reveals the genetic architecture of red peel coloration and dwarfism in pear

In pear, most fruit coloration studies have focused on the regulation mechanisms of anthocyanin accumulation, and despite recent reports of several fruit coloration QTLs, only a few genes have been cloned and validated. In Asian pear, a major QTL for red-blushed pears was previously identified on LG5 through analysing an Asian pear population crossed between the cultivars ‘Mantianhong’ and ‘Hongxiangsu’ (Xue et al., 2017). Our previous studies confirmed the red-blushed trait was on LG5 analysed by QTL mapping using an $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid population derived from a red-blushed Asian pear ‘Bayuehong’, and identified the PyMYB114 gene which was responsible for red-blushed pears (Yao et al., 2017). In European pears, by analysing seven crosses of European cultivars with red- and green-peel colour phenotypes, Dondini et al. reported that the red colour of ‘Max Red Bartlett’ was controlled by a single dominant gene located on LG4 (Dondini et al., 2008). A similar result was also found by using a genome-wide association study (GWAS) approach on an interspecific population derived from ‘Max Red Bartlett’ progenies (Kumar et al., 2019). Although PyMYB10 is a key activator in anthocyanin biosynthesis, it is not the determinant for ‘Max Red Bartlett’, as it is on LG9 (Pierantoni et al., 2010). Recently, the genetic control of the red foliage and red peel traits of the Asian pear ‘Red Zaosu’ was localized to the same QTL as that reported in ‘Max Red Bartlett’, indicating that the genetic control of ‘Red Zaosu’ may be the same as, or closely related to, ‘Max Red Bartlett’ (Xue et al., 2018). Subsequently, the B-box zinc finger BBX24 gene with a 14-bp deletion mutation in ‘Red Zaosu’ was identified using bulked segregant analysis (Ou et al., 2020). However, it remains unknown whether $B B X 2 4$ gene was the determinant for red peel colour. These results suggest the genetic mechanisms behind red coloration differ between most European pears and Asian pears. 

In the present study, we constructed an F1 pear population derived from a cross of ‘Red Zaosu’ $\times$ ‘Cuiyu’, which co-segregated at 1:1 

with red/green foliage and red-/green-peeled fruits, along with the 1:1 ratio with dwarf and non-dwarf plants. Our initial mapping of SNPs linked to the red peel trait on LG4 using BSA-seq and QTL analysis (Figure 2a–c), which is consistent with previous reports (Ou et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2018). Moreover, the dynamic QTL analysis mapped the dwarfing phenotype on LG4, supporting the observation that red colour co-segregates dwarfing plants. We identified a Bbox zinc finger protein PyBBX24DN14 on LG4 through analysis of SVs and RNA-seq date. PyBBX24DN14 $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ overexpression in pear calli, pear and strawberry fruits, Arabidopsis, and tobacco plants confirmed that it acts as an active allele of PyBBX24 in controlling both red peel coloration and plant dwarfism (Figure 9). Building on the knowledge that red peel trait of ‘Max Red Bartlett’ was anchored to LG4 (Dondini et al., 2008; Kumar et al., 2019; Xue et al., 2018), we found that the $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { C 5 4 4 T }$ allele was located in the interval between two SNPs (S161_15221 and S39_462595) in the previous report (Kumar et al., 2019) and is the causal gene for peel colour variation in ‘Max Red Bartlett’ (Figure S14 and S15). 

According to genetic mapping and functional verification, we determined that variants of PyBBX24 are link to both red colour of fruit and dwarfing traits. Single genes that simultaneously control multiple complex traits have been found in crops. In rice, ABNORMAL FLOWER AND DWARF1 (AFD1) can regulate plant height, floral development, and grain yield (Ren et al., 2016). In addition to plant height and yield, OsMYB110 also controls lodging resistance (Wang et al., 2023), while OsMADS17 simultaneously controls grain number and weight (Li et al., 2023). In apple, MdbHLH3 regulates several fruit quality traits, including fruit colour, malate, and soluble sugar accumulation (Xie et al., 2012; Yu et al., 2021, 2022). Fruit colour and dwarfism are both important agronomic traits. Although BBX family genes were previously reported to regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis, their role in association with the dwarfing trait remained unexplored.

# $\mathsf { P y B B } \mathsf { X } 2 4 ^ { \Delta \mathsf { N } 1 4 }$ is a repressor of plant height

BBX proteins play important roles in numerous cellular processes including seedling photomorphogenesis, flowering, abiotic and biotic stresses, and hormonal signalling networks (Gangappa and Botto, 2014). For example, Arabidopsis BBX18/DBB1a promotes hypocotyl elongation under blue light (Wang et al., 2011b), whereas bbx24 and bbx25 mutant seedlings develop short hypocotyls under light conditions (Gangappa et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2012). In contrast, Arabidopsis BBX21 represses hypocotyl elongation, while hypocotyl lengths of bbx21 mutant seedlings grow longer under same light conditions (Xu et al., 2017). However, the molecular mechanism for the BBXs in the regulation of plant dwarfism in fruit crops has never been reported. Here, we showed that PyBBX24 is a negative regulator of plant height, and overexpression of $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ led to a dwarf phenotype in Arabidopsis and tobacco. In Arabidopsis, BBX18/DBB1a promotes the hypocotyl elongation in response to blue light, which however could be impaired by a gibberellin (GA)-biosynthesis inhibitor. Moreover, in the BBX18/DBB1a overexpression plants, the expression of two GA metabolic and catabolic genes GA2ox1 and GA2ox8 is suppressed (Wang et al., 2011b), suggesting that BBX18/DBB1a is involved in the GA signalling pathway. Likewise, the expression of GA2ox1 is significantly decreased in Arabidopsis bbx21 mutant plants and increased in BBX21 transgenic lines (Xu et al., 2017). These results suggest that BBX TFs can regulate transcription of the genes in the GA metabolic pathway. In this study, the total bioactive GA level in stems of red-dwarf plants was significantly lower than that in green-standard pear plants (Figure 6f), and a similar patten was observed in the transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobaccos compared with the transgenic PyBBX24 tobaccos and EV control (Figure 6g), while application of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ can fully or partially rescue the dwarfing phenotypes of reddwarf pear plants and the transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobaccos. 

A previous report has shown that overexpression of AtGA2ox8 causes dwarfism in Arabidopsis, N. tabacum, and B. napus (Schomburg et al., 2003; Zhou et al., 2012). Our results revealed that PyGA2ox8 overexpression led to dwarfism in tobacco, which is similar to the height phenotype observed in transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobacco plants (Figure S11). RNA-seq date and RT-qPCR of pear stems showed that PyGA2ox8 was not expressed in green-standard but expressed in red-dwarf pear stems, suggesting that $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ is required for activation of GA2ox8 transcription. In support of these findings, the expression of NtGA2ox8 in transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobaccos was remarkably higher than that of in the transgenic PyBBX24 tobaccos and EV control, with no significant difference in height between the latter two (Figure S11). Moreover, transient overexpression assays and EMSA confirmed that $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ enhanced the transcription of PyGA2ox8 through directly binding to the G-box motif on its promoter (Figure 6h–j). Accordingly, PyBBX24DN14, but not PyBBX24, can directly activate the expression of $P y G A 2 0 x 8$ gene, inducing deactivation and degradation of the bioactive GAs, thereby resulting in plant dwarfism (Figure 9). Thus, we infer that PyBBX24DN14 induces GA-mediated plant dwarfism and may form a module of $\mathsf { P y B B } \times 2 4 ^ { \Delta N 1 4 }$ -PyGA2ox8 cascade signalling to inactivate GA and fine-tune plant height.

# Genetic map construction and QTL analysis

Young leaves of 307 individuals (red dwarf:green standard, 163:144) were collected for DNA extraction and quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping. The GBS sequencing data of $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid generation and WGS sequencing data of ‘Red Zaosu’ and ‘Cuiyu’ were used for SNP-calling and bin-marker construction. The sequencing reads were firstly trimmed for removal of the adapters and low-quality base using Trimmomatic v0.39 (Bolger et al., 2014). Then, the high-quality reads were mapped to the ‘Cuiguan’ pear genome (Gao et al., 2021) using Burrows-Wheeler Alignment v 0.7.16 (BWA) (Li and Durbin, 2009). SAMtools was used to convert the SAM (sequence alignment/map format) file into binary SAM (BAM) file, and the BAM file was further sorted and marked duplicated reads using Picard software (https://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/). The resulting BAM file was used as an input file for SNP calling and filtering using GATK v4.14.0 software (McKenna et al., 2010), and the SNP with minor 

allele frequency $( \mathsf { M A F } ) > 0 . 0 5$ , minor supporting read depth ${ \mathrm { > } } 6 ,$ and max-missing rate $< 0 . 0 5$ SNPs were extracted for the following analysis. 

The high-quality SNPs were converted into the three marker types $( \mathsf { n n } \times \mathsf { n p } ,$ , $\left| \mathsf { m } \times \mathsf { I I } _ { \iota } \right.$ , and ${ \mathsf { h k } } \times { \mathsf { h k } } )$ ) as described in Joinmap4.1 (Stam, 1993), and BinMarkers-v2 was used to create bin-markers (with $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { k b }$ window in this study) for reducing the redundant markers (https://github.com/lileiting/Binmarkers-v2). A total of 4651 bin-markers $( 8 8 7 \mathrm { h k } \times \mathrm { h k } , 2 3 6 5 \mathrm { ~ l m } \times \mathrm { l l } ,$ , and 1399 $\mathsf { n n } \times \mathsf { n p } )$ ) were constructed. The segregation rate of bin-markers was calculated and distorted markers (chi-square threshold of 0.001) were removed. Finally, 3840 bin-markers were extracted for linkage map construction (765 hk 9 hk, $1 9 2 0 \ | \mathrm { m } \times \mathrm { l } | ,$ and $1 1 5 5 \ : \mathsf { n m } \times \mathsf { n p } )$ ). Lmxll and hkxhk markers were used for maternal map construction, and nnxnp and hkxhk markers were used for paternal map construction. The consensus genetic map was merged using the common markers shared by maternal and paternal map using MergeMap software (Wu et al., 2010). QTL analysis was performed using the MapQTL v6.0 with the peel colour phenotype. An interval mapping model was chosen for plant height QTL analysis. Kruskal-Wallis (K-W) test was used for fruit peel colour QTL. The threshold for QTL significance was obtained by 1000 permutation tests $( P < 0 . 0 1$ ). 

For BSA-seq analysis, equal amounts of DNA of 15 green and 15 red plants were mixed to construct the red and green pools. Then a 350 bp DNA sequencing library of two parents (‘Red Zaosu’ and ‘Cuiyu’), red pool and green pool were constructed. The raw fastq data were trimmed to remove the adapters and low-quality reads using Trimmomatic v0.39 (Bolger et al., 2014), and the high-quality reads were mapped to the pear ‘Cuiguan’ 

genome (Gao et al., 2021) using Burrows-Wheeler Alignment v 0.7.16 (BWA) (Li and Durbin, 2009). SAMtools was used to convert the SAM (sequence alignment/map format) file into a binary SAM (BAM) file, and the BAM file was further sorted. Duplicated reads were marked using Picard software (https://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/). The resulting BAM file was used as an input file for SNP calling and filtering using GATK v4.14.0 software (McKenna et al., 2010). QTLseqr/R package was used to calculate the frequency and G statistic value for each SNP, and then 2 Mb window was used for calculating the tricubesmoothed $\mathsf { G } ^ { \prime }$ value. The threshold for QTL region identification was set with FDR rate lower than 0.01.

The CDSs of PyBBX24 and $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ were fused in-frame with green fluorescent protein (GFP) and cloned into the pCAM-BIA1301 vector driven by the CaMV 35S promoter using specific primers (Table S6), respectively. # Anthocyanin content analysis

The extraction of anthocyanins from pear peels, leaves, stems, transgenic pear calli, strawberry fruit, and Arabidopsis seedlings was followed as previously reported (Yao et al., 2017). The weighed fresh samples were ground into powder and suspended in methanol with $0 . 1 \%$ HCL at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ in a $1 0 \mathrm { - m L }$ tube. Then the supernatant was collected into new tubes after centrifugation of the mixture and used for quantification at 

530, 620 and 650 nm using UV–vis spectrophotometer (UV-1800, MAPADA).The anthocyanin content was calculated followed the Greey formula: $\mathsf { T A } = \mathsf { O D } \lambda \times V \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } / ( \mathbb { { E } } \lambda \times T ) . !$ Dk = (OD530–OD620)–0.1 (OD650–OD620), where TA represents total anthocyanin content (nmol $\mathfrak { g } ^ { - 1 }$ ). V for final volume (mL), and T for sample weight (g). $\mathfrak { E } \lambda = 4 . 6 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 4 }$ stands for the molar extinction coefficient at 530 nm. Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. The quantification of tobacco leaves and flowers was conducted based on previous protocols (Andre et al., 2012). Briefly, the freeze-dried samples were accurately weighed and placed into a $1 . 5 – \mathsf { m l }$ tube, then added 1 mL extraction buffer containing $80 \%$ methanol, $1 9 . 5 \%$ Milli-Q water, and $0 . 5 \%$ formic acid, and incubated on a shaking platform for 4 h at room temperature. Afterwards, samples were centrifuged for 10 min at $1 0 \ 0 0 0 \ 9 ,$ and collected the supernatants. An $8 0 0 – \mu \ L$ aliquot of the supernatant was subjected to rotatory evaporation and then dissolved in 500-lL of $20 \%$ methanol. It was then filtered to a vial for LC–MS analysis.

ChIP-qPCR assays were performed as previously reported with minor modification (Mao et al., 2020). The 35S::PyHY5-GFP, 35S::PyBBX24-GFP and 35S::PyBBX24DN14-GFP constructs were transformed into pear calli as described above. Briefly, 2-week-old transgenic pear calli were harvested and fixed in $1 \%$ formaldehyde for 15 min. Then, 2 M glycine was added to quench the cross-linking reaction for 10 min. Chromatin was extracted and sheared into 100–500 bp fragments by sonication (Diagenode, Bioruptor Plus). The chromatin complex was immunoprecipitated by the anti-GFP antibody (ab290; Abcam), anti-PyBBX24 antibody $( 1 . 4 7 \ m g \ m L ^ { - 1 }$ , Synthesized by ABclonal). The DNA purification was performed using the DNA purification kit. Enrichment of 

immunoprecipitated chromatin was detected by qPCR, and three replicates were performed. The IgG was used as the negative control for normalizing the ChIP enrichment fold in pear calli. Primers used for the ChIP-qPCR are listed in Table S6. 

# Application and quantitation of GA

Approximately 1-month-old red-dwarf seedlings of $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ population were sprayed with $1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ . Green/red pear seedlings grown uniformly were sprayed with water, used as the control. Three transgenic tobacco lines overexpressing $P y B B \times 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ grown in the soil were sprayed with $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ , and three water-treated transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { A N 1 4 }$ tobacco lines were used as the control. The newly sub-cultured transgenic $P y B B X 2 4 ^ { 4 N 1 4 }$ plants grown in the medium were treated with $5 ~ \mathsf { m g } \textsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ . For pear seedlings and transgenic tobaccos grown in the soli, treated plants were sprayed on the whole plants twice per day for 5 days. For tobacco lines grown in the medium, $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ was added in the selected medium. Approximately $0 . 5 ~ 9$ of fresh pear stems or freeze-dried tobacco leaf tissue were used for quantification. The procedures of extraction, purification, and quantitation of endogenous activated GAs were as previously described (Liu et al., 2018), and the levels of GAs $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 4 }$ , GA ) were determined by HPLC–MS/MS (Agilent 1290; Applied Biosystems 6500 Quadrupole Trap).
