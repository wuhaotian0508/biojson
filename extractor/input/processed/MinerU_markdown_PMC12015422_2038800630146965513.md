# The Spodoptera frugiperda L-aminoacylase degrades fatty acid-amino acid conjugates and promotes larvae growth on Zea mays

Bin Li 1,2, Mei Luo1 , Xiaofeng Liu1,3, Wangpeng Shi2 , Jinfeng Qi 4 , Shaoqun Zhou 1 & Guirong Wang 1,5 

Oral secretions (OS) contain diverse functional molecules that play important roles in the molecular interactions between insect herbivores and their host plants. Components of OS have been hypothesized to facilitate adaptation of specialized herbivores towards their preferred hosts. In this study, we identified an L-aminoacylase-encoding gene, SfruACY, that was preferentially up-regulated in the salivary glands of Spodoptera frugiderpa larvae when feeding on maize leaves compared to artificial diet. The protein product was confirmed to catalyze the in vitro degradation of fatty acid-amino acid conjugates (FACs), the classic plant defense elicitors commonly found in the OS of lepidopteran caterpillars. Generation of a homozygous SfruACY knock-out line with the CRISPR-Cas9 technology further revealed that the activity of this gene could promote the growth of S. frugiperda larvae on maize leaves but was not required for larvae growth on artificial diet. Finally, comparative transcriptomic analyses of maize leaves showed more pronounced inducible defense responses when attacked by the SfruACY knocked-out larvae than the wildtype intruders. These experimental evidences support that the inducible expression of SfruACY by maize leaf diet in the salivary glands of S. frugiperda larvae can lower the FAC contents in their OS, and hence facilitate larvae growth likely by inducing weaker plant defense responses. Our findings provide a mechanistic explanation for a longstanding observation that S. frugiperda larvae induce weaker plant defense responses, and shed light on transcriptional regulation as a potential mean for insect herbivores to adapt towards their preferred host plant species. 

Plants in nature have been constantly exposed to herbivorous insects ever since their colonization of the terrestrial environments. To cope with this perennial threat, plants have evolved diverse defense strategies against insect herbivores, ranging from toughened physical barriers to toxic or deterring metabolites1 . While some of these plant defensive traits are constitutively present as preventative measures, many others are produced ad hoc, presumably to conserve materials and energy for better growth and higher 

fertility2 . To obtain the heavily guarded plant nutrients for their own survival, insect herbivores have also developed various counter-defense methods to compromise the sophisticated plant defense mechanisms3 . 

In the last decade, fall armyworms (FAW, Spodoptera frugiperda) have emerged as an ideal model system to study the molecular and biochemical interactions between plants and insect herbivores. As an agricultural pest, FAW has been successfully managed by transgenic crops expressing Bacillus 

thuringiensis insecticidal proteins in its native range4 . Since 2016, the global spread of this invasive pest has posted a major threat to regional crop production and food security in parts of Africa and Asia, and its impact is expected to exacerbate with global climate change5–7 . Compared to other agricultural pests in the Spodoptera genus such as S. littoralis, FAW shows a clear preference towards grasses, especially maize (Zea mays) both in nature and under laboratory conditions8–10. This dietary specialization towards maize has been partially explained by two S. frugiperda UDPglycosyltransferases (UGTs) that could re-glycosylate and thereby deactivate benzoxazinoid aglucones, the characteristic defensive metabolites of maize11,12. Across the Spodoptera genus, presence-absence variation and differential transcriptional regulation of UGTs have been shown to explain the divergence among generalists and specialist species13–15. 

Besides detoxification, S. frugiperda larvae can also interfere with the inducible plant defense mechanisms of maize for their own success as another host-adaptive mechanism. For example, Ray et al. 16. reported that a maize-produced endochitinase protein present in the feces of S. frugiperda larvae could suppress the accumulation of insecticidal protease inhibitors and promote larvae survival. More recently, it has been shown that S. frugiperda larvae attack and treatment by their oral secretions (OS) induced significantly lower amount of emitted plant volatiles, indicating that the plant defense-suppressing signal cues may also exist in the ${ \mathrm { O S } } ^ { 1 7 }$ 

Insect OS sensu lato is a complex mixture of various glandular secretions and gut regurgitant spitted out by insect herbivores through their mouthparts during feeding of plant tissues. Despite its small volumes, insect OS is known to contain diverse functional molecules, and play an important role in the molecular interactions between plants and insect herbivores18,19. A class of the most prevalent bioactive molecules present in the lepidopteran insect OS is fatty acid amino acid conjugates (FACs). These ester molecules formed between plant-synthesized unsaturated 18-carbon fatty acid and insect-produced glutamines or glutamates can activate plant mitogenactivated protein kinase signaling cascades, jasmonic acid signaling, and the emission of herbivore-induce plant volatiles20–22. Biosynthesis of FACs occurs in the midgut, though the exact enzymes that catalyze this reaction remain unknown 23–2 5. On the other hand, the hydrolytic enzyme that breaks the ester bond and degrade FACs into fatty acids and amino acids, Laminoacylase, has been functionally characterized in a couple of species26–28. 

The quantitative profiles of FACs in insect OS can vary among different insect species. A survey of FACs in the OS of 29 lepidopteran species showed extensive variation, with $N _ { ☉ }$ -linolenoyl-L-glutamine and $N$ -linoleoyl-L-glutamine presence in all 29 species29. More recently, Ling et al. 30 have shown that five lepidopteran species that have a longer history of feeding on cultivated maize have lower FAC contents in their OS compared to two newlyemerged pests of maize due to higher rate of degradation31. This observation led us to hypothesize that FAC degradation may serve as a host-adaptive mechanism for insect herbivores. 

In this study, we identified an FAW L-aminoacylase gene (SfruACY) through comparative transcriptome analysis of larval salivary glands feeding on artificial diet (AD) or maize seedling leaves. Through in vitro enzymatic activity assays and characterization of a CRISPR-Cas knock-out mutant line (i.e., $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - } )$ ), we demonstrated that the SfruACY protein was involved in FAC degradation, lowering plant inducible defense responses, and thereby facilitated the survival of FAW larvae on maize. These findings reveal that FAW larvae can adapt towards its preferred host plant by lowering the content of plant defense elicitor molecules in their OS, and indicate SfruACY as a potential molecular target for precise management of this destructive agricultural pest. 

# Results

# Maize leaf diet induced expression of SfruACY in the larvae salivary glands

The salivary glands from fourth-instar FAW larvae were dissected for RNAseq analyses after feeding on artificial diet (AD) or maize leaves for 6 h (Fig. 1a). Principal component analysis of the resulting gene expression dataset showed that different diets can indeed influence the tissue-specific 

gene expression in FAW larvae salivary glands (Fig. 1b and Data S2). In total, we identified 136 up-regulated and 130 down-regulated genes in the maize leaves-fed larvae compared to those fed with AD (Fig. 1c and Data S3). Singular enrichment analyses of the Gene Ontology (GO) terms revealed that up-regulated genes by maize leaf diet were disproportionally involved in several ribosomal RNA-related biological processes, whereas downregulated genes were enriched towards aldehyde dehydrogenase activities (Data S4). Among maize leaf-induced genes, a putative $L$ -aminoacylaseencoding gene (SfruACY) became noteworthy because (1) its orthologs in other lepidopteran species are known to degrade FACs, and (2) its encoded protein has been previously identified in two published proteomics studies of FAW larvae OS26,32,33. $\mathrm { O S } ^ { 2 6 , 3 2 , 3 3 }$ 

To further determine the specificity of the maize diet-induced expression of SfruACY, we performed an independent qRT-PCR experiment on the salivary gland tissues isolated from larvae feeding on AD, maize leaves, and cotton leaves. As a result, both plant tissue diets could significantly induce SfruACY expression, but the induced level of expression was significantly higher in the maize leaf-fed group (Fig. 1d). By contrast, the inducibility of the ortholog of this gene in the generalist Helicoverpa armigera larvae salivary gland was comparable between either plant diet in the same experiment (Fig. S1). Tissue-specific qRT-PCR experiments showed that SfruACY was highly expressed in the guts and salivary glands of fourth-instar larvae, with very low expression in the Malpighian tubules and the fat bodies (Fig. 1e). 

In congruence to the homology-based annotation, the predicted peptide sequence of SfruACY showed $7 0 . 5 5 \%$ identity with the functionally characterized ACY protein of Heliothis virescens34. A homology search with the SfruACY peptide sequence as the query in six lepidopteran species and Drosophila melanogaster revealed that this gene was a conserved single-copy gene across these species, and the structure of the gene family tree was consistent with the phylogenetic relationships among these species (Fig. 1f). The expression patterns of SfruACY, in combination with its structural conserveness across the Lepidoptera Order, suggested that this protein may possess conserved hydrolytic activity towards FACs as its H. virescens ortholog2 6 

# SfruACY catalyzed FAC degradation in vitro

Since the expression levels of SfruACY in salivary glands varied by the larvae’s diet, we investigated the biological effect of this transcriptional difference by quantifying the contents of three major FACs (volicitin, Nhydroxylinoleoyl-L-glutamine, and N-linoleoyl-L-glutamine) in the OS of larvae fed with AD, maize leaves, or cotton leaves. While the contents of volicitin and N-hydroxylinoleoyl-L-glutamine were elevated to a similar level in maize leaf- and cotton leaf-fed larvae compared to those fed with AD, the N-linoleoyl-L-glutamine concentration was only induced in larvae fed with maize leaves (Fig. S2). Overall, the lack of consistency between the FAC contents in OS and the SfruACY gene expression in salivary glands suggested that the transcriptional regulation of SfruACY may not be the sole factor in determining the diet-induced FAC dynamics. 

To specifically test the catalytic activity of SfruACY protein, its coding sequence was cloned and expressed in the Escherichia coli strain BL21with a GST tag for in vitro enzymatic assay (Fig. 2a). Incubation of freshly collected FAW larvae OS with purified SfruACY-GST proteins resulted in significant depletion of the three major FACs when compared to the GST control groups (Fig. 2b–d). In the same experiment, the concentration of L-glutamine, the common amino acid moiety of the three FACs, was significantly increased after co-incubation with the purified SfruACY-GST protein (Fig. 2e). Results of this in vitro experiment supported that SfruACY could hydrolyze the major FACs found in the OS of FAW larvae in their corresponding amino acid and fatty acid moieties.

To confirm that SfruACY is involved in FAC hydrolysis in vivo, we collected OS from the $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutant and the wildtype larvae after transient feeding on maize leaves for FAC quantification. In accordance with the earlier observations on pupation rate and pupal weight, we found no significant difference in larvae fresh weight between the mutant strain and wildtype after feeding on AD for seven days. However, in the same experiments, mutant larvae grown on living maize seedlings were significantly smaller than the wildtype larvae (Fig. 3f, g). These results suggested that SfruACY was not involved in the normal larval growth and developmental processes, but could facilitate larval growth when feeding on maize leaf tissues. 

# SfruACY suppressed inducible defense-related gene expression in maize

Since SfruACY could degrade FACs in the OS of FAW larvae and promote larvae growth on maize plants but not on AD, we hypothesized that 

SfruACY was involved in the suppression of maize inducible defense response. To test this hypothesis, we performed a comparative transcriptomics analysis between maize seedling leaves infested with either wildtype or $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutant larvae. In the same experiment, nonattacked maize seedling leaves were also examined as a negative control. Principal component analysis revealed distinct transcriptomic profiles of the three groups of samples, suggesting that the two FAW genotypes elicited differential responses in maize gene expression (Fig. S5a and Data S5). Consistently, we found over five thousand differentially expressed genes (DEGs; up-regulated: 3467; down-regulated: 2055) between the SfruACY−/ −-infested and the non-infested groups, which almost doubled the number of DEGs identified between the wildtype-infested and the non-infested groups (up-regulated: 1720; down-regulated: 1099), indicating that the lack of SfruACY led the larvae to elicit more pronounced transcriptional responses in maize (Fig. S5b; Data S6 and 7). 

To further elucidate the influence of SfruACY on maize gene expression, we directly compared the transcriptomic data collected from maize leaf samples infested with either wildtype or SfruACY−/− mutant larvae. In result, 512 genes expressed higher in the mutant-infested group, whereas 102 genes showed the opposite expression pattern (Fig. 4a and Data S8). Gene Ontology term enrichment analyses of the up-regulated genes in the mutant-infested group showed significant over-representation of defenserelated genes, such as those involved in response to abscisic acid (GO:0009737), response to wounding (GO:0009611), response to ethylene (GO:0009723), defense response (GO:0006952), and response to jasmonic acid (GO: 0009753; Fig. 4b, c). # Discussions

Changes in diet are known to profoundly influence the physiology and gene expression of insect herbivores. Some coleopteran herbivores, for example, can adjust their gut digestive enzyme profiles in response to the induced plant protease inhibitors, hampering the protective effects of this plant inducible defense measure35. As another example, Roy et al. have shown that the gut microbiome of S. littoralis could be significantly affected by transgenerational diet changes36. In FAW, feeding on different plant species has been reported to elicit accumulation of different proteins in the larvae $\mathrm { O S } ^ { 3 2 }$ . Since the biochemical constituents of insect OS can in turn have significant 

impacts on the plant perception of and defense responses against insect herbivores, the diet-elicited changes in insect secretions have the potential to facilitate insect herbivores’ survival on host plants. In this study, we 

demonstrated that feeding on maize tissues induced the expression of SfruACY, leading to lowered FAC content in larvae OS, and weaker induction of plant defense response (Fig. 5).

Fig. 5 | A working model of SfruACY in FAW and maize interaction. The expression level of SfruACY was induced by the maize diet. Meanwhile, the larval SfruACY protein promoted FAC degradation in larval OS, attenuating inducible plant defense response and promoting larvae survival.


In a previous study, we analyzed whole-larvae transcriptomic data after feeding on AD or leaves of different maize cultivars for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , and found that most of the DEGs were associated with developmental processes, suggesting that feeding on a susceptible maize inbred line could promote maturationrelated gene expression37. In this study, we improved the spatiotemporal resolution of RNA-seq analyses, which facilitated the discovery of specific genes that are more directly relevant to the acclimation of FAW larvae towards the maize leaf diet (Fig. 1b, c). Consistent with its high expression in the salivary glands, the encoded protein of SfruACY has been previously identified in the larvae saliva in two quantitative proteomics study, but its function has not been specifically investigated32,33. In other lepidoptran larvae, the ACY protein has been primarily studied as a gut-expressed enzyme34,38,39. In congruence with these previous reports, we confirmed that the ACY orthologs were highly conserved across the Lepidoptera Order and was also highly expressed in the gut tissues of FAW larvae (Fig. 1e, f). 

The in vitro hydrolytic activities of ACY proteins towards the classic herbivore-associated molecular pattern, FACs, have been previously demonstrated for other Noctuid species26,39. After confirming these in vitro enzymatic activities of the ACY ortholog in FAW (Fig. 2), we further generated a homozygous knock-out mutant line with the CRISPR-Cas technology (Fig. 3a, b). This genetic mutant not only confirmed the FAChydrolytic function of SfruACY in vivo (Fig. 3c–e), but also allowed us demonstrated that this gene is required for FAW survival on maize but is not required for larvae growth and development on AD (Figs. 3f, g and S4). However, limited by the efficiency of gene editing by egg micro-injection, we were only able to obtain a single frameshift deletion allele of SfruACY. Hence, we could not fully rule out the possibility that the phenotypes observed in this study may be specific to this particular edited allele. This shortfall could be addressed by obtaining and testing more independent loss-of-function alleles of SfruACY in the future. It would also be interesting to examine whether or not the orthologous aminoaclyases in other lepidopteran species, which have been shown to degrade FAC in vitro, are also important for their survival on different host plants. 

Studies of insect herbivore-induced plant volatiles have repeatedly found that FAW elicited lesser amount of plant volatiles compared to other 

chewing caterpillars, and this phenomenon has been postulated to reflect the specialization of FAW towards maize17,40. In the last decade, various components of the OS and feces of FAW larvae have been reported to contribute to the suppression of maize defense response s16,32,41–43. In addition to plant defense suppressors, depletion of elicitor molecules, specifically FACs, has also been suggested to be an effective mean for insect herbivores to adapt towards their preferred host plants17,30,34,44. In support of this hypothesis, infestation by the $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutant larvae or exogenous treatment with their OS elicited more pronounced defense-related gene expression in maize leaves compared to wildtype (Fig. 4). Interestingly, the transcriptional expression levels of SfruACY in salivary glands were stronger in larvae fed with maize leaves, the preferred host plant species of FAW, than those fed with cotton leaves, although the OS of wildtype FAW larvae fed with maize contained comparable if not higher levels of FACs than their siblings fed with cotton leaves (Fig. 1d). By contrast, this diet-specific induction of ACY was not observed in Helicoverpa armigera (Fig. S1). This disparity between a specialist (S. frugiperda) and a generalist (H. armigera) implied that dietspecific induction of ACY expression may be one of the means by which FAW adapts towards maize. 

Last but not least, the ACY protein has been hypothesized to help insect herbivores to cope with variation in the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio of their diets38. Though our investigations focused primarily on the plant inducible defenses, it should be recognized that AD and maize leaves were almost certain to have different nutrient compositions. Hence, the difference in larvae performance between the $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutant and wildtype may also reflect the importance of this gene when feeding on the specific nutrient profile of maize leaves. It would be interesting to further test this hypothesis by comparing $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutant and wildtype larvae growth on different AD recipes in the future. Moreover, our experimental colony of FAW has been maintained on an AD with $1 \mathrm { m g / g }$ of ampicillin Na and streptomycin sulfate (Data S1). This is likely to have a profound impact on the insect gut microbiota, which is crucial for insect growth and host adaptation45. While this factor would not affect the validity of the present laboratory study given that both wildtype and $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ larvae were kept on the same AD, we acknowledge that the function of SfruACY may be unpredictably affected in natural FAW populations with intact gut microbiota, which require further investigations under more natural environmental conditions. 

From a practical perspective, the functional importance of SfruACY in host adaptation of FAW suggests that this gene could be an ideal target for sustainable management of this infamous agricultural pest. Unlike traditional chemical pesticides that targets essential insect physiological and developmental processes, precise pest management by targeting a nonessential, host-adaptive gene, such as SfruACY, is likely to pose a significantly lower selective pressure on the pest population, thereby reducing the risk of pesticide resistance evolution46,47. This approach could provide a valuable supplement to the current pest management scheme for FAW, in complement to the adoption of transgenic crops and/or chemical pesticides. 

In conclusion, we demonstrate that SfruACY can deplete FACs in the oral secretions of FAW larvae, thereby suppressing the inducible defense responses of its preferred host plant. This finding reveals the control of secreted plant defense elicitor molecules as a potential host adaptation mechanism of this important specialist insect herbivore, and indicates SfruACY as a promising target for RNA interference-based pest management strategies. 

# Methods

# Plant and insect materials

The reference temperate maize inbred line B73 was used for all experiments involving plant tissue. The fall armyworm colony was kindly provided by Dr. Yutao Xiao’s group at the AGIS. The S. frugiperda adults, eggs, and larvae were originally collected from more than 20 different non-Bt corn fields from RuiLi (Yunnan, China) in January 2019, and this experimental colony has been since kept under laboratory conditions. Specifically, FAW larvae were reared on artificial diet at $2 6 \pm 2 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $5 5 \pm 1 0 \%$ relative humidity with $1 4 \mathrm { h }$ light:10 h dark. Adults were held under similar 

conditions and fed with $1 0 0 { \mathrm { g / L } }$ sugar solution. The artificial diet for larvae was prepared with ingredients listed in Data S1. Maize seedlings were grown in 1 L pots (5 seedlings per pot) filled with commercial potting soil (Pindstrup) mixed with $2 0 \%$ (v/v) vermiculite, and kept in growth chambers with long-day light conditions (16 h) and temperature variation of $2 4 \pm 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ day, $2 0 \pm 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ night. Two-week-old maize seedlings were used for FAW bioassay and transcriptomic analyses when the second leaf was fully expanded. 

# Sample preparation, data collection, and data analyses for RNA-seq

For the insect salivary gland RNA-seq, FAW larvae were reared until early fourth instars on artificial diet to ensure consistent growth stages. After $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ of starvation, the fourth instar larvae were transferred to artificial diet or B73 maize leaves to allow feeding for $^ \mathrm { 6 h }$ . Salivary glands were dissected from actively feeding caterpillars. Cold anaesthetized insects were placed ventrally, and the salivary glands were removed, rinsed in DEPC water, and snap frozen in liquid nitrogen. For each biological replicate of the RNA-seq experiment, dissected salivary gland tissues from fifteen insects were pooled, and either diet group included four biological replicates. For the maize leaf RNA-seq, 1 s instar FAW larvae were caged on the second leaf of each seedling with a perforated $( 2 \times 2 \times 8 \mathrm { c m } )$ transparent PVC box for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ alongside with empty cage control samples. All larvae were starved for 6 h prior to the start of the experiments to ensure a prompt feeding start. Caged leaf areas from three maize seedlings of each group were collected into separate $1 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tubes and snap frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent RNA-seq analyses. 

Total RNA of FAW larvae salivary glands and maize leaves was extracted with the TRIzol protocol. The integrity of the RNA was verified using $1 \%$ agarose gels and an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer and RNA 6000 Nano Kit (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA). For each sample, 1 µg RNA was used to generate a paired-end sequencing library using NEBNext® UltraTM RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina® (NEB, USA) following manufacturer’s recommendations, and index codes were added to track sequences to each sample, and library quality was re-assessed on the Bioanalyzer 2100 system. For each sample, at least 6 Gb of raw data was obtained. We entrusted Tianjin Novogene Co. to complete this study’s RNA extraction and sequencing work. The authors designed the experimental process and approved and reviewed the data produced by the company. 

After removing adaptor and low-quality reads, the remaining clean paired-end reads were mapped onto either the FAW reference genome (Accession no. WUTJ00000000) or maize B73 reference genome v4 with the STAR aligner48–50. Raw read counts were calculated with HTseq-count, and differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were calculated with the DESeq2 package with a cutoff of FDR $< 0 . 0 5$ and $\log 2 \mathrm { F C } > 1 ^ { 5 1 }$ . Raw read counts were converted to fragments per kilobase per million mapped (FPKM) values for principal component analyses. Gene ontology term enrichment analysis of the maize DEGs was performed with the agriGO web tool with a cutoff of $\mathrm { F D R } < 0 . 0 5 ^ { 5 2 }$ . The script used for this RNA-seq data processing procedure is summarized in the Supplementary Methods. 

# Quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNA of FAW tissues was reverse transcribed using the HiScript® III 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (+gDNA wiper) (Vazyme). The qRT-PCR was performed on a CFX-96™ PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad) with the reagent of Taq Pro Universal SYBR qRT-PCR Master Mix (Vazyme). The PCR conditions were as follows: 1 cycle at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 s , 4 0$ cycles at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s, and 1 cycle at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s. A two-fold dilution series of cDNA was used to construct a relative standard curve. Melting curve analysis and standard curves were performed from 55 to $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to assess primer specificity and PCR efficiencies, respectively. The relative expression and each target gene were assessed according to the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method, incorporating PCR efficiency after normalization with the housekeeping genes Rpl13 in FAW and Tub-5 in maize B73. All qRT-PCR primers were listed in Data S9. 

# ACY gene phylogeny construction

The homologous sequences of ACY in different insects were extracted from GenBank (the GenBank accession numbers are listed in Data S10). The phylogenetic tree of ACYs was constructed using MEGA 5.0 with the neighbor-joining method. Node support was calculated using a bootstrap procedure based on 1000 replicates with pair gap deletions. 

# In vitro expression, western blot analysis, and enzymatic activity assay of SfruACY

To obtain the full-length coding sequence of SfruACY, a pair of genespecific primers was designed and used in the PCR amplification of pools of total cDNA from salivary glands (Data S9). The PCR reaction was performed under the following conditions: $9 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles of $9 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 s, $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 s .$ , and $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $9 0 s$ , and a final extension at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min. The PCR products were ligated into the vector pEASY-Blunt (TransGen Biotech, China), and the sequence was verified by Sanger sequencing53. The correct CDS sequence was then sub-cloned into the expression vector pGEX4T-1 with a glutathione S-transferase tag by using ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme Biotech Co., Ltd, Nanjing, China). The specific primers are listed in Data S9. The positive plasmids were checked by sequencing and transformed into the E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells. 

A single positive colony was picked from LB agar plates containing $5 0 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ of Ampicillin, and incubated overnight in $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of Ampicillindosed LB broth. The seed culture was then diluted to 1:100 with 5 L of fresh LB broth and shake-incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 200 rpm. After 4 h of incubation, $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to reach a final concentration of 1 mM, and the culture was moved to $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 200 rpm. After $1 5 \mathrm { h }$ of incubation, the bacterial cells were harvested by centrifugation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , 4000 rpm for 10 min, resuspended in a lysis buffer, sonicated in icecold water for 30 min. Glutathione Sepharose 4B agarose (GE Healthcare) was added to the supernatant containing the target proteins. After incubation for 1 h, the mixture was transferred into a disposable column and washed extensively with lysis buffer. 

Eight microliters of either GST or SfruACY-GST recombinant protein extracts were combined with $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 { \times } \mathrm { S D S }$ loading buffer, heated at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and subjected to electrophoresis on a $1 0 \%$ SDS-PAGE gel. Proteins were then transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes using a semi-dry electroblotter (FTB 95, China). Membranes were blocked with milk $( 5 \% , \mathrm { w } / \mathrm { v } )$ diluted in Tris-buffered saline containing Tween20 (TBS-T, $0 . 0 5 \%$ ). Blots were then incubated overnight a $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with Anti-GST mouse monoclonal antibodies (1:5000; #30901ES10; Zuoke). Following primary antibody incubation, blots were washed and incubated with Anti-Mouse secondary antibody (#CW0102S; Cwbio) diluted at 1:5000 at room temperature for 1 h in $5 \%$ milk in TBST-T. The proteins were finally detected with the ECL kit (#ZE171-101L, GenStar) on the iBrightTM FL1500 Imaging System (Thermo, USA). 

Ten microliters purified recombinant enzyme (SfruACY-GST; $0 . 4 \mathrm { m g / m l } )$ ) and GST protein $( 0 . 4 \mathrm { m g / m l } )$ were added to $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of freshly collected OS, respectively. These mixtures were incubated at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. The reaction was terminated by boiling for 5 min, and ready for the sample preparation procedure for FAC quantification as delineated below. This enzymatic activity assay was repeated twice, with three replicated reactions per experiment. 

# Oral secretion collection and FAC quantification

Fourth to fifth instar FAW larvae were gently held and stimulated by a $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ pipette tip at the mouth cavity to provoke oral secretion. The collected OS samples were centrifuged at $1 2 { , } 0 0 0 \times$ rpm for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to remove food debris, and the supernatants were stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for later use. 

For the quantification of FACs, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of OS was diluted with $9 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ methanol, filtered through $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ syringe filters, and used for LC-MSbased targeted quantitative metabolomics analysis on the Agilent 1290II-6460 platform29. Briefly, $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of each sample was injected onto an RRHD SB-C18 column (Agilent Technology) maintained at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and the liquid 

phase was run on a 15-min linear gradient, ramping from $4 0 \%$ to $1 0 0 \%$ acetonitrile, flowing at $0 . 2 \mathrm { m L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ . The mass spectrometry was set at negative mode of electron spray ionization, and source temperature was set at $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The capillary voltage and collision energy were set at $3 5 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ and $8 0 \mathrm { V }$ , respectively. Sheath gas was set to flow $1 0 \mathrm { L / m i n }$ and 45 psi. The results analyzed with a Shimadzu LCMS-8040 system. Based on previously reported [M-1]- ions of known FACs, three monitoring $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ channels were set at 421, 423, and 407, which corresponded to volicitin, N-hydroxylinoleoyl-L-glutamine, and $N .$ -linoleoyl-L-glutamine, respectively29. Each sample was infused with [D6] JA-Ile pre-injection as an internal standard. Areas of peaks from each of the extracted ion chromatograms were integrated and then normalized by the internal standard peak area, and the normalized peak areas were compared between the two types of OS by Student’s t-tests. This experiment was repeated twice with four biological replicates per experiment. 

# Homozygote mutant construction by CRISPR-Cas9

The construction of SfruACY mutants with the micro-injection of CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleo-protein (RNP) protocol was performed according to a previous report54. To obtain an accurate target for gene knockout, 20 adult moths were used to extract genomic DNA individually. The genomic DNA was then used as templates to amplify the coding sequence of SfruACY fragment with a pair of gene-specific primers. The PCR products were sent to sequence the genotypes. Two single guide RNAs were designed to target the first two exons of SfruACY. For either sgRNAs, the 12 bp seed region immediately approximate to the PAMs could not be fully matched to any other parts of the FAW genome according to BLAST searches, suggesting low risks of off-target editing. The two sgRNAs were synthesized according to the manufacturer’s instructions (GeneArt Precision gRNA Synthesis Kit, ThermoFisher Scientific, Pittsburgh). The DNA template of each sgRNA was generated by using synthetic forward and reverse oligonucleotides with the Tracr Fragment þ T7 Primer Mix. The PCR was performed with a pair of designed primers (the forward primer: GAAATTAATACGACTCACTA-TAG + target sequence, the reverse primer: TTCTAGCTCTAAAAC $^ +$ target sequence reverse complement) under the conditions: $9 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 s ,$ 32 cycles of $9 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 s and $5 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for ${ 1 5 s , }$ followed by a final extension of $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min. PCR assembly and in vitro transcription (IVT) were conducted according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The synthesized sgRNA was purified with a gRNA Clean Up Kit (ThermoFisher Scientific, Pittsburgh, PA) and diluted into working concentration with nucleasefree water. 

For embryo delivery of the RNPs, freshly collected FAW eggs (within 30 min of oviposition) were used for micro-injection. For each egg, the mixture of ${ 3 0 0 } \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ of either sgRNA and $1 5 0 \mathrm { n g / \mu L }$ of Cas9 protein (GeneArt Platinum Cas9 Nuclease ThermoFisher Scientific, Pittsburgh, PA) was injected using a FemtoJet and injectMan NI 2 microinjection system (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany). The injected eggs were incubated in a sterile Petri dish at 26 ± 1 °C, $6 0 \pm 1 0 \%$ RH for 3–4 days until hatching. 

Larvae hatched from the micro-injected eggs were reared to adults (G0 generation) and hybridized with wildtype adults to generate F1 individuals. When the F1 grew into adults, a middle leg of each individual was removed for genomic DNA, using a TIANamp Genomic DNA Kit (TIANGEN, Beijing, China). The genomic DNA was used as templates to amplify a 682 bps-long fragment including the target site. Heterozygous F1 mutants were first screened by agarose gel electrophoresis and further confirmed by Sanger sequencing. Heterozygous F1 individuals with the same edited alleles were crossed to generate F2. The resulting F2 individuals were further screened for homozygous edited mutants following the same process. 

# Insect performance bioassay

For insect bioassays, two maize seedlings are placed in a transparent PVC box $5 \mathrm { c m } \times 5 \mathrm { c m } \times 3 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ ), with ten FAW neonates. Eight boxes were set up for either wildtype or $S f r u A C Y ^ { - / - }$ mutants. For each round of experiment, equal number of wildtype and mutant neonates were put on artificial diet as controls. After seven days of feeding, larvae were recovered and weighed, 

and the fresh weights of larvae feeding on the same diet were compared between insect genotypes with Student’s t-tests. This experiment was repeated twice. 

# Statistics and reproducibility

All statistical analyses and graphical representations were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.0 software (San Diego, CA, USA). Experimental data are presented as mean ± SEM. The tissue expression profile of SfruACY was evaluated by one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s HSD, while qPCR quantification, FACs measurements, and insect bioassay data were analyzed using Student’s t-tests. In vitro enzymatic assay, insect performance bioassay, and qPCR measurements have been repeated at least twice to obtain reproducible results. Comprehensive descriptions of experimental design and statistical methodologies are provided in the respective figure legends.
