# ABSTRACT

Engineering plants with reduced lignin content can result in pleiotropic growth defects. In stems of Arabidopsis plants with reduced expression of hydroxycinnamoyl CoA: shikimate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HCT), the plastidial glucose 6‐phosphate: phosphate co‐transporter GPT2 is highly overexpressed, and this coincides with reduced lignin levels and extensive transcriptional and metabolic reprogramming. To explore the potential relationship between GPT2 expression and lignin accumulation, GPT2 transcript levels were evaluated in a suite of Arabidopsis thaliana and Medicago truncatula lignin‐ defective lines. We also examined lignin levels and composition, and transcriptomic and metabolic profiles in GPT2 loss‐of‐ function, GPT2 overexpression, and wild‐type Arabidopsis plants. Loss of GPT2 had no effect on lignin, but its overexpression caused a decrease in stem lignin levels due to reduced accumulation of both guaiacyl and syringyl lignins and their associated monolignol pools. HCT transcript levels were diminished in 35S‐GPT2 lines, indicating a potential transcriptional regulatory connection between lignin biosynthesis and GPT2. Based on our transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses, we suggest that GPT2 operates to balance the flux between the biosynthesis of lignin and light‐protective phenylpropanoid derivatives. 

# 1 | Introduction

Many studies have been undertaken to modify the accumulation of lignin in plant cell walls to improve biomass processing for paper, pulp and forage (Chen and Dixon 2007; Dixon and Barros 2019; Ralph et al. 2019; Shi et al. 2016). It is commonly observed that constitutive perturbations in the monolignol pathway, a branch of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis that supplies the monomeric building blocks of lignin, lead to the ectopic expression of defence response genes, often accompanied by severely reduced plant growth (Ha et al. 2021). The mechanisms behind these effects are poorly understood (Bonawitz and Chapple 2013; Ha et al. 2021; Muro‐Villanueva et al. 2019), but in some cases involve release of defence‐ inducing elicitors from incorrectly assembled secondary cell 

walls (Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2020; Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2011; Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2018; Liu et al. 2023). 

Although downregulation of specific monolignol pathway genes may result in selective activation of defence responses, the overall extent of genetic reprogramming in these plants is often much more extensive, with a significant proportion of the changes not directly associated with defence (Ha et al. 2021). In a study on inflorescence stems of Arabidopsis HCT‐RNAi lines, the most highly differentially expressed gene compared to wild‐type plants was GPT2 (AT1G61800) (Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2020), which encodes a glucose 6‐phosphate/inorganic phosphate translocator that localises to the plastid inner envelope membrane (Dyson et al. 2015; Kammerer et al. 1998). This gene is also strongly upregulated in the cinnamoyl CoA 

reductase 1 (ccr1) mutant but is not induced in wild‐type plants by elicitors derived from the cell walls of either modified plant (Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2020). 

GPT2 is usually expressed at low levels in plant tissues, and its loss of function is not lethal, unlike that of GPT1, which encodes a ubiquitously expressed G6P/Pi translocator that localises to both plastids and peroxisomes (Baune et al. 2020; Niewiadomski et al. 2005). Although GPT2 is not essential for plant viability (Knappe et al. 2003; Niewiadomski et al. 2005), it is required for correct partitioning of carbohydrate during acclimation to photosynthetic light (Athanasiou et al. 2010) and is strongly overexpressed in a series of Arabidopsis mutants with defects in starch biosynthesis (Kunz et al. 2010). The major function of GPT2 appears to be in adaptation to high light levels through sugar sensing, resulting in import of G6P into plastids for biosynthesis of starch or other molecules derived from glycolysis or the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway (Dyson et al. 2014; Weise et al. 2019), although it has been reported that the gene is also induced during bacterial infection (Niewiadomski et al. 2005). GPT2 expression can change dramatically in response to light or sugar supply (Athanasiou et al. 2010; Jeong et al. 2018; Weise et al. 2019). GPT2 induction in response to light requires expression of the redox‐responsive transcription factor 1 (RRTF1, a member of the ethylene response factor subfamily B‐3 of the ERF/AP2 transcription factor family) and high amounts of cytosolic triose phosphate (Weise et al. 2019). GPT2 is also highly expressed in mutants of MYB56, a transcription factor that regulates anthocyanin biosynthesis, seed size and flowering in Arabidopsis (Jeong et al. 2018). 

Theoretically, the metabolic link between monolignol biosynthesis and GPT2 might involve the partitioning of glucose‐ 6‐phosphate between the pentose phosphate pathway (Kruger and Von Schaewen 2003) and starch biosynthesis. Glucose 6‐phosphate is the substrate for the first enzyme in the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway that provides both the NADPH utilised in the side‐chain reduction reactions of monolignol biosynthesis (Pryke and ap Rees 1977) and the erythrose 4‐phosphate that enters the shikimate pathway for synthesis of the aromatic amino acid L‐phenylalanine as the initial precursor of phenylpropanoid compounds (Figure 1). The oxidative pentose phosphate pathway enzyme 6‐phosphgluconate dehydrogenase 2 appears to control flux into aromatic amino acid biosynthesis (Tang et al. 2024). Shikimate itself is also directly involved in monolignol biosynthesis via the HCT reaction, in which coumaroyl CoA is trans‐esterified to form coumaroyl shikimate, the substrate for introduction of the second hydroxyl group into the aromatic nucleus (Hoffmann et al. 2004; Schoch et al. 2001). The shikimate moiety is then removed (Vanholme et al. 2013) (Figure 1). The exact reason for the operation of this apparently energetically unfavourable cycle remains unclear (Dixon and Barros 2019). It is possible that reductions in monolignol biosynthesis could result in re‐partitioning of glucose 6‐phosphate from provision of monolignol pathway precursors to starch (Dauwe et al. 2007). A second possible link between lignin biosynthesis and a G6P/Pi co‐translocator might be inorganic phosphate itself, as transport of Pi and other ions, or signals for Pi sensing, might be disrupted in 

plants with incorrectly thickened cell walls in vascular conducting tissue (Bonawitz and Chapple 2013; Ha et al. 2021; Lin et al. 2014). 

To further explore the basis for the overexpression of GPT2 in some lignin modified plants, and to determine whether it links to systemic changes in primary metabolism, we examined transcriptomic and metabolomic changes in HCT‐RNAi, gpt2‐1, and constitutively overexpressing GPT2 Arabidopsis lines. Our results confirmed that overexpression, but not loss of GPT2, leads to reduced lignin levels. Moreover, HCT expression is reduced when GPT2 is overexpressed. These results, coupled with in‐depth metabolomic analyses, suggest involvement of GPT2 in a regulatory mechanism to balance lignin biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid‐based photoprotection. 

# 2 | Materials and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Material and Growth Conditions

Arabidopsis hydroxycinnamoyl CoA: shikimate hydroxycinnamoyl transferase (HCT)‐RNAi (AT5G48930) lines were as previously described (Li et al. 2010). gpt2‐1 (GK‐454A06) and gpt2‐3 (GK‐780F12) mutants (Dyson et al. 2014) were obtained from TAIR (AGI for GPT2, At1g61800). Overexpression lines Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2] and gpt2‐1 [35S‐GPT2] were generated by transforming wild‐type Col‐0 and gpt2‐1 plants with the 35S‐ GPT2 vector (Niewiadomski et al. 2005). 

Seeds were sterilised then stratified for 2 days at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before growing in hydroponic media at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16‐h light/8‐h dark photoperiod (light intensity ${ \sim } 1 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Plant lines used for lignin thioacidolysis, transcriptomic and metabolite analysis were grown in $0 . 5 \mathrm { x }$ Noren medium (Norén et al. 2004) for 9–10 weeks before harvesting stem tissues for lignin extraction. 

# 2.2 | Determination of Lignin Levels, Non‐Targeted Metabolome Extraction and Analysis

For lignin compositional analysis, stem tissues were frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground, sequentially extracted with methanol, chloroform/methanol (2:1, v/v), methanol, and water, and then lyophilised. Lignin composition was determined using a modified thioacidolysis method (Chen et al. 2021; Zhao et al. 2010) with $^ { 4 , 4 ^ { \prime } }$ ‐ethylidene bisphenol as internal standard. Three biological replicates were measured for each genotype. 

For metabolome analysis, polyphenols and polar compounds were extracted from frozen stem tissues and compound identification was performed by LC‐MS/MS at the Bioanalytical facility at the University of North Texas using ultrahigh performance liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry as described previously (Cocuron et al. 2019). Metabolites were identified and quantified using authentic standards. Three biological replicates were measured. Metabolite data were processed and visualised using MetaboAnalyst (Pang et al. 2024).

# 2.3 | Histochemical Analysis

Plants were grown for 5–6 weeks until the primary stem had ceased elongation. Plant cross sections were made 2 cm from the base of the stem. Qualitative phloroglucinol staining for lignin was carried out as described (Liu et al. 2021). 

# 2.4 | RNA Preparation, Sequencing and Data Analysis

Three replicates of stems from each genotype were harvested individually, frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground to a fine powder. Total RNA was isolated using the QIAGEN RNeasy plant mini kit (QIAGEN Inc., Valencia, CA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. RNA concentration and purity were determined both spectrophotometrically by measuring absorbance at 230, 260, 280, and $3 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ and by fluorometry using a Qubit. The mRNA sequencing library was prepared using the TruSeq stranded mRNA library method. The sequencing platform was NextSeq. 500/550 High Output Kit v2.5 from Illumina. Reads were mapped to the Arabidopsis genome (TAIR10.1; using RNA STAR). After mapping, 

differential expression analysis was conducted using DESeq. 2 (Love et al. 2014) and GO‐term enrichment was performed along with KEGG pathway analysis using Galaxy resources provided by the HPRC TAMU facility (GRACE supercomputer). 

# 2.5 | Real‐Time qPCR

To evaluate GPT2 transcripts in each genotype, total RNA was isolated using the QIAGEN RNeasy plant mini kit (QIAGEN Inc., Valencia, CA), then cDNA was generated via reverse transcription with a SuperScript III First‐Strand Synthesis System (Invitrogen) and oligo(dT) primers according to the manufacturers' instructions. Quantifications of gene expression were performed on a QuantStudio 6 Flex RealTime PCR System (Thermo Scientific) using PowerUp SYBR Green Master Mix (ThermoFisher Scientific). qPCR reactions were performed with the following primer combinations: 1414, $5 ^ { \prime }$ ‐GTTCTTTGATG ATGCTTGTCTCTTG $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ and 1415, ${ 5 ^ { \prime } }$ ‐CTACAGCGACCGGGA ACAGA ${ \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime }$ to flank exons 1‐2; 1404, $5 ^ { \prime }$ ‐GGTTGGCAAAATGCG GTTT ${ 3 ^ { \prime } }$ and 1405, $5 ^ { \prime }$ ‐GTGCCACTACCCACCAGACA $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ to flank exons 3–4; ElF4A_FP, ${ 5 ^ { \prime } }$ ‐GGGTATCTATGCTTACGGTT T‐3′ and EIF4A_RP, $5 ^ { \prime }$ ‐CTGCTGGATCACATCAAGAC ${ \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime }$ as a 

control. The efficiency of each primer set was validated by determining the slope of $\mathrm { C _ { t } }$ versus dilution plot on a dilution series. Individual reactions were used to quantify each transcript level in a given cDNA sample, and the average $\mathrm { C _ { t } }$ from duplicated reactions within the same run was used for quantification. Data were normalised to EIF4A as an internal control. 

# 2.6 | Statistical Analysis

Statistical treatment of data was performed by ANOVA using Fisher's least significant difference procedure for multiple‐ comparison test. Duncan's Multiple Range test (DMRT) was used as a post hoc test to measure specific differences between data samples.

# 3.1 | Overexpression of  Is Observed in GPT2Response to Independently Downregulating Multiple Steps in Monolignol Biosynthesis

To determine whether overexpression of GPT2 is commonly seen in plants with disrupted monolignol biosynthesis, we interrogated transcriptomics datasets for stem tissue of multiple lignin pathway mutants or knock‐down lines in both Arabidopsis thaliana and the 

model legume Medicago truncatula (Table 1). GPT2 was overexpressed in both species following disruption of expression of the monolignol pathway genes HCT, CCR, caffeoyl shikimate esterase (CSE), caffeic acid/5‐hydroxyferulic acid 3‐O‐methyltransferase (COMT) and caffeoyl CoA 3‐O‐methyltransferase (CCoAOMT). It was also overexpressed in the dwarf Arabidopsis ref‐8 mutant with loss of function of coumaroyl shikimate $3 ^ { \prime }$ ‐hydroxylase, the enzyme catalysing the reaction subsequent to that of HCT (Table 1). The growth defect in this mutant is rescued by loss of function of two subunits of the Mediator complex (Dolan et al. 2017), but this did not suppress the upregulation of GPT2 (Table 1). However, GPT2 was not overexpressed as a consequence of gain or loss of function of F5H in Arabidopsis, which alters lignin composition rather than quantity by affecting a much later step in the monolignol pathway (Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2018; Marita et al. 1999; Meyer et al. 1998), or by loss of function of cinnamate 4‐hydroxylase (C4H), 4‐coumarate CoA ligase 1 (4CL1), CSE or cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) in Arabidopsis (Table 1). Table 1 also records the large (78.5‐fold) increase in GPT2 expression in the Atmyb56 mutant affecting anthocyanin biosynthesis (Jeong et al. 2018).

# 3.3 | Lignin Content and Composition Following Altered Expression of HCT or GPT2

To better understand the reasons for induction of GPT2 following downregulation of the lignin pathway, we examined a series of Arabidopsis lines with gain or loss of function of GPT2. Two independent mutant alleles, gpt2‐1 and gpt2‐3, were analyzed, and we also generated 35S‐GPT2 overexpressing lines in both the gpt2‐1 and wildtype Col‐0 backgrounds. 

Analysis of total lignin as determined by thioacidolyisis revealed that, as previously reported, HCT‐RNAi lines exhibited strongly reduced lignin levels (Figure 3a). A significant but smaller reduction in lignin level was observed in plants overexpressing GPT2 in either the gpt2‐1 mutant or Col‐0 backgrounds (Figure 3a). However, loss of function of GPT2 in independent gpt2‐1 and gpt2‐3 allelic insertion mutations did not result in a significant change in total lignin level (Figure 3a). Consistent with the total lignin content, stem height was significantly reduced in both HCT‐RNAi and Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2] lines but remained unaffected in the mutant lines (Supporting Information S1: Figure S1). Rosette areas also showed no significant differences between lines (Supporting Information S1: Figure S2). 

Lignin from HCT‐RNAi lines showed the characteristic increase in H‐units at the expense of a reduction in content of both G and S lignin units (Figure 3b–d, Supporting Information S1: Figure S3). Neither GPT2 loss nor overexpression resulted in altered content of H‐units, but the reduction in lignin content in 35S‐GPT2 overexpression lines was associated with reductions in both G and S units (Figure 3c,d). Phloroglucinol staining indicated that spatial deposition patterns of lignin were unchanged in stems of HCT‐ RNAi, gpt2 mutant, and Col‐0[35S‐GPT2] lines (Figure 4). 

The absence of a lignin phenotype in gpt2‐1 mutants suggests potential redundancy. Loss of GPT1 is lethal, so testing redundancy by this translocator through a mutational approach is not possible. We therefore examined expression of GPT1 and other plastidial transporters, but all were slightly downregulated or not significantly altered in the gpt2‐1 background (Supporting Information S1: Figure S4) so it is unclear if any of these genes contribute to redundant or compensatory activities unless these occur at the protein or catalytic levels.

Metabolomic analysis of stem tissue of Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2] plants showed similar reductions in the levels of sinapyl alcohol, sinapaldehyde, 5‐hydroxyconiferaldehyde and coniferyl alcohol (Figure 5b, Supporting Information S3: Table S2), consistent with the reductions in both G and S lignin units in these plants. Notably, sinapyl alcohol was the metabolite with the largest reduction in levels in both HCT‐RNAi and 35S‐GPT2. However, the pattern of upregulated metabolites was very different from that of the HCT‐ RNAi plants. Firstly, apart from phloretin (low significance), no 4‐hydroxy‐substituted aromatic compounds accumulated, consistent with the retention of HCT function. The parallel analysis of gpt2‐1 mutant stems showed, consistent with the lignin analysis, no effects on monolignol pathway intermediates nor any effect to the directly opposite to consequences of GPT2 overexpression (Figure 5c, Supporting Information S3: Table S2). Loss of function of GPT2 resulted in a reduction in the levels of several amino acids and increases in the levels of some TCA cycle intermediates (Figure 5c). Levels of tyrosine and tyramine, a trace amine derived from tyrosine, were both increased in 35S‐GPT2 and decreased in the gpt2‐1 mutant. Trehalose was significantly reduced in gpt2‐1.

# 4 | Discussion

The consistently observed induction of GPT2 in plant stems with impaired lignin biosynthesis suggests a previously unexplored link between primary and secondary metabolism. Arabidopsis possesses two plastidial glucose 6‐phosphate:Pi co‐ transporters, of which GPT1 is essential for plant survival whereas GPT2 appears to function primarily in response to acclimation to high light (Knappe et al. 2003; Niewiadomski et al. 2005). Most studies have addressed GPT2 function in leaves, the major site of carbon assimilation (Dyson et al. 2015; Dyson et al. 2014; Jeong et al. 2018). Our studies address GPT2 function in stems, the major site of lignification, which nevertheless possess functional chloroplasts in the epidermal region. Although we measure metabolic changes in stem tissues, we are not able to rule out that some metabolites could be translocated from leaves. 

Our first observation of a massive induction of GPT2 following impairment of lignin biosynthesis was from RNAseq analysis of 

T2 lines of Arabidopsis expressing an HCT‐RNAi construct (Gallego‐Giraldo et al. 2020). These plants were semi‐dwarf and were characterised by extensive genetic reprogramming leading to activation of a suite of defence response genes (Gallego‐ Giraldo et al. 2020). Through subsequent generations, the growth phenotype became attenuated, although the plants retained the reduced levels of overall lignin, and increased proportion of H‐lignin, characteristic of the original material. Importantly, they retained elevated expression of GPT2 in the absence of extensive defence/stress responses, suggesting that GPT2 induction is a result of altered lignin level rather than stress reprogramming. Furthermore, the ectopic expression of GPT2 resulted in downregulation of HCT accompanied by reduced levels of G and S lignin, further highlighting the importance of GPT2 in the regulation of lignin biosynthesis. 

It was important to study the metabolic consequences of both gain and loss of function of GPT2. Previous studies have primarily looked at effects of loss of function on leaf metabolism, particularly that of photosynthetic carbon fixation metabolites (Dyson et al. 2014; Kunz et al. 2010; Niewiadomski et al. 2005; Weise et al. 2019). Paradoxically, our initial RNAseq analysis indicated elevated levels of GPT2 transcripts in both the gpt‐2 and gpt‐3 mutants. Subsequent analysis using PCR‐primers spanning the T‐DNA insertion sites in the third intron in both mutant alleles revealed that the mutants generated truncated transcripts consistent with being true loss of function alleles. 

Both metabolomic and transcriptomic analyses indicated broad metabolic effects of alteration of GPT2 expression in stem tissue. Significantly altered metabolites in HCT‐RNAi stems included coniferyl alcohol, 5‐hydroxyconiferaldehyde, sinapaldehyde and sinapyl alcohol, the reduced levels of which are consistent with the downregulation of the monolignol pathway before the introduction of the 3‐hydroxyl group by the combined activities

Similar changes in all these metabolites were observed in the GPT2 over‐expressor lines but were unaffected in the gpt2‐1 mutant. Interestingly, coumaric acid accumulated in both HCT‐RNAi and 35S‐GPT2 lines, but coumaraldehyde and coumaryl alcohol, specific precursors of H‐lignin, were only accumulated in the former. This is consistent with the increase in H units in the lignin of HCT‐RNAi, but not 35S‐GPT2 plants. The increase in the percentage of H‐units in lignin from 35S‐GPT2 plants was solely due to the decrease in G and S units. Together, these data suggest that the overexpression of GPT2 (which is far greater in the 35S‐GPT plants than in the progeny HCT‐RNAi lines) directs H‐unit precursors away from the monolignol pathway. 

HCT downregulation was associated with downregulation of many of the genes of the monolignol pathway whereas this was not the case with Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2]. Instead, overexpression of GPT2 resulted in downregulation of only the three genes associated with the non‐oxidative reactions in the conversion of coumaryl moieties to caffeyl moieties (4CL2, HCT, CSE). Arabidopsis possesses another route for the direct conversion of coumarate to caffeate, via ascorbate peroxidase 1 (Barros et al. 2019). The observation that no specific H monolignol precursors accumulated in the Col‐0 [35S GPT2] lines, unlike in HCT‐RNAi lines, suggests some preservation of HCT function or operation of the alternative pathway in Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2], in addition to the re‐direction of H‐unit precursors. 

Uniquely, overexpression of GPT2 resulted in the accumulation of a range of amino acids, intermediates of central carbon metabolism, nucleotides and nucleotide sugars. Consistent with the function of GPT2 in the transport of G6P to chloroplasts for conversion via the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway, levels of sedoheptulose 7‐phosphate and xylulose 5‐phosphate, interconverted via transketolase in the pentose phosphate pathway, were increased. Among the amino acids that increased were the three aromatic amino acids phenylalanine, tyrosine (and its derivative tyramine) and tryptophan, the products of the shikimate pathway. Phenylalanine is the initial precursor of all phenylpropanoid compounds. In monocots, tyrosine is involved in the lignin biosynthesis pathway. Specifically, PTAL, an ammonia‐lyase enzyme, converts L‐tyrosine to p‐coumaric acid (an intermediate in the phenylpropanoid pathway) and is hypothesised to mitigate the effect of loss of function of HCT in Brachypodium distachyon (Barros et al. 2016). However, a PTAL homologue is yet to be identified in dicots like Arabidopsis. Tyramine is synthesised from tyrosine by the action of tyrosine decarboxylase (TyDC), a member of a family of aromatic L‐amino acid decarboxylases (Lehmann and Pollmann 2009). It is a precursor of many plant secondary metabolites, including alkaloids and hydroxycinnamic acid‐conjugated amides (Xu et al. 2020), the latter of which are deposited in the cell wall where they are proposed to contribute to plant defence by creating a physical barrier against pathogens (Trezzini et al. 1993). Based on these data, increased GPT2 expression may aid in eliciting defence and compensation responses via modifications in tyrosine and tyramine production. The observation that tyramine is the metabolite with the largest fold‐decrease in amount in gpt2‐1 plants supports the idea that GPT2 regulates flux into the chloroplastic shikimate pathway, presumably through the link to the pentose phosphate pathway (Tang et al. 2024). 

The most striking result of the metabolomic analysis was the large increase in multiple nucleoside derivatives, including a range of nucleoside triphosphates and nucleotide diphosphate sugars, in Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2] plants. The latter compounds represented a nearly full complement of precursors for the biosynthesis of pectins and hemicelluloses (Delmer et al. 2024). This response did not occur in the HCT‐RNAi lines, suggesting that it does not represent a reprogramming of cell wall biosynthesis to compensate for the change in lignin content/composition. UDP‐glucuronic acid 4‐epimerase and GATL5, both of which are associated with pectin biosynthesis, were upregulated in 35S‐GPT2 and downregulated in gpt2‐1. Further studies should address whether GPT2 plays a role in balancing the biosynthesis of pectin with that of lignin. 

Our transcriptomic data are consistent with many of the metabolic changes recorded above. Most interesting was the reciprocal relationship between GPT2 and HCT transcript levels. However, 4CL2, which is expressed in vascular tissue for lignin biosynthesis (although providing much less substrate for this pathway than 4CL1) (Li et al. 2015), was also strongly downregulated in Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2]. Downregulation of HCT and 4CL2 is consistent with the observed decrease in G‐ and S‐lignin and their immediate precursors in 35S‐GPT2. The lack of increase in H‐lignin and its specific precursors coumaraldehyde and coumaryl alcohol in 35S‐GPT2 suggests that the associated downregulation of 4CL2 results in a change in the pool of coumaroyl CoA so that it is targeted away from the lignin pathway. This targeting may be facilitated by the induction of 4CL3 (in both HCT‐RNAi and 35S‐GPT2). Interestingly, At4CL3 encodes the 4CL isoform with the least expression in belowground organs and the strongest response to UV irradiation (Ehlting et al. 1999) and has been associated with UV‐protective flavonoid biosynthesis (Li et al. 2015). 

Of the flavonoid‐related molecules identified as induced in both HCT‐RNAi and 35S‐GPT plants in the present study, phloretin is formed by CHS‐catalysed condensation of one molecule of 4‐hydroxy‐dihydrocinnamoyl‐CoA with 3 molecules of malonyl CoA (Gosch et al. 2009). It is unclear whether the CoA activation happens pre‐ or post‐reduction of the coumarate side chain. 

Trehalose‐6‐phosphate (T6P) was found to be elevated in both HCT‐RNAi and Col‐0 [35S‐GPT2]. Metabolite and transcript data also show trehalose levels were reduced significantly in gpt2‐1 and expression of TPPB was altered in all three lines. In response to change in sugar levels, T6P regulates starch synthesis in Arabidopsis via redox activation of ADP‐glucose pyrophosphorylase, which is dependent on the expression of SNF1‐ related protein kinase (SnRK1) (Kolbe et al. 2005). In vitro assays also showed that G6P, a substrate for T6P, inhibits SnRK1 activity (Toroser et al. 2000). Interestingly, related SnRK2 kinases, which are main drivers of ABA‐triggered plant stress responses, repress SnRK1 function by sequestration of their complexes (Belda‐Palazón et al. 2020). This activity is relevant to lignin biosynthesis because phosphorylation of the master lignin transcription factor, NAC SECONDARY WALL THICK-ENING PROMOTING FACTOR 1 (NST1) by a family of SnRK2s, transcriptionally activates a suite of downstream secondary cell‐wall biosynthesis genes, some of which are involved in the biosynthesis of cellulose and lignin (Liu et al. 2021). It is 

possible that ‘sugar sensing’ of G6P and T6P could alter lignin biosynthesis through transcriptional feedback regulation mediated by the SnRK proteins. 

Approximately $3 0 \%$ of fixed carbon is directed to the synthesis of lignin and related phenypropanoid natural products (Tohge et al. 2013), representing a major sink for fixed carbon. Furthermore, formation of one molecule of the S‐lignin precursor sinapyl alcohol requires the expenditure of 2 molecules of ATP, 2 molecules of NADPH, 2 molecules of S‐adenosyl methionine and the cycling of CoA and shikimate. It has been suggested that “GPT2 alters the distribution of metabolites between compartments and that this plays an essential role in allowing the cell to interpret environmental signals” (Dyson et al. 2015). We suggest, therefore, that GPT2 is involved in regulating the flux of carbon away from lignin and towards primary metabolism and possibly flavonoids and other UV protectants, which may include simple phenylpropanoids such as coumaric acid, which has recently been shown to possess unexpected photochemical properties that allow it to act as a UV shield (Gonzalez Moreno et al. 2022). An open question is the mechanism whereby increased flux through the pentose phosphate and aromatic amino acid pathways as a result of GPT2 overexpression downregulates HCT. An obvious metabolic connection is shikimate, the intermediate in plastidial aromatic amino acid biosynthesis that also exists in a cytosolic pool as (cycled) co‐substrate for the HCT‐reaction. Total shikimate levels were reduced in HCT‐RNAi plants, but unaffected in 35S‐GPT plants. Our results point to the importance of understanding the regulation of shikimate distribution between cytosol and chloroplast, and how this might directly or indirectly affect transcriptional control of lignin biosynthesis via HCT. 

GPT2 is induced by transfer of plants to high light conditions (Dyson et al. 2014). The accumulation of flavonoids when GPT2 is overexpressed may therefore also require high light as a signal. The GPT2‐mediated response could occur as a rapid means of protection against high light levels and involve downregulation of 4CL2 and HCT. These ideas provide further evidence for the intricate play between light, sugar metabolism and sensing, and lignin biosynthesis (Rogers et al. 2005). Developed here from the study of lignin modification, this model, which implicates GPT2 in both primary and secondary metabolism, will now require further testing in the context of transient changes in illumination.
