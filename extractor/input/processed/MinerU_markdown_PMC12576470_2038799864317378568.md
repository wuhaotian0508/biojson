# ABSTRACT

Nitrogen availability remains a principal constraint to crop productivity. Plants cannot directly assimilate the abundant nitrogen available in our atmosphere; instead, they rely on the uptake of inorganic forms of nitrogen, such as ammonium and nitrate from the soil. Nitrogen is a limiting nutrient in wheat production, and wheat yields are very responsive to nitrogen fertilisation. Only diazotrophic bacteria can convert atmospheric nitrogen to ammonia via biological nitrogen fixation (BNF), and although improving BNF in wheat has been a longstanding objective, there have been no descriptions of successful modification of wheat crops showing increased BNF in the literature. Here we describe the use of polycistronic multiplexed CRISPR to modify the flavone biosynthetic pathway of hexaploid wheat (Triticum aestivum) plants, generating DNA-edited plants with increased apigenin content. The apigenin-enriched plants exude apigenin into the soil, inducing the colonisation of the roots and subsequent formation of biofilms in soil by diazotrophic bacteria. The low permeability of the biofilm to oxygen protected the bacterial nitrogenase and stimulated BN. Under nitrogen-limiting conditions, apigenin-enriched wheat lines exhibited increased nitrogen content, improved photosynthetic performance, and higher grain yield relative to wild-type controls. This work demonstrates the feasibility of engineering associative BNF in cereals via metabolic reprogramming of root exudation, offering a sustainable route to reduce dependence on synthetic nitrogen fertilisers. 

# 1 | Introduction

Nitrogen is essential to plant growth and crop production. In contrast to legume crops which have evolved the capacity to associate with nitrogen-fixing bacteria (diazotrophs), lodged inside host plant root nodules (Udvardi and Poole 2013), cereal crops depend exclusively on the uptake of inorganic forms of nitrogen, such as nitrate and ammonium, from the soil. 

Although cereal crops demand high N-fertiliser (Masclaux-Daubresse et al.  2010), their Nitrogen Use Efficiency (NUE) is relatively low and only $3 0 \% - 3 5 \%$ of the N-fertiliser is taken up by the plant (Raun and Johnson 1999). The inefficient NUE of cereals greatly contributes to nitrate contamination of soils and ground water, leading to serious health challenges to the general population (Essien et al. 2020). the eutrophication of water bodies (Withers et al. 2014), and inorganic N-fertilisers are volatilised to nitrous oxide that depletes the ozone layer in the stratosphere, contributing to global warming (Thompson et al. 2019). 

The addition of N-fertilisers to cereal crops contributes to enhanced food production, and more inorganic N-fertiliser will be needed to support a further increased food production required to satisfy the needs of an increasing world population (Omara et al. 2019). These additional inputs of N-fertilisers would make grain production more expensive, with a considerable aggravation in environmental pollution. Thus, there is a growing need to develop sustainable alternative agricultural practices aimed at reducing the excessive use of inorganic N-fertilisers. 

The conversion of atmospheric nitrogen to ammonia by soil diazotrophs can supplement crop' N needs, contributing to crop productivity of both legume and non-legume species, and reducing the amount of inorganic N-fertiliser used in agriculture (Herridge et  al.  2008; Van Deynze et  al.  2018). Diazotrophs produce nitrogenase, an enzymatic complex that catalyses the conversion of atmospheric $\Nu _ { 2 }$ to $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ , a process called Biological Nitrogen Fixation (BNF). BNF is hindered by the presence of oxygen, which inhibits the enzyme nitrogenase irreversibly and represses its synthesis (Robson and Postgate  1980; Hill  1988; Dixon and Kahn  2004). Thus, BNF by free-living diazotrophs in aerated agronomical soils is limited by the presence of oxygen. The regulation of nitrogen fixation and ammonia assimilation, via Glutamine synthetase and GOGAT, is associated to maximise the use of fixed nitrogen by the bacteria (Colnaghi et  al.  1997; Bueno Batista and Dixon  2019). Nevertheless, micromolar amounts of ammonia, converted to ammonium at physiological pH, can be excreted out of the bacteria cells (Ortiz-Marquez et  al.  2012; Barney et  al.  2015). The ammonium excreted can be readily transported into the plant by the action of highly efficient high-affinity transporters (AMTs, AKT1, and NSCC) localised at the root cells plasma membranes (Bloom et al. 2002; Hachiya and Sakakibara 2017). 

Several strategies aimed at engineering BNF in cereals have been reported. Bacterial nitrogenase genes have been expressed in both yeast and in plants. The nifH gene was expressed in tobacco chloroplasts (Buren et al. 2017), while the nif operon from Klebsiella pneumoniae was introduced into tobacco mitochondria (Allen et al. 2017). Also, the nitrogenase co-factor maturase NifB was expressed in tobacco mitochondria and chloroplasts (He et al. 2022), as well as rice mitochondria (Jiang et al. 2022). Although expression was attained in all the above-mentioned cases, the expressed proteins were inactive. Research aimed at the induction of root organogenesis in cereals, like legume nodules, has also been reported. Expression of nodulation genes and the induction of nodule-like structures have been reported in rice roots (Shen and Feng 2024, and references therein), but functional nitrogen-fixing nodules have not yet been achieved. The overexpression of transcription factors that regulate root development and control stem cell definition in rice led to the formation of root nodule-like structures (Hiltenbrand et al. 2016), which resemble nodules morphologically but lack the complex features required for symbiotic establishment with rhizobia. While this suggests some potential for engineering nodulelike organs in cereals, these structures do not support key 

requirements for symbiosis, such as infection thread formation and appropriate vascularity. Moreover, the maintenance of microaerophilic conditions in these structures needed for nitrogenase function is still unresolved (Guo et al. 2023). 

A synthetic biology approach was used to create a plant-bacteria signalling circuit to improve N-fixation activity of bacteria associated with the roots of a target plant. Barley was engineered to produce scyllo-inosamine (SI), a rhizopine signalling molecule, and the bacterium Azorhizobium caulinodans was transformed to express a SI-uptake system (Haskett et al. 2022). This system allowed a tight rhizopine-dependent control of factors driving the expression and activity of the nitrogenase from bacteria colonising the barley roots. This activation was specific to the genetically modified barley and supported the notion of generating plant-controlled symbiosis where the nitrogen-fixing bacteria could associate specifically with a particular target plant species (Haskett et al. 2022; Guo et al. 2023). 

Recently, a strategy for the induction of BNF in cereals through the formation of biofilms by soil diazotrophic bacteria was postulated (Yan et  al.  2022). Flavone biosynthetic pathways in rice were modified using CRISPR/Cas9, inducing the increase of apigenin content in rice plants growing under limiting N-conditions. The increase of apigenin content in CRISPRmodified plants, with the concomitant apigenin increase in root exudates, promoted biofilm formation in soil diazotrophs with the concomitant induction of BNF that resulted in a significant increase in grain yield as compared to the wild-type plants growing under similar N-limited conditions (Yan et al. 2022). 

Here, we successfully used polycistronic multiplexed CRISPR to modify the flavone biosynthetic pathway of hexaploid wheat plants, generating CYP75B knockouts that resulted in increased apigenin contents. The apigenin-enriched wheat plants exuded apigenin into the rhizosphere, inducing the formation of bacterial biofilms that supported increased BNF, allowing significant yield increases in plants growing under limited N conditions. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Generation of Apigenin-Enriched Hexaploid Wheat Lines

Flavonoids are important secondary metabolites that influence numerous physiological response processes in plants and symbiotic relationships with the soil microbiota through root exudation (Mierziak et al. 2014). We have shown previously that the flavone apigenin, and its glycosylated derivatives like apigenin-7-O-glucoside, exuded from rice plant roots, induced root colonisation and the formation of biofilms by soil diazotrophic bacteria. The bacterial biofilms created microaerophilic conditions that could favour the activity of bacterial nitrogenase, resulting in enhanced atmospheric nitrogen fixation by the soil diazotrophic bacteria in the rhizosphere (Yan et al. 2022). 

To achieve higher apigenin concentrations in rice plants, we used Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) to edit the CYP75B genes, encoding flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylases (F3′H) that catalyse the conversion of apigenin 

to luteolin. The rice CYP75B subfamily of CYTOCHROME P450 (CYP) comprises two groups of genes, CYP75B3 and CYP75B4, encoding ${ \mathrm { F } } 3 ^ { \prime } { \mathrm { H } } { \mathrm { s } }$ and $F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H s }$ , respectively. CYP75B3 and CYP75B4 mediate the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylation of the B-ring of flavonoids (Shih et al. 2008). In addition, CYP75B4 also catalyses the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylation of the 3′methoxylated flavone chrysoeriol, a component of tricin biosynthesis (Lam et al. 2015). 

To identify wheat homologues of the rice CYP75B, we performed a BLAST search using the amino acid sequences of rice CYP75B (LOC_Os10g17260 and LOC_Os10g16974) and identified 11 wheat homologues (TraesCS1A02G442200, TraesCS1A02G442300, TraesCS1B02G476400, TraesCS1D02 G450100, TraesCS2B02G613200, TraesCS6A02G012600, Traes CS6B02G018800, TraesCS6D02G015200, TraesCS7A02G411700, TraesCS7B02G310900, TraesCS7D02G404900) (Figure S1) that were annotated as $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ enzymes and scored 0.0 E-value in Uniprot (https://www.uniprot.org/). Genes located at chromosomes 1 and 2 (TraesCS1A02G442200, TraesCS1A02G442300, TraesCS1B02G476400, TraesCS1D02G450100, and TraesCS2B02G613200) were more similar to the rice CYP75B3 gene, while genes located at chromosomes 6 and 7 (TraesCS6A02G012600, TraesCS6B02G018800, TraesCS 6D02G015200, TraesCS7A02G411700, TraesCS7B02G310900, and TraesCS7D02G404900) were more similar to the rice CYP75B4 gene (Figure S2). As seen in other monocot plants, the Thr-to-Leu substitution at the C-terminal region is conserved in all wheat CYP75B4 homologue genes (Lam et al. 2015; Park et al. 2016, Figure S1). The 11 wheat CYP75B homologue genes were sequenced by Sanger sequencing using the primers listed in Table S1, and genomic DNA was isolated from the wild-type wheat ‘Fielder’ variety. Seven out of the 11 genes had DNA sequences with some differences compared to the sequences of the Chinese Spring variety from the databases (IWGSC, https:// plants.ensembl.org/Triticum_aestivum/Info/Index) that led to some amino acid changes (Figures S3–S9). Two gene sequences, TraesCS1A02G442200 and TraesCS6A02G012600, contained premature stop codons (Figures  S3 and S6); therefore, we excluded these two genes from further analysis. 

The F3′H activities of the proteins encoded by the wheat CYP75B homologue genes (located at chromosomes 1A, 1B, 1D, 2B, 6B, 6D, 7A, 7B and 7D) were functionally analysed for their ability to convert apigenin to luteolin. Each gene was expressed in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, microsomes from the transformed yeast were isolated, and the activities of the expressed proteins were analysed. The microsomes were incubated with apigenin and NADPH, and the formation of luteolin was detected by HPLC-MS/MS analysis (Figure S11). Luteolin synthesis was detected in all the recombinant yeast strains, although recombinant yeast expressing TraesCS6BO2G0188000 (at chromosome 6B) and TraesCS6BO2G0152000 (at chromosome 6D) displayed peaks similar to that of the vector control (Figure S11), suggesting the lack of $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ activity of the enzymes encoded by these genes. $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ activities of the proteins encoded by the different CYP75B analogs were also tested in  vivo by constitutively expressing each homologue in the Arabidopsis transparent testa7 (tt7) mutant, which lacks a functional $F 3 ^ { \prime } H$ gene (Shih et  al.  2008). Transgenic tt7 plants expressing $F 3 ^ { \prime } H$ genes displayed the accumulation of anthocyanins when grown under N limitation (Shih et  al.  2008). The constitutive expression of 

the wheat CYP75B-homologue genes (except genes located at chromosomes 6B and 6D), under the control of a 35S promoter, also rescued the phenotype, inducing the accumulation of anthocyanins when grown under limiting N (Figure S11). Taken together, these results indicated that the proteins encoded by wheat CYP75B genes located at chromosomes 1A, 1B, 1D, 2B, 7A, 7B and 7D encoded enzymes with $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ activity both in vitro and in vivo. 

To simultaneously edit all nine wheat CYP75B homologue genes, we designed four different gRNAs (Figure 1a). gRNA[1] and gRNA[3] targeted common sequences in CYP75B3 homologues located at chromosomes 1 and 2, and gRNA[2] and gRNA[4] targeted common sequences in CYP75B4 homologues located at chromosomes 6 and 7 (Figure 1a). A tRNA polycistronic gRNA expression system was used to process the four gRNAs from a single transcript (Xie et al. 2015; Figure 1a). To enhance the efficiency and speed of wheat callus regeneration, a GRF4-GIF1 chimera gene was included in the transformation construct (Debernardi et al. 2020; Figure 1a). 

Deep sequencing showed that a few CYP75B-edited lines displayed a high percentage of indels in multiple CYP75B4 genes (Figure 1b). Notably, gene editing was not detected in CYP75B3 genes located at chromosomes 1 and 2 (Figures S6 and S7). As it is possible that the gRNAs for CYP75B3 genes were not effective, we constructed another CRISPR vector that had a different gRNA for targeting CYP75B3 genes. However, no calli were regenerated with this vector despite several transformation attempts. This could be because, although the second CRISPR vector was efficient in editing CYP75B3 genes, wheat calli that lack CYP75B3 failed to regenerate as CYP75B3 is essential for callus regeneration in wheat. Expression analyses showed that CYP75B3 genes, but not CYP75B4 genes, were expressed at the callus stage (Figure S12). Also, no $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ plants lacking CYP75B3 expression were rescued after transformation, suggesting that CYP75B3 could play a role(s) in plant regeneration and/or development. Only one line of transformed plants, lacking CYP75B3 at chromosome 1D (line #L1, Figure  1b), survived the regeneration stage but displayed severe growth defects, suggesting that the CYP75B3 gene could play a role in vegetative growth as well. In the CRISPR lines with higher apigenin contents (lines #L3, #L12, #L24 and #L25) (Figure 1c), all three CYP75B4 genes located at chromosome 7 were edited (Figure 1b; Figure S10), suggesting that they contributed equally to apigenin breakdown and that their simultaneous silencing contributed to attaining higher apigenin concentrations. 

Both apigenin and apigenin-7-O-glucosides were shown to induce biofilm formation in diazotrophic bacteria (Yan et al. 2022). Nonetheless, the ratio between apigenin aglycone and its glycosides fluctuated in the plant root exudates, likely due to the complex enzymatic reactions among the microbiota of the rhizosphere (Hartwig and Phillips 1991), and the action of microbial glycosidases (Hyung Ko et al. 2006; Meech et al. 2019). To eliminate the influence of these variations in the quantification of apigenin in the plant tissues and exudates, we opted for the release of apigenin aglycone from its glycoside forms using acid hydrolysis of the plant extracts and exudates (Lam et al. 2015) for the determination of apigenin contents. # 2.2 | The CYP75B-Edited Wheat Lines Displayed Enhanced Biological Nitrogen Fixation and Increased Seed Yield Under Limiting N-Fertiliser Conditions

To assess the effect of apigenin exuded by the roots of the wheat CRISPR plants on bacterial biofilm formation, seedlings were grown hydroponically in the presence of the diazotroph Azospirillum brasilense expressing noeLpro::GFP (Lemer et  al.  2009) (to indicate biofilm formation) and a constitutive genepro::mcherry (to indicate the bacterial localisation). Roots from the wheat CRISPR plants displayed the attachment of Azospirillum brasilense (as indicated by the mCherry fluorescence) (Figure  2). Coincident with the mCherry fluorescence, there was a GFP fluorescence that indicated the expression of noeL (encoding GDP-mannose 4,6-dehydratase, associated with exopolysaccharide synthesis), critical to biofilm formation (Figure 2). The merging of both mCherry and GFP signals in the roots from CRISPR plants was higher than in the roots from WT plants, indicating the bacteria-root association in the CRISPR plants (Figure 2a). 

The higher GFP/mcherry fluorescence ratio seen in the CRISPR plants indicated the biofilm-associated noel expression (Figure 2b). Previous work suggested that apigenin exuded from the roots of DNA-modified rice plants influenced the structural organisation of the root microbiome, potentially favouring an increase in diazotrophic bacterial populations (Yan et al. 2022). Six replicates of soil bacterial DNA associated with the endosphere, rhizoplane, and rhizosphere of 8-week-old WT and CRISPR wheat plants grown in an agronomic soil mixture under limiting N-conditions were isolated and bacterial 16S from each compartment was amplified and sequenced (Figure 3a; Figure S13). The Shannon diversity index (Shannon 1948) was used to measure both the richness and the abundance of bacteria in each sample. Principal coordinates analysis (PCoA), measured by Bray–Curtis distance (Bray and Curtis 1957), revealed that microbiota recruited by the CRISPR plants clustered separately from the wild-type plants in all three compartments, demonstrating that the plant genotype played a significant role in shaping the microbiota (Figure 3a; Figure S13). PERMANOVA analysis (Anderson 2001) (Figure S13) showed that the genotype significantly affected the composition of microbiome diversity in all three compartments: endosphere $( F _ { 1 , 9 } = 1 . 2 7$ , $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 2 4$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 0 1 ,$ ), rhizoplane $( F _ { 1 , 9 } { = } 1 . 3 2$ , $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 1 7$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 0 2 ,$ ), and rhizosphere $( F _ { 1 , 1 0 } { = } 1 . 2 6$ , $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 1 1 2$ , $p { = } 0 . 0 0 2 ,$ ), with no significant interaction effects in any model.

Recently, Yan et al. (2022) demonstrated a novel approach for the generation of BNF in rice. Following the identification of plant metabolites able to induce biofilm formation in diazotrophs, they edited rice Cytochrome P450 genes (CYP75B), encoding flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime }$ hydroxylases $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ and $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } ,$ ) associated with the catabolism of apigenin, a flavone identified as a hostderived biofilm inducer from a chemical screen (Yan et al. 2022). The accumulation of apigenin in the DNA-edited plants led to increased apigenin in the root exudates. The root exudates induced biofilm formation by the diazotrophs in the rhizosphere and the concomitant generation of microaerophilic conditions that increased the diazotrophic BNF. The induced BNF resulted in increased grain yield in the modified rice plants when grown under nitrogen-limiting conditions (Yan et al. 2022).

As a result, we successfully introduced mutation in wheat CYP75B4 genes in all three genomes which validated the functional flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ hydroxylases and its increased grain yield phenotype under lower nitrogen fertilisation. This will pave the road for functional genomics and crop improvement by mutagenesis in traditionally challenging hexaploid wheat. When wheat plants were grown hydroponically in the presence of Azospirillum brasilense, roots from wheat CRISPR plants showed the attachment of apigenin-induced biofilms containing bacteria (Figure 2). Azospirillum brasilense is a wellcharacterised plant growth-promoting rhizobacterium with associative nitrogen-fixing capabilities that colonises the rhizoplane and root hairs of grasses and is effective in wheat (Cassan et  al.  2020). Once the biofilm formed, its functional matrix (Extracellular Polymeric Substances, EPS), with its low oxygen permeability (Patel and Bott 1991), created a microaerophilic environment that enabled nitrogenase activity. 

The proportion of nitrogen-fixing bacteria in the rhizoplane and endosphere of CRISPR roots was higher than in wild-type roots 

(Figure 3; Figure S13), indicating that the apigenin-induced biofilms not only could contribute to protect nitrogenase from the harmful effects of oxygen, but also facilitate the colonisation of the host roots by soil diazotrophic bacteria (Figure  2). Biofilm production by diazotrophs associated with the CRISPR wheat roots facilitated $\Nu _ { 2 }$ -fixation activity (either acetylene reduction or assimilation of $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ under N-limited conditions, Figure 4a,b). Notably, no significant N-fixation activity was detected under control conditions $( 1 0 0 \%$ N), since BNF and nitrogenase synthesis are inhibited by inorganic and organic nitrogen compounds (Stewart  1980). Wheat plants grown under limiting N conditions $5 0 \%$ and $3 0 \%$ N, equivalent to 75 and 45 ppm N, respectively) showed the effect of N-deficiency on growth and seed yield (Figures 5 and 6). The growth and seed yield penalty brought about by N-limiting conditions was much smaller in the CRISPR plants, and their spike number and seed yield per plant were double that of wild type grown under the same N-limiting conditions. Our results indicated that the wheat CRISPR plants attained better growth and higher yields under N-limiting conditions through BNF by the activity of the soil diazotrophic bacteria biofilms associated with the roots of the CRISPR plants (Figure 6). 

N deficiency resulted in reduced photosynthesis and chlorophyll content (Figure  7a,b). However, this reduction was less pronounced in wheat CRISPR lines grown at $5 0 \%$ and $3 0 \%$ N. The reduction in chlorophyll content was closely related to the decrease in N nutrition, since N is a fundamental component of these pigments which negatively affects photosynthesis (Mu and Chen 2021). During plant development, particularly in the reproductive growth stage, nutrient uptake declines, and nutrients—including nitrogen—are remobilised to support the 

development of reproductive organs (Pask et  al.  2012). The contribution of the N fixed by diazotrophs was evident in the higher N content of leaves from CRISPR plants grown at $3 0 \%$ N. Notably, the grain N content in wheat CRISPR plants grown under limited N remained comparable to that of control conditions, whereas wild-type plants grown under these conditions displayed a $3 0 \%$ reduction in seed N content (Figure 7d). 

In conclusion, our results confirm the novel strategy proposed by Yan et al. (2022) and provide evidence of the viability of engineering BNF in a hexaploid cereal like wheat. Different strategies have been postulated for many years to achieve nitrogen fixation in cereals. These include the expression of bacterial genes in the target plant (Allen et al. 2017), the generation of bacterial inoculants that could supply a large part of the N needed by cereals, attempts to engineer the formation of nodule-like structures (Hiltenbrand et  al. 2016), etc. These strategies were unable to demonstrate fixation of atmospheric nitrogen in agronomically significant quantities. Bacterial nitrogenases expressed in cereals were unstable and rapidly degraded. Genetically engineered bacteria, although displaying higher nitrogen-fixation activity, must compete for nutrients to survive and grow with hundreds of different bacteria present in the rhizosphere. Nodule-like structures in cereal roots cannot generate and maintain microaerophilic conditions needed for nitrogenase synthesis and activity. 

Here, we demonstrated a novel experimental approach in which DNA from hexaploid wheat plants was modified to increase the production of a biofilm-stimulating metabolite. The apigenin exuded by the roots into the soil stimulated the formation of biofilms by the diazotrophic bacteria which protected the nitrogenase and induced biological nitrogen fixation. When grown at limiting N conditions, the modified wheat plants displayed increased growth and grain yield as compared to the control plants. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant Growth Conditions and Treatments

Seeds of wheat (Triticum aestivum var. Fielder) were surface sterilised in a diluted bleach solution (Yan et al. 2022). The sterilised seeds were placed onto wet germination paper and kept at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 3 days, and then transferred to a plant growth chamber at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 3 days, and an additional 5 days under light conditions (16 h light/8 h dark) at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The germinated seeds were planted into 3-l pots filled with an agronomic soil mixture (1:1:1:1 mix of sand, Pumice, Peat and composted bark). Plants were grown in a greenhouse at 16 h/8 h day/night and $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity, and fertilised weekly with Hoagland and Arnon (1938) nutrient solution containing different nitrogen concentrations (see below). Fertiliser was provided through a Tefen MixRite 2.5 injector from two concentrated fertiliser tanks in the $0 . 5 \%$ injection ratio. The plants were watered with 1 L per pot weekly for 6 weeks and $1 . 3 \mathrm { L }$ afterwards. 

Wild type plants (WT) and three independent lines of CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated CYP75B homologues-edited (lines L3, L12 and 

L25) were grown in the greenhouse under three different nitrogen concentrations: $1 0 0 \%$ N (150ppm N), $5 0 \%$ N (75 ppm N), $3 0 \%$ N (45 ppm N). The experimental design was defined in six blocks, each block followed a random distribution within the greenhouse. Each block represented a $4 \times 3$ factorial experimental design with six replications. Each block consisted of four different wheat lines (WT, L3, L12, and L25) subjected to three different N-fertiliser treatments. 

# 4.2 | Yield Parameters

At harvest stage, spikes, shoots, and seeds were harvested and counted from each plant. Twelve plants per phenotype and treatment were used to measure grain yield (g of dry seed/ plant) as well as the harvest index $\mathrm { \Delta } H _ { \mathrm { i } } { = } \mathrm { g }$ seeds/total areal biomass). 

# 4.3 | Photosynthesis and Chlorophyll Measurements

Photosynthesis and chlorophyll content were measured using a modified procedure based on Yan et  al.  (2022). Net $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ assimilation was measured in 5-week-old wheat plants using a LI-COR 6400-40 (in the morning $9 { \mathrm { - } } 1 0 { \mathrm { a . m } } .$ ., $1 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light, $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $7 0 \%$ RH). The same leaf was used for chlorophyll measurements: $5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ tissue was ground in liquid nitrogen, extracted twice with $8 0 \%$ acetone, and centrifuged. Absorbance of supernatants was measured at 663 and $6 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ (Tecan Infinite M Plex plate reader, Tecan Group Ltd., Switzerland) and chlorophyll content was calculated following Arnon (1949). 

# 4.4 | Total Nitrogen Content Measurements

Leaves from 5-week-old wild-type and CRISPR wheat plants were harvested from all nitrogen treatments and freeze-dried. Seeds were harvested after maturity from wild type and CRISPR wheat plants. Fine powder of leaves and seeds were submitted to the UC Davis Analytical Laboratory to measure the total nitrogen content using “Total Nitrogen Kjeldahl method” (Horneck and Miller 1998). 

# 4.5 | $\mathbf { \bar { \tau } } ^ { 1 5 } \mathbf { N } _ { 2 }$ Incorporation Assays

Roots from 5-week-old WT and CRISPR wheat plants, grown under control (100% N) and N-limited conditions $3 0 \%$ or $5 0 \%$ N), were excised 5 cm below the stem-root junction and placed with adherent soil (after shaking off loosely adherent soil) in 20- mL glass vials. Soil from treated pots without plants was used as bulk soil control. Ten millilitres of air from each sealed vial was replaced with $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ gas, and the vials were subsequently incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. After incubation, soil attached to the roots was carefully removed to prevent bacterial and soil contamination in the $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ measurements. The roots were then dried at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days and ground to a fine powder. The samples were then analysed at the UC Davis Stable Isotope Facility. is $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } / ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { N }$ of the soil or plant material while the $R _ { \mathrm { a i r } }$ used was the N isotope ratio in the atmosphere air $( R _ { \mathrm { a i r } } { = } 0 . 0 0 3 6 7 6 5 )$ . 

# 4.6 | Acetylene Reduction Assay

To determine nitrogenase activity in the soil/root interface (root zone), an acetylene reduction assay (ARA) (Stewart et  al.  1967) was performed according to Sriskandarajah et al. (1993) with some modifications. Roots from 5-week-old WT and CRISPR wheat plants, grown at $1 0 0 \%$ and $5 0 \%$ N, were excised below the stem-root junction and placed with attached soil in 100-mL glass bottles. The atmospheric gas in the vials was replaced with argon using a vacuum-argon chamber, and the vials were sealed with rubber serum stoppers. Subsequently, $1 0 \%$ of the headspace was replaced with acetylene gas, and the samples were incubated in the dark at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. Ethylene production was then measured using an Agilent 7890 gas chromatograph coupled with an Agilent 5975C mass spectrometer detector. Chromatograms were used to quantify ethylene production, which was compared to a standard curve generated from known ethylene concentrations. 

# 4.7 | Apigenin Measurements in Leaf, Root and Exudates

Apigenin content was measured in the leaves of 5-week-old wild-type and CRISPR wheat plants (L3 and L25) grown under $1 0 0 \%$ N, and in the roots and root exudates of the same lines grown under 30% N. Plants were removed from the pots, washed, and placed in $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ sterile Milli-Q water for 3 days in light-protective containers. The exudates were collected, filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ Corning $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ tube top vacuum filter system, and freeze dried. Roots and leaves were freezedried, ground into fine powder, dissolved in HPLC-grade methanol, and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until measurement. Then, the freeze-dried samples were weighed and soaked in methanol. After vortexing, the samples were sonicated at room temperature for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ to facilitate extraction, and then centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min. The supernatants were collected into new tubes, while the pellets were washed with methanol two more times. The collected supernatants were dried under vacuum, reconstituted with $1 \%$ (v/v) aqueous acetic acid, and purified by solid phase extraction (SPE) using an Iris-N cartridge column (Canadian Life Science, Ontario, Canada). The samples were eluted with methanol containing $1 \%$ (v/v) acetic acid, dried under vacuum, and the pellets were dissolved in $5 0 \%$ methanol for measurement by a liquid chromatography electron spray ionisation tandem mass spectrometry (LC-ESI-MS/MS). The recovery rate of apigenin 7-O-glucoside and apigenin through column purification was determined using the same purification procedures with authentic compounds. Total apigenin levels were determined by measuring apigenin after acid hydrolysis to release apigenin from its glucosides, following Lam et  al.  (2015) with minor modifications. The methanol extracts were treated with 1 N HCl in $5 0 \%$ methanol at $9 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, followed by an SPE as described above, prior to LC-ESI-MS/MS analysis. Flavonoid levels were measured by LC-ESI-MS/MS (Agilent 6410 Triple Quadrupole LC/ 

MS system, Agilent Technologies, Sta Clara, CA) equipped with a C18 column (ZORBAX Eclipse XDB-C18, $4 . 6 \times 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , 1.8-Micron, Agilent). MS/MS settings were as follows (positive mode): for apigenin, $m / z = 2 7 1 / 1 5 3$ (for quantification), fragmentor voltage: $1 6 0 \mathrm { V } _ { ; }$ , collision energy: $3 3 \mathrm { e V } ,$ , cell accelerator voltage 1 V; $m / z = 2 7 1 / 1 1 9$ (for confirmation), fragmentor voltage: 160 V, collision energy: $4 0 \mathrm { e V }$ , cell accelerator voltage: 1 V. Flavonoid concentration was calculated by normalising the peak area against the standard curve. Apigenin (SMB00702) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO). 

# 4.8 | Constructs for In Vitro Enzyme Assay and Arabidopsis tt7 Complementation

All nine wheat CYP75B genes were artificially synthesised and flanked with Gateway (Invitrogen, Waltham, MA) attL1 and attL2 sequences. Some codons were optimised for yeast expression and to pass the requirements for DNA synthesis without changing amino acid sequences revealed from genomic sequencing. These entry clones were recombined via LR reaction into pYES-DEST52 (Invitrogen) for yeast and into pEarleyGate 100 (Earley et  al. 2006) for Arabidopsis. The yeast constructs were transformed into INVSc1, Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Invitrogen) using Frozen-EZ Yeast Transformation II kit (Zymo Research, Irvine, CA) as described in the manufacturer's manual. Transformed cells were selected on agar plates containing synthetic minimal medium without uracil (SC-U). Arabidopsis constructs were transformed into tt7 mutant (SALK_124157C) through the floral dip method (Clough and Bent 2008). 

# 4.9 | Protein Expression in Yeast and Microsomal Protein Isolation

Yeast microsome extractions were performed as described previously (Urban et al. 1994). Microsomes were pelleted by centrifugation at $1 5 0 0 0 \times g$ for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and resuspended in $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl, pH 7.4, and 1 mM EDTA containing $2 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol. The final preparations $( 1 0 - 2 0 \mathrm { m g }$ protein $\mathrm { m } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } .$ ) were kept at $- 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until further use. 

# 4.10 | In Vitro Enzyme Assays

Protein concentrations of microsome preparations were determined by the Bradford protein assay (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA). $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ and $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ activities were assessed by measuring the conversion of apigenin to luteolin. The assays were performed for 1 h at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in $0 . 1 \mathbf { M }$ potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.0), 5 mM NADPH, 5 mM L-glutathione, 1 mM apigenin, and $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of microsomal protein. The reaction was stopped by extraction with an equal volume of ethyl acetate (twice). The ethyl acetate fraction was evaporated to dryness under vacuum, and the pellet was redissolved in methanol for LC–MS/MS analysis.

# 4.12 | Arabidopsis tt7 Complementation

Harvested seeds from Arabidopsis $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ mutant (tt7) expressing each of the nine wheat CYP75B genes were germinated on soil, and the seedlings were selected by three spray applications of BASTA $( 1 2 0 \mathrm { m g m L ^ { - 1 } }$ , 1:1000 in water). # 4.13 | Quantitative Real-Time PCR

RNA was extracted from 50 to $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of tissue using the Qiagen RNeasy Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Redwood City, CA). # 4.14 | qPCR Quantification of nifH Gene

For the nifH gene standard, the gene was amplified with F2 (TGYGAYCCIAAIGCIGA) and R6 (GCCATCATYTCICCIGA) primers (Marusina et al. 2001) using pooled DNA samples from all compartments as template. The desired size of bands was cloned into pGEM-T Easy (Promega, Madison, WI) and verified by Sanger sequencing. of genomic DNA, $2 0 0 { - } 5 0 0 \mathrm { n M }$ of F2 primer and nifH379R64 (GGCATNGCRAARCCRCCRCA) primer (Wang et  al.  2022) under the following conditions: 10 min at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by 40 cycles of 15 s at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 1 min at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.15 | Generation of Wheat CYP75B Mutants by Multiplex CRISPR Editing

Multiplex of four gRNAs, with tRNAs in between, was assembled with an AarI digestion/ligation process. Briefly, three gRNA-tRNA fragments flanked with the spacer sequences and AarI sites were amplified using the primers listed in the Table S2 and a plasmid harbouring the gRNA-tRNA sequence as a template. The three fragments were assembled through an AarI digestion/ligation reaction, and the desired size of the band (0.5kb) was recovered from gel extraction. Nesting PCR was performed using the primers listed in the Table S2 and the three $0 . 5 \mathrm { k b }$ fragments as templates. The desired size of the band was recovered and used for AarI digestion/ligation cloning into JD633 (Debernardi et  al.  2020). The construct was then transformed into wheat var. Fielder via Agrobacterium strain EHA105 at the UC Davis Transformation Facility. Hygromycin resistant-regenerated calli were rooted, transplanted to soil, and delivered as independent $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ lines. 

# 4.16 | Genotyping With Next Generation Sequencing

DNA was isolated from $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ , $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ , and $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ lines using the CTAB method (Murray and Thompson 1980). To detect editing events, the 150- to 300-bp region flanking the target site was amplified in the first round PCR with primers that are specific to genes of interest with adaptor sequences as listed in Table S5. All gene amplicons were pooled together for each sample and barcoded with the second round PCR primers listed in Table S5. The resulting PCR products were pooled in equal quantities, purified using columns, and sent for sequencing at MGH CCIB DNA Core Facility (Cambridge, MA) for deep sequencing. The obtained fastq files were filtered, trimmed, demultiplexed, and mapped for variant calling using web-based tools (https://junli. netlify.app/apps/). 

# 4.17 | Metagenome Sampling

To assess the diazotroph community associated with wheat roots, wild-type and apigenin-enriched CRISPR plants were grown in soil under different N-fertiliser concentrations for 8 weeks, as described above. Ten centimetres root segments were harvested and rhizosphere, rhizoplane, and endosphere were separated as described previously (Edwards et al. 2015). For DNA extraction, the rhizosphere, rhizoplane, and endosphere samples were first homogenised with 2-mm metal beads in a 2mL Eppendorf tube using a Geno/Grinder 2010 (Spex SamplePrep/Cole-Parmer, Illinois, USA). DNA from all the homogenised samples was further extracted by DNeasy PowerSoil Kit (QIAGEN Sciences Inc., Maryland, USA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. 

# 4.18 | 16S Metagenomic Sequencing

Primers 799F (TCGTCGGCAGCGTCAGATGTGTATAAGAG ACAG(spacer)TCAACMGGATTAGATACCCKG) and 1193R ( GTCTCGTGGGCTCGGAGATGTGTATAAGAGACAG(sp acer)TCACGTCATCCCCACCTTCC) were used to amplify the V5–V7 domain of the 16S rRNA gene using a two-step PCR procedure. In the first step of the amplification, both forward and reverse primers included an Illumina adapter sequence (bold), a variable-length spacer (either no spacer, or 1, 2 or 3 nucleotides), to increase sequence diversity and improve sequencing quality, a linker sequence (italicised), and the 16S rDNA target sequence (underlined). In the second step, each sample was barcoded with a unique combination of forward and reverse barcodes using forward primer (AATGATACGGCGACCACCGAGATCTA CACNNNNNNNNTCGTCGGCAGCGTC) consisting of the Illumina P5 adapter sequence (bold), a unique 8 nt barcode (N), a partial matching sequence of the forward adapter used in the first step (underlined), and reverse primers (CAAGCAGAAGA CGGCATACGAGATNNNNNNNNGTCTCGTGGGCTCGG) consisting of the Illumina P7 adapter sequence (bold), unique 8 nt barcode (N), and a partial matching sequence of the reverse adapter used in the first step (underlined). First- and secondstep PCRs were performed using Kapa2G Robust Hot Start Polymerase (Sigma Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) following the manufacturer's protocol. 

The final PCR products were quantified on the Qubit 4.0 instrument using the Qubit High Sensitivity dsDNA kit (Invitrogen Corporation, Waltham, MA) and individual amplicons were pooled in equal concentrations. The pooled library was cleaned using Ampure XP beads (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA, USA), then checked for quality and proper amplicon size on an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). The library was sequenced as a 300-bp-paired-end run using an Element AVITI Sequencing System (Element Biosciences, San Diego, CA) in the Genome Center DNA Technologies Core, University of California, Davis. Target amplification, barcoding and library preparation was performed at the UC Davis Host Microbe Systems Biology Core facility. 

# 4.19 | 16S Metagenomic Analysis

Briefly, sequences were demultiplexed and primers were trimmed using dbcAmplicon (https://github.com/msettles/ dbcAmplicons). The remaining sequence processing occurred in the dada2 version 1.16 R package (Callahan et al. 2016), beginning with forward and reverse reads trimming to 270 and 180 bp, respectively, following manual inspection of read quality profiles. Forward and reverse reads were filtered to allow a maximum of two expected errors. After error estimation and sample inference, forward and reverse reads were merged, and a sequence table was generated for unique sequence (actual sequence variants/ASVs) and sequence counts per sample. Assignments to species were based on exact sequence matching between ASVs and sequences references strains. We removed ASVs that could not be classified as bacteria, as well as sequence variant singletons or shorter than 350 bp. 

Principal coordinates (Gower  1966) and PERMANOVA (Anderson  2001) analyses for Beta-diversity were conducted on Bray-Curtis distances (Bray and Curtis 1957) of log-transformed TMM-normalised (Robinson and Oshlack 2010) taxon counts. The PERMANOVA analysis used the function adonis2 in the R package vegan, version 2.6-8. Analyses were conducted using R version 4.4.1 (2024-06-14). Centroids for each treatment were estimated based on coordinate means. The model incorporated inverse variance weights to address differences in variability between groups. 

The proportion of nitrogen fixing bacteria in each sample was estimated from the sequence counts for nitrogen fixing bacterial species divided by the sequence counts for all bacterial species. FAPROTAX v 1.2.10 was used to identify nitrogen fixing taxa. The proportion of nitrogen fixing bacteria was compared between both types of samples (genotypes) using a weighted linear model that included effects for genotype, compartment, and genotype-compartment interaction. The model incorporated inverse variance weights to address differences in variability between groups. Analyses were conducted using R version 4.4.1 (2024-06-14). 

# 4.20 | Statistical Analysis

All values between the different genotypes and N-treatments related to plant growth, grain yield, nitrogen fixation, chlorophyll contents and photosynthetic rates were analysed with the GraphPad Prism 10.3 software. One-way ANOVA analysis and multivariate analysis of the means were performed with significance set at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ .
