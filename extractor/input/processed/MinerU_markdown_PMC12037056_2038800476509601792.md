# The emergence and loss of cyclic peptides in Nicotiana illuminate dynamics and mechanisms of plant metabolic evolution

Elliot M. Suha,b,c,d, Jakob K. Reinhardta,b,c,d , and Jing-Ke Wenga,b,c,d,1 

Affiliations are included on p. 7. 

Edited by Ian Baldwin, Max-Planck-Institut fur chemische Okologie Abteilung fur Molekulare Okologie, Jena, Germany; received November 29, 2024; accepted March 18, 2025 

Specialized metabolism plays a central role in how plants cope with both biotic and abiotic stresses in order to survive and reproduce within dynamic and challenging environments. One recently described class of plant-specific, ribosomally synthesized, and posttranslationally modified peptides are the burpitides, which are characterized by the installation of distinct sidechain macrocycles by enzymes known as burpitide cyclases. While they are found across many plant families and exhibit diverse bioactivities, little is known about their evolution or how new variants arise. Here, we present the identification of a burpitide cyclase, resurrected from a defunct pseudogene from the model organism Nicotiana attenuata, the coyote tobacco. By repairing the pseudogene ΨNatBURP2 and expressing it heterologously in Nicotiana benthamiana, we successfully reconstituted its original enzymatic activity. As an autocatalytic peptide cyclase, it installs a unique C–C bond between the tyrosine side chain and a specific backbone $\alpha$ -carbon of a heptapeptide core motif, resulting in burpitides dubbed “nanamins.” Despite its pseudogenization in $N .$ attenuata, we found that the closely related species, Nicotiana clevelandii, retains the wild-type gene and produces nanamins. Phylogenetic analyses and targeted mutagenesis experiments reveal that this chemotype must have evolved from the duplication and neofunctionalization of a more promiscuous ancestral gene. This work highlights how novel peptide chemotypes may rapidly emerge and disappear in plants, while expanding the molecular toolkit for engineering novel peptides with applications in agriculture and drug discovery.

Plant peptides, which include the cyclotides ( 1 ), orbitides ( 2 ), defensins ( 3 ), thionins ( 4 ), snakins ( 5 ), and burpitides ( 6 ), are a diverse group of secondary metabolites that play important roles in how plants adapt to their environmental niches. They have been shown to mediate pathogen/herbivore defense, growth regulation, stress responses, metal chelation, and root–microbiome interactions and are distinguished by the structural features conferred by their various posttranslational modifications ( 6 – 8 ). While many of these ribosomally synthesized and posttranslationally modified peptides (RiPPs) are cyclized head-to-tail or by disulfide bridges ( 9 , 10 ), the burpitides instead contain distinctive sidechain macrocycles installed by catalytic BURP domains ( 6 ). The BURP domain is a plant-specific protein family defined by a highly conserved sequence motif of four sets of interspaced cysteine histidine residues and was originally named after the founding members BNM2, USP1, RD22, and $\mathrm { P G 1 \beta }$ ( 11 ). Although these proteins have long been studied, for example, for their roles in mediating drought resistance ( 12 ) and overexposure to transition metals ( 13 ), their catalytic activity was only recently discovered: the copper-dependent formation of C–C, C–O, and C–N bonds necessary for peptide macrocyclization ( 14 ). While the ecological functions of most burpitides remain elusive, they have been shown to exhibit analgesic ( 15 , 16 ), hypotensive ( 17 ), and tubulin-binding properties ( 18 – 20 ). The growing interest in peptide therapeutics highlights the importance of exploring new cyclic peptide scaffolds, such as those of burpitides, and macrocyclases as key to performing cyclization reactions that are otherwise chemically challenging or unfeasible ( 21 , 22 ). Additionally, the versatility of burpitide biosynthetic systems has sparked interest in utilizing them as platforms to introduce and engineer genetically encoded agronomic traits ( 23 ).

# Significance

While the class of ribosomally synthesized, post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs) represents a major source of antibiotics and bioactive compounds, plant RiPPs are relatively understudied compared to those from microbial sources. Here, we resurrect an extinct peptide cyclase from the coyote tobacco through analysis of its functional relatives in other species. This cyclase installs a carbon–carbon macrocycle into heptapeptides, expanding the diversity of plant-derived cyclic peptides. By interconverting two distinct cyclases through targeted mutations, we illuminate how these enzymes evolve new functions. This work highlights the evolutionary dynamics of plant peptide natural products and their potential applications in drug discovery and biotech crop development, while illustrating how genomic archaeology can reveal lost biosynthetic capabilities.

In the case of fused burpitide precursor genes, these tandem repeats encode several copies of the precursor peptide, linked by recognition sequences, which are autocatalytically cyclized by the C-terminal BURP domain. The first gene identified with this structure was LbaLycA , which produces the angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) and renin-inhibiting lyciumin peptides isolated from Lycium barbarum , the goji berry ( 17 , 23 ). Lyciumins contain a macrocyclic link between the indole nitrogen of a tryptophan and the $\alpha$ -carbon of a glycine within the core peptide and are produced by many members of the plant family Solanaceae ( 23 ). However, several lineages within Solanaceae appear to have lost the ancestral lyciumin precursor gene, an evolutionary pattern commonly observed in specialized metabolism ( 30 , 31 ). For example, Nicotiana benthamiana ( Fig. 1A ), which is commonly used for transient gene expression, is devoid of any lyciumin precursor gene homolog. Therefore, previous studies employed it as a convenient host for the heterologous reconstitution of LbaLycA ( 23 ). 

Upon further investigation of the thirteen Nicotiana species with sequenced genomes, we found that only the coyote tobacco (Nicotiana attenuata ) retains a direct homolog of the LbaLycA , which we termed NatBURP1 ( Fig. 1B ). Within the field of chemical ecology, N. attenuata is a well-studied model organism with respect to its complex interactions with herbivores ( 32 ). In response 

to herbivory, $N .$ . attenuata can dynamically adjust its chemical defenses initiated by the methyl jasmonate signaling system ( 33 ). These include altering its flowering timing to attract different pollinators ( 34 ), attracting predatory insects to feed on its herbivores ( 35 ), or producing defense compounds or proteins to deter or resist herbivory, such as triterpenoids, trypsin proteinase inhibitors, or the green leaf volatile caffeoylputrescine-hexenal ( 36 – 38 ). These studies demonstrate how $N .$ . attenuata actively engages with and shapes its biotic environment in order to survive and reproduce. While this chemical plasticity helps $N .$ . attenuata thrive in challenging ecosystems, the maintenance of such defenses also incurs a metabolic cost, and the evolutionary arms race ensures that not every adaptation will remain effective indefinitely ( 39 – 41 ). The identification of a lyciumin precursor gene in $N .$ . attenuata raised intriguing questions about the evolutionary dynamics of cyclic peptides within Nicotiana , prompting us to investigate its peptide products and their biosynthetic machinery.

We identified two distinct cyclopeptides within its roots (lyciumins P and Q) consistent with chemotypes encoded by the gene NatBURP1 (Fig.  1C), which we hypothesized to be a “fused” burpitide precursor responsible for the biosynthesis of lyciumin (14). We confirmed the activity of this gene by cloning NatBURP1 into the pEAQ-HT vector, transforming it into Agrobacterium tumefaciens LBA4404, and then syringe-infiltrating the leaves of N. benthamiana (42–44), which resulted in the corresponding production of lyciumins P and Q (Fig. 1C). 

The nonrepetitive regions of NatBURP1 share $9 0 \%$ and $8 3 \%$ amino acid sequence identity to LbaLycA and StuBURP, lyciumin precursors from L. barbarum and Solanum tuberosum , respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 ). This close homology suggests a common progenitor gene carried by the last common ancestor of these three species, which dates to approximately 24 Mya ( 45 ). If lyciumin is indeed ancestral, it may explain why N. benthamiana can serve as a suitable host for its production, despite having lost its own lyciumin precursor gene. Previous studies have shown that the BURP domain functions solely as a peptide cyclase, indicating that the full biosynthesis of lyciumin most likely involves other enzymes (such as peptidases and glutaminyl cyclotransferases) that lyse the recognition sequence and convert N-terminal glutamine into pyroglutamate ( 23 ). While it was originally assumed that N. benthamiana was able to complete the biosynthesis of lyciumin when transfected with LbaLycA using nonspecific processive enzymes, it is also possible that $N .$ benthamiana has inherited pathway-specific biosynthetic machinery, despite lacking a lyciumin precursor gene itself. 

While studying NatBURP1 , we identified another locus within the genome of $N .$ . attenuata, located just 0.8 Mbp away on the same chromosome. This locus encodes ΨNatBURP2 , which shares $8 3 \%$ protein sequence identity to NatBURP1 , suggesting that the two are closely related (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 ). However, ΨNatBURP2 cannot function as a lyciumin precursor because it lacks the lyciumin motif $\mathrm { [ Q P X _ { 5 } W ] }$ , where X represents any amino acid. Instead, ΨNatBURP2 encodes several repeats of the core peptide [QPWGLMY], which is one amino acid shorter and contains a C-terminal tyrosine instead of tryptophan ( Fig. 1 ). Nevertheless, ΨNatBURP2 appears to be a pseudogenized locus due to a frameshift mutation and the presence of several premature stop codons ( Fig. $1 D _ { \ell }$ ). 

Resurrecting the Pseudogene ΨNatBURP2. To investigate the original function of this pseudogene, we aligned its exons with homologous genes within Solanaceae to reconstruct a functional coding DNA sequence. The frameshift was corrected, and the two premature stop codons were replaced with homologous coding codons from NatBURP1. To address the challenges posed by repetitive regions during DNA synthesis, we reduced the number of tandem repeats from 11 to 5, enabling the de novo synthesis of the resurrected gene called 5x_Nna (Fig. 1E). 

After cloning it into the pEAQ-HT vector, the transient expression of 5x_Nna in N. benthamiana produced an analyte matching the cyclic form of the peptide [QPWGLMY] ( Fig. 1F ). The mass of the compound indicates the loss of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 3 }$ molecules, consistent with cyclization and pyroglutamate formation, which are known to occur during the biosynthesis of burpitides such as lyciumin and moroidin ( 14 , 18 ). To elucidate the structure of this peptide, termed “nanamin” (from Nicotiana ), we purified 8 mgs of peptide from $2 0 0 \mathrm { g }$ of N. benthamiana fresh leaf tissue infiltrated with the 5x_Nna construct. During the purification process, the compound was observed to oxidize spontaneously, which we hypothesized to be due to the oxidation of methionine to methionine sulfoxide ( 46 , 47 ). The oxidized 

peptide was purified and structurally elucidated and was shown to contain three posttranslational modifications, including oxidized methionine and N-terminal pyroglutamate as hypothesized (SI Appendix, Figs. S2–S8 and Table S1 ). The cyclic bond was revealed to be a link between the phenolic metacarbon of the C-terminal tyrosine and the $\alpha$ -carbon of glycine ( Fig. 1F ), which classifies nanamin as a hibispeptin-type burpitide based on its $\mathrm { C } ( \mathsf { s p } ^ { 2 } ) \mathrm { - C } ( \mathsf { s p } ^ { 3 } )$ bond. While other members of this group are linked from sidechain to sidechain ( 6 , 25 , 48 ), nanamin’s cyclic bond instead involves the backbone $\alpha$ -carbon of its glycine residue. The activation of the $\alpha$ -carbon appears unique to the cyclic bonds of lyciumin (N-C) and nanamin (C–C), a similarity which likely stems from the common evolutionary origin of NatBURP1 and ΨNatBURP2 . 

Identification of Species Retaining the Wild-Type Nanamin Precursor Gene. Consistent with the pseudogenization of ΨNatBURP2, tissue extracts of $N .$ . attenuata do not contain nanamin (Fig. 1F). To investigate whether any other Nicotiana species still retain the wild-type nanamin genotype and chemotype, we grew and analyzed four additional tobacco species: Nicotiana clevelandii, N. quadrivalvis, N. pauciflora, and N. obtusifolia. While N. obtusifolia is only distantly related to $N .$ . attenuata, the two species are believed to have hybridized approximately 1 Mya, resulting in N. clevelandii and N. quadrivalvis as distinct hybrid descendants of both lineages (49, 50) (Fig. 2). Notably, we detected the presence of nanamin A in root extracts of both N. clevelandii and N. pauciflora, as well as its oxidized form, which we have termed nanamin B (Fig. 2C). We were moreover able to confirm the presence of lyciumins in N. clevelandii, N. pauciflora, and N. quadrivalvis, but not N. obtusifolia (SI Appendix, Figs. S9–S14). 

Based on these leads, we used publicly available RNAseq datasets to assemble transcriptomes for N. clevelandii and N. pauciflora ( 51 , 52 ). From the assembled N. clevelandii transcriptome, we were able to identify both the full-length lyciumin and nanamin precursor genes, designated as NclBURP1 and NclBURP2 , respectively (SI Appendix, Table S2 ). We confirmed the activity of NclBURP2 by transiently expressing it in $N .$ benthamiana and found that it exhibited the same activity as 5x_Nna . Although sequence reads supporting the presence of the nanamin precursor gene were detected in the transcriptome assembled for Nicotiana pauciflora , insufficient coverage prevented the reconstruction of the full-length sequence. This limitation was likely due to the absence of root tissue samples in the raw data. Our data therefore show that at least two close relatives of $N .$ . attenuata retain the nanamin chemotype, where NclBURP2 was characterized unequivocally as the wild-type nanamin precursor gene from $N .$ . clevelandii.

Using 5x_Nna as the basis, we performed an alanine scan of the core peptide sequence [QPWGLMY]. By selectively mutating amino acids within this sequence to alanine, we were able to determine which residues were essential for cyclization and which were not. After infiltrating each construct into N. benthamiana, we did not observe the cyclization of the peptides [QPWALMY] or [QPWGLMA]. When the nanamin cyclase from 5x_Nna was paired with lyciumin core peptides, we did not detect the formation of lyciumin or any other cyclic peptide ( Fig. 4B ). However, when the lyciumin cyclase from NatBURP1 was paired with nanamin core peptides, it produced two isomeric products: Nanamin A and another cyclic peptide with the same mass but a distinct retention time ( Fig. 4A ). The molecular weight and fragmentation pattern of the isomer indicates that it contains a macrocyclic bond distinct from that of nanamin (SI Appendix, Fig. S23 ). While less efficient and less specific, the lyciumin cyclase exhibited latent catalytic activity by installing cyclic bonds into the nanamin core peptide. In contrast, the nanamin cyclase only generates one major product from its native substrate, but cannot accept the non-native lyciumin core peptide ( Fig. 4C ). We hypothesized that specific adaptive mutations must have occurred in the ancestral lyciumin cyclase to ultimately give rise to the derived nanamin cyclase. 

To identify the residues responsible for this functional transition, we generated structures for the two proteins using AlphaFold 3 ( 53 ) and aligned them (SI Appendix, Fig. S24 ). With the inclusion of the short oligopeptide [QPWGLMY] and two ${ \mathrm { C u } } ^ { 2 + }$ ions as inputs, the structural models predicted the coordination of both copper ions in the center of the BURP domain and the localization of the core peptide [QPWGLMY] to the catalytic center ( Fig. 4D ). This structural comparison allowed us to identify five distinct residues in the vicinity of the active site ( Fig. 4E ). We then constructed two chimeric BURPs by swapping these residues between the two proteins ( Fig. 4E ). These mutant cyclases were paired with either the nanamin or lyciumin core peptides and expressed in N. benthamiana for functional testing. While both mutagenized cyclases retained the ability to produce nanamin in vivo, the 

mutagenized lyciumin cyclase lost its ability to produce lyciumin, while the mutagenized nanamin cyclase gained this ability ( Fig. 4 A and $B$ ). Although the catalytic efficiency of the mutants was reduced, we successfully interconverted the two peptide cyclases by swapping just five residues. 

Given that the nanamin precursor gene is observed only in a few closely related Nicotiana species, the most parsimonious scenario is that it was derived from an ancestral lyciumin precursor within the Nicotiana genus ( Fig. 2 A and B ). Examining the active site sequences of other homologs supports this hypothesis: At each of the five critical residues, StuBURP contains residues identical to NatBURP1 and distinct from ΨNatBURP2 ( Fig. 4E ). If NatBURP1 represents the ancestral state more closely, its catalytic promiscuity suggests that ΨNatBURP2 likely neofunctionalized substrate first, through mutations that converted an ancestral lyciumin core peptide into a nanamin core peptide. Subsequent mutations in the BURP domain would have followed, harnessing its latent ability to cyclize nanamin while enhancing this newly selected activity, until its ancestral function was eventually lost. While the progenitor gene likely encoded multiple core peptides, “purifying” cycles of duplications and reductions could have homogenized the tandem repeats ( 27 ). Although ΨNatBURP2 was ultimately pseudogenized in N. attenuata , the gene and its associated chemotype have been retained in its close relatives, N. clevelandii and $N .$ . pauciflora .

This compact macrocycle, combined with N-terminal pyroglutamate formation and extensive sequence mutability at noncritical positions, makes nanamins particularly attractive for peptide engineering, alongside previously reported lyciumins ( 23 ), moroidins ( 18 , 23 ), and cyclopeptide alkaloids ( 25 ). The programmability of these peptides may offer solutions to key challenges faced by peptide therapeutics: Cyclization and N-terminal protection confer proteolytic stability, and mutability and relatively small size $( < 1 , 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { D a } ) }$ may help to address the problem of membrane permeability ( 54 – 56 ). Furthermore, the nanamin cyclase’s ability to form challenging C–C bonds in small macrocycles—typically difficult to achieve through chemical synthesis—underscores the value of enzymatic approaches in accessing novel peptide scaffolds. 

The serendipitous discovery of ΨNatBURP2 joins a rich history of pseudogene studies that illuminate evolution in action. Classic examples include the pseudogenization of the L-gulono- $\gamma$ -lactone oxidase (GULO) gene in primates, marking the loss of vitamin C synthesis following dietary changes ( 57 ), and the pseudogenization of rhodopsins in cave-dwelling fish after moving to lightless environments ( 58 ). However, successfully resurrecting pseudogenes remains challenging, as the deleterious mutations that rapidly accumulate following the initial pseudogenization event are expected to quickly narrow the paths for successful resurrection ( 59 ). Our relative ease in resurrecting ΨNatBURP2 from $N .$ . attenuata provides a rare glimpse into an evolutionary transition in progress, while the preservation of nanamin biosynthesis in its close relatives N. clevelandii and N. pauciflora allowed us to document the relatively recent gain and loss of a novel chemical trait in plants. In contrast to ancient protein resurrections, which typically reveal conservation of core mechanisms, our study demonstrates how novel enzymatic functions can emerge and disappear over relatively short evolutionary timescales in response to changing ecological environments. 

Classical molecular evolution studies of the glucocorticoid receptors showed that evolutionary trajectories tend to be irreversible due to the accumulation of “restrictive” mutations that optimize new specificities while making ancestral conformations structurally incompatible, creating an epistatic ratchet that constrains evolutionary reversal ( 60 ). In contrast, the study of specialized metabolic enzymes like plant sesquiterpene synthases reveals remarkable evolutionary plasticity, such that alternate product specificities are mutationally accessible through just one or a few mutations ( 61 ). This flexibility likely reflects adaptation to fluctuating ecological pressures, where maintaining catalytic promiscuity and diverse product profiles provides selective advantages over strict functional optimization ( 62 ). 

While the catalytic domains of the lyciumin and nanamin cyclases were interconverted by the exchange of five active-site residues, these experiments still emphasize how evolution can be both directional and difficult to reverse. In order to evolve backward, the nanamin precursor gene would need to regain both the precursor peptide and the substrate specificity that it no longer retains. And while we found that the nanamin cyclase lost its ability to produce lyciumin, we also found that it exhibits a wide tolerance for substrates containing the “correct” glycine and tyrosine residues. The substrate tolerance of both enzymes provides compelling evidence that promiscuity and evolvability may be positively selected traits. Moreover, the structure of these enzymes suggests that this promiscuity may be mitigated by the covalent localization of substrate to enzyme that is characteristic of fused 

burpitide precursors. Harnessing this substrate tolerance, in conjunction with biophysical and computational insights, lays the groundwork for engineering this unique family of peptide cyclases with diverse, desirable functions and properties.
