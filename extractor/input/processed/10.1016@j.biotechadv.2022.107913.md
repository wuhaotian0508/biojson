# ABSTRACT

Heterologous synthesis of proteins or peptides in plant-based systems, referred to as plant molecular farming, is a practical and safe approach for the large-scale and cost-effective production of therapeutic biomolecules. In this context, monocotyledonous plants, and especially cereals, have been considered attractive vehicles for producing high-value recombinant proteins. The endosperm, as the largest grain storage compartment, offers an appropriate environment for long-lasting protein accumulation. During the last decades, fascinating progress has been achieved in the gene transfer technology and genetic manipulation of the monocot crops using either Agrobacterium tumefaciens or direct gene transfer by biolistic methods. Our group has recently expressed biologically active recombinant human peptide cathelicidin in barley grains using endosperm-specific promoter and brought such engineered lines to field cultivation under current EU regulations for genetically modified organisms. This article reviews the most recent advances and strategies for the production of biopharmaceutical proteins in transgenic monocots, highlighting various aspects involved in recombinant protein accumulation in grains, and discussing current bottlenecks and perspectives for the biosynthesis of therapeutic molecules using different monocot plant platforms.

# 1. Introduction to monocotyledonous crops as bioreactors for molecular farming

Molecular farming can be defined as the use of plant cells or tissues to make biopharmaceutical proteins or peptides (Fischer and Emans, 2000; Rybicki, 2017). For such products, plants offer specific advantages over other eukaryotic or even prokaryotic systems in terms of large-scale and low-cost production of therapeutic proteins. Moreover, plants as an intrinsically safe platform contain no known replicating human pathogens. Plants also generally allow non-native proteins to be synthesized while retaining their proper folding and activity (Buyel, 2019; Mirzaee et al., 2018; Yusibov et al., 2011). However, some disadvantages of plant systems like low-yield recombinant protein production (resulting from low-level expression, post-transcriptional gene silencing, or instability of the recombinant proteins), non-mammalian type of glycosylation, and high-cost downstream processing, may work against the full exploitation of plants as therapeutic protein bioreactors. The power of plant genetic engineering could overcome the limitations and facilitate the production of certain pharmaceutical proteins (Burnett and Burnett, 2020; Matsuo and Atsumi, 2019; Moustafa et al., 2016; Obembe et al., 2011).

The first plant-made therapeutic recombinant protein, human growth hormone, was produced in tobacco and sunflower (Barta et al., 1986), but the first commercialized plant-derived product, avidin, was obtained from maize (Hood et al., 1997). Afterward, more than 100 pharmaceutical proteins have been expressed in a variety of plant platforms (reviewed by Fischer and Buyel, 2020; Yao et al., 2015).

One of the most often used platforms for molecular farming are two Nicotiana species, tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) and its close relative Nicotiana benthamiana, as non-food/feed leafy crops with well-established transgenic techniques and remarkable biomass yields. The second are cereal crops, especially maize, rice, and barley since their seeds are native places for accumulation and protection of proteins in large amounts (Buyel, 2019; Moustafa et al., 2016; Ramessar et al., 2008a; Spiegel et al., 2018).

A major disadvantage of recombinant protein production in leafy crops is the instability of the protein product in the aqueous environment of the leaves that provides conditions for proteolytic degradation (Gomes et al., 2019). The leaf tissue must be therefore frozen or dried before being transported to a processing facility, or processed immediately at the growth facility site (Ma et al., 2003; Twyman et al., 2003). By contrast, a seed-based bioreactor system provides an appropriate

biochemical and physiological environment for long-term storage at ambient temperatures without significant degradation of proteins, while showing lower content of phenolic components and undesired proteolytic enzymes, which facilitates downstream processing of the produced recombinant proteins (Fischer et al., 2004; Kermode, 2012; Sabalza et al., 2013).

In monocotyledonous plants, including cereals, the endosperm is highly differentiated tissue occupying the major part of the grain and containing well-developed organelles for the accumulation of storage proteins required for germination. These proteins are generally targeted to the secretory pathway, where they create specialized storage compartments such as endoplasmic reticulum protein bodies or protein storage vacuoles. Within these storage organelles, molecular chaperones and disulfide isomerases assist protein folding and confer stability, while a dry environment of the mature seed diminishes proteolytic degradation and non-enzymatic hydrolysis (Arcalis et al., 2014; Burnett and Burnett, 2020). Accordingly, the most favorable destinations for recombinant protein accumulations in seeds are the intracellular protein storage organelles of the endosperm, where similar to storage proteins, the recombinant protein will be placed in an environment providing high folding and storage capacity and stability (Boothe et al., 2010; Hensel, 2020; Mirzaee et al., 2021).

Cereal plants are among the most cultivated crops worldwide. In 2019/2020, the estimated world production of cereal grain was around 2.7 billion metric tons (FAO, 2021). Therefore, it is not surprising that a cereal seed-based platform has been considered for the production of pharmaceutical proteins on a large scale. In addition, edible seeds have GRAS (Generally Regarded As Safe) status for human consumption from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, making them ideally suited for the development of orally delivered vaccines or therapeutic proteins that can be administered as whole seeds, flakes, or flour with minimal purification (Naqvi et al., 2011; Sabalza et al., 2014).

Given the numerous advantages of cereal plants as a heterologous protein production platform, ProdiGene, Inc. (College Station, TX, USA) was the first company to introduce commercial production of plant-based recombinant proteins such as avidin (Hood et al., 1997), $\beta$ -glucuronidase (Witcher et al., 1998), trypsin (Howard and Hood, 1998), and aprotinin (Zhong et al., 1999), in the late 1990s. All these products were obtained from transgenic maize grains and marketed by Sigma-Aldrich Fine Chemical (St. Louis, MO, USA). Subsequently, Ventria Bioscience (Junction City, CO, USA) established the commercial production of two rice-derived proteins, recombinant human lactoferrin (Broz et al., 2013), and lysozyme (Nandi et al., 2002). Similarly, ORF Genetics (Kopavogur, Iceland) has engineered barley for producing a range of endotoxin-free cytokines and human-like growth factors in grain endosperm and commercialized dermatological, cosmetic, and personal care products (ORF Genetics, 2021) (Table 1).

# 2. Gene transfer technology status of monocots plants

Foreign genes can be incorporated into the cereal's genome by particle bombardment or by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation

(Fig. 1). The first successful generation of transgenic cereals through particle bombardment of wheat embryogenic callus was reported in 1992 (Vasil et al., 1992). The major disadvantage of biolistic-mediated transformation is the possible multiple integrations of the transgene, which can negatively affect gene expression or inheritance over the generations. Moreover, the bombardment of plant tissue by the microprojectiles causes significant tissue and cell damage (Hensel, 2020; Mrízová et al., 2014). Recently, Liu et al. (2019) illustrated that biolistic transformation of rice and maize results in simple insertions together with extreme genome damage. However, Ismagul et al. (2018) showed that altering the DNA/gold coating procedure by the use of nanogram amounts of vector DNA improves the frequency of single-copy transgene insertion. Nowadays, the biolistic method is widely employed for transient expression studies (Arevalo-Gallegos et al., 2020; Jordan et al., 2011; Li et al., 2018; Mookkan, 2018; Nakayama et al., 2013), as well as transgene-free genome editing using CRISPR/Cas9 (Liang et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2016).

Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, the most commonly used method for genetic transformation of dicotyledonous plants, was long considered not applicable to the cereals since as monocots they are not natural hosts of the Agrobacterium spp. The breakthrough in monocot transformation using Agrobacterium tumefaciens came from a better understanding of critical factors or parameters required for efficient cereal infection and gene transfer. Using explants with a high proportion of actively dividing cells, namely immature embryos, exploiting hypervirulent Agrobacterium strains, and optimizing co-cultivation, callus induction, and regeneration media, have been found essential for successful cereal transformation by Agrobacterium (Christou et al., 2004; Hensel, 2020; Sood et al., 2011; Vasil, 2005). Rice was the first cereal species transformed by Agrobacterium (Raineri et al., 1990), and the technique was later adapted for maize (Ishida et al., 1996), wheat (Cheng et al., 1997), and barley (Tingay et al., 1997).

In succeeding years, numerous monocots were transformed by Agrobacterium (Singh and Prasad, 2016); however, transformation efficiency still remains variable and rather limited due to the recalcitrance of many genotypes to embryogenic callus formation and subsequent regeneration of transgenic plants, which has been considered as a major hurdle. Lowe et al. (2016) described a novel transformation approach, involving overexpression of maize morphogenic transcription factor genes Baby boom (Bbm) and Wuschel2 (Wus2), which induced recovery of transgenic calli and boosted transformation frequencies from 0 to $2\%$ in the controls to 25 to $51\%$ in the presence of Wus2 and Bbm in maize, sorghum, rice, and sugarcane.

In barley, the excellent regeneration capacity of the Golden Promise cultivar has been used in many transformation events, albeit it is important to establish high-efficient regeneration systems on local barley varieties that have superior agronomic characteristics (Bo et al., 2015; Mrizova et al., 2014). Hisano and Sato (2016) identified three Transformation Amenability (TFA) alleles responsible for transformation efficiency, and illustrated that genetic substitution of TFA loci from Golden Promise to recalcitrant barley cultivars could efficiently promote the development of transformed lines. The plant defense system generates reactive oxygen species in response to the Agrobacterium infection, which may lead to the formation of a protective layer on the cell surface, browning-rendered cell death, or necrosis (Li et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2013). Accordingly, callus browning is another obstacle in developing an efficient Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and represents problems for in vitro culture and transformation regeneration. This gene was found responsible for mitigating callus browning in rice, where its upregulation consequently improves the genetic transformation efficiency, therefore could be manipulated to create recipient cereal varieties more amenable to genetic transformation.

# 3. Approaches to optimize transgene expression in cereal grains

Several molecular strategies have been applied to maximize foreign gene expression in transgenic plants. These include codon usage

optimization, use of strong promoters to boost the rate of transcription, incorporation of $5^{\prime}$ and $3^{\prime}$ untranslated sequences of mRNAs for increased mRNA stability and translational efficiency, and subcellular targeting of recombinant proteins to achieve proper folding, posttranslational modification, and stability (reviewed by Abiri et al., 2015; Fischer et al., 2004; Habibi et al., 2017; Kermode, 2012).

Gene promoters, as the most important transcriptional regulatory factor, directly determine the temporal and spatial distribution and levels of the corresponding transcripts, thus playing a vital role in manipulating the expression patterns of target genes. Generally, strong constitutive promoters such as the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter in dicots and maize ubiquitin-1 (Ubi-1) promoter in monocots have been used extensively to drive transgene expression (Fischer et al., 2009; Li et al., 2021; Ma and Wang, 2012; Xu et al., 2017), although the transcriptional nature of constitutive promoters often results in poor growth of the host plants and affects yields. Conversely, restricting the expression of a heterologous protein to a tissue or organ using specific promoters can reduce these adverse effects (Gao et al., 2019; Lau and Sun, 2009). For example, average amounts of recombinant proteins under the control of maize constitutive ubiquitin-1 promoter were 3 to 50-fold lower than those under the control of rice endosperm-specific glutelin B-1 promoter in barley grains (Eskelin et al., 2009). Hence, several endosperm-specific promoters with high promoter activity have been isolated and used in molecular farming (Table 2). In general, promoters may not have the same effectivity across different species, nonetheless, it has been shown that the use of promoters with less sequence homology to the native ones, but having similar tissue specificity, helps in avoiding homology-based gene silencing (Furtado et al., 2009). Particularly, strong heterologous gene expressions were achieved in barley grains using the oat globulin Glo1 promoter (Hensel et al., 2015; Panting et al., 2021; Vickers et al., 2006). Moreover, the rice glutelin GluB-1 promoter has been commonly used as an endosperm-specific promoter in barley (Eskelin et al., 2009; Kamenarova et al., 2007; Patel et al., 2000; Tanasienko et al., 2011). In recent years, other strategies have been also developed to further boost foreign protein production in cereal grain-based platforms. It has been reported that reduction of endogenous seed storage proteins resulted in enhancement of the heterologous protein abundance, which was accounted for by the proteome rebalancing mechanism to compensate for the deducted endogenous seed storage proteins and by the availability of deposition space (Entesari et al., 2018; Kawakatsu et al., 2010; Takaiwa, 2013). Takaiwa et al. (2021) reported that the accumulation of human transforming growth factor- $\beta 1$ (hTGF- $\beta 1$ ) in transgenic rice seeds can be improved to an extraordinary high level of $452~\mu \mathrm{g}$ per grain by the $26\mathrm{kDa}\alpha$ globulin endosperm-specific promoter along with the simultaneous suppression of major seed storage proteins, glutelins and prolamins, through RNA interference (RNAi). In barley grains, Panting et al. (2021) have recently improved the yield of the human epidermal growth factor (EGF) protein by crossing the expression line with the mutant deficient in B-hordein storage protein synthesis. They have demonstrated that allocation of resources normally used for an abundant seed storage protein production to the synthesis of a protein of interest can accelerate the production of the specific heterologous protein in barley grains. Furthermore, the expression of the recombinant human serum albumin (rHSA) has been increased in rice seeds by a knock-in strategy to integrate the rHSA gene into the locus of the rice storage protein glutelin A1 by site-specific integration via nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway. The accumulation of rHSA in the knock-in lines was significantly higher than that of the random integration lines, suggesting a compensation mechanism to regulate the homeostasis of protein contents in rice endosperm (Pang et al., 2020).

# 4. Strategies to facilitate downstream processing and recovery of grain-produced recombinant proteins

Downstream processing is typically defined as the series of unit operations required to isolate, purify, and concentrate the recombinant proteins. In transgenic plants, the most efficient method for the extraction of recombinant proteins depends on a tissue selected for their expression. In this way, the extraction and downstream processing will be facilitated once a large amount of the recombinant protein is secreted into the smaller organs such as seeds.

# 4.1. Extraction of grain-produced recombinant proteins

As a fact, proteins are susceptible to damage by a wide variety of reagents, and operating conditions, including pH and temperature changes, salt types or/and their concentration, lyophilization, and moisture levels (Azzoni et al., 2002; Ma et al., 2020b; Menkhaus, 2002). Therefore, processing conditions must be carefully modified and optimized to obtain functional proteins. Salts, detergents, and protease inhibitors are often added to cell lysis and protein extraction solutions/buffers to enhance recombinant protein extraction efficiency, but the pH of the extraction buffers is considered to be the most important factor (Menkhaus, 2002). It has been shown that as long as the yield and stability of protein of interest are not affected, a lower pH value of extracting buffer is preferable to improve the purity of recombinant protein recovered from maize kernels since high pH values boost the extraction of contaminants including native proteins, phenolic compounds and lipids (Azzoni et al., 2002, 2005). To improve protein extraction efficiencies from cereal seeds, different additives to extraction buffers have been applied such as reducing agents (e.g., $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol and dithiothreitol), protease inhibitors, salts (e.g., NaCl and $\mathrm{CaCl}_2$ ), and non-ionic detergents (e.g., Triton X100 and NP-40). In cereals, some efforts have been made to separate the desired proteins according to their physical and chemical properties (Azzoni et al., 2002;

Kusnadi et al., 1998; Ou et al., 2014). For instance, the effects of temperature, buffer pH, ionic strength, and mixing time on the extraction of transferrin from rice seed flour were examined, where the best extraction conditions were those using $25\mathrm{mM}$ Tris-HCl, $\mathsf{pH}7.5$ for $30\mathrm{min}$ at room temperature (Zhang et al., 2010). For the extraction of avidin and $\beta$ -glucuronidase from maize seeds it was $20\mathrm{mM}$ Tris buffer, $\mathsf{pH}7.9$ containing $500\mathrm{mMNaCl}$ , and $50\mathrm{mM}$ sodium phosphate buffer, $\mathsf{pH}7.5$ respectively (Kusnadi et al., 1998).

# 4.2. Purification of grain-expressed recombinant proteins

It is worth mentioning that along with the recombinant protein, grain protein extracts contain impurities like non-target endogenous proteins, mineral nutrients, vitamins, different organic compounds, polysaccharides, lipids, and small molecules. During purification, these impurities can negatively affect the stability and activity of the recombinant proteins or interfere with the isolation and purification procedure (Barros, 2012). Native endogenous polypeptides with similar properties to the target recombinant proteins and peptides could be particularly difficult to remove (Christou et al., 2004). One of the advantages of cereal grains is that they contain low amounts of phenolic compounds, which makes the downstream process easier and less expensive compared to the common molecular farming platform, i.e. tobacco leaves (Sabalza et al., 2013). Nevertheless, the main storage components in cereal grain are polysaccharides (predominantly starch), which may promote unwanted microbial growth during storage or increase the viscosity of the processed sample which negatively affects filtration procedures (Azzoni et al., 2005; Branlard and Bancel, 2007). To overcome the abovementioned challenges that affect recombinant protein purification several new strategies have emerged in the last years to improve the current techniques and to develop superior purification alternatives.

As with other protein sources, the purification procedure of grain-expressed recombinant proteins typically relies on adsorption chromatography. The use of a specific chromatographic resin is dependent on individual recombinant protein properties, such as size, charge, hydrophobicity, and affinity to specific ligands, as well as properties of endogenous proteins (Wilken and Nikolov, 2012). Various conventional ion - exchange, hydrophobic interaction, and gel filtration chromatography resins have been applied to purify recombinant proteins from cereal seeds with purities over $70\%$ (Wilken and Nikolov, 2012). As an example of the affinity-based purification scheme, aprotinin from maize seeds was purified by taking advantage of aprotinin native affinity to trypsin and subsequent removal of co-purifying endogenous corn trypsin inhibitor using its specific interaction with immobilized metal affinity chromatography (IMAC) resin (Azzoni et al., 2002). The procedure yielded aprotinin with $79\%$ purity and an overall recovery of $49\%$ . An et al. (2013) applied a heparin affinity column for purification of a heparin-binding polypeptide (basic fibroblast growth factor, bFGF) from rice seeds, obtaining recombinant protein with a purity of more than $95\%$ and final recovery of $4.5\%$ . Another common example of the use of native interactions between a target protein and its ligand is the purification of monoclonal antibodies using protein A chromatography (Hober et al., 2007) which has been successfully applied also to the purification of antibodies from cereal grains (Rademacher et al., 2008; Vamvaka et al., 2016).

To develop more universal purification platforms, special amino acid sequences with defined binding properties, referred to as a "tag", are engineered into the protein of interest. Many different tags are available, which can facilitate not only purification but also solubility and stability of recombinant proteins (Esposito and Chatterjee, 2006; Fong et al., 2010; Loughran and Walls, 2017). For instance, the thioredoxin (Trx), glutathione S-transferase (GST), maltose-binding protein (MBP), and small ubiquitin-like modifier (SUMO) (Esposito and Chatterjee, 2006) have been developed to facilitate the solubility of the protein of interest. Recently it has been reported that fusing MBP to the target recombinant

protein stabilized the protein of interest in barley grains (Holásková et al., 2018) and facilitated its affinity-purification (Mirzaee et al., 2021). Different affinity tags have been used to facilitate recombinant protein purification from plants, including cereal seeds (Fujiwara et al., 2010; Erlendsson et al., 2010; Nahampun et al., 2013; Mirzaee et al., 2021). However, the use of affinity tags for a commercial-scale purification may not be economical since the costs of affinity chromatography resins significantly contribute to overall downstream processing expenses (Dawson, 2019; Fong et al., 2010), which can account for even up to $80\%$ of the overall production costs (Schillberg et al., 2005). Nevertheless, some cost-effective methods can be designed using the resins having a high binding capacity and permitting multiple regeneration cycles, such as commercial protein A resins (Fong et al., 2010).

Over the last decade, alternative low-cost methods have been also explored for downstream processing in monocots, especially the application of novel fusion tags for non-chromatographic purification of recombinant proteins that bring technical simplicity, ease the scale-up procedure, reduce cost and improve labor and time efficiency (Bundo et al., 2019; Conley et al., 2011; Tian and Sun, 2011). In seeds, simple recovery of protein of interest can be achieved by targeting heterologous proteins to oil body surface via oleosin fusion proteins using an oleosin gene promoter. Oil bodies are free from contaminating proteins and can be easily isolated from other cellular compartments by flotation centrifugation (Bhatla et al., 2010; Conley et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2015). Therefore, this strategy has the potential of reducing the costly and time-consuming chromatographic steps (Ling, 2007; Yang et al., 2015). Through oleosin fusion technology, recombinant antifungal peptides were expressed and accumulated to significant levels in transgenic rice grains (yields up to $20~\mu \mathrm{g}\cdot \mathrm{g}^{-1}$ ) (Bundo et al., 2019). Thermally responsive elastin-like polypeptides (ELPs), composed of a repeating elastin sequence, provide another new and efficient strategy in the purification of recombinant proteins with yield increase even up to 40 to 60-fold. Such fusion proteins can be efficiently purified by the inverse transition cycling technique using the thermally activated phase-separation behavior of ELPs while maintaining the activity and stability of the target protein (Floss et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 2019). For transgenic rice seeds, Tian and Sun (2011) developed an alternative method to achieve cost-effective downstream purification using an ELP-intein coupled system. In this work, the self-cleavage ability of inteins was exploited to release the biologically active recombinant protein, while the purification could be performed only by using centrifugation and filtration with optimized temperature, pH, and chemical conditions. In addition to providing an efficient non-chromatographic purification, ELPs can substantially improve expression levels of recombinant protein when positioned at the C-terminus of target protein (Floss et al., 2010).

Another tool to produce and recover a high level of recombinant proteins is a fusion of proteins of interest with Zera, a proline-rich N-terminal domain of $\gamma$ -zein derived from the maize, that play a critical role to induce the formation of protein bodies in maize kernels (Llop-Tous et al., 2010). This system is based on the assembly of the proteins into so-called protein bodies containing a high concentration of the desired protein that is protected from proteolysis. Protein body induction through fusion to the $\gamma$ -zein domain facilitates the recovery of fused proteins through density-based separation methods (Peng et al., 2020; Torrent et al., 2009). Similarly, hydrophobin fusion tags induce protein body formation while changing the hydrophobicity of the fusion protein, thus enabling efficient purification by an aqueous two-phase separation (Linder et al., 2004; Peng et al., 2020; Reuter et al., 2016). More recently, a new class of a four-helix bundle structure of the DAMP4 carrier protein has been designed to facilitate the purification process of recombinant antimicrobial peptide pexiganan from bacteria. This strategy relies on the superior thermal and chemical stability of DAMP4 and allows chromatography-free purification of the target peptide via selective thermochemical precipitation (Zhao et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2018), which can be possibly applied as a cost-effective strategy in molecular farming.

In many cases, removal of the fusion tags is necessary as they may alter the bioactivity and the structure of target recombinant proteins. This cleavage step represents additional difficulties including the use of expensive proteases or hazardous chemicals and the possibility of nonspecific cleavage (Zhao et al., 2013). Alternative low-cost methods to substitute the proteolytic cleavage such as an application of inteins or chemical cleavage can be used in downstream processing of transgenic seeds. The self-cleavage property of inteins can be used to cleave from target protein both at its N- or C-terminus in response to pH shifts or thiol reagents (Tian and Sun, 2011). Intein-based separations can be economically competitive for large-scale recombinant protein production, especially with the use of pH- and temperature-controlled inteins and low-cost buffer systems (Nanda et al., 2020). Chemical cleavage is another alternative method that requires an acidic environment and allows a simple and effective purification of target recombinant proteins (Pane et al., 2016). It can be performed with dilute acids such as $0.1\mathrm{M}$ acetic acid or HCl when the linker amino acid sequence contains an acid cleavable site such as Asp-Pro-Pro (DPP) or Asp-Pro-Ser (DPS) sequence (Pane et al., 2016; Zhao et al., 2015). This simple and inexpensive strategy has so far been used for proteins expressed in Escherichia coli but it can also be suitable for medium/large scale production of recombinant proteins in the plant seed-based expression system.

# 5. Biopharmaceutical products in the cereal-based platform

Plant-made pharmaceuticals have a better potential for large-scale production of therapeutic recombinant proteins with lower cost, cheaper cultivation conditions, and well-understood manufacturing practices than other heterologous systems (Fischer et al., 2012; Mir-Artigues et al., 2019; Nandi et al., 2016). Development of the plant-based systems has been recently reviewed in Moon et al. (2020). Vaccine storage and administration are often major problems in developing countries (Lobato Gomez et al., 2021) and oral vaccines provide good conditions for alleviating the substantial burden of diseases such as hepatitis B, cholera, and diarrhea (Karaman et al., 2012; Nochi et al., 2007; Thanavala et al., 2005; Yuki et al., 2021). Recombinant antibodies or vaccines expressed in monocot seeds remained stable and maintained immunogenicity at room temperatures for years (Xu et al., 2012). For instance, a rice-based oral cholera vaccine (MucoRice-CTB) remained stable after more than 3 years of storage at room temperature without loss of immunogenicity (Tokuhara et al., 2010). Therefore, cereal seeds provide an ideal platform for oral vaccines with a cost-efficient solution for long-term storage compared with other non-seed tissues (Nochi et al., 2007; Shen et al., 2018). Additionally, unlike in vegetative plant tissue, targeting recombinant antigens to endogenous storage organelles, such as protein bodies in rice seeds, can protect recombinant antigens against proteolytic digestion by gastric acid in the gastric system. The reason is that protein bodies can function like a natural capsule for the oral delivery of vaccine antigens to the gastric system and induction of antigen-specific immune responses (Nochi et al., 2007; Nochi et al., 2009; Takagi et al., 2010; Tokuhara et al., 2010).

One of the major advantages of the oral vaccination system using plant-made antigens is conferring both mucosal (IgA) and systemic (IgG) immunity (Hayden et al., 2015; Nahampun et al., 2015). However, diseases prevented by mucosal immunity have been considered the most suitable candidate for treatment using plant-based edible vaccines or antibodies (Moon et al., 2020). In this context, an example of an effective oral immunogen is the hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), prepared by bioencapsulation in maize kernels, which induced a strong immune response in mice (Hayden et al., 2015). Moreover, long-term immunological systemic and mucosal responses induced by wafers made from the HBsAg expressing maize seeds and administered by oral delivery were also confirmed (Hayden et al., 2015). Additionally, the fusion of antigens to transmucosal carriers such as the cholera toxin B subunit from Vibrio cholerae and the heat-labile enterotoxin B subunit of E. coli promotes antigen delivery to the immune system and facilitates

the successful production of safe and effective edible plant-based vaccines (Baldauf et al., 2015; Chan and Daniell, 2015; Chia et al., 2011; Lakshmi et al., 2013). Such carriers were successfully produced in rice seeds, e.g., the cholera toxin B subunit (MucoRice-CTB) with the protein yield of $0.15\%$ of seed weight or the variable domain of a rotavirus-specific llama heavy-chain antibody fragment (MucoRice-ARP1) with the protein yield of $11.9\%$ of the total protein (Moon et al., 2020; Nochi et al., 2007; Tokuhara et al., 2013).

Pharmaceutical proteins recently produced in seeds of cereals are e.g., influenza virus H3N2 nucleoprotein in maize (Nahampun et al., 2015), anti-HIV antibody (Hensel et al., 2015), and human antimicrobial peptide cathelicidin in barley (Holaskova et al., 2018; Mirzaee et al., 2021), a purification-free oral cholera vaccine (MucoRice-CTB; Kashima et al., 2016) and oral allergy vaccine in rice (Takaiwa et al., 2019), and an oral TM-1 vaccine against chronic respiratory disease in wheat (Khan et al., 2021). Some of the plant-produced pharmaceutical proteins have passed pre-clinical or clinical testing stages and are close to commercialization. Promising examples of diagnostic and therapeutic proteins production in monocots that have advanced to phase I clinical trials include a maize-produced edible vaccine against enterotoxigenic $E.$ coli and the swine transmissible gastroenteritis (Chikwamba et al., 2002) conducted by ProdiGene (College Station, TX, USA) and the oral rice-based cholera vaccine MucoRice-CTB (Yuki et al., 2021) developed by the Institute of Medical Science, the University of Tokyo. The human lactoferrin and lysozyme produced by Ventria Bioscience (Junction City, CO, USA) in rice, and ISOkine and DERMOkine products containing human growth factors produced in barley by ORF Genetics (Kopavogur, Iceland) advanced to the market.

# 6. Current bottlenecks

Production of plant-made products from cereals brings up several concerns for public acceptance as they are the main food and feed crops. It is essential to note that certain cereals such as rice, barley, wheat, and oat (but not maize or rye) are autogamous species (self-pollinating) with limited seed dispersal (Rehbinder et al., 2009). Moreover, plants that are used for the production of biopharmaceuticals are only released into the environment if they are approved safe. Self-pollinating cereals such as wheat and barley have been declared GRAS (generally recognized as safe) by the European regulatory agency EFSA (Hensel, 2011) and they offer easy handling and superb scalability.

There are currently several therapeutic recombinant proteins in monocots in advanced stages of translational research (ClinicalTrials.gov, 2021; Kashima et al., 2016; Mirzaee et al., 2021; Yuki et al., 2021) or production (Grabowski et al., 2014). Nevertheless, major limitations still exist for plant-based oral vaccines, for example, lower expression levels in plant systems, low immunogenicity against pathogens, and

lower biomass (Egelkrout et al., 2012; Fahad et al., 2015). However, the major advantage of seed-based expression system in monocots could be the elimination of the purification process and specialized storage and transport conditions, which can lead to significant cost reduction in the case of oral vaccines, where the foods derived from these cereal grains become a vaccine itself (Kurup and Thomas, 2020; Yao et al., 2015). Environmental containment concerns, regulatory approval concerns, and non-mammalian glycosylation are the other obstacles that limit the extensive commercialization of plants as a biofactory (Burnett and Burnett, 2020; Ramessar et al., 2008b; Xu et al., 2011). These factors may even restrict the choice of the type of industrial products and recombinant medicines that can be manufactured in plants. An important potential problem with protein expression in seed crops, especially cross-pollinating cereals such as maize and rye, is the concern about the spread of foreign genes via pollen or seed dispersal (Table 3).

There are many generally applicable strategies to deal with these concerns, such as the use of self-pollinating cereals (e.g., barley, rice, and wheat), male-sterile varieties, or terminator technology to prevent hybrid seed germination (Al-Ahmad et al., 2004; Murphy et al., 2007; Ramessar et al., 2008b). The other strategies are the use of inducible promoters to express recombinant products just before or after harvest and the design of inactive recombinant fusion proteins which require subsequent processing to generate the active form of the target protein (Burnett and Burnett, 2020; Daniell et al., 2001; Obembe et al., 2011; Murphy et al., 2007). Furthermore, planting of transgenic plants at different times to local food crops and with enough spatial isolation from other plants (for example at least $1.6\mathrm{km}$ for maize), and growing plants under controlled environmental conditions or in a hydroponic or cell suspension culture systems can also be applied (Obembe et al., 2011; Ramessar et al., 2008b; USDA, 2003).

As promising as this technology may be, drawbacks of plant-based expression systems for the business potential of plant-derived biopharmaceuticals are related to the large investments and the long periods for regulatory approval. Therefore, it may be more advantageous to focus on non-therapeutic products, such as diagnostic, technical, and cosmetic products, to enable faster-gained approval for general human use due to the less stringent regulatory requirements.

Another important consideration before embarking on the production of recombinant products is the issue of post-translational protein modifications (correct folding, glycosylation, and disulfide bridge formation) in plant-based production platforms, which are necessary to produce active, functional, and safe recombinant therapeutics (Barolo et al., 2020; Margolin et al., 2018; Margolin et al., 2020). These differences can influence the physicochemical and biological properties of therapeutic proteins such as the solubility, stability, folding, distribution, serum half-life, activity, cell surface receptor function, and immunogenic potential (Hermeling et al., 2004). To overcome this problem, glycoengineering of plant expression systems to produce human-compatible glycan structures has been used as one of the strategies (Fischer et al., 2018; Ghosh, 2020). Moreover, the GlycoDelete technology has been applied in plant seeds for the efficient production of therapeutic proteins without plant-specific $N$ -glycan modifications (Piron et al., 2015). Some reports on overcoming these limitations include knock-out/knock-down of the plant's endogenous glycosyltransferases, $\alpha 1,3$ -fucosyltransferases and $\beta 1,2$ -xylosyltransferase (Gomord et al., 2010; Twyman et al., 2003) and subsequently expressing mammalian glycosyltransferases and supporting proteins in the transgenic plant (Bakker et al., 2001; Strasser et al., 2014). Another strategy, which was reported by He et al. (2012) for maize seeds, is the combined use of mRNA regulatory $(5^{\prime}$ - and $3^{\prime}$ -UTR) and signal-peptide coding sequences to endoplasmic reticulum protein bodies for producing $\alpha$ -L-iduronidase for treatment of a human lysosomal storage disease. Some advantages of using this strategy are the synthesis of desired recombinant products containing predominantly high-mannose $N$ -linked glycans and the production of a recombinant product that is directly amenable to downstream modifications. Recently it has been shown that fusing a small domain, containing $N$ -glycosylation sites, to the protein of interest is an effective approach for improving the expression levels of recombinant products in plants (Kang et al., 2018). However foreign proteins, which do not require such modifications, are better candidates for the in planta production. Recombinant antibody fragments such as single-chain variable fragment (scFv), disulfide-stabilized Fv fragments (dsFv's), and antigen-binding fragments (Fabs) do not need glycosylation and can be easily expressed in plant systems (Tripathi and Shrivastava, 2019). The recombinant antibody 2G12 which has a broad HIV-1-neutralizing activity, retained the full functionality when produced in maize endosperm (Rademacher et al., 2008). It was also found that most of the glycans were trimmed extensively, indicating that there is a significant endoglycanase activity in maize endosperm. Therefore, using maize plants as a platform to produce recombinant antibodies can minimize the addition of plant-specific glycans and reduce any immunoreactive potential from the recombinant proteins. Besides, the fusion of the desired protein to the oil-body protein oleosin can be applied to avoid posttranslational modifications (e.g., glycosylation) in the endoplasmic reticulum lumen (Alireza and Nader, 2015; Castilho and Steinkellner, 2012). The market placement of some plant-based cosmetic/diagnostic products shows the plants' capacity as a competitive production platform for the manufacturing of high-end products in the near future.

# 7. Future perspectives

The post-translational modifications play a key role in proteins' structure and function, as an integral part of the biopharmaceutical proteins. Bioinformatics methods and in silico prediction tools together with molecular modeling and simulation have recently emerged to gain deeper molecular insights into the key function of post-translationally modified proteins (Audagnotto and Dal Peraro, 2017). Moreover, to obtain higher quality and purity standards of biopharmaceutical and diagnostic products with more efficiency and predictably lower costs, the post-translational modification in plants is expected to be improved by using new gene-editing systems. For example, the recent CRISPR/Cas9 prime editing methods that are being developed (Anzalone et al.,

2019; Xu et al., 2020; Zong et al., 2017), may be well assisted with mass spectrometry-based proteomic approaches for screening and characterizing post-translational modifications (Virág et al., 2020).

A major barrier that hinders the widespread acceptance of plant-derived products is the lack of regulatory approval (Shanmugaraj et al., 2020), mainly due to the intense and very costly examination of the newly prepared genetically modified plant by regulatory authorities as well as the public (Park and Wi, 2016). However, the regulatory burden for plant-derived proteins used in industry, cosmetics, or veterinary medicine is much lower than that for human therapeutical proteins (Schillberg et al., 2019). Such plant-made recombinant protein products can facilitate further development and economic sustainability of the technology. Increased knowledge of the current plant-based production systems and understanding of their limitations will enable manufacturers and developers to make faster and better-informed decisions on the ideal expression systems available to produce a particular therapeutic recombinant protein in plants.

Nowadays, the world needs to provide an allocation framework for fair and equitable access of all populations to pharmaceuticals, especially in developing and poor countries. One potential solution is using transgenic cereal plants, where stability in an agriculturally challenging area, ease of commercial-scale production, and long-term storage capacity make them an ideal platform to produce the therapeutic recombinant protein using local infrastructure (Table 3). At present, plant-derived non-pharmaceutical proteins that are on the market outnumber the therapeutic products, where the only commercialized in planta produced pharmaceutical is the recombinant glucocerebrosidase (taliglucerase alfa, also called Elelyso) for the treatment of Gaucher disease obtained from transgenic carrot cells (Grabowski et al., 2014), which was developed by Protalix Biotherapeutics (Carmiel, Israel) and is currently marketed by Pfizer. The HIV-neutralizing monoclonal antibody P2G12 produced in tobacco plants, which was developed in the scope of the EU Pharma-Planta project (2004–2011) and successfully tested in a single clinical trial in the UK (Ma et al., 2015), presents a significant step in the commercial development of plant-derived pharmaceutical proteins. The antibody is now in phase I clinical trials for intravenous infusion at the St. George's, University of London, UK (ClinicalTrials.gov, 2021). Monoclonal antibody (ZMapp) produced in tobacco after the 2014 outbreak of Ebola virus disease also advanced to clinical trials (Mulangu et al., 2019). Other trials have been conducted with plant-based vaccines against malaria (Chichester et al., 2018), influenza (Pillet et al., 2019), and recently also for COVID-19 vaccine candidates (Maharjan and Choe, 2021; Ward et al., 2021).

# 8. Concluding remarks

This review presents the most recent advances and strategies for the production of recombinant products in transgenic cereals. Cereal grains are one of the most favorable bioreactors for plant molecular farming, in which heterologous proteins can be efficiently expressed and stably stored in the largest grain storage compartment, endosperm. This study discusses the feasibility, simplicity, and efficiency of grain-based recombinant protein synthesis machinery, which reveals the importance of developing novel approaches to establish grain-based expression systems, highlights various aspects involved in recombinant protein accumulation in grain and discusses current barriers and perspectives for the biosynthesis of therapeutic molecules using different cereal plant platforms.

Nowadays, the Agrobacterium-mediated transformation is commonly used for the genetic transformation of cereals, where different strategies have been successfully applied to overcome the transformation bottlenecks. To maximize foreign gene expression in transgenic cereal plants, several endosperm-specific promoters with high promoter activity have been incorporated in cereal grain-based molecular farming. Most recently, the expression of foreign proteins in cereal seeds was further boosted by the reduction of endogenous seed storage proteins through

proteome rebalancing mechanism. As such, particular attention needs to be paid to the development of efficient bioprocessing strategies for large-scale and low-cost recombinant protein production, especially suitable protein extraction methods and purification approaches.

In recent years, low-cost downstream processing using novel fusion tags/proteins allowing simple, scalable, and cost-effective production of biopharmaceutical proteins in cereal plants, have been developed. Currently, much effort is being devoted to overcoming the limitations of low yields and recovery of the expressed proteins and bio-safety issues, as the major obstacles in the commercialization of cereal-based recombinant products. At present, there are several cereal-produced nonpharmaceutical products on the market as cell culture supplements, research reagents, and cosmetic ingredients that were able to reach the market, which demonstrate that a grain-based expression system can potentially become economically successful as a competitive platform for the manufacturing of high-quality, biologically active recombinant therapeutics to reinforce the traditional pharmaceutical industry for improving human welfare.
