# Neofunctionalization of an OMT cluster dominates polymethoxyflavone biosynthesis associated with the domestication of citrus

Polymethoxyflavones (PMFs) are a class of abundant specialized metabolites with remarkable anticancer properties in citrus. Multiple methoxy groups in PMFs are derived from methylation modification catalyzed by a series of hydroxylases and $O$ -methyltransferases (OMTs). However, the specific $OMTs$ that catalyze the systematic $O$ -methylation of hydroxyflavones remain largely unknown. Here, we report that PMFs are highly accumulated in wild mandarins and mandarin-derived accessions, while undetectable in early-diverging citrus species and related species. Our results demonstrated that three homologous genes, CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5, are crucial for PMF biosynthesis in citrus, and their encoded methyltransferases exhibit multisite $O$ -methylation activities for hydroxyflavones, producing seven PMFs in vitro and in vivo. Comparative genomic and syntenic analyses indicated that the tandem CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 may be duplicated from CreOMT6 and contributes to the genetic basis of PMF biosynthesis in the mandarin group through neofunctionalization. We also demonstrated that N17 in CreOMT4 is an essential amino acid residue for C3-, C5-, C6-, and $\mathrm{C3^{\prime} - O}$ -methylation activity and provided a rationale for the functional deficiency of OMT6 to produce PMFs in early-diverging citrus and some domesticated citrus species. A 1,041-bp deletion in the CreOMT4 promoter, which is found in most modern cultivated mandarins, has reduced the PMF content relative to that in wild and early-admixture mandarins. This study provides a framework for reconstructing PMF biosynthetic pathways, which may facilitate the breeding of citrus fruits with enhanced health benefits.

Polymethoxyflavones (PMFs) have been demonstrated to possess certain anticancer properties against gastric (1), skin (2, 3), breast (4), lung (5), and some other cancers (6, 7). They are flavones with multiple methoxy $(\mathrm{OCH}_3)$ groups attached to benzopyrone skeletons. It has been reported that the $O$ -methylated moieties are crucial to the stereo-structures and bioactivities of PMFs (8, 9). Multiple $O$ -methylated PMFs can more easily target and penetrate cancer cells than other flavonoids (10-12) and exhibit broad anti-inflammatory activities (13) to protect the cardiovascular system (14) and prevent metabolic disorders (15). During the COVID-19 pandemic, "Chenpi" (Citri reticulata pericarpium) is one of the most frequently used components of traditional Chinese medicine to alleviate associated symptoms in China (16). PMFs are the main bioactive components responsible for the anti-inflammatory effects of Chenpi (17-19). PMFs extracted from Citrus depressa were identified as potential inhibitors targeting multiple variants of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, particularly 5-demethylnobiletin (20). Liposoluble PMFs in citrus are mainly distributed in fruit peels, leaves, and flowers (12, 21), where they provide a basic barrier against pathogens (22) and ultraviolet (UV) radiation (23).

Flavonoid content varies widely among different plant species. Multiple $O$ -methylated flavonoids have been identified in plants belonging to the Asteraceae, Lamiaceae, Fabaceae, and Rutaceae families (8, 24). Highly $O$ -methylated PMFs are the most abundant in citrus (12) and have been extracted from different species in citrus, particularly from the peel of mandarin fruits (21, 25). Citrus is a member of Aurantioidae, a subfamily of the Rutaceae family, and comprises 33 genera and 210 species (26-29). Genomic analysis has facilitated the categorization of citrus into three distinct groups: citrus-related genera, early-diverging citrus, and domesticated citrus (29). Domesticated citrus can be further divided into three groups, including the mandarin group, the pummelo-citron group, and a group consisting of Fortunella hindsii, C. polyandra, C. glauca, and C. australasica (29). # Significance

Polymethoxyflavones (PMFs) are natural bioactive compounds with health-promoting properties.

However, the genetic basis for PMF biosynthesis remains unknown, making it challenging to breed important crops for higher PMF content. Here, we found that three tandemly duplicated OMTs are major genetic determinants of natural variations of PMF content in citrus and traced the initial emergence of these OMTs to wild mandarins, which appears to be the origin of the specialized PMF pathway in citrus. A 1,041-bp deletion from the CreOMT4 promoter is associated with the decrease in PMF levels in domesticated mandarins. These findings provide significant insights into the evolution of PMF biosynthesis and may help improve the production of citrus crops with anticancer properties by breeding or metabolic engineering.

However, remarkable interspecific introgression signatures have contributed to the diversification of cultivated mandarins and mandarin hybrids (30). Indeed, many genes controlling flavonoid biosynthesis have been subjected to selection during domestication. For example, the Ruby2-Ruby1 cluster has been subfunctionalized for the regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis (31). Recently, we analyzed the PMF profiles of 116 types of citrus germplasm (21) and found differences in PMF content in both fruit flavedo and leaves among different citrus species. Given the remarkable anticancer properties of PMFs and their diverse structures and species-specific accumulation patterns, it is essential to investigate the genetic basis of the PMF metabolic pathway.

A range of hydroxylation, glycosylation, and methylation reactions contribute to flavonoid diversity (24, 32). Flavonoid $O$ -methyltransferases (OMTs), flavonoid hydroxylases, and flavone $O$ -demethylases influence the accumulation of PMFs (8, 33, 34). Hydroxylation is the most common modification of flavonoids. Indeed, $78\%$ of flavonoids are hydroxylated (34). Moreover, hydroxylation often takes precedence over methylation (8). OMTs play a critical role in catalyzing the terminal modification of hydroxylated flavones and often contribute to specialized metabolite biosynthesis in plants. OMTs typically exhibit high substrate specificity, such as in peppermint (Menthax piperita) (35) and sweet basil (Ocimum basilicum) (36). Generally, tandemly duplicated gene clusters (TDGCs) contain homologous genes, while plant biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) contain two or more nonhomologous genes encoding enzymes involved in the same biosynthetic pathway (37, 38). A TDGC comprising five $OMT$ genes contributes to wogonin biosynthesis in Chinese skullcap (Scutellaria baicalensis) (39). Nine sequenced citrus genomes were predicted to contain 38 to 111 $OMT$ genes. Specifically, "Mangshan" mandarin (C. reticulata Mangshan), "Clementine" mandarin (C. reticulata Clementine), and sweet orange appear to possess the most $OMT$ genes, ranging from 105 to 111. However, pummelo, citron, ichang papeda, kumquat, and Chinese box orange have fewer $OMT$ genes, ranging from 38 to 79 (21). CdOMT5 from C. depressa has low $O$ -methylation activity for the C3, C5, C6, and C7 hydroxyl groups of monohydroxy flavones in vitro (40). CrOMT1 from "Ougan" mandarin can catalyze highly efficient $O$ -methylation of tricin (23), which is present in citrus in trace amounts (41). An OMT from mandarin can effectively $O$ -methylate the $\mathrm{C}3^{\prime}$ OH group (42), and CitOMT2 from two nobiletin-rich mandarins can $O$ -methylate 7,8-dihydroxyflavone at C8-OH with strict substrate specificity in vitro (43). Although it has been well documented that citrus species accumulate diverse PMFs containing three to seven methoxy groups, the key OMTs and the genetic mechanisms governing PMF biosynthesis in citrus have not been systematically investigated.

Here, we present a comprehensive profile of PMFs from both wild and cultivated citrus, as well as from two citrus hybrid populations. Subsequently, we employed forward and reverse genetics to successfully identify a cluster of three tandemly duplicated genes that encode OMTs and are involved in PMF biosynthesis. We demonstrate that the encoded OMT proteins are responsible for $O$ -methylating the multiple hydroxyl groups of hydroxyflavones and contribute to PMF biosynthesis. Through genomic analysis, we propose a possible evolutionary trajectory of PMF biosynthesis based on the expansion of $OMT$ genes and subsequent neofunctionalization occurring during citrus domestication. An analysis of the OMTTDGCs allowed the identification of a relationship between a promoter variation in CreOMT4 and $O$ -methyltransferase activity, which may account for the distinct PMF content in wild and cultivated mandarins.

# Results

Natural Variations of PMF Content in Citrus. We detected a broad distribution of PMF levels in 147 citrus accessions that included Citrus-related genera, early-diverging citrus, and domesticated citrus (Fig. 1A and SI Appendix, Fig. S1 and Dataset S1). PMFs were not detectable in Citrus-related species including Chinese box orange (Atalantia buxfoliata), and in early-diverging citrus species, such as trifoliata orange (Poncirus trifoliata), "mangshanyegan" (C. mangshanensis), and ichang papeda (C. ichangensis). Similarly, no PMFs were detected in species from the domesticated citrus, including kumquat (Fortunella hindsii), honghe papeda (C. hongheensis), citron (C. medica), and pummelo (C. grandis, SI Appendix, Fig. S1A and Dataset S1). Notably, mandarins (C. reticulata) showed remarkably high levels of 11 PMFs (Fig. 1B and SI Appendix, Figs. S1B and S2 and Dataset S1). Compared with that of modern cultivated mandarins, the fruit flavedo of wild and early-admixture mandarins accumulated significantly higher levels of total PMFs and four main PMFs, including nobiletin (NOB), tangeretin (TAN), sinensetin (SIN), and 5-demethylnobiletin (5-DNOB) (Fig. 1B). Moreover, accessions derived from mandarins, such as sweet oranges, sour oranges, and grapefruits, accumulated varying levels of PMFs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 and Dataset S1).

Identification of an OMT Cluster Responsible for PMF Biosynthesis. To understand the genetics underlying PMF biosynthesis in citrus, we constructed an $\mathrm{F}_1$ population by crossing C. reticulata "Red tangerine" (RT), which accumulates PMFs, with "Trifoliata orange" (TO), which does not accumulate detectable levels of PMFs. The $\mathrm{F}_1$ individuals showed variable levels of PMF accumulation in both fruit flavedo and leaves (Fig. 2A and SI Appendix, Fig. S4A). Tissues from individuals with high and low accumulation levels of PMFs were pooled, respectively. The two distinct pools were then compared through bulked segregant analysis (BSA, SI Appendix, Table S1). The results demonstrated prominent peaks on scaffolds 2 and 4, which are consistent with two loci controlling the accumulation of PMFs (Fig. 2B). To further determine whether these peaks indicate major loci that promote the accumulation of PMFs, we used a distinct segregating population derived from a cross between C. reticulata Clementine (CLM) with low levels of PMFs and Precocious trifoliata orange" (PTO) with undetectable levels of PMFs (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). Similarly, this population was analyzed through BSA, and the results indicated three loci controlling the accumulation of PMFs (Fig. 2B). The BSA results of the two populations were overlapped, resulting in the definition of a 360-kb candidate region on scaffold 4 (Fig. 2B) that contains 18 predicted genes (SI Appendix, Table S2). Five highly homologous $O$ -methyltransferase genes, including CreOMT3, CreOMT4, CreOMT5, CreOMT7, and CreOMT8, were identified in this candidate region (Fig. 2C). CreOMT7 and CreOMT8 appear to be nonfunctional pseudogenes as they are lack of the conserved PF08100 domain, a hallmark of functional plant $O$ -methyltransferases (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). Among the remaining three loci, the locus on Scaffold 2 contains CreOMT9, and the other two loci do not have genes that encode OMTs (Fig. 2C). Therefore, we selected the remaining OMTs as candidate genes responsible for the disparity in PMF content in citrus.

We further performed an integrated metabolome and transcriptome analysis to identify genes that may contribute to the accumulation of PMFs. HPLC analysis results indicated that PMFs were accumulated at high levels in the flavedo of RT (RT-F), but not detectable in the juice sacs of RT (RT-J) and the flavedo of "Huanong Red" pummelo (HR-F) and TO (TO-F, Fig. 2D). To further explore the role of the OMT cluster in PMF biosynthesis, we individually and transiently overexpressed CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 in the flavedo of "Guoqing No. 1" satsuma mandarin (Fig. 3D). We quantified the abundance of nine PMFs in OMT-overexpressing flavedo and compared them with the empty vector control. The data indicated that OMT overexpression increased the levels of ISIN, SIN, NOB, HPMF, 8-OH-HXMF, and TAN in the flavedo (Fig. 3E). The total PMF content in the flavedo of CreOMT3-OE, CreOMT4-OE, and CreOMT5-OE fruits was averagely increased by $11.6\%$ , $16.9\%$ , and $14.0\%$ , respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S17A). CreOMT3-OE flavedo had significantly higher $(P < 0.05)$ levels of TMF2 and 5DNOB, while CreOMT4-OE and CreOMT5-OE flavedo showed significantly lower levels of 5DNOB (Fig. 3E). In vitro enzymatic assays demonstrated that CreOMT4 and CreOMT5 could continuously convert 5DNOB into NOB (SI Appendix, Figs. S8J and S13E). These data provide a rationale for the reduced levels of 5DNOB in the CreOMT4-OE and CreOMT5-OE flavedo.

Furthermore, we used RNA interference (RNAi) to downregulate the expression of CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 in Ota Ponkan mandarin, as its flavedo is rich in nine PMFs. The flavedo of the RNAi fruit showed decreases in the expression levels of CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5, and also the levels of ISIN, NOB, TMF2, TAN, and GAD (Fig. 3 F and G). The total PMF content decreased by an average of $12.9\%$ , $14.0\%$ , and $18.6\%$ in the CreOMT3-RNAi, CreOMT4-RNAi, and CreOMT5-RNAi fruits, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S17B). The levels of

SIN and TMF1 significantly decreased in the flavedo of CreOMT3-RNAi and CreOMT4-RNAi fruits, while the level of 5DNOB showed considerable decreases in CreOMT4-RNAi and CreOMT5-RNAi fruits (Fig. 3G).

Based on the enzymatic activities and substrate regioselectivities of CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5, as well as the PMF profiles observed in citrus germplasm and tissues, we propose a citrus-specific PMF biosynthetic pathway (SI Appendix, Fig. S18). CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 were found to be complementary in the $O$ -methylation of hydroxyflavones and help produce seven citrus-specific PMFs.

Amino Acid Residues Crucial for O-Methylation Activity in CreOMT4 and CreOMT6. Although the amino acid sequences of CreOMT4 and CreOMT6 shared $94.62\%$ identity (SI Appendix, Table S4), their activities were significantly different. We conducted an amino acid sequence alignment for all OMT proteins that we purified (SI Appendix, Fig. S23). The 17th amino acid residue is Asn (N) in CreOMT4 but Asp (D) in CreOMT6. The 168th amino acid residue is Thr (T) in CreOMT4 but Ala (A) in CreOMT6. Considering the distinct multisite O-methylation activities of CreOMT4 and CreOMT6, we used site-directed mutagenesis to test whether these residues influence the activities of these enzymes by exchanging them in CreOMT4 and CreOMT6, and then compared their activities with those of wild-type CreOMT4 and CreOMT6 in O-methylation assays (Fig. 5C). Interestingly, CreOMT4-N17D could not O-methylate most sites except for C7 of LYS (Fig. 5C and SI Appendix, Fig. S24). In contrast, CreOMT6-D17N could O-methylate the C3, C5, C6, and $\mathrm{C3^{\prime}}$ sites of hydroxyflavones. The 5-O-methylation activity of CreOMT4-T168A was significantly greater than that of wildtype CreOMT4. However, its capacity to O-methylate the C3 site showed a moderate decrease (Fig. 4C and SI Appendix, Fig. S24F). Molecular docking analysis using the predicted protein structure of CreOMT4 indicated that the substrates GAD and SAM bind near the active site (SI Appendix, Fig. S25). Additionally, Asn (N17) and Thr (T168) were located outside the active site of CreOMT4.

Influence of a MITE-Containing 1,041-bp Fragment in the OMT4 Promoter on PMF Accumulation. The 2-kb promoter region and full-length coding sequences (CDSs) were identified for each of the four OMT genes in citrus from Group I to IV (Fig. 5B). The promoter sequences of OMT3, OMT4, and OMT5 were highly conserved (SI Appendix, Fig. S26A). Miniature inverted-repeat transposable elements (MITEs) are major drivers of gene cluster formation and metabolic diversification in eudicots (44, 45). More than 20 MITEs were detected in the promoters of OMT3 to OMT6 in citrus (Dataset S7). In the promoters of OMT3 to OMT6 from Group IV, 11 MITEs were inserted in similar positions and had similar sequences (Fig. 5B).

We found a 1,041-bp fragment containing two MITEs, which was located 654 bp upstream of the translational start codon in the OMT4 gene in Mangshan mandarin and Ota Ponkan mandarin, but not in the cultivated mandarins Clementine (Fig. 5B). Transient dual-LUC assay was performed to assess the transcriptional activity of the 1,041-bp fragment and the OMT4 promoters from two sources: the modern cultivated Clementine mandarin with absence of the 1,041-bp fragment (CreOMT4-Pro) and Mangshan mandarin with presence of the 1,041-bp fragment (CrmOMT4-P1). Additionally, CrmOMT4-P1 was divided into three segments to test whether the two MITEs influence the promoter activity. After infiltrating Nicotiana benthamiana leaves with Agrobacterium strains harboring different plasmids, we found that CrmOMT4-P1 produced three times higher levels of LUC activity than CreOMT4-Pro (Fig. 5D), confirming that the 1,041-bp fragment induces an increase in the expression of CreOMT4. CrmOMT4-P2 and CrmOMT4-P3 contained two and one MITE,

respectively, and produced nearly twice as much LUC activity as CreOMT4-Pro. These data indicated that the region containing the two MITEs (i.e., 899 to 1,052 bp upstream of the translational start codon in the CrmOMT4 promoter) can induce increases in the expression of CreOMT4.

To determine the effect of the 1,041-bp DNA fragment on PMF content, we genotyped 72 mandarins. Three recognized wild mandarin species, including "Tachibana" tangerine (28), Mangshan mandarin (30), and "Dakeng" wild tangerine, all showed high levels of PMF accumulation and contained only a long OMT4 promoter with the 1,041-bp fragment (i.e., they are homozygous for the ancestral OMT4 allele). To test whether the 1,041-bp fragment is present in the OMT4 promoter of mandarins, a specific primer was designed for PCR-based genotyping to demonstrate its presence in 19 wild mandarins and 29 early-admixture mandarins, which have high PMF contents (SI Appendix, Fig. S27). Conversely, the fragment was deleted in modern cultivated mandarins with low PMF contents. These results further confirmed that the 1,041-bp fragment containing two MITEs has a significant impact on PMF metabolism. The loss of this fragment in the OMT4 promoter of modern cultivated mandarins may account for their lower OMT4 gene expression and PMF content.

# Discussion

In this study, two segregating populations derived from citrus parents with extremely different levels of PMFs were used to identify genetic loci essential for PMF biosynthesis, resulting in the identification of major genes involved in PMF biosynthesis. O-methylation occurs at up to seven sites during the accumulation of PMFs, which is consistent with multiple OMT genes contributing to PMF biosynthesis in citrus. Indeed, the expression of CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 is strongly correlated with the accumulation of six PMF monomers and the total PMFs in six citrus species (SI Appendix, Table S3). CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 possess robust O-methylation activities at multiple sites on hydroxyflavones, including C3, C5, C6, C7, $C3^{\prime}$ , and $C4^{\prime}$ . Citrus PMFs are derived from the flavone branch of the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway, with flavone synthase (FNS) converting flavonoid biosynthesis to flavone biosynthesis (46). Hydroxylation and subsequent methylation of the flavone skeleton lead to a variety of distinct PMFs. CitFNSII-1 and CitFNSII-2 were recently identified and shown to stimulate the increase in total PMF content in citrus (47). CrOMT1 (Ciclev10026344m.g) is responsible for catalyzing high-rate C6 and C8 O-methylation (23), while CitOMT2 (Ciclev10015724m.g) only performs limited O-methylation of C8 (43). The CsCCoAOMT1 enzyme catalyzes the conversion of luteolin and baicalein to chrysoeriol and several 6- and 7-O-methylated derivatives (48). Although CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 showed limited or negligible catalytic activities toward flavones with multiple hydroxyl groups such as API and DIO, they can work collaboratively to effectively O-methylate other sites in different hydroxyflavone substrates, contributing to the production of diverse PMFs, especially fully O-methylated flavones.

We demonstrate that the remarkable natural variations of PMF content in citrus can be mainly attributed to OMTs, the terminal modifying enzymes of hydroxyflavones. These OMT enzymes are responsible for the final steps of PMF biosynthesis. The natural variations of flavones in rice showed similar results, which were mainly attributed to two glycosyltransferases (49). Therefore, it is possible that the enzymes involved in the final steps of $O$ -methylation contribute more to the natural variations of PMF abundance than those involved in the earlier stages of the pathway. Moreover, the

transcription levels of CrOMT1 and CsCCoAOMT1 were correlated with two PMF monomers (SI Appendix, Table S3), and these proteins could be synthesized from coding sequences derived from different types of citrus germplasm (https://citrus.hzau.edu.cn). These facts indicate that CrOMT1 and CsCCoAOMT1 may not be responsible for the variation of PMFs among different citrus species. Overall, the data strongly suggest the important role of the OMT cluster in PMF biosynthesis.

Based on the catalytic properties that we observed for each enzyme on diverse flavone substrates, we proposed a PMF $O$ -methylation pathway (SI Appendix, Fig. S18), which incorporates the regioselectivities of major OMT enzymes. Although several key enzymes that catalyze the hydroxylation of flavones remain unknown, our proposed metabolic pathway can generally outline the biosynthesis of seven PMF monomers. Hydroxylated PMFs, including 5DNOB, $3^{\prime}$ -DNOB, GAD, and SAL, serve as intermediate PMFs and are converted by CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 into three primary PMFs, namely NOB, TAN, and $5,7,8,4^{\prime}$ -tetramethyl flavone (TMF1, Fig. 1B). Hopefully, the proposed PMF biosynthetic pathway will facilitate the development of tools for cost-effective production of PMFs in

microbes. Although the OMT cluster encodes versatile $O$ -methyltransferases involved in PMF biosynthesis, PMF biosynthesis is a complex pathway that involves multiple hydroxylases and $O$ -demethylases, all of which may also be regulated by a series of transcription factors.

TDGCs and BGCs are associated with some specialized metabolic pathways and have been successively identified using genome-guided and metabolite-based GWAS strategies in several plants (39, 50). Neofunctionalization of gene clusters occurs when duplicated genes acquire novel functions and contributes to evolutionary novelties (51). For instance, the tandem duplication and neofunctionalization of CHS-2 and FNSII-2, as well as the tandem duplication and subfunctionalization of SbPFOMT5, both play important roles in the evolution of $4^{\prime}$ -deoxyflavone biosynthesis in Scutellaria (39). The CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 present in both wild and early-admixture mandarins are versatile and can $O$ -methylate hydroxyflavones, producing a wide range of PMFs. In contrast to OMT6 from both Groups I and II, CreOMT4 and CreOMT5 possess C3-O-methylation activity, which demonstrates typical neofunctionalization in the mandarin-derived group (Fig. 6A-D). Compared with wild mandarins, modern cultivated mandarins showed much lower expression levels of CreOMT3, CreOMT4, and CreOMT5 and total PMF content (Fig. 5A), indicating that domestication probably has reduced the $O$ -methylation activity encoded by this OMT cluster (Fig. 6 D and E). A molecular marker for the 1,041-bp fragment (SI Appendix, Fig. S27) will be useful for selecting citrus germplasm with high accumulation levels of PMFs. Our previous research has shown that MITEs can affect the expression of genes involved in important processes, such as asexual reproduction (27), and influence plant traits and metabolic pathways that affect fruit color (53). Here, the 1,041-bp fragment containing two MITEs in the CreOMT4 promoter was confirmed to contribute to the expression of CreOMT4. However, the specific cis-elements in this MITE-containing fragment that induce increases in the transcription of CreOMT4 remain unclear. Additionally, it remains to be ascertained whether the 1,041-bp MITE-containing fragment in the promoter region of CreOMT4 affects the transcription of CreOMT3 and CreOMT5.

In summary, we demonstrate that the OMT cluster and OMT6 encode a set of enzymes that contribute to PMF biosynthesis and

describe the evolutionary history of the OMT cluster and OMT6 in citrus. We found that the OMT cluster may have been derived from a tandem duplication of CreOMT6, followed by mutations that changed the D17 residues into N17 in OMTs, resulting in the accumulation of diverse PMFs. During domestication, PMF biosynthesis gradually became less robust in citrus due to natural and artificial selection, resulting in gradual attenuation or loss of PMFs from modern cultivated mandarins, probably due to the loss of a 1,041-bp fragment in the OMT4 promoter. Our results also show that the major OMTTDGC is responsible for $O$ -methylation of hydroxyflavones at multiple sites, providing a genetic framework for PMF-based biofortification programs to benefit human health.
