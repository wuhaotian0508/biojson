# Arabidopsis Tubby domain-containing F-box proteins positively regulate immunity by modulating PI4Kβ protein levels

# Summary

• The Tubby domain, named after the TUBBY protein in mice, binds to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate. Arabidopsis has 11 Tubby domain-containing proteins referred to as Tubby-Like Proteins (TLPs). Of the 11 TLPs, 10 possess the N-terminal F-box domain, which can interact with SKP-like proteins and form SKP1-Cullin-F-box E3 ligase complexes. Although mice TUBBY has been extensively studied, plant TLPs’ functions are scarcely detailed. 

• In this study, we show that the Arabidopsis Tubby-like protein 6 (TLP6) and its redundant homologs, TLP1, TLP2, TLP5, and TLP10, positively regulate Arabidopsis immune responses. Furthermore, in an immunoprecipitation mass spectrometry analysis to search for ubiquitination substrates of the TLPs, we identified two redundant phosphoinositide biosynthesis enzymes, phosphatidylinositol 4-kinase $\beta$ proteins (PI4Kβs), PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2, as TLP interactors. 

• Importantly, TLP6 overexpression lines fully phenocopy the phenotypes of the pi4kβ1,2 mutant, while TLP6 overexpression also leads to increased PI4Kβ2 ubiquitination and reduction in its protein level in a proteasome-dependent manner. Most significantly, TLP6 overexpression does not further enhance the autoimmunity of the pi4kβ1,2 double mutant, supporting the hypothesis that TLP6 targets the PI4Kβs for ubiquitination and degradation.

# Introduction

Plants have complex immune systems to fend off pathogen infections (Jones & Dangl, 2006). Membrane-localized pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) recognize diverse molecular patterns generated from microbial activities to elicit pattern-triggered immunity (PTI; Monaghan & Zipfel, 2012; Bigeard et al., 2015). By comparison, effector-triggered immunity (ETI) is mediated by the intracellular nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat receptors (NLRs; Jones et al., 2016; van Wersch et al., 2020; Zhou & Zhang, 2020), which detect pathogen effectors either by direct binding or indirectly by detecting the effector induced modifications of host proteins. 

Plant immune responses are intricately controlled. During infection, defense is quickly elicited, but later dampened to avoid autoimmunity. Disruption in this homeostasis can lead to plants becoming more susceptible or exhibiting constitutive immune responses. For example, snc1 carries a gain-of-function mutation in the NLR Suppressor of npr1-1 Constitutive 1 (SNC1) (Zhang et al., 2003; Cheng et al., 2011). This mutation leads to more stabilized SNC1, resulting in autoimmunity and associated dwarfism (Cheng et al., 2011). As the size of plants inversely correlates with its defense output, this mutant has been successfully used as a sensitized background for various genetic screens to search for novel immune regulators (Johnson et al., 2012). 

The ubiquitination pathway is used to regulate almost all biological processes in eukaryotes. It consists of three major enzymes: the ubiquitin-activating enzyme (E1), the ubiquitinconjugating enzyme (E2), and the ubiquitin ligase (E3). The E3 ligase interacts with the substrate and the E2, largely conferring substrate specificity (Duplan & Rivas, 2014). In plant genomes, E3 encoding genes have greatly expanded, suggesting a high degree of functional specialization and their critical roles in plant biology. For example, the Arabidopsis genome includes 2 E1, 37 E2, and over 1500 E3 genes (Smalle & Viersta, 2004; Vierstra, 2009). However, the functions of most E3s have not been defined since single mutants often do not have any observable phenotype, suggesting a potential high level of redundancy among E3s with high sequence similarity. To find novel E3 ligases involved in the regulation of immune responses, we conducted a genetic screen where candidate E3 ligases were overexpressed in the snc1 genetic background. The resulting transgenic plants were screened for enhancement or suppression of the snc1 autoimmunity. From this SNIPER (snc1 Influencing Plant E3 ligase Reverse genetic) screen, a number of novel immune regulators that modulate immune output have been discovered (Tong et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2020; Ao et al., 2021). 

Here, we describe a group of F-box proteins identified from the SNIPER screen (Xu et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018). They belong to the Tubby-Like Protein (TLP) family in Arabidopsis due to their N-terminal Tubby domain (Lai et al., 2004). In mice, mutations in TUBBY or tubby-like proteins (TULPs) can lead to obesity, blindness, and hearing loss. The Tubby domain was shown to bind specifically to negatively charged phosphoinositide species (Coleman & Eicher, 1990; Kleyn et al., 1996; Santagata et al., 2001; Carroll et al., 2004; Wang et al., 2018). The Tubby-like family of proteins TLPs are widely found in animals including humans, mice (North et al., 1997), Caenorhabditis elegans (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2005), and Drosophila melanogaster (Ronshaugen et al., 2002). They are also present in plants such as Arabidopsis (Lai et al., 2004), poplar (Yang et al., 2008), rice (Kou et al., 2009), wheat (Hong et al., 2016), maize and sorghum (Yulong et al., 2016), and apple (Xu et al., 2016) in expanded gene families. Unlike animal TLPs, most plant TLPs possess N-terminal F-box domains (Wang et al., 2018), suggesting that they serve as parts of the complex SKP1-Cullin-F-box (SCF) E3 ligases. 

Our analysis revealed that Tubby-like protein 6 (TLP6) regulates immunity through modulating the homeostasis of the phosphatidylinositol-4-OH Kinase $\beta$ proteins (PI4Kβs). PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2 are two redundant enzymes (Preuss et al., 2006). The Arabidopsis PI4Kβs have been implicated in root growth and the pi4kβ1,2 displays constitutively active defense responses (Preuss et al., 2006; Šašek et al., 2014; Antignani et al., 2015). Overall, our study reveals a novel role for a subset of Arabidopsis TLPs in regulating plant immunity by modulating PI4Kβ protein levels. 

# Materials and Methods

# Phylogenetic analysis and tree construction

The amino acid sequences of the Arabidopsis thaliana TLPs (11 proteins), Chlamydomonas reinhardtii P.A.Dang. TLP2, Homo sapiens L. TULP1,and Mus musculus L. TUBBY were obtained from NCBI. The sequences were aligned using MUSCLE align in MEGAX. The alignment was trimmed to include just the C terminal Tubby domain (InterPro domain ID-IPR000007). This alignment was then used to predict the evolutionary history of the TLPs. 

The evolutionary history was inferred using the maximum likelihood method and JTT matrix-based model (Jones et al., 1992). The percentage of replicate trees in which the associated taxa clustered together in the bootstrap test (1000 replicates) are shown next to the branches. Initial tree(s) for the heuristic search were obtained automatically by applying Neighbor-Join and BioNJ algorithms to a matrix of pairwise distances estimated using the JTT model and then selecting the topology with superior log likelihood value. Evolutionary analyses were conducted in MEGAX (Kumar et al., 2018).

# Results

# Overexpression of Tubby-like protein 6 (TLP6) enhances plant immunity

The SNIPER reverse genetic screen was conducted to identify novel E3 ligases involved in regulating plant immune responses. Arabidopsis annotated E3s with $> 1 . 7$ -fold induction after pathogen elicitor treatment, or E3s that were identified as potential targets of the master immune regulator SARD1 (Sun et al., 2015), were selected for overexpression analysis (Tong et al., 2017). CaMV 35S promoter-driven candidate E3s were expressed in the snc1 (suppressor of npr1-1 constitutive 1) autoimmune mutant background (Zhang et al., 2003). Among the transgenic plants, snc1 plants expressing 35S promoter-driven N-terminal 3FLAG epitope-tagged $T L P 6 ( 3 F L A G - T L P 6 O E )$ displayed smaller size and leaf curliness, morphological features which are often associated with constitutively active defense responses (autoimmunity; Fig. 1a,b). In their progeny, this enhanced dwarfism co-segregated perfectly with the glufosinate (Basta) resistance conferred by the selectable marker linked to the 3FLAG-TLP6 transgene, indicating that the enhanced dwarfism is due to the presence of the transgene. 

To examine whether the snc1-enhancing effect conferred by the 3FLAG-TLP6 OE is specific to snc1, the same transgene was transformed into the Col-0 WT background. WT plants overexpressing 3FLAG-TLP6 appeared severely dwarfed compared with the control (Fig. 1c,d). In addition, these transgenic plants exhibited enhanced resistance against the virulent 

oomycete pathogen Hyaloperonospora arabidopsidis Noco2 (Ha Noco2; Fig. 1e). Together, these data suggest that TLP6 is a positive regulator of plant immunity. When overexpressed, TLP6 enhances immune responses. 

# TLP6 belongs to the Tubby family of F-box proteins

TLP6 belongs to the Tubby-Like Protein family (Carroll et al., 2004; Lai et al., 2004; Wang et al., 2018). There are 11 TLPs encoded in the Arabidopsis genome (Fig. 2a). Ten of them, except for TLP8, possess an N-terminal F-box motif. TLPs 1, 3, 6, 7, 9, 10, and 11 have previously been shown to interact with Arabidopsis SKP-Like proteins (ASKs; Bao et al., 2014). In addition, in the Harvard Yeast Interactome database, TLP1 can interact with ASK1. These data suggest that the F-box containing TLPs are indeed parts of SCF complex E3 ligases. From phylogenetic analysis, TLP2 is the closest homolog to TLP6, with TLPs 1, 10, and 5 forming a sister clade (Fig. 2a). 

# TLP2/1/5/10 seem to function redundantly with TLP6

To test whether other Arabidopsis TLPs have redundant roles in plant immune regulation, the phylogenetically related TLP2, TLP1, TLP5, and TLP10 were overexpressed in snc1.Like 3FLAG-TLP6 OE, expression of 35S promoter-driven TLPs 1,2, 5, and 10 also resulted in autoimmunity-associated dwarfism in snc1 (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, these transgenic lines displayed enhanced resistance to Ha Noco2 compared with snc1 (Fig. 2c), suggesting that like TLP6, TLP2, TLP1, TLP5, and TLP10 have similar redundant functions in enhancing plant immunity. As some of these TLPs have previously been shown to interact with ASKs (Bao et al., 2014), the immune-promoting phenotype of the overexpression lines is most likely due to the TLPs’ E3 ligase activities. 

We further examined the expression pattern of TLP6 and its close homologs under biotic stress conditions using the Bio-Analytical Resource’s (BAR) e-Northern expression browser (Toufighi et al., 2005; Wang et al., 2011). Upon infection by Ha Emwal, which triggers ETI mediated by RPP4 in WT plants, the expression of TLP6 and TLP2 was significantly induced in WT-resistant plants, and to a lesser extent in the rpp4 susceptible seedlings (Fig. 2d). TLP2 and TLP6 exhibit the most similar co-regulation, supporting their close phylogenetic relationship (Fig. 2a; Wang et al., 2011). Together, these data suggest that these five TLPs likely function redundantly in modulating Arabidopsis immune response. Their overexpression enhances immunity. 

# Immunoprecipitation–mass spectrometry analysis to search for TLP2 interactors

Since overexpression of the above-mentioned TLPs led to enhanced immunity, we hypothesized that the SCF-dependent degradation of their common ubiquitination substrate is responsible for enhancing immunity. To search for the ubiquitination substrate, an immunoprecipitation–mass spectrometry (IP-MS) experiment was performed to identify the interacting partners of the TLPs. Performing IP-MS using plants overexpressing the WT F-box protein would defeat the purpose of identifying its substrate, since its overexpression would lead to substrate degradation. To overcome this obstacle, IP-MS was performed using plants overexpressing the dominant-negative (DN) form of the F-box protein, where the F-box domain that is required for its interaction with ASKs, is deleted. These dominant-

negative F-box proteins can still interact with their ubiquitination target but are incapable of ubiquitinating them. Such an approach has been used routinely to search for SCF substrates (Lee et al., 2018; Ao et al., 2021). Here, the IP-MS analysis was carried out with transgenic lines showing the highest 3FLAG-DN-TLP2 protein expression levels. 

From the IP-MS data, TLP2 peptides were the most enriched of all peptides identified, whereas no TLP2 peptides were present in the negative controls WT or 3FLAG-GFP samples (Fig. 3a), supporting that the IP was highly successful in specifically enriching TLP2 and its associated proteins. In addition to TLP2, the other TLP family members, TLP6, TLP10, and TLP1, were also pulled down with DN-TLP2 (Fig. 3a). This is not surprising as F-box proteins are known to oligomerize with themselves and their redundant family members (Tang et al., 2007; Kirk et al., 2008; Li & Hao, 2010; Dezfulian et al., 2016). It is interesting that TLP6, which is the closest to TLP2 phylogenetically, was enriched more and in all three repeats, whereas TLP1, the least phylogenetically related to TLP2, was only enriched in one repeat (Fig. 3a), suggesting certain level of unequal redundancy among the TLPs. The absence of TLP1, TLP5, or TLP10 in some repeats also suggests that true interactors may not be present in all IP repeats likely due to their low abundance. These TLP peptide reads formed controls for us to evaluate the likely ubiquitination substrate. We short-listed the candidate substrates of TLP2 based on their presence in at least one repeat with more than 10 peptides, and their absence in the negative control repeats. We also examined their possible roles in plant immunity based on literature searches and annotations. Interactors whose knockouts had a known immunerelated phenotype, or an immune-related morphological phenotype were also included (Fig. 3b). 

# Knockout analysis of TLP2 interactors identified in the IP-MS

Since overexpression of the TLPs enhance immunity, we hypothesized that the knockout of its substrate should also lead to enhanced immunity. Knockout mutants of the shortlisted candidates were generated either by using the egg-cell-based CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing system or by obtaining T-DNA insertional lines (Z. Wang et al., 2015). Crosses were performed where necessary to obtain higher order mutants to address potential genetic redundancy. Of the shortlisted candidates, cap8,9, 10, sar1a, and the mca4,5,6,7 mutants displayed WT morphology (Fig. 3c), and immunity to Ha Noco2 (Fig. 3d). Only the pi4kβ1,2 mutant displayed dwarfism and morphological similarities to the TLP6 overexpression lines. 

The nduv1 homozygous seeds only germinated after a week on $\%$ MS plates containing $3 \%$ sucrose. The seedlings were then transplanted to soil after 14 d. The nduv1 mutant displayed WT morphology in the initial 2 wk of growth on soil (Fig. 3e), but then displayed developmental defects as described before (Fromm et al., 2016). We assayed its immune status using Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato DC3000 (Pst DC3000). As shown in Fig. 3(f), the nduv1 plants supported less growth of the bacteria compared with the WT. Though nduv1 displayed enhanced immunity, its morphological characteristics and extreme developmental defects did not match the phenotype of the TLP6 overexpression lines. We therefore excluded it from further study. 

Interestingly, the redundant phosphatidylinositol 4-OH Kinase βs (PI4Kβs) coimmunoprecipitated with DN-TLP2. More importantly, peptides unique to both PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2 were identified (Fig. 3b). This is significant because both PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2 are low-abundance proteins. According to PaxDB’s Arabidopsis whole organism integrated protein abundance dataset, the relative levels of PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2 are 0.42 and 0.01 ppm, respectively (M. Wang et al., 2015). As a comparison, the level of another low-abundance protein, SNC1, is $1 2 . 3 \mathrm { p p m }$ . As the two PI4Kβ proteins are genetically redundant (Preuss et al., 2006), presence of both of them in the IP-MS sample indicates that their pull-downs are not likely to be nonspecific events. We therefore focused the subsequent study on testing whether PI4Kβs could be the ubiquitination substrates of TLP6. 

To confirm the protein–protein interaction between TLPs and PI4Kβs, Co-IP analysis was performed. As shown in Fig. 3(g), 3HA-DN-TLP6 could indeed be pulled down by either 3FLAG-DN-TLP6 or the native 3FLAG-TLP6. Furthermore, HA-DN-TLP6 could be pulled down by 3FLAG-PI4Kβ2 (Fig. 3h). These results confirm that TLP6/2 can interact with the PI4Kβs. 

# The pi4kβ1,2 double mutant exhibits enhanced immunity and phenocopies the TLP6 OE line

The pi4kβ1,2 double mutant carries loss-of-function mutations in redundant phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate biosynthesis enzymes, PI4Kβ1 and PI4Kβ2 (Preuss et al., 2006). PI4Kβs catalyze the phosphorylation of the membrane lipid phosphatidylinositol (PI) to form phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate (PI4P), a lipid component of the plasma membrane and other organellar membranes. They were known to have roles in regulating plant development and a host of cellular functions including plant immune responses (Marković & Jaillais, 2022; Zarreen et al., 2023). The enhanced disease resistance and constitutive high levels of the defense hormone salicylic acid (SA) in pi4kβ1,2 have been previously described (Šašek et al., 2014; Antignani et al., 2015). It displays constitutive PR gene expression and enhanced immunity to Ha Noco2,and the bacterial pathogens Pseudomonas syringae pv. maculicola ES4326 (Psm ES4326) and Pst DC3000 (Sasek et al., 2014; Kalachova et al., 2020). 

As pi4kβ1,2 plants resemble TLP6 OE in both size, and immunity to Ha Noco2 (Fig. 3c,d), we further examined their roots as pi4kβ1,2 was reported to have short roots (Delage et al., 2012; Šašek et al., 2014). As shown in Fig. 4(a,b), the 3FLAG-TLP6 OE roots were intermediate between WT and the pi4kβ1 2 plants. In addition to short roots, pi4kβ1 2 displays an aberrant root hair morphology that has been described previously (Preuss et al., 2006). Similarly, when we examined root hair morphology, we observed that pi4kβ1,2 had stunted root hairs (Fig. 4c). In addition, we observed that the 3FLAG-TLP6 OE lines had similar root hairs although the defects were not as severe as those shown by pi4kβ1,2 (Fig. 4c). Furthermore, free SA levels in the 3FLAG-TLP6OE lines were intermediate between that in WT and pi4kβ1,2 (Fig. 4d). Lastly, the pi4kβ1,2 snc1 triple mutant displayed enhanced leaf curliness and was morphologically like the snc1 3FLAG-TLP6 OElines (Fig. 4e). Taken together, the 3FLAG-TLP6 OE lines are phenotypically like pi4kβ1,2, supporting the hypothesis that PI4Kβs might be ubiquitinated by TLP6 for further 

degradation. TLP6 overexpression would lead to the reduction in the PI4Kβ protein levels, leading the overexpression lines to display phenotypic similarities to pi4kβ1,2. 

# Autoimmunity displayed by both the TLP6 OE line and pi4kβ1,2 is partly dependent on EDS1 and AGB1

It has been previously shown that Enhanced Disease Susceptibility 1 (EDS1), an essential component of TIR-type NLR (TNL) signaling, is required for the immunity displayed by pi4kβ1,2 (Šašek et al., 2014). To test whether EDS1 is similarly required for the autoimmunity of TLP6 OE, we generated the TLP6 OE eds1-2. It is worth noting that the TLP6 OE line 3.2 used here for the epistasis analyses is not the 3FLAG-TLP6 OE lines described earlier. Here, TLP6was overexpressed under 35S promoter without any epitope tag. Nevertheless, it displayed a similar short root and autoimmunity-associated dwarfism and enhanced resistance to Ha Noco2 (Fig. 5a-d). As shown in Fig. 5(a-c,e-g), eds1-2 partially suppressed the autoimmunity-associated dwarfism and enhanced resistance to Ha Noco2 displayed by both the TLP6 OE line and pi4kβ1,2. 

We also tested whether the Arabidopsis heteromeric G protein $\beta$ subunit (AGB1), which is required for PTI responses (Liu et al., 2013), is required for the autoimmunity displayed in the TLP6 OE line and pi4kβ1,2. Unlike eds1-2, introducing the agb1 mutation did not suppress the morphological phenotype of the TLP6 OE line or pi4kβ1,2 (Fig. 5a-c,e-g). The agb1 mutation, however, slightly suppressed the Ha Noco2 immunity of the TLP6 OE line (Fig. 5c). Similarly, the agb1 mutation seemed to slightly suppress the immunity of pi4kβ1,2 but this suppression is not statistically significant when compared to the Ha Noco2 growth in pi4kβ1,2(Fig. 5g). Consistent with previous observation (Sasek et al.,2014), neither eds1-2 nor agb1 could suppress the root length defect of the $T L P 6 O E$ line or pi4kβ1,2 (Fig. 5d, h). Taken together, EDS1 and AGB1 are partly required for the autoimmunity displayed by the TLP6 OE line and pi4kβ1,2. These epistasis analyses reveal that similar immune pathways are involved in the autoimmunity displayed by the TLP6 OE line and pi4kβ1,2. This further confirms the resemblance between TLP6 OE and pi4kβ1,2 plants. 

# TLP6 and DN-TLP6 have opposite effects on PI4Kβ2 levels

To further test whether TLP6 is involved in the protein turnover of the PI4Kβs, we examined the PI4Kβ2 levels upon co-expression with TLP6. As shown in Fig. 6(a), when 3FLAG-TLP6 was transiently co-expressed with 2HA-PI4Kβ2 in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves, the 2HA-PI4Kβ2 level was much lower than when it was expressed alone, supporting the hypothesis that TLP6 targets PI4Kβs for ubiquitination and degradation. 

To corroborate this observation, we generated stable Arabidopsis transgenic lines expressing 2HA-PI4Kβ2 under the control of the 35S promoter. The 35S::2HA-PI4Kβ2 transgene is able to complement the morphological and immune phenotypes of pi4kβ1,2 I (Fig. S1). We introduced the 35S::TLP6 transgene in the background of transgenic plants possessing 35S::2HA-PI4K-β2. As shown in Fig. 6(b,c), TLP6 overexpression led to reduced 2HA-PI4Kβ2 levels. Conversely, co-expressing a dominant-negative (DN) version of the F-box protein would lead to the stabilization of the substrates (Ao et al., 2021). As shown in Fig. 6 

(d), overexpression of DN-TLP6 with a deletion of the F-box domain indeed led to increased PI4Kβ2 levels. 

From the microarray data displayed in Fig. 2(d), it is evident that when RPP4 containing WT plants were sprayed with ETI-inducing Ha Emwa1, TLP2, and TLP6 show transcriptional upregulation from 0.5 d postinoculation (dpi) that lasts until at least 6 dpi. However, when Ha Emwal was sprayed on the susceptible rpp4 mutant, the induction of TLPs was seen only at 0.5 dpi. To test whether such induction correlates with decrease in the PI4Kβ2 level, we sprayed 35S::2HA-PI4Kβ2 plants with either ETI-inducing Ha Emwa1 or the non-ETIinducing Ha Noco2. In agreement with the microarray data, we observed a reduction in 2HA-PI4Kβ2 levels at 0.5 dpi when sprayed with either strain of Ha. At 6 dpi, 2HA-PI4Kβ2 reaches steady-state levels in plants sprayed with the non-ETI-inducing Ha Noco2. However, plants sprayed with ETI-inducing Ha Emwa1 still display reduced 2HA-PI4Kβ2 level at 6 dpi (Fig. 6e). Thus, TLP6 overexpression negatively affects PI4Kβ2 levels, which depends on its F-box domain, and this effect of TLP6 on PI4Kβ2 levels is also observed under physiological conditions at time points when TLP2 and TLP6 are induced after infection. 

# Overexpression of TLP6 does not further enhance the autoimmunity of the pi4kβ1,2 mutant

To further genetically test our hypothesis that TLP6 targets PI4Kβs for degradation, we overexpressed TLP6 in the pi4kβ1,2 background. According to our model, since both the PI4Kβs are knocked out in pi4kβ1,2, TLP6OE should not further enhance the autoimmunity of pi4kβ1,2. As shown in Fig. 6(f,g), unlike TLP6 overexpression in snc1 or Col-0, TLP6 overexpression does not further enhance the dwarfism of pi4kβ1,2. This further supports that the enhanced immunity seen in the TLP6 overexpression lines depends on the presence of the PI4Kβ proteins. 

# TLP6 overexpression enhances degradation of the PI4Kβ2 through the proteasome

Since co-expression of TLP6 with PI4Kβ2 led to reduced PI4Kβ2 levels (Fig. 6a-c), we tested whether this reduction is dependent on the proteasome. As shown in Fig. 7(a), with the reversible proteasome inhibitor MG132, TLP6-dependent reduction in PI4Kβ2 level is abolished. Furthermore, higher protein accumulation and high molecular weight (MW) smeary bands in the HA-PI4Kβ2 samples were observed in the presence of Carfilzomib, an irreversible proteasome inhibitor, suggestive of polyubiquitination of the proteins (Fig. 7b). These data indicate that PI4Kβ2 is actively turned over by the proteasome, and TLP6 overexpression leads to proteasome-dependent degradation of the PI4Kβs. 

# TLP6 overexpression enhances ubiquitination of PI4Kβ2

To test whether TLP6 directly contributes to the ubiquitination of the PI4Kβs, we coexpressed 2HA-PI4Kβ2 along with 3FLAG-TLP6, infiltrated the leaf with MG132 12 h before tissue collection and included MG132 throughout the protein extraction and IP process. However, we were unable to observe higher MW species of 2HA-PI4Kβ2 in the HA-IP samples (Fig. S2). One possibility that we could not detect any higher MW 2HA-PI4Kβ2 species could be that the reversible proteasome inhibitor MG132 could not prevent the fast turnover of ubiquitinated 2HA-PI4Kβ2. To overcome this problem, we performed the same assay with the addition of the irreversible proteasome inhibitor, Carfilzomib. With 

Carfilzomib in the extraction and wash buffers used for the IP (we did not infiltrate since infiltration of Carfilzomib led to massive cell death within hours of infiltration), we were able to detect higher MW species in the HA-IP samples (Fig. 7c). Since these higher MW species are absent in the EV IP sample, the smears seem specific to 2HA-PI4Kβ2. Additionally, since these high MW species are only observed in the presence of Carfilzomib, they are likely the ubiquitinated forms of 2HA- PI4Kβ2. Furthermore, co-expression of TLP6 significantly enhanced the amount of the higher MW species but co-expression of the DN-TLP6 did not, indicating that the E3 ligase function of TLP6 is essential for the generation of the high MW species of PI4Kβ2 (Fig. 7c). It is worth noting that in this assay, likely due to the addition of high level of detergents ( $1 \%$ NP-40 and $0 . 0 5 \%$ SDS), to facilitate better extraction and enrichment of 2HA-PI4Kβ2, we did not observe Co-IP of the FLAG-tagged TLP6 species with immunoprecipitated 2HA-PI4Kβ2 (Fig. 7c). Taken together, these results support that TLP6 overexpression likely enhances ubiquitination of the PI4Kβs. 

# tlp1,2,5,6,10 quintuple mutant, DN-TLP6 OE lines, and the PI4Kβ2 OE lines display WT levels of immunity

To further test our hypothesis, we generated and analyzed the tlp1,2,5,6, 10 quintuple knockout mutant in comparison with the PI4Kβ2 OE lines. According to our model, they should share similar phenotypes. The tlp1,2,5,6, 10 mutant plants and the 2HA-PI4Kβ2 OE lines displayed no obvious morphological defects. In addition, the tlp1,2,5,6, 10 KO mutant lines and the PI4Kβ2 OE lines displayed WT levels of susceptibility to Ha Noco2 and Psm ES4326 (Fig. 8a,b). Similarly, TLP6 DN lines displayed WT levels of susceptibility to Ha Noco2 and Psm ES4326 (Fig. S3). Since TLP6, PI4Kβ1, and PI4Kβ2 seem to have a role in root growth, we also tested the root immunity of the tlp1,2,5,6, 10 and the DN-TLP6 and PI4Kβ2 OE lines using the root pathogens Pseudomonas spp. N2C3 and Pseudomonas fuscovaginae SE-1,and the beneficial Pseudomonas spp.WCS365.However, we did not observe enhanced colonization of the roots by either the pathogenic or the beneficial Pseudomonas strains (Fig. S4). Thus, consistent with our hypothesis, the tlp knockout mutants, and DN-TLP6 OE lines show the same immune phenotype as PI4Kβ2 OElines. 

# Discussion

Through our reverse genetic SNIPER screen, we have identified a subclade of the Arabidopsis TLPs, which positively regulate plant immunity by modulating the PI4Kβ protein levels. 

Plant TLPs have been implicated in several biological processes. Arabidopsis TLP3 and TLP9 were found to be redundantly involved in ABA signaling (Bao et al., 2014). Cicer arietinum (chickpea) TLP1 contributes to resistance to drought,salinity,and oxidative stresses (Wardhan et al., 2016). Rice TLPs have been implicated in biotic stress responses as their transcription was upregulated upon pathogen infection (Cai et al., 2008; Kou et al., 2009). In a number of studies based on large-scale transcriptional analysis in response to stresses, TLPs have been implicated in both biotic and abiotic stress responses such as 

responses to high and cold temperatures, salinity, exogeneous ABA, salt, and drought, in Malus domesticus, Medicago truncatula, Triticum aestivum, and Zea mays (Gharaghani et al., 2015; Hong et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2016; Yulong et al., 2016). Although these studies point to the importance of TLPs in various plant stress responses, the mechanism by which the TLPs are functioning remains elusive. 

In this study, we found that the Arabidopsis TLP6 enhances immunity upon overexpression. In addition to TLP6, the phylogenetically closely related TLPs 2, 10, 1, and 5, positively regulate Arabidopsis immunity (Fig. 2a-c). They are also induced during infection (Fig. 2d), likely contributing to promoting immunity. As some of these TLPs were shown to interact with ASKs (Bao et al., 2014), they probably serve as parts of SCF E3 ligase complexes to target their substrate proteins for ubiquitination and degradation. During infection, they modulate plant immune responses by degrading negative regulators of immunity to promote defense. Using IP-MS on plants overexpressing DN-TLP2, several TLP2 interacting candidates were identified (Fig. 3; Table S3). Through reverse genetics analysis, we identified PI4Kβs as the most likely substrates for the TLPs (Fig. 3). The significance of the other interactions identified in IP-MS (Table S3) needs to be sorted out in the future. 

In our IP-MS, we observed significant enrichment of the PI4Kβs. Such interaction was confirmed by the Co-IP assays. In addition, the PI4Kβs are low-abundance proteins (M. Wang et al., 2015), explaining their enrichment in only one out of three IP-MS repeats. This is similar to the enrichment of TLP2 homologs, TLP10, and TLP1 is only two and one IP-MS repeats respectively. 

Multiple lines of evidence support our hypothesis that TLP6 targets PI4Kβs for ubiquitination and degradation. First, although the PI4Kβs are of extremely low abundance, both PI4Kβ1 and its redundant paralog PI4Kβ2 were found in the IP-MS analysis of DN-TLP2 overexpression lines. Second, without exception, the pi4kβ1,2 mutant phenocopies the TLP6 OE lines with regard to both root development and immunity (Fig. 4). These phenotypic resemblances were confirmed in both the snc1 autoimmune background and through the epistasis analysis with eds1 and agb1. Although eds1-2 can partially suppress the aboveground rosette phenotypes, it has no effect on suppressing the root length defects of the TLP6 OE line or pi4kβ1,2 (Fig. 5). Furthermore, TLP6 expression leads to reduction in protein levels of PI4Kβ2 in the heterologous transient N. benthamiana, expression system (Fig. 6a). In the transgenic Arabidopsis lines, the amount of HA-tagged PI4Kβ2 is inversely proportional to the TLP6 expression level, further supporting that the PI4Kβ2 depletion is directly proportional to the level of TLP6 expression (Fig. 6b,c). Additionally, DN-TLP6 expression led to increased PI4Kβ2 levels (Fig. 6d). Furthermore, a reduction in the PI4Kβ2 levels coincides with the time points after Ha infection when TLP2 and TLP6 are induced (Fig. 6e). As TLP6 was previously shown to interact with ASKs, components of SCF E3 ligase complexes, this PI4Kβ protein depletion is most likely a result of the SCFTLP6 E3 ligase activity (Bao et al., 2014). In support, when TLP6 was overexpressed in pi4kβ1,2, no enhanced autoimmunity was observed (Fig. 6f,g). 

We observed that in the presence of the proteasome inhibitor MG132, TLP6 overexpressiondependent reduction in PI4Kβ2 levels is lost (Fig. 7a). Furthermore, in the ubiquitination assay (Fig. 7c), we observed increased amounts of high MW species of PI4Kβ2. Additionally, co-expression of the F-box domain deleted DN-TLP6 did not enhance the amount of the high MW species in the IP samples, suggesting that the E3 ligase function of TLP6 is essential for the generation of the high MW species of PI4Kβ2. Since these high MW species are absent in the EV IP sample, the smear should come from 2HA-PI4Kβ2. Combined with the fact that the smear only occurs in the presence of the proteasome inhibitor Carfilzomib, these high MW species are most likely polyubiquitinated 2HA-PI4Kβ2. However, it is curious that the high MW PI4Kβ2 species occurred only in the presence of the potent irreversible proteasome inhibitor Carfilzomib and not in the presence of the reversible and less potent MG132 (Figs 7c, S2). We speculate that the polyubiquitinated 2HA-PI4Kβ2 are likely very rapidly turned over by the proteasome, and only Carfilzomib that irreversibly inactivates the proteasome preserves these polyubiquitinated 2HA-PI4Kβ2 species during the IP process, enabling their detection in the immunoblot. 

Since overexpression of the TLPs and the resulting degradation of the PI4Kβs enhances immunity, we predicted that there could be a reduction in immunity in the TLP knockout, the DN-TLP6 OE lines, and the PI4Kβ2 OE lines where the levels of PI4Kβs are increased. Contrary to our expectation, we observed that tlp1,2,5,6, 10, the TLP6-DN OE lines, and the PI4Kβ2 OElines did not display reduced immunity against tested pathogens including the oomycete Ha Noco2 (Figs 8a, S3c) and bacterial virulent pathogen Psm ES4326 (Figs 8b, S3d). We further tested whether the root immunity is compromised in the above genotypes (Fig. S4a,c), but we did not see increased susceptibility to the root pathogens Pseudomonas fuscovaginae SE-1,and Pseudomonas spp. N2C3 either. Additionally, these genotypes did not support enhanced colonization of the root system by the beneficial Pseudomonas spp. WCS365 (Fig. S4b). We only saw enhanced resistance in the TLP6 OE lines and pi4kβ1,2. Allthese results show that increasing the PI4Kβ levels by either overexpression or generating tlp KO or TLP DN OE lines does not suppress plant immunity. As seen in Fig. 6(e), the TLPs’ effect on PI4Kβ protein levels is mainly seen during the initial time points after infection with Ha Emwa1. Thus, it is possible that there are subtle differences during these early time points that cannot be captured in our infection experiments. Alternatively, the PI4Kβs homeostasis may be under other unknown regulatory controls to prevent their overaccumulation. This is supported by the fact that even in our tagged PI4Kβ2 OE lines, we were never able to observe large protein accumulations. 

Previous studies on the pi4kβ1,2 mutant have revealed the role of the PI4Kβs in root growth (Preuss et al., 2006; Delage et al., 2012; Šašek et al., 2014; Antignani et al., 2015). Though the autoimmune phenotype of the mutant has been previously described, it is unclear why it displays such an enhanced resistance phenotype (Šašek et al., 2014). Its autoimmunity is dependent on SA, and EDS1, since mutations in EDS1, a gene involved in SA biosynthesis/ signaling, can suppress the enhanced resistance phenotypes of pi4kβ1,2 (Šašek et al., 2014 and current study). However, the rosette and root phenotypes of the mutant seem to have a differential need for SA and EDS1 since mutations in SA biosynthesis/signaling genes and in EDS1 can suppress its immunity but not its root length defect (Šašek et al., 2014 and 

current study). It has been previously shown that the kinase-inactive version of PI4Kβ1 is not able to complement the phenotype of pi4kβ1,2 (Antignani et al., 2015). The most likely cause of the autoimmunity displayed by pi4kβ1,2 is due to the mutation in the PI4Kβs and the resulting depletion in its kinase activity-dependent PI4P and downstream PI(4,5)2P lipid levels. Since phosphatidylinositol species, PI4P, PI(4,5)2P, and other plant lipids have been shown to have various roles in plant immune responses (Xing et al., 2021), it is possible that the perturbed lipid homeostasis in the mutant is causing its autoimmunity. This does not preclude the possibility that the PI4Kβs could have other kinase activity independent of their PI4K activity and the loss of this kinase activity leads to autoimmunity in pi4kβ1,2. Further study is needed to clarify the exact contribution of the PI4Kβs to plant immunity.
