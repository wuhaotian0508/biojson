# SUMMARY

CLAVATA3/EMBRYO SURROUNDING REGION (CLE) peptides are 12–13 amino acid-long peptides that serve as positional signals in plants. The core CLE signaling module consists of a CLE peptide and a leucine-rich repeat receptor-like kinase, but in flowering plants, WUSCHEL-RELATED HOMEOBOX (WOX) transcription factors are also incorporated to form negative feedback loops that regulate stem cell maintenance in the shoot and root. It is not known when WOX genes were co-opted into CLE signaling pathways, only that mosses and liverworts do not require WOX for CLE-regulated stem cell activities. We identified 11 CLE-encoding genes in the Ceratopteris genome, including one (CrCLV3) most similar to shoot meristem CLE peptide CLAVATA3. We performed the first functional characterization of a fern CLE using techniques including RNAi knockdown and synthetic peptide dosage. We found that CrCLV3 promotes cell proliferation and stem cell identity in the gametophyte meristem. Importantly, we provide evidence for CrCLV3 regulation of the WOX gene CrWOXA during the developmental stage when female gametangium formation begins. These discoveries open a new avenue for CLE peptide research in the fern and clarify the evolutionary timeline of CLE-WOX signaling in land plants. 

Keywords: Ceratopteris, meristem, CLAVATA3/EMBRYO SURROUNDING REGION, gametangium. 

# INTRODUCTION

Land plants rely on a plethora of plant-specific hormonal signals to regulate their responses to environmental changes and coordinate developmental processes. These hormones can be small peptides, such as the members of the CLAVATA3/EMBRYO SURROUNDING REGION (CLE) family, which play important roles in meristem maintenance, vascular tissue development, root nodule formation, and response to drought (reviewed by Fletcher, 2020). A mature CLE peptide is produced by cleaving a highly conserved 12 amino acid active region from a propeptide (Ni et al., 2011; Stuhrwohldt et al., € 2020). The mature CLE is secreted into the extracellular space where it can interact with its receptors in an autocrine or paracrine manner, even traveling long distances through the vascular tissue from root to shoot in the case of Arabidopsis CLE25 (Carbonnel et al., 2023; Rojo et al., 2002; Takahashi et al., 2018). 

The action of CLE peptides to regulate the Arabidopsis shoot apical meristem (SAM) is the most well-characterized CLE signaling pathway. WUSCHEL (WUS), a homeobox transcription factor, is produced by cells of the organizing center and moves acropetally to the stem cells, where it 

promotes stem cell division and expression of CLV3 (Perales et al., 2016; Schoof et al., 2000). Stem cell-secreted CLV3 is sensed by its leucine-rich repeat receptor-like kinase CLAVATA1 and several co-receptors in the organizing center, which leads to repression of further WUS expression (Clark et al., 1997; Guo et al., 2010; Hu et al., 2018; Song et al., 2006). This negative feedback loop maintains the stem cell population while allowing for daughter cells to contribute to new organ formation at the periphery of the SAM. Within Arabidopsis, CLE peptides also work in signaling pathways with WUSCHEL-RELATED HOMEOBOX (WOX) genes in the root apical meristem and vascular cambium (Etchells et al., 2013; Sarkar et al., 2007). The monocots rice and maize also use WOX-CLE signaling to regulate meristem activity (Liu et al., 2021; Ohmori et al., 2013). 

In contrast, while bryophytes have both CLE and WOX genes, they function in separate processes. Physcomitrium patens CLE genes PpCLE1-7, coordinate cell division planes of the gametophore apical cell and contribute to protonemal cell fate (Nemec-Venza et al., 2022; Whitewoods et al., 2018). PpWOX13a/b are not involved in gametophyte CLE functions, and instead play a role in the response to 

wounding and in early sporophyte development (Sakakibara et al., 2014). The liverwort Marchantia polymorpha genome contains only two CLEs: MpCLE1 and MpCLE2, which negatively and positively regulate apical cell proliferation, respectively (Hirakawa et al., 2019, 2020). Neither of these CLEs requires the activity of the single M. polymorpha WOX gene, MpWOX, for proper regulation of apical cell proliferation (Hirakawa et al., 2020). Therefore, WOX-CLE signaling is thought to be a derived trait of land plants that evolved after the divergence of bryophytes (Hirakawa & Bowman, 2015; Whitewoods, 2021). Very little is known of the function of CLEs in non-flowering plants other than the bryophytes, thus it is unclear how and when WOX transcription factors were integrated into CLE-receptor signaling pathways. 

As seed-free vascular plants, ferns offer an intermediate position in the land plant phylogeny ideal for examining both the functional diversification of CLEs and the relationship between WOX and CLE genes. The fern Ceratopteris richardii (hereafter, Ceratopteris) is a model homosporous fern with an independent photosynthetic gametophyte generation. A Ceratopteris hermaphrodite gametophyte is a macroscopic single-cell-layer prothallus that contains a multicellular meristem and has recently become a powerful system for studying meristem dynamics (Geng et al., 2022; Withers, Kvamme, et al., 2023). The Ceratopteris genome contains five known WOX genes (Nardmann & Werr, 2012). CrWOX13A and CrWOX13B share homology with Arabidopsis WOX13 and bryophyte WOX genes, while CrWOXA, CrWOXB, and CrWUL are similar to Arabidopsis WOX genes such as WOX8/9 and WUS (Nardmann & Werr, 2012; Wu et al., 2019; Youngstrom et al., 2019, 2022). CrWOXB promotes cell proliferation in both generations and is expressed in the gametophyte meristem (Withers, Kvamme, et al., 2023; Youngstrom et al., 2019). CrWUL, while expressed in both generations, primarily functions in the sporophyte vascular tissue (Youngstrom et al., 2022). In the sporophyte, CrWOXA is upregulated in response to 2,4-D exposure and acts to upregulate CrWUL via a TAATGG cis-element within the CrWUL promoter (Yu et al., 2020). Hirakawa and Bowman (2015) demonstrated that a fern dosed with a synthetic version of a CLE, TRACHEARY ELE-MENT DIFFERENTIATION INHIBITORY FACTOR (TDIF), responds similarly to Arabidopsis after TDIF peptide exposure, which suggests the conservation of downstream signaling components. Bioinformatic analyses in additional fern genomes have detected sequences encoding CLE peptides, but the endogenous functions of these peptides remain unknown (Furumizu et al., 2021). 

To fill the gap in our knowledge about the rise of WOX-CLE collaboration, we investigated CLE signaling in the Ceratopteris gametophyte. We identified and classified 11 Ceratopteris CLE (CrCLE ) peptide genes and four CLV1/BAM homologs. Subsequent experiments 

concentrated on the closest fern homolog of Arabidopsis CLV3 which we named CrCLV3 (Ceric.21G017600). CrCLV3 is expressed in the gametophyte meristem and developing antheridia. We created knockdown lines of CrCLV3 in Ceratopteris and discovered that CrCLV3 functions to promote cell divisions of the gametophyte. Peptide treatment promoted morphological changes including meristem enlargement and inhibition of female gametangium initiation. These gain-of-function phenotypes are the opposite of those exhibited in knockdown lines. Moreover, peptide treatment caused transcriptional changes in multiple CrWOX genes. Significantly, CrCLV3p downregulated CrWOXA in the absence of protein synthesis. These results support the conserved role of CLEs in cell division observed across land plant clades and highlight the possibility that WOX-CLE signaling arose prior to the divergence of ferns and seed plants. 

# RESULTS

# The Ceratopteris genome contains CLAVATA signaling components

A CLE gene has limited homology within the pre-propeptide sequence, with the C-terminal CLE motif being the most informative region for classification, as it represents the mature peptide (Goad et al., 2017; Ito et al., 2006; Oelkers et al., 2008; Strabala et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2020). To identify CLE genes in the Ceratopteris genome, a representative list of Arabidopsis CLEs from the clustering groups defined by Goad et al. (2017), seven Azolla filiculoides CLEs (AfCLEs), and four Salvinia cucullata CLEs (ScCLEs) were used as BLASTp queries into the Ceratopteris predicted proteome (Marchant et al., 2021), which resulted in identification of 10 CrCLEs. Queries into a de novo Ceratopteris transcriptome also identified one additional SN-type CLE that was not annotated in the published genome. CLANS clustering is effective for assigning newly identified CLEs to functionally relevant groups (Goad et al., 2017; Oelkers et al., 2008). A combination of 81 CLE proteins from 11 species (see Table S1 for sequence information) were grouped using CLANS with either the isolated CLE motif (Figure 1a) or pre-propeptide sequence (Figure S1). Clustering with the CLE motif resolved the sequences into five distinct groups, which we labeled Group 1 (HN-type), Group 2 (SN-type), Group 3 (At-HH-type), Group 4 (CLV3 HH-type), and Group 5 (CLE40 HH-type) (Figure 1a). Group 1 consisted of genes from Arabidopsis, all three of the ferns examined, Selaginella moellendorffii, and P. patens, which suggests the CLE motif structure with H11 and N12 is highly conserved across land plant lineages. Group 2 contained all the CLEs with the CLE41/44 (hereafter, TDIF) sequence (HEVPSGPNPISN). # Gametophytes with reduced CrCLV3 expression exhibit diminished cell proliferation

Bryophyte CLV3 homologs function to promote stem cell divisions in the gametophyte, while in Arabidopsis, CLV3 has the opposite effect in the SAM, negatively regulating 

proliferation (Clark et al., 1995; Fletcher et al., 1999; Hirakawa et al., 2020). To assess the function of CrCLV3 in the fern, we generated stable transformant lines expressing an RNAi construct targeting the CrCLV3 transcript via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation (Bui et al., 2015). From over 30 independent transformant lines, five lines were chosen for quantification of CrCLV3 knockdown in ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ sporophytes (Figure S3). Three lines (clv3kd-6, clv3kd-12, and clv3kd-18) were used for subsequent phenotyping during ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ gametophyte development. 

We harvested hermaphrodite gametophytes of wild-type and clv3kd lines at 7, 10, and 13 dpp to assess total cell number, antheridium number, and archegonium number (Figure 3a–d). At 7 dpp, clv3kd lines already exhibited a decrease in cell number $( 1 1 5 \pm 2 5 )$ compared to the wild-type $( 2 0 9 \pm 5 7 )$ (Figure 3e). The clv3kd lines experienced a significant delay in growth at 10 and 13 dpp, with an average of $3 5 \%$ and $40 \%$ fewer cells than wild-type, respectively (Figure 3e). This result suggests that CrCLV3 functions to promote cell proliferation in hermaphrodites. 

Production of archegonia and antheridia were calculated as a ratio of organ number per cell, to account for the 

decreased cell proliferation in the clv3kd lines. Antheridium production was comparable between wild-type and clv3kd gametophytes at 7 and 10 dpp (Figure 3f). By 13 dpp, wild-type plants produced antheridia more frequently (0.019 antheridia per cell), while clv3kd lines remained at a lower rate similar to earlier time points (0.009 antheridia per cell for clv3kd-6 and clv3kd-18 and 0.005 antheridia per cell for clv3kd-12). This result suggests that CrCLV3 promotes antheridium formation once gametophytes reach sexual maturity (Figure 3f). In contrast, wild-type and clv3kd plants did not differ in the number of cells per archegonium at 7 or 13 dpp, despite the decreased cell number in clv3kd lines (Figure 3f); however, during the initial production of archegonia from 7 to 10 dpp, clv3kd lines produced significantly fewer archegonia per cell than wild-type plants (Figure 3g). These results suggest that CrCLV3 plays a positive role in both male and female gametangia formation, although it functions at different times during sexual maturation. 

# Exogenous CLV3p promotes cell divisions and inhibits archegonium formation

Synthetic mature CLE peptides have been used in multiple species successfully to create gain of function phenotypes and are an established method of classifying members of this gene family (Hirakawa et al., 2020; Hirakawa & Bowman, 2015; Song et al., 2013, 2021). As we observed a decrease in cell number in clv3kd lines, we hypothesized that exogenous CrCLV3 peptide would produce the opposite effect, resulting in larger plants with more cells. 

Plants were grown on media containing different amounts of synthetic CrCLV3 peptide (CrCLV3p) from 0 to 13 dpp, after which we assessed the plants for morphological differences. While plants dosed with 100 nM CrCLV3p appeared to maintain a chordate shape and gametangium production similar to mock-dosed plants, those dosed with 500 nM or $1 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathsf { C r C L V } 3 \mathsf { p }$ exhibited an elliptical shape and a lack of archegonia (Figure 4a–d). Upon further investigation of samples with stained nuclei, we discovered that 500 nM CrCLV3p-treated plants (1175 cells 355) and $1 ~ \mu \mu$ CrCLV3p-treated plants (1292 cells $\pm \nobreakspace 2 6 1$ ) significantly increased cell number compared to plants grown on mock media (908 cells 194) (Figure 4e–i). The marginal meristems of gametophytes exposed to $1 \mu \mu \tau$ were remarkably wide (Figure 4h) as opposed to the gradual decrease in cell density seen in mock-treated plants due to cell differentiation (Figure 4e,h). 

Gametophytes dosed with CrCLV3p experienced a dosage-dependent inhibition of antheridium production at 500 nM and $1 \mu \mathrm { m } \mathsf { C r C L V } 3 \mathsf { p }$ , with fewer antheridia seen near the meristem (Figure 4j). The most dramatic effect of CrCLV3p was the sharp decrease in archegonium production; CrCLV3p was able to completely abolish archegonium development at 500 nM or 1 lM CrCLV3p (Figure 4k). Taken 

together, these results show that Ceratopteris gametophytes can perceive exogenous CrCLV3p at concentrations as low as 500 nM, to which they respond by promoting cell proliferation in the meristem area and inhibiting sexual organ development. 

In contrast to the effect of CrCLV3p, application with CrTDIFp at $5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ or $1 ~ \mu \mu$ did not result in noticeable changes to gametophyte morphology (Figure S4a–c). Upon quantification of nuclei and organ production, CrTDIFp failed to induce differences compared to mock-treated plants (Figure S4d–f). Due to the inability of TDIFp to affect the meristem architecture or archegonium formation, we used TDIFp as a peptide treatment control in later experiments. 

# Pulse-dosing with CLV3p causes double meristem formation by promoting stem cell identity

Treatment with CrCLV3p for a prolonged duration resulted in increased meristem activity and inhibition of reproductive structure formation. To dissect the effect of CrCLV3p on cell proliferation and gametangium development, we exposed gametophytes to CrCLV3p in two growth periods: during early growth (0–8 dpp) with subsequent basal medium recovery to target meristem formation and vegetative growth, or during late growth only (8–13 dpp) to target reproductive development. 

Multiple phenotypes were unveiled in gametophytes treated during early growth. First, a striking new phenotype, double meristem, emerged among $20 \text{‰}$ of the gametophytes treated with all concentrations of $\mathsf { C r C L V 3 p }$ during early growth (Figure 5a,b) that was never seen in the mock-treated samples (Figure 5a) and in only $1 1 - 1 2 \%$ of plants exposed to CrCLV3p concentrations 500 nM or above for the whole duration of 0–13 dpp growth (Figure S5). Only those exposed to 500 nM CrCLV3p showed a slightly higher number of cells $( 1 1 5 9 \pm 2 7 0 )$ compared to mock-treated plants $( 9 7 3 \pm 1 5 7 )$ (Figure 5c). Third, 500 nM dosage was also the only concentration that increased antheridium production, an effect not seen in plants continuously exposed to the peptide (Figures 4j and 5d). Lastly, archegonium production was unaffected (Figure 4e).

# CLV3 signaling downregulates the expression of CrWOX genes

WOX transcription factors function in feedback loops with CLE peptides in Arabidopsis meristems (Brand et al., 2000; 

Schlegel et al., 2021; Stahl et al., 2009), although it is not known if such a regulatory partnership exists in Ceratopteris. As Ceratopteris CrWOXA and CrWOXB were expressed in the gametophyte meristem (Figure S6), we sought to examine the participation of all known CrWOX genes in response to CrCLV3p. We performed RT-qPCR on samples exposed to either mock treatment or 1 lM CrCLV3p from 0 to 13 dpp. The expression levels of CrWOXA, CrWOXB, and CrWOX13A were significantly downregulated by CrCLV3p treatment, while CrWUL and CrWOX13B were not affected (Figure 7a). Within the same samples, the expression of CrCLV3 and two similar CrCLE genes (CrCLE40 and CrCLE2) were also measured. CrCLV3 was dramatically downregulated in response to CrCLV3p treatment, while CrCLE40 and CrCLE2 remained unchanged, which suggests Ceratopteris gametophytes have a feedback mechanism to control the levels of CrCLV3p (Figure 7a). The control CLE peptide TDIFp induced a different response, leading to an increase in CrWOX13A expression and a decrease in CrCLV3 expression (Figure 7b). 

The response of multiple CrWOX and CrCLE genes to CrCLV3p treatment led us to further examine their expression levels during the windows of CrCLV3p effect on meristem proliferation (6–8 dpp) and on archegonium inhibition (8–10 dpp). We additionally incorporated the protein synthesis inhibitor cycloheximide (CHX) to exclude the effects of altered protein levels downstream of CrCLV3p signaling. When gametophytes were exposed to $1 \mu \mu \tau$ from 6 to 8 dpp, CrWOXA and CrWOXB expression did not change compared to mock treatment with or without 10 lM CHX (Figure 7c). In contrast, CrCLV3 expression was significantly lower in CrCLV3p-treated plants than in the mock-treated group in the absence and presence of CHX (Figure 7c). Together, these results indicate that the response of CrWOXA to CrCLV3p occurs after the 6–8 dpp developmental timeframe, which suggests CrWOXA is not responsible for the change in cell proliferation at the meristem. Conversely, the feedback mechanism responsible for downregulating the endogenous CrCLV3 gene in response to CrCLV3p treatment does not require protein synthesis.

# DISCUSSION

CLE peptides are among the most well-characterized small signaling peptide families in angiosperms, due to their 

important functions in regulating stem cell proliferation at shoot and root meristems, as well as in the vascular cambium (Hirakawa et al., 2010; Schoof et al., 2000; Stahl et al., 2009). In recent years, studies in moss and liverwort have revealed the conserved role of CLEs in coordinating cell division planes and influencing stem cell growth (Hirakawa et al., 2019, 2020; Nemec-Venza et al., 2022; Whitewoods et al., 2018). The CLE gene family has expanded over evolutionary time, with angiosperms having a far higher number of CLE genes than bryophytes (7 in P. patens and 2 in M. polymorpha, 33 in Arabidopsis) (Carbonnel et al., 2023; Clark et al., 1995; Whitewoods et al., 2018). Consistent with this, we identified 11 loci in Ceratopteris encoding CLEs which separated into four clusters: those like moss CLEs in Group 1, two genes encoding the exact TDIF mature peptide in Group 2, several genes grouped with AtCLV3, and a single gene grouped with AtCLE40 (Figure 1a). The predicted mature CrCLV3 peptide differed from AtCLV3 by one residue, a level of homology not seen between AtCLV3 and either moss or liverwort CLEs. 

CrCLV3 expression was detected in the marginal meristem of hermaphrodites (Figure 2e–g), similar to the expression pattern of M. polymorpha CLV3 homolog MpCLE2 at the apical notch (Hirakawa, 2022). Reduction in CrCLV3 expression by RNAi was able to reduce cell proliferation (Figure 3e), a likely consequence of lessened meristematic activity. Surprisingly, CrCLV3 was also expressed in developing antheridia (Figure 2h,i) and crclv3kd lines experienced a delay in antheridium formation during later development (Figure 3f). The Ceratopteris antheridium undergoes several asymmetric cell divisions to form the differentiated ring, cap, and spermatogenous cells of the antheridia (Gifford & Adriance, 1989; Withers, Falls, et al., 2023). Thus, it is possible that reduced expression of CrCLV3 could impact the ability of somatic cells of the prothallus to complete the asymmetric divisions necessary to form a functional antheridium. Future studies examining the organization of cell divisions in crclv3 plants may reveal the precise defects responsible for the crclv3kd antheridium phenotype. 

Application of exogenous CrCLV3p caused drastic morphological changes opposite to those in crclv3kd lines, including expansion of the marginal meristem reminiscent of the fasciated SAM in Arabidopsis clv3 mutants (Brand et al., 2000). Our results indicate that CrCLV3 may act to promote cell proliferation of the stem cells (Figure 4a–i), an opposite response compared to Arabidopsis CLV3pinduced suppression of stem cell proliferation in the SAM. The function of CrCLV3 in the gametophyte provides further support for the idea that the ancestral function of CLV3-like peptides is stem-cell promotion proposed by Hirakawa (2022). Interestingly, the effect of CrCLV3p on cell proliferation was most effective during a brief window of 

development from 6 to 8 dpp when the marginal meristem has been specified but has not yet generated any archegonia (Figure 5a–e). During this same window of time, pulsedosage of CrCLV3p was able to induce the formation of multiple meristems as a consequence of increasing the number of cells participating in active cell divisions (Figures 5b and 6d), similar to the effects of MpCLE2p on liverwort branching (Hirakawa et al., 2020). Characterizing the timing of marginal initial specification by viewing the in situ changes of CrCLV3 and auxin biosynthesis gene expression during double meristem formation would be informative. As in situ hybridization is not quantitative, other methods such as a CrCLV3 promoter-driven reporter should be used in the future. As CrCLV3 is expressed in the marginal meristem cells during this period, it is possible that CrCLV3p acts as an autocrine signal received by marginal initial cells. An alternative possibility is that the marginal initials and surrounding cells are unable to perceive $\mathsf { C r C L V 3 p }$ , while the cells outside of the meristem express a receptor capable of binding to CrCLV3p, thereby leading to downregulation of CrCLV3 outside of the meristem, thus limiting the number of proliferating cells. The latter hypothesis is supported by our observation that dosage with CrCLV3p downregulated endogenous CrCLV3 gene expression (Figure 7a,c). 

It is crucial that the localization of the Ceratopteris CLV1/BAM receptor homologs and additional co-receptors be identified to clarify the directionality of CrCLV3p action on the gametophyte meristem cells. As we did not find evidence of CrCLV3p-induced expression changes for CrWOXA or CrWOXB in the absence of protein synthesis prior to 8 dpp (Figure 7c,d), it is possible CrCLV3 functions in conjunction with different transcription factors to influence meristem proliferation, such as M. polymorpha MpCLE2p and its downstream effector MpJINGASA (Takahashi et al., 2023). Future studies investigating upstream regulatory factors that establish CrCLV3 expression in the marginal meristem may connect the response of marginal cells to CrCLV3p with transcription factors determining meristem cell proliferation in Ceratopteris. 

A recent study by Geng et al. (2022) of cell proliferation dynamics in the hermaphrodite gametophyte proposed that a mobile signal must be generated from the marginal meristem that determines the spatial arrangement of archegonia. Archegonia always occur near the marginal meristem in undamaged plants and will cease to form when the original meristem is ablated, instead beginning to form once a new meristem is specified elsewhere (Geng et al., 2022; Withers, Kvamme, et al., 2023). CrCLV3 was expressed in the marginal meristem and exposure to exogenous CrCLV3p greatly disrupted normal archegonium formation (Figures 2f,g, 4d,h and 5g,j), making CrCLV3 a candidate for the positional signal-directing archegonium formation. Furthermore, peptide treatment 

during the late growth period, but not in the early growth period, affected the number of archegonia (Figure 5e,j), which suggests that only cells at a particular state can acquire archegonium cell fate. 

CLE signaling pathways involving WOX transcription factors have been viewed as a derived trait restricted to seed plant lineages, due to the lack of evidence for WOX function in CLE signaling in bryophytes P. patens and M. polymorpha (Hirakawa et al., 2020; Sakakibara et al., 2014; Whitewoods et al., 2018). Ceratopteris gametophytes dosed with CrCLV3p for 13 days exhibited significant downregulation of multiple WOX genes, including members of both the T1WOX clade (CrWOX13A) and T3WOX clade (CrWOXA and CrWOXB) (Figure 7a). However, when peptide treatment was restricted to 8–10 dpp gametophytes, it revealed that only CrWOXA was down-regulated in the absence of protein synthesis (Figure 7d). As CrWOXA expression was detected in the meristem (Figure S6), we propose that cells expressing CrCLV3 produce CrCLV3p, which diffuses to nearby cells and provides information about the location of the meristem, leading to archegonium production interior to the marginal meristem initials without consuming the initial cells and arresting growth. Interestingly, Arabidopsis WUS is expressed in the nucellus during ovule development and promotes proper development of the integument via a non-cell-autonomous signal (Gross-Hardt et al., 2002). It is possible that the CLE-T3WOX clade signaling during the development of female reproductive structures we observed in Ceratopteris is conserved among vascular plants. The relationship between CrCLV3p and CrWOXA is a new avenue for future studies examining the existence of feedback loops from CrWOXA back to CrCLV3 and the role of CrWOXA in female gametangium development. 

Taken together, we propose a model (Figure 8) for CrCLV3 activity in the marginal meristem of the gametophyte. Prior to 6 dpp of development, the effects of CrCLV3p are minimal, as the marginal meristem initials have not been established. Upon meristem specification, CrCLV3 is expressed in the marginal initial cells, promoting anticlinal divisions of these cells and presumably diffusing into the surrounding meristem area. Under normal conditions, daughter cells are eventually pushed outside the range of CrCLV3 and begin differentiation into somatic prothallium cells and antheridia (cells displaced to the left and right sides) or archegonia (cells displaced interior to the meristem). When grown on exogenous CrCLV3p, cell proliferation is increased, and more cells maintain the undifferentiated state of the marginal initials. The presence of CrCLV3p outside of its normal diffusion range also prevents daughter cells of the marginal meristem from attaining archegonium cell fate, accompanied by downregulation of CrWOXA. CrCLV3p dosage after meristem specification increases the number of marginal initial-like cells.

Upon release from exogenous $\mathsf { C r C L V 3 p }$ after 8 dpp, meristem boundary regulation returns to the meristem and represses the marginal initial identity of cells internal to the widened meristem, resulting in the eventual separation of marginal cells into two distinct meristems. Factors determining marginal meristem size restrictions may include auxin biosynthesis genes expressed on either side of the meristem (Withers, Kvamme, et al., 2023). 

In conclusion, the action of CLV3 homologs to promote gametophyte stem cell identity appears to be conserved between liverworts and ferns. CrCLV3 may also provide positional information about the meristem to the rest of the plant body to coordinate gametangium production, an aspect of gametophyte CLE functions not previously described. Our work also provides the first evidence for WOX-CLE signaling in a seed-free plant, placing the evolution of this regulatory relationship much earlier in land plant evolution. Additionally, the connection between Ceratopteris CLE and WOX genes detected here was observed in the gametophyte generation, which suggests WOX-CLE signaling is not restricted to the sporophyte generation in vascular plants. 

# EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# Sequence identification and classification

Azolla filiculoides and S. cucullata CLE sequences were identified using Fernbase (Li et al., 2018) and BLASTP queries with representative Arabidopsis sequences from the groupings defined by Goad et al. (2017) (see Table S1), with a relaxed expected threshold of 10 to capture hits to the CLE motif. Arabidopsis and fern CLE 

sequences were used as queries for the Ceratopteris predicted proteome (Marchant et al., 2021) using the same method. An additional search in a de novo transcriptome yielded one additional Ceratopteris CLE that was not annotated in the genome. All fern CLE sequences were manually verified to contain C-terminal CLE motifs within their predicted protein sequences. Annotation information for sequences used in this study are provided in Table S1. 

CLEs were classified by grouping sequences into predicted functional groups using BLAST+ 2.8.1 legacy blast PERL scripts (Camacho et al., 2009) to determine high-scoring pairs (HSP) and the Java executable of CLANS (Frickey & Lupas, 2004) to cluster sequences based on their HSP values. For clustering with only the CLE motifs or pre-propeptide sequences, CLANS settings were as follows: the e-value was set to 1E-4 and the P-value set to 1.2E-9. Clusters were determined by the convex clustering option, with a standard deviation cutoff of 0.5 and a minimum of 2 sequences per cluster. Representations of residues in the CLE motif were generated using WebLogo 3 (Crooks et al., 2004). 

CrCLE and CrWOX promoter sequences were defined as 2000 bp upstream of the start codon for Ceratopteris sequences deposited in Phytozome (Goodstein et al., 2012; Marchant et al., 2021), except in the case of CrWOXA and CrWUL which already had defined promoters as described by Yu et al. (2020). PlantPAN3.0 (Chow et al., 2019) was used to predict cis-element binding motifs based on database entries from Arabidopsis and two custom elements defined by Yu et al. (2020) (a Ceratopteris auxin response element: GAGACA and a CrWOXA binding site: TAATGG). Heatmaps and Venn diagrams were generated using the R packages “dplyr” (Wickham et al., 2023), “ggplot2” (Wickham, 2016), and “ggvenn” (Yan, 2023). 

# Plant growth conditions

Ceratopteris richardii strain RN3 (Carolina Biological Supply Company, Burlington, NC, USA) was used as a wild-type in this study. Wild-type and crclv3kd spores were surface sterilized as described previously (Withers, Falls, et al., 2023). Spores were plated on basal media (½ strength Murashige and Skoog salts, pH 6.0, $0 . 8 \%$ agar, and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ ampicillin) or peptide media (basal media supplemented with synthetic CLE peptide CrCLV3p or TDIFp (Biomatik, Wilmington, DE, USA), sequences provided in Figure 1). Plants were grown under humidity domes at $\scriptscriptstyle { 2 6 ^ { \circ } C }$ with a light/dark cycle of 16 h/8 h using $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } \ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s e c } ^ { - 1 }$ fluorescent light intensity. Plants were inverted after 8 dpp to avoid self-fertilization. For postfertilization samples, plates with 13 dpp gametophytes were flipped lid-side up and flooded with sterile water to induce fertilization. Sporophytes were generated by moving individual hermaphrodites in 24-well cell culture plates containing basal media, and fertilization was triggered by the application of sterile water to each well. Sporophytes with six leaves were transferred to basal media without agar for 2 weeks to encourage rooting, followed by transfer to PRO-MIX $\textcircled{8}$ All-Purpose Soil Mix (Premier Tech Horticulture, Quakertown, PA, USA) to grow at room temperature with a light/dark cycle of $1 6 \ : \mathsf { h } / 8$ h under $8 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } } ~ { \mathsf { m } } ^ { - 2 } { \mathsf { s e c } } ^ { - 1 }$ fluorescent light. 

Gametophytes grown for 0–8 and 8–13 dpp peptide treatment or cycloheximide (CHX; ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) experiments were germinated on sterilized cellophane to facilitate transfer between media types. Hermaphrodite gametophytes exposed to short-term pulse-dose peptide experiments were transferred between media types by hand using an 18-gauge needle. 

# Stable and transient transformation

A 326 bp fragment of CrCLV3 was cloned into the pENTR entry vector and then recombined into the pH7GWIWG2 RNAi 

destination vector using Gateway technology, as described previously (Bui et al., 2017; Curtis & Grossniklaus, 2003). The resulting RNAi vector was introduced to the Agrobacterium strain GV3101 from Escherichia coli with an E. coli helper strain containing the pRK 2013 plasmid (Bui et al., 2015). Transformation of gametophyte tissue using Agrobacterium tumefaciens was performed as described previously in Bui et al. (2015). Successfully transformed gametophytes $( \mathsf { T } _ { 0 } )$ were selected with $5 ~ \mu 9 ~ \mathsf { m } ! ^ { - 1 }$ hygromycin, and resistant gametophytes were transferred to 24-well culture plates with basal media for self-fertilization. ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ sporophytes were grown as described above and spores were harvested from 31 independent transgenic lines. Five lines were used for verification of knockdown in ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ sporophytes. Phenotyping of ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ gametophytes from three transgenic lines are presented here. 

# In situ hybridization

A 326 bp fragment of the CrCLV3 coding region was cloned and inserted into the pENTR/D-TOPO vector (Invitrogen, Waltham, MA, USA), and the plasmid DNA was used as a template for labeled RNA probes. A total of 314 bp DIG-labeled RNA probes were generated for CrCLV3 as described previously (Youngstrom et al., 2019). The same technique was used to clone a 461 bp region of the CrWOXA coding region, followed by generation of 461 bp RNA probes. Similarly, CrWOXB in situ probes were generated using an existing clone (Youngstrom et al., 2019). All primer sequences for cloning and in situ probe templates can be found in Table S2. Gametophyte tissue was harvested at 7, 10, or 13 dpp from plants grown on basal media and fixed by incubation in FAA (formaldehyde:ethanol:acetic acid, $3 . 7 \% : 5 0 \% : 5 \ \mathsf { v } / \mathsf { v } )$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at room temperature. Tissue was stored in $70 \%$ ethanol at $\mathtt { - 2 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ until use. CrCLV3 in situ hybridization was performed as described previously in Youngstrom et al. (2019) with the following modifications: probe concentration was 1 ng $\mathsf { m } \mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 }$ hybridization solution. CrWOXB and CrWOXA in situ hybridization was performed as described in Withers, Kvamme, et al. (2023). Samples were viewed on a Zeiss compound light microscope and imaged with a Zeiss Axiocam ERc 5s digital camera (Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). Two biological replicates were performed for each probe set. 

# RT-qPCR analyses

Wild-type expression of CrCLV3 transcripts was measured in 7, 10, and 13 dpp tissues harvested and flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The 30–50 mg samples were stored at $\mathtt { - 8 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ until usage. For knockdown verification of crclv3kd transformant lines, ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ sporophytes were grown until the first sporophyll (leaf 13) emerged. Leaf 13 was harvested at a total length (from plant base to tip) of 8–12 cm. Gametophyte samples exposed to long-term CrCLV3p or TDIFp dosage were germinated on media supplemented with synthetic peptide or mock treatment $\left( { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } \right)$ and grown for 13 days before harvesting as described above. Gametophytes pulse-dosed with $\mathsf { C r C L V 3 p }$ or mock treatment were germinated on a layer of cellophane over basal media, transferred to either $1 \mu \mu \tau r \tau L \nu 3 \rho$ (6–8 dpp pulse-dose) or 500 nM CrCLV3p (8–10 dpp pulse-dose) for $4 8 \ \mathrm { h } _ { i }$ , then immediately harvested. The same procedure was used for gametophytes exposed to $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ CHX during the pulsedose experiments, with an additional CHX pre-treatment for 30 min, after which gametophytes were moved to their respective treatment regimens $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ CHX or $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ CHX and 500 nM or $1 ~ \mu \mu$ CrCLV3p). 

Frozen tissue was ground with a mortar and pestle, followed by RNA extraction with the Quick RNA Miniprep (Plus) kit (Zymo Research, Irvine, CA, USA). A 500 ng template was used to generate cDNA via Mu-MLV reverse transcriptase (New England 

Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) and N9 random primers (IDT, Coralville, IA, USA). RT-qPCR was performed as previously described by Withers, Kvamme, et al. (2023). Primers for RT-qPCR are listed in Table S2. A melting curve analysis was performed and analyzed to detect non-specific products after amplification. Fold change expression was calculated using the DDCt method using CrUBQ for internal control (Schmittgen $\&$ Livak, 2008). $N \geq 2$ for gametophyte samples post-fertilization. $N \geq 3$ for all other RT-qPCR analyses presented. Two technical replicates were performed for each biological replicate. 

# Phenotyping assays

The crclv3kd lines were grown on basal media for 7, 10, or 13 days, cleared in $100 \%$ ethanol, and stained with Hoechst 33342 as described previously to visualize nuclei (Youngstrom et al., 2022). Plants were mounted in $50 \%$ glycerol and imaged with a Leica stereomicroscope (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany) and a Qicam camera (Qimaging, Surrey, BC, Canada) using a DAPI filter. Composite images of 13 dpp gametophytes were generated using the Auto Align Layers (Reposition Projection setting) tool in Adobe Photoshop (Adobe-Inc., 2022) to align multiple images. Images were prepared for object identification using custom ImageJ macros for background signal minimization and removal of nongametophyte objects as described previously and available on Github (Withers, 2021; Youngstrom et al., 2022). A subsample of three images from each treatment, line, or timepoint was manually counted using the Cell Count tool in FIJI (Schindelin et al., 2012) to calibrate the CellProfiler pipeline for image processing. In cases of test images not scoring within $\pm 5 0$ cells of manual count values, images were brightened by adjusting the maximum value in FIJI within the range of 15–36 for 7 and 10 dpp images or 5–15 for composite 13 dpp images. A minimum sample size of 15 gametophytes were processed for all cell-counting experiments. Antheridia and archegonia were manually scored using the Cell Count tool in FIJI. 

Classification of CrCLV3p-treated gametophytes as normal (less than ${ \mathfrak { g } } 0 ^ { \circ }$ between lobes), wide (greater than $9 0 ^ { \circ }$ between lobes), or double meristems were analyzed in FIJI. First, the Straight-Line tool was used to create a $1 5 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ line extending from the central marginal meristem cell, followed by a perpendicular line. The Angle tool was used to mark the intersection of gametophyte tissue with the left side of the perpendicular line, the base of the 150-micron line, and the right side of the perpendicular line, thus creating an angle describing the meristem. Gametophytes with archegonia formation distal to the meristem or meristems with two angles not separated by differentiated tissue were also classified as wide. 

Aniline blue staining solution was prepared as described in Zavaliev and Epel (2015). Fresh 8 dpp gametophyte tissue was submerged in stain for $1 \ \mathsf { h } ,$ , transferred directly to a microscope slide, and imaged using a Nikon D3200 DSLR camera (Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) through the ocular of a Zeiss microscope (excitation filter $4 8 8 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , dichroic mirror $5 1 0 \ \mathsf { n m } _ { i }$ , and emission filter $5 2 0 \ \mathsf { n m } _ { \cdot }$ with an ISO setting of 200 and exposure time of 8 sec. Images were processed in FIJI by adjusting the minimum and maximum pixel intensity values to 15 and 125, respectively. Contiguous cells with bright staining on all cell walls were classified as “meristem cells.” 

# Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were performed in GraphPad Prism 10.0.2 (GraphPad-Software, 2024). RT-qPCR analysis of CrCLV3 expression in 7, 10, and 13 dpp wild-type gametophytes and 

post-fertilization time-course were tested using one-way ANOVA with Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test. Evaluation of crclv3kd phenotypes in gametophyte tissue used two-way ANOVA with Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test. One-way ANOVA with Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test was used for quantification of cell number and organ number in CrCLV3p-dosed gametophytes and number of aniline blue-stained marginal meristem cells, except in the case of organ numbers during 0–8 and 8–13 dpp, in which Kruskal–Wallis with Dunn’s multiple comparisons test was used instead. To compare the frequency of normal, wide, and double meristems, a Fisher’s exact test was performed with Bonferroni correction. For RT-qPCR relative quantification of CrWOX and CrCLE expression in 0–13 dpp CrCLV3p-dosed gametophytes, multiple t-tests with Holm–Sid- ak correction were performed. RT-qPCR data from gametophytes dosed with CrCLV3p in combination with cycloheximide were tested using one-way ANOVA with Sid- ak’s multiple comparisons test.
