# ABSTRACT

The fruit of chestnut rose (Rosa roxburghii Tratt.) contains exceptionally high levels of L-ascorbic acid (AsA) (∼1762 mg/100 g fresh weight), approximately 40-fold higher than those found in sweet orange (Citrus sinensis), which is well known for its high AsA content. However, the molecular mechanisms driving such high accumulation in chestnut rose remain unclear. Here, we report that the genes R. roxburghii PECTIN METHYLESTERASE (RroxPME), D-GALACTURONATE REDUCTASE (RroxGalUR), and DEHYDROASCOR-BATE REDUCTASE 2 (RroxDHAR2) play crucial roles in AsA accumulation in chestnut rose fruit. By comparing R. roxburghii with the closely related Rosa multiflora, which has low AsA concentrations, we identified a 545-bp insertion in the promoter of RroxGalUR. We found that TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 2 (RroxTTG2), a well-known key regulator of trichome development, binds to the W-box-containing inserted region of the RroxGalUR promoter as well as the promoters of RroxPME and RroxDHAR2. In contrast, in sweet orange, CsTTG2 can bind only to CsPME. Furthermore, RroxTTG2 retains its conserved role in the regulation of trichome development during early fruit development, suggesting its spatiotemporal specificity in regulating both trichome development and AsA biosynthesis. To evaluate the application value of this pathway in other species, we heterologously expressed RroxTTG2, RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 in lettuce (Lactuca sativa L.), which increased AsA concentrations in the transgenic lines by up to $355 \%$ (an increase from approximately 2 to 10 mg/100 g fresh weight). This study provides insights into mechanisms underlying AsA accumulation in chestnut rose and the spatiotemporal transcriptional regulation of AsA biosynthesis and trichome development.

# INTRODUCTION

L-ascorbic acid (AsA; also known as vitamin C) enhances plant resistance to biotic and abiotic stress, making it a crucial antioxidant in plants (Smirnoff and Wheeler, 2000; Chew et al., 2003; Drouin et al., 2011; Noshi et al., 2016). Humans lack the ability to produce AsA due to mutations in the gene encoding the final enzyme in the biosynthesis pathway, L-gulonolactone oxidase (Chatterjee et al., 1960; Nishikimi et al., 1994); therefore, 

agricultural crops rich in AsA are important dietary sources of this key antioxidant. AsA concentrations vary widely in food crops, although there are many well-known crops with high AsA levels, including kiwifruit (Actinidia deliciosa) (Nishiyama et al., 2004; Liu et al., 2022) and sweet orange (Ma et al., 2020). In most plant species, the L-galactose pathway is the primary pathway for AsA biosynthesis, although genes in three other pathways also play significant roles in AsA biosynthesis (Wheeler et al., 1998; Agius et al., 2003; Bulley and Laing, 2016;

Ishikawa et al., 2018; Broad et al., 2020; Smirnoff and Wheeler, 2024). 

Chestnut rose (Rosa roxburghii Tratt.) is distinguished by its unique fruit morphology, with a surface densely covered with trichomes (fruit spines). This fruit also accumulates extremely high AsA levels, reaching $1 0 0 0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \ \mathsf { m g } / 1 0 0 \ \mathsf { g }$ fresh weight (FW), which is approximately 25-fold higher than AsA levels in kiwifruit (Actinidia deliciosa, ${ \sim } 8 0 \ \mathrm { m g } / 1 0 0 \ \mathsf { g } \ \mathsf { F W }$ ) (Nishiyama et al., 2004), 40-fold higher than those in sweet orange $( \sim 5 0 \ : \mathrm { m g } / 1 0 0 \ : \mathrm { g } \ : \mathsf { F W } ) \ : \emptyset$ Xu et al., 2013; Ma et al., 2020), and 100-fold higher than those in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum L.; ${ \sim } 2 0 \mathrm { m g } / 1 0 0 \mathrm { g } \mathrm { F W } )$ (Huang et al., 2014). Although AsA concentrations in R. roxburghii fruit are high, mechanisms underlying this accumulation remain poorly understood. Due to limitations of stable genetic transformation, only a few genes have been confirmed to positively influence AsA biosynthesis through heterologous transformation, including GDP-D-MANNOSE-3’,5’-EPIMERASE (GME) (Huang et al., 2014), GDP-MANNOSE PYROPHOSPHORYLASE (GMP), GDP-L-GALACTOSE PHOSPHORYLASE (GGP), DEHYDROASCORBATE REDUCTASE (DHAR) (Huang et al., 2014), and L-GALACTOSE-1, 4-LACTONE DEHYDROGENASE (GalLDH) (Liu et al., 2013; Zong et al., 2023). Additionally, with the publication of the R. roxburghii genome and transcriptome, an increasing number of candidate genes with significant roles in the accumulation of high AsA levels have been identified (Zong et al., 2023). In addition, recent studies have found that the transcription factors cycling DNA binding of One Finger (DOF) factor 3 (CDF3), elongated hypocotyl 5 (HY5), basic leucine zipper (bZIP), and WRKY family members positively regulate AsA biosynthesis in R. roxburghii (Huang et al., 2024; Su et al., 2025). The WRKY transcription factor family in plants regulates stress response genes, secondary metabolic pathways, and hormone signaling networks, coordinating dualdefense mechanisms against biotic and abiotic stress while also participating in molecular cascades involved in developmental regulation (Javed and Gao, 2023; Li et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025). However, limitations in establishing stable genetic lines in R. roxburghii have prevented a comprehensive understanding of key candidate genes and pathways, and mechanisms driving the accumulation of high levels of AsA in R. roxburghii remain unclear. 

In addition to antioxidants such as AsA, plants have a variety of defense mechanisms to protect against abiotic and biotic stress (Conklin, 2010; Smirnoff, 2011). Trichomes, a common specialized structure on plant aerial organs, provide protection against herbivores and insects, as well as increase epidermal thickness to reduce heat and water loss (Tiwari and Pragya, 2016; Fambrini and Pugliesi, 2019; Chalvin et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024). Trichomes can be unicellular or multicellular, and they can be characterized as glandular or non-glandular based on their secretory ability (Chalvin et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024). The development of different trichome types is regulated by unique mechanisms, such as the classic R2R3-MYB/basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH)/WD-40 repeat transcription factor complex (Arabidopsis thaliana [At] GLABROUS 1 (GL1; AtGL1)-GLABRA 3 (AtGL3)/ENHANCER OF GLABRA 3 (AtEGL3)-TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 1 (AtTTG1), MBW) model in A. thaliana (L.) Heynh. (Pattanaik et al., 2014; Berhin et al., 2022), the WOOLLY and HAIR genes in tomato (Yang et al., 2011; Chang et al., 2018), Cucumis sativus (Cs) GL1 (CsGL1) and CsGL3 in cucumber (C. sativus L.) 

(Li et al., 2015a; Pan et al., 2015), and Lactuca serriola (Ls) WOX-SPINE 1 (LsWS1) in prickly lettuce (L. serriola L.) (Sun et al., 2024). The development of trichomes covering the surface of chestnut rose fruit is regulated by specific genes (Ma et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021). The transgenic expression of RroxGL1 can restore trichome formation in Arabidopsis gl1 mutants (Huang et al., 2019). It remains unclear whether the unique trichome structure of chestnut rose fruit is associated with their high AsA levels. 

Gene editing strategies for regulatory elements and the heterogeneous expression of a pathway to create new genotypes with desirable traits in crops are currently receiving a great deal of attention. In lettuce, editing upstream open reading frames (uORFs) of the GDP-L-GALACTOSE PHOSPHORYLASE 2 (LsGGP2) gene can increase AsA concentrations in leaves (Zhang et al., 2018). In rice (Oryza sativa L.), the high-efficiency multi-gene vector system TransGene Stacking II (TGS II) was used to create ‘‘aSTARice,’’ which accumulates high levels of astaxanthin (Zhu et al., 2018). In Artemisia annua L., seven genes were combined using multi-gene assembly technology to promote glandular trichome development and reconstruct the artemisinin biosynthesis pathway (Li et al., 2023). However, applications specifically targeting mechanisms to enhance AsA accumulation for plant improvement remain limited. 

Here, we explored the mechanism responsible for high AsA levels in chestnut rose fruit and identified a cis-acting sequence that alters the regulation of a key gene, thus linking AsA biosynthesis with trichome development. Our findings provide a theoretical basis for the potential improvement of other fruits with lower AsA levels.

# RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 promote AsA accumulation

Although genes in the D-galacturonic acid pathway have been identified, a detailed understanding of their functions remains limited (Xu et al., 2013). Previous studies confirmed that GalUR gene expression is associated with changes in AsA levels in strawberry fruit during ripening and varies among different Fragaria species (Agius et al., 2003). Heterologous overexpression supported that Fragaria ananassa (Fa) GalUR (FaGalUR) and RroxDHAR2 may promote AsA accumulation (Agius et al., 2003; Huang et al., 2014; Cai et al., 2015). Here, we identified candidate genes (RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2) that were positively correlated with AsA accumulation, which was confirmed by quantitative PCR (qPCR) and RNA-seq data (Figures 2A and 2B). Compared with the control group (wild type [WT]), transgenic expression of RroxPME, RroxGalUR, or RroxDHAR2 genes in lettuce leaves 

resulted in average increases in AsA concentration of 1.0- to 1.7-fold, 1.2- to 2.0-fold, and 0.8- to 2.7-fold, respectively (Figures 2C–2F). Similarly, transgenic expression of the candidate genes in Arabidopsis leaves resulted in maximum increases in AsA concentrations of 0.6-fold, 0.3-fold, and 0.3-fold (Supplemental Figure 3). Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) experiments revealed that Tobacco rattle virus (TRV)-RroxGalUR significantly reduced AsA concentrations (Supplemental Figure 4). These results suggest that RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 actively contribute to AsA accumulation, with RroxGalUR possibly playing a major role.

${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ is a stress response signaling molecule in plants (Qiao et al., 2024). In fruit grown in different regions, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels were inversely proportional to AsA concentrations, indicating that ambient temperatures did not induce stress in R. roxburghii fruit (Supplemental Figure 18A). A temperature simulation experiment further confirmed that AsA accumulation in R. roxburghii fruit responded to temperature changes and was inversely correlated with ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels (Supplemental Figure 18A). When plants were maintained at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 40 days, AsA concentrations were approximately 2.0-fold higher than those in plants grown at outdoor ambient (average temperature $2 7 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } { - } 3 6 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } ;$ Supplemental Table 10; Supplemental Figures 18B and 18C), reaching approximately 2763 mg/100 g FW (Supplemental Figure 18C). RNA-seq and qPCR analyses revealed that RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 were upregulated in response to low temperature exposure confirming these genes crucial roles in AsA accumulation in R. roxburghii (Supplemental Figure 19). 

Notably, RroxTTG2 was found to bind not only to the RroxGalUR promoter but also the RroxPME and RroxDHAR2 promoters, thereby activating the expression of these genes (Supplemental Figure 20). However, similar variations were not found in the promoter regions of PME, GalUR, or DHAR1 in citrus species (Supplemental Figure 21). Dual-LUC and yeast onehybrid assays showed that CsTTG2 could only bind to the CsPME promoter to activate its expression (Supplemental Figure 22). Additionally, simulated temperature treatments of C. sinensis cv. Tarocco fruit indicated that AsA accumulation in juice sacs did not significantly change in response to temperature fluctuations (Supplemental Figure 23). These results suggest that both the insertion in the RroxGalUR promoter and the regulation of multiple genes by RroxTTG2 may be key factors underlying the high AsA accumulation in R. roxburghii. 

# RroxTTG2 spatiotemporally regulates trichome development and AsA biosynthesis

Given that TTG2 is a key regulator of trichome development in plants (Johnson et al., 2002), we conducted gene expression analysis of RroxTTG2 at different stages of flower and fruit 

development to investigate its effect on trichome development and AsA accumulation (Figure 4A). RroxTTG2 was expressed at higher levels during the early stage (B_S1) of trichome development and was not significantly correlated with AsA accumulation during this period $( r _ { 1 } ~ = ~ 0 . 6 3 5$ , $p _ { 1 } ~ = ~ 0 . 5 6 2 )$ (Figure 4A). In contrast, during the fruit-development stages (S1–S4), RroxTTG2 expression was significantly correlated with AsA accumulation $( r _ { 2 } = 0 . 9 7 8$ , $p _ { 2 } = 0 . 0 2 2 )$ ) (Figure 4A). We next investigated the relationship between trichome development and AsA accumulation and confirmed that GL3 and EGL3, members of the MBW complex involved in the regulation of trichome development, bind to RroxTTG2 and activate its expression (Figure 4A; Supplemental Figure 24). However, the expression of RroxGL3 and RroxEGL3 was not significantly correlated with AsA accumulation throughout flower and fruit development (Figure 4A; Supplemental Figure 24). Notably, RroxGL3 exhibited a relatively higher correlation with AsA accumulation during the B_S1–B_S3 stages than RroxEGL3 (Figure 4A; Supplemental Figure 24). Therefore, in addition to elucidating the role of RroxTTG2 in trichome development and AsA accumulation, further study of RroxGL3 may provide additional insights into the potential link between these two traits. 

To further verify the involvement of RroxTTG2 in AsA regulation, we used VIGS to silence TTG2 in R. roxburghii fruit and examined its effects on AsA biosynthesis. Seven days after VIGS injection, RroxTTG2 expression was significantly reduced and the AsA concentration decreased by $14 \%$ in pTRV-RroxTTG2 plants compared to plants infiltrated with the empty vector (EV) (Figure 4B). Leaf AsA concentrations increased by $3 8 \% { - } 8 0 \%$ and $5 4 \% - 8 2 \%$ (Figures 4C-E) and leaf trichome numbers increased by an average of $1 1 \% - 5 2 \%$ and $1 3 1 \% - 1 7 7 \%$ (Figures 4F and 4G), in transgenic Arabidopsis plants expressing RroxTTG2 and RroxGL3, respectively, compared to WT (Col-0). Consistently, trichome numbers and AsA concentrations in the leaves of Atttg2 (stock number: SALK_204777C) and Atgl3 (stock number: SALK_120957C) mutants were significantly lower than in Col-0 $( p _ { A t t t g 2 } = 1 . 4 4 \times 1 0 ^ { - 2 }$ , ${ p _ { A t g / 3 } = 5 . 3 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 3 } }$ (Figures 4F and 4G). Additionally, transgenic expression of RroxTTG2 and RroxGL3 rescued the defects in trichome numbers and AsA concentrations in the leaves of Atttg2 and Atgl3 Arabidopsis mutants, respectively (Figures 4D–4G). The heterologous overexpression of RroxGL3 and RroxTTG2 in Arabidopsis also enhanced anthocyanin accumulation (Supplemental Figure 25). 

In summary, RroxTTG2 exhibits temporal and spatial expression differences and cross-regulates both trichome development and AsA accumulation. Functional analyses confirmed that RroxGL3 and RroxTTG2 regulate both trichome development and AsA accumulation.

# DISCUSSION

In higher plants, at least four pathways are recognized as potentially participating in AsA biosynthesis. Among these, genes from the L-galactose pathway have been confirmed to play a significant role in AsA biosynthesis across multiple species (Agius et al., 2003; Bulley and Laing, 2016; Ishikawa et al., 2018). However, mechanisms underlying high AsA levels are not well 

understood. In many species, GGP expression has been shown to be regulated by uORFs, contributing to AsA accumulation (Zhang et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2023a; Mou et al., 2025). However, analyses of $5 ^ { \prime }$ untranslated regions (UTRs) of GGP in both R. roxburghii (with high AsA levels) and R. multiflora (with low AsA levels) revealed the presence of uORF sequences in both species (Supplemental Figure 27). This finding suggests that negative feedback regulation via uORFs may not be the key factor driving differential AsA accumulation between R. roxburghii and R. multiflora. This study showed that genes related to the D-galacturonic acid pathway (PME and GalUR), particularly GalUR, play critical roles in AsA accumulation in R. roxburghii. These findings suggest that the D-galacturonic acid pathway is important for AsA biosynthesis, as also observed in citrus and strawberry fruit (Agius et al., 2003; Xu et al., 2013; Cai et al., 2015). Additionally, the DHAR gene in the downstream cyclic regeneration pathway promotes AsA biosynthesis.

# METHODS

# Plant materials

Fruit from R. roxburghii (Guinong 5) was collected in 2019 from healthy, stable, fruit-bearing plants over five years of age that were grown outdoors in Guiyang, Guizhou Province. Samples were harvested at four key stages of fruit development: Rrox_S1–Rrox_S4 (30, 50, 60, and 80 days after flowering [DAF]). R. multiflora and R. chinensis ‘‘Old Blush’’ were cultivated outdoors at Huazhong Agricultural University. R. multiflora was sampled at four stages during fruit development (Rmul_S1– Rmul_S4). Mature fruit of different citrus varieties were collected from plants cultivated outdoors in Yunnan, Hunan, Chongqing, Guangxi, Guangdong, and Hubei Provinces and at the National Citrus Breeding Center at Huazhong Agricultural University (Wuhan, China). Sweet orange (C. sinensis cv. Tarocco) (XC_S1–XC_S5) and A. buxifolia (Abu_S1–Abu_S5) fruit were collected from plants cultivated outdoors at Huazhong Agricultural University. Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. These materials were frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ for future use. 

Nicotiana benthamiana was used for transient expression experiments. After germination, tobacco plants were transplanted to a growth room maintained at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ with a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod and grown for 4–5 weeks before use. 

# Temperature treatments

R. roxburghii fruit-bearing trees over four years old were selected for temperature treatment and were maintained either in artificial climate incubators or outdoors in 2022 (Supplemental Table 10). At 60 DAF, plants were transferred to an incubator $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 16 h light/8 h dark, $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) for 40 days, and samples were collected at 20 and 40 days. In field experiments, fruit was collected at 100 DAF in Guiyang (Guizhou Province), Laifeng County (Hubei Province) and Huazhong Agricultural University (Wuhan, Hubei Province) in 2023. Three trees with consistent growth potential were selected as biological replicates. All samples were frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . 

# AsA measurement

AsA and total AsA concentrations were measured using a previously described method (Huang et al., 2014). Briefly, $0 . 5 \ 9$ of sample was added to $5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of $2 \%$ (w/v) metaphosphoric acid solution, followed by ultrasonication at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min. The mixture was then centrifuged at 12000 rpm for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , and the supernatant was collected for further analysis. Subsequently, $8 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of supernatant was mixed with $4 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of $_ { 0 . 2 \textsf { M } }$ dithiothreitol and allowed to react at room temperature for 15 min. Next, $4 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of $2 \%$ metaphosphoric acid solution was added and mixed thoroughly to measure total AsA concentration. The resulting solution was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter and analyzed using an Agilent 1260 high-performance liquid chromatography instrument. A Shimadzu Wondasil C18 column $( 5 \mu \mathsf { m } _ { : }$ , $4 . 6 \times 1 5 0 \ : \mathrm { m m }$ ) was used for chromatographic separation, with $0 . 1 \%$ (w/v) metaphosphoric acid aqueous solution as mobile phase A and pure methanol as mobile phase B. The flow rate was set at $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ with a mobile phase composition of A:B at a volume ratio of 98:2. The injection volume was $1 0 \mu \up$ , and the injection temperature was maintained at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . Data were collected using an ultraviolet (UV) detector over a wavelength range of 195–540 nm, with absorbance quantification at a maximum absorption wavelength of 243 nm for AsA. Calibration curves were generated using vitamin C analytical standards. 

# Genome assembly and annotation

Genome assembly was performed for R. roxburghii Guinong 5 (GN-05) using 43.16 Gb of Nanopore data, 42.56 Gb of Hi-C data, and 21.78 Gb of 

Illumina data (Supplemental Table 1). NextDenovo (https://github.com/ Nextomics/NextDenovo) was used to assemble the Nanopore reads into contigs. NextPolish was used for error correction with Illumina reads (Hu et al., 2020). Corrected contigs were linked to scaffolds using the 3D-DNA pipeline with Hi-C reads (Dudchenko et al., 2017). Construction results were manually corrected using Juicebox (Durand et al., 2016), and 3D-DNA was used to generate the corrected pseudochromosome sequence. 

For genome annotation, gene models were generated based on ab initio gene prediction, homology searches, and RNA-seq data. For ab initio gene prediction, AUGUSTUS (Stanke et al., 2006) and GlimmerHMM (Majoros et al., 2004) were employed using default parameters. RNAseq reads were generated from a mixture of tissues (Supplemental Table 4). Trinity software was utilized to perform genome-guided and de novo transcriptome assembly. The BEAKER2 annotation strategy was applied for protein homology-based annotation (Br�una et al., 2021). PASA was used to update annotations of protein-coding genes by incorporating PASA alignment evidence (Haas et al., 2003), correcting exon boundaries, adding UTRs, and modeling alternative splicing based on PASA alignment assemblies. 

# RNA-seq and analysis

RNA-seq was performed on R. roxburghii fruit at different developmental stages, R. roxburghii fruit samples exposed to different temperatures, R. multiflora fruit development samples, C. sinensis cv. Tarocco fruit development samples, and A. buxifolia fruit development samples. Clean reads from R. roxburghii and R. multiflora var. multiflora were aligned to the R. roxburghii reference genome using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2019), and clean reads from C. sinensis blood orange and A. buxifolia were aligned to the sweet orange reference genome (http://citrus.hzau.edu.cn/). For gene function annotation, EggNOG (Huerta-Cepas et al., 2019) was used to annotate genes, and Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes identifiers were extracted from the results.

Based on previous studies, AsA-related genes in the genomes of R. roxburghii and citrus were identified (Bulley et al., 2009; Cruz-Rus et al., 2010; Xu et al., 2013). Protein sequences for gene families in the AsA metabolic pathway of A. thaliana were downloaded from The Arabidopsis Information Resource (TAIR) database (https://www. arabidopsis.org/) and subjected to BLASTP searches against the genomes of R. roxburghii and C. sinensis (http://citrus.hzau.edu.cn/) to identify homologous genes.

After thoroughly grinding samples in liquid nitrogen, 0.1 g was mixed thoroughly with $9 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of phosphate-buffered saline (PBS; $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m o l / l }$ , pH 7.0– 7.4). The mixture was centrifuged at 10 000 rpm for 10 min, and $10 \%$ of the supernatant was collected for further testing. Subsequently, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of the reaction solution was transferred to a 96-well transparent

# Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS)

The VIGS method was performed as previously described (Padmanabhan and Dinesh-Kumar, 2009; Tang et al., 2021). A 191-bp gene fragment of RroxTTG2 (from the ATG start codon) was amplified and homologously recombined into the pTRV2 vector (Padmanabhan and Dinesh-Kumar, 2009). A. tumefaciens cells were resuspended in a solution of $1 0 \ m M$ ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , 10 mM MES, and $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone (AS) to an optical density at 600 nm of 0.6–0.8. Then, pTRV1 and pTRV2 suspensions were mixed at a 1:1 ratio before injection. Mixtures of Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 strains containing either pTRV1-EV and pTRV2-EV (negative control, EV) or pTRV1-EV and pTRV2-RroxTTG2 (RroxTTG2), along with the helper plasmid P19, were injected into different parts of the same fruit (60 DAF). Seven days post injection, samples were collected from the injection sites, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for subsequent use. Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11. 

# Genetic transformation

Complete ORF sequences of RroxGL3, RroxTTG2, RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 were cloned into the overexpression vector pK7WG2D. Three AsA synthesis-related enzyme-encoding genes (RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2), a transcription factor gene (RroxTTG2), and the ENHANCED GREEN FLUORESCENT PROTEIN-NEOMYCIN PHOSPHOTRANSFERASE II (eGFP-NPTII) marker gene for dual selection driven by the CAULIFLOWER MOSAIC VIRUS 35S (CaMV35S) and POL-YUBIQUITIN 10 (AtUBQ10) promoters were also cloned into the binary plasmid pGGK-GFP (Lin et al., 2022). The multi-gene expression vector carrying all target genes was designated pGGK-GFP-4TU (with a transfer DNA of ∼12 kb). In addition, approximately $2 . 0 – \mathsf { k b }$ of promoter and coding sequences (CDSs) of the candidate genes RroxPME, RroxGalUR, and RroxDHAR2 were cloned into the binary plasmid pGGK-GFP along with the CaMV35S-driven RroxTTG2 gene. The multigene expression vector carrying all target genes was designated pGGK-GFP-35S (with a transfer DNA of ∼15 kb). PCR analysis verified the vector structure and confirmed its stability in Escherichia coli and A. tumefaciens. Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11. 

Plasmids were transformed into an Agrobacterium strain, which was then used to transform A. thaliana (Col-0) or mutants (Atttg2, stock number: SALK_204777C; Atgl3, stock number: SALK_120957C, obtained from AraShare, a non-profit Arabidopsis share center (http://www.arashare. cn), using the floral dip method described by Clough and Bent (1998). An A. tumefaciens resuspension solution was prepared using a $5 \%$ (w/v) sucrose aqueous solution combined with $0 . 0 2 5 \%$ Silwet-77. Bacteria were resuspended to an optical density at $6 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ of 0.6–0.8. Inflorescences of 30-day-old A. thaliana plants were used for infection and immersed in the solution for 15 s. After the initial infection, inflorescences were placed under water-retaining conditions for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , then transferred to standard growth conditions with a photoperiod of 16 h of light and $^ { 8 \mathfrak { h } }$ of darkness. The infection procedure was repeated every 7–10 days. Mature seeds were harvested in batches, thoroughly dried, and stored in a sealed container at room temperature. 

For genetic manipulation of Italian lettuce (Lactuca sativa L. var. ramosa Hort.) (Curtis et al., 1994), seeds were disinfected in $50 \%$ bleach for 15 min and rinsed thoroughly with sterile water four times. Disinfected seeds were placed on Murashige and Skoog (MS) culture medium and incubated in a culture room at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under light. For cotyledon preparation, cotyledons were excised from 4- to 7-day-old seedlings and pre-cultured on MS medium containing 0.1 mg/l 6-benzylaminopurine (6-BA), $0 . 1 \mathrm { { \ m g / l } }$ naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA), and ${ 2 0 } \mathsf { m g / }$ AS at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in the dark for 1 day. Subsequently, the cotyledons were immersed in an Agrobacterium suspension for 5–7 min and transferred to co-culture MS medium $( 0 . 1 \ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { I }$ 6-BA, $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m g / l }$ NAA, 20 mg/l AS) and incubated in the 

dark at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ for 2 days. For differentiation, the tissues were placed in medium containing $0 . 1 \ \mathsf { m g } / 1$ 6-BA, $0 . 1 \ \mathsf { m g } / 1$ NAA, $3 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g / l }$ Timentin, and 50 mg/l kanamycin. Following differentiation, the tissues were transferred to rooting medium (half-strength MS, 0.1 mg/l NAA, 300 mg/l Timentin). Transgenic plants were grown in a glass greenhouse at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ with a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h darkness. Transgenic-positive plants were identified by UV light using a GFP filter and by PCR. Three independent lines were selected for each transgenic combination for phenotypic analysis. Four-week-old T3 transgenic Arabidopsis leaves and 6-week-old T1 transgenic lettuce leaves were selected for AsA concentration measurement. 

# Assessment of trichome phenotypes

Transgenic T2 Arabidopsis seeds were obtained through screening, sown for observation, and cultured at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ under a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h dark. After approximately 4 weeks of growth, the abaxial surface of Arabidopsis rosette leaves from each transgenic plant was photographed, and the number of trichomes was counted using ImageJ software. WT (Col-0) and mutant plants were maintained under identical conditions for observation, cultivation, and data collection with a minimum of five plants analyzed per genotype. 

# Determination of anthocyanin content

Anthocyanins were measured as described by Mita et al. (1997). Briefly, samples were ground into a powder in liquid nitrogen, and $_ { 0 . 1 \ 9 }$ of powder was added to 2 ml of extraction buffer (methanol containing $1 \%$ hydrochloric acid). The mixture was shaken thoroughly at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . After centrifugation at 10 000 rpm for 5 min, the supernatant was collected, and absorbance was measured at 530 and 657 nm using a spectrophotometer (A530 and A657). Anthocyanin content was calculated using the formula $( \mathsf { A } 5 3 0 - 0 . 2 5 \times \mathsf { A } 6 5 7 ) / \mathsf { F W }$ , and expressed as units per gram FW (unit/g FW). 

# Dual-LUC assay

Upstream sequences (approximately 2.0 kb) for the candidate genes RroxPME, RroxGalUR, RroxDHAR2, RroxTTG2, CsPME, CsGalUR, and CsDHAR1 were cloned and ligated into the pGreen-0800-LUC vector (Hellens et al., 2005). CDSs of the candidate transcription factors RroxGL1, RroxGL3, RroxEGL3, RroxTTG2, and CsTTG2, as well as members of the WRKY and MYB gene families, were ligated into the pK7WG2D vector for overexpression. Plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 along with the helper plasmid P19. The pK7WG2D EV was used as a control. Both control and experimental plasmids were injected into the same leaf. Following injection, samples were incubated in the dark for 1 day, followed by 2 days of light exposure. The abaxial surface of the injected tobacco leaves was coated with $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ luciferin and allowed to react in darkness for 5 min. Bioluminescence was imaged using a NightShade imaging apparatus (LC985), and leaves were also assayed for LUC and Renilla LUC activity according to the instructions for the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega Corporation) (He et al., 2022). Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11. 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

Full-length CDSs of candidate transcription factors RroxTTG2 and RroxGL3 were ligated into the pGADT7 vector to construct the recombinant plasmids AD-RroxTTG2, AD-RroxGL3, and AD-CsTTG2. The promoters (approximately 2.0 kb upstream of the ATG start codon) of RroxPME, RroxGalUR, RroxDHAR2, RroxTTG2, CsPME, CsGalUR, and CsDHAR1 were cloned into the pHIS2 vector to construct the recombinant plasmids pHIS2-RroxPMEpro, pHIS2-RroxGalURpro, pHIS2-RroxD-HAR2pro, pHIS2-RroxTTG2pro, pHIS2-CsPMEpro, pHIS2-CsGalURpro, and pHIS2-CsDHAR1pro. The recombinant plasmids and AD EV were co-transformed separately into Y187 yeast strains as negative controls. Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11.

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The full-length CDS of RroxTTG2 was ligated into the pET28a-His tag vector through homologous recombination. The recombinant plasmid was then transformed into the E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain. Protein expression was induced at $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at 120 rpm for 12 h. Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11. 

# $\beta$ -Glucuronidase (GUS) activity assay

Primers were designed based on the 545 bp insertion sequence in the RroxGalUR promoter using Primer 5, with the LTR cis-acting element mutated from CCGAA to AAAAA. The DNA fragment was cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector by homologous recombination to generate the proRroxGalURc ::β-GLUCURONIDASE (GUS) and proRoxrGalURmt:: GUS fusion expression vectors. Sequencing-validated plasmids were extracted from E. coli and transformed into A. tumefaciens EHA105. Stable genetic transformation of tobacco was performed following previously published methods (Lu et al., 2024). DNA was extracted from the proRroxGalURc ::GUS and proRoxrGalURmt::GUS transgenic tobacco plants; target bands were amplified using specific primers and sequenced. After confirming sequence accuracy, plants were transplanted into nutrient pots. After four weeks of normal growth in a growth chamber, transgenic plants were subjected to $\mathtt { 3 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and leaves were collected and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. GUS enzyme activity was measured using a GUS assay kit (Suzhou Geruisi Bio-Tech). Each treatment was repeated three times. Related primers are listed in Supplemental Table 11.
