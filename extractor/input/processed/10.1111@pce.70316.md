# ABSTRACT

Lysine deficiency in staple crops like maize, rice, and wheat remains a major cause for global protein malnutrition, underscoring the urgent need for effective biofortification strategies. This review critically examines recent advances in enhancing lysine content, spanning conventional breeding and metabolic engineering to cutting-edge precision genome editing. While conventional breeding, exemplified by Quality Protein Maize, has improved lysine levels, it is often constrained by yield and quality trade-offs. Metabolic engineering strategies, including overexpression of lysine biosynthetic genes, suppression of catabolic genes, and modification of storage proteins, have achieved substantial lysine enrichment but face regulatory and consumer acceptance challenges due to their transgenic nature. The advent of CRISPR/Cas technology now enables precise, transgene-free editing of key enzymes such as DHDPS, AK, and LKR/SDH offering a powerful alternative, though concerns regarding off-target effects and pleiotropy remain. While integrating multi-omics with AI-driven predictive modelling can optimise metabolic flux for higher lysine yield, coupling next-generation genome editing with speed breeding offers a transformative route to develop high-lysine, high-yielding crops for sustainable nutritional security.

# 1 | Introduction

Malnutrition is estimated to affect more than two billion people worldwide, largely due to poor dietary diversity and dependence on nutrient-poor staple crops (Lowe 2021). Crop biofortification is pivotal in combating malnutrition, specifically by addressing deficiencies of essential amino acids (EAAs). Amino acids are the fundamental building blocks of proteins, playing vital roles in human health. They contribute to key physiological processes, including tissue repair, immune system regulation, hormone production and enzymatic function (Gruca and Dufour 2018). Among the nine EAAs, lysine is the most critical one required for protein synthesis, collagen production, and calcium utilisation (Gunarathne et al. 2025). Despite its significance, lysine deficiency remains a major nutritional concern, particularly in populations which rely on staple crops such as maize, rice, and

wheat, which are inherently low in lysine. This inherent deficiency establishes lysine as the first limiting amino acid in cereal-based diets, that is, the EAA in shortest supply relative to human nutritional requirements (Leinonen et al. 2019). This deficiency leads to malnutrition, reduced cognitive development, and stunting, posing serious public health concerns (Gunarathne et al. 2025). Inadequate protein supplies put $20\%$ of the population at the risk of deficiency. At the same time, the supply of lysine, an EAA, poses a greater concern, being inadequate for $33\%$ of households overall and especially for most of the poorest households (Muleya et al. 2022).

Lysine deficiency also impairs the body's ability to utilise other EAAs effectively. This trigger increased amino acid catabolism, leading to poor net protein utilisation and hence impaired growth, even when total protein intake appears adequate

(Holecek 2025; Zeng et al. 2013). Consequently, lysine deficiency significantly reduces the biological value of dietary protein. In contrast, diets with sufficient lysine are associated with enhanced overall amino acid utilisation in the body and significantly improved serum protein levels.

Although animal-based proteins are a rich source of lysine and offer significant nutritional benefits, they are often out of reach for many people (Figure 1). Therefore, biofortification is considered as a sustainable and cost-effective approach for enhancing the nutritional value of crops via increasing their lysine

content (Yang et al. 2021). Biofortification would be a better alternative to the other approaches relying on dietary supplementation due to the logistical and economic implications of commercial nutrition products. It is an attractive and sustainable approach to enhance lysine content in staple crops, ensuring that they are nutritionally enriched while being affordable and easily accessible (Wang and Galili 2016).

Global-food security initiatives like the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, have recognised the need for nutrient rich crops to reduce malnutrition (FAO and IFAD 2019). Lysine

Advancements in biotechnology, such as CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing and metabolic engineering, have provided a precise and efficient way for enhancing lysine content of major cereals like rice and wheat (Kumar et al. 2022; Rastogi et al. 2025). In addition, recent developments in synthetic biology have permitted reprogramming of plant metabolism to enhance amino acid biosynthesis (Hasan 2024). Despite these advancements, very few studies have explored novel methods for lysine biofortification. Therefore, a comprehensive review of existing knowledge is required to understand the current challenges, and identify future research directions.

This review summarises past and current strategies for lysine biofortification and outlines future directions to improve lysine content while minimising associated drawbacks. We have delved into the aspartate pathway and its complex regulation, evaluated the successes and shortcomings of conventional breeding and metabolic engineering, and critically assessed the transformative potential of CRISPR-based genome editing and AI-driven multi-omics strategies. Furthermore, we have explored the role of lysine in plant stress responses and addressed the ongoing challenges and future prospects for developing high-lysine, high-yielding crop varieties to combat malnutrition and to ensure food security.

# 2 | Lysine Biosynthesis and Regulation in Plants

# 2.1 | Overview of the Aspartate Pathway for Lysine Biosynthesis

Lysine is synthesised in plants via the aspartate pathway, which also produces methionine, threonine, and isoleucine (Galili and Amir 2013). Starting with aspartate, an intermediate of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, the pathway goes through several enzymatic reactions to produce lysine (Figure 2). Since animals lack this pathway, it becomes a crucial target for enhancing the nutritional value of crops (Azevedo et al. 1997). The pathway is closely regulated to maintain amino acid balance and optimise resource distribution under varying developmental and environmental conditions (Garg et al. 2018; Yang et al. 2020; Isogai et al. 2024).

# 2.2 | Metabolic Crosstalk and Interaction With Other Amino Acids

Lysine biosynthesis in plants is tightly regulated within the aspartate family pathway, where lysine, threonine, methionine, and isoleucine share common precursors and are interconnected through complex feedback regulation (Galili 2002; Kishor et al. 2020). A primary approach to increase lysine content is to enhance flux toward lysine synthesis by strategically reducing flux into competing pathways (Figure 2). Aspartate semialdehyde (ASA) serves as the key branch point, directing metabolic flow either toward lysine or toward threonine, methionine, and isoleucine. Mutations in dihydrodipicolinate synthase (DHDDS) in Arabidopsis highlights this crosstalk: $dhps2$ mutants accumulate

threonine due to reduced DHDPS levels (Craciun et al. 2000; Foissner et al. 2000), while simultaneous loss of $dhdps1$ and $dhdps2$ is fatal for gamete development and embryogenesis, underscoring the essential role of the enzyme (Jones-Held et al. 2012). Another effective approach to shift this flux balance is to reduce the activity of the competing homoserine pathway. For instance, Arabidopsis mutants lacking homoserine dehydrogenase (AK-HSDH2) show decreased flux into threonine and methionine and a corresponding rise in lysine abundance, providing more precursor for DHDPS (Clark and Lu 2015). This is consistent with kinetic modelling demonstrating that partial inactivation of enzymes like threonine synthase or homoserine kinase can strongly favour lysine accumulation (Curien et al. 2009). An additional layer of regulation involves methionine, as its derivative S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) can trigger a compensatory downregulation of lysine biosynthesis, as observed in maize (Hacham et al. 2008). Furthermore, branched-chain amino acids such as leucine also modulate lysine biosynthetic genes by altering central metabolic intermediates (Fondi et al. 2007).

Besides the aspartate family, lysine metabolism cross-converts with several other diverse biological processes. Lysine malonylation functions as a posttranslational modification regulating metabolism, stress, and defence in Platycodon grandiflorus (Yang et al. 2025). Talukder et al. (2023) showed that lysine biosynthesis is also influenced by amylopectin metabolism, with the double mutant $(wx1wx1 / o2o2)$ showing a synergistic effect, accumulating $15.6\%$ more lysine (total) and $16.6\%$ more tryptophan than single mutants, highlighting the coordinated role of waxy1 and opaque2 during kernel development (Talukder et al. 2023). The opaque2 mutant of maize showed increased accumulation of lysine-rich proteins and highly crystalline starch (Jia et al. 2013). In high free lysine (HFL) rice, lysine concentrations augmented serotonin biosynthesis, which resulted in the dark-brown endosperm (Yang et al. 2018). This putative link is mediated by the lysine catabolite 2-aminoadipate, highlighting the metabolic crosstalk between lysine and serotonin biosynthesis. The high-lysine trait in Arabidopsis seeds disrupts TCA cycle function, resulting in slow seed germination (Angelovici et al. 2011). Lysine accumulation has also shown distinct effects on lipid biosynthesis. In crops like canola, soybean, and maize, elevated lysine has been linked to a significant reduction in oil content (Falco et al. 1995; Medici et al. 2009), potentially due to rerouted carbon flux toward amino acid synthesis. However, transgenic maize lines engineered to downregulate zeins and overexpress CordapA exhibited enhanced oil content, highlighting the genotype-specific metabolic flexibility (Huang et al. 2005).

Collectively, these findings indicate that targeting key branch points of the lysine pathway, for example, by inactivating enzymes like AK or HSD and employing feedback-insensitive variants of enzymes like AK and DHDPS, can effectively redirect metabolic flux toward lysine (Mishra et al. 2022). Promising biofortification methods could combine CRISPR/Cas-based knockdowns of competing pathways (e.g., HSD or SAM synthetase) with the overexpression of these insensitive enzymes. However, this engineering must be undertaken with a systems-level perspective, as lysine accumulation triggers extensive metabolic crosstalk. # 3 Strategies for Lysine Biofortification

Given the importance of lysine in human diet, diverse strategies have been adopted to biofortify its levels in plants, each having its own advantages and disadvantages. Some employ conventional plant breeding methods, while others utilise biotechnological interventions (Figure 3). The common goal of all these strategies is to increase lysine levels without compromising crop yield and quality.

# 3.1 | Conventional Breeding

Conventional breeding has long been central to crop improvement and has been widely used to improve lysine content in cereals (Yang et al. 2022). Its strength lies in its established methodology and high public acceptance, as it avoids the regulatory hurdles associated with transgenic approaches (Garg et al. 2018). However, the success of this approach depends on the availability of genetic variations, which is often limited in cultivated crops (Yang et al. 2021). This lack of diversity necessitates exploration of wild relatives and landraces as a source of novel genetic variation (Khoury et al. 2022). These variants may come into existence either naturally or through mutagenic processes.

Natural variants with higher lysine content have been identified through the screening of the germplasm collections. For instance, screening of rice resources revealed lines such as the Dharial mutant, which showed a fivefold increase in total lysine $(3.86\mathrm{mg / g})$ and Milyang320, which displayed a $22\%$ increase, highlighting the genetic potential for lysine biofortification

However, the frequent co-occurrence of high lysine with the floury endosperm phenotype due to a defect in endosperm development, as seen in the Milyang320 mutants, reveals a critical weakness of conventional approaches. Similarly, induced mutagenesis through chemicals such as EMS

(Ethyl Methanesulfonate) in barley or MNU (N-Methyl-N-nitrosourea) in rice generated mutants with up to $59.5\%$ higher total lysine, but again, often with pleiotropic defects like floury endosperm (Kumamaru et al. 1997; Moecs et al. 2019). A major gap in knowledge exists in fully understanding the genetic

networks and modifier genes that control these pleiotropic effects (Maqbool et al. 2021).

To overcome these drawbacks, recurrent selection and backcrossing have been employed to enhance the nutritional quality of the crops (Jangra et al. 2024). For example, the o2 mutant in maize was backcrossed with elite lines to develop QPM, where genetic modifiers restored kernel hardness while preserving the improved amino acid profile (Maqbool et al. 2021). This was achieved through two key strategies: selecting modifiers within opaque-2 populations that increased the frequency of modified kernels, and recombining elite opaque-2 families with hard endosperm (Maqbool et al. 2021). Similar strategies have been applied to barley and sorghum, where lys3a mutants in barley and P721 opaque mutants in sorghum were efficiently incorporated into breeding programs, leading to increased protein composition with better lysine content (Moehs et al. 2019). Duo et al. (2023) examined 48 maize inbreds for their methionine, lysine, and tryptophan content at three locations and identified some promising genotypes, such as CML165 (lysine: $0.320\%$ and tryptophan: $0.090\%$ ) and CML186 (lysine: $0.389\%$ and tryptophan: $0.083\%$ ), both having balanced amino acid profiles. However, while such genotypes highlight the potential for nutritional enhancement, breeding programs should be strategic in the selection of modifier genes that could reduce the adverse impacts on essential agronomic traits such as yield and kernel hardness (Vasal 2000).

# 3.2 | Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS)

Recent breakthroughs in molecular breeding, especially in the field of MAS, have significantly improved the efficiency of lysine biofortification in crops. In comparison to traditional breeding, MAS offers many advantages, including faster identification of lysine-rich plants, reduced linkage drag, and shorter breeding cycles (Vasal 2000; Babu et al. 2012). MAS has been successfully used to backcross lysine-rich traits into elite germplasm (Table 1). A prime example is the development of QPM through restoration of kernel hardness in $o2$ mutants while maintaining the enhanced lysine content (Tandzi et al. 2017; Sarika et al. 2018; Pukalenthy et al. 2020; Maqbool et al. 2021). The use of functional markers such as MGU-InDel10-o2 that distinguish QPM from normal inbred lines permit precise and quick selection (Chand et al. 2024). Recently, MAS has been employed for gene pyramiding in maize (Wu et al. 2024). In addition, marker-assisted backcross breeding has been effectively used to introgress $o2$ , opaque16 (o16), crtRB1, and $wx1$ into elite maize inbred lines, thereby enhancing $\beta$ -carotene, lysine, and tryptophan contents without compromising agronomic performance (Chandran et al. 2019; Chandrasekharan et al. 2022; Talukder et al. 2023).

Significant improvements in genetic selection have been made through the discovery of novel quantitative trait loci (QTLs) and molecular markers associated with high lysine content in maize, wheat and rice via genome-wide association studies (GWAS) (Crossa et al. 2017; Oddy et al. 2023). Recently, GWAS has identified 248 and 71 quantitative trait nucleotides linked to lysine content in rice grains and leaves, respectively (He et al. 2024). Interestingly, certain loci, such as LOC_Os01g21380 have been associated with lysine accumulation, thus providing

promising targets for MAS and breeding with genomic selection (GS). Similarly, Zhang et al. (2024) have identified 12 QTLs associated with lysine content in maize accounting for $4.42\% - 12.66\%$ of the phenotypic variation. These findings provide valuable genetic targets for marker-assisted selection and gene pyramiding in high-lysine breeding programs. In wheat, single-nucleotide polymorphisms near lysine biosynthesis or storage proteins genes have been used in MAS to enhance nutrient levels without compromising agronomic performance (Babu et al. 2012). Maize has been the main focus for lysine biofortification using MAS, but similar methods have also been applied to other crops such as rice, soybean, barley, and sorghum by introgressing major QTLs from wild relatives (Shi et al. 2009; Massafaro et al. 2016; Yang et al. 2016; Moehs et al. 2019; Singer et al. 2023).

A key weakness in these efforts is the potential for linkage drag, the co-introgression of undesirable genes from donor parents, which MAS mitigates but does not fully eliminate, requiring extensive backcrossing and background selection (Babu et al. 2012). Furthermore, conventional breeding often yields recessive alleles and lacks tissue specificity, limiting commercial applicability (Houmard et al. 2007). The prevailing conceptual framework guiding future research is the transition from MAS to GS, which uses genome-wide markers to predict the breeding value for complex, polygenic traits like lysine content, potentially overcoming the limitation of focusing only on known, large-effect genes (Crossa et al. 2017).

Although conventional breeding and marker-assisted strategies have generated substantial genetic gains, significant questions remain regarding their efficiency under rapid climate change and evolving pest pressures. Future research should also focus on exploring under-utilized germplasm collections, improving predictive breeding through GS and environmental modelling, and integrating molecular data with field phenotyping. Additionally, developing multiparent advanced generation intercross populations and applying machine learning to selection indices could further accelerate trait pyramiding and adaptability across environments.

# 3.3 | Metabolic Engineering for Lysine Biofortification

Metabolic engineering represents the most targeted and potent strategy for lysine biofortification, directly controlling the core regulatory mechanism that limits its accumulation. While conventional breeding works with existing variation, metabolic engineering redesigns metabolic pathways to overcome homoeostasis, potentially increasing lysine without pleiotropic effects on yield. However, this approach is complex and accompanies significant gaps in our understanding of plant metabolic networks.

# 3.3.2 | Aspartate Kinase (AK)

AK is a key regulatory point in the lysine biosynthesis pathway (see Sections 2.1 for its enzymatic role and regulation). Plants possess two AK isoenzymes; a lysine-sensitive isoenzyme and a threonine-sensitive isoenzyme (Varisi et al. 2008). Lysine binding to AK initiates conformational changes that decrease its enzymatic activity, as shown in structural studies employing crystal structure of AK (Kotaka et al. 2006). In Corynebacterium pekinense, Liu et al. (2021) identified mutations at critical residues (T379N/A380C) which disrupted the inhibitory conformations and enhanced AK activity by 22.79-fold. Likewise, Arabidopsis AK-HSDH2 knockout mutants had higher aspartate-derived amino acids and similar increases of Asp, Lys, and Met were also observed in $ak1-1$ , $ak2-1$ , $ak3-1$ , and $akhsdh1-1$ mutants (Clark and Lu 2015). The expression of feedback-insensitive $E.$ coli AK in tobacco and soybean led to a significant increase in free lysine content (Karchi et al. 1994;

Falco et al. 1995). Recently, Fan et al. (2022) reported a feedback-insensitive AK mutant $(A380M)$ in rice, which exhibited a 4.8 times increase in enzyme activity along with improved substrate affinity. Similarly, Liu et al. (2024) developed an AK variant in Corynebacterium glutamicum that reduced inhibition by lysine and threonine, resulting in a lysine yield of $172.3\mathrm{g / L}$ and enhanced glucose conversion efficiency.

# 3.3.3 | Dihydrodicolinate Synthase (DHDPS)

DHDPS governs the committed step toward lysine biosynthesis (details provided in Sections 2.1). Binding of lysine to the allosteric site triggers conformational changes that modify the active site of the enzyme, leading to a more stable inactive form (Griffin et al. 2012). Structural studies of DHDPS from Vitis vinifera (2.4 Å resolution) showed that lysine binding affects Tyr132, a key catalytic residue, which disrupts proton transfer in the active site and lowers the enzyme efficiency

(Atkinson et al. 2013). In A. thaliana, structural variations in the rotamer conformation of Trp116 affect lysine binding affinity and account for the variation in feedback regulation (Hall et al. 2021). In Escherichia coli, site-directed mutagenesis near lysine-binding site effectively lowered feedback inhibition and boosted lysine production by $46\%$ (Geng et al. 2013), while a monomeric version (L197D/Y107W) retained catalytic function but lost sensitivity to lysine-mediated inhibition (Muscroft-Taylor et al. 2010).

Transgenic approaches confirm the utility of feedback-insensitive DHDPS. The overexpression of bacterial genes involved in lysine biosynthesis, such as $dapA$ from Corynebacterium and $lysC$ from $E.$ coli, has been shown to enhance free lysine levels in the canola and soybean (Falco et al. 1995). However, soybean seeds with very high lysine ( $\geq 15\%$ of total amino acids) became wrinkled and showed poor germination, indicating that excessive lysine accumulation can negatively affect seed development and viability. In contrast, a substantial increase in seed lysine content was also observed in transgenic rice expressing a feedback-insensitive DHDPS variant under a seed-specific promoter with normal plant growth and seed germination (Long et al. 2013). Rathore et al. (2025) developed a mutated variant of DHDPS in rice, which increased grain lysine content by $29\%$ and total protein levels by $15\%$ with these nutritional improvements retained even after cooking. Similarly, high lysine lines were developed by point mutation in DHDPS1 and crossing these lines with other high-lysine lines led to the development of pyramided lines (PRD and PRA), which contained 5.4- and 8.4-fold higher total lysine than wild-type rice, respectively, without affecting seed quality, seed appearance, or agronomic traits (Ye et al. 2022). The expression of lysine-insensitive DHDPS in Camelina sativa, either by modifying the endogenous enzyme or by expressing feedback-insensitive C. glutamicum DHDPS, resulted in an increase in seed lysine content by $13.6\% - 22.6\%$ without affecting seed germination and early seedling development, though seed yield was reduced (Huang et al. 2022).

Yang et al. (2021) achieved lysine biofortification in rice by alleviating feedback inhibition of aspartate kinase and DHDPS, generating high-lysine transgenic lines that maintained normal growth, development, and seed appearance comparable to wild-type plants. Earlier metabolic engineering strategies have combined feedback-insensitive AK and DHDPS expression with LKR/SDH gene silencing in rice, producing pyramid lines (HFL1 and HFL2) with $\sim 25$ -fold higher seed lysine (Yang et al. 2016). These lines showed improved physicochemical properties and unchanged starch composition, though plants were slightly taller and seeds developed a dark-brown endosperm. Yang et al. (2018) revealed that the dark-brown endosperm in HFL rice was due to enhanced serotonin biosynthesis, driven by TDC3 overexpression and jasmonate pathway activation during late endosperm development. Mishra et al. (2022) employed new metabolic engineering strategies by using both constitutive (CaMV35S) and endosperm-specific glutelinD1 promoter combinations to assess the effects of expression strategy on lysine accumulation. Seed-specific expression increased lysine by $58\%$ in mature seeds without any morphological or physiological penalties, demonstrating its advantage for safe lysine biofortification.

Key challenges in metabolic engineering for lysine fortification remain in understanding how engineered levels of lysine interact with broader metabolic and developmental networks. Determining the threshold at which elevated lysine causes pleiotropic effects and optimising spatio-temporal control of the pathway activity are major open questions. Future research should integrate systems-level modelling with precision engineering tools such as tissue-specific or inducible promoters and explore novel feedback-insensitive enzyme variants to achieve stable, high-lysine crops without yield penalties.

# 3.4 | Promoter Editing for Lysine Biofortification

Tight regulation of lysine biosynthetic genes offers opportunities to enhance their accumulation in a tissue-specific or environment-responsive manner. Constitutive overexpression of the lysine pathway genes often triggers pleiotropic effects including developmental abnormalities, metabolic disruptions, and yield penalties, primarily due to lysine toxicity in nontarget tissues. To overcome these challenges, tissue-specific or inducible expression systems that respond to developmental cues or environmental stimuli provide a safer and more effective alternative. Such conditional regulation enables lysine enrichment only when and where it is beneficial, such as in developing seeds or under stress conditions, thereby minimising negative impacts on plant growth while achieving biofortification goals (Galili 2002; Ufaz and Galili 2008; Mishra et al. 2022).

Inducible promoters have been successfully applied in microbes to redirect carbon flux toward lysine. For example, Huang et al. (2021) engineered a hyperosmotic stress-inducible system in Corynebacterium glutamicum by leveraging the MtrA/MtrB-dependent P_NCgl1418 promoter. This promoter was used to overexpress the lysine exporter gene lysE concurrently with a CRISPR interference system that targeted and repressed the expression of competing metabolic genes (gltA, pgi, hom). This coordinated strategy, activated under salt stress, resulted in a 64-92-fold increase in lysine production with minimal metabolic burden (Huang et al. 2021).

In plants, a comparable strategy employs developmental promoters to achieve spatio-temporally restricted lysine accumulation. For example, seed-specific promoters (e.g., phaseolin, glutelin) have been used to express feedback-insensitive DHDPS or lysine-rich proteins (LRPs) during seed maturation, significantly increasing seed lysine content without affecting yield (Angelovici et al. 2009; Zhu and Galili 2004; Long et al. 2013). The precision of this strategy can be further enhanced using synthetic promoters for optimised, fine-tuned expression.

The critical role of tissue-specific targeting is exemplified in soybean and maize. In soybean, where the embryo is the main seed component, expressing a bacterial feedback-insensitive DHDPS (Section 3.3) with an embryo-specific promoter increased free lysine by 100-fold (Falco et al. 1995). A contrasting result in maize, whose seed is primarily endosperm, showed that only embryo-specific (not endosperm-specific) expression of the same enzyme significantly boosted lysine levels (Frizzi et al. 2008). This demonstrates that the precise selection of a promoter driving metabolic engineering is essential for successful biofortification.

Recent progress in CRISPR-based promoter editing has made it possible to achieve targeted editing of native promoter sequences to either improve or inhibit gene expression without introducing exogenous DNA. This method of promoter engineering can increase the transcription of lysine biosynthesis genes (e.g. AK and DHDPS, as detailed in Section 3.3) upon stress or developmental cues to perform conditional biofortification. The potential of such a strategy has been outlined in genome-edited crops in which specific modifications in promoter regions produced gene expression patterns with minimal off-target effects (Huang et al. 2022).

Fine-tuned synthetic promoter libraries and engineering of cis-regulatory elements also increase the capacity to engineer inducible systems. Libraries of synthetic stress-inducible promoters have been screened in microbial and algal systems to identify optimal constructs to overproduce lysine under individual environmental conditions (Li et al. 2020; Szymczyk and Majewska 2024). Such ideas could be applied into crop systems with the use of tools, like ATAC-seq and promoter-editing CRISPR tools to identify or engineer inducible variants of promoters to control lysine metabolism. Collectively, the use of promoter editing and inducible expression strategies represents a powerful toolkit for enabling lysine biosynthesis in response to defined cues such as drought, salinity, or seed maturation. Restricting expression to edible tissues or stress environment will reduce lysine-mediated growth penalties and enhance the practicality of field-scale lysine biofortification.

Future studies should address how specific promoter elements and cis-regulatory motifs control lysine pathway genes across diverse crops. The stability and field performance of CRISPR-edited promoters also warrant thorough investigation. Future research should integrate multi-omics approaches, including ATAC-seq and transcriptomics, with synthetic promoter engineering to develop finely tunable, context-responsive regulatory systems. Such strategies will advance the rational design of crops with sustainable, environment-adaptive lysine biofortification.

# 3.5 | Suppressing Lysine Catabolism

Lysine catabolism via LKR/SDH represents a major obstacle to lysine accumulation in plants (Figure 4B). Long et al. (2013) demonstrated that suppressing lysine catabolism through LKR/ SDH knockdown is a highly effective strategy for accumulating free lysine in seeds. In transgenic rice co-expressing feedback-insensitive AK and DHDPS, LKR/SDH suppression resulted in a dramatic 60-fold increase in seed-free lysine, far exceeding the 12-fold increase observed in leaves, while still maintaining normal plant growth and seed germination (Long et al. 2013). This stark contrast highlights that lysine levels in seeds are predominantly regulated by their catabolism rather than biosynthesis. In maize, endosperm-specific RNAi-mediated suppression of LKR/SDH was successfully employed to inhibit lysine catabolism. This targeted approach resulted in a significant accumulation of free lysine, particularly during the initial stages of kernel development (Houmard et al. 2007; Reyes et al. 2009). Building on this, a synergistic approach combining deregulated lysine biosynthesis with targeted catabolism suppression was demonstrated in maize. The expression of a

feedback-insensitive aspartate kinase (CordapA) coupled with LKR/SDH suppression within a single transgene cassette achieved remarkably high lysine accumulation, with levels exceeding $4000\mathrm{ppm}$ in seeds (Frizzi et al. 2008). Collectively, these studies underscore that suppressing lysine catabolism, especially when combined with enhanced biosynthesis, is a profoundly effective strategy for the biofortification of EAAs in major grain crops. However, an important weakness and knowledge gap remain. Studies show that high lysine levels in leaves do not necessarily lead to high lysine in seeds (Long et al. 2013), suggesting that free lysine is not efficiently moved into seeds and therefore lysine breakdown should be targeted specifically to seeds. In addition, the long-term effects of completely blocking a major amino acid breakdown pathway are still unclear and need further investigation.

# 3.6 | Manipulation of Lysine-Rich Storage Proteins (LRSP)

Another approach of lysine biofortification focuses on increasing the levels of lysine-rich storage proteins within seeds (Figure 4C), which safely sequesters lysine in a stable, protein-bound form to avoid the metabolic imbalances and pleiotropic effects often associated with free amino acid accumulation (Galili and Amir 2013). Efforts have been centred on two strategies: overexpressing native or heterologous lysine-rich storage proteins and suppressing lysine-poor storage proteins.

The overexpression of the cotton LRP gene, $GhLRP$ , into maize led to the enhancement of L-lysine from $16.2\%$ to $65\%$ over the base level without affecting agronomic or quality traits, with stable inheritance across multiple generations (Yue et al. 2014). Similarly, co-expression of LRP gene $SBgLR$ from potato and tomato ERF transcription factor gene, $TSRF1$ in maize improved lysine content by $8.70\%$ to $30.43\%$ and protein content by $7.7\%$ to $24.38\%$ in $\mathrm{T}_{1}$ transgenic maize plant, while conferring improved salt tolerance (Wang et al. 2013). Seed-specific expression of the LRP gene sb401 significantly increases both total lysine (by $16.1\%$ to $54.8\%$ ) and total protein content in maize seeds over six generations without affecting vegetative parts and seeds of the transgenic plants (Yu et al. 2005). Likewise, Liu et al. (2016) expressed the LRP gene from Psophocarpus tetragonolobus in rice employing an endosperm-specific glutelin1 promoter, resulting in a $30\%$ increase in total lysine and enhanced level of other amino acids with stable inheritance over three generations, though some lines displayed undesirable grain phenotypes, partially alleviated in some hybrids. A significant $35\%$ increase in total lysine content in rice grains was achieved through the overexpression of lysine-rich histone proteins (RLRH1 and RLRH2) using seed-specific promoter, while maintaining balanced amino acids and avoiding unfolded protein response, though transgenic seeds showed varying degrees of chalkiness correlated with protein and lysine accumulation (Wong et al. 2015). In maize, expression of Arabidopsis microtubule-associated protein (AtMAP18), boosted lysine and protein content, accompanied by the protein body formation within endosperm tissue (Chang et al. 2015). Importantly, hybrid progeny derived from crossing these transgenic lines with wild-type inbreds maintained these nutritional improvements without compromising agronomic performance, demonstrating their breeding stability and

practical potential (Chang et al. 2015). Furthermore, overexpression of $AmA1$ significantly improved total protein content $(1.06\% -12.87\%)$ and lysine levels in rice while maintaining the seed viability and yield (Xu et al. 2017). Synthetic proteins designed with lysine-rich motifs have also been explored for lysine biofortification. In rice, expression of synthetic genes TKTKK1 and TKTKK2 resulted in increased lysine $(33.87\%)$ , threonine $(21.21\%)$ , total amino acids $(19.43\%)$ , and crude protein content (20.45) with limited effect on other agronomic traits%) (Jiang et al. 2016). Major gap in knowledge, however, persists in understanding how to best design these synthetic proteins to ensure their proper folding and integration into protein bodies without triggering ER stress or compromising seed morphology.

RNAi-based silencing has also been employed as an effective strategy to manipulate storage protein composition for enhancing lysine levels (Kawakatsu and Takaiwa 2010; Kawakatsu et al. 2010). Silencing of 19- and $22\mathrm{-kD}\alpha$ -zeins in maize increased kernel lysine concentrations without altering kernel texture, with concomitant increase in LRPs (Huang et al. 2006). The synergy of this approach with metabolic engineering was demonstrated by combining zein suppression with a feedback-insensitive biosynthetic enzyme (CordapA), which more than doubled the total lysine content (Huang et al. 2005). These results indicate the potential of integrating metabolic engineering with storage protein alteration for improving the nutritional quality. However, potential drawbacks include the risk of metabolic imbalance due to altered nitrogen partitioning, variable silencing stability across environments or generations, possible off-target effects, and regulatory challenges inherent to transgenic approaches Overall, while effective and synergistic with metabolic engineering, RNAi-mediated storage protein alteration requires careful optimisation to balance nutritional gains with seed quality and stability. Moreover, Future research should combine multi-omics, protein engineering, and genome editing to refine LRSP manipulation. An effective strategy could merge synthetic LRPs with RNAi suppression of lysine-poor proteins, integrated through breeding to ensure stable nutrition and seed quality.

# 3.7 | Lysine-Histidine Transporters (LHTs)

The LHTs are integral membrane proteins in plants that mediate the uptake and translocation of lysine and histidine (Guo et al. 2020; Rabby et al. 2022). Previously, Long et al. (2013) reported that although lysine accumulated in leaves of certain transgenic lines, a corresponding increase was not observed in seeds, suggesting limited transport of free lysine from source to sink. Therefore, the primary strength of targeting LHTs for biofortification lies in their potential to directly influence the source-to-sink partitioning of amino acids, a critical process that determines nutrient allocation to developing seeds (Tegeder 2014). LHT1, a high-affinity neutral and acidic amino acid transporter, is expressed in roots and mesophyll cells, indicating its role in uptake and internal distribution (Tegeder and Ward 2012). Loss-of-function mutants of OsLHT1 in rice exhibited stunted growth and low yield (Wang et al. 2019), while Arabidopsis LHT1 mutants showed impaired amino acid uptake, reduced growth, and lower biomass (Svennerstam et al. 2007). Similarly, systematic identification of the LHT family members in tea plants highlights their potential roles in amino acid transport and physiology

(Huang et al. 2024). These findings emphasise that proper LHT function is required for amino acid homoeostasis and normal plant development.

LHTs have roles beyond simple nutrient transport, adding a layer of complexity. Arabidopsis plants overexpressing LHT2 displayed altered sensitivity to 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC), implicating it in ACC transport and ethylene biosynthesis (Choi et al. 2019). This pleiotropy poses a major challenge, raising the unresolved question of whether LHT expression can be manipulated specifically in seeds or vascular tissues to enhance lysine loading without compromising plant vitality and yield.

A critical gap in knowledge is the functional redundancy and substrate specificity among different LHT family members across crops. While systematic identification has been performed, the exact role of each isoform in long-distance transport and seed loading remains poorly characterised, limiting the selection of optimal candidates for biofortification. Furthermore, the link to ethylene signalling adds complexity, as unintended manipulation could have unpredictable effects on plant development and stress responses. Future research should identify the specific roles of different LHT isoforms and their amino acid preferences. Using CRISPR along with seed or phloem-specific promoters could help fine-tune LHT activity to load more lysine into seeds without harming plant growth.

# 3.8 | Lysine Fortification Through CRISPR/Cas Technology

Genome editing offers a more precise and non-transgenic method of altering lysine metabolism, potentially reducing regulatory hurdles and increasing public acceptance compared to conventional GMOs (Bhatta and Malla 2020; Kumar et al. 2022; Das et al. 2024).

CRISPR/Cas has been efficiently used in storage protein modifications contributing to the lysine deficiency in crops. By targeting specific genes, researchers have achieved proteome rebalancing, leading to increased lysine content without compromising key agronomic traits. For example, lysine content in maize has been improved through targeted CRISPR/Cas9-based gene editing of the $19\mathrm{kDa}$ alpha-zein gene family without using the o2 mutation, which is known to have strong pleiotropic effects (Hurst et al. 2024). Editing some of the $19\mathrm{kDa}$ zeins (but not all) increased lysine content by $\sim 30\%$ while retaining vitreous endosperm, kernel texture and a functional O2 transcription factor. Further insights into the regulation of storage proteins in maize have been derived from work on $27\mathrm{kD}\gamma$ -zein, a key protein involved in protein body formation. Li et al. (2018b) identified ZmbZIP22 as a key regulator of $27\mathrm{kD}\gamma$ -zein expression and developed CRISPR/Cas9 zmbzip22 mutants, which exhibited a significant reduction in the expression of this gene. zmbzip22 mutations resulted in an increased ratio of non-zeins in storage proteins, thereby enhancing the total amounts of EAAs such as lysine ( $\sim 8\%$ ), tryptophan ( $\sim 4\%$ ), and methionine ( $\sim 3\%$ ), while retaining vitreous endosperm. Recently, multiplexed CRISPR/Cas9-mediated silencing of LKR/SDH has demonstrated 1.5-1.9-fold increase in the total lysine content in the US rice variety Presidio without impacting the agronomical traits (Rastogi et al. 2025).

In sorghum, CRISPR/Cas9-based editing of the K1C gene family reduced $\alpha$ -kafirin levels, leading to improved protein digestibility and higher lysine content in $\mathrm{T}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{T}_{2}$ seeds (Li et al. 2018a). Further, CRISPR/Cas9 was used to target the $K2G$ gene in sorghum, encoding $\gamma$ -kafirin, a protein responsible for poor digestibility and lysine deficiency. The edited lines showed a reduction in $\gamma$ -kafirin content from $12.75\%$ to $19.22\%$ , with a corresponding increase in protein digestibility from $26.91\%$ to $74.31\%$ , alongside improved growth vigour in mutant plants (Li et al. 2024). These findings demonstrate the high efficiency of CRISPR/Cas9 in targeting gene families with high sequence similarity, offering a promising strategy for developing nutritionally enhanced crops.

While CRISPR/Cas technology offers a precise and transgene-free route to lysine fortification, several key weaknesses and uncertainties remain. A significant challenge is the risk of unintended off-target mutations, which could disrupt other astronomically important genes despite improvements in targeting specificity. Furthermore, pleiotropic effects are not fully eliminated and the efficiency of multiplex editing, necessary for targeting gene families, can be variable, and the long-term stability of edited traits across generations and diverse environments requires further validation. Therefore, a major focus should be on predictive biology, using artificial intelligence (AI) and advanced computational models to predict the pleiotropic consequences of editing before experimentation.

Recent advances, such as base, prime, and promoter editing, are redefining the landscape for the crop improvement by enabling precise, site-specific modifications without introducing foreign DNA sequences. Such edits can mimic naturally occurring allelic variations, allowing them to be categorised as nontransgenic in several regulatory jurisdictions. This distinction substantially reduces the time and cost associated with approval and can enhance consumer acceptance by addressing biosafety concerns. Practical implementation will depend on transparent regulatory frameworks, traceability mechanisms, and proactive communication with stakeholders. Streamlining approval pathways for targeted, cis-genic, or transgene-free edits could accelerate market adoption and ensure that genome editing contributes effectively to sustainable agriculture.

Despite remarkable advances in molecular tools, several open questions limit their field-scale application. These include an incomplete understanding of complex gene networks, off-target mutations in genome editing, and the scarcity of validated candidate genes for stress tolerance and yield enhancement. Future research should prioritise multi-omics integration to identify functional alleles, develop base- and prime-editing systems with higher precision, and establish regulatory frameworks that encourage responsible deployment of edited crops. Expanding comparative genomic resources across species will further strengthen translational breeding pipelines. Integrating these molecular advances with data-driven approaches, including artificial intelligence, may further refine predictive design of lysine pathways.

# 3.9 | AI Assisted Strategies for Lysine Biofortification

New AI and machine learning (ML) algorithms can provide powerful tools to drive the development of lysine biosynthesis

using predictive modelling and optimised enzyme design. Innovations in ML-assisted directed evolution have made it possible to do data-driven protein design. Such models learn about sequence-function relationships from libraries of variants and propose optimised enzyme designs without the need for large-scale experimental analyses. These methods speed up enzyme optimisation and are effective in the enrichment of functional variants (Yang et al. 2019; Wu et al. 2019).

This technology is particularly useful for enzymes such as DHDPS and AK in the lysine pathway, where small improvements in catalysis or reductions in feedback inhibition can cause dramatic increases in overall flux. Current industrial engineering work with $E$ coli uses data-integrated ML models and metabolic flux analysis to identify bottleneck steps and target gene section to increase amino acid production (Becker and Wittmann 2018; St. John and Bomble 2019; Yang et al. 2020).

Moreover, AI-driven de novo enzyme design platforms have been used to generate novel proteins with high catalytic efficiency, diversifying the synthetic biocatalyst design toolbox (Cui et al. 2025). Such approaches might be used in constructing feedback-insensitive or high-turnover DHDPS/AK fusion proteins appropriate to plant lysine production. Furthermore, there are AI models like BioNavi NP that predict entire biosynthetic production mechanisms on the basis of a set of building blocks, and propose upstream or downstream gene targets, which could influence the lysine production (Zheng et al. 2022). The incorporation of such biological retrosynthesis tools can be of assistance in coming up with new regulatory nodes or transporters that have an indirect impact on lysine accumulation.

Omics datasets from plant systems (transcriptomic, proteomic, metabolomic) allow training of ML models with the goal of associating genotype to lysine contents. Combined with enzyme design packages, this can aid systems-level identification of key control points, such as feedback loops or transporter bottlenecks, and guide specific genome editing. The adoption of such approaches to lysine biofortification has the potential to bring with it the ability to integrate precision, predictive capability, and scalability, enabling the rational design of high lysine crops with limited trial and error.

To provide an overview of the methodological landscape, Figure 5 contrasts traditional and modern strategies for lysine biofortification. While conventional approaches such as mutagenesis, classical hybridisation, and agronomic fortification have historically contributed to crop improvement, they are often slow and limited in precision. In contrast, modern tools such as protein engineering, AI, gene editing, and speed breeding offer faster, more targeted, and more efficient pathways to generate lysine-enriched cultivars.

Although substantial progress has been made in understanding stress signalling and tolerance pathways, their integration to design resilient crops remains incomplete. Major gaps persist in elucidating how multiple stresses interact within the plant, how molecular tolerance mechanisms translate into agronomic performance, and how to accurately predict responses under variable field conditions. Future research should focus on network-level modelling of stress responses, time-resolved multi-omics analyses, and rigorous field validation of candidate genes and pathways. Additionally, unravelling the roles of epigenetic regulation and stress memory will be crucial for engineering durable and heritable stress resilience.

# 5 | Challenges in Lysine Biofortification

# 5.1 | Metabolic Constraints and Agronomic Trade-Offs

One of the major challenges in lysine biofortification is the stringent metabolic regulation of aspartate pathway, which governs lysine biosynthesis and is tightly controlled by feedback inhibition of key enzymes such as AK and DHDPS, as discussed

in Section 3.3 (Zhu and Galili 2004; Curien et al. 2008). Although overexpression of feedback-insensitive variants has been attempted, but free lysine usually induced counteractive resistance responses that lead to lysine breakdown or to other metabolites (Zhu and Galili 2004). In addition, the LKR/SDH pathway (as discussed in Section 3.5) vigorously degrades lysine, and downregulation of such a pathway may enhance lysine levels, but can also cause undesired metabolic disruptions that will affect plant growth and seed viability (Long et al. 2013). Beyond metabolic regulation, lysine biofortification also poses agronomic challenges, as excessive lysine levels can disrupt nitrogen assimilation and resource allocation to other essential pathways, resulting in growth retardation, reduced protein content, and yield penalties (Rharrabti et al. 2001; Varisi et al. 2008; Yang et al. 2020). Moreover, protein modifications aimed at enhancing lysine content may also alter seed storage protein composition, ultimately affecting kernel texture and processing quality (Mertz et al. 1964).

# 5.2 | Technical Challenges in Genetic Engineering

A primary technical hurdle in genetic engineering is ensuring precision. Although CRISPR/Cas9 is highly specific, it can nevertheless trigger off-target mutations. These occur when the guide RNA binds to genomic sites with sequences similar to the intended target, a risk mitigated but not eliminated by advanced computational design tools and high-fidelity Cas enzyme variants. Furthermore, achieving stable, heritable edits in polyploid crops remains challenging due to the need to edit multiple gene copies simultaneously (Zhang et al. 2018). Similarly, RNAi has shown potential for silencing catabolic genes, but unintended off-target silencing of nontarget genes remains a concern. The success of traditional transgenic methods is also hampered by challenges such as gene silencing, positional effects where the insertion site disrupts gene function, and complex regulatory requirements. A universal issue across all these techniques is that the introduction of new genetic elements can disrupt native regulatory networks, potentially leading to unpredictable pleiotropic effects (Kocsisova and Coneva 2023).

# 5.3 | Regulatory and Consumer Acceptance Issues

Transgenic lysine-rich crops face extensive biosafety assessments along with extended approval procedures, especially in the European Union and various Asian countries. Genome editing technologies (CRISPR/Cas) may offer promising alternatives to GM crops as they facilitate precise modifications without inserting foreign DNA into target organisms. However, the regulatory frameworks for crops modified through gene editing differ among the countries, so global commercialisation attempts become more challenging (Entine et al. 2021).

# 6 | Future Prospects

# 6.1 | Advanced Genome Editing Technologies

The cutting-edge genome editing technologies have recently opened doors for precise manipulation of lysine biosynthetic

pathway. Prime editing can modify lysine-binding domains in key enzymes like AK and DHDPS (enzymes discussed in section 3.3), thus making them lysine-insensitive, alleviating feedback resistance and permitting lysine accumulation without disrupting other metabolic processes (Yang et al. 2021). It also enables fine-tuning of gene expression through precise regulatory mutations. Multiplex genome editing also holds great promise by simultaneously targeting the enzymes discussed in Sections 3.3 and 3.5 (AK, DHDPS, and LKR/SDH), synergistically enhancing lysine levels while maintaining plant growth and yield. Additionally, CRISPR-based promoter editing will also help to enhance lysine content in tissue-specific manner. This spatial regulation minimises lysine-induced toxicity and growth penalties, thereby improving the overall feasibility of lysine biofortification in crops.

# 6.2 | Speed Breeding and Multi-Omics Approaches

Speed breeding combined with genome editing offers an exceptional opportunity to accelerate the development of lysine-biofortified crops. Speed breeding shortens the generation cycles using optimised light, temperature, and photoperiod conditions, enabling faster crop improvements, particularly in crops like wheat and barley, where the breeding time can be reduced up to $50\%$ (Watson et al. 2018). This approach, when combined with CRISPR-mediated alteration in lysine metabolic pathway, will facilitate rapid introgression of beneficial alleles into elite cultivars while ensuring high yields and adaptability.

Also, multi-omics approaches integrate genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics for identifying key regulatory networks controlling lysine metabolism. A recent metabolomics study in maize indicated that lysine accumulation is closely related with carbon-nitrogen flux and also with amino acid transport, suggesting that optimisation of these pathways could further enhance lysine content (Song et al. 2023). Moreover, posttranslational modifications such as lysine acetylation and succinylation have emerged as important regulatory method influencing the activity of key metabolic enzymes involved in lysine biosynthesis (Hasan and Kurata 2018; Xia et al. 2022). Recently, transcriptome analysis of wheat plants identified trypsin-like transcripts, that are most abundantly expressed 14-28 days after anthesis (Das et al. 2024). CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout of these transcripts can enhance lysine content in mature endosperm proteins, offering a targeted approach for nutritional improvement.

# 6.3 | Systems Biology and Metabolic Modelling

Systems biology and metabolic modelling provide powerful frameworks to understand and manipulate complex metabolic networks for nutrient fortification. By combining system biology with CRISPR-mediated genome editing, scientists can further optimise lysine biosynthesis (Hasan 2024). Advanced metabolic modelling can also predict the effects of different interventions to develop more effective biofortification strategies. Furthermore, combining the analysis of environmental factors with multi-omics data will enable scientists to develop homegrown lysine-enriched plant varieties that are specifically adapted to local agricultural conditions.

# 6.4 | Addressing Societal and Regulatory Challenges

Despite these recent advancements, commercialisation of lysine-biofortified crops continues to face challenges due to regulatory hurdles and limited consumer acceptance. Genome-edited crops produced using promoter editing and prime editing methods, which involve no foreign DNA incorporation, might gain regulatory approval more easily compared to conventional transgenic methods. India, Japan and the United States have already started implementing less stringent regulations for genome-edited crops, which could ease market access for CRISPR-improved biofortified varieties. Alternatively, lysine biofortification can be achieved through conventional nongMO breeding programs using MAS, which would stand out as another option in regions having a stricter regime for GMOs and gene- edits. A multidisciplinary approach that integrates advanced breeding tools with conventional strategies can lead to the development of climate-resilient, nutrient-rich crop varieties, helping to combat global malnutrition and strengthen food security.

# 7 | Concluding Remarks

Lysine is a key limiting amino acid in cereals, and its enrichment is vital to combat global malnutrition (Tien Lea et al. 2016). Previously, conventional breeding for lysine enrichment in cereals consistently faced unintended side effects on yield and quality. Metabolic engineering emerged as a more effective strategy to overcome these trade-offs by directly targeting key enzymatic bottlenecks (e.g., feedback-insensitive DHDPS). However, while this approach proved the scientific feasibility of high-lysine traits, its reliance on transgenic technologies often led to significant regulatory and consumer acceptance challenges, hindering widespread adoption (Wenefrida et al. 2013).

The advent of precision genome editing offers a clear path to overcome the limitations of both previous eras (Ku and Ha 2020). We hypothesise that CRISPR/Cas9-mediated strategies, specifically, the base-editing of DHDPS and AK to make them feedback-insensitive, endosperm-specific fine-tuning of DHDPS and AK genes coupled with the complete knockout of the catabolic enzyme LKR/SDH can rewire lysine metabolism for accumulation without the yield penalty. By using promoter editing to modify native genes, this approach also avoids the transgenic elements that created regulatory hurdles for earlier metabolic engineering efforts, potentially streamlining the path to consumer acceptance.

To ensure this strategy succeeds, integrated multi-omics and AI-driven modelling are critical for predicting metabolic trade-offs and identifying optimal editing targets. Furthermore, crop-specific strategies are essential to achieve a successful outcome. In maize, lysine improvement must not compromise biomass or yield, while in rice, nutritional enhancement must also preserve cooking quality. Finally, integrating these precise edits with speed breeding platforms will be vital for rapidly testing this hypothesis, evaluating agronomic performance, and deploying non-transgenic, high-lysine cereals that are finally free from the historical constraints of yield penalties and regulatory delays.
