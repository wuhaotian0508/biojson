# Summary

Grain chalkiness leads to the deterioration of grain appearance quality, which affects grain processing quality and the market value of rice. Gibberellin plays a crucial role in seed germination and plant growth, but its mechanism on endosperm starch synthesis and rice grain chalkiness formation remains largely elusive. Here, we identified a grain white belly (chalkiness in the belly area of grain) gene, SWB9, which encodes a kinesin-4 protein with a conserved ATPase domain and a coiled-coil domain. The mutation of SWB9 affects the starch structure, resulting in a grain white belly. SWB9 regulates endogenous gibberellin synthesis and accumulation in endosperm by directly binding to the promoter of ent-kaurene oxidase genes (KO1, KO2 and KOL5) encoding gibberellin-biosynthetic enzymes, and negatively regulates their expression. The loss of SWB9 function resulted in higher gibberellin content in the endosperm of swb9 than that of the wild type. Besides, a MYB transcription factor, MYB61 binds to the promoter of SWB9 and activates its expression. The grain of myb61 showed the same white belly phenotype as swb9, while overexpression of SWB9 in myb61 inhibited the grain white belly phenotype. Furthermore, the exogenous $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment showed increased grain chalkiness, and high gibberellin treatment can induce the reduced expression of MYB61, and then weaken the inhibitory effect of SWB9 on the expression of KO1, KO2 and KOL5, so as to break the homeostasis of endogenous gibberellin in the endosperm. Meanwhile, MYB61 directly binds to the promoter of amylopectin synthesis-related genes, SSIIa, BEIIb, ISA1 and PUL, at the GAMYB element and activates their expression, further affecting the distribution of amylopectin chain length. Our findings uncover a new insight into the gibberellin dose-dependent feedback regulation loop in rice endosperm that determines grain chalkiness formation. 

# Introduction

Genetic improvement of grain quality has gained more interest in rice breeding. Improvement in living standards has prompted an increase in demand for the consumption of good quality rice (Wu et al., 2022). Grain quality includes processing, appearance, cooking and eating, nutrition and milling quality (Fitzgerald et al., 2009; Gong et al., 2017). Chalkiness is an important factor in grain appearance quality. According to the site of chalkiness occurrence, chalkiness is divided into white back, white basal, white belly, white core and white milky (Zhao et al., 2022). Starch and storage proteins are the two major components of rice endosperm, accounting for approximately $80 \%$ and $10 \%$ , respectively (He et al., 2024). Natural or artificial defects in genes mediating starch and storage proteins biosynthesis portray abnormalities in the development of endosperm, eventually affecting the chalkiness phenotype (Cao et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2019). In recent years, several genes involving the formation of grain chalkiness have been found. However, the reasons for chalkiness formation are complex; the metabolic pathway and regulatory mechanism still need to be studied further. 

Kinesins are a class of motor proteins and can move along microtubule cables powered by the hydrolysis of ATP. The kinesin-4 

subfamily contains a highly conserved ATPase domain at the N-terminal region, a long coiled-coil domain in the middle, and a globular domain at the C-terminal end (Kong et al., 2015). Kinesin-4 proteins are involved in diverse cellular functions including cell division, cell elongation and transportation of cytoplasmic organelles (Hirokawa, 1998; Sheng et al., 2018). In Arabidopsis, Fragile Fiber1(FRA1) encoding a kinesin-4 protein is involved in the microtubule control of cellulose microfibril order and cell elongation (Zhu et al., 2015). VKS1 (Varied Kernel Size 1) encoding a kinesin-14 protein can regulate mitosis and cytokinesis in early maize endosperm development (Huang et al., 2019). BC12 (Brittle Culm12), a kinesin-4 protein, controls cell-cycle progression and wall properties in rice (Zhang et al., 2010). However, the roles of kinesin proteins in regulating grain quality in rice are not clear. 

Abundant studies have shown that gibberellin is crucial for seed germination, root and stem elongation, leaf expansion, flowering and fruit patterning (Fu and Harberd, 2003; Liao et al., 2019; Peng et al., 1999; Ueguchi-Tanaka et al., 2005). Gibberellin (GA) biosynthesis is a complex pathway and requires the synergistic cooperation of a series of enzymes including ent-copalyl diphosphate synthase 1 (CPS1), ent-kaurene synthase 1 (KS1), ent-kaurene oxidases (KO1, KO2, KO3, KO4 and KO5), ent-kaurenoic acid oxidase (KAO) and various GA oxidases (Li et al., 2022). In this study, we characterised a rice mutant with white belly endosperm, small and white belly 9 (swb9). SWB9 encoding a kinesin-4 protein is regulated by a MYB family transcription factor MYB61 and directly binds to the promoter of KOs (KO1, KO2 and KOL5) and suppresses their expression. Besides, high gibberellin induces the decreasing expression of MYB61, and MYB61 regulates the expression of SSIIa (starch synthase IIa), BEIIb (starch branching enzyme IIb), ISA1 (isoamylase1) and PUL (starch debranching enzyme) and then affects amylopectin chain length distribution. The study reveals a MYB61-SWB9-KOs molecular pathway to affect amylopectin chain length distribution by regulating gibberellin biosynthesis in rice endosperm, eventually determining grain chalkiness formation. 

# Results

# The swb9 mutant exhibits a defect in endosperm appearance

A mutant with white belly endosperm named swb9 (small and white belly 9) was obtained from a rice mutant library generated by ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) mutagenesis of japonica ‘Nipponbare’ (NIP). Compared with wild-type (WT), the grain endosperm of swb9 was opaque in the belly region while the rest of the region was transparent (Figure 1a). As previous studies confirmed, chalkiness is associated with changes in endosperm structure and component contents (She et al., 2010; Wu et al., 2022). Analysis of transverse sections of the endosperm using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) revealed that the starch granules (SGs) in the belly region of the swb9 endosperm cells were rounded and loosely packed while the SGs in WT endosperm cells were irregularly polyhedral and densely packed (Figure 1b). Transmission electron microscope (TEM) analysis of developing endosperm cells at 6 days after fertilisation (DAF) was 

carried out. Abundant incomplete compound starch granules were found in the belly region of endosperm cells of swb9 (Figure 1c), which suggested that the amyloplasts were disintegrated and the starch granules were dispersive in the belly endosperm cells. In addition, SEM analysis of starch or insoluble glucan granules purified from mature kernels of WT and swb9 showed that the individual SGs in the swb9 endosperm were much smaller compared with those in WT endosperm (Figure 1d,e). 

Compared with WT, the plant height, panicle length and primary branch number of swb9 significantly decreased, while days to heading and tiller number significantly increased (Figure S1A–J). Grain length and thickness of swb9 significantly decreased compared with WT (Figure S1K,M), while grain width significantly increased (Figure S1L). The swb9 mutant showed no difference in grain-filling rate compared with WT (Figure S2A). The thousand grain weight and the weight of the developing grain of swb9 significantly reduced compared with WT (Figures 1f and S2B,C). Quantification of chalkiness showed that chalky grain rate and chalkiness degree were significantly higher in swb9 compared with WT (Figure 1g,h). 

# Starch physicochemical properties are altered in the swb9 mutant

To figure out the differences in grain quality between swb9 and WT, we examined some starch physicochemical properties. The results demonstrated that protein and soluble sugar contents significantly increased in swb9 compared with WT (Figure S2D,E). Amylose and total starch contents in swb9 significantly reduced compared with WT (Figures 1i and S2F). To examine the alteration of amylopectin structure, we determined the chain length distribution of endosperm amylopectin in WT and swb9. Compared with WT, the proportions of chains with a degree of polymerisation (DP) obviously increased in the range of 6–12 and slightly increased in the range of 22–52 in swb9, while the proportions of chains with DP in the range of 13–21 decreased (Figure 1j). 

Besides, gel consistency was significantly lower in swb9 compared with WT (Figure S2G). The viscosity pattern of swb9 pasting starch was definitely similar to that of WT, while the viscosity of swb9 was always lower than that of WT at the same temperature (Figure S2H). Compared with WT, the onset, peak and conclusion gelatinisation temperature decreased in the swb9 endosperm starch. However, the gelatinisation enthalpy was a little higher (Figure S2I). # SWB9 encodes a nucleus and cytoplasm located kinesin protein

To identify the gene responsible for the swb9 phenotype, marker mapping and Mut-Map methods were used in combination, and the genetic analysis showed the chalkiness phenotype of the swb9 mutant was caused by a recessive mutation in a single gene (Figure S3). Eventually, we focussed on LOC_Os09g02650. To figure out the mutation site, we sequenced the genomic and coding DNA sequences of SWB9 in WT and the swb9 mutant. An SNP (A to T) on the ninth intron in the swb9 caused 8 bp deletion happening in the post-transcriptional splicing process, which resulted in early termination of translation (Figure 1k,l). To confirm that LOC_Os09g02650 is the responsible gene in regulating the white-belly phenotype, we generated transgenic complementation plants in the swb9 genetic background and knock-out plants in the NIP genetic background. These results of agronomic characters and starch physicochemical characteristics suggested that the complementation plants could restore the phenotype of the swb9 and the knock-out plants had a similar phenotype to the swb9 (Figures S4, S5 and 1m), which supported the candidate gene. 

We examined the expression of SWB9 in different tissues using reverse transcription-quantitative PCR; the results identified that SWB9 was expressed in all examined tissues, though they were highly expressed in stem and seed endosperm at 25 DAF (Figure S6A). SWB9 encodes a kinesin-4 protein of 1035 amino acids with a 117 KD molecular mass (Li et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2010). SWB9 protein was localised in the nucleus and the cytoplasm (Figure S6B). 

# MYB61 binds to the SWB9 promoter and activates its transcription

To investigate the possible molecular mechanism of SWB9 causing white belly in rice, we analysed the promoter region of SWB9 using the PlantCARE website (http://bioinformatics.psb. ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/). We identified a Myb-binding site in the promoter region of SWB9. According to previous reports, many MYB proteins were related to stress resistance (Chen et al., 2021; Qiu et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2022), but less were related to endosperm development. We constructed a $5 ^ { \prime } .$ - biotin-labelled DNA probe with a promoter region between 

400 bp and 1 bp to pull down SWB9 promoter-binding proteins. Firstly, we incubated the synthesised probe with nuclear protein extracts from 10 to 15 DAF NIP endosperm to pull down potential SWB9 promoter-binding proteins. After Sodium dodecyl sulphate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and silver staining, the potential target protein band enriched in SWB9-P (mixture including potential SWB9 promoter-binding protein) compared with the CK (mixture with no nuclear protein) was observed (Figure S7A). Then, this protein band was excised and analysed by mass spectrometry (MS). Based on the MS results, MYB61 (LOC_Os01g18240) belonging to the MYB family was identified as one of the potential SWB9 promoter-binding proteins (Figures 2a and S7B). 

To further determine the ability of MYB61 to bind to the SWB9 promoter, we divided the SWB9 promoter into three fragments to construct $\mathsf { p l a c Z i 2 \mu }$ vector for yeast one hybrid (Y1H) assays. The Y1H assays revealed that MYB61 could directly bind to the promoter of SWB9 in three different regions (Figure 2b). Moreover, we investigated the transactivation assay of MYB61 in a dual luciferase (LUC) reporter system. The three SWB9 promoter fragments were also used to construct 190LUC vectors as reporters that were transformed into rice protoplasts. The results showed that MYB61 had strong luciferase activity binding to the three different regions, which confirmed that MYB61 could bind to the SWB9 promoter in the three different regions to activate SWB9 expression (Figure 2c). Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) further confirmed that MYB61 could directly bind to the SWB9 promoter (Figures 2d and S7C). Besides, we obtained the myb61 mutant in the ZH11 background (Gao et al., 2020). The expression levels of SWB9 and MYB61 were remarkably reduced in the endosperm of myb61 compared with those in WT (Figures 2j and S9M), which were consistent with the results of the luciferase activity assay.

# The regulatory role of SWB9 in gibberellin synthesis is organ-specific

We used an RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) based transcriptome analysis with 15 DAF grains to further figure out the function of SWB9 in regulating grain quality. 447 up-regulated genes and 59 down-regulated genes were found (Figure S11A). KEGG analysis showed that the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) are mainly involved in sugar metabolism, biosynthesis of various plant secondary metabolites, and starch and sucrose metabolism (Figure S11B). Transcriptome and RT-qPCR analysis of transcript levels of genes related to starch synthesis in NIP and swb9 indicated that starch and sucrose metabolism were altered (Figures S11C and S12A). In addition, we focussed on diterpenoid biosynthesis, which is the precursor pathway for Gibberellin (GA) biosynthesis. Therefore, we supposed that SWB9 was related to 

gibberellin biosynthesis or signalling transduction pathway. To determine whether the GA signal transduction pathway was altered in swb9, we analysed its response to exogenous $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment. Rice seedlings at the three-leaf stage were exogenously treated with different concentrations of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ for 1 week. The sheath length of the second leaf of both swb9 and WT increased as the concentration of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ increased (Figure 3a). 

To determine whether the GA synthesis in swb9 endosperm was altered, we analysed the expression of various genes related to the GA biosynthesis pathway. The results revealed that the expression of KS1, GA3ox2, GA2ox3, KO1, KO2 and KOL5 genes significantly increased in swb9 endosperm compared with WT (Figure 3b). These results suggested that the endogenous level of GAs was higher in swb9 developing endosperm compared with the WT (Figure 3c). Meanwhile, the concentration of endogenous GAs in the swb9 leaf was significantly lower compared with WT (Figure 3d). Hence, we perceived the regulatory roles of SWB9 to KOs in endosperms and leaves were quite different and may be organ-specific.

# MYB61 activates transcription of amylopectin synthesis genes in a gibberellin dose-dependent way

We proved that high gibberellins in the swb9 endosperm affected starch synthesis and caused grain chalkiness. However, the concrete molecular mechanisms by which gibberellins regulate grain chalkiness are unclear. Previous studies have shown that the expression of MYB61 was indirectly induced by gibberellins signalling (Huang et al., 2015). We analysed the relative expression level of MYB61 in NIP endosperm with gradient concentration $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment. The results showed that the expression level of MYB61 reduced in endosperm under $1 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } _ { \ast }$ , $1 0 ~ \mu \ M$ and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mu \ G \ A _ { 3 }$ compared with $0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } ~ \mathsf { G } \mathsf { A } _ { 3 } ,$ , while it increased under $0 . 1 \ \mu \mathsf { M } \ \mathsf { G } \mathsf { A } _ { 3 }$ (Figure 6A). The results indicated that the expression level of MYB61 was sensitively inhibited by gibberellins in a dose-dependent way. MYB61 could bind a GAMYB element, a motif bound by GA-responsive MYBs (Huang et al., 2015; Washio, 2003). We analysed the promoter region of genes related to starch synthesis using PlantCARE (Figure S15A). We found the GAMYB element in the promoters of SSIIa, BEIIb, ISA1 and PUL, genes related to amylopectin synthesis. The results of yeast-one-hybrid, transient transactivation and EMSA assays showed that MYB61 could directly bind to the promoter of SSIIa, BEIIb, ISA1 and PUL and activate the expression of these genes, while MYB61 could not bind to the promoter of GBSSI (granulebound starch synthase) (Figures S15B and 6b,c). Besides, the expression levels of SSIIa, ISA1 and PUL were significantly reduced 

in the endosperm of myb61 compared with WT, while the expression level of GBSSI showed no difference (Figure 6d). These results proved that MYB61 regulated amylopectin synthesis by activating the expression of SSIIa, BEIIb, ISA1, and PUL. Therefore, high gibberellins increased grain chalkiness by suppressing the activation of MYB61 to four amylopectin synthesis genes. 

# Discussion

Genetic improvement of rice grain quality (RGQ) is a key target in rice breeding. Among various indicators of RGQ, grain appearance quality is of major concern. Chalkiness is a major component of grain appearance quality. There are many reasons for chalkiness; typically, abnormal starch development can affect endosperm development leading to chalkiness, and many genes involved in the starch synthesis pathway are directly related to chalkiness (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2022). Gibberellin (GA) plays significant roles in seed germination, root and stem elongation, leaf expansion and flowering (Liao et al., 2019; Qin et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2023; Yamaguchi et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2023). However, hardly any research on the role of GA in regulating grain chalkiness has been reported so far. GA is biosynthesised from geranylgerany1 diphosphate (GGDP) and are eventually biosynthesised under the influence of terpene synthases, ent-copalyl diphosphate synthase, and ent-kaurene synthase. Ent-kaurene oxidases (KO) catalyse the conversion of ent-kaurene to ent-kaurenoic acid, an early step of the GA biosynthetic pathway (Sakamoto et al., 2004). Several studies have reported the involvement of various KOs such as KO1, KO2 and KO5 in GA biosynthesis in rice (Li et al., 2011, 2022). Interestingly, in our study, we found that SWB9 could bind to the promoter of KOs (KO1, KO2 and KOL5) (Figure 3f,g). The expression level of KOs significantly increased in the endosperm of swb9 while significantly decreased in the leaf of swb9 compared with the WT (Figures 3B and S12F). Moreover, the endogenous level of GAs was higher in the swb9 endosperm while lower in the swb9 leaf compared with the WT (Figure 3c,d). Recent studies have reported that gene expression was organ-specific (Zhang et al., 2024). It implies to us that the regulatory pathways of SWB9-KOs in the endosperm and leaf of the swb9 mutant are quite different, and it may be organ-specific regulation. 

Nowadays, 136 species of gibberellins have been discovered, and four gibberellins $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 4 }$ and $\mathsf { G A } _ { 7 } )$ ) are highly bioactive in plants compared with other GAs (Hedden and Phillips, 2000; Yamaguchi, 2008). The conversion between bioactive and non-bioactive gibberellins maintains the homeostasis of gibberellins in plants. Bioactive $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ undergoes $2 \beta \cdot$ - hydroxylation catalysed by the action of gibberellin 2-oxidases (GA2ox) to form non-bioactive ${ \mathsf { G A } } _ { 8 } ,$ , which is the major pathway of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ inactivation. We measured the concentration of endogenous bioactive gibberellins $( \mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 4 }$ , GA ) in the developing endosperm of the swb9 mutant and the wild type. Specifically, the concentration of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ on 15 DAF and 30 DAF significantly decreased in the endosperm of the swb9 compared with WT, while those on other tested periods increased. The expression level of GA2ox3 was significantly increased in the swb9 endosperm compared with WT (Figure 3b). These results suggested that the process of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ inactivation could be accelerated by the increased expression level of $G A 2 0 x 1$ in the swb9 endosperm, which breaks the homeostasis of $\mathsf { G A } _ { 1 }$ by chance. SWB9 encodes a kinesin-4 protein with a conserved ATPase domain at the N-terminal region and a coiled-coil domain in the middle, and is a new allele of BC12/GDD1/MTD1. BC12/GDD1/MTD1 plays an important role in cell elongation and cell-cycle progression. The bc12/gdd1/mtd1 mutant portrayed dwarfism, reduced mechanical strength in culms and increased tiller number, but did not study and describe phenotypes related to grain quality (Li et al., 2011; Yu et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2010). In our study, swb9 showed a white belly chalky endosperm phenotype, indicating that the SWB9 has a new function in regulating the chalkiness of rice grains. In addition, we found that SWB9 regulates gibberellin biosynthesis in endosperm by suppressing KOs expression to further regulate grain chalkiness. The regulatory role of SWB9 in gibberellin synthesis is organ specific. Mutation of SWB9 leads to an increase in GA concentration in endosperm and the GA content in the swb9 leaves is reduced, which is consistent with that in gdd1 leaves (Li et al., 2011). Meanwhile, we noticed that the expression levels of CPS1 and GA2ox1 was significantly increased in the swb9 leaves, and the expression levels of KS1, KS2, GA20ox2, GA3ox2 and GA2ox3 were strongly reduced in 

the swb9 leaves, while these genes were unaffected in gdd1 (Li et al., 2011; Figure S12F). This may be due to a different genetic background or mutation pattern. 

We revealed a MYB61-SWB9-KOs module regulating grain chalkiness via gibberellin biosynthesis in rice endosperm. In this molecular pathway, SWB9, a kinesin-4 protein located in the nucleus and cytoplasm, mainly functions as a transcription factor reflecting the function of nuclear localisation. Kinesins are a member of the intracellular transport system and use microtubules as tracks to transport diverse cellular cargos (Sharp et al., 2000). Functional loss of kinesins affects microtubule stability, which regulates cell size and cell division (Drummond, 2011). Rice microtubule-localised protein OsIQD14 (OsIQ67-DOMAIN14) regulates microtubule rearrangements in rice hull cells to control grain shape (Yang et al., 2019). SRG (Small and Round Grain) with a kinesin motor domain, located in the nucleus and membranes, regulates cell division and expansion (Miao et al., 2025). VKS1 (Varied Kernel Size 1), a kinesin-14 protein located in microtubules and the nucleus, regulates mitosis and cytokinesis in early maize endosperm development (Huang et al., 2019). According to these previous reports, kinesins can regulate grain size and endosperm development, which suggests the cytoplasm-localised function of SWB9 to regulate grain size 

and the formation of chalkiness. The grain length, width and thickness of the swb9 mutant had great changes compared with WT; hence, we propose that the grain size is regulated by SWB9 as a kinesin protein. Meanwhile, we believe SWB9, as a kinesin protein, can also regulate grain chalkiness. 

MYB transcription factor family plays essential roles in plant growth and development, including cell morphology and response to plant multiple stresses, while less in grain quality (Cominelli and Tonelli, 2009; Liu et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2017). In this study, we discovered that MYB61 could activate SWB9 expression and regulate the formation of rice grain chalkiness. Besides, MYB61 had a small effect on plant stature while plant height was severely reduced in the swb9 mutant (Figures S1A, S9B and S10D). It indicated that SWB9 had a pleiotropic effect on plant and grain phenotype and MYB61-SWB9 pathway mostly regulated the grain phenotype. Interestingly, we found the expression level of MYB61 in endosperm was reduced under high $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment and MYB61 could regulate the expression of SSIIa, BEIIb, ISA1 and PUL. Amylopectin synthesis is determined by starch synthase (SS), starch branching enzyme (SBE) and starch debranching enzyme (DBE) (Bahaji et al., 2014; Dong et al., 2024; Satoh et al., 2003). SSIIa and BEIIb are the members of SS and SBE, respectively. ISA1 and PUL are the members of DBE. Starch structure is related to amylopectin chain length distribution and starch granule morphology. Amylopectin chains are formed of A chains (DP 6–12), B chains $( \mathsf { D P } > 1 3 )$ ) and C backbone chains (Pfister et al., 2014). Previous studies reported chalkiness was related to more A chains (Wang et al., 2022). Our results revealed that there are more A chains in swb9, myb61 and OE-KOs compared with WT (Figures 1j, 2h and 4d–f). NIP with $1 0 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment showed significantly higher chalkiness phenotype and had obviously more A chains compared with $0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } \mathsf { G } \mathsf { A } _ { 3 }$ treatment, while NIP with $0 . 1 { - } 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ GA3 treatment indicated significantly lower chalkiness phenotype and had fewer A chains (Figure 5a–c,h). Besides, the granule morphology of chalky starch was rounded and loosely packed (Figures 1m and 2e). Therefore, we considered high gibberellins concentration increases amylopectin A chains and affects SGs morphology, and thus increases grain chalkiness. This finding revealed that MYB61 was a crucial link between gibberellins and grain chalkiness, which plays a crucial role in explaining how gibberellins regulate the formation of grain chalkiness. Based on the results obtained in this study, we propose a model for the role of gibberellin in regulating grain chalkiness (Figure 7). The MYB61-SWB9-KOs module regulates grain appearance quality via maintaining GA homeostasis in rice endosperm. These findings uncover a new insight into the GA feedback loop in rice endosperm determining grain chalkiness formation by regulating the distribution of amylopectin chain length. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The swb9 mutant was identified from an EMS-induced mutant library of Oryza Sativa japonica cultivar Nipponbare (NIP). Two $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ populations, the cross of swb9 $\times$ Taichung Native 1 (TN1, indica) and swb9 9 NIP, were used for genetic analysis and gene mapping. The gene knockout lines in the study were generated by CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing technology. A genomic fragment including the $2 . 0 – \boldsymbol { \mathrm { k b } }$ promoter and the coding sequence of SWB9 was cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector to generate a 

complementation line. The gene coding sequences were cloned into the pCUbi1390 vector to generate overexpression lines. All plants used in this study were grown in paddy fields during the normal growing seasons at the China National Rice Research Institute in Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province of China. Water and fertilisers were applied according to the standard field cultivation methods. 

# Microscopy analysis

The brown rice was cut transversely to prepare samples as described previously for scanning electron microscopy analysis (Kang et al., 2005). Images were obtained with a HITACHI S3400N scanning electron microscope (S-3400N, Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan). To observe the ultrastructure of amyloplasts, 6, 9, and 12 DAF grains were observed with a transmission electron microscope (H-7650, Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan). 

# Analysis of starch physicochemical properties in mature grains

Chalky grain rate and chalkiness degree were analysed according to the China Agriculture Industry Standard NY/T 83-2017 (Agricultural Industry Standard of China, 2017). Total starch and amylose contents were measured using a starch assay kit (Megazyme, Wicklow, Ireland) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Total protein content and total soluble sugar content were determined according to the method described previously (Kang et al., 2005; Li et al., 2017; Masuko et al., 2005). To determine the distribution of amylopectin chain length, $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of milled rice flour was weighed into a 25 mL volumetric flask and added with 5 mL 0.05 M NaAc solution at PH 3.5. The samples were heated at $1 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min and digested with isoamylase (Megazyme, Wicklow, Ireland), then measured using capillary electrophoresis (PA800plus Pharmaceutical Analysis System, Beckman Coulter, CA, USA). Gel consistency was determined and analysed according to the China Agriculture Industry Standard NY/T147-88 (Agricultural Industry Standard of China, 2002). To determine the pasting properties of endosperm starch, 3 g of milled rice flour $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ or less, $1 4 \%$ moisture basis) was transferred into a sample vessel containing $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of distilled water. The sample was mixed and measured by Rapid Visco Analyser (RVA Techmaster, Newport Scientific, Narrabeen, Australia). Thermal characteristics were measured with a Modulated Differential Scanning Calorimeter (MDSC, DSC1 STARe system, METTLERTOLEDO) as described previously with minor modifications (Kweon et al., 2000). The gelatinisation and swelling modes of endosperm starch in urea solution were measured according to a previous report (Nishi et al., 2001). 

# Identification of SWB9

To map the SWB9 locus, 239 individuals with opaque endosperm were selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from swb9 9 TN1. The polymorphic SSR markers were selected to link to SWB9. The Mut-map method was used to map SWB9 according to the previous description (Abe et al., 2012). A recessive pool containing 20 individuals with white belly endosperm was selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from swb9 9 NIP; NIP was the dominant pool. Both the bulked DNA samples were subjected to whole-genome sequencing using an Illumina HiSeqTM PE150 platform (Novogene, Beijing, China). These short reads were aligned to the Nipponbare reference sequence to enable the identification of reliable SNPs. # Subcellular localisation of SWB9

To verify the subcellular localisation of SWB9, the coding regions without a termination codon were cloned into the pYBA1132- GFP vectors. The fusion constructs and an empty control vector (pYBA1132-GFP) were transformed into rice protoplasts separately. GFP fluorescence signals were detected using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica TCS SP5, Germany). The primers used in this assay are listed in Table S1. 

# DNA pull-down assay

A biotin-labelled double-stranded oligonucleotide probe for the 400 to 1 fragment of the SWB9 promoter sequence was commercially synthesised and labelled using an EMSA Probe 

Biotin Labeling Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). The nuclear protein was extracted from Nipponbare seeds using a Plant Nuclear Protein Extraction Kit (X–Y Biotechnology, Shanghai, China). Briefly, 1 mg of total protein extract was mixed and incubated with $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of probe and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of streptavidin beads (Invitrogen, Shanghai, China). The mixtures were then isolated using a Magnetic shelf and boiled at $1 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min. The collected samples containing the bound proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and further silver stained using a PAGE Gel Silver Staining Kit (Solarbio, Beijing, China). Silver-dyed protein strips were used for MS experiments commercially performed by Luming Biological Technology (Shanghai, China). 

# Yeast-one-hybrid assay

For the yeast-one-hybrid assay, the CDS of MYB61 and SWB9 was cloned into the pB42AD vector, while different truncated versions of the promoter sequences of SWB9 and the promoter regions of KO1, KO2, and KOL5 were cloned into the $\mathsf { p l a c Z i 2 \mu }$ vector. Both bait and prey plasmids were co-transformed into yeast strain EGY48. The assay was performed using a Clontech 

Yeast-One-Hybrid System (Takara, Beijing, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. The primers used in this assay are listed in Table S1. 

# Transactivation activity assay in yeast

The coding sequence of SWB9 and VP16 was fused to the GAL4- DNA-binding domain in pGBKT7. The pGBKT7, pGBKT7-SWB9, pGBKT7-VP16 and pGBKT7-VP16-SWB9 vectors were transformed into the yeast strain Y2HGold. The assay was performed using a yeast transcription assay kit (Coolaber, Beijing, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. The primers used in this assay are listed in the Table S1. 

# Luciferase transient transactivation assay

For the luciferase activity assay, the different truncated versions of the promoter sequences of SWB9 and the promoter regions of KO1, KO2 and KOL5 were inserted into the 190LUC vector as reporters. The coding sequences of MYB61 and SWB9 were inserted into the NONE vector as effectors. To analyse the transcriptional activity of SWB9, the coding sequences of SWB9 were inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK-BD and pGreenII 62-SK-BD-VP16 vectors as effectors. pGreenII 0800-35S-5 9 GAL4-LUC vector was used as a reporter. The protoplasts were extracted from the shoots of two-week-old seedlings of 9311, an indica rice. The LUC signal was detected by the Dual-Luciferase $\textcircled{1}$ Reporter Assay System (Promega E1910) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The relative LUC activity was calculated from the ratio of LUC/Renilla LUC (REN). Three biological replicates were used for each sample. The primers used in this assay are listed in the Table S1. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The MBP-MYB61 and GST-SWB9 proteins were purified. The EMSA probes were commercially synthesised by Sunya Biological Technology (Hangzhou, China). The EMSA Probe Biotin Labelling Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) was used to label the probes, and non-labelled probes were used as competitors. Probe sequences are listed in the Table S1. The DNA binding reaction was performed for 20 min at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and then electrophoresed using $6 \%$ acrylamide gels. The DNA probes were transferred to a nylon membrane (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). Finally, the oligo bands were detected using a Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). 

# RNA-sequencing and quantitative real-time PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis

The construction and deep sequencing of RNA libraries were accomplished with the assistance of Gene+ Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China) on the DNBSEQ-T7/Gene+Seq-2000 platform. The differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were performed using DEseq2 and defined by an FDR (false discovery rate) $\le 0 . 0 5$ and an absolute value of log2 ratio ≥1 with DEseq2. 

Total RNA was extracted from different plant tissues by using a Trizol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). RNA was reversetranscribed into cDNA using a ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Kit (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan). qPCR was performed using a Light Cycler 480 device (Roche) with the SYBR Green Real-time PCR Master Mix (Toyobo). The RT-qPCR conditions were as follows: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 30 s, 40 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $( 5 \ s + 1 5 \ s )$ and $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 35 s. The gene-specific primers were described previously (She et al., 2010). The Ubiquitin gene (Os03g0234200) was used as an internal control, and the relative expression level was calculated by the 

$2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T }$ method (Schmittgen and Livak, 2008). The primer sequences used in this analysis are listed in the Table S1. 

# Quantification of endogenous GAs

Fresh rice grains were frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground to a fine powder and extracted with a mixed extract containing isopropyl alcohol, hydrochloric acid and water. An internal standard solution was added to the samples in the pretreatment process. The samples were quantified and identified using an HPLC (Agilent1290, USA)-MS/MS (AB SCIEX-6500Qtrap, AB SCIEX, USA) by the Nanjing Kawensi Testing Technology Co., Ltd (Nanjing, China). Three biological replicates were used for each sample. 

# Statistical analysis

A two-sided Student’s t-test was used to test the significance of differences between two groups using Excel 2021. Duncan’s test was performed using GraphPad Prism v.8.0.2. The chi-square test was performed using the chisq.test() function in R v.4.3.2.
