# Gibberellin signaling regulates pectin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis

Received: 23 September 2024 

Accepted: 14 April 2025 

Published online: 30 April 2025 

Check for updates 

Yan Xu 1,6, Jinge Du1,2,6, Ruili Hao1 , Siqi Ma 3 , Yujiao Ma1 , Gongke Zhou4 , Ruibo Hu 1,5 & Shengjun Li 1,5 

Pectin is an abundant polysaccharide with essential roles in various biological processes. Despite considerable advances in understanding the regulatory mechanisms of pectin biosynthesis, the influence of phytohormones on this process remains unclear. Here we report that gibberellins (GA) promotes pectin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis. The DELLA proteins, as GA signaling repressors, interact with TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA2 (TTG2) and components of the MYB-bHLH-WD40 (MBW) complex, the key regulators of pectin biosynthesis, to repress their transcriptional regulatory activities. Furthermore, the MBW proteins and TTG2 physically interact and synergistically activate the downstream target GLABRA2, whereas this interaction and collaboration are competitively attenuated by DELLAs. Genetic analyses validate that GA-mediated pectin biosynthesis relies on functional TTG2 and MBW proteins. Moreover, the pectin biosynthesis mediated by the GA-DELLA-MBW-TTG2 module contributes to GA-regulated seedling growth. Our findings reveal the significance of the GA-DELLA-MBW-TTG2 signaling cascade in the regulation of pectin biosynthesis and plant development. 

Pectin is a class of heterogeneous polymers composed of acidic polysaccharides that play essential roles in various biological processes, including cell adhesion, wall integrity maintenance, and environmental sensing1–4 . Pectin polysaccharides are generally classified into three major types, namely homogalacturonan (HG), rhamnogalacturonan-I (RG-I), and rhamnogalacturonan-II (RG-II)4 . The biosynthesis of pectin occurs in the Golgi apparatus, catalyzed by various interconversion enzymes, including glycosyltransferases (GTs), pectin methyltransferases (PMTs), and pectin acetyltransferases5 . For instance, HG is synthesized by the galacturonosyltransferase (GAUT) family of GTs, including GAUT16 , GAUT47 , GAUT76 , GAUT8/QUASIMODO1 (QUA1)8 , and GAUT119 , which transfer D-galacturonic acid (D-GalA) from UDP-D-GalA to the forming HG backbone. Additionally, PMTs such as QUA2/TUMOROUS SHOOT DEVELOPMENT2 (TSD2)10–12, QUA313, 

COTTON GOLGI-RELATED2 (CGR2), and CGR314 add methyl groups to the C6 carboxyl of GalA. In the process of RG-I biosynthesis, RHAMNOSE BIOSYNTHESIS2 (RHM2)/MUCILAGE-MODIFIED4 (MUM4) and UDP-Rha/UDP-Gal TRANSPORTER2 (URGT2) are responsible for the biosynthesis and transport of the UDP-L-rhamnose (Rha) substrate, respectively15–18. RG-I: Rhamnosyltransferase1 (RRT1) and RG-I:Galacturonosyltransferase1 (RGGAT1)/MUCILAGE-RELATED 70 (MUCI70) transfer L-Rha and D-GalA, respectively, to the developing RG-I backbone19,20. Furthermore, GALACTURONOSYLTRANSFERASE-LIKE 5 (GATL5) also participates in the biosynthesis of RG-I21.

hemicelluloses22,23. Recent studies utilizing the Arabidopsis seed mucilage as a model system have unveiled a multilayered transcriptional regulatory network governing pectin biosynthesis24. Remarkably, the MYB-bHLH-WD40 (MBW) complex, consisting of the WD40 protein TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA1 (TTG1), MYB transcription factors (TFs) MYB5 and TRANSPARENT TESTA2 (TT2), and bHLH TFs ENHANCER OF GLABRA 3 (EGL3) and TT8, plays a crucial role in controlling seed mucilage biosynthesis25–27. The MBW complex directly activates the expression of GLABRA2 (GL2), encoding a homeobox TF that regulates RG-I biosynthesis by activating the expression of MUM4, RRT1, and $G A T L 5 ^ { 2 8 , 2 9 }$ . In addition, the WRKY TF TTG2, another downstream target of the MBW complex, directly promotes GL2 transcription28,29. Despite considerable advances in understanding the regulatory mechanisms of pectin biosynthesis, the involvement of phytohormones in this process remains largely unexplored. 

Gibberellin acid (GA) is a crucial phytohormone that controls diverse aspects of plant growth and development, such as seed germination, hypocotyl elongation, leaf expansion, and floral transition30. Upon GA binding to its receptor GIBBERELLIN-INSENSITIVE DWARF1 (GID1), GID1 undergoes a conformational switch and interacts with DELLAs, the master negative regulators of GA signaling31,32. The formation of the GA-GID1-DELLA complex enhances the interaction of DELLAs with an F-box type E3 ubiquitin ligase, resulting in the degradation of DELLAs and subsequent the activation of downstream GAresponsive processes by various TFs. The Arabidopsis genome encodes five DELLA proteins, namely REPRESSOR OF ga1-3 (RGA), GIBBERELLIC ACID INSENSITIVE (GAI), RGA-LIKE 1 (RGL1), RGL2, and RGL3, performing partially redundant functions in distinct tissues and organs33–35. Previous studies have indicated that GA plays a crucial role in the development of Arabidopsis seed coat36, where seed mucilage is synthesized. Furthermore, GAs are highly biosynthesized in seeds during the linear- to bending-cotyledon stages37, coinciding with the period of mucilage production38. These findings suggest a potential involvement of GA in seed mucilage production and pectin biosynthesis. 

In this study, we revealed the pivotal role of GA signaling in regulating pectin biosynthesis in seed coat and seedlings. Enhanced GA signaling, achieved through exogenous GA application or DELLA disruptions, increases pectin biosynthesis, whereas decreased GA levels have the opposite effects. DELLAs physically interact with components of the MBW complex, hindering its assembly and transcriptional regulatory activity. On the other hand, DELLAs physically interact with TTG2, interfering with the TTG2-GL2 regulatory pathway. Importantly, the MBW proteins and TTG2 physically interact and synergistically activate GL2 expression, whereas DELLAs competitively attenuate this interaction and collaboration. Through genetic analyses, we revealed that GA-DELLA signaling regulates pectin biosynthesis by modulating the functions of the MBW proteins and TTG2. Furthermore, this signaling cascade plays a crucial role in the GA-regulated seedling development by modulating cell adhesion. Altogether, our findings reveal the significant role of GA-DELLAs signaling in the intricate molecular regulation of pectin biosynthesis. 

# Results

# GA plays a crucial role in pectin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis seed coat

To explore the role of GA in pectin biosynthesis, we first investigated its effect on the production of Arabidopsis seed mucilage, which is a well-studied model system of pectin metabolism. We treated the wildtype (WT) plants with excess $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ , an active form of GA, or paclobutrazol (PAC), an inhibitor of GA biosynthesis, and examined the seed mucilage production. ${ \bf G } { \bf A } _ { 3 }$ treatment significantly increased mucilage accumulation in the WT seeds, while PAC application remarkably reduced mucilage level (Fig.1a–c and Supplementary Fig. 1a, b). Correspondingly, the expression levels of genes involved in mucilage 

pectin biosynthesis, such as GL2, MUM4, GATL5, RRT1, and URGT2 were dramatically up-regulated by $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ and down-regulated by PAC (Fig. 1d). Subsequently, we examined the mucilage phenotype of the GAdeficient mutant ga1-3. Since ga1-3 is infertile without exogenous GA application39, we infiltrated its inflorescence once with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M G } \mu _ { 3 }$ and acquired a small number of seeds, which produced only a little mucilage (Fig. 1a–c and Supplementary Fig. 1c). Interestingly, its mucilage amount was further increased by $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \ G \ A _ { 3 }$ infiltration, and reached the WT-level after a daily application of $1 0 0 \mu \mu \ G \ A _ { 3 }$ (Fig. 1a–c). Similarly, ga3ox1-3, a GA biosynthesis mutant with moderate defect40, produced significantly reduced mucilage compared to the Columbia-0 (Col-0) control, while this defect was completely restored by exogenous ${ \bf G } { \bf A } _ { 3 }$ application (Fig. 1e–g). Correspondingly, the expression of mucilage biosynthesis genes was down-regulated in ga3ox1-3 (Fig. 1h). Collectively, these results suggest that GA positively regulates the biosynthesis of pectin in seed mucilage. 

# DELLA proteins inhibit GA-mediated pectin biosynthesis in seed coat

Given the pivotal roles of DELLA proteins in GA signaling, we investigated their potential involvement in seed mucilage production. Gene expression analyses revealed that DELLAs expression negatively correlated with the expression of mucilage synthesis genes during seed development (Supplementary Fig. 2), indicating a negative regulatory role of DELLAs in seed mucilage synthesis. Next, we examined the seed mucilage phenotypes of mutants with defective DELLA proteins, including rga- $\cdot 2 8 ^ { 3 5 }$ , rga-24 gai-t6 (dellaD) 41, rga-t2 gai-t6 rgl1-1 rgl2-1 $( d e l l a Q ) ^ { 4 2 }$ , and rga-t2 gai-t6 rgl1-1 rgl2-1 rgl3-1 (dellaP) 43. There was no significant difference between rga-28 and the Col-0 control (Supplementary Fig. 3a, b). However, compared to the Landsberg erecta (Ler) control, dellaD showed a significant increase of mucilage content, which was further increased in dellaQ, with a comparable level to dellaP (Fig. 2a–c and Supplementary Fig. 3c, d). Additionally, the dellaD, dellaQ, and dellaP mutants were insensitive to $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ or PAC treatment concerning seed mucilage production (Fig. 2a–c and Supplementary Fig. 3c, d). Moreover, the mucilage defect phenotype of ga1-3 was partially rescued by the rga-2 mutation (ga1-3 rga- $2 ^ { 3 9 } .$ ), and completely rescued by the rga-24 gai-t6 double mutations (ga1-3 dellaD41) (Fig. 2d, e). Taken together, these results indicate that DELLA proteins are partially redundant in inhibiting GA-mediated seed mucilage production, with RGA and GAI playing dominant roles, RGL1 and RGL2 playing moderate roles, while RGL3 has a minor or negligible role. 

We further conducted cytological observations of the developing seeds from Ler and dellaP. The seed coat cells of dellaP exhibited enlarged cell size and elevated mucilage production compared to those of Ler (Fig. 2f). In addition, scanning electron microscopy of mature dry seeds revealed significant enlargement of seed coat cells in dellaP (Supplementary Fig. 3e, f). These observations suggest that disruptions of DELLAs result in enlarged seed coat cells and increased mucilage production. 

As Arabidopsis seed mucilage is mainly composed of RG-I, we performed immunolabeling assays with an RG-I-specific antibody, CCRC-M3644, to investigate the potential alteration of RG-I content in the mucilage of della mutants. In line with the elevated mucilage amount, dellaD, dellaQ, and dellaP mutants showed significantly increased CCRC-M36 labeling areas compared with Ler (Fig. 2g and Supplementary Fig. 3g). Similarly, ${ \bf G } { \bf A } _ { 3 }$ treatment enlarged the CCRC-M36-labeled area in WT (Supplementary Fig. 3h). In addition, an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) was performed with Ler and dellaP mucilage using the CCRC-M36 antibody, confirming a noteworthy increase in RG-I content (Fig. 2h). Accordingly, quantification of monosaccharide compositions demonstrated that the dellaP mucilage contained significantly increased levels of GalA and Rha (Fig. 2i), the main hydrolysates of RG-I. Consistent with these 

observations, genes required for RG-I biosynthesis showed increased transcription levels in dellaP compared to Ler (Fig. 2j). These results indicate that GA-DELLA signaling plays a crucial role in pectin biosynthesis in seed mucilage. 

# DELLAs interacts with components of the MBW complex

Given that DELLAs typically regulate the expression of downstream genes by interacting with various $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { T F } } \boldsymbol { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 3 1 }$ , we performed a series of yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays to explore if DELLAs interact with the reported upstream regulators of seed mucilage biosynthesis. We first investigated the potential interactions of DELLAs with TTG1, MYB5, TT2, EGL3, and TT8, which are components of the MBW complex in regulating seed mucilage production25–27. Since the full-length DELLAs and DELLAsN (containing the N-terminal DELLA domain), along with MYB5 and TT2, exhibited auto-activation in yeast cells when fused with GAL4 DNA binding domain (BD) (Supplementary Fig. 4), we resorted to examine the interactions between BD-fused DELLAsC (containing the C-terminal GRAS domain) and the activation domain (AD)-fused MBW components. The results showed that ${ \mathsf { G A l } } ^ { \mathrm { c } }$ physically interacts with TTG1, MYB5, and TT2, while $\mathsf { R G A } ^ { \mathrm { c } }$ just displays interaction with MYB5 and TT2. In contrast, ${ \mathsf { R G L 1 } } ^ { \mathrm { c } }$ , RGL2C , and ${ \tt R G L 3 } ^ { \tt C }$ interact with all five proteins of the MBW complex (Fig. 3a). To validate the interactions between DELLAs and MBW proteins in plant cells, GAI was selected as the representative DELLA protein in the subsequent analyses. We next 

performed split luciferase (split-LUC) complementation assays in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. Strong LUC signals were detected upon co-expression of GAI-nLUC with cLUC-TTG1, cLUC-MYB5, or cLUC-TT2, while no interaction signal was observed when these nLUC- or cLUC-fused proteins were co-expressed with cLUC or nLUC, respectively (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Fig. 5a). Moreover, coimmunoprecipitation (co-IP) assays showed that TTG1 and MYB5 were co-precipitated with GAI in N. benthamiana leaves (Fig. 3c, d). In addition, immunoprecipitation coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (IP-MS) and co-IP assays using developing siliques from pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2 and 35S:GFP transgenic plants revealed that RGA, GAI, and RGL2 co-precipitated with MYB5-GFP, but not with the GFP control (Fig. 3e and Supplementary Data 1), demonstrating that these DELLAs physically interact with MYB5 in vivo.

# Discussion

Pectin is a class of complex acidic polysaccharides abundant in plant primary cell walls, the middle lamella, and seed mucilage, playing crucial roles in plant development and stress responses1–3 . Considerable progress has been made recently in elucidating the molecular mechanisms regulating pectin biosynthesis24. However, it remains unclear if phytohormones participate in this process. Despite the well-known role of GA in regulating plant growth and development, its relationship with pectin biosynthesis remains unexplored. This study reveals the crucial role of GA in pectin biosynthesis and provides deep insights into the underlying molecular mechanisms. We uncovered a GA-DELLA-MBW-TTG2 signaling module governing pectin biosynthesis in seed coat and seedlings. Under low GA conditions, DELLAs interact with TTG2 and components of the MBW complex to interfere with their interactions and 

repress their transcriptional regulatory activities, leading to impaired expression of downstream genes (e.g., GL2) and reduced pectin biosynthesis (Fig. 10a). However, elevated levels of GA trigger the degradation of DELLAs, releasing TTG2, MYB5, TTG1, and EGL3 to form functional complexes and activate the expression of downstream genes including GL2. This leads to enhanced pectin biosynthesis, which contributes to seed mucilage production and seedling growth (Fig. 10b). 

As a critical structural component of the cell wall, pectin interacts with other wall components (e.g., cellulose, hemicelluloses, and lignin) and influences their deposition and/or modification, thereby playing a crucial role in determining cell wall structural integrity, which is critical for plant development and defense, as well as biofuel production1,4,47. For instance, reduction of HG levels by down-regulating the GAUT4 gene in poplar and switchgrass leads to altered wall structure, resulting in enhanced biomass yield and saccharification efficiency7 . Therefore, the significant role of GA signaling in pectin biosynthesis highlights the potential of this regulatory pathway to improve biomass production and stress resistance. 

HG, constituting ${ \sim } 6 5 \%$ of total pectin, is the most abundant and thoroughly studied pectin subtype1,5 . It plays a crucial role in cell-cell adhesion and plant growth. For instance, the mutations of HG biosynthesis genes QUA1, QUA2, and Na+ AND $K ^ { + }$ -SENSITIVE 1 (NKS1) resulted in stunted growth accompanied by severe defects in cell adhesion2,8,11,12. Similarly, defects in growth and cell adhesion were observed in ga1-3, ttg1-1, and ttg2-1 seedlings, albeit the cell adhesion phenotype was not as severe as that in qua1, qua2, and nks1 mutants. This implies that the GA-DELLA-MBW-TTG2 module regulates seedling growth likely via modulating HG biosynthesis. GA has been widely 

known to promote plant growth via mediating cell elongation and proliferation48,49. Our findings extend its function to cell-cell adhesion and provide new insights into the cytological mechanisms by which GA stimulates plant growth. 

As a key regulator of RG-I biosynthesis in seed coat, GL2 also regulates pectin biosynthesis in seedlings, evidenced by the reduced GalA content in the gl2-8 seedlings. Interestingly, unlike ga1-3, ttg1-1, and ttg2-1, the gl2-8 seedlings exhibited no HG-deficit phenotype. This suggests the potential involvement of additional factor(s) that function redundantly or parallelly with GL2 in the regulatory pathway of HG-mediated growth. Alternatively, it is possible that GL2 regulates the biosynthesis of RG-I rather than HG in seedlings. RG-I constitutes $20 \%$ of total pectin5 . However, the understanding of its biological role lags behind that of HG. Besides GL2, several genes (e.g., DF1, MUM4, URGT2, RRT1, and GATL5) responsible for RG-I biosynthesis have been identified over the past two decades by utilizing Arabidopsis seed mucilage system24, further studies on these genes are required to clarify the potential role of RG-I in plant growth and development. 

TTG2 and GL2 are known as direct targets of the MBW complex in regulating mucilage biosynthesis29. Recently, we have reported that TTG2 could directly activate GL2 expression28. Here, we provide new findings that TTG2 and the MBW components (MYB5 and EGL3) physically interact and synergistically activate GL2 expression. Therefore, besides the MBW complex, another activator complex comprising at least TTG2 and MYB5/EGL3 can be formed to activate GL2 expression (Fig. 10b). Surprisingly, in our IP-MS assays using developing siliques of pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2, none of TTG2 nor other TFs of the MBW complex (i.e., TTG1, EGL3, and TT8) were precipitated (Supplementary Data 1). One possibility is that these TF complexes are inherently unstable or formed only transiently during seed development. Alternatively, the GFP tag fused to the C-terminus of MYB5 might cause a steric hindrance, impeding the interaction between MYB5 and TTG1/ EGL3/TT8/TTG2. 

Moreover, since the MYB and bHLH components of the MBW complex vary among different biological processes26,27,50, it is intriguing to clarify the TTG1-associated MYB and bHLH TFs involved in the regulation of pectin biosynthesis during seedling development. MYB5 and TT2 are partially redundant in regulating seed mucilage biosynthesis. The myb5 tt2 double mutant shows more severe mucilage defects than the myb5 single mutant, despite that the tt2 single mutant has no mucilage phenotype27. In seedlings, although MYB5 overexpression rescued the seedling growth defects of ga1-3 and ga3ox1-3, the myb5-2 single mutant exhibited largely normal seedling growth without significant defects in pectin biosynthesis or cell adhesion (Supplementary Fig. 11). This suggests that MYB5 might either function redundantly with other MYB TF(s) or play a relatively minor role in the regulation of pectin biosynthesis during seedling development. In the future, identification of TTG1-interacting MYB and bHLH TFs in seedlings will facilitate elucidating the components of the MBW complex governing pectin biosynthesis in seedlings. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The Arabidopsis thaliana ecotypes Landsberg (Ler) and Columbia-0 (Col-0) were used in this study. Mutant lines rga-24 gai-t6 (dellaD), rgat2 gai-t6 rgl1-1 rgl2-1 (dellaQ), and ga1-3 rga-24 gai-t6 (ga1-3 dellaD) were provided by Prof. Xiangdong Fu at the Institute of Genetics and Developmental Biology, Chinese Academy of Sciences. Mutant lines ga1-3 (CS3104), ga1-3 rga-2 (CS3742), ga3ox1-3 (CS6943), rga-28 (SALK_089146C), rga-t2 gai-t6 rgl1-1 rgl2-1 rgl3-1 (dellaP, CS16298), ttg1-1 (CS89), myb5-2 (SALK_105723C), ttg2-1 (CS277), and gl2-8 (SALK_130213) were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (http://www.Arabidopsis.org). To generate the pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2 complementary lines, a genomic sequence of MYB5 (−926 to $+ 1 2 0 2$ ) containing its promoter and coding region 

was inserted into the EcoR I site of the pCAMBIA2301 vector, with GFP inserted before the stop codon of MYB5. This construct was subsequently introduced into the myb5-2 mutant by the floral dip method. The pTTG2:TTG2-GFP/ttg2 line was documented in our previous study28. To generate the 35S:6MYC-MYB5 transgenic lines, the fulllength coding sequence of MYB5 was amplified and inserted into the EcoR I site of a modified pBI121-6MYC vector, which was subsequently introduced into Ler, ga1-3, or ga3ox1-3 by the floral dip method. The transgenic lines were screened for resistance to kanamycin $( 2 5 \mathrm { m g L ^ { - 1 } } )$ . Arabidopsis seeds were surface-sterilized and synchronized for 2 days at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ in darkness and then sown on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (1/2 MS) solid medium. Seeds in the ga1-3 background were soaked with $1 0 0 \mu \ M \ G \ A _ { 3 }$ for 3 days before sowing to promote germination. The plants were grown at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under long-day conditions (16 h light/ 8 h dark) in a growth chamber. 

# $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ and PAC treatments

For the $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ or PAC treatment of seedlings, surface-sterilized seeds were sown on 1/2 MS solid medium supplemented with $1 0 0 \mu \ M \ G \ A _ { 3 }$ or $1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ PAC. For the $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ or PAC treatment of plants grown in soil, plants after the transition to flowering were consistently sprayed with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ or $1 0 / 5 0 / 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (as indicated) PAC daily until the end of flowering. Alternatively, for the low-concentration ${ \bf G } { \bf A } _ { 3 }$ treatments of plants in the ga1-3 background, the inflorescences were infiltrated once with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ or $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \ G A _ { 3 }$ solution containing $0 . 0 3 \%$ (v/v) of the surface-active agent Silwet-77. 

# Gene expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted from developing siliques (during the linearto bending-cotyledon stages) using the CTAB method51, or from seedlings or leaves using the TransZol reagent (TransGen). The TransScript One-Step gDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis SuperMix (TransGen) was employed to synthesize cDNA from $1 . 0 \mu \mathbf { g }$ of total RNA. Subsequently, qPCR reactions containing diluted cDNA samples, the PerfectStart Green qPCR SuperMix (TransGen), and specific primers (Supplementary Data 2) were conducted using the QuantStudio 1 Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems). ACTIN2 served as the normalization control, and the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C T } }$ method was utilized to calculate the relative expression levels. 

# Microscopy and image analysis

For the observation of seed mucilage phenotypes, mature dry seeds were suspended in 1 mL of $0 . 0 1 \%$ (w/v) Ruthenium red (Sigma-Aldrich) and shaken at 220 rpm for 1 h to remove the outer non-adherent layer of mucilage. After rinsing twice with water, the stained seeds were photographed using a BX51 light microscope (Olympus). Mucilage thickness was measured by using the Image J software (version 1.8.1). 

The sectioning and microscopy of developing seeds were performed as described previously28. More than 10 siliques at each development stage were selected for sectioning and examination. 

For the observation of cell adhesion, hypocotyls of 4-day-old darkgrown seedlings on the sugar-free medium were observed under an LSM 900 microscope (ZEISS) and photographed using an Axiocam 208 color camera (ZEISS). 

# Compositional analyses of seed mucilage and cell walls

The extraction of seed mucilage and quantification of monosaccharide compositions were performed as previously described52. The total mucilage amount was calculated by adding the amounts of nonadherent and adherent mucilage. The total content of specific monosaccharide was the sum of its contents in non-adherent and adherent mucilage layers. 

For the preparation of cell walls, 7-day-old seedlings were harvested, freeze-dried, and ground into a fine powder. The alcoholinsoluble residue (AIR) was extracted through sequential washes with 

$80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol, chloroform: methanol (2:1, v/v), increasing ethanol series $6 5 \%$ , $80 \%$ , and $100 \%$ [v/v]), and acetone, followed by drying in a $7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ oven. The volume of reagents used was four times that of the powder. To remove the starch, $5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of dried AIR was incubated with 5 mL of $80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol in a boiling water bath for 3 min, repeated twice, followed by enzymatic treatment with ${ \bf 6 0 U 0 }$ -amyloglucosidase and 10 U $\alpha$ -amylase in 0.1 M NaAc $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 5 . 0 \right)$ at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 h. The resulting precipitate was washed sequentially with $80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol, pure ethanol, and acetone. Finally, the starch-free AIR was dried in a $7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ oven. 

For the extraction of pectic polysaccharides, $2 \mathrm { m g }$ of starch-free AIR was suspended in 1 mL of $0 . 5 \%$ ammonium oxalate and heated in a boiling water bath at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. The suspension was centrifuged for 10 min at 10,000 rpm, and the supernatant was collected. The precipitate was suspended with another 1 mL of $0 . 5 \%$ ammonium oxalate and heated in a boiling water bath at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. After centrifugation, the supernatants were combined as pectin extract. The uronic acid content was determined using the meta-hydroxydiphenyl method53. Briefly, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 5-fold-diluted pectin extract was transferred into a new 2 mL microtube and hydrolyzed with $6 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of concentrated sulfuric acid containing $1 2 . 5 \mathsf { m M }$ sodium tetraborate (Sigma-Aldrich) for 5 min at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . After cooling on ice, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $0 . 1 5 \%$ (w/v) meta-hydroxydiphenyl (Sigma-Aldrich) in $0 . 5 \%$ (w/v) NaOH was added and mixed thoroughly, then incubated at room temperature for 5 min. Absorbance was measured at $5 2 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . The D-GalA (Sigma-Aldrich) solutions ranging from $0 . 0 1 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mu \mathrm { L }$ to $0 . 0 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mu \mathrm { L }$ were included in the measurement as the standard samples. 

# Immunolabeling assay and ELISA

The immunolabeling of RG-I in seed mucilage using the CCRC-M36 antibody was performed as previously described28. Briefly, mature dry seeds were blocked with $3 \%$ (w/v) nonfat milk, incubated with 10-fold diluted CCRC-M36 antibody and subsequently with 1,000-fold diluted Alexa Fluor488-tagged donkey anti-mouse immunoglobulin G (Thermo Fisher Scientific, A21201), and counterstained with the cellulose dye S4B (Sigma-Aldrich). Images were captured with a FluoView FV1000 confocal laser scanning microscope (Olympus). 

For ELISA, the total mucilage was extracted from 50 mg mature dry seeds suspended in 5 mL distilled water by ultrasonic treatment. An aliquot of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of mucilage extracts was coated onto 96-well microtiter plates at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. Following removal of the coating solutions, the plates were blocked with $3 \%$ (w/v) nonfat milk, incubated with the CCRC-M36 antibody, and subsequently with 1000- fold diluted horseradish peroxidase-conjugated anti-mouse secondary antibody (Abcam, ab205719). After washing, $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of substrate solution $( 1 0 \mathrm { m L } 0 . 1 \mathrm { M }$ sodium acetate [pH 5.5], $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L } 1 \%$ [w/v] tetramethylbenzidine, and 18 μL $6 \%$ [v/v] ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 } )$ was added and incubated for 10 min at room temperature. The reaction was terminated by adding $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 2 M ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S } { \sf O } _ { 4 } .$ , and the absorbance was measured at $4 5 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . 

# Protein-protein interaction assays

In the Y2H assays, the full-length or truncated coding sequences of genes were inserted into the pGBKT7-rec or pGADT7-rec vector (Clontech) and fused with the GAL4 DNA BD or GAL4 AD, respectively. The BD and AD recombinant plasmids were co-transformed into the yeast Y2HGold (Clontech) cells by the LiCl-PEG method. The transformants were selected on the SD/-Leu/-Trp medium, then pointed on SD/-Trp/- Leu/-His/-Ade medium supplemented with $1 2 5 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ Aureobasidin A (AbA, Solarbio) to assess interactions. 

For Y3H assays, the full-length coding sequence of TTG1 was cloned into the pBridge (Clontech) vector and fused with GAL4BD, generating the BD-TTG1 construct. To generate the BD-TTG1 pMET:GAI construct, the full-length coding sequence of GAI was inserted downstream of the MET promoter in the BD-TTG1 construct, fused with the 

HA tag. The recombinant pBridge plasmids were co-transformed with the AD-EGL3 or AD-TTG2 plasmid into the yeast strain Y2HGold. The transformants were selected on the SD/-Leu/-Trp medium, then pointed on SD/-Trp/-Leu/-His/-Ade/-Met medium supplemented with $1 2 5 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ AbA. GAI under the control of the pMET promoter was expressed in the absence of Met. 

For BiFC assays, the full-length coding sequences of TTG1, MYB5, EGL3, and TTG2 were cloned into the pVYNE(R) or pVYCE(R) vectors, with their N-terminus fused to the nYFP or cYFP. The empty pVYNE(R) and pVYCE(R) vectors were used as negative control plasmids. The different combinations of plasmids as indicated were co-transformed with the nucleus marker 35S:NLS-mCherry into Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts as previously described54. After incubation overnight, fluorescence was observed with a FluoView FV1000 confocal laser scanning microscope (Olympus). For the split-LUC assays, the full-length coding sequences of genes were inserted into the pCAMBIA1300-nLUC or pCAMBIA1300- cLUC vector55 and introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 via electroporation. Over-night cultured GV3101 strains harboring recombinant plasmids were re-suspended in a working solution $\mathbf { ( 1 0 \ m M \ M g C l _ { 2 } }$ , 10 mM 2-[N-morpholino] ethanesulfonic acid sodium, and $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Acetosyringone, pH5.7-5.9) at a concentration of $\mathrm { O D } 6 0 0 { = } 1 . 0$ . Subsequently, tobacco leaves were soaked with 1 mM D-Luciferin potassium salt (Abcam, ab143655), and the resulting luminescence was captured using the NIGHTSGADE LB985 in vivo plant imaging system (BERTHOLD) after a 5-min dark adaptation period. For analyzing the impact of GAI on the interactions, equal amounts of GV3101 strain harboring either 35S:GAI-3HA or 35S:GFP-3HA were co-cultured and co-infiltrated with the strains carrying nLUC-tagged and cLUC-tagged plasmids. Protein expression levels were detected by Western blot assays with the anti-LUC (Sigma-Aldrich, L0159) and anti-HA (TransGen, HT801) antibodies. Antibodies were used in 1:1000 dilution. 

For co-IP assays in tobacco leaves, the full-length coding sequences of genes were cloned into the pCAMBIA1300-GFP vector (fused with GFP at the C-terminus), the pBINGFP2 vector (fused with GFP at the N-terminus), the pBI121-6MYC vector (fused with the 6MYC tag), or the pCAMBIA1301-3HA vector (fused with the HA tag). The empty vectors expressing the GFP, HA, or MYC tags alone were used as negative controls. The different combinations of plasmids as indicated were transiently co-expressed in N. benthamiana leaves. After $4 0 \mathrm { h }$ of infiltration, total proteins were extracted using the Plant Cell lysis buffer for Western and IP (Beyotime, P0043) supplemented with phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (Solabio) and a proteinase inhibitor cocktail (Calbiochem). After centrifugation at $1 6 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ for 20 min, the supernatant was incubated with $3 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of GFP-Trap Agarose beads (ChromTek) for 1 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with gentle rotation. After 5 washes of the beads with phosphate-buffered saline, proteins on the beads were eluted through boiling for 10 min and then separated with $10 \%$ SDS-PAGE. The Western blot detection was performed using the BeyoECL Moon detection reagent (Beyotime) and imaged using a Fusion SOLO S system (VILBER), with the EvolutionCapt Edge software. Proteins were detected by the anti-GFP, anti-MYC, or anti-HA antibodies (TransGen, HT801, HT101, and HT801, respectively). Antibodies were used at a dilution of 1:1000.

the same as described above. Endogenous GAI was detected by an anti-GAI rabbit polyclonal antiserum (PhytoAB, PHY3624S) with 1:1000 dilution. 

For the IP-MS assays, ${ \boldsymbol { 2 } } \mathbf { g }$ of developing siliques were harvested from pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2 complementary plants treated with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathbf { G } \mathbf { A } _ { 3 }$ or $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ PAC. The 35S:GFP transgenic plants served as a negative control. Siliques were crosslinked immediately with $1 \%$ paraformaldehyde for 30 min on ice in a vacuum chamber. Total proteins were extracted using the Plant Cell lysis buffer for Western and IP (Beyotime, P0043) supplemented with phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (Solabio) and a proteinase inhibitor cocktail (Calbiochem). To reduce non-specific binding, protein extracts were pre-cleared with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of Binding control Agarose beads (ChromTek) for 1 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with gentle rotation. After brief centrifugation, the supernatant was incubated with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of GFP-Trap Agarose beads (ChromTek) for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ with gentle rotation. After 10 washes of the beads with phosphate-buffered saline, proteins bound on the beads were eluted by boiling in SDS-PAGE sample buffer for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and then briefly separated in a $10 \%$ SDS-PAGE gel. The gel regions containing proteins were cut and subjected to LC-MS/MS analysis with the TimsTOF MALDI PharmaPulse system (Bruker) at Applied Protein Technology (Zhejiang). Three biological replicates were performed. The MS/MS database search was performed using the Maxquant software (version 1.6.17.0) against the NCBI Arabidopsis thaliana protein database with the following parameters: Protease cleavage site, Trypsin; Fixed modifications, Oxidation (M) and Acetyl (Protein N-term); MS/MS deisotoping tolerance, 7 (FTMS), 0.15 (ITMS), and 0.01 (TOF); Minimum peptide length $= 7$ ; $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 1$ . Proteins with more than 2-fold intensity in pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2 compared to 35S:GFP with a significance threshold of $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ were considered as candidates for MYB5-interacting proteins. 

# Dual-LUC assays

To generate the effector constructs, the full-length coding sequences of TTG1, MYB5, EGL3, TTG2, and GAI were inserted into the pBI221 vector. The empty vector was used as a negative control. The pGreenII 0800-LUC-pGL2 reporter construct was generated in our previous work28, in which the GL2 promoter (2066 bp upstream of ATG) was inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector upstream of the firefly LUC $( f { - } L U C )$ reporter gene. The Renilla LUC (r-LUC) gene under the control of the CaMV35S promoter in the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector was used as an internal control for normalization. The isolation and transformation of Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts were performed following the established protocol54. The firefly luciferase (fLUC) and renilla luciferase (rLUC) activities were measured using the Dual Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (TransGen), and the f-LUC/r-LUC ratio was calculated. 

# ChIP assays

Developing siliques (1–2 g) at 3–9 DPA were harvested from the 35S:GFP, pMYB5:MYB5-GFP/myb5-2, and $p T T G 2 { : } T T G 2 { \cdot } G F P / t t g 2$ transgenic plants, which were daily treated with or without $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ GA3 or $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ PAC. The samples were fixed with $1 \%$ paraformaldehyde and subjected to vacuum crosslinking twice for 15 min each. Crosslinking was terminated by adding 0.125 M glycine and vacuumizing for 5 min. Chromatin was isolated following a previously described method56, and sonicated to generate DNA fragments averaging ~500 bp in size. The chromatin was then incubated with anti-GFP (TransGen, HT801) antibody (1:200 dilution) overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and subsequently precipitated with the Protein G Agarose (Millipore). The precipitated DNAprotein complexes were eluted, de-crosslinked, and treated with proteinase to remove proteins as described in ref. 56. The DNA was extracted with the phenol-chloroform method. The enrichment was represented by Percent Input $( \%$ Input) calculated by 2-(Ct [IP]-Ct [Input])/ $\mathrm { I D F ^ { * } 1 0 0 \% }$ , where IDF (Input dilution factor) $=$ (fraction of the Input chromatin saved)−1 .
