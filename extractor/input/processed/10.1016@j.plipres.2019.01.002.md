# A B S T R A C T

Phytol, the prenyl side chain of chlorophyll, is derived from geranylgeraniol by reduction of three double bonds. Recent results demonstrated that the conversion of geranylgeraniol to phytol is linked to chlorophyll synthesis, which is catalyzed by protein complexes associated with the thylakoid membranes. One of these complexes contains light harvesting chlorophyll binding like proteins (LIL3), enzymes of chlorophyll synthesis (protoporphyrinogen oxidoreductase, POR; chlorophyll synthase, CHLG) and geranylgeranyl reductase (GGR). Phytol is not only employed for the synthesis of chlorophyll, but also for tocopherol (vitamin E), phylloquinol (vitamin K) and fatty acid phytyl ester production. Previously, it was believed that phytol is derived from reduction of geranylgeranyl-diphosphate originating from the 4-methylerythritol-5-phosphate (MEP) pathway. The identification and characterization of two kinases, VTE5 and VTE6, involved in phytol and phytyl-phosphate phosphorylation, respectively, indicated that most phytol employed for tocopherol synthesis is derived from reduction of geranylgeranylated chlorophyll to (phytol-) chlorophyll. After hydrolysis from chlorophyll, free phytol is phosphorylated by the two kinases, and phytyl-diphosphate employed for the synthesis of tocopherol and phylloquinol. The reason why some chloroplast lipids, i.e. chlorophyll, tocopherol and phylloquinol, are derived from phytol, while others, i.e. carotenoids and tocotrienols (in some plant species) are synthesized from geranylgeraniol, remains unclear.

# 1. Introduction

Chlorophyll is the most important photosynthetic pigment in plants and algae. Large amounts of chlorophyll are synthesized during plant growth and broken down during senescence, and in green leaves, chlorophyll is subject to constant turnover [1]. Chlorophyll is composed of a porphyrin head group which contains magnesium, and of phytol, a prenyl side chain bound in ester linkage [2]. Phytol is hydrolyzed from chlorophyll in a 1:1 molar ratio, rendering phytol one of the most abundant isoprenoids in the biosphere.

Plants contain two pathways for de novo isoprenoid synthesis, the mevalonate pathway, which is localized to the cytosol and peroxisome, and the plastidic 2-methylerythritol-phosphate (MEP, or non-mevalonate) pathway [3,4]. In proteomic studies, many of the isoprenoid biosynthesis enzymes were localized to specific organelles, some even to different suborganellar structures like the chloroplast envelopes, thylakoids, stroma or plastoglobules, the lipid droplets in the stroma [5]. Thus, a detailed scheme of the interconnection and subcellular organization of isoprenoid metabolism has emerged. Information on pathways, genes and mutants has been compiled in the excellent Arabidopsis thaliana Isoprenoid Pathway Database (AtIPD; www.atipd.ethz.ch) [3,6].

Phytol is an isoprenoid alcohol ("prenol") with 20 carbon atoms and one double bond [2]. It is structurally related to geranylgeraniol, which is based on the same prenol backbone but carries 3 additional double bonds. Free phytol is usually a minor component of chloroplasts. In its activated form, phytol carries two phosphate groups bound to the hydroxy group. Phytyl-diphosphate (phytyl-PP) was for a long time believed to be synthesized from geranylgeranyl-diphosphate (GG-PP) via reduction of 3 double bonds by geranylgeranyl reductase (GGR). GGR can reduce geranylgeranyl moieties bound to chlorophyll or to diphosphate, and there has been a long debate which of the two possible pathways represents the most relevant one in vivo. The recent characterization of a thylakoid-bound chlorophyll synthesis complex harboring the light harvesting chlorophyll binding like proteins LIL3:1 and LIL3:2, enzymes of chlorophyll de novo synthesis and GGR, as well as the identification of the phytol phosphorylation pathway for conversion of free phytol into phytyl-PP, led to the conclusion that in vivo most geranylgeraniol is reduced to phytol in the form of geranylgeranylated chlorophyll (GG-chlorophyll).

Chlorophyll is not the only lipid containing a C20 prenyl group, because additional lipids in chloroplasts are synthesized from phytol or geranylgeraniol. At present, it remains unclear why some of these

chloroplast lipids are derived from phytol, while others are synthesized from geranylgeraniol, i.e. which functional relevance the degree of desaturation of the prenyl side chain plays in vivo. For some chloroplast lipids (i.e. tocochromanols), several forms with different degrees of unsaturation derived from phytol, geranylgeraniol or tetrahydrogeranylgeraniol (i.e. tocopherols, tocotrienols, tocomonoenols), were identified, depending on the plant species and organ. Plants and cyanobacteria contain only phylloquinol (synthesized from phytol), while menaquinones derived from unsaturated polyprenyls are found in bacteria. Fatty acid phytyl esters accumulate during stress in all plants analyzed, but in addition, fatty acid geranylgeranyl esters were also detected in some plant species. Carotenoids in plants and cyanobacteria are synthesized by condensation of two molecules of GG-PP followed by the introduction of additional double bonds, resulting in the production of highly unsaturated pigments [7]. The conversion of the geranylgeranyl moiety into the phytyl group, the "recycling" of free phytol via phosphorylation, as well as the regulation of these processes are fundamental for chloroplast isoprenoid synthesis and are the focus of this review article.

# 2. Isoprenoid synthesis

# 2.1. Mevalonate and methylerythritol phosphate pathways

Plants have acquired two pathways, the mevalonate and the 2-methylerythritol-4-P (MEP) pathways, which provide the building blocks for isoprenoid synthesis, i.e. the C5 isoprenoid precursors isopentenyl-PP and dimethylallyl-PP (Fig. 1) [4]. The mevalonate pathway is localized to the cytosol and peroxisome and is present in all eukaryotes [3,4]. The first step of the mevalonate pathway includes the synthesis of acetoacetyl-CoA by acetoacetyl-CoA synthase (AACT1, AACT2). The AACT proteins localize to the cytosol, while AACT2 was also found in a proteomics study of peroxisomes [8,9]. 3-Hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA (HMG-CoA) is synthesized by condensation of acetoacetyl-CoA with another molecule of acetyl-CoA catalyzed by 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA synthase in the cytosol (HMGS). The following reduction to mevalonate by HMG-CoA reductase is the committed and rate limiting step. Arabidopsis harbors two genes encoding HMG-CoA reductase (HMGR1, HMGR2) [10]. The two HMGR proteins localize to the ER, with the catalytic domain facing the cytosol [11,12]. HMGR activity is inhibited by lovastatin (also called: mevilonin). Mevalonate is phosphorylated by mevalonate kinase (MK) which localizes to the cytosol [13]. A second phosphorylation catalyzed by 5-phosphomevalonate kinase (PMK) yields 5-diphospho-mevalonate. Next, mevalonate-diphosphate decarboxylase (MPDC1, MPDC2) produces isopentenyl-PP from 5-diphospho-mevalonate. Interestingly, PMK and MPDC1 were localized to the peroxisomes, while MPDC2 contains a putative peroxisomal targeting sequence [13]. Finally, isopentenyl-PP is converted into dimethylallyl-PP by isopentenyl-PP Δ-isomerase (IDI, IPI). Two genes (IDI1/IPI1, IDI2/IPI2) encode proteins whose mRNAs are transcribed from different transcription start sites. The long polypeptides IDI1L and IDI2L are targeted to the chloroplasts or mitochondria, respectively, but the proteins also show dual targeting with minor transfer of IDI1L to the mitochondria and IDI2L to the chloroplasts. The short polypeptides IDI1S and IDI2S are transferred to the peroxisomes [14–17]. Therefore, IDI1L is expected to participate in the plastidial MEP pathway (see below), while IDI1S and IDI2S presumably are involved in the last steps of the mevalonate pathway in the peroxisomes. Single mutants idi1 (SALK_006330) or idi2 (SAIL_604 or SALK_014625) grow similarly as wild type, but homozygous idi2 plants of line SALK_014625 show altered sepal and petal morphologies. The idi1 idi2 (SALK_006330, SAIL_604) double mutant shows dwarfism and male sterility, but the combination of idi1 (SALK_006330) with idi2 (SALK_014625) is lethal, suggesting that the idi2 line SAIL_604 harbors residual IDI2 activity, while idi2 (SALK_014625) represents a null allele [15,16]. Condensation of dimethylallyl-PP with two isopentenyl-PP, catalyzed by farnesyl-PP synthase (FPS1, FPS2) results in farnesyl-PP synthesis [18,19]. FPS1 is translated from two alternative start codons giving rise to long (FPS1L) and short (FPS1S) polypeptides.

FPS2 codes for a shorter polypeptide similar to FPS1S. FPS1L localizes to the mitochondria, and FPS1S and FPS2 were predicted to localize to the cytosol. However, the FPS2 ortholog from periwinkle was shown to be targeted to the peroxisomes, indicating that the late steps of mevalonate synthesis including farnesyl-PP synthesis might be peroxisomal localized [20]. The single mutants fps1 and fps2 are viable, but the fps1 fps2 double mutant is embryolethal [19]. Farnesyl-PP can be elongated to geranylgeranyl-PP or polyprenyl-PP (Fig. 1). In conclusion, the mevalonate pathway enzymes mostly localize to the cytosol, but the late steps including PMK, MPDC, IDI1S, IDI2S and FPS2 are presumably found in the peroxisomes [13,21]. The reason for the compartmentation of the mevalonate pathway remains unclear.

In plastids of plants and in bacteria, isopentenyl-PP and dimethylallyl-PP are produced via the non-mevalonate or MEP pathway [4]. First, 1- deoxyxylulose-5-P (DOXP) is synthesized from glyceraldehyde-3-P and pyruvate catalyzed by 1-deoxyxylulose-5-P synthase (DXS) [22]. The cla1 mutation affects DXS activity and causes an albino phenotype [23]. Next, 1-deoxy-xylulose-5-P reductoisomerase (DXR) converts 1-deoxyxylulose-5- P into MEP. Inhibition of DXR by fosmidomycin blocks the synthesis of isoprenoids through the MEP pathway and is lethal for plants. MEP is the substrate for 2-methyl-4-diphosphocytidyl-erythritol (CDP-ME) synthesis by 2-methylerythritol-4-P cytidylyltransferase (CMS) [24–26]. 2-Methyl-4- cytidinediphospho-erythritol is phosphorylated at the C2 position by 2- methyl-4-cytidinediphospho-erythritol kinase (CMK) [26]. 2-Methyl-erythritol-2,4-cyclodiphosphate synthase (MEcPP synthase, MCS) produces the cyclic compound MEcPP [27]. 4-Hydroxy-3-methylbut-2-enyl-PP is produced from MEcPP by 4-hydroxy-3-methylbut-2-enyl-diphosphate synthase (HDS) [28]. The ceh1 mutant affected in HDS expression accumulates the substrate MEcPP, which at the same time is a chloroplastderived retrograde signal regulating the transcription of stress-related genes in the nucleus [29]. Finally, isopentenyl-PP and dimethylallyl-PP are synthesized by 4-hydroxy-3-methylbut-2-enyl-PP reductase (HDR) [30]. The two hemiprenyl-PPs are interconverted by isopentenyl-PP $\Delta$ -isomerase (IDI1L, IDI2L, see above) (Fig. 1).

The finding that mutations in MEP pathway genes result in severe growth retardation (e.g. cla1 albino mutant) suggested that isoprenoids derived from the mevalonate pathway cannot substitute for the deficiency in flux through the MEP pathway. However, several studies indicated that there is some exchange of intermediates between the mevalonate and MEP pathway. For example, feeding of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ or $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ labeled mevalonate to Arabidopsis plants resulted in the accumulation of labeled C atoms in carotenoids and in the phytyl side chains of chlorophyll and tocopherol [31]. Feeding of mevalonate partially alleviated the severe deficiency in thylakoids of the cla1 mutant, while treatment with lovastatin (inhibitor of the mevalonate pathway) led to an aggravation [32]. Farnesyl-PP was the preferred prenyl-PP taken up by liverwort chloroplasts [33]. Furthermore, partial inhibition of the two cytosolic farnesyl-PP synthases (FPS1, FPS2, see above) in the fps1 fps2 double mutant affected sterol and ubiquinone synthesis, but also led to a decrease in chlorophyll and carotenoid contents [18]. Thus, mevalonate-derived intermediates, e.g. isopentenyl-PP or farnesyl-PP, might contribute to the synthesis of chloroplast isoprenoids [31].

Deficiency in GG-PP production through the MEP pathway causes a stoichiometric over-accumulation of porphyrin intermediates of chlorophyll synthesis including chlorophyllide which causes oxidative stress [34]. In agreement with this scenario, fosmidomycin toxicity is alleviated in Arabidopsis plants by porphyrin synthesis inhibitors, or in mutants affected in porphyrin synthesis. Furthermore, phytol feeding suppressed the fosmidomycin toxicity in wild type, but not in the vte5 mutant deficient in phytol kinase (chapter 4.1), indicating that phosphorylation of exogenously supplied phytol is required to compensate for the loss of the MEP pathway [34].

The gene At2g34630 was proposed to encode geranyl-PP synthase (GPPS) which condenses two C5-PP units [35,36], but this protein was later shown to harbor polyprenyl-PP synthesis activity (PPPS) [37] (Chapter 5.2.). Enzymes of the geranyl-PP synthase/geranylgeranyl-PP synthase (GPPS, GGPPS) subfamily of IDS/PTS enzymes catalyze the successive condensation of dimethylallyl-PP with up to 3 molecules of isopentenyl-PP. The IDS/PTS family contains additional subfamilies, i.e. polyprenyl-PP synthases/solanesyl-PP synthases (PPPS/SPS, chapter 5.2) and farnesyl-PP synthases (FPS1, FPS2, chapter 2.1). Furthermore, GGPPS proteins of two so-called 'small subunit' subfamilies (SSU, type I and type II) show sequence similarity to IDS/PTS enzymes. However, SSU type I and SSU type II GGPPS proteins lack one or both of the aspartaterich $\mathrm { D D } ( \mathrm { X } ) _ { 2 - 4 } \mathrm { D }$ (X, any amino acid) motifs required for prenyl binding in active GGPPS enzymes. Therefore, SSU type I and SSU type II polypeptides are catalytically inactive, but they bind to full length GGPPS enzymes ('large subunit', LSU) in a heterodimeric complex, thereby modifying the specificity of the GGPPS LSU. SSU type I and type II sequences are related, but fall into two phylogenetically distinct clades [38,39]. The synthesis of geranyl-PP was elucidated in hop, where a GGPPS LSU forms a heterodimer with a GGPPS SSU type I polypeptide which per se is inactive [38]. While the GGPPS LSU produces mostly GG-PP, its specificity is shifted to the production of geranyl-PP after binding to SSU type I. Arabidopsis contains a family of 12 GGPPS proteins, seven of which are localized to the chloroplast [16,40]. GGPPS12 (At4g38460) functionally resembles GGPPS SSU type I from hop, because it is inactive on its own, but in combination with the Arabidopsis GGPPS11 LSU (At4g36810), it shifts the GGPPS11 activity from the production of GG-PP towards geranyl-PP [38]. GGPPS12 belongs to the GGPPS SSU type II subfamily. GGPPS11 shows high

ubiquitous expression and therefore provides the housekeeping function for geranyl-PP and GG-PP production in chloroplasts [40,41]. The ggpps11-3 and ggpps11-4 null mutants are embryolethal while knockdown alleles (ggpps11-1, ggpps11-5) are variegated or pale green, dwarfed and contain reduced amounts of carotenoids, chlorophyll, plastoquinol-9, tocopherol and phylloquinol [41,42].

# 3. Chlorophyll metabolism

# 3.1. Chlorophyll synthesis

The synthesis of chlorophyll has been reviewed in numerous articles, e.g. [43–45]. Therefore, only the final steps are discussed here (Fig. 2). The tetrapyrrole head group of chlorophyll harbors four pyrrole rings, and the fifth ring (E ring) is formed in a reaction catalyzed by Mg-protoporphyrin IX monomethylester cyclase (CHL27, CRD1) [46]. A second subunit (YCF54 or LCAA) binds to CHL27 and is required for CHL27 activity [47]. CHL27, YCF54 and ferredoxin NADPH reductase (FNR1) which delivers electrons to the cyclase, physically interact and presumably form a protein complex [48]. In cyanobacteria, CycI (the CHL27 ortholog) interacts with further downstream enzymes of chlorophyll synthesis, i.e. protochlorophyllide oxidoreductase (POR), divinyl reductase (DVR) and geranylgeranyl reductase (ChlP, GGR) [49]. CHL27 in Arabidopsis also interacts with PORB, PORC, GGR and the negative regulator of chlorophyll synthesis FLU [50,51]. Therefore, the late steps of chlorophyll synthesis are localized in a protein complex including CHL27 and YCF54 at the thylakoid membranes (Fig. 2, highlighted in blue).

By screening a maize population of $\sim 5 0 0 0$ lines for alterations in tocochromanol contents and composition, two major quantitative trait loci (QTL) were identified (QTL5, GRMZM2G036455; QTL24, GRMZM073351) that are highly similar to Arabidopsis PORB sequences. These results indicated that the activity of PORB has a strong impact on tocopherol levels not only in green tissues, but also in non-green seeds [69]. Indeed, low amounts of chlorophyll a and b, chlorophyllide a and b and pheophytin a were found in the maize embryos, although at levels ${ \sim } 5 0 0$ times lower than in leaves, and at least 100 times lower than tocopherol in embryos. While it is intriguing that PORB, which is also present in the LIL3 protein complex of chlorophyll synthesis, is a major factor regulating tocopherol synthesis in maize kernels, the question of how the rather large amount of prenyl groups required for tocopherol synthesis can be generated through the low capacity of chlorophyll synthesis in maize embryos, remains unclear.

# 3.2. Chlorophyll degradation

Chlorophyll is continuously synthesized and degraded during turnover (Fig. 3). During biotic or abiotic stress or during leaf senescence, metabolism shifts from anabolism to catabolism, and chlorophyll degradation is strongly increased [1]. Degradation of photosynthesis-associated proteins is an important aspect of nutrient mobilization during stress, as protein degradation represents an abundant source for nitrogen. In addition, the degradation of chlorophyll results in a further release of nitrogen [70]. Moreover, chlorophyll is degraded during seed maturation in Arabidopsis and fruit ripening, e.g. in tomato [71–73].

Chlorophyll b is converted into chlorophyll a prior to degradation. To this end, chlorophyll b is reduced to 7-hydroxymethyl chlorophyll a by one of the chlorophyll b reductases non-yellow coloring1 (NYC1) or NYC1-like (NOL) [74,75]. 7-Hydroxymethyl chlorophyll b is further reduced to chlorophyll a by 7-hydroxymethyl-chlorophyll a reductase (HCAR) [76].

The fact that plants contain very high chlorophyllase activity was already described by Willstätter and Stoll in 1911 [77]. Indeed, highly active chlorophyllases isolated from Arabidopsis and other plants were presumed to be involved in chlorophyll degradation [78]. However, the Arabidopsis chlorophyllases CLH1 and CLH2 do not localize to the plastid but rather to the ER and tonoplast, and the clh1 clh2 double mutant is still capable of chlorophyll breakdown [79]. Therefore, CLH proteins are not involved in chlorophyll degradation during senescence or turnover in vivo. Interestingly, chlorophyllide a, the product of chlorophyll degradation by CLH, can act as anti-microbial and antiherbivory compound. Therefore, CLH1 and CLH2 might play a role in plant protection after cell disruption during plant pathogen interactions, because upon cell rupture, chlorophyll from the chloroplast gets in contact with the extraplastidic CLH proteins [80,81].

The initial step of chlorophyll a degradation is the removal of the central $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ cation, resulting in pheophytin a production. The removal

of $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g } ^ { 2 + }$ was for a long time assigned to a non-defined Mg-chelating substance, and it was not clear whether the reaction might be enzymecatalyzed or not [71]. Recently, the stay green (SGR) protein, which was known to stimulate chlorophyll degradation, was identified as a functional Mg dechelatase [82]. The stay green mutation was already identified by Gregor Mendel, because this locus determines the yellow or green color of cotyledons in the pea i mutant [83]. Three genes encoding Mg dechelatase are found in the Arabidopsis genome, SGR1, SGR2 and SGR-LIKE (SGRL). The corresponding enzymes SGR1 and SGR2 mainly use chlorophyll a as substrate, while SGRL dechelates the $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ cation of chlorophyllide a [82]. The enzyme responsible for dephytylation during chlorophyll degradation is pheophytin pheophorbide hydrolase or pheophytinase (PPH) [84]. Arabidopsis pph mutant plants reveal a staygreen phenotype. PPH does not hydrolyze chlorophyll a, but rather pheophytin a [85]. Therefore, the breakdown product, pheophorbide a, can only enter catabolism during senescence and cannot be utilized for chlorophyll re-synthesis during chlorophyll turnover. Pheophorbide a is converted into red chlorophyll catabolite by pheophorbide a oxigenase (PAO) [86,87]. Red chlorophyll catabolite is the first non-green breakdown product of chlorophyll, due to the opening of the tetrapyrrole ring. Non-fluorescent chlorophyll catabolites are produced in subsequent degradation steps [71]. The breakdown of chlorophyll is regulated by different transcription factors. Recently, the NAC transcription factor ANAC046, was identified which binds promoter regions of NYC1, SGR1, SGR2 and PAO and thereby positively regulates chlorophyll breakdown during senescence [88]. The ABA-responsive element (ABRE)-binding transcription factors ABF2, ABF3 and ABF4 bind to the promotors and induce expression of SGR1, SGR2, PAO and NYC1. ABA-induced chlorophyll degradation is partially abolished in the abf2 abf3 abf4 triple mutant as well as in the ABA-insensitive triple mutant snrk2.2 snr2.3 snrk2.6 [89]. Furthermore, the NAC domain containing transcription factor ATAF1 was shown to be coregulated and presumably involved in regulation of PAO expression and chlorophyll degradation [90].

In Arabidopsis seeds, chlorophyll is broken down during maturation requiring the activity of chlorophyll hydrolyzing enzymes [73]. As shown in chapter 5.1., phytyl-PP required for tocopherol synthesis is provided by phosphorylation of chlorophyll-derived phytol. Therefore, the seeds of the pph mutants were expected to be deficient in phytol and tocopherol synthesis. However, pph mutant seeds are not tocopheroldeficient, indicating that seeds harbor an alternative enzyme for chlorophyll hydrolysis. Recently, chlorophyll dephytylase (CLD1) was identified to be responsible for chlorophyll hydrolysis and turnover in green leaves [91]. CLD1 shows sequence similarity with PPH, it localizes to plastids and can dephytylate chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b and pheophytin a in vitro. The cld1-1 mutant harbors a point mutation leading to increased CLD1 activity and accumulates chlorophyllide a, suggesting that chlorophyll a is the in vivo substrate for CLD1. Chlorophyllide a can be used as substrate for chlorophyll synthase CHLG during the chlorophyll turnover cycle (Fig. 3). In line with this scenario, chlorophyllide a accumulated in the heat-sensitive Arabidopsis chlg mutant at high temperature, because chlorophyllide a was not efficiently re-esterified during chlorophyll turnover [92]. It is not clear whether CLD1 also contributes to chlorophyll degradation in seeds.

# 4. Phytol metabolism

Senescence and abiotic stress result in ultrastructural changes of chloroplasts. The amount of thylakoid membranes decreases, while starch granules and plastoglobules proliferate [93]. These ultrastructural changes are correlated with alterations in the lipid composition, because chlorophyll and galactolipids are degraded while triacylglycerols, tocopherols and fatty acid phytyl esters accumulate [94,95]. Triacylglycerols, tocopherols and fatty acid phytyl esters are deposited in the plastoglobules to avoid the accumulation of the nonesterified breakdown products of galactolipids and chlorophyll, i.e. fatty acids and phytol [96]. Chlorophyll degradation results in the

release of large amounts of phytol [96–98]. Using radioactive labeling studies, Ischebeck et al. showed that free phytol produced by chlorophyll hydrolysis can be re-incorporated into chlorophyll, or employed for the synthesis of tocopherol and fatty acid phytyl esters [99].

# 4.1. Phytol phosphorylation

In 1980, Soll et al. already reported that chloroplasts harbor a kinase activity capable of phosphorylating phytol for tocopherol synthesis, and that this activity was also able to phosphorylate geranylgeraniol [56,100]. Two different kinases in Arabidopsis chloroplasts, phytol kinase and phytyl-P kinase act consecutively to convert free phytol into phytyl-PP which is required for tocochromanol and phylloquinol synthesis [99].

The gene encoding the first kinase (phytol kinase, VTE5, At5g04490) was isolated based on the tocopherol deficient vte5 mutant of Arabidopsis [101] (Fig. 3). After heterologous expression in E. coli, VTE5 was able to convert phytol into phytyl-P. The corresponding vte5 mutant of Arabidopsis contains only ${ \sim } 2 0 ~ \%$ and ${ \sim } 3 5 ~ \%$ of wild type amounts of tocopherol in seeds and leaves, respectively, indicating that VTE5 is important for tocopherol synthesis in these two organs. Similarly, downregulation of VTE5 activity in tomato using an RNAi approach resulted in a ${ \sim } 8 0 ~ \%$ decrease in tocopherol in leaves and fruits [72].

The gene catalyzing the second phosphorylation step (phytyl-P kinase, VTE6, At1g78620) was isolated based on a phylogenetic approach [68]. Heterologous expression in E. coli showed that VTE6 harbors phytyl-P kinase activity (Fig. 3). Arabidopsis vte6 mutant plants show strong growth retardation and almost no tocopherol in the leaves. This indicates that phytyl-PP required for tocopherol synthesis in leaves is entirely derived from the phytol phosphorylation pathway. Interestingly, the double mutant vte5 vte6 grows much better and contains more chlorophyll in the leaves than the vte6 single mutant, while it is still completely tocopherol-deficient. Phytol and phytyl-P accumulate in vte6, but not in vte5 vte6 plants, suggesting that one or both of these metabolites might be detrimental for growth [68]. Recently, Wang et al. showed that the vte6 mutant does not only lack tocopherol, but is also deficient in phylloquinol, indicating that the phytol phosphorylation pathway provides the substrates for tocopherol and phylloquinol synthesis [102].

# 4.2. Fatty acid phytyl ester synthesis

More than 50 years ago, fatty acid phytyl esters were detected in chlorotic leaves of Norway maple (Acer platanoides) [97,98]. The bulk of phytol is bound to chlorophyll in green leaves, while it is esterified to fatty acids in yellow leaves. The authors assumed two independent reactions for the release of phytol from chlorophyll and for the subsequent acylation to fatty acids. Furthermore, marine bacteria (e.g. Marinobacter hydrocarbonoclasticus) synthesize fatty acid phytyl esters as alternative carbon and energy reservoir [103,104]. Green leaves of Arabidopsis contain almost no fatty acid phytyl esters. During chlorotic stress including senescence, cold stress or nitrogen deprivation, fatty acid phytyl esters are produced in high amounts [93,99,105]. In contrast to the fatty acid composition of whole Arabidopsis leaves, fatty acid phytyl esters contain large amounts of medium-chain fatty acids (10:0, 12:0, 14:0) and 16:3 [93,96,105]. While the phytyl moiety of fatty acid phytyl esters is derived from chlorophyll, the origin of the fatty acids is not clear. Medium-chain fatty acids (10:0, 12:0, 14:0) are intermediates of fatty acid de novo synthesis in the stroma [106], and 16:3 occurs almost exclusively at the sn-2 position of monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) in Arabidopsis [107].

Increased levels of fatty acid phytyl esters can also be observed as a result of a block in the phytol phosphorylation pathway. Arabidopsis mutants with a defect in phytol kinase (VTE5) or phytyl-P kinase (VTE6) activity accumulate fatty acid phytyl esters in the leaves, most likely as a result of an increase in free phytol [68]. Similarly, leaves of tomato VTE5

knockdown lines exhibit increased levels of fatty acid phytyl esters compared to wild type, especially when exposed to high light or high temperature [72,108].

The enzymes catalyzing the esterification of free phytol, phytyl ester synthases 1 and 2 (PES1, PES2) belong to the family of esterase/lipase/ thioesterase (ELT) proteins. Expression of PES1 and PES2 is upregulated during senescence [96]. PES1 and PES2 localize to the plastoglobules, the site of fatty acid phytyl ester accumulation [96,109,110]. The Arabidopsis genome harbors six members of the ELT family, ELT1 (PES1), ELT2 (PES2), ELT3, ELT4, ELT5 and ELT6. The contents of fatty acid phytyl esters are strongly decreased in the pes1 pes2 double mutant. However, the amounts of 16:0-phytol and 18:3-phytol are unchanged. Therefore, these fatty acid phytyl esters might be produced by the remaining ELT enzymes. Transient expression of Arabidopsis PES2 together with a fatty acid reductase (FAR) in Nicotiana benthamiana resulted in the synthesis of phytyl esters enriched with medium-chain fatty acids [111].

Some plants accumulate fatty acid geranylgeranyl (GG) esters in addition to fatty acid phytyl esters. GG esters were found in mosses, in Norway spruce wood (Picea abies), spinach chloroplasts, cotton buds and olive oil [112–116]. Overexpression of a plastidic isopentenyl-diphosphate synthase (IDS1) involved in GG-PP synthesis resulted in the accumulation of GG esters in the needles of white spruce (Picea glauca) [117]. The fatty acid GG esters are presumably synthesized by spruce orthologs of PES/ELT. It remains unknown why some plants accumulate GG esters in addition to phytyl esters.

Furthermore, tomato petals express a gene orthologous to Arabidopsis PES/ELT, designated PYP1 (pale yellow petal 1). PYP1 encodes an enzyme involved in the esterification of xanthophylls in the petals, thus contributing to their yellow color [118]. PES/ELT enzymes might contribute to esterification xanthophylls in other species where xanthophyll/carotenoid esters were found, e.g. spinach (Spinacia oleracea), beech tree (Fagus sylvatica) and common broom (Sarothamnus scoparius) [94,95].

The upregulation of fatty acid phytyl ester synthesis is part of the metabolic reprogramming during leaf senescence. PES1 expression is induced by the NAC transcription factor RD26, which regulates metabolic changes during leaf senescence [119]. Chlorophyll turnover in leaves is a continuous process and is enhanced under stress [1]. Chlorophyll levels vary dependent on the diurnal rhythm and in response to changing light conditions [120]. A change in light irradiation rapidly results in decreased chlorophyll synthesis in phytoplankton [121]. Variations in light intensity, temperature or other adverse effects affect chlorophyll synthesis and turnover, resulting in the release of free phytol which is converted into fatty acid phytyl esters. Interestingly, the amount of fatty acid phytyl esters decreases upon disappearance of stress [96]. Thus, fatty acid phytyl esters represent an intermediate sink for phytol and fatty acids. The accumulation of free alcohols and free fatty acids is detrimental to membrane stability [122]. Esterification renders the alcohols and fatty acids less polar, a prerequisite for the deposition of fatty acid phytyl esters in plastoglobules.

# 5. Prenylquinone synthesis

Phytyl-PP and GG-PP are the substrates for the synthesis of prenyl lipids in the chloroplasts, i.e. tocochromanols (tocopherols/tocotrienols), phylloquinol and carotenoids. While tocotrienols and carotenoids are derived from the polyunsaturated GG-PP, tocopherols and phylloquinol are synthesized from phytyl-PP.

# 5.1. Tocopherol, tocomonoenol and tocotrienol synthesis

Tocochromanols constitute a group of prenylquinols synthesized in cyanobacteria and in chloroplasts of eukaryotic algae and plants. In the chloroplasts, tocochromanols are stored in the plastoglobules and thylakoid membranes [108,109]. Depending on the nature of the prenyl side chain, four forms of tocochromanols can be distinguished, i.e. tocopherols, tocotrienols, plastochromanol-8 and tocomonoenols [124]. Tocopherols carry a fully saturated prenyl side chain derived from phytol, the side chain of tocotrienols contains three double bonds and originates from geranylgeraniol. Tocopherols, tocotrienols and tocomonoenols harbor different numbers of methyl groups on the chromanol ring: δ-Tocopherol, δ-tocotrienol and δ-tocomonoenol carry one, $\beta \cdot$ -, and $\gamma$ -forms carry two, and $\alpha$ -forms carry three methyl groups at different positions (Fig. 5). Tocopherols are abundant in seed embryos, but also occur in leaves and other tissues. Tocotrienols are found in the seed endosperm of monocotyledonous plants like wheat, but also in seeds of a few dicotyledonous species (e.g. Umbelliferae family) [130]. Tocomonoenols were detected in Kalonchoe, Phaseolus, Kiwi fruit (Actinidia), oil palm (Elaeis guineensis) and pumpkin (Curcubita), and even low amounts of tocodienols were found in palm oil [129,131]. Furthermore, Arabidopsis seeds, in particular oil-deficient mutants (wri1, dgat1), accumulate $\gamma$ -tocomonoenol in addition of tocopherols [128]. Tocochromanols are important antioxidants and establish the class of vitamin E compounds essential for the human diet [132]. Tocochromanols scavenge reactive oxygen species and free radicals and therefore can protect polyunsaturated fatty acids in membranes or storage lipids from oxidation [133].

The chromanol head group of tocochromanols is derived from the shikimate pathway (for a review see [134]). Chorismate which is produced through the shikimate pathway is converted into prephenate by chorismate mutase (CM). Arabidopsis contains 3 proteins (CM1, CM2, CM3) which harbor chorismate mutase activity when expressed in yeast or E. coli [135–137]. While CM1 and CM3 localize to the chloroplast,

CM2 encodes a cytosolic isoform. Arogenate is synthesized from prephenate by prephenate aminotransferase (PAT). The PAT enzyme (At2g22250) was originally isolated from Arabidopsis and designated 'prokaryotic-type aspartate aminotransferase' because it harbors aspartate aminotransferase activity, is similar to cyanobacterial sequences and localizes to the chloroplast [138]. The At2g22250 protein of Arabidopsis and the orthologous petunia enzyme were later found to harbor PAT activity, and it was shown that the pathway from prephenate via arogenate to phenylalanine, catalyzed by PAT and arogenate dehydratase (ADT), is the major route for phenylalanine synthesis [139,140] (see below). Next, arogenate is converted into tyrosine by arogenate dehydrogenase (ADH, ADS, TyrAAT). Arabidopsis contains two ADH enzymes with arogenate dehydrogenase activity, both of which localize to the chloroplast [141,142].

p-Hydroxyphenylpyruvate (HPP) is produced from tyrosine by tyrosine aminotransferase (TAT). Arabidopsis contains at least 7 TAT genes [143]. TAT1 (At5g53970) represents the major activity for tocopherol synthesis, because Arabidopsis tat1 mutant leaves contain only $5 0 { - } 7 0 \%$ of wild type tocopherol levels [144]. TAT1 and the closely related TAT2 protein (At5g36160) localize to the cytosol [145]. Interestingly, the gene TAT1 (At5g53970) was later found to encode an enzyme which transfers an amino group from tyrosine to phenylpyruvate (PPY), i.e. to harbor phenylpyruvate aminotransferase (PPY-AT) and tyrosine aminotransferase (TAT) activities thus producing phenylalanine and HPP in the cytosol [146]. Only one of the 7 TAT proteins is predicted to harbor a chloroplast targeting sequence (At2g24850, TAT3), but its activity has not been characterized.

As indicated above, the major pathway for phenylalanine synthesis is the direct dehydration/decarboxylation of arogenate via arogenate dehydratase (ADT). Arabidopsis harbors 6 genes encoding enzymes with ADT activity, all of which localize to the chloroplast [142,147]. Three ADT enzymes also harbor low prephenate (PDT) activity (ADT1/ PDT1, ADT2/PDT2, ADT6/PDT6) [147]. Like PPY-AT/TAT1, HPPD in Arabidopsis is localized to the cytosol [124,149]. The subcellular localization of HPPD differs in other plant species. In soybean, HPPD is transcribed from two start codons. The long polypeptide harbors a signal sequence targeting HPPD to the chloroplast, while the short polypeptide of HPPD remains in the cytosol. In maize, HPPD is localized to the chloroplast [150], and in tomato, two different HPPD transcripts are found, one encoding a polypeptide with a putative chloroplast targeting sequence [151]. Several strategies were employed to increase the flux through the shikimate pathway and to boost the amount of HGA available for tocochromanol synthesis. These approaches included the introduction of a bifunctional chorismate mutase/prephenate dehydrogenase (CM/ PDH, TyrA) from Erwinia herbicola or E. coli [152,153]. This bacterial enzyme catalyzes the conversion of chorismate via prephenate into HPP, thus bypassing the intermediates arogenate and tyrosine of the plant pathway and avoiding the feed-back inhibition of chorismate mutase and arogenate dehydrogenase by tyrosine. Overexpression of TyrA in combination with HPPD in seeds of Arabidopsis, rapeseed and soybean and in Arabidopsis leaves resulted in HGA and total tocochromanol accumulation. Interestingly, a large proportion of the

additional tocochromanol $( 4 0 - 7 0 \% )$ ) accumulated in the form of tocotrienols. In a related strategy, the yeast prephenate dehydrogenase (PDH, Tyr1) which converts prephenate into tyrosine was overexpressed in tobacco leaves, in combination with HPPD. Similar to the transgenic TyrA/HPPD plants, the Tyr1/HPPD plants accumulated large amounts of tocochromanols in the leaves, with a considerable proportion of tocotrienols [154]. Another approach to alter the precursor supply for tocochromanol synthesis included the overexpression of the E. coli bifunctional chorismate mutase/prephenate dehydratase (CM/PDT, PheA) which converts chorismate into phenylpyruvate [155]. Leaves of transgenic Arabidopsis plants accumulated phenylalanine and HGA, which can be explained by an increased export of phenylpyruvate produced by PheA from the chloroplast. In the cytosol, phenylpyruvate is converted into phenylalanine accompanied with the deamination of tyrosine to HPP by PPY-AT/TAT1 [146]. HGA is then produced from HPP, and the accumulation of HGA finally results in increased production of $\gamma$ -tocopherol and $\gamma$ -tocotrienol [155].

In addition, overexpression of HPPD alone in Synechocystis (which contains an endogenous TyrA gene) resulted in an increase in HGA and tocochromanol, $2 0 ~ \%$ of which was in the form of tocotrienol [152]. Finally, a soybean mutant was isolated which harbors a mutation in homogentisate dioxygenase (hgo1). The corresponding enzyme HGO1, is involved in HGA degradation in the cytosol. Soybean hgo1 mutant seeds accumulated HGA and in addition, contained higher amounts of tocopherol and tocotrienol [156]. Taken together, these results demonstrate that HGA accumulation causes an increase in tocotrienol production, presumably mediated by increased condensation of HGA with GG-PP by homogentisic acid phytyltransferase (HPT, VTE2, see below). Arabidopsis VTE2 shows more activity with phytyl-PP than with GG-PP [157], but under conditions when phytyl-PP becomes limiting, VTE2 also employs GG-PP for tocotrienol synthesis. In agreement with this scenario, co-overexpression of GGR with HPPD (see above) in Synechocystis resulted in the conversion of a large amount of tocotrienols into tocopherols. However, co-overexpression of GGR in the transgenic seeds of Arabidopsis, rape and soybean expressing TyrA and HPPD, did not result in the conversion of tocotrienols into tocopherols [152]. In plants, GGR is part of the LIL3 chlorophyll synthesis complex, while in Synechocystis, GGR was not yet found in the corresponding HliC/HliD/Ycf39 complex (chapter 3.1). This might explain why in plants, reduction of the geranylgeranyl to the phytyl group by GGR could be differently regulated as compared to Synechocystis.

Tocopherol is synthesized via the condensation of HGA and phytyl-PP catalyzed by VTE2 which forms 2-methyl-6-phytyl-benzoquinol (MPBQ) [158] (Fig. 5). Homogentisic acid geranylgeranyltransferase (HGGT), a VTE2-related prenyltransferase found in tocotrienol-containing species, is involved in the synthesis of tocotrienols [157,159]. Expression of barley HGGT in transgenic Arabidopsis plants resulted in the increase of large amounts of tocotrienols without affecting growth [159]. For tocomonoenol synthesis, HGA is condensed with THGG-PP, a partially reduced form of GG-PP, catalyzed by VTE2 in Arabidopsis [128]. Closure of the second ring of the tocochromanol head group is conducted by tocopherol cyclase (VTE1) [160]. Ring closure of MPBQ gives rise to δ-tocopherol. Methylation of MPBQ is catalyzed by VTE3 yielding 2,3-dimethyl-5-phytyl-1,4-benzoquinone (DMPBQ). DMPBQ can be converted into $\gamma$ -tocopherol by VTE1. Further methylation by $\gamma$ -tocopherol methyltransferase (VTE4) results in the conversion of $\gamma$ -tocopherol and δ-tocopherol into $\alpha$ -tocopherol and $\beta$ -tocopherol, respectively [126,161].

Arabidopsis lines carrying mutations in one of the VTE genes show changes in the amount or composition in tocochromanols. Under normal conditions, tocopherol-deficient mutants (vte1, vte2, vte4, vte5) grow similarly as wild type [101,160,163,164]. However, the vte3 mutant as well as the vte6 mutant with a defect in phytyl-P kinase activity (chapters 4.1., 5.2) are severely dwarfed and are incapable of photoautotrophic growth [68,165]. The tocopherol deficient vte1 and vte2 mutants show reduced seed longevity, and seedling growth of vte2

plants, but not of vte1, is defective [164]. This difference was explained by the accumulation of the biosynthetic precursor DMPBQ which harbors antioxidant activity in vte1, while DMPBQ is absent from vte2.

# 5.2. Plastoquinol-9 and plastochromanol-8 synthesis

Plastoquinol-9 (PQ-9) is the lipid electron carrier of photosystem II and is derived from condensation of HGA with solanesyl-PP, catalyzed by HGA solanesyl transferase (HST), and methylation by VTE3. The enzyme activities required for PQ-9 synthesis, HST and the methyltransferase VTE3, were previously localized to the chloroplast, in particular to the inner envelope [166]. The solanesyl side chain (C45) is derived from solanesyl-PP which is produced by successive condensation of nine C5-PP units. Enzyme assays after expression in E. coli showed that two IDS/PTS enzymes, SPS1 and SPS2, accept farnesyl-PP or GG-PP for further condensation with isopentenyl-PP, resulting in the synthesis of solanesyl-PP (C45-PP) [167,168]. While the localization of SPS1 remained unclear, SPS2 was localized to the chloroplast. Therefore, SPS2 is the most likely candidate for solanesyl-PP production for plastoquinol synthesis [168]. Another prenyltransferase, designated polyprenyltransferase (PPTS), is also involved in C45-PP production. PPTS carries a putative mitochondrial signal sequence and therefore might be involved in ubiquinol synthesis [37]. Finally, PQ-9 is produced by methylation of 2-methyl-6-solanesylbenzoquinol by VTE3. The vte3-2 mutant of Arabidopsis lacks PQ-9, is severely dwarfed and only survives on sucrose containing medium [165].

Plastochromanol-8 (PC-8) is synthesized from PQ-9 by VTE1 [125,162] (Fig. 5). Thus, PC-8 harbors the same head group as $\gamma$ -tocopherol which carries two methyl groups, but a much longer side chain of 40 C atoms. To study the role of PC-8 in Arabidopsis, a double mutant vte1 vte2 was generated which lacks tocopherols, PC-8 and the tocopherol precursor DMPBQ. The vte1 vte2 double mutant shows a more severe phenotype compared with either parent, in particular with regard to lipid oxidation in seeds and alterations in seedling development, suggesting that PC-8 provides an important antioxidant function in addition to tocopherols [162]. On the other hand, the vte1 vte2 plants still contain high amounts of PQ-9, which is structurally related to DMPBQ. PQ-9 strongly increases during stress and also harbors antioxidant activity [169].

# 5.3. Phylloquinol synthesis

Phylloquinol (vitamin K1) is the lipid electron carrier in photosystem I. Like tocopherol, phylloquinol is found in the thylakoid membranes and in plastoglobules [108,170]. Vitamin K is essential for the human diet because it serves as co-factor during $\gamma$ -carboxylation of glutamate residues in proteins involved in blood coagulation (e.g. prothrombin) and bone formation (osteocalcin). In contrast to plants, bacteria contain so-called menaquinols (for methyl-naphthoquinols) which harbor the same head group as phylloquinol but longer (5 and more isoprene units) and polyunsaturated side chains. [171]. Menaquinols serve as electron carriers in bacteria and establish the vitamin K2 group of compounds. In humans and animals, phylloquinol taken up by animals from the diet, and (long chain) menaquinol taken up from gut bacteria, are converted into menaquinol-4 (side chain with 4 unsaturated isoprene units) [172]. This conversion is mediated by cleavage of the side chain, releasing menadione to which subsequently a geranylgeranyl group is attached [172]. The function of the double bonds in the side chain of bacterial menaquinol and the reasons why plants exclusively produce phylloquinol, while humans and animals convert plant phylloquinol and bacterial menaquinols into menaquinol-4, remain unclear.

# 6. Conclusions

Phytol and geranylgeraniol are two C20 prenol alcohols crucial for isoprenoid metabolism in the chloroplast. Geranylgeraniol is reduced by GGR, most likely in the form of GG-chlorophyll within the chlorophyll synthesis complex containing LIL3, GGR, POR, CHLG and additional proteins. Free phytol released from chlorophyll can be phosphorylated by VTE5 and VTE6 resulting in the production of phytyl-PP which is available for the synthesis of tocopherols and phylloquinol. Decreased GGR activity in rice or tobacco, or in the lil3:1 lil3:2 mutant of Arabidopsis, results in the accumulation of GG-chlorophyll and partially reduced forms of chlorophyll. It remains unclear why geranylgeraniol is not also employed for tocotrienol synthesis in these plants. On the other hand, transgenic Arabidopsis plants accumulating HGA after expression of microbial enzymes (TyrA, Tyr1, PheA), thereby bypassing part of the shikimate pathway, do produce tocotrienols. Tocotrienols in TyrA, Tyr1 and PheA plants are synthesized by VTE2 which was shown to be active with GG-PP in addition to phytyl-PP. These results imply that the relative amounts of HGA, GG-PP and phytyl-PP, or the existence of subplastidic pools of these substrates, are critical for the regulation of geranylgeranyl and phytyl metabolism. For most phytol-derived chloroplast lipids, a geranylgeraniol counterpart exists in plants, bacteria or humans and animals, i.e. tocopherol/tocotrienol, phylloquinol/menaquinol-4, fatty acid phytyl esters/fatty acid geranylgeranyl esters, but the functions of the additional double bonds in the prenyl side chains remain unclear.
