# Summary

Late blight caused by Phytophthora infestans is the most serious disease of potatoes. Here we present the effectiveness of the host-induced gene silencing (HIGS) technology against an amino acid biosynthesis gene of the pathogen to increase the resistance against the plant-infecting oomycete in the field. A RNAi hairpin construct directed against the acetolactate synthase (ALS) gene of Phytophthora infestans was transferred into potato. HIGS-ALS potato lines displayed efficient target gene silencing revealed by a luciferase reporter gene assay. Plant-derived siRNAs targeting the oomycete’s ALS gene were detected by small RNA sequencing. ALS gene expression of P. infestans was reduced during the early infection stages of HIGS-ALS potatoes, as shown by qRT-PCR. HIGS-ALS plants revealed an enhanced late blight resistance in detached leaf assays. ALS gene silencing also conferred strong enhanced late blight resistance to the HIGS lines in trials under near-field conditions in Europe and in field trials in the USA against European and US P. infestans isolates, respectively. These results demonstrated the value of the HIGS technology for the development of a new quantitative resistance source for potato against Phytophthora infestans. 

# Introduction

Potatoes are grown in almost all countries of the world and provide more calories, vitamins and nutrients per area of land sown than other staple crops (Nunn and Qian, 2011). The introduction of the potato from South America into Europe was responsible for a significant portion of the increase in population and urbanization, first in Europe and later in parts of Asia. According to conservative estimates, the introduction of the potato into the Old World accounts for approximately 25–26 percent of the increase in total population and 27–34 percent of the increase in urbanization between 1700 and 1900 (Nunn and Qian, 2011). The potato has not lost its role as an important staple crop and is, after wheat and rice, the third most important crop consumed by humans (Haverkort et al., 2009). Although the impacts of climate change on potato are favourable compared to other major crops (Challinor et al., 2014), potato yield and quality are still threatened by diseases. 

Late blight caused by the oomycete Phytophthora infestans is a major constraint to the production of potato, resulting in lower yield and tuber rot during storage (Kamoun et al., 2015). The most popular control strategy against late blight is complete disease prevention by weekly spraying of a mixture of preventive and systemic fungicides (Ivanov et al., 2021). Plant breeding efforts have been made for more than 130 years but have yet to 

provide a durable solution for late blight resistance (Kamoun et al., 2015). 

Introgression of single late blight resistance (Rpi) genes from the wild species Solanum demissum by breeding was not sustainable since the resistance was always broken down (Fry et al., 2015; Haverkort et al., 2009). More than 20 Rpi genes have been mapped and cloned from different Solanum species (Ballvora et al., 2002; Foster et al., 2009; Huang et al., 2005; Park et al., 2005; Pel et al., 2009; van der Vossen et al., 2005; Vossen et al., 2016; Witek et al., 2016, 2021). Stacking several R genes in potato might be a promising way to develop durable, high-level resistance (Haverkort et al., 2009; Jo et al., 2014; Witek et al., 2021). The stacking of three R genes showed a positive effect on late blight resistance as compared to a single R gene and conferred complete field resistance (Ghislain et al., 2019). 

The transfer of a pattern recognition receptor (PRRs) gene is another strategy for a more durable late blight resistance and provided lower levels of quantitative resistance for single receptors (Albert et al., 2015; Du et al., 2015; Torres Ascurra et al., 2023). Several transgenic concepts like the constitutive expression of pathogenesis-related proteins PR-1a and PR-5 (Zhu et al., 1996), the transfer of the resveratrol synthase gene (Thomzik et al., 1997), the expression of antimicrobial peptides (Osusky et al., 2005) or the inactivation of susceptibility genes by RNAi or CRISPR/Cas9 (Kieu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2016) resulted 

in an increase in plant resistance against P. infestans under laboratory conditions. 

P. infestans is a diploid organism with a high genetic variation and a high number of effector proteins located in dynamic regions of the genome which might promote rapid adaptation to resistant plants (Coomber et al., 2024; Haas et al., 2009; Vleeshouwers et al., 2011). P. infestans colonizes potato as well as tomato plants. Its sporangia are spread by wind or water droplets and release motile zoospores under cool and damp environmental conditions. Those zoospores encyst and penetrate the plant surface by germ tubes. Following the development of appressoria, primary and secondary hyphae grow inside the host tissue and differentiate haustoria. The biotrophic stage switches to necrotrophic growth which leads to the formation of sporangiophores that emerge through host stomata. First necrotic spots are detectable after 3 days, followed by sporulation and subsequently new host plants are reached through the spread of sporangia through air or water droplets (Fry, 2008; Leesutthiphonchai et al., 2018). 

Grenville-Briggs et al. (2005) showed that five distinct amino acid biosynthesis genes, a methionine synthase (PiMET ), a ketol-acid reductoisomerase (PiKARI ), a tryptophan synthase (PiTRP ), an acetolactate synthase (PiALS ) and a threonine synthase (PiTS ) were upregulated in germinating cysts with appressoria when compared to non-sporulating mycelium. Four of these P. infestans genes were also upregulated, although to lower levels, during the early, biotrophic phase of the interaction in potato, and all were considerably upregulated during the transition (48 hpi) to the necrotrophic phase of the interaction. The authors concluded that the biosynthesis of the amino acids encoded by these genes is important during the establishment of potato infection by P. infestans. 

Gene silencing mechanisms have been identified in mammals, plants, but also in fungi and can influence diverse levels of genome regulation like transcription, RNA processing, RNA stability, translation, chromatin structure and chromosome segregation. Small RNAs specifically bind to complementary nucleic acid sequences and thereby guide modulating proteins to these targets, mainly resulting in the inhibition of gene expression at the targeted locus (Carthew and Sontheimer, 2009). In plants, the RNAi mechanism is used as a tool to manipulate gene expression, for example, by introducing self-complementary hairpin RNA (hpRNA) for specific gene silencing. The resulting dsRNA causes sequencespecific mRNA degradation: DICER-like enzymes recognize the dsRNA and cleave the molecule into small RNA duplexes (sRNAs; 21–23 bp) that are bound by a ribonucleoprotein complex (RNA induced silencing complex; RISC). Unwinding the sRNA duplex leads to single stranded siRNA and an active RISC which binds homologous mRNA via base-pairing, finally resulting in mRNA degradation (reviewed in Rosa et al., 2018; Chaudhary et al., 2024). 

Movement of dsRNA and siRNA occur between host plants and their parasites and can be used to target genes of plant invading organisms in a mechanism called host-induced gene silencing (HIGS) according to Nowara et al. (2010). Control of pathogen development on the host plant via HIGS during host-pathogen interaction was shown to depend on the selected target gene: transcript localisation and expression levels as well as its protein stability might affect silencing efficacy and subsequently the HIGS-derived impact on pathogen defence (Karimi and Innes, 2022; Nowara et al., 2010; Yin et al., 2011). This technique has been successfully applied for gene silencing in plant pathogens and pests like fungi (Capriotti et al., 2024; Koch 

et al., 2013; Nowara et al., 2010; Panwar et al., 2018; Wang and Dean, 2022; Zhu et al., 2017), nematodes (Dutta et al., 2015; Fairbairn et al., 2007; Huang et al., 2006) and insects (Abdellatef et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2015). 

Enzymes of the RNAi machinery have been identified for P. infestans (Vetukuri et al., 2011) and RNAi is used as a standard method to characterize genes by transient or stable gene silencing in this oomycete (Abrahamian et al., 2016; Whisson et al., 2005). Plant-mediated gene silencing of the Phytophthora infestans genes PiGPB1, PiGAPDH and PiAVR3a by hairpin constructs restricted the growth of the late blight pathogen on transgenic potato plants in growth chamber assays (Jahan et al., 2015; Sanju et al., 2015). 

RNAi can also be used for plant protection by directly spraying dsRNA on plant surfaces. This strategy is named spray-induced gene silencing (SIGS) according to Koch et al., 2016. SIGS was used to increase the Fusarium graminearum resistance of barley (Koch et al., 2016), Botrytis cinerea resistance of tomato fruits, strawberry fruits, grapes, lettuce, onion and rose (Wang et al., 2016) and to improve the resistance of oilseed rape to B. cinerea and Sclerotinia sclerotiorum (McLoughlin et al., 2018). SIGS was also successfully applied to reduce the infection of potato leaves by P. infestans in a detached leaf assay by downregulation of the genes PiGPB1, PiHmp, PiCut3 and PiEndo3 (Kalyandurg et al., 2021). The genes PiHmp and PiCut3 proved to be suitable RNAi candidates to control late blight by SIGS under field conditions (Wang et al., 2023). 

In contrast to the large number of studies which show that HIGS is effective against fungi under laboratory and greenhouse conditions (reviewed in Machado et al., 2018; Rajam and Chauhan, 2021; Koch and Wassenegger, 2021), knowledge about the efficiency of HIGS under field conditions is limited. Field data are restricted mainly to some members of the Deuteromycota and are missing except for F. graminearum for pathogens classified as Ascomycota, Basidiomycota and Oomycetes. Enhanced fungal resistance was reported for HIGS wheat plants under near field conditions against Fusarium culmorum (Chen et al., 2016) and in the field against F. graminearum (Cheng et al., 2015). Under field conditions, HIGS banana plants were more resistant against Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. cubense (Dou et al., 2020) and transgenic HIGS maize plants showed less accumulation of the toxin aflatoxin after infection by Aspergillus flavus (Raruang et al., 2020). 

In this study we demonstrated that HIGS is an effective tool to control potato late blight infection in the field. Expression of a RNAi construct in the plants led to the formation of siRNAs which target a specific P. infestans gene. This resulted in decreased transcript abundance in the oomycete during infection and subsequently in less severe disease symptoms and strongly reduced infection dynamics on HIGS plants both in the greenhouse and in the field.

# HIGS lines ALS1 and ALS2 silence the expression of a reporter gene–ALS fusion construct

The efficiency of the transferred RNAi hairpin cassette to downregulate the target gene expression was validated in the transformed potato lines ALS1, ALS2 and ALS3. The assays were done with a luciferase fusion system as described by Birch et al., 2010. The transcriptional luciferase fusion construct pd35S-Luc- $A L S _ { \mathrm { e x o n 4 } }$ was cloned with the HIGS target sequence 

of the PiALS exon 4 inserted downstream of the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -untranslated region of the luciferase reporter gene from Photinus pyralis (Figure 1c). The expression of the reporter gene fusion construct was under the control of the 35S CaMV promoter with a doubled 35S enhancer sequence. The vector p70SRuc, which expressed the luciferase gene of Renilla reniformis under the control of the 35S promoter, was used as a normalization vector. Functional HIGS plants should generate siRNAs that target the PiALS sequence of the reporter gene transcripts and subsequently lead to reduced luciferase activity. After transient biolistic co-transformation of the luciferase fusion construct $\mathsf { p d } 3 5 5  – L u c – A L S _ { \mathsf { e x o n } 4 }$ and the normalization construct p70SRuc into leaf discs of the HIGS-ALS and control lines, the reporter gene activities were measured. As controls, leaves from the transformation genotype Hermes and the line RNAi-GFP were used, as this construct was stably transformed with an RNAi construct against the gene of the green fluorescent protein (GFP). The lines ALS1 and ALS2 showed a strong reduction of normalized reporter gene activity in all assays compared to the transformation genotype Hermes, the ALS3 and the transgenic RNAi-GFP line as shown in Figure 2a. In the mean of 3 experiments, $2 8 \%$ and $23 \%$ of Photinus pyralis luciferase activity were detected for ALS1 and ALS2, as well as $7 7 \%$ luciferase activity for ALS3 compared to the controls Hermes and RNAi-GFP (Figure 2a). These functional data showed that the ALS1 and ALS2 lines, but not the ALS3 line, express an effective RNAi construct for silencing PiALS gene expression in planta.

# Expression of the PiALS gene is reduced in the pathogen during infection of HIGS-ALS potato leaves

The silencing of the PiALS gene expression in P. infestans was determined by a qRT-PCR analysis during the infection of leaves from the HIGS-ALS lines. Potato leaves of the HIGS lines ALS1 and 

ALS2 as well as the non-transgenic cultivar Hermes were infected in a detached leaf assay. Leaf samples were taken 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6 dpi and isolated RNA analysed for the expression of the acetolactate synthase and actin gene of the oomycete. The normalized transcript levels of the PiALS gene from P. infestans were significantly reduced in the lines ALS1 and ALS2 in comparison to the control cultivar Hermes during the early, biotrophic phase of the interaction at 1 and 2 dpi. At 3 dpi, only the line ALS2 showed a significant silencing of the PiALS gene compared to Hermes. During the necrotrophic phase of the interaction at 4, 5 and 6 dpi, the PiALS transcript levels of the RNAi lines ALS1 and ALS2 showed no significant difference compared to the non-transgenic line (Figure 2e). These data showed that the HIGS lines downregulate the expression of the PiALS gene of the pathogen in the early but not in the late phase of leaf infection.

# Discussion

HIGS was successfully applied to downregulate the expression of the PiALS gene in P. infestans and to increase the late blight resistance under laboratory and field conditions. The HIGS lines ALS1 and ALS2 showed enhanced resistance against an aggressive European P. infestans isolate under near-field conditions in two-year trials in Germany and against a US isolate in a one-year trial at 3 locations in the USA. 

The selection of the candidate gene is the most critical step for the HIGS strategy. Appropriate candidate genes of P. infestans are either virulence factors like the Avr3a gene (Sanju et al., 2015) or genes controlling critical steps of metabolic pathways (Jahan et al., 2015). Grenville-Briggs et al. (2005) concluded that the amino acid biosynthesis genes PiMET, PiKARI, PiTRP, PiALS and PiTS and the corresponding amino acids are important for the pathogenicity of P. infestans. The results of this study confirmed that the PiALS gene plays an important role for the virulence of P. infestans. 

In addition to amino acid biosynthesis genes, amino acid transporters and nitrogen assimilation genes are also suitable targets. Several amino acid transporters of P. infestans were identified which were upregulated either in infected leaves or tubers or only in leaves. The transcript level of a nitrate transporter of P. infestans (PiNTR) was 100-fold upregulated in infected potato leaves. The nitrate transporter was co-regulated with a nitrate reductase (PiNR) and a nitrite reductase (PiNIR), which convert nitrogen into ammonium and amino acids. Downregulation of the nitrate transporter in P. infestans resulted in a loss of pathogenicity (Abrahamian et al., 2016). 

In a first molecular assay, the RNAi-mediated silencing activity of the lines ALS1, ALS2 and ALS3 was determined. The lines ALS1 and ALS2 showed a strong silencing of a transiently transformed PiALS-luciferase reporter gene construct in contrast to line ALS3. This result correlated with the resistance data from the detached leaf assay. The HIGS target reporter gene assay proved to be a good molecular tool to identify potato lines with strong gene silencing activity. The assay might be a first screening method for HIGS plants to select promising lines for resistance assays. 

Small RNA sequencing and alignment of the reads to the PiALS gene revealed that the long dsRNA transcripts of the RNAi hairpin construct pRNAi-ALS are processed into siRNAs which match the exon 4 of the PiALS gene. The 21-nt siRNAs are the most prominent siRNAs derived from the silencing vector in the HIGS potatoes according to RNA blot analysis and small RNA sequencing. The siRNAs are generated with the help of Dicerlike (DLC) proteins in plants. Plants possess a core set of 4 DLC proteins which generate siRNAs of different sizes (Rosa et al., 2018). DCL3 is specifically required to produce 24-nt siRNAs, and DCL4 is required to produce 21-nt siRNAs (Karimi and Innes, 2022). The number of siRNAs which were assorted to exon 4 of the PiALS gene is very high in the HIGS lines ALS1 and ALS2 as was shown in two independent RNA sequencing experiments. In total, $1 1 \% - 1 5 \%$ of all detected potato siRNAs were derived from the silencing construct. The PiALS specific siRNAs were already detectable at the time point of inoculation (0 dpi) and their proportion of the total siRNA molecules did not change during infection (2–4 dpi). This showed that the RNAi hairpin construct is strongly expressed by the constitutively active 35S CaMV promoter and the siRNA machinery is working well in the HIGS lines. 

The expression of the PiALS gene in P. infestans is reduced in the early phase of leaf infection of the HIGS lines as shown by qRT-PCR. A significant reduction of PiLAS transcripts was only detectable at 1 and 2 dpi for HIGS line ALS1 and at 1, 2 and 3 dpi for line ALS2 in the DLA. At the later time points 4–6 dpi, the PiALS gene expression of ALS1 and 2 was comparable to the PiALS expression of the non-transgenic cultivar Hermes. At the early time points 1–3 dpi, the inoculated leaves showed no visible symptoms, and the pathogen was still in the biotrophic period of infection. With the appearance of necrotic areas at 4 and 5 dpi, 

the pathogen entered the necrotic phase of infection. The gene silencing of ALS2 is stronger than the gene silencing of ALS1, as seen at 1 and 2 dpi, which explained why ALS2 could still suppress PiALS expression at 3 dpi in contrast to ALS1. 

Obviously, siRNAs present in the early phase of infection are sufficient for gene silencing of the pathogen. During the necrotrophic phase of infection, which is characterized by maceration of plant tissue and propagation of the oomycete (4–6 dpi) silencing of the pathogen ALS gene was not detectable. The uptake of plant siRNAs might mainly take place at the haustorial membrane present in the vital plant cells and might be severely limited in necrotrophic hyphae. Studies on the uptake of siRNAs are needed to substantiate this assumption. 

P. infestans might also use effectors to reduce the RNA silencing mechanism during the late phase of potato infection. Two RXLR effectors from the oomycete plant pathogen Phytophthora sojae suppress RNA silencing in plants by inhibiting the biogenesis of small RNA, as described by Qiao et al. (2013). These RXLR effectors might become an ideal HIGS target for optimizing the stability of HIGS. 

P. infestans can spread dramatically in a field after successfully infecting a single potato leaf. Under favourable conditions, the pathogen can complete the cycle from infection to sporulation in 4 days. In the field, this cycle is repeated multiple times during one growing season, resulting in billions of spores and a continuous increase in disease pressure (Kamoun et al., 2015). The reduction of the number of spores and sporangia during each infection cycle is a crucial step for dampening the infection dynamics and improving resistance. 

The HIGS lines ALS1 and ALS2 displayed, in contrast to line ALS3 and the non-transgenic cultivar Hermes, enhanced leaf blight resistance, as shown with an average reduction of $5 7 \%$ and $6 8 \%$ sporangia production in the detached leaf assays. The relevance of repeated infection cycles and sporangia production for disease development was specifically considered by the execution of the tests under near-field conditions in Germany. A single punctual inoculation of only 2 leaves in each plot corresponded to natural infection and allowed a better measurement of infection dynamics than a leaf test in the laboratory. 

After the selective inoculation of the plots, the spread of late blight was severely delayed for the ALS1 and ALS2 lines from the inoculation time point until the final disease scoring at 32 dpi. The two HIGS lines showed less plot destruction compared to Hermes at each scoring time point. In the mean of the 2-year trials, late blight infestation of the ALS2 line was reduced by $50 \%$ compared to Hermes 5 weeks after inoculation. 

In the US field trials, each plot was also inoculated locally, but additionally, infected spreader rows were used for continuous spore production. Again, enhanced resistance was observed at 3 locations against a US late blight isolate, as shown by nearly $60 \%$ less plot destruction of line ALS1 and $50 \%$ less destruction of the ALS2 plots at the final scoring time point at 42 dpi. 

The results of the near-field and field trials demonstrated that silencing of the PiLAS gene of P. infestans by the HIGS lines ALS1 and ALS2 resulted in a substantial improvement in late blight resistance at different locations and under different environmental conditions. Furthermore, the HIGS approach caused a broad resistance against different P. infestans isolates. 

After the effectiveness of HIGS against P. infestans had already been shown in growth chamber assays (Jahan et al., 2015; Sanju et al., 2015) this study showed that HIGS will also allow disease control of potatoes against the oomycete P. infestans in the field. 

Despite the detection of PiALS-specific siRNAs in the HIGS lines and the reduction of PiALS transcripts during infection, some questions are still not answered in the presented study. Neither the uptake of PiALS-specific siRNAs by the pathogen nor the siRNA-mediated degradation of PiALS transcripts in P. infestans was shown. Therefore, the observed growth inhibition of $P .$ infestans is most likely, but not proven, to be caused by HIGS. 

HIGS is a promising strategy to generate new quantitative resistance sources for potato against P. infestans. The combination of the HIGS approach by silencing a pathogen gene with a qualitative resistance mediated by late blight resistance genes could be a good strategy to develop a more durable late blight resistance. The combination of a quantitative and qualitative resistance gene with a different mode of action should rule out a complete collapse of resistance. 

# Material and methods

# Plant and P. infestans cultivation

Potatoes of the cultivars Hermes, Russet Burbank and the derived transgenic lines were grown in 3 L pods for biolistic transformation assays and in 5 L pods for detached leaf assays in the greenhouse at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ day and $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ night temperatures. 

The late blight isolate Gross-Luesewitz was cultivated for maintenance and infection assays on tuber slices of surface sterilized potatoes in a plastic box. After one week of cultivation, the mycelium with the sporangia was carefully harvested from the potato slices and incubated in water at $1 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $4 \textrm { h }$ to induce the release of zoospores. The zoospores were used to reinfect fresh potato slices or to infect potato leaves after determination of zoospore concentration. 

# Vector cloning

The RNAi hairpin vector pRNAi-ALS was cloned by inserting the exon 4 sequence of the PiALS (PITG_03410) gene in sense and antisense orientation into the vector pRNAi. The pRNAi vector contains the CaMV 35S promoter, a multiple cloning site (MCS), the AtAAP6 intron from the Arabidopsis thaliana amino acid permease, a second MCS and the CaMV 35S terminator. The 300 bp large sequence of the PiALS exon 4 was PCR amplified from genomic DNA of P. infestans using the primer S1258 (50 - CGCTCGAGGCTGGATCTCG CGCTGAGGT-30 ) and S1259 (50 - TTGATATCGCGGAAGGCGAG AGACATCG-30 ) that added a XhoI and a SmaI restriction site to the DNA fragment. The PCR fragment of exon 4 was digested with XhoI and SmaI and was cloned in sense orientation into the MCS upstream of the AtAAP6 intron of the pRNAi vector. The resulting vector was digested with SmaI-SalI and the PCR fragment of exon 4 was cloned as an XhoI-SmaI fragment in antisense orientation in the MCS downstream of the AtAAP6 intron to obtain pRNAi-ALS. The RNAi expression cassette containing the 35S promoter, the PiALS exon 4 fragment in sense orientation, the AtAAP6 intron 2, the PiALS exon 4 fragment in antisense orientation and the 35S terminator was cloned into the binary vector pGPTV-Kan (Becker et al., 1992). The resulting vector was named pRNAi-ALS-Kan. 

# Stable transformation of the HIGS construct into potato

Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transformation of potato was performed according to a slightly modified protocol (Pel et al., 2009) by using the antibiotic Kanamycin. Internodes of the potato variety Hermes were transformed with the binary vector pRNAi-ALS-Kan. Kanamycin-resistant shoots were placed 

on rooting medium and tested for construct integration via diagnostic PCR. A 553 bp large fragment of the nptII gene was amplified with the primer Bo2299 (50 -GTGGAGAGGC-TATTCGGTA-30 ) and the primer Bo2300 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GTGGAGAGGC-TATTCGGTA-30 ). The exon 4 of the PiALS gene in sense orientation of the silencing construct was detected as a 370 bp product with the primers S334 (50 -ATCCCACTATCCTTCGCAAG-30 ) and S1259 (see above). The PiALS exon 4 sequence in antisense orientation was amplified as a 450 bp large DNA fragment with the primer S329 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CTAAGGGTTTCTTATATGCT-CAAC-30 ) and S1259 (see above). 

The copy number of the transferred RNAi cassette was determined by TaqMan real-time PCR analysis and DNA-blot analysis. The nptII gene was quantified with the primers nptIIxxxf01 (50 -GCTGTGCTCGACGTTGTCA-30 ), nptIIxxxr01 (50 - CGGCACTTCGCCCAAT AG-30 ) and the probe nptIIxxxMGB (50 - AAGCGGGAAGGGACT-30 ). The Solanum tuberosum gene StEF1, which encodes the S. tuberosum elongation factor 1, was used for normalization. The StEF1 gene was determined with the primers StEF1Bxxxf04 (50 - CGAGCACTGGTGCATATCCAT-30 ), StEF1Bxxxr04 (50 - TCACTGCCCAGGTCATCATC-30 ) and the probe StEF1BxxxMGB.1 (TCTGGCCAGGATGGT). As a control, a genetically modified potato line that carries one copy of the transferred construct including the nptII gene was included in the qPCR analyses. DNA blot analysis was done to identify a control line with one integrated nptII gene. The genomic DNA was digested with XbaI, which cuts once in the T-DNA. The blot was hybridized with the radioactively labelled Nos promoter-nptII fragment. 

# RNA analysis

Total RNAs were isolated from infected and uninfected potato leaves by Trizol reagent (Invitrogen). For qRT-PCR, the first strand cDNA was prepared using RevertAid First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Thermo Scientific). Real-time PCR was performed on a ViiA7 Cycler with SYBR Green Supermix (Life Technologies), following a two-step protocol: $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 10 min, 40 cycles of denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 s and annealing/extension at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 min. 

The expression of the PiALS gene was quantified by real-time PCR amplification of an 81 bp large cDNA product encoded by exon 3 of the gene using the primer S1432 $( 5 ^ { \prime } -$ CAGCTCGAGGATGTCGTGTA 30 ) and S1433 $( 5 ^ { \prime } -$ GCGCTCGACGTAGTTCTTTT 30 ). The PiALS gene expression was normalized against the expression of the ß-tubulin gene (PITG_00156) of P. infestans, which was amplified with the primer S1446 (50 - TTTCCCCGGTCAGCTGAACT-30 ) and S1447 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -TGTCAGAGG AGCGAAACCAA-30 ). 

For the preparation of the small RNAs libraries preparation, RNA was isolated by using the miRVana Kit (Quiagen). Preparation of 12 small-cDNA libraries (cDNA synthesis and ligation of adaptors, reverse transcription, gel purification (PAGE), PCR amplification, library purification and QC), sequencing of small-RNA samples on Illumina HiSeq 2000 v3.0 ( $1 \times 1 0 0$ bp single read module) was performed by Eurofins AG (Ebersberg, Germany). 

siRNA reads were sequenced with the Illumina HiSeq system using the single end 100 bp module. To exclude sequencing adapters, two rounds of trimming were carried out using the fastq-mcf tool in the package ea-utils, until no more adapters could be located by the quality control tool FastQC. Because sRNA data from both P. infestans and S. tuberosum is present, a novel reference genome was necessary. Therefore, a novel reference 

was compiled containing (a) all chromosomes from the PGSC DM v4.03 potato assembly and (b) all supercontigs from the P. infestans T30-4_1 assembly (http://www.broadinstitute.org/). Alignments were made to this combined reference genome using the aligner Bowtie2 with default settings. Those alignments containing mismatches were filtered out by the program bamutils from the package NGSutils. To obtain a draft annotation for $P .$ . infestans, transcripts were mapped against the available supercontigs using gmap v2014-06-10 with default settings except for GFF3 output and number of specified cores (Wu and Watanabe, 2005). All data were then visualized in the genome browser JBrowse (Westesson et al., 2013). 

The UEA sRNA workbench (Stocks et al., 2012) tool mirCat was used to localize new miRNA from the datasets. Lastly, the transcript annotation from gmap was used to quantify the number of reads aligned to each transcript in the P. infestans supercontigs using the tool bedtools multicov v2.19.0 (Quinlan and Hall, 2010). Thereafter, the numbers of raw reads aligned were normalized by the number of aligned reads from each library (Reads Per Million aligned reads, RPM). 

# Control of efficient target gene silencing in transgenic HIGS plants

For the reporter gene assay according to Birch et al. (2010) the reporter gene plasmid pd35S-Luc-ALSexon4 was constructed based on the plasmid pd35S-Luc. The vector pd35S-Luc contained the CaMV 35S promoter with a doubled enhancer sequence, a MCS, the coding sequence of the luciferase gene from Photinus pyralis, with a modified intron PIV2 originating from the potato gene St-LS1 (Eckes et al., 1986), a second MCS and the Nos terminator of the nopalin synthase gene from A. tumefaciens. Exon 4 of the PiALS gene fragment was inserted as a HindIII-SalI fragment into the MCS downstream of the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end of the luciferase gene, creating a transcriptional fusion. The resulting plasmid was named $\mathsf { p d } 3 5 5  – L u c – A L S _ { \mathsf { e x o n } 4 }$ . Functional HIGS plants were identified due to their ability to silence the fused luciferase-target gene sequence via measuring their luciferase activity after bombardment of transgenic leaf discs with the reporter gene construct. Plasmid pd35S-Luc-ALSexon4 was mixed with the normalization vector p70SRuc (Schmidt et al., 2004) before DNA coating of the microcarriers. The normalization vector p70SRuc expressed the luciferase gene of Renilla reniformis under the control of the CaMV 35 promoter and doubled enhancer sequence. Leaf discs (1 cm diameter) from leaves of 6–8 weeks old greenhouse plants of the cultivar Hermes were cut with a cork borer, mingled and put on $\mathsf { M S } + 0 . 4 \ \mathsf { M }$ mannitol agar plates for bombardment. Eight leaf discs on each agar plate were bombarded with the DNA-coated micro-carriers, using a pressure of 9.3 MPa (1350 psi). Luciferase activity was measured via the Dual Luciferase- Reporter Assays (Promega) as described (Schmidt et al., 2004). 

# Detached leaf assay (DLA)

Potato plants from transgenic lines and non-transgenic control varieties were grown in 5 L pots in the greenhouse for 6–8 weeks. Four leaves from different lines were placed in plastic boxes on moist Grodan mats to assure high humidity. In total, 10 leaves from 10 plants were analysed for each line in one assay in a randomized design. The top 5 leaflets of each potato leaf were locally inoculated with drops of zoospores $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ with 600 zoospores). The first leaflet was inoculated with two drops and the 2nd–5th leaflets with one drop. Boxes were incubated at $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in a day/night rhythm in the greenhouse. After one day, 

lids of the plastic boxes were slightly opened to assure some air circulation in the boxes. Optical scoring of disease symptoms as well as quantification of sporangia was performed at 6 dpi. After optical scoring, the infected leaflets of each leaf were incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in Falcon tubes with 5 mL $H _ { 2 } O$ on a roll mixer to wash off sporangia. The number of sporangia was counted under the microscope in a Thoma chamber. 

For the gene expression experiment and siRNA sequencing, the DLA was performed as described above but with 10 droplets of a P. infestans zoospore suspension on each leaflet. Four leaflets were taken daily for each line from 0 to 6 dpi. 

# Field trials and trials under near-field conditions

Field trials in the USA were conducted at 3 locations in Minnesota. Plants were grown from tubers in a randomized block design. There were 4 plots of each cultivar at a location. Each plot contained 24 plants in 4 rows. Spreader rows of susceptible potatoes were frequently spray inoculated with zoospores of the complex US-isolate #1044 at the end of July and the beginning of August. In addition, a point inoculation was made in the centre of each plot. Optical disease scoring (0–100) was done five times from 20 dpi until 42 dpi. 

Evaluation of resistance in Europe (Germany) was performed by resistance assays with transgenic plants grown under near-field conditions. In March, rooted in-vitro plants were transferred from in-vitro cultures to a conventional greenhouse and grown in $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ pots for 3 weeks. In April, the young potato plants were planted in natural soil in a special foil grid greenhouse. The roof of the 300 square meter foil grid house was composed of mesh wire, which allowed the plants to be exposed to rain, wind and temperature as in a field trial. In the first year, 6 plants from each line were planted per plot, and 3 plots per line were arranged in a randomized design. In the second year, 6 plots were planted for each line. To exclude the release and spread of pollen, the potato flowers were removed manually from each plant during the growing season. 

Eight weeks after planting, two plants from each plot were inoculated, whereas one leaf of the plant was spray inoculated with $7 5 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of a zoospore solution of P. infestans $1 0 ^ { 4 }$ zoospores $\times \mathrm { \ m L } ^ { - 1 } )$ ). The leaf was covered with a plastic bag for 2 days to ensure high humidity at the inoculated leaf to promote P. infestans infection. Late blight disease progress was monitored by optical scoring and photographically documented. Criteria to evaluate the infection process were defined as follows: 0: no infection, 1: marginal/slight infection; infection of 1/2 of the leaflets of the inoculated leaf; 2: infection of more than 1/2 of the leaflets of the inoculated leaf; 3: infection of all leaflets of the inoculated leaf; 4: infection spreads out to other leaves of the inoculated plant; 6: infection spreads out to other plants of the lot; 8: infection strongly spreads out to other plants of the lot; 10: $10 \%$ of the plant is infected / destroyed; 20: $20 \%$ of the plant is infected / destroyed; 100: $100 \%$ of the plant is infected / destroyed. Scores 0–3 only evaluate the infection of the inoculated leaf, whereas scores 4–100 evaluate the infection progress of the whole plant and the entire plot. 

Statistical analysis of data was done by ANOVA (Analysis of variance between groups) and pair-wise t-test.
