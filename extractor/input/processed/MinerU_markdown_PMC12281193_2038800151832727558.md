# ABSTRACT

Sugar-mediated regulation of hormone signaling is crucial for optimizing growth under normal conditions and ensuring survival during environmental stress. Previous studies have shown that sugar starvation induces the degradation of BRASSINAZOLE RESISTANT 1 (BZR1), the master transcription factor of the brassinosteroid (BR) signaling pathway, thereby inhibiting growth. However, the molecular mechanism linking sugar signaling to BZR1 degradation remains unknown. To identify proteins that mediate starvationinduced BZR1 degradation, we performed a quantitative proteomic analysis of the BZR1 interactome under starvation conditions and identified UBIQUITIN PROTEIN LIGASE 3 (UPL3) as a sugar-regulated protein that promotes BZR1 degradation and regulates growth and survival in response to sugar availability. upl3 mutants showed increased BZR1 accumulation and larger seedling size compared to the wild type under sugar-limiting conditions, but not when grown on sugar-containing medium, which indicates that UPL3 mediates BZR1 degradation and growth inhibition under sugar-limited conditions. Although upl3 mutations promoted growth under short-term starvation, they substantially reduced survival under long-term starvation. The enhanced growth phenotype of upl3 was also observed when target of rapamycin (TOR) was inactivated, but not when BR biosynthesis was blocked, suggesting that UPL3 acts downstream of sugar-TOR signaling to regulate BZR1 degradation. Furthermore, UPL3 protein levels increased posttranscriptionally in response to starvation and TOR inhibition, and decreased upon sugar treatment. Our study identifies UPL3 as a key molecular link between sugar signaling and BR signaling. We propose that sugar-TOR signaling inhibits UPL3 to promote BZR1 accumulation and growth, thereby optimizing plant growth and survival in response to sugar availability.

# INTRODUCTION

Plant growth is driven by photosynthetically derived sugar and is regulated by growth hormones. Sugar is both the structural precursor that supports growth and the energy source for all cellular functions, and its production (by photosynthesis) and consumption (by growth) must be balanced to optimize growth and survival. As a result, sugar-mediated regulation of growth hormone pathways is crucial to maintain cellular homeostasis, maximize 

growth under favorable conditions, and ensure survival under stress conditions. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying sugar–hormone crosstalk are not fully understood. 

Brassinosteroids (BRs) are major growth-promoting hormones (Kim and Wang, 2010). BRs are perceived by the plasma membrane-localized receptor kinase BRASSINOSTEROID-INSENSITIVE 1 (BRI1) (Li and Chory, 1997; Wang et al., 2001), which initiates a signaling cascade that activates the BZR1 

family of transcription factors, including BZR1 and BZR2 (also known as BRI1-EMS-SUPPRESSOR 1 [BES1]) (Wang et al., 2002). Under low BR levels, BZR1 and BZR2 are phosphorylated by the GSK3-like kinase BRASSINOSTEROID-INSENSITIVE 2 (He et al., 2002; Yin et al., 2002), which results in their exclusion from the nucleus and loss of DNA-binding activity (Vert and Chory, 2006; Gampala et al., 2007). BR signaling inactivates BRASSINOSTEROID-INSENSITIVE2 (Kim et al., 2009), which leads to BZR1 and BZR2/BES1 dephosphorylation by PROTEIN PHOSPHATASE 2A (Tang et al., 2011) and their accumulation in the nucleus (He et al., 2005; Yin et al., 2005). BZR1 binds to thousands of target genes (Sun et al., 2010); it directly activates gene expression by recruiting the BRAHMA-associated SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex, and represses genes by recruiting the TOPLESS general repressor and histone deacetylases (Oh et al., 2014a; Zhu et al., 2024). BR signaling controls BZR1 patterning in the shoot meristem to determine the shoot architecture, and maintains a BZR1 gradient along the developmental zones of the root meristem to control root growth rate (Gendron et al., 2012; Chaiwanon and Wang, 2015). A recent study showed that extensive genetic and epigenetic variation in ZmBZR1 binding influences major agronomic traits in maize (Hartwig et al., 2023). 

In addition to BR, a wide range of endogenous and environmental signals act through the BZR1 family of transcription factors. For example, BZR1 is essential for skotomorphogenesis and cell elongation in response to auxin, gibberellin, and shade or darkness through interactions with AUXIN RESPONSE FACTOR 6, the gibberellin-signaling component DELLA proteins, and PHYTOCHROME-INTERACTING FACTOR 4 (PIF4) (Bai et al., 2012; Oh et al., 2012, 2014b). BZR1 regulates apical hook development and cotyledon greening through interactions with SMALL AUXIN UP RNA17, PHYTOCHROME-INTERACTING FACTORS, ETHYLENE-INSENSITIVE 3 (EIN3), EIN3-LIKE 1, and GROWTH-REGULATING FACTOR 7 (Li and He, 2016; Wang et al., 2020, 2023; Zhao et al., 2021). BZR1 and BZR2/BES1 mediate hypocotyl elongation in response to UV and blue light by interacting with the photoreceptors UV RESISTANCE 8 and CRYPTOCHROME 1 (Liang et al., 2018; He et al., 2019). BZR1 and BZR2/BES1 also modulate biotic and abiotic stress responses through interactions with stressresponsive factors (Lozano-Duran et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2017; Ye et al., 2017; Qi et al., 2021). BZR1 is regulated by redox-dependent oxidation in response to hydrogen peroxide signaling, which enables it to promote stomatal opening (Tian et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020). Recent studies have shown that BZR1 acts downstream of the EPIDERMAL PATTERNING FACTOR-like (EPFL) peptide and ERECTA receptor kinase in regulating megaspore mother cell specification (Cai et al., 2023) and is inactivated during mitosis (Vukasinovic et al., 2025). 

Degradation of BZR1 and BZR2/BES1 is a major mechanism of growth regulation in response to stress and sugar availability. Sugar signaling regulates BZR1 in a light-dependent manner, promoting BZR1 accumulation in the dark but increasing its phosphorylation in the light (Zhang et al., 2016, 2021). Sugarregulated BZR1 degradation is mediated by TARGET OF RAPAMYCIN (TOR) and an autophagy-dependent mechanism (Zhang et al., 2016). Several proteins that mediate the ubiquitination and degradation of BZR1 and its homolog BZR2/ 

BES1 have been identified. These include SEVEN IN ABSENTIA OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA (SINAT) proteins, which mediate light-regulated BZR2/BES1 degradation; PLANT U-BOX40 (PUB40), which mediates BZR1 degradation in roots; and DOMINANT SUPPRESSOR OF KAR2 and BES1-ASSOCIATED F-BOX1 (BAF1), which are involved in BZR2/BES1 degradation through a selective autophagy pathway (Nolan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021). However, the protein that interacts with BZR1 and mediates starvation-induced BZR1 degradation and growth arrest remains unidentified. 

UBIQUITIN-PROTEIN LIGASE 3 (UPL3) and its homolog UPL4 play important roles in the ubiquitination and degradation of many hormone-specific transcriptional activators (Wang et al., 2022). UPL3 is a member of the HECT (homologous to E6-AP carboxyl terminus) family of proteasome-associated ubiquitin E3 ligases (Rotin and Kumar, 2009). The upl3 mutant, which is also known as the kaktus mutant, exhibits a wide range of phenotypes, including hyperbranched trichomes with increased endoreduplication (Downes et al., 2003; El Refy et al., 2003), hypersensitivity to gibberellin (Downes et al., 2003), increased seed size (Miller et al., 2019), and defects in salicylic acid (SA)- induced gene expression and pathogen defense (Furniss et al., 2018). UPL3 mediates the ubiquitination and degradation of key transcriptional regulators associated with these processes (Patra et al., 2013; Xue et al., 2023). Intriguingly, a recent study showed that UPL3/4 receives target proteins from pathwayspecific ubiquitin ligases and mediates the proteasomal degradation of the transcription factors NPR1 and EIN3, which function in the SA and ethylene pathways, respectively (Wang et al., 2022). However, the upstream signal that regulates UPL3 is still unknown, as is the extent of its downstream targets. 

In the present study, we performed immunoprecipitation and quantitative mass spectrometry analyses of the BZR1 interactome using seedlings grown under sugar starvation. This analysis identified UPL3 as a BZR1 interactor. Further genetic and biochemical experiments provided evidence of the essential role of UPL3 in BZR1 degradation, growth arrest, and seedling survival under sugar starvation. These results demonstrate that UPL3 mediates sugar-dependent regulation of BZR1 degradation and plays an important role in optimizing growth and survival in response to sugar availability. This key molecular link between sugar signaling and a BR-responsive transcription factor is crucial for plant growth and resilience in changing environments.

# UPL3 acts downstream of TOR to promote starvationinduced suppression of BR signaling and plant growth

To test whether UPL3 plays a role in sugar-dependent regulation of BR signaling, we grew upl3 seedlings on sugarcontaining or sugar-free medium in the light for 4 days and then shifted them to the dark for 3 days. The growth of wildtype seedlings on sugar-free medium was attenuated by the depletion of photosynthate and the starvation-induced degradation of BZR1 (Zhang et al., 2016). The upl3 mutants exhibited morphology similar to the wild type on sugar-containing medium but developed longer hypocotyls on sugar-free medium (Figure 3A and 3B), which suggests that UPL3 is required for growth arrest under starvation conditions. In contrast, the baf1 mutant showed shorter hypocotyls than the wild type under sugar starvation (Figure 3A and 3B). When grown on medium containing 1 μM PPZ, the upl3 and baf1 mutants developed short hypocotyls similar to the wild type (Figure 3C and 3D), 

which suggests that their hypocotyl phenotypes are BRdependent. 

To test whether UPL3 acts downstream of TOR, we analyzed the effect of TOR inhibition on upl3 phenotypes. Similar to starvation (Figure 3A and 3B), TOR inhibition using AZD8055 arrested growth in the wild type; however, it was less effective in upl3-26 and slightly more effective in baf1 (Figure 3E and 3F). Similarly, inducible RNAi-mediated silencing of TOR (using tor-es) (Xiong and Sheen, 2012) inhibited seedling growth, but this effect was largely suppressed in the upl3 mutant background (Figure 3G and 3H). These results support the conclusion that UPL3 mediates growth inhibition induced by starvation and TOR inactivation.

# DISCUSSION

In plants, sugar is produced by photosynthesis and primarily consumed by growth-related processes such as cell wall synthesis. It is an essential energy source for all cellular functions and for viability. Balancing sugar production and consumption is crucial for maintaining optimal growth under normal conditions and ensuring survival under stress conditions, when photosynthesis is reduced. As a result, the coupling between sugar-sensing and hormone signaling pathways is necessary to maintain energy homeostasis and to optimize growth and survival under changing environmental conditions. When photosynthate 

stresses, BR signaling is attenuated to reduce growth and conserve sugar for survival. Therefore, understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying sugar-BR crosstalk is important for improving plant productivity and stress resilience. 

Previous studies by our group and others have shown that sugar starvation and TOR inactivation promote the degradation of BZR1 and its homolog BZR2/BES1 (Zhang et al., 2016; Nolan et al., 2017). In the present study, we used a proteomics approach to identify BZR1-interacting proteins under starvation conditions. Genetic and biochemical experiments demonstrated that UPL3 interacts with BZR1 to promote its degradation and inhibit growth under sugar-limited conditions, thereby improving survival under extreme sugar starvation. 

Identification of in vivo interacting proteins that mediate the degradation of a target protein is challenging because the interaction promotes degradation and reduces the levels of the bait protein. Furthermore, the protein that specifically mediates starvationinduced BZR1 degradation likely only interacts with BZR1 under sugar-limited conditions. Therefore, we grew BZR1- overexpressing transgenic plants under starvation conditions and used SIL-IP-MS to quantitatively distinguish BZR1-binding proteins from non-specific background proteins. This approach identified 28 known BZR1 interactors and their family members, along with 41 putative novel BZR1 interactors that showed enrichment comparable to that of the known interactors. A series of experiments confirmed the role of UPL3 in starvation-induced BZR1 degradation. First, the UPL3–BZR1 interaction was confirmed by coIP and bimolecular 

fluorescence complementation assays. Second, loss-of-function upl3 mutants exhibited increased BZR1 protein levels when grown without supplemental sugar but normal levels when grown on a sugar-containing medium (Figure 4), which indicates a specific role for UPL3 in starvation-induced BZR1 degradation. Third, starvation and TOR inhibition increased UPL3 protein levels, and UPL3 overexpression reduced BZR1 accumulation (Figures 1 and 4). 

Consistent with the accumulation of BZR1, upl3 mutants display a range of phenotypes that resemble those of plants overexpressing BRI1 under sugar-limited conditions. These phenotypes include elongated petioles and narrow leaves, observed when grown under low light in soil or when grown on sugar-free medium followed by transfer to darkness (Figures 2 and 3), and reduced survival after prolonged darkness (extreme starvation) (Figure 5). However, upl3 mutants showed no obvious hypocotyl phenotypes when grown on medium containing sugar. Similarly, upl3 plants grown in a greenhouse under strong spring sunlight showed much weaker petiole phenotypes than those grown under the lower light intensity of growth chambers (Supplemental Figure 9 compared to Figure 2B), consistent with reduced sugar limitation under stronger light. Whether upl3 mutants display no discernible phenotype under stronger summer sunlight, as on sugarcontaining medium, remains to be tested. Together, these results demonstrate that UPL3 levels decrease when sugar levels are high, which increases BZR1 accumulation and promotes growth. Conversely, UPL3 levels increase when sugar is limited, promoting BZR1 degradation and thereby inhibiting growth and reducing sugar consumption. Therefore, UPL3 couples sugar sensing with hormone signaling to balance supply and demand in plant growth. Because both UPL3 and BZR1 are ubiquitously expressed across all plant organs (Supplemental Figure 10), this sugar–BR crosstalk mechanism is likely important in all plant tissues and organs. UPL3 may serve as a target for engineering crops with improved productivity and resilience to changing environments. 

UPL3 is a member of the HECT family of ubiquitin E3 ligases, which are proteasome-associated E3 ligases. HECT E3s accept ubiquitin from an E2 enzyme and covalently link it to a conserved Cys residue in their HECT domain before transferring ubiquitin to the substrate protein (Rotin and Kumar, 2009). The Arabidopsis genome encodes seven HECT E3 ligases grouped into four subfamilies: UPL1/2, UPL3/4, UPL5, and UPL6/ 7 (Downes et al., 2003). The UPL3/4 subfamily is composed of approximately 200-kDa proteins with a C-terminal HECT domain and N-terminal Armadillo repeats. 

Genetic studies have demonstrated broad functions of UPL3 in the regulation of plant development, hormone responses, stress responses, and immunity (Downes et al., 2003; El Refy et al., 2003; Furniss et al., 2018; Miller et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022). Several UPL3 target proteins have been identified: the transcription factors GLABROUS 3 and ENHANCER OF GLABROUS 3 and the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor KRP2, all involved in trichome development (Patra et al., 2013; Xue et al., 2023); the LEC2 transcription factor in the regulation of seed development (Miller et al., 2019); NPR1 in SA signaling and immunity (Wang et al., 2022); and the ethylene response factor EIN3 (Wang et al., 2022). Furthermore, a recent proteomics study suggested that UPL3 is involved in the ubiquitination and degradation of proteins associated with carbohydrate metabolism; the upl3 mutant accumulates lower levels of sugar and starch (Lan et al., 2022). Our finding that UPL3 accumulation is regulated by sugar-TOR signaling suggests that UPL3 may mediate the sugar-dependent regulation of these additional pathways and play a broader role in cellular and developmental responses to sugar availability. 

This function of HECT-domain ubiquitin ligases such as UPL3 is likely conserved, as they also have important roles in autophagy regulation in animals (Melino et al., 2019). 

UPL3, as a proteasome-associated ubiquitin ligase, acts downstream of pathway-specific E3 ubiquitin ligases to regulate the activity and degradation of the hormone-responsive transcription factors NPR1 and EIN3 (Wang et al., 2022). For example, NPR1 ubiquitination appears to be a sequential process. The initial ubiquitination by a Cullin-RING E3 ligase promotes its chromatin association and transcriptional activity. Subsequently, polyubiquitination by the E4 ligase UBE4 inhibits NPR1 transcriptional activity. The transfer of NPR1 from UBE4 to UPL3 leads to further polyubiquitination and degradation of inactive NPR1. Similarly, there is evidence that UPL3 and its homolog UPL4 mediate EIN3 polyubiquitination and degradation following its initial ubiquitination by the F-box E3 ligase EBF1/2 (Wang et al., 2022). This transfer from a pathway-specific ubiquitin ligase to a proteasome-associated UPL may represent a general mechanism for the processive degradation of UPL targets (Wang et al., 2022). Supporting this model, a recent proteomics study showed an overall increase in ubiquitinated proteins in the upl3 mutant (Lan et al., 2022). Consistent with these findings, we observed increased levels of ubiquitinated BZR1 in upl3 (Figure 4F), which suggests that UPL3 promotes the degradation of BZR1 proteins ubiquitinated by other E3 ligases. As a starvation-activated protein that promotes the degradation of multiple hormone-responsive transcription factors, UPL3 is a potential target for improving plant productivity and resilience to changing climatic conditions. 

BZR1 and its close homolog BZR2/BES1 are targeted by several ubiquitin E3 ligases, including PUB40 (Kim et al., 2019), SINAT (Yang et al., 2017), and BAF1 (Wang et al., 2021). Our BZR1 IP-MS analysis under darkness-induced starvation detected BAF1 but not SINAT or PUB40, probably because BZR1 interacts with SINAT and PUB40 in a light-dependent or tissue-specific manner (Yang et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2019). BAF1 was recently reported to function as an E3 ubiquitin ligase that interacts with BZR2/BES1 and promotes its selective autophagy (Wang et al., 2021). Overexpression of BAF1 promoted selective autophagy of BES1/BZR2, reduced BES1/BZR1 accumulation and BRresponsive growth, and promoted survival under starvation (Wang et al., 2021). However, the baf1 mutant showed no significant growth phenotype under normal conditions in soil or on medium, and showed only subtle long-hypocotyl phenotypes when grown on medium containing PPZ or BR (Wang et al., 2021). Although baf1 showed reduced survival after long-term starvation (Wang et al., 2021), this phenotype was much weaker than that of upl3 (Figure 5). The previous study (Wang et al., 2021) did not assess the growth of baf1 under starvation conditions; we unexpectedly found that the baf1 mutant exhibits a short-hypocotyl phenotype under starvation and TOR suppression (Figure 3A–3F). Consistent with their opposing growth phenotypes, baf1 primarily increased the levels of phosphorylated BZR1, whereas upl3 increased the dephosphorylated (active) form (Supplemental Figure 5E). The hypocotyl length of the upl3/baf1 double mutant was intermediate between the baf1 and upl3 single mutants and similar to the wild type (Supplemental Figure 11). These additive effects suggest an intriguing and complex relationship 

between BAF1 and UPL3 in regulating BZR1/2. BAF1 and UPL3 may differentially affect the phosphorylated and unphosphorylated forms of BZR1/2 or alternatively affect the pool of ubiquitinated BZR1, which is increased by both BAF1 overexpression (Wang et al., 2021) and upl3 mutation (Figure 4F). One possibility is that BAF1 causes monoubiquitination or short-chain ubiquitination of BZR1 and BZR2, which increases their transcriptional activity but also primes them for UPL3-mediated polyubiquitination and degradation, analogous to the regulation of NPR1 and EIN3 (Wang et al., 2022). Future studies are required to test the hypothesis that UPL3 acts downstream of BAF1 and other E3 ligases in a ubiquitin ligase relay that dynamically modulates BZR1/2 activity and degradation. 

BZR1 and BZR2/BES1 are also regulated by the deubiquitinating enzymes UBP12 and UBP13 (Wang et al., 2013; Park et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2022), which have been proposed to mediate recovery from starvation stress (Xiong et al., 2022). Intriguingly, UPL3 interacts with UBP12 (Lan et al., 2022). Future studies are required to determine how the activities of E3 ligases, E4 ligases, and deubiquitinating enzymes are orchestrated by various signaling pathways to dynamically regulate BZR1/BZR2 levels and optimize growth and survival under both normal and stress conditions. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The upl3-26 T-DNA insertion mutant (SALK_116326C) was obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center. The baf1 mutant (GABI_001F08), which carries the same allele as reported previously (Wang et al., 2021), was obtained from the German Plant Genomics Program - Koelner Arabidopsis T-DNA lines (GABI-Kat). The upl3-4 and 35S::YFP-UPL3/upl3 seeds (Furniss et al., 2018) were kindly provided by Dr. Steven H. Spoel. Genotyping was performed using the primers listed in Supplemental Table 1 and the Phire Tissue Direct PCR Master Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific, F170L). The BR-related mutants bzr1-1D, det2 (Wang et al., 2002), BZR1-CFP/Col-0 (He et al., 2005), BZR1-YFP/ Col-0 (Gendron et al., 2012), and BRI1-GFP (Wang et al., 2001) have been previously described. All plants were in the Col-0 ecotype background. All plants were grown in a growth room under a 16 h light/ 8 h dark photoperiod at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathtt { - } 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ or in a greenhouse with natural sunlight for general growth, phenotype observation, and seed harvesting. 

# Phenotypic analysis of seedlings

For phenotypic analysis of hypocotyls, seeds were sterilized with $7 5 \%$ (v/v) ethanol and sown on solid agar plates $( 0 . 8 \%$ Phytoblend) containing half-strength Murashige and Skoog medium $( \mathsf { p H 5 . 7 } )$ supplemented with sugar or hormone inhibitors as specified. After 3 days of incubation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , seeds were transferred to a growth chamber at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ under constant light or darkness. For starvation treatment, plants were grown in the light for 4 days and then transferred to the dark for varying durations. Photographs were taken, and hypocotyl lengths were measured using ImageJ software. For starvation survival assays, seedlings were grown on sugar-free medium in constant light for 4 days, transferred to darkness for 9–12 days, and then returned to light for 7 days. Photographs were taken, and survival rates were calculated. Seedlings that remained green were considered alive. At least 45 seedlings were included in each of the 3 replicates. 

# Vector construction

The full-length coding sequences of UPL3 or BZR1, excluding the stop codons, were cloned into the pENTR/SD/D-TOPO plasmid. The UPL3 TOPO 

or BZR1 TOPO entry clone was then inserted into the modified 35S:: gcpCAMBIA1390-4myc-6His vector or the pEarleyGate 101 vector to generate the 35S::UPL3-myc and 35S::BZR1-YFP constructs. All primers used in this study are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Tobacco infiltration and coIP

Agrobacterium (strain GV3101) cells harboring the constructs BZR1:: BZR1-YFP, 35S::YFP, or 35S::UPL3-Myc were resuspended in infiltration medium (10 mM 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid [pH 5.6], $1 0 \ m M$ ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 0 1 \%$ Tween 20, and $1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ acetosyringone), mixed as indicated, and infiltrated into leaves of N. benthamiana. Tissues were harvested $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ later for coIP and immunoblot analysis. 

After grinding in liquid nitrogen, tissues were resuspended in IP extraction buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl [pH 7.5], 50 mM NaCl, $10 \%$ glycerol, $0 . 2 5 \%$ Triton X-100, $0 . 2 5 \%$ NP-40, 1 mM PMSF, Pierce protease inhibitor cocktail [Thermo Fisher Scientific], and PhosStop cocktail [Roche]) and sonicated for 20 s (1 s on/off). After centrifugation at 14 000 rpm for 15 min, the supernatant was incubated with anti-GFP Magarose beads (Smart-Lifesciences, SM038005) for 1 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The beads were washed three times with 1 ml of IP buffer without detergent. Proteins were eluted with $5 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ SDS sample buffer and separated by SDS-PAGE. 

# Chemical inhibitor treatments

Seedlings were grown in liquid half-strength Murashige and Skoog medium supplemented with $3 0 \ m M$ glucose for 5 days, then treated with $5 \mu \mathsf { M }$ AZD8055 (Selleck, S1555) or DMSO (mock) for the indicated durations. Tissue was harvested for immunoblotting. 

To observe inflorescences, 5-day-old YFP-UPL3/upl3-4 seedlings grown in half-strength Murashige and Skoog medium with 15 mM glucose were treated with $1 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ AZD8055 for 4 h. 

# Confocal microscopy and fluorescence quantification

Laser-scanning confocal microscopy was performed using a Leica SP8 or Stellaris 8 Falcon system. In each experiment, at least seven roots and hypocotyls were analyzed per treatment. Cortex cells in the root meristem zone and in the hypocotyls were imaged and quantified using ImageJ. Identical laser settings were used within each experiment. 

# Protein extraction and immunoblot analysis

For protein extraction, plants were frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground, weighed, and mixed with $_ { 2 \times }$ SDS buffer (0.125 mM Tris-HCl [pH 6.8], $4 \%$ SDS, $20 \%$ glycerol, and $2 \%$ β-mercaptoethanol). Samples were heated at $\mathfrak { s } \mathfrak { s } ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 10 min, centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 g$ for 10 min, separated on a $7 . 5 \%$ acrylamide gel, then blotted onto polyvinylidene fluoride membranes (Millipore, IPVH0010) using 192 mM glycine and 25 mM Tris-HCl. Membranes were blocked for 1 h at room temperature in blotting buffer (140 mM NaCl, 10 mM KCl, 8 mM ${ \mathsf { N a } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { H P O } } _ { 4 }$ , 2 mM ${ \sf K H } _ { 2 } { \sf P O } _ { 4 }$ , $0 . 5 \%$ skim milk, and $0 . 1 \%$ Tween 20 [pH 7.4]). The blots were incubated with the primary antibodies (anti-GFP, TransGen Biotech, HT801, at 1:1000 dilution; anti-BZR1, custom-made, at $1 \mu \ g / \mathsf { m l }$ ; anti-actin, Sigma, A2228, at 1:5000 dilution; and anti-tubulin, Abmart, M20023, at 1:5000 dilution) for 1 h at room temperature or overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Secondary antibodies were used at 1:5000 dilution for 1 h.
