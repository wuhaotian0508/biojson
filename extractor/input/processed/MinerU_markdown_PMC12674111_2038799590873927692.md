# S T R U C T U R A L B I O LO G Y

# Molecular basis of very-long-chain fatty acid elongation by the CER6-GL2 enzyme complex in plant wax biosynthesis

Yaqi Liu1,2 †, Yuan Chen1,2,3 †, Xue Zhang1,2 , Mengxue Li1,2,3 , Ji Wang1,4 , Zhao Yang1,2 , Miaolian Ma1,2 , Zhiwei Zhao2,5 , Hongtao Liu2,5 , Fang Yu4 , Peng Zhang1,2 * 

Plant cuticular waxes, crucial hydrophobic barriers, are primarily composed of aliphatics derived from very-longchain fatty acids (VLCFAs; $> \mathsf { C } 2 8$ ) synthesized by the endoplasmic reticulum fatty acid elongase complex. The core catalytic subunit, CER6 (KCS6), requires interaction with the BAHD protein GL2 to elongate acyl chains beyond C28. We determined the cryo–electron microscopy structure of the maize CER6-GL2 (ZmCER6-ZmGL2) heterotetramer bound to coenzyme A (CoA) and malonyl-CoA, revealing a membrane-anchored ZmCER6 homodimer, with each cytosolic catalytic domain having a substrate tunnel. Structural and biochemical analyses suggest that ZmGL2’s amino terminus binds ZmCER6 and remodels its substrate tunnel into a continuous hydrophobic channel at their interface, enabling acyl-chain elongation. CER6 uses a distinct Cys-His-Asn catalytic triad, differing from the histidine-dependent catalysis of mammalian elongases. GL2 acts noncatalytically to modulate CER6 activity. Comparative analyses suggest that species-specific substrate preferences arise from divergent CER2/GL2 interactions. This work elucidates the acyl-chain elongation mechanism of plant VLCFA biosynthesis and provides a foundation for engineering stress-resilient crops via wax modulation.

# INTRODUCTION

Plant cuticular waxes form an essential hydrophobic barrier on primary aerial organs, serving as a critical interface between plants and outer environment. This protective layer prevents nonstomatal water loss, restricts pathogen invasion, maintains organ boundary integrity, and modulates developmental processes (1–5). Cuticular wax is mainly constituted of very-long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs; C28-C34) and their derivatives (aldehydes, alkanes, ketones, and wax esters) (6), which has been extensively investigated through genetic approaches. Great progresses include the identification of cer (eceriferum) mutants in Arabidopsis (7–11), gl (glossy) mutants in maize (Zea mays) (12–16), and wsl (wax crystal-sparse leaf) mutants in rice (Oryza sativa) (17, 18), which collectively established endoplasmic reticulum (ER)–localized fatty acid elongase (FAE) complex as the core machinery for VLCFA production. 

VLCFA biosynthesis initiates in plastids via fatty acid synthase–mediated production of C16/C18 acyl precursors from acetyl–coenzyme A (CoA) (19–22), followed by ER-localized elongation executed by the conserved multienzyme FAE complex comprising four enzymes: $\beta .$ - ketoacyl-CoA synthase (KCS), $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA reductase (KCR), $\beta .$ - hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydratase (HCD), and enoyl-CoA reductase (ECR) (23–27). While KCR, HCD, and ECR exhibit broad substrate promiscuity, KCS enzymes serve as the rate-limiting step of elongation process by catalyzing the condensation reaction of specific VLCFA substrates (28,  29). Notably, two distinct elongation systems exist in eukaryotes: the plant-specific KCS family members and the ELO-type condensing enzymes in mammals, yeast, and plants, including 

Arabidopsis and bryophytes (30–37), suggesting distinct mechanistic strategies for acyl chain elongation across eukaryotes. In Arabidopsis, the CER6 (or $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA synthase KCS6) enzyme, differing from PKSs by featuring an N-terminal transmembrane domain (29, 38–40), predominantly controls wax biosynthesis, with cer6 mutants showing severe wax deficiency in stems and pollen coats (7, 8, 41, 42). However, biochemical studies revealed CER6 alone cannot produce the C30-C34 fatty acids that are predominant in plant cuticular waxes (43). This puzzle was resolved with the discovery of CER2-family proteins (e.g., AtCER2 and ZmGL2), which belong to the BAHD (named for the first four enzymes described in this clade: benzylalcohol O-acetyltransferase; anthocyanin O-hydroxycinnamoyltransferase; N-hydroxycinnamoyl/ benzoyltransferase; and deacetylvindoline 4-O-acetyltransferase) acyltransferase superfamily (9, 11–13, 44, 45). These catalytically inactive regulators enable elongation beyond C28 via unconventional interactions with KCS family members, establishing a previously unrecognized mechanism for VLCFA biosynthesis (46–50). 

However, several remaining questions are awaited to answer. How do membrane-embedded KCS enzymes coordinate with soluble CER2/GL2 partners? More critically, how does the activity of KCS6/ CER6 is modulated by CER2/GL2 proteins lacking the canonical BAHD acyltransferase function (3, 15, 18, 43). Here, we characterized the CER6-GL2/CER2 protein complexes and solved the highresolution cryo–electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure of maize CER6-GL2 bound with CoA or malonyl-CoA. Combining with in vitro enzymatic assay experiment, we elucidated its catalytic activity and acyl-chain elongation mechanism in VLCFA biosynthesis. 

# RESULTS

# GL2 forms a complex with CER6 to confer condensation activity

Previous studies have established that GL2/CER2 homologs function as essential modulators of CER6-mediated VLCFA elongation (15, 18, 43, 48, 50). Through homology searching using Arabidopsis 

CER6 (AtCER6; UniProt, Q9XF43) as a bait, we identified CER6 (UniProt, B4G0N2) (ZmCER6) as the putative catalytic partner of GL2 (ZmGL2; UniProt, A0A804MAL4) in maize, which consistent with the previously identified gene ZmKCS6 (51, 52). Structural modeling revealed ZmCER6’s conserved architecture: two N-terminal transmembrane helices followed by a C-terminal catalytic module housing FAE or $\beta$ -ketoacyl-ACP synthase III (KASIII) domain (fig. S1) (53– 56). Coexpression in insect cells generated functional ZmCER6- ZmGL2 complex (fig.  S2A), which exhibited complex-dependent 

elongation activity in liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS)–based in vitro enzymatic assay. The recombinant ZmCER6- ZmGL2 protein complex catalyzes sequential condensation of 30:0- CoA with malonyl-CoA to produce 32:0-ketoacyl-CoA, while neither subunit alone showed activity (Fig. 1, A and B). Further enzymatic assay analysis revealed a biphasic catalytic capacity; while ZmCER6- ZmGL2 protein complex preferentially elongated 30:0-CoA, it also converted 28:0-CoA to 30:0-ketoacyl-CoA and 26:0-CoA to 28:0-ketoacyl-CoA (Fig. 1, C and D).

Comparative analysis with Arabidopsis AtCER6-AtCER2 (fig. S2B) revealed species-divergent substrate preferences: Although both complexes converted 28:0-CoA to 30:0-ketoacyl-CoA, ZmCER6- ZmGL2 uniquely exhibited 30:0-CoA elongation activity (Fig. 1, B, C, and E, and fig. S2C). These findings establish CER2/GL2 homologs as evolutionary switches that reprogram CER6 substrate specificity to generate organism specific VLCFA profiles. 

# Structure determination and architecture of ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex

To delineate the molecular mechanism of ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex, we pursued cryo-EM structural determination under distinct substrate-bound states: malonyl-CoA (MLC), 28:0/30:0 acyl-CoA, and CoA (reaction product). Comparative analyses included both wild type (ZmCER6-ZmGL2) and its catalytically inactive mutant (ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2). Despite systematic optimization of sample preparation and iterative data processing (figs. S3 and S4), no continuous density corresponding to the 28:0/30:0 acyl chain was resolved in either wild-type or mutant complexes incubated with 28:0/30:0 acyl-CoA substrates. Only fragmented electron density peaks were observed for the CoA moiety in these conditions (fig. S5, A and B). 

By contrast, well-defined density maps for CoA and malonyl-CoA were unambiguously resolved in two complexes (fig. S5, C and D): ZmCER6-ZmGL2 bound to CoA (ZmCER6-ZmGL2-CoA) and ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2 bound to malonyl-CoA (ZmCER6C222A-

ZmGL2-MLC) (Fig. 2, A to D, and fig. S6, A and B). The final atomic models encompass residues 68 to 494 of ZmCER6 and 14 to 424 of ZmGL2, with refinement statistics detailed in table S1. Structural superposition revealed near-identical conformations between the ZmCER6-ZmGL2-CoA and ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2-MLC structures (root mean square deviation of $0 . 4 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ for 1378 aligned Cα residues), indicating minimal conformational rearrangements upon substrate binding. Consequently, the ZmCER6-ZmGL2-CoA structure was selected as the representative model for structure description. 

Structural analysis revealed that two ZmCER6 protomers form a face-to-face homodimer, with each monomer contributing a membrane-embedded transmembrane helix that anchors the complex within the lipid bilayer, followed by a C-terminal catalytic domain (Fig.  2, A to D). Each ZmCER6 protomer independently engages one ZmGL2 molecule through extensive interfacial contacts at the cytosolic side, while the two ZmGL2 molecules fall apart in divergent orientations. This arrangement results in an inverted V-shaped architecture (dimer of dimer), establishing a 2:2 stoichiometry for the ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex. Notably, each ZmCER6 protomer harbors a catalytic tunnel spatially organized to bind a CoA molecule or malonyl-CoA, respectively (Fig.  2 and fig.  S6). This tunnel’s architecture aligns with its proposed role in acyl-CoA substrate recruitment during fatty acid elongation, a critical step in cuticular wax synthesis.

# Interactions between ZmCER6 and ZmGL2

Given the essential role of ZmCER6-ZmGL2 heterodimer formation in catalytic activity (Fig.  1B), we systematically investigated their structural interplay. Structural analysis revealed an extensive 

interaction interface (buried surface area of $8 1 6 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 \cdot }$ ), mediated primarily by the α1 helix of $\mathrm { Z m C E R 6 }$ and the α2 helix, $\beta 1 / \beta 6$ sheets, and $\beta 5 \AA - \beta 6$ connecting loop of ZmGL2 (Fig. 3, A and B). Notably, key residues $\mathrm { A r g } ^ { 1 0 2 }$ , $\mathrm { { T h r } ^ { \tilde { 1 } 0 7 } }$ , $\mathrm { \dot { G } l u } ^ { 1 1 0 }$ , His111, and $\mathrm { T y r } ^ { 1 4 6 }$ from ZmCER6 form a network of salt bridges and hydrogen bonds with $\mathrm { T h r } ^ { 2 3 }$ , Asp104, $\mathrm { C y s } ^ { 1 0 5 }$ s105, Ar g108, and $\mathrm { G l u } ^ { 1 1 1 }$ from $\mathrm { Z m G L } 2$ . Functional validation via pull-down assays demonstrated that alanine substitution of $\operatorname { H i s } ^ { 1 1 1 }$ in ZmCER6 markedly reduced heterodimer stability, while $\mathrm { T y r } ^ { 1 4 6 }$ or $\mathrm { T h r } ^ { 1 0 7 }$ mutations caused complete dissociation (Fig. 3C). Reciprocally, alanine mutations of Asp104, $\mathrm { T h r } ^ { 2 3 }$ , and $\mathrm { A r g } ^ { 1 0 8 }$ in ZmGL2 abolished complex stability, while C105A (Cys105 to Ala) substitution exhibited milder effects (Fig.  3D). These results not only delineate the specificity of ZmCER6 and ZmGL2 interactions but also support prior findings on the role of N-terminal domains in substrate specificity among CER2-like proteins (48).

# CoA/malonyl-CoA–binding site

The substrate-binding site is clearly defined in ZmCER6-ZmGL2- CoA structure (Fig. 4A). Constituted by α2, α5, α6, α10, α12, β7, β8, β9, and the $\beta 1 0 \mathrm { - } \beta 1 1$ connection loop, the CoA-binding site exhibits 

a tunnel shape featured by a positively charged entrance and a hydrophobic interior (Fig. 4, A and B). Computational analysis using DoGSiteScorer online software (66) identified a continuous $^ { \alpha } \mathrm { U }$ -shaped” tunnel with each ZmCER6-ZmGL2 heterodimer (Fig.  5A). The acyl-chain–binding tunnel/channel is formed by helices α1, α2, α6, α8, α10, and β7-β8 from ZmCER6, along with β1, β6, 

and α2 from ZmGL2. # Mechanistic model of CER6-GL2/CER2–mediated VLCFA elongation

We propose that the CER6-GL2/CER2 complex drives iterative twocarbon extensions of VLCFAs through a coordinated catalytic cycle. While CER6 alone functions as the core elongate machinery, its autonomous activity (AtCER6 like) or lack of activity (ZmCER6 like) ends up with incompatibility of its substrate-binding tunnel with acyl-chain substrates longer than C28 (Fig. 6A). The GL2 binding remodels the structure of CER6 through its N-terminal domain, which may reshape and/or expand the substrate-binding tunnel dimension by creating a continuous hydrophobic channel at the CER6-GL2 interface that accommodates elongating chains beyond C28 (Fig. 6B). The elongation process may observe a similar mechanism proposed previously in PKS and KAS (57, 58, 67–70), initiating 

from the recruitment of an acyl-CoA $\scriptstyle ( > \mathbf { C } 2 8 )$ into the CER6 active site. Within the active site, the catalytic cysteine $\mathrm { ( C y s ^ { 2 2 2 } ) }$ executes nucleophilic attack on the thioester bond, and the transacylation takes place to form a covalent acyl-enzyme intermediate while liberating CoA for metabolic recycling. The releasing of CoA from the substrate-binding tunnel is followed by entrance of malonyl-CoA. Malonyl-CoA undergoes decarboxylation to generate a reactive acetyl carbanion, which attacks the carbonyl group of the acyl intermediate, producing $\beta$ -ketoacyl-CoA and extending the chain by two carbons. Therefore, the binding of CER6 with different GL2/CER2 isoforms may generate variable channels at the heterodimer interface to enable substrate-specific elongation.

Notably, plants exhibit evolutionary divergence from mammalian and yeast elongation systems. While mammalian elongation of very-long-chain fatty acid enzymes use a conserved HxxHH motif in their seven-transmembrane domains to extend C18-C26 acyl chains (37), plants use a modular two-phase mechanism: CER6 homologs (KCS-family enzymes) mediate C18-C28 elongation, with subsequent GL2-dependent extension to C34 during wax biosynthesis. Crucially, structural comparisons reveal that CER6 uses a Cys-His-Asn catalytic triad, which is evolutionarily conserved in KCS and KASIII enzymes (38, 39, 54–56, 58, 59). This catalytic specialization explains why the conserved HxxxD motif in GL2 proteins, positioned distal to the active site, primarily serves structural roles in complex stabilization rather than direct chemical catalysis. 

Our structural analysis resolves a key mechanistic paradox: although catalytically inert, GL2-family proteins allosterically remodel CER6’s substrate-binding tunnel through N-terminal interactions (Fig. 5), reshaping and/or expanding the tunnel by creating a continuously hydrophobic channel at the CER6-GL2 interface to accommodate ${ > } \mathrm { C } 2 8$ acyl substrates. This steric complementarity provides an elegant evolutionary solution for chain-length determination, where combinatorial pairing of CER6 with distinct GL2 isoforms may result in variable-lengths of channels at the heterodimer interface that enable substrate-specific elongation, a hypothesis supported by the expansion of GL2-family proteins in vascular plants (15,  36,  47,  48). These also add the complexity of the substrate specificity mechanism among different KCSs, whose substrate diversification also involves the GL2-interaction region (helix α1) (72). Additionally, our work informs potential biotechnological implications through modulating the resolved CER6-GL2 interface and explores isoform-specific modulatory networks governing wax diversity across plant species, offering insights into modifying cuticular wax composition for improved drought tolerance or pest resistance. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Gene cloning, protein expression, and purification

Wild-type ZmCER6 (UniProt, B4G0N2), AtCER6 (UniProt, Q9XF43), ZmGL2 (UniProt, A0A804MAL4), and AtCER2 (Uni-Prot, Q39048) genes were cloned from maize cDNA (B73). The coding region of ZmCER6 was cloned into pFastbac Dual vector under the control of AcMNPV polyhedrin (pH) promoter using restriction site Bam HI, the N-terminal of ZmCER6 fused with six histidine tag; ZmGL2 coding region fused with a Flag tag in the N terminus, which was cloned into Xho I restriction site under the control of AcMNPV p10 promoter. All mutants here were produced by site-directed mutagenesis. 

The ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex was coexpressed in Sf9 insect cells. The Sf9 insect cells were grown in Sf-900 II medium (Thermo Fisher Scientific) at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 100 rpm. We used Bac-to-Bac baculovirus expression system to generate recombinant protein. The recombinant plastid was transformed to DH10Bac Escherichia coli strain. We selected recombinant Bacmid DNA to transfect insect cells using Cellfectin reagent. P1 baculovirus was collected after 72 hours. Then, we amplified P1 viral stock to generate a high-titer P2, P3, and 

P4 baculovirus. When Sf9 cells density reached $2 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathrm { c e l l s / m l } ,$ the P4 baculovirus was used to infect cells for the protein expression. The ratio of baculovirus and insect cells was 1:100. 

The insect cells were harvested after infected 48 to 72 hours. The cell pellets were collected by centrifugation at $5 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . All the protein purification process was conducted at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Cell pellets were resuspended in buffer A $[ 2 0 \ \mathrm { m M }$ Hepes (pH 7.5), 100 mM NaCl, and 1 mM tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine] supplemented with phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, deoxyribonucleases, $\mathrm { M g } ^ { 2 + }$ , and protease inhibitor cocktail (EDTA-free, APExBIO). Insect cells were lysed through high-pressure cell disruptor at 500 bar. The crude cell debris was removed by centrifuged at ${ 2 0 , 0 0 0 g }$ for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ . The supernatant was further centrifuged at $1 5 0 { , } 0 0 0 g$ for 1 hour. Then, the cell membrane was collected and solubilized with $1 \%$ (w/v) $n$ -Dodecyl- $\cdot \beta$ -ᴅ- maltoside (Anatrace) and $0 . 2 \%$ cholesteryl hemisuccinate tris salt for 2 hours. After centrifugation at ${ 2 0 , 0 0 0 g }$ for 1 hour, the supernatant was collected and binding with anti-flag affinity resin (GenScript) for 1 hour. The anti-flag affinity resin was washed with 20–column volume buffer A supplemented with $0 . 0 1 \%$ glyco-diosgenin (GDN). The protein was eluted with Flag peptide $( 0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / m l } )$ ) supplemented with $0 . 0 1 \%$ GDN. After concentrating to $1 0 \mathrm { m g / m l } ,$ , the protein was injected into size exclusion chromatography using a Superose 6 Increase 10/300 GL column (Cytiva). The column was preequilibrated with buffer A supplemented with $0 . 0 1 \%$ GDN. The peak fractions were collected and concentrated for cryo-EM sample preparation. Expression and purification of AtCER6-AtCER2 complex followed a same protocol as above. 

# Cryo-EM sample preparation and data collection

The ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex samples were incubated with CoA (3 mM, Sigma-Aldrich) or 30:0-CoA/28:0-CoA (2 mM, Sigma-Aldrich) substrate for 1 hour before cryo-EM grid preparation. The ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2 mutant samples were incubated with malonyl-CoA (5 mM, Sigma-Aldrich) substrate for 1 hour before cryo-EM grid preparation. The 300-mesh gold grid (C-Flat; CF-1.2/1.3-3Au-50) was used for protein complex samples. The cryo-EM samples at concentrations of $1 0 \mathrm { m g / m l }$ were produced using a Vitrobot Mark IV (FEI) machine. The temperature and humidity were set up as $8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 0 0 \%$ . Blot time was set up as 3 s. Blot force and blot total were set up as $- 1$ and 1, individually. Then, grid was plunged into liquid ethane for quick freezing. The cryo-EM samples were screened on a JEM-F200 200 kV transmission electron microscope using a $. \mathrm { K } \times 4 \mathrm { K }$ Gatan Rio16 camera. 

# Cryo-EM image processing

All movie images were aligned with MotionCor2 (73). The resulting dose-weighted micrographs were imported into cryoSPARC (74) (v.3) for patch-based contrast transfer function estimation. 

For cryo-EM image processing of ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complex in presence of 30:0-CoA, 924,101 particles were automatically picked using cryoSPARC from 4600 micrographs. After two rounds of twodimensional (2D) classifications and 3D classification, the best representative class with 207,945 particles were selected for nonuniform refinement and local refinement, resulting in a 3.30- and 3.26-Å map. The overall resolutions were estimated on the basis of the goldstandard Fourier shell correlation 0.143 criterion. The details related to data processing are summarized in fig. S3 (A to E). 

For cryo-EM image processing of ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2 complex in presence of 28:0-CoA, 362,909 particles were automatically picked using cryoSPARC from 3989 micrographs. After two rounds of 2D classifications and 3D classifications, the best representative 

class with 94,244 particles was selected for nonuniform refinement, resulting in a 2.88-Å map. The overall resolutions were estimated on the basis of the gold-standard Fourier shell correlation 0.143 criterion. The details related to data processing are summarized in fig. S3 (F to J). 

For cryo-EM image processing of ZmCER6-ZmGL2 bound to CoA, 350,210 particles were automatically picked using cryoSPARC from 2149 micrographs. After two rounds of 2D classifications and 3D classifications, the best representative class with 47,943 particles was selected for nonuniform refinement and local refinement, resulting in a 3.12- and 3.01-Å map. The overall resolutions were estimated on the basis of the gold-standard Fourier shell correlation 0.143 criterion. The details related to data processing are summarized in fig. S4 (A to E). 

For cryo-EM image processing of ZmCER6C222A-ZmGL2 bound to malonyl-CoA, 810,093 particles were automatically picked using cryoSPARC from 4301 micrographs. After two rounds of 2D classifications and 3D classifications, the best representative class with 156,994 particles was selected for nonuniform refinement and local refinement, resulting in a 3.01- and 2.96-Å map. # Model building and refinement

The ZmCER6 and ZmGL2 atomic models were built using the predicted model of AlphaFold II (75) as a starting template. The predicted structure was docked into the density map and manually adjusted and rebuilt by COOT (76). The resulting model was refined using PHENIX (77) with secondary structure and geometry restraints. The structures were validated with MolProbity (78), and refinement statistics can be found in table S1. All structure figures were prepared in ChimeraX (79).
