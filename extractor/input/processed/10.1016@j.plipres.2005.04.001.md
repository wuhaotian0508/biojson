# Abstract

Coenzyme A (CoA) is a ubiquitous essential cofactor that plays a central role in the metabolism of carboxylic acids, including short- and long-chain fatty acids. In the last few years, all of the genes encoding the CoA biosynthetic enzymes have been identified and the structures of several proteins in the pathway have been determined. CoA is assembled in five steps from pantothenic acid and pathway intermediates are common to both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. In spite of the identical biochemistry, remarkable sequence differences among some of the prokaryotic and eukaryotic enzymes have been revealed by comparative genomics. Renewed interest in CoA has arisen from the realization that the biosynthetic pathway is a target for antibacterial drug discovery and from the unexpected association of a human neurodegenerative disorder with mutations in pantothenate kinase. The purpose of this review is to integrate previous knowledge with the most recent findings in the genetics, enzymology and regulation of CoA biosynthesis in bacteria, plants and mammals.

# 1. Introduction

# 1.1. The biochemical pathway for coenzyme A biosynthesis

Coenzyme A (CoA) is an indispensable cofactor in all living organisms, where it functions as an acyl group carrier and carbonyl-activating group in a number of central biochemical transformations, including the tricarboxylic acid cycle and fatty acid metabolism. About $4 \%$ of known enzymes utilize CoA as an obligate cofactor and CoA is involved in over 100 different reactions in intermediary metabolism [1,2]. CoA is the source of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine, the prosthetic group of carrier proteins of fatty acid, polyketide and nonribosomal peptide synthases [3–5]. The biosynthesis of CoA from pantothenic acid is an essential and universal pathway in prokaryotes and eukaryotes. The CoA biosynthetic genes in bacteria, plants and mammals are known; however, there are some exceptions that will be highlighted in the review. CoA is assembled in five steps

# 1.2. Pantothenate: the specific precursor of CoA

Animals and some microbes lack the capacity to synthesize pantothenate and are totally dependent on the uptake of exogenous pantothenate. However, most bacteria, such as Escherichia coli, Salmonella typhimurium, Corynebacterium glutamicum [1,2,6], plants and fungi, such as Neurospora crassa [7] and Aspergillus nidulans [8], synthesize pantothenate, and so pantothenate is found virtually everywhere in biology [6]. In fact, the term pantothenate is derived from the Greek ‘‘pantothen’’ meaning ‘‘from all sides.’’ E. coli produces and secretes 15 times more pantothenate than required for intracellular CoA biosynthesis [9], pointing out the contribution of the intestinal flora to the pantothenate supply available to mammals. In fact, a controlled study shows that sufficient quantities of pantothenate are obtained from ruminant microorganisms to maintain animals without pantothenate supplementation [10]. Thus, due to the ubiquitous nature of pantothenate, a vitamin $\mathbf { B } _ { 5 }$ deficiency in humans has not been reported.

# 1.3. Pantothenate synthesis in plants

Like microbes, plants synthesize pantothenate de novo (for review, see [11]). Genes encoding PanB and PanC have been identified in the Arabidopsis thaliana genome [12]. The cDNAs of these genes are able to complement the pantothenate auxotrophic phenotype of the respective $E .$ . coli mutants [12]. Subcellular localization studies indicate that both PanB1 and PanB2 are targeted to mitochondria whereas PanC is found in cytosol [12]. This implies that pantothenate synthesis in plants occurs in different subcellular compartments and there must be transporters for the pathway intermediates. It is hypothesized that plants may synthesize pantothenate by two independent routes, possibly in two subcellular compartments, via the linear or lactone forms of the pathway intermediates [11].

# 2. Enzymes of the CoA biosynthetic pathway

# 2.1. Pantothenate transport in bacteria

Pantothenate is taken up by virtually all bacteria and is essential for those lacking de novo pantothenate biosynthesis, such as Streptococcus pneumoniae, Lactobacillus lactis and Hemophilus influenzae [13]. In E. coli, pantothenate uptake is mediated by pantothenate permease, also termed the PanF protein, encoded by the panF gene. PanF uses a sodium-cotransport mechanism to concentrate pantothenate from the medium [14,15]. PanF is predicted to contain 12 transmembrane hydrophobic domains connected by short hydrophilic chains, a topological motif characteristic of other cation-dependent permeases of the major facilitator superfamily of proteins [14,16]. The transport system is highly specific for pantothenate, with a $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $0 . 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and a maximum velocity of $1 . { \bar { 6 } } \operatorname { p m o l } / \operatorname { m i n } / 1 0 ^ { 8 }$ cells [15,17]. Overexpression of the PanF protein in $E .$ . coli produces a 10-fold increase in the rate of pantothenate uptake and concomitant elevation of the steady-state

intracellular concentration of pantothenate [14]. However, the CoA levels remain unaffected, indicating that PanF activity does not regulate CoA biosynthesis. Also, the rate of pantothenate transport is not affected by the intracellular CoA concentration [14]. Pantothenate is effluxed from E. coli which excretes large amounts of pantothenate that it has synthesized de novo [9,18]. Pantothenate excretion is still active in panF mutants which are unable to take up the vitamin [19], showing that the uptake is unidirectional, and pointing to the existence of a distinct and uncharacterized efflux system.

# 2.2. Pantothenate transport in yeast

Yeast is a pantothenate auxotroph, although the requirement for pantothenate can be overcome by the addition of $\beta$ -alanine to the growth medium. The product of the FEN2 gene of Saccharomyces cerevisiae is a hydrogen ion-pantothenate symporter belonging to the major facilitator superfamily localized on the plasma membrane [20]. The Fen2p protein is essential for growth on minimal medium and the primary sequence bears no resemblance to the PanF transporter from E. coli [21] or the mammalian multivitamin transporter [22]. The Fen2p transporter has high affinity for pantothenate with a $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $3 . 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and, interestingly, fen2 mutations confer resistance to the fungicide, fenpropimorph. The drug interferes with ergosterol synthesis, which is dependent on acetyl-CoA, and is transported into cells via the pantothenate symporter. The liz1 gene of Schizosaccharomyces pombe encodes an active transport protein for pantothenate which is homologous with the Fen2p protein of S. cerevisiae [23]. Both the Fen2p and Liz1 transporters are required for pantothenate uptake at low extracellular concentrations. Liz1 deletion mutants are viable because they can synthesize some pantothenate from uracil, but exhibit slow growth and delayed septation, similar to mutants with defects in fatty acid biosynthesis.

# 2.3. Pantothenate transport in higher eukaryotes

The transport of pantothenate by the rat small intestine was first thought to occur by simple diffusion [24], but now there is strong evidence for a specific transport mechanism. The process is unidirectional, sodium-dependent and saturable, with a pantothenate $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $1 7 \mu \mathrm { M }$ measured across the brush-border membrane of rat jejunum and chick intestinal cells [25]. The transport is active, driven by an electrochemical gradient, and occurs against a concentration gradient [25,26]. Similar pantothenate uptake systems are described in primary parenchymal cells with a $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $1 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [26], and in renal brush-border membranes with a $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $1 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [27]. Pantothenate, biotin and lipoate all use the same transporter, as first described in human placental epithelial cells [28,29]. This sodium-dependent multivitamin transporter was cloned from three sources: rat placenta, a human choriocarcinoma cell line, and rabbit intestine [22,30–32]. The cDNAs of the cloned transporters predict almost identical proteins of about $6 9 \mathrm { k D a }$ consisting of 635 amino acids. Expression of the cloned cDNAs in either mammalian cells or Xenopus laevis oocytes results in the appearance of sodium-dependent transport of pantothenate with a $K _ { \mathrm { t } }$ of $2 { \ - } 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ that is inhibited by either biotin or lipoic acid. The mammalian pantothenate transporter belongs to the sodium-coupled glucose transporter family and the proteins in this family share a common core of 13 transmembrane helices [21,33]. Unlike many other cell types, normal human

erythrocytes are impermeable to pantothenate, but when infected with the malaria parasite, Plasmodium falciparum, the vitamin is readily taken up and phosphorylated [34]. The data point to the existence of a plasmodial transport protein that is coupled to hydrogen ion movement and has a low affinity for pantothenate $( K _ { \mathrm { t } } = 2 3 \mathrm { m M } )$ .

Because the last enzyme in the plant pantothenate synthesis is localized in the cytosol [12] whereas pantothenate kinase is localized in the chloroplast [35], there must be a transporter to move pantothenate synthesized from the cytosol to the chloroplast.

# 2.4. Bacterial pantothenate kinases

Pantothenate kinase, also known as PanK or CoaA, catalyzes the ATP-dependent conversion of pantothenate to $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenate, the first committed step in CoA biosynthesis. The enzyme is encoded by the coaA gene that was first identified in S. typhimurium [36] and $E .$ . coli [37], and subsequently in numerous other bacteria by comparative genomics [13,38]. CoaA proteins from E. coli [39] and Staphylococcus aureus [40] have been expressed and purified. In particular, the E. coli enzyme has been extensively characterized and is considered the prototypical bacterial CoaA, and is structurally distinct from the eukaryotic counterparts [13,38,41]. In contrast, the CoaA from S. aureus and the putative CoaA from Bacillus anthracis are moderately related to the eukaryotic PanK proteins and unrelated to the $E .$ . coli CoaA.

The $E .$ . coli coaA transcript has two translation initiation sites and the CoaA protein was first identified as a mixture of two peptides, the shorter protein lacks the first eight N-terminal residues [39]. Both peptides are active and the enzyme primarily exists as a homodimer of the larger $3 6 \mathrm { k D a }$ subunits, but can also function as a heterodimer [42]. The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ affinity constants for pantothenate and ATP are estimated to be 36 and $1 3 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively. Kinetic analysis demonstrates that the phosphorylation proceeds by an ordered sequential mechanism, with ATP binding before pantothenate. The binding of ATP is highly cooperative [42]. CoaA activity is inhibited, in vivo and in vitro, by unacylated CoA and less efficiently by CoA thioesters [43], the end products of the pathway. The control is stringent, as illustrated by the fact that a 76-fold overexpression of CoaA protein produces only a 2.7-fold increase in the CoA cofactor level [39]. Early studies with partially purified enzyme preparations show that the CoA inhibition is competitive with ATP and that both ligands bind to kinetically indistinguishable sites on the enzyme [43]. The interference of CoA with the ATP binding is confirmed by site-directed mutagenesis of a conserved lysine within the A-type ATP binding motif [44], namely $\mathrm { G X } _ { 4 } \mathrm { G K S }$ . Mutation of Lys101 to Met yields the CoaA[K101M] mutant protein which is catalytically inactive and does not bind ATP or CoA [42].

The crystal structures of the $E .$ . coli CoaA enzyme in binary complexes with CoA or AMPPNP, a nonhydrolyzable ATP analog, provide detailed insight into the binding of ATP and the CoA allosteric inhibitor [45]. Superimposition of the CoaA  CoA and CoaA  AMPPNP structures reveals that the ADP moieties of the two ligands bind in very different orientations and locations. However, the $\nsim$ and $\beta$ -phosphates of CoA occupy the same space at Lys101 as the $\beta .$ - and $\gamma$ -phosphates of AMPPNP, thus explaining the competition between ATP and CoA in solution and the loss of CoA binding as well as catalytic activity in the CoaA[K101M] mutant [42]. The thiol group of CoA fits tightly in a space defined by the aromatic residues Phe244, Phe252, Phe25 9 and Tyr262.

Thus, steric hindrance due to the presence of an acyl chain explains why acetyl-CoA is less potent than unacylated CoA in inhibiting CoaA enzyme activity [43]. The residues involved in the binding of the second substrate, pantothenate, were recently identified in the CoaA  ADP $\cdot$ pantothenate ternary structure, and are conserved in 13 bacterial CoaAs [46]. In particular, Asp127 and Tyr240, responsible for facilitating the deprotonation of the C1–OH and for interacting with the carboxyl group of pantothenate, respectively, are also conserved in the eukaryotic enzymes [41]. The pantothenate binding site is characterized by remarkable flexibility. Superposition of the CoaA  ADP Æ pantothenate and CoaA  CoA structures, and docking of the pantothenamide analog N5-Pan [46,47], reveal that the pantothenate moieties of both the allosteric regulator and the bulky substrate analog are accommodated within the same binding groove. However, the binding of each ligand transmits a distinct conformational to the protein.

The genomic sequence information for a number of bacterial pathogens is uncovering new and different proteins that catalyze the first step in CoA biosynthesis. The CoaA protein from S. aureus (SaCoaA) has a distinct primary sequence that has limited homology with the mammalian PanK proteins but does not resemble the prototypical bacterial CoaA of $E .$ . coli. The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values for pantothenate and ATP are 23 and $3 4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively, indicating that the SaCoaA may be somewhat more reactive in the presence of lower intracellular ATP concentrations compared to the E. coli enzyme [48]. In contrast to all known PanKs, the SaCoaA activity is not feedback-regulated by CoA or CoA thioesters [48]. The absence of feedback regulation at the PanK step of CoA biosynthesis results in accumulation of high concentrations of intracellular CoA. The bacteria produce CoA in proportion to the precursor in the medium with no evidence for regulation at the SaCoaA or other downstream steps. Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Helicobacter pylori clearly have four of the five genes necessary to encode the proteins that comprise the CoA pathway [13,38] but do not have a recognizable pantothenate kinase gene in their chromosomes. A recent patent reports a unique gene in Bacillus subtilis, called coaX, that complements the E. coli coaA15(Ts) mutant strain, suggesting that it encodes a unique pantothenate kinase. CoaX homologs are found in bacteria lacking the coaA gene, indicating that there is another bacterial pantothenate kinase that needs to be biochemically characterized.

# 2.5. Pantothenate kinase in fungi

The first eukaryotic pantothenate kinase gene was identified in A. nidulans [41] and encodes a protein that bears little resemblance to the E. coli enzyme. A genetic selection strategy was used for cloning a panK gene library by functional complementation of the E. coli temperature-sensitive coaA15(Ts) mutant strain [37]. The A. nidulans panK gene is located on chromosome 3, is interrupted by three small introns, and is expressed constitutively. The gene is predicted to encode a $4 6 . 9 \mathrm { k D a }$ protein which is highly related to the predicted sequence of the $Y d r 5 3 I w$ gene product in the S. cerevisiae genome [41]. The PanK enzyme has an estimated $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $6 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for pantothenate and a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $1 4 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for ATP at saturating concentrations of the second substrate. Other nucleotides can replace ATP to some extent in the assay, with GTP being the most active alternative substrate and supporting up to $70 \%$ of the activity. The dissimilarities in the primary sequences of the bacterial CoaA and the fungal PanK proteins reflect distinct differences in the regulatory properties of the enzymes. In contrast to $E .$ coli CoaA, which is inhibited by CoA

and to a lesser extent by its thioesters, the A. nidulans PanK is selectively and potently inhibited by acetyl-CoA. Although the acetyl-CoA inhibition is competitive with respect to ATP, the binding sites for acetyl-CoA and ATP are likely different from those found in the $E .$ . coli enzyme. The residues flanking the lysine in the proposed ATP binding site of PanK from A. nidulans do not resemble either a type A or a type B nucleotide binding site [44].

The yeast S. cerevisiae has a gene that is homologous with the A. nidulans PanK $( Y d r 5 3 I w )$ [41], although the biochemical activity has not yet been confirmed. Another protein encoded by $Y G R 2 0 5 w$ bears a close structural resemblance to the E. coli CoaA protein [49]. The crystal structure of the $Y G R 2 0 5 w$ protein has a mononucleotide fold and binds sulphate in a kinase-like fashion. The nature of the phosphate-accepting substrate remains to be determined but is suspected to be a small metabolite, like pantothenate.

# 2.6. Pantothenate kinase in plants

Plant pantothenate kinase was first purified from spinach leaves [35]. This enzyme is localized in the chloroplast where the demand for CoA is high for de novo fatty acid synthesis and lipid metabolism [35]. The activity of plant pantothenate kinase is inhibited by malonyl-CoA, but not by CoA or acetyl-CoA [35]. Importantly, pantothenate kinase activity increases linearly in germinating spinach seeds during the period when the stored triacylglycerol is mobilized through $\beta$ -oxidation to acetyl-CoA [35]. A. thaliana pantothenate kinase (AtCoaA) sequence was identified by sequence homology with A. nidulans PanK. AtCoaA is located on chromosome 1, and it shows strong homology to yeast pantothenate kinase and human PanK1b. Only poor homology is observed between AtCoaA and bacterial CoaA. The activity of AtCoaA is verified in vitro, but little is known about its regulatory properties [50].

# 2.7. Pantothenate kinase in mammals

The first of four genes putatively encoding pantothenate kinase (PanK) in mammals was discovered and termed Pank1 in mouse [51]. The mPank1 gene consists of seven introns and eight exons and is located on chromosome 19 (19C2-3). Two complete cDNAs arise from alternate splicing of the Pank1 transcript [51]. The two different mRNAs encode two isoforms of the mouse PanK1 protein, termed mPanK1a and mPanK1b, which have the same 363 amino acid catalytic domain encoded by exons 2 through 7, but have distinctly different amino termini encoded by alternating first exons. The predicted sequence of the mPanK1 catalytic domain is not related to E. coli CoaA but has significant homology with the A. nidulans PanK. The mPanK1a transcript is most highly expressed in heart and kidney, whereas $\mathrm { m P a n K 1 \beta }$ mRNA is detected primarily in liver and kidney. The $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ and $\beta$ isoforms were overexpressed heterologously in COS-7 cultured cells and their activities were confirmed in vitro. The different amino termini confer different regulatory properties on the mPanK1 proteins. The $\mathrm { m P a n K 1 \beta }$ activity in vitro is stimulated by CoA and weakly inhibited by acetyl-CoA [51] whereas the $\mathrm { m P a n K 1 } \alpha$ activity is inhibited by free CoA and long-chain acyl-CoA, and more potently inhibited by acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA [52]. The 184 amino acid extension of mPanK1a attached to the amino terminus of the catalytic core likely encodes a regulatory domain which adjusts the basal response of the core protein to CoAlinked intermediates.

Subsequently, the fumble (fbl) locus in the fruit fly, Drosophila melanogaster, was proposed to encode a PanK protein. Fbl encodes the sole Drosophila PanK-like protein and is essential in flies because excision of a major portion of the fbl gene results in pupal neuroblast abnormalities and lethality [53]. Three isoforms are predicted based on splice variants identified by northern blot. The largest is termed FumbleL and is about $5 3 \mathrm { k D a }$ and found in the cytoplasm, with defined staining at the plasma membrane during most of the cell cycle. However, during mitosis, the FumbleL protein is concentrated around the spindle during metaphase, at the cleavage furrow during anaphase, and near the spindle midbody at telophase. Insertion of a $P -$ -element in the promoter region of the fbl gene reduces expression of the putative PanK and affects dividing cells in Drosophila testes [53]. The cells exhibit aberrant mitosis and meiosis, with abnormalities in bipolar spindle organization, chromosome segregation and contractile ring formation. Reduced expression in female flies renders them sterile and uncoordinated. Although the biochemical activity of the Fumble protein has not been demonstrated, the high degree of protein sequence homology with the PanK proteins from Aspergillus and mouse strongly supports its annotation. The question then arises as to the function of the various PanK associations with the mitotic apparatus. Is phosphopantothenate the important metabolite, or is CoA biosynthesis required or regulated by these associations?

Additional mammalian PanK genes are predicted based on the existence of a number of expressed sequence tags that are highly related to mouse PanK1. The homologous human PANK1, PANK2, PANK3 and PANK4 genes were first described in conjunction with the mapping of a hereditary neurodegenerative disorder to the PANK2 gene [54]. These four genes map to human chromosomes 10, 20, 5 and 1, respectively, and also map to the syntenic loci on mouse chromosomes 19, 2, 11 and 4, respectively. The 3.0 and $1 . 8 \mathrm { k b }$ transcripts derived from PANK1 are highly expressed in human liver and kidney, with lower levels in heart and skeletal muscle, in agreement with the pattern of expression in mouse tissues [51]. The mouse Pank1 transcripts are larger, though, estimated at 3.6 and 3.1 kb. A $1 . 8 5 \mathrm { k b }$ transcript of human PANK2 is reported to be expressed in most tissues, with liver having the highest level [54]. Independent investigation of human PANK2 expression indicates two different transcripts, 2.4 and $1 . 3 5 \mathrm { k b }$ in size, predicted to encode 2 protein isoforms [55], both of which are different from the isoform predicted from the $1 . 8 5 \mathrm { k b }$ transcript. These data reflect some initial confusion about PANK2 gene expression and the size of the human PanK2 protein but much of the discrepancy is resolved by the recent demonstration of the mitochondrial processing of the largest PanK2 protein, a product of the largest $2 . 4 \mathrm { k b }$ transcript, into two smaller proteins [56]. The level of the $1 . 6 5 \mathrm { k b }$ transcript of human PANK3 is highest in liver, followed by muscle and heart. Altogether these data suggest that PANK expression is very important to liver function. PANK4 is expressed at its highest levels in muscle and heart [54]. The predicted protein encoded by PANK4 has the least homology with the three other PanK proteins and has a significantly longer extension at the carboxy terminus that lacks homology to known proteins opening the possibility that it may not be a PanK.

Mammals not only have multiple Pank genes, but there also are multiple PanK protein isoforms encoded by at least two genes to date. The human PANK1 gene encodes two protein isoforms, termed hPanK1a and hPanK1b, similar to the two PanK1 isoforms found in mouse [52]. The mouse and human $\mathrm { P a n K 1 \beta }$ proteins are almost identical with each other and smaller than their $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ counterparts. However, the larger hPanK1a differs from the mouse homolog in that it has a distinctively different N-terminus with an additional 38 residues compared to mPanK1a.

The activities of both the human and mouse PanK1 isoforms have been verified in vitro [51,52,57]. Three hPanK2 isoforms arise from processing of the longest $6 2 . 7 \mathrm { k D a }$ isoform following transport to the mitochondria [55,56]. The PanK2 proteins localize to mitochondria in human [55,56,58] and in mouse [58], thus distinguishing them from other PanKs that do not possess mitochondrial-targeting sequences. The hPanK2 protein translated from the most $5 ^ { \prime }$ start site is sequentially cleaved at two sites by the mitochondrial processing peptidase, generating a transient $5 9 . 2 \mathrm { k D a }$ intermediate and a long-lived $4 7 . 4 \mathrm { k D a }$ mature protein [56]. Mitochondrial targeting sequences are located in both the largest precursor peptide and the intermediate peptide, and the biochemical activity of the $4 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ protein is confirmed [56]. Two additional hPanK2 isoforms are described in the literature [55,58], the smallest of which is $3 0 . 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ and thought to arise from transcript initiation at an alternate exon [55]. However, a major portion of PanK consensus protein sequence would be absent from the predicted N-terminus of this $3 0 . 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ version, indicating that the biochemical activity of this isoform would likely be compromised. A separate $5 0 . 6 \mathrm { k D a }$ PanK2 isoform is reported to initiate at a leucine residue rather than a methionine [58], but this protein is predicted from an expressed sequence tag that is probably truncated and lacks $5 ^ { \prime }$ coding sequence [55,56]. This putative isoform retains one of the two mitochondrial cleavage sites and is likely to be active, since it includes the $4 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ isoform sequence, but is unclear whether it is actually expressed. There is a single PANK3 mRNA. Shorter versions in the expressed sequence tag database are likely due to incomplete extension of the tagged cDNAs. Even a minor truncation of the mRNA at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -end would result in the expression of a protein lacking significant consensus catalytic sequence.

# 2.8. Bacterial $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase

The $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase activity in $E .$ . coli was initially associated with a $3 5 \mathrm { k D a }$ protein reported to contain a covalently bound pyruvoyl group [59]; however, this turned out to be incorrect. More than a decade later the dfp gene [60,61], recently renamed coaBC, was found to encode a flavin mononucleotide (FMN)-containing bifunctional enzyme responsible for both the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase and the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase activities [62,63]. These activities are fused together in almost all prokaryotes with the exception of streptococci and enterococci which possess separate genes predicted to encode CoaB and CoaC proteins [13,38]. B. anthracis contains a gene predicted to encode a monofunctional CoaB in addition to the bifunctional CoaBC [13].

The $E .$ . coli CoaBC protein is a homododecamer of $4 3 . 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ subunits. The translation initiation site is not obvious from genomic DNA sequence analysis, but amino terminal sequencing identifies $\mathbf { A } \mathbf { T } \mathbf { G } ^ { 2 5 }$ as the start codon of the coaBC gene and confirms the cleavage of the initiator methionine from the mature protein [62]. The two individual activity domains have been expressed and purified [64,65]. The carboxy terminal domain (CoaB) encompasses residues 191– 406 [64,65], forms homodimers and catalyzes the formation of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine from $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenate and cysteine [65]. The reaction requires CTP [63] and occurs via an activated acyl-cytidylate intermediate [65,66], which is subsequently attacked by the amino group of cysteine. Site-directed mutagenesis identifies $\mathbf { A s n } ^ { 2 1 0 }$ , $\mathrm { A r g } ^ { \dot { 2 } 0 6 }$ and $\mathrm { A l a } ^ { 2 7 6 }$ as residues involved in the second half-reaction and these residues are proposed to bind cysteine [65,66].

Interestingly, the use of CTP [63] might be a characteristic feature of bacterial CoaB proteins since human [67] and partially purified rat liver [68] $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetases use ATP. Prokaryotic CoaB domains and eukaryotic monofunctional counterparts show little sequence similarity [13,38,67]. However, Lys289 and $\mathbf { A } \mathbf { s } \mathbf { n } ^ { 2 1 0 }$ within the bacterial conserved motifs KXKK and NXSSGK, respectively, are strictly conserved even across archea and eukarya [65]. Mutation of these residues results in loss of $E .$ . coli $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthase activity [65].

The amino terminal domain (CoaC) includes residues 2–190 and contains a bound FMN [62,64]. It possesses $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase activity and, just like the full-length enzyme, exists as a homododecamer. This domain shows sequence similarity to Staphylococcus epidermis EpiD [62,64], a peptidyl-cysteine decarboxylase flavoprotein required for lantibiotic biosynthesis [69–71] and to A. thaliana AtHAL3a [72], a flavoprotein involved in plant growth and salt tolerance which catalyzes the same reaction as CoaC. EpiD, AtHAL3a and the CoaC domains are members of the homooligomeric flavin-containing cysteine decarboxylase (HFCD) family [62]. Sequence alignment of CoaC with other bacterial $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylases reveals the existence of characteristic GG/SIAXYK, $\mathrm { P A M N X } _ { 2 } \mathrm { M X }$ and $\mathrm { P X } _ { 6 } \mathrm { C X } _ { 3 } \mathrm { G X G }$ motifs, important for both FMN and substrate binding [64]. Site-directed mutagenesis within these protein regions identifies critical residues for enzymatic activity, such as Gly14, Asn125 and $\dot { \mathrm { C y s } } ^ { 1 5 8 }$ [62,64]. The proposed substrate recognition clamp, $\mathrm { P X } _ { 6 } \mathrm { C X } _ { 3 } \mathrm { G X G }$ , is also conserved in the eukaryotic monofunctional $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylases [38,64], including AtHAL3a, which are relatively similar to their bacterial counterparts. As shown for EpiD [70,73] and AtHAL3a [74,75], the decarboxylation reaction catalyzed by the CoaC domain proceeds via a thioaldehyde intermediate formed by FMN-dependent oxidation of the cysteine moiety of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine [62,76]. Decarboxylation of this intermediate to $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoyl-aminoethenethiol, followed by reduction through the flavin reduced form completes the catalytic cycle.

# 2.9. $4 ^ { \prime }$ -Phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase in plants

Two Arabidopsis proteins, AtCoaB and $A t C o \mathrm { a B } ^ { * }$ , were identified as homologues of the human monofunctional phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase (PPCS) [50]. Although these two proteins share $90 \%$ sequence identity, there are two significant differences between them. A 24-amino acid N-terminal extension (Met1 to $\mathrm { A s n } ^ { 2 4 }$ ) and an insertion of 22 amino acids $\mathrm { ( V a l ^ { 1 2 7 } }$ to $\mathrm { G l u } ^ { 1 4 8 }$ ) in AtCoaB is absent from $A t C o \mathrm { a B } ^ { * }$ [50]. Both AtCoaB sequences are highly homologous to lower and higher eukaryotic orthologs but share little similarity with the CoaB domain of the $E .$ . coli bifunctional protein. However, residues important for the activity and structure of the $E .$ . coli enzyme are conserved in the plant protein. The activity of AtCoaB is confirmed in vitro. Like the cloned human PPCS, AtCoaB uses ATP instead of CTP, as substrate [50,67]. Whether or not $A t C o \mathrm { a B } ^ { * }$ is a functional enzyme still remains an open question.

The flavoproteins AtHAL3a (AtCoaC1) and AtHAL3b (AtCoaC2) were first characterized as homolog of the yeast halotolerance protein SIS2/HAL3 and they are related to plant growth and tolerance to salt and drought. AtHAL3a was later shown to possess the phosphopantothenoylcysteine (PPC) decarboxylase signature sequence and indeed catalyzes the reaction [65,77]. There is

no experimental evidence that AtHAL3b can catalyze the same decarboxylation reaction. AtCoaC1 shows sequence homology to the N-terminal domain of bacterial flavoprotein CoaBC and both are members of the HFCD protein family. AtCoaC1 is a trimer and its active site residues $\mathrm { H i s } ^ { 9 0 }$ and $\mathrm { C y s } ^ { 1 7 5 }$ were identified by site-directed mutagenesis [77]. AtCoaC1 catalyzes the decarboxylation of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine via an oxidatively decarboxylated intermediate containing an aminoethenethiol group [74]. The reduction of the aminoethenethiol intermediate is dependent on the active site residue $\bar { \mathrm { C y s } ^ { 1 7 5 } }$ in AtCoaC1 and the cofactor $\mathrm { F M N H } _ { 2 }$ [74]. The reaction mechanism is consistent with the crystal structure of AtCoaC1 mutant C175S in complex with the reaction intermediate pantothenoyl-aminoethenethiol and $\mathrm { F M N H } _ { 2 }$ [75].

# 2.10. Mammalian $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase

A comparative genomics approach was used to identify the human genes encoding the four enzymatic steps following PanK in CoA biosynthesis [67]. The gene encoding $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine synthetase (PPCS) is located on chromosome 1, and the distinct gene encoding $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine decarboxylase (PPCDC) is located on chromosome 15. These genes exist as single copies in the human genome, in contrast to the four homologous genes that encode the confirmed and putative PanKs. The biological functions of the human PPCS and PPCDC enzymes were verified by a novel high-throughput complementation technique based on transposon mutagenesis and genetic footprinting in $E .$ . coli [67]. The cloned human cDNAs complemented the loss of the essential CoA biosynthetic enzymes in the bacteria, thus making the endogenous genes non-essential and able to incorporate disruptive transposon insertions. Both the PPCS and PPCDC activities, encoded by the two proteins, were overexpressed in E. coli, purified, and the predicted activities were confirmed in vitro.

The human PPCS catalyzes the synthase reaction only, and utilizes ATP for the activation of substrate in the ligation reaction four times more efficiently than CTP [67], in contrast to the E. coli CoaBC bifunctional enzyme which shows a distinct preference for CTP [63]. The human PPCS protein is a dimer of identical monomers [78]. Some features of the monomer fold resemble a group of NAD-dependent enzymes, while other features resemble the ribokinase fold. The structure predicts a ping-pong mechanism with initial formation of an acyladenylate intermediate, followed by release of pyrophosphate, binding of cysteine, and then reaction to form the final products, $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine and AMP. The selection of cysteine rather than serine as a high affinity substrate is a key mechanistic question for human PPCS since the modeling of cysteine in the active site does not associate the amino acid with any highly conserved residues in the protein. Therefore, a clear answer awaits a structure determination with a substrate analog bound at the active site. The heterologously expressed human PPCDC is thought to be a flavin-binding protein, like the homologous activity from $E$ . coli [63], although earlier results had classified mammalian PPCDC as a pyruvoyldependent enzyme [79]. The human PPCDC is a $2 2 \mathrm { k D a }$ protein of 204 amino acids and is $50 \%$ identical and $6 3 \%$ similar to the A. thaliana PPCDC encoded by the AtHAL3a gene whose structure in known [75,80]. Investigation of the human PPCDC protein structure is in progress and the protein crystal structure reveals that it is a trimer with each monomer binding the flavin mononucleotide cofactor [78].

# 2.11. Bacterial $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopanthetheine adenylyltransferase

$4 ^ { \prime }$ -Phosphopanthetheine adenylyltransferase, also termed CoaD or PPAT, catalyzes the $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g } ^ { 2 + }$ - dependent reversible transfer of the adenylyl group of ATP to $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine. The enzyme was first isolated from Corynebacterium ammoniagenes (formerly Brevibacterium ammoniagenes) and is a trimer with $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ affinity constants of 0.19 and $0 . 5 3 \mathrm { m M }$ for $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine and ATP, respectively [81]. The kdtB gene was recently identified to encode the $E .$ . coli $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase, and was renamed coaD [82]. Purified $E .$ . coli CoaD contains 0.5 mole of CoA per mole of enzyme, exists in solution as a homohexamer of $1 7 . 8 \mathrm { k D a }$ subunits, and is arranged as a dimer of trimers [82,83]. Kinetic analysis of the reverse reaction reveals that a ternary complex between CoaD and both substrates is formed before catalysis; however, it is not known if the order of substrate binding is important. The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values for pyrophosphate and dephospho-CoA for the reverse reaction are $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { m M }$ and $7 . 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively [82]. The crystal structures of CoaD in binary complexes with the substrates, product and the putative inhibitor CoA show that CoaD is an allosteric hexameric enzyme characterized by half-of-sites reactivity. In fact, only one trimer within the hexamer binds dephospho-CoA and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine [83–85]. Although bound ATP is observed in all six subunits, the electron density indicates full binding solely to one protomer [83,84]. CoA is suggested to allosterically regulate CoaD through feedback inhibition [9,85–87]. The cofactor is proposed to exert its inhibitory effect on CoaD by mimicking the binding of the phosphopantetheine moiety of the substrate and product in one of the trimers (B subunit) [85]. This dephosphoCoA- or phosphopantetheine-like binding induces conformational changes in the other trimer (A subunit) that force CoA to bind in a unique conformation. In particular, superimposition of the CoaD  CoA, CoaD  $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine and CoaD  dephospho-CoA structures reveals that the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine and the corresponding moiety of CoA in the A subunit occupy the same site but in different orientations. In contrast, the adenylyl moieties of CoA and dephospho-CoA bind to different sites due to steric hindrance introduced by the phosphate group attached at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -position of the CoA ribose. Interestingly, no electron density is observed for the adenylate group of CoA in the B subunit.

Sequence homology identifies CoaD as a member of the nucleotidyltransferase ${ \alpha } / { \beta }$ phosphodiesterase superfamily which includes at least 100 aminoacyl-tRNA synthases [82,88]. This family is characterized by a conserved H/TXGH motif that plays a role in nucleotide binding and stabilization of the pentacoordinate transition state arising from nucleophilic attack on the $\textsf { \textbf { \textsf { X } } }$ -phosphate of ATP [89]. CoaD displays a dinucleotide-binding fold similar to class I aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases, and the binding of ATP supports a model in which the $\pmb { \alpha }$ -phosphate of ATP undergoes nucleophilic attack by the phosphate group of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine in an in-line displacement mechanism [82,84]. The corresponding pentacoordinate transition state is likely to be stabilized by His18, the last histidine of the T/HXGH motif. His18, Lys42, $\mathrm { { A r g } ^ { 9 1 } }$ and $\mathrm { S e r } ^ { 1 2 9 }$ are conserved in all the known PPAT sequences [82,84]. In fact, hydrogen-bonding to $\mathrm { L y s } ^ { 4 2 }$ and $\mathrm { T h r } ^ { 1 0 }$ appears to be crucial for orienting the nucleophile, while $\mathrm { \bar { A } r g ^ { 9 1 } }$ , Ser128, Ser129 and $\mathrm { S e r } ^ { 1 3 0 }$ direct the $\beta .$ - and $\gamma$ -phosphates of ATP [84]. This arrangement of active site residues allows effective interaction between the substrates without the need for a direct involvement in acid-base or covalent catalysis [84]. The crystal structure of apo-CoaD from Mycobacterium tuberculosis [90] reveals that the enzyme is a symmetric hexamer in its apo form, but ligand binding is predicted to induce

conformational changes. The CoaD  $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine binary complex from Thermus thermophilus shows that the overall fold of the hexameric enzyme is very similar to that of $E .$ . coli, but $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine is bound to all six subunits [91].

The human phosphopanthetheine adenylyltransferase is fused to dephospho-CoA kinase in a bifunctional enzyme (see below) [67,92,93]. It does not share significant sequence similarity with its prokaryotic counterparts [13,38,67]. This fact and the availability of crystal structures make the bacterial CoaD an attractive target for antibacterial drug design.

# 2.12. Bacterial dephospho-CoA kinase

The gene encoding E. coli dephospho-CoA kinase (DPCK or CoaE), was identified as yacE by a BLAST search using the amino terminal sequence of the C. ammoniagenes CoaE protein [81,94,95]. The $E .$ . coli gene is renamed coaE, and several homologs from prokaryotic and eukaryotic genomes correspond to this sequence [13,38,94]. The E. coli CoaE protein utilizes ATP to phosphorylate dephospho-CoA at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyl group of the ribose moiety, the last step in CoA biosynthesis. The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values for dephospho-CoA and ATP are estimated at 0.74 and $0 . 1 4 \mathrm { m M }$ , respectively [94]. The enzyme was initially isolated as a $2 2 \mathrm { k D a }$ monomer, but sulfate ions promote the formation of trimers in solution and in the crystal structure [94,96]. Each CoaE monomer shows a fold similar to that found in nucleotide and nucleoside kinases containing the conserved P-loop (or Walker) motif $\mathrm { G X } _ { 4 } \mathrm { G K T } / \mathrm { S }$ [44]. The P-loop was predicted to bind the $\beta$ -phosphate of ATP [96] and this interaction is confirmed by the structure of the monomeric $H .$ . influenzae CoaE  ATP binary complex [97]. E. coli and H. influenzae CoaE sequences are $4 8 \%$ identical and have very similar structures despite a difference between their oligomeric states [96,97]. Superimposition of the two structures reveals the areas of sequence divergence including residues involved in the trimerization of the $E .$ . coli enzyme. The structure of H. influenzae CoaE reveals a three-domain organization that characterizes nucleotide and nucleoside kinases: the nucleotide binding domain, the substrate binding domain and the lid domain [97]. The substrate and the lid domains are very mobile and upon substrate binding they close over the catalytic site to direct the phosphate group transfer. Two prolines, Pro90 and $\mathrm { P r o } ^ { 1 3 4 }$ , are highly conserved and are probably essential for the lid flexibility [97]. Other conserved residues are $\mathrm { G l y } ^ { 1 4 }$ and Lys15 of the P-loop, $\mathrm { A r g } ^ { 1 4 0 }$ whose side chain interacts with the adenine base of ATP, Thr8 which is probably involved in dephospho-CoA binding, and $\mathsf { A s p } ^ { 3 3 }$ which is proposed to activate the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -OH group of the ribose for the attack on the $\gamma$ -phosphate of ATP [96,97].

# 2.13. Plant $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase and dephospho-CoA kinase

The Arabidopsis AtCoaD sequence was identified by its strong homology to the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase of the human bifunctional CoA synthase. The similarity of the plant enzyme to the $E .$ . coli monofunctional adenylyltransferase is low. Unlike AtCoaD, AtCoaE shares high degree of homology with bacterial CoaE and the least homology is observed with the dephospho-CoA kinase domain of the human bifunctional CoA synthase. The activity of both AtCoaD and AtCoaE has been confirmed in vitro [50]. AtCoaD is specific for $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine and does not accept $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantothenoylcysteine as a substrate [50].

# 2.14. $4 ^ { \prime }$ -Phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase and dephospho-CoA kinase in mammals

The bifunctional $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase/dephospho-CoA kinase (PPAT/ DPCK), also known as CoA synthase, is found on human chromosome 17 [67,92]. An unconfirmed monofunctional DPCK was identified by homology with the bacterial DPCK and is also located on chromosome 17 [67]. The human CoA synthase was identified independently using two distinct approaches in the same year, namely, a comparative genomics approach [67], and a more conventional approach where proteolytic fragments of a purified porcine enzyme were sequenced and oligonucleotide primers were designed to clone the cDNA [92]. The protein contains regions with sequence similarity to the monofunctional $E .$ . coli PPAT and DPCK [92]. The mouse CoA synthase homolog was cloned [93] and, when expressed in cultured mammalian cells, localizes to the outer mitochondrial membrane [93]. A hydrophobic segment on the amino terminus anchors the protein to the outer membrane of the organelle and the enzyme activity is stimulated by phosphatidylcholine and phosphatidylethanolamine, suggesting that these phospholipids may modulate the activity in vivo. The recent localization of the CoA synthase on the cytoplasmic aspect of the outer mitochondrial membrane [93,98] resolves a long-standing question about the compartmentation of the pathway which could not reconcile the cytosolic location of the upstream enzymes with the association of the CoA synthase within mitochondria [99,100]. The central domains of all of the proteins catalyzing PPAT activities, including those from bacterial and mammalian species, contain the conserved HXXH motif in combination with a Rossman fold which is characteristic of the nucleotidyltransferase superfamily [88,101]. A carboxy terminal truncation of the PPAT/DPCK bifunctional enzyme has only PPAT activity which proceeds to form dephospho-CoA. Using the PPAT peptide, the reverse reaction is roughly equivalent to the forward reaction, with similar $k _ { \mathrm { { c a t } } } / K _ { \mathrm { { m } } }$ values for both substrates and products in both directions [67]. However, the adenylyltransferase reaction of the intact bifunctional enzyme becomes irreversible when the dephospho-CoA intermediate is channeled to the phosphorylation reaction leading to the formation of the final CoA product. The estimated dephospho-CoA $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ for the reverse PPAT reaction is $1 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ whereas the $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ for the forward DPCK reaction is $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [92]. Taken together, these data suggest that the final step of CoA formation pulls the pathway to completion in mammalian systems.

# 2.15. Phosphopantothenate conversion to CoA in yeast

The yeast genome contains orthologs of all of the human CoA biosynthetic genes, suggesting that the pathway in yeast is similar to that in E. coli and humans [67]. An alternate CoA biosynthetic pathway is claimed to exist in S. cerevisiae [102] in which a $4 0 0 \mathrm { k D a }$ protein complex synthesizes CoA from the substrates L-cysteine, D-pantothenic acid and ATP, but the weak biochemical data are not supported by comparative genomics. In this complex, a $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ADP- $4 ^ { \prime }$ -pantothenic acid intermediate is proposed to react with the $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -amino group of cysteine to form $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ADP- $4 ^ { \prime }$ -pantothenylcysteine. The pantothenylcysteine would then undergo decarboxylation to yield dephospho-CoA, followed by phosphorylation by ATP to form CoA. A CoA hydrolase activity which catalyzes the hydrolysis of CoA to form $3 ^ { \prime } { , } 5 ^ { \prime }$ -ADP and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine, and a dephospho-CoA pyrophosphorylase activity is claimed to be associated with a $1 0 { - } 1 5 \mathrm { k D a }$ subunit of the complex. However, the genes in the yeast genome predicted to encode the PPCS

(YIL083C) and the PPAT (YGR277c) in the conventional pathway are found to be essential, as determined in a systematic gene knockout study (Saccharomyces Genome Database) [103]. These data argue that an alternative pathway for CoA synthesis probably does not exist.

# 2.16. Enzymes that degrade CoA

Relatively little is known about CoA degradation. In mammals, CoA can be dephosphorylated by a lysosomal phosphatase to dephospho-CoA [104] and both CoA and dephospho-CoA are cleaved by a plasma membrane nucleotide pyrophosphatase [105–108]. These activities are considered to be nonspecific, however, because of their localization in the cell, broad substrate selectivities and low affinities for unacylated CoA [6]. Indirect evidence suggests that degradation of the CoA moiety of acyl-CoA occurs largely outside of mitochondria in conjunction with increased fatty acid uptake [109,110]. The biochemical nature of the CoA degradation under these conditions is not known, although the previously described nucleotide pyrophosphatase [105–108] has a preference for acyl-CoAs as indicated by a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $8 { \ - }  { - } 9  { \mu }  { \mathbf { M } }$ . Acyl-CoA formation is linked to fatty acid uptake [111–113] and degradation of cytosolic acyl-CoAs may limit the accumulation of these molecules [6]. However, the phenotype of the transgenic mouse which overexpresses acyl-CoA synthetase in heart [114] argues that acyl-CoA degradation is not a regulatory process, since the hearts of the transgenic animals accumulate triglyceride, develop hypertrophy, left ventricular dysfunction and the mice experience premature death.

A peroxisomal CoA diphosphatase, also known as nudix hydrolase, is a candidate enzyme that degrades CoA and was recently cloned from S. cerevisiae [115] and mouse [116]. The yeast PCD1 nudix hydrolase is active toward CoA, with oxidized CoA disulfide being the preferred substrate. Nudix hydrolases are pyrophosphatases that hydrolyze nucleoside diphosphates linked to another moiety and are characterized by a highly conserved consensus sequence, $\mathrm { G X } _ { 5 } \mathrm { E X } _ { 7 } \mathrm { R E U X E E X G } \Phi$ , where $\Phi$ is usually Ile, Leu or Val [117]. The nudix hydrolases that recognize CoA also have a NuCoA motif which folds as a two-stranded hairpin followed by a $9 0 ^ { \circ }$ turn of the second strand at a conserved proline residue, typified by the structure of the enzyme from Deinococcus radiodurans [117]. The products of CoA hydrolysis are $3 ^ { \prime } { , } 5 ^ { \prime }$ -adenosine monophosphate and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine. The function of the Pcd1p protein in yeast may be to remove potentially toxic oxidized CoA disulfide from peroxisomes in order to maintain the capacity for $\beta$ -oxidation of fatty acids [115], or it may participate in the regulation of CoA and acyl-CoA levels in this organelle in response to metabolic demand [116]. The nudix hydrolases are a widespread family of enzymes that function to eliminate potentially toxic nucleotide metabolites and perhaps regulate their concentrations and availability [115]. A knockout of the gene in yeast or in mice would allow one to test the effect on CoA levels and determine if this hydrolase exerts some control over those reactions that utilize CoA.

The degradation of pantetheine to pantothenate and cysteamine, an anti-oxidant, is an important pathway for CoA metabolism [118,119]. This biochemical reaction is carried out by a family of glycosylphosphatidylinositol-linked, membrane-associated, extracellular pantetheinases called Vanins. There are three members of the Vanin family in humans (Vanin-1, -2 and -3) and two members in mice (Vanin-1 and -3). The most extensively studied member is the Vanin-1 gene that was identified as a gene highly expressed in the embryonic gonad [120–122]. Vanin-1 null mice develop normally, but tissues that would normally express Vanin-1 have a defective stress response

[118,123]. Vanin-1 expression is upregulated in response to oxidative stress [124] suggesting that Vanin-1 plays a role in the tissue response to oxidative stress through the synthesis of the antioxidant cysteamine. This metabolic pathway is a unique example of CoA-derived pantetheine functioning as an intermediate in the synthesis of another molecule and may explain the high turnover of CoA in some mammalian tissues.

# 3. Regulation of coenzyme A levels

# 3.1. Variation in cellular and tissue CoA content

The composition of the intracellular CoA pool in E. coli varies in size and composition depending on the carbon source for growth. The CoA pool is highest in bacteria growing on glucose, about $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , and acetyl-CoA is the predominant thioester [2,43]. In contrast, the amount of total CoA is much lower in bacteria growing on a mixture of amino acids derived from hydrolysed casein, about $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , indicating that CoA is important for the conversion of glucose to amino acids through the Krebs cycle. Consistent with this interpretation, when pantothenate auxotrophs growing on minimal medium with glucose as the sole carbon source are deprived of pantothenate, the succinyl-CoA pool becomes limiting for amino acid and protein synthesis, leading to growth arrest [125]. The size of the acetate pool (or carboxylic acid pool) plays a role in determining the amount of CoA in bacteria. When the acetyl-CoA pool is quickly and dramatically reduced in an acetate auxotroph of $E .$ . coli, the total amount of intact CoA drops correspondingly, with the excess CoA being hydrolyzed and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine effluxed out of the cell [87]. On the other hand, elevation of the intracellular CoA by overexpression of the CoaA enzyme in E. coli [39,126] causes an increase in carbon flux to acetate production, providing that enough supplemental pantothenate precursor is available [126]. Increased acetate production by the cell is not only reflected by increased acetyl-CoA, but also by increased extracellular acetate [127].

CoA accumulates to millimolar levels in S. aureus [128]. In addition to mediating metabolic cycles, CoA controls the intracellular redox state in the absence of glutathione and the associated glutathione reductase and peroxidase homologs which are lacking in this bacterium. The accumulation of CoA to significantly higher levels than in $E .$ . coli arises from the lack of feedback inhibition on the SaCoaA enzyme [48]. Bacillus cereus, B. subtilis, B. anthracis and Bacillus thuringiensis similarly lack the glutathione-mediated antioxidant system [129] and substitute a pantetheine- or CoA-disulfide reductase system. In addition to a redox role, protein-CoA disulfides in Bacillus megaterium accumulate during spore formation and contribute to the mechanisms that maintain metabolic dormancy [130]. Although there are reports of CoA:glutathione mixed disulfides in $E .$ . coli [131], these derivatives do not occur in vivo but rather arise from the oxidation of the sample following extraction [43].

Animal tissue levels of CoA cover a wide range and change in response to various extracellular stimuli, including hormones, nutrients and cellular metabolites. Hepatic CoA levels are among the most responsive to changes in metabolic state and range from 136 to $4 3 4 \mathrm { n m o l / g }$ tissue. Heart contains approximately $1 0 0 \mathrm { n m o l / g }$ tissue, whereas the contents of kidney, testes, diaphragm, adrenal and gastrocnemius muscle decrease in that order [6]. Levels of intracellular CoA increase in rat liver in conditions such as diabetes [132–135], fasting [24,132–135], treatment with hypolipidemic

drugs [10,24,133,136], and feeding a high fat diet [133,137]. Changes in the total amount of CoA can be accompanied by a redistribution of molecular species. For example, a shift from high levels of long-chain fatty acyl-CoA thioesters during fasting to very low levels during glucose refeeding, is accompanied by an inverse shift in unacylated CoA from low to high levels under the same conditions [137]. Total CoA levels decrease in response to insulin [138], and increase in response to glucagon or glucocorticoids [139], presumably reflecting a rapid change in PanK activity response, in addition to other factors. Tumors in animals exhibit low CoA levels [140,141] corresponding to increased glycolytic metabolism and less reliance on oxidative phosphorylation and the tricarboxylic acid cycle. Most of the available information on the CoA levels is not recent and further work is needed to characterize the CoA pools and identify the various PanK isoforms in responsive cell types to advance our understanding of the role for CoA regulation in various metabolic adjustments.

# 3.2. Compartmentation of CoA

Eukaryotic cells contain sequestered pools of CoA that are essential for activating carboxylic acid metabolites and the availability of intracellular CoA is limited by the membranes surrounding the mitochondria and peroxisomes. Cytosolic concentrations of CoA are estimated between 0.02 and $0 . 1 4 \mathrm { m M }$ in animal tissues, where it is used for lipid synthesis, microsomal fatty acid oxidation, protein modifications and membrane trafficking. Mitochondrial CoA concentrations are significantly higher and range from 2.2 to over $\mathbf { 5 } \mathrm { m M }$ , and peroxisomes are estimated to contain 0.7 mM CoA [133,142,143]. The total cellular long-chain acyl-CoA concentration is reported to be in the nM to $\mu \mathrm { M }$ range and varies significantly with different metabolic states. About $50 \%$ of longchain acyl-CoAs are associated with microsomes, $1 5 \%$ in the cytosolic fraction and only small amounts detected in mitochondria and peroxisomes [144]. The concentration of the individual CoA thioesters are tightly controlled due to the high biological activity displayed by some of them, including the regulation of gene expression [145], membrane trafficking [146] and modulation of ion channel activities [147]. The acyl-CoA binding protein in cytosol efficiently binds and buffers the free long-chain acyl-CoA concentration to maintain it in the low nanomolar range [148,149]. In mammals, mitochondrial CoA is used as a cofactor in the tricarboxylic acid cycle and fatty acid $\beta$ -oxidation and the concentrations of free CoA and its thioesters regulate the rates of these processes [150]. CoA also donates the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine prosthetic group to activate mitochondrial ACP which is involved in mitochondrial fatty acid synthesis, a process that resembles the type II system in prokaryotes and is linked to lipoic acid synthesis [151–155]. Peroxisomes play a major role in the $\beta$ -oxidation of very long-chain fatty acids and have high concentrations of CoA [133,156], most of which appears to be bound to matrix components [157]. The transfer of activated acyl moieties across organelle membranes, to and from the CoA pools in mitochondria and peroxisomes, is achieved through the carnitine system, consisting of the carnitine acetyltransferases, carnitine acyltransferases and acyl-carnitine carriers [158–160]. In yeast, CoA is ligated to long-chain fatty acids and transported into peroxisomes as the intact acyl-CoA by ABC transporters [161,162] which show extensive amino acid sequence similarity to the human ALD protein [163]. A correlation exists between changes in cellular CoA, long-chain acyl-CoA, peroxisomal $\beta$ -oxidation and cellular acyl-CoA thioesterase activity [144], and so acyl-CoA thioesterases could play an important role in maintaining sufficient CoA levels for optimal peroxisomal function

[144]. The concentration of unacylated CoA determines the rate of oxidation-dependent energy production in both mitochondria and peroxisomes, and the interorganelle transport of CoAlinked metabolites helps to maintain CoA availability [160].

CoA transport into mitochondria is mediated by a carrier-mediated transport system. In plants, CoA transport is sensitive to uncoupling agents and is temperature dependent, with saturation kinetics characterized with an apparent $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ and a velocity of $\mathrm { \bar { 5 } \ n m o l \ m i n ^ { - 1 } \ m g ^ { - 1 } }$ protein [164]. In rat heart, CoA first binds to adenine recognizing sites on the mitochondria and then CoA is transported into mitochondria by a specific, energy-dependent uptake process [165]. Uptake is sensitive to both the electrochemical gradient and the pH gradient across intact mitochondrial membranes [166], and disruption of these gradients causes net efflux of sequestered CoA [167]. Sulfhydryl reagents substantially inhibit CoA transport, and appear to affect the carrier directly rather than indirectly through disruption of the mitochondrial membrane potential [168]. The yeast mitochondrial carrier Leu5p, encoded by the yeast gene YHR002w, and its human homolog, Graves disease protein, are required for accumulation of CoA in the matrix [169]. Deletion of LEU5 is accompanied by a 15-fold reduction of mitochondrial CoA levels but does not affect the cytosolic CoA content. As a consequence, the activities of several mitochondrial CoA-dependent enzymes are decreased in the Leu5p-deficient cells which, as a result, exhibit a petite phenotype, i.e., they cannot grow well on most nonfermentable carbon sources [170]. These data strongly support the idea that mitochondrial CoA is transported from the cytosol to support mitochondrial function rather than synthesized from pantothenate in situ.

Human PanK2 localizes to mitochondria [55,56,58] and is proposed to initiate intramitochondrial CoA biosynthesis [58]. It was originally thought that the last steps in CoA biosynthesis catalyzed by the PPAT and the DPCK were located inside mitochondria [99,171]. However, more recent experiments demonstrate that both activities are part of a single bifunctional protein, also termed CoA synthase [67,92,93], that localizes to the cytoplasmic aspect of the mitochondrial membrane rather than inside this organelle [172]. These data agree with an earlier independent report that the PPAT and DPCK mitochondrial enzyme activities responsible for the synthesis of CoA from $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine are on the outer membrane [98]. Together, the available data argue against the hypothesis that a complete CoA biosynthetic pathway is located within mitochondria. On the other hand, a putative and discrete monofunctional DPCK (Accession Number gi13623688) is identified by homology searches [67] and may have a functional mitochondrial targeting signal. The expression of this gene may possibly account for DPCK activity that has been found in the intermembrane space, on the outer side of the inner membrane [98,99].

The endoplasmic reticulum is another compartment for CoA. Formation of $O _ { \ l }$ -acetylated gangliosides and other sugars occurs in the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum and an acetyl-CoA transporter is responsible for moving the substrate for the acetylation reaction from the cytosol into the lumen [173]. This transporter has multiple transmembrane segments and belongs to a superfamily of proteins that are widely expressed [174]. It is not clear how the CoA generated from the transfer of acetyl-CoA to sugar residues is returned to the cytoplasm.

# 3.3. Feedback regulation of pantothenate kinase

PanK is the primary rate-limiting step in CoA biosynthesis and is controlled by CoA and CoA thioesters, the end-products of the pathway. Feedback regulation of PanK by different CoA

molecular species controls overall CoA availability in response to a cells metabolic status. In E. coli, the CoA pool is made up of mainly acetyl-CoA, followed by nonesterified CoA, succinyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA. Long-chain acyl-CoA is not abundant. The total amount of CoA and the relative distribution of the CoA varieties are a function of the carbon source in which the $E .$ . coli bacterium is cultured [43]. A shift from glucose to acetate as the carbon source results in an overall reduction of the CoA pool size to about half, from 400 to $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , and is accompanied by an increase in the CoA:acetyl-CoA ratio [43] along with a decline in the ATP levels [175]. E. coli growing on glucose produces fifteen times more pantothenate than the amount actually utilized to synthesize CoA [9,18], illustrating that the CoaA enzyme limits the rate of pantothenate conversion into CoA. The excess pantothenate is released into the medium. The CoaA enzymatic activity in vivo is reduced in conjunction with the shift from glucose to acetate, and reflects the fact that the CoaA enzyme is less sensitive to inhibition by acetyl-CoA, the major CoA species in cells grown on glucose, and more sensitive to inhibition by nonesterified CoA, the major CoA species in cells grown on acetate. The lower sensitivity of the bacterial PanKs to acetyl-CoA allows the CoA pool to expand and accommodate the metabolic demands of cells dividing rapidly using glucose as a carbon source.

A single residue in the CoaA protein can be mutated to yield an enzyme, CoaA[R106A], that is refractory to feedback inhibition in vitro [86]. Cells expressing the CoaA[R106A] mutant protein at essentially single copy levels produce more than twice the amount of CoA synthesized by the wild-type protein when expressed in single copy in the coaA15(Ts) strain background which lacks pantothenate kinase activity at elevated temperature. This experiment was not possible until the structure of the CoaA protein was determined and the unique binding mode of the CoA inhibitor was revealed [45]. These data confirm the hypothesis that the biochemical feedback regulation of the enzyme activity is operating in vivo to limit the amount of CoA produced. Furthermore, the feedback inhibition of the CoaA enzyme by CoA is competitive with ATP binding at the active site [42,43,45]. Thus, the CoaA biosynthetic activity not only can be modulated in response to a shift in acetate flux to regulate the rate of CoA production [43], but also can be coordinated with the energy state of the cell, where a reduction in the ATP level would allow for more binding of the feedback inhibitor. Together, changes in the predominant CoA species and ATP levels function in concert to modulate the rate of CoA biosynthesis.

The SaCoaA has an atypical primary sequence, with little homology, if any, to $E .$ . coli CoaA and $1 8 \%$ homology with the eukaryotic PanKs [40]. The SaCoaA is not regulated by feedback inhibition by CoA or CoA thioesters [48] and, as a result, the intracellular CoA level is not limited except by the amount of input pantothenate [48]. Although this enables the bacterium to achieve very high CoA levels that function in maintaining the intracellular redox state, one disadvantage to unregulated CoA biosynthesis is depletion of the pantothenate supply [86]. Pantothenate depletion and reduction of the CoA level does not result in cell death, at least in E. coli [125], but rather the cells go into growth stasis, similar to the metabolic dormancy associated with reduction in the free CoA level in B. megaterium [130].

The PanK enzymes in eukaryotes are also feedback regulated by CoA and CoA thioesters. The A. nidulans PanK is selectively and potently inhibited by acetyl-CoA and palmitoyl-CoA in a competitive manner with respect to ATP [41]. The mouse PanK1 isoforms, mPanK1a and mPanK1b, are differentially regulated by CoA species. Both mouse PanK1 isoforms possess a common 363 amino acid catalytic domain. The mPanK1 $\beta$ is a smaller protein compared to the

mPanK1a isoform, with a 10-residue amino terminus linked to the catalytic domain [51,52]. The mPanK1b is stimulated by free CoA in vitro and inhibited weakly by acetyl-CoA [51]. These properties are intrinsic to the catalytic core, since the short ten amino acid extension at the N-terminus in $\mathrm { m P a n K 1 \beta }$ is unlikely to have significant impact on the overall properties of the protein. In contrast, the $\mathrm { m P a n K 1 } \alpha$ isoform is distinguished by a 184-residue amino terminal domain linked to the catalytic domain [52]. The addition of the $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ domain confers feedback inhibition by free CoA and long-chain acyl-CoA, with an approximate $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of $3 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for each, and increases the inhibition by acetyl-CoA to an $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of about $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and malonyl-CoA to an $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of about $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [51,52]. Thus, the $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ regulatory domain adjusts the basal response of the core protein to the composition of the CoA pool which, in turn, is determined by the extent and nature of the oxidative metabolism in the cell. The human PanK2 protein is very sensitive to long-chain acyl-CoA, acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA, with an approximate $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of $1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for each inhibitor, whereas unesterified CoA is reported to be much less effective, with an $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of $6 0 { - } 7 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [56]. The human PanK2 protein is localized and processed in the mitochondria and its sensitivity to the acyl-CoAs and CoA suggests that its activity is severely diminished under most metabolic circumstances. All of the other PanK enzymes are located in the cytosol [138,176,177] and thus cannot sense a significant proportion of the total CoA pool directly. Although the feedback responses of the other mammalian PanK3 and PanK4 isoforms have not yet been characterized, it is likely that these enzymes will also be sensitive to feedback inhibition by CoA and its thioesters.

# 3.4. Regulation by the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine adenylyltransferase

While primary control of CoA biosynthesis occurs at the CoaA step, secondary regulation at CoaD is evident from the release of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine from the bacteria to the outside medium [9,178]. The CoaD becomes more important when the regulation at the CoaA site is disrupted [86] or when the CoaA protein is overexpressed [39]. The amount of intracellular and extracellular $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine increases under both circumstances and reflects restriction of the rate of flux through the CoA biosynthetic pathway at the CoaD. The $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine cannot be transported back into the cells [178]. The CoaD protein is probably feedback regulated by free CoA [9,85–87], similar to the CoaA. This hypothesis is based on the time- and concentration-dependent correlation between accumulation of intracellular CoA and exit of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine from cells [9,87] and is supported by biochemical studies of the CoaD protein. When purified from E. coli, CoA remains bound to the CoaD protein in a ratio of 1 mole per 2 moles of protein [82], and a crystal structure of CoaD with bound CoA shows the inhibitor bound in the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine site [85]. Surprisingly, regulation at the CoaD step is not evident when S. aureus cells are radiolabeled with a pantothenate precursor [48], suggesting that the homologous protein in this bacterium is significantly different from the $E$ . coli enzyme. On the other hand, the mammalian CoA synthase, which includes both the PPAT and DPCK activities, appears to be feedback inhibited either by CoA or CoA thioesters [51]. The $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine pool is almost as high as the pantothenate pool in mammalian cells and when PanK1 is overexpressed, the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine pool increases almost 3-fold. Direct assessment of the inhibition of activity in vitro by different CoA molecular species has not yet been done.

# 3.5. Regulation by gene expression

Whereas feedback regulation of PanK enzymatic activity limits CoA biosynthesis, the level of coaA gene expression in $E .$ . coli and most bacteria sets the upper threshold for the intracellular CoA concentration. The coaA promoter has poor homology with the consensus $E .$ . coli promoter sequences and the coaA coding sequence is populated largely by low usage codons [39]. As a result, the bacterial CoaA protein is found at low abundance relative to the average $E .$ . coli protein. Amplification of the coaA gene on a multicopy plasmid in E. coli results in 76-fold higher enzyme activity and almost a 3-fold increase in the steady-state level of CoA [39]. Although the CoA level is increased in cells overexpressing the CoaA protein, feedback inhibition of the enzyme activity prevents the CoA concentration from increasing without limit. There is no evidence as yet for coaA transcriptional control in bacteria and these organisms appear to manage the CoA availability primarily by biochemical regulation.

In contrast, mammalian cells and tissues modulate PanK expression to modify CoA levels in the long-term response to diet and disease, but the mechanisms that govern these adaptive responses are largely unknown. Tissue levels of CoA change in response to chronic metabolic stress such as starvation, alcoholism, diabetes and tumor growth [6], in an effort to promote utilization of alternate fuel sources. CoA availability can limit metabolic substrate flux as exemplified by the effects of pantothenate deficiency. Extremely low CoA resulting from pantothenate deficiency in animal models and humans is associated with hypoglycemia, increased sensitivity to insulin, elevated serum triglycerides and hepatic steatosis [6,179–181], consistent with an inability to degrade fatty acids. Enforced overexpression of mPanK1b demonstrates the dramatic effect of gene expression on intracellular CoA levels, where the pantothenate supply is no longer in excess and a 13-fold increase in CoA content is observed [51]. These data suggest that tissues with higher PanK protein levels utilize pantothenate at a faster rate, in which case the exogenous pantothenate supply and transport would also play a role in determining the cellular CoA concentration. Increasing the exogenous pantothenate supply to heart tissue elevates cellular CoA content [182] where mPanK1a is expressed at a particularly high level [51].

The levels of liver PanK activity decrease following fasting and increase following feeding [139,183–188], increase during diabetes [134,135] and increase after treatment with hypolipidemic agents [187,189,190], suggesting that changes in PanK protein expression, as well as biochemical feedback regulation (discussed above), are involved in altering the physiological status of the tissues. One of the molecular mechanisms underlying the regulation of human PanK1 expression is mediated by the peroxisome proliferator activated receptor transcription factor, also known as PPARa [57]. Bezafibrate, a PPARa agonist and hypolipidemic agent, selectively stimulates transcription of $h P A N K 1 \alpha$ in cultured HepG2 hepatoblastoma cells, resulting in higher hPanK1 protein and enzyme activity. Bezafibrate also stimulates CoA and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine production in the same time frame, indicating that transcriptional regulation is one mechanism for controlling the CoA levels by the fibrate class of hypolipidemic drugs. CoA tissue regulation by PPARa-regulated $\mathrm { P a n K } 1 \alpha$ expression is consistent with the need to significantly increase the availability of CoA to support the accelerated processing of CoA thioesters through the $\beta$ -oxidation pathway. Analysis of the PANK1a promoter sequence using the TRANSFAC database [191] reveals the presence of additional binding sites for transcription factors involved in the regulation of carbohydrate and lipid metabolism, including a putative carbohydrate response element, a

sterol-regulatory element binding protein site (SREBP), and a glucose response element (GlRE) [57]. In particular, the potential regulation of PanK expression by glucose through the GlRE element and by lipids via the SREBP site are the most important aspects to investigate in light of the central importance of CoA in the utilization of these fuel sources for energy production and carbon flux for biosynthesis. The relative expression levels of the different mammalian PanK isoforms in different tissues is not known and also needs to be addressed.

# 3.6. Regulation by CoA degradation

CoA can be dephosphorylated to yield dephospho-CoA or hydrolyzed by cleavage of the phosphodiester bond to yield $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine and $3 ^ { \prime } { , } 5 ^ { \prime }$ -adenosine mononucleotide. Actual measurements of CoA turnover rates or investigation of the regulation of CoA turnover has not been done, however. An alternate to direct CoA degradation occurs through the transfer of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine moiety of CoA to carrier proteins, such the acyl carrier protein (ACP) of bacteria or the fatty acid synthase (FAS) in eukaryotes. The $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine is the prosthetic group that activates these proteins and enables them to form thioester linkages with carboxylic acids. Formation of the active holo-ACP is catalyzed by ACP synthase [192,193], and the prosthetic group is removed by a still uncharacterized ACP phosphodiesterase [178]. Formation of the active mammalian FAS is mediated by a cytoplasmic $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheinyl transferase with broad substrate specificity [194]. This mechanism provides a way to coordinate the level of active ACP or FAS with the level of CoA, and these proteins are present in considerably lower concentrations compared to CoA. ACP is the essential acyl-group carrier that interacts with the enzymes of fatty acid biosynthesis in bacteria and FAS is the multifunctional polypeptide that produces long-chain fatty acids from acetate and malonate in eukaryotes. The turnover of the ACP prosthetic group is four times faster than the rate of new ACP protein synthesis during recovery from CoA deprivation, and drops about an order of magnitude during logarithmic growth, when the CoA level is high [195]. The physiological role of this apparently futile cycle of holo-ACP synthesis and degradation is still unknown, but the process allows the phosphopantetheinyl prosthetic group to either re-enter the CoA biosynthetic pathway or exit from the cell and can regulate the intracellular CoA concentration. Mitochondrial ACP proteins with a $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine prosthetic group participate in mitochondrial fatty acid synthesis in fungi and higher eukaryotes [196,197]. Presumably the prosthetic group, which is derived from mitochondrial CoA [198], turns over and contributes to determining the mitochondrial CoA concentration, but experiments addressing this question have not yet been done.

A second regulatory mechanism that has been characterized in bacteria to some extent is mediated directly by a distinct phosphodiesterase [87]. The CoA pool responds to a rapid decrease in the acetate supply by dramatic reduction of the acetyl-CoA pool, transient increase in the free CoA, followed by CoA degradation to $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine which is effluxed to the culture medium [87]. Protein prosthetic group turnover does not appear to be involved in this very rapid adjustment of the CoA pool and recent data suggest that the nudix hydrolases may catalyze the reaction. Nudix hydrolases have been identified in bacteria [117], Caenorhabditis elegans [199], and in the peroxisomal compartments of yeast [115] and mouse [116], and not only hydrolyze CoA but also acyl-CoA thioesters, yielding either the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphopantetheine or the acyl-phosphopantetheine product, respectively [199].

# 3.7. Future developments in the investigation of CoA regulation

Research interest in the area of CoA metabolism has recently accelerated because of two major breakthrough discoveries. First, the cloning of the first eukaryotic pantothenate kinase from fungus [41] revealed a completely different protein sequence that was unlike the $E .$ . coli CoaA [39], but homologous with proteins encoded by several mammalian genes [51,52,54,200]. With this discovery came the realization that the CoA biosynthetic pathway, particularly the pantothenate kinase step, was a prime target for the identification and development of novel antibacterial drugs. The requirement for CoA in numerous metabolic processes makes it essential for bacterial proliferation in the host [1], and the dissimilarity between the bacterial and mammalian sequences suggests that unique inhibitors can be identified that would be effective against resistant microbial pathogens. The pantothenamide class of inhibitors is effective against the bacterial CoA biosynthetic pathway [47] due to metabolic conversion to the inactive ACP analog resulting in the inhibition of cell membrane biosynthesis [48,201]. The identification and cloning of the human CoA synthase [67,92] also reveals significant divergence from the bacterial CoaD orthologous sequence [82], indicating that this step in the CoA biosynthetic pathway would also be amenable to drug intervention. It is probably not coincidence that the two reactions catalyzed by PanK and the CoA synthase are the regulated steps in most bacteria and higher organisms, and these two steps are also represented by multiple genes or isoforms in mammalian species. There remains much to be learned about the tissue-specific functions and regulation of the various isoforms.

The second breakthrough was made when a human hereditary disease, neurodegeneration with brain iron accumulation (NBIA), formerly known as Hallervorden-Spatz syndrome, was mapped to the PANK2 gene [54]. This discovery revealed an unforeseen role for the PanK2 isoform in neurological function. Patients suffer from a progressive impairment of movement, speech and cognition [202,203] and mutations in the hPanK2 protein have been identified in a number of them [204–206]. The PanK2 protein localizes to mitochondria [55,56,58] but the role of PanK2 in mitochondrial CoA metabolism is unknown. Some of the clinical mutations render the PanK2 protein inactive, but others retain biochemical activity [56]. A loss of mouse PanK2 activity through gene disruption does not reproduce the neurodegenerative phenotype [207]. These collective results are puzzling in the context of what we know today about CoA biosynthesis and metabolism, and suggest that there may be alternative functions for the mammalian PanK proteins. The unique localization of the Fumble ortholog to the mitotic apparatus in Drosophila also suggests additional active roles for this protein in cellular homeostasis. Interest in the clinical manifestations of NBIA disease will continue to stimulate research in this area, broaden the scope of our understanding about CoA metabolism, and hopefully reveal the underlying physiological causes so that they can be addressed therapeutically.
