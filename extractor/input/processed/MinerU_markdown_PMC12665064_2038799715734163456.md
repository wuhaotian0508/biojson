# ABSTRACT

Salvia miltiorrhiza produces pharmacologically bioactive diterpenoid tanshinones which are regulated by jasmonate (JA) and abscisic acid (ABA) signalling, while their crosstalk in this process remains unclear. Here, we demonstrated that MeJA and ABA acted synergistically to enhance tanshinone biosynthesis. We reported that a novel bZIP transcription factor, SmbZIP5, was involved in ABA- and JA-induced tanshinone biosynthesis. ABA-activated SmSnRK2.6 kinase phosphorylated SmbZIP5 at four residues (S33, S127, S129, T171), enhancing its stability and transactivation activity. Conversely, the JA signalling repressor SmJAZ9 physically interacted with SmbZIP5, suppressing its transactivation activity. Overexpression of SmbZIP5 enhanced the tanshinone production and biosynthetic genes expression, while knockout of SmbZIP5 abolished ABA- and MeJA-induced tanshinone accumulation. Moreover, SmbZIP5 directly bound to the ABA-responsive element (ABRE) in the promoter of SmGGPPS1 and CYP76AK1. These findings suggest that a SnRK2-bZIP-JAZ regulatory module integrates ABA and JA signalling to coordinately activate tanshinone biosynthesis, providing novel insights into phytohormone crosstalk and a potential strategy for metabolic engineering in S. miltiorrhiza. 

# 1 | Introduction

Salvia miltiorrhiza (Danshen) is a valuable traditional Chinese medicinal plant widely used for treating cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases (Wang et al. 2017; Wei et al. 2023). The 

roots of S. miltiorrhiza accumulate tanshinones, which are diverse abietane diterpenoids including cryptotanshinone (CT), tanshinone IIA (TIIA), dihydrotanshinone (DHT) and tanshinone I (TI) (Jiang et al. 2019). These representative compounds mainly compose the bioactive ingredients of S. miltiorrhiza, and 

they have been reported to possess cardiovascular protective effects (Li et al. 2018), anti-tumour activity (Sun et al. 2022) and anti-oxidant activity (Lu et al. 2022). With the genome of S. miltiorrhiza sequenced (Xu et al. 2016; Ma et al. 2021), the complete biosynthetic pathway of tanshinones has been gradually enlightened. Geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP) is the precursor of tanshinones, which is synthesised by geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthase (GGPPS) (Kai et al. 2010). Then, GGPP undergoes cyclization and rearrangement to form miltiradiene, which is catalysed by CPP synthase (CPS1) and kaurene synthaselike (KSL1) (Gao et al. 2009; Cui et al. 2015). Recently, several CYP450 family members (CYP76AH1, CYP76AH3, CYP76AK1 and CYP71Ds) (Guo et al. 2013, 2016; Ma et al. 2021) and two 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases have been elucidated to participate in the downstream steps of the tanshinone biosynthesis pathway (Song et al. 2022; Pan et al. 2023). 

The adequate biosynthesis and accumulation of plant specialised metabolites are spatially and temporally monitored by a number of transcription factors (TFs). To date, studies have shown that the transcriptional regulation of tanshinone biosynthesis is regulated by different TF families, such as bHLH, WRKY, ERF and bZIP, which could act as positive or negative regulators in tanshinone biosynthesis. For example, SmbHLH10, SmERF1L1, SmERF128, SmERF73, SmWRKY1/2 and SmWRKY61 positively regulate tanshinone biosynthesis (Cao et al. 2018; Xing et al. 2018; Deng et al. 2019; Huang et al. 2019; Zhang et al. 2019; Zheng et al. 2021; Chen et al. 2022). SmbHLH3, SmbHLH92 and SmbZIP1 function as negative regulators of tanshinone biosynthesis (Deng et  al.  2020; Zhang, Lv, et  al.  2020; Zhang, Xing, et al. 2020). 

In the presence of ABA, the ABA molecule is recognised and binds to the PYR/PYL/RCARs (PYRABACTIN RESISTANCE/ PYR1-LIKE/REGULATORY COMPONENT OF ABSCISIC ACID RECEPTOR) namely ABA receptors leading to PROTEIN PHOSPHATASE 2C (PP2C) recruitment, which forms a PYR/ RCAR-PP2C complex and represses PP2C activity. Then, SNF1- related kinases 2 (SnRK2s) released from this complex to phosphorylate downstream TFs, activating ABA-responsive gene expression (Kobayashi et  al.  2005; Furihata et  al.  2006; Fujii et al. 2007; Miyazono et al. 2009; Nakashima et al. 2009; Cutler et  al.  2010). Previous studies have shown that ABA enhances the accumulation of artemisinin in Artemisia annua. A bZIP transcription factor, AabZIP1, connects ABA signalling with artemisinin biosynthesis, which activates the expression of ADS and CYP71AV1 to promote artemisinin accumulation (Zhang et al. 2015). It was reported that ABA promoted camptothecin synthesis in Camptotheca acuminata (Wang et  al.  2022) and increased the accumulation of anthocyanins in blueberry (Oh et  al.  2018). However, the molecular mechanism of candidate TFs in ABA-mediated tanshinone biosynthesis remains obscure. 

JA is a critical phytohormone in regulating plant secondary metabolism. In JA signalling, JA is conjugated with Ile to form the bioactive molecule, JA-Ile. Then, JA-Ile is sensed and perceived by the F-box protein CORONATINE IN SENSITIVE 1 (COI1), leading to the degradation of JASMONATE ZIM-DOMAIN PROTEIN (JAZ) and release of transcription factors to modulate JA-regulated defence and growth (Chini et al. 2007; Thines et al. 2007; Yan et al. 2007; Sheard et al. 2010). JAZ proteins are 

key repressors in JA signalling, interacting with and repressing the transcription factors activity by recruiting TOPLESS (TPL) corepressor or NOVEL INTERACTOR OF JAZ (NINJA) adapter protein (Pauwels et al. 2010). AaMYC2 interacts with AaJAZ8 to promote artemisinin accumulation by directly binding to and inducing the expression of CYP71AV1 and DBR2 (Shen et al. 2016). AaJAZ8 inhibits artemisinin synthesis by interacting with AaTCP14 and AaORA (Ma et al. 2018). JA promotes the synthesis of anthocyanins and proanthocyanidins in apples. Studies have shown that MdJAZ1 prevents the formation of a complex between MdTRB1 and MdMYB9, thus affecting the accumulation of anthocyanins mediated by JA (An et al. 2021). The interaction of JAZ and FIL/YAB1 can regulate the accumulation of anthocyanin (Boter et al. 2015). Moreover, JA regulates the biosynthesis of alkaloids in Catharanthus roseus (Patra et al. 2018) and tobacco (Shoji and Hashimoto 2011; Bian et al. 2022). JA also regulates the biosynthesis of triterpenes in sweet basil (Misra et al. 2014). 

In this study, we reported that simultaneous application of ABA and MeJA got a better effect on promoting tanshinone biosynthesis than either hormone. We identified that SmbZIP5 was phosphorylated by SmSnRK2.6 and promoted the accumulation of tanshinones. Moreover, SmbZIP5 also acted as a positive regulator in MeJA-induced tanshinone biosynthesis by interacting with SmJAZ9. Biochemical analyses revealed that SmbZIP5 could directly bind to the SmGGPPS1 and CYP76AK1 promoter via the ABRE motif and induced its expression. This study provides insights into the regulatory network of ABA and JA synergy in the regulation of plant specialised metabolites. More importantly, this study contributes to the molecular breeding of S. miltiorrhiza varieties with high tanshinone production. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | MeJA and ABA Synergically Promote Tanshinone Biosynthesis

Exogenous application of ABA or MeJA significantly enhanced tanshinone biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots, respectively. However, combined treatment with ABA and MeJA resulted in even higher tanshinone accumulation, indicating a synergistic effect on tanshinone biosynthesis (Figure  1a,b). Under co-treatment, cryptotanshinone (CT) levels increased approximately 28-fold compared to the mock control, whereas individual ABA and MeJA treatments led to 9.5-fold and 5.16- fold increases, respectively (Figure 1c). Moreover, the expression of several tanshinone biosynthetic genes was higher under the treatment of combined ABA and MeJA, compared with MeJA or ABA alone, especially SmHMGR1, SmDXR, SmGGPPS1 and SmKSL1, which were about 12.5-, 8-, 15.4- and 8.9-fold higher than mock, respectively (Figure  1d). These results suggested that MeJA and ABA synergistically promoted tanshinone biosynthesis.

Given that SmSnRK2.6 is a SnRK family member, we then tested whether SmSnRK2.6 directly phosphorylated SmbZIP5. The GST-SmbZIP5 and SmSnRK2.6-His recombinant proteins were expressed in Escherichia coli and purified, respectively. Then, an in vitro phosphorylation assay was applied with purified proteins and the phosphorylation of SmbZIP5 was detected with Phos-tag reagent. As shown in Figure 2e, a mobility shift was clearly observed corresponding to phospho-SmbZIP5 proteins when SmSnRK2.6 and SmbZIP5 were co-incubated with a time-course phosphorylation assay, suggesting that SmSnRK2.6 phosphorylated SmbZIP5. Furthermore, the phosphorylation of SmbZIP5 by SmSnRK2.6 was also detected with anti-phospho-Ser/Thr antibody (Figure  2e). To map the phosphorylation site(s) of SmbZIP5, the phosphorylatable SmbZIP5 was isolated by SDS-PAGE and analysed by liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS). Four phosphorylation sites (S33, S127, S129 and T171) were found in phosphorylatable SmbZIP5 protein (Figure 2f; Figure S1). To further confirm that these four phosphorylation sites were crucial for the phosphorylation of SmbZIP5, we mutated the S33, S127, S129 and T171 sites to positively charged alanine (A) to form SmbZIP5S33A, SmbZIP5S127A, SmbZIP5S129A and SmbZIP5T171A to mimic the non-phosphorylated state, respectively. The interaction between SmbZIP5 mutants and SmSnRK2.6 was not affected in Y2H assays (Figure S2). The migration of SmbZIP5S33A, SmbZIP5S127A, SmbZIP5S129A and SmbZIP5T171A were faster than SmbZIP5, indicating that these four phosphorylation sites played a comparable role for the phosphorylation of SmbZIP5 (Figure  2g). In addition, to identify that SmSnRK2.6 phosphorylated SmbZIP5 in plant cells, we co-expressed SmbZIP5-FLAG and SmSnRK2.6-GFP proteins in tobacco leaves. After total protein extraction, SmbZIP5-FLAG protein was immunoprecipitated and detected with anti-phospho-Ser/Thr antibody. SmbZIP5- FLAG was phosphorylated by the SmSnRK2.6-GFP in plant cells (Figure 2h). To confirm the four phosphorylation sites' crucial role, the phosphorylation of the SmbZIP5 quadruple-mutant was generated in which S33, S127, S129 and T171 sites were replaced with alanine at one time. We found that the quadruplemutant almost could not be phosphorylated by SmSnRK2.6 in  vitro and in  vivo (Figure  S3). Together, these observations showed that SmSnRK2.6 phosphorylated SmbZIP5. 

# 2.3 | SmbZIP5 Positively Regulates Tanshinone Biosynthesis

Tissue expression analysis revealed that SmbZIP5 was predominantly expressed in main roots, followed by lateral 

roots, with the lowest levels detected in flowers and leaves (Figure  S4a), which was consistent with the distribution of tanshinones, implying that SmbZIP5 has a strong relationship with the accumulation of tanshinones. Moreover, the expression of SmbZIP5 was induced by ABA, but not by MeJA (Figure  S4b). Subcellular localisation analysis showed that SmbZIP5 is located in the nucleus, consistent with its function as a TF (Figure S4c). 

Having ascertained that SmbZIP5 interacted with SmSnRK2.6, we speculated that SmbZIP5 may also play a positive role in tanshinone biosynthesis. To test this point, the SmbZIP5- overexpressing hairy roots were generated (Figure  3a). The transcript levels of SmbZIP5 in OE lines (OE-SmbZIP5-5, 7 and 22) were significantly increased compared with EV control lines (Figure S5). Metabolic content analysis showed that the content of tanshinones including CT, DHT, TIIA and TI in OE-SmbZIP5 lines elevated about 1.3–2.52, 1.16–2.87, 1.65–2.65 and 2.99–3.48 folds of the control lines, respectively (Figure 3b,c). Moreover, we found that the expression of tanshinone biosynthetic genes, including SmHMGS, SmGGPPS1, SmCPS1, CYP76AH3 and CYP76AK1 was significantly upregulated in the OE-SmbZIP5 lines (Figure  3d), suggesting that overexpression of SmbZIP5 enhanced the accumulation of tanshinones. 

To further dissect the function of SmbZIP5 in tanshinone biosynthesis, RNAi-SmbZIP5 transgenic hairy roots were obtained (Figure  4a). Ri-SmbZIP5-4, Ri-SmbZIP5-10 and Ri-SmbZIP5-11 lines with decreased expression of SmbZIP5 were chosen for further study (Figure S6). The content of CT, DHT, TIIA and TI in RNAi-SmbZIP5 lines was obviously reduced to 0.41–0.48, 0.34–0.57, 0.17–0.54 and 0.42–0.5 folds compared to the control lines, respectively. RNAi of SmbZIP5 significantly decreased tanshinone accumulation, as compared to that of the EV control (Figure 4b,c). The expression of SmHMGS, SmCPS1, SmKSL1, SmGGPPS1, CYP76AH1, CYP76AH3, CYP76AK1 and SmTIIAS in RNAi-SmbZIP5 lines was significantly decreased, compared with the control (Figure 4d). Above all, we revealed that SmbZIP5 was a positive regulator in tanshinone biosynthesis.

# 3 | Discussion

Salvia miltiorrhiza, commonly known as Danshen, is a prized medicinal plant extensively used in traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) and various therapeutic applications (Shi et al. 2016). Tanshinones are the primary bioactive constituents of S. miltiorrhiza, exhibiting diverse pharmacological activities such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant and cardioprotective effects (Li et al. 2018). JA and ABA are considered important inducers in the promotion of tanshinone production and the molecular mechanism of ABA or JA on tanshinone biosynthesis has been studied. The SmMYC2-SmMYB36 complex is involved in MeJAmediated tanshinone biosynthesis (Cao et al. 2024). SmERF73 is partly mediated by the jasmonic acid (JA) signalling pathway to regulate tanshinone biosynthesis via interaction with SmJAZ3 (Zheng et al. 2021). ABA-responsive transcription factor bZIP1 is involved in modulating the biosynthesis of tanshinones (Deng 

et  al.  2020). However, little is known about the interplay between ABA and JA on tanshinone biosynthesis. 

Previous studies have shown the crosstalk between ABA and JA in different plants. For example, in Arabidopsis, the ABA receptors PYL4 and PYL5 are induced by JA; the pyl4 and ply5 mutants showed a JA-hypersensitive phenotype (Lackman et al. 2011). In tomato, ABA induces the degradation of ERF.D2 to enhance the endogenous concentration of JA, thereby controlling drought tolerance (Zhu, Li, et  al.  2025). TaABI5, the core transcription factor of ABA signalling in wheat, interacts with the TaJAZ1 protein, thus regulating wheat seed germination (Ju et al. 2019). In rice, ABA promotes the biosynthesis of JA and inhibits seed germination through the SAPK10-bZIP72- AOC pathway (Wang et  al.  2020). Another study showed that the JAZs proteins were also negative regulators of ABA signalling and JAZs proteins integrated ABA and JA signalling to regulate seed germination by interacting with ABI3 and ABI5 (Pan et  al.  2020). AabHLH113 mediated JA and ABA signalling to regulate artemisinin accumulation (Yuan et al. 2023). To elucidate the molecular mechanism underlying the synergistic regulation of tanshinone accumulation by ABA and JA, we first examined the transcriptional responses of two core regulators, SmSnRK2.6 and SmJAZ9, to MeJA and ABA treatments. At the transcription level, SmSnRK2.6 was not induced by MeJA, and SmJAZ9 was not induced by ABA (Figure  S13). These results

Previous studies showed that SmJAZ9 negatively regulated JA-mediated tanshinone accumulation (Shi et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2023). Hence, we speculated that JAZs, the repressors of JA signalling, may associate with SmbZIP5. Subsequently, we found that SmJAZ9 interacted with SmbZIP5 (Figures S9 and S10), as no interaction was detected between SmJAZ9 and SmSnRK2.6 (Figure S14). SmJAZ9 suppressed the transactivation activity of SmbZIP5 (Figure 7a). These results comprehensively prove that SmbZIP5 is involved in the regulation of JA-mediated tanshinone biosynthesis. 

In view of previous studies that have suggested that SmSnRK2.6 was a key kinase in ABA signalling (Jia et al. 2017) and played a regulatory role in tanshinone biosynthesis (Liu et  al.  2024). We initially employed SmSnRK2.6 as bait to screen the cDNA library and found that SmSnRK2.6 interacted with and phosphorylated SmbZIP5 (Figure  2). Phosphorylation affects the function, subcellular localisation and stability of proteins. For instance, ABA promotes the phosphorylation of ZmbZIP29, ZmABI19 and Opaque2 through SnRK2.2, enhancing the transcriptional activation of these TFs and thus promoting the endosperm filling of maize (Yang et  al.  2022). Moreover, MPK8 phosphorylates bZIP68, enhancing its DNA-binding activity to the DREB1.7 promoter, which regulates cold tolerance (Li et  al. 2022). The phosphorylation of WRKY33 by CPK5/CPK6 enhanced the DNA-binding activity and the phosphorylation of WRKY33 by MAPK3/MAPK6 promoted the transactivation activity (Zhou et  al.  2020). Phosphorylation of OsbZIP86 (Gao et  al.  2022) and bZIP21 (Wu et  al.  2022) enhanced their DNA-binding activity and transactivation activity, respectively. Here, we found that the non-phosphorylatable SmbZIP5 protein bound to equal amounts of probes compared with the phosphorylatable SmbZIP5 protein in the EMSA assay, suggesting that the DNA-binding affinity of SmbZIP5 was unaffected by SmSnRK2.6-mediated phosphorylation (Figure 7c). Conversely, SmSnRK2.6-mediated phosphorylation regulated the transactivation activity of SmbZIP5 (Figure 7a). Interestingly, we identified four phosphorylation sites of SmbZIP5 that were located in the DNA-binding region (bZIP domain) (Figure S1), indicating that these four phosphorylation sites did not affect the DNAbinding activity, due to these sites being not located in the bZIP domain of SmbZIP5. We also observed that ABA promoted the phosphorylation of SmbZIP5 proteins and that the stability of SmbZIP5 was improved by ABA, suggesting that ABA-mediated phosphorylation of SmbZIP5 enhanced its stability (Figure 5). 

Given that SmbZIP5 interacted with SmJAZ9 and SmSnRK2.6 simultaneously, we tested whether SmSnRK2.6 and SmJAZ9 competed with each other for binding to SmbZIP5. We then divided SmbZIP5 into different truncated versions and independently fused them with the Gal4-binding domain to generate bait vectors (Figure S15). Deletion of the N-terminal 300 residues of SmbZIP5 (BD-SmbZIP5-C 301–392) eliminated its interaction with SmSnRK2.6, while deleting its C-terminal fragment (BD-SmbZIP5-N 1–300) had no effect. Contrary to SmSnRK2.6, deletion of the C-terminal fragment of SmbZIP5 (BD-SmbZIP5-N 1–300) eliminated its interaction with SmJAZ9, suggesting that the C-terminal of SmbZIP5 interacted with SmJAZ9 and the 

N-terminal of SmbZIP5 interacted with SmSnRK2.6, respectively (Figure  S15). To study the potential competitive mechanisms of the three proteins, luciferase complementation assays (LCA) were applied. The SmSnRK2.6-NLuc and CLuc-SmbZIP5 vectors were generated and introduced into tobacco leaves. The luciferase activity could be detected when SmSnRK2.6-NLuc and CLuc-SmbZIP5 co-expressed. Moreover, the activity was not affected when SmJAZ9 was also expressed, indicating that SmJAZ9 and SmSnRK2.6 do not compete for SmbZIP5 binding, suggesting independent regulatory mechanisms for JA and ABA signalling pathways through SmbZIP5 (Figure S16). 

Notably, SmbZIP1 was found to directly bind to the ABRE motif in the SmGGPPS1 promoter (the same cis-element recognised by SmbZIP5) but functionally represses SmGGPPS1 transcription (Deng et al. 2020). To find the relationship between SmbZIP1 and SmbZIP5, a phylogenetic analysis was conducted. SmbZIP1 and SmbZIP5 were divided into different subgroups in which SmbZIP1 (subgroup H) shared close homology with HY5 in Arabidopsis and SmbZIP5 (subgroup A) clustered with ABI5- like proteins from other species (Figure S17). Y2H assay showed that SmbZIP1 and SmbZIP5 did not interact (Figure  S18a). EMSA results demonstrated that SmbZIP1 and SmbZIP5 competitively bind to the ABRE cis-element in the SmGGPPS1 promoter. Intriguingly, SmbZIP5 exhibited higher binding affinity for the ABRE motif compared to SmbZIP1, suggesting a potential mechanism for its dominant transcriptional activation of SmGGPPS1 despite the repressive role of SmbZIP1 (Figure S18b). 

In summary, we found that SmbZIP5 positively regulated tanshinone biosynthesis by directly binding to the promoter of SmGGPPS1 and CYP76AK1 and activating its expression. ABAactivated SmSnRK2.6 phosphorylates SmbZIP5, enhancing its transactivation activity and stability, which promotes tanshinone accumulation. JA releases SmbZIP5 from the repression of SmJAZ9 to enhance tanshinone production. Together, SmbZIP5 integrates ABA and JA signalling to coordinately promote tanshinone biosynthesis. Our findings shed light on the mechanism of hormones associated with plant specialised metabolites and provide a powerful target for S. miltiorrhiza metabolic engineering and molecular breeding (Figure 7f).

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Treatment

For tissue-expression analysis, the roots, leaves, flowers and stems from 1-year-old S. miltiorrhiza plants which grew in greenhouse were sampled and frozen in liquid nitrogen (Deng et  al.  2019). The hairy roots used for hormone treatment were obtained from S. miltiorrhiza seedlings infected with Agrobacterium rhizogenes strain C58C1 (Shi et al. 2016). For hormone treatment, the fresh and newly cultured hairy roots grown on 1/2 Murashige and Skoog (MS) solid medium were selected and re-cultured with $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L } 1 / 2$ MS liquid medium for 50 days. Then, filter sterilised hormone stock solution $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ABA or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MeJA) was added to the hairy roots, and harvested after 3 days of induction (Shi et  al.  2016). Nicotiana benthamiana were planted with nutritional soil in pots and cultivated for 5 or 6 weeks before A. tumefaciens infection. 

# 4.2 | Quantitative Real-Time Polymerase Chain Reaction (qRT-PCR)

Total RNA extraction was carried out using the FastPure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme) and reverse transcription for cDNA synthesis was carried out with FastKing-RT SuperMix (TianGen BioTech) according to the manufacturer's instructions. ChamQ Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme) was used for qRT-PCR on the StepOne Plus Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems). The relative expression level was calculated with the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ algorithm. Primers used in this study were listed in Table S1. 

# 4.3 | Detection of the Tanshinone Content by HPLC

The 50-day-old hairy roots were fully dried in an oven at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Then, the dehydrated hairy root material was pestled into fine powder, and a total of $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ dried powder was extracted with $1 6 \mathrm { m L }$ solution containing methanol: chloroform (3:1 v/v). The extraction and concentration method of tanshinone, and HPLC detection protocols were conducted according to previous studies (Deng et  al.  2019). Four tanshinone standards (Yuanye), including DHT, CT, TI and TIIA, were preserved in our lab. 

# 4.4 | Generation of Transgenic Hairy Roots

In order to generate the RNAi-SmbZIP5 construct, a 200 bp specific coding sequence of SmbZIP5 was inserted into the pFGC5941 vector between the NcoI and AscI restriction sites. After that, the specific fragment was then reversely inserted into the BamHI and XbaI restriction sites of pFGC5941, which already contained the specific coding sequence. To generate the SmbZIP5-overexpression construct, the full-length coding region was ligated to the pCAMBIA2306 binary vector under the control of the CaMV 35S promoter, generating the 35S: SmbZIP5-FLAG construct. The knockout vector of SmbZIP5 was constructed using the CRISPR/Cas9 system, and sgRNA was designed and introduced into the pCAMBIA2300 vector. These constructs were transformed into the A. tumefaciens strain C58C1, and transgenic hairy roots production was based on our previous study (Shi et  al.  2021). After transformation, positive hairy lines were identified by PCR using genomic DNA as the template and followed by sequencing. All primers and gRNAs used in this study were listed in Table S1. 

# 4.5 | Yeast Two-Hybrid Assay

Matchmaker GAL4-based Two-Hybrid System 3 was used to perform Y2H assays according to the manufacturer's protocol. The full-length coding sequence of SmSnRK2.6 (namely SmAPK1, Zhu, Peng, et al. 2025) and SmJAZ9 (Shi et al. 2016) was cloned into pGBKT7 vector. SmbZIP5 and truncated SmbZIP5 were cloned into pGADT7 vector. Recombinants of SmSnRK2.6-pGBKT7 or SmJAZ9-pGBKT7 were transformed into yeast AH109 strain with SmbZIP5-pGADT7 or truncated SmbZIP5-pGADT7, respectively. These transformed yeast cells 

were cultured on SD/-Leu/-Trp solid medium plate and SD/- Ade/-His/-Leu/-Trp solid medium plate at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3–4 days. All primers used in this study were listed in Table S1. 

# 4.6 | Pull-Down Assay

The pull-down assays were carried out as reported previously (Liu et  al.  2023). The open reading frame (ORF) of SmbZIP5 was ligated into the pGEX-4T-1 vector to produce GST-tagged SmbZIP5 protein. The ORF of SmSnRK2.6 or SmJAZ9 was ligated into the pET32a vector to produce Histagged SmSnRK2.6 or SmJAZ9 protein, respectively. Then, these constructs were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells to induce protein production. For GST-SmbZIP5 protein production, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) and $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ were applied. For His-SmSnRK2.6 and His-SmJAZ9 protein induction, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG and $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ were applied. Recombinant proteins of GST-SmbZIP5 and His-SmSnRK2.6/His-SmJAZ9 were incubated with GST Mag-Beads (Sangon Biotech) at room temperature for 1 h, and then the beads were eluted for western blot analysis with anti-His antibody (TransGen Biotech). 

# 4.7 | Bimolecular Fluorescent Complimentary Assay

The BiFC assays were performed as described previously (Liu et  al.  2023). The CDS sequence of SmbZIP5 was amplified and ligated into the pXY106 vector between BamHI and XbaI restriction sites to generate the pXY106-nYFP-SmbZIP5 construct. The CDS sequences of SmSnRK2.6 and SmJAZ9 were amplified and ligated into the vector pXY104 between BamHI and XbaI restriction sites to generate the pXY104- SmSnRK2.6-cYFP construct and the pXY104-SmJAZ9-cYFP construct, respectively. These recombinant nYFP and cYFP vectors were individually transformed into GV3101 and coinfiltrated into tobacco leaves. After infiltration for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , the fluorescence was captured by a confocal laser-scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM-710). 

# 4.8 | Co-Immunoprecipitation Assay

Vectors of 35S:SmbZIP5-FLAG and 35S:SmSnRK2.6-GFP or 35S:SmJAZ9-GFP were co-transformed into tobacco leaves. After inoculation for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , the total proteins of tobacco leaves were extracted with native lysis buffer (Beyotime). Co-IP assay was performed according to the manufacturer's instruction. Total proteins were incubated with Anti-FLAG Magnetic Beads (Beyotime) for 3 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and then the beads were washed with PBS buffer. The protein samples were eluted for western blot analysis with anti-FLAG or anti-GFP antibody (TransGen Biotech). 

# 4.9 | Phosphorylation Assay

For in  vitro phosphorylation assay, $5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ purified recombinant protein GST-SmbZIP5 or different mutated versions of 

GST-SmbZIP5 (GST-SmbZIP5S33A, GST-SmbZIP5S127A, GST-SmbZIP5S129A and GST-SmbZIP5T171A) and $0 . 5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ purified recombinant protein SmSnRK2.6-His were co-incubated in kinase reaction buffer (20 mM Tris–HCl pH 7.5, 20 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 1mM DTT, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ ATP) at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h with or without phosphatase in a total of $4 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ and the reaction mixtures were added with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ $5 \times \mathrm { S D S }$ loading buffer and heated at $9 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min to end the reaction. To detect phosphorylated SmbZIP5 proteins, a phosphoprotein mobility-shift assay was applied by adding phos-tag (Wako) of $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \ \mathrm { M n C l } _ { 2 }$ in $1 0 \%$ (w/v) SDS-PAGE gel. The mobility shift was visualised by anti-GST (TransGen Biotech) monoclonal antibody. Moreover, the reaction mixtures were separated on common $1 0 \%$ (w/v) SDS-PAGE without phos-tag and subjected to immunoblot analysis with anti-phospho-Ser/Thr antibody. 

For in vivo phosphorylation assay, Agrobacterium strains containing the 35S:SmbZIP5-FLAG and 35S:SmSnRK2.6 constructs were cultured in YEB liquid medium overnight, and the cells were pelleted and resuspended in Agrobacterium induction buffer (10 mM MES, pH 5.6, 10 mM $\mathrm { { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone). Then, the suspensions were co-infiltrated into tobacco leaves with a 1-mL needleless syringe. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ infiltration, tobacco leaves proteins were extracted with lysis buffer (Beyotime) and SmbZIP5-FLAG protein was immunoprecipitated by FLAG beads (Beyotime). The immunoprecipitated proteins were released from the affinity beads by SDS-PAGE loading buffer and detected by immunoblot analysis with anti-phospho-Ser/Thr antibody. 

In order to explore the phosphorylation level of SmbZIP5 in hairy roots after ABA induction, the transgenic hairy roots that overexpressed SmbZIP5-FLAG were treated with or without ABA. Total proteins were extracted from these hairy roots with lysis buffer (Beyotime) and SmbZIP5-FLAG protein was immunoprecipitated. The immunoprecipitated proteins were subjected to phos-tag SDS-PAGE and detected by anti-FLAG antibody. 

# 4.10 | Cell-Free Degradation Assay

For the cell-free protein degradation assay, 30-day-old seedlings were harvested and ground to a fine powder in liquid nitrogen. Total protein was extracted in degradation buffer $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris– HCl, pH 7.5, 10 mM NaCl, 10 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5mM DTT and $0 . 2 5 \mathbf { M }$ Sucrose). After centrifugation (12 000 rpm for 30 min, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ), the equal amount of total proteins was incubated with an equal amount of recombinant GST-SmbZIP5 proteins at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for the indicated times. GST-SmbZIP5 was separated on SDS-PAGE and the residual levels of GST-SmbZIP5 were detected using a GST antibody. The IMAGE J software was used to test the abundance of protein. 

# 4.11 | Yeast One-Hybrid Assay

The Y1H assay was performed as previously reported (Shi et al. 2021). The full coding region of SmbZIP5 was inserted into the pB42AD vector between HindIII and XhoI as prey. The triple tandem copies of the ABRE motifs on tanshinone biosynthesis gene promoter were annealed and inserted into pLacZi2μ vector 

between EcoRI and XhoI as baits. The baits and prey were cotransformed into yeast strain EGY48 and plated onto selective medium (SD/-Ura/-Trp). And then, the yeast colony was selected and tested on SD/-Ura/-Trp medium containing $2 0 \%$ galactose and $2 0 \%$ raffinose, with 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-galact opyranoside (X-Gal). 

# 4.12 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

We performed a Dual-LUC assay as described previously (Shi et al. 2021). The promoter of the tanshinone biosynthesis gene was isolated and ligated into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector, generating a reporter construct. Then, the reporter construct was transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 (psoup). The full-length coding region of SmbZIP5, SmSnRK2.6 and SmJAZ9 was amplified and ligated into the pHB vector as effectors, respectively. Then, the effectors were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. After culturing overnight $( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 6$ for each strain), the cells were resuspended in Agrobacterium induction buffer and infiltrated into tobacco leaves with indicated combinations. With 2 days of infiltration, the leaves of tobacco were harvested, and the luciferase and renilla activities were measured (Promega) as described before with three biological repeats. 

# 4.13 | Luciferase Complementation Assay

We performed LCA assay as described previously (Chen et  al.  2008). The full-length coding sequence of SmbZIP5 was amplified and ligated into pCAMBIA1300-cLUC vector between KpnI and SalI restriction sites to generate 35S:cLUC-SmbZIP5 construct. The ORF sequence of SmSnRK2.6 was amplified and ligated into vector pCAMBIA1300-nLUC between KpnI and SalI restriction sites to generate 35S:SmSnRK2.6-nLUC construct. These recombinants were individually transformed into GV3101 and co-infiltrated into tobacco leaves. After infiltration for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , the luciferase activity was tested (Promega) as described before (Shi et al. 2022) with three biological repeats. 

# 4.14 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay

We performed the EMSA assay as previously reported (Shi et  al.  2021). The GST-SmbZIP5 protein was expressed in $E$ . coli BL21 (DE3) cells and purified with BeyoGold GST-tag Purification Resin. The phosphorylated GST-SmbZIP5 protein was obtained by inoculating native GST-SmbZIP5 protein with SmSnRK2.6 protein in kinase buffer for 1 h and then purified. The EMSA probes in the SmGGPPS1 or CYP76AK1 promoter were commercially synthesised with $3 ^ { \prime }$ end biotin-labelled and annealed to form the double-strand probe. We used the Chemiluminescent Biotin-labelled Nucleic Acid Detection Kit (Beyotime) according to the manual. Briefly, these purified proteins (SmbZIP5 or SmbZIP1) were inoculated with the binding reaction buffer for 20 min. Then, the mixtures were added with probes with or without competitive probes for another 30 min at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and the samples were subjected to $6 \%$ EMSA gel electrophoresis. After being electrotransferred to a nylon membrane, the shifted band was visualised with CCD. 

# 4.15 | Chromatin Immunoprecipitation

ChIP-qPCR assay was performed as described previously with minor modifications (Yin et al. 2020), using the EpiQuik Plant ChIP Kit following the manufacturer's instructions. Hairy roots of 35S:SmbZIP5-FLAG or 35S-FLAG were grown on halfstrength MS liquid medium for 30 days when the fresh weight was about 3 g. The crosslink was carried out by adding $3 7 \%$ formaldehyde in the hairy root medium to a final concentration of $1 \%$ for 15 min at vacuum and 2 M glycine solution was added to a final concentration of $0 . 1 2 5 \mathrm { M }$ for 5 min at vacuum. Then, the sample was washed with ultrapure water and ground into fine powder with liquid nitrogen for chromatin isolation. The chromatin was sheared by ultrasonication to produce the clipped DNA fragments of 200–1000 bp. The anti-FLAG antibody (Beyotime) was used to immunoprecipitate the protein-DNA complex. After DNA purification, the enrichments of the immunoprecipitated DNA from SmGGPPS1 or CYP76AK1 promoter were analysed by qPCR and primers used in this study are listed in Table  S1. Three biological replicates were performed.
