# Re-examining the role of the glyoxylate cycle in oilseeds

Peter J. Eastmond and Ian A. Graham

Oil is the primary seed storage reserve in many higher plants. After germination, this reserve is mobilized in order to support growth during early seedling development. The glyoxylate cycle is instrumental in this metabolic process. It allows acetyl-CoA derived from the breakdown of storage lipids to be used for the synthesis of carbohydrate. Recently, Arabidopsis mutants have been isolated that lack key glyoxylate cycle enzymes. An isocitrate lyase mutant has provided the first opportunity to test the biochemical and physiological functions of the glyoxylate cycle in vivo in an oilseed species.

Seed germination and post-germinative growth are crucial stages in the life cycle of higher plants1. During this period, the seedling must adapt its metabolic and developmental programmes to the prevailing environmental conditions to achieve photoautrophism before its nutrient reserves become exhausted1. Seeds contain large quantities of carbon stored as carbohydrate, lipid and protein2. From the onset of germination (radicle emergence from the seed coat), the seed storage reserves are converted to soluble metabolites that can be transported around the seedling and used to support growth and respiration2. Specialized metabolic programs have evolved to ensure the rapid and efficient use of storage reserves upon the initiation of germination.

The composition of seed storage reserves and the organ in which they are deposited differ depending on the species2. In many seeds, the major reserve is oil (Table 1). In these ‘oilseeds’, a specialized metabolic pathway called the glyoxylate cycle plays a central role in the use of stored oil3,4. A detailed knowledge of the steps involved in the breakdown of different storage reserves and the degree to which a given species is dependent on these reserves for seed germination and seedling establishment will be important to biotechnology. Many of the world’s major plant crops are grown for their seed. Modification of seed storage reserves to produce improved or completely new products is now a major target. Considerable progress has been made in this area, particularly in relation to novel oil production5. However, it is important that seeds with modified storage reserves remain viable. In this article, we review the state of knowledge about the glyoxylate cycle and highlight recent advances in our understanding of its biochemical and physiological function.

Glyoxylate cycle and oilseed metabolism

The glyoxylate cycle was first discovered in microorganisms, where it plays an essential role in allowing growth on two-carbon compounds such as

acetate and ethanol6. Pioneering research by Harry Beevers and co-workers showed that the glyoxylate cycle also exists in germinating oilseeds, where it is situated in specialized single-membrane-bound organelles called glyoxysomes3,4. In oilseeds, there is extensive conversion of storage lipids to soluble carbohydrate after the onset of germination7. Lipase catalyses the release of fatty acids from triacylglycerol stored within oil bodies. The fatty acids produced are activated to acyl-CoAesters and enter the glyoxysome, where they are broken down to acetyl-CoAvia the $\beta$ -oxidation spiral8. This acetyl-CoA then serves as a substrate for the glyoxylate cycle.

In essence, the glyoxylate cycle is a modified form of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle that bypasses the decarboxylative steps, allowing the net production of carbon skeletons, with no carbon lost as CO2 (Refs 3,6) (Fig. 1). Three of the five enzymes that make up the pathway are also found in the TCA cycle (citrate synthase, aconitase and malate dehydrogenase). The action of two additional enzymes unique to the glyoxylate cycle, namely isocitrate lyase (ICL) and malate synthase (MLS), bypass the decarboxylation steps of the TCA cycle (Fig. 1). Two moles of acetyl-CoA are introduced with each turn of the cycle, resulting in the synthesis of one mole of the four-carbon compound succinate (Fig. 1). Early studies showed that all the cycle’s enzymes are located in the glyoxysome, with the exception of aconitase, for which the evidence was inconclusive9. More recent studies using castor bean (Ricinus communis) and pumpkin (Cucurbita spp.) have shown that aconitase is cytosolic in germinating oilseeds10,11.

In the simplest scheme of the glyoxylate cycle, succinate passes from the glyoxysome into the mitochondrion and enters the TCA cycle, where it is converted to malate (Fig. 1). This malate is then exported to the cytosol in exchange for succinate and is converted to oxaloacetate by cytosolic malate dehydrogenase. Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) carboxykinase catalyses the conversion of oxaloacetate to PEP and this ultimately fuels the synthesis of soluble carbohydrates via gluconeogenesis4 (Fig. 1). Several studies have shown that, when the glyoxylate cycle is operative in germinating oilseeds, the activity of the decarboxylative steps of the TCA cycle are suppressed, favouring the synthesis of carbohydrate over respiration12,13 (Fig. 1).

The glyoxylate cycle can also be represented by a different scheme in which the requirements for co-factors are balanced14. In this modification, a malate–aspartate shuttle operates between the mitochondria and glyoxysome to recycle reducing equivalents. This shuttle results in the production of NAD+ through the operation of glyoxysomal malate dehydrogenase14. Recent work in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae has shown that a mutation in peroxisomal malate dehydrogenase impairs fatty acid $\beta$ -oxidation, suggesting that the enzyme also provides NAD+ for this pathway15. Plant glyoxysomal malate dehydrogenase might play a similar role16.

Glyoxylate cycle as an anaplerotic pathway The provision of carbon skeletons for carbohydrate synthesis is not the only proposed function of the glyoxylate cycle. This pathway has also been suggested to have an anaplerotic role (after the Greek word that means ‘filling up’) in

microorganisms6 and plants17. To understand this role, it is first necessary to examine TCA cycle metabolism. In addition to being the principal pathway of oxidative metabolism and energy production in the cell, the TCA cycle also provides intermediates for the biosynthesis of various compounds, including amino acids, purines, pyrimidines, haem and chlorophyll (Fig. 1). Because biosynthetic pathways constantly draw carbon away from the TCA cycle, the operation of the cycle would be impaired were it not for processes that replenish the intermediates. These processes have been termed anaplerotic pathways.

In plants, PEP carboxylase is the primary anaplerotic enzyme during photoautotrophic growth, catalysing the production of oxaloacetate from the glycolytic intermediate PEP (Fig. 1). However, when only two-carbon compounds such as ethanol, acetate or acetyl-CoA are available for oxidative metabolism, the irreversible nature of the pyruvate kinase reaction prevents the net conversion of acetyl-CoA to PEP (Fig. 1). In this situation, glycolytic intermediates are not available to serve as anaplerotic substrates and the glyoxylate cycle could perform this essential function through the net production of succinate from acetyl-CoA.

Both ICL and MLS are single-copy genes in Arabidopsis, which simplifies the interpretation of mutant phenotypes. # Conversion of lipid to carbohydrate

Early experiments using castor bean endosperm suggested that the glyoxylate cycle enzymes must be essential to account for the conversion of lipid to sugar4,9. Analysis of Arabidopsis icl mutants has shown that the net conversion of acetate to carbohydrate is blocked during post-germinative growth, confirming that the glyoxylate cycle is an absolute requirement for this metabolic process in plants18. Furthermore, in vitro experiments conducted on castor bean endosperm suggest that ICL plays an important role in controlling the rate of flux of carbon from acetate to sugar in this tissue20. Interestingly, alternative pathways for growth on two-carbon compounds have been proposed in some fungi21 but, on the basis of the above evidence, these are unlikely to exist in higher plants.

# Lipid asarespiratory substrate

Generally, lipids are believed not to be a significant respiratory substrate in higher plants22. However, it has been suggested that lipids can be respired in some oilseeds6,23,24. This ability to respire lipids appears to relate to the physiology of the oilseed species. Some oilseeds (e.g. castor bean) store oil in the endosperm (Table 1). This tissue senesces after germination and essentially all the carbon stored within must be exported to the growing seedling in the form of carbohydrate4. The demand for respiration in this tissue is therefore minimal. By contrast, many oilseeds, including Arabidopsis (Table 1), store oil within the cotyledons of the embryo. These tissues are transformed into photosynthetic organs after germination. In these ‘cotyledonary’ oilseeds, there is more demand for carbon skeletons to support respiration and biosynthesis in the storage tissue and less demand for their intercellular transport.

When Arabidopsis icl mutant seedlings are grown in the dark, lipid breakdown and hypocotyl growth are strongly inhibited18. However, the addition of exogenous sugars allows lipid breakdown to proceed normally and rescues growth. The capacity of Arabidopsis glyoxylate cycle mutants to catabolize lipids efficiently without the net synthesis of carbohydrates strongly implies that two-carbon compounds produced in the glyoxysome are rapidly respired18. It is not yet clear how carbon skeletons from lipid breakdown are made available for respiration in plants. Recent work has shown that the glyoxylate cycle is not required for growth of S. cerevisiae on fatty acids15. Here, two-carbon units are transferred from the peroxisome to the mitochondrion via an acetyl-carnitine shuttle, where they are used to drive respiration15,25. A mechanism similar to that in S. cerevisiae might operate in Arabidopsis (Fig. 3). Alternatively, work using germinating sunflower (Helianthus annuus) and lettuce (Lactuca sativa) seed suggests that citrate and oxaloacetate might shuttle between the glyoxysome and mitochondrion, allowing a TCA cycle to operate spanning the two organelles23,24 (Fig. 3). The movement of citrate out of the glyoxysome is necessary for glyoxylate cycle function because aconitase is cytosolic10,11. This process could therefore account for the transfer of carbon skeletons to the mitochondrion in Arabidopsis icl mutant seedlings.

Physiological significance

Surprisingly, in spite of being incapable of using stored lipid reserves for the synthesis of carbohydrate,

Arabidopsis icl and mls mutant seeds germinate and become photoautotrophic when grown on agar plates18,19 or on soil in the glasshouse (Fig. 4). The apparent redundancy of the glyoxylate cycle in

Arabidopsis cannot be explained solely by the existence of non-lipid seed-storage reserves (Table 1). Disruption of fatty acid $\beta$ -oxidation leads to an arrest in

Arabidopsis seedling development immediately after germination26, proving that lipid reserves are essential for seedling establishment (Fig. 4). The fact that Arabidopsis seedlings can respire lipids is therefore crucial for explaining why the glyoxylate cycle is redundant in this species whereas $\beta$ -oxidation is not.

Although the glyoxylate cycle can be described as being non-essential in Arabidopsis, it does play an important physiological role because the survival rate of icl mutant seedlings declines when they are grown in suboptimal light conditions18. In nature, environmental

conditions are rarely optimal. Areduction in the level or duration of available light during early postgerminative growth is sufficient to reduce the frequency of seedling establishment18. After prolonged darkness, Arabidopsis icl mutant seedlings cannot recover when transferred to the light, even though they retain a respiratory carbon source in the form of lipid reserves18. Presumably, non-lipid-derived storage reserves are rapidly depleted and, once they are exhausted, the seedling lacks both anaplerotic and gluconeogenic substrates to gain photosynthetic competence.

By contrast, when sufficient light is available early in post-germinative growth, non-lipid reserves are just sufficient to allow development of the photosynthetic apparatus in the icl mutant. This is the case provided that they are primarily used for biosynthetic and anaplerotic processes, and not for respiration, as must be the case in $\beta$ -oxidation mutants. However, in endospermic oilseeds, the glyoxylate cycle is likely to be essential during post-germinative growth regardless of light conditions because lipid must first be converted to carbohydrate and exported before it can serve as a source of carbon skeletons for respiration and biosynthesis in growing tissue.

Regulation of glyoxylate cycle gene expression The co-ordinate expression of the genes encoding ICL and MLS is controlled transcriptionally during the germination and post-germinative growth of oilseeds27,28. These genes are also induced during pollen development29, embryogenesis27,30 and senescence of cotyledons and leaves31. The induction during foliar senescence is consistent with the proposal that a functional glyoxylate cycle operates to enable the mobilization of the abundant thylakoid membrane lipids that are broken down at this time31.

ICL and MLS enzyme activities can be induced in Pimpinella anisum cell cultures when sucrose is removed from the medium32,33. This, combined with the discovery that ICL and MLS expression is not confined to germinating oilseeds, led to the hypothesis that ICL and MLS expression could be regulated by metabolites in higher plants34. Feeding various hexose sugars and sugar analogues to cucumber cell cultures and mesophyll protoplasts confirmed this hypothesis and implicated hexokinase in the sugar-mediated repression of ICL and MLS (Refs 17,34). The expression of these genes is also induced in detached cotyledons and in vivo in cucumber roots in response to shading or defoliation of the shoot35,36. This induction is blocked by exogenous sucrose35,36. Deletion analysis of the promoter regions of the cucumber ICL and MLS genes has identified separate conserved sequence elements that are necessary for induction during germination and in response to altered carbohydrate status37–39.

# Future prospects

Recent genetic studies have confirmed that the glyoxylate cycle plays an important role in both

gluconeogenesis and anaplerotic processes during germination and post-germinative growth of oilseeds18. However, seedling establishment and the efficient respiration of lipids in Arabidopsis icl mutant seedlings show that, under favourable growth conditions, the pathway is redundant18. It is possible that this observation could be extended to other cotyledonary oilseeds (Table 1). Whether the glyoxylate cycle is essential to endospermic oilseeds remains to be formally determined. However, the absolute requirement for this pathway for the net conversion of lipid to sugars suggests that this is the case18.

The pathway by which carbon derived from two-carbon compounds is transferred from the glyoxysome to the mitochondrion in Arabidopsis is not known. The isolation of mutants disrupted in glyoxysomal citrate synthase should clarify whether a mechanism similar to that operating in S. cereviseae25 or that proposed in sunflower and lettuce23,24 is present, because only the second process would require this enzyme. To address the mechanism by which $\mathrm { N A D ^ { + } }$ is regenerated for the glyoxylate cycle and fatty acid $\beta$ -oxidation, it will be useful to identify mutants in glyoxysomal malate dehydrogenase, because this enzyme has been proposed to play a central role in both processes14,15.

In addition to its role in post-germinative growth, the glyoxylate cycle is also expressed in other tissues such as senescing leaves, pollen, developing embryos and various tissues that are deprived of carbohydrate29–36. Arabidopsis icl mutants lack a visible phenotype throughout their life cycle when grown in the glasshouse18. Although there is no obvious morphological phenotype, there might be perturbation of metabolism. An investigation of the biochemical phenotype of the glyoxylate cycle mutants using approaches such as metabolite profiling should help to establish whether this is the case.

The induction of glyoxylate cycle gene expression during germination is probably part of an overall metabolic programme that enables the rapid mobilization of storage reserves. Genes encoding enzymes of fatty acid $\beta$ -oxidation42–44 and gluconeogenesis45 are induced co-ordinately with those of the glyoxylate cycle18 (Fig. 2). The identity of the signal(s) that induces all these genes is not known. Work carried out in cereals clearly shows that gibberellic acid and abscisic acid play important roles in the regulation of genes involved in storage reserve mobilization46. Although these phytohormones also control germination in Arabidopsis1, it has not yet been clearly established whether or not they also regulate genes involved in lipid mobilization. The precise mechanism by which metabolic signals control lipid breakdown is also unknown. The use of promoter–reporter gene fusions and transgene screening of mutant seedling populations derived from such lines47 might offer one way to dissect the signal transduction pathways involved in glyoxylate cycle regulation.
