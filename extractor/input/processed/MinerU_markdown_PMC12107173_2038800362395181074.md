# A vetiver-specific terpene synthase VzTPS9 contributes to the high attractiveness of vetiver to rice stem borer

Qiang Lia,b,1, Kang Hea,1, Yanhui Lub , Bingbing Hea , Xusong Zhengb , Zhongxian Lub,2, Fei Lia,c,2 , and Hongxing Xub,2

Vetiver (Vetiveria zizanioides) is highly attractive to the rice stem borer (Chilo suppressalis, RSB) and is widely utilized as a trap plant for RSB control in East Asia. However, the underlying mechanism driving this high level of attractiveness remains unclear. In this study, we identified volatiles emitted by vetiver using SPME/GC–MS and found that cedrol constitutes $1 2 . 1 5 \%$ of the total volatile profile. Both Y-tube olfactometer and electroantennography assays revealed that cedrol is highly attractive to female RSB moths at a concentration of $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mu \mathrm { L }$ . To investigate the mechanism responsible for the high level of cedrol in vetiver, we sequenced and assembled a chromosome-level genome of vetiver, identifying a vetiver-specific terpene synthase, VzTPS9, which is responsible for the synthesis of cedrol from farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP). Subsequently, we constructed a transgenic rice line by integrating $V z T P S 9$ into the rice genome. Enzyme assays and gene expression analyses demonstrated that the transgenic rice produced higher levels of cedrol, which were positively correlated with $V z T P S 9$ expression levels, and consequently, with increased attractiveness to female RSB moths. These findings suggest that increased expression of $V z T P S 9$ in vetiver leads to elevated cedrol synthesis, contributing to its enhanced attractiveness to RSB. This work uncovers the molecular mechanism behind vetiver’s high attractiveness to RSB and provides valuable insights for developing more effective strategies for utilizing vetiver as a trap plant in RSB control.

Vetiver (Vetiveria zizanioides ), native to Thailand and Java, is renowned for its aromatic roots, which are commonly used to extract essential oils for perfumes ( 1 ). In its native regions, vetiver has also been employed in various traditional applications, such as weaving straw mats and constructing thatched houses, contributing to a fragrant and insect-repellent environment ( 2 ). In 1988, vetiver was introduced to China as part of the World Bank’s South China Red Soil Development Project. Its ability to conserve water and adsorb heavy metals made it a valuable tool in preventing soil erosion during the early stages of red soil reclamation ( 3 ). 

Surprisingly, upon its introduction to China, vetiver was observed to attract lepidopteran pests, including Chilo suppressalis (rice stem borer, RSB) and Chilo partellus . For example, C. partellus preferred to lay eggs on vetiver rather than maize ( 4 ), and RSB deposited up to four times more eggs on vetiver than on rice ( 5 ). However, because vetiver is not a native host plant for RSB, it does not provide an ideal food source for RSB larvae due to its nutrient-poor nature. RSB larvae feeding exclusively on vetiver experience nutritional imbalances. Moreover, the toxic compounds in vetiver inhibit the activity of detoxifying enzymes in the larvae, such as cytochrome P450 and carboxylesterase, leading to larval mortality. As a result, most larvae that feed on vetiver fail to complete their life cycle and die during the second or third instar ( 4 , 6 , 7 ). While vetiver negatively impacts RSB larvae, it is worth noting that the pest does not naturally feed on vetiver in its native habitat. The combination of high attractiveness and incomplete larval development makes vetiver an effective trap plant for pest control. For instance, in Zhejiang Province, China, planting vetiver along field ridges reduced rice damage caused by RSB by more than $5 0 \%$ , and RSB populations decreased by over $7 0 \%$ ( 8 ). Despite these promising outcomes, the molecular mechanism behind the strong attraction of vetiver to RSB remains poorly understood. 

Given the significant role of plant volatiles in insect attraction, we hypothesized that the volatiles emitted by vetiver contribute to its high attractiveness to RSB. To test this, we first identified the volatile compounds released by vetiver and found that cedrol accounted for $1 2 \%$ of the total volatile profile. # Significance

Vetiver (Vetiveria zizanioides ), native to Thailand and Java, is highly attractive to the rice stem borer (Chilo suppressalis , RSB), a major rice pest in Asia. As a result, it is widely used as an effective trap plant for controlling RSB in rice-producing regions. We identify cedrol, a terpenoid volatile, as the key attractant and, through vetiver genome analysis, pinpoint a vetiver-specific terpene synthase gene, VzTPS9 , responsible for the high production of cedrol. Transgenic rice expressing VzTPS9 also produces more cedrol, mimicking vetiver’s enhanced attraction to RSB. This work highlights the role of VzTPS9 in cedrol production and offers different strategies for utilizing vetiver as an effective pest control tool.

To investigate the basis for the high concentrations of cedrol in vetiver, we sequenced and assembled a chromosomelevel genome of vetiver and identified the biosynthetic pathway responsible for cedrol production. The vetiver genome has a total size of 894.8 Mb, with a scaffold N50 of 82.3 Mb (SI Appendix, Table  S1). Hi-C scaffolding successfully anchored the genome 

sequences to 10 chromosomes (9), yielding a high-quality genome suitable for downstream analyses. Repeat masking identified 663.4 Mb of repeat sequences, accounting for $7 4 . { \dot { 1 } } 4 \%$ of the total genome. Genome annotation predicted 49,301 proteincoding genes, along with 1,112 miRNAs and 685 transfer RNAs (SI Appendix, Table S1 and Fig. S2).

VzTPS9: A Vetiver-Specific TS with High Enzyme Activity. Following the synthesis of prenyl pyrophosphates, TPSs catalyze the conversion of these intermediates into terpenoids. In this study, we identified 53 TPS genes in vetiver (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S10). Phylogenetic analysis revealed that several adjacent TPS genes on the chromosomes are closely related, suggesting a common origin from one or more ancestral TPS genes. These 53 TPS genes were grouped into five distinct clades, with the TPS-b clade being specific to vetiver, containing two genes: VzTPS9 and VzTPS10. Interestingly, both Nipponbare and Minghui 63 rice strains lack these two TPS genes (Fig.  3). Additionally, vetiver exhibits a reduced number of genes in the TPS-e/f clade but a higher gene count in the TPS-a and TPS- $^ { \cdot \mathrm { g } }$ clades. Transcriptomic analysis and qRT-PCR revealed that VzTPS9 and VzTPS10 are highly expressed in vetiver leaves (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). 

To further investigate the roles of VzTPS9 and VzTPS10 in synthesizing attractive volatiles, we synthesized the coding DNA sequences of $V z T T P S \mathcal { G }$ and VzTPS10 , optimized their codons, and cloned them into Escherichia coli for prokaryotic expression. The purified proteins were then subjected to in vitro enzyme assays using GPP, FPP, and GGPP as substrates. The reaction products were analyzed by SPME/GC–MS. Comparison of the resulting spectra with mass spectrometry libraries and standard retention times revealed that VzTPS9 utilizes FPP to produce both linalool and cedrol, while VzTPS10 did not produce any detectable terpenoids from these substrates ( Fig. 4 and SI Appendix, Fig. S12 ). To explore the evolutionary origins of $V z T T S 9$ and identify additional potential trap plants, we conducted an orthologous gene analysis and constructed an evolutionary tree. The results revealed that the orthologs of $V z T P S 9$ are distributed across 20 species from six subfamilies. Notably, the TPSs from the Panicoideae subfamily were most closely related to VzTPS9 , including Panicum halli , Panicum miliaceum , Digitaria exilis , M. lutarioriparius , Panicum virgatum , and S. bicolor (SI Appendix, Fig. S13 ). Further validation is required to determine whether these species can attract the RSB.

# Discussion

Although vetiver has long been recognized as an effective trap plant for controlling RSB in rice-growing regions across East Asia, the molecular basis of its strong attractiveness has remained unclear for decades. In this study, we identified cedrol, a major volatile emitted by vetiver, as a key factor driving this attraction ( Fig. 5F ). Cedrol, a sesquiterpene commonly found in woody plant families such as Cupressaceae, Taxaceae, and Pinaceae ( 10 – 12 ), has been widely utilized in medicine for its anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and sedative properties ( 13 ). In pest control, cedrol has been shown to repel Blacklegged ticks (Ixodes scapularis ) ( 14 ) while attracting African malaria mosquitoes (Anopheles gambiae ) for oviposition ( 15 ). 

To elucidate how vetiver produces high levels of cedrol, we generated a chromosome-level genome assembly and reconstructed the complete MVA and MEP pathways responsible for terpenoid biosynthesis. Comparative analysis with related plants led to the identification of a vetiver-specific gene, VzTPS9 , belonging to the TPS-b clade, which catalyzes the conversion of FPP into cedrol. Notably, vetiver is not a native host plant for RSB and was initially introduced to nonnative regions for soil and water conservation. This is a reported case of a volatile compound from a 

nonnative plant exhibiting strong attractiveness to a major insect pest. The high-quality genome assembly provides insights into the emergence of a vetiver-specific TS responsible for producing a key volatile. The finding that vetiver’s ability to trap RSB may have been an incidental outcome that underscores the potential 

for using genome-guided approaches to identify new trap plants for pest control. 

Further exploration of the evolutionary history of $V z T T S 9$ and its orthologs may reveal additional effective trap plants, warranting further investigation. Our evolutionary analysis identified $V z T P S \bar { 9 }$ orthologs across multiple species within the Panicoideae subfamily, suggesting a broader distribution of cedrol biosynthesis potential. However, whether these species also possess the ability to attract RSB remains to be determined and requires empirical validation. 

Despite vetiver’s demonstrated success as a trap plant in rice fields, cedrol alone exhibits limited attractiveness to RSB, implying that additional volatiles contribute to its overall effect. In this study, we focused on the three most abundant volatiles emitted by vetiver, identifying cedrol—the third-most abundant—as the only compound with direct attractiveness. However, the potential roles of less abundant volatiles remain unexplored and warrant further investigation. Moreover, the combinatory effects of volatile blends should be examined, as previous studies have shown that mixtures often elicit stronger responses than individual components. For instance, combining acetoin, ethyl lactate, and methionol with acetic acid and ethanol significantly enhanced attraction in Drosophila suzukii (Spotted wing fruitfly) ( 16 ). Similarly, a blend of (Z)-7-dodecene acetate, (Z)-9- tetradecene acetate, and hexadecen-1-ol, all of which function as female sex pheromones in Macdunnoughia crassisigna , triggered significant electrophysiological responses in male antennae, whereas the individual compounds failed to do so ( 17 ). 

Although eicosane (the most abundant vetiver volatile) and nonanal (the second-most abundant) did not independently attract RSB, prior research has demonstrated that their combination with camphor—a known sex pheromone—significantly enhanced attraction, increasing lure efficiency by 2.5- to fivefold compared to camphor alone. These findings underscore the importance of investigating interactions between cedrol and other vetiver volatiles to optimize attractant formulations for RSB management. Understanding how volatile blends influence insect behavior will be crucial for developing more effective, field-applicable pest control strategies. 

# Materials and Methods

Plant Materials. Vetiver (V. zizanioides) was propagated from stem cuttings, transplanted, and cultivated in a greenhouse at the Zhejiang Academy of Agricultural Sciences, Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province, China. Greenhouse conditions were maintained a $2 5 \pm 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ with a photoperiod of 16 h of light and 8 h of darkness. Nipponbare rice, used in transgenic experiments, was grown under identical conditions. 

Insect Rearing. C. suppressalis (RSB) larvae were collected from paddy fields in Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province, and maintained in a phytotron under controlled conditions: $2 7 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , 7 0 \pm 5 \%$ relative humidity, and a 16:8-h light/dark photoperiod. Larvae were reared on an artificial diet, while adults were provided with a $1 0 \%$ honey solution. 

SPME/GC–MS Analysis. Volatile compounds and enzymatic reaction products were analyzed using SPME coupled with GC–MS. Whole vetiver plants were placed in a sealed container to collect plant volatiles, with the soil surface covered to minimize root and soil volatile interference. The container was incubated in a $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \complement$ water bath for 30 min to promote volatile emission. An SPME fiber was positioned above the leaves to capture the released volatiles. 

For enzymatic assays, reaction mixtures were incubated in $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m L G C }$ headspace vials (Agilent), and volatiles were absorbed onto an SPME fiber at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 min. The SPME fiber was subsequently injected into an Agilent 5977B GC–MS system equipped with an HP-5MS column. Samples were introduced at $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \complement ,$ , followed by a 2-min isothermal hold at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ . The column temperature was increased at a rate o $2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ per minute to $1 6 0 ^ { \circ } \complement ,$ where it was held for 2 min, then ramped at $8 ^ { \circ } \complement$ per minute to $2 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ with a final isothermal hold at $2 7 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 20 min (18). Relative compound abundance was determined through peak area normalization. 

Field Experiments. To evaluate the attractiveness of eicosane and nonanal to RSB, field experiments were conducted in a rice field in Jinhua, Zhejiang Province, with a plot size of approximately $2 0 0 \mathsf { m } \times 2 0 0 \mathsf { m }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S16). Eicosane and nonanal were dissolved in hexane at concentrations o $0 . 0 1 , 0 . 1 , 1 , 1 0 _ { i }$ , and $5 0 \mu { 9 } / \mu \ L ,$ and 3 mL of each solution was applied to attractant devices. These devices were placed at $5 0 – \mathsf { m }$ intervals in the center of the test field, with three replicates per concentration. Only one concentration was tested per field. A commercial sex attractant for RSB served as a positive control. After 1 wk, the attractant devices were collected, and the number of attracted RSB adults was recorded. 

Y-Tube and H-Tube Olfactometer Assays. A Y-tube olfactometer (2.5 cm diameter, 21 cm trunk length, 17 cm branch length, $1 2 0 ^ { \circ }$ branch angle) was used to assess volatile attractiveness at $2 5 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \complement$ . Humidified air was purified through activated carbon and supplied at a rate of 0.5 L/min. Absorbent cotton pads, placed at the end of each branch, were treated with $1 0 \mu \iota$ of cedrol, camphor, or α-pinene in hexane at concentrations of 0.02, 0.2, 2, 20, and $2 0 0 \mu \ g / \mu \iota$ . A control pad contained only hexane. 

Female RSB moths (2 to 3 d old) were used, as vetiver primarily attracts females for oviposition. Moths were starved for 6 h before introduction into the Y-tube trunk. Moth behavior was observed for 30 min; a choice was recorded if a moth moved at least one-third of the way into a branch and remained there for at least 5 min. If no choice was made, the response was recorded as “no choice.” To eliminate positional bias, the Y-tube arms were alternated after every three trials. Between trials, the apparatus was cleaned with ethanol and air-dried. A total of 60 replicates were performed per concentration. 

The attractiveness of VzTPS9-expressing transgenic rice was assessed using a modified H-tube olfactometer. The chamber had a 9 cm diameter and a 50 cm height, with two connected columns separated by a 2 cm diameter, 30-cm-long tube. A small opening in the middle allowed for moth introduction. Experimental conditions mirrored those in the Y-tube assays, with moths starved before testing. After releasing the moths, the system was kept in darkness, and the number of moths in each chamber was recorded after 12 h. Each replicate involved 10 moths, and after three replicates, the positions of transgenic and wild-type rice plants were switched. Six replicates were performed per transgenic rice line. 

EAG Recording. EAG responses were measured in 2- to 3-d-old female C. suppressalis moths exposed to different odorant concentrations. Antennae were excised at the base, and the tips were trimmed to approximately 1 mm. Each antenna was secured to a PR electrode using conductive adhesive (Spectra360). $\mathsf { A } 1 0 \mu \mathsf { L }$ odorant solution (prepared as described above) or a solvent control was applied to a filter paper strip $( 4 . 0 \mathsf { c m } \times 0 . 7 \mathsf { c m }$ ) inside a Pasteur pipette. Odor stimuli were delivered in 0.7-s pulses at 30-s intervals. Each antenna was tested with only one odorant concentration. 

The relative EAG response (rEAG) was calculated as 

$$
\mathrm{rEAG}(\%) = \frac{\mathrm{EAG}(\mathrm{x})}{(\mathrm{EAG}(\mathrm{ck1}) + \mathrm{EAG}(\mathrm{ck2})) / 2}\times 100,
$$

where EAG (x) represents the amplitude (mV) of the EAG response to odor, EAG (ck1) denotes the amplitude (mV) of the EAG response to hexane before testing odor, and EAG (ck2) is EAG response to hexane after testing (19). 

Genome and Transcriptome Sequencing. The genomic DNA of V. zizanioides was extracted from fresh young leaves using the QIAGEN Genomic Reagent Kit, while total RNA was isolated from roots, stems, and leaves using Trizol reagent, following the manufacturer’s protocols. Sample integrity and purity were assessed using a Nanodrop 2000, all extracts were stored a $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \complement$ until further use. For transcriptome sequencing, a paired-end library with a 350 bp insert size was prepared and sequenced on the Illumina HiSeq X platform using standard protocols. Whole-genome sequencing was conducted on the PacBio Sequel II system, utilizing a Single Molecule Real-Time (SMRT) bell DNA library with a 20 kb insert size. The genome and transcriptome data could be available in InsectBase2.0 (http://v2.insect-genome.com/Vziz). 

Genome Size Estimation and K-mer Analysis. Genome size estimation was performed using flow cytometry following a previously described protocol (20). Approximately $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of fresh leaf tissue was rinsed in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, 0.01 M, pH 7.4), blotted dry, and minced in ice-cold Galbraith buffer 

(45 mM MgCl2, 20 mM MOPS, 30 mM sodium citrate, 0.1% (v/v) Triton X-100, pH 7.0). The homogenate was filtered through a $4 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon mesh and centrifuged at $2 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement ,$ and the supernatant was mixed with $5 0 0 \mu \iota$ of PBS. Following RNase A digestion $( \neg 0 \mu \mathfrak { g } )$ for 10 min, propidium iodide $( 2 5 \mu 9 )$ was added, and the samples were incubated in the dark for 15 min. Genome size was estimated using a BD FACSCalibur™ flow cytometer. 

For genome surveying, 79.9 Gb of high-quality Illumina reads were analyzed via K-mer analysis using JELLYFISH (21). A 21-mer analysis was conducted to estimate genome size following the Lander–Waterman algorithm (genome size = K-mer number/depth) (22), while heterozygosity was inferred from the ratio of heterozygous peaks. 

Chromosome-Level Genome Assembly of V. zizanioides. For de novo assembly, 52.8 Gb of clean PacBio reads were processed with HiFiasm using default parameters. Circular consensus sequencing (CCS) reads were assembled into scaffolds based on sequence overlaps, and heterozygous contigs were filtered using purge_haplotigs (23), guided by read depth distribution and sequence similarity. 

High-quality Hi-C data were generated from fresh V. zizanioides leaf tissue. Leaves were finely chopped and incubated in $2 \%$ formaldehyde at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 min, with agitation for every 2 min. Cross-linking was quenched with $2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { M }$ glycine for 20 min. The samples were drained, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and processed into Hi-C libraries following standard protocols. Sequencing on the Illumina HiSeq X platform yielded 117.8 Gb of clean reads after filtering. HiCUP was used to generate a nonredundant interaction matrix (24), which was employed to anchor contigs onto chromosomes using the 3D-DNA pipeline (25), followed by manual curation in JuiceBox (26). Genome completeness was assessed using BUSCO v5.0 with the embryophyta_odb9 dataset (27). 

Gene Prediction and Functional Annotation. Repeat annotation was performed using the MAKER-P pipeline (28). Miniature inverted-repeat transposable elements (MITEs) were identified using MITE-Hunter, and long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons were annotated with LTRharvest and LTRdigest (29, 30). Additional repetitive sequences were identified via RepeatModeler (31). To filter out gene fragments, sequences were compared against a plant protein database using ProtExcluder (28). The script and database for this pipeline can be accessed at Repeat Library Construction-Advanced (http://weatherby.genetics.utah.edu/MAKER/wiki/index. php/Repeat_Library_Construction-Advanced). The final repeat library was annotated using RepeatMasker, referencing both the de novo repeat library and Repbase (32). 

Protein-coding genes were predicted using an integrative approach combining ab initio, transcriptome-based, and homology-based methods. The BRAKER2 pipeline, incorporating GeneMark-EP and AUGUSTUS, was used for ab initio gene prediction (33). 

Homolog-based predictions utilized protein sequences from O. sativa, S. bicolor, Setaria viridis, Triticum aestivum, and Z. mays, which were aligned to the genome with Genome Threader. Transcriptomic data were aligned using HISAT2 and assembled with StringTie (34, 35). Open reading frames (ORFs) were refined using TransDecoder (https://github.com/TransDecoder/TransDecoder), and consensus gene models were generated via EVidenceModeler (EVM) by integrating results from all three approaches (36). Functional annotation was performed using DIAMOND BLASTP against the NCBI Non-Redundant Protein Database, Swiss-Prot, and KEGG (37), while protein domain annotation was conducted using HMMER against Pfam (38). 

Noncoding RNAs were identified using specialized tools. Ribosomal RNAs (rRNAs) were annotated with RNAmmer, and tRNAs were detected using tRNAscan-SE (39, 40). Small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs) and microRNAs (miRNAs) were annotated by mapping genomic sequences to the Rfam14.6 database using Infernal. The Z parameter was calculated as $Z =$ (total residues $\times 2 \times \mathsf { C M }$ number) $^ { 1 1 0 ^ { 6 } } ,$ , where total residues were determined using esl-seqstat, and CM number refers to the count of covariance models in Rfam (41). 

Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) were identified using a stringent pipeline. Transcripts were first assembled with HISAT2 and StringTie, and those longer than 200 bp with class codes “i, $\widetilde { \mathsf { d } } _ { \prime } ^ { \prime \prime } \widetilde { \mathsf { U } } _ { \prime } ^ { \prime \prime }$ or $" \times "$ were selected for further analysis (42). Coding potential was assessed using FeeLnc, with protein-coding genes serving as a training set in “shuffle” mode, and CPC2 for confirmation. Transcripts classified as “noncoding” by CPC2 were designated as lncRNAs (43, 44). 

Comparative Genomics Analysis and Phylogenetic Construction. The protein sequences of 10 grass species—C. serrulatus, V. zizanioides, E. crusgalli, M. lutarioriparius, O. brachyantha, O. sativa, S. spontaneum, S. bicolor, and 

Z. mays—were analyzed, with A. comosus serving as an outgroup. The C. serrulatus protein sequences were annotated using a previously established pipeline, while E. crusgalli sequences were retrieved from the Rice Weedomics database (http:// ibi.zju.edu.cn/RiceWeedomes/Echinochloa/). Single-copy orthogroups were identified using OrthoFinder (45), and protein sequences were extracted via in-house scripts. Multiple sequence alignments were performed using MAFFT and trimmed with TRIMAL under default parameters (46, 47). A phylogenetic tree was constructed in IQ-TREE with the VT amino acid substitution model, as determined by ProtTest (48, 49), and branch support was assessed using 1,000 bootstrap replicates. 

Species divergence times were estimated using r8s (50), incorporating seven calibration points from the TimeTree database (51): a) M. lutarioriparius– S. spontaneum (5.53 to 8.43 Mya), b) S. spontaneum–S. bicolor (8.4 to 12.1 Mya), c) S. bicolor–Z. mays (9.39 to 12.47 Mya), d) Z. mays–E. crusgalli (18.7 to 30.6 Mya), e) E. crusgalli–O. sativa (42 to 52 Mya), f) O. sativa–O. brachyantha (6.9 to 17.7 Mya), and g) O. sativa–A. comosus (102 to 120 Mya). Gene family expansions and contractions were analyzed using CAFE, with statistical significance set at $P <$ 0.05 (52). Functional enrichment analysis of these gene families was conducted using ClusterProfiler (53). 

Identification of Terpenoid Synthesis Genes. Terpenoid biosynthesis genes were identified through DIAMOND BLASTP searches against known TS genes, applying a $6 0 \%$ similarity threshold (SI Appendix, Fig. S17) (37). To enhance accuracy, HMMER was used to refine gene identification based on conserved motifs. For genes involved in the MVA and MEP pathways, sequences were aligned using MUSCLE and converted to STO format via ALTER (http://www.sing-group.org/ ALTER/). Hidden Markov Model (HMM) files were generated with hmmbuild, and TPS motif profiles (PF01397 and PF03936) were obtained from the Pfam database. Candidate sequences containing conserved motifs were identified using hmmsearch and subsequently verified against the Conserved Domains Database (CDD). Z. mays terpenoid synthesis genes were sourced from a previous study (54). 

Validation by RT-PCR and Isolation of Full-Length cDNAs. To validate the identified terpene synthesis genes, total RNA was extracted from root, stem, and leaf tissues using Trizol reagent. RNA quality and concentration were assessed with a Nanodrop 2000 spectrophotometer. First-strand cDNA synthesis was performed using the HiScript III 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit $( + 9 0 N A$ wiper) in a $2 0 \mu \iota$ reaction containing $1 \mu \ g$ of total RNA, $2 \mu \updownarrow 1 0 \times$ RT Mix, $2 \mu \iota$ HiScript III Enzyme Mix, 1 μL Oligo(dT)20VN, and RNase-free water. PCR amplification was conducted in a $5 0 \mu \iota$ reaction using $2 \times$ Rapid Taq Master Mix (Vazyme Biotech) and genespecific primers (SI Appendix, Table S3). The thermal cycling profile included an initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 5 min, followed by 35 cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for ${ 1 5 s , }$ $5 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for ${ 1 5 s , }$ and $7 2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 s. Amplified products were resolved on a $1 . 5 \%$ (w/v) agarose gel. Gene-specific primers were designed using Primer3.0 (55). 

qRT-PCR. qRT-PCR was performed to quantify gene expression in V. zizanioides and O. sativa. RNA extraction and cDNA synthesis followed the same protocols as described earlier. Reactions were carried out in a $2 0 \mu \iota$ mixture containing 10 μL iTaq Universal SYBR Green Supermix (Bio-Rad), $6 \mu \iota$ RNase-free water, $2 \mu \iota$ cDNA, and $\mathbb { 1 } \mu \iota$ each of forward and reverse primers. Relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C T } }$ method. S-adenosylmethionine synthetase was used as the reference gene for vetiver, while expansin served as the reference gene for rice (56, 57). 

Transcriptome-Based Expression Analysis of VzTPSs Genes. Expression levels of VzTPS genes were quantified using RSEM, with transcriptome indices built based on VzTPS annotations. Transcriptome data were obtained from root, stem, and leaf tissues, with two replicates per sample. FPKM values were calculated under default RSEM parameters. 

Codon-Optimized Gene Synthesis. Codon-optimized sequences of VzTPS9 and VzTPS10 for E. coli expression were synthesized by BioRu (Wuhan, China). Optimization was performed to enhance translation efficiency and ensure successful prokaryotic expression. 

In Vitro Expression and Enzyme Assays of VzTPS9 and VzTPS10. Codonoptimized VzTPS9 and VzTPS10 were cloned into the pET30a vector to produce His-tagged recombinant TPS proteins. Protein purification was performed using Ni-NTA affinity chromatography (48). Enzyme assays were conducted in $2 0 0 \mu \iota$ 

of assay buffer (50 mM HEPES, 7.5 mM MgClâ‚‚, 100 mM KCl, 5 mM dithiothreitol, $1 0 \%$ glycerol [v/v], and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ prenyl pyrophosphate substrates [GPP, FPP, GGPP]) at pH 7.0. Reactions were incubated a $3 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 1 h (18). 

Generation of VzTPS9-Expressing Transgenic Rice. VzTPS9 was cloned into the pBWA(V)KS vector, a modified version of pCAMBIA2300 (purchased from BioRun, Wuhan, China), which incorporates a CaMV 35S promoter, NOS terminator, and M13 forward primer for verification. The construct was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 via electroporation. Bacteria were cultured in LB medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ with shaking (180 rpm) for 30 min and then plated on LB agar and incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in the dark for 48 h. Monoclonal colonies were validated and stored. 

Sterilized rice seeds were sequentially treated with $7 5 \%$ ethanol, $1 5 \%$ sodium hypochlorite, and sterile water before germination at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \complement$ under light for $2 0 ~ \mathsf { d }$ to induce callus formation. The EHA105 strain carrying pBWA(V)KS-VzTPS9 was suspended in an infection medium $( 3 4 . 7 2 \ : 9 / \mathrm { L }$ MS medium, 1.5 mg/L 2,4-D, 1.0 g/L casamino acids, $5 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone, $\mathsf { p H } 5 . 2 \mathsf { ) }$ to an $0 \mathsf { D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.2. Calli were immersed in the solution for 15 min, then incubated on coculture medium (34.72 g/L MS medium, $1 . 5 \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L 2 } , \mathsf { 4 - D } , \mathsf { 5 \mu M C u S O } _ { 4 } , \mathsf { 5 \mu M A g N O } _ { 3 } , 0 . 7 \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { L }$ proline, $0 . 5 { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathfrak { L } }$ MES, 8 g/L agarose, 50 mM acetosyringone, $\mathsf { p H } 5 . 8 )$ ) at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark for $7 2 \mathrm { h }$ . 

Transformed calli were selected on medium containing 2 mM glyphosate and 100 mg/L Timentin. Positive calli were subcultured onto differentiation and rooting media. The resulting seedlings were transferred to sterile soil and cultivated until the tillering stage for further analysis. 

Identification of VzTPS9 Orthologs. To identify orthologs of VzTPS9, protein sequences of all plant TPS genes were retrieved from the UniProt database. Candidate sequences exhibiting over $8 0 \%$ coverage with VzTPS9 were identified through BLAST screening. A phylogenetic tree was constructed using IQ-TREE to determine the orthologous relationships of VzTPS9. 

Statistical Analysis. Y-tube and H-tube olfactometer assays were analyzed using chi-square tests. One-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s HSD test was applied to field experiments, EAG responses, and qRT-PCR results. Statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 8.0.
