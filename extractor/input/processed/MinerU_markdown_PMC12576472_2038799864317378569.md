# ABSTRACT

Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), defined as transcripts $> 2 0 0 \mathrm { n t }$ without protein-coding capacity, play crucial regulatory roles in plant growth and development. While numerous lncRNAs exist in plants including cotton (Gossypium spp.), few are functionally characterised. G. hirsutum serves as both a premier fibre crop and significant oil source, where fatty acid desaturase 2 (GhFAD2-1) is a key gene for the synthesis of polyunsaturated fatty acids in cottonseed. In this study, we confirmed that the GhFAD2-1 located on the D subgenome generated a tightly linked homologous copy through a tandem duplication event. Due to sequence divergence in the promoter and gene body following the duplication event, this homologous sequence was identified as a long non-coding RNA (lncRNA) and designated as pseudo-GhFAD2-1 (pGhFAD2-1). Knocking out pGhFAD2-1 increased linoleic acid content and reduced seed size, whereas over-expressing pGhFAD2-1 had opposite effects. pGhFAD2-1 interacts with histone deacetylase GhHDT1 and 40S ribosomal protein GhRPS12, transcriptionally recruiting GhHDT1 to suppress GhFAD2-1 expression and translationally inhibiting GhFAD2 protein synthesis by competitively binding RPS12 with GhFAD2-1 mRNA, ultimately disrupting cottonseed fatty acid biosynthesis. The impact of pGhFAD2-1 on seed size seems to be achieved through the ABA biosynthesis and signalling pathways via GhHDT1, with an increasing level of ABA leading to smaller seeds. The results expand our knowledge on the origin, function and regulatory mechanism of plant lncRNAs and provide new targets and pathways for genetic manipulation of cottonseed oil and seed size. 

# 1 | Introduction

Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) are the transcripts with a length over 200nt and without protein coding capacity (Mercer et  al.  2009). Many plant lncRNAs have been identified through bioinformatics analysis of transcripts generated from various tissues using next-generation (short reads) and third-generation (long reads) sequencing technologies (Yang et  al.  2023), with several having been functionally characterised and demonstrated 

to play crucial regulatory roles in plant growth and development. For instance, lncRNA-COOLAIR is the antisense transcript of FLOWERING LOCUS C (FLC), a key gene involved in the Arabidopsis thaliana vernalization regulation pathway. It interacts with the polycomb complex (PRC2) and inhibits FLC expression under cold conditions, and COOLAIR-mediated repression of FLC is reversible under warm environmental conditions (Swiezewski et al. 2009). Similarly, under cold treatment conditions, COLDAIR, an approximately 1100-nt long lncRNA, generated from the first 

intron of the FLC gene in Arabidopsis in the same orientation as FLC, induces epigenetic modifications of FLC by recruiting PRC2 to FLC chromatin, inhibiting FLC expression and regulating the vernalization process (Heo and Sung  2011). In addition, studies on crops such as corn (Dai et al. 2004), rice (Ding et al. 2012; Liu et al. 2018; Fang et al. 2019; Wang, Wendel, and Hua 2018; Wang, Luo, et al. 2018), tomato (Wang et al. 2016; Cui et al. 2017) and rapeseed (Song et al. 2013) have shown that plant lncRNAs play crucial regulatory roles in growth and development and stress responses. In cotton, some lncRNAs related to fibre development have been identified using RNA-seq (Wang et al. 2015; Zou et al. 2016, 2022; Salih et al. 2019; Zheng et al. 2021); several lncRNAs related to fertility, disease resistance and responses to low temperature, salt and drought stresses have also been reported (Hamid et al. 2020; Lu et al. 2016; Deng et al. 2018; Zhang et al. 2019, 2021, 2022; Wang, Wang, et  al.  2021; Wang, Han, et  al.  2021; Cao et  al.  2021; Wu et  al.  2022). Despite this progress, so far, studies on lncRNAs in cotton are still in the infancy stage and the functional mechanisms of lncRNAs are largely elusive in plants. 

Gossypium hirsutum is not only the most important fibre crop, but also an important oil crop. Mature cotton kernels contain about $2 5 \% { - } 4 0 \%$ oil, of which linoleic acid (C18:2) accounts for over $5 0 \%$ of the total fatty acid content (Liu et al. 2017; Ma et al. 2021). This polyunsaturated fatty acid is synthesised from oleic acid (C18:1) by fatty acid desaturase 2 (FAD2) (Liu et al. 2017). Unlike rice and Arabidopsis, Gossypium hirsutum has a complex allopolyploid genome (AADD; $2 n { = } 4 x { = } 5 2$ ), evolved from multiple rounds of genome duplication events (Zhang et al. 2015; Li et al. 2015). Analysis of the GhFAD2 gene family revealed eight homologues in G.hirsutum, with four copies in the A subgenome and four in the D subgenome. Each GhFAD2 located on the D subgenome has a homoeologous copy on the A subgenome (Liu et al. 2019). Additionally, Gh_D13G2237 is adjacent to Gh_D13G2238 (GhFAD2-1D) and shares high sequence homology with it, suggesting that it likely originated from the tandem duplication of Gh_D13G2238. Gh_D13G2237 was annotated to encode a protein lacking the third conserved histidine cluster commonly found in plant FAD2 proteins (Zhang et  al.  2015). GhFAD2-1 (including Gh_A13G1850 and Gh_D13G2238 located on chromosome 13 of the A and D subgenome, respectively) is preferentially expressed in developing cottonseeds and is a key gene controlling the transition from C18:1 to C18:2 and determining the content of C18:2 in cottonseeds (Liu et al. 2002, 2019; Chen et al. 2021; Ma et al. 2021). 

In this study, we aimed to know whether Gh_D13G2237, like Gh_D13G2238 (GhFAD2-1D), functions in the metabolism of fatty acids and the determination of oil accumulation in cottonseeds, and if so, how it fulfils its functionality. We found that the cDNA we cloned from the Gh_D13G2237 locus is different from the annotated coding sequence of Gh_D13G2237, and that it also differs from Gh_D13G2238 due to sequence divergence in both the promoter and coding region after its origin via the tandem duplication event, which occurred in the ancestral species of the D genomes. We demonstrated the cloned cDNA to be a long noncoding RNA and named it as pseudo-GhFAD2-1 (pGhFAD2-1). pGhFAD2-1 is specifically expressed in 20–50 days post-anthesis (DPA) cottonseeds with low levels and regulates the relative percentage contents of C18:1 and C18:2 in cottonseed oil as well as seed size. Manipulating the level of pGhFAD2-1 might provide an alternative way for regulating oil quality and seed size. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | pGhFAD2-1 Is a Tandem Duplicate of GhFAD2-1D With Altered Splicing Features

Using cDNA of G. hirsutum variety ‘Xinluzao 33’ as a template, we cloned the full-length transcript of the Gh_D13G2237 locus by RACE technology, which is 1476 bp long (Figure  S1A). We thus designated the cloned transcript of Gh_D13G2237 as pseudo-GhFAD2-1 or pGhFAD2-1. Between the cloned fulllength cDNA of Gh_D13G2237 and its locus annotated coding sequence (876 bp in length), there is only a 224-bp identical sequence, which is the third exon of the annotated Gh_D13G2237 (Figure  S1B). Comparing the cDNA of pGhFAD2-1 and its corresponding genomic DNA found that pGhFAD2-1 contains two exons (203- and 1273-bp) and a 2442 bp long intron (Figure S1C). The intron covers the 1230 bp promoter, the first and second exons, and the first intron and part of the second intron of the annotated Gh_D13G2237 sequence. Gh_D13G2237 was annotated as a fatty acid desaturase encoding gene because of its sequence similarity with Gh_D13G2238 (GhFAD2-1D) (Zhang et al. 2015). However, the annotated cDNA sequence of Gh_D13G2237 (876 bp) in the CottonGen database is a chimeric construct composed of partial transcripts from Gh_D13G2238 and pGhFAD2-1. Notably, RT-PCR assays using gene-specific primers failed to amplify the full-length 876 bp cDNA in our experimental validation. Subsequently, we validated the presence/ absence of the annotated Gh_D13G2237 sequence across multiple genetic backgrounds. We successfully cloned the identical pGhFAD2-1 sequence from 10 diverse upland cotton accessions (including Tahe 2, Xinluzao 61 and Shidamian 196), which was fully consistent with our findings in Xinluzao 33. Moreover, the annotated Gh_D13G2237 sequence was not detected in any of these cotton varieties. Then, we examined the latest reported cotton reference genomes, and at the corresponding genomic locus, no gene is currently annotated. In the cotton T–T genomes (http://jinlab.hzau.edu.cn/T2TCottonHub/), we identified a partial mRNA transcript at this locus that shows high homology to GhFAD2-1, yet it is also not annotated as the GhFAD2-1 gene. These results demonstrate that the previously annotated $G h _ { - }$ D13G2237 sequence does not exist as a contiguous transcript and likely arises from bioinformatic misassembly or sequencing artefacts. Our results also indicate that the difference between the cloned and annotated coding sequences highlights the importance of experimental verification of the identity of annotated genes, particularly in complex polyploid genomes like cotton. Compared with the absence of an intron in the Gh_D13G2238 (GhFAD2-1D) coding region, pGhFAD2-1 appears to have undergone a replication of GhFAD2-1D at its own location, and during the replication process, a 1221 bp sequence was inserted into the CDS of replicated GhFAD2-1D, resulting in the loss of a portion of its coding sequence (227 bp). The inserted partial sequence (995 bp), partial coding sequence of GhFAD2-1D and partial sequences at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ ends of GhFAD2-1D form a new transcription sequence (1476 bp). But the changed splicing event made the 1476 bp full-length cDNA no longer able to encode a fatty acid desaturase, only with a 318 bp open reading frame. 

Given that $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ is a tandem duplicate of Gh_D13G2238 and the high homology between the 1st exon (203-bp) of pGh-FAD2-1 and the promoter sequence of Gh_D13G2238, we 

compared the genomic sequence of pGhFAD2-1 (Figure  S1D) with the corresponding sequence of Gh_D13G2238 (a single exon gene; including part of its promoter, coding region and part of the potential $3 ^ { \prime }$ UTR) (Figure S1D). According to the sequence alignment, the two sequences showed high homology in three regions, with other regions being quite divergent (Figure S1E). The first region covers ${ \sim } 6 0 0$ -bp distal promoter (i.e., ${ 5 ^ { \prime } } { \mathrm { . } }$ -end) sequence of Gh_D13G2238 shown in Figure S1D, corresponding to the 1st exon of pGhFAD2-1 and part of its intron; the second region starts from the proximal promoter (i.e., immediate upstream of the translation start site ATG) of Gh_D13G2238, covering ~800-bp of the coding sequence of Gh_D13G2238, whose corresponding sequence of $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ is part of the large intron; the third region is located at the flanking region of the stop codon (TAA) of Gh_D13G2238, and the corresponding sequence in pGhFAD2-1 is transcribed. These sequence similarities and differences imply that, after the duplication event, the duplicated copy had experienced sequence divergence in its promoter and gene body, which changed the splicing event of the gene and the coding capacity of the resultant transcript, i.e., pGhFAD2-1. 

To trace the origin and understand the evolution of pGhFAD2-1, we searched the orthologous sequences of pGhFAD2-1 in 26 diploid and tetraploid cotton species using the 1476-bp pGh-FAD2-1 transcript as a query. The results indicated the presence of a pGhFAD2-1 ortholog in all AD genome tetraploid species and all D genome diploid species, but absent in the A-, B-, E-, F-, G- and K-genome diploid species, despite the presence of the entire or part of the 203-bp 1st exon of pGhFAD2-1 (Table S1; Figure S2). These results indicate that the sequence duplicated includes the genomic DNA covering the entire Gh_D13G2238 and part of its upstream sequence (not including the sequence homologous to the 1st exon of pGhFAD2-1 and ~210-bp of its downstream sequence) and that the duplication event occurred in the common ancestor of all D genome species after it diverged from its sister species. Additionally, the FAD2-1 gene is highly conserved across Gossypium species, with near-identical coding regions-especially the histidine-containing catalytic motifs that drive C18:1 to C18:2 conversion. The gene maintains consistent length, genomic position and an intronless structure (e.g., GhFAD2-1 in G. hirsutum and GbFAD2-1 in G. barbadense), and it exists as single-copy homologues in both A and D subgenomes with minimal divergence, likely due to post-polyploidization coevolution preventing redundancy (Table S2; Figure S2). Minor interspecies/varietal SNPs occur but rarely disrupt core function, mostly residing in non-coding regions or causing subtle enzymatic shifts (e.g., adjusted C18:1/C18:2 ratios). Such exceptional conservation arises from FAD2-1 essential role in oil composition and the close phylogeny of Gossypium species. 

# 2.2 | pGhFAD2-1 Regulates Fatty Acids Components Synthesis

pGhFAD2-1 is only expressed specifically in the middle and late stages of cottonseed development (20–50 DPA), and its expression level is generally low (Figure  S3). To investigate whether the expression of pGhFAD2-1 affects the fatty acid composition of cottonseeds, 13 independent transgenic cotton lines over-expressing pGhFAD2-1 (OE) were obtained by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Meanwhile, nine 

pGhFAD2-1 knockout mutants were generated using CRISPR-Cas9-mediated gene editing using two sgRNAs targeting the specific sequences of pGhFAD2-1. Over-expression lines OE1, OE6, OE7 and OE10 with high pGhFAD2-1 expression levels, and knockout lines KO3, KO5, KO6 and KO9 with large deletions were selected for further characterisation by generating homozygous progeny. PCR detection confirmed that the Cas9 nuclease was no longer present in the KO lines, and sequencing results of the predicted off-target sites showed no nonspecific editing (Figure S4). There were no obvious differences in plant phenotypes between the transgenics and untransformed controls (CK) (Figure  1A–D). The pGhFAD2-1 expression levels of the selected transgenic progeny were further verified and compared to those of CK (Figure 1E). 

During cottonseed development, compared to CK, KO and OE showed an upregulated and downregulated GhFAD2-1D, respectively (Figure  1F). It indicates that pGhFAD2-1 has a cisregulatory effect on the expression of GhFAD2-1D. Interestingly, the expression of GhFAD2-1A, the homoeolog of GhFAD2-1D located in the A subgenome, also showed similar expression changes as that of GhFAD2-1D during seed development (Figure  1G). To investigate the consequence of up/downregulation of $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ on the content of fatty acid compositions, we compared changes of fatty acids in developing and mature cottonseeds of CK, KO and OE. A total of 38 fatty acid compositions were identified and $\propto$ -linolenic acid (C18:3) was mainly accumulated at the early stages of seed development (before 20 DPA). After 20 DPA, palmitic acid (C16:0), C18:1 and C18:2 were apparently accumulated and their contents reached over $9 2 \%$ of the total fatty acid compositions. The relative percentage content of stearic acid (C18:0) (around $3 \%$ ) was relatively stable during cottonseed development. Compared to CK, the transgenic plants had a relatively stable overall profiles of C16:0 and C18:0 (Figure S5). In OE, C18:1 showed a continuous increase from 20 to 60 DPA, and reached $2 1 . 3 6 \%$ in the mature seeds, with an increase of $1 2 . 5 0 \%$ and $2 9 . 3 8 \%$ compared to those in CK and KO, respectively (Figure 1H). Correspondingly, during seed development, OE had a C18:2 content lower than CK and KO. Finally, the relative percentages of C18:2 in mature seeds of KO and CK were $5 2 . 5 8 \%$ and $5 0 . 8 6 \%$ , respectively, which were $6 . 9 1 \%$ and $3 . 4 1 \%$ higher than the $4 9 . 1 8 \%$ of OE (Figure 1I). This result indicates that the conversion from C18:1 to C18:2 was attenuated in OE, resulting in more accumulation of C18:1. For KO, this was exactly the opposite. This trend was consistent with the changes of GhFAD2-1 expression levels in KO and OE. These results also suggest that in the biosynthetic steps from C16:0 to C18:2 during seed development, OE and KO seeds accumulate more C18:1 and C18:2, respectively. 

An interesting phenomenon is that the cottonseed oil content of KOs ranged from $1 9 . 3 5 \%$ to $2 0 . 6 2 \%$ , which was $2 . 2 1 \%$ to $8 . 8 1 \%$ higher than that of CK. On the contrary, the oil content of OEs ranged from $1 7 . 3 8 \%$ to $1 8 . 6 5 \%$ , with a decrease of $1 . 5 8 \%$ to $8 . 2 8 \%$ compared to CK. Correspondingly, the oil content of KO kernels was $3 3 . 0 5 \% - 3 4 . 7 8 \%$ , which was higher (a $2 . 1 6 \% { - } 7 . 5 1 \%$ increase) than those of CK (Figure  1J,K). Transgenic lines KO6 with the highest oil content and OE1 with the lowest oil content were used in all subsequent experiments. Through electron transmission electron microscopy, it was clearly observed that the distribution of oil bodies in KO6 cottonseeds was significantly tight, and the accumulated oil bodies were more pronounced (Figure 1L,M). Cottonseed oil is mainly synthesised in large quantities at 30–50 DPA. In order to investigate how pGhFAD2-1 participates in the process of fatty acid metabolism, RNA-seq analysis was conducted on cottonseeds at three key developmental stages: 30 DPA, 40 DPA and 50 DPA. We first investigated the expression dynamics of the major genes involved in fatty acid biosynthesis (Figure 1N). The de novo biosynthesis of fatty acids takes place mainly in the plastid. The first step of fatty acid biosynthesis is the formation of malonyl coenzyme by acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC), which is a key rate-limiting enzyme in fatty acid biosynthesis. GhACC (Gh_A01G1574 and Gh_ D01G1885) was mainly expressed in 30–50 DPA seeds and had relatively higher expression levels in KO than in CK and OE. Malonyl-CoA is catalysed by fatty acid synthase (FAS) to produce 16:0 carbon palmitoyl-ACP (C16:0-ACP) via the recurring 

reactions. The elongation of the C16:0-ACP to the C18:0-ACP is catalysed by $\beta$ -ketoacyl-ACP synthase II (KASII). Cotton has four pairs of homoeologous GhKASII (Ma et  al.  2021). Two GhKASII genes (Gh_A08G2201 and Gh_D08G2566) had relatively high expression levels from 30 DPA to 50 DPA, suggesting that they might play an important role in contributing to the conversion of C16:0-ACP to C18:0-ACP. There was no significant difference in the expression levels of these two GhKASII genes (Gh_A08G2201 and Gh_D08G2566) among KO, CK and OE. C18:0-ACP is desaturated by Δ9-stearoyl-ACP desaturase (SAD) to form monounsaturated C18:1-ACP. Twelve GhSADs have been identified in the cotton genome (Ma et al. 2021). Similar to GhKASII, there was no significant difference in the expression levels of GhSADs among KO, CK and OE. Two pairs of GhSAD homoeologs (Gh_A05G2865 and Gh_D05G3162, Gh_A10G1286 and Gh_D10G1197) had high expression levels at 30–50 DPA. The acyl group from acyl-ACP can be hydrolysed by two different classes of acyl-ACP thioesterases (FATs), FATA and FATB, to release free fatty acids. Two pairs of homoeologous FATA genes (Gh_A08G1740 and Gh_D08G2090, Gh_A08G0436 and Gh_D08G0524) and 

two pairs of homoeologous FATB (Gh_A06G0514 and Gh_ D06G0571, Gh_A07G0026 and Gh_D07G0034) were highly expressed during cottonseed development. PUFAs in cottonseed are synthesised by GhFAD2 and GhFAD3 located in the ER. The results showed that in KO, the expression levels of GhFAD2-1 (Gh_A13G1850 and Gh_D13G2238) were significantly higher than those in WT and OE, consistent with the qRT-PCR results. Apart from GhFAD2, DEGs related to the biosynthesis of unsaturated fatty acids also included GhFAD3 (Gh_A07G0946 and Gh_D07G1026). 

In seeds, Triacylglycerol (TAG) is the main storage form of fatty acids and the main component of vegetable oils (accounting for almost $1 0 0 \%$ ). TAGs are synthesised in the ER and packaged into lipid droplets. Two enzymes catalyse DAG to produce TAG: phospholipid: diacylglycerol acyltransferase (PDAT) and acyl-CoA: diacylglycerol acyltransferase (DGAT). GhPDAT2 (Gh_A07G0967 and Gh_D07G1046) had a relatively high expression level in cottonseeds, while there was no significant difference in its expression levels among KO, CK and OE. The gene expression levels of the GhDGATs were relatively low in the early and middle stages of cottonseed development but increased sharply at 50 DPA. Particularly at 50 DPA, GhDGAT1 (Gh_A07G0119 and Gh_D07G2379) and GhDGAT2 (Gh_A11G0514 and Gh_D11G0596) were highly expressed and their expression levels were significantly higher in KO than in CK and OE. 

# 2.3 | pGhFAD2-1 Regulates Cottonseed Size

By comparing the phenotypes of mature cottonseeds, it was found that compared with CK, the length and width of seeds in KO were shortened, and the 100-seed weight decreased, while the length, width and 100-seed weight of seeds of the OE increased significantly. Among them, the length and width of KO seeds were 8.44 and $3 . 9 7 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively, which were $6 . 8 4 \%$ and $6 . 1 5 \%$ lower than those of CK seeds (9.06 and $4 . 2 3 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively) and $8 . 3 6 \%$ and $9 . 7 7 \%$ lower than those of OE seeds (9.21 and $4 . 4 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , respectively). The 100-seed weight of KO was $8 . 5 3 \mathrm { g }$ , which was $8 . 7 7 \%$ and $1 4 . 6 1 \%$ lower than that of CK (9.35 g) and OE $( 9 . 9 9 \mathrm { g } )$ , respectively (Figure 2). It indicates that regulating the expression of $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ affects cotton seed size, thereby altering the 100-seed weight of the cottonseeds. Furthermore, we used CaseViewer to observe the slices of seeds at different developmental stages, and the results showed that from 10 DPA to 20 DPA, the seeds were in a rapid growth and expansion stage. At 30–50 DPA, the size of the cottonseed decreased significantly and then stabilised (Figure S6). At the maturity stage (60 DPA), there was a downward trend in seed size, indicating that the size of the cottonseed did not change significantly after 30 DPA, only slightly decreasing when the cottonseed was dehydrated and matured. During seed development, the seed length and width of the KO showed a significant downward trend compared to those of CK and OE. The longitudinal cutting measurements of cottonseeds at different stages showed that the seed circumference and area of KO were significantly lower than those of CK, especially after 30 DPA. The phenotype of the OE was the opposite. The results indicate that regulating the expression of pGhFAD2-1 affects the size of cottonseeds. 

# 2.4 | pGhFAD2-1 Lacks Protein-Coding Capacity

Several ORFs were predicted in the pGhFAD2-1 transcript using the ORF Finder and the longest ORF is 318 bp for 105 aa, but no functional domains were found in any of the pGhFAD2-1 ORFs based on a search against protein family and domain databases (Pfam and SMART). This result indicates that pGhFAD2-1 may not have the ability to encode protein(s). To further investigate the coding potential of pGhFAD2-1, the full-length transcripts of pGhFAD2-1 and GhFAD2-1, as well as the predicted pGhFAD2-1 ORF were, respectively, cloned into a GFP expression vector containing an artificially mutated start codon (Figure  3A). GFP fluorescence imaging showed no detectable signals in cells expressing the pGhFAD2-1-GFP or pGhFAD2-1 ORF-GFP constructs (Figure  3B), consistent with Western blot results (Figure 3A). In contrast, the GhFAD2-1-GFP positive control exhibited robust fluorescence and protein expression, confirming the validity of the experimental system. Furthermore, using recombinant pYES2 vectors harbouring either the full-length pGh-FAD2-1 transcript, the full-length GhFAD2-1 coding sequence or the predicted pGhFAD2-1 ORF, we individually transformed yeast cells. SDS-PAGE analysis detected no protein expression from constructs containing either the full-length pGhFAD2-1 transcript or its predicted ORF. In contrast, the GhFAD2-1 positive control yielded the expected band (Figure 3C). These results indicate that pGhFAD2-1 lacks protein-coding capacity and suggest it may function as a lncRNA. 

# 2.5 | Identification of Interacting Proteins of pGhFAD2-1

To search for proteins interacting with pGhFAD2-1, RNA pulldown experiments were conducted. Based on RNA pull-down analysis, potential interacting proteins of pGhFAD2-1 were identified and screened. Further mass spectrometry identification and bioinformatics analysis revealed that among all differentially abundant proteins, histone deacetylase 1 (HDT1) associated with chromosomal epigenetic modifications showed the most significant difference. Moreover, RNA pull-down assay specifically identified 40S ribosomal protein S12 (RPS12) in the experimental group, with no detection in the control group. To confirm the potential interactions between HDT1 or RPS12 and pGhFAD2-1, we performed RNA immunoprecipitation (RIP) assays using antibody specific to HDT1 or RPS12. Subsequent qRT-PCR analysis of the RIP samples revealed significant enrichment of pGhFAD2-1 in HDT1- or RPS12-specific immunoprecipitates, demonstrating specific binding (Figure 4).

# 2.8 | pGhFAD2-1 Is Involved in the Feedback Regulation of GhHDT1 Expression

To investigate the molecular mechanism, we employed MST to measure the binding affinity between GhHDT1 protein and either the GhHDT1 promoter (HDT1-pro) or pGhFAD2-1. $K _ { \mathrm { d } }$ for the interaction between HDT1 protein and HDT1-pro was determined to be $0 . 5 1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Figure  6E). Upon addition of $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ to the binding system of HDT1 protein and HDT1-pro, the $K _ { \mathrm { d } }$ value increased significantly to $7 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Figure  6F), which was notably higher than the $K _ { \mathrm { d } }$ values of HDT1-pro binding to HDT1 protein or $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ alone (Figure 6G). These findings indicate that the presence of $p G h$ - FAD2-1 disrupts the binding between HDT1 protein and its own promoter, suggesting that pGhFAD2-1 may bind to HDT1 protein, thereby attenuating HDT1-mediated feedback inhibition of GhHDT1 transcription. In addition, the binding curve of HDT1 and pGhFAD2-1mut showed no interaction, indicating that pGhFAD2-1mut lacks the ability to bind to HDT1 (Figure 6H). This further demonstrates that the pGhFAD2-1 edited transcript loses its capacity to regulate downstream targets. 

Luciferase reporter assays further demonstrated that cotransformation of the HDT1-pro:LUC construct with pGreenII 

62sk-HDT1 significantly suppressed relative luciferase activity (LUC/REN) compared to the HDT1-pro:LUC control (Figure 6I). Conversely, co-expression of pGreenII 62sk-pGhFAD2-1 with both HDT1-pro:LUC and pGreenII 62sk-HDT1 restored LUC/ REN activity to a level comparable to the control, indicating that pGhFAD2-1 alleviates HDT1-mediated substrate feedback inhibition.

# 2.11 | Exogenous Application of ABA Reduces Cottonseed Size

ABA plays a dominant role in the maturation and dormancy of plant seeds. It also plays an important role in plant growth and development by improving plant tolerance to abiotic stress and inhibiting premature seed germination (Yan and Chen  2017; Tuan et al. 2018; Vishal and Kumar 2018). The results presented in the last section suggest a role of ABA in regulating the size of cottonseeds. We therefore used in  vitro ovule cultures supplemented with different concentrations of ABA to test the hypothesis. The results showed that ABA had a negative impact on ovule development; the higher the concentration, the worse the impact (Figure  7L). After 15 days of culture, the length and width of the ovules treated with $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ABA were 0.54 and 

$0 . 3 3 \mathrm { c m }$ , respectively, reduced by $2 5 . 8 4 \%$ and $2 9 . 4 1 \%$ , compared to the ovules untreated by ABA (Figure 7M). 

# 2.12 | The Relationship Between Chromatin Accessibility and Gene Transcription Activity

Given that GhHDT1 is a chromatin modification protein, we used Assay for Targeting Accessible-Chromatin with highthroughput sequencing (ATAC-seq) in combination with RNAseq to investigate how pGhFAD2-1 and its interacting protein GhHDT1 regulate genes related to fatty acid synthesis and oil accumulation. ATAC-seq generated a total of 1547.74 M clean reads, with $9 4 . 7 3 \%$ to $9 6 . 2 1 \%$ of them mapped to the cotton reference genome. Meanwhile, RNA-seq analysis was conducted on cottonseeds at the same developmental stage. The epigenome and transcriptome data showed good reproducibility (Figures S11 and S12A,B). Based on ATAC-seq, the most open chromatin was located at the intergenic regions (Figure S12C). The peaks of open chromatin in 30–40 DPA cottonseeds were primarily situated near the transcription start site (Figure S12D), with $3 4 \%$ and $1 2 \%$ of the ATAC-seq peaks differentially accessible at 30 DPA and 40 DPA, respectively (Figure S12E). The DEGs at 30 DPA and 40 DPA identified in RNA-seq were grouped into six clusters using K-means clustering analysis (Figure  S12F, Table S4). DEGs of each cluster were subjected to gene ontology enrichment analysis according to Liu et al. (2023). The results showed that the DEGs were enriched in the fatty acid metabolism pathway, lipid synthesis pathway and plant hormone signalling pathway (Table S5). 

In C1, genes related to oil accumulation, such as GhDGAT2, were significantly expressed in KO (Figure  S12G). In C5, genes related to hormones such as ABA HYPERSENSITIVE GERMINATION1 (AHG1) were more significantly activated in KO (Figure S12G). Genes in C2 were significantly enriched in the ABA signalling pathway, such as SRK2E, which had a significantly lower expression level in OE than in KO during 30–40 DPA (Figure S12G,H). C3 and C4 contained genes related to fatty acid synthesis and metabolism in cottonseeds, such as GhFAD2, LACS7 and KCS11 (Figure S12G,H). 

Of the KO versus CK and OE versus CK DEGs, $2 4 . 8 4 \%$ and $1 7 . 7 2 \%$ of them, respectively, had at least one ATAC-seq peak in their promoter or genic region (Figure 8A, Table S6). Between KO versus CK and OE versus CK, a total of 11 660 common DEGs were regulated by chromatin accessibility based on their overlapping with ATAC peaks in these two comparisons (Figure  8A, Table  S7). These common DEGs had varying expression patterns and could be separated into six clusters. At 30 DPA developing cottonseeds, DEGs of the C1 cluster showed high expression in KO and low expression in OE. At 40 DPA, DEGs of the C4 cluster exhibited high expression in KO and low expression in OE. At 30–40 DPA, DEGs of the C3 cluster exhibited low expression in KO. These three clusters (C1, C3 and C4) included a total of 6585 DEGs (Figure 8B), and they were predicted to play important roles in biological processes such as response to lipid, abscisic acid–activated signalling pathway, regulation of abscisic acid–activated signalling pathway and fatty acid biosynthetic process (Figure 8C). The representative genes of these clusters included FAD2, NCED, Abscisic Acid 

Deficient 2 (ABA2) and Phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase 2 (PDAT2) (Figure 8C, Table S8). At 30 DPA, the expression levels of GhFAD2-1, GhABA2 and GhPDAT2 were decreased in OE due to the reduction in chromatin accessibility (Figure 8D). It is worth noting that while suppressing GhHDT1 did not significantly alter the expression levels of GhPDAT2, it did affect the expression level of GhFAD2-1 (Figure 8E). 

# 3 | Discussion

# 3.1 | Evolutionary Origin of pGhFAD2-1 as a Non-Coding RNA in Cotton

For the diploid species of the Gossypium genus, it has been proposed that the ancestor of the D genome diploid species $2 n = 2 x = 2 6$ ) diverged from its sister that evolved to become other diploid cotton species about 5–10 million years ago (MYA) (Cronn and Wendel  2004). The allotetraploid cotton species (AD, $2 n = 4 x = 5 2$ ) originated from interspecific hybridization between diploid A- and D-genome species at approximately 1–2 MYA (Paterson et al. 2012). Gossypium raimondii $2 n = 2 x = 2 6$ , $\mathrm { D } _ { 5 } ^ { } ,$ ) served as a D-genome donor and participated in the genetic evolution of G. hirsutum (Wang et al. 2012; Wendel 1989), which becomes the largest cultivated species in terms of planting area because of its high yield and strong adaptability and contributes about $9 5 \%$ of the world's cotton fibre. 

Based on sequence analysis, pGhFAD2-1 orthologs $\dot { p }$ -FAD2-1) were found only in the D subgenome of allotetraploid species and all the D genome diploid species, but not in other diploids, including species with the A, B, E, F, G or K genome, suggesting that the duplication event and sequence divergence occurred in the common ancestor of the D genome diploid species. After the duplication event, the duplicate experienced sequence divergence in its promoter and gene body, creating novel canonical splicing donor and acceptor that alters the transcript of the original duplicate, leading to a new transcript without protein coding capacity. $p$ -FAD2-1 has been stably passed from the ancient D genome ancestor into the allotetraploids, indicating that, despite lack of protein coding capacity, it may function as a long noncoding RNA regulating the function of other genes.

Histone acetylation and deacetylation regulate gene expression by modulating chromatin structure: acetylation neutralises histone lysine positive charges, reducing DNA interaction and promoting an open chromatin conformation (Shahbazian and Grunstein  2007), while deacetylation typically inhibits transcription. GhHDT1, a plant-specific HDT-family protein, interacts with pGhFAD2-1 and represses GhFAD2-1 expression (Figure  5), likely by remodelling chromatin at the GhFAD2-1 promoter upon recruitment by pGhFAD2-1 via sequence similarity. During cottonseed

In plant gene regulation, feedback inhibition typically occurs when gene products (e.g., proteins or RNAs) retroactively bind their own regulatory elements (e.g., promoters), suppressing transcription or translation. This negative feedback mechanism critically modulates plant stress responses, development and metabolic homeostasis. Non-coding RNAs also participate in 

such regulatory circuits; for example, Arabidopsis COOLAIR lncRNA binds the FLC promoter, recruits PRC2 to deposit H3K27me3 and represses FLC transcription (Swiezewski et  al.  2009). Histone deacetylase (HDAC) functions as a key regulator of chromatin architecture and gene expression. Murine HDAC1 recruits NF-Y to its own promoter via SP1 motifs, subsequently repressing its transcription (Schuettengruber et al. 2003). In this study, GhHDT1 expression levels and protein abundance were significantly reduced in KO lines but elevated in OE lines and MST and dual-luciferase assays further suggest that pGhFAD2-1 induces GhHDT1 expression. 

RPS12, a key component of plant small ribosomal subunits, is primarily involved in the core processes of protein synthesis. In plants, sequence-homologous RNAs (e.g., non-coding RNAs or pre-mRNAs) regulate ribosome assembly or specific mRNA translation efficiency by competitively binding to identical or adjacent sites on RPS12. When bound RNAs impede RPS12 integration into ribosomes or mRNA binding, global translation efficiency may decrease or specific gene translation may be suppressed (Phreaner et al. 1996). Conversely, certain RNAs can enhance translation specificity or efficiency by stabilising RPS12 conformation or guiding it to target mRNAs (Rodgers et al. 2023). MST experiments in this study confirmed that both GhFAD2-1 and pGhFAD2-1 competitively bind to RPS12. This result aligns with the phenotype observed following pGhFAD2-1 knockout, in which GhFAD2-1 expression, protein abundance and enzymatic activity, along with the relative C18:2 content, were all upregulated. Furthermore, the knockdown of GhHDT1 expression led to increased GhFAD2-1 expression but did not significantly alter its protein abundance, enzymatic activity or fatty acid profile. Collectively, these findings demonstrate that pGhFAD2-1 attenuates GhFAD2-1 expression at the translational level through competitive binding to RPS12, thereby regulating the relative content of cottonseed fatty acid composition. 

GhFAD2-1 is a key enzyme gene and serves as a pivotal step in the synthesis of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) in cottonseed oil, directly governing the content of C18:2 within the oil. During the rapid oil accumulation period, oil synthesis genes (such as DGAT and PDAT) are co-expressed with FAD2-1, synergistically promoting oil and linoleic acid accumulation and the oil content shows a positive correlation with the expression levels of FAD2-1. It has also been reported that the biocatalysis of seed-specific or -abundant expression FAD2 in soybean, perilla, rice and spruce revealed that their catalytic activity was strongly correlated with the total oil content of their seeds in nature (Zhao et al. 2019). However, in natural germplasm, the relationship between oil content and the expression of FAD2- 1 is complex and context-dependent, primarily influenced by plant species, genetic background and metabolic trade-offs. Regarding the specific regulatory mechanism by which regulating the expression level of pGhFAD2-1 also affects oil content in our study, this issue still needs to be further analysed in our future research. Additionally, while KO lines showed increased oil content, the concurrent reduction in seed size led to lower total seed weight, resulting in no significant increase in final total oil yield. Conversely, OE lines exhibited larger seeds but reduced oil content; these two effects are mutually exclusive and fail to synergize. Based on our current findings, pGhFAD2-1 may therefore not be an ideal target for improving cotton oil yield, 

as its regulatory role is constrained by the negative correlation between oil content and seed size. 

# 3.3 | GhHDT1, A Target of pGhFAD2-1, Regulates Cottonseed Size via the ABA Signalling Pathway

Histone deacetylation has been implicated in the regulation of hormone responses in plants. ABA plays an important role in seed maturation and dormancy (Gutierrez et  al.  2007; Finkelstein et  al.  2008) and recent studies reveal a relationship between the function of ABA and acetylation modification of histones (Yamamuro et al. 2016; Han et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2013). The expression of Arabidopsis HDA6 and HDA19 is induced by ethylene and jasmonic acid (JA) (Zhou et al. 2005) and knocking out HDA6 and HDA19 causes ABA hypersensitivity (Chen and Wu  2010; Chen et  al.  2010). In Arabidopsis, the RPD3 subfamily members HDA9 and HDA15 are required for plant thermal response and function as suppressors of negative regulators of ABA signalling (Baek et  al.  2020; Lee and Seo  2019; Han et  al.  2023). The 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid dioxygenase (NCED) catalyses the 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid cleavage reaction and is the rate-limiting step in ABA biosynthesis and Arabidopsis NCED over-expression plants accumulated more ABA (Iuchi et al. 2001; Wan and Li 2006). It has been reported that hd2ahd2b plants had a significantly higher ABA content than WT plants and NCED9 was significantly upregulated in hd2ahd2b (Han et al. 2023). In our study, compared to CK, KO showed an increase in the expression levels of the genes involved in the ABA synthesis pathway, particularly GhNCED. In line with transcriptomic data, ABA content was relatively high in KO cottonseed kernels and lower in OE. Wei et al. (2022) reported that TSA can significantly promote the accumulation of ABA, JA and gibberellin (GA) in cotton ovules cultured in vitro and it indicated that histone deacetylation inhibits ABA synthesis and signal transduction to promote fibroblast initiation by regulating key genes in multiple plant hormone pathways. We found that, based on HDT1-RNAi, reducing the GhHDT1 expression levels increases ABA synthesis in cottonseeds and consequently reduces seed size reduction. 

Histone deacetylation-mediated auxin responses have also been reported (Yamamuro et al. 2016). The studies of TOPLESS coinhibitors of auxin signalling have begun to reveal how HAT and HDAC complexes are recruited to target gene loci in response to hormone or environmental signals and how the cumulative effects of histone acetylation and deacetylation are controlled or integrated into plant hormone signalling pathways (Long et  al.  2006; Yamamuro et  al.  2016). In plants, the TSAmediated inhibition of HDACs resulted in chromatin conformational changes that enhance gene expression due to histone hyperacetylation (Görisch et al. 2005). In Arabidopsis, YUCCA genes YUC1 and YUC10 and transcription factors including LEC1 and LEC2 involved in auxin biosynthesis were found to be distinctly upregulated during TSA-induced somatic embryogenesis (Wójcikowska et  al.  2018) and TSA-induced explants accumulated salicylic acid (SA) and ABA, suggesting the role of histone acetylation in regulating hormone-related responses (Wójcikowska et al. 2024). In our study, regulating the expression levels of pGhFAD2-1 affected the content of its interacting protein GhHDT1 in developing cottonseeds. During this 

process, the expression levels of the key genes, GhYUC, GhAUX and GhARF, involved in IAA synthesis and signal transduction, showed significant changes. However, although the content of IAA increased in the cottonseeds of KO and HDT1-RNAi plants, there was no significant difference. 

Our results indicated that reducing the level of GhHDT1 during cottonseed development significantly promotes the accumulation of ABA, consistent with the results reported by Wei et al. (2022). In general, our research suggests that regulating the expression of pGhFAD2-1 affects the synthesis of hormones, primarily ABA, during cottonseed development by affecting the expression of its interacting protein GhHDT1. ABA plays an important role in seed development (McCarty 1995; Finkelstein and Gibson 2002; Kanno et al. 2010; Léon-Kloosterziel et al. 1996), and the synthesis and response of ABA are important for regulating seed size (Zhang et al. 2023). ABA negatively mediates seed size by influencing the timing of endosperm cellularisation and ABA 

signalling component ABSCISIC ACID-INSENSITIVE5 (ABI5) directly binds to the promoter region of SHORT HYPOCOTYL UNDER BLUE1 (SHB1) during early seed development (Cheng et  al.  2014). Consistent with the published results, we found that KO cottonseeds contained higher levels of ABA and were smaller; in contrast, OE cottonseeds contained relatively low levels of ABA and were relatively larger. 

Based on the results, we propose a working model for the roles of pGhFAD2-1 in regulating C18:1 and C18:2 ratios of seed oil and seed size (Figure 9). At the transcriptional level, pGhFAD2-1, as a lncRNA, recruits GhHDT1 to interact with the GhFAD2-1 promoter, thereby suppressing its expression. At the translational level, pGhFAD2-1 inhibits GhFAD2 protein synthesis by competitively binding to RPS12 with GhFAD2-1 mRNA, ultimately disrupting fatty acid component biosynthesis during cotton seed development. # 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Cloning and Analysis of pGhFAD2-1 Sequence

The genome sequences and annotation files of 26 different Gossypium species were downloaded from CottonGen (https:// www.cottongen.org). For Gh_D13G2237, we found that its transcript based on our transcriptome data (Liu et al. 2019; Ma et al. 2021) is different from the annotated Gh_D13G2237 coding sequence from G. hirsutum (AD1_NBI). We then used rapid amplification of cDNA ends (RACE) to clone the full-length cDNA of Gh_D13G2237 (hereafter pGhFAD2-1). To that end, total RNA was extracted from cottonseeds at 30 DPA using NucleoSpin RNA Plant (TakaRa). The $5 ^ { \prime }$ and ′ cDNA fragments of $p G h$ - FAD2-1 were, respectively, amplified with a universal primer A mix (UPM, the SMARTer RACE $5 ^ { \prime } / 3 ^ { \prime }$ Kit, Clontech) and $p G h$ - FAD2-1 specific primers (Table S9). A touchdown PCR was performed according to the user manual of the SMARTer RACE $5 ^ { \prime } / "$ Kit. The cDNA sequence of pGhFAD2-1 was then used as a query to identify its homologous sequences in the genomes of 25 other cotton species through blast search, which were then used in sequence alignment. 

# 4.2 | Generation of pGhFAD2-1 Over-Expression and Gene-Editing Plants

The pGhFAD2-1 over-expression vector with seed-specific promoter was constructed using gateway technology according to Liu et al. (2019). For gene editing, two sgRNAs targeting pGhFAD2-1 were designed and assembled into the same pRGEB32-GhU6.7-NPTII expression vector according to Qin et  al.  (2020). The recombinant vector was transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (LBA4404) for cotton transformation with the Upland cotton cv. YZ-1 as recipient. Tissue culture and generation of transgenic plants were carried out by following the previously described method (Liu et al. 2017). 

# 4.3 | Western Blot

Total protein was extracted from cottonseed and was quantified by Bradford assay. Then $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ protein samples of each group were separated by denaturing polyacrylamide electrophoresis on $1 2 \%$ sodium dodecylsulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) gels and resolved proteins were electroblotted onto polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes (Millipore) in a transferring buffer $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris, $0 . 2 { \mathrm { M } }$ glycine and $2 5 \%$ methanol) using electro-transfer. After blocking with $5 \%$ skimmed milk, the membranes were incubated with anti-histone deacetylase antibody (Agrisera), anti-fatty acid desaturase 2 antibody (Beyotime), anti-40S Ribosomal Protein S12 antibody (Beyotime) or anti- $\cdot \beta$ -actin antibody (MERCK), 

followed by incubation with HRP-conjugated Affinipure Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG $\left( \mathrm { H } + \mathrm { L } \right)$ (Proteintech). 

# 4.4 | RNA Pull-Down and RNA Immunoprecipitation (RIP)

The synthesis of $p G h F A D 2 – 1$ was carried out with MAXIscript Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The interaction proteins of pGhFAD2-1 were examined using the Pierce Magnetic RNA-Protein Pull-Down Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) following the instructions of the manufacturer. After washing and eluting, the pGhFAD2-1- binding protein complexes were analysed by mass spectrometry (MS, 5600-plus, AB SCIEX). Furthermore, western blot analysis was performed for the interacting proteins of pGhFAD2-1 identified by MS. 

The Magna RIP kit (Millipore) was used in the RIP assay following the manufacturer's instructions. Cottonseeds were ground into powder in liquid nitrogen and then lysed in complete RIP lysis buffer. After centrifugation of the samples at $1 2 0 0 0 \times g$ for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the supernatants were immunoprecipitated with antibodies and incubated with magnetic protein A/G beads (Sigma-Aldrich) for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The immune complexes were washed five times with a wash buffer. An aliquot of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ immunocomplexes was resuspended in SDS-PAGE buffer and used in Western blotting analysis. The remaining immunocomplexes were purified using Trizol and ethanol precipitation and subjected to evaluation of the expression of pGhFAD2-1 by qRT-PCR analysis. The histone deacetylase antibody (Agrisera) and the 40S Ribosomal Protein S12 antibody (Beyotime) were, respectively, used for identifying HDT1 and RPS12. The input served as the loading control and IgG as the specific binding control in the RIP assay. 

# 4.5 | Subcellular Localization Analysis

For subcellular localization analysis, the GhHDT1 coding sequence was cloned into the pAN580-GFP vector to generate the 35S::GhHDT1-GFP construct, which was then introduced into the A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. The subcellular localization of GhHDT1 was determined in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaf epidermal cells by A. tumefaciens infiltration. GFP fluorescence was observed using the Zeiss LSM 800 confocal microscope $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after the injection. 

# 4.6 | Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH) Analysis

Using SweAMI technology, specific oligonucleotides targeting pGhFAD2-1 or GhFAD2-1 were synthesised (Table  S9) and used as probes for pGhFAD2-1 and GhFAD2-1, respectively. After rinsing with PBS, cottonseeds were immediately fixed with in  situ hybridisation fixative for over $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ . FISH experiments were carried out according to Dresselhaus and Bleckmann  (2020). The nuclei were stained using DAPI and the signals were observed and photographed by confocal microscopy (Nikon). 

# 4.7 | Analysing the Protein Encoding Ability of pGhFAD2-1

The entire pGhFAD2-1 transcript, pGhFAD2-1 ORF sequences and FLAG-tag sequence were, respectively, amplified and cloned into pGreenII-62-SK vector, in which the GFP start codon (ATGGTG) was mutated to ATTGTT (pGreenII-62- SK-GFPmut). The constructed vectors were, respectively, transformed into Agrobacterium LBA4404 and transiently transformed into tobacco cells to observe GFP fluorescence signal. Meanwhile, the pGhFAD2-1 transcript and pGhFAD2-1 ORF sequences were, respectively, cloned into the pYES2 vector and transformed into Saccharomyces cerevisiae INVScI. The total protein of yeast was extracted and an equal amount of protein lysates was resolved in $1 0 \%$ SDS-PAGE to observe the differential bands. 

# 4.8 | Protein Purification

The target proteins were expressed as C-terminal $6 \times \mathrm { H i s } .$ - tagged fusion proteins. Full-length GhHDT1 or GhRPS12 was cloned into the pCZN1 vector and transformed into E. coli BL21. Bacterial cultures were grown overnight at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by induction with $0 . 4 \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl- $\beta$ -Dthiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. For protein purification, cells were lysed by sonication in Buffer A $2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–Cl, pH 8.0, 300 mM NaCl, $0 . 5 \%$ NP-40, 10 mM imidazole). His-tagged proteins were affinity-purified using nickel beads (Invitrogen), eluted with Buffer B $2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–Cl, $\mathrm { p H } ~ 8 . 0$ , 300 mM NaCl, $2 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole), and subsequently dialyzed and concentrated. 

# 4.9 | Biotin-Coupled RNA Pull-Down Assay

Biotin-labelled pGhFAD2-1 was generated via in vitro transcription using RNA polymerase (Yeasen) and Bio-16-UTP (Thermo Fisher). RNA-bead complexes were formed by incubating $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of biotin-labelled pGhFAD2-1 with Pierce streptavidin magnetic beads, washing twice with $1 \times \mathrm { P B S T }$ , then incubating with in vitro transcribed GhFAD2-1 mRNA $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ retained as input). After magnetic separation and four washes, bound RNA was eluted twice using preheated biotin/TE buffer $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 15 min each), with supernatants pooled for qRT-PCR analysis of captured GhFAD2-1 mRNA. 

# 4.10 | Microscale Thermophoresis (MST)

Recombinant proteins were dialyzed against MST buffer (PBS containing $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween-20) and labelled with an MST fluorescent dye (NanoTemper MO-L018) following the manufacturer's protocol. The single-stranded nucleotide sequence corresponding to the residual fragment of edited pGhFAD2-1 in KO plants (designated pGhFAD2-1mut) was chemically synthesised. Binding affinities between fluorescently labelled proteins (GhHDT1 and GhRPS12) and ligand nucleic acids (pGhFAD2-1, pGhFAD2-1mut, GhFAD2-1, and HDT1- pro) were measured using a MONOLITH NT.115 system (NanoTemper Technologies) in PBST binding buffer (PBS 

with $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween-20). For each assay, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of target protein was mixed with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of serially diluted ligand (twofold dilutions) at room temperature, with biological replicates performed to ensure reproducibility. Dose–response curves were generated by plotting ligand concentration against normalised fluorescence changes. Dissociation constants $( K _ { \mathrm { d } } )$ were determined using NanoTemper Analysis software, and curve fitting was performed in GraphPad Prism 9 with $9 5 \%$ confidence intervals. 

# 4.11 | Luciferase Complementation Assay

pGhFAD2-1 and the coding sequences of GhHDT1 were, respectively, cloned into pGreenII 62-SK, while the HDT1 promoter was inserted into pGreenII 0800-LUC as a reporter. All constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101, and tobacco leaves were co-infiltrated with bacterial suspensions containing either: (1) HDT1-pro:LUC alone, (2) HDT1-pro:LUC and pGreenII 62-SK-HDT1 or (3) HDT1-pro:LUC, pGreenII 62-SK-HDT1 and pGreenII 62-SK-pGhFAD2-1, with pGreenII 62-SK-AtMYB75/0800-LUC-AtDFRpro serving as a negative control. Luciferase activities were measured using a dual-luciferase reporter assay kit (Yeasen, 11402ES60), with LUC values normalised to Renilla REN for relative activity quantification, employing three biological and three technical replicates. 

# 4.12 | Cottonseed Oil and Fatty Acid Composition Analysis

Sampling of cottonseeds at different developmental stages followed our previous method (Ma et al. 2021). Seed oil was extracted and calculated according to Liu et al. (2019) and Ma et al. (2021). For fatty acid composition analysis, the KOH-methanol method was used for fatty acid methylation, and the qualitative and quantitative analyses were performed using GCMS-QP2020, according to Liu et al. (2019) and Ma et al. (2021). 

# 4.13 | Construction of RNAi Vector of GhHDT1 and Cotton Transformation

To make the RNAi construct targeting GhHDT1 of G. hirsutum, the full-length GhHDT1 was amplified from G. hirsutum cv. YZ-1. Based on RNAi vector pANDA35HK and using the gateway technology, the RNAi vector for GhHDT1 was successfully constructed and transferred into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain LBA4404, which was then used to transform hypocotyl explants of YZ1 to obtain HDT1-RNAi transgenic plants as previously described (Jin et  al.  2006; Liu et al. 2017). 

# 4.14 | Seed Phenotyping

Cottonseed length and width were measured using conventional methods. One hundred-seed weight was measured by weighing 100 healthy seeds. Cottonseed size at different times was observed using an upright optical microscope (Nikon Eclipse E100). Seed area and perimeter at different times were 

measured by image software (NIS elements viewer 5.21). The distribution of oil bodies in cottonseeds was observed by transmission electron microscopy according to Liu et al. (2019). 

# 4.15 | Determination of Phytohormones

The quantitative analysis of hormones in cottonseeds was conducted according to Pan et al. (2010). Cottonseeds were ground into powder in liquid nitrogen. Fifty milligrams of sample was dissolved in 1 mL methanol/water/formic acid (15:4:1, V/V/V). The contents of phytohormones were detected by MetWare (http://www.metware.cn/) based on the AB Sciex QTRAP 6500 LC–MS/MS platform. 

# 4.16 | RNA-Seq Analysis

Total RNA was extracted from 30 to 60 DPA cottonseeds of transgenic plants and non-transgenic plants using the RNAprep Pure Plant Kit (Tiangen) according to the instructions provided by the manufacturer. For each sample, $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA was used for the preparation of the RNA sequencing library. RNA sequencing was conducted by Biomarker Technologies Co. Ltd. (Beijing) using the HiSeq.2500 platform. After quality control, the clean paired-end reads were mapped to the G. hirsutum reference genome (TM-1, Zhang et al. 2015) and the gene expression levels were estimated by fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments mapped reads (FPKM). 

# 4.17 | In Vitro Ovule Culture

Cotton bolls at 1 DPA from YZ-1 and HDT1-RNAi plants were sterilised with $7 5 \%$ ethanol for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ followed by washing 5 times with distilled water. About 100 sterilised ovules were carefully dissected under sterile conditions and were cultured at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 15 days in liquid BT medium, respectively, supplemented with 1 or $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Abscisic acid (ABA) (Beasley and Ting  1973). The ovules cultured in BT medium without ABA were used as controls. Ovule length and width were, respectively, observed and measured at 3, 6, 9, 12 and 15 days after culture. 

# 4.18 | Quantitative Real-Time PCR (qRT-PCR) Analysis

Total RNA was extracted from seeds using RNAiso Plus (Takara). qRT-PCR was carried out according to Liu et al. (2019) using the LightCycler 480 II (Roche). All qRT-PCR reactions were performed in triplicate. The relative expression levels of the target genes were calculated with the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). 

# 4.19 | Yeast One-Hybrid (Y1H) Analysis

Y1H analysis was performed using a commercial kit (Clontech) as described in the manufacturer's protocol. Briefly, pAbAi 

vectors harbouring a fragment of GhFAD2-1 were integrated into the yeast genome (Y1H Gold strain). A yeast reporter vector, p53-AbAi construct, was used as a positive control (Clontech). The coding sequence of GhHDT1 was amplified from G. hirsutum cv. YZ-1 and then was introduced into the pGADT7 AD vector (Clontech). Yeast transformation and evaluations of interactions were performed as previously described (Cheng et al. 2013). After culture on selection plates for 3 days, activation of the yeast growth was observed on (SD/−Leu) medium containing $3 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ Aureobasidin A. 

# 4.20 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA)

Light Shift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (ThermoFisher SCIENTIFIC) was used in this experiment. The GST-GhHDT1 protein was purified using glutathione-agarose beads (BD biosciences) after incubation. The biotin-labelled probes (Table S9) were synthesised by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai). The detailed procedure of EMSA was following the manufacturer's instructions. Photos were taken using a charge-coupled device (CCD) camera. 

# 4.21 | ATAC-Seq (Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin With High Throughput Sequencing)

Cottonseeds at 30 DPA and 40 DPA were used for ATAC-seq. The transposing reaction system was configured with the Tn5 Transposase. The seed cell nuclei were suspended with the transposing reaction system, and the DNA was purified after incubating at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. Raw reads were processed using the Cutadapt software to remove the adapters and reads with a length less than 35 bp and low quality. The high-quality reads were aligned to the TM-1 cotton reference genome (G. hirsutum, ad1, NBI) using the Bowtie2 software to obtain the alignment efficiency of each sample and the position information of the reads on the genome. DeepTools v2.07 was used to map the density distribution of sequencing reads in the 3-kb interval upstream and downstream of the TSS of each gene. The quantification of ATAC-seq and the correlations between transcriptome and epigenome data were calculated according to Liu et al. (2023) and Zhang et al. (2024). 

# 4.22 | Statistical Analysis and Primer Sequences

Microsoft Excel 2019 and SPSS 18.0 (SPSS, Chicago, USA) were used for data processing and analysis of variance. The means of treatments were compared according to Duncan's multiple range tests at the 0.05 probability level. Different letters were used to indicate significant differences; in the figures, error bars represent the standard deviations of the means. Origin 2021 (Origin Lab, Northampton, USA) was used to plot the figures. All gene-specific primers for vector construction and qRT-PCR are listed in Table S9.
