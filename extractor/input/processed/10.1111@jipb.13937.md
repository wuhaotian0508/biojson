he phytoparasites of the Orobanchaceae family invade the root systems of host plants such as sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), maize (Zea mays), and tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) by producing haustoria, which competitively take up water and nutrients from the host, ultimately causing serious yield losses (Li et al., 2023). Seed germination of phytoparasitic species is triggered by the specific perception of host‐ derived strigolactones (SLs), a class of phytohormone biosynthesized through the carotenoid pathway, which activate downstream signal transduction by interacting with the DWARF14 (D14) receptor and its homologs, regulating critical physiological processes, such as branch development, nutrient uptake, and stress responses in various plants (Hu et al., 2024).

Early strategies have attempted to prevent parasitic plant seed germination primarily by repressing SL biosynthesis in host plants. For instance, mutations in the host plant sorghum Non‐dormant Axillary bud 1 (NAB1) gene disrupted SL biosynthesis and thus reduced Striga seed germination. However, this approach concurrently caused significant yield penalties, including diminished kernel size (length, width, and thickness) and 1,000‐kernel weight in the host plants (Chen et al., 2018). Moreover, SL‐deficient mutants showed reduced ABA responsiveness and decreased anthocyanin biosynthesis, which may contribute to their increased drought susceptibility (Li et al., 2019). These findings demonstrated that SL deficiency disrupted both host plant growth homeostasis and abiotic stress responses. Subsequently, researchers have developed another strategy, utilizing specific SL analogs (e.g., sphynolactone‐7 and GR24) to induce suicidal seed germination of parasitic plants (Uraguchi et al., 2018). This approach effectively reduced parasitic plant seed viability by triggering suicide germination in environments devoid of host plants. Nevertheless, the development of SL analogs requires further investigation into their structure–activity relationships and comprehensive biosafety assessments to ensure ecological security and practical applicability of SL analogs.

Recently, Shi et al. (2025) identified two genes encoding ATP‐binding cassette subtype G (ABCG) transporters in sorghum, namely SL TRANS-PORTER 1 (SbSLT1) and SbSLT2. Plants carrying double knockout mutations of these genes significantly suppressed SL exudation from the host roots into the rhizosphere. As a result, this genetic modification effectively blocked seed germination of the parasitic plants, significantly contributing to reducing crop yield losses in Striga‐infested fields (Figure 1A). Concurrently, Ban et al. (2025) demonstrated that the dual‐function ABCG transporters SlABCG44 and SlABCG45 in tomato mediated both root‐to‐ rhizosphere exudation of SL and their long‐distance transport from roots to aerial tissues. Interestingly, SlABCG45 expression was strongly induced by phosphate (Pi) deficiency, which serves as an environmental signal inducing SL biosynthesis and exudation, whereas SlABCG44 expression was weakly responsive to Pi deficiency. Thus, the single knockout mutant

of SlABCG45 showed dramatically decreased SL exudation, paving the way for developing phytoparasite‐avoiding crops (Figure 1B). These breakthroughs demonstrated that the specialized SL transporters SbSLT1/2 in sorghum and SlABCG44/45 in tomato shared the conserved molecular mechanisms, functioning through SL exudation to promote phytoparasitic seed germination.

Subsequently, AlphaFold2 analyses and mutation site verification revealed that phenylalanine residues (SbSLT1F693/SbSLT2F642) in the flexible loop of the substrate release region formed a conserved core element of the transport channel, with site‐directed mutagenesis significantly reducing the SL transport efficiency of SbSLT1 and SbSLT2 (Shi et al., 2025). The homologous proteins SbSLT1‐LIKE/SbSLT2‐LIKE lost transport activity in the absence of this conserved site in sorghum, further confirming the central role of this domain in the SL‐specific transport. Subsequently, a protein sequence comparison demonstrated cross‐group conservation of the phenylalanine residue between monocotyledonous and dicotyledonous SL transporters (e.g., SbSLT1/2, ZmSLT1/2, and SlABCG44), suggesting the evolutionary conservation of this transport mechanism in angiosperms.

Current studies have indicated that SL transporters localize to the root plasma membrane system, with their expression being co‐regulated by low‐Pi stress, SL biosynthesis, and SL signaling (Ban et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025). Pi deficiency induced the expression of ABCG family transporter genes, facilitating SL exudation from the roots into the rhizosphere (Shi et al., 2025). Notably, NODULATION SIGNALING PATHWAY 1/2 (SlNSP1/2) directly bound to the promoter of SlABCG45 to drive its transcription, establishing a positive feedback regulatory loop that coupled SL “biosynthesis–exudation” dynamics in tomato (Ito et al., 2022; Ban et al., 2025). This molecular network enhanced the efficiency of plant nutrient uptake through combined morphological (root/tiller modulation) and chemical (SL exudation) adaptations, boosting crop resilience to nutrient stresses.

It is noted that loss‐of‐function mutations in SL transporter‐ encoding genes have less effect on the growth and development of plants than do the corresponding mutations in SL‐biosynthetic genes. SlABCG45 knockout lines exhibited significantly impaired root‐to‐shoot SL transport and slightly increased shoot branching but had little effect on fruit size (Ban et al., 2025). These findings suggested that endogenous SL homeostasis imbalance may have affected developmental processes through feedback regulation. The classical theory posits that SLs negatively regulated tillering by accelerating the degradation of transcriptional repressors D53 (Hu et al., 2024). This might indicate the existence of a novel tillering regulatory mechanism that is not closely associated with SL concentration. The specific molecules involved in this process, such as those in the D53 protein degradation pathway or in microenvironmental signal reprogramming in axillary buds, require further investigation.

In agricultural applications, SL transporter‐deficient genotypes exhibited increased resistance to parasitic plants and significantly improved productivity in fields infested with phytoparasite seeds (Ban et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025). Especially, knockout of SlABCG45 significantly improved the tomato resistance to different broomrape species through modulation of the SL transport system, thereby elevating fruit yield in Phelipanche‐infested fields (Ban et al., 2025). Critically, the arbuscular mycorrhizal fungal (AMF) colonization assay revealed the decreased expression of AMF marker genes in the SlABCG45 knockout mutant (Ban et al., 2025), suggesting that SL transport defects may impair mycorrhizal symbiosis establishment by interfering with symbiotic signal transduction. Future agricultural practices require monitoring to reduce the impact of SL modulation on AMF symbiosis in Pi‐deficient soils, while tracking the impact of SL dynamics on microbial communities and crop nutrition uptake to address soil Pi pollution and long‐term sustainability goals.

# CONCLUDING REMARKS AND PERSPECTIVES

Studies in both sorghum and tomato revealed the molecular mechanisms of SL transport, with potential application for combating phytoparasitic infestation (Ban et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025). SLs are critical molecules enabling the communication between host plants and microorganisms, as evidenced by their dual function in promoting AMF symbiosis and seed germination of parasitic weeds. Therefore, we proposed the following research perspectives:

(i) A key debate centers on whether this process is driven by a host‐regulated active signal or represents a stress response induced by external environmental factors such as nutrient deprivation or symbiotic signals. The underlying mechanisms governing the active or passive exudation of SLs will thus emerge as a core research focus.   
(ii) Pi deficiency synergistically induced the expression of genes related to both SL biosynthesis and exudation, but intracellular SL accumulation triggered negative feedback inhibition of the SL biosynthesis pathway. Thus, the regulatory network coordinating SL biosynthesis and exudation needs to be systematically dissected.   
(iii) The significant impact of root‐accumulated SLs on aerial organ development suggested potential long‐distance transport pathways in root‐to‐shoot systems. The molecular basis underlying this inter‐organ regulatory network urgently requires clarification.

Interestingly, the interaction between host plants and parasitic weeds resembles an “arms race”, with each party evolving novel strategies to fight the other, resulting in intricate co‐evolutionary relationships. Plants have evolved a functional division between canonical and noncanonical SLs. The latter SLs primarily act as signals to microbes and plants, while the former are primarily phytohormones (Zhou et al., 2025). Host plants alter the composition of root exudates to confuse parasitic weeds (Li et al., 2023). Concurrently, parasitic weeds enhance signal sensitivity through the cooperative multi‐receptor system S. hermonthica HYPO‐SENSITIVE TO LIGHT 4‐9 (ShHTL4‐9), enabling the detection of nanomolar SL concentrations and recognition of structural variants (Wang et al., 2022). Such interactive pressures shape the complexity of the SL signaling system, with the exudation mechanisms being jointly influenced by genetic regulation,
