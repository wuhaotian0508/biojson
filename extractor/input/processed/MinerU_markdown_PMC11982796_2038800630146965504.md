# GCN5-related histone acetyltransferase HOOKLESS2 regulates fungal resistance and growth in tomato

Namrata Jaiswal $\textcircled{1}$ , Chao-Jan Liao , Ayomi Indika Hewavidana and Tesfaye Mengiste 

Department of Botany and Plant Pathology, Purdue University, West Lafayette, IN 47907, USA 

Author for correspondence: Tesfaye Mengiste Email: mengiste@purdue.edu 

Received: 18 June 2024 Accepted: 5 February 2025 

New Phytologist (2025) 246: 1217–1235 doi: 10.1111/nph.70025 

Key words: Botrytis cinerea, chromatin, fungal resistance, histone acetylation, HOOKLESS, plant architecture, plant defense. 

# Summary

 The functions of histone acetyltransferases (HATs) in the genetic control of crop traits and the underlying mechanisms are poorly understood. We studied the function of tomato HOOKLESS2 (SlHLS2), a member of the GCN5 family of HATs, through genetic, molecular and genomic approaches. 

 Tomato hls2 mutants generated through CRISPR-cas9 gene editing show enhanced susceptibility to fungal infection, accelerated dark-induced senescence, grossly altered plant architecture, and loss of fertility accompanied by high levels of auxin accumulation. 

 Components of the basal and induced transcriptome underlying the extensive phenotypic impact of HLS2 were uncovered. HLS2-dependent differentially expressed genes were enriched for genes implicated in photosynthesis, protein ubiquitination, oxylipin biosynthesis, autophagy, and response to biotic stimuli. In particular, induced expressions of the BTB/POZ and TAZ domain-containing protein gene (SlBT1), AUTOPHAGY-RELATED PROTEIN 11, ACYLSUGAR ACYLTRANSFERASE 3 (ASAT3), and multiple jasmonate biosynthesis genes require functional HLS2. 

 SlHLS2 associates with the SlBT1 promoter, and histone acetylation at the chromatin of SlBT1 was reduced in the Slhls2 mutant suggesting direct regulation of SlBTB1 by HLS2. SlBTB1 is an adapter of an E3 ubiquitin-protein ligase complex (CUL3-RBX1-BTB), which mediates ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of proteins. HLS2 is degraded after fungal inoculation, which is stabilized by inhibition of the 26S proteasome. Overall, tomato HLS2 functions in pathogen responses, plant architecture, and fertility. 

# Introduction

Different biological processes are regulated through the activation or repression of gene expression, which in turn occurs through transcriptional and post-transcriptional regulatory networks. Histone modifications are key players in the activation and repression of gene expression underlying various biological processes (Pandey, 2002) as they modulate the accessibility of DNA to enzymes involved in DNA processing. Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) and deacetylases (HDACs) mediate the reversible modifications of histone tails, modulating gene expressions in development and responses to environmental cues. HATs are ‘writer’ proteins that add an acetyl group on histones lysine residues and, generally, cause gene activation (Marmorstein & Roth, 2001; Carrozza et al., 2003; Ma et al., 2013). 

GCN5-related N-acetyltransferases (GNATs) contain an N-terminal HAT domain and a C-terminal bromodomain, considered to be a targeting motif (Dhalluin et al., 1999; Ornaghi et al., 1999). GCN5 is the primary HAT that regulates plant gene expression (Servet et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 2017). Mutations or downregulation of Arabidopsis GCN5 increases plant sensitivity to a variety of stresses and causes pleiotropic 

developmental phenotypes, such as dwarfism, aberrant organ development, and flower organ identity (Kim et al., 2018; Kotak et al., 2018; Poulios & Vlachonasios, 2018). Epigenetic reprogramming through GCN5 promotes competency for shoot regeneration by controlling shoot apical meristem through control of meristem-regulatory genes and promotes callus formation through expression of root stem cell factors (Kumar et al., 2021). GCN5 promotes transcription of WUSCHEL-related homeobox (WOX ), SCARECROW (SCR), and PLETHORA (PLT ) stem cell regulators through histone acetylation (HAc) at their promoters (Kim et al., 2018). 

Histone acetylation is a significant regulatory mechanism in plant gene expression (Yamamuro et al., 2016; Jiang et al., 2020; Ueda & Seki, 2020). It is also associated with memory for systemic acquired resistance (Jaskiewicz et al., 2011; Gkizi et al., 2021). GCN5 complexes with the adaptor proteins alteration/deficiency in activation 2 (ADA2) (Mao et al., 2006), and this complex functions in growth and stress responses (Hu et al., 2015; Zheng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019). ADA2b mediates HAc at auxin-responsive genes (Anzola et al., 2010). The transcription factor (TF) bZIP11 interacts with ADA2b and recruits the ADA2b-GCN5 to the auxin-responsive gene 

Gretchen Hagen 3.3 (GH3.3) and indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), which leads to increased H3K27 acetylation (Weiste & Dr€oge-Laser, 2014). GCN5 regulates leaf development and trichome morphogenesis by stimulating its transferase activity of ADA2b (Kotak et al., 2018). The induction of heat-responsive genes is impaired in the Arabidopsis gcn5 mutant is impaired (Hu et al., 2015) and modulates the expression of ECERIFERUM3 (CER3), a gene implicated in wax biosynthesis, by acetylation of H3K9/14 lysine residues (Wang et al., 2018). 

The role of HATs in plant immunity has not been explored except for Arabidopsis CBP/p300-type HAC1 and HAC5. Arabidopsis HAC1/5 function in salicylic acid (SA)-mediated immunity (Jin et al., 2018). In Arabidopsis, HAC1/5 form a coactivator complex with NON-EXPRESSER OF PR GENES (NPR1), and TGA (TGACG-BINDING FACTOR) to activate the expression of PATHOGENESIS RELATED (PR) genes (Jin et al., 2018). HAC1/5 mutation diminishes PR induction and plant resistance to pathogens. HAC1 also regulates responses to jasmonoyl-isoleucine (JA-ile), which is the active form of JA. HAC1 acetylates histones associated with MYC2 target genes to promote their expression. MYC2 interacts with Mediator complex subunit, MED25 and recruits HAC1 to target genes (An et al., 2017). Also, HAC-dependent HAc is necessary for primed innate immunity and bacterial resistance in Arabidopsis (Singh et al., 2014). In soybean, GCN5-mediated HAc at defense-related genes is important for their activation and plant immunity (Kong et al., 2017). Arabidopsis HLS1 is a GNC5 like HAT that interacts with MEDIATOR SUBUNIT 18 (MED18) and associates with the promoter of WRKY33 to mediate the defense response (Liao et al., 2016). HLS1 acts as a positive regulator during thermos-morphogenesis by interacting with PIF4 and forms a regulatory module by targeting the promoter of the PIF4-targeted gene (Jin & Zhu, 2018; Jin et al., 2020). The pear fruit HLS1 positively regulates HAc at the WRKY31 promoter and induces the expression of SWEET15, which is a sugar transporter required for sugar accumulation in fruits (Li et al., 2020). 

HATs are known to control diverse biological functions in model plant and animal systems. However, their function in plant biotic interactions, especially in crop plants, has not been characterized. To address this gap, we studied the functions of tomato HOOKLESS2, which is a HAT showing features of the GCN5 family protein. Tomato SlHLS2 was induced by pathogens and the plant hormones ethylene, auxin, and abscisic acid. Significantly, tomato hls2 (Slhls2) mutants generated through CRISPR-cas9 gene editing show enhanced susceptibility to fungal pathogens, accelerated dark-induced senescence, altered plant architecture, and loss of fertility. Transcriptome analysis revealed genes implicated in biological processes, such as photosynthesis, protein ubiquitination, oxylipin biosynthesis, transcription, and responses to biotic stimulus were expressed in HLS2-dependent manner. Among these, SlHLS2 is required for the expression of BTB/POZ and TAZ domain-containing protein (SlBT1), AUTOPHAGY-RELATED 11 (ATG11), ACYLSUGAR ACYL-TRANSFERASE 3 (ASAT3), and SAC DOMAIN-CONTAIN-ING PROTEIN 8 (SAC8). Interestingly, SlHLS2-Myc associates with the SlBT1 locus and, consistent with a predicted function in 

HAc, the level of H3Ac at SlBT1 chromatin was reduced in the Slhls2 mutant, suggesting SlBT1 is a direct target of SlHLS2. Overall, HLS2 is required for plant growth traits, fertility, and pathogen responses that are underpinned by HAc and its broad-spectrum impact on the transcriptome. 

# Materials and Methods

# Phylogenetic analysis

Sequences for HATs were obtained from https://solgenomics. net/ for tomato (Solanum lycopersicum L.) and https://www. arabidopsis.org/ for Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana (L.) Heynh). These sequences were used for constructing a phylogenetic tree using Molecular Evolutionary Genetics Analysis Version X (MEGA X) (Kumar et al., 2018). Sequences were aligned using MUSCLE software with the default value. Then, the aligned sequences were used to construct a phylogenetic tree using the maximum likelihood method and JTT matrix-based model (Jones et al., 1992). 

# CRISPR cas9 construct and generation of SlHLS2-myc transgenic lines

To generate Slhls2 mutants, two specific guide RNAs (gRNAs) were designed (Supporting Information Table S1) as we recently described (Bvindi et al., 2022) using the CRISPR Plant GRNA DESIGN software https://www.genome.arizona.edu/crispr/ CRISPRsearch.html. The two gRNAs were cloned into the CRISPR/Cas9 vector pKSE401 according to Xing et al. (2014). To generate SlHLS2-myc transgenic lines, full-length HLS2 gene was amplified from tomato cDNA using a gene-specific primer (Table S1). The HLS2 cDNA was cloned into a gateway entry vector, pCR8 GW TOPO, followed by LR reaction using LR Clonase to the destination vector pEarleyGate 203. The constructs were electroporated into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. 

# Plant growth, pathogen assays, hormone treatments, and senescence assays

Tomato wild-type (WT) plants, mutants, and overexpression lines (Castlermart II, CMII background) were grown in plastic pots containing compost soil (Sun Gro Metro Mix 510) in a growth room or glasshouse with a photoperiod extended to $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ under fluorescent lights at a temperature of $2 0 { - } 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Botrytis cinerea and Alternaria solani culture preparations and plant disease assays were used as recently described by Xu et al. (2018) and Jaiswal et al. (2022). Assays were conducted by drop-inoculation of $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { l } }$ of $1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores $\mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 }$ spore suspension on tomato detached leaves. Disease lesions were measured at 3 d postinoculation (DPI). 

Bacterial disease assays were done as previously described (AbuQamar et al., 2008). Leaves of 5- to 6-wk-old tomato plants were infiltrated with bacterial suspensions $( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 0 0 0 2$ or $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ in $1 0 \ \mathrm { m M } \ \mathrm { M g C l _ { 2 } } ,$ ) of the Pseudomonas syringae pv tomato DC3000-DavrptoB. To determine bacterial growth, leaf disks 

from infiltrated leaves were collected at 0 and 3 DPI. Each experiment for the bacterial growth assay was performed in three replicates. At each time point, three leaf disks were collected from WT and Slhls2 mutant plants for each replicate. Leaf disks of the same size were collected using a hole puncher, and bacterial titer per leaf area was determined. 

For hormones treatments, tomato WT seeds (Castlermart II, CMII genetic background) were surface-sterilized in $7 0 \%$ ethanol for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , followed by $2 0 \%$ commercial bleach containing $0 . 1 \%$ Tween-20 at room temperature for $2 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and finally washed four times with sterile water for 10 min each. Sterilized seeds were incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 2 d }$ to for even germination, and then plated on MS complete medium containing $1 \%$ sucrose, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ MES $\mathrm { p H } ~ 5 . 7 – 5 . 8$ , and $0 . 8 \%$ agar. Seedlings were grown in box in a growth chamber at $2 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ : ~ } 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ photoperiod for 10–12 d. For hormones treatment, 10- to 12-d-old seedlings were transferred to $\%$ MS liquid media supplemented with mock or $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ of abscisic acid (ABA), $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ IAA, or $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ 1-aminocyclopropane-1- carboxylic acid (ACC) with gentle shaking at room temperature. Seedlings were harvested at 4 and $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 8 h }$ after the treatment for RNA extraction and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen as described (Bvindi et al., 2022). 

Dark-induced senescence was done as described previously (Liao et al., 2016). In brief, comparable leaves from 4- to 5-wk-old plants were detached and placed in water-saturated plates and incubated in the dark for 10 d. The total Chl content was measured by absorbance at 647 and $6 6 5 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ on a NanoDrop 2000c spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific, Wilmington, Delaware, USA). 

# Quantification of reactive oxygen species

The luminol-based protocol for reactive oxygen species (ROS) assay (Bisceglia et al., 2015) was used with minor modification to quantify ROS levels. Briefly, three leaf disks were collected from each 4-wk-old Slhls2-2 and WT tomato plants. Three biological replicates and 12 technical replicates for each line and treatments were used in each experiment. Leaf disks were washed with water three times every 30 min and kept overnight in the dark at room temperature. On the second day, leaf disks were transferred to a 96-well plate and submerged in a solution containing luminol, horseradish peroxidase, and $2 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ chitin or 1 lM flg22, and luminescence detection was conducted for at least $4 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ in an Infinity M200 Pro plate reader (Tecan U.S. INC., Morrisville, NC, USA). 

# Statistical analysis

All statistical parameters are mentioned in the figure legends. Data represent mean $\pm \nobreakspace \mathrm { S D }$ and mean $\pm \ \mathrm { S E }$ for three biological replicates. For the pathogen infection assays, n represents the number of samples in each experiment. Statistical significance between two groups was analyzed by two-way ANOVA/Dunnett’s multiple comparison followed by Bonferroni post hoc test. Different letters and asterisks indicate the statistical significance mentioned in figure legends for each experiment. At least three biological replicates were included. Statistical analysis was 

performed by GRAPHPAD PRISM 9. All experiments were repeated at least two to three times with similar results. 

# Additional descriptions for materials and methods

Transgenic and gene-edited lines were generated through Agrobacterium mediated transformation. Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-qPCR, qPCR, and RNA-seq experiments were conducted on mock or B. cinerea-inoculated tomato leaf tissue. Total RNA was isolated with TRIzol reagent from 10-d-old seedlings, or from leaves, meristems, sepals, petals, stamens, and carpels of 6-wk-old tomato plants according to the manufacturer’s instruction (Invitrogen). Nicotiana benthamiana leaf tissue transiently expressing the epitope-tagged constructs was harvested $3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ after agroinfiltration, flash frozen in liquid nitrogen, and total protein extracted for immunoblot analyses. For subcellular localization, HLS2 epitope-tagged with green fluorescent protein (GFP) were expressed in N. benthamiana leaves. Confocal microscopy was performed on epidermal cells using a Zeiss LSM880 Axio Examiner upright confocal microscope. Additional experimental details and a list of genes and accession numbers are presented in Methods S1. 

# Results

Tomato HOOKLESS2 is a histone acetyltransferase gene induced by pathogens and plant hormones 

A genome-wide analysis of histone modifiers in tomato identified 32 putative HATs, of which 26 were described as HISTONE ACETYLTRANSFERASE GCN5 1 (HAGs) (Aiese Cigliano et al., 2013). Tomato HOOKLESS2 (HAG7) and HOOKLESS1 are putative GCN5-like acetyltransferase with acetyltransferase domain (PF00583), which are closely related to Arabidopsis HOOKLESS1 (HLS1). Tomato contains two copies of HAG7 (HAG7.1 and HAG7.2), annotated as tomato HOOKLESS1 (SlHLS1, Solyc02g064690.2.1) and HOOKLESS2 (SlHLS2, Solyc02g092490.2.1) (Fig. 1a), a designation that is used hereafter. SlHLS1 and SlHLS2 are on chromosome 2 and share $7 6 \%$ amino acid sequence identity, which suggests that they likely arose from a tandem duplication event. Arabidopsis HLS1, SlHLS1, and SlHLS2 all contain three exons and two introns, indicating conserved genomic structures between the two plant species (Fig. S1a). Initial studies were conducted on tomato HLS1 and HLS2, but we were not able to recover tomato hls1 mutant lines; hence, this study focused on HLS2. 

The expression patterns of tomato HLS2 were studied in different tissues using reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction. SlHLS2 was expressed in all tomato tissues with a notably higher expression in petals, followed by sepals, which showed threefold lower expression compared with the petals but about sixfold higher expression compared with leaves (Fig. 1b). SlHLS2 was highly induced by B. cinerea at $4 8 \ \mathrm { h }$ after inoculation (Fig. 1c) and in response to ABA, IAA, and ACC as early as $^ { 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after treatment (Fig. 1d–f), but its expression was reduced after SA treatment (Fig. 1g).

# Tomato hls2 mutant exhibits grossly altered plant stature and loss of fertility

CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing was used to generate mutant lines and determine the biological function of tomato HLS2. Two gRNAs were designed to generate mutations in the SlHLS2 gene (Fig. 2a; Table S1). To ensure that the gRNAs were specific and to avoid off-target mutagenesis, the GRNA DESIGN software was used (www.genome.arizona.edu/crispr). Several chimeric and biallelic SlHLS2 mutations were recovered in the $\mathrm { { T _ { 0 } } }$ generation that was screened by PCR using primers specific to the HLS2 gene region (Fig. S1b). The hls2-1 allele carried a deletion of 10 bp, the hls2-2 mutant carried a deletion of 42 bp, and 

the third allele, hls2-3, showed two nucleotide (C and A, one side each) additions on both gRNAs (Fig. 2b). The mutations in hls2- 2 and hls2-3 completely disrupt the SlHLS2 protein, but the - hls2-1 caused a frameshift with a premature stop codon (Fig. S2a, b). The chimeric and biallelic mutations for SlHLS2 showed a lack of fertility, producing parthenocarpic fruit. Due to the infertility of the homozygous Slhls2 mutants, plants carrying homozygous Slhls2 mutations were selected by self-pollination of heterozygote plants from independent mutations and used for various studies. Heterozygote, hls2/HLS2 plants were generated by crossing independent chimeric or biallelic Slhls2 mutants with WT plants and were used to maintain plants carrying homozygous alleles. 

Plant carrying the hls2-2 and hls2-3 alleles showed growth phenotypes with severely reduced plant stature generating bushy plants that have no apical dominance (Figs 2c, S2c, S3a–c). In seedlings, until the formation of the second pair of leaves, no morphological differences in the leaves were apparent between Slhls2 mutants and WT tomato plants. However, a 10- to 15-d delay in seed germination of Slhls2 and slower growth was observed. The Slhls2 mutants are affected in leaf morphology with Slhls2 mutants showing altered leaf and leaflet formation (Fig. 3a,b). The internode length was reduced (Fig. 3c), but the number of branches was significantly increased in the hls2 mutants (Figs 3d, S2c, S3c). The initiation of reproductive development was delayed in the Slhls2 mutants, and the inflorescence was significantly affected, resulting in dwarf plants (Figs 3e, S3a, b). The leaves developed much slower, and leaves had fewer leaflets. Specifically, Slhls2-2 leaves lacked the basal pair of secondary and intercalary leaflets and had fewer intercalary leaflets (Fig. 3a, b, white arrow heads). Slhls2-1 showed an intermediate level of overall growth phenotype and an intermediate number of leaflets. The flowers of Slhls2-2 and Slhls2-3 show disorganized structure (Fig. 3e, upper panel) and altered shapes of floral organs (Fig. 3e, lower panel) and were unable to produce fruits or produced parthenocarpic fruits. 

The altered plant growth, infertility, loss of apical dominance, and seedling growth phenotype are reminiscent of altered auxin functions. In the presence of low exogenous auxin (IAA), the Slhls2 mutant shows high regeneration and more roots per explant than the WT (Fig. S4a–c). Slhls2 mutant plants show an auxin phenotype with shorter hypocotyl and less lateral roots as compared to WT tomato plants (Fig. S4d,e). Consistently, Slhls2 mutant plants accumulated higher levels of total auxin (Fig. S4f). The Slhls2-1 plants were partially fertile but produced far fewer fruits and seeds. Overall, SlHLS2 promotes seed germination, initiation and growth of leaves, leaf morphology, leaflets, and flower organs and fertility. 

# Tomato HLS2 is required for resistance to necrotrophic fungi

In B. cinerea disease assays, the hls2-1, hls2-2, and hls2-3 mutant plants displayed increased susceptibility with larger disease lesions than WT plants (Fig. 4a,b). Similarly, Slhls2 mutant lines displayed enhanced susceptibility to the fungal pathogen A. solani, the causal agent of early blight disease, implicating HLS2 in resistance to another necrotrophic fungus (Fig. S5a). To test the role of SlHLS2 in bacterial resistance, Slhls2 mutant plants were infiltrated with the less virulent strain of the bacterial pathogen P. syringae (Pst DC3000-DAvrPtoB). The Slhls2 mutants (hls2-2 and hls2-3) supported lower levels of bacterial growth, whereas the smaller deletion allele (hls2-1) showed no significant difference in bacterial growth (Fig. S5b). This strain was used to assess resistance independent of the Pto/prf regulated pathways. To test whether overexpression of SlHLS2 is sufficient for resistance to B. cinerea, we generated transgenic tomato plants expressing epitope-tagged (HLS2-myc plants). Three independent SlHLS2- myc transgenic lines with increased SlHLS2 expression were 

selected (Fig. S5c). The transgenic plants were more resistant to B. cinerea than WT plants (Fig. 4c,d). The Arabidopsis mutant in the HLS1 gene that is closely related to tomato HLS2 showed accelerated dark-induced senescence. We wanted to test whether this function is conserved in both plant species. Tomato Slhls2 mutants displayed early leaf senescence marked by extensive chlorosis when detached leaves were placed in the dark for 10 d (Fig. 4e). The Slhls2 mutant contained significantly reduced Chl amount consistent with the dark-induced senescence phenotype (Fig. 4f). This contrasts with tomato hls2 plants grown in the glasshouse and growth chamber that show extremely delayed senescence. 

# Chitin-induced accumulation of ROS and SlHLS2 protein degradation

Since SlHLS2 is required for plant resistance to fungal pathogens, we tested elicitor-induced accumulation of ROS as an early defense response. Chitin and flg22-induced accumulation of ROS was significantly reduced in Slhls2 mutant plants (Fig. 5a, b). Next, to understand the impact of chitin on SlHLS2 protein accumulation, we infiltrated water (control) or chitin into N. benthamiana leaves transiently expressing SlHLS2-Myc. SlHLS2 protein level slightly increased 5 minutes after mock treatment (Fig. 5c). However, SlHLS2 accumulation was reduced after chitin treatment, but in the presence of proteasome inhibitor, MG132 restored SlHLS2 accumulation, revealing potential proteasome-mediated SlHLS2 degradation (Fig. 5d). 

# Tomato HLS2 regulates genome-wide changes in gene expression

The transcriptome of Slhls2 mutant and WT tomato plants were compared at $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ after mock or B. cinerea inoculation using RNA-sequencing. Gene expression patterns were analyzed to determine gene expression that may account for the altered defense responses and altered growth of the mutant. The data from three biological replicates were analyzed using Galaxy pipeline for RNA-seq (Batut et al., 2021). Reads were mapped to tomato genome (ITAG v.4.0) using BOWTIE 2 and then counted using htseq (Anders et al., 2015). Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were determined by DESEQ2 using the model (hls2 mock: WT mock, hls2 B.c: WT B.c, WT mock: WT B.c, hls2 mock: hls2 B.c). Based on log twofold change in gene expression and a false discovery rate value (adjusted $P -$ -value $\leq 0 . 0 5$ ), 3774 B. cinerea-induced and 3495 B. cinerea suppressed genes were identified from infected WT plants (Table S2A,B). By comparison, 1583 genes were induced in the Slhls2 mutant by B. cinerea and 1857 genes were suppressed. (Table S3A,B). To determine genes regulated by SlHLS2 in mock-inoculated plants, we identified DEGs regulated by genotype (WT vs hls2 mock-treated) based on adjusted $P -$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ and cutoff $\log _ { 2 }$ fold change $> 2$ . # Gene regulatory network of SlHLS2 regulated genes

A gene regulatory network (GRN) of SlHLS2 regulated genes (from 239 DEGs) was constructed using a virtual plant platform (Fig. 7b; Table S15) (Katari et al., 2010). Three TFs were identified as the hubs in the network. (1) Solyc06g071230 encoding a homeodomain-like super family protein (orthologues to Arabidopsis At1G19000) that encodes a protein family involved in development, metabolism, and biotic and abiotic stress responses (Bai et al., 2018); (2) Solyc09g089550 (ZF-HD2) encodes a zinc finger-homeodomain protein 2 similar to Arabidopsis ZF-HD5 (AT1G75240, ATHB33), which is involved in abscisic acid response and regulation of transcription (Wang et al., 2011a; Ferela et al., 2023); and (3) Solyc04g071770 encodes ethylene response TF 2a, orthologous to Arabidopsis AP2/ERF (ERF2) TF family (AT2G33710) implicated in pathogen responses (Yang et al., 2021). In agreement with this, in the network modules regulated by SlHLS2 in response to B. cinerea, GO terms ‘JA biosynthesis pathway’ and ‘response to JA stimuli’ were significantly enriched (Fig. 7c; Table S16). The network was also enriched with GO terms related to ‘regulation of defense response’, ‘oxylipin biosynthesis and metabolic pathway’, and ‘response to biotic stimuli’ (Fig. 7c; Table S16). The network modules included genes involved in protein ubiquitination, MVB and autophagy pathway, acylsugar biosynthesis, and JA biosynthesis pathway. In our study, the expression of the hub gene SlAP2/ERF2, as well as genes in the indicated pathways, was significantly induced by B. cinerea in WT but not in Slhls2 mutant. 

# Induced expression of SlHLS2 regulated genes

The RNA-seq data described in the preceding section revealed that SlHLS2 regulates diverse genes, including those with direct roles in plant immunity. Pattern recognition receptors, disease resistance proteins, WRKY TFs, E3 ubiquitin transferases, acyl sugar biosynthesis genes, cytochrome P450, JA biosynthesis, and autophagy-related genes exhibited significantly lower B. cinereainduced expression in inoculated Slhls2 mutants. The expression of some of these genes, which expression was highly induced in WT plants in response to B. cinerea but not much in Slhls2 mutants, was further analyzed using quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (Fig. 8a–h). Induced expression of Increased Sodium Tolerance 1 (IST1) homolog, regulator of Vps4 activity in MVB (multivesicular bodies) pathway, was reduced in B. cinerea-inoculated Slhls2 mutant plants (Fig. 8a). The induced expression of AUTOPHAGY-RELATED GENE (ATG11) (Fig. 8b), phosphoinositide phosphatase SUPPRES-SOR OF ACTIN 8 (SAC8), ASAT3, and GDSL LIPASE-LIKE 1 

(GLIP1) were all compromised in Slhls2 mutants (Fig. 8c–e). The cytochrome P450 (CYP707A) (Solyc01g108210) with ABA $8 { ^ \prime }$ -hydroxylase activity is a key enzyme in ABA catabolism (Kushiro et al., 2004) whose expression was also significantly lower in hls2 mutants after B. cinerea inoculation relative to the WT (Fig. 8f). Botrytis cinerea-induced expression of SlBT1 gene (BTB-TAZ (BT)-domain proteins) and kinesin, which is involved in development and defense, were reduced in hls2 mutants (Fig. 8g,h). Similarly, we identified many LRR-RLKs, disease resistance proteins, and TFs that are regulated by SlHLS2 (Fig. 6, Clusters 4 and 5; Fig. 7a, Clusters 2 and 3; Tables S11, S13). SlBT1 gene expression was highly induced in B. cinereainoculated WT plants but significantly lower in Slhls2 mutants (Fig. 8g). These expression patterns correlated with the B. cinerea susceptibility of the hls2 mutant plants. 

Next, the GRN was analyzed to determine what genes are coregulated with SlBT1 that is one of the genes expressed in SlHLS2-dependent manner. Importantly, three TFs were found to be co-regulated with SlBT1 in response to B. cinerea (Fig. 9a). First, Solyc06g062670, which is similar to AT1G55110, encodes a zinc finger TF indeterminate domain 7 protein (IDD7). In Arabidopsis, IDD family TFs are suppressors of growth and immunity to $P .$ syringae, and form a hub that mediates the canonical regulation of the antagonistic SA and JA in plant immunity (V€olz et al., 2019). Second, Solyc03g116890 encodes SlWRKY39 which in Arabidopsis is an important regulator of biotic and abiotic stress responses (Sun et al., 2015; Bai et al., 2018). Interestingly, the third TF (Solyc04g071770, AP2/ERF), the hub for the network in Fig. 7(b) which regulates many TFs and other genes in the network (Fig. 9b), is co-regulated with SlBT1. The GRN was significantly enriched with GO terms related to ‘regulation of defense responses’, response to biotic stimuli specially ‘response to JA stimuli’, response to chitin, and ‘oxylipin & JA biosynthesis’ process (Fig. 9c; Table S17). SlHLS2 regulated DEGs also included several WRKY TF family proteins, VQ motif containing proteins, MAPKs, acylsugar biosynthesis, and JA biosynthesis genes which have been implicated in stress responses to several pathogens (Tian et al., 2024). In our study, the expression of SlWRKY39 and AP2/ERF genes were highly upregulated in response to B. cinerea in WT plants relative to Slhls2 mutant (Fig. $^ \mathrm { 9 d , e }$ ). Overall, SlHLS2 regulates genes in different pathways which are required for overall plant fitness and resistance to pathogens.

HLS2 is required for the transition to flowering and reproduction, as observed by the lack of flowering and seed set in Slhls2 mutants. In addition, the Slhls2 mutant accumulated elevated levels of auxin, consistent with an auxin-related plant growth phenotype. Furthermore, transcriptome studies revealed an expression profile that may account for the extensive growth and plant immunity phenotypes in Slhls2 mutants. Expression of genes implicated in photosynthesis, cuticle development, aging, salicylic acid metabolism, response to auxin, meristem initiation, ribosome biogenesis, protein ubiquitination, responses to biotic stimulus, oxylipin biosynthesis and metabolic process, and autophagy was significantly impacted in the Slhls2 mutant, suggesting that SlHLS2 is a major regulator of diverse processes. Expression of tomato SlBT1 gene that encodes a BTB/POZ and TAZ domain-containing protein was significantly lower in the hls2 mutant. Consistently, SlHLS2 associates with the promoter of SlBT1, and H3Ac levels increased at the chromatin of SlBT1 in HLS2-dependent manner. BTB/POZ proteins are scaffold proteins involved in gametogenesis and plant defense (Boyle 

et al., 2009; Zhou et al., 2022). In sum, tomato HLS2 promotes fungal resistance and plant growth by modulating basal and induced expression of genes, likely through modification of HAc or indirectly by recruiting other complexes. 

The transcriptome profiling of the Slhls2 mutant revealed the broad impact of HLS2 consistent with its drastic phenotypes. The expression of the autophagy-related 11 (ATG11) gene is dependent on functional HLS2. Interestingly, Arabidopsis HLS1 acetylates AUTOPHAGY-RELATED PROTEIN18a to promote autophagy during nutrient starvation in Arabidopsis (Huang et al., 2024), a phenotype that may be related to the enhanced senescence of tomato and Arabidopsis hls mutants. Tomato HLS2 is an orthologue of the Arabidopsis HOOKLESS1 that is required for hormone-mediated growth and plant defense (Chaabouni et al., 2016; Liao et al., 2016). Arabidopsis and tomato mutants share the same disease response phenotypes, showing functional conservation. Tomato HLS1 and HLS2 rescue the hookless phenotype of the Arabidopsis hls1 mutant (Chaabouni et al., 2016). The Arabidopsis hls1 mutant maintained fertility but showed 

enhanced senescence and early maturity. However, the dramatic growth phenotypes observed in the tomato hls2 mutant are severe. In addition, B. cinerea-induced expression of ASAT3 gene was compromised in Slhls2 mutants. Acylsugars provide immune activity against insect pests of tomatoes (Rodriguez et al., 1993; Marchant et al., 2020). In ChIP-qPCR assays, H3Ac and HLS2 association with the ASAT3 promoter was not significantly different from the IgG control, which suggest the regulation of ASAT3 by SlHLS2 is indirect. 

Broad-complex, tramtrack, and bric-a-brac (BTB) family proteins are diverse proteins that contain the BTB domain for the protein–protein interaction. They have been implicated in diverse cellular functions, such as transcription regulation, protein degradation, chromatin remodeling, and cytoskeletal regulation (J. Li et al., 2018). Arabidopsis BT1, BT2, and BT4 bind to bromodomain transcriptional regulators, and the BT2 promoter binds to TF TELOMERASE ACTIVATOR1 (TAC1) to regulate telomerase activity (Du & Poovaiah, 2004; Ren et al., 2007). The BTB domain-containing protein Arabidopsis NPR1 is involved in disease resistance (Boyle et al., 2009). Arabidopsis BT4 was induced by wounding and B. cinerea inoculation, and the mutants showed increased susceptibility to B. cinerea likely through JA/ET signaling pathways (Zhou et al., 2022). 

Gene regulatory network analyses of DEGs identified TFs as the hubs in the SlHLS2 network. These TF genes (Solyc06g071230, Solyc09g089550, and Solyc04g071770) encode homeodomain-like, zinc finger-homeodomain, ethylene response factor proteins, respectively, that are implicated in development, metabolism, and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses, and ABA regulation of transcription (Wang et al., 2011a; Ferela et al., 2023). Similarly, TFs that encode zinc finger protein Indeterminate Domain 7 protein, SlWRKY39, and AP2/ERF genes linked to plant growth suppressors and basal immunity (V€olz et al., 2019) were co-regulated with SlBT1. Interestingly, the tomato AP2/ERF TF (Solyc04g071770) that is also co-regulated with SlBT1 is the hub gene for the network. These data suggest the strong regulatory impact of HLS2 on other regulatory proteins, such as TFs and components of the plant proteasome. 

Tomato contains 32 putative HATs (Aiese Cigliano et al., 2013). Tomato HLS2 and HLS1 and Arabidopsis HLS1 show features of the GCN5 HATs that are known to modulate H3Ac (Aiese Cigliano et al., 2013). Similar to Arabidopsis GCN5, tomato HLS2 is likely a transcriptional coactivator through regulation of HAc. The chromatin acetylation mediated by SlHLS2 is evidenced by the direct association with the SlBT1 promoter, enrichment of H3Ac, and gene expression. The results are consistent with previous studies that showed Arabidopsis HLS1 modulates HAc and gene expression (Liao et al., 2016). Mutation in Arabidopsis HLS1 impaired responses to bacterial and fungal pathogen, and reduced AtHLS1 recruitment and H3 acetylation at the WRKY33 locus (Liao et al., 2016; Jin & Zhu, 2018). Moreover, HLS1 is associated with a transcriptional co-regulatory complex by directly interacting with MED18. HLS1 recruits MED18 to the WRKY33 promoter, enhances the expression of WRKY33, and boosts host immunity (Liao et al., 2016). Tomato HLS2 localized to nucleus, consistent with 

a role in HAc. However, AtHLS1 acetylated ATG18 and controlled autophagy (Huang et al., 2024), which shows that Arabidopsis HLS1 acetylates nonhistone proteins. 

SlHLS2 affects the expression of defense and nondefense-related genes. A recent study proposed the dual role of GCN5 in transcriptional regulation through the activation and repression of SAmediated immunity by controlling H3K14Ac distribution (Kim et al., 2020). Interestingly, the Phytophthora effector, PsAvh23, suppresses the activation of defense genes in soybean by breaking the association of the co-activator complex GCN5 /ADA2 (Kong et al., 2017). PsAvh23 binds and relocates soybean transacetylase GmTAP1 to the nucleus to acetylate histone H2A and H3 to promote susceptibility to Phytophthora sojae (H. Li et al., 2018). 

In Arabidopsis, HAC1 and HAC5 are HATs that complex with NPR1 and TGA TFs, and this HAC-NPR1-TGA complex is recruited to PR genes to facilitate transcription and SA-triggered immunity (Jin et al., 2018). Our data show that tomato Slhls2 mutant plants are resistant to $P .$ . syringae, which is consistent with the expression profile of genes involved in SA biosynthesis and metabolic process, such as phenylalanine ammonia lyase, calmodulin binding protein like (SARD1), a key regulator of ISOCHORISMATE SYNTHASE 1 involved in SA biosynthesis, and several methyl esterases that were highly upregulated in Slhls2 mutant. SARD1 regulates SA biosynthesis by binding to the promoter of ICS1 and other defense-related genes; thus, it triggers a defense response by stimulating SA biosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2011b). 

Finally, tomato HLS2 has a drastic impact on plant growth, plant physiology, and immune responses. The extensive changes in basal and induced gene expression and changes in auxin levels may underlie these major functions. However, further molecular and biochemical studies are required to dissect mechanisms of HLS2 function in growth and plant immunity. The continued proliferation of meristematic growth in Slhls2 mutant is a fascinating aspect of HLS2 function that warrants further studies through the identification and characterization of HLS2-containing protein complexes.
