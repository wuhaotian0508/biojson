# ABSTRACT

The dissemination of insect-borne plant pathogens relies on their ability to influence vector behavior. Certain bacteria-infected plants exhibit increased attractiveness to vectors; however, the underlying mechanisms remain poorly characterized. Huanglongbing (HLB), a devastating citrus disease, is primarily caused by the bacterium ‘‘Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus’’ (CLas) and transmitted by psyllid vectors. In this study, we demonstrate that the effector protein SDE5, secreted by CLas, suppresses the biosynthesis of volatile terpenoids in host citrus plants, thereby enhancing psyllid attraction. Biochemically, SDE5 functions as an inhibitor of bacterial C-type lysozyme, facilitating both CLas infection and psyllid vector attraction. Two plant U-box (PUB) E3 ligases, PUB10 and PUB21, are recruited by SDE5 to promote ubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of MYC2, a key transcription factor in jasmonate signaling and terpene-based anti-herbivore defenses. Furthermore, SDE5 interferes with MYC2 dimerization, diminishing its ability to activate terpene biosynthesis genes. This dual suppression markedly reduces volatile terpenoid emissions in SDE5-transgenic citrus lines, resulting in increased psyllid attraction and enhanced psyllid fitness. Conversely, the anti-proteolysis peptide 3-14 (APP 3-14), which stabilizes the MYC2 protein and inhibits the HLB pathogen, enhances volatile terpenoid emission and repels psyllids. These findings provide a novel strategy for disrupting mutualistic interactions between plant bacterial pathogens and insect vectors by modulation of olfactory defense.

# INTRODUCTION

Insect-borne plant diseases, caused by viruses, bacteria, phytoplasmas, and fungi, present serious threats to global agriculture and ecosystems (Eigenbrode et al., 2018). The transmission of plant pathogens by insect vectors is a complex biological process involving plant–pathogen–insect tripartite interactions. A thorough understanding of these interactions is essential for the effective management of vector-borne diseases. Plants infected by various insect-borne pathogens often exhibit morphological 

and metabolic alterations that increase their attractiveness to vectors. Among these metabolic changes, plant volatile organic compounds (VOCs) comprise a mixture of volatile secondary metabolites. VOCs, including terpenoids and aromatic compounds, function as critical olfactory cues that assist insects in locating host plants and suitable habitats, thereby playing pivotal roles in mutualistic interactions between pathogens and vectors through their shared host plants. For example, our previous studies demonstrated that the begomovirus-encoded βC1 protein and the orthotospovirus non-structural protein NSs suppress

MYC-regulated terpene defenses in plants, enhancing vector attraction and performance (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2019). Therefore, the use of plant volatiles as attractants may serve as an effective strategy for managing insect-borne diseases. 

Bacterial vector-borne pathogens, such as Candidatus Phytoplasma and Candidatus Liberibacter, secrete proteins that support both the establishment of physical interactions with vectors and the modulation of plant defense mechanisms to increase insect attraction and colonization (Huang et al., 2020). A notable example involves the phytoplasma effector proteins SAP11 and SAP54, which alter plant development and defense hormone biosynthesis by destabilizing host transcription factors such as TCP or MADS-box proteins, thus promoting vector attraction and fecundity (Sugio et al., 2011; Orlovskis et al., 2025). Some bacterial pathogens increase insect attraction to infected host plants by modifying visual or olfactory cues. The molecular basis of visual signaling has been partially elucidated. For example, the phytoplasma effector SRP1 destabilizes chloroplastic glutamine synthetase in rice, leading to leaf chlorosis (visual yellowing) that attracts leafhopper vectors (Zhang et al., 2024). Olfactory cues are also critical in mediating the indirect behavioral responses of vectors to infected plants. Apple trees infected with Candidatus Phytoplasma mali and citrus plants infected with Candidatus Liberibacter spp. exhibit alterations in their complex VOC profiles, making them more attractive to psyllid vectors (Mann et al., 2012; Eigenbrode et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2022). However, the molecular mechanisms underlying these effects, particularly those involving bacterial effector-mediated reprogramming of plant olfactory signals, remain largely uncharacterized. 

Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus (CLas) is the principal bacterial pathogen responsible for huanglongbing (HLB), recognized as the most severe disease affecting citrus worldwide (Bove´ , 2006; Gottwald, 2010; Huang et al., 2021). This pathogen is primarily transmitted by the Asian citrus psyllid (Diaphorina citri Kuwayama). The HLB pathogen and its psyllid vector have been documented in 53 countries. A key mechanism by which CLas influences the host involves the secretion of Sec-delivered effectors (SDEs). These effectors are essential for modulating plant defense responses and facilitating bacterial colonization (Prasad et al., 2016). SDEs are known to inhibit immune-related protease activity and suppress host cell death (Jain et al., 2015; Clark et al., 2018). For instance, SDE1 exhibits multifunctionality by targeting various host proteins, including DEAD-box RNA helicase DDX3 and papain-like cysteine proteases (PLCPs), to interfere with plant defense responses (Clark et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2020). Our recent studies have demonstrated that SDE5 suppresses jasmonic acid (JA)-mediated defense by promoting PUB21-mediated degradation of MYC2, thereby increasing citrus susceptibility to HLB (Zhao et al., 2025). Understanding the impacts of SDEs on host defenses and vector interactions is essential for developing effective HLB management strategies. Consequently, research has also focused on the defense responses of citrus plants against the psyllid vector. Infection by CLas alters plant volatile biosynthesis to enhance psyllid performance, thereby accelerating CLas-driven HLB epidemics (Mann et al., 2012). This enhancement establishes an indirect mutualistic relationship between CLas and psyllid vectors through their shared citrus hosts; however, the mechanisms underlying this mutualism remain insufficiently understood. 

Field-deployable strategies include the application of synthetic volatile blends or plant-beneficial microbes (e.g., Bacillus spp.) to mask pathogen-induced odors, disrupting the chemical communication pathways exploited by pests and pathogens to locate and infect host plants (Fincheira et al., 2021). In this study, we explored how anti-proteolysis peptides (APPs) derived from the human microbiota modulate citrus olfactory defense to disrupt mutualistic interactions between the HLB pathogen and psyllid vectors. We found that the Sec-delivered effector SDE5 functions as a C-type lysozyme inhibitor, enhancing psyllid attraction and fitness in citrus plants. This lysozyme inhibition is essential for the role of SDE5 in promoting psyllid attraction. SDE5 destabilizes the MYC2 protein and impairs its dimerization, thereby suppressing host olfactory defense responses against psyllids. A human microbiota-derived APP, APP3-14, known for its bactericidal activity and ability to stabilize MYC2, reduces psyllid attraction and thus improves biological control of HLB transmission. These findings highlight the critical role of APP3-14 in the field management of HLB, enabling concurrent suppression of both HLB pathogens and psyllid vectors. 

# RESULTS

# HLB pathogen infection enhances psyllid vector attraction by suppressing olfactory defense

The HLB pathogen CLas influences the host preference behavior of its vector, the Asian citrus psyllid D. citri Kuwayama, under controlled laboratory conditions (Mann et al., 2012). Our field observations confirmed this behavioral preference of psyllids for CLas-infected pummelo (Citrus maxima), a member of the subfamily Aurantioideae within Rutaceae. Pummelo trees have been cultivated in Southeast Asia for over 3000 years (Huang et al., 2025). The most widely grown commercial citrus cultivars are located in the subtropical regions of China. Subsequent laboratory experiments, using a Y-tube olfactometer two-choice assay to eliminate visual cues, demonstrated that CLas-infected 10-year-old C. maxima plants were more attractive to D. citri adults compared with healthy plants (Figure 1A; Supplemental Figures 1A and 1B). A greater number of psyllids remained on CLas-infected C. maxima plants relative to healthy citrus plants (Supplemental Figure 1B). Additionally, the survival rate of D. citri was elevated on CLas-infected citrus plants (Supplemental Figure 1C). Vector attraction induced by other vector-borne pathogens is primarily mediated through plant VOCs, including terpene hydrocarbons (monoterpenes and sesquiterpenes) and their oxygenated derivatives (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021). In this study, VOCs from branches of healthy and CLas-infected C. maxima plants were analyzed using gas chromatography– mass spectrometry (GC–MS) combined with static headspace solid-phase microextraction (SPME). Figure 1B shows that 11 terpenes were consistently detected in the headspace of C. maxima plants. Notably, the emission levels of two volatile monoterpenes $( \beta \cdot$ -ocimene and ${ \bf { \alpha } } ^ { \bf { \alpha } }$ -terpinene) and five volatile sesquiterpenes $( \beta$ -bisabolene, cedrene, (E)- $\beta$ -farnesene, $\beta$ -caryophyllene, and ylangene) were significantly reduced in CLas-infected C. maxima plants compared with healthy plants. Among these terpenes, $\beta \cdot$ -caryophyllene was most abundant in healthy C. maxima plants, exhibiting a $5 1 . 9 \%$ emission reduction in CLas-infected specimens.

which functions as a repellent to D. citri (Alque´ zar et al., 2021). Consequently, we examined the expression levels of terpene synthase (TPS) genes in citrus, based on prior functional

analysis of TPS genes within the C. sinensis genome (Alque´ zar et al., 2017) (Supplemental Table 1). The expression of several putative Arabidopsis TPS21 homologs—namely TPS21-6, TPS21-7, TPS21-8, and TPS21-9—was significantly downregulated in CLas-infected citrus (Figure 1C). Among these downregulated genes, TPS21-7 exhibited the most pronounced repression, with expression levels reduced to less than $5 \%$ of those observed in healthy citrus. Previous studies demonstrated that TPS21-6 (CsSesquiTPS5a) catalyzes the formation of alloaromadendrene, ledene, and $\beta$ -cadinene, whereas TPS21-9 (CsSesquiTPS1) catalyzes the formation of (Z)-β-- cubebene and $\alpha$ -copaene (Alque´ zar et al., 2017). Furthermore, we determined that TPS21-8 expression in Nicotiana benthamiana plants predominantly produced $\beta$ -ocimene, myrcene, and D-limonene (Supplemental Figure 1D). These findings prompted investigation into the potential role of TPS21-7 in $\beta$ -caryophyllene biosynthesis. 

Functional characterization of this putative sesquiterpene synthase TPS21-7 was conducted through expression of recombinant proteins in Escherichia coli followed by in vitro enzyme assays using (E,E)-farnesyl diphosphate as the substrate. Volatile terpenes produced by TPS21-7 recombinant proteins were analyzed using GC– MS. The major sesquiterpene product of TPS21-7 was tentatively identified as $\beta$ -caryophyllene, constituting $8 1 . 8 1 \% \pm 0 . 9 2 \%$ of the total terpene content, accompanied by three minor products—7- epi-sesquithujene, (E)- $\beta$ -farnesene, and sesquisabinene—representing $4 . 7 9 \% \pm 0 . 1 1 \%$ , $5 . 5 4 \% \pm 0 . 3 2 \%$ , and $7 . 8 6 \% \pm 0 . 7 0 \%$ , respectively (Figure 1D). The production of $\beta \cdot$ -caryophyllene and (E)- $\beta$ -farnesene was further validated by TPS21-7 expression in N. benthamiana plants (Supplemental Figure 1E). To assess the individual roles of $\beta$ -caryophyllene and (E)- $\cdot \{ \beta \cdot$ -farnesene in plant– psyllid interaction, we performed two-choice assays, in which both compounds significantly repelled D. citri compared with the solvent control (Figure 1E and Supplemental Figure 1F). Thus, TPS21-7 expression reduced psyllid attraction relative to YFP expression in citrus leaves, as evidenced by decreased percentages of recaptured D. citri and fewer remaining psyllids on TPS21-7-expressing citrus plants compared with YFP-expressing controls (Supplemental Figures 1G and 1H). These results indicate that TPS21 contributes to defense against psyllids and help explain the increased attraction of psyllids to CLas-infected citrus relative to healthy citrus. 

# SDE5 improves the host preference of the psyllid vector on citrus

Previous studies identified approximately 86 putative SDE proteins containing functional Sec-dependent secretion signal peptides (Prasad et al., 2016). Given that the molecular weights of wellcharacterized mature SDE proteins—such as SDE1 (Clark et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2020), SDE5 (Zhao et al., 2025), SDE15 (Pang et al., 2020), SDE19 (Huang et al., 2024), and SDE10 (Zhang et al., 2025)—are below 15 kDa, we focused on secreted proteins smaller than 15 kDa that lack a transmembrane domain and are unique to CLas. This analysis narrowed the scope to seven CLasspecific SDEs (Supplemental Table 2). To determine which SDE protein(s) influence vector attraction on CLas-infected citrus, we utilized the synthetic tobacco rattle virus (sTRV) system for transient overexpression of heterologous SDE proteins fused with GFP in 2- month-old C. sinensis plants (Supplemental Figures 2A and 2B). 

The sTRV-GFP construct, which expresses green fluorescent protein (GFP), served as the control (Qu et al., 2012). No significant differences in attraction to D. citri were observed between control citrus and those expressing SDE1, SDE4, SDE6, or SDE8 (Figures 2A and 2B). However, citrus plants expressing SDE7 and SDE9 showed reduced attraction to D. citri compared with control plants. Among these seven SDE proteins, only SDE5 increased citrus attractiveness to D. citri (Figures 2A–2C). This finding was supported by the observation that stable ectopic expression of SDE5 in transgenic citrus attracted more D. citri than vector control citrus, as indicated by elevated percentages of recaptured psyllids and increased numbers of remaining psyllids on stable SDE5- transgenic lines (Figures 2D and 2E). 

We next examined whether SDE5 influences TPS21 expression and terpene synthesis in citrus plants. Both transient and stable ectopic expression of SDE5 in citrus significantly reduced TPS21-7 transcript levels relative to control plants (Figure 2F and Supplemental Figure 3A). Consistent with the reduced expression of TPS21 genes, transient expression of SDE5 lowered the emission of two sesquiterpene products, $\beta$ -caryophyllene and (E)-β-farnesene (Supplemental Figure 3B). Furthermore, two stable SDE5-transgenic citrus lines exhibited a marked reduction in the emission of the sesquiterpene $\beta$ -caryophyllene and the monoterpene $\beta$ -myrcene, which significantly decreased psyllid attraction compared with the solvent control (Figure 2G; Supplemental Figures 3C and 3D). Additionally, citrus plants expressing SDE5 had lower mortality rates relative to control plants and those expressing SDE6 (Supplemental Figure 3E), suggesting that SDE5 contributes to the suppression of plant defense. Plant defenses against insect herbivores involve a complex network of key genes, including vegetative storage protein 1 (VSP1), VSP2, MYB16, phytoalexin deficient 3 (PAD3), superroot 1 (SUR1), and branched-chain aminotransferase 4 (BCAT4) (Gatehouse, 2002; Stam et al., 2014; Yang et al., 2022). These genes participate in the biosynthesis of defensive metabolites. Consistent with the improved survival of psyllids on SDE5-expressing plants, we observed that SDE5 suppressed the expression of several defense-related genes, such as VSP1, PAD3, SUR1, and BCAT4 (Supplemental Figure 3F). These findings indicate that SDE5 reduces citrus defensive metabolites, thereby enhancing psyllid attraction and fitness. 

# SDE5 acts as a lysozyme inhibitor to promote pathogen infection and psyllid attraction

To clarify the potential function of SDE5 during CLas infection, we analyzed its amino acid sequence. SDE5 is conserved within the CLas species (Zhao et al., 2025). Outside CLas, the highest sequence homology was found in another HLB pathogen, Candidatus Liberibacter africanus (CLaf), with only $61 \%$ identity (Supplemental Figure 4A). Using the PFAM tool and sequence alignment, we observed that the SDE5 protein contains an LprI domain (Pfam PF07007) (Supplemental Figure 4A). # SDE5 suppresses MYC2 transcriptional activity by interfering with its dimerization

Our previous study demonstrated that MYC2 directly regulates TPS transcript levels by binding to the G-box or G-box-like motif (CANNTG) (Li et al., 2014). Within the promoter region of the TPS21-7 gene, two G-box-like elements were identified (Figure 5A). To assess the binding affinity of MYC2 to these elements, we performed an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) using purified recombinant GST-MYC2 protein. GST-MYC2 bound to both G-box-like 1 (CACATG) and G-box-like 2 (CATTTG), but not to a mutant of G-box-like 1 (TTCAAA) (Figure 5B). The DNA-binding specificity of MYC2 was further validated 

concentrations of the cold competitor (Supplemental Figure 6A). These findings confirm that MYC2 directly binds to the TPS21-7 promoter. 

Given that MYC2 positively regulates TPS21-7 expression, which is also suppressed by SDE5, we examined whether SDE5 affects the trans-activity of MYC2. For this purpose, we utilized constructs containing the TPS21-7 promoter linked to a luciferase (LUC) reporter, along with yellow fluorescent protein (YFP)- SDE5, YFP-SDE6, and YFP-MYC2 as effectors (Figure 5C). YFP alone served as the negative control effector. MYC2 significantly enhanced LUC activity; SDE5, but not SDE6, decreased MYC2- induced LUC activity (Figure 5D). Furthermore, the SDE5S68A mutant failed to suppress MYC2 trans-activity, but the SDE5A64E mutant retained the ability to reduce MYC2 trans-activity. Collectively, these results suggest that SDE5 attenuates MYC2 activity in promoting TPS21-7 transcription. 

MYC2 is known to form homodimers and heterodimers with other MYC-related basic helix–loop–helix transcription factors, facilitating binding to the G-box and G-box-like motifs and subsequently activating downstream gene expression (Ferna´ ndez-Calvo et al., 2011; Kazan and Manners, 2013). We initially confirmed that SDE5 localizes to both the nucleus and cytoplasm of plant cells; MYC2–SDE5 interactions occurred 

within the nucleus (Supplemental Figure 6B). Structural modeling predicted that SDE5 interferes with MYC2 dimerization (Figure 5E). To validate this prediction, we utilized a modified bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay to assess the impact of SDE5 on MYC2 dimerization. In cells coexpressing SDE5 but not SDE6, the interaction signal intensity of MYC2–MYC2 dimers was reduced to approximately $50 \%$ of the original level (Figure 5F and Supplemental Figure 6C), indicating that SDE5 may disrupt MYC2 dimerization. This observation was further supported by a protein competition pull-down assay. The amount of GST-MYC2 pulled down by MBP-MYC2 decreased with increasing concentrations of His-SDE5 (Figure 5G). In contrast, increasing the amount of His-SDE6 did not affect MYC2 self-association. These results demonstrate that SDE5 suppresses MYC2 trans-activity by interfering with its dimerization. 

# The APP3-14 peptide reduces psyllid attraction to HLBpositive citrus by enhancing MYC2-regulated terpene defense

Previous studies indicated that Arabidopsis PUB10 and citrus PUB21 regulate plant MYC2 stability (Jung et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2025). We extended these findings by demonstrating that citrus PUB10 interacts with and ubiquitinates citrus MYC2 (Supplemental Figures 7 and 8A). As a control, a conserved cysteine-to-alanine (C-to-A) mutation was introduced into the U-box domain of PUB proteins, generating dominant-negative (DN) mutants that impair E3 ligase activity (Gonza´ lez-Lamothe et al., 2006; Jung et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2025). The PUB10DN (C260A) and PUB21DN (C39A) mutants exhibited reduced E3 activity and nearly lost their ability to ubiquitinate MYC2 (Supplemental Figure 8). Considering the direct interaction between citrus MYC2 and SDE5, we inferred that SDE5 influences MYC2 ubiquitination levels. Although SDE5 did not alter the E3 ligase activities of these PUB proteins, it enhanced MYC2 ubiquitination mediated by PUB10 and PUB21 but not by PUB16. These results suggest that SDE5 promotes MYC2 ubiquitination through PUB10 and PUB21. 

To further investigate the impact of SDE5 on MYC2 accumulation, we assessed MYC2 protein levels using transient expression systems in N. benthamiana. Co-expression with PUB10 or PUB21 led to substantial MYC2 degradation, with reductions exceeding $60 \%$ . In contrast, inactive PUB10DN or PUB21DN mutants did not alter MYC2 levels relative to the YFP controls (Supplemental Figures 9A and 9B). Notably, co-expression of SDE5 synergistically enhanced PUB10- or PUB21-mediated degradation, resulting in an approximately $80 \%$ reduction in MYC2 levels. Based on these findings, we hypothesized that MYC2 accumulation would decline after CLas infection in citrus. Indeed, endogenous MYC2 protein levels were reduced by more than $80 \%$ in CLas-infected citrus relative to healthy plants (Supplemental Figure 9C). 

MYC2 functions as a substrate of PUB10 and PUB21 E3 ligases, implying that PUB10 and PUB21 suppress MYC2-mediated terpenoid synthesis. This hypothesis is supported by the observation that PUB21 overexpression significantly decreased the transcript levels of four TPS21 genes, including TPS21-6 and TPS21-7 (Figure 6A). Consistent with these findings, two 

volatile sesquiterpenes, $\beta$ -caryophyllene and (E)- $\cdot \beta \cdot$ -farnesene, were substantially reduced in stable PUB21-overexpressing transgenic citrus plants (PUB21 OE) compared to vector control plants (Figure 6B). Conversely, these terpenes exhibited an approximately two-fold increase in stable PUB21DN-overexpressing transgenic citrus lines (PUB21DN OE). The suppression of terpene biosynthesis by PUB21 was expected to affect psyllid host preference. In agreement with this speculation, PUB21 OE citrus plants attracted more D. citri adults, whereas PUB21DN OE lines attracted fewer D. citri adults than vector control plants (Figure 6C and Supplemental Figure 9D). Additionally, transient overexpression of PUB10 or PUB21 in citrus plants resulted in increased attraction of D. citri adults (Supplemental Figures 9E and 9F). These results suggest that PUB10 and PUB21 negatively regulate citrus resistance to the psyllid vector. 

Building on the observation that APP3-14 application to CLasinfected citrus resulted in higher endogenous MYC2 levels (Zhao et al., 2025), we speculated that APP3-14 alters psyllid attraction and the release of volatile terpenoids in HLB-positive citrus. To test this hypothesis, we conducted insect two-choice assays focused on CLas-infected citrus plants, comparing APP3-14 treatment and APP1 treatment. As expected, APP3 or APP3-14 treatment reduced psyllid attraction relative to APP1 treatment on CLas-infected citrus, evidenced by lower percentages of recaptured psyllids and fewer remaining psyllids on APP3- or APP3-14-treated HLB-positive plants (Figure 6D and Supplemental Figure 9G). Because SDE5-mediated psyllid attraction depends on its lysozyme inhibitor activity, we examined the potential impact of APP3-14 on SDE5 function. Co-application of APP3-14 with SDE5 reduced the inhibitory effect of SDE5 relative to SDE5 alone, suggesting that APP3- 14 diminishes the efficacy of SDE5 as a lysozyme inhibitor (Figure 6E). Notably, the reduction in psyllid attraction was correlated with increased terpenoid emission. APP3-14 treatment elevated the emission levels of $\beta \cdot$ -myrcene and $\beta$ -caryophyllene compared with APP1 treatment (Figure 6F). Collectively, these results suggest that APP3-14 deters psyllid vectors by enhancing the production of volatile terpenoids. 

# DISCUSSION

Insect-borne pathogens have evolved complex mechanisms to modulate host metabolic pathways, facilitating their transmission through manipulation of insect vectors. A common strategy employed by these pathogens involves altering host VOCs, which function as attractants or repellents for insect vectors. For example, infection by Plasmodium falciparum induces the release of aldehydes and monoterpenes in humans, enhancing mosquito attraction to infected individuals (Emami et al., 2017). Similarly, insect-borne plant viruses suppress plant MYC2- mediated terpene defenses to improve vector performance (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2019). Concerning the psyllid-transmitted HLB pathogen, CLas infection significantly modifies the VOC profiles of citrus plants, increasing emission of methyl salicylate (MeSA) while reducing levels of methyl anthranilate and D-limonene (Mann et al., 2012). This CLas-induced elevation in MeSA enhances psyllid attraction. Citrus MYC2 is crucial for defense against psyllids because methyl jasmonate application alters VOC emissions, influencing psyllid behavior and potentially reducing colonization and feeding activity (Patt et al., 2018). By modulating the production of terpenoids and other VOCs, MYC2 enhances the attraction of psyllid natural enemies, providing an additional layer of defense (Shi et al., 2019). Our findings indicate that $\beta$ -caryophyllene and (E)- $\beta$ -farnesene emitted by citrus plants function as psyllid repellents (Figure 1) and that MYC2 plays dual defensive roles (Figure 4). In addition to TPS21-mediated volatile repellence, MYC2 overexpression increases the production of non-volatile metabolites ingested during feeding, resulting in psyllid mortality. Consequently, psyllid avoidance of MYC2-activated plants is evolutionarily rational, as settling on these hosts reduces their survival. Along with their roles in insect defense, terpenes contribute to plant resistance against microbial pathogens. $\beta$ -Caryophyllene and its derivative, caryophyllene oxide, inhibit the growth of several plant-pathogenic fungi, including Sclerotinia sclerotiorum and Fusarium oxysporum (Hilgers et al., 2021). The discovery of lysozyme inhibitors as virulence factors in certain Gram-negative bacterial pathogens, such as Yersinia pestis and M. tuberculosis, provides critical insights into microbial strategies for evading host immunity and engaging in ecological competition (Russell et al., 2011; Callewaert et al., 2012). Lysozyme, a key component of the innate immune system, targets peptidoglycan in bacterial cell walls, serving as a frontline defense against infection. As a bacterium that lacks a conventional peptidoglycan layer and exclusively resides in the phloem of host plants, CLas raises intriguing questions about the evolutionary function of its secreted lysozyme inhibitor, SDE5, identified here as a potential determinant of fitness. By neutralizing lysozyme activity in the phloem microenvironment, SDE5 may suppress competing bacteria that depend on peptidoglycan integrity, thus securing ecological dominance for HLB pathogens. This hypothesis is consistent with the pathogen’s reliance on secreted effector proteins—SDEs—to manipulate host physiology and microbial interactions (Hu et al., 2021). The role of SDE5 as a lysozyme inhibitor underscores its contribution to HLB pathogenesis and its potential as a therapeutic target. The development of smallmolecule inhibitors or peptide mimics to disrupt SDE5–lysozyme interactions could help suppress HLB pathogens by restoring lysozyme activity to control microbial competitors. Future 

# Plant Communications

research should confirm SDE5’s specificity for lysozyme, identify key structural motifs responsible for its inhibitory function, and assess its impact on HLB bacterial survival in planta. Such efforts will not only advance HLB management but also inform innovative strategies to counter effector-driven microbial competition in other plant and animal pathogens. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Citrus plants used in the experiments were C. sinensis and C. maxima. Branches from 10-year-old C. maxima ‘‘Sanhongmiyou’’ plants, collected from a field orchard in Hainan Province, served as healthy and CLasinfected material for psyllid bioassays and volatile compound analysis. C. sinensis ‘‘sweet orange’’ plants were utilized for transient expression of SDE genes and served as the citrus material for MYC2 silencing. All citrus plants were grown in a greenhouse maintained at ${ } ^ { 2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ with $50 \%$ relative humidity. 

# Plasmid constructs

For transient expression, the coding regions of seven SDEs lacking the signal peptide were individually amplified from CLas genomic DNA using gene-specific primers, with a start codon added to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end of each forward primer. The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) products were purified and cloned into the psTRV2 vector to generate psTRV2-SDE constructs. For virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) vectors, an 800-bp coding region specific to C. sinensis MYC2 was cloned into the psTRV2 vector to generate the psTRV2-MYC2 construct. 

For in vitro protein competition assays, full-length open reading frames encoding MYC2 were cloned into the pGEX-DC or pMAL-DC vectors to generate GST-MYC2 or MBP-MYC2 fusion constructs. The 282-bp coding region of SDE6 lacking the signal peptide (amino acids 1–23) and the 336-bp coding region of SDE5 lacking the signal peptide (amino acids 1–20) were separately cloned into the pET-28a vector to generate His-SDE fusion constructs. These protein expression constructs were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3). 

For transactivation assays, the promoter fragment of TPS21-7 $( \sim 1 . 2 \mathsf { k b } )$ ) was cloned into the pGWB435 vector to generate TPS21-7 promoter:LUC fusion constructs. PCR products of SDE6, SDE5, and the full-length MYC2 coding sequence were individually cloned into pH7YWG2 to generate YFP-tagged constructs under the control of the cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter. Plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105. 

# sTRV-based VIGS and transient expression of heterologous SDE genes

The sTRV system-based VIGS was performed as previously described (Qu et al., 2012). Xanthomonas citri subsp. citri (Xcc) strains were agroinfiltrated into citrus leaves as described earlier (Teper et al., 2020). Xcc bacterial suspensions ( ${ 1 0 } ^ { 6 }$ colony-forming units/ml) were syringeinfiltrated into expanded leaves prior to transient expression of heterologous genes in citrus plants. C. sinensis seedlings with two true leaves were vacuum-infiltrated with Agrobacterium-ed carrying psTRV1 and psTRV2-MYC2 to generate MYC2 VIGS plants. Plants co-infiltrated with psTRV1 and psTRV2 served as vector controls. In this study, the sTRV system was also used to overexpress SDE genes in citrus. Leaves of 2-month-old citrus plants were infiltrated with Agrobacterium harboring psTRV1 and psTRV2-SDEs to generate plants transiently expressing SDEs throughout the canopy. 

# Psyllid bioassays

A two-port divided Y-tube olfactometer was used to evaluate the behavioral response of D. citri, as previously, described with modification (Mann et al., 2012). Branches from 10-year-old field-grown C. maxima ‘‘San-

hongmiyou’’ plants with comparable physiological status, supported by stems in hydroponic tubes containing half-strength Murashige and Skoog (1/2 MS) nutrient solution, served as odor sources within odor bottles connected to both arms of the Y-shaped olfactometer. A healthy field sample was used as the mock control. An atmospheric sampling instrument facilitated airflow, which was purified using activated carbon and humidified with water, into the base arm of the olfactometer. The gas flow rate was maintained at 300 ml/min. D. citri adults were individually released into the olfactometer, with odor sources randomly assigned to one arm and switched every 30 insects to prevent bias. The behavioral response of each D. citri adult was tested against non-infected and CLas-infected citrus plants. For terpene bioassays, odor sources were applied to filter paper with a $1 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \vert$ solution of standard chemicals in n-hexane, using n-hexane alone as the control. D. citri then made its choice as described. Tests were conducted with eight groups, recording 30 valid choices. Insect two-choice assays using stable transgenic 2-year-old C. sinensis lines or transient gene-expressing 2-month-old C. sinensis plants were performed as previously described (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2025). Two citrus plants with similar physiological status were placed separately in a cage $( 3 0 \times 3 0 \times 3 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ . One hundred D. citri adults were released at the center between the two plants. After 40 min, psyllids settled on each plant were recaptured, and the number on each plant was recorded. Six to eight biological replicates were conducted for this experiment. 

Short-term feeding experiments were performed to evaluate the effect of leaf quality on D. citri survival. Detached citrus branches were maintained with stems in hydroponic tubes containing 1/2 MS nutrient solution to preserve leaf vitality. D. citri adults were fed on citrus leaves placed in covered cups. Eighty psyllids per citrus genotype were examined. The number of live psyllids was recorded daily, and mortality within 5 days was quantified. 

# Volatile analysis by GC–MS

For volatile analysis of C. maxima and C. sinensis plants, volatiles emitted from 1 g of leaves were collected using an SPME fiber (Supelco, Bellefonte, PA, USA) composed of $5 0 / 3 0 – up \mu \mathrm { m }$ DVB/CAR/PDMS (Supelco, 57328-U). Plant volatiles were analyzed by GC–MS (Shimadzu, QP2010) equipped with a DB5MS column (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA; $3 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } \times$ $0 . 2 5 \ \mathrm { m m } \ \times \ 0 . 2 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ . The SPME fiber was thermally desorbed in the injector at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 4 min. The oven temperature was programmed to rise from $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to $2 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ at a rate of $7 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } / { \mathsf { m i n } }$ . Collection, isolation, and identification of volatiles were performed as previously described (Li et al., 2013). 

# Functional analysis of TPS21-7

Two micrograms of GST or GST-TPS21-7 recombinant protein, $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of (E,E)-farnesyl diphosphate (Sigma, F6892), and reaction buffer were placed in 2-ml Teflon-sealed glass tubes. An SPME fiber was inserted into the headspace, and samples were incubated for 30 min at ${ } ^ { 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ . The fiber was then transferred to the GC injector $( 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , and GC–MS conditions were established as described above. 

# RT–qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from citrus plants using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen, 74904). Reverse transcription was performed with $2 \ \mu \ g$ of RNA per sample using TransScript One-Step gDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis SuperMix (TransGen Biotech, AU311-02). Real-time PCR was conducted on a Bio-Rad CFX96 system using Thunderbird SYBR qPCR Mix (Toyobo, QPS-201) and gene-specific primers listed in Supplemental Table 3. The citrus COX gene served as the internal control (Morgan et al., 2012). 

# Fluorescence-based lysozyme sensitivity assay

Lysozyme activity was measured using the EnzChek Lysozyme Assay Kit (Invitrogen, E22013), in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. 

A HEWL lysozyme standard curve was generated using the DQTM lysozyme substrate, consisting of fluorescein-conjugated M. lysodeikticus cells. Reaction mixtures were prepared in $1 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \up$ volumes containing 100 units/ml HEWL with or without SDE5 and its mutant proteins. Samples were then incubated for 30 min at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Fluorescence was measured using a microplate reader with excitation/emission settings of ∼485/530 nm. HEWL inhibitory activity was assessed based on fluorescence intensity. 

# Yeast two-hybrid analysis

All constructs were transformed into yeast strain AH109 using a modified lithium acetate method. The coding region of SDE5 (excluding the putative signal peptide) was cloned into the pGBT9 vector to generate the BD-SDE5 construct. To confirm the interaction between SDE5 and MYC2, full-length citrus MYC2 wascloned into the pGAD424 vector via LR reaction to generate the AD-MYC2 construct. Yeast strain AH109 was co-transformed with pGBKT7-SDE5 or SDE5 mutant constructs and pGAD424-MYC2. Yeast co-transformants carrying the indicated plasmids were spotted onto synthetic medium (SD/− Leu/− Trp/− His) supplemented with 5 mM 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole. 

# In vitro protein competitive interaction assay

GST-, MBP-, and His-fusion proteins were individually purified using glutathione Sepharose (GE Healthcare, 17-5132-01), amylose resin (New England Biolabs, E8021S), and Ni-NTA agarose (Qiagen, R90101) beads, in accordance with each manufacturer’s protocol. For the competitive pulldown assay, specified amounts of His-SDE5 or His-SDE6 were mixed in 1 ml of binding buffer with $2 \mu \ g$ of MBP-MYC2 fusion protein and $5 0 \mu \up$ of amylose resin beads at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight. After washing, $2 \mu \ g$ of GST-MYC2 fusion protein were added, and the mixture was incubated for $^ { 3 \mathfrak { h } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Following incubation, binding beads were washed six times. Associated proteins were separated on $10 \%$ sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gels and detected by immunoblotting with an anti-GST antibody. 

# Ubiquitination assay

The in vitro ubiquitination assay in E. coli was performed as previously described (Han et al., 2017). Coding regions of MYC2, PUB10, PUB10DN, PUB21, or PUB21DN were cloned into the pCDFDuet or pACYCDuet vectors. E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells containing various combinations of expression vectors were cultured and induced with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (Sigma, I5502). After induction, bacterial cultures were maintained at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 10 h and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ overnight. Crude protein extracts were separated via sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and analyzed by immunoblotting using anti-MBP or anti-myc antibodies. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

EMSA was performed as previously described (Li et al., 2014). GST-fusion proteins of MYC2 were purified from E. coli BL21 cells using glutathione Sepharose beads (17-0756-01, GE Healthcare). DNA fragments containing G-box-like 1 (AGG ATC ACT ACT GAG CAC ATG ACA AAG TAC CAA CG), G-box-like 2 (TCT CTA TAA ATA GAG CAT TTG TGT CTT GAG GTT TT), and a mutant G-box-like 1 (AGG ATC ACT ACT GAG TTC AAA ACA AAG TAC CAA CG) within the TPS21-7 promoter were labeled with biotin. EMSA was performed using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (20148, Thermo Scientific), in accordance with the manufacturer’s protocol. 

# Luciferase activity assay

TPS21-7 promoter:LUC served as the reporter construct. 35S:YFP, 35S: SDE5, 35S:SDE6, and 35S:MYC2 were used as effector constructs. Leaves of 6-week-old N. benthamiana were co-infiltrated with the indicated constructs. After incubation for 2 days, samples were collected, and LUC activity was measured using a microplate reader as previously described (Zhao et al., 2019). Eight biological replicates were included in each experiment. 

# Statistical analysis

Differences in gene expression levels, volatile organic compound concentrations, lysozyme inhibition ratios, and average counts of enhanced YFP fluorescence were assessed using Student’s t-tests for comparisons between two treatments or lines. Differences in relative LUC activity, lysis ratios, gene expression levels, and volatile compound concentrations were evaluated with one-way analysis of variance, followed by Duncan’s multiple range test to identify significant differences among lines or treatments. Differences in two-choice D. citri assays between lines or treatments were analyzed using Wilcoxon matched pairs tests (with two dependent samples).
