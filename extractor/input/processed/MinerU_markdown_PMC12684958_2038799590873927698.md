# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Timosaponins, as steroidal saponins, are the primary active constituents and quality biomarkers in Anemarrhena asphodeloides Bunge. Despite their significance, the biosynthetic pathways of timosaponins have not been thoroughly investigated. 

Objectives: This study aims to delineate the biosynthetic pathway of timosaponins in A. asphodeloides, elucidate the catalytic mechanisms of the key cycloartenol synthase (CAS), and investigate the antifungal properties of timosaponins. 

Methods: Genes were cloned from A. asphodeloides and heterologous expressed in yeast, tobacco or

bacillus coli. Site-directed mutagenesis and molecular docking were used to elucidate the catalytic mechanism of CAS. Antifungal assays were conducted to evaluate the antifungal activities of timosaponins. 

Results: In this study, we elucidated the biochemical functions of seven genes involved in timosaponins biosynthesis in A. asphodeloides. Among three candidate OSC genes, AaOSCR12 was identified as the gene encoding cycloartenol synthase, which is responsible for the skeleton cyclization in timosaponin biosynthesis. Six residues (257H, 369N, 448T, 507V, 558P, 616Y) were identified as the critical catalytic active sites of CAS (AaOSCR12). Sterol methyltransferase (AaSMT1) and sterol side-chain reductase (AaSSR2) were found to be the subsequent enzymes and the branching points leading to phytosterol and cholesterol biosynthesis, respectively. Two oxide reductase genes, AaCYP90B27 and AaCYP90B2, were responsible for post-modification of cholesterol, serving as a precursor of timosaponins. A key 26-O- $\cdot \beta$ -glucosidase (AaF26G1) was identified as facilitating the conversion of furostanol-type timosaponins into spirostanoltype timosaponins. Antifungal assays revealed that spirostanol-type timosaponin III exhibits superior antifungal activity compared to furostanol-type timosaponin II, potentially linked to plant defense mechanisms involving AaF26G1. 

Conclusion: This study utilized a multi-chassis cross-identification strategy, revealing key enzymes in the timosaponin biosynthetic pathway and offering novel insights into plant defense mechanisms against microbial pathogens.

# Introduction

Anemarrhena asphodeloides Bunge, belonging to the family Liliaceae and the genus Anemarrhena, is a monocotyledonous herbaceous plant known as ‘‘Zhimu” in Chinese [1]. The main active components are steroidal saponins, called timosaponins, which possess remarkable biological activities and exhibits a wide range of pharmacological effects [2–4]. Timosaponins include two types steroidal saponins, namely furostanol-type and spirostanol-type timosaponins. Timosaponin AIII belongs to the spirostanol-type steroidal saponins and demonstrates potent cytotoxicity and induces apoptosis in various tumor cells, including breast cancer cells [5], HeLa cells [6], lung cancer cells [7], and pancreatic cancer cells [8]. Besides, timosaponin BII is the most abundant component, belonging to the furostanol-type steroidal saponins, and has been shown to enhance learning and memory abilities and effectively combat Alzheimer’s disease [9]. In Zhimu dried rhizomes, the total timosaponins account for up to approximately $6 \%$ of its weight [3,10]. However, the biosynthetic pathway of steroidal saponins in A. asphodeloides remains largely uncharacterized and requires further elucidation. 

In monocotyledonous plants, such as those in the Liliaceae and Dioscoreaceae families, steroid saponins are important secondary metabolites and are present in high concentrations [11]. Steroid saponins have also been found to accumulate in small amounts in the dicotyledonous plants Solanum lycopersicum and Trigonella foenum-graecum [12]. The steroid saponin biosynthesis is complex, involving more than 20 enzymes, and occurs in three stages: skeleton formation, aglycone synthesis, and post-modification. The mevalonic acid (MVA) pathway in the cytoplasm and the methylerythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) pathway in the plastids are the initiating pathways for the biosynthesis of steroidal saponins, with the MVA pathway considered to be the more predominant route [13,14]. In 2016, Sonawane et al. [15] provided the first complete elucidation, showing that under the catalysis of a series of enzymes, 2,3-oxidosqualene cyclizes to form the skeleton of steroidal saponins, cycloartenol. Both the cholesterol and the sterol biosynthetic pathways use cycloartenol as a precursor, under the catalysis of sterol side chain reductase 2 (SSR2) and sterol methyltransferase 1 (SMT1), respectively. Subsequently, Sonawane et al. [16] demonstrated that the cholesterol pathway diverged from the sterol pathway. In 2023, Xue et al. [17] revealed the diosgenin biosynthesis network using cholesterol as a precursor in Paris polyphylla. However, there are still studies that suggest the phytosterol pathway may also participate in the biosynthesis of steroidal sapo-

nins, and both pathways may coexist in plants [18,19]. Research on enzymes involved in the post-modification of steroidal saponin is limited; SaGT4A from Atropa belladonna is the first reported UGT associated with this process [20], and a 26-O- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ -glucosidase from Dioscorea zingiberensis has been suggested to play a role in plant defense [21]. However, further studies are required to expand our understanding. 

Here, we focused on elucidating the biosynthetic pathway of timosaponins in A. asphodeloides (Fig. 1). Functional characterization of enzymes involved in the synthesis pathways of phytosterols and cholesterol was mainly performed in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, assisted by Nicotiana benthamiana and Escherichia coli chassis. In particular, the catalytic mechanism of AaOSCR12 was extensively investigated, revealing six critical catalytic sites. Further research on AaCYPs identified the presence of a specific catalytic enzyme, AaCYP90B27, and a catalytic promiscuous enzyme, AaCYP90B2. Additionally, the functional analysis of AaF26G1 in vitro unraveled the logic of the conversion from furostanol-type to spirostanoltype steroidal saponins and investigated the antifungal properties of timosaponins. In conclusion, this study contributes to a deeper understanding of the cholesterol biosynthetic pathway in plants.

# Results

Prediction of phytosterols and steroidal saponins biosynthetic genes in A. asphodeloides 

Based on sequence homology, fourteen potential genes (AaHMGR, AaOSCL6, AaOSCL10, AaOSCR12, AaSSR2-1, AaSSR2-2, AaSMT1, AaCPI1, AaCPI2, Aa7DR1, Aa7DR2, AaCYP90B2, AaCYP90B27, AaF26G1) possibly associated with the biosynthetic pathways of phytosterols and steroidal saponins were screened from the transcriptome sequence data by the local blast with known bait sequences (Table S1-S6). The relative expression levels of these candidate unigenes were detected by RT-qPCR in different tissues, including rhizome (R), lateral root (LR), leaf (L), and root bark (RB) of A. asphodeloides by RT-qPCR (Fig. S1). The results showed that, except for the AaOSC unigenes, the remaining candidate unigenes for timosaponin biosynthesis were highly expressed in the rhizomes and lateral roots and less expressed in the leaves (Table S7). The three AaOSC unigenes show the highest expression in the root bark. These results are consistent with the accumulation patterns of steroid sapogenins in A. asphodeloides, which can assist us in the gene screening process and are of great significance. 

# Functional characterization and key catalytic sites of AaOSC

Sonawane et. al [15] revealed the biosynthetic pathway of plant cholesterol overlapped with phytosterols for the first time. Both phytosterols and steroidal saponins share the common cycloartenol precursor. Cycloartenol was cyclized by 2,3-oxidosqualene cyclases (OSC) (Fig. S2). Thus, AaOSC played an essential role in the biosynthesis of timosaponins. To identify the function of AaOSC unigenes, plasmid vectors pESC-HIS-AaOSCL6, pESC-HIS-AaOSCL10, and pESC-HIS-AaOSCR12 were constructed and transformed into the high-yielding 2,3-oxidosqualene chassis strain p24, which was engineered by our lab previously [26]. Compared to the empty vector control, product (1) and product (2) peaks were detected in total ion chromatogram (TIC) of the transgenic yeast with pESC-HIS-AaOSCR12 (Fig. 2a, Fig. S2). However, no significant product was observed in the other two homologous unigenes AaOSCL6 and AaOSCL10. Product (1) was identified as cycloartenol by comparing with standard, according to their retention times and mass spectra. Product (2) was putatively identified as 31-norcycloartenol according to the comparison with literature mass spectra [16] and NMR spectrum (Fig. S3-S5). It was speculated that a methyl group at C4 was removed from cycloartenol (1), which degraded by the endogenous sterol- $. 4 \alpha$ -methyl oxidase (SMO) complex erg25, erg26, and erg27 within yeast [30]. Tobacco is a commonly used plant heterologous expression system, the three homologous unigenes AaOSCL6, AaOSCL10, and AaOSCR12 were built into the tobacco expression vector pEAQ-HT-DEST1 (referred to as pDEST1) and transformed into A. tumefaciens EHA105, then injected into tobacco leaves. The results in tobacco were con-

sistent with that in yeast, only AaOSCR12 could improve the product of cycloartenol (1) (Fig. S6). Based on these results, it can be confirmed that only AaOSCR12 was characterized as CAS that catalyzes the 2,3-oxidosqualene to produce cycloartenol (1) as the precursor of phytosterols and steroidal saponins. 

The amino acid sequence identities between AaOSCR12, AaOSCL6, and AaOSCL10 are more than $9 7 . 2 4 \ \%$ , with 53 different amino acids. However, only AaOSCR12 can catalyze the formation of cycloartenol (1), indicated the active catalytic sites should among the differential 53 amino acid residues. AaOSCL6 and AaOSCL10 share high sequence similarity, differing by only six amino acids. None of which are located within the active pocket. Consequently, AaOSCL10 was selected as a representative for catalytic site research. Using homology modeling and molecular interaction analysis of AaOSCR12 and AaOSCL10 via AlphaFold2 [31], most candidate differential residues among within the active pocket were identified in the latter half of the AaOSC protein sequence (amino acid sequence 249–761). To narrow down the selection range, we exchanged the nucleotide sequence corresponding to amino acids 1–249 of AaOSCR12 with the AaOSCL10 nucleotide sequence. The recombinant sequence, named AaOSCL10R12, still produces cycloartenol (1) (Fig. 2b), indicating the 1–249 fragment with less impact on catalytic activity. 

Within the range of 249–761 amino acid residues of AaOSCR12, six residues differential with AaOSCL10 were found located near the active pocket (257H, 367P, 369 N, 480P, 481I, and 554 V), while other four residues 448I, 507 V, 558P, and 616Y were positioned at a distance of 5 Å within substrate (Fig. S7). Sequence alignment with 27 known CASs revealed that ten residues were conserved in monocotyledons, dicotyledons, and lower plants’ CAS (Fig. S8). However, AaOSCL6 and AaOSCL10 exhibited differences at these conserved sites. Therefore, these ten residues have been hesitant to be the potential catalytic activity sites. 

Mutual mutations of AaOSCR12 at the ten selected sites were performed. The six mutants AaOSCR12H257Q, AaOSCR12N369I, AaOSCR12T448I, AaOSCR12V507I, AaOSCR12P558S, and AaOSCR12Y616H failed to synthesize cycloartenol (1), indicating that these six sites are critical catalytic active sites (Fig. 2c, 2d). On the contrary, the rest four mutual mutations AaOSCR12P367S, AaOSCR12P480Q, AaOSCR12I481V, AaOSCR12V554I still observed cycloartenol (1) at different degrees (Fig. 2d), indicating they were not necessarily critical residues. 

In order to restore the catalytic activity of AaOSCL10, a series of combinational mutations were built and tested. When all six key residues are mutated to the corresponding residues in AaOSCR12, the AaOSCL10-6M produce trace amounts of cycloartenol products, although the yield was only $0 . 9 \mathrm { ~ ‰ ~ }$ of that in the wild-type AaOSCR12 (Fig. 2e). Based on these findings, we can infer that residues 257H, 369 N, 448 T, 507 V, 558P, and 616Y are the essential catalytic amino acid residues in AaOSCL10. 

Ile481 influences cyclization and deprotonation of Arabidopsis thaliana CAS by steric bulk effect [32]. As the steric bulk of residue at position 481 decreases, the proportion of cycloartenol is also decrease, accompanied by an increase in the proportion of other by-products. Therefore, we hypothesize that substituting valine with isoleucine at position 481 in AaOSCL10-6M could enhance its CAS function. Consequently, AaOSCL10-7M (Q257H-I369N-I44 8T-V481I-I507V-S558P-H616Y) mutant was engineered. 

Metabolite profiling showed that AaOSCL10-7M had a roughly 104-fold increase in cycloartenol (1) production relative to AaOSCL10-6M (Fig. 2f), verifying that the 481st residue mutation impacts the catalytic activity of AaOSC. Moreover, the wildtype AaOSCR12 exhibited about an 18-fold greater cycloartenol yield compared to the AaOSCL10-7M mutant, suggesting that, beyond residue 481, additional amino acid residues may also be pivotal for the catalytic activity of AaOSC in CAS.

Sterol C-24 methyltransferase type 1 (SMT1) referentially catalyzes the methyl addition to cycloartenol. The SMT1 can be a critical slow enzyme in the extended biosynthetic post-lanosterol/ cycloartenol sequence which limits and controls D5-phytosterol composition [11,15–17,33]. However, the phytosterol pathway in A. asphodeloides has not yet been elucidated. Biosynthetic pathway of cholesterol and its downstream metabolites in A. asphodeloides 

Sterol side-chain reductase 2 (SSR2) is involved in the crucial bifurcation point of cycloartenol (1), competing with SMT1 for the same precursor, ultimately leading to the production of cholesterol in Solanaceae plants, which is a supposed precursor to timo-

saponins [11,17]. To verify the SSR2 function from A. asphodeloides, two homologous unigenes pESC-LEU-AaSSR2-1 and pESC-LEU-AaSSR2-2 vectors were constructed and transformed into strain R12, leading to the discovery of compound (6). However, the blank control also revealed a small amount of compound (6), speculated to be a product of the endogenous gene erg4 in yeast (Fig. 3c, S11) [17]. So, we knocked out the erg4 gene in R12 and introduced the AaSSR2-1 and AaSSR2-2 genes separately. Interesting, erg4 is an essential gene for yeast viability, yeast strains normally cannot survive when lacking erg4, but the introduced AaSSR2-1 and AaSSR2-2 genes can survive the yeast and complemented the function of erg4 [35]. The lengths of AaSSR2-1 and AaSSR2-2 genes are approximately 2,200 bp, whereas the erg4 nucleotide sequence is 1,422 bp. PCR amplification and sequencing were performed to ensure successful erg4 knockout and SSR insertions (Fig. S12). Both R12Derg4::AaSSR2-1 and R12Derg4::AaSSR2-2, upon expression, could produce cycloartanol (6), confirmed by its mass spectra. So, both AaSSR2-1 and AaSSR2-2 can catalyze the D24 (25) reduction of the cycloartenol (1) side chain to generate cycloartanol (6). 

The biosynthetic pathway of cholesterol has been documented in Solanaceae plants and P. polyphylla [15,17]. Consequently, we initiated our analysis from cholesterol to elucidate the biosynthesis of distinctive timosaponins in A. asphodeloides. Itkin et al. # Antifungal action of timosaponins

The F26G enzyme participates in the defense responses of plants against microorganisms, insects, and herbivores [48–51]. Similarly, A. asphodeloides stores a significant amount of furostanol-type steroidal saponins in its rhizome. F26G is also highly expressed in these rhizomes, indicating its potential involvement in defense mechanisms. The antifungal activity of spirostanol-type saponins has been known for a long time [41,42,52]. But their corresponding furostanol-type saponins usually show little to no activity [52,53]. Therefore, we investigated the antifungal activity of the representative furostanol-type timosaponin II (14), and spirostanol-type timosaponin III (15), which are the most abundant timosaponins in A. asphodeloides. 

The common fungal species in modern agricultural cultivation, including R. solani, F. oxysporum, and A. solani, which belong to Deuteromycotina, have been extensively studied [54]. Hence, these three fungal strains were selected as the target organisms to study the antifungal effects of timosaponins. Each fungus was separately inoculated on PDA plates containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ of timosaponin II, timosaponin III, or DMSO. The antifungal effects of timosaponin II and III were not significant against F. oxysporum or A. solani, but they exhibited notable antifungal effects against R. solani (Fig. 6a). Spirostanol-type timosaponin III exhibited superior antifungal activity compared to validamycin. Furostanol-type timosaponin II exhibits weak antifungal activity against R. solani, showing a larger colony diameter than that of the validamycin group and the timosaponin III group (Fig. 6b). Therefore, R. solani was selected as the target organism, validamycin was used as the positive control, and DMSO was used as the negative control to establish a dose–response curve for the antifungal activity of timosaponins. (Fig. 6c). # Discussion

Anemarrhena asphodeloides, a widely used traditional Chinese medicinal herb, is highly valued for its steroidal saponins, particularly timosaponins, which are considered the primary bioactive 

compounds. The quality of A. asphodeloides is commonly evaluated based on the content of these saponins, which are closely associated with their geographic origin and authenticity. Timosaponins, as monocot-specific steroidal saponins, are products of intricate and lengthy biosynthetic pathways involving numerous enzymes and multiple catalytic steps. While significant progress has been made in understanding the biosynthesis of steroidal saponins in other medicinal plants, such as P. polyphylla and Dioscorea spp., research on the biosynthetic pathways of timosaponins in A. asphodeloides remains limited. Elucidating the biosynthetic pathway of timosaponins in A. asphodeloides presents an opportunity to uncover the genes and enzymes responsible for their production. Such findings would provide critical insights into the metabolic networks underlying the synthesis of these compounds. Additionally, this knowledge could serve as a foundation for the genetic improvement of A. asphodeloides, enabling the breeding of superior cultivars with enhanced saponin content. 

OSC genes play a crucial role in the cyclization of steroidal saponin and triterpenoid saponin backbones. In most plants, multiple OSC genes have been identified, with 13 found in A. thaliana and 11 in Oryza sativa [55]. In Dioscoreaceae and Liliaceae, the primary pharmacologically active compounds are steroidal saponins [11]. A. asphodeloides, a member of the Liliaceae family, is known for its steroidal saponins, commonly referred to as timosaponins, while triterpenoid compounds are rarely detected in this species. Through transcriptomic analysis of A. asphodeloides, we identified three homologous OSC genes: AaOSCR12, AaOSCL6, and AaOSCL10, while only AaOSCR12 exhibited catalytic activity. By performing site-directed mutagenesis, sequence alignment, and homology modeling, we identified six critical amino acid residues essential for the catalytic activity of AaOSCR12. Subsequently, we introduced mutations into the inactive AaOSCL10, substituting the six critical residues with the corresponding amino acids from AaOSCR12. This modification partially restored the catalytic activity of AaOSCL10. These findings provide insights into the catalytic mechanisms of OSCs and offer a basis for enzyme engineering to enhance or modify OSC functionality. Such advancements could facilitate the production of specific saponin backbones, supporting both the study of OSC enzymology and the biotechnological application of A. asphodeloides. 

The content of steroidal saponins in A. asphodeloides is closely associated with the copy number of biosynthetic pathway genes and the catalytic activity of key enzymes. Significant variations in saponin content have been observed among plants from different geographic regions, ranging from high to low [1]. CAS, a pivotal enzyme in the biosynthesis of timosaponins, acts as both a key enzyme and a rate-limiting step in the pathway. The enzymatic activity of CAS is influenced by six critical amino acid residues, including AaOSCL10 and AaOSCL6. Mutations at these sites can result in complete loss of enzymatic activity. Additionally, it is hypothesized that point mutations in the OSC gene may occur in A. asphodeloides populations from various regions. Such mutations could lead to diminished activity of OSC enzymes, subsequently reducing the accumulation of steroidal saponins. This reduction impacts the quality and medicinal value of A. asphodeloides derived from different locations. To address these variations, systematic studies focusing on the OSC gene and the complete biosynthetic pathway of timosaponins are essential. These investigations would enhance our understanding of the genetic and enzymatic mechanisms underlying saponin production. Moreover, such insights could lay a solid foundation for breeding superior A. asphodeloides cultivars and establishing robust quality control standards, ensuring consistent efficacy and quality of medicinal products derived from this plant.

Steroid saponins can form molecular complexes with cholesterol in fungal membranes, exhibiting antimicrobial activity [56]. Pre-formed precursors of defensive compounds are initially non-toxic to the plant itself and only transform into toxic products after tissue damage [57]. The antifungal activity of spirostanol-type saponins has been known for a long time [41,42,52]. Previous studies indicate spirostanol-type saponins show antifungal activity, their corresponding furostanol-type saponins usually show little to no activity, indicating that the spirostanol-type saponins serves as the antifungal activity compounds [53,58]. The identified AaF26G1 in this study demonstrates the capability to convert non-antifungal furostanol-type steroidal saponins into spirostanol-type steroid sapogenins, thereby exerting antifungal activity. 

Our research on the enzymatic catalytic mechanism and the antifungal activity of timosaponins has important implications for both pharmaceutical and agricultural fields. The insights gained from our study could potentially lead to the design of new antifungal compounds, which could be valuable in the treatment of fungal infections. Additionally, understanding the biosynthetic pathways of timosaponins could contribute to the improvement of crop resistance to fungal diseases, thus enhancing agricultural productivity. 

# Conclusions

In this study, we elucidated the biosynthetic pathways of phytosterols and timosaponins in A. asphodeloides, with a focus on the extensive characterization of AaOSCR12. Six critical amino acids (257H, 369N, 448T, 507V, 558P, 616Y) were identified as active sites. The divergence step, catalyzed by AaSMT1 and AaSSR2, leads to the formation of phytosterols and cholesterol. Specific CYP450 enzymes, AaCYP90B27 and AaCYP90B2, were identified. The functional characterization of AaF26G1 was achieved in vitro, revealing the conversion mechanism from furostanol to spirostanol timosaponins. Spirostanol-type timosaponin III exhibited superior antifungal activity compared to furostanol-type II, potentially linked to AaF26G1-mediated plant defense. The identified genes have been deposited in NCBI under accession numbers PP163422 to PP163430.
