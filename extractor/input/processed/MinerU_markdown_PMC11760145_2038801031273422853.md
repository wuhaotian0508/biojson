Supporting Information 

ABSTRACT: Steviol glycosides (SGs) are noncaloric natural sweeteners found in the leaves of stevia (Stevia rebaudiana). These diterpene glycosides are biosynthesized by attaching varying numbers of monosaccharides, primarily glucose, to steviol aglycone. Rebaudioside (Reb) D and Reb M are highly glucosylated SGs that are valued for their superior sweetness and organoleptic properties, yet they are present in limited quantities in stevia leaves. This study aims to improve the substrate preference and catalytic efficiency of UDP-sugar-dependent glycosyltransferase UGT91D2 from stevia, which acts as a bottleneck in the biosynthesis of Reb D and Reb M. We modeled the structure of UGT91D2 and substituted two amino acid residues, Y134 and V155, which are located near the glycosyl acceptor and donor, respectively. Expression of the $U G T 9 { \cal { 1 } } D 2 ^ { V { \cal { 1 } } { \cal { 5 } } { \cal { 5 } } T }$ in budding yeast significantly enhanced the production of Reb D and Reb M. Furthermore, transient expression in Nicotiana benthamiana revealed that the V155T substitution improved the glucosylation activity of UGT91D2, suggesting that this substitution enhances UDP-glucose binding and reduces side reactions involving nonglucose donors. By coexpressing multiple stevia UGT genes in N. benthamiana, we successfully produced highly glucosylated SGs from steviol. Our results provide insights into the substrate specificity of UGT91D2 and contribute to the engineering of SG biosynthesis. 

KEYWORDS: UDP-glucose, UDP-sugar-dependent glycosyltransferase, stevia, steviol glycoside 

# 1. INTRODUCTION

It is important to establish a sustainable supply of natural alternatives to the high-caloric sugars due to health concerns associated with excessive consumption of sucrose and fructose.1 Stevia (Stevia rebaudiana), a perennial herb plant native to Paraguay in South America and a member of the Asteraceae family, has emerged as a valuable resource for providing a noncaloric natural sweetener.2 The sweetness of stevia extract is primarily due to the substantial accumulation of steviol glycosides (SGs) in its leaves, which constitute $1 0 { - } 3 0 \%$ of the plant’s dry weight.3 These SGs are intensely sweet, ranging from 150 to 300 times sweeter than sucrose.4 SGs are glycosides composed of tetracyclic diterpene aglycone steviol (St) and glycan chains of varying lengths. The glycans, mainly composed of glucose (Glc), are attached to the C13 hydroxyl group and the C19 carboxyl group of the St aglycone, which are referred to as R1 and R2, respectively (Figure 1).5,6 

Stevia leaves contain a diverse range of SGs, with stevioside (Stv) and rebaudioside (Reb) A being the most abundant.3 Additional highly glucosylated and branched SGs, such as Reb D and Reb M exhibit enhanced sweetness and superior organoleptic properties,7,8 but are present in trace amounts $( 0 . 3 \mathrm { - } 0 . 4 \%$ of dry weight) in stevia leaves.3 As both Reb D and Reb M are increasingly being used in food and beverage industries,9 advanced technologies such as recrystallization, chromatography, and membrane separation, are required to purify these specific SGs from crude stevia leaf extracts.10 Consequently, 

conventional breeding and transgenic approaches aimed at increasing the content of the desirable sweet SGs in stevia plants have become a major focus of research.11 −13 

The biosynthesis of SGs involves the formation of the diterpenoid St, followed by glycosylation of the aglycone.3 A network of glycosylation reactions generates a series of SGs from St, relying on four UDP-sugar-dependent glycosyltransferases (UGTs), UGT85C2, UGT74G1, UGT91D2, and UGT76G1, each of which uses UDP-sugars as glycosyl donors (Figure 1A).14 It remains unclear whether the formation of all SGs indicated in Figure 1A depends solely on these four UGTs, or whether other undefined enzymes are involved. Enzyme promiscuity may also contribute to the production of small amounts of SG analogs containing other monosaccharides, such as rhamnose (Rh) and xylose (Xy) (Figure 1B).3 Such SGs with nonglucose moieties generally exhibit lower sweetness intensity and poorer taste quality compared to their glucosylated counterparts.15,16

UGT76G1 adds a third Glc to a St-attached Glc moiety in SGs via a $\beta$ -1,3 linkage,17 while UGT91D2 adds a second Glc via a $\beta \mathrm { . }$ - 1,2 linkage.18 UGT76G1 catalyzes glycosylation on both the R1 and R2 sides by accommodating SG substrates in opposite orientations within its hydrophobic cavity.5,19,20 Similarly, dualorientation SG recognition has been reported for Oryza sativa UGT91C1 (OsUGT91C1), which is a rice homologue of UGT91D2 with its crystal structure determined, catalyzing β-1,2 glycosylation of SGs on both the R1 and R2 sides.6 In contrast to OsUGT91C1, which glucosylates SGs on both sides almost equally,6 UGT91D2 exhibits reduced activity on the R2 side compared to the R1 side,21,22 leading to limited accumulation of certain SGs in stevia, such as Reb D and Reb M.3 

To increase the production of commercially valuable SGs in stevia such as Reb D and Reb M, it is crucial to address the metabolic bottleneck associated with UGT91D2-mediated 

glucosylation on the R2 side.21,22 To address this question, we conducted protein engineering of UGT91D2 based on its model structure. Using yeast transformation technology and transient gene expression experiments in Nicotiana benthamiana, the current study demonstrates that V155T substitution significantly improves the sugar-donor specificity of UGT91D2, thereby suppressing undesired side reactions and increasing accumulation of highly glucosylated SGs. 

# 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1. Plant Materials. Surface-sterilized N. benthamiana seeds were germinated on half-strength Gamborg B5 medium solidified with $0 . 7 \%$ (w/v) agar and supplemented with $3 \%$ (w/ v) sucrose. Two-week-old seedlings were then transferred to soil (JA Nipi Horticulture Soil No. 1; Nippon Hiryo, Gunma, Japan) in plastic pots. Six-week-old N. benthamiana plants were used for the experiments. The plants were grown in a culture room at 25 $^ \circ \mathrm { C }$ in 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod. 

# 2.2. Structural Modeling and Docking Simulation.

Model structures of UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T were generated using the SWISS-MODEL,23 based on the OsUGT91C1:UDP:steviol 19-O-glucoside (S19G) complex structure (PDB ID: 7ES1).6 The initial structures of UDP-Glc, rubusoside (Rub), and Stv were derived from SMILES string inputs and optimized using the eLBOW AM1 QM method via the eLBOW program in PHENIX (version 1.17.1).24 AutoDock Vina25 was employed as the docking engine to predict the binding interactions of UDP-Glc, Rub, and Stv with the model structures of UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T and the reported structure of OsUGT91C1 in OsUGT91C1:UDP:S19G complex. For UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T, the side chains of residues T155, T156, S376, and D397 were set as flexible during the UDP-Glc docking calculations. For Rub and Stv docking calculations, the side chains of residues Y134, T135, M164, D217, and M221 were set as flexible. In the case of OsUGT91C1, the side chains of residues L150 and D381 were set as flexible for the UDP-Glc docking calculations, while the side chains of residues V129, F130, M155, I159, and F208 were set as flexible for Rub and Stv docking calculations. The binding affinities of the UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T models and OsUGT91C1 for substrates are summarized in Table S1. 

2.3. Enzymatic Analysis in Yeast. The full-length coding sequences of UGT genes from stevia (GenBank IDs: UGT85C2, AAR06916.1; UGT74G1, AAR06920.1; UGT76G1, ACT33422.1; UGT91D2, LC835675.1) with appropriate restriction sites were synthesized by Eurofins Genomics (Tokyo). The nucleotide sequences corresponding to amino acid residues substituted in UGT91D2 variants are as follows: ACC, AGC, and GCC for T155, S155, and A155, respectively; and TTT, ATT, and CTT for F134, I134, and L134, respectively. The sequences of UGT91D2 and its variants, which included NotI and PacI sites, were digested with the respective enzymes and inserted into the corresponding sites on the pESC-TRP vector (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). Similarly, the UGT85C2 sequence, with BglII and SalI sites, was digested and cloned into the BamHI and SalI sites on the pESC-URA vector (Agilent Technologies) to create pESC-URA-85C2. A pESC-HIS-74G1−76G1 vector, which expresses both UGT74G1 and UGT76G1, was constructed using the pESC-HIS vector (Agilent Technologies) by inserting UGT74G1 into the NotI and PacI sites and UGT76G1 into the BamHI and SalI sites. The three vectors-pESC-URA-85C2, pESC-HIS-74G1− 76G1, and a pESC-TRP vector harboring UGT91D2 or one of UGT91D2 variants-were introduced into Saccharomyces cerevisiae strain YPH499 using the lithium acetate method.26 Transformed yeasts were selected on amino acid dropout synthetic medium (Agilent Technologies), containing Glc as the carbon source and solidified with agar. 

A single colony of yeast cells was propagated in 2 mL of amino acid dropout synthetic liquid medium containing Glc overnight at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . After centrifugation at ${ 5 , 0 0 0 g }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , the cells were resuspended in $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of amino acid dropout synthetic medium with galactose, supplemented with St at a concentration of 10 $\mu \mathrm { g / m L } ,$ and cultured for an additional 48 h at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After cultivation, the culture was centrifuged to separate the supernatant from the cell pellet. The supernatant was adsorbed onto a Sep-Pak C18 6 cc Vac Cartridge (Waters Corporation, Milford, MA), which had been prewashed with acetonitrile and equilibrated with water. The cartridge was then washed with $2 0 \%$ (v/v) acetonitrile and eluted with $8 0 \%$ $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ acetonitrile. After drying, the eluate was dissolved in $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $5 0 \%$ (v/v) 

acetonitrile. SGs in the extracts were analyzed by LC-MS/MS as described below. 

2.4. Agrobacterium-Mediated Transient Overexpression. The full-length coding sequences of UGT genes (GenBank ID: UGT91D1, AAR06918.1; IDs of other UGTs were shown above) were amplified by PCR. The amplified fragments were cloned into the pENTR/D-TOPO vector (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). After sequence confirmation, the coding regions in pENTR/D-TOPO were transferred into the pGWB2 vector27 using LR Clonase II (Thermo Fisher Scientific) to generate binary vectors. 

We used the binary vector pBI121 as a control and the p19 vector for silencing suppression.28 The vectors were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 using the heat shock method. The genes were transiently overexpressed in the youngest fully expanded leaves of plants, as previously described.29 Bacterial suspensions of UGT overexpression vector(s) or a control vector pBI121 and the p19 vector were combined in a 7:3 ratio by volume. When multiple UGT vectors were used, the suspension of four UGT vectors, or a mixture of three UGT vectors and one control vector, were combined in equal volumes.

3.1. UGT91D2 Model Structure. We utilized a registered sequence of UGT91D2 (GenBank ID: LC835675.1), which includes 12 additional amino acids at the N terminus compared to a previously reported sequence (Figure S1).21 UGT91D2 shares $4 0 \%$ amino acid sequence identity $5 6 \%$ similarity) with the functionally similar OsUGT91C1 from rice (Figure S1).6 A model structure of UGT91D2 was generated based on the reported OsUGT91C1 structure in complex with UDP and S19G (OsUGT91C1:UDP:S19G, PDB ID: 7ES1).6 The sugar donor, UDP-Glc, and sugar acceptors, SGs, were docked into the UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1 structures (Figures 2 and S2− S4). As substrates for glycosylation on the R1 and R2 sides, Rub and Stv were docked into the UGT91 structures, respectively, with the R1 and R2 sides of Rub and Stv oriented toward the catalytic center at the bottom of the active site cavity, respectively (Figures 2 and S2−S4). The near-identical positions of the UDP and St skeletons in the reported and simulated OsUGT91C1 structures support the reliability of the docking simulation (Figure S2). 

The overall polypeptide structures of the UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1 show a high degree of similarity, with Cα Root Mean Square Deviations (RMSDs) of 0.19 Å. Both UGT91 proteins share a common active site architecture, featuring a catalytic dyad of H and D residues�H38/D133 in UGT91D2 and H27/D128 in OsUGT91C1�positioned and oriented nearly identically (Figures 2 and S4). The catalytic H residue, stabilized by the D residue, acts as a general base, abstracting a proton from glycosyl acceptors and playing a key role in the nucleophilic $S _ { \mathrm { N } } 2$ reaction mechanism, a common reaction mechanism for plant UGTs.35 

The sugar donor UDP-Glc is optimally oriented for the glycosylation reaction through interactions with multiple amino acid residues, many of which are located within the PSPG (Plant Secondary Product Glycosyltransferase) box in the C-terminal domain (Figures 2 and S1). This region, often conserved in plant UGTs, plays a crucial role in recognizing the UDP moiety of UDP-sugars.36 The UDP is positioned almost identically in both UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1 structures (Figures 2 and S4). Invariant residues within the PSPG box, such as W355/Q358/ E381 in UGT91D2 and W339/Q342/E365 in OsUGT91C1, likely contribute to the recognition and positioning of UDP, as reported in other UGT structures (Figures 2 and S1).19,37 In contrast, the Glc moiety of UDP-Glc, which is transferred during glycosylation, is shifted by 2.2 and $3 . 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } }$ away from the catalytic center in UGT91D2 complexes compared to its position in the OsUGT91C1 counterparts (Figure 2). In OsUGT91C1, L150 is positioned near the Glc moiety of UDP-Glc, forming a pocket that provides more space to accommodate the $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime \prime } { \cdot } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O H }$ group of the sugar moiety compared to V155, in UGT91D2 (Figures 2, S3, and S4). Superimposing the UGT91 structures shows that the distances between ${ \mathrm { V 1 5 5 - C } } \gamma$ in UGT91D2 and the ${ \mathrm { C } } { 5 ^ { \prime \prime } } { \cdot } { \mathrm { C } } { \mathrm { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { O H } }$ in OsUGT91C1 are 2.4 and $1 . 9 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ for the Ruband Stv-bound structures, respectively, suggesting potential steric hindrance (Figure S4). The substitution of L150 with V155 in UGT91D2 may influence the positioning of the Glc moiety, thereby affecting catalytic activity. 

The residues surrounding the sugar acceptors, Rub and Stv, show greater variability between UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1 compared to those around UDP-Glc (Figures 2 and S1). This variability may facilitate flexible recognition of different SGs and allow for different orientations of the same SGs. When comparing the positions of the SGs between UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1, Rub is positioned similarly in both structures (Figure 2A). However, Stv, particularly its Glc moiety at R2 (which undergoes further glycosylation), is shifted by 2.0 Å toward the protein (away from the catalytic center) in UGT91D2 compared to its position in OsUGT91C1 (Figure 2B). Superimposing Rub and Stv in each UGT91 structure reveals that the two SGs are positioned nearly identically in OsUGT91C1 (Figure S3A). In contrast, Stv (especially its Glc moiety) is shifted by 2.1 Å toward the protein in UGT91D2 compared to Rub (Figure S3B). This shift of Stv in UGT91D2, but not in OsUGT91C1, positions the SG substrates slightly farther from the catalytic centers, which may explain the limited glycosylation on the R2 side by UGT91D2. Multiple residues differ between the UGT91 enzymes (Figures 2 and S1), potentially contributing to the differential recognition of Stv. One such variant residue is Y134 in UGT91D2, located near the relevant Glc moiety of Stv (Figure 2). The substitution of Y134 with V129 in OsUGT91C1 likely creates additional space in the cavity, influencing SG recognition. 

In the following sections, we will focus on two specific residues, V155 and Y134, in UGT91D2, which are thought to play critical roles in the recognition of the Glc moieties in UDP-Glc and SGs, respectively. Variations in these residues may explain the differences in substrate binding observed between the UGT91 enzymes. Substitutions of V155 and Y134 are likely to significantly affect substrate recognition and enzymatic activity.

In contrast, the accumulation of a minor SG Reb I increased with the expression of the four native UGTs (3UGT $^ +$ UGT91D2), similar to other minor SGs, and this increase was not diminished by $U G T 9 { \cal { 1 } } D 2 ^ { \mathrm { V 1 5 5 T } }$ expression $\left( 3 \mathrm { U G T } + \mathrm { U G T 9 1 D 2 ^ { \mathrm { V 1 5 5 T } } } \right)$ (Figure 6). Reb I contains only Glc as sugar moieties (Figure 1B) and is likely formed from Reb A through $\beta$ -1,3 glycosylation, probably mediated by UGT76G1 rather than UGT91D2.

# 4. DISCUSSION

4.1. Regiospecificity of UGT91D2. UGT91D2 catalyzes the $\beta$ -1,2 glucosylation of SGs on both the R1 and R2 sides, using UDP-Glc as the sugar donor. However, glucosylation activity on the R2 side is significantly lower compared to the R1 side.21,22 This regiospecificity of UGT91D2 primarily contributes to the more higher accumulation of Stv and Reb A in stevia, 

while the accumulation of highly glucosylated SGs such as Reb D and Reb M is lower.3 This pattern of SG accumulation was also observed in budding yeast (Figure 3) and N. benthamiana (Figures 4B,D and 5), even when expressing a variant UGT91D2V155T that encodes an enzyme with improved activity. This result indicates that the V155T substitution only partially mitigate the limited glycosylation on the R2 side. 

Structure-guided engineering of UGT91D2 revealed that the V155T substitution significantly increased the glycosylation activity on the R2 side. Substantial increases of Reb E, Reb D, and Reb M levels were observed in yeast (Figure 3) and N. benthamiana (Figures 4B,D and 5) when expressing $U G T 9 { \cal { 1 } } D 2 ^ { V I { \cal { 5 } } { \cal { 5 } } T }$ . In contrast, no marked increase in the R1-side glycosylation due to the V155T substitution was observed. Significant, albeit minor (less than 1.5 fold), increases in Sb and Reb B were detected in these transient N. benthamiana gene expression experiments (Figures 4A and 5), while no increase in Stv and Reb A was observed (Figures 4B and 5). Despite these results, improved sugar-donor specificity was noted for glycosylation on both the R1 and R2 sides, as evidenced by the accumulation of minor SGs (discussed below). Indeed, the structural alterations associated with the V155T substitution seem to significantly affect UDP-sugar recognition, but not on SG recognition, which is expected given the location of the residue. SGs are positioned similarly in the UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T model structures, with a comparative degree of Stv shift observed in both (Figures S6 and S7). However, in the Rub- and Stv-bound UGT91D2V155T structures, the Glc moiety of UDP-Glc is shifted toward the catalytic center by 1.4 and 2.6 Å, respectively, compared to the UGT91D2 counterparts (Figure S7). This highlights the critical role of this residue in UDP-sugar recognition. These shifts in both the Rub- and Stvbound models also support the observed improvement in sugardonor specificity for glycosylation on both the R1 and R2 sides. 

To explain the seemingly contradictory results mentioned above, it is reasonable to consider that the improvement in activity due to the V155T substitution is more pronounced in the more restricted glycosylation on the R2 side compared to the R1 side, as reflected in the observed accumulation of major SGs. 

A comparison of the UGT91 structures revealed that Stv, a substrate for R2-side glycosylation, is shifted away from the catalytic center compared to Rub, a substrate for the R1-side reaction, in UGT91D2 (Figure S3B). This shift is not observed in OsUGT91C1 (Figure S3A). These results suggest that the distinct positioning of the sugar acceptors for R1- and R2-side glycosylation in UGT91D2 contributes to its regiospecific properties. Reflecting ambiguous specificities toward sugar acceptors, the amino acid residues surrounding SGs that contribute to SG recognition are usually variable and less conserved. Indeed, many amino acids in the presumed SGbinding region differ between UGT91D2 and OsUGT91C1 (Figures 2 and S1). We focused on one of these residues, Y134 in UGT91D2, and performed substitutions, but these did not lead to an improvement in activity (Figure 3). To improve R2-side glycosylation activity by shifting the substrate binding position, further modifications of multiple residues involved in substrate recognition would likely be required. 

To further investigate these questions in detail, in vitro enzyme assays using purified recombinant proteins will be necessary to quantitatively evaluate UGT91D2 enzymatic activity on each side. 4.2. Sugar-Donor Specificity of UGT91D2. In N. benthamiana, the expression of the variant UGT91D2V155T instead of the native UGT91D2, significantly suppressed the accumulation of shunt products (Figures 4B,D and 6). These products were formed through β-1,2 glycosylation with UDPsugars other than UDP-Glc, such as UDP-Rh and UDP-Xy. The side reaction products include analogs of Stv (Dul A), Reb A (Reb C, Reb F), Reb D (Reb J), and Reb M (Reb N) (Figure 1B). The further reduction in these minor SGs suggest that the V155T substitution suppresses the ambiguous recognition of UDP-sugars, thereby enhancing specificity toward UDP-Glc in UGT91D2-catalyzed glycosylation on both the R1 and R2 sides. This improvement in donor specificity supports the V155Tdependent enhancement of UGT91D2 activity on the R1 side, even though this enhancement was not strongly supported by the accumulation results of major SGs, as discussed above. 

To understand how the V155T substitution in UGT91D2 increase specificity toward UDP-Glc, UGT91D2V155T model structures in complex with the substrates (UGT91D2V155T:UDP-Glc:Rub and UGT91D2V155T:UDP-Glc:Stv) were closely examined around this particular residue. As a result of the shift in the Glc moiety of UGT-Glc (mentioned earlier in Figure S7), in both UGT91D2V155T models, the $\mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \circ } -$ OH of the Glc acceptor and the C1’’ of the Glc donor are positioned near the catalytic H38, at distances of 2.9 and $4 . 0 \mathring \mathrm { A } _ { \varepsilon }$ , respectively (Figure 7). Notably, the Glc moiety of UDP-Glc forms direct or indirect polar interactions with residues such as T155, T156, S376, and D397 in both UGT91D2V155T models (Figure 7). 

In particular, the ${ \mathrm { C } } { 5 } ^ { \prime \prime } { \cdot } { \mathrm { C } } { \mathrm { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { O H } }$ group of the Glc moiety forms a hydrogen bond with the oxygen atom in the T155 side chain at a distance of 2.8 Å (Figure 7). Although the size of the cavity accommodating the $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime \prime } { \cdot } \mathrm { C H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O H }$ are comparable between 2UGT91D2 and UGT91D2V155T, the hydrogen bond formation in UGT91D2V155T brings the $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime \prime } { \cdot } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { \bar { O } H }$ closer to the catalytic center (Figure S8). Consequently, the $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime \prime } { \cdot } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } \mathrm { H }$ 

is positioned similarly in the UGT91D2V155 T and OsUGT91C1 (Figure S8C,D). This polar interaction is unique to UDP-Glc, as corresponding $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime \prime } { \mathrm { - } } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { H } _ { 3 }$ in UDP-Rh and UDP-Xh cannot form a hydrogen bond with the hydroxy group in T155 (Figure S9). This interaction, specific to UDP-Glc, may contribute to the improved sugar-donor specificity observed in UGT91D2V155T. 

4.3. Insights into SG Biosynthesis Engineering. Plant UGTs usually exhibit ambiguous substrate specificities, a trait also observed in the two GGTs involved in SG biosynthesis, UGT76G1 and UGT91D2.14 These enzymes can accept a diverse range of SGs and UDP-sugars as glycosyl acceptors and donors, respectively. Both UGT76G1 and UGT91D2 are critical for producing highly glucosylated and branched SGs, such as Reb D and Reb M, which are valued for their desirable commercial properties. Consequently, they are key targets for engineering strategies aimed at enhancing SG production in stevia. Naturally occurring alleles of UGT76G1 have been shown to influence enzyme activity, sometimes resulting in the reduction or elimination of specific SGs in stevia plants.40−42 Recent advances in structure-guided engineering of UGT76G1 and UGT91D2 have led to the development of enzyme variants with enhanced properties.21,43−45 

In this study, we demonstrate an example of engineering the UGT91D2 enzyme, specifically through the V155T substitution. This modification enhances the enzyme’s specificity for sugar donors, effectively minimizing undesired side reactions and significantly boosting the production of highly glucosylated SGs. These may involve targeting the UGT91D2 gene in the stevia genome using advanced CRISPR-Cas technologies with base editing46 and prime editing47 to enable precise DNA modifications. It is noteworthy also that when producing SGs from the steviol precursor, by expressing UGTs from stevia, we found that the V155T substitution in UGT91D2 had a more pronounced effect on SG accumulation in the plant host N. benthamiana (Figure 5) than in budding yeast (Figure 3). These findings may indicate the presence of hitherto undefined plantspecific mechanisms that could enable the improved activity of UGT91D2 to be more effectively exerted on SG accumulation. This highlights the potential of targeted editing to replace the 

native UGT91D2 allele with one encoding the V155T variant in SG-producing stevia plants. 

Moreover, the heterogeneous production of valuable SGs in microorganisms, such as Escherichia coli and yeast, presents an attractive alternative, with several attempts having been reported.48−50 The UGT91D2 variant with improved activity could be adapted for use in the microbial systems as well. The advancements demonstrated in this study represent a significant step forward in enhancing the biosynthesis of next-generation stevia sweeteners.
