# MYB transcription factors that colour our fruit

Andrew C. Allan, Roger P. Hellens and William A. Laing

HortResearch, Mt Albert Research Centre, Private Bag 92169, Auckland, New Zealand

Anthocyanin concentration is a primary determinant of plant colour. Fruit anthocyanin biosynthesis is controlled by a distinct clade of R2R3 MYB transcription factors. In apple, three recent papers describe the discovery of MYB genes activating skin, flesh and foliage anthocyanic colour. These findings lead the way to new approaches in the breeding and biotechnological development of fruit with new colour patterns.

# Anthocyanins in our food and drink

Anthocyanins, in combination with carotenoids or chlorophylls, are responsible for almost all fruit colouration. Millennia of plant breeding and domestication have produced numerous crops where the levels and distribution of anthocyanin pigments vary widely and change during development and in response to the environment. The accumulation of anthocyanin pigments in fruit and vegetables is an important determinant of ripeness and quality. These pigments also provide essential cultivar differentiation for consumers and are implicated in the health attributes of our foods. Three recent papers describe the transcriptional control of anthocyanin levels in apple [1–3]. Although in many fruits anthocyanic red, purple and blue compounds are attractive and have been selected for by humans, in rice, red cultivars have poorer qualities and have been selected against [4].

Anthocyanins belong to the diverse group of ubiquitous secondary metabolites known as flavonoids. In plants, flavonoids are believed to have a variety of functions, including defence and protection against light stress; the pigmented anthocyanin compounds play an important reproductive role as attractants in plant and animal interactions.

# Colour-controlling transcription factors

Anthocyanin accumulation is controlled through the coordinated expression of genes encoding the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway enzymes. From studies in a diverse array of plant species, it is apparent that this coordinated expression is controlled at the transcriptional level, usually by an R2R3 MYB and/or a basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) transcription factor (TF).

# MYB transcription factors

Plant MYBs have been implicated in controlling pathways as diverse as secondary metabolism (including the anthocyanin pathway), development, signal transduction and disease resistance [5]. They are characterized by a structurally conserved DNA-binding domain consisting of single

or multiple imperfect repeats; those associated with the anthocyanin pathway are of the two-repeat (R2R3) class. There are 133 R2R3 MYB TF-encoding genes in Arabidopsis classified into 24 subgroups [6]. The PRODUCTION OF ANTHOCYANIN PIGMENT 1 (PAP1, or AtMYB75) MYB of Arabidopsis [7] falls into subgroup 10 [6] (Figure 1). Its protein sequence demonstrates a high degree of amino acid conservation with other known MYB regulators of anthocyanin production. Purple plants are produced when PAP1 was overexpressed in transgenic Arabidopsis. This is a result of the upregulation of several genes in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway [7,8]. In addition, another R2R3 MYB from ripening strawberry, FaMYB1, acts to repress transcription of anthocyanin-related genes [9].

# bHLH transcription factors

The bHLH TF family has also been implicated in a range of functions in plants, frequently in conjunction with MYBs. They are defined by a bHLH domain of ${ \sim } 1 8$ basic amino acids followed by two regions of hydrophobic $\alpha$ -helices separated by an intervening loop. In Arabidopsis, there are 161 bHLH TF-encoding genes divided into 12 subgroups [10].

The R2R3 MYB TFs that regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis have been shown to interact closely with bHLH TFs [11,12]. Examples of functional partnerships between colour-related MYBs and bHLHs include the maize ZmC1 MYB and ZmB bHLH, the petunia AN2 MYB and AN1 and JAF13 bHLHs, and the Antirrhinum Ros1, Ros2 and Ve MYBs and the Mut and Del bHLHs [11,13–15]. Over-expressing Arabidopsis lines of PAP1 have elevated transcript levels of the TT8 gene that encodes a bHLH protein involved in regulating condensed tannin and anthocyanin biosynthesis [8,16]. The dependency of MYB proteins on a bHLH co-regulator is linked to a small signature motif in the R2R3 binding domain of the MYB protein [17].

# Transcriptional control of anthocyanins in fruit

Recently, there has been a surge in research on the underlying mechanism regulating fruit colour via plant anthocyanin production. Most studies focus on plants that we love to eat, ferment and use to decorate, including grape, apple, sweet potato, tomato, pepper, petunia and snapdragon. In particular, red coloration of fruit skin is an important determinant of consumer preference and marketability, and is arguably nowhere more important than in the differences between white and red grapes. Grapes (Vitis vinifera) are classified as either red or white, based on the presence or absence of anthocyanin in the berry skin. This phenotype is controlled from a single genetic locus [18,19]. More recently, advances have been achieved in apple (Malus x domestica) where three groups have identified the R2R3 MYB TFs responsible for apple anthocyanin accumulation [1–3]. In a paper seeking to examine the control of apple fruit skin anthocyanins, MdMYBA was isolated from a pale-red skinned apple cultivar called ’Tsugaru’ and characterized in a deep-red skinned cultivar called ‘Jonathan’ [1]. Similarly, MdMYB1 [3] was isolated from the red skinned apple ‘Cripps Pink’. MdMYB1 expression correlated with anthocyanin synthesis in red regions of the fruit skin. In addition, when fruit that had been kept in the dark in bags for several months were exposed to sunlight, the transcript of MdMYB1 increased over several days, correlating with skin anthocyanin accumulation. The expression of MdMYBA is induced by UV-B irradiation and low-temperature treatment, both of which are known

to be important in the promotion of skin colour. Both groups [1,3] have also shown that transcripts of their MYB skin genes are significantly more abundant in red skin apple cultivars compared to non-red skin cultivars. In a red foliaged and red fleshed apple variety there was ectopic expression of a MYB called MdMYB10 in all tissues examined [2]. In other apple cultivars, this gene was only expressed in red tissue (e.g. fruit skin). From sequence alone we cannot be certain whether these genes (MdMYB10, MdMYB1 and MdMYBA) are alleles or are closely linked gene duplications.

# Future prospects – biotech and bio-discovery

The identification of the MYB TFs controlling anthocyanic colour in apples has opened up several exciting lines of development for perennial fruit plants. One of the major problems of fruit breeding is long generation times, for instance, apples usually require five years from germination to fruiting. In addition, the progeny trees or vines are

large and therefore take up much land area. Consequently, biotechnological approaches, including marker-assisted breeding and genetic transformation, are of particular importance in these species.

In apple, the two MYB genes, which control red colour in fruit skin and flesh, both segregate with phenotype and have been mapped [1,3,21], and are now available for marker-assisted breeding programmes. This is important in ensuring that desirable traits are retained in crosses between red- and green-fruited varieties. In addition, because the apple MYB genes that control fruit colour have been isolated, intragenic [22] approaches are now viable. Not only can new genetically modified apple varieties be directly created with the desired fruit colour using the apple gene that controls this, from its own ‘natural’ promoter, but also the red gene can be used in other systems. For example, the red colour can be used as a natural and very obvious selectable marker to follow development in genetically modified plants.

Lastly, the phenotypes resulting from differences in expression of MdMYB10, MdMYBA and MdMYB1 provide clues as to which alleles are responsible for the differences

between cultivated apples. Using sequence information from different apple varieties, it is possible to follow the human-controlled ‘evolution’ of this important trait. This is particularly important because these differences in colour patterning appear to result from differences in expression of the MYB genes, whose coding sequences are virtually identical between apple varieties. Alterations in the promoters of these genes might be the drivers of this diversity.

Once again, a MYB TF appears to be the primary determinant of differences in fruit colour between cultivated perennial plant varieties, as has been reported for annual species such as potato, tomato, pepper [23] and in various species of Antirrhinum [15]. In the case of apple and grape, it appears that it is the MYB component from the proposed regulatory complex of MYB–bHLH that is primarily responsible for the level of fruit anthocyanic colour [1–3].

# The ongoing saga of Agrobacterium–host interactions

Mery Dafny-Yelin, Avner Levy and Tzvi Tzfira

Department of Molecular, Cellular and Developmental Biology, University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, MI 48109, USA

Infection of plant cells by Agrobacterium leads to activation of specific mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK). In a recent paper, Djamei et al. (2007) showed that MAPK-mediated phosphorylation of VirE2-interacting protein 1 (VIP1) is required for its translocation into the host-cell nucleus and for activation of a pathogenesis-related gene, and that Agrobacterium uses the phosphorylated VIP1 to deliver its transfer-DNA molecule into the host cell. These findings join a long line of evidence showing how this clever bacterium has developed ways of using and abusing host biological systems for its own needs.

# Agrobacterium activates and represses various defense genes

Agrobacterium tumefaciens, a gram-negative soil-borne bacterium, is known more as the favored tool for plant genetic engineering than as a disease-causing bacterium. Nevertheless, similar to many other plant pathogens, this causative agent of crown gall disease is capable of eliciting a wide spectrum of defense responses in infected plant tissues [1,2]. It has recently been proposed that Agrobacterium infection activates the induction of various defense genes at early stages of the infection process.
