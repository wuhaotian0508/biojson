# Summary

OSW-1 is a cholestane saponin uniquely produced in Ornithogalum saundersiae and exhibits a potential antitumor activity. The enzymes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis in O. saundersiae, however, remain unclear. Herein, cholesterol was confirmed to be the precursor of OSW-1 using stable-isotope labeling method. Next, two cytochrome P450s, OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11, and their partner cytochrome P450 reductase OsCPR2 were mined by comparable transcriptome analysis and demonstrated to be involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis. Specifically, OsCYP90B94 catalyses cholesterol to 22R-hydroxycholesterol, which is then converted to $1 6 \beta \mathrm { . }$ -hydroxy-22-oxocholesterol, a precursor of OSW-1 by the second P450 OsCYP90G11 via two consecutive oxidation reactions. Unlike their homologues, OsCYP90G11 and OsCYP90B94 displayed broader catalytic promiscuity. OsCYP90B94 recognizes both cholesterol and $7 \beta$ -hydroxycholesterol, while OsCYP90G11 can react with cholesterol, 22R-hydroxycholesterol and $1 6 \beta , 2 2 R \mathrm { \cdot }$ - 

dihydroxycholesterol, suggesting their involvement in more than one pathway besides OSW-1 biosynthesis. This study lays a foundation for the complete characterization of OSW-1 biosynthesis. 

# Introduction

OSW-1 (Figure 1A), a steroidal saponin isolated from the bulbs of an ornamental plant Ornithogalum saundersiae, shows amazing anticancer activities against various cancer cells (Kubo et al., 1992; Mimaki et al., 1997; Zhang et al., 2017). Its anticancer effect is around 10–100 times higher than that of many anticancer drugs commonly used in clinic, such as mitomycin C, adriamycin, cisplatin, camptothecin and paclitaxel. Importantly, OSW-1 exhibited lower toxicity towards normal cells than that of cancer cells, demonstrating its relatively high safety (Tang et al., 2013). OSW-1 exerts anticancer effects by repressing cancer progression and suppressing migration, invasion and angiogenesis (Zhan et al., 2021). Therefore, OSW-1 is deemed as a potential lead compound for an anticancer drug. 

There are now two approaches preparing OSW-1. Plant extraction is the main source of OSW-1. OSW-1 content in the plant species, however, is low (a few parts per million of the dry weight) (Kubo et al., 1992), limiting its availability and increasing the price of OSW-1. The total synthesis of OSW-1, which has been achieved in several laboratories (Deng et al., 1999; Morzycki and Wojtkielewicz, 2002; Xue et al., 2008; Yu and Jin, 2002), is the second approach for preparing OSW-1. The chemical synthesis of OSW-1, however, usually suffers from the cumbersome protection/deprotection and the frequent usage of toxic agents (Deng et al., 1999; Morzycki and Wojtkielewicz, 2002; Tang et al., 2013; Xue et al., 2008; Yu and Jin, 2002). Chemically synthesized OSW-1 is not commercially available. The limited OSW-1 supply prevented the druggability researches of OSW-1. Hence, a promising alternative for the large-scale preparation of OSW-1 is highly desirable. 

The great progress made in the synthetic biology of natural products is now opening an avenue for the biomanufacturing of natural products through engineered cell factories. Introducing the biosynthetic pathways of natural products into chassis cells can form cell factories capable of producing natural products. Microbial production of natural products, such as artemisinic acid (Ro et al., 2006), vinblastine (Zhang et al., 2022) and opioids (Galanie et al., 2015), has been achieved in the engineered cell factories. If a biosynthetic pathway of OSW-1 is introduced into the chassis, the resulting cell factories will yield OSW-1 on a large scale. Therefore, the bioproduction of OSW-1 is a promising alternative to plant extraction and chemical synthesis. However, the mechanisms underlying OSW-1 biosynthesis remain unknown. 

OSW-1 bears the same skeleton as cholesterol (CHR), an important biosynthetic precursor of many bioactive steroidal derivatives, such as steroidal glycoalkaloids and steroidal saponins (Kunert et al., 2023; Umemoto et al., 2016). Thus, a two-stage biosynthetic pathway for OSW-1 was proposed: the formation of CHR and the modification of CHR to yield OSW-1 (Figure 1A). CHR is synthesized from acetyl-CoA via the classic cholesterogenesis pathway (Sonawane et al., 2016), which is well characterized, and the enzymes responsible for the cholesterogenesis pathway are conserved. Unlike the conserved cholesterogenesis pathway, the modification of CHR to form OSW-1 remains unclear. Structurally, three consecutive C-16, C-17 and C-22 oxidations firstly occur in CHR to form a polyoxygenated aglycone (polyOA), which is glycosylated by two consecutive glycosylations to form a steroidal saponin. Finally, acylation modifications towards the steroidal saponin result in the formation of OSW-1 (Figure 1A). As indicated by the proposed pathway, oxidations of CHR occur in the early step of the conversion of CHR to OSW-1.

# Results

# Chemical identification of O. saundersiae

The authenticity of source plants is crucial for identifying the biosynthesis pathway of natural products. O. saundersiae was previously reported to contain OSW-1 (Kubo et al., 1992). To verify the validity of the plant material used in this study, the existence of OSW-1 in the dichloromethane crude extract of the plant material was analysed using liquid chromatographymass spectrometry (LC–MS). A peak with a retention time (Rt) of 38.3 min, consistent with that of the OSW-1 standard, was present in the dichloromethane crude extract. The molecular formula of this compound was deduced to be $C _ { 4 7 } \mathsf { H } _ { 6 8 } \mathsf { O } _ { 1 5 }$ based on its sodium adduct ion at m/z 895.44482 $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N a } ] ^ { + }$ , which was consistent with the OSW-1 standard (Figure S1). Further NMR analysis assigned this compound as OSW-1 (Figures S2–S8; 

Table S1). This evidence, together with the morphological characteristics, confirmed the plant used in this study was O. saundersiae. 

# Cholesterol is a precursor of OSW-1

Cholesterol is thought to be a precursor of OSW-1 owing to its similar skeleton structures. To confirm this notion, D7-CHR was fed to the seedlings of O. saundersiae for 3 weeks (Figure 1B). To adsorb more D7-CHR from the culture medium, the seedlings should be allowed to grow more roots. Hence, in the course of the cultivation of seedlings in the solid medium, indole butyric acid (IBA) was supplemented to promote the growth of roots (Schlicht et al., 2013). After three consecutive subcultures in a solid culture medium, the roots of the seedlings are very abundant, making them very suitable for absorbing CHR (Figure 1B). After transferring sterile seedlings with well-developed roots to liquid culture medium containing D7-CHR for 3 weeks, LC–MS was used to detect the generation of isotope-labelled OSW-1. As shown in Figure 1C, D7-labelled OSW-1(D7-OSW-1) was detected by tracing the m/z 902.48810 $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N a } ] ^ { + }$ ion corresponding to the molecular formula of $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 7 } \mathsf { H } _ { 6 1 } { } ^ { 2 } \mathsf { H } _ { 7 } \mathsf { O } _ { 1 5 } \mathsf { N a }$ . Also, endogenous non-labelled OSW-1 was detected by monitoring the $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N a } ] ^ { + }$ ion with an m/z value of 895.44580. In the control seedlings, the endogenous non-labelled OSW-1 could be detected (m/z 895.44623 $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N a } ] ^ { + } )$ ), while D7-OSW-1 was not detected (Figure 1D). These data collectively confirmed that CHR is a precursor of OSW-1. Furthermore, the $m / z + 7$ isotopic peak of D7-OSW-1, derived from D7-CHR, indicated that the side chain of D7-CHR was retained in the conversion of CHR to OSW-1 (Figure 1E).

# OSW-1 varies among different tissues

As shown in Figure 1A, there are at least three enzymes, namely oxygenases, glycosyltransferases and acyltransferases, involved in the conversion of CHR to OSW-1 (Figure 1A). The oxidation of CHR to form polyOA has been proposed to be the first stage of OSW-1 biosynthesis. Although other oxygenases may participate in CHR oxidation, CYP450s were the preferred candidates, as previous studies have shown that P450s can hydroxylate CHR (Christ et al., 2019). CHR hydroxylases are key modifying enzymes specifically responsible for a certain group of steroidal skeletons in the biosynthesis of CHR-derived natural products (Cheng et al., 2021; Christ et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022). The knockdown of CHR hydroxylases causes a dramatic decline of CHR-derived metabolites (Umemoto et al., 2016). These facts suggested that the expression of CHR hydroxylases correlated positively with OSW-1 accumulation. It was thus arguably proposed that no or low expression of cholesterol hydroxylases would cause no or little accumulation of OSW-1 accordingly. As such, the tissues containing little or no OSW-1 were helpful for screening candidate P450 genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis. Consequently, we investigated the tissue distribution of OSW-1 to discover tissues having little or no OSW-1 accumulation. OSW-1 content in five tissues (flowers, scapes, leaves, bulbs and roots) was then quantified by HPLC using the external standard method (Figure 2A-a). The result showed that the accumulation of OSW-1 differs significantly between different tissues. The highest OSW-1 content was detected in the bulb, suggesting the higher expression of genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis in this tissue (Figure 2A-a). On the contrary, no 

apparent OSW-1 was observed in the scape, indicating that no or only trace expression of the genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis occurred (Figure 2A-a). Consequently, the bulb and the scape were selected as the plant material to identify the P450 genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis. It can be concluded that the genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis were significantly and differentially expressed between these two tissues. Comparative transcriptome analysis between bulb and scape was thus performed to mine the key genes responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis. 

# Exploring differentially expressed CYP450s through comparative transcriptome analysis

To identify candidate genes involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis, two tissues (bulb and scape) with significantly different OSW-1 accumulation were selected for comparative transcriptome analysis (Figure 2B). After quality control of the original sequencing data, a total of 24 436 350 reads with an $\mathsf { N } _ { 5 0 }$ of 2192 bp and a GC content of $4 7 . 7 \%$ were produced, and a total of 111 332 unigenes were obtained with an $N _ { 5 0 }$ of 1599 bp after fragment assembly. Furthermore, 49 115 unigenes were annotated using public databases (NR, NT, GO, KOG, Swiss-Prot, Pfam and KEGG). Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript sequence per Millions base pairs sequenced (FPKM) values were calculated to represent the gene expression levels and the genes with DESeq2 Padj $< 0 . 0 5$ and |log2FoldChange $> 1$ were selected as differentially expressed genes (DEGs). A total of 124 DEGs were annotated as CYP450s (Figure 2C), 193 as glycosyltransferases, 101 as acyltransferases, 27 as Fe (II)/2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (2OGDs) and one as a cytochrome P450 reductase 

(OsCPR2, Cluster-23084.32652) (Figure 2D). These genes may be involved in the structural modification of the parent nucleus of OSW-1. 

P450s are the preferred biocatalysts capable of hydroxylating CHR. Thus, we prioritized the analysis of 124 DEGs encoding P450s (Figure 2C). Based on the Blast X analysis and prediction of Open Reading Frame (ORF) Finder, we further screened 96 full-length candidates from 124 CYP450 DEGs. Of these full-length CYP450s, 10 candidate genes involved in the brassinosteroid (BR) or steroid biosynthesis pathway were selected for comprehensive functional characterization. Of the 10 candidates, two members, Cluster-40111.0 and Cluster-23084.50752, were annotated to encode CYP450s belonging to the CYP90B subfamily, some of which were capable of hydroxylating C-22 of CHR (Fujita et al., 2006; Yin et al., 2018) (Figure 2E). 

# Physicochemical property and conservation analysis of candidate genes

Sequence analysis showed that Cluster-40111.0, Cluster-23084.50752 and Cluster-23084.32652 had an ORF of 1446 bp, 1464 bp and 2112 bp, encoding proteins with 481, 487 and 703 amino acids, respectively. The theoretical molecular weights of the three proteins were 54.6, 55.6 and $7 7 . 8 ~ \mathsf { k D a }$ , and their calculated isoelectric points were 8.85, 8.29 and 5.21, respectively (Table S2). 

Three absolutely-conserved domains were identified in Cluster-40111.0 and Cluster-23084.50752 using the Conserved Domain Search Service (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Structure/cdd/ wrpsb.cgi). The first conserved motif (AGXETS) is located in helix I, forming a binding pocket for an oxygen molecule, and is required for catalytic activity (Werck-Reichhart and Feyereisen, 2000; Zhou et al., 2016). The second consensus sequence (EXXR) in helix K is a key motif to stabilize the core structure (Werck-Reichhart and Feyereisen, 2000). The remaining heme domain (FXXGXRXCXG), the most characteristic feature of P450, is responsible for the enzyme to bind heme (Werck-Reichhart and Feyereisen, 2000; Zhou et al., 2016) (Figure S9). 

By aligning the amino acid sequences of Cluster-23084.32652 with the reported CPRs from other plants, eight conserved domains, including a membrane- anchor domain, a P450-binding domain, two FMN-binding domains, two FAD-binding domains and two NADPH-binding domains, were identified (Figure S10). The membrane-anchor region, which is located at the N-terminal, is required for membrane anchoring (Jensen and Moller, 2010). The FMN-, FAD- and NADPH-inding domains interact with coenzymes FMN, FAD and NADPH, respectively, and are responsible for electron transfer. The P450-binding domain is involved in binding to CYP450 (Figure S10). 

# Phylogenetic analysis of candidate genes

To explore the evolutionary origins and the functional roles of Cluster-40111.0, Cluster-23084.50752 and Cluster-23084.32652, neighbour-joining phylogenetic trees based on multiple sequence alignment were constructed (Figure 2D,F). The phylogenetic tree of Cluster-40111.0, Cluster-23084.50752 with multiple CYP450s from other species was constructed using a neighbour-joining procedure (Table S3). Cluster-40111.0 clustered with CYP450s belonging to the CYP90B subfamily and was most closely related to DzCYP90B71 with $7 9 . 6 2 \%$ identity, which catalyses 22(R)-hydroxylation of CHR in the early steps of diosgenin biosynthesis (Zhou et al., 2022). Hence, Cluster-40111.0 was deduced to encode a CYP450 

displaying CHR C-22 hydroxylase activity (Figure 2F). Cluster-23084.50752 was grouped into the same cluster as CYP450s belonging to the CYP90G subfamily and was most similar to DzCYP90G6 $( 6 8 . 6 9 \%$ identity), a CHR hydroxylase from Dioscorea zingiberensis (Zhou et al., 2022). The two CYP450s were submitted to the nomenclature committee and designated OsCYP90B94 (Cluster-40111.0) and OsCYP90G11 (Cluster-23084.50752), respectively. 

Phylogenetic analysis of Cluster-23084.32652 and other plant CPRs was performed (Table S4). CPRs are divided into two distinct classes, Class I and Class II (Ro et al., 2002). Cluster-23084.32652 is grouped into Class II CPRs, in which a longer N-terminal sequence was present to the hydrophobic region. Hence, the CPR encoded by Cluster-23084.32652 was thus named tentatively as OsCPR2 (Figure 2D). 

# OsCYP90B94 catalyses cholesterol to 22 (R)-hydroxycholesterol

Genes OsCYP90B94 and OsCPR2 were isolated from O. saundersiae by RT-PCR using gene-specific primers (Tables S5 and S6). After sequence verification, the catalytic activity of OsCYP90B94 was determined in CHR-producing yeast strain RH6829 (Souza et al., 2011), yeast microsomes and Nicotiana benthamiana, respectively. 

First, genes OsCYP90B94 and OsCPR2 were cloned into pESC-Leu vector and pESC-Ura vectors, respectively (Table S7). The resulting plasmids pESC-Leu-OsCYP90B94 and pESC-Ura-OsCPR2 were then co-introduced into the CHR-producing yeast strain RH6829 for functional identification. After galactose induction, the strain co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCPR2 yielded an obvious peak (compound 1, m/z 384 [M-H O]) with an Rt of 16.5 min and a trace peak 2 with an Rt of 18.4 min besides the substrate CHR, as illustrated by gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC–MS) analysis (Figure 3A). # OsCPR2 is a NADPH-cytochrome P450 oxidoreductase

As indicated above, galactose-induced co-expression of OsCYP90B94 and OsCPR2 could convert CHR to form 22R-OHCHR (Figure 3). On the contrary, the engineered RH6829 containing both OsCYP90B94 and OsCPR2 without galactose induction could not convert CHR to form 22R-OHCHR (Figure 3D). In addition, when OsCYP90B94 was expressed alone in yeast under galactose induction, there were no additional peaks, as illustrated in GC–MS analysis, suggesting that no yeast redox partners interacted with OsCYP90B94 (Figure 3D). These results collectively indicate that OsCPR2 was a redox partner and required for OsCYP90B94 activity (Figure 3D).

# OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 are responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis

Although the in vitro activities of OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 have been identified, it remains unclear whether these two CYP450s were involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis. Hence, the expression of these two P450 genes in various tissues was explored by reverse transcription-quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR). As indicated in Figure 2A-b,A-c, no detectable expression of these two P450 genes was observed in the scape without the apparent OSW-1 accumulation. No observable expression of these two P450 genes in the scape was also confirmed by semi-quantitative RT-PCR (sqRT-PCR) (Figure S46). On the contrary, the apparent expressions of these two CYP450 genes were observed in other tissues (roots, bulbs, leaves and flowers) that accumulated apparent OSW-1. A reasonable explanation that OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 are responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis is thus proposed. Furthermore, the two P450s were required for OSW-1 biosynthesis. Hence, the expression silence of these two P450s was consistent with the absence of OSW-1 in the scape. 

On the contrary, the expression of these two P450 genes displayed differential expression profiles in all five tissues. The expression of OsCYP90B94 in different tissues was ranked as follows: root $>$ flower $>$ bulb $>$ leaf $>$ scape (Figure 2A-b). Unlike the expression of OsCYP90B94, the highest expression of OsCYP90G11 was detected in the flowers followed by roots, bulbs, leaves and scapes (Figure 2A-c). Furthermore, the expression profiles of these two P450 genes were not positively correlated with OSW-1 contents in various tissues. Cumulatively, these data indicate that other genes are involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis besides OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11. 

Unlike OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11, OsCPR2 was expressed in all tissues including the scape (Figure 2A-d), indicating OsCPR2 is involved in multiple pathways besides OSW-1 biosynthesis. Also, this finding suggested that OsCPR2 can interact with multiple CYP450s besides OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11, consistent with the previous notion that a single P450 reductase can interact with different P450s (Etzerodt et al., 2017; Ro et al., 2002).

# Discussion

The presence of OSW-1 in O. saundersiae has been confirmed for over 30 years (Kubo et al., 1992). The biosynthetic pathway of OSW-1, however, has never been clarified. OSW-1 is one of the cholestane glycosides. Although CHR is a precursor of spirostanoltype steroids (Cheng et al., 2021; Christ et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022) and steroidal alkaloids (Augustin et al., 2015; Umemoto et al., 2016), it is unclear whether CHR is the precursor of cholestane glycosides. In this study, we first demonstrated convincingly that CHR is a precursor of OSW-1 using D7-CHR (Figure 1). This result suggests that only CYP450s capable of acting on CHR are candidate enzymes involved in the first oxidation step of OSW-1 biosynthesis. This finding excluded most CYP450s, thereby increasing the chances of identifying CYP450s responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis. 

The P450 monooxygenases responsible for the first oxidation step control the access of CHR into the biosynthesis pathway of CHR-derived steroids, such as spirostanol-type steroids (Cheng et al., 2021; Christ et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022), steroidal alkaloids (Augustin et al., 2015; Umemoto et al., 2016) and OSW-1. As such, the inactivation of these CYP450s usually results in little accumulation of steroids in plants (Umemoto et al., 2016). In this regard, the inactivation of CYP450s responsible for the early oxidation steps in OSW-1 biosynthesis is expected to yield little or no OSW-1 in O. saundersiae. We inferred that the P450s responsible for the first oxidation step of OSW-1 biosynthesis may be expressed at very low levels in plant tissues with low or no OSW-1. Therefore, we tried to screen plant tissues with little or no OSW-1 content in O. saundersiae. To our delight, the scape was demonstrated to be the plant tissue containing a trace amount OSW-1 (Figure 2A). This finding indicated that CYP450s responsible for OSW-1 biosynthesis possibly displayed little or no expression in scape. Meanwhile, we observed a high OSW-1 content in bulb, indicating that CYP450 genes displayed significant expression in this tissue. P450s showing differential expression in bulbs and scapes were suggested to be involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis. To further narrow down the range of positive CYP450s, we used comparative transcriptomics techniques to analyse CYP450s displaying differential expression between scapes and bulbs. As such, only CHR hydroxylases with tissuespecific expression can be the candidates for the first hydroxylation step in OSW-1 biosynthesis. Two P450s, OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11, were thus screened successfully. Their involvement in OSW-1 biosynthesis was subsequently demonstrated by RTqPCR and sqRT-PCR (Figure 2). 

Cholesterol 22-hydroxylation is known as a committed step in the biosynthesis of CHR derived steroids such as spirostanol 

saponins (Zhou et al., 2022), steroid alkaloids (Augustin et al., 2015; Umemoto et al., 2016) and brassinosteroids (Christ et al., 2019; Fujita et al., 2006). The stereospecificity of CHR 22-hydroxylases varies among CHR-derived compounds. 22Rspecific CHR 22-hydroxylases such as DzCYP90B71 (Zhou et al., 2022), PpCYP90B27 (Yin et al., 2018), VcCYP90B27V1 and VcCYP90B27V2 (Augustin et al., 2015), mainly initiate the pathway from CHR to spirostanol saponins (Zhou et al., 2022) and steroid alkaloids (Augustin et al., 2015; Umemoto et al., 2016). BR-biosynthetic CHR 22-hydroxylases such as AtCYP90B1 (Fujita et al., 2006) and TfCYP90B51 (Christ et al., 2019), are capable of oxidizing CHR to form both 22R- and 22S-OHCHR. OsCYP90B94 was grouped into the same clade as DzCYP90B71, PpCYP90B27, VcCYP90B27V1, and VcCYP90B27V2, suggesting OsCYP90B94 is a 22R-specific CHR 22-hydroxylase (Figure 2). In vitro experiments had confirmed this speculation (Figure 3). This indicates that OsCYP90B94 is not a BR-biosynthetic initiator. These evidences, together with in vivro and in vivo experimental results (Figures 2 and 3), collectively support the notion that OsCYP90B94 participates in OSW-1 biosynthesis. After a comprehensive investigation, the conversion from CHR to $1 6 \beta$ -hydroxy-22-oxocholesterol (12) was demonstrated to occur in the biosynthesis of OSW-1. OsCYP90B94 initiates this conversion, catalysing CHR to 22R-OHCHR (Figures 3 and 7). Subsequently, 22R-OHCHR was converted into 16b,22R-diOHCHR, and then into $1 6 \beta$ -hydroxy-22-oxo-cholesterol (12) through two consecutive oxidations catalysed by OsCYP90G11 (Figures 3, 5 and 7). The conversion pathway from CHR to $1 6 \beta$ -hydroxy-22-oxocholesterol (12) was also observed in the biosynthesis of diosgenin distributed in D. zingiberensis, Paris polyphylla, and Trigonella foenum–graecum (Christ et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022). In D. zingiberensis, the conversion from CHR to $1 6 \beta \cdot$ - hydroxy-22-oxo-cholesterol (12) is accomplished by two P450s, DzCYP90B71 and DzCYP90G6 (Zhou et al., 2022). Unlike the conversions catalysed by two CYP450s in O. saundersiae and D. zingiberensis, this conversion from CHR to 16b-hydroxy-22- oxo-cholesterol (12) in P. polyphylla and T. foenum–graecum is achieved by a single CYP450, PpCYP90G4 (P. polyphylla) or TfCYP90B50 (T. foenum–graecum). Notably, OsCYP90B94 and DzCYP90B71 are 22R-specific CHR 22-hydroxylases that catalyse the conversion of CHR to 22R-OHCHR. In addition to CHR, however, OsCYP90B94 could react with $7 \beta$ -OHCHR, displaying substrate promiscuity (Table S8). 

OsCYP90G11 catalyses two consecutive oxidations. Although being clustered more closely with P450s belonging to the CYP90G family, such as VcCYP90G1v3, DzCYP90G6 and PpCYP90G4, OsCYP90G11 shows different activities from these CYP90G proteins (Figure 2F). VcCYP90G1v3 from Veratrum californicum was a steroid C-22 hydroxylase, oxidizing an existing hydroxyl group at position 22 of 22R-OHCHR, 22,26- dihydroxycholesterol and 22-hydroxy-26-aminocholesterol, respectively (Augustin et al., 2015). DzCYP90G6 from D. zingiberensis was a C16-hydroxylase, catalysing a C16-hydroxylation of 22R-OHCHR (Zhou et al., 2022). PpCYP90G4 from P. polyphylla could convert CHR to 16b,22S-diOHCHR (Christ et al., 2019). 

Unlike the three homologues, OsCYP90G11 is more like a collection of these three proteins, possessing the activities of steroid C-22 oxidase, C16-hydroxylase, and $1 6 \beta , 2 2 S $ -hydroxylase. OsCYP90G11 can oxidize an existing hydroxyl group at position 22 of 16b,22R-diOHCHR to form $1 6 \beta$ -hydroxy-22-oxo-cholesterol 

(12), displaying steroid C-22 oxidase activity (Figures 6C and 6D). OsCYP90G11 also has C16-hydroxylase activity, catalysing 22R-OHCHR to form 16b,22R-diOHCHR (5) (Figures 6A and 6B). Furthermore, OsCYP90G11 was demonstrated to be a 16b,22Shydroxylase, hydroxylating CHR to yield 16b,22S-diOHCHR (Figure 4). Taken together, OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 displayed novel properties significantly different from their respective homologues. 

OsCYP90G11 and OsCYP90B94 are involved in the early hydroxylations in OSW-1 biosynthesis, controlling the access of metabolic flux into OSW-1 biosynthesis. Hence, the inactivation of these two P450s usually results in no accumulation of OSW-1. No expression of these two P450 genes and no OSW-1 accumulation was detected in the scape, suggesting that the absence of OSW-1 was possibly due to the inactivation of these two P450s in scape (Figure 2A). This fact demonstrated the involvement of these two P450s in OSW-1 biosynthesis. 

OsCYP90G11 and OsCYP90B94 are promiscuous P450s, recognizing more than one substrate. The substrate promiscuity of these two P450s suggested their involvement in more than one biosynthetic pathway besides OSW-1 biosynthesis. Hence, the expression of OsCYP90G11 and OsCYP90B94 is not proportional to the accumulation of OSW-1 in roots, bulbs, leaves and flowers (Figure 2A). Consequently, this existing evidence confirmed the involvement of OsCYP90G11 and OsCYP90B94 in OSW-1 biosynthesis. 

In addition to OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11, a third hydroxylase, steroid $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylase, is involved in OSW-1 biosynthesis. We examined the possible $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylases in RH6829 co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11. Unfortunately, no $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylases were determined. $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylase catalysed the substrate 12 to form polyOA. Substrate 12 was unstable and was easily converted into compounds 7, 8 and 11, resulting in no accumulation of substrate 12 in the yeast (Figure 5A). The insufficiency of the substrate 12 was assumed to be one of the reasons why $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylase had not been identified. Later, we used N. benthamiana co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 to examine $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylases. Although N. benthamiana co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 could yield substrate 12, the yield of substrate 12 was trace amount and the enzymatically synthesized polyOA catalysed by a $1 7 \alpha$ -hydroxylase might be neglected. In summary, the production of the substrate 12 in N. benthamiana co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 should be boosted to facilitate the functional identification of $1 7 \alpha \cdot$ -hydroxylases. Additionally, 17-hydroxylation of the substrate 12 may be catalysed by oxygenases other than CYP450s. Therefore, additional oxygenases should be examined in N. benthamiana co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11. 

# Methods

# Chemicals, plant material and strain

CHR, 22(S/R)-OHCHR and other OHCHRs were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. D7-CHR(side chain-labelled;25,26,26,26,27,27,27- D7) was purchased from Aladdin Scientific (Shanghai, China). OSW-1 standard was obtained from Cayman Chemical (Michigan). O. saundersiae Baker plant material was obtained from Yunnan province, China. The stable CHR-producing yeast RH6829 was a gift from Prof. Howard Riezman of the Department of Biochemistry at the University of Geneva (Souza et al., 2011). WAT11, which 

does not produce CHR, is a laboratory Saccharomyces cerevisiae strain (Alagoz et al., 2022). 

# Isolation and analysis of OSW-1

OSW-1 isolation was carried out as follows. Briefly, the dried powder of the bulbs of O. saundersiae was extracted ultrasonically with methanol three times. The combined extracts were concentrated under reduced pressure to dryness. The resultant residue was suspended in ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and partitioned successively with petroleum ether and methylene chloride three times, respectively. The combined methylene chloride fractions were evaporated to dryness under reduced pressure, and the residue was re-dissolved with HPLC grade methanol. The methanol solution was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filter and analysed on a 1200 HPLC system coupled with a C-18 column $( 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , $4 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m m } \times 1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , ZORBAX SB-C18). The elution was carried out using phase A (water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid) and phase B (methanol) with the following gradient program: $6 0 \% - 9 0 \%$ B at 0–50 min, $9 0 { - } 1 0 0 \%$ B at 50–60 min, $100 \%$ B at 60–70 min. The flow rate, the injection volume and the detection wavelength were set to 1 mL/min, $6 0 ~ \mu \ L$ and $2 5 4 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , respectively. The pure OSW-1 was collected by semi-preparative HPLC (isocratic solvent, $A C N - H _ { 2 } O$ , $8 7 \%$ ) for further structure identification. NMR analyses of OSW-1 were performed on a Bruker AVANCE III HD 600 MHz spectrometer. 

# Stable-isotope labeling experiments

Seeds of O. saundersiae were successively aseptically treated with sterile ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ for 30 min, $7 5 \%$ ethanol for 2 min, $3 \%$ NaClO for 10 min and sterile ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ for 5 min, and then transferred to sterile solid MS medium containing $3 \%$ sucrose for one month to make them grow to seedlings. Seedlings were transferred to 1/2 MS solid medium supplemented with a final concentration of $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g / L }$ IBA to grow for an additional 15 days. After two successive subcultures, the seedlings were transferred to 1/2 MS liquid medium and maintained statically for 15 days. Subsequently, the seedlings were transferred to $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ fresh 1/2 MS liquid medium supplemented with D7-CHR solution (3.0 mg, dissolved in acetone $( 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } )$ -Tween 80 $( 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } ) .$ ) and continued to grow for 3 weeks before being harvested. The seedlings were grown at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in a photoperiodic cycle of 16-h light/8-h darkness. Plants treated with the acetone $( 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } )$ -Tween 80 $( 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } )$ solvent mixture were used as the control. 

The harvested seedlings of O. saundersiae were lyophilized and ground to powder for OSW-1 analysis as detailed above. 

# Transcriptome and expression analysis

Fresh tissues (bulbs and scapes) were sent to Novogene (Beijing, China) for transcriptome analysis according to the service provider’s standard protocol. 

Total RNA was extracted from various tissues (roots, bulbs, leaves, flowers and scapes) using an Ultrapure RNA Kit (CWBIO, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The extracted RNA was used as a template for CYP450 cDNA isolation using the gene-specific primers by RT-PCR (Table S5). Also, the extracted RNA was employed for RT-qPCR, which was performed with UltraSYBR Mixture (CWBIO, Beijing, China) using LightCycler 480 (Roche, Mannheim, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Data analysis of relative gene expression level was accomplished using comparative Ct $( 2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } } )$ method. Additionally, the extracted RNA was used for sqRT-PCR analysis. The Actin2 gene from O. saundersiae was 

used as the internal standard in both RT-qPCR and sqRT-PCR analysis. 

# Bioinformatic analysis

Amino acid sequences of OsCYP90B94, OsCYP90G11 and OsCPR2 were identified by the online tool Open Reading Frame Finder (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gorf/orFigurecgi). Prot-Param was used to determine physical and chemical parameters of OsCYP90B94, OsCYP90G11 and OsCPR2, including amino acid length, molecular weight, theoretical isoelectric point, total number of negatively charged residues $( \mathsf { A s p } + \mathsf { G l u } )$ , total number of positively charged residues (Arg + Lys), estimated half-life, instability index, aliphatic index and grand average of hydropathicity (http://web.expasy.org/protparam/). SignalP-4.1 was used to predict the presence and location of signal peptide cleavage sites (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/SignalP-4.1/). TMHMM Server v. 2.0 was used to predict transmembrane helices in proteins (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/TMHMM/). Cell-PLoc 2.0 was used to predict subcellular localization (http://www.csbio.sjtu.edu.cn/bioinf/Cell-PLoc-2/). Conserved domain was analysed and predicted using CD-Search (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/cdd/?term=). Phylogenetic analysis was performed using MEGA X software via the Neighbour-Joining statistical method with 1000 bootstrap replications. Multiple alignments of protein sequences were performed by Clustal W. The amino acid sequences of CYP450s from other plants were downloaded from the NCBI database. The P450 sequences were submitted to the P450 nomenclature committee for the assignment of a P450 family name (https://drnelson.uthsc. edu/nomenclature/). 

# Construction of the expression plasmids

The full-length cDNA fragment of the candidate CYP450 genes was amplified and then inserted into the cloning vector pEASY $\textcircled{1}$ - Blunt, thereby forming recombinant pEASY $\textcircled{1}$ -Blunt-derived plasmids. Using these recombinant pEASY $\textcircled{1}$ -Blunt-derived vectors as the templates, the ORF of 10 candidate P450s and OsCPR2 genes was amplified by PCR and then ligated with yeast expression plasmids using the Seamless Assembly Cloning Kit (CloneSmarter Tech) according to the protocol provided by the manufacturer. The resulting constructs were verified by direct sequencing. The constructed plasmids were summarized in Table S7. The primers used for vector construction in this study were listed in Table S6. 

# Functional assays in yeast

The recombinant yeast plasmids were introduced into the CHRproducing yeast strain RH6829 via electroporation in a precooled $2 \mathsf { m m }$ Gene Pulser cuvette (Bio-Rad, Hurcules) at $1 . 5 \mathrm { ~ k V } .$ , and transformants were screened on appropriate SD drop-out media at $3 0 \textdegree$ for 4 days. The positive colonies were cultured in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of appropriate SD drop-out media with $2 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / \mathsf { L }$ glucose at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The yeast cells were collected by centrifugation at 8000 rpm for 5 min, washed twice with ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and then resuspended in $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of YPL medium $1 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ yeast extract, $2 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ peptone, $2 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / \mathsf { L }$ galactose, $2 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ raffinose) to grow for $7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The fermentation broth was centrifuged at 8000 rpm for 10 min to harvest the cells, which were subsequently extracted with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of acetone (TGREAG, Beijing, China) and sonicated at $4 0 0 ~ \mathsf { H z }$ for $4 \textrm { h }$ . The extracts were dried at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and then dissolved in chromatography-grade acetone, which was then filtered with a $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filter. The metabolites were analysed by 

GC–MS using the Shimadzu TQ8050 with triple-quadrupole mass spectrometer. The separation of metabolites was achieved with an injector temperature of $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and the carrier helium gas at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. The temperature program was as follows: starting at $2 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min, followed by heating the column to $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ at $1 0 \ { ^ \circ } C / { \mathsf { m i n } }$ and holding at $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 25 min. 

Also, the extracted metabolites were analysed by reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) at an Agilent 1260 HPLC system. All samples were detected using a C-18 column $( 4 . 6 ~ \mathsf { m m } ~ 1 . \mathsf { D } ~ \times ~ 2 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ , SilGreen, Beijing, China) at a wavelength of $2 0 3 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , and the column temperature was kept at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . 

# Purification and NMR analysis of compounds 1–11

The cells from the 20-L culture of RH6829 co-expressing OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 were harvested in glass bottles by centrifugation at 8000 rpm for 10 min. Ultrasound-assisted extraction of the harvested cells was performed in acetone for $4 \textrm { h }$ . The acetone extract was evaporated under reduced pressure to yield a dark brown residue, which was separated further on silica gel using gradient elution with n-hexane–acetone (50:1, 20:1, 10:1, 5:1, 2:1, 1:1 and 0:100) to yield A-G fractions, or with petroleum ether–acetone (20:1, 10:1, 5:1, 2:1) to yield I-IV fractions. Fraction D included compounds 1, 3 and 5. Water and methanol were used as solvent A and B, respectively. The gradient program comprised a linear gradient from $90 \%$ to $100 \%$ B (0–30 min), and the flow rate of the mobile phase increased from 0.8 to 1 mL/min, affording compound 1. Compounds 3 and 5 were purified by HPLC using $C H _ { 3 } C N - H _ { 2 } O$ (88:12) as the mobile phase. Compounds 2 and 4 were purified from fraction E by HPLC $( C H _ { 3 } C N - H _ { 2 } O$ , 80:20). Fraction C contained compounds 6–10. Compound 6 was purified using methanol– $\mathsf { \Pi } \cdot \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O }$ (90:10). Data from 1D NMR (1 HNMR,13CNMR) and 2D NMR (HSQC, ${ } ^ { 1 } { \mathsf { H } } { - } ^ { 1 } { \mathsf { H } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { O } } { \mathsf { S } } { \mathsf { Y } }$ , HMBC, NOESY) were recorded on a Bruker AVANCE NEO 700 MHz (2, 5 and 6) or Bruker AVANCE III-400 system (1, 3, 4) and used to assign the chemical shifts of these compounds. It was difficult to isolate the pure compounds 7–10 due to their instability. The identification of compounds 7 and 8 was carried out by ${ \mathsf { L } } { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { - M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ as described previously (Zhou et al., 2022). Compounds 9 and 10 were identified by high resolution mass spectrometry. 

Compound 11 was purified from fraction IV with semipreparative HPLC. Water and acetonitrile were used as solvent A and B, respectively. The gradient program comprised a linear gradient from $80 \%$ to $90 \%$ B (0–50 min). The sample was prepared with deuterated chloroform $( \mathsf { C D C l } _ { 3 } )$ for 700 MHz NMR analysis. 

# Microsome assays

Yeast microsomes were prepared as previously described with minor alterations (Liu et al., 2003). Yeast cells were collected by centrifugation at 4000 rpm for 5 min and broken by highpressure cell crusher with 1000 bar in $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ ice-cold potassium phosphate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H 8 . 0 } \right)$ containing 1 M sorbitol and $1 4 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } \parallel$ - mercaptoethanol. The resulting homogenate was centrifuged at 10000 g at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $1 0 \ \mathsf { m i n }$ , and the resulting supernatant was then ultracentrifuged at $1 4 0 0 0 0 \ 9 $ for 90 min at $4 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The cytosolic fraction was discarded, and the resulting pellet was washed gently with 0.1 M potassium phosphate ice-cold buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H 8 . 0 } \right)$ containing 0.4 M sucrose and $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ glutathione. After washing, the pellet with microsomal membranes was resuspended in $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ } \mathsf { M }$ ice-cold potassium phosphate buffer (pH 

8.0) containing $0 . 4 ~ \mathsf { M }$ sucrose, $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ glutathione and $10 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, and then kept frozen at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The in vitro enzymatic reactions were carried out in $5 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ M ~ }$ potassium phosphate buffer $( \mathsf { p H } 8 . 0 )$ containing 2 mg microsome protein, 5 mM NADPH, $5 \mathrm { \ m M \ N q C l _ { 2 } }$ and $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ CHR as a supplementary substrate. The reaction was incubated at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 0, 1, 2 and 4 h, respectively. The reaction mixtures were extracted three times with an equal volume of ethyl acetate. The resultant extracts were dried at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and dissolved with $2 5 0 ~ \mu \iota$ chromatographic acetone, and then detected by GC–MS according to the above method. 

# Transient expression in N.benthamiana

Genes OsCYP90B94 and OsCYP90G11 were amplified from the pESC-LEU-OsCYP90B94 and pESC-LEU-OsCYP90G11, respectively. Each gene was inserted into the pEAQ-HT vector between Xho I and Sal I sites. The expression constructs were introduced into competent Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 (Sangon Biotech, China). The positive transformant was cultured in 10 mL LB medium containing the appropriate antibiotics at 28 $^ \circ \mathsf C$ overnight with continual shaking at 200 rpm. An overnight culture $( 0 . 3 ~ \mathsf { m L } )$ was transferred into $3 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of the fresh medium for an additional overnight culture. Freshly grown A. tumefaciens cells were pelleted and then re-suspended in infiltration buffer $1 0 \ \mathrm { m M } \ \mathrm { M g } \mathsf { C l } _ { 2 }$ , 10 mM MES, pH 5.6, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone) to adjust ${ \mathsf { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ to 0.6. Five-week-old N. benthamiana leaves were infiltrated on the abaxial side with A. tumefaciens harbouring target constructs using a 1 mL syringe without needles. CHR (dissolved in trace acetone $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } )$ was dispersed in ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and infiltrated into the abaxial airspaces of the leaves at 3 days postinfiltration. The leaves of three plants were harvested for product analysis at 7 days post-infiltration. 

# Metabolite extraction of N. benthamiana leaves

The permeated leaves were freeze-dried and ground into a powder. The powder samples $( 1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g } )$ were extracted with 10 mL trichloromethane for 1 h in an ultrasound-assisted fashion. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 5 min, the organic phase was transferred to a fresh tube. The resultant extracts were evaporated to dryness. To remove macromolecules such as sugar and chlorophyll from the extracted sample, saponification was performed by adding saponification reagent (absolute ethyl alcohol:water: $K O H = 9 \mathsf { m L } \colon 1 \mathsf { m L } \colon 1 \mathrm { ~ g } )$ ) at $7 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 1 h. The obtained saponified solution was dried by rotary evaporation. Subsequently, 5 mL water and 5 mL trichloromethane were added to re-suspend the saponified sample. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 5 min, the organic phase was collected and then evaporated with a rotary evaporator. The gathered sample was derivatized with $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ trichloromethane and 50 lL N,O-Bis (trimethylsilyl)trifluoroacetamide at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ before GC–MS analysis. The analysis was performed using an ISQ 7610 Single quadrupole GC–MS system coupled with a TG-5SILMS capillary column $( 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ film thickness).
