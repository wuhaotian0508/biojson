# Summary

The endeavour to elevate the nutritional value of oat (Avena sativa) by altering the oil composition and content positions it as an optimal crop for fostering human health and animal feed. However, optimization of oil traits on oat through conventional breeding is challenging due to its quantitative nature and complexity of the oat genome. We introduced two constructs containing three key genes integral to lipid biosynthesis and/or regulatory pathways from Arabidopsis (AtWRI1 and AtDGAT1) and Sesame (SiOLEOSIN) into the oat cultivar ‘Park’ to modify the fatty acid composition. Four homozygous transgenic lines were generated with a transformation frequency of $7 \%$ . The expression of these introduced genes initiated a comprehensive transcriptional reprogramming in oat grains and leaves. Notably, endogenous DGAT, WRI1 and OLEOSIN genes experienced upregulation, while genes associated with fatty acid biosynthesis, such as KASII, SACPD and FAD2, displayed antagonistic expression patterns between oat grains and leaves. Transcriptomic analyses highlighted significant differential gene expression, particularly enriched in lipid metabolism. Comparing the transgenic oat plants with the wild type, we observed a remarkable increase of up to $34 \%$ in oleic acid content in oat grains. Furthermore, there were marked improvements in the total oil content in oat leaves, as well as primary metabolites changes in both oat grains and leaves, while maintaining homeostasis in the transgenic oat plants. These findings underscore the effectiveness of genetic engineering in manipulating oat oil composition and content, offering promising implications for human consumption and animal feeding through oat crop improvement programmes. 

# Introduction

As the rapid human population growth dictates increased food demand, the need to improve the fatty acid profile of crops became urgent to improve the nutritional quality of food for healthier lives (Godfray et al., 2010). Additionally, the use of high-lipid crops in livestock feed leads to an increase in carcass fat and provides a denser source of energy (Wiseman and Agunbiade, 1998). In the human diet, oils and fats provide a rich source of calories and bioactive lipid nutrients. They serve an important role in the frying and cooking industry. Worldwide demand for vegetable oil is projected to increase exponentially in the coming years due to increasing food, fuel and industrial consumption. Global plant oil demand for food use is expected to increase by more than $1 6 \%$ in the next 5–10 years (OECD/FAO, 2017). 

Lipids possess a higher proportion of C–H bonds as compared to C–O bonds in carbohydrates and as a result, store more energy per unit of mass. Oil content in seeds is an important quality trait in plant breeding. High biomass crops engineered for improved lipid content in vegetative tissues have been considered a valuable source of energy-dense lipids (Troncoso-Ponce et al., 2013). Moreover, lipid-rich plants are useful as biofuels, since these 

macromolecules are hydrophobic and easier to extract than cellulosic-derived fuels (Chapman et al., 2013). Therefore, improving the oil content and profile of plant tissues is of greater interest and plant-derived oils are one of the fastest growing agricultural commodities. However, it remains a challenge to improve oil content and profile in agronomically important crops (Vanhercke et al., 2019). 

Cultivated hexaploid oat (Avena sativa L.) is an emerging cereal crop with multiple uses for animal feeding and human food consumption. Its unique nutritional profile is attributed to a high content of soluble dietary fibre, protein, lipids, vitamins and minerals (Gulvady et al., 2013). Particularly, oat seed oil content is the highest (up to $1 8 \%$ ) among cereal grains (Banas et al., 2007). To further promote oats as a healthy food source, current efforts are focused on optimizing the oil composition in the oat grain. The high monosaturated fatty acid in oat oil is especially desirable with its improved oxidative stability and shelf life to avoid hydrogenation, a process to introduce trans fats that are associated with a number of health problems in humans (Ascherio and Willett, 1997; Welch, 2011). The aerial portion of oat plants is also considered as one of the important sources for silage (Cuddeford, 1995). Increasing the oil content in oat vegetative tissues improves the energy density of biomass and 

indirectly leads to a reduction of carbon emission. Previously, the improvement of oat nutritional quality traits lagged behind the other cereal crops due to its genetic complexity. However, the availability of a recently sequenced oat genome provided more powerful tools to develop new oat cultivars with improved oil traits (Kamal et al., 2022). 

Conventional breeding techniques are cumbersome and inefficient in the improvement of oat traits (Babiker et al., 2015; Keneni et al., 2012; Ullstrup, 1972). As an example, it took over nine breeding cycles to achieve $1 8 \%$ increase in groat-oil content from 98.2 to $1 5 8 . 4 \ : 2 \ : \ : 9 / 1 \ : 9$ in oat. Consequently, despite the increased oil content, these oat lines were more susceptible to disease and incurred yield loss (Frey and Holland, 1999). Meanwhile, the use of genetic engineering has been proven in a variety of species to increase oil content in multiple plant tissues. The most recent approach involved an integration of fatty acid synthesis (‘Push’), triacylglycerol (TAG) assembly (‘Pull’) and lipid turnover (‘Protect’) steps, in which overexpression of lipid biosynthesis-related genes was widely tested to increase the oil content in various plant tissues (Arias et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2017; Vanhercke et al., 2014). The WRINKLED1 (WRI1) gene in Arabidopsis, an AP2/EREBP transcription factor, has been reported to be positively involved in regulating fatty acid synthesis (Cernac and Benning, 2004). The ectopic expression of BdWRI1 proves to be useful in TAG accumulation but also causes a cell death phenotype in vegetative tissues (Focks and Benning, 1998; Yang et al., 2015). Similarly, it has been shown the diacylglycerol acyltransferase (DGAT) gene in Arabidopsis controls the final rate-limiting step in TAG biosynthesis. When DGAT is upregulated, buildup of TAGs occurred, increasing lipid proportion by up to $70 \%$ in developing seed (Jako et al., 2001; Lung and Weselake, 2006). Correspondingly, OLEOSIN is an oil body protein in Arabidopsis and contributes to the protection of TAGs in lipid droplets (Huang, 1992). The upregulation of OLEOSIN resulted in the protecting and storing of the lipids (Wahlroos et al., 2003). To the best of our knowledge, the utilization of these three genes (DGAT, WRI1 and OLEOSIN ) in the improvement of oil traits in oat has not been reported in the literature. 

Overexpression of DGAT, WRI1 and OLEOSIN in a combination of two or three genes led to a substantial increase in oil content in plant vegetative tissue. In Arabidopsis, co-expression of OLEOSIN and DGAT1 resulted in up to a 3-fold increase in leaf fatty acid content. These two genes improved the vegetative tissue’s ability to assimilate carbon dioxide and increased leaf biomass by $50 \%$ (Winichayakul et al., 2013). In tobacco leaves, the synergistic expression of WRI1 and DGAT1 led to an increase of 100-fold in TAG content. In terms of the lipid profile, the amount of polyunsaturated lipids dropped and, in turn, the amount of monounsaturated lipids increased (Vanhercke et al., 2013). Vanhercke et al. (2018) reported that a combination of WRI1, DGAT2 and OLEOSIN increased TAG content by 100-fold in sorghum leaves. By either suppressing the SDP1 lipase or overexpressing AtLEC2 in tobacco plant co-expressing three transgenes, the TAG content was further elevated in leaf tissues (Vanhercke et al., 2017). The endogenous lipid metabolic network was found to be adapted to tobacco leaf oil engineering and the introduction of LEC2 enhanced TAG stability to unleash the futile cycles in oil-accumulating leaves (Johnson et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2020). However, few studies investigated the effect of genetic engineering on the transcriptome and metabolism of different tissues from plants. 

Previously, it has been shown that the oat spring cultivar ‘Park’ can be successfully transformed via the biolistic method. The use of calli from the embryo of mature seeds has a $100 \%$ regeneration rate (Cho et al., 1999). Here, we successfully introduced the transgenes (DGAT1, WRI1 and OLEOSIN ) from two different constructs into the genome of oat ‘Park’ through a biolistic delivery system and obtained four homozygous oat transgenic lines. Using transcriptomic and metabolic analyses, we revealed that transgene-mediated transcriptional reprogramming led to primary metabolic changes in both mature oat seeds and leaves. Our study demonstrated not only the feasibility of metabolic engineering in improving oat oil traits, but also a valuable oat source as a healthy food and animal feed.

# Generation of transgenic oat plants co-expressing AtDGAT1, AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN genes

To overexpress three exogenous genes in oat, we used two expression constructs, pJP3679 and pJP3502 to perform oat transformation. The T-DNA of the binary plasmid construct pJP3679 is composed of Arabidopsis thaliana DGAT1 (AtDGAT1) gene under the control of the Pisum sativum vicilin promoter and two genes, A. thaliana WRI1 (AtWRI1) and Sesamum indicum OLEOSIN (SiOLEOSIN ), driven by the Phaseolus vulgaris phaseolin storage protein promoters, respectively (Figure 1a). Whereas, in the T-DNA region of the second plasmid pJP3502, the strong constitutive promoter CaMV-35S was chosen to express AtD-GAT1 gene and A. thaliana Rubisco small subunit (SSU) promoters were used for the expression of AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN genes (Figure 1a). Finally, PAT and NPTII were inserted as selectable marker genes for pJP3679 and pJP3502, respectively. 

A total of 140 and 175 calli derived from oat ‘Park’ seeds were used for biolistic transformation with pJP3679 and pJP3502, respectively (Figure 1b). Following transformation and antibiotic selection, we retained 30 regenerated lines in total. Among them, ten lines for pJP3679 and eight lines for pJP3502 were determined as positive transgenics by PCR analysis (Table S1). The presence of all three transgenes was confirmed using genespecific primers and then the selected T0 lines were advanced to quest for homozygosity. Homozygous lines were identified at either T2 or T3 generation, from which three of them, D21, D22 and D23, were transformed with pJP3679 and D35A with pJP3502 (Table S2). All subsequent experiments have been performed only on the plant materials derived from these homozygous transgenic lines. The transgene copy number ranged from 2 to 16, determined by ddPCR (Table S2). The expression levels of the three transgenes from all four homozygous lines were assessed by real-time reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) in two different tissues (Figure 2a). We observed the expressions of three transgenes from all four transgenic oat lines in both seeds and leaves (Figure 2a). However, the extent of their expression showed variation between pJP3679 (D23) and pJP3502 (D35A) transformed plants (Figure 2a). Overall, the expression of AtDGAT1, AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN in D35A is higher than that of pJP3679 transformed plants in either seeds or leaves. # Transgenic oat seeds presented improved fatty acid composition

To explore the effect of the expression of the three transgenes on the seed fatty acid composition, we examined the fatty acid profile of mature seeds harvested from four transgenic (D21, D22, D23 and D35A) and WT plants by gas chromatography (GC). Compared to WT, we identified substantial and consistent alterations to seed fatty acid composition among all four transgenic lines (Figure 6a). All four of them showed a significant increase in the content of oleic acid (C18:1) in the range of $3 0 . 1 2 \% - 3 4 . 3 3 \%$ compared to $2 7 . 9 7 \%$ in WT. The contents of two saturated fatty acids, palmitic acid (C16:0) and stearic acid (C18:0), as well as linoleic acid (C18:2) were lower than WT. However, no significant difference was detected in the $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic acid (ALA, C18:3) content between the transgenic lines and WT (Figure 6a). It is worth noting that the extent of increase in oleic acid content and decreases in palmitic and stearic acid content in D35A are slightly smaller than that in the other three transgenic lines. These results highlight an elevation in monounsaturated fatty acid content but a reduction in saturated and polyunsaturated fatty acid contents in mature seeds of the transgenic lines.

# Overexpression of the three transgenes leads to primary metabolite changes in oat seeds

We further investigated the effects of the three transgenes expression on the primary metabolism in the mature seeds. Seed protein, oil and starch contents were analysed for four transgenic lines and WT. The level of protein was significantly reduced in all transgenic lines compared to WT. The protein contents of four transgenic lines ranged between $1 7 . 5 7 \%$ and 

$2 0 . 1 2 \%$ whereas WT accumulated $2 2 . 6 0 \%$ (Figure 6b). The quantification of total seed oil content revealed no significant difference in total oil content between the four transgenic lines and WT. The starch content of D21 and D23 were not affected. D22 possessed comparatively higher starch levels than WT while only D35A presented considerably reduced levels of starch content. These data demonstrated that the seed composition was affected by the expression of AtDGAT1, AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN genes.

# Oil content increased but protein content decreased in transgenic oat leaves

To evaluate the effects of the transgenes on leaf composition, leaves were sampled at three different stages: vegetative (stage 2–3), boot (stage 4) and dough (stage 8) (Reeves and Sraon, 1976). The assessment of protein, oil and fatty acids contents was carried out in leaves collected from both transgenic and WT plants at three stages (Figures 7 and S2). At the vegetative stage, leaves from pJP3679 transformed plants, D21, D22 and D23, accumulated significantly higher levels of total oil content compared to WT. The significant increases in oil content were also observed in D21 leaves at the boot stage and D22 leaves at the dough stage (Figure 7). For pJP3502 transformed plants, D35A leaves reached the highest oil content $( 4 . 0 9 \% )$ at the boot stage. In contrast to oil content, protein content showed a substantial reduction in all four transgenic lines (Figure 7). Interestingly, the fatty acid profiles of boot leaves in all four transgenic lines remained similar and showed no significant difference compared to WT (Figure S2). 

# No morphological changes were observed between transgenic and wild-type oat plants

We investigated multiple agronomically important traits to evaluate whether the transgenes expression imposes an impact on the oat plant growth and development. The overall results indicated that no significant changes were observed between the transgenic and WT lines. In detail, the size of oat seeds was uniform among transgenic and WT lines (Figure S3a). Two of three pJP3679 transformed plants (D21 and D23) showed significantly higher 10 grains weight compared to WT (Figure S3b) while no difference was observed between the pJP3502 transformed plant (D35A) and WT. We also found that the inner structure and components of oat seeds remained the same between the transgenic and WT lines (Figure S3c,d). The cellular structure of transgenic oat leaves did not show a significant 

difference compared to WT leaves (Figure S4). Most transgenic plants grew to a similar size as WT with the exception of D21 showing more tillers (Figure S5). 

# Discussion

In comparison to traditional oil crops, due to low quantity of oil in oat seeds, it is not currently used as a source of edible oil. However, within the cereal species, it contains a high amount of lipids, marking its potential as a valuable energy resource (Wilde et al., 2019). Thus, improvement in oat oil content and composition could be a potential nutritional and technological target. In the past, efforts were initiated to genetically improve the oil content in oat. Studies indicate that oil content in oat is a quantitative trait with a polygenic pattern of inheritance (Schipper and Frey, 1991). Analysing different mapping populations, six QTLs for oil content have been reported (Zhu et al., 2004). Due to the nutritional importance of unsaturated fatty acids (FAs) for both humans and animals, there is a substantial interest in the FAs composition of oat oil (Hizbai et al., 2012). Although higher proportions of unsaturated FAs are desired for human cardiovascular health benefits, polyunsaturated FAs adversely affect the flavour and storage quality of oat. Therefore, decreased linolenic acid (C18:3) content may improve postharvest stability (Ludwig et al., 2018). Despite this, monounsaturated FA, oleic acid (C18:1) has received more attention from the food processing industry because it promotes greater stability, longer shelf life and trans fat-free oil products. Oleic acid content was also found to be positively correlated with seed oil content, therefore an important target for the improvement in the oat oil content as well as the oil quality for human food. 

In the current study, we introduced the combination of three genes, DGAT1, WRI1 and OLEOSIN that play critical roles in fatty acid biosynthesis and neutral lipid assembly. Efforts for an integrated metabolic engineering strategy have been made in different plant species where dramatic alterations in oil content

In potatoes, the expressions of atWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN were also detected in both leaves and tubers, though a tuber-specific promoter was used (Liu et al., 2017). By comparing the expression levels of three transgenes between D23 and D35A, pJP3502 demonstrated a larger transcriptional effect on both oat seed and leaf tissues. While the constructs that we utilized were originally developed for oil modifications in dicot species, it is interesting to note that the transgenes driven by dicot promoters were expressed ectopically in oat. To our knowledge, we have not encountered studies in literature where similar observations have been made. 

Furthermore, we profiled the expression of endogenous DGAT, WRI1 and OLEOSIN genes between transgenic and WT oat lines (Figure 2b). The upregulation of AsWRI1 and AsDGAT1 expression was detected in D35A leaves while AsWRI1 and AsOLEOSIN exhibited higher expression in D23 seeds. This is different from Larkin et al. (2021) where upregulation of TaOLEOSIN and TaDGAT genes was reported but no changes in TaWRI1 expression were observed between transgenic wheat grains and control. We speculate such differences could result from the plant species used in the studies. The lack of expression in the transgenic lines may be due to the presence of tandem repeats which influence gene silencing (Emani et al., 2002; Kohli et al., 1999). However, the stable expression of the desired genes does not guarantee a significant change in the lipid profile. 

The transcriptomic analysis revealed both up- and down-regulation genes in the transgenic oat mature seeds and boot leaves (Figure 3). The higher expression of the transgenes in D35A leaves resulted in a larger number of differentially expressed genes in leaves. Compared to the seeds of D23 carrying the pJP3679 construct, pJP3502 transformation led to more broad transcriptomic changes in D35A leaves, among which the up-regulated genes outnumbered the down-regulated one. This could be implied by ectopic expression of genes in monocot where dicot promoters lost their tissue specificity. Although D23 and D35A were produced from two different construct transformation, there is a small subset of overlap between the 

up-regulated and down-regulated genes in D23 seeds and D35A leaves (Table S3). The GO analysis also illustrated the functional classification of DEGs between D23 seeds and D35A leaves (Figure 5). Both groups possessed terms associated with lipid biosynthesis, such as phospholipid transport, lipid transporter activity, cellular metabolic process and catalytic activity. Terms like photosynthetic membrane and chloroplast represented the tissuespecific background. Arias et al. (2022) performed a similar GO enrichment analysis using transgenic soybean with AtDGAT1 and AtWRI1 where strong induction of genes encoding lipases and FA biosynthesis inhibitors was noted. 

In contrast to the transgenic oat leaves, the fatty acid composition of the transgenic oat seeds was significantly affected by the overexpression of transgenes. GC analysis revealed a decline in saturated fatty acids and linoleic acid contents, but an elevation of oleic acid contents in the transgenic oat seeds (Figure 6a). This is different from the reported observation in transgenic soybean seeds that oleic acid content is reduced but palmitic acid and linoleic acid are accumulated (Arias et al., 2022). However, no significant difference in five major fatty acid contents was detected between transgenic and WT oat leaves (Figure S2). In tobacco leaves, increased palmitic acid, oleic acid and linoleic acid contents and reduced linolenic acid content were observed in the high oil transgenic lines (Vanhercke et al., 2014). Based on the expression analysis of genes within the fatty acid biosynthetic pathway, the increased oleic acid content is associated with upregulation of three AsSACPD genes expression in D23 seeds while the expression of AsFAD2 was largely not affected. In oat leaves, no difference in AsSACPD expression is identified between D35A and WT (Figure 4). Future detailed studies are needed to explore the relationship between down regulation of fatty acid biosynthetic genes and fatty acid composition in the transgenic oat leaves. 

Overexpression of transgenes also resulted in the primary metabolic changes in oat seeds and leaves. The overall protein content showed a reduction, but the total oil content was not significantly affected in the transgenic oat seeds (Figure 6b). This is in line with earlier observations in the transgenic soybean seeds (Arias et al., 2022). Like previous studies in tobacco (Vanhercke et al., 2014), we observed that oil content in oat leaves first increases from vegetative to boot stage and decreases from boot to dough stage in D21, D35A and WT (Figure 7). Given that the 

oat leaf biomass reached the maximum weight during the boot stage, we primarily focused on this stage to perform transcriptomic and metabolic analysis. 

The accumulation of storage lipids in different plant tissues has important biotechnological applications. Using a combination of DGAT1, WRI1 and OLEOSIN genes, we observed that there is a great potential to enhance the oil content and its composition in oats. In recent years, oleic acid is receiving great attention for its health benefits as well as its property of slowing down the oxidative rancidity during shelf life (Sales-Campos et al., 2013). Since oleic acid content has been found to be positively correlated with oil content in oat, this may be an important target for plant breeders to improve the oat oil content and its composition for a healthy lifestyle. 

# Experimental procedures

# Oat transformation

The transformation was conducted using our previously described protocol (Cho et al., 1999; Mahmoud et al., 2022). Mature seeds of spring oat cultivar (‘Park’) were sterilized for $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ in a $20 \%$ (v/v) bleach solution, followed by three washes with sterile water. The sterilized seeds were then put on Murashige and Skoog-based regeneration media and maintained under dim light (16 h, 10–30 lE) and a temperature $2 4 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } }$ . The germinated roots and shoots were manually removed and embryonic regions were subcultured on callus induction media DBC3 supplemented with BAP, $\mathsf { C u S O _ { 4 } }$ and 2,4-D (Cardinal et al., 2016). After 3 weeks, high-quality green calli (about 35 pieces per plate) containing embryogenic and nodular patterns were selected for transformation using the biolistic delivery system (Figure 1b). pJP3679 and pJP3502 constructs contain AtDGAT1, AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN genes driven by dicot promoters (Kaur et al., 2022). The plasmid pAct1HPT-4 contains the hygromycin phosphotransferase (hpt) gene under the control of rice actin1 promoter (Cho et al., 1999), which was co-bombarded with either pJP3679 or pJP3502 to use hpt as a selectable marker. Subsequently, the bombarded calli were left on osmotic media overnight before being transferred to DBC3 media. Calli were exposed to DBC3 media supplemented with $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m q } / \mathsf { L }$ of hygromycin after 1 week. Healthy calli were sub-cultured on fresh media containing the same concentration of hygromycin after 2–3 weeks. Survived calli after three rounds of selection were regenerated on a regeneration medium and maintained in the rooting media before being transferred to soil. Generated plantlets were grown to maturity under $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of daylight at $1 0 0 0 \mu \ E$ and day and night temperatures at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , respectively. The DNA from putative transgenic plants was extracted and transgenic status was first tested by amplifying hpt gene. PCR screenings were performed for PAT gene in pJP3679 and for NPTII gene in pJP3502. To identify homozygous oat lines, 24–30 seeds from each line were planted for genotyping. The T-DNA copy number was measured by digital droplet PCR (ddPCR) as described in Cai et al. (2021) using endogenous Acc1 (acetyl-CoA carboxylase) with known copy number as the reference genes. Briefly, genomic DNA was digested by DrdI with one restriction cutting site within the transgenes (PAT or NPTII ) but no site within the amplicons for both target and reference genes. 

The ddPCR reaction was assembled in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ per reaction, including EvaGreen ddPCR supermix (1864033, Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA), $2 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ of forward and reverse primers, 20 ng digested genomic DNA and nuclease-free water. The droplet generation was followed by the manufacturer’s instructions, using DG8 cartridges, gaskets and cartridge holder in a $\mathsf { Q } \mathsf { X } 2 0 0$ droplet generator (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). The $4 0 ~ \mu \ L$ droplet emulsion was transferred to a 96-well, semi-skirted ddPCR plate, sealed with a pierceable foil and placed in a thermal cycler. The PCR program contains $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min, 50 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 s and $5 8 . 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 min, $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min, $9 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min and holds at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a $2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / \mathsf { S }$ ramping at each step. After PCR amplification, the plate was placed in a QX200 droplet reader (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) for fluorescence detection. QX manager software (2.1 Standard Edition) was used for data analysis. 

# Metabolic quantification analysis

Oat fatty acid composition was determined from acid methylation of 225 mg of groat flour samples as described (Doehlert et al., 2013). The $2 \%$ sulphuric acid solution of $1 . 6 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ was added to the flour in $1 5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ screw-top tubes and the tubes were sealed and allowed to reflux at $6 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2 h, vortexing every 20 min. The tubes were cooled to room temperature and then, $1 . 6 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of a potassium carbonate solution was added to neutralize the acid. An internal standard was added $( 8 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of $4 . 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ methyl heptadecanoic acid). Then, the mixture was vortexed and methylated fatty acids were extracted with $1 . 6 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of hexane. The mixture was vortexed extensively and centrifuged at $1 2 4 0 . 5 g$ for 10 min and the hexane layer was separated and sealed in gas chromatography vials. Methylated fatty acids were analysed with a Supelco SP-2560 gas chromatography column ( $1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } \times \mathrm { ~ 0 . 2 5 ~ m m }$ i.d., 0.20 ım film). The gas chromatography initial oven temperature was $1 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min and raised to $2 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ at a rate of $4 ^ { \circ } / \mathsf { m i n }$ and the detector was HP5972 Mass Selective Detector. A Supelco 37-component FAME mix (Supelco, Bellefonte, PA, USA) was used as the standard for calibration. The quantification of oat seed oil content was performed using an HR-MAS NMR spectrometer (ADVANCE III HD 600, Bruker Cooperation, Billerica, MA, USA) located at the Research Institute of the McGill University Health Centre as described by Kushwaha (2021). Oat leaf oil was extracted by shaking 100-mg leaf sample in $2 \mathsf { m l }$ of petroleum ether at frequency of 1000 rounds per minute (16.67Hz) for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ using an Eppendorf Thermo Mixer C (extraction repeated two times). After extraction, the sample and petroleum ether mixture was centrifuged at 1688.5 g for 10 min. The supernatant was evaporated for 1 h at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in a CentriVap (Labconco, KS, USA). The oil quantity was determined by weighing after evaporation. The nitrogen, carbon and sulfur contents were determined using a FlashSmart Elemental Analyser (Thermo Scientific). The factor to convert N to protein was 6.25 (AACC International Approved Method 46-30.01). Oat starch was determined according to AACC International Approved Method 76-13.01 following total starch (a-amylase/amyloglucosidase) assay protocol using the Megazyme Total Starch Assay Kit (AA/AMG, Ireland). 

# RNA extraction and transgene expression profiling by qPCR

Mature seeds and boot leaves collected from oat transgenic and WT lines were frozen in liquid nitrogen, grounded to fine powder and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ prior to RNA isolation. Total RNA was isolated from finely crushed seed and leaf samples using the 

Spectrum Plant Total RNA Kit (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) following manufacturer’s protocol with minor modifications. Each RNA sample was measured on a NanoDrop ND-1000 (NanoDrop Technologies, Washington, DE, USA) for its quality and quantity and then treated with DNase I (Invitrogen, USA) to remove genomic DNA contamination. Total $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of extracted RNA was used in cDNA synthesis with AffinityScript QPCR cDNA Synthesis kit (Agilent technology, Canada). IDT primer design tool (www.idtdna.com) was used for designing gene-specific primers from coding region of AtDGAT1, AtWRI1 and SiOLEOSIN. The qPCR was performed using Supergreen qPCR master mix (WINSENT Bioproducts) and the CFX384 Touch Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad). With three biological, two technical replicates, the relative gene expression was determined by $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C } \mathsf { q } }$ method using AsEP and AsEIF4A as reference genes (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). The primer sequences are listed in Table S4. 

# Sample preparation for RNA sequencing

Total RNA was extracted from D23 and Park WT mature seeds and D35A and Park WT boot leaves with three biological replicates using the Spectrum Plant Total RNA Kit (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) following manufacturer’s protocol with minor modifications. Each RNA sample was quantified using the NanoDrop One microvolume spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific). A total of 12 RNA samples were sent to the Centre d’expertise et de services Genome Quebec for library preparation using Nextera DNA Library Preparation Kit (Illumina). The libraries were sequenced via the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 with 100 bp paired-end reads. 

# Differential expression analysis

FASTQ raw reads were analysed with FastQC (version 0.11.5) for quality check (Andrews, 2010). The cDNA library of Avena sativa OT3098 were obtained via Ensembl Plants (https://plants. ensembl.org/info/data/ftp/index.html) as the transcriptome reference. We use Kallisto (version 0.43.1) to perform pseudoalignment and read quantification (Bray et al., 2016). The output of Kallisto was used for differential expression analysis at the gene level via Sleuth (version 0.30.0) (Pimentel et al., 2017). Beta values, representing estimated log2 fold-change, were generated via the Wald test. Genes displaying significant differential expression FDR (q value ${ \underline { { < 0 . 0 5 } } } )$ and fold change ≥2 (log2 $( \mathsf { F C } ) \ge 1$ ) were selected for further analysis. 

# Gene set enrichment analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analyses of DEGs were performed using g:Profiler (https://biit.cs.ut.ee/gprofiler/gost) (Raudvere et al., 2019). The redundant GO terms were removed using Revigo (http://revigo.irb.hr). Volcano plot, Venn diagram and enrichment dot bubble graph were made using the R packages (Tang et al., 2023). 

# Light and confocal fluorescence microscopy imaging

The samples were dehydrated with acidified 2,2-dimethoxypropane, followed by three acetone–Spurr epoxy series before being embedded in fresh Spurr epoxy resin and polymerized at $7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 0 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The samples

All samples were imaged with Zeiss Axio Imager Z1 light microscope at the Eastern Canadian Plant Phenotyping Platform (ECP3) Multi-Scale Imaging Facility, McGill University, Sainte-Anne-de-Bellevue, Quebec, Canada. 

Fresh leaf samples were harvested and then sectioned by razor blade. The thin hand sections were fixed in $3 \%$ paraformaldehyde with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), pH 7.2 for 1 h at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , then overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The samples were post-fixed in $1 \%$ osmium tetroxide at room temperature for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , followed by three consecutive water rinses, 30 min each. The samples were stained with $2 \ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ BODIPY 505/515 in 50 mM PIPES pH 7 for 10 min and then washed in 50 mM PIPES pH 7 (Vanhercke et al., 2018). The samples were mounted on slides with AF1 solution and then covered by the #1.5 thickness coverslips. All samples were imaged with a confocal microscope, Zeiss LSM710-Airyscan at ECP3 Multi-Scale Imaging Facility.
