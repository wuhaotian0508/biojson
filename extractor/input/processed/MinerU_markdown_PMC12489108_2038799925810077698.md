# HY5 integrates light and electrical signaling to trigger a jasmonate burst for nematode defense in tomato

Plants have evolved sophisticated defense systems to protect against herbivory, including the systemic induction of jasmonic acid (JA) synthesis. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying this process remain poorly understood. Here, we report that root-knot nematode (RKN) attack induced a phyBdependent accumulation of ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) in the leaves, which activates the expression of JA biosynthesis genes and HY5 itself in tomato. In addition, the systemic transmission of GLUTAMATE RECEPTOR-LIKE 3.5 (GLR3.5)-dependent electrical signals induced by RKN triggers the physical interaction between CALMODULIN 2 (CaM2) and HY5 to amplify the transcriptional regulation of HY5 and JA synthesis. HY5 functions as a systemic signal that moves from leaves to roots to maintain the electrical signaling from roots to leaves by activating GLR3.5 expression. Together, these results reveal a HY5-dependent systemic signaling cascade that integrates light and electrical signals to activate JA-mediated defense against nematodes in tomato. 

Plants have evolved sophisticated mechanisms that result in the rapid accumulation of a key defense hormone, jasmonates (JAs), which trigger defenses against herbivores, nematodes, and necrotrophic pathogens through their signaling pathways1–3 . The JA signaling network consists of two antagonistic branches: the MYC2 branch, which is involved in wound response and defense against insect herbivores, and the ERF branch, which enhances resistance to necrotrophic pathogens and nematodes4,5 . JA and its derivatives are primarily produced from $\alpha$ -linolenic acid (α-LeA) from the chloroplast membrane through a series of oxidative processes. These processes are catalyzed by lipoxygenases (LOXs), allene oxide synthase (AOS), and allene oxide cyclase (AOC), which together drive the formation of the JA precursor, 12-oxo-phytodienoic acid (OPDA), a compound essential for resistance6,7 . This rapid induction and subsequent spread of JAs is crucial for activating systemic defense responses throughout the plant8,9 . JAs not only accumulate in the wounded local leaves but also in 

distal leaves within seconds to minutes after wounding8–11. Meanwhile, JA biosynthesis is activated in the leaves when the roots are challenged with root-knot nematode (RKN)12. Such inter-organ communication systems enhance plant resistance to insect herbivores and microbial pathogens. 

Light is not only a source of energy but also a key environmental signal regulating plant defense13. JA synthesis is induced by light14. Whereas low red to far-red (R/FR) light attenuates JA-mediated defense responses15–18 via a mechanism regulated by the red light photoreceptor, phytochrome B (phyB)16,17. ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) is a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor that acts downstream of photoreceptors19. It functions as a pivotal regulator of the light-mediated transcriptional network to integrate light signals with other stimuli, such as temperature and hormonal cues20. HY5 is involved in the responses to low-temperature stress through the coordinated regulation of gibberellin (GA) and abscisic acid (ABA)

Moreover, photoactivated HY5 has been shown to act as a shoot-to-root mobile signal that regulates the light promotion of nutrient absorption, nodule formation, and root growth22–24. In contrast, little is known about whether HY5 regulates JA-mediated defense. 

Due to their sessile nature, plants possess a highly sophisticated defense system that perceives environmental changes and triggers systemic stress responses. In addition to light signals, the activation of systemic resistance involves multiple long-distance signals, including RESPIRATORY BURST OXIDASE HOMOLOG (RBOH)-dependent reactive oxygen species $( \mathsf { R O S } ) ^ { 2 5 }$ , GLUTAMATE RECEPTOR-LIKE (GLR)- dependent electrical and calcium ion $( \mathbf { C _ { 2 } } ^ { 2 + } )$ signals26,27, which propagate through the vasculature28,2 9. Wounding triggers a GLR3.3 and GLR3.6-dependent electrical transmission pathway, leading to the induction of JA biosynthesis in the distal leaves in Arabidopsis26. The GLR family of ion channels also propagates $\mathbf { C _ { 2 } } ^ { 2 + }$ signals to activate JA synthesis27,30. However, the mechanisms by which electrical $/ { \bf C } { \bf a } ^ { 2 + }$ signals induce JA synthesis in response to pest attacks are poorly understood. 

Plant-parasitic nematodes, which attack a number of economically important crops, cause approximately $1 2 . 3 \%$ of annual crop losses, worth over $\$ 157$ billion globally31,32. We have previously shown that RKN infection in tomato triggers the systemic transmission of GLR3.5- dependent electrical signals from attacked roots to the leaves to initiate JA biosynthesis in the leaves12. This systemic signaling loop, which integrates electrical signals with JA via root-shoot-root communication, is essential for the induction of defense responses in distal organs. The present study, which focuses on RKN resistance and JA synthesis, provides evidence that HY5 is a central hub for RKN resistance by regulating JA synthesis. Crucially, we demonstrate that the root-to-shoot transmission of GLR3.5-dependent electrical signaling induces the interaction between CALMODULIN 2 (CaM2) and HY5 that enhances the transcription of HY5 itself and also the expression of JA biosynthesis genes in the leaves. Moreover, the shoot-to-root transport of HY5 enhances the electrical activity through the activation of GLR3.5 expression in the stems and roots. This HY5-mediated crosstalk between root and shoot is important for the regulation of both JA biosynthesis in the leaves and the RKN resistance in the roots. 

# Results

PhyB plays a critical role in JA biosynthesis and RKN resistance To determine whether the root-knot nematode (RKN; Meloidogyne incognita) induced JA biosynthesis is regulated by light, four-leaf stage wild type (WT) tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) plants that had been inoculated with 1,000 infective second-stage juveniles (J2s) per plant, were either kept in darkness for 24 hours (h) or exposed to 12 h dark followed by 12 h light. While RKN infection induced a significant increase in the accumulation of JA and jasmonoyl-isoleucine (JA-Ile, the active JA) in the leaves and roots for tomato plants after 12 h dark followed by $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { h } }$ white (W) light, no significant changes in the accumulation of JAs were observed in tomato plants kept in continuous darkness for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ (Supplementary Fig. 1a). Next, we compared nematode resistance and JA accumulation in tomato plants grown under W, monochromatic red (R) or blue (B) light with or without supplementary far red (FR) light (+FR, −FR). Tomato plants grown under R light had fewer RKN galls on the roots at 28 dpi and a more significant increase in JA accumulation at 1 dpi than those kept under B light or W light conditions (Fig. 1a–c and Supplementary Fig. 1b, c). In addition, exposure to supplementary FR greatly attenuated nematode resistance and decreased JA accumulation in tomato plants grown under R and W light but had little effect on tomato plants grown under B light. When JA biosynthesis-defective mutants (suppressor of prosysteminmediated responses2; spr2) were grown under W or R light, no significant differences in the numbers of RKN galls were observed between the two light conditions (Supplementary Fig. 1d), suggesting 

that R light-induced RKN resistance is dependent on JA production. Hence, R light and FR light positively and negatively regulated JAdependent RKN resistance, respectively. 

Next, we compared susceptibility to RKN attack in WT, the FR light photoreceptor mutant phyA, the R light photoreceptor mutant phyB1phyB2 (phyB), and the B light photoreceptor mutant cry1a. Nematode resistance was higher in phyA mutants than WT but lower in phyB (Fig. 1d and Supplementary Fig. 2a). However, the cry1a mutants exhibited a similar resistance to the WT. Consistent with this finding, RKN induced significant JA accumulation in the leaves and roots as well as higher levels of transcripts encoding components of the JA biosynthesis pathway, such as LOXF, AOS, and AOC, in the leaves of WT, phyA, and cry1a mutants (Fig. 1e, f, Supplementary Fig. 2b, c). These RKN-induced increases were not observed in the phyB mutants. These results strongly suggest that phyB plays a central role in lightdependent regulation of JA biosynthesis in RKN resistance. 

We next determined the respective contributions of phyB in shoots and roots to JA synthesis and nematode resistance by reciprocally grafting WT plants with the phyB mutants at the two-leaf stage. Compared to grafts with WT as scions (WT/WT and WT/phyB), grafts with phyB as scions (phyB/phyB and phyB/WT) showed increased RKN susceptibility and less RKN-induced JA accumulation in both the leaves and roots (Fig. 1g–i, Supplementary Fig. 2d). Notably, there were no significant differences in RKN resistance or JA accumulation between WT/WT and WT/phyB or between phyB/phyB and phyB/WT. Furthermore, RKN-induced increases in LOXF, AOS and AOC transcripts were abolished in grafts with phyB as scions (Supplementary Fig. 2e). These results suggest that phyB in the shoot, but not in the root, is a key component in light-induced RKN resistance and JA biosynthesis. 

# HY5 regulates phyB-dependent JA biosynthesis and RKN resistance

HY5, a key transcription factor in the light signaling pathway, is regulated by photoreceptors33,34. As shown in Supplementary Fig. 3, phyA, phyB, and CRY1A all contribute to HY5 accumulation under Mock conditions. However, RKN-induced HY5 accumulation is specifically compromised in phyB mutants, suggesting that phyB is crucial for RKN-induced accumulation of HY5. Similarly, after RKN infection, the leaves of phyB/WT grafts exhibited lower levels of HY5 proteins and transcripts compared to those in WT/WT grafts (Fig. 2a, b). To dissect the function of HY5 in nematode resistance further, we compared RKN-induced gall numbers, JA accumulation, and the levels of JA biosynthesis gene transcripts in WT and two hy5 mutant lines (hy5#1 and hy5#4) that were generated using a CRISPR/Cas9 approach35. The hy5 mutants had a lower resistance to RKNs than WT and showed compromised RKN-induced increase in JAs accumulation in the leaves and the roots, together with lower levels of LOXF, AOS, AOC and HY5 transcripts (Fig. 2c and Supplementary Fig. 4a–d). Additionally, RL further enhanced RKN resistance in HY5-OE lines compared to WL and BL (Supplementary Fig. 4e). Reciprocal grafting experiments revealed that grafts with hy5 as the scion (hy5/hy5 and hy5/WT) exhibited decreased RKN resistance compared to grafts with WT as the scions (WT/WT and WT/hy5) (Fig. 2d). While RKN attack induced significant increases in JAs accumulation in the leaves and roots of the WT/WT and WT/hy5 grafts, as well as increased LOXF, AOS, AOC and HY5 transcripts in the leaves, such increases were not observed in the leaves of the hy5/hy5 and hy5/WT grafts (Fig. 2e–g, Supplementary Fig. 4f). Therefore, we conclude that RKN-induced JA biosynthesis and nematode resistance are under the control of HY5 in the shoots.

# CaM2 mediates an electrical $/ { \bf C } { \bf a } ^ { 2 + }$ signaling cascade that activates light signaling, leading to JA biosynthesis and RKN resistance

GLR3-dependent electrical signals are essential for wound-induced systemic induction of JA biosynthesis, whilst GLR family ion channels are associated with the propagation of ${ \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { 2 + }$ signaling27. The exogenous application of the $\mathbf { C _ { 2 } } ^ { 2 + }$ channel inhibitor, ruthenium red (RR), significantly suppressed the RKN-induced JAs accumulation and the 

expression of LOXF, AOS and AOC in the leaves of WT plants (Supplementary Fig. 6a, b). However, due to already significantly attenuated responses in hy5, RR treatment had little effect on the accumulation of JA, JA-Ile and transcripts of JA biosynthesis genes in the hy5 mutant leaves. Crucially, mutation to GLR3.5 reduced the RKN-induced accumulation of HY5 and CaM proteins, as well as the accumulation of CaM2 transcripts, but had little effect on the expression of other CaM genes (Fig. 3a–c). While silencing of HY5 compromised RKN-induced increases in JA accumulation, the over-expression of HY5 resulted in an increased accumulation of JAs in both the WT and glr3.5 mutants (Supplementary Fig. 6c, d). 

Analysis of the transcriptome data revealed that CaM2 is the most highly expressed CaM gene in leaves (Supplementary Fig. 7a). Additionally, the RKN-induced expression of CaM2 was significantly reduced in phyB mutants but remained unchanged in phyA and cry1a mutants relative to WT (Supplementary Fig. 7b). Based on these findings, two CRISPR/Cas9 cam2 mutant lines (cam2#1 and cam2#2)37 were used to explore the role of CaM2 in the RKN-induced JA burst. # Discussion

The data presented here provide new insights into the mechanisms by which RKN indirectly triggers the phyB-dependent accumulation of HY5 in shoots, which in turn activates the expression of JA biosynthesis genes as well as HY5 itself. The systemic transmission of electrical signals from the root triggers the physical interaction between CaM2 and HY5 in the leaves, leading to an enhanced transcriptional activity of HY5. Simultaneously, the mobile pool of shoot-derived HY5 directly activates the expression of GLR3.5 in the roots, stems, and leaves to enhance RKN-induced electrical signaling, leading to the amplification of the HY5 activity for the rapid JA burst that facilitates plant defense against RKN (Fig. 6i). 

We present data showing that R light and the leaf photoreceptor, phyB, trigger a systemic signaling pathway that regulates root resistance to RKN in a JA-dependent manner. To date, several studies have demonstrated that local resistance to pathogens and herbivory is dependent on the light conditions15,41–43. However, little is known about how the light conditions experienced by the shoot alter resistance in the roots. This study provides evidence that RKN resistance was decreased under low R/FR light ratios or in grafts with phyB as scions (Fig. 1a, g). Together, these findings indicate that the prevailing light conditions can regulate root resistance to RKN attack in a systemic manner. Grafting experiments further provided evidence that the expression of phyB in the shoots determines root defenses against RKN (Fig. 1g). Notably, the RKN-induced JA burst was compromised in grafts with phyB mutants as scions (Fig. 1h, i), suggesting that resistance to RKN is largely dependent on the capacity of phyB-mediated JA synthesis in the leaves rather than in the roots. However, it remains unclear whether RKN attack directly or indirectly activates phyB. Additionally, endogenous circadian rhythms have been observed in several microorganisms, including the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans44. It is still uncertain whether RKN attack on plant roots follows a circadian rhythm or if the timing of infection is influenced by plant signals such as JAs. 

HY5 acts downstream of sensory photoreceptors, including phytochromes, cryptochromes, and the UV-B receptor, establishing it as a central module in light signaling45,46. In addition, HY5 acts in multiple phytohormonal pathways, integrating and fine-tuning their responses to regulate a wide range of plant processes47,48. While RL, FR and BL all 

induce the accumulation of HY5 protein during photomorphogenesis, we demonstrated here that, under WL conditions, RL via phyB primarily regulates the accumulation of HY5 in response to RKN infection (Fig. 2). This finding is in agreement with our earlier finding that phyB plays a predominant role in the induction of HY5 protein in the adult plant response to light quality changes35. Furthermore, HY5 functions as a transcriptional activator of the expression of several genes involved in JA synthesis (Fig. 2). Consistent with this proposed role for phyB, grafting experiments demonstrated that HY5-dependent JA biosynthesis mainly occurred in the shoots (Fig. 2e–g). Therefore, HY5 not only participates in the regulation of the signaling of phytohormones such as $\mathsf { G A } \mathsf { s } ^ { 2 1 }$ , ABA49, brassinosteroid $( \mathtt { B R } ) ^ { 5 0 }$ , and strigolactones $( \mathsf { S L s } ) ^ { 3 5 }$ , but it is also involved in JA signaling because it functions as a transcriptional activator of several genes encoding enzymes of the JA biosynthesis pathway. As JA is an important hormone in plant stress responses, these findings support the concept that HY5 plays a major role in plant responses to abiotic and biotic stresses. 

To date, little information has been available concerning how plants activate JA synthesis in defense against potential threats. The synthesis of JA begins in the plastids, primarily from α-LeA released from chloroplast membranes51. RKN infection significantly induced JA accumulation in the roots of spr2 rootstocks but much less so in WT roots grafted to spr2 scions12. Similarly, Grebner et al. demonstrated that JA production in the roots is lower than in the shoots52. Our study found that plants transport a substantial amount of JA from the shoots to the roots, initiating a systemic signaling process to mount a defense against infection. This process requires light signaling, as demonstrated in grafts with phyB and hy5 as scions (Figs. 1, 2). Furthermore, we demonstrated that HY5-induced transcription of JA biosynthesis genes is important for JA accumulation. The upregulation of three key JA biosynthetic genes and JA accumulation was apparent only in the presence of RKN attack (Figs. 2, 3), as was observed in our early study with Helicoverpa armigera as stimuli53. An outstanding question in this system is how JA biosynthesis in the leaves is activated in response to root-generated signals. Several studies have shown that GLR3.3 and GLR3.6 mediate wound-induced propagation of electrical signals and herbivore-triggered long-distance transmission of ${ \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { 2 + }$ signals that activate JAs biosynthesis in the distal leaves of Arabidopsis that have never directly experienced the stress26,27. Recently, we showed that RKNs generate a systemic propagation of GLR3.5-dependent electrical signals from the attacked roots to the shoots in order to induce JA biosynthesis in the leaves12. Meanwhile, post-translation regulation is important for JA biosynthesis. Previously, we have shown that ROS/ $\mathbf { C _ { 2 } } ^ { 2 + }$ -induced MAPK1/2 activity plays a crucial role in JA biosynthesis only under RKN12. Thus, both transcriptional and post-translation regulation are important for JA biosynthesis in plant response to RKN infection. The data reported here show that RKN attack induces the GLR3.5-dependent expression of CaM2 and an accumulation of the HY5 protein (Fig. 3a–c). As the predominant ${ \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { 2 + }$ sensor, CaM translates local $[ \mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + } ]$ changes into specific physiological responses by modifying the activities of CaM-binding proteins following $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ binding54,55. Crucially, we show that HY5 is a CaM2-binding protein (Fig. 4). Moreover, the physical interaction between HY5 and CaM2 enhances the transcriptional activity of HY5 with respect to JA biosynthesis genes and to HY5 itself (Fig. 5a–d). Moreover, the levels of JAs and RKN resistance were decreased in the $H Y 5 ^ { H } o x$ and HY5N ox plants because of the weakened interactions between CaM2 and HY5 (Fig. 5e–g). These findings demonstrate the importance of CaM2-HY5 complex formation in RKN-induced JA biosynthesis and nematode resistance. 

Wounding, like RKN attack, generates the systemic propagation of GLR-dependent electrical signals from the roots to the leaves of several plant species30. However, the regulatory mechanisms involved have remained unclear, highlighting the need for further investigation. 

Previous studies showed that photoactivated HY5 protein is mobile and can move from the leaves through the stems to the roots to promote nodule organogenesis in soybean, nitrogen (N) acquisition in Arabidopsis and phosphate (P) and iron (Fe) uptake in tomato by regulating gene expression in roots22,24,35,40. In this study, we demonstrate that HY5 protein is translocated from the shoot to the root in tomato plants, both in the presence and absence of RKN infection (Fig. 6). However, the exact mechanism by which HY5 protein is translocated from local leaves to distant tissues remains unclear. Interestingly, we observed that the accumulation of 35S promoterdriven HY5-HA in the rootstock was further increased following RKN infection (Fig. 6b), accompanied by RKN-induced CaM2 accumulation in the leaf (Fig. 3b). These findings suggest that the interaction between CaM2 and HY5 may contribute to the stabilization of HY5 protein. Nevertheless, the precise molecular mechanism underlying CaM2-mediated HY5 stabilization remains to be elucidated. Furthermore, our findings reveal that the shoot-to-root transport of HY5 is essential for the continuous transmission of root-to-shoot electrical signals through the transcription activation of GLR3.5 (Fig. 6). Therefore, the root-to-shoot transmission of GLR3.5-dependent electrical signaling that is induced through the interaction of CaM2 with HY5 enhances the transcriptional activity of HY5 and JA biosynthesis genes. Meanwhile, the shoot-to-root transport of HY5 enhances electrical activity by regulating the expression of GLR3.5. Importantly, CaM2 is critical for the RKN-induced HY5 accumulation, as evidenced by the results that HY5-OE cam2 plants accumulated significantly less HY5 protein than HY5-OE (Supplemental Fig. 8g). In addition, we reported earlier that electrical activity increased at 6 h and reached a peak $2 4 { - } 4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after RKN infection12. Here, we also found that the electrical activity was low under dark conditions (Supplementary Fig. 10), suggesting that the RKN effector (or other stimuli) alone is not sufficient for the maintenance of electrical activity, but rather that light is also essential for the continuous propagation of the electrical signal. This reciprocal crosstalk between electrical and light signaling is not only important for the activation of JA synthesis and nematode resistance, but it is also crucial for the maintenance of systemic electrical signaling. 

In summary, the results presented in this study demonstrate that HY5 integrates light and electrical $/ { \mathsf { C } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { 2 + }$ signals to promote JA synthesis in RKN resistance. It is likely that HY5 is a key regulator of $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } / \mathrm { C a M } \cdot$ induced JA accumulation at two control points: firstly, the interaction between CaM2 and HY5 enhances the transcriptional capacity of HY5 with regard to JA biosynthesis (Fig. 5); secondly, this interaction activates the expression of HY5 itself and so amplifies the first process to ensure a rapid JA burst (Fig. 5a–d). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that HY5 positively regulates host plant immunity by integrating electrical and light signals. We conclude that HY5 acts as an important regulatory hub at the nexus of plant development and biotic/abiotic stress responses. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Seeds of wild-type (WT) tomato (Solanum lycopersicum L. cv. ‘Ailsa Craig’, ‘Castlemart’, ‘Moneymaker’), phytochrome A (phyA) mutant, phytochrome B1 (phyB1) and phytochrome B2 (phyB2) double mutant (phyB) and cryptochrome 1a (cry1a) mutant in the Moneymaker background were obtained from the Tomato Genetics Resource Center (University of California, Davis, CA, USA). The spr2 mutant (Castlemart background) was kindly provided by Prof. C.Y. Li (Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing, China). The hy5 mutant lines (hy5#1 and hy5#4), cam2 mutant lines (cam2#1 and cam2#2), glr3.5 mutant and HY5 overexpressing (HY5-OE) transgenic plants in the Ailsa Craig background were previously obtained12,35,37,56. 

To generate transgenic lines overexpressing HY5, $H Y 5 ^ { H }$ and $H Y 5 ^ { N }$ in the hy5#4 background (HY5ox, HY5H ox and $H Y S ^ { N } O x )$ , the coding 

sequence (CDS) of HY5 was obtained by PCR-amplifying from tomato cDNA, and $H Y 5 ^ { H }$ and $H Y 5 ^ { N }$ were generated using a Fast Mutagenesis Kit (Vazyme, C214-01). The synthesized sequences were inserted into the pFGC1008-HA vector, containing a CaMV 35S promoter, using primers listed in Supplementary Table 1. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and introduced into Ailsa Craig57. The transgenic plants HY5ox, $H Y { \bar { S } } ^ { H } o x ,$ and HY5N ox were identified by reverse transcription (RT)-quantitative realtime PCR (qPCR) and immunoblot analysis. To silence HY5 in WT and glr3.5, virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) experiments were carried out using tobacco rattle virus (TRV)-based vectors (pTRV1/2)58, and the primers used for construction are listed in Supplementary Table 1. Plants with a silencing efficiency of $70 \%$ were used for further experiments (Supplementary Fig. 11). The HY5-OE cam2, HY5-OE glr3.5, and hy5 cam2 plants were obtained by genetic crossing with cam2#1, glr3.5, hy5#4, and HY5-OE plants. Tomato seeds were sown in pots containing a mixture of sand and vermiculite (1:1) and watered with 1/2 Hoagland’s nutrition solution. The grown conditions were maintained at a $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { h } }$ photoperiod, $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (day/night) temperature, and 400 μmol $\mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD). 

To elucidate the respective role of phyB and HY5 in the shoots and roots regarding nematode resistance and JA biosynthesis, shoots from two-leaf stage WT, phyB, and hy5#4 plants were self-grafted or reciprocally grafted onto rootstocks of WT, phyB, and hy5#4 plants, respectively. To elucidate the mobility of HY5, shoots from two-leaf stage WT and HY5-OE plants were grafted onto rootstocks of WT. After three days of acclimatization under a dark and high-humidity environment, the grafted plants were exposed to the aforementioned growth conditions. 

# Root-knot nematode infection and resistance assay

The root-knot nematode (Meloidogyne incognita; RKN) populations were maintained on susceptible tomato plants before being used for plant infection. RKN infection was performed via the method described by Wang et al.12. Four-leaf stage tomato plants were inoculated with 1000 infective second-stage juveniles (J2s) per plant in 5 mL of water applied evenly to the surface of the growth substrates with a pipette at a radius of 2 cm around the primary roots. 

For the measurement of phytohormones, mRNA, and protein levels, leaves and roots were collected from six independent tomato plants $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ after inoculation, and two plants were pooled to make one sample, resulting in three biological replicates. No.3 and 4 leaves were sampled and rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen. The roots were removed from the growth substrate and washed in ${ \bf d d H } _ { 2 } { \bf O }$ to remove excess substrate. After drying with absorbent paper, the roots were snap frozen and stored at $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for further analysis. 

To visualize the infective J2s on the roots, the roots were washed and placed in $5 \%$ NaClO for 5 mins, followed by rinsing with tap water at 3 days post-inoculation (dpi). The roots were then boiled in $3 . 5 \%$ acid fuchsin stain solution for 3 min, after which the roots were rinsed with tap water, destained in acidified glycerol and observed with an Olympus BX61 optical microscope. Four weeks after RKN inoculation, the roots were carefully rinsed and the fresh root weights were measured. To visualize root gall organogenesis, the roots were stained with $3 . 5 \%$ acid fuchsin using the aforementioned methods. Nematode susceptibility was assessed by counting the number of root galls per plant and calculating the number of galls per gram of fresh root weight59. More than 15 plants of each genotype were used to determine of the galls on individual root systems in each experiment. The experiments were repeated three times with similar results. 

# Light treatments and pharmacological treatments

To determine the effect of light on JA biosynthesis and electrical activity, the tomato seedlings at the four-leaf stage were exposed to light conditions (12 h dark/12 h light) or to continuous darkness. To 

determine the effect of light quality on JA biosynthesis and nematode resistance, the tomato seedlings at the four-leaf stage were exposed to white light (WL; $4 0 0 { \cdot } 7 6 0 \mathrm { n m } ,$ ), monochromatic red light (RL; $6 1 0 { - } 6 6 0 \ : \mathrm { n m } )$ , or monochromatic blue light (BL; 430-480 nm). Half of the plants marked as $\prime _ { \mathrm { + F R } ^ { \prime } }$ were supplemented with far-red light (FR; $7 1 0 { \cdot } 7 4 0 \ : \mathrm { n m } )$ ), and another half marked as ‘-FR’ did not receive FR light. All kinds of light spectrum diagrams were shown in Supplementary Fig. 12. For different light treatments, plants were kept in a chamber with a $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { h } }$ photoperiod and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ intensity (WL, RL, and BL) at the top of the canopy using 20 W LED lamps (PHILIPS, Netherlands). FR illumination was achieved by 20 W LED lamps (PHILIPS, Netherlands). The light condition was measured by the Spectral PAR meter SPIC-30SE (EVERFINE, China). 

For exogenous chemical treatment, ruthenium red (RR) was applied onto the leaves of the four-leaf stage tomato at a concentration of $2 0 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ before the RKN infection. 

# JA and JA-Ile quantification

The phytohormones JA and JA-Ile were extracted from tomato leaves and roots following the method of Wang et al. 12 with minor modifications. Briefly, $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of frozen leaf or root tissue was homogenized in 1 mL of ethyl acetate with internal standards D6- JA (OlChemIm, Czechoslovakia) and D6-JA-Ile (Quality control chemicals, USA) at final concentrations of $1 0 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ and $4 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively. Samples were shaken in the dark at 200 rpm for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ and centrifuged at $1 8 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The supernatants were collected, and the pellet was reextracted with 1 mL of ethyl acetate. Combined extracts were dried under a gentle ${ \Nu } _ { 2 }$ stream, re-dissolved in $0 . 5 \mathsf { m L }$ of $70 \%$ methanol $( \nu / \nu )$ , and centrifuged. The resulting supernatants were transferred to vials for LC-MS/MS analysis (Varian 320-MS LC/MS, Agilent) using an Agilent Zorbax XDB C18 column. The mobile phase consisted of water containing $0 . 0 5 \%$ formic acid (solvent A) and methanol (solvent B), with the following gradient: 0–1.5 min, 60:40 (A:B); 1.5-8 min, 0:100 (A:B); 8-13 min, 60: 40 (A:B), at a flow rate of $0 . 3 \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { \cdot 1 }$ . The column temperature was maintained at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and the injection volume was $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . Detection was performed in negative electrospray ionization mode using MRM transitions: $\mathbf { m } / z$ $2 0 9 . 1  5 9 . 1$ (JA), $2 1 4 . 3  6 2 . 1$ (D6-JA), $3 2 2 . 0  1 3 0 . 0$ (JA-Ile), and $3 2 8 . 5  1 3 0 . 1 $ (D6-JA-Ile). Each treatment included three biological replicates. 

# RNA isolation and RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted using an RNA extraction kit (Tiangen, DP419) and reverse-transcribed using a ReverTra Ace qPCR RT kit (Vazyme, R223) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Gene expression analyses were measured via RT-qPCR using a Light Cycler 480 II detection system (Roche) with a SYBR Green PCR Master Mix Kit (Vazyme, Q711). The relative transcript levels were normalized to ACTIN2 and UBI3. The sequences of the primer pairs used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplementary Table 2. 

# Protein extraction and western blotting

For protein extraction, leaf or root samples were ground to powder in liquid nitrogen and immunoblot analysis was performed60. For western blotting, proteins were separated by a $10 \%$ SDS-PAGE gel and then transferred to nitrocellulose membranes. For the detection of HY5 and CaM, specific antibodies against HY5 and CaM proteins (Shanghai Jiayuan Bio Co., Shanghai, China) were employed. The HA-tagged HY5 protein was detected with an anti-HA monoclonal antibody (Abcam, ab187915). Actin protein was detected with an anti-Actin polyclonal antibody (Abcam, ab197345). Goat anti-rabbit horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-linked antibody (Cell Signaling Technology, 7074S) and goat anti-mouse HRP-linked antibody (Bio-Rad, 1706516) were used as secondary antibodies. Signal detection was performed using an 

enhanced chemiluminescence kit (FDbio, FD8030). Actin protein was used as the loading control. Uncropped and original immunoblotting images were provided in Supplementary Data. 

# ChIP-qPCR

ChIP assays were performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions of EpiQuikTM Plant ChIP Kit (Epigentek, P-2014). Approximately $1 . 5 { \mathrm { g } }$ of leaf or root samples were harvested from WT, HY5-OE, cam2#1, HY5-OE cam2, hy5#4, HY5ox, HY5Hox, and HY5N ox plants at 1 dpi, WT and HY5-OE of which were under WL or RL conditions. Chromatin was immunoprecipitated using an anti-HA antibody (Ab) (Abcam, ab187915), with a goat anti-mouse immunoglobulin G (IgG) (EMD Millipore, AP124P) serving as the negative control. Both immunoprecipitated and input samples underwent qPCR analysis to identify DNA enrichments. ChIP results were presented as fold changes by dividing ChIP signals with the Ab by those with IgG, which were defined as one. The primers utilized in ChIP-qPCR are listed in Supplementary Table 3. 

# EMSA analyses

Full-length CDS of HY5 and CaM2 was PCR-amplified from tomato cDNA (Supplementary Table 1). The His-tagged HY5 and CaM2 fusion proteins were affinity purified56. Oligonucleotide probes were end-labeled with biotin following the instructions of Biotin 3’ End DNA Labeling Kit (Thermo Fisher, 89818) and converted to double-stranded DNA. Protein-DNA complexes were analyzed following the instructions of Light Shift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Fisher, 20148)61 with minor modifications. Briefly, in the EMSAs, verifying the effect of CaM2 on the DNA-binding capacity of HY5 in vitro, biotin-labeled probes were incubated in binding buffer $( 5 \mathsf { m M C a C l } _ { 2 } ,$ , 5mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 50 mM KCl, $2 . 5 \%$ glycerol, and $\bf { 1 0 m M }$ EDTA) with or without proteins at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 30 min. The probes used for EMSAs are detailed in Supplementary Table 4. 

# Dual-luciferase assays

For dual-luciferase assays, the promoters of LOXF, AOS, AOC, HY5 and GLR3.5, and full-length coding sequences (CDS) of CaM2, HY5, $H Y 5 ^ { H }$ , and $H Y 5 ^ { N }$ were PCR-amplified (Supplementary Table 1). These promoters and CDS were subsequently inserted into the pGreen II 0800- LUC and pGreen II 62-SK vectors, respectively. All recombinant plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101, and mixtures of A. tumefaciens with pGreen II 62-SK vectors and pGreen II 0800-LUC at a ratio of 10:1 were infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. Infected tobacco plants were exposed to WL or RL conditions. Three days post-infiltration, firefly luciferase (LUC) and Renilla luciferase (REN) activities were assayed using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega, E1910). The ratio of LUC/REN of the empty vector (EV) plus promoter was defined as one. The regulatory effect of each protein-promoter interaction was validated through at least three independent experiments. 

# Split-luciferase assays

For split-luciferase (split-LUC) assays, the sequence of $H Y S ^ { \Delta C B D }$ was generated using a Fast Mutagenesis Kit (Vazyme, C214-01). The fulllength sequences of HY5, HY5H, HY5N , HY5ΔCBD, and CaM2 were cloned into the pDNOR-207 vector using the Gateway BP Clonase (Invitrogen, 11789020) and then transformed into pCAMBIA-GW-nLUC and pCAMBIA-GW-cLUC using the Gateway LR Clonase (Invitrogen, 11791020), respectively. Co-transfection of nLUC (N-terminal fragment of firefly luciferase) and cLUC (C-terminal fragment of firefly luciferase) recombinant vectors was performed in N. benthamiana leaves. At 48 h post-infiltration, split-LUC assay images were captured using an HRPCS5 Photon Counting camera (Photek) 10 min after the abaxial sides of leaves were sprayed with 1 mM luciferin (MedChemExpress, 115144-35-9). The relative luminescence units (RLU) were calculated using Image32 software (Photek). Primers used for construction are listed in Supplementary Table 1. 

# BiFC assays

For BiFC assays, full-length CDS of CaM2 and HY5 were cloned into p2YC and p2YN provided by Prof C. Mao (Zhejiang University, Hangzhou, China), constructed to be fused with a C-terminal HA tag upstream of the yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) sequences, and then transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Mixtures of A. tumefaciens harboring p2YC-CaM2 and p2YN-HY5 were infiltrated into the leaves of transgenic N. benthamiana expressing nucleus-located signaling (RFP-H2B) with an empty vector serving as a negative control. At $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post-infiltration, the fluorescence images were captured using a Nikon A1 confocal microscope. The excitation/emission wavelengths were $4 8 8 \mathrm { n m } / 5 0 0$ to 530 nm for green fluorescent protein (GFP), 514 nm/520 to $5 6 0 \mathsf { n m }$ for YFP, and 561 nm/580 to $6 2 0 \mathsf { n m }$ for red fluorescent protein (RFP). Primers used for construction are listed in Supplementary Table 1. 

# Pull-down assays

For pull-down assays, full-length sequences of CaM2, HY5, HY5H , HY5N and $H Y S ^ { \Delta C B D }$ were PCR-amplified (Supplementary Table 1). GST-CaM2, His-HY5, His-HY5H , His-HY5N , and His-HY5ΔCBD fusion proteins were affinity-purified according to previous methods62. Total $1 0 \mu \mathbf { g }$ of GST and GST-CaM2 proteins were pre-incubated with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ glutathione agarose beads (Thermo Fisher, 16100) in $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ pull-down buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl at pH 7.5, 100 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA and $10 \%$ glycerol) with $0 . 5 \%$ Triton X-100 at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h with gentle shaking. The agarose beads were harvested by centrifugation at $1 0 0 0 g$ for 1 min and then incubated with ${ 5 0 0 } \mu \mathrm { L }$ pull-down buffer supplemented with $5 \mu \ g$ Histagged proteins with gentle shaking at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. The agarose beads were collected and washed four times with the pull-down buffer containing $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100. Bound proteins by agarose were released by boiling in $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ 2×SDS-PAGE loading buffer for 5 min and analyzed by Western Blot with an anti-His antibody (Thermo Fisher, MA1-21315) and an anti-GST antibody (Thermo Fisher, MA4-004). 

# Co-IP assays

For Co-IP assays, binary vectors pCAMBIA2300-HY5-HA and pCAM-BIA2300-CaM2-GFP were expressed in the leaves of N. benthamiana for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ through Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, using an empty vector as a negative control63. The leaves were then infiltrated with $2 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ for 2 h. Each GFP-tagged soluble protein was immunoprecipitation in 1 mL Co-IP buffer ( $5 0 \mathsf { m M }$ Tris-HCl at pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, $0 . 5 \%$ Triton, $2 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L } 0 . 4 \ M$ DTT, 2 µL 1 M ${ \mathrm { N a } } _ { 3 } { \mathrm { V O } } _ { 3 } ,$ 2 µL 1 M NaF, and 1×protease inhibitor [Sigma-Aldrich]) with $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ high-affinity GFP-trap magnetic agarose beads (Chromotek, AB_2631358). After 3 h incubation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with gentle shaking, the agarose beads were washed four times with washing buffer $( 5 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ Tris-HCl at pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, and $0 . 1 \%$ Triton), and once with 50 mM Tris-HCl at pH 7.5. Bound proteins by agarose beads were released by boiling in 50 μL 2×SDS-PAGE loading buffer for 5 min and analyzed by Western Blot with an anti-HA antibody (Abcam, ab187915) and an anti-GFP antibody (Genescript, A01704). In addition, $5 0 \mu \nu$ of samples in Co-IP buffer were prepared as a protein loading control prior to the addition of agarose beads. Primers for vector construction are listed in Supplementary Table 1. 

# Electric potential recordings

The tomato seedlings at the three-leaf stage were used for electrical activity measurement. At $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ post-inoculation with RKN, electric potential recordings were detected on the surface of the stems of WT with 12 h/12 h (dark/light) treatment or in continuous darkness, and the stems of WT and hy5#4, as well as the scion stems of reciprocal grafts between WT and hy5#4, via the method described by Wang et al.12.
