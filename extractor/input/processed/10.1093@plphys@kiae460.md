# Abstract

Sphingolipid homeostatic regulation is important for balancing plant life and death. Plant cells finely tune sphingolipid biosynthesis to ensure sufficient levels to support growth through their basal functions as major components of endomembranes and the plasma membrane. Conversely, accumulation of sphingolipid biosynthetic intermediates, long-chain bases (LCBs) and ceramides, is associated with programmed cell death. Limiting these apoptotic intermediates is important for cell viability, while overriding homeostatic regulation permits cells to generate elevated LCBs and ceramides to respond to pathogens to elicit the hypersensitive response in plant immunity. Key to sphingolipid homeostasis is serine palmitoyltransferase (SPT), an endoplasmic reticulum– associated, multi-subunit enzyme catalyzing the first step in the biosynthesis of LCBs, the defining feature of sphingolipids. Across eukaryotes, SPT interaction with its negative regulator Orosomucoid-like (ORM) is critical for sphingolipid biosynthetic homeostasis. The recent cryo-electron microscopy structure of the Arabidopsis SPT complex indicates that ceramides bind ORMs to competitively inhibit SPT activity. This system provides a sensor for intracellular ceramide concentrations for sphingolipid homeostatic regulation. Combining the newly elucidated Arabidopsis SPT structure and mutant characterization, we present a model for the role of the 2 functionally divergent Arabidopsis ceramide synthase classes to produce ceramides that form repressive (trihydroxy LCB-ceramides) or nonrepressive (dihydroxy LCB-ceramides) ORM interactions to influence SPT activity. We describe how sphingolipid biosynthesis is regulated by the interplay of ceramide synthases with ORM-SPT when “enough is enough” and override homeostatic suppression when “enough is not enough” to respond to environmental stimuli such as microbial pathogen attack.

# Introduction

Sphingolipids are essential eukaryotic lipids whose biosynthetic regulation impacts the life and death of cells. In plants, sphingolipids are abundant endomembrane lipids and the major lipid class in the plasma membrane (Haslam and Feussner 2022). As structural membrane components, sphingolipids contribute to plant resilience to abiotic stresses and are involved in microbial pathogen recognition and immunity through their enrichment in the apoplastic leaflet of the plasma membrane (Cassim et al. 2020; Carmona-Salazar et al. 2021). Sphingolipids are also major lipid components of plasma membrane microdomains and contribute to cell surface processes through their interactions with microdomain-associated proteins (Cassim et al. 2020; Carmona-Salazar et al. 2021). In addition, the sphingolipid biosynthetic intermediates long-chain bases (LCBs) and ceramides and their phosphorylated forms are associated with triggering programmed cell death (PCD) and other signaling roles, including drought-mediated guard cell closure (Huby et al. 2020).

A fundamental question for all living organisms is how does a cell “know” how much of a given lipid class is needed for viability and growth or for responding to environmental stimuli? This question is especially critical for sphingolipids because of the PCD-associated bioactivity of LCBs and ceramides (Abbas et al. 1994; Liang et al. 2003; Berkey et al. 2012). In plants, PCD is an

important physiological process for the hypersensitive response to microbial pathogens that promotes localized cell death to reduce infection of the entire plant (Pitsili et al. 2020). As such, sphingolipid biosynthesis requires strong homeostatic regulation to balance the requirement of sphingolipids for cell viability and growth, while limiting the accumulation of their PCD-inducing biosynthetic intermediates until needed for pathogen resistance.

While sphingolipid homeostasis involves the regulatory balance of biosynthetic and catabolic reactions, this review will focus on emerging information regarding how plant cells sense intracellular sphingolipid concentrations and adjust de novo biosynthesis to meet the needs of endomembranes and signaling processes. We also integrate knowledge from the study of Arabidopsis sphingolipid mutants and transgenic lines and the recent cryo-electron microscopy structure of Arabidopsis serine palmitoyltransferase (SPT), the enzyme that catalyzes the first step in LCB biosynthesis (Liu et al. 2023). Using this information, we provide mechanistic insights into the reversible regulation of sphingolipid biosynthesis. We also provide a model that highlights the importance of a single LCB hydroxyl group for mediating sphingolipid homeostasis and for deregulation of sphingolipid biosynthesis to enable responses to environmental perturbations, such as microbial pathogen infection.

• Ceramide accumulation resulting from deregulation of sphingolipid long-chain base biosynthesis is associated with PCD and immunity to microbial pathogens.

# Basics of sphingolipid biosynthesis

The long-chain base (LCB) is the unique and defining component of sphingolipids in all eukaryotic organisms (Fig. 1, A and B) (Luttgeharm et al. 2016b). LCBs arise from the condensation of serine and palmitoyl-CoA catalyzed by the endoplasmic reticulum (ER)-localized SPT to form 3-ketosphinganine (Hanada 2003). The SPT product is subsequently reduced by 3-ketosphinganine reductase to form the LCB sphinganine (or dihydrosphingosine), an

18-carbon molecule with 2 hydroxyl groups (or “dihydroxy”) at the C-1 and C-3 positions (Chao et al. 2011) (Fig. 2). Sphinganine can be further modified by hydroxylation and desaturation to form up to 9 different LCBs in plants (Markham et al. 2013). Most notably, hydroxylation of sphinganine at the C-4 position by the LCB C-4 hydroxylase yields the tri-hydroxy or 3 hydroxyl-containing LCB phytosphingosine (Chen et al. 2008). The dihydroxy and trihydroxy LCBs represent the 2 major classes of LCBs. As discussed below, the structural difference between these LCBs (i.e. 1 hydroxyl group) has major implications for sphingolipid metabolism and its homeostatic regulation. In addition to hydroxylation, dihydroxy and trihydroxy LCBs can also be desaturated at the Δ8 position in the cis or trans configuration (Chen et al. 2012). This reaction is catalyzed by sphingoid LCB Δ8 desaturase that is believed to use a ceramide-bound LCB as its substrate (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). In addition, plant dihydroxy LCBs can also be diunsaturated with a double bond introduced in the trans configuration at Δ4 position, followed by Δ8 cis or trans desaturation (Michaelson et al. 2009). The plant Δ4 desaturase that catalyzes this reaction is believed to use the free LCB sphinganine as its substrate (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b).

LCBs are typically considered intermediates in sphingolipid metabolism, with the vast majority linked at the LCB amine group to a fatty acid. The resulting molecule, referred to as a ceramide, has 2 hydrocarbon chains and is structurally similar to diacylglycerol found in glycerophospholipids (e.g. phosphatidylcholine) (Markham et al. 2013) (Fig. 1A; Fig. 2). Ceramides form the hydrophobic portion of glycosphingolipids that constitute the bulk of sphingolipids in the endomembranes and plasma membranes of plant cells (Fig. 2). Ceramides are formed by the activity of ceramide synthases that catalyze the condensation of a fatty acid (acyl)-coenzyme A (CoA) with the LCB amine group (Fig. 1A; Fig. 2). Two classes of ceramide synthases, referred to as Class I and II ceramide synthases, typically occur in plants (Markham et al. 2011; Ternes et al. 2011; Luttgeharm et al. 2015). These 2 classes are distinguished by strikingly different substrate specificities for LCBs and fatty acyl-CoAs. In Arabidopsis, Class I ceramide synthase represented by the “LAG1 homolog $2 ^ { " }$ or LOH2 ceramide synthase displays strong substrate specificity for dihydroxy LCBs and C16 fatty acyl-CoAs (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). The Class II ceramide synthases in Arabidopsis are represented by LOH1 and LOH3 ceramide synthases (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). In contrast to LOH2 ceramide synthase, LOH1 and LOH3 ceramide synthases have strong substrate specificity for trihydroxy LCBs and very longchain fatty acyl (VLCFA)-CoAs (C20–C26), particularly 24:0-CoA and 26:0-CoA in the case of LOH1 ceramide synthase (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). In addition, LOH1 ceramide synthase, whose corresponding gene is more highly expressed than LOH3, is nearly 300-fold more sensitive to inhibition by the

mycotoxin fumonisin B1 (FB1) than the LOH2 and LOH3 ceramide synthases (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). The differing substrate specificities of LOH2 and LOH1 ceramide synthases are reflected in the dichotomy of ceramides found in Arabidopsis: dihydroxy LCB-C16 fatty acid and trihydroxy LCB-VLCFA ceramides (Markham et al. 2006; Markham and Jaworski 2007). In addition, the LOH2 ceramide synthase-derived dihydroxy LCB-C16 fatty acid ceramides are typically more enriched in GlcCers, while the trihydroxy LCB-VLCFA ceramides are more prevalent in GIPCs in Arabidopsis (Markham et al. 2006; Markham and Jaworski 2007). This illustrates how the presence or absence of the C-4 hydroxyl group is important for the metabolic fate of LCBs due to the distinct substrate specificities of ceramide synthases.

# SPT—primary regulatory point for sphingolipid biosynthesis

SPT is considered to be the primary point for the regulation of sphingolipid biosynthesis across all eukaryotic organisms. While genes encoding SPT subunits may be subject to transcriptional regulation under certain cellular conditions (Linn et al. 2006; Cassim et al. 2020), post-translational processes are recognized as a principal mechanism for regulation of SPT activity (Davis et al. 2018). This allows for more dynamic fine-tuning of SPT activity in response to fluctuating intracellular sphingolipid levels and for rapid responses to abiotic and biotic stresses.

SPT is a multi-subunit, ER-localized enzyme that uses a pyridoxal phosphate cofactor bound to its LCB2 subunit to catalyze the

condensation of serine to palmitoyl-CoA (Luttgeharm et al. 2016b). The LCB2 subunit interacts with the LCB1 subunit to form the SPT active site. Arabidopsis has 2 LCB2 genes (AtLCB2a; AtLCB2b) and a single LCB1 gene (AtLCB1) (Tamura et al. 2001; Chen et al. 2006; Dietrich et al. 2008). More recently, a third SPT subunit, referred to as the small subunit of SPT or ssSPT, was identified in mammals (Han et al. 2009). The Arabidopsis ssSPT homologs are 56-amino acid polypeptides encoded by 2 genes: ssSPTa and ssSPTb (Kimberlin et al. 2013). Mutants that have complete loss of any of these subunits, including lcb2alcb2b, lcb1, and ssspta mutants, display pollen lethality, and strong RNAi suppression of these genes results in severe dwarfing (Chen et al. 2006; Dietrich et al. 2008; Teng et al. 2008; Kimberlin et al. 2013). These phenotypes indicate that sphingolipids are not only essential in Arabidopsis but are also important for sustaining growth.

In addition to its core subunits, SPT physically interacts with the ER-associated Orosomucoid-like or ORM protein, which, in response to elevating ceramide levels, inhibits SPT activity (Davis et al. 2018). (Note: ORM proteins in mammals are referred to as “ORMDLs”.) Gene-edited mutants for the 2 ORM genes in Arabidopsis AtORM1 and AtORM2 display uncontrolled LCB biosynthesis, resulting in nonviable seeds that hyperaccumulate free ceramides (Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). Notably, the ceramides that accumulate are enriched in dihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides derived from LOH2 ceramide synthases (Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). These responses are phenocopied by the expression of a gene encoding a mutant LCB1 that lacks its transmembrane domain (TMD) in an Atlcb1 background (Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020), indicating that negative regulation of SPT is conferred through physical interaction of ORM with the LCB1 TMD. Overall, these observations indicate that ORM-mediated negative SPT regulation is essential for suppressing sphingolipid accumulation to nonlethal concentrations.

The recent cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure of the Arabidopsis SPT complex has provided details about the stoichiometry and arrangement of the SPT subunits and inhibitory interaction between SPT and ORM (Fig. 3). Similar to mammalian SPT (Li et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2021), the Arabidopsis SPT-ORM1 complex is organized as a dimer, with each protomer having a single subunit of LCB1, LCB2b, ssSPTa, and ORM1 (Liu et al. 2023). In the mammalian complex, the transmembrane helix of LCB1 is domain swapped (Fig. 3). Apart from some differences in their N and C termini, the overall structures of the 4 subunits of the Arabidopsis SPT-ORM1 complex are highly conserved with those of the human SPT-ORMDL (Li et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2021; Liu et al. 2023). The membrane-associated and spanning domains of the individual subunits are analogous to those in the human and yeast SPT-ORM complexes (Li et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2021; Fiorillo et al. 2022; Xie et al. 2024). LCB1 is tethered to the membrane by a single N-terminal TMD, while the ORM subunit has 4 transmembrane helices, the third of which is in extensive contact with the LCB1 TMD from the opposing protomer. ssSPTa has 1 transmembrane helix flanked by a cytosolic interfacial helix. Through this interfacial helix, ssSPTa interacts with the interfacial helix of LCB2. This interaction stabilizes membrane association of LCB2, which lacks any membrane-spanning domains (Liu et al. 2023).

Mutational studies implicated M28 of human ssSPTa and V25 of human ssSPTb in acyl-CoA substrate chain length preference (Harmon et al. 2013). # How do cells regulate sphingolipid homeostasis?

Homoeostatic regulation of sphingolipid lipid synthesis is critical for sustaining cell function and growth and for the ability to rapidly respond to perturbations, such as those arising from environmental stimuli, including microbial pathogen attack. Evidence from Arabidopsis mutants and transgenic lines, combined with the SPT cryo-EM structure, supports a mechanistic model for sphingolipid homeostatic regulation in plants. We also describe how this model can be applied to understanding how plants respond to microbial pathogen infection.

# Insights from Arabidopsis mutants and transgenics and mycotoxin treatments

By combining sphingolipidomics (the comprehensive profiling of the content and composition of sphingolipid chemical forms) with the evaluation of Arabidopsis mutants and transgenics, a number of insights have been gained regarding perturbation of sphingolipid homeostasis in plants (Kimberlin et al. 2013; Luttgeharm et al. 2015; Li et al. 2016; Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020).

Notably, Arabidopsis mutants or engineered lines that have strongly enhanced LCB flux through the LOH2 ceramide synthase display greater than twofold higher sphingolipid concentrations on a per-mass basis (Chen et al. 2008; Luttgeharm et al. 2015). This phenotype is also observed in lines defective in C-4 LCB hydroxylation that lack or have reduced ability to make trihydroxy LCBs via mutations that knock out or suppress expression of the sphingoid base hydroxylase SBH genes (Chen et al. 2008). A similar phenotype is observed in lines engineered for LOH2 overexpression (Luttgeharm et al. 2015). In both cases, plants hyperaccumulate a predominance of sphingolipids containing ceramides with dihydroxy LCBs paired largely with C16 fatty acids. These lines are dwarfed, where the extent of dwarfing correlates with levels of sphingolipids arising from the LOH2 ceramide synthase, and expression of several pathogen resistance genes is elevated. Conversely, overexpression of LOH1 or LOH3 ceramide synthases, with strong specificity for trihydroxy LCBs, do not hyperaccumulate sphingolipids. These findings suggest that enrichment of dihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides generated by LOH2 ceramide synthase are unable to feedback regulate SPT activity, in contrast to trihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides generated by LOH1 or LOH3 ceramide synthases. Recent findings with a Physcomitrella patens sbh mutant unable to produce trihydroxy LCBs are consistent (Steinberger et al. 2021). This mutant had ${ \sim } 2 . 5$ times higher sphingolipid content than the wild type, but the sphingolipid hyperaccumulation was completely suppressed by exogenously supplied trihydroxy-LCBs (Steinberger et al. 2021).

The mycotoxin FB1 has been widely used for studies of PCD in plants (Stone et al. 2000; Zeng et al. 2020). FB1 is a potent inhibitor of ceramide synthases, particularly the Arabidopsis LOH1 ceramide synthase (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). Many mutants that display reduced FB1-induced PCD response have been associated with defects in SPT subunits, consistent with a role of sphingolipid accumulation in PCD induction (Shi et al. 2007; Teng et al. 2008; Shao et al. 2020). Because of this, FB1 has emerged as a tool for studying mechanisms underlying sphingolipid biosynthetic perturbation. FB1 treatment of Arabidopsis not only results in strong increases in free LCBs but also a large increase in dihydroxy ceramides derived from the LOH2 ceramide synthase (Shi et al. 2007; Teng et al. 2008; Kimberlin et al. 2013, 2016; Shao et al. 2020). This further points to the role of LOH2 ceramide synthase in generating ceramides (or subsequent glycosphingolipids) that antagonize the ability of ORMs to function to negatively regulate SPT.

Interestingly, RNAi suppression of ORMs accentuated FB1’s cytotoxicity and accumulation of LCBs and LOH2-derived ceramides with dihydroxy LCBs and C16 fatty acids (Kimberlin et al. 2016). In addition, Arabidopsis ORM RNAi lines displayed increased LOH2 ceramide synthase activity and largely decreased Class II LOH1/ LOH3 ceramide synthase activity (Kimberlin et al. 2016). Thus, in addition to negative regulation of SPT, ORMs may also influence LOH2 ceramide synthase activity for a dynamic interplay between ORM and LOH2 ceramide synthase in sphingolipid homeostatic regulation, possibly through a phosphorylation/dephosphorylation mechanism, as has been shown in regulation of S. cerevisiae ceramide synthase activity (Fresques et al. 2015).

# Structure-guided insights into ORM-mediated SPT regulation

ORMs are recognized across eukaryotes as the primary SPT regulator (Davis et al. 2018). How ORMs perceive cellular sphingolipid status and mediate reversible negative regulation of LCB biosynthesis had not been well understood, particularly in mammals and plants. The S. cerevisiae ORM contains an N-terminal extension that is

subject to reversible phosphorylation in response to cellular sphingolipid concentrations (Roelants et al. 2011). Biochemical studies suggest that phosphorylation affects ORM-SPT interaction to provide homeostatic regulation of the SPT-ORM complex, although this was not observed in the recent cryo-EM structure of the S. cerevisiae ORM (Xie et al. 2024). The N-terminal extension is notably absent from the human ORMDL and plant ORM (Davis et al. 2018; Han et al. 2019). Therefore, alternative mechanisms were proposed in these eukaryotes. One hypothesis is that ORMDLs/ORMs sense ceramide concentrations through binding of these biosynthetic intermediates to reduce SPT activity. Evidence to support this hypothesis come from in vitro activity assays of HeLa cell lysates in which added ceramides of the natural D-erythro stereoisomer confer SPT activity inhibition (Davis et al. 2019). Similar results, consistent with direct ceramide interaction with ORMDLs/ORMs, were observed for suppression of S. cerevisiae SPT activity (Davis et al. 2019). Direct evidence for ceramide sensing by ORM to regulate SPT was obtained from the cryo-EM structure of the Arabidopsis ORM-SPT complex (Liu et al. 2023). This structure contained ceramide bound to ORM (Fig. 3). To determine if this binding has a regulatory function, the Arabidopsis SPT complexes, with or without ORM, were purified from HEK cells and assayed for activity in the presence of ceramides containing different LCBs (Liu et al. 2023). Short-chain ceramides (i.e. LCBs paired with non-natural C6 fatty acids) were used to maintain ceramide solubility. No SPT inhibition was observed in the absence of ORM or in the presence of ORM without added ceramide (Liu et al. 2023). However, inclusion of ORM and C6 ceramides containing the trihydroxy LCB phytosphingosine (t18:0; “phytoceramides”) resulted in maximal SPT inhibition (Liu et al. 2023). C6 ceramides containing the dihydroxy LCB dihydrosphingosine (d18:0; “dihydroceramides”) did not inhibit SPT, and C6 ceramides containing the Δ4 unsaturated dihydroxy LCB sphingosine (d18:1Δ4trans) conferred intermediate SPT inhibition, with approximately tenfold higher half-maximal inhibitory concentration than phytosphingosine-containing ceramides (Liu et al. 2023). These results collectively indicate that the Arabidopsis ORM senses intracellular ceramide concentrations by binding ceramides and suppressing SPT activity. The structure of the ceramide, particularly that of the LCB component, strongly influences the SPT inhibition by ORM (Fig. 4).

More in-depth investigation of the cryo-EM structure suggests a mechanism for the inhibitory effects of ceramide-bound ORM on SPT activity. The N terminus of ORM interacts with the N terminus of LCB2 to affect the entry of the palmitoyl-CoA substrate into the SPT active site (Liu et al. 2023) (Fig. 4). When no ceramide is bound to ORM, the N terminus of ORM is flexible and allows the entry of palmitoyl-CoA into the SPT active site (Liu et al. 2023). Conversely, the binding of ceramide to ORM results in the formation of a hybrid $\beta$ -sheet in the ORM N terminus that interacts with LCB2 to block palmitoyl-CoA entry into the SPT active site (Liu et al. 2023). The latter scenario is the ORM-induced inhibitory state that exists during conditions of elevated, excess intracellular ceramide concentrations (Fig. 4). During conditions of sphingolipid demand, ceramide concentrations decline to a point where ceramide binding to ORM becomes limiting and SPT is shifted to a non-inhibitory state.

LOH1 and LOH3 ceramide synthases primarily generate VLCFAcontaining ceramides found in GIPCs, the major Arabidopsis glycosphingolipid. As such, flux through these ceramide synthases is important for the maintenance of growth and other basal functions of sphingolipids in cells. Given that plants are sessile, a need also exists for rapid responses to environmental perturbations. The ability to shift LCB flux through the LOH2 ceramide synthase to generate dihydroxy LCB-ceramides would provide a mechanism for overriding ORM inhibition of SPT to derepress sphingolipid biosynthesis. Thus, the accumulation of dihydroxy LCB-ceramides via LOH2 ceramide synthase would maintain SPT in a nonrepressed state to provide a burst of bioactive LCBs (dihydroxy and trihydroxy) as well as ceramides for PCD-associated processes, such as the hypersensitive response for pathogen defense. It has also been proposed that induction of flux through LOH2 ceramide synthase may provide a “safety valve” or shunt for suppressing the bioactivity of LCBs and ceramides by directing LCB and ceramide flux to a less bioactive

form as GlcCers (Luttgeharm et al. 2015). Triggers for enhanced LOH2 ceramide flux in response to environmental stimuli are not fully clear, although LOH2 transcriptional upregulation has been shown, for example, with FB1 treatment and with large white butterfly Pieris brassicae egg extract treatments (Groux et al. 2022; Zeng et al. 2022). In both cases, these treatments lead to PCD and accumulation LOH2-type ceramides (Groux et al. 2022; Zeng et al. 2022). In rice, LOH2 ceramide synthase gene expression increases in response to drought and cold stress. It remains possible that interactions (direct or indirect) between ORM and LOH2 ceramide synthase regulate production of dihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides (Kawahara et al. 2016). Overall, this model and its supporting evidence emphasize the importance of a single hydroxyl group in LCBs (dihydroxy vs trihydroxy LCBs) and the divergent substrate specificities of Class I and II ceramide synthases for homeostatic regulation of de novo sphingolipid biosynthesis.

# Implications for microbial pathogen resistance

The sphingolipid biosynthetic pathway increasingly has been recognized for its central importance in microbial pathogen immunity (Berkey et al. 2012). This pathway is a target of mycotoxins, such as FB1 and AAL toxin, produced by fungal pathogens including Fusarium moniliforme and Alternaria alternata (Abbas et al. 1994).

Alterations in sphingolipid biosynthesis may influence membrane integrity, which is important for the functions of cell surface immune receptors, known as pattern recognition receptors (Couto and Zipfel 2016). These act to sense and detect invading microbes by recognizing conserved microbial molecules called pathogen or microbe-associated molecular patterns (Couto and Zipfel 2016). Of particular importance to SPT- and ceramide synthase– mediated sphingolipid homeostasis are the numerous reports that LCB and ceramide accumulation trigger PCD and the regulation of defense responses against pathogens. Notably, Pseudomonas syringae infection has been shown to induce accumulation of LCBs and ceramide-containing trihydroxy LCBs (Peer et al. 2010; Magnin-Robert et al. 2015) with associated downstream effects on plant defense. PCD induction underpins the hypersensitive response that limits the systemic transmission of pathogens (Pitsili et al. 2020). The PCD association of LCB and ceramide accumulation has been linked to signaling pathways that involve $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ , generation of reactive oxygen species, and the activation of mitogen-activated protein kinases (Liang et al. 2003; Chen et al. 2006; Saucedo-García et al. 2011; Thuleau et al. 2013; Shao et al. 2020). In the case of FB1 treatment and LOH2 ceramide synthase overexpression, PCD is associated with ENHANCED DISEASE SUSCEPTIBILITY1 (EDS1)- PHYTOALEXIN DEFICIENT4 (PAD4) and salicylic acid (SA) biosynthesis (Bi et al. 2014; König et al. 2022; Zeng et al. 2022). Regarding SPT regulation, Arabidopsis ORM RNAi and mutants displayed early senescence phenotypes, hydrogen peroxide production, increased expression of pathogendefense genes, and enhanced resistance to Pseudomonas syringae strain DG3 infection (Li et al. 2016; Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). In addition, through the characterization of mutants, CSI and CSII ceramide synthases were found to have differing effects on the EDS1-PAD4 and SA-mediated defense pathways (Zeng et al. 2022).

The model proposed has direct implications for the regulation of LCB and ceramide production for plant defense and immunity pathways. Enhanced flux through the LOH2 ceramide synthase would override ORM suppression of SPT activity to not only enhance production of ceramides from the LOH2 ceramide synthase

but also increase total production of free LCBs from increased SPT activity, similar to the observed effect of FB1 treatment (Kimberlin et al. 2013, 2016) (Fig. 5B). Though not yet determined, dihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides may have an antagonistic effect on the binding of LOH1/LOH3-generated trihydroxy-containing ceramides to ORM for SPT activity repression. This would allow the continued production of LOH1/LOH3 ceramides with trihydroxy LCBs and VLCFAs, which also have demonstrated apoptotic activity (Magnin-Robert et al. 2015).

# Balancing biosynthetic and catabolic reactions for sphingolipid homeostasis

Although the focus of this review is the regulation of de novo sphingolipid biosynthesis, the catabolism or turnover of long-chain bases, ceramides, and glycosphingolipids contribute to sphingolipid homeostasis and signaling. For example, genes for LCB catabolism were transcriptionally upregulated in Arabidopsis ORM mutants that hyperaccumulate ceramides (Li et al. 2016; Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). LCB catabolism requires phosphorylation by LCB kinases for degradation of LCB-1-phosphates (LCB1-Ps) to long-chain aldehydes and ethanolamine-phosphates by LCB-1-P lyase (Lambour et al. 2022). Arabidopsis dpl1 null mutants, defective in the gene encoding this enzyme, have no obvious growth phenotypes or altered sphingolipid profiles under optimal growth conditions but do show enhanced PCD induction in response to FB1 treatment (Tsegaye et al. 2007). The reversible conversion of LCBs to LCB-1-Ps by kinases and phosphatases contributes not only to LCB catabolism but also to LCB bioactivity (Lambour et al. 2022). Consistent with its role in catabolism, Arabidopsis mutants defective in ORM suppression of SPT activity accumulate LCB-1-Ps to concentrations ${ > } 2 0$ -fold higher than wildtype lines (Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). LCB phosphorylation also has been linked to reducing LCB-associated PCD induction, which contributes to microbial pathogen responses (Alden et al. 2011). This concept, however, has more recently been put into question by the demonstration that LCB-induced cell death in Arabidopsis

correlates with increases in free LCBs but is not antagonized by enhanced concentrations of phosphorylated LCBs (Glenz et al. 2019). LCB-1-Ps have also been shown to have distinct signaling functions, including second messenger-type activity in ABA control of guard cell closure (Ng et al. 2001; Coursol et al. 2003).

The conversion of ceramides to ceramide-1-Ps by ceramide kinase activity has also been shown to reduce the ability of ceramides to trigger PCD (Liang et al. 2003; Bi et al. 2014). This effect has been evidenced by the increased accumulation of free ceramides and an early senescence phenotype of the Arabidopsis ceramide kinase mutant acd5 (Bi et al. 2014). These phenotypes are accentuated when acd5 is combined with the acer mutant that is defective in alkaline ceramidase that catalyzes the turnover of ceramides to its component LCBs and fatty acids (Li et al. 2022).

# Summary and open questions

The reviewed findings offer strong evidence supporting the role of ORM proteins in conjunction with the distinct products generated by Class I and Class II ceramide synthases in regulating the repression and derepression of SPT activity. The model points to the binding of trihydroxy LCB ceramides from Class II ceramide synthases (LOH1 and LOH3 ceramide synthases) to ORM confer SPT repression. Conversely, dihydroxy LCB ceramides from Class I ceramide synthases (LOH2 ceramide synthase) to ORM results in reduced repression of SPT. This provides a mechanism for homeostatic regulation of SPT to generate enough sphingolipids for basal cellular activities and for repression of SPT by elevated trihydroxy LCB ceramides “when enough is enough.” At times when cells need “more than enough” sphingolipids, such as in response to environmental perturbations, enhanced production of dihydroxy LCB ceramides provides a mechanism for overriding ORM repression of SPT activity. While this model is compelling, questions remain about ORM function and the mechanism for ORM-mediated control of sphingolipid homeostasis.

# What structural features of ceramides are important for ORM repression of SPT activity?

Direct evidence for the differing abilities of dihydroxy and trihydroxy LCB-containing ceramides to confer ORM-dependent repression of SPT come from in vitro assays with the more soluble C6 fatty acid ceramides (Liu et al. 2023). It is not clear, however, whether ceramide fatty acid chain lengths contribute to the effectiveness of ceramides to confer ORM-dependent regulation of SPT. LOH2 ceramide synthases in dicots typically produce ceramides with C16 fatty acids, while LOH1 ceramide synthases typically generate ceramides with $\geq \mathbf { C } 2 2$ fatty acids (Luttgeharm et al. 2016a, 2016b). It also not clear if Δ8 unsaturation and the combined Δ4 and Δ8 unsaturation often found in plant LCBs affect ORM function (Haslam and Feussner 2022). To test this and also extend in vitro findings to the plant, it may be possible to engineer novel LCB structures into Arabidopsis ceramide synthase mutants and assess responses to homeostatic perturbations, such as those resulting from FB1 treatment and genuine pathogen infection. A more complete structural understanding of how ORMs bind ceramides and how ceramides of different structures affect the formation of ORM repressive structures remains.

# Is ORM regulation of SPT for sphingolipid homeostasis important for cellular processes beyond housekeeping functions, including development?

Mammals and plants have multiple isoforms of ORMs/ORMDLs, LCB2s, and ssSPTs. In humans, a number of neural diseases arise

from mutations in different SPT subunits that impair ORMDLmediated SPT regulation (Liu et al. 2022; Srivastava et al. 2023; Dohrn et al. 2024; Naruse et al. 2024). In Arabidopsis, complete ORM knockout impairs seed viability, and mutants from combinations of edits in both Arabidopsis ORM genes plants had severely altered development (Gonzalez-Solis et al. 2020). Also, mutations in 1 of the 2 ORM genes of the legume Aeschynomene evenia were recently shown to confer ceramide accumulation, altered lateral root development, and reduced rhizobia-induced root nodulation (Nouwen et al. 2024). These findings suggest that perturbations in sphingolipid homeostasis in specific cell types and at specific developmental stages can have profound effects on processes beyond cellular housekeeping. Whether these effects result from subtle alterations in membrane properties and associated protein activities or from direct effects on developmental pathways is not clear. This may also have implications for plant immunity if insect or microbial effectors or fungal mycotoxins have differential effects on specific plant ORM and SPT subunit isoforms, as recently indicated with PCD-inducing insect egg extract (Groux et al. 2022).

# Do ORMs have any additional functions mediated by binding to other partners?

ORM has generally been considered to function exclusively as a sphingolipid metabolic regulator via its physical interactions with SPT (Davis et al. 2018). Two recent studies in Arabidopsis have questioned this view. One study showed that ORM RNAi lines are more resistant to Pseudomonas syringae pathogen infection, while ORM overexpression lines were more sensitive to Pseudomonas syringae (Yang et al. 2019). Evidence was also provided that ORM functions as a selective autophagy receptor, marking the immune receptor kinase FLAGELLIN-SENSING 2 (FLS2) through physical interaction for autophagic turnover (Yang et al. 2019). As such, altered levels of ORM result in increased or reduced amounts of FLS2 for immunity. A second study showed that Arabidopsis orm1 and orm2 mutants are defective in ER-associated unfolded protein response and that these proteins physically bind to and regulate the stability of a component of the ER-associated degradation complex (Wang et al. 2023). Another intriguing possibility is that ORM and LOH2 ceramide synthase physically interact to coordinate deregulation of SPT activity. Overall, these studies suggest that ORM proteins may be more general metabolic regulators in plant cells through their binding to partners beyond the SPT complex.

# Outstanding Questions

• What structural features of ceramides are important for ORM repression of SPT activity?   
• Is ORM regulation of SPT for sphingolipid homeostasis important for cellular processes beyond housekeeping functions, including development?   
• Do ORMs have any additional functions mediated by binding to partners other than SPT?   
• How is ORM regulation of SPT coordinated with responses to microbial pathogens for immunity?
