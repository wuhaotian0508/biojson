Specific plants belonging to the Solanaceae family, which have been used medicinally for 3000 years, yield pharmaceutical tropane alkaloids (TAs), including hyoscyamine, anisodamine, and scopolamine, that are essential anticholinergic drugs for treating various disorders [1]. The TA-producing plant species are from the genera Anisodus, Anthocercis, Atropa, Brugmansia, Datura, Duboisia, Hyoscyamus, Mandragora, and Scopolia (Fig. 1). Because low TA levels in plants limit the drug supply and increase manufacturing costs, for over a century, chemists have sought alternative methods for synthesizing hyoscyamine and scopolamine. While early successes such as Robinson’s syntheses were significant milestones, the high cost of chemical synthesis led to a reliance on plant cultivation for the commercial production of TAs. Metabolic engineering and synthetic biotechnology, which are based on elucidating biosynthetic pathways, now offer promising solutions for the production of TAs.

# 1. Biosynthesis of medicinal TAs

The identification of TA biosynthetic genes started in the early 1980s, and after nearly 40 years, the biosynthetic pathway of TAs has been fully elucidated in Atropa belladonna (A. belladonna), revealing thirteen biosynthetic enzymes that use ornithine and phenylalanine as precursors to produce scopolamine (Fig. 2). This pathway is divided into four modules: Module I (ornithine to tropine), Module II (phenylalanine to phenyllactylglucose), Module III (littorine formation), and Module IV (littorine to scopolamine).

Module I enzymes synthesize tropine from ornithine. Nicotine and TA biosynthesis share an initial pathway involving ornithine decarboxylase (ODC), putrescine N-methyltransferase (PMT), and N-methylputrescine oxidase (MPO), culminating in N-methyl-D1- pyrrolinium. Type III polyketide synthase (PYKS) and tropinone synthase (CYP82M3) produce tropinone from N-methyl-D1-pyrrolinium. Tropinone reductase I (TRI) then converts tropinone to tro-

pine, a precursor for medicinal TAs [2]. Module II consists of three enzymes for the biosynthesis of phenyllactylglucose from phenylalanine. Phenylalanine:4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate aminotransferase (ArAT4) converts phenylalanine into phenylpyruvate, which is subsequently reduced to form phenyllactate under catalysis by phenylpyruvic acid reductase (PPAR) [3]. Phenyllactate UDP-glycosyltransferase 1 (UGT1) catalyzes phenyllactate and UDP-glucose to produce phenyllactylglucose, the acyl donor for littorine synthesis [4]. Littorine synthase (LS), belonging to the serine carboxypeptidase-like acyltransferase family, is the only enzyme in Module III that condenses phenyllactylglucose and tropine through esterification to produce littorine [4]. Module IV, composed of three enzymes, performs scopolamine biosynthesis from littorine. Littorine mutase (CYP80F1) catalyzes the oxidation of littorine with rearrangement to generate hyoscyamine aldehyde. An oxidoreductase, named hyoscyamine aldehyde reductase (HAR) or hyoscyamine dehydrogenase (HDH), reduces hyoscyamine aldehyde into hyoscyamine. Finally, hyoscyamine $6 \beta$ -hydroxylase (H6H) catalyzes the $6 \beta$ -hydroxylation of hyoscyamine to form anisodamine and then converts anisodamine into scopolamine by epoxidation [5].

# 2. Evolution of the TA biosynthetic pathway

TAs are found in Solanaceae, Convolvulaceae, Rhizophoraceae, Erythroxylaceae, and other plant families. TAs are classified into two groups on the basis of their chemical structure: those derived from tropinone and those derived from ecgonine. Tropinonederived alkaloids, including hyoscyamine, anisodamine, scopolamine, and calystegine, are mainly found in the first three families, whereas ecgonine-derived alkaloids, such as cocaine, are restricted to Erythroxylaceae. The scattered phylogenetic distribution of TAs once led to the hypothesis of convergent evolution. Subsequent comparative genomic and enzymatic analyses have shown that Solanaceae and Erythroxylaceae acquired the capacity to produce TAs independently, each through its own ancestral pathway [6].

Within Solanaceae, only a limited number of genera produce TAs, raising questions about the evolutionary origin of TA biosynthesis in this family. Genomic analyses indicate that the early steps of the pathway, from L-ornithine to tropine, are conserved, which

In downstream steps, genes such as LS and CYP80F1 are either missing or disrupted in nonproducing species such as Capsicum annuum, S. tuberosum, and Iochroma cyaneum [7]. These lineage-specific losses provide a better explanation for the uneven distribution of TAs within Solanaceae than multiple independent evolutionary events. Together, the current evidence supports the existence of a complete TA biosynthetic pathway in the common

ancestor of Solanaceae, followed by differential gene loss during diversification. Reintroducing the missing genes into nonproducing Solanaceae plants could restore TA biosynthesis and enable new applications in metabolic engineering.

# 3. Engineering the production of medicinal TAs

The most popular strategy for engineering TA biosynthesis is to overexpress TA biosynthetic genes in hairy root cultures. The roles of ten TA biosynthetic genes, ODC, PMT, PYKS, CYP82M3, TRI, UGT1, LS, CYP80F1, HDH and H6H, in engineering the production of TAs

were investigated in hairy root cultures from different TA-producing plant species. The engineering of TA production was well reviewed by Gong et al. [8]. Notably, the overexpression of two or more TA biosynthetic genes usually enhances the biosynthesis of TAs through a synergistic effect.

In addition to biosynthesis-related gene manipulation, transcriptional regulation represents a promising strategy for enhancing TA production. In Anisodus acutangulus (A. acutangulus), AaWRKY11 activates H6H and promotes scopolamine biosynthesis in hairy roots [9]. In A. belladonna, a low-nitrogen-responsive basic helix-loop-helix transcription factor (LNIR) activates multiple biosynthetic genes and increases TA accumulation in hairy root cultures [10]. As transcription factors can regulate multiple genes simultaneously, combining them with rate-limiting enzymes offers a promising approach for improving TA production in both native plants and synthetic systems.

Hairy root cultures remain unviable for commercialization because there is no established method for efficient cultivation on an industrial scale, so the development of engineered plants holds significant value in the pharmaceutical industry. A. belladonna is the only TA-producing industrial crop for which transgenic plants can be developed efficiently. TA biosynthetic genes such as ODC, PMT, H6H and their combinations are employed to generate transgenic A. belladonna plants with high-yield TAs. In particular, we used G2 5-enolpyruvylshikimate-3-phosphate synthase (G2-EPSPS), identified from Pseudomonas fluorescens, as a marker gene, combined with H6H to establish transgenic homozygous lines of A. belladonna, which produce high levels of scopolamine and have strong tolerance to herbicides in the field (Table 1).

After elucidation of the TA biosynthetic pathway, scientists designed five metabolic modules, with 34 chromosomal modifications in engineered yeast cells, to produce hyoscyamine and scopolamine. The enzymes functioning in these modules were chosen from different origins, including yeast, bacteria, plants, and animals, for optimized catalytic efficiency and are localized to diverse subcellular positions in yeast cells, such as the cytosol, mitochondrion, peroxisome, vacuole, and tonoplast, to cover all the compartments involved in TA biosynthesis. This optimized yeast synthetic system could produce hyoscyamine and scopolamine but at a very low titer, which is far from the desired industrial level [5].

# 4. Perspectives

The elucidation of the biosynthetic pathway of TAs in A. belladonna provides essential biological components for engineering TA production. Enzymes that catalyze the same reactions in different species display varying catalytic activities. For example, the

H6H enzyme of Hyoscyamus niger (H. niger) outperforms those of other TA-producing plants when engineering scopolamine production [10]. Consequently, it is imperative to employ comparative biochemical techniques for identifying TA biosynthetic enzymes with high catalytic efficiency in other species. Moreover, a powerful technique for generating high-efficiency enzymes is rational design and evolution on the basis of protein structure analysis.

From ornithine and phenylalanine to scopolamine, there are thirteen biosynthesis enzymes belonging to various protein families that exhibit diverse catalytic activities. Most of these TA biosynthetic genes have been shown to increase TA production in planta via a single-gene overexpression strategy. However, the ability of overexpressing a single gene to promote TA production is typically restricted. The overexpression of multiple genes, on the other hand, may result in increased yields of TAs owing to the synergistic effects of various TA biosynthetic genes. Consequently, the combination of various TA biosynthetic genes should be investigated in plant metabolic engineering to increase TA production. Furthermore, the biosynthetic pathway of TAs is situated in a complex metabolic network in which certain metabolic pathways may compete against TA biosynthesis. Inhibiting competitive biosynthetic pathways could increase metabolic flux toward the biosynthesis of TAs. To produce TAs in large quantities, overexpressing TA biosynthetic genes while simultaneously inhibiting competitive metabolic fluxes should be considered. Unfortunately, the biosynthetic pathway competing with hyoscyamine and scopolamine remains poorly understood.

A. belladonna is a frequently utilized plant species for engineering TA production, largely because of its widespread cultivation and ease of obtaining transgenic lines. In addition to A. belladonna, numerous solanaceous plant species have been found to produce TAs. Compared with A. belladonna, other species of TA-producing plants have their own advantages. Anisodus luridus (A. luridus) yields greater biomass; H. niger produces scopolamine as its main alkaloid; Duboisia $\times$ hybrid, one of the primary sources for commercial TAs production, contains hyoscyamine and scopolamine at high levels. These TA-producing plants may be utilized as plant chassis for TA production through metabolic engineering. Therefore, establishing genetic operation technologies for such TA-producing plant species is necessary for engineering TA production.

The de novo biosynthesis of TAs in engineered yeast cells represents a significant advance in the production of these valuable drugs using synthetic biotechnology. Nonetheless, the levels of hyoscyamine and scopolamine produced by engineered yeast cells remain extremely low and are thus insufficient for commercial purposes.
