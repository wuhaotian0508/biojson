# Summary

Key words: arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM), carotenoid biosynthesis, isoprenoids, apocarotenoids, methylerthritol phosphate (MEP) pathway, reactive oxygen species (ROS), root plastids, secondary metabolism.

Plant root-colonizing arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi activate the methylerylthritol phosphate pathway, carotenoid biosynthesis and oxidative carotenoid cleavage in roots, leading to $C_{13}$ and $C_{14}$ apocarotenoids, that is, cyclohexenone and mycorradicin derivatives. Mycorradicin causes the characteristic yellow coloration of many AM roots accumulating within a complex mixture of unknown components. The accumulating $C_{13}$ cyclohexenones exhibit various ring substitutions and different glycosyl moieties. Transcript levels of the first two enzymes of the MEP pathway, 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate synthase and 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate reductoisomerase, and of the carotenoid pathway, phytoene desaturase and $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase, along with a carotenoid-cleaving dioxygenase, are markedly increased in AM roots. This correlates with proliferation and reorganization of root plastids. These results allow at this point only speculation about the significance of apocarotenoid accumulation: participation in the production of signaling molecules and control of fungal colonization or protection against soil-borne pathogens; protection of root cells against oxidative damage of membranes by reactive oxygen species; and promotion of the symbiotic interactions between plant roots and AM fungi.

# I. Introduction

Soil-borne fungi of the phylum Glomeromycota (Schüßler et al., 2001) colonize the roots of most terrestrial plants in almost all ecosystems to form arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) roots (Smith & Read, 1997). The most important feature of this mutualistic symbiosis is the exchange of reduced carbon from the plant vs mineral nutrients, especially phosphate, and water from the fungus. Another beneficial consequence of this symbiosis is the increased resistance against biotic or abiotic stressors (Kjøller & Rosendahl, 1996; Dumas-Gaudot et al., 2000; Linderman, 2000).

It is well known that secondary metabolites are required for the interaction of a plant with its environment, for example, in defense reactions against herbivores and microbial pathogens (Bennett & Wallsgrove, 1994; Dixon et al., 2002; Gatehouse, 2002) as well as in mutualistic symbiotic interactions, such as in legume nodulation (Peters et al., 1986; Siqueira et al., 1991). However, in contrast to what is known about secondary metabolism in microbial pathosystems (Dixon, 2001; Bednarek et al., 2005) or in the formation of legume root nodules (Long, 2001), our knowledge about secondary metabolites involved in the development and maintenance of functional AMs is limited (Harrison, 1998; Strack et al., 2003; Bécard et al., 2004; Hause & Fester, 2005).

Arbuscular mycorrhizal roots of Medicago truncatula and Medicago sativa exhibit changes in the patterns of accumulating isoflavones (Harrison & Dixon, 1993; Larose et al., 2002). There are reports on root flavonoids that promote spore germination of AM fungi (Gianinazzi-Pearson et al., 1989; Tsai & Phillips, 1991) and it has been reported that a phosphate deficiency-induced C-glycosylflavonoid stimulates arbuscular mycorrhiza formation in Cucumis melo roots (Akiyama et al., 2002). Roots of Cucumis sativus accumulate AM fungus-induced triterpenoids (Akiyama & Hayashi, 2002). Cell wall-bound phenolics in Allium cepa roots (i.e. 4-coumarate-1 and $N$ -feruloyltyramine as major components) may be involved in the control of mycorrhizal root colonization and could also be responsible for the resistance to pathogenic fungi (Grandmaison et al., 1993). Arbuscular mycorrhizal roots of Hordeum vulgare exhibit a transiently increased accumulation of $N$ -hydroxycinnamoylputrescine and -agmatine (Peipp et al., 1997) that can also be induced in nonmycorrhizal roots by application of methyl jasmonate. This result agrees with those showing transcriptional induction of jasmonate biosynthesis in AM roots of $H$ . vulgare (Hause et al., 2002). Furthermore, suppression of allene oxide cyclase (AOC), the key enzyme of jasmonate biosynthesis, in hairy roots of $M$ . truncatula led to a decrease of jasmonate levels that was accompanied by a significant reduction of the degree of colonization by Glomus intraradices (Isayenkov et al. 2005).

The most prominent recent example of secondary metabolites, which may be involved in regulation of the development of AM roots are sesquiterpenoid lactones (strigolactones),

derived from precursor carotenoids (Matusova et al., 2005). This is a group of compounds, previously isolated as seed germination stimulants for the root-parasitic weeds Striga and Orobanche (Bouwmeester et al. 2003). It has been shown that these isoprenoids are also responsible for hyphal branching in the close vicinity of plant roots, just before the formation of fungal appressoria on the root surface, and may stimulate the production of symbiotic signals (Akiyama et al., 2005). With regard to the parasitic weeds, they may find their potential hosts by detecting strigolactones that are released from AM roots upon phosphate deficiency in the molecular dialogue with AM fungi (Akiyama & Hayashi, 2006). The authors discuss that the different amounts of strigolactones exuded by almost all plant roots are consistent with the host specificity of AM fungi. Furthermore, it has been suggested that strigolactones may play a variety of different ecological roles (Humphrey & Beale, 2006).

Whereas strigolactones are produced usually in trace amounts, the carotenoid-derived isoprenoids described in this review accumulate to high levels in many plant species studied. Since their discovery in maize (Klingner et al., 1995a) and barley (Maier et al., 1995), they have been detected in a large variety of plants (Fester et al., 2002a). Furthermore, a recent study indicates that the stimulation of carotenoid metabolism in AM roots is a general phenomenon (Fester et al., 2005).

In this review we summarize some 10 yrs of research on chemical, biochemical, molecular and cytological aspects of the metabolism of apocarotenoids, oxidative carotenoid cleavage products, in AM roots. However, a definite function of apocarotenoids in these roots has still not been established. Nevertheless, we present some ideas about their possible function to point out future directions of research on isoprenoid metabolism in AM symbioses.

# II. Mycorradicin

In a study of the roots of some mycorrhizal legumes, thought to be infected by fungal parasites, Jones (1924) discovered a characteristic yellow or greenish-yellow discoloration of these roots, limited to the 'cell sap of the cortical cells outside the endodermis'. Seventy years later, Klingner et al. (1995a, 1995b) described isolation and identification of a yellow-colored compound from mycorrhizal roots of gramineous plants (e.g. Zea mays, H. vulgare, Triticum aestivum and Panicum miliaceum). Spectroscopic analyses (nuclear magnetic resonance and mass spectrometry) of the compound after esterification with diazomethane revealed an all- $E - 4,9$ dimethyldodeca-2,4,6,8,10-pentaenedioic acid $(\mathrm{C_{14}})$ , called mycorradicin. The occurrence of this compound correlated with a yellow coloration of AM roots from these plants. The compound was not found in plants with uncolored AM roots (e.g. from Tagetes). This correlation, however, seemed not to be valid in all cases, since the compound could not be extracted from the yellowish roots of some monocotyledons

(e.g. A. cepa and A. porrum; Klingner et al., 1995b). Trace amounts of mycorradicin were also found in non-mycorrhizal roots, e.g. $0.2\%$ in Ornithogalum umbellatum roots compared with the amount found in the respective mycorrhizal ones (Schliemann et al., 2006). This has also been described in an earlier study, where mycorradicin was detected in seven out of 46 species analyzed (Fester et al., 2002a).

The structure of mycorradicin resembles the structures of bixin $(\mathrm{C}_{24})$ from Bixa orellana (Jondiko & Pattenden, 1989) and crocin $(\mathrm{C}_{20})$ from Crocus sativus (Pfander & Wittwer, 1975). There are also structural similarities to azafrin $(\mathrm{C}_{27})$ from Escobedia species (Eschenmoser & Eugster, 1975) and to another $\mathrm{C}_{27}$ apocarotenoid from Boronia megastigma (Cooper et al., 2003). Because these compounds are derived from the oxidative cleavage of precursor carotenoids, a similar biosynthetic origin was suggested for mycorradicin (Klingner et al., 1995b). As in the case of crocin that is present in planta in its glycosylated form (crocin), mycorradicin was found not to accumulate as the free dicarboxylic acid in AM roots. It could only recently be detected in trace amounts in AM roots of $O$ . umbellatum along with a series of as yet unknown low-molecular mycorradicin derivatives (Schliemann et al., 2006). In roots of $Z$ . mays, mycorradicin was shown to be part of a yellow-colored complex mixture of various esterification products (Fester et al., 2002a), which in high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) analyses exhibit a broad elution profile that cannot be resolved into its individual components. Only after mild alkaline hydrolysis of the purified yellow complex, is mycorradicin liberated and detected by its characteristic UV absorbance ( $\lambda_{\max}$ at $379~\mathrm{nm}$ and $398~\mathrm{nm}$ ). It was assumed that mycorradicin is esterified to as yet unknown compounds (Schliemann et al., 2006) or, in case of $Z$ . mays, bound in diesters, oligo-esters or even polyesters to glycosylated cyclohexenone derivatives (Fester et al., 2002a). By subjecting crude methanolic extracts of AM roots from various plant species to alkaline hydrolyses, it was possible to detect mycorradicin in all yellow-colored AM roots as well as in small amounts in many uncolored AM roots.

# III. Cyclohexenone derivatives

Concomitantly to the discovery of mycorradicin, studies on secondary metabolites in roots of some cereals, colonized by the AM fungus Glomus intraradices, resulted in the identification of another AM-specific apocarotenoid, a $C_{13}$ cyclohexenone derivative, (i.e. blumenol C 9-O-(2'-O- $\beta$ -glucuronosyl)- $\beta$ -glucoside) called blumenin (Maier et al., 1995). The structure of blumenin is shown in Table 1 together with a number of related compounds identified in subsequent studies on isoprenoid compounds in AM roots.

Related cyclohexenone derivatives are also observed in exudates of nonmycorrhizal maize roots (Park et al, 2004) and in above-ground plant parts. The aglycon blumenol C has been described together with blumenol A and B to accumulate

in leaves of Podocarpus blumei (Podocarpaceae) (Galbraith & Horn, 1972). Respective glycosides have been isolated from species of various plant families, such as Nicotiana tabacum (Solanaceae) (Aasen et al., 1974; Kodama et al., 1984), Epimedium grandiflorum (Berberidaceae) (Miyase et al., 1988), Capparis spinosa (Capparidaceae) (Calis et al., 2002) and Glochidion zeylanicum (Euphorbiaceae) (Otsuka et al., 2003). Related ionone structures were identified as aroma compounds from Rubus idaeus (Rosaceae) (Pabst et al., 1992) and Camellia sinensis (Theaceae) (Ma et al., 2001). Because these compounds have been described to be derived from oxidative cleavage of precursor carotenoids, as discussed for mycorradicin, a similar biosynthetic origin for the cyclohexenone derivatives observed in AM roots could be assumed. A further well-known compound that resembles the accumulating cyclohexenone derivatives and is produced by the oxidative cleavage of a precursor carotenoid is the plant hormone abscisic acid and there are various other products of plant carotenoid oxygenases (Giuliano et al., 2003; Auldridge et al., 2006b).

The occurrence of blumenin in some cereal mycorrhizas (Maier et al., 1995) initiated a study on their distribution within the Poaceae. Inoculation of 61 members of the Poaceae family with Glomus intraradices induced marked changes in the patterns of HPLC/DAD-detectable metabolites from root extracts of all species investigated. Among these metabolites accumulation of blumenin and related cyclohexenone derivatives was mostly observed in the tribes Poeae, Triticeae and Avenae (Maier et al., 1997). In addition, various related structures accumulating specifically in AM roots of $O$ . umbellatum (Schliemann et al., 2006) and members of the Solanaceae (Maier et al., 1999, 2000) as well as of the leguminous plants Lotus japonicus (Fester et al., 2005) and $M$ . truncatula (W. Schliemann, unpublished) were identified. These compounds differ in ring substitutions and in the nature and degree of glycosylation, including sugar moieties esterified with malonic acid (Table 1). The formation of cyclohexenone derivatives in AM roots is highly specific. In the case of $H$ . vulgare effects of biotic stress such as infections of roots with pathogens or endophytes as well as treatments with abiotic stressors such as heat, cold, high light intensities, heavy metals and drought as causes for the formation of cyclohexenone derivatives could definitely be excluded (Maier et al., 1997).

Summarizing the data on the occurrence of the accumulating compounds, mycorradicin levels were examined in mycorrhizal roots of 46 different plant species. Apart from a high variation in quantities (less than $1\mathrm{nmol}$ up to $300\mathrm{nmolg}^{-1}$ root fresh weight), the compound was detected in all eight monocotyledons and in 15 out of 38 dicotyledons analyzed (Fester et al., 2002a). In monocotyledons, mycorradicin was even found in AM roots of plants without detectable cyclohexenone derivatives (i.e. Nardus stricta, Panicum miliaceum, Sorghum bicolor) according to Maier et al. (1997). Thus, the cleavage reaction appears to be common to AM roots of monocotyledons and widespread in AM roots of dicotyledons. As described later, there is additional evidence that even in the case of dicotyledons lacking detectable apocarotenoids, the activation of carotenoid biosynthesis in AM roots is a general phenomenon (Fester et al., 2005).

Although the final structure of the accumulating esterification products of mycorradicin in AM roots from $Z$ mays could not be elucidated, it could be demonstrated that a glycosylated cyclohexenone derivative (blumenol C cellobioside) is part of the yellow apocarotenoid complexes in AM roots of this plant (Fester et al., 2002a). This esterified structure of AM-dependent $\mathrm{C_{13}}$ isoprenoids occurs along with the already described free glycosylated cyclohexenone derivatives. Accordingly, both carotenoid fragments are partly integrated into the esterification products after enzymatic modifications. These products are insoluble in water (Fester et al., 2002a) and are finally deposited as small lipophilic droplets in the vacuoles of root cortical cells (Klingner et al., 1995b; Fester et al., 2002a).

Increased transcript levels of two key enzymes of the MEP pathway, 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate synthase (DXS; Walter et al. 2000) and 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate reductoisomerase (DXR; Walter et al. 2000; Hans et al., 2004), provided additional evidence regarding the role of this pathway in the accumulation of the proposed carotenoid cleavage products. In studies on AM fungal root colonization of the model plant M. truncatula (Cook, 1999; Journet et al., 2001), a close analysis of the genes coding for DXS demonstrated the existence of two isogenes, $D X S1$ and $D X S2$ . Expression of $D X S2$ was induced upon AM colonization of the root systems, whereas $D X S1$ was only expressed at high constitutive levels in photosynthetic tissues (Walter et al., 2002). Because a similar division of labor was observed for corresponding $D X S$ isoforms in Z. mays and Lycopersicon esculentum, the existence of different DXS isoforms for the production of isoprenoids involved in primary metabolisms $(D X S1)$ and isoprenoids involved in secondary metabolism $(D X S2)$ has been proposed to be a general phenomenon in shoot tissues (Walter et al., 2002). However, there is not much known about the regulation of the MEP pathway in nonmycorrhizal plant roots, except for an interesting report on involvement of DXS2 in ginkgolide biosynthesis in Ginkgo biloba (Kim et al. 2006). The demand for isoprenoids in plant growth and development, met by DXS1, vs presumed ecological functions in variable environmental circumstances, met by DXS2, requires a tight spatiotemporal metabolic regulation.

The activation of carotenoid biosynthesis in AM roots of $N.$ tabacum was demonstrated by analyzing phytoene desaturase (PDS) a key enzyme of carotenoid biosynthesis. Several independent approaches confirmed the activity of this enzyme, catalyzing the second committed step of this pathway, the oxidation of phytoene to $\zeta$ -carotene. Increased transcript levels of this enzyme in AM roots were shown by reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) as well as an increased activity of the respective promoter by the analysis of transgenic tobacco plants carrying suitable promoter-glucuronidase (GUS) fusion constructs (Fester et al., 2002b). Increased transcript levels have also been shown for the $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase (ZDS; Lohse et al. 2005). At the metabolite level, increased amounts of phytoene and reduced amounts of mycorradicin were detected in AM roots of $N.$ tabacum upon inhibition of PDS activity (Fester et al., 2002b) by its specific inhibitor norflurazon.

The decrease in mycorradicin accumulation after the inhibition of PDS clearly shows the participation of carotenoid biosynthesis in the accumulation of these mycorrhiza-specific apocarotenoids. The identification of precursor carotenoids in tobacco as well as in other plants, however, has not been successful to date. Apart from minor amounts of $\zeta$ -carotene no carotenoids have been observed in the roots of $Z$ . mays or $M$ . truncatula. By contrast, $N$ . tabacum roots contained considerable amounts of carotenoids such as violaxanthin or neoxanthin, independently from the mycorrhizal status of the roots. The substantial accumulation of apocarotenoids, for example, in AM roots from $Z$ . mays, with respect to the lack of possible precursors can only be explained by the assumption that in AM roots all intermediates of the pathway leading to the apocarotenoids exhibit a high turnover (Fester et al., 2002b).

As already stated, screening of AM roots from various plant species resulted in a number of dicotyledons that do not accumulate detectable amounts of mycorradicin. Experiments using the PDS inhibitor norflurazon showed, however, that a missing accumulation of apocarotenoids is not indicative for a low activity of carotenoid biosynthesis. Upon application of norflurazon, increased amounts of phytoene were observed in AM roots when compared with noncolonized roots of Centaurea cyanus, Petroselinum crispum, Papaver somniferum, Tagetes erecta and Ruta graveolens, which do not accumulate bound mycorradicin in detectable amounts in AM roots (Fester et al., 2005). Accordingly, all these species react by the activation of carotenoid biosynthesis to fungal colonization of their roots. This reaction therefore seems to be a general response of plant roots specific to the AM symbiosis.

In recent years the description of various carotenoid cleaving dioxygenases (CCDs) catalyzing the oxidative cleavage of carotenoids at different positions led to the discovery of a novel enzyme family (Giuliano et al., 2003). The CCDs catalyze the formation of hormones, pigments, flavors, aromas and defense compounds and there is an increasing understanding about the divergent roles of carotenoid cleavage products in plants (Bouvier et al., 2005; Auldridge et al., 2006a). Some new properties of these products were found in many aspects of plant growth and development. For example, two CCDs (CCD7 and CCD8) produce a novel, graft-transmissible hormone that controls axillary shoot growth (Auldridge et al., 2006b).

Carotenoid cleaving dioxygenases specific for acting at the $9,10/9'$ , $10'$ positions resulting in the accumulation of $\mathrm{C}_{14}$ and $\mathrm{C}_{13}$ apocarotenoids (mycorradicin and cyclohexenone derivatives) have been described by Schwartz et al. (2001) and are widely distributed in the plant kingdom. Strong transcriptional activation of a CCD in AM roots of $Z$ . mays has recently been shown (J. Hans et al., unpublished; Lohse et al., 2005). High constitutive transcript levels were found in the leaves of this plant, possibly involved in carotenoid turnover (Auldridge et al., 2006). The actual substrate of this CCD in

AM roots is not yet known. A possible candidate may be lutein or lactucaxanthin (Siefermann-Harms et al., 1981), the latter (included in Fig. 1) showing ionone moieties structurally most closely related to the accumulating cyclohexenone derivatives. Lactucaxanthin, however, is a rare carotenoid (Cunningham & Gantt, 2001) that has not yet been described from plant roots.

In subsequent reactions, the $\mathrm{C_{14}}$ dialdehyde as one product of the oxidative cleavage reaction is assumed to be oxidized to give mycorradicin, while the corresponding $\mathrm{C_{13}}$ ketones are reduced to the respective alcohols. These cyclohexenone derivatives are subjected to various glycosyltransferase reactions and mycorradicin may be bound to some of these glycosylated cyclohexenone derivatives, for example, in $Z.$ mays (Fester et al., 2002a), or esterified to various as yet unknown compounds (e.g. in $O.$ umbellatum; Schliemann et al., 2006) (Fig. 1).

The first CCD, characterized from the pathway leading to apocarotenoids was VP14 from $Z.$ mays, catalyzing the formation of abscisic acid (Tan et al., 1997). A possible role of this hormone in AM roots was suggested from the fact that its level increases in AM roots of $Z.$ mays (Danneberg et al., 1992; Bothe et al., 1994) and Glycine max (Meixner et al. 2005). Although abscisic acid plays an important role in root development, particularly in development of lateral roots (Tan et al., 2003) and in general in roots under stress (Hartung et al., 2005), its function in AM roots has not yet been elucidated.

# VI. Root plastids

Because the MEP pathway, as well as carotenoid biosynthesis, is located in plastids, changes in plastid structure and metabolism in AM roots have been analyzed to understand the cytological and biochemical background of the accumulation of apocarotenoids. Electron microscopy gave early indications for the proliferation of cell organelles in root cortical cells colonized by AM fungi (Bonfante-Fasolo, 1984; Gianinazzi-Pearson, 1996). In plastids the occurrence of unusual elongated forms has been described for AM roots of $O$ . umbellatum (Scannerini & Bonfante-Fasolo, 1977; Dexheimer et al., 1990). Similar structures, associated with a general increase in plastid numbers, have been observed by confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) of AM roots of $N$ . tabacum transformed with a plastid-targeted green fluorescent protein (GFP) (Fester et al., 2001) as shown in Fig. 2. These plastids are obviously connected to each other, developing large networks covering the arbuscules. This process is based on the formation of plastid tubular extensions known as stromules (stroma-filled tubules; Kohler et al., 1997).

Increases in the levels of various fatty acids and amino acids as well as of corresponding transcripts allowed the identification of fatty acid and amino acid biosyntheses as the main plastid metabolic pathways activated besides carotenoid biosynthesis upon AM colonization. An increased production of fatty and amino acids appears to be necessary, in particular in the early phase of arbuscule formation, when the plant cell has to form the periarbuscular membrane covering the growing and branching arbuscule. # VII. Significance of carotenoid metabolism

The observation that carotenoid biosynthesis is activated in AM roots of virtually all plant species studied (Fester et al., 2005), suggests a functional significance of this phenomenon. This assumption is supported by results showing a strong dependence of root colonization and frequency of arbuscle formation on activities of the MEP and carotenoid pathways. The high flux through the carotenoid pathway in AM roots of all plants investigated is not reflected by detectable amounts of carotenoids (Fester et al., 2002b). It rather leads to the accumulation of the respective apocarotenoids, parts of a widely occurring yellow-colored complex with mycorradicin as chromophore that most likely does not play any significant role when deposited in the vacuoles of root cells (Klingner et al., 1995b; Fester et al., 2002a). However, a major portion of glycosylated cyclohexenone derivatives in $Z.$ mays roots remains free and is not found in this complex, as described for a number of other plants (Table 1). The accumulation of apocarotenoids (Fester et al., 2002a) and the activation of the early steps of the MEP pathway in $M.$ truncatula correlate with arbuscle senescence and with the formation of elongated, irregularly shaped plastids (Lohse et al., 2006).

Conclusions based on the results of research on isoprenoids in AM roots, summarized in this review, allow at this point only speculation on possible functions of free cyclohexenone derivatives and mycorradicin or its carotenoid precursor:

# 1. Participation in the production of signaling molecules and control of fungal colonization

This point is supported by the similarity of cyclohexenone derivatives to trisporic acid C, a well-known fungal (zygomycete) mating factor (Sutter et al., 1973). Conversely, results from feeding experiments with a cyclohexenone derivative (blumenin) indicated a strong negative effect on early stages of AM root formation of $H.$ vulgare (Fester et al., 1999). Blumenin applied to split-root systems of this plant resulted in systemic suppression of root colonization of roots already colonized by an AM fungus (Vierheilig et al., 2000). Thus, one may speculate that they are involved in protecting plants against uncontrolled AM fungal colonization or infection

by soil-borne pathogens, as shown for cyclohexenone derivatives isolated from $Z.$ mays roots (Park et al., 2004).

# 2. Protection of root cells against oxidative damage of membranes by reactive oxygen species

This possible role of the carotenoid metabolism in AM roots is supported by the finding that ROS as second messengers can induce carotenoid biosynthesis (Bouvier et al., 1998; Iigusa et al., 2005) and that ROS are accumulating in AM roots, in particular during arbuscle senescence (Salzer et al., 1999; Fester & Hause, 2005). Intracellular accumulation of $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ was observed in the plant cytoplasm close to intact as well as collapsing fungal structures, whereas intercellular $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ was located at the surface of fungal hyphae (Fig. 6). It remains to be analyzed whether cyclohexenone derivatives and enhanced ROS concentrations play key roles in arbuscles senescence. The latter seem to be involved in legume nodule senescence (Puppo et al., 2005). It has also been shown with mycorrhizal legume hosts that an intensified production of $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ is mainly associated with collapsing arbuscules (Lanfranco et al., 2005) and it has been concluded that $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ may be required for signaling fungal cell death. It has been discussed that expression of a fungal superoxide dismutase (SOD) gene may protect arbuscle-containing cells against host defense responses.

Formation of carotenoids and their cleavage products may also be essential for the unique role of AM-related plastids and may protect membranes from oxidative damage (Sujak et al., 1999) or affect membrane rigidity (Hara et al., 1999), limiting oxygen penetration to the hydrophobic membrane core (Gruszecki & Strzalka, 2005).

However, we should keep in mind in this discussion that the product of the MEP pathway, isopentenyldiphosphate, may lead to other yet unknown biologically active isoprenoids which could play a crucial role in AM roots.

# 4. Final points that need attention

First, if we realize that mycorradicin should accumulate together with cyclohexenone derivatives in a stoichiometric relation (i.e. two moles of $C_{13}$ cyclohexenones for each mole of mycorradicin from symmetrical cleavage by CCD1; Schwartz et al., 2001), it should be noted that most of the $C_{14}$ carotenoid cleavage product is not detectable by the analytical methods applied (Fester et al., 2005; Schliemann et al., 2006). For example, in AM roots of $O$ . umbellatum, M. truncatula and L. japonicus the levels of cyclohexenone derivatives are approximately 3-, 4- and even 25-times higher than those of the corresponding extractable mycorradicin, respectively. Thus, the key player in AM associations may be this still undetected part of the $C_{14}$ carotenoid cleavage product, provided that it is not degraded.

Second, one should keep in mind that the precursor carotenoids in AM roots have not been detected so far, obviously because of high CCD activities, most likely located in the cycloplasm as was shown for CCD1 in Crocus sativus (Bouvier et al. 2003). A mycorrhiza-inducible CCD has been identified from $Z$ mays roots and has been shown to be a CCD1 homolog (EMBL accession number AY773278; J. Hans, T. Fester and M. H. Walter, unpublished). CCD1 is the only member of the CCD protein family without a plastid transit peptide (Tan et al., 2003; Simkin et al., 2004; Auldridge et al., 2006a). Thus, we may assume that not the precursor carotenoids but the $\mathrm{C}_{14}$ carotenoid' produced, mycorradicin, especially its nondetectable portion, may play a crucial role in AM roots, possibly scavenging some of the accumulating cytoplasmic $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ . This assumption disagrees, however, with a report that mycorradicin did not suppress elicitor-induced accumulation of $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2$ at least in cell cultures of $N$ tabacum and $M$ sativa (Schroder et al. 2001). Nevertheless, it will be a challenging task in future studies to focus on the metabolic route of this as yet undetected $\mathrm{C}_{14}$ product in AM roots. Furthermore, the role of the stimulated carotenoid pathway in AM roots that do not accumulate apocarotenoids at all (Fester et al., 2005) is still a mystery.

Finally, considerably more work is required to verify one of the speculations raised in this review and to appreciate the role of carotenoids and apocarotenoids in mycorrhizal plants. Current studies focus on a detailed investigation of $A$ . rhizogenes-mediated transformation of $M$ . truncatula roots with DXS2-RNAi constructs, to determine which phase of the arbuscle life cycle is particularly affected by dramatic reduction of apocarotenoid accumulation.

Future studies should be focused on localization and metabolism of as yet undetected apocarotenoids in some plant species, especially mycorradicin, and evaluation of biological activities of the cyclohexenone derivatives. In addition, more far-reaching transgenic approaches on carotenoid metabolism, e.g. transformation of AM roots with $CCD1$ -RNAi constructs, and plastid reorganization, e.g. suppression of FtsZ gene expression, in AM roots will lead to new insights

into the strategies of plants and fungi to develop and maintain AM symbioses.
