# The knockout of Gγ subunit HvGS3 by CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing improves the lodging resistance of barley through dwarfng and stem strengthening

Yanyan Jiang1,2 · Ruiyin Xue2,3 · Yanzi Chang2,4 · Dong Cao2,4 · Baolong Liu2,4  · Yun Li2,4

Received: 19 August 2024 / Accepted: 9 February 2025 / Published online: 27 February 2025 $\circledcirc$ The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2025

# Abstract

$\mathrm { G } \gamma$ subunits participate in multiple biological processes, but their biological function in barley is unknown. Here, CRISPR/ Cas9 gene editing was used to knockout HvGS3 in barley. The height of hvgs3 plants were reduced by $3 7 . 8 \sim 4 3 . 1 \%$ compared to wild type, and the culm lodging resistance index (CLRI) of the second internode of stems was increased by $7 6 . 6 \%$ . The decrease in cell length of the second internode was similar to its node length. The shorter cells may be the main reason for the declines in the internode length and plant height. The number and area of vascular bundles, the epidermal thickness, and the mechanical tissue thickness were signifcantly higher in hvgs3 due to the higher lignin content. Transcriptome analysis showed higher expression of structural genes related to lignin biosynthesis. Gibberellin (GA) biosynthesis was suppressed through the down-regulation of the GA3ox gene, and the application of gibberellin restored the plant height of hvgs3, indicating that plant height was altered by hindering gibberellin biosynthesis. These results shed new light on the functions of the $\mathrm { G } \gamma$ subunit GS3 and provide a resource for breeding new lodging-resistant barley cultivars.

In regions with extremely dry, cold climates, barley is often cultivated as the major food resource because other crops such as wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) and rice (Oryza sativa L.) fail to grow (Mahdi et al. 2008). Moreover, research on the health benefts of $\beta$ -glucans is rekindling interest in barley as a functional food source for humans (Tudorica et al. 2004; Keenan et al. 2007).

Plant height is the main factor that affects cultivation adaptability, harvest efficiency, and grain yield (Guo et al. 2020). In cereals such as rice, wheat, and barley, mature ears at the top of the plants become heavy, making the plants prone to lodging and reducing grain yield and quality. Dwarf varieties can improve lodging resistance and realize increases in yield. The “Green Revolution” of the 1960s boosted rice, wheat, and barley yields by adopting the cultivation of semi-dwarf varieties (Pingali et al. 2012). Four major semi-dwarfing genes, uzu1, sdw1/ denso, hcm1 and ari-e, are employed in barley breeding programs. Semi-brachytic 1 (uzu1) in East Asian barley is characterized by a missense mutation caused by a single nucleotide substitution in the brassinolide-responsive gene HvBRI1 (Chono et al. 2003; Jing et al. 2000). Sdw1/ denso is a deletion mutation of the gibberellin 20-oxidase

gene, a key gene in gibberellin synthesis (Xu et  al. 2017). The mutant showed a decrease in plant height of $2 0 { - } 3 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ , the mutant allele is used in cultivars grown in North America, Australia, and Europe (Xu et al. 2017; Bélanger et al. 2018; Jia et al. 2009). Short culm 1 (hcm1) is present in six-rowed barley grown in the USA, and the allele led to a 10-cm reduction in plant height (Franckowiak et al. 2000; Yu et al. 2010). The 1 bp insertion mutation of the G protein γ subunit gene HvDep1, known as Breviaristatum-e (ari-e), is widely employed as a semidwarfing gene in spring European cultivars (Ellis et al. 2002). Only a few barley semi-dwarfing genes have been successfully applied in specific regions for the improvement of barley, and this has restricted further improvement of barley yields.

G protein complexes are present in all eukaryotes and comprise alpha $( \alpha )$ , beta $( \beta )$ , and gamma (γ) subunits that play a pivotal role in modulating diverse biological processes (Hurowitz et al. 2000). In contrast to the diverse multi-subunits of the G protein complexes found in animals, diploid plants possess only one $\alpha$ subunit, one $\beta$ subunit, and a limited number of γ subunits. The $\gamma$ subunits encompass type A, type B, and type C variants. Notably, plant-specific type C Gγ subunits play a key role in governing plant growth and development and mediating responses to biotic and abiotic stress (Jones et al. 2003; Botto et al. 2009), making them a prominent target for plant breeding. In rice, grain size is co-regulated by three types of C Gγ subunits, namely DEP1, GGC2, and GS3. DEP1 and GGC2 improve grain size by tailmediated signaling. GS3 reduces grain size by blocking the interaction of DEP1 and GGC2 with the $\mathrm { G \beta }$ subunit. DEP1 and GGC2 are associated with plant lodging resistance, the number of grains per spike, and the efficiency of nitrogen fertilizer utilization (Wang et al. 2024; Li-Ying et al. 2022; Sun et al. 2014). The functionally deficient recessive mutation of the GS3 gene improves the appearance of the rice and enhances its tolerance to high temperatures and alkaline soils (Kan et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2023). Only two $\mathrm { G } \gamma$ subunits, HvDEP1 and $H \nu G S 3$ , exist in barley (Foster et al. 2001). The allele ari- $e$ with the function loss of HvDep1 in barley variety Golden Promise exhibits plant dwarfism, accompanied by reductions in leaf, ear, and grain size (Ellis et al. 2002). However, the function of $H \nu G S 3$ remains unknown.

In this study, we edited the HvGS3 gene in barley variety Golden Promise by CRISPR/Cas9. The plant height of hvgs3 was reduced by $3 7 . 8 \sim 4 3 . 1 \%$ , and the stem lodging resistance index was increased by $7 6 . 6 \%$ compared to the wild type in the field. A histological structure analysis and a transcriptome analysis were employed to determine the mechanism producing the phenotype.

# Materials and methods

# Identifcation of GS3 genes and phylogenetic analysis

For identifying the Gγ gene in barley, we performed an all-by-all blast with BLASTP (E-value cutoff: $\mathrm { l e } ^ { - 1 0 }$ ) using the protein sequences of Hordeum vulgare Golden Promise, Hordeum vulgare MorexV3, and seven other representative species (Triticum monococcum, Triticum aestivum, Aegilops tauschii, Zea mays, Sorghum bicolor, Oryza sativa, and Arabidopsis thaliana) (Table S1) as both query and subject. To remove hits to conserved motifs and short sequence fragments, we excluded blast hits where the length of the alignment was less than half of the length of either the query or the subject sequences. Blast hits were clustered with MCL using an infation value of 1.4, and gene families were obtained (Li et al. 2003). After blasting the protein sequences of each species against the reported Gγ genes of Oryza sativa (Type A OsRGG1_LOC_ $O s O 3 g 4 3 4 8 0 . I$ , Type B OsRGG2_LOC_Os02g04520.1, and Type C OsGGC2_08t0456600-01, OsDEP1_LOC_ $O s O 9 g 2 6 9 9 g 9 . I$ and OsGS3_BAH89190.1), the best hit was chosen for every species. The gene family for each species in which each type $\mathrm { G } \gamma$ gene resided was inferred from the best hit. Finally, a phylogenetic tree was constructed with MEGA10 using the identifed gene families of each species (Kumar et al. 2016). The sequence alignment was performed using MUSCLE software (Robert et al. 2004).

# Vector construction

To edit the HvGS3 gene in barley, two target sequences (T1: AAGTCCCCGCTCGACCCCTG; T2: CCGCCAGAT CAGCTTCCTCG) were designed to recognize the conserved coding regions within exon 1 of the HvGS3 gene using the online software CRISPR-GE (http://skl.scau.edu. cn/home). The pYLCRISPR/Cas9Pubi-B (Addgene number: #66,188) was kindly provided by Mr. Liu Yaoguang from the South China Agricultural University (Guangdong China) (Ma 2015; Xie et al. 2017). Construction of a sgRNA vector was conducted following previously published protocols. A target adaptor was ligated to the BsaIdigested sgRNA intermediate vector (NEbiolabs, Beijing, China), and PCR was performed to generate the sgRNA expression cassette. The PCR-amplifed fragments were then assembled into the pYLCRISPR/Cas9Pubi-B binary plasmid in one round of cloning using the Golden Gate ligation method (Ma 2015; Xie et al. 2017). The recombinant vector was transferred into chemically competent

cells of Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 following the standard manual protocol (Huayueyang, Beijing, China).

# Agrobacterium‑mediated transformation

The spring barley variety ‘Golden Promise’ was grown in a greenhouse under controlled conditions of $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ during $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light and $1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ during 8 h of darkness. When the immature embryos were $1 . 5 { - } 2 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ in diameter, immature grains were collected from the centers of the spikes for use in the transformation.

Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of ‘Golden Promise’ was performed as described by Harwood (Harwod et al. 2014). A single colony of Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 carrying the vector with Cas9 and two sgRNA cassettes was inoculated in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of MG medium. The immature embryos were separated from sterile grains and co-cultivated with EHA105 infection solution for three days at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. The embryos were then transferred to a selective medium containing $5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ phosphinothricin (PPT) (Coolaber, Beijing, China) and cultured for six to eight weeks at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. Proliferating calli were transferred to a transition medium containing $3 \mathrm { m g / L }$ PPT and cultured at $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ low light/8 h dark photoperiod. The plantlets were then transferred to a regeneration medium containing $3 \mathrm { m g / L }$ PPT and cultured for two weeks at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod. Putative transgenic plants were transferred to soil and grown to maturity in the greenhouse under controlled conditions of $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ light photoperiod and $1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ during 8 h of darkness.

# Detection of transgenic plants and identifcation of mutation types

Genomic DNA was isolated from the leaves of putative transgenic plants in $\mathrm { { T _ { 0 } } }$ generation using a Plant Genomic DNA Kit (DP305) (TaKaRa, Beijing, China). PCR was performed with the primers ubiF/R (Table S2) for the detection of T-DNA insertions. The primers TOMGS3F/R (Table S2) were designed against the targeted regions, and PCR was performed using a Hi-TOM Gene Editing Detection Kit (Qingxue Biotech, Xi'an, China) to identify the mutant hvgs3 in $\mathrm { { T _ { 0 } } }$ and $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ generation. Amplifcation products were sequenced using an Illumina HiSeq PE150 system (Novogene, China). The sequencing results were analyzed using Hi-TOM (Highthroughput Tracking of Mutations, http://www.hi-tom.net/hitom/). Nucleotide sequences were analyzed using the AlignX program (Invitrogen, CA, USA).

# Phenotype identifcation of the edited lines and the wild type

The $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ edited lines and wild type plants were cultivated in a greenhouse at the Northwest Institute of Plateau Biology, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Qinghai, China. The growth conditions were maintained with a 16-h photoperiod at $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and an 8-h dark period at $1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ edited lines and the wild type plants were grown in three biological replicates, each comprising 20 plants. At maturity, plant height, grains per spike, and spike length were individually measured for all plants within each replicate. Additionally, grains from each replicate were harvested and analyzed using the WSEEN grain testing system (WSeen; http://www.wseen. com) to determine grain length, width, and thousand-grain weight.

# Determination of stem characteristics

The $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant, derived from the homozygous edited line hvgs3-5, was utilized for the analysis of stem characteristics, determination of lignin content, and histological structure examination. The $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant and wild type plants were cultivated in the transgenic experimental feld. Three experimental plots were established for both the $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant and wild type, each with an area of $\mathrm { { 1 m } } ^ { 2 } \left( 1 \times \mathrm { { 1 ~ m } } \right)$ . The investigation was conducted using three biological replicates, with each replicate containing three plants. The stems were subsequently cleaned and desiccated using absorbent paper before the determination of the center of gravity, defned as the distance from the base to the equilibrium point of the stem (inclusive of spike, leaves, and sheath). The measurements were performed using a meter ruler, excluding the length of the awns. The lengths and thicknesses of the frst, second, third, fourth, and ffth internodes of the stem (from bottom to top) were measured with a ruler and a caliper. After the removal of the leaf sheath, the mechanical robustness of the stem was evaluated at the second internode from the base by using a plant stem strength tester (Zhejiang Topu Yunnong Technology Co., Ltd, China). The apparatus was positioned midway along the internode and pushed downwards until the stem fractured. The maximum force necessary to fracture the stem was recorded as the culm mechanical strength (CMS). The culm lodging-resistant index (CLRI) was computed as CMS divided by the center of gravity height (CHCG) (Li et al. 2009).

# Determination of the lignin content

We randomly selected the second internode from the base of both the $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant and wild type plants grown in three experimental feld plots during the grain-flling stage. Three replicates of each sample were collected and dried

at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to a constant weight. After grinding, the samples were passed through sieves with pore sizes of $5 9 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and $2 9 7 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ . Then, $5 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of the sieved sample was weighed and put into a $1 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tube. The lignin content was determined using a Lignin Content Test Kit (Solarbio, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. A spectrophotometer (BioTek Epoch 2, USA) with the wavelength set to $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ was zeroed using glacial acetic acid. After acetylation, the samples were removed from the supernatant for determination of the lignin content using the formula Lignin content.

# Histological structure analysis

The second internode from the base of both the $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant and wild type plants grown in three experimental feld plots were used for histological structure analysis. The 5 mm segments from the same location of the second internode were collected and sent to Wuhan Servicebio Technology Co., Ltd, China for parafn sectioning, The cross sections were scanned and imaged using a 3DHISTECH (Hungary) panoramic section scanner. After imaging using Image-Pro Plus 6.0, U.S.A., the thickness of the epidermis, the thickness of the mechanical tissues, the thickness of the thin-walled tissues, and the areas of fve large vascular bundles and fve small vascular bundles were measured using millimeters as the standard unit in each section, and the mean values were calculated. The numbers of large and small vascular bundles in each section were counted. The longitudinal sections were stained with $0 . 0 5 \%$ (w/v) toluidine blue and images were collected with a light microscope (OLYMPUSmicroscope, Japan). Cell length was calculated using the cellSense Standard software.

# Transcriptome analysis

All internode tissues from the stems of $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ hvgs3 mutant and wild type plants at the jointing stage, grown in three experimental feld plots, were collected. Total RNA was extracted from the pooled internode tissues of each stem using an RNAprep Pure Plant Kit (Tiangen, Beijing, China)

for transcriptome analysis. Six libraries, each sample with three biological replicates, were prepared and sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 2000 system (Personalbio, China). The raw reads were processed with Fastq to obtain clean reads by removing low-quality reads (Liao et al. 2014). Alignment was performed using Hisat2 (Chen et al. 2018). Feature-Counts was used to calculate expression levels, and Deseq2 was used to identify diferentially expressed genes (Love et al. 2014; Kim et al. 2015). The threshold $P$ -value was set at a false discovery rate $\leq 0 . 0 5$ , and the signifcance of diferential expression was determined using the criteria of |log2ratio|≥ 1. Signifcantly enriched GO and KEGG terms from the set of diferentially expression unigenes were identifed using a hypergeometric test.

# Gibberellin response assays

We randomly selected wild type and hvgs3 seeds were placed in Petri dishes containing moist flter paper. A completely randomized trial design was used, with 6 treatments The concentration of Gibberellin A3 (GA3) (ACMEC, Shanghai, China) solution was set at 0 M, $1 0 ^ { - 4 } \mathbf { M }$ , $1 0 ^ { - 5 } \mathbf { M }$ , $1 0 ^ { - 6 } \mathbf { M }$ , $1 0 ^ { - 7 } \mathbf { M }$ and $1 0 ^ { - 8 } \mathbf { M }$ . Thirty seeds were used for each treatment with three replications. Distilled water soaking served as the control group (CK). After germination, the seeds were placed in a $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ incubator for light cultivation. At the one bud and one leaf stage, a pipette was used to transfer GA3 at concentrations from $1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ to $1 0 ^ { - 8 } \mathbf { M }$ to the tillers of the newly emerged second and frst leaves for drop treatment. Each concentration group was inoculated with 3 mL. After 14 days, when the seedlings had reached the one bud and two leaves stage, the heights of the wild type and hvgs3 seedlings were measured with a ruler.

# Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was performed in GraphPad Prism 7.00 (San Diego, CA, USA) Hollow plots represented individual data points. Signifcance was tested using two-tailed Student’s $t \cdot$ -tests, and $P$ -values and are listed in the fgure legends.

‘Golden Promise’ encodes the functional $\mathrm { G } \gamma$ protein, and its genotype is GS3/GS3. Two guide RNAs (gRNAs) were designed to target the coding regions of HvGS3. The frst target region was located at 19 bp after the initiator codon, and the second target region was located 40 bp after the frst target region (Fig. 1A). After transformation, a total of 12 regenerated plants were obtained, and all showed the integration of the CRISPR/Cas9 vector into the genome by detection of the Ubi gene (Fig. S2). Targeted mutagenesis in transgenic plants was examined by amplifcation of the exon 1 region of the $H \nu G S 3$ gene and DNA sequencing. Based on Hi-TOM technology, the target regions in the regenerated plants were amplifed and sequenced to characterize the mutation types.

The target regions of fve $\mathrm { { T _ { 0 } } }$ plants were edited, resulting in one or two mutations, with an editing efciency of $4 2 \%$ . This editing efciency is comparable to that reported in previous CRISPR studies conducted on Golden Promise (Zeng et al. 2020; Jiang et al 2023). The genotype of 5 T plants were $G S 3 / g s 3$ or $g s 3 / g s 3$ . Interestingly, the mutation sites occurred at the fourth nucleotide before the PAM region. A nucleotide ‘A’ or ‘C’ was deleted or inserted except for one allele that carried a 59 bp deletion (Fig. 1B). This deletion was apparently produced by the two target sites being edited simultaneously. Because the nucleotide number of the insert and deletion was not a multiple of three, a frameshift occurred in all new alleles. Type C Gγ subunits contain an organ size regulation (OSR) domain, a transmembrane domain (TM), and a cys-rich region (referred to as “tail” hereafter), and a von Willebrand factor type C (VWFC) (Botella et al. 2012; Mao et al. 2010). The targeted sequences resided in the OSR region (Fig. 1C), and the frameshift caused all alleles to lose the OSR region. Two alleles lost both the OSR and cys-rich regions. These edited lines would be predicted to lose the intact functional protein of HvGS3. The T-DNA-free homozygous editing lines (gs3/ gs3) were selected in the $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ generation of fve editing plants, and phenotypic analysis is performed in $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation. The plant heights of homozygous $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ mutants were reduced by an average of $3 7 . 8 \sim 4 3 . 1 \%$ $\textstyle P < 0 . 0 1 { \dot { } }$ ) (Fig. 1D, E; Table S2), and the grains per spike, thousand-grain weights, spike length, and grain length of the fve lines were lower than those of the wild type (GS3/GS3) (Fig. 1F-H and Table S3). There were no signifcant diferences in the number of tillers or grain width (Table S3), and we chose the allele 3–4 for further analysis.

# The lodging resistance of the wild type and hvgs3

To further investigate the genetic functions of $H \nu G S 3$ , for detection of T-DNA insertions, PCR was performed with primers ubiF/R (Table S2), the T-DNA-free $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ homozygous mutant $( g s 3 / g s 3 )$ and the wild type (GS3/GS3) were planted

in multiple plots for feld tests. The mutant showed a $4 2 . 7 \%$ decrease in plant height compared to the wild type, similar to the phenotype from the greenhouse (Table S4). The barley stems are composed of internodes, and there were fve internodes in hvgs3 and the wild type. All internode lengths of hvgs3 were shorter than those of the wild type. The node length decreased successively from stem bottom to top, with the percentages of reduction being $2 7 . 1 \%$ , $3 3 \%$ , $1 8 . 1 \%$ , $1 8 . 5 \%$ and $7 . 2 2 \%$ (Fig. 2A, B, Table S5). The second internode had the maximum reduction in amplitude. The diameters of the frst, second and ffth internodes of the stem (from bottom to top) were greater in hvgs3 than in the wild type (Fig. 2C, D, Table S5). Compared to the wild type, the center of gravity height of the mutant was reduced by $4 2 . 3 \%$ $( P { < } 0 . 0 1 )$ (Fig. 2F, Table S4), and the stem strength of the second internode from the base was increased by $5 7 . 1 \%$ $( P { < } 0 . 0 1 )$ (Fig. 2G, Table S4). The CLRL was calculated based on the center of gravity height and stem strength. The CLRL was $2 8 \%$ higher in hvgs3 than in the wild type (Fig. 2H, Table S4). In the feld, lodging occurred in the wild type, and hvgs3 showed lodging resistance (Fig. 2E).

# Diferences in microscopic structure

The knockout of HvGS3 exhibited greater length reduction in second internode. A histological analysis of the second internodes was carried out to search for microscopic structural diference between the $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ mutant hvgs3 and the wild type. The length of stem cells in hvgs3 was found to be signifcantly shorter than that of the wild type, with a reduction of $3 6 \%$ compared to the wild type, as observed in our analysis of the second internode (Fig. 3A, B; Table S6). The reduction in the cell length of the second internode was similar to that of its node length. The shorter cells may be the main reason for the decline in the internode length and plant height. Transverse sections were also used to examine the diferences in the strength of the internodes (Fig. 3C). The amounts of epidermal and mechanical tissue increased, while the amount of parenchyma decreased. The epidermal, mechanical, and parenchyma tissue thicknesses of hvgs3 were $2 1 . 6 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $6 8 . 3 4 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , and $2 3 0 . 3 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , respectively, and were $1 6 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , $5 1 . 0 4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , and $3 3 2 . 1 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ in the wild type, respectively (Fig. 3F-H, Table S6). # Transcriptome diferences in the second internode between hvgs3 and the wild type

To further examine the regulatory mechanisms of $H \nu G S 3$ , we performed transcriptomic sequencing and analyzed differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the second internodes of the wild type (GS3/GS3) and hvgs3 (gs3/gs3) plants in $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ generation. We identifed a total of 3418 DEGs between hvgs3 and the wild type, with 1,650 genes up-regulated and 1768 genes down-regulated (Fig. 4A; Table S7). KEGG pathway enrichment showed that 440 DEGs could be categorized into cellular process, environmental information processing, genetic information processing, and organismal systems functions, and almost all of the 539 DEGs were associated with metabolism. Within the metabolism and environmental information processing category, 41 and 32 DEGs were enriched in phenylpropanoid biosynthesis and

plant hormone signal transduction, respectively (Fig. 4B; Table S8).

In the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway, the expression levels of genes encoding 4-coumarate: coenzyme A ligases, hydroxycinnamoyl CoA shikimate/quinate hydroxycinnamoyl transferases, cinnamoyl-CoA reductases, cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenases, and ferulate5-hydroxylase and peroxidases related to lignin biosynthesis were higher in the second internodes of hvgs3 than in the wild type, and their log2FoldChange values ranged from 1.23 to 4.84 (Fig. 4C). These changes were induced by the knockout of HvGS3 and were consistent with the higher lignin content in hvgs3.

The GA 3-oxidase (GA3ox) enzyme is a critical component in the biosynthesis of active GA in plants, directly contributing to the production of bioactive forms such as GA1, GA3, and GA4. In the plant hormone signaling pathway, GA3ox was signifcantly down-regulated in hvgs3 (Fig. 4D), and the log2FoldChange value was − 5.61. The hvgs3 also exhibited sensitivity to exogenous gibberellin treatment, as it appeared to regulate the internode length and plant height through the GA biosynthesis pathway.

The hvgs3 line showed lodging resistance in the feld, and the reduction in plant height may have been one of the reasons. In this study, we also observed that the strength of the second internode from the base was increased by $5 7 . 1 \%$ , and the lignin content was increased by $2 2 \%$ . All structural genes related to lignin biosynthesis were more highly expressed in hvgs3, and this can explain the enhanced lignin content and stem strength. Lignin biosynthesis is positively correlated with lodging resistance. In wheat, the lignin biosynthesis

related genes 4CL, CCR, C3H, F5H, TaCAD1 and TaCM were strongly associated with lodging resistance (Ma et al. 2009, 2010). The improvement of stem strength should be another reason to the lodging resistance of hvgs3.

The knockout of HvGS3 reduced the grain size, and the plant height, which was similar to the AGG3 (GS3-like) in Arabidopsis. Through the knockout of OsGS3 improved the grain size in rice, overexpression of OsGS3 in agg3-2 (a knockout mutant of AGG3 with decreased organ size) showed increased fower organ, silique length, and seed size (Sun et al. 2018). Constitutive overexpression of the AGG3 has an overall positive efect on the growth and stress responses in Arabidopsis and Camelina (Choudhury et al. 2014; Jagdeep et al. 2018). Sun et al. (2018) proposed GS3 reduces grain size by blocking the interaction of DEP1 and GGC2 with the $\mathrm { G \beta }$ subunit in rice. The function of GS3 depends on the genetic background.

Barley had two type $\mathbf { \boldsymbol { C } } \mathbf { \boldsymbol { G } } \mathbf { \boldsymbol { \gamma } }$ subunits DEP1 and GS3 (Fig. S1), while DEP1 was functional defciency in the wild type Golden Promise. The function of $H \nu G S 3$ should be further studied under diferent genetic background including functional DEP1.

# Conclusion

In this study, we created a new dwarfng germplasm resource displaying lodging resistance via gene editing of $H \nu G S 3$ . The newly created hvgs3 displayed reduced expression of GA3ox, an altered Gibberellin signaling pathway, decreased average cell length, and a dwarf phenotype. At the same time, hvgs3 activated the lignin biosynthesis pathway, increased the lignin content, and improved the stem strength. The increase in the lodging resistance may have been caused by both the dwarf phenotype and the stem strengthening. This research provides new information concerning the function of $\mathrm { G } \gamma$ subunit GS3 and a new resource for dwarf barley breeding.
