# ABSTRACT

Caffeine is a defensive alkaloid primarily accumulated in tea leaves to defend against pathogens. But the regulatory mechanism for caffeine biosynthesis in response to fungal infection and the trade-off between specialised metabolite and plant growth remain elusive. Here, we report that jasmonic acid (JA)-regulated CsMYB184 is the key for caffeine biosynthesis in tea leaves. Exogenous MeJA promoted caffeine biosynthesis in tea leaves and enhanced the resistance against fungal pathogens. JASMONATE ZIM-domain proteins (JAZs) interacted with CsMYB184 to repress CsMYB184 transactivation of TCS1. Meanwhile, JA effector CsMYC2 directly bound to and activated CsMYB184 transcription, but inhibited plant growth by directly modulating GA inactivation through gibberellin 2-oxidase 4 (CsGA2ox4). By contrast, gibberellins (GA) stimulated tea plant growth and inhibited caffeine biosynthesis, rendering tea plants more susceptible to fungal pathogens, via competitive DELLA proteins interacting with JAZs to relieve JAZ inhibition of CsMYB184. CsDELLA-JAZ-MYC2-MYB184-TCS1 formed an activation–repression loop to regulate developmental and hormonal control of caffeine biosynthesis in response to fungal pathogens, which balance the tea plant growth and defence response trade-offs. The study reveals JA-GA antagonistic regulation of caffeine biosynthesis as one of the innate immunity defences, meanwhile balancing the growth and defence of tea plants via crosstalk between JA and GA signalling. 

# 1 | Introduction

Tea has become the second most popular nonalcoholic beverage consumed after water, due to containing rich metabolites with multiple health benefits, such as caffeine (Yang and 

Hong 2013). Those compounds not only contribute to tea flavour and health functions (Zhang et al. 2020), but also play crucial roles in tea plant interactions with environmental stresses (Chen, Lin, et al. 2023; Zeng et al. 2021). While tea plants are also often exposed to various biotic and abiotic stresses, they 

have evolved various defence mechanisms, such as densely spaced trichomes to cover the apical buds and young leaves, thick circular waxes deposited on leaf surfaces and various specialised metabolites accumulated in the young tissues to survive the adversary environments (Li, Fu, et  al.  2022; Li et  al.  2020). In particular, the apical buds and young leaves and stems of tea plants can synthesise a large number of chemically diverse defensive compounds to build up the chemical defence and innate immune system. Especially, tea characteristic secondary metabolites, such as caffeine, catechins and theanine, are not only evolved to defend against the biotic and abiotic stresses, but also contribute to various tea flavours and health functions of tea (Gu et al. 2022; Jing et al. 2024; Li et al. 2023; Zhu et al. 2019, 2022). 

Caffeine, a purine alkaloid with strong neuron stimulation activity, is essential for the tea quality and health benefits, but also plays roles in plant pollination, allelopathy and defence against herbivore attack and pathogen infection (Fernandes et al. 2012; Kim et al. 2011; Wright et al. 2013; Zhou et al. 2020). In tea plants, tea caffeine synthase (TCS1) is the key Nmethyltransferase (NMT) responsible for caffeine biosynthesis (Kato et al. 2000). CsMYB184 and other transcription factors, such as CsNAC7, CsS40, CsbHLH1 and CsbHLH80, have been characterised to regulate TCS1 or NMT genes and the caffeine biosynthesis (Jin et  al.  2024; Li et  al.  2024; Li, Ye, et  al.  2022; Ma et  al.  2022; Yao et  al.  2023). CsMYB184 is also involved in the regulation of both trichrome initiation and development and galloylated cis-catechins biosynthesis in tea plant development and is a selected gene during tea domestication (Li, Fu, et  al.  2022; Li et  al.  2020). Although data showed that CsMYB184 is regulated by hormones such as jasmonic acid (JA), abscisic acid (ABA) and insect attack, the exact role of CsMYB184 in the regulation of caffeine biosynthesis in tea leaf defence response remains to be explored (Li, Fu, et al. 2022; Li, Ye, et al. 2022). 

As an important hormone involved in the regulation of plant development and defence responses against mechanical damage, herbivorous insects and necrotrophic pathogens, JA signalling has been extensively studied in plants (Pieterse et al. 2014; Wasternack and Hause 2013). JA signalling is ubiquitous in the plant kingdom, and the JASMONATE ZIM-domain (JAZ) family proteins are the JA signalling repressors (Chini et al. 2007; Howe et al. 2018; Thines et al. 2007). Under regular conditions with low JA/jasmonoyl-isoleucine (JA-Ile) levels, the stabilised JAZ proteins interact and repress the transcription regulators of JA responses, MYCs (Howe et al. 2018; Li et al. 2021). But when bioactive JAs are increased and perceived, JAZ proteins are degraded, resulting in the release of transcription factors MYCs from JAZ repression and activating downstream JA responses, such as plant development, metabolism, defences against pathogen infection and insect attack (Howe et al. 2018; Jin et al. 2023). MYC2 and paralogues serve as the effectors and master regulators of the JA signalling pathway by specifically binding to the G-box element in the promoters of downstream genes or directly interacting with other transcription factors (Fernández-Calvo et al. 2011; Kazan and Manners 2013). Conservatively, JA signalling, CsJAZ1 and CsMYC2 also regulate catechins biosynthesis and tea plant resistance to insect attack (Li, Zhang, et al. 2022; Lv et al. 2023; Zhu et al. 2022). 

Gibberellins (GAs) primarily growth-regulating hormones that also regulate the balanced growth and defences, through interacting with other hormones, such as JA (Hou et al. 2010; Song et  al.  2014; Yang et  al.  2012). DELLA repressor proteins have been identified as the major GA signalling components (Boss and Thomas  2002; Claeys et  al.  2014). The stabilised DELLA proteins can competitively interact with JAZ proteins to amplify the JA signalling or with MYC2 to inhibit MYC2 sequestration by JAZ to modulate secondary metabolism (Hong et  al.  2012; Hou et  al.  2010; Panda et  al.  2022). In Arabidopsis, DELLA could sequester JA responsive MYC2, leading to suppression of sesquiterpene synthase genes (Hong et  al.  2012). In Solanum, MYC1 and MYC2 were interfered with by DELLA in steroidal glycoalkaloids biosynthesis through protein–protein interaction (Panda et al. 2022). Additionally, the JA-associated MYC2 can directly bind to and activate the promoter of genes involved in GA catabolism, such as gibberellin 2-oxidases (GA2oxs), to promote GA inactivation (Jin et al. 2023; Panda et al. 2022). 

Although JAZ-DELLA regulation of growth-defence trade-offs has been extensively described in several plants, there are fewer studies to investigate the regulation of specialised metabolites and their role in immunity by JAZ-DELLA antagonism. Here, we found that the JA and GA signalling interplay and coordinative regulation of specialised caffeine biosynthesis in tea leaves is mediated by CsMYB184. As the key regulator of caffeine accumulation in young leaves of tea plants, CsMYB184 expression also responds to JA and GA hormones and pathogen infection. Furthermore, JAZ proteins, CsJAZ1, CsJAZ12 and CsJAZ13, were identified as the caffeine repressors and interacted with the caffeine activator CsMYB184 to repress the TCS1 expression at the post-transcription level. Meanwhile, CsMYC2 mediated the JA signalling regulation of CsMYB184 and indirectly regulated caffeine biosynthesis and also directly modulated GA inactivation through modulating CsGA2ox4 expression to participate in growth regulation. CsJAZ12-CsDELLA1/2 protein interaction complexes integrate JA and GA signalling to balance the growth and defence of tea plants. Our study demonstrated the mechanism about caffeine biosynthesis regulation by fungal infection. The JA and GA signalling cross-talk regulates specialised metabolite biosynthesis in tea plants under normal growth conditions or pathogen infection.

To further explore the physiological function of caffeine in tea plants, we analysed the caffeine distribution in young tea leaves. Bismuth potassium iodide staining showed that the caffeine (yellow colour) is distributed throughout the tea leaves, but the distribution is uneven (Figure  1H; Figure  S4A). The caffeine was mainly accumulated in mesophyll cells, particularly the palisade cells. Interestingly, caffeine was also detected in epidermal cells and cuticle, as shown by anatomic analysis and cuticular wax profiling with gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) (Figure S4B). To confirm the caffeine distribution in cuticle, the total wax was extracted using chloroform. Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) analysis indicated the significant metabolic variation between cuticle and cuticle-removed leaves. In the leaves, caffeine and epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG) were the dominant compounds, but only caffeine was detected in cuticle with the LC–MS method (Figure 1I; Figure S4C). Then we further observed the dynamic variation of caffeine in the cells of tea plants during fungal infection. Bismuth potassium iodide staining showed that an array of caffeine was redeposited in the epidermal cells and cuticle during C. gloeosporioides infection (Figure 1J). 

# 2.2 | JA Signalling Regulates Caffeine Biosynthesis and Enhances Resistance Against the Fungal Pathogen

To explore how caffeine participates in responding to the fungal infection of tea plants, we subjected tea leaves to C. gloeosporioides infection. Transcriptome analysis revealed that fungal infection significantly upregulated caffeine biosynthetic genes, particularly TCS1, compared with the control (CK) (Figure 2A; Table  S2). Concurrently, caffeine also increased under fungal infection (Figure 2B). 

JA is a defensive plant hormone that plays a critical role in resistance to both biotic and abiotic stresses. Our investigation into the JA biosynthesis pathway showed that fungal infection synergistically activated the expression of JA biosynthetic genes (Figure 2A; Table S3). Metabolic analysis confirmed elevated levels of JA and JA-Ile in response to fungal infection (Figure 2C). 

The activation of both caffeine and JA metabolic pathways by fungal infection indicates that caffeine and JA work together to enhance tea plant resistance to the stress. 

To explore the interaction between JA signalling and caffeine biosynthesis, we used exogenous methyl jasmonate (MeJA) to simulate JA treatment for tea leaves from about 2-year-old cutting seedings. Transcriptome data showed that MeJA treatment not only significantly activated the expression of endogenous JA biosynthetic genes (Figure 2D; Table S4) but also significantly enhanced the caffeine production (Figure  2E), along with increased expression of caffeine biosynthetic genes (Figure  2D; Table S4). 

We also examined the role of JA in tea plant resistance to fungal stress. MeJA treatment improved resistance to fungal anthracnose disease and significantly reduced the lesion area caused by C. gloeosporioides strain (Figure 2F,G). On the other hand, the JA biosynthesis inhibitor, sodium diethyldithiocarbamate (DIECA), clearly repressed the endogenous JA and JA-Ile biosynthesis (Figure  S5), as well as the caffeine biosynthesis and the expression of TCS1 and CsMYB184 compared with the control (CK) (Figure 2H,I). Meanwhile, we found that the CsMYB184 transcript increased significantly in tea leaves under MeJA treatment (Figure 2J,K). A previous study has shown that purine alkaloids are exclusively synthesised in young tea leaves and decreased over leaf maturation (Zhang et al. 2022). We also found that the profiling of purine alkaloids was similar to the profiling of JA and JA-Ile in developing tea leaves (Figure 2L; Figure  S6A,B). Gene expression pattern analysis showed that the expression pattern of CsMYB184 was highly similar and seemingly correlated with the expression patterns of JA signalling regulator genes CsJAZs and CsMYC2 over tea leaf development (Figure S6C). 

Given that JA signalling plays a central role in defence metabolism, caffeine may be regulated directly by JA signalling. To explore the transcriptional regulation mechanism of JAassociated caffeine biosynthesis, the cis-elements in the 1.5-kb promoter region of CsMYB184 were screened. Interestingly, two G-box motifs were identified, which are binding sites of the JA signalling regulator CsMYC2 (Figure  2M). The yeast-one hybrid (Y1H) assay confirmed the binding of CsMYC2 to the CsMYB184 promoter (Figure  2N). Further, CsMYC2 activated the CsMYB184 promoter-driven $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) reporter gene compared with controls in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Figure  2O) and Arabidopsis protoplasts (Figure  2P). When CsMYC2 was knocked down in tea leaves with antisense oligodeoxynucleotide (asODN), this led to the repressed expression of CsMYB184 and TCS1, as well as the reduced caffeine content, compared with the sense ODN (Figure 2Q,R). Summarily, those results indicate that JA plays a crucial role in caffeine biosynthesis in tea leaves under fungal infection.

# 2.5 | CsGA2ox4 Catabolises Bioactive GAs

Fungal infection and JA treatment repressed the growth of tea plants, but significantly upregulated the expression of CsGA2ox4, implying that CsGA2ox4 plays a key role in fungal infection- and JA treatment-associated growth inhibition. Thus, firstly the function of CsGA2ox4 in tea plants was evaluated. To examine whether CsGA2ox4 is able to oxidise bioactive GAs, enzyme activity analysis in  vitro was performed. The MBP-CsGA2ox4 protein was purified and incubated with bioactive $\mathrm { G A } _ { 1 }$ and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 4 }$ respectively. The boiled recombinant protein with different substrates was used in control reactions. LC–MS data showed that MBP-CsGA2ox4 could convert $\mathrm { G A } _ { 4 }$ to $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 4 }$ and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 1 }$ to $\mathrm { G A } _ { 8 }$ in vitro (Figure 7A; Figure S13A,B). 

Further, in  vivo activity of CsGA2ox4 was evaluated by gene overexpression in tobacco plants (Nicotiana tabacum). As expected, a significant dwarf phenotype was observed in CsGA2ox4 overexpression tobacco plants (OE-1, OE-2) compared with the empty vector overexpression plant (EV) (Figure  7B,C; Figure  S13C). Morphological examination of the parenchymal cells of the OE-1 plant at the first internode (Between first leaf and second leaf) showed that their average length was significantly decreased compared with the EV plant (Figure  7D,E; Figure  S13D). GA profiling analysis showed that the level of bioactive $\mathrm { G A } _ { 1 }$ was significantly reduced, but the level of inactive $\mathrm { G A } _ { 8 }$ was significantly increased in the overexpression line (OE-1) (Figure 7F). 

Then we investigated how JA regulated the CsGA2ox4 gene. EMSA assay showed that the JA signalling regulator CsMYC2 could directly bind to the G-box element of the CsGA2ox4 promoter region (Figure 7G). Furthermore, firefly luciferase activities were significantly higher in the CsMYC2-expressing area than in the EV-expressing area in the N. benthamiana leaf, but the luciferase activity activated by CsMYC2 was significantly abolished by additionally expressing CsJAZ12 (Figure 7H), suggesting that the CsMYC2-CsJAZ12 module could directly modulate the expression of CsGA2ox4.

# 3.1 | Caffeine: A Broad-Spectrum Defensive Toxin Against Multiple Biotic Stresses in Plants

Caffeine is a well-known purine alkaloid that contributes to the flavour of tea, but its physiological functions in plants are not fully understood (Zeng et al. 2021). Initially, caffeine was thought to act as a signalling compound in plants, inhibiting seed germination of competitors or stimulating the nervous system of pollinators (Arnold et al. 2021; Ransom 1912; Wright et al. 2013). Recent studies have shown that caffeine also plays crucial roles in plant–microorganism interactions (Kim et al. 2011; Kim and Sano 2008). Wang et  al. reported that endogenous caffeine could suppress 

fungal growth by inhibiting cell wall expansion (Wang et al. 2016), and our transcriptome analysis further showed that fungal selfreplication was significantly suppressed after caffeine intake. 

Coevolution is a result of mutual adaptation between plants and pests or pathogens (Rausher  2001). In caffeine-free ancestral plants, caffeine's effects against pathogens and pests not adapted to caffeine are more pronounced. A recent study pointed out that transgenic rice producing caffeine in leaves could simultaneously improve broad-spectrum resistance, including bacteria, fungi and insects, without affecting yield (Park et al. 2022). Caffeine protects plants from pathogens and insects through two pathways: directly inhibiting DNA and RNA replication in pathogens and insects as a phytoalexin (this study), and significantly activating the host plant's endogenous defence system. For example, in tea plants, caffeine can activate the endogenous JA defence pathway in response to elevated $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ (Li et al. 2016), while in rice, caffeine enhances the salicylic acid (SA) defence pathway by activating calcium signalling (Park et  al.  2022).

The regulation of alkaloid biosynthesis by JA signalling through the JAZ-MYC2 protein complex seems conserved across plants. Alkaloids, a diverse group of compounds irregularly distributed across certain families or species, form via metabolic pathways with dramatic variations to protect plants from biotic damage (Leite Dias and D'Auria  2025). # 3.3 | Crosstalk Between JA and GA for Balancing Disease Resistance and Growth in Tea Plants

When plants face multiple stresses, they synthesise large amounts of secondary metabolites to improve resistance. However, this can inhibit growth, as plants balance resistance and growth (He et al. 2022). In this study, we confirmed that JA and GA are crucial hormones antagonistically mediating growth and defence in tea plants. When tea plants were exposed to fungal stress or exogenous MeJA, the expression levels of bioactive GA biosynthetic genes, such as ent-copalyl diphosphate synthase (CPS) and entkaurene synthase (KS), were not significantly disturbed. Notably, the genes involved in GA inactivation, such as CsGA2ox4, were dramatically activated by these stimuli, suggesting that JA antagonises GA functions by promoting GA inactivation and inhibiting growth to enhance resistance. Our data further showed that promoting tea plant growth with GA significantly reduced resistance to fungal pathogens. Metabolic analysis suggested that reduced caffeine concentration could partially explain the decreased resistance with elevated bioactive GA in tea leaves. 

DELLA proteins are repressors of GA signalling (Boss and Thomas 2002; Claeys et al. 2014). Previous studies have shown that GA promotes DELLA protein degradation to participate in metabolic processes, such as anthocyanin biosynthesis (Xie et  al.  2016). In tea plants, two conserved DELLA proteins, CsDELLA1 and CsDELLA2, were identified. Overexpression assays in tobacco and tea hairy roots validated CsDELLA1 and CsDELLA2 as traditional GA signalling repressors. Transcriptome analysis showed that the expression of several growth-related genes was significantly suppressed in CsDELLA1 or CsDELLA2 overexpressing tobaccos (Figure S16). In tea plants, inhibition of the expression of CsDELLA1 or CsDELLA2 significantly suppressed caffeine production, as well as resistance to fungal infection, indicating that DELLA proteins were the activators of caffeine biosynthesis, and further that DELLA proteins were involved in GA negatively regulating caffeine biosynthesis. 

The interaction between GA and JA is a critical regulatory mechanism in plants, primarily mediated through the stability of DELLA and JAZ proteins (Yang et al. 2012). This interaction is essential for balancing plant growth and defence responses, particularly in tea plants. DELLA proteins are known to interact with JAZ proteins, mediating the crosstalk between GA and JA signalling pathways (Yang et al. 2012). In tea plants, CsDELLA1 and CsDELLA2 can interact with over half of the CsJAZ proteins, highlighting the complexity of this interaction network. CsDELLA1 and CsDELLA2 interaction with CsJAZ12 disrupts its binding to CsMYB184, allowing CsMYB184 to promote caffeine biosynthesis. Additionally, this interaction facilitates the release of CsMYC2, which further enhances GA inactivation by regulating the CsGA2ox4 gene. This results in the inhibition of plant growth while promoting defence mechanisms. Under normal conditions, JA signalling is suppressed, leading to the accumulation of JAZ proteins, which interact with CsMYB184, CsMYC2 and DELLA proteins. This suppresses defensive metabolism (e.g., caffeine biosynthesis) and promotes GA signalling, thereby supporting plant growth. Under stress conditions, JA biosynthesis and signalling are activated, leading to the degradation of JAZ proteins. # 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant Materials, Growth Conditions and Pathogen Infection

All tea plant varieties (Camellia sinensis L.) were grown at the tea plantation of Anhui Agricultural University (Hefei, Anhui Province, China) and were used for gene cloning, phenotype 

survey and metabolomic analyses. The tea cutting seedlings (Shuchazao and Longjing43 cultivars, about 2-year-old) and the tea seedlings (the seeds from Shuchazao cultivar) were cultivated in the artificial climate chamber $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 16/8 h photoperiod, $6 5 \%$ humidity). The pathogenic fungus used in this study, C. gloeosporioides, was identified previously (Jeyaraj et al. 2019), dropped onto potato dextrose agar (PDA) plates, and incubated in an incubator at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . For fungal inoculation, $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of spore suspension $( 1 0 ^ { 7 }$ conidia $\mathrm { m l ^ { - 1 } }$ ) or $5 \mathrm { m m }$ diameter fungal plugs were inoculated onto the wounded potted tea leaves. The leaves inoculated with the same volumes of sterile distilled water or no fungal plugs served as controls. The lesion area of leaves was measured by ImageJ software after infection. Meanwhile, the leaves were harvested at different times for further analysis. All the tobacco and Arabidopsis plants were grown at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a $1 6 / 8 \mathrm { h }$ photoperiod at approximately $1 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

# 4.2 | Targeted Metabolite Profiling

Caffeine was profiled by high performance liquid chromatographic (HPLC) according to the method described previously (Li et  al.  2020). Briefly, about $0 . 0 5 \mathrm { g }$ samples were mixed with 1mL $8 0 \%$ methanol extraction solution. The solution buffer with samples was sonicated for 1h at room temperature and then shaken overnight at $1 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Following centrifugation at 12000rpm for 10min, the supernatants were filtered with a Nylon membrane $( 0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ . Then the caffeine was confirmed according to the caffeine standard compound by HPLC at $2 7 8 \mathrm { n m }$ . For quantification of caffeine, the peak area was introduced into the standard curve. At least three biological replicates were included. 

For phytohormone analyses, about $0 . 1 5 \mathrm { g }$ samples were ground in liquid nitrogen and then extracted with 1 mL ethyl acetate solution. After strongly vortexing, the supernatants were collected by centrifugation at 12000 rpm for 10 min. The supernatants were evaporated by nitrogen and then redissolved in $1 5 0 { - } 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ $5 0 \%$ methanol $\scriptstyle ( \mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ solution. The contents of phytohormone, including JA, JA-Ile, $\mathrm { G A } _ { 1 }$ , $\mathrm { G A } _ { 4 }$ , $\mathrm { G A } _ { 8 }$ and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 4 }$ , were measured on an LC–MS/MS System (SCIEX Triple Quad 5500) equipped with a C18 column (Phenomenex, $4 . 6 \times 2 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $5 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ) according to the previous method (Yue et  al.  2025), with a f low rate of $0 . 4 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ and a column temperature of $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The injection volume was $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ and the mobile phase was water (contained $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid $( \nu / \nu )$ and 5 mM Ammonium formate, A) and acetonitrile (B). The mass spectrometer was operated under negative ionic mode with the Electrospray Ionisation (ESI). The gas temperature was set to $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the gas flow rate was set to $6 \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the temperature of sheath gas, with $1 1 \mathrm { L / m i n }$ flow rate, was set to $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The data were acquired and analysed with Analyst software. The phytohormone compounds were identified by comparing the standard substances. The peak area was introduced into the standard curve for quantification. At least three biological replicates were included. 

# 4.3 | Caffeine Extraction From Tea Wax

About $3 \mathrm { g }$ of healthy tea leaves (Shuchazao cultivar) were fully submerged in a glass plate containing $1 5 \mathrm { m L }$ chloroform. The 

plate was stirred gently for extraction of cuticular waxes for 15 s at room temperature. The extraction solution was transferred to a new vial, and fresh chloroform (15mL) was added to the same tea leaves in the plate, and the extraction was repeated for another 15s. The two chloroform extractions were combined and dried with a mild stream of nitrogen gas. Then the substrate was resuspended with $8 0 \%$ methanol for HPLC and LC–MS analysis or with $6 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ chloroform for GC–MS analysis. For GC–MS analysis, the samples were injected into the GC–MS platform (Shimadzu, Japan) equipped with an Agilent DB-1 capillary column. The instrument settings follow our previous methods (Zuo et al. 2023). The detection temperature was $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , staying for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The mobile phase was helium with a speed of $1 . 2 \mathrm { m L / m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ . Caffeine was identified by comparing the mass spectra in the NIST database and comparing the caffeine standard. The ethyl caprate $( 0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 } )$ was served as the internal standard for quantification. At least five repeats for each sample were performed. 

# 4.4 | Assessing the Antifungal Effects of Caffeine

To test the effects of caffeine on the growth of fungal pathogens, $5 \mathrm { m m }$ diameter fungal plugs were inoculated, respectively, on the centre of fresh PDA plates containing different concentrations of caffeine (0, 0.5, 1, 2 and 5 mM). After incubation for 3 days at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the fungal growth area was measured using ImageJ software from three independent biological replicates with more than 10 PDA plates for each caffeine concentration. Meanwhile, the fungal pathogens were collected from the plates containing 0 or $2 \mathrm { m M }$ caffeine for RNA-seq analysis. 

# 4.5 | Cloning and Construction of Binary Vectors for Plant Expressing

The open reading frames (ORFs) of CsJAZs, CsMYB184, CsMYC2, CsDELLAs and CsGA2ox4 were amplified from leaf cDNAs prepared from Camellia sinensis L. cv. Shuchazao using gene-specific primers (Table S8), and the sequences were identified by Sanger sequencing. The ORFs of genes were then subcloned into the binary vector pBI121, respectively, for tobacco transformation or tea plant transformation. 

# 4.6 | Transformation of Tobacco and Hairy Roots of Tea Plants

For tobacco transformation, the CDSs of CsDELLA1, CsDELLA2 and CsGA2ox4 were recombined into pBI121 and then transformed into A. tumefaciens EHA105 respectively. The tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum cv, Xanthi) transformation was performed according to the described methods (Shen et  al.  2019). For tea seedling hairy root transformation, the pBI121-CsJAZ1, -CsJAZ12 and -CsJAZ13 constructs were transformed into A. rhizogenes strain ATCC 15834 by electroporation, respectively. The positive transformants were confirmed by RT-PCR, and further were used to transform about 3-month-old tea seedlings as described by Alagarsamy et al. (2018). At least three independent transgenic lines were generated for each construct and were used for experimental examination. 

# 4.7 | RNA Extension and Quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR)

Plant total RNA isolation and qRT-PCR were performed as previously described (Li, Fu, et  al.  2022). Briefly, about $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total purified RNA from each sample was used to generate the cDNA using the SuperScript III kit (Invitrogen). qRT-PCR was carried out on the iQ5 real-time PCR machine with gene-specific primers (Table S8). The data were acquired and parsed by the manager software (Bio-Red). The housekeeping genes CsACTIN or AtACTIN were used to normalise the relative expression of related genes using a modified $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method. 

# 4.8 | Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H) and One-Hybrid (Y1H) Assays

For Y2H assays, the ORFs of target genes were subcloned in frame with the GAL4 BD into the pGBKT7 or subcloned in frame with the GAL4 AD into the pGADT7 vector, respectively, according to previously described (Li, Fu, et  al.  2022). Different combinations were cotransformed into the yeast strain AH109 to identify the protein interactions by the growth test on the SD medium without Trp, Leu, His and Ade (QDO, SD/-T-L-H-A). For Y1H assays, approximately 2.0 kb CsMYB184 promoter sequence was inserted into pHIS2.1 vector. Similarly, the ORF of CsMYC2 was subcloned into pGADT7 to generate the CsMYC2-AD construct. The CsMYC2-AD construct was transformed into Y187 harbouring pHIS2-proCsMYB184 to examine whether CsMYC2 is the direct target of CsMYB184. The protein and DNA interaction was tested on the SD medium without His. 

# 4.9 | BiFC And Subcellular Localisation

For BiFC assay, ORFs of target genes (stop codon removed) were subcloned in frame with the YFPN into the pFGC-YN173 vector, or subcloned in frame with the YFPC into the pFGC-YC155 vector. The resulting constructs were then transformed into $A$ . tumefaciens strain GV3101, respectively, for transient assays in tobacco leaves. The strains harbouring different constructs were mixed and infiltrated into leaves of about 3-week-old tobacco plants (Nicotiana benthamiana) according to the method described previously (Li, Fu, et al. 2022). For subcellular localisation analysis, the ORFs of the genes CsJAZ1, CsJAZ12, CsJAZ13, CsDELLA1 and CsDELLA2 were amplified and cloned, respectively, into the pLGFP1301 vector. The constructs were respectively transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 strains for transient expression. Three-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves were used for infiltration with strains harbouring the resulting constructs. The Green fluorescent protein (GFP) fluorescence signals were detected using confocal microscopy (Leica DMi8 M; Germany) at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after infiltration. 

# 4.10 | Pull-Down Assay

The ORFs of CsJAZ1, CsJAZ12 and CsJAZ13 were subcloned into the pGEX-4-T vector to generate GST-CsJAZ1, GST-CsJAZ12 and GST-CsJAZ13, respectively. Similarly, the ORF 

of CsMYB184 was inserted into the pMAL-c2x vector to produce MBP-CsMYB184. The GST, GST-fused CsJAZ1, CsJAZ12, CsJAZ13 and MBP-fused CsMYB184 were expressed in Escherichia coli BL21 and purified by amylose resin beads as described previously (Gao et al. 2021). For the in vitro pull-down assay, about $5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of purified GST, GST-CsJAZ1, GST-CsJAZ12 and GST-CsJAZ13 proteins were respectively incubated with equal MBP-CsMYB184 protein for 2 h at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with gentle rocking in pull-down buffer $2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.4), $2 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, 1mM EDTA, 1mM PMSF and 1mM DTT) containing $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ amylose resin beads. After centrifugation, the residue was further washed five times with TBS buffer $\mathrm { \Delta } ^ { \prime } 1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris (pH 7.4), 150mM NaCl, 1mM EDTA and $1 \%$ Triton X-100). The proteins were resuspended in SDS loading buffer and immunoblotted using the anti-MBP antibody (Proteintech Group) (1:5000) and anti-GST antibody (Beyotime) (1:5000). The immunoblotting signals were scanned with the BioRad (ChemiDoc MP) imaging system. 

# 4.11 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA)

The ORF of CsMYB184CT, CsMYB184CT or CsMYC2 was inserted into the pMAL-c2x vector, and then the constructs were translated into the E. coli BL21 cells, respectively, for fusion protein purification. The MBP-fused CsMYB184NT, CsMYB184CT or CsMYC2 proteins were purified by immobilised glutathione beads (Sangon Biotech Co. Ltd., Shanghai, China) as described previously (Gao et  al.  2021). The EMSA was performed with about $2 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of purified proteins using the EMSA kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer's protocol in $6 . 6 \%$ nondenatured polyacrylamide gel. 

For competitive EMSA assay between CsMYB184 and CsJAZs, $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ MBP-CsMYB184 protein was incubated with equal or twofold GST-CsJAZ1, GST-CsJAZ12, and GST-CsJAZ13 proteins, respectively. Then, the interaction strength between protein complexes and labelled probes was detected using the EMSA kit. The MBP-CsMYB184 alone with labelled probes was the control. For competitive EMSA assay among CsMYB184, CsJAZ12 and CsDELLA1s, $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ MBP-CsMYB184 protein was incubated with equal GST, GST-CsJAZ12 or a mix of GST-CsJAZ12 and GST-CsDELLAs proteins, respectively. Then, the interaction strength between protein complexes and labelled probes was detected using the EMSA kit. 

# 4.12 | Promoter Trans-Activation Assays

Approximately $2 . 0 \mathrm { k b }$ sequence upstream of the translation start codons of the CsMYB184 and TCS1 was amplified from tea genomic DNA using the special primers (Table  S8) and then inserted, respectively, into pGreenII0800-LUC to generate the luciferase reporter constructs. The effector genes CsMYB184, CsJAZ1, CsJAZ12, CsJAZ13, CsMYC2 and CsDELLAs were cloned, respectively, into pGreenII62-SK to generate the effector constructs. Different combinations of reporter and effector constructs were cotransfected in Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts according to the method described previously (Yoo et al. 2007). Luciferase activities were measured by using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega) according to the 

manufacturer's protocol. The Renilla luciferase activity acted as the internal control to normalise the reporter gene expression values. The relative LUC activity was calculated by normalising to that of REN in three biological triplicates. 

# 4.13 | Firefly Luciferase Complementation Imaging (LCI) Assays

The CsMYB184 and CsJAZ12 were cloned, respectively, into pCAMBIA1300-nLUC and pCAMBIA1300-cLUC to generate the CsMYB184-LUCc and CsJAZ12-LUCn constructs. Then the resulting constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 respectively for LCI assays in tobacco leaves. The strains harbouring CsMYB184-LUCc and CsJAZ12- LUCn constructs with the CsDELLAs-pBI121 or the empty pBI121 were mixed (1:1:1) and infiltrated into leaves of about four-week-old tobacco plants (Nicotiana benthamiana). After infiltration, tobacco plants were cultured at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ with a 16/8 h photoperiod. The luminescence was detected after $1 \mathrm { m M }$ luciferin (Sigma, L9504) supplementation with $0 . 0 1 \%$ Triton X-100 in darkness. The images of luminescence were captured using a low-light-cooled CCD imaging apparatus (NightSHADE evo LB985N). 

# 4.14 | Treatment on Tea Plants

For hormone treatment experiments, the tender tea shoots of about 2-year-old cutting seedlings were sprayed with $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Methyl Jasmonate (MeJA), $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Sodium diethyldithiocarbamate, trihydrate (DIECA) or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ gibberellins $( \mathrm { G A } _ { 3 } )$ solution, respectively, while the controls were treated with distilled water. The shoots treated by MeJA were harvested at 0, 12, 24 and $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after treatment for metabolism/transcriptome analyses or section analysis and those treated by $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ were harvested at 0, 12, $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ and 5 days after treatment for metabolism and transcriptome analyses. 

For fungal pathogen treatment, the young leaves of about 2-year-old cutting seedlings or tea seedlings were infected with the C. gloeosporioides pathogen according to the method as described previously (Wang et  al.  2021). The tea cultivars (Camellia sinensis var. sinensis), including AJBC (anjibaicha), HSZY (huangshanzaoya), FY (fuyun), ZN113 (zhenong113), LJ (longjing), WNZ (wuniuzao) and SCZ (shuchazao), were used for treatment. The leaves treated with C. gloeosporioides were harvested at 1 to 3 weeks after treatment for metabolism and RNAseq analyses. Meanwhile, the young leaves were also treated by MeJA $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , GA (50 or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ ), PAC (50 or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ), caffeine (2 mM) or ODN, and then were infected with the C. gloeosporioides pathogen 1 to 3 days after treatment. The lesion area of leaves was measured by ImageJ software to illustrate the fungal resistance after infection. 

# 4.15 | Microscopic Observations

For sections, the first internode (between first leaf and second leaf) was collected and fixed in FAA solution $5 0 \%$ ethanol, $5 \%$ formaldehyde, $5 \%$ acetic acid and $4 0 \%$ distilled water) overnight. 

The samples were covered with $5 \%$ agar after dehydration in ethanol with different concentrations. Approximately 50- to $1 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ -thick sections were cut using a vibratome (VT1200S, Leica, Germany). Then a microscope (DM3000, Leica, Germany) was used for imaging the sections, and the cell length was measured with the ImageJ software. 

# 4.16 | Enzymatic Activity Assay

Enzymatic activity assay of CsGA2ox4 was performed according to the method described previously (Jin et  al.  2023). Briefly, the full-length ORF of CsGA2ox4 was introduced into the pMAL-c2x vector, and then the construct was transformed into the E. coli BL21 cells for fusion protein purification. The MBP-fused CsGA2ox4 protein was purified by immobilised glutathione beads (Sangon Biotech Co. Ltd., Shanghai, China) as described previously (Gao et al. 2021). The enzymatic reaction mixtures $( 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L } )$ contained $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ recombinant CsGA2ox4 protein, $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ GA or $\mathrm { G A } _ { 4 }$ , 5 mM DTT, $1 3 . 3 \mathrm { m M }$ $\propto$ -ketoglutarate, $1 3 . 3 \mathrm { m M }$ ascorbate, $0 . 6 7 \mathrm { m M }$ iron(II) and $5 \mu \mathrm { g } \mu \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ BSA in $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 8.0). The reaction mixture was incubated in an incubator at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 to $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , and then the reaction was stopped by supplying 1/5 volume of 1 M HCl. After that, the ethyl acetate was used for product extraction. After centrifugation at $1 4 0 0 0 \times g$ for 1 min, the upper organic phase was isolated and evaporated by nitrogen and then redissolved in $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ methanol (v/v) solution before LC–MS/MS analysis. 

# 4.17 | Caffeine Staining Assay

The fresh cross-sections of tea leaves were obtained using a vibratome (VT1200S, Leica, Germany). The bismuth potassium iodide was diluted to $0 . 0 4 \mathrm { g / m L }$ in acetic acid. An appropriate amount of staining solution was added to fully cover each sample on the slide. After 30 s, the solution was removed with distilled water for observation with an upright microscope (DM3000, Leica, Germany) under bright field. 

# 4.18 | Transient Transformation and Antisense Oligonucleotides (AsODN) Assay in Tea Plants

The pBI121-CsJAZ12 or pBI121-CsMYC2 and pBI121 plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101 and then were injected into both sides of the leaves with the main vein as the boundary respectively. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ of culture, tea samples were collected respectively to determine the related gene expression and caffeine content. Gene suppression of CsJAZs, CsMYC2 and CsDELLAs in tea plants using AsODN was carried out as described previously (Xie et al. 2014) with slight modifications. Briefly, the AsODN of target genes was designed and selected respectively using Soligo software (Xie et  al.  2014) (Table  S8). The tender tea shoots (SCZ cultivar) with leaves were cut off and partially inserted into Eppendorf tubes to incubate with $2 \mathrm { m L }$ of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ AsODN solution in a plant incubator, and the solution with sense oligonucleotides of the corresponding gene as the control. After 18 to $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of incubation, the tender shoots were collected for further metabolism and transcriptome analysis. Three independent measurements consisted of 10 tea shoots. 

# 4.19 | RNA-Seq Analysis

For RNA-seq, total RNA was extracted separately from samples with the Trizol reagent (Invitrogen, USA) and was purified. The quality and concentration of total RNA were evaluated by Agilent 2100. RNA-seq data were generated with the sequencing libraries constructed by the TruSeq Sample Preparation Kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) on an Illumina HiSeq2000 platform. After removing reads containing adapter, poly-N and lowquality reads, the clean data were obtained and mapped to the fungal genome reported previously (Kong et al. 2023), tobacco genome (Edwards et al. 2017) or tea genome (Wei et al. 2018). Transcripts per million (TPM) were introduced to qualify the expression levels of transcripts. At least two biological repeats were carried out. All transcriptome data generated and used in this study can be downloaded from the tea genomic database Tea Plant Information Archive (TPIA) and the NCBI (PRJNA798825). 

# 4.20 | Bioinformatic Analysis

All heatmap of gene expression patterns were constructed using the TBtools software (Chen, Wu, et al. 2023). The ‘corrplot’ in R package was used for correlation analysis and drawing diagrams (Koutecky 2015). Briefly, the young leaves of different cultivars were used for catechins and caffeine content and lesion area analysis, respectively. Then correlation analysis was performed using the contents of catechins and caffeine with the lesion area in different cultivars. Phylogenetic trees were constructed with MEGA11 software using the neighbour-joining method with 1000 bootstrap replications, and multiple alignments were constructed using the ClustalW2 program. 

# 4.21 | GUS Activity Assays

The promoter of CsMYB184 amplified from tea cultivar (SCZ) was subcloned into pBI121 vector to generate proCsMYB184::- GUS reporter system. Then the A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 harbouring the resulting construct was infiltrated into the about 4-week-oldN.benthamiana leayes,Meanwhile,the infiltrated leaves were treated with Dimethyl sulfoxide or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MeJA. About 2 days after infiltration, the GUS activity was measured by the leaf staining method as described previously (Li, Ye, et  al.  2022). For the CsMYB184 promoter activation assay, the GUS driven by the CsMYB184 promoter with CsMYC2 or GFP control was coexpressed in the same N. benthamiana leaf mediated by A. tumefaciens infiltration. About 2 days after infiltration, GUS activity was detected by the leaf staining method.
