# Summary

Heat stress (HS) has become a major factor limiting crop yields worldwide. HS inhibits plant growth by ROS accumulation, and NADPH oxidases (Rbohs) are major ROS producers in plants. Here, we show that CRISPR/Cas knockout of the OsRbohB (OsRbohB-KO) significantly increased rice tolerance to HS imposed at various different growth stages. We produced OsRbohB-KO and OsRbohB-overexpression (OsRbohB-OE) lines in a japonica cultivar, Nipponbare. Compared with nontransgenic wild-type (WT) plants, the OsRbohB-KO lines showed a significant increase in chlorophyll contents $( 5 . 2 \% - 5 8 . 0 \% )$ ), plant growth $( 4 8 . 2 \% -$ $6 5 . 6 \%$ ) and grain yield $( 8 . 9 \% - 2 0 . 5 \% )$ , while reducing HS-induced ROS accumulation in seeds $( 2 1 . 3 \% - 3 3 . 0 \% )$ ), seedlings $( 1 3 . 0 \% - 3 0 . 4 \% )$ ), anthers $( 1 3 . 1 \% - 2 0 . 3 \% )$ ) and grains $( 9 . 7 \% -$ $2 2 . 1 \%$ ), under HS conditions. Analysis of yield components revealed that the increased yield of OsRbohB-KO plants was due to increased starch synthetase activity, spikelets per panicle $( 2 . 0 \% - 9 . 3 \% )$ , filled spikelets $( 4 . 8 \% - 1 5 . 5 \% )$ ), percentage of filled spikelets $( 2 . 4 \% - 6 . 8 \% )$ and 1000-grain weight $( 2 . 9 \% - 7 . 4 \% )$ under HS conditions during the reproductive stage. Grain milling and appearance quality, and starch content were also significantly increased in OsRbohB-KO plants under HS conditions during the mature stage. Furthermore, OsRbohB-KO significantly upregulated the expression levels of heat shock-related genes, OsHSP23.7, OsHSP17.7, OsHSF7 and OsHsfA2a, in rice seedlings and grains under long-term HS conditions. Conversely, OsRbohB-OE resulted in phenotypes that were opposite to OsRbohB-KO in most cases. Our results suggest that suppression of OsRbohB provides an effective approach for alleviating heat damage and improving grain yield and quality of rice under long-term HS conditions. 

# Introduction

Heat stress has become a major limiting factor for global crop production owing to extremely high temperatures or continuously hot weather (Xu et al., 2018). Plants grown under high-temperature conditions suffer from various types of damage to cells or organs induced by heat stress (Xu et al., 2021). Rice (Oryza sativa L.) is one of the most important food crops for the world’s population, and its yield is expected to decrease by $3 . 2 \%$ owing to a $1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ increase in the mean temperature worldwide (Janni et al., 2020). Rice suffers from different types of damage during its entire growth period when rice is subjected to heat stress. At the germination stage, heat stress at temperatures ${ \ge } 3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ markedly inhibits the germination rate, young buds and radicles (Yang et al., 2021). In rice leaves, heat stress leads to withering, water loss and chlorophyll loss; suppressing the photosynthetic rate and resulting in cell and membrane damage which causes seedling death (Li et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022a). Rice is most sensitive to heat stress during the reproductive 

growth stage. Heat stress damages floral organs and decreases pollen viability in rice during the heading-flowering stage (Wang et al., 2019). At the grain filling stage, heat stress suppresses yield by affecting the starch biosynthesis capacity and reducing rice grain appearance, milling and cooking quality (Ren et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022). Therefore, it is vital to investigate the response of rice to heat stress and explore effective approaches for alleviating heat injury in rice. 

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) play an important signalling role in the regulation of plant growth and response to environmental stress factors (Zhang et al., 2017; Li and Kim, 2021). Under normal conditions, ROS levels are in a dynamically balanced state that is mainly regulated by ROS production and the ROS-scavenging system in plant cells (Zandalinas and Mittler, 2018). However, excess accumulation of ROS occurs in plants when they experience environmental stresses, such as high temperature, salinity and drought, which can disrupt the steady state of ROS (Choudhury et al., 2017; You and Chan, 2015). This may induce severe oxidative stress, which 

is an important mechanism by which heat stress damages plants (Li et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2021). In rice seeds, excess ROS accumulation causes membrane injury and inhibits bud growth of rice under heat stress (Yang et al., 2021). Heat stress results in the over-accumulation of ROS in rice leaves, which causes chlorophyll loss and membrane injury, leading to leaf withering at the seedling stage (Liu et al., 2022a). At the heading-flowering stage, heat stress causes a decrease in ROS-scavenging capacity, resulting in overaccumulation of ROS in rice anthers, which leads to a decline in pollen viability and spikelet fertility at the meiosis stage (Zhao et al., 2017). At the grain filling stage, excess accumulation of ROS in rice grains is an important factor that causes a decrease in filled spikelets, grain weight and final grain yield (Liu et al., 2022b; Zhao et al., 2017). In addition, heat stress stimulates ROS overaccumulation, resulting in an imbalance in redox homeostasis in rice grains, which leads to a decline in rice grain appearance quality (Nevame et al., 2018; Suriyasak et al., 2017). Therefore, reducing ROS content in plant cells to alleviate oxidative stress is an important and effective approach to improve the grain yield of crop plants under heat stress conditions (Dwivedi et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022b). 

Under both normal and environmental stress conditions, plants generate ROS via plasma membrane nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidases, also known as respiratory burst oxidase homologues (Rbohs) (Sagi and Fluhr, 2006). Rbohs regulate plant growth in many plant species (Hu et al., 2020), and multiple Rbohs have been identified, including rice (Groom et al., 1996), tomatoes (Amicucci et al., 1999), tobacco (Yoshioka et al., 2001), Arabidopsis thaliana (Sagi and Fluhr, 2006) and maize (Nestler et al., 2014). They also participate in multiple physiological processes in rice, such as root growth (Wang et al., 2023), seed germination (Zhang et al., 2014), signal regulation in immunoreactions (Yoshiaki et al., 2005) and responses to stress factors (Shi et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2016). In rice, at least nine genes have been predicted to encode Rbohs (Liu et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2013). Yoshiaki et al. (2005) reported that OsRbohB (Accession No. AK120739, Gene ID: Os09g0438000) is involved in ROS production in rice. We previously reported that different expression patterns were observed in rice under heat stress conditions at different growth periods (Liu et al., 2022a, 2023a) and that heat stress induced markedly higher expression levels of OsRbohB in rice seedlings and grains at the seedling, heading and grain filling stages (Liu et al., 2022a, 2023a). In addition, OsRbohB was expressed in all examined tissues including seedling leaves, flag leaf, floret, lemma, palea, stamen, pistil and grain throughout all growth stages. It exhibited significantly higher expression levels in leaves, florets, stamens, pistils and grains (Liu et al., 2023a). These results indicate that OsRbohB-mediated ROS production plays an important role in the response of rice plants to heat stress. 

In the present study, we aimed to determine the effects of OsRbohB knockout or overexpression on rice under heat stress conditions. Several OsRbohB-knockout mutants (OsRbohB-KO) using the CRISPR/Cas9 system and OsRbohB-overexpression (OsRbohB-OE) lines were generated. Growth conditions, ROS accumulation, physiological metabolism, grain yield and gene expression in the OsRbohB-KO and OsRbohB-OE lines were investigated under normal and heat stress conditions. Our results showed that knockout of OsRbohB reduces heat 

stress-induced ROS overaccumulation and improves heat stress tolerance in rice. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

Nipponbare (Oryza sativa subsp. japonica cv Nipponbare) was used as the wild-type (WT) plant in all experiments. The sequence of OsRbohB (Gene ID: Os09g0438000, Accession: AK120739) was obtained from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database. Mutations in OsRbohB were induced using the CRISPR/Cas9 vector system as previously reported (Ma et al., 2015), and target sites were designed using the web-based tool CRISPR-GE/target Design (http://skl.scau.edu. cn/) (Xie et al., 2017), according to the target design principles as described by He et al. (2021). To generate the CRISPR/Cas9 knock mutants, a single guide RNA (sgRNA) targeting the open reading frame (ORF) of OsRbohB (Figure S1a,b) were designed using CRISPR-P (Liu et al., 2017). The sgRNA was cloned into the CRISPR plasmid BGK032 to generate the OsRbohB-KO mutant line (Figure S1b) (Lu et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2022). The target sequence was 5’-GAGTGGCCATAGGCGCATCG-30 . The Oligo dipolymer was created according to the Oligo sequence: UP (50 - 30 ): TGTGTGAGTGGCCATAGGCGCATCG; LW (50 -30 ): AAACC-GATGCGCCTATGGCCACTCA. Then, the Oligo dipolymer was constructed to the CRISPR/Cas vector BGK032. After the progress of transreceptive state and plasmid extracting, the CRISPR/Cas vector was verified by the sequence of PUV4-R: 5’- TCCCAGTCACGACGTTGTAA-30 . These vectors were used to transform Nipponbare plants at the Biogle Gene Editing Center (Changzhou, China) (Zhang et al., 2022). For the OsRbohB-overexpressing transgenic rice plants, the full-length open reading frame of the OsRbohB gene was inserted into the pEXT06/g vector to construct pEXT06/g-OsRbohB to generate OsRbohB-overexpressing transgenic plants (Figure S1c–e) (Zhang et al., 2022). The OsRbohB gene under the control of the ubiquitin promoter was then transformed into WT rice using an Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain EHA105)-mediated technique, according to the method described by Toki et al. (2006). Rice transformation was performed using Wimi Biotechnology (http://www.wimibio.com/), according to the method described by Huang et al. (2022). 

Several primary transformants (T0 plants) were used to produce T1 generation seeds, according to previously reported experimental methods (Liu et al., 2022c). Hygromycin B-resistance (Figure S1f) and genomic DNA tests (Figure S2) were used to select the T1 progenies, which were then selfed to yield T2 seeds homozygous for the transgene, as described by Liu et al. (2022c). Southern blot analysis was conducted for the copies test of the transgenic plants using the hygromycin resistance gene as a probe (Yuan et al., 2019). The southern blot analysis was performed by Zoonbio Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Nanjing, China) using DIG DNA labelling and detection kit (Roche), according to the methods described by Yuan et al. (2019). In addition, qRT–PCR was performed to determine the transcriptional levels of OsRbohB in different lines, compared with WT plants. The primers were designed using Primer 5.0 software outside the gene editing zone. Two OsRbohB-overexpression lines (OE-3 and OE-5) showing apparent overexpression in OsRbohB and two OsRbohB-knockout lines (osrbohb-8 and osrbohb-15) showing apparent base deletion with different callus were used for further experiments. 

# Heat stress application at the germination stage

After surface sterilization, 50 seeds from the different rice lines were germinated in a Petri dish under control and heat stress conditions, as previously described (Yang et al., 2021). The temperature of the no-stress (CK) condition was set as $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and the temperature of the heat stress (HS) condition was set as $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Yang et al., 2021). All the treatments were performed in a controlled growth chamber (SPT-P150C; Shanghai CIMO Medical Instrument Co., Ltd., China) with a 12-h photoperiod and five biological replicates. 

# Heat stress application at the seedling stage

Seeds from the different rice lines were cultivated to approximately the three-leaf stage in a controlled growth chamber under $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ day $1 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ night, $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ photoperiod, and 350 mmol photons $\mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity; according to the experimental methods described by Liu et al. (2022a). The rice seedlings were then transferred to unstressed or heat-stressed conditions. Heat stresses of $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (HS-40) and $4 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (HS-43) were applied to rice seedlings. The growth temperature of the unstressed condition was set as $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ day $1 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ night, and the temperature of heat stress was set as $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ day/35 °C and $4 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ day $1 3 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ night for HS-40 and HS-43, respectively; according to previous studies on the rice response to heat stress in South China (Huang et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2022a; Shi et al., 2015). After 7 days of heat stress, rice seedlings were transferred to unstressed conditions and allowed to recover for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . Rice leaves were sampled to measure growth, physical indices and gene expression levels. Each treatment was repeated five times. 

# Heat stress application at the reproductive growth stage

Seeds from the different rice lines were sown in a nursery bed with humus soil, as described by Liu et al. (2022b). Healthy rice seedlings from the different lines at approximately the three-leaf stage were transplanted into pots, with a height of $2 8 \ c m$ and an internal diameter of $2 5 \ \mathsf { c m }$ , containing $1 0 ~ \mathsf { k g }$ of experimental paddy soil for further growth. The paddy soil was pounded, sifted, dried and then mixed before being placed into the pots and contained $2 6 . 3 ~ \mathrm { g / k g }$ organic matter, $1 . 9 \ 9 / \mathsf { k g }$ total nitrogen, $1 5 . 3 \mathrm { \ m g / k g }$ readily available phosphorus and 106.8 mg/kg readily available potassium. A previously published fertilization method (Wang et al., 2017) was followed. Briefly, 4.5 g a compound fertilizer, in which the levels of N, P and K were all $1 5 \%$ , was applied to each experimental paddy soil pot, and 3.0 g of urea was applied to each pot at the tillering stage for the after manuring. Two clumps of three rice seedlings were transplanted to each pot. Rice plants were cultivated to the booting stage under normal conditions using traditional cultivation management methods. 

Rice plants from the different lines were transferred to unstressed or heat-stressed conditions at the initial heading stage (Ji et al., 2023). The temperature for the unstressed conditions was set as $3 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ day $( 7 ; 0 0 - 1 9 ; 0 0 ) / 2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ night (19:00–7:00), and the temperatures for the heat stress conditions were set as $3 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ from 9:00 to 16:00 and $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ at other times, according to previous studies on the growth of rice in response to heat stress in South China (Huang et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2022b; Shi et al., 2015). The CK group represented rice plants grown under unstressed conditions until maturity. Four detailed heat stress treatment periods were used during the reproductive stage: 1–7 days after initial heading (HS1), 8–14 days after initial heading (HS2), 15–21 days after initial heading (HS3) and 

22–28 days after initial heading (HS4) (Ji et al., 2023). Rice plants were subjected to heat stress for 7 days in each treatment group and then transferred to unstressed conditions for growth to the mature stage. During the heat stress period of 8–14 days after initial heading (8–14 days AIH) and 15–21 days after initial heading (15–21 d AIH), rice grains were sampled to measure ROS content, antioxidant enzyme activities and starch synthesis-related activities. At the mature stage, rice plants were sampled to measure growth indices, yield components, grain appearance, cooking indices and grain physicochemical properties. Plants from the different rice lines were transferred to heat stress conditions from 0 to 21 days after initial heading (0– 21 days AIH), and rice grains were sampled during this heat stress period to measure gene expression levels. Each treatment was repeated five times. 

# Measurement of the germination rate, bud length and main root length

Germination of rice seeds was recorded every $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ during the germination period. Rice seeds were considered to germinate when the bud length was equal to half the length of the seed and the root length was equal to the length of the seed, according to the method described by Lv et al. (2013). The final germination rate, bud length and main root length were calculated on the seventh day (Yang et al., 2021). 

# Measurement of seedling growth

Photographs of growing rice seedlings were taken on the after 4th, 5th, 6th and 7th day of treatment and after recovery for 24 h. At each time point, the withered leaf rate was investigated and recorded as 1 if the whole leaf was dry and brown, whereas it was recorded as 0.5 if half of the leaf was dry and brown (Liu et al., 2022a). 

# Measurement of the chlorophyll content of rice seedlings

Chlorophyll content was measured as described by Wellburn and Lichtenthaler (1984), with some modifications as described by Liu et al. (2022c). Briefly, leaf samples (0.1 g) were extracted using a 5-mL mixture of ethanol $( 2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L } )$ and acetone $( 2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L } )$ ). The absorbance of the supernatant was measured at 645 and 663 nm using a spectrophotometer (UV-2700; Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan). Total chlorophyll content was calculated using the following formula: $( 2 0 . 2 9 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 4 5 } + 8 . 0 5 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 6 6 3 } )$ V/ $( 1 0 0 0 ~ \times ~ \mathsf { W } )$ . The ‘V’ and ‘W’ indicated volume of the extracting solution and weight leaf samples, respectively, in the formula. 

# Measurement of the relative water content of rice seedlings

The relative water content of leaves was measured according to the method described by Wei et al. (2015). Briefly, five rice seedlings were cut, divided into shoots and roots, and the fresh weights of the shoots were measured. Subsequently, the shoot samples were dried in a forced air oven at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then heated at $7 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until a stable mass was reached before the dry mass was determined. The relative water content $( \% )$ was calculated based on the fresh and dry weights. 

# Measurement of MDA content and MI (membrane injury) of rice seedlings

The MDA content was determined by the thiobarbituric acid reaction as described by Heath and Packer (1968). According to 

the experimental methods described in our previous studies (Liu et al., 2022a, 2022c), rice leaf samples were homogenized in 1 mL of $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ phosphate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p } \mathsf { H } \ 7 . 8 \right)$ after being smashed at the refrigerated condition with liquid nitrogen and then centrifuged at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \times \pmb { g }$ for 15 min. Subsequently, $4 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of gsupernatant was mixed with $1 \ : \mathsf { m L }$ of $0 . 5 \%$ thiobarbituric acid, and the mixture was boiled for 20 min. The absorbance of the resulting supernatant was measured at 532, 600 and 450 nm after cooled and centrifuged using a spectrophotometer (UV-2700, Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan). The MDA concentration was calculated using the following formula: $6 . 4 5 \times ( \mathsf { A } _ { 5 3 2 } - \mathsf { A } _ { 6 0 0 } ) -$ $0 . 5 6 \times \mathsf { A } _ { 4 5 0 }$ . Finally, the MDA content in the leaf was calculated according to the fresh leaf weight. 

MI was evaluated using the relative electrolytic conductivity (Liu et al., 2022a; Wei et al., 2015). Relative electrolytic conductivity is represented by the electrolytic conductivity of the leaf effusion before and after boiling (Wei et al., 2015). Rice leaves were randomly selected from each treatment group for the different lines and washed with deionized water to remove the surface-adhered electrolytes. Leaf samples were then submerged in $1 5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of deionized water in $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ conical tubes and incubated at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 hour. Electrical conductivity of the effusion was measured using a DDS-12 conductivity meter (Lida Inc., Shanghai, China) and recorded as R1. The leaf samples were then killed by heating in a boiling bath for 40 min. After cooling to $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , the electrical conduction of the effusion was measured again and recorded as R2. MI was evaluated using the formula: MI $( \% ) = { \sf R } 1 / { \sf R } 2 \times 1 0 0 \%$ . 

# Measurement of plant growth, grain yield and yield components at the mature stage

At the mature stage, all rice plants were harvested to determine the following parameters: shoot length (SL), shoot dry weight (SDW), primary branches (PB) and secondary branches (SB) of panicles, panicle length (PL), panicle weight (PW) and yield components including panicle number (PN), spikelets per panicle (SP), number and weight of filled spikelets (FS) and empty spikelets (ES), percentage of filled spikelets (PFS), thousand grain weight (TGW), harvest index (HI) and grain yield (GY). The GY and yield components were measured according to the method described by Liu et al. (2022c). The TGW and aboveground biomass were adjusted to a $0 . 1 4 ~ 9 / 9$ moisture content on a dry weight basis. HI was calculated as the GY divided by the aboveground biomass. 

# Measurement of rice grain appearance and milling quality

The grain milling quality was measured, including the ratio of brown rice, milled rice and head rice from rice grains, according to the methods described in the National Standard of the People’s Republic of China (GB/T17891-1999, high-quality paddy). As described in the National Standard, brown rice is represented by a hulled grain with no polishing, which retains most of its bran layer, endosperm and germ; milled rice by polished grain, with the peel and aleurone removed; and head rice by milled rice whose length reaches three-quarters or more of the average length of the whole grain. The appearance of milled rice was measured according to the experimental methods described in previous studies (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2013), and included the incidence of chalky kernels and chalkiness, which were represented by the ratio of white opaque parts in the rice grains. 

# Measurement of starch, amylose and amylopectin content in rice grains

Starch content was measured according to the method described by Lopez-Delgado et al. (2005). An ethanol reagent (v/v) was used to separate soluble sugars and starch from the samples. The starch was decomposed to glucose by the acid hydrolysis method, and the glucose content was determined using anthrone colorimetry (Liu et al., 2020). Finally, the starch content of the samples was calculated based on the glucose concentration, according to the method described by Lopez-Delgado et al. (2005). 

Amylose and amylopectin were extracted from milled rice using $80 \%$ ethanol (v/v) and diethyl ether. A potassium hydroxide solution was then added to dissolve the sediment containing amylose and amylopectin, and potassium iodide-iodine reagent was added. The absorbance at 550, 620 and 743 nm was then measured to calculate the content and percentage of amylose and amylopectin, according to the method described by Ahmed et al. (2015). 

# Measurement of ROS levels

The ${ \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ content was measured by monitoring nitrite formation from hydroxylamine in the presence of ${ \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ , using the method described by Elstner and Heupel (1976). The absorbance of the aqueous solution at 530 nm was measured to calculate the levels of ${ { \sf O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ from the chemical reaction of ${ \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ and hydroxylamine. 

The ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ content was measured by monitoring the absorbance at 415 nm of the titanium-peroxide complex, as previously described by Brennan and Frenkel (1977). The absorbance values in the aqueous solution were measured at $4 1 5 \ \mathsf { n m }$ to calculate the levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ . 

According to the theory and methods aforementioned, the analytical reagents used to measure the $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ contents were acquired from the determination kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Suzhou Michy Biomedical Technology Co., Ltd., China) (Liu et al., 2022a, 2022b). 

# Measurement of antioxidant enzyme activities

The activity levels of superoxide dismutase (SOD), peroxidase (POD), catalase (CAT) and ascorbate peroxidase (APX) were measured using a UV–visible spectrophotometer (UV-2700, Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan) at the relevant wavelengths (Liu et al., 2019). The SOD (EC 1.15.1.1) activity was determined using the nitro blue tetrazolium (NBT) method described by Giannopolitis and Ries (1977). One unit of SOD activity was defined as the amount of enzyme required to cause $50 \%$ inhibition of NBT reduction as monitored at 560 nm. POD (EC 1.11.1.7) activity was determined by assessing the rate of guaiacol oxidation in the presence of $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ (Klapheck et al., 1990). One unit of POD activity was defined as the change in absorbance per minute at 470 nm. CAT (EC 1.11.1.6) activity was determined based on the reaction level with $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ per unit time (Aebi, 1984). One unit of CAT activity was defined as the catalytic degradation amount of $1 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ per g tissue per minute. APX (EC 1.11.1.11) activity was determined by measuring the rate of ascorbic acid oxidation by $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ (Nakano and Asada, 1981). One unit of APX was defined as the oxidized amount of 1 nmol ascorbic acid per gram of tissue per minute. 

# Measurement of starch synthetase activities

ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase, EC 2.7.7.27) activity was determined by measuring the rate of increase in NADPH 

levels at $3 4 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , according to the method described by Choix et al. (2014). The activities of soluble starch synthase (SSS, EC 2.4.1.13) and granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS, EC 2.4.1.21) were determined by measuring the rate of ADP generation in the reaction between ADPG and glucan, according to the method described by Jiang et al. (2003). The absorbance at 340 nm was measured to calculate the activity of SSS or GBSS. Starch-branching enzyme (SBE; EC 2.4.1.18) was used as the catalyst for the transformation of amylose to amylopectin at 660 nm. The activity of SBE was determined by the rate of absorbance decrease of the starch-iodine complex at 660 nm, according to the method described by Jiang et al. (2003). The activity of the starch-debranching enzyme (DBE, EC 2.4.1.41) was determined by measuring the content of reducing sugars generated by DBE, according to the method described by Wang et al. (2013). The reducing sugar content was measured using the 3, 5-dinitrosalicylic acid method, according to the method described by Rahman et al. (1998). Sucrose synthase (SS, decomposition direction, EC 2.4.1.13) catalyses the decomposition of sucrose into fructose and uridine diphosphate glucose (Schrader and Sauter, 2002). The SS activity was determined by measuring the reducing sugar content using the 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid method described by Schrader and Sauter (2002). 

# RNA isolation and quantitative real-time PCR (qRT–PCR)

Rice grains were snap frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground using a benchtop ball mill (Scientz-48; Ningbo Scientz Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Ningbo, China) at $5 0 ~ \mathsf { H z }$ for 30 s. Total RNA was extracted using the TRIzol reagent (TaKaRa Bio Tokyo, Japan), and first-strand cDNA was synthesized using M-MLV reverse transcriptase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), according to the manufacturers’ protocols. qRT–PCR was performed to determine the transcriptional levels of OsRbohB (Liu et al., 2012; Yoshiaki et al., 2005) and four heat shock-related genes, OsHSP23.7 (Zou et al., 2012), OsHSP17.7 (Sato and Yokoya, 2008), OsHSF7 (Liu et al., 2009) and OsHsfA2a (Wang et al., 2013). Gene-specific primers were designed using the Primer 5.0 software (Table S1). The housekeeping gene $\beta$ -actin (GenBank ID: X15865.1) was used as an internal standard. The PCR amplification was performed as previously described (Liu et al., 2022a) using a qTOWER2.2 (Analytic Jena, Germany). The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C T }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). 

# Experimental design and statistical analyses

All experiments were performed with five biological replicates. Statistical analyses were performed using the statistical software SPSS version 21.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) and Duncan’s multiple range test was used to analyse the differences among the different lines, and $P < 0 . 0 5$ was considered statistically significant. 

# Results

# Phenotypic analysis of OsRbohB-KO and OsRbohB-OE plants

OsRbohB-KO caused a base deletion in the gene sequence in the OsRbohB-KO lines. A 55 base and 35 base deletion in the exon region was introduced into the ORF of osrbohb-8 and osrbohb-15 rice lines, respectively (Figure S2a). Southern blot analysis revealed that the transgenic lines of OE-3 and OE-5 showed single copy of 

OsRbohB was integrated into the genomes (Figure S2b), with obvious higher expression levels of OsRbohB. Compared with WT plants, the expression levels of OsRbohB were significantly decreased in the OsRbohB-KO lines, whereas it was significantly increased in the OsRbohB-OE plants (Figure S2c). The plant length of the OsRbohB-KO was slightly shorter than that of the WT plants, whereas the OsRbohB-OE showed a slightly longer plant length than that of the WT plants (Figure S3). Significant differences in plant length among the different groups were observed at the seedling and heading stages, whereas no significant differences were observed at the filling stage. 

# OsRbohB-KO improved the germination rate and young bud growth under heat stress conditions

Compared to the WT, OsRbohB-KO significantly inhibited seed germination under unstressed conditions, as shown by the lower germination rates in the OsRbohB-KO lines at 24, 48, 72 and 96 h (Figure 1). Heat stress at 24 and $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ promoted seed germination, as shown by the higher germination rates compared to unstressed conditions, whereas long-term heat stress inhibited seed germination (Figure 1b). With continued heat stress, a higher seed germination rate in the OsRbohB-KO lines was observed at 120 and 144 h compared to WT. Plants generate excess ROS under environmental stress, which cause irreversible damage to cellular membranes, DNA, RNA and proteins (Choudhury et al., 2017). Excess ROS-induced cellular injury is an important mechanism by which stress factors affect plant growth (Kao, 2017; Sewelam et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2017). Alkaline stress causes severe root injury, and seedling death in rice is observed owing to the extreme accumulation of ROS in the roots induced by alkaline stress (Liu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2017). Yang et al. (2021) subjected rice seeds to heat stress conditions for 7 days and showed that the growth of buds and radicles was inhibited owing to severe membrane injury induced by the overaccumulation of ROS in these structures. Heat stress results in the upregulation of the OsRboh family of genes in rice seedlings and causes excess accumulation of ROS in rice leaves, followed by increased chlorophyll loss, water loss, membrane injury and even leaf withering (Liu et al., 2022a). In the present study, the growth of WT plants and OsRbohB-OE lines was better than that of the OsRbohB-KO lines at the germination (Figure 1) and seedling stages (Figure 2) under normal conditions, which is consistent with the fact that appropriate levels of ROS are required for normal rice growth (Miller et al., 2010). Under long-term heat stress, OsRbohB-KO decreased the ROS production rate in rice cells and mitigated MI in rice buds, leaves and anthers (Figures 1, S4, and 3), ultimately promoting rice plant growth. These data suggest that OsRbohB-KO decreased the ROS content in rice under heat stress and mitigated damage to the cellular structure to support normal growth at the vegetative growth stage. 

It is well known that heat stress during the reproductive growth stage has the most severe impact on rice yield (Prasad et al., 2017). Previous studies have shown that heat stress causes pollen abortion, affects the meiosis process and starch synthesis capacity and decreases grain yield and quality (Chen et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022). These effects on grain yield have been reported to be associated with ROS overaccumulation in rice cells (Liu et al., 2022b; Suriyasak et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2014). Excess accumulation of ROS in rice anthers decreases pollen viability and heat shock proteins (HSPs) contents, which is associated with the formation of filled spikelets, grain filling and chalkiness under heat stress at the grain filling stage (Liu et al., 2022b; Nevame et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2014). Therefore, decreasing ROS accumulation in rice floral organs and grains is an effective approach to improve grain yield and quality during the reproductive growth stage (Dwivedi et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022b; Shi et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2017). In the present study, the OsRbohB-KO lines showed significantly lower ROS and MDA content in the anthers (Figure 3) at the head-flowering stage and lower ROS content in the grains (Figure 4) at the grain filling stage, leading to better filled spikelets, higher PFS and TGW, and a higher harvest index and grain yield at the mature stage. Furthermore, OsRbohB-KO improved the appearance, milling and cooking of rice grains during maturation. We also found that the activity levels of antioxidant enzymes (Figures 3 and 4) and starch synthesis-related enzymes (Figure S5) in the OsRbohB-KO lines were lower than those in the WT and OsRbohB-OE lines during the early stages of heat stress, whereas they were significantly higher in the OsRbohB-KO lines under long-term heat stress conditions. Severe long-term heat stress caused excess accumulation of ROS, resulting in serious damage to the cellular structure; decreased activity levels of antioxidant and starch synthesis-related enzymes; inhibition of the starch synthesis process; decreased amylose and amylopectin content; and decreased grain yield and quality. Collectively, our results 

show that under long-term heat stress conditions, OsRbohB-KO improved rice grain yield and quality; mainly by alleviating ROS overaccumulation and improving starch synthesis capacity in rice grains during the reproductive growth stage. 

Plants cope with various environmental stress factors by adjusting their gene expression networks (Liu et al., 2019, 2022a). Many abiotic stress-related genes and proteins have been reported to participate in the regulatory network involving the response of rice to heat stress (Deng et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2022). Among these genes and proteins, HSPs are vital defence mechanisms in rice and are regulated by heat shock transcription factors (HSFs) (Hagymasi et al., 2022; Santoro, 2000). Under heat stress, the expression of HSP- and HSF-related genes can be induced by ROS or plant hormones (Haider et al., 2022; Jacob et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2022a). In the present study, the expression of four heat shock-related genes, OsHSP23.7, OsHSP17.7, OsHSF7 and OsHsfA2a, was induced by heat stress factors, and a higher degree of induction was observed in OsRbohB-OE lines during the early heat stress period (Figure 6). However, the expression of all four heat shock-related genes exhibited a single-peak curve that decreased at the end of the heat stress period, and the expression levels in the OsRbohB-KO lines were significantly higher than those in the WT and OsRbohB-OE lines (Figure 6). These data suggest that under heat stress conditions, ROS participates in the synthesis of HSPs by regulating the expression of heat shockrelated genes. However, severe or long-term heat stress decreases HSP content by inducing overaccumulation of ROS in plant cells (Zhang et al., 2014). 

Previous studies have shown that heat damage to rice is positively correlated with the increasing temperature and duration (Prasad et al., 2006; Yang et al., 2020). Seed germination and seedling growth of rice are severely inhibited when subjected to heat stress for a 7-day period (Liu et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2021). During the reproductive growth stage, different degrees of heat injury in rice are associated with high temperature duration, and a high temperature duration of 5–7 days can result in a severe effect on rice spikelet fertility (Wang et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2008). In the present study, rice seeds, seedlings and plants of the WT or OsRbohB-OE lines showed better growth status and higher antioxidant enzyme activity than the OsRbohB-KO lines under normal or short-term heat stress conditions, which were probably associated with more appropriate ROS levels. However, a high temperature duration of 5–7 days or more induced the continued accumulation of ROS; resulting in multiple damage to the cell structure or physiological metabolism, including higher MI and MDA content (Figures 1 and 3), and decreased ROS-scavenging activity (Figures 3 and 4), starch synthesis capacity (Figure S5) and expression levels of heat shock-related genes (Figure 6) in rice. These results indicate that long-term heat stress causes severe injury to rice, mainly due to the excess accumulation of ROS in cells and subsequent MI, imbalance in the enzyme reaction system and decrease in HSPs. 

In this study, we have shown that genome-editing knockout of OsRbohB reduced the ROS accumulation and thus improved cellular integrity, floral development, starch synthesis and ultimately grain yield and quality under heat stress conditions. These findings highlight OsRbohB as a key negative regulator of heat stress response in rice, making it a valuable target for breeding heattolerant varieties through precise genome editing or targeted mutations (Figure 7). Additionally, it also presents a target for developing specific chemical inhibitors for the OsRbohB enzyme 

through a chemical biology approach, providing another potential means to enhance heat tolerance in rice (Figure 7). These results provided an effective approach in breeding heat-resistant rice varieties for potential agricultural use in high grain quality rice varieties, such as high-yield and cooking quality indica rice varieties.
