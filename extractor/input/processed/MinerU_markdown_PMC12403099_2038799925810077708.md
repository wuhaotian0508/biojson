# Factors underlying a latitudinal gradient in the S/G lignin monomer ratio in natural poplar variants

The chemical composition of wood plays a pivotal role in the adaptability and structural integrity of trees. However, few studies have investigated the environmental factors that determine lignin composition and its biological significance in plants. Here, we examined the lignin syringyl-to-guaiacyl (S/G) ratio in members of a Populus trichocarpa population sourced from their native habitat and conducted a genome wide association study to identify genes linked to lignin formation. Our results revealed many significant associations, suggesting that lignin biosynthesis is a complex polygenic trait. Additionally, we found an increase in the S/G ratio from northern to southern geographic origin of the trees sampled, along with a corresponding metabolic and transcriptional reprogramming of xylem cell wall biosynthesis. Further molecular analysis identified a mutation in a cell wall laccase genetically associated with higher S/G ratios that predominate in trees from warmer lower latitudes. Collectively, our findings suggest that lignin heterogeneity arises from an evolutionary process enabling poplar adaptation to different climatic challenges.

Wood has been used for millennia as fuel, construction material, and for making tools, furniture, and paper. The main constituents of wood are carbohydrates, including cellulose (40 to $5 0 \%$ ) and hemicellulose (15 to $2 5 \%$ ), and lignin (20 to $3 0 \%$ ). The important industrial and societal uses of carbohydrates derived from plants are indisputable. However, it was not until the last decade that lignin emerged as a highly valuable raw material for the synthesis of chemicals and replacement of polymers derived from fossil fuels ( 1 , 2 ). Today, nearly half of the global wood supply originates from planted forests, with short-rotation species like Populus increasingly cultivated to meet the demand for timber, pulp, and renewable materials. 

The lignin biosynthetic pathway is a major carbon sink in plants, especially in trees, representing up to $3 0 \%$ of the total biomass ( 3 ). Lignin synthesis in plant cells starts in the plastids, with the formation of the aromatic amino acids derived from the shikimate pathway ( 4 ). The aromatic ring of phenylalanine is decorated through a series of enzymatic reactions to produce lignin monomers or monolignols ( Fig. 1 ). Once synthesized in the cytosol, monolignols are exported across the plasma membrane to the cell wall, where lignin polymerization is mediated by peroxidases (PRXs) and laccases (LACs) ( 5 ), forming the three primary subunits: $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -hydroxyphenyl (H-units), guaiacyl (G-units), and syringyl (S-units) ( Fig. 1 ). The molecular structure of the lignin polymer has practical implications, affecting the efficiency of chemical delignification processes in paper pulp and biofuel production ( 6 – 8 ). For instance, angiosperm wood with high syringyl-to-guaiacyl (S/G) ratio is easier to delignify using less chemicals and energy per ton of dry wood and resulting in higher pulp quality and yield ( 9 , 10 ). Conversely, wood with low S/G ratios is more resistant to chemical and biological deconstruction ( 11 , 12 ), limiting carbon cycling within forest ecosystems and contributing to global carbon sequestration. 

The evolution of lignin biosynthesis has been crucial for the adaptation of plants to terrestrial environments. The emergence of this biochemical pathway enabled plants to develop robust arboreal structures, resist microbial decay, and transport water efficiently, allowing trees to conquer terrestrial habitats and shape the course of life on Earth ( 13 ). Notably, earlier-evolving gymnosperm softwoods are devoid of S-units, whereas angiosperm hardwoods such as poplars typically contain S-rich lignins ( 14 ). In general, H-units are present in plant stems at very low levels $( < 5 \% )$ , and catechyl or C-units have only been found naturally in seed coats of a few species ( 15 – 17 ). # Significance

Lignin is a phenolic polymer in plant cell walls that provides mechanical support and facilitates water and nutrient transport through the vascular system. While lignin composition is known to change in response to environmental factors, its natural variation and adaptive significance remain poorly understood. This study demonstrates a latitudinal gradient in lignin composition in 433 poplar trees collected from the natural range of this species along the Northwest coast of North America. We found a coordinated metabolic and transcriptional shift and identified a mutation in a cell wall laccase genetically associated with this geographical pattern. These findings advance our understanding of the ecological function of lignin across latitudes and offer insights for developing sustainable forestry and bioenergy practices.

For example, certain lignin subunits can act as deterrents to pests and pathogens ( 21 , 22 ) and their proportions can be altered in response to environmental factors such as drought, mechanical stress, and ultraviolet radiation ( 23 , 24 ), helping trees adapt to changing environments. Previous studies have shown that this stress-induced lignin deposition is coordinated through regulatory networks involving transcription factors (TFs) such as NACs and MYBs ( 25 , 26 ), as well as microRNAs like miR397 that regulate laccase genes during lignin polymerization under stress conditions ( 27 – 29 ). While these observations show that the relative proportion of the lignin subunits in the polymer is tightly regulated spatially, temporally, and in response to environmental stimuli, the precise genetic and biochemical factors that determine lignin composition across a broad environmental range remain largely unexplored. In this study, we used a high-throughput lignin analysis protocol to examine wood samples from 433 poplar trees (Populus trichocarpa ) collected from the natural range of this species along the Pacific coast in northwestern North America. By integrating population genomics, protein structural modeling, enzyme specificity studies, and geographic analyses, we provide a rationale to explain a gradient in plant lignin monomer composition based on latitude. This study also emphasizes the role of environmental factors in shaping the biochemical pathways of plants. 

# Results

Native Poplar Trees Display Diverse Lignin Profiles. To better understand the natural phenotypic variation of lignin in poplar populations, we first measured the amount and composition of 

lignin in developing xylem tissue samples collected from 433 trees of a wild-sourced $P .$ trichocarpa association population, clonally propagated, and grown for 5 y in a common garden located in Clatskanie, Oregon (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). Young developing xylem samples were obtained by gently removing the bark of the tree stem and collecting the xylem cells using razor blades (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). We extracted cell wall material from these tissues and estimated the total lignin content determined by the acetyl bromide method and the lignin monomer distribution by thioacidolysis followed by GC-MS (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S2 A and B). Our dataset revealed a wide inherent variation in lignin composition among the natural poplar population (Fig. 2A and Dataset S1). Lignin composition ranged from 0.1 to $5 . 5 \%$ H, 0.0 to $3 . 4 \%$ C, 17 to $6 5 \%$ G, and 34 to $8 3 \%$ S subunits, and S/G ratios ranged from 0.5 to 4.8, which indicates that the proportional variation in S/G ratio is due to significant changes in the levels of both thioacidolysis-released monomers (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). On average, the H:C:G:S molar ratio of lignin in newly formed wood samples from 433 poplar trees was 0.6:0.4:41:58, with an S/G ratio of 1.51 (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S2A). The fragmentation 

patterns and retention times of the C-lignin monomer identified in poplar matched those from positive controls extracted from vanilla seed coats, supporting the presence of trace amounts of Clignin $( < 4 \% )$ in young poplar xylem tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 $A$ and $B$ ). 

Our results also showed variability in the total amount of lignin deposited in the young developing xylem tissues, ranging from 43 to $1 3 8 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ per g of cell wall residue (CWR), with an average 65 $\mathrm { m g / g }$ CWR across the entire poplar population (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B ). We noticed that the CWRs of the xylem samples from the trees with the highest lignin content (above $1 1 5 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ per g CWR) displayed a darker color than the samples with average lignin levels (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). When observed under the microscope, the samples with the highest lignin content showed intact cell walls, indicating the presence of multiple tissues, probably originating from epicormic branches formed in the stems of these trees and harvested during sample collection. These samples were excluded for downstream analyses. Lignification Is a Highly Polygenic Trait. The same developing xylem tissue samples were used for RNA extraction and whole genome sequencing to determine the loci genetically associated with the natural variation in lignin content and composition (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B). Genome-wide association study (GWAS) analyses were conducted to identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) associated with lignin quantity and composition (Fig.  2B). The thioacidolysis lignin yields and the composition of the less abundant H- and C-subunits were associated with more significant SNPs ${ } ^ { \circ }$ $< 1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 8 } )$ ) than the S and G lignin proportion and S/G ratios $( P > 1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 8 } $ ). The GWAS results identified SNPs significantly associated with lignin traits on all 19 chromosomes annotated to the v.4.1 P. trichocarpa reference genome (Fig. 2B and SI Appendix, Table S1). Ten genes were identified as candidates by GWAS analysis with associations with phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (COMT1, MYB15, WAT1, CAD1, and DIR5) and cell wall synthesis and organization (IRX2, EXP12, WRKY6, and RING) based on Arabidopsis thaliana homolog annotations in The Arabidopsis Information Resource (www.arabidopsis.org). For example, the association between WAT1 and the S and G lignin composition in the natural poplar population is supported by the reduced levels of S-lignin seen in wat1 knockout mutants of A. thaliana (30). Similarly, the MYB15-dependent synthesis of H-lignin observed in A. thaliana (31) is consistent with the GWAS implication of MYB15 involvement in H-lignin deposition in our poplar-developing xylem samples. Additionally, 41 loci including genes with undefined roles in lignification and mainly associated with different aspects of plant growth and development (EMB1381, EMF1, MGP2 CEP12, or C3H and C2H2 TFs), cell division (PHB3, WOX11, and GRAS and NAC TFs), and defense or stress response (P2K2, PUB23, RPP27, RPP5L, RLK1, and WAKL4 and WAKL9) were identified as candidates (SI Appendix, Table S1). In line with previous studies (32, 33), our GWAS analyses revealed many significant associations, suggesting that lignin biosynthesis is a complex polygenic trait. 

Laccase Expression Correlates with Lignin Composition. To further identify genes associated with the phenotypic variation in lignin composition, we first determine the canonical lignin pathway genes in the poplar wood samples characterized (SI  Appendix, Table  S2). The members of the different lignin gene families were identified based on previous studies in poplar (34–36), their expression levels in the developing xylem tissues, and the protein sequence similarity $( > 6 0 \% )$ to their A. thaliana counterparts. Our analysis identified a total of 63 candidate lignin biosynthetic genes, 110 PRXs, and 60 LACs (Dataset S2). The core lignin genes were considered those with expression levels ${ > } 0 . 5 \%$ of PtCOMT1 (Potri.012G006400), the most highly expressed lignin gene in developing xylem, and included 17 LACs, 5 PALs, 5 PRXs, 3 APX/C3Hs, 3 COMTs, 3 CCoAOMTs, 3 C4Hs, 2 CADs, 2 CSEs, 2 F5Hs, 2 HCTs, 2 4CLs, 1 CCR, and 1 C3´H (Dataset  S3). A short list of these genes, including 20 monolignol biosynthetic genes and 17 lignin polymerizing genes, with FPKM values above $2 \%$ of PtCOMT1 is shown in SI Appendix, Table S2. Interestingly, LACs were the most prominent lignin gene family, making up 12 out of the 17 putative lignin polymerizing genes (SI Appendix, Table S2), suggesting that the LAC gene family plays an active role in lignin formation in poplar xylem tissues. 

To explore how these core lignin genes influence the S/G ratio in lignin, we compared their transcript levels in poplar trees exhibiting extreme phenotypes. We focused on 24 trees with the highest and lowest S/G ratios (aver. $\mathrm { S } / \mathrm { G } = 2 . 7 2$ vs. $\mathrm { S } / \mathrm { G } = 0 . 7 4$ ) ( Fig. $3 A$ and $B$ and Dataset S4 ). The extreme highest and lowest S/G ratio genotypes did not differ in the total amount of lignin present in the young wood samples characterized ( Fig. 3C ). Ferulate 5-hydroxylase (F5H) is a cytochrome P450-dependent monooxygenase required for the biosynthesis of S-lignin in poplar ( 37 ). As expected, both poplar F5H genes (Potri.005G117500 and Potri.007G016400) were upregulated in the genotypes with high S-lignin deposition ( Fig. 3D ). Other lignin biosynthetic genes, such as C4H1 (Potri.013G157900) and CCoAOMT4 (Potri.008G136600), were also upregulated in the high S/G ratio genotypes, whereas APX1/ C3H2 (Potri.016G084800) was upregulated in the genotypes with the highest G-units of lignin. Most core lignin biosynthetic genes showed positive correlation in their expression, except COMT2 (Potri.015G003100), COMT4 (Potri.014G106600), and CAD2 (Potri.001G307200) (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). In contrast to LACs, all lignin PRXs, including the three bifunctional cytosolic APX1/ C3Hs ( 38 ), exhibited a negative correlation with most lignin genes, and a higher association with the deposition of G-lignin, supporting a different functional role of the lignin polymerizing enzymes (PRX and LAC, Fig. 1 ) in poplar wood cell walls. Notably, specific LACs exhibited contrasting expression patterns. For example, LAC4-4 (Potri.008G064000), LAC4-6 (Potri.010G193100), and LAC17-3 (Potri.006G087100) were highly upregulated in trees with the highest S-unit deposition, whereas LAC5-2 (Potri.010G183600) and LAC12-1 (Potri.008G073800) were upregulated in trees with the highest proportion of G-lignin ( Fig. $3 D$ and Dataset S3 ). Overall, these results suggest that the expression patterns of specific laccase genes could influence the lignin S/G monomer ratio in poplar trees. 

PtLAC17-3 Oxidizes S- and C- Over G-Monomers, Excluding the H Monomer. To investigate the potential role of LAC genes in determining lignin composition in poplar, we selected and cloned PtLAC17-3 (Potri.006G087100) and PtLAC5-2 (Potri.010G183600) based on their transcript abundance levels and differential expression in trees with extreme S/G ratios (Fig. $3 D$ and Dataset S3). The full-length DNA sequences, with the signal peptide coding sequence removed to optimize expression, were human codon-optimized and subcloned into the pGEn2‐DEST expression vector. This construction was subsequently transfected into HEK293 cells for protein expression (Fig. 4A). PtLAC17-3 was successfully expressed and purified from the human kidney cell extracts and showed a molecular weight between $1 1 0 \mathrm { k D a }$ and $1 3 0 \mathrm { k D a }$ under denaturing conditions in SDS-PAGE (Fig. 4A). However, despite many attempts, we were unable to express PtLAC5-2. Therefore, in vitro competition assays were performed with PtLAC17-3 toward all monolignols in a single reaction mixture, and substrate uptake was analyzed by HPLC (Fig. 4B). There was a 30 to $4 0 \%$ inhibitory effect of C-OH and/ or S-OH on the oxidation of G-OH by PtLAC17-3. A Single PtLAC17-3 Mutation Boosts G-Monomer Oxidation. To provide insight into possible determinants of substrate specificity, we compared X-ray crystal structures of a laccase from Zea mays (ZmLAC3) that preferentially oxidizes S-OH substrates (43) to structural models of PtLAC17-3 and PtLAC5-2 generated with AlphaFold2 (Fig.  4 $D$ and $E$ and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S8). Both PtLAC variants share ${ \sim } 4 5 \%$ sequence identity with ZmLAC3 (SI Appendix, Fig. S9) and their active site residues are identical with one exception (Fig.  4 $E$ and $F _ { . }$ ). A444 in PtLAC17-3 is replaced by T441 at the corresponding position in PtLAC5-2. Previous research has shown that PtLAC14 (Potri.006G 096900), required for the deposition of G-lignin (44), also possesses a polar residue (S432) instead of the T441 found in PtLAC5-2 (Fig. 4F). Moreover, two other LACs highly expressed in G-rich poplar wood, PtLAC17-5 and PtLAC2, differ at two active site positions compared to PtLAC17-3. In both enzymes, V443 and 

A444 are substituted with F441 and G442, respectively (Fig. 4F), suggesting that these two active site residues could influence Gmonomer oxidation. 

To test whether single or double mutations in the active site of the PtLAC17-3 are able to shift the substrate preference toward G-OH, we generated the single mutants A444T and A444S, and the double mutant $\mathrm { V } 4 4 3 \mathrm { F } + \mathrm { A } 4 4 4 \mathrm { G }$ ( Fig. 4F ). The kinetic analysis indicated that all three mutants, particularly A444T, were more efficient than the wild-type PtLAC17-3 protein at oxidizing G-OH, especially after longer incubation times ( Fig. 4G ). We then determined the kinetic properties of wild-type and A444T mutant PtLAC17-3. Wild-type PtLAC17-3 exhibited ninefold higher catalytic efficiency $( k _ { c a t } / K _ { m } )$ for oxidizing both S-OH and C-OH over G-OH, and enzyme displayed higher $K _ { m }$ and lower $V _ { m a x }$ values for G-OH than for S-OH (SI Appendix, Table S3 ), confirming that PtLAC17-3 kinetically prefers the S-OH monolignol rather than G-OH. In addition, the activity of the A444T mutant toward G-OH increased to 4.5-fold in $k _ { c a t } / K _ { m }$ when compared to that of the wild-type protein. The ratios between the $k _ { c a t } /$ $K _ { m }$ values for G-OH and S-OH for the wild-type PtLAC17-3 and the A444T mutant were approximately 9.1 and 2.5, respectively (SI Appendix, Table S3 ). Therefore, the A444T mutant displayed higher activity toward G-OH than the wild-type protein. It is likely that subtle differences in the positioning of side chains or, as observed in X-ray structures of ZmLAC3 (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ), displaceable water molecules in the active site can influence substrate binding specificity. Thus, minor changes in the active site of PtLAC17-3 can lead to substantial differences in monolignol oxidation rates in vitro. Genetic validation in poplar will ultimately be required to confirm the role of PtLAC17-3 in lignin polymer composition. 

Poplars with Lower S/G Are Found at Higher Latitudes. The relative distribution of lignin monomers in plant tissues typically varies in response to environmental stress (25). To investigate the ecological significance of the observed broad natural variation in lignin composition, we first mapped the geographical origin of the poplar variants with extreme lignin phenotypes. We observed a significant clinal increase in S/G ratio from north to south. The poplar trees with the highest S/G ratios (2.3 to 4.8) originated from southern locations, whereas the lowest S/G ratios (0.5 to 0.8) were predominantly sourced from northern latitudes (Fig. 5A and SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Notably, the geographic distribution of variants with extreme lignin composition correlates with terrestrial temperature but not with precipitation patterns (Fig. 5A and SI Appendix, Fig. S10). These results show that poplar trees with high S/G are mainly distributed in low latitudes with high temperature areas. To further confirm this finding, we performed an SNP-based phylogenetic analysis using the whole-genome sequencing data of the population. This analysis identified five major genetic groups or subpopulations (Clades I-V) that clustered poplar variants with the same geographical origin (Fig. 5 B and C). These data show that there is genome-wide genetic structure at spatial scale. 

To investigate the influence of natural selection and spatial adaptation on lignin composition, we performed two complementary tests. First, we found that quantitative differentiation $( \mathrm { Q } _ { \mathrm { S T } } )$ in $G \%$ , $S \%$ , and S/G was greater than genome-wide marker differentiation at neutral sites among subpopulations $( \mathrm { F _ { S T } } )$ , indicating that divergence in lignin composition between subpopulations is driven by natural selection ( Fig. $5 D$ ). We also observed a clinal increase in S/G values from northern to southern subpopulations ( Fig. $5 E _ { \prime }$ ). Second, we analyzed the relationship between lignin composition and latitude, which showed significant 

correlations consistent with spatially divergent selection ( Fig. 5F ). These results in total indicated that subpopulations from lower latitudes display higher S/G, supporting the notion that latitude significantly influences the adaptive genetic variation in lignin composition throughout the sampled distribution of $P _ { \cdot }$ trichocarpa . 

To further identify possible links between allelic variations and changes in lignin composition, we extended the Allele Catalog Tool ( 45 ) for $P _ { \cdot }$ trichocarpa . Among the 20 monolignol biosynthetic genes examined (Dataset S5 ), only the allelic variants in Pt4CL1 (Potri.001G036900) displayed a significant genetic association with the variation in the S/G ratio, with each of the two mutations leading to higher S/G ratios (SI Appendix, Fig. S11A ). These mutations (C54Y and E137D) were found in the AMP-dependent synthetase/ligase domain of Pt4CL1 and exhibited no discernible geographic pattern (SI Appendix, Fig. S11B ). Among 17 lignin polymerizing genes analyzed (Dataset S5 ), we found a H64D mutation in PtLAC4-2 (Potri.006G097000) genetically associated with increased lignin S/G ratios ( Fig. 5G ). Interestingly, poplar variants carrying the PtLAC4-2 alleles resulting in the H64D mutation are more frequent in lower latitudes. In contrast, the distribution of variants carrying the wild-type PtLAC4-2 alleles shifts toward northern areas in BC, Canada ( Fig. $_ { 5 G }$ and SI Appendix, Table S4 ). Furthermore, we performed genetic differentiation $( \mathrm { F _ { S T } } )$ analysis in a 50-kb sliding window surrounding PtLAC4-2 (SI Appendix, Fig. S12 ). The maximum weighted $\mathrm { F } _ { \mathrm { S T } }$ reached 0.073 in the window Chr06:7415001 to 7425000, directly overlapping the gene. This elevated differentiation in genotypes with low S/G and high H64D allele frequency supports the hypothesis that PtLAC4-2 has experienced spatially varying selection associated with S/G variation across the natural range of P. trichocarpa . 

Poplars with Different S/G Display Cell Wall Metabolic and Transcriptomic Changes. To elucidate the biochemical pathways leading to lignin precursors in poplar, we compared the metabolic profiles of xylem samples from trees with extreme lignin composition. In trees with high S/G lignin ratios, we observed increased levels of primary metabolite precursors of the phenylpropanoid pathway, including phenylalanine, shikimate, 3-dehydroxyshikimate, and quinate (Fig.  6A and SI  Appendix, Fig. S13). Additionally, all the intermediates of the lignin pathway prior to the 3-hydroxylation (C3H) step were elevated in the high S/G poplar samples. Furthermore, the early flavonoid precursors naringenin chalcone and naringenin were substantially elevated (up to 40-fold) in the S-rich xylem samples (SI Appendix, Table S5). The metabolite profiles showed a clear shift after the C3H step, with higher levels of G-OH, S-OH, and related precursors such as caffeate, ferulate, sinapate, coniferaldehyde, and sinapaldehyde in the low-S/G samples (Fig. 6A and SI Appendix, Fig. S13). The metabolomics data show that there is an active flux from primary to secondary metabolism in trees with high S/G ratios. 

In addition, we examined the transcriptional changes in trees with extreme lignin phenotypes (SI Appendix, Fig. S14A ). Second, the differential expression levels and regulation of key lignin biosynthetic genes, particularly OMTs and F5Hs, come into play to favor G- or S-monolignol formation. Finally, the rate and substrate specificity of LACs determine the incorporation of available monomers into the lignin polymer, thus influencing the final S/G lignin monomer ratio. 

# Discussion

This study was initiated to identify genes linked to lignin formation using GWAS. Our results show that lignification is a polygenic trait influenced not only by structural genes directly involved in monolignol biosynthesis, but also additional genes with undefined roles in lignification (SI Appendix, Table S1 ). Previous association genetic studies have reported similar observations ( 32 , 33 ), suggesting that lignin biosynthesis is a complex trait subject to interactions between genes that function across different metabolic and regulatory pathways. Taking these findings into account, using CRISPR/Cas9 for multiplex genome editing may offer future avenues to modify multiple genes and thus tailor lignin composition in poplar ( 52 , 53 ). The challenge for this will be to overcome the current limitations in generating gene-edited trees without transgenesis ( 8 ). Despite their economic value, lignin and other cell wall traits are often overlooked in commercial forest tree breeding programs. Future tree breeding approaches for improving lignin traits for more efficient chemical pulping, biofuel production, or carbon sequestration must consider the quantitative genetic architecture of these traits and their correlation with plant growth, which is limited in plants and largely unexplored in woody angiosperms. 

The unusual C-lignin monomer was identified by GC-MS at very low levels $( < 4 \bar { \% } )$ with an expected retention time elution between the G and S monomers (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 A and B ). This report of C-lignin deposition in poplar opens a venue for genetic engineering to enhance its production. C-lignin, previously identified in OMT-deficient pine root cell suspension cultures ( 54 ) and seed coats of a number of species within the Orchidaceae, Cactaceae, Cleomaceae, and Euphorbiaceae families ( 15 – 17 ), features a unique homogeneous chemical structure useful for manufacturing renewable chemicals and biomaterials ( 55 , 56 ). One observation of our study is that PtLAC17-3 readily oxidizes caffeyl alcohol, the precursor of C-lignin (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 and Table S3 ). Furthermore, the increased levels of glycosylated caffeyl alcohol in low S/G poplars (SI Appendix, Fig. S14 ), suggest 

that there may be glycosyltransferases (UGTs) that are responsible for retaining caffeyl alcohol in the vacuoles when S-lignin biosynthesis is repressed. These findings may explain the absence of incorporation of highly hydroxylated monomers ( Fig. 1 ) into natural lignin polymers from vascular cells as a protective strategy to safeguard cellular components from radical oxidation ( 57 ). 

An interesting observation of our transcriptomic analysis was that both $O$ -methyltransferases (COMT and CCoAOMT ) were among the most highly expressed lignin genes in developing xylem, suggesting an active methylation of caffeate moieties at the free acid, CoA thioester, and aldehyde levels ( Fig. 1 ). Notably, COMT1 (Potri.012G006400) was the sixth most abundant transcript after dehydrin (ERD14 , Potri.005G248100), tubulin (TUA2 , Potri.002G111900), arabinogalactan protein (FLA11 , Potri.015G129400), and two genes of unknown function (Potri.002G223100 and Potri.014G162800) (Dataset S8 ). COMT1 (Potri.012G006400), CCoAOMT1 (Potri.009G099800), and CCoAOMT2 (Potri.001G304800) were also found among the 35 most highly expressed transcripts in the transcriptome dataset, along with genes involved in one-carbon metabolism (S-adenosylmethionine synthases), cell wall biogenesis (arabinogalactan proteins), cell division (tubulins), and stress response genes such as dehydrins (ERD14 , Potri.005G248100), lipid transfer proteins (DIR1 , Potri.014G149900), or rotamases (ROC1 , Potri.009G130100 and ROC3 , Potri.004G168800) (Dataset S8 ). Therefore, the abundance of OMTs coupled with the weak laccase specificity for the H-monolignol, might account for the predominance of G- and S-units and the low levels of H-units in natural lignins. Considering that the largest carbon flux from primary metabolism appears to be directed toward the flavonoid pathway ( Fig. 6A ), and that several precursors in the H-OH branch, including $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumarate and $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaraldehyde, were significantly upregulated in high S/G trees, we speculate that a portion of the $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaroyl-CoA pool that is diverted toward flavonoid biosynthesis, may also feed into the H-OH branch, leading to the overaccumulation of H-OH in the soluble pool. 

Similarly, the high levels of naringenin chalcone in trees with high S/G ratios are also intriguing. This open-chain flavonoid is a precursor of naringenin via chalcone isomerase (CHI), which is a critical step for producing several major classes of flavonoids such as flavanones, flavonols, and anthocyanins ( 58 ). Flavonoids are known for their roles as UV protectants ( 59 ), and naringenin chalcone was the most abundant metabolite in the phenylpropanoid pathway shown in Fig. 6A . Strikingly, we found weak expression levels of CHI and all members of the chalcone synthase family (CHS) in young xylem tissues. Only one member of the CHS family (Potri.014G145100) showed higher expression in trees with high S/G (Dataset S2 ). These findings indicate that, in addition to the production of S-lignin, the metabolic flux from primary metabolism in lower latitudes is also channeled into the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway. This redirection could be an adaptation to the increased UV exposure characteristic of these regions. Supporting this model, four FAR1-family TFs that have been shown to promote the expression of UV- and light-responsive genes, including those in the flavonoid pathway ( 60 ), were consistently upregulated in high S/G trees (Dataset S7 ). Overall, our metabolomic and gene coexpression data analysis suggests that the coordination of cell wall-related traits may be a determining factor in poplar adaptation to changing environments. 

A key finding of our study is the role of LACs in determining lignin composition as an adaptive response to the environment. Recent studies have highlighted the key role of microRNAs, particularly miR397, in regulating laccase expression and lignin synthesis in response to biotic and abiotic stress. For example, in A. thaliana , the 

interaction between miR397b and LAC2 was shown to regulate root lignification in response to water and phosphate deficiency ( 61 ). Similarly, in Malus hupehensis , miR397b negatively regulates resistance to Botryosphaeria dothidea by targeting MhLAC7 , whereas in pear, the PcMYB44–miR397–PcLAC regulatory module mediates lignification in response to fungal pathogens Alternaria alternata and B. dothidea ( 28 , 62 ). Additionally, the overexpression of cotton GhLAC15 in A. thaliana results in higher levels of G-lignin and enhances resistance to Verticillium infection ( 63 ). While miRNAs or specific TFs (SI Appendix, Fig. S16 ) could regulate the expression of PtLAC17-3 , we do not anticipate a role for miRNAs in controlling PtLAC4-2 function, as miRNAs act at the posttranscriptional level, rather than inducing DNA mutations ( 27 ). In our work, PtLAC4-2 was the only lignin gene exhibiting a mutation genetically associated with higher S/G ratio that was more prevalent in trees from lower latitudes ( Fig. 5G and SI Appendix, Table S4 ). Interestingly, PtLAC4-2 has fewer N-glycosylation sites than PtLAC17-3, and possesses a splice variant lacking the signal peptide, suggesting potential differences in protein localization (Movie S1 ). Moreover, the H64D mutation in PtLAC4-2 located outside the active site may play a role in mediating posttranscriptional protein–protein interactions or downstream signaling pathways. Further research is needed to explore how these potential interactions could influence monolignol substrate specificity and their significance in poplar adaptation to different latitudes. 

While our GWAS analysis identified several candidate genes associated with lignin and cell wall biosynthesis ( Fig. $2 \bar { B }$ and SI Appendix, Table S1 ), it is noteworthy that individual $L A C$ gene polymorphisms did not emerge as significant associations at the threshold of −log10 $( P ) > 5$ . Despite this being consistent with previous literature ( 32 , 33 ), our study presents multiple lines of evidence suggesting that $L A C s$ may influence lignin S/G composition in natural poplar populations. First, transcriptomic analysis of trees with extreme S/G phenotypes revealed significant differential expression of specific $L A C$ genes (e.g., PtLAC17-3 upregulated in high S/G, PtLAC5-2 upregulated in low S/G; Fig. $3 D$ ). This finding suggests that the expression levels of certain LACs, and some cell wall master regulators (SI Appendix, Fig. S16 ) are closely correlated with the final S/G ratio. Second, in vitro enzymatic characterization of PtLAC17-3 showed its substrate preference for S-OH over G-OH ( Fig. 4 $B$ and $C$ and SI Appendix, Table S3 ), providing biochemical evidence that laccase specificity can influence the lignin monomer composition. Furthermore, targeted mutational analysis of PtLAC17-3 (A444T) showed that a single amino acid change can substantially shift its catalytic efficiency toward G-OH ( Fig. $^ { 4 G }$ and SI Appendix, Table S3 ), highlighting the potential of minor laccase sequence variations to impact the S/G lignin monomer ratio. 

Lignin composition in wild plants is known to vary in response to the environmental conditions. For instance, eucalyptus trees naturally distributed in southern locations exhibit a higher S/G than their northern counterparts ( 64 , 65 ), suggesting that this observation could be part of a global trend across multiple plant species. In fact, conifers, which generally thrive in cooler northern latitudes, produce lignins that consist primarily of G-units ( 54 ). Such variation in lignin S/G ratios across latitudes may be an evolutionary adaptation to multiple factors, given the primary roles of the lignin structure on stem mechanical integrity ( 66 , 67 ), maintenance of water conduction ( 20 , 68 , 69 ), and plant defense ( 21 , 22 , 25 ), each of which is probably affected by spatially varying selection pressures ( 70 ). However, the ecological function and potential evolutionary advantage of specific lignin compositions remain to be experimentally tested. We speculate that the lignin S/G ratio may confer advantages under environmental conditions at different latitudes, including temperature, UV exposure, 

drought cycles, or pressure to pathogens or herbivory, by changing the plasticity of the cell wall. Given the differential expression of cell wall-associated master regulators found in poplar trees with extreme lignin composition (SI Appendix, Figs. S14 and S15 ), it would be interesting to investigate whether other cell wall components (i.e., cellulose, hemicellulose, or pectin), also exhibit latitude-dependent variation and contribute to a coordinated remodeling of the cell wall in adaptation to their environment. 

In conclusion, despite the ecological and economic importance of lignin, little is known about the natural variation that exists in its composition within plant species and the adaptive significance of such variation. In this study, we reported evidence of a latitudinal gradient in lignin composition in 433 poplar trees collected from the natural range of this species along the Northwest coast of North America. Poplar trees originating from southern latitudes displayed increased S/G lignin monomer ratios. Furthermore, we identified mutations in cell wall laccase genes that are genetically associated with significant increases in lignin S/G ratios that predominate in trees collected from southern geographic regions. These results suggest that the ecological function of lignin differs across latitudes and is partially conferred by cell wall LACs. Gaining a deeper understanding of this lignin-based adaptability could be applied to predict how forests will respond to future climate change and to develop sustainable forestry practices. 

# Materials and Methods

Methods used in this study are described in detail in SI  Appendix, as indicated below. 

Plant Material. Poplar genotypes were obtained from the DOE Center for Bioenergy Innovation collection of P. trichocarpa and clonally propagated in a common garden in Clatskanie, OR. A total of 433 developing xylem tissue samples were harvested from 5-y-old trees for RNA-seq and lignin phenotyping. Sequencing was conducted at the DOE Joint Genome Institute using the Illumina HiSeq2500 platform and reads were analyzed to calculate transcript abundance in FPKM, as described in SI Appendix. 

Lignin Phenotyping. Lignin phenotyping was performed following established methods (38). Thioacidolysis reactions were carried out using 5 mg of CWR in microreaction vessels at $1 0 0 { } ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $4 \ h ,$ and lignin monomers were identified by GC-MS at $2 3 9 m / z$ for H-units, $2 6 9 m / Z$ for G-units, $2 9 9 m / Z$ for S-units, and 327 m/z for C-units. Total lignin content was quantified using the acetyl bromide method, as detailed in SI Appendix. 

GWAS Analyses. Whole-genome resequencing of the P. trichocarpa association population was performed using the Illumina HiSeq2500 platform a $\sim 1 5 \times \mathsf { c o v } .$ - erage, and reads were aligned to the $\mathtt { v 3 . 0 }$ reference genome. SNPs and InDels were called using SAMtools/BCFtools and annotated with SnpEff. The resulting dataset is publicly available (https://doi.ccs.ornl.gov/ui/doi/55). GWAS was conducted using EMMAX with 8,253,066 SNPs (MAF > 0.05), and SNPs with $- \mathsf { l o g } _ { 1 0 } ( P )$ $> 5$ were considered significant. 

Laccase Expression and Enzyme Assays. PtLAC17-3 was synthesized and cloned into a Gateway-compatible donor vector. The ORF was recombined into the mammalian expression vector pGEn2-DEST to generate a His-tagged version of the protein for purification. The recombinant fusion protein was expressed in HEK293 cells and purified using a HisTrap column on an ÄKTA Pure system. Laccase activity assays were performed using recombinant PtLAC17-3 with monolignol substrates, and monolignol oxidation was quantified on an Agilent 1290 HPLC system. LC-MS/ MS was performed using a Dionex UltiMate 3000 with a Bruker Compact qTOF-MS/ MS with product ion scans in negative mode (m/z 50 to 1,500) to detect sinapyl, coniferyl, and caffeyl alcohol dimers and trimers, as described in SI Appendix. 

Laccase Structural Models. We analyzed the sequences and structural models of seven P. trichocarpa LACs with gene expression correlated with either S- or G-lignin deposition. Structural models for PtLAC17-3 and PtLAC5-2 were generated using AlphaFold2, with both proteins showing high model confidence, with 

pLDDT scores of 96.5 and 97.0, respectively. Monolignols were superimposed from the crystal structure of ZmLAC3, and active site residues and key mutations within 5 Å were visualized in PyMOL. 

Poplar Allele Catalog Tool. The Allele Catalog Tool is a web-based platform for visualizing and analyzing allelic variation in genes associated with plant traits. We expanded this tool to include P. trichocarpa using SNP datasets from the natural population. The datasets were integrated into the KBCommons platform and are accessible at: https://kbcommons.org/system/tools/AlleleCatalogTool/Ptrichocarpa. 

The Poplar Allele Catalog Tool was used to identify potential associations between mutations in lignin genes and the lignin S/G ratio composition as detailed in SI Appendix. 

Evidence of Selection for Lignin Composition. To assess population structure, we constructed a maximum likelihood phylogenetic tree using $\mathord { \sim } 5 . 6 9$ million ${ \mathsf { S N P S } } ,$ and performed identity-by-state (IBS) clustering using the SNPRelate R package. To investigate phenotypic divergence and potential selection, we estimated quantitative trait differentiation $( 0 _ { \mho } )$ for lignin traits using variance components derived from linear mixed models and calculated $9 5 \% \mathrm { C l }$ via bootstrap resampling. $0 _ { \mathsf { S I } }$ values were compared to $\mathsf { F } _ { \mathsf { S I } }$ estimates $\mathsf { \Gamma } ( \mathsf { F } _ { \mathbb { S } } = 0 . 1 7 \pm 0 . 0 6 )$ ) from previous studies. We further performed sliding-window $\mathsf { F } _ { \mathsf { S T } }$ analysis around PtLAC4-2 to detect selection signals associated with the H64D allele. Detailed description of these methods is available in SI Appendix. 

Metabolic Profiling. Metabolite profiling of methanolic extracts from young poplar wood was performed by MetWare Biotechnology Inc. using LC-ESI-MS/ MS on a SCIEX ExionLC system coupled to an AB Sciex 6500 Q TRAP instrument. Extracts were prepared from $2 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of freeze-dried xylem powder, extracted in $7 0 \%$ methanol, and analyzed by UPLC-MS/MS using established protocols. 

GO Enrichment. mRNA expression levels were compared between the $2 4 ~ P .$ trichocarpa genotypes with the highest and lowest S/G ratios. Fold changes and corresponding P-values were used to construct volcano plots and identify genes significantly upregulated with G-lignin $\mathsf { F C } < 0 . 7 5$ ; $P < 0 . 0 5$ ) or S-lignin $( \mathsf { F C } >$ $1 . 2 5 ; P < 0 . 0 5$ ) deposition. The methods used for hierarchical clustering, WGCNA, and GO enrichment analysis are available in SI Appendix.
