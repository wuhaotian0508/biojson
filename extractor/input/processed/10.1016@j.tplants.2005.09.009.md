# Regulation of volatile benzenoid biosynthesis in petunia flowers

Robert C. Schuurink1 , Michel A. Haring1 and David G. Clark2

1 Department of Plant Physiology, University of Amsterdam, Swammerdam Institute for Life Sciences, 1098 SM Amsterdam, The Netherlands   
2 Department of Environmental Horticulture, University of Florida, Gainesville, FL 32611, USA

The petunia flower has served as a model for the study of several physiological processes including floral development, self-incompatibility, anthocyanin biosynthesis and ethylene signalling during senescence. More recently, Petunia hybrida ‘Mitchell’ has been used to understand the complex regulation of volatile benzenoid biosynthesis, which occurs predominantly in flower petal tissues. Benzenoid biosynthesis is temporally and circadian controlled and is tightly downregulated by ethylene during floral senescence. Using targeted transcriptomics and gene knockouts, both biosynthetic genes and a transcription factor regulating benzenoid synthesis have been recently discovered and characterized. It appears that benzenoid production is regulated predominantly by transcriptional control of the shikimate pathway, benzenoid biosynthesis genes and S-adenosyl-methionine cycle genes.

# Volatile benzenoid biosynthesis

Floral scents have been studied extensively by chemists because of commercial interest from the perfume industry and by biologists who recognized their importance in attracting pollinators. Most floral volatiles are classified as either terpenoids or benzenoids but there are also fatty acid and carotenoid derivatives, and nitrogen- or sulfurcontaining compounds. Plants can use these volatile molecules in combination with floral form and colour to attract pollinators that enable or facilitate reproduction. For instance, Petunia integrifolia, a species with violetcoloured small, broad flowers lacking scent, is visited by bees during the day, whereas Petunia axillaris, a species with white slender flowers, which is visited by hawk moths at night, starts to produce scent strongly at dusk [1, 2]. Both the P. axillaris rhythmic emission and scent composition of predominantly volatile benzenoids have been preserved in the white flowering Petunia hybrida cultivar Mitchell [3]. Although P. axillaris and P. integrifolia are the known progenitor species of P. hybrida, they are not known to have produced natural hybrids in nature even though they are located in the same geographic locations in Argentina, Uruguay and Brazil. This suggests that the interaction between their primary pollinators is likely to be specific [1].

The study of volatile benzenoid biosynthesis in flowers started over a decade ago with the model system Clarkia breweri, from which salicylic acid methyl transferase and benzylalcohol acetyltransferase were isolated and characterized [4,5]. Whereas C. breweri flowers emit volatiles continuously, rose (Rosa hybrida) and snapdragon (Antirrhinum majus) have emission peaks during the day [6,7]. In rose petals, several scent biosynthesis genes are regulated at the transcript level during flower development [8,9]. Snapdragon benzoic acid methyl transferase (BAMT) is partially regulated at the transcript and protein level in a circadian rhythm that coincides with methyl benzoate (MeBA) emission. However, the dominant oscillating factor over a $2 4 \mathrm { { h } }$ time-span is the concentration of benzoic acid, the precursor of MeBA [7]. The level of benzoic acid is also tightly controlled in petunia Mitchell [7], which has emerged as the model of choice to study volatile benzenoid synthesis, emission and regulation. Petunia Mitchell is a colchidiploid, which is easy to transform and is a physiological and genetic model plant for many laboratories [10]. It predominantly emits volatile benzenoids (Table 1) in the evening and night. These volatile benzenoids are mostly produced by the petals; emission decreases during pollination-induced senescence [3,11,12].

Benzenoid metabolism in petunia petal tissue has recently been modelled with the aid of isotope labelling and metabolic flux analyses [13]. This model indicates that all volatile benzenoids (Table 1) in petunia are derived from phenylalanine. Phenylacetaldehyde and phenylethylalcohol are synthesized from phenylalanine, whereas the other volatile benzenoids have trans-cinnamic acid (t-CA) as the precursor, which is made from phenylalanine by the activity of phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) (Figure 1a). The $t$ -CA side chain is shortened through the action of the $\beta$ -oxidative and non- $\beta$ -oxidative pathways, with benzaldehyde and benzylbenzoate as key intermediates between phenylalanine and benzoic acid [13]. Vanillin and the phenylpropanoid isoeugenol, which are also emitted by petunia Mitchell (Table 1), are likely to be derived from molecules downstream of $p$ -coumaric acid. The cellular localization of the volatile benzenoids and isoeugenol still needs to be resolved (Box 1) but it is known that the biosynthetic enzymes are almost exclusively expressed in the epidermal cells of the floral organs in C. breweri and snapdragon [14,15]. Because early studies showed that genes involved in benzenoid biosynthesis are transcriptionally regulated in petunia, targeted transcriptomics approaches have been undertaken to identify biosynthetic genes and/or key regulators. Julian Verdonk et al. [17] used the rhythmic regulation of scent production for differential screens, with an additional discriminating layer provided by a comparison with a non-fragrant petunia cultivar. Beverly Underwood et al. [12] compared the transcriptome of flower petals with that of the rest of the plant to isolate petal-specific genes. Next, the transcriptome of ethylene-treated petunia Mitchell and transgenic ethylene-insensitive flowers (CaMV35STetr1-1) [18] were compared to isolate ethylene-regulated genes. It was then realized that floral scent was tightly regulated by ethylene during senescence [12]. Together, four

# Regulation of the floral shikimate pathway

The shikimate pathway links carbohydrate metabolism to the synthesis of aromatic amino acids, which can in turn act as precursors for various primary and secondary metabolites, such as benzenoids. Most enzymes of the shikimate pathway have been purified from various plants and are well characterized. There is no evidence for an aromatic amino acid feedback-sensitive DAHP synthase as there is in bacteria. Feedback inhibition occurs post chorismate. Chorismate mutase (CM), the first committed step in Phe and Tyr biosynthesis, is inhibited by Phe and Tyr, whereas anthranilate synthase (AS), the first committed step in Trp synthesis, is inhibited by Trp [19].

Remarkably, little is known about the regulation of this central pathway at the gene expression level. DAHP SYNTHASE is induced by wounding and by pathogen attack in Arabidopsis [20], whereas Arabidopsis CM1 is induced by pathogens [21]. In petunia, only the 5-enolpyruvylshikimate-3-phosphate synthase (EPSPS) gene has been well studied because of its importance as a target for the herbicide glyphosate [22]. Its expression is barely detectable in leaves but high levels are expressed in petals [23]. Transcription of EPSPS is initiated at multiple sites in leaves but transcription of this gene in petals is initiated almost entirely from only one of these sites [23] Petunia petals have three cell layers. A zinc-finger protein that binds to the EPSPS promoter is expressed in the same petal cell types at the same stage of development as EPSPS is expressed [25]. The role of this zinc-finger protein in regulating EPSPS expression has never been investigated but the tight correlation with EPSPS expression suggests a regulatory role. More convincing is the recent identification of a transcriptional regulator of the shikimate pathway in petunia petals: ODORANT1 (ODO1) [17]. This R2R3-like MYB specifically regulates the floral shikimate pathway. Expression of ODO1 peaks during the night when emission of volatile benzenoids is highest, and transcript levels of ODO1 are low the next morning. The expression of genes in the shikimate pathway shows a rhythm similar to that of ODO1 and volatile benzenoid emission. The increase in ODO1 expression precedes the emission of volatile benzenoids. Down-regulation of ODO1 in transgenic petunia Mitchell plants strongly reduces volatile benzenoid emission because of a reduced expression of DAHP synthase, EPSPS, CM and PAL (Figure 1a). Subsequently, this causes a reduction in the accumulation of, for example, benzoic acid, which is up to 20 times lower

in plants where ODO1 is suppressed compared with the wild-type plants. ODO1 seems to be a major regulatory factor because it is able to activate the EPSPS promoter in leaves [17].

Several plant MYBs have been identified that regulate PAL [26,27]. Whether orthologues of these MYBs are expressed in petunia petals emitting volatile benzenoids remains to be investigated. PAL expression is downregulated by high levels of $t$ -CA in green tissues of tobacco and bean [28,29], and induced by high levels of Phe in Pinus [30]. However, high levels of Phe in petunia petals do not influence PAL activity or transcript levels, although $t$ -CA accumulation occurs [13]. Given that levels of $t$ -CA increase about tenfold in petunia Mitchell petals at night [12] concurrent with increased PAL transcript levels [3], it seems that a different type of PAL-regulation operates in this system.

# Regulation of benzenoid biosynthesis genes

The rhythmic emission of volatile benzenoids by petunia Mitchell starts in the evening and is preceded by the expression of biosynthetic genes. Classical constant dark and light experiments to investigate the rhythmic biosynthesis of volatile benzenoids revealed that light and probably energy play a primary role whereas circadian factors are of lesser importance [12]. BSMT expression is normally cyclic. It continues to cycle when plants are placed in constant dark or light at lower and higher levels, respectively, but MeBA emission loses it rhythmicity in both conditions. The interrelationship between the regulation of the shikimate pathway, the benzenoid biosynthesis genes and the SAM-cycle is still unclear although genes from all three classes are transcriptionally upregulated preceding increases in benzenoid emission [3,12,17]. There are two lines of evidence that the shikimate pathway influences the benzenoid synthesis genes and the SAM cycle. First, when MeBA production starts, SAM synthetase is upregulated, as well as the two other genes from the SAM cycle, methionine synthase and S-adenosylhomocystein hydrolase [3]. The transcriptional upregulation of these three genes is absent when ODO1 is suppressed but this has no influence on steady-state SAM levels [17], which are apparently tightly controlled because of high ATP costs [31] and involvement in other cellular processes (Figure 1b). This suggests that induction of volatile benzenoid production involves a mechanism that simultaneously increases production of SAM. Second, suppression of ODO1 results in upregulation of the biosynthetic genes BSMT and BPBT. This suggests that the biosynthetic genes are under a regulatory mechanism that might involve precursor levels because it is known that BSMT is down-regulated by high Phe levels [13]. Moreover, BSMT is also highly expressed in P. hybrida W138 petals (J.C. Verdonk and R.C. Schuurink, unpublished), which emit minute amounts of MeBA [32] and in which ODO1 is poorly expressed [17]. Thus, W138 has the capacity to produce volatile benzenoids but the increased flow through the shikimate pathway, regulated by ODO1, is probably lacking. ODO1 is likely to correspond with the scent locus SCE1 because crossing this locus into W138 results in a fragrance that resembles $P .$ axillaris in complexity and in quantity [32]. The transcription factors regulating the benzenoid biosynthetic genes and SAM-cycle genes still need to be identified (Box 1).

# Ethylene regulation

In petunia Mitchell, benzenoids are emitted from the time of anthesis throughout the remaining five to seven days before the flower naturally senesces. If the flower is pollinated, ethylene produced by the ovaries and petals as a result of successful fertilization not only accelerates corolla senescence but also rapidly coordinates the downregulation of biosynthesis and emission of all benzenoids produced [11,12]. Ethylene is now known to control benzenoid synthesis at many levels within the cells of the petunia corolla (Figure 3). It coordinates the downregulation of transcription of the biosynthetic genes BSMT, BPBT and PAL [11,12]. Subsequently, it is responsible for a decrease in synthesis of the ratelimiting substrates used in benzenoid synthesis, namely $t$ -CA and benzoic acid [12]. When combined with the observation that ethylene also decreases the methylation index through the alteration of the SAM:S-adenosylhomocystein ratio in snapdragon [11], it is clear that ethylene is a major factor that can override normal benzenoid synthesis. This concept is not surprising if the proposed biological functions for these benzenoids in petunia corollas are considered.

Normally, the plant will spend a fair amount of metabolic energy in maintaining benzenoid synthesis because of the importance of the corolla for ensuring successful reproduction. For example, during peak periods of rhythmic benzenoid synthesis, BSMT transcripts comprise almost $1 \%$ of the total mRNA expressed in petunia corollas, resulting in the emission of approximately 300 nmol of $\mathbf { M e B A / h / g }$ fresh weight [12]. Assuming a typical SAM pool size of 0.3–30.0 nmol per g [11,33], the supply of methyl groups for BSMT to convert BA into MeBA would require the SAM pool to turn over a considerable amount. This implies that MeBA synthesis is probably the major fate of SAM in petunia flowers. Once successful fertilization occurs, the corolla has served its purpose of providing visual and olfactory cues to pollinators. Given that petunia is known not to mobilize and transport substrates from the corolla to the developing ovaries [34], it apparently uses ethylene as a means to signal the cessation of metabolically expensive processes such as benzenoid synthesis. The plant then changes its metabolic focus from one of maintaining the corolla to ensure successful pollination to one of supporting the developing seeds.

# Prospects for engineering

Ultimately, a potential biotechnology application resulting from the study of benzenoid synthesis and regulation might be realized through attempts to engineer plants for altered floral scent. DNA sequences for several putative

benzenoid synthesis and regulatory genes are now available from several petunia Mitchell flower cDNA libraries (http://www.tigr.org/tigr-scripts/tgi/T_index. cgi?species $=$ petunia), and groups are working to knock out or over-express them individually in transgenic plants. Results from studies on these genes are likely to provide the foundational information needed to determine which genes encode the rate-limiting enzymes in benzenoid synthesis necessary for engineering enhanced scent. Once these genes are identified, significant alterations in floral scent might result from the combining of mutant or transgenic knockout and/or over-expressors of the crucial steps of benzenoid synthesis through traditional breeding. Genes encoding enzymes that catalyse the last reactions in benzenoid synthesis such as BSMT are expressed at high levels in petunia corollas and are not likely to be rate limiting, whereas genes encoding transcription factors such as ODO1 might be key regulators. Once the genes encoding enzymes catalysing reactions between t-CA and benzoic acid are identified (Box 1), all the factors will be available to assemble a complete genetic pathway that would provide both the substrates and the enzymes necessary for synthesizing high levels of benzenoids such as methylbenzoate. To understand completely which compounds are most desirable for engineering scent, it will be imperative to screen these plants with human olfactory panels. This concept has been tested successfully but work in this area is in its infancy. Transgenic BSMT RNAi knockout petunias with a $9 9 \%$ reduction in emission of methyl benzoate have been detected as different from normal petunia Mitchell by four out of five humans exposed to them [12]. However, the consensus of the panel surveyed was that flowers emitting less sweetsmelling methyl benzoate were not as desirable as flowers from petunia Mitchell. In addition, plants engineered for changes in the volatile emission profile will be of interest for biological studies because some insects presumably use fragrance for the location of flowers as a potential source of nectar. Given that the primary volatile organic compounds produced by petunia Mitchell are benzenoids, and that hawkmoths are attracted to this plant, it is reasonable to suggest that key components of pollinator attraction might be found here.
