# SUMMARY

Pinosylvin and its derivatives are defense-related stilbenoids that contribute to pathogen and decay resistance of Scots pine ( L.) heartwood. While most enzymes in the pinosylvin biosynthesis Pinus sylvestrispathway in Scots pine have been identified, the enzyme responsible for activating cinnamic acid to cinnamoyl-CoA, an essential precursor for pinosylvin biosynthesis, has remained unknown. In this study, we explored Scots pine transcriptomic data to observe the expression profiles of the 4-coumarate:CoA ligase (4CL) genes and produced the enzymes in  to assay their kinetic properties. Nicotiana benthamianaWe identified and characterized four 4CL isoforms, one of which (Ps4CL2) was both co-expressed with pinosylvin biosynthesis genes under stilbene-inducing conditions and exhibited an unusually high affinity for cinnamic acid surpassing that of previously characterized cinnamate-activating 4CLs. Through site-directed mutagenesis and domain-swapping experiments with the closely related Ps4CL3, we showed that substrate preference in Ps4CL2 results from multiple interacting regions within the enzyme. Our findings establish Ps4CL2 as the prime candidate for cinnamate activation as the last missing enzyme for pinosylvin biosynthesis in Scots pine and provide insight into enzyme specialization within the 4CL family. 

Keywords: 4-coumarate:CoA ligase, pinosylvin biosynthesis, cinnamate, substrate specificity, Scots pine, Pinus sylvestris. 

# INTRODUCTION

Phenylpropanoids are a vast family of plant metabolites that underlie terrestrial plant life. They have diversified into chemically and functionally distinct compounds, fulfilling essential functions in plants, including UV protection, structural support, attracting pollinators, and protecting plants from pathogens. Among these, stilbenes represent a group of defense-related phenylpropanoid derivatives that are synthesized in response to biotic and abiotic stresses. 

To date, two distinct biosynthetic pathways have been identified for stilbenoids in plants. The first and predominant variant is resveratrol, a stilbene derived from 4-coumaric acid and known from grapevines, peanuts, spruce, and many other plant species (Hammerbacher et al., 2011; Capruciu, 2025; Sales & Resurreccion, 2014). In these plants, resveratrol functions as a phytoalexin, 

contributing to defense against pathogenic microbes (Chang et al., 2011; Langcake, 1981). Beyond its role in plant protection, resveratrol exhibits potent antioxidant activity and has been associated with various health-promoting properties in humans (Kalantari & Das, 2010). Pine species, on the other hand, accumulate the cinnamic acid-derived stilbenes pinosylvin (PS) and its monomethyl ether (PSME) in the heartwood, where they contribute to the decay resistance of the pine wood (Harju & Venal€ ainen,€ 2006). Biosynthesis of PS and PSME is also induced in the sapwood and in needles upon wounding or pathogen attack, or by UV-C in laboratory experiments (Gehlert et al., 1990). PS and PSME are important compounds for both tree health and timber quality. As heartwood develops only in adult trees, breeding for pine heartwood decay resistance would benefit from early selection based on detailed molecular knowledge of the pathway.

Biosynthesis of PS starts from the conversion of the amino acid phenylalanine into cinnamic acid by the enzyme phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL), which subsequently proceeds directly into cinnamoyl-CoA instead of 4-coumaric acid, followed by extension of this molecule into pinosylvin by the enzyme stilbene synthase (STS) (Figure 1). 

While the enzymes for the initial and final steps of the PS pathway have been identified, the enzyme catalyzing the conversion of cinnamic acid to cinnamoyl-CoA has remained unknown. The analogous reaction for 4-coumaric acid in resveratrol biosynthesis is catalyzed by 4-coumarate:CoA ligase (4CL), a well-characterized enzyme necessary also for biosynthesis of flavonoids and lignin that all land plants share. 4CL belongs to the adenylate-forming enzyme superfamily characterized by two key sequence motifs known as Box I (SSGTTGLPKGV) and Box II (GEICIRG). Box I sits near the N-terminus and plays a role in ATP binding and adenylation, while Box II is closer to the C-terminus and contains the cysteine residue that directly takes part in catalysis and CoA recognition (Becker-Andre et al., 1991; Uhlmann & Ebel, 1993). These motifs are crucial for the two-step reaction mechanism where the enzyme first adenylates the hydroxycinnamic acid substrate and then forms the CoA thioester. 

Different 4CLs activate various hydroxycinnamic acids or their methyl ethers, including caffeic, ferulic, and in some cases sinapic acid, but typically do not accept cinnamic acid to any degree. Few exceptions, such as At4CL1 from Arabidopsis thaliana and Ri4CL2 from Rubus idaeus, have been reported in this respect (Costa et al., 2005; Kumar & Ellis, 2003). Unusual substrate preferences have also prompted structural and modeling studies aimed at identifying the molecular determinants behind the substrate specificity in 4CL enzymes. For instance, Ehlting et al. (2001) identified two adjacent substrate-binding domains between amino acid position 276 and 413 of At4CL2, and Schneider et al. (2003) refined this analysis using 3D modeling of At4CL2 against gramicidin S synthetase, another adenylate-forming enzyme the crystal structure of which was available, and proposed 12 residues likely involved in substrate specificity. Later, the crystal structures of two plant 4CLs, Poto4CL1 from Populus tomentosa (Hu et al., 2010) and Nt4CL2 from Nicotiana tabacum (Li & Nair, 2015), have provided insights to the structural basis of substrate specificity of 4CL enzymes. 

In some plants, a cinnamate:CoA ligase (CNL) has been shown to catalyze the conversion of cinnamate into cinnamoyl-CoA. Since its first discovery in the early 2000s (Kaneko et al., 2003), the interest in CNL has grown due to its specialized role in directly activating cinnamate in the 

benzoic acid pathway (Gaid et al., 2012; Teotia et al., 2019). While CNL catalyzes a similar CoA ligation reaction as 4CL, it exhibits distinct substrate specificity and is evolutionarily distant from 4CL (Gaid et al., 2012). Although CNL has been documented in several plant species, its presence in gymnosperms has not been detected, despite a growing amount of gymnosperm sequence data. 

In this study, we aimed to identify and characterize the enzyme responsible for cinnamic acid activation in the PS biosynthesis pathway of Scots pine (Pinus sylvestris L.) by utilizing transcriptomic datasets derived from developmentally regulated (i.e., heartwood formation) and stress-induced (wounding and UV-C) conditions known to trigger stilbene accumulation (Lim et al., 2016, 2021; Paasela et al., 2023). While CNL-like transcript fragments could not be detected, we discovered that one of the Scots pine 4CL isoforms, Ps4CL2, is co-expressed with stilbene pathway genes and has high catalytic efficiency toward cinnamic acid, demonstrating that this enzyme fulfills the role of cinnamate activation in pinosylvin biosynthesis. 

# RESULTS

# 4CL genes in Scots pine show correlated expression with stilbene synthase and pinosylvin -methyltransferase encoding genes

Among the available transcriptome libraries, we utilized the heartwood/sapwood libraries to identify candidate genes involved in the stilbene biosynthesis pathway. The reads were mapped against the Pinus EST collection version 9.0 (Lim et al., 2016). Eleven tentative consensus (TC) sequences annotated as 4-coumarate:CoA ligase showed elevated gene expression levels in the transition zone (TZ) to heartwood compared with sapwood (SW). While many of these TCs were only fragments, three longer transcripts (TC195290, TC166358 and TC191762) exhibited higher expression in the TZ compared with SW. TC195290 and TC166358 were full-length coding sequences, and the corresponding Scots pine sequences amplified from cDNA were designated as Ps4CL1 and Ps4CL3, respectively. TC191762 was a partial sequence, and the full-length coding sequence obtained by ${ \mathfrak { H } } ^ { \prime } .$ - and ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ -RACE was named Ps4CL2. This cloning effort also resulted in a fourth 4CL candidate, named Ps4CL4. 

Our de novo assembly of the Scots pine transcriptomes from stilbene-inducing conditions (Lim et al., 2016, 2021; Paasela et al., 2023) showed that all four 4CL genes (- Ps4CL1-4) are expressed, with no additional 4CL-encoding genes found. Amino acid sequence alignment of Scots pine 4CLs revealed the presence of the conserved Box I and Box II motifs (Figure 2) and showed varying degrees of similarity, with Ps4CL1 standing out as different from the other three (Table S1). Three of the four Scots pine 4CL genes (Ps4CL2-4) were co-expressed with PsSTS and 

PsPMT2, showing higher expression under stilbeneinducing conditions and thus indicating their possible involvement in pinosylvin biosynthesis (Figure 3). Ps4CL1, on the other hand, showed a similar expression pattern to PsHCT and PsC3’H, two genes specific to the lignin biosynthesis pathway. Lignin biosynthesis is active throughout the wounding experiment in 5-year-old Scots pine seedlings, including the control (Lim et al., 2021) and, as we previously showed, in the TZ between sapwood and heartwood where pinosylvin is synthesized (Lim et al., 2016). 

We also investigated the possibility of identifying CNL-like sequences using amino acid sequences of angiosperm CNLs or conifer CNL-like sequences as queries. The Scots pine RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) gene assembly (Lim et al., 2021) exhibited limited similarity to known CNLs at very low coverage (less than $1 8 \% )$ . Subsequent read mapping revealed extremely low expression levels for all of these CNL-like contigs, and their expression pattern did not match that of PsSTS and PsPMT2. The absence of meaningful RNA-seq evidence shows that nothing similar to CNL is expressed in Scots pine. 

# Scots pine 4CLs exhibit unique evolutionary divergence

The coding sequences of 4CL genes from various plant species were used to construct a phylogenetic tree (Figure 4), which distinguished the 4CLs according to their evolutionary relationships to the previously established class I, class II, and class III categories (Chen et al., 2014; Hamberger & Hahlbrock, 2004). Ps4CL1 formed a clade with several pine genes that share up to $9 9 \%$ nucleotide sequence identity. Suppression of the P. radiata 4CL1 gene resulted in weakly lignified tissue (Wagner et al., 2009), strongly suggesting that the pine 4CL genes in this clade have a role in lignin biosynthesis. Ps4CL1 was not co-expressed with PsSTS and PsPMT2 (Figure 3) but was constitutively expressed in the stems of 5-year-old pine seedlings and in needles. 

Ps4CL2-4 formed a clade distinct from the lignin-related 4CL genes. Notably, Ps4CL2 clustered closely with an uncharacterized 4CL from P. taeda, whereas Ps4CL3 and Ps4CL4 clustered closely to 4CL3 from P. taeda, with a P. strobus 4CL1 positioned between these two clades. The CNL genes from angiosperms formed a separate clade, confirming that CNL genes are evolutionarily divergent from 4CL genes (Figure 4, panel E).

Enzymatic assays were conducted for all four Scots pine 4CLs for their efficiency in activating cinnamic and 4-coumaric acid and to explore their kinetic properties (Table 1). As controls, we selected two Arabidopsis 4CLs, At4CL1 and At4CL5. At4CL1 is responsible for the major 4CL1 activity in Arabidopsis and can utilize 4-coumaric and cinnamic acid (the latter with a high Km) as substrates (Costa et al., 2005; Ehlting et al., 1999; Li et al., 2015), while At4CL5 was reported not to be able to activate cinnamate at all (Costa et al., 2005). Additionally, two 4CLs from raspberry (Rubus idaeus) were included (Ri4CL1 and Ri4CL2), as the latter has been shown to be able to activate cinnamate (Kumar & Ellis, 2003). 

Among the Scots pine 4CLs, Ps4CL2 exhibited the highest specificity (Kcat/Km) toward cinnamic acid, notably three to four times higher than the positive controls Ri4CL2 and At4CL1. A closer examination of the individual Kcat and Km values showed that while Ps4CL2 did not necessarily have the highest conversion rate (Kcat) compared with other Scots pine 4CLs, it had a remarkably low Km for cinnamate, indicating strong substrate affinity. 

Regarding the alternative substrate, 4-coumaric acid, Ps4CL1 showed the highest specificity, followed by Ps4CL4. Ps4CL2 did not discriminate between cinnamic acid and 4-coumaric acid, while Ri4CL2 (the second-best 

cinnamate user in this set) strongly preferred 4-coumarate, as did Ri4CL1, At4CL1, and At4CL5. 

# Substrate preference in Ps4CL2 is likely influenced by amino acid differences throughout the enzyme

To uncover possible amino acids responsible for high affinity of Ps4CL2 toward cinnamate, we compared the amino acid residues between Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3, which show $90 \%$ amino acid sequence similarity but notably different substrate preferences. Structure-based predictions for important amino acids involved in substrate specificity have been proposed for At4CL2 based on homology modeling, and for Poto4CL1 and Nt4CL2 based on crystal structures (Hu et al., 2010; Li & Nair, 2015; Schneider et al., 2003). Notably, all of these residues and domains are located between the conserved Box I and Box II of the enzyme (Figure 5), and therefore, this region is referred to as the substrate-binding region (SBR) (Ehlting et al., 2001). We used AlphaFold2 to predict the 3D structure of Ps4CL2, Ps4CL3, and At4CL2, and the models showed excellent structural alignment with the solved structure of Poto4CL1 and Nt4CL2 (Figure S3). Using these predicted models and the two crystal structures, we identified the amino acids within 5 A of the bound substrate, adenylated 4-coumarate (Figure 5, highlighted in cyan). This difference is intriguing, as the tyrosine residue at this position interacts with the aromatic ring of the hydroxycinnamate substrate (Li & Nair, 2015). We therefore hypothesized that this specific difference might be responsible for the high affinity of Ps4CL2 for cinnamic acid. 

To test this hypothesis, we performed site-directed mutagenesis to exchange these residues, from amino acids VF263 present in Ps4CL2 to amino acids IY in Ps4CL3, and vice versa. We expected that the introduced mutations would potentially alter the structure, polarity, or charge of the binding pocket, thereby lowering its affinity to cinnamic acid and consequently increasing the Km value for the mutant Ps4CL2, and vice versa for the mutant Ps4CL3. The mutation VF263IY altered the behavior of the Ps4CL2 enzyme, but in an unexpected way: the Km value for cinnamic acid remained low, indicating a sustained high affinity, while the Kcat was significantly reduced, suggesting lower catalytic turnover (Table 2). A similar pattern was observed with the mutant Ps4CL2 assayed with 4-coumaric acid, where substrate binding remained unaffected, but catalytic activity was compromised. Interestingly, introducing the corresponding mutation IY266VF into Ps4CL3 showed the opposite effects. The mutated Ps4CL3 maintained a high Kcat toward both cinnamic and 4-coumaric acid, but exhibited significantly increased Km values, thus reflecting reduced substrate affinity toward both substrates. These results show that the two amino acids are important for substrate binding and catalysis, but they do not have a stand-alone role for either, indicating important interaction with other parts of the enzyme. 

# Substrate preference of Ps4CL2 is a result of multidomain interactions

As the amino acid substitutions did not account for Ps4CL2’s affinity toward cinnamic acid, we generated 

chimeric fusion enzymes between Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 to investigate the role of SBR in this preference, compared with other 4CL isoforms in Scots pine. The chimeric enzymes were designed with fusion points in Box I and II, partitioning the enzymes into N-terminus, SBR, and Cterminus, and thus allowing us to investigate the regions of the enzymes responsible for the affinity to cinnamic acid and catalytic rate (Figure 6). The chimeric enzymes were labeled according to the origin of the three portions that constructed the protein. 

The chimeric enzymes with exchanged SBR (fusions 2–3-2 and 3–2-3) showed nearly identical features as the two amino acid exchange mutations described above (Table 2). Combining the N-terminus of Ps4CL2 with the C-terminus of Ps4CL3 (irrespective of SBR: fusions 2–3-3 and 2–2-3) increased the specificity toward cinnamate (but not toward 4-coumarate). On the contrary, the N-terminus of Ps4CL3 by itself (fusion 3–2-2) decreased the specificity of the enzyme toward cinnamate, but combining it with SBR of Ps4CL3 (fusion 3–3-2) helped improve the specificity. Interestingly, the fusion enzymes affected the catalytic rate and the affinity; in most cases, the fusions performed well in one aspect but not in the other (Table S2). The efficiency of the enzymes, particularly Ps4CL2 toward cinnamate, is a complex interplay between different parts of the enzyme.

Biochemically, both CNL and 4CL are capable of catalyzing the activation of cinnamate to cinnamoyl-CoA. However, investigation of the Scots pine transcriptome (Lim 

et al., 2016, 2021; Paasela et al., 2023) and the genome of the closely related species P. taeda (Sundell et al., 2015) failed to reveal sequences similar to CNL. This absence is consistent with the fact that no true CNL enzymes have been reported in any coniferous plant species. Phylogenetic analysis (Figure 4) showed that CNLs form a distinct clade from 4CLs, indicating that these genes are evolutionarily distant. 

Given this, we turned our attention to the enzyme 4CL, which activates hydroxycinnamic acids into their CoA thioesters, before the pathway branches into lignin, flavonoid, and stilbene biosynthesis. Different 4CL enzymes of different plant species show different efficiency with hydroxylated or methoxylated cinnamic acids, but are very rarely reported to activate cinnamate itself (Hamberger & Hahlbrock, 2004; Li et al., 2015; Li & Nair, 2015). Using a transcriptomic approach, we identified four 4CL isoforms in Scots pine that are expressed in tissues accumulating stilbenes. Three isoforms, Ps4CL2-4, were co-expressed with STS and PMT2, while Ps4CL1 was associated with lignin biosynthesis (Figure 3). 

We assayed all four of the Scots pine 4CLs with cinnamic and 4-coumaric acids. One of the enzymes, Ps4CL2, stood out as an efficient user of the substrate cinnamic acid, showing a significantly low Km value of $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } } _ { i }$ , compared with the other 4CLs tested under our experimental conditions (Table 1). The cellular concentration of cinnamate is estimated to be in the range of $1 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ (Klempien et al., 2012), and thus Ps4CL2 is well-suited to catalyze reactions at this substrate level at a physiologically relevant rate. The cinnamate-utilizing 4CLs from Arabidopsis and raspberry, on the other hand, had a higher turnover number than Ps4CL2, but their low affinity makes it unlikely that cinnamate is a true substrate for these enzymes. 

By contrast, Ps4CL1, Ps4CL3, and ${ \sf P s 4 C L 4 }$ preferred 4- coumaric acid instead of cinnamic acid as their substrate, and thus these isoforms likely contribute to the lignin and flavonoid pathways. Ps4CL1 shares $9 8 \%$ sequence identity with Pt4CL1 from P. taeda, which has been previously reported to have a Km of 79 and $7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ to cinnamic and 4- coumaric acids, respectively (Voo et al., 1995). Downregulation of the closest homolog of Ps4CL1 in P. radiata resulted in weakly lignified tissues, suggesting a role in lignin biosynthesis (Wagner et al., 2009). Ps4CL3 and Ps4CL4 are closely related to Pt4CL3, an enzyme likely involved in flavonoid biosynthesis typically derived from 4-coumaric acid, as ectopic expression of Pt4CL3 was shown to alter flavonoid metabolism (Chen et al., 2014). It is of note, however, that pines also accumulate cinnamate-derived flavonoids, such as pinocembrin (Alperth et al., 2023; Loman, 2011; Sinyeue et al., 2023). 

To crack the basis by which Ps4CL2 is able to utilize cinnamic acid, we analyzed the amino acid sequence 

alignment between Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3, the two pine enzymes with high sequence similarity but contrasting substrate preference, and compared the sequences to the previously studied At4CL2 and the crystalized Poto4CL1 and Nt4CL2 (Hu et al., 2010; Li & Nair, 2015; Schneider et al., 2003). Previous studies have hypothesized that the most important factor determining substrate specificity in 4CL enzymes is the size and hydrophobicity of the binding pocket. Most 4CLs are predicted to have very similar binding pockets and highly conserved residues around the pocket (Hu et al., 2010), with a conserved tyrosine residue that interacts with the aromatic ring of the adenylated hydroxycinnamate substrate through $\pi$ -stacking (Li & Nair, 2015). Supporting this hypothesis, Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 sequences align well (Figure 5) and their predicted 3D structures are highly similar to the crystal structures of Poto4CL1 and Nt4CL2 (Figure S3). Of residues predicted to be important for substrate binding (Hu et al., 2010; Li & Nair, 2015; Schneider et al., 2003) or which are close to the bound substrate in the models, Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 differ only by one pair of adjacent amino acids. In Ps4CL2, the conserved substrate binding tyrosine is replaced by phenylalanine (VF in Ps4CL2 instead of IY in Ps4CL3), making the binding pocket more hydrophobic. 

To determine if the more hydrophobic binding pocket is crucial for binding the more hydrophobic substrate (cinnamic acid), we reciprocally exchanged the two residues between Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3. In Ps4CL2, targeted mutagenesis of the two residues reduced catalytic rate without altering Km, for both cinnamic and 4-coumaric acid. On the contrary, mutant Ps4CL3 carrying the reciprocal mutations exhibited opposite kinetic properties than the mutant Ps4CL2. The mutated Ps4CL3 bound less efficiently, not only to 4-coumaric acid but also cinnamic acid, but the catalytic rate is not affected. Although the underlying reasons remain unclear, similar result has also been observed for Nt4CL2, where mutagenesis from amino acid Y to F on the corresponding position led to maintained Kcat but increased Km (Li & Nair, 2015). These results highlight the complexity behind Ps4CL2’s substrate specificity, for which we propose that rather than relying on one or two amino acids, Ps4CL2 appears to incorporate multiple domains across the enzyme to efficiently bind cinnamic acid. 

To better locate the regions responsible for substrate preference in Ps4CL2, we conducted domain-swapping with Ps4CL3. Chimeric enzymes were generated by exchanging the N-terminus, SBR, and C-terminal regions using the conserved Box I and II as the fusion points. Exchange of the SBR between the two enzymes largely duplicated the effect of exchanging the two amino acids (VF vs. IY), indicating that these two amino acids were the main determinants of the region defined as SBR. Interestingly, chimeric Ps4CL3 harboring the N-terminus of Ps4CL2 (fusion 2–3-3) displayed a high catalytic efficiency on cinnamic acid (but 

reduced affinity), suggesting that the N-terminus is a region of importance for cinnamate use in Scots pine 4CL. This is contrary to a previous finding in Arabidopsis where the Nterminus of 4CL was considered non-contributory to substrate preference (Ehlting et al., 2001). When the C-terminal regions of the enzymes were exchanged, there were mixed effects on Kcat and Km. This result is in line with the report from Stuible et al. (2000) which indicated the C-terminus of 4CL enzymes being important for its catalytic activity and is further supported by the fact that amino acids in the Cterminus come close to the substrate (Figure 5). Mutations in one or several amino acids in the C-terminus region could also harm or completely abolish the catalytic activity of the enzyme (Stuible et al., 2000). Our results suggest that the substrate specificity of pine 4CLs toward cinnamic acid arises from an interplay between all regions of the enzyme and not from only the SBR as previously suggested based on studies Arabidopsis and poplar. 

Based on these results, we propose that Ps4CL2 is the enzyme that plays a critical role in pinosylvin biosynthesis in Scots pine. In phylogeny, all characterized gymnosperm 4CL genes form together a separate clade (class III, Figure 4). Ps4CL1 groups with 4CLs from different Pinus species involved in lignin biosynthesis. Ps4CL2-4 form a separate clade from these, which is split between pine and spruce sequences. All pine species synthesize the stilbene pinosylvin, but spruce species synthesize resveratrol and its derivatives (Hammerbacher et al., 2011). Among Ps4CL2-4, Ps4CL2 formed a distinct clade together with an uncharacterized 4CL from P. taeda, suggesting a potentially divergent function in planta, compared with the other 4CLs. A P. strobus 4CL reported to be strongly upregulated alongside STS gene following pine wood nematode infection (Hwang et al., 2021) also clustered close to Ps4CL2. Although not yet investigated, the necessary cinnamate-activating enzyme in other pine species can be predicted to be found among the ones close to Ps4CL2 in phylogeny. A final proof for involvement of Ps4CL2 in pinosylvin biosynthesis would come from down regulation of Ps4CL2 expression in transgenic Scots pine, but such an approach is prohibitively challenging (Haggman et al., 1997). Nevertheless, discovering a cinnamate-activating CoA ligase with high cinnamate affinity and co-expressed with pinosylvin biosynthesis genes gives a solution to the last remaining riddle of this pathway. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Amplification of the 4CL encoding cDNAs and generation of entry vectors

Scots pine 4CL sequences were amplified from different cDNA libraries using PCR and Phusion High-Fidelity polymerase (ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The cDNA libraries were prepared using SuperScriptTM III Reverse Transcriptase (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and $1 \ \mu \mathfrak { g }$ of total RNA according to the manufacturer’s 

instructions. The coding sequence of Ps4CL1 was amplified from cDNA synthesized from RNA isolated from the developing xylem of 5-year-old seedlings (Lim et al., 2021). The Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 were amplified from UV-induced 6-week-old seedlings (Paasela et al., 2023). A. thaliana At4CL1 and At4CL5 genes were amplified from cDNA prepared from stem and cauline leaves. Raspberry RNA was extracted from berries that had turned pink but were not fully ripe. 

Full-length Ps4CL1 and Ps4CL3 sequences were retrieved from the Pinus EST collection version 9.0 (Lim et al., 2016) and from published studies of P. radiata and P. taeda (Chen et al., 2014; Wagner et al., 2009). These sequences were used to design gene-specific primers for Ps4CL1 and Ps4CL3. Since only a fragment of Ps4CL2 was present in the Pinus EST collection version $9 . 0 , 5 ^ { \prime }$ and ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ RACE were first performed to obtain the full-length sequence using SMART RACE ${ \mathfrak { J } } ^ { \prime } / { 3 ^ { \prime } }$ Kit (TakaraBio, Shiga, Japan) according to the manufacturer’s instructions using specific ${ \sf 5 ^ { \prime } }$ and ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ RACE primers. Since only ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ race was successful, to obtain the ${ \sf 5 ^ { \prime } }$ end of the gene, a contig encoding the 4CL2 gene was retrieved from P. taeda genome v 1.01, and primers flanking the coding area were designed. All primer sequences are listed in Table S3. 

Race fragments and full-length 4CL2 and 4CL3 PCR products were amplified from cDNA and first cloned into pJET1.2 (Thermo-Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Isolated plasmids were further used as templates to create final PCR products with Gateway attB flanking sequences. For the other 4CLs the final PCR products containing full-length open reading frames with attB flanking sequences were amplified from cDNA libraries and cloned directly into entry vector pDONRzeo. All entry vectors were generated through the Gateway BP reaction (Katzen, 2007) using primers specific for each gene (Table S3). Vectors were transformed into Escherichia coli DH5a competent cells and verified by sequencing. 

# Heterologous protein expression and purification

The verified entry vectors were cloned into the hypertranslatable expression vector pEAQ-HT-DEST2 (Sainsbury et al., 2009) with an N-terminus histidine tag through Gateway LR reaction (Katzen, 2007). The resulting plasmids were transformed into E. coli DH5a competent cells and the plasmids were extracted and verified by sequencing. Verified plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain C58C1(pGV2260) (Deblaere et al., 1985), plated onto selective agar and incubated for 3 days at $\mathsf { 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ . The pure culture was inoculated into 5 mL LB medium, grown overnight at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ with shaking at 200 rpm, pelleted at $7 0 0 0 \times \pmb { g }$ for 10 min, and adjusted to ${ 0 \mathsf { D } _ { 6 0 0 } } = 0 . 5$ g in agroinfiltration buffer $\begin{array} { r } { ( 1 0 \ \mathsf { m a } \ \mathsf { M } \ \mathsf { g C l } _ { 2 } , } \end{array}$ 10 mM MES-KOH, pH 5.6, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ acetosyringone). The mixture was infiltrated into the abaxial surface of 6-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Bashandy et al., 2015; Li, 2011) until the full leaf was infiltrated. After infiltration, the plants were grown for 6 days at $\textstyle 2 3 ^ { \circ } \complement$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod. About $1 6 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g } }$ of the infiltrated leaf tissue was harvested, ground to powder in liquid nitrogen, and soluble proteins were extracted with $6 . 4 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ phosphate buffer (Cytiva Life Sciences, Marlborough, MA, USA) containing $2 0 \mathsf { m m }$ imidazole and supplemented with EDTAfree protease inhibitor (Roche, Mannheim, Germany) and $0 . 2 \%$ bmercaptoethanol. His-tagged 4CL proteins were purified from centrifugally cleared extracts using a single His SpinTrap column according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Cytiva Life Sciences), finally eluted in $0 . 4 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ phosphate buffer with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { M }$ imidazole, and the buffer was exchanged to $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris–HCl (pH 7.5) using PD MiniTrap G25 Columns (Cytiva Life Sciences). The purified proteins were analyzed in pre-cast SDS-PAGE gel (Bio-Rad Mini PROTEAN TGX, Hercules, CA, USA) to assess their purity and confirm their 

predicted molecular weight calculated with the ExPASy Compute pI/Mw tool (Figure S4; Gasteiger et al., 2005). Protein concentrations were determined using the BCA protein assay (ThermoFisher Scientific) with BSA as the standard. The purified 4CL enzymes were stored at $\mathtt { - 8 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ until further analysis. 

# Spectrophotometric assay of 4CL enzyme kinetics and product identification using HPLC

The expressed Scots pine enzymes were functionally characterized using HPLC assay. Reaction mixture was prepared as described by Knobloch and Hahlbrock (1977) with modifications, containing 150 mM Tris–HCl $\left( \mathsf { p } \mathsf { H } \ 7 . 8 \right)$ and final concentration of 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), 0.33 mM coenzyme A (CoA), 5 mM magnesium-adenosine triphosphate (Mg-ATP), $ 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrate, and $5 ~ \mu \ L$ of the extracted enzymes in a total volume of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ . The reaction was incubated for 10 min and subsequently stopped by adding two volumes of methanol, followed by centrifugation for 10 min. HPLC analysis was performed using Agilent 1100 series (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) equipped with Zorbax Rc C-18 column. The mobile phase consisted of the following solvents: Solvent A, Milli-Q water containing $0 . 0 8 \%$ trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), and solvent B, acetonitrile containing $0 . 0 8 \%$ TFA. A gradient from 5 to $9 5 \%$ of solvent B was run for 22 min at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. As reference compounds, cinnamoyl- and coumaroyl-CoA from TransMIT GmbH (Giessen, Germany) were used. Detection was carried out using diode array detector at 280, 310, and 333 nm. Flow rate, solvent composition, and pump pressure were continuously recorded during each run to ensure stability and reproducibility. 

Enzyme kinetics were measured at room temperature using a spectrophotometric assay with a reaction mixture as described above, but with $0 . 1 { - } 4 . 3 ~ \mu 9$ of 4CL enzyme with a total volume of $2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ . Purified enzyme extracts were initially assayed at their extracted concentrations and then diluted as required to achieve measurable activities. The activity was assessed on a 96-well plate with cinnamic and 4-coumaric acid as substrates, following absorbance at 310 and $3 3 3 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , respectively, utilizing a microplate reader (Synergy H1; Agilent Technologies). For the Michaelis constant (Km) and maximum velocity (Vmax) measurement, six final substrate concentrations (6.25, 12.5, 25, 50, 100, and $2 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ were used. For enzymes exhibiting lower apparent Km values, the substrate concentration range was adjusted accordingly to ensure accurate measurement. 

# Data analysis

All initial velocity $( v _ { 0 } )$ values were extracted using the default settings of the microplate reader software. The Km and Vmax values were calculated by non-linear curve fitting using Origin(Pro), Version 2024b. OriginLab Corporation, Northampton, MA, USA. Standard deviations were calculated from three replications. Kcat values were calculated by dividing the maximum velocity $( V _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ by the molar concentration of active enzymes. 

# Site-directed mutagenesis

Site-directed mutagenesis was performed to substitute the amino acid pair VF from Ps4CL2 at position 263 with IY from Ps4CL3, and vice versa, following the protocol by Zheng et al. (2004) with modifications. The primer pair was designed with the intended mutation sequences in the middle of the primers (Table S3). Linear PCR was performed using 500 ng of $\mathsf { P s 4 C L 2 }$ and Ps4CL3 in the entry plasmid as the template with the following PCR program: $\mathfrak { g } 5 \mathrm { \textdegree C }$ for 3 min, 30 cycles of $9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 min, $5 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 s and $\scriptscriptstyle { 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 8 min. The 

PCR was carried out with only one primer in each reaction to amplify single-stranded molecules. The single-stranded amplicons were then mixed and reannealed by heating at $9 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min, allowing to cool slowly, and then digested with DpnI to remove the background template. The products were transformed into E. coli DH5a competent cells, and the inserts were verified by sequencing. 

# Generation of chimeric constructs

Chimeric enzyme sequences were generated by assembling fragments from Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 using the Golden Gate Cloning method (Engler & Marillonnet, 2014). Each gene was divided into three fragments at the conserved Box I and Box II. The fragments were amplified from the entry vectors using PCR with gene-specific primer pairs (Table S3). To ensure compatibility at the swap sites, primers were designed to include a BsaI recognition site adjacent to four common nucleotides at each swap junction. Fragment generation used 100 ng of plasmid template, both forward and reverse primers, and an extension time of 1 min for 15 cycles following the PCR program described above. 

Full chimeric constructs were assembled by digesting the fragments with BsaI to create sticky ends, followed by ligation with corresponding fragments using a thermal cycler with the following program: 10 cycles of $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 5 min and $\uparrow 6 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $1 0 { \mathrm { ~ m i n } } ,$ , followed by $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 5 min and ${ 8 0 ^ { \circ } } \complement$ for 10 min. The PCR mixture contained T4 ligation buffer, FastDigest BsaI restriction enzyme (ThermoFisher Scientific), T4 DNA ligase (ThermoFisher Scientific), and the respective fragments from Ps4CL2 and Ps4CL3 in a final volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \ L$ . A final PCR was conducted to generate attB sequences flanking the chimeric constructs, which were cloned into destination vectors using the Gateway cloning system, transformed to E. coli DH5a and verified by sequencing, as previously described. 

# Phylogenetic analysis of Ps4CL-like proteins

Previously published coding sequences of 4CLs and CNLs from various plant species (Table S4) were extracted from the National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI). Additional spruce 4CL sequences previously described by Chen et al. (2014), Picea abies 4CL1, P. abies 4CL3, P. glauca 4CL1 and P. glauca 4CL2, were also included in the analysis. A homologous Pinus taeda 4CL-like sequence (Pt4CLx) was obtained by BLAST search using Ps4CL2 sequences against the PlantGenIE database (Sundell et al., 2015, https://plantgenie.org, accessed on 08.2024). The full-length Ps4CL1-4 sequences from Scots pine described in this study have been deposited in GenBank under accession No. PX438698, PX438699, PX438700, and PX438701, respectively. 

Multiple sequence alignment was performed using the MUS-CLE algorithm in MEGA-X (Kumar et al., 2018). Phylogenetic tree was built using default parameters with the maximum likelihood method, and branch support was assessed by 1000 bootstrap replications.
