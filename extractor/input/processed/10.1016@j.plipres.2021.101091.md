# Headgroup biosynthesis of phosphatidylcholine and phosphatidylethanolamine in seed plants

![](images/7ad6a3edf07a6e87cf2a732c7294148b868e3fcc95c161c754f707a94e209d42.jpg)

Yuki Nakamura

Institute of Plant and Microbial Biology, Academia Sinica, Taipei, Taiwan

# ABSTRACT

Phospholipid biosynthesis is crucial for plant growth and development. It involves attachment of fatty acids to a phospho-diacylglycerol backbone and modification of the phospho-group into an amino alcohol. The biochemistry and molecular biology of the former has been well established, but a number of enzymes responsible for the latter have only recently been cloned and functionally characterized in Arabidopsis and some other model plant species. The metabolism involving the polar head groups of phospholipids established by past biochemical studies can now be validated by available gene knockout models. Moreover, gene knockout studies have revealed emerging functions of phospholipids in regulating plant growth and development. This review aims to revisit the old questions of polar headgroup biosynthesis of plant phosphatidylcholine and phosphatidylethanolamine by giving an overview of recent advances in the field and beyond.

# 1. Introduction

Phospholipids are highly conserved constituents of cellular membranes from bacteria to mammals and seed plants. The two most abundant phospholipid classes in eukaryotes are phosphatidylcholine (PtdCho) and phosphatidylethanolamine (PtdEtn). Many prokaryotes lack PtdCho but commonly possess PtdEtn [1]. In seed plants and algae, early lipid research focused mainly on galactolipids and triacylglycerol (TAG) because of their relevance to photosynthesis and storage oil accumulation, and phospholipid metabolism was studied mostly in the context of providing precursors for the biosynthesis of these lipid classes. Upon entering the post-genomic era, functional gene knockout studies revealed the phospholipid function in lipid signaling involving phosphatidic acid (PA) or phosphoinositides. More recently, regulatory roles of other phospholipid classes such as PtdCho or phosphatidylserine (PtdSer) have been revealed [2]. The biochemistry of phospholipid metabolism is well documented in various plant species. However, the identification of enzymes responsible for each reaction step and functional validation by gene knockout study in the model plant Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) have progressed relatively slowly as compared with study of other parts of lipid metabolism. In recent years, a number of enzymes concerning the headgroup biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn have been investigated in Arabidopsis, which not only validated previously established biochemical features in the metabolism but also its commitment to plant growth and development that could not be investigated without gene knockout studies. In this article, I focus on the headgroup biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn by summarizing the

findings of the enzyme function catalyzing each reaction step.

# 2. Biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn

# 2.1. Overview

The metabolic map focusing on the headgroup biosynthesis of glycerolipids in Arabidopsis is shown in Fig. 1, which consists of 5 compartments: de novo synthesis that produces precursory lipids for the biosynthesis of the other 4 groups of lipid classes (gray); phosphatidylglycerol (PtdGro), phosphatidylinositol (PtdIns) and cardiolipin (CL) originating from PA (orange); PtdCho, PtdEtn and PtdSer derived from diacylglycerol (DAG)(yellow); triacylglycerol (TAG)(blue); and the glycoglycerolipids monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG), digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG) and sulfoquionovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG) synthesized in plastids (green). In this article, I describe the "yellow" compartment, except for PtdSer, which is reviewed elsewhere [3]. The overview of the entire glycerolipid metabolism in Arabidopsis is described in [4].

The major advance in understanding the headgroup biosynthesis of PtdEtn and PtdCho includes 1) the importance of serine to phosphoethanolamine (PEtn) biosynthesis, 2) the identification and characterization of major enzymes responsible for PtdEtn biosynthesis from serine, and 3) phospho-base methyltransferases (PMTs) as the bottleneck in PtdCho biosynthesis. The following sections describe the biochemical and physiological features of each enzyme.

# 2.2. Serine decarboxylase (SDC)

SDC converts serine to ethanolamine (Etn) in one reaction step. This reaction is unusual because serine decarboxylation occurs while the serine is attached to a DAG backbone and not to free serine in most organisms. This enzyme activity was first revealed in plants and detected only in malarial parasites other than plants to date. Gene manipulation studies of Arabidopsis SDC1 demonstrated that SDC1 is involved in the production of Etn, PtdEtn and PtdCho, and its function is crucial for embryogenesis [13]. Of note, SDC1 is localized at mitochondria and interacts with amino acid metabolism [16]. How mitochondrial SDC1 competes with the metabolism of other amino acids for ER-localized PtdEtn and PtdCho biosynthesis and how it affects embryogenesis are emerging issues.

Earlier biochemical analysis with protein extracts of Lemna fronds [5], cucurbit seedlings [6] and leaf extracts of Arabidopsis, rapeseed, bush daisy, pea, wheat, and spinach [7] indicated possible SDC activity. Later, the first cloning of cDNA encoding SDC was achieved in Arabidopsis and rapeseed by using a different approach [7]. In Arabidopsis, the identification of SDC was based on sequence homology to other amino acid decarboxylases. Arabidopsis SDC expressed in $E$ coli showed

SDC activity in vitro. That expressed in a yeast $\Delta psd1\Delta psd2$ mutant defective in PtdSer decarboxylase activity [8] complemented the Etn requirement of the mutant. In rapeseed, a cDNA from a library that complements the Etn requirement of a yeast $\Delta cho1$ mutant was identified as an SDC. These SDC polypeptides are $90\%$ identical in amino acid sequences and belong to the amino acid decarboxylase group II. Arabidopsis SDC does not attack D-serine, L-phosphoserine, PtdSer, or other amino acids, so it is highly specific to serine and is thus distinct from PtdSer decarboxylase [7]. SDC homologs are widely distributed in plants; however, the substrate specificity does not appear strictly defined by the sequence similarity. For example, SDC-like polypeptides in Medicago truncatula and Cicer arietinum catalyze the decarboxylation and oxidative deamination of a few amino acids (leucine, methionine, phenylalanine, and tryptophan) and generate their corresponding acetaldehydes [9]. Apart from seed plants, the malaria parasite Plasmodium possesses SDC activity that may contribute to PtdCho biosynthesis [10,11].

Later, a null mutant allele, $sdc1-2$ , was shown to have an embryonic lethal phenotype [13]. The embryos of $sdc1-2$ showed developmental arrest at the heart stage. The phenotype was genetically complemented by transgenic SDC1 driven by its own promoter or constitutive 35S promoter. In the overexpression lines showing about 20- to 40-fold increase in SDC1 transcript levels (Pro35S:SDC1 $sdc1-2$ ), Etn content was significantly elevated in rosette leaves and mature siliques. This effect confirmed an earlier report of transient expression of Arabidopsis SDC1 in tobacco showing increased Etn content [14]. Also, PtdCho and PtdEtn levels were increased in leaves and mature siliques of Pro35S:SDC1 $sdc1-2$ , which suggests that conversion of serine to Etn by SDC1 is involved in the synthesis of these phospholipid classes. Because serine is a key amino acid whose metabolism involves multiple incoming and outgoing pathways [15], a significant question is how the PtdCho and PtdEtn

biosynthesis pathway competes with amino acid metabolism against the acquisition of serine, such as in glycine, cysteine and thymidylate biosynthesis. Levels of major amino acids were quantified with use of Pro35S:SDC1 sdc1-2 to upregulate the biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn [13]. The levels of glycine, glutamine, aspartate and threonine were decreased in the overexpression line [16]. This suggests that upregulating PtdEtn/PtdCho biosynthesis may increase the competition for the same serine pool that is involved in glycine, glutamine, aspartate and threonine biosynthesis, an example of metabolic cross-talk between phospholipid and amino acid metabolism.

Regarding subcellular localization, in contrast to membrane-bound PtdSer decarboxylase, SDC is a soluble protein. With a transgenic Arabidopsis plant expressing ProSDC1:SDC1-Venus in sdc1-2 that complements the embryonic lethal phenotype, SDC1-Venus fluorescent signal was localized at mitochondria in leaves and roots [16]. Although biochemical relevance of mitochondrial localization remains to be

addressed, Arabidopsis CTP:phosphorylethanolamine cytidylyltransferase 1 (PECT1), which catalyzes two steps after SDC1 reaction and is embryonic-lethal when knocked out, also showed mitochondrial localization [17]. How SDC1 plays a role under stress conditions remains for investigation. Treating Arabidopsis with heavy metal ions such as $\mathrm{Ni}^{2+}$ or $\mathrm{Mn}^{2+}$ induced transcript levels of SDC1, yet its physiological role is unknown [18].

# 2.3. Choline/ethanolamine kinases (CEKs)

Cho and Etn kinases catalyze ATP-dependent phosphorylation of Cho and Etn, respectively. Since the first discovery of Cho kinase in 1953 [19], opinions differed as to whether Cho and Etn kinases are derived from a single enzyme (e.g., [20,21]). Recent studies in Arabidopsis showed that CEK4 is the only Etn kinase involved in PtdEtn and PtdCho biosynthesis in vivo and essential for embryogenesis, whereas CEK1, 2, and 3 function as Cho kinases in vivo [36,38]. The developmental impact of CEK4 and possible requirement of Cho kinase in PtdCho biosynthesis and plant development await further investigation.

In plants, the first Cho kinase activity was reported in 1957 from rapeseed (Brassica campestris) and phosphorylated Cho but not Etn [22]. Then, a number of plant species and tissues were examined for Cho kinase activity assay [23-29]. Plant Etn kinase activity was initially detected in spinach leaf [30], which was followed by a comparative enzyme activity assay with germinating soybean [31]. The biochemical properties revealed by these works suggested that Cho and Etn kinases are separate enzymes. In 1979, the first purification of both kinases was achieved in soybean: purified Cho kinase exhibited substrate preference for Cho over Etn of 3:1 or more [32], but purified Etn kinase showed substrate specificity to Etn [33]. Later on, cloning and enzymatic characterization of 2 Cho kinase cDNAs from soybean indicated their high substrate specificity to Cho over Etn [34].

In Arabidopsis, 4 putative Cho kinases and 1 putative Etn kinase were tested for changes in transcript levels under low temperature, salinity and osmotic stresses [35]. However, 1 of 4 putative Cho kinases (At1g34100) was suggested to be a pseudogene because its transcript level was barely detectable [35], and no suitable start codon was identified in its sequence [36]. Thus, the remaining putative kinases were grouped into a single enzyme family owing to high amino acid sequence similarity and designated the CEK family, CEK1 to CEK4 [36]. In agreement with results of earlier enzyme purification in soybean [32,33], these 4 CEK isoforms showed distinct substrate specificity in vitro. Enzyme activity assay with recombinant proteins showed that CEK4 and CEK3 specifically phosphorylated Etn and Cho, respectively. CEK1 and CEK2 showed substrate preference for Cho over Etn [37,38]. In vivo pulse-chase analysis showed that the production of $\left[{ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\right] \mathrm{PCho}$ after $\left[{ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\right] \mathrm{Cho}$ labeling but not $\left[{ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\right] \mathrm{Etn}$ labeling was reduced in the knockout mutant of CEK1, which suggests that CEK1 functions as a Cho kinase in vivo [37]. The effect of CEK1 knockout on Cho kinase activity was more pronounced in roots than shoot [38]. The knockout mutants of CEK2 and CEK3 had a much weaker effect on Cho kinase activity than did the CEK1 mutant, and none of these 3 CEKs affected Etn kinase activity [38]. Thus, in vivo, CEK1, CEK2, and CEK3 function as a Cho kinase, whereas CEK4 functions as an Etn kinase.

The distribution of the 4 CEKs is well documented at tissue and subcellular levels [36-38]. In CEK4-GUS cek4-1 transgenic Arabidopsis, which complements the embryonic lethal phenotype, CEK4-GUS was expressed predominantly in the hypocotyl, leaf vasculature, and young stamens [36]. By contrast, CEK1-GUS was preferentially expressed in cotyledons, true leaves, roots, and petals [37], which showed a rather complementary expression pattern with CEK4-GUS [36]. CEK2-GUS and CEK3-GUS were preferentially expressed in hypocotyls, leaf vasculature, inflorescence stems, and roots [38]. In flowers, CEK2-GUS was expressed in stigma, with CEK3-GUS expressed in young stamens [38]. At the subcellular level, CEK1, CEK2 and CEK3 were localized at the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and CEK4 at the plasma membrane and

possibly at the ER in root cells [36-38].

The physiological role of the 4 CEKs is described on the basis of the characterization of gene knockout mutants. Among the 4 CEKs, knockout of $CEK4$ conferred an embryonic-lethal phenotype, with seed development arrested at the heart stage [36]. Although knockout mutants of the remaining 3 CEKs did not show the lethal phenotype, the seedling roots of $cek2-1$ and $cek3-1$ but not $cek1-1$ were shorter, with abnormal morphology in columella cells [38]. The effect in $cek3-1$ was complemented by transducing genomic $CEK3$ and by supplementing PCho exogenously, which suggests that the root phenotype may be caused by PCho shortage. Although the $cek1-1$ mutant does not show any morphological phenotype under normal growth conditions, the seedlings showed a hypersensitive phenotype upon treatment with tunicamycin, a reagent that interferes with protein glycosylation and causes ER stress owing to an accumulation of misfolded protein [37]. Because $cek1-1$ shows increased Cho level and decreased PCho content [37] but no alteration in the content of PtdCho and other polar glycerolipids [36], the ratio of Cho to PCho may be important in the ER stress response. Regarding the effect on lipid metabolism, overexpression of $CEK4$ increased the content of PC and PE in seedlings and siliques, but knockout of $CEK1$ , $CEK2$ , and $CEK3$ did not alter lipid content [36]. $CEK4$ knockout may have lethal effect because it is the only Etn kinase in vivo. In plants, de novo synthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn depends on the production of PEt n because Cho compounds are assumed to be produced from PEt n by methylation. Unlike heterotrophic animals that take up Cho through diet, which is readily used for the synthesis of PtdCho, plant cells may not use Cho as a major precursor for PtdCho biosynthesis because PCho produced from PEt n may be the main source of PtdCho biosynthesis (see Section 2.5). This situation may explain why Etn kinase has a more crucial effect than Cho kinases in knockout mutants [36-38].

# 2.4. Phosphoethanolamine/phosphocholine phosphatase (PECP)

The activity to dephosphorylate PEtn or PCho has been less investigated and was discussed in different physiological contexts. PCho phosphatase, likely does not contribute to PtdCho biosynthesis but rather to Cho production, which is a precursor for glycine betaine, an osmoprotectant in some plant species. PEtn phosphatase activity has been even less investigated because the reaction is opposite to the direction of de novo phospholipid biosynthesis, and the role of PEtn or Etn other than being a precursor for PtdEtn and PtdCho remains elusive.

Plant PCho phosphatase activity was initially reported in several plant species, but the activity was at low levels [23]. Use of tobacco leaves revealed that the activity was purified to 200- or 300-fold and appeared to be caused by a non-specific acid phosphatase [23]. In recent years, investigation of this enzyme activity focused on the context of phosphate starvation. A metabolic conversion, termed membrane lipid remodeling, occurs during phosphate starvation [39]: primary phospholipid classes in the cellular membrane such as PtdCho are replaced by non-phosphorus galactolipid DGDG [40]. A series of studies identified key enzymes involved in this metabolic conversion: non-specific phospholipase C (NPC) catalyzes the first step of PtdCho hydrolysis that produces DAG and PCho [41,42], then DAG is galactosylated by MGDG synthase [43] and DGDG synthase [44]. Because a primary biological significance of this conversion is to retrieve scarce phosphate sources from the membrane phospholipids, how PCho is further dephosphorylated by PCho phosphatase to liberate free phosphate is an important open question. PHOSPHATE STARVATION-INDUCED 2 (PS2) has been a widely used marker gene for investigating the phosphate starvation response [45]. Because PS2 is homologous to the haloacid dehalogenase (HAD) superfamily, to which a few important lipid phosphatases belong (e.g., phosphatidate phosphohydrolase 1 [PAH1], PAH2 and phosphatidylglycerophosphate phosphatase [PGPP1]; [46,47]), PS2 may encode PCho phosphatase. Although recombinant PS2 did not show PCho phosphatase activity but rather inorganic pyrophosphatase activity in vitro [48] and thus was renamed PPsase1,

its close homolog PECP1 was found to encode phosphatase activity that dephosphorylates PEtn and PCho [49]. The recombinant PECP1 protein exhibited $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ -dependent phosphatase activity with optimal pH ranging from $\mathrm{pH}6$ to 8, showing higher catalytic efficiency for PEtn than PCho [49]. PECP1 transcript level is induced by phosphate starvation [50].

The in vivo function of PECP1 and PS2/PPsPase1 was investigated by characterizing T-DNA knockout mutants and overexpressors [50-53]. In membrane lipid remodeling under phosphate starvation, phosphatases that dephosphorylate free phosphate from the phosphate-containing head group are unknown, so genetic ablation of phosphate supply from membrane phospholipids is not possible. Thus, whether phosphate-starved plants indeed utilize a phosphate source from membrane phospholipids remains unknown. Gene co-expression analysis indicated that PECP1 and PS2 are highly co-expressed with genes involved in membrane lipid remodeling under phosphate starvation [51]. The double mutant but not either of the single mutants reduced Cho level only under phosphate starvation, which suggests a functional redundancy between PECP1 and PS2 in vivo although PS2 does not likely use PCho or PEtn as a primary substrate in vitro [48]. A more detailed characterization of these mutants supported the redundancy in PCho and PEtn levels, although they do not affect membrane lipid content or free phosphate homeostasis under phosphate starvation [52]. However, in vivo overexpression study showed that PECP1 but not PS2 is mainly a PEtn phosphatase [50,53]. Functional reporter assays indicated that PECP1 and PS2 have an overlapping tissue expression pattern in vegetative growth, mainly in hypocotyls and leaf veins, with a distinct pattern in reproductive growth (PECP1 in anthers but PS2 in anther filaments); at the subcellular level, they localized similarly at the ER [53]. The mutants showed few morphological phenotypes, which could vary depending on minor growth conditions: the root length of pecp1-1 was slightly shorter than the wild type under phosphate starvation [50], but pecp1-1 ps2-3 did not show significant differences [51]. Root hair growth was impaired in the mutants of PS2, including ps2-1, under phosphate starvation [54]; however, this finding was not confirmed for two mutant alleles of PS2 or the double mutants [52].

# 2.5. Phospho-base N-methyltransferases (PMTs)

Because Cho is the trimethylated form of Etn, methylation of the Etn moiety is a primary metabolic process to produce the Cho moiety. PMTs catalyze the methylation of PEt, monomethyl PEt and dimethyl PEt to produce PCho. The PMT pathway is the primary route for PCho production in plants, nematodes (e.g., Caenorhabditis elegans) and the apicomplexan parasite Plasmodium falciparum [55-60]. In Arabidopsis, genetic disruption of all 3 PMTs proved this finding because the biosynthesis of PCho or PtdCho was not detected in the triple mutants [59,60]. In contrast, in yeast and mammalian cells, methylation of the Etn moiety occurs exclusively at the phosphatidyl base (e.g., PtdEtn) [61]. This difference is also noteworthy in explaining the origin of free choline and glycine betaine in plants [64]. Because this enzyme family potentially takes a wide range of substrates, different abbreviations have been used, which often causes confusion. In this review, "phospho-base $N$ -methyltransferases (PMTs)" will be used as the standard nomenclature based on a recent discussion of this issue [59].

Initial studies of plant PMTs focused on the biosynthesis of PtdCho for major membrane lipid constituent and glycine betaine for a potent osmoprotectant in some plant species. Plant PMT activity was detected in radiotracer experiments by using water-stressed barley leaves [62,63] and salinized sugarbeet leaves [64]. Using the duckweed Lemna paucicostata, radiolabeling experiments showed that more than $99\%$ of methylation occurs at PEt n over PtdEtn or Etn [55]. A comparison among Lemna, soybean and carrot revealed that, although the first methylation occurs exclusively for PEt n, second and third methylations involve an interspecies variety [65,66]. For the biosynthesis of PtdCho, the major pathway in Lemna is 3 sequential methylations of PEt n to

produce PCho, whereas in soybean the second and third methylations occurred at the phosphatidyl base after monomethyl PEt n is converted to monomethyl PtdEtn. In carrot, both pathways work in parallel. Furthermore, in olive callus, an alternative methylation pathway involves the first 2 methylations exclusively at the phospho-base level, followed by conversion of dimethyl PEt n to dimethyl PtdEtn and the final methylation at the phosphatidyl base [67]. Methylation to free Etn is detected in castor bean endosperm [68] and the enzyme activity of Etn methyltransferase has been characterized [69]. Whether this pathway is commonly found in other plant organs or species remains unknown.

Arabidopsis has 3 copies of PMTs: PMT1 (At3g18000), PMT2 (At1g48600), and PMT3 (At1g73600). The enzyme activity of these PMTs was initially tested by complementing the yeast mutant $\Delta$ cho2 $\Delta$ opi3, the double knockout of 2 methyltransferases [70] responsible for the conversion of PtdEtn to PtdCho [71]. This mutant is unable to produce PtdCho and thus requires Cho supplementation for optimal growth [70]. By expressing Arabidopsis PMT1 (designated NMT1 in the publication), the choline auxotrophy in $\Delta$ cho2 $\Delta$ opi3 was rescued, which suggests that PMT1 can catalyze all 3 steps of methylation by creating a pathway that converts PEtn to PCho in yeast [72]. Likewise, PMT2 (designated PMEAMT in the publication) was examined for genetic complementation of $\Delta$ cho2 $\Delta$ opi3 [73]. The mutant cell harboring PMT1 recovered the growth by supplementing Etn, but the growth of one cell harboring PMT2 was rescued by monomethyl Etn but not Etn, which suggests that PMT2 cannot catalyze the first methylation of PEtn. Regarding PMT3, two splice variants PMT3.1 and PMT3.2 both complemented the $\Delta$ cho2 $\Delta$ opi3, which suggests that PMT3 may catalyze all methylation steps as in PMT1 [74,75]. A more rigorous steady-state kinetic analysis of the PMTs was performed by using purified recombinant PMT proteins expressed in $E.$ coli [76]. From the comparison of Kcat/Km values, PMT1 showed a 3-fold preference for PEtn over monomethyl PEtn or dimethyl PEtn, whereas both PMT2 and PMT3 showed preference for PEtn with 1.5- to 2-fold higher catalytic efficiency as compared with the other 2 substrates [76].

Besides Arabidopsis, PMT has been cloned from spinach [77], wheat [78,79], corn (Zea mays) [80], and canola (Brassica napus) [81]. The spinach PMT was isolated by functional complementation of $\Delta$ cho2 in Schizosaccharomyces pombe and was shown to methylate PEtn but not Etn or PtdEtn [77]. Although it catalyzes all 3 methylation steps, truncation of the C-terminal methyltransferase domain retained activity only to the first methylation [77]. In wheat, 2 copies of PMT were reported. The first isoform, TaPEAMT1/WPEAMT, catalyzes the 3 sequential methylations; the N-terminal domain was responsible for the 3 methylations, but the C-terminal domain was suggested to have regulatory functions [78]. The second isoform, TaPEAMT2, showed 4-fold higher specific activity than TaPEAMT1 and has distinct enzymatic properties [79].

Unlike nematodes and $P$ falciparum PMTs that contain one methyltransferase motif, plant PMTs contain 2 methyltransferase domains in tandem: the N-terminal domain catalyzes the first methylation of PEtn to produce monomethyl PEtn and the C-terminal domain catalyzes the second and last methylation reactions to produce PCho [72,77]. The molecular basis for the di-domain structure was investigated by determining the X-ray crystal structures of Arabidopsis PMT1 and PMT2, which revealed unique features in active sites in each methyltransferase domain that use different chemical mechanisms for catalysis [76]. As a functional demonstration of structural study, Tyr-131 and Tyr-246 in the first and second methyltransferase domains were found crucial for the substrate specificity [76]. Aside from the methyltransferase domains, the N-terminal sequence in two splice variants of PMT2 was suggested to be important by comparing PMT2.1 and PMT2.2, which has 16 extra amino acid residues in its N-terminus and rescues the growth phenotype of Δcho2 Δopi3 better than does PMT2.1 [60]. PMT activity was inhibited by PCho and S-adenosyl-L-homocysteine (AdoHcy) in spinach [77,82], wheat [79] and Arabidopsis [73]. In addition to the effect possibly by feedback inhibition, upstream open reading frame 30

(uORF30) located upstream of Arabidopsis PMT1 responded to Cho or PCho and induced the translational repression of the downstream ORF [83], which suggests a possible mode of translational regulation via an uORF. Regarding the subcellular localization of PMT activity, enzyme activity was detected in the microsomal fraction (15,000 to 100,000 g pellet) in Lemna, soybean, and carrot [66] but was cytosolic in spinach leaves [84]. Further clarification of the subcellular localization of cloned plant PMTs is awaited.

The 3 Arabidopsis PMTs show differential tissue distribution of transcripts. On the basis of public transcriptomic data analyzed with the Genevestigator database (genevestigator.com), the most striking difference is that PMT1 and PMT2 but not PMT3 were abundant in roots [59]. However, RT-qPCR analysis did not clearly support this feature and both PMT1 and PMT3 were more abundant in leaf than other tissue [75], whereas PMT2 had similar transcript levels between roots and leaf tissue [60]. Reproductive organs such as flowers are not the preferred organs for expression of PMTs; however, PMT2.2, the minor splice variant of PMT2, was specific to flower buds and the transcript level exceeded that of PMT2.1 in this organ; all the other organs examined showed much lower transcript levels of PMT2.2 than PMT2.1 [60]. The results of GUS reporter analysis showed that PMT1 and PMT3 were abundant in vascular tissues in young rosette leaves and roots, although in roots PMT1 was more abundant than PMT3 [74,75]. PMT2.1 also showed similar GUS expression patterns as PMT1 in seedlings except that in roots, PMT2.1-GUS stain was more confined to root tips as compared with PMT1-GUS, showing a broader staining pattern [59,60]. During reproductive growth, the inflorescence stem showed abundant PMT1-GUS and PMT2.1-GUS but not PMT3-GUS expression [60,75]. Floral organs did not have an intense GUS signal: signals were observed only in the septum of pistils (PMT1-GUS and PMT2-GUS), young floral buds (PMT2-GUS), and ovules (PMT3-GUS) [59,60,75].

The physiological role of PMTs in plant growth and development has been addressed by using Arabidopsis mutants of the 3 PMTs. Among the single mutants, the short root phenotype of the \( pmt1 / xpl \) [85] is the only reported mutant phenotype (i.e., \( pmt2 \) and \( pmt3 \) single mutants have no mutant phenotype observed) [74,76]. The double mutant \( pmt1 \) \( pmt3 \) showed severe dwarfism [74,75], which was enhanced in the \( pmt1 \) \( pmt2 \) \( pmt3 \) triple mutant, for seedling lethality [59,60]. The \( pmt1 \) \( pmt2 \) mutant showed a slightly enhanced short-root phenotype of \( pmt1 \) [59], whereas \( pmt2 \) \( pmt3 \) featured no growth phenotype [60,74]. The short-root phenotype of \( pmt1 \) is the best-characterized mutant phenotype of the PMTs. The phenotype was initially found by screening T-DNA mutagenized lines showing remarkable alterations in root development. The gene locus for \( PMT1 \) was thus named XIPOTL1, which means swelling or tumefaction in the Nahuatl language [85]. The \( xpl \) mutant had a shortroot length with increased lateral root number [85]. The \( xpl \) roots featured cell death along with globular and collapsed cells in epidermis [85]. The root phenotype was completely rescued by exogenous supplementation of PCho, but PCho content in \( xpl \) roots was not reduced significantly [85]. Also, genetic complementation using the \( PMT1 \) promoter sequence showed that \( PMT3 \) and \( PMT2.2 \) but not \( PMT2.1 \) rescued the short root phenotype of \( pmt1 \) [60,75], so the root phenotype may be defined by the expression pattern of \( PMT1 \) rather than the enzyme properties of PMT isozymes. In a separate forward genetic screen, the defective primary root 2 (dpr2) mutant carried a mutation in \( PMT1 \) [86]. The dpr2 mutant affected the root stem cell niche, division zone, and elongation and differentiation zones. In the root tip, PIN-FORMED (PIN) protein abundance, PIN2 polar distribution, and general endocytosis were impaired. The elongation and differentiation zones accumulated excess hydrogen peroxide and auxin, which accelerated cell differentiation and thus led to root apical meristem consumption. These observations all support that \( PMT1 \) knockout specifically affects the root but not shoot, which is contrary to a more global pleiotrophic effect shown by anti-sense suppression of \( PMT1 \) that caused pale-green leaves, early senescence, and temperature-sensitive male sterility [87]. Besides PMT1, PMT2 plays a minor role in root growth because the \( pmt1-1 \) \( pmt2-

1 double mutant showed further reduced short-root length and cell viability of $pmt1-1$ , although no growth phenotype was observed in $pmt2-1$ and $pmt2-2$ [59]. Like $PMT2$ mutants, $PMT3$ single knockout conferred no growth phenotype [74,75], even though levels of PCho and Cho were nearly half that of the wild type [74]. The seedlings of $PMT1$ and $PMT3$ double knockout mutants produced pale-green cotyledons and narrow true leaves [74,75]. The dwarfed development was extended to reproductive growth with aborted flowers and gametophytes [75]. Despite such pleiotrophic growth defect, the seeds of double homozygotes were viable [74,75]. Of note, the siliques homozygous for $pmt1$ and heterozygous for $pmt3$ contained normal-looking seeds [74,75], whereas those heterozygous for $pmt1$ and homozygous for $pmt3$ harbored aborted seeds, which suggests that PMT1 and PMT3 play synergistic but unequal roles in embryogenesis and seed development [75]. Regarding the other 2 possible double mutants, $pmt1$ $pmt2$ showed a slightly enhanced short-root phenotype of $pmt1$ [59] and some reduced inflorescence stem height [60]. Also, $pmt2$ $pmt3$ has no morphological phenotype documented [60,74]. However, the $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ triple mutant showed further enhanced defective growth of $pmt1$ $pmt3$ , whose seedling growth was totally arrested by 14 days [59] and caused a seedling-lethal phenotype [59,60]. Thus, the 3 PMTs are essential for plant viability.

The metabolite phenotypes of these $pmt$ mutants provide some important clues as to the role of each isozyme. Comparative analysis indicated that PCho, Cho and PtdCho content in the single and double mutants was highly correlated with PCho and Cho but not PtdCho content [74]. Among the single mutants examined (pmt1-2, pmt2-2, and pmt3-1), only $pmt3-1$ showed reduced PCho and Cho levels, about a half that of the wild type, but PtdCho level was unaffected. The similar extent of reduction was observed in $pmt2-1$ $pmt3-2$ but not $pmt1-2$ $pmt2-2$ , and $pmt1-2$ $pmt3-1$ had trace levels of PCho and Cho, which resulted in a PtdCho decline to about one half that of the wild type [74]. This result suggests that PtdCho level receives a fine control other than the activity of PMTs. In the $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ triple mutant, de novo biosynthesis of PCho and PtdCho was totally abolished [59,60]. Thus, in Arabidopsis, PMT is the sole pathway for PCho and PtdCho biosynthesis, and this "PtdCho-less" mutant demonstrated an essential role in post-embryonic plant growth and development. Because PtdCho is the most abundant membrane phospholipid class, reduced PtdCho level in $pmt1$ $pmt3$ double mutants and $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ triple mutants altered the content (in tissue weight basis) [60,75] as well as composition (in mol% basis) [59,74] of membrane lipids. Because the PtdEtn biosynthesis pathway is unaffected in the triple mutant [60], changes in other lipid classes are likely caused by the secondary effect of the stunted growth phenotype (e.g., reduced galactolipid contents in $pmt1$ $pmt3$ mutants could result from the pale-green phenotype of the seedlings [74]). Dissecting the primary and secondary effects of PMT knockout on the altered lipid profiles is challenging but an important outstanding issue.

External environmental cues that stimulate PMT include salinity in spinach [77,82,84], corn [80], and Arabidopsis [76,87]; low temperature in wheat [78,79]; and infection with the soil microbe Bacillus subtilis in Arabidopsis [88]. High salinity induced choline biosynthesis via PEt methylation [89], PMT activity [84] and cloned spinach PMT [77]. In Arabidopsis, antisense suppression of PMT1 caused salt hypersensitivity [87], and overexpression of corn PMT (ZmPEAMT1) enhanced salt tolerance [80]. Characterization of Arabidopsis pmt mutants under other stress conditions is not reported.

# 2.6. Phospholipid N-methyltransferase (PLMT)

PLMT, also called phosphatidylethanolamine $N$ -methyltransferase (PEMT) in animal cells, methylates phosphatidyl-bases such as PtdEtn, monomethyl PtdEtn and dimethyl PtdEtn, which serve as the main route to synthesize PtdCho in mammalian cells and yeast [61]. As described in the previous section, this enzyme activity has been detected in some plant species such as soybean, carrot and olive [65-67]. However, unlike

in yeast or animal cells, plant PLMT does not seem to catalyze a major route for PtdCho biosynthesis because 1) PMT activity is more commonly found in various plant species, 2) the activity for the first methylation (PtdEtn methylation) has not been detected in any plant species examined, and 3) the triple mutant of all PMTs completely abolished de novo PtdCho biosynthesis activity in Arabidopsis [59,60]. From the homology with rat PLMT, a single copy cDNA was found in Arabidopsis and soybean that is distinct from PMT and capable of second and third but not first methylation of PtdEtn [90]. The mutant of Arabidopsis PLMT (atplmt) accumulated monomethyl and dimethyl PtdEtn but did not show altered contents of PtdCho and PtdEtn and did not have any morphological phenotype. This finding agrees with the result of the PMT triple knockout mutant, which shows that the PMT pathway is essential for PtdCho biosynthesis. Thus, at least in Arabidopsis, PLMT activity does not contribute mainly to PtdCho biosynthesis.

# 2.7. CTP:phosphorylcholine cytidylyltransferase (CCT)

CCT converts PCho to CDP-Cho. In animal cells, this enzyme has been studied extensively because it exerts strong flux control in PtdCho biosynthesis. In plants, CCT activity was also postulated to be a rate-limiting factor for PtdCho biosynthesis [91] according to initial biochemical characterization of this enzyme activity in the stem of onion [24] and pea [91,92] as well as rye roots [93]. Arabidopsis has 2 copies of CCT, CCT1 and CCT2 [95,96]. However, how they contribute to PtdCho biosynthesis remains unclear in vivo because the lipid phenotype of the double knockout mutant remains to be analyzed.

The first cloning of plant CCT was achieved from a root cDNA library of B. napus, in which 4 copies of CCT were cloned and complemented by yeast cct mutant [94]. This was followed by the cloning of CCT1 [95] and CCT2 [96] in Arabidopsis. Under low temperature, CCT activity was increased [97]. Unlike CCT1 activity, CCT2 activity was increased by low temperature at transcript and protein levels, which suggests that these 2 CCT isoforms may have a distinct role under low temperature [96,97]. Polar glycerolipid composition of cct1 and cct2 mutants showed no significant change from the wild type [97], so the double mutant [98] may need to be analyzed for lipid content to demonstrate the impact of CCTs on PtdCho biosynthesis. Aside from the gene knockout study, an involvement of CCT1 in PtdCho biosynthesis was demonstrated in an effort to address why net PtdCho biosynthesis is stimulated in $pah1$ $pah2$ [99], a double knockout of soluble PA phosphatase that accumulates PA [46,100]. The accumulation of PtdCho in $pah1$ $pah2$ was suppressed by disrupting CCT1, but overexpression of CCT1 had no significant effect on PtdCho biosynthesis [99]. The enzyme activity of recombinant CCT1 was stimulated by lipid vesicles containing PA; however, truncation of the predicted C-terminal amphipathic lipid binding domain made it constitutively active, which replicated the $pah1$ $pah2$ phenotype when overexpressed in planta. Thus, CCT1 contributes to PtdCho biosynthesis and is regulated by the abundance of PA [99]. Another mechanism to regulate CCT1 activity is phosphorylation at Ser-187 residue via sucrose nonfermenting 1-related protein kinase 1, which showed substantially reduced activity, by $70\%$ [101]. The regulatory mechanism for CCT2 is not well documented. A recent report showed that CCT2 is involved in pathogen recognition by nucleotide-binding, leucine-rich repeat protein and basal plant innate immunity, although its molecular details await further investigation [102].

# 2.8. CTP:phosphorylethanolamine cytidylyltransferase (PECT/ECT)

PECT/ECT converts PEt n to CDP-Etn. Whether CCT and PECT are distinct enzymes was argued because a search for PECT activity in plants was unsuccessful [103,104]. However, CCT activity from pea stems was unaffected by PEt n [105], which suggests that a separate PECT enzyme may be present in plants [106]. Arabidopsis PECT1 is involved in PtdEtn biosynthesis, localized at mitochondria, and crucial for embryogenesis [109]. Why it is mitochondria-localized and crucial for embryogenesis

awaits further investigation.

The first PECT activity was measured from the endosperm of castor bean [107]. The enzyme activity was confined to membranes, mainly from the outer membrane of mitochondria and the rest in the ER, which requires cations such as $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ , optimal pH at 6.5, and is sensitive to trypsin treatment, so the active sites are exposed to cytoplasm [107]. The enzyme activity was purified approximately 118-fold, and a prominent 35-kD protein was detected as a possible PECT in castor bean [108]. Arabidopsis has a single PECT, designated PECT1, whose cDNA was isolated, and the recombinant gene product showed PECT enzyme activity in vitro [109]. A null mutant pect1-6 showed developmental arrest at the octant stage of embryo development, which resulted in the embryonic lethal phenotype in homozygotes [17]. Another leaky and thus viable mutant allele, pect1-4, with PECT activity decreased by $74\%$ , showed only delayed embryo maturation and reduced fertility under ambient temperature but dwarfism at low temperature. The trans-heterozygotic mutant pect1-4/pect1-6 retained PECT activity, which was only $20\%$ of that of the wild type, showing a severely dwarfed growth phenotype under normal temperature. In both pect1-4 and pect1-4/pect1-6, the amount of PtdEtn was decreased and that of PtdCho was increased in rosette leaves. These results suggest that PECT1 is involved in PE biosynthesis in vivo and is crucial for plant growth and development. Also, a reciprocal increase in PtdCho content at the expense of PtdEtn suggests that PEt n may be a common precursor for the biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn. Similar changes in PtdCho and PtdEtn levels were obtained when PECT1 was partially suppressed by an artificial microRNA targeting PECT1 [110]. The triple knockout of PMTs, which methylates PEt n to produce PCho, featured abolished de novo PtdCho biosynthesis activity [59,60]. Regarding the mitochondrial localization of PECT activity, purified mitochondria from 5-week-old rosette leaves of pect1-4 grown under short-day conditions showed reduced PE level and cytochrome oxidase pathway capacity to $43\%$ of that of the wild type, which suggests the importance of PECT1 in maintaining mitochondrial respiration capacity [111]. The PECT1 transcript showed oscillatory profiles under long- and short-day conditions; however, whether this profile has any physiological relevance remains unknown [112].

# 2.9. Aminoalcohol aminophosphotransferases (AAPTs)

The last step of PtdCho and PtdEtn biosynthesis is the transfer of PCho or PEtn moieties to a diacylglycerol backbone. This reaction is catalyzed by AAPT. Unlike animals, bacteria and yeast, plants do not seem to have AAPTs specific to PtdEtn and PtdCho, although different AAPT isozymes show substrate preferences.

Earlier studies of plant AAPT activity stem from remarkable differences in PtdEtn biosynthesis pathways between animals and bacteria, which attracted research interest to investigate PtdEtn biosynthesis in higher plants. In microsomal fractions of spinach leaves [104,113], potato tubers [114], and developing soybean seeds [115] as well as the purified ER fraction of caster bean endosperm [116,117], AAPT activity had no distinguishable substrate specificity, with an $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ requirement for activity and was found exclusively in the ER fraction. This is contrary to the situation in animals and yeast in which separate AAPTs are involved in PtdCho and PtdEtn biosynthesis [118,119]. Following the first molecular cloning in soybean (AAPT1; [120]), AAPT was cloned in Chinese cabbage (AAPT1 and AAPT2) [121,122], Arabidopsis (AAPT1 and AAPT2) [123], B. napus (AAPT1) [124], Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (EPT) [125], and wheat (TaAAPT1 and TaAAPT2) [126], with their sequences highly similar to mammalian and yeast AAPTs. The in vivo function of AAPTs was evidenced by overexpression of B. napus AAPT1 in Arabidopsis [124] and suppression of AAPT1 and AAPT2 in Arabidopsis [127]. The overexpression of BnAAPT1 increased the content of PtdCho by $25\%$ of that of the wild type but with no effect on PtdEtn [124], which suggests that BnAAPT1 is either specific to PtdCho biosynthesis in vivo or the host Arabidopsis plants have a mechanism to

maintain the contents of PtdEtn but not PtdCho after the overexpression. In the gene knockout study of Arabidopsis AAPTs, a double homozygous mutant was not isolated, possibly because of embryonic lethality, but neither of the single homozygous mutants showed any observable mutant phenotypes [127]. RNAi-based suppression of AAPTs affected plant growth and decreased PtdEtn but not PtdCho content, which suggests that PtdCho could be compensated by an additional AAPT-independent pathway in Arabidopsis [127]. Apart from the involvement of AAPTs in normal plant growth and development, study of AAPT function focused on the context of plant cold acclimation because PtdCho biosynthesis is higher under freezing than nonfreezing temperatures $(5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ vs $20^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ [93]. Indeed, Chinese cabbage AAPT2, BnAAPT1 and TaAAPT1 were all transcriptionally induced by cold temperature [122,124,126], and transgenic Arabidopsis overexpressing BnAAPT1 enhanced tolerance to growth under low temperature [124]. Whether RNAi suppression lines of Arabidopsis AAPTs show susceptible growth phenotype under low temperature awaits future study.

# 3. Future perspectives

The recent years have shown significant advances in understanding plant PtdCho and PtdEtn biosynthesis owing to the identification and functional characterization of most enzymes in Arabidopsis. Table 1 summarizes observations reported for mutants and transgenic plant lines of each enzyme. Gene knockout studies for each enzyme confirmed the major route of PtdCho and PtdEtn, which was mostly as proposed by earlier biochemical analysis with many plant species. For example, ample evidence suggested PMT as the sole route for PtdCho biosynthesis in seed plants, which was indeed the case after the Arabidopsis $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ triple mutant was found devoid of PtdCho biosynthesis activity [59,60]. With the outcomes of gene knockout studies, one could admire the power of biochemical study before the postgenomic era that quite accurately established the metabolic pathway. A large advantage of having knockout models is clearly to elucidate the role of respective enzymes in plant function in vivo. The $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ mutant showed seedling lethality by 2 weeks after germination, which suggests that PtdCho biosynthesis is not essential for embryonic growth but is crucial for post-embryonic growth [59,60]. This finding is contrary to $pect1$ mutants, which are devoid of PtdEtn biosynthesis, causing the embryonic lethal phenotype [17]. The 2 reaction steps from serine to PEtN (serine to Etn by SDC1, and Etn to PEtN by CEK4) are shared between PtdCho and PtdEtn biosynthesis pathways, and knockout of both SDC1 and CEK4 causes the embryonic lethal phenotype [13,36]. Because this morphological phenotype resembles that of $pect1$ than $pmt1$ $pmt2$ $pmt3$ , a likely cause of the common embryonic lethal phenotype is lack of PtdEtn biosynthesis. Why PtdEtn but not PtdCho is required for embryonic growth and PtdCho is required for post-embryonic growth remains an open question. Identification of primary enzymes for each reaction step also allows for studying the subcellular localization of the pathway. PtdCho and PtdEtn are widely considered to be synthesized in the ER. However, all the reaction steps from the beginning do not necessarily have to be at the ER. Of note, SDC1 and PECT1 are localized at mitochondria, whereas CEK4 is at the plasma membrane and ER but not in mitochondria [17,36,74]. The precise subcellular localization of CCT1, CCT2, AAPT1 and AAPT2 has not been reported. Besides, how their isoforms differentially contribute to PtdCho and PtdEtn biosynthesis requires more characterization of the double mutant, especially for CCT1 and CCT2. Multiple organelles may be involved in the total biosynthesis of PtdCho and PtdEtn. Finally, the diversity of the PtdCho biosynthesis pathway among eukaryotes is a general open question. For example, animal and yeast use the PtdEtn methylation pathway, whereas plants, nematodes and malarial parasites use PEtn methylation. More specifically, within the plant kingdom, interspecies difference with regard to the primary methylation route raises questions as to why they use different routes after the first methylation at PEtn (see Section 2.5). The evolutionary context of the PtdCho biosynthesis pathway may be an

interesting topic of future research.
