ABSTRACT: Nicotianamine synthases regulate the biosynthesis of two mineral chelators key for rice grain biofortification, nicotianamine (NA) and $2 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxymugineic acid (DMA). We produced transgenic rice expressing OsNAS2 under the OsRCc3 promoter $\left( R c N 2 \right)$ and mutated OsLpa1 in these (lpa1 RcN2) and the wild-type (lpa1) to enhance essential mineral accumulation in grains while lowering phytic acid (PA) levels. NA and DMA contents were higher in the brown grains of RcN2, lpa1 RcN2, and lpa1 plants. The grains of these lines accumulated increased levels of essential minerals, with lpa1 RcN2 exhibiting the greatest increases. PA-bound iron and zinc levels were lower in lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 grains, while NA- and DMA-bound iron and zinc levels were higher in RcN2, lpa1 RcN2, and lpa1 grains. Field-grown RcN2 plants showed no significant growth penalties, unlike lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2. Furthermore, lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 grains were chalky, a property that facilitates rice flour production. 

KEYWORDS: biofortification, OsNAS2, NA, DMA, iron, zinc, low phytic acid, chalky grain 

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Rice (Oryza sativa) grains are the primary source of dietary calories and essential micronutrients for over $5 0 \%$ of the human population. This section of humanity mainly includes people from low- and middle-income countries with limited access to other nutrient-rich foods.1 Although they consume sufficient calories, they suffer from varying levels of malnutrition due to inadequate intake of essential micronutrients, causing ŌĆ£hidden hungerŌĆØ.2 This phenomenon is more severe among those who prefer white or polished rice over brown rice, as polishing removes the micronutrient-rich embryo and aleurone layer of the grain.3,4 In fact, polished rice grains can lose up to $9 7 \%$ of their iron (Fe) and $45 \%$ of their zinc $\mathrm { { ( Z n ) } }$ contents compared to brown rice grains.5,6 An average of 372 million children aged six months to five years and 1.2 billion nonpregnant adult women are reported to be malnourished due to a deficiency in at least one of the micronutrients Fe, Zn, or vitamin $\mathbf { A } ,$ with about $5 0 \%$ of affected individuals residing in Asia.7 Insufficient intake of micronutrients, including vitamins and minerals, can have severe health consequences, such as birth defects, compromised immunity, poor growth and development, intellectual disabilities, and even death.7,8 Pregnant women and children are the most vulnerable groups, as they require higher mineral and vitamin intake for normal growth and physiological functions.9,10 

Addressing this global health problem through dietary diversification remains a challenge in developing countries, where various development programs such as vitamin and 

mineral supplementation campaigns often face shortcomings due to a lack of funds and inadequate social infrastructure. Thus, direct improvement of the nutritional quality of staple food crops through biofortification provides a necessary and effective long-term solution.2,11,12 Raising the micronutrient content of staple food crops is known as biofortification, which can be achieved through agronomic or genetic approaches.13ŌłÆ15 Genetic biofortification, using conventional breeding or various biotechnological methods, has shown promising results in sustainably enhancing micronutrient 11,16,17 Conventional breeding is limited by the gene pool of the target crop species, whereas biotechnological approaches can incorporate a wider range of genetic resources, including those from other species, to enhance crop micronutrient content more efficiently and in a more targeted manner. Therefore, these approaches are more promising for rice biofortification.17ŌłÆ19 Many micronutrient contentŌłÆrelated genes have been deployed for this purpose. For example, the rice NICOTIANAMINE SYNTHASE (NAS) genes OsNAS1, OsNAS2, and OsNAS3 (hereafter NAS1, NAS2, and NAS3)

NAS enzymes biosynthesize nicotianamine (NA), a metal chelator essential for mineral translocation, distribution, and metal homeostasis in plants. NA can chelate Fe and Zn as well as other minerals such as copper $\left( \mathrm { C u } \right)$ and manganese (Mn).29ŌłÆ31 NA is a precursor for the biosynthesis of the phytosiderophore $2 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxymugineic acid (DMA), which is catalyzed by nicotianamine aminotransferase (NAAT) and DMA synthase (DMAS).32 Like NA, DMA can chelate metals such as Fe and Zn, and both NA and DMA are secreted by plant roots.29,32,33 Notably, when greater amounts of NA and DMA are secreted into the rhizosphere, the chelators can bind to more metals and ultimately enhance metal translocation to rice grains.2,34 For instance, activation-tagged mutants of NAS2 and NAS3 with higher expression of either gene accumulate more Fe, Zn, and other essential minerals in their grains, and these minerals have higher bioavailability, along with much higher NA and DMA levels, compared to wild-type (WT) grains.21ŌłÆ23 Simultaneous overexpression of NAS2 and NAS3 resulted in the greatest improvements in Fe, Zn, NA, and DMA contents in brown grains compared to the WT and the single activation-tagged lines.24 Overexpression of each of the three NAS genes individually under the control of the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter has also been reported to significantly raise Fe and Zn levels in grains.20,27 Importantly, these studies emphasize that NAS2 is more effective than NAS1 or NAS3 for Fe and Zn biofortification of rice grains.20,27 Other studies have also utilized NAS2 in combination with other metal homeostasisŌłÆ related genes, with NAS2 expression driven by constitutive or tissue-specific promoters significantly enhancing mineral levels in rice grains. For instance, transgenic indica rice plants engineered with NAS2 driven by the 35S promoter and the soybean (Glycine max) Ferritin gene SferH-1 driven by an endosperm-specific promoter accumulated more Fe and Zn in polished grains with little to no yield loss during field trials.28 Moreover, transgenic indica rice plants with phloem-specific expression of NAS2 combined with endosperm-specific expression of a Ferritin gene from Pisum sativum achieved significantly enhanced Fe and Zn contents in polished grains without any significant yield loss, although these micronutrients reached higher levels when the phloem-specific promoter was replaced by a constitutive promoter.25 However, NAS2 has not yet been singly the subject of tissue-specific expression to efficiently biofortify rice without noticeable growth penalties. 

The OsRCc3 promoter is a tissue-specific promoter that primarily controls gene expression in roots.35 Transgenic plants overexpressing OsNAC10 under the control of the OsRCc3 promoter showed greater tolerance for drought, salinity, and cold stresses at the vegetative stage. Moreover, these plants exhibited significantly higher grain yield under drought conditions compared to control plants and those constitutively overexpressing OsNAC10 under the control of GOS2 promoter.36 Although the OsRCc3 promoter mainly drives gene expression in roots, its transcriptional activity was also observed in the phloem cells of nodes and internodes.37 Transgenic plants expressing the vacuolar arsenic (As) sequestration genes Yeast Cadmium Factor 1 (ScYCF1) from fission yeast (Schizosaccharomyces pombe) or the rice C-type ATP-binding cassette transporter gene OsABCC1 under the control of the OsRCc3 promoter accumulated significantly 

lower As levels in their grains without any growth penalty.37 The tissue-specific OsRCc3 promoter is therefore a promising tool for engineering transgenic rice plants with enhanced nutrient accumulation for grain biofortification without compromising overall plant growth, while also limiting the accumulation of toxic elements in grains. 

Beyond being consumed as cooked brown or white rice grains, rice is ground to make flour that is well recognized for its versatility in culinary applications. As a gluten-free and hypoallergenic substitute for wheat flour, it has become increasingly popular among individuals with dietary restrictions or sensitivities.38,39 Rice flourŌłÆbased foods are also gaining popularity among the general population due to changing dietary preferences, mainly in developed countries.40 Outside of Asia, where rice remains a dietary cornerstone, rice flourŌłÆ based products have seen robust uptake in Europe and North America, especially in the form of gluten-free breads, pastas, and snacks. This growth is driven by health-conscious consumers and those with gluten sensitivities, as well as ongoing processing innovations that improve the texture and shelf life of rice flour.41,42 Biofortified rice grains enriched with essential minerals will benefit populations who consume rice flourŌłÆbased foods. However, the hardness of rice grains presents a significant obstacle in producing high-quality flour with desirable characteristics, such as fine particle size and minimal starch damage.43 By contrast, the endosperm of chalky grains consists of loosely packed starch granules, lowering grain hardness and making them less susceptible to starch damage during milling, thereby facilitating the production of finer flour.44 Rice flour with lower starch damage $( 4 \mathrm { - } 6 \% )$ and finer particle size $\left( 7 5 - 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } \right)$ have been identified as key factors in producing bread with desirable characteristics to enhance consumer acceptance.45,46 The use of additives such as, hydrocolloids and other gelling agents further enhances dough rheology, bread texture and overall consumer acceptability of gluten-free breads made with rice flour.42 

Chalky grains offer a cost-effective means of producing highquality rice flour via simple dry milling and do not require the higher capital investment and operational complexity needed for wet milling, jet milling, or cryogenic milling techniques, which are associated with high energy use, wastewater disposal challenges, and additional drying or sterilization steps.47 Incorporating the chalkiness trait into biofortified grains can thus be a useful approach to producing rice flour with enhanced mineral content. Notably, a rice low phytic acid (lpa) mutant was reported to produce chalky grains.48 Phytic acid (PA) is an antinutrient that acts as a strong chelator of metal ions, limiting the bioavailability of essential minerals and remaining a significant challenge to effective biofortification.49,50 Therefore, lower PA levels would ensure greater bioavailability of essential minerals in rice grains. For instance, a point mutation in Low phytic acid 1 (OsLpa1, hereafter Lpa1; LOC_Os02g57400), also known as 2-PHOSPHOGLYCER-ATE KINASE (2-PGK) resulted in a significantly lower PA content in the grains of the mutant, together with greater chalkiness compared to WT grains.48 Other mutant alleles of this gene, namely, KBNT lpa 1ŌłÆ1, DR1331ŌłÆ2, and Os-lpa-XQZ-1, also result in lower amounts of PA in their grains.51ŌłÆ53 

In addition, the mutants KBNT lpa 1ŌłÆ1 and DR1331ŌłÆ2 in the japonica background showed PA levels lower by $2 8 \%$ and $4 8 \%$ relative to their WT, respectively, whereas Os-lpa-XQZ-1, in an indica background, exhibited about a $3 5 \%$ drop in PA content 

compared to WT grains, along with higher levels of essential minerals such as Fe and Zn.51,53 

In this study, we implemented a biotechnological approach to produce plants expressing NAS2 in a tissue-specific manner in RcN2, plants lacking Lpa1 function in lpa1, and plants combining RcN2 and lpa1 in lpa1 RcN2. We used the rice tissue-specific promoter $O s R C c 3 ^ { 3 5 , 3 7 }$ to produce RcN2 plants, and CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing to generate lpa1 plants. Our aim in mutating Lpa1 was to promote grain chalkiness and diminish PA content to facilitate the production of biofortified rice flour with higher mineral content. We grew all plants under field conditions and subjected them to phenotypic inspection for any growth defects while assessing the mineral contents of their polished and brown grains. 

# 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1. Plant Materials and Growth Conditions. The rice cultivar Dongjin (Oryza sativa L. ssp. japonica) was used as the WT in this study. RcN2, lpa1, and lpa1 RcN2 plants were generated and used in this study. Plants were grown in a growth chamber at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } C / 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ (day/night) under a 14-h light/10-h dark photoperiod with $7 0 { - } 8 0 \%$ relative humidity. During the summer season, plants were cultivated in a designated paddy field for genetically modified rice at Kyung Hee University, Republic of Korea, under natural conditions. Field-grown plants were used for phenotypic analysis and for measuring the contents of grain minerals, PA, NA, and DMA. 

2.2. Generation of RcN2 Plants. The full-length coding sequence of NAS2 was cloned into the pENTR/D-TOPO entry vector, and the correct clone was confirmed by colony PCR and Sanger sequencing. The resulting entry clone was then recombined with a destination vector (pCAMBIA3301 as the vector backbone) containing the OsRCc3 promoter via LR recombination using the Gateway cloning system. The resulting construct was confirmed by colony PCR and Sanger sequencing and introduced into Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens) strain LBA4404. The BAR gene was used as the selection marker in plants. Transgenic rice plants were generated by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of seed-derived calli.54 A total of 12 independent transgenic plants were generated and subjected to expression analysis. Two transgenic plants exhibiting the highest NAS2 expression levels were chosen for further study, and progeny from the $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation onward were used in all subsequent experiments. 

2.3. Generation of lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 Plants. To generate lpa1 plants using CRISPR/Cas9, a 20-nucleotide target sequence was selected using the CRISPRdirect web tool (https://crispr.dbcls.jp/). The selected target sequence was cloned into the pOs-sgRNA entry vector and subsequently recombined into the pH-Ubi-cas9ŌłÆ7 destination vector using the Gateway system.55,56 The final construct was confirmed by colony PCR and Sanger sequencing, and introduced into Agrobacterium LBA4404. The hygromycin resistance gene was used as the selection marker in plants. lpa1 RcN2 plants were generated by Agrobacterium-mediated cotransformation of seed-derived calli with a mixture of LBA4404 strains each harboring the RcN2 and lpa1 constructs.54 A total of nine independent lpa1 plants and 14 independent lpa1 RcN2 plants were generated. Two lpa1 plants exhibiting homozygous mutations, and two lpa1 RcN2 plants exhibiting the highest NAS2 expression levels and homozygous mutations were selected for further study. Progeny from the $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ generation onward were used in all subsequent experiments. 

2.4. RNA Isolation and RT-qPCR Analysis. Total RNA was isolated from the roots of two-week-old WT, transgenic, and NAS2 activation-tagged mutant (2D) seedlings grown in Yoshida solution using a FastPure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme Biotech, Nanjing, China). Yoshida solution was prepared as described in Lee et al.,24 adjusted to pH 5.7, and replaced every 2 days. First-strand cDNA was synthesized from 1 ╬╝g of total RNA using ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Master Mix (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan). qPCR was performed using Prime Q-Master Mix 2X (GeNetBio, Daejeon, South Korea) on a Rotor-Gene Q real-time PCR cycler (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). The rice UBIQUITIN5 gene (OsUBQ5; LOC_Os01g22490) was used as an internal control.5 The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ method. Primer sequences used for RT-qPCR are listed in Supplementary Table S1. 

2.5. Measurement of PA Content in Brown Grains. PA content was measured using a K-PHYT assay kit (Megazyme Inc., Bray, Ireland), following the manufacturerŌĆÖs instructions. Briefly, mature brown grains were finely ground and extracted in 0.66 M HCl overnight at room temperature. One milliliter of each extract was centrifuged at $1 3 , 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the supernatant was used in the colorimetric assay.

# 3. RESULTS

3.1. Generation of RcN2 Plants and Incorporation of lpa1. NAS2 catalyzes the biosynthesis of NA, which facilitates the production of DMA; NA and DMA are both secreted by rice roots.29,32,33 In this study, we used the OsRCc3 promoter35,37 to overexpress NAS2 in rice roots to generate RcN2, thereby enhancing the mineral content of grains (Figure 1a). To generate chalky rice grains with low PA content, we also used the CRISPR/Cas9 tool to mutate Lpa1, producing lpa1 mutant plants with low PA content. We also generated lpa1 plants carrying the RcN2 transgene via cotransformation, with the aim of enhancing grain mineral content and introduce chalkiness to facilitate flour production (Figure 1b). 

We checked NAS2 expression levels in the roots of WT, RcN2, lpa1, and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings (Figure 1c). We observed significantly higher NAS2 transcript levels in the roots of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings compared to those of WT, while NAS2 was expressed at similar levels in WT and the two Lpa1 

knockout lines, lpa1ŌłÆ1, and lpa1ŌłÆ2. NAS2 expression was highest in the roots of RcN2ŌłÆ2 seedlings, reaching levels about 11-fold higher than in WT. NAS2 expression levels were about 5.7-fold, 7-fold, and 5.8-fold higher in the roots of $R c N 2 - 1$ , lpa1 RcN2ŌłÆ1, and lpa1 RcN2ŌłÆ2 seedlings relative to the WT, respectively. Additionally, when we examined NAS2 expression in the roots of 2D, its expression level was about 4-fold higher than in WT (Figure S1).

# 4. DISCUSSION

4.1. A Combined Strategy Using Tissue-Specific NAS2 Expression and Lower PA Levels Enables Efficient Mineral Biofortification in Rice Grain. Biofortification is a promising strategy to combat malnutrition resulting from insufficient intake of essential micronutrients and has demonstrated success in improving health outcomes across various populations.72 In this study, we expressed NAS2 in roots with the tissue-specific OsRCc3 promoter to efficiently biofortify rice grains. To further facilitate biofortification by lowering the PA content and introducing chalkiness to rice grains to assist with rice flour production, we knocked out Lpa1 into the RcN2 background. Tissue-specific NAS2 expression resulted in higher relative NAS2 transcript levels in the roots of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings compared to those of WT. Notably, NAS2 was expressed at higher levels in the roots of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings than those of the 2D. PA content was significantly lower in mature brown grains of lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 plants, with drops ranging from $3 1 \%$ to $4 1 \%$ compared to WT grains, whereas RcN2 and WT grains did not differ in their PA contents. Previous studies have reported similar declines in the PA contents of lpa1 mutants in japonica and indica backgrounds, ranging from $2 8 \%$ to $4 9 \%$ .48,51,53 When we measured the NA and DMA contents of grains from WT and other lines, we established that NA and DMA accumulated to higher levels in the mature brown grains of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 plants relative to WT grains. These results are in agreement with previous studies utilizing NAS2 overexpression that have reported higher levels of NA and DMA in mature grains of the transgenic plants compared to 21ŌłÆ25,27,28 

NAS2 synthesizes NA, which is the precursor to DMA. NAS2 expression was driven by the OsRCc3 promoter, which is primarily active in root cortical cells and the phloem companion cells of nodes.35,37 NA and DMA produced in 

root cortical cells bind Fe and Zn taken up from the soil and contribute to xylem-mediated transport to shoots via transporters, while NA in phloem companion cells in nodes facilitates the translocation of metals chelated to NA and DMA to grains. The heterologous expression of HvNAS1 from barley (Hordeum vulgare) in rice was shown to increase NA and DMA contents in the roots and shoots, supporting the idea that NAS2 overexpression would lead to elevated NA and DMA pools.73 As we only quantified NA and DMA contents in brown grains, and not in the roots or phloem tissue, the possible contribution of root-derived or phloem-derived NA and DMA to grain accumulation remains speculative and will require targeted follow-up studies, such as root and phloem profiling of NA and DMA levels or isotope tracing. We observed greater accumulation of NA and DMA in the grains of lpa1 mutants compared to WT; similarly, grains of lpa1 RcN2 lines also showed a slight increase in their NA and DMA contents compared to RcN2 grains. Since lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 grains contained lower amounts of PA, NA and DMA would face less competition from PA to form complexes with metal ions, thereby contributing to a higher accumulation of NA and DMA complexed to metal ions. 

PA and DMA both form strong Fe(III) complexes, and potentiometric data indicate that certain phytate species can exhibit conditional stability constants exceeding those of DMA under specific pH and ionic strength conditions.74,75 In this study, we detected Fe(III) associated with both PA and DMA. A similar association was reported in maize (Zea mays), suggesting that Fe speciation in grains is determined by thermodynamic stability, kinetic factors, ligand accessibility (including steric and solubility constraints for PA), and subcellular compartmentalization.76 While PA likely serves as a storage form of Fe, DMA may represent a more dynamic and readily mobilizable Fe reservoir, even in the presence of PA.76 In line with this idea, lpa1 grains had greater amounts of various minerals than WT, a phenotype that was even more pronounced in lpa1 RcN2 grains. Lower PA levels are therefore associated with higher mineral contents in crop grains, especially raising the bioavailability of minerals for humans and other monogastric animals.77,78 Additionally, the modest rise in NA and DMA levels along with those of Fe and Zn observed in lpa1 grains likely reflects direct or compensatory effects due to improved metal mobility, induced metal transporters, changes in chelator metabolism, and altered seed loading dynamics. Similarly, the grains of KBNT lpa1ŌłÆ1 plants were reported to contain higher Zn levels compared to WT, whereas the contents of Fe, Mg, and K were almost identical between the two genotypes.79 Another study using an lpa1 mutant, Os-lpa-XQZ-1, reported significantly higher amounts of Fe, Zn, and Ca compared to WT grains.53 Importantly, polished RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 grains contained significantly more Fe and Zn than polished WT grains, reaching the established targets of for efficiently biofortified white r $1 5 \mu \mathrm { g / g }$ Fe and polish $2 8 \ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g } \mathrm { Z n }$ RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2, Fe and Zn levels were comparable to those reported for other NAS2-overexpressing lines. Constitutive overexpression of NAS2 under the 35S promoter yielded 4- and 2-fold enhancements of Fe and Zn levels in polished grains compared to those of WT.20 Likewise, 2D polished grains displayed 3- and 2.7-fold higher contents of Fe and Zn.22,23 I n an indica rice cultivar coexpressing 35S-driven NAS2 and endosperm-specific promoter-driven GmFer-H1, polished grains showed 7.5- and 3.8-fold higher levels in Fe 

and Zn, respectively, relative to the WT.28 While the abovementioned studies focused mainly on Fe and Zn, we observed significantly greater accumulation of other essential microminerals as well, such as Mn and Cu. We hypothesize that the higher amounts of Mn and Cu in the polished grains of the transgenic lines are due to the enhanced NA and DMA production of these plants. NA and DMA can form complexes with Mn and Cu, in addition to Fe and $\mathrm { Z n } , ^ { 8 1 - 8 3 }$ and the higher NA and DMA contents of the transgenic lines should help take up more NAŌłÆmetal and DMAŌłÆmetal complexes from the soil and relocate them to the grains. Notably, the amounts of these trace metals were found significantly higher after polishing the brown grains of transgenic lines, particularly Fe and Mn amounts. This finding suggests that the translocation of NAŌłÆ metal and DMAŌłÆmetal complexes predominantly occur into the grain endosperm. 

Additionally, the levels of NA and DMA bound to Fe and Zn were higher in the brown grains of transgenic lines relative to those of WT, as measured by SECŌłÆICPŌłÆMS analysis, with the highest levels observed in lpa1 RcN2 grains. By contrast, PAbound Fe and Zn accumulated to lower levels in lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 grains. Higher levels of NA-bound and DMA-bound Fe and Zn, together with the lower levels of their PA-bound forms, would be likely to result in improved bioavailability of Fe and Zn. It has been reported that the grains of NAS2 overexpressing plants contain higher Fe and Zn levels in the endosperm with greater bioavailability.22,23 Bioavailability of the enhanced Fe and Zn in the 2D grains was confirmed by two independent mouse-feeding studies.22,23 An in vitro Caco-2 cell assay was also employed which further corroborated the increased Fe bioavailability from the grains of NAS2 overexpressing plants.28 PAŌłÆFe(III) complexes exhibit poor bioavailability due to their high stability, low solubility at intestinal pH, and the absence of endogenous phytase activity in the human gastrointestinal tract. Consequently, Fe remains tightly bound and is largely inaccessible for absorption.84 By contrast, Fe(II/III) complexes with NA and DMA are watersoluble and form moderately stable complexes that do not precipitate under physiological conditions. Recently, Murata et al. demonstrated that NAŌłÆFe complexes can be absorbed directly in the human small intestine via the proton-coupled amino acid transporter PAT1.85 Together, these findings suggest that the higher bioavailability of NAŌłÆFe and DMAŌłÆFe arises from favorable solubility and lability and from active transport mechanisms that are absent for PAŌłÆFe. Furthermore, the lower levels of PAŌłÆFe observed in RcN2 grains may reflect the enhanced NA and DMA contents of these grains, and consequently, their higher competition for binding to Fe with PA. 

Moreover, we measured higher $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g }$ and K contents in the brown and polished grains of lpa1, RcN2, and lpa1 RcN2 plants compared to WT. Macro-minerals such as Mg and K are present in higher amounts than micro-minerals and are essential in animal diets due to their roles in enzyme activation, muscle contraction, nerve function, and fluid balance.86,87 NA binds weakly to Mg in vitro. 88 However, there is no evidence that biologically relevant $\mathrm { N A - M g }$ or DMAŌłÆMg complexes form in planta, nor that NA or DMA chelate K. Thus, future studies should evaluate whether such complexes form at physiological concentrations and whether NA or DMA influence $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g }$ and K homeostasis indirectly. Enhanced NA and DMA contents may improve the efficiency of metal transporters, thereby affecting $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g }$ and K uptake to 

maintain ionic balance.89,90 We did measure higher expression levels for several metal homeostasisŌłÆrelated genes in the roots of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings. Overexpression of NAS2 enhances DMA production via the action of OsNAAT1 and OsDMAS1,32 while OsTOM1 facilitates the secretion of DMA into the soil.64 OsIRT1 and OsYSL2 are key genes regulating Fe levels in rice plants.91 Specifically, OsIRT1 facilitates Fe uptake from the soil, while OsYSL2 aids in the translocation of NAŌłÆ Fe and NAŌłÆMn complexes.65,66 OsCOPT1 is involved in Cu uptake and distribution,67 while OsZIP4 and OsZIP5 are important for Zn uptake and distribution.68,69 These genes were expressed at higher levels in the roots of RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings, which may contribute to greater and more efficient transport of Fe, Zn, Mn, and Cu to the grains later during plant development. Additionally, the expression levels of OsMGT1 and OsHAK5, involved in $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g }$ and K uptake,7 0,71 were also higher in RcN2, lpa1, and lpa1 RcN2 seedlings, which may reflect physiological adjustments to maintain ionic balance in response to altered metal homeostasis. 

4.2. RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 Lines Provide Practical Applications for Nutritional Rice Improvement and Flour Use. Transgenic rice plants overexpressing NAS2 have been reported to be compromised in their agronomic traits, such as lower yield and altered growth patterns. While overexpression enhances the accumulation of essential minerals in grains, it may also lead to imbalances in nutrient homeostasis and metabolic processes, thus adversely affecting development and productivity.20,24 Growth penalties may also result from Fe-mediated oxidative stress.2 3 Importantly, fieldgrown RcN2 plants did not show any noticeable growth penalty compared to the WT. Culm length, tiller number, panicle length, grain number, grain weight, and fertility were similar between RcN2 and WT. The OsRCc3 promoter-driven expression of NAS2 may therefore promote optimal mineral distribution and limit or prevent mineral imbalance. In a japonica background, constitutive overexpression of NAS2 negatively influenced agronomic traits, whereas transgenic plants in an indica background exhibited minimal or no significant yield loss.24,28 We produced the RcN2 plants used in this study in the Dongjin japonica background and did not observe any significant negative effects on their agronomic traits. The OsRCc3 promoter is active in nodes and internodes in addition to roots.37 In rice, nodes play a central role in directing minerals taken up by roots into the phloem for delivery to developing grains.92,93 Thus, enhanced NA levels in nodes may facilitate efficient loading of NAŌłÆmetal and DMAŌłÆ metal complexes into the phloem. Furthermore, transporter genes expressed in nodes can help prevent the accumulation of toxic metals.93 Notably, RcN2, lpa1, and lpa1 RcN2 grains did not accumulate more As than WT grains. A previous study using OsRCc3 promoter-driven ScYCF1 and OsABCC1 showed lower As accumulation without growth penalty in rice.37 

Rice lpa1 mutants have lower grain PA content but often exhibit poor agronomic traits, resulting in yield losses.44,49,94,95 

The lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 plants showed significant growth penalty compared to WT and RcN2 plants. PA serves as a major storage form of P, and lower PA levels lead to greater levels of free inorganic phosphate, which can affect germination, seedling vigor, and development.53,96 Despite their yield loss, these lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 plants offer nutritional benefits by enhancing mineral bioavailability and contributing to lower P-related water pollution.77 Other lpa1 mutant alleles exhibit clear agronomic penalties. Kishor et al. 

showed that plants with mutation of Lpa1 are shorter, with fewer tillers, and lower grain yield.48 The KBNT lpa1ŌłÆ1 and DR1331ŌłÆ2 display a $5 \mathrm { - } 1 0 \%$ drop in grain dry weight.51 Likewise, mutants in INOSITOL 1,3,4-TRISPHOSPHATE 5/6- KINASE 6 (OsITPK6) or MULTIDRUG RESISTANCE PROTEIN 5 (OsMRP5) accumulate substantially lower PA levels but suffer from lighter grains, delayed seedling vigor, and lower yields.97,98 By contrast, several lpa lines have been engineered without detectable growth or yield defects. Seedspecific RNA interference (RNAi)-mediated knockdown of OsIPK1 or MYO-INOSITOL-1-PHOSPHATE SYNTHASE (OsMIPS), and the Tos17 insertion mutants in SULTR-LIKE P DISTRIBUTION TRANSPORTER (SPDT) maintain normal agronomic performance.99ŌłÆ101 Combining RcN2 with these lpa mutants may be a promising strategy to further enhance nutritional benefits while minimizing yield penalties. Importantly, only lpa1 mutants are characterized by a chalky endosperm phenotype, a potentially advantageous feature for rice flour processing not observed in other lpa backgrounds. We observed $4 4 - 5 8 \%$ chalky area in lpa1 and lpa1 RcN2 grains, in contrast to less than $4 \%$ in WT. An SEM analysis confirmed that these grains contained loosely packed spherical starch granules. Although chalky rice grains are generally considered less desirable for consumption, they offer advantages for rice flour production. The softer grain texture makes grinding easier and limits starch damage, improvingflour quality.38,102 

In conclusion, RcN2 and lpa1 RcN2 plants showed efficient biofortification of essential macro- and micro-minerals in their polished and brown grains. Importantly, field-grown RcN2 plants showed no significant agronomic penalty compared to WT and represent a promising genetic resource for breeding biofortified rice grains. Enhanced chalkiness facilitates energyefficient milling and produces a finer flour with lower starch damage, ideally suited for gluten-free food applications, while significantly lower PA levels markedly improve mineral bioavailability. Although grain yield is lower in lpa1 RcN2, the observed chalkiness and enhanced grain mineral accumulation suggest a potential to offset yield losses through improved processing and nutrient delivery. However, quantitative milling and economic assessments are required to confirm this. Biosafety and regulatory assessments, including the characterization of transformation events, transgene copy number, insertion site, off-target analyses, and full compositional testing, should be completed prior to any confined trials or release, in accordance with country-specific regulatory requirements. Translation activities, including introgression of the traits into elite cultivars, multienvironment performance trials, quantitative milling and flour assessments, and small sensory studies, will support the translation of this approach into functional biofortified rice flourŌłÆbased products.
