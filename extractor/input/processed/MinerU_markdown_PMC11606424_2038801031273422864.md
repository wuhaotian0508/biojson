# Summary

The manipulation of multiple transcription units for simultaneous and coordinated expression is not only key to building complex genetic circuits to accomplish diverse functions in synthetic biology, but is also important in crop breeding for significantly improved productivity and overall performance. However, building constructs with multiple independent transcription units for fine-tuned and coordinated regulation is complicated and time-consuming. Here, we introduce the Multiplex Expression Cassette Assembly (MECA) method, which modifies canonical vectors compatible with Golden Gate Assembly, and then uses them to produce multi-cassette constructs. By embedding the junction syntax in primers that are used to amplify functional elements, MECA is able to make complex constructs using only one intermediate vector and one destination vector via two rounds of one-pot Golden Gate assembly reactions, without the need for dedicated vectors and a coherent library of standardized modules. As a proof-of-concept, we modified eukaryotic and prokaryotic expression vectors to generate constructs for transient expression of green fluorescent protein and $\beta$ -glucuronidase in Nicotiana benthamiana, genome editing to block monoterpene metabolism in tomato glandular trichomes, production of betanin in tobacco and synthesis of $\beta$ -carotene in Escherichia coli. Additionally, we engineered the stable production of thymol and carvacrol, bioactive compounds from Lamiaceae family plants, in glandular trichomes of tobacco. These results demonstrate that MECA is a flexible, efficient and versatile method for building complex genetic circuits, which will not only play a critical role in plant synthetic biology, but also facilitate improving agronomic traits and pyramiding traits for the development of next-generation elite crops. 

# Introduction

Plant genetic engineering has played a pivotal role in crop improvement by modifying or introducing useful traits, such as increased resistance to insects and diseases, and tolerance to herbicide and stress. In the past years, the commercial cultivation of transgenic crops has contributed greatly to increasing crop yields and reducing the use of pesticide and insecticide, the emission of greenhouse gas and the cost of crop production (Kumar et al., 2020). However, the improvement of most crop traits is achieved through genetic modification of single or a few genes (Zhao et al., 2024). To improve the productivity and overall performance of crops, it is necessary to manipulate multiple genes simultaneously and coordinatively. 

Two approaches have been used for gene stacking and trait pyramiding. One is to stack transgenes in different T-DNA inserts using methods such as genetic crosses of transgenic plants carrying different transgenes, re-transformation of transgenic plants with a new construct or co-transformation of a set of separate constructs for different transgenes (Zhao et al., 2024). This has been successfully used to produce transgenic crops such as golden rice, insect-resistant maize and yellow wilt disease-resistant cotton (Dafny-Yelin and Tzfira, 2007; Gassmann et al., 2014; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Nandy et al., 2015; 

Paine et al., 2005). However, these methods are not only laborious and time-consuming for genetic transformation and progeny screening, but also face the problem of genetic segregation of transgenes, due to their variable location in the genome. The other approach is to organize multiple genes into a single T-DNA for transformation, in which genes can be controlled by independent promoter, untranslated region (UTR) and terminator (individual expression cassette), or transcribed in the form of polycistronic mRNA and translated independently via viral internal ribosome entry sites (IRESs), or transcribed and translated into a fusion protein and cleaved by the self-cleavage 2A peptide (Chen et al., 2016; Dafny-Yelin and Tzfira, 2007; Ha et al., 2010; Ma et al., 2024; Rajput et al., 2022; Urwin et al., 2000; Zhao et al., 2014). However, this approach is challenged by the technical hurdle of producing complex constructs for high-level and co-ordinated expression in plants. 

Several cloning systems have been established for gene stacking, such as the transgene-stacking and TransGene Stacking II (TGSII) systems that are based on Cre/loxP recombination (Lin et al., 2003; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2017; Zhu and Liu, 2021), the jStack system that utilizes yeast homologous recombination for assembly (Shih et al., 2016), and the GAANTRY (gene assembly in Agrobacterium by nucleic acid transfer using recombinase technology) that utilizes unidirectional site-specific recombinases for assembly in Agrobacterium rhizogenes (Collier 

et al., 2018; Hathwaik et al., 2021). Based on newly developed cloning methods like Golden Gate Assembly (GGA), Gibson assembly, In-Fusion cloning and Gateway cloning (Engler and Marillonnet, 2011; Gibson et al., 2009; Hamilton et al., 2007; Walhout et al., 2000), toolboxes have been developed for producing plant expression constructs. Of these, the MoClo, GreenGate and GoldenBraid systems are based on GGA (Engler et al., 2014; Lampropoulos et al., 2013; Sarrion-Perdigones et al., 2013); GuanNan Stacking system (GNS) uses GGA and Gateway cloning (Qin et al., 2022); and the Pyramiding Stacking of Multigenes (PSM) system is based on Gibson assembly and Gateway cloning (Zeng et al., 2023). These toolboxes consist of a set of specially designed donor, entry and destination vectors, in which the overhang sequences used for fusion (the ‘syntax’) are fixed. All functional parts or modules should be cloned into proper vectors, which are associated with their positions in the final construct, so that they can be assembled in the desired orientation and order. Additionally, a coherent library of functional modules for the construction. 

Over the past decades, a large number of canonical vectors have been produced for various applications. They are based on the classic cloning method that uses type IIP (for ‘palindromic’) restriction endonucleases and DNA ligase to clone a DNA fragment into the vector. In a ‘digestion-ligation’ cycle, a single or a few fragments can be inserted, and this process depends on the availability of unique restriction sites in both the insertion and the vector, which is usually limited. This makes the production of constructs with multiple transcription units complicated and time-consuming. Here, we introduce the Multiplex Expression Cassette Assembly (MECA) method, which modifies conventional vectors compatible with GGA, and then uses them to produce complex constructs directly or hierarchically. As a proof of concept, we show the process for vector modification, and their applications in transient expression, genome editing and metabolic engineering in plants and microbes. Since MECA does not require any special vector and coherent library of modules, and the syntax for the junction can be defined freely, these features highlight MECA as a highly efficient, versatile and flexible method for building complex genetic circuits, which will facilitate genetic engineering and synthetic biology in plants. 

# Results

The concept of MECA includes: (1) modifying conventional vectors to be compatible with GGA, and (2) using modified vectors to build complex genetic circuits for engineering. 

# Modification of conventional vectors for GGA

MECA modifies conventional vectors compatible with GGA, which exploits the ability of type IIS (for ‘shifted cleavage’) restriction endonucleases to cleave the DNA at positions away from their recognition sequences (Figure S1a). With appropriately placed recognition sites and designed cleavage sequences, the sticky ends generated by enzymatic digestion can be annealed, allowing the seamless assembly of multiple fragments in an orderly and simultaneous manner (Figure S1b). To this end, a conventional vector can be made compatible with GGA by inserting a pair of type IIS restriction sites at the selected position and removing corresponding restriction sites in its backbone sequence. Here, we modified pUC19, pBI121 and pET28a to be compatible with GGA, and then used them as intermediate vector, eukaryotic expression vector and prokaryotic expression vector for construction, respectively. 

The plasmid pUC19 is a widely used small and high copy number vector that contains 13 restriction sites for gene cloning (Yanisch-Perron et al., 1985). We proposed to insert a pair of outward-facing Esp3I restriction sites flanking the lacZ module, which would enable not only Esp3I-based GGA, but also blue-white screening for the easy identification of recombinant colonies. Since the lacZ module contains a canonical multiple cloning site (MCS), we used a BpiI-based GGA reaction for modification. The lacZ module was amplified by PCR using primers with add-on tail of Esp3I and BpiI sites, while the pUC19 backbone was amplified using primers containing BpiI sites with overhang sequences complementary to those of the lacZ module (Figure 1a). Their PCR products were subjected to the BpiI-based one-pot GGA reaction, which generated the pUC19-GG plasmid that is compatible with Esp3I-based GG assembly (syntax of ‘TCAG’ and ‘GCGA’, Figure 1a). 

The pBI121 is one of the earliest binary vectors produced for plant transformation (Jefferson, 1987), and has been widely used for the generation of stable transgenic plants (overexpression, gene silencing, etc.) and transient expression of foreign genes until recently (Bashiri et al., 2023; Pavlichenko and Protopopova, 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). According to its publicly available nucleotide sequence (GenBank Accession: AF485783.1) (Chen et al., 2003), we proposed to replace the 35S::GUS::NOS cassette with the BsaI-flanked lacZ module, and retain the left and right borders of the T-DNA for the plant genome integration, as well as the NOS::NPTII::NOS cassette for kanamycin resistance selection of transgenic plants. To accomplish this, the lacZ module was amplified with primers containing BsaI restriction sites and additional BpiI sites, which generated sticky ends of ‘TCGA’ and ‘AATT’ after BpiI digestion. # Construction of genetic circuits in modified conventional vectors

Two approaches can be used to generate expression cassettes for eukaryotic and prokaryotic expression by GGA. One approach is to assemble functional parts (in the form of PCR products, plasmids or a mixture of both) directly into the destination vector. The other approach generates multi-cassette constructs hierarchically: Individual parts are first assembled into an intermediate vector forming a transcription unit, then these expression cassettes are assembled into the destination vector (Figure 2). To achieve this, the primers used to amplify the functional parts are designed to contain a type IIS restriction site (enzyme I) with tailored complementary overhang sequences for the seamless ligation (Figure 2a). In addition, the forward primer of the first element and the reverse primer of the last element of each expression cassette should contain another type IIS restriction site (enzyme II) between the enzyme I site and the hybridization sequence (Figure 2a). In these primers, the overhang sequences at the fusion site (also the type IIS endonuclease cleavage sequence) can be chosen freely, following the principle that the fusion sites between the adjacent fragments are complementary, and the first and the last fusion sites in the assembly are complementary to the sites of the vector. By embedding the enzyme II restriction sites in the primers, only one intermediate vector is required to host various expression cassettes flanked by different adaptors for the second round of assembly. 

Using primers with such structural features, functional parts amplified by PCR can be assembled into the intermediate vector by the enzyme I-based GGA, generating expression cassettes flanked by the enzyme II restriction sites (Figure 2b). Then, in the second round of assembly, the expression cassettes can be assembled into the destination vector by the enzyme II-based GGA, generating the multi-cassette construct (Figure 2c). 

To test the feasibility of these approaches of assembly, and to demonstrate the application of MECA in engineering, we used the vectors produced above for genome editing and metabolic engineering in plants and E. coli. 

# Case 1: Constructs for transient expression of green fluorescent protein (GFP) and $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) in Nicotiana benthamiana

Transient expression is a rapid and convenient method to produce foreign proteins in plants. It has played important roles in studying gene function, elucidating metabolic pathways and producing therapeutic proteins and vaccines (Eidenberger et al., 2023; Goodin et al., 2008; Sainsbury and Lomonossoff, 2014). However, in most cases, the co-expression of multiple genes is achieved by co-infiltration of several expression 

constructs that harbour single gene, making it difficult to fine-tune the expression of various genes co-ordinately. 

To test the effectiveness of MECA, we first made the 35S:: GFP::OCS construct via the direct assembly approach. The CaMV 35S promoter, GFP and OCS terminator were amplified using primers containing BsaI sites with complementary overhangs. Then the purified PCR products were directly assembled into pBI121-GG in a BsaI-based one-pot GGA reaction, generating the plant expression construct of 35S::GFP::OCS (named pGFP-121, Figure 3a). The agroinfiltration-mediated transient expression of pGFP-121 in N. benthamiana resulted in green fluorescence in leaf epidermal cells at 5 days post-infiltration (DPI, Figure 3b), indicating successful expression and translation of this transcription unit in the plant. 

We then made a construct containing expression cassettes of 35S::GFP::OCS and 35S::GUS::OCS via the hierarchical approach. All primers used to amplify the 35S promoter, GFP, GUS and OCS terminator were designed to contain customized Esp3I sites. In addition, the forward primers of the 35S promoter and the reverse primers of the OCS terminator were designed to contain tailored BsaI sites between the Esp3I site and the hybridization sequence. The first round of Esp3I-based GGA using their purified PCR products generated expression cassettes of 35S::GUS::OCS (pGUS) and 35S::GFP::OCS (pGFP) in pUC19-GG, respectively, both of which are flanked by BsaI sites with complementary overhangs (Figure 3c). The second round of BsaI-based GGA joined these cassettes into pBI121- GG, producing the multi-cassette construct pGUS-GFP (Figure 3c). Transient expression of pGUS-GFP in N. benthamiana resulted in both green fluorescence in leaves (Figure 3d) and $\beta$ -glucuronidase activity that increased since 2 DPI (Figure 3e), and the presence of both proteins could be detected by western blot (Figure 3f).

# Case 3: Heterologous production of betanin in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum)

Engineering a specialized metabolic pathway in a heterologous host is a promising and cost-effective approach to meet the rapidly increasing demand for natural bioactive compounds for human health (Volk et al., 2023). Plants utilize their photosynthetic and carbon assimilation machinery to produce all types of metabolites from light, water, soil and $C 0 _ { 2 }$ without any special equipment, making them an ideal chassis for producing natural bioactive compounds at low cost and large scale (Pouvreau et al., 2018). However, compared to the great achievements made in the metabolic engineering in microbial hosts like E. coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae, little progress has been made in plants (Yang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2020). One of the challenges in plant synthetic biology is the difficulty to make genetic circuits in which multiple genes are transcribed and translated coordinately, and propagated stably to the next generation (Rizzo et al., 2023). Therefore, we used MECA to make constructs for metabolic engineering in tobacco. 

Betanin is an alkaloid pigment synthesized in Caryophyllales order plants, such as the red beet (Beta vulgaris) (Polturak and 

Aharoni, 2019). In addition to serving as an important food colorant, betanin is also beneficial to health due to its bioactivities as an antioxidant, anti-inflammatory and reactive oxygen species scavenger (Esatbeyoglu et al., 2015). The biosynthesis of betanin begins with the hydroxylation of tyrosine to 3,4-dihydroxy-Lphenylalanine (L-DOPA) by cytochrome P450 monooxygenases of the CYP76ADa family (e.g. CYP76AD1 from red beet). L-DOPA is then oxidized by CYP76ADa family P450s to cyclo-DOPA, or by DOPA 4,5-dioxygenase (DODA) to betalamic acid. Spontaneous condensation of cyclo-DOPA with betalamic acid forms betanidin, which is then glycosylated at its 5-hydroxyl position by betanidin 5-O-glucosyltransferase (5GT), producing betanin (Figure 5a) (Polturak and Aharoni, 2018). By incorporating CYP76AD1, DODA and 5GT as a single ORF separated by sequence encoding the self-cleavage 2A peptide (known as ‘RUBY’), betanin has been produced in plants like Arabidopsis, rice, cotton and carrot (Deng et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; He et al., 2020). 

We proposed to synthesize betanin in tobacco using an alternative strategy, in which each gene is controlled independently by its own promoter and terminator. We first assembled expression cassettes of 35S::CYP76AD1::OCS, 35S::DODA::OCS and 35S::5GT::OCS separately in pUC19-GG. To enhance the metabolic flux towards the precursor tyrosine, an additional cassette of 35S::ADH::OCS was made to express the feedback insensitive arogenate dehydrogenase (ADHa), which catalyses the conversion of L-arogenate to tyrosine (Timoneda et al., 2018). These cassettes were assembled into pBI121-GG, generating the multi-cassette plasmid pBeta (Figure 5b). Transient expression of pBeta in N. benthamiana resulted in the accumulation of red pigment in leaves (Figure S2a), indicating that these genes could be transcribed and translated to support the biosynthesis of betanin. The construct pBeta was then introduced into tobacco (Figure S2b). The PCR analysis showed that the intact T-DNA region was integrated in the tobacco genome, and the quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis revealed that all genes were transcribed in the transgenic plants (Figure S3). This is consistent with the purple-red colour of the transgenic plants due to the accumulation of betanin (Figures 5c,d, S2b).

# Case 5: Metabolic engineering of $\mathfrak { B }$ -carotene in E. coli

To test the effectiveness of the MECA method for the synthetic biology in a microbial host, we engineered E. coli to produce $\beta \cdot$ - carotene, an unoxidized carotenoid that is abundant in plants such as carrot and sweet potato (Eggersdorfer and Wyss, 2018). As the precursor of vitamin A and a natural colouring pigment, bcarotene is of great commercial importance in the pharmaceutical, cosmetics and food industries (Barreiro and Barredo, 2018). Currently, $\beta$ -carotene is produced either by direct extraction from natural sources including vegetables, fruits, algae, fungi and bacteria $( \sim 2 \% - 3 \%$ of the total consumption), or by chemical synthesis $( > 9 0 \% )$ ), which is not only difficult but also controversial in food safety (Jing et al., 2022). 

Heterologous production of $\beta$ -carotene in a microbial host has been achieved, with a core set of enzymes including geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthase (crtE), phytoene synthase (crtB), phytoene desaturase (crtI) and lycopene cyclase (crtY) (Figure 7a) (Kim et al., 2006). In prokaryotes, the translation 

initiation occurs co-transcriptionally, with the RNA polymerase (RNAP) and the ribosome physically interacting with each other, and the ribosome binds to the ribosome binding site (RBS) of the mRNA as soon as it emerges from the RNAP (Kohler et al., 2017). We organized the crtE/B/I/Y genes into an operon-like structure in pET28-GG, in which all genes are controlled by the T7 promoter and lac operator. These genes are transcribed as a single mRNA, and translated into individual proteins mediated by the rbs located upstream of their ORFs (pCart, Figure 7b). The E. coli strain harbouring pCart exhibited a yellowish colour when grown in medium containing IPTG (Figure 7c). 

In E. coli, the MEP pathway is the exclusive isoprenoid precursor biosynthetic pathway, in which the first enzyme 1- deoxy-D-xylulose-5-phosphate synthase (DXS) catalyses the ratelimiting formation of 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate (DXP) from pyruvate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (G3P) (Rinaldi et al., 2022). To increase the yield of $\beta$ -carotene, we cloned the tomato SlDXS2, which is responsible for the biosynthesis of monoterpenoids specifically in glandular trichomes (Paetzold et al., 2010). SlDXS2 was co-assembled it with crtE/B/I/Y genes, generating the pCart $+ \mathsf { D }$ construct (Figure S8). The E. coli strain transformed of pCart+D plasmid showed an orange colour, indicating a higher level of $\beta$ -carotene accumulation (Figure 7c). Cultivation of E. coli strains harbouring pCart and pCart+D in flask for $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ resulted in $\beta$ -carotene yields of approximately 20 and $6 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ , whereas the growth of E. coli was not affected (Figure 7d). These results suggest that MECA could also modify prokaryotic expression vectors for synthetic biology, such as the metabolic engineering of $\beta$ -carotene in E. coli. 

# Discussion

Coordinated and simultaneous manipulation of the transcription of multiple genes is critical in modern crop breeding for more effective trait improvement. Over the past decades, a large number of vectors have been produced for applications including gene cloning, gene silencing, heterologous expression and genome editing. However, most of them are based on the classic cloning method that uses type IIP restriction endonucleases to manipulate single or a few DNA fragments, and the process of building a multi-cassette construct is highly challenging. MECA is an emerging method to use these canonical vectors for highefficiency production of complex constructs without altering their special apparatus and functions, such as antibiotic resistance and replication origin in E. coli and Agrobacterium. MECA modifies vectors to be compatible to GGA by inserting a pair of outwardfacing type IIS restriction sites, and removing corresponding sites in the backbone. As a proof of concept, we used these vectors to produce constructs for transient expression, genome editing and metabolic engineering in plant and microbial hosts, suggesting that MECA is a versatile method that can handle DNA fragments for diverse applications. Although only 1–4 expression cassettes $( < 1 3 \mathrm { ~ k b } )$ were assembled in the binary vector pBI121 (cases 1–4), we believe that more cassettes could be loaded in the vector. In a recent report, six transcription units were integrated into the pBI121 vector by more than five rounds of Cre/LoxP-mediated recombination and restriction-ligation reactions. Golden Gate Assembly utilizes Type IIS restriction enzymes to generate tailored sticky ends on cut DNA fragments to achieve assembly of DNA fragments. Recently, by using data-optimized assembly design, a 40 kb T7 phage genome was assembled from 52 fragments in a one-pot GGA reaction (Pryor et al., 2022), suggesting that GGA is a powerful technology for DNA cloning. Based on the GGA technology, several cloning toolboxes have been developed, such as the MoClo, GreenGate and GoldenBraid systems (Engler et al., 2014; Lampropoulos et al., 2013; Sarrion-Perdigones et al., 2013). These systems consist of ‘core vectors’ (special plasmids containing type II restriction sites and various prefix/suffix sequences for assembly) and a library of modularized and standardized DNA parts. For example, the MoClo toolbox contains 75 core vectors, including 17 level 0 vectors for hosting standardized DNA parts (promoters, ORFs, UTRs, etc.), 14 level 1 vectors as backbones for the assembly of single expression cassette, 16 level 2, level M and level P vectors for the multiplex assembly of expression constructs, 21 end-links for filling ends at the level-1/M/P and 7 dummies for filling position gaps in the 

construct (Engler et al., 2014). To make a multi-cassette construct, all individual functional parts should be standardized (cloned into appropriate level 0 vectors), then they are assembled into appropriate level 1 vectors (single-cassette), and into level 2, level M or level P vectors generating multi-cassette constructs (Weber et al., 2011). In these systems, the syntax for the junction is fixed in special vectors, thus each element or module should be placed in an appropriate vector, which is associated with its position in the construct when it is assembled. In contrast, MECA embeds the prefix/suffix sequences (syntax, which can be arbitrary) in primers, and uses the PCR products for the first round of assembly (single-cassette). This introduces the syntax into the intermediate plasmids, which are then used for the second round of assembly (multi-cassette). By this strategy, MECA is able to use only one intermediate vector (pUC19-GG) and one destination vector to produce complex constructs for transient expression, genome editing and metabolic engineering and does not require a standardized library for the construction process. 

In addition to GGA, other DNA cloning technologies like the Gateway cloning and Gibson assembly, have also been used to develop toolboxes for making multiplex constructs, such as the GuanNan Stacking system (GNS) (Qin et al., 2022) and the Pyramiding Stacking of Multigenes (PSM) system (Zeng et al., 2023). It is a considerable challenge to establish a complex system in which heterologous genes are precisely regulated at the transcriptional and post-translational levels in plants. By approaches like independent translation of polycistronic mRNA 

via IRES, or cleavage of fusion protein by the self-cleavage 2A peptide (Chen et al., 2016; Ha et al., 2010; Ma et al., 2024), it is difficult to control the abundance of proteins individually. In contrast, in constructs generated by MECA, all heterologous genes are organized as independent transcription units for finetuned and coordinated regulation. Thus, in addition to metabolic engineering as demonstrated here, MECA can be used to improve traits that are controlled by complex gene network, such as the yield (Bailey-Serres et al., 2019), nutritional quality (Zhu et al., 2020), tolerance to salinity, drought, flood and extreme temperature (Husaini, 2022), broad-spectrum resistance to diseases and pathogens (Derbyshire et al., 2024; Nelson et al., 2018). In summary, MECA is a flexible, efficient and versatile method for building complex genetic circuits, which will not only play a critical role in plant synthetic biology, but also facilitate genetic engineering for improving agronomic traits and pyramiding of multiple complex traits for the development of next-generation elite crops. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Wild-type (WT) tobacco (cultivar ‘K326’) and tomato (cultivar ‘Ailsa Craig’) were grown in a greenhouse under a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h dark at a temperature of $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Transgenic tomato and tobacco plants were germinated and selected on Murashige and Skoog (MS) plates containing $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin, and transgenic seedlings were transferred to soil after 14 days. For seed collection, these plants were transferred to larger pots and allowed to complete their life cycle in the greenhouse. 

Nicotiana benthamiana seeds were germinated in the dark for 5 days, and young seedlings were transferred to individual pots and grown in a growth chamber at $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , $70 \%$ humidity and under a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h dark. Four-week-old plants were used for syringe infiltration and transient expression assays. 

# Golden Gate assembly

One-pot Golden Gate Assembly was performed as described (Bird et al., 2022; Marillonnet and Grutzner, 2020). Briefly, a $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ GGA reaction was prepared by mixing 1 lL $1 0 \times \mathsf { T } 4$ DNA ligase buffer (250 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.6, 50 mM ${ \sf M g } { \sf C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5 mM ATP, and 5 mM DTT), 1 lL T4 DNA ligase (New England Biolabs, Cat. no. M0202L), $0 . 5 ~ \mu \iota$ Esp3I (New England Biolabs, Cat. no. R0734L) or BsaI (New England Biolabs, Cat. no. R3733L), or BpiI (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. no. ER1012), 20 fmol of each plasmid or PCR product and adequate amount of $H _ { 2 } O$ . The reaction mixture was incubated in a thermocycler for 4 h at 37 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , or 15–30 cycles of $3 7 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \ 5$ min and $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ 10 min. The reaction mixture was transformed immediately into E. coli strain DH5a. 

# Plasmid construction

pUC19-GG 

The pUC19 backbone was amplified by PCR using primers pUC19-F-BpiI and pUC19-R-BpiI. The lacZ was amplified by PCR using primers lacZ-F-BpiI and lacZ-R-BpiI. The purified PCR products of pUC19 and lacZ were mixed and subjected to BpiI-based one-pot GGA. The reaction mixture was transformed into the E. coli strain $\mathsf { D H 5 } \mathsf { \alpha }$ and selected on Luria Broth (LB) medium supplemented with $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ ampicillin, $2 0 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ IPTG and $2 0 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indoyl- $\beta$ -d-galactopyranoside (X-gal). Colonies with blue colour were selected and confirmed by sequencing. 

pBI121-GG 

The lacZ was amplified by PCR using primers lacZ-121-F-E and lacZ-121-R-E. The PCR product was digested with BpiI and purified. The pBI121 plasmid was digested with HindIII and EcoRI, and purified after $1 \%$ agarose electrophoresis separation. The lacZ and pBI121 backbone were mixed and subjected to a ligation reaction containing 2 lL $1 0 \times \mathsf { T } 4$ DNA ligase buffer and 1 lL T4 DNA ligase, and incubated at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . # Fluorometric GUS activity assay

The fluorometric assay of $\beta$ -glucuronidase activity was carried out in 96-well microtitre plates as described (Blazquez, 2007). The reaction was terminated by the addition of $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ sodium carbonate $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m } )$ . Fluorescence (excitation $3 6 5 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , emission $4 5 6 \ \mathsf { n m }$ ) was measured by Infinite 200 plate reader (Tecan, Switzerland). A standard curve was generated using 4-MU (4-methyl-umbelliferone, Sigma-Aldrich, M1381). Each assay was performed in triplicate. Total protein concentration was measured by the Bradford method with bovine serum albumin as standard (Bradford, 1976).

# Quantification of betanin

The leaf samples ( $1 0 0 \ \mathsf { m g } )$ of transgenic tobacco plants were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen, extracted with 1 mL $50 \%$ methanol on ice for 15 min, followed by centrifugation at $1 6 0 0 0 \pmb { g }$ for 10 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ g. # Analysis of volatile terpenes, thymol and carvacrol by GC–MS

The extraction and analysis of volatile terpenes in tomato, and thymol and carvacrol in tobacco, were performed using the same protocol. Tomato or tobacco leaves $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g } )$ were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen, and extracted with 1 mL $n$ -hexane containing $2 { \mathrm { ~ n g / \mu L } }$ nonyl acetate (Sigma-Aldrich, S364258) by vortexing at room temperature for 2 min. After centrifugation at 16000 $g$ for 5 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , the supernatant was collected for analysis. 

The extract was analysed using an Agilent 7890B gas chromatography coupled with an Agilent 5977 quadrupole mass analyser. The chromatographic separation was performed on a DB-5MS fused-silica capillary column $( 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m } \times$ $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ , Agilent, CA, USA) with helium as the carrier gas at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. The injector temperature was maintained at $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The oven temperature ramp was $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, 60– $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ at $1 0 ~ { ^ \circ } C / \operatorname* { m i n }$ and $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min. The temperature of MS quadrupole and ion source (electron impact) were set to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The collision energy was $7 0 ~ \mathrm { e V }$ . Mass data were acquired in a full-scan mode (m/z 50–500). Metabolites were identified by comparing with the mass spectra of the NIST Mass Spectral Library (NIST14). 

# Production and quantification of $\beta$ -carotene

The plasmids of pCart or pCart+D were transformed into E. coli stain BL21. Single colonies were inoculated into 10-mL glass tubes containing 2 mL of LB medium (containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin) and cultured overnight at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and 220 rpm. The overnight culture was inoculated into $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of LB (containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin) in a 250-mL flask at a final ${ \mathsf { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.05, and incubated at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and 220 rpm until $\mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 5$ . Then, IPTG $( 0 . 1 ~ \mathsf { m m } )$ was added to the culture, and cultivated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and 220 rpm for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . An appropriate volume of culture was sampled at every 6 h for determination of the ${ \mathsf { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ and the $\beta \cdot$ - carotene content. 

Escherichia coli cells (2 mL) were harvested by centrifugation at $1 0 \ 0 0 0 \ \pmb { g }$ for 5 min, suspended in 1 mL of acetone and gincubated at $5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min in the dark. After centrifugation at $1 3 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min, the acetone supernatant was collected gand filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ membrane. The $\beta$ -carotene content was analysed using a 1290 Infinity II chromatography system equipped with a diode array detector (Agilent, USA). Five microlitres of extracts were analysed on a Zorbax Eclipse Plus C18 column $( 4 . 6 ~ \mathsf { m m } \times 1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ , $5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ particle size, Agilent, USA) using the mobile phase of methanol/acetonitrile/dichloromethane $= 2 1 { : } 2 1 { : } 8$ at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. $\beta$ -carotene was monitored at $4 5 3 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ and quantified by comparing with a calibration curve created with the authorized standard (Sigma-Aldrich, C4582). All experiments were performed in triplicate, and the results represent the means $\pm \mathsf { S . D }$ . of three independent experiments.
