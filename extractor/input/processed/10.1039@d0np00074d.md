# Biosynthesis and heterologous production of furanocoumarins: perspectives and current challenges

Joana L. Rodrigues $\textcircled{1}$ * and L´ıgia R. Rodrigues $\textcircled{1}$

Covering: up to October 2020

Furanocoumarins are plant secondary metabolites used to treat several skin disorders, such as psoriasis and vitiligo, and also with other potential therapeutic activities. Furanocoumarins are extracted from plants where they accumulate in low amounts over long growth periods. In addition, their extraction and purification are difficult in an environmentally unfriendly and expensive process. Hence, new sustainable and greener production schemes able to overcome such limitations ought to be developed. While the heterologous production of simple coumarins has been demonstrated, the biosynthesis of more complex furanocoumarins remains greatly unexplored. Although several important steps of the pathway have been elucidated in the last decade, the complete pathway has not been completely unravelled. In this paper, we review the natural conversion of amino acids into furanocoumarins, as well as the heterologous expression of each enzyme of the pathway. We also explore the challenges that need to be addressed so that their heterologous production can become a viable alternative.

# 1. Introduction

Furanocoumarins are phenolic compounds derived from coumarins produced in several higher plants. They originate from the phenylpropanoid pathway and are subjected to numerous chemical modications. Their chemical structure consists of a furan ring fused to the coumarin umbelliferone (Fig. 1). They are potentially toxic compounds whose production is triggered by several types of stresses, including fungal infections in plants, acting as toxins to the attacking organism (phytoalexins or elicitors). Besides acting as a defense mechanism to phytopathogens, herbivores or insects, they are also toxic to humans. Direct contact with the sap from some plants containing furanocoumarins (e.g. Heracleum mantegazzianum) causes inammation, burns and irritating sensitivity to sunlight.1

Linear furanocoumarins such as xanthotoxin and bergapten are used in the clinic. There are currently several drugs in the market (e.g. 8-MOP®, Oxsoralen®, Melanocyl®, Uvadex®) with these active principles approved by FDA/EMA and are commonly used in PUVA therapy (Psoralens $^ +$ UVA) due to their enhanced activity in UV radiation presence (photoactive). For some diseases PUVA therapy is the rst-line of treatment (e.g. mycosis fungoides, most common type of cutaneous T-cell lymphoma)2 while for other diseases as vitiligo and psoriasis, PUVA therapy is used in several severe and difficult-to-treat

cases.3 Moreover, several clinical trials are ongoing to evaluate furanocoumarins potential to treat gra versus host disease, Crohn's disease and many cancer types.4–6

Chemical synthesis of furanocoumarins is complex and expensive.7 Therefore, furanocoumarins are commonly extracted from several plants, for example psoralen is usually extracted from Ficus carica and Psoralea corylifolia and xanthotoxin from Ammi majus and other plants,8,9 despite their accumulation in low amounts $\big ( 0 . 1 - 2 . 3 \% \big ) ^ { 1 0 - 1 3 }$ over very long growth periods due to a tight metabolism regulation. In addition, the extraction and purication of these compounds is difficult and expensive since it requires the separation of other natural compounds with similar chemical structures in an environmentally unfriendly process. Consequently, new sustainable and greener processes able to overcome such limitations ought to be developed. Compared to chemical synthesis and extraction methods, heterologous microbial production may have several benets since it is not limited by season, plant availability and/or regional and environmental factors and it is a renewable, environmentally friendly and sustainable approach.14,15 In addition, it has the advantage of not requiring contacting with the plants that produce furanocoumarins, which can be very harmful to humans. Therefore, the study of furanocoumarins pathway is highly desirable and can also elucidate how to further shutdown their production in plants with agricultural and ornamental relevance.

# 2.1 Conversion of amino acids to umbelliferone

Umbelliferone precursor is the amino acid phenylalanine (Fig. 1). Phenylalanine is transformed into cinnamic acid by phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) and cinnamic acid is converted to $p$ -coumaric acid by the enzyme cinnamate-4- hydroxylase (C4H), a cytochrome P450 (CYP450) monooxygenase.16 The enzyme 4-coumarate-CoA ligase (4CL) converts the carboxylic acid in the respective CoA ester, i.e. $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA. All these steps are well characterized in plants and different hosts since are common in the production of several plant secondary metabolites, such as curcuminoids, stilbenoids and avonoids.14,15,17

Aerwards, the CoA ester is converted to a coumarin molecule via ortho-hydroxylation of the aromatic ring by a 2- oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase (2OGD) and by spontaneous side-chain trans–cis isomerization and nally lactonization. This step was rst claried in Arabidopsis thaliana where feruloyl-CoA $6 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylases (AtF60 H1 and $A t \mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } 2 $ ) were used to produce the coumarin scopoletin from feruloyl-CoA.18 Based on this report, Vialart et al.10 combined a bioinformatics approach and a cDNA library screening to identify putative 2OGDs in Ruta graveolens. They identied a $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA $2 ^ { \prime }$ - hydroxylase $\left( R g \mathbf { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathbf { H } \right)$ that converted $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA to dihydroxycinnamoyl-CoA. This enzyme in addition to synthesizing umbelliferone in R. graveolens, also converts feruloyl-CoA into the coumarin scopoletin.10 Matsumoto et al.19 also identi-ed several 2OGD enzymes in Ipomoea batatas (IbF60 H1-1, IbF60 H1-2, IbF60 H1-3, IbF60 H2-1-1, IbF60 H2-1-8 and IbF60 H2-2- 1). IbF60 H1 enzymes showed activity towards feruloyl-CoA to form scopoletin, while $I b \mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } 2$ enzymes showed similar affinity for both substrates (feruloyl-CoA and $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA). 2ODG from Peucedanum praeruptorum showed high $\mathbf { { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathbf { { H } } }$ activity towards $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA $( P p { \bf C } 2 ^ { \prime } { \bf H } )$ and only trace activity for feruloyl-CoA,20 while 2OGD from Glycine max showed only activity towards feruloyl-CoA (GmF60 H).21

So far, only some steps of the furanocoumarins pathway have been tested in a heterologous organism. Frequently, in heterologous pathways, a tyrosine ammonia lyase (TAL) is used to replace PAL and C4H since it is able to use tyrosine as precursor and to convert it directly to $p$ -coumaric acid.15 The 2OGD enzymes from A. thaliana, R. graveolens, I. batatas and P. praeruptorum ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 ^ { \prime } { \mathrm { H } }$ and $\mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } \big ]$ were also already successfully expressed and characterized in E. coli BL21.10,13,18–20 The molecular characterization of 2OGD enzymes opened the door to the heterologous production of simple coumarins. Lin et al., 22 Yang et al.23 and Zhao et al.24 constructed in E. coli the biosynthetic pathway of some coumarins, including umbelliferone, the precursor of furanocoumarins. Lin et al.22 used 4CL enzymes

from A. thaliana (At4CL1 and At4CL2) and Petroselinum crispum (Pc4CL2) to transform hydroxycinnamic acids to the CoA esters. To convert $p$ -coumaroy-CoA to umbelliferone, the $\mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ enzyme from I. batatas $( I b \mathbf { F 6 ^ { \prime } H } 2 { \cdot } 1 { \cdot } 1 ) ^ { \mathbf { 1 9 } }$ was selected. To produce scopoletin the authors used $A t \mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } 1$ enzyme that presents high orthohydroxylation activity towards feruloyl-CoA.18 By combining these enzymes with $P c \mathbf { 4 C L 2 }$ that has broad specicity, they were able to produce $4 . 3 \mathrm { ~ m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of umbelliferone and $2 7 . 8 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of scopoletin from $p$ -coumaric acid and ferulic acid (1 mM), respectively. For de novo biosynthesis of coumarins in E. coli BW25113, codon-optimized TAL from R. graveolens $\left( R g \mathrm { T A L } \right)$ was selected. At4CL1 was carefully chosen due to demonstrated higher catalytic efficiency towards $p$ -coumaric acid. For de novo scopoletin production, the authors overexpressed 4-hydroxyphenylacetate 3-hydroxylase (4HPA3H) from E. coli to convert $p$ -coumaric acid to caffeic acid, and At4CL2 to convert caffeic acid to caffeoyl-CoA since it has higher affinity and catalytic activity towards this substrate. Then, caffeoyl-CoA was transformed in feruloyl-CoA using caffeoyl-CoA $o$ -methyltransferase (CCoAOMT) from A. thaliana. The last step was performed by $A t \mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } 1$ . By using the complete articial pathways, they obtained $2 . 4 3 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ of umbelliferone and $3 . 0 6 ~ \mathrm { m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ of scopoletin. Scopoletin production was positively inuenced by tyrosine availability, while for umbelliferone its increased availability had a negative effect. To increase tyrosine availability, Lin et al.22 overexpressed in a plasmid ppsA and tktA along with feedback inhibition resistant (r) versions $t y r A ^ { f b r }$ and $a r o G ^ { f b r }$ . The genes ppsA and tklA, that encode phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) synthase and transketolase, respectively, allowed to increase the availability of PEP and erythrose 4- phosphate, the amino acids precursors. The overexpression of the r versions $t y r A ^ { f b r }$ and $a r o G ^ { f b r }$ allows to increase the amino acids overexpression since the native enzymes (TyrA/PheA – bifunctional chorismate mutase/prephenate dehydratase and DAHPS – 3-deoxy-D-arabino-heptulosonate-7-phosphate synthase) are feedback inhibited by tyrosine and phenylalanine, the end products, and by tyrosine repressor (TyrR).

In a different study, Yang et al.23 used TAL from Saccharothrix espanaensis, 4CL from Oryza sativa (Os4CL3) that presented high activity and broad specicity,25 and two ${ \mathrm { F } } 6 ^ { \prime } { \mathrm { H } } s$ from I. batatas $( I b \mathbf { F 6 } ^ { \prime } \mathbf { H } 2 { - } 2 { - } 1$ and IbF60 H2-1-8) for de novo production of umbelliferone $\left( 6 6 . 1 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } } \right)$ in E. coli BL21. To produce the coumarin esculetin $\left( 6 1 . 4 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 } \right)$ ) from glucose they also used coumarate-3-hydroxylase (C3H) from S. espanaensis that allows the conversion of $p$ -coumaric acid to caffeic acid. In addition, they produced $8 2 . 9 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ of umbelliferone, $5 2 . 3 \mathrm { \ m g \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ of esculetin and $7 9 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of scopoletin from $p$ -coumaric acid, caffeic acid and ferulic acid, respectively. According to the authors, the higher yields compared to the study of Lin et al., 22 were related to the increase of $\mathrm { F } 6 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ enzyme solubility. Since dioxygenases have usually low solubility, and therefore are poorly expressed in E. coli, they fused ${ \mathrm { F } } 6 ^ { \prime } { \mathrm { H } } s$ to glutathione Stransferase (GST) which helped to increase its solubility and stability. Tyrosine availability was also increased in this study by overexpressing ppsA, tktA, $t y r A ^ { f b r }$ and aroGr and deletion of TyrR and TyrA genes. However, it was concluded that better results were obtained when only tyrA and aroG sensitive

versions were overexpressed to produce umbelliferone, while to produce esculetin it was better to only overexpress $t y r A ^ { f b r }$ and $a r o G ^ { f b r }$ . This study is in agreement with Lin et al.22 that also concluded that too much tyrosine had a negative impact in umbelliferone production. Finally, Yang et al.23 also deleted the gene that encodes YbgC thiosterase in order to decrease the possible degradation of the thioesters $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA and caffeoyl-CoA. The results improved signicantly indicating that YbgC degraded the intermediate CoA esters.

In a more recent study, Zhao et al.24 used RNA-seq to characterize the genes associated to umbelliferone pathway in Angelica decursiva. The functional expression of AdPAL, Ad4CL and $A d \mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ in E. coli was successful. However, AdC4H, that is a CYP450 membrane protein, did not show functional expression in E. coli and it was deduced that a eukaryotic system would be more suitable for its expression. Therefore, they used RgTAL in their pathway together with Ad4CL and $A d \mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ . Umbelliferone production in E. coli K12 (MG1655) was around $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ and in E. coli K12 (MG1655) tyrosine overproducing strain was around $7 5 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ . This overproducing strain overexpressed tktA, tyrAr , aroGr , aroB (3-dehydroquinate synthase), aroE (shikimate dehydrogenase) and aroK (shikimate kinase I), all important to drive the carbon ow to tyrosine. In addition, the genes that encode TyrR, PheA and TrpE (anthranilate synthase) that direct the pathway to phenylalanine and tryptophan biosynthesis, respectively, were deleted. Moreover, 4CL step, known to be frequently a bottleneck in heterologous pathways26,27 was thoroughly studied. Several 4CL enzymes were evaluated and it was concluded that Pc4CL1 allowed to obtain higher titers (almost $2 0 0 \ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of umbelliferone). Aerwards, they used mutagenesis to improve Pc4CL1 activity. $\mathbf { Q } 2 7 2 \mathbf { H } /$ F267L mutation in Pc4CL1 in combination with induction/ culture conditions optimization allowed to reach $3 5 6 . 5 9 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ umbelliferone, the highest titer obtained so far.

Recently, the coumarin scopoletin was also produced in Saccharomyces cerevisiae BY4742.28 AtF60 H1 and $P c 4 \mathrm { C L }$ were integrated in the yeast genome and were used to convert ferulic acid, a lignin monomer, to scopoletin. $p$ -Coumaric acid, also a main monomer of lignin, was as well converted to scopoletin with caffeic acid and ferulic acid as intermediates using 4HPA3H from Pseudomonas aeruginosa, NADPH-avin oxidoreductase (HpaC) from Enterobacteriaceae and caffeic acid Omethyltransferase (COMT) from A. thaliana. The study was able to produce $4 . 7 9 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of scopoletin from pretreated lignin. All these studies regarding coumarins biosynthesis pave the way for the future success of furanocoumarins heterologous production.

# 2.2 Conversion of umbelliferone to the furanocoumarins psoralen and angelicin

Umbelliferone in the presence of dimethylallylpyrophosphate (DMAPP), the prenyl donor substrate, is converted to demethylsuberosin or osthenol by a prenyltransferase (PT) (Fig. 2). This is a key step since it is the entry point into the furanocoumarins biosynthetic pathway and opens the pathway to linear and angular furanocoumarins.16,29 The rst PT enzyme

involved in the furanocoumarin biosynthesis was isolated by Karamat et al.29 in P. crispum (PcPT). This enzyme had high substrate specicity towards umbelliferone and also a high preference for its carbon 6 position leading to demethylsuberosin. Osthenol (the carbon 8-prenylated derivative) was also formed but, in this case, the enzyme showed less activity. PcPT was identied due to its homology to a previously identied PT enzyme from Citrus limon (ClPT1a).30 ClPT1a used geranyl diphosphate (GPP) as prenyl donor to produce 8-geranylumbelliferone from umbelliferone. Therefore, ClPT1a was not considered to be involved in furanocoumarins biosynthesis. To express ClPT1a in yeast the authors deleted 36 amino acids from N-terminal correspondent to a transit peptide related to the plastid localization. Aer these enzymes being identied other PT enzymes were characterized. Munakata et al.11 reported the isolation of two PTs from Pastinaca sativa (PsPT1 and PsPT2). PsPT1 is more efficient in the synthesis of demethylsuberosin, while PsPT2 catalyzes more efficiently osthenol. More recently, a PT enzyme from F. carica (FcPT1a) was also characterized.31 Osthenol was not detected using FcPT1a. All these PT enzymes were expressed in Nicotiana benthamiana, a plant that does not produce furanocoumarins and is infected by Agrobacterium, a bacterium that uses horizontal transfer to transfer DNA to plants.11,29,31 The authors characterized the enzymes of P. crispum, P. sativa and F. carica in vitro and stated that these PT enzymes are not functionally expressed in yeast.11,29 Therefore, it was suggested that the strategy to express ClPT1a in yeast could be useful to express PT enzymes from furanocoumarins pathway since the removal of N-terminal transit peptides may bring PT closer to its substrates and prenyl donors which can enhance the prenylation efficiency in microbes.30,32 However, more recently, Bu et al.33 functionally expressed in vivo PcPT and PsPT2 in Streptomyces xiamenensis and produced $7 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of demethylsuberosin and $0 . 7 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L ^ { - 1 } }$ of osthenol, respectively, from umbelliferone. The production in E. coli was also successful.

Aer prenyltransferase step, demethylsuberosin and osthenol are converted by cyclization to marmesin and columbianetin, respectively, by CYP450 dependent monooxygenases. Until now, as far as we know, there is no report of marmesin synthase (MS) and columbianetin synthase (CS), and their correspondent genes, at the molecular level. There is only a study that reported the production of marmesin by incubating microsomes isolated from A. majus cells in the presence of demethylsuberosin, NADPH (reduced nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate) and oxygen.34 The marmesin synthase enzyme was inhibited by the presence of cytochrome c and carbon-monoxide (irradiated with blue light – $4 5 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ ), con-rming that it is a CYP450 enzyme. Currently, there are several strategies that can be used to identify such enzymes in plants producing furanocoumarins (Section 3.1). However, recently, Bu et al.33 used a substrate promiscuous enzyme from S. xiamenensis (XimD – avin-dependent monooxygenase) to produce marmesin and columbianetin in S. xiamenensis and E. coli. XimD combined with PcPT and PsPT2 produced $5 . 2 \mathrm { ~ m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ of marmesin and trace amounts of columbianetin, respectively, from umbelliferone. Psoralen and angelicin are also produced aer the action of a CYP450.34 Marmesin and columbianetin are transformed into psoralen and angelicin by psoralen synthase (PS) and angelicin synthase (AS), respectively, in a very analogous mechanism to MS and CS. Aerwards, they described the functional expression of other two genes encoding PSs from Apium graveolens (celery), CYP71AJ2 and from P. sativa (parsnip), CYP71AJ3.36 Additionally, they isolated an AS from P. sativa, CYP71AJ4. identify PS from P. corylifolia.37 The functional characterization of this gene is still to be accomplished. Chen et al.38 also identied a gene from Citrus paradisi with possible PS activity and associated with low content of furanocoumarins. Other enzymes such as CYP71AJ27 from Heracleum lanatum were identied as putative PSs.39

Some of these enzymes were expressed in a heterologous organism.35,36 Firstly, Larbat et al.35 tried to express CYP71AJ1 in S. cerevisiae strains (WAT11, WR, WAT21) and in E. coli. The expression failed possibly due to insufficient membrane association or low translational efficiency caused by an unfavorable codon bias. Functional expression in yeast was accomplished aer the authors replaced the N-terminal amino acids of CYP71AJ1 by the corresponding N-terminal anchor sequence of CYP73A1, a C4H enzyme from Helianthus tuberosus, that presents high activity in S. cerevisiae WAT11 strain40. This WAT11 strain has integrated in its genome A. thaliana NADPH-CYP450 reductase 1 (AtCPR1), a redox partner to enhance plant CYP450s activity. Aerwards, CYP71AJ2, CYP71AJ3 and CYP71AJ4 from celery and parsnip were also expressed in S. cerevisiae WAT11 but, in these cases the N-

terminal was not replaced as the transformants activity was enough for kinetic identication.36 More recently, Jian et al.41 identied PS and AS from P. praeruptorum (CYP71AJ49 and CYP71AJ51, respectively) and characterized them in S. cerevisiae WAT11 through in vivo and in vitro assays by replacing the rst 33 aa with CYP73A1 N-terminal.

# 2.3 Conversion of psoralen and angelicin into hydroxylated and methoxylated forms

Psoralen and angelicin are oen converted into other furanocoumarins. For example, xanthotoxol and bergaptol are hydroxylated forms of psoralen and are obtained due to hydroxylation reactions catalyzed by psoralen 8-hydroxylase (P8H) and psoralen 5-hydroxylase (P5H), respectively. P5H was characterized in A. majus cell suspensions also as a CYP450 enzyme.42 Recently, a P8H from P. sativa (CYP71AZ4) was characterized at the molecular level.43 This discovery opens the door for identifying these enzymes in other plants using bioinformatic tools. This enzyme was expressed in the yeast S. cerevisiae WAT11 and was characterized in vitro regarding its function. Yeast microsomes expressing CYP71AZ4 demonstrated that it prefers psoralen as substrate and also accepts several simple coumarins including scopoletin.

Xanthotoxol and bergaptol are then O-methylated to xanthotoxin and bergapten, respectively. The enzymes responsible for these reactions were identied as $S _ { \ l }$ -adenosyl-L-methionine (SAM) furanocoumarin O-methyltransferases (OMT) and it was concluded that distinct enzymes were used to $o$ -methylate each substrate. In the last years several enzymes from different plants (A. majus, Glehnia littoralis, Angelica dahurica and P. praeruptorum) were identied and expressed in E. coli and were all denominated bergaptol 5-OMT (B5OMT) since they were highly specic for bergaptol and did not accept xanthotoxol as substrate.44–47 B5OMT from A. dahurica (AdB5OMT) and P. praeruptorum (PpB5OMT) were overexpressed in E. coli BL21 and bergapten was also produced in vivo.46,47 AdB5OMT activity in vivo was higher than in vitro possibly due to the protein extraction and purication processes.46 However, Zhao et al.47 concluded that $P p \mathbf { B } 5 \mathbf { O M T }$ activity was similar in vivo and in vitro using the same purication method reported by Lo et al.46 E. coli was able to convert the supplemented bergaptol to bergapten and secrete it to the medium. It was not necessary to supply SAM to detect bergapten in vivo. Recently, Song et al.48 identied a putative xanthotoxol 8-OMT (X8OMT) in G. littoralis.

Xanthotoxin and bergapten are converted through hydroxylation by xanthotoxin 5-hydroxylase (X5H) and bergapten 8- hydroxylase (B8H) to 5-hydroxy-xanthotoxin and 8-hydroxybergapten, respectively. The enzymes responsible for the biosynthesis of 5-hydroxy-xanthotoxin were recently identied in A. majus (CYP71AZ1) and P. sativa (CYP71AZ6).43 More recently, a X5H in Citrus hystrix (CYP82D64v2) and C. paradisi (CYP82D64v1) was also characterized at the molecular level.12 All these enzymes were efficiently expressed in S. cerevisiae WAT11 and WAT21 engineered to overexpress AtCPR1 or AtCPR2, respectively.12,43 These enzymes did not demonstrated activity towards bergapten.

Finally, 5-hydroxy-xanthotoxin and 8-hydroxy-bergapten can be converted to isopimpinellin by 5-hydroxy-xanthotoxin 5-Omethyltransferase (5HX5OMT) or 8-hydroxybergapten 5-Omethyltransferase (8HB8OMT), respectively.31 However, the enzymes responsible for the conversions were never characterized at the biochemical and molecular level, which makes the pathway uncertain. In the case of angular furanocoumarins, the enzymes are also unknown but it is speculated that angelicin is converted to sphondin and isobergapten and then these furanocoumarins are transformed in pimpinellin.

# 3. Current challenges

Currently, there are several bottlenecks in the heterologous production of furanocoumarins. Some of the challenges are common to the heterologous production of other plant compounds derived from the phenylpropanoids pathway, such as curcuminoids, avonoids and stilbenoids, and include the availability of tyrosine (or phenylalanine) as precursor and the right choice of enzymes such as TAL and 4CL, that perform the rst steps of the pathway. These bottlenecks have been extensively described elsewhere14,15 and will not be addressed here. In this section, strategies to discover some missing enzymatic steps of the pathway, to increase DMAPP and SAM availability and CYP450 enzyme activity, among others are highlighted.

# 3.1 Unravelling new genes encoding enzymes from the furanocoumarins pathway

Despite the furanocoumarins therapeutic properties and extensive pathway investigation, several details regarding their biosynthesis and many pathway enzymatic steps remain unknown. This compromises the furanocoumarins heterologous production and it is currently the biggest challenge. Some of the steps have been characterized at the biochemical level42 but the genes have not been identied yet.

Furanocoumarins production in plants is triggered by several different types of stresses, including UVB light,29 wounding,13,43 chemical elicitors,11,20 phytophagous insects and fungal infections.34,35,43 These biological or chemical elicitors induce specic enzymes needed for the furanocoumarins production and can be used to increase furanocoumarins accumulation in plants. Therefore, elicitation can be useful to discover the missing genes encoding for some enzymes of the furanocoumarins pathway. The plant transcriptome and/or proteome prole from elicited and non-elicited (control) cultured cells can be compared. First, enzyme activity can be evaluated in protein extracts from elicited cells and controls. Aer conrmation of the desired enzyme activity, transcriptomes can be analysed for example by RNA-sequencing and proteomes by iTRAQ (isobaric tags for relative and absolute quantitation) and liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry. The differentially expressed genes (and proteins) may be used for BLAST search and annotation against the NCBI database. Currently, there are several tools available for gene prediction that can be used to identify the open reading frames (ORFs). Sequences identied as potential genes of interest may be

tested for functional analysis aer gene cloning and heterologous expression in E. coli and S. cerevisiae. Since the target enzymes may have low activity in these hosts as described for other enzymes from the furanocoumarins pathway, other chassis can be used such as N. benthamiana. $^ { 1 1 , 2 9 } A .$ . majus would be an interesting model for elicitation since it is reported to accumulate high amounts of furanocoumarins aer elicitation including marmesin and xanthotoxin.34,35,43 Sequence-based screening has been successfully used to identify novel enzymes from the furanocoumarins pathway due to high degrees of homology between species.10–12,31,48 For example, X8OMT candidates may possibly be identied using BLASTn against B5OMT (that perform a similar step in bergapten pathway) (Fig. 2) that were already identied in several plants, including A. majus.44–47

However, function based-screening should also be considered when the target enzyme has never been identied in any organism. In this case RNA from elicited and/or non-elicited plants can also be extracted and converted to cDNA and used to create a library. However, unlike in sequence-based screening, the library instead of completely sequenced is cloned in an expression vector and tested in different chassis. Only clones with the desired catalytic activity are sequenced using next generation sequencing (NGS) and the predicted ORFs are tested separately to nd the novel gene or genes. Function based-screening has the disadvantage of sometimes not allowing to identify positive clones due to expression complications, for example, caused by translational problems. To minimize this problem several hosts may be tested. This strategy has been used successfully in metagenomics approaches to nd novel gene sequences with desired functions49 but, as far as we know, has never been attempted to nd novel genes from the furanocoumarins pathway.

# 3.2 DMAPP availability

Several studies concluded that DMAPP availability was not enough for heterologous PT reactions.50,51 For example, biotransformation of selected substrates by whole E. coli cells carrying a PT enzyme from the mushroom Antrodia camphorate was only observed in reactions with DMAPP supplemented as in in vitro reactions.50 Prenyl donors such as DMAPP are produced through both mevalonate (MVA) and 2-C-methyl-D-erythritol 4- phosphate (MEP) pathways in plants. However, in microbes the prenyl donor synthesis occurs differently. In yeast, only MVA pathway can be found while in bacterium such as E. coli only the MEP pathway is responsible for DMAPP synthesis. DMAPP availability in microbes has been previously identied as a limiting step in heterologous production of aromatic compounds, including avonoids.32,50,51

Currently, there are several strategies to manipulate the MVA and MEP pathways and increase DMAPP availability. In the MVA pathway, 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase (HMGR) has been identied as a rate-limiting step. Therefore, a truncated form of HMGR (tHMGR) (missing 530 amino acids of the N-terminal) is frequently overexpressed to eliminate feedback regulation.51 Several studies also downregulate the

endogenous GPP synthase (GPPS) and farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP) synthase (FPPS) that convert DMAPP and its isomer isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP) in other prenyl donors.51 The overexpression of IPP-DMAPP isomerase (IDI) that converts IPP to DMAPP has also been tested to increase DMAPP availability in yeast.52 Studies also concluded that the overexpression of heterologous Enterococcus faecalis genes of the upper part of the MVA pathway (from acetyl-CoA to mevalonate) allow to obtain a better ux than the native genes of S. cerevisiae. 52

The MEP pathway has been also extensively studied in order to increase DMAPP availability. The MEP pathway as the MVA pathway contains several control points and bottlenecks. The 1- deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate (DXP) synthase (DXS) enzyme has been proven to be a rate-limiting enzyme of the MEP pathway since it is feedback-regulated by DMAPP and IPP. DXP reductoisomerase (DXR) is also considered a rate-limiting enzyme due to low activity. Therefore, both DXS and DXR have been frequently overexpressed and their promoters replaced by stronger ones.53 Some studies overexpressed native DXS enzymes while others overexpressed the ones from Bacillus subtilis, Rhodobacter capsulatus, Paracoccus aminophilus, among others.54 Other identied bottlenecks are 4-hydroxy-3- methylbut-2-enyl-diphosphate synthase (HDS) and 4-hydroxy-3-methylbut-2-enyl-diphosphate reductase (HDR) that are avodoxin dependent. The overexpression of ferredoxin dependent enzymes from photosynthetic organisms improved enzymes catalytic activity and promoted metabolic ux in E. coli.53

Another strategy to increase DMAPP availability is to take advantage of the synergy between the MVA and MEP pathways. Several studies proved that it is possible to insert a functional MVA pathway in E. coli53 and a functional MEP pathway in S. cerevisiae.55 Usually, E. coli strains engineered with MVA pathway allow higher productions of isoprene (produced from DMAPP) than strains whose endogenous MEP pathway was engineered.53

Although most studies are focused in optimizing the conversion of the precursors of the pathways, acetyl-CoA in the case of MVA pathway and pyruvate and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate in the MEP pathway, to DMAPP and then to the nal product, there are also studies focused in optimizing the precursors supply. For example, E. coli containing the MVA pathway was engineered to increase acetyl-CoA availability by decreasing the formation of byproducts such as lactate, acetate and ethanol.56

# 3.3 CYP450 activity

Some enzymes from the furanocoumarins pathway were not functionally expressed in microbes. As seen for some PT enzymes,30 PS and AS are better expressed in S. cerevisiae aer removal of several amino acids from the N-terminal hydrophobic region.35,41 These enzymes are membrane-bound associated enzymes and their N-terminal frequently contains a transit peptide to plastids such as chloroplasts or to mitochondria. Currently, there are several bioinformatics tools that allow to predict the transit peptides and subcellular localization

and also transmembrane regions. The identied N-terminal amino acids can be simply removed or in the case of CYP450s enzymes such as PS and AS, the N-terminal can be replaced by the N-terminal of other CYP450s that are very well expressed in yeast (e.g. CYP73A1).35,41 Although S. cerevisiae contains an endogenous monooxygenase system (CYP450 and CPR), it revealed to be insufficient for recombinant CYP450 expression. Therefore, to increase plant CYP450 activity several studies frequently overexpress CPR enzymes such as the ones of A. thaliana.12,35,43,51

E. coli does not have any CYP450 or CPR identied in its genome and cannot recognize signal peptides from these plant enzymes which oen leads to misfolding and malfunctioning of CYP450 enzymes.57 Therefore, it is frequently considered less suitable for their heterologous expression. However, studies demonstrated that mammalian CYP450 could be successfully expressed aer N-terminal modications of CYP450 hydroxylases and reductases. Several studies replaced the second codon of the heterologous protein by a codon that codied alanine (Ala) (a preferred second codon, present in highly expressed genes such as $\beta$ -galactosidase gene) or replaced the rst 1–30 codons of the N-terminal transmembrane region for Met–Ala or for a ‘universal’ N-terminal sequence (e.g. MALLLAVF, MAKKTSSKGK).58–62 These modications led to an increase in solubility and abundant expression of CYP450s in E. coli and are now frequently used in the expression of heterologous pathways. For example, Stahlhut et al., 60 aiming to produce the avonoid setin in E. coli, fused CYP450 avonoid monooxygenases from different organisms to a CPR from Catharanthus roseus to facilitate their interaction. Both sequences were codon optimized for E. coli, the rst 4 codons of CYP450 were replaced by ATGGCG (Met–Ala) and the rst 69 codons of CPR were removed. Tamaki et al.59 replaced the N-terminal transmembrane region for a portion of the N-terminus of various mammalian CYP450 and co-expressed a human CPR in order to obtain high activity of a CYP450. Li et al.62 also tested truncated versions of CYP450 and AtCPR for avones production. The authors concluded that CPR did not need to be truncated since it should be present in lower levels than CYP450 to obtain higher productions, as it is observed in plants. Bacterial ferredoxins and ferredoxin reductases partners may also be explored for CYP450 expression in E. coli. 63,64 Other studies, in addition to the modication of the N-terminal sequence also co-express chaperones such as the ones from GroE system to facilitate the folding of CYP450, and hemA gene that encodes 5-amonolevulinic acid synthase, to overexpress the heme precursor.58,65 Although CYP450 N-terminal modications and other proteins overexpression are not straightforward and are time consuming, they can be the solution to achieve higher expression of CYP450 in microbes.

# 3.4 SAM availability

Several steps from the furanocoumarins pathway are catalyzed by OMT enzymes (e.g. B5OMT)44–47 that are SAM-dependent. SAM is obtained from the amino acid L-methionine. B5OMT in vivo assay in E. coli showed that bergapten can be produced

from bergaptol using endogenous SAM.46,47 However, several studies showed that to obtain high production of the target compounds, L-methionine should be supplemented to the culture medium or the SAM pathway should be optimized to increase SAM availability.66 SAM synthase gene (metK) is usually overexpressed.66 The gene that encodes the transcriptional repressor MetJ is oen deleted since it inhibits the expression of several methionine biosynthetic genes from the SAM pathway while the MetR gene is overexpressed since it activates the transcription of methionine synthase (MetH/MetE).67 MetJ, methionine and SAM also inhibit metA transcription and MetA activity and cysteine inhibits CysE activity. Therefore, r versions of metA and cysE $( m e t A ^ { f b r }$ and $c y s E ^ { f b r } )$ were overexpressed.66 Similar approaches were used to increase the SAM availability in S. cerevisiae. SAM synthase is encoded by SAM1 and SAM2 genes in S. cerevisiae. SAM2 gene, contrarily to SAM1 gene, is not inhibited by the accumulation of SAM. Therefore, several studies overexpress SAM2 gene in a plasmid or integrate another copy into the genome.68

# 4. Future perspectives

Furanocoumarins have demonstrated a great therapeutic potential and are used as rst-line of treatment in specic cases. The use of microbes to produce furanocoumarins is a more sustainable and environmentally friendly process. However, furanocoumarins heterologous production is still in an early stage and has a long way to go. There are some steps in the pathway whose molecular characterization is still missing. Nevertheless, recent novel enzymes discoveries are encouraging. Anyway, there is a lot of work that can be done with the steps already identied while the enzymes from the missing steps are not unravelled. Each step needs to be optimized before its assembly in the whole pathway. The missing enzymes, the challenges in the expression of the ones already identied and extender substrates availability were identi-ed in this review as the most important bottlenecks of the pathway. In the future, as some of them resolve, others will emerge and synthetic biology strategies57,69,70 will be essential to balance the pathway and optimize the production levels of furanocoumarins. Genome integration or modular co-culture engineering26 may be strategies to be considered given that the biosynthetic pathway is long and precursors and extender substrates availability also need to be optimized, thus imposing a high metabolic burden to the microorganism. The heterologous production of furanocoumarins and the exploitation of this pathway enzymes open the door to a world of unexplored furanocoumarins and may allow to nd natural or unnatural furanocoumarins with novel and interesting therapeutic properties.
