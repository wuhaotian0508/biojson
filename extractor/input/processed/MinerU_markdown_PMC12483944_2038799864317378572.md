# Summary

Saponins constitute a class of characteristic compounds within quinoa grains. In this study, metabolomic analysis identified 20 triterpene saponins, the majority of which were exclusively localized within the pericarp. Furthermore, three crucial glycosyltransferases were identified in quinoa. CqCsl2 was characterized as a 3-O-glucuronosyltransferase, CqUGT73FG1 as a 20 -O-glucosyltransferase and CqUGT74BB3 as a C-28 carboxyl glucosyltransferase involved in triterpenoid glycosylation. To elucidate the catalytic mechanisms of CqUGT74BB3, molecular docking simulations and site-directed mutagenesis experiments revealed His13 and Asp104 as critical catalytic residues. Moreover, C-28 carboxyl glycosylation of oleanolic acid was observed to occur only when the C-3 position was modified with either mono-glucuronidation or di-Oglycosylation. This specific modification strategically positions the C-28 carboxyl group in close proximity to the catalytic centre of CqUGT74BB3 (His13-Asp104), enabling the glycosylation reaction. We successfully synthesized key triterpene saponins, exemplified by ginsenoside Ro, in tobacco. These findings have significant implications for future metabolic engineering endeavours aimed at developing quinoa varieties with enhanced nutritional and medicinal attributes. 

# Introduction

Saponins are structurally complex amphipathic glycosides of steroids and triterpenoids that are widely produced by plants (Vincken et al., 2007). From a chemical structure perspective, saponins are high-molecular-weight glycosides composed of one or more sugar chains (glycons) linked to an aglycone. They exhibit diverse biological activities, such as acting as allelochemicals that inhibit competing plants and antimicrobial agents essential for plant survival (Moses et al., 2014) and deterring herbivory through their bitter taste and toxicity to animals (Augustin et al., 2011; Desai et al., 2009; Dong et al., 2020; Moses et al., 2014; Qasim et al., 2020). In clinical applications, some saponin compounds, such as QS-21 from the soap bark tree, glycyrrhizic acid from licorice, aescin from the horse chestnut tree and ginsenoside Rh1 from ginseng, have become commercially available (Jo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Reed et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2016). 

Overall, glycosyltransferases play a crucial role in the glycosylation reactions of triterpenoid saponin biosynthesis, endowing triterpenoid saponins with various functions through different 

sugar moieties. Enzymes reported to have glycosyltransferase activity include uridine diphosphate-dependent glycosyltransferases (UGTs), glycosyl hydrolase family 1 (GH1) and cellulose synthase-like enzymes (CsyGT), which are involved in the production of glycosylated triterpenoids. To date, some triterpene UGTs have been identified, some of which catalyse the initial addition of sugars to the backbone, while others facilitate further glycosyl attachment (Chen et al., 2023a, 2023b; Jozwiak et al., 2020; Jung et al., 2014; Louveau et al., 2018; Nomura et al., 2019; Orme et al., 2019; Rahimi et al., 2019; Reed et al., 2023; Shibuya et al., 2010). Notably, the glucuronidation of saponins is mediated not only by UGT family enzymes but also by cellulose synthase-like enzymes (CsyGT), which can facilitate the attachment of UDP-glucuronic acid (UDP-GlcA) at the C-3 position of saponins (Chung et al., 2020; Jozwiak et al., 2020). Moreover, two acyl-glucose-dependent glucosyltransferases belonging to glycoside hydrolase are required for the formation of saponarioside B by adding D-quinovose in soapwort tree and avenacins by adding D-glucose in oat (Jo et al., 2024; Orme et al., 2019). 

Quinoa (Chenopodium quinoa Willd.), a pseudocereal crop renowned for its high protein content and balanced amino acid profile, constitutes a valuable dietary source and exhibits considerable phenotypic diversity, with grain colours ranging from white, red and black to grey (Yang et al., 2021). A distinctive feature of quinoa seeds is the accumulation of triterpenoid saponins, secondary metabolites that impart bitterness and mild toxicity (El Hazzam et al., 2020; Kuljanabhagavad and Wink, 2009; Mad et al., 2006). Although these saponins decrease seed palatability, they confer significant agricultural benefits, including enhancing resistance to fungal pathogens, bacterial infections and insect herbivory. These natural defence properties highlight quinoa saponins as promising candidates for the development of environmentally friendly biopesticides and sustainable crop protection agents. Moreover, the bioactivities of quinoa saponins, such as antifungal, antibacterial and antiinflammatory effects, provide potential applications in food preservation and functional food industries (San Martın et al., 2008; Singh and Kaur, 2018; Stuardo and San Martın, 2008; Yao et al., 2014). The primary aglycones in quinoa are oleanane-type triterpenoids, such as oleanolic acid (OA), hederagenin (HE), phytolaccagenic acid (PA) and serjanic acid (SA) (El Hazzam et al., 2020; Kuljanabhagavad and Wink, 2009; Mad et al., 2006; Singh and Kaur, 2018). Saponin aglycones in quinoa undergo extensive modifications at the C-3 and C-28 positions. A single oligosaccharide sugar chain, typically consisting of two or three sugar units, is attached to the C-3, while a single monosaccharide is attached to the C-28. Glycosylation significantly modulates the pharmacological activity of saponins from quinoa. For instance, glucuronidation at the C-3 of OA forms calenduloside E (1, hereafter abbreviated as CE), which exhibits antidiabetic and antiarrhythmic effects (Campose et al., 2014; Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022). CE can also serve as a substrate to generate zingibroside $\mathsf { R } _ { 1 }$ (3, $\mathsf { Z R } _ { 1 }$ , 3-O- $\{ \beta$ -D-glucopyranosyl- $( 1  2 )$ - $\beta$ -D-glucopyranosiduronic acid}-OA) and ginsenoside Ro (4, GRO,3-O-{b-D-glucopyranosyl-( $\displaystyle  1  2 $ - $\beta$ -D-glucopyranosiduronic acid}-28-O- $\{ \beta$ -D-glucopyranosyl}-OA), which possess diverse glycosylation modifications with anti-HIV-1 and antithrombotic activities, respectively (Hasegawa et al., 1994; Kwon et al., 2015; Shin et al., 2019; Yoshizaki et al., 2013). 

Identification of the key enzymes involved in triterpenoid biosynthesis in quinoa is crucial for molecular breeding aimed at developing cultivars with reduced saponin content or enhancing the medicinal properties. While the role of 2,3-oxidosqualene cyclases in the biosynthesis of $\beta$ -amyrin and rare triterpene skeletons in quinoa has been established (Zhou et al., 2024), the enzymes responsible for the subsequent modification of these triterpenoids remain largely uncharacterized. Recent studies have shown that the TSARL1 mutation in quinoa specifically regulates the biosynthesis of saponins at a certain step after the 2,3-oxidation of oleanolic acid (Trinh et al., 2024). However, research on specific glycosylation modification in quinoa is still unknown. 

In this study, we quantified the total saponin content and the level of 20 triterpenoid saponins in grains from 12 quinoa varieties, including red, black, grey and white grain colours. Red grains exhibited the highest triterpene saponin contents and were selected for further investigation. The enzymatic activities of CqCsl2, CqUGT73FG1 and CqUGT74BB3, key enzymes involved in the glycosylation pathway, were characterized. 

These enzymes ultimately contribute to the biosynthesis of GRo with quinoa in vitro and in vivo. Molecular docking and site-directed mutagenesis of critical residues revealed that CqUGT74BB3 specifically catalyses the glycosylation of the C-28 position of saponins. These findings significantly advance our understanding of triterpene saponin biosynthesis in quinoa and provide valuable insights for future metabolic engineering efforts aimed at manipulating triterpene glycosylation pathways. 

# Results

# Identification, categorization and distribution of triterpenoid saponins in quinoa

To select an optimal quinoa variety for triterpenoid glycosylation studies, we first quantified the total triterpene saponin content in grains from 12 varieties representing red, black, white and grey phenotypes (Figure 1A). Red grains exhibited the highest saponin levels, ranging from 31.6 to $5 3 . 8 ~ \mu \ g / \ g$ . Black grains followed with concentrations between 19.8 and $2 7 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ , while white grains contained 3.3 to $1 0 . 8 ~ \mu \ g / \ g$ . Grey grains showed the lowest levels, ranging from 2.9 to $5 . 2 ~ \mu \ g / \ g$ (Figure 1B). To characterize the triterpene saponin composition, we identified 20 distinct saponins (Table S1, Figure S1; Figure 1C). Based on the glycosylation patterns at the C-3 hydroxyl group of the triterpene aglycone, these saponins were classified into three groups: Class I comprised 11 saponins (compounds 1–11) with glucuronic acid (GlcA) substitutions at the C-3 position; Class II contained six saponins (compounds 12–17) featuring arabinose substitutions; Class III included three saponins (compounds 18–20) with glucose substitutions (Figure 1C). 

We further analysed saponin distribution across developmental stages by profiling 15-day-old seedlings and mature grains. Compounds 1, 3 and 4 were detected in both seedlings and grains, whereas the other 17 saponins were exclusively present in mature grains (Figure 1D). Spatial metabolomics revealed that saponins were predominantly localized to the pericarp of quinoa grains (Figure 1E). 

# Identification of CqUGTs and CqCsls

By HMMsearch, 139 CqUGTs were identified in the quinoa genome (Table S2, Figure S2). Amino acid sequence alignment was performed on these 139 CqUGTs and reported UGTs involved in catalysing triterpenoid saponins (Table S3), and nine CqUGTs were identified. These nine CqUGTs and their homologous proteins with characterized functions were classified into five categories through phylogenetic analysis (Figure 2a). UGTs in Clade I and Clade V are predicted to be involved in C-3 glycosylation or sugar chain elongation of triterpenoids, including CqUGT73FG1, CqUGT73FF1 and CqUGT71F11. Clade II is probably involved in C-3/C-28 glycosylation, including CqUGT73FH1. Clade III is associated with glucosylation/arabinosylation at the C28 position of triterpenoids, including CqUGT74BB3. Clade IV is putatively responsible for glucuronylation at the C3 position of triterpenoids, including CqUGT73DN2, CqUGT73DN4, CqUGT73DN5 and CqUGT73DN6. 

Determination of expression levels of these CqUGTs indicated that CqUGT71F11, CqUGT73FF1 and CqUGT73FG1 had high expression in grains and low expression in seedlings, which was similar to the accumulation pattern of triterpenoid glycosides.

# Substrate specificity of CqGTs reveals a sequential glycosylation mechanism in quinoa saponins

To explore the substrate promiscuity of these CqGTs, we selected three classes of triterpene saponins: Class 1: aglycones (OA and 

HE), Class 2: C3 non-glucuronic acid-modified saponins (OA3- Ara) and Class 3: C3-glucuronic acid-modified saponins (CE (1), ZR1(3) and Ch Iva (2)). CqCsl2 utilizes UDP-GlcA as the sugar donor, while CqUGT73FG1 and CqUGT74BB3 use UDP-Glc. # In vivo functional validation of CqUGTs in Nicotiana benthamiana

To validate the in vivo catalytic functions of the CqUGTs, we reconstituted a triterpene biosynthetic pathway in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves via transient co-expression. As a foundational step, we introduced three upstream genes involved in oleanolic acid (OA) biosynthesis—AcOSC6, AcCYP716110 and AstHMGR —which successfully led to OA accumulation in the infiltrated leaf tissues (Figure 5A,B). Building upon this OA-producing system, co-expression of CqCsl2 resulted in the production of CE (1), confirming its function as a C-3 glucuronosyltransferase. When CqCsl2 was co-expressed with either CqUGT73FG1 or CqUGT74BB3, the pathway was extended to yield $\mathsf { Z R } _ { 1 }$ (3) or ChIVa (2), respectively, indicating each enzyme’s substrate specificity and role in further glycosylation. As anticipated, simultaneous expression of CqCsl2, CqUGT73FG1 and CqUGT74BB3 led to the production of GRo (4), a triterpene saponin with both C3 and C28 glycosylations (Figure 5B). These results collectively validate the proposed sequential glycosylation pathway and confirm the in planta enzymatic activities of the CqUGTs involved in triterpene saponin biosynthesis.

triterpenoid compounds in plants (Bonisch€ et al., 2014). Moreover, triterpenoid glycosides play a vital role in plant defence against phytophagous insects due to their bitterness and slight toxicity, and function as plant hormones that regulate growth (Osbourn et al., 2011; Sobolewska et al., 2016). Quinoa saponins comprise a complex mixture of triterpene glycosides, including HE, PA, SA, spergulagenic acid, OA and derivatives of 3b,23,30-trihydroxyolean-12-en-28-oic acid, featuring hydroxyl and carboxylic acid groups at positions C-3 and C-28 (Kuljanabhagavad et al., 2008). The triterpenoid saponins identified in quinoa in this study are primarily concentrated in grains, with extremely low levels in seedlings. The mass spectrometry imaging of grains revealed that triterpenoids predominantly accumulated in the pericarp. This is similar to oats, where their triterpenoids are primarily concentrated in the husk (Hu and Sang, 2020). This accumulation pattern plays a protective role, helping seeds resist attacks from microorganisms and herbivores, thereby increasing seed survival rates and the continuation of species (Moses et al., 2014). 

Under similar agronomic conditions, metabolite accumulation patterns are different in different varieties. For example, in black, white and red quinoa grains, accumulation patterns of amino 

acids, alkaloids, phenolic acids, flavonoids and saponins were revealed (Qian et al., 2023; Medina-Meza et al., 2016). Therefore, the identification of saponins in different quinoa varieties is of great significance for both agronomy and pharmacology. In this study, it is clarified that the red grain varieties had the highest abundance of triterpenoids followed by the black ones, and the grey ones had the lowest triterpenoid contents. Our findings are consistent with a previous research which reported that the black grain variety B2 had lower saponin content than the red one R1, and the white one W1 had the lowest saponin content (Han et al., 2019). Notably, black-grained variety B1, which does not exhibit a typical black grain phenotype, displayed higher saponin content and was therefore excluded from this analysis. 

Recent research has highlighted that the glucosylation of plant triterpenoids is not only mediated by UDP-glucosyltransferases (UGTs), but also by cellulose synthase-like enzymes CsyGTs, which have been identified in various species, including Spinacia oleracea (SoAP5), C. quinoa (CqCsl1), Beta vulgaris (BvCsl), Medicago sativa (MsCsl), Glycine max (GmCsl), Glycyrrhiza uralensis (GuCsl) and Lotus japonicus (LjCsl) (Chung et al., 2020; Jozwiak et al., 2020). These enzymes can catalyse glucuronosylation at the C3 position of OA and HE using UDP-GlcA as a donor substrate (Jozwiak et al., 2020). In this study, CqCsl2, which 

shares $9 4 . 4 1 8 \%$ identity with CqCsl1, was functionally clarified to effectively catalyse the glucuronosylation of OA and HE at the C3. Additionally, substrate promiscuity analysis revealed that CqCsl2 had specific activity for UDP-GlcA. Prior to Csls, some UGTs have been reported to catalyse the glucuronidation of triterpenoid C3. It has been found that CuUGAT in licorice catalyses the sequential glucuronidation of glycyrrhetinic acid, resulting in the formation of glycyrrhizic acid (Xu et al., 2016). Phylogenetic analysis showed that CqUGT73DN2, CqUGT73DN4, CqUGT73DN5 and CqUGT73DN6 clustered in the same Clade IV as CuUGAT. However, in vitro enzyme activity assays failed to detect their activity towards OA-3-OH glucuronidation. Given that CuUGAT has been repeatedly reported to be non-functional (Chung et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2016), the glucuronidation activity of this clade remains questionable. 

Molecular docking of CE with the CqUGT74BB3-UDPG complex revealed that His13 within the catalytic dyad forms a hydrogen bond with the C28 group of CE. This interaction facilitates deprotonation at the C-28 group, enabling nucleophilic attack on the C1 carbon of UDP-Glc, while Asp104 stabilizes the catalytic conformation. Previous studies have reported the involvement of histidine residues in proton transfer during glycosyltransferase reactions, consistent with our findings (Huang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020). In this study, CqUGT74BB3 exhibited high activity towards CE (1) and $\mathsf { Z R } _ { 1 }$ (3), both possessing C-3 glucuronides, but not towards OA and HE. Similarly, QS21 biosynthetic studies in Quillaja saponaria suggest that C-3 glycosylation likely precedes modifications at the C28. Our findings corroborate this hypothesis. Molecular docking of OA and OA-3Ara with the CqUGT74BB3-UDPGlc complex indicated that the C-28 position of OA/OA-3Ara cannot readily approach the His13-Asp104 catalytic dyad, likely hindering the glycosylation capacity of CqUGT74BB3. When OA3-Ara undergoes glycosylation to form OA3-Ara-Glu, CqUGT74BB3 shows activity in vitro. Molecular docking simulations indicate that its C-28 can also approach the His13-Asp104 catalytic dyad, consistent with the observed enzymatic activity. 

Nicotiana benthamiana possesses a sophisticated secondary metabolism system, making it an ideal model for studying the biosynthesis and metabolic engineering of natural products. The functions of triterpenoid glycosyltransferases from various plant sources have been validated using tobacco as a chassis, including the biosynthesis of Quilla jasaponaria saponins, Oleane-type triterpenes glycosylation from Saponaria vaccaria and Yossoside V biosynthesis from spinach (Chen et al., 2023; Jozwiak et al., 2020; Orme et al., 2019; Reed et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). In this study, we successfully detected OA after overexpressing above genes in tobacco. On this basis, co-injection of CqCsl2, CqUGT73FG1 and CqUGT74BB3 successfully de novo synthesized GRo and detected relevant intermediates, proving that these genes in quinoa presumably function in the biosynthesis pathway of triterpenoid saponins. 

In summary, we identified members of the CqUGT family in quinoa and characterized the functions of CqCsl2, CqUGT73FG1 and CqUGT74BB3 through in vitro and in vivo experiments. CqCsl2 was characterized as a 3-O- glucuronosyltransferase for OA or HE, while CqUGT73FG1 was identified as a glucosyltransferase or xylosyltransferase for CE (1) and Ch IVa (2). The glucosylation leads to the production of $\mathsf { Z } \mathsf { R } _ { 1 }$ (3) and GRo (4). CqUGT74BB3 was characterized as a C-28 glucosyltransferase for CE/OA3-Ara-Glc. These results reveal the sequential glycosylation process and the regularity of glycosylation products in the 

biosynthesis of triterpene saponins in quinoa. Our findings provide potential enzymatic tools for the development of medicinal triterpene compounds and candidate target genes for breeding quinoa varieties with lower saponin content for consumption. Additionally, our results offer valuable insights for metabolic engineering of complex triterpene glycosylation pathways. 

# Materials and methods

# Standards and chemical reagents

The OA (CAS:508-02-1), HE (465-99-6), CE (CAS 26020-14-4), Ch IVa (51415-02-2); ZR1 (CAS 80930-74-1) and GRo (34367-04- 9) standards were obtained from ChemFaces WuHan China. The OA-3-O-a-L-arabinopyranoside (CAS 2221000-99-1) standards were obtained from BioBioPha Co., Ltd. 

# Plant materials and extraction

The study investigated four representative quinoa varieties based on grains colour: red quinoa (009, Bolivian 300-4 and Shanxi No. 1), white quinoa (KM-3, KM-4 and KM-15), black quinoa (Yana, KM-14 and Shanxi No. 2) and grey quinoa (KM-10, KM-11 and KM-13) each representing different grain colours. Red quinoa Bolivian 300-4 grains were selected for further examination and grown in a greenhouse under controlled conditions of $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , substrate consists of 2:1:1 of peat soil garden soil and vermiculite. The plants were exposed to long-day conditions (16 h light/8 h dark), and humidity was maintained between $50 \%$ and $70 \%$ . Two weeks after sowing, 30 uniformly healthy quinoa seedlings were randomly harvested, pooled as one biological replicate, resulting in a total of three biological replicates. Both seedlings and grains were freeze-dried and ground into powder. A precise $0 . 1 5 ~ 9$ of the powder was weighed and placed into 5 mL centrifuge tubes, followed by the addition of $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $90 \%$ ethanol solution. The mixture was vortexed and sonicated at room temperature $( 1 0 0 ~ H z )$ for 3 min, with the process repeated three times. After overnight incubation at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , the solution was sonicated again for 30 min and adjusted to 2 mL with ethanol. The resulting solution was filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ microporous membrane, and the filtrate was centrifuged for 1 to collect the supernatant for further analysis. 

# Determination of total triterpene content

The dried quinoa grains are ground into powder and sieved through a 40-mesh screen. Precisely weigh 0.1 g of the powder and place it in a flask with a stopper. Add 10 mL of methanol and perform ultrasonic extraction $( 8 0 ~ \mathsf { k W } )$ for 30 min. Let it stand overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Collect the supernatant and filter it to obtain the sample solution. Prepare a stock solution of OA at a concentration of $1 \ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ . Pipette 25, 50, 100, 150, 200, 300 and $4 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of the stock solution into glass test tubes. Evaporate the solvent at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Add $2 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of $5 \%$ vanillin-acetic acid solution and $4 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of concentrated sulphuric acid to each tube. After a 15-min water bath at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , add 5 mL of glacial acetic acid. Use a sample without OA as the blank control. Measure the absorbance at $5 4 3 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ and plot the standard curve. Pipette $5 0 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of the quinoa sample solution into a glass test tube and follow the same preparation method as above. 

# Metabolomic detection and analysis

Metabolomic characterization was performed using an Agilent UPLC 1290II coupled with a G6546 time-of-flight mass 

spectrometer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). The standard conditions for both qualitative and quantitative analysis are as follows: the chromatography was conducted with an Ultimate-XB-C18 $( 3 ~ \mu \mathsf { m }$ , 300 A, $2 . 1 \times 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } )$ . The mobile phase comprised $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid solution (A) and acetonitrile (B), with a flow rate of 0.3 mL/min. The gradient elution profile was as follows: $5 \%$ B from 0 to 2 min, increasing to $1 8 . 5 \%$ B from 2 to $2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ , then to $4 1 \%$ B from 2.5 to 10.5 min, increasing to $5 9 \%$ B from 10.5 to 11 min, increasing to $7 7 \%$ B from 11 to 18 min, increasing to $100 \%$ B from 18 to 22 min and finally reaching $100 \%$ B from 22 to 24 min. The injection volume was $3 ~ \mu \iota$ . The Q-TOF-MS system operated in electrospray ionization mode, allowing both positive and negative ionization. Operational parameters included a sheath gas temperature of $3 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , a sheath gas flow rate of 11 units, and a nozzle voltage of 500 V. Capillary voltages were set at 3500 V for both ionization modes. The drying gas was heated to $3 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a flow rate of 7 L/min. The mass spectral acquisition ranged from $m / z 1 0 0$ to 1700 for MS and 50 to 1700 for MS/MS, managed by the MassHunter software version 4.1. 

Enzyme activity was assessed using an Agilent Eclipse Plus C18 RRHD column $( 2 . 1 \times 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 ~  { \mu \mathrm { m } } )$ ). The remaining conditions were unchanged, and the elution gradient was modified as follows: $5 \%$ B from 0 to 2 min, increasing to $1 8 . 5 \%$ B from 2 to 2.5 min, then to $4 1 \%$ B from 2.5 to 10.5 min, increasing to $5 9 \%$ B from 10.5 to 11 min, increasing to $100 \%$ B from 11 to 12 min and finally maintaining $100 \%$ B from 12 to 16 min. 

For triterpenoid saponins with available standards, identification was performed by comparing the retention times and MS/MS fragmentation patterns of the standards with those obtained from quinoa samples. For saponins without authentic standards, identification was achieved by interpreting their MS/MS fragmentation patterns in combination with predicted fragmentation information of putative triterpenoid structures generated using the ChemDraw software. 

# MALDI-imaging

Quinoa grains of Bolivia Red (300-4) were embedded in a mould with internal dimensions of $3 \times 2 0 \times 1 2 \mathrm { m m }$ , using a $5 \%$ CMC solution. The samples were then sectioned at $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thickness using a cryostat (‘HM550VP’, MICROM GmbH, Walldorf, Germany) and mounted on indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass slides. To enhance ionization, the samples were treated with a matrix solution containing 10 mg/mL DHB in a 1:1 (v/v) mixture of methanol and water. Other experimental parameters were based on previous protocols published by our group (Liu et al., 2024b; Nie et al., 2024). MSI analysis was performed at a spatial resolution of $1 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , and data were processed using the MirionV3 software. 

# Identification, analysis and candidates of CqCsl and CqUGT in quinoa

HMM files for the hidden Markov models (HMMs) of UDPGT (PF00201) were downloaded from the Pfam online database (http://pfam-legacy.xfam.org/). Using the Tbtools software (Chen et al., 2020), sequences containing UDPGT protein structures were retrieved from the quinoa genome (accession number: GCA_900626175.1), with duplicates and incomplete protein sequences manually excluded. The CqUGT amino acid sequence obtained was submitted to the UGT Nomenclature Committee to acquire its specific designation (https://labs.wsu.edu/ugt/). Candidate CqUGTs were compared with 107 Arabidopsis UGT family 

members from the TAIR database and 6 UGTs from maize groups O, P and Q obtained from NCBI (Figure S3) (Li et al., 2014). Phylogenetic trees were constructed using the MEGA-X software (https://www.megasoftware.net) with the maximum likelihood (ML) method and 1000 bootstrap replicates for evolutionary analysis of CqUGT. Two homologous genes named CqCsl1 and CqCsl2 were identified by homologous search based on the protein sequence GmCsl1 (BBN60789.1) that catalyses triterpene3-O-OH glucuronidation in soybean. 

# Expression profiles of UGT genes in C. quinoa

To study the expression patterns of genes related to the glycosylation triterpene saponins in quinoa, we sequenced the RNA of Bolivian 300-4 grains and seedlings using the Illumina HiSeq2000 platform. High-quality RNA was extracted from quinoa grains and seedling samples using the TaKaRa MiniBEST Plant RNA Extraction Kit. Libraries were constructed according to the manufacturer’s instructions and sequenced on the Illumina HiSeq2000 platform. The raw reads were filtered with identical parameters, and a contigs database was generated using custom Perl scripts. RNA-Seq reads from each tissue were mapped to annotated genes in the published quinoa genome (PI614886) using default parameters (Jarvis et al., 2017). The expression level of each gene was assessed by Fragments Per Kilobase Per Million mapped Fragments (FPKM), and the expression of CqUGT genes was visualized using the heatmap tool (Chen et al., 2020). The GeneBank IDs for the raw transcriptomic data of the Bolivian (300-4) quinoa grains and seeding are as follows: SAMN44078480, SAMN44078481, SAMN44078482, SAMN44078483, SAMN44078484 and SAMN44078485.

# Identification of enzyme function and kinetics

To screen the activity of CqUGT74BB3 and CqUGT73FG1 enzymes, $\mathsf { a } - 5 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L }$ enzyme assay system was prepared containing $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of 163 mM sugar donor, 1 lL of $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ substrate, $5 ~ \mu \ g$ of purified protein and 100 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5) to a final volume of $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ . The microsomal protein of CqCsl2 could not be purified. Therefore, a 150-lL enzyme assay system was prepared, containing $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of 163 mM sugar donor, $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of $1 0 \mathsf { m M }$ substrate receptor and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of microsomal protein, with the remaining volume made up with Tris–HCl (pH 7.5). Each reaction 

mixture was incubated at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and then terminated the reaction with an equal volume of methanol. After evaporation at $4 5 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ , the samples were reconstituted with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of methanol and filtered through a $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ microporous membrane for subsequent HPLC-MS/MS analysis. To analyse the kinetic parameters of recombinant proteins with different substrates, optimal reaction temperature and pH were first determined. Each $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ reaction system contained 3 mM UDP-glucose (UDP-Glc), $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ substrate (OA for CqCsl, and CE for CqUGT74BB3 and CqUGT73FG1) 100 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5) and $5 ~ \mu \ g$ of purified protein. The reaction mixtures were incubated at various temperatures (4, 10, 25, 30, 37, 45 and $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ ) for 60 min to identify the optimal temperature. For pH optimization, experiments were conducted using citric acid-sodium citrate buffer (100 mM, pH 4.5–6.5), Tris–HCl buffer (100 mM, pH 7.0–8.5) and $N a _ { 2 } C O _ { 3 } / N a H C O _ { 3 }$ buffer $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M } .$ , pH 9.0–10.0). 

Enzyme kinetic assays were then performed under the optimized pH and temperature conditions. Each $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ reaction contained $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of $1 6 3 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sugar donor, $0 { - } 4 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrate acceptor, 100 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.5) and $5 ~ \mu \ g$ of purified protein. Heat-inactivated protein $( 1 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ was used as a control. Reactions were initiated by adding the enzyme and incubated at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 30 min. All assays were performed in triplicate. Enzyme kinetics were analysed using nonlinear regression fitting with the GraphPad Prism 8 software (GraphPad). 

# Agrobacterium injection of N. benthamiana leaves

To heterologously synthesize GRo in N. benthamiana, we introduced the AcOSC6 from Aesculus chinensis, AqcCYP716110 from Aquilegia coerulea and AstHMGR from Avena sativa pEAQ-HT plasmids via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, aiming to produce OA (Sun et al., 2023). Five days post-infiltration, leaf samples were harvested for metabolite extraction and subsequently analysed using HPLC-QTOF-MS.
