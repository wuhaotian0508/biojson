# ABSTRACT

The polyacylation modification of anthocyanins constitutes a pivotal step for plants to develop persistent bright blue floral pigmentation, but the biosynthesis and regulatory mechanisms of polyacylated anthocyanin remain poorly understood. In the blue cineraria (Pericallis hybrida), an important ornamental species, a unique anthocyanin structure is characterised by 7- and $3 ^ { \prime } { \mathrm { . } }$ -polyacylated delphinidin derivatives that generate its distinctive colour. Here, we identified two modification genes PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 in the anthocyanin metabolic pathway, which determine the formation of polyacylated delphinidin derivatives in cineraria. RNAi-mediated silencing of these genes resulted in the transgenic lines exhibiting significantly reduced pigmentation, resulting in faded or pale purple colours in ray florets and leaves. Further investigation revealed that co-expression of PhF3′5′H and PhBGLU12 in the leaves of pink cineraria enabled the detection of multiple polyacylated modified delphinidin derivatives accumulating. The MYB activator PhMYB6 was shown to directly activate the transcription of PhF3′5′H and PhBGLU12, thereby promoting polyacylated anthocyanin biosynthesis. Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) and dual-luciferase assay demonstrated that PhWRKY44 influences the anthocyanin metabolic pathway by directly activating the expression of PhMYB6. Notably, PhWRKY44 expression was reciprocally regulated by PhMYB6, establishing a mutually reinforcing positive feedback loop. Functional validation demonstrated that silencing PhWRKY44 reduced anthocyanin accumulation in cineraria. These findings reveal an intricate process and regulatory module for the biosynthesis of polyacylated anthocyanins and provide genetic resources for the molecular breeding of blue ornamental varieties. 

# 1 | Introduction

Flower colour is a critical phenotypic trait in ornamental plants, exhibiting extensive naturally occurring colour variation (Noda  2018). However, pure and vibrant blue remains exceptionally scarce within angiosperm species. Due to the frequent preference for blue, flowers with blue hues are highly favoured 

in the floriculture industry (Dyer et  al.  2021). From an evolutionary perspective, blue pigments serve as visual cues for pollinators, attracting them to the flowers and thereby improving the plant's chances of reproduction (Dyer et al. 2016). While blue floral pigmentation mechanisms exhibit diversity, they all possess highly complex anthocyanin structures ensuring the chromatic stabilisation in weakly acidic or neutral environments 

(Yoshida et al. 2007). In plants with blue flowers such as gentian, delphinium and butterfly pea, the blue pigmentation arises from anthocyanins modified with multiple aromatic or aliphatic acyl groups, known as polyacylated anthocyanins (Sasaki and Nakayama 2015). The complex polyacylation modification contributes to maintaining anthocyanin stability at both low and high pH levels and improving transport efficiency (Matsufuji et al. 2007; Gamage et al. 2021). Additionally, the position and number of aromatic acyl groups in polyacylated anthocyanin can influence the pigment coloration (Nishizaki et al. 2013). 

The polyacylated anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway is intricate, involving phenylalanine and enzymes including PAL, CHS, CHI, F3H, $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ , $\mathrm { F } 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H }$ , DFR and ANS, which facilitate anthocyanin production through sequential reactions. Subsequently, glycosyltransferase (GT) and acyltransferase (AT) catalyse polyacylation at specific sites of anthocyanins in a sequential manner (Tanaka et al. 2008). Although the genes for initial anthocyanin synthesis are conserved, those responsible for further modifications exhibit species-specificity characteristics, resulting in limited research progress on advanced modification enzymes (Tasaki et al. 2019). 

Structural analysis of natural blue floral anthocyanin reveals that polyacylation modifications at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ or 7 positions of anthocyanins contribute to the blue shift in flower colour (Sasaki and Nakayama 2015; Tanikawa et  al.  2023). Among the pigments with resolved structures, cinerarin [delphinidin-3-O-(6-O-malonyl- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranoside)-7-O-(6- O - (4 - O - (6 - O -caffeoyl- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranosyl)caffeoyl)- $\cdot \beta$ -D - glucopyranoside)- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ -O-(6-O-caffeoyl- $\beta$ -D-glucopyranoside), abbreviated as Dp3MalG-7d(CafG)-3′CafG], the dominant pigment in blue cineraria, is a rare delphinidin derivative featuring concurrent polyacylation modifications at both $3 ^ { \prime }$ and 7-positions (Lu et  al.  2021; Sasaki and Nakayama  2015; Goto et  al.  1984). This structure enables cineraria to exhibit a pure and stable blue colour, implying that it contains valuable genetic resources for blue flower formation. While the structural genes responsible for anthocyanin biosynthesis in blue cineraria have been isolated (Jin et al. 2016), the modification genes involved in key positions of the polyacylated delphinidin derivative remain understudied. 

Glycosylation enhances anthocyanin solubility and stability (Alseekh et  al.  2020). The glycosylation modifications are primarily mediated by the UDP-glycosyltransferase (UGT) and $\beta$ -glucosidase (BGLU) enzymes (Sasaki and Nakayama 2015). In higher plants, the UDP-glucose flavonoid 3-O-glucosyltransferase (UFGT) typically catalyses the initial glycosylation modification at the 3-position (Muhammad et al. 2022). BGLUs demonstrate greater substrate diversity compared to UGTs; however, their roles in anthocyanin glycosylation remain underexplored. In Delphinium grandiflorum, the BGLU enzymes DgAA7GT and DgAA7BG-GT are specifically involved in 7-position glycosylation and subsequent acylated glucoside extension (Izumi et al. 2017). Similarly, AaAA7GT from Agapanthus africanus catalyses the 7-position glycosylation of anthocyanins. In  vitro enzymatic assays demonstrated that AaAA7GT represents exclusive catalytic activity towards anthocyanin receptors with a glycoside at the 3-position (Miyahara et al. 2012). 

Acylation plays a crucial role in stabilising anthocyanins at acidic pH and protecting sugar bonds from degradation (Alseekh et  al.  2020). Plant ATs are categorised into two major classes: BAHD and SCPL (Wilson et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2024). BAHD ATs employ acyl-CoA as a donor substrate to acylate various secondary metabolites, including terpenoids and flavonoids (Xu et al. 2023). In cineraria, the BAHD-type AT 3MaT transfers a malonyl group to the 3-position glucose of anthocyanins, resulting in the synthesis of anthocyanin 3-O-glucose- $\cdot 6 ^ { \prime \prime }$ -malonyl (Suzuki et al. 2003). The second class, the serine carboxypeptidase-like (SCPL) enzymes, utilise acyl-glucose as a donor substrate. SCPL ATs involved in anthocyanin acetylation have been identified in Arabidopsis (AtSAT), grape (VvSCP31), and delphinium (DgSCPL2) (Fraser et  al.  2007; Nishizaki et  al.  2013; Bontpart et  al.  2018). These vacuole-localised SCPLs play a significant role in the terminal stages of anthocyanin acetylation, catalysing the production of polyacylated anthocyanins. 

The biosynthesis of specialised metabolites, including polyacylated anthocyanins, is frequently regulated synergistically by MYB activators through synergistic interactions between transcription factor families. MYB transcription factors (TFs), particularly R2R3-MYB subgroups 5 and 6, play central roles in anthocyanin biosynthesis (Yang et al. 2022). In gentian, GtMYB3 interacts with GtbHLH1 to upregulate GtA5/3′AT expression (Nakatsuka et  al.  2008). Similarly, PpMYB9 and PpMYB10 in peach enhance the expression of glycosyltransferase genes PpUGT78A2 and PpUFGT, respectively (Zhou et  al.  2016). Notably, WRKY transcription factors coordinate with MYB to regulate pigmentation pathways via their conserved WRKY domains that bind W-box motifs(C/T)TGAC(T/C) (Dai et al. 2021). This WRKY-MYB interplay exhibits species-specific regulatory complexity. In petunias, WRKY TF TTG2 regulates PH5 expression via the AN11-AN1-PH4 complex, altering vesicle pH and consequently affecting flower colour (Verweij et  al.  2016). In Asiatic lilies, LhWRKY44 activates MYB promoters to enhance anthocyanin accumulation in response to light and drought stress (Bi et  al.  2023). Therefore, the functional integration of WRKY DNA-binding specificity and transcriptional activation of MYB and WRKY establishes a cross-family regulatory network for modulating flower colour. 

The unique structure of the primary pigment cinerarin in blue cineraria suggests its exceptional value as a genetic resource for blue-flower breeding. This pigment features rare delphinidin derivatives with polyacylation modifications at both the $3 ^ { \prime }$ and 7-positions, which are critical for its blue coloration. This study explores the molecular mechanism underlying blue floral pigmentation through polyacylated modifications. We identified two modification enzymes—the glycosyltransferase PhBGLU12 and AT PhSCPL2—that sequentially mediate 7- and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -polyacylation of delphinidin derivatives. PhBGLU12 and delphinidin-branch gene PhF3′5′H are activated by the MYB regulator PhMYB6. Upstream, we characterised a dual-function WRKY transcription factor, PhWRKY44, which directly promotes polyacylated anthocyanin accumulation in blue cineraria via activation of PhMYB6. Furthermore, PhWRKY44 and PhMYB6 reciprocally activate each other's promoters, forming a positive feedback loop that amplifies polyacylated anthocyanin biosynthesis. These findings elucidate the molecular mechanisms of blue flower formation through delphinidin derivative 

polyacylation and hierarchical regulation of polyacylated anthocyanin biosynthesis.

# 2.1 | Polyacylated Delphinidin Derivatives Determine the Pure and Stable Blue Coloration of Cineraria PeB

Cineraria exhibits a variety of flower colours, including the blue cultivar with stable and vibrant coloration. In blue cineraria (PeB), the abaxial leaf side and ray florets share a consistent colour (Lu et al. 2021), while the abaxial side of pink cineraria (PeP) remains nearly colourless. To elucidate the anthocyanins responsible for the blue coloration in flowers of PeB and to investigate pigment consistency between leaves and ray florets within the same cultivar, we employed ultra-performance liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-MS/ MS) to analyse the anthocyanin components in the two organs across different ‘Early Perfection’ cultivars. Samples were analysed at $5 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , with retention times and peak areas recorded to identify components based on chromatographic retention patterns. In the ray florets of PeP and PeB, 4–5 anthocyanin compounds were detected, while the ray florets of PeW and leaves of PeP contained negligible anthocyanin levels (Figure  1a–c). PeB leaves and ray florets showed highly consistent anthocyanin compositions, which facilitates our investigation into 

the modification and accumulation of anthocyanins in leaves (Figure 1c). 

Qualitative analysis of ray florets anthocyanins revealed that the pink cultivar (PeP) predominantly contains pelargonidinbased anthocyanins (Pg, $7 9 . 3 \%$ ), with a minor component of cyanidin-based anthocyanins (Cy, $2 0 . 7 \%$ ). The blue cultivar (PeB) mainly accumulates delphinidin-based anthocyanins (Dp, $8 4 . 6 \%$ ) and a small amount of Cy $( 1 5 . 4 \% )$ (Table S3). The results were closely aligned with those of Jin et  al.  (2016) in cineraria ‘Jester’. The mass spectrometry assay was employed to analyse the anthocyanin components. # 2.2 | PhF3′5′H, PhBGLU12, and PhSCPL2 Are Key Branch and Polyacylated Modification Genes in the Biosynthetic Pathway of Delphinidin-Based Anthocyanins

In previous work, a flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime } , 5 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase $( P h F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H )$ gene was identified in the transcriptome of the cineraria ‘Jester’, specifically expressed in blue variety ray florets (Jin et  al.  2016). In this study, its specific expression in PeB ray florets and leaves was confirmed (Figure  1a). However, the solitary expression of $P h F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H$ is insufficient to enable the blue cineraria to accumulate complex polyacylated delphinidin derivatives. Following the previous screening of genes associated with anthocyanin modification, specifically glycosyltransferase and AT (Lu et  al.  2021), PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 were candidates for further research. Their expression levels in ray florets and leaves were measured using RTqPCR. PhBGLU12 was highly expressed in PeB, and PhSCPL2 was highly expressed in all cultivars that accumulate anthocyanins (Figure  2a). Anthocyanin primary and secondary modifications occur in different organelles, with BGLUs or SCPL2s responsible for the secondary modification primarily localised in the vacuole (Sasaki and Nakayama 2015). To validate the functions of the two genes, recombinant vectors PhBGLU12-GFP and PhSCPL2-GFP were constructed for subcellular localisation analysis. The results indicated that both PhBGLU12-GFP and PhSCPL2-GFP co-localised with the Tonoplast marker (T-mCherry), suggesting that both proteins are localised to the tonoplast and may participate in the secondary anthocyanin modification in blue cineraria (Figure 2b). 

Subsequent systematic functional characterisation of the structural gene and modifying genes was conducted utilising multiple approaches. Given the similar anthocyanin composition in the ray florets and leaves of PeB, we silenced PhF3′5′H in the leaves to validate its function. A significant fading phenotype was observed after 15 days (Figure 2c). PhF3′5′H silencing led to a marked decrease in anthocyanin content (Figure 2d). Anthocyanin composition analysis using UPLC on the faded tissues demonstrated that the content of each component was significantly reduced (Figure  2e), indicating PhF3′5′H plays a crucial role in the delphinidin biosynthesis in PeB. The transient transformation assay was performed to silence PhBGLU12 in blue cineraria leaves, which caused noticeable fading in the leaves and a decrease in the expression levels of 

PhBGLU12 (Figure  2f). Compared to the control (TRV2), the content of anthocyanins B2 (Dp3MalG-7d(CafG)-3′CafG) and B3 (Cy3MalG-7d(CafG)-3′CafG) in the silenced tissues both decreased, while the content of B1 (Dp3MalG-7CafG-3′CafG) increased (Figure 2g). 

Further studies used stable transformation to silence PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 in PeB, generating a positive transgenic line (PhBGLU12-RNAi and PhSCPL2-RNAi) (Figure 3a–c). Compared to the control vector (CK), PhBGLU12 silencing caused leaf fading from the base towards the margin, leaving only a light blue colour at the leaf margin (Figure 3b). Meanwhile, the ray florets of the transgenic line changed from blue to an extremely pale purple (Figure 3b). The expression level of PhBGLU12 was significantly suppressed, with no notable effect on other genes in the PhBGLU12-RNAi line (Figure 3d). Using UPLC to analyse anthocyanin components in the PhBGLU12-RNAi line, it was observed that compared to CK, the content of anthocyanins B2, B3, and B4 all significantly decreased, while B1 increased (Figure 3e). Phylogenetic analysis has indicated that PhBGLU12 clusters with DgAA7GT from D. grandiflorum and DcAA5GT from Dianthus caryophyllus (Lu et  al.  2021), with DgAA7GT responsible for the 7-O-glycosylation modification of anthocyanins (Matsuba et al. 2010). Based on the above results, it is reasonable to infer that PhBGLU12 catalyses the second glycosylation step at the 7-position in blue cineraria (Figure 3f). 

In PeB, stable transformation silenced PhSCPL2, resulting in the transgenic line PhSCPL2-RNAi, with the ray florets turning to a lavender colour and the leaves turning to greyish-purple (Figure 3c). The anthocyanin composition structures were elucidated in detail by UPLC-MS/MS. A significant decrease was detected in all original anthocyanin components, while a new compound, B0, emerged as the main chromogenic component in the PhSCPL2-RNAi line (Figure 3e). The molecular ion peak of B0 was detected at $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z } 8 7 5$ , and it possessed a characteristic fragment ion of delphinidin at $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z } ~ 3 0 3$ . The released fragment ion at $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ 551 was indicative of the molecular ion losing one hexose (Glu) and one caffeoyl group (Caf) $( \mathrm { [ D p 3 M a l G + H ] ^ { + } } )$ ), and the fragment ion at $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ 713 was indicative of the molecular ion losing one caffeoyl group (Caf) $\mathrm { ( [ D p 3 M a l G - 7 G + H ] ^ { + } }$ or [Dp3Mal- $3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { G } + \mathrm { H } ] ^ { + } )$ (Table  S4). It is speculated that the structure of B0 is a delphinidin glycoside without acylation at the 7-position and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -position (Dp3MalG-7G- ${ \cdot } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { G }$ ). From the results obtained, it is inferred that PhSCPL2 is implicated in caffeoylation modifications at the 7- and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -positions of anthocyanins in blue cineraria (Figure 3g).

# 3.1 | The Complex Modification Structure Is Key to Pure Blue Flowers in Cineraria PeB

The blue colour of cineraria is mainly derived from cinerarin. Goto et al. (1984) determined its structure and confirmed that it is an acylated anthocyanin highly stable in neutral aqueous solutions. Our study analysed anthocyanin profiles in different cultivars, focusing on the blue cineraria PeB, to understand its unique coloration mechanism. The pigment composition analysis revealed that the major anthocyanin in PeB is consistent with previous research, exhibiting polyacylation modifications at the 7-position and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -position of delphinidin-based anthocyanin (Figure  1d). This specific structure imparts a vibrant and stable blue coloration to PeB. Similar pigments responsible for the blue coloration in flowers, such as ternatins in Clitoria ternatea and lobelinin in Lobelia erinus, are characterised by complex aromatic acyl modifications at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -or 7-positions of their respective anthocyanins (Nishizaki et al. 2013; Kazuma et al. 2004; Hsu et al. 2017). Particularly, research on Gentiana triflora indicates that the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -position caffeoyl residue on gentiodelphin contributes more to the blue coloration than the 5-caffeoyl residue (Murayama et al. 2021). Comparative analysis of the major anthocyanin present in PeP and PeB revealed similar polyacylation modifications across different cultivars. Nonetheless, the blue coloration was only achieved with delphinidin as the foundational anthocyanin. 

Many commercial cut flower species, such as roses, chrysanthemums, and carnations, have achieved purple or violet colours by introducing the exogenous $F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H$ gene (Katsumoto et al. 2007; Noda et al. 2013; Brugliera et al. 2013; Tanaka and Brugliera 2013). However, the quest for stable and pure blue pigmentation has largely remained unsuccessful, indicating the necessity for further modification of delphinidin. Co-transforming two genes, CamF3′5′H and $C t A 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } G T _ { : }$ , resulted in blue-coloured transgenic chrysanthemums, confirming that glycosylation at specific positions of delphinidin can effectively alter flower coloration to blue (Noda et al. 2017). The complex modification structure of cinerarin suggests that blue cineraria potentially contains ideal genetic resources for breeding blue-flowered cultivars. 

# 3.2 | PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 as Essential Modification Enzymes Promoting Blue Colour Formation in Delphinidin-Based Anthocyanins

Glycosyltransferases involved in flavonoid glycosylation are divided into UGTs and BGLUs. In contrast to the UGTs typically localised in the cytoplasm (Ciarkowska et  al.  2018), BGLUs associated with anthocyanin glycosylation are mainly located in the vacuole, which is consistent with our subcellular localisation results (Figure  2b). BGLUs are typically responsible for advanced glycosylation. For instance, AtBGLU10, identified in Arabidopsis, is involved in the final step of anthocyanin glycosylation at the 3-position (Miyahara et al. 2013). Silencing PhBGLU12 resulted in decreased levels of anthocyanin components B2(Dp3MalG-7d(CafG)-3′CafG) and B3 (Cy3MalG-7d(CafG)-3′CafG), alongside an increase 

in B1(Dp3MalG-7CafG-3′CafG), indicating its necessity for mediating the second step of 7-polyacylation (Figure 3b). The phenotypic changes following PhBGLU12 silencing suggest that this glycosylation modification is crucial for delphinidin derivative stability and blue shift (Figure 3e). 

Acylation also plays a significant role in enhancing flavonoid stability and protecting glycosides from degradation (Tohge et  al.  2016). PhSCPL2 effectively acylates the glucoside at the 7 and the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -position. This polyacylation process resulted in a colour change from purple to blue. Silencing PhSCPL2 through RNAi in cineraria resulted in a significant change in leaf and ray floret coloration (Figure 3c). Similar research indicates that knocking out Gt5/3′AT in gentians leads to the loss of aromatic acyl modifications at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ and 5 positions, causing the original blue flowers to turn pale mauve (Tasaki et al. 2019). 

# 3.3 | PhMYB6 and PhWRKY44 Act as Transcriptional Activators to Stimulate Polyacylated Delphinidin Accumulation in Blue Cineraria

Previous research has established that PhMYB3 and PhMYB6, both members of the SG6 R2R3-MYB, positively regulate anthocyanin accumulation in cineraria (Cui et al. 2021). However, they exhibit distinct regulatory mechanisms on downstream genes. PhMYB3 specifically targets the transporter gene PhGST3 (Cui et  al.  2021), while PhMYB6 uniquely controls both PhF3′5′H and PhBGLU12 in the current study (Figure  5). Therefore, PhMYB6 is regarded as the selective MYB TF responsible for the regulation of polyacylated anthocyanin biosynthesis. This result further elucidates the differential roles of these transcription factors in the regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis. A similar regulatory mechanism was observed in kiwifruit (Peng et al. 2019). Only AcMYB110 activated the expression of F3′H, while neither AcMYB10 nor AcMYB110 could activate $F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H$ . Moreover, the overexpression of AcMYB10 or AcMYB110 led to a significant increase in total anthocyanin content, despite low levels of delphinidin. 

WRKY TFs have been reported as important regulators in plant stress response, growth, and development. They regulate secondary metabolite accumulation, thereby affecting plant resistance (Wang, Chen, Chen, et al. 2023; Wang, Chen, Xu, et  al.  2023). For instance, WRKY8 modulates grapevine responses to UV-B/C stress by regulating stilbene biosynthesis (Mu et al. 2023), and SlWRKY50 stimulates the jasmonic acid signalling pathway to improve cold tolerance in tomato (Wang, Chen, Chen, et al. 2023; Wang, Chen, Xu, et al. 2023). In recent years, WRKY has also been considered a pivotal regulator in plant coloration. TTG2/PH3-like genes have been demonstrated to affect colour by controlling vesicle pH in grape and petunia, and by regulating proanthocyanidin transport within Arabidopsis seed coats (Verweij et al. 2016; Amato et al. 2019; Gonzalez et al. 2016). In apple, MdWRKY41 and MdWRKY40 have been involved in anthocyanin accumulation in the fruit flesh and skin, respectively (Mao et al. 2021; An et  al.  2019). In this study, by screening differentially expressed genes in the transcriptome, we identified a TTG2- like WRKY transcription factor, PhWRKY44. We identified PhWRKY44 as a gene involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, 

with its expression levels positively correlating with anthocyanin accumulation (Figure  6a). Subsequent studies have revealed that PhWRKY44 promotes anthocyanin accumulation in cineraria by positively regulating the transcription levels of PhMYB6 (Figure 6b–f). 

# 3.4 | WRKY-MYB Cooperatively Regulates the Accumulation of Polyacylated Delphinidin Derivatives in Blue Cineraria

WRKY regulation of anthocyanins is dependent on the involvement of MYB or the MBW complex, and the interactions between WRKY and MYB exhibit remarkable complexity and species-specificity. In red-skinned pears, PyWRKY26 interacts with PybHLH3 to activate PyMYB114, enhancing anthocyanin accumulation in the skin (Alabd et al. 2022). Certain WRKY TFs, such as MdWRKY10 in apple, directly target MdMYB10 as a downstream regulatory element. Meanwhile, an InDel insertion within the MdWRKY10 promoter, which contains a W-box element, further stimulates the expression of MdWRKY10, consequently increasing anthocyanin accumulation in the fruit flesh (Wang et  al.  2024). In this study, PhWRKY44 and PhMYB6 mutually activate each other's transcription, establishing a feedback amplification mechanism that further promotes anthocyanin accumulation in blue cineraria (Figure 5). This illustrates the complex and speciesspecific interactions between WRKY and MYB in regulating anthocyanin levels. Such complex feedback relationship is not uncommon in anthocyanin metabolic regulation, but predominantly occurs between anthocyanin-associated MYB activators and repressors. In apples, a feedback regulatory loop between the two ethylene-responsive MYB transcription factors MdMYB17 and MdMYB1 controls the expression of identical downstream structural genes. MdMYB17 represses MdMYB1 transcription, while MdMYB1 induces MdMYB17 transcription, thus maintaining anthocyanin biosynthesis (Wang et al. 2022). 

The polyacylation modification of anthocyanins significantly influences the coloration of plant flowers and fruits. Particularly in the polyacylation modification of delphinidin derivatives, specific modification sites and modifying enzymes are crucial for the colour and stability of blue flowers. In this study, we identified several key genes involved in the formation of polyacylated delphinidin derivatives in blue cineraria. $P h F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H$ determines the formation of the delphinidin backbone. Following primary glycosylation and acylation modifications, PhBGLU12 participates in the second glycosylation step at the critical 7-position, enhancing anthocyanin stability. PhSCPL2 is responsible for caffeoyl modifications at both the 7- and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -positions, facilitating a blue shift in flower coloration and ultimately forming the main pigment cinerarin. Upstream of these genes, PhMYB6 simultaneously activates PhF3′5′H and PhBGLU12, serving as the key MYB transcription factor regulating the biosynthesis of polyacylated delphinidin derivatives. PhMYB6 and PhWRKY44 form an amplified feedback loop through direct mutual transcriptional activation, working collaboratively to boost anthocyanin accumulation. This regulatory mechanism drives substantial accumulation of 

polyacylated delphinidin derivatives in cineraria, endowing the flowers with intensely saturated blue coloration (Figure 7). Taken together, these findings deepened our understanding of the polyacylated delphinidin biosynthesis pathway and its complex regulatory mechanisms. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

‘Early Perfection’ series of cineraria (TAKII SEED, Japan) with four colours (white, pink, and blue, abbreviated as PeW, PeP, PeB) was used in this study. This germplasm is an F1 hybrid derived from controlled crosses between wild Pericallis spp. This cultivar has undergone multiple cultivation trials, demonstrating consistent flower and leaf colour stability. The developmental stages of ray florets were classified into five stages according to Jin et  al.  (2016). Plants were grown in a controlled environment chamber under a 16 h light/12 h dark photoperiod and $6 0 \%$ humidity, with a constant temperature of $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - } 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.2 | Identification of Anthocyanins by UPLC-MS/MS

Anthocyanin extraction was performed following the method described by Lu et  al.  (2021). The samples were analysed by Acquity UPLC (Waters, Manchester, UK) with an Acquity UPLC HSS T3 column. The retention time and peak area of the samples were measured at a wavelength of $5 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ . Structural analysis of anthocyanins was performed using a XevoTQ-MS triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (Waters, Milford, USA). Mass spectrometry results were analysed using MassLynx 4.1 software. 

# 4.3 | RNA Extraction, Full-Length cDNA Isolation and RT-qPCR Analysis

Total RNA was extracted using a Quick RNA Isolation Kit (Huayueyang Biotechnology Co. Ltd., Beijing, China) and the M-MLV reverse transcription system (Promega, USA) was utilised for the reverse transcription of RNA. RT-qPCR was performed on a CFX96 real-time system (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA, USA). Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \triangle \mathrm { C T } }$ method. Primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

# 4.5 | Subcellular Localisation

PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 were respectively cloned into the pCAMBIA1300-eGFP vector and co-transformed with TmCherry (used for vacuolar membrane localisation of the fluorescent protein) into N. benthamiana leaves. GFP fluorescence signals were observed using a Leica TCS SP8 laser confocal microscope (Leica, Germany). 

# 4.6 | Virus-Induced Gene Silencing in Cineraria

Fragments of 300–500 bp from the specific nucleotide regions of full-length coding sequences (CDS) were amplified and cloned into the pTRV2 vector. The recombinant vectors, pTRV1 and pTRV2, were co-transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. Leaf infiltrations were performed as previously described (Li et al. 2020). 

# 4.7 | Generation of Transgenic Cineraria

PhBGLU12 and PhSCPL2 fragments were inserted into pART27 RNAi vector. Recombinant plasmids and the empty vector were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. The hypocotyls of cineraria (PeB) were transformed using the 

Agrobacterium-mediated method. Genomic DNA from successful transformants was extracted using Plant Genomic DNA Kit (TIANGEN, Beijing, China). The primers used for cloning and detection are listed in Table S1. 

# 4.8 | Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H) Analysis

The GAL4-based Two-Hybrid System (Clontech, USA) was employed to identify protein–protein interactions. The CDSs of PhWRKY44 and PhMYB6 were individually cloned into pGADT7 (activation domain, AD) or pGBKT7 (binding domain, BD) vectors. Recombinant plasmids were co-transformed into the Y2H Gold yeast strain and grown on SD/−Trp/−Leu, SD/− Trp/−Leu/−His/−Ade, and SD/−Trp/−Leu/−His/−Ade+X-α- Gal media at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. 

# 4.9 | Yeast One-Hybrid (Y1H) Analysis

Promoter sequences were amplified from the DNA of cineraria ray florets. Y1H assays were performed using the Yeastmaker Yeast Transformation System (Clontech, USA). The activation domain recombinant vectors and recombinant bait vectors (pAbAi) were introduced into Y1H Gold with the corresponding reporters. Transformants were selected on SD/−Leu medium. Positive 

clones were transferred to SD/−Leu medium supplemented with aureobasidin A (AbA). Yeast cells were grown at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. 

# 4.10 | Bimolecular Luminescence Complementation Assay

The CDSs of PhWRKY44 and PhMYB6 were cloned into pCAMBIA1300-cLuc (cLUC) and pCAMBIA1300-nLuc (nLUC) vectors and transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. The N. benthamiana leaves were transiently co-transformed with different combinations. 1 mmol fluorescein potassium salt was sprayed onto the abaxial surface of the leaves, and fluorescence signals were measured using the LB983 NightOwl II Molecular Imaging System. 

# 4.11 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

The CDSs were cloned into the expression vector pGreenII 62- SK (effector). The promoters were separately cloned into the pGreenII 0800-Luc vector (reporter). The recombinant vectors were introduced into the Agrobacterium GV3101-pSoup strain, and the infiltration solution containing effectors and reporters was applied to N. benthamiana leaves at equal concentrations. The activities of luciferase (LUC) and renilla luciferase (REN) were measured using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega, USA) and a GloMax 20/20 Luminometer (Promega, USA). The LUC/REN ratio was calculated to determine the influence of the transcription factor on the transcriptional activity of downstream genes. Luminescence imaging of LUC was captured using the LB983NightOwl II Molecular Imaging System (Berthold, Germany). The primers used for cloning are listed in Table S1. 

# 4.12 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay

PhMYB6-His fusion protein was expressed and purified from E. coli BL21 (DE3). The $3 ^ { \prime }$ biotin-labelled DNA probes are shown in Table  S1. Double-stranded DNA probes were formed by annealing two complementary oligonucleotides. PhMYB6-His and PhWRKY44-His proteins were mixed separately with their corresponding probes and incubated at room temperature for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) was performed using a LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Thermo Scientific, 20148) according to the manufacturer's protocol. 

# 4.13 | Statistical Analysis

The data were presented as mean $\pm \mathrm { S D }$ from three biological replicates and three technical replicates. Statistical analysis was detected using Student's t-tests; significant differences between means were defined by $^ { * * } p < 0 . 0 1$ .
