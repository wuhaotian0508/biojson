# SUMMARY

Strigolactones (SLs) are plant hormones that regulate diverse developmental processes and environmental responses. They are also known to be root-derived chemical signals that regulate symbiotic and parasitic interactions with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi and root parasitic plants, respectively. Since the discovery of the hormonal function of SLs in 2008, there has been much progress in the SL research field. In particular, a number of breakthroughs have been achieved in our understanding of SL biosynthesis, transport and perception. The discovery of the hormonal function of SL was quite valuable not only as the identification of a new class of plant hormones, but also as the discovery of the long-sought-after SL biosynthetic and response mutants. These mutants in several plant species provided us the genetic resources to address fundamental questions regarding SL biosynthesis and perception. Such mutants were further characterized later, and biochemical analyses of these genetically identified factors have uncovered the outline of SL biosynthesis and perception so far. Moreover, new genes involved in SL transport have been discovered through reverse genetic analyses. In this review, we summarize recent advances in SL research with a focus on biosynthesis, transport and perception.

Keywords: strigolactone, plant hormone, biosynthesis, transporter, receptor, symbiosis.

# INTRODUCTION

Strigolactones (SLs), a class of carotenoid-derived terpenoid lactones, were initially characterized as germination stimulants for root parasitic plants such as witchweeds (Striga spp.) (Cook et al., 1966). SLs were later identified to be root-derived symbiotic signals for arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi (Akiyama et al., 2005). In 2008, the third function of SLs, inhibition of shoot branching, was discovered (Gomez-Roldan et al., 2008; Umehara et al., 2008). SLs have been studied for over 40 years mainly from a natural products chemistry approach as allelochemicals in the rhizosphere. On the other hand, plant biologists have studied the mechanisms of shoot branching regulation from physiological and genetic approaches. These studies suggested the presence of an as-yet-unidentified shoot branching inhibitor derived from carotenoids. The discovery of SLs as plant hormones combined these separately established research fields, and significantly facilitated the studies of these multifunctional plant signaling molecules. In the past

decade, there have been a number of critical discoveries in the SL research field, particularly in the studies on SL biosynthesis, transport and perception.

The elucidation of the SL biosynthetic pathway had been a long-standing question until recently. One significant breakthrough was the discovery of an SL precursor molecule called carlactone (CL). CL was initially identified as an enzymatic product of all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene in an in vitro study (Alder et al., 2012). Later, CL was proved to be an endogenous biosynthetic precursor for SLs (Seto et al., 2014). Now, the downstream pathway from CL has also been well characterized, as described below in detail.

One significant discovery concerning SL transport was the identification of an ATP-binding cassette (ABC)-type transporter in petunia (Petunia hybrida), PLEIOTROPIC DRUG RESISTANCE1 (PDR1), which is responsible for the exudation of SLs from roots (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Recently, the involvement of its homologous genes in SL transport was also reported (Xie et al., 2015a; Banasiak et al., 2020). However, the detailed mechanisms of SL

exudation from roots and in planta translocation remain unclear.

In 2009, an SL-insensitive rice (Oryza sativa) mutant, dwarf14 (d14), was characterized, and its causal gene was found to encode an $\alpha/\beta$ -fold hydrolase superfamily protein (Arite et al., 2009). Based on the genetic and biochemical data, D14 is now widely accepted to be an SL receptor. However, its signaling mechanism is still under debate because D14 has catalytic activity to hydrolyze SLs.

In this review, we will summarize the latest knowledge regarding SL biosynthesis, transport and perception.

# SL BIOSYNTHESIS

# Chemical structures of SLs

SL was classically named after strigol and strigol-related compounds that stimulate germination of parasitic plants. Since strigol was isolated from cotton over 50 years ago, naturally occurring SLs have been isolated and identified from various plant species. The history, chemistry and distribution of SLs are extensively reviewed elsewhere (Zwanenburg and Pospisil, 2013; Wang and Bouwmeester, 2018; Yoneyama et al., 2018b). SLs are proposed to be classified into two groups, canonical and non-canonical SLs, based on their chemical structures (Figure 1). C-2' configurations of natural SLs identified so far have been $(2'R)$ . Canonical SLs have a tricyclic lactone structure composed of three rings (ABC-rings) connected to a butenolide group (D-ring) via an enol-ether bridge (Figure 1). At least the enol-ether bridge and the D-ring parts were shown to be critical for their biological activities, especially for plants (Mangnus et al., 1992; Boyer et al., 2012; Umehara et al., 2015). Canonical SLs can be further classified into strigol-and orobanchol (ORO) types based on the stereochemistry of the C-ring (they have a $\beta$ - and an $\alpha$ -oriented C-ring, respectively) (Figure 1). In contrast, non-canonical SLs lack typical ABC-rings but possess both an enol-ether bridge and D-ring moieties (Kim et al., 2014; Ueno et al., 2014; Charnikhova et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2019) (Figure 1). In this section, recent understandings of SL biosynthesis will be summarized.

# Core pathway of SL biosynthesis

Before SLs were identified as a novel class of plant hormones regulating shoot branching, experiments using carotenoid-deficient mutants and carotenoid biosynthetic inhibitors have suggested that SLs are biosynthesized through the carotenoid pathway (Matusova et al., 2005). Subsequently, a series of recessive mutants with increased shoot branching greatly helped us to understand SL biosynthesis (Dun et al., 2009; Beveridge and Kyozuka, 2010). These mutants include Arabidopsis more axillary growth (max), pea (Pisum sativum) ramosus (rms), petunia decreased apical dominance (dad) and rice dwarf/high

tilling dwarf (d/htd), which have been obtained in genetic studies that aimed to reveal a novel shoot branching regulator other than classical hormones such as auxin and cytokinin (CK). First, the Arabidopsis max3 and max4, the pea $rms5$ and $rms1$ and the rice $d17$ and $d10$ mutants, which are defective in CAROTENOID CLEAVAGE DIOXYGENASE 7 (CCD7) and CCD8, respectively, were revealed to be SL-deficient mutants (Gomez-Roldan et al., 2008; Umehara et al., 2008). Then, the involvement of novel iron-binding proteins, rice D27 and Arabidopsis AtD27, in SL biosynthesis was reported (Lin et al., 2009; Waters et al., 2012a). In 2012, Alder et al. conducted an excellent biochemical analysis in vitro by using recombinant D27, CCD7 and CCD8 proteins from Arabidopsis, pea and rice (Alder et al., 2012). They showed that sequential reactions by these three enzymes produce a non-canonical SL-like compound, carlactone (CL), from all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene via 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene and 9-cis- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal (Alder et al., 2012) (Figure 2). The catalytic characteristics of D27, CCD7 and CCD8 enzymes were reviewed in detail recently (Jia et al., 2018). Later, CL was shown to be the endogenous compound in Arabidopsis and rice by liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) analysis (Seto et al., 2014). Moreover, feeding experiments using stable isotope-labeled CL demonstrated that CL is converted to 4-deoxyorobanchol (4DO) and ORO, both of which are endogenous canonical SLs in rice, suggesting that CL is an intermediate of SL biosynthesis in vivo (Seto et al., 2014). Importantly, the absolute configuration of endogenous CL at the C-11 position in the D-ring, which corresponds to C $2^{\prime}$ in natural SLs, was determined to be (11R) (Seto et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2014) (Figure 2). Thus, the stereospecificity of the CCD8-catalyzed reaction to yield (11R)-CL is considered to be critical for the stereochemistry of SLs.

The CYP711A subfamily of cytochrome P450 oxygenases functions in converting CL into both canonical and noncanonical SLs in vascular plants. The involvement of CYP711A in SL biosynthesis was first implicated in Arabidopsis. It was shown that the Arabidopsis max1 mutant, defective in CYP711A1, exhibited the hyper-branching phenotype as max3 and max4 (Stirnberg et al., 2002). Grafting experiments have suggested that MAX1 functions downstream of MAX3 and MAX4 (Booker, 2005). In addition, the max1 mutant showed a drastic increase in the endogenous CL level, suggesting that CL is a substrate for MAX1 (Seto et al., 2014). In 2014, two groups reported the functions of CYP711A1/MAX1 in Arabidopsis and CYP711A2/Os900 and CYP711A3/Os1400 in rice (Abe et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2014). In Arabidopsis, the recombinant MAX1 protein expressed in yeast microsomes was shown to convert CL into carlactonoic acid (CLA) via 19-hydroxy-CL, suggesting that MAX1 catalyzes three-step oxidations of the methyl group at the C-19 position to a carboxylic acid (Abe et al., 2014) (Figure 2). Future analysis of CYP722Cs from various plants will give us clues to understand the diversity of canonical SLs and their biological functions.

# Non-canonical SL biosynthesis

For non-canonical SLs, methyl carlactonoate (MeCLA) can be a key intermediate because some non-canonical SLs such as zealactone/methyl zealactonoate from maize, heliolactone from sunflower (Helianthus annuus), and lotuslactone from L. japonicus have the same chemical substructure as MeCLA (Ueno et al., 2014; Charnikhova et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2019) (Figures 1 and 2). MeCLA was first discovered in Arabidopsis (Seto et al., 2014). Feeding experiments demonstrated that MeCLA was produced from CLA in planta (Abe et al., 2014). Moreover, MeCLA could inhibit the shoot branching phenotype of both max1 and max4 and could shift the melting temperature of AtD14 protein (see below), suggesting that MeCLA can act as a branch-inhibiting hormone in Arabidopsis (Abe et al., 2014). MeCLA was reported to be further metabolized by LATERAL BRANCHING OXIDOREDUCTASE (LBO), a 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase (2OGD), in Arabidopsis (Brewer et al., 2016). The enzymatic product of

LBO is likely more active in inhibiting shoot branching as the lbo mutant had increased levels of MeCLA but showed a weaker but significant hyper-branching phenotype compared with the max4 mutant (Brewer et al., 2016). More recently, the LBO product has been identified to be hydroxymethyl-carlactonoate (1'-OH-MeCLA) though the incubation of LBO with MeCLA produced more CLA than 1'-OH-MeCLA (Yoneyama et al., 2020a) (Figure 2). Maize, sorghum and tomato LBO homologs were also shown to catalyze the same enzymatic reaction as Arabidopsis LBO. It is likely that 1'-OH-MeCLA is unstable and readily converted to CLA enzymatically or non-enzymatically (Yoneyama et al., 2020a). To improve our understanding of the bioactive form of SLs, it will be important to clarify whether the main function of LBO is the production of 1'-OH-MeCLA or the demethylation of MeCLA.

In L. japonicus, it was recently shown that 18-hydroxy-MeCLA, which is likely produced from MeCLA or 18-hydroxy-CLA, was converted to lotuslactone (Mori et al., 2020b). This finding supports the idea that MeCLA and its derivatives are probable precursors of non-canonical SLs. Additionally, the same group identified a novel 2OGD named LOTUSLACTONE-DEFECTIVE (LLD) (Mori et al., 2020a). The $LLD$ -defective mutant could not produce lotuslactone, suggesting that LLD is involved in lotuslactone biosynthesis probably downstream of MeCLA or $1^{\prime}$ -OH-MeCLA. The discovery of novel metabolites or the identification and functional analysis of new biosynthetic enzymes, including a methyltransferase converting CLA to MeCLA, will provide further insight into non-canonical SL biosynthesis. Recently identified hydroxyl (3-OH-, 4-OH- and 16-OH-)CL derivatives may be candidates as in vivo substrates for LBO and LLD enzymes (Baz et al., 2018; Yoneyama et al., 2020a).

# REGULATION OF SL BIOSYNTHESIS

# Nutritional regulation

Various types of regulation of SL biosynthesis have been revealed recently. One of the important regulators of SL biosynthesis is the signal of phosphorus deficiency. Root exudates from phosphorus-deficient plants have been reported to stimulate hyphal elongation and branching of AM fungi (Elias and Safir, 1987; Nagahashi and Douds, 2000). These observations are consistent with the fact that colonization with AM fungi enables host plants to efficiently take up inorganic nutrients, particularly phosphorus, from soil (Harrison, 2005). Then, SLs were identified as symbiotic signals that can induce hyphal branching and stimulate cell proliferation and spore germination in AM fungi. These findings indicate that SLs have a critical role in nutrient uptake via AM fungi in the rhizosphere (Akiyama et al., 2005; Besserer et al., 2006). Subsequent LC-MS/MS analyses of root exudates have shown that the

levels of SLs are significantly increased under phosphorus-deficient conditions in many plant species (Andreo-Jimenez et al., 2015). Moreover, nitrogen deficiency has been shown to increase SL levels, but this type of regulation is likely dependent on plant species (Yoneyama et al., 2012; Andreo-Jimenez et al., 2015). These observations suggest that low nutrient-dependent SL production is closely related to the nutrient acquisition strategy of plants.

At the molecular level, it was shown that gene expression of D27, CCD7/D17, CCD8/D10, CYP711A2/Os900 and CYP711A5/Os1900 was regulated by phosphorus availability and well correlated with endogenous 4DO levels in rice roots (Umehara et al., 2010). In the legume Medicago truncatula, the GRAS-type transcription factors NODULATION SIGNALING PATHWAY1 (NSP1) and NSP2 were shown to be involved in the upregulation of MtD27 under phosphorus-deficient conditions (Liu et al., 2011). Microarray analysis revealed that MtD27 and CYP711A12/MtMAX1 were downregulated in the roots of both nsp1 and nsp2 knockout mutants. Moreover, it was demonstrated that MtD27 expression and SL production in the phosphate-starved roots were severely decreased in nsp1 and nsp2 mutants. The nsp1 nsp2 double mutant was also shown to exhibit the reduced infection of AM fungi (Liu et al., 2011). Interestingly, NSP1 and NSP2 orthologs were shown to be involved in SL biosynthesis in non-legume rice plants. The 4DO and ORO levels in exudates of phosphate-starved roots were significantly decreased in the rice nsp1 nsp2 double knockdown lines, in which the transcripts of D27 were greatly reduced (Liu et al., 2011). A recent study has also shown that mycorrhization-induced expression of SI/IAA27, encoding an AUXIN/INDOLE-3-ACETIC ACID (AUX/IAA) protein that represses auxin signaling, positively regulates gene expression of SI-NSP1, SI-D27 and CYP711A21/ SI-MAX1 in mycorrhized and non-mycorrhized roots of tomato (Guillotin et al., 2017). These observations suggest that NSP1/2-mediated control of gene expression of D27 and probably CYP711A may be widely conserved in various plants, and this module may have an essential role in the phosphorus starvation response to establish symbiosis with AM fungi. More recently, the rice D14-LIKE pathway, which is paralogous to the SL signaling pathway and indispensable for AM symbiosis, has been shown to positively regulate SL biosynthesis (Choi et al., 2020). Intriguingly, NSP2 expression was induced by D14-LIKE signaling, indicating that the D14-LIKE pathway acts upstream of NSP2 in regulating SL synthesis (Choi et al., 2020). It will be an interesting subject to clarify whether the D14-LIKE pathway is involved in SL production under phosphorus-deficient conditions.

# Feedback regulation

SL biosynthesis is also regulated by its own signaling. Generally, the endogenous levels of plant hormones are finely

and tightly maintained by feedforward and feedback regulation to maintain their homeostasis. In SL biosynthesis, CCD7 and CCD8 are likely conserved target sites of negative feedback regulation. Gene expression analyses have shown that the transcript levels of CCD7 and CCD8 genes were increased in SL-deficient and SL-insensitive mutants in several plant species like Arabidopsis, pea, petunia and rice (Foo et al., 2005; Snowden et al., 2005; Johnson et al., 2006; Arite et al., 2007; Umehara et al., 2008; Drummond et al., 2009; Hayward et al., 2009; Mashiguchi et al., 2009). The upregulation of CCD7 and CCD8 was reversibly canceled by exogenous application of GR24 (Figure 1), a synthetic SL analog, in wild-type (WT) and SL-deficient plants (Umehara et al., 2008; Mashiguchi et al., 2009). Besides, AtD27 was shown to be under the feedback regulation in Arabidopsis as it was upregulated in max mutants (Waters et al., 2012a). LC-MS/MS analyses also revealed that the levels of 4DO and SL biosynthetic intermediates such as CL and CLA were drastically increased in SL-insensitive mutants of Arabidopsis or rice (Umehara et al., 2008; Arite et al., 2009; Abe et al., 2014; Seto et al., 2014). These pieces of evidence strongly support the idea that SL biosynthesis is controlled by negative feedback regulation.

# Hormonal and other regulations

SLs have been implicated to interact with other plant hormones. The effect of auxin on SL biosynthesis has been an important subject because auxin plays a crucial role in apical dominance and inhibits axillary bud outgrowth. Auxin has been shown to induce gene expression of $CCD7$ and $CCD8$ in Arabidopsis, pea and rice (Johnson et al., 2006; Zou et al., 2006; Arite et al., 2007; Hayward et al., 2009; Xu et al., 2015). In Arabidopsis, auxin was demonstrated to increase the expression of $CCD7/MAX3$ and $CCD8/MAX4$ in the basal cauline internodes (Hayward et al., 2009). The transcript levels of $MAX3$ and $MAX4$ were significantly reduced in the auxin-resistant iaa12/bodenlos mutant, which exhibits constitutive repression of auxin signaling. Moreover, the hyper-branching phenotype of the iaa12 mutant was rescued by exogenous application of GR24 to rosette axils (Hayward et al., 2009). Similarly, GR24 could reduce the increased branching of auxin signaling mutants in Arabidopsis (Brewer et al., 2009). These results indicate that auxin can act upstream of SL action by upregulating $CCD7$ and $CCD8$ genes.

Another hormone that can control SL biosynthesis is CK, which has been proposed to function antagonistically with SL (Dun et al., 2011). CK was demonstrated to inhibit auxin-inducible MAX4 expression in the roots of Arabidopsis (Bainbridge et al., 2005). In rice, benzyladenine, a synthetic CK analog, was shown to repress gene expression of D27, D17 and D10 in roots, tiller nodes and tiller buds (Xuet al., 2015). More recently, it was reported that exogenously applied natural and synthetic CKs locally

suppressed the levels of 4DO in phosphorus-deficient roots of rice, though the regulations of SL biosynthesis by CK and phosphorus availability signaling are likely independent (Yoneyama et al., 2020b). Moreover, CYP711A2 and CYP711A3, as well as D27, D17 and D10, were shown to be downregulated by CK (Yoneyama et al., 2020b). These observations indicate that CKs negatively control SL biosynthesis.

In addition to auxin and CK, gibberellin (GA) has been demonstrated to severely suppress SL biosynthesis in rice and L. japonicus (Ito et al., 2017; Mori et al., 2020a). In rice, it was shown that the application of bioactive GAs even at $100\mathrm{pm}$ drastically decreased 4DO and ORO levels in roots (Ito et al., 2017). GA suppressed SL production via the GIBBERELLIN INSENSITIVE DWARF1 (GID1)-DELLA signaling pathway and downregulated SL biosynthetic genes such as D27, D17, D10, CYP711A2 and CYP711A3. Moreover, GA-deficient Tan-ginbozu rice, in which the 4DO level was higher than that in the control plant, was shown to be insensitive to GR24 with respect to tiller bud length (Ito et al., 2017). It is possible that GA signaling participates in SL-mediated inhibition of tiller bud outgrowth in rice. Transcriptome analysis also showed that SL biosynthetic genes such as MAX4 and LBO were downregulated by GA treatment in Arabidopsis (Lantzouni et al., 2017). Additional studies will reveal the physiological meaning of the crosstalk between GA and SL.

More recently, it has been demonstrated that SL biosynthesis is regulated by zaxinone, a novel endogenous carotenoid-derived molecule, in Arabidopsis and rice (Wang et al., 2019; Ablazov et al., 2020). In rice, zaxinone negatively regulates SL biosynthesis in roots under both normal and phosphorus-deficient conditions. In the phosphate-starved roots, zaxinone was shown to downregulate the transcript levels of D27, D17, D10 and CYP711A2 independently of SL signaling (Wang et al., 2019). By contrast, in Arabidopsis roots, zaxinone induced MAX3 and MAX4 under normal and phosphate-starved conditions. The increased MeCLA levels were also observed in zaxinone-treated roots (Ablazov et al., 2020).

Multiple developmental signals and environmental stimuli also regulate SL biosynthesis. For example, the transcription of MAX1, MAX3 and MAX4 was induced during leaf senescence in Arabidopsis (Ueda and Kusaba, 2015). Also, SL biosynthetic mutants exhibited a delayed senescence phenotype, and the application of GR24 induced leaf senescence, suggesting that SLs synthesized in older leaves function in leaf senescence in Arabidopsis (Ueda and Kusaba, 2015). Moreover, abiotic stresses have been shown to regulate SL biosynthesis in several plant species (Ha et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2015; Visentin et al., 2016). Because SLs have been shown to possess various functions as plant hormones and allelochemicals, future studies will reveal the molecular mechanism controlling the

transcriptional or post-transcriptional regulation of SL biosynthesis.

# SL TRANSPORT

# Root-to-shoot movement of SLs

Plants have evolved the transport systems of endogenous hormones to coordinate their growth and to adapt to environmental conditions (Borghi et al., 2015; Park et al., 2017). SLs function as symbiotic signals for AM fungi as mentioned above, and they are exuded from roots into the soil, especially under low phosphorus or nitrogen conditions (Andreo-Jimenez et al., 2015). SLs also function as long-distance signals from roots to shoots. Grafting experiments by using dad, max and rms mutants have suggested that SLs produced locally in the shoot are sufficient to suppress axillary bud elongation because WT scions grafted onto rootstocks of SL-deficient mutants exhibited the normal phenotype (Napoli, 1996; Beveridge, 2000; Turnbull et al., 2002). However, it was shown that WT rootstocks could rescue the hyper-branching phenotype of scions of SL-deficient mutants, suggesting that SLs or their biosynthetic precursor(s) synthesized in roots can move acropetally to shoots. As SLs are actively produced in roots in phosphate- and nitrogen-limited conditions, root-derived SLs in the shoot are suggested to be signals integrating the shoot architecture and nutrient availability in the soil (Umehara et al., 2010; Kohlen et al., 2011; de Jong et al., 2014).

There are possible candidates for the mobile form of SLs. The grafting experiment between SL-deficient mutants enables us to speculate the movement of SL biosynthetic intermediates. Booker et al. showed that max1 rootstocks could rescue the shoot branching phenotype of max4 scions, suggesting that CL can be a mobile signal because CL is the only known intermediate downstream of MAX4 and upstream of MAX1 (Booker, 2005). In addition, CL accumulated at an extremely high level in the max1 mutant (Seto et al., 2014). It was also reported that max1 but not max4 rootstocks could rescue the shoot branching phenotype of Atd27 scions, suggesting that 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene and 9-cis- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal are not mobile signals (Lin et al., 2009; Waters et al., 2012a). These physiological data are reasonable because the biosynthesis of SLs from all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene to CL is operated in plastids (Auldridge et al., 2006; Arite et al., 2007; Lin et al., 2009; Waters et al., 2012a) (Figure 3). Moreover, it was also demonstrated that the branching phenotype of max1 scions was recovered by Ibo rootstocks, suggesting that CLA, MeCLA and/or their unknown metabolites can be a long-distance mobile signal (Brewer et al., 2016). In addition to physiological studies, LC-MS/MS analysis of the xylem sap provides a clue to consider the movement of SLs. # ABCG/PDR-mediated SL transport

In 2012, a reverse genetic approach revealed that PDR1, a G-type ABC transporter, plays a role in transporting SLs in petunia (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Strong expression of PDR1 was observed at hypodermal passage cells (HPCs) in

roots, where the hyphae of AM fungi penetrate during mycorrhization. PDR1 expression in roots was also upregulated in response to phosphate starvation and colonization with AM fungi, indicating that PDR1 functions in releasing SLs from HPCs into the rhizosphere (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Actually, the $pdr1$ mutant significantly decreased the levels of ORO in root exudates and displayed reduced interaction with AM fungi (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Later, subcellular localization analysis revealed that PDR1 is localized at the apical membrane of hypodermal cells in the root tip and at the outer lateral membrane of HPCs above the root tip (Sasse et al., 2015). The movement of exogenously applied isotope-labeled GR24 in the $pdr$ mutant was much slower than that of WT (Sasse et al., 2015). Because DAD1/CCD8 is expressed in cortex cells of the root tip, PDR1 is proposed to play a dual role; SL shootward transport from root tips where SL is biosynthesized and SL export toward the soil from HPCs above the root tip (Sasse et al., 2015) (Figure 3). More recently, it was reported that MtABCG59/MtPDR23, a homolog of PDR1, is expressed in the root tip and the cortical cells in M. truncatula, which lacks HPCs (Banasiak et al., 2020). Similar to those of the petunia PDR1, transcript levels of MtABCG59 were increased under phosphate-limiting conditions. Interestingly, as observed in the $pdr1$ mutant, the MtABCG59-defective mutants were shown to reduce the colonization rate of AM fungi compared with WT (Banasiak et al., 2020). These findings suggest the involvement of PDR-type transporters in SL export from roots during mycorrhization in

Solanaceae and Fabaceae species despite the anatomical differences of roots.

In addition to SL transport in the root tissue, PDR1 and its homologs may have a role in SL transport in the aerial parts (Figure 3). The $pdr1$ mutant was reported to exhibit a hyper-branching phenotype (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Moreover, a knockdown line of NtPDR6, a close homolog of PDR1 in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum), has more secondary stems with lateral branches compared with WT (Xie et al., 2015a). PDR1 is shown to be expressed at the vasculature and nodes near leaf axes but not in the dormant buds (Kretzschmar et al., 2012). Similarly, NtPDR6 expression was observed in nodes at lower parts of the stem (Xie et al., 2015a). Their mutant phenotypes and expression patterns indicate that the PDR-type transporters function in SL transport for inhibiting shoot branching in Solanaceae species. However, it remains to be discovered which molecules are substrates for PDR1. ORO is one of the promising candidates because ORO levels in root exudates were decreased in the $pdr1$ mutant (Kretzschmar et al., 2012; Wakabayashi et al., 2019). Alternatively, PDR1 may be a multi-substrate transporter for SLs and their biosynthetic intermediates. To improve our understanding of the transport system of SLs, it is crucial to reveal the substrate specificity of PDR1 and its orthologs. Furthermore, as PDR-type transporters are conserved beyond Solanaceae and Fabaceae species (Nuruzzaman et al., 2014; Borghi et al., 2015), it is intriguing to examine whether the transport of SLs by PDR-type transporters is general in other plants.

# SL PERCEPTION

# Identification of the SL signaling genes from SL-insensitive mutants

When the hormonal function of SL was discovered, some shoot branching mutants were found to be SL biosynthetic mutants, whose phenotypes could be restored by exogenous SL treatment (Gomez-Roldan et al., 2008; Umehara et al., 2008). On the other hand, the Arabidopsis max2, the rice $d3$ and the pea rms4 mutants were characterized to be SL-insensitive. The causal genes of these three mutants from different plant species encode orthologous F-box proteins in the same genetic clade. Thus, it was suggested that SL signal transduction includes a process of the ubiquitination-dependent target protein degradation, as is often seen in the signaling pathways of other plant hormones, such as auxin, GA and jasmonates (JAs) (Gray et al., 2001; Ueguchi-Tanaka et al., 2005; Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007). In the case of auxin and JAs, the F-box proteins TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE1 (TIR1) and CORONATINE INSENSITIVE1 (COI1), respectively, have a critical role in the perception of the corresponding ligand molecule. TIR1 and COI1 form a co-receptor complex with a repressor

of signaling, AUX/IAA and JASMONATE ZIM-domain (JAZ), respectively (Kepinski and Leyser, 2005; Lin et al., 2009; Sheard et al., 2010). Thus, MAX2/D3/RMS4 was initially expected to be directly involved in SL perception. However, in 2009, another SL-insensitive mutant, $d14$ , was characterized from rice, and it was found that the causal gene encodes an $\alpha/\beta$ -fold hydrolase superfamily protein (Arite et al., 2009). Curiously, GID1, a soluble receptor of GA also belongs to the same superfamily (Ueguchi-Tanaka et al., 2005). Thus, the discovery of D14 allowed researchers to predict that D14, but not MAX2/D3/RMS4, might function as a receptor in the SL signaling pathway. However, there was a critical difference between D14 and GID1; D14 possesses the conserved catalytic amino acids, including Ser, His and Asp (catalytic triad), whereas the His residue is replaced by Val in the GID1 sequence, in agreement with its non-catalytic ability. From this observation, it was also anticipated that D14 acts as a biosynthetic enzyme that converts SL into other metabolite(s), which might be an active form of the hormone in the shoot branching inhibition pathway. The identification of the $d14$ mutant highly attracted the interest of many researchers, and thereafter this research area became highly competitive.

# Biochemical function of D14

In 2012, the first report on the biochemical analysis of the D14 family proteins was published by Hamiaux et al. using the petunia ortholog DECREASED APICAL DOMINANCE2 (DAD2) (Hamiaux et al., 2012). They solved the crystal structure of DAD2, and demonstrated that the catalytic triad is structurally conserved in DAD2. The results also showed that DAD2 has an internal hydrophobic cavity likely to be large enough to accommodate an SL molecule. They also found that DAD2 catalyzes the hydrolysis of an SL synthetic analog, GR24, resulting in the production of two molecules, ABC formyltricyclic lactone (ABC-FTL) and hydroxymethylbutenolide (HMB) (Figure 4a). Later, this hydrolytic degradation of SL was confirmed to be a common reaction catalyzed by the D14 family proteins (Zhao et al., 2013; de Saint Germain et al., 2016; Seto et al., 2019). In addition, they adopted a method called differential scanning fluorimetry (DSF), which allows us to evaluate protein-chemical interactions by measuring the chemical-inducible melting temperature (Tm) shift of the target protein, in order to analyze the DAD2-SL interaction. They found that GR24 lowered Tm of DAD2. Interestingly, this Tm shift was not observed with two catalytic triad mutants, DAD2:S96A and DAD2:H246A, both of which had significantly decreased hydrolase activity. Moreover, DAD2:96A did not complement the dad2 mutant phenotype, implicating that the catalytic function of DAD2 is correlated with its in vivo shoot branching inhibition. They also found that DAD2 interacts with the MAX2 ortholog in petunia (PhMAX2) in an SL-dependent manner using yeast two

hybrid assays. In this report, it was not clearly demonstrated whether DAD2 acts as an SL receptor or not, because of its catalytic function with the ligand molecule. Moreover, the necessity of the catalytic function of DAD2 for its shoot branching inhibition was not conclusively proved.

In 2013, Nakamura et al. reported a structure of the rice D14 protein obtained by soaking a D14 crystal with an SL analog, $(-)$ -ent-2'-epi-GR7 (0.2 M) (Nakamura et al., 2013). They found that one of the hydrolyzed products, HMB, bound at the entrance region of the ligand binding pocket. They also found that application of HMB alone at high concentration could weakly inhibit shoot branching of the SL biosynthetic mutant. According to these results, they proposed that the D14-HMB complex would have a critical role in transmitting the SL signal. However, there has been no evidence to support the notion that the D14-HMB complex is involved in the SL signaling pathway.

In late 2013, there was a significant breakthrough in this research field; D53, a repressor of SL signaling, was identified and characterized (Jiang et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2013). D53 was characterized from a rice SL-insensitive dominant mutant, and was found to interact with D14 in an SL-dependent manner. Moreover, the D53 protein was shown to be degraded through the 26S proteasome pathway in a manner that requires the function of the F-box protein D3. # Hydrolysis-dependent signaling models

In 2016, Yao et al. reported a non-canonical hormone perception model in which a covalently linked hydrolysis intermediate molecule of SL has a critical role in promoting the interaction of D14 with the F-box protein D3 (Yao et al., 2016) (Figure 5a). They solved the crystal structure of the SL-inducible signaling complex composed of Arabidopsis D14 (AtD14), rice D3 (F-box) and Arabidopsis Skp1 (ASK1). Intriguingly, in this complex the conformation of AtD14 was drastically altered compared to its apo structure. In the apo structure of AtD14, four $\alpha$ -helices form a V-shaped lid structure, and a loop containing the Asp residue of the catalytic triad (Asp loop) exists between this V-shaped structure. They contribute to the formation of a ligand binding pocket with a reasonable size for SL molecules (Figure 5c). The catalytic Ser is present at the bottom of the pocket, and it was expected that the methyl butenolide part (D-ring) of SL is the target of the nucleophilic attack by the Ser residue (Kagiyama et al., 2013). In the conformationally altered AtD14 structure, the V-shaped lid had a closed state, and the electron density for the Asp loop was completely missing, suggesting that this region becomes highly disordered. The closed conformation of the V-shaped lid resulted in the significant decrease in the ligand binding pocket size, but the authors found an extra electron density in this smaller pocket. They considered this electron density to be a covalently linked reaction intermediate structure derived from the SL D-ring part (Figure 5a,c). Furthermore, by LC-MS/MS analysis, they found that the His residue of the catalytic triad is modified with a D-ring-derived C5 unit molecule. Taken together, the authors proposed that the SL-derived D-ring part is covalently linked to form a bridge between the Ser and His residues in the crystal structure. They concluded that the covalently linked intermediate molecule, which they named CLIM, plays a critical role in triggering the conformational change of D14, which is necessary for the complex formation with D3 (Figure 5a). # Hydrolysis-independent signaling models

In 2019, Seto et al. reported another model, in which the intact SL molecule has a critical role in establishing the active signaling state of D14 (Seto et al., 2019) (Figure 5b). They found that the Tm shift of D14, which was examined by DSF experiments, was induced by bioactive SL molecules, but not by inactive SL analogs. They performed a time-course DSF experiment coupled with hydrolysis monitoring to dissect the chemical signal that induces the Tm shift. The data indicated that the uncleaved SL level is highly correlated with the degree of the Tm shift of D14. This result strongly suggests that the Tm shift is induced by the intact SL molecule. Seto et al. also showed that a

catalytically inactive AtD14 $^{\text{D218A}}$ mutant was able to complement the atd14 mutant phenotype in an SL-dependent manner, demonstrating that the hydrolytic degradation of SL by D14 is not necessary for its signal transduction. As mentioned above, Yao et al. reported that the size of the active site is drastically reduced after the conformational change of AtD14. However, Seto et al. found that the disordered conformational change of the Asp loop resulted in enlargement of the active site pocket to a different direction, and this newly formed pocket was shown to have enough capacity to accommodate the intact SL molecules (Figure 5d). Taken together, they concluded that the conformational change of D14 is triggered by intact SL, but not the hydrolysis intermediate. The conformational change of D14 once leads the disruption of the catalytic triad formation, whereas this form is the active state for SL signaling. Moreover, Seto et al. proposed that after the signal transduction, D14 returns to the apo form, which is enzymatically active, and deactivates the SL molecules by hydrolytic degradation (Figure 5b). Thus, D14 would be a dual-functional receptor that is responsible for both the perception and the deactivation of the bioactive SL molecules.

Around the same time, Shabek et al. reported a different model based on the two different conformations of the D3 F-box protein which were characterized by X-ray crystallographic analyses (Shabek et al., 2018). In one of these two states, the C-terminal helix (CTH) domain has a more flexible conformation, and this domain was found to interact with D14 in an SL-dependent manner. The hydrolysis activity of D14 is inhibited by the interaction with D3 CTH, which was found to be re-activated in the presence of D53. They solved the crystal structure of the D14-D3 CTH domain complex, which was induced by SL, and proposed the presence of the intact SL molecule in the active site of D14. Thus, they also proposed that the signal transduction does not require SL hydrolysis. However, they concluded that the signaling complex formation process does not require the conformational change of D14, which was different from the model proposed by Seto et al.

In addition to being plant hormones, SLs have been well recognized as allelochemicals that induce seed germination of root parasitic plants. In 2015, three groups independently reported the characterization of SL receptors in a root parasitic plant, Striga hermonthica (Conn et al., 2015; Toh et al., 2015; Tsuchiya et al., 2015). The receptors belong to the HYPOSENSITIVE TO LIGHT (HTL) or KARRIKIN INSENSITIVE 2 (KAI2) (hereafter referred to as HTL) family, which is a paralogous family of D14. D14-LIKE of rice (see above) is an ortholog of HTL. Previously, HTL was characterized to be the receptor of the smoke-derived seed germination inducer karrikin (KAR) in Arabidopsis (Waters et al., 2012b) (Figure 1). The presence of the endogenous

ligand for this protein family, which would be a new class of plant hormones, has been proposed, yet it has been unidentified so far (Conn and Nelson, 2015). Intriguingly, in the genome of many root parasitic plant species, the HTL family members are present at higher copy numbers than those in non-parasitic plants, and at least 11 HTL genes were found in S. hermonthica. In addition, phylogenetic analysis has shown that most HTLs in parasitic plants are classified into a specific clade that has diverged from the HTL clade of non-parasitic plants. Moreover, transgenic Arabidopsis seeds independently expressing some of HTLs (ShHTLs) in the divergent clade of S. hermonthica germinated in response to SL, but not to KAR, suggesting that ShHTLs act as the SL receptor for germination in S. hermonthica seeds. Furthermore, among 11 ShHTLs, ShHTL7 was demonstrated to be extremely sensitive to SL compared to other ShHTLs (Toh et al., 2015). Recently, a potent agonist for ShHTL7, which can induce S. hermonthica germination at the femtomolar range, was discovered through a chemical screening approach along with the structural optimization of the screened lead chemical (Uraguchi et al., 2018). During this process the authors found that even some non-hydrolyzable analogs could induce S. hermonthica germination, suggesting that the hydrolysis process is dispensable for their activity, which is consistent with the model proposed by Seto et al. On the other hand, the most active compound, which they named sphynolactone-7, could be hydrolyzed by ShHTL7. Therefore, the authors proposed that the SL hydrolysis process is essential to gain the femtomolar level potency for some reason. Yasui et al. also identified non-hydrolyzable-type SL agonists that target D14 (Yasui et al., 2019). Taken together, it is likely that at least the initiation of the SL signaling process does not require the hydrolytic degradation of SL by D14. Recently, Burger and Chory reanalyzed the structural data of the AtD14-D3-ASK1 complex reported by Yao et al, and proposed a third model, in which the D-ring part is covalently attached to only the His residue. They proposed that this structure fits to the electron density better than the iodine ion (Bürger and Chory, 2020).

In conclusion, it seems to be difficult at present to integrate all of these different models to propose one consistent signaling mechanism. Further experiments will be needed to obtain a complete understanding of the SL perception and signaling mechanisms by an unprecedented hydrolase receptor that retains its catalytic function.

# PERSPECTIVE

In this review, we summarized the recent topics on SL biosynthesis, transport and perception. Especially since SL was identified as an endogenous hormone, our knowledge on SL biology has rapidly and extensively developed. However, many important questions remain to be answered in the future.

With respect to SL biosynthesis, several hints have recently been reported but the bioactive form of SLs that regulates various aspects of plant growth and development is still uncertain. LBO has been shown to play a crucial role in producing the branch-inhibiting hormones in the later stage of SL biosynthesis. Because LBO-like genes are widely conserved in seed plant species (Brewer et al., 2016), further biochemical and genetic analyses of LBO-like 2OGDs may provide cues for understanding the synthesis of bioactive SLs. Moreover, the deactivation pathway(s) of SLs are also not established, although the hydrolysis of SLs by the D14 receptor was proposed as one of the SL inactivation mechanisms (Seto et al., 2019). Identification of additional enzymes will be key to understand the whole picture of SL biosynthesis and metabolism.

Also, there are still many questions with respect to the movement of SLs. Especially, similar to their biosynthesis, an endogenous compound that can move within plants has been unclear. Moreover, ABCG/PDR-mediated SL transport has not been shown beyond Solanaceae and Fabaceae species. As SL biosynthesis is finely regulated by nutrient conditions and other plant hormones, local and long-distance SL transport and exudation of SLs from roots are indispensable for the hormonal action of SLs and interaction with AM fungi, respectively. Elucidation of the molecular mechanisms underlying SL transport in various plant species will provide cues to understand how plants effectively utilize the biosynthesized SLs as signaling molecules.

Although there has been much progress in our understanding of the SL perception and signaling mechanisms, confusion may arise from the fact that several different models have been reported. X-ray crystallography has provided some important pictures to dissect the molecular mechanism of SL signaling. However, we cannot deny the notion that such data also have become causes of confusion. Although such approaches continue to be powerful tools, we need to be careful to interpret the data. In particular, in vivo experimental data such as genetic evidence that supports the structural data will be necessary to draw firm conclusions. Although SLs are also known to be symbiotic signals for AM fungi, the SL receptor in AM fungi has not been identified yet. A search for D14 homologous genes in AM fungus genomes could be an effective approach to identify an SL receptor. However, we were not able to obtain such genes by a BLAST search using the D14 sequence as a query. Complete understanding of the SL perception mechanisms in plants and AM fungi would pave the way to develop rational, precise and selective tools to control the versatile functions of SL.
