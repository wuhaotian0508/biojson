# ABSTRACT

Stalk rot, primarily caused by Fusarium graminearum $( F g )$ and Pythium inflatum $( P i )$ , is a major maize disease responsible for significant yield losses. The molecular mechanisms governing defence against these pathogens remain poorly understood. To uncover key miRNAs and their regulatory genes, small RNA, degradome, and transcriptome sequencing data were integrated to explore maize's response to stalk rot. A total of 363 miRNAs, including 113 novel ones, were identified from 12 sRNA libraries, with 305 differentially expressed miRNAs (DEMs) significantly responding to $F g$ and $P i$ infection. Degradome analysis detected 120 DEMs. Through transcriptome sequencing and weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA), $6 F g / P i$ -responsive regulatory modules, centered on hub genes, were identified from 8308 differentially expressed genes activated or repressed by the two pathogens. A machine-learning approach revealed complex regulatory networks within these significant pathogenresponsive modules. WGCNA highlighted ZmMYB74, targeted by zma–miR319, as a key hub gene regulator in these networks. Transgenic plants overexpressing ZmMYB74 showed compromised resistance to stalk rot pathogens, with reduced lignin deposition, whereas knockout or suppression of ZmMYB74 resulted in significantly enhanced resistance. Two In/Dels in the promoter region associated with ZmMYB74 transcription were linked to changes in maize resistance to stalk rot. ZmMYB74 functions as a transcriptional repressor, negatively regulating the expression of ZmCAD, a positive regulator of plant disease resistance involved in lignin biosynthesis. Isolating the resistance gene ZmMYB74 will not only aid in developing durable disease-resistant maize varieties but also enhance understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying stalk rot resistance. 

# 1 | Introduction

Maize (Zea mays L.), one of the most economically significant crops globally, is susceptible to a variety of diseases, with stalk 

rot emerging as one of its most destructive afflictions (Mueller et  al.  2020). Stalk rot is a prevalent soil-borne disease, caused by a range of Fusarium or Pythium fungi, either independently or through co-infection (Hou et al. 2023; Sun et al. 2024; Wang 

et  al.  2017). Among these, Fusarium graminearum $( F g )$ and Pythium inflatum $( P i )$ are the primary pathogenic agents responsible for widespread infection in China in recent years (Liu, Ma, et al. 2019). This disease typically results in a $1 0 \%$ reduction in yield, with losses reaching $3 0 \% - 5 0 \%$ in areas with high incidence (Chen et al. 2017). Additionally, stalk rot induces lodging in maize plants, further diminishing yields, compromising kernel quality, and impeding the effectiveness of maize mechanical harvesting (Gai et al. 2018; Sun, Ruan, et al. 2021). Resistance to stalk rot is known to be a complex, quantitative trait controlled by multiple genes involved in dynamic regulatory networks (Duan et al. 2019; Hou et al. 2023; Pè et al. 1993; Rashid et al. 2022; Song et al. 2015; Toman and White 1993; Wang et al. 2017; Yang et al. 2010; Ye et al. 2019). In the era of omics-driven research, integrative approaches utilising multi-layered omics data offer a promising path for a comprehensive understanding of maize stalk rot resistance mechanisms. 

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small non-coding RNAs that regulate the post-transcriptional expression of target genes (Raza et  al.  2023). Next-generation sequencing techniques, such as small RNA (sRNA) sequencing and degradome sequencing, are powerful tools for identifying conserved and novel miR-NAs and their target gene interactions (German et  al.  2009). Furthermore, integrating miRNA research with mRNA sequencing data enables a deeper understanding of the regulatory networks in which miRNAs participate (Song et al. 2019). These miRNAs and their targets are integral to plant growth, development, and stress responses in various species (Islam et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2012, 2014; Zhou, Liu, et al. 2020). For instance, the miRNA156-SPL (squamosa promoter binding protein-like) module temporally regulates trichome distribution and flowering time in Arabidopsis thaliana (Wang et al. 2009; Yu et al. 2010). The miR167-OsARF12 module plays a pivotal role in rice cell development and auxin signalling (Zhao et al. 2023), while the miR166-OsHB4 module controls drought resistance through leaf rolling and stem xylem development in rice (Zhang et al. 2018). Through combined miRNA–mRNA analysis, Jiao et al. (2022) demonstrated that miR408a acts as a negative regulator of maize drought stress, primarily by modulating reactive oxygen species accumulation in the root system. 

Numerous studies have highlighted the role of miRNAs in regulating plant defence responses to biotic stress (Chen et al. 2021; Zhou, Cao, et  al.  2020). For example, miR159a acts as a positive regulator in rice resistance to Magnaporthe oryzae (Chen et al. 2021). In maize, miR408b exhibits increased sensitivity to Fusarium verticillioides infection and impedes immunity by suppressing lignin biosynthesis when overexpressed in transgenic plants (Zhou, Cao, et  al.  2020). The zma-unmiR4-ZmGA2ox4 module plays a pivotal role in balancing growth and resistance to Fusarium verticillioides ear rot in maize (Xu et al.2022). 

Extensive genetic and omics studies have identified various quantitative trait loci (QTLs) and genes linked to resistance to $F g$ and $P i$ in maize stalk rot (Yang et  al.  2010; Ma et  al.  2017; Duan et  al.  2019; Zhang, Zheng, et  al.  2024). For instance, ZmCCT, ZmAuxRP1, and ZmHIR3 confer resistance to $F g$ infection through distinct regulatory pathways (Wang et al. 2017; Ye et  al.  2019). For resistance to $P i$ stalk rot, a limited number of loci have been precisely mapped, including RpiQI319-1 

and RpiQI319-2 (Song et al. 2015), Rpi1 (Yang et al. 2005), and RpiX178-1 and RpiX178-2 (Duan et al. 2019). However, the molecular mechanisms underlying maize plant responses to these two pathogens remain poorly understood, especially with regard to the role of miRNAs and their target regulatory modules. This study aims to integrate sRNA-seq, degradome-seq, and mRNAseq data to create a comprehensive dataset for identifying key miRNA-target modules and regulatory networks involved in resistance to $F g .$ - and $P i$ -induced stalk rot. 

MYB transcription factors (TFs) were first identified in Zea mays (Paz-Ares et  al.  1987), and many MYB TFs have since been characterised across various plant species (Dubos et al. 2010). These factors are extensively distributed in higher plants, playing pivotal roles in regulatory networks controlling development, metabolism, and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses, especially in relation to pathogen/pest resistance, nutrient and toxic substance absorption, drought/salt resistance, trichome development, stamen formation, leaf senescence, and phenylpropanoid-flavonoid/terpenoid biosynthesis (Dubos et al. 2010; Wu et al. 2024). MYB TFs are considered central to the development of protein- and RNA-mediated resistance in plants against destructive biotic agents, such as bacteria, fungi, viruses, nematodes, and oomycetes (Biswas et al. 2023). For example, OsMYBxoc1 regulates rice resistance to Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzicola by directly repressing the transcription of the iron transport gene OsNRAMP5 (Zhang, Sun, et al. 2024). Cotton GhMYB36 enhances resistance to Verticillium dahliae and drought stress by activating the expression of the disease resistance-related gene PR1 (Liu et  al.  2022). In Brassica juncea, BjMYB1 induces the expression of BjCHI1, contributing to defence against Botrytis cinerea (Gao et al. 2016). ZmMYB31 promotes the expression of the CBF gene, enhancing plant resistance to oxidative stress (Li, Lin, Zhang, and Sui  2019). However, only a few studies have functionally characterised the roles of maize MYB TFs in biotic stress and immune responses to pathogen invasion (Wang, Hou, et al. 2021; Yu et al. 2023). 

Recent studies have highlighted the involvement of several R2R3–MYB TFs in lignin biosynthesis and pathogen defence across various plant species (Yu et  al.  2023). For instance, AtMYB15 plays a pivotal role in defence-induced lignification and basal immunity in Arabidopsis, enhancing resistance to Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato DC3000 (Pst DC3000) by promoting lignin deposition (Chezem et  al.  2017). Similarly, GhODO1 in cotton directly activates the expression of Gh4CL1 and GhCAD3, leading to lignin synthesis and improved resistance to V. dahliae (Zhu et  al.  2022). In rice, OsMYB30, OsMYB55, and OsMYB110 specifically activate the expression of lignin biosynthesis-related genes (Os4CL3, Os4CL5, OsPAL6, and OsPAL8), contributing to enhanced pathogen resistance (He et  al.  2020; Kishi-Kaboshi et  al.  2018; Li et  al.  2020). Lignin, a biopolymer, provides mechanical strength to plant cell walls and forms a critical physical barrier that helps plants resist pathogen invasion (Barros et al. 2015; Cesarino 2019; Vanholme et al. 2010). In crops, downregulation or knockout of lignin biosynthesis genes (LBGs) such as phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL), caffeic acid O-methyltransferase (COMT), caffeoyl-CoA O-methyltransferase (CCoAOMT), and cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) significantly reduces lignin accumulation, facilitating fungal pathogen infections (Cao et  al.  2023; 

Bhuiyan et al. 2009; Li, Lin, Wang, et al. 2019; Yang et al. 2017). Additionally, lignin deposits, similar to the Casparian strip, act as physical barriers that restrict pathogen proliferation within the extracellular matrix, thereby enhancing plant defence mechanisms (Lee et al. 2019). 

This study identified ZmMYB74, a dominant gene conferring resistance to maize stalk rot, encoding an R2R3-type MYB TF, through integrative sRNA, degradome, and transcriptome analyses. Variations in stalk rot resistance associated with ZmMYB74 are linked to polymorphisms within its promoter region, affecting its expression levels. ZmMYB74 functions as a transcriptional repressor, negatively regulating the transcription of the target gene ZmCAD, which positively regulates plant resistance and is involved in lignin biosynthesis and deposition during fungal pathogen invasion. Our findings underscore the critical role of ZmMYB74 in modulating maize resistance to stalk rot, offering a valuable genetic target for breeding maize varieties resistant to the fungal pathogens responsible for this disease. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Identification of Conserved and Novel miRNAs by sRNA and Degradome Sequencing

Compared to the stalk rot-resistant inbred line Qi319, the susceptible maize line Ye478 exhibited more severe rot symptoms in the roots and lower stalk interior (Figure 1a,b), as well as a significant increase in both stalk rot score on average $( \mathrm { S R S } _ { \mathrm { A } } )$ and disease severity index (DSI) (Figure 1c) after inoculation with $F g$ and $P i$ . To investigate the role of maize miRNAs during infection with $F g$ and $P i$ , this study analysed miRNA expression profiles from the roots and leaves (composite samples consisting of both seedling leaves and stems, hereinafter the same for all subsequent samples) of Fg- and $P i$ -inoculated plants of the two inbred lines, Ye478 and Qi319, as well as mock-treated controls, using sRNA sequencing technology. A total of 363 unique miRNAs were identified from 12 sRNA libraries (Table S1, Figure S1a), including 250 known miRNAs and 113 novel miRNAs. Among these, 250 known miRNAs and 42 novel miRNAs were classified into 54 miRNA families, with the majority belonging to miR395, miR166, miR169, and miR159, each containing 20 or more members (Figure S1b). To confirm the miRNAs identified by sRNA sequencing, three degradome libraries (Fg, Pi, and mock) were constructed using the same samples. A total of 167 miRNAs were detected, all of which were present in the sRNA libraries (Table S2, Figure S1c). These miRNAs were classified into 25 miRNA families, with the majority belonging to miR395, miR166, and miR159, consistent with the sRNA data (Tables S1 and S2, Figure S1b). 

# 2.2 | Expression Profiles of miRNA in Response to ${ \cal F } { \bf g }$ and Pi Infection

To elucidate the involvement of miRNAs in $F g$ and $P i$ infection, differentially expressed miRNAs (DEMs) were identified by comparing infected and mock treatments within the same tissues and genotypes. Pairwise differential expression analysis 

between strain treatments and mock groups revealed 87–162 DEMs across different tissues and genotypes (Figure  1d,e). zma-miR319a-3p, zma-miR319b-3p, zma-miR319c-3p, zmamiR319d-3p, and zma-miR156a-3p were all positively responsive to $F g$ and $P i$ infection in comparison with mock-treated maize leaves from both genotypes (Figure 1f). In maize roots, 33 DEMs, including nine novel miRNAs and 24 known miR-NAs such as miRNA166 (j/k/n-3p), miRNA171 (d/e/i/j-3p), miRNA398 (a/b-3p/5p), miRNA408 (a/b-3p/5p), and miRNA528 (a/b-3p/5p), showed significant differential expression in response to pathogen infection compared to mock controls. These were identified across four pairwise comparisons: S03 versus S07, S03 versus S10, S04 versus S08, and S04 versus S11 (Figure 1f, Table S3). Notably, zma-miR319a/b/c/d-3p was also significantly upregulated in $F g$ -infected roots of Ye478 and Piinoculated roots of Qi319, similar to the expression observed in leaf tissues (Figure 1f). 

Comparisons of differential responses were further conducted between the two genotypes, between the two pathogen strains, and between the two tissues under identical conditions. The number of conserved DEMs was 18 for strain comparisons, 27 for genotype comparisons, and 27 for tissue comparisons (Figures  S1d–f and Table  S3). Among these, 12 members of miR395 were consistently upregulated in response to $P i$ infection compared to $F g$ treatment across all tissues and genotypes (Figure  1f). zma-miR159c/d-3p, zma-miR159c/d-5p, zmamiR319b/d-5p, and miRNA166 $( \mathrm { j } / \mathrm { k } / \mathrm { n } { - } 3 \mathrm { p }$ and a/b/d-5p) were identified as the main conserved DEMs between leaf and root tissues (Figure 1f, Table S3). 

To validate the high-throughput sequencing results, Northern blotting was performed to confirm the expression levels of miRNA159 and miRNA319 in mock-, Fg-, and $P i$ -inoculated maize seedlings. The Northern blotting data were largely consistent with the results obtained from sRNA sequencing (Figure 1g,h).

# 2.4 | zma-miR319 Positively Regulated Maize Resistance to Fungal Infection

To investigate the role of zma-miR319 in regulating maize resistance to stalk rot pathogens, a previously reported virus-based miRNA silencing (VbMS) vector, ZMBJ-CMV- $\cdot 2  { \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } } }$ -STTM (Liu, Liu, et al. 2019), was used to suppress miRNA expression in maize. A short tandem target mimic (STTM) consisting of two target mimics corresponding to zma-miR319, separated by a 48 nt stem-loop-like linker sequence, was designed and inserted downstream of the truncated 2b coding sequence of plasmid pCMV201- $2 \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } }$ to generate pCMV201- $\cdot 2  { \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } } } ^ { }$ -STTM319 (Figure  3a). This plasmid, along with two other ZMBJ-CMVbased miRNA silencing vectors representing ZMBJ-CMV RNA1 and RNA2, was used to express zma-miR319 STTM in maize. By 14 days post-inoculation (dpi), zma-miR319 expression in maize seedlings inoculated with ZMBJ-CMV-STTM319 was strongly suppressed (Figure  3b). The downregulation of zma-miR319 using ZMBJ-CMV-STTM319 also resulted in the increased expression of its predicted target genes (Figure 3c). When root tips of zma-miR319-silenced maize seedlings were challenged with stalk rot pathogens, the silenced plants exhibited more severe symptoms compared to control plants at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation (hpi), as confirmed by the DSI (Figure 3d). These results indicate that the effective and specific silencing of zma-miR319 promotes infection by stalk rot pathogens in maize seedlings. 

To further explore the role of zma-miR319 in maize resistance to stalk rot pathogenic fungi, we also generated zma-miR319 overexpressing maize lines using sugarcane mosaic virus (SCMV)- mediated overexpression vector pSCMV (Li et  al.  2025). Compared with the wild-type plants, the expression of zmamiR319 was increased by 6.4-fold in zma-miR319 overexpression (SCMV-miR319) lines (Figure S4a). The expression levels of zma-miR319 target genes ZmMYB74 and ZmMY138 were significantly decreased by approximately $8 1 \% - 8 4 \%$ in SCMVmiR319 plants (Figure S4b). Overexpression of zma-miR319 inhibited infection of maize stalk rot pathogens in maize plants (Figure  S4c). Previous reports showed that heteroexpression of Osa-miR319 in switchgrass reduced lignin content (Liu et  al.  2020). Similarly, overexpression of zma-miR319 also reduced lignin content in maize seedlings post-inoculation with stalk rot pathogens (Figure S4d). These data demonstrated that zma-miR319 positively regulated maize resistance against fungal infection of stalk rot pathogens. 

# 2.5 | Potential Roles of ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138 in the Maize Response to Stalk Rot Pathogens Infection

To further explore the potential functions of differentially regulated genes during stalk rot pathogen infection, ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138 were selected, as they displayed similar expression patterns during $F g$ and $P i$ infection in both Qi319 and Ye478 plants (Figure S6). One clade within the MYBs is the gibberellic 

acid (GA-) inducible MYBs (Dubos et al. 2010). Previous studies indicated that MYB74 and MYB138 belong to this clade based on phylogenetic relationships in typical R2R3–MYB proteins from maize (Du et  al.  2012). The expression levels of these two genes were examined in GA3-treated seedlings. The data showed that ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138 were induced by GA3 at different time points post-treatment (Figure 3e). Compared to control plants, the GA3-treated maize seedlings exhibited more pronounced dark lesions at 48 hpi with $F g$ and Pi (Figure S7a,b). 

An enhanced brome mosaic virus (BMV)-based virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) system (Ding et al. 2018) was used to analyse the potential impact of ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138 on pathogen infection. Silencing efficiency was assessed, showing that the transcript levels of ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138 were reduced by approximately $8 0 \% - 8 5 \%$ in the silenced maize plants compared to control plants (Figure  3f). In the silenced plants, the expression of other ZmMYB homologues remained unaffected (Figure  S8), confirming the successful silencing of ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB138. 

At 10 dpi, maize Va35 plants pre-inoculated with BMV-GFP and chimeric-BMVCP5 were challenged with $F g$ and Pi. The infection of these pathogens was evaluated by assessing symptom development and DSI at 48 hpi. The pathogens caused more severe symptoms in the control and ZmMYB138-silenced plants compared to the ZmMYB74-silenced plants. The DSI data corresponded with the symptom progression, showing that silencing of ZmMYB74 led to a $2 7 \%$ and $3 2 \%$ reduction in DSI at 14 dpi compared to wild-type and ZmMYB138-silenced plants, respectively (Figure 3g,h). 

To further verify the infection dynamics in stalk rot pathogenesis, mutants with transposon insertion alleles of ZmMYB138 (ZmMYB138-m1::Mu1) and ZmMYB74 (ZmMYB74-m2::Mu1) were used; the plants of these two mutants did not have any noticeable differences from normal siblings in overall plant stature or in reproductive development (Figure S7c,d). Following inoculation with stalk rot pathogens, ZmMYB74-m2::Mu1 mutant roots exhibited lower disease incidence and slower lesion progression compared to both the ZmMYB138-m1::Mu1 and control plants (Figure  S7e,f). These findings suggest that ZmMYB74, rather than ZmMYB138, plays a critical role in regulating maize resistance to stalk rot pathogens.

# 2.7 | ZmMYB74 Is Involved in Regulating the Interaction Between Fungal Pathogens and Host Maize Plants

To further explore the role of ZmMYB74 in the molecular interaction between stalk rot pathogens and maize, an enhancedexpression (EE) construct containing the native gene and 35S promoters was generated and transformed into maize inbred line B104. Homozygous lines were obtained and propagated to $\mathrm { T } _ { 4 }$ generations (Figure  S10a,c–d). The transgene-positive $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ lines exhibited a 25- to 37-fold increase in expression relative to their non-transgenic siblings $\left( \mathrm { E E ^ { - } } \right)$ (Figure  4a). At 48 hpi, roots of $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ seedlings displayed severe rot symptoms, including dark-brown lesions and root shrinkage, indicating increased susceptibility to $F g$ and Pi compared to the non-transgenic seedlings, which showed only mild symptoms (Figure  S11a). Transgenic maize lines (RNAi+) were generated using RNA interference (RNAi) to silence ZmMYB74 (Figure  S10c,e–g), resulting in a 2.8- to 3.9-fold reduction in ZmMYB74 expression (Figure 4b). These RNAi-positive seedlings exhibited increased resistance to stalk rot compared to their transgene-negative siblings across $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ to $\mathrm { T } _ { 4 }$ families (Figure  S11b). In contrast, the enhanced-expression transgenic seedlings displayed significantly higher DSI values compared to their non-transgenic siblings (Figure  4c), while the RNAi lines showed notably lower DSI values than the control (Figure 4d). To monitor the spread and colonisation of fungal hyphae in transgenic maize roots, wheat-germ agglutinin-Alexa Fluor 488 conjugate (WGA-AF488) staining was used. In the roots of ZmMYB74 $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ seedlings, most epidermal and cortical cells collapsed, and a dense mass of fungal hyphae colonised the epidermal and cortical cells as well as the vascular cylinder. A similar pattern of hyphal colonisation was observed in the non-transgenic siblings, though to a lesser extent (Figure 4e). In contrast, the roots of ZmMYB74 RNAi seedlings showed less extensive hyphal spread, with most hyphae confined to the epidermal and a few cortical cell layers, and only a few extended into the vascular cylinder (Figure 4e). These observations were further supported by the measurement of the area density (integrated optical density, IOD, per area) of green fluorescence (Figure S11c,d). 

The changes in subcellular organelles in the upper-root segments adjacent to the infected root segments were observed using transmission electron microscopy (TEM). In the roots of $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ seedlings, parenchyma cells in the cortex displayed severe abnormalities, including disintegration of the nucleus and organelles, with a discontinuous plasma membrane. These abnormalities included mitochondrial linearization, swollen mitochondria with distorted cristae, vacuolated mitochondria, degraded nuclei, and numerous thick, membrane-coated $F g$ or $P i$ hyphae scattered within the inter- and intracellular spaces (Figure  S12b,d). In contrast, the roots of transgenenegative plants exhibited similar morphological changes in the 

parenchyma cells of the cortex, with fungal hyphae clustering in the inter-cellular space, but not within the intracellular spaces (Figure  S12a,c,e,g). In the exodermis of diseased root segments from RNAi+ seedlings, subcellular organelle changes similar to those observed in the transgenic-negative and $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ lines were seen. However, individual thin fungal hyphae were predominantly located in the extracellular space, rather than in the inter- or intracellular spaces (Figure S12f,h). Notably, in contrast to the thin cell walls and disintegration observed in the transgenic-negative and ZmMYB74 $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ roots, the $\mathrm { R N A i ^ { + } }$ lines showed noticeable cell wall thickening, characterised by dark, electron-dense deposits between dead and dying cortical cells (Figure S12f,h). 

In a field test, adult transgenic and non-transgenic plants were inoculated with $F g$ and Pi. Consistent with previous results, the RNAi transgenic plants exhibited significantly enhanced resistance to stalk rot fungal pathogens compared to their $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ and transgene-negative siblings, as indicated by the typical stalk rot symptoms and DSI (Figure 4f–i). Collectively, these data strongly support the conclusion that ZmMYB74 plays a negative role in maize resistance to $F g$ and $P i$ infection, with ZmMYB74 transcript levels being closely linked to resistance performance. 

# 2.8 | Lignin Is Involved in the Responses of Maize Plants to the Fungal Infection

Previous studies have highlighted the role of lignin in plant defence against fungal, bacterial, and viral infections (Chezem et  al.  2017; Li, Lin, Wang, et  al.  2019; Wang and Balint-Kurti 2016; Yang et al. 2017; Yuan et al. 2019). The enrichment of the lignin synthesis-related phenylpropanoid pathway following fungal invasion (Figure S13), overexpression of zma-miR319 reduced lignin content in maize seedlings post-fungal infection (Figure  S4d), and cell wall thickening observed in the roots of RNAi seedlings (Figure  S12f,h) prompted us to investigate whether lignin also plays a role in maize defence against stalk rot fungal pathogens. To explore this, the BMV-derived VIGS system was employed to knock down the expression of key genes in the monolignol biosynthetic pathway, including ZmCAD, ZmPAL, and ZmCCoAOMT2. Silencing efficiency and specificity were confirmed through qRT-PCR, demonstrating successful gene silencing (Figure 5a). 

To examine the impact of silencing monolignol biosynthetic genes on maize infection by stalk rot pathogens, the transiently silenced seedlings were challenged with $F g$ and $P i$ at 7 dpi. Rot symptoms and DSI were measured, revealing that silencing of ZmCAD, ZmPAL, and ZmCCoAOMT2 facilitated fungal infection (Figure 5b). 

To determine whether the increased fungal infection was due to reduced lignin levels in the VIGS plants, lignin content in the roots was measured at 48 hpi. The results showed that the wildtype Va35 line contained $6 3 . 2 0 \mathrm { - } 7 1 . 6 7 \mathrm { m g }$ of lignin per g dry cell wall, while the ZmCAD, ZmPAL, and ZmCCoAOMT2 silenced lines had lower lignin levels $( 3 9 . 3 6 - 5 4 . 9 4 \mathrm { { m g } }$ lignin per g dry cell wall, respectively) (Figure 5c). These data support the model that lignin contributes to pathogen resistance.

To investigate the distribution of lignin deposition in the roots of transgenic plants, transverse ultrathin sections were prepared from the lower-root segments adjacent to lesions, which lacked visible rot symptoms, and were subjected to Maüle staining. Lignification or thickening of the cell wall was observed in the hypodermal cells of RNAi-transgene-positive roots, but not in $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ transgenic roots. High lignin levels were deposited in the exodermal and vascular cylinders of the RNAi+ roots, while weak staining was observed in the vascular tissues of enhancedexpression transgene-positive roots (Figure  5f). Analysis of lignin content revealed that the roots of ZmMYB74-silenced seedlings accumulated more lignin than those of the $\mathrm { E E ^ { + } }$ and transgene-negative lines at 48 hpi (Figure 5g,h). These findings confirm that ZmMYB74 is essential for defence-induced lignin deposition. 

# 2.10 | ZmMYB74 Represses Lignin Biosynthesis by Regulating ZmCAD Expression in Maize Plants

To elucidate the regulatory mechanism by which ZmMYB74 represses lignin biosynthesis, DAP-seq was performed to identify genes directly bound by ZmMYB74. Consistent peaks identified from two independent biological replicates were used to pinpoint the binding sites. A model-based analysis using MACS2 with a cutoff $p { < } 0 . 0 1$ revealed 6134 peaks as putative ZmMYB74 binding sites (Table S15). The distribution of these binding peaks across the genome showed that all 10 chromosomes were represented (Table  S15). Notably, $7 7 \%$ of the peaks were located in intergenic regions, $7 . 8 \%$ in promoter regions, and $1 . 4 \%$ and $2 . 3 \%$ in the $5 ^ { \prime }$ UTR and $3 ^ { \prime }$ UTR, respectively (Figure 6a). The binding peaks were predominantly concentrated 2-kb upstream of the transcription start sites (TSSs) and 1000-bp downstream of the transcription termination sites (TTSs) (Figure 6b). To identify the ZmMYB74 binding motifs, 400-bp flanking sequences around all genic peaks were analysed. Among the identified motifs, the core sequence AA(C)CT(A)A(G/T)C(A)C(T/A) was the most abundant ( $\dot { p }$ -value $=$ 1e−33) (Figure 6c, Figure S14). 

A TF-centered Y1H assay was conducted to further identify the motifs bound by ZmMYB74 (Figure  S16). Insertion sequences were assembled to obtain unique sequences, which were aligned using the MEME program, resulting in the identification of 10 potential motifs (Table S16) bound by ZmMYB74. Y1H analysis was employed to confirm whether these motifs could indeed be bound by ZmMYB74. Five motifs were randomly selected for further analysis. Three tandem copies of each motif were cloned into the pHIS2 vector and used in Y1H assays. The results confirmed that ZmMYB74 could bind to all five selected motifs (Figure  S14). Among the 10 motifs, only motif AC-I (ACCAACC) and AC-IV (ACCAAAC) were found in the promoter regions of lignin biosynthetic genes (LBGs) (Table  S17), consistent with the DAP-seq results and validating the accuracy of the identified ZmMYB74-binding motifs. To further confirm 

the direct binding of ZmMYB74 to motifs AC-I and AC-IV, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) were performed using biotin-labelled DNA oligonucleotides containing tandem repeats of these motifs as probes. EMSA results showed that the ZmMYB74 protein could interact with the AC-I and AC-IV motifs, resulting in retarded binding bands. These shifts were reduced by the addition of corresponding unlabelled competitors but not by a non-specific DNA fragment (Figure 6d). 

MYBs regulate lignin biosynthesis by directly targeting the conserved AC and SMRE elements in the promoters of LBGs (Ma and Constabel  2019; Zhong and Ye  2012). Multiple instances of AC-I and AC-IV were identified in the promoters of several LBGs (Figure 6e). To confirm the binding of ZmMYB74 to these motifs in maize cells, a chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assay was performed. ChIP–qPCR revealed the recruitment of ZmMYB74 to the AC-I and AC-IV elements within the ZmCAD promoter but not to the ZmHCT or ZmCCoAOMT1 promoters (Figure 6e). Point-by-point Y1H was conducted to further confirm the direct binding of ZmMYB74 to the ZmCAD promoter (Figure  6f). Additionally, a dual LUC reporter assay was performed in maize protoplasts to assess the transcriptional repression activity of ZmMYB74. When ZmMYB74 was co-expressed with the ZmCAD promoter driving a LUC reporter gene, LUC expression was repressed by approximately $9 0 \%$ compared to the control, which lacked ZmMYB74 expression (Figure 6g). These results conclusively demonstrate that ZmMYB74 acts as a transcriptional repressor, binding to the AC elements of ZmCAD, a key gene in the lignin biosynthesis pathway. 

# 3 | Discussion

Numerous studies have highlighted the significant role of miR-NAs in plant responses to biotic stresses. However, few studies have focused on the miRNAs and their targets in maize stalk rot caused by $F g$ and $P i$ . In particular, limited information is available regarding genetic resistance to $P i$ in maize. Therefore, investigating the expression patterns of miRNAs in roots and leaves under $F g$ and $P i$ stresses can provide valuable insights into the resistance mechanisms of maize to stalk rot. In this study, 305 DEMs significantly responsive to $F g$ and $P i$ infection in the roots and leaves of Qi319 and Ye478 were identified using sRNA sequencing (Figure  1d,e). Among these, 120 known miRNAs were also observed in the degradome data, including miR159, miR166, miR167, miR171, miR398, miR408, and miR528. The potential of these miRNA families to enhance tolerance to various biotic stresses in crop plants has been summarised in a recent review (Raza et al. 2023). However, the regulation of maize stalk rot resistance by miRNAs remains understudied. The key miRNAs identified in this study, which displayed differential expression patterns in response to the pathogens, may help elucidate the mechanisms through which miRNAs contribute to stalk rot resistance in maize. 

Using WGCNA, six key gene co-expression networks, centered around hub genes, were identified in response to $F g$ and Pi treatments. Maize stalk rot, caused by various pathogens, is one of the most devastating diseases leading to significant production losses and quality deterioration worldwide (Wang et  al.  2017). While the disease is primarily caused by multiple pathogens, including several fungal species (especially Fusarium spp. and Pythium spp.), as well as oomycetes and bacteria (Belisário et al. 2022), previous studies on maize stalk rot resistance have mainly focused on Fusarium stalk rot (Cao et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2017; Ye et al. 2019). Research on disease resistance genes against Pythium stalk rot in maize is limited, despite the widespread occurrence of Pythium spp. as pathogens in maize stalk rot (Liu et al. 2024; Song et al. 2015; Yang et al. 2002). Growing evidence suggests that miRNAs in plants have evolved adaptive mechanisms to combat pathogen attacks (Song et  al.  2019). Several studies have elucidated the role of miRNAs in maize immunity against F. verticillioides ear rot (Zhou, Cao et al. 2020; Qu et al. 2023), while few have analysed miRNA-mediated resistance mechanisms against stalk rot infection in maize. The present study demonstrated that zma-miR319 suppresses the expression of ZmMYB74, enhancing the repression of the ZmCAD gene, which is involved in lignin deposition, following stalk rot pathogen invasion in maize, through multi-omics analysis of miRNA, degradome, and transcriptome (Figure 7). Two In/Del mutations in the promoter region reduce the expression of ZmMYB74 in the resistant inbred line Qi319, impairing its binding effect with ZmCAD. As a result, ZmMYB74 fails to recruit ZmCAD for transcriptional suppression, leading to lignin deposition post-fungal invasion, thereby conferring resistance to stalk rot (Figure  7). Importantly, knockout of ZmMYB74 significantly improves seed yield under stalk rot infection. The identification of ZmMYB74 provides a gene-based target for improving maize resistance to stalk rot, either through gene editing or further marker-assisted selection. 

Several SNPs were identified between the two alleles (Figure S9), but these SNPs did not affect the transcript splicing of the 

ZmMYB74 gene (Figure  S15a,b). However, they did influence the transcriptional levels in the resistant and susceptible inbred lines (Figure 3j), suggesting the existence of additional regulatory mechanisms that contribute to transcriptional variation in ZmMYB74. Based on experimental data, the inbred lines were classified into two heterotic groups: the resistant haplotype (Hap 1) and the susceptible haplotype (Hap 2), according to the natural variations in two In/Dels (In/Del-21 and In/Del-8) within the ZmMYB74 promoter (Figure  3i, Figure  S9). This implies that the ZmMYB74-mediated quantitative resistance to maize stalk rot is primarily driven by the expression level of ZmMYB74. The cis-acting regulatory elements potentially involved in In/Del-21 and In/Del-8 were analysed using the New PLACE software (https://www.dna.affrc.go.jp/PLACE/?actio $\mathrm { n } =$ newplace, Higo et al. 1999). In the promoter haplotype 1 (resistant), a GATA box and a CT-rich motif were found in InDel-21 and InDel-8, respectively (Figure  S15c). These cis-elements, however, were absent at the corresponding positions in the promoter haplotype 2 (susceptible). This suggests that specific TFs likely interact with these motifs to modulate ZmMYB74 expression, contributing to the observed resistance variations to stalk rot pathogens in maize. However, further research is required to confirm this hypothesis. Another In/Del (In/Del-18) was identified in the coding region of the Qi319 allele but not in other resistant lines. The In/Del-18 causes the deletion of six amino acids without introducing frame-shifting mutations (Figure S9). Interestingly, no significant difference in the transcriptional level was observed between Qi319 and other resistant inbred lines (Figure 3j, Figure S6g). Moreover, this In/Del lies outside the zma-miRNA319 RNA cleavage site (Figure S5), indicating that In/Del-18 does not affect transcriptional activity. Therefore, the In/Del-20 within the ZmMYB74 coding sequence of the Qi319 allele is not associated with its transcriptional activity. Collectively, maize varieties with promoter deletions in ZmMYB74 represent valuable genetic resources for breeding maize with enhanced resistance to stalk rot pathogens.

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Pathogenic Fungi

Seeds from maize inbred lines that exhibited resistance (Qi319) and susceptibility (Ye478) to maize stalk rot were maintained in our lab. The mutants of ZmMYB74 (ZmMYB74-m2::Mu1, accession number UFMU-02166) and ZmMYB138 (ZmMYB138- m1::Mu1, accession number UFMU-07639) were kindly provided by the Maize Genetics Cooperation-Stock Center (Maize COOP) and propagated in the plant growth house of the Henan Academy of Agricultural Sciences. Location of transposon insertion in 

ZmMYB74 and ZmMYB74 were confirmed by sequencing the PCR fragments amplified using gene-specific and transposonspecific primers, respectively (Table S14). The wild-type maize genotype B73 and the inbred line B104, used for maize transformation, were kindly provided by Dr. Hallauer at Iowa State University, maintained, and propagated in the Sears Plant Growth Greenhouse at the University of Missouri (Columbia). Seeds of cv. Va35 plants used for BMV inoculation were obtained from the University of Nebraska, Lincoln. 

The pathogenic fungi causing maize stalk rot, $F g$ (isolate #D73) and $P i$ (isolate #19-2), were isolated from infected maize stem tissues. The colonies were single-spore sub-cultured on PDA (BD Difco Potato Dextrose Agar, Sparks, MD, USA) media at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days and harvested for inoculum preparation. 

# 4.2 | Artificial Inoculation of Maize Seedlings and Disease Severity Evaluation

Maize seedlings were artificially inoculated with mycelial plug inocula according to a previously described protocol (Cao et  al.  2023). Primary roots inoculated with mycelial inocula were scored for symptoms in the diseased region based on a rating scale of 1–5, and the DSI $( \% )$ was calculated (Ye et al. 2019). 

# 4.3 | Artificial Inoculation and Symptom Scoring of Maize Stalks

Maize plants at the flowering stage or the fifth-leaf stage (for target gene-silenced seedlings) were used for stalk inoculation, following previously described protocols (Cao et al. 2021; Zhang et  al.  2016). To evaluate stalk rot symptoms, inoculated internodes of individual maize plants were split $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ (seedlings) or 40 days (adult plants) after inoculation, and symptoms were observed using a score of 0, 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 according to a previously established classification standard (Hou et al. 2023). 

# 4.4 | sRNA Sequencing and Data Analysis

Twelve sRNA cDNA libraries (S01–S12) were sequenced from leaves (as in this context, “leaf tissue” designates a homogenate comprising both the foliar and stem tissues of the seedlings) and roots of the two inbred lines under Fg, Pi infection, and mock conditions using Illumina HiSeq 2500 (Biomarker Technologies, Beijing, China) (Table  S1). Raw reads were processed using Bowtie v1.0.0 to remove rRNA, tRNA, snRNA, snoRNA, and repeats. Known miRNAs were identified by mapping unique sequences, with lengths between 18 and 25 nucleotides, to the miRBase database (http://www.mirbase.org/) using miRDeep2 v2.0.5 software (Friedländer et  al.  2012). New miRNAs were predicted by comparing with known miRNAs from miRBase using the miRDeep2 software. The expression level of miRNAs was represented by TPM (transcripts per million). miRNAs were considered differentially expressed with |log (fold change) $| > 1$ and $F D R < 0 . 0 5$ , as determined by the edgeR package. Target genes of miRNAs were predicted using TargetFinder v1.6 software (Bo and Wang 2005). For the same genotype and tissue, pairwise differential analysis of miRNAs was performed 

between $F g$ -infected and mock-treated groups, and between Piinfected and mock-treated groups. Holding genotype and treatment constant, pairwise analysis was conducted between root and leaf tissues. Under identical treatments and tissues, pairwise analysis was performed between the two genotypes Qi319 and Ye478. 

# 4.5 | Degradome Sequencing and Data Analysis

RNA from mock (S01–S04), Fg (S05–S08), and Pi (S09–S12) treatments were pooled to generate three degradome libraries. 50-bp single-end sequencing was conducted on an Illumina HiSeq2500 platform following the manufacturer's protocol at LC-Bio (Hangzhou, China). Quality control of the degradome sequences was performed using fastp v0.10.1 (Chen et al. 2018). High-quality reads were analysed with ACGT101-DEG v3.1 (LC Sciences, Houston, Texas, USA) and CleaveLand v3.0 (Addo-Quaye et  al.  2009) to predict miRNA-cleaved targets. Read abundance was normalised to transcripts per billion (TPB). Predicted target genes were categorised into five groups (0–4) as previously described (Sun, Xiong, et al. 2021). 

# 4.6 | Transcriptome Sequencing and Data Analysis

For transcriptome sequencing, RNA was extracted from the leaves (refer to the composite samples consisting of both seedling leaves and stems) and roots of two inbred lines under mock, $F g$ , and $P i$ treatments, with three biological replicates. A total of 36 transcriptome libraries were constructed using the Illumina mRNA-Seq sample preparation kit (Illumina, San Diego, USA). Paired-end sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 at LC-Bio (Hangzhou, China). After filtering, clean reads were aligned to the maize B73 RefGen_v3 genome (ftp://ftp.ensemblgenomes.org/pub/plants/release-24/ fasta/zea_mays/) using Hisat v2.0 (Kim et al. 2015). Fragment per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads (FPKM) was calculated using StringTie v1.3.0 (Pertea et al. 2015). DEGs in pairwise comparisons between $F g$ -infected and mock-treated groups, and between $P i$ -infected and mock-treated groups, were identified using the edgeR package with thresholds of $ { \lVert { \mathbf { o g } _ { 2 } } \rVert }$ (foldchange) $\geq 1$ and $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ . To validate pairwise comparison results of key DEGs, a multi-factor analysis of variance was implemented in R using aov function, with genotype, treatment, tissue, and biological replicate as fixed factors. Statistical significance was defined as a $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ . For significant sources, Tukey's honestly significant difference test was used for multiple comparison, with statistical significance defined as an adjusted $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ . 

# 4.7 | Co-Expression Network Construction

To identify co-regulatory factors influencing maize stalk rot, WGCNA was performed on 8038 DEGs across 36 samples using the WGCNA package in R. A soft threshold power of seven was selected for network construction based on signed $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 9$ . The minimum module size was set to 50, and mergeCutHeight was set to 0.15 to merge similar modules. Each module was assigned a colour, and significant modules were defined based on $p$ -value 

$< 0 . 0 5$ for module-sample associations in at least two samples. The total connectivity and correlation with module eigengene (KME) were calculated within each significant module. The top $5 \%$ of genes with the highest connectivity were classified as hub genes. These hub genes were used as regulators, with their targets predicted using the GENIE3 machine-learning method (gene network inference with ensemble of trees 3) in R (Huynh-Thu et al. 2010). A weight value threshold of 0.10 was applied. Gene interaction relationships were visualised with Cytoscape v3.9.1. 

# 4.8 | Gene Ontology (GO) Enrichment Analysis

GO annotations were performed using OmicStudio tools (https://www.omicstudio.cn/tool). Significant GO terms were defined with a $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 5$ . 

# 4.9 | Northern Blotting Analysis of miRNAs

Approximately $4 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA was prepared for Northern blot analysis of miRNAs. For qRT-PCR assays, RNA was extracted by pooling samples from three independent experiments, each with at least three biological replicates. The total RNA was resolved on a $1 5 \%$ urea polyacrylamide gel, transferred electrophoretically to a Hybond-NX membrane (GE Healthcare, Buckinghamshire, UK) using a semi-dry transfer apparatus (Bio-Rad, CA, USA), and cross-linked chemically via 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl) carbodiimide (EDC). For probe labeling, $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ probe, $2 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 0 \times \mathrm { T 4 }$ PNK buffer (New England Biolabs, Beverly, MA, USA), $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $[ \gamma ^ { \smash { 3 2 } } \mathrm { P } ]$ ATP $( - 1 0 \mu \mathrm { C i } / \mu \mathrm { L } )$ , $1 9 . 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ , and $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of T4 Poly Nucleotide Kinase (New England Biolabs) were mixed and incubated in a water bath at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. Blots were pre-hybridised and hybridised overnight at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ using ULTRAhyb-Oligo hybridization buffer (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA). Post-hybridization washes were conducted at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , twice, using $2 \times$ SSC and $0 . 2 \%$ sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS). # 4.10 | Maize Transformation and Event Analysis

To construct the ZmMYB74 enhanced-expression (EE) binary constructs for functional validation, the DNA fragment containing the native ZmMYB74 gene, including the promoter, coding sequence, and $3 ^ { \prime }$ -untranslated region was amplified using homologous recombination primer pairs (Table  S14). The fragment was inserted into the binary vector pFGC5941 (Kerschen et  al.  2004) (Figure  S10a). To construct the ZmMYB74-RNAi vector, gene-specific fragments were amplified and fused in a head-to-head orientation into the CHS intron flanking regions of pFGC5941 (Table S14, Figure S10b). 

Maize transformation and event analysis were performed at the Plant Transformation Core Facility, University of Missouri-Columbia. Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain AGL1)-mediated transformation was conducted following an improved protocol 

based on previous methods (Frame et al. 2006) using the elite maize inbred line B104. 

To assess the segregation of the RNAi insert and selectable marker gene, at least 20 plants from each $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ event were screened using PCR and leaf-painting analysis with $5 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ glufosinate (Liberty, Aventis Crop Science, USA). Chi-squared tests were used to analyse segregation ratios of the bar gene and RNAi insert in $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ plants based on herbicide resistance and PCR results, respectively. $\mathrm { T } _ { 2 }$ progeny from the $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ generation were similarly analysed to identify homozygous $\mathrm { T } _ { 1 }$ lines for further analysis. 

For Southern blot analysis, genomic DNA was extracted from transgenic B104 plants using a modified CTAB-based protocol. A total of $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of genomic DNA was digested with BamH I, a restriction enzyme that cuts only once within the T-DNA of plasmids pFGC5941. DNA fragments were separated on a $1 . 0 \%$ agarose gel and transferred to a Zeta-Probe GT nylon membrane (Bio-Rad, CA, USA). DNA was fixed to the membrane by UV cross-linking. Hybridization and membrane washing were performed at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ following the manufacturer's guidelines. The Prime-It RmT Random Primer Labeling Kit (Stratagene, USA) was used to generate $\mathsf { \Omega } ^ { 3 2 } \mathrm { P }$ -labelled probes for target genes or RNAi inserts, which were amplified by PCR using primers listed in Table S14. 

# 4.11 | BMV-Mediated Gene Silencing

An improved BMV silencing vector (Ding et al. 2018) was used for target gene knockdown. Sequence-specific DNA fragments corresponding to target genes were amplified from maize cv. Va35 seedlings cDNA using specific primers (Table S14) and ligated into the pC13/F3CP5 RNA3 vector. A control RNA3 vector plasmid, containing a 250-bp insert from a variant of the GFP gene, was used to produce pC13/F3CP5: GFP (Ding et al. 2018). 

Agrobacterium tumefaciens cultures carrying VIGS vectors were infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves to induce BMV viral multiplication as described previously (Ding et  al.  2018; Zhu et al. 2014). Subsequently, maize cv. Va35 seedlings were inoculated with BMV for VIGS, and the viral artificial inoculation procedures, along with the evaluation of silencing efficiency and specificity, followed our earlier protocols (Cao et al. 2023). 

# 4.12 | Virus-Base miRNA Silencing

For miRNA silencing in maize seedlings, the VbMS vector ZMBJ-CMV- $\cdot 2  { \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } } }$ -STTM was used to silence zma-miR319. To construct the ZMBJ-CMV- $2 \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } }$ -STTM319 plasmid, a 48 nt oligonucleotide was synthesised and used as a template for STTM (short tandem target mimic) PCR amplifications using specific primers (Table  S14). A three-nucleotide (nt) sequence (CTA) was inserted into the STTM molecules at the position corresponding to nucleotides 10–11 of zma-miR319 as described by Yan et  al.  (2012). The resulting STTM319 fragments were cloned into the pCMV201-2b vector using the In-Fusion HD Cloning kit (TaKaRa Bio). For ZMBJ-CMV-based VbMS, plasmids pCMV101, pCMV301, pCMV201- $\cdot 2 \mathrm { b } _ { \mathrm { N 8 1 } }$ -STTM319, and its derivatives were individually introduced into Agrobacterium 

tumefaciens strain C58C1. A. tumefaciens cultures were prepared, and N. benthamiana leaves were infiltrated as previously described (Wang et  al.  2016). At 3 dpi, the agro-infiltrated N. benthamiana leaves were harvested for maize inoculation. Virus infection protocols and suppression efficiency assessments were conducted following our previously established methods (Liu, Liu, et al. 2019). 

# 4.13 | SCMV-Mediated Over Expression

A newly developed SCMV-based vector (Li et al. 2025) was used for miRNA over expression in maize plants. The zma-miR319 precursor was inserted into either the NIb/CP or P1/HC-Pro junction site of SCMV to produce virus-mediated over expression (VOX) vectors. Virus inoculation of maize plants according to previous description (Li et  al.  2025). Briefly, A. tumefaciens harbouring the SCMV constructs were syringe infiltrated into fully expanded leaves of N. benthamiana for viral multiplication. Crude sap inoculums were prepared by grinding Agroinfiltrated leaves at 7 dpi, and directly rubbed onto 2–3-seedling stage of maize. Inoculated plants were incubated in dark and humid conditions under plastic covers at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , then the covers were removed and the plants were grown in the greenhouse at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of illumination. At 4 and 7 dpi, samples were collected for investigation of the efficiency and stability of the heterologous gene expressed from the SCMV-based vectors. All experiments were replicated at least three times. 

# 4.14 | Quantitative Real-Time RT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from various samples using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) and treated with RNase-free DNase I (TaKaRa, Dalian, China) to eliminate contaminating DNA, following the manufacturer's protocol. Firststrand cDNA was synthesised using $2 . 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA in a $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction with SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase (Invitrogen) and random hexamer primers. For quantitative qRT-PCR, 10- fold diluted cDNA, gene-specific primers (listed in Table  S14), and SYBR Green qPCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems Inc., Foster City, CA, USA) were combined and analysed on an ABI 7500 Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems). ZmTub4 was used as the internal control for normalisation. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \mathrm { t } } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen  2001). Each analysis was performed with at least three biological replicates, each containing three technical replicates. 

# 4.15 | WGA Staining

At 48 hpi, upper-root segments adjacent to the diseased root sections were harvested for preparation of free-hand transverse ultrathin sections, approximately $5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ in thickness, to monitor fungal hyphal growth. 

The sections were co-stained with WGA-AF488 and propidium iodide (PI) (Redkar et al. 2018). WGA-AF488 stained fungal hyphae green, while PI stained plant cell walls red. After staining, the slides were scanned and digitised using a Pannoramic MIDI 

II (3DHISTECH, Budapest, Hungary) equipped with differential epifluorescence (GFP filter set for WGA-AF488: 450–490 nm excitation, 500–550 nm emission; TX2 filter set for PI: excitation $5 4 0 { - } 5 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , emission $6 0 8 \mathrm { - } 6 8 3 \mathrm { n m }$ ). Images were analysed using the Pannoramic Viewer software (3D HISTECH). The WGA-AF488 signal intensity in the cross-sections was quantified by measuring the area density of green fluorescence using Image-Pro Plus 6.0 (Media Cybernetics, Rockville, MD, USA). Area density represents the IOD of the WGA signal per area. 

# 4.16 | Observation With Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM)

Root segments 2 cm above the disease lesion were collected for TEM at 48 hpi. TEM was performed as previously described (Jiao et  al.  2021). Briefly, the segments were cut into $1 \times 1 \mathrm { m m }$ pieces, fixed overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in $2 . 5 \%$ glutaraldehyde, and further fixed for 2 h in $1 \%$ osmium tetroxide in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.0). After dehydration through a graded ethanol series $5 0 \%$ , $7 0 \%$ , $8 0 \%$ , $9 0 \%$ , $9 5 \%$ , and $1 0 0 \%$ ), the tissue samples were embedded in Spurr's resin. Ultrathin sections were cut using a Leica EM UC 7 ultramicrotome (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany), and double-stained with uranyl acetate and lead citrate for 15 min each. The ultrathin sections were observed using a TEM (H-7650; Hitachi, Chiyoda City, Japan) operated at $8 0 \mathrm { k V } .$ 

# 4.17 | Lignin Histochemical Staining

Maize upper-root segments immediately adjacent to diseased root regions were collected and frozen in liquid nitrogen. Transverse ultrathin sections were prepared using a Leica CM 1850 cryostat at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Leica Microsystems Inc., Buffalo Grove, IL, USA). The samples were subjected to Maüle reactions according to previously established protocols (Nakano and Meshitsuka 1992). Images were captured using a CCD camera (Colour Coolview; Photonic Science, Milham, UK) attached to an inverted microscope (Leitz DMRIBE, Leica Microsystems). 

# 4.18 | DAP-seq

For DAP-seq, the full-length CDS of ZmMYB74 was inserted into the pFN19K HaloTag expression vector (Promega). DNA was extracted from young leaves of B73 using a modified Cetyltrimethylammonium Bromide protocol (Allen et al. 2007), fragmented, and ligated with adaptors. The Halo-ZmMYB74 protein was immobilised on Magne Halo-Tag beads (Promega). Subsequently, the Halo-tagged TF ZmMYB74 was expressed in a wheat germ system (Promega), followed by washing and incubation with the DNA library. After washing, DNA was eluted and amplified using indexed TruSeq primers (Aoke Science, Wuhan, China). Sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq system with 150-bp reads (Berry Genomics, Beijing, China). Primers are listed in Table S14. Raw data were processed using trim_galore (version 4.9) to remove adapters and lowquality reads, with the following parameters: $- \mathrm { j } 8 - \mathrm { q } 2 5$ --length 36 --stringency 3. The filtered reads were mapped to the B73 reference genome using Bowtie 2 (version 2.5.4) under default settings. Two replicates were used for peak calling with macs2 

(version 2.2.9.1) at a probablity value of 0.01 (-p 0.01), using the HALO control for background subtraction. Reproducible peaks were annotated using ChIPseeker (version 1.40.0). 

# 4.19 | TF-Centered Y1H Analysis

TF-centered Y1H analysis was performed as previously described (Jin et  al.  2023; Wang, Wang, et  al.  2023). Briefly, a random short DNA sequence insertion library was constructed as the prey library using the yeast recombination method (Ruiyuan Biotech, Nanjing, China). To analyse motifs bound by ZmMYB74, the ZmMYB74 coding sequence was cloned into the pGADT7-Rec2 vector (Clontech, CA, USA) to construct the bait vector pGADT7-Rec2-MYB74. The bait was then transformed into yeast containing the random DNA insertion library (prey library) using the Yeastmaker Yeast Transformation System 2 (Clontech). The transformed cells were selected on SD/−His/− $\mathrm { L e u } / \mathrm { - T r p } + 3 0 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-aminotriazole (3-AT). Yeast transformants on each plate were harvested, washed with YPD Plus liquid medium, and pooled to form the hybridization library. Positive clones were further subjected to high stringency selection, after which the inserts in the pHIS2 plasmids were sequenced. The insertion sequences, along with the left and right flanking sequences (“GGG” and “CCC”), were analysed to identify known cis-acting elements using the PlantCARE program (http://bioin formatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/) and the MEME program (https://meme-suite.org/meme/tools/streme) with default parameters. Primers for TF-centered Y1H analysis are listed in Table S14. 

# 4.20 | Verification of the Binding of Motifs to TF Using Yeast One Hybrid

Three tandem copies of different candidate cis-acting elements were cloned into the pHIS2 vector as reporter vectors, and the primers used for plasmid construction are listed in Table  S14. The reporter vector was transformed into yeast strain Y187 along with the pGADT7-Rec2-MYB74 plasmid, then plated on TDO (SD/−Leu/−Trp/−His) media supplemented with $3 0 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-AT for yeast one-hybrid assays. 

# 4.21 | EMSA

The full-length CDS of ZmMYB74 was cloned into the pET-28a expression plasmid (Novagen, Madison, WI, USA) and expressed in Escherichia coli strain BL21 (DE3) cells. The cells were cultured in LB medium at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at $2 2 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ until the $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ reached 0.5. The cells were then incubated overnight at $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking to induce recombinant protein expression, followed by collection by centrifugation at $8 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The collected cells were resuspended in lysozyme solution and lysed by sonication. The lysate was centrifuged at $1 6 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and the supernatant was applied to a His-bind resin (EMD Chemicals Inc., San Diego, CA, USA) column for purification of His-ZmMYB74 fusion proteins according to the manufacturer's guidelines. Biotin-labelled probes were synthesised and labelled at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end by TsingKe Biotech (Beijing, China). The EMSA was conducted using the LightShift 

EMSA Optimization and Control Kit (Thermo Scientific). His-ZmMYB74 protein was incubated with biotin-labelled probes at room temperature for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and unlabeled probes were used as competitors to test the binding specificity. Free and His-ZmMYB74-bound probes were separated on a $1 0 \%$ native polyacrylamide gel, and biotin-labelled probes were electroblotted onto nitrocellulose membranes and detected using a Fusion FX6 chemiluminescence imaging system (VILBER, France). 

# 4.22 | ChIP–qPCR

To verify whether the identified interaction occurred in plants, ChIP–qPCR was performed. ZmMYB74 was fused with GFP and stably transformed into maize embryos for overexpression. The resistant calluses were harvested for ChIP analysis. ChIP was carried out using anti-GFP antibody as previously described (Gendrel et  al.  2005). Enriched DNA fragments were quantified by RTqPCR using SYBR Green qPCR Master Mix (Applied Biosystems Inc.), with ZmTub4 as an internal control for normalisation. The primers used for this analysis are listed in Table S14. 

# 4.23 | Dual Luciferase Assay

For effector construct preparation, the coding sequences of ZmMYB74 were inserted after the 35S promoter of the pGreenII 62-SK vector. ZmCAD promoter fragments were recombined into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector at the Hind III and BamH I restriction sites to generate the pZmCAD::LUC plasmid as a reporter. The Renilla luciferase (REN) gene, driven by the 35S promoter in the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector, was used as an internal control. Transient dual-luciferase assays were conducted in maize protoplasts isolated from the leaves of 12-day-old etiolated seedlings of the inbred line B73. Maize protoplasts were prepared and transfected following the method described by Cao et al. (2012). The luciferase signal was detected using dual-luciferase assay reagents according to the manufacturer's instructions (Promega). Relative LUC activity was calculated by normalising LUC activity to REN activity.
