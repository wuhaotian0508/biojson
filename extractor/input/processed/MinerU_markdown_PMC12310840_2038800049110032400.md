# Summary

Plants inevitably encounter a diverse array of constantly changing environmental stresses, and drought stands out as one of the most severe threats to plants. Abscisic acid (ABA) and jasmonic acid (JA) work synergistically to increase plant drought tolerance, but their interplay during drought response remains elusive. Here, we uncovered that ABA induced the degradation of a negative transcription regulator, ethylene responsive factor (ERF.D2), in tomato drought tolerance. We identified that ERF.D2 was phosphorylated at Ser-52 by calcium-dependent protein kinase 27 (CPK27) in an ABA-dependent manner and underwent subsequent PUB22-mediated ubiquitination. Degradation of ERF.D2 leads to the increase of the transcript levels of JA biosynthesis genes, allene oxide cyclase (AOC) and 12-oxophytodienoic acid reductase 3 (OPR3), and endogenous concentration of JA, thus enhancing tomato plant drought tolerance. These findings demonstrate a novel insight into the molecular mechanism of ABA–JA synergistic interaction during tomato drought tolerance. 

# Introduction

As sessile organisms, plants must cope with constantly changing environments that often stress their growth and development. Among the various abiotic stresses, drought is a severe threat that significantly impacts crop productivity and global food security (Gupta et al., 2020). Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) is one of the most consumed vegetable crops globally, cultivated with high water demand throughout the growing season (Du et al., 2018). Tomato yields decreased by $2 6 \%$ when irrigation water was limited by $50 \%$ (Cui et al., 2020). However, most studies about the molecular mechanisms of drought tolerance are conducted in model plants, such as Arabidopsis and rice (Oryza sativa) (Gupta et al., 2020). It will be valuable to explore the drought response mechanisms in tomato to generate horticultural crop cultivars better adapted to drought stress in a changing climate. 

Abscisic acid (ABA) plays a dominant role in drought stress response by reducing growth, closing stomata, and acting as an integral signalling molecule that can regulate gene expression (Waadt et al., 2022). Mutations to PYRABACTIN RESISTANCE 1 LIKE (PYL), an ABA receptor, resulted in impaired drought tolerance (Zhao et al., 2016a). In the ABA signalling pathway, SNF1-related protein kinase 2s (SnRK2s) are activated by ABA and confer plant drought tolerance (Soma et al., 2020). Similarly, the calcium-dependent protein kinases (CPKs) are another kind of crucial ABA-responsive kinase and are capable of transmitting ABA signals into downstream phosphorylation events (Delormel and Boudsocq, 2019). Many studies have recorded the roles of CPKs in plant drought tolerance. For instance, Arabidopsis 

AtCPK1 (Huang et al., 2018), AtCPK4/11 (Zhu et al., 2007), and AtCPK8 (Zou et al., 2015) all play positive roles in ABA-mediated drought tolerance. In rice, drought-induced OsCPK4 also conferred plant drought tolerance via modifying lipid metabolism (Campo et al., 2014). Our previous data showed that tomato CPK27 mediated ABA signalling and enhanced plant drought tolerance by increasing soluble sugar accumulation (Zhu et al., 2024b). However, the mechanisms of CPK27-mediated tomato drought tolerance are still elusive and need further investigation. 

In addition to the ABA signalling pathway, other phytohormones also determine the drought tolerance of plants. Jasmonic acid (JA) is a vital hormone that is widely reported to positively regulate plant drought tolerance by promoting leaf stomatal closure, activating antioxidant systems, and increasing osmotic pressure (de Ollas et al., 2015; Xing et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023). JA is synthesized from phospholipids via a series of reactions catalysed by several enzymes, among which allene oxide cyclase (AOC) and 12-oxophytodienoic acid reductase (OPR) play vital roles (Wasternack and Song, 2016). Of note, JA biosynthesis was significantly induced by drought stress, thus leading to increased drought tolerance in sweet potato (Li et al., 2024). The positive JA signalling component MYC2 also enhanced tomato drought tolerance by promoting the JA and ABA contents in guard cells (Xu et al., 2022). Inversely, overexpression of OsJAZ9/10, repressors of JA signalling, attenuated rice plant drought tolerance (Singh et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b). Although the positive effects of JA in drought tolerance were documented, the mechanism of interplay between ABA and JA is a longstanding puzzle. 

Drought stress and ABA generally induce changes in numerous gene expressions under the control of a complex transcriptional regulatory network. Among the transcription factors, APETALA2/ethylene-responsive factors (AP2/ERFs) are vital transcription factors, acting as transcriptional activators or repressors, which bind to GCC boxes in the promoters of target genes (Feng et al., 2020). Many ERFs, such as OsERF71, OsERF62, PtoERF15, ZmERF21, and NtERF172 were identified as positive regulators in drought tolerance by regulating root growth, cell wall lignification, or ROS scavenging (Kong et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2022b; Xiong et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020). In contrast, overexpression of ERF.B1 in tomato repressed drought resistance, leading to a more sensitive phenotype to osmotic stress compared with wild-type (Wang et al., 2022a). In addition, another transcriptional repressor, ERF3, also negatively regulates drought tolerance in rice by reducing cellulose content (Sun et al., 2022). Therefore, specific ERFs may play distinct roles in drought response. Despite recent advances in understanding the roles of ERFs, the underlying mechanism of how ERFs integrate ABA with other hormones, particularly JA, to orchestrate the crucial transcriptional responses during drought stress response remains largely unknown. 

In response to drought stress, the negative regulators should be eliminated to increase plant tolerance. Ubiquitination-mediated proteasome degradation is a crucial mechanism for protein homeostasis management in eukaryotes. Plant U-box (PUB) E3 ligases are a group of U-box-containing E3 ligases, specifically ubiquitinating substrates to undergo 26S proteasomal degradation (Sharma and Taganna, 2020; Yang et al., 2021). In fact, multiple PUB E3 ubiquitin ligases have been identified as important components in response to drought stress. For instance, Arabidopsis PUB11 plays a negative role in drought tolerance by degrading the receptor-like protein kinases LEUCINE-RICH REPEAT PROTEIN 1 (LRR1) and KINASE 7 (KIN7) (Chen et al., 2021). PtPUB27 and OsPUB41 also act as negative regulators of drought stress response in potato and rice, respectively (Seo et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2020). On the contrary, overexpression of wheat TaPUB4 and potato PtPUB25 enhanced drought tolerance in transgenic Arabidopsis (Kim et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). However, the specific PUB functions in tomato plant drought resistance are still uncovered. 

In this study, we have identified ERF.D2 as a negative regulator in tomato drought tolerance. Subsequent investigations unveiled that ERF.D2 is phosphorylated by ABA-activated CPK27, which triggers ERF.D2 degradation by E3 ligase PUB22-mediated ubiquitination. Moreover, the degradation of ERF.D2 relieves transcriptional suppression of JA biosynthesis, which eventually induces JA accumulation to improve drought tolerance in tomato. These findings may deepen our understanding of the crosstalk between ABA and JA in drought response and provide potential genetic targets for the generation of drought-tolerant tomato varieties. 

# Results

# ERF.D2 is a target of CPK27 and negatively regulates drought tolerance in tomato

Our previous studies demonstrated that CPK27 mediates ABA signalling to promote tomato drought tolerance, and an ERF.D2 was identified as a potential interactor with CPK27 based on a yeast two-hybrid screen (Zhu et al., 2024b). We first examined the expression of ERF.D2 in response to drought, ABA, and 

mannitol treatments, and the results showed that the transcript levels of ERF.D2 were significantly increased under these treatments (Figure S1). To investigate whether ERF.D2 regulates tomato drought tolerance, two independent homozygous mutant lines, erf.d2#2 and erf.d2#6 (2 and 1 bp deletion in the first exon leading to an early stop codon at 52 sites and 56 sites, respectively), and two individual overexpression lines were generated (Figure S2). To test for stress tolerance, plants were subjected to drought stress by withholding water for 10 days. Surprisingly, the erf.d2 mutants exhibited attenuated indications of damage compared with WT, while overexpression of ERF.D2 reduced drought tolerance (Figure 1a). Furthermore, detached erf.d2 leaves displayed a lower rate of water loss relative to WT, while the OE-ERF.D2 leaves showed an increased rate (Figure 1b). Drought stress resulted in damage to cell membrane integrity, corresponding with a significant increase in electrolyte leakage and malondialdehyde (MDA) content in WT plants, while these increases were attenuated in erf.d2 mutants but significantly elevated in OE-ERF.D2 lines (Figure 1c,d). Consistently, ABA induced stomatal closure of WT, and the erf.d2 mutants and OE-ERF.D2 lines were more sensitive and insensitive to ABA treatment, respectively (Figure S3A,B). As a transcription factor, ERF.D2 was localized in the nuclear, and ABA or mannitol treatments had no influence on its subcellular localization (Figure S3C). Collectively, these results indicated that ERF.D2 negatively regulated tomato drought tolerance. 

To confirm the interaction between ERF.D2 and CPK27, we then performed a Y2H assay and the results showed that ERF.D2 directly interacted with CPK27 (Figure 2a). To further verify the interaction of ERF.D2 with CPK27 in vitro, pull-down assays were employed to demonstrate that His-CPK27 could be pulled down by MBP-ERF.D2 in vitro instead of the control MBP protein (Figure 2b). In addition, luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assays in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves showed that the ERF.D2–CPK27 interactions could be further enhanced by ABA treatment (Figure 2c,d). Consistently, co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP) also showed that the interaction between CPK27 and ERF.D2 was strengthened by exogenous ABA application (Figure 2e). To determine whether this interaction affected ERF.D2 protein stability, we performed a cell-free degradation assay of MBP-ERF.D2 protein incubated with the protein extracts of cpk27 mutants and WT plants. The results showed that MBP-ERF.D2 abundance was much higher when incubated in cpk27 mutant protein extractions than in WT (Figure 2f,g). Furthermore, transient expression of Myc-CPK27 reduced ERF.D2-GFP protein levels in N. benthamiana leaves, and this reduction was amplified by the application of protein synthesis inhibitor cycloheximide (CHX), but repressed by proteasome inhibitor (MG132; Figure 2h,i), indicating that the CPK27-mediated degradation of ERF.D2 might depend on the ubiquitin– proteasome system. Collectively, these results suggest that ABA promotes the interaction of CPK27 with ERF.D2, potentially leading to its degradation.

To identify the proteins that are responsible for the degradation of ERF.D2, we performed immunoprecipitation followed by mass spectrometry (IP-MS) using OE-ERF.D2 (#12) plants leaves, treated with ABA and MG132 before sample collection. Among putative ERF.D2-interacting proteins (Dataset S1), we focused on a U-box E3 ligase protein, PUB22, which could mediate protein ubiquitination (Wu et al., 2024a). We generated pub22 mutants and found that these mutants showed drought-sensitive phenotypes with an increased rate of water loss, elevated 

drought-induced electrolyte leakage, and MDA contents compared with WT (Figure S5A–D). 

Then, multiple protein–protein interaction assays demonstrated that ERF.D2 directly interacted with PUB22 in an ABA-dependent manner (Figure S6A–D). To investigate whether ERF.D2 is ubiquitinated by PUB22, in vitro ubiquitination assays were performed using purified MBP-ERF.D2 and GFP-PUB22- STREP proteins. The ubiquitinated forms of MBP-ERF.D2 were only observed in the presence of E1, E2, HA-tagged ubiquitin (Ubi), and PUB22. Lacking one of these factors led to the failed ubiquitination of the ERF.D2 protein (Figure 5a). In addition, we explored whether ERF.D2 is ubiquitinated by PUB22 in vivo and found that the ubiquitination level of ERF.D2 was significantly increased in the presence of PUB22, and ABA further enhanced

# ERF.D2 integrates ABA and JA signals to regulate tomato drought tolerance

Given that ABA could induce the degradation of ERF.D2 and ERF.D2 repressed JA biosynthesis (Figures 4 and 7), we assume that ERF.D2 might be vital in coordinating ABA and JA signalling to regulate tomato drought tolerance. To further investigate the in-depth mechanism of ERF.D2 in the ABA signal pathway, we applied exogenous ABA on OE-ERF.D2 lines and found that ABA failed to rescue the drought-sensitive phenotype of OE-ERF.D2 transgenic lines (Figure 8a,b). Furthermore, we also silenced several canonical ABA-related genes in erf.d2 mutant background, including PYL7 (encoding ABA receptor), CYP707 (encoding an ABA catabolic enzyme), and SnRK2.6 (encoding an ABA signalling component), which were previously reported as the crucial regulators in plant drought tolerance (Liang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2016b; Zhong et al., 2020). Notably, silencing these genes in erf.d2 mutants showed similar drought tolerance to erf.d2 mutant plants (Figure 8c,d), which suggested ERF.D2 acts downstream in the ABA signalling pathway to regulate plant drought tolerance. In addition, we also generated the cpk27 erf.d2 and pub22 erf.d2 double mutants and found these double mutants displayed stronger drought tolerance compared with cpk27 and pub22 single mutants, which displayed a severe wilting phenotype after withholding water for about 10 days (Figure 8e–h). Consistently, the reduced expression levels of AOC, OPR, and JA contents in cpk27 and pub22 mutants under drought stress were rescued by mutating ERF.D2 (Figure 8i–k). Taken together, these results substantiate that the ERF.D2 acts downstream of ABA-regulated CPK27 and PUB22 to regulate JA biosynthesis in tomato drought response.

# Multifaceted roles of ERFs in plant drought tolerance

In response to drought, the expression of stress-associated genes was regulated by various transcription factors. ERFs are plantspecific transcription factors and belong to the large AP2/ERF multigene family (Klay et al., 2018). Previously, several studies suggested that ERFs acted as integrative nodes in the regulation of a series of drought-responsive genes in many plant species, and different ERFs may play distinct roles. For instance, overexpression of OsERF71 increased rice drought tolerance by increasing cellulose content in plants, especially in vascular bundles (Lee et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2022). In apple, MdERF38 was found to interact with MdMYB1, promoting droughtinduced anthocyanin biosynthesis (An et al., 2020). In addition, overexpression of Populus ERF15 and Arabidopsis ERF1 both promoted plant drought resistance via modifying cell wall thickness in the xylem by binding to GCC box or DRE/CRT elements in the promoter region of its target genes (Cheng et al., 2013; Kong et al., 2023). In tomato, overexpression of SlERF1, SlERF5, and SlERF84 enhanced tolerance to drought stress (Li et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2011; Pan et al., 2012). 

However, several ERFs have also been found to play negative roles in plant drought tolerance. For example, overexpression of OsERF3 significantly increased rice drought susceptibility due to decreased cellulose content in the xylem (Sun et al., 2022). The Betula platyphylla BpERF11 also negatively regulated osmotic tolerance by modulating various physiological processes (Zhang et al., 2016). Herein, we have identified a novel negative regulator, ERF.D2, during drought stress in tomato. Knocking out ERF.D2 increased plant drought tolerance, while the overexpression lines showed drought-sensitive phenotypes (Figure 1). Similarly, a recent study also uncovered the function of another negative regulator, SlERF.B1, where overexpression in tomato and Arabidopsis caused significantly high sensitivity to mannitol and drought treatment compared with WT (Wang et al., 2022a). Of note, our data showed that the expression of ERF.D2 was significantly induced by drought stress and ABA treatment (Figure S1), and the expression of drought negative regulators, including OsERF3, BpERF11, and SlERF.B1 were also increased by ABA or osmotic stress (Wang et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2013, 2016). These ERFs may be involved in the negative feedback regulation. Environmental stresses induced their expression to activate their downstream stress-responsive gene expression. After that, their function was repressed to inhibit the overactivation of the stress-related signalling pathway through negative feedback regulation, so as to prevent excessive damage to plant cells. 

Based on the function, ERFs can be divided into two kinds of transcriptional regulators: activators or repressors. Repressive 

transcriptional regulators inhibit target gene expression either passively, by competing with activators on promoters, or actively. Active repressors include independent repression domains to recruit corepressor proteins, thus inhibiting the initiation of transcription (Plant et al., 2021). EAR motifs (DLNxxP and LxLxL) are well known to be involved in plant transcriptional repression (Ma et al., 2017; Ohta et al., 2001). For example, the EAR motifs in NtERF3 are necessary to repress the transcription of its target genes (Ohta et al., 2001). In addition, a fusion of the EAR domain to the VP16 activation domain can completely inhibit the transactivation of VP16 (Ohta et al., 2001). However, sequence analysis did not find the canonical EAR motif in the ERF.D2 protein (Figure S8). Of note, a previous study found that an atypical sequence, LxL, present in the D53 protein, was a functional EAR motif and contributes to the interaction of D53 and TOPLESS corepressors (Ma et al., 2017). Based on this, we have found an LxL sequence in the ERF.D2 protein (Figure S8). Interestingly, another repressive ERF transcription factor, SlERF.B1, also possesses an LxL sequence, which is not found in other activating ERFs in tomato, including SlERF1 and SlERF84 (Figure S8). It is reasonable to speculate that the LxL motif might act as a key motif to determine the active or negative role of tomato ERFs in transcription regulation, which needs verification in future studies. 

# Post-transcriptional modifications of ERF.D2 determine its protein stability

Post-translational modifications (PTMs) enable organisms to respond precisely and rapidly to environmental stimuli by modulating protein activity. Phosphorylation and ubiquitination are common PTMs and play crucial roles in protein quality control in eukaryotes (Lee et al., 2023). Herein, we demonstrated that CPK27 could directly phosphorylate ERF.D2 at Ser-52 in an ABAdependent manner (Figure 3a–d). Mutation of Ser-52 to mimic the dephosphorylated form of ERF.D2 resulted in increased protein stability (Figure 3e,f). In addition, we also discovered that ABA and CPK27-mediated phosphorylation enhanced the ubiquitination of ERF.D2 by PUB22 and led to subsequent 26S proteasome-mediated degradation (Figure 5b–g). Knocking out ERF.D2 in $c p k 2 7$ and pub22 mutants increased their drought tolerance, further highlighting the significance of phosphorylation and ubiquitination on ERF.D2 function (Figure 8). These results demonstrate that CPK27 and PUB22 work synergistically to regulate ERF.D2 protein stability. # ERF.D2 cross-links ABA and JA signalling pathways to regulate tomato drought tolerance

ABA plays a dominant role in plant drought stress resistance, and numerous studies have reported that ERFs are involved in ABAmediated drought tolerance. For instance, ABA highly induced 

the expression of GmERF75, and overexpression of GmERF75 enhanced Arabidopsis and soybean osmotic stress tolerance (Zhao et al., 2019). In rice, overexpression of OsERF83, which was induced by ABA, significantly increased drought tolerance by regulating drought-responsive genes (Jung et al., 2021). Interestingly, we have found that exogenous ABA significantly reduced the ERF.D2 protein level (Figure 4), which was regulated by CPK27- and PUB22-mediated post-transcriptional modifications (Figures 2 and 5). Several pieces of evidence further supported the negative function of ERF.D2 in ABA-regulated drought tolerance. First, the ABA-induced stomatal closure was enhanced in erf.d2 mutants, but attenuated in OE-ERF.D2 lines (Figure S3A, B). Second, ABA application increased WT plant drought tolerance rather than OE-ERF.D2 transgenic lines (Figure 8a,b). In addition, the enhanced drought resistance of erf.d2 mutants was not impaired by silencing ABA-related genes, including PYL7, CYP707, and $S n R K 2 . 6$ (Figure 8c,d). These results proved that ERF.D2 acts as a negative component downstream of the ABA signalling pathway in tomato drought response. Interestingly, the Arabidopsis ERF type protein RAP2.6, a homologous protein of ERF.D2, played a positive role in the ABA signalling pathway (Zhu 

et al., 2020). The molecular mechanisms underlying the distinct functions of these two homologous proteins in the ABA signalling pathway require further investigation. 

As a class of vital transcription factors, ERFs can regulate JA biosynthesis to cope with diverse stresses. In tomato, low temperature-induced SlERF.B8 could trigger JA biosynthesis by binding to LOXD, AOC, and OPR3 promoters to promote cold tolerance (Ding et al., 2022). During herbivory, ethylene-induced ERF15 and ERF16 promoted JA biosynthesis, increasing cotton bollworm resistance in tomato (Hu et al., 2021a). However, whether ERFs regulate JA biosynthesis during drought remained elusive. Unexpectedly, we provide evidence here that ERF.D2 repressed JA biosynthesis by binding to the promoters of AOC and OPR3 to suppress their expression (Figure 7). Similarly, the banana MaERF10 was also found to bind to the promoters of JA biosynthesis genes, including MaLOX7/8, MaAOC3, and MaOPR4, thus repressing their expression and JA content during cold stress (Qi et al., 2016). 

Notably, we reported that JA biosynthesis acts downstream of ABA in plant drought tolerance, evidenced by ABA-induced ERF.D2 degradation and ERF.D2-repressed JA biosynthesis (Figures 4 and 7). Besides ERF.D2, several other transcription factors also participated in ABA-JA signal crosstalk. For example, ABA promoted the transcription of MYC2, which activated the JA biosynthesis genes to increase endogenous JA accumulation (Avramova, 2017). Similarly, bZIP72 was phosphorylated by SAPK10, which is an ABA-inducible SnRK2 type kinase, and then promoted the expression of AOC and accumulation of JA content, thus mediating the interplay between ABA and JA (Wang et al., 2020). However, some other studies posit that the effects of JA on plant drought tolerance depend on ABA signalling. For instance, exogenous application of JA increased leaf ABA concentration, while JA deficiency decreased ABA content in citrus (de Ollas et al., 2013). Methyl jasmonate (MeJA)-induced stomatal closure was impaired in ABA-deficient Arabidopsis mutant or wild-type plants pretreated with ABA biosynthesis inhibitor (Hossain et al., 2011). Furthermore, MYC2, a JA-positive regulator, directly repressed the expression of the negative regulator of ABA signalling, SlPP2C1, to promote ABAmediated stomatal closure (Zhao et al., 2023). These results suggest that the interplay between ABA and JA may be reciprocal and that JA could regulate ABA signalling in a feedback manner. This sophisticated crosstalk ensures plants flexibly adjust their physiological state to cope with multiple environmental stresses. 

In summary, this study revealed ERF.D2 as a vital hub in ABA and JA crosstalk during drought stress. The phosphorylation of ERF.D2 mediated by ABA-activated CPK27 enhanced its ubiquitination by PUB22. ERF.D2 negatively regulated tomato drought tolerance through JA biosynthesis repression. This intricate mechanism provides putative targets for improving horticultural crop tolerance in response to the scarcity of global water resources through molecular breeding. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant material and growth condition

Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum cv. Condine Red, CR) was used as wild-type (WT) in this study. The erf.d2 mutants were generated using the CRISPR-Cas9 technique (Figure S2A). The $c p k 2 7$ and pub22 mutants were generated as previously described (Wu et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024b). The $C p k 2 7$ erf.d2 and pub22 erf.d2 double mutants were generated by crossing erf.d2 with 

cpk27 and pub22, respectively. To generate overexpression transgenic lines, the coding sequence (CDS) of ERF.D2 was PCR-amplified from tomato cDNA and inserted into pAC402. The resulting construct was transformed into CR to generate 35S: ERF.D2-GFP (OE-ERF.D2) plants. The GFP-tagged transgenic plants, identified by Western blot (Figure S2B) with 150–250 times the normal levels of ERF.D2 transcript (Figure S2C), were used in this study. To generate the VIGS vectors, a 300 bp fragment of CDS in target genes, generated by the SGN VIGS Tool (https://vigs.solgenomics.net/), was amplified from cDNA using TransStart $\textcircled{1}$ FastPfu DNA Polymerase (AP221, TransGen Biotech, China) and cloned into the pTRV2 using the Spark DNA Quick Ligation Kit (AK0901, Shandong Sparkjade Biotechnology Co., Ltd.). Then, VIGS vectors were electroporated into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. The leaves of plants infected with A. tumefaciens were taken for gene expression detection. The tomato VIGS plants with higher than $70 \%$ silencing efficiency were selected for experiments (Figure S9). 

Germinated tomato seeds were sown in pots filled with a mixture of peat and vermiculite (2:1, v/v) and received Hoagland nutrient solution. The growth conditions were $1 4 ~ { \mathsf { h } } / 1 0 ~ { \mathsf { h } }$ (day/- night) photoperiod, 25 °C/20 $^ \circ \mathsf C$ (day/night) ambient air temperature, $4 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l / m } } ^ { 2 } / { \xi }$ photosynthetic photon flux density. 

# Drought tolerance analysis

For drought tolerance assay, different genotypes of tomato plants, about 30-days-old, were subjected to drought stress by withholding water supply for a duration of up to 10 days. Plant drought tolerance was assessed by quantifying water loss, MDA contents, and relative electrolyte leakage. For the water loss analysis, detached terminal leaflets of leaves no. 3 and 4 were cut and placed on a piece of weighing paper in the growth chamber. The leaves were periodically weighed every hour for $5 \textrm { h }$ . Water loss is shown as a percentage of the original fresh weight. For MDA content and relative electrolyte leakage, each genotype of tomato plants was grown under normal conditions or withheld from water for 7 days before detection. The MDA contents were measured using MDA Content Assay Kit (BC0025, Beijing Solarbio Science & Technology Co., Ltd.). The relative electrolyte leakages were determined as previously described (Hu et al., 2021b).

The electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) assay was performed based on a previous study (Zhu et al., 2024a). Singlestranded oligonucleotides were biotin end-labelled using the Biotin $3 ^ { \prime }$ -End DNA Labeling Kit (Pierce, Appleton, WI, USA) and annealed to obtain double-stranded probes. The EMSAs were performed using purified MBP-ERF.D2 protein and probes under the instruction of a Light Shift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The DNA probes used in the EMSA are listed in Table S1. The protein–nucleic acid complex structures were predicted by Hefei Kejing Biotechnology Co., Ltd. 

The dual-LUC assay was performed based on a previous study with minor modifications (Zhu et al., 2024a). The promoters of AOC or OPR3 were amplified and inserted into the pGreen II 0800-LUC vector. The constructs were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 with the pSoup vector. A. tumefaciens containing ERF.D2-GFP and LUC-AOC or LUC-OPR3 constructs were mixed at a 10:1 ratio and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves at a concentration of $\mathrm { O D 6 0 0 } = 0 . 7 5$ . At 3 days after infection, the transcriptional activity of the promoter was determined using the Dualucif $\textcircled{8}$ Firefly & Renilla Assay Kit (F6075, US EVERBRIGHT, Suzhou, China). 

For the ChIP-qPCR assays, 30-days-old OE-ERF.D2 plants were grown under normal or drought conditions for 7 days. Approximately, $1 . 0 ~ 9$ leaf samples were collected and subjected to formaldehyde to cross-link DNA and proteins. The ChIP assay was further carried out using the EpiQuikTM Plant ChIP Kit (Epigentek). Chromatin was immunoprecipitated with anti-GFP antibody. The goat anti-mouse IgG antibody (Abcam, ab205719, UK) was used as the negative control. ChIP-qPCR was performed with specific primers as given in Table S1. 

# Cell-free protein degradation assays

Briefly, total proteins were extracted from the 30-days-old indicated tomato plants using the extraction buffer. The recombinant MBP-ERF.D2 protein and its variants were then incubated in the total protein extracts. The mixture was incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for indicated periods, and the proteins were collected and separated by SDS–PAGE. The recombinant MBP-ERF.D2 protein and its variants were detected by immunoblot with anti-MBP antibody. The HSP70 protein was detected by immunoblot with anti-HSP70 antibody (A12948, ABclonal). 

# Statistical analysis

Unless otherwise stated, at least three independent biological replicates were sampled for each determination. For RT-qPCR, 

each biological replicate consisted of an independent sample that was pooled from two leaves, each taken from a different plant. For the measurement of electrolyte leakage, MDA, and JA contents, each biological sample was collected from six individual plants grown under identical conditions to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of the results. The obtained data were subjected to GraphPad InStat software 8.3.0 for Windows (GraphPad Software Inc., La Jolla, CA) for statistical analysis.
