# ABSTRACT

Panax vietnamensis, a medicinally valuable perennial herb, is highly susceptible to leaf blight under cultivation; however, the molecular mechanisms underlying this disease remain poorly understood. In this study, we identified Neofusicoccum ribis as the causal agent of P. vietnamensis leaf blight through pathogen isolation and fulfilment of Koch's postulates. Transcriptomic analysis revealed activation of phytohormone signalling (salicylic acid, jasmonic acid, and melatonin [MT]) and phenylpropanoid metabolism during infection. Among these, MT exhibited superior efficacy in inducing lignin biosynthesis compared to other hormones, with exogenous application of MT significantly enhancing lignin accumulation and improving disease resistance by 8 days post-inoculation. Further, we identified PvWRKY40 as a negative regulator of lignin synthesis, which directly binds to the W-box motif in the PvCOMT2 promoter to suppress its expression. MT counteracted this repression by downregulating PvWRKY40. Heterologous overexpression of PvCOMT2 in Nicotiana benthamiana increased lignin content and conferred enhanced resistance to Fusarium oxysporum. This study reveals a novel MT-PvWRKY40–PvCOMT2 regulatory axis governing lignin-mediated defence in P. vietnamensis, providing critical insights for combating leaf blight in cultivated ginseng. 

# 1 | Introduction

The genus Panax (Araliaceae, Apiales), comprising 15 recognised species and 7 subspecies as of 2020 (Boopathi et al. 2020), represents a taxonomically compact yet pharmacologically significant group primarily distributed in East Asia and eastern North America. Renowned for their medicinal properties, Panax species produce tetracyclic triterpenoid saponins as their principal 

bioactive compounds (Zhou et  al.  2018). Among them, P. ginseng (Asian ginseng), P. notoginseng (sanqi), P. quinquefolius (American ginseng), and P. japonicus (Japanese ginseng) have been extensively studied. Notably, Panax vietnamensis, first discovered in central Vietnam (Dung and Grushvitski 1985), has attracted increasing attention because of its exceptionally high saponin content (Le et al. 2015). Phytochemical analyses reveal that P. vietnamensis saponins are predominantly of ocotillol-type compounds, with majonoside-R2 accounting for over $5 0 \%$ of total saponins $5 . 6 \%$ dry weight), alongside dammarane-type saponins (ginsenosides Rh1, Rb1, Rb2, Re, Rg1; notoginsenosides R1, R2) and oleanolic acid derivatives (Duc et  al.  1994). Pharmacological studies have demonstrated its broad therapeutic potential, including anti-inflammatory (Jeong et  al.  2015; Lee et  al.  2015), antitumor (Konoshima et  al.  1999; Le et  al.  2015), hepatoprotective (Tran et al. 2001, 2002) and neuroprotective activities (Nguyen et al. 1996; Vu-Huynh et al. 2019). This pharmacological prominence has driven rapid cultivation expansion, with planting areas in Honghe Prefecture, China, reaching 53 ha and fresh rhizomes commanding prices of $\yen 80000/\mathrm{ k g }$ (Li et al. 2022). 

However, intensive monoculture practices have predisposed P. vietnamensis to pathogen outbreaks. Although root rot caused by Fusarium oxysporum poses significant threats (Chen et al. 2024), foliar diseases—particularly leaf blight—have emerged as major constraints. Leaf blight, reported in over 80 plant species including major crops (e.g., Zea mays, Oryza sativa) and medicinal plants (e.g., Leonurus japonicus, P. notoginseng), exhibits pathogen-specific symptomatology. For example, Pestalotiopsis trachicarpicola induces chlorotic lesions in P. notoginseng (Lin et  al.  2023), whereas Neofusicoccum ribis—the causal agent of P. vietnamensis leaf blight identified in our preliminary study— causes progressive chlorosis, desiccation and premature leaf abscission (Ai et  al.  2024). With annual incidence rates reaching $2 0 \%$ , this disease results in substantial economic losses; however, its molecular basis remains poorly understood. 

Plants employ multilayered defence strategies against pathogens, including pattern-triggered immunity (PTI), effectortriggered immunity (ETI), phytoalexin biosynthesis, and cell wall reinforcement via lignin deposition. Recent studies highlight the dual role of melatonin (MT) in plant immunity: direct antifungal activity at high concentrations (Chen et al. 2018) and priming stomatal immunity through mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signalling at low doses (Yang et al. 2021). Additionally, MT regulates secondary metabolism through epigenetic regulation (Gao et al. 2020) and lignin biosynthesis (Zhao et al. 2022; Li et al. 2019). 

WRKY transcription factors (TFs), characterised by their conserved WRKYGQK motif and zinc-finger domains, are central regulators of plant immunity and lignin biosynthesis. For example, AtWRKY13 activates lignin biosynthesis genes by binding the AtNST2 promoter (Li et  al.  2015), whereas LrWRKY11 in Lilium regale enhances resistance to Fusarium through LrDIR1- mediated lignin accumulation (Deng et al. 2024). Emerging evidence also suggests a link between WRKYs and MT signalling in stress responses (Zhu et al. 2023; Sun et al. 2023). 

Here, we integrated transcriptomic and molecular approaches to elucidate the defence mechanisms of P. vietnamensis against N. ribis. Our results demonstrate that MT-mediated suppression of PvWRKY40 derepresses PvCOMT2 expression, thereby enhancing lignin biosynthesis and disease resistance. This study 

not only delineates a novel MT-WRKY-lignin regulatory axis but also provides actionable insights for sustainable management of P. vietnamensis leaf blight. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Neofusicoccum ribis is the Pathogen of Leaf Blight in P. vietnamensis

Pathogenic fungi were isolated from leaf blight-infected P. vietnamensis leaves collected in the P. vietnamensis planting base. A dominant isolate (strain 13) exhibited morphological characteristics of Neofusicoccum: colonies transitioning from white to dark grey on the obverse and white to black on the reverse side on potato dextrose agar (PDA) (Figure 1a), with elliptical conidia (Figure 1b). Phylogenetic analysis on the basis of the rDNA internal transcribed spacer (ITS) (NCBI accession number: OR960635) and translation elongation factor-1α gene (TEF-1α) (NCBI accession number: PP789254) using maximum parsimony in MEGA found strain 13 within the N. ribis clade alongside isolates from Prunus sp. and Mangifera indica (Figure  1c). Pathogenicity assays confirmed N. ribis as the causal agent: inoculated leaves developed chlorosis (Day 2), yellowing (Day 4), defoliation (Day 6) and necrosis (Day 8), culminating in plant death (Figure 1d). Reisolated pathogens matched N. ribis via ITS/TEF-1α sequencing (Figure  S1), fulfilling Koch's postulates. This study identifies N. ribis as a novel and aggressive pathogen responsible for P. vietnamensis leaf blight. 

# 2.2 | Transcriptome Analysis of P. vietnamensis Infected by N. ribis

Transcriptomic profiling of N. ribis-infected P. vietnamensis was performed at 0, 2, 4, 6 and 8 days post-inoculation (dpi). RNAseq generated high-quality clean reads (10.48–14.02 million per sample; $Q 3 0 > 9 6 \%$ , GC content $4 2 \% - 4 6 \%$ ). Differential expression analysis on the basis of FPKM values revealed progressive upregulation/downregulation of genes over time: 242 upregulated/1176 downregulated (2 dpi vs. 0 dpi), increasing to 4007 upregulated/6907 downregulated (8 dpi vs. 0 dpi) (Figure 2a). Venn diagram analysis identified 346 persistently upregulated and 834 persistently downregulated genes (Figure 2b). Enrichment of these core differentially expressed genes (DEGs) highlighted phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (e.g., flavonoid metabolism) and phytohormone signalling pathways (e.g., MAPK cascades, ethylene response, jasmonate response, salicylate responses and defence activation) (Figure 2c,d, Tables S3 and S4). These findings underscore the dynamic transcriptional reprogramming in P. vietnamensis during N. ribis infection, with sustained activation of metabolic and defence-associated pathways.

# 2.6 | Mechanistic Validation of PvWRKY40–PvCOMT2 Regulatory Interplay

MT upregulated PvCOMT1/2 expression while downregulating PvWRKY40. Promoter analysis identified W-box motifs in PvCOMT1/2, suggesting potential regulation by PvWRKY40. Dual-luciferase assays revealed PvWRKY40 selectively suppresses PvCOMT2 (Figure  6a,b), with MT treatment reversing 

this repression (Figure 6c). Electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) using prokaryotically expressed PvWRKY40 confirmed direct and specific binding to the PvCOMT2 promoter W-box motif (−491 bp), evidenced by cold probe competition and abolished binding with mutated probes (Figure 6d,e). These results establish PvWRKY40 as a direct transcriptional repressor of PvCOMT2, with MT antagonising this interaction to promote lignin biosynthesis.

# 3 | Discussion

# 3.1 | Neofusicoccum ribis as a Novel Pathogen of P. vietnamensis Leaf Blight

Neofusicoccum ribis (Ascomycota: Botryosphaeriaceae), initially identified as a pathogen of Ribes spp. (Tai 1979), has since been associated with various diseases in multiple hosts, including stem blight in blueberry (Heger et al. 2022) and postharvest decay in apple (Jurick et  al.  2013). This study is the first to confirm N. ribis as the causal agent of leaf blight in P. vietnamensis, characterised by rapid symptom onset (4 dpi; Figure 1d) in contrast to slower progression in Hevea brasiliensis (10 days; Nyaka et al. 2013) and Prunus armeniaca (7 days; Ilyukhin and Ellouze  2023). Notably, although Pestalotiopsis 

trachicarpicola induces leaf blight in P. notoginseng (7-day latency; Lin et  al.  2023), N. ribis demonstrates greater aggressiveness in P. vietnamensis, correlating with its severe yield impact $2 0 \% - 3 0 \%$ annual incidence; Ai et al. 2024). This hostspecific pathogenicity highlights the questions about N. ribis's potential to infect P. notoginseng and vice versa, warranting cross-inoculation studies. 

# 3.2 | Lignin and Phytohormone Dynamics During N. ribis Infection

Plants deploy lignin and phytohormones as key defence strategies. In P. vietnamensis, infection by N. ribis induced the coordinated upregulation of SA, JA and MT biosynthesis (Figure 3f–h), paralleling phenylpropanoid pathway activation (Figure 2). Lignin accumulation, a conserved defence mechanism observed in rice (Oryza sativa; Wang et  al.  2025) and cassava (Manihot esculenta; Yan et al. 2024), peaked at 4 dpi (Figure 3e), coinciding with the onset of physiological collapse (Ai et al. 2024). # 3.4 | MT Signalling via the PvWRKY40–PvCOMT2 Axis

MT's role in lignin biosynthesis, previously observed in cotton (Li et  al.  2019) and peony (Zhao et  al. 2022), remains mechanistically undefined. Here, MT suppressed PvWRKY40 expression (Figure 5f–h), thereby relieving transcriptional repression of PvCOMT2 and promoting lignin accumulation (Figure  6c). This mechanism differs from canonical MT signalling via the PMTR1–G protein complex or MPK3/6 cascades (Wei, Li, et al. 2018; Yang et  al.  2021). Notably, MAPK-WRKY cross-talk reported in Arabidopsis (Wang et  al.  2023) suggests potential upstream signalling intermediates linking MT to PvWRKY40 repression. Further studies should clarify whether MT-activated kinases phosphorylate PvWRKY40, thereby modulating its stability or DNA-binding affinity. 

This study delineates a novel defence axis in P. vietnamensis: MT-mediated suppression of PvWRKY40 derepresses 

PvCOMT2, leading to lignin accumulation that impedes N. ribis progression (Figure 8). Although SA/JA contributes to broader defence responses, MT's specificity for lignin induction highlights its potential as a targeted biostimulant. Future research should explore the cross-talk between MT, SA and JA in Panax defence; evolutionary divergence in COMT1/2 regulation across Panax species; application of MT or PvCOMT2-overexpressing lines to manage leaf blight in field. These findings enhance our understanding of MT–WRKY interactions and offer actionable strategies for sustainable P. vietnamensis cultivation.

# 4.3 | Neofusicoccum ribis Infestation of Leaves in P. vietnamensis

As previously reported (Yang et al. 2022), the leaves of 3-year-old P. vietnamensis were inoculated with a $5 { \mathrm { m m } } N .$ ribis fungus block by the needle puncture method, kept in the greenhouse for heat preservation and moisture preservation, and after hyphae were removed at 0, 2, 4, 6 and 8 dpi, washed with sterile water three times. 

# 4.4 | RNA Extraction and RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from P. vietnamensis leaves using TRIzol reagent (Takara). A $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ RNA sample was reverse transcribed using PrimeScript RT reagent kit (with gDNA Eraser; Takara). All primers used for RT-qPCR are listed in Table  S1. PvACTIN7 was used as an internal reference gene. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). 

# 4.5 | Transcriptome Analysis

RNA samples extracted from N. ribis infected P. vietnamensis at 0, 2, 4, 6 and 8 dpi were sequenced by Illumina NovaSeq X Plus sequencing platform (NCBI BioProject ID: PRJNA1212020). 

# 4.6 | Lignin Content Detection

Panax vietnamensis leaves were dried and ground into powder. $0 . 1 \mathrm { g }$ sample was hydrolysed with 4 M NaOH solution at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . After cooling, 1.6 mL 6 M HCl was added for neutralisation. After centrifugation at $_ { 1 5 , 5 0 0 g }$ for 5 min, $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of supernatant was extracted twice with 1 mL of ethyl acetate, then the organic phase was collected, evaporated with nitrogen, reconstituted with ultrapure water, and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter, and their concentrations were analysed by HPLC-MS, as described previously (Hou et al. 2024). 

# 4.7 | Endogenous Hormone Content Detection

Three grams of  P. vietnamensis leaves was weighed, ground in liquid nitrogen, and placed in a test tube, isopropanolwater-hydrochloric acid mixed extract was added, $8 \mu \mathrm { L } 1 \mu \mathrm { g } /$ mL internal standard solution was added, and it was shaken at low temperature for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Dichloromethane was added, shaken at low temperature for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , and centrifuged at low temperature at $_ { 1 5 , 5 0 0 g }$ for 5 min, organic phase was collected, and light was avoided; it was blown dry with nitrogen, redissolved with methanol $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid), filtered with $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter membrane, and detected with HPLC-MS/MS/MS. Using methanol $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid) as a solvent, the standard solutions of SA, JA and MT with gradients of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 2, 5, 20, 50 and $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ were prepared. HPLC: mobile phase A is methanol $/ 0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, and mobile phase B is water ${ \it 1 0 . 1 \% }$ formic acid. Agilent Poroshell 120 SB-C18 reversed-phase chromatographic column $( 2 . 1 \times 1 5 0$ , $2 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ ) was used. The gradient programme of chromatography was $0 { - } 1 \mathrm { m i n }$ , $a = 2 0 \%$ ; 1–9 min, A increased to $8 0 \%$ ; $9 { - } 1 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , $A = 8 0 \%$ ; 10–10.1 min, A decreases to $2 0 \%$ ; 10.1–15 min, $A = 2 0 \%$ . Sample volume: $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ , column temperature: $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.8 | Exogenous Hormone Treatment

$1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ SA, JA and MT solutions were prepared and sprayed on the leaves of 3-year-old P. vietnamensis. Samples were taken at 0, 3, 6, 9 and $1 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ after spraying, and the collected samples were quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.9 | Subcellular Localisation

The subcellular localisation of PvWRKY40 (NCBI accession number: PQ899221) was analysed. The coding region of PvWRKY40 was amplified by gene-specific primers (Table S1). The PvWRKY40 gene was fused with the YFP (yellow fluorescent protein) gene, and under the control of the CaMV 35S promoter, the vector pCAMBIA1300-35S-PvWRKY40-YFP was generated. The YFP signal was observed by a laser scanning confocal microscope 

(Nikon). The image capture wavelength is YFP fluorescence (excitation: 511nm, emission: 525nm) and chlorophyll autofluorescence (excitation: $6 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , emission: 675nm). 

# 4.10 | Construction of Transgenic N. benthamiana Plants

The target gene cloned in the early stage of the experiment was connected to the pBWA(V)HS linear vector by homologous recombination method to obtain pBWA(V)HS-PvCOMT2×MYC recombinant vector. N. benthamiana was transformed by leaf disc method, and transgenic plants were cultivated. Overexpression positive plants of PvCOMT2 were identified by hygromycin screening. 

# 4.11 | Disease Resistance Detection

The 5 mm plugs of F. oxysporum were inoculated on the leaves of transgenic plants by needle puncture and kept warm and moist in the greenhouse. After 3 days of treatment, the phenotype was observed. 

# 4.12 | Agrobacterium-Mediated Overexpression of PvCOMT2 and PvWRKY40 in P. vietnamensis Callus

The overexpression vector was transformed into P. vietnamensis callus with reference to the constructed transient expression system (Xian et  al.  2023). As mentioned above, RNA was extracted from P. vietnamensis and reverse-transcribed into cDNA, PvCOMT2 (NCBI accession number: PQ899222) and PvWRKY40 genes were amplified, and overexpression vectors pBWA(V)HS-PvCOMT2 and pBWA(V)HS-PvWRKY40 were constructed. The ligation products were transformed into competent Escherichia coli, and recombinant expression plasmids were extracted from $E$ . coli, transformed into competent cells of Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 and co-cultured with P. vietnamensis callus. 

# 4.13 | Double Luciferase Reporter Gene Experiment

Using cDNA as a template, the sequence of PvWRKY40 was cloned (Table  S2), and the promoter sequence of PvCOMT2 was cloned to construct on the pGreen-62-sk vector, and the promoter sequence was constructed on the pGreen-0800-LUC vector (Takara). The constructed vector was transferred into A. tumefaciens GV1301(pSoup) and coated on Luria Bertani (LB) medium with rifampicin and kanamycin for inverted culture for 2 days, the Agrobacterium was gently scraped off the plate and put in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ LB medium with antibiotics for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ , then the bacteria were centrifuged and precipitated, and it was resuspended with suspension medium (magnesium chloride and acetosyringone) to $\mathrm { { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 1 . 0$ and allowed to stand at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . N. benthamiana was injected and $2 \mathrm { m g / L }$ firefly fluorescein potassium salt was sprayed after 2 days for observation. The fluorescence intensity was detected by using the double fluorescein reporter gene detection kit (Xiasheng Bio). 

# 4.14 | EMSA

The full-length coding sequence (CDS) of PvWRKY40 was cloned into the prokaryotic expression vector pET-28a- $6 \times \mathrm { H I S }$ to construct HIS-PvWRKY40. The transformed cells were cultured at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 4 \mathrm { h }$ in LB medium supplemented with kanamycin. Positive clones were selected and propagated by shaking at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 4 { - } 0 . 5$ . Isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside (1 mM, IPTG) was added to trigger protein expression and incubated at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 140 rpm for $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ . Then, the cells were centrifuged, 4 times the weight of bacterial lysate was added, and it was precipitated, resuspended, and ultrasonically crushed for 20 min. It was centrifuged at ${ 1 8 , 0 0 0 g }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Finally, protein was purified by using a His-fusion protein purification kit (BeyotimeBiotechnology). The EMSA probe was synthesised in Sangon Biotech, and protein was fused with the labelled probe according to the instructions of the EMSA kit (Beyotime Biotechnology) to prepare a non-denatured polyacrylamide gel, and then experiments including electrophoresis detection, membrane transfer, washing, and development were completed.
