# ABSTRACT

Saline-­ alkali stress inhibited the normal growth and development of plants, which seriously restricted the yield of crops. Maize is one of the most important crops in the world. However, the mechanism of maize in response to saline-­ alkali stress is still largely unknown. Through the observation of growth parameters and the detection of physiological and biochemical indicators in saline-­ alkali tolerant (22KN3894) and saline-­ alkali sensitive (H23146) maize inbred lines, this study found that compared with H23146, 22KN3894 accumulated less ROS content and more total flavonoids content, while the degree of root damage and ion toxicity was relatively small. Full-­ length transcriptome and broadly targeted metabolome were used to analyse the response mechanism of extreme maize inbred lines to saline-­ alkali stress. 22KN3894 accumulated more metabolites such as sugars and flavonoids. There were significant differences in the contents of flavonoid metabolites and genes related to flavonoid synthesis between the two materials. Weighted gene co-­ expression network analysis and co-­ expression network analysis based on RNA-- Seq data suggested that the ZmWRKY82 gene might respond to saline-­ alkali stress by regulating the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway. ZmWRKY82 directly bound to the W-­ box in the ZmCHI6 promoter and promoted its expression. The above results showed that ZmWRKY82 could improve the antioxidant capacity by promoting the transcription of ZmCHI6 and the synthesis of flavonoids, thereby resisting saline-­ alkali stress. These findings provided novel insights for improving maize saline-­ alkali stress tolerance, demonstrating that flavonoids played pivotal roles in plant stress adaptation, and laid the foundation for future mech‑ anistic studies and breeding improvement. 

# 1 | Introduction

Plants are affected by environmental stresses during growth and development, including saline-­ alkali, drought, cold, and high temperatures, which severely restrict normal plant growth and development (Wang, Hu, et al. 2023; Yang and Guo 2018). Saline-- alkali soil is one of the most common soil types worldwide, and 

its impact on agricultural production and the ecological envi‑ ronment has become a global issue. It is estimated that more than $5 0 \%$ of arable land will be affected by salinization by 2025 (Zhang, Yu, et al. 2023). However, at present, a large number of studies focus on the study of salt stress, but there are relatively few studies on saline-­ alkali stress, which restricts the devel‑ opment of crop yield enhancement technology in saline-­ alkali 

land. Therefore, cultivating saline-­ alkali tolerant crops is an important means to deal with soil salinization and meet future food demand. 

The natural alkali salts in saline-­ alkali land are sodium hy‑ drogen carbonate $\left( \mathrm { N a H C O } _ { 3 } \right)$ and sodium carbonate $( \mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 } )$ (Ge et al. 2010). Compared with neutral salt stress, it not only has a high salt concentration, but also has the characteristics of a high pH value, which will cause comprehensive damage from osmotic stress, ion toxicity, oxidative stress, and high pH stress. Therefore, the damage of saline-­ alkali to plants is much greater than that of neutral salt (Fang et  al.  2021; Ge et al. 2010). Under saline-­ alkali stress, the ability of plant roots to absorb water becomes poor, which will cause physiological drought, leading to the inhibition of plant growth and devel‑ opment (Yuan et al. 2021). The main manifestations are that the leaves lose water and wilt, chlorosis and yellowing occur, the plants are short, the roots turn yellow, and the number of fibrous roots decreases (Zhou et al. 2023). Saline-­ alkali stress first destroys the cell membrane and changes the permeability of the membrane. The degree of plasma membrane damage in plant leaves will increase with the increase of saline-­ alkali stress. Among them, the relative conductivity and MDA con‑ tent are important indicators to reflect the degree of cyto‑ plasmic extravasation and the structural integrity of the cell membrane. When the relative conductivity is greater, the MDA content is higher, indicating that the degree of cell mem‑ brane damage is more serious (Cao et al. 2022; Rao et al. 2023; Shen et al. 2023). At the same time, the metabolic balance in the body will also be destroyed, which will produce a large amount of reactive oxygen species (ROS), cause intracellular oxidative stress, and damage proteins, lipids, and DNA, thus leading to cell withering (Baxter et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2022). Among them, superoxide anion $( \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ and hydrogen peroxide $( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } )$ are common ROS (Suzuki et al. 2012). 

Flavonoids are a class of secondary metabolites that widely exist in the plant kingdom. According to their chemical struc‑ ture, they can be divided into different subtypes, including flavonols, flavanols, flavonoids, isoflavones, flavanones, proanthocyanidins, and anthocyanins. These compounds are synthesised by the phenylpropanoid pathway through the catalysis of a series of enzymes (Han et  al.  2023). These enzymes mainly include chalcone synthase (CHS), chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavonoid 3-­ hydroxylase (F3H), flavonol synthase (FLS), flavonoid synthase (FNS) and flavanone $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3'H) (Lam et  al.  2022). Flavonoids, as anti‑ oxidants, can respond to environmental stress by activating various antioxidant enzymes and scavenging a large amount of ROS generated during stress, thereby alleviating oxidative damage. Previous studies in plants have reported that flavo‑ noid content influences adaptation to saline-­ alkali stress. For example, in Malus halliana, saline-­ alkali stress toler‑ ance is enhanced by MhPR1 leaves, as shown by transcrip‑ tomic analyses (Zhang et  al.  2022). In G. longituba, $\mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 }$ stress significantly increased flavonoid biosynthesis (Wang, Song, et  al.  2024). In alfalfa, transcriptomic and metabolo‑ mic analyses were employed to dissect the complex response mechanisms of two alfalfa varieties to combined cold and saline-­ alkali stress. MsMYB12 was shown to enhance flavo‑ nol accumulation and antioxidant capacity by upregulating 

MsFLS13 expression, thereby improving tolerance to the combined stress (Liu et al. 2024). RNA interference-­ mediated suppression of MsFLS13 expression reduced flavonol accumu‑ lation under saline-­ alkali stress, leading to decreased stress tolerance (Zhang, Zhao, et  al.  2023). In rose, the integrated analysis of metabolome and transcriptome data revealed that the abundance of flavonoids was highly correlated with MYB5, which promoted the accumulation of procyanidins (PA) by di‑ rectly regulating the expression of ANR. Saline-­ alkali stress strongly inhibited the transcription of MYB5 in the saline-- alkali-­ sensitive rose cultivar ‘Fenghua’ and decreased the lev‑ els of procyanidins and anthocyanins (Wang, Du, et al. 2024). In glycyrrhiza uralensis, physiological, metabolomic, and tran‑ scriptomic analysis revealed that many flavonoids and their pathway genes were affected under saline-­ alkali stress, indi‑ cating that flavonoids played an important role in glycyrrhiza uralensis tolerance to saline-­ alkali stress (Lv, Wen, et al. 2024; Wang et al. 2020). In sorghum, the metabolome and transcrip‑ tome of sorghum under normal conditions and moderate and severe saline-­ alkali stress were integrated and analysed, and the level of flavonoids was found to be increased. Compared with severe stress, the accumulation of most flavonoid com‑ ponents was higher under moderate saline-­ alkali stress (Ma et al. 2020). In summary, metabolomics serves as one of the key approaches for investigating plant saline-­ alkali stress tol‑ erance mechanisms. Flavonoids play a crucial role in plant ad‑ aptation to saline-­ alkali stress. 

CHI is a key enzyme in the synthesis of flavonoids. In chry‑ santhemums, upregulation of the CHI gene promoted the bio‑ synthesis of luteolin, quercetin, and rutin in the “Taraneh” cultivar, and luteolin and apigenin in the “Azita” cultivar (Hodaei et  al.  2018). In hops, grape, citrus, and Arabidopsis, CHIL has been demonstrated to participate in a flavanone and flavone (CFL) biosynthetic metabolon and assist the cyclization of nar‑ ingenin chalcone to (2S)-­ naringenin, which leads to the efficient influx of substrates to chalcone synthase (CHS) and improves the catalytic efficiency of CHS (Ban et al. 2018; Jiang et al. 2015; Zhao et  al.  2021). Overexpression of CitCHIL1 in citrus and Arabidopsis significantly increased flavonoid content, whereas RNA interference-­ mediated silencing of CitCHIL1 in citrus reduced CFL levels by $4 3 \%$ (Zhao et  al.  2021). In Arabidopsis, loss-­ of-­ function mutations in CHIL significantly reduced pro‑ anthocyanidin and flavonol levels in seeds, while anthocyanin accumulation in leaves remained unaffected. Overexpression of CHIL partially restored the phenotype of chil mutants and enhanced proanthocyanidin and flavonol accumulation in wild-­ type plants (Jiang et  al.  2015). In rice, OsCHI3 enhanced drought tolerance by scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS) through coordinated regulation of flavonoid and abscisic acid (ABA) metabolic pathways (Liu, Liu, et  al.  2025). In soybean, overexpression of GmCHI4A and GmCHI4B increased the total isoflavone content and enhanced salt tolerance of cotyledon hairy roots and composite plant (Zhang et al. 2024). In Atriplex canescens, overexpression of AcCHI conferred enhanced toler‑ ance to salt and osmotic stress in transgenic Arabidopsis plants. The transgenic lines exhibited greater biomass accumulation, lower $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ content, and reduced relative plasma membrane permeability compared to wild types (Feng et  al.  2023). The above studies indicate that CHIL plays an important role in the synthesis of flavonoids and abiotic stress. 

The transcriptional regulatory network controls the signalling pathways that regulate biological processes in plants under salt stress, enabling plants to mitigate the harmful effects of high salt on their growth and development. The WRKY gene family plays an important role in plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses (Ma et  al.  2023; Wu et  al.  2021). WRKY can be divided into three subfamilies, and the WRKY mem‑ bers who regulate salt tolerance are mainly distributed in 2b, 2c, 2d, and 2e. WRKY protein recognises and activates plant defence-­ related genes containing W-­ box motifs in a variety of promoters, so that they can better cope with stress environ‑ ment (Zhang, Zhao, et al. 2023). In soybean, GsWRKY40 and GsbHLH92 could reduce plant ROS accumulation and improve plant salt tolerance by inhibiting the expression of GmSPOD1 (Li, Xue, et  al.  2024). In cotton, GhWRKY207 enhanced drought tolerance by promoting the expression of GhCSD3 and GhFSD2 genes (Zhang, Li, Hon, et al. 2025). In poplar, the PalWRKY77 transcription factor negatively regulated salt tol‑ erance and abscisic acid signalling (Jiang et al. 2021). In mul‑ berry, MaWRKYIIc7 bound to W-­ box elements and positively regulated the expression of MaNCED1 and MaRD29A, thereby coordinately enhancing drought tolerance (Su et al. 2025). In tomato, SlWRKY81 enhanced saline-­ alkali tolerance by reg‑ ulating spermidine (Spd) synthesis and $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / K ^ { + } }$ homeostasis through its interaction with SlJAZ1, which mediates the JA pathway (Shang et al. 2024). In C. equisetifolia, overexpression of CeqWRKY11 enhanced the tolerance of transgenic ephedra seedlings to NaCl and ${ \mathrm { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ stress by upregulating the ex‑ pression of abiotic stress-­ related genes, especially CeqHKT1 and CeqPOD7 (Zhao et al. 2024). In wheat, the TaWRKY55-- TaPLATZ2 module negatively regulated saline-­ alkali stress tolerance (Wei et al. 2025). In tomato, the SlWRKY42-­ SlMYC2 module coordinately enhanced saline-­ alkali tolerance by ac‑ tivating both jasmonic acid signalling and spermidine bio‑ synthesis pathways (Liu, Shang, et  al.  2025). An increasing number of studies indicated that WRKY could influence fla‑ vonoid biosynthesis (Zhang, Sun, et al. 2023). For example, in grape, VvWRKY70 regulated flavonol biosynthesis under light and high-­ temperature conditions by targeting VvBCH2 and VvCHS3 (Wei et al. 2023). In black wolfberry, LrWRKY32 and LrMYB94 bound to the promoters of LrFLS and LrCYP75B1, thereby promoting flavonol accumulation (Du et al. 2024). A co-­ expression analysis of metabolomics and transcriptomics in two different disease-­ resistant genotypes of moso bam‑ boo revealed that PeWRKY30 was a key TF co-­ expressed with flavonoid accumulation in yellow moso bamboo, poten‑ tially involved in biotic or abiotic resistance in bamboo (Ma et  al.  2024). In grapevine, the co-­ expression of VqWRKY56 and VqbZIPC22 significantly increased the transcriptional levels of VvCHS3, VvLAR1, and VvANR genes. Overexpression of VqbZIPC22 promoted proanthocyanidin (PA) accumulation and enhanced resistance to powdery mildew (Wang, Dong, et al. 2023). In M. crassipes, McWRKY43 conferred tolerance to low-­ temperature stress by regulating flavonoid biosynthe‑ sis (Yu et al. 2024). In apple, 5-­ Aminolevulinic acid activates the MdWRKY71-­ MdMADS1 module to enhance anthocyanin biosynthesis (Zhang, Li, Zhang, et  al.  2025). In potato, the transcription factor WRKY41-­ F3'H module finely regulated flavonoid metabolism and cold resistance (Bao et  al.  2025). However, in maize, the role of WRKY transcription factors in conferring saline-­ alkali stress tolerance through regulation 

of flavonoid biosynthesis-­ related gene expression remains unreported. 

Maize (Zea mays L.) is an important global staple crop and is sensitive to saline-­ alkali stress. Different maize inbred lines exhibit varying degrees of sensitivity to saline-­ alkali stress, with some inbred lines evolving stronger abilities to adapt to abiotic stress (Fu et  al.  2017; Hanks et  al.  1978). For exam‑ ple, Zhang et al. discovered that suppressing ZmGS3 function enhanced alkali tolerance while effectively increasing maize yield (Zhang, Sun, et  al.  2023). Li et  al. identified candidate genes responsive to saline-­ alkali stress (including a flavonoid biosynthesis-­ related gene) using GWAS and RNA-­ seq data, but did not investigate the molecular mechanisms (Li et al. 2022). Cao et  al. found that under saline-­ alkali conditions, $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ bound ZmNSA1 and triggered its degradation via the 26S pro‑ teasome, then increased the transcript levels of MHAs, thereby enhancing root $\mathrm { H ^ { + } }$ efflux and promoting $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ eff lux medi‑ ated by SOS1 $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / H ^ { + } }$ antiporter (Cao et  al.  2020). Although research on maize under saline-­ alkali stress has made some progress, the specific regulatory mechanisms of ZmWRKY and ZmCHI in saline-­ alkali stress and their interactions remain unclear. Here, we selected maize inbred lines with different levels of resistance to saline-­ alkali stress. 22KN3894 exhibited strong saline-­ alkali tolerance, while H23146 was sensitive to saline-­ alkali stress. Using transcriptomics and widely tar‑ geted metabolomics technologies, a core regulatory network was constructed, and the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway was identified as a key metabolic pathway. The critical transcrip‑ tion factor ZmWRKY82 was further characterised, and an in‑ tegrated analysis was conducted to elucidate maize's response mechanisms to saline-­ alkali stress. This study revealed for the first time that ZmWRKY82 promoted the accumulation of flavonoids by directly regulating the expression of ZmCHI6, playing a key role in the response of maize to saline-­ alkali stress. The discovery of the ZmWRKY82-­ ZmCHI6 module not only expands the functional understanding of ZmWRKY tran‑ scription factors, but also provides new insights into the flavo‑ noid metabolism pathway and the novel functions of ZmCHI genes in saline-­ alkali stress. This study provides germplasm resources and theoretical basis for cultivating new varieties of saline-­ alkali tolerant maize. 

# 2 | Materials and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

A total of 80 maize inbred lines were selected as materi‑ als (Table  S2), and all materials were provided by the Joint Laboratory of Plant Biotechnology, Jilin Agricultural University, Changchun, China. Seeds that were close in size and full of seeds were selected for the test. The seeds were sterilised with $1 0 \%$ NaClO solution for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and rinsed five times with dis‑ tilled water. The seeds were blotted with filter paper to remove the surface water and then laid flat in a petri dish lined with a double layer of moist filter paper for germination. The diame‑ ter of the petri dish was 9 cm. The maize inbred lines used for saline-­ alkali concentration screening were neatly spread 120 seeds in each petri dish, and the rest of the maize inbred lines were neatly laid with 50 seeds in each petri dish (Li et al. 2022). 

The incubator temperature was $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , relative humidity was $6 0 \%$ , $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light and 8 h of dark cycles, and the light intensity was $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } { \cdot } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } { \cdot } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . The successfully germinated seeds were transplanted into pots and five seedlings were set in each pot in three replications. 

# 2.2 | Screening of Saline-Alkali Tolerant Materials

The optimal saline-­ alkali concentration for the treatment was first determined. The saline-­ alkali concentration gradients were 0 (CK) and 25, 50, 75, 100, 125, $1 5 0 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ mixed saline-- alkali solution (molar ratio $\mathrm { N a H C O _ { 3 } ; N a _ { 2 } C O _ { 3 } = 9 : 1 }$ , pH 9.1–9.2) (Table S1; Cui et al. 2023). We selected 9 materials for the pre-- experiment, and when the plants reached the 3-­ leaf stage, each material was treated with gradient saline-­ alkali concentration. During the saline-­ alkali treatment, $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of saline-­ alkali solution was added to the basin every 2 days. The optimal treat‑ ment concentration at the seedling stage was selected by phe‑ notypic and growth index measurements after 12 days of stress treatment. The optimal concentration was used to evaluate the saline-­ alkali tolerance at the seedling stage according to the growth index. 

# 2.3 | Agronomic Traits and Physiological and Biochemical Index Detection

After 12 days of saline-­ alkali stress, plant height, root length, fresh weight, and dry weight of each maize inbred line were determined, and the specific method was referred to Volenec (Volenec  1999). One saline-­ alkali tolerant material and one saline-­ alkali sensitive material were selected, and the physio‑ logical and biochemical indexes under saline-­ alkali treatment were determined (Ma et al. 2021). The contents of total flavo‑ noids, MDA, ${ \mathrm { O } } _ { 2 } { , } ^ { - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , and the activities of CAT, SOD, and POD in the roots were determined using a reagent kit (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer's in‑ structions. Relative conductivity (REC): the root system was cut and put into an Erlenmeyer flask, $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of distilled water was added, sealed, and shaken at room temperature for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ , and the initial conductivity (S1) was detected at this time. The sam‑ ples were reacted in a boiling water bath for $1 5 \mathrm { m i n }$ , cooled to room temperature, and tested for final conductivity (S2). REC $\mathrm { ( \% ) = S 1 / S 2 \times 1 0 0 . }$ . Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. 

NBT staining method: The root tips and leaves were taken and immediately placed in a centrifuge tube containing $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of NBT staining solution. The samples were completely submerged and stained for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ at room temperature. The NBT staining solution was discarded, and $9 5 \%$ ethanol was added to immerse the sample. The samples were destained in a water bath at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , with the $9 5 \%$ ethanol replaced every 10 min. Once all the green colour of the samples had faded, the samples were removed, and the staining results were recorded. DAB staining method: The samples were completely immersed in DAB stain and incubated overnight at room temperature. Decolorise with $9 5 \%$ ethanol in a water bath at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until clarified and photographed for ob‑ servation (Jiao et al. 2024). Three biological replicates were per‑ formed for each sample. 

Determination of $\mathrm { K ^ { + } }$ , $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ content in plant root sys‑ tems: The samples were dried at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days and then pul‑ verised in a small ball mill. Each sample was digested with $5 \mathrm { m L }$ of nitric acid $( 6 5 \% )$ at $9 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 6 - 8 \mathrm { h } }$ . The ion content in the samples was determined by an inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometer (ICP-­ OES; Optima 2100 DV; Perkin Elmer, USA) after dilution with distilled water (Shen et  al.  2024). Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. 

# 2.4 | Transmission Electron Microscopy

A $3 \mathrm { m m } ^ { 2 }$ section of the maize root tip was taken, and the sample surface was gently rinsed with PBS to remove any impurities. The sample was quickly immersed in electron microscopy fix‑ ative and fixed at room temperature for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ , then transferred to $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for storage. The fixed samples were rinsed three times with $0 . 1 \mathbf { M }$ phosphate buffer (PB, pH 7.4), each rinse lasting 15 min. The tissue blocks were then transferred into $1 \%$ ${ \mathrm { O s O } } _ { 4 }$ in 0.1 M PB (pH 7.4) and fixed for $_ { 1 - 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at room temperature. After that, the tissue blocks were washed in 0.1 M PB (pH 7.4) three times, each for 15 min. Tissues were sequentially immersed in $30 \% - 5 0 \% -$ $7 0 \% - 8 0 \% - 9 0 \% - 9 5 \% - 1 0 0 \% - 1 0 0 \%$ alcohol for 15 min each time and isoamyl acetate for 15 min. The samples were dried using a critical point dryer. Specimens were attached to metallic stubs using carbon stickers and sputter-­ coated with gold for 30 s. Images were observed and captured using a scanning electron microscope. Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. 

# 2.5 | Paraffin Section

The sections were rehydrated in two changes of BioDewax and Clear Solution and $1 0 0 \%$ I–100% II– $- 7 5 \%$ alcohol. Each step takes anywhere from 5 min. Finally, rinse with running water. Put sections into safranin O staining solution for 2 min and three cylinders of anhydrous ethanol for rapid dehydration. Put slides into $5 0 \%$ , $7 0 \%$ and $8 0 \%$ alcohol for 3–8 s. Sections into plant solid green staining solution stain for 6–20 s, anhydrous etha‑ nol three-­ cylinder dehydration (Time: 5/10/20 s). Put sections into three cylinders of xylene for 5 min. Finally, mount the tis‑ sue section with neutral balsam. Observed under a microscope and took images. Three biological replicates were performed for each sample. 

# 2.6 | Metabolomic Profiling

After 12 days of saline-­ alkali stress treatment, root samples of maize inbred lines were taken and immediately frozen with liquid nitrogen, and the samples were preserved at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Three replicates of each sample were used for metabolomics analysis by ultra performance liquid chromatography tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-­ MS). Samples were processed and assayed according to Xiao et  al.  (2024). Metabolite analyses were performed by Wuhan Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China) (http://www.metware.cn/) by an untar‑ geted metabolomics approach. The sample extracts were an‑ alysed using a UPLC-­ ESI-­ MS/MS system (UPLC, ExionLC 

$\mathbf { A D } ^ { \prime }$ https: //sciex.com.cn/) and Tandem mass spectrometry system (https://sciex.com.cn/). The analytical conditions were as follows: UPLC: column, Agilent SB-­ C18 $( 1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $2 . 1 \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m } )$ ; the mobile phase consisted of solvent A, pure water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, and solvent B, acetonitrile with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid. Sample measurements were performed with a gradient program that employed the starting conditions of $9 5 \%$ A, $5 \%$ B. Within 9 min, a linear gradient to $5 \%$ A, $9 5 \%$ B was programmed, and a composition of $5 \%$ A, $9 5 \%$ B was kept for 1 min. Subsequently, a composition of $9 5 \%$ A, $5 . 0 \%$ B was adjusted within 1.1 min and kept for 2.9 min. The flow velocity was set at $0 . 3 5 \mathrm { m L }$ per minute; the column oven was set to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; the injection volume was $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The eff luent was alternatively connected to an ESI-­ triple quadrupole-­ linear ion trap (QTRAP)-­ MS. The ESI source operation parameters were as follows: source temperature $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; ion spray voltage (IS) $5 5 0 0 \mathrm { V }$ (positive ion mode)/−4500 V (negative ion mode); ion source gas I (GSI), gas II (GSII), curtain gas (CUR) were set at 50, 60, and 25 psi, respectively; the collision-­ activated disso‑ ciation (CAD) was high. QQQ scans were acquired as MRM experiments with collision gas (nitrogen) set to medium. DP (declustering potential) and CE (collision energy) for individ‑ ual MRM transitions were done with further DP and CE op‑ timization. A specific set of MRM transitions was monitored for each period according to the metabolites eluted within this period. Based on the metabolites detected, principal compo‑ nent analysis (PCA) was performed using the factoextra pack‑ ages in the R package to reveal correlations between samples. The data were subjected to UV (unit variance scaling) and clustered heat map analysis was performed for all samples. Differential metabolites were screened using VIP values from the OPLS-­ DA model in combination with P-­ value/FDR from univariate analysis. The conditions for screening differential metabolites were $\mathrm { V I P } > 1$ $> 1$ and fold change $\geq 2$ . Metabolites Biological Role (MBROLE) was used to analyse the enrich‑ ment of DAMs. K-­ means cluster analysis was performed on all the screened differential metabolites. A weighted correla‑ tion network analysis (WGCNA) was constructed to obtain hub metabolites that were highly correlated with saline-­ alkali tolerance. 

# 2.7 | Transcriptome Analysis

The transcriptome sequencing workflow includes RNA ex‑ traction, RNA detection, library construction, and on-­ machine sequencing. After 12 days of saline-­ alkali stress treatment, root samples from maize inbred lines were collected, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , with three in‑ dependent biological replicates prepared for each sample. The sequencing was performed by Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China). Adapter pruning and depletion of low-­ quality and short-­ read raw reads generated by the Illumina HiSeq se‑ quencing platform were performed using FASTP 0.23.2. HISAT2 2.2.1 was used to compare Clean Reads with maize reference ge‑ nomes to obtain location information on the reference genome or genes, as well as sequence feature information unique to se‑ quencing samples. Visualisation by IGV software. FPKM (frag‑ ments Per Kilobase of transcript Per Million fragments mapped) was used as a measure of transcript or gene expression levels, and featureCounts 2.0.3 was used for this process. DESeq2 1.22.1 

was used for differential expression analysis between samples. Differential expression genes (DEGs) were screened with FDR $< 0 . 0 5$ and $| \mathrm { l o g } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C } | \geq 1$ as the threshold. The identified DEGs were annotated using KEGG pathway enrichment analysis (www.kegg.jp). WGCNA (v1.47) was used to construct a regula‑ tory network for the co-­ expression of flavonoid-­ related metabo‑ lites and related genes in maize (Langfelder and Horvath 2008). Visualisation of co-­ expression networks using Cytoscape_3.3.0 (Su et al. 2014). 

# 2.8 | Yeast One-Hybrid Assays

The promoter region of the target gene was amplified from maize genomic DNA and fused with the pHIS2 vector to gen‑ erate a bait vector. Meanwhile, the ORF of ZmWRKY82 was cloned into pGADT7 to generate a prey vector. The recombi‑ nant plasmid was cotransformed into the yeast receptor Y187 using the polyethylene glycol/lithium acetate method and grown on SD/−Leu/−Trp medium (Coolaber, Beijing, China). Screening was performed on SD/−Leu/−Trp/−His solid me‑ dium (Coolaber) supplemented with $8 0 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-­ amino-­ 1,2,4-- triazole (3-­ AT) and incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3–7 days. The experiment was repeated 3 times (Lv, Zhu, et  al.  2024). The primers are listed in Table S10. 

# 2.9 | Dual-Luciferase (LUC) Reporter Assay

The ZmCHI6 promoter was amplified and cloned into the pGreenII 0800-­ LUC (pLUC) vector and the ORF of ZmWRKY82 was cloned into the pGreenII 62-­ SK (pSK) vector (Table  S10). The pLUC and pSK positive vectors were introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 and co-­ injected into tobacco leaves for 72 h. D-­ fluorescein potassium salt $( 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ was sprayed on transformed Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After incuba‑ tion in the dark for 5 min, Luc images were captured using a microluminescence-­ cooled CCD imaging device (chemproht 1300B/LND, 16-­ bit; Roper Scientific, Martinsried, Germany), and luminescence activity was measured. The images were processed using IMAGEJ software (NIH, New York, USA). Transient expression experiments were performed in 3 indepen‑ dent biological replicates (Li, Xu, et al. 2024). 

# 2.10 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA)

The full-­ length CDS of ZmWRKY82 was amplified and ligated into the pET-­ 32a vector. The recombinant plasmid was trans‑ formed into Escherichia coli BL21 competent cells, and His-- tagged fusion protein (His-­ ZmWRKY82) was obtained after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ induction at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Isopropyl $\beta$ -­ D-­ 1-­ thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to the culture medium at a final concen‑ tration of $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ . Protein purification was performed using the His-­ Tag Protein Purification Kit (CWbio, Beijing, China). $5 ^ { \prime }$ -biotin-­ labelled DNA probes and mutant probes were syn‑ thesised and annealed. EMSA was conducted following the instructions of the Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Coolaber Co. Ltd., Beijing, China). The bands were scanned using TOUCH IMAGER (E-­ BLOT, Shanghai, China). All primers used are listed in Table S10. 

# 2.11 | Identification of Saline-Alkali Resistance of ZmWRKY82 in Yeast

The ZmWRKY82 gene was ligated into the pYES2 vector. Both the negative control (empty pYES2) and recombinant plasmid (pYES2-­ ZmWRKY82) were transformed into yeast competent cells (INVSC1 strain). Individual colonies were cultured in YPDA liquid medium until reaching an $\mathrm { \Delta O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ between 0.6 and 1.0. Each yeast strain was serially diluted (5 ten-­ fold gra‑ dients) in YPDA medium and spot-­ plated onto SD-­ URA solid media with or without saline-­ alkali stress (1.2 M). The plates were then incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 6 { - } 4 8 \mathrm { h }$ before colony growth was analysed. 

# 2.12 | Constructs and Generation of Transgenic Plants

The cDNA of ZmWRKY82 was amplified using specific prim‑ ers and subsequently ligated into the pCAMBIA3301 vec‑ tor. The recombinant plasmid was then transformed into GV3101 Agrobacterium competent cells and introduced into Arabidopsis inflorescences via Agrobacterium-­ mediated flo‑ ral dip transformation. $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ generation seeds were screened for positive transformants on 1/2 MS solid medium supplemented with $2 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ hygromycin. Finally, $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ generation transgenic positive plants were verified by RT-­ qPCR analysis. The Arabidopsis mutants atwrky51-1 (SALK_022198C) and atwrky51-2 (SALK_066489) were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (The Ohio State University, Columbus, OH, USA). # 2.13 | Identification of Saline-Alkali Tolerance of Transgenic Arabidopsis

Arabidopsis seeds were germinated in a mixture of nutrient soil and perlite $( 3 { : } 1 , \mathrm { v } / \mathrm { v } )$ . At the four-­ leaf stage, seedlings were trans‑ ferred to small plastic pots $( 7 \mathrm { c m } \times 7 \mathrm { c m } )$ containing the same soil mixture, with four plants per pot. After 3 weeks, seedlings of similar size and growth status were selected for saline-­ alkali treatment. The seedlings were irrigated every 2 days with either 0 mM (control) or $1 2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ saline-­ alkali solution for a total of 12 days. Each experiment included three independent biological replicates. 

# 2.14 | Antisense Oligonucleotides Assay

The antisense oligonucleotide assay was performed, as pre‑ viously described (Lin et al. 2024), to silence the target gene ZmWRKY82. # 3.1 | Screening of Saline-Alkali Tolerance Materials

In order to simulate the various saline-­ alkali conditions that may exist in the saline-­ alkali soil in the field, we first tested a mixture of two alkaline salts $( \mathrm { N a H C O } _ { 3 } { : } \mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 } { = } 9 { : } 1 )$ ) at differ‑ ent concentrations and measured their effects on various growth indicators of maize seedlings. 

Nine randomised maize materials were treated with different con‑ centrations, and significant changes in all the traits were found in different materials. In order to eliminate biological differences among different inbred lines, we used the saline-­ alkali tolerance coefficient (relative value) to characterise the saline-­ alkali toler‑ ance of the inbred lines. According to the analysis of the wilting degree of leaves and the changes of various growth indexes, the re‑ sults showed that after 12days of treatment, $1 2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ mixed saline-- alkali (112.5mM NaHCO and $1 2 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 }$ ; $\mathrm { p H } 9 . 1 { \sim } 9 . 2 )$ showed the largest range of variation and was the most reliable treatment concentration to assess the saline-­ alkali tolerance of maize (Figure S1A). Except for nega‑ tive correlations between RRV and RPH/RRL, RRT and RPH, and RRAD and other physiological traits, all other traits showed positive correlations (Figure S1B). We observed that two maize in‑ bred lines showed high tolerance to saline-­ alkali conditions, and the composite index $D$ value reached more than 0.7. The 20 maize inbred lines were sensitive to saline-­ alkali, and the comprehensive index $D$ value was below 0.4. Based on the degree of leaf wilt and $D$ -­ value, we categorised all materials into 5 classes (Figure S1C, Table S3).

# 3.2 | Phenotypic Analysis of Saline-Alkali Extreme Materials

To better understand the mechanism of saline-­ alkali tolerance in maize, we selected two maize genotypes, 22KN3894 (saline-- alkali tolerant, hereinafter referred to as KN3894) and H23146 (saline-­ alkali sensitive, hereinafter referred to as H146), as subsequent materials. To investigate the reasons for the supe‑ riority of KN3894 over H146, we observed its phenotype and quantitatively analysed its physiological and biochemical in‑ dicators. The study found that KN3894 showed less leaf wilt‑ ing than H146 under saline-­ alkali treatment, and the effect of saline-­ alkali on roots was relatively small. The number of vir‑ tual roots of H146 was significantly less than that of KN3894 (Figure 1A). In addition, MDA content, an important indica‑ tor of stress-­ induced plasma membrane damage, increased more in H146 (Figure 1E). The antioxidant activities of SOD, POD, and CAT, relative water content, and flavonoid content of KN3894 were higher than those of H146 (Figure 1B–D,G,L), while the relative electrolyte leakage of leaves was lower than that of H146 (Figure 1F). 

The increased $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ content likely induced ionic toxicity, while the reduced $\mathrm { K ^ { + } }$ and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ levels potentially compromised cel‑ lular osmoregulation and signalling. In this study, both ma‑ terials exhibited significantly elevated $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ accumulation and decreased $\mathrm { K ^ { + } }$ and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ concentrations under saline-­ alkali stress, resulting in higher $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } / K ^ { + } }$ ratios. This ion imbalance presumably exacerbated osmotic stress and ionic toxicity (Figure 1H–K). NBT and DAB staining as well as $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ content determination showed that a large amount of ROS 

accumulated in H146 compared with KN3894 (Figure S2A–F). These physiological results showed that KN3894 was a saline-- alkali tolerant genotype, H146 was a saline-­ alkali sensitive genotype, and KN3894 grew better than H146 under saline-- alkali stress. 

In order to further explore the response of the root system of the two materials to saline-­ alkali stress, the maize root tips were observed by scanning electron microscopy and paraffin section. The results demonstrated that under normal growth conditions, the surface of the maize root tips was smooth, with tightly and orderly arranged cells and a full cell morphology. The vascular bundles were clearly visible and intact, exhib‑ iting excellent functionality in transporting water, inorganic salts, and organic substances. Under saline-­ alkali stress, the surface of maize root tips became uneven, with cells arranged irregularly and exhibiting a shrunken and collapsed mor‑ phology. The vascular bundles were disrupted. In the saline-- alkali sensitive inbred line H146 root tip cells were almost completely collapsed, and the vascular bundles were severely disrupted, significantly restricting the transport of nutrients. In contrast, the saline-­ alkali tolerant inbred line KN3894 ex‑ hibited less severe cell shrinkage and vascular bundle damage compared to H146 (Figure 2A,B).

# 4.2 | Flavonoid Biosynthetic Pathway Responds Positively to Saline-Alkali Stress

Flavonoids are important secondary metabolites produced by plants in response to environmental stress, with good scaveng‑ ing capacity for ROS (Han et al. 2023). They help plants resist saline-­ alkali and other stresses to a certain extent. Flavonoids enhance the stability of cell membranes by interacting with membrane lipids, thereby reducing ion leakage and lipid per‑ oxidation. For example, in rice, apigenin pretreatment reduced the damage caused by salt stress to seedlings, possibly by regu‑ lating the selective absorption of ions by roots and their trans‑ port to stems, thereby maintaining a high $\mathrm { K ^ { + } / N a ^ { + } }$ ratio. It may also have activated the antioxidant defence system (Mekawy et  al.  2018). In pigeon pea, naringin regulated the molecular mechanism of flavonoid biosynthesis and enhanced the salt tol‑ erance of pigeon pea (Wang, Wang, et al. 2023). In addition, fla‑ vonoids participate in the regulation of plant hormone signalling pathways, such as JA and ABA, coordinating the plant's stress responses. Additionally, abiotic stress also has a significant im‑ pact on the synthesis of flavonoid secondary metabolites (Zhang et al. 2024). For example, in alfalfa, under combined saline-­ alkali and cold stress, a large accumulation of flavonoid metabolites and flavonoid biosynthesis-­ related genes was observed in the ZD materials compared to the BM. The MsMYB12 gene might have responded to the combined stress by regulating the flavo‑ noid biosynthesis pathway (Liu et al. 2024). Specific flavonoids potentially associated with the salt-­ alkali tolerance of NQ-­ 1 in alfalfa were identified through integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses. Additionally, transcription factors that may have regulated flavonoid biosynthesis under saline-­ alkali stress were also identified (Zhang, Tao, et  al.  2025). In maize, GWAS and RNA-­ seq analyses revealed that one of the candidate genes associated with seedling alkali tolerance was enriched in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway (Li et  al.  2022). In potato, 

compared to the cultivated potato (S. tuberosum L.), the key fla‑ vonoid biosynthesis gene, flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ '-­ hydroxylase (F3'H), was significantly upregulated in the wild species. Loss of function of ScF3'H weakened the cold tolerance of the wild species, while overexpression of this gene in the cultivated species enhanced its cold tolerance. Metabolomic analysis indicated that F3'H pro‑ duced more downstream products by adding hydroxyl groups (▬OH) to the flavonoid ring structure, and these flavonoid com‑ pounds enhanced cold tolerance by increasing ROS scavenging capacity (Bao et al. 2025). In Populus tomentosa, the IAA17.1/ HSFA5a module enhanced salt tolerance by regulating flavonol biosynthesis and ROS levels in lateral roots (Song et al.  2024). Consistent with previous studies, we also found through me‑ tabolomic analysis that flavonoid compounds accumulated in large amounts under saline-­ alkali stress, with the accumula‑ tion of flavonoids in the saline-­ alkali tolerant maize inbred line KN3894 being significantly higher than that in the saline-­ alkali sensitive maize inbred line H146 (Figure 3, Figure S3). 

The key genes of flavonoid biosynthesis pathway include PAL, C4H, CHS, CHI and F3H (Jaakola  2013). We analysed the transcriptional reprogramming differences in key metabolic pathways during saline-­ alkali stress responses between the two genotypes and found that genes specifically expressed in KN3894 were mainly enriched in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway (Figure  4D–I). Through WGCNA analysis, we found that the green module was highly correlated with the 12-­ day saline-­ alkali treated KN3894 samples (Figure  5B). It was also significantly correlated with several flavonoid metabolites that accumulated significantly, including Dihydroquercetin, Nepetin, Tricetin, Apigenin, and Naringenin (Figure  5C). We identified genes related to flavonoid biosynthesis in the module, with ZmCHI6 being the most central gene (Figure 5D). Among the 15 structural genes annotated by this pathway, the related genes including CHI, FLS, F3'H were up-­ regulated in KN3894, and the expression level was significantly higher than H146, which was consistent with the change of flavonoid metabolite accumulation (Figure 5E,F). 

ZmWRKY TF family is widely involved in the response of plants to abiotic stresses and plays a crucial role in resisting abiotic stresses such as drought, saline-­ alkali, and cold. For example, in maize, ZmWRKY20 and ZmWRKY115 interacted to inhibit the expression of ZmbZIP111 by directly binding to its pro‑ moter and increased the sensitivity of maize seedlings to salt stress (Bo et al. 2022). In tomato, SlGSTU43 enhanced salt tol‑ erance by regulating lignin biosynthesis through interactions with SlCOMT2, SlMYB71, and SlWRKY8 (Yuan et  al.  2024). # 5 | Conclusion

Saline-­ alkali stress seriously affects plant growth and yield. In this study, we physiologically compared two saline-­ alkali ex‑ treme materials (22KN3894 and H23146) and analysed their response mechanisms under saline-­ alkali stress using transcrip‑ tomics and metabolomics. We found that compared with H23146, flavonoid metabolites accumulated significantly in 22KN3894, the content of flavonoid synthesis-­ related genes was also higher, and the ability to scavenge ROS was stronger. ZmWRKY82 was able to bind to the promoter of the flavonoid synthesis gene ZmCHI6 to promote flavonoid expression, thereby enhanc‑ ing the antioxidant capacity of plants, which suggested that the accumulation of flavonoid compounds might have helped plants resist saline-­ alkali stress (Figure  9). The results of this study contributed to a better understanding of the regulatory mechanisms by which plants responded to saline-­ alkali stress and held significant implications for maize breeding. In the future, molecular markers associated with ZmWRKY82 could 

be developed to facilitate marker-­ assisted selection in breeding programmes. Moreover, the saline-­ alkali-­ tolerant maize inbred line 22KN3894, which exhibited high ZmWRKY82 expression, could be used as a parental line in hybrid breeding to develop new maize varieties with both saline-­ alkali tolerance and high yield. This would significantly enhance maize productivity in saline-­ alkali soils and contribute to global food security.
