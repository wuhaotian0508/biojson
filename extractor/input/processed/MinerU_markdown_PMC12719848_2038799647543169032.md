# RING-Type E3 Ligase OsRFPH2-6 Ubiquitinates Nuclear Factor Y Subunit OsNF-YA1 to Suppress Rice Immunity

# ABSTRACT

RING-type E3 ubiquitin ligases play crucial roles in plant immunity by ubiquitinating their downstream substrates for degradation. We previously revealed, using CRISR/Cas9-mediated knockout mutant lines, that the RING-H2 E3 ubiquitin ligase OsRFPH2-6, transcriptionally regulated by the NAC transcription factor ONAC083, negatively modulates rice blast resistance. In this study, we further confirmed the negative role of OsRFPH2-6 in rice immunity through overexpression lines and investigated its underlying regulatory mechanism. OsRFPH2-6 possesses E3 ubiquitin ligase activity, which depends on each cysteine residue within its RING domain. OsRFPH2-6 interacts with and ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1, a subunit of nuclear factor Y transcription factor, leading to its degradation. Knockout of OsNF-YA1 attenuates rice blast resistance and weakens chitin-induced immune responses. Transcriptome analysis revealed that OsNF-YA1 regulates a set of defence-associated genes. Notably, some negative immune regulators, like ONAC083, MNAC3 and OsSGR, were upregulated, whereas several positive immune regulators, such as genes encoding for phytoalexin biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid pathway enzymes, hydrolytic enzymes and pathogenesis-related proteins, including OsPAL6, OsATL15, OsCPS4, OsWRKY6, OsPR10a, and chi11, were downregulated in osnf-ya1 mutant plants. These findings suggest that OsNF-YA1 positively regulates rice immunity by modulating the transcription of a set of immune-associated genes. This study extends the previously established ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6 regulatory module in rice immunity by integrating OsNF-YA1 as a downstream substrate of OsRFPH2-6, offering possibilities to improve rice disease resistance through targeted genome editing of the ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6-OsNF-YA1 module. 

# 1 | Introduction

Rice (Oryza sativa), a major global food crop, is frequently threatened by multiple pathogens that significantly reduce its yield and quality. Among the most destructive diseases affecting rice are blast (Magnaporthe oryzae), bacterial leaf blight (Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae) and sheath blight (Rhizoctonia solani) (Niño-Liu et  al.  2006; Ebbole  2007; Singh et  al.  2019; Wang, Ji, et  al.  2025). To combat pathogen infection, plants have evolved a sophisticated innate immune system consisting 

of pathogen/microbial/damp-associated molecular pattern (P/M/DAMP)-triggered immunity (PTI) and effector-triggered immunity (ETI) (Ngou et al. 2022; Jones et al. 2024). PTI, the first layer of defence, is initiated upon recognition of pathogenderived molecules by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) on the cell surface, whereas ETI, the second layer of defence, is activated by recognising pathogen effectors through nucleotidebinding domain and leucine-rich repeat receptor (NLR) proteins (Zhou and Zhang  2020; Yuan, Jiang, et  al.  2021; Yuan, Ngou, et al. 2021; Zhang et al. 2023; Nabi et al. 2024). During 

the intricately interrelated PTI and ETI responses to pathogen infections (Yuan, Jiang, et  al.  2021; Yuan, Ngou, et  al.  2021), timely activation and sustained maintenance of immune response pathways require the inactivation of multiple regulatory proteins, primarily through the ubiquitin-26S proteasome system (UPS) (Zeng et al. 2006; Adams and Spoel 2018; Copeland and Li 2019; Yin et al. 2019; Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024), as well as the transcriptional reprogramming of a large set of genes mediated by the concerted action of different types of transcriptional factors (TF) (Huang, Catinot, and Zimmerli 2016; Wani et al. 2021; Chen et al. 2024; Dong et al. 2024; Xiong et al. 2025). 

Post-translational modifications (PTM), such as ubiquitination and phosphorylation/dephosphorylation, are critical regulators of plant immunity (Withers and Dong  2017; Soni et  al.  2024). Ubiquitination is catalysed by a cascade of enzymatic reactions involving E1 ubiquitin-activating enzyme, E2 ubiquitinconjugating enzyme and E3 ubiquitin ligase (McDowell and Philpott 2020; Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024). E3 ligases have been extensively studied and shown to play pivotal roles in rice immunity (Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024). There are four main types of E3 ligases: homology to the E6-associated protein C terminus (HETC), plant U-box (PUB), really interesting new gene (RING) and Cullin-RING (CRLs) (Vierstra 2009). Among these, the RING-type E3 ligases are the most extensively reported immunomodulatory factors in rice (Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024). For example, XA21 binding protein 3 (XB3) (Wang, Pi, et  al.  2006), BLAST AND BTH-INDUCED 1 (OsBBI1) (Li et  al.  2011), AvrPiz-t Interacting Protein 6 (APIP6) (Park et al. 2012; Ning et al. 2015; You et al. 2022, 2023; Shi et al. 2024), Microtubule-associated C4HC3-type E3 Ligase (OsMEL) (Fu et  al.  2022), DECREASED GRAIN SIZE1 (DGS1) (Wang, Zheng, et al. 2024), RING DOMAIN LIGASE5 (OsRGLG5) (Liu et al. 2023), OsRING113 (Zhang et al. 2024), IPA1 Interactor 1 (IPI1) (Gao et al. 2021; Yi et al. 2024) and Zinc Finger C3H2C3- type domain-containing Protein 1 (OsZFP1) (Liu et  al.  2021) positively regulate rice immunity against M. oryzae, X. oryzae and/or R. solani. In contrast, ENHANCED BLIGHT AND BLAST RESISTANCE1 (EBR1) (You et al. 2016), RING-finger E3 ubiquitin ligases interacting with WRKY1 (OsREIW1) (Wang et al. 2023), Arabidopsis Tóxicos En Levadura (ATL) subfamily member OsATL32 (Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Li, et al. 2024; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et  al.  2024) and Jumonji C domaincontaining protein OsJMJ715 (Zhang et  al.  2021) negatively regulate rice immunity against M. oryzae, X. oryzae or brown planthopper. The RING-H2 Finger E3 Ubiquitin Ligase 2's (RFPH2s) are a subclass of RING-type E3 ligases characterised by a conserved C3H2C3 RING-finger motif (Cys- $\cdot X _ { 2 }$ -Cys-X(9–39)- $\mathrm { C y s - X _ { ( 1 - 3 ) } - H i s - X _ { ( 2 - 3 ) } - C y s - X _ { 2 } - C y s - X _ { ( 4 - 4 8 ) } - C y s - X _ { 2 } - C y s ) }$ $\cdot X _ { ( 1 - 3 ) }$ $\cdot X _ { ( 2 - 3 ) }$ $\cdot X _ { 2 }$ located in their N-terminal regions (Lim et al. 2010). In rice, OsRFPH2-6 and OsRFPH2-10 have been shown to negatively regulate immunity against M. oryzae and rice dwarf virus, respectively (Liu et al. 2014; Bi et al. 2023; Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). 

The E3 ligases exert their biological and biochemical functions by ubiquitinating their substrate proteins for degradation via the UPS pathway, and dozens of substrates for some immuneassociated RING-type E3 ligases in rice have been recently identified (Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024). For example, OsBBI1 targets multiple substrate proteins, including the eukaryotic translation initiation factor OseIF5A4, the eukaryotic translation 

elongation factor OseEF1A, and jasmonic acid (JA) signalling corepressors such as OsJAZs and OsNINJA1, all of which are negative regulators of rice immunity (Wang, Ye, et  al.  2024; Wang, Yan, et al. 2025; Bi et al. 2025). IPI1 (Gao et al. 2021; Xu et  al.  2024; Yi et  al.  2024), DGS1 (Wang, Zheng, et  al.  2024), OsMEL (Fu et al. 2022), XB3 (Wang, Pi, et al. 2006) and EBR1 (You et al. 2016) target diverse substrates, all of which modulate immune signalling and defence responses upon fungal, bacterial and viral infections in rice. Some RING-type E3 ligases have been reported to target immune-associated TFs. For instance, APIP6, OsRING113, OsREIW1 and APIP10 target IPA1, APIP5, OsWRKY31 and OsVOZ1/OsVOZ2 TFs, respectively (Wang, Wang, et  al.  2021; Wang et  al.  2023; Shi et  al.  2024; Zhang et al. 2024). Interestingly, pathogen effectors can interact with and stabilise host RING-type E3 ligases to facilitate infection. For instance, OsATL32 negatively regulates rice immunity by targeting the small GTPase OsRac5-NADPH oxidase OsRbohB module and phosphatase OsPPKL2 for degradation, and the M. oryzae effector AvrPiz-t promotes the degradation of OsRac5 by stabilising OsATL32 (Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Li, et al. 2024; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et al. 2024; Yan et al. 2025). Despite these advancements, the molecular and biochemical network of the immune-associated RING-type E3 ligases in rice immunity remains to be fully elucidated. 

Nuclear factor-Y (NF-Y), also known as a CCAAT-binding factor (CBF) or heme activator protein (HAP), is a highly conserved TF family found in eukaryotic organisms from yeast to humans. It consists of three subunits: NF-YA, NF-YB and NF-YC (Chaves-Sanjuan et al. 2021). The NF-YB and NF-YC subunits do not harbour the DNA-binding domains but can assemble into heterodimers in the cytoplasm through their histone folding domains, creating molecular scaffolds that facilitate trimerisation with the NF-YA subunit in the nucleus (Myers and Holt  2018; Siriwardana  2024). Upon trimerisation, NF-YA recognises the specific CCAAT cis-element in the promoters of target genes, thereby transactivating their transcription. This cis-element is present in ${ \sim } 3 0 \%$ of eukaryotic gene promoters (Bucher 1990; Mantovani 1998; Nardone et  al.  2017; Siriwardana  2024). Unlike the large gene families of NAC, WRKY and ERF TFs in plants (Yuan et al. 2019; Wani et  al.  2021; Dong et  al.  2024; Xiong et  al.  2025), the NF-Y TF family is relatively small. For instance, Arabidopsis contains 10 NF-YA, 13 NF-YB and 13 NF-YC subunits (Siefers et al. 2009), while rice contains 11 NF-YA, 11 NF-YB and 12 NF-YC subunits (Yang et  al.  2017). NF-Y TFs are widely involved in plant growth, development and stress adaptation (Siriwardana  2024; Chouaibi et  al.  2025). Several studies have revealed the important roles of NF-Y TFs in plant immunity. For instance, Arabidopsis AtNF-YB2/3 and AtNF-YC4 (Qi et al. 2019; Lin, Lan, et al. 2024), soybean GmNF-YC4-2 (O'Conner et  al.  2021), Medicago truncatula MtNF-YA1-1 (Rey et  al.  2016), cassava MeNF-YA1/3, MeNF-YB11/16 and MeNF-YC11/12/15 (He et  al.  2020; Zheng et  al.  2024), grapevine VqNF-YC9 (Zhang et  al.  2025), and watermelon ClNF-YA2/3, ClNF-YB1/8 and ClNF-YC2/4 (Jiang et al. 2022) have been implicated in plant disease resistance against diverse fungal, bacterial and viral pathogens. In rice, OsNF-YA2 (OsHAP2E) and OsNF-YC12 positively modulate resistance to fungal, bacterial and nematode diseases (Alam et  al.  2015; Chen et  al.  2025). Conversely, OsNF-YA1/2/10 negatively 

regulate viral resistance by suppressing JA signalling (Tan et al. 2022), while OsNF-YC1/2 interact with OsNF-YA1/2 to inhibit their transcriptional activation activity, thereby suppressing OsNF-YA2-mediated susceptibility to viral pathogens (Tan et  al.  2024). Additionally, overexpression of miR169a increases susceptibility to M. oryzae by targeting OsNF-YA genes (Li et al. 2017). These studies highlight the crucial roles of OsNF-Ys in rice immunity; however, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain largely unknown, particularly regarding the assembly of immune-associated OsNF-YA/B/C trimers and their downstream target genes. 

Previous studies have demonstrated that OsRFPH2-6, encoding a putative RING-type E3 ligase, is transcriptionally activated by the ONAC TF, ONAC083, but is targeted and repressed by microRNA1439 during M. oryzae infection (Bi et al. 2023; Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). Knockout of OsRFPH2-6 enhanced rice blast resistance (Bi et al. 2023; Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). In the present study, we further investigated the biochemical activity of OsRFPH2-6 and explored its underlying mechanism in rice immunity by identifying putative interactors. Our results reveal that OsRFPH2-6 possesses E3 ligase activity and ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1, a subunit of the OsNF-Y TFs, leading to its degradation. Additionally, we found that OsNF-YA1 positively regulates rice immunity, including blast resistance and PTI responses, and modulates the expression of a large set of defence genes. These findings establish a new regulatory mechanism in which OsRFPH2-6 negatively modulates rice immunity by ubiquitinating and mediating degradation of OsNF-YA1. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Negative Roles of OsRFPH2-6 in Rice Immunity

To examine the role of OsRFPH2-6 in rice immunity, we generated OsRFPH2-6 overexpression (OE) lines in cv. ZH11 background, and obtained two independent lines, OsRFPH2-6-OE1 and -OE2, in which the transcript levels of OsRFPH2-6 were 28.39- and 80.53-fold higher than wild type (WT) (Figure S1). In punch inoculation assays with M. oryzae strain RB22, the OsRFPH2-6-OE lines developed lesions $1 7 7 \% - 1 9 4 \%$ larger and exhibited $4 1 5 \% - 7 2 2 \%$ more fungal growth compared to WT ZH11 (Figure  1a–c). Consistently, the expression levels of defence genes, such as OsPR8, OsPBZ1 and OsWRKY53, were significantly downregulated in OsRFPH2-6-OE lines (except for OsWRKY53 under noninfection conditions), with or without M. oryzae infection (Figure 1d). These data further confirm the negative role of OsRFPH2-6 in rice blast resistance by suppressing the expression of defence genes, consistent with previous observations obtained in osrfph2-6 knockout lines (Bi et al. 2023; Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). 

We then investigated whether OsRFPH2-6 plays a role in rice PTI by analysing chitin-triggered reactive oxygen species (ROS) bursts and the expression of PTI marker genes such as OsWRKY45 in OsRFPH2-6-OE plants. Without chitin stimulation, no significant differences in ROS bursts and OsWRKY45 expression were detected in OsRFPH-2-6-OE lines compared 

with ZH11 (Figure  1e,f). However, upon chitin stimulation, chitin-triggered ROS bursts and the expression level of OsWRKY45 were markedly boosted in OsRFPH2-6-OE lines compared with ZH11 (Figure  1e,f). These data indicate that OsRFPH2-6 also plays a negative role in chitin-triggered PTI in rice. 

# 2.2 | E3 Ubiquitin Ligase Activity of OsRFPH2-6

OsRFPH2-6 encodes a 162-amino acid (aa) protein that contains a conserved RING domain at the C-terminal and a transmembrane (TM) motif at the N-terminal (Figure  2a; Lim et  al.  2010). To explore whether OsRFPH2-6 possesses E3 ligase activity, we purified GST-OsRFPH2-6 protein and performed an in vitro E3 ligase activity assay. The results revealed that GST-OsRFPH2-6 self-ubiquitinated in the presence of E1, E2 and ubiquitin, as evident by a polyubiquitinated smear detected by immunoblotting with anti-GST antibody or anti-FLAG antibody, whereas no detectable ubiquitination of GST-OsRFPH2-6 or GST alone was observed in negative controls (Figure 2b). Considering that the conserved cysteine residues in RING domains of RING-type E3 ligases are critical for E3 ligase activity (Lin et al. 2008; Suh and Kim 2015), we examined the role of cysteine residues in the RING domain of OsRFPH2-6 for its E3 ligase activity. For this, we individually mutated cysteine residues at positions 101, 104, 120, 128, 140 and 143 in the OsRFPH2-6 protein (Figure 2a) and assessed their E3 ligase activity in vitro. The results showed that mutation of these conserved cysteine residues completely abolished the E3 ligase activity (Figure  2c). These data indicate that OsRFPH2-6 is an active E3 ligase and its E3 ligase activity is dependent on the cysteine residues in its RING domain.

# 2.4 | OsRFPH2-6-Mediated Ubiquitination and Degradation of OsNF-YA1

E3 ligases often regulate rice immune responses by ubiquitinating their substrates for degradation via the UPS pathway (Yan, Wang, Bi, and Song 2024). Thus, we first analysed whether OsRFPH2-6 ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1. In in  vitro ubiquitination assays with E1, E2 and ubiquitin, we observed clear polyubiquitinated bands in the presence of both OsNF-YA1-HIS and GST-OsRFPH2-6 in the reactions when detected using anti-FLAG antibody (Figure 4a, lanes 5, 6 and 7 in the middle panel). The intensity of these polyubiquitinated bands increased with the increasing amounts of OsNF-YA1-HIS and were more prominent than the self-polyubiquitinated GST-OsRFPH2-6 band (Figure  4a, lane 9 in the middle panel). Notably, polyubiquitinated bands of OsNF-YA1-HIS were detected using anti-His antibody, and the intensity of these bands increased with the increasing amounts of OsNF-YA1- HIS (Figure 4a, lanes 5, 6 and 7 in the lower panel), comparable to the polyubiquitinated bands detected using anti-FLAG antibody (Figure  4a, lanes 5, 6 and 7 in the middle panel). However, the self-polyubiquitinated bands of GST-OsRFPH2-6 in the presence of OsNF-YA1-HIS (Figure 4a, lanes 5, 6 and 7 in the upper panel) were comparable to that in the absence of 

OsNF-YA1-HIS (Figure  4a, lane 9 in the upper panel) when detected using anti-GST antibody, indicating that the E3 ligase activity of OsRFPH2-6 is not affected by OsNF-YA1. These results suggest that OsRFPH2-6 ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1 in vitro. 

Next, we investigated whether OsRFPH2-6 mediates the degradation of OsNF-YA1 using in planta and in vitro degradation assays. In the in planta degradation assays, where OsRFPH2-6 was transiently co-expressed in N. benthamiana leaves, OsNF-YA1-HA protein levels gradually decreased in the absence of MG132, with a $6 2 \%$ reduction at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after agroinfiltration (Figure  4b). This degradation was partially suppressed by MG132 (Figure 4b). In in vitro cell-free degradation assays, the amounts of OsNF-YA1-HIS protein decreased more slowly in total protein extracts from osrfph2-6 lines, but more rapidly in total protein extracts from OsRFPH2-6-OE plants compared to those from ZH11 plants (Figures 4c and S4a). The degradation of OsNF-YA1-HIS in total protein extracts from OsRFPH2-6-OE and ZH11 plants was inhibited by MG132 (Figures 4c and S4a). The His tag itself remained stable in the total protein extracts from all plant lines (Figure  S4b,c). These results indicate that OsRFPH2-6 ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1 and promotes its degradation through the UPS pathway.

# 2.5 | Positive Roles of OsNF-YA1 in Rice Immunity

To investigate the role of OsNF-YA1 in rice immunity, we constructed CRISPR/Cas9-mediated OsNF-YA1 knockout lines and identified two homozygous lines osnf-ya1-1 and osnfya1-2, each containing a 1 bp insertion (Figure S5). In punch inoculation assays with M. oryzae strain RB22, the osnf-ya1 lines exhibited larger lesions and supported more fungal growth, with lesion area and fungal growth $1 9 9 \% - 2 0 9 \%$ and $1 3 9 \% - 1 4 0 \%$ greater than those in ZH11 at 5 dpi (Figure 5a–c). We also investigated the involvement of OsNF-YA1 in rice PTI by analysing chitin-induced ROS bursts, the expression of PTI marker gene OsWRKY45, and activation of mitogenactivated protein kinases in osnf-ya1. Without chitin stimulation, no significant differences in ROS bursts and OsWRKY45 expression were detected in osnf-ya1 plants compared with ZH11, except for a downregulation of OsWRKY45 expression in osnf-ya1-1 line at 60 min (Figure 5d,e). After chitin stimulation, the ROS bursts and OsWRKY45 expression were dramatically decreased in osnf-ya1 leaves compared with those of ZH11 (Figure 5d,e). Additionally, the activities of OsMPK3 and OsMPK6 were very low in untreated osnf-ya1 and ZH11 

leaves; however, the MAPK activity in osnf-ya1 leaves was significantly lower than in ZH11 leaves after chitin stimulation (Figure  5f). These results showed that OsNF-YA1 positively regulates rice blast resistance and chitin-triggered PTI responses, including ROS bursts, OsWRKY45 expression and MAPK activation.

# 3 | Discussion

E3 ligases, key components of the UPS pathway, have been welldocumented in plant immunity (Craig et al. 2009; Duplan and Rivas  2014; Zhou and Zeng  2017; Yin et  al.  2019; Yan, Wang, 

Bi, and Song 2024). For instance, XB3, OsBBI1, APIP6, OsMEL, DGS1, OsRGLG5, OsRING113, IPI1 and OsZFP1 positively regulate rice immunity against M. oryzae, X. oryzae and/or R. solani (Wang, Pi, et al. 2006; Wang, Zheng, et al. 2024; Li et al. 2011; Park et al. 2012; Ning et al. 2015; Gao et al. 2021; Liu et al. 2021, 2023; Fu et al. 2022; You et al. 2022, 2023; Shi et al. 2024; Yi et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024), whereas OsRFPH2-6, OsRFPH2-10, EBR1, OsREIW1, OsATL32 and OsJMJ715 negatively regulate rice immunity against M. oryzae, X. oryzae or brown planthopper 

(Liu et al. 2014; You et al. 2016; Zhang et al. 2021; Bi et al. 2023; Wang et  al.  2023; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Li, et  al.  2024; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et  al.  2024). Previous studies have revealed that OsRFPH2-6, regulated by the NAC TF ONAC083 and microRNA1439, negatively regulates rice blast resistance using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout mutants (Bi et al. 2023, Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). In this study, we further confirmed the negative role of OsRFPH2-6 in rice immunity using OsRFPH2- 6-overexpressing lines and revealed the E3 ligase activity of OsRFPH2-6. We also discovered that OsRFPH2-6 interacts with and ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1, a subunit of NF-Y TF complexes, leading to its degradation. Furthermore, we revealed that OsNF-YA1 positively regulates rice immunity by modulating a set of immune-associated genes. Together with our previous observations, these findings establish an immune regulatory module: M. oryzae-induced ONAC083 activates the transcription of OsRFPH2-6, which in turn targets OsNF-YA1 for degradation, thereby modulating rice immunity. 

OsRFPH2-6 contains a typical RING domain and a putative TM motif (Figure 2a). The conserved cysteine residues in the RING domain are critical for the E3 ligase activity of RING-type E3 ligases (Lin et al. 2008; Suh and Kim 2015). OsRFPH2-6 possesses E3 ligase activity, as it was self-ubiquitinated in an in vitro assay (Figure  2b), and the six cysteine residues in its RING domain are essential for its activity (Figure  2c). Phenotypic analyses of both knockout mutants and overexpression lines confirmed that OsRFPH2-6 negatively contributes to rice immunity, including blast resistance and chitin-triggered immune responses (Figure 1; Bi et al. 2023; Liu, Wang, et al. 2025). This is in parallel to the negative role of OsRFPH2-10 in rice antiviral immunity (Liu et  al.  2014). Y2H screening and protein– protein interaction assays revealed that OsRFPH2-6 interacts with and ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1 for degradation, in both in planta and in cell-free assays (Figures  3 and 4). These results establish a direct biochemical connection between OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1. Given the negative immune role of OsRFPH2-6, its downstream substrates should be positive regulators of rice immunity. Our functional analysis using osnf-ya1 knockout mutants revealed that OsNF-YA1 confers resistance to rice blast disease (Figures  5a–c) and plays a key role in chitin-triggered immunity  (Figure  5d–f). NF-Y TFs have been shown to play indispensable roles in plant immunity (Alam et  al.  2015; Rey et al. 2016; He et al. 2020; O'Conner et al. 2021; Tan et al. 2022; Jiang et  al.  2022). For instance, OsNF-YA2 and OsNF-YC12 positively regulates rice resistance to blast, bacterial leaf blight and nematode diseases (Alam et  al.  2015; Chen et  al.  2025), while OsNF-YA1, -YA2 or -YA10 negatively regulate rice resistance to two viral diseases (Tan et  al.  2022). The positive role of OsNF-YA1 in blast resistance (Figure  5) contrasts with its negative function in rice antiviral immunity (Tan et al. 2022), likely due to the complex and distinct mechanisms rice employs to counteracts different pathogens. 

Different types of TFs are critical for transcriptional reprogramming during immune responses (Yuan et al. 2019; Wani et al. 2021; Tan et al. 2022; Xiong et al. 2025). Recent studies have revealed the connection between TFs from different families and E3 ligases in plant immunity (Yu et al. 2013; Liu et al. 2019; Zavaliev et al. 2020; Ma et al. 2021; Gao et al. 2024; Wang, Gao, et al. 2025; Lin et al. 2023; Zhao et al. 2024, 2025). For instance, the maize 

E3 ligase ZmMIEL1 targets two TFs, ZmMYB83 and ZmAHL25, to regulate immunity (Karre et  al.  2021; Liang et  al.  2025). In rice, APIP6, OsRING113, OsREIW1 and APIP10 target rice TFs IPA1, APIP5, OsWRKY31 and OsVOZ1/OsVOZ2, respectively (Wang, Wang, et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2023; Shi et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024). Notably, both OsRFPH2-6, as an E3 ligase to ubiquitinate OsNF-YA1 for degradation, and ONAC083, as a TF to activate OsRFPH2-6, negatively regulate blast resistance (Figure 1; Bi et al. 2023). These findings extend the previously established ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6 regulatory module in rice immunity by integrating OsNF-YA1 as a downstream substrate of OsRFPH2-6. 

The NF-Y TF is composed of three subunits NF-YA, NF-YB and NF-YC, in which NF-YB and NF-YC form heterodimers in the cytoplasm and interact with NF-YA to form trimers in the nucleus (Chaves-Sanjuan et al. 2021). The NF-YA subunit is responsible for recognising the CCAAT cis-element in target gene promoters (Nardone et al. 2017; Myers and Holt 2018; Chaves-Sanjuan et al. 2021; Siriwardana 2024), thereby modulating the transcription of target genes. Our RNA-seq-based transcriptome analysis revealed that knockout of OsNF-YA1 reprogrammes the transcription of numerous genes involved in different GO pathways and KEGG categories in osnf-ya1 plants (Figures 6a,b and S6). A set of previously reported immune-associated genes was shortlisted as either upregulated or downregulated DEGs in osnf-ya1 plants (Figure 6c,d). Because OsNF-YA1 positively regulates rice blast resistance and chitin-triggered immunity (Figure 5), it is expected that positive immune regulators should be downregulated in osnf-ya1 plants, while the genes negatively regulating immunity should be upregulated. This hypothesis is supported by the expression patterns of several immuneassociated genes in osnf-ya1 plants with or without M. oryzae infection. Our RNA-seq and reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) assays revealed upregulated expression of negative immune regulators such as MNAC3, ONAC083 and OsSGR (Bi et  al.  2023; Wang, Bi, et  al.  2024; Xie et  al.  2025), as well as upregulated expression of several positive immune regulators such as OsATL15 (Xiao et  al.  2022, 2025), OsCPS4 (Toyomasu et  al.  2014, 2015), OsWRKY6 (Hwang et  al.  2011; Choi et al. 2015; Im et al. 2022, 2024), OsPAL6 (Wang, You, et al. 2022; Liu, He, et al. 2025), OsPR10a (Huang, Lin, et al. 2016; He et al. 2018) and chi11 (Maruthasalam et al. 2007) (Figures 6c,d and 7). Notably, some genes involved in phytoalexin biosynthesis, including CYP76M6 (Wu et al. 2013) and OsDXS3 (Valette et al. 2020), and those in the phenylpropanoid pathway, such as OsPAL6 (Wang, You, et al. 2022; Liu, He, et al. 2025) and Os4CL1 (Gui et al. 2011; Sun, Li, et al. 2013), were downregulated in osnf-ya1 plants (Figures 6c,d and 7). Together with the enriched KEGG pathways of ‘diterpenoid biosynthesis’ and ‘phenylpropanoid biosynthesis’ for the downregulated DEGs in osnf-ya1 plants (Figure S6b), it is likely that OsNF-YA1 affects the phytoalexin biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid pathway to regulate rice immunity, which needs further investigations. However, we also observed that the expression patterns of some immune regulators in osnf-ya1 plants was contrary to their roles in rice immunity. For instance, OsTPS35, which plays a positive role in resistance to insect pests (Chen et  al.  2024), and OsNAC30, which negatively regulates bacterial leaf blight (Son et al. 2024), exhibited upregulated and downregulated expression in osnf-ya1 plants, respectively (Figure 6c,d). This contrast may arise from the distinct roles of these genes in rice immunity against 

different pathogens. For instance, ONAC083 negatively regulates rice resistance to M. oryzae without participating in resistance to bacterial leaf blight (Bi et al. 2023). Similarly, OsNF-YA1 negatively impacts antiviral immunity but plays a positive role in blast resistance (Figure 5; Tan et al. 2022). However, whether the identified DEGs in osnf-ya1 plants are the direct target genes of OsNF-YA1 remains to be investigated. Furthermore, NF-Y TFs exert their functions in regulating transcription of target genes through the assembly of NF-Y complexes with all three subunits (Chaves-Sanjuan et al. 2021). There are 11 OsNF-YA, 11 OsNF-YB and 12 OsNF-YC genes in the rice genome (Yang et  al.  2017). Future studies should explore which OsNF-YB and OsNF-YC subunits are assembled with OsNF-YA1 to form functional NF-Y TF complexes that regulate the transcription of immune-associated genes and modulate rice immune responses upon pathogen infection. Therefore, characterising the OsNF-YA1-centered NF-Y trimer complexes and their direct target genes will provide novel insights into the molecular regulatory mechanisms of OsNF-YA1 in rice immunity. 

Based on our experimental data and previous reports (Bi et al. 2023), we propose a simplified working model for the function of the ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6-OsNF-YA1 network in rice immunity (Figure 8). In this model, ONAC083 is upregulated by M. oryzae infection, and ONAC083 activates the expression of OsRFPH2-6 by binding to the ACGCAA cis-element in its promoter. OsRFPH2-6, in turn, interacts with and ubiquitinates OsNF-YA1 for degradation, thereby disrupting the positive role of OsNF-YA1 in rice immunity, including blast resistance. This study provides a foundation for improving rice disease resistance through the modification of the ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6- OsNF-YA1 module via targeted genome editing. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

For gene expression analysis and the generation of transgenic lines for OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1, the rice cultivar Zhonghua11 (ZH11; Oryza sativa subsp. japonica) was used as the wild type (WT). N. benthamiana was employed for transient expression experiments. Rice seeds were pregerminated for 3 days at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , planted into a mixed soil (vermiculite:peat $\mathrm { s o i l } = 3 { : } 1$ ) and grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $6 0 \%$ relative humidity (RH) under a 14 h light/10 h dark photoperiod. N. benthamiana plants were grown in a growth room at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a $6 0 \%$ relative humidity (RH) and a 14 h light/10 h dark photoperiod (Bi et al. 2023; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Li, et al. 2024; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et al. 2024). 

# 4.2 | Characterisation of Transgenic Lines

To generate OsRFPH2-6 overexpression (OE) lines, the coding sequence of OsRFPH2-6 was cloned into the pCAMBIA-1300s-GFP vector to generate a recombinant vector. For the osnf-ya1 knockout lines, target sequences in OsNF-YA1 were selected using the CRISPR-P website (http://crispr.hzau.edu.cn/cgi-bin/ CRISPR/CRISPR), and the corresponding sgRNA expression cassettes were cloned into the pYLCRISPR $\mathbf { \dot { C } a s 9 P } _ { U b i }$ to generate the CRISPR/Cas9 recombinant vector. These recombinant vectors were then introduced into ZH11 via Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation. The OE lines and CRISPR/Cas9 lines were subsequently characterised as described previously (Wang, Bi, et al. 2021).

FIGURE 8 | Proposed model for the ONAC083-OsRFPH2-6-OsNF-YA1 regulatory network in rice immunity. OsRFPH2-6, an E3 ligase that negatively regulates rice immunity against Magnaporthe oryzae, is transcriptionally activated by ONAC083, a NAC transcription factor that also functions as a negative regulator of rice immunity to M. oryzae. Upon M. oryzae infection, ONAC083 expression is upregulated and subsequently activates OsRFPH2-6 transcription by binding to the ACGCAA cis-element in its promoter region. OsRFPH2-6 then interacts with OsNF-YA1, mediating its ubiquitination and subsequent degradation through the UPS pathway. This molecular cascade ultimately disrupts the positive regulatory role of OsNF-YA1 in rice resistance to M. oryzae, thereby contributing to the negative regulation of rice immunity.


Magnaporthe oryzae strain RB22 was grown on complete medium (CM) at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for spore production for 2 weeks, and spores were then collected for disease assays (Talbot et  al.  1993; Bi et  al.  2023). Detached rice leaf surfaces were inoculated with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquots containing a standardised spore concentration $( 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores/mL in $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween 20 solution) and incubated in the dark at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ under $1 0 0 \%$ RH, and then moved to the growth room. Lesion length was recorded at 5 days postinoculation (dpi), and leaf samples were collected for measurement of in planta fungal growth presented as fold changes in the genomic levels of M. oryzae MoPot2/rice ubiquitin (OsUbi) genes via qPCR analysis (Bi et al. 2023). 

# 4.4 | RT-qPCR Assays

Total RNA was extracted from plants with or without corresponding treatment using RNA Isolater reagent (Vazyme). First-strand cDNA was synthesised using the reverse transcription system (Vazyme) according to the manufacturer's recommendations. For qPCRs, a $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ mixture was preapared, contained $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 2×AceQ qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix (Vazyme), $0 . 1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ cDNA, $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ each of the primers (Table S4) and double-distilled water. The reactions were performed on a CFX96 real-time PCR system (Bio-Rad). The rice 18S rRNA gene was used as an internal control to normalise the data, and relative gene expression was calculated by the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } }$ method (Wang, Bi, et al. 2021). 

# 4.5 | ROS Assays

Leaf discs from OsRFPH2-6-OE, osnf-ya1 and ZH11 plants were preincubated overnight in $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ doule-distilled water in the wells of 96-well plates under growth conditions. Prior to chemiluminescent signal collection, the plates were exposed to light for at least 2 h. The reaction buffer contained $2 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ luminol (Sigma-Aldrich), $2 0 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ horseradish peroxidase (Sigma-Aldrich), $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ chitin (Sigma-Aldrich), with double-distilled water replacing chitin in the control samples. Chemiluminescent signals were recorded immediately after adding reaction buffer in 96-well plates using a Synergy HT plate reader (Biotek Instruments). ROS bursts were quantified and presented as relative luminescence units (RLUs) (Bi et al. 2023). 

# 4.6 | Y2H Assays

The coding sequences of OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1 were cloned into pGBKT7 and pGADT7 vectors, respectively. Paired pGBKT7 and pGADT7 recombinant vectors, or empty vectors, were cotransformed into yeast strain Y2H Gold using the lithium acetate/single-stranded carrier DNA/polyethylene glycol method (Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et al. 2024). Transformed yeast cells were initially cultured on SD/−Leu/−Trp medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days, and then diluted onto SD/−Leu/−Trp/−His/−Ade medium (Clontech) containing $4 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ X-α-Gal and $1 2 5 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ aureobasidin A at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 days. The interaction between the proteins was assessed based on the growth performance of yeast cells (Wang, Bi, et al. 2021). 

The coding sequences of OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1 were individually cloned into p2YC and p2YN vectors, respectively, and transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101. Agrobacteria harbouring the recombinant vectors, or empty vectors, were co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves expressing a red nuclear marker protein RFP-H2B (Chakrabarty et  al.  2007), and incubated under growth conditions. YFP and RFP fluorescent signals were visualised using an LSM880 confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss) at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after agroinfiltration. 

# 4.8 | LCI Assays

The coding sequences of OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1 were individually cloned into Nluc and Cluc vectors and introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101, respectively. Agrobacteria carrying the plasmids were coinfiltrated in pairs into N. benthamiana leaves and the plants were placed in a dark room for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . After treatment with fluorescein substrate, fluorescent signals were detected using the 5200 Chemiluminescent Imaging System (Tanon Sci & Tech) (Bi et al. 2025). 

# 4.9 | GST Pull-Down Assays

To perform GST pull-down assays, the coding sequences of OsRFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1 were cloned into pGEX-4T-3 and pET-32a vectors, respectively, and expressed in Escherichia coli Rosetta DE3 to purify GST-ORFPH2-6 and OsNF-YA1-HIS fusion proteins. GST-OsRFPH2-6 and the GST-tag protein were first incubated with the glutathione resin column in GST pulldown buffer $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl, pH 7.4, $5 \%$ glycerol, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), 1 mM PMSF, $0 . 0 1 \%$ Nonidet P-40 and 150 mM KCl) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. Subsequently, OsNF-YA1-HIS protein was added and incubated for an additional 2 h. After washing with phosphate-buffered saline, the supernatant was collected using glutathione buffer and subjected to immunoblotting using anti-GST and anti-His antibodies (GenScript) (Bi et al. 2025). 

# 4.10 | Subcellular Localisation

Agrobacteria carrying pCAMBIA1300-OsRFPH2-6-GFP, pCAMBIA1300-OsNF-YA1-GFP and pCAMBIA1300-OsRFPH2- 6-mCherry were infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves expressing a red nuclear marker protein RFP-H2B either separately or in combination (Chakrabarty et al. 2007). The agroinfiltrated plants were then placed in a dark room for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ . Fluorescent signals were detected using the Zeiss LSM780 confocal laser scanning microscope (Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et al. 2024). 

# 4.11 | RNA-Seq Analyses

RNA-seq analysis was conducted on leaf samples from 3-week-old osnf-ya1 and ZH11 plants grown under normal conditions, using the Novaseq 6000 platform (Illumina) by 

BioMarker Technologies (Beijing, China). DEGs were defined as genes with $\geq 2$ -fold expression change and an FDR $\le 0 . 0 1$ . GO/ KEGG enrichment analysis were performed as described previously (Wang, Bi, et al. 2021; Bi et al. 2023). 

# 4.12 | E3 Activity and Ubiquitination Assays

The OsNF-YA1-HIS, GST-OsRFPH2-6 and GST-OsRFPH2-6- cysteine mutated fusion proteins were prepared as described in Section  4.8. The in  vitro E3 activity and ubiquitination assays were conducted as previously described (Bi et al. 2023). Briefly, reactions were carried out in a $3 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ buffer (50 mM Tri-HCl, pH 7.4, 100 mM KCl, 5 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 2mM ATP and $2 \mathrm { m M }$ DTT) containing 1 mg ubiquitin (Boston Biochem), 110 ng E1 (Merck Millipore), $1 0 0 \mathrm { n g }$ human recombinant E2 (UBCh5a2, Abcam), $2 \mu \mathrm { g }$ OsNF-YA1-HIS, GST-OsRFPH2-6 (or GST-OsRFPH2-6- cysteine mutated proteins) or GST-tag protein (negative control). For E3 ligase activity, only GST-OsRFPH2-6 or GST-OsRFPH2- 6-cysteine mutated proteins were included. Reactions were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 { - } 3 \mathrm { h }$ , terminated with SDS buffer and resolved on SDS-PAGE gels. Immunoblotting was performed using anti-FLAG (Sigma-Aldrich), anti-His (GenScript), and anti-GST (GenScript) antibodies. Chemiluminescent signals were detected using the Tanon-5200 Chemiluminescent Imaging System. 

# 4.13 | | Protein Degradation Assays

For cell-free degradation assays, $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g }$ OsNF-YA1-HIS protein was added to total protein extracts from OsRFPH2-6-OE, osrfph2-6 and ZH11 plants in degradation buffer $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl, pH 7.4, 10 mM NaCl, 10 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5mM DTT and $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ ATP) (Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Li, et al. 2024; Yan, Wang, Bi, Wang, Noman, et  al.  2024). The reactions were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and stopped by adding SDS loading buffer at the indicated time points. The samples were separated on SDS-PAGE and subjected to immunoblotting using anti-His (GenScript) and anti-actin (GenScript) antibodies. To assess protein degradation in planta, adgrobacteria carrying either pFGC1008- OsNF-YA1-HA or pCAMBIA1300-OsRFPH2-6-GFP were infiltrated into leaves of 4-week-old N. benthamiana leaves, either individually or in combination. The leaves were treated with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132 or dimethylsulphoxide (DMSO) (as a mock control) at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation. Total proteins were subsequently extracted at the indicated time points and detected by immunoblotting using anti-HA (Sigma-Aldrich), anti-GFP (Abcam) and anti-actin (GenScript) antibodies. The grey values of target bands in cell-free and in planta protein degradation assays were calculated using ImageJ software and normalised with the first lane. 

# 4.14 | Statistical Analysis

All experiments were conducted at least three times. Data analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 9 and the Data Processing System (DPS). Statistical significance was assessed 

using a two-tailed Student's t test or one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey's multiple range test.
