# ABSTRACT

The basis of modern pharmacology is the human ability to exploit the production of specialized metabolites from medical plants, for example, terpenoids, alkaloids, and phenolic acids. However, in most cases, the availability of these

valuable compounds is limited by cellular or organelle barriers or spatio-temporal accumulation patterns within different plant tissues. Transcription factors (TFs) regulate biosynthesis of these specialized metabolites by tightly controlling the expression of biosynthetic genes. Cutting-edge technologies and/or combining multiple strategies and approaches have been applied to elucidate the role of TFs. In this review, we focus on recent progress in the transcription regulation mechanism of representative high-value products and describe the transcriptional regulatory network, and future perspectives are discussed, which will help develop high-yield plant resources.

Keywords: biosynthesis, medicinal plants, omics, secondary metabolism, transcription factors

# INTRODUCTION

Specialized metabolites are a diverse group of compounds not directly involved in plant growth, development, or reproduction (Yang et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2021). However, they play a crucial role in plant defense and adaptation to external cues and stresses. Medicinal plants are known to produce a wide range of specialized metabolites classified into terpenoids, alkaloids, and phenylpropanoids according to their chemical structure and biosynthetic pathways (Figures 1, 2), which have been extensively studied for their therapeutic properties (Shi et al., 2021a; Zheng et al., 2023).

Terpenoids are derived from the five-carbon building block isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP), which are synthesized through the 2-C-methyl-D-erythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) and/or mevalonate (MVA)

pathways, diversifying their structure and biological activities (Patra et al., 2013). Some well-known terpenoids found in medicinal plants include the sesquiterpenoid artemisinin, the diterpenoids taxol, tanshinones, and triptolide, and the triterpenoid saponins (Kai et al., 2011a; Lu et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2017a; Huo et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2023). Another important class of secondary metabolites found in herbs is alkaloids, featured with a nitrogen atom in their chemical structure (Wang et al., 2021). Alkaloids are synthesized through multiple routes, mainly originating from amino acids. Common groups of alkaloids contain the indole alkaloid, including camptothecin (Hao et al., 2023), vinblastine and vincristine (Zhang et al., 2011), benzylisoquinoline alkaloids, for example, berberine and sanguinarine (Yamada et al., 2011; Beaudoin and Facchini, 2013), or the tropane alkaloids consisting of hyoscyamine and scopolamine (Qiu et al., 2018).

Many alkaloids show pharmacological activities and are usually used as drug leads. The phenylpropanoids group also represents a diverse group described in medicinal plants, including phenolic acids, lignin, and flavonoids being characterized by diverse biological activities such as antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties (Yang et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2021).

The production and accumulation of secondary metabolites are tightly regulated in the plant, tissue, cell, and organelle levels, challenging the current tools and bio-engineering strategies. For example, artemisinin is produced in particular glandular trichomes in Artemisia annua (Lu et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2017). Tanshinones mainly accumulate in the roots of Salvia miltiorrhiza (Kai et al., 2011a; Ma et al., 2021), as well as a higher content of ginsenosides in Panax ginseng roots (Hou et al., 2021). Taxol is isolated from the stem bark of the Taxus species (Yu et al., 2023). Rich anthocyanins are produced in stems of Dendrobium candidum (Jia et al., 2021). In addition, some secondary metabolites are distributed in a spatiotemporal pattern. For example, sugars and pigments accumulate at the ripening stage in citrus fruits (Feng et al., 2021). The epicatechin content in the rhizomes of Fagopyrum dibotrys increases by $25\%$ at the withering stage compared with normal flowering (Guo et al., 2022). In Gelsemium elegans, 19E-16-epi-voacarpine mainly accumulates in the epidermis region in mature stems but in the pith region at the seedling stage (Wu et al., 2022). However, the reasons why secondary

metabolites do not accumulate to higher levels have been a topic of many researchers. In some cases, tissue or organ or developmentally specific ways limit the yield of secondary metabolites in medicinal plants. For instance, artemisinin production in A. annua plant accounts for only $0.1\% - 1.0\%$ dry weight (Zhang et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2017). The content of vinblastine and vincristine in Catharanthus roseus is minimal and mainly produced in the aerial parts (Suttipanta et al., 2011). Therefore, multiple approaches and technologies have been used to boost the production of valuable secondary metabolites. For example, various elicitors have been applied to improve certain secondary metabolite contents, including phytohormone methyl jasmonate (MeJA), abscisic acid (ABA), salicylic acid (SA), brassinosteroids (BRs), and ethylene (Eth) or metal ions such as $\mathrm{Ag^{+}}$ or $\mathrm{Cu}^{2+}$ (Shi et al., 2021a). Genetic engineering of biosynthetic genes also effectively enhanced secondary metabolite content (Shi et al., 2021a). Utilizing a push-pull strategy by modulating specific biosynthetic genes including 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase (HMGR), geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthase (GGPPS), 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate synthase (DXS), and 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate reductoisomerase (DXR) in S. miltiorrhiza, such as overexpression of HMGR-GGPPS, GGPPS-DXS2, HMGR-DXR, significantly improved tanshinone content (Kai et al., 2011a; Shi et al., 2014, 2016). Further, transcription factors (TFs) that regulate secondary metabolite production by controlling the target genes involved in special pathways have been characterized, classified into a variety of families such as bHLH, MYB, AP2/ERF, WRKY, bZIP, NAC, MADS and GRAS (Yang et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). Technologies containing clustered regularly interspaced palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR-associated protein 9 (CRISPR-Cas9) and virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) have been successfully applied in various medicinal herbs to explore the function of different TFs (Hsieh et al., 2013; Ma et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2018; Garg et al., 2021; Shi

et al., 2021a). In this review, we focus on recent advances in several representative plants for the transcriptional regulation of well-known products.

# STRATEGIES TO EXPLORE POTENTIAL TF REGULATING THE PRODUCTION OF SECONDARY METABOLITES

With the rapid development of sequencing technology, the whole genome sequences of many medicinal plants have been sequenced and analyzed, including A. annua, S. militiorrhiza,

Papaver somniferum, P. ginseng, Ganoderma lucidum (Chen et al., 2012; Xu et al., 2016, 2017; Guo et al., 2018; Shen et al., 2018). Transcription factors within the whole genome have been annotated into different families. For instance, 1,620 TF genes have been found in S. miltiorrhiza (Xu et al., 2016). A sum of 778 TFs was identified in Ophionrhiza pumila genome (Rai et al., 2021). The role of TFs in regulating the production of desired metabolites has been extensively investigated. In many cases, exogenous applications of elicitors/inducers were exploited to increase the content of certain valuable products (Zhang et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2017; Zheng et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2021a, 2021b). Following the profiling of the desired metabolites, expression patterns of TFs were examined. Over 350 TFs exhibited were up-regulated following S. miltiorrhiza root treated with MeJA and yeast extract (YE) (Zhou et al., 2017). Spatio-temporal expression patterns were also addressed to identify key TFs. Transcriptome information from different tissues has also been reported (Xu et al., 2016; Hao et al., 2023). The above characteristics provided the basis for screening candidate TF genes. Using the corresponding screening strategies, including (i) in high homology with known-function genes or a specific TF family, (ii) induced by stress or elicitors or regulated by other TFs and/or high expression in specific tissue/organ or species, (iii) similar co-expression pattern with biosynthetic pathway genes, (iv) yeast one-hybrid (Y1H)/two-hybrid screening, (v) others, dozens of TFs have been successfully identified and characterized.

# TRANSCRIPTIONAL REGULATION OF VALUE-ADDED PRODUCT BIOSYNTHESIS IN MEDICINAL PLANTS

The genome and transcriptome pool provide a fast and reliable basis for screening potential TFs (Xu et al., 2016; Shi et al., 2021a). Plant systems such as hairy roots, transgenic plants, and plant callus have been widely used to study the function of TFs within medicinal plants. It is reported that a regenerated transgenic system has been established in only a few medicinal plants, which restricts deep study (Shi et al., 2021a). Hairy roots, also called genetically transformed root cultures, offer another excellent system for identifying gene function, with the advantage of faster production of desired compounds (Shi et al., 2021a).

# Transcription factors regulating the biosynthesis of terpenoids

Terpenoids are a large and structurally diverse class of secondary metabolites derived from IPP and DMAPP (Shi et al., 2021a). Thousands of terpenoids have been found in the natural world and can be divided into several sub-classes, including monoterpenoids, sesquiterpenoids, diterpenoids, triterpenoids, and some derivatives depending on the number of isoprenes (Yang et al., 2012). These terpenoids

have a wide range of biological activities and are often used as natural products in medicine, cosmetics, and food additives (Wang et al., 2021). They are known for their antimicrobial, antifungal, antiviral, and anticancer properties. The biosynthetic pathway of terpenoids mainly consists of three main steps: (i) The formation of precursors IPP and DMAPP; (ii) the generation of immediate precursors including farnesyl diphosphate (FPP), geranyl diphosphate (GPP), and geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP); and (iii) the formation and modification of terpenoids catalyzed by different kinds of enzymes. Here, we focus on the transcriptional regulatory network of several representatives, such as artemisinin, taxol, triptolide, tanshinones, and saponins (Table 1).

# Sesquiterpenoid—artemisinin

Artemisinin, China's treasure to the world, is highly effective in treating malaria extracted from the herb A. annua, and it is specifically synthesized and stored in glandular trichomes (GSTs) (Hassani et al., 2020). Unfortunately, the artemisinin content in A. annua is low, limiting the supply of artemisinin (Lu et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2017). Increasing the density of GSTs and elicitor treatment are effective strategies to enhance artemisinin biosynthesis, accompanied by exploring various TFs (Figure 3). A MYB TF AaMIXTA1 was screened from the GST transcriptome database; overexpression of AaMIXTA1 led to an increase in GST numbers and the artemisinin content in transgenic plants. A contrary result was observed in AaMIXTA1 repressed lines (Shi et al., 2018). JA-inducible AaHD1 positively controlled GST initiation, and overexpression of AaHD1 increased the density of GSTs and improved artemisinin content (Yan et al., 2017). In addition, two AaHD1-interacting TFs, AaMYB16 and AaMYB5, also regulated GST initiation, and AaMYB16 showed a positive effect, while AaMYB5 had the opposite effect (Xie et al., 2021). The HD-ZIP IV TF, AaHD8, directly regulated AaHD1 expression and interacted with AaMIXTA1 to form a regulatory complex for promoting GST initiation (Yan et al., 2018). Otherwise, MYB TFTLR1 and TLR2 negatively regulated trichome development (Lv et al., 2022). Apart from increasing GST density, JA and ABA treatments were effective strategies to increase the content of artemisinin (Jing et al., 2009; Xiang et al., 2015). For example, AaMYC2, the core of JA signaling, positively regulated artemisinin biosynthesis by activating cytochrome P450-dependent hydroxylase (CYP71AV1) and double bond reductase (DBR2) (Shen et al., 2016). Trichome-specific TF AaORA activated the expression of amorpha-4,11-diene synthase (ADS), CYP71AV1, DBR2, and aldehyde dehydrogenase 1 (ALDH1) to promote artemisinin biosynthesis (Lu et al., 2013). AaORA protein-protein interaction with AaTCP14 synergistically activated AaDBR2 and AaALDH1 to promote artemisinin biosynthesis, which AaJAZ8 negatively regulated (Ma et al., 2018). ABA enhanced artemisinin biosynthesis through AaCYP71AV1 and AaADS expression modulated by AabZIP1, which was phosphorylated by AaAPK1 (Zhang et al., 2015a, 2018a). # Diterpenoid-taxol

Taxol is an effective anticancer drug against ovarian, breast, and head cancers originally isolated from stem barks of Taxus trees, with increasing market demand (Yu et al., 2023). As a

diterpenoid, a series of enzymes catalyzed from the common precursor GGPP to taxol have been identified, including taxadiene synthase (TS), taxadien-5α-ol O-acetyltransferase (TAT), taxane 2a-O-benzoyl-transferase (TBT), 10-deacetylbaccatin III 10-O-acetyltransferase (DBAT), and $3^{\prime}$ -N-debenzoyl-2'-deoxytaxol-N-benzoyltransferase (DBTNBT) (Yu et al., 2023). Tissue-specific or phytohormone-responsive TFs have been investigated for regulating taxol biosynthesis, which accelerates the breeding of high taxol-yield plant species (Figure 4). For example, a phloem-specific R2R3-MYB TF TmMYB3 from Taxus media was involved in taxol biosynthesis by activating TS and TBT expression (Yu et al., 2020). MeJA effectively induced taxol biosynthetic genes, especially the TcTASY gene. Using the JRE element in the TcTASY promoter as bait, TcERF12, and TcERF15 were screened. TcERF12-overexpressing cells decreased TcTASY expression, resulting in lower content of taxol, while TcERF15 positively regulated TcTASY gene and taxol biosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2015b). Meanwhile, MeJA-induced TcMYC2a was identified as an activator of taxol biosynthesis via direct regulation of the TASY gene and the previously mentioned MeJA-responsive TF TcERF15 in Taxus chinensis

(Zhang et al., 2018b). Apart from the positive regulators, several MeJA-inducible negative regulators in Taxus cuspidata, were characterized, including TcJAMYC1, TcJAMYC2, and TcJAMYC4 (Lenka et al., 2015). ABA was another effective inducer in taxol biosynthesis. Overexpression of an ABA-responsive MYB TF TcMYB29a in T. chinensis cell suspension cultures increased taxol by activating taxadiene-5 $\alpha$ -hydroxylase (T5OH) expression (Cao et al., 2022). Also, SA enhanced taxol production. The SA-induced TcWRKY33 positively regulated taxol biosynthesis by activating TcDBAT and TcERF15 in T. chinensis (Chen et al., 2021). To expand understanding of taxol transcriptional regulation, a single-cell transcriptome of Taxus mairei was conducted, and several novel TFs were identified (Yu et al., 2023). The endodermal cell-specific MYB47 significantly activated T5OH expression to improve taxol biosynthesis, and xylem parenchyma cell-specific bHLH68 targeted TAT and 5- $\alpha$ -taxadienol-10- $\beta$ -hydroxylase (T10OH) to positively regulate taxol production. In contrast, another xylem parenchyma cell-located NAC2 was a negative regulator of taxol by repressing DBTNBT and DBAT expression (Yu et al., 2023). # Diterpenoid-triptolide

Triptolide is a valuable diterpenoid produced in the medicinal plant Tripterygium wilfordii (commonly named Lei Gong Teng), with various pharmacological activities such as anti-inflammatory or anticancer (Ma et al., 2007; Gao et al., 2021). Recent studies focused on the exploration of the triptolide biosynthesis pathway, and upstream and downstream pathway genes have been successfully characterized, including TwHMGR1, TwHMGR1, TwDXS2, TwGGPPS, TwCPS (copalyl diphosphate synthase), TwMS (miltiradiene synthase), and TwCYP728B70 (Tu et al., 2020). Transcription regulation of triptolide also attracted wide attention. Two bHLH TFs grouped into the lIle clade, TwMYC2a, and TwMYC2b, were isolated and characterized from T. wilfordii. Gene knock-down experiments by RNA interference (RNAi) of TwMYC2a or TwMYC2b enhanced triptolide production in transgenic hairy roots by upregulating the biosynthetic genes TwHMGR1, TwDXR, TwMS and TwCPS (Huo et al., 2021a). Alternatively, MeJA-elicited TwTGA1 increased triptolide accumulation by regulating the target genes TwTPS27a and TwTPS27b (Huo et al., 2021b).

# Diterpenoid-tanshinones

Tanshinones are well-known and investigated bioactive compounds in S. miltiorrhiza, mainly accumulated in roots, especially in the area of periderm (Kai et al., 2011a; Ma et al., 2021). Tanshinones have a broad cardiovascular and cerebrovascular protective effect (Kai et al., 2011a). Tanshinone biosynthetic pathway genes have been systematically characterized, including CPS1/2, KSL (kaurene synthase-like), and several CYPs (Gao et al., 2009; Guo et al., 2013, 2016; Ma et al., 2021). It is worth noting that SmCPS1, SmCPS2, SmKSL1, CYP76AH1, and CYP76AH3 are arranged in a biosynthetic gene cluster (Ma et al., 2021). It is speculated that TFs regulating pathway genes involved in gene clusters will be more efficient for genetic engineering. Elicitors such as MeJA, ABA, YE, and $\mathrm{Ag^{+}}$ effectively improved tanshinone content (Chen et al., 2001; Zhou et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2021b). Applying $100\mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$ MeJA in S. miltiorrhiza enhanced the expression level of pathway genes such as CPS1, KSL1, and CYP76AH1, leading to a higher content of tanshinones (Zhou et al., 2017). ABA application at $50\mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$ induced tanshinone accumulation in S. miltiorrhiza (Shi et al., 2021b). YE and $\mathrm{Ag^{+}}$ treated S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots produced more tanshinone than control lines (Ge and Wu, 2005). Transcription factors directly and indirectly regulating tanshinone biosynthesis have been studied (Figure 5). Due to high homology with reported WRKY genes, MeJA-responsive SmWRKY1 was selected and transgenic assay revealed that SmWRKY1 positively regulated tanshinone biosynthesis by activating SmDXR (Cao et al., 2018). Another WRKY TF SmWRKY2 up-regulated expression levels

of SmCPS1, leading to the boost of tanshinones (Deng et al., 2019). MeJA-inducible AP2/ERF TF SmERF1L1 played a positive role in tanshinone biosynthesis through directly regulating SmDXR (Huang et al., 2019), while SmERF115 negatively regulated tanshinone production (Sun et al., 2019). YE- $\mathrm{Ag^{+}}$ induced and root-hyperexpressed SmERF73 regulated SmDXR1 and three clustered genes SmCPS1, SmKSL1, and SmCYP76AH3 via the GCC-box promoter motif (Zheng et al., 2021). SmERF6 enhanced tanshinone accumulation by promoting the expression of SmCPS1 and SmKSL1 (Bai et al., 2018), and SmERF128 acted as a positive regulator by regulating clustered genes SmCPS1, SmKSL1, and SmCYP76AH1 to increase tanshinone content (Zhang et al., 2019). SmMYB9b and SmMYB98 also promoted tanshinone biosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2017b; Hao et al., 2020). Overexpression of ABA-inducible SmbZIP3 significantly enhanced tanshionone content in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots by activating SmERF128 and SmMYB9b, while introduction of SmWRKY34 decreased tanshinone by downregulating expression of SmbZIP3 and SmGGPPS (Shi et al., 2022a).

# Triterpenoid-ginsenosides

Medicinal plants of the Panax genus such as $P_{\cdot}$ ginseng, $P_{\cdot}$ notoginseng, $P_{\cdot}$ quinquefolium and $P_{\cdot}$ japonicus produce a bioactive specialized group of triterpenoid saponins also called ginsenosides, with high medicinal and commercial value (Wu et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2016; Jiang et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023). Ginsenosides include two main types, oleane-type pentacyclic triterpenoid saponins and dammarane-type tetracyclic triterpenoid saponins, in which the biosynthetic pathway consists of more than 20 enzymatic steps (Jiang et al., 2021). Several groups of TF families have been shown to take part in regulating ginsenosides production. Overexpression of $PgGRAS68-01$ in the hairy roots of $P_{\cdot}$ ginseng inhibited ginsenoside content (Liu et al., 2023a). Overexpression of Chaetomium globosum-induced $PgWRKY4X$ led to a significant increase in ginsenosides by activating squalene epoxidase ( $PgSE$ ) (Yao et al., 2020). Further, transgenic Arabidopsis showed improved salt and drought tolerance (Sun et al., 2013). # Transcriptional regulation of alkaloid biosynthesis

Alkaloids are a diverse group of naturally occurring compounds found in various medicinal plants, which are known for their pharmacological properties and have been used for centuries (Yamada and Sato, 2021). Alkaloids contain terpenoid indole alkaloids (TIAs), benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIAs), tropane alkaloids (TAs). Various TFs involved in alkaloid biosynthesis have been investigated in different plant species. Here, advances in transcription regulation of camptothecin (TIA) in Camptotheca acuminata and O. pumila

(Table 2), vinblastine and vincristine (TIA) in Catharanthus roseus, berberine (BIA) in Coptis japonica, hyoscyamine and scopolamine (TA) in Anisodus acutangulus and Atropa belladonna are described.

# Terpenoid indole alkaloid—camptothecin

Commercially available CPTs, such as topotecan and irinotecan, are commonly applied to treat various cancers (Zhao et al., 2017; Hevener et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2023). # Terpenoid indole alkaloids in C. roseus

Catharanthus roseus, a plant of the Apocynaceae family, produces a range of TIAs with wide pharmaceutical applications, including monoindole alkaloids and bisindole alkaloids (Memelink and Gantet, 2007; Liu et al., 2016). Bisindole alkaloids vinblastine and vincristine are well-known compounds with anti-tumor activities. The biosynthetic pathway of TIAs contains tens of enzymatic steps involving two major pathways, shikimate and MEP, which could be regulated by various TFs classified into different types. CrERF5, an AP2/ERF TF clustered with strictosidine $\beta$ -glucosidase (SGD), ORCA3, and desacetoxyvindoline 4-hydroxylase (D4H), was found to regulate the MIA pathway, especially the biosynthesis of bisindole alkaloids by activating TDC (Pan et al., 2019). Using the JERE element in the STR promoter as bait, ORCA2 was successfully screened by a Y1H assay (Menke et al., 1999). Overexpression of ORCA2 in C. roseus hairy roots up-regulated TIA and TIA-related genes, including anthranilate synthase $\alpha$ subunit (Asα), TDC, G10H, LAMT, STR, tabersonine 16-hydroxylase (T16H), D4H, and increased the content of ajmalicine, serpentine, catharanthine (Li et al., 2013). Overexpression of MeJA-induced CrORCA3 in C. roseus cells mediated by Agrobacterium transformation not only increased genes ASa and DXS involved in TIA precursor synthesis but also induced TIA biosynthetic

genes such as TDC, STR, CPR, and D4H, leading to an increase in TIAs (van der Fits and Memelink, 2000). CrORCA3 formed a cluster with CrORCA4 and CrORCA5 (Paul et al., 2017). CrMYC2 activated the expression of CrORCA3 in C. roseus cell line by binding to the JRE sequence in the promoter (Zhang et al., 2011). Further, the co-expression of CrMYC2-CrORCA3 demonstrated a synergistic effect on TDC and CPR (Paul et al., 2017). Inconsistent with the result in cells, CrMYC2-overexpressing hairy roots produced decreased TIAs, including catharanthine, tabersonine, and ajmalicine with a lower expression level of ORCA3. It was demonstrated to be mediated by the competitive interaction of CrMYC2 and CrGBF1 on binding to the T/G-box on TDC and ORCA3 promoter (Sui et al., 2018). CrWRKY1 positively regulated serpentine biosynthesis by activating TDC gene (binding to the W-box element in the promoter), while repressing expression of several TIA-biosynthesis activators such as ORCA2, ORCA3, and CrMYC2 (Suttipanta et al., 2011). Overexpression of BIS1 (iridoid synthesis 1, a bHLH TF) in cell line MP183L boosted production of MIAs including strictosidine, ajmalicine, serpentine, and tabersonine by transactivation of MEP genes and the iridoid genes, which was complementary to ORCA3 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). BIS2, a homolog of BIS1, co-expression with iridoid genes, positively modulated MIA biosynthesis in C. roseus suspension cells (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016). Transient overexpression of CrGATA1 (a light-induced GATA TF) in C. roseus seedlings resulted in higher expression levels of D4H by binding to the GATC-motifs and enhanced vindoline accumulation (Liu et al., 2019b). All these TFs might be an effective tool for metabolic engineering to accumulate appreciable MIAs in C. roseus plants, or hairy roots and cell cultures. For example, combing overexpression of CrORCA3 and TIA-pathway gene SGD significantly promoted the total amount of TIAs in hairy roots of C. roseus (Sun and Peebles, 2016). Combinatorial overexpression of CrMYC2 $^{D126N}$ , BIS1 and ORCA3 showed a tremendous promotion of TIA production such as 16-hydroxytabersonine and horhammericine (Schweizer et al., 2018).

Benzylisoquinoline alkaloids—berberine, sanguinarine  
Benzylisoquinoline alkaloids are a diverse group of natural compounds with a broad range of bioactive properties, such as antimicrobial, analgesic, and sedative activities (Yamada and Sato, 2021). These alkaloids are widely distributed in various medicinal herbs, such as berberine in Coptis japonica and sanguinarine in Eschoscholtzia california. Here, TFs regulating berberine and sanguinarine biosynthesis are introduced. Overexpression of CjWRKY1 in C. japonica protoplasts enhanced expression of berberine biosynthetic genes, while suppression of CjWRKY1 using RNAi resulted in marked reduction of tyrosine decarboxylase (TYDC), (S)-norcoclaurine synthase (NCS), (S)-norcoclaurine 6-O-methyltransferase (6OMT), (S)-coclaurine-N-methyltransferase (CNMT), (S)-N-methylcoclaurine 3'-hydroxylase (CYP80B2), 3'-hydroxy-N-methylcoclaurine-4'-O-methyltransferase (4'OMT), berberine bridge enzyme (BBE) and canadine synthase (CYP719A1),

indicating that CjWRKY1 acted as a positive regulator in berberine biosynthesis (Kato et al., 2007). Subsequently, using 6OMT as the target, CjbHLH1 was screened from candidate TFs. RNAi of CjbHLH1 in C. japonica protoplasts decreased the transcript level of the above genes like CjWRKY1 (Yamada et al., 2011). Two homologous bHLH genes were isolated from E. californica, namely EcbHLH1-1 and EcbHLH1-2. Suppression of EcbHLH1-1 or EcbHLH1-2 in stable transgenic cells of E. californica by RNAi reduced the expression of biosynthetic genes, with a decrease accumulation in sanguinarine (Yamada et al., 2015).

# Tropane alkaloids—hyoscyamine, scopolamine

Tropane alkaloids, including hyoscyamine and scopolamine, are extensively used to treat diseases related to parasympathetic nervous systems (Kai et al., 2011b, 2012; Zhang et al., 2022a). Common TA-producing medicinal plants are from Solanaceae species such as A. acutangulus and A. belladonna (Kai et al., 2011b, 2012; Qiu et al., 2020). A. acutangulus has been used for centuries in Yunnan province to treat anesthetic agents, which produced a higher content of TAs than other TA-producing plants (Kai et al., 2012). Tropane alkaloids are mainly biosynthesized in the roots of A. acutangulus and A. belladonna plants. Next-generation sequencing of roots from A. acutangulus indicated that several TF families were potentially involved in TA regulation, including MYB, WRKY, AP2/ERF, and bHLH (Cui et al., 2015). AaWRKY11 activated hyoscyamine $6\beta$ -hydroxylase (H6H1) expression to promote TA biosynthesis in A. acutangulus (Zhou et al., 2024). An auxin-induced LBD TF AbLBD1 inhibited hyoscyamine and scopolamine biosynthesis by regulating the expression level of putrescine N-methyltransferase (PMT), type III polyketide synthase (PYKS), tropinone synthase (AbCYP82M3), and AbH6H, while promoting lateral and adventitious roots initiation (Zeng et al., 2020).

# Transcription factors as regulators of phenolic compounds metabolism

Phenylpropanoids are a highly diverse group of secondary metabolites derived from the amino acid phenylalanine and, to a certain extent, from tyrosine (Yang et al., 2012). They are synthesized through the phenylpropanoid pathway, which involves a series of enzymatic reactions. Phenylpropanoids have a wide range of biological activities, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties. # Phenolic acids in S. miltiorrhiza

Like tanshinones, phenolic acids are another group of bioactive compounds in S. miltiorrhiza exhibiting wide biological activities, which arouse great interest in medicinal plant research. Representative components of phenolic acids contain salvianolic acid B and rosmarinic acid (Di et al., 2013). Generally, phenolic acids are biosynthesized by two

pathways: tyrosine-derived and phenylpropanoid branches (Di et al., 2013). Committed steps catalyzed by PAL (phenylalanine ammonia-lyase), TAT (tyrosine aminotransferase), HPPR (hydroxyphenylpyruvate reductase), C4H (cinnamic acid 4-hydroxylase), 4CL (4-coumarate coenzyme A ligase), RAS (rosmarinic acid synthase), and CYP98A14 involved in phenolic acid biosynthesis have been identified (Xiao et al., 2011; Di et al., 2013). Also here, inducer treatment is effective in promoting phenolic acid biosynthesis, and the transcription regulation mechanism has been intensely investigated under the treatments of MeJA, ABA, and SA (Dong et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2021b). As a core TF in the JA signaling pathway, MYC2 plays a crucial role in secondary metabolism. Overexpression of SmMYC2 significantly promoted phenolic acid biosynthesis by activating SmTAT1, SmPAL1, and SmCYP98A14 (Yang et al., 2017; Du et al., 2018). Overexpressing of SmbHLH37 led to reduced production of phenolic acids by inhibition of SmTAT1 and SmPAL1, suggesting that SmbHLH37 antagonized with SmMYC2 in phenolic acid biosynthesis (Du et al., 2018). SmbHLH37-interacting partner SmbHLH53 repressed the expression of SmTAT1 (Peng et al., 2020). Two MeJA-inducible R2R3 MYB TFs, SmMYB1 and SmMYB2, acted as positive regulators in phenolic acid biosynthesis by upregulating the expression of SmCYP98A14 (Deng et al., 2020a; Zhou et al., 2021). Additionally, SmMYB1 interacted with SmMYC2 to further activate SmCYP98A14 (Zhou et al., 2021). SmMYB97, an interacting protein with SmJAZ8, positively regulated phenolic acid biosynthesis by promoting SmPAL1 and SmTAT1 expression (Li et al., 2020). Transgenic S. miltiorrhiza plants overexpressing SmMYB52 accumulated higher content of phenolic acids, with SmTAT1, Sm4CL9, SmC4H1, and SmHPPR1 as direct targets (Yang et al., 2021). Notably, SmMYB52 inhibited root growth (Yang et al., 2021). SmTTG1 contributed to phenolic acid biosynthesis in S. miltiorrhiza. Using SmTTG1 as bait, SmMYB111 was screened from the complementary DNA (cDNA) library of S. miltiorrhiza. Overexpression of SmMYB111 significantly increased the accumulation of phenolic acids, while silencing SmMYB111 resulted in the opposite effect (Li et al., 2018). Another activator in phenolic acid biosynthesis, an AP2/ERF member SmERF115, directly regulated expression of SmRAS1 by binding to the GCC-box, also resulting in upregulation of PAL3, 4CL5, TAT3, and RAS4 in an as yet unknown manner (Sun et al., 2019).

Shi et al. (2021b) revealed that ABA with a concentration of $50\mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$ induced a pronounced increase in phenolic acids in S. miltiorrhiza hairy roots. Overexpression of ABA-responsive SmbZIP1 in S. miltiorrhiza produced a higher content of phenolic acids, while knock-out lines using CRIPSR-Cas9 led to the opposite effect. The biochemical results indicated that SmbZIP1 could bind to the G-Box-like1 motif located in the promoter region of C4H1 (Deng et al., 2020b). # TRANSCRIPTION FACTORS MODULATING STRESS RESPONSE IN MEDICINAL PLANTS

Medicinal plants are cultivated in diverse environmental conditions, ranging from temperature extremes to nutrient deficiencies, affecting plant growth, survival and/or productivity (Jiang et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2019). TFs regulate secondary metabolism and act as key players in orchestrating transcriptional responses to stress. Several families of TFs have been identified as important regulators of stress responses. For example, overexpression of AaNAC1 in A. annua plants enhanced tolerance to Botrytis cinerea infection and drought stress (Lv et al., 2016). The yield and quality of P. notoginseng were significantly affected by root rot caused by pathogenic fungi such as Fusarium solani, and MeJA treatment significantly induced the resistance to F. solani, leading to the identification of a candidate MeJA-induced WRKY TF, PnWRKY9. Overexpression of PnWRKY9 increased the tolerance to F. solani in tobacco, while its suppression using RNAi in P. notoginseng leaves increased susceptibility to F. solani. Biochemical experiments proved that PnWRKY9 directly trans-activated PnDEFL1, an F. solani resistance-related gene (Zheng et al., 2022). Likewise, P. notoginseng leaves with PnWRKY15 suppression mediated by RNAi were more sensitive to F. solani accompanying with deuce of an osmotin-like protein PnOLP1, while PnWRKY15-overexpressed tobacco evidently were more resistant to F. solani (Su et al., 2023). Transient overexpression of ThERF46 in Tetrastigma hemsleyanum protected the plants from cell membrane damage, enhancing more tolerance to cold stress (Xie et al., 2022). In Isatis indigotica hairy roots, IiWRKY34 expression enhanced tolerance to salt and drought stress (Xiao et al., 2020). Heterologous expression of DcWRKY5 isolated from Dioscorea composita increased salt and drought tolerance in Arabidopsis by activating the superoxide dismutase (AtSOD1) and AtABF2 (Yu et al., 2023), and DcWRKY3 improved salt tolerance in Arabidopsis (Yu et al., 2022b). Ectopic expression of Dendrobium officinale WRKY69 in Arabidopsis increased the rate of seed germination under salt tress (Zhang et al., 2022c). PsAP2, a wound-induced

AP2/ERF from $P_{\cdot}$ somniferum, increased tolerance to dehydration stress in transgenic tobacco plants (Mishra et al., 2015). Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF)-treated $P_{\cdot}$ quinquefolius produced higher content of ginsenoside, and several families including NAC, bHLH and WRKY were identified after AMF treatment (Ran et al., 2023). In addition, TF families responsive to diverse stress have been identified, such as YABBY family in Platycodon grandiflorus under salt stress and drought induction (Kong et al., 2023), HD-ZIP gene family in $D.$ officinale under cold stress (Yang et al., 2022), AP2/ERF TF family in $P_{\cdot}$ ginseng facing cold stress (Chen et al., 2020). Further research into the regulatory mechanisms governing TF activity and crosstalk will enhance our understanding of how medicinal plants respond and adapt to stressful environments.

# CONCLUSION AND PROSPECTS

High-value desired secondary compounds produced in medicinal plants benefit human health while in low concentration. Numerous TFs classified into multiple families have been shown to regulate the biosynthesis of certain products via modulating biosynthetic pathway genes. Notably, how TFs are screened in medicinal plants has greatly changed. Initially, in high homology TFs with known functions were the main means to screen candidate TFs. Genomics, transcriptomics, and single-cell RNA sequencing recently provided novel insights, facilitating the identification of new TFs candidates regulating secondary metabolism in medicinal plants. Progress in transcriptional regulation has achieved remarkable progress in several medicinal plants owing to stable genetic transformation systems and omics data availability, such as in A. annua and S. miltiorrhiza. Currently, most studies have focused on the effect of individual hormones or environmental factors on the biosynthesis of active compounds. For example, transcription regulation in S. miltiorrhiza concentrated on the JA or ABA regulating tanshinone and phenolic acid biosynthesis. Studies on how JA-ABA or other signaling regulates secondary metabolism in S. miltiorrhiza are still limited. More attention should be focused on crosstalk between hormones or environmental factors in medicinal plants to understand the relationship between environment and product accumulation. Further, it is challenging that the biosynthetic pathways of most natural products in medicinal plants remain unclear, which limits the exploration of regulatory networks. For example, the specific enzymes catalyzing rosmarinic acid to salvianolic acid B in S. miltiorrhiza remains unknown, while the entire pathway of tanshinone biosynthesis needs to be further studied. In addition, synthetic biology provides an alternative to produce desired bioactive compounds in microbes such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Escherichia coli or other plant species. Using the approach of synthetic biology, only precursors of such bioactive compounds are obtained. For example, engineering Coleus forskohlii TPS1 and SmKSL1 from S. miltiorrhiza enables yeast to produce high-level production

of miltiradiene. Therefore, more emphasis should be placed on understanding complete biochemical pathways of specific metabolites.

In summary, we discuss different strategies to identify candidate TFs regulating biosynthesis of valuable compounds distributed in terpenes, alkaloids, and phenylpropanoids in different medicinal plants, and describe recent advances in transcriptional regulatory networks focusing on several representative bioactive compounds, which will help in understanding the regulatory mechanism of secondary metabolites in medicinal plants, as well as potential in breeding for high-quality medicinal plant resources.
