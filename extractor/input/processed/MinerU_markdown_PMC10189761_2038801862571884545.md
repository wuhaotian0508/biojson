# Abstract

The fungal disease Glomerella leaf spot (GLS) seriously impacts apple production. As a nonprotein amino acid, $\gamma$ -aminobutyric acid (GABA) is widely involved in biotic and abiotic stresses. However, it is not clear whether GABA is involved in a plant's response to GLS, nor is its molecular mechanism understood. Here, we found that exogenous GABA could significantly alleviate GLS, reduce lesion lengths, and increase antioxidant capacity. MdGAD1 was identified as a possible key gene for GABA synthesis in apple. Further analysis indicated that MdGAD1 promoted antioxidant capacity to improve apple GLS resistance in transgenic apple calli and leaves. Yeast one-hybrid analysis identified the transcription factor MdWRKY33 upstream of MdGAD1. Electrophoretic mobility shift assay, $\beta$ -glucuronidase activity, and luciferase activity further supported that MdWRKY33 bound directly to the promoter of MdGAD1. The content of GABA and the transcription level of MdGAD1 in the MdWRKY33 transgenic calli were higher than that of the wild type. When MdWRKY33 transgenic calli and leaves were inoculated with GLS, MdWKRY33 positively regulated resistance to GLS. These results explained the positive regulatory effects of GABA on apple GLS and provided insight into the metabolic regulatory network of GABA.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Glomerella leaf spot (GLS), a fungal disease caused by Colletotrichum fructicola, seriously threatens apple production, causing severe early defoliation, reducing yield, and affecting flower bud formation in the second year. GLS was first discovered in the United States in the 1970s (Taylor, 1971). In China, GLS has occurred in different apple-producing areas since it was found in 2011, and is increasingly becoming one of the most important diseases threating the apple industry in China (Zhang et al., 2019). Once leaves have been infected 

by GLS, spots appear in 2 days and expand quickly, followed by yellowing and dropping of apple leaves (Zhang et al., 2019). Due to the short onset of GLS and the serious effects on tree vigour, fruit yield, and fruit quality, it is of the utmost importance to prevent GLS infection and improve the resistance of plants to GLS. Currently, GLS is prevented by spraying chemical fungicide (Shan et al., 2021), but chemical treatment leads to issues such as food safety, pollution, and increased costs for orchard management. It is therefore particularly critical to explore more effective ways to improve plant resistance to GLS and reveal the mechanism of resistance of apple to GLS.

Previous studies have indicated that an effective way to enhance the disease resistance of plants is to influence plant metabolism and reactive oxygen species (ROS), and activate hormones and signalling pathways, which results in a series of reactions to pathogen infection. For example, ROS such as hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide anion $( \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ are important regulators of the plant's defence against pathogens (Levine et al., 1994). The ROS scavenging system can regulate and maintain ROS homeostasis (Torres et al., 2006). Overexpression of MdIAA24 enhances resistance to apple GLS by enhancing the activity of antioxidant enzymes (Wang, Huang, et al., 2022). Secondary metabolites, such as amino acids and phenolic compounds, are directly or indirectly involved in plant defence responses to pathogens. Moreover, some transcription factors also affect pathogen infection by promoting metabolite synthesis. For example, the group ΙΙc WRKY-MAPK pathway promotes flavonoid biosynthesis to improve the resistance of cotton to Fusarium oxysporum (Wang, Guo, et al., 2022). AtERF72 positively regulates resistance to Botrytis cinerea by activating camalexin biosynthesis (Li, Liu, et al., 2022). 

$\gamma$ -Aminobutyric acid (GABA) is a four-carbon nonprotein amino acid that was first found in potato tubers (Steward et al., 1949). GABA metabolism, also known as a GABA shunt, is a process in which l-glutamate produces GABA through glutamate decarboxylase (GAD) and then GABA is converted into succinic acid under succinic semialdehyde dehydrogenase to shuttle into the tricarboxylic acid cycle (Bouché & Fromm, 2004). As the main enzyme in GABA synthesis, GAD is conserved in plants. When plants are subjected to biotic and abiotic stresses, the expression of GAD is induced to increase GABA content to enhance the tolerance of plants to stress (Bouché et al., 2004). Studies have verified that GABA plays a key role in plant resistance to biotic and abiotic stresses (Ramesh et al., 2017; Tarkowski et al., 2020). Xu et al. (2021) demonstrated that GABA improved the drought resistance of Arabidopsis thaliana by affecting stomata. Exogenous GABA could reduce the accumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and enhance the ascorbic acid–glutathione cycle to improve the tolerance of muskmelon seedlings to saline–alkali stress (Jin et al., 2019). Our previous study found that exogenous GABA improved apple resistance to Marssonina apple blotch (Liu et al., 2022). However, the responses of apple to GLS after treatment with GABA are not clear, and the mechanism by which GABA impacts the resistance of apple to disease has not yet been reported. Further studies are therefore essential to understand the function of GABA in biotic stress. In addition, most studies on GADs are on model plants (Miyashita & Good, 2008; Wang et al., 2019). Little is known about the role of GADs in perennial woody plants, such as apples, especially the molecular mechanism of GABA in relation to stress response. 

In this study, we found that exogenous GABA could significantly alleviate apple GLS. The key GABA synthesis gene, MdGAD1, was identified in apple, and it was found that the MdWRKY33-MdGAD1 module positively regulated apple GLS. These findings clarified the molecular mechanism of GABA synthesis and laid a theoretical foundation for further study of the possible role of GABA in preventing 

apple GLS. Insight was also obtained into the use of GABA in the artificial selection of fruit trees. 

# 2  |  RESULTS

# 2.1  |  Exogenous GABA alleviates damage to apple caused by C. fructicola

After inoculating apple leaves with C. fructicola, we found that the content of GABA increased significantly (Figure S1). For further study of the effects of GABA on apple GLS, the leaves of 2-month-old cv. Royal Gala GL-3 variant seedlings were inoculated with C. fructicola, and the phenotype is shown in Figure 1a. The leaves treated with exogenous GABA showed smaller lesion length and higher endogenous GABA content (Figure 1b,c). Under normal conditions, there was no difference in phenotype between the control (CK) and GABA (G) treatments, but the content of endogenous GABA in the G treatment was significantly higher than that in the CK. We further evaluated the antioxidant capacity in apple leaves (Figure 1d,e). The staining and content of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $\bar { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 } ^ { - . }$ confirmed that C. fructicola inoculation (GLS treatment) could induce apple leaves to produce a large amount of ROS. However, exogenous GABA could significantly reduce the content of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $\bar { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 } \bar { \cdot }$ in inoculated seedlings. In addition, the activity of superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and peroxidase (POD) was evaluated. Compared with the CK group, SOD activity increased by 0.4 times in inoculated seedlings. SOD activity in the $_ { \mathsf { G L S + G } }$ treatment was 1.1 times higher than that of the GLS treatment. This trend was similar to the detected CAT and POD activity. These results showed that exogenous GABA could enhance the antioxidant capacity of apple leaves after inoculation with C. fructicola. 

# 2.2  |  Identification of the key synthetase for GABA synthesis in apple

Phylogenetic analyses showed that MdGAD1 was homologous with SlGAD1 (Figure S2). The expression of MdGAD1 in apple roots, stems, leaves, and fruits was higher than the expression of MdGAD2 and MdGAD3 (Figure S3a). The expression of MdGAD1 and the content of GABA in 28 apple germplasm accessions had the greatest correlation, and the Pearson's correlation was 0.808 (Figure S4). The enzyme activity catalysis test also confirmed that MdGAD1 could catalyse l-Glu to produce GABA in vitro (Figures 2a and S3b). Furthermore, the GABA content of calli overexpressing MdGAD1 was observed to be significantly higher than that of the wild type (WT) (Figure 2b,c). Subcellular localization showed that the MdGAD1-GFP signal was in the cytoplasm after being induced by 1 M sorbitol (Figure 2d), while AtCBL1-GFP signal was found in the outer membrane ring under plasmolysis conditions (Figure S5). These results showed that MdGAD1 may play an important role in apples.

# 2.4  |  MdWRKY33 binds to the MdGAD1 promoter and activates its transcription

To reveal the upstream regulatory factors of MdGAD1, a cDNA library was screened by yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay using the P1 fragment of MdGAD1 (−859 to −1637) as bait, and the transcription factor MdWRKY33, known to be related to disease resistance, was identified as an interactor (Figure 4a,b). 

To further verify the binding of MdWRKY33 to the MdGAD1 promoter, we examined the expression of MdWRKY33-activated MdGAD1 using the $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) reporter gene (Figure 4c). GUS staining and relative GUS activity of apple calli cells with pMdGAD1::GUS + 35S::MdWRKY33 were greater than that of pMdGAD1::GUS. The results of the luciferase (LUC) assay showed that the luminescence signal of 35S::MdWRKY33 $^ +$ pMdGAD1 increased significantly in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves compared with the combinations of $6 2 { - } 5 { \sf K } + 0 8 0 0 $ -LUC, 35S::MdWRKY33 + 0800-LUC, or 62-SK + pMdGAD1 (Figure 4d,e). The activity of LUC in 35S::MdWRKY33 + pMdGAD1 was also significantly higher than 

that in 62-SK + pMdGAD1. These results indicate that MdWRKY33 directly bound to the MdGAD1 promoter in vivo. 

Furthermore, electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) showed that MdWRKY33 could recognize and bind the W-box (TTGACC) sequence of the promoter of the target gene MdGAD1 (Figure 4f), whereas MdWRKY33 was unable to bind when this sequence was mutated, which indicated that MdWRKY33 directly bound to the MdGAD1 promoter in vitro.

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

The signalling molecule GABA plays an important role in plant biotic and abiotic stresses by hormone regulation, organic acid metabolism, ion absorption and transport, photosynthesis system, antioxidant system, and phenolic metabolism (Li, Liu, Sun, et al., 2020; Ramesh et al., 2015, 2017; Xu et al., 2021). For example, the application of exogenous GABA can improve the drought resistance of apple seedlings through the abscisic acid signalling pathway (Liu et al., 2021). The GABA-regulated glutathione pathway and antioxidant enzymes are involved in chlorothalonil-induced 

pesticide detoxification in plants (Shan et al., 2022). Exogenous GABA reduces ethylene synthesis in the roots of Caragana intermedia to adapt to salt stress (Shi et al., 2010). Our previous studies found that exogenous GABA in apple improved the resistance of apple to drought, alkaline and cadmium stress as well as Marssonina apple blotch (Li, Liu, Peng, et al., 2020; Li, Li, et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021, 2022). Herein, we found that the content of GABA increased significantly after inoculation with C. fructicola (Figure S1), and we observed the effects of exogenous GABA application on reducing apple GLS. Treated apple plants had milder symptoms with smaller spots and stronger antioxidant capacity after inoculation with C. fructicola compared to those without GABA treatment (Figure 1). Furthermore, the calli and leaves overexpressing MdGAD1, which accumulated more GABA, showed higher resistance to C. fructicola infection (Figure 3). These results indicate that the increase in GABA content contributes to the resistance of apple to GLS. 

As the main enzyme for GABA synthesis in plants, different GADs may play various roles in different tissues. In apple, three GAD genes were identified (Trobacher et al., 2013). In this study, we identified MdGAD1 as the key gene related to GABA synthesis based on the analysis of 28 distinct apple germplasms and various apple tissues (Figures S2–S4). # 4  |  EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# 4.1  |  Plant materials and experimental design

In early June 2019, the leaves of different germplasm resources (Table S1) were sampled from the Horticultural Experimental Station and the campus of Northwest A&F University, Yangling $( 3 4 ^ { \circ } 2 0 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N }$ , $1 0 8 ^ { \circ } 2 4 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { E }$ ), China. There were three independent biological repeats for each sample, which were collected from three different trees. C. fructicola-induced expression analysis of MdGAD1 and MdWRKY33 was performed on C. fructicola-resistant cultivar Fuji. 

The materials for tissue expression analysis and inoculation with C. fructicola were GL-3 (a variant of cv. Royal Gala) (Dai et al., 2013). The tissue culture method of GL-3 followed that described by Guo et al. (2019). After rooting, seedlings were transplanted into soil/ perlite (5:1, vol:vol) and grown for 30 days, with ${ \boldsymbol { 1 } } / { \boldsymbol { 2 } } \times$ Hoagland's nutrient solution (Hoagland & Arnon, 1950) used to irrigate every 3 days. Twenty seedlings of the same size were randomly divided into the following four treatment groups: CK (control), G $( 0 . 5 \mathsf { m M }$ GABA under normal condition), GLS (inoculation with C. fructicola), and $\mathsf { G L S } + \mathsf { G }$ $0 . 5 \mathsf { m M }$ GABA and inoculation with C. fructicola). The G and $_ { \mathsf { G L S } + \mathsf { G } }$ groups were irrigated with $0 . 5 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ GABA, meanwhile the CK and GLS treatments were irrigated with water every 3 days. 

After three irrigation events, two to five leaves of the same size from the top of the plant were inoculated with C. fructicola in vitro. 

# 4.2  |  Inoculation with C. fructicola

After culturing on potato glucose agar at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 week, fungal spores were transferred to a liquid medium $( 5 0 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ sugar, $\boldsymbol { 1 0 } ~ \mathsf { g / L }$ ${ \mathsf { K N O } } _ { 3 }$ , $5 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L } \mathsf { K H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { P O } _ { 4 }$ , $2 . 5 \ : \mathrm { g / L \ : M g S O } _ { 4 } . 7 \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { \mathrm { i } }$ , and $0 . 0 2 { \mathrm g } / \mathrm { L } \mathsf { F e } _ { 2 } ( \mathsf { S O } _ { 4 } ) _ { 3 } )$ and cultured at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 140 rpm for 3–5 days, and then centrifuged to collect spores. Spores were then suspended in deionized water with a concentration of $1 0 ^ { 5 } { \mathrm { c f u / m L } }$ . 

WT and transgenic apple calli were cultured in a medium $( 4 . 4 3 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ Murashige & Skoog basal medium, $3 0 \mathrm { g / L }$ sucrose, $1 . 5 \mathrm { m g } / \mathrm { L } 2 \mathrm { - } 4 \mathrm { D }$ , and $0 . 4 \ : \mathrm { m g / L }$ 6-benzylaminopurine) for 10 days, and then inoculated with a 1-cm diameter disk of C. fructicola from potato medium. The phenotype was observed after 7 days of incubation at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in darkness. 

The transient transformation of apple leaves was inoculated with C. fructicola. Three days later, each leaf was inoculated with 10 $\mu \iota$ of spore suspension on the left and right sides; leaves were then incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in the dark. Apple leaves treated with exogenous GABA were inoculated with a $4 0 \mathrm { - \mu L }$ suspension of C. fructicola on the left and right sides and incubated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. 

# 4.3  |  GAD enzyme activity assay

C5X-G1 forward (F) and reverse (R) primers (Table S2) were used to clone the MdGAD1 gene by PCR. The cloned gene was inserted into the pMAL- $\cdot \mathsf { c } 5 \times$ vector with a maltose-binding protein (MBP) tag to generate an MdGAD1-MBP fusion vector. Positive clones were transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) and inoculated into $2 0 0 \mathsf { m L }$ of Luria-Bertani medium with $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ampicillin. The positive clones were cultured at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a shaker to ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ 0.6, and then isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside was added to a final concentration of $0 . 8 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ . Next, the cultures were shaken at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and 80 rpm for $^ { 3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , following which they were centrifuged at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ at $2 3 0 0 \times g$ for 5 min. Cultures were then resuspended in $1 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of phosphate-buffered saline, and then $1 \ \mathrm { m g / L }$ (final concentration) of lysozyme was added. The cells were crushed using an ultrasonic cell grinder for 10 min (interval 10 s). The supernatant was obtained after centrifugation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $1 3 { , } 5 0 0 \times g$ for 5 min. The protein was purified on an MBP-binding resin column and finally detected using SDS-PAGE. 

The enzyme activity of GAD was assayed according to Mei et al. (2020) and Trobacher et al. (2013) with some modifications. The reaction mixture was made up to a total volume of $5 0 0 \mu \ L$ containing sodium citrate-dihydrogen phosphate buffer $\left( \mathsf { p H } \ 5 . 6 \right)$ , $0 . 2 5 \mathsf { m M }$ pyridoxal-5-phosphate, $1 0 ~ \mu \vert$ of MdGAD1 protein sample, $5 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { \Omega } \llangle \mathsf { - \Omega }$ glutamate, $1 \mathsf { m M C a C l } _ { 2 }$ , and $\mathsf { 1 m M }$ calmodulin. The reaction was maintained at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min, and then terminated with 1 M hydrochloric acid (1/20 volume). The samples were filtered with a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter and examined by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS). 

# 4.4  |  NBTStaining of leaves for ROS

The leaves were stained for hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide anion $( { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 } ^ { - . . } )$ by the $^ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine-HCl and nitrotetrazolium blue chloride method (Rangani et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2018). 

# 4.5  |  Determination of ROS content and antioxidant enzymes

${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , $\bar { \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ , and antioxidant enzymes, including SOD, CAT, and POD, were extracted using a Comin Biotechnology kit following the manufacturer's instructions (Suzhou). Briefly, $_ { 0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { g } }$ of fresh leaves was harvested for each biological repetition. Extraction and colour reactions of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 } , { \sf O } _ { 2 } \bar { . }$ ., SOD, CAT, and POD were conducted with the specific reagents in the kits (Li, Liu, Peng, et al., 2020). The absorbance was measured with a UV2600 spectrophotometer (Shimadzu). 

# 4.6  |  Measurement of GABA content

The methods described in Jin et al. (2019) and Li, Liu, Peng, et al. (2020) were followed for the extraction and determination of GABA 

with some modifications. Briefly, $_ { 0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of fresh sample was homogenized with $1 \ \mathrm { m L }$ of $50 \%$ ethanol solution (containing 0.1 M HCl), then centrifuged for $1 5 \mathsf { m i n }$ at $1 3 , 5 0 0 \times g$ , filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter, and finally diluted 10 fold with methanol (Sigma-Aldrich) for detection by LC–MS (LC: AC, ExionLC; MS: Q-trap5500, AB Sciex Pret. Ltd). Separation used an Inertsil ODS-4 column $3 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ particle size, $3 \times 1 5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ ; GL Sciences Inc.). The mobile phases A and B were $0 . 1 \%$ methanoic acid and acetonitrile, respectively. The GABA standard (A2192) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. The GABA retention time was $1 . 2 9 \mathrm { m i n }$ . 

# 4.7  |  RNA extraction and quantification, and gene expression analysis

The total RNA from different tissues was extracted using an RNA Prep Pure Plant kit (Tiangen Biotech Co., Ltd). The RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA and quantitative PCR analysis used a TRANS kit following the manufacturer's protocol. The relative gene expression was calculated following Livak and Schmittgen (2001). The primer sequences of GABA shunt-related genes referred to Brikis et al. (2018) and Trobacher et al. (2013). Md18S rDNA was used as a stable reference gene. The primers are listed in Table S2. 

# 4.8  |  Subcellular localization

According to the method described by Yang et al. (2021), Yin et al. (2022), and Zheng et al. (2020), the full-length CDS without the termination codon of MdGAD1 (NM_001294057.1) and MdWRKY33 (NM_001294127.1) was inserted into the pCAMBIA2300-GFP vector. AtCBL1 (At4g17615) served as a plasma membrane-localized marker. The primer sequences are shown in Table S2. The plasmids of the unmodified pCAMBIA2300-GFP vector, 35S-MdGAD1-GFP recombinant vector, and 35S-MdWRKY33-GFP were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 and transformed into epidermal cells of 5-week-old N. benthamiana leaves (Yang et al., 2000). After 48 h of infection, the leaf tissue around the injection site was collected and green fluorescent protein (GFP) fluorescence was observed by laser confocal microscope (TCS-SP8-SR). The nucleus was identified by 4,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole dihydrochloride staining. 

# 4.9  |  Transcriptional activation assays in yeast

The full-length MdWRKY33 fragment was cloned into the pGBKT7 vector, and the recombinant vector and pGADT7 were cotransformed into a yeast strain following the lithium acetate/polethylene glycol method; the yeast culture was then spread on synthetic dropout (SD)/−Trp−Leu and SD/−Trp/−Leu/−His/−Ade solid media. After 2 days, positive clones were selected to determine transcriptional activation activity. 

# 4.10  |  Yeast one-hybrid assay

Y1H was conducted using a Matchmaker hybrid Gold Y1H Library Screening System (Clontech). The pAbAi-proMdGAD1 (P1: nucleotides −859 to −1637) fusion vector was constructed and transformed into yeast Y1H Gold cells. AbA (aureobasidin A) was screened in SD medium without uracil. After determining the concentration of AbA, Y1H Gold yeast transformed with pAbAi-proMdGAD1 was made into competent cells, transformed with cDNA library plasmids, and then added to SD/−Leu solid medium coated with the appropriate concentration of AbA. After 3–5 days, the colonies were suspended in liquid SD/−Leu medium, cultured overnight at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ , and then the plasmids were extracted and sequenced. The positive results were verified by the point-to-point Y1H method. 

# 4.11  |  Dual-luciferase assay

The determination of luciferase followed the method described by An et al. (2018). The MdWRKY33 CDS was inserted into pGreenII62- SK (35S::MdWRKY33) as the effector vector. The promoter fragment of MdGAD1 (nucleotides −149 to −1637) was inserted into the reporter vector pGreenII0800-Luc (pMdGAD1::LUC). The effector and reporter vectors were transformed into A. tumefaciens then observed and luciferase activity determined 48–72 h after infiltration into N. benthamiana leaves. 

# 4.12  |  GUS analysis

The promoter sequence (nucleotides −41 to −1987) of MdGAD1 was cloned into the pCAMBIA0390 vector containing a GUS reporter gene to generate the pMdGAD1::GUS reporter plasmid. The CDS of MdWRKY33 was introduced into a pCAMBIA2300 vector to construct the 35S::MdWRKY33 expression vector as the effector plasmid. GUS staining and detection followed that described by An et al. (2018) and Xi et al. (2012). 

# 4.13  |  EMSA

The fragment of 690 bp encoding 230 amino acids (215–445 amino acids) of the WRKY domain in MdWRKY33 was inserted into pET-32a vectors (wiHis tag). The probe used in the test was synthesized by Sangon Biotech Co., Ltd. The EMSA used a LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

# 4.14  |  Genetic transformation

Full-length CDSs of MdGAD1 and MdWRKY33 were inserted into the pCAMBIA2300 (with CaMV 35S promoter) vector to generate pCAMBIA2300-MdGAD1 and pCAMBIA2300-MdWRKY33, 

respectively. Primer sequences are shown in Table S2. A. tumefaciens EHA105 carrying the pCAMBIA2300-MdGAD1 and pCAMBIA2300-MdWRKY33 plasmid were used for genetic transformation of Orin apple calli according to Xie et al. (2012). 

# 4.15  |  Transient transformation

Apple leaves were incubated with A. tumefaciens carrying plasmids of OE-MdGAD1 and OE-MdWRKY33 or RNAi-MdGAD1 and RNAi-MdWRKY33. The transient transgenic apple leaves were obtained in vacuum environment (An et al., 2020). 

# 4.16  |  Statistical analysis

The data were analysed with a Tukey's test using Excel 2007 and SPSS v. 22.0 software. Data were considered significant at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ .
