# Biosynthesis of the Plant-Produced Toxin Strychnine Elucidated

Sanath K. Kandy and Jonathan R. Chekan*

Abstract: While natural products isolated from plants have historically been an important source of bioactive molecules, elucidating their biosynthetic pathways has remained a challenging process. Strychnine is the one such compound known for its neurotoxicity and use as a rodent pesticide. Considerable interest has been shown in the synthesis of strychnine for many decades, yet little was known about its biosynthesis. A recent report from the O'Connor lab has solved this long-standing puzzle using modern transcriptomics and transient expression approaches to uncover each enzyme responsible for the production of strychnine along with an unexpected final non-enzymatic step.

Plants have been an important source of bioactive metabolites throughout human history. With the advent of molecular biology and sequencing technologies, it has become possible to reconstruct and validate entire pathways, enabling both pathways engineering and discovery of new enzyme catalysts. Unfortunately, linking a plant natural product to its biosynthetic route has been a challenging and time-consuming process due to large genome sizes, repeated use of common enzyme families, and only sporadic clustering of genes in the genome. New sequencing, analytical, protein expression, and computational techniques have created opportunities to solve these challenging biosynthetic problems. An impressive new study by the O'Connor group that elucidates the complete biosynthesis of the well-known natural product strychnine (10) highlights the value and robustness of these approaches.

Strychnine is a neurotoxic terpene indole alkaloid primarily found in plants from the Strychnos genus and has traditionally been used as a rodent pesticide. It antagonistically binds to glycine and acetylcholine receptors and leads to the overexcitation of the neurons associated with these receptors, producing uncontrollable muscle spasms and contractions.[1] Both the toxic and purported medicinal effects of strychnine-containing seeds were well known even before its isolation in 1818 from Strychnos nux-vomica.[2] Due to its high level of complexity, it took over 100 years from initial isolation to the structure of strychnine to be determined.[3,4] Despite the long interest in strychnine, little was known about its biosynthesis. A partial hypothesis was

published in 1948[5] and radioisotope-labeling studies were later performed which implicated key biosynthetic intermediates.[6,7]

To answer this centuries-old biosynthetic question, the O'Connor group used tissue-specific RNA-sequencing paired with metabolomics.[8] This approach correlated the production of strychnine with gene co-expression to generate lists of candidate biosynthetic enzymes. Target genes identified from this analysis were then tested for catalytic activity in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves through Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transient expression, allowing for rapid screening and combinatorial pathway generation (Figure 1).

The O'Connor group first focused on elucidating the pathway between two previously identified strychnine intermediates, geissoschizine (1) and the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde (6) (Figure 2). They noticed the structural similarity between the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde and a known metabolite, dehydropreakuammicine (2), and hypothesized that dehydropreakuammicine could be a biosynthetic intermediate that is transformed into the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde through a multi-step pathway. In Catharanthus roseus, the geissoschizine oxidase CrGO carries out the conversion of geissoschizine to akuammicine (3), a spontaneously deformylated product of dehydropreakuammicine.[9] A search for homologs of CrGO in the S. nux-vomica transcriptome identified SnvGO. If the biosynthesis progresses through a dehydropreakuammicine intermediate, there must exist an $\alpha/\beta$ hydrolase that carries out the hydrolysis of the dehydropreakuammicine ester moiety which permits decarboxylation before the spontaneous deformylation to form alkammicine. The O'Connor group used SnvGO as a bait for co-expression analysis to identify $\alpha/\beta$ hydrolases. Two of the five selected candidates, SnvNS1/SnvNS2, facilitated the formation of the decarboxylated product norfluorocurarine (4) in a $N$ . benthamiana transient co-expression experiment (Figure 2).

In order for norfluorocurarine to progress towards the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde, enzymes with hydroxylation and alkene reduction activities are required. Cytochrome P450s (CYP450s) and medium-chain dehydrogenases/reductases (MDRs) are often involved in alkaloid biosynthesis and were the suspected candidates to catalyze these two reactions. Co-expression analysis with SnvGO used as bait yielded five CYP450s and four MDRs. A combinatorial transient-expression approach was carried out wherein identified genes were co-expressed in $N.$ benthamiana geissoschizine-infiltrated leaves along with SnvGO and SnvNS1. Co-expression of one of the CYP450s, SnvNO (norfluorocurarine oxidase), formed the hydroxylated product 18-OH norfluorocurarine (5) (Figure 2). Upon co-infiltration with one of the candidate MDRs, SnvWS (MDR Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde synthase), the intermediate 18-OH norfluorocurarine was consumed leading to the formation of the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde (Figure 2).

While the reaction steps that progress the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde to strychnine were unknown, it was previously shown that two acetate-derived carbons are added to the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde to form the

piperidone ring found in strychnine.[7] In order to elucidate this biosynthetic step, the O'Connor lab relied on comparison with the strychnine-nonproducer Strychnos sp. Instead of generating strychnine, this plant accumulates diaboline (7), an alkaloid that is likely derived from the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde via $N$ -acetylation. After confirming a shared biosynthetic pathway leading to the Wieland-Gumlich aldehyde in Strychnos sp., the transcriptome was searched for BAHD acyltransferase family members. Transient co-expression of one candidate, SpAT, catalyzed the predicted $N$ -acylation to complete the biosynthesis of diaboline (Figure 2). A homolog of SpAT was found in the S. nux-vomica transcriptome (SnuAT) and its transient co-expression with SnvGO, SnvNS1, SnvNO, SnvWS, and a malonyl-CoA synthetase from Arabidopsis thaliana along with infiltration with geissoschizine lead to the formation of a malonylated product, prestrychnine (8) (Figure 2).

Trace amounts of strychnine and isostrychnine (9) were detected in the leaf extracts containing prestrychnine (Figure 2). The products accumulated over time when stored at room temperature and almost complete conversion was observed in leaves that were harvested four weeks post infiltration. Numerous enzymes from diverse families were tested and none were found to increase the rate of strychnine production. Surprisingly, this suggested that the conversion of prestrychnine to strychnine and isostrychnine is spontaneous and does not require a biosynthetic enzyme. However, this does not completely rule out an enzymic transformation. To conclude their study, the O'Connor group discovered an additional methyltransferase and two hydroxylases to further process strychnine to the natural product brucine (11) (Figure 2).

Overall, this is a noteworthy study from the O'Connor lab that successfully elucidates the biosynthesis of the plant

alkaloid strychnine, a widely recognized and historically important molecule. In addition to uncovering five biosynthetic enzymes en route to strychnine, their results suggest the presence of an unexpected non-enzymatic step to complete the transformation. This work not only represents an important accomplishment in the study of alkaloid biosynthesis but will facilitate the engineering of new strychnine derivatives and production methods.
