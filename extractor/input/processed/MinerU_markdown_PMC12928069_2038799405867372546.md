# Exploitation of Host-Derived N-Feruloylputrescine by Fusarium graminearum Promotes DON Biosynthesis and Wheat Rachis Colonisation

# ABSTRACT

Plants reprogramme their metabolism upon pathogen attack, producing compounds that can either enhance immunity or be exploited by pathogens. Metabolomic profiling of wheat during Fusarium graminearum infection revealed pronounced accumulation of phenolamides, driven by activation of their biosynthetic pathways. Notably, exogenous $N$ -feruloylputrescine (Ferput), a representative phenolamide, enhanced resistance to wheat stripe rust, powdery mildew and rice blast but increased susceptibility to Fusarium head blight. Ferput inhibited fungal infection in the lemma while promoting rachis colonisation, indicating pathogen- and tissue-specific effects. Mechanistic analyses showed that Ferput stimulates deoxynivalenol (DON) biosynthesis by inducing TRI gene expression, toxisome formation and DON-associated cellular differentiation, underlying the shift from lemma resistance to rachis susceptibility. Together, these findings highlight the context-dependent roles of phenolamide in plant–pathogen interactions and suggest that, under specific pathological contexts, defence-associated metabolites can be exploited by pathogens to enhance their virulence. This insight underscores the necessity of considering the dual functional roles of plant metabolites when engineering broad-spectrum disease resistance. 

Upon pathogen infection, plants undergo extensive reprogramming of their transcriptional and metabolic networks, resulting in dynamic shifts in metabolite accumulation and turnover (Escaray et al. 2022). Metabolomics provides a powerful approach to systematically capture these changes, offering critical insights into the metabolic basis of plant disease resistance (Shen et al. 2023). Despite the well-characterised regulatory networks of disease-resistance genes, the mechanisms by which metabolites mediate plant–pathogen interactions remain poorly understood (Piasecka et  al.  2015). Secondary metabolites contribute to plant defence through multiple strategies, functioning either as direct antimicrobials or as modulators of conserved immune responses, including callose deposition 

and programmed cell death (Kumar et  al.  2025; Zeier  2013). Conversely, pathogens can manipulate plant metabolism via effector proteins (Wang et al. 2024; Cai et al. 2023), and may even exploit plant-derived metabolites to promote invasive growth and enhance virulence, although such instances remain rarely documented. Pathogens differ in their responses to plant metabolites and capacity to manipulate or exploit these metabolites, leading to marked complexity and diversity in plant–pathogen interactions. Exploring the roles of metabolites in infections by diverse pathogens can reveal both conserved and pathogen-specific host defence mechanisms, providing a foundation for developing crop varieties with broad-spectrum disease resistance. 

To investigate the dynamic metabolic changes and their functional roles during Fusarium graminearum—wheat interaction, we performed an untargeted metabolomic analysis on wheat spike (cultivar Norm) collected at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation (hpi) with the wild-type PH-1, using sterile water-inoculated spikes as the mock control (Figure S1). (All methods are described in Text S1) Among the identified metabolites, 47 were significantly upregulated ( $\mathrm { F C } \geq 2$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ in infected wheat heads compared to untreated control (Table S1). Remarkably, 23 $( 4 9 \% )$ of these upregulated metabolites are phenolamides (Figure S1), indicating that phenolamides represent the dominant metabolite class induced by $F .$ graminearum infection. Notably, coumaroylputrescine, previously reported to accumulate in a resistant nearisogenic line (NIL-R) more abundantly than in a susceptible line (NIL-S) upon $F .$ graminearum infection (Kage et al. 2017), was also identified in this study. Furthermore, polyamines, the key precursors for phenolamide biosynthesis, are known to be induced by $F .$ graminearum infection in a deoxynivalenol (DON)- dependent manner (Bönnighausen et al. 2019). Together, these results indicate phenolamides as a central metabolic response to $F .$ graminearum infection. Indeed, phenolamides accumulation represents a conserved defence mechanism across multiple plant families, including Solanaceae, Brassicaceae and Poaceae (Muroi et  al.  2012; Royer et  al.  2016; Morimoto et  al.  2018). However, no clear link between specific phenolamide types and particular pathogens or plant hosts has been established. 

To better illustrate the relationships among these differential metabolites during early F. graminearum infection of wheat heads, we constructed a comprehensive metabolic pathway map (Figure  1A). Metabolomic profiling revealed that tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle intermediates remained largely stable, suggesting that the core energy metabolism remains relatively stable during early infection. Similarly, sugar metabolism exhibited only modest perturbations, with a slight decrease in sugars coupled with a moderate increase in their derivatives. In contrast, more dynamic changes were identified in amino acid metabolism: 14 of the 283 detected amino acids were significantly upregulated, while 4 were downregulated. Notably, ornithine and its derivatives were markedly elevated, whereas one cyclic amino acid, Cyclo (d-Leu-l-Pro), showed a striking 16-fold reduction. More pronounced changes were also observed in redox homeostasis: glutathione disulphide (GSSG) dropped 5-fold, and its reduced counterpart (GSH) increased 1.8-fold. In contrast, a broad spectrum of phenolamides was strongly induced. These metabolites are synthesised via the conjugation of ferulic acid or $p$ -coumaric acid with either agmatine or putrescine, highlighting the robust activation of this biosynthetic pathway. 

The remarkable accumulation of phenolamides in response to F. graminearum infection prompted us to perform an in-depth analysis of their biosynthetic pathway, which relies on the coordinated synthesis of phenolic acids and polyamines. The phenolic acid branch originates from l-phenylalanine, which undergoes a series of enzymatic conversions to generate key intermediates such as $p$ -coumaric acid, caffeic acid and ferulic acid (Bassard et  al.  2010). In parallel, the polyamine branch is derived from l-arginine, which is successively converted into agmatine and putrescine. These precursors then undergo specific conjugation reactions: phenolic acids couple with putrescine via PHT1/2 and PHT3 to form various putrescine-derived 

phenolamides $p$ -coumaroylferuloylputrescine, N-p-coumaroyl-$N ^ { \prime }$ -feruloylputrescine, N-p-coumaroylhydroxyputrescine, $p$ -coumaroylputrescine, $N \mathbf { \mathcal { N } ^ { \prime } }$ -diferuloylputrescine, Nferuloylputrescine, N-feruloylhydroxyputrescine, dihydroferuloylputrescine), whereas conjugation with agmatine catalysed by AHT1 and THT1 produces multiple agmatine-derived phenolamides (N-feruloylhomoagmatine, $N _ { ☉ }$ -feruloylmethylagmatine, feruloylagmatine, N-feruloylhydroxyagmatine, N-p-coumaroyl hydroxydehydroagmatine, $N { - } p$ -coumaroylhydroxyagmatine, $N \mathrm { . }$ -( $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-glycosyl)- $p$ -coumaroyl agmatine, $p$ -coumaroylagmatine) (Figure S2). 

To identify the underlying genetic basis for phenolamide biosynthesis, we characterised the expression of key genes involved in this pathway in wheat. A BLASTP search using rice PHT3 as a query identified 15 homologues in wheat. These genes were barely expressed in uninfected wheat head, but their transcript levels increased progressively at 24, 48 and 72 h after inoculation with F. graminearum (Figure  1B). According to WheatOmics data, all PHT3 homologues were strongly induced by flg22 and also responded to chitin treatment (Figure 1B). Similarly, a total of 14 AHT1 homologous genes were identified in wheat, whose expression was rapidly induced at 24 hpi, and remained stable throughout the later infection period (48 and 72 hpi) (Figure 1C). Furthermore, these AHT1 homologues were also responsive to flg22 and chitin. The coordinated induction of PHT3 and AHT1 homologues upon fungal infection and pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP) treatment supports the involvement of phenolamide biosynthesis in plant–pathogen interactions. 

Because the phenolamide biosynthetic pathway is strongly activated in wheat during F. graminearum infection and closely associated with plant defence responses, we selected $N$ -feruloylputrescine (Ferput) as a representative phenolamide to further investigate its role in plant immunity. Wheat heads were presprayed with Ferput (30 or $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ) $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ before inoculation with F. graminearum PH-1 conidia, and disease severity was assessed 14 days post-inoculation (dpi). Interestingly, compared with the untreated control, Ferput-treated wheat displayed a slight increase in the number of infected spikelets, suggesting that Ferput may increase susceptibility to F. graminearum infection (Figure 2A). 

To determine whether this effect is pathogen-specific, we evaluated Ferput's influence on three additional fungal diseases: wheat stripe rust, powdery mildew and rice blast. In contrast, Ferput pretreatment markedly reduced the number of urediniospore pustules produced by Puccinia striiformis f. sp. tritici (Pst) and Blumeria graminis f. sp. tritici (Bgt) in a concentrationdependent manner (Figure 2B,C). Similarly, Ferput application significantly suppressed blast lesion development in both rice and barley following Magnaporthe oryzae infection, resulting in smaller and fewer lesions (Figures 2D and S3). Microscopic analysis further revealed that Ferput restricted the expansion of infectious hyphae within rice leaf sheaths (Figure  S4). These results demonstrate that Ferput plays distinct roles in different host–pathogen systems. While it enhances resistance against foliar pathogens including Pst, Bgt and M. oryzae, it promotes susceptibility in the F. graminearum–wheat interaction. Fusarium graminearum exhibits a multistage infection strategy in wheat heads, with lemma invasion representing the early stage and rachis colonisation serving as a critical step for spikelet-to-spikelet spread. Lemma and rachis infections represent two distinct processes, particularly in their dependence on DON toxin (Jansen et al. 2005; Maier et al. 2006). To assess the impact of Ferput on these infection stages, we compared fungal 

growth in the lemma and rachis after inoculation with the $F .$ graminearum PH-1, with or without Ferput treatment. By 3 dpi, extensive hyphal growth was evident in lemma tissues, whereas exogenous Ferput application significantly reduced visible invasive hyphae (Figure  2E). Microscopic analysis of WGA488- stained lemma confirmed that Ferput significantly restricted hyphal proliferation during the early stage of wheat lemma infection (Figure  S5A). Consistently, quantitative PCR (qPCR) measurement of fungal biomass in lemma tissues also revealed significantly lower levels in Ferput-treated samples compared with the control (Figure  2F). Although abundant hyphae were detected in the vascular tissues of both treated and untreated samples, Ferput application led to noticeably denser hyphal colonisation (Figure 2E). This was further confirmed by qPCR analysis, which showed a significant increase in fungal biomass in the rachis upon Ferput treatment (Figure 2F). Furthermore, exogenous Ferput application suppressed F. graminearum infection in wheat coleoptiles, underscoring the tissue-specific effects (Figure  S5B). Together, these results demonstrate that Ferput effectively suppresses fungal infection in lemma tissues and coleoptiles, yet enhances DON-dependent spread in the rachis, highlighting its contrasting, stage- and tissue-specific roles in the wheat–F. graminearum interaction. 

To investigate the basis of this inhibitory effect in lemmas, we first analysed the expression of key immunity-related genes, including those in the salicylic acid (SA) pathway (TaPR1, TaPR2, TaPR5 and TaNPR1) and jasmonic acid (JA) pathway (TaOPR3, TaMYC2 and TaCOI1). However, Ferput treatment caused no or only minor changes in these genes compared with the mock control (Figure  S6A). Because phenolamines have been reported to promote lignin accumulation (Alber et al. 2019), we then assessed lignin deposition in lemma tissues and found that Ferput treatment significantly increased lignin accumulation (Figure S6B,C), which may strengthen physical barriers and inhibit early F. graminearum invasion. By contrast, Ferput promoted invasive hyphal growth in the wheat rachis. Because DON plays a crucial role in the hyphal spread of F. graminearum through the rachis (Ilgen et al. 2009; Jansen et al. 2005), we hypothesised that Ferput enhances rachis infection in a DON-dependent manner. Consistently, DON accumulation in inoculated kernels was increased 1.9-fold by Ferput treatment at 14 dpi compared to the untreated control (Figure 2G). Furthermore, when tested in a DON-inducing medium where Ferput was supplied as the sole nitrogen source, replacing putrescine or $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 }$ , DON accumulation after 7 days of cultivation increased by more than 4.5-fold and 12.8-fold compared to putrescine  and $\mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 }$ , respectively (Figure  2H). This 

indicates that Ferput can stimulate DON production in $F .$ . graminearum, thereby facilitating its invasive growth in the wheat rachis. Indeed, F. graminearum employs multiple strategies to promote infection, such as secreting effectors that modulate wheat defence responses, disrupt energy homeostasis, or alter amino acid metabolism (Jiang et  al.  2020; Shang et  al.  2024, 2025). However, the exploitation of plant defence metabolites to generate virulence factors remains rarely reported. 

Because DON biosynthesis is controlled by the TRI gene cluster, we next examined the effect of Ferput on TRI gene expression. After 3 days of treatment, transcription of four core TRI genes (TRI1, TRI5, TRI10 and TRI12) was markedly upregulated compared with putrescine (Figure 2I), consistent with the observed enhancement of DON production. In addition, DON biosynthesis involves DON-associated cellular differentiation (hyphal swellings) and toxisome assembly. Microscopic observations showed that Ferput significantly promoted the development of hyphal swellings after $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ of treatment (Figure S7). Furthermore, the cytochrome P450 oxygenase Tri1p, a key enzyme in trichothecene biosynthesis, was fluorescently tagged to monitor toxisome formation. GFP imaging revealed that Ferput strongly enhanced toxisome assembly at the same time point (Figure 2J). These findings demonstrate that Ferput promotes DON biosynthesis by activating TRI gene transcription, stimulating toxisome formation and inducing DON-associated cellular differentiation. DON biosynthesis is influenced by multiple factors, including pH and reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Mentges and Bormann 2015; Ponts et al. 2007). Moreover, several metabolites, such as putrescine, have been reported to induce DON production (Gardiner et al. 2009). In this study, we found that Ferput exerts an even more pronounced effect than putrescine, acting as a stronger DON inducer. 

To determine whether Ferput affects fungal growth under culture conditions, we grew the wild-type strain PH-1 on potato dextrose agar  supplemented with Ferput (30 or $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ) for 3 days. Compared to the control, Ferput treatment showed no 

apparent effect on colony morphology or growth (Figure S8A). The conidial germination and germ tube elongation in liquid yeast extract-petone-dextrose (YEPD) medium were not changed as well (Figure S8B). We further tested the effect of Ferput on M. oryzae growth on complete medium (CM) plates containing 30 or $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Ferput. Similar to $F .$ . graminearum, Ferput had no apparent effect on colony growth of M. oryzae (Figure S8C). These results suggested that Ferput does not affect vegetative growth in either $F .$ . graminearum or M. oryzae. 

In conclusion, our study reveals that phenolamides, a class of infection-induced metabolites in wheat, play dual and contextdependent roles in plant resistance. We revealed that while exogenous Ferput enhances resistance to wheat stripe rust, powdery mildew and rice blast, it promotes Fusarium head blight by stimulating DON biosynthesis (Figure  2K). These findings demonstrate that specialised pathogens can hijack host defence-associated metabolites to enhance their virulence, highlighting the complexity of achieving broad-spectrum disease resistance.
