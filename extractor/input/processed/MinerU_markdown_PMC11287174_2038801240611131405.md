# ABSTRACT

Transitory starch is an important carbon source in leaves, and its biosynthesis and metabolism are closely related to grain quality and yield. The molecular mechanisms controlling leaf transitory starch biosynthesis and degradation and their effects on rice (Oryza sativa) quality and yield remain unclear. Here, we show that OsLESV and OsESV1, the rice orthologs of AtLESV and AtESV1, are associated with transitory starch biosynthesis in rice. The total starch and amylose contents in leaves and endosperms are significantly reduced, and the final grain quality and yield are compromised in oslesv and osesv1 single and oslesv esv1 double mutants. Furthermore, we found that OsLESV and OsESV1 bind to starch, and this binding depends on a highly conserved C-terminal tryptophan-rich region that acts as a starch-binding domain. Importantly, OsLESV and OsESV1 also interact with the key enzymes of starch biosynthesis, granule-bound starch synthase I (GBSSI), GBSSII, and pyruvate orthophosphote dikiase (PPDKB), to maintain their protein stability and activity. OsLESV and OsESV1 also facilitate the targeting of GBSSI and GBSSII from plastid stroma to starch granules. Overexpression of GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB can partly rescue the phenotypic defects of the oslesv and osesv1 mutants. Thus, we demonstrate that OsLESV and OsESV1 play a key role in regulating the biosynthesis of both leaf transitory starch and endosperm storage starch in rice. These findings deepen our understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves and reveal how the transitory starch metabolism affects rice grain quality and yield, providing useful information for the genetic improvement of rice grain quality and yield.

# INTRODUCTION

Starch accounts for more than $80 \%$ of the total storage compounds in rice (Oryza sativa) grains and is directly related to rice grain quality and yield. Starch biosynthesis involves a series of complex and intricately regulated enzymatic reactions (Jeon et al., 2010). Photosynthates produced in leaves are transported to grains in the form of sucrose, which is then converted to ADP-glucose (ADPG) by sequential enzymatic reactions. As a substrate, ADPG is used to synthesize amylose by granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS) and to form amylopectin via the starch synthase, starch-branching enzyme, and starch-

debranching enzyme (Umemoto et al., 2002; Satoh et al., 2003; Bahaji et al., 2014). In addition to the above key enzymes, many proteins involved in carbon metabolism, the respiratory chain, and plastid development, such as Floury endosperm (FLO) proteins, participate in endosperm starch biosynthesis (Kang et al., 2005; Peng et al., 2014; Teng et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023). In addition, many transcription factors, such

as RSR1, bZIP58, MADS29, NF-YB1, and Opaque3, assist in endosperm starch biosynthesis by regulating the transcription of genes encoding starch biosynthesis enzymes (Nayar et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2013, 2020a; Bello et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2022). 

In addition to serving as the primary storage compound in the endosperm, starch also occurs in the leaves and stem sheaths of cereal crops. During the day, excess photosynthetic products are stored as starch in chloroplasts; however, this leaf starch is degraded at night to provide energy for plant growth. Therefore, leaf starch is referred to as ‘‘transitory starch.’’ During the grainfilling stage, carbohydrates in leaves and stem sheaths are transported to endosperm cells to synthesize storage starch. Therefore, transitory starch biosynthesis and degradation also affect endosperm starch biosynthesis, ultimately affecting grain quality and yield (Scofield et al., 2009). The biosynthesis and degradation of transitory starch are well understood in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana). Transitory starch is biosynthesized in chloroplasts: ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ is first fixed by the photosynthetic Calvin cycle and then converted by a series of enzymes into fructose-6- phosphate. Fructose-6-phosphate is transformed by sequential enzymatic reactions into glucose-6-phosphate, glucose-1-phosphate, and ADPG, which is used to produce amylose and amylopectin via the GBSS, SS, starch-branching enzyme, and starchdebranching enzyme (Bahaji et al., 2014). Soluble SSs serve as the key enzymes that initiate glucan biosynthesis in leaves. A starch-binding protein AtPTST2 interacts with SS4 to mediate the binding of SS4 to its substrate glucan, thereby initiating starch granule (SG) formation (Szydlowski et al., 2009). In addition, AtPTST1 also binds to SGs to facilitate the targeting of GBSS to SGs (Seung et al., 2015). At night, transitory starch stored in leaves is degraded to provide a carbon source, and thus providing energy for plant growth and development. To initiate degradation, starch is phosphorylated by glucan water dikinase and phosphoglucan water dikinase, followed by decomposition into glucose and maltose by $\beta$ -amylase and isoamylase (ISA), which are finally transported to heterotrophic organs in the form of sucrose for their growth and development, or transported to the amyloplasts of sink cells for the biosynthesis of storage starch (Fettke et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2021). Recently, two starch-binding proteins, Early Starvation 1 (AtESV1) and Like Early Starvation (AtLESV), were found to associate with leaf starch metabolism. Specifically, AtESV1 is believed to affect leaf starch degradation by regulating the activities of glucan water dikinase and phosphoglucan water dikinase (Feike et al., 2016; Malinova et al., 2018; Song et al., 2021). In rice, GBSS-BINDING PROTEIN 1 (OsGBP1), a homolog of AtPTST1, mediates the binding of GBSSI and GBSSII to developing SGs to promote amylose biosynthesis in leaves and endosperms, ultimately affecting rice grain quality and yield (Wang et al., 2020b). However, despite our understanding of the molecular mechanisms governing the biosynthesis and degradation of Arabidopsis transitory starch and rice endosperm storage starch, the molecular mechanism of rice leaf transitory starch biosynthesis remains elusive. Furthermore, its role in rice grain quality and yield is not well understood. 

Some starch biosynthesis- and degradation-related enzymes and non-enzymatic proteins contain a distinct starch-binding domain (SBD) or surface-binding site (Wilkens et al., 2018). 

# OsLESV and OsESV1 promote starch biosynthesis

Insoluble polysaccharides can be degraded by specific hydrolytic enzymes that are formed when catalytic modules append to one or more non-catalytic carbohydrate-binding modules (CBMs). Many CBM families exhibit functional characteristics of SBDs (Guille´ n et al., 2010; Wilkens et al., 2018). CBM48 represents a typical SBD, and multiple members of the CBM48 domain-containing protein family have been shown to bind to starch. These proteins include Arabidopsis AtPTST1, AtPTST2, AtPTST3, and AtSEX4 and rice OsGBP1 and FLO6, all of which have been reported to interact with key starch biosynthesis enzymes, thereby participating in the biosynthesis of leaf transitory starch or endosperm storage starch (Christiansen et al., 2009; Kotting et al., 2009 € ; Janecek et al., 2011 - ; Peng et al., 2014; Seung et al., 2015, 2017; Wang et al., 2020b). A recent study has shown that the tryptophan (Trp)-rich regions of AtESV1 and AtLESV form atypical carbohydrate-binding surfaces (Liu et al., 2023). Therefore, identifying additional functional proteins that contain SBDs will advance our understanding of starch biosynthesis and degradation mechanisms. 

Here, we identify two starch-binding proteins, OsLESV and OsESV1, the rice orthologs of AtLESV1 and AtESV1, that are associated with transitory starch biosynthesis in rice. We report that the C-terminal Trp-rich regions function as SBDs, and are essential for the binding of OsLESV and OsESV1 to starch. We also demonstrate that OsLESV and OsESV1 function upstream of and maintain the protein stability and enzyme activity of GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB. Both starch-binding proteins also guide GBSSs towards SGs to mediate the biosynthesis of leaf transitory starch and endosperm storage starch, thereby affecting rice grain quality and yield. These findings enhance our understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves and provide useful information for the genetic improvement of rice grain quality and yield.

# OsLESV and its homolog OsESV1 function in transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves

The predicted OsLESV protein is composed of 618 amino acids, containing a Trp-rich region (between amino acids 351 and 606) at the C terminus (Supplemental Figure 2B). We found that the expressed protein OsESV1 (LOC_Os03g04100), an Arabidopsis ESV1 homolog, has the highest sequence similarity to OsLESV in the rice genome (Supplemental Figure 2A; Feike et al., 2016; Song et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). OsESV1 (LSD2) is composed of 431 amino acids and shares a C-terminal Trp-rich region with OsLESV (LSD1). OsLESV and OsESV1 homologs are present in other plant species, such as sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), maize (Zea mays), Setaria italica, and Triticum urartu, and share a highly conserved C-terminal Trp-rich domain (Supplemental Figure 2A– 2C). To investigate the biological functions of OsLESV and OsESV1, we obtained their single mutants oslesv-1, oslesv-2, osesv1-1, and osesv1-2 and double mutant oslesv esv1 in the Nip genetic background by CRISPR–Cas9 gene editing (Figure 2A and 2B). The transcript levels of OsLESV and OsESV1 and their protein abundance were all significantly decreased in the single and double mutants compared with Nip (Figure 2C–2F). 

Given the significantly decreased transitory starch content in lsd1 leaves compared with its wild-type YK17, we measured the starch content in leaves of the oslesv and osesv1 single mutants, as well as the oslesv esv1 double mutant. Leaf iodine staining at the end of the day showed dark blue coloration in Nip leaves and lighter blue coloration in osesv1-1 and osesv1-2 leaves, whereas the leaves of oslesv-1, oslesv-2, and oslesv esv1 were brown. At the end of the night, the leaves of the wild type and all mutants were dyed brown, however, the coloration of wild-type leaves remained slightly bluer than that of the mutants (Figure 2G). Transmission electron microscopy analyses revealed that several SGs were present in each chloroplast of the wild-type mesophyll cells; by contrast, fewer SGs were observed in osesv1-1, and almost no well-developed SGs were observed in oslesv-1 or oslesv esv1 chloroplasts (Figure 2H and 2I). We also found that the total starch and amylose contents in leaves of the single and double mutants of OsLESV and OsESV1 were significantly lower at the end of the day compared with those in the wild type, and almost no amylose was detected in oslesv-1 or oslesv esv1 leaves (Figure 2J and 2K). We also measured the contents of sucrose and non-structural carbohydrates in wildtype and mutant leaves and found that more sucrose accumulated in leaves of the single and double mutants compared with 

the wild type; however, the contents of total non-structural carbohydrates were significantly reduced in mutant leaves compared with wild-type leaves (Figure 2L–M). Taken together, our data suggest that OsLESV and OsESV1 are crucial for transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves and that OsLESV and OsESV1 may have partially overlapping functions, as the double mutant displayed significantly greater impairment in its ability to accumulate transitory starch in leaves. 

# The C-terminal Trp-rich regions of OsLESV and OsESV1 are essential for their binding to starch

We examined the temporal and spatial expression profiles of OsLESV and OsESV1 in the wild type and oslesv-1 or osesv1-1. OsLESV and OsESV1 were constitutively expressed in all examined tissues; OsLESV was expressed at a higher level in leaves and young endosperms, whereas OsESV1 was expressed at a higher level in leaves. Moreover, the transcript levels of OsLESV and OsESV1 were higher in the wild type than in oslesv-1 or osesv1-1 in all examined tissues (Supplemental Figure 3A and 3B). As a product of photosynthesis, starch accumulates in plant leaves during the day and gradually converts to sucrose during the night (Smith and Zeeman, 2020). Consistent with this notion, we found that OsLESV and OsESV1 were induced by carbon starvation. Specifically, the expression levels of OsLESV, OsESV1, and two carbon starvation-induced marker genes (GBSSII and SnRK1a), gradually decreased during the day with the accumulation of starch and gradually increased at night with the degradation of starch in both wild-type and oslesv-1 or osesv1-1 plants (Figure 3A and 3B; Supplemental Figure 3C). 

We then examined the subcellular localization of OsLESV and OsESV1 by transiently expressing OsLESV–GFP (green fluorescent protein) and OsESV1–GFP fusion constructs in rice protoplasts and Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. The GFP fluorescence of both fusion proteins was exclusively detected in chloroplasts, where it was associated with discrete bodies likely to be SGs (Figure 3C; Supplemental Figure 3D). GBSSI is an SG-binding protein localized to chloroplasts when expressed in rice protoplasts (Hebelstrup et al., 2017). When we co-expressed the GBSSI–CFP fusion protein with OsLESV–GFP or OsESV1–GFP in rice protoplasts, the GFP signals of the OsLESV–GFP and OsESV1–GFP fusion proteins overlapped perfectly with the CFP signal of the GBSSI–CFP fusion protein in protoplasts (Figure 3D). Immunoblot analysis further detected OsLESV and OsESV1 in starch extracted from the wild-type leaves, but not oslesv-1 or osesv1-1 leaves. Although OsLESV and OsESV1 were present in insoluble and soluble fractions in wild-type leaves, the proteins were mainly present in the insoluble component that contained starch at the end of the day, when starch levels are highest. At the end of the night, when starch levels are the lowest, OsLESV and OsESV1 were mainly present in the soluble component (Figure 3E). In summary, our results indicate that OsLESV and OsESV1 are SG-binding proteins.

# OsLESV and OsESV1 modulate endosperm starch biosynthesis and affect rice grain quality and yield

The biosynthesis and degradation of transitory starch in plant leaves affect plant growth and development (Abt and Zeeman, 2020). Therefore, we investigated whether rice grain characteristics are influenced by OsLESV and OsESV1. The mature endosperm of oslesv was opaque; that of osesv1 showed severe white belly, and that of the double mutant oslesv esv1 appeared floury and shrunken (Figure 4A and 4E; Supplemental Figure 4B–4D). Scanning electron microscopy of the transverse sections of mature endosperms showed that SGs were polyhedral and tightly packed in the wild type but irregularly rounded and loosely packed in the single and double mutants (Figure 4B; Supplemental Figure 4B–4D). # DISCUSSION

# OsLESV and OsESV1 mediate transitory and storage starch biosynthesis in rice

The photosynthetic products biosynthesized in leaves are stored in mesophyll cells in the form of transitory starch during the day; at night, the transitory starch is degraded to provide energy for plant growth, and the starch in leaves and stem sheaths is transported to endosperm cells in the form of sucrose to produce storage starch (Smith and Zeeman, 2020; Huang et al., 2021). The molecular mechanism of transitory starch synthesis in rice leaves is poorly studied. Here, we identified two proteins, OsLESV and OsESV1, orthologs of the Arabidopsis AtLESV and AtESV1, respectively, that are associated with transitory starch biosynthesis in rice. The total starch and amylose contents were significantly lower in the leaves of single and double mutants of OsLESV and OsESV1 compared with wild-type (Figure 2). OsESV1 has also been reported to influence SG formation in gravity-sensitive tissues—when OsESV1 is mutated, rice plants show a loose plant architecture with a larger tiller angle (Song et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2023). Similarly, the osesv1 and oslesv esv1 mutants exhibited a loose plant architecture compared with wild-type plants (Supplemental Figure 6A). The contents of starch and sucrose in the stem sheath of mutants were also significantly reduced (Supplemental Figure 11A–11D), accompanied by an increase in the expression levels of sugar-transport-related genes in the stem sheath, potentially as a result of negative feedback regulation (Supplemental Figure 11E). Furthermore, the starch and amylose contents, development of compound starch grains, and physicochemical properties of endosperm storage starch were all compromised in oslesv, osesv1, and oslesv esv1 mutants, resulting in poor grain quality and reduced yield (Figure 4). Therefore, the biological functions of OsLESV and OsESV1 in rice differ from those of their homologs AtLESV and AtESV1 in Arabidopsis, which are primarily involved in transitory starch biosynthesis (Feike et al., 2016; Malinova et al., 2018). Recently, OsLESV was reported to modulate compound SG initiation in the endosperm, and its mutant flo9 is defective in amylopectin biosynthesis, leading to grains exhibiting FLO, which is generally consistent with our results (Yan et al., 2024). In this study, we found that OsLESV and OsESV1 were involved in the biosynthesis of both transitory starch in leaves and storage starch in endosperms, and further research of OsLESV and OsESV1 will help us to better understand the molecular mechanism of transitory starch biosynthesis and its role in rice grain quality and yield. 

# OsLESV and OsESV1 bind to starch via a Trp-rich region and mediate the targeting of GBSSs to SGs

Subcellular localization and starch-binding experiments revealed that OsLESV and OsESV1 can bind to starch, amylose, and amylopectin (Figure 3C–3F; Supplemental Figure 3D–3F). Starch-binding proteins typically possess surface-binding sites or SBDs (Wilkens et al., 2018). CBM48 represents a relatively common type of SBD (Guille´ n et al., 2010; Wilkens et al., 2018), found in many enzymes and non-enzymatic proteins associated with starch biosynthesis in rice. The non-enzymatic OsGBP1 and FLO6 proteins contain a C-terminal CBM48 domain that is crucial for their binding to starch (Wang et al., 2020b). In this study, we found that OsLESV, OsESV1, as well as their Arabidopsis and maize homologs, all possess a highly conserved C-

terminal Trp-rich region and could bind to starch (Figure 3F; Supplemental Figures 3E–3G and 2; Feike et al., 2016). Further protein truncation experiments showed that this region was essential for their binding to starch (Figure 3G; Supplemental Figure 3H and 3I), which aligns with recent research (Liu et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024). CBM domains typically contain conserved aromatic amino acid residues that align proteins with the non-polar facets of glucose residues in starch (Christiansen et al., 2009). Amino acid sequence analysis revealed that the C-terminal Trp-rich regions of OsLESV, OsESV1, and their homologs also contain several conserved aromatic amino acids (Supplemental Figure 2C), which may be crucial for their binding to starch. Therefore, our results suggest that the highly conserved C-terminal Trp-rich region in OsLESV, OsESV1, and their homologs in other plants, serves as an SBD that is essential for the involvement of these proteins in starch biosynthesis and degradation. 

In addition, GBSS is the only enzyme in amylose biosynthesis that lacks an SBD. Therefore, some non-enzymatic proteins assist in the binding of GBSS to SGs (Tatge et al., 1999). A recent study has shown that OsGBP1, a homolog of Arabidopsis AtPTST1, could mediate the binding of GBSS to developing SGs, thereby regulating amylose biosynthesis in leaves and endosperms and ultimately affecting rice grain quality and yield (Wang et al., 2020b). Here, we found that OsLESV and OsESV1 co-localized with GBSSI and interacted with GBSSI, GBSSII. And GBSSs were mostly located in the insoluble (SG-containing) protein fraction of both endosperms and leaves. However, in oslesv and osesv1 mutants, a significant portion of GBSSI and GBSSII in endosperms and leaves, respectively, was found in the soluble protein fraction (Figures 3D, 5A–5C, and 5N–5P). These results suggest that OsLESV and OsESV1 mediate the targeting of GBSSs to SGs. A more recent study reported that OsLESV facilitates the targeting of ISA1 to SGs and modulates starch biosynthesis and endosperm development (Yan et al., 2024). Therefore, we believe that OsLESV and OsESV1 are important nonenzymatic proteins that may facilitate the targeting of many key starch metabolism-related enzymes, such as GBSSs and ISA1, to SGs, thereby affecting the biosynthesis and degradation of leaf transitory starch and endosperm storage starch in rice (Figure 7).

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The lsd1 mutant was obtained from rice (Oryza sativa) indica cv. YK17 through ethyl methanesulfonate mutagenesis. The single and double mutants of OsLESV and OsESV1 in the japonica cv. Nip background were generated by CRISPR–Cas9 gene editing. All plants mentioned in this study were grown in a paddy field or a greenhouse at China National Rice Research Institute. 

# Map-based cloning of OsLESV

Seeds with an opaque endosperm phenotype similar to that of lsd1 were selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from a cross between lsd1 and Nip and subsequently identified as homozygous recessive individuals. A total of 1129 homozygous recessive $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals were used to fine map the LSD1 locus (OsLESV). Simple sequence repeat and insertion/ deletion markers with nucleotide polymorphisms between YK17 and Nip were used to identify markers linked to OsLESV (Supplemental Table 1). 

# Vector construction and plant transformation

For the complementation experiment, a genomic fragment that contained the 2.0-kb promoter region and the OsLESV coding sequence from YK17 was cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector. To generate the OsLESV, OsESV1, and PPDKB knockout lines via the CRISPR–Cas9 system, synthetic guide RNA targeting sites were introduced into the BGK03 vector (Biogle, Hangzhou, China). To generate the OE lines, the GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB coding sequences were individually cloned into the pKK-35S vector (Biorun, Wuhan, China). The complementation vector was transformed into lsd1, the CRISPR–Cas9 vector was transformed into Nip, and the OE vectors were transformed into oslesv-1 and osesv1-1 by 

# Plant Communications

Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation. The primers used for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Microscopy

Mature grains were ruptured transversely, and the exposed surfaces were coated with gold. The ruptured transverse surfaces were observed using a Hitachi SU-8010 scanning electron microscope (Tokyo, Japan). To observe the development of SGs in endosperms and chloroplasts in leaves, samples of grains at 9 days after flowering (DAF) and flag leaves were prepared for analysis with a Hitachi H-7650 transmission electron microscope. The 9 DAF grains were also used to prepare semi-thin sections $( 0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m m } )$ that were stained with $\mathsf { I } _ { 2 } \mathrm { - } \mathsf { K }$ and examined under a light microscope. 

# RNA extraction and RT–qPCR analysis

TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA) was used for RNA extraction. The roots, stems, leaves, and grains at 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, and 21 DAF were prepared for total RNA isolation. A ReverTra Ace qPCR RT kit (Toyobo, Osaka, Japan) was used for first-strand cDNA synthesis. Quantitative PCR was used to measure gene expression levels, with Ubiquitin (Os03g0234200) as the internal reference. The primers used for qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Subcellular localization

To generate GFP and CFP fusions, protein-coding sequences without stop codons were cloned into 1132-GFP and pA7-CFP vectors, respectively. The resulting fusion constructs, OsLESV–GFP, OsESV1–GFP, and GBSSI–CFP, were transformed into rice protoplasts or Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Waadt et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2011). A confocal laser scanning microscope (Karl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) was used to observe fluorescent signals. Primers used for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Y2H, BiFC, and in vitro pull-down assays

To construct vectors for Y2H assays, the coding sequences of OsLESV, OsESV1, GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB were PCR-amplified and cloned into the PGADT7 prey or PGBKT7 bait vector. The Y2H assay was carried out using the Clontech Y2H system (Dalian, China). For BiFC assays, the coding sequences of GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB were individually cloned into the pSPYCE vector, and the coding sequences of OsLESV or OsESV1 were cloned into the pSPYNE vector. The fusion vectors were transiently expressed in rice protoplasts, and the fluorescence signals were examined by confocal laser scanning microscopy. For in vitro pull-down assays, the coding sequences of OsLESV and OsESV1 were PCR-amplified and cloned into the pGEX-4T-1 vector to obtain GST fusion proteins. The coding sequences of GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB were individually cloned into the pET28a vector to generate His fusion proteins. The fusion proteins and empty tags were expressed in Escherichia coli (E. coli) DE3 cells and purified. The pull-down assay was carried out as described by Zhang et al. (2015a). Primers used for vector construction in the Y2H, BiFC, and pull-down assays are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Purification of SGs from rice leaves and endosperms

Wild-type and mutant leaves and grains were homogenized in extraction buffer $_ { ( 0 . 5 \% }$ [v/v] Triton X-100, 50 mM Tris–HCl [pH 8], and 0.2 mM EDTA) with a mortar and pestle and filtered through a $1 5 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ nylon mesh. The insoluble substances were resuspended in extraction buffer again for a second round of extraction. The SGs were collected by centrifugation at $3 0 0 0 g$ in $9 5 \%$ (v/v) Percoll (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) for 20 min. Next, the SGs were washed successively in $0 . 5 \%$ (w/v) SDS and water. Finally, the precipitate was washed once in $80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol and then freezedried under vacuum overnight. 

# Iodine staining and starch content determination in leaves

Flag leaves were harvested at the end of the day and the end of the night, decolorized in $80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol, and stained in Lugol solution (Sigma) for 

# OsLESV and OsESV1 promote starch biosynthesis

30 min. After staining, excess Lugol was removed by soaking the samples in ${ \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ for optimal visualization of the amylose–iodine complex in starch. To quantify starch levels, leaves were ground in liquid nitrogen and dried in an oven at ${ } ^ { 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ to constant weight. The total starch and amylose contents in the leaf samples were determined with a starch determination kit, Megazyme K-TSTA (Wicklow, Ireland), and an amylose determination kit, Megazyme K-AMYL. 

# Analysis of starch characteristics and physicochemical properties of endosperm

The total starch and amylose contents of mature endosperm were determined as described above. The protein and lipid contents were measured as described previously (Kang et al., 2005). The chain length distribution of amylopectin was determined by digesting 5 mg of endosperm flour with isozymes (Megazyme), followed by capillary electrophoresis (PA 800 Plus Drug Analysis System, Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA, USA). Starch pasting characteristics were determined by mixing 3 g of endosperm flour in an aluminum tank containing $2 5 \ \mathsf { m l }$ of distilled water and measured with a rapid viscosity analyzer (Newport Scientific, Narrabeen, NSW, Australia). For gelatinization temperature analysis, 5 mg of grain powder and $1 0 ~ \mu \up$ of distilled water were mixed in an aluminum cup, sealed, and analyzed using a differential scanning calorimeter (DSC1; Mettler Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland). 

# GBSS and PPDKB enzyme activity assays

Grains and flag leaves were harvested 10 DAF to measure enzyme activities. The GBSS Assay kit (Solarbio, Beijing, China) and PPDK Assay kit (Keming, Suzhou, China) were used to determine the enzyme activities of GBSS and PPDKB, respectively. 

# Plant protein extraction and western blot analysis

To extract total protein, grains and leaves were harvested at the end of the day at 10 DAF and homogenized in $1 . 5 \mathsf { m }$ of extraction buffer. The total protein was obtained from the supernatant by centrifugation. Equal amounts of protein were loaded onto an SDS–PAGE gel, transferred to a PVDF membrane, and immunoblotted with the appropriate antibodies. Soluble and insoluble proteins were extracted from leaves and grains as previously described (Seung et al. (2015). Soluble and insoluble proteins were loaded onto an SDS–PAGE gel for immunoblot analysis, with $\beta$ -actin as an internal control. 

# Cell-free degradation test

The cell-free degradation test was performed as described previously (Wang et al., 2020c). Briefly, $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of purified recombinant protein and ${ 3 0 0 ~ \mu \ g }$ of total protein extracted from grains or leaves collected at 10 DAF were incubated in PBS buffer at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ . The protein abundance of GBSSI, GBSSII, and PPDKB was determined by immunoblot, with $\beta$ -actin as the internal control. 

# Starch-binding assay

Recombinant protein was mixed with maize total starch and amylose, as well as potato amylopectin, in binding buffer for 40 min at room temperature and then centrifuged at $1 0 \ : 0 0 0 g$ for 3 min. The supernatant was retained, and the pellet was washed six times. After adding an equal volume of SDS loading buffer to the pellet and supernatant and boiling at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 min, the proteins of interest were detected by immunoblot. 

# Statistical analysis

Student’s t-test and one-way ANOVA, followed by Duncan’s multiple comparisons test, were performed using GraphPad Prism 9 software (https://www.graphpad.com), and detailed descriptions of the statistical analyses can be found in the figure legends.
