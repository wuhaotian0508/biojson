# The role of sterols in plant growth and development

# Abstract

Sterols found in all eukaryotic organisms are membrane components which regulate the fluidity and the permeability of phospholipid bilayers. Certain sterols in minute amounts, such as campesterol in Arabidopsis thaliana, are precursors of oxidized steroids acting as growth hormones collectively named brassinosteroids. The crucial importance of brassinosteroids upon growth and development has been established through the study of a set of dwarf mutants affected in brassinosteroid synthesis or perception. Some of these dwarfs are, in fact, deficient in the final steps of sterol biosynthesis and their developmental phenotypes are primarily caused by a depletion in the sterol precursor for brassinosteroids. Recently, the characterization of genes encoding sterol biosynthetic enzymes and the isolation of novel plant lines affected in the expression of those genes, either by insertional or classical mutagenesis, overexpression or cosuppression, have shed new light on the involvement of sterols in biological processes such as embryonic development, cell and plant growth, and fertility, which will be presented and discussed in this review article.

# 1. Introduction

Plant sterols have been extensively studied in past years with a major focus on biosynthetic and biochemical aspects [1,2]. Sterols are synthesized via the mevalonate pathway of isoprenoid metabolism [3]. Key enzymes of this pathway including HMGR (3-hydroxy-3-methyl glutaryl coenzyme A reductase) or SMT (sterol-C-methyltransferase) have been monitored in microsomal fractions of diverse plant tissues or organs suggesting that, in principle, most cell types are capable of producing the pathway end-products cholesterol, campesterol and sitosterol. Particularities of the plant sterol biosynthetic pathway have been described in comparison to yeast or mammalian pathways; specifically, plants cyclise 2,3-oxidosqualene into cycloartenol (and not lanosterol), whose 9b,19-cyclopropane ring is further metabolized owing to the enzymatic conversion of cycloeucalenol into obtusiofoliol [1,4,5] (Fig. 1). Further downstream of the pathway, 24-methylene lophenol is transformed into 24-ethylidene lophenol by a sterol-methyltransferase which confers to the green world the capability to produce 24-ethyl sterols as the predominant molecular species [6–8]. Plant sterols like other sterols are primary components of cellular membranes where they regulate fluidity and permeability. A minor proportion of these compounds serve as precursors to steroid derivatives, and were only recently recognized as a new class of plant growth regulators called brassinosteroids. The demonstration of such a role through biosynthetic and genetic approaches has been reviewed in excellent articles [9–12]. The characterization of sterol biosynthetic mutants in Arabidopsis thaliana was also recently reported [13–17]. Severe developmental alterations were observed and the interpretation of these sets of data have led to the idea that plant sterols might function in growth and development, a possibility that had largely been ignored.

This short review article summarizes what is known in this field. More generally, although the role of sterols in the cell biology has been widely explored, new functions for these molecules are continuously discovered, further illustrating their involvement in diverse aspects of metabolism and development, such as the role of cholesterol in synaptogenesis for instance [18].

# 2. Sterol distribution

Most of the higher plant sterols are found as free sterols (b-OH at C3) which serve as membrane components. A minute amount of sterols, predominantly campesterol and its epimer 22(23)-dihydrobrassicasterol, form the pool of brassinosteroid precursors [19]. A low-abundance class of sterols are esters linked to fatty acids [20]. The tobacco mutant sterov (sterol overaccumulation) and transgenic plants, both which exhibit higher HMGR enzyme activity, are characterized by a dramatic increase in the mevalonate flux resulting in an accumulation of sterols as cytoplasmic steryl esters found in lipid droplets [21–24]. The fatty acyl moieties of these esters are mainly C16:0, C18:1 and C18:2 (H. Schaller, unpublished results). Such an intra-cellular distribution of a metabolic surplus (biosynthetic intermediates and end-products) in so-called sterolosomes subsequently regulates the concentration of free sterols in the membranes, which is of great importance with respect to sterol homeostasis. Plants having such an hypersterolemia phenotype are however morphologically and developmentally very similar to the wildtype. In the wild type, steryl esters have, thus far, not been assigned to specific roles, either metabolic or biological. The clarification of this aspect of sterol metabolism will rely on the characterization of genes encoding sterol acyl transferase(s) and sterol acyl hydrolase(s) in plants. Steryl esters are found in wild-type Brassica napus in specialized cell types. Indeed, the lipid globuli of the elaioplasts found in tapetal cells were shown to contain steryl esters in addition to triglycerides [25,26]. An involvement of these esters in the structure or function of the pollen coat has been suggested [27]. The esters could serve to facilitate germination of the pollen tube on the stigmas of plants from the dry stigma-type as is the case for triglycerides [28] and as an adhesive component of the pollen coat for insect-mediated pollination.

A second low-abundance class of sterols constitutes sterol glucosides and sterol acyl glucosides [29,30]. These conjugated molecules comprise mainly $\Delta ^ { 5 }$ -sterols and are synthesized and located in the plasma membrane [31]; variations in their relative concentrations together with other lipids have been related to the adaptability of plants to low temperatures [32]. It has also been tentatively suggested that sterol glucosides might prime cellulose synthesis in the cotton fiber model [33]. Free and conjugated forms of sterols are distributed in most of the plant cell types. The regulation of the proportion of these two forms is, thus far, unknown but is most probably involved in a fine tuning of the concentration of free sterols in the plasma membrane [34].

# 3. Is there a functional organization of the sterol biosynthetic pathway in the model plant A. thaliana?

Campesterol and sitosterol are synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum via the mevalonate pathway leading to the production of the precursor squalene. Post-squalene plant sterol metabolism has been documented in detail in review articles [35–37] and biochemistry of certain enzymes has been carefully examined [38–40]. The biosynthetic scheme which is given in Fig. 1 may be divided into three units according to the sterol composition of plants in which genetic lesion(s) result in a truncated pathway (replacement of pathway end-products by structurally distant intermediates) or a pathway leading to novel proportions of the end-products. Biosynthetic needs for plant viability sets the limit between an upstream and a downstream metabolic unit at the isomerization of the $\Delta ^ { 8 }$ into a $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -double bond by a sterol isomerase [41], (Fig. 1) followed by $\mathrm { C } 4 \alpha \cdot$ -demethylation [42]. Indeed, biosynthetic mutants in the upstream or the downstream units display radically distinct types of developmental phenotypes. Severely impaired

plant development leading to mortality characterizes mutants from the upstream unit prior to the $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -double bond; viable mutants classified in a downstream metabolic unit have novel developmental traits depicted in the next paragraphs. Furthermore, the orientation of post-squalene metabolic fluxes towards the 24-desmethyl sterol (cholesterol-type side chain), the 24-methyl sterol (campesterol-type side chain), and the 24-ethylsterol (sitosterol-type side chain) biosynthetic segments (Fig. 1) through the expression of Sterol methyltransferases represents a third transverse metabolic unit controlling developmental aspects in plants.

# 4. Sterol mutants in the early biosynthetic steps

Mutants of the FACKEL locus were isolated by different approaches independently in two laboratories [15,43]. Ell (fk-J79) was isolated in a T-DNA mutagenesis experiment in which seeds were germinated in the dark for 12 days then illuminated with white light. Seedlings which displayed a constitutive cytokinin response were selected [43]. The mutant shows an extra-long-lifespan and a pleiotropic effect on development including an altered pattern of divisions in the embryonic apical-basal axis, aberrant morphological patterns at both the seedling and whole plant level, a severe dwarf phenotype, quasi-sterility, and delayed senescence for individuals displaying nonlethal post-embryonic development. Another trait of ell is an impaired vascular development which comes from an incomplete provascular cell elongation during embryogenesis [43]. Other alleles of fackel were isolated in a screen for mutations affecting the body organization of arabidopsis seedlings and/or embryos [44]. Six fackel alleles induced in LER ecotype by X-rays or EMS mutagenesis show a common major characteristic of defective cell divisions in embryogenesis temporally located at the globular stage [15]. Molecular analysis of the mutants identified FACKEL as the sterol-14-reductase [15,43], with a detailed sterol analysis reported by Schrick et al. [15]. Fackel seedlings accumulated more than $21 \%$ of total sterols as intermediates with a conjugated $\Delta ^ { 8 , 1 4 }$ -dienic structure, thus demonstrating a biosynthetic block at the $\Delta ^ { 1 4 }$ -reduction step. This accumulation resulted in a decreased amount of $\Delta ^ { 5 }$ -sterols, especially campesterol, which was detected in trace quantities. The amount of sitosterol in the mutant was not as affected. Jang et al. used a gas chromatography-selected ion monitoring technique on ell (fk-J79) and measured a substantial reduction in campesterol and also a marked decrease in the bioactive brassinosteroids castasterone and brassinolide [43]. Biochemical complementation of fackel with brassinosteroids during germination failed to restore a wild-type phenotype indicating that the lack of these molecules was not the primary cause of the growth defect which has been located in embryonic development. This observation is in accordance with the importance of brassinosteroids in post-embryonic development [10–12]. The failure of the sterol biochemical complementation experiments can be attributed to the complications related to the lipophilic nature of sterols adversely affecting their bioavailability upon administration.

Accumulation of $\Delta ^ { 8 , 1 4 }$ -sterols in plant cells also occurs after treatment with the sterol analog inhibitor 15-azasterol [45,46]. Arabidopsis germinated in the presence of 15-azasterol showed an extremely stunted development undergoing rapid arrest (Fig. 2A) with a concomitant accumulation of $71 \%$ of $\Delta ^ { 8 , 1 4 }$ -sterols (H. Schaller, Table 1). # 5. Sterol mutants between the $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -reduction step and pathway end-products

Mutants affected in the downstream biosynthetic unit of the sterol pathway were isolated on the basis of their dwarfism. Genetic lesions in the genes encoding $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -sterol-C5(6)-desaturase (STE1/ $D W A R F 7 / B U L I )$ [13,47–49], $\Delta ^ { 5 , 7 }$ -sterol- $\cdot \Delta ^ { 7 }$ -reductase (DWARF5) [50] and $\Delta ^ { 2 4 }$ -sterol-isomerase/ reductase $( D I M / D W A R F I / L K B I )$ [14,51,52] (Fig. 1) were characterized. In Arabidopsis or in pea, several alleles of these genes resulted in altered growth and development. The mutants are dwarfs that share a number of typical traits with the brassinosteroid biosynthetic dwarfs cpd [53] and dwarf4 [54,55] including short stems, reduced fertility, prolonged life cycle and the round curled leaves of the cabbage phenotype [56] (Fig. 3). The C5(6)-desaturase mutants accumulated $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -sterols ( $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -campesterol and $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -sitosterol), up to $60 \%$ of the total sterols in the case of the weak allele ste1 [13] and up to $9 0 \%$ in the case of bul1 and dwarf 7 [49]. The almost total absence of detectable amounts of campesterol in bul1 and dwarf 7 results in a lack of brassinosteroid synthesis mandatory for growth [48].

Biochemical complementation of dwarf 7 [48] or bul1 [49] after treatment with nanomolar concentrations of brassinolide was achieved in the case of germinating seedlings and inflorescences, where hypocotyl and pedicel length, respectively, were monitored. # 6. The ratio of cholesterol, campesterol and sitosterol

Both 24-methyl cholesterol [campesterol and its 24S-epimer 22(23)-dihydrobrassicasterol] and 24-ethyl cholesterol (sitosterol) are major pathway end-products of the sterol pathway in most higher plants. The proportions of cholesterol, campesterol and sitosterol are determined by the activity of sterol methyltransferases which catalyse the transfer of two carbon atoms from S-adenosyl methionine first to the C24 position then to the $C 2 4 ^ { 1 }$ position on the sterol side chain in a non consecutive reaction sequence. There are two classes of sterol methyltransferases, SMT1 and SMT2. SMT1 catalyses the methylation of cycloartenol to yield 24-methylene cycloartanol whereas SMT2 is responsible for the methylation of 24-methylene lophenol to yield 24-ethylidene lophenol (Fig. 1) [63–65]. The model plant Arabidopsis contains three genes encoding sterol methyl transferases : At SMT1, At SMT2:1 and At SMT2:2. A screen of transposon-mutagenized seed pools for plants displaying abnormal root system formation on agar plates led to the isolation of the smt1 null mutants [16]. Four alleles of smt1 shared features of reduced growth and stunted siliques. The observation of poor fertility was not the result of altered fertilization but rather was attributed to a high proportion of aborted embryos caused by a recessive maternal defect, based on reciprocal crosses between wild-type and smt1. The smt1 pollen was able to fertilize wild-type ovules but, conversely, wild-type pollen on smt1 stigmas failed to produce seeds [16].

Sterol analysis of smt1 lines was performed at the whole plant level and showed an accumulation of cholesterol $2 6 \%$ compared to $6 \%$ in the wild-type) at the expense of sitosterol $2 2 \%$ compared to $4 8 \%$ in the wild-type). The accumulation of cholesterol, but not of cycloartenol (substrate of SMT1) (Fig. 1), in smt1 Arabidopsis leaves indicated that cycloartenol can undergo enzymatic reactions subsequent to SMT1 in the sterol pathway. In other words, the production of high amounts of cholesterol in Arabidopsis results from a by-pass of SMT1. Such a by-pass was

not present in smt1 tobacco obtained via transgenesis. Antisense or co-suppressed tobacco plants were shown to accumulate cycloartenol, (but not cholesterol) indicating that, for this plant species, there was a strong substrate specificity in the successive biosynthetic steps [8]. The presence of campesterol and sitosterol in smt1 Arabidopsis null mutants suggests SMT2 was capable in planta of methylating cycloartenol. This additional biochemical function for SMT2 has been also observed in the case of tobacco overexpressing At SMT2:1. Microsomes from such plants displayed elevated SMT2 enzyme activity as well as elevated SMT1 enzyme activity in similar proportions [8,66]. The unchanged level of campesterol in smt1 plants compared with the wild-type most probably excludes a modified brassinosteroid biosynthetic capability or concentration level in smt1. Therefore, the growth and development pattern of smt1 null mutants is likely to be due to the altered ratio of cholesterol to sitosterol. Complementation of smt1 with SMT1 constructs was successfully achieved [16].

In order to evaluate the physiological significance of a defined ratio of campesterol to sitosterol, a transgenic approach using an At SMT2:1 cDNA cloned behind a 35S promoter led to the characterization of overexpression or cosuppression of At SMT2:1 [17]. A selective modification of the sterol profile was observed in the case of either an increase or a decrease of the SMT 2 :1 message. This modification affected essentially two molecular species of the pathway end-products mixture, campesterol and sitosterol, as was the case for the modulated ratio of cholesterol to sitosterol in smt1. Plants overexpressing SMT2:1 accumulate sitosterol at the expense of campesterol and thus display a novel balance between the 24-methyl and 24-ethyl segments of the pathway (Fig. 1). These fertile plants had a reduced stature (Fig. 3) due to a reduction of cell length but could be rescued by exogenous 24-epibrassinolide. It was therefore proposed that the growth defect was due to a lack of sufficient amounts of campesterol as precursor to brassinosteroids. In fact, high sitosterol plants are of reduced size with respect to the wild-type with a maximum reduction of $5 0 \%$ of plant height. As such, these plants do not display the typical dwarfism of brassinosteroid mutants.

The Arabidopsis lines characterized by a cosuppressed SMT2:1 have a remarkably high campesterol content and low sitosterol content (Table 1) demonstrating the predominance of the 24-methyl sterol pathway [17]. Biochemical characterization of these mutants has shown that the proportion of the two epimers of 24-methyl cholesterol, namely, campesterol and 22(23)- dihydrobrassicasterol (determined by ‘H-NMR of a HPLC-purified 24-methyl cholesterol peak), were unchanged compared to the wild-type or to low campesterol plants (H. Schaller, unpublished results). High campesterol plants show a complex developmental phenotype consisting of a reduced but not dwarf stature (Fig. 3) with a cell length comparable to wild-type, an increased branching and proliferation of secondary stems, an altered flower morphology with serrated edges of petals and sepals, and a very low fertility [17]. A ‘sterol map’ of low and high campesterol plants has been created based on the ratio of campesterol to sitosterol throughout the plant tissues and organs [17]. The important aspect of such an analysis is that certain organs or tissues like flowers and siliques are far more responsive to a novel sterol profile than others such as stems. Indeed, sexual reproduction is highly affected when the ratio of campesterol to sitosterol differs by a factor of 10, and yet vegetative growth such as stem elongation is only moderately affected when the ratio of campesterol to sitosterol differs by a factor 30 to the wild-type. Finally, the isolation and characterization of smt1 and smt2:1 Arabidopsis lines demonstrates that the ratio of 24-alkyl sterols must be properly balanced in order to fit the sterol needs of the plant cell with respect to growth requirements.

# 7. Concluding remarks

The biochemical and biological characterization of a set of plant mutants distributed along the post-squalene biosynthetic segment of the sterol pathway has provided the experimental basis to broaden our knowledge regarding sterol function in development. The lethality of fackel at very early developmental stages indicates that sterols from the downstream metabolic unit are essential cell components. In addition, the abnormal embryonic cell division patterning leading to abberrant morphology of cotyledons, hypocotyl, radicle and meristems of fackel led to the emerging idea that some sterols serve as signalling and/or regulatory molecules for normal development. From the ‘membrane environment’ point of view, an appropriate composition of sterols in the cell membranes is crucial for optimal enzymatic activity, ion and metabolite transport or channelling, protein–protein and protein–lipid interactions, signal transduction and finally to face fluctuating environmental conditions. Experimental data on the genetic modulation of the plasma membrane $\mathrm { H } ^ { + }$ -ATPase has proven the importance of membrane components with respect to development [67]. A similar modulation of the proton pump activity was also achieved by selectively modifying the sterol composition of plant membranes [68].

The characterization of viable mutants associated with the downstream biosynthetic unit of the sterol biosynthetic pathway, from $\Delta ^ { 7 }$ -sterol intermediates to $\Delta ^ { 5 }$ -sterols, has raised the important question of the respective impact of sterols and of brassinosteroids on the phenotype of the adult plant. The effect of brassinosteroids on cell elongation processes has been documented [55] and partly shown to be responsible for the reduced fertility of the mutants due to lack of stamen elongation [49]. However, observations gathered from the work with diminuto, bul1 and smt1 and smt2:1 suggest an important role for sterols in plant biology. High campesterol mutants (smt2:1) as well as low campesterol mutants (SMT2:1 overexpressors) are quantitatively modified in their brassinosteroid-precursor pool of campesterol but do not display the typical skotomorphogenesis/ photomorphogenesis response of post-campesterol brassinosteroid biosynthetic mutants or brassinosteroid receptor-kinase BRI 1 mutants [69]. These mutants are of reduced size (though not dwarfs) indicating qualitative sterol requirements for optimal vegetative growth in terms of the relative proportions of cholesterol, campesterol and sitosterol. The rescue of dim and bul1 with exogenously applied brassinosteroids was partially successful but led to mostly infertile plants. High phenotypic similarity was observed with smt1 and smt2:1 mutants. It is likely that their reduced fertility was not due to a lack of brassinosteroids because these plants were not impaired in the post-campesterol biosynthetic segment as previously stated. The reproductive plant sterol phenotype is therefore related to the altered ratio of pathway end-sterols. Further experiments will help to elucidate the roles of sterols during the embryonic, post-embryonic and flowering stages in model plants in an effort to clearly distinguish the direct effects sterols have on these processes from the indirect effects related to modified protein function in an altered membrane environment.

# 8. Note added

The implication of plant sterols in multiple aspects of plant development has been further documented very recently in the model species Arabidopsis thaliana.

The mutant hydra1 was shown to carry a defect in the gene encoding the $\Delta ^ { 8 } { - } \Delta ^ { 7 } .$ -sterol isomerase; its severe embryonic and post-embryonic morphogenetic inhibitions were proposed to be linked to constitutive alterations of hormone perception and signalling indicating that the mutant was defective in membrane-dependent processes [70]. A genetic study of the sterol biosynthetic pathway using crosses between mutants belonging to either one or the other metabolic units described in this article presented the interactions occurring between steroidogenic proteins [71]. The characterization of the cvp1 mutant isolated on the basis of an altered cotyledon vascular patterning led to the identification of the genetic defect in SMT2;1 causing an accumulation of campesterol as described in Ref. [17] of the present article.
