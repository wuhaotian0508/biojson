# Summary

It is well known that the overall quality of japonica/geng rice is superior to that of indica/xian rice varieties. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying the quality disparities between these two subspecies of rice are still largely unknown. In this study, we have pinpointed a gene homologous to SLR1, termed LCG1, exhibiting significant expression during early caryopsis development and playing a specific role in regulating rice chalkiness and taste by affecting the accumulation of grain storage components, starch granule structure and chain length distribution of amylopectin. LCG1 physically interacts with OsBP5 and indirectly influences the expression of the amylose synthesis gene Waxy (Wx) by hindering the transcriptional activity of the OsBP5/OsEBP89 complex. Notably, sequence variations in the promoter region of LCG1 result in enhanced transcription in japonica rice accessions. This leads to elevated LCG1 expression in $\mathsf { C S S L - } L C G \uparrow ^ { \mathsf { N i p } }$ , thereby enhancing rice quality. Our research elucidates the molecular mechanism underlying the impact of the LCG1-OsBP5/OsEBP89-Wx regulatory pathway on rice chalkiness and taste quality, offering new genetic resources for improving the indica rice quality. 

# Introduction

Rice stands as a pivotal crop, meeting the daily energy demands of over half the global population (Wing et al., 2018). Recent strides in economic growth and living standards have spurred a growing inclination towards premium rice among consumers. The quality of rice grains encompasses various facets including appearance, milling, cooking, taste, and nutritional attributes (Yang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2009). Of these, customers and markets favour rice varieties boasting outstanding appearance marked by uniform shape and a translucent endosperm (Zhou et al., 2015). However, grain chalkiness, characterized by the opaque portion within the translucent endosperm due to altered and loosely packed starch granules (SGs) and/or protein bodies (PBs) (Yao et al., 2020), emerges as a significant concern. This undesirable trait not only impacts consumers’ initial impression but also detrimentally influences milling, cooking, and tasting qualities (Misra et al., 2020). Grain chalkiness has become a significant challenge in assessing the quality of rice varieties worldwide, exacerbated by factors (Misra et al., 2019; Sreenivasulu et al., 2015), with such as global warming and high-yielding breeding practices. Consequently, mitigating grain chalkiness has emerged as a paramount objective in rice quality improvement breeding efforts. 

Grain chalkiness is a complex quantitative trait influenced by both environmental conditions and multiple genetic factors. Adverse environmental factors such as shading (Deng et al., 2018, 2021), high temperatures (Ren et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020), low moisture (Wada et al., 2014), and nutrient deficiencies (Wei et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2015) can significantly 

contribute to chalkiness by disrupting enzymic activity involved in starch and protein biosynthesis. Despite the identification of numerous trait loci (QTLs) associated with chalkiness on all 12 rice chromosomes through studies on natural populations, the cloning of chalkiness-related genes remains a formidable challenge (Gao et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2009). Only a handful of significant chalkiness genes, including Chalk5 and WCR1, has been successfully cloned (Li et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2022). Chalk5, the first gene cloned through forward genetics, encodes a vacuolar $\mathsf { H } ( + )$ -translocating pyrophosphatase (V-PPase) and plays a role in cellular endomembrane trafficking, impacting the percentage of grain chalkiness (PGC) (Li et al., 2014). Similarly, WCR1 (WHITE-CORE RATE1), encoding an F-box protein, has been found to negatively regulate endosperm chalkiness by reducing reactive oxygen species production and delaying programmed cell death, ultimately enhancing grain quality. Notably, the allele WCR1A with higher affinity to its transcriptional activator OsDOF17 is associated with lower chalkiness due to increased transcriptional levels (Wu et al., 2022). Furthermore, various genes affecting chalkiness have been identified through reverse genetics, including those involved in starch (Ryoo et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2016), protein (Ren et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2012), and lipid (Wang et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2020), biosynthesis, as well as transcription factors regulating the expression of storage material genes (Feng et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2016). Chalkiness can also be influenced by disruptions in sugar transporter function (Sun et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2018), and imbalances in energy metabolism (Cai et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2020). Additionally, rice 

grain shape plays a crucial role in appearance quality, with genes such as GW7, GW8, GS9, and GS3 positively impacting grain appearance (Liu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2012, 2015). Despite advancements, the molecular mechanisms governing chalkiness formation in rice remain largely elusive, necessitating further investigations into this intricate process. 

The GRAS family comprises transcription factors unique to plants, named after the first three proteins discovered: GIBBER-ELLIC ACID INSENSITIVE (GAI), REPRESSOR of GAI (RGA), and SCARECROW (SCR) (Bolle, 2004). GRAS proteins typically consist of a variable N-terminal domain and a highly conserved C-terminal GRAS domain, housing five conserved motifs: LHRI, VHIID, LHRII, PFYRE, and SAW (Hofmann, 2016). These proteins are known to participate in various physiological processes including plant growth, development, signal transduction, and responses to environmental stresses (Bolle, 2004; Golldack et al., 2013). DELLA proteins, a subfamily of GRAS proteins, feature a DELLA/TVHYNP domain at the N-terminus and a C-terminal LHR motif facilitating interactions with other proteins. In Arabidopsis, five DELLA proteins act as inhibitors in gibberellin (GA) signalling pathways, regulating plant growth and development (Marın-de la Rosa et al., 2015; Navarro et al., 2008; Stewart Lilley et al., 2013). Rice possesses SLENDER RICE1 (SLR1), a typical DELLA protein, with mutants exhibiting distinct phenotypes due to GA insensitivity (Ikeda et al., 2001; Itoh et al., 2002). Rice also contains two SLR1 homologues, SLRL1 and SLRL2, with C-terminal domains highly similar to SLR1 but lacking the N-terminal DELLA domain (Itoh et al., 2005). Overexpressing SLRL1 in the slr1 mutant background can rescue the elongated seedling phenotype, indicating that SLRL1 is also a GA signalling inhibitor (Itoh et al., 2005). Recently, SLRL2, with a highly similar amino acid sequence to SLRL1, has been reported to interact with NF-YB1 to co-regulate the key granule-bound starch synthase gene Wx and positively regulate rice taste quality (Wang et al., 2024). The regulatory module, NF-YB1-SLRL2-bHLH144, is central to mediating ABA regulation of rice quality. 

In this study, we identified LCG1 as a key gene contributing to chalkiness in rice through map-based cloning, which is identical to the recently reported SLRL2 gene. Our research further reveals that LCG1 exhibits specific expression during early caryopsis development, and loss of function results in altered grain nutrient content and amylopectin structure, ultimately leading to increased chalkiness and diminished taste quality. LCG1 engages with the transcriptional regulatory complex OsBP5/OsEBP89, hindering its transcriptional activation of the Wx gene responsible for amylose synthesis, consequently regulating the amylose content (AC) in the endosperm. This mechanism directly influences the chalkiness and taste quality of rice. Analysis of natural variation unveiled superior haplotypes H3 and H5, predominantly found in japonica rice, which exhibit significant reductions in rice chalkiness and hold promise for enhancing the appearance and taste quality of indica rice varieties. Our findings shed light on the genetic underpinnings of chalkiness formation and offer novel gene targets for breeding improvement efforts. 

# Results

# Map-based cloning of LCG1

In our study, we screened a series of chromosome segment substitution lines (CSSLs) developed in our laboratory, using the indica rice variety 9311 as the recipient parent and Nipponbare as 

the donor parent (Tu et al., 2022). Through this screening process, we identified a CSSL, named Q307 which exhibited a notable reduction in chalkiness compared with 9311 (Figure 1b). Remarkably, Q307 showed no significant differences in main agronomic traits such as plant height, tiller number, heading date, seed setting rate, and grain shape when compared with 9311 (Figures 1c–g and S1b–e). However, both the chalky grain percentage (CGP) and chalky grain degree (CGD) of Q307 were substantially lower than those of 9311 (Figure 1h,i). Genome sequencing revealed that Q307 carried a single genomic segment from Nipponbare on chromosome 5 (Figures 1j and S1a). Consequently, we designated the causal gene responsible for reducing chalkiness in Nipponbare as Less Chalk Grain1 (LCG1). 

Through screening the exchanged chromosomal substitution lines in the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ segregation population (comprising more than 8000 individual plants) and assessing grain appearance quality, we successfully mapped the candidate gene to a 33.86 Kb interval between markers M541 and M543 (Table S1). Within this interval, gene annotation revealed eight coding genes. However, based on the previous transcriptome sequencing data, only LOC_Os05g49930 (SLRL2) showed high expression level in filling caryopsis (Figure S2a). Additionally, we observed multiple sequence polymorphisms between 9311 and Nipponbare in this locus, including an amino acids switch (Figure S2b). To verify whether the SNPs in the promoter region might alter promoter activity, qRT-PCR analysis was carried out and revealed higher expression of this candidate gene in filling caryopsis of CSSL-Q307 compared with 9311(Figure S2d), a finding further confirmed by a dual-luciferase reporter gene assay (Figure S2e–g). Further examination of sequences near these SNPs revealed the presence of multiple SNPs within predicted cis-acting elements (Table S2), which could potentially affect the binding of transcription factors, thereby regulating the expression of the LCG1 gene. 

# Confirmation of the LCG1 gene

To validate whether LOC_Os05g49930 serves as the causal gene of LCG1, we performed knockout experiments in the japonica variety, Zhonghua 11 (ZH11) background. We generated three homozygous mutant lines with 1 bp insertion, 2 bp deletion, and 5 bp deletion, respectively, all resulting in frameshift mutations (Figures 2g and S3). However, other agronomic traits showed no noticeable changes (Figures 2a–c,h–j and S4). 

To further validate the function of LCG1, we introduced a $r 3 . 6 ~ { \sf k b }$ genomic fragment of LCG1, inclusive of the promoter region (Figure S5a), into the knockout mutants, thereby generating complemented transgenic plants. # LCG1 affects the accumulation of grain storage components

To explore the anatomical differences underlying the pronounced chalky grain phenotype in LCG1 mutants, samples of developing caryopsis at 6 DAP from the wild-type and LCG1 knockout mutants were collected for semi-thin section observation. It was observed that $L C G 1  – K O 1$ and $L C G 1  – K O 2$ mutants accumulated fewer SGs with larger volumes in the central region of the endosperm (Figure 4a–c). Scanning electron microscope observation revealed that the SGs in the endosperm of mature seeds from all three KO mutants were spherical and loosely arranged (Figure 4d–g). These findings suggest that the developing caryopsis of LCG1 knockout mutants exhibits abnormalities characterized by slow starch filling and loosely packed SGs.

# Loss-function of LCG1 leads to deterioration in taste quality

The taste quality of rice is intricately linked to AC, amylopectin chain length distribution and the presence of storage proteins and lipids. Given the altered accumulation of grain storage components in the LCG1 mutants, we further explored the impact of LCG1 loss on rice taste quality. Cooked rice from the three KO mutants exhibited a lacklustre appearance (Figure 6a–d). Notably, comprehensive scores for taste quality, appearance, adhesiveness, and balance were significantly diminished (Figure 6f–j), while hardness markedly increased (Figure 6k). The results from Rapid Visco Analyser (RVA) revealed a distinct RVA profile in the KO 

mutants compared with wild-type ZH11 (Figure 6e). Specifically, peak viscosity and breakdown values were notably lower in the KO mutants (Figure 6l,n), while setback values were higher (Figure 6m), with the setback value surpassing the breakdown value, indicative of poorer taste quality. Additionally, we evaluated the taste quality of CSSL-Q307 and 9311. In contrast to the mutant assessment, CSSL-Q307 harbouring the LCG1 allele from Nipponbare exhibited significantly superior taste quality compared with 9311 (Table S3). Taken together, these findings suggest that introducing the genomic fragment of Nipponbare containing LCG1 gene can simultaneously reduce grain chalkiness and enhance the taste quality of rice.

# LCG1 hinders the transcriptional activation of the OsBP5/OsEBP89 complex on the Wx gene

Previous research has demonstrated that OsBP5 interacts with the EREBP family transcription factor OsEBP89 to form a complex that activates the expression of Wx, thus regulating amylose synthesis. To further investigate the relationship between LCG1 and OsBP5, we co-transfected tobacco leaves with LCG1, OsBP5, OsEBP89, and proWxb ::LUC. Compared with the negative control, OsBP5 and OsEBP89 effectively activated Wx expression. However, the addition of LCG1 significantly inhibited Wx expression (Figure 7e,f), indicating that LCG1 affects the transcriptional activation of the OsBP5- OsEBP89 complex. Similar results were obtained when protoplasts were transformed with LCG1, OsBP5, OsEBP89, and proWxb ::LUC (Figure 7g). To analyse the effect of LCG1 on Wx expression in vivo, caryopsis were sampled at 10, 15, and 20 DAP to test Wx gene expression. The results showed that Wx was significantly upregulated after the loss of LCG1 function (Figure 7h), suggesting that LCG1 might regulate Wx gene expression in rice by inhibiting the OsBP5-OsEBP89 transcriptional complex. 

# Natural variation in LCG1

To investigate the relationship between grain chalkiness and nucleotide polymorphisms at the LCG1 locus, we utilized the core rice germplasm resources assembled by our lab comprising 378 rice varieties collected worldwide. The genomes of these 378 rice varieties were analysed to identify superior haplotypes of LCG1. In the 2-kb upstream promoter region, fifteen polymorphisms were detected, including 2 insertion/deletions (Indels) and thirteen single-nucleotides polymorphisms (SNPs) (Figure 8a). These sequence variations potentially alter transcription factor binding sites and may impact promoter activity (Table S2). Additionally, we identified two SNPs in the open reading frame, one of which resulting in amino acid substitutions (Figure 8a). 

Based on the sequence variations of LCG1 in the 378 core rice germplasm resources, five haplotypes were identified and named Hap1-Hap5, respectively (Figure 8a). Hap1, Hap2, and Hap4 are primarily found in indica rice varieties, accounting for approximately $7 4 . 1 9 \%$ , $8 8 . 1 1 \%$ , and $6 5 . 9 5 \%$ of the varieties in these haplotypes. Hap3 and Hap5 are mainly found in japonica rice varieties, accounting for approximately $9 5 . 8 3 \%$ and $8 8 . 2 3 \%$ , respectively (Figure 8b). Based on the CGP and CGD phenotype and nucleotide variations, we found that the accessions of Hap3 and Hap5, which are mainly japonica varieties, had lower CGP and CGD values compared with those of Hap1 and Hap2 (Figure 8c,d). Accordingly, 9311 carries the $L C G 1 ^ { \mathsf { H a p 1 } }$ allele, while Nipponbare harbours the $L C G \mathcal { I } ^ { \mathsf { H a p 3 } }$ allele. 

Furthermore, these five haplotypes of LCG1 display specific geographical distribution patterns. The inferior Hap1 and Hap2 haplotypes, associated with higher chalkiness, are primarily distributed in tropical regions at lower latitudes. In contrast, the low-chalkiness elite Hap3 haplotype is distributed in traditional high-quality rice planting areas such as East Asia and Europe, whereas Hap5 is predominantly found in Brazil. # Discussion

In rice, starch, protein, and lipids constitute the three main storage components in the endosperm, and their synthesis is tightly regulated by genetic factors. An imbalance in the

We infer that LCG1 precisely regulates the grain-filling process by inhibiting the expression of genes related to amylose and amylopectin synthesis at the early grain-filling stage, thus ensuring an orderly grain-filling process. Consistently, LCG1-KO mutants exhibited a significant increase in SG volume and surface (Figure 5c,d) accompanied by a reduction in the number of starch particles resulting in larger gaps between SGs (Figure 4a–g). In summary, LCG1 regulates the expression of genes related to starch and protein synthesis in the endosperm, influencing the accumulation of storage components and the regularity of SGs, ultimately contributing to grain chalkiness in rice. 

Rice starch is composed of amylose and amylopectin, with varying content and physicochemical properties among varieties, resulting in differences in gelatinization temperature, gel consistency, and taste quality (Li and Gilbert, 2018; Peng et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2009). The Wx gene plays a crucial role in amylose synthesis in the endosperm being regulated by several transcription factors (Zhang et al., 2019). These include the heterotrimeric complex formed by OsNF-YB1 with bHLH144 and OsNF-YC12, the heterodimeric complex formed by OsBP5 with OsEBP89, the complex formed by OsNAC24 with OsNAP, and the heterodimeric complex formed by OsMADS14 with OsNF-YB1 (Bello et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2003). The recently published work on SLRL2 gene (same to LCG1) by Wang et al. discovered that SLRL2 regulates the expression of the key granule-bound starch synthase gene Wx by modulating transcriptional activity of the NF-YB1-YC12-bHLH144, ultimately positively regulating rice taste quality (Wang et al., 2024). Our research cloned the candidate gene LCG1, which interacts with OsBP5 (Figure 7a–c) and negatively regulates the expression of the Wx gene by inhibiting the activity of the OsBP5-OsEBP89 complex. This inhibition is corroborated by the upregulation of Wx expression in developing caryopsis of LCG1-KO mutants (Figure 7f,g). Consequently, LCG1 modulates the expression of the Wx gene by suppressing the OsBP5-OsEBP89 module (Figure 7h), impacting amylose synthesis 

and consequently affecting rice taste quality. These two independent studies complement each other, providing a comprehensive analysis of the molecular regulatory network of SLRL2/LCG1 on taste quality. 

Enhancing grain quality stands as a primary objective in contemporary rice breeding endeavours, wherein chalkiness and taste quality serve as pivotal benchmarks for assessing superior rice varieties. Thus, delving into elite gene reservoirs that concurrently enhance chalkiness and taste quality and integrating them into rice quality enhancement efforts represents a crucial avenue for future research. Previous investigations across multiple studies have underscored the substantial impact of both the expression level of the Wx gene and the functionality of its protein on rice’s taste quality and chalkiness formation (Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021). Among existing high-quality varieties, the prevalent $W x ^ { \mathrm { b } }$ allele, which governs moderate to low levels of amylose, predominates. Nevertheless, significant variation persists in apparent AC among these varieties, constituting the primary cause for taste quality disparities. Consequently, further finetuning the expression of Wx in rice varieties harbouring the $W x ^ { \mathrm { { b } } }$ allele to regulate AC around $1 5 \%$ can yield benefits for enhancing grain taste quality (Isshiki et al., 1998; Shao et al., 2020). In this study, haplotype analysis unveiled that rice accessions of Hap3 and Hap5, primarily comprising japonica accessions, exhibit lower CGP and CGD compared with the indica-dominant Hap1 and Hap2 haplotypes, indicative of a distinct segregation between indica and japonica accessions in LCG1. Sequence comparison elucidated that LCG1 aligns with the Hap1 haplotype associated with high chalkiness in indica rice 9311, while in Nipponbare (japonica), LCG1 aligns with the Hap3 haplotype linked to low chalkiness. The expression level of CSSL-Q307 in developing caryopsis significantly surpasses that of 9311 (Figure S2d), and the promoter activity of $L C G \mathcal { I } ^ { \mathrm { N i p } }$ surpasses that of LCG19311 $L C G \thinspace 1 ^ { 9 3 1 1 }$ (Figure S2g) effectively reducing rice chalkiness. More crucially, LCG1 finely modulates the expression of the Wx gene by inhibiting the transcriptional activity of the OsBP5/ OsEBP89 complex, thereby further managing the AC in grains (Figures 7a–c,f–h and 8f,g), which is a key regulatory locus for taste quality disparities between indica and japonica subspecies and among rice varieties carrying the superior allele $W x ^ { \mathrm { { b } } }$ . The linked molecular markers $L C G \mathcal { I } ^ { \mathrm { N i p } }$ can be used for the molecular design of high-quality rice breeding. The isolated near-isogenic line containing $L C G \mathcal { I } ^ { \mathrm { N i p } }$ can be directly used as an improved twoline restorer line for two-line hybrid rice variety cultivation. Hence, dissecting the molecular mechanisms governing how LCG1 regulates grain chalkiness formation and exploring elite alleles for rice quality, particularly under the unique ecological conditions of “few sunny days, high humidity, and low diurnal temperature variation” in Southwest China, holds paramount significance for breeding high-yield, high-quality rice varieties. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The CSSL-Q307 line was derived from the backcross population BC4F1, wherein the indica rice variety 9311 served as the recipient parent and the japonica rice variety Nipponbare as the donor parent (Tu et al., 2022). For the generation of LCG1 knock-out mutants and pro35S::LCG1-HA transgenic plants, the japonica rice variety ZH11 was used for transformation. All genetic materials utilized in this study, including CSSLs, wild type 

and mutants, were cultivated in experimental fields of Sichuan Agricultural University at Chengdu (N 30.41°, E103.49°) and Lingshui (N $1 8 . 5 1 ^ { \circ }$ , E110.04°) under natural conditions. Each material was planted in four rows with ten plants per row at a planting density of $2 6 . 7 \times 1 6 . 7 c m$ , using standard rice field management techniques. 

# Trait measurements

Upon full maturation of the seeds, five plants from the middle of each plot were selected to assess agronomic traits, including plant height, tiller number, effective panicle number, panicle length, kernel weight per panicle (KGW), grain weight (GW), grain length (GL), chalkiness grain percentage (CGP), and chalkiness grain degree (CGD) were determined using the WanShen SC-E rice appearance quality testing system. Data processing and t-tests were conducted using Excel 2010. Taste quality was evaluated using a taste meter (STA1B, SATAKE, Japan) (Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022). RVA measurement was performed utilizing a Rapid Visco Analyser (model Micro Visco-Amylo-Graph, Germany) (Chen et al., 2022). The SG size distribution was measured using a Malvern laser particle size analyser, Mastersizer 3000 (Malvern Instruments Ltd, Worcestershire, UK). Additionally, the chain length distribution of amylopectin was analysed utilizing an electrochemical detection method with a Thermo ICS5000 ion chromatography system $1 { \mathsf { C S 5 0 + } } _ { \cdot }$ , Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

# Mapping-based cloning

For finely mapping the LCG1 locus, we devised new molecular markers (Indels) utilizing the reference sequences of 9311 and Nipponbare to discern exchanged plants harbouring distinct fragments in the backcross segregating population. The ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 5 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 2 }$ population, comprising over 8000 individuals, was cultivated in Wenjiang, Chengdu, in 2020. Subsequently, the expression profiles of candidate genes within the interval were scrutinized using RNAseq data obtained from various tissues of the indica rice variety, Shuhui R527. 

# Transgene construct preparation and rice transformation

The knock-out vector targeting LCG1 via CRISPR/Cas9 was constructed following established protocols (Ma et al., 2015). To generate the fusion overexpression vector, a $1 . 7 – 1 \mathrm { { k b } }$ promoter fragment from ZH11 was fused with a $1 . 5 – \mathrm { k b }$ coding region and subsequently inserted into the HA-tagged vector pCAMBIA1300 using homologous recombination. For the construction of the overexpression vector driven by the 35S promoter, the 1.5-kb coding region sequence from ZH11 was inserted into the HAtagged vector pCAMBIA1300. Additionally, to generate the GUS vector, a 1.7-kb promoter fragment from ZH11 was inserted into the vector DX2181 to drive the expression of the GUS gene. These plasmids were then introduced into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 strain and transferred into ZH11 through Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. 

# Grain storage components analysis

Following dehulling, the mature seeds are ground into powder. Similarly, fresh caryopsis at various developmental stages are ground in liquid nitrogen and subsequently stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for subsequent testing. The determination of apparent AC is conducted using the previously established method (Hu et al., 2020). Total starch content is assessed utilizing a starch determination kit (DF-1-Y; Suzhou Comin; http://www.cominbio. 

com). Furthermore, the total protein and lipid content are determined following previously described protocols (Kang et al., 2005). 

# Microscopy analysis

To observe the SGs of wild-type and KO mutant seeds, crosssections of the brown rice are affixed to an aluminium stub, goldsputtered, and imaged utilizing a JSM-7500F scanning electron microscope (JEOL, Japan). Additionally, caryopsis samples from wild-type and KO mutants (at 5 days after pollination, DAP) are obtained for semi-thin sections using a Leica RM2255. These samples are sliced into $4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thick sections, stained with Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS), and subsequently photographed under a light microscope (Leica, DM2700P). 

# qRT-PCR analysis

RNA extraction and qRT-PCR experiments were carried out following previously established protocols (Tu et al., 2022). Total RNA was extracted from various rice tissues using the Total RNA kit I (OMEGA). Reverse transcription was performed using the PrimeScript RT reagent kit (TaKaRa). on 500 ng of total RNA. qRT-PCR experiments were conducted utilizing the PerfectStart-Green qPCR SuperMix (Transgen, Beijing) along with specific quantitative primers. The UBQ5 (LOC_Os01g22490) gene and FhaB (LOC_Os06g21120) were utilized as internal references, and the corresponding primers are listed in Table S1. 

# GUS staining

The leaf blade, internode, root, spikelet, and developing caryopsis (5, 10, 15d, 20 DAP) of GUS transgenic plants were subjected to staining according to the instructions provided with the GUS staining solution (Coolaber, Beijing). Subsequently, they were photographed using a LEICA S6 D stereomicroscope. The stained caryopsis underwent dehydration in a graded ethanol series, followed by embedding in Technovit 7100 resin (Heraeus Kulzer, Germany). The embedded samples were then sectioned into $4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thick tissue slices and observed under a light microscope (Leica, DM2700P), with images subsequently captured. 

# Immunofluorescence assay

The caryopsis of 5DAP were collected from GUS transgenic plants and promptly fixed in a $4 \%$ paraformaldehyde EDC (Thermo Scientific) solution for $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ . Subsequently, they underwent dehydration through an ethanol gradient and clearing in xylene before being embedded in paraffin. Sections were then cut using a microtome (Leica RM2235), with $1 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ slices placed on slides (Leica X-tra). The sections were rehydrated and immersed in $0 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { m o l / L }$ citrate buffer, heated to boiling for 10 min, cooled, and the buffer was discarded. Subsequently, the sections were washed three times in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). The washed sections were gently placed in 10-mM PBS containing $1 . 5 \%$ (v/v) Glycine, $5 \%$ (w/v) BSA, $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20, and incubated for $6 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ at room temperature. Subsequently, the sections were transferred to 10-mM PBS containing $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) BSA, $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20, $0 . 8 8 \%$ (w/v) NaCl, and left at room temperature for 20 min. Finally, the sections were placed in 10 mmol/L PBS containing $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) BSA and left at room temperature for an additional 20 min. The primary antibody (Anti-GUS, Abcam) was diluted at a 1:300 ratio in PBS containing $3 \%$ (w/v) BSA. $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of the diluted antibody (AB50148; Abcam) was applied to each slide, covered with a coverslip, and incubated in a humidified box at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Once the sections returned to 

room temperature, they were placed in 10 mmol/L PBS containing $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) BSA, $2 . 9 \%$ (w/v) NaCl, $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v) Tween 20, and left for 10 min. The secondary antibody (Alexa Fluor 488, Abcam) was then dropped onto the slides at a 1:300 ratio. The samples were washed thrice with 10 mM PBS, each for 15 min. Finally, the immunofluorescence signal was observed using a confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM880; Carl Zeiss). 

# Subcellular localization

The coding regions of LCG1, OsMYB30 (Lv et al., 2017), and OsRac1 (Zhou et al., 2019) were amplified from cDNA obtained through reverse transcription, and the fragments were individually inserted into the pCAMBIA2300 vector carrying GFP or RFP tags via homologous recombination. Consequently, we generated the LCG1-GFP, nuclear marker OsMYB30-RFP, and plasma membrane marker OsRac1-RFP vectors. These vectors, including LCG1-GFP coupled with OsMYB30-RFP and LCG1-GFP coupled with OsRac1-RFP, were transiently expressed in rice protoplasts. Subsequently, the aforementioned plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and transformed into tobacco. The resulting signal was visualized using a confocal laser scanning microscope (STELLARIS STED/EM CPD300; Leica). 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays

To conduct the screening test for LCG1 interacting proteins, we utilize the rice cDNA library previously constructed in the laboratory, derived from developing caryopsis tissue. The Y2H experimental method provided by Clontech was used for this purpose. First, we amplify the coding sequence of LCG1 and clone it into the pGBKT7 vector to generate pGBKT7-LCG1. Next, we co-transform the library plasmid with pGBKT7-LCG1 into prepared Y2HGold yeast competent cells and spread them onto SD/-Trp/-Leu/-His/-Ade/X- $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathtt { Q } \mathbf { \xi }$ -Gal/AbA selection medium plates. Incubation is carried out in a constant temperature incubator set to $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3–5 days, until single clones grow to a size of $1 { - } 2 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ . The blue-positive clones are then transferred to secondary screening medium for further validation. Finally, the positive clones are transferred to SD/-Trp/-Leu/-His/-Ade/AbA liquid medium, and the plasmid is extracted using the Yeast Plasmid Extraction Kit (product number D1160; Solarbio, Beijing) for subsequent sequencing. 

# BiFC assay

The BIFC (Bimolecular Fluorescence Complementation) experiment was conducted following established protocols. The coding regions of LCG1 and OsBP5 were cloned into the ${ \mathsf { Y } } { \mathsf { F } } { \mathsf { P } } ^ { \mathsf { N } }$ (Yellow Fluorescent Protein N-terminal) and ${ \mathsf { Y F P } } ^ { \mathsf { C } }$ (Yellow Fluorescent Protein C-terminal) vectors, respectively. These constructs were transiently expressed in tobacco young leaves. Subsequently, fluorescent signals were visualized using a confocal laser scanning microscope (STELLARIS STED/EM CPD300; Leica). 

# Split-LUC assay

The coding regions of LCG1, OsBP5, OsMYB30 were cloned into the pCAMBIA 1300-Nluc and pCAMBIA 1300-cLUC vectors, respectively. These plasmids were then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and transiently expressed in tobacco leaves. Following expression, the transformed leaves were immersed in a buffer solution containing 5 mM luciferin and left in the dark for 5 min. Subsequently, they were photographed using a multi-functional imaging system (model ChemiDoc; Bio-Rad). 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay for transactivation analysis

For transcription activation analysis in protoplast systems, the coding regions of LCG1, OsBP5, and OsEBP89 coding regions were inserted into the vector pGreenII 62-SK to construct effector vectors. Additionally, a 2 kb fragment of the $W x ^ { \mathrm { { b } } }$ promoter was inserted into the vector pGreenII 0800-LUC to construct reporter vectors. As a control, the CaMV 35S promoter driving the Renilla luciferase (REN) gene was used. In the experimental setup, $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of the effector plasmid and $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of the reporter plasmid were cotransformed into protoplasts for transient expression studies. Cotransformation of reporter plasmid and empty vector combinations served as controls. Following transformation, luciferase values were measured using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (BiYunTian) in a Promega Glomax chemiluminescence detector. 

Transcription activation analysis in tobacco involved the individual insertion of LCG1, OsBP5, and OsEBP89 coding region sequences into the HA-tagged pCAMBIA1300 vector to construct effector vectors. Additionally, a 2 kb Wxb promoter sequence was inserted into the LUC-reporter gene-containing pCAM-BIA1300 vector to construct reporter vectors. These plasmids were then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 and transiently expressed in tobacco leaves. 

# Natural variation analysis for LCG1

For natural variation analysis of LCG1, genomic sequences from 378 rice accessions were obtained from Rice RC (http://ricerc. sicau.edu.cn/). Haplotype analysis based on SNPs and InDels in LCG1 across cultivated rice varieties (frequencies ${ > } 0 . 0 5$ ) was conducted. Further, the CGP and CGD among 198 rice accessions bearing 5 haplotypes were investigated. The geographical distribution of the 378 cultivated rice varieties worldwide was analysed using mapping software. 

# Data analysis

In data analysis, significant differences for qRT-PCR analyses, agronomic traits, chalkiness-related traits and taste qualities assessment, were determined using two-tailed Student’s t-test for pairwise comparison, with data presented as mean $\pm$ standard deviation.
