- The pharmaceutically valuable monoterpene indole alkaloids (MIAs) in Catharanthus roseus are derived from the indole and iridoid pathways that respond to jasmonate (JA) signaling.   
- Two classes of JA-responsive bHLH transcription factor (TF), CrMYC2 and BIS1/BIS2, are known to regulate the indole and iridoid pathways, respectively. However, upregulation of either one of the TF genes does not lead to increased MIA accumulation. Moreover, little is known about the interconnection between the CrMYC2 and BIS transcriptional cascades and the hierarchical position of BIS1/BIS2 in JA signaling.   
- Here, we report that a newly identified bHLH factor, Repressor of MYC2 Targets 1 (RMT1), is activated by CrMYC2 and BIS1, and acts as a repressor of the CrMYC2 targets. In addition, we isolated and functionally characterized the core C. roseus JA signaling components, including CORONATINE INSENSITIVE 1 (COI1) and JASMONATE ZIM domain (JAZ) proteins. CrMYC2 and BIS1 are repressed by the JAZ proteins in the absence of JA, but de-repressed by the $\mathsf{SCF}^{\mathsf{COI1}}$ complex on perception of JA.   
- Our findings suggest that the repressors, JAZs and RMT1, mediate crosstalk between the CrMYC2 and BIS regulatory cascades to balance the metabolic flux in MIA biosynthesis.

# Introduction

Catharanthus roseus is the major source of an array of monoterpene indole alkaloids (MIAs; vinca alkaloids), including the chemotherapeutics vinblastine and vincristine (Lucas et al., 2010). MIAs are derived from tryptamine and secologanin, products of the indole and iridoid pathways, respectively (Supporting Information Fig. S1). Using a forward genetic approach and homology-based cloning, many pathway genes and regulators have been identified. The AP2/ERF transcription factors (TFs), ORCA2 and ORCA3, are the major regulators of several MIA pathway genes (Fig. S1). In addition, WRKY TF (CrWRKY1), bZIP TFs (CrGBF1 and CrGBF2), MYB-like protein (CrBPF-1) and AT-hook factors are known to regulate the MIA pathway (Patra et al., 2013b; Thamm et al., 2016; Zhou & Memelink, 2016). Recently, two AP2/ERF TF genes, ORCA4 and ORCA5, which form a physical cluster with ORCA3, have also been identified as regulators of the MIA pathway (Paul et al., 2017). The basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) TF, CrMYC2, directly activates ORCA2 and ORCA3, but indirectly activates ORCA4 and ORCA5. CrMYC2 binds to a qualitative sequence (T/G-box) in the ORCA3 promoter and acts as an on/off switch, whereas

AT-hook TFs regulate ORCA3 expression by binding to the A/ T-rich quantitative sequence in the promoter in response to jasmonate (JA) (Vom Endt et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2011). CrMYC2 and ORCA3 have limited effects on the expression of genes in the secoiridoid branch of the MIA pathway. The JA-responsive TFs, bHLH iridoid synthesis 1 and 2 (BIS1 and BIS2), are major activators of the secoiridoid pathway (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015, 2016). BIS1 and BIS2 form homo/ heterodimers to transactivate the gene promoters of enzymes that catalyze the sequential conversion of geranyl diphosphate to loganic acid. In addition, both BIS1 and BIS2 transactivate the iridoid pathway gene promoters in Catharanthus MP183L cells and boost the production of secoiridoids. It is interesting to note that, in C. roseus hairy roots overexpressing BIS1, the CrMYC2-ORCA3 target genes are either not affected or downregulated (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). These findings suggest that MIA biosynthesis is co-regulated by the independent BIS1/BIS2 and CrMYC2 transcriptional cascades. Moreover, these two transcriptional cascades probably interact to fine tune the precursor flux for MIA accumulation in Catharanthus. Whether and how the two cascades interact in the JA signaling pathway remain to be elucidated (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015).

JA signaling is a multi-component pathway. The core JA-responsive module $\mathrm{SCF}^{\mathrm{CO11}}$ is an E3 ligase complex consisting of the F-box protein CORONATINE INSENSITIVE 1 (CO11), Arabidopsis SKP1-like protein (ASK1/2), CULLIN1 (CUL1) and RING BOX protein 1 (RBX1). Other components of the JA signaling pathway include the JASMONATE ZIM-domain (JAZ), NOVEL INTERACTOR of JAZ (NINJA) and the transcriptional co-repressor, TOPLESS (TPL) proteins (Xu et al., 2002; Pauwels et al., 2010). The expression of the MIA structural and regulatory genes is JA responsive. However, JA-responsive regulation of the MIA pathway in Catharanthus remains insufficiently understood. The JA signaling pathway related to MIA biosynthesis is unique because it simultaneously affects two independent regulatory cascades, controlled by two bHLH TFs from different subgroups (i.e. CrMYC2 of subgroup IIIe and BIS1/2 of IVa). We hypothesized that the $\mathrm{SCF}^{\mathrm{CO11}}$ -JAZ complex and other factors mediate the interaction between the CrMYC2 and BIS1/2 cascades, subsequently modulating the precursor flux for MIA biosynthesis.

In this study, we identified a JA-responsive bHLH TF, Repressor of MYC2 Targets 1 (RMT1), which is activated by both CrMYC2 and BIS1, and acts as a passive repressor of CrMYC2 function. In addition, we cloned and functionally characterized the components of the Catharanthus SCF $^{\text{COI1}}$ complex and JAZ proteins, and established that the Catharanthus SCF $^{\text{COI1}}$ -JAZ complex plays a central role in the crosstalk between the CrMYC2 and BIS cascades. Our results suggest that CrCOI1 acts upstream of a bHLH TF network, consisting of CrMYC2, BIS1/2 and RMT1, to fine tune MIA biosynthesis in Catharanthus. The presence of the passive repressor, RMT1, in the MIA pathway also provides a possible explanation for the observation that the overexpression of either CrMYC2 or BIS1 does not lead to the overall upregulation of the downstream pathway genes and TIA accumulation.

# Materials and Methods

# Plant material, growth conditions and treatments

Catharanthus roseus (L.) G. Don (var. Little Bright Eye) seeds, obtained from NESeed (nseed.com), were surface sterilized using $30\%$ (v/v) commercial bleach for $15\mathrm{min}$ , rinsed five times with sterile distilled water and incubated in the dark at $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for germination on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium. After 2 d, plates were transferred to a growth room, at $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , with constant light.

For methyl jasmonate (MeJA) induction, seedlings were incubated with $100\mu \mathrm{M}$ MeJA for $2\mathrm{h}$ at room temperature, whereas transgenic hairy roots were treated for $24\mathrm{h}$ . In both cases, an equal amount of ethanol was added to the medium for mock treatment (Sun et al., 2009). For the induction of CrCOII expression in PTA-COII overexpressing hairy root lines, $30\mu \mathrm{M}$ dexamethasone (DEX; Sigma) was added in 1/3 Schenk and Hildebrandt (SH) medium (Caisson Laboratories, Smithfield, UT, USA) and incubated for $16\mathrm{h}$ . To test for CrCOI-JAZ interactions, $10\mu \mathrm{M}$

coronatine was used for $14\mathrm{h}$ to treat the transfected tobacco protoplasts.

# Plasmid construction and generation of Catharanthus transgenic hairy roots

To generate transgenic hairy roots, CrCOII and CrJAZ open reading frames (ORFs) were amplified from Catharanthus seedling cDNA using gene-specific primers (Table S1) and cloned into a modified pCAMBIA2301 vector, containing the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator. The resulting plasmids were mobilized into Agrobacterium rhizogenes R1000 by freeze-thaw (Weigel & Glazebrook, 2006). Transgenic hairy roots were developed as described previously (Choi et al., 2004; Suttipanta et al., 2011). The pCAMBIA2301 vector was used for the development of vector-only control roots.

For conditional overexpression, CrCOI1 was cloned into the pTA7001 vector under the control of the DEX-inducible promoter (Moore et al., 2006) and mobilized into A. rhizogenes R1000 by freeze-thaw. Three-week-old Catharanthus seedlings were used for the generation of hairy roots overexpressing CrCOI1 as described previously (Choi et al., 2004; Suttipanta et al., 2011).

# Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) of CrCO11 in C. roseus

A 220-base-pair DNA fragment containing the $5^{\prime}$ untranslated region (UTR) of CrCOII was amplified from seedling cDNA using gene-specific primers (Table S1) and cloned into the vector pTRV2 (Padmanabhan & Dinesh-Kumar, 2009) to generate plasmid pTRV2-COII. VIGS was performed as described previously (Liscombe & O'Connor, 2011) (see Notes S1 for details).

# In silico analysis of putative MIA regulators in Catharanthus

The bHLH sequences and clustering were performed as described previously (Schluttenhofer et al., 2014). In brief, the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) Conserved Domain Database was used to search Medicinal Plants Genomics Resource (MPGR) predicted sequences for protein domains. The presence of the bHLH domain in protein sequences was identified using Excel. Expression data for ZCTs, GBFs and each protein with a bHLH domain were obtained from MPGR. Hierarchical clustering and imaging were conducted using GENEPATTERN (http://genepattern.broadinstitute.org/gp/pages/login.jspf). JA induction was classified if gene expression was upregulated by more than two-fold in 24-h-treated hairy root cultures compared with 0-h controls.

# Yeast two-hybrid analysis

For yeast two-hybrid assay, the desired genes were cloned in pBD-GAL4 Cam (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA, USA) and pADGAL4-2.1 (Stratagene) vectors. The resulting plasmids were cotransformed into yeast strain AH109 (Clontech, Mountain View,

CA, USA), using the polyethylene glycol/lithium acetate (PEG/LiAc) method, as described previously (Pattanaik et al., 2010). Colonies growing on synthetic dropout (SD) medium lacking leucine and tryptophan (-Leu-Trp) were screened for growth on either triple selection SD medium lacking histidine, leucine and tryptophan (-His-Leu-Trp), supplemented with $10\mathrm{mM}$ 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (3-AT, Sigma), or on quadruple selection SD medium lacking adenine, histidine, leucine and tryptophan (-Ade-His-Leu-Trp). For testing COI1-JAZ interaction, $200\mu \mathrm{M}$ coronatine (Sigma) was added to the triple and quadruple selection SD media.

# Plasmid construction and transient gene expression assay in Catharanthus protoplasts

For the isolation of Catharanthus mesophyll protoplasts, young leaves were collected from 6-wk-old plants, and mesophyll protoplasts were isolated following the method described previously (Lin, 1983). The effector plasmids were generated by cloning BIS1, CrMYC2 and CrJAZ1 into a modified pBlueScript (pBS) vector containing the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator. A pBS plasmid, containing uidA under the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator, was used as control. After $14 - 16\mathrm{h}$ of incubation at room temperature, total RNA was isolated from the electroporated protoplasts using an RNeasy Plant mini kit (Qiagen, Germantown, MD, USA) and used for cDNA synthesis. Gene expression was monitored by quantitative reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR).

# Plasmid construction and transient gene expression assay in tobacco protoplasts

The reporter plasmids used in tobacco protoplast assay contained the fire-fly luciferase coding sequence fused to the RMT1, ORCA3, G10H or BIS1 promoters and rbcS terminator. To generate mutant ORCA3 promoter, two T/G-box motifs (AACGTG) in the ORCA3 promoter were changed to (AAAATG) individually (ORCA3-M1 and ORCA3-M2) or in combination (ORCA3-M1M2) by site-directed mutagenesis using the protocol described previously (Zheng et al., 2004). The effector plasmids were generated by cloning the full-length coding sequences of CrMYC2, BIS1, CrJAZ1 or RMT1 into a pBS vector containing the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator. A plasmid containing the $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) gene under the control of the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator was used as an internal control. Protoplast isolation, electroporation and luciferase assay were performed as described previously (Pattanaik et al., 2010). Luciferase activity was normalized against GUS activity and expressed as fold activation relative to the control (reporter only). All experiments were repeated three times with four replicates for each sample.

# Tobacco protoplast-based two-hybrid assay

For tobacco protoplast-based two-hybrid assays, the reporter plasmid (5XRE)- $35S_{pro}:LUC$ was generated by cloning five tandem repeats of the GAL-responsive elements (5XRE),

upstream of a minimal CaMV35S promoter, in a modified pKYLX80 vector (Schardl et al., 1987; Bai et al., 2011). The effector plasmids were constructed by fusion of Catharanthus JAZ ORFs with the GAL4 DNA-binding domain (amino acids 1-147) in a modified pBS vector containing the mirabilis mosaic virus (MMV) promoter and rbcS terminator (Dey & Maiti, 1999). CrCOI1 cDNA was fused with the VP16 transcription activation domain under the MMV promoter and rbcS terminator in a modified pBS vector. A pBS plasmid, $35S_{pro}:uidA$ , containing uidA under the CaMV35S promoter and rbcS terminator, was used as an internal control. Reporter, effector and internal control plasmids were electroporated into tobacco protoplasts (Nicotiana tabacum L. cv Xanthi), and luciferase and GUS activities, in transfected protoplasts, were measured as described previously (Pattanaik et al., 2010).

# Bacterial recombinant protein production and electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

In order to produce recombinant CrMYC2 and RMT1 proteins, the corresponding ORFs were cloned into the pGEX4T1 vector (GE Healthcare Biosciences, Pittsburgh, PA, USA). The resulting plasmids were transformed into BL21 cells containing pRIL (Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA). For the induction of recombinant protein expression, $0.1\mathrm{mM}$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to the cell cultures at $A_{600}c$ 1.0 and incubated for $3\mathrm{h}$ at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ .The cells were harvested and lysed using CelLytic B (Sigma). The glutathione-S-transferase (GST) fusion proteins were bound to glutathione sepharose 4B columns (Amersham, Pittsburgh, PA, USA) and then eluted by $10\mathrm{mM}$ reduced glutathione in $50~\mathrm{mM}$ Tris-HCl buffer $(\mathrm{pH}8.0)$

For EMSA, DNA probes were synthesized with or without biotin labeling (Integrated DNA Technology, Coralville, IA, USA). The 43-bp probes (Table S1) were designed to include the T/G-box element present in the ORCA3 promoter (GenBank accession no. AJ251250). Oligos were annealed to produce double-stranded probes for EMSA. The DNA-binding reactions were carried out in $15\mathrm{mM}$ HEPES, $\mathrm{pH}7.5$ $35\mathrm{mM}$ KCl, $1\mathrm{mM}$ EDTA, $\mathrm{pH}8.0$ $6\%$ glycerol, $1\mathrm{mM}$ dithiothreitol (DTT), $1\mathrm{mM}$ $\mathrm{MgCl}_2$ and $1\mu \mathrm{g}$ of poly dI-dC in a final volume of $20~\mu \mathrm{l}$ . Purified proteins were incubated with 40 fmol DNA probe at room temperature for $30\mathrm{min}$ . For the competition experiment, cold probes were added in an excess molar ratio (500 and 1000 times). The DNA-protein complexes were resolved by electrophoresis on $6\%$ nondenaturing polyacrylamide gels and then transferred to BiodyneB modified membrane $(0.45\mathrm{mm})$ Pierce, Rockford, IL, USA). The band shifts were detected by a chemiluminescent nucleic acid detection module (Pierce) and exposed to X-ray films.

# Alkaloid extraction and estimation

For alkaloid extraction, tissue samples were flash frozen in liquid nitrogen and ground to a powder. Samples were twice extracted in methanol $(1:100\mathrm{w / v})$ for $24\mathrm{h}$ on a shaker. Pooled extracts were dried using a rotary evaporator and diluted in methanol,

$10\mu \mathrm{l}\mathrm{mg}^{-1}$ of the initial sample. Samples were analyzed with high-performance liquid chromatography, followed by electrospray-injection in tandem mass spectrometry, as described previously (Suttipanta et al., 2011). Known standards were run to identify elution times and mass fragments.

# Results

# CrMYC2 and BIS1 are components of a negative regulatory loop

Previous studies have shown that overexpression of $BIS1$ has negative effects on CrMYC2-ORCA3-regulated genes. Conversely, ORCA3 has limited effects on the BIS1 target genes in the Catharanthus cell line (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). To further validate the effects of CrMYC2 and BIS1 on MIA pathway gene expression, we transiently overexpressed $BIS1$ or $CrMYC2$ in Catharanthus leaf mesophyll protoplasts and monitored gene expression by qRT-PCR. CrMYC2, although inducing the expression of its target genes (ORCA3, TDC and STR), did not alter $BIS1$ expression; however, $G10H$ and 7DLH, key targets of BIS1, were significantly repressed (Fig. 1a). In agreement with previous findings (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), $BIS1$ overexpression in mesophyll protoplasts resulted in the repression of CrMYC2 targets, such as ORCA3, STR and, possibly, TDC, but

did not alter CrMYC2 expression (Fig. 1b). The results suggest that CrMYC2 and BIS1 are components of a negative regulatory loop that maintains a balance of precursor flux. As CrMYC2 and BIS1 did not have an apparent effect on each other's expression, we assumed that additional regulators are involved in repressing the expression of their target genes. In Arabidopsis, JAZ proteins are known to interact with MYC2 and participate in a negative feedback loop to attenuate the JA signal (Moreno et al., 2013). In mesophyll protoplasts, overexpression of CrMYC2, but not BIS1, significantly induced the expression of CrJAZ1 and CrJAZ2 (Fig. 1c). However, overexpression of CrJAZ1 in mesophyll protoplasts repressed ORCA3 and G10H, two key targets of CrMYC2 and BIS1, respectively, although the effects on CrMYC2 and BIS1 were not apparent (Fig. 1d).

Hierarchical cluster analysis of all putative Catharanthus bHLH TFs and other regulators of the MIA pathway, including ZCTs, GBFs and BPF-1, revealed two bHLH TFs, bHLH05 (CRO_T000734) and bHLH92 (CRO_T027656), which are tightly co-expressed with CrMYC2 (Fig. 2a). Further analysis classified bHLH92 and bHLH05 to bHLH subgroups II and IIId, respectively (Fig. S2a). We therefore predicted that bHLH05, hereafter termed Repressor of MYC2 Targets 1 (RMT1), is involved in the regulation of CrMYC2 activity. Consistent with this possibility, RMT1 expression was significantly increased in Catharanthus seedlings treated with MeJA for $24\mathrm{h}$ (Fig. S2b).

In Catharanthus mesophyll protoplasts, overexpression of CrMYC2 or BIS1 significantly induced RMT1 expression (Fig. 2b). Overexpression of RMT1 significantly reduced ORCA3 expression, but did not significantly affect CrMYC2 and BIS1 (Fig. 2c). CrMYC2, but not BIS1, activated the RMT1 promoter in tobacco protoplasts (Fig. 2d). The subgroup IVa bHLH factors are known to bind N-box (CACGAG) elements in the target promoters to regulate their expression (Yamamura et al., 2015; Mertens et al., 2016). BIS1 activates the iridoid pathway genes containing N-box and/or G-box elements in their promoters, suggesting the involvement of these cis-elements for the coordinated regulation of the targets (Van Moerkercke et al., 2016). The RMT1 promoter contains several E-box (CANNTG) motifs, but lacks the N-box motif. BIS1 did not activate the RMT promoter in tobacco protoplasts possibly because of the absence of the N-box in the promoter. BIS1 induced RMT1 expression in Catharanthus protoplasts (Fig. 2b), suggesting the involvement of an unidentified TF.

Next, we generated transgenic hairy root lines, constitutively expressing $RMT1$ under the control of the CaMV35S promoter. The transgenic status of the hairy roots was verified by PCR and two independent lines were chosen for further analysis (Fig. S2c). Overexpression of $RMT1$ ( $RMT1-OX$ ) significantly repressed the expression of ORCA3 and STR. The transcript level of CrMYC2 was also reduced in transgenic hairy roots, whereas BIS1 expression remained unchanged (Fig. 2e). In addition, the concentrations of catharanthine, tabersonine and ajmalicine were decreased significantly in $RMT1-OX$ hairy roots compared with the empty vector (EV) control (Fig. 2f).

# RMT1 and CrMYC2 bind to the T/G-box element in the ORCA3 promoter

CrMYC2 activates ORCA3 expression by binding to the T/G box (AACGTG) within the jasmonate-responsive element (JRE) of the ORCA3 promoter (Zhang et al., 2011), whereas RMT1 represses ORCA3 expression (Fig. 2c,e). We next tested the relationship between RMT1 and CrMYC2 in the regulation of the CrMYC2 targets. The ORCA3 promoter-luciferase reporter plasmid was electroporated into tobacco protoplasts, together with plasmids expressing CrMYC2 or RMT1. As expected, CrMYC2 overexpression significantly induced the luciferase activity driven by the ORCA3 promoter (Fig. 3a). By contrast, the luciferase activity was reduced significantly when

RMT1 was expressed alone or with CrMYC2 (Fig. 3a). As RMT1 does not contain any known repressor domain, we suspected that RMT1 forms a heterodimer with CrMYC2 to antagonize its activity on the target promoter. We performed yeast two-hybrid assay, but did not detect dimerization between RMT1 and CrMYC2. We thus reasoned that CrMYC2 and RMT1 probably compete for binding to the same cis-elements on the ORCA3 promoter. We performed a competition assay by increasing the ratio of RMT1 plasmid DNA to that of CrMYC2 in the protoplast transactivation assay. The CrMYC2-induced luciferase activities gradually declined with increasing RMT1 concentration (one-, 2.5- and five-fold of that of CrMYC2; Fig. 3a). However, BIS1-induced luciferase activity, driven by the $G10H$ promoter, was not significantly affected by the co-expression of RMT1 (Fig. 3b).

We speculated that RMT1 acts as a passive repressor that competes with CrMYC2 to bind to the T/G-box sequences. Of the two T/G-box (AACGTG) sequences present in the ORCA3 promoter, one has been reported previously as the possible binding site for CrMYC2 (Zhang et al., 2011). To determine whether RMT1 binds to the same cis-element as CrMYC2, we mutated the two AACGTG motifs to AAAATG (Fig. 3c; upper panel). Mutations in both T/G-boxes affected the activation and repression of ORCA3 by CrMYC2 and RMT1, respectively (Fig. 3c), suggesting that CrMYC2 and RMT1 bind to the same cis-elements and that both motifs are important for promoter activity. Binding of RMT1 to the T/G-box in the ORCA3 promoter was further verified by EMSA. CrMYC2 was used as a positive control for the experiment. Recombinant GST-tagged RMT1 and CrMYC2, purified from Escherichia coli using GST affinity chromatography (Fig. S2d), were used in EMSA with a $5^{\prime}$ biotin-labeled, 43-bp DNA probe containing the ORCA3 promoter T/G-box. Non-labeled (cold) probe was also used for the competition experiment. We found that, similar to CrMYC2, RMT1 interacted with the T/G-box elements, resulting in a mobility shift. The specificity of the DNA-protein interaction was confirmed by the competition experiment, in which the addition of excess non-labeled probe eliminated the interaction between RMT1 and labeled probe (Fig. 3d).

Collectively, these findings suggest that CrMYC2, BIS1, RMT1 and JAZ are part of a negative regulatory loop that is involved in the crosstalk between the JA-responsive CrMYC2- and BIS-regulated cascades. In leaves, internal phloem-associated parenchyma (IPAP) cell and epidermis are the major sites of operation for the BIS1 and CrMYC2 cascades, respectively (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). Transcript levels of CrMYC2, BIS1, RMT1, CrJAZ1 and CrCOI1 in both IPAP-enriched cells (veins) and epidermal cells (nearly veinless) were measured using qRT-PCR. The CrMYC2 and RMT1 transcripts were moderately higher in epidermal cells (nearly vein-less), compared with BIS1. There was no significant difference in the transcript levels of CrJAZ1 and CrCOI1 in IPAP-enriched and epidermal cells (Fig. S2e). The coexistence of CrMYC2, BIS1, RMT1 and CrJAZ1 transcripts in both tissues further supports the presence of a regulatory loop controlling the two cascades.

# CO11 genes are conserved in Catharanthus and other medicinal plants

The JA co-receptor, COI1, as well as MYC2 and JAZs, are key components of the JA signaling pathway in plants. The JA-responsive CrMYC2 and BIS1/2 regulatory cascades probably act downstream of COI1 to modulate MIA biosynthesis in Catharanthus. However, little is known about how JA functions in most medicinal plants, including Catharanthus. We thus searched medicinal plant databases to identify putative orthologs of Arabidopsis COI1. A single COII gene was identified from each of the Apocynaceae family members, including Catharanthus (Table S2; Fig. S3). The deduced amino acid sequence of C. roseus COI1 (CrCOI1) showed high identities with those of COI1 proteins from Solanum lycopersicum $(73\%)$ , Nicotiana attenuata $(71\%)$ and Arabidopsis $(70\%)$ (Fig. S4). In

addition, we showed that CrCOI1 interacts with the $\mathrm{SCF}^{\mathrm{COI1}}$ complex components of Catharanthus (Fig. S5). Genetic complementation of the Arabidopsis coi1-30 mutant using CrCOI1 (CrCOI1-OX (coi1-30)) and subsequent molecular analysis suggested that CrCOI1 is a functional ortholog of Arabidopsis COI1 (Fig. S6).

# CrJAZs interact with CrCO1 in a coronatine-dependent manner

JAZ proteins are negative regulators of JA signaling and are degraded on interaction with COI1 (Pauwels & Goossens, 2011). Two JAZ genes, CrJAZ1 and CrJAZ2, have been identified previously in Catharanthus (Zhang et al., 2011). To further characterize the JA signaling cascade in Catharanthus, we identified three more JAZ genes, CrJAZ3, CrJAZ8 and CrJAZ10, in

the Catharanthus genome (Fig. S7b-f). The expression of Catharanthus JAZs is induced by JA and JAZ proteins localized in the nucleus (Fig. S8a-f). The JAZ proteins are known to form homodimers and/or heterodimers, mediated through the conserved TIFY domain (Chini et al., 2009). To establish a CrJAZ dimerization network, the five CrJAZs were expressed either as bait or prey in a yeast two-hybrid assay. Dimerization of the Catharanthus JAZs is largely similar to that reported for Arabidopsis JAZs (Fig. S8g).

Physical interaction between COI1 and JAZ is required for the regulated degradation of JAZ proteins in the presence of the bioactive JA-Ile. To determine the possible CrJAZ-CrCOI1 interaction, we performed yeast two-hybrid assays for all five CrJAZs in the presence of coronatine, a bacterial phytotoxin that structurally and functionally mimics JA-Ile (Melotto et al., 2008). Coronatine was essential for direct physical interaction of CrCOI1 with CrJAZ1, CrJAZ3 and CrJAZ10 (Fig. 4a). The CrJAZ3-CrCOI1 and CrJAZ10-CrCOI1 interactions were weaker than that of CrJAZ1-CrCOI1, even in the presence of coronatine, as observed by cell growth on selection plates. The absence of a typical $\mathrm{X}_5\mathrm{PY}$ motif in the Jas domain of CrJAZ10 could be the cause of this weak interaction. CrJAZ2 and CrJAZ8 failed to interact with CrCOI1, even in the presence of $200~\mu \mathrm{M}$ coronatine. Notably, the C-terminal domain of the Jas motif,

critical for interaction with COI1, is conserved in CrJAZ2, suggesting that sequences other than the Jas motif are possibly involved in dimerization. The absence of a canonical Jas degron in the C-terminal domain of CrJAZ8 probably hinders the CrJAZ8-CrCOI1 interaction. Sequence diversity in the Jas motif of Arabidopsis JAZ proteins results in great variability in the ligand-dependent interaction of COI1-JAZ (Shyu et al., 2012).

We also used a tobacco protoplast-based two-hybrid assay (Patra et al., 2013a) to independently verify the CrJAZ-CrCOI1 interaction. When co-electroporated with the luciferase reporter plasmid, three plasmids expressing the GAL4 DNA-binding domain (BD)-CrJAZ fusions (BD-CrJAZ1/2/10) individually repressed the background luciferase activity, whereas BD-CrJAZ3 increased the luciferase activity by almost three-fold. BD-CrJAZ8 did not alter the luciferase activity (Fig. 4b). Co-electroporation of the activation domain (VP16)-CrCOI1 fusion (VP16-CrCOI1), together with BD-CrJAZ1, partially recovered the luciferase activity, implicating the CrCOI1-CrJAZ1 interaction in vivo. However, in the absence of coronatine, co-expression of VP16-CrCOI1 and CrJAZ3/8/10 did not recover the luciferase activity (Fig. 4b). These results are in agreement with those from the yeast two-hybrid experiments.

# CrJAZ proteins interact with CrMYC2, RMT1 and CrNINJA

In the JA signaling cascade, several bHLH TFs, including MYC2, are known targets of JAZ proteins. The MYC2-JAZ interaction is critical for the expression of numerous early JA-responsive genes related to developmental and metabolic pathways (Dombrecht et al., 2007). In Catharanthus, CrMYC2 controls the expression of ORCA2 and ORCA3, which, in turn, activate a subset of the structural genes in the MIA pathway (Zhang et al., 2011). We thus tested whether CrJAZ proteins interact with CrMYC2 using yeast two-hybrid assay. We confirmed that all five CrJAZ proteins physically interact with CrMYC2 (Fig. 5a), suggesting that the CrJAZ proteins act as corepressors of CrMYC2. Our results clearly indicate that CrCOI1 modulates the CrMYC2 cascade through interaction with CrJAZ. In addition, RMT1 interacts with CrJAZ1 in yeast cells (Fig. S8h). However, it remains unclear whether the CrCOI1-CrJAZ complex also regulates the BIS1 cascade, which is independent of CrMYC2.

We then tested the interactions of CrJAZ proteins with BIS1. As a result of the extremely high self-activation of BIS1 in yeast cells, the yeast two-hybrid result was inconclusive. We thus used a protoplast-based, protein-protein interaction assay as described previously (Patra et al., 2013a). In plant cells, BIS1 significantly activated the expression of a luciferase reporter driven by either the 5XGALRE-35S promoter (Fig. 5b), or that of $G10H$ (Fig. 5c), an iridoid pathway gene. Activation of the reporter was significantly reduced when BIS1 was co-expressed with CrJAZ1, CrJAZ2 or CrJAZ10, indicating the formation of a BIS1-CrJAZ repressor complex that represses the BIS1 targets (Fig. 5b,c). However, as the in vitro pull-down and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assays did not work, we assume that the repression of BIS1 activity by CrJAZ1 is indirect and possibly involves additional factors in the plant cell.

Most of the Arabidopsis JAZ proteins by themselves are not transcriptional repressors because they lack the N-terminal LxLxL-type repressor motif (Shyu et al., 2012). Instead, they recruit the co-repressor, TPL, with the help of NINJA (Pauwels et al., 2010). We thus expected that CrJAZ proteins would interact with CrNINJA. We cloned the ORF coding CrNINJA based on sequence homology. All CrJAZs, with the exception of CrJAZ8, interacted with CrNINJA in yeast cells (Fig. 5d). The emerging picture implies that the CrJAZ repressor complex, consisting of CrJAZ-NINJA-TPL, is central to the CrCOI1-regulated repression and de-repression of the CrMYC2 and BIS1

cascades. We next investigated the biological significance of such complex protein-protein interactions.

# CrJAZ proteins repress CrMYC2 and BIS1 activity in transgenic hairy roots

We hypothesized that CrJAZ proteins repress the transcriptional activation of CrMYC2 and BIS1. We generated transgenic Catharanthus hairy root lines constitutively expressing the five individual CrJAZ genes (Fig. S8i). The independent transgenic lines CrJAZ1-OX, CrJAZ2-OX, CrJAZ8-OX and CrJAZ10-OX grew normally; however, all CrJAZ3-OX lines were severely stunted. qRT-PCR measurements showed significant reduction in expression of CrMYC2, ORCA3, BIS1 and G10H in CrJAZ1-OX, CrJAZ2-OX, CrJAZ8-OX and CrJAZ10-OX hairy roots (Fig. 5e-h).

# Silencing of CrCO1 in planta alters JA-induced gene expression and MIA biosynthesis

The effects of CrCOI1 on JA-responsive gene expression and MIA biosynthesis were examined using two different approaches. First, CrCOI1 expression in Catharanthus leaves was suppressed using VIGS (Liscombe & O'Connor, 2011). CrCOI1 expression was measured in young leaves of plants inoculated with either EV or the VIGS vector for silencing of CrCOI1 (pTRV2-COI1). CrCOI1 expression was repressed by $60\%$ in COI1-VIGS plants (Fig. 6a), where the expression of CrMYC2, BIS1, BIS2, CrJAZ1 and CrJAZ2 was also reduced (Fig. S9a). Notably, the concentrations of catharanthine, ajmalicine, tabersonine and vindoline were decreased significantly in COI1-VIGS plants compared with the EV control (Fig. 6b).

To further substantiate our findings, we generated CrCOI1 RNAi transgenic hairy roots, and the effects of CrCOI1 on JA-responsive gene expression and MIA biosynthesis were examined. Two independent lines showing $c.70 - 80\%$ reduction of CrCOI1 transcripts were selected for further experiments (Figs 6c, S9b). We observed that the basal expression of CrMYC2, ORCA3, BIS1 and BIS2, as well as G10H, was reduced in CrCOI1-RNAi hairy roots compared with the EV control (Figs 6c, S9c). This supports the hypothesis that repression of CrCOI1 significantly affects the turnover of CrJAZ repressors and results in the attenuation of endogenous cellular JA signals. To determine whether the silencing of CrCOI1 affects JA-induced gene expression in the MIA pathway, the EV control and CrCOI1-RNAi hairy roots were treated with $100\mu \mathrm{M}$ MeJA for $24\mathrm{h}$ . qRT-PCR analysis revealed that the expression of the major regulators and their targets was greatly induced by MeJA in the EV control, but significantly decreased in CrCOI1-RNAi hairy roots (Figs 6c, S9c). Reduced expression of the pathway genes correlated well with decreased accumulation of MIAs in CrCOI1-RNAi hairy roots (Fig. 6d). The complementary results obtained from the two experiments strongly suggest that CrCOI1 is crucial for JA signal perception and acts upstream of both CrMYC2- and BIS-dependent regulatory cascades.

# CrCO11 overexpression in Catharanthus hairy roots increases MIA production

Next, we generated CrCOI1-OX transgenic hairy roots, with CrCOI1 expression driven by the constitutive CaMV35S promoter (Fig. S9d). Constitutive overexpression of CrCOI1 resulted in stunted hairy roots with poor frequency of survival, probably as a result of hypersensitivity to JA. Inhibition of root growth by JA has been documented in a number of plant species, including Arabidopsis and rice. The JA-insensitive Arabidopsis coi1 mutants exhibited better root growth than the corresponding controls on JA-supplemented medium (Xie et al., 1998; Yang et al., 2012). We also found that CrCOI1-RNAi transgenic roots exhibited better growth than the EV control on SH medium in the presence or absence of $100\mu \mathrm{M}$ MeJA (Fig. 7a). The improved growth of CrCOI1-RNAi transgenic roots is probably a result of consequential effects on the gibberellic acid pathway when the JA signal cascade is altered, a phenomenon observed previously in Arabidopsis and rice (Yang et al., 2012).

In order to alleviate the developmental problem encountered in constitutive CrCO11 expression, we developed transgenic hairy

roots in which CrCOI1 expression was driven by the DEX-inducible promoter (pTA-COII) (Gimenez-Ibanez et al., 2014) (Fig. S9e). The transgenic hairy roots containing the inducible expression system did not show growth defects. CrCOI1 expression was induced after the addition of $30\mu \mathrm{M}$ DEX for $16\mathrm{h}$ (Fig. 7b). Two highly inducible pTA-COII-OX lines were selected for gene expression analysis. The expression of CrMYC2, BIS1, BIS2, ORCA3, RMT1 and their targets was significantly upregulated (Fig. 7c). The changes in gene expression were accompanied by increased MIA accumulation in transgenic hairy roots (Fig. 7d). These results indicate that CrCOI1 enables the balanced expression of the CrMYC2 and BIS1 cascades, thus fine-tuning the accumulation of MIAs.

In addition, our protoplast-based assay demonstrated that RMT1 antagonizes CrMYC2 activity on the target promoter. As RMT1 does not interact with CrMYC2, it is unlikely that RMT1 antagonizes CrMYC2 activity through protein-protein interaction. Our protoplast-based assay indicates that RMT1 represses CrMYC2 activity on a target promoter in a dosage-dependent manner (Fig. 3), suggesting competition for the same binding sites. In Arabidopsis, the subgroup IIId bHLH TFs act as repressors of the activity of TT8-MYB75 and MYC2 by binding to G-box motifs on target promoters (Song et al., 2013).

We also showed that the Catharanthus JAZ proteins form homo/heterodimers in yeast and plant cells (Fig. S8g). In addition, we demonstrated that overexpression of CrJAZ1, CrJAZ2, CrJAZ8 or CrJAZ10 results in the repression of the CrMYC2 and BIS1 cascades (Fig. 5e-h). The increased accumulation of JAZ proteins in overexpression lines leads to the formation of complexes with CrMYC2, and probably with BIS1, to repress their activities. Moreover, CrJAZ proteins (CrJAZ2 and CrJAZ8), which do not interact with CrCOI1, are presumably more stable and act as negative regulators of the CrMYC2 and BIS1 cascades. These results demonstrate the functional redundancy of CrJAZ proteins and validate their pertinent role as negative regulators of both CrMYC2-ORCA3 and BIS1 cascades. Unlike the observation in CrJAZ1-OX hairy roots, overexpression of CrJAZ1 did not significantly affect BIS1 expression in protoplasts (Fig. 1d). This was probably because of the difference between the magnitudes of TF expression in a transient system (i.e. protoplasts) and stable transgenic lines.

The dimeric anticancer alkaloid anhydrovinblastine and its precursor catharanthine are known to interfere with cell division. Higher accumulation of these compounds may cause toxicity to Catharanthus cells, as in cancer cells. A recent study has shown that VIGS of CrNPF2.9, a tonoplast transporter of strictosidine in Catharanthus, causes cell death in the leaf, suggesting that increased accumulation of strictosidine is probably cytotoxic (Payne et al., 2017). MIAs are known to be defense compounds that respond to defense signals. It is thus reasonable to expect that Catharanthus has evolved a network of activators and repressors to balance the production of cytotoxic defense molecules, such as MIAs. Moreover, the biosynthesis of MIAs is an energy intensive process, and increased accumulation of MIAs in response to various biotic and abiotic signals can negatively affect plant growth and development. The expression of CrMYC2, BIS1/2, CrJAZs and RMT1 is induced in response to JA treatment. Coordinated induction of activators and repressors in response to JA suggests that they are involved in the fine-tuning of the expression of target genes and in balancing the accumulation of MIAs in Catharanthus.

The fragmentary knowledge on the molecular mechanism of MIA biosynthesis is a major challenge for biotechnological manipulation of the pathway. The overexpression or silencing of transcriptional regulators in hairy roots or cell lines has had limited success in increasing MIA production (Peebles et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2010; Pan et al., 2012; Van Moerkercke et al., 2015), indicating a complex molecular mechanism that balances MIA accumulation in Catharanthus. In addition, the MIA biosynthetic pathway is distributed among different cell types, and the majority of the pathway occurs in the leaf epidermis and IPAP cells (Van Moerkercke et al., 2015). Some MIA precursors are synthesized in roots and probably transported to the aerial tissues. In order to allow the metabolic outputs to be adjusted spatially and temporally, evenly distributed transcriptional control of pathway genes is necessary.

Our work has established that a regulatory loop consisting of activators (CrMYC2 and BIS1/2) and repressors (JAZs and RMT1) operates in the MIA biosynthetic pathway. The CrMYC2 and BIS cascades interact through repressors to fine tune and balance the accumulation of MIAs. Here, we propose a model depicting the regulatory network, featuring four bHLH factors (CrMYC2, BIS1/2, and RMT1), that mediates JA-induced MIA biosynthesis in Catharanthus. Overexpression of CrMYC2 (upper-left panel, Fig. 8) or BIS1 (lower-left panel, Fig. 8) significantly activates the repressors, CrJAZ or RMT1, respectively, which, in turn, repress the expression of iridoid or indole pathway genes (Figs 1, 2). Increased CrMYC2 activity induces CrJAZ proteins which suppress the activities of both CrMYC2 and BIS1, probably through protein-protein interaction. As a consequence, the upregulation of CrMYC2 decreases BIS1 activity, evidenced by the decrease in G10H and 7DLH expression (Fig. 1a). However, increased BIS1 activity also results in a reduction in CrMYC2 activity, accompanied by decreased expression of ORCA3 and STR (Fig. 1b). This effect is mediated by RMT1, which is activated by BIS1 (Fig. 2b). RMT1 antagonizes CrMYC2 activity by competing for the same binding sites on the target promoters (Fig. 3a). Such differential repression of pathway genes would affect the accumulation of precursors from the indole or iridoid branch, resulting in substrate flux imbalance. CrCOI1 sits atop the regulatory network to coordinately regulate the expression of the activators and repressors (right panel, Fig. 8). On perception of the JA signal, CrCOI1 interacts with the CrJAZ proteins and presents them to the 26S/UPS (ubiquitin proteasome system) for degradation. De-repression of the activators simultaneously activates both BIS1- and CrMYC2-dependent cascades. The elucidation of the negative regulatory loop, involving CrJAZ and RMT1, provides an explanation of why the upregulation of one of the two cascades does not lead to an increase in MIA. The repressors probably participate in the fine-tuning of the magnitudes of activation of CrMYC2/ORCA3 and BIS1, consequently determining the accumulation of MIA. Collectively, our work reveals the molecular mechanism by which the Catharanthus SCF $^{\mathrm{COI1}}$ complex controls an array of activators and repressors in order to overcome the substrate flux imbalance during MIA production.
