# Glycosylated cannabinoids in Cannabis sativa and enzyme design to modulate their synthesis

Zoe Pinkasa , Olga Khersonskyb , Paula Bermana , Nikolay Kuzmichc , Ilana Rogacheva , Sarel J. Fleishmanb , and Asaph Aharonia,1 

Affiliations are included on p. 11. 

Edited by Dean DellaPenna, Michigan State University, East Lansing, MI; received June 19, 2025; accepted August 20, 2025 

Despite extensive study of its chemical composition and long history of medicinal use, the occurrence of glycosylated cannabinoid derivatives in Cannabis sativa has not been documented to date. Here, we identified glycosylated cannabinoids and their common intermediate olivetolic acid (OA) in various $C .$ sativa tissues and cultivars. We moreover identified four UDP-glycosyltransferases (UGTs) from C. sativa with OA glycosylating activity. Enhancing the water solubility of cannabinoids through glycosylation holds potential for pharmaceutical development and cosmetic applications. However, glycosylation of pathway intermediates such as OA may divert metabolic flux away from cannabinoid production, complicating efforts to engineer glycosylated forms. To resolve this, we applied FuncLib design to an AlphaFold-predicted structure of one of the identified enzymes CsUGT14, generating active-site variants. Through functional screening, we identified mutants that display increased specificity toward cannabinoid end products over the OA intermediate. Moreover, we recognized a single point mutation that dictates OA positioning within the active site, thereby altering isomer formation. These findings expand the known repertoire of natural cannabinoids and provide a rare example of crystallography-free enzyme design to improve stability, substrate selectivity, and isomer specificity. Furthermore, this work lays the foundation for the tailored biosynthesis of soluble glycosylated cannabinoids in heterologous systems.

Cannabis sativa is a renowned plant used for seed oil and fiber production from its subspecies hemp and for medicinal and recreational applications from the drug type subspecies “Marijuana” ( 1 ). The medicinal properties of $C .$ . sativa arise from cannabinoids, a group of approximately 140 terpenophenolic compounds that interact with the endocannabinoid system. Major cannabinoids include $\Delta ^ { 9 }$ -tetrahydrocannabinol (THC), cannabidiol (CBD), cannabigerol (CBG), cannabinol (CBN), and cannabichrome (CBC), all initially synthesized in their acidic forms and can undergo decarboxylation by heat or time; for example, $\Delta 9$ -tetrahydrocannabinolic acid (THCA) is converted into THC. While neutral type cannabinoids are generally more bioactive, all forms exhibit some level of pharmacological activities ( 2 , 3 ). 

Cannabinoid biosynthesis takes place in glandular trichomes and involves multiple cellular compartments. The process begins in the cytosol where an acyl-activating enzyme (AAE) converts hexanoate to hexanoyl-CoA ( 4 ). Then, olivetol synthase (OLS) and olivetolic acid cyclase (OAC) catalyze the formation of olivetolic acid (OA) ( 5 ). Olivetolic acid enters the chloroplast, where it is condensed with geranyl diphosphate (GPP) from the methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway, by a prenyl-transferase called GOT ( 6 ), forming CBGA, the precursor for all pentyl-type cannabinoids. Further steps take place as CBGA is exported out of the chloroplast and secretory cells into the extracellular space of the trichomes’ storage cavity, where THCA synthase, CBDA synthase, and CBCA synthase catalyze the conversion of CBGA into their respective cannabinoids ( 7 ) ( Fig. 1A ). Additional cannabinoids can form naturally over time, such as CBNA, which arises from THCA degradation ( 8 ). Further diversity results from the incorporation of fatty acid intermediate with varying side-chain lengths, such as the C3 divarinic acid (DA), which leads to the formation of $\Delta ^ { 9 }$ -tetrahydrocannabivarinic acid (THCVA) and other varinic cannabinoids ( 9 , 10 ). 

Due to the commercial potential of heterologous production of cannabinoids, there are long-standing attempts to improve yield, reduce costs, and generate novel cannabinoids in various systems ( 11 , 12 ). Efforts to engineer plants such as Nicotiana benthamiana have been hampered by low product yields partially owing to intrinsic native glycosylation of OA and CBGA ( 13 ). # Significance

Cannabinoids are the primary active compounds in Cannabis sativa and hold significant medical potential. However, their high lipophilicity limits bioavailability, an issue which can be resolved by promiscuous glycosylation. Here, we reveal glycosylation as a natural modification occurring in the Cannabis plant. We detected glycosylated cannabinoids and intermediates and identified native glycosyltransferases involved in this process. Using merely computational structure prediction tools, we engineered enzyme mutant variants with enhanced activity, altered isomer production, and selectivity toward cannabinoids, without relying on crystal structures. These findings provide insight into cannabinoid metabolism and demonstrate a broadly applicable strategy for rational enzyme design, enabling the development of cannabinoid derivatives for medical and industrial applications.

Glycosylation has also been suggested as a possible way to modify cannabinoids, which are hydrophobic compounds with poor water solubility ( 15 , 16 ). This modification can not only alter their bioavailability but also modulate their interactions with biological targets, potentially leading to improved bioactivities ( 17 ). Several promiscuous enzymes from non-cannabinoid-producing plants, such as Stevia rebaudiana and 

Oryza sativa , have been reported to glycosylate cannabinoids in vitro ( 13 , 16 ). More recently, cannabinoid glycosylation was demonstrated in yeast using an enzyme from Catharanthus roseus ( 18 ). 

Despite the prevalence of glycosylation in plant specialized metabolism, glycosylated cannabinoids have only been naturally observed so far in Helichrysum umbraculigerum . This South-African plant does not produce THCA or CBDA but synthesizes CBGA 

and a variety of CBGA-type analogues, including the unique aromatic compound heliCBGA, which is derived from dihydrostilbenic acid (DHSA) instead of OA via a parallel pathway. Helichrysum umbraculigerum accumulates glycosylated forms of both intermediates (e.g., OA and DHSA) and cannabinoids (e.g. CBGA and heliCBGA). It comprises several glycosyltransferases capable of glycosylating cannabinoids and their intermediates in vitro ( 19 ). 

In plants, glycosylation is typically catalyzed by uridine diphosphate (UDP)-dependent glycosyltransferases (UGTs), which transfer UDP-sugars onto small molecules. Despite low sequence homology, crystallography of over 30 plant UGTs reveals a similar 3D structure ( 20 ). The C-terminal domain, which harbors the sugar donor binding site, is more conserved and typically exhibits high specificity for the sugar molecule. The main sugar donors include UDP-galactose, UDP-xylose, UDP-mannose, and UDP-glucuronic acid, with UDP-glucose (UDP-Glc) being the most commonly used by plant UGTs. The acceptor pocket is formed by more variable regions in the N-terminal domain, leading to considerable diversity in substrate preferences and promiscuity levels among UGTs ( 21 ). Additionally, variations in the positioning of the sugar acceptor within the active site can result in glycosylation at a certain position and formation of distinct glycosylation isomers, a trait known as regiospecificity ( 22 ). 

Plant UGTs have been extensively studied, not only for their natural roles but also for their applications in metabolic engineering and natural-product biosynthesis. When looking to improve efficiency of native enzymes such as UGTs, protein engineering offers a powerful strategy to enhance activity and tailor substrate specificity. For instance, to improve the glycosylation efficiency of the triterpene-specific UGT74AC1 from Siraitia grosvenorii , error-prone PCR was used to generate 5,000 random clones. A colorimetric screening assay identified five critical amino acid positions, and combining the beneficial mutations led to a 200-fold increase in catalytic efficiency ( 23 ). In contrast, rational design relies on structural insight to guide site-directed mutagenesis, typically requiring fewer variants and minimal screening. This approach is often limited by the need for high-resolution structural data, traditionally obtained through X-ray crystallography, a costly and technically demanding process ( 24 ). A notable example is UGT74AN2 from Calotropis gigantea , for which resolved crystal structures complexed with various cardiotonic steroid acceptors enabled the generation of a triple mutant with dramatically improved activity toward alternative sugar donors such as UDP-glucuronic acid and UDP-galactose ( 25 ). Latest strategies in protein engineering rely on structure prediction tools such as AlphaFold, which can reduce the need for experimental structure determination ( 26 ), as demonstrated in this study. 

Despite numerous studies profiling cannabinoids in C. sativa, not a single one reported the natural existence of their glycosylated forms. In this study, we detected glycosylated cannabinoids and intermediates in $C .$ sativa , at levels comparable to other key intermediates. Additionally, we identified four UGT enzymes from C. sativa , that are capable of glycosylating OA in vitro generating diverse isomers including one that is not found in the plant. Among them, CsUGT11 and CsUGT14 were further characterized, revealing differences in gene expression, substrate specificity, and kinetic properties. We then focused on CsUGT14, and through two rounds of combinatorial point mutations guided by AlphaFold-based structural predictions and docking simulations, successfully stabilized the enzyme for in vitro expression, altered its catalytic activity and regiospecificity, and enhanced its glycosylation efficiency toward cannabinoid substrates. Our findings provide direct evidence for the presence of glycosylated 

cannabinoids in $C .$ . sativa and demonstrate a successful case of UGT engineering for tailored enzymatic activity, achieved solely through computational modeling without the need for an experimentally determined structure. 

# Results

C. sativa Accumulates Glycosylated Cannabinoids and Key Intermediates. At the outset of this study, we conducted a broad chemical screen of $2 0 ~ C .$ . sativa cultivars, using high-resolution LC–MS with both analytical standards and published spectra (27) (SI  Appendix, Table  S1). In total, we detected 28 known acidic cannabinoids of five major subclasses: CBGA, THCA, CBDA, CBNA, and CBCA (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). We selected five cultivars for further study based on their distinct chemotypes: high C3 (varinic) cannabinoid (M16), high THCA (M17), high CBDA (M18), a Hemp low-cannabinoid cultivar (M19), and CBGA-dominant (M20). 

To date, glycosylated cannabinoids have not been reported in C. sativa , and similarly here in our initial chemical screen, we were unable to detect such metabolites. To perform targeted screen in the absence of glycosylated standards, we conducted recombinant enzyme assays using a previously characterized O. sativa UGT (OsUGT) reported to glycosylate OA and other cannabinoids in vitro ( 16 , 19 ). The recombinant enzyme assays were conducted with OA, DA, CBGA, THCA, CBDA, CBCA, or CBNA as acceptors, and UDP-Glc as the sugar donor. Chemical analysis of the products from the enzyme assays revealed metabolites with the expected m/z values corresponding to mono- and diglucosides of all acceptors (m/z shift from nonglycosylated metabolite of 162.053 and 324.106, respectively), which were further used as references for targeted metabolite searches. When multiple glycosylated isomers were detected, they were labeled #1, #2, or #3 in order of increasing retention times (as detailed in SI Appendix, Table S2 ). We next chose female flowers and leaves for targeted analysis of glycosylated forms in $C .$ . sativa , as these tissues are enriched in cannabinoids ( Fig. 1B ). In this approach, we successfully detected glycosylated OA and DA in the five analyzed cultivars and in both tissue types. Glycosylated CBGA and CBDA were detected in the floral tissues of all cultivars examined, as well as in the foliar tissues of a subset of cultivars ( Fig. $1 B$ and SI Appendix, Figs. S2 and S3 and Table S2 ). Although recombinant OsUGT produces two isomers of glycosylated OA and DA, only OAG1 and DAG1 were observed in planta ( Fig. 1B ). We were also unable to detect diglucosides in any of the C. sativa cultivars (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). In some flower samples we detected metabolites with m/z values and retention times matching those of THCA and CBNA glycosides. However, the signals were too weak to extract a clean MS/MS fragmentation spectra, leaving the identification of these compounds uncertain. Glycosylated CBCA was not detected in any of the plant samples examined. 

Glycosylation is known to alter the cellular localization of small molecules ( 20 ). Since OA is synthesized in the cytosol but enters the chloroplast to be converted to CBGA, we hypothesized that OA glycosylation may serve as a regulatory checkpoint in the cannabinoid biosynthetic pathway ( Fig. 1A ). Therefore, we set to further understand the role of intermediate glycosylation and its relationship with the cannabinoid pathway throughout plant development. We collected from a single high THCA cultivar root, stem, and leaf tissues 3 wk following flowering initiation, and flower tissues 3, 6, 9, and 12 wk from flowering initiation. In those tissues, we wanted to measure the concentrations of OA, OAG1, OAG3, CBGA, THCA, CBDA, and THCVA. In the absence of an analytical standard of OAG1 and OAG3 required for measuring absolute 

concentration, we performed enzymatic hydrolysis using a commercial almond $\beta$ -glucosidase on a purified OAG1 fraction. The resulting release of OA was monitored over time by LC–MS, and absolute OA concentrations were used to extrapolate the levels of OAG1 and OAG3 in $C .$ . sativa tissues (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). 

In mature female flowers, we detected both OAG1 and OAG3 ( Fig. 2A ). The compounds followed an accumulation pattern similar to that of major cannabinoids, with peak levels observed at 9 wk after the onset of flowering ( Fig. 2B ). To enable direct comparison between metabolites, we assessed their absolute concentration. As expected, THCA was by far the most abundant compound, reaching a maximum of $5 8 , 1 2 6 ~ \mathrm { p p m }$ . This was followed by CBGA at $1 { , } 7 3 0 \mathrm { p p m }$ , THCVA at 256 ppm, and CBDA at $9 2 \mathrm { p p m }$ ( Fig. $2 C$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S6B ). In contrast, pathway intermediates and their glycosylated forms were present at much lower levels. We measured OAG1 maximum at $3 . 1 2 \mathrm { p p m }$ , OAG3 at $1 . 7 5 \mathrm { p p m }$ , and free OA at only 1.5 ppm ( Fig. 2C ). 

C. sativa UGT Glycosylating Cannabinoids and Their Intermediates. To identify enzymes forming glycosylated OA and cannabinoids, we extracted all genes annotated as UGTs from the C. sativa genome (Dataset S1). We generated a phylogenetic tree and selected candidates that clustered with other UGTs shown to glycosylate cannabinoids from $H .$ umbraculigerum, O. sativa, and Stevia rebaudiana (19) (Fig. 3A). To reinforce the selection of candidate UGTs, we performed differential gene expression analysis using three published transcriptomics datasets from various $C .$ . sativa tissues (28), trichome types (29), and cultivars 

(30). All libraries were normalized by counts per million (CPM) normalization and compared for identifying UGTs with similar expression patterns to cannabinoid pathway genes (i.e., highly expressed in female flowers and lowly expressed in roots, stems, and male flowers; Fig. 3B). 

Following this analysis, we selected 17 C. sativa UGT candidates (CsUGT1- CsUGT17 ) for an enzyme activity screen. The entire set of UGTs was individually expressed in Escherichia coli, and recombinant protein assays using lysates were analyzed for enzyme activity with OA and UDP-Glc (SI Appendix, Fig. S7A ). Four out of the 17 candidates (i.e., CsUGT3, CsUGT5, CsUGT11, and CsUGT14) glycosylated OA in vitro. The results were confirmed with purified enzymes and CsUGT14 appeared to be the most active. Interestingly, the 4 candidates synthesized the isomer OAG2, which was not observed in the plant extracts. We confirmed that this is indeed an OAG structural isomer by conducting an enzyme assay with a UGT from S. rebaudiana (SrUGT), which was reported to produce 3 OAG isomers in vitro ( 19 ). CsUGT11 was the only enzyme which produced predominantly OAG1, the common isomer found in the plant (SI Appendix, Fig. S7B ). Alas, none of the CsUGT candidates produced OAG3 in vitro. We next measured the expression levels of the active CsUGT genes in various tissues of a single high THCA plant and found that CsUGT3 , CsUGT11, and CsUGT14 showed significantly elevated expression levels in flowers across all flowering stages compared to leaves, while CsUGT5 exhibited a similar trend, though not statistically significant ( Fig. 3C ). We next focused on two candidates and examined the enzyme activity of CsUGT11 and CsUGT14 with UDP-Glc and different cannabinoid substrates ( Fig. 4A ). Both UGTs were active with the pathway intermediates OA and DA. In terms of regioselectivity, each enzyme produced a distinct isomer not only for OA, as previously described, but also for DA; CsUGT11 produced DAG1, the common isomer found in the plant while CsUGT14 formed DAG3 which we did not detect in plants. With cannabinoid as sugar acceptors, CsUGT14 displayed broader substrate specificity, glycosylating 10 out of the 12 examined substrates, i.e., OA, DA, CBGA, CBDA, THCA, CBCA, CBNA, THCVA, CBG, and CBD. The enzyme generated a single isomer for each glycosylated cannabinoid, except in the case of CBGA, for which it produced both CBGA-G1 and CBGA-G2 isomers. Kinetic assays revealed 

that CsUGT14 has a higher catalytic efficiency $( k _ { c a t } / K _ { m } )$ than CsUGT11 for both OA and UDP-Glc ( Fig. 4B ). 

Computational Design of the CsUGT14 Enzyme Induces a Pronounced Shift in the Isomeric Form of Olivetolic Acid. In contrast to most plant pathways of specialized metabolites, in which end products are glycosylated to high degree, the cannabinoid pathway displays significant glycosylation of intermediates (i.e., OA) and relatively little glycosylation of end products. Glycosylation of the intermediate OA diverts the pathway, posing a challenge for end product cannabinoid production in heterologous systems (13). The high $K _ { m }$ and $k _ { c a t }$ of CsUGT14 for OA suggest limited activity at low substrate concentrations, but rapid catalysis when OA is abundant. The docking simulations with OA were based on the OAG1 isomer, as it is the only OAG isomer structurally elucidated via NMR (19). Next, we used the FuncLib active-site design algorithm, which combines Rosetta atomistic energy calculations with phylogenetic data to design multipoint substitutions that are both structurally tolerated and are in accordance with natural evolution (32). Using FuncLib, we designed 33 variants of CsUGT14 with 5 to 7 designs tailored for each substrate (M1-M33). Each variant carried 2-to-5-point mutations localized to the active-site wall (first shell), while deliberately avoiding changes to the catalytic residues or the cofactor-binding site (SI  Appendix, Table  S3 and Dataset  S3). Each of the enzyme variants was individually cloned into $E _ { \ast }$ . coli, purified, and assayed in vitro with the five different substrates used for molecular docking. 

We found that 20 variants produced predominantly OAG1 instead of OAG2, which is the common isomer synthesized by the wild type (WT) enzyme ( Fig. 5A ). However, only mild improvements in activity with various cannabinoids were observed (SI Appendix, Fig. S9 ). During the course of this study, AlphaFold3 became publicly available ( 33 ) and was subsequently used to understand the effect of FuncLib mutations on enzymatic activity and isomerization patterns. In the 20 variants carrying Glu86Gln mutation, AlphaFold3 accurately predicted a substrate positioning that 

favors the formation of the OAG1 isomer. We therefore suggest that this shift in isomerization resulted from electrostatic repulsion between the OA carboxyl group and the Glu86 side chain, which is relieved upon substitution with the Gln residue. Docking predictions also suggested that in the OAG2 isomer, the glucose group is likely attached to the carboxyl moiety of OA ( Fig. 5 B and C ). 

Computational Protein Design–Based Enhancement of CsUGT14 Protein Stability and Specific Enzyme Activity Toward Cannabinoids. We hypothesized that many of our early designs could be inactive due to the marginal stability and expression of CsUGT14, and therefore used the Protein Repair One Stop Shop (PROSS) (34) algorithm, which is based on the same principles as FuncLib, but introduces mutations outside of the active site. We tested a PROSS design (CsUGT14-Stbl) encoding 50 mutations (SI Appendix, Fig. S10) and found enhanced expression in E. coli and a $\bar { 1 } 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ increase in apparent melting temperature (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). We also sought to compare the kinetic properties of the stabilized CsUGT14 variant to the native enzyme. To do this accurately, it was necessary to reevaluate the kinetic parameters of the recombinant CsUGT14, as the WT enzyme used in this analysis had undergone codon optimization for expression in $E$ . coli, in contrast to the previously characterized version amplified directly from C. sativa. The results revealed that CsUGT14-Stbl exhibited altered kinetic behavior relative to the WT enzyme, showing slightly reduced affinity for both OA and UDP-Glc, yet demonstrating a higher catalytic turnover rate for both substrates (SI Appendix, Fig. S12).

Interestingly, some variants displayed different isomerization patterns depending on whether the mutations were introduced on WT or stabilized background ( Fig. 5A and SI Appendix, Fig. S13 ). For instance, all the designs for OA produced the docked OAG1 isomer on the WT background, while half of them produced the OAG2 isomer on the stable PROSS background. The computational models, however, did not suggest any changes in the active site. We speculate that the activity differences are due to variances in the dynamics of the WT and the PROSS-stabilized design, in light of the loops surrounding the active-site pocket (SI Appendix, Fig. S14 ). 

Structural analysis based on AlphaFold3 docking further suggested that many of the improved designs possess an expanded active-site pocket, enabling better accommodation of the aliphatic side chains present in cannabinoid substrates ( Fig. 6 $D$ and $E$ ). Based on these experimental and docking results, we sought to further improve the activity of CsUGT14 and performed a second round of active site design using FuncLib. Eighteen additional variants (B1-B18) were designed, introducing mutations within the active site of variant S31, one of the most improved designs from the first round of mutations (SI Appendix, Table S4 ). While only mild improvements in glycosylation activity were obtained with CBGA and CBDA (SI Appendix, Fig. S15 ), design B4 demonstrated substantial improvement in activity with CBCA and THCA substrates ( Fig. 6C ), which can be explained by further enlargement of the active site and ligand positioning in a productive mode for THCA glycosylation ( Fig. 6F ). Interestingly, some of the new variants also produced a previously undetected glycosylated THCA isomer (THCA-G1), which was absent in assays with OsUGT and appeared at very low levels with SrUGT. In addition, we detected OAG3 produced by CsUGT14 variants (SI Appendix, Fig. S16 ). 

In summary, we used a rational design approach based on structural predictions to engineer CsUGT14 variants with improved activity toward cannabinoid substrates. By combining active-site designs from FuncLib with a stabilized PROSS background, we generated variants with increased activity toward cannabinoid glycosylation. AlphaFold3 docking analyses helped rationalize differences in isomerization patterns, revealing that a single Glu86Gln mutation altered substrate orientation and active-site interactions. 

# Discussion

Cannabinoids and Their Intermediates Are Glycosylated in C. sativa. In this study, we report on naturally occurring glycosylated cannabinoids in $C .$ . sativa. Notably, we detected OA, DA, CBGA, and CBDA glycosides across all tested cultivars and tissues. To date, native glycosylated cannabinoids have only been reported in $H .$ . umbraculigerum, an Asteraceae plant unrelated to $C .$ . sativa, which produces CBGA and CBGA-type cannabinoids (19). Correlating with our findings, this plant glycosylates both cannabinoid and their intermediates. Interestingly, as in $C .$ . sativa, diglucosylated metabolites were not detected in $H .$ umbraculigerum plant extracts, although they may form during in vitro assays with promiscuous UGT enzymes. Helichrysum umbraculigerum also produces acylated CBGA and HeliCBGA; however, we did not detect any acylated cannabinoids in C. sativa. While levels of glycosylated cannabinoids were not quantified in $H .$ . umbraculigerum, in $C .$ . sativa, we examined the accumulation profile and concentration of OAG1 and OAG3 which were found to be similar to OA levels. 

Glycosylation of metabolites often alters their subcellular localization. For example, in tobacco, a cytosolic salicylic acid (SA) glucosyltransferase produces SA glycosides that are subsequently 

sequestered in the vacuole ( 35 ). Similarly, glycosylation of intermediates may serve as a regulatory mechanism in compartmentalized pathways such as in cannabinoid biosynthesis, manipulating the cytosol-chloroplast balance of the free OA intermediate. However, to better understand the function of cannabinoid glycosylation will require generating transgenic plants with either silenced or overexpressing UGT genes, which was beyond the scope of this study. Exploring the function of the identified CsUGTs in other plant systems may also provide insights; yet in our attempts to overexpress these genes in $N .$ . benthamiana and other plant species, high levels of intrinsic, nonspecific OA glycosylation obscured the effects of the introduced CsUGT enzymes. 

It is intriguing that, despite extensive efforts to detect and identify the full repertoire of cannabinoids in $C .$ . sativa , glycosides have yet to be reported. It is even more surprising given the demonstration of highly promiscuous enzymes from $O$ . sativa , S. rebaudiana, and C. roseus that are highly active with cannabinoid substrates ( 16 , 18 ), and moreover, the attempts to engineer cannabinoids in $N .$ . benthamiana that showed significant endogenous glycosylation activity with OA ( 13 ). Despite the relatively late discovery of glycosylated cannabinoids, the findings here show that $C .$ sativa cannabinoids are not different than other classes of specialized metabolites and include high structural diversity that is in part due to glycosylation. 

C. sativa Produces Two Isomers of Glycosylated Olivetolic Acid and Cannabinoids. Through a genome-wide survey, we identified four CsUGTs capable of glycosylating OA in in vitro assays. While CsUGT3, CsUGT11, and CsUGT14 belong to the UGT71K1 and UGT89A2 families previously associated with OA glycosylation (19), CsUGT5 is a member of the UGT90A1 family, which had not been reported to possess such activity. Notably, recombinant CsUGT3, CsUGT5, and CsUGT14 all produced the OAG2 isomer in vitro, which we could not detect in C. sativa, while CsUGT11 was the only enzyme that produced OAG1, the natural isomer detected in the plant. In terms of substrate specificity, we examined two candidates and found that CsUGT11 displayed narrower activity, glycosylating only the pathway intermediates OA and DA, whereas CsUGT14 exhibited broader activity in vitro, glycosylating CBDA and CBGA (also detected in $C .$ sativa), as well as THCA, CBCA, CBNA, THCVA, CBG, and CBD which were not detected in the plant, or were observed in too low quantities for absolute identification. It remains to be determined whether the glycosylated CBGA and CBDA observed in the plant are derived from OAG1 produced by CsUGT11 or instead result from direct glycosylation of the core cannabinoids, generated by the CsUGT14 enzyme. 

Rationalized Computational Enzyme Design Improves UGT Protein Stability and Increases Cannabinoid Glycosylation as Compared to the Intermediate Olivetolic Acid. Stabilization of CsUGT14 yielded a protein with higher expression levels and thermal stability. The stable variant was also more active relative to the unmodified protein, probably due to higher proportion of properly folded protein. Enzyme variants with active site modifications on the stable background showed improved glycosylation of cannabinoid end products, notably reflected in a higher glycosylation ratio of cannabinoids to OA. Second round of mutations, designed with the same principle on the background of the most improved variant from the first round, boosted CBCA and THCA glycosylation activity even future. Remarkably, these improvements were achieved without the use of crystallographic data and despite the challenges of working with UGTs, which generally show low sequence homology, highly 

flexible loop regions and are typically modeled based on human proteins (36, 37). This is a demonstration of AlphaFold3 (33) predictions accuracy and potential as a powerful tool for structural and functional enzyme analysis. 

C. sativa is a medicinally important plant that has shown therapeutic potential for the treatment of epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, chronic pain, inflammatory bowel disease, various types of cancer, and many other diseases or conditions ( 38 , 39 ). Yet the active compounds in $C .$ sativa , cannabinoids, are not used as standalone drugs, except for synthetic THC. Cannabinoids are highly lipophilic, which poses challenges for their pharmaceutical application due to poor aqueous solubility, limited absorption, and low bioavailability. 

Glycosylation, even when applied promiscuously (as in glycorandomization), is a powerful and flexible tool that can alter a molecule’s properties and is useful for new drug development. This was demonstrated with digitoxin, a phytosteroid used for the treatment of heart failure, where oxidation followed by neoglycosylation produced analogs with altered cytotoxicity and improved anticancer activity ( 40 ). Glycosylation is also a well-documented strategy for enhancing the water solubility of small molecules; therefore, glycosylation of cannabinoids could facilitate the development of improved formulations. Our findings open up possibilities for drug development and demonstrate the potential in creating tailored biocatalysts for specific applications. 

# Methods

Plant Material and Growth Conditions. Unless otherwise stated, C. sativa cultivars used in this study were feminized plants propagated from cuttings and obtained from the Agricultural Research Organization, Volcani Center, Israel. Hermaphrodite hemp plants were purchased as edible seeds of the brand “DEVERT”. Plants were grown under stable conditions at a temperature of $2 4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ During the vegetative growth stage, plants were exposed to $\textstyle { 1 6 \ h / 8 \ h }$ (light/dark) photoperiod. For flowering induction and growth, the photoperiod was adjusted to $1 2 \mathsf { h } / 1 2 \mathsf { h }$ (light/dark). 

Metabolite Analysis by LC–MS. For cannabinoid extraction, 100 mg of frozen plant tissue was resuspended in $3 0 0 \mu \iota$ ethanol, followed by vortexing. The suspension was incubated while shaking at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 10 min and sonicated for 20 min. Finally, the extracts were centrifuged for 5 min at maximum speed and filtered through $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filters. For glycosylated cannabinoids, methanol was used instead of ethanol. Samples were analyzed using an ultraperformance liquid chromatograph (UPLC, Waters Acquity) with a diode array detector (DAD) connected to various MS detectors, equipped with an electrospray ionization source. The following MS detectors were used: a quadrupole time-of-flight MS (XEVO qTOF systems, Waters) for cannabinoid library screening, a triple quadrupole MS (XEVO TQ-S systems, Waters) for enzyme kinetics, and an Orbitrap Exploris 240 or IQX MS (Thermo Scientific) for all other LC–MS analysis. Metabolites were separated using the UPLC HSS T3 column for diglycosides, or UPLC BEH C18 (Waters Acquity) in all other cases, with water and acetonitrile containing $0 . 1 \%$ FA as mobile phases. Detailed LC–MS parameters for each experiment are provided in SI Appendix, Supplementary Method S1. Chemical standards used were ${ > } 9 5 \%$ pure unless otherwise stated. All cannabinoids were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Rehovot, Israel), except for THCA which was purchased from Silicol Scientific Equipment Ltd. (Or Yehuda, Israel). OA $( > 9 0 \%$ pure) and DA $( > 9 0 \%$ pure) were purchased from Cayman Chemical (Ann Arbor, MI). 

Absolute Quantification of OA, OAG1, OAG3, and Cannabinoids. Absolute quantification of OA, THCA, CBDA, CBGA, and THCVA was performed using external calibration curves injected alongside plant extracts. Samples from flowers, leaves, and stems were further diluted in ethanol to fit within the linear range of the calibration curves. Due to the lack of an analytical standard for OAG1 or OAG3, semiquantification was performed using the calibration curve of OA and enzymatic hydrolysis of purified OAG1 isolated from H. umbraculigerum leaf 

extracts by medium-pressure liquid chromatography (SI Appendix, Fig. S5, full details appear in SI Appendix, Supplementary Method 2). 

Phylogenetic Comparison. The C. sativa reference genome CS10 (RefSeq GCF_900626175.2) was retrieved from NCBI, and all genes annotated as UGTs were selected for analysis. The UGT sequences from H. umbraculigerum were obtained from Berman et al. (19). Sequence alignments and a maximum likelihood tree were constructed in the MEGA11 software with 1,000 bootstrap replications (41). The 117 amino acid sequences used in this analysis are provided in Dataset S1. 

Differential Expression Analysis. Fifty-nine libraries from three published transcriptomes of C. sativa containing different tissues (28), trichome types (29), and cultivars (30) were used to mine UGT sequences. Using the “edgeR” R package, raw counts were normalized by CMP normalization, and low-expressed genes were filtered out (Dataset S2). Genes previously known to participate in cannabinoid biosynthesis were singled out, and the data were visualized in heatmap using Excel. 

qRT-PCR of UGTs and Cannabinoid Pathway Genes. C. sativa RNA was isolated from stems and roots using the “Spectrum™ plant total RNA” kit (Sigma-Aldrich), and from flowers and leaves using the hot phenol method. RNA extracts were treated with DNase-1 (Sigma-Aldrich), and cDNA was prepared using “Highcapacity cDNA Reverse Transcription Kit” (Applied Biosystems) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Specific gene primers were designed using Primer3web (version 4.1.0) and are listed in SI Appendix, Table S5. Quantitativereal-time PCR was performed with Fast SYBR Green reagent (Applied Biosystems) in a Quantstudio RT-PCR system (Applied Biosystems). The fold change was normalized to CsActin $3 ^ { - \Delta \Delta c t }$ gene levels and leaves tissue, according to the formula $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta c t }$ t. 

Recombinant Protein Expression. Coding sequences of candidate UGTs, were amplified from a cDNA mix of C. sativa leaves and flowers, using “Phusion® HF” DNA polymerase (NEB) and primers specified in SI Appendix, Table S5. The genes for CsUGT5, CsUGT6, CsUGT15, and CsUGT16 could not be amplified and were therefore synthesized as gene fragments by Twist Bioscience. Genes were cloned into pET28a or pCold-TF vectors and transformed into E. coli BL21(DE3) pLysS cells. For CsUGT14 variants screen, gene constructs (M1-M33 and B1-B18 variants) were codon optimized for E. coli expression, synthetized, and cloned into the pET-29b(+) vector using NdeI and XhoI restriction sites by Twist Bioscience. For generating mutants in the CsUGT14-stbl background (S1–S33), a 500 bp segment was synthesize and cloned into a preconstructed pET- $\cdot 2 9 \mathsf { b } ( + )$ -stbl using one-step isothermal DNA assembly (42). All constructs were sequenced to verify integrity and transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells. For protein induction, transformed cells were grown overnight in Luria-Bertani (LB) medium supplemented with appropriate antibiotics. The culture was diluted into 50 mL fresh LB medium and incubated a $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ while shaking, unti $0 \mathsf { D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.6 was reached. The cells were then cooled down to ${ 1 6 } ^ { \circ } \complement _ { \prime }$ , and isopropy $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added to a final concentration of 1 mM. The cultures were incubated overnight at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \complement$ while shaking and collected by centrifugation a $4 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement .$ . The bacterial pellets were lysed by sonication in lysis buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, $1 0 \%$ glycerol, 0.5 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, protease inhibitor [Sigma-Aldrich] and $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ lysozyme). The resulting lysates were directly used for activity assays by LC–MS. 

Computational Design Methods. The structure of CsUGT14 was modeled using AlphaFold2 ColabFold web server (43). The ligands were docked using the protocol specified in the SI Appendix, Supplementary Method 3. Stabilization was performed using PROSS2 web server (44) and a design carrying 50 point mutation was chosen for characterization. Active site design was performed by the FuncLib algorithm (32), using the pssm scores obtained from PROSS2. Active site residues in direct contact with the ligands were chosen for design (Gly13, Phe43, Lys46, Leu49, Glu86, Ile89, Phe151, Met155, Thr192, Tyr204, Leu207, and Phe211), cutoffs of 0 for pssm scores and $+ 2 . 0$ for ΔΔG were used to constrain the sequence space. Designs with 2 to 5 mutations were modeled and ranked by total protein score, and 5 to 7 designs for each substrate from the top 30 were manually selected for experimental characterization (SI Appendix, Table S3). For design at round 2, the variant M31 was modeled with cannabinoid ligands 

and UDP-Glc with AlphaFold3. The AlphaFold3 package was locally installed as described in https://github.com/google-deepmind/alphafold3. Active site design was performed as at Round 1, and the designed residues were I85, M89, F125, M151, M155, M204, and L207 (SI Appendix, Table S4). 

CsUGT14 Mutation Variant Expression and Purification. Transformed BL21 cells were grown overnight in 0.5 mL 2YT medium supplemented with appropriate antibiotics. The following day, $2 0 0 \mu \iota$ of the grown culture was transferred into 10 mL fresh 2YT medium containing antibiotics and incubated a $3 7 ^ { \circ } \complement$ while shaking at 220 RPM until an $0 \mathsf { D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.6 was reached. Protein expression was induced by adding IPTG to a final concentration of 0.2 mM followed by incubation at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $2 0 \mathsf { h }$ with shaking. Cells were then harvested by centrifugation at $4 , 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min and stored overnight at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \complement$ . Frozen cell pellets were resuspended in $2 5 0 \mu \iota$ lysis buffer (100 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 300 mM NaCl, 10 mM imidazole, 1:10,000 benzonase nuclease [Sigma-Aldrich], and $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ lysozyme), vortexed thoroughly, and lysed by sonication. The lysate was centrifuged at max speed for 10 min, and the supernatant was transferred to a new 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube containing $1 0 0 \mu \iota$ of prewashed Ni-NTA beads (ADAR biotech). The mixture was incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \complement$ with rotation for 3 h. For washing, the beads were resuspended in 1 mL of 20 mM imidazole solution, followed by elution with 200 µL of 250 mM imidazole. The imidazole eluate was cleaned and concentrated by Amicon Ultra 10 K centrifugal filters (Sigma-Aldrich), according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The purified protein was immediately used for activity assays. Peak areas were converted to $\mu \ g / \ m \ L$ according to an appropriate calibration curve, and the results were normalized to protein concentration, quantified from SDS–PAGE gel images (SI Appendix, Fig. S17) using ImageJ software. 

Enzyme Assays and Kinetics. For the lysate assay, 80 mM UDP-Glc and 80 mM OA were added to $2 0 \mu \iota$ enzyme solution to a total volume of $5 0 \mu \iota$ in $1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ Tris-HCl buffer. The mixture was incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for $\harpoonleft ,$ after which the reaction was stopped by adding $5 0 ~ \mu \iota$ methanol. The solution was vortexed and 

centrifuged at maximum speed for 10 min, and the supernatant was collected for LC–MS analysis. For the purified enzyme assay, the protocol was adjusted to start from 100 mL culture. Protein purity was verified by running SDS–PAGE gel with instantBlue Coomassie protein stain (Abcam). Two microliter purified protein was combined with 80 mM UDP-Glc and 25 mM substrate to a total volume of 50 µL in 100 mM Tris-HCl buffer. The remaining steps were the same as those used for the lysate assay. Kinetic assays were performed with $2 \mu \ g$ purified enzymes, using varying $( 0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ to 3 mM) and constant (1 mM) concentrations of each substrate as previously described (19).
