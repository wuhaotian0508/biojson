# Transcriptomic and biochemical analyses of the accumulation of sucrose in mungbean (Vigna radiata (L.) Wilczek) leaves after pod removal

Jungmin ${ \mathsf { H } } \mathsf { a } ^ { 1 , 2 }$ · Sangrea Shim1,3 · Taeyoung Lee4  · Eunsoo Lee5  · Xuefei Yang5  · Haneul Jeong5  · Moon Young Kim1,5 · Suk‑Ha Lee $1 , 5 \oplus$

# Abstract

Key message Synchronous pod maturity is critical for increasing grain yield. The candidate genes involved in synchronous pod maturity were identifed through RNA-seq and HPLC.

Abstract Mungbean (Vigna radiata [L.] Wilczek), an important source of carbohydrate and protein in Asia, is characterized by nonsynchronous pod maturity; consequently, harvesting is labor intensive. Because pod maturity is associated with synthesis and remobilization of sucrose, we examined changes in sucrose levels and transcriptome in leaf (source) tissues after pod (sink) removal using two genotypes, VC1973A and V2984; VC1973A had higher synchronicity in pod maturity than V2984. After pod removal, much higher number of pods were produced in V2984 than VC1973A. The sucrose content of leaf tissues signifcantly decreased in V2984 because it continued to utilize assimilates from leaves for producing new pods, but signifcantly increased in VC1973A because of the loss of sink. Transcriptome analysis revealed that the number of diferentially expressed genes was approximately fourfold higher in VC1973A than in those of V2984 after pod removal. The expression of two paralogous genes (Vradi01g05010 and Vradi10g08240), encoding beta-glucosidase enzymes, signifcantly decreased in VC1973A after pod removal and was signifcantly lower in depodded VC1973A than depodded V2984, indicating these two genes may participate in sucrose utilization for seed development by regulating the level of glucose. The results of this study will help elucidate the genetic basis of synchronous pod maturity in mungbean and facilitate the development of new cultivars with synchronous pod maturity.

# Introduction

Mungbean (Vigna radiata [L.] Wilczek) is a legume cultivated mostly in South, East, and Southeast Asia for its edible seeds and sprouts which are an excellent cheap source of carbohydrates, protein, folate, and iron (Day 2013). Mungbean is a fast-growing crop with the ability to fx atmospheric nitrogen through symbiotic association with rhizobia and is perfect to grow in rotation with other grain crops, such as rice (Oryza sativa L.) and wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) (Graham and Vance 2003; Yaqub et al. 2010). Because of the nutritional and economical importance of mungbeans, the global consumption of mungbeans has increased, and the area of production has correspondingly increased up to 6 million ha over the last decade (Nair et al. 2012; Kim et al. 2015).

The harvest index of mungbean is relatively low because of nonsynchronous pod maturity compared with that of most soybean (Glycine max L.) cultivars, which have determinate

growth habit and synchronous maturity (Tian et al. 2010; Alam Mondal et  al. 2011). If harvesting is performed only once in mungbean, when most but not all, pods reach mature, because it keeps producing pods even after the frst harvest, a large portion of the yield potential is lost because early harvest accounts for only $\sim 5 0 \%$ of the total yield (Iqbal et al. 2015). However, delayed harvesting could also result in large yield losses because mature and dried pods are more likely to shatter or fall of the plant, and mungbean plants are more susceptible to pest and pathogen attack at later growth stages. To avoid yield loss, multiple harvests are needed; however, this involves additional time and labor. Moreover, each harvest must be performed carefully so that plants are not damaged, which makes the use of mechanical tools ineffcient (Iqbal et al. 2015).

Crop yield is highly dependent on the relationship between source and sink tissues, where newly developed sink tissues are supplied with assimilates from photosynthetic source tissues. In soybean, several studies have been conducted for understanding the efect of the alteration of source-sink ratio. To increase or decrease the number of pods and seeds in soybean, source activity in soybean has been modifed by increasing carbon dioxide $( \mathbf { C O } _ { 2 } )$ levels (Hardman and Brun 1971), irradiance (Schou et al. 1978), shading (Andrade and Ferreiro 1996), and defoliation (Board and Tan 1995). Depodding increases the supply of assimilates to the remaining seeds, resulting in larger seed size in soybean. (Egli et al. 1976). Growth habit, another important trait determining yield potential in grain legume, has been intensively studied in soybean. Shoot determinacy in soybean has been reported to be controlled by the shoot identity gene Dt1 (Tian et al. 2010) and a dominant MADS domain factor gene Dt2 (Ping et al. 2014). In the presence of Dt1, Dt2 produces semi-determinate plants with terminal racemes. In mungbean, although indeterminate stem growth has been reported to be modulated by a single gene, VrDet1 (Li et al. 2018), important agronomic traits, such as inforescence development and synchronous maturity, that directly afect grain yield in mungbean, have not been investigated thoroughly.

In this study, we evaluated the efect of harvest at R6 growth stage on pod development in mungbean. VC1973A, a widely grown cultivar, exhibits higher synchronicity in pod maturity than V2984, a Korean landrace. To understand the diferences in synchronous pod maturity between the two genotypes, sucrose content and transcriptome of VC1973A and V2984 were analyzed after pod removal, and the inforescence ar

chitecture of both genotypes was reported. We also evaluated the production of sucrose in leaves and utilization of this assimilate for reproductive development. The results of this study will advance our understanding of the reproductive development in mungbean and facilitate the

development of improved cultivars with synchronous pod maturity.

# Materials and methods

# Plant material

Two mungbean genotypes, VC1973A and V2984, were planted at the Seoul National University Experimental Farm in Suwon, South Korea $( 3 7 . 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } ,$ , $1 2 7 . 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ). Pods were harvested weekly for eight weeks from 10 plants of each genotype to measure synchronicity in pod maturity in 2016. For RNA and sucrose extraction, leaf samples were collected from three plants per cultivar and treatment in 2017. Pod removal at the R6 stage was used as treatment for both genotypes; intact plants were used as a control. All pods including immature ones were removed at the R6 stage (103rd day after sowing), when at least $9 0 \%$ of the pods reach maturity. VC1973A is a mungbean cultivar that was developed at The World Vegetable Center in 1982, and V2984 is a Korean landrace grown in the Kyung-Ki province of South Korea. The fourth trifoliate leaf was sampled from depodded plants at 5 days after pod removal, and at the same time from control plants of both genotypes (Kasai 2008). Leaf samples were stored at $- 7 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until needed for analysis. Same samples were used for high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis and RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analyses.

# RNA‑seq

Total RNA was extracted from leaf samples of VC1973A and V2984 using Ribospin Plant (GeneAll, Seoul, Korea). A total of 12 cDNA libraries (three libraries per genotype per treatment) were constructed using the TruSeq® Stranded mRNA LT Sample Prep Kit (Illumina Inc., CA, USA). The quality and quantity of samples used for RNA-Seq analysis were examined using 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies). RNA samples were sequenced using the TruSeq SBS Kit v3 on the Illumina HiSeq 4000 platform. Raw RNA-Seq reads were deposited in the Sequence Read Archive (SRA) database of the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) (Table S1).

# HPLC analysis of sucrose content

Mungbean leaves were dried and ground to a fne powder. Each sample $( \sim 1 \ \mathrm { g ) }$ was stirred in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $8 0 \%$ ethanol and incubated at room temperature for 1 day. Subsequently, the mixture was fltered through a syringe flter $( 0 . 2 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ pore size, $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ diameter) (GVS Life Science, Sanford, ME, USA). Quantitative analysis of sucrose $9 9 . 5 \%$ (Sigma) was performed at $7 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in distilled water using Dionex ultimate

3000 HPLC System (Thermo Dionex, USA) equipped with Shodex RI0191 (Shodex, Japan) and a Sugar-pak column $( 6 . 5 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m }$ , Waters Co., USA). Solvent fow rate was maintained at $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ , and the injection volume used was $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ . Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted and followed by mean separation with the least signifcant diference (LSD) method using the agricolae package in R.

# Identifcation of diferentially expressed genes (DEGs)

Raw RNA-Seq reads were mapped onto mungbean reference genome using tophat2 (Table S1) (Kim et al. 2013; Kang et al. 2014). Mapped reads were counted using HTseq, and a comparison of gene expression levels between samples and statistical analysis were conducted using edgeR (Robinson et al. 2010; Anders et al. 2015). Values of counts per million (CPM) were computed, and DEGs were defned as genes with a fold-change $\mathrm { ( F C ) } > 2$ between samples and $p$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ . Genes with $\mathrm { C P M } < 1$ in all six libraries per sample were removed. Amino acid sequences of DEGs were mapped to the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG; https://www.genome.jp/kegg/tool/map_pathway1. html) pathways.

# Validation of RNA‑Seq data by quantitative reverse‑transcription PCR (qRT‑PCR)

The cDNA was synthesized using Bio-Rad iScript™ cDNA Synthesis Kit (Cat. 170–8891; Hercules, CA, USA). The quantitative reverse-transcript (qRT) PCR was conducted using Bio-Rad iQ™ SYBR Green Supermix Kit (Cat. 170–8882) on a LightCycler® 480 (Roche Diagnostics, Laval, QC, Canada). Primers for qRT-PCR were designed using Primer3 (https://primer3plus.com/) (Table S2). IF5A was used as an internal control for the normalization of target gene expression. Each sample was analyzed in triplicate,

and statistically signifcant diferences were determined using the Student’s t test $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ .

# Results

# Plant architecture and synchronous pod maturity of mungbean genotypes

Inforescences of both mungbean genotypes, VC1973A and V2984, showed compound raceme inforescence architecture and indeterminate inforescence development (Fig. 1a, Figure S1). However, because the number of branches and nodes per branch was lower in VC1973A than in V2984, the number of foral meristems, which produce inforescences and consequently pods, was also lower in VC1973A than V2984 (Figure S2A). For both genotypes, pods were harvested once a week for 8 weeks and it was observed that the number of the harvested pods of both genotypes was the highest at week 2 (R6 growth stage), although the yield of V2984 peaked again at week 7 (Fig. 1b). Therefore, although V2984 showed higher yield due to the second pod maturity, VC1973A was characterized as more synchronous in pod maturity than V2984.

# Pod development after the frst harvest

To investigate pod development after the frst harvest in VC1973A and V2984, the number of pods newly developed was counted at 0, 5, and 10 days after pod removal at the R6 stage. Among control plants, the number of pods continued to increase in V2984 but not in VC1973A, because V2984 continued to fower and produce pods on newly developed branches (Figure S2A). After depodding, although both genotypes produced new pods, the number of newly developed pods was much lower in VC1973A than in V2984 (Figure S2B). These results further confrm that VC1973A exhibits higher synchronicity in pod maturity than V2984.

# Sucrose content of leaf tissues after depodding

Sucrose content of the fourth trifoliate leaf of control and depodded plants of VC1973A and V2984 was measured at the ffth day after the depodding treatment (Table 1, Figure S3) (Kasai 2008). The main efects of genotype and treatment on sucrose content were not observed (Table 1). However, control plants of V2984 showed signifcantly higher sucrose content $\mathrm { \cdot 1 0 9 7 ~ m g / g / L ) }$ than those of VC1973A $( 9 1 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / g / L } )$ (Figure S3). After pod removal, sucrose content of VC1973A increased signifcantly from 914 to $1 1 4 8 ~ \mathrm { m g / g / L }$ , whereas that of V2984 showed a signifcant decline from 1097 to $8 3 3 ~ \mathrm { m g / g / L }$ . Although no signifcant efects of genotype and treatment were observed on the sucrose content of leaf tissues, the efect of genotype × treatment interaction on sucrose content was highly signifcant $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ (Table 1).

# Analysis of DEGs after depodding

RNA-Seq analysis generated 4.7–7.3 Gbp data per library (Table S1), and RNA-seq reads were mapped onto the mungbean reference genome sequence (Kang et al. 2014). Comparison of RNA-Seq data between genotypes revealed 490 and 815 DEGs in control and depodded plants, respectively. Regardless of pod removal, of the 490 and 815 DEGs, 225 genes were commonly detected as DEGs (Table S3). Comparison between treatments revealed 331 and 84 DEGs in VC1973A and V2984, respectively. Of the 331 and 84, 17 DEGs were common to both genotypes (Table S4). These results indicate that common pathways that response to the frst harvest exist between VC1973A and V2984, although there are many genotype-specifc genes.

To identify which pathways were enriched by DEGs, we mapped the DEGs to the KEGG database (Fig. 2). While similar pathways were enriched by DEGs detected between control and depodded plants of each genotype, carbon metabolism and plant hormone signal transduction pathways were enriched by DEGs detected between depodded plants of VC1973A and V2984 (Fig. 2a). # DEGs involved in starch and sucrose metabolism

A total of 4, 11, and 4 DEGs detected between control vs. depodded plants of VC1973A, depodded plants of VC1973A vs. V2984, and control plants of VC1973A vs. V2984, respectively, were involved in starch and sucrose metabolism. Of these, four DEGs (Vradi01g05010.1, Vradi08g02630.1, Vradi08g04870.1, and Vradi10g08240.1) were common to various comparisons (Table  S5); Vradi01g05010.1 and Vradi10g08240.1 were common to the comparisons between control vs. depodded plants of VC1973A and depodded plants of V2984 vs. VC1973A, whereas Vradi08g02630.1 and Vradi08g04870.1 were common to the comparisons between control plants of V2984 vs. VC1973A and depodded plants of V2984 vs. VC1973A (Table 2, Fig. 2). The expression levels of Vradi01g05010.1 and Vradi10g08240.1 were down-regulated after pod removal in VC1973A $\mathrm { l o g } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C }$ 1.48 and 1.64, respectively), and both genes exhibited lower expression levels in depodded plants of VC1973A than in those of V2984 $( \log _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C }$ 2.06 and 1.85, respectively) (Table 2). In both treatments (control and depodded), the expression of Vradi08g02630.1 was higher in V2984 than in VC1973A $\mathrm { l o g } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C }$ 1.14 and 1.13, respectively), whereas that of Vradi08g04870.1 was higher in VC1973A than in V2984 $\mathrm { l o g } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F C }$ 2.07 and 2.73, respectively). We also validated the RNA-Seq data of these four DEGs (Vradi01g05010.1, Vradi08g02630.1, Vradi08g04870.1, and Vradi10g08240.1) by qRT-PCR (Fig. 3). The IF5A gene showed the most stable expression compared with actin, actin4, and CYP20 and was used as an internal control.

Between VC1973A and V2984, 490 DEGs were detected in control plants, and 815 DEGs were detected between depodded plants. Of these 815 DEGs, only $2 8 \%$ (225) were common to both genotypes, suggesting that these two genotypes respond to the frst harvest in diferent ways, as demonstrated by the diferential enrichment of pathways among the DEGs in each genotype (Fig. 2b). Between control vs. depodded plants, the number of DEGs was fourfold higher in VC1973A (331) than in V2984 (84). Gene ontology (GO) terms related to defense and environmental stress responses, such as biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, cutin, suberin and wax biosynthesis, favonoid biosynthesis, and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, were highly enriched among DEGs detected in depodded plants of VC1973A compared with those of V2984 (Fig. 2b). DEGs (271) detected only in control vs. depodded comparison in VC1973A (average $\mathrm { F C } = 2 . 6 2 $ ) showed no signifcant diference in expression in depodded plants vs. control plants of V2984 (average $\mathrm { F C } = 1 . 3 4 )$ , although the overall expression levels of these genes in V2984 were as high as those in VC1973A (Fig. 4). This suggests that VC1973A responds more dynamically to frst harvest. This is consistent with the observation that the mungbean cultivar VC1973A shows better stress resistance than mungbean landraces (Hong et al. 1983).

After pod removal, sucrose level increased in VC1973A but decreased in V2984 (Table 1). Among the common DEGs involved in starch and sucrose metabolism, Vradi01g05010.1 and Vradi10g08240.1 were down-regulated in VC1973A after pod removal and showed higher expression levels in depodded plants of V2984 than in those of VC1973A. The Arabidopsis thaliana genes $A t 3 g I 8 0 8 0 . I$ and $A t 4 g 2 I 7 6 0 . I$ , orthologs of Vradi01g05010.1 and Vradi10g08240.1, respectively, are annotated as glycoside hydrolase family 1 beta-glucosidases (BGLU44 and BGLU47) (Xu et al. 2004). These enzymes catalyze the hydrolysis of $\beta$ -D-glucosidic bonds in beta-D-glucosides and oligosaccharides (EC 3.2.1.21), thus releasing

glucose (Figure S4) (Babcock and Esen 1994). Although BGLUs had not been reported to afect directly the levels of sucrose in legume crops, Vradi01g05010.1 and Vradi10g08240.1 exhibited a negative correlation with sucrose content of leaves in this study, possibly by regulating the content of glucose. Additionally, Vradi08g02630.1 and Vradi08g04870.1 were expressed at higher levels in V2984 and VC1973A, respectively, regardless of the treatment.

Synchronous pod maturity in mungbean is a complex trait afected by multiple factors, such as fowering time, maturity time, inforescence structure and plant architecture. Because indeterminate inforescences develop from each node on branches, plant morphology can afect homogeneous fowering, eventually determining synchronous pod maturity. Genetic studies for each trait may need to be conducted to fully elucidate synchrony of pod maturity in mugnbean. In this study, we reported inforescence structure of two representative mungbean cultivars and changes in pod development after the frst harvest. Although the functions of the candidate genes need to be characterized to fully elucidate genetic basis underlying synchrony in pod maturity, two BGLU homologous genes were identifed as candidate genes associated with diferences in sucrose content between genotypes and treatments (control vs. depodded plants). Our results will help identify key genetic factors responsible for pod development in mungbean and will enable the breeding of new cultivars with synchronous pod maturity, facilitating cost-efective harvesting.
