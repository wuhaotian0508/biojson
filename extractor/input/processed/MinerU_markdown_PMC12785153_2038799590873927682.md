# ABSTRACT

With rising living standards, consumers’ demand for color diversity and nutritional quality in tomato products has increased. Flavonoids are a key determinant of peel color and nutritional value in tomato fruit, where their biosynthesis is controlled by various phytohormones, including brassinosteroids (BRs). However, the underlying mechanism by which BR regulates flavonoid biosynthesis remains unclear. Here, we show that exogenous BRs suppress flavonoid accumulation, whereas reduced endogenous BR levels in RNAi lines of SlCYP90B3, a rate-limiting BR biosynthetic gene, result in increased flavonoid content in the fruit peel. We further demonstrate that BRI1-EMS-suppressor1 (SlBES1), a basic helix–loop– helix transcription factor essential for BR signaling, not only regulates fruit firmness but also represses flavonoid accumulation by directly binding to the promoters of the flavonoid biosynthetic genes SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3’H. Additionally, SlBES1 modulates a hierarchical transcriptional cascade by repressing the expression of SlMYB12, further suppressing flavonoid biosynthesis. Moreover, the homologous transcription factor brassinazole-resistant1 (SlBZR1) enhances SlBES1-mediated repression of flavonoid accumulation. Specifically, SlBES1 predominantly inhibits flavonoid biosynthesis, whereas SlBZR1 primarily enhances carotenoid pathway activity. Notably, variation in SlBES1 is correlated with flavonoid content during tomato domestication. Collectively, these results highlight a novel role for SlBES1 as a negative regulator of flavonoid biosynthesis, offering potential strategies for flavonoid biofortification in tomato.

# INTRODUCTION

Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) is a major vegetable crop and has also become a model system for research on fruit development and ripening. Beyond its agronomic value, tomato fruit is an important dietary source of phytonutrients, particularly flavonoids and carotenoids, two major classes of bioactive compounds with distinct physiological roles in both plants and humans. Flavonoids are water-soluble polyphenolic secondary metabolites predominantly concentrated in the fruit peel (Warren et al., 2003; Adato et al., 2009). In plants, flavonoids contribute to the coloration of fruits and flowers and provide protection against both biotic stressors and abiotic factors such as UV radiation (Schaefer et al., 2002; Naik et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2022). Flavonoids are also common in the human diet, being prominent components of foods like vegetables, fruits, and tea. They can reduce the risk of disease due to their antioxidant 

effects and additional bioactivities, including antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties (Schijlen et al., 2004). Due to their beneficial effects on human health, flavonoids are now considered indispensable components in nutraceutical, pharmaceutical, medical, and cosmetic applications (Tohge and Fernie, 2017). In contrast, carotenoids are lipophilic terpenoid pigments that accumulate primarily in the flesh of fruits, contributing to fruit color and acting as powerful antioxidants with well-documented health benefits (Mes et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2022). The quality of tomato fruit is evaluated on the basis of both external appearance and internal composition; color is a critical determinant of consumer preference (Kang et al., 2017). Tomato fruit color is influenced by the relative levels of different pigments, mainly the accumulation of carotenoids in the flesh and flavonoids in the peel, together with chlorophyll degradation during ripening (Ballester et al., 2010; Yang et al., 2022). Although lycopene, a

carotenoid, is the most abundant antioxidant in tomato fruit, the endogenous levels of flavonoids are relatively low and insufficient to provide the expected health benefits (Mes et al., 2008). Therefore, tomato represents an ideal system for investigating the regulatory mechanisms that underlie flavonoid biosynthesis. 

The biosynthesis of flavonoids, initiated by the precursor 4- coumaroyl coenzyme A (CoA) from the phenylpropanoid pathway, has been well characterized (Zhang et al., 2015). Chalcone synthase (CHS), the first enzyme in this pathway, converts 4-coumaroyl CoA into naringenin chalcone (NarCh) (Ballester et al., 2016). Subsequent enzymatic reactions transform NarCh into various flavonoids through distinct branches of the main pathway. Most biosynthetic genes involved in flavonoid production have been identified, including chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H), flavonoid ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3′ H), flavonol synthase (FLS), dihydroflavonol 4-reductase, and anthocyanidin synthase (Iijima et al., 2008; Mintz-Oron et al., 2008; Fernandez-Moreno et al., 2016). In tomato fruit, the very low expression of CHI causes accumulation of its substrate, NarCh, which is among the most abundant flavonoid components in tomato. NarCh accumulates in the fruit peel during ripening and contributes to the yellow coloration of tomato skin (Muir et al., 2001; Bovy et al., 2002). 

Regulation of the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway occurs predominantly at the transcriptional level through transcription factors (TFs) such as R2R3-MYB, basic helix–loop–helix, and WD40 proteins, acting individually or as part of complexes (Xu et al., 2015a, 2015b). Among these, MYB TFs have been extensively investigated as central regulators of the MBW complex, which governs flavonoid production in diverse plant species (Liu et al., 2015), including maize (Cone et al., 1986), grape (Kobayashi et al., 2002), Arabidopsis (Baudry et al., 2004), and petunia (Quattrocchio et al., 2006). In tomato, SlMYB12, an R2R3-MYB TF, has been identified as a master regulator of flavonoid biosynthesis by directly activating the transcription of multiple flavonoid biosynthetic genes (Luo et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2015). Previous studies demonstrated that SlMYB12 controls accumulation of NarCh, the yellow flavonoid, in the epidermis of tomato fruit. Both naturally occurring and experimentally induced MYB12-deficient mutants produce colorless peels (Adato et al., 2009; Ballester et al., 2010; Zhu et al., 2018). A 603-bp deletion in the upstream regulatory region of SlMYB12 is present in most pink-fruited accessions and is considered the main cause of their colorless peel (Lin et al., 2014). 

In addition to regulatory genes, flavonoid biosynthesis is also influenced by signals such as phytohormones. Brassinosteroids (BRs) are polyhydroxylated steroid phytohormones that play essential roles in regulating plant growth, development, and stress responses (Kim and Russinova, 2020). BRs are synthesized from campesterol through a series of enzymatic steps. In Arabidopsis, cytochrome P450, encoded by DWARF4 (DWF4), catalyzes C-22 hydroxylation, the rate-limiting step in BR biosynthesis (Choe et al., 2001; Divi and Krishna, 2010). In tomato, the fruit-specific SlCYP90B3 has been identified as an 

ortholog of Arabidopsis DWF4 (Hu et al., 2020). Genetic and molecular studies in model plants have extensively defined the core components of the BR signaling cascade, spanning from cell-surface receptors to central TFs (Nolan et al., 2020). BRs are detected by a plasma membrane-localized receptor–coreceptor complex that includes BR-insensitive1 (BRI1) and BRI1-associated receptor kinase1 (BAK1) (Nam and Li, 2002). BAK1 associates with BRI1 to form a complex that enhances signaling output through reciprocal transphosphorylation, leading to phosphorylation of BR signaling kinase1 (BSK1) (Wang et al., 2008; Bajwa et al., 2013). Activated BSK1 binds and activates the phosphatase BRI1 suppressor1 (BSU1), which in turn dephosphorylates and inhibits BR-insensitive2 (BIN2), a kinase that acts as a negative regulator of BR signaling (Tang et al., 2008). Inactivation of BIN2 permits unphosphorylated TFs BES1 and BZR1 to accumulate in the nucleus, where they function as master positive regulators of BR signal transduction by binding to the promoters of BRresponsive target genes and initiating specific physiological responses (Wang et al., 2002; Yin et al., 2002; He et al., 2005). 

In recent years, the regulatory effects of BRs, as a novel and versatile class of plant hormones, on flavonoid biosynthesis have been increasingly reported. For instance, application of 24- epibrassinolide (EBL) induces the accumulation of anthocyanins and proanthocyanidins in Arabidopsis shoots (Lee et al., 2024), grapevine pericarp (Xu et al., 2015a, 2015b), and Cucumis sativus (Ahammed et al., 2017). BRs positively regulate flavonoid accumulation in grape berry (Xi et al., 2013), strawberry (Zahedipour-Sheshglani and Asghari, 2020), and apple calli (Zheng et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023). Conversely, application of brassinolide reduces flavonoid content in redfleshed apple seedlings and calli, whereas brassinazole treatment enhances flavonoid accumulation (Wang et al., 2021). In Arabidopsis, the key BR signaling TF BES1 suppresses flavonol biosynthesis and coordinates plant growth with UV-B stress responses (Liang et al., 2020). However, the molecular mechanism by which BR signaling components regulate flavonoid metabolism in tomato has not been reported. Our previous work demonstrated that both exogenous BR application and endogenous BR levels promote tomato fruit ripening and increase carotenoid content (Hu et al., 2020). Nevertheless, the roles of BR biosynthetic genes and signal transduction factors in flavonoid biosynthesis remain unclear. Although several studies have demonstrated the effects of BRs on flavonoid biosynthesis and fruit coloration, most examined only changes in flavonoid content or gene expression following exogenous BR treatment. Therefore, the precise mechanism underlying BR-mediated regulation of flavonoid production in tomato requires further investigation. 

Here, we employed molecular and genetic approaches to investigate the role of BRs in the tomato flavonoid pathway. Both exogenous and endogenous BRs inhibited flavonoid accumulation and the expression of flavonoid-related genes. Genetic analyses supported these findings, as tomato plants with SlBES1 knockout or SlBIN2 overexpression (OE) displayed substantially elevated flavonoid levels. Mechanistic studies indicated that the BR signaling TF SlBES1 directly binds and represses the flavonoid biosynthetic genes SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H, thereby reducing flavonoid levels. # RESULTS

# BRs inhibit flavonoid biosynthesis

To evaluate the effects of BRs on flavonoid synthesis in tomato, we first treated fruits harvested at the mature green (MG) stage with $3 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ EBL (Figure 1A). Because NarCh is among the most abundant flavonoids in tomato, representing more than $90 \%$ of total flavonoids and accumulating in the pericarp during ripening (Krause and Galensa, 1992; Wang et al., 2020), we measured NarCh levels in the fruit peel as an indicator of total flavonoid content. Flavonoid levels were assessed on day 12 after treatment. EBL treatment significantly reduced flavonoid content compared with untreated controls (Figure 1B). 

Flavonoid biosynthesis originates from the phenylpropanoid pathway and proceeds through enzymatic reactions mainly controlled by flavonoid biosynthetic genes (Figure 1C). SlMYB12 is a key regulator of this pathway, broadly targeting and activating transcription of flavonoid biosynthetic genes, and plays a critical role in NarCh production in tomato fruit (Figure 1C). We next investigated whether the differences between EBL-treated and control plants were also evident at the transcript level. Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) analysis showed that EBL treatment suppressed the expression of the regulatory gene SlMYB12 as well as the flavonoid biosynthetic genes SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H (Figure 1D).

# SlBES1 directly targets genes to repress the expression of flavonoid biosynthetic genes

To investigate whether SlBES1 inhibits flavonoid biosynthesis by directly binding to target genes, the promoters of SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H were cloned. Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assays were then conducted to test the interaction of SlBES1 with the promoters of flavonoid-associated genes. The SlBES1 coding sequence (CDS) was inserted into the pGADT7 (AD) vector; the promoters of SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H were cloned into the pAbAi vector. Due to strong self-activation, the proSlCHS1 construct could not be validated by this approach. Yeast cells co-transformed with the empty AD vector and the corresponding promoters served as negative controls. The Y1H results demonstrated that SlBES1 directly interacts with the SlCHS2 (Figure 3A) and SlF3′ H (Figure 3B) promoters. 

BZR TFs are known to recognize BRRE (CGTGT/CG) and E-box (CANNTG) motifs in the promoters of target genes (Yin et al., 2005). Analysis of the proSlCHS1, proSlCHS2, and proSlF3′ H sequences revealed several E-box motifs within their 2-kb upstream regions. To further confirm in vivo binding of SlBES1 to these promoters, chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP) assays were performed using transgenic tomato fruits expressing 35S::SlBES1-myc, with WT (AC) fruits carrying the myc tag as a negative control. Promoter regions were divided into distinct fragments according to E-box distribution. Enrichment of these promoter regions in DNA co-immunoprecipitated with the myc antibody or with a non-specific control antibody (pre-immunized mouse immunoglobulin G) was quantified by qPCR. The analysis showed strong enrichment of SlBES1 at these promoter regions, indicating that SlBES1 might regulate the expression of SlCHS1 (Figure 3C; Supplemental Figures 3A and 3B), SlCHS2 (Figure 3D; Supplemental Figures 3C and 3D), and SlF3′ H (Figure 3E; Supplemental Figures 3E and 3F) by binding to E-box elements. 

Finally, we performed dual-luciferase assays to verify the effect of SlBES1 on the promoter activity of flavonoid biosynthetic genes. Promoter sequences were individually inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC (LUC) reporter vector, while the full-length CDS of SlBES1 was cloned into the pGreenII 62-SK effector vector. Compared with the control, leaves co-infiltrated with SlBES1-SK and proSlCHS1-LUC (Figure 3F), proSlCHS2-LUC (Figure 3G), or proSlF3′ H-LUC (Figure 3H) exhibited remarkably attenuated luciferase signals, suggesting that SlBES1 suppresses the transcription of flavonoid biosynthetic genes. Collectively, these results confirm that SlBES1 functions as a repressor upstream of SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H. 

# SlMYB12 mediates the hierarchical transcriptional regulation of SlBES1

Flavonoid accumulation in the tomato fruit peel is controlled by a SlMYB12-regulated transcriptional network (Adato et al., 2009). 

In the current study, SlMYB12 strongly activated proSlCHS1 (Supplemental Figure 4A) and proSlCHS2 (Supplemental Figure 4B), demonstrating that SlMYB12 directly targets and induces biosynthetic genes to boost flavonoid production. To assess whether SlBES1 directly regulates SlMYB12, we cloned its promoter. The Y1H assay showed that SlBES1 interacts with proSlMYB12 in vitro (Figure 4A). ChIP-qPCR further demonstrated that immunoprecipitated SlBES1 significantly enriched the P1 and P2 regions of proSlMYB12 containing the E-box, confirming its in vivo binding (Figure 4B). A dual-luciferase assay confirmed that SlBES1 represses the activity of proSlMYB12 (Figure 4C). These results suggest that SlBES1 inhibits SlMYB12 transcription by binding to E-box motifs in its promoter. 

Beyond direct regulation, we investigated whether SlBES1 also controls flavonoid biosynthesis through a SlMYB12-dependent pathway. WT and bes1 fruits were subjected to a virus-induced SlMYB12 silencing assay (Figure 4D). Silencing SlMYB12 in the bes1 background (SlMYB12-TRV/bes1) abolished the highflavonoid phenotype observed in bes1 (Figure 4E). Consistent with this finding, SlMYB12 transcript levels in SlMYB12-TRV/ bes1 lines were comparable to those in the control (Figure 4F). Taken together, these results demonstrate that SlBES1 might modulate flavonoid biosynthesis through inhibition of SlMYB12. 

Tomato fruits with red flesh display yellow skin due to accumulation of the flavonoid NarCh in the pericarp (Yang et al., 2023). The SlMYB12 knockout mutation disrupts NarCh accumulation, producing a colorless pericarp and, ultimately, pink fruits (Deng et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019). To assess whether the SlMYB12 mutation was also influenced by BRs, EBL was applied to fruits of two pink and two red tomato varieties (Supplemental Figure 5A). In red tomatoes, NarCh content significantly declined after EBL treatment. In contrast, no visible change was observed in pink tomatoes, where NarCh levels remained very low $( < 0$ . $5 \mu \mathsf { g } \cdot \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ fresh weight [FW]) (Supplemental Figure 5B). Similarly, expression of most flavonoid-related genes significantly decreased after EBL treatment, such that transcript levels in pink tomatoes were consistently lower than those in red tomatoes (Supplemental Figures 5C–5F). Although lycopene (Supplemental Figure 6A), $\beta \cdot$ -carotene (Supplemental Figure 6B), lutein (Supplemental Figure 6C), and total carotenoid content (Supplemental Figure 6D) all increased, there was no clear difference between pink and red tomatoes after treatment. The expression of SlPSY1, a key rate-limiting gene in carotenoid biosynthesis, showed the same pattern (Supplemental Figure 6E). These observations indicate that BRs regulate flavonoid biosynthesis in an SlMYB12-dependent manner without altering carotenoid accumulation.

# DISCUSSION

# BRs are critical in the modulation of flavonoid accumulation

BRs are an essential class of steroid hormones that regulate fruit development and quality formation. Application of exogenous BRs influences multiple important quality traits in tomato (Zhu et al., 2015), grape (Zheng et al., 2020), carambola (Zhu et al., 2021), and medlar (Ekinci et al., 2019). In recent years, the effects of BRs on flavonoids have also been investigated. In the current study, EBL treatment reduced flavonoid content and suppressed the expression of associated genes (Figure 1A–1D), consistent with previous findings in Arabidopsis (Liang et al., 2020) and apple (Wang et al., 2021).

In addition to exogenous treatments, endogenous BRs influence flavonoid accumulation. In Arabidopsis, BR-deficient mutants such as psc1 and dwf4-102 show reduced jasmonateinduced anthocyanin accumulation (Peng et al., 2011), whereas det2 mutants exhibit elevated flavonoid levels (Liang et al., 2020). Similarly, BR signaling mutants bri1 and BES1-RNAi reduce cytokinin-induced anthocyanin accumulation but display increased flavonoid content (Yuan et al., 2015; Liang et al., 2020). In our study, SlCYP90B3- RNAi lines accumulated higher flavonoid levels, while SlCYP90B3-OE lines showed lower flavonoid content (Figure 1E), indicating that endogenous BRs likely inhibit flavonoid biosynthesis. However, SlMYB12 expression was reduced in SlCYP90B3-OE but not increased in SlCYP90B3- RNAi, despite upregulation of the structural genes SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H (Figure 1F–1I). This may reflect that the reduction in BR biosynthesis in SlCYP90B3-RNAi lines is insufficient to reach the threshold required for effective regulation of SlMYB12, given that RNAi reduces but does not eliminate gene expression compared with WT. Furthermore, OE of the BR-positive regulator SlBES1 decreased flavonoid content, while OE of the negative regulator SlBIN2 increased it (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 2A). Together, these findings clarify that BRs inhibit flavonoid biosynthesis in tomato. 

# SlBES1 inhibits the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway via a hierarchical transcriptional cascade

BR TFs regulate plant growth and fruit development by activating or repressing downstream target genes by specifically binding to promoter regions containing E-box and BRRE motifs (Li et al., 2018). As master TFs of BR signaling, SlBZR1 and SlBES1 represent central nodes integrating multiple signaling cascades, and many of their direct target genes have been identified. For example, BZR1 directly binds to the PAP1 promoter to promote anthocyanin accumulation (Lee et al., 2024), whereas BES1 suppresses flavonol accumulation in Arabidopsis by directly inhibiting MYB11, MYB12, and MYB111, which activate flavonol biosynthesis (Liang et al., 2020). In apple, MdBZR1 suppresses the expression of MdCOL6, a positive regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis, by binding its promoter (Wang et al., 2023). In tomato, flavonoid accumulation at the fruit surface is controlled by an SlMYB12- centered transcriptional network (Adato et al., 2009). MYB12 activates a broad set of target genes, including CHS, CHI, F3H, and FLS, by binding to the MRE element (Supplemental Figure 4; Luo et al., 2008; Zhang et al., 2015). Prior studies linked SlMYB12 to variation in fruit flavonoids through population-level analyses and identified SlBES1.2 (BRI1-EMS-SUPPRESSOR 1.2) as a potential regulator of flavonoid content by modulating MYB12 expression (Yang et al., 2023). 

# Plant Communications

Nonetheless, the precise regulatory mechanism has remained unclear. Our study provides new insights into the molecular mechanism by which the BR signaling component SlBES1 regulates flavonoid biosynthesis. 

Beyond single-gene regulation, hierarchical transcriptional cascades are gaining increasing recognition. For example, SlERF. G3-like not only directly activates structural genes in the flavonoid pathway but also regulates flavonoid biosynthesis by interacting with FUL1 during tomato fruit ripening (You et al., 2023). In pear, PpERF9 directly binds the PpMYB114 promoter, while also inhibiting PpRAP2.4, which itself activates PpMYB114, thereby modulating anthocyanin biosynthesis (Ni et al., 2023). Here, we showed that SlBES1 directly binds E-box motifs in the promoters of SlMYB12, SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H (Figure 3), implying that SlMYB12 and its associated metabolic network are involved in SlBES1-mediated control of flavonoid biosynthesis. Moreover, the observation that SlBES1 indirectly represses flavonoid biosynthesis through SlMYB12 highlights multiple layers of regulatory activity by SlBES1 (Figure 4). 

# SlBES1 and SlBZR1 co-regulate tomato fruit coloration via diverse metabolic pathways

The BR signaling pathway mediates the dephosphorylation of closely related TFs, including BES1 and BZR1, which have high similarity and likely perform partially redundant functions (He et al., 2002; Yin et al., 2005). In Arabidopsis, reduction of BES1 and its closest homolog, BZR1, both result in a semidwarf phenotype resembling that of weak BR loss-of-function mutants (Yin et al., 2005). SlBES1 and SlBZR1 expression levels increased during the early stage of tomato fruit ripening, indicating their involvement in the regulation of fruit ripening (Meng et al., 2023). 

Although BES1 and BZR1 encode plant-specific proteins with $8 8 \%$ identity at the amino acid level (Vert et al., 2005), there is evidence that they also perform distinct roles. In Arabidopsis, bes1 mutants display constitutive BR responses, such as elongated, bent petioles and pale-green leaves under light conditions (Choe et al., 2001; Wang et al., 2001). In contrast, bzr1 mutants show a semidwarf phenotype (Wang et al., 2002). Previously, we confirmed that SlBZR1 predominantly regulates carotenoid accumulation in tomato (Meng et al., 2023), whereas SlBES1 mainly promotes tomato fruit softening (Liu et al., 2021). This may reflect a more complex relationship among family members in mediating BR responses. Here, we observed that SlBES1 plays a central role in regulating flavonoid biosynthesis because the effect of SlBZR1 alone was minimal (Figure 2A). Notably, the bzr1 bes1 double mutant accumulated higher levels of flavonoids (Figure 2A). Co-expression of SlBES1 and SlBZR1 exerted stronger repression on downstream target genes than SlBES1 alone (Figure 5). This discrepancy suggests that while SlBES1 is the primary regulator of flavonoid biosynthesis, the influence of SlBZR1 is amplified in combination with SlBES1. Fine-tuning of tomato fruit coloration by BRs is achieved through the coordinated action of SlBES1 and SlBZR1. 

In tomato, fruit color is determined by the accumulation of different pigments, with carotenoids in the flesh and flavonoids in the peel (Yang et al., 2022). A previous study indicated that OE of CrtB increases total carotenoid levels and results in 

minimal anthocyanin accumulation in apple callus (Cao et al., 2015). Cuticle thickness is significantly increased in psy1-cr1 mutants or SlCHS1-silenced tomatoes (Cao et al., 2022). Induction of carotenoid biosynthesis by SlWRKY35 was able to reduce flavonoid levels (Yuan et al., 2022). In our study, we found that reduced expression of carotenoid pathway genes was accompanied by elevated expression of flavonoid pathway genes in BR signaling deletion mutants (Figure 6), suggesting competition between the two pathways for common substrates. These findings demonstrate that metabolic flux between carotenoid and flavonoid pathways can be redirected by BRs. As essential BR signaling components, SlBES1 and SlBZR1 may serve as ‘‘regulatory hubs of metabolic flux,’’ optimizing the allocation of metabolites and the flow of energy. Repression of the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway limits flux through the phenylpropanoid pathway, allowing more precursors to be redirected toward carotenoid biosynthesis. 

Building on previous reports and the current findings, we developed a model to elucidate how BR signaling is transduced to regulate color formation in tomato (Figure 7). When BRs are insufficient, the receptor BRI1 and its co-receptor BAK1 remain separated. The transcription factors SlBES1 and SlBZR1, in their phosphorylated state, are unable to enter the nucleus to exert regulatory functions. When BRs are present, sequential phosphorylation and dephosphorylation events lead to degradation of dephosphorylated SlBIN2. Furthermore, high endogenous BR levels in tomato fruit lead to the nuclear accumulation of dephosphorylated SlBZR1 and SlBES1, which then bind to promoter regions of BR-responsive genes. Dephosphorylated SlBZR1 binds to E-box elements within promoters of ethylene biosynthetic genes (SlACO1 and SlACO3) and the carotenoid biosynthetic gene SlPSY1, activating their transcription and promoting ethylene production and carotenoid accumulation (Meng et al., 2023). In contrast, dephosphorylated SlBES1 binds to Ebox motifs in the promoters of flavonoid-related (SlMYB12, SlCHS1, SlCHS2, and SlF3′ H), repressing their expression and directly inhibiting flavonoid biosynthesis. Moreover, SlBES1 indirectly suppresses flavonoid biosynthesis through inhibition of SlMYB12. Because SlMYB12 binds MRE elements to control target genes such as SlCHS1 and SlCHS2, a SlBES1– SlMYB12–SlCHS regulatory cascade represses flavonoid biosynthesis. Additionally, SlBZR1 might act synergistically to strengthen SlBES1-mediated suppression of flavonoid pathways. In conclusion, the positive regulation of carotenoids by SlBZR1 together with the negative regulation of flavonoids by SlBES1 jointly determines fruit coloration in tomato.

This variation was significantly associated with the levels of two representative flavonoid metabolites, SlFM1809 and SlFM1806 (Supplemental Figure 8). However, there was no correlation between SlBZR1 variation and flavonoid content (Supplemental Figure 8), providing further evidence that differences in flavonoid content during tomato domestication are mainly determined by SlBES1. 

Taken together, our study not only clarifies the regulatory mechanism of SlBES1 in BR-mediated tomato flavonoid biosynthesis but also provides valuable gene resources and a molecular framework for quality improvement in tomato 

breeding. We suggest that SlBES1 knockout through gene editing could serve as a strategy for molecular breeding of storagetolerant tomato cultivars with enhanced flavonoid content, without altering carotenoids or other nutritional components (Liu et al., 2021). 

Given that flavonoids are primarily concentrated in the skin of tomato fruits, it is feasible to breed cultivars enriched in flavonoids in both peel and flesh. Thus, hybridization of the transgenic tomato E8-SlMYB12 with bes1 may represent an effective approach for improving color diversity and nutritional quality of tomato fruits in the future. Further studies are needed to better understand the biological significance of BR-mediated pigment biosynthesis in climacteric fruit crops in natural conditions and to elucidate evolutionary drivers underlying selection of the SlBES1 haplotype during domestication. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Tomato (S. lycopersicum) cultivar ‘Ailsa Craig’ (AC) was used as the WT. The lines SlCYP90B3-OE (SlCYP90B3-OE-4 and SlCYP90B3-OE-5), SlCYP90B3-RNAi (SlCYP90B3-RNAi-9 and SlCYP90B3-RNAi-10), 

# Plant Communications

SlBES1-OE, bes1, SlBZR1-OE, bzr1, bzr1 bes1, SlBIN2-OE, and bin2 have been previously generated in our laboratory (Hu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2023). Plants were grown in a greenhouse under standard conditions (16/8 h day/night at $2 8 / 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . Fruits were collected at distinct ripening stages, including the MG, breaker (B), pink (P), and red (R) stages (Giovannoni, 2004). Tomato peels were frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until further use. 

# Vector construction and plant transformation

The SlCYP90B3-OE, SlBES1-OE, and SlBIN2-OE constructs were generated using the pGWB17 vector, while the SlBZR1-OE construct was generated using the pK7FWG2.0 vector (Dong et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2023). The CDSs of SlCYP90B3, SlBES1, and SlBIN2 were fused into the binary vectors pGWB17 or pK7FWG2.0 using Gateway technology (Nakagawa et al., 2007). The SlCYP90B3-RNAi construct was prepared as described previously (Hu et al., 2020). All vectors were transformed into ‘Ailsa Craig’ using the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain LBA4404-mediated cotyledon transformation method (Liu et al., 2019). 

# Generation of the CRISPR-Cas9 mutant

The bes1, bzr1, bzr1 bes1, and bin2 mutants were generated using the CRISPR-Cas9 system as previously described (Liu et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2023). Single guide RNAs targeting SlBES1, SlBZR1, and SlBIN2, as well as two single guide RNAs targeting both SlBES1 and SlBZR1, were designed using CRISPR-P (http://cbi.hzau.edu.cn/crispr/). The bes1, bzr1, and bin2 constructs were assembled into the pYAO:hSpCas9 binary plasmid, whereas the bzr1 bes1 double mutant was cloned into the pYLCRISPR/Cas9 vector (Ma et al., 2015). These binary vectors were introduced into ‘Ailsa Craig’ by Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain LBA4404. Mutations in the targeted regions were verified by PCR and DNA sequencing, and homozygous T2 transgenic plants were selected for subsequent experiments. 

# EBL treatment

Tomato fruits were collected at the MG stage, and fruit stalks were infiltrated with $3 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ EBL (Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) using the vacuum osmosis method. Solvent-treated fruits served as controls. After treatment, fruits were stored in a standard plant culture room at ${ } ^ { 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } }$ and sampled at days 1, 4, 8, and 12 for analysis. Three independent biological replicates were performed for each genotype/treatment combination at each time point; each replicate consisted of three fruits harvested from the same node of a single plant. 

# Flavonoid extraction and HPLC analysis

For flavonoid extraction, 1 g of liquid nitrogen–milled powder was suspended in 3 mL of $80 \%$ methanol and subjected to ultrasound-assisted extraction for 30 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Extracts were centrifuged at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , and the supernatants were filtered through $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filters prior to high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis. 

Flavonoid content was quantified using an HPLC system (Shimadzu) equipped with an SPD-M20A diode array detector and a C18 column $( 3 \mu \mathsf { m }$ , $4 . 6 \times 2 5 0 \ : \mathrm { m m }$ ; Elite Analytical Instruments, Dalian, China). The mobile phases consisted of water (solvent A) and acetonitrile (solvent B), both HPLC grade. The flow rate was maintained at $1 ~ \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , and $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of extract was injected into the auto-sampler. The detection wavelength was set at 370 nm to monitor NarCh. Flavonoid peak areas were identified based on spectral characteristics and quantified against standard compounds (NarCh, Sigma). 

# RNA extraction and quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNA was extracted from fruit peel samples using RNAiso Plus (Takara, Japan) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. First-strand cDNA was synthesized from $1 ~ \mu \ g$ RNA with the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit containing gDNA Eraser (Takara). qPCR was carried out with TB Green 

(Takara). Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. Relative gene expression was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C } t }$ method. 

# Y1H assay

The CDS of SlBES1 was cloned into the pGADT7 (AD) vector to generate a prey construct. Promoter fragments of SlMYB12 (− 296 to − 1191 bp upstream of the translation start site), SlCHS1 (− 44 to − 535 bp), SlCHS2 (− 933 to − 1150 bp), and SlF3′ H (− 560 to − 802 bp) were inserted separately into the pAbAi vector to create bait constructs. Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 1. The proSlMYB12-pAbAi, proSlCHS1-pAbAi, proSlCHS2-pAbAi, and proSlF3′ H-pAbAi plasmids were linearized with BstbI restriction endonuclease. These bait constructs were transformed into the yeast strain Y1H Gold and integrated into the yeast genome to generate bait reporter strains. The minimal inhibitory concentration of aureobasidin A (AbA) was empirically determined to prevent self-activation. Prey vectors were then transformed separately into the bait reporter strains, which were grown on synthetic dropout medium lacking Leu, with or without AbA, at ${ } ^ { 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The empty AD vector served as the negative control. 

# ChIP-qPCR assay

ChIP-qPCR was performed as described previously (Liu et al., 2019). Approximately $_ { 3 \ L { 9 } }$ of $0 . 5 \mathsf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ peel pieces from SlBES1-OE fruits at the MG stage was collected, cross-linked with $1 \%$ (w/v) formaldehyde under vacuum for 10 min, and ground into powder in liquid nitrogen. Chromatin complexes were isolated and sonicated using the Bioruptor Plus (Diagenode, Antwerp, Belgium). Five micrograms of mouse monoclonal anti-cmyc antibody (clone 9E10, immunoglobulin G; Roche, Basel, Switzerland) was used to immunoprecipitate the protein–DNA complexes. The precipitated samples were washed, purified, and analyzed by qPCR to assess enrichment of DNA fragments. The ACTIN2 gene served as the internal control. Primer sequences are provided in Supplemental Table 1. Error bars represent the standard deviation of three biological replicates. 

# Dual-luciferase assay

The CDSs of SlBES1 and SlBZR1 were cloned into the pGreenII 62-SK vector to generate effector plasmids, while promoter regions of SlMYB12 (− 296 to − 1191 bp upstream of the translation start site), SlCHS1 (− 44 to − 535 bp), SlCHS2(− 933 to − 1150 bp), and SlF3′ H (− 560 to − 802 bp) were individually ligated into the pGreenII 0800-LUC (LUC) vector to construct reporter plasmids. Effector and reporter plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 together with the pSoup helper plasmid. The corresponding Agrobacterium combinations were infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves using a 1 mL syringe, and leaves were harvested 48–72 h later. Luminescence images and signal intensity were captured with a NightOWL II LB983 Ultrasens backlit imaging system (Berthold Technologies, Bad Wildbad, Germany) using Indigo software. Each tobacco leaf was sprayed with 100 mmol L− 1 luciferin and kept in darkness for 5 min prior to luminescence detection. 

# Virus-induced gene silencing

A specific coding fragment of SlMYB12 (1–300 bp) was ligated into the pTRV2 vector to generate SlMYB12-pTRV2, which was then introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101. Agrobacterium cultures carrying SlMYB12- pTRV2 or pTRV1 were collected and resuspended in infiltration buffer (10 mM MgCl₂, 10 mM 2-Morpholinoethanesulphonic acid [pH 5.6], and $1 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ acetosyringone) to an ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 1.0 (Fu et al., 2005). After incubation in the dark for 3 h at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , cultures of SlMYB12-pTRV2 and pTRV1 at a 1:1 ratio were infiltrated into the stipes of MG-stage fruits using a syringe. Fruits injected with empty TRV served as controls. Transgenic samples were collected and imaged 9 days after inoculation. Three independent biological replicates were analyzed for each genotype/treatment combination and time point, with each replicate consisting of three fruits harvested from the same node of a single plant. 

# RNA-seq

WT, bzr1, bes1, and bzr1 bes1 fruits at the MG, B, and P stages were collected for total RNA extraction. Illumina MiSeq libraries were constructed according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) and sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform. 

# DAP-seq

DAP-seq binding assays were performed by Bluescape Scientific Biotechnology(Hebei, China). 

# Carotenoid extraction and HPLC analysis

Carotenoid extraction and quantification were performed as described previously (Liu et al., 2012). Peak areas were identified based on spectral characteristics and quantified against standard compounds (lutein, lycopene, and $\beta$ -carotene; Sigma). 

# Metabolome GWAS

Genomic resequencing data from 356 tomato accessions were used to construct the association panel as described earlier (Zhu et al., 2018). The greatest natural variation within a 0.5 Mb interval surrounding the GWAS signal was identified as a candidate for causal natural variation. 

# Quantification and statistical analysis

Statistical methods are described in the relevant sections under Methods and in the figure legends. Data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 9 software. Two-tailed Student’s t tests were used for pairwise comparisons $^ { \star } P < 0 . 0 5$ , $^ { \star \star } P < 0 . 0 1$ , $^ { \star \star \star } P < 0 . 0 0 1$ ). Tukey’s test $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ and one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) were utilized for multiple comparisons. Specific statistical tests used are indicated in the figure legends.
