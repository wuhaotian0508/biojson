ABSTRACT: Pyrethrins from Tanacetum cinerariifolium are natural pesticides that exhibit high knockdown and killing activities against flying insects such as diseasespreading mosquitoes. Despite the increasing demand for pyrethrins, the mechanism of pyrethrin biosynthesis remains elusive. To elucidate it, we for the first time created pyrethrin mimetic phosphonates targeting the GDSL esterase/ lipase (GELP or TcGLIP) underpinning pyrethrin biosynthesis. The compounds were synthesized by reacting mono-alkyl or mono-benzyl-substituted phosphonic dichloride with pyrethrolone, the alcohol moiety of pyrethrin I and II, and then $p$ - nitrophenol. $n$ -Pentyl (C5) and $n$ -octyl (C8)-substituted compounds were the most potent of the $( S ) _ { \mathrm { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathrm { c } } ,$ and $( R ) _ { \mathrm { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathrm { c } }$ diastereomers, respectively. The (S)- pyrethrolonyl group is more effective than the (R)-pyrethrolonyl group in blocking TcGLIP, consistent with the features predicted by TcGLIP models complexed with the (S)p,(S)c-C5 and $( R ) _ { \mathrm { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathrm { c } } { - } { \mathrm { C } } 8$ probes. The $( \dot { S } ) _ { \mathrm { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathrm { c } } { - } { \mathrm C } S$ compound suppressed 

pyrethrin production in $T .$ . cinerariifolium, demonstrating potential as a chemical tool for unravelling pyrethrin biosynthesis.

# INTRODUCTION

Tanacetum cinerariifolium, a member of the Asteraceae family, produces potent pyrethrin insecticides which accumulate in the disc florets (Figure 1A).1−4 Pyrethrins modulate voltagesensitive sodium channels, impairing nerve conduction and rapidly poisoning insects.5 Pyrethrins also act on antennae odorant receptors, repelling some flying insects.6,7 Due to these activities, pyrethrins and pyrethrum extract, from T. cinerariifolium flower heads, are often used for pest control. Meanwhile, pyrethroids, synthetic pyrethrin analogues with higher insecticidal efficacy and simpler production, are employed more intensively than pyrethrins to control agricultural and household insect pests.8 However, natural pyrethrins remain viable products as they exhibit highly selective toxicity toward insects over vertebrates and have low environmental persistency, which satisfies consumers’ demand for safe, low-persistence, and carbon-neutral products. Thus, elucidating the mechanism of pyrethrin biosynthesis in T. cinerariifolium for enhanced production is essential. 

Pyrethrins are esters of mono-terpene chrysanthemic or pyrethric acid with octadecanoid pathway products, the rethrolone alcohols (pyrethrolone, cinerolone, and jasmololone), resulting in the six pyrethrin members (Figure 1A).2−4 To date, both the involvement of chrysanthemyl diphosphate synthase in cyclopropane ring formation and enzymes that convert chrysanthemic acid to pyrethric acid and facilitate rethrolone biosynthesis are identified.3,4 In addition, we 

discovered a GELP called “TcGLIP” that catalyzes ester bond formation during the final pyrethrin biosynthesis step.9 GELPs are a large family of enzymes that facilitate homeostasis in microorganisms, plants, insects, and vertebrates.10−12 

Notably, ${ \scriptstyle \mathrm { > } } 1 0 0$ GELPs are expressed in Oryza sativa and Arabidopsis thaliana, yet many functions of GELPs remain unknown.11,12 TcGLIP is the only GELP for which the native substrates are known to function as a defense against herbivores, with the substrate structure−activity relationship studied in detail.9,12,13 TcGLIP undergoes feedback control by coenzyme A (CoA) released in the pyrethrin I (1)-forming reaction of (1R,3R)-chrysanthemoyl CoA and (S)-pyrethrolone.13 The TcGLIP gene is expressed dominantly in flowers and leaves but much less in roots, accounting for pyrethrin content variations in these tissues.9 The TcGLIP catalytic efficiency order of (S)-pyrethrolone $>$ (S)-cinerolone $>$ (S)- jasmololone is similar to that of pyrethrin $\mathrm { I } / \mathrm { I I } >$ cinerin $\mathrm { I } / \mathrm { I I } >$ jasmolin I/II in T. cinerariifolium, indicating a TcGLIP contribution in pyrethrin amount and composition.13 How-

T. cinerariifolium is self-compatible and contains paralogous TcGLIP-like genes in its genome.14 Hence, conventional genetics is difficult for investigating whether TcGLIP is a determinant of pyrethrin amount and composition. An alternative is to employ chemical tools able to collectively knockdown TcGLIP and its paralogues. The enzyme catalyzes the acyltransferase reaction for pyrethrin synthesis, in which Ser40 in the catalytic triad attacks the carbonyl carbon of the (1R,3R)-chrysanthemoyl CoA thioester, forming a chrysanthemoylated enzyme via the oxyanion intermediate I, which then interacts with the (S)-rethrolone alcohol substrates yielding pyrethrins via the oxyanion intermediate $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { I I } } ^ { 1 5 }$ (Figure 1B). Like organophosphate pesticides that target acetylcholine esterases, phosphonates also interact with Ser40, irreversibly blocking TcGLIP (Figure 2A). Thus, for the first time, we synthesized phosphonates with (S)-pyrethrolone and its enantiomer to investigate structure−activity relationships to block the enzyme and then evaluated the potency to reduce pyrethrin biosyn-

thesis in T. cinerariifolium. We report that one compound is particularly active and may be used as a tool to chemically knockdown pyrethrin biosynthesis.

It is probably due to a higher steric hindrance of pyrethrolone than $p$ -nitrophenol in the nucleophilic attack to the substituted phosphonochloridate.

To better understand the structure−activity relationships, we modeled TcGLIPs complexed with 5a and 8b using AlphaFold2 and AutoDock Vina and energy-minimized the models with Gromacs (Figure 4), taking into consideration that the chrysanthemoyl moiety enters the catalytic center first and then rethrolone accesses the acylated enzyme in the case of native substrates (Figure 1B) and that the phosphorus atom of inhibitors is located sufficiently close to Ser40 to permit nucleophilic attack of the phosphorus atom by the residue. The model revealed that the C5 alkyl chain of 5a could interact 

with Pro211 located inside the binding site, while the alkyl chain of 8b can escape from such steric interaction, accounting at least in part for the different structure−activity relationships for the $( \bar { S } ) _ { \mathfrak { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathfrak { c } }$ and $( R ) _ { \mathrm { p } } , ( S ) _ { \mathrm { c } }$ compounds. On the other hand, the conjugated diene in the side chain of the pyrethrolonyl group underwent $\pi - \pi$ and hydrophobic interactions with the aromatic residues (Phe56, Tyr297, and Phe320), in support of higher affinity of (S)-pyrethrolone than (S)-jasmololone lacking such moiety for the enzyme.13 

These models also revealed that the carbonyl oxygen of the (S)-pyrethrolonyl moiety could form a hydrogen bond with the main chain NH of Gly114 (see the hashed line in Figure 4A,B). Elimination of this bond is predicted to reduce the activity, explaining the activities of 5a and 5b containing (S)- pyrethrolonyl groups higher than those of their enantiomers 12a and 12b containing (R)-pyrethrolonyl groups (Table 1). As such, the TcGLIP−inhibitor complex models (Figure 4) can explain the structure−activity relationships (Table 1). Nevertheless, X-ray crystal structure determination of TcGLIP complexed with inhibitors is necessary to verify the models’ accuracy. 

To examine the capacity for in vivo pyrethrin-production suppression, T. cinerariifolium seedlings were incubated with each compound, and pyrethrin I and II in the leaves were quantified. Among the series “a”, 5a reduced pyrethrin I, and 4a, 5a, and 6a reduced pyrethrin II in T. cinerariifolium leaves (Figure 5A,B), in line with the observed in vitro structureactivity relationships (Table 1). However, no series “b” compound had a significant effect on pyrethrin biosynthesis in vivo (Figure 5C,D). Notably, 8b, despite its high in vitro activity, did not affect pyrethrin biosynthesis. The high hydrophobicity of 8b $\left( \mathrm { C L o g } P \ = \ 6 . 2 4 \right)$ may prevent its translocation from the culture medium to TcGLIP. Indeed, 5a and 8b were detected at $2 7 . 2 \pm 1 6 . 6$ and $1 . 2 2 \pm 0 . 9 4 ~ \mathrm { p m o l }$ from 1 mg leaves [mean $\pm$ standard error of the mean ${ \bf \nabla } ( n =$ 4)], confirming limited translocation of 8b. 

In conclusion, we have created novel pyrethrin-mimetic phosphonates as probes to target TcGLIP. Compound 5a most clearly inhibited TcGLIP and suppressed pyrethrin accumulation in the leaves of T. cinerariifolium. Therefore, 5a is useful not only in elucidating mechanisms of TcGLIP−substrate interactions but also enables key pyrethrin biosynthesis determinants to be identified by examining the genetic response of T. cinerariifolium to exposure.

# EXPERIMENTAL SECTION

Chemical Reagents. All chemical reagents used for synthesis were used without further purification. 

Instruments. High-resolution mass (HRMS) spectra were obtained using a Q-Tof Premier MS spectrometer (Waters, USA). Optical rotation was measured by a DIP-370 (Na lamp, $5 8 9 \ \mathrm { n m }$ JASCO, Japan). The compound concentration at which its specific rotation was determined was described in $\mathrm { g } \mathrm { d } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ . NMR spectra were recorded at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on a RESONANCE ECX-500 spectrometer ${ \mathrm { ( J E O L , } }$ Japan) using deuterated solvent signals as the standard. Melting points were uncorrected. Crystallographic data for 9a and 9b were collected on a Varimax/Saturn $^ { 7 2 4 + }$ using a CCD diffractometer with Mo $\mathrm { K } \alpha$ radiation (Rigaku, Japan). The CrystalClear-SM 1.4 SP1 program (Rigaku, 2008) was used to integrate the diffraction profiles. Crystal structures were solved by a direct method using the SHELXT program and refined using the SHELXL program.16,17 Anisotropic thermal parameters were used to refine all non-H atoms. 

Purity of 2a−12a and 2b−12b. Preparation of Recombinant TcGLIP. Recombinant TcGLIP was prepared as previously described.9,13,15 TcGLIP was expressed functionally as the fusion protein with maltose binding protein using the pMAL-c4E vector (New England Biolabs, USA) in the Origami B strain of Escherichia coli (Merck Millipore, USA) with $0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { \ m M }$ isopropyl- $\beta$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside as an inducer. The recombinant TcGLIP was purified with amylose resin (New England Biolabs) and a Mono Q column (Cytiva, USA) and dissolved in $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl buffer $\left( \mathrm { p H } \ 7 . 5 \right)$ . 





Enzyme Inhibition Assay. TcGLIP $[ 4 \mu \mathrm { g \ m L ^ { - 1 } }$ in $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl $\left( \mathrm { p H } \ 7 . 5 \right) \mathrm { . }$ ] was incubated with each compound [final dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) concentration $= 1 \% ]$ at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , and the enzyme solution was mixed with an equal volume of substrate mixture [final substrate concentrations: (1R,3R)-chrysanthemoyl CoA, $2 \mathrm { m M }$ ; (S)-pyrethrolone $\mathbf { \mu } = 1 \mathbf { \mu } _ { \mathbf { m M } } ]$ at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ . The reaction was stopped by acetic acid and extracted with an equal volume of $n$ - hexane. Pyrethrin I concentrations in the extract were determined in terms of UV absorption at $2 3 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ by HPLC using a Cadenza CD-C18 column with an $8 0 \%$ $\mathrm { M e C N } { - } 2 0 \%$ water mixture at a flow rate of $1 . 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } ~ \mathrm { { m i n } ^ { - 1 } } }$ as previously described.13 





Data Fitting. The TcGLIP inhibition−concentration data were fitted by the following equation using Prism 9 (GraphPad software, USA). 



$$
Y = \frac {1 0 0}{1 + 1 0 ^ {(\log L - \log \mathrm {I C} _ {5 0}) n _ {\mathrm {H}}}}
$$

In the equation, Y is the percent of control activity of TcGLIP, $L$ is the inhibitor concentration (M), $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ is the half inhibition concentration (M), and $n _ { \mathrm { H } }$ is the Hill coefficient. Data were obtained as mean $\pm$ standard error of the mean $\left( n = 3 \right)$ ). 

Effects on Pyrethrin Contents in the Seedlings of T. cinerariifolium. The young seedlings of T. cinerariifolium were aseptically cultured in the modified Murashige Skoog (MS) medium containing $0 . 3 \%$ gellan gum at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $\bar { 1 } 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ light $3 0 { - } 4 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 } ) / 8$ h dark cycle. The modified MS medium contains Murashige and Skoog’s (MS) macro- and micronutrients18 plus Ringe and Nitsch’s organic constituents.19 Compounds in DMSO were mixed in the MS medium to achieve a final DMSO concentration of $0 . 1 \%$ (v/v). A petiole with a single leaf from the T. cinerariifolium seedling was inserted in the modified MS medium in the absence (control, $0 . 1 \%$ DMSO) and presence of the compounds with $0 . 1 \%$ DMSO and incubated for 2 weeks at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. Each leaf was minced in $2 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ acetone, and the acetone extract $( 1 ~ \mathrm { m L } )$ filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter. Pyrethrin I and II in the extract were determined by HPLC. Pyrethrin I/II were monitored at $2 3 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ using a Cosmosil 3PBr column $( 3 . 0 \times 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m } ,$ Nacalai Tesque, Japan) with a $6 5 \%$ acetonitrile $- 3 5 \%$ ammonium acetate (50 mM) mixture at a flow rate of $0 . 6 ~ \mathrm { m L ~ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ . When the effects of 4a and 4b on the pyrethrin production were determined, HPLC analyses of pyrethrin II were performed using a Cadenza CD-C18 column with an $8 0 \%$ acetonitrile $- 2 0 \%$ water mixture at a flow rate of $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { m L }$ $\mathrm { { m i n } ^ { - 1 } }$ . Experiments were repeated with 10 biological replicates for the control $0 . 1 \%$ DMSO) and 5 biological replicates for the treatment of the compounds. 

Statistical analysis of the pyrethrin biosynthesis inhibition data was conducted by one-way ANOVA (false discovery rate analysis20) at a level of $q < 0 . 0 5$ using Prism 9. 

TcGLIP Models in Complex with Phosphonate Probes. The docking model of TcGLIP with phosphonate probes (5a and 8b) was constructed using AlphaFold2 (Version 2.2.3),21 AutoDock Vina (Version 1.1.2),22 and Gromacs (Version 2022.2).23 First, a structure model of TcGLIP was constructed by AlphaFold2, while the initial inhibitor structures were drawn by Chem3D (Version 20.1), and their geometries were optimized by MOPAC using the PM7 method. Binding sites for the phosphonate inhibitors were identified by 

AutoDock Vina with permitting rotation of any unconjugated single bond of inhibitors. The search box was manually set to cover the entire groove of TcGLIP by Chimera (Version 1.16). From candidate TcGLIP−inhibitor complex models indicated by AutoDock Vina, we chose the models in which the phosphorus atom was in the proximity of Ser40 and the (S)-pyrethrolonyl moiety was located close to the catalytic site entrance following the TcGLIP reaction mechanism (Figure 1B). 

The structure coordinates obtained by AutoDock Vina were energy-minimized by Gromacs with CGenFF24,25 and CHARMM36 force field updated in July 2022.26
