# SUMMARY

Bitterness in citrus fruit is conferred by flavanone-neohesperidosides, whose accumulation is catalyzed by a single enzyme flavanone-7-O-glucosides-1,2-rhamnosyltransferase (1,2RhaT), expressed in both leaves and fruit. To eliminate citrus bitterness, we used CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing to inactivate the 1,2RhaT gene in grapefruit (Citrus paradisi) and “Carrizo” citrange (Citrus sinensis $\times$ Citrus trifoliata). Edited lines displayed frameshift mutations that introduced premature stop codons, effectively abolishing the synthesis of the bitter neohesperidosides naringin, neohesperidin, and poncirin. Metabolomic analyses in leaves from 1,2RhaTmutant lines confirmed the absence of bitter flavanone-neohesperidosides and a compensatory increase in the tasteless flavanone-rutinosides hesperidin, didymin, and narirutin. Since 1,2RhaT is encoded by a single gene, our findings in leaves are expected to be identical for fruit and thus demonstrate a strategy for developing non-bitter citrus cultivars while retaining health-benefitting flavonoid levels. Furthermore, cold-hardy citrus species that are currently unacceptably bitter due to high flavanone-neohesperidoside levels may become useful sources for introduction of cold-hardiness following inactivation of the 1,2RhaT gene. This approach thus paves the way for expanding grapefruit markets and breeding cold-hardy, palatable citrus varieties that are better suited to a wider range of climates. 

Keywords: bitterness, Citrus paradisi, Citrus sinensis, Citrus trifoliata, CRISPR/Cas9, genome editing, sgRNA, transformation. 

# INTRODUCTION

The sensation of bitterness is one of five basic taste-sensing mechanisms in humans that underlie dietary choices. Of the five basic tastes (sweet, bitter, sour, salty, and umami), the bitter taste-sensing mechanism is the most diverse at the molecular level, involving over 30 genes coding for “bitter-receptor”-variants (Mennella & Bobowski, 2015; Wooding et al., 2021), thus allowing for recognition of a wide variety of toxic natural compounds. By signaling the presence of toxins in foods before they are ingested, bitter perception functions as a survival mechanism that leads to toxin avoidance (Wooding et al., 2021); it is especially beneficial in infants and children, who are very sensitive to bitterness (Mennella et al., 2005). At the same time, this toxin-avoidance mechanism appears to signal many “false positives,” including safe and nutritious compounds recognized by the bitter receptors and consequently perceived as bitter and sometimes even repulsive, thus limiting the consumption of a 

variety of nutritious foods, especially in children. Since the sensitivity to bitterness diminishes somewhat with age (Mennella et al., 2005), bitter foods that lack toxins, such as grapefruit, become part of some adults’ diets, presumably as an acquired taste (Cowart et al., 1994). Bitterness in citrus fruit and juice is attributed to natural compounds belonging to two different phytochemical groups: flavonoids and triterpenoids. “Primary bitterness”—characteristic of fresh fruit and freshly squeezed juice of grapefruit (Citrus paradisi) and several other citrus species, is attributed to flavanone-7-O-neohesperidosides, a subgroup of glycosylated flavonoids. “Secondary bitterness”—the bitterness that accumulates over time in citrus juices exposed to atmospheric oxygen—is attributed to the oxidation of limonoids, which are tetranortriterpenes (Hasegawa & Miyake, 1996). While there are a range of debittering methods available for primary and secondary bitterness of citrus juice (Konwar et al., 2024; Purewal & Sandhu, 2021; Shaw et al., 2000), a genetic-engineering approach could 

simplify and shorten processing, thus raise costeffectiveness. More importantly, there are no currently available methods to reduce the bitterness of varieties consumed as fresh fruit. 

The biosynthesis of flavonoids affecting primary bitterness in citrus has been extensively studied, and the genes and encoded enzymes involved in the bitter flavor-determining step have been cloned and functionally characterized (Frydman et al., 2004, 2013). In grapefruit, two rhamnosyltransferases (1,2RhaT and 1,6RhaT) compete for rhamnosylation of a common substrate: flavanone-7-O-glucoside (Figure 1). 1,2RhaT catalyzes the biosynthesis of the bitter neohesperidosides (naringin, poncirin, and neohesperidin), whereas 1,6RhaT catalyzes the biosynthesis of the tasteless rutinosides (narirutin, didymin, and hesperidin) (Figure 1). 

In a recent study, Chen et al. (2021) showed that 1,2RhaT is the major factor in primary bitterness of citrus fruit, where a full correlation was found between bitterness in 87 citrus varieties and the 1,2RhaT functional allele. Among citrus species, pummelo (Citrus maxima) was detected as the genetic origin for bitterness in cultivated edible citrus varieties, by donating a functional allele to its hybrid progeny. Grapefruit, for example, results from a cross between mandarin (Citrus reticulata) and pummelo (Wu et al., 2018), which explains its typical bitterness. However, mandarins and hybrids such as sweet orange and lemon (Citrus sinensis and Citrus limon, respectively), which did not inherit the functional allele of 1,2RhaT, do

Citrus fruits with high bitterness differ in neohesperidoside level and composition; in grapefruit, naringin is the major compound causing bitterness, whereas in trifoliate orange (Citrus trifoliata, previously Poncirus trifoliata), high levels of several compounds, such as naringin, neohesperidin, and poncirin, make the fruit unacceptably bitter and consequently inedible (Jeffries et al., 2025; Mou et al., 2021). 

Citrus fruits are considered to contribute nutritious metabolites for a balanced diet; they are the highest producers of flavanone glycosides, which confer a variety of health benefits as antioxidants and anti-inflammatories, preventing infections and boosting the immune system in humans (Benavente-Garcıa et al., 1997; Shamloo et al., 2015; Tripoli et al., 2007). Elimination of bitterness in grapefruit may enable higher consumption of this healthbenefiting fruit, while “Carrizo” citrange, a widely used rootstock, may serve as a parent for crosses aimed to produce new varieties with inherited cold-hardiness as well as huanglongbing tolerance (Belknap et al., 2011). With the aim of demonstrating the potential to abolish primary bitterness in citrus, we targeted the gene 1,2RhaT in grapefruit and a trifoliate orange hybrid (C. sinensis $\times \ c$ . trifoliata) using genome editing. The resulting lines lacked the typical bitter flavanone neohesperidosides with no reduction in overall flavonoid levels due to compensatory production of the analogous flavanone rutinosides.

# Inactivating the “Carrizo” citrange 1,2RhaT by genome editing abolishes biosynthesis of bitter flavanone-neohesperidosides with concomitant increased synthesis of tasteless flavanone-rutinosides

About 300 “Carrizo” citrange epicotyl explants were subjected to Agrobacterium-mediated transformation targeting the 1,2RhaT gene using Cas9/sgRNA (Figure 3). Two independent transgenic lines, CTr34 and CTr60, were identified (Figure S1). The presence of Cas9 in the genome of these lines was confirmed using specific primers (Table S2). Sequencing analysis with 1,2RhaT-specific primers (RTg1) revealed targeted modifications (Table S2). In both CTr34 and CTr60, Cas9/sgRNA-mediated mutations in 1,2RhaT resulted in a 1-base insertion (Figure 4) at the fourth base from the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) 

site. This insertion led to the formation of a premature termination codon 13 bases downstream of the PAM region, resulting in a truncated protein. Metabolomic analysis of young leaves from “Carrizo” transformants was performed to assess changes in flavanone glycosides. Poncirin, the predominant bitter compound in wild-type “Carrizo,” was completely absent in CTr34 and CTr60. Similarly, the bitter compounds naringin and neohesperidin were undetectable. In contrast, levels of the tasteless flavanone didymin, along with narirutin and hesperidin, were elevated (Table 1; Figure 5).

# DISCUSSION

In the current study, we addressed the possibility of abolishing bitterness in citrus while maintaining the overall level of flavonoids, which are considered health-benefitting compounds. Grapefruit was chosen as a target organism due to the potential to dramatically alter the taste of a widely consumed fruit, while the rootstock “Carrizo” citrange was chosen due to its potential use in breeding as a parent that bestows cold-hardiness and HLB tolerance. 

We demonstrated the ability to abolish primary bitterness in citrus by disrupting the 1,2RhaT gene via genome editing. Abolishing or moderating bitterness has the potential to lead to, respectively, non-bitter or less bitter variants of existing grapefruit varieties. Because grapefruit flavor is not limited to bitterness, but rather is composed of a variety of metabolites, including the grapefruit essence compound nootkatone (Berry et al., 1967), non-bitter or mildly bitter grapefruit variants may appeal to individuals who currently do not consume this fruit, such as children and young adults, thus opening new markets for grapefruit growers. 

The ability to abolish or moderate bitterness opens possibilities for breeding cold-hardy edible citrus varieties based on crosses with the deciduous trifoliate orange (C. trifoliata), which bestows extreme cold hardiness— together with unacceptable bitterness—to progeny. Cold-hardiness is a desirable trait to improve tree survival under extreme temperature shifts, which are becoming more common due to climate change, as well as to develop varieties designed for a wider climatic cultivation range. In addition to cold-hardiness, trifoliate orange shows tolerance and sometimes even resistance to the devastating citrus HLB disease, encouraging breeders to incorporate it into breeding programs (Jeffries et al., 2025). # Plasmid construction and Agrobacterium-mediated transformation

Genome editing of 1,2RhaT was conducted based on the generation of transgenic plants expressing Cas9 and the relevant guide (g) RNA (Figure 3; Table S2). We constructed a vector containing plant codon-optimized Cas9 (pcoCas) fused to the nuclear-localization sequence at the C-terminal end according to Khedia et al. (2025); Cas9 expression was driven by a double cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter, and a neomycin phosphotransferase-II (NptII ) gene (conferring kanamycin resistance) enabled selection for transgenic plants. Single guide RNA (sgRNA) was designed to target the coding region of 1,2RhaT using CRISPR2.0. This gRNA was expressed under the control of a short NekU6 promoter (pU6) from Arabidopsis. The sgRNA-expression cassette was synthesized and cloned into pMA plasmid by GeneArt (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The pMA-sgRNA plasmid was digested with MluI and HindIII, and the released sgRNA cassette was ligated using T4 ligase into the compatible sites of the pRCNptII-Cas9 vector according to manufacturer protocol (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) to derive the binary vector pRCNptII-Cas9NekU61,2RhaTsgRNA1 (T-DNA construct; Figure 3). 

Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 containing binary vector pRCNptII-Cas9NekU61,2RhaTsgRNA1 was used for transformation as described by Orbovic and Grosser (2015). A single Agrobacterium colony was cultured in LB medium containing $5 0 ~ { \mu \ g } ~ { \mathsf { m l } } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin and $1 0 \ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mathsf { m l } ^ { - 1 }$ rifampicin on a shaker at $\mathsf { 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 2 days. On the day of the experiment, the culture was centrifuged and resuspended to a final $O D _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.8 in MS medium supplemented with $ 1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone. Epicotyl segment explants were cut obliquely into 1-cm long segments and incubated with the Agrobacterium culture for 10 min in a vacuum 

chamber $( 4 5 0 \ \mathrm { m m H } \mathfrak { g }$ ), blotted dry on sterile paper towels, and placed on solid CM medium (MS salts and vitamins supplemented with 13.2 lM BAP, $0 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ NAA, $3 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } ~ { \mathsf { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ sucrose) and $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ acetosyringone and incubated in the dark at $\mathsf { 2 6 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 2 days. The explants were then transferred to regeneration medium supplemented with the antibiotics kanamycin $( 7 5 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ \mathsf { m l } ^ { - 1 } )$ and cefotaxime $( 5 0 0 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } \ : \mathsf { m l } ^ { - 1 } )$ and cultured in the dark for 2 weeks at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ followed by transfer to light conditions: 16 h light/8 h dark cycle using cool white fluorescent light (75 mmol $\mathsf { s e c } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } )$ at $\mathsf { 2 6 ^ { \circ } C }$ and $7 5 \%$ relative humidity. 

# Micrografting

Elongated transgenic shoots were micrografted onto rootstock seedlings of “Carrizo” citrange that were germinated according to Orbovic and Grosser (2015), to expedite the recovery of transgenic plants. After a month of growth in sterile tubes containing grafting medium (MS, $7 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } ~ \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ sucrose), the grafted shoots were potted in a soil mix containing $70 \%$ peat and $30 \%$ perlite (w/w), supplemented with slow-release fertilizer (Even-Ari, Moshav, Israel). 

# Molecular analysis of transformants and edited plants

Genomic DNA was extracted from young leaves of regenerated plants (“Carrizo” and “Duncan”) using the GenEluteTM Plant Genomic DNA Kit according to the manufacturer’s protocol. PCR was performed using a ProFlex Thermal Cycler (Thermo Fisher Scientific) with Cas9-specific primers. 

Gene-specific primers (RTg1; Table S2) were designed to amplify the 1,2RhaT genomic DNA region corresponding to the sgRNA binding site and flanking regions. PCR was conducted using the proofreading Phusion High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and the amplified 350-bp products were outsourced for Sanger sequencing (Hylabs Sequencing Services, Rehovot, Israel). 

# Extraction of flavanone glycosides and quantification by LC–MS

Young leaf samples (0.2 g) were frozen in liquid nitrogen and pulverized at ${ } ^ { 2 8 \mathsf { H z } }$ for 1 min, using TissueLyser III (Qiagen, Venlo, The Netherlands). The powder was mixed with butanol (1:1, v/v), centrifuged at $1 0 0 0 0 \ { \pmb q } , \ 4 ^ { \circ } { \pmb C }$ for 15 min, and the butanol fraction was centrifuged again at $1 0 \ 0 0 0 \ \pmb { g }$ for 10 min. A 3-ml aliquot of the upper phase was dried in a speed-vacuum centrifuge, and the precipitate was dissolved in $5 \mathrm { \ m l }$ of $100 \%$ methanol and filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \cdot  { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filter. LC–MS analysis was conducted in a UPLC-Triple Quadrupole-MS apparatus (Waters Xevo TQ mass spectrometry system). The samples were refiltered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ Millex-HV Durapore (PVDF) membrane before injection into the LC–MS apparatus. Separation was performed on a $2 . 1 \times 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ i.d., $1 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ UPLC BEH C18 column. The mobile phase consisted of water (phase A) and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile (phase B) following the linear-gradient program: $100 \%$ to $9 5 \%$ A over 0.1 min, $9 5 \%$ to $5 \%$ A over 5 min, held at $5 \%$ A for 2 min, then back to the initial conditions $( 9 5 \%$ A) for 3 min. The flow rate was $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l } ~ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ and the column temperature was kept at $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ . All of the analyses were performed with the ESI source in positive-ion mode, with a capillary voltage of $3 . 2 \mathrm { ~ k V } ,$ a cone voltage of ${ } _ { 3 0 } \lor ,$ a desolvation temperature of $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ a desolvation gas flow rate of $8 5 0 \mathsf { L } \mathsf { h } ^ { - 1 }$ , and a source temperature of $\mathfrak { 1 5 0 \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ . Quantification was performed with MRM acquisition by monitoring the 581/152, 581/273 transitions, $\mathsf { R T } = 6 . 0 7$ (dwell time of 161 msec for each transition) for didymin, hesperidin, naringin,
