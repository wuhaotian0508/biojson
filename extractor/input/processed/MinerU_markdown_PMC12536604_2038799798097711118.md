# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Plant-fungal interactions stimulate endophytic fungi to produce a plethora of metabolites that enhance plant growth and improve stress resistance. Opines, naturally occurring compounds formed through the condensation of amino acids with $\propto$ -keto acids or sugars, have diverse biological functions and are mainly present in bacteria. Interestingly, investigations have revealed the presence of opine synthases (OSases) in fungal species as well, and their functions are yet to be studied. 

Objectives: The objective of this study is to investigate the occurrence of OSases in fungal species, identify their products, and characterize the potential biological activity of the metabolites. 

Methods: We identified a putative class of OSases in fungi through sequence similarity network (SSN) analysis. The function of these enzymes was elucidated using methods including protein heterologous expression, in vitro biochemical characterization, in vivo gene knock-out, as well as product isolation and identification. Additionally, we conducted plant activity testing on the secondary metabolites through foliar spraying and performed transcriptomic analysis to uncover their functions. 

Results: A quarter of the PF18631 family members, which contain the C-terminal helical bundle domain of cucumopine synthase, are derived from endophytic fungi. Some of these enzymes catalyze the synthesis of tryptopine A (1-acetyl-3-carboxy- $\beta$ -carboline) by condensing L-tryptophan and methylglyoxal. The 

tryptopine A can act as a growth regulator, promoting plant growth and transcriptionally reprogramming photosynthesis-related pathways, while enhancing the rate of plant photosynthesis by $2 5 \%$ . 

Conclusion: The findings of this study suggest that tryptopine A plays a crucial role as a signaling molecule in the establishment and maintenance of mutualistic associations between endophytic fungi and host plants, thereby broaden our comprehension of fungal-plant symbiosis.

# Introduction

Fungal endophytes are a diverse group of microorganisms that colonize plant tissues without causing any harm to their hosts [1–3]. They are ubiquitous in the plant kingdom and have been isolated from almost all parts of plants [4,5]. The interest in utilizing endophytic fungi for sustainable agriculture is growing rapidly due to their potential to enhance crop yield through direct and indirect mechanisms [6–9]: (i) improving nutrient acquisition [10], (ii) modulating plant development [11], (iii) protecting plants from pathogens and herbivores [12], (iv) enhancing stress tolerance in plants [13], and (v) optimizing photosynthesis [13]. 

Fungal endophytes serve as rich sources of biologically active natural compounds. These fungi produce a wide diverse range of secondary metabolites, including alkaloids, terpenoids, polyketides steroids, phenols, and peptides [14]. Many of these secondary metabolites exhibit potent antimicrobial and antioxidant activities, which help protect plants against both biotic and abiotic stresses [14,15]. Additionally, endophytes also synthesize plant growthpromoting hormones such as gibberellin (GA), indole acetic acid (IAA), salicylic acid, jasmonic acid-isoleucine (JA-Ile), and cytokinin [16,17]. 

With the advancement of sequencing technology, the strategy of genome mining has been established to guide targeted discovery of natural products [18–20]. This approach, combined with efficient genome editing, optimized expression systems, and artificial intelligence technology, has significantly improved the specificity and success rate in discovering active natural products. Consequently, thousands of new compounds have been isolated and identified from endophytic fungi, leading to the identification of numerous biosynthetic pathways and enzymes [21]. 

Despite apparent progress, significant gaps still exist in this interdisciplinary field of research. It remains highly challenging to explore how each endophyte and its host plant coordinate metabolic biosynthesis [22], particularly regarding the real biological activities of these small molecules. The primary reason for this is that the current mainstream view considers natural products as the primary source of clinical drugs, with research on natural products mostly focusing on drug development and screening models usually emphasizing antimicrobial and anticancer properties [14,23]. Successful developments include the antibiotic penicillin, cholesterol-lowering lovastatin, and antifungal drug echinocandin, etc.[24]. Additionally, molecular biological functions are often unrelated to genetic information, making the discovery of these biological activities very challenging. These reasons have led to the neglect of the positive effects of secondary metabolites on plant growth and development. In fact, the variety of natural active compounds generated by endophytes results from their close biological relationship with host plants [25,26]. Exploring secondary metabolites from endophytic fungi holds great promise for discovering novel plant growth regulators. 

Opines, also known as N-(carboxyalkyl) amino acids, consist of a diverse range of amino acid and $\alpha$ -keto acid substrates and play various functional roles, widely distributed in marine invertebrates and bacteria (Fig. 1A). It has been almost a century since the discovery of the first opine in 1927. In octopuses and other cephalo-

pods, anaerobic glycolysis produces octopine as the end product instead of lactate dehydrogenase, thereby regenerating $\mathsf { N A D } ^ { + }$ in muscle tissue through the catalytic action of octopine dehydrogenase on the reductive condensation of pyruvate and arginine [27]. Agrobacterium naturally integrates genes encoding auxin and cytokinin into the plant genome through T-DNA transfer, forcing plant cells to replicate and causing crown galls. Additionally, T-DNA encodes a gene responsible for synthesizing opines, smallmolecule compounds that play crucial roles in crown gall formation and cytotoxicity transfer in plants [28,29]. Opines serve as carbon and nitrogen sources for invasive bacteria while also acting as signal molecules to activate quorum sensing in Agrobacterium. Also, opines have been reported in other bacteria such as Pseudomonas spp., Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and Staphylococcus aureus, etc. [30–32]. 

The opines synthases (OSases) can be classified into three distinct types based on the nature of their substrate and the mechanism of their reaction (Fig. 1B) [33]: (I) Opine dehydrogenases catalyze the formation of opines through a Schiff’s base condensation between amino acid and $\mathscr { X }$ -keto acid, followed by reduction to yield the final product such as octopine, vitopine and alanopine, etc. This reaction requires NAD(P)H as reducing cofactors [34]; (II) In the presence of L-glutamine and D-glucose, the Schiff’s base intermediate undergoes an irreversible Amadori rearrangement to produce santhopine and then reduced to form mannopine. Such reactions are commonly observed in the Maillard reaction and protein glycosylation, involving condensation reactions between glucose and amino groups in free amino acids or polypeptides [35]; (III) Cucumopine synthase and mikimophine synthase catalyze the condensation of L-histidine and $\alpha$ -ketoglutaric acid. The intermediates formed do not undergo reduction but instead undergo intramolecular cyclization via Pictet-Spengler reaction without requiring any cofactor; hence, these enzymes are also referred to as Pictet-Spenglerases (PSases) (Fig. S1) [36,37]. 

In our previous study, Streptomyces-derived PSase StnK2, which catalyzes the formation of a tetrahydro- $\cdot \beta$ -carboline $\left( \mathrm { T H } \beta \mathsf { C } \right)$ scaffold from (2S,3S)- $\cdot \beta$ -methyl tryptophan and D-erythrose-4-phosphate during the biosynthesis of the antitumor antibiotic Streptonigrin [38]. Additionally, during the biosynthesis of Marinacarbolines A, McbB from Marinactinospora thermotolerans facilitates the condensation reaction between L-tryptophan and oxaloacetaldehyde or methylglyoxal to generate the $\beta$ -carboline scaffold (Fig. 1A) [39,40]. The amino acid sequences of these two enzymes exhibit certain similarities (McbB $3 6 . 4 \%$ ; StnK2 37.6%) to mikimopine synthase; like mikimopine synthase, the N-terminus of StnK2 and McbB was weakly homologous to the C-terminus (from amino acid 192 to 310) of the cucumopine synthase [36]. Thus, these $\beta \cdot$ carboline scaffolds can be classified as opines based on their structural characteristics. Notably, while investigating these PSases in bacteria, we discovered that homologous proteins are widely distributed in fungi such as Aspergillus spp. and Beauveria spp., etc. This finding suggests that fungi probably serve as another significant source of opine compounds.

# Results

# Cucumopine synthase-like proteins are widely distributed in fungi

As an OSase, cucumopine synthase is also regarded as a Pictet-Spenglerase (PSase) which catalyzes the synthesis of a large variety of compounds with very high enantioselectivity and significantly enhances molecular complexity in a single step (Fig. S1). Based on the structural elucidation of a representative PSase, McbB, the Protein Families Database defines a class of proteins as the PF18631 family, which shares the cucumopine synthase Cterminal helical bundle domain. The entire family currently consists of 471 proteins derived from various species. Visualizing the 

superfamily of PF18631, through sequence similarity network (SSN) analysis used an alignment score threshold of 80 and a maximum sequence length of 500 residues (Fig. 2). The analysis unveiled a broad diversity in species origins within these families, primarily encompassing 330 bacteria (Rhizobium, Agrobacterium, Streptomyces, Nonomuraea, Collinsella, Clostridium, etc.), 115 fungi (Aspergillus, Penicillium, Fusarium, Beauveria, and Cordyceps, etc.), 9 plants (Cuscuta campestris and Nicotiana glauca), and 12 archaea (Candidatus, Bathyarchaeota) (Fig. S2). It is plausible that plant OSases may have resulted from ancient Agrobacterium transformation events followed by spontaneous regeneration of transformed cells into naturally genetically modified organisms [49]. Surprisingly, these enzymes are also widely distributed in endophytic fungi. The phylogenetic tree analysis of fungal and plantderived proteins in the PF18631 family is shown in Fig. S3. 

However, only a few cases of type III OSases have been reported, for instance, cucumopine synthase and mikimopine synthase in Agrobacterium rhizogenes, as well as McbB and StnK2 catalyzing the synthesis of key intermediates during the corresponding natural products biosynthesis (Fig. 2)[36,38,39]. Collectively, the analysis of SSN not only broadened our understanding of the biosynthetic landscape of opines in the cucumopine family but also laid a foundation to guide future investigations on these small molecules. Furthermore, as endophytic fungi closely interact with plants, they are likely capable of synthesizing opines that have diverse structures and functions different from those produced by bacteria or invertebrates. Therefore, we conducted functional validation experiments on fungus-derived OSases. 

# Fungal opine synthases catalyze the formation of tryptopines

The entomopathogenic fungus Beauveria bassiana can survive and colonize living plant tissue as endophytes [50–52]. Genomic analyses revealed the presence revealed the presence of an opine synthase encoding gene, BBA_06474, in B. bassiana 0062 (CGMCC7.34). Expression analysis showed the transcripts of BBA_06474 were induced by plants (Fig. S4), implying its involvement in biological processes during plant-fungal interactions. 

To determine the functionality of the BBA_06474 gene, the coding region of the BBA_06474 gene was cloned from a B. bassiana 0062 cDNA sample and expressed in E. coli. High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) analysis of the recombinant strains culture extracts showed that two additional peaks were generated in comparison with the control containing the empty vector (Fig. 3B, traces i and v). The product exhibited significant ultraviolet absorption at 219 nm, 287 nm and $3 7 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ , consistent with the characteristic UV absorption profile of $\beta$ -carboline (Fig. S5). The high-resolution mass spectrometry (HR-MS) analysis revealed that the chemical formula of 1 and 2 was deduced to be $\mathsf { C } _ { 1 4 } \mathsf { H } _ { 1 0 } \mathsf { N } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 3 } \left( m \right/$ z 255.0777 $[ \boldsymbol { \mathsf { M } } ^ { \mathrm { ~ + ~ } } \boldsymbol { \mathsf { H } } ] ^ { + }$ , calcd for 255.0764) and $\mathrm { C } _ { 1 3 } \mathrm { H } _ { 1 0 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 } 0$ $( m / z$ 211.0875 $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { H } ] ^ { + }$ , calcd for 211.0866) respectively, which implied that the two compounds were likely tryptophan-derived opines (Figs. S6 and S7). Upscaled fermentation of E. coli BL21/ pColdI/ BBA_06474 followed by isolation yielded analytically pure quantities of compounds l and 2. # Discussion

In this work, we identified a class of OSases that are widespread in fungi. According to our classification of OSases, the fungal 

OSases in this study belong to type III, the most significant feature of these enzymes is that the generated Schiff’s base intermediates undergo Pictet-Spengler cyclization reaction with nucleophilic amino acid side chains to generate complex heterocyclic compounds. We analyzed the cucumopine synthase family proteins and discovered that approximately $2 5 \%$ of the family is present in fungi, which exhibit a close phylogenetic relationship with plants. In vitro enzyme activity characterization confirms that certain enzymes within this protein family possess the ability to catalyze tryptopines synthesis. Our study highlights the previously overlooked significance of the PF18631 family as an essential class of OSases capable of potentially catalyzing more intricate opine structures. The unique structure and species origin of tryptopines suggest their involvement in specialized biological functions. Based on the catalytic mechanism of type III OSase, we can predict that phenylalanine with nucleophilic groups or modified tryptophan and histidine may be synthetic precursors of fungal opine, and there are potentially more novel opines waiting to be discovered.

Therefore, an important question raised by our study is why endophytic fungi synthesize tryptopines, and whether they also contribute to microbial-plant interactions akin to Agrobacterium-derived opines. Structurally, tryptopine A has an indole ring similar to auxin, implying that it has growth-regulating functions similar to auxin. However, unlike auxin, tryptopine A promotes root growth within a very narrow concentration range. In addition, tryptopine A induces only a limited number of auxin response genes, such as SAURs (Table S6i), suggesting distinct mechanisms of action compared to auxin. The KEGG and GO enrichment analyses showed that tryptopine A has a high degree of specificity in its impact on photosynthesis. (Fig. 5A–F, Fig. 6A–D). This is different from the modulation of photosynthesis by other phytohormones such as auxin, cytokinins, gibberellins, and strigolactones, which are essential to regulating photosynthetic rate under optimal conditions both in indirect and direct ways [55]. In addition, although auxin participates in modulating chloroplast development during all parts of the plant growth and development process, accumulating evidence suggests auxin plays a major indirect role in photosynthesis [56–59]. Therefore, our results tend to indicate that tryptopine A is a new potential direct activator of photosynthesis. Considering that the concentration of tryptopine A used was $2 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ $( 1 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ , which is similar to the commonly used concentrations of auxins, cytokinins, abscisic acid, and other plant hormones $\left( 5 - 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } \right)$ , it suggests that tryptopine A probably act as a phytohormone [60]. 

Endophytic fungi that enhance photosynthesis in plants have been extensively reported. For instance, B. bassiana can increase the photosynthetic rates of tobacco plants and promote chlorophyll accumulation [61]. Trichoderma and Epichloё typhina improve the efficiency of photosynthesis in their host orchard grass [62,63]. The fungal-dependent upregulation of photosynthetic efficiency is usually attributed to the acquisition of N and P sinks, resulting in improved nutrition that leads to higher concentrations of chlorophyll, photosynthetic enzymes, ATP, and inorganic P (Pi) in leaves which could stimulate rates of photosynthesis [64–66]. The endophytic fungi-synthesized compound, tryptopine A, plays a pivotal role in establishing a symbiotic relationship with host plants. Specifically, these fungi enhance the photosynthetic capacity of host plants by tryptophan A and utilize the host’s photosynthetic products to support their own growth. Therefore, our findings unveil an innovative mechanism of symbiosis between endogenous fungi and plants. 

Tryptopine A could directly elevate net photosynthesis rate by foliar spraying method, indicating that, in environments with frequent changes in light intensity (such as shifting clouds, changing sun angles, and shading of blades from each other), the application of tryptopine A to plants may enhance the photon absorption and exciton processing for more efficient conversion of light into chemical energy [67]. It also reflects that tryptopine A has the potential for application in agricultural production. To the best of our knowledge, this study represents the first report demonstrating the specific regulation of plant photosynthesis by fungal secondary metabolites. However, further experimental exploration is needed to understand the underlying mechanism of action, including potential targets or receptors. Additionally, understanding the effect of tryptopine A can contribute to a better comprehension of the overall mechanism underlying plant photosynthesis. 

Meeting the increasing demands for global food production in the coming decades requires a boost in agricultural productivity [68]. The potential crop yield relies on the availability of solar energy during the growing season and the efficiency with which crops capture photosynthetically active light, convert it into bio-

mass, and allocate that biomass to harvested products [67,69]. Therefore, enhancing the efficiency and productivity of photosynthesis in crop plants is crucial to address this grand challenge. Given that photosynthesis rate significantly influences crop yield, research on tryptopine A is expected to contribute to improved agricultural productivity. Biosafety is a crucial factor to consider when evaluating the use of metabolites as plant growth regulators. We conducted preliminary cytotoxicity tests on tryptopine A, and the results showed no inhibitory effect on Escherichia coli, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Aspergillus nidulans, Galleria mellonella (insect) at concentrations $1 0 ^ { 2 } – 1 0 ^ { 4 }$ times higher than the actual usage level (Fig. S15). Further comprehensive biosafety evaluation experiments will be completed before large-scale application. 

# Conclusion

We have identified a class of OSases that are widely present in endophytic fungi and responsible for the synthesis of the opinelike compound tryptopines. Tryptopine A, as a new plant growth regulator, elevates the photosynthesis rate and promotes plant growth. Our study expands the role of secondary metabolites in plant-fungal symbioses. Further research is needed to identify potential targets or receptors of tryptopine A and explore its applications in sustainable agriculture production.
