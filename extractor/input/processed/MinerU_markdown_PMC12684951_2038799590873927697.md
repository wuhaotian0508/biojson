# Introduction

Cotton fibers serve as crucial raw materials in the textile industry. The two primary cultivar species of allotetraploid cotton worldwide are G. hirsutum and G. barbadense. G. hirsutum (upland cotton) is known for its high yield and adaptability, albeit with moderate fiber quality, while G. barbadense (sea-island cotton) is highly esteemed for its superior fiber quality and disease resistance [1–3]. The narrow genetic background of upland cotton, coupled with the negative correlations between fiber quality and yield, has presented challenges for the genetic improvement of its fiber quality [4–6]. The comprehensive exploration and utilization of genomic resources from sea-island cotton will help us to understand the fiber quality differences of the two species and to promote the genetic improvement of fiber quality in upland cotton [7]. Recent studies have identified numerous quantitative trait loci (QTL) associated with fiber quality using chromosome segment substitution lines (CSSLs) or backcross inbred lines (BILs) derived from G. hirsutum and G. barbadense [8–12]. However, due to the limited mapping accuracy, very few causal genes have been characterized [13,14]. 

Cotton fibers, which are single-cell trichomes originating from the epidermal layer of ovules, undergo a developmental process encompassing four interconnected stages: initiation, elongation, secondary cell wall (SCW) thickening, and maturation [15,16]. Each development phase of fiber plays a critical role in determining fiber quality, with the elongation stage primarily influencing fiber length (FL), and the SCW thickening stage predominantly determining fiber strength (FS) [17,18]. Fiber strength, a key physical attributor of cotton fibers, is quantifiable using the automated high-volume instrument (HVI) and is a crucial factor in evaluating fiber quality [19]. It represents the fiber’s capacity to withstand a load or force before breaking. Since further improvement in strength is highly desired by the textile industry, researches on the cellular and genetic determinants of fiber strength have become active in recent years. For instance, studies have shown that WLIM1a simultaneously improved fiber strength and fineness by promoting the formation of a thinner and denser secondary wall [20], overexpression of GbEXPATR delayed SCW synthesis and produced longer, finer, and stronger fibers [21], and GhUBX enhanced fiber strength by increasing helix production [13]. In other plant species, the increasement in mechanical strength is mainly achieved by thickening the cell wall or improving the cell wall composition and structure, including microfibril helix and orientation [22–24]. 

The cell wall of plants provides structural support and protection, determining their morphology and mechanical properties [25]. Specifically, SCWs are a composite structure mainly composed of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, primarily deposited in some specialized cell types [26]. It provides strong mechanical support to the cells and the plant body due to its exceptional strength and rigidity [27]. Notably, mature cotton fibers are pre-

dominantly comprised of SCWs, with cellulose accounting for over $90 \%$ of their composition, making them an ideal model for investigating cell wall structure and cellulose synthesis [15,28,29]. Cellulose consists of linear chains of $\beta \cdot$ -1,4-linked glucose units. These chains align to form cellulose microfibrils, which are the main load-bearing fiber of the cell wall and have a stiffness comparable to steel [30,31]. In a previous study, three cellulose synthase genes, GhCesA4, GhCesA7, and GhCesA8 were sharply upregulated during fiber SCW thickening stage [3]; and knock-out of these genes resulted in the impairment of SCW cellulose deposition in fiber cells [32]. However, the precise influence of SCW cellulose biosynthesis and deposition patterns on the strength of cotton fibers remain unclear. Understanding of how compositional and structural alterations of SCW affect the mechanical properties of the fiber cell wall is still limited. 

Indeed, the regulation of SCW formation is governed by a complex transcriptional network that is relatively conserved among different plant species. This network comprises of a group of NAC and MYB master switches along with their downstream transcription factors [33]. In cotton, GhTCP4 activates the biosynthetic pathway of fiber SCWs and interacts with GhHOX3 to regulate the transition of cotton fiber from elongation to thickening in an antagonistic manner [34]. The NAC transcription factor (TF) GhFSN1 has been identified as a positive regulator in the fiber SCW thickening, which bolsters fiber SCW thickening accompanied by a concomitant reduction in fiber length [35]. The MYB TF GhMYB7 directly activates the promoters of GhCesA4, GhCesA7, and GhCesA8 through distinct cis-elements, thereby promoting the biosynthesis of cellulose and SCW thickening [36]. In Arabidopsis, NAC transcription factor AtNTL9 has been found to inhibit cambial activity and the transition to secondary growth in stem vasculature [37]. AtNTL9 is also implicated in the regulation of leaf senescence and phloem callose accumulation [38,39]. However, the role of NTL9 in fiber development and secondary wall transcriptional regulation in cotton remains unexplored. 

In this study, a backcross inbred line (BIL) BS41, derived from interspecific cross between upland cotton E22 and sea-island cotton 3–79, was identified for its superior fiber quality. This study aims to (1) clone the major gene(s) controlling fiber strength in BS41 through QTL mapping, map-based cloning, and gene function verification; (2) characterize the molecular mechanisms influencing the formation of cotton fiber strength in relation to the regulation of secondary wall synthesis and cell wall structure. Thereby, our study will lay the foundation for the genetic improvement of cotton fiber quality. 

# Methods

Plant materials and growth conditions 

BS41 with excellent fiber quality was selected from backcross inbred lines $( \mathsf { B C } _ { 1 } \mathsf { F } _ { 8 } )$ derived from a cross between G. hirsutum cv. 

Emian22 (E22) and G. barbadense acc. 3–79 (3–79) [40]. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants $( \mathsf { E } 2 2 \times \mathsf { B } \mathsf { S } 4 1$ ) were self-pollinated in the summer of 2017 at Huazhong Agricultural University (HZAU) to produce $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ progenies. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population including 1230 individuals was generated for screening recombinants and fine mapping. An $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ family comprising 180 individuals and an $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 4 }$ family containing 254 individuals were generated for QTL fine mapping as well as the allele effect detection. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population was planted in the summer of 2018 at Wuhan $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , 114°E), China. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ population was cultivated in the winter of 2020 at Hainan ( $\mathrm { E } 1 0 9 ^ { \circ }$ , N18°), China. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 4 }$ population was cultivated in the summer of 2021 at Wuhan $( 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 1 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ), China. 

# Determination of yield and fiber quality traits

In the field, ten plants of the parental lines and transgenic lines were planted in each row with $0 . 4 \mathrm { m }$ space. Ten normal ripening cotton bolls in the middle branch were collected for yield and fiber quality measurements. Phenotype for each material was measured over three times. The yield traits primarily include boll weight (BW), seed weight (SW), lint weight (LW), and lint percentage (LP). Mature fiber samples $_ { ( > 8 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } ) }$ were collected to evaluate fiber quality using the High Volume Instrument (HVI) method (HFT9000, Premier, India). The fiber quality traits primarily consist of fiber length (FL), fiber strength (FS), fiber Micronaire (FM), fiber elongation (FE), fiber uniformity (FU), and short fiber index (SF). The fiber quality of the parental lines is shown in Table S1. 

# Development of molecular markers and QTL mapping

A total of 5152 molecular markers on the high-density genetic map were used for polymorphism screening between E22 and BS41 [41]. To screen fiber strength major QTL, the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population containing 1230 individuals was used for bulked segregant analysis (BSA). The genomic DNA of samples was extracted as previously described [42]. DNA samples extracted from leaves of 20 extreme phenotypic individuals (each for high FS phenotype and low FS phenotype) were mixed to construct two BSA pools. Polymorphic markers were further screened between the two parents and the two pools. Sequence alignment was performed between G. hirsutum TM-1 and G. barbadense 3–79 based on the previously released reference genome sequences [43], and InDels with insertion and deletion base pairs equal to or larger than 4 bp were screened out for marker development. The InDel markers developed in this study are listed in Table S2. 

All the polymorphic markers (Table S3) were used for population genotyping. Genetic linkage mapping and QTL analysis were performed using QTL Icimapping 4.0 [44]. The ICIM-ADD method was used for QTL mapping for the fiber quality traits. For major QTL, the logarithm of odds (LOD) threshold was obtained based on a 1000 permutation test. 

# Prediction of candidate genes and comparative sequencing

The annotations of all candidate genes were obtained according to the TM-1 reference genome [43]. The Arabidopsis database (https://www.arabidopsis.org/) was used for sequence alignment and homologous gene detection. The expression profile of candidate genes was obtained from another cotton genome database (https://www.cottongen.org/) [45]. The conserved domain of NTL9 protein was predicted by the Pfam database (http://pfam. xfam.org/) and SMART database (https://smart.embl-heidelberg. de/). 

The CDS of NTL9 was amplified using cDNA of E22, BS41, and 3– 79. Amplified fragments of CDS were cloned into pGEM-T Easy vector and subsequently transformed into E. coli TOP10 strain for 

sequence analysis. The final sequences were analyzed by DNAMAN software (https://www.lynnon.com/pc/framepc.html). 

Promoter amplification was performed using genomic DNA from E22 and BS41. The 1100-bp promoter sequence of NTL9E22 and $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ was constructed into the pGreen II 0800-LUC vector and then sequenced. 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR

All samples for RNA extraction were ground into powder with a pestle in liquid nitrogen. For gene expression assays, total RNA was extracted using a DP441 RNAprep Pure Plant Plus Kit (Polysaccharides & Polyphenolics-rich) (TIANGEN Biotech, Beijing, China). DNase I (NEB) was used to remove the remaining genomic DNA in RNA, and M−MLV (Promega) was used for reverse transcription. RT-qPCR was performed using qPCR SYBR Green Mix (Applied Biosystems) and followed the same steps as previously described [46]. GhUBQ7 (GenBank accession No. DQ116441) was used as the internal reference to standardize and calculate gene expression levels [47]. 

# Vector construction and cotton transformation

In the cotton transgenic experiment, two types of overexpression vectors (35S::NTL9E22 and exp::NTL9BS41) and one CRISPR vector for GhNTL9 were constructed. Specifically, the CDS of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathbb { E } 2 2 }$ was inserted downstream of the CaMV35S promoter in the pk2GW7 vector. The CDS of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ was inserted downstream of the GbEXPA2 promoter in a fiber-specific expression vector, which is modified from the pGWB407 vector and provided by Li et al., [21]. For the construction of CRISPR/Cas9 vector, sgRNAs were designed using the CRISPR-P web tool [48]. A single sgRNA that specifically targeting GhNTL9-A11 rather than GhNTL9-D11 was designed and then ligated to the pRGEB32-GhU6.7-NPT II vector as described previously [49]. These three vectors were individually transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain (GV3101). The upland cotton accession Jin668 was used for genetic transformation experiments and served as a wild-type control [50]. All the transgenic lines were cultivated in the summer of 2023 at Huazhong Agricultural University, Wuhan, China. 

To detect CRISPR editing site, transgenic plants of CRISPR lines were initially examined for the presence of both the transgene fragment and Cas9, using two primer sets (u6-7 and inf-NTL as, cas_F and cas_R). And the target fragments of the editing site were amplified using a specific primer set (CR NTL-Target_F and CR NTL-Target_R) and then cloned into the pGEM-T Easy vector for sequencing. 

# Microscopic analysis

In the measurement of fiber cell wall thickness, fibers from both the parents and transgenic lines at 30 days post-anthesis (DPA) were used to prepare paraffin sections as described previously [21]. Then, the $8 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ paraffin sections were dewaxed and stained with safranin O-fast green. Fiber transverse sections were imaged using a differential interference contrast microscope (Zeiss, HAL 100) equipped with a CCD camera. Cell wall thickness was measured using ImageJ software. Three biological replicates were set up for each material, and at least 50 fibers were measured for each replicate. 

In the observation of fiber cell wall surface structure, cotton samples with a normal shape, located in the middle of the plants, were collected and naturally air-dried for two days to ensure thorough drying. Then the natural mature fibers were treated with gold spray and examined by a biological scanning electron microscope (JSM-6390LV) under $4 0 0 \times$ and $3 5 0 0 \times$ magnification. In the statis-

tical analysis of spiral numbers, due to random left-handed or right-handed twisting or the absence of twisting in the fibers, only the spiral portions of the fibers were selected for measuring spiral numbers. For the statistical analysis of the cellulose microfibril density, the number of microfibers within a $1 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ length perpendicular to the fiber was randomly measured three times for each replicate. Three biological replicates were selected for each material. 

# RNA-seq analysis

For the RNA-seq analysis of the OE2 line and Jin668, three or more cotton bolls grown under the same conditions in a greenhouse were selected at 20 DPA. The fibers were then collected and immediately immersed in liquid nitrogen for mixed grinding. Total RNA was extracted using a DP441 RNAprep Pure Plant Plus Kit (Polysaccharides & Polyphenolics-rich) (TIANGEN Biotech, Beijing, China). The extracted RNA was used to construct RNA-seq libraries, and three replicates were sequenced for each sample using the Illumina HiSeq 2000 system. Sequences are available on the Sequence Read Archive https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sra, at accession PRJNA1157997. The clean RNA-seq reads were mapped to the TM-1 reference genome [43] by HISAT version 2.0 [51]. The transcripts per kilobase million (TPM) value [52] of each gene was calculated by Cufflinks (v2.2.1) software [53]. DEseq2 was used to identify DEGs. DEGs were determined using the threshold of $P \leq 0 . 0 5$ and |log2FoldChange| $> 0 . 5 8 4 9$ (corresponding to an expression difference of more than 1.5-fold). The heat map of gene expression was made by TBtools software [54]. 

The transcriptome data of DEGs in TM-1 and Hai7124 (H7124) were obtained from cotton database (http://yanglab.hzau.edu.cn/ CottonMD/) [55]. 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay

In the assessment of the transcriptional activation capacity of GhNTL9, the full-length sequence of GhNTL9 (BD-NTL9), the Nterminal sequence of GhNTL9 (N-NTL9), and the C-terminal sequence of GhNTL9 (C-NTL9) were cloned into the pGBKT7 vector and then transferred into the Y2HGold yeast strain. These Y2H strains were cultured on SD-T/X-a-gal solid agar plates for 3 days at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to detect its autoactivation (based on colony color). 

The cDNA libraries of 10 DPA and 20 DPA cotton fibers were constructed into the pGADT7 vector and transformed into the yeast strain Y187. Since BD-NTL9 and C-NTL9 are self-activating, the N-NTL9 was used as bait to screen cDNA library and identify the interaction genes. Yeast two-hybrid screening was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Clontech, PT1172-1, PT4084-1). The genes captured by the bait were sequenced and point-by-point verified to confirm the initial interaction. The positive and negative controls used in this research were in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions (Clontech, PT4084-1). 

# Subcellular localization and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay

The full-length sequence of GhNTL9 was constructed into the GFP-tagged pMDC-43 vector, and the recombination construct was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Transient expression was performed in tobacco epidermal cells. The GFP fluorescence was visualized by confocal microscopy (Olympus FV1200) at 2 days following Agrobacterium transfection. For the BiFC assay, the full-length sequence of GhNTL9 (without the stop codon) was constructed into the pXY104 vector, and the fulllength sequences of GhMYB6, GhNFYC2, GhNFYC4, and GhDPB were constructed into the pXY106 vector. The recombinant constructs 

were also transformed into GV3101 to perform transient expression in tobacco epidermal cells. The YFP fluorescence was examined by confocal microscopy (Olympus FV1200) at 2 days following Agrobacterium transfection. 

# Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay

The 1100 bp promoter sequence of GhNTL9 was cloned into the pHIS2 vector and transformed into the yeast strain Y2H. The Y2H yeast convertor of pHIS2-pNTL9 was cultured on SD-TH solid agar plates with different 3-AT concentrations (50, 75, 100, 125, 150, $1 7 5 \mathrm { m M }$ ) at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days to ascertain the optimal concentration of 3-AT that effectively inhibits the expression of pHIS2-pNTL9. A fiber cDNA library has already been constructed and transformed to yeast strain Y187. Then, the Y2H yeast convertor of pHIS2- pNTL9 was employed as bait to screen the fiber cDNA library at SD-TLH solid agar plates containing 100 mM 3-AT for the detection of upstream regulatory genes. The positive clones were amplified from yeast cells for sequencing. To verify positive clones, these clones were diluted with sterile water and cultured on SD-TLH solid agar plates with 100 mM 3-AT at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days. The pGADT7-empty vector was transformed into yeast strain Y187 and used as a negative control. 

# Phylogenetic analysis, nucleotide diversity and haplotype analysis

Genome data of 3 diploid species $( \mathsf { A } _ { 1 } , \mathsf { A } _ { 2 }$ , and ${ \sf D } _ { 5 }$ ) and 7 allotetraploid species $[ ( \mathsf { A D } ) _ { 1 }$ to $( \mathsf { A D } ) _ { 7 } ]$ obtained from cotton database were used to retrieve NTL9 homologous genes [45]. The amino acid sequences of NTL9 were aligned using the CLUSTALW program. The Gossypium phylogenetic tree was constructed using the MAGA7.0 software and neighbor-joining method with 1000 bootstrap replicates. 

The genomic sequences and natural variation sites of NTL9 in 4180 cotton accessions were obtained from the cotton database (http://yanglab.hzau.edu.cn/CottonMD/) [55], Among them, 3741 G. hirsutum and 387 G. barbadense cotton accessions were used for frequency statistics of the 24-bp InDel variation types respectively. In addition, another 336 G. barbadense cotton accessions were used for haplotype and phenotypic analysis of the 24-bp InDel, genomic sequences and phenotypic data for fiber strength of these accessions were obtained from open-access articles [56]. The 503 upland cotton materials used for molecular marker amplification were numbered ZY1-ZY523 [5].

# Results

# A major QTL is mapped for fiber strength in BS41

An interspecific BIL population derived from G. hirsutum cv. E22 and G. barbadense acc. 3–79 was constructed in our previous research [40]. An inbred line named BS41 was identified in this BIL population for its superior fiber quality. BS41 produces higher-quality fiber similar to 3–79, with $1 0 \%$ longer fiber length and $30 \%$ higher fiber strength compared with E22 (Fig. 1a-c; Table S1). Specifically, the fiber strength of BS41 reached to $3 3 . 9 3 \pm 0 . 8 7 \mathrm { c N } _ { i }$ /tex, while that of E22 was $2 6 . 2 0 \pm 0 . 1 4 \ : \mathrm { c N / t e x }$ . 

To identify the genetic factors controlling fiber strength, an $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population $\mathbf { \tilde { n } } = 1 2 3 0 $ ) using E22 and BS41 as parents was generated to perform QTL mapping. Fiber strength in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population 

showed continuous variation, exhibiting the characteristics of quantitative traits inheritance (Fig. S1a). Polymorphic marker screening and BSA were conducted between the two parental lines and the two pools (low strength pool and high strength pool). A total of 5152 markers on the interspecific map constructed in our laboratory [41] were used for polymorphism screening between the parental lines E22 and BS41. Among them, 359 showed polymorphism, with the maximum number of polymorphic markers on chromosome 11. After BSA, markers on chromosome 11 such as MUSS281, NAU3008, MON_SHIN1579, MON_CGR6766, HAU3031, and HAU0618 displayed polymorphism between the two extreme pools (Fig. S1b). Subsequently, 106 InDel markers were designed within the candidate regions on chromosome 11 (Table S2). Ultimately, a total of 37 polymorphic markers (16 markers from the high-density genetic linkage map and 21 InDel markers specifically developed for this study) were utilized for genetic linkage map constructing and QTL mapping (Table S3). 

A major QTL for fiber strength was identified as qFS-A11-1, with a logarithm of odds (LOD) score of 118.18, explaining $1 3 . 2 \%$ of the 

phenotypic variation (Fig. S1c, Table S4). This QTL was located between the markers FSID-19 and HAU3356, spanning approximately 51-kb on chromosome 11. To narrow the qFS-A11-1 interval, heterozygous recombinants around qFS-A11-1 were selected to generate $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ and $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 4 }$ populations. QTL mapping of the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 3 }$ population confirmed that qFS-A11-1 was a stable QTL for fiber strength (Fig. S1d-e; Table S4). Subsequently, through genotypic and phenotypic analyses of recombinants in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 : 4 }$ population, qFS-A11-1 was finally delimited to a 14.6-kb region flanked by markers FSID-45 and FSID-33 (Fig. 1d-e). It is worth mentioning that a gene marker, designated as NTL9-24, was co-segregation with the fiber strength. The BS41 allele exhibited an approximate increase of 4.4 cN/tex in fiber strength compared with the E22 allele (Fig. S1f).

# NTL9 enhances fiber strength

Transgenic experiments were conducted to validate the function of NTL9 and to explore the effect of coding sequences of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathbb { E } 2 2 }$ (GhNTL9) and $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ (GbNTL9) on fiber strength. Specifically, two overexpression vectors were constructed: 35S::NTL9E22 driven by the 35S promoter, and exp::NTL9BS41 driven by the fiber-specific promoter proGbEXPA2. Additionally, a CRISPR vector for GhNTL9 were utilized. These vectors were then transformed into the cotton accession Jin668 whose endogenous gene of NTL9 is same as E22. Then, all three kinds of transgenic materials were planted in the same environment at Huazhong Agricultural University in the summer of 2023. 

Three independent transgenic lines of $3 5 S { : : } N T L 9 ^ { \mathsf { E } 2 2 }$ (E170-9, E170-10, and E170-12) were obtained and used for further analysis. RT-qPCR was conducted on the 20 DPA fibers, revealing a significant upregulation of NTL9 expression in the transgenic lines compared with the wild type Jin668 (Fig. 2a). Subsequently, fiber quality of mature fibers was measured using High Volume Instrument (HVI), and the fiber strength of overexpression lines (31.50 $\pm \ : \ : 1 . 1 8 \ : \ : \mathrm { \ c N } /$ tex for E170-9, $3 4 . 4 7 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 4 7$ cN/tex for E170-10, 31.43 ± 0.40 cN/tex for E170-12) was stronger than that of Jin668 $( 2 9 . 2 2 \pm 0 . 4 9 \mathrm { \ c N / t e x } )$ (Fig. 2b, Table S6). However, fiber length was found to be shorter in the transgenic lines compared with Jin668 (Fig. 2c-d). These results suggest that the overexpression of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { E } 2 2 }$ driven by the 35S promoter can enhance fiber strength, although it has a negative impact on fiber length. 

The CDS of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ was overexpressed using the fiber-specific promoter proGbEXPA2. RT-qPCR showed that the expression levels of NTL9 were upregulated by approximately 2 to 4 folds in three independent transgenic lines OE1, OE2, and OE3 (Fig. 2e). The fiber strength of OE lines $\langle 3 2 . 1 3 \pm 1 . 7 2 \mathrm { c N } _ { \prime }$ /tex for OE1, $3 1 . 1 0 \pm 0 . 4 4 \mathrm { c N } / \cdot$ - tex for OE2, $3 3 . 3 4 \pm 1 . 6 2 \mathrm { c N }$ /tex for OE3) was remarkably increased compared with wild type Jin668 (Fig. 2f; Table S6). For fiber length, no apparent differences were observed between the OE lines and Jin668 (Fig. 2g-h), suggesting that the specific overexpression of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ in fibers exerts a beneficial influence on fiber strength without affecting fiber length. 

In addition, CRISPR/Cas9 technology was used to create knockout mutants by introducing targeted mutations in the first exon of GhNTL9, and five independent transgenic plants were obtained. However, only two editing events were detected: five-base deletions (N1, N3, N4, and N5) and three-base deletions (N2) (Fig. 2i). Three CRISPR lines, N1, N2, and N5 were selected for expression analysis and phenotypic assessment. The expression levels of NTL9 in CRISPR lines were downregulated (Fig. 2j). But there were no significant differences in fiber quality traits between the CRISPR lines and Jin668, except for line N5, which exhibited a decrease in fiber length (Fig. 2k-l; Table S6). Thus, knocking out of GhNTL9 did not alter fiber strength. 

Furthermore, the morphological characteristics of transgenic lines were investigated. The 35S::NTL9E22 overexpression lines exhibited vigorous vegetative growth, characterized by an abundant profusion of verdant leaves (Fig. S3a-b). Additionally, there was a significant reduction in yield-related traits in 35S::NTL9E22 overexpression lines. In contrast, no significant changes in plant morphology and yield traits were observed between the exp:: $N T L \bar { g } ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ fiber-specific overexpression lines and Jin668 (Table S7). Taken together, our results indicate that both $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { E } 2 2 }$ and NTL9BS41 act as positive regulators of fiber strength, and specific overexpression of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ in fibers enhances fiber strength without compromising yield or other fiber quality traits. 

# NTL9 enhances fiber strength by altering the deposition pattern of cellulose microfibrils

Fiber strength is a complex trait that is influenced not only by the secondary wall thickness but also by the morphological structure of cotton fiber cells. To gain deeper insights into the cellular morphological factors contributing to fiber strength, the morphology and thickness of 30 DPA cell wall in the parental lines were analyzed. Fiber cross-sections of E22 exhibited a round shape, while those of BS41 and 3–79 were slightly compressed and flattened (Fig. 3a). E22 had a smaller cavity and thicker cell walls (6. $3 2 \pm 0 . 7 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } )$ ) compared with BS41 and 3–79 $( 3 . 8 5 \pm 0 . 8 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ and $3 . 5 0 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 5 4 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , respectively) (Fig. 3d), indicating that increased cell wall thickness does not necessarily correlate with higher fiber strength. Moreover, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed to observe the surface structure of mature cotton fibers. The twisting degree in the spiral part of the fiber was largely increased in BS41 and 3–79 compared with E22 (Fig. 3b and e). NTL9 interacts with MYB6 and functions as a downstream gene in the ethylene signaling pathway 

NTL9 is a member of the NAC transcription factor gene family. Protein structure prediction of NTL9 showed a NAM domain in the N terminal and a transmembrane domain in the C terminal (Fig. 5a). Transient expression of NTL9-GFP in N. benthamiana leaves showed that NTL9 was subcellular localized in the nucleus and on the endoplasmic reticulum (Fig. S8a). 

To determine whether NTL9 is a transcriptional activator, BD-NTL9, N-NTL9 and C-NTL9 (full-length, N terminal, and C terminal of NTL9) constructs were transferred into yeast strain Y2H, respectively (Fig. 5b). As shown in Fig. 5c, yeast transformants containing BD-NTL9 or C-NTL9 could hydrolyze X-a-Gal to produce blue products, indicating that the NTL9 protein has transcriptional activation activity, and its trans-activation ability is mainly determined by the C-terminal rather than the N-terminal. 

To explore the regulatory mechanism of NTL9, a Y2H assay was conducted using N-NTL9 yeast transformants. Four interacting genes were identified: GhNFYC2 (Ghir_A11G031910, Nuclear transcription factor Y subunit C-2), GhMYB6 (Ghir_D11G017140, Transcription repressor MYB6), GhDPB (Ghir_A09G015540, Transcription factor-like protein DPB), and GhNFYC4 (Ghir_A01G009100, Nuclear transcription factor Y subunit C-4) (Fig. 5d). The expression pattern of these four interacting genes was further investigated in E22, with GhMYB6 and GhDPB showing higher expression during the SCW thickening stage (Fig. S8b). However, only the interaction between NTL9 and MYB6 was confirmed by BiFC experiment (Fig. 5e). Moreover, the expression pattern of GhMYB6 was examined in parental lines, and a striking contrast emerged, as GhMYB6 expression displayed an obvious decrease in BS41 during the fiber secondary wall thickening stage (15–30 DPA), while NTL9 expression exhibited an opposite trend (Fig. S1h, Fig. 5f). This contrasting pattern indicated that GhMYB6 may function as a negative regulator on fiber strength. 

To screen the upstream target genes of NTL9, a 1100-bp fragment of GhNTL9 promoter was cloned into the pHIS2 vector for Y1H assay. The pHIS2-pNTL9 construct was transformed into yeast strain Y2H, and the transcriptional activation activity of pHIS2- pNTL9 construct was inhibited at a concentration of 100 mM 3- amino-1,2,4-triazole (3-AT) (Fig. S9). After screening the cotton fiber cDNA library, nine transcription factors, including GhBHLH153, GhERF7, GhGATA15, GhCO3, GhERF12, GhBZIP44, GhERF9, GhGATA8, and GhERF5 were identified as binding to the GhNTL9 promoter (Fig. 5g, Table S11). Among these, four ethylene-responsive transcription factors (GhERF7, GhERF12, GhERF9, and GhERF5) were noteworthy. These findings indicate that NTL9 may function as a downstream gene in the ethylene signaling pathway, thereby establishing a potential connection between ethylene signaling and fiber strength. 

The 24-bp insertion-type InDel unique to G. barbadense positively influences fiber strength 

To trace the evolution of the NTL9 homologous genes across diverse cotton species, a phylogenetic analysis was conducted on three diploid ancestors (A1, A2, and ${ \sf D } _ { 5 }$ ) and seven allotetraploid cotton species $[ ( \mathsf { A D } ) _ { 1 }$ to $( \mathsf { A D } ) _ { 7 } ]$ . The clustering analysis revealed that NTL9 on chromosome A11 and D11 originated independently from the A genomes (G. herbaceum and G. arboreum) and the D genome (G. raimondii), respectively (Fig. S10a). Subsequently, variations at three sites within the CDS of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathbb { E } 2 2 }$ and $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ were investigated across diverse cotton species and sub-genomes. 

Among these, the T haplotype SNP (position $\textbf { + 1 3 9 }$ ) and the 12- bp deletion-type InDel (position $+ \ 1 3 5 6 ^ { \cdot }$ ) were exclusively present in G. hirsutum or its related species G. stephensii. In contrast, the 24-bp insertion-type InDel (position $+ 5 8 3$ ), which is derived from the ancestral A genome, was continuously retained in G. barbadense, G. darwinii, and G. mustelinum (Fig. S10b). 

The natural variation of NTL9 was further explored using genomic data of 4180 cotton germplasms in a cotton database. A total of 11 SNPs and the 24-bp InDel were identified in the gene body (Table S12). Subsequently, haplotype detection of the 24-bp InDel in accessions of G. hirsutum $( G h )$ and G. barbadense $( G b )$ revealed that the majority $( 8 9 . 7 \ \% )$ of $G b$ accessions harbored Hap1 haplotype (24-bp insertion), while the majority $( 9 9 . 6 \% )$ of Gh accessions harbored Hap2 haplotype (24-bp deletion) (Fig. S10c). This 24-bp InDel is highly independent and conserved during the breeding process of G. barbadense and G. hirsutum, consistently preserving their respective allelic types. To assess the impact of the 24-bp InDel on cotton fiber strength variation, haplotype and phenotypic analysis was conducted using $3 3 6 ~ G$ . barbadense accessions. The Hap1 haplotype $( \boldsymbol { \mathrm { n } } = 3 3 5 ^ { \cdot }$ ) exhibited a 3.44 cN/tex improvement in fiber strength relative to the Hap2 $\left( \mathrm { n } = 1 \right)$ , suggesting a potential correlation between the 24-bp InDel and fiber strength (Fig. S10d). The 24-bp insertion-type (Gb haplotype) positively contributes to fiber strength, contrasting with the 24-bp deletion-type (Gh haplotype). 

In addition, molecular marker (namely NTL9-24) targeting the 24-bp InDel was developed (Table S2). The successful amplification of this 24-bp InDel across 503 G. hirsutum accessions provided empirical evidence that the majority of these upland cottons indeed harbored the Hap2 haplotype (Fig. S11). This molecular marker can effectively differentiate Gb and Gh genotypes and can be utilized as a genetic marker for the fiber strength gene NTL9, thus facilitating molecular marker-assisted selection and breeding for fiber strength. 

# Discussion

# qFSA11-1 is a stable major QTL for fiber strength

Cotton fiber quality improvement is a major breeding objective in cotton. However, Chinese upland cotton cultivars are mainly derived from limited foreign germplasm sources, resulting in a narrow genetic base and low diversity [57]. Modern upland cotton germplasm harbors fewer favorable alleles for fiber quality, many of which remain underutilized [6]. And numerous studies have confirmed a strong negative correlation between fiber quality and yield traits, particularly between fiber strength and lint yield [12], which constraints restrict simultaneous improvement of fiber quality and yield traits. In this study, the inter-specific cross between G. hirsutum cv. E22 and G. barbadense acc. 3–79 produced BS41 with superior fiber quality (Fig. 1a-c, Table S1). Subsequently, map-based cloning and functional analysis of fiber strength QTL were conducted using E22 and BS41 as parents. The objective was to exploit genetic resources from G. barbadense, ultimately promoting the improvement of fiber quality in upland cotton.

As a typical quantitative trait, its phenotype is also significantly influenced by the environment [58–60]. In recent decades, numerous QTL associated with key agronomic traits of cotton have been identified across various genetic populations [5,8,61,62]. Both NTL9E22 and NTL9BS41 positively regulate fiber strength 

NAC transcription factor NTL9 was identified as the candidate gene by expression analyses (Fig. 1g-j). NAC TFs are plant-specific transcription factors that are widely involved in plant growth and development, cell wall building, biological and abiotic stress response [69]. Gene family analysis in upland cotton identified 283 NAC TFs, among which GhNAC106 (identical to NTL9) exhibited elevated expression during the transition from fiber elongation to SCW thickening [70], implying a potential role for NTL9 in SCW formation. 

Meanwhile, there were also 1 SNP and 2 InDels between the two parents in the CDS region of NTL9 (Fig. 1k). Both 35S::NTL9E22 and exp:: $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ overexpression lines increased fiber strength, whereas knocking out GhNTL9 had minimal effect on fiber strength (Fig. 2b, f, and k; Table S6), suggesting that two genotypes of NTL9 can positively regulate fiber strength, and GhNTL9 may have functional redundancy. In fact, our attempts to overexpress $\dot { N T I } 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ using the 35S promoter were unsuccessful, because the $\mathrm { T } _ { 0 }$ generation of 35S:: $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ transgenic plants did not develop normal bolls, and no seeds were produced. Given that $3 5 S { : : } N T L 9 ^ { \mathsf { E } 2 2 }$ led to smaller bolls, shorter fibers, and delayed leaf senescence (Fig. 2c-d; Fig. S3a-c; Table S7), it is suspected that constitutive expression of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ in upland cotton may strongly disrupt normal growth and development, which may be also a reason that $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ is only exist in G. barbadense because sea-island cottons show vigorous vegetative growth in Wuhan, Hubei, China. 

By analyzing natural variations in NTL9, a 24-bp InDel was identified in the second exon, showing high conservation and independence between G. barbadense and G. hirsutum (Fig. S10b-c). This 24- bp insertion mutation (Gb haplotype) positively influences fiber strength (Fig. S10d). During the historical breeding process, transferring this favorable allele from G. barbadense into G. hirsutum through natural interspecific hybridization has proven challenging. The BIL line BS41 exhibits high fiber quality but suffers from reduced yield, smaller boll size, lower lint yield, and shorter plant height [71]. It is evident that introgression fragments negatively impact yield and other traits due to linkage drag, despite their contribution to excellent fiber quality. Therefore, the development of molecular markers linked to NTL9 gene is necessary. A molecular marker named NTL9-24 for the 24-bp InDel was developed (Fig. S11; Table S2), which can act as a genetic marker specifically targeting the GbNTL9 gene to enhance fiber strength in upland cotton. 

$N T L 9 ^ { E 2 2 }$ and $N T L 9 ^ { B S 4 1 }$ enhance fiber strength by promoting the dense accumulation and organization of cellulose microfibrils 

The secondary wall provides plants with shape, support, and protection. Numerous studies have shown that increasing cell wall thickness and optimizing its composition and structure are effective ways to enhance plant mechanical properties [24,72,73]. How-

ever, the factors influencing cotton fiber strength are complex, and the relationship between fiber strength and cell wall thickness, composition, and structure remains unclear. Some researches indicated that fiber strength may increase with thicker cell walls, while other studies found that fiber strength can enhance through optimization of cell wall structure, even with thinner walls [13,20,21,74,75]. These findings imply that secondary wall thickness may be not always positively correlated with fiber strength. Reports suggest that G. barbadense, with stronger fibers, has thinner fiber cell walls. Whereas G. arboreum and G. hirsutum, with moderate fiber strength, have thicker fiber cell walls [28], which is consistent with our observations (Fig. 3a and d). Additionally, we noticed that the fiber surfaces of 3–79 and BS41 with ultrahigh strength fibers are very smooth, and the packing of cellulose microfibrils is very tight, whereas the fiber surface of E22 is relatively rough, and the arrangement of cellulose microfibrils is more dispersed (Fig. 3a-e). In the transgenic plants, despite no significant change in cell wall thickness (Fig. S4a-b), the accumulation and arrangement of cellulose microfibrils on the fiber surface became more uniform, neat, and compact both in 35S::NTL9E22 and exp:: $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ overexpression lines (Fig. 3g-j, Fig. S4c). 

In practical textile production, fibers exhibiting both fineness and strength are preferred. Coarser fibers may exhibit enhanced tensile properties, but their reduced natural twisting and weaker inter-fiber bonding hinder the formation of yarn strength; conversely, finer fibers, though with slightly lower tensile strength, exhibit stronger inter-fiber cohesion. For yarns of the same count, a finer fiber leads to a higher fiber density per unit cross-sectional area, thereby facilitating greater yarn strength. Taken together, it is supported that controlling fiber fineness, optimizing fiber cell wall structure, and improving cell wall mechanical properties are effective strategies for enhancing fiber strength.

# Conclusion

Our study identified NTL9 as a critical gene for enhancing fiber strength. Specific overexpression of $N T L 9 ^ { \mathrm { B S 4 1 } }$ in fibers enhanced fiber strength without compromising yield and other fiber quality traits. The increase in fiber strength is achieved by inhibiting the premature expression of SCW-related genes, thereby delaying the cellulose deposition and modifying the organization and arrangement of cellulose microfibers on the cell wall. Additionally, a molecular marker NTL9-24 specially developed from GbNTL9 can be utilized in marker-assisted selection breeding. Our study reveals a promising gene for improving fiber quality in upland cotton, and provides new insights into the genetic regulation of fiber strength.
