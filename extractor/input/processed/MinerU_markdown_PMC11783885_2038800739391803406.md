# ABSTRACT

Flavonoids, the largest class of polyphenols, exhibit substantial structural and functional diversity, yet their evolutionary diversification and specialized functions remain largely unexplored. The genus Scutellaria is notable for its rich flavonoid diversity, particularly of 6/8-hydroxylated variants biosynthesized by the cytochrome P450 subfamily CYP82D. Our study analyzes metabolic differences between Scutellaria baicalensis and Scutellaria barbata, and the results suggest that CYP82Ds have acquired a broad range of catalytic functions over their evolution. By integrating analyses of metabolic networks and gene evolution across 22 Scutellaria species, we rapidly identified 261 flavonoids and delineated five clades of CYP82Ds associated with various catalytic functions. This approach revealed a unique catalytic mode for 6/8-hydroxylation of flavanone substrates and the first instance of 7-O-demethylation of flavonoid substrates catalyzed by a cytochrome P450. Ancestral sequence reconstruction and functional validation demonstrated that gradual neofunctionalization of CYP82Ds has driven the chemical diversity of flavonoids in the genus Scutellaria throughout its evolutionary history. These findings enhance our understanding of flavonoid diversity, reveal the intricate roles of CYP82Ds in Scutellaria species, and highlight the extensive catalytic versatility of cytochrome P450 members within plant taxa.

# INTRODUCTION

The natural world is teeming with diverse natural products that offer a rich opportunity for discovery of novel pharmaceuticals, and many of these compounds may have multiple activities and structural variations (Wurtzel and Kutchan, 2016). This structural diversity is a consequence of the diversification of 

biosynthetic pathways throughout evolution, driven by neutral evolution, drift, and selection (Hernandez Lozada et al., 2022;

Dadras et al., 2023; Meteignier et al., 2023). Plant metabolic systems are both complex and diverse, offering a unique arena for studying the evolutionary mechanisms and trajectories of complex metabolic features (Mint Evolutionary Genomics Consortium, 2018). Interactions between bioactive constituents in plants, chemical evolution, and taxon-specific functional genes contribute to the distinct metabolic profiles observed among species (Lichman et al., 2020b; Hu et al., 2023). A focus on plant taxa allows for exploration of the relationships between metabolic profiles and essential biosynthesis genes, enabling rapid investigation of both natural product diversity and gene functionality (Lichman et al., 2020a). 

Flavonoids are a diverse category of plant-specialized metabolites, comprising over 9000 distinct structures (Williams and Grayer, 2004; Yonekura-Sakakibara et al., 2019). The broad distribution of flavonoids among embryophytes, excluding hornworts, suggests convergent evolution in plants that conferred similar defense and tolerance traits against diverse environmental stresses (Bowman et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2020). In plant taxa, ancestral proteins often diverged over time, developing distinct catalytic functions and substrate specificities to generate specialized metabolites. These metabolites, which may be producedina lineage-specific manner, makeasignificant contribution to plant survival and reproduction (Rieseberg et al., 2023). Their genesis is shaped by the evolutionary neofunctionalization or defunctionalization of genes within biosynthetic pathways, processes that have been crucial for the diversification of plant taxa (Weng, 2014). However, most studies of plant metabolic pathways have predominantly centered on individual species, providing only a superficial exploration of flavonoid diversity, particularly in downstream biosynthetic pathways (Yonekura-Sakakibara et al., 2019; Wen et al., 2020). This underscores the need to deepen our understanding of the mechanisms that underlie the evolution of the diverse flavonoids observed among modern plant taxa. 

Flavonoid biosynthesis begins with phenylalanine and is initially catalyzed by core enzymes of the general phenylpropanoid pathway such as phenylalanine ammonia lyase, cinnamate 4-hydroxylase, and 4-coumarate CoA ligase, yielding precursors for the major phenolic secondary metabolites of land plants (Wen et al., 2020). During evolution, the duplication of plant biosynthesis genes often originated from primordial metabolism genes, and this is particularly evident for genes encoding enzymes such as chalcone synthase (CHS) and chalcone isomerase (CHI), which initiate flavonoid biosynthesis (Yonekura-Sakakibara et al., 2019). However, an analysis of six genes related to anthocyanin biosynthesis indicated slower evolutionary rates for upstream genes than for downstream genes (Rausher et al., 1999). This suggests that enzymes responsible for the final steps of metabolite biosynthesis may play a more integral role in determining the diversity and abundance of metabolites than early pathway enzymes. Hydroxylation, which is a common downstream reaction in flavonoid biosynthetic pathways, serves as a foundation for generating diverse substitutions such as Omethylation and O-glycosylation (Peng et al., 2017). Particularly noteworthy is the occurrence of 6/8-hydroxylation, as reported in limited species of genera such as Scutellaria (Zhao et al., 2018a; Zheng et al., 2023), Lotus (Hiraga et al., 2021), Ocimum (Berim and Gang, 2013; Berim et al., 2014), and Erigeron (Gao et al., 

2022). The enzymatic functions associated with the cytochrome P450 subfamily CYP82D center on the C-6/8 hydroxylation of flavone substrates, producing flavonoids with specialized functions such as baicalein, norwogonin, and scutellarein in Scutellaria baicalensis Georgi and Scutellaria barbata D. Don (Zhao et al., 2018a; Xu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022). Thus, an understanding of the evolutionary mechanisms behind the lineage-specific formation of flavonoids in Scutellaria is important, given the core functions of the CYP82D subfamily. In addition, exploring the dynamics of CYP82D subfamily members during the evolution of the entire plant CYP family could provide insights into the interplay between functional divergence and genome evolution of CYP82Ds. 

Scutellaria is the second-largest genus of the family Lamiaceae, comprising approximately 500 species globally (Zhao et al., 2020; Salimov et al., 2021). Among these are traditional medicinal herbs such as S. baicalensis, S. barbata, and Scutellaria lateriflora L. (Shen et al., 2021), with S. baicalensis roots and whole S. barbata plants having been documented in the Chinese Pharmacopeia (Yang et al., 2022). Lineage-specific flavonoids such as baicalein, wogonin, norwogonin, scutellarein, and their glycosides are the most abundant components of Scutellaria, possessing robust antibacterial (Rajendran et al., 2016), antiviral (Liu et al., 2021), antioxidant (Tu et al., 2016), anticancer (Xu et al., 2021), hepatoprotective (Zhou et al., 2018), and neuroprotective properties (Zhao et al., 2018b). Expression of known genes of the flavone biosynthetic pathway leads to the production of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavone apigenin, which is further hydroxylated and glycosylated to generate scutellarein in the aerial parts of S. baicalensis (Zhao et al., 2016). However, specialized root-specific $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones accumulate in large quantities in the roots of S. baicalensis and are synthesized through a relatively recently evolved pathway (Zhao et al., 2016, 2019). Whether these metabolic differences between plant tissues represent isolated evolutionary events or consistent variations due to lineage differentiation within the genus remains uncertain. Given the existence of lineagespecific and tissue-specific flavonoids, the genus Scutellaria offers a useful model in which to examine the lineage-specific diversity of flavonoid metabolism in plants. 

With recent advances in omics technologies, tracing the evolutionary origin, distribution, and composition of metabolites on a lineage scale has become feasible, offering insights into ancient or novel functions and the emergence and expansion of their biosynthetic pathways (Lichman et al., 2020b; Hu et al., 2023). The aim of the current study was to clarify the factors that contribute to the diversity of flavonoids across 22 extant Scutellaria species. Our investigation reveals diverse modifications of flavone and flavanone aglycones, underscoring the observed breadth of biosynthetic pathways for flavonoids with specialized functions in this genus. The analysis of metabolic networks and gene evolution enabled us to delineate four types of catalytic functions of CYP82Ds that govern flavonoid biosynthesis, considering substrate selectivity and competition. As well as observing previously unknown 6/8-hydroxylation reactions involving flavanone substrates, we discovered a unique phenol deoxymethylation at the C-7 position of flavonoid substrates within the cytochrome P450 family. # RESULTS

# Scutellaria phylogeny inferred from flavonoid structural features and metabolomics data

Our initial investigation into S. baicalensis and S. barbata, two clinically significant Scutellaria species, revealed shared structural features among their most abundant flavonoids. These features primarily involved two types of chemical modification: 6/8- hydroxylation and 7-O-glucuronization (Figure 1A). Interestingly, the two most abundant flavonoids in the aerial parts of both species, carthamidin-7-O-glucuronide and isocarthamidin-7-O-glucuronide, featured flavanones as their aglycones (Figure 1A). This unique structural aspect has been overlooked in our understanding of flavonoid biosynthetic pathways in S. baicalensis, but is critical for comprehending these pathways. Because flavanone derivatives are the most abundant flavonoids in the aerial parts of these medicinal plants, understanding the catalytic mechanisms involved in flavanone aglycone biosynthesis is crucial for understanding the evolutionary mechanisms that govern flavonoid diversity in Scutellaria. 

To gain deeper insights into the structural features of Scutellaria species, we performed phylogenetic and metabolomic analyses to explore the impact of chemical diversity on species evolution. Our investigation included 22 Scutellaria species, alongside four Lamiaceae family species as outgroups (Supplementary Data 1). Sequencing 21 new transcriptomes and retrieving additional data from public resources yielded a total of 26 datasets for analysis of nuclear gene sequences (Supplementary Data 1). Using a coalescent method, we constructed a Scutellaria phylogeny by curating five sets of orthologous groups (OGs) labeled 460, 1064, 1461, 2025, and 2442 through a multi-step procedure that considered alignment matrices and species coverage (Figure 1B; Supplemental Figure 1). The monophyly of Scutellaria had $100 \%$ bootstrap support (BS), consistent with previous studies (Zhao et al., 2020; Salimov et al., 2021). Our Scutellaria sampling revealed two distinct subclades, clade- $\pmb { \alpha }$ , with four species, and clade- $\beta$ , with 18 species. This robust phylogenetic framework provided a strong foundation for comprehensive lineage-wide metabolomic profiling. 

Metabolomics applied to Scutellaria species allowed extensive exploration of flavonoid diversity. Following peak detection, alignment, and normalization via the data-processing tool MS-DIAL, we performed orthogonal partial least squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) modeling of the resulting peak table in SIMCA-P to scrutinize distinctive chemical-structure features that emerged during intra-genus evolution of clade- $\pmb { \alpha }$ and clade- $\beta$ in both roots and leaves (Figure 1C and 1E). A total of 

37 and 25 metabolites were identified in roots and leaves, respectively, which could potentially serve as chemical markers for these tissues (variable importance in projection (VIP) value $\geq 3$ , $p < 0 . 0 5$ ; Figure 1D and 1F; Supplementary Data 2 and 3). In roots, comparison between clade- $\pmb { \alpha }$ and clade- $\beta$ highlighted the relative abundance of metabolites hydroxylated at C-6/8 in clade- $\pmb { \alpha }$ , which were derived from flavone aglycones. By contrast, clade- $\beta$ showed a preference for flavanone-based metabolites, with fewer $_ { 6 / 8 }$ -hydroxylation events and more ${ } ^ { 2 \prime } / 6 \prime$ -hydroxylated and O-sulfated derivatives. Similar patterns were obtained in leaf tissue, where both clades used flavanones as the predominant aglycone structure, highlighting the pivotal role of flavanone derivatives in the biosynthesis of flavonoids in Scutellaria. 

# Assessing the diversity of flavonoid structures in Scutellaria

To rapidly screen for the number of substituent groups, we established precursor ion lists using chrysin and pinocembrin as fundamental flavone and flavanone aglycones, respectively. These lists incorporated hydroxyl, O-methyl, and O-sulfenyl groups (Figure 2A; Supplementary Data 4). We performed a Pearson correlation analysis between the extracted ion chromatograms from the precursor ion lists and the total ion current chromatograms for each species. This method enabled us to map the distribution of flavone and flavanone aglycones across species and tissues (Figure 2A). By selecting the top 12 peaks from the total ion current chromatograms as index metabolites, we analyzed the roots of S. baicalensis and Scutellaria tapintzensis C.Y.Wu & H.W.Li. Our analysis revealed that S. baicalensis roots are rich in flavones, whereas S. tapintzensis roots contain abundant flavanones, highlighting the dominant aglycone structures in these tissues (Figure 2A). Across 22 Scutellaria species, lines that produced either flavone or flavanone aglycones converged, emphasizing the crucial role of specific aglycones in the evolution of Scutellaria flavonoids (Figure 2B). As for leaves, flavanone aglycones were found to be the dominant aglycone in a greater number of species, indicating a clear preference for flavanones in leaf tissues. 

The use of precursor ion lists for flavonoids and flavanones enabled efficient screening of metabolites associated with O-methylation and O-sulfation across 22 species. Our analysis revealed that O-methylation occurs in both aglycone types, with a significantly higher abundance in roots than in leaves (Figure 2C). Monomethoxylated flavonoids were predominant across most species, particularly in the roots of S. baicalensis, where derivatives with up to four methoxy substitutions were detected (Figure 2C). In addition, roots consistently produced only monosulfated flavonoids (Figure 2D). Further investigation showed that both flavone and flavanone aglycones were susceptible to sulfation. # A model for flavonoid biosynthesis in genus Scutellaria

We identified 261 flavonoids in the roots and leaves of 22 Scutellaria species on the basis of reference standards, mass spectral fragmentation, and retention patterns (Supplementary Data 5), revealing the roles of 6/8-hydroxylation, ${ } ^ { 2 \prime / 6 \prime }$ -hydroxylation, Omethylation, and O-sulfation in contributing to their chemical diversity (Figure 3A; Supplemental Figures 2–9). Building on the established model of flavonoid biosynthesis in S. baicalensis (Zhao et al., 2018a; Pei et al., 2022), our findings revealed a variety of catalytic reactions with dual aglycones, flavones, and flavanones. We observed 6/8-hydroxylation of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones in both roots and leaves, notably in Scutellaria mairei H. Le´ v., which also accumulated high levels of baicalin and baicalein in leaves (Supplemental Figure 3). This challenges the conventional understanding of differences in flavonoid biosynthesis between underground and aerial parts of S. baicalensis (Zhao et al., 2016). Across all 22 species, 7-O-glucuronidation emerged as the predominant storage form of flavonoids, with lower levels of C-glycosylated and O-glucosylated compounds. However, flavonoids that underwent both sulfation and glucuronidation were not observed, suggesting potential O-sulfation at the C-7 position (Hashiguchi et al., 2014). In this regard, critical questions about the proposed flavonoid biosynthetic model for Scutellaria remain, such as whether the 6/8-hydroxylation of flavanone substrates is linked to CYP82Ds and how substrate competition at the C-7 position is managed (Figure 3B). Research indicates that OMTs from S. baicalensis (SbFOMT3/5/6) enable 7-O-methylation, with the C-7 position predominantly undergoing O-glucuronidation, whereas O-methylation occurs mainly at the C-6/8 positions under the influence of SbPFOMT2/5 (Figure 3B) (Cui et al., 2022). To address these uncertainties, we examined the enzymatic functions of CYP82D subfamily members, focusing on substrate selectivity and competition. 

# Functional divergence of the CYP82D subfamily based on substrate selectivity

We examined the evolution of CYP82D subfamily genes by performing a comprehensive phylogenetic analysis of 22 Scutellaria species. The analysis included 135 putative full-length sequences and produced a highly informative maximum likelihood (ML) tree (Figure 4A). This tree categorized the CYP82D subfamily into five distinct taxonomic clades: CYP82D507, -506, -504, -509, and -511, as named by Dr. David Nelson (Supplementary Data 6; Supplemental Figure 10). Notably, each clade showed cohesive gene grouping corresponding to the taxonomic lineages of cladea and clade- $\cdot \beta$ (Figure 4A). 

To evaluate differences in CYP82D activities across clades, we selected 16 CYP82D members from S. baicalensis and S. barbata, representing all five clades, for in vitro functional valida-

tion (Gao et al., 2022). This validation focused on 6/8-hydroxylation of flavone aglycones using a yeast microsome expression system. Previous research indicated 8-hydroxylation activity for the CYP82D507, CYP82D506, and CYP82D504 clades, whereas the CYP82D509 and CYP82D511 clades exhibited 6-hydroxylation activity, suggesting an evolutionary sequence in which the F8H function of CYP82D genes preceded development of the F6H function in Scutellaria (Gao et al., 2022). We observed more efficient 6/8-hydroxylation in clades CYP82D506, CYP82D504, and CYP82D511 in S. baicalensis and S. barbata (Figure 4B; Supplemental Figure 11). Except for clade CYP82D511, only the CYP82D504c from S. baicalensis (SCbaiCYP82D504c) used both chrysin and apigenin as a substrate, producing 8-hydroxylated flavonoid products (Figure 4B; Supplemental Figure 12). Furthermore, within the final ML tree clades, SCbarCYP82D511a also showed development of a C-30 hydroxylation function (Supplemental Figure 12). 

We examined the catalytic capacity of all CYP82D members in S. baicalensis and S. barbata using pinocembrin and naringenin as substrates. None of the selected CYP82D members could use pinocembrin (Figure 4C). However, using naringenin, the enzymes SCbaiCYP82D511 and SCbarCYP82D511a produced two products simultaneously, carthamidin (6-hydroxylated naringenin) and isocarthamidin (8-hydroxylated naringenin) (Figure 4C). Notably, both enzymes of the CYP82D511 clade displayed 6- and 8-hydroxylation activity with naringenin as the substrate, with the 6-hydroxylation being predominant (Figure 4C). SCbai CYP82D506 and SCbaiCYP82D504c could use naringenin, but only the 6-hydroxylated product was detectable, owing to lower catalytic efficiency (Supplemental Figure 13). The substrate selectivity for pinocembrin and naringenin reflected the distinct metabolic profiles of S. baicalensis and S. barbata, with $4 ^ { \prime }$ - hydroxyflavanones abundant in the leaves of S. barbata and a lack of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavanones in the roots of S. baicalensis (Figure 4D). To verify the catalytic function of CYP82Ds in planta, SCbaiCYP82D511 in pEAQ-HT vectors and naringenin were inoculated into leaves of Nicotiana benthamiana, and carthamidin synthesis was examined. Unlike control leaves, leaves expressing SCbaiCYP82D511 showed a novel peak identical to the carthamidin standard, thus demonstrating carthamidin biosynthesis (Supplemental Figure 14). However, glycosyltransferases in N. benthamiana can also use naringenin to produce 7-O-naringenin glucoside as the main storage form, significantly influencing the biosynthesis of carthamidin (Supplemental Figure 14).

# The evolution of four types of catalytic function in CYP82Ds governs flavonoid diversity in Scutellaria

The four distinct catalytic functions of CYP82Ds, which play a crucial role in the biosynthesis of various flavonoids in Scutellaria, were summarized (Figure 6C). By integrating predictive markers for these functions with metabolic profiles across Scutellaria species, we developed a predictive model for catalytic functions of CYP82Ds within the genus (Supplemental Figure 18). The 

prevalence of $^ { 6 / 8 }$ -hydroxylated flavones across all 22 species highlights the dominance of type I 6/8-hydroxylation functions of CYP82Ds in Scutellaria. Specific components such as carthamidin, isocarthamidin, and dihydrobaicalein, along with their 7-Oglucuronide derivatives, were identified as markers for type II and type IV catalytic functions in certain species. However, the widespread occurrence of 7-O-glucuronide resulting from metabolic flux induced by 7-O-demethylation made it challenging to identify markers for prediction of type III functions. Therefore, predictions could be made for types I, II, and IV, with conjecture for type III, across all 22 Scutellaria species. Expanding beyond S. baicalensis and S. barbata, we selected two additional species—S. mairei and S. tapintzensis—for catalytic validation because of their unique metabolic profiles (Supplemental Figures 2 and 3). We focused on their respective CYP82D enzymes within the final clade (CYP82D511) to evaluate functional divergence across types I–IV. Our results showed that SCtapCYP82D511 exclusively exhibited a type I catalytic function (Supplemental Figure 19). By contrast, SCmaiCYP82D511 displayed all catalytic functions except for type IV, as shown by the absence of dihydrobaicalein in both leaf and root samples (Supplemental Figure 20). 

To clarify the mechanisms behind the different catalytic activities of CYP82Ds toward $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavanones and $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavanones, we used molecular docking to identify key amino acid residues in SCbaiCYP82D511. Docking simulations with naringenin indicated that residues D113 and G388 play a critical role in 6/8- hydroxylation by enabling the binding of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavanones as substrates (Figure 7A). Even when G388 was mutated, the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyl group of naringenin was still able to form a hydrogen bond with the carbonyl group of residue 389 (Figure 7A). By contrast, pinocembrin exhibited distinct binding behavior, and no hydrogen bonds were observed (Figure 7A). To further confirm the importance of these residues for the 6/8-hydroxylation of flavanones, we created SCbaiCYP82D511 mutants: D113A, G388A, V389A, and the double mutant G388A/V389A. Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) analysis revealed that, although the catalytic efficiency in producing carthamin and isocarthamin was reduced in the mutants, catalysis was still possible (Figure 7B). These findings underscore the essential role of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyl group in binding with residues 388 and 389. Notably, residue 389 plays a particularly crucial role, as the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyl group can consistently form a bond with it, regardless of amino acid substitution, ensuring successful catalysis (Supplemental Figure 21).

# DISCUSSION

Our metabolome analysis expands the widely accepted view that certain biosynthetic pathways are shared between underground (root-specific $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones) and aerial parts (leaf-specific $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavones) only obtained from S. baicalensis (Zhao et al., 2016, 2018a, 2019; Pei et al., 2022). For example, in species such as S. mairei and Scutellaria violacea var. sikkimensis, roots and leaves have distinct metabolic profiles, with substantial amounts of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones, such as baicalein and baicalin, accumulating in their leaves (Supplemental Figure 3). To investigate this selective accumulation of flavones, we assessed the transcriptional output of key functional genes (CHS, CHI, FNSII, and CYP82Ds) across six representative Scutellaria species, including S. mairei, Scutellaria yunnanensis H. Le´ v., S. tapintzensis, and Scutellaria quadrilobulata Y. Z. Sun (Supplementary Data 7 and Supplemental Figure 22). In S. mairei leaves, FNSII-2, FNSII-1, and CYP82D511 were highly expressed, leading to substantial accumulation of 6-hydroxylated $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones. In addition, whereas S. baicalensis and S. barbata showed significant differences in FNSII-2 and FNSII-1 expression between roots and leaves, the other four species exhibited more consistent expression levels, resulting in similar metabolic profiles across tissues (Supplemental Figure 22). Enhanced CHI expression in leaves of all species except S. baicalensis contributed to the increased accumulation of flavanone derivatives in leaves (Supplemental Figure 22). As a result, an assessment of the transcriptional 

output of CHI, FNSII-2, and FNSII-1 facilitated an evaluation of the accumulation pattern of crucial aglycones across different Scutellaria tissues. Furthermore, key genes acting downstream of the CYP82Ds were found to significantly influence final metabolic profiles. Therefore, a comprehensive analysis of both the upstream gene expression affecting aglycone production and the diversity of downstream key functional genes can greatly enhance our understanding of metabolic diversity among tissues of different plant taxa. 

The identification of $_ { 6 / 8 }$ -hydroxylation functions in S. baicalensis led to an in-depth investigation into the catalytic roles of CYP82D subfamily members in flavonoid biosynthesis (Berim and Gang, 2013). Recently, functional CYP82Ds have gained increasing attention for their roles in the biosynthesis of flavonoids, lignans, and terpenoids, primarily focusing on hydroxylation reactions (Lau and Sattely, 2015). Previously, the cytochrome P450-dependent hydroxylase CYP71D9 of soybean (Glycine max) was the only known enzyme with F6H function, accepting flavones as substrates (Latunde-Dada et al., 2001). Beyond the cytochrome P450 family, LjF8H, a flavoprotein monooxygenase-like enzyme, has also shown broad substrate acceptance among flavonoids, converting naringenin primarily into isocarthamidin and secondarily into carthamidin (Hiraga et al., 2021). Our study revealed that the four cytochrome P450 family members SCbaiCYP82D506, SCbai CYP82D504c, SCbaiCYP82D511, and SCbarCYP82D511a have evolved to encompass both F6H and F8H functions using naringenin as a substrate. This evolution has resulted in distinct product profiles compared with those documented for LjF8H (Hiraga et al., 2021). Intriguingly, these four CYP82D members exclusively use $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavanones as substrates, highlighting the crucial role of substrate selectivity in shaping the unique metabolic profiles of underground and aerial tissues in S. baicalensis and S. barbata. The ability to produce $4 ^ { \prime } \cdot$ - hydroxylated flavonoids in aerial parts, coupled with the catalytic capacities of SCbaiCYP82D511 and SCbarCYP82D511a for flavanones, led to a prevalence of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavanones in the leaves of S. barbata and S. baicalensis (Figure 4D; Supplemental Figure 8). By contrast, the lack of catalytic ability for $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavanones resulted in the accumulation of primarily $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavone derivatives in roots of both species (Figure 4D; Supplemental Figure 6). The detection of only dihydrobaicalin suggested the presence of another biosynthetic pathway for production of $4 ^ { \prime } \cdot$ - deoxyflavanones (Figure 4D). Therefore, both substrate selectivity and functional diversity of CYP82Ds play crucial roles in forming diverse 6/8-hydroxylated flavanones and flavones in Scutellaria. Demethylation is rare in plants, with limited research on N- and O-demethylation in biosynthetic pathways, primarily linked to alkaloid production (Farrow and Facchini, 2013; 2015; Hagel and Facchini, 2010b, 2010a; Ikezawa et al., 2008; Kluza et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2007). In flavonoid biosynthesis, the only known instance of 7-O-demethylation is attributed to ObF7ODM1, a 2- oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase (2-ODD) in sweet basil (Berim and Gang, 2013; Berim et al., 2015). Unlike CYP82Ds from sweet basil, which require a 7-O-methyl group for 6-hydroxylation (Berim and Gang, 2013; Berim et al., 2015), SCbaiCYP82D511 can catalyze 6-hydroxylation independently and has evolved its own 7-O-demethylation function to eliminate substrate competition and direct metabolic flux toward the production of baicalein and norwogonin in S. baicalensis roots (Figure 5E). Notably, SCbaiCYP82D511 does not catalyze the 7-O-demethylation of flavone derivatives hydroxylated at the 6/8-position (Figure 5C), which are present intrace amounts. This preference for 7-O-glucuronidation as the storage form for flavonoids in S. baicalensis roots is significant. Moreover, the formation of dihydrobaicalein may be facilitated through 7-O-demethylation by SCbaiCYP82D511 (Figure 6A). However, the precise precursor of dihydrobaicalein, including the potential involvement of 7-O-methyldihydrobaicalein, remains unclear owing to the absence of a reference standard. In addition, the O-methyl group closer to the 4-carbonyl group is more easily removed to form a phenolic hydroxyl group, forming hydrogen bonds with the carbonyl group, as observed in chemical synthesis methods based on flavonoid substrates (Chu et al., 2004). Therefore, SCbaiCYP82D511 holds potential for applications in the biosynthesis of 7-O-demethylated products, even those with a methyl group at positions relatively distant from the 4-carbonyl position. 

Assessing the catalytic functions of SCmaiCYP82D511 and SCtapCYP82D511 demonstrated the independence of type I–IV functions across various substrates (Supplemental Figure 19 and 20). In clade- $\pmb { \alpha }$ , full functionalization of CYP82D511 primarily directed metabolic flux toward 6/8-hydroxylated and 7-O-glucuronidated flavone derivatives via type I and III functions, yielding bioactive compounds such as baicalin, norwogonin, wogonin, and scutellarin. By contrast, type II and IV functions of CYP82D511 primarily contributed to the accumulation of 6/8- hydroxylated and corresponding 7-O-glucuronidated $4 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxyflavanone derivatives. These metabolic differences between clade- $\mathcal { A }$ and clade- $\cdot \beta$ species likely result from the defunctionalization of CYP82D511 in clade- $\cdot \beta$ . The catalytic traits of nine CYP82D ancestors in 22 Scutellaria species indicated that different catalytic functions (type I–IV) evolved from an 8-hydroxylation function that initially accepted only apigenin as a substrate. Throughout the evolutionary history of CYP82Ds in Scutellaria, both defunctionalization and neofunctionalization have frequently emerged. As the 

last evolutionary clade, the ancestor of CYP82D511 evolved to exhibit type II and III catalytic functions, leading to significant metabolic innovations within the genus Scutellaria. 

Research on non-traditional models, such as medicinal species and non-crop plants, is still in its early stages (Dhabalia Ashok et al., 2024). However, our findings strongly advocate for Scutellaria species as a broadly relevant model with which to explore the evolution of flavonoid diversity. An investigation of whole-genome duplication (WGD) events among Scutellaria species relied on synonymous substitutions per site $( K s )$ among paralogs. The analysis revealed no common WGD event within the genus. Instead, it confirmed only two known WGD events: the gamma event shared by core eudicots (average $K s = 2 . 1 8$ ) and the WGD event shared by Lamiaceae (average ${ \cal K } s ~ = ~ 0 . 9 4 )$ (Supplementary Data 8). In the context of Scutellaria, pivotal upstream genes, namely CHS, CHI, and FNSII, play crucial roles in providing adequate flavanone and flavone aglycones to ensure the metabolically necessary diversity of downstream biosynthetic products. CHS catalyzes the initial step in flavonoid biosynthesis. We found that only S. baicalensis and Scutellaria likiangensis Diels possess both CHS-1 and CHS-2 genes, whereas the other 20 Scutellaria species and 47 representative Lamiaceae species possess only CHS-2-like, which shares substantial amino acid sequence identity with SCbaiCHS-2 (Supplemental Figure 23). This suggests that the ancestor of the CHS lineage in Scutellaria likely originated as CHS-2, with CHS-1 evolving through duplication only in S. baicalensis and S. likiangensis. Furthermore, the tandem duplication of FNSII-1 and FNSII-2 across all 22 Scutellaria species suggests its significance as a genetic hallmark (Supplementary Data 9). This feature contrasts with 47 representative Lamiaceae species, which possess only one FNSII-like gene in 42 species, signifying the importance of this tandem duplication in Scutellaria species (Supplementary Data 10). The Ks values for orthologous gene pairs suggest that the duplication of FNSII-1 and FNSII-2 likely occurred after the shared WGD event in Lamiaceae, potentially preceding speciation of the Scutellaria genus (Supplementary Data 8). Following tandem duplication, FNSII-2 underwent subfunctionalization to produce FNSII-1, which exclusively accepts pinocembrin as the substrate, thereby enhancing the biosynthesis of $4 ^ { \prime }$ -deoxyflavones within Scutellaria and contributing in particular to the formation of baicalin (Zhao et al., 2016). 

Tandem duplications are evident in both F6H and F8H functional members, indicating a conserved evolution within the CYP82D subfamily of S. baicalensis and S. barbata (Xu et al., 2020). # METHODS

# Plant materials

We sampled 22 Scutellaria species and four outgroup species (Holmskioldia sanguinea Retz., Petraeovitex bambusetorum King & Gamble, Clerodendrum bungei Steud., and Pogostemon cablin (Blanco) Benth.). To ensure an ample supply of materials suitable for transcriptome and chemical analyses, we collected samples during either the vegetative or flowering stage. During the vegetative stage, we collected three tissues (roots, stems, and leaves), and during the flowering stage, we also collected flowers. To ensure identical tissue types for transcriptome and chemical analyses, we produced fine powders of plant tissues and then separated them into aliquots. Professor Chun-Lei Xiang from the CAS Key Laboratory for Plant Diversity and Biogeography of East Asia at the Kunming Institute of Botany, Chinese Academy of Sciences, identified all the plants. Voucher specimens have been archived at the Institute of Chinese Materia Medica, Shanghai University of Traditional Chinese Medicine. Supplementary Data 1 provides additional details regarding the collected samples. 

# RNA isolation, transcriptome sequencing, assembly, annotation, and analysis

To obtain comprehensive transcripts, we used a mixed-tissue approach during library construction, and the resulting transcript database served as the basis for our phylogenetic investigations. For transcriptome profiling, we generated libraries for individual leaves and roots from representative plants, namely S. tapintzensis, S. mairei, S. quadrilobulata, S. barbata, S. yunnanensis, and S. baicalensis. Total RNA was extracted using the TransZol Plus RNA kit (TransGen Biotech, Beijing, China), and cDNA libraries were prepared using the Illumina TruSeq RNA Sample Preparation kit (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA). Sequencing was performed by Shanghai Majorbio Bio-pharm Biotechnology (Shanghai, China) using a NovaSeq 6000 sequencer (Illumina), resulting in $2 \times 1 5 0 \cdot$ - bp paired-end reads. Quality control and trimming of the raw paired-end reads were performed using fastp (https://github.com/OpenGene/fastp) with default parameters. The clean data were then subjected to de novo assembly into contigs using the Trinity assembler (Grabherr et al., 2011). For transcriptome annotation, all assembled transcripts were searched against the following databases: NR, Pfam, Swiss-Prot, and the Arabidopsis thaliana proteome. BLASTX was used for the A. thaliana proteome and the NR and Swiss-Prot databases with an E-value cutoff of 1e 5. Pfam search results were generated using HMMER v3.1b2 (with $\mathsf { E } < 1 \mathsf { e } { - } 5 )$ (Mistry et al., 2013). RSEM software was used to calculate gene 

expression levels (transcripts per million reads [TPM]) (Li and Dewey, 2011), and differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014) with a threshold of $p < 0 . 0 5$ and $\| \mathsf { o g } 2 \mathsf { F C } \| \geq \ 1$ . We used the Heatmap Illustrator function of TBtools-II to create gene heatmaps (Chen et al., 2023). 

# Ortholog identification, single/low-copy gene selection, and phylogeny

After filtering to retain the longest transcript isoforms for all 22 species tested, all assembled transcripts underwent protein sequence prediction with TransDecoder v5.5 (Haas et al., 2013); the output was used to identify OGs using OrthoFinder v2.5 (Emms and Kelly, 2019). Taking into account potential biases arising from fragmented or incorrect assemblies, limited informative sites, and various other factors, the resulting five gene sets, including four low-copy gene sets (i.e., 1064 OGs, 1461 OGs, 2025 OGs, and 2442 OGs) and one single-copy gene set (i.e., 460 OGs), were used to construct a phylogenetic tree. The protein sequences obtained from the five gene sets were aligned using MAFFT v7.487 (Katoh and Standley, 2013) with default settings. Poorly aligned regions were trimmed using trimAl v1.4. rev (Capella-Gutierrez et al., 2009). The phylogenetic tree for each OG was constructed using RAxML-NG v1.0.3 (Kozlov et al., 2019) with 100 replicates under the $\mathsf { J T T } + \mathsf { I } + \mathsf { G 4 }$ model. To obtain BS values for all nodes, the phylogenetic trees for the five OG sets with 100 replicates from RAxML-NG were coalesced using ASTRAL v5.6 (Mirarab et al., 2014). Among all 22 species, H. sanguinea, P. bambusetorum, C. bungei, and P. cablin were chosen as outgroups. 

# Evolution analysis

$K _ { S }$ -based age distributions for paralogs in all 22 species were constructed using the ‘‘wgd’’ pipeline (Zwaenepoel and Van de Peer, 2019). In brief, paralogous gene families were identified using Diamond v0.9.18.119 with an E-value cutoff of 1e-10 (Buchfink et al., 2015), and the paralogs were systematically clustered using the Markov clustering algorithm (Enright et al., 2002). Genes within each paralogous gene family were aligned using MAFFT v7.487 (Katoh and Standley, 2013) with default parameters. The Ks values for all paralogous gene pairs within each family were calculated using the CodeML program in the PAML package v4 (Yang, 2007). Ks values were node-weighted to mitigate redundancy in the phylogenetic tree construction for each family using FastTree v2.1.7 (Price et al., 2009). Ks values for gene pairs related to flavone biosynthesis were calculated using TBtools-II (Chen et al., 2023). 

Genes containing the PFAM domain PF00067 were considered to be members of the cytochrome P450 family. Open reading frames were identified, and sequences shorter than 900 bp were excluded. To identify CYP82D and CYP82D-like genes in newly sequenced transcriptomes, we performed a gene model comparison (BLASTP) against 19 CYP82D genes from the S. baicalensis and S. barbata genomes $( \mathsf E < 1 \mathsf e { - } 5$ , amino acid sequence identity ${ > } 5 5 \%$ ). Protein sequences encoded by the selected CYP82D genes were aligned using MAFFT v7.487 (Katoh and Standley, 2013) and TrimAl v1.4 rev1 (Capella-Gutierrez et al., 2009). The trimmed alignment was used to construct an ML tree with ModelTest-NG v0.1.7 (Darriba et al., 2020) and RAxML-NG v1.0.376 (Kozlov et al., 2019), using 18 CYP82D genes from species outside Scutellaria as the outgroup. FNSII and FNSII-like genes from 47 Lamiaceae species (Mint Evolutionary Genomics Consortium, 2018) and Scutellaria were identified by applying BLASTP to gene models (BLASTP) with SbFNSII-1 (KT963453.1) and SbFNSII-2 (KT963454.1).

CHS was downloaded from TAIR (https://www.arabidopsis.org/) and used as a BLASTP query against the protein database of 22 Scutellaria species and 47 Lamiaceae species $( \mathsf E < 1 \mathsf e { - } 5 )$ . # Chemical standards and sample preparation

Reagents were purchased as follows: formic acid (high-pressure liquid chromatography [HPLC] grade) was from Fisher Scientific (Fair Lawn, NJ, USA), distilled water was from Watsons (Hong Kong, China), warfarin (internal standard) was from Sigma-Aldrich (Madrid, Spain), leucine encephalin was from Waters (Milford, MA, USA), acetonitrile and methanol (HPLC grade) were from Merck (Darmstadt, Germany), and 78 reference standards (purity ${ > } 9 8 \%$ ) were from Shanghai Standard Technology (Shanghai, China). Supplementary Data 12 presents detailed information for the reference standards. 

Roots and leaves of each species were freeze-dried under vacuum (Scientz-100F). The freeze-dried samples were crushed into homogeneous powders and sieved with a 40-mesh sieve. Under ultrasonication (53 kHz, 350 W) for 60 min at room temperature, each powder (0.01 g) was dissolved in 1 ml of $70 \%$ methanol (v/v) that contained warfarin $( 5 ~ \mu \varrho / \mathrm { m l } )$ as an internal reference. Following centrifugation at $1 2 0 0 0 g$ at room temperature, all supernatants were stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until analysis by ultra-HPLC coupled with quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry (UPLC–QTOF– MS). A quality-control sample was prepared by mixing $1 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ of each individual sample to produce a pooled sample that was analyzed to assess the inherent drift of the UPLC–QTOF–MS system. 

# LC–MS analysis

The samples were analyzed using an ACQUITY UPLC system (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) equipped with a binary pump, autosampler, and column compartment. Analytes in each sample were separated using an ACQUITY UPLC T3 column ( $2 . 1 \ : \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \ : \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ ) at constant temperature $( 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ with two mobile-phase solvents: A $( 0 . 1 \%$ v/v formic acid in water) and solvent B $( 0 . 1 \%$ v/v formic acid in acetonitrile). The gradient elution program was as follows: $2 \% - 1 5 \%$ B over 0–5 min; $1 5 \% - 3 0 \%$ B over 5–22 min; $3 0 \% - 6 0 \%$ B over 22–25 min; $6 0 \% - 9 5 \%$ B over 25– 30 min; $9 5 \%$ B over 30–33 min; and final re-equilibration at $2 \%$ B for 3 min. The injection volume was $2 ~ \mu \mu$ , and the flow rate was $0 . 4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ . 

A mass spectrometer (Xevo G2-XS QTOF; Waters) was coupled to an electrospray ionization source. Mass spectrometry (MS) was performed using the following instrument settings: sample cone, $\mathfrak { 3 0 \ V }$ ; source temperature, $1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ desolvation temperature, $4 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; cone gas flow, 50 l/h; desolvation gas flow, $6 0 0 ~ | / \mathsf { h }$ ; and capillary voltage, $2 . 5 \mathsf { k V }$ (negative-ion mode) or $3 ~ { \mathsf { k V } }$ (positive-ion mode). The mass range was set to 50–1200 with a 0.35-s scan time. The low collision energy was set to 6 eV (negative-ion mode) or $1 0 \in \mathsf { V }$ (positive-ion mode), and the high collision energy was set to 30 eV (negative- and positive-ion mode). Leucine enkephalin $( 2 ~ \mathsf { n q } / \mu \ I )$ was used as an external reference for mass correction during data collection, and sodium formate $( 0 . 5 \mathsf { m M } )$ ) was used to calibrate the instrument. The mass spectra were viewed and analyzed using MassLynx v.4.2 (Waters, Milford, MA, USA). 

# Data processing

Raw mass spectrum data were converted to an analysis base file format (ABF Converter, https://www.reifycs.com/AbfConverter/) and imported into the data-processing tool MS-DIAL v 4.60 (https://systemsomicslab. github.io/compms/msdial/main.html) for peak detection, alignment, and normalization to obtain peak tables in both positive- and negative-ion 

mode (Tsugawa et al., 2020). Parameters were as follows: MS1 tolerance, 0.01 Da; ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ tolerance, 0.02 Da; retention time range, 1–30 min; MS1 mass range and MS/MS mass range, 100–1000; minimum peak height, 20 000; mass slice width, 0.1 Da; MS/MS abundance cutoff, 800 amplitude; retention time tolerance, 0.15 min. Adduct types as $[ M + \mathsf { H } ] ^ { + }$ , $[ \mathsf { M } +$ $\mathsf { N a l ^ { + } }$ , $[ M + \mathsf { K } ] ^ { + }$ , $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N } \mathsf { H } 4 ] ^ { + }$ , and $\boldsymbol { [ 2 \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { H } ] } ^ { + }$ were selected for positive-ion mode, whereas [M – H]– , $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { H C O O } ] ^ { - }$ , $[ M + N a - 2 H ] ^ { - }$ , $[ M + \mathsf { K } \mathsf { - } 2 \mathsf { H } ] ^ { - }$ , [2M – H]– , and $[ 2 \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { F } \mathsf { A } \mathrm { - } \mathsf { H } ] ^ { - }$ were selected for negative-ion mode. The relative content of warfarin $( 5 ~ \mu \varrho / \mathrm { m l } )$ was calculated in MS-DIAL for data normalization. 

# Chemometric analysis

Data in the normalized feature tables were $\mathsf { l o g } _ { 2 }$ -transformed and imported into SIMCA-P 14.1 (Umetrics, Umea, Sweden). Metabolite data for 22 species were subjected to principal-component analysis and OPLS-DA to study relative metabolite abundance among different Scutellaria species. 

# Heterologous expression in yeast, yeast microsome isolation, and in vitro activity assays

Candidate CYP82D genes were amplified using the primers provided in Supplementary Data 13 and then subcloned into the epitope-tagged vector pESC-His using restriction sites EcoR I and Not I. Yeast strain WAT11, which harbors the A. thaliana NADPH-CYP reductase ATR1 (Urban et al., 1997), was used for transformation with individual recombinant vectors, each of which carried one of the candidate genes. As a control, WAT11 was also transformed with empty pESC-His. After 72 h of cultivation at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ on SD dropout medium (–His), a single positive colony for each transformant was selected and expanded through two stages of cultivation (5 and ${ 2 5 0 } \mathsf { m l }$ ) in SD-His liquid medium. The cells were collected by centrifugation and washed three times using yeast extract peptone dextrose medium supplemented with $2 \%$ galactose. Induction was achieved by incubating the cells in the same medium with shaking for 16 h at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ , followed by centrifugation at $7 0 0 0 g$ for 5 min to recover the cells into a 50-ml tube. Tris–EDTA buffer (TE) consisted of $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Tris–HCl and 1 mM EDTA (pH 7.4). The cells were resuspended in ${ 2 5 } \mathrm { m }$ of TEK buffer (0.1 M KCl in TE buffer) and left at room temperature for 5 min. The cells were then recovered and resuspended in TESB (0.6 M sorbitol in TE buffer). Cell lysis was performed at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \mathtt { - } 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ using a low-temperature ultra-highpressure continuous flow cell disrupter (JNBIO, China) with three repetitions. After centrifugation at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for $3 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ , each supernatant was transferred to a tube containing 10 ml of polyethylene glycol (PEG)–NaCl solution consisting of PEG4000 and 0.15 M NaCl. Microsomes were collected by centrifugation at $1 2 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 30 min and then resuspended in TEG buffer $5 \%$ [v/v] glycerol in TE buffer). In vitro activity assays were performed in a $1 . 5 – \mathsf { m }$ microtube with a $5 0 0 – \mu \mu$ $\mu \mu \mu$ reaction system comprising 50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.4), $3 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ NADPH, $5 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ substrate, 0.5 mg microsomal protein, and an ATP-regenerating system (2 U glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, 1 mM dithiothreitol, $2 . 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ flavin adenine dinucleotide, $2 . 5 \mu \mathsf { M }$ flavin mononucleotide, and 5 mM glucose-6-phosphate). The reaction mixtures were incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 24 h with shaking and then extracted three times with $5 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ ethyl acetate. 

# AlphaFold structure prediction and molecular docking

Multiple sequence alignment of candidate CYP82D members was performed using MultAlin (http://multalin.toulouse.inra.fr/multalin/), and the output was submitted to ESPript 3.0 (Robert and Gouet, 2014). The protein structure of SCbaiCYP82D511 was predicted using AlphaFold2 (https://alphafold.com/). AutoDockTools (v.1.5.6) was used to prepare the protein models and substrates, including heme, pinocembrin, and naringenin. Molecular docking of substrates with SCbaiCYP82D511 was performed using AutoDock Vina (Forli et al., 2016), and the molecular graphics were rendered with PyMOL 2.4 (http://www.pymol.org/). 

# Ancestral sequence reconstruction

Using the matched sequence file, phylogenetic tree file, and configured control file, we estimated the hypothetical ancestor sequences of 

CYP82D subfamily members with the JTT $^ +$ GAMMA model of the CodeML program in PAML v4.9. Regions with alignment gaps were analyzed using the parsimony method to determine the ancestral residue bases. The estimated ancestral genes were then synthesized and codon optimized for functional identification in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. 

# Transient expression of SCbaiCYP82D511 in N. benthamiana and metabolite analysis

Full-length cDNAs of GFP (control) and SCbaiCYP82D511 were cloned into the pEAQ-HT plasmid and transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. The bacterial cultures were grown overnight and then centrifuged at $1 6 0 0 g$ for 10 min. After discarding the supernatant, the bacterial pellet was resuspended in $1 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ to an optical density at 600 nm of 0.8–1.0. Acetosyringone was added to a final concentration of $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ , and the suspension was incubated for 3 h. The leaves of N. benthamiana were infiltrated with the bacterial suspension using a 2-ml syringe and incubated in the dark for 24 h. After 48 h, naringenin, pinocembrin, and tectochrysin, diluted in 10 mM MgCl2, were injected into the leaves. The plants were then cultured for an additional 4 days. The infiltrated leaves were collected, ground in liquid nitrogen, freeze-dried, and analyzed using LC–MS.
