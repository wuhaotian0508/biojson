# Summary

Upland cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) is a principal economic crop and a fundamental raw material for the textile industry. The quality of cotton fibres is significantly influenced by the synthesis of cell wall polysaccharides. This study focuses on GhIRX10, a beta-

1,4-xylosyltransferase crucial for xylan backbone synthesis. Overexpression of GhIRX10 enhances xylan synthesis, which impacts fibre elongation and secondary cell wall thickening. GhMYB102, identified as a direct regulator of GhIRX10 expression, was confirmed through comprehensive validation. Overexpression of GhMYB102 resulted in a similar phenotype as OE-GhIRX10: 

increased cell wall thickness and reduced fibre length. Overexpression of GhMYB102 

upregulated the expression of key cell wall synthesis-related genes, including GhCESA4/7/8, 

GhIRXs, GhCESAs, GhGUXs, GhTBLs, GhXTHs, and GhXXTs. Consequently, the cellulose and hemicellulose contents in OE-GhMYB102 lines were significantly increased. GhMYB102 was also validated as a target gene regulated by GhFSN1 and GhMYB7, with the ability to reciprocally regulate GhFSN1 expression. In summary, we propose a regulatory model where GhMYB102 promotes the expression of GhIRX10 and other cell wall-related genes, thereby affecting fibre quality. This study elucidates the regulatory network of secondary cell wall synthesis in cotton and provides potential targets for improving fibre quality through molecular breeding. 

# Introduction

Xylan is the primary hemicellulose in the secondary cell wall (SCW) of dicotyledonous plants and comprises a xylose backbone predominantly linked by b-1,4-xylose (Scheller and Ulvskov, 2010). The xylose residues on the xylan backbone are typically substituted with arabinose (Ara), glucose uronic acid (GlcA), 4- O-methylglucose uronic acid (4-O-Me-GlcA), and acetyl groups (Rennie and Scheller, 2014). Xylan forms high-order structures through hydrogen bonding and van der Waals forces with cellulose microfibrils, ultimately constituting the complete cell wall framework (Heinonen et al., 2022; Khodayari et al., 2021). Xylan plays a pivotal role in the proper bundling and alignment of cellulose microfibrils within plant secondary cell walls (Crowe et al., 2021). 

The synthesis of the xylan backbone is primarily catalysed by a ternary complex composed of IRX10, IRX9, and IRX14, with their homologues IRX10L, IRX9L, and IRX14L also exhibiting specific activities (Anders et al., 2023; Jensen et al., 2014; Ren et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2010). The Atirx10 mutant presents hindered synthesis of woody tissue cell walls (Brown et al., 2009). The irx10 irx10L double mutant shows severe growth restriction, accompanied by collapsed cell walls and a markedly reduced xylan content (Wu et al., 2009). Similarly, mutants irx9 and irx14 display dwarfism, delayed development, and abnormal woody tissue (Ren et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2010). In vitro enzymatic activity experiments demonstrated that only IRX10 possesses 

xylosyltransferase activity and is capable of de novo xylan synthesis, confirming the indispensable role of IRX10 in xylan backbone synthesis (Wang et al., 2022b). The overexpression of OsIRX10 in rice leads to a marked increase in cell wall thickness; conversely, mutant Osirx10 results in significantly reduced cell wall thickness alongside decreased plant stem length (Chen et al., 2013). In addition, the methylation and acetylation of the xylan backbone have important effects on the structure and physicochemical properties of xylan and the morphology of the cell wall (Gallinari et al., 2024; Lyczakowski et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2013; Yuan et al., 2016), which further indicates that xylan plays an important role in maintaining the normal shape and biological function of the cell wall. 

Cotton fibres are unbranched single-cell structures derived from ovule epidermal cells and formed by expansion and elongation (Haigler et al., 2012; Kim and Triplett, 2001). The development of cotton fibres consists of five overlapping stages: initiation, elongation, transition, SCW synthesis, and maturation (Paterson et al., 2012). During the synthesis phase of the SCW, the elongation rate of cotton fibre cells gradually slows down and eventually stops, while there is a substantial synthesis of cellulose and a significant thickening of the fibre cell walls. The onset and duration of the SCW synthesis phase directly influence both fibre elongation and cell wall thickness, affecting fibre properties, such as fibre length, strength, and micronaire value (Wen et al., 2023). Therefore, the SCW synthesis stage of fibre development is a crucial research phase for improving fibre quality. 

Several genes that are highly expressed during SCW synthesis have been identified and are involved in the synthesis of cell wall polysaccharides such as cellulose and hemicellulose. These genes significantly influence fibre morphology and quality traits. Notably, the cellulose synthases GhCESA4, GhCESA7, and GhCESA8 are critical for SCW cellulose synthesis and are highly expressed during this phase. In mutants, plant height is relatively short, fibre SCW synthesis is severely limited, fibre length is significantly reduced, and fibres are flat (Wen et al., 2022). GhGLU18, a $\beta$ -1,3-glucanase, has been shown to promote fibre elongation and secondary wall thickening (Fang et al., 2023). The overexpression of GhMUR3 leads to the upregulation of the expression of numerous genes related to SCW synthesis. This upregulation affects key fibre properties, including fibre length and cell wall thickness (Wu et al., 2024). As the principal hemicellulose, xylan has been shown to significantly influence fibre development. GhGT43A1 and GhGT43C1 are homologous genes of IRX9 and IRX14, respectively, in Arabidopsis. The heterologous expression of GhGT43A1 and GhGT43C1 in irx9 and irx14 mutants can restore cell wall defects and increase cell wall thickness (Li et al., 2014). GhGT47B, a homologue of AtFRA8, catalyses the synthesis of the xylan-reducing end tetrasaccharide sequence (Guo et al., 2024). In RNAi-GhGT47B lines, the fibre length, fibre strength, and micronaire value were shown to decrease significantly, and the fibre cell wall was thinner (Guo et al., 2024). 

The thickening of the plant SCW is mainly regulated by the NAC-MYB module. Within this module, the NAC family includes key regulatory factors such as VND6 and VND7 (vascular-related NAC domain), which regulate SCW synthesis in xylem vessel cells, and NST1 and NST3 (NAC ssecondary wall thickening promoting factors), which regulate SCW synthesis in phloem fibre cells. The MYB family mainly comprises functionally redundant core regulatory factors, specifically MYB46 and MYB83, that participate in the regulation of the secondary wall (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhong and Ye, 2015b). In cotton, GhFSN1, a homologue of AtNST1, was identified as a vital regulator of SCW synthesis in cotton fibres (Zhang et al., 2018). In OE-GhFSN1 lines, fibre length was significantly reduced, and the cell wall thickness was considerably increased; conversely, in RNAi-GhFSN1 lines, fibre characteristics were significantly reversed (Zhang et al., 2018). AtMYB46 and AtMYB83, essential node genes regulating secondary wall synthesis, are directly regulated by SND1 (Zhong and Ye, 2012). In the double mutant myb46/myb83, the cell wall collapsed, and SCW synthesis was severely inhibited, resulting in dwarfism (McCarthy et al., 2009). GhMYB7 directly regulates the expression of GhCESA4, GhCESA7, GhCESA8, and GhFSN1 through the SCW MYB response element (SMRE) ACC(A/T)A (A/C)(T/C), and the fibre cell wall of OE-GhMYB7 lines is relatively thick (Huang et al., 2021). GhKNL1 regulates fibre elongation and SCW synthesis by repressing downstream genes (GhMYB46, GhCESAs) in cotton (Wang et al., 2022a). 

In this research, we cloned the GhIRX10 and confirmed its catalytic activity in xylan synthesis, as well as its impact on fibre quality. RNA-seq results revealed the influence of OE- and RNAi-GhIRX10 on other genes related to cell wall synthesis. Subsequently, GhMYB102 was identified and confirmed as a central regulatory factor that directly controls the expression of GhIRX10. Moreover, a combination of DAP-seq and RNA-seq demonstrated that GhMYB102 plays a significant role in regulating the thickening of the fibre secondary cell wall. In 

summary, we propose a molecular model in which GhMYB102 regulates the expression of GhIRX10, GhCESAs, and other cell wall-related genes. This model confirms the effects of GhMYB102 and GhIRX10 on fibre quality, refines the regulatory network of the secondary cell wall (SCW), and provides potential molecular resources for breeding programmes aimed at improving fibre quality. 

# Results

# GhIRX10 is functionally associated with fibre development

Four cotton homologues of the IRX10 gene were identified based on the amino acid sequence of AtIRX10. Sequence alignment revealed a high degree of similarity between these GhIRX10s and AtIRX10 (Figure 1b). Notably, GhIRX10_A03/D02 (GH_ A03G1759/GH_D02G1915) exhibited high expression levels in 20–25 DPA fibres, whereas GhIRX10_A12/D12 (GH_A12G1592/GH_D12G1593) expression was barely detectable (Figure 1a). Only a single site was found to differ between the amino acid sequences of GhIRX10_A03 and GhIRX10_D02 (Figure 1b). Comparative analysis revealed that GhIRX10_A03 exhibits a higher level of expression within the fibres (Figure 1a). Consequently, GhIRX10_A03 was selected for cloning and subsequent in-depth investigation. GhIRX10 contains a transmembrane domain at the N-terminus of its amino acid sequence (Figure 1c). Subcellular localization revealed that the full-length GhIRX10 is localized to the Golgi apparatus (Figure 1d,e), whereas a version of GhIRX10 lacking its transmembrane domain exhibits noticeable aggregation within the cytoplasm (Figure 1d), indicating that the transmembrane structure plays a role in the proper localization of GhIRX10. Correlative analysis indicated that the expression level of GhIRX10 exhibits an inverse trend with fibre length (Figure 1f,g). Combined with the characteristic expression of GhIRX10 in fibres, this suggests that GhIRX10 may play a role in fibre development and has the potential to affect fibre quality. 

# GhIRX10 increases xylan synthesis and affects fibre elongation and secondary wall thickening

To evaluate the effects of GhIRX10 on fibre quality, multiple independently overexpressing (OE-GhIRX10) and RNA interference-silenced (RNAi-GhIRX10) lines were developed. The expression levels of GhIRX10 in these transgenic lines were quantified via RT-qPCR (Figure 2a). A high-volume instrument (HVI) revealed a significant decrease in fibre length and an increase in fibre strength in the OE-GhIRX10 lines (Table 1 and Figure 2b–d). The mature fibres of the OE-GhIRX10 lines presented reduced twisting and a more rounded periphery (Figure 2e,f), whereas fibres from the RNAi-GhIRX10 lines presented a ‘flat belt’-like morphology (Figure 2e,f). Furthermore, the cross-sections of mature fibres were considerably greater in the OE-GhIRX10 lines than in the RNAi-GhIRX10 lines (Figure 2g, h). Xylo-oligosaccharide content was measured via high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). The xylo-oligosaccharide content significantly increased in the OE-GhIRX10 lines but significantly decreased in the RNAi-GhIRX10 lines (Figure 2i), confirming that GhIRX10 promoted xylan synthesis. # GhMYB102 regulates GhIRX10 expression through the “TTAGGT” cis-acting element

Seven common MYB binding sites (MBSs) were identified between the GhIRX10-A03 and GhIRX10-D02 promoter sequences. Notably, the MBS_1, MBS_2, and MBS_3 binding sites all contained the core sequence ‘TTAGGT’ (Figure 4a). Based on the distribution of these MBSs, the full-length promoter was divided into four fragments of different lengths. The corresponding GUS reporter vectors were constructed and designated pIRX10-1 to pIRX10-4 (Figure 4b). The pIRX10-1 lines presented intense staining in multiple tissues, including the stem, leaf, inflorescence, fruiting stalk, and epidermal hair (Figure 4c). In contrast, the staining strength of the pIRX10-2 lines was significantly reduced, and the pIRX10-3 and pIRX10-4 lines presented almost no staining (Figure 4c). These findings indicate that the critical promoter sequence of GhIRX10 may be located between pIRX10-1 and pIRX10-2. Moreover, this region contains three identical ‘TTAGGT’ MYB binding sites, which suggests that ‘TTAGGT’ may be a preferred candidate binding site for a regulatory factor regulating GhIRX10 expression. 

To pinpoint regulatory factors that specifically bind to the ‘TTAGGG’ motif, a total of 22 AtMYBs were obtained by analysing the DAP-seq data of Arabidopsis using the tomtom tool of the MEME website (Table S2). Following this, 107 GhMYB genes were identified in upland cotton by homologous comparison (Table S3). Among these, only a few were expressed in fibres, and GH_A11G3587 (GhMYB102) showed a highly similar expression profile to GhIRX10 (Figure S1; Figure 4d). Consequently, GhMYB102 was highlighted as a prime candidate gene for modulating the expression of GhIRX10. 

GhMYB102 contains two ‘SNAT’ DNA-binding conserved domains in the N-terminus and is a representative member of the R2R3-MYB class of transcription factors (Figure 4e). A yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay confirmed that GhMYB102 could bind to the promoter sequence of GhIRX10 (Figure 4f). Additionally, GhMYB102 promotes the expression of GhIRX10 by binding to the promoter sequence between pIRX10-1 and pIRX10-2 (Figure 4g–i). Furthermore, GhMYB102 expression was significantly upregulated in RNAi-GhIRX10 lines (Figure 4j). To further investigate this regulatory relationship, we created overexpression (OE) lines of GhMYB102, in which GhIRX10 expression was significantly upregulated (Figure 4k). Collectively, these results provide compelling evidence that GhMYB102 regulates GhIRX10 expression and suggest a feedback regulatory mechanism in which the absence of GhIRX10 induces high GhMYB102 expression. 

# GhMYB102 regulates fibre length and cell wall thickness

To determine the positive regulatory impact of GhMYB102 on fibre development, three independent overexpression lines (OE-GhMYB102) were created (Figure 5a). The fibre length significantly decreased in the OE-GhMYB102 lines (Figure 5b,c, Table 2), whereas the fibre strength significantly increased (Figure 5d). Additionally, the OE-GhMYB102 fibre cell wall thickness increased significantly (Figure 5e,f). These fibre phenotypes corroborate the regulatory influence of GhMYB102 on fibre 

length and cell wall thickening, which is consistent with the observed phenotypes in the OE-GhIRX10 lines. 

# GhMYB102 plays an essential role in regulating fibre secondary cell wall synthesis

The genome-wide target genes of GhMYB102 were identified via DNA affinity purification sequencing (DAP-seq). DAP-seq yielded 89 828 peaks, with $1 5 . 3 4 \%$ (13 784) located within promoter regions (Figure 6a,b). The genes corresponding to these peaks were enriched predominantly in the cell membrane (GO:0016020), cell wall (GO:0005618), and various carbohydrate metabolism pathways (Figure S2a–c). MEME-chip was employed to identify the binding motifs of GhMYB102 based on these peaks, and 5512 peaks distributed across 4730 gene promoters were significantly enriched for a specific motif (Figure 6c; Table S4). This motif contains a core ‘TTAGGT’ sequence, which matches the MBS1-3 elements in the GhIRX10 promoter. CentriMo analysis revealed that the core “TTAGGT” sequence is highly similar to several MYB factor-binding motifs, including the well-characterized MYB46 (MA1040.1) (Figure 6d). 

Expression trend analysis of the 4730 candidate genes revealed that the expression profiles of genes in clusters 6, 8, 12, and 15 (a total of 1583 genes) correlated with those of GhMYB102, suggesting that these genes are potential targets of GhMYB102 (Figure S3). This gene set included multiple genes related to cell wall polysaccharide synthesis, such as those involved in xylan synthesis (e.g. IRX10, IRX9, IRX7, GUX1, GUX2, and TBLs), xyloglucan synthesis (e.g. XXT2, XTH30, and KATAMARI1), and cellulose synthase (Table S5). GO enrichment analysis confirmed that these 1583 candidate genes were significantly associated with the Golgi membrane and cell wall synthesis (Figure 6e). 

A total of 6093 upregulated DEGs were identified between at least two OE-GhMYB102 lines and the WT (Figure S2d,e). These upregulated DEGs were involved primarily in carbohydrate metabolism and cell wall polysaccharide synthesis, among other biological pathways (Figure S2f). Subsequently, a total of 239 overlapping genes were obtained from 6093 Up-DEGs and 1583 candidate target genes (Figure 6f). Notably, GO enrichment analysis revealed that these 239 genes were significantly enriched in pathways related to cellulose synthesis (Figure 6g). In addition, the contents of cellulose and hemicellulose in OE-GhMYB102 significantly increased (Figure 6h). 

CESA4, 7, and 8 are the main cellulose synthases involved in SCW synthesis. RNA-seq and RT-qPCR confirmed the significant upregulation of GhCESA4, 7, and 8 expression in the OE-GhMYB102 lines (Figure 7a,b). Furthermore, the promoter of GhCESA4, 7, and 8 contained the binding motif ‘TTAGGT’ of GhMYB102, and EMSA confirmed that GhMYB102 is directly bound to these motifs (Figure 7c). Collectively, these findings suggest that GhMYB102 target genes are intimately linked to cell wall synthesis and that GhMYB102 can directly regulate the expression of GhCESA4, 7, and 8, thus playing a crucial role in the secondary wall thickening of fibres.

Xylan is a linear polymer of b-1,4-linked xylosyl (Xyl) residues, with a degree of polymerization ranging from 90 to 120 (Jacobs and Dahlman, 2001; Pena~ et al., 2007). Xylan backbone synthesis is catalysed by IRX10 of the GT47 family, along with IRX9 and IRX14 of the GT43 family, which function in a ternary complex (Rennie and Scheller, 2014; Zeng et al., 2010; Zhong et al., 2019). A detailed enzyme activity analysis demonstrated that IRX10 has xylan synthase activity to catalyse the de novo synthesis of xylan, which is crucial for determining the structure of xylan nanofunctional clusters (Wang et al., 2022b). In the present study, GhIRX10 was cloned from upland cotton and found to be highly expressed during the secondary wall thickening phase of cotton fibre development (Figure 1a). Notably, the expression level of GhIRX10 was negatively correlated with fibre length (Figure 1f,g). These findings suggest that GhIRX10 may be important for fibre development and fibre quality. 

In rice, the Osirx10 mutant presented reduced plant height, thinner cell walls, and lower xylan content (Chen et al., 2013). The overexpression of PeIRX10 in the irx10l( / ) irx10( / ) double mutant rescued the plant growth phenotype and restored the secondary wall thickness (Wang Jie et al., 2016). In this study, 

the overexpression of GhIRX10 in cotton promoted the synthesis of xylan, leading to decreased fibre length and a significant increase in cell wall thickness (Figure 2). These results confirmed that GhIRX10 possesses xylanase activity and is crucial for fibre development and fibre quality. Furthermore, the overexpression of GhIRX10 also significantly induced the expression of GhIRX9/- GhIRX14 and their homologues GhIRX9L/GhIRX14L in transgenic lines (Figure 2j,k). These findings indicate that elevated GhIRX10 expression induces the upregulation of the expression of other xylan synthase genes, increasing the xylan content.

# Conclusion

In this study, we have elucidated the impact of GhIRX10 and its regulatory factor GhMYB102 on fibre quality traits such as cell wall thickness and fibre length, and have conducted an in-depth analysis at the transcriptional level of the molecular mechanisms affecting fibre quality. Based on these findings, we propose a molecular model by which the GhMYB102-GhIRX10 module influences the development of cotton fibre secondary walls (Figure 9). In this model, GhMYB102 and GhMYB46 function both synergistically and competitively, and are directly regulated by GhFSN1 and GhMYB7. On the one hand, GhMYB102 can directly regulate the expression of GhIRX10 and other genes related to hemicellulose synthesis, promoting cell wall development; on the other hand, it directly regulates the expression of GhCESA4, GhCESA7, and GhCESA8, increasing cellulose synthesis. In conclusion, this study identified new genes involved in secondary wall synthesis, expanded the regulatory network of the secondary wall, and provided new insights into fibre quality improvement.

# Materials and methods

# Vector construction and genetic transformation in cotton

The full-length CDS sequence of GhIRX10 and a specific 300 bp fragment (Table S1) were cloned into an overexpression vector (pBI121) and a post-transcriptional gene silencing vector (pHELLSGATE 4), respectively. The recombinant plasmids were then introduced into competent cells of Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Genetic transformation was performed using the Agrobacterium-mediated cotton cotyledon transformation method, as described by Jin et al. (2006), resulting in the acquisition of independent transgenic lines. Positive lines were meticulously screened and identified through kanamycin treatment coupled with the amplification of specific fragments using specific primers. Subsequently, the expression levels of these positive strains were quantified using qRT-PCR. For details primer information, please refer to Table S1. 

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Cotton plants (Gossypium hirsutum Jin668 and its transgenic lines) were cultivated in the experimental fields of the Cotton Research Institute, Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences, located in Anyang, Henan Province, China. The plants were managed following standard field management practices. On the day of flowering, cotton bolls were designated as 0 days postanthesis (DPA). Samples of cotton bolls were collected at 0, 10, 15, and 20 DPA. The bolls were carefully peeled to remove husks, and the ovules and fibre samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. These samples were then stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for RNA extraction and biochemical analysis. 

# RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis

Fibre samples were thoroughly ground using a mortar and pestle in liquid nitrogen and then aliquoted into $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ centrifuge tubes for subsequent use. Total RNA was extracted from the fibres using the RNAprep Pure Plant Kit (Polysaccharrides & Polyphenolics-Rich) (Tiangen, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA sequencing libraries were prepared 

using the NEBNext- UltraTM RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina-(NEB, USA) and sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with a PE150 model. After trimming the adapter and removing low-quality sequences to obtain clean data, the clear reads were aligned to the reference genome of upland cotton (TM-1_V2.1, ZJU) (Hu et al., 2019) using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2019). The number of reads mapped to each gene was counted using HTSeq (Putri et al., 2022), and expression levels were normalized using the FPKM (fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads). The differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014), with the criteria of |log2FoldChange| $> \ 1$ and a P-value $< 0 . 0 5$ . The transcriptome raw data for different developmental stages of upland cotton fibres were obtained from the BioProject database under the accession number PRJNA490626. The acquired raw data were subjected to expression analysis using the aforementioned methods. 

cDNA synthesis was performed using the HiScript II Q RT SuperMix for qPCR Kit with gDNA wiper (Vazyme, China). Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was carried out using an UltraSYBR One Step RT-qPCR Kit (Cwbio, China) on a 7500 Fast Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, USA). A cotton histone-3 gene (GhHIS3, AF024716) served as an endogenous control for normalizing expression levels. The relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C t }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001) and were expressed as the mean $\pm$ standard deviation of three biological replicates. Statistical analysis was conducted using GraphPad Prism9 software (Graph-Pad Software, USA) with Student’s t-test, and differences were considered statistically significant at $P < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Determination of xylo-oligosaccharide by highperformance liquid chromatography

Mature fibres were thoroughly ground using a grinder. A measured amount of the homogeneous fibre powder was weighed and placed into a $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ colorimetric tube. The fibres were submerged with $1 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of water, vortexed to mix, and sonicated for 30 min. Anhydrous ethanol was added to reach the calibration mark, and the mixture was homogenized. After centrifugation, $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of the supernatant was taken, and the 

ethanol was evaporated under a stream of nitrogen. The sample was redissolved to a final volume of $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ with water, and 2 mL was reserved for the measurement of free xylose. 

For the extraction of hydrolysed xylose, 8 mL of the supernatant was mixed with $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $4 \mathsf { M }$ sulfuric acid and hydrolysed at $1 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 90 min. After cooling to room temperature, the pH was adjusted to 5–7 with sodium hydroxide. Anhydrous ethanol was added to a final volume of $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ . The mixture was then centrifuged at 8000 rpm for 5 min, and the supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ membrane filter into a sample vial for the determination of hydrolysed xylose content. 

The analysis was performed using an Agilent 1260 Infinity II system (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, USA) equipped with an Xtimate- Sugar-Ca chromatographic column $3 0 0 \ \mathsf { m m } \ \times$ $7 . 8 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ , $6 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } } ,$ ) and a differential refractometer detector. The mobile phase was a $0 . 0 0 5 ~ \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { L }$ sulfuric acid aqueous solution, with a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. 

# Detection of cellulose and hemicellulose content

Mature fibres from various genotypes were collected and ground using a RETSCH MM 400 tissue grinder to obtain fibre powder for subsequent biochemical assays. Cellulose content was determined using the Updegraff method as described by Updegraff (Updegraff, 1969). The fibre powder was sequentially washed in $70 \%$ ethanol, a mixture of chloroform and methanol (1:1, v/v), and acetone. The insoluble residue was pelleted by centrifugation and dried using a centrifuge. The pellets were treated with Updegraff reagent in a boiling water bath for 30 min, then washed twice with water and acetone. Following this, they were centrifuged at $1 6 ~ 0 0 0 ~ \times ~ \pmb { g }$ for 5 min. After drying the pellets by centrifugation, 1 mL of $67 \%$ (w/w) sulfuric acid was added to each tube and allowed to oscillate at room temperature for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ until no visible particles remained. The glucose content was measured using the anthrone colorimetric method (Kumar et al., 2018). A standard curve was plotted using glucose at varying concentrations, and the samples were assayed in the same manner. 

The hemicellulose content was detected using the Hemicellulose Content Detection Kit (ml077332, mlbio, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The hemicellulose was hydrolysed into reducing sugars, which reacted with 3,5- dinitrosalicylic acid (DNS) to form a brown-red product detectable at $5 4 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ via a spectrophotometer. Standard solutions of varying concentrations were prepared to establish a calibration curve, and the samples were measured using the same method. Each sample was tested in triplicate, with results expressed as mean $\pm \textsf { S D }$ , and significance analysis was conducted using a Student’s t-test. 

# Cotton fibre quality analysis

To evaluate the quality of mature fibres from different genotypes, a high-volume instrument (HVI) was used. For each sample, a minimum of $1 0 ~ 9$ of fibre was obtained by randomly selecting at least 20 naturally mature cotton bolls from the middle fruit branches of at least 15 plants. The fibres were then extracted after ginning. The HVI test was conducted using the Premier HFT 9000 (Premier Evolvics Pvt Ltd, Coimbatore, India) at the Cotton Fibre Quality Inspection and Testing Center of the Ministry of Agriculture in China (Anyang, Henan Province, China). Before testing, samples were equilibrated under constant temperature and humidity for 1–2 days to ensure consistent results. The data 

reported are presented as means $\pm \textsf { S D }$ from three independent experiments. Statistical comparisons with the wild-type (WT) were performed using Student’s t-test, with significance indicated by $^ { * } P < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Observation of fibre micromorphology

The morphological characteristics of mature fibres were examined using a Hitachi SU3500 scanning electron microscope (SEM) platform (Hitachi, Japan), operated at an accelerating voltage of $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k V } .$ . For cross-sectional observation of mature fibres, the fibre bundles were initially embedded and fixed with a Spurr Embedding Kit. Following vacuum treatment to eliminate air bubbles, the samples were cured at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 day and then further cured at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for an additional day. Ultra-thin sectioning was performed using a Leica EM UC7 ultramicrotome (Leica Microsystems, Germany), with the sections set to a thickness of $1 . 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ . The Leica TCS SP8 (Leica Microsystems, Germany) confocal laser scanning microscope (CLSM) was utilized for detailed observations and photography. The cell wall thickness was measured employing ImageJ software, and subsequent statistical analysis and significance testing were conducted using GraphPad Prism 9 (GraphPad Software, USA) with one-way ANOVA. 

# Subcellular localization analysis of GhIRX10

The full-length coding sequence of GhIRX10, as well as a version lacking the transmembrane domain, were cloned and each sequentially inserted into the pBI121 vector for in-frame fusion with the EGFP coding sequence. The constructs were then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. For co-transformation, tobacco leaves were used along with a construct expressing the Golgi marker GmMan1 (1–49): mCherry. The fluorescence in the tobacco leaf cells was then detected using a Leica TCS SP8 (Leica Microsystems, Germany) confocal laser scanning microscope (CLSM). 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

The promoter fragment and the full-length CDS sequence of the transcription factor were individually cloned into the pHIS2 and pGADT7 vectors, respectively. The recombinant plasmids were co-transformed into the yeast strain Y187. Transformed yeast cells were grown on selective SD-Leu-Trp agar plates at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3 days. Subsequently, positive clones were selected and further cultivated on SD-Leu-Trp-His agar plates at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3 days. 

# GUS staining assay

The target promoter fragment was cloned into the HindIII and BamHI sites of the pBI121 vector. Arabidopsis thaliana transformation was achieved using Agrobacterium-mediated floral dip. T2 generation Arabidopsis plants were harvested, and various tissues were collected. b-glucuronidase (GUS) staining was performed on the whole seedlings and multiple tissues using a GUS staining kit (Huayueyang Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The promoter fragment and the target transcription factor’s CDS sequence were separately cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC and pGreenII 62-SK vectors, respectively. The recombinant plasmids were then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 + pSoup. Transient transformation of Nicotiana benthamiana leaves was performed using Agrobacterium. Two 

to three days post-infiltration, the leaves were harvested. The abaxial surface was uniformly coated with a solution of D-Luciferin potassium salt using a soft brush. The leaves were then incubated in darkness for 5 min to allow for the accumulation of luciferase activity. Luminescence imaging was conducted using a Tanon 5200 chemiluminescence imaging system (Tanon, China), with automatic exposure mode and default settings for other parameters. 

For the quantitative analysis of luciferase activity, a segment of the infiltrated tobacco leaves was placed into a 2.0 mL centrifuge tube containing steel balls, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and subsequently ground using a RETSCH MM 301 tissue grinder. The Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (Vazyme, China) was employed for the quantitative detection of the luminescent signal, adhering to the manufacturer’s protocol. The relative luminescence units (RLUs) were measured with an Infinite Lumi fluorescence microplate reader (Tecan, Switzerland), which sequentially detected the luminescence intensities of LUC (firefly luciferase) and REN (Renilla luciferase). The luminescence intensity of REN served as an internal control, and the LUC/REN ratio was calculated to determine the relative luminescence value. Each sample was assayed in triplicate, and statistical significance was evaluated using a t-test. 

# DNA affinity purification sequencing (DAP-seq)

DNA affinity purification sequencing (DAP-Seq) is a technique for identifying DNA-binding motifs of a target protein in vitro. In essence, it employs cell-free expression systems to produce the protein of interest, which is then subjected to affinity purification with a genomic DNA fragment library. The protein–DNA complexes are then affinity purified, and the captured DNA fragments are sequenced using high-throughput sequencing technology. For detailed methodology, refer to Bartlett et al. (2017). The prepared DNA library was incubated with the HaloTag fusion protein, followed by affinity purification, elution, and PCR amplification to produce the sequencing library. Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform using paired-end (PE150) mode to generate raw data. Clean reads were aligned to the cotton genome reference (TM-1_V2.1, ZJU) (Hu et al., 2019) using BWA-MEM (Li, 2013). The distribution of reads around transcription start sites (TSS) was analysed using DeepTools2 to create density plots and corresponding heatmaps (Ram-ırez et al., 2016). Motifs were identified using the MEME-ChIP tool from the MEME Suite (Bailey et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2014). 

# Protein expression and electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The full-length coding sequence of GhMYB102 was cloned into the pGEX-5X-1 vector, which was then transformed into the Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) strain. The bacterial cells were induced with $0 . 2 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactoside (IPTG) and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight. Following incubation, the cells were collected by centrifugation. The fusion protein GST:: GhMYB102 was purified using GST-tag Protein Purification Kit (Beyotime, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. DNA probes were biotinylated using an EMSA Probe Biotin Labeling Kit (Beyotime, China), with sequences provided in Table S1. The EMSA was performed using a chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Beyotime, China), following the manufacturer’s protocol.
