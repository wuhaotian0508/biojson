# ABSTRACT

Leaf colour mutants play an important role in understanding chlorophyll metabolism and photosynthesis. In this study, we characterised a temperature‐sensitive yellow leaf cotton mutant. Genome re‐sequencing and comparison identified a 10.327 Kb deletion on the D12 chromosome (D12:670726‐681053) in the mutant. The deletion region contains two annotated genes, GH_D12G0047 and GH_D12G0048. Investigations integrating gene mapping, comparative transcriptome analysis, gene annotation, virus‐induced gene silencing and gene complementation, found deletion of GH_D12G0047 or GhSCY2D, a crucial constituent of the Sec2 complex essential for the function of chloroplasts, being responsible for the yellow leaf phenotype. The yellow leaf mutant had disrupted chloroplast structure and hindered chlorophyll synthesis when temperature was below $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ but regained normal green leaf colour at $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . By analysing the transcriptome data and hormonal level changes of the mutant under conditions of $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , it was found that the jasmonic acid signalling pathway and GhSCY2 work in concert to maintain the structural integrity of chloroplasts. The outcomes of the study reveal the indispensable role of GhSCY2 and jasmonic acid in sustaining chloroplast homoeostasis, providing new insights into the regulation of cotton leaf colour and paving the way for advancement in high photosynthetic efficiency breeding strategies. 

# 1 | Introduction

Cotton is one of the most important economic crops in the world, serving as a vital natural source for the textile, food and feed industries (Chen et al. 2021; Liu et al. 2020; Paterson et al. 2012). Crop photosynthetic efficiency affects water use efficiency, nutrient use efficiency and land use efficiency, and is 

closely related to yield (Smith et al. 2023). Leaf colour is an important factor influencing plant photosynthetic efficiency and growth status. Changes in leaf colour usually lead to alterations in the photosynthetic capacity of plants, ultimately affecting plant biomass accumulation. Leaf colour mutants have great potential for studying chlorophyll synthesis processes, chloroplast development mechanisms and photosynthetic 

mechanisms (Song et al. 2018). Leaf colour mutants can also serve as morphological markers to assist in breeding and purifying new varieties (Du et al. 2020). 

According to their sensitivity to temperature and light, leaf colour mutants can be classified into temperature‐sensitive and light‐sensitive types (Yang et al. 2023), with the temperature‐ sensitive type being further divided into heat‐sensitive (Wang et al. 2016) and cold‐sensitive (Sun et al. 2017). In recent years, many leaf colour mutants sensitive to temperature or light intensity have been reported, with most mutants being found to be caused by mutations in genes related to chlorophyll synthesis and chloroplast development, which alter chlorophyll content, ultimately leading to leaf colour variation (Ma et al. 2023; Wang et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2016; Ye et al. 2016). 

Magnesium chelatase is a key enzyme in the synthesis of chlorophyll, catalysing the chelation of protoporphyrin IX with $\mathrm { M g ^ { 2 + } }$ to form magnesium protoporphyrin IX. This process is a key step in the chlorophyll biosynthetic pathway and plays a pivotal role in regulating the synthesis of chlorophyll. In the cotton variety Sumian 22, transcriptional repression of ABCI1 inhibits the synthesis of magnesium protoporphyrin IX, protochlorophyll and chlorophyll (Gao et al. 2021). In strawberries, mutations in the CHLI gene lead to a yellow‐green leaf phenotype, which partially recovers under low light conditions (Ma et al. 2023). In cotton, non‐synonymous mutations in the GhCHLI gene or reducing the expression level of this gene can result in leaves displaying a light green colour (Mao et al. 2018; Zhu et al. 2017). The development of chloroplasts from proplastids into fully functional chloroplasts requires three stages of differentiation. TCD5 encodes a monooxygenase that plays an important role in chloroplast development. The loss of this gene in rice leads to temperature sensitivity of leaf development at the P4 stage. The mutant tcd5 produces white leaves at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (low temperature) and normal green leaves at high temperature $( 3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ (Wang et al. 2016). Ribosomal RNAs (rRNAs) undergo numerous modifications during transcription and maturation; the homoeostasis of rRNA modifications is essential for chloroplast biogenesis in plants. Pseudouridine synthase (OsPUS1) mediates the pseudouridine (Ψ) modification of rice chloroplast rRNAs. The loss of function of OsPUS1 leads to abnormal chloroplast development and an albino seedling phenotype at low temperatures (Wang et al. 2022). SOP10, a PLS‐E2 type PPR protein, participates in RNA C‐to‐U editing of NAD2, NAD6 and RPS4 transcripts, and regulates the intron splicing of NAD4 and NAD5 transcripts. sop10 acts as a suppressor of ospus1 and can partially rescue the defective phenotype of ospus1 due to reduced reactive oxygen species (ROS) production caused by reduced functionality of mitochondrial complex I (Zu et al. 2023). 

The development of chloroplasts involves a series of protein transport processes, with precursor proteins encoded by nuclear genes to be transported by the translocon on the outer chloroplast membrane and translocon on the inner chloroplast membrane complex (TOC‐TIC) on chloroplasts (Jarvis and López‐Juez 2013; Paila et al. 2015; Richardson et al. 2017). Loss of TOC‐TIC components can lead to severe phenotypes, such as embryonic lethality and whitening in plants (Bauer et al. 2000; Hust and Gutensohn 2006; Jarvis et al. 1998; Kikuchi et al. 2013). Some nuclear‐encoded proteins require assistance from the Secretory (Sec) system after being transported into the chloroplast to 

integrate them into the thylakoid and inner chloroplast membrane (Fernandez 2018). Studies have found that the Sec system is located on the bacterial cytoplasmic membrane, as well as on the chloroplast membranes of plants and algae, indicating its conserved localisation (Liu et al. 2015). Unlike most bacteria, chloroplasts in green algae and plants contain two distinct Sec translocation/integration systems: One is the Sec1 system located on the thylakoid membrane, and the other is the Sec2 system located on the inner chloroplast membrane (Fernandez 2018). The Sec1 translocation system is relatively well‐studied, consisting of two interacting membrane components, SCY1 and SECE1, and a soluble motor protein, SECA1 (Yuan et al. 1994; Nakai et al. 1994; Schuenemann et al. 1999). Loss of any of these components results in seedling whitening and growth inhibition (Liu et al. 2010; Skalitzky et al. 2011; Williams‐Carrier et al. 2010). Skalitzky et al. (2011) identified components of the second system in Arabidopsis, named SCY2 and SCEA2. Loss of function in either SCY2 or SECA2 leads to arrest at the globular stage and embryo lethality in Arabidopsis (Skalitzky et al. 2011). Li et al. (2015) identified the protein encoded by At4G38490 through bioinformatics and protein modelling as the third component of the Sec2 complex (SECE2) and found that this gene specifically interacts with the SCY2 component in vivo. The heterozygous progeny of sece2‐1 (FLAG_005D06), caused by a T‐DNA insertion in the third exon, developed both wild‐type embryos and abnormal embryos, and on a sucrose‐containing medium, the mutants established as translucent small seedlings (Li et al. 2015). TIC40 and FTSH12 are transported by the Sec2 system, and the integration of TIC40 is inhibited by antibodies against SCY2 and SECE2, suggesting the involvement of Sec2 in inner envelope membrane (IEM) integration (Li et al. 2017; Xu et al. 2021). Although the components of the Sec2 complex have been identified in Arabidopsis, there have been no reports on the composition and function of the Sec2 system in other plants, including cotton. Research on the regulation of leaf colour by the Sec2 system in with temperature and jasmonic acid has also never been conducted in plants. 

In this study, we found a temperature‐sensitive yellow leaf cotton mutant and cloned the responsible gene (GH_D12G0047 or GhSCY2D). Employing transcriptomic and physiological assays, we preliminarily elucidated the mechanisms responsible for the temperature‐sensitive leaf colour variations of the mutant. Furthermore, the function of each component of the Sec2 complex, including SCY2, was further validated using the VIGS technique. In addition, this study reveals a new role of jasmonic acid in regulating leaf colour in a temperature‐ dependent manner.

# 2.2 | Chloroplast Ultrastructure of the Yellow Leaf Mutant Is Impaired Under Low Temperature

Through transmission electron microscopy, the chloroplast ultrastructure of the mutant and wild type under two different temperatures was compared. At $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the wild type had a well‐developed chloroplast structures, stacked thylakoid membranes forming grana, clear membrane structures and large starch grains (Figure 2A, c,d,g,h). However, at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , compared with the wild type, the mutant had poorly developed chloroplast structure, fewer and weaker grana formed by stacked thylakoids and no large starch grains in the chloroplasts (Figure 2A, a–d). This indicates that the chloroplast development of the mutant was defective and the function of chloroplast was degraded at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . At $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , development of chloroplasts in the mutant was similar to the wild type, with well‐stacked thylakoids and large starch grains, indicating recovery of chloroplast function and having no negative impact on photosynthetic capacity (Figure 2A, e–h).

# 2.4 | Genetic Analysis of the Yellow Leaf Mutation

Genetic analysis was conducted to investigate the nature of the mutation and to map the genetic locus responsible for the mutant phenotype (Tables 1 and S2). Leaves of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 1 }$ progeny exhibited normal green. Of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population with a total of 338 plants in 2022, 251 and 87 were green and yellow leaf plants, 

respectively. Of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population with 554 plants in 2023, 431 and 123 plants showed green and yellow leaf, respectively. Chi‐ square tests indicated that the segregation pattern was consistent with the Mendelian segregation ratio of 3:1 $( \chi ^ { 2 } < \chi ^ { 2 } \phantom { . } _ { 0 . 0 5 } = 3 . 8 4 )$ ). Therefore, the yellow leaf trait is controlled by a single recessive gene.

The results showed that only downregulation of GH_D12G0047 (GhSCY2D) induced leaf 

yellowing phenotype, accompanying with a noticeable decrease in both chlorophyll and carotenoid contents (Figures 3D,E and S6). 

To confirm the role of GH_D12G0047 (GhSCY2D) in regulating leaf colour, we overexpressed GhSCY2D from the wild type in the yellow leaf mutant (Figure 3F). The overexpression transgenics showed a significant increase in the expression level of GhSCY2D compared to the mutant and a green leaf phenotype (Figure 3F,G). Subcellular localisation analysis using the 35S::GH_D12G0047‐GFP fusion vector in tobacco leaves indicated that GH_D12G0047 is localised in the chloroplasts (Figure S7). We analysed the evolution of GhSCY2D and its homologues in other species (Figure 4, Table S6), including algae, Arabidopsis thaliana, rice, tobacco and cotton. The unresolved ratio in the tree was $< 3 0 \%$ , indicating the reliability of the tree (Figure 4A). GhSCY2D was located in the same clade as the SCY2 genes in A. thaliana, Oryza sativa, Nicotiana attenuate, Physcomitrium patens and Klebsormidium nitens (Figure 4B). Furthermore, the analysis of sequence heterogeneity indicates that the differences among the various sequences were relatively small, suggesting that this gene is highly conserved in different species (Figure 4C). 

# 2.6 | Identification of the Components of the Sec2 System in Cotton

The Sec system in A. thaliana has a high similarity with the bacterial transport system (Fernandez 2018). The Sec2 complex is a transport system located on the inner membrane of chloroplasts, consisting of SCY2, SECA2 and SECE2 (Fernandez 2018). However, the components of the Sec2 system in cotton has not yet been reported. 

We here identified GH_A02G1232 and GH_D02G2469 being the cotton orthologs of Arabidopsis SECA2 (AT1G21650) and SECE2 (AT4G38490), respectively. Structural variants (SVs) play an important role in plant evolution and agricultural production, and they are related to traits such as environmental adaptation, disease resistance and fruit size (Lye and Purugganan 2019). Li et al. (2023) discovered that a 244 bp deletion in Sgal12g015720 in cultivated tomatoes leads to the pseudogenisation of Sgal12g015720, and consequently changed plant architecture and yield. Further experiments revealed that Sgal12g015720‐OE transgenic plants had more lateral branches and more than twice the total number of fruits compared to wild‐type plants (Li et al. 2023). 

The function of the SCY2 gene has not been reported in crops other than the model plant Arabidopsis. In this study, we identified a naturally occurring temperature‐sensitive yellow 

leaf colour mutant in cotton. The leaf colour change in this mutant was regulated by the growth period and temperature. Compared to normal plants, the mutant has abnormal chloroplast structure, reduced chlorophyll content and decreased photosynthetic rate. Fine mapping revealed that the mutant was due to deletion of GhSCY2D, one of the components of the Sec2 system primarily localised to the IEM of chloroplasts. Loss‐of‐ function mutations in SCY2 and SECA2 in Arabidopsis result in tuber stagnation and embryo lethality (Skalitzky et al. 2011). However, the yellow leaf cotton mutant described in this study exhibited yellow leaves without embryo lethality. Through expression analysis in different tissues, it was found that GhSCY2D was highly expressed in cotton seeds (Figure S5A), and its homoeologous gene (GhSCY2A) was expressed normally in the mutant, which might be the main reason for the absence of embryo lethality. The SCY2 gene in Arabidopsis has been 

found to be involved in the integration and transport of the inner chloroplast membrane. TIC40 is considered a component of the TIC complex located on the inner chloroplast membrane (Li et al. 2017). When the expression level of SCY2 was knocked down, it was found that the expression levels of TIC40 and TIC110 were significantly reduced, indicating that the Sec2 system mediates the integration of TIC complex‐associated inner membrane proteins (Li et al. 2017). Thus, we speculate that the absence of GhSCY2D leads to the inability of TIC complex‐associated proteins to be integrated into the inner membrane. Therefore, nuclear‐encoded proteins related to photosynthesis cannot enter the chloroplast but accumulate in the cytoplasm, disrupting chloroplast structure, resulting in abnormal jasmonic acid synthesis and a decrease in chlorophyll content. In subsequent research, we will conduct relevant experiments to explore the changes in chloroplast precursor proteins before and after the recovery of leaf colour. 

The mutant leaf colour returned to normal at $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . To elucidate the relationship between temperature and leaf colour, we performed transcriptome analysis and found that the jasmonic acid‐mediated signalling pathway might be the most crucial one involved in temperature‐dependent leaf colour change in the mutant (Figure 6A,B). While some studies have shown that jasmonates can induce leaf senescence, our study reveals a different effect: low concentrations of methyl jasmonate can restore the normal green colour of yellowing leaf mutants, indicating that jasmonic acid may have a dual role in regulating leaf colour and development. However, the specific mechanism by which methyl jasmonate contributes to the restoration of leaf colour in mutant Y remains to be further investigated. Methyl jasmonate plays an important role in both biotic and abiotic stress responses in plants. In peas, the exogenous application of MeJA increased the activities of antioxidant enzymes SOD, POD and CAT, as well as improved the photosynthetic rate and pigment content (Manzoor et al. 2022). When plants are stressed, ROS is produced internally, and chloroplasts activate retrograde signalling to prevent damage caused by the accumulation of ROS. Jasmonic acid participates in downstream responses of ROS‐triggered chloroplast‐to‐nucleus retrograde signalling (Bali et al. 2023). Due to the low photosynthetic capacity and abnormal jasmonic acid synthesis, the mutant accumulated ROS in the cells. Promoter analysis and expression level studies indicate that GhSCY2A and GhSCY2D can respond to temperature changes. This is another factor that allows mutant Y to restore leaf colour at $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , in addition to methyl jasmonate; this result also suggests that GhSCY2A and GhSCY2D may play important roles in stabilising chloroplast structure under stress conditions (Figure S8). In rice, it has been found that after heat stress, the E3 ligase TT3.1 is translocated to the endoplasmic reticulum and ubiquitinates the chloroplast precursor protein TT3.2, leading to its degradation in vacuoles, thereby protecting the chloroplast from damage (Zhang et al. 2022). 

In conclusion, our study demonstrated that the yellow leaf colour of the mutant is temperature‐sensitive and is caused by the deletion of GhSCY2D located on chromosome D12. At $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the expression level of GhSCY2A was low (Figure S8F), which might be not high enough to compensate the effect caused by loss‐of‐function of GhSCY2D, leading to abnormalities in the 

Sec2 system and the structure of chloroplasts. And the structural abnormalities of chloroplasts result in a decrease in the mutant's photosynthetic capacity and the production of a large amount of ROS within the chloroplasts (Figure 7A). The precursor of jasmonic acid, OPDA, is synthesised in the chloroplasts and needs to be transported to the cytoplasm via the JASSY complex. The disruption of the chloroplast structure impedes the transport of OPDA, leading to the obstruction of jasmonic acid synthesis and the subsequent accumulation of ROS. High temperature conditions $( 3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ can mitigate the defective effect caused by the loss of function of GhSCY2D, restoring the structure of the chloroplasts, enabling ROS scavenging, and finally rescuing the leaf colour of the mutant (Figure 7B). Therefore, the restoration of leaf colour of the mutant under the high‐temperature conditions is likely attributed to two factors. First, there was an upregulation in the expression of GhSCY2A (Figures S8F and 7B), the homoeologous gene of GhSCY2D, which may be pivotal for maintaining chloroplast homoeostasis. Second, the high temperature enhanced synthesis of MeJA, which enhanced the scavenging of ROS in the mutant (Figure 7B). GhSCY2D plays an important role in maintaining chloroplast homoeostasis and the transport of inner membrane proteins in chloroplasts. Overexpression of GhSCY2D may help maintain photosynthetic rates in cotton under the conditions of high temperature or intense light, such as midday of sunny days, thereby increasing cotton yield. Additionally, the gene offers a valuable genetic resource for high‐light‐efficiency breeding. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Population Construction and Genetic Mapping

An $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population segregating for the leaf colour was generated by cross the mutant SD18‐46 (yellow leaf, Y) to SD22‐116 (green leaf, G). $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ plants grown in two consecutive years (338 plants in 2022 and 554 plants in 2023) were used in genetic analysis of the mutant trait (Table 1). Yellow and green segregants of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population were used in mapping of the genetic locus responsible for the mutant traits using BSA‐seq analysis, and fine mapping was done by genotyping by targeted sequencing (GBTS) technology using the segregating population. Chi‐ square goodness‐of‐fit tests were performed using SPSS to analyse the segregation ratio of the mutant trait. 

# 4.2 | Temperature Treatment and Sample Collection

The seeds of the experimental materials were germinated in a $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ incubator, and seedlings were grown in a cultivation box at temperatures of $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively (Light intensity: 10 000 lux; light cycle: 16 h light/8 h dark; humidity: $6 0 \%$ ). When the mutants and wild‐type plants grew to 2–3 true leaves, leaves from plants showing uniform growth were collected for transcriptome and metabolome analyses, qRT‐PCR, transmission electron microscopy observation and chlorophyll extraction.

# 4.3 | Extraction and Measurement of Photosynthetic Pigments

To determine the pigment content, functional leaves (the fourth leaf from the top) from both mutants and wild type plants at different stages (40, 60 and 75 days after germination) were collected. A $_ { 0 . 1 \mathrm { g } }$ sample was weighed and finely chopped. The $_ { 0 . 1 \mathrm { g } }$ samples were placed into $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of extraction solution ( $9 5 \%$ anhydrous ethanol) and were left in the dark for $7 2 \mathrm { { h } }$ . Using a UV‐visible spectrophotometer (Uvmini‐1240), the OD values of the samples were measured at wavelengths of 470, 663 and 645 nanometres, and the amounts of chlorophyll a (Chla), chlorophyll b (Chlb), total Chl and carotenoids (Car) were calculated according to the formula described by Lichtenthaler et al. (1987). Three technical replicates were performed for each sample. 

# 4.4 | Transmission Electron Microscope Observation of Chloroplasts

Leaves were cut into approximately $1 \mathrm { m m } ^ { 2 }$ pieces with a sharp blade, fixed in a $2 \%$ glutaraldehyde solution $\mathrm { ( p H = 7 } . 4 ) ,$ ), and then vacuumed at the bottom of a centrifuge tube. Following the method previously described (Shan et al. 2003), the chloroplast ultrastructure of SD18‐46 (Y) and SD22‐116 (G) was 

observed using a Hitachi HT7700 transmission electron microscope. 

# 4.5 | Analysis of Hormones in SD18‐46 (Y) Before and After Leaf Colour Recovery

To investigate the impact of hormones on the leaf colour of SD18‐46 (Y), we employed ultra performance liquid chromatography and tandem mass spectrometry to determine the hormone content of Y treated at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (yellow leaf) and $3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (recovering the leaf colour to green) for 4–5 days. Following the acquisition of mass spectrometry data, qualitative analysis was conducted using the MWDB database built based on standard samples. Quantitative analysis was then performed using standard curves, with standard solution concentrations ranging from 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, 10, 50, 100, 200, to $5 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ . 

Given the extremely low content of MeJA (methyl jasmonate) in the mutant when its leaf colour is yellow (at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ), we exogenously applied MeJA solution $\mathrm { ( 0 . 1 5 m m o l / L ) }$ ) to the mutant seedlings after their cotyledons had fully unfolded to investigate whether MeJA could replace a higher temperature $( 3 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ to recover the leaf colour to green. To ensure the effectiveness of MeJA, we sprayed MeJA twice daily, once in the morning and once in the evening. 

# 4.6 | Analysis of Flavonoid in SD18‐46 (Y) Before and After Leaf Colour Recovery

A total of $5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of the test sample powder was added to $1 . 2 \mathrm { m L }$ of pre‐cooled methanol‐water internal standard extraction solution. Next, the supernatant was retained and filtered through a microporous membrane and the obtained samples were analysed using UPLC‐MS/MS. The liquid chromatography conditions included the chromatographic column, mobile phase, gradient elution and a flow rate of $0 . 3 5 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The mass spectrometry conditions were as follows: electrospray ionisation (ESI) temperature at $5 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ; ion spray voltage (IS) at 5500 V (positive ion mode)/−4500 V (negative ion mode); ion source gas I (GSI), gas II (GSII) and curtain gas (CUR) set to 50, 60 and 25 psi, respectively; and collision‐induced dissociation parameters set to high. QQQ scanning was performed in the MRM mode with nitrogen as the collision gas set to medium. The declustering potential (DP) and collision energy (CE) for each MRM ion pair were optimised through further adjustment. Specific MRM ion pairs were monitored during each period based on the metabolites eluted at that time. 

# 4.7 | Transcriptome Analysis and Quantitative Real‐Time PCR

Seedlings of the yellow and green leaf segregants from the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population were used for transcriptome analysis. Transcriptome sequencing and analysis were performed by Metware. Both the yellow and green leaf samples included three biological replicates, with each replicate containing 4–5 cotton seedlings of uniform growth. 

For qRT‐PCR, total RNA was extracted using the Plant RNA EASYspin Plus Kit (Aidlab Biotechnologies Co. Ltd.). After quality control, the total RNA was converted into first‐strand cDNA using the Easy Script One‐Step cDNA Removal and cDNA Synthesis Super Mix kit (AE311‐03). The reaction conditions included incubation at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min and a heat treatment at $8 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 s. In the qRT‐PCR experiment, we chose the GH_A11G1110 (GhUBQ7) as the internal control to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the experimental results (Table S10). In each $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction, we added $3 0 0 \mathrm { n g }$ of cDNA and $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of AceQ qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix, strictly following the manufacturer's protocol. The PCR reaction programme was set as follows: initial denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \thinspace s$ , followed by 40 cycles of denaturation at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 s and annealing/extension at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s. Three biological replicates were used for each sample, with each biological replicate including three technical replicates. And the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method was used in analysis of relative expression level of the target genes. 

# 4.8 | BSA‐Seq Analysis and Fine Mapping

The yellow (30 plants) and green (30 plants) leaf sergeants of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population were used to generate two DNA pools for BSA‐seq analysis. For each pool, an equal amount of DNA from individual plants was pooled. DNA from the two parents was also extracted and re‐sequenced. Briefly, the process 

included in BSA‐seq and analysis was DNA quantification and library creation, library quality control, illumina sequencing (illumina HiSeq platform) at a coverage of $3 0 \times$ , and aligning the clean reads (after quality control) to the reference genome (http://cotton.zju.edu.cn/). The GATK software was used to call SNPs between the two pools, the SNPs were then used in genetic mapping using the Euclidean distance (ED) algorithm and SNP‐index method. The Δ(SNP‐ index) value for each variant locus was calculated using the QTLseqr (R package) software, and the distribution of $\Delta$ (SNP‐index) values across the genome was examined using a sliding window of $2 \mathrm { M b }$ . Genome regions exceeding the threshold line were considered as candidate regions associated with the mutant trait. 

To narrow down the candidate region identified by the BSA‐seq experiment, $3 3 8 \mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals were further genotyped using 20 SNP/InDel markers located in the candidate region using the GBTS platform (Guo et al. 2019). A second GBTS genotyping with six markers (PB1, PB2, PB3, PB4, PB5, PB6) was performed to further narrow down the region (Table S3). The BSA‐seq and GBTS genotyping were carried out by Wuhan GENOSEQ Co. Ltd. 

# 4.9 | Virus‐Induced Gene Silencing

To identify the gene responsible for the yellow leaf trait from a list of candidate genes, we constructed VIGS vectors for GH_D12G0047, GH_D12G0048, GH_D12G0050, GH_D12G0055 and GH_D12G0060 (Table S10). Target sequences (180–300 bp) of these candidate genes for VIGS experiments were obtained through the online Sol Genomics Network VIGS tool, and the amplified target fragments were ligated by one‐step ligation to a tobacco rattle virus (pTRV2) vector, which was then transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. After identifying the gene GhSCY2D (GH_D12G0047) causing leaf yellowing, we further constructed VIGS vectors for GH_A02G1232 (targeting sequence: 292 bp) and GH_D02G2469 (targeting sequence: 228 bp) that might interact with GhSCY2D (Table S10). After Agrobacterium carrying the target gene was cultured in LB liquid medium with the addition of Kan and Rif for 2–3 days, the culture was centrifuged at $4 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 10 min, adjusted the OD value to be between 0.6 and 0.8 with the induction solution, and dark‐treated for $^ { 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . The dark‐treated bacterial solution was mixed with pTRV1 in equal proportions for the injection of cotton cotyledons; the injected cotton plants were placed in the incubator at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - } 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ under dark. 

# 4.10 | Shoot Apical Meristem Cell‐Mediated Plant Transformation

To verify the function of the GhSCY2D gene in controlling leaf colour, we cloned the CDS sequence of GhSCY2D from plants with normal leaf colour and ligated it into the WMV067 vector using a one‐step cloning method (Table S10). Then, using the shoot apical meristem cell‐mediated transformation approach (Ge et al. 2023), we transformed the GhSCY2D gene into the yellow leaf mutant for complementation. 

# 4.11 | Subcellular Localisation

The cDNA sequence of GH_D12G0047 was amplified from the WT (G), then used to construct the 35S::GH_D12G0047‐GFP fusion vector through recombination reaction (Table S10), which was then transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. After incubation at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 days, single clones were selected and cultured in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of YEB liquid medium with corresponding antibiotic for 1–2 days. The cultures were collected, and the bacterial suspension was suspended in $1 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { M g C l _ { 2 } }$ until the OD600 reaching around 0.6. The suspended bacterial solution was injected into tobacco leaves, followed by incubation under weak light for 2 days before observation under a confocal laser scanning microscope. Chlorophyll excitation and emission wavelengths were set at 640 and 675 nm, respectively, while GFP excitation and emission wavelengths were set at 488 and $5 1 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. 

# 4.12 | Genome Visualisation and Evolutionary Tree Analysis

Whole genome resequencing results were visualised using the IGV tool. To investigate the expansion of GhSCY2D in different organisms, CDS sequences from algae, A. thaliana, rice, tobacco and cotton were obtained through BLAST search with $E$ ‐value $< 1 \mathrm { E }$ −10 (Table S6). All the acquired sequences were aligned using MAFFT with the L‐INS‐i algorithm. The phylogenetic signal was tested by IQ‐TREE 2 (Minh et al. 2020) with 3000 quartets, the TEST model and an ignored tree search (Strimmer and von Haeseler 1997). By utilising ModelFinder (Kalyaanamoorthy et al. 2017), we identified the best‐fitting model $\left( \mathrm { H K Y + F } \right)$ based on the Bayesian Information Criterion. Subsequently, we employed MrBayes v3.2.7a software to conduct three parallel runs, evaluating clade support in the phylogenetic tree using local posterior probabilities. The resulting tree file was then visualised using FigTree v1.4.3 (http://tree.bio.ed.ac.uk/software/figtree/). 

# 4.13 | Identification and Interaction Analysis of the Components of the Sec2 System

The A. thaliana SECA2 (AT1G21650) and SECE2 (AT4G38490) protein sequences were used as queries for searching orthologs in the cotton genome (http://cotton.zju.edu.cn/), which identified GH_A02G1232 (GhSECA2) and GH_D02G2469 (GhSECE2) as the potential components of the Sec2 system in cotton. The protein sequences of GhSCY2D, GhSECE2 and GhSECA2 were subjected to the AlphaFold3 algorithm for prediction of protein spatial structure. The AlphaFold model utilises feature engineering methods to embed the amino acid sequences into a feature space. These features include one‐hot encoding of the amino acid sequences, multi‐ sequence alignment information, shape parameters of template molecular skeletons, and others. Docking algorithms were then employed to find the optimal binding conformation within the search space by using various search algorithms. 

# 4.14 | Protein Interaction Assay

Based on the predicted interaction between GhSECA2 and GhSCY2D, we selected the coding sequence from 1 to 600 bp of 

GhSECA2 for interaction verification (Tables S10 and S11). Subsequently, GhSCY2D‐MYC and GhSECE2‐GFP fused with MYC and GFP tags, respectively, as well as the truncated GhSECA2‐GFP, were cloned into the pBWA(V)H2STMVΩ vector. These constructs were then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 via electroporation for Co‐ IP assay. Tobacco plants approximately 1 month old were selected, and their leaves were injected with the Agrobacterium strains harbouring the genes of interest. After 2 days, 1 g of tobacco leaves was ground, and $6 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of IP Lysis Buffer containing Protease Inhibitor Cocktail $( 1 \times )$ was added. The mixture was vortexed and left to stand on ice for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ (vortexing every 10 min). Following centrifugation at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 15 000 rpm for 10 min, the supernatant was collected and again centrifuged to obtain the total protein, which was stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for future use. In advance, $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of the target antibody was added to protein A/G beads and left to stand for $^ { 1 \mathrm { h } }$ to allow the beads to bind to the antibody. After 1 h, the beads were resuspended in $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of pre‐cooled PBS, centrifuged at 500 rpm for 1 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and this process was repeated three times. The total protein solution was then added to the beads and mixed overnight to allow the antibody to bind to the bait protein. SDS sample buffer $( 4 \times )$ was added to both the Input and IP groups, and the samples were heated in a metal bath at $9 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min before centrifugation at $1 5 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 5 min. The supernatants were collected and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Finally, the obtained samples were subjected to western blot analysis for hybridisation with the antibody. 

GhSCY2D was ligated to the pPR3‐N vector to serve as the Prey protein, while GhSECA2 and GhSCEE2 were ligated to the pBT3‐STE vector to function as the Bait proteins (Table S10). These constructs were then transformed into NMY51 competent cells for yeast two‐hybrid validation. Single colonies grown to 2–3 mm on the double dropout (DDO) plates were picked and suspended in $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $0 . 9 \%$ NaCl solution. The suspensions were vortexed to disperse the cells and then spotted onto DDO, TDO/X and QDO/X solid media plates. The plates were inverted and incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3–5 days until colonies emerged.
