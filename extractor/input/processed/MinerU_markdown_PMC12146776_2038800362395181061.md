# Arabidopsis HOOKLESS1 acts as a histone acetyltransferase to promote cotyledon greening during seedling de-etiolation

Greening immediately after etiolated-seedling’s emergence from the soil is critical for plants to initiate their autotrophic life cycle through photosynthesis. The greening process relies on a complex transcriptional network that fine-tunes the biosynthesis of chlorophyll and prevents premature development of chloroplasts. In this study, we identified the Arabidopsis HOOKLESS1 (HLS1) as a key regulator of light-induced cotyledon greening. Our results demonstrated that HLS1 is essential for the proper expression of greening-related genes controlling chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development. Loss of HLS1 severely disrupts the Pchlide-to-Chlide transition and impairs reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging in etiolated seedlings upon light exposure, leading to catastrophic ROS burst and even photobleaching. Biochemical assays revealed that HLS1 is a histone acetyltransferase mediating the deposition of H3K9ac and H3K27ac marks at multiple greening-related genes, thereby promoting their transcriptional activation. Genetic analysis further confirmed that HLS1’s promotive effect on the greening process is fully dependent on its histone acetyltransferase activity. Moreover, the loss of HLS1 also interrupts the promotive effect of ethylene signaling on the greening process by reducing the binding of ETHYLENE-INSENSITIVE 3 to the promoter region of POR genes, thus inhibiting the activation effect of ethylene signaling on the expression of PORs. Collectively, our study reveals that HLS1 acetylates histones to activate greening-related genes, optimizing chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast development during dark-to-light transition in seedlings.

Efficiently adapting to the ever-changing light environment is a prerequisite for autotrophic plants to survive on the earth ( 1 ). For seedlings germinating in darkness beneath the soil, proper de-etiolation upon initial exposure to light is crucial. This transition encompasses morphological changes at the organ level, including cotyledon opening and inhibition of hypocotyl elongation, as well as metabolic adjustments, marked by cotyledon greening, which signifies the acquisition of photosynthetic capacity ( 2 ). 

Cotyledon greening involves two key processes: chlorophyll biosynthesis and chloroplast maturation ( 3 ). In etiolated seedlings, the immature chloroplast is in the form of an etioplast, and chlorophyll biosynthesis is halted at the stage of protochlorophyllide (Pchlide), which is phototoxic and can generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) upon light irradiation if not efficiently converted to chlorophyllide (Chlide) ( 3 ). NADPH: protochlorophyllide oxidoreductases (PORs) are enzymes responsible for the light-dependent reduction of Pchlide to Chlide, a crucial step in chlorophyll biosynthesis during greening ( 4 ). PORs form higher-order lattices in etioplasts and disassemble into active enzymes upon light exposure. This ensures efficient chlorophyll production when seedlings first encounter light and plays a crucial role in linking chlorophyll biosynthesis to chloroplast membrane biogenesis ( 5 , 6 ). Thus, expression of sufficient PORs and keeping a proper ratio between PORs and Pchlide are crucial for successful greening. 

The light-induced transition from etiolation to de-etiolation involves a complex interplay between photoreceptors, transcription factors, and chromatin-modifying enzymes, which together ensure proper developmental responses to changing light conditions ( 3 , 7 ). Transcriptional regulation lies at the heart of these processes ( 8 ). Numerous transcription factors, such as PHYTOCHROME-INTERACTING FACTORS (PIFs) and ETHYLENE-INSENSITIVE 3/EIN3-LIKE1 (EIN3/EIL1), have been shown to be crucial in promoting cotyledon greening ( 9 , 10 ). PIFs are well-known negative regulators of photomorphogenesis, which are rapidly degraded upon light exposure, thereby allowing for the activation of light-responsive genes ( 11 ). # Significance

Cotyledon greening is a crucial process for seedlings to survive the dark-to-light transition during their soil emergence. While the complex strategies employed by etiolated seedlings to ensure successful greening have been extensively studied, the mechanisms underlying the activation of greening-related genes at the chromatin level remain unclear. In this study, by combining multiple approaches, we identified HOOKLESS1 (HLS1) as a histone acetyltransferase to regulate the proper expression of greening-related genes, which ultimately contribute to the successful cotyledon greening upon light exposure. Our work provides insights into the biological role and biochemical nature of HLS1 and uncovered an uncharacterized linkage between HLS1 and ethylene signaling during light-induced cotyledon greening.

These transcription factors act in concert to regulate the expression of genes involved in chlorophyll biosynthesis, chloroplast development, and other processes essential for cotyledon greening ( 3 , 8 ). 

Moreover, accumulating evidence suggests that epigenetic regulation is also involved in controlling cotyledon greening during de-etiolation, and several epigenetic factors have been implicated in seedling de-etiolation ( 7 , 14 ). For example, HISTONE ACETYLTRANSFERASE OF THE TAFII250 FAMILY 2 (HAF2) and GENERAL CONTROL NON-REPRESSIBLE 5 (GCN5) have been implicated in the de-etiolation process by modulating histone acetylation levels at light-responsive loci ( 15 , 16 ). The histone deacetylase HDA15 has been reported to repress chlorophyll biosynthetic and photosynthetic genes in etiolated seedlings by interacting with PIF3 and modifying the acetylation status of its target loci ( 17 ). However, the specific histone acetyltransferases involved in regulating cotyledon greening during de-etiolation remain unidentified. 

Previous studies have indicated that key regulators of skotomorphogenesis, such as PIFs, play pivotal roles in light-induced cotyledon greening ( 10 ). The Arabidopsis HOOKLESS1 (HLS1) gene encodes a well-known factor required for proper skotomorphogenesis, especially for maintaining the apical hook that protects the meristem during seedling emergence from the soil ( 18 – 20 ). HLS1 is known to function downstream of ethylene and light signaling, contributing to the maintenance of the apical hook through its regulation of differential cell elongation ( 12 , 20 , 21 ). Despite HLS1’s established role in skotomorphogenesis, its potential involvement in cotyledon greening remains unclear. 

In addition to its function in apical hook development, recent research has highlighted HLS1’s roles in various aspects of plant development and stress responses ( 22 ). HLS1 has been implicated in seed germination ( 23 ), thermomorphogenesis ( 24 ), autophagy during carbon starvation ( 25 ), and defense responses to pathogen attack ( 26 ). Initial sequence analysis indicated that HLS1 may possess acetyltransferase activity ( 19 ), and further studies have demonstrated its involvement in regulating histone acetylation, particularly in the context of plant immunity ( 26 ). Moreover, biochemical evidence from recent studies has confirmed that HLS1 acts as an acetyltransferase, capable of acetylating a nonhistone protein target ATG18a, which is crucial for autophagy ( 25 ). These findings provide important insights into the biochemical properties of HLS1 and raise questions about its potential substrates and broader physiological roles in plant development and response to environment. Thus, it remains to be determined whether HLS1 can function as a histone acetyltransferase in a wider context and how this activity contributes to its various physiological functions ( 27 ). 

Here, we demonstrated that HLS1 is a bona fide histone acetyltransferase, and its activity is crucial for the transcriptional activation of greening-related genes and thereby the process of cotyledon greening. We also found that HLS1 is required for the ethylene-mediated promotion of cotyledon greening. These findings provide insights into the integrative regulation of cotyledon greening by light and phytohormones at the chromatin level. 

# Results

HLS1 Promotes the Light-Induced Cotyledon Greening of Etiolated Seedlings. In addition to its essential role for apical hook development, the potential involvement of HLS1 in 

skotomorphogenesis had also been implicated through its identification during a genetic screening of mutants exhibiting deetiolated/constitutive photomorphogenic phenotype in darkness, suggesting that HLS1 may also be important for the transition from skotomorphogenesis to photomorphogenesis (28). To test this possibility, we examined the cotyledon-greening phenotype of etiolated seedlings germinated in the dark for 4 to 6 d followed by exposure to white light for 2 days $( 4 { - } 6 \mathrm { D } + 2 \mathrm { L } )$ ). For the Col-0 wild-type (WT) control, the majority of etiolated seedlings successfully transited to green seedlings, and the greening rate remained $8 6 \%$ when seedlings were grown in the dark for 6 days followed by 2 days’ white light exposure $( 6 \mathrm { D } + 2 \mathrm { L } )$ (Fig. 1 $A$ and $B$ ). In contrast, loss of HLS1 (hls1-1) significantly reduced the greening rate of etiolated seedlings (Fig. 1A). Only $6 0 \%$ of hls1-1 etiolated seedlings turned green under $\mathrm { 4 D } + 2 \mathrm { L }$ condition, and its greening rate further decreased to $1 7 \%$ and $0 \%$ under $\mathrm { 5 D } + 2 \bar { \mathrm { L } }$ and $\bar { 6 } \mathrm { D } + 2 \mathrm { L }$ conditions, respectively (Fig.  1B). The defective cotyledon-greening phenotype of hls1-1 was fully rescued by the transgenic overexpression of HLS1 under a 35S constitutive promoter, designated as HLS1ox/hls1-1 (Fig. 1A). HLS1ox/hls1-1 even exhibited a significant higher greening rate than WT under $6 \mathrm { D } + 2 \mathrm { L }$ condition, reaching to about $9 5 \%$ (Fig. 1B). Consistent with the cotyledon greening phenotype, the total chlorophyll content, as well as the contents of chlorophyll a and chlorophyll b, were markedly lower in hls1-1 than in WT, which were also fully complemented in HLS1ox/hls1-1 (Fig.  1C). These results demonstrate that HLS1 plays an essential role in regulating lightinduced cotyledon greening of etiolated seedlings. 

The disruption in the balance between the levels of Pchlide and the level of PORs in darkness primarily results in greening defects and even photobleaching of etiolated seedlings after they are transferred into light ( 10 , 29 ). To explore the underlying reasons for greening defects of hls1-1 , etiolated seedlings were collected to measure the fluorescence emitting from either Pchlide in darkness or its direct metabolite, Chlide, upon $3 0 ~ s$ of light irradiation. Before light irradiation, hls1-1 seedlings exhibited similar fluorescence intensity of Pchlide than that of WT ( Fig. $1 D \nearrow$ ). However, the level of Chlide in hls1-1 was about $4 0 \%$ to that in WT after 30 s of light irradiation ( Fig. $1 D \nearrow$ ), indicating that the loss of HLS1 compromises the Pchlide-to-Chlide transition. Unsuccessful Pchlide-to-Chlide transition can induce the overaccumulation of ROS during the greening process of etiolated seedlings, which leads to the photobleaching upon light exposure ( 9 ). Thus, we further explored whether ROS burst was involved in the HLS1-regulated greening process by using the ROS indicator $2 ^ { \prime } , 7 ^ { \prime }$ -dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate $\mathrm { ( \bar { H } _ { 2 } D C F D A ) }$ ( 30 ). As expected, hls1-1 exhibited significantly stronger $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { D C F D A }$ fluorescent signals as compared to WT under the $5 \mathrm { D } + 2 \mathrm { L }$ condition ( Fig. 1 E and F ). In contrast, a lower $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { D C F D A }$ fluorescent signal was detected in HLS1ox/hls1-1 under identical conditions ( Fig. 1 $E$ and $F _ { . }$ ). These results indicate that HLS1 plays a critical role in promoting the Pchlide-to-Chlide transition and, consequently, mitigating the cellular toxicity of ROS during the greening process.

In the darkness, the sizes of both mesophyll cells and etioplasts were similar between WT and hls1-1 ( Fig. 1 H and I ). Loss of HLS1 Results in Genome-Wide Histone H3 Acetylation Level Change. Previously, HLS1 has been reported to regulate the level of Histone 3 acetylation (H3Ac) at ABI5 and WRKY33, promoting their expression in response to both abiotic and biotic stress (23, 26). To study how HLS1 regulates greening-related genes and its potential linkage to epigenetic modification at target genes, we analyzed the differential distribution of H3K9ac and H3K27ac between WT and hls1-1 through chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by DNA sequencing (ChIP-seq). In hls1-1, the total H3K9ac and H3K27ac levels were dramatically reduced as compared with the WT control, indicating that HLS1 influences the genome-wide deposition of these histone acetylation marks in vivo (Fig. 3 A and B). To further explore the role of HLS1-regulated deposition of H3K9ac and H3K27ac in regulating gene expression, we analyzed the correlations between the HLS1-dependent H3K9ac/H3K27ac modifications and gene expressions of etiolated seedling (Dataset S3). The expression of 1180 HLS1-dependent H3K9ac/H3K27ac marked genes were activated by HLS1 in 4-d-old etiolated seedlings (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 and Fig. 3C). Among them, numerous greening-related genes, such as GRFs, PORs, LIL2:3, and $C \bar { P } 2 9 a$ , exhibited decreased levels of H3K9ac/H3K27ac modifications in hls1-1 as compared to WT (Fig. $3 D$ ). 

We also used ChIP-qPCR to validate our ChIP-seq results and found that loss of HLS1 strongly decreased the deposition of both H3K9ac and H3K27ac at the regulatory regions of GRFs, PORs, and LIL3:2 ( Fig. $3 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S2 ). To directly assess the effect of HLS1 on the deposition of H3K9ac at the regulatory regions of both PORA and PORB , we generated stable transgenic plants expressing HLS1 under a $\beta$ -estrogen inducible promoter in hls1-1 mutant background (ER8pro::HLS1/hls1-1 ). Compared to the mock control, $\beta$ -estrogen supplement fully rescued the greening defects in 6-d-old etiolated seedlings of ER8pro::HLS1 /hls1-1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 ). Agreeing with the phenotype observation, transient induction of HLS1 gene expression by $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \beta$ -estrogen for $^ \mathrm { ~ 4 ~ h ~ }$ was sufficient to upregulate the expression of PORs ( Fig. 3F ). We also monitored that the induction of HLS1 can rapidly guide the deposition of H3K9ac at the regulatory regions of both PORA and PORB ( Fig. $3 G$ ). These results indicate that HLS1 is required for the proper genome-wide distribution of H3K9ac and H3K27ac in etiolated seedlings, and such epigenetic modifications are closely correlated with its regulation on targeted genes’ expression. 

Numerous studies have demonstrated that both the transcriptional expression of HLS1 and its oligomerization state are significantly regulated during the de-etiolation process ( 12 , 36 , 37 ), suggesting that the activity of HLS1 is dynamically modulated under these conditions. To further elucidate the role of HLS1 in 

regulating histone acetylation during the dark-to-light transition, we performed additional ChIP-seq experiments to compare the genome-wide distribution of H3K27ac between WT and hls1-1 seedlings at the early stages of light exposure. Our analysis revealed that, compared to etiolated seedlings, the number of genes with HLS1-dependent H3K27ac marks was gradually reduced during de-etiolation, suggesting that HLS1-mediated histone acetylation is significantly impaired during de-etiolation (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 and new Dataset S3 ). 

HLS1 Acetylates Histone H3 In  Vitro and Binds to Greening-Related Genes In Vivo. The acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 has recently been well demonstrated by revealing that HLS1 directly interacts with and acetylates ATG18a in responding to starvation in green seedlings (25). To test whether such HLS1-ATG18a module also participates in light-induced cotyledon greening, we observed the greening phenotype of 6-d-old etiolated seedlings and found that atg18a mutant plants exhibited a similar greening rate as compared to WT, indicating that the promotive effect of HLS1 on the greening process of etiolated seedling is independent of ATG18a (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). 

Besides ATG18a, whether HLS1 can also acetylate histones remains unclear ( 26 ). Through an in vitro acetylation assay, we uncovered that the acetylation level of histone H3 was much higher when purified MBP-HLS1 proteins were added in the presence of the acetyl donor acetyl-CoA ( Fig. 4A ), indicating that HLS1 is indeed a histone acetyltransferase. Conversely, MBP– HLS1 (LV), which harbors the V108A and L151A mutations that impair the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 ( 25 ), reduced the acetylation of histone H3 in vitro as compared to the MBP-HLS1 wildtype version ( Fig. 4A ), indicating that these two residual sites are also important for the acetylation of histone H3 by HLS1. Additionally, an in vivo acetylation assay using ER8pro::HLS1/hls1-1 was performed to examine the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 in vivo. In comparison with the mock treatment, induction of HLS1 by 4-h $\beta$ -estrogen treatment induced higher level of H3ac, H3K9ac, and H3K27ac ( Fig. 4B ), indicating that HLS1 is indeed a histone acetyltransferase in vivo. 

Furthermore, we examined whether the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 was essential for the effect of HLS1 on the greening process of etiolated seedlings. In comparison with HLS1ox/hls1-1 , the ratio of green cotyledons in 6-d-old etiolated seedlings of HLS1ox (LV)/hls1-1 is significantly lower ( Fig. 4 $C$ and $D$ ), and these etiolated seedlings also exhibited much stronger $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { D C F D A }$ signals after being irradiated with light ( Fig. 4 $C$ and $E$ ). These results indicate that the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 is essential for its promotive effects on the greening process of etiolated seedlings. 

To further examine whether HLS1 binds to the regulated genes in vivo, we used transgenic plants expressing a complementary hemagglutinin (HA)-tagged HLS1 in hls1-1 mutant background for ChIP-qPCR (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 ) ( 37 ). The results showed that HA-HLS1 is enriched at the regulatory regions of PORA, PORB, GRF2, GRF7, and LIL3:2 in vivo, confirming that these genes are targets of HLS1 ( Fig. 4F ). Taken together, we conclude that HLS1 activates the transcription of greening-related genes by binding to their regulatory regions and acetylating the histone H3 at these gene loci.

It acts as an intersection between external signals and internal pathways, regulating the expression of many genes involved in greening. 

When etiolated seedlings are grown in darkness under soil coverage, the expression of HLS1 is strongly upregulated through a complex regulatory network ( 22 , 38 ). It is well known that HLS1 is a key regulator in controlling the development of the apical hook during the skotomorphogenesis of etiolated seedlings ( 19 ). As a protective structure, the defective development of the apical hook in hls1-1 etiolated seedlings significantly reduces the emergence ratio of the hls1-1 mutant under certain soil coverage conditions, compared to WT ( 39 ). Interestingly, our results further elucidate an additional role of HLS1, where it mitigates cotyledon photobleaching and promotes chlorophyll biosynthesis upon the initial exposure of etiolated seedlings to light. Our transcriptomic analysis ( Fig. 2 and Dataset S1 ) shows that HLS1 activates GRFs , which, in combination with PIFs and BZR1, facilitate POR expression ( 33 ). HLS1 also directly regulates POR transcription by establishing histone acetylation in their regulatory regions. Furthermore, HLS1 upregulates LIL3:2 , which stabilizes POR proteins ( 40 ). Notably, PORs and their regulated Pchlide-to-Chlide transition are critical for both chlorophyll synthesis and PLB organization ( 5 , 41 ). Indeed, the POR mutants severely impair the greening of etiolated seedlings ( 34 ). Beyond chlorophyll synthesis, HLS1 also regulates genes involved in chloroplast development, such as CP29A (chloroplast RNA processing) ( 42 ), ARC1/2 (chloroplast division) ( 43 ), as well as SIGMA factors (SIG1–SIG6) and PEP-associated proteins essential for RNA polymerase activity ( 44 ). These findings suggest that the defective greening in the hls1-1 mutants is due to combined regulation of greening-related genes.

Before soil emergence, etiolated seedlings integrate both environmental stimuli and internal signaling to activate the expression of HLS1, contributing to proper skotomorphogenesis. Beyond ensuring the development of the apical hook, HLS1 functions as a histone acetyltransferase, increasing the level of histone acetylation at greening-related genes to facilitate the light-induced cotyledon greening of etiolated seedlings. HLS1 promotes the expression of various greeningrelated genes, including the transcription factors GRFs, the PORs, genes involved in plastids development, and genes encoding antioxidative enzymes. Loss-offunction of HLS1 leads to a ROS burst and subsequent photobleaching of etiolated seedlings upon light irradiation. During the dark-to-light transition, HLS1- mediated histone acetylation is also crucial for the binding ability of EIN3/EIL1 to its target genes, such as PORs, and required for the promotive effects of ethylene signaling on de-etiolation. Collectively, HLS1 orchestrates the integrative regulation of cotyledon greening by light and ethylene signaling.


Notably, HLS1 has been widely reported to be strongly downregulated and inactivated following light irradiation ( 12 , 20 , 36 , 37 ). However, our analysis reveals that HLS1 also significantly influences light-regulated gene expression during the dark-to-light transition ( Fig. 2 C and $D _ { e }$ ). This finding suggests that HLS1 may function as a darkness-specific component, helping plants adapt to the dark-to-light transition by preparing them in the dark and facilitating rapid adaptation upon light exposure. Previous studies have also highlighted the essential role of HLS1 in helping seedlings adapt to darkness-induced nutrient starvation through the acetylation of nonhistone proteins ( 25 ). Therefore, the biological function of HLS1 in the dark-to-light transition warrants further investigation, including its roles in photoperiod and circadian rhythms. 

HLS1 Is a Bona Fide Histone Acetyltransferase. Although HLS1 was first cloned nearly 30 y ago, researchers continued to work tirelessly to elucidate its biochemical function. Encouragingly, significant progress has been made in recent years. HLS1 has been reported to influence histone H3 acetylation at specific chromatin loci, leading to the transcriptional activation of genes (23, 26, 45, 46), supporting the idea that HLS1 may acetylate histones. However, recombinant HLS1 protein was unable to acetylate histones directly in vitro (26). Nonetheless, the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 is evolutionarily conserved and required for its function in processes such as apical hook development, thermomorphogenesis, and flowering (19, 47). Recently, Huang et al first verified that HLS1 is a bona fide acetyltransferase through 

both in vitro and in vivo acetylation assays, which inspired us to revisit the acetyltransferase activity of HLS1 on histones. 

Our study confirmed that HLS1 functions as a bona fide histone acetyltransferase during the greening process of etiolated seedlings. Previous studies have shown that histone acetyltransferases, such as HAF2, GCN5, and NuA4, are essential for regulating the expression of light-responsive genes ( 16 , 48 , 49 ). However, the specific histone acetyltransferase responsible for the greening process in etiolated seedlings remains unclear. Our finding that HLS1 performs this role fills this gap in knowledge. Future research could investigate the cofactors associated with HLS1-type histone acetyltransferase complexes, or whether HLS1 represents a specialized type of acetyltransferase complex that functions specifically under dark conditions. 

HLS1 Facilitates the Binding of EIN3 to PORs and Enhances the Effect of Ethylene on the Cotyledon Greening Process. Ethylene signaling is essential for plant growth and stress adaptation (50). Previous studies have established the dominant role of EIN3/ EIL1-dependent gene expression regulation in plant ethylene responses (13, 51). Meanwhile, emerging evidence suggests that epigenetic modifications at target gene loci may provide an additional layer of control over ethylene responses (50, 52). Notably, the C-terminal END of EIN2, an essential component of the ethylene signaling pathway, is cleaved and translocated into the nucleus (52–54), where it is directed to specific target genes and interacts with EIN2 nuclear-associated protein 1 (ENAP1) to modulate histone acetylation levels (52–54). Recently, 

ENAP1 was reported to interact with histone deacetylases SRT1/2 to decrease histone acetylation, resulting in ethylenemediated transcriptional repression (55). However, the histone acetyltransferases responsible for ethylene-mediated transcriptional activation remain unidentified. In this study, we found that HLS1 mediates ethylene-induced promotion of cotyledon greening by facilitating EIN3 binding to greening-related genes through histone acetylation (Fig. 5). 

Previous studies indicated that EIN3/EIL1-mediated regulation of the greening process by ethylene can be attributed to three main mechanisms: First, EIN3/EIL1 inhibit the expression of genes involved in tetrapyrrole biosynthesis, reducing Pchlide accumulation in etiolated seedlings ( 56 ). Second, EIN3/EIL1 directly binds to the E-box cis-elements of PORA and PORB , activating their transcription and ensuring the Pchlide-to-Chlide transition upon light irradiation ( 10 ). Third, EIN3/EIL1 repress the expression of genes encoding light-harvesting complexes (LHCs), preventing premature chloroplast maturation ( 57 ). Our study found that the loss of HLS1 severely impaired the ethylene-induced transcriptional activation of PORs , while having limited effects on the inhibition of LHCs and genes involved in tetrapyrrole biosynthesis. These observations suggest that EIN3 and HLS1 have overlapping yet distinct roles during seedling greening. Understanding the mechanisms behind their specificity in regulating greening-related genes will be an interesting direction for future research.
