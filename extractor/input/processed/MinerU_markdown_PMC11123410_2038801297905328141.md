# Summary

Muscat flavour represents a group of unique aromatic attributes in some grape varieties. 

Biochemically, grape berries with muscat flavour produce high levels of monoterpenes. 

Monoterpene biosynthesis is mainly through the DOXP/MEP pathway, and VvDXS1 encodes the first enzyme in this plastidial pathway of terpene biosynthesis in grapevine. A single-point mutation resulting in the substitution of a lysine with an asparagine at position 284 in the VvDXS1 protein has previously been identified as the major cause for producing muscat flavour in grapes. In this study, the same substitution in the VvDXS1 protein was successfully created through prime editing in the table grape Vitis vinifera cv. ‘Scarlet Royal’. The targeted point mutation was detected in most of the transgenic vines, with varying editing efficiencies. No unintended mutations were detected in the edited alleles, either by PCR Sanger sequencing or by amplicon sequencing. More than a dozen edited vines were identified with an editing efficiency of more than $50 \%$ , indicating that these vines were likely derived from single cells in which one allele was edited. These vines had much higher levels of monoterpenes in their leaves than the control, similar to what was found in leaf samples between field-grown muscat and non-muscat grapes. 

# Introduction

Remarkable progress has been made in the past few years by utilizing clustered regularly interspersed short palindromic repeats/CRISPR-associated (CRISPR/Cas)-mediated genome editing for trait development in grapevine (Vitis spp.) (Clemens et al., 2022; Iocco-Corena et al., 2021; Li, Li, et al., 2020; Li, Jiao, et al., 2020; Najafi et al., 2023; Nakajima et al., 2017, 2023; Olivares et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2016, 2020; Ren et al., 2019; Scintilla et al., 2022; Sunitha and Rock, 2020; Tu et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2022). These reports have primarily focused on using CRISPR/Cas genome editing to disable or restore gene function by creating insertion or deletion (indel) mutations. The CRISPR/- Cas9 editing system relies on single-guide RNA (sgRNA) to direct the endonuclease, Cas9, to a targeted genomic location by using a 20-bp spacer sequence. Once the target DNA is found, Cas9 helicase activity unwinds the target DNA sequence, activating the RuvC and HNH nuclease domains to cleave the genomic target 3 bps upstream of the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) 50 -NGG-30 . This results in the formation of a DNA double-strand break that is repaired by the cell’s endogenous DNA repair mechanisms (Adli, 2018). 

The two main DNA repair pathways are non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) and homology-directed repair (HDR). The NHEJ repair pathway is the predominant DNA repair pathway in eukaryotes and commonly results in imprecise DNA repair, leading to the generation of indels causing frameshift mutations or forming premature stop codons in coding sequences and knocking out gene function (Lieber, 2010). The HDR DNA repair 

pathway, conversely, is a precise repair pathway that uses homologous sequences to facilitate precise DNA repair, albeit occurring at low frequencies (Adli, 2018). Exploiting the HDR pathway is often challenging due to the low editing efficiency and the requirements of packaging and supplying a donor template. However, many trait modifications require precise nucleotide changes where NHEJ and HDR strategies are not suitable to achieve the desired outcome. 

Recently, two new CRISPR/Cas-mediated editing systems, base editing and prime editing, have been developed that subvert the NHEJ and HDR repair pathways by exploiting alternative repair mechanisms. Both base editing and prime editing enable precise editing of a DNA sequence without introducing double-stranded breaks. Base editing relies on using a Cas9 nickase fused to a deaminase, either cytosine deaminase (CBE) or adenine deaminase (ABE), to deaminate the targeted base, converting C-T and A-G, respectively, and has been applied to a number of plant species with impressive successes (Molla et al., 2021; Verma et al., 2023). A successful attempt using CBE was recently made in grapevine targeting tonoplastic monosaccharide transporter genes and GAI1 (Ren et al., 2022). 

Prime editing (PE), on the other hand, is a newer technique that uses a prime editor, a fusion of Cas9 nickase and a reverse transcriptase. PE also employs a special gRNA called the PE guide RNA (pegRNA). The PE strategy relies on an RNA donor template embedded in the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end of the pegRNA containing the intended nucleotide changes. The reverse transcriptase fused to nCas9 is able to repair the nicked target DNA sequence by using the RNA repair template of the pegRNA to precisely incorporate desired nucleotide changes to the genomic target and offers even greater 

precision and flexibility in editing a gene than base editing and HDR (Anzalone et al., 2019). Successes have been reported in using PE for modifying genes in plant species, but the editing efficiency was quite low with the early versions of prime editors (Jiang et al., 2020; Li, Li, et al., 2020; Li, Ma, et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2021; Perroud et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020). Extensive efforts have been made to improve various components of PE for enhancing editing efficiency in both mammalian and plant systems (Chen and Liu, 2023). These efforts include the uses of paired pegRNAs with optimized prime binding sites targeting the same mutation (Lin et al., 2021), a more stable pegRNA with an addition of a structured RNA motif such as evopreQ1, MS2 or Cys4 (Feng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Nelson et al., 2022) or a modified pegRNA with introduced same-sense mutations in the RT template or apegRNA with a more stable secondary structure (Chen et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022), an engineered plant prime editor (ePPE) with a modified reverse transcriptase (deletion of its ribonuclease H domain and incorporation of a viral nucleocapsid protein with nucleic acid chaperone activity) (Zong et al., 2022) or ePPEplus with a V223A substitution in the reverse transcriptase (Ni et al., 2023), an improved prime editor, PEmax, with improved Cas9 nuclease activity (Chen et al., 2021), and the introduction of a dominant negative form of MLH (MLH1dn), a specific DNA mismatch repair gene (Chen et al., 2021), or co-introduction of an NHEJ-restraining, 53BP1-inhibitory ubiquitin variant (Li, Ding et al., 2023, Li, Zhang et al., 2023). Improvements were also reported with fusing T5 exonuclease to the N-terminus of PE (Liang et al., 2023), or by adding a Rad51 ssDNA binding protein domain between the Cas9 H840A nickase and the reverse transcriptase (Song et al., 2021), and by splitting the Cas nickase and the reverse transcriptase with optimized reverse transcriptase (Grunewald€ et al., 2023). Some of these PE improvements have been adapted for gene editing in a few plant species, including wheat, rice, maize, and moss (Gupta et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022; Li, Ding et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023; Perroud et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2023). 

PE has not been previously reported in grapevine or other woody species. In this report, we describe a success in using PE to modify a grape aroma gene, VvDXS1, for producing high-level monoterpenes conferring muscat flavour in the table grape Vitis vinifera cv. Scarlet Royal. Scarlet Royal is a red seedless table grape widely grown in California, USA, and it has large, wellcoloured, attractive berries with firm, meaty flesh and a sweet and neutral flavour (Hashim-Buckey and Ramming, 2008). Adding muscat flavour to this neutral-flavour cultivar could make it even more appealing to consumers. Muscat flavour represents a group of unique aromatic attributes found in some wine and table grapes. Biochemically, grape berries with muscat flavour, compared with those without, produce much higher levels of monoterpenes, including linalool, geraniol, citronellol, nerol, and a-terpineol, which give the fruit a distinctive floral aroma. Besides berries, monoterpenes can be detected in other grape tissues, such as leaves, tendrils, and flowers (Gunata et al., 1986; Luan and Wust,€ 2002; Matarese et al., 2014). In both grape berries and leaves, monoterpene biosynthesis is mainly through the mevalonate-independent 1-deoxy-d-xylulose 5-phosphate/2Cmethyl-d-erythritol 4-phosphate (DOXP/MEP) pathway (Luan and Wust,€ 2002). VvDXS1 encodes the first enzyme, 1-deoxy-D-xylulose 5-phosphate synthase 1, for this plastidial pathway of terpene biosynthesis in grapevine. A single-point 

mutation in this gene has been identified as the major cause of muscat flavour in grapes, both genetically and biochemically (Battilana et al., 2009, 2011; Dalla Costa et al., 2018; Duch^ene et al., 2009; Emanuelli et al., 2010). This point mutation leads to substitution of a lysine with an asparagine at position 284 in the VvDXS1 protein and a higher catalytic efficiency of the VvDXS1 enzyme (Battilana et al., 2011) and is responsible for muscat flavour in $9 5 \%$ of all the tested muscat-flavoured grape cultivars (Emanuelli et al., 2010). It was further demonstrated that a transgenic microvine with this muscat VvDXS1 allele (noted as $V v D X S \boldsymbol { \mathcal { I } } ^ { m }$ thereafter) produced a higher level of monoterpenes in fruits (Dalla Costa et al., 2018). The desired G-T or G-C changes to create the muscat $V v D X S \boldsymbol { \mathcal { I } } ^ { m }$ allele are not feasible through current base editors (Chen and Liu, 2023). In this study, the PE construct designs utilized several recent PE improvements, including PEmax, dual epegRNAs (pegRNAtevopreQ ), and MLH1dn, to create an edited mutant $V v D X S \boldsymbol { 1 } ^ { e }$ allele mimicking the natural muscat-producing $V v D X S \boldsymbol { \mathcal { I } } ^ { m }$ . More than 1000 transgenic ‘Scarlet Royal’ vines were examined in this study, and the desired point mutations were detected in most vines with various editing efficiencies. To our surprise, no unintended mutations such as indels were detected in either the edited alleles or non-edited alleles by PCR sequencing or amplicon sequencing. We identified more than a dozen vines with an editing efficiency of more than $50 \%$ , indicating that these vines were derived from single cells in which one allele was likely edited. Furthermore, these vines produced significantly higher levels of various monoterpenes in their leaves than the control vines. 

# Results

# Design of the prime editing constructs

Most muscat grape cultivars have asparagine (Asn) at position 284 (Asn284, codon AAT) in the VvDXS1 protein, while nonmuscat cultivars have lysine (Lys) (Lys284, codon AAG) (Emanuelli et al., 2010). We sequenced the 4.7-kb DXS1 genomic region in ‘Scarlet Royal’ from the “ATG” start codon to the “TGA” stop codon and confirmed that Lys284 was coded by “AAG” as the reference genome (Data S1). We designed PE constructs with the spacer sequence (gatcacaagt aatactaacct or agaattacgagaggttgcca) to change AAG into AAC for converting Lys284 (AAG) to Asn284 (AAC). We chose “AAC” to code for Asn284 so that an AclI restriction enzyme (AACGTT) site can be introduced for the engineered Muscat allele $( V V D X S \mathcal { I } ^ { e } )$ (Figure 1a,b). 

To enhance PE efficiency, we incorporated a few improvements to the editing components into our construction designs. Firstly, we used paired pegRNAs targeting the same mutation (Lin et al., 2021), and these two pegRNAs were expressed as polycistronic pegRNAs separated by tRNAs (Figure 1b–d) (Xie et al., 2015). Secondly, we added a structured RNA motif, trimmed evopreQ1 (tevopreQ1), to stabilize the pegRNAs (Figure 1c) (Nelson et al., 2022). Thirdly, we used the improved prime editor, PEmax, with improved Cas9 nuclease activity (Figure 1d). Finally, we overexpressed a dominant negative grape form of MLH1 (35S:VvMLHdn), a component of the mismatch repair system, in the construct PD2 (Figure 1d) (Chen et al., 2021). In the PD1 construct, we had a 35S:eGFP cassette instead of 35S:VvMLHdn to monitor the transformation process (Figure 1d).

# Generation and characterization of transgenic vines

PD1 and PD2 were introduced into the embryogenic callus of ‘Scarlet Royal’ via agrobacterium-mediated transformation. The transformed callus was plated on MDM plates, which had no added plant hormones but were supplemented with hygromycin for positive selection of transgenic tissue and timentin for eliminating agrobacterium. Embryos started to appear on MDM plates about 50 days after transformation. Embryos derived from the construct PD1 transformation showed fluorescent signals, suggesting that these embryos were transgenic. Germinated embryos were transferred to rooting medium plates with hygromycin and developed into plantlets under light conditions in 2–4 weeks. The plantlets were transferred into Magenta boxes with the same rooting media supplemented with $5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ hygromycin and cultured under a 16 h light/8 h dark condition. In about one month after the transfer, the transgenic plants were 

$2 { \cdot } 5 \ \mathsf { c m }$ tall with sizable leaves for sampling. A leaf sample was collected for each individual vine in a sterile condition. Genomic DNA was isolated from the leaf samples and used for the evaluation of the prime editing efficiency of the target gene. 

We regenerated and screened 465 PD1 and 688 PD2 transgenic vines through AclI restriction enzyme digestion of the target PCR products amplified from leaf genomic DNAs (Table 1 and Figure 2). This screening strategy was based on the design that a prime-edited $V v D X S \boldsymbol { 1 } ^ { e }$ allele should contain an AclI restriction site in the targeted sequence (AAGgtt to AACgtt) (Figure 1), and two smaller bands from the PCR amplicon of the edited allele would be expected after AclI digestion. In contrast, if the intended editing was not successful, the PCR amplicon would remain intact without being cut by AclI.

# PE efficiency

We amplified and sequenced amplicons of the target sequence region from $2 4 ~ P D 1$ vines and 38 PD2 vines to quantify their PE efficiencies. These vines included those showing high editing efficiencies and some with little evidence of editing based on their AclI digestion patterns and Sanger sequencing profiles (Table 1, Table S2). Editing efficiencies for individual vine samples were determined by the PE-analyser based on their amplicon library sequencing reads (Hwang et al., 2021). They varied from $2 . 7 \%$ to $8 6 . 3 \%$ (Table S2). Reads with indels were very few in all transgenic samples investigated and comparable to what were found in the control samples, further supporting that PE for this target is a precise process (Table S2). Vine D1P84, described above, had an editing efficiency of approximately $8 6 \%$ . For samples with similar G/C peaks at the PE target site revealed from the Sanger sequencing chromatograms, the PE efficiency was very close to $50 \%$ with a range of $4 6 \% - 5 3 \%$ (Figure 2, Table S2), further validating the screening method of AclI digestion of genomic PCR products in coupling with Sanger sequencing. On the other hand, some samples, which had no visible smaller bands from AclI digestion and no $" { \mathsf { C } } "$ peak on the Sanger sequencing chromatogram, were found to have some targeted $\mathsf { G } \to \mathsf { C }$ editing by amplicon sequencing, but the editing efficiencies were very low. 

Overall, the editing efficiencies for PD1 and PD2 transgenic vines were low. Amplicon sequencing of two independent 

transgenic callus pools transformed with PD1 showed editing efficiencies of $4 . 5 \%$ and $7 . 0 5 \%$ (Table S2). Among the 465 PD1 transgenic vines, one vine, PD1P84, had a very high PE efficiency $( \sim 8 6 \% )$ and two vines had editing efficiencies over $45 \%$ (Table 1, Table S2). Together, they accounted for about $0 . 6 5 \%$ of PD1 vines. For the 688 PD2 transgenic vines evaluated, 16 vines (or $2 . 3 2 \%$ ) had editing efficiencies between $45 \%$ and $7 5 \%$ (Table 1). In general, more PD2 vines exhibited high PE efficiencies than PD1 vines (Figure 2, Table 1, Table S2). Considering potential sampling and technical variations often observed in determining editing efficiencies, those PD1 and PD2 vines with editing efficiencies of $45 \%$ or more most likely had one VvDXS1 allele edited at the single cell stage. 

Because no single transgenic vine was found to have $100 \%$ editing efficiency, continued editing would be expected to take place in some cells of the transgenic vines. To investigate how the proportion of cells with edited $V v D X S 1 ^ { e }$ would change in a transgenic vine, we clonally propagated two or more vines in tissue culture from each of 14 original transgenic vines with relatively high editing efficiencies and estimated the editing efficiencies of the clonally propagated vines through amplicon sequencing (Table 2). As anticipated, most derived clones had similar or higher editing efficiencies than the original transgenic vines. For example, for PD1P8 and PD1P222, while the mother vines had an editing efficiency of $2 7 \%$ and $3 5 . 4 \%$ , respectively, the editing efficiencies for the two clonal vines reached over $50 \%$ . Interestingly, some exceptions were observed. One clonal vine of PD2P561 showed a lower editing efficiency $( 3 2 . 8 1 \% )$ ) than the mother vine $( 4 9 . 6 0 \% )$ ) (Table 2). Similarly, one clonal vine, PD1P84.3 from the PD1P84 line, had much lower editing efficiency $( 5 1 . 0 5 \% )$ ) than the original vine $( 8 6 . 3 0 \% )$ ), while some clonal vines had more than $9 7 \%$ (Table 2). These exceptions could be a result of sampling and/or technical variations, but chimerism in the original mother vine and/or continuing editing in the current vine could also be a possible explanation.

# Content of monoterpenes was enhanced in the edited vines

The content of 12 monoterpenes was measured in a total of 68 leaf samples, including 12 from 6 field-grown muscat and nonmuscat grape cultivars, 47 greenhouse-grown edited vines from 11 transgenic lines, and 9 unedited control vines (Table S4). As expected, several major monoterpenes responsible for muscat flavour, including $\beta$ -myrcene, linalool, citronellol, and geraniol, were found in amounts several-fold higher in the vines with edited $V v D X S 1 ^ { e }$ alleles than the control (Figure 4a, Table S4). On average, peak areas were found to be approximately 3 and 4 times higher for $\beta$ -myrcene and linalool in edited vines than the control, respectively, which was similar to the folds of changes between the field-grown muscat and non-muscat grapes 

(Figure 4a). For geraniol, more than 6 folds of changes were found between edited and non-edited vines, while close to 4 folds of changes were found between field muscat and nonmuscat grapes. The edited line PD1P84 with the highest editing frequency, as expected, produced notably higher folds of change than the changes in the other edited lines for $\beta$ -myrcene, limonene, linalool, and geraniol (Figure 4a, Table S4). On the other hand, while there was no significant difference between field-grown muscat and non-muscat grapes for citronellol, the edited vines produced significantly more citronellol than the unedited vines in the greenhouse (Figure 4a). Interestingly, two other monoterpenes, neric acid and citral, were observed in most muscat grape cultivars and edited vines, but rarely in nonmuscat grape cultivars and unedited control vines (Table S4). 

Because VvDXS1 is primarily involved in terpene biosynthesis, no significant impact on vine development and growth was expected due to the edited change. Indeed, no obvious phenotypic difference was observed at both young and mature vine stages between edited and unedited vines (Figure 4b). This is consistent with the fact that natural muscat grapes show no apparent phenotypic deficiency compared with non-muscat grapes in the field. 

# Discussion

Because a single nucleotide needs to be changed $\mathsf { G } \to \mathsf { T }$ or C) for engineering a muscat $W D X S \mathcal { I }$ allele and $\mathsf { G } \to \mathsf { T }$ or C change is not an ideal target for base editing, PE was selected for this objective. We conducted an initial study using a prime construct that was based on the first-generation PE3 system carrying single pegRNA, a nick sgRNA, and the first-generation prime editor (Anzalone et al., 2019; Li, Li, et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020). 

We did not detect any editing above negative controls in either stable transgenic callus or transgenic vines by amplicon sequencing (data not shown). Subsequently, we incorporated several recent developments in PE into the design of the PD1 and PD2 constructs in this study. The construct design improvement included the use of PEmax, dual epegRNAs (pegRNA-tevopreQ1), and MLH1dn (Chen et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021; Nelson et al., 2022). This effort resulted in the successful editing of VvDXS1 as described. In this study, PD2 produced a better editing efficiency than PD1, and one major difference was the presence of 35S:MLHdn in PD2, suggesting that the 35S:MLHdn component is helpful (Table 1, Figure 1). Recently, remarkable improvements in PE efficiencies were reported in maize and rice by incorporating similar advancements, including the use of epegRNA, PEmax, and MLHdn, except using dual pegRNAs, as we adopted in this study (Gupta et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022; Qiao et al., 2023). Interestingly, MLHdn did not appear to make much difference in PE efficiency in rice protoplasts but had higher efficiency in producing homozygous mutations in transgenic plants (Jiang et al., 2022). Furthermore, the MLH1dn component did not introduce extra random mutations as revealed by whole genome sequencing (Gupta et al., 2023). 

In this study, we observed that PE of the VvDXS1 gene was very specific, without many indels or unintended point mutations in the target region. This is consistent with the findings of low offtarget changes by PE in other systems (Chen and Liu, 2023; Jin et al., 2021). Such high fidelity is promising for precise modification of target genes for trait improvement in plants. In this study, $< 2 . 3 \%$ of transgenic lines were observed to have an editing efficiency above $45 \%$ , which was used as an indicator for at least one allele being edited in an edited plant (Qiao et al., 2023). In consideration of the fact that hygromycin selection was applied, the overall editing efficiency was not high. By contrast, in rice, a similar PE5max system yielded $4 7 . 2 \%$ (for knock-in of a 30- bp element), $8 8 . 5 \%$ (for a 2-bp change), and close to $2 8 \%$ (3-bp change) lines with desired edits in at least one allele in the T0 generation (Gupta et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). In maize, the same PE5max system produced $9 . 6 \% - 2 9 . 1 \%$ of plants with an editing efficiency over $45 \%$ (Qiao et al., 2023). In moss, using epegRNAs alone with the first-generation prime editor could improve PE efficiency dramatically (3 to 143 times improvement) (Perroud et al., 2023). Such a large discrepancy in editing efficiencies is most likely due to the fact that different organisms respond differently to PE (Biswas et al., 2022; Tiwari et al., 2023). Nevertheless, our work represented the first known report of successfully using PE for precisely editing a gene in grapevine and woody species. To our knowledge, the only reported PE study in dicotyledon species was conducted in tomato using the PE3 system with PE codon optimization and improved PE expression (Lu et al., 2021). They identified one or two edited plants for two out of seven targeted sites. However, no phenotype was detected, likely due to chimerism. The PE efficiency in grapevine could be further improved by adapting more improvements into construct designs, such as enhanced expression of epegRNAs (Jiang et al., 2022), introduction of silent mutations in the RT portion of the pegRNAs (Li et al., 2022; Perroud et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022), optimization of the multiplexing strategy to efficiently expressing multiple epegRNAs (Jiang et al., 2022; Ni et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2023), culture of the transformed embryogenic callus at higher temperature (Gupta et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2022), adding nicotinamide, a histone deacetylase inhibitor, in the transfection/transformation medium (Liu et al., 

2022; Wang et al., 2023), and using a newer generation of PE with improved RT activity (Ni et al., 2023). 

Because of the long juvenile process in grapevines, fruit samples from the edited vines are not available soon for muscat flavour evaluation. However, many monoterpenes responsible for muscat flavour are also produced in leaf and other tissues in grapevines (Matarese et al., 2014), providing an opportunity to prove the functionality of the edited $V v D X S 1 ^ { e }$ allele. We analysed monoterpenes from leaf samples of field-grown muscat and nonmuscat grape cultivars and determined that muscat grape cultivars produced several folds more monoterpenes in their leaves than that in non-muscat cultivars, including muscat-related monoterpenes found in ripen grape berries such as geraniol, linalool, citronellol, and $\beta$ -myrcene. As expected, we found significant increases in monoterpenes in the edited vines compared with the control. Among the promising lines we generated in this study, PD1P84 showed an editing efficiency of about $8 6 \%$ , and some of its derived clonal cuttings had edited efficiencies close to $100 \%$ and, as expected, produced higher levels of various monoterpenes than other edited vines (Table S4). 

Chimerism is usually observed in edited plants due to the low chance of having a target allele edited at the single embryogenic cell stage, including grapevine (Song et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022). One possible scenario is that neither of the two alleles of a target gene was edited at the single cell stage, and both unedited alleles could be subject to continuing editing as the transformed cell with an editing construct propagates. An edited plant from such a cell would expect to have variable levels of editing chimerism in its tissues. This was likely what took place in those edited lines, which had editing efficiencies much lower than $50 \%$ in this study. However, in PD1P84 and several other edited lines that had editing efficiencies close to $50 \%$ or higher, one VvDXS1 allele was most likely edited at the single cell stage before the embryo stage, and the other unedited allele was continuously edited in some parts of the edited plants. Because of the lack of polymorphisms in VvDXS1 in ‘Scarlet Royal’ (Data S1), we couldn’t distinguish a VvDXS1 allele that was edited at the single cell stage from the one edited at a later vine developmental stage. Nevertheless, clonally propagated vines from PD1P84 showed a progressive increase in the overall editing efficiencies (some close to $9 8 \%$ ) (Table 2), which supports the explanation. Continuing propagation and selection of clones from this PD1P84 line may allow us to create a new vine with both VvDXS1 alleles stably and homogenously edited. Chimerism in edited plants could potentially be reduced by enhancing editing efficiencies at the single cell stage through the use of optimized editing constructs and tissue culture protocols. 

Demonstration of the feasibility of precisely editing a VvDXS1 allele opens many exciting opportunities for improving the muscat flavour attribute in grape cultivars. For example, most muscat varieties are heterozygous for the muscat allele (Emanuelli et al., 2010), and we could enhance their muscat flavour by converting the non-muscat allele into a muscat one. Similarly, we could edit non-muscat wine and table grapes to make them muscat-flavoured, a desired trait for some markets. Likewise, the technology could be applied to modifications of other genes given the versatility of the PE (Anzalone et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2023; Li, Ding et al., 2023). In woody species, the application of PE technology has not been widely adapted. The PE work presented in this study was based on stably expressing editing components. While GMO concerns associated with 

genome editing could be resolved by segregating out the transgenic components through conventional crosses, a significant challenge remains when there is a desire to add muscat flavour to an existing elite grape for which the genetic makeup should otherwise be entirely maintained. There is extensive ongoing effort in developing transgene-free editing systems, and one such system is to enrich transiently edited target genes using co-editing genes such as ALS, MAR1, and PAR1 as selection markers (Huang et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Rinne et al., 2021). Because most transgene-free editing systems rely on transient editing, further optimization of various editing components for enhancing editing efficiencies continues to be a key area for future research. 

# Material and methods

# PE constructs

We made our version of PEmax with the rice codon-optimized PE (pYPQ166-OsPE2, Addgene 141 080). We introduced two mutations (R221K and N394K) by synthesis of the fragment with these two codon changes between SgrDI and NheI sites (~840 bp) and replaced the corresponding fragment in pYPQ166-OsPE2 to create pYY166-OsPEmax. Our PEmax differs from the published PEmax in lacking the linker and the extra NLSs at the C-terminal (Chen et al., 2021). 

For the design of pegRNAs, we used the web app developed by Lin et al., 2021 (http://www. plantgenomeediting.net/) to determine desirable paired pegRNA options, spacer sequence choices, length of PBS, and the RT template. To assist downstream characterization of transgenic callus and vines, we introduced an AclI restriction site into the engineered muscat allele (VvDXS1e ) (GCCAAGgttagta to GCCAACgttagta). For the design of a small guide RNA scaffold, we used the “flip and extension” sgRNA scaffold with improved Cas9 binding and editing efficiencies (Chen et al., 2013). Furthermore, we added a tevopreQ1 sequence (CGCGGTTCTATCTAGTTACGC GTTAAACCAACTAGAA) to the end of each pegRNA to prevent degradation and improve editing efficiency (Nelson et al., 2022). To express multiple pegRNAs under one promoter, we adopted the polycistronic tRNA-gRNA system (Xie et al., 2015). The tRNAepegRNA1-tRNA-epegRNA2 sequences were synthesized by GenScript with cloning sites (BamHI/KpnI) at their ends so that the fragments could be cloned into the gateway entry vector pYPQ141B-RZ-Lb. To further optimize pegRNA expression, we replaced the AtU3 promoter with the grape U6.2 promoter (Ren et al., 2021). 

To further improve the PE system in grapes, we introduced a dominant negative form of a DNA mismatch repair enzyme (MLH1dn) into our construct design (Chen et al., 2021). A blast search of the human MLH1 sequence in the grape genome retrieved a gene with a coded protein sequence of $40 \%$ similarity (LOC100854779). We deleted the last three conserved amino acids, which are catalytically important and highly conserved for MLH1 across species, synthesized the corresponding DNA fragment, and placed it under a 35S promoter (35S:VvMLHdn) with flanking sequences complimentary to the pMDC32-SpeI site. This 35S:VvMLHdn was cloned into the gateway binary vector pMDC32, cut by SpeI via HiFi assembly, to create pMDC32-35S: MLMdn (pMMdn). Meanwhile, we also replaced the 35S promoter in pMMdn with the grape Ubi2 promoter to improve PE expression (Ren et al., 2021) (pUMdn). To assist visualization of transformation efficiency in callus and plants, we had a 35S:eGFP cassette inserted 

in the pMDC32 binary vector (pMGF ) (Figure 1). The final constructs were built by gateway assembly with a binary vector (pMGF or pUMdn), an entry vector with PEmax (pYY166-PEmax), and an entry vector for the polycistronic tRNA-pegRNAs (141BU6- epegRNAs) to form PE constructs PD1 and PD2 (Figure 1, Data S1). 

# Callus transformation, generation, and cultivation of transgenic vines

Embryogenic callus was induced from the immature inflorescence of ‘Scarlet Royal’ following a published protocol (Perl et al., 1995). We followed published procedures for Agrobacterium transformation and callus selection with some modification as described below (Yang et al., 2022). Liquid callus culture was used for transformation in this study. Embryogenic ‘Scarlet Royal’ calli $( { \sim } 1 \ 9 )$ were transferred from solid CIM plates to a 500-ml flask with 100 mL gCSM liquid media $2 . 4 3 ~ 9$ MS (M519), 0.25 g Casein, 30 g sucrose, 0.1 mg 6-BAP, 1.1 mg 2,4-D, 7.5 mL glycerol per litre, $\mathsf { p H } 5 . 8 \mathsf { , }$ ) one month before transformation. The liquid media was changed weekly and may be divided into two flasks if the callus mass gets too dense. Right before agrobacterium-mediated transformation, the callus in liquid media was divided and transferred into multiple 125 -mL flasks with fresh gCSM media plus $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone (gCSM-AS). The amount of agrobacterium suspension used for each transformation was calculated, so the final OD600 for the agrobacterium in the callus mixture was about 0.2. Once the desired amount of agro-suspension was added to a flask containing the callus gCSM-AS for a final OD600 of 0.2, the agro-callus mixture was shaken at $2 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ~20 min. After Agrobacterium inoculation, we followed the previously published procedures (Yang et al., 2022) for co-incubation and washing. The washed callus was plated on MDM plates (4.43 g MS, $3 0 ~ 9$ sucrose, pH5.8, and 8 g TC agar (A175)) supplemented with $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ Timentin. After one week at $2 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , the callus was then transferred to MDM supplemented with $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ Timentin and $5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ Hygromycin plates (MDM-150 T-5H). In 21–25 days, callus was transferred to fresh MDM-150 T-5H plates. After another 3 weeks, some embryos started to appear. For callus transformed with the PD1 construct, which contained a 35S:eGFP cassette, most embryos were fluorescent under blue light, suggesting that the embryos were transgenic. More embryos were detected in the next 20–30 days. Embryos were transferred to plates containing germination/rooting medium supplemented with $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m g / L } }$ Timentin and 5 mg/L hygromycin (4.43 g MS; 15 g sucrose; 1 g active charcoal; 0.2 mg Indole-3-Butyric Acid (K-IBA, I560); pH 5.8; 7 g/L agar (A111)). The plates were placed under 16 h light and 8 h dark conditions. Two to four weeks later, the green plantlets were transferred to Magenta boxes with the same rooting medium supplemented with $5 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ hygromycin. 

For vines showing high editing efficiencies (see below for methodology), 2–3 clonal vines were propagated from the original vine as cuttings and rooted in rooting medium in a Magenta box. The clonally propagated vines were potted in soil in 4-inch pots after a decent root system was developed. The newly potted vines were covered with plastic bags for 1–2 weeks to acclimate the transition from a Magenta box to a growth chamber. The vines were transferred to one-gallon pots after about one month. Vines in one-gallon pots were put in a greenhouse with a cycle of $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light and $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ dark at $2 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . 

# Estimation of PE efficiencies

Leaf samples were collected from individual transgenic vines in Magenta boxes, and their genomic DNA was extracted with a 

Qiagen DNeasy kit. To amplify the genomic region of $D X S \mathcal { 1 }$ covering the PE target region, genomic PCR was carried out with the primer pairs 1529 and 1530 (662 bp amplicon) (Figure 1, Table S1) using $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of genomic DNA as a template with GoTaq DNA polymerase (Promega) in a $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ reaction (started with $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles of $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 s, $5 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 s and $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $4 5 \ 5 ,$ ended with $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min, and hold at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ). 5 lL PCR product was digested by AclI in a $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ reaction (5 lL PCR product, 0.5 lL $1 0 \times$ SmartCut buffer, $0 . 3 ~ \mu \iota$ AclI (NEB, R0598L), and $4 . 2 ~ \mu \ L \ \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } )$ at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The PCR product digested with AclI was examined on a $1 . 2 \%$ agarose gel. The 662 bp genomic DNA with the expected prime edited $V v D X S \boldsymbol { 1 } ^ { e }$ allele (AAGgtt AACgtt) would be cut into two smaller fragments (366 bp and 296 bp). The relative PE efficiency was assessed by the relative portion of the smaller fragments (366 bp and 296 bp) to the undigested larger fragment (662 bp). PCR samples with promising AclI digestion patterns were purified and sequenced with primer 1529 to examine the chromatographic peak patterns at the PE target locus. 

We selected and examined a few dozens of the transgenic vines for their editing efficiencies in the targeted VvDXS1 region through amplicon sequencing. While most of the selected vines showed clear band patterns of successful PCR-AclI digestion, we also included some vines that didn’t have such promising AclI digestion. Amplicon libraries covering the PE target were prepared using the primer pairs 1582F and 1515R (246 bp amplicon) (Figure 1, Table S1). The amplicon sequencing data were analysed by the PE-analyser (Hwang et al., 2021) (http://www.rgenome.net/pe-analyzer/). In the analysis, the 246- bp genomic sequence was used as the reference, and “AGAAT-TACGAGAGGTTGCCA” was used as the pegRNA spacer. Other parameters included the input mutation pattern “AACGTT” with “sub” at position 3, 70 bp for comparison range, minimum frequency $" 1 "$ , and prime editing marker $\pm ~ 5$ bp. Mapped reads and primer editing efficiencies were exported and further sorted in Excel using the edited signature sequence on the AclI site. 

# VvDXS1e expression analysis

Leaf samples from individual transgenic vines in Magenta boxes were collected and ground into powder in liquid nitrogen. The same leaf power was aliquoted for both genomic DNA extraction and total RNA extraction. Total leaf RNA extraction, trace genomic DNA removal, and reverse transcription were performed as described by Yang et al. (2022). The amplicon targeting the editing window was amplified either by genomic PCR (1529/1530) or by RT-PCR (1705/1706). Both genomic PCR and RT-PCR products were purified and sequenced by Sanger sequencing. The peaks at the editing target site were compared between genomic PCR and RT-PCR sequencing profiles to determine if the edited $V v D X S \boldsymbol { 1 } ^ { e }$ is expressed similarly to the un-edited ${ \cal V } v { \cal D } { \cal X } S { \cal 1 } ^ { w t }$ allele. Full-length VvDXS1 cDNA was also amplified with the primer pair 1686/1694 from multiple transgenic lines. The RT-PCR products were cloned into a pJET1.2 vector (K1231; ThermoFisher Scientific). The edited status of VvDXS1 in individual clones was verified by sequencing the colony PCR amplicon in the PE target region. 

# Monoterpene analysis

Two sets of leaf samples were collected for the analysis of monoterpenes. One set of leaf samples was collected from fieldgrown grape cultivars from the USDA Cold-Hardy Grape Collection in Geneva, NY, USA. Three known muscat cultivars 

of ‘Canada Muscat’ (PI58808), ‘Mills’ (PI588196), and ‘New York Muscat’ (PI588065) and 3 non-muscat cultivars of ‘Suffolk Red’ (PI597242), ‘Fredonia’ (PI597098), and ‘Kensington’ (PI59711) from the repository were included in this study (Table S3). All field grape cultivars are French-American hybrids (hybrids involving V. vinifera and North American Vitis species). Leaves from two vines of each grape cultivar were taken as two independent samples for analysis. For each vine, leaf samples were collected from one shoot, starting with the first open leaf from the tip to the $6 ^ { \mathrm { { t h } } }$ or $7 ^ { \mathrm { t h } }$ node until the total leaf weight reached ${ \sim } 1 . 2 \ 9$ . These samples were used as references for the analysis of the greenhouse transgenic material. The second set of leaf samples was collected from greenhouse vines, which included 47 transgenic vines representing 11 independently edited events and 9 control vines, which also had the ‘Scarlet Royal’ background from an unrelated project (Table S4). For each individual vine in the greenhouse, leaf samples were collected from the primary shoot, starting from the shoot tip (the first open young leaf at the 2nd or 3rd node) down to the mature leaves at the 8th-11th node, to have enough tissues weighing over 1.2 g. The leaf tissue collected from both the field and greenhouse was stored in 50-mL tubes and flash frozen in liquid nitrogen until further processing. The leaf samples were ground in 15-mL tubes with steel balls in liquid nitrogen using a Geno/Grinder and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . 

One gram of ground tissue from each vine was placed into 10 mL headspace vials with 2 mL citrate–phosphate buffer, $0 . 2 \ 9$ NaCl, and $2 ~ \mu \iota$ 1-heptanol as an internal standard following Matarese et al. (2014) and stored at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ prior to analysis. Samples were analysed using an Agilent 7890A gas chromatograph equipped with a 5977A mass spectrometer and a PAL RSI 120 autosampler. Volatile compounds were extracted using a solid-phase micro-extraction (SPME) DVB/CWR/PDMS fibre (Part No. 5191–5874, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). All instrument parameters followed those of Matarese et al. (2014) except the lowest oven temperature was set to $3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ instead of $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and the column used for separation was a DB-5MS column $( 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } .$ , Part No. 122-5532E; Agilent Technologies). The identities of the monoterpenes $\beta$ -myrcene, linalool, citronellol, and geraniol were confirmed using analytical standards purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, MO) by matching retention times and molecular weights. Other compounds were putatively identified by the highest match in the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) reference library (Table S4). Both sample and standard peak areas were processed using Agilent OpenLab CDS software and exported for statistical analysis. 

T-tests were conducted to detect statistical differences between field-grown muscat and non-muscat grapes for individual monoterpenes. The folds of changes in the peak areas for individual monoterpenes between field-grown muscat and non-muscat grapes were also calculated. T-tests and folds of change analyses were also conducted for the greenhouse material, comparing 7 distinct transgenic lines with the greenhouse control as well as all transgenic vines collectively with the control.
