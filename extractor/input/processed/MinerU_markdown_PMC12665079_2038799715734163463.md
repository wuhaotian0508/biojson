# ABSTRACT

Atractylodes lancea, a vital medicinal plant, is renowned for its bioactive sesquiterpenoids, particularly atractylon. Despite its therapeutic importance, the genetic and biosynthetic pathways underlying atractylon production remain insufficiently understood. In this study, we present a comprehensive chromosome-scale genome assembly of A. lancea with a contig N50 of 263 Mb, advancing the existing genomic knowledge with unprecedented detail. Utilising an integrative omics approach, we coupled genome resequencing across 245 diverse plant accessions, metabolomics and transcriptomics comparisons among different A. lancea genetic backgrounds to profile the sesquiterpenoid divergency and construct a biosynthetic map of the sesquiterpenoids. Metabolite-based genome-wide association studies (mGWAS) reveal significant genetic variations related to atractylon diversity, offering terpene synthase (TPS) gene AlTPSa47 may be involved. Our work further identified and characterised key genes within the TPS family. Catalytic characterisation showed that five of them processed FPP into 12 different sesquiterpenoids. Metabolic profiling determined that transgenic roots overexpressing AlTPSa47 not only increased $\beta$ -elemene and germacrene B, two of its enzymatic products, but also enhanced atractylon, notably confirming AlTPSa47 is essential in the initial steps of atractylon biosynthesis through rigorous transgenic validation. Collectively, our findings provide foundational resources for advancing breeding programs and enhancing the production of therapeutic compounds in A. lancea, marking a significant leap forward in medicinal plant biotechnology. 

# 1 | Introduction

In Asteraceae family, Atractylodes is a plant genus endemic to East Asia and has unique medicinal properties and chemical markers (Ma et al. 2023). There are five species in Atractylodes, 

A. lancea, A. macrocephala, A. koreana, A. japonica and A. carlinoide (Wang et  al.  2021). All Atractylodes except for $A$ . carlinoide were recorded as phytomedicine in Chinese historical texts (Wang et al. 2021). Among the Atractylodes genus, the rhizomes of A. lancea (commonly known as ‘Cangzhu’ in Chinese) 

are collectively known as atractylodis rhizome (AR) (Zhang, Wang, et al. 2023). In the past practical experience, AR was often used as epidemic prevention medicine. For instance, during the COVID-19 epidemic, AR played an indispensable role as one of the important components of the prescription (Sun et al. 2020; Han et  al.  2023). Phytochemical and pharmacological activity analyses have reported that the dry rhizomes of A. lancea contain multiple sesquiterpenes (e.g., atractylon, beta-eudesmol and hinesol), monoterpenes and polyacetylenes (e.g., atractylodin) (Wang et al. 2008; Lehner et al. 1997). Atractylon is a sesquiterpene reported only in Atractylodes. Previous pharmacological studies have shown that atractylon has broad pharmacological effects, such as antiviral (Cheng et  al.  2016), anti-tumor (Sun et al. 2022) anti-inflammatory (Lin et al. 2020), protection of dopaminergic neurons (Li et al. 2022) and natural acaricidal (Kim et al. 2007). 

Terpenes, especially sesquiterpenoids, are important and diverse secondary metabolites widely produced in Asteraceae (Padilla-Gonzalez et  al.  2016; Collaboration Research Group for Qinghaosu. 1977). The parthenolide exhibiting exceptional anti-cancer (Liu et  al.  2021) and anti-inflammatory (Kwok et  al.  2001) properties is a germacrane sesquiterpene lactone. A new guaiane-type sesquiterpenoid dimer has been isolated from the Artemisia atrovirens and identified with antihepatoma activity (Su et  al.  2021). Previous studies revealed many terpenoids exhibit specificity to certain Asteraceae varieties or species, raising species-specific functions (Cane 1999). Terpene synthases (TPSs) are responsible for the synthesis of the various terpene molecules which play important roles in the accumulation of diverse terpenoids (Tholl 2015). Depending on their function, TPSs can be divided into seven subfamilies (TPS-a, TPS-b, TPS-c, TPS-d, TPS-e/f, TPS-g and TPS-h), of which TPS-a are dedicated to the production of sesquiterpenoids via catalysing precursor farnesyl diphosphate (FPP) (Chen et  al.  2011; Zhi et al. 2024). Due to the increased availability of plant genomes, it has been reported that there is expansion rather than loss of TPS genes in Asteraceae, such as 122 TPSs in A. argyi (Jia et al. 2014) and 140 TPSs in Chrysanthemum nankingense (Jiang et  al.  2024), with the majority representing TPS-a and TPS-b subfamilies. A. lancea enriches active sesquiterpenoids (Zhang, Bai, et al. 2023). However, the current research mainly focuses on the upstream biosynthesis of terpenoids in A. lancea (Zhang, Ding, et al. 2023; Chen, Zhang, et al. 2023), and the biological function of AlTPS underlying the biodiversity of AR sesquiterpenoids remains unclear (Wu et al. 2023). 

Recently, a high-quality genome of A. lancea, featuring a contig N50 of 1.18 Mb, was assembled (Zhang et  al.  2024). Based on reference genome and resequencing data from different Atractylodes species, researchers profiled the basic population genetic characteristics of Atractylodes species. Notably, A. lancea has been divided into three sub-groups (MA, SA, NA) (Zhang et  al.  2024). Combining with the metabolome analysis, they found metabolome alterations in different distribution areas are probably associated with local adaptation (Zhang et  al.  2024). Moreover, metabolite-based genome-wide association studies identified several candidate genes, such as AlZFP706 and AlAAHY1, exhibiting significant correlations with atractylodin and hinesol levels, respectively (Zhang et al. 2024). This extensive and comprehensive analysis has provided the Atractylodes 

research community with a lot of valuable information, but the biosynthesis mechanisms underlying the accumulation of atractylon in A. lancea remain unclear. 

In this study, we report the use of long-reads sequencing technologies and assembly pipelines to sequence and assemble a gap-less (17 gaps in a 4 Gb genome, with a contig N50 of 263 Mb) genome of A. lancea. To investigate the biosynthesis and diversity of sesquiterpenes in A. lancea, we employ an integrative strategy that combines genomics, transcriptomics and metabolomics data. This multi-omics approach allows for a deeper understanding of the biosynthetic pathway, as each dataset provides complementary insights. Together, these data provide a comprehensive understanding of sesquiterpenes diversity among subgroups of A. lancea and a key TPS-a gene involved in the early biosynthetic steps of atractylon.

# 2.1 | De Novo Assembly of the Genome of A. lancea

Atractylodes lancea is a diploid plant with 12 chromosomes in one haplotype $2 \mathrm { n } = 2 \mathrm { x } = 2 4$ ; Figure  S1, Figure  1A). The estimated genome size of A. lancea is 4.17 Gb, with a $1 . 3 \%$ level of heterozygosity and $7 0 \%$ of repeat sequences (Figure  S2) through $k$ -mer $\left( k = 2 1 \right)$ ) analysis based on 251 Gb of short read (Table S1). Meanwhile, a total of 218 Gb HiFi reads ${ \sim } 5 0 \times$ coverage) (Table S2) were used for de novo assembly, resulting in a 4.45 Gb draft genome with a contig N50 of 263 Mb (Table S3). Based on the Hi-C matrix, 2262 contigs were anchored onto 12 pseudochromosomes, achieving a chromosome-level genome with a length of 4.24 Gb (Figure S3, Table S3). The strong interaction signal along the diagonal of proximal regions suggested the Hi-C assemblies of the A. lancea genome were of high quality (Figure  S3). As a result, we obtained a gap-less (17 gaps in total, Figure 1B, Table S3) reference genome of A. lancea. A combination of ab  initio, homology-based search and Iso-seq data prediction, 58 919 high-confidence protein-coding genes with an average length of 305 aa in the A. lancea genome were annotated (Table S3). Approximately $9 4 \%$ of these coding genes were further annotated using seven public databases (Table S4). We further annotated noncoding genes, profiling 722 ribosomal RNAs (rRNAs), 4624 transfer RNAs (tRNAs), 405 small-nuclear RNAs (snRNAs) and 6259 small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs) (Table S5). To assess genome integrity, we used BUSCO (v3.1) to evaluate the assembled genome, revealing $9 5 . 4 \%$ completeness for the genome and $9 3 . 4 \%$ for the protein-coding genes (Figures S4 and S5, Table S3). The long terminal repeat (LTR) assembly index (LAI) value was 14.85, indicating a high-quality reference assembly with a high level of contiguity of the repetitive and intergenic regions (Table S3). 

Single-copy orthologous genes help minimise the complexity caused by gene duplication and loss events. This allows for a clearer understanding of the true evolutionary divergence and phylogenetic relationships between species. # 2.2 | Genomic Divergence Among A. lancea Accessions

To explore the genetic variation and population history of $A$ . lancea, we carried out the genome resequencing of $2 4 5 A$ . lancea accessions collected from different areas of China, which represent most of the phenotypic and geographical diversity (Figure  1D). Two A. macrocephala accessions were also resequenced and used as an outgroup (Figure 1D–F, Figure S11 and Table S6). Approximately $6 . 1 \mathrm { T b }$ of raw sequence was generated on the DNBSEQ-T7 platform, with an average depth of 8x and aligned to the A. lancea genome. A total of 93 096 556 variants were identified across accessions, among which 84 090 199 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were used in further analysis (Table S7, Figures S9 and S10). 

Based on these SNPs, phylogenetic tree, population structure and principal component analysis (PCA) were performed to understand the genetic basis of A. lancea population (Figure S11, Figure 1E,F). Both phylogenetic tree (Figure S11) and PCA (Figure  1F) revealed that the 245 accessions of $A$ . lancea were divided into three groups, termed as group green, blue and red. The group red was located mainly in North China while the green and blue were overlapped in central China (Figure  1D). Additionally, the three groups all exhibited high nucleotide diversity $( \mathrm { P I } > 1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 3 } )$ , with green showing the highest nucleotide diversity (Figure S12). For genetic structure analysis, K values that represent the number of genetic groups or populations that are hypothesized to exist within the sampled individuals from two to five were evaluated (Figure  1E). When $K = 3$ , the population genetic divergence supported the result of phylogenetic analysis and PCA (Figure  S11 and Figure  1E,F). Intriguingly, when $K = 2$ , the group red and blue fused as one category, rather than red and green which have similar geographical distribution joined as 

one (Figure  1E), suggesting a closer genetic relationship between group blue and red. The fixation index $( F _ { \mathrm { s t } } )$ value indicated that the greatest differentiation was between group green and red, whereas the smallest differentiation was between group blue and red (Figure  S13), consistent with the genetic structure analysis. 

# 2.3 | Metabolite Variation Across Three A. lancea Phylogenetic Groups

To investigate the relationship between metabolite divergence of atractylodis rhizoma (AR) and various genetic backgrounds, we collected a total of 9, 7 and 14 AR belonging to group green, blue and red respectively from their original place. Non-targeted metabolomics was performed by LC–MS/ MS while broad-target metabolomics was performed by GC– MS. After removing ultra-low abundance signals and repeats, 1249 metabolites were detected in positive (POS) electrospray ionisation (ESI) while 513 metabolites were detected in negative (NEG) electrospray ionisation (ESI) (Table S8). Moreover, 32 volatile organic components were detected by GC–MS (Table S9). Metabolite identification for AR constituents was assigned based on databases and standards. The terpenoids were found to be the most abundant in AR after analysing all 1794 substances (Figure 2A, Tables S8 and S9). Through cluster analysis of all annotated terpenoids, differences between groups were found (Figure  S14). PCA of the metabolite profiles of different AR phylogenetic groups via LC–MS/MS and GC–MS showed that group red was clearly separated while groups green and blue were partially overlapped, which was consistent with their geographical distribution (Figures  1D and 2B,C), suggesting the metabolite divergence of AR is partially based on their growing environment. In addition, we performed pairwise comparisons to detect metabolic features that were differentially accumulated among three groups of A. lancea accessions (Figure 2D). Results showed that differentiated accumulated metabolites (DAMs) between groups blue and green, which shared a similar habitat (Figure  1D) were the least. DAMs between groups red and blue that possess a closer genetic relationship (Figure  1E) were less than DAMs between red and green, indicating that the metabolite divergence of AR is not only based on their growing environment, but also depends on their genetic differentiation.

# 2.4 | mGWAS-Based Investigation of Genetic Basis Underlying the Metabolite Variation of A. lancea

As aforementioned, the metabolite divergence of A. lancea is based on both genetic basis and their habitat. To get rid of the influence from environment and investigate the genetic basis underlying the metabolite divergence of A. lancea, we planted 

all A. lancea accessions we collected in the same garden located at Yingshan in Hubei province. After 1 year of growth, roots from all $^ { 1 2 3 } A$ . lancea accessions were harvested. In total, 22 volatile components were analysed using GC–MS while atractylon, $\beta$ -eudesmol, hinesol and atractylodin have been quantified by standard substances (Table S13). Subsequently, we conducted mGWAS analyses using GC–MS data and genome resequencing 

data of these 123 A. lancea accessions. Consequently, a strong peak located at 44.32–47.53 MB region on chromosome 1 was associated with the abundance of atractylon (Figure 3A). This region included 218 significant SNPs $( - \mathrm { l o g 1 0 ( P ) } \geq 9 )$ and 35 candidate genes (Tables S14 and S15, Figure 3B). Interestingly, this region was also a signature of selective sweep in the $F _ { \mathrm { s t } }$ analyses between group blue and red (Figure 3A). 

Among 35 candidate genes, augustus_Chr4_gene014873 was annotated as a MYB gene, augustus_Chr4_gene014890 was annotated as a Cytochrome P450 coding gene, while PB.10534 and augustus_Chr4_gene014844 were annotated as TPS genes (Table S15). To study the expression pattern of all 35 candidate genes in different tissues from different genetic backgrounds, we collected four different tissues, including leaf (L), stem (S), rhizome (RZ) and root (R), from each group for RNA-seq. Augustus_Chr4_gene014873 is not expressing in all tissues that we have tested (Figure  3C). Out of 35 candidates, three biosynthetic-related genes, augustus_Chr4_gene014890 (CYP), PB.10534 and augustus_Chr4_gene014844 (TPSs), showed a preferential expression in the root and rhizome, where atractylon accumulated. In addition, PB.10534 was the second highest abundance among these 35 candidate genes, while augustus_ Chr4_gene014844 could only code a peptide with a length of 132 aa, which probably was a pseudogene. As aforementioned, atractylon is one of the sesquiterpenoids with low abundance in the group blue (Figure 2G). PB.10534 expressed at a higher level in root and rhizome in group green and red. Interestingly, it barely expressed in group blue, which led us to speculate that PB.10534 might be involved in atractylon accumulation in AR (Figure 3B,C). 

# 2.5 | Characterisation of AlTPS-a Genes Involved in Sesquiterpenoids Biosynthesis

Atractylon is not the only sesquiterpenoid that showed differential abundance in different groups of AR. To understand the reason for the sesquiterpenoids divergence in different groups of AR mentioned above (Figure 2D–G) and determine which TPS gene is in charge of sesquiterpene biosynthesis, a total of 106 TPS genes were identified in the A. lancea genome (Figure S15). Then, TPS genes from seven other Asteraceae species and A. thaliana were used to construct a phylogenetic tree. According to the phylogenetic relationships, the TPS genes were classified into TPS-a, TPS-b, TPS-g and TPS-c/e/f subfamilies (Figure 4A). In total, 65 TPS-a subfamily genes were identified in A. lancea, which were termed AlTPSa01 to AlTPSa65 (Figure  4A, Table  S16). We found that the TPS-a could be divided into two clades and Clade 2 could be further divided into two subclades. Among them, Clade 2-2 is bigger than the other clades and seems significantly expanded and specialised in Asteraceae (Figure 4A). 

In a previous study, TPS-a genes from Asteraceae were broadly analysed. A TPS-a gene termed as germacrene A synthase coding gene (GAS) from A. annua belonging to Clade 1 produces germacrane sesquiterpenoids (Bertea et al. 2006). In Clade 2-1, HaTPS12 from H. annuus was demonstrated to be involved in bisabolene sesquiterpenoid catalysis (Aschenbrenner et  al.  2016). In Clade 2-2, epi-Cedrol synthase (ECS1), $\beta$ -caryophyllene 

synthase gene (QHS1) and amorpha-4,11-diene synthase (AMS) from A. annua processed caryophyllane sesquiterpenoids, humulane sesquiterpenoids, cedrane sesquiterpenoid and cadinane sesquiterpenoids that included the precursor substances of artemisinin (Mercke et  al.  2000; Fang et  al.  2017; Hua and Matsuda 1999; Cai et al. 2002). These studies implied that TPS-a genes from Clade 2 might possess broader lineage-specific catalytic ability. 

Using RNA-seq data of leaf, stem, rhizome and root from different groups, we found a notable expressing pattern of AlTPS- $a$ genes. AlTPS-a genes with considerable expression levels in Clade 1 showed a similar expression pattern that higher accumulated in root and the expression patterns among three groups were almost the same (Figure 4B). Conversely, AlTPS- $a$ genes in Clade 2 showed various expression patterns. Some of them, such as AlTPSa05, AlTPSa07 and AlTPSa47, expressed at a higher level in both rhizome and root. Remarkably, the accumulation patterns of AlTPSa47, AlTPSa51 and AlTPSa53 were different among three groups. The expression of AlTPSa47 was much lower in group blue than in red and green; AlTPSa51 showed higher abundance in group blue and red than in green, while AlTPSa53 expressed at a higher level in group blue than the others (Figure 4B). These results implied that AlTPS-a genes from Clade 2 might have broader biological functions during the development of A. lancea and might be in charge of the sesquiterpenoid divergence among different groups of A. lancea. 

Notably, PB.10534 identified in genomic loci associated with the synthesis of atractylon was termed AlTPSa47 (Figure 3), which belongs to Clade 2-2 (Figure 4A,B). As mentioned above, TPS-a genes in Clade 2-2 seem significantly expanded and specialised in Asteraceae, and may play roles in the biosynthesis of various sesquiterpene skeletons. To address the evolutionary relationship of AlTPSa47 within the Asteraceae family, phylogenetic and synteny analyses of AlTPSa47 were performed using seven Asteraceae species. As a result, we found that AlTPSa47 maintains strong syntenic relationships across almost all Asteraceae species (Figure 4C). Moreover, multiple sequence alignments of AlTPSa47 orthologs in the Asteraceae family were carried out, and AlTPSa47 orthologs are conserved in amino acid sequences (Figure S16), suggesting their conserved or similar functions.

# 2.7 | AlTPSa47 Is Involved in Early Biosynthetic Steps of Atractylon

Our mGWAS analyses identified AlTPSa47 as a key candidate gene involved in atractylon biosynthesis. Meanwhile, in  vitro catalysis assays demonstrated AlTPSa47 processed FPP directly to germacrane sesquiterpenoid, Germacrene B (6) (Figure 5B), rather than atractylon, providing speculation that germacrene B may be a precursor of atractylon. CYP450 enzymes can modify sesquiterpene structure by introducing oxygen atoms into the molecular structure, often leading to the production of various secondary metabolites (Weitzel and Simonsen 2015). One CYP gene, named Augustus_Chr4_gene014890, was identified in genomic loci associated with the synthesis of atractylon (Figure 3). Thus, we checked if Augustus_Chr4_gene014890 could catalyse germacrene B or other products produced by AlTPSa47 via tobacco transient expression. As a result, AlTPSa47 produced germacrene B (6) and caryophyllene (5) in tobacco leaves. However, when AlTPSa47 and Augustus_Chr4_gene014890 genes were co-expressed in tobacco leaves, no extra product was generated compared with AlTPSa47 overexpressing leaves (Figure  S17), suggesting this CYP gene may not be involved in atractylon biosynthesis. 

To further validate the biological function of AlTPSa47, we developed an in-house Agrobacterium-mediated transgenic method, which was modified from ‘CDB’ (Cao et  al.  2022), to obtain transgenic roots of A. lancea overexpressing AlTPSa47 (Figure  6A). In total, we got 20 transgenic plants with GFPpositive roots. Ten of GFP-positive roots were harvested and 

collected as one AlTPSa47 overexpressing (OE) biological replicate while GFP-negative roots were collected as control. After qRT-PCR assay, each OE line was more than 100 times overexpressed (Figure S18). The volatile oils of OE lines as well as controls were detected by GC–MS; as a result, $\beta$ -elemene (4) and germacrene B (6), two direct products of AlTPSa47, showed significant accumulation in both transgenic lines (Figure 6B,C). Atractylon was increased twice in OE line 1 and four times in OE line 2 (Figure 6D). In addition, we carried out virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) assay to interfere with the expression level of AlTPSa47 in the rhizomes of 3-year-old A. lancea plants (Figure  6E). After 10 days of infection, rhizome samples were harvested and analysed by qRT-PCR. Three AlTPSa47-VIGS rhizomes, where the expression of AlTPSa47 was decreased to around $5 0 \%$ , were used in GC–MS analysis (Figure  S19). The results revealed a significant decrease of $\beta$ -elemene (4), germacrene B (6) and atractylon in AlTPSa47 silencing rhizomes (Figure 6F–H). 

Therefore, we speculated that germacrene B is an important intermediate in the atractylon biosynthesis; AlTPSa47 is involved in the early biosynthetic steps of atractylon. 

# 3 | Discussion

With detailed genomic knowledge, scientists can selectively breed plants with higher concentrations of desirable medicinal compounds. In a previous study, Zhang et al. published a chromosome-level assembly of A. lancea using PacBio singlemolecule real-time (SMRT) and Hi-C sequencing (Zhang et  al.  2024). Taking advantage of HiFi sequencing technology, we obtained a 4.45 Gb draft genome with a contig N50 of 263 Mb. Based on the Hi-C matrix, 2262 contigs were anchored onto 12 pseudochromosomes, achieving a gap-less genome with a length of 4.24 Gb, the BUSCO of which showed $9 5 . 4 \%$ completeness for the genome and $9 3 . 4 \%$ for the proteincoding genes. This might be a platinum genome reference for Atractylodes or the Asteraceae research community. By merging datasets from our study and a previous study (Zhang et al. 2024) may lead to a higher-quality genome assembly and annotation.

Metabolite-based GWAS is used to reveal the relationship between genotype and metabolite formation. Zhang et al. (2024) identified several candidate genes related to essential oils via mGWAS using 130 A. lancea wild accessions. Notably, AlZFP706 and AlAAHY1 exhibited significant correlations with atractylodin and hinesol levels (Zhang et al. 2024). They also identified AlPVPSP13, a putative vacuolar protein sorting-associated protein coding gene, which showed correlation with atractylon

In this study, our data implied that the metabolite divergence of AR is partially based on their growing environment. To get rid of the influence from the environment, we built a garden and planted all A. lancea accessions in the same habitat. After 1 year of growth, roots from $^ { 1 2 3 A }$ . lancea accessions were harvested, which were used for GC–MS analysis and mGWAS later on. As a result, we located a 3.21 Mb region harbouring 35 candidate genes correlated with atractylon accumulation (Figure 3). Interestingly, three biosynthetic-related genes showed preferential expression in the root and rhizome, where atractylon accumulated. One of them, termed AlTPSa47, was further confirmed to be involved in the early biosynthetic steps of atractylon. Therefore, avoiding excessive interference from environmental factors on metabolite variation may aid in identifying medicinal quality traitrelated candidate genes. 

The accumulation level of other medicinal components in A. lancea, such as atractylodin, Hinesol, beta-eudesmol, was also involved in mGWAS analyses in our study. However, no locus was identified when the significance threshold for identifying associated SNPs was set at $1 0 ^ { - 9 }$ . When we set the $p$ -value at $1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ , we achieved several loci. However, the genes within these loci did not show a significant correlation with the accumulation of these medicinal components. Metabolic traits are often influenced by complex, multifactorial networks involving multiple genes, enzymes and environmental factors. In this study, we controlled environmental factors by planting all A. lancea accessions together. However, we could only obtain enough roots for metabolic analysis from 123 accessions 1 year after planting. Small sample sizes may lead to insufficient statistical power, reducing the likelihood of detecting significant associations between genotype and metabolites. To identify candidate genes involved in the biosynthesis of medicinal components in A. lancea, we may increase the population size and measure the metabolite content repetitively. 

# 4 | Conclusion

In conclusion, our study provides the first chromosome-level genome of A. lancea, offering a high-resolution reference for this important medicinal plant. We also generated a comprehensive genomic variation map of $2 4 5 A$ . lancea accessions, which sheds light on the genetic diversity and classification of this species. By identifying several key TPS genes involved in sesquiterpenoid biosynthesis, we have laid a solid foundation for future efforts to enhance the production of valuable secondary metabolites. These findings not only deepen our understanding of A. lancea's genetic makeup but also pave the way for the development of more efficient breeding strategies and biotechnological interventions aimed at improving key agronomic traits. 

# 5 | Methods and Materials

# 5.1 | Plant Materials and Genome Assembly

The A. lancea material used for whole genome sequencing was obtained from Hong'an County, Hubei Province, China $3 1 . 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N } .$ , $1 1 4 . 6 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { E }$ ). Karyotype analysis was performed on the 

young root tips; the diploid plants were observed under the microscope. Genomic DNA was extracted from young leaf tissue using a modified CTAB method and was used for DNBSEQ-T7 short read sequencing, PacBio long-read HiFi sequencing and Hi-C library construction and sequencing. 

The raw NGS data were initially processed using fastp (v0.20.0) with default parameters to remove adapter sequences and lowquality bases, yielding clean reads. These clean reads were then analysed with Jellyfish (v2.2.8) for $k$ -mer frequency profiling (parameters: $- \mathrm { m } 2 1 - \mathrm { s } 2 0 \mathrm { G } \cdot \mathrm { t } 1 2 \cdot \mathrm { C } )$ ), generating a $k$ -mer frequency distribution histogram. Based on this frequency distribution, the genome size, heterozygosity and repeat ratio were estimated using GCE (v1.0.2) (Liu et al. 2020) (parameter: -H1). 

Firstly, the PacBio HiFi reads were de novo assembled with Hifiasm (v0.18) (Cheng et  al.  2021) (parameters: -t 4 -l 3) to generate a contig level genome. Subsequently, HiCUP (v0.8.1) (parameter: --bowtie2 --format Sanger) was used to filter the original Hi-C data, retain the valid interaction data and further remove the PCR duplication through Juicer v1.6 (the default parameter) to generate a deduplicated Hi-C interaction pair file (merged_nodups.txt). The contig assembly and Hi-C interaction pairs were input into 3d-DNA (v180922) (default parameters) to generate the initial chromosome assembly file (.assembly) and Hi-C interaction matrix (.hic) and then the chromosome boundaries, contig orientation and ligation relationships were visually adjusted in Juicerbox (v1.11.08) to export the revised assembly file. Finally, the revision file was re-imported into 3d-DNA (v180922) for iterative optimization to obtain a chromosome-level genome. Meanwhile, telomeric structures were identified by detecting repetitive TTAGG sequences. 

The gene models were predicted by EVidence Modeler (Haas et  al.  2008), embedded in a pipeline that integrates evidences from ab initio predictions, homology-based searches and RNA-Seq-guided gene prediction. The output included a set of consistent and non-overlapping sequence assemblies, which were used to describe the gene structures. Finally, gene functions were inferred according to the best match of the alignments to the NCBI, NR, TrEMBL, KOG and Swiss-Prot protein databases using BLASTP $\left( \mathrm { v } 2 . 6 . 0 + \right)$ and the KEGG database with an $E$ - value threshold of 1E-5. Non-coding RNA were identified by tR-NAscan-SE (v1.3.1) (Chan and Lowe 2019), RNAMMER (v1.2) and cmsan (v1.1.2) software. 

TEs were identified by RepeatMasker (v4.0.9) (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen  2009) using both Repbase library and the de novo library. The long terminal repeat retrotransposons (LTR-RT) were annotated using LTR-harvest (v1.5.11), LTR-finder (v1.0.7) (Xu and Wang 2007) and LTR-retriever (v2.7). 

# 5.2 | Evolution Analysis

OrthoFinder (Emms and Kelly  2019) was employed for inference of orthologous groups in the 14 selected species. The 14 genomes consisted of A. thaliana and 13 species from Asteraceae. To improve the accuracy of the phylogenetic tree, 

the proteins corresponding to single-copy direct orthologous gene pairs were taken redundantly (threshold set to $> 9 0 \%$ coverage and $> 9 0 \%$ similarity) and then entered into the muscle (Edgar 2004) programme for alignment. A neighbourjoining (NJ) phylogeny was set up based on the concatenated alignments of all single-copy genes using IQ-TREE (Minh et al. 2020). Combined with the fossil time of each species in the timetree (Kumar et al. 2022) database, the species divergence time was calibrated. Gene family contraction and expansion analysis for 14 species was performed using the cafe (De Bie et al. 2006) programme. Finally, the evolutionary tree was beautified using the iTOL (Letunic and Bork 2024) online programme. 

Paralogous and orthologous gene pairs were identified in syntenic blocks using the MCScanX (Wang et  al.  2012) software, while Ka and Ks were calculated using KaKs_Caculator (Wang et al. 2010). We then filtered out zero values and plotted the data with the ggplot2 package. Finally, the Ks frequency distribution map was plotted using the ggplot2 package. 

# 5.3 | Population Materials and Genetic Analyses

Two hundred and forty-five A. lancea accessions were collected from almost all A. lancea growing areas in different provinces of China and cultivated in Yingshan County, Hubei Province, China. Two Atractylodes macrocephala accessions were from Bozhou city, Anhui Province, China and Enshi city, Hubei Province, China. All accessions aforementioned were used for resequencing. Fresh young leaves were collected from single individuals of each accession and were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. Young leaves were used for DNA extraction (RaPure Plant DNA Mini Kit, Magen) for sequencing using the DNBSEQ-T7 platform. 

The resequencing data in fastq format were compared to the reference genome by using the bowtie2 (v2.3.4.1) program, sorted by samtools (v1.9), picard (v2.18.5) and other programs and then entered into GATK4.0 to detect SNPs & InDel. To obtain reliable results for the population history of A. lancea, SNPs and InDels were filtered out using vcf-tools (v0.1.15) with a quality score $\leq 3 0$ , read coverage $\leq 5$ , minor allele frequency (MAF) $\leq 0 . 0 5$ and missingness rate $\ge 0 . 7 .$ . 

Based on all the filtered SNPs, the population structure of the 247 accessions was analysed by admixture (v1.3.0) (Alexander et  al.  2009) software. The clustering of the samples was assumed to be 2–5. The clustering results were cross-verified, and the optimal number of clusters was determined to be 3 based on the minimum point of the cross-validation error rate. To explore the phylogenetic relationships of the 247 accessions, a root phylogenetic tree with A. macrocephala as the outgroup was constructed using the maximum likelihood method based on all the filtered SNPs using RAxML (v8.2.12) software. Meanwhile, the genetic relationship matrix (GRM) of the 247 accessions was first computed using the –bfile module in gcta (v1.92.4) (Yang et al. 2011), followed by PCA analysis based on the GRM using ‘gcta –grm’. 

Using the vcf-tools (v0.1.15) program, PI values were calculated based on a sliding window method (with a window size of 

$1 0 0 \mathrm { k b } ) \phantom { 0 }$ ), while $F _ { \mathrm { s t } }$ values were calculated using the same method (with a window size of $3 \mathrm { k b }$ ). 

# 5.4 | Metabolite Quantification

For non-targeted metabolomic analysis, UHPLC-Q-Exactive Orbitrap/MS was performed in our study. Each sample was precisely weighed with $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ fine-dry powder and extracted by sonication using $1 . 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of $7 0 \%$ methanol (w/v) containing internal standard for 30min. Extract of A. lancea rhizome was analysed by the Thermo Scientific Q Exactive plus MS system (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). Chromatographic separation was performed on Waters ACQUITY UPLC T3 Column $( 2 . 1 \times 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 8 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , phenomenex). The mobile phase was composed of water (A) with $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid added and acetonitrile (B) with $0 . 0 4 \%$ acetic acid added. The gradient procedure was as follows: 0min, $2 \%$ B; 0.5 min, $2 \%$ B; 15 min, $9 8 \%$ B; 17 min, $9 8 \%$ B; 17.1 min, $2 \% \mathrm { ~ B ~ }$ ; 20min, $2 \%$ mobile phase B. The flow rate was set at $0 . 3 5 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . Injection volume: $3 \mu \mathrm { L }$ ; Column temperature: $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Full MS and ddMS2 scans were acquired in positive and negative ion mode, respectively. In the positive ion mode, full MS scan parameters were set as follows: spray voltage $3 . 5 \mathrm { k V } ,$ , sheath gas flow 40, auxiliary gas flow 10, drying gas temperature $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , capillary temperature $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , resolution 70000, AGC target 3e6. Maximum IT 100ms and scan range $1 0 0 { - } 1 5 0 0 m / z$ . Dd-MS2 scan parameters were as follows: resolution 17500, AGC target 1e5, Maximum IT 50ms, loop count 10, Top N 10 and isolation window $1 . 0 m / z$ . In the negative ion mode, MS parameters were all the same as the positive ion mode. MS/MS fragmentation spectra were analysed to determine the structure of the fragmented molecules or were compared with the spectral data of available reference compounds in specific databases (PMhub 1.0) (Tian et al. 2024). 

For GC–MS analysis, each sample was precisely weighed with $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ fine-dry powder and added 5 mL of n-hexane (containing $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of cyclohexanone at a concentration of $9 4 7 . 8 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ as the internal standard). Shaked at $2 5 0 \mathrm { r / m i n }$ for 15 min; then centrifuged at $3 0 0 0 \mathrm { r / m i n }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . Collected the supernatant and transfered it to a $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ volumetric flask for later use. Added $4 \mathrm { m L }$ of n-hexane to the centrifuge tube and repeated the extraction. Combined the two supernatants in the volumetric flask to bring the volume to $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ . The metabolites were identified and quantitatively analysed using the GC–MS (Shimadzu, QP2010ULTRA). For the GC analysis, a $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot from each sample was injected into a GC system, integrating a DB-5MS column with a particle size of $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ( $0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m } \times 3 0 \mathrm { m }$ , $0 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ particle size). Helium was the carrier gas with a 1 mL/min flow rate. The inlet temperature was $2 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The injection mode used the non-split flow technique. The heating protocol began with an initial period at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min, followed by a temperature increase to $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at a rate of $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ . It was then raised to $1 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C / m i n }$ , further increased to $1 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ , and finally elevated to $2 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { m i n }$ , which was maintained for 2 min. The mass spectrometer was operated in Scan mode for both the identification and quantification of analytes. The GC– MS was operated in electron ionisation (EI) mode at 70 eV, scanning a mass range from $m / z 3 5$ to 400 to capture spectral data. Compounds were identified by comparison with the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST 17) database library and the retention indices. 

Non-targeted metabolites with relative abundance $< 5 0 0$ in all samples were regarded as lowly accumulated metabolites and were removed from further analyses. Using the total panel of metabolite values as the reference control, all data were normalised and then PCA clustering was performed using the ggplot2 package in R version 4.4.1. Meanwhile, both the ggplot2 and dplyr packages were used in R version 4.4.1 for volcano plot analysis. The relative contents of different groups of metabolites were displayed by histogram using GraphPad Prism 10.1.2 software. 

# 5.5 | Metabolite-Based Genome-Wide Association Study

To find potential SNP or genes associated with secondary metabolisms, all filtered SNPs and metabolite contents were associated by efficient mixed-model association expedited (EMMAX) software (Kang et  al.  2010). In brief, prior to analysis, quality control was performed to exclude SNPs with a minor allele frequency (MAF) below $5 \%$ , genotyping call rate lower than $7 0 \%$ , and individuals with more than $3 0 \%$ missing genotypes. To account for population structure and relatedness, we computed a kinship matrix using the emmax-kin based on the genotypic data. We computed a population structure matrix using the admixture (v1.3.0) (Alexander et  al.  2009) based on the genotypic data. The kinship matrix and population structure matrix were used in the mixed linear model to adjust for familial relatedness and minimise potential false positives arising from population stratification. Association analysis was performed using EMMAX, which employs a linear mixed model (LMM) to estimate the effect of each SNP on the phenotype of interest while controlling for population structure and relatedness. The significance threshold for identifying associated SNPs was determined using a $p$ -value that was set at $1 0 ^ { - 9 }$ . 

# 5.6 | TPS Family Identification and Phylogenetic and Evolutionary Analyses

To identify putative TPS genes from A. lancea, A. annua, C. morifolium, H. annuus, C. tinctorius, C. cardunculus, L. sativa, T. erecta and A. thaliana, we used two Pfam domains (PF03936 and PF01397) to search the protein sequence. Phylogenetic trees for the TPS family were constructed using RAxML version 8.2.12 with the PROTGAMMAAUTO model and 1000 bootstrap replicates. Finally, we used the iTOL (Letunic and Bork 2024) online software (http://itol.embl.de) to visualise and beautify the phylogenetic tree. 

# 5.7 | RNA-Seq Analysis

Next-generation sequencing clean reads were mapped to the A. lancea genome using HISAT2 (v2.2.1). Additionally, SAMtools (v1.9) was used to convert SAM files to BAM files. Finally, StringTie (v1.3.4) and Perl were used to obtain the expression matrix. The expression matrix of genes in different tissues was analysed using TPM data and visualised with TBtools (Chen, Wu, et al. 2023). 

# 5.8 | Syntenic Analysis of AlTPSa47 in Seven Plant Species and Phylogenetic Analysis of These Plants

Sequences of AlTPSa47 homologues were identified from six other Asteraceae species curated in GenBank. Then, their positional and protein sequence information was obtained from genomic data. The aligned genes (AlTPSa47 and its homologous genes) along with the positional and protein sequence information of their 10 upstream and downstream genes, were input into JCVI (orthologous function), and a local synteny plot was subsequently constructed. Meanwhile, the phylogenetic relationship among A. lancea and six other Asteraceae species was constructed using phyML to understand their evolution. 

# 5.9 | Enzyme Activities of TPS Genes

The ORF of the 11 TPS genes (AlTPSa01, AlTPSa03, AlTPSa05, AlTPSa07, AlTPSa47, AlTPSa50, AlTPSa51, AlTPSa52, AlTPSa53, AlTPSa56 and AlTPSa57) was isolated and subcloned into the BamHI/XhoI sites of pET28a using ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit according to the manufacturer's introductions. The recombinant plasmids and pET28a lacking an insert (control) were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) competent cells. After $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG induction at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight, the proteins were harvested and purified by affinity chromatography on nickel-nitrilotriacetic acid-agarose (Qiagen, http://www.qiagen. com). The purity of the isolated proteins was verified by the densitometry of the SDS-PAGE gels after Coomassie Brilliant Blue staining. The concentration of purified proteins was determined by the Bradford method. 

Assays for TPS protein activity were carried out in a 1 mL assay buffer $3 0 \mathrm { m M }$ HEPES, pH7.5, 5 mM DTT, 25 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 } ,$ ) containing $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ purified TPS proteins and $6 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ FPP. The mixture was incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for an hour and then at $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 min. After incubation, the synthesised volatiles were collected using the DVB/CAR/PDMS headspace sampler. 

# 5.10 | Molecular Docking

Significant targets identified through network and enrichment analyses were selected for molecular docking simulations. Protein models of these targets were retrieved from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) (https://www.rcsb.org/), while the structure file of $( E , E )$ -FPP was sourced from the PubChem database. The preprocessing of proteins and ligands was conducted using a sequential approach. Initially, PrankWeb (https://prankweb.cz/) was employed to predict the binding pocket, which informed the adjustment of the grid box size and position. Subsequently, AutoDockTools was used to add missing hydrogen atoms, calculate molecular charges and finalise the grid box settings for the protein docking region. Following these steps, a configuration file for Vina was generated. AutoDock Vina was then employed to conduct 100 docking simulations, with the complex conformation exhibiting the most favourable binding energy being retained in PDB format. PyMol (v3.0.4) was subsequently utilised 

to analyse the interactions between the ligand and receptor and to export visual representations of the results. 

# 5.11 | Site-Directed Mutagenesis

Site-directed mutagenesis was performed on the amino acid residues of AlTPSa47, which interact with the substrate. Genespecific primers containing mutant sites were used to amplify the full-length coding sequence of AlTPSa47. The obtained mutation sequences were cloned into the overexpression vector pET28a for further analysis. 

# 5.12 | Agrobacterium-Mediated Transgenic Roots of A. lancea

Transgenic roots of A. lancea were conducted using an Agrobacterium-mediated method established in our lab. The plasmid used in this study was a binary construct (pK7WG2D and pID:GFP) carrying the reporter gene GFP. The gene of interest was constructed using pK7WG2D as the backbone, and the transgene vector was used to transform Agrobacterium competent cells that had been prepared using a freeze–thaw technique. Transformed colonies were selected to infect the seedlings of A. lancea. Transgenic roots (expression of the GFP gene) were observed 2 weeks after the experiment started. 

# 5.13 | Virus-Induced Gene Silencing of A. lancea Rhizomes

A 407-bp conserved fragment of AlTPSa47 was inserted into the pTRV2 vector to generate the recombinant pTRV2-AlTPSa47 construct. Subsequently, pTRV1, pTRV2 and pTRV2-AlTPSa47 were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 via the freeze–thaw method. Cultures of pTRV1 were mixed in a 1:1 ratio with either pTRV2-AlTPSa47 (silencing treatment) or the pTRV2 empty vector (control). 

Rhizomes were rinsed in distilled water, and the whole rhizome was divided into two parts: one part was used for infection with pTRV1 mixed with pTRV2-AlTPSa47, and the other part was used for infection with pTRV1 mixed with the pTRV2 empty vector. The rhizomes were wounded lightly before infiltration using a clean razor blade to facilitate the entrance of Agrobacteria carrying the TRV plasmids. Then, $- 0 . 8 \mathrm { M P a }$ vacuum was applied in a chamber for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ (Di Stilio et al. 2010). Following infiltration, rhizomes were potted in soil and grown in the greenhouse. The samples were collected 10 days postinfiltration, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until processing.
