# P L A N T S C I E N C E S

# Neofunctionalized RGF pathways drive haustorial organogenesis in parasitic plants

Maxwell R. Fishman1 , Anne Greifenhagen1 , Takanori Wakatake1 †, Anuphon Laohavisit1 ‡, Ryoko Hiroyama1 , Sachiko Masuda1 , Arisa Shibata1 , Satoko Yoshida2 , Ken Shirasu1,3 * 

Parasitic plants will initiate rapid de novo organogenesis of a specialized feeding structure called a haustorium upon contact with their hosts. Currently, little is known about the internal signals regulating haustorium development. Here, we identify root meristem growth factor (RGF) peptides in Phtheirospermum japonicum as endogenous inducers of prehaustorium formation. Treatment with specific RGF peptides in the absence of hosts triggered prehaustoria and induced expression of PjYUC3, a gene required for auxin biosynthesis and prehaustorium formation. CRISPR-mediated knockouts showed that PjRGFR1 and PjRGFR3, receptors activated by the haustoriumspecific RGF peptides PjRGF2 and PjRGF5, are essential for prehaustorium formation, revealing functional redundancy. Phylogenetic analyses indicate that PjRGF2 is broadly conserved among Orobanchaceae, whereas PjRGF5 appears to have recently evolved through segmental tandem multiplication. Our findings establish RGF peptides and their corresponding receptors as critical components of haustorium developmental signaling and provide insights into the evolutionary trajectories that shape plant parasitism.

# INTRODUCTION

Parasitism has independently evolved 12 to 13 times from autotrophic angiosperms and has resulted in around $1 \%$ of angiosperms being either root or shoot parasites (1,  2). Parasitic plants occur as obligate nonphotosynthetic holoparasites or obligate photosynthetic hemiparasites that both require a host to survive or as facultative parasites that can survive without a host (3). To parasitize hosts, parasitic plants form a direct connection with host plants through a specialized organ called a haustorium (2). Haustoria typically arise de novo upon host contact and progress through specific developmental stages, starting with the prehaustorium, followed by the formation of intrusive cells that penetrate the host tissue, and culminating in the establishment of a vascular connection (2). This intimate linkage enables parasitic plants to secure water, minerals, and nutrients from their hosts, a process essential for survival of obligate parasites (1–3). 

The family Orobanchaceae consists predominantly of root parasites (4), encompassing agriculturally devastating obligate parasites in the genera Orobanche, Phelipanche, Alectra, and Striga, as well as comparatively benign facultative parasites in genera like Pedicularis and Phtheirospermum (5,  6). Most members of this family initiate prehaustoria formation upon the perception of lignin- and phenolicbased host-derived haustorium-inducing factors (HIFs), even in the absence of a host (7, 8). Prehaustoria can also initiate spontaneously in some parasitic plants, such as Striga hermonthica  (8) and Pedicularis kansuensis Maxim (9). The first identified HIF was 2,6-dimethoxy- $\boldsymbol { p }$ -benzoquinone (DMBQ) (10). DMBQ is a ligninderived quinone that induces prehaustoria in Striga and most other hemiparasitic plants in Orobanchaceae (11). The obligate holoparasites Phelipanche and Orobanche develop prehaustoria in response to cytokinins, sphaeropsidones, and very high concentrations of DMBQ 

(12, 13). DMBQ has become a central tool to dissect the early signaling pathway underlying haustorium development, particularly in the facultative parasites Phtheirospermum japonicum and Triphysaria versicolor, and in the obligate hemiparasite S. hermonthica (14–18). DMBQ and other similar quinone-based HIFs are first sensed by CARD1-like (CADL) receptor kinases (15), triggering $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ signaling in roots and activating a mitogen-activated protein kinase cascade. These early signals result in the differential regulation of hundreds of genes, leading to the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the de novo biosynthesis of auxin through the up-regulation of YUCCA3 monooxygenase (YUC3) specifically at the site of haustorium initiation (14,  16,  19). Both ROS and auxin are essential for haustorium development; however, the signaling pathways linking the initial CADL signaling cascade to downstream factors such as YUC3 are largely unknown. 

Root meristem growth factor (RGF) peptides (also referred to as GLV or CLEL peptides) were initially identified for their role in maintaining the root apical meristem (RAM) in Arabidopsis thaliana (from hereon referred to as Arabidopsis) but also regulate lateral root elongation and spacing, as well as root gravitropism (20–23). They consist of a family of sulfated plant peptide hormones present in all land plants, outside of hornworts and mosses, that are sulfated on a conserved tyrosine residue by tyrosylprotein sulfotransferases (TPSTs) (20, 24). They serve as ligands for the RGF receptor (RGFR)/RGF insensitive (RGI) leucine-rich repeat receptor-like kinases (from hereon referred to as RGFRs) where the conserved tyrosine, proline, and penultimate histidine/asparagine in the mature peptide are critical for interacting with RGFRs or their co-receptors (25–27). The regulatory role of RGF peptides in maintaining the RAM is partially accomplished by stabilizing the AP2-type transcription factor PLETHORA2 (PLT2) through the production of superoxide radical $( { \mathrm O } _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ (28). Moreover, RGF peptides selectively regulate a subset of auxin-responsive genes and mobilize PIN proteins to specify auxin localization (23, 29). RGF peptides are plausible regulators of haustorium signaling. 

In this current study, we identified three RGF peptides in P. japonicum (PjRGFs) that are up-regulated following DMBQ treatment. Treatment of these DMBQ-induced PjRGF peptides led to an 

up-regulation of PjYUC3 and induced prehaustoria in P. japonicum. Two of these PjRGF peptides are specifically expressed in the prehaustorium, reflecting the localization patterns of a P. japonicum PLT2 homolog (PjPLT) and $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ . Further analysis of RGF peptides in several Orobanchaceae species uncovered a single conserved haustoriumspecific RGF peptide that also induces prehaustoria in S. hermonthica. Genetic analysis further revealed that knockouts of two PjRGFRs reduced the number of DMBQ-induced prehaustoria in $P .$ japonicum hairy roots. In addition, hairy root knockouts of a PjRGF peptide and a separate PjRGFR resulted in irregular cell division at the apex of the prehaustorium. Together, we established RGF peptides in Orobanchaceae as endogenous prehaustorium inducers, placing RGF signaling downstream of DMBQ and upstream of YUC3 in the haustorium development pathway. These data suggest that parasitic plants neofunctionalize the RGF signaling pathway for de novo organogenesis, contributing to the evolution of plant parasitism. 

# RESULTS

# RGF peptides are up-regulated during haustorium induction

To identify signaling components activated by DMBQ perception, we analyzed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data from P. japonicum seedlings. Because subtilase-encoding genes, which code for apoplastic proteases, are important for haustorium development (30), we searched for apoplastically cleavable peptide hormone–encoding genes that were up-regulated in response to DMBQ treatment. From this approach, we identified a gene encoding an RGF peptide, from hereon called PjRGF1, which was induced following DMBQ treatment (fig. S1, A and B, and table S1). Although only four RGF peptide–encoding genes were initially annotated in the P. japonicum genome (31), additional mapping and annotation revealed another 13 putative RGF peptide–encoding genes, nine of which were found to be expressed in P. japonicum (Fig. 1, A and B; figs. S1, A and B, and S2, A to C; and tables S1 and S2). The expression of these other RGF peptide–encoding genes was very low in the RNA-seq data for all but PjRGF7, which was down-regulated following DMBQ treatment (fig. S1B and table S1). This may be due to the RNA-seq data coming from whole seedlings rather than roots, as a majority of RGF peptides are mainly expressed in roots (32). 

Given that the RNA-seq data did not discern whether any other PjRGFs were HIF responsive, reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was performed on cDNA from P. japonicum roots treated for 6 hours with either DMBQ or exudate from the host plant Nicotiana benthamiana. These data showed that PjRGF1, PjRGF2, and PjRGF5 were up-regulated following DMBQ treatment, whereas only PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 were induced by N. benthamiana exudate (Fig. 1B). There are six annotated genes encoding RGF peptides in the S. hermonthica genome (fig. S3A). One of these peptides was homologous to PjRGF2 and was named ShRGF2. As was done with P. japonicum, RT-qPCR was performed on cDNA taken from S. hermonthica seedlings that had been treated with DMBQ for 6 hours. Notably, ShRGF2 was up-regulated 6 hours post–DMBQ treatment (fig. S3, A and B), suggesting that RGF peptides may play a conserved role in the haustorium developmental signaling pathway across multiple parasitic species within Orobanchaceae. 

# RGF peptides induce prehaustoria in parasitic plants

RGF peptides induce a variety of root-related phenotypes in autotrophic plants (20, 29). Here, unexpectedly, application of PjRGF1, 

PjRGF2, or PjRGF5 to $P .$ japonicum induced prehaustoria (Fig. 1, C and D, and fig. S4A). The overall efficacy of the PjRGF peptide treatments was lower than that of DMBQ and varied among individual peptides. The highest concentration of RGF peptides tested was $2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , and, within this range, both PjRGF1 and PjRGF5 showed maximal induction at $2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , while PjRGF2 was most effective at $5 \mathrm { n M }$ (Fig. 1C). Higher concentrations of PjRGF2 sometimes resulted in nonspecific cell division in the epidermis and cortex (fig. S4B). Prehaustorium formation begins with controlled cell division in the transition zone (7), so nonspecific cell division likely reduced the number of prehaustoria formed and suggests that P. japonicum is particularly sensitive to PjRGF2. Unsulfated versions of the PjRGF peptides were tested for prehaustorium induction activity using concentrations at or close to the tested concentration that maximally induced prehaustoria by each PjRGF peptide. The unsulfated PjRGF1 and PjRGF2 peptides showed only a weak effect, but unsulfated PjRGF5 has prehaustorium induction activity (Fig.  1C). Unsulfated RGF peptides can be sulfated by endogenous TPSTs following treatment (20). There are two genes found in the P. japonicum genome that encode TPSTs (table  S3). Given that the prehaustorium induction assay was scored 2 days posttreatment (DPT), it is possible that one or both PjTPSTs added a sulfate group to unsulfated PjRGF1, PjRGF2, and PjRGF5 and increased the activity of these peptides. S. hermonthica was also challenged with synthesized RGF peptide, and, similar to what was observed in P. japonicum, ShRGF2 treatment of S. hermonthica also induced prehaustoria (fig.  S3, C and D). These induced structures were morphologically similar to DMBQ-induced prehaustoria in both P. japonicum and S. hermonthica, and their induction rate was dose dependent in P. japonicum (Fig. 1, C and D; and figs. S3, C and D, and S4A). Overall, these findings demonstrate that PjRGF1, PjRGF2, PjRGF5, and ShRGF2 are capable of inducing prehaustoria. 

PjYUC3 up-regulation was previously found to be downstream of HIF treatment, functioning as an important component during prehaustorium development (14). Consistent with this observation, treatment of P. japonicum roots with PjRGF1, PjRGF2, or PjRGF5 induced PjYUC3 expression 6 hours after treatment (Fig. 1E), although to a lesser extent than DMBQ. The DR5 synthetic auxin response reporter system has previously been used in P. japonicum to visualize auxin response localization in the prehaustoria (14). We used it to identify auxin response localization before any perturbations (fig.  S5) and upon DMBQ treatment, PjRGF2 peptide treatment, or association with host roots (Fig. 2A). Only PjRGF2 was tested in this assay because P. japonicum was most sensitive to this RGF peptide (Fig. 1C) and prehaustoria were morphologically similar between PjRGF peptides (Fig. 1D). In keeping with the previous findings that auxin response accumulates at the apex of the P. japonicum prehaustorium where PjYUC3 is expressed (14), auxin response localized in the epidermis at the apex of the prehaustorium upon treatment with PjRGF2, DMBQ, or upon association with host roots (Fig. 2A). This is in contrast to auxin response localization solely in the root tip before any prehaustorium-inducing treatments (fig. S5). These data suggest that the prehaustorium development pathway triggered by PjRGF peptides converges on PjYUC3-mediated auxin biosynthesis, echoing the pathway induced by classic HIFs.

Initial observations of untreated P. japonicum roots showed that PjRGF1pro was expressed throughout the root, with the strongest expression at the epidermis. PjRGF1pro expression was also seen at developing lateral roots (Fig.  2B and fig. S6A). In contrast, the PjRGF2pro was typically not observed in a majority of the hairy root images (fig. S2B) However, weak expression in epidermal cells in the differentiation zone and weak expression in the RAM were seen occasionally (fig. S7, A and B). The PjRGF5pro was initially expressed in the cortex, stele, and developing lateral root primordia (Fig. 2B and fig. S6A). In addition, we checked the spatial expression patterns of the PjRGF3 promoter (PjRGF3pro) and the PjRGF4 promoter (PjRGF4pro), two PjRGFs that were not induced by HIF treatment. Both PjRGF3pro and PjRGF4pro were expressed in the RAM, and PjRGF4pro was also expressed in developing lateral roots (fig. S6, A and B). Following these initial observations, expression of these PjRGFpros was checked in the prehaustorium. At 1 day post– DMBQ treatment and 1 day postinoculation with a host root, PjRGF2pro and PjRGF5pro were clearly expressed in the prehaustorium, while PjRGF1pro was expressed in the epidermis throughout the root, regardless of prehaustorium formation (Fig. 2B). PjRGF3pro and PjRGF4pro were not expressed in the prehaustorium (fig. S6B). The expression pattern of the PjRGF2pro and the PjRGF5pro in the prehaustorium differed. The PjRGF2pro was most strongly expressed at the apex of the DMBQ-induced prehaustorium while it was expressed throughout the center and at the base of the prehaustorium when a host was present (Fig. 2B). The expression of PjRGF2pro at 1 day postinfection (DPI) was similar to the location of procambium-like cells in the haustorium (7). In comparison, the PjRGF5pro was expressed at the epidermal cells of both DMBQ and host-induced prehaustoria (Fig. 2B). The expression profile of the PjRGF2pro was asymmetrical during prehaustorial development, while expression of the PjRGF5pro occurred on the developing prehaustorium either symmetrically or asymmetrically at times. Given that PjRGF1, PjRGF2, and PjRGF5 were upregulated at 6 hours post–DMBQ treatment (Fig. 1B), we hypothesized that their expression might localize at this early time point to the transition zone where prehaustoria form. At 6 hours post–DMBQ treatment, PjRGF2pro expression was observed in the epidermis and cortex of the transition zone, and PjRGF5pro showed increased expression in the transition zone relative to untreated roots (fig.  S8). Unlike the PjRGF2pro and PjRGF5pro, there was no noticeable difference in the expression pattern or intensity of the PjRGF1pro at 6 hours post– DMBQ treatment. This may be due to the fact that PjRGF1 expression only increased two- to threefold following DMBQ treatment (Fig. 1B), and, therefore, the strong expression of PjRGF1pro before DMBQ treatment may mask a modest increase in expression. Collectively, these data indicate that PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 are prehaustoriumexpressed RGF peptides likely contributing to haustorium developmental signaling, while other PjRGF peptides may not play a major role. 

# PjPLT localizes to the prehaustorium

If RGF peptides indeed influence haustorium development, a recognizable marker of their activity should be detectable in the prehaustorium. In Arabidopsis, RGF peptides induce $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ production that then stabilizes PLT2 (28). Likewise, $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ was previously shown to accumulate in the prehaustoria of S. hermonthica (19). Consistent with these findings, staining with the $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ stain nitro blue tetrazolium (NBT) showed that $\mathrm { O _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ also accumulated in the prehaustorium of 

P. japonicum (fig.  S9). These observations suggest that a PLT transcription factor might localize to the prehaustorium during its development. Among six predicted PLT homologs in P. japonicum, only four contain two AP2 domains characteristic of PLTs (fig. S10A). The closest homolog to Arabidopsis PLT2 is Pjv1_00021464, which was named PjPLT (fig. S10B). Under its native promoter, mNeonGreentagged PjPLT (mNG-PjPLT) localized to the RAM before DMBQ treatment or host inoculation (fig. S11A). At 1 day post–DMBQ treatment, mNG-PjPLT was observed in the RAM, the transition zone, and the outer cell layers at the apex of the prehaustorium. mNG-PjPLT also localized within the prehaustorium at 1 DPI, shifting to the center of the prehaustorium (Fig. 2C). This pattern appears to be regulated posttranslationally, as a transcriptional reporter for PjPLT was expressed broadly in the meristem, transition, and elongation zones as well as the developing prehaustorium, both at 1 DPT DMBQ and at 1 DPI (fig. S11B). Given that PjRGF peptides induce prehaustoria and RGF peptides stabilize PLT2 in Arabidopisis, we anticipated that, upon exogenous PjRGF treatment, PjPLT would localize to the developing prehaustorium. mNG-PjPLT was present in the prehaustorium following PjRGF1, PjRGF2, or PjRGF5 treatment (Fig. 2C). However, mNG-PjPLT was also found throughout an elongated meristemic zone and in the interior of the prehaustorium (Fig. 2C), diverging from its localization under DMBQ treatment or at 1 DPI. Nevertheless, following both DMBQ and PjRGF treatment, mNG-PjPLT was found in the prehaustorium, indicating that differences in localization still result in successful prehaustorium formation.

# Several PjRGFRs are expressed in the prehaustorium

The receptors for RGF peptides, known as RGFRs/RGIs, were first identified in Arabidopsis (25–27). Similar to RGF peptides, RGFRs are conserved throughout land plants. A phylogenetic analysis of RGFRs from 360 species reveals three main clades and two subclades (fig. S18) (6, 35, 36). P. japonicum encodes six RGFRs, more than most sequenced parasitic Orobanchaceae species (table  S4). PjRGFR1, PjRGFR2, PjRGFR3, and PjRGFR5 were found in clades or subclades that also had an Arabidopsis RGFR homolog, while PjRGFR4 and PjRGFR6 fell into subclades that had no Arabidopsis homolog (fig.  S18). RT-qPCR analysis of cDNA extracted from P. japonicum root tips 6 hours post–DMBQ or N. benthamiana exudate treatments showed that, out of all the PjRGFRs, only PjRGFR3 showed any change in expression as its expression significantly decreased in response to DMBQ treatment (fig. S19). Some LRR-RLKs show transcriptional regulation, including FLS2 and BRI1 (38, 39), and it was possible that PjRGFR3 down-regulation was a response to PjRGF2/5 signaling downstream of HIF perception. 

Spatial expression analysis using PjRGFR promoter– $. 3 \times$ Venus– NLS reporter lines (PjRGFRpro) was performed to further elucidate the role of PjRGFRs during prehaustorium development. Reflective of the RT-qPCR data, all PjRGFRpros were expressed in the root prior to HIF treatment with the specific localization of each PjRGFRpro 

varying (Fig. 4A and fig. S20). The expression patterns of PjRGFR1pro, PjRGFR3pro, and PjRGFR5pro all resembled the expression patterns of their closest homolog in Arabidopsis (25–27). Specifically, PjRGFR1pro was expressed throughout the root, PjRGFR3pro was expressed in the inner cortex of the root, and PjRGFR5pro was most strongly expressed in the RAM and transition zone (Fig. 4A). PjRGFR2 is closely related to PjRGFR1, but the spatial expression pattern of the PjRGFR2pro differed and was only found in the epidermis (Fig. 4A). The PjRGFR4pro is expressed throughout the root beginning at the transition zone and PjRGFR6pro is weakly expressed starting at the differentiation zone (Fig. 4A). Furthermore, PjRGFR3pro and PjRGFR-5pro were found to be expressed in developing lateral roots (fig. S20), while it was unclear whether the other PjRGFRpros were similarly expressed in lateral roots. Several of the PjRGFRpros showed some localization to the prehaustorium (Fig. 4A). Unexpectedly, PjRGFR-3pro expression was not among the PjRGFRpros localized to the prehaustorium. Rather, PjRGFR1pro, PjRGFR2pro, PjRGFR4pro, and PjRGFR5pro showed some localized expression in the prehaustorium. Both PjRGFR1pro and PjRGFR2pro displayed asymmetric expression along the prehaustorium at 1 DPT DMBQ treatment and at 1 DPI, while the PjRGFR4pro and PjRGFR5pro were asymmetrically expressed in the inner cortex of the developing prehaustorium at 1 DPI. The PjRGFR5pro also showed some specificity to the prehaustorium at 1 DPT DMBQ. The PjRGFR6pro was weakly expressed at 1 DPT DMBQ treatment and at 1 DPI. Together, these data imply that multiple P. japonicum RGFRs likely coordinate prehaustorium development. 

Given the difference between RT-qPCR data and promoterdriven expression patterns, we next assessed which PjRGFRs directly bind to PjRGF2 and PjRGF5. As chimeric LRR-RLK receptors have previously aided in probing ligand-receptor interactions (40), we generated PjRGFR-BRI1 chimeras to evaluate their activation by PjRGF2 or PjRGF5 (fig. S21). A shift in the BES1 transcription factor from its phosphorylated to dephosphorylated state served as a readout of receptor activation. In N. benthamiana leaves coexpressing the PjRGFR-BRI1-HA variants and BES1-HA, treatment with 100 nM PjRGF2 for 30 min activated PjRGFR1-BRI1-HA, PjRGFR3- BRI1-HA, and PjRGFR4-BRI1-HA (Fig. 4B), while 100 nM PjRGF5 for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ activated PjRGFR3-BRI1-HA and PjRGFR4-BRI1-HA (Fig. 4C). These results suggest that PjRGFR1, PjRGFR3, and PjRGFR4 function as primary receptors in the prehaustorium signaling cascade of P. japonicum.

# DISCUSSION

Previous insights into the internal signals driving haustorium development beyond the initial HIFs were lacking. Here, we show that RGF peptides function as endogenous signals that positively regulate haustorium formation following DMBQ treatment and are also capable of inducing prehaustoria in P. japonicum and S. hermonthica in the absence of HIFs. Another recently described endogenous peptide in P. japonicum, PjCLE1, can also induce prehaustoria (44). However, PjCLE1 is not expressed in the prehaustorium phase and instead is expressed later, after xylem bridge formation. In contrast, as expected of peptide hormones involved in prehaustorium development, PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 are expressed at early time points following HIF perception (Fig.  1B). Peptide hormones often act redundantly, as demonstrated by RGF, CLE, and TOLS peptides, which all negatively regulate lateral root development in Arabidopsis (45–47). It is therefore possible that multiple peptide hormones regulate haustorium development in parasitic plants, and each can independently induce prehaustoria in $P .$ japonicum. Further work is needed to establish whether additional peptide hormones participate in the P. japonicum prehaustorium induction pathway. 

Earlier work has shown that de novo biosynthesis of auxin through PjYUC3 is essential for haustorium formation (14). Consistent with this role, treatment of P. japonicum with PjRGF1, PjRGF2, and PjRGF5, like DMBQ, up-regulated PjYUC3 in root tips (Fig. 1D). This differs from RGF peptides in Arabidopsis as they are linked to auxin signaling through PIN protein mobilization rather than local auxin biosynthesis (22, 23, 29). This does not rule out the possibility that PjRGF peptides also influence PjPIN distribution in the haustorium, as there is overlap with the spatial expression pattern of PjRGF2pro and PjRGF5pro with PjPIN1 and PjPIN2 (Fig.  2B) (48). However, PIN inhibition in P. japonicum does not affect prehaustorium formation (48). Therefore, it is more likely that 

cell-specific up-regulation of PjYUC3 is the main mechanism through which PjRGFs induce prehaustoria. The RGF peptide pathway in P. japonicum, while operating under the distinct context of haustorium formation, likely diverges from Arabidopsis beyond PLT2 stabilization given the fact that PjRGF2 extends the gradient of PjPLT in the RAM in a way reminiscent of RGF peptides in Arabidopsis (Fig.  2C) (20,  28). A similar divergence was observed between CADL-based DMBQ signaling in P. japonicum and CARD1-based DMBQ signaling in Arabidopsis (15). Therefore, RGF peptide signaling represents another evolutionary innovation in parasitic plants. Further characterization of PjRGF peptides should illuminate additional roles these signals play and the genes they regulate during haustorium development. 

The conservation of RGF peptides in plants, coupled with their unique usage in P. japonicum and S. hermonthica, highlights the need to explore how genes evolve in connection with parasitism. Although our current understanding of plant parasitism is limited, the multiplication and neofunctionalization of KAI2d/ShHTL strigolactone receptors from KAI2 in Orobanchaceae serve as a notable precedent (49, 50). Our findings indicate that PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 represent another set of genes co-opted specifically for parasitic function. Phylogenetic analyses revealed that these two peptides acquired haustorial functionality through distinct evolutionary trajectories: PjRGF2 aligns with other Orobanchaceae homologs, while PjRGF5 clusters with nonhomologous RGF peptides (Fig. 3B). This implies that PjRGF5 is likely a more recent innovation in P. japonicum, arising through a series of segmental tandem duplications and neofunctionalization (Fig.  3A). This may have begun with a duplication of PjRGF8, given the similarity of the PjRGF8 and PjRGF5 prepropeptides and the presence of LTR TEs around PjRGF8 and PjRGF5 copies (Fig. 3, A and B). Tandem duplication followed by neofunctionalization is not unusual in RGF peptides and is seen in Arabidopsis with RGF7, GLV8, and an uncharacterized RGF peptide AT3g02245 (51). PjRGF2, in contrast, is preserved as a single-copy gene in both parasitic and nonparasitic Orobanchaceae species (Fig.  3, B and C, and table  S2), suggesting that its parasitism function may have emerged via functional divergence soon after parasitism originated in the family (fig. S17). Investigating the regulatory mechanisms that control PjRGF2 expression in nonparasitic and parasitic lineages will further unravel the evolution of plant parasitism. 

Haustorium development is integral to plant parasitism, and RGF signaling is now shown to be essential for prehaustorium formation in P. japonicum. Specifically, knockouts of PjRGFR1 and PjRGFR3 significantly reduced DMBQ-induced prehaustorium formation (Fig. 5B). In Arabidopsis, two RGFRs, AtRGFR1 and AtRGFR5, similarly regulate lateral root elongation (21). However, while PjRGFR1 is closely related to AtRGFR1, PjRGFR3 falls in a different clade than AtRGFR5 and is more closely related to AtRGFR3 (fig. S18). AtRGFR3 has not been shown to regulate lateral root elongation and is instead found to potentiate the immune response (52). The spatial expression pattern of PjRGFR3 was similar to that of AtRGFR3 as they were both expressed in the inner cortex of the root (Fig. 4A) (27). It could be that P. japonicum has co-opted an immunity-related RGFR, in PjRGFR3, for haustorial developmental signaling in a similar way to how it co-opted the CADLs (15). Thus, parasitic plants in Orobanchaceae may have combined genes originally involved in lateral root development or immunity signaling for prehaustorium development. 

On the basis of our PjRGFR-BRI1 chimera assay, PjRGFR1 is solely activated by PjRGF2, whereas PjRGFR3 is activated by both PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 (Fig. 4, B and C), implying that PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 act redundantly. As such, a double mutant for PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 may be necessary to observe the phenotype seen in pjrgfr3 and pjrgfr1 knockouts. Although ectodomain-BRI1 chimera assays were originally used to validate known ligand-receptor pairs (40), our results show that they can help pinpoint receptor candidates for endogenous signaling peptides but not necessarily reflect findings found genetically. This is likely the case in complex systems like the RGF signaling pathway as was observed in our data for PjRGFR2 and PjRGFR4. In the case of PjRGFR4, our results suggest that PjRGFR4 likely responds to both PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 in planta, but we could not discern a phenotype in the pjrgfr4 knockouts (Fig. 4, B and C). Notably, PjRGFR4pro and PjRGF2pro expression localizes to the base of the haustorium at 1 DPI (Figs. 2B and 4A), hinting that PjRGFR4 may modulate haustorium development after the initial prehaustorium stage. In contrast, PjRGFR2 appears to regulate cell division at the apex of the prehaustorium in a similar fashion to PjRGF2, but it does not bind to either PjRGF2 or PjRGF5 in planta (Figs. 5 and 4, B and C). This would suggest that while PjRGF2 regulates cell division at the apex of the prehaustorium, there are likely redundant PjRGF peptides other than PjRGF2 and PjRGF5 that play a role in this aspect of prehaustorium development and activate PjRGFR2. Therefore, further investigation into the P. japonicum RGF signaling pathway could provide enlightenment on haustorium development beyond the initial prehaustorium phase and explain how RGF peptide signaling differs in the prehaustorium as compared to the RAM and developing lateral roots (fig. S24). Collectively, our work establishes endogenous RGF peptides and their cognate receptors as critical components of the haustorium developmental signaling pathway in parasitic plants, paving the way for future studies on how these pathways have evolved and diversified. 

# Limitations of this study

The understanding that was derived in this study focused on the RGF signaling pathway and the role it plays in prehaustorium formation in parasitic plants. While it was shown that this pathway, through PjRGFR1 and PjRGFR3, is involved in prehaustorium formation and, through PjRGF2 and PjRGFR2, regulates cell division in the developing prehaustorium, a qualitative change in prehaustorium development was not observed. This is possibly because, as exemplified in this study, of the redundancy found in the PjRGF signaling pathway during prehaustorium development. All transformation in P. japonicum is performed in hairy roots due to difficulties surrounding stable transformation in this plant. We attempted to make multigene knockouts of PjRGFR1/3/4 using hairy root CRISPR, but we were only able to successfully produce pjrgfr1/3/4 knockouts at a rate that was not feasible for adding to this study. From this experience, we concluded that multiple gene knockouts in P. japonicum using hairy root CRISPR are currently not realistic. Given these limitations, we were only able to determine the phenotype associated with knocking out a single gene at a time and were not able to investigate redundancies between PjRGFs or PjRGFRs. This left us unable to fully investigate the redundancies between PjRGFR1, PjRGFR3, and PjRGFR4 and also redundancies between PjRGF2 and PjRGF5. Even more so, given the multiple copies of PjRGF5 in the genome, we were unable to determine whether a pjrgf5 mutant had a haustorium-related phenotype. The difficulty of making multigene hairy root mutants in P. japonicum 

also limited the study to only PjRGFs and PjRGFRs. The mechanistic detail of the role of PjPLT during prehaustorium development was not more thoroughly investigated given that PLT transcription factors typically act redundantly as well (53). The ability to create stable transformants in P. japonicum would enable greater insight into haustorial signaling pathways and the biology of parasitic plants in general.

# Plant material and growth conditions

P. japonicum seeds were surface-sterilized with $5 0 \%$ bleach, sown on $\%$ Murashige and Skoog medium $( \mathrm { M S } ) + 1 \%$ sucrose, and stratified at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 day in the dark. Plates were placed either flat or upright in the growth chamber at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark. After 3 days, plants were moved to 16-hour day/8-hour night cycle [long day (LD) condition]. Six-day-old P. japonicum seedlings were used for hairy root transformation. Seedlings not used for hairy root transformation were transferred to $0 . 7 \%$ water agar $+ 4 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ MES $\mathrm { ( p H 6 . 4 ) }$ after 8 days of growth. Eleven-day-old seedlings were then transferred to $2 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of water $+ 4 . 5 \ \mathrm { m M }$ MES (pH 6.4) for HIF assays. A. thaliana Col-0 seeds were surface sterilized as previously described (48), sown on $\% \mathrm { M S } + 1 \%$ sucrose, stratified at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 days, and grown under $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ LD conditions. Plates were placed upright in the growth chamber for parasitism assays. N. benthamiana seeds were surface sterilized with $5 0 \%$ bleach, sown on $\% \mathrm { M S } + 1 \%$ sucrose, and placed under $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ LD conditions. Seven-day-old N. benthamiana seedlings were transferred to $2 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of water $\mathrm { ~ + ~ } 4 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ MES (pH 6.4). After 1 to 2 weeks, seeds were treated with $1 0 ~ \mathrm { n M }$ strigol to induce germination. 

# P. japonicum hairy root transformation

Hairy root transformation was performed as previously described with slight modifications (54). Briefly, Agrobacterium rhizogenes AR1193 strains were streaked from glycerol stocks on LB supplemented with the appropriate antibiotics 4 days before transformation and grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Two days before transformation, streaked A. rhizogenes AR1193 was used to seed a 3-ml LB liquid culture that was then grown overnight at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After 10 to 14 hours of growth, 1 ml of the A. rhizogenes LB culture was added to $5 \mathrm { m l }$ of Agrobacterium minimal medium (AB):MES salts and grown overnight at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The next day, the bacteria were pelleted and resuspended in $4 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of 10 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 } + 1 0 \ \mathrm { m M }$ MES $( \mathrm { p H } 5 . 5 ) + 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone $+ 0 . 0 1 \%$ Silwet. Six-day-old P. japonicum seedlings were immersed in this solution, sonicated, and vacuum infiltrated and then transferred to $8 5 + 1 \%$ sucrose $+ \ 1 0 \ \mathrm { m M }$ MES $\mathrm { ( p H ~ } 5 . 5 ) \mathrm { ~ + ~ }$ $4 5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone agar plates and kept in the dark for 2 days at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Seedlings were then transferred to $8 5 + 1 \%$ sucrose $^ +$ cefotaxime (300 mg/liter) and kept under $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ LD conditions. Hairy roots typically emerged in 3 to 4 weeks. Plants with hairy roots were transferred to $0 . 7 \%$ water agar $+ 0 . 0 1 \%$ glucose $^ +$ Timentin $\mathrm { ( 1 0 0 m g / }$ liter; GoldBio) and were grown for at least 4 days under $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ LD conditions before further use.

# Alignments and phylogeny

The sequences from annotated and unannotated Orobanchaceae prepropeptides along with several sequences for RGF-like prepropeptides from liverwort and moss species used as an outgroup were aligned using MUSCLE (version 3.8.1551) (61). P. japonicum AP2 transcription factors were first identified through BLAST using PLT1 and AIL1 from Arabidopsis as subjects. An alignment of P. japonicum and Arabidopsis AP2 transcription factors was made using MUSCLE (version 3.8.1551). Alignments were rendered with ESPRIT 3.0 (62). The maximum-likelihood phylogenetic tree for RGF prepropeptides was constructed using MEGA11 with a bootstrap value of 500, while for the AP2 transcription factors, phylogenetic tree UGENE (version 48.1) was used with a bootstrap value of 100 (63,  64). RGFR sequences from 355 species were taken from Ngou et  al. (65). The RGFRs from five additional Orobanchaceae species were added to this group to make a group of RGFRs from 360 species. Sequences were aligned and phylogenetic trees were constructed as described by Ngou et al. (65). EFR and its homologs were used as an outgroup.

# RNA-seq analysis

P. japonicum seedlings were germinated and grown in ½ MS liquid medium supplemented with $0 . 2 5 \%$ sucrose under LD conditions at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ After 2 weeks, $5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ DMBQ and DMSO as a mock control were supplemented to the liquid medium, and then, whole seedlings were collected and snap frozen at six time points: 0 hour, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ , 30 min, 1 hour, 6 hours, and 24 hours.
