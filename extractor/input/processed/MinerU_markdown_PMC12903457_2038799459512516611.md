# ABSTRACT

Triacylglycerol (TAG) degradation plays an important role in seed oil accumulation, but how this catabolic process is transcriptionally regulated in developing seeds remains poorly understood. Here, we identify a MYB-like helix–turn–helix (HTH) transcriptional regulator, PHR1-like 7 (PHL7), that enhances TAG degradation and decreases seed oil accumulation in Arabidopsis thaliana. PHL7 knockout (KO) plants (phl7) exhibited an approximately $10 \%$ increase in seed oil content, whereas PHL7 overexpression (OE) decreased oil content by $8 \% - 1 3 \%$ . Compared with wild-type plants, phl7 displayed reduced expression of TAG degradation genes, including SUGAR-DEPENDENT 1 (SDP1), whereas OE lines exhibited elevated expression. Chromatin immunoprecipitation, electrophoretic mobility shift assays, and transactivation assays demonstrated that PHL7 binds the SDP1 promoter and activates its expression. We further found that PHL7 binds phosphatidic acid (PA), a key intermediate in TAG biosynthesis, and that PA suppresses PHL7 binding to the SDP1 promoter. The lysine residue at position 61 (K61) is required for PA interaction, and introducing the PHL7K61S mutation into phl7 failed to restore seed oil content to wild-type levels. Together, these findings indicate that PHL7 promotes SDP1 expression, whereas PA attenuates this activity, revealing a lipid-mediated mechanism that balances TAG degradation and seed oil accumulation.

# INTRODUCTION

Seed oil accumulation is a complex biological process involving multiple metabolic pathways and regulatory mechanisms (Chapman and Ohlrogge, 2012). Among these processes, triacylglycerol (TAG) degradation, which occurs throughout the seed life cycle, plays a key role in seed development and final oil storage. The initial step of TAG degradation involves the hydrolysis of TAG into fatty acids (FAs) and glycerol by TAG lipases (Li-Beisson et al., 2010, 2013; Xu and Shanklin, 2016). In Arabidopsis thaliana (A. thaliana), more than 30 putative TAG lipases have been annotated (Li-Beisson et al., 2013; Troncoso-Ponce et al., 2013; McGlew et al., 2015; Xu and Shanklin, 2016), and several have been characterized and validated as TAG 

lipases, including SUGAR-DEPENDENT 1 (SDP1), SUGAR-DEPENDENT 1-LIKE (SDP1L), LIPASE 1 (LIP1), Myzus persicaeinduced lipase 1 (MPL1), and SENESCENCE-ASSOCIATED GENE (SAG101) (Li-Beisson et al., 2013; Xu and Shanklin, 2016). SDP1 was initially identified through screens for sugar-dependent mutants during seed germination (Eastmond, 2006; Kelly et al., 2011). Subsequent studies showed that downregulation of SDP1 expression increases seed oil content in crops such as Brassica napus, Glycine max, Jatropha curcas, and Physaria fendleri (Kelly et al., 2013; Kim et al., 2014; Kanai et al., 2019; Azeez et al., 2022; Aznar-Moreno et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2024). Together, these findings indicate that lipid degradation plays an important role in seed oil accumulation; however, the transcriptional regulation of TAG degradation in developing seeds remains poorly understood. 

# Plant Communications

Phosphatidic acid (PA) is a central lipid metabolic intermediate and an important cellular mediator (Stace and Ktistakis, 2006; Liu et al., 2013; Kim and Wang, 2020; Kolesnikov et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). Recent studies indicate that one mode of PA signaling involves its interaction with transcription factors (TFs), thereby affecting gene expression and modulating plant phenotypes (Yao et al., 2024). Screening for PA-binding TFs using an Arabidopsis TF cDNA library identified two PAinteracting TFs, LATE ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL (LHY) and CIRCADIAN CLOCK ASSOCIATED (CCA1). LHY and CCA1 are key regulators of the circadian clock and promote FA biosynthesis and seed oil accumulation (Kim et al., 2019, 2023). In contrast, AThook motif nuclear-localized protein 4 (AHL4) was identified as a PA-binding protein that suppresses TAG degradation during seed germination (Cai et al., 2020). Our previous PA-binding screen identified additional putative PA-interacting TF candidates (Kim et al., 2019), motivating further investigation of PA and its potential role in the transcriptional regulation of TAG catabolism in developing seeds. 

PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1 (PHR1)-LIKE 7 (PHL7) is one such candidate that may bind PA (Kim et al., 2019). PHL7 belongs to the MYB-like helix–turn–helix (HTH) TF family in Arabidopsis, which includes PHR1 and PHL1 to PHL7. Among these, PHR1, PHL1, PHL2, PHL3, and PHL4 play crucial roles in phosphate uptake, transport, and utilization (Zhou et al., 2008; Bustos et al., 2010; Guo et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2016; Ruan et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021). Although the function of PHL7 has not been characterized in Arabidopsis, a recent study suggests that a rice homolog contributes to phosphate homeostasis (Yang et al., 2024). Here, we show that PHL7 promotes TAG degradation during seed development and decreases seed oil accumulation. We further demonstrate that the lipid mediator PA interacts with PHL7 and suppresses its promotion of seed oil degradation. 

# RESULTS

# The MYB-like HTH protein PHL7 interacts with PA

Our previous lipid-binding proteomic screen identified PHL7 as a candidate protein that co-precipitated with PA (Kim et al., 2019). To validate this interaction, PHL7 was expressed and purified from Escherichia coli (E. coli) (Figure 1A). A protein– lipid overlay assay showed that PHL7 bound PA but not other phospholipids tested, including phosphatidylcholine (PC), phosphatidylglycerol (PG), and phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) (Figure 1B). Among the PA species tested, PHL7 bound PAs containing 18:1 and 18:2 acyl chains (18:1–18:1, 18:2–18:2, and 16:0–18:2 PA), but not PAs composed solely of saturated 16:0 or 18:0 acyl chains (Figure 1C). A liposome-binding assay further showed that PHL7 co-precipitated with liposomes containing PA species enriched in 18:1 and 18:2, but not with liposomes containing saturated 16:0 or 18:0 PAs (Figure 1D). Together, these results demonstrate that PHL7 specifically interacts with PA, and preferentially binds PA species containing at least one unsaturated 18:1 or 18:2 acyl chain.

Protein–lipid overlay assays showed that, aside from L1 and L6, the other fragments (L2, L3, L4, and L5) did not show detectable PA-binding signals (Figure 2B), suggesting that the N-terminal region containing the MYB-like HTH motif contributes to PA binding. To identify specific residues involved in PA interaction, we compared the amino acid sequence of PHL7 with those of related MYB family TFs. The MYB-type HTH domain showed strong conservation (Figure 2C and Supplemental Figure 1). Positively charged arginine (R) and lysine (K) residues are known to participate in PA binding (Zhang et al., 2004, 2009; Kooijman et al., 2007; Shen et al., 2020). Therefore, we mutated the N-terminal R and K residues to serine (S) and glycine (G), respectively (Figure 2C). PA-binding assays indicated that the K61S mutation $( \mathsf { P H L 7 } ^ { \mathsf { K G 1 S } } )$ reduced PA binding compared with wild-type (WT) PHL7 (PHL7WT) (Figure 2D). A liposome-binding assay further confirmed the reduced PA binding of ${ \mathsf { P H L 7 } } ^ { \mathsf { K 6 1 S } }$ (Figure 2E). These findings indicate that lysine 61 (K61) contributes to PA binding. Interestingly, the L4 fragment containing both the MYBtype HTH and the coiled-coil domain did not bind PA (Figure 2A and 2B). This suggests that the coiled-coil region, in the absence of the C-terminal sequence, may inhibit PA binding. 

# PHL7 decreases seed oil accumulation

To investigate the function of PHL7 (At2g01060), we obtained two T-DNA insertion mutants, phl7-1 (CS882922) and phl7-2 (SALK_034222), and identified homozygous lines by PCR genotyping and real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR) analysis (Figure 3A–3C). Both mutants exhibited negligible transcript levels compared with WT (Figure 3C), suggesting disruption of PHL7 expression. Additionally, we generated plants overexpressing PHL7 under the control of the CaMV-35S promoter. qPCR analysis of T1 plants showed up to a 126-fold increase in PHL7 transcript levels in the leaves of PHL7 overexpression (OE) lines (Figure 3D). Immunoblotting confirmed the production of PHL7-HA protein (Figure 3E), and OE12 and OE13 lines were selected for further analysis based on their different expression levels of PHL7. Next, we complemented phl7-1 by introducing either the genomic sequence of WT PHL7 $( P H L 7 ^ { W T } )$ or a mutant with impaired PA binding $( P H L 7 ^ { K G 1 S } )$ under control of the native PHL7 promoter. qPCR analysis showed that PHL7 expression in the $P H L 7 ^ { W T }$ complementation (Com) lines Com1, Com5, and Com9 was comparable to WT, whereas the $P H L 7 ^ { K 6 1 S }$ Com lines Com15, Com16, Com17, and Com18 showed higher expression (Figure 3F). 

PHL7 exhibited high expression across all tissues tested, with particularly strong expression in flowers and seeds, compared with other MYB-like HTH transcriptional regulators (Supplemental Figure 1). We therefore examined its impact on seed oil content in mature seeds. Seed oil content increased by approximately $10 \%$ in both phl7 mutants but decreased by $8 \% - 1 3 \%$ in PHL7 OE lines. In phl7-1, seed oil content was restored to WT levels by complementation with $P H L 7 ^ { W T }$ , confirming that loss of PHL7 function leads to decreased seed oil accumulation (Figure 4A). By contrast, expression of $P H L 7 ^ { K 6 1 S }$ in the phl7-1 background failed to restore seed oil content to WT levels (Figure 4A). These results indicate that PHL7 suppresses seed oil accumulation and that its PA-binding activity is required for this function.

# Effects of PHL7 alterations on responses to P deficiency and seed germination

PHL7 is a member of the PHR1-like family. PHR1, PHL1, PHL2, PHL3, and PHL4 are known to be involved in phosphate (P) uptake, transport, and utilization in plants; however, the contribution 

of PHL7 to the Arabidopsis response to P deficiency remains unclear. Thus, we tested the effects of PHL7 alterations on plant responses to P starvation, using the phr1 mutant as a control because PHR1 is a well-established regulator of this process (Rubio et al., 2001; Sun et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2024). Seedlings of the phl7-1 mutant showed no significant differences in root architecture or in the expression of P-responsive genes compared with WT under P-deficient conditions (Supplemental Figure 6). Similarly, the sdp1 mutant showed no detectable response to P starvation (Supplemental Figure 7). These results indicate that loss of PHL7 does not compromise responses to phosphate deficiency in Arabidopsis seedlings. 

We also tested whether PHL7 alterations affect seed germination, as PHL7 enhances SDP1 expression, which contributes to TAG degradation during germination (Eastmond, 2006). During the first 36 h of imbibition and in the absence of sucrose, phl7 mutant seeds germinated more slowly, whereas PHL7-OE seeds germinated faster than WT (Supplemental Figure 8A). # DISCUSSION

Transcriptional regulation plays an important role in orchestrating seed oil accumulation (Focks and Benning, 1998; Cernac and Benning, 2004; Kong et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022). Considerable progress has been made in understanding the transcriptional regulation of seed oil biosynthesis but far less is known about the regulation of TAG degradation during seed development (Yang et al., 2022). In this study, we identify PHL7 as a regulator that promotes the expression of genes involved in TAG degradation in developing seeds. Loss of PHL7 increased seed oil content, whereas PHL7 OE decreased it, indicating that PHL7 functions as a negative regulator of seed oil accumulation during seed development. 

A previous study showed that AHL4 regulates the expression of genes involved in TAG degradation during early seed germination and seedling growth (Cai et al., 2020). Although both PHL7 and AHL4 transcriptionally regulate TAG degradation, they exert opposite effects. PHL7 enhances SDP1 expression, whereas AHL4 suppresses it. Consistently, AHL4 knockout (KO) lines germinate faster, whereas AHL4 OE delays germination. In contrast, phl7 mutants show slightly delayed germination, whereas PHL7-OE seeds germinate faster than WT. Our results further suggest that PHL7 primarily affects TAG degradation and oil accumulation during seed development, whereas AHL4 acts mainly during germination and early seedling growth. These results also indicate that PA is a shared regulator of both PHL7 and AHL4 but with different consequences for TAG accumulation. PA binding to PHL7 attenuates its ability to enhance SDP1 expression and TAG degradation, thereby promoting TAG accumulation in developing seeds. By contrast, PA binding to AHL4 enhances TAG hydrolysis and degradation during seedling establishment. 

The PA–PHL7 interaction raises several intriguing questions regarding the subcellular location, sources, and functional significance of this interaction. Immunoblot analyses indicate that PHL7 is predominantly localized in the nucleus, suggesting that PA–PHL7 complexes likely form in the nucleus and do not involve nucleocytoplasmic shuttling. Several enzymes have been implicated in modulating nuclear PA levels (Yao et al., 2024). For example, the phospholipase D (PLD) double mutant pldα1 pldδ displays a $3 5 . 5 \%$ reduction in nuclear PA, whereas pah1 pah2 mutants show a $3 2 . 5 \%$ increase in nuclear PA compared with WT (Cai et al., 2020). In addition, PA produced by plasmamembrane-associated PLDδ can translocate into the nucleus in response to heat stress (Kim et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). A recent study further revealed that the DAG kinase DGK5, which 

phosphorylates DAG to PA, translocates into the nucleus upon stress (Li et al., 2024). Thus, nuclear PA may originate from enzymes associated with the nucleus and/or from extranuclear PA transported into the nucleus. Future studies will be needed to decipher whether the PA that binds PHL7 comes from de novo PA biosynthesis in the endoplasmic reticulum or from membrane lipid turnover. 

The nuclear PA–PHL7 interaction may act as a signal responding to cellular lipid metabolic status. PA is a central intermediate in TAG biosynthesis and also a mediator modulating various metabolic and cellular processes (Zhang et al., 2004; Kim et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Kim and Wang, 2020; Yao et al., 2024). Our results indicate that PA binding to PHL7 attenuates its promotion of SDP1 expression, thus suppressing TAG degradation. On the other hand, PA interacts with LHY/CCA1 and reduces their enhancement of KASII expression in developing seeds of Arabidopsis (Kim et al., 2023). These findings support the idea that PA–TF interactions may sense cellular PA levels and finetune the balance between lipid biosynthetic and degradative activities. They also raise the question of how PA influences both PHL7, which is involved in TAG degradation, and LHY/ CCA1, which are involved in FA biosynthesis. In our study, we found that PA (18:1- and 18:2-PA) competitively inhibited PHL7 binding to the SDP1 promoter. In contrast, PA (specifically 16:0-PA) competitively inhibits LHY/CCA binding to the KASIII promoter (Kim et al., 2019, 2023). These observations suggest that distinct TFs may preferentially interact with specific PA species, thereby modulating seed oil biosynthesis and degradation during seed development. 

To probe how PA affects TF function, we mapped the PA-binding site of PHL7 to its N-terminal region that contains the MYB-type HTH motif. Interestingly, the PHL7 fragment (L4), which contains both the MYB-type HTH motif and the coiled-coil domain, failed to bind PA. This suggests that the coiled-coil region—potentially involving negatively charged residues such as LHEQLE—may interfere with PA interaction. However, this inhibition appears to be relieved by sequences in the downstream C-terminal region, as full-length PHL7 retains PA-binding activity. In addition, we identified Lys 61 (K61) as a key residue required for PA binding. Complementation of the phl7-1 mutant with PHL7 driven by its native promoter restored seed oil content, whereas ${ \mathsf { P H L 7 } } ^ { \mathsf { K 6 1 S } }$ failed to rescue the oil phenotype (Figures 3F and 4A). These results suggest that the PA–PHL7 interaction is functionally important for regulating seed oil accumulation. Notably, K61 of PHL7 corresponds to K271 of PHR1, and mutation of K271 abolishes PHR1 binding to the MYB–P1BS motif (Jiang et al., 2019). Likewise, we found that mutation of K61 similarly impaired PHL7 binding to the P1BS motif in the SDP1 promoter. Together, these findings indicate that K61 of PHL7 is required for interaction with both PA and target DNA, providing a mechanistic explanation for how PA suppresses PHL7 DNA-binding activity and transcriptional function. An intriguing but unresolved observation is that only lines with 2- to 3-fold higher expression levels were obtained in Com PHL7K61S plants, whereas Com PHL7WT lines exhibited expression levels comparable to WT. One possible explanation is that expression of the PA-binding $\mathsf { P H L 7 } ^ { \mathsf { W T } }$ is maintained under homeostatic control, whereas this regulatory mechanism may be compromised in the PA-binding–defective ${ \mathsf { P H L 7 } } ^ { \mathsf { K 6 1 S } }$ variant. 

While PHL7 KO and OE decreased and increased SDP1 expression, respectively, PHL7 alterations also impacted the expression of other lipases. Although PHL7 binds the SDP1 promoter, whether it affects the expression of other lipases directly or indirectly requires further investigation. Nevertheless, the elevated expression of multiple lipases likely explains why levels of TAG, DAG, and phospholipids were decreased in PHL7-OE lines, as some lipases can hydrolyze both TAG and DAG. For example, SDP1 exhibits similar specific activities toward TAG and DAG (Eastmond 2006), and natural variation in LIP1 has been associated with decreases in both lipids (Garbowicz et al., 2018). Lipases can also affect membrane lipid levels by altering the flux of FAs, DAG, and PA, which serve as precursors for membrane lipid biosynthesis. In Arabidopsis, loss of SDP1 increases TAG, PC, and PE accumulation, whereas OE of SDP1 decreases TAG content and accelerates FA turnover (Fan et al., 2014). Moreover, in PHL7-OE seeds, reduced DAG levels were accompanied by increased expression of the DAG-producing enzyme PAH2 and decreased expression of the DAG-utilizing enzyme PDAT2, suggesting compensatory responses to decreased cellular DAG levels. 

PHL7 belongs to the PHL family, members of which have been extensively studied for their roles in responses to phosphate availability. The finding that PA–PHL7 interactions regulate TAG hydrolysis and content raises the possibility that PA may act as a P sensor connecting P availability to TAG production and seed germination. However, WT and PHL7-KO plants displayed similar root growth responses to P deficiency. In addition, PHL7-OE plants developed purple leaves under P deficiency, like WT, whereas the phr1 mutant maintained green leaves. These results suggest that, unlike PHR1, PHL7 may not be a key player in plant P starvation at the seedling stage. Consistent with this notion, a recent study showed that single KOs of PHL1 or PHL4 showed negligible phenotypes under low P, despite their closer evolutionary relationship to PHR1 (clade A) than to PHL7 (Wang et al., 2024). While these results do not rule out redundant functions among PHL family members in P utilization, our results clearly indicate that PHL7 has additional roles, such as modulation of TAG degradation. Further analyses will be needed to determine whether PA functions as a P sensor that connects P availability with seed oil metabolism. 

In summary, this study demonstrates that the MYB-like HTH transcriptional regulator PHL7 suppresses seed oil accumulation and reveals a mechanism in which PHL7 binds to the SDP1 promoter to enhance expression of this TAG-degrading enzyme. Our results also indicate that the central lipid metabolite PA binds to PHL7 and attenuates its activation of SDP1, thereby reducing TAG degradation and promoting seed oil accumulation.

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Seeds of PHL7 T-DNA insertion mutants (phl7-1, CS882922; phl7-2, SALK_034222), the PHR1 T-DNA mutant (SALK_067629C), and the SDP1 T-DNA mutant (SALK_102887) in A. thaliana were obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (https://abrc.osu.edu/). Homozygous mutants were confirmed by PCR using primer pairs designed with the T-DNA Primer Design tool (http://signal.salk.edu/tdnaprimers.2. html). To obtain PHL7 OE lines, the PHL7 coding sequence was amplified from Arabidopsis cDNA using primer pairs containing XbaI and SmaI restriction sites (Supplemental Table 2). The amplified fragment was cloned into the pBin35sRed1 vector, driven by the CaMV 35S promoter and tagged with HA. The binary vector was introduced into WT Arabidopsis plants via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation (Clough and Bent, 1998). Positive OE plants were screened by observing T0 seeds under a green flashlight with a red-light filter and then confirmed by PCR genotyping. 

To complement the phl7 mutants, the full genomic sequence of PHL7, including a 1.5-kb promoter region, was amplified using primers with an AscI site (Supplemental Table 2) and ligated into the pEC291 binary vector. The ${ \mathsf { P H L 7 } } ^ { \mathsf { K 6 1 S } }$ construct was generated in the same manner, except for the K61 mutation. Both constructs were transformed into the phl7-1 background via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Positive Com plants were selected by surface-sterilizing the seeds and planting them on half-strength Murashige–Skoog (MS) medium containing hygromycin B ( $1 0 0 \mathrm { \ m g / m l } )$ . After two weeks, putative transformants were transferred to soil and confirmed by PCR using primers specific to the vector and PHL7 (Supplemental Table 2). 

For phenotyping, plants were grown in a PGW40 growth chamber (Conviron, Canada) under the same conditions (temperature/light cycle of 16 h at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in light/8 h at $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in darkness). For phosphate stress assays, seeds were sown on phosphate-sufficient medium $\mathsf { 1 } \mathsf { m M K H _ { 2 } P O _ { 4 } }$ on halfstrength MS agar plates) or phosphate-deficient medium (0 mM ${ \sf K H } _ { 2 } { \sf P O } _ { 4 }$ on half-strength MS agar plates). For germination assays, seeds were sown on half-strength MS medium with or without $1 \%$ (w/v) sucrose. 

To collect developing seeds, three individual plants per genotype were marked at the main branch and four secondary branches (Supplemental Figure 11). The silique adjacent to each mark was used to determine sampling time. For each collection, three siliques near the mark were detached, gently opened, and the seeds were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for subsequent analyses. 

# Real-time qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from seedlings and seeds using the RNeasy Plant Mini Kit (Qiagen, cat. #74904). Because seeds contain high levels of oils, proteins, and polyphenols that can interfere with RNA quality, polyvinylpyrrolidone (Sigma, cat. #9003-39-8) was included to improve RNA purification (Kanai et al., 2017). RNA samples were treated with the DNA-free Kit (Invitrogen, cat. #AM1906) to remove genomic DNA. RNA integrity and concentration were assessed by agarose gel electrophoresis and NanoDrop spectrophotometry, respectively.

# Lipid extraction and analysis

To measure seed oil content and FA composition, 5–10 mg of oven-dried developing or mature seeds were weighed and placed in glass tubes with Teflon-lined screw caps. Methanol $( 1 . 5 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { I } )$ containing $1 . 5 \%$ (v/v) $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S O } _ { 4 }$ and $0 . 0 1 \%$ (w/v) butylated hydroxytoluene (BHT) was added, followed by $2 5 ~ \mu \up$ of $1 6 . 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l / m l } }$ heptadecanoic acid (17:0 FA; Sigma, cat. #506-12-7) as an internal standard. Tubes were tightly capped and incubated at ${ \mathfrak { g o c } }$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , then cooled to room temperature. Then, 1.0 ml of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ and 2.0 ml of hexane were added, and samples were briefly vortexed and centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 10 min. Approximately 1 ml of the upper organic phase was transferred to vials for gas chromatography (GC) analysis. GC parameters (Shimadzu, Brevis GC-2050) followed previously published conditions (Kim et al., 2023). In this experiment, 17:0 FA was used as the internal standard to quantify the levels of oil and FAs. Seed oil content is presented as a percentage of seed dry weight. Changes are expressed as percentage increases or decreases relative to WT. 

For lipidomic profiling, total lipids were extracted from developing seeds following an established method (Zien et al., 2001). Seeds were immersed in 2 ml of isopropanol at $7 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $0 . 0 1 \%$ BHT for 15 min, cooled, and then extracted three times with $3 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of chloroform/ methanol (2:1, v/v) containing $0 . 0 1 \%$ (w/v) BHT. Combined extracts were then washed with 1 ml of 1 M KCl, followed by a wash with 1 ml of water. The upper phase was discarded, and the lower organic phase was dried under nitrogen gas. For each 1 mg of seed dry weight, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of chloroform was added to redissolve the lipid fraction. Samples were mixed with chloroform/methanol/ammonium acetate $( 3 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ in water) (300:665:35 [v/v/v]) and internal standards to a final volume of $1 . 4 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ and measured by electrospray ionization coupled with tandem mass spectrometry. The contents of phospholipids and DAG were quantified according to the internal standards: 0.3 nmol PA (di-14:0-PA, di-Phy(20:0)-PA), 0.6 nmol PC (di-12:0-PC, di-24:1-PC), 0.3 nmol PE (di-12:0-PE, di-Phy(20:0)-PE), 0.3 nmol PG (di-14:0-PG, di-Phy(20:0)-PG), and 1 nmol DAG (di-15:0-DAG). Phospholipids were quantified by comparing lipid peak intensities with the corresponding internal standards (Welti et al., 2002; Liu and Wang, 2016). DAG species were detected as $[ M + \mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ] ^ { + }$ ions through neutral loss scans, corrected, combined, and subsequently quantified using the internal standard di-15:0-DAG) (Li et al., 2024). 

A thin-layer chromatography (TLC) plate (silica gel $6 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 5 4 }$ , Merck, cat. #1.05715) was used to separate TAG from total lipids. Twenty microliters of total lipids was spotted onto the TLC plate, which was then developed in a 70:30:1 (v/v/v) mixture of hexane, diethyl ether, and acetic acid for approximately 1 h. The plate was air-dried under a fume hood and then exposed to iodine vapor to visualize TAG. 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)

ChIP was performed using the EpiQuik Plant ChIP Kit (Epigen Tek, cat. #P-2014) to investigate interactions between PHL7 and genomic DNA. In brief, Arabidopsis plants overexpressing HA-tagged PHL7 were used. A total of ${ { 2 \mathord { - } } 3 } \ { 9 }$ of siliques from 45-day-old Arabidopsis plants was harvested and immediately crosslinked in $1 \%$ formaldehyde under vacuum for 10 min. Crosslinking was quenched with 1.25 ml of 2 M glycine under vacuum for an additional 5 min. After two washes with distilled water, samples were ground in lysis buffer I (CP3C). The resulting homogenate was filtered through two layers of Miracloth and centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 20 min. The pellet was resuspended in 1 ml of lysis buffer II (CP3D) and centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for $1 0 \mathrm { \ m i n }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to enrich nuclei. The 

supernatant was carefully removed, and the pellet was resuspended in $3 0 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ of lysis buffer III (CP3E) containing $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol. An additional ${ 3 0 0 } \mu \iota$ of CP3E was added to a new 1.5-ml vial, and the resuspended pellet was layered on top and centrifuged at 14 000 rpm for 60 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The supernatant was removed, and the chromatin pellet was resuspended in $5 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of lysis buffer IV (CP3F) containing protease inhibitor cocktail (PIC). DNA was sheared by sonication (10 cycles of 15 s on and 45 s off at power level 3) using a Fisher Scientific Sonic Dismembrator Model 100. Fragment size was checked by digesting $1 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu \mu$ of crosslinked DNA with proteinase K prior to agarose gel analysis. The length of sheared DNA was between 100 and 400 bp (Supplemental Figure 12). The above supernatant DNA (input) was incubated in HA-bound strip wells at room temperature for 2 h, and WT DNA without the HA tag served as a negative control. Strip wells were washed six times to remove unbound proteins. After digestion with proteinase K, reversal of crosslinks, and DNA purification according to the manufacturer’s instructions, the final DNA was used for ChIP–PCR. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

EMSA was conducted with minor modifications to a previously described protocol (Kim et al., 2023). DNA probes consisted of a 49 bp fragment from the SDP1 promoter containing the P1BS motif and a mutated version in which the PHL7 binding site within the motif was disrupted (Supplemental Table 2). To prepare DNA probes, single-stranded complementary oligonucleotides were self-annealed in TE buffer (10 mM Tris, 50 mM NaCl, and 1 mM EDTA $\left[ \mathsf { p H } \ 8 . 0 \right] )$ by incubating at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 5 min followed by slow cooling to room temperature. The resulting double-stranded DNA (20 pmol total) was incubated with purified PHL7 protein $( 1 - 2 0 \mu 9 )$ and PA $\mathfrak { i } ( 0 . 1 \mathfrak { - } 1 0 \mu \mathfrak { g } )$ at room temperature for 30 min. After the incubation, reactions were supplemented with $30 \%$ glycerol and separated on a $2 \%$ (w/v) agarose gel containing SYBR at $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { V }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min. DNA bands were visualized using an iBright FL1500 imaging system (Invitrogen). 

# Transactivation assay

Transactivation assays were performed using effector and reporter constructs in vitro. For effectors, the PHL7 coding sequence was ligated into p35S-FAST/eYFP as described previously, and an empty vector lacking PHL7 served as a control (Guo et al., 2014). For reporters, either the 2128-bp upstream region of SDP1 containing the P1BS motif or a shorter 1242-bp region lacking the motif was ligated into pFLASH-fixer containing luciferase, replacing the KASIII promoter using SacI and AscI digestion (Kim et al., 2023). Primer sequences are provided in Supplemental Table 2. 

Transformed Agrobacterium cultures were resuspended in infiltration buffer (10 mM ${ \sf M g S O _ { 4 } }$ , 10 mM 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid [MES, pH 5.2], 100 mM acetosyringone). Agrobacterium-mediated transformation was used to co-infiltrate the leaves of 3-week-old tobacco plants with the indicated combinations of effector and reporter constructs. After 2 days, total protein was extracted from infiltrated leaves, and 50 mg of protein was mixed with $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ luciferin in a 96-well plate. Luminescence and YFP fluorescence were quantified using a microplate reader (Infinite M200pro, Tecan).
