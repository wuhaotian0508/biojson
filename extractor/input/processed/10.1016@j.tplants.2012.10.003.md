The strigolactones are rhizosphere signaling molecules as well as a new class of plant hormones with a still increasing number of biological functions being uncovered. Here, we review a recent major breakthrough in our understanding of strigolactone biosynthesis, which has revealed the unexpected simplicity of the originally postulated complex pathway. Moreover, the discovery and localization of a strigolactone exporter sheds new light on putative strigolactone fluxes to the rhizosphere as well as within the plant. The combination of these data with information on the expression and regulation of strigolactone biosynthetic and downstream signaling genes provides new insights into how strigolactones control the many different aspects of plant development and how their rhizosphere signaling role may have evolved.

# Strigolactones induce the germination of parasitic plants

The strigolactones are a small class of carotenoid-derived compounds. To date, 15 different members have been structurally characterized, but even more have been tentatively identified [1,2]. Their typical structure comprises four rings (A–D), of which the C–D part is highly conserved and essential for biological activity, whereas the A and B rings show considerable variation due to different side groups [3] (Figure 1). The first natural strigolactone to be identified was strigol, which was isolated as a germination stimulant of the root parasitic plant Striga lutea (witchweed) [4]. Since then, a series of other strigolactones have been identified as germination stimulants of other root parasitic plants belonging to the witchweeds (Striga spp.), broomrapes (Orobanche and Phelipanche spp.) and Alectra spp., in many different host (and non-host) plant species [2,5–8]. Root parasitic plants have little or no photosynthetic capacity and limited seed reserves. After germination, their survival is determined by the possibility to connect to a host plant within a couple of days of germination, which they then exploit as a source of water, assimilates and nutrients [9]. Strigolactones can induce parasitic plant germination at extremely low concentrations. Susceptible hosts for Striga spp. include staple crops such as maize (Zea mays), millet and sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), making these parasites a

serious agricultural pest, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa where they constitute the main biotic cause of crop loss [10]. Broomrapes (Orobanche and Phelipanche spp.) also constitute a serious threat to agriculture and are weeds in crops growing under more temperate climatic conditions such as legumes, sunflower (Helianthus annuus), rapeseed (Brassica napus), and tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) [11].

# Biological relevance of strigolactones in a broader context

As could perhaps have been anticipated, strigolactones not only have negative effects in host plants. Strigolactones are involved in the regulation of symbiosis between plants and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) [12]. Approximately $80 \%$ of land plants engage in symbiosis with AMF of the Glomeromycota [13]. In this symbiosis, fixed carbon from the plant is exchanged for mineral nutrients from the fungus in arbuscules, which are highly branched fungal structures in the root cortical cells of the host plant. The extensive network of fine fungal hyphae in the soil extends the plant root system in a resource-efficient manner, allowing the capture of a range of nutrients from larger soil volumes. This is particularly important for nutrients with low mobility in soil such as phosphorus [13]. Strigolactones in the rhizosphere trigger hyphal branching in AMF, a key step in the root colonization process [13]. Consistent with their role in activating AMF symbiosis, strigolactone biosynthesis and exudation from roots is upregulated by nutrient deficiency [14–17]. Strigolactones have also been reported to be involved in communication in the primitive plant Physcomitrella patens [18]. Knocking out putative strigolactone biosynthetic genes in P. patens, such as $P p C C D 8$ , leads to a reduction in the secretion of a signal inhibiting protonema growth of neighboring colonies in a quorum sensing-like manner which could be complemented with synthetic strigolactone GR24 [18].

It is not clear how strigolactones are perceived by plants or fungi. A strigolactone receptor on the plasma membrane of these organisms seems likely given that the strigolactone signal would be encountered first at the plasma membrane. However, to date, a strigolactone-specific receptor has not been described in plants or fungi; however, see below for further discussion of strigolactone perception. # Strigolactone biosynthesis

The lack of strigolactones in carotenoid-deficient mutants and the reduced levels of strigolactones in exudates of plants treated with inhibitors of carotenoid biosynthesis suggested that strigolactones are derived from carotenoids [1]. Given that strigolactones consist of an ABC-ring and a D-ring, it was assumed that these two moieties have different origins and are coupled in a later biosynthetic step. This led to the suggestion that carotenoids provide the ABC-part, arising via a complex pathway from a $\mathrm { C _ { 1 5 } }$ - carotenoid cleavage product [1,21]. The breakthrough discovery of the role of strigolactones in the regulation of shoot branching and tillering revealed that two carotenoid cleavage dioxygenases (CCDs), CCD7 and CCD8, are involved in the proposed carotenoid cleavage [19,20] (Figure 2). Genetic evidence in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) suggested that a cytochrome P450 (CYP), More Axillary Growth 1 (MAX1), is also required for strigolactone biosynthesis [19,20,22] and this was later confirmed by strigolactone measurements [23] (Figure 2). Mutants in orthologs of max1 in other plant species have not been reported [24], possibly owing to redundancy. Indeed, in the rice (Oryza sativa) genome, five other CYP genes with homology to MAX1 have been detected [25]. In 2009, a study showed that rice Dwarf27 (D27), an iron-binding enzyme with (at that time) unknown catalytic properties, was also required for strigolactone formation [24,26].

CCD7 and CCD8 belong to a ubiquitous family of enzymes that catalyze the oxidative cleavage of carotenoid double bonds, yielding carbonyl products called apocarotenoids [27,28]. Some carotenoid-cleavage dioxygenases cleave apocarotenoids instead of carotenoids [29]. Other, related enzymes, such as the 9-cis-epoxycarotenoid cleavage dioxygenases (NCEDs), which produce xanthoxin (the precursor of abscisic acid) are stereo-specific and exclusively cleave 9-cis-configured xanthophylls [30]. Initially, it

was suggested that the Arabidopsis CCD7 cleaves alltrans- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene $\bf ( C _ { 4 0 } )$ into $\beta$ -ionone $\mathrm { ( C _ { 1 3 } ) }$ and all-trans-$\beta$ -apo- $\mathbf { 1 0 ^ { \prime } }$ -carotenal $\bf ( C _ { 2 7 } )$ , which is then cleaved by AtCCD8 to the $\mathbf { C } _ { 1 8 }$ -ketone $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone and a presumed $\mathrm { C _ { 9 } }$ - dialdehyde [31,32]. However, the $\mathbf { C } _ { 1 8 }$ compound $\beta$ -apo-13- carotenone did not appear to be a logical intermediate in the biosynthesis of the $\mathrm { C _ { 1 4 } }$ strigolactone backbone, and CCD7 and CCD8 function were therefore examined more carefully [33].

The assumption that the AtCCD7 substrate is all-transconfigured was based on its activity in all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ -caroteneaccumulating Escherichia coli strains [32,34] and on in vitro incubations with $\beta$ -carotene extracted from plant tissues [32]. However, carotenoids and apocarotenoids are sensitive to photo- and thermo-isomerization in E. coli cells and, without appropriate high-performance liquid chromatography-separation, all-trans- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene samples prepared from plant tissues may contain other isomers, such as 9-cis- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene, that may serve as the AtCCD7 substrate. This presumption was substantiated by a recent in vitro investigation in which pure synthetic carotenoid isomers were used [33]. This study demonstrated that heterologously expressed CCD7 from Arabidopsis, pea (Pisum sativum) and rice is stereo-specific, cleaving only 9-cis- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene (Figure 2). Moreover, comparison with authentic standards shows that $\beta$ -apo- $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathbf { 1 0 ^ { \prime } }$ -carotenal produced by CCD7 maintains the cis-configuration of the C9–C10 double bond. The discovery of the isomer-specificity of CCD7 paved the way for the elucidation of the major step in the pathway that connects carotenoids with strigolactones. In vitro incubations with CCD8 uncovered the unusual catalytic property and isomer-selectivity of this enzyme, enabling the formation of the strigolactone-like compound carlactone from 9-cis- $\cdot \beta$ -apo- $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathbf { 1 0 ^ { \prime } }$ -carotenal in one step; supposedly involving a series of different reactions, including cis–trans-isomerization, Baeyer–Villiger-like rearrangements and repeated dioxygenations [33] (Figure 2). Carlactone has strigolactone-like biological activity (it stimulates germination of Striga and rescues the high-tillering phenotype of strigolactone biosynthetic mutants $d \boldsymbol { { 1 0 } }$ and $d 2 7$ in rice) and its structure already strongly resembles strigolactones in that it consists of a $\mathbf { C _ { 1 9 } }$ -skeleton with a $\mathrm { C _ { 1 4 } }$ -moiety connected via an enol– ether bridge to a butenolide group corresponding to the D-ring in the strigolactones (Figures 1 and 2).

The requirement of a 9-cis-configured substrate subsequently raised questions about its origin. The plant enzyme lycopene- $\cdot \beta$ -cyclase, which catalyzes the synthesis of $\beta$ -carotene, has been shown to be stereo-selective and to exclusively form all-trans-configured $\beta$ -carotene [35]. Given that hormone biosynthesis is tightly regulated, it seemed unlikely that the 9-cis- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene required for strigolactone biosynthesis arises via a non-enzymatic and less-controlled mechanism such as photo-isomerization. Therefore, the existence of an all-trans to 9-cis-isomerase was postulated. Considering that the strigolactone biosynthetic enzyme D27 is plastid localized (implying involvement early in the strigolactone pathway) [26], the catalytic activity of this enzyme was investigated and D27 was indeed shown to have all-trans to 9-cis- $\cdot \beta$ -carotene isomerase activity [33] (Figure 2). The biosynthesis of strigolactones is likely controlled by transcriptional regulators but our understanding of this is still limited. In nitrogen-starved Medicago truncatula, the expression of D27 as well as of a MAX1 ortholog in roots was shown to be NODULATION SIGNALING PATHWAY 1 (NSP1) and NSP2 dependent [37]. Phosphate starvation, which increases D27 expression in wild type plants, did not increase D27 expression in the nsp1 mutant, and only a mild increase was observed in the nsp2 mutant. These observations suggest that NSP1 and NSP2 are transcription factors directly involved in the expression of the strigolactone biosynthetic genes. Interestingly, the M. truncatula D27 promoter region contains several nodulation-responsive elements. These conserved cis-regulatory binding elements had previously been identified in the promoter region of the early nodulin (ENOD) nodulation-responsive ENOD11 and

were shown to be involved in the binding of the NSP1 transcription factor [38]. Multiple factors (e.g., auxin and phosphate levels) affect the strigolactone levels in plants. However, it still needs to be determined whether auxin related transcription factors such as AUXIN-RESPONSIVE PROTEIN/INDOLEACETIC ACID-INDUCED PROTEIN, AUX/IAA, and/or AUXIN RESONSE FACTORs, ARFs [39] or phosphate related transcription factors (e.g., PHOS-PHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1, PHR1 [40,41]) directly affect transcription of strigolactone biosynthesis genes.

# Strigolactone perception

In both monocots and dicots, two proteins – the Leu-rich repeat F-box protein MAX2/RMS4/D3 and the $\alpha / \beta$ -fold hydrolase D14 (also known as D88 or HTD2 in rice and DAD2 in petunia, Petunia hybrida) (Figure 2) – have been shown to be required for strigolactone responses [42–49]. MAX2 has been shown to participate in a SKP1-CUL1-F-box-protein (SCF)-type ubiquitin ligase complex that is similar to the SCF complex in which the auxin receptor TRANSPORT INHIBITOR RESPONSE 1 (TIR1) is involved, and is believed to catalyze the ubiquitination of specific proteins destined for proteasomal degradation [43,50].

Taking into account the structural similarity between MAX2 and TIR1 and the involvement of $\alpha / \beta$ -fold hydrolases such as the gibberellin receptor GIBBERELLIN INSENSITIVE DWARF 1 (GID1) in hormone perception [51], both MAX2 and D14 are considered candidates for the strigolactone receptor. Analogous to GID1, it could be that D14 is involved in strigolactone hydrolysis a process required for targeting unknown protein substrate(s) to the SCFMAX2 complex for protein degradation. Indeed, recently the binding of GR24 and its hydrolysis by DAD2, the petunia D14 ortholog, was reported [52]. The hydrolysis of GR24 resulted in two compounds, one being the formyl tricyclic lactone of GR24 (ABC rings), while the other compound, being larger than the first, could not be identified [52]. Hydrolysis of the bridge between the C- and Dring of the strigolactone molecule matches previous observations that this structure is essential for biological activity. However, the biological relevance of the second compound is not clear. The authors also showed that the hydrolytic activity of DAD2 is a prerequisite for the protein–protein interaction of the DAD2–GR24 complex with PhMAX2. The crystal structure of DAD2 confirmed the presence of a catalytic cavity in which strigolactones would fit. The cavity contained three highly conserved amino acid residues (a catalytic triad) and analysis of mutants of these amino acids showed that these are the active site residues required for hydrolytic activity of DAD2.

It has been reported that karrikins, smoke-derived compounds that are structurally similar to strigolactones, have a similar effect on the germination of Arabidopsis as the synthetic strigolactone GR24 and that this effect requires MAX2 [53]. Recently, it has been shown that a protein that is closely related to D14, called D14-like, is required for karrikin signaling, but D14 and D14-like differ in their specificity for karrikin and GR24 [49]. Although karrikins may not be the natural ligand for Arabidopsis D14s, the different D14 proteins could be implicated in the

response to other karrikin-like molecules or even to different strigolactones. For example, the different D14 proteins, in combination with a specific strigolactone, could be responsible for a different plant growth response. It will be of great interest to compare the different D14 proteins with the crystal structure of petunia DAD2 and to study whether there are differences in the catalytic cavity that correlate with differences in substrate specificity.

# Localization of strigolactone biosynthesis gene expression and hormonal action in the plant root

Analytical studies have shown that roots contain relatively high levels of strigolactones, whereas in other tissue types, such as hypocotyl, stem, and leaves, strigolactone levels are usually low or even undetectable [54]. Of course this does not mean that localized high concentrations of strigolactones in certain cell types in the shoot do not exist. There are strong indications that strigolactones that are produced in the shoot are involved in the suppression of bud outgrowth. As later paragraphs will deal with these aspects, this paragraph specifically focusses on strigolactone biosynthesis and hormonal action in the roots.

The localization of strigolactone biosynthetic gene expression gives an impression where in the root strigoactones are produced. Rice CCD7 (HTD1) and CCD8 (D10) (Figure 2), for example, are expressed throughout the vascular parenchyma cells of the root [55,56]. In Arabidopsis, MAX1 is also predominantly expressed in the vascular tissue of the root [22]. CCD8 (MAX4) is highly expressed in the columella root cap of primary and lateral roots [57,58], and treatment with the synthetic auxin 1-naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA) resulted in upregulation of CCD8 expression in the (pro)vasculature tissue of the primary root and cortical tissue of the root apex elongation zone [57,58] (Figure 3). Interestingly, the Arabidopsis e-FP browser (http://bar.utoronto.ca/efp/cgi-bin/efpWeb.cgi) also shows a relatively high level of MAX2 and D14 expression specifically in the elongation zone, suggesting colocalization of strigolactone biosynthesis and downstream signaling in that particular region [59].

Root growth is the combined result of cell division and subsequent cell elongation. Cell fates are determined by local auxin levels, which are established through a complex circuit of polar auxin transport [60] and auxin synthesis [61]. The elongation zone plays an important regulatory role in the dynamics of this circuit. Through this zone, auxin is transported in a lateral direction from the epidermis back into the main polar auxin transport stream (PAT) (Figure 3) [60,62]. Lateral root initiation originates in this zone [63,64]. Given that strigolactones have been shown to regulate auxin transport in the main stem [65], it is tempting to speculate that strigolactones mediate the regulation of (primary) root growth and lateral root initiation by regulating lateral auxin reflux in the elongation zone. Indeed, in a recent study, the crown roots of strigolactone-deficient rice mutants were found to be shorter than those of the corresponding wild type [66] and strigolactones in tomato were shown to compensate for the inhibitory effect of exogenously applied auxin on root elongation [67].

A mechanistic explanation for this effect is that the upregulation of strigolactone biosynthesis under low phosphate, prevents excessive auxin production in the shoot and, hence, controls the amount of auxin being transported to the roots [68], whereas auxin sensitivity in roots is increased [78]. This demonstrates that auxin is not only feeding back on strigolactones [79] but also vice versa – as is obvious from the reduced auxin levels in leaf tissue when plants are grown in the presence of GR24 [68] – resulting in a delicate balance between both hormones.

# Strigolactone exudation into the rhizosphere

After biosynthesis, part of the strigolactone pool in the root leaves the root through exudation and enters the rhizosphere, where strigolactones act as signaling compounds. Until recently, it was not known whether strigolactone exudation was an active process. This is also a relevant question for the development of parasitic plant control strategies. Possibly, a reduction in strigolactone exudation (through selection or molecular breeding) leaves endogenous strigolactone levels unaltered and thus does not affect plant architecture. A mild decrease in strigolactone levels in the root exudates of CCD8 RNA interference knockdown lines in tomato does not compromise AM fungal symbiosis to a large extent; however, it does result in a major increase in resistance against Phelipanche ramosa infection [80].

Recent work [81] has shed light on the mechanism involved in strigolactone transport and exudation. A screen for the ATP-binding cassette subfamily G/pleiotropic drug resistance (PDR) families of transporters functioning as efflux carriers of AM-promoting factors, such as strigolactones in petunia, resulted in the isolation of PhPDR1. Whereas strigolactone levels in root extracts of Phpdr1 mutants are similar to wild type, they were found to be severely reduced in the root exudate [81]. This resulted in reduced symbiotic interaction with the AMF species Glomus intraradices. The expression of PhPDR1 in roots was induced by phosphate starvation, AM fungal colonization, the synthetic strigolactone GR24, and NAA. In the root, PhPDR1 is predominantly localized in the plasma membrane of individual sub-epidermal cells of lateral roots. # Long-distance transport of strigolactones

The long-distance transport of a compound that inhibits branching has been reported several times (see, e.g., [82–85]. These grafting studies, in which hypocotyl grafts were made between strigolactone mutant root stocks and wild type shoots and vice versa, have shown that a compound that inhibits branching can be transported from the root to the shoot, which suggests that strigolactones could be transported through the xylem. However, grafting experiments have also shown that transport from the root

is not a prerequisite for branching inhibition demonstrating that local production in the shoot can be sufficient to inhibit branching (Figure 4). Nevertheless, transport of strigolactones is a useful mechanism as it allows fine-tuning and specifically regulating the root to shoot (branching) ratio in relation to rhizosphere conditions. Or more generally, a more global strigolactone distribution allows the coordination of different strigolactone-mediated processes throughout the entire plant and may also be a prerequisite for the negative feedback that strigolactones have on auxin production in the shoot [68]. Recently, the strigolactones orobanchol and orobanchyl acetate were detected in the xylem sap of Arabidopsis and tomato [23] (Figure 4). Under phosphate starvation the level of orobanchol increased, which is consistent with the finding that strigolactones are involved in the reduction in branching and tillering under phosphatelimiting conditions [23,25]. Nevertheless, the relative contribution of xylem sap-derived strigolactones to this process is unknown, particularly given that under low-phosphate conditions the strigolactone biosynthesis genes in the shoots

of rice are also upregulated just as in the roots [25] (Figure 4). Another issue that still needs to be explained is why the strigolactone profile found in tomato root exudates (low orobanchol and high solanacol and didehydroorobanchol) is different from that found in xylem sap (only orobanchol and orobanchyl acetate) [23]. How could this specificity be explained? Could it be that different strigolactones are produced in or transported to different tissues in which they have different biological functionality? Are there different transporters for different strigolactones? Does the efficiency with which strigolactone transporters, such as PhPDR1, transport different strigolactones vary? Or are maybe even multiple strigolactone transporters involved, each having its own specific localization and strigolactone transport efficiency?

# Strigolactones in the stem vasculature

As already mentioned, strigolactone biosynthesis is not restricted to the roots. In rice, CCD8 (D10), CCD7 (HTD1) and D27 are also expressed in the stem vascular bundle [26,55,56]. In Arabidopsis, MAX1 is expressed in the cambial region of the vascular bundle and in xylemassociated parenchyma cells in the stem [22]. Interestingly, strigolactones were recently shown to induce meristematic activity in the vascular cambium, which leads to increased secondary growth [86]. This adds yet another role to the rapidly expanding list of strigolactone functions and fits well with the strigolactone-mediated architectural responses to environmental conditions. A smaller and bushier plant needs less support from its main stem than one that is showing rapid vertical growth. A recent study has elegantly shown that local strigolactone signaling in the vascular cambium is sufficient to stimulate secondary growth [86]. This is another example of a strigolactonemediated process for which strigolactone biosynthesis seems to be colocalized with strigolactone signaling, just as in the regulation of root growth described above. Surprisingly, however, the authors showed that the strigolactones have a direct effect on cambium growth, and not through auxin. Currently, no data exist on the underlying physiological mechanism of how strigolactones stimulate secondary growth. The effect that strigolactones have on tip growth and mitochondrial activity in AM fungi, however, is also not likely to be mediated by auxin. Interestingly, tip growth of xylem fibers is known to occur in the cambial and radial-expansion zones [87]. Moreover, strigolactones are also known to be involved in other tip growth related processes such as in root hairs [67,69] and rhizoid elongation [88]. Strigolactone induced stimulation of root-hair growth also involves an auxin independent component [69]. Therefore, it is tempting to speculate about a mechanistic link in strigolactone action between these different processes.

# Strigolactone fluxes and the control of bud outgrowth

The mechanism of the regulation of bud outgrowth by strigolactones is not completely resolved. Recently, a detailed review [89] dealt with this topic and, therefore, we will only briefly summarize if and how information on local expression patterns of strigolactone biosynthetic and downstream signaling genes can support the postulated

mechanistic models that explain the strigolactone-mediated regulation of bud outgrowth.

It has been proposed that one of the prerequisites for an axillary bud to grow out is that it can release its auxin into the main PAT stream of the stem [89,90]. Given that the dormant buds of Arabidopsis do not have a vascular connection with the stem, it is hypothesized that a process called canalization initiates the formation of vascular tissue when an overcapacity in PAT occurs near the bud. This overcapacity allows lateral influx of auxin into the PAT stream, thus initiating auxin efflux from the bud [91]. Auxin transport overcapacity of the PAT stream will hence stimulate auxin flux between the bud and the stem vasculature, resulting in the formation of auxin transporting cell files and newly formed vascular tissue [92]. With respect to the sink strength of the PAT stream in the stem, it has been shown that strigolactones reduce the accumulation of the auxin efflux protein PIN1 on the basal side of stem xylem parenchyma cells, leading to a reduction of auxin transport through stem segments and, hence, to a lower PAT capacity [65]. Given that PIN1 [93] and MAX1 [22] presumably colocalize in stem xylem parenchyma cells, it seems plausible that locally produced strigolactones are involved in regulating the local capacity of the PAT stream and, therefore, can influence the initiation of auxin transport from axillary buds so that they can grow out. This mechanism would not require PDR1 activity and may explain why Phpdr1 mutant plants only show a mild branching phenotype even though there is PDR1 expression in the region subtending the axillary buds [81].

Although the above described theory proposes that regulation of bud outgrowth resides in strigolactone controlled, carrier limited, auxin transport in the PAT stream, it was also shown that both wild type and strigolactone biosynthesis mutants in Arabidopsis and pea were equally capable of transporting additional amounts of exogenously applied auxin [94]. These findings were recently confirmed in a computer modeling study and this would suggest that an increased auxin level in the bud itself is the main driving force in this source–sink relationship [95]. Perhaps strigolactones are primarily involved in the regulation of secondary growth of the bud connective tissue as described above [86] and/or feedback regulation on auxin homeostasis as described in the paragraph on root growth [68].

In Arabidopsis, expression of the strigolactone biosynthetic genes CCD8 and MAX1, and the downstream signaling gene MAX2, have also been reported to be relatively high in nodal tissue close to the buds. Perhaps, strigolactone production at that specific location interferes with the formation of connective vascular tissue between the bud and PAT stream as described above. Interestingly, in petunia, PhPDR1 is also reported to be expressed in the bud region although it is not completely clear whether this expression occurs inside or outside the vascular tissue (Figure 4). Maybe local secretion of strigolactones into the apoplast facilitates its local distribution and/or may even be involved in strigolactone transport into the buds. Interestingly, new data from pea indeed provide evidence that strigolactones are likely to be imported into dormant buds (Figure 4) and that this import contributes to the regulation of apical dominance [96,97]. This mechanism,

known as the second messenger theory, has been the subject of speculation for a long time: apically derived auxin in the stem, as a first messenger, was thought to regulate the production of a second messenger that moves into the bud to regulate its activity [98]. Both cytokinin and strigolactones were candidates for this role. Expression levels of strigolactone and cytokinin biosynthesis genes show that both hormones are likely to be antagonistically regulated by auxin [44,55,79,94,99,100]. A study in pea has now shown that strigolactones and cytokinins applied through the xylem or locally, interact antagonistically with each other on bud outgrowth. The presence of strigolactones, either applied or present in wild type plants, resulted in increased resistance to cytokinins. This antagonistic effect was found to converge at the joint regulation of the expression of the TCP transcription factor BRANCHED1 (BRC1, in Arabidopsis and pea and TEO-SINTE BRANCHED1, TB1, in monocots). Indeed, in rice the homolog of TB1, FINE CULM 1 (FC1) has also been shown to be under the control of strigolactones [101]. BRC1/TB1 act downstream of strigolactones in the regulation of shoot branching and are highly expressed in and below dormant axillary buds. The mutants brc1 and tb1 in dicots and monocots, respectively, show increased bud outgrowth or tillering phenotypes [94,101–103]. In pea, strigolactones decreased the cytokinin-mediated downregulation of BRC1/TB1 expression resulting in decreased sensitivity to cytokinins [97]. Applying strigolactones and cytokinins to Arabidopsis and pea has a different effect on their bud outgrowth: this could be because pea has a more advanced vascular connection between buds and the main stem than Arabidopsis [96,97].

# Strigolactones and seed germination – the link with light signaling

An intriguing evolutionary-related question is how the seeds of root parasitic plants have evolved the capacity to germinate in response to exogenous strigolactones even though their germination is not induced by light or even inhibited by light [104,105]. The evolution of strigolactone responsiveness would be easiest to understand if strigolactones also have a function in germination of non-parasitic plants. Indeed, several groups have shown that strigolactones play some role in the germination of Arabidopsis [53,106]. Strigolactone mutants show reduced germination under adverse conditions, which can be rescued by the application of the synthetic strigolactone GR24, for which the presence of the strigolactone downstream signaling F-box protein MAX2 is a prerequisite [106]. This allows Arabidopsis to be used as a model plant to study molecular pathways involved in strigolactone-mediated germination. Evidence for the link between light and strigolactones comes from the fact that MAX2 was originally identified in a screen for photomorphogenetic defects in seedling growth [107]. Initial reports suggested that light-signaling phenotypes are restricted to the max2 mutant because the biosynthetic mutants in the strigolactone pathway, max1, max3, and max4, did not show defects in seed germination and seedling de-etiolation [108]. However, recent results show that the Arabidopsis strigolactone biosynthetic mutants do have a reduced germination

phenotype under unfavorable high-germination temperature [106]. The effects of strigolactones on Arabidopsis seed germination are mediated by reducing the abscisic acid (ABA) to gibberellic acid (GA) ratio and this also seems to be the effect of strigolactones in Striga hermonthica seeds, suggesting a common hormonal regulation once germination has been triggered [106]. Whether or how the effect of strigolactones in non-parasitic plants relates to altered light signaling involved in triggering germination and how light signaling is surpassed in parasitic seed germination still needs further investigation. Another study showed that (in the dark) strigolactones interact with light signaling pathways through the control of Constitutive Photomorphogenic 1 (COP1) levels in the nucleus, which finally affects the levels of transcription factor HY5 [109]. This would imply that strigolactones mimic the light signal. Also suggestive for a link between light and strigolactones is the report on a mutation in a component of the PSII protein complex PsbP Domain Protein 5, PPD5 [110]. This mutation had little effect on photosynthetic electron transfer, but caused striking plant developmental and morphological defects including the formation of additional rosettes originating from axillary buds and increased root branching. The root-branching phenotype could be complemented with the synthetic strigolactone GR24 suggesting that the mutation in PPD5 somehow affects strigolactone biosynthesis

It remains unresolved how parasitic plant seeds evolved the capacity to respond to exogenous, host-derived, strigolactones that only occur in the rhizosphere in minute concentrations. Considering the possible role of D14 in strigolactone binding, as suggested above, a change in the expression of D14 – or D14-LIKE as that was shown to be primarily involved in germination [49] – D14(-LIKE) sensitivity, or localization in parasitic plant seeds may be responsible for this altered strigolactone detection. However, a possible role of MAX2 should also be considered. A change in the ligand binding of either D14 or MAX2 through amino acid changes in the binding site could be responsible for the host specificity of parasitic plants by responding only to specific strigolactones. Considering that strigolactones are crucial for the regulation of several important physiological processes in plants, a less likely explanation for the dependence of root parasitic plant seed germination on exogenous strigolactones is the loss of strigolactone biosynthetic capacity. Expressed sequence tags (ESTs) generated from parasitic plants with homology to strigolactone biosynthetic as well as downstream signaling genes are present in publicly available databases (Parasitic Plant Genome Project, http:// ppgp.huck.psu.edu/; Striga hermonthica EST Database http://striga.psc.riken.jp/) but the functionality of these genes remains to be determined.

# Future challenges: structural diversity and biological specificity in strigolactones

In the past few years, our knowledge about strigolactone biology has increased at a rapid pace. This has resulted in answers to many questions but has also generated new questions and challenges. One important challenge is to resolve the last step(s) in the biosynthesis of 5-deoxystrigol. Attempts to detect the intermediate carlactone in

plants have been unsuccessful: this is likely to be because of its rapid conversion by the next enzyme(s) in the pathway and a lack of a mutant affected in this step [33]. The availability of sufficient amounts of carlactone should greatly assist in elucidating further steps in strigolactone biosynthesis, including the activities of MAX1, and hence chemical synthesis of this compound would be highly desirable. However, as mentioned, 15 different strigolactones have been structurally characterized in plants, while even more have been tentatively identified [2] (Figure 5). These strigolactones are postulated to be derived from 5- deoxystrigol through the action of common biosynthetic enzymes such as cytochrome P450s, acetyl transferases, and $O$ -methyl transferases (Figure 5) (with the exception perhaps of solanacol, which seems to requires some lesscommon biosynthetic steps) [2,21] and an important challenge will be to identify the enzymes that catalyze these biosynthetic conversions. Mutants are likely to be an important tool in achieving this. For example, as discussed, in M. truncatula and rice, the transcription factors NSP1 and NSP2 were shown to affect strigolactone biosynthesis [37]. Although nsp1 mutant plants did not produce strigolactones, the strigolactone production of M. truncatula nsp2 changed from didehydro-orobanchol in wild type to orobanchol in the mutant. These results provide new tools to

identify the underlying enzymes responsible for the conversion of orobanchol to didehydro-orobanchol.

The availability of such mutants and the possibility to make transgenic lines with altered strigolactone composition should also allow further studies on the biological specificity of the structural strigolactone variants in all the different processes they control. Striking examples of the differences in biological specificity of individual strigolactones are already available from a number of studies. For example, orobanchol is a relatively strong inducer of hyphal branching in the AM fungus Gigaspora margarita [111], a poor inducer of germination in the parasitic plant Striga hermonthica [112], but induces high levels of germination in Orobanche and Phelipanche spp. [113]. Recently, it was shown that different strigolactones also exhibit differential efficiency in the inhibition of axillary bud outgrowth in pea [114]. As mentioned above, the different biological effects of different strigolactones may relate to the different types of D14(like) proteins. The diversification in structure and biological activity of the strigolactones is likely to be the result of the positive and negative selective pressure on plants: for example, positive selection to exude strigolactones that are more effective in activating AM fungi in combination with negative selective pressure (or selection by breeders) to reduce germination of parasitic

plants. Biological specificity can be obtained through structural diversification, for which new enzymes need to evolve; however, changes in the transport and perception of the signaling molecules need to co-evolve with the structural diversification and/or can play an independent role in the creation of biological specificity. Studies on the genetic and possibly functional diversity in domains of D14 and PDR1 that are involved in strigolactone binding and transport, respectively, will be of great interest.

# Concluding remarks

In conclusion, since the discovery that strigolactones not only play signaling roles in the rhizosphere, but are also important growth regulators within the plant, our knowledge about their biosynthesis, downstream signaling, and resulting action is rapidly accumulating. However, a lot still needs to be discovered. We still only partially understand how strigolactones are biosynthesized, distributed throughout the plant and secreted into the soil; how strigolactones can affect different physiological processes within plants and other organisms like AM fungi; and we are only just beginning to understand the relevance of strigolactone diversity. Moreover, at the moment the only information with regard to the latter is based on strigtolactones derived from root extracts and exudates, mostly isolated from plants challenged with phosphate starvation to boost strigolactone levels. Information concerning the presence and identity of strigolactones (or further downstream products derived thereof) in other parts of the plant and growing under normal (non-phosphate starved) conditions is completely lacking. Future research, including the development of more sensitive analytical or reporter techniques, should shed more light on all these knowledge gaps.
