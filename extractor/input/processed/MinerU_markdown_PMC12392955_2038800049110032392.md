# Summary

Starch synthesis is crucial for crop yield and quality. This study reveals an O11-ZmSSRP1 module of kernel starch biosynthesis in maize (Zea mays). We identify STARCH SYNTHESIS REGULATING PROTEIN 1 (ZmSSRP1) positively regulates amylose and amylopectin-dependent starch synthesis in maize endosperm. ZmSSRP1 encodes a highly conserved plastid-localized protein that is highly expressed in developing endosperm. Loss-of-function zmssrp1 mutants created by CRISPR/Cas9 exhibit much smaller starch granules and significantly reduced amylose and amylopectin synthesis, while overexpression of ZmSSRP1 causes larger starch granules and an increased amylose and amylopectin accumulation. RNA-seq analysis revealed that the expression levels of several genes involved in the synthesis of amylose and amylopectin were significantly down-regulated in the zmssrp1 mutant compared to the wild-type. Furthermore, we found that OPAQUE11 (O11), a core transcription factor essential for maize endosperm development and nutrient metabolism, transactivates the expression of ZmSSRP1 by binding to its promoter, and functions upstream of ZmSSRP1 in the regulatory pathway governing starch synthesis in the maize endosperm. The present work demonstrates that the O11-ZmSSRP1 module positively regulates starch synthesis in maize kernels, potentially paving the way for future genetic improvements of maize quality. 

# Introduction

Maize (Zea mays L.) is a global staple crop, providing a major resource of metabolizable energy for humans and livestock and industrial raw material. Starch, the major storage carbohydrate in the maize kernel, comprising about $7 5 \%$ of the dry endosperm matter, directly influences maize quality and yield (Liu et al., 2016). In the normal maize endosperm, starch is structurally composed of ${ \sim } 2 5 \%$ linear amylose and ${ \sim } 7 5 \%$ branched amylopectin (Nelson and Pan, 1995). The ratio of amylose to amylopectin directly influences the appearance, usage and quality of food products and processing (Li et al., 2017). 

Starch synthesis is a complex biochemical regulation process that is finely coordinated by a series of key enzymes working in concert to form a stable molecular structure (Huang et al., 2021). With decades of in-depth research, enormous achievements have been made in understanding the function of key starch synthesis-related genes (SSRGs) and regulatory networks (Huang et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2023). Key enzymes include sucrose synthase (SUS), adenosine diphosphate glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase, the small unit encoded by brittle2 (bt2), and the large by shrunken2 (sh2)), starch synthase (SS, divided into granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS), encoded by waxy (wx) and soluble starch synthase (SSS)), starch-branching enzyme (SBE, encoded by amylose extender1 (ae1)) and starch-debranching enzyme (DBE, encoded by sugary1 (su1)) (Li et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2016). Among them, GBSS is a major 

enzyme primarily responsible for elongating the linear chain of glucose polymers in amylose starches, while SSS, SBE and DBE form a well-coordinated machinery complex for amylopectin biosynthesis (Ball and Morell, 2003; Huang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). Loss of function of GBSS at the waxy locus eliminates amylose synthesis, resulting in ${ \sim } 1 0 0 \%$ amylopectin, which gives the endosperm a dull and smooth appearance like candle wax (Nelson and Pan, 1995). Loss of isoamylase-type DBE (ISA) typically results in reduced starch content, abnormal amylopectin structure, altered granule morphology and accumulation of abnormally highly branched polysaccharides in maize endosperm (Kubo et al., 2010). 

In addition to the starch metabolic enzymes, key transcription factors that regulate maize endosperm starch biosynthesis have been discovered, such as Opaque11 (O11) (Li and Song, 2020). O11, an endosperm-specific basic/helix–loop–helix (bHLH) transcription factor, coordinates cellular development and nutrient metabolism by regulating the expression of key transcription factors in endosperm development, nutrient metabolism and multiple carbohydrate metabolic enzymes (Feng et al., 2018; Grimault et al., 2015). The absence of O11 results in reduced endosperm size, morphological defects in embryos, and especially a reduced starch content with abnormal starch granules (Feng et al., 2018). However, the exact functional mechanism by which O11 regulates starch synthesis in maize remains largely unknown. 

Many non-enzymatic proteins containing starch-binding domains, such as carbohydrate-binding module 48 (CBM48), 

have been revealed to participate in the process of starch metabolism (Seung et al., 2017, 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). CBM48s are conserved glucan binding domains found in proteins involved in starch metabolism in plants, such as the phosphoglucan phosphatases, branching enzymes and debranching enzymes (Janecek et al., 2011; Meekins et al., 2014; Moller et al., 2016). Arabidopsis CBM48 domain-containing protein PROTEIN TARGETING TO STARCH 1 (AtPTST1) binds to starch granules and interacts directly with GBSS and presumably aids GBSS localization in Arabidopsis leaves (Seung et al., 2015). Loss of AtPTST1 leads to a great reduction in the amount of starch-bound GBSS, as well as amylose content (Seung et al., 2015). The rice PTST1 homologue, OsGBP (GBSS-BINDING PROTEIN) interacts with OsGBSSI and OsGBSSII to mediate their binding to the developing starch granules and influence starch biosynthesis (Wang et al., 2019). Unexpectedly, the loss of OsGBP has negligible effects on amylose content in the endosperm (Wang et al., 2019). AtPTST2 interacts specifically with and transfers suitable oligosaccharides to SS4 for starch granule initiation in leaves (Seung et al., 2017). AtPTST2 also interacts with MAR BINDING FILAMENT-LIKE PROTEIN1 (MFP1) and MYOSIN-RESEMBLING CHLOROPLAST PROTEIN (MRC), two proteins that influence the number of starch granules per chloroplast in leaves (Seung et al., 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). Loss of AtPTST2 leads to a remarkable reduction in starch granule numbers per chloroplast and accumulation of ADP-glucose in leaves (Seung et al., 2017). While Arabidopsis AtPTST2 affects starch granule number and ADP-glucose levels, its rice homologue, FLO6, interacts with OsISA1, plays a distinct role as a starch-binding protein in starch biosynthesis and compound granule formation (Peng et al., 2014). Recently, FLO6 was reported to cooperate with OsLESV (LIKE EARLY STARVATION1) to form a non-enzymatic molecular module responsible for OsISA1 localization on starch granules to modulate starch biosynthesis and endosperm development in rice (Yan et al., 2024). Moreover, OsLESV interacts with starch biosynthesis enzymes, such as GBSSI and GBSSII, to maintain their protein stability and enzyme activity and facilitates the binding of GBSSI and GBSSII to starch granules from plastid stroma (Dong et al., 2024). AtPTST3 interacts with AtPTST2, and the elimination of AtPTST3 leads to a reduction in the number of starch granules in leaves (Seung et al., 2017). The impact of PTST homologues on starch biosynthesis in Manihot esculenta (MePTST) and Hordeum vulgare (HvPTST) has been established (Bull et al., 2018; Saito et al., 2017; Zhong et al., 2019). Recently, we reported that the overexpression of maize ZmSSRP1 and ZmCBM48-1, two non-enzymatic proteins containing the CBM48 domain, increases starch synthesis in rice (Chen et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022). However, the involvement of maize CBM48-containing domain proteins in endosperm starch synthesis remains to be elucidated. 

In this study, we report the O11-ZmSSRP1 module regulating starch synthesis in maize endosperm. ZmSSRP1 positively regulates starch synthesis. The loss of function of ZmSSRP1 results in smaller starch granules and reduced starch biosynthesis. Conversely, ZmSSRP1 overexpression increases starch granules’ size and starch biosynthesis through altered amylose and amylopectin content. Moreover, O11 acts as a transcriptional activator, binding to the ZmSSRP1 promoter and driving its expression. Collectively, our data suggest that the O11-ZmSSRP1 module mediates starch synthesis in maize endosperm. 

# Results

# ZmSSRP1 is an orthologue of OsFLO6 protein in maize, and ZmSSRP1 is predominantly expressed during maize endosperm development

ZmSSRP1 (Zm00001d033529) encoded a 56.3-kDa protein of 541 amino acids. Using Simple Modular Architecture Research Tool (SMART) signature finders, we found that ZmSSRP1 contains four distinct low-complexity regions and a characteristic C-terminal CBM48 domain (Figure 1a). Protein Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLASTP) searches, using the amino acid sequence as a query, indicated that it was a putative orthologue of FLOURY ENDOSPERM 6 (FLO6) in maize, a CBM48 domain-containing protein. We constructed a phylogenetic tree using the protein sequence from various plant species, including Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), rice (Oryza sativa), sorghum (Sorghum bicolor), soybean (Glycine max), cassava (Manihot esculenta), barley (Hordeum vulgare), wheat (Triticum aestivum), corn (Setaria italica), dogtail (Setaria viridis) and maize (Zea mays) (Figure 1b). Three paralogs of ZmSSRP1 were found in maize, and their sequences formed two distinct clades on the tree (Figure 1b). ZmSSRP1 and ZmCBM48-1 are two closely related proteins, and the full amino acid sequences of ZmSSRP1 and ZmCBM48-1 shared $5 5 . 6 \%$ identity and $5 8 . 4 \%$ similarity under BLASTp alignment (Figure S1). Also, we found the full amino acid sequences of ZmSSRP1 and OsFLO6 shared $5 6 . 7 \%$ identity and $6 8 . 8 \%$ similarity (Figure S2), and their typical C-terminal CBM48 domain shared $8 2 . 5 \%$ identity and $8 7 . 5 \%$ similarity (Figure 1c). These results indicated that ZmSSRP1 is an orthologue of OsFLO6 protein in maize. 

We then analysed its subcellular localization, ZmSSRP1-GFP fusion proteins driven by the CaMV 35S promoter were expressed by a transient transformation of maize leaf cells (Figure S3a). The results showed that the GFP fluorescence signals were detected in small patches within chloroplasts indicated by the autofluorescence signals (Figure S3a). This suggests that ZmSSRP1 is likely localized to the chloroplasts. Real-time quantitative PCR (RTqPCR) analysis revealed that ZmSSRP1 is constitutively expressed in all examined tissues, with higher expression levels detected in leaves, pollen and developing kernels (3, 6 and 9 days after pollination, DAP) (Figure S3b). Further examination of the expression profile of ZmSSRP1 during kernel development showed that the ZmSSRP1 transcript was highly expressed in endosperm, with an increasing trend and peaking at 15 DAP, but very low expression was detected in the embryo during different developmental stages of maize kernels (Figure 1d).

# Discussion

Starch synthesis in maize endosperm is a well-orchestrated process controlled by a complex interplay of various enzymes such as GBSSs, SSs, SBEs and DBEs, which are coordinately involved in regulating starch synthesis (Han et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2023). In addition to these enzymes, many non-enzymatic proteins, especially the CBM48 domaincontaining proteins, have been reported to regulate the biosynthesis of leaf transitory starch or endosperm storage starch (Seung et al., 2017, 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). Our findings reveal a novel regulatory pathway, mediated by the 

O11-ZmSSRP1 module, governing starch synthesis within maize endosperm. Transcription factor O11 acts as a positive regulator of amylose and amylopectin synthesis by directly binding to the ZmSSRP1 promoter and activating its expression (Figure S15). 

O11 has been reported to coordinate endosperm development and nutrient metabolism (Feng et al., 2018). The o11 mutants exhibit a reduced endosperm and decreased starch and protein accumulation (Feng et al., 2018). The exact molecular mechanism of O11 in starch biosynthesis is still to be elucidated. Here, we demonstrated that O11 promotes starch biosynthesis by affecting the accumulation of amylose and amylopectin (Figures S9 and S10). This was supported by the observation that loss-of-function o11 mutants displayed significantly smaller starch granules and much less starch, amylose and amylopectin contents, whereas overexpressing O11 exhibited opposite phenotypes in comparison with the o11 mutants (Figure S9). Consistently, the expression of genes related to amylose and amylopectin synthesis is also affected (Figure S10). O11 directly regulates key TFs in endosperm development (NKD2 and ZmDOF3), nutrient metabolism (O2 and PBF), multiple carbohydrate metabolic enzymes and cytoplasmic pyruvate orthophosphate dikinase 1 and 2 (cyPPDKs), which are key molecular switches in the balance between starch and protein accumulation (Feng et al., 2018). In this study, ZmSSRP1 was identified as a novel regulatory component in the O11-mediated starch biosynthesis pathway within maize endosperm (Figure 5). 

ZmSSRP1 encodes a CBM48 domain-containing protein (Figure 1 and Figure S1) (Chen et al., 2022). Across plant species and even across tissues, the functions of CBM48 domaincontaining proteins have been indicated to have relatively conservation yet not identical. There are three SSRP1 homologues in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana, AtPTST1-AtPTST3) and 

maize (Zea mays, ZmCBM48-1, ZmSSRP1 and ZmGBP ), respectively (Figure 1b) (Seung et al., 2015). AtPTST1 and its homologues have been reported to be involved in amylose synthesis; the loss of PTST1 leads to defective amylose-dependent starch synthesis but does not affect amylose content in Arabidopsis leaves, rice and barley grains and cassava root tubers (Bull et al., 2018; Seung et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2019). PTST2 also participates in the regulation of starch synthesis. AtPTST2 is crucial for starch granule initiation, influencing the number of starch granules within chloroplasts (Seung et al., 2017, 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). AtPTST2 deficiency leads to an increase in amylose-dependent starch contents and minor alterations in amylopectin chain length distribution (Seung et al., 2017, 2018). In rice endosperm, the loss of FLO6, a rice PTST2 homologue, exhibits reduced total starch and amylopectin accumulation but does not alter amylose content and chain length distribution (Peng et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2024). In potato tubers, there are two PTST2 paralogs (PTST2a and PTST2b) and PTST2b is identified as an atypical PTST2 isoform that is involved in starch granule morphogenesis (Hochmuth et al., 2024). In maize, ZmSSRP1 and ZmCBM48-1 are two closely related proteins (Figure 1 and Figure S1). In this study, we found that ZmSSRP1 regulates starch synthesis by affecting both amylose and amylopectin contents in maize endosperm. The loss of ZmSSRP1 leads to the reduction of both amylose and amylopectin contents, and starch granule size in the endosperm (Figure 4). Conversely, ZmSSRP1 overexpression resulted in increased amylose and amylopectin content, along with larger starch granules (Figure 4). ZmCBM48-1 may also regulate starch synthesis, as the overexpression of ZmCBM48-1 could upregulate many starch synthesis genes, increase starch content and change the starch composition and starch structure in rice (Peng et al., 2022). However, the molecular mechanism by which ZmCBM48-1 regulates starch synthesis in rice and whether ZmCBM48-1 maintains the same function in maize remains to be elucidated. ZmSSRP1 and ZmCBM48-1 exhibit a similar expression pattern, especially during maize endosperm development (Figure S12) (Peng et al., 2022), but the absence of ZmSSRP1 does not affect the expression level of ZmCBM48-1 (Figure S13). In future studies, it will be interesting to determine whether they play distinct roles or work together by analysing their protein interactions, screening their respective interacting partners, generating maize ZmCBM48-1 overexpression and knockout lines or their double mutants. We also noticed that starch amylose and amylopectin content phenotypes are more extreme in the $0 1 1 \times 2 m s s r p 1 - 1$ double mutant than in either single mutant (Figure 6b–d), indicating more than a simple downstream response of ZmSSRP1 from O11. A much more complex regulatory network may be involved in the O11-ZmSSRP1 module-mediated starch synthesis. Additionally, PTST3 may also play an important role in starch biogenesis. AtPTST3 interacts and is partially redundant with AtPTST2 in starch granule initiation (Seung et al., 2017). 

The precise mechanism of ZmSSRP1 in starch synthesis in maize remains to be elucidated. In Arabidopsis and rice, its homologues interact with different starch biosynthesis enzymes to affect starch contents. In Arabidopsis leaves, AtPTST1 interacts directly with GBSS and mediates its targeting to starch granules for regulating amylose content (Seung et al., 2015). In rice (Oryza sativa) grains, OsGBP, a rice PTST1 homologue, binds and mediates OsGBSSI and OsGBSSII to starch granules in rice endosperm (Wang et al., 2019). In Arabidopsis leaves, AtPTST2 

interacts with SS4, MFP1 and MRC, but does not interact with GBSS and ISA (Seung et al., 2017, 2018; Vandromme et al., 2019). In rice grains, FLO6 interacts with OsLESV and OsISA1 and facilitates ISA1 localization on starch granules (Peng et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2024). OsLESV interacts with GBSSI, GBSSII and PPDKB to maintain their protein stability and enzyme activity and facilitates the binding of GBSSI and GBSSII to starch granules (Dong et al., 2024). Actually, we also analysed the interaction of ZmSSRP1 with ZmGBSS1, ZmISA1 and ZmSS4 via luciferase complementation imaging (BiFC) assay and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (LCI) assay in tobacco leaves. However, no detectable positive results were obtained in these assays (Figure S14). We cannot rule out that ZmSSRP1 interacts with these starch biosynthesis enzymes within maize endosperm, as there are limitations of these interaction assays (such as protein stability or interaction might be compromised when termini are masked or the dynamic nature of protein complexes). ZmSSRP1 is homologous to OsFLO6 but there are functional differences. Firstly, the differences in their N-terminal regions are shared $4 7 . 9 \%$ identity and $6 0 . 9 \%$ similarity (Figure S2). OsFLO6 interacts with OsISA1 or OsLESV via its N-terminal region (Peng et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2024). Secondly, there are many differences between FLO6 and SSRP1 in regulating starch synthesis. OsFLO6 regulates starch granule formation and starch synthesis. In the flo6 mutant, the starch and amylose content are decreased, and the amylopectin chain length distributions and the morphology of starch granules are changed (Peng et al., 2014). ZmSSRP1 positively regulates starch, amylose and amylopectin content, as well as starch granules’ sizes (Figure 4). Thirdly, there may be differences in the species specificity of the interacting proteins. OsFLO6 could interact with the starch synthesis enzyme OsISA1 or the non-enzymatic protein OsLESV, which forms complexes with OsISA1, GBSSI, GBSSII and PPDKB to regulate their localization, protein stability and enzyme activity (Dong et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2024). ZmSSRP1 may also form complexes with starch synthesis enzymes or non-enzymatic proteins. Furthermore, the regulation of interactions between transcription factors is also critical in starch synthesis (Chen et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). In further research, we will prioritize detecting O11-ZmSSRP1 module interaction partners and upstream regulatory factors using multiple techniques (e.g. coimmunoprecipitation, yeast two-hybrid, proximity labelling and yeast one-hybrid), which could provide more information into the mechanism by which O11-ZmSSRP1 influences starch biosynthesis in maize. Additionally, exploring the multiple physiological roles of ZmSSRP1 beyond starch synthesis would be valuable. 

In conclusion, our findings reveal a novel O11-ZmSSRP1 regulatory module that O11 transactivates the expression of ZmSSRP1, positively regulates starch synthesis by mediating the accumulation of both amylopectin and amylose in maize endosperm (Figure S15). These findings could expand our understanding of starch synthesis, providing new insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying starch biosynthesis in maize. Furthermore, we also enriched the genetic resources of maize endosperm starch content regulation, and the overexpressing O11 and ZmSSRP1 may provide potential avenues for maize yield or quality genetic improvement. Future breeding programs may wish to unlock their desirable gene haplotypes or create novel beneficial alleles using gene editing technology (e.g. by editing the cis-elements in O11 and ZmSSRP1 promoter to control gene expression, or through upstream open reading frame editing-mediated translation regulation) to fine-tune 

starch content, and then precisely introduce these alleles into the parental lines of high-yielding maize hybrids to breed commercially viable varieties with increased maize kernel starch content. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Maize inbred line B73 was used for gene expression analysis of ZmSSRP1. The double mutants of the $0 1 1 \times 2 m s s r p 1 - 1$ were generated by crossing the o11 with the zmssrp1-1 mutant, and then self-fertilization for five generations to obtain homozygous double mutants. Plants were grown in a greenhouse at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a $1 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light/10 h dark photoperiod. Twelve representative tissues, including root, stem, leaf, tassel, filament, cluster, embryo (10, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27 and 30 DAP embryo) and endosperm (10, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27 and 30 DAP embryo), were collected from a life cycle of maize. All collected tissues were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for further analysis. All plants were cultivated in a field at the Anhui Agriculture University campus in Hefei, Anhui, China. 

# Plasmid construction and transformation

To investigate the $Z { \mathsf { m S S R P 1 } }$ function, various plasmids were constructed. For the CRISPR/Cas9 constructs, two target sites, $5 ^ { \prime } .$ - TGGTGGCAGGCATATTCCAA- $3 ^ { \prime }$ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CCACTAAAACATCT-GAAGCA- $3 ^ { \prime }$ , were designed for ZmSSRP1 using the online tool CRISPR-P (http://cbi.hzau.edu.cn/crispr/). The sgRNA sequences were synthesized, and the CRISPR/Cas9 constructs were generated using maize genetic transformation techniques with the inbred line KN5585 as a receptor following a previously published protocol (Xing et al., 2014). Transgenic positive plants were backcrossed with KN5585, and two knockout lines were generated in the self-pollinated offspring. Seeds from the homozygous T3 generation of transgenic plants were used in experiments. For overexpression of ZmSSRP1, the ORF was amplified from B73 and cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector. The recombinant plasmid vectors were introduced into Agrobacterium strain EHA105 and used to transform different maize cultivars. For the subcellular localization experiments, the CDSs of ZmSSRP1 were amplified from cDNA pools of 7 DAP (day after pollination) endosperm, and the fragments were cloned into pCAMBIA1305-GFP in frame with the GFP gene sequence. For protein purification, the full-length coding sequence of O11 was cloned into pCZN1 (NdeI and XbaI ) vector to generate the $3 \times 1 1 5  – 0 1 1$ construct. For the dualluciferase reporter assay, the CDS of O11 was cloned into pGreenII-62SK driven by the CaMV 35S promoter, and the promoter regions of the target genes were cloned into pGreenII-0800-LUC upstream of the luciferase gene. Finally, for the BiFC assays, the CDSs of ZmSSRP1, ZmGBSS1 and ZmISA1 were fused in the frame with nYFP and cYFP, respectively. All the plasmids for transient expression assays were transformed into maize protoplasts by the PEG-mediated method or into N. benthamiana leaves by the Agrobacterium-mediated method. All primers used are listed in Table S2. 

# Light microscopy assay

Immature maize kernels from both wild-type and mutant plants (harvested from the same ear) were sectioned for light microscopy analysis. Kernels were cut along the longitudinal axis for paraffin section preparation and along the horizontal axis for resin section preparation. The procedures were performed following a previously established protocol (Chen et al., 2017). 

# Agrobacterium-mediated infiltration of N. benthamiana leaves

A strain of Agrobacterium strain GV3101 containing different fusion constructs was grown in LB medium at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in a shaker for $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . The bacterial cells were harvested and resuspended in 10 mM MES, pH 5.6, $1 0 \mathrm { \ m M \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 } }$ plus $1 5 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone (Sigma-Aldrich) and then kept at room temperature for at least 3 h without shaking. After incubation, the Agrobacterium cultures containing different constructs were infiltrated into the leaves of 5- to 6-week-old N. benthamiana plants using a syringe. The infiltration targeted the abaxial (underside) of the leaves. 

# Measurement of starch and protein contents

Twenty mature kernels of the wild-type or mutants (harvested from the same ears) were collected and soaked in water, after which the endosperm was separated from the embryo and pericarp. The wild-type and o11 mutant endosperm tissues were pooled separately, ground in liquid $\mathsf { N } _ { 2 }$ and dried to a constant weight for subsequent analysis. Total starch was measured using an amyloglucosidase/a-amylase starch assay kit (Megazyme) according to the manufacturer’s protocol and adapted as follows. A 0.2-mL aliquot of aqueous ethanol $8 0 \%$ [v/v]) was added to a 100-mg sample to aid dispersion, and $3 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ thermostable aamylase was immediately added. The tube was incubated in a boiling water bath for 6 min and placed in a bath at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ; $0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m L }$ amyloglucosidase was then added. The tube was stirred with a vortex mixer and incubated at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 min. Duplicate aliquots $( 0 . 1 ~ \mathsf { m L } )$ of the diluted solution were transferred to a new tube; 3.0 mL of glucose oxidase/peroxidase (GOPOD) reagent was added, and the tubes were incubated at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 20 min. The absorbance of each sample and the D-Glc control at 510 nm was measured against the reagent blank. Total protein contents were measured according to a previously described protocol (Wu et al., 2019). The amylopectin and the amylopectin were measured using an amylopectin and amylopectin assay kit (A152-2-1, Jcbio, Nanjing, China) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The sugar was measured as previously described (Peng et al., 2022). All assays were performed for 3 biological replicates using kernels (about 20 kernels for each replicate) from three different ears for each genotype. 

# Phylogenetic analysis

To understand the evolutionary relationships of ZmSSRP1, related protein sequences were retrieved from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) non-redundant protein database using a protein BLAST search with ZmSSRP1 as the query sequence. Amino acid sequences were aligned with MUSCLE in the MEGA5.2 software package using the default settings for protein multiple alignment. Evolutionary distances were computed using Poisson correction analysis. The bootstrap method with 1000 replicates for phylogeny testing was used. 

# Subcellular localization of ZmSSRP1

The ZmSSRP1-GFP and 35S:GFP expression constructs were extracted and purified using a Plasmid Maxi Kit (Aidlab; PL1301) and a DNA Pure-Spin Kit (Aidlab; DR0201), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Maize protoplasts were isolated and transfected using previously established methods (Li et al., 2025). Briefly, mesophyll protoplasts were isolated from pooled etiolated leaves of 12-day-old maize plants and diluted to $6 \times 1 0 6$ protoplasts/mL. Protoplasts were then transformed with 

ZmSSRP1-GFP or 35S:GFP (as a control) constructs using the PEG method and resuspended to $2 \times 1 0 5$ protoplasts/mL. After 16–24 h dark incubation, protoplasts were visualized by a Zeiss LSM780 laser scanning confocal microscope (LSCM). GFP fluorescence was excited with a $4 8 8 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ laser and detected using the bandpass 505–530 nm emission filter. Image analysis was performed using the Zeiss 780 suit imaging software. 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNA was isolated from wild-type and plants of various genotypes using Trizol (Ambion) reagent. The isolated RNA was treated with DNase I (Invitrogen) to eliminate genomic DNA contamination. $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of the isolated RNA was used to detect its concentration and purity on a spectrophotometer (One Drop OD-1000). RNA was subsequently reverse-transcribed to first-strand cDNA with a Reverse Transcription System (Promega). $0 . 2 ~ \mu \ g$ cDNA was used as the template in $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ reaction volumes, and the amplifications were performed using a CFX Connect Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad). Maize Actin2 (ZmActin2) was used as the internal control for normalization of gene expression. 

# Protein purification

O11 was fused to a $3 \times 1 1 5$ tag. The encoding plasmid was transformed into E. coli Rosetta, and recombinant protein was extracted and purified by incubating with high-affinity Ni-Charged resin (Genscript) at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 \textrm { h }$ . After washing and elution, the $3 \times { \mathsf { H i s - O } } 1 1$ protein was separated by SDS-PAGE and subjected to immunoblot analysis with anti-His (TransGen Biotech, HT501, 1:5000). 

# EMSA

Oligonucleotide probes of the ZmSSRP1 promoter were synthesized and labelled with biotin at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end with a Biotin $3 ^ { \prime }$ End DNA Labeling Kit (Thermo) according to the standard procedures. Each probe was mixed with purified recombinant protein at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 20 min in reaction buffer $( 2 0 ~ \mu \ L )$ containing $1 0 \times$ binding buffer, $50 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, 100 mM MgCl2, $1 ~ \mu \ g / \mu \ L$ poly (dI-dC), $5 0 ~ { \mathsf { m M } } ~ { \mathsf { K C } } { \mathsf { I } }$ and $1 \%$ (v/v) NP-40. Biotin-labelled DNA was detected according to the instructions of the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo). The luminescence was visualized on a Tanon-5200 M imaging system. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

Dual-luciferase reporter assays were performed as previously described (Li et al., 2024). The CDSs of the O11 gene were cloned into expression vectors including pGreen-62SK and pCAMBIA1305 driven by the CaMV 35S promoter. The promoter regions of the target genes were cloned into pGreen-0800-LUC just upstream of the LUC coding sequence. The plasmid constructs were cotransformed as needed into maize protoplasts, and after overnight incubation, the protoplasts were collected and luciferase activity was measured using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega). This allowed us to assess if O11 activation influences the promoter activity of target genes. 

# Histological analysis

Immature WT and mutant kernels at 10 and 15 DAP were collected from the same ear for paraffin sectioning. The kernels were fixed in $2 \%$ FAA solution for $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ at room temperature and were dehydrated in an ethanol gradient series. Subsequently, the samples were immersed in paraffin with xylene and sectioned 

with a microtome. Sections were stained and imaged using an OLYMPUS BX53 Microscope. 

# Scanning electron microscopy and transmission electron microscopy

Freshly collected immature wild-type (WT) and mutant kernels at 15 days after pollination (DAP) were used for scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis, respectively. Established protocols were followed for sample preparation (Wang et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019). Images were captured with the S3400N SEM and H7600 TEM (Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan). Starch granule sizes were determined by measuring SEM images. 

# Transcriptome analysis (RNA-seq)

The details for RNA-seq were as described previously. Total RNA was extracted from 21-DAP maize kernel endosperm using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNase-free DNase was used to eliminate DNA contamination. The concentration and quality of total RNA (from 3 independent groups for each genotype) were determined on a NanoDrop 2000/2000c Spectrophotometer. Sequencing libraries were generated by Novogene Co., Ltd. using a NEBNextUltra RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB) following the manufacturer’s recommendations; index codes were added to attribute sequences to each sample. The index-coded samples were clustered using a HiSeq 4000 PE Cluster Kit (Illumina) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. After cluster generation, the libraries were sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq4000 platform as 150-bp paired-end reads. Clean reads were aligned to the B73 maize genome (RefGen version 4.47) using TopHat. Gene expression levels were calculated with Cufflinks (version 2.1.1) and normalized to Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads (FPKM). The DESeq package in R software was used to identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs) (Love et al., 2014). False discovery rate $( \mathsf { F D R } ) < 0 . 0 5$ and fold-change $( \mathsf { F C } ) > 1 . 5$ were set as cutoffs for significant DEGs. The GO analysis was performed using the database agriGO v2.0 (http://systemsbiology.cau.edu.cn/agriGOv2/index.php). 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

The Y1H assay was performed with the Y1HGold strain, in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions (MATCHMAKER One Hybrid System; Clontech). The O11 CDS (full-length coding sequence) was inserted into the pGADT7-Rec (AD-rec) vector. The promoter of the ZmSSRP1 was inserted into the pAbAi vector. The vector combinations were co-transformed into the yeast strain Y1HGold and grown on SD/ Leu or SD/ Leu with Aureobasidin A (Aba) medium. Cells were diluted in three concentrations, from left to right, and photos were taken after 3 days. The plates were incubated for 2–3 days at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Yeast cells co-expressing pAbAip53pro and AD-rec-P53 were used as the positive control. pAbAi-ZmSSRP1pro and AD-rec were used as the negative control. 

# Luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assay and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assay

To investigate protein–protein interactions between ZmSSRP1, ZmGBSS1, ZmISA1 and ZmSS4, two complementary assays were utilized: luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) and bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC). For the LCI assay, ZmSSRP1-nLuc, cLuc-ZmGBSS1, cLuc-ZmISA1 and cLuc-ZmSS4 constructs were generated. These constructs were electrophoresed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and then co-infiltrated 

into the leaves of N. benthamiana. After 48–72 h, the luciferase signal was measured using an LB 985 imaging system. Each experiment was performed at least three times with similar results. The interaction pair of DBR1 and SIC was used as positive control (Wu et al., 2022). 

For BiFC assay, 35S:ZmSSRP1-nYFP and 35S:ZmGBSS1-cYFP, 35S:ZmISA1-cYFP and 35S:ZmSS4-cYFP constructs were generated. These constructs were co-expressed in the maize leaf protoplasts. After $7 2 \ \mathrm { h } ,$ , any interaction between the proteins of interest would bring the YFP fragments together, allowing visualization of a complete YFP molecule and the generation of a fluorescent signal using a confocal microscope. Empty vectors were included as negative controls to assess background fluorescence. 

# Statistical analysis

Data processing of means, standard deviations and $P .$ -values was performed with Microsoft Excel (2016): AVERAGE, STDEV.S and Student’s t-test, respectively. The one-way ANOVA test in the SPSS software was employed to determine whether there are significant differences among 3 or more groups. The P-value and false discovery rate (FDR) of DEGs in the RNA-seq data were calculated by the DESeq2 R package (with default arguments).
