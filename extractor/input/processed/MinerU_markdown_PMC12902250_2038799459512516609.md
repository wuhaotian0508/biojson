# ABSTRACT

Anthocyanins are plant pigments that play diverse roles in plant growth, adaptation, and stress tolerance. Anthocyanin biosynthesis is tightly regulated, but the underlying regulatory mechanisms remain unclear. Here, we identify a regulatory module composed of the DNA-binding protein VAL1 (VIVIPAROUS1/ABI3- LIKE 1) and a SIN3 (SWI-INDEPENDENT 3)-like histone deacetylase complex that dynamically regulates anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis thaliana. Under normal growth conditions, VAL1 recruits the SNL (SIN3-Like)–HDA19 (HISTONE DEACETYLASE 19) complex (SNL–HDA19c) to the PRODUCTION OF ANTHOCYANIN PIGMENT 1 (PAP1) locus for histone deacetylation. Moreover, the negative regulators of jasmonic acid (JA) signaling, JASMONATE-ZIM DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins, interact with VAL1 and further stabilize the binding of VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c to PAP1 chromatin. These molecular interactions transcriptionally repress PAP1 and inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis. Upon JA accumulation, JAZs are degraded, resulting in the release of both VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c from the PAP1 chromatin. This release leads to an immediate increase in histone acetylation, promoting transcriptional activation of PAP1 and anthocyanin production. These findings elucidate a regulatory module (VAL1–JAZ–SNL–HDA19c) that represses anthocyanin biosynthesis under normal growth conditions and further reveal how the stress hormone JA rapidly induces anthocyanin production, enabling plants to adapt to their growth conditions. 

Key words: anthocyanin biosynthesis, histone deacetylase complex, SIN3-Like, HDA19, PAP1, jasmonate

# INTRODUCTION

Anthocyanins are water-soluble pigments that contribute to plant growth, environmental adaptation, and various stress responses. As pigments, they are responsible for red, purple, and blue hues in various plant tissues and organs, particularly in reproductive organs where pigmentation attracts insects for pollination and seed dispersal. In addition, anthocyanins increase the nutritional 

value of certain fruits (Glover and Martin, 2012; LaFountain and Yuan, 2021). As sessile organisms, plants are exposed to various environmental stresses throughout their life cycle. In response to biotic and abiotic stresses such as herbivore feeding, pathogen infection, drought, salt stress, low temperature, nutrient deficiency, and high-light stress, anthocyanin biosynthesis is induced as part of the plant’s stress tolerance or defense strategy (Lillo et al., 2008; Nakabayashi et al., 2014;

Truong et al., 2018; Naing and Kim, 2021). Functioning as potent antioxidants, anthocyanins help mitigate oxidative stress by scavenging reactive oxygen species, thereby enhancing abiotic stress tolerance or defense against herbivores and pathogens (Nakabayashi et al., 2014; LaFountain and Yuan, 2021; Naing and Kim, 2021). 

The expression of structural genes in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway is coordinately activated by a highly conserved transcription factor (TF) complex known as myeloblastosis (MYB)–basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH )–WD40 (MBW) in flowering plants (Ramsay and Glover, 2005; Grotewold, 2006). In Arabidopsis, four MYB TFs are involved in the activation of anthocyanin biosynthesis genes, including PAP1/MYB75 and three homologs likely arisen from tandem duplication of PAP1. Among them, PAP1 functions as a master regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis seedlings (Gonzalez et al., 2008; Dubos et al., 2010). In addition to MYBs, three bHLH TFs, GLABRA 3 (GL3), EGL3 (Enhancer of GL3), and TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 8 (TT8), act as co-activators of anthocyanin biosynthesis genes (Zumajo-Cardona et al., 2023). Upon induction by environmental stress, PAP1 expression increases and PAP1 forms the MBW complex with bHLHs, and the constitutively expressed WD40-domain protein TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 1 (Walker et al., 1999; Naing and Kim, 2021). This complex then activates the expression of biosynthetic genes of the anthocyanin pathway, leading to anthocyanin accumulation in response to stress (LaFountain and Yuan, 2021). 

The plant stress hormone jasmonic acid (JA) is known to induce anthocyanin biosynthesis (Qi et al., 2011) and is widely distributed across land plants (Howe et al., 2018). The synthesis of JAs is induced by various abiotic and biotic stresses and promotes stress tolerance or defense (Howe et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023). For example, JA signaling is essential for responding to wounding and herbivore attacks (Chung et al., 2008; Qi et al., 2011). The primary receptor for JAs is the F-box protein CORONATINE INSENSITIVE 1 (COI1), which functions as a subunit of the E3 ubiquitin ligase complex SCFCOI1 (Katsir et al., 2008; Yan et al., 2009; Sheard et al., 2010). In the presence of JAs, COI1 forms a co-receptor complex with the JASMONATE-ZIM DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins, negative regulators of JA signaling, resulting in their degradation (Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007; Katsir et al., 2008; Yan et al., 2009, 2013; Sheard et al., 2010). The JAZ protein family in Arabidopsis comprises 13 members (JAZ1 to JAZ13), each possessing a conserved Nterminal ZIM (ZINC-FINGER EXPRESSED IN INFLORESCENCE MERISTEM) domain and a C-terminal JA-associated (Jas) domain (Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007; Thireault et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2021). In the absence of JAs, JAZ proteins bind to JAresponsive TFs via their Jas domains, inhibiting TF activities and thus effectively repressing the expression of JA-responsive genes (Fernandez-Calvo et al., 2011; Qi et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2015). For instance, in the absence of JAs, JAZs bind to PAP1 and the bHLH TFs, including TT8, GL3, and EGL3, thereby interfering with formation of the MBW complex (Qi et al., 2011). Upon JA accumulation, JAZ degradation releases PAP1 and bHLH proteins, enabling assembly of the transcription-activation complex MBW and subsequent activation of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (Qi et al., 2011). 

# An HDAC complex regulates anthocyanin biosynthesis

In addition to JAZ-mediated inhibition, the activity of the MBW complex is negatively regulated by several other proteins. Under dark conditions, the RING-finger protein COP1 (CONSTITUTIVE PHOTOMORPHOGENIC 1) directly interacts with nuclear PAP1, mediating its ubiquitination and proteasome-dependent degradation (Maier et al., 2013). Similarly, in the absence of gibberellic acid, DELLA proteins physically associate with PAP1 and several bHLH subunits of the MBW complex, destabilizing this transcriptional activation complex (Qi et al., 2014). At the transcription level, JAs and multiple abiotic stresses, such as wounding, high light, low temperature, and salt stress, can induce PAP1 expression to promote anthocyanin biosynthesis. However, the molecular mechanisms underlying this transcriptional activation remain poorly understood (Shan et al., 2009; Shin et al., 2013; Lee et al., 2016; Naing and Kim, 2021). 

Eukaryotic gene expression is often regulated by chromatin modifications, with histone acetylation serving as a key mechanism. In general, acetylation of histone tails promotes chromatin opening and activation of gene transcription (Roth et al., 2001; Espinosa-Cores et al., 2020). Histone acetylation levels are dynamically controlled by the balance between histone acetyltransferases (HATs) and histone deacetylases (HDACs) (Xiong et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023). HATs promote gene expression by acetylating histone lysine residues, whereas HDACs remove these acetyl groups, promoting chromatin closing and transcriptional repression (Roth et al., 2001; Pandey et al., 2002; Cui et al., 2023). In Arabidopsis, over 20 HDACs have been identified, 18 of which are homologs of the yeast RPD3 (Reduced Potassium Deficiency 3) superfamily (Pandey et al., 2002; Alinsug et al., 2009). These HDACs function within multi-protein complexes to deacetylate the histones of target genes and repress their expression (Mehdi et al., 2016; Feng et al., 2021). Several RPD3-type HDAC complexes have been identified in Arabidopsis, including the SNL (SIN3-Like) complex, the SANT complex comprising Switching-Defective Protein 3 (Swi3), Adaptor 2 (Ada2), Nuclear Receptor Corepressor (N-CoR), and Transcription Factor IIIB, and the ESANT complex bearing the ELM2–SANT-domain-containing protein (Feng et al., 2021). These complexes regulate the expression of various genes involved in growth, development, and environmental responses in plants. 

The SIN3 protein, first identified in yeast, functions as a scaffold protein in the assembly of HDAC complexes and is highly conserved across yeast, plants, and mammals (Kadamb et al., 2013). In Arabidopsis, six SIN3 homologous genes have been identified, named SIN3-LIKE 1 (SNL1) through SNL6 (Bowen et al., 2010; Huang et al., 2019). SNL1 and SNL2 have been shown to control seed dormancy (Wang et al., 2016), and both proteins, in conjunction with HISTONE DEACETYLASE 9 (HDA9) or HDA19, participate in the regulation of floral transition and light response in plants (Huang et al., 2019; Ning et al., 2019; Jing et al., 2021). Biochemical analyses have revealed that SNLs, together with the WD40-domain protein MSI1 (MULTICOPY SUPPRESSOR OF IRA1), HDC1 (HISTONE DEACETYLATION COMPLEX 1), and HDA19, form an SNL–HDAC complex (Mehdi et al., 2016; Feng et al., 2021). This SNL–HDAC complex deacetylates core histones at several genes within the abscisic acid signaling pathway, fine-tuning the response to abiotic salt stress (Mehdi et al., 2016). Because most biotic and abiotic stresses encountered by plants occur for a limited duration, 

plant stress responses are often short-lived and dissipate once the stimulus is removed or withdrawn (Zhang et al., 2022). To date, whether and how an SNL–HDAC complex mediates dynamic control of hormone or stress responses in plants remains largely unknown. 

Here, we report that the SNL–HDA19 complex mediates dynamic control of anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to JA in Arabidopsis. We found that SNL1 to SNL5 function in partial redundancy and, together with HDC1 and HDA19, repress the expression of the master regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis, PAP1. The DNA-binding protein VAL1 binds to cis-regulatory elements in the proximal promoter region of PAP1 and recruits the SNL– HDC1–HDA19 complex (hereafter SNL–HDA19c), which deacetylates PAP1 chromatin, thereby repressing PAP1 expression and anthocyanin biosynthesis under normal growth conditions. In addition, JAZ proteins directly interact with VAL1 to enrich VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c on PAP1 chromatin, further repressing PAP1 expression. Upon JA induction, JAZs are degraded, and both VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c are released from PAP1 chromatin. This results in a sharp increase in histone acetylation, transcriptional activation of PAP1, and anthocyanin accumulation. Following JA withdrawal, VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c are reloaded onto PAP1 chromatin, restoring histone deacetylation and PAP1 repression. Together, these results reveal that the JA– JAZ and VAL1–SNL–HDA19c regulatory modules work together to modulate histone acetylation dynamics at the PAP1 locus, providing a mechanism for fine-tuned and reversible control of anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to environmental stimuli. This mechanism ensures rapid biosynthesis of the potent antioxidants anthocyanins in response to the stress hormone JA but prevents their overproduction once stress signals subside. 

# RESULTS

# SNL1-5 function in partial redundancy to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis

To investigate the functional redundancy among SNL genes in Arabidopsis, we used a previously generated series of higher-order snl mutants (Huang et al., 2019). Under standard growth conditions, unexpectedly, the snl1/2/3/4 quadruple mutant exhibited purplered pigmentation in the petiole and shoot apical meristem regions, indicating an accumulation of anthocyanins (Figure 1A). Quantification of anthocyanin levels revealed that the snl2, snl3, snl4, and snl5 single mutants synthesized moderately more anthocyanins than the wild type (WT), whereas snl1 was comparable to WT (Figure 1B). Interestingly, the anthocyanin contents were much higher in the snl1/2, snl1/3, and snl1/4 double mutants compared to their respective single mutants (Figure 1B), revealing functional redundancy between SNL1 and SNL2, SNL3, or SNL4. Furthermore, the snl2/3, snl2/4, and snl3/ 4 double mutants accumulated more anthocyanins than their corresponding single mutants, suggesting an additive effect of anthocyanin accumulation in the double mutants (Figure 1B), further supporting partial functional redundancy among these genes. 

Furthermore, the anthocyanin content in the snl1/2/3/4 quadruple mutant was much higher than in the respective triple mutants (Figure 1C). Anthocyanin content in the quintuple mutant snl1/2/ 3/4/5 was further increased, with a 1.5-fold increase relative 

to the quadruple mutant (Figures 1A and 1D). To confirm whether the mutations in SNL1 to SNL5 were indeed responsible for anthocyanin overaccumulation, we performed complementation experiments. Transformation of snl1/2/3/5 with a WT copy of genomic SNL1 or SNL2, and snl2/3/4/5 with genomic SNL3, SNL4, or SNL5 restored anthocyanin contents to those of the respective triple mutants (Figure 1E). Taken together, these results show that SNL1 to SNL5 function in partial redundancy to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis plants grown under normal conditions. 

# SNLs repress PAP1 expression to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis

The MYB–bHLH–WD40 transcriptional activation complex plays a crucial role in activating anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (Ramsay and Glover, 2005; Grotewold, 2006). The MYB transcription factor PAP1 is a master regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis in Arabidopsis seedlings (Gonzalez et al., 2008). Under normal growth conditions, PAP1 is repressed or expressed at a very low level in seedlings (Gonzalez et al., 2008). However, in response to endogenous and environmental stimuli, PAP1 is induced, triggering anthocyanin biosynthesis (Gonzalez et al., 2008; Naing and Kim, 2021). Hence, PAP1 functions as a major transcriptional activator controlling the level of anthocyanins (Gonzalez et al., 2008; Dubos et al., 2010). To examine whether SNLs regulate PAP1 expression, we measured its transcript levels in WT and snl1/2/3/4/5 seedlings (grown under normal conditions) and observed a 16-fold increase (Figure 2A). In addition, PAP2 expression was strongly derepressed (Figure 2B), although PAP2 expression was much lower than that of PAP1 in seedlings. To determine whether PAP1 is required for the anthocyanin accumulation observed in the snl1/2/3/4/5 mutant, we introduced a loss-of-function pap1 allele into the snl1/2/3/4/5 mutant. Anthocyanin accumulation was eliminated in the pap1 snl1/2/3/4/5 seedlings (Figures 2C and 2D). Together, these results show that SNLs repress PAP1 expression to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis in seedlings grown under normal conditions. 

# SNL–HDA19c represses PAP1 to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis

SNL proteins function as scaffolds to assemble MSI1, HDA19, and HDC1 into SNL–HDA19c (Feng et al., 2021). To assess whether SNLs function through SNL–HDA19c to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis, we examined PAP1 expression in a loss-of-function hda19 mutant (Feng et al., 2021). PAP1 expression was de-repressed in the hda19 mutant compared to WT (Figure 2E). Consistently, the anthocyanin content in hda19 was much higher than that in WT, but was restored to WT levels by a genomic copy of HDA19 fused with green fluorescent protein (GFP) (HDA19:GFP) (Figures 2F and 2G). In addition, we found that the pap1 mutation eliminated anthocyanin accumulation in hda19 (Figures 2F and 2H). These results show that HDA19, like SNLs, represses PAP1 expression to inhibit anthocyanin biosynthesis. Notably, the extent of PAP1 de-repression in hda19 is less than that in the snl1/2/3/4/5 mutant, suggesting that other HDACs may also partner with SNLs for PAP1 repression. Moreover, hda19 displayed pleiotropic phenotypes compared to snl1/2/3/4/ 5 (Figures 2D and 2F), implying that HDA19 may function, at least in part, independently of SNLs.

# JA triggers the release of VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c from PAP1 chromatin to induce PAP1 expression

Plants are frequently exposed to various environmental stresses that induce the biosynthesis of anthocyanins as part of their adaptive defense responses (Naing and Kim, 2021). Given that JA plays a crucial role in promoting anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to both biotic and abiotic stress in plants (Howe et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023), we envisioned that JA signaling may regulate PAP1 expression. Hence, we investigated the role of SNL– HDA19c in anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to JA signaling. We applied methyl-JA (MeJA, a functional derivative of JA) to young seedlings cultured in liquid MS (Murashige and Skoog) medium (under sterile conditions) for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ , followed by JA withdrawal or elimination from the medium. Subsequently, PAP1 expression was measured in both WT and snl1/2/3/4/5. JA treatment for 6 h strongly induced PAP1 expression, while treatment for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ led to even higher expression levels in both WT and the quintuple mutant. This induction was reversed upon JA withdrawal, as PAP1 expression strongly declined after 12 h (Figure 5A). Thus, a brief JA treatment or JA rise causes a transient transcriptional activation of PAP1 expression. Examining the changes in anthocyanin content over the course of JA treatment revealed a gradual accumulation of anthocyanins that declined 3 days after JA withdrawal in both WT and snl1/2/3/4/5 (Supplemental Figure 3C). This temporal pattern indicates that the transient transcriptional activation of PAP1 leads to a temporal anthocyanin accumulation in response to JA treatment. It is noteworthy that the decline in anthocyanin content occurs later than the decrease in PAP1 mRNA expression upon JA withdrawal, likely reflecting the sustained activities of anthocyanin biosynthetic enzymes and/or the relatively slower degradation of anthocyanins.

# DISCUSSION

In this study, we demonstrate that the VAL1–SNL–HDA19c regulatory module represses PAP1 expression and anthocyanin biosynthesis under normal growth conditions, and mediates dynamic control of PAP1 expression in response to the stress hormone JA in Arabidopsis. Under normal growth conditions, VAL1 

(or VAL2) recruits SNL–HDA19c to PAP1 chromatin for histone deacetylation. The JAZ proteins further stabilize the binding of VAL1 and SNL–HDA19c to PAP1 chromatin, resulting in transcriptional repression of PAP1 and consequent inhibition of anthocyanin biosynthesis. Upon JA accumulation, JAZs are degraded, and the regulatory module of VAL1 (VAL2)–SNL– HDA19c is released from PAP1 chromatin, resulting in transcriptional activation of PAP1. Once JA concentration decreases, JAZs and VAL1–SNL–HDA19c are reloaded onto PAP1 chromatin to turn off PAP1 expression in a timely manner. This molecular mechanism ensures the rapid activation of anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to stress and effective prevention of its excessive production after the removal of a stress stimulus, to balance plant growth and stress tolerance or response.

The expression of MYB TFs, including PAP1, PAP2, and two other members, is repressed in Arabidopsis seedlings under normal growth conditions (Gonzalez et al., 2008). The rapid transcriptional activation of PAP1 in response to JA promotes formation of the MBW complex for transcriptional activation of structural genes in anthocyanin biosynthesis. These findings 

In the broader JA signaling pathway, the JAZ proteins are essential negative regulators that repress the expression of JAresponsive genes in the absence of JA. JAZs can associate with the adaptor protein NINJA (NOVEL INTERACTOR OF JAZ), which interacts with the general co-repressor TOPLESS (TPL) (Pauwels et al., 2010; An et al., 2022). TPL often engages HDACs to repress gene expression (Szemenyei et al., 2008; Plant et al., 2021), suggesting a JAZ–NINJA–TPL–HDAC regulatory module for the transcriptional repression of JAresponsive genes (Pauwels et al., 2010). In this study, we found that the regulatory module VAL1 (VAL2)–SNL–HDAC plays a crucial role in the repression of a subset of JA-responsive genes (Figure 6C). The regulatory module NINJA–TPL–HDAC likely functions in concert with VAL1–SNL–HDAC to repress the expression of JA-responsive genes in the Arabidopsis genome in the absence of JA. 

In this study, we have found that the VAL1 (VAL2)–SNL–HDA19c regulatory module mediates dynamic control of PAP1 expression and anthocyanin biosynthesis in response to JA. However, anthocyanin accumulation is also light dependent (Maier et al., 2013; Shin et al., 2013). How do high-light intensity and other abiotic stresses antagonize VAL1–SNL–HDACs to induce PAP1 expression and consequently anthocyanin biosynthesis? The bZIP TF ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) plays a crucial role in light signaling and photomorphogenesis (Osterlund et al., 2000). Under high light, the level of nuclear HY5 protein increases, and HY5 recognizes the G box and ACE box located in the proximal promoter region of PAP1 to activate its expression (Shin et al., 2013). Interestingly, both the HY5- binding sites and the VAL1 and VAL2 recognition sites (RY motifs) are near the PAP1 proximal promoter region. Therefore, under high-light stress, HY5 binds to the PAP1 proximal promoter and may antagonize the VAL1–SNL–HDA19c regulatory module, transcriptionally activating PAP1 and consequently anthocyanin biosynthesis. The transcriptional activation of PAP1 by other abiotic stresses, such as salt stress, osmotic stress, and low

temperature, may also involve a temporary release of VAL1–SNL– HDA19c from PAP1 chromatin, leading to anthocyanin biosynthesis and stress tolerance. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

All Arabidopsis thaliana mutants and transgenic lines are in the Col-0 background. The single, double, triple, and quadruple mutants of snls, snl1/2/3/ 4/5, pap1, esant1/2, sant1/2/3/4, hdc1, hda19, val1, val2, val1/2, pSNL4- SNL4:HA (in snl1/2/3/4/5), JAZ1ΔJas, and pVAL1-VAL1:FLAG (in val1/2) have been described previously (Thines et al., 2007; Yuan et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2019, 2023; Feng et al., 2021). 

Seeds were surface-sterilized and sown on half-strength MS plates supplemented with agar, followed by stratification for 2 days at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . After germination, seedlings were grown on half-strength MS agar plates supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose or transferred to soil. Seedlings were grown in growth chambers under standard long-day conditions, consisting of a photoperiod of $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ of light/8 h of darkness at $_ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and light intensity of approximately $7 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ provided by cool white lamps. 

# Plasmid construction

To generate the pHDA19-HDA19:GFP construct, a 1.5-kb promoter region and 2.6-kb coding sequence of HDA19 without the stop codon were cloned in frame with the GFP coding sequence in pMDC110 (Curtis and Grossniklaus, 2003). To prepare PAP1:GUS and $P A P 1 / / P M t ! G U S$ constructs, the $2 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mathsf { k b }$ promoter without or with mutated RY motif (G to A, C to A) and 1.4-kb coding sequence without the stop codon were cloned in frame with the GUS coding sequence in pMDC162 (Curtis and Grossniklaus, 2003). To generate GST:JAZ1 and GST:JAZ4, the full-length coding sequence for JAZ1 (0.8 kb) or JAZ4 $( 0 . 9 ~ \mathsf { k b } )$ was cloned into the pGEX-4T-1 vector in frame with the GST (glutathione S-transferase) coding sequence. 

To create VAL1:MBP:His, the coding sequence of VAL1 (2.4 kb) was cloned into the pET-28 vector in frame with the MBP (maltose binding protein) coding region fused with six copies of the His tag. To construct His:- SUMO-VAL $\eta ^ { B \bar { 3 } }$ , the coding fragment for VAL1B3 (amino acids 289 to 396) was cloned into pET-28a downstream of and in frame with 6×His:SUMO. 

# mRNA expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted from the aerial parts of ∼11-day-old seedlings using the Eastep Super Total RNA Extraction Kit (Promega) following the manufacturer’s instructions. To remove potential genomic DNA contamination, RNA samples were treated with RNase-free DNase I prior to cDNA synthesis. Complementary DNA was synthesized from ${ \sim } 1 . 0 \mu \ g$ of total RNA using an oligo(dT) primer and Moloney Murine Leukemia Virus reverse transcriptase (Gu et al., 2013). Quantitative PCR was performed on an ABI QuantStudio 5 or 6 System with the SYBR Green PCR master mix (Thermo Fisher, 4367659). Three biological replicates were analyzed. The constitutively expressed TUB2 served as an internal control for normalization. Primer sequences for PAP1, PAP2, and DFR are described in Supplemental Table 2. 

# Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)

ChIP assays were conducted as described previously (Wang et al., 2014) with minor modifications. Briefly, seedlings with or without JA treatment were cross-linked with $1 \%$ formaldehyde. Total chromatin was extracted using lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES [pH 7.5], 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, $0 . 1 \%$ deoxycholate, $0 . 1 \%$ SDS, $1 \%$ Triton X-100, 10 mM sodium butyrate, and $1 \times$ EDTA-free protease inhibitor cocktail [Roche, 4693159001]), followed by sonication and immunoprecipitation using anti-H3ac (Abcam, ab47915), anti-H3K9/18ac (Millipore, 07-593), antihemagglutinin (HA) (Sigma, H6908), anti-FLAG (Sigma, F7425), or anti-

GFP (Abcam, ab290). Immunoprecipitated DNA fragments were purified and analyzed by quantitative PCR (qPCR) to measure the amounts of fragments of the gene of interest using an ABI QuantStudio 5 or 6 system with the SYBR Green PCR master mix (Thermo Fisher, 4367659). For each sample, three biological replicates were analyzed, with the retrotransposon AtSN1 or TUB2 as internal controls (Yu et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2019). Primer sequences are described in Supplemental Table 2. 

# JA treatment

MeJA (Sigma, 392707) was prepared as a 25 mM stock solution in absolute ethanol. For treatments, $2 5 \mu \mathsf { M }$ JA was applied to 5-day-old seedlings grown in liquid half-strength MS medium supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose. Ethanol was used as a mock control. Seedlings were collected at different time points. For JA withdrawal experiments, $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after JA treatment, the seedlings were rinsed and transferred to a JA-free liquid medium of half-strength MS supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose. For the treatment of soil-grown seedlings, ${ \sim } 1 1$ -day-old $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ seedlings expressing VAL1-FLAG and SNL4-HA or VAL1:FLAG and JAZ1ΔJas (genotyped) were sprayed with $2 5 \mu \mathsf { M }$ JA or mock $( 0 . 1 \%$ ethanol). 

# Anthocyanin measurement

Anthocyanin extraction and measurement were conducted as previously described (Lei et al., 2011). In brief, ${ \sim } 1 1$ -day-old seedlings grown on halfstrength MS agar plates supplemented with $1 \%$ sucrose or liquid-cultured seedlings were harvested and ground to a fine powder in liquid nitrogen. Anthocyanins were extracted using methanol containing $1 \%$ (v/v) hydrochloric acid and quantified using a spectrophotometer. Absorbance at 530 and 657 nm was measured. The relative anthocyanin content was calculated using the formula (A530 − A657) divided by the weight of fresh tissue (g FW). 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

The yeast two-hybrid assay was conducted using the Matchmaker GAL4 system (Clontech) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Fulllength sequences or gene fragments of interest were cloned into the pGADT7 or pGBKT7 vectors. Paired constructs were then transformed into the yeast strain AH109 using the lithium acetate method, followed by spreading of yeast cells on a synthetic dropout minimal medium lacking leucine, tryptophan, histidine, and adenine (− Leu/− Trp/− His/− Ade) to observe potential protein–protein interactions. 

# Co-immunoprecipitation (Co-IP)

Co-IP assays were performed as previously described (Jiang et al., 2011) with minor modifications. In brief, total proteins were extracted from F1 seedlings (from genetic crossing) expressing VAL1:FLAG and SNL4:HA (with or without JA treatment), VAL1:FLAG and HDA19:GFP, or VAL1: FLAG and JAZ1:HA. Immunoprecipitations were conducted using anti-HA magnetic beads (SAE0197, Sigma) or anti-FLAG magnetic beads (M8823, Sigma). Magnetic bead-bound proteins were washed three times, followed by protein elution. Subsequently, western blotting was performed with anti-FLAG (A8592, Sigma), anti-GFP (Ab290, Abcam), or anti-HA (11867423001, Roche). Chemiluminescent signals were captured using a Tanon 5200 chemiluminescence imaging system or by autoradiography films and developed. 

# Protein pull-down assay

Protein pull-down assays were conducted as described previously (Li et al., 2021). GST-tagged JAZ1, GST-tagged JAZ4, and MBP:His-tagged VAL1 were expressed in the Escherichia coli (E. coli) strain BL21, followed by protein extraction. Equal volumes of protein extracts of VAL1:MBP:His and JAZ1:GST or VAL1:MBP:His and JAZ4:GST were mixed and subjected to pull-down using anti-His magnetic beads (HY-K0209, MedChemExpress), followed by washing three times with phosphate-buffered saline containing $1 \%$ Triton X-100 to remove non-specific interactions. The proteins bound to the beads were then eluted for western blotting with anti-GST (K000478P, Solarbio) to detect JAZ1:GST and JAZ4:GST. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

VAL1B3 (amino acids 289 to 396) (Tao et al., 2019) was expressed in and purified from the E. coli strain BL21 as follows. E. coli cells were collected and resuspended in lysis buffer (25 mM Tris [pH 8.0] and 400 mM NaCl), followed by sonication in lysis buffer supplemented with 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride and cOmplete EDTA-free protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche, 04693132001). The VAL1B3 fusion protein (His:SUMO-VAL1B3) was purified from the clarified whole-cell lysate with a gravityflow column packed with HisSep Ni-NTA Agarose Resin (Yeasen, 20502ES10) and eluted with lysis buffer supplemented with $2 5 0 \mathsf { m M }$ imidazole. The eluted proteins were further purified by size-exclusion chromatography (Superdex 200 Increase 10/300 GL, GE Healthcare) in a buffer consisting of 25 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0) and 400 mM NaCl. 

Oligonucleotide probes were synthesized and 5’-labeled with biotin (Tsingke Biotechnology, Beijing, China). Probe sequences are described in Supplemental Table 2. EMSA was performed using a Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Beyotime, GS009) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, the biotin-labeled probes were incubated with the VAL1B3 fusion protein at room temperature for 20 min, and the free and bound probes were separated in an acrylamide gel. An unlabeled probe was used as a competitor, and an RY-box–mutated probe served as a negative control.

Student’s t-test and one-way ANOVA (analysis of variance) were performed using GraphPad Prism (version 9.5.1).
