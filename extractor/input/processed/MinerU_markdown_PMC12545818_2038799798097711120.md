# ABSTRACT

Ficus carica L., an ancient crop of considerable economic value, exhibits distinctive biological traits, including flowerless fruit (syconium) development, psoralen biosynthesis, and drought tolerance. In this study, we present a chromosome-level genome assembly (323.03 Mb, $N 5 0 = 2 3 . 8 2$ Mb) of F. carica, revealing an evolutionary divergence from the closely related genus Broussonetia approximately 50 million years ago. Comparative genomic analyses revealed a contraction in the s-domain receptor-like kinase gene family, which is associated with unisexual fruit formation, and identified conserved MADS-box transcription factor genes (FcAGL6, FcAP2, and FcSEP1/2) that regulate syconium development through spatiotemporal expression patterns. Metabolomic profiling demonstrated tissue-specific accumulation of bioactive compounds, with roots serving as the primary reservoir for psoralen. We also identified key genes—FcANS and FcCHS10, involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, and FcMS, involved in psoralen biosynthesis—and validated their functions using a newly established transient transformation system. Transcriptomic analysis under drought stress identified the NAC transcription factor FcJA2 as a central regulator of stress tolerance that enhances reactive oxygen species scavenging and osmotic regulation by activating FcPP2C5 and FcP5CS. Overexpression of FcJA2 significantly improved drought resistance. Further analyses demonstrated that FcJA2-mediated drought-response modules are conserved across the Moraceae. These findings provide valuable insights into the genetic mechanisms underlying fruit development, specialized metabolite biosynthesis, and stress tolerance in F. carica, offering potential applications for crop improvement and advancing our understanding of genome evolution and environmental adaptability in the Moraceae family.

# RESULTS

# Genome assembly and annotation

We sequenced and assembled the genome of the widely cultivated $F$ . carica ‘Brunswick’ cultivar using the Illumina and 

PacBio platforms with scaffolding assisted by chromatin conformation capture (Hi-C). $k \cdot$ -mer analysis $( \mathsf { k } = 1 7 )$ of 32.63 Gb of Illumina paired-end reads estimated a genome size of 380.59 Mb, with $0 . 7 5 \%$ heterozygosity and $5 0 . 8 8 \%$ repetitive sequence content (Supplemental Table 1-1). The PacBio platform generated 96.51 Gb of long-read single-molecule sequencing data, providing approximately $2 9 8 . 7 6 \times$ coverage of the genome (Supplemental Table 2). De novo assembly produced 260 contigs with an N50 of 2.66 Mb, resulting in a total assembly size of 323.01 Mb (Supplemental Table 3-1). 

Karyotyping confirmed that F. carica possesses 13 chromosome pairs $( 2 \mathsf { n } = 2 6 \mathsf { \Omega }$ ; Supplemental Figure 1B). To generate pseudochromosomes, Hi-C data were used to extend scaffolds and anchor them to chromosomes, resulting in a final assembly of 323.03 Mb, consisting of 80 scaffolds with a scaffold N50 of 23.82 Mb (Supplemental Table 3-2). The total anchored chromosome length accounted for $9 7 . 5 3 \%$ of the assembled genome (Supplemental Table 3-3). Genome completeness was assessed using the Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs (BUSCO) dataset, which showed that $9 8 . 9 \%$ of complete genes were captured (Supplemental Table 4). Core Eukaryotic Genes Mapping Approach evaluation further showed that $9 6 . 3 7 \%$ (239 of 248) of conserved core eukaryotic genes from the gene library of 6 eukaryotic model organisms were successfully identified (Supplemental Table 4). Compared with previously published F. carica genomes—namely ‘Horaishi’ (Mori et al., 2017), ‘Dottato’ (Usai et al., 2020), and ‘Orphan’ (Bao et al., 2023)—our ‘Brunswick’ assembly exhibits the highest scaffold N50 value and the greatest BUSCO completeness (Supplemental Table 1-2). Collectively, these results confirm that the present genome assembly achieves highquality chromosome-level resolution. 

For genome annotation, we performed RNA sequencing for five tissues (root, stem, leaf, sarcocarp, and pericarp), followed by PacBio full-length transcriptome sequencing of a mixed library from these tissues. This enabled the prediction of 23 828 protein-coding genes (Supplemental Table 5), of which $9 2 . 1 \%$ were functionally annotated (Supplemental Table 6). In addition, $9 4 . 5 7 \%$ of annotated mRNAs were successfully mapped back to the genome. We also identified 645 miRNAs, 511 tRNAs, 217 rRNAs, and 544 small nuclear RNAs (Supplemental Table 7). There were 134.77 Mb of repetitive sequences, accounting for $4 4 . 0 3 \%$ of the assembly (Supplemental Table 7). 

These findings, combined with the exceptional completeness of the assembly, establish the ‘Brunswick’ genome as a valuable resource for comparative genomics in F. carica. 

# Comparative genomic features and chromosomal evolution

We performed a comparative analysis of genomic features, including gene number, exon and intron length, coding sequence length, untranslated regions, and repetitive sequence content, between F. carica and five related Moraceae species: Ficus hispida, Ficus microcarpa, Ficus hirta, Morus alba, and Morus notabilis (Supplemental Table 1-3). The F. carica genome contains 23 828 predicted genes, fewer than F. hispida (29 390), F. microcarpa (27 210), F. hirta (28 623), M. alba (24 280), and M. 

notabilis (25 149). Structurally, the F. carica genome comprises 124 620 exons and 100 792 introns, with average lengths of 272.37 bp and 430.01 bp, respectively. This gene structure is similar to that of F. hirta (average exon and intron lengths of 375.18 bp and 429.85 bp) but differs from M. alba, which has shorter exons (239.79 bp) and longer introns (554.81 bp). Repetitive elements account for $4 4 . 0 3 \%$ of the F. carica genome, a proportion lower than in F. microcarpa $( 4 8 . 9 3 \% )$ and M. alba $( 5 2 \% )$ but slightly higher than in M. notabilis $( 3 9 . 9 5 \% )$ . 

Collinearity analyses revealed extensive genomic rearrangements, including chromosome fusions, splits, and inversions, between F. carica and its close relatives (F. hispida, F. microcarpa, and M. alba). Comparative chromosomal analyses indicated that chromosome 6 in F. carica formed independently through the fusion of two ancestral chromosomes corresponding to chromosomes 2 and 7 in F. hispida and to chromosomes 2 and 14 in F. microcarpa (Supplemental Figure 11A–11E). Furthermore, chromosomes 2 and 5 in F. carica correspond to chromosome 3 in F. hispida, and chromosome 7 in F. carica corresponds to chromosomes 10 and 13 in M. alba (Supplemental Figure 11F and 11G). 

Multiple inversion events were also identified. Compared to F. hispida, inversions occurred on F. carica chromosomes 3 (vs. chromosome 1), 2 (vs. chromosome 3), 5 (vs. chromosome 3), 8 (vs. chromosome 13), 9 (vs. chromosome 9), 12 (vs. chromosome 8), and 13 (vs. chromosome 6) (Supplemental Figure 11D and 11E). Compared to F. microcarpa, inversions were observed on chromosomes 2 (vs. chromosome 7), 3 (vs. chromosome 1), 5 (vs. chromosome 3), 8 (vs. chromosome 13), 12 (vs. chromosome 8), and 13 (vs. chromosome 6) (Supplemental Figure 11B and 11C). Compared to M. alba, inversions were observed on chromosome 5 (vs. chromosome 6) and chromosome 7 (vs. chromosomes 10 and 13) (Supplemental Figure 11F and 11G).

MADS-box family genes are central to the ABCDE model of floral organ identity (ABCDE) model of flower development. To investigate how sepal or receptacle curling leads to the flowerless fruit structure (syconium) in F. carica (Rosianski et al., 2016), we constructed a phylogenetic tree using homologous sequences from Arabidopsis, Nymphaea (water lily), B. papyrifera (a representative mulberry species in Moraceae), and F. carica (Figure 3A). We identified 71 MADS-box genes in F. carica (27 type I and 44 type II), comparable to the 75 MADS-box genes (24 type I and 51 type II) in B. papyrifera (Supplemental Table 10), in which flowers are fully exposed. Collinearity analysis revealed much greater synteny among MADS genes in F. carica, F. microcarpa, and F. hispida than between these Ficus species and either B. papyrifera or M. alba (Supplemental Figure 18).

# Metabolite profiling of F. carica reveals its bioactive compounds

F. carica is rich in secondary (specialized) metabolites throughout the entire plant (Wang et al., 2024). To comprehensively identify its bioactive compounds, we performed metabolomic analyses across multiple tissues and organs, identifying a total of 4370 metabolites. The leaves contained the greatest number of specialized metabolites, with 1897 distinct compounds, whereas the latex had the fewest with 1095 compounds (Figure 4A). 

Among these, 189 metabolites were shared across all tissues and organs. Their composition was relatively similar across categories, with the highest proportions belonging to lipids, lipidlike molecules, phenylpropanoids, and polyketides, whereas alkaloids and their derivatives were the least abundant. Additional metabolites exhibited tissue and organ specificity, including 449 in roots, 325 in stems, 435 in leaves, 263 in sarcocarp, 248 in pericarp, and 207 in latex (Figure 4B). 

To identify bioactive compounds with potential health benefits, we analyzed 7 major classes of bioactive substances: terpenoids (651 compounds), flavonoids (391 compounds), alkaloids (57 compounds), cholines (20 compounds), coumarins (91 compounds), fatty acids (416 compounds), and phenols (72 compounds) (Supplemental Table 12). Tissue distribution analysis revealed that terpenoids (295 compounds) and phenols (49 compounds) displayed the greatest diversity in the leaves. In contrast, flavonoids (209 compounds), coumarins and their derivatives (71 compounds), as well as cholines (15 compounds), were predominantly enriched in the roots. Fatty acids and conjugates (111 compounds) were also highly abundant in the stem, whereas alkaloids (29 compounds) were largely concentrated in the sarcocarp and stem (Figure 4C–4J).

Whole-genome analysis of F. carica identified 64 genes from 12 enzyme families involved in flavonoid biosynthesis, including FcCHS, FcCHI, FcF3H, FcF3′ H, FcF3′ 5′ H, FcFNS, FcFLS, FcDFR, FcANS, FcANR, FcLAR, and FcUFGT. To identify the functional genes associated with anthocyanin biosynthesis, we analyzed the sarcocarp transcriptome at the three developmental stages and identified 27 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway, including 12 FcCHS genes, 1 FcCHI gene, 1 FcF3H gene, 2 FcF3′ H genes, 1 FcF3′ 5′ H gene, 3 FcDFR genes, 1 FcANS gene, 1 FcANR gene, and 5 FcUFGT genes (Figure 5B). Combined with quantitative analysis of sarcocarp anthocyanin content, the S2 stage was identified as a critical period for anthocyanin accumulation. During this stage, several genes—including FcANS, FcCHS3 (FC12G00890), FcUFGT (FC04G01699), FcCHS4 (FC12G00891), FcDFR (FC02G01666), and FcCHI (FC04G01037)—were significantly upregulated relative to the S1 and S3 stages, with FcANS showing more than a 30-fold increase (Supplemental Table 13-1). 

In contrast to the sarcocarp, anthocyanin content in the pericarp increased significantly during the S3 stage. RNA-seq analysis of the pericarp revealed that the expression of several FcCHS genes, including FcCHS11 (FC12G00899), FcCHS2 (FC12G00841), FcCHS12 (FC12G00900), FcCHS10 (FC12G00898), and FcCHS7 (FC12G00894), increased more than sixfold at the S3 stage compared with the S1 and S2 stages (Supplemental Table 13-2). Correlation analysis showed that the correlation coefficients between FcCHS (FC12G00898, FC12G00841, FC12G00895, and FC12G00899) and anthocyanin content were all greater than 0.98 $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ ), with FcCHS10 (FC12G00898) exhibiting the strongest correlation (Supplemental Table 13-3). 

Comparative analysis revealed that FcANS (FC10G01375) and FcCHS10 (FC12G00898) are highly conserved among the Moraceae, with their promoters containing multiple MYB, MYC, and W-box elements (Supplemental Figures 13 and 14). Transcriptomic data showed that both genes reached peak expression during the final stage of fruit development in F. carica (variety ‘Zibao’) (Supplemental Figure 14F) and were markedly upregulated in mature fruits relative to immature fruits in B. papyrifera (Supplemental Figure 14H). Their expression was also substantially higher during the fruit-coloring stage in black mulberry relative to white mulberry (Supplemental Figure 14G). Based on these results, we propose that FcANS and FcCHS10 play key roles in anthocyanin accumulation in Moraceae. To investigate their functions, we cloned FcANS and FcCHS10 and performed functional studies. We constructed overexpression vectors for both genes and established a transient transformation technique via pedicel transport. Fruits overexpressing these genes exhibited significantly redder coloration compared to those transformed with empty vector controls (Figure 5C). Quantitative analysis of anthocyanin content revealed increases of $2 0 4 . 1 \%$

# FcMS is a key modulator of psoralen biosynthesis in F. carica

Psoralen, a potent furocoumarin compound, is well-known for its pharmacological properties. F. carica plants are one of the primary sources of psoralen (Marrelli et al., 2012). Psoralen biosynthesis occurs via the furanocoumarin biosynthetic pathway. In our metabolomics data, we identified eight metabolites associated with this pathway across multiple tissues and organs, including roots, stems, leaves, fruits, and latex. Notably, five of these metabolites were most abundant in the roots, namely umbelliferone, psoralen, xanthotoxol, bergapten, and 5- hydroxyxanthotoxin. We also identified 13 F. carica genes associated with furanocoumarin biosynthesis, including FcPT1, FcC4M, and FcMS. RNA-seq analysis revealed that FcC4M (FC05G01206), FcMS (FC11G00178), and FcC82C4 (FC01G0 0754) were expressed at significantly higher levels in roots than in other organs (Figure 6A). Integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic correlation analysis produced a gene–metabolite interaction network and demonstrated that FcMS expression (FC11G00178) was positively correlated with psoralen content (Figure 6A). Furthermore, correlation analysis between the expression of the 13 identified genes and the levels of the 8 metabolites revealed highly significant correlations between FcMS (FC11G00178) and 3 of the metabolites, each with correlation coefficients (r) of at least 0.95 $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ ). The strongest correlation was observed with psoralen, with ${ \sf r } = 0 . 9 9 7$ (Figure 6B). 

Based on these findings, we speculate that FcMS (FC11G00178) functions as a hub gene in the furanocoumarin biosynthetic pathway and potentially regulates psoralen biosynthesis. Interestingly, we also identified a homologous FcMS locus in F. carica, FC11G00180, which shares $93 \%$ sequence similarity with FC11G00178. To assess the roles of FC11G00178 and FC11G00180 in psoralen biosynthesis, overexpression vectors for both loci were constructed. Transient transformations were performed using an Agrobacterium rhizogenes-mediated system developed for F. carica. Compared with empty vector control (PF) plants, FC11G00178-overexpressing plants exhibited a 2.5-fold increase in gene expression (Figure 6C) and an $8 5 \%$ increase in psoralen content (Figure 6D and 6G). In contrast, FC11G00180- overexpressing plants showed a 5-fold increase in gene expression (Figure 6E) but no significant change in psoralen content (Figure 6F and 6G). In virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) assays, silencing of FcMS (FC11G00178) reduced transcript levels by 16.67-fold relative to tobacco rattle virus (TRV) controls, accompanied by a $4 7 \%$ decrease in psoralen content (Figure 6J).

# DISCUSSION

F. carica is an ancient fruit tree with exceptional adaptability and high economic value. In this study, we successfully assembled the genome of the main cultivated variety, F. carica ‘Brunswick,’ and investigated the molecular mechanisms underlying the development of key biological traits in F. carica (Figure 7). 

We generated a chromosome-level genome assembly (323.03 Mb) with an N50 of 23.82 Mb, anchoring $9 7 . 5 3 \%$ of the genomic sequences to 13 pseudochromosomes. BUSCO evaluation indicated that $9 4 . 2 \%$ of plant-specific genes were complete, which confirmed the accuracy and completeness of the assembly. Phylogenetic analyses revealed that F. carica shares close evolutionary relationships with F. erecta, F. microcarpa, and F. hispida, further validating the quality of this assembly as a valuable resource for functional genomics in F. carica. 

The syconium, a hallmark of the Ficus genus, is a complex floral structure composed of numerous small seeds and drupoid units that develop within a fleshy receptacle, ultimately forming a single, compound, multi-flowered fruit (commonly known as the fig) (Rosianski et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2020). However, the molecular basis of syconium formation remains unclear. MADSbox family genes play critical roles in the regulation of diverse developmental processes, including floral organ patterning, gametophyte cell division, and fruit differentiation (Dinneny and Yanofsky, 2005; Colombo et al., 2008). Central to these processes is the well-established ABCDE model of floral organ formation, in which class A and E genes are responsible for sepal formation (Liu et al., 2018). In phylogenetically related Rosaceae species, such as apple and strawberry, fruit also develops from the receptacle in a process regulated by genes such as AP2 (class A) and SEP1/2 (class E) (Ireland et al., 2013; Lu et al., 2024). In this study, we compared large-scale transcriptomic data from post-anthesis fruit buds of various F. carica and M. alba cultivars, early-stage F. carica fruit peel, and early-stage B. papyrifera fruit. We observed that genes such as FcSEP1/2, FcAP2, FcFUL, and FcAGL6 were significantly upregulated in F. carica fruit buds and maintained high expression throughout fruit development. Comparative genomic analyses with other Moraceae, including M. notabilis and M. alba, revealed that FcAGL6, together with A- and E-function genes (FcAP2, FcFUL, and FcSEP1/2), has undergone strong purifying selection in F. carica. These findings provide strong evidence that FcAGL6 and A- and E-function genes, particularly FcAP2, FcFUL, and FcSEP1/2, are critical regulators of fig receptacle development. In contrast, MADS-box genes associated with B-, C-, and D-functions were markedly upregulated in the sarcocarp during early fruit development, which indicates their roles in floral organ formation within the fig flesh. Collectively, these results support a new hypothesis for the formation of the Ficus syconium (fig fruit), in which distinct MADS-box genes act in a coordinated manner across different stages of fruit development.

Domestication of F. carica began approximately 11 200–11 400 years ago (Ramadan, 2023), and most modern cultivars exhibit parthenocarpy. To investigate the genomic basis of this trait, we conducted comparative genome 

analyses of the primary cultivar ‘Brunswick’ (F. carica) and wild Ficus species, including F. erecta, F. microcarpa, and F. hispida. We identified a pronounced contraction in the RLK gene family, which is critical for pollen recognition. In particular, the contraction of s-domain RLK genes in F. carica appears to have resulted from long-term artificial domestication and seedless vegetative propagation and may be linked to the molecular mechanisms underlying F. carica parthenocarpy. This pattern, also observed in other fruit crops such as banana and citrus (Maupile´ et al., 2024), highlights the significant role of artificial selection in shaping the fruiting strategies of F. carica. 

In plants, specialized metabolites are known for their diverse biological activities and are increasingly important in drug development and the food industry due to their antioxidant, antiinflammatory, and antibacterial properties. These metabolites are essential components of the plant’s innate immune system, providing protection against pathogen invasion and environmental stress. In F. carica, a wide variety of specialized metabolites, including flavonoids, coumarins, and terpenoids, have been identified (Wang et al., 2024). The fruit (Mopuri and Islam, 2016), leaves (Ammar et al., 2015; Li et al., 2021), and latex (Lazreg-Aref et al., 2012) are the primary storage sites for these bioactive compounds. Our systematic analysis of F. carica tissues revealed that terpenoids and phenols are most abundant in the leaves, whereas flavonoids, coumarins and their derivatives, and cholines are concentrated in the roots. Fatty acids and their conjugates predominate in the stem, whereas alkaloids are abundant in both the pericarp and the stem. This 

comprehensive metabolite distribution suggests that F. carica possesses considerable nutritional and therapeutic potential. 

Anthocyanins, which contribute to red, purple, and blue pigmentation in fruits, have well-documented health benefits, including the prevention of heart disease and cancer (Lu et al., 2023). In F. carica, anthocyanin biosynthesis is regulated by key enzymes, including FcCHS, FcCHI, and FcANS. Because anthocyanin levels are a key indicator of fruit quality in F. carica, significant research has focused on understanding their regulation. The transcription of genes such as CHS, CHI, and ANS correlates with increased anthocyanin content, particularly during fruit maturation (Li et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Liang et al., 2024). Transcriptomic analysis across developmental stages and fruit tissues identified 27 DEGs related to anthocyanin biosynthesis. Correlation analysis showed that the expression patterns of FcCHS10 and FcANS closely matched the fluctuations in anthocyanin levels in the pericarp and sarcocarp, respectively, which suggests potential roles in anthocyanin biosynthesis. Previous attempts to functionally validate predicted anthocyanin biosynthesis genes in F. carica were limited by the absence of an established transformation system (Wang et al., 2024). To overcome this challenge, we established an efficient transient transformation system for F. carica fruit. Using this system, we validated the roles of FcCHS10 and FcANS in regulating anthocyanin accumulation. Our results suggest that anthocyanin accumulation in the pericarp is primarily driven by sustained FcCHS10 upregulation, whereas in the sarcocarp, it is mainly associated with increased FcANS expression, particularly during the S2 developmental stage. These results indicate that FcCHS10 and FcANS have distinct regulatory functions in anthocyanin biosynthesis across different fruit tissues. 

Psoralen, a coumarin compound, exhibits a broad range of biological activities, including efficacy against osteoporosis, tumors, viral and bacterial infections, and inflammation (Ren et al., 2020). As a key metabolite in the furanocoumarin biosynthetic pathway, psoralen has attracted considerable attention. Several key psoralen biosynthetic enzymes have been identified in members of the Apiaceae and Fabaceae families (Larbat et al., 2007; Bourgaud et al., 2014; Krieger et al., 2018). In F. carica, psoralen was initially reported as the predominant furanocoumarin in leaves (Ammar et al., 2015), with earlier studies indicating that its highest levels occurred in leaves instead of bark or wood (Conforti et al., 2012). However, our findings demonstrate that psoralen in F. carica accumulates most strongly in the roots, exceeding the levels in the leaves by 17.6-fold, the pericarp by 18.64-fold, the stem by 5.68-fold, and the sarcocarp by 62.17-fold. This discrepancy likely reflects the focus of earlier studies on aerial organs, thereby overlooking underground tissues. Consequently, in addition to the leaves, the roots of F. carica represent a previously underappreciated reservoir for psoralen production. To further elucidate psoralen biosynthesis, we performed a comprehensive genomic analysis of F. carica and identified 13 genes closely associated with the furanocoumarin pathway, including FcPT1, FcC4M, and FcMS. Integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses revealed that FcMS expression correlated strongly with the accumulation of five key metabolites associated with the furanocoumarin pathway, with the highest correlation observed for psoralen. Furthermore, A. rhizogenes-mediated transformation 

validated the critical role of FcMS in enhancing psoralen accumulation in F. carica. This represents the first functional confirmation of an enzyme that regulates psoralen biosynthesis in the Moraceae family. 

Most Ficus species are evergreen, whereas F. carica is deciduous (Gonza´ lez-Rodrı´guez and Peters, 2010; Salehi et al., 2021). Some Ficus species use leaf abscission to combat drought stress; for example, Ficus racemosa modulates leaf turgor and sheds leaves to reduce water loss and prevent xylem embolism (Yuan et al., 2024). Antioxidant enzymes such as POD, SOD, CAT, glutathione S-transferase (GST), and GSHPx are essential for mitigating drought-induced oxidative stress by scavenging excess ROS and preserving cellular integrity. For example, Ficus septica significantly upregulates POD2, POD4, SOD2, and SOD1 under drought conditions, which enhances ROS scavenging (Parveen et al., 2019). In addition, flavonoids and coumarins function as antioxidants during drought stress (Nakabayashi et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2023). In our study, F. carica showed significant upregulation of seven PODs, one GST, and one GSHPx under drought stress. Furthermore, coumarin biosynthetic genes (FcPT1, FcCYP82C4, and FcMS) and flavonoid biosynthetic genes (FcCHS, FcFLS, FcCHIL, and FcFNS) were strongly induced, which indicates that both antioxidant enzymes and specialized metabolites contribute to drought response in F. carica. 

The NAC family of transcription factors plays a central role in plant drought responses. FcJA2, a NAC member, activates ABA biosynthetic genes to enhance stress tolerance (Du et al., 2014). Our transcriptomic analysis revealed that the NAC family was the most strongly induced TF family under drought conditions in F. carica, with $F C J A2$ showing the strongest positive correlations within the regulatory network of transcription factors and structural genes. Overexpression of FcJA2 in F. carica markedly improved drought resistance, as evidenced by increased proline accumulation, enhanced activity of antioxidant enzymes, and reduced MDA and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels. Correlation analysis identified strong associations between FcJA2 and FcPP2Cs as well as FcP5CS. Multiple NAC binding elements were found in the promoters of FcPP2C5, FcP5CS, and FcJA2. Functional assays using yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) and LUC assays confirmed that FcJA2 directly targets these promoters. Clade A PP2Cs are key regulators of the ABA signaling pathway under drought stress, dephosphorylating stressrelated TFs to upregulate their expression and enhance drought tolerance (Zhao et al., 2024). In addition, PP2Cs promote the activity of antioxidant enzymes, further improving drought resistance (Zhang et al., 2022). P5CS, which is essential for proline biosynthesis, is a key osmotic regulator that mitigates ROS stress and has been linked to improved drought resistance in multiple species (Feng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024a; Longkumer et al., 2024). Integrating our Y1H and LUC findings with the observed increases in antioxidant enzyme activity, proline content, and FcPP2C5 and FcP5CS expression under drought, we propose that FcJA2 functions as a positive regulator of FcPP2C5 and FcP5CS to enhance drought tolerance. Furthermore, by binding to its own promoter, FcJA2 increases its own transcription, thereby reinforcing the expression of targeted drought-responsive genes and further enhancing drought tolerance.
