# Summary

Colour change is an important event during fruit ripening in blueberry. It is well known that miR156/SPLs act as regulatory modules mediating anthocyanin biosynthesis and ethylene plays critical roles during colour change, but the intrinsic connections between the two pathways remain poorly understood. Previously, we demonstrated that blueberry VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 affects the accumulation of anthocyanins and chlorophylls in tomato and Arabidopsis. In this study, we first showed that VcMIR156a overexpression in blueberry led to enhanced anthocyanin biosynthesis, decreased chlorophyll accumulation, and, intriguingly, concomitant elevation in the expression of ethylene biosynthesis genes and the level of the ethylene precursor ACC. 

Conversely, VcSPL12 enhanced chlorophyll accumulation and suppressed anthocyanin biosynthesis and ACC synthesis in fruits. Moreover, the treatment with ethylene substitutes and inhibitors attenuated the effects of VcMIR156a and VcSPL12 on pigment accumulation. Protein-DNA interaction assays indicated that VcSPL12 could specifically bind to the promoters and inhibit the activities of the ethylene biosynthetic genes VcACS1 and VcACO6. Collectively, our results show that VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 alters ethylene production through targeting VcACS1 and VcACO6, therefore governing fruit colour change. Additionally, VcSPL12 may directly interact with the promoter region of the chlorophyll biosynthetic gene VcDVR, thereby activating its expression. These findings established an intrinsic connection between the miR156/SPL regulatory module and ethylene pathway. 

# Introduction

Colour change is a significant and easily observable event that occurs during plant development, particularly during fruit ripening and leaf senescence. This process can greatly impact the nutritional and quality value of crops, particularly those that bear fleshy fruit. Colour change is typically determined by the coordination of chlorophyll breakdown and pigment accumulation, with anthocyanins often being the primary pigments responsible for red, blue, and purple hues (Karlova et al., 2014). Although the biosynthetic genes and regulatory factors of anthocyanins have been extensively studied during fruit colour change (Jaakola, 2013; Karppinen et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023), our understanding of the regulation of chlorophyll metabolism in fruit is still limited, with only a few well-documented transcriptional regulators (Ma et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a). The orchestration of chlorophyll breakdown and pigment accumulation to achieve colour change during fruit ripening remains largely unknown. 

MicroRNA156s (miR156s) are a highly conserved family of small RNAs in plants that post-transcriptionally regulate the 

expression of SQUAMOSA PROMOTER BINDING PROTEINLIKE (SPL) genes, guiding transcript cleavage and/or translational repression (Chen et al., 2010). As such, miR156/SPLs are considered regulatory modules that govern various processes of plant growth and development (Wang and Wang, 2015). Recently, increasing evidence has indicated that miR156/SPL modules play important roles in regulating anthocyanin accumulation in plants. For instance, in Arabidopsis, miR156-targeted SPL9 negatively regulates anthocyanin biosynthesis by inhibiting the formation of the MYB-bHLH-WD40 complex (Gou et al., 2011) and also directly suppresses the expression of DFR to control anthocyanin biosynthesis (Cui et al., 2014). Similarly, in a few fleshy fruit-bearing crops, such as Chinese sand pear and blood-fleshed peach, miR156/SPLs negatively regulate anthocyanin accumulation by suppressing the expression of PpMYB10.1 and inactivating BLOOD-PpNAC1 heterodimer, respectively (Qian et al., 2017; Su et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2015). Interestingly, published evidence suggests that miR156 is also negatively involved in the regulation of chlorophyll accumulation in Arabidopsis, tea plant, and alfalfa (Arshad et al., 2017; Fan et al., 2015; Feng et al., 2016). Recently, we reported that 

overexpression of three blueberry SPLs (VcSBP2, VcSBP12, and VcSBP13a) can promote chlorophyll accumulation in Arabidopsis (Xie et al., 2021). These findings suggest that miR156/SPLs also regulate chlorophyll metabolism. However, whether miR156/SPLs are involved in the regulation of chlorophyll accumulation in fruit remains to be fully explored. Furthermore, the coordination of anthocyanin accumulation and chlorophyll metabolism during fruit colour change is not well understood. 

Ethylene plays a critical role in fruit colour change, and its biosynthesis involves several enzyme steps, including SAM SYNTHETASE (SAMS), ACC SYNTHETASE (ACS), and ACC OXYGENASE (ACO) (Pattyn et al., 2021). Upon biosynthesis, ethylene activates downstream transcriptional regulatory cascades, triggering diverse biological processes (Pattyn et al., 2021). Several studies have focused on understanding the transcriptional regulation of the enzymatic ethyleneproducing machinery and ethylene signalling (Hu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2016, 2017, 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2020). Interestingly, some studies suggest that SPLs positively regulate the ethylene signalling pathway to control developmental programmes, including wheat spike development, cotton somatic embryogenesis and fruit ripening (Adaskaveg et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2021; Manning et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2018a). However, our understanding of the intrinsic connections between miR156/SPL-mediated regulation and the ethylene pathway remains limited. It is unclear if miR156/SPLs govern fruit colour change by affecting ethylene production and its signalling, particularly since ethylene promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis and chlorophyll breakdown in fruits while SPLs are known to negatively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis (Cui et al., 2014; Gou et al., 2011; Li et al., 2020b; Qian et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2015). Thus, it is essential to explore whether negative intrinsic links between miR156/SPLs-mediated regulation and the ethylene pathway exist in plants (Dubois et al., 2018; Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021; Ji and Wang, 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Yin et al., 2016). 

Blueberry (Vaccinium spp.) is a widely cultivated berry-bearing shrub that is economically important due to its rich anthocyanin content (Wu et al., 2006). The colour change accompanied by anthocyanin accumulation is a critical process for the quality formation of blueberry fruit, making it an ideal material for studying fruit colouration (Zifkin et al., 2012). While several transcriptional regulators and phytohormones have been documented to be implicated in regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis in blueberry fruit, knowledge regarding the regulatory network underlying the entire process of colour change is still limited (Farneti et al., 2022; Jaakola et al., 2010; Karppinen et al., 2021; Lafferty et al., 2022a,b; Wang et al., 2022b; Xie et al., 2022; Zifkin et al., 2012). In a previous study, we demonstrated that VcMIR156a can influence anthocyanin and chlorophyll accumulation through its direct target VcSPL12 in tomato and Arabidopsis (Li et al., 2020b). In this study, we showed that VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 coordinates anthocyanin accumulation and chlorophyll breakdown by regulating ethylene production in tissue culture seedlings and fruit of the blueberry. We also demonstrated that VcSPL12 represses the transcription of the ethylene biosynthetic genes VcACS1 and VcACO6 by binding to their promoters and activates the expression of the chlorophyll biosynthetic gene VcDVR, enhancing chlorophyll accumulation. These findings provided new insights into the connections between the ethylene biosynthetic pathway and miR156/SPL-mediated regulations in plants. 

# Results

# VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 affects the accumulation of chlorophylls and anthocyanins in blueberry

To further investigate the functional roles of VcMIR156a in blueberry, we generated transgenic blueberry lines overexpressing VcMIR156a, and three independent lines (OX-1, OX-3, and OX-6) were selected for further study (Figure S1a,b). The transgenic lines showed an obvious red shade with light green on seedlings clonally propagated by tissue culture (referred to as tissue culture seedlings hereafter), especially tender shoots (Figure 1a and Figure S1a). Subsequently, we measured the contents of chlorophylls and anthocyanins using spectrophotometry. Our results showed that the chlorophyll contents in transgenic lines were decreased to $6 1 \% - 7 5 \%$ of WT, whereas VcMIR156a overexpression significantly elevated the anthocyanin levels, especially in $O X - 1$ and $O X - 3$ with more than a 2-fold change compared to WT (Figure 1b,c). These results indicated that VcMIR156a is involved in regulating the accumulation of anthocyanins and chlorophylls in blueberry. 

To further understand the effects of VcMIR156a on the accumulation of pigments, we investigated the transcriptomic and metabolomic differences between the VcMIR156OX line and WT using tender stems of tissue culture seedlings as materials. Consequently, we identified 1513 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and 225 differentially expressed metabolites (DEMs) (Figure S1c,d). Chlorophylls are synthesized from glutamate through the tetrapyrrole pathway and assembled into the photosynthetic protein complex in chloroplasts. Our transcriptome data identified 36 chlorophyll-associated genes, including VcDVR and VcPORAs, which encode enzymes involved in chlorophyll biosynthesis and were down-regulated in the VcMIR156OX line (Figure 1d and Dataset S1). In addition, VcMIR156a overexpression suppressed the expression of the genes involved in chlorophyll-protein complex formation and chloroplast biogenesis, such as VcLPA3 and VcCIA2 (Figure 1d). qRT-PCR confirmed the down-regulation of these genes (VcDVR, VcPORA, VcLHCB, VcLPA3 and VcCIA2) in VcMIR156OX lines (Figure 1e). These results suggested that VcMIR156a negatively regulates chlorophyll accumulation in blueberry by suppressing chlorophyll biosynthesis, chlorophyll-protein complex formation, and chloroplast biogenesis. 

Anthocyanins are known to be derived from phenylpropanoid and synthesized via the anthocyanin branch of the phenylpropanoid pathway. Our transcriptomic data showed that nine types of enzyme genes were detected (Figure 1f and Dataset S1). A significant number of them were found to be transcriptionally upregulated by the overexpression of VcMIR156a, including VcPALs, Vc4CLs, VcCHSs, VcF3Hs and VcUFGTs. Interestingly, it was also observed that VcMIR156a promoted the transcript accumulation of VcMYBA (Figure S1e), a positive regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis. Moreover, five genes were selected for qRT-PCR validation, and similar expression patterns were observed for all of them (Figure 1g). Consistent with the transcriptomic data, metabolomic data showed that the four upstream intermediates of the phenylpropanoid pathway were significantly increased in the VcMIR156OX line, including cinnamate, p-Coumara, naringenin chalcone and narigenin (Figure 1f), which are metabolized by the enzymes PAL, PTAL, CHS and CHI, respectively. # The VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 module regulates colour change via affecting ethylene production pathway

Our transcriptomic analysis revealed a substantial number of differentially expressed genes involved in plant hormone biosynthesis and signalling pathways, with ethylene-associated genes representing the largest proportion (almost $30 \%$ ) (Figure S3a and Dataset S1). Further observation demonstrated that all ethylene biosynthetic genes, including seven VcSAMSs, four VcACSs, and 

four VcACOs, were upregulated by VcMIR156a overexpression, with the exception of G30255.t1 (VcSAMS) and CUFF.8159 (VcACO6) (Figure 3a and Figure S3b,c), indicating that VcMIR156a has positive effects on the expressions of ethylene biosynthetic genes. In contrast, ethylene receptors and their downstream genes, such as VcETRs, VcCTRs, VcEIN2s and VcEIN3s, remained unchanged or showed a slight transcriptional downregulation in the VcMIR156OX line (Figure 3a). Subsequently, seven genes were selected for qRT-PCR analysis. As indicated in Figure 3b, most of them showed a similar expression pattern to the transcriptomic data. These results suggested that VcMIR156a may primarily affect ethylene production rather than its signalling. Moreover, our metabolomic data indicated that the contents of all three ethylene precursors, L-methionine, S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), and 1-aminocyclopropane-1- carboxylic acid (ACC), were significantly increased in the VcMIR156OX line (Figure 3c). These results suggested that VcMIR156a may be involved in the regulation of ethylene production by activating ethylene biosynthetic genes.

# VcSPL12 regulates colour change of blueberry fruit through affecting ethylene production pathway

To investigate whether VcSPL12 participates in the regulation of colour change during fruit growth and ripening, gene transient expression and virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) were conducted, as described in the Method section. Two fragments of VcSPL12 were chosen for VIGS and named VIGS-VcSPL12-1 (V12- 1) and VIGS-VcSPL12-2 (V12-2) (Figure 4a). Subsequently, red colouration, representing anthocyanin accumulation, was observed in V12-1 and V12-2 fruits around the infiltration site, while no visible colour change appeared in the transiently VcSPL12-overexpressing fruits (VcSPL12OX-trs) and CK (Figure 4b and Figure S4a). qRT-PCR analysis confirmed that VcSPL12 was indeed transiently overexpressed or suppressed in these infiltrated 

blueberry fruits (Figure S4b). Furthermore, the contents of chlorophylls and anthocyanins were measured spectrophotometrically. As shown in Figure 4c, the total chlorophyll content decreased to $73 \%$ and $90 \%$ of CK in V12-1 and V12-2 fruits, respectively, while an increased chlorophyll level (1.42-folds) was observed in VcSPL12OX-trs fruits. Conversely, the anthocyanin content was significantly increased by 2.8 and 12.0 folds in V12-1 and V12-2, respectively, compared to CK, while no significant difference was observed in anthocyanin accumulation between CK and VcSPL12OX-trs fruits (Figure 4d). Consistent with the accumulation pattern of anthocyanins, qRT-PCR analysis indicated that the anthocyanin biosynthetic genes VcCHS, VcF3H, and VcUFGT were transcriptionally elevated in V12-1 and V12-2 and depressed in VcSPL12OX-trs (Figure 4e). Additionally, two positive regulators of anthocyanin biosynthesis, VcMYBA and VcMYBPA1, were also transcriptionally promoted in V12-1 and V12-2, while the expression of VcMYBPA1 was significantly decreased in VcSPL12OX-trs (Figure S4c). These results suggested that VcSPL12 can indeed regulate the colour change of blueberry fruit. 

Next, to investigate if VcSPL12 affects ethylene production in blueberry fruit, we conducted HPLC-MS/MS analysis to determine the content of the ethylene precursor ACC. As described in the Methods section, we used transient VcSPL12 overexpression and silencing approaches to assess its effect on the ACC level. As shown in Figure 4f and Figure S4d–g, the ACC content in fruit was decreased to $4 4 \%$ of CK by the transient VcSPL12 overexpression, whereas a 2.33-fold increase was observed for the ACC level in transiently VcSPL12-silencing fruits as compared to CK. These results demonstrated that VcSPL12 inhibits ethylene production pathway in blueberry fruit. 

To evaluate the effect of VcSPL12-mediated regulation of ethylene production on fruit colour change, VcSPL12OX-trs as well as V12-1 and V12-2 fruits were growing for 11 days after infiltration and then subjected to ethephon and 1-MCP treatment for 3 days (Figure 4g). Under mock condition (CONTROL), the total chlorophyll content in V12-1 and V12-2 fruits was decreased to $6 3 \%$ of CK, while the anthocyanin content was increased by 2.63 and 4.66 times compared to CK (Figure 4h,i and Figure S4h, i), respectively. The opposite pattern was observed for the accumulation of anthocyanins and chlorophylls in VcSPL12OXtrs (Figure 4h,i and Figure S4h,i). When subjected to the 1-MCP treatment, the contents of chlorophylls and anthocyanins in both VcSPL12OX-trs and VcSPL12-silencing fruits (V12-1 and V12-2) were similar to the level of CK under the 1-MCP treatment (Figure 4h,i and Figure S4h,i). Conversely, under the ETH - treatment the difference between CK and VcSPL12OX-trs or VcSPL12-silencing fruits was narrowed down in the accumulation of anthocyanins and chlorophylls compared to the ones under mock conditions (Figure 4h,i and Figure S4h,i). These results implied that the effects of VcSPL12 on fruit colour change may be simulated by the pharmacological manipulation of the ethylene level. 

# VcSPL12 negatively regulates ethylene production through directly targeting VcACO6 and VcACS1

To investigate whether VcSPL12 directly regulates ethylene biosynthesis, the yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay was used to determine its ability to bind to the promoters of eight ethylenerelated genes. SPL proteins have been shown to preferentially bind to the consensus sequence TNCGTACAA, with GTAC as its essential core (Chen et al., 2010), which was searched for in the 

promoter regions of the eight genes (Figure S5a). Promoter fragments containing the consensus sequence were cloned and constructed into the pLacZi vector as prey, while VcSPL12 was cloned and introduced into the pB42AD vector as bait. The Y1H assay showed that VcSPL12 was able to physically interact with the promoters of VcACO6 and VcACS1, as blue colonies were observed when these promoters were used as preys with VcSPL12 as bait (Figure S5b). However, no blue colonies were observed for the promoter fragments of the other six genes and negative controls (Figure S5b). These results suggest that VcSPL12 may specifically bind to the promoters of VcACO6 and VcACS1. Notably, the promoter fragments of VcACS1 and VcACO6 contained three and one putative binding sites, respectively (Figure S5a). To further investigate the contribution of each putative binding site to the physical interaction, the three putative binding sites of the cloned promoter fragment of VcACS1 were individually deleted or altogether designated as pVcACS1-m1/- m2/-m3/-m4 (Figure 5a). The Y1H assay indicated that blue colonies could still be observed when VcSPL12 acted as bait and pVcACS1-m1/-m2/-m3 were used as preys, while no blue colonies were shown when the three binding sites were simultaneously deleted (Figure 5b). In addition, the putative binding site of pVcACO6 was also deleted and named pVcACO6-m1 (Figure 5a), and no blue colonies were observed when pVcACO6-m1 was used as prey and VcSPL12 as bait (Figure 5b). These results indicated that the binding sites are likely necessary for the binding of VcSPL12. 

Furthermore, the dual-luciferase reporter system was used to validate the binding of VcSPL12 to the promoter fragments in Nicotiana benthamiana. Specifically, the above promoter fragments were fused to the LUCIFERASE reporter gene of pGREENII0800, including pVcACS1::LUC, pVcACS1-m1::LUC, pVcACS1-m2::LUC, pVcACS1-m3::LUC, pVcACS1-m4::LUC, pVcACO6::LUC and pVcACO6-m1::LUC, as well as pVcERF19-2:: LUC, while VcSPL12 was introduced into the vector pCAM-BIA2300 as an effector (pLac::VcSPL12). We co-infiltrated different combinations of reporters and effectors into tobacco leaves and measured the ratio of firefly luciferase activity to Renilla luciferase activity (LUC/REN). The results showed that VcSPL12 could bind to the promoters of VcACS1 and VcACO6 and inhibit their transcriptional activities in vivo (Figure 5c,d). No significant difference was observed between the control and the combination of pVcERF19-2 and pLac::VcSPL12 (Figure S5c,d). When all the putative binding sites were simultaneously deleted, the ratio of LUC/REN reached the level of the control (Figure 5d), indicating the disappearance of VcSPL12 binding. These results implied that the three binding sites are critical for VcSPL12 binding with different affinity. Similarly, the ratio of LUC/REN was shifted from 0.79 to 1.03 (a control level) when the putative binding site for pVcACO6 was deleted (Figure 5d), suggesting its decisive role for VcSPL12 binding. 

Next, we investigated whether and how VcSPL12 regulates the expressions of VcACS1 and VcACO6 in blueberry fruit using transient overexpression and VIGS approaches. As shown in Figure 5e, both VcACO6 and VcACS1 were transcriptionally inhibited in fruit transiently overexpressing VcSPL12 (VcSPL12OXtrs), while significantly enhanced expressions were observed in two types of transiently VcSPL12-silenced fruits (V12-1 and V12- 2). The expression correlation coefficient (r) values between VcSPL12 and VcACS1 as well as VcSPL12 and VcACO6 reached 0.819548 and 0.789612, respectively (Figure S6), indicating a strong negative correlation between their expressions. # VcSPL12 positively regulates chlorophyll accumulation through directly targeting the chlorophyll biosynthetic gene VcDVR

Since VcSPL12 promotes chlorophyll accumulation in blueberry, we next explored whether VcSPL12 can directly target 

chlorophyll-associated genes. Three representative genes related to chlorophyll biosynthesis (VcDVR, VcPORA, and VcHEMH) were chosen to test the binding ability of VcSPL12 using the Y1H assay. Subsequently, two promoter fragments containing multiple consensus sequences were cloned for VcDVR (pVcDVR-1 and pVcDVR-2), one for VcPORA (pVcPORA), and one for VcHEMH (pVcHEMH), which were then introduced into the vector pLacZi as preys. pB42AD-VcSPL12 acted as bait. Furthermore, pVcDVR-1 was fused to the LUCIFERASE gene (pVcDVR-1::LUC) as a reporter, while pLac::VcSPL12 located in pCAMBIA2300 acted as an effector. As shown in Figure 6c,d, the ratio of LUC/REN for pVcDVR-1 was significantly increased. The ratio value was up to 1.14 for pVcDVR-1, while the control showed a ratio value of 1 (Figure 6d). might bind to the VcDVR promoter to promote its transcriptional activity. 

Next, we investigated if the expression of VcDVR can be modulated by VcSPL12 in fruit using transient overexpression and VIGS approaches. As shown in Figure 6e, the expression of VcDVR was upregulated by 1.67-fold in fruit transiently overexpressing VcSPL12 as compared to WT. # Discussion

Ethylene plays imperative roles in diverse developmental programmes from modulating organ growth to initiating fruit ripening (Dubois et al., 2018; Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021). Meanwhile, miR156/SPLs have been recognized as key regulatory hubs to control a wide variety of developmental processes (Wang and Wang, 2015). However, the connections between the ethylene pathway and miR156/SPL-mediated regulation are very poorly understood. To date, only a few studies have reported that SPLs seemingly regulate developmental programmes through the ethylene pathway, including somatic embryogenesis in cotton (Wang et al., 2018a), spike development in wheat (Cao et al., 2021), and fruit ripening in tomato (Adaskaveg et al., 2021; Manning et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2020). In the present study, we documented that VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 can regulate ethylene production to control colour change in blueberry. Our transcriptomic and metabolomic data showed that VcMIR156a overexpression in tissue culture seedlings of blueberry not only elevated the expressions of the ethylene biosynthetic genes (VcSAMSs, VcACSs, and VcACOs) but also enhanced the levels of the ethylene intermediates SAMS and ACC (Figure 3a–c). Conversely, VcSPL12 decreased the expressions of the ethylene biosynthetic genes VcSAMS1 and VcACS1 (Figure 3d). Furthermore, the transient overexpression of VcSPL12 repressed not only the expressions of VcACS1 and VcACO6 but also the ACC level, whereas the opposite alteration was observed when VcSPL12 was silenced (Figures 4f and 5e). Although the content of ACC closely represents ethylene production, it has been demonstrated that ACC can act as a signalling molecule independent of the ethylene pathway (Li et al., 2020a). In the present study, the treatments of two ethylene substitutes (ETH and ACC) led to similar effects on the colour change of tissue culture seedlings (Figure 3e–h), implying that the ACC production controlled by VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 might, at least partially, inflow towards ethylene production. It has been well known that ethylene can promote colour change, usually accompanied by massive anthocyanin accumulation and chlorophyll breakdown (Chen et al., 2022; Jaakola, 2013; Shemer et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2022a). Here, we showed that the effects of VcMIR156a and VcSPL12 on the accumulations of anthocyanins and chlorophylls were clearly attenuated by ethylene substitutes and inhibitors (Figures 3e–h and 4g–i), which supports a scenario that VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 may act as upstream players of the ethylene pathway to control colour change. Generally, the transcriptional regulation of the rate-limiting enzymes ACS 

and ACO is considered a key way to control ethylene production (Li et al., 2016; Pattyn et al., 2021). To date, a number of transcriptional regulators have been functionally elucidated to regulate the expressions of ACSs and/or ACOs in plants (Hu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2016, 2017, 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2020). Our protein-DNA interaction assays showed that VcSPL12 physically bound to the promoter regions of VcACO6 and VcACS1 and inhibited their activities (Figure 5a–d), while their expressions in fruits were altered by VcSPL12 accordingly (Figure 5e). Therefore, we proposed that SPLs may negatively regulate ethylene production by directly targeting the ethylene biosynthetic genes ACO and ACS, thereby affecting colour change. 

Several studies have shown that SPLs promote ethylene production and signalling. For instance, TaSPL14 was found to be involved in the regulation of wheat spike development by directly regulating the expressions of ethylene-responsive genes such as TaEIL1, TaRAP2.11, and TaERF1 (Cao et al., 2021). In tomato, silencing of CNR-SPL led to a loss of ethylene burst at the fruit ripening stage, with the expressions of ethylene biosynthetic genes being inhibited or delayed (Adaskaveg et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022). It is well known that ethylene promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis and chlorophyll breakdown (Chen et al., 2022; Dubois et al., 2018; Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021; Wang et al., 2022a). Conversely, SPLs are generally considered negative regulators of anthocyanin biosynthesis. However, some studies have indicated that overexpression of certain SPL family members can enhance anthocyanin accumulation in different plant species, such as MdSPL2-like and MdSPL33 in apple callus and fruit (Yang et al., 2019) and SmSPL7 in Salvia miltiorrhiza (Chen et al., 2021). Our data demonstrated that VcSPL12 represses anthocyanin biosynthesis and chlorophyll breakdown (Figures 2c,d and 4c,d), which is opposite to the roles of ethylene in colour change. It is not surprising since SPL genes show functional diversification across both evolutionary clades and species through a combination of differences in regulation, biochemical function, and/or downstream targets (Preston and Hileman, 2013; Xu et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2023). Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that VcSPL12 exerts a negative effect on ethylene production to meet the demands of colour change. 

Blueberry fruit exhibits a dark green colour during fruit growing stages, followed by de-greening at the onset of ripening, and then achieves its desirable colour with successive anthocyanin accumulation (Zifkin et al., 2012). Several studies have suggested that blueberry fruit possibly shows climacteric features, implying an important role of ethylene in blueberry fruit colouration (Colle et al., 2019; Farneti et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2018b, 2022b; Yan et al., 2020). In the present study, we provided evidence to support the scenario that VcSPL12 negatively regulates ethylene production to affect blueberry fruit coloration by directly inactivating the expressions of VcACO6 and VcACS1. Ethylene production is a complex reaction catalysed by multiple ACSs and ACOs in fruit (Ji and Wang, 2021). Different enzyme isoforms of ACSs and ACOs constitute distinct biosynthetic systems to enable levels of ethylene production. Generally, system I contributes to satisfying the needs of fruit growth, whereas system II can autocatalytically burst ethylene production to activate fruit ripening (Fenn and Giovannoni, 2021; Ji and Wang, 2021). Our transcriptomic data allowed four VcACOs and VcACSs to identified in blueberry (Figure 3a). Among the four VcACOs, VcACO6 exhibited a maximal expression level in 

blueberry based on the DCt value of qRT-PCR (Dataset S2). Further observation showed that VcACO6 remained at a fairly low level during fruit growth and remarkably increased at the onset of fruit maturation until ripening (Figure 5f). These results are consistent with the transcriptomic data from Gupta et al. (2015) that CUFF.8159 (ACO6) showed the maximal FPKM value among seven ACOs in blueberry fruit, and its transcripts were dramatically increased in pink and ripe fruits. Coincidentally, it was reported that an increase in ethylene production appeared at the onset of blueberry fruit ripening (Wang et al., 2022b). Thus, we assumed that VcACO6 might act as a major family member for ethylene burst to satisfy the needs of blueberry fruit ripening. Noticeably, VcSPL12 and VcACO6 showed reverse expression patterns during fruit growth and ripening (Figure 5f). Our previous study suggested that VcMIR156a is a major source of mature miR156 for VcSPL12 cleavage at the onset of fruit ripening (Li et al., 2020b). Since VcSPL12 can directly repress the expression of VcACO6 (Figure 5), we speculated that the VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 module and VcACO6 might form a cascade to govern ethylene production, therefore satisfying the needs of fruit colouration. 

A few studies showed that transcriptional regulators such as MYBA and MYBPA1 might be positively involved in the regulation of anthocyanin accumulation by directly targeting anthocyanin biosynthetic genes in blueberry (Karppinen et al., 2021; Lafferty et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2020b; Plunkett et al., 2018). Noticeably, our data showed that VcMIR156a promoted the transcript accumulation of VcMYBA in blueberry seedling (Figure S1e), while VcSPL12 negatively affected the expressions of VcMYBA and VcMYBPA1 in fruit (Figure S4c), indicating a connection between VcSPL12 and these two VcMYBs. Previously, we reported that VcSPL12 can physically interact with VcMYBPA1 but not VcMYBA (Li et al., 2020b). These results suggested that the VcMIR156a/VcSPL12-meidated regulatory mechanisms might be more complicated during blueberry fruit colour change. Since VcMIR156a/VcSPL12 can regulate ethylene production, it will be important to explore the connections between the well-known regulators of pigment change (such as VcMYBPA1 and VcMYBA) and the ethylene pathway in the future. 

Based on these findings, we proposed that during fruit growing stages, a high abundance of VcSPL12 depresses ethylene production and maintains ethylene at a basal level, leading to chlorophyll accumulation and inhibition of anthocyanin biosynthesis. At the initiation of fruit ripening, VcMIR156a-derived miR156 cleaves the transcripts of VcSPL12, leading to the production of more ethylene, which triggers chlorophyll breakdown and anthocyanin biosynthesis (Figure 7). These findings greatly enhance our understanding of the functional roles of the miR156/SPLs and their regulatory mechanisms in plants, particularly in fruit coloration in blueberry. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Eight-year-old blueberry trees (Vaccinium corymbosum, cv. ‘Northland’ and ‘Bluetta’) were grown at the experimental station at Jilin University (Changchun, China). Fruits at six different developmental stages were randomly harvested from at least six different blueberry trees as previously described (Zifkin et al., 2012), including green pad (F1S), green cup I (F2S), green cup II (F3S), light green (FWS), pink (FPS) and blue (FBS) fruits. Tissue culture seedlings of transgenic blueberry lines and untransformed 

control (WT) were grown in WPM medium (PhytoTech) with $2 \%$ sucrose, $0 . 7 \%$ agar, 1 mL/L vitamins, $2 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ Zeatin and $\mathsf { p H } ~ 5 . 2$ or substrate (moss peat:vermiculite $= 3 : 1$ ) under long-day conditions $1 6 ~ \mathsf { h }$ light, $2 3 ^ { \circ } C / 8 \textsf { h }$ h dark, $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ). 

Arabidopsis and tobacco (N. benthamiana) plants were grown in growth chambers under long-day conditions (16 h light/8 h dark) at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with $7 0 \% - 8 0 \%$ relative humidity. 

# Generation of transgenic blueberry and Arabidopsis

The genomic fragment of VcMIR156a (447 bp) and the fulllength coding sequence (CDS) of VcSPL12 (1377 bp) were amplified using gene-specific primers (Table S1) and cloned into pBI121 vector. The full-length CDSs of VcACS1 (1467 bp) and VcACO6 (960 bp) were also cloned and introduced into the pDONR207 vector, which were subsequently recombined into the destination vector pEarlyGate101. All binary vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (GV3101) for plant transformation. 

Blueberry transformation for VcMIR156a and VcSPL12 was conducted using Agrobacterium-mediated leaf disc infection as described previously (Song, 2015). Arabidopsis transformation for VcACS1 and VcACO6 was performed using Agrobacteriummediated floral dipping method as described previously (Zhang et al., 2006). Transgenic blueberry lines and transgenic Arabidopsis lines were confirmed using PCR approach. 

# Transcriptome and metabolome analysis

The tender stems of the VcMIR156a-overexpressing transgenic blueberry line and untransformed control were collected, frozen in liquid nitrogen, and used for transcriptome sequencing and wide-targeted metabolome analysis. 

RNA-Seq libraries were prepared using the NEBNext UltraTM RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB) following the manufacturer’s instructions and sequenced on an Illumina highthroughput platform. Paired-end reads were generated and mapped to the blueberry reference genome (Gupta et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2019) using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2019). The threshold for significant differential expression was set at $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 1$ and $| \mathsf { l o g } _ { 2 } ( \mathsf { F o } | \mathsf { d c h a n g e } ) | \geq 1$ . The data used for heatmap generation is shown in Dataset S1. The data that support the findings of this study are openly available in NCBI, reference number PRJNA956817. 

Sample preparation for metabolome analysis was conducted as described previously (Chen et al., 2013). The filtered supernatant was analysed using Ultra Performance Liquid Chromatography and Tandem Mass Spectrometry (UPLC-MS/MS). The screening criteria used were |Foldchange| $\geq 2$ , $P$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ and $\mathsf { V I P } > 1$ . 

# RNA isolation and qRT-PCR analysis

Total RNAs were extracted from tissue culture seedlings, fruits at different developmental stages, transiently VcSPL12- overexpressing and silencing fruits, and Arabidopsis leaves. qRT-PCR was performed using a CFX ConnectTM Real-Time System (Bio-Rad, Singapore) and RealStar Green Fast Mixture (with ROX) (GenStar, China), using VcGAPDH or AtACTIN8 as internal references. The data were analysed using the software BioRadCFX Manager.

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The full-length coding sequence of VcSPL12 was cloned and inserted into the pCAMBIA2300 vector (pCAMBIA2300-VcSPL12) 

and then transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Meanwhile, the promoter fragments were inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector as reporters and transformed into GV3101 with the pSoup helper plasmid. A dual-luciferase reporter assay was performed as described previously (Li et al., 2014). Briefly, Agrobacterium containing the pCAM-BIA2300 empty vector, or pCAMBIA2300-VcSPL12 was mixed with Agrobacterium containing each of the reporter vectors and then co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. The ratio of transactivation activities of firefly luciferase and Renilla luciferase was assessed using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega). 

# Virus-mediated gene silencing (VIGS) and transient expression in fruit

Virus-mediated gene silencing was performed as described by Liu et al. (2002). Two specific fragments of VcSPL12 mRNA were selected and named SPL12-1 (400 bp) and SPL12-2 (342 bp). The two fragments were cloned and introduced into the pTRV2 vector, and then transformed into GV3101. Agrobacterium containing the pTRV1 vector acted as the assistant, which was mixed with Agrobacterium harbouring pTRV2, pTRV2-SPL12-1, or pTRV2-SPL12-2, respectively. Mixtures were infiltrated into one side of on-tree fruit at the green cup I stage (F2S) as described previously (Karppinen et al., 2021). Meanwhile, VcSPL12 was 

introduced into pBI121 vector and transformed into GV3101 for transient expression in fruit, which was also infiltrated into ontree fruits at the F2S stage. Infiltrated fruits were grown for another 2 weeks under natural conditions, and peels around infiltration site were collected for further study. 

To investigate the effect of VcSPL12 on fruit colour change by pharmacological manipulation of ethylene levels, infiltrated fruits were grown for another 11 days under natural conditions, and then collected and sealed in three transparent plastic boxes, which were subjected to the treatment of 1-MCP and ethephon for 3 days, according to the method as described previously (Li et al., 2022). Peels around infiltrated site were collected for measurements of pigment contents. 

# Measurement of 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) content

HPLC-MS/MS analysis was performed to determine the ACC content in fruit peels. Peels from control (CK), transiently VcSPL12-overexpressing, and silencing fruits were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen. ACC extraction was carried out according to the method described previously (Seo et al., 2007). ACC standard products with different concentrations (0.5, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50 and $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { n g / m L } ) }$ were utilized to create a standard curve. 

# Treatment of ethylene substitutes and inhibitors

Two-month-old tissue culture seedlings of transgenic blueberry and an untransformed control were trimmed to equal length. The trimmed tissue culture seedlings were treated with $2 0 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ ethephon, $1 5 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ ACC, $4 0 ~ { \mu \ M } ~ \mathsf { C o C l } _ { 2 }$ , and $1 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { L } / \mathsf { L }$ 1-MCP. The treated tissue culture seedlings were grown for 5 weeks under long-day conditions ( $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light, $2 3 \ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / 8$ h dark, $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ . The basal part of tissue culture seedlings was collected to measure the contents of chlorophylls and anthocyanins. 

# Measurement of chlorophyll and anthocyanin contents

The contents of anthocyanins and chlorophylls were measured using spectrophotometry in tissue culture seedlings of untransformed control and transgenic blueberry lines overexpressing VcMIR156a and VcSPL12. The peels around the infiltrated site were collected from transiently VcSPL12-overexpressing and silencing fruits to investigate the change of pigments. 

Anthocyanins were extracted according to the method described previously (Gou et al., 2011). The absorbance values at 530, 620, and 650 nm were measured using a Bio-Rad spectrophotometer. Chlorophylls were extracted according to the method described previously (Lichtenthaler and Wellburn, 1983). The absorbance values at 645 and 663 nm were measured using a Bio-Rad spectrophotometer. The contents of anthocyanins and chlorophylls were calculated accordingly. 

# Experimental replicates and statistical significance

All the experiments were performed in at least three biological replicates, and three technical replicates for each biological replicate were conducted. A one-way ANOVA was applied to determine the significant difference. All the primers used are listed in Table S1.
