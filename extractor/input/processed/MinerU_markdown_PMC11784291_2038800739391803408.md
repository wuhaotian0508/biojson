# ABSTRACT

Proper mitochondrial function is crucial to plant growth and development. Inhibition of mitochondrial translation leads to mitochondrial proteotoxic stress, which triggers a protective transcriptional response that regulates nuclear gene expression, commonly referred to as the mitochondrial unfolded protein response (UPRmt). Although the UPRmt has been extensively studied in yeast and mammals, very little is known about the UPRmt in plants. Here, we show that mitochondrial translational stress inhibits plant growth and development by inducing jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis and signaling. The inhibitory effect of mitochondrial translational stress on plant growth was alleviated in the JA-signaling-defective mutants coi1-2, myc2, and myc234. Genetic analysis indicated that Arabidopsis mitochondrial ribosomal protein L1 (MRPL1), a key factor in the UPRmt, regulates plant growth in a CORONATINE-INSENSITIVE 1 (COI1)-dependent manner. Moreover, under mitochondrial translational stress, MYC2 shows direct binding to G boxes in the ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR 109 (ERF109) promoter. The induction of ERF109 expression enhances hydrogen peroxide production, which acts as a feedback loop to inhibit root growth. In addition, mutation of MRPL1 increases JA accumulation, reduces plant growth, and enhances biotic stress resistance. Overall, our findings reveal that JA plays an important role in mediating retrograde signaling under mitochondrial translational stress to balance plant growth and defense. 

Key words: mitochondrial translational stress, mitochondrial retrograde signaling, mitochondrial unfolded protein response, jasmonic acid, ERF109, hydrogen peroxide

# INTRODUCTION

As sessile organisms, plants constantly encounter adverse environmental conditions such as drought, extreme temperatures, nutrient deficiencies, and pathogen attacks. These stresses can strongly limit plant growth and development, causing substantial yield and economic losses in agriculture (Leisner et al., 2023). Mitochondria are not only the targets of various stresses but also crucial regulators of redox and metabolic homeostasis during plant responses to environmental perturbations (Taylor et al., 2002; Vanlerberghe, 2013). 

Under stress conditions, mitochondria release certain compounds that signal the nucleus to steer the expression of 

stress-responsive genes, enabling the appropriate assembly and function of mitochondrial proteins (Woodson and Chory, 2008). This communication between the mitochondrial and nuclear transcriptional machineries, commonly referred to as mitochondrial retrograde signaling, contributes to the maintenance of organellar and cellular function (Kerchev et al., 2014; Law et al., 2014; Ng et al., 2014). One of the retrograde signaling responses that maintains mitochondrial protein homeostasis is the mitochondrial unfolded protein response

(UPRmt), which can be triggered by mitochondrial proteotoxic stresses such as an imbalance between nuclear and mitochondrial proteins (Houtkooper et al., 2013), dysfunction of mitochondrial membrane potential (Rolland et al., 2019), mutations in the mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) (Lin et al., 2016), impairment of the protein quality control system (mtPQC) (Quiro´ s et al., 2016), and excessive levels of unassembled or unfolded proteins within mitochondria (Zhao et al., 2002). The UPRmt is a protective response that promotes the expression of nuclear genes to repair the impaired mitochondria and maintains proteostasis by fine-tuning the expression of mitochondrial chaperones and proteases in the mtPQC network (Andreux et al., 2013). 

Research on mitochondrial retrograde signaling in plants has centered principally on chemical and genetic perturbations of mitochondrial function (Kerchev et al., 2014; Kacprzak et al., 2020). It has been reported that the UPRmt in Arabidopsis thaliana can be activated by doxycycline (Dox), which inhibits mitochondrial translation, or by the mutation of mitochondrial ribosomal protein L1 (MRPL1). Activation of the UPRmt by chemical or genetic interference triggers a transient oxidative burst, leading to the activation of mitogen-activated protein kinases and hormonal signaling to restore mitochondrial proteostasis (Wang and Auwerx, 2017). In addition, ALTERNATIVE OX-IDASE 1A (AOX1a), an alternative mitochondrial terminal oxidase, is induced by disruption of the mitochondrial electron transport chain or by the tricarboxylic acid cycle, making AOX1a an important marker for the mitochondrial retrograde response (Dojcinovic et al., 2005; Zarkovic et al., 2005; Rhoads and Subbaiah, 2007; Giraud et al., 2009; Millar et al., 2011; Wang and Auwerx, 2017). Several factors that mediate plant mitochondrial retrograde regulation have been identified to date, including ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE 4 (ABI4) (Giraud et al., 2009), CYCLIN-DEPENDENT KINASE E1 (CDKE1) (Ng et al., 2013b), UDP-GLUCOSYLTRANSFERASE 74E2 (UGT74E2) (Kerchev et al., 2014), WRKY40 (Van Aken et al., 2013), NO APICAL MERI-STEM/ARABIDOPSIS TRANSCRIPTION ACTIVATION FACTOR/ CUP-SHAPED COTYLEDON 13 (ANAC013) (De Clercq et al., 2013), and ANAC017 (Ng et al., 2013a; Van Aken et al., 2016; Meng et al., 2019; Kacprzak et al., 2020; He et al., 2022). 

Increasing evidence indicates that mitochondrial retrograde signals interact with phytohormones to regulate plant growth and development (Berkowitz et al., 2016). Changes in mitochondrial function affect the activity of several phytohormone pathways and the expression of nucleus-encoded mitochondrial proteins (Berkowitz et al., 2016; Welchen et al., 2021). In Arabidopsis, loss-of-function mutation of mtHSC70-1 was reported to induce mitochondrial retrograde signaling, which inhibited auxin biosynthesis and transport (Li et al., 2021). Moreover, 3-(2-furyl) acrylic acid and antimycin A (AA), which can activate mitochondrial retrograde signaling, have also been found to negatively affect the auxin-signaling machinery (Kerchev et al., 2014). Perturbation of mitochondrial proteostasis activates the plant UPRmt, which causes constitutive activation of ethylene responses in etiolated Arabidopsis seedlings, leading to growth inhibition. Ethylene treatment induces the expression of AOX genes and genes encoding mitochondrial ribosomal proteins, which helps to alleviate mitochondrial dysfunction (Wang and Auwerx, 2017; Zhu et al., 2018). In addition, a previous study showed that ethylene-

responsive genes were induced by the UPRmt but that expression of these genes was unaffected by mutation of ANAC017, indicating that ethylene signaling is potentially ANAC017 independent in mitochondrial retrograde signaling (Kacprzak et al., 2020). A recent study reported that ANAC017 is a master regulator of mitochondrial stress, and several plant hormones, especially auxin and ethylene, are known to contribute to regulation of the mitochondrial retrograde response (He et al., 2022). Despite the progress made in understanding plant mitochondrial retrograde signaling, the mechanisms through which phytohormones participate in the UPRmt remain largely unknown. 

In this study, we report that JA participates in plant growth repression under mitochondrial translational stress through the MYC2-mediated activation of ERF109. Hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ production stimulated by ERF109 is required for the inhibition of plant growth under translational stress. Compared with previously identified mitochondrial retrograde signals, which alleviate oxidative stress (by regulating AOX1a) or maintain proteostasis (by regulating mtHSP70s), JA mediates the retrograde response in a COI1-dependent manner under mitochondrial translational stress, which contributes to the repression of plant growth and activates biotic stress resistance, helping plants to adapt to varying environments. 

# RESULTS

# Mitochondrial translational stress activates JA signaling

Dox, an inhibitor of mitochondrial translation, induces mitochondrial proteotoxic stress to reshape animal and plant development by modulating mitokines and phytohormones, respectively (Houtkooper et al., 2013; Moullan et al., 2015; Wang and Auwerx, 2017). Consistent with the results of a previous study (Wang and Auwerx, 2017), activity of mitochondrial complexes I and IV and the supercomplex $( 1 _ { 1 } + | 1 | _ { 2 } )$ was reduced in Dox-treated plants compared with controls (Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B). We performed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis of wild-type (WT) seedlings exposed to Dox (PRJNA1025135). Several mitochondrially encoded genes were significantly downregulated after Dox treatment (log2[fold change] > 1.0; $p \texttt { < } 0 . 0 5 )$ (Supplemental Figure 1C and Supplemental Table 2). Moreover, immunoblot assays using anti-Nad9, Cox2, and Atp1 antibodies showed that levels of Nad9 (complex I), Cox2 (complex IV), and atp1 (complex V) were reduced after Dox treatment compared with the mock treatments (Supplemental Figure 1D). Treatment with chloramphenicol (CAP), another inhibitor of mitochondrial translation, produced almost the same results as Dox treatment (Supplemental Figure 1D). We also measured oxygen consumption rates (OCRs) using isolated mitochondria and found that the OCR of isolated mitochondria decreased after 1 h of both Dox and CAP treatments compared with the mock treatments (Supplemental Figure 1E). Overall, these results provide further evidence that Dox inhibits mitochondrial function in plants. 

To examine whether the JA signaling pathway is involved in seedling responses to mitochondrial translational stress, we first used the sensitive JA reporter Jas9-Venus (Larrieu et al., 2015); decreased Jas9-Venus signals indicate an increase in JA action. We exposed Jas9-Venus transgenic plants to Dox or methyl jasmonate (MeJA), an active form of JA, for 2 h.

The results showed an increase in JA action upon Dox or MeJA exposure (Figure 1A). In parallel, we generated AOSpro:GUS transgenic plants to monitor JA biosynthesis by $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) staining. When 7-day-old AOSpro:GUS seedlings were exposed to Dox, GUS accumulation was significantly enhanced compared with the untreated control (Figure 1B). Moreover, mass spectrometry (MS)-based quantification revealed that Dox treatment significantly increased the JA concentration by $1 3 2 . 5 \%$ in 7-day-old WT seedlings compared with the control group (Figure 1C). Importantly, the mrpl1-3 mutant, in which mitochondrial translation is repressed (Wang and Auwerx, 2017), exhibited higher JA levels than WT plants (Figure 1C). 

Next, to monitor JA biosynthesis during the UPRmt, 7-day-old Arabidopsis seedlings were exposed to Dox for 1 h, and the relative expression levels of JA biosynthesis genes (AOS, AOC1, AOC3, OPR3, LOX2, and LOX3) were quantified by realtime reverse transcriptase–quantitative PCR (RT–qPCR). We observed that Dox increased the transcript levels of these genes (Figure 1D). However, the transcript levels of JA biosynthesis genes and Jas9-Venus expression were restored after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of Dox treatment (Supplemental Figure 2A–2C). Intriguingly, several members of the JAZ gene family (JAZ1, JAZ2, JAZ5, and JAZ10), which encode key repressors of JA signaling, were significantly upregulated in Dox-treated plants compared with the controls (Supplemental Figure 2D). It is likely that the 

expression of JAZ genes is induced by Dox to fine-tune the JA pathway and prevent negative effects of high JA level on plant growth and development. We next investigated whether JA biosynthesis was also affected in the mrpl1-3 mutant. We found that JA biosynthesis genes were expressed to higher levels in mrpl1-3 than in the WT, suggesting that JA production was increased in the mrpl1-3 mutant (Figure 1E). To verify this finding, we crossed the mrpl1-3 mutant with the Jas9-Venus line. The results showed reduced expression of Jas9-Venus in the mrpl1-3 background, suggesting that mutations in MRPL1 increase the JA level (Figure 1F and 1G). We also confirmed that the decrease in Venus fluorescence in mrpl1-3 Jas9-Venus plants was not caused by altered expression of Jas9-Venus (Supplemental Figure 2E). 

To further investigate the UPRmt response in Dox-treated plants, we analyzed transcriptional changes in the seedlings by RNA-seq analysis (PRJNA1025135). Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were visualized using volcano plots (Supplemental Figure 3A). We identified 3433 DEGs (1025 upregulated and 2408 downregulated) in Dox-treated seedlings compared with controls (Supplemental Figure 3A and Supplemental Table 3). Gene ontology (GO) analysis showed that these DEGs were enriched in cellular response to oxidative stress, fatty acid, wounding, hypoxia, and other functions related to decreased oxygen levels (Supplemental Figure 3B and Supplemental Table 4). These results are consistent with our OCR analysis of isolated 

mitochondria (Supplemental Figure 1E). Next, we examined expression changes of genes involved in mitochondrial retrograde regulation (MRR) and JA response in this dataset and in transcript profiles (GEO: GSE78862) from our previous study (Wang and Auwerx, 2017). We found that many MRR marker genes (Van Aken et al., 2009), including AOX1a, NDB2, and HSP23.5, as well as JA-responsive genes (GO:0009753), were induced in both datasets (Supplemental Figure 3C and 3D). However, the transcript levels of JA biosynthesis genes were reduced in both datasets (Supplemental Figure 3E), which is likely due to long-term exposure to Dox treatment (Supplemental Figure 2). These results are consistent with our RT–qPCR results (Supplemental Figure 2C), in which the expression of JA biosynthesis genes, including AOS, AOC3, OPR3, and LOX3, decreased significantly after 24 h of longterm Dox treatment. The RNA-seq dataset for mrpl1 (Wang and Auwerx, 2017) was then compared with the transcript profiles of other mutants defective in mitochondrial translation, such as mtran1-2/2-2 (Tran et al., 2023) and rps10 (Adamowicz-Skrzypkowska et al., 2020). The mutants showed increased transcript levels of many JA-related genes (Supplemental Figure 3F), consistent with the increased JA action we observed in mrpl1-3 (Figure 1C and 1E–1G). Overall, our transcriptomic data suggest that impaired mitochondrial translation activates MRR and JA responses in plants. 

We next examined the influence of mitochondrial respiration inhibitors such as AA (inhibitor of complex III) and rotenone (inhibitor of complex I) (Kacprzak et al., 2020) on the JA response. AA and rotenone treatment did not affect the expression of JA biosynthesis genes (Supplemental Figure 2F). We also investigated whether JA biosynthesis was affected in mutants of two nac017 alleles (anac017-1, rao2-1) (Ng et al., 2013a) and ndufs4 (which lacks complex I) (Meyer et al., 2009). We found that the expression of JA biosynthesis genes was not significantly altered in anac017-1, rao2-1, or ndufs4 mutants compared with WT plants (Supplemental Figure 2G), suggesting that perturbation of mitochondrial respiration does not activate JA signaling, whereas mitochondrial translational stress does activate JA signaling. Further investigation is required to explore the similarities and differences between mitochondrial respiratory and translational stresses. Taken together, these results demonstrate that mitochondrial translational stress triggers JA biosynthesis and response in plants. 

# Mitochondrial translational stress inhibits root meristem growth through JA signaling

To evaluate the effect of mitochondrial translational stress on root growth in Arabidopsis, we assessed primary root growth in plants grown in media containing different Dox concentrations. In WT plants, primary root growth decreased significantly when the Dox concentration increased from 0.5 to $2 0 \ \mu { \sf g } / \mathsf { m l }$ (Figure 2A and 2B), suggesting that mitochondrial translational stress inhibits plant growth in a dose-dependent manner. In addition, Dox also reduced chlorophyll content in a dosedependent manner (Supplemental Figure 4A), suggesting strong coordination of functions between mitochondria and chloroplasts. 

We next explored the mechanism of root growth inhibition upon Dox exposure. In Arabidopsis, the stress-induced reduction in 

root meristem size is caused by decreased cell-division rates, a reduction in cell-division activity, and loss of the division potential of meristematic cells (Rowe et al., 2016). We measured the lengths of root meristem zones under Dox treatment and found that Dox decreased the length of primary root meristem zones in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 2C and 2D). To examine the Dox-induced inhibition of root elongation in detail, we measured the length of the non-root-hair zone (NRHZ) and found that it was significantly shorter in Dox-treated plants compared with controls (Supplemental Figure 4B and 4C). Moreover, root meristem length was shorter in the mrpl1-3 mutant compared with WT plants (Supplemental Figure 4D and 4E). To explore the role of JA signaling in translational stress, we measured root meristem growth of the JA receptor mutant coi1-2 (Xu et al., 2002) following Dox treatment. Our results showed that the root meristem zone was longer in the coi1-2 mutant than in the WT, Columbia-0 (Col-0), under Dox treatment, suggesting that COI1 is required for Dox-mediated inhibition of root meristem growth (Figure 2G and 2H). 

Reduction in the length of the root meristematic zone is known to be caused by loss of the division potential of meristematic cells (Baluska et al., 2010). We therefore used a mitotic cyclin B1 (CYCB1) promoter-driven GUS reporter to monitor cell-cycle progression, specifically the $\mathsf { G } _ { 2 }$ -to-M transition during the cell cycle (Colo´ n-Carmona et al., 1999). The results showed that exogenous Dox application significantly reduced GUS activity compared with the control in both the root tip and the lateral root primordium (Figure 2E and 2F). Furthermore, the expression of cell-cycle-related genes, including A-TYPE CYCLIN-DEPENDENT KINASE 1 (CDKA;1), CYCB1;1, CYCB3;1, CYCLIN D1;1 (CYCD1;1), CYCD2;1, CYCD3;1, CYCD4;1, CYCD4;2, E2Fa, and E2Fb, was significantly reduced in roots under Dox treatment (Supplemental Figure 4F). These results suggest that mitochondrial translational stress inhibits root elongation by affecting the cell cycle in the meristem zones. 

# JA signaling negatively regulates plant growth under mitochondrial translational stress

The inhibition of primary root elongation by exogenous JA application is often used as a method to identify key components of the JA-signaling pathway (Staswick et al., 1992; Chini et al., 2007). The inhibition of primary root growth by Dox resembles the typical phenotypes of JA-restrained primary root elongation. Thus, we hypothesized that JA is involved in plant growth inhibition under mitochondrial translational stress. We used the pCOI1:COI1-Venus (Larrieu et al., 2015) transgenic line to monitor the expression of the JA receptor gene COI1 in root tips. The COI1-Venus signals were increased in root tips exposed to Dox for 3 h (Supplemental Figure 5A and 5B), indicating that COI1 is activated under mitochondrial stress. However, the effect of Dox on root growth inhibition was greatly reduced in the COI1 loss-of-function mutant coi1-2 compared with Col-0 (Figure 3A–3C; Supplemental Figure 6A and 6B). Like the coi1-2 mutant, coi1-1 (null allele mutant) was less sensitive to Dox than Col-0 in terms of primary root growth (Supplemental Figure 5C and 5D). Moreover, phenotypic analysis showed that stem growth inhibition was partially alleviated in the coi1-2 mutant compared with Col-0 under mitochondrial stress (Figure 3D). Previous studies have shown that MYC2 can bind to the promoters of several JA biosynthesis genes and positively regulate their expression (Hou et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2020). # MYC2 binds to the promoter of ERF109 and activates its transcription

To explore potential regulators that act downstream of JA under mitochondrial translational stress, we focused on ERF109, which regulates plant growth under stresses and is a direct target of JA signaling (Moullan et al., 2015; Kong et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020). The expression of ERF109 was transiently stimulated by $2 0 \mu \ g / \ m \mu$ or $1 \ \mu { \sf g } / \mathsf { m l }$ Dox. Specifically, the transcript level of ERF109 peaked with 130-fold and 75-fold induction at 20 min after $2 0 ~ \mu \ g / \ m \mu$ and $1 ~ \mu \varrho / \mathsf { m } \mu$ Dox treatment, respectively, and it was restored to basal levels at 2 h (Figure 5A). In our RNAseq analysis, ERF109 expression was also upregulated by Dox treatment (Supplemental Figure S9). Moreover, RT–qPCR assays showed strong induction of ERF109 after Dox treatment, which was reduced in the myc2 mutant and greatly diminished in the coi1-2 mutant (Figure 5B), suggesting that Dox induces ERF109 ina COI1-and MYC2-dependent manner. These results are consistent with a previous study in which water-spray-responsive expression of ERF109 depended on MYC2/MYC3/MYC4 (Van Moerkercke et al., 2019). Strikingly, we also found that the induction of ERF109 expression was regulated redundantly by MYC2, MYC3, and MYC4 (Figure 5B). Given that JA and ethylene pathways interact extensively (Song et al., 2014; Zhu, 2014) and that ethylene signaling is induced by Dox (Wang and Auwerx, 2017), we investigated whether Dox-induced expression of ERF109 was dependent on ethylene signaling. RT–qPCR assays showed that Dox-induced expression of ERF109 was attenuated in etr2-1, ein2, ein3-1, and ein4 mutants (Figure 5C). On the basis of this finding, we concluded that both JA and ethylene signaling are required for the induction of ERF109 expression under mitochondrial translational stress. 

Having ascertained that myc2 lines showed decreased expression of ERF109, we next investigated whether MYC2 interacts with the ERF109 gene. Previous studies have shown that MYC2 binds to the G-box (CACGTG) and G-box-like motifs in the promoters of target genes (Dombrecht et al., 2007). Our promoter analysis revealed four G-box-like motifs in the promoter region of ERF109 (Figure 5D). We examined the interaction between MYC2 and ERF109 through a chromatin immunoprecipitation–PCR (ChIP– qPCR) assay using 35S::MYC2-GFP and 35S::GFP (control) plants. Chromatin immunoprecipitated with anti-GFP antibody was markedly enriched in the P1 and P3 regions of the ERF109 promoter (Figure 5D), indicating that MYC2 directly binds to the ERF109 promoter in vivo. Further supporting evidence was obtained from yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) experiments, which showed 

that the pB42AD-MYC2 fusion protein could bind to the three tandem repeats of both G1 and G2 motifs in the ERF109 promoter but not to mutated G-box motifs (Figure 5E). These results indicate that MYC2 binds to the promoter of ERF109. 

To further investigate the role of MYC2 in the regulation of ERF109 transcription, we performed transient expression assays using Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. MYC2 and JAZ1 genes were driven by the 35S promoter as effectors, and the promoter of ERF109 was fused to the luciferase (LUC) gene as a reporter (Figure 5F). As expected from a previous report (Van Moerkercke et al., 2019), co-expression of the MYC2 effector with the LUC reporter dramatically activated ProERF109:LUC activity compared with the GFP control (Figure 5G). However, we also observed that co-expression of JAZ1, a representative JAZ gene that interacts with and inhibits MYC2 (Chini et al., 2007; Thines et al., 2007), significantly repressed MYC2- activated ProERF109:LUC activity (Figure 5H). Taken together, these results suggest that MYC2 directly binds to G-box-like motifs in the ERF109 promoter to activate its expression, whereas JAZ proteins attenuate the effect of MYC2. 

# ERF109 acts downstream of MYC2 to regulate plant growth under mitochondrial translational stress

To study the role of ERF109 in plant growth and development under mitochondrial translational stress, we identified the erf109 loss-offunction mutant and 35S-ERF109 transgenic lines (Supplemental Figure 10A–10D). The roots of Col-0, erf109, and 35S-ERF109 plants were subsequently examined. Under normal conditions, the erf109 mutant exhibited a primary root length similar to that of Col-0. However, erf109 mutants had longer primary roots and greater shoot fresh weights than Col-0 seedlings when grown on medium containing Dox (Figure 6A and 6B), suggesting that ERF109 is required for the inhibition of root growth under Dox treatment. In 35S-ERF109 plants, overexpression of ERF109 triggered plant-stress responses and reduced plant growth under both control and Dox conditions.In addition,Dox-induced inhibition of stem growth was partially alleviated in erf109 mutants compared with Col-0 plants (Figure 6C). We also generated the mrpl1-3 erf109 double mutant by crossing mrpl1-3 with erf109. The mrpl1-3 erf109 double mutant exhibited longer primary roots than the mrpl1-3 mutant (Figure 6D and 6E), indicating that the root growth defects of mrpl1-3 are partially dependent on the presence of functional ERF109. Together, these results indicate that ERF109 is essential for reshaping plant growth under mitochondrial translational stress. 

To confirm that Dox induces ERF109 expression through MYC2, we generated myc2 ERF109pro:ERF109-GUS plants by crossing the myc2 mutant with an ERF109pro:ERF109-GUS line. Upon exogenous Dox treatment, GUS activity was markedly induced inERF109- pro:ERF109-GUS roots (Figure 6F) and to a lesser extent in myc2 ERF109pro:ERF109-GUS roots (Figure 6G), suggesting that mitochondrial stress induces ERF109 expression through MYC2 in the root. Collectively, our data revealed that ERF109 acts downstream of MYC2 in the mitochondrial translational stress response.

# Mutation of MRPL1 induces JA-mediated plant defense

JA signaling regulates plant responses to insect attack and pathogen infection (Wasternack and Hause, 2013). Because MRPL1 deficiency promotes JA production, we investigated whether MRPL was involved in JA-regulated plant defense against insect attack and pathogen infection. The mrpl1-3 mutant plants showed reduced consumption by Spodoptera exigua larvae and repressed larval growth compared with WT plants (Supplemental Figure 13A–13C). Next, we performed two-choice tests between mrpl1-3 and WT plants, and the results showed that mrpl1-3 plants attracted fewer larvae than WT plants $( \sim 7 3 \%$ for mrpl1-3, ${ \sim } 2 7 \%$ for WT) (Supplemental Figure 13D). To further investigate whether resistance to necrotrophic pathogens was altered in the mrpl1-3 mutant, plants were inoculated with $5 ~ { \mu \mu }$ of Botrytis cinerea spore suspension. We found that mrpl1-3 mutant plants exhibited greater resistance to B. cinerea than WT plants and confirmed this finding by measuring lesion area 

(Supplemental Figure 13E and 13F). Consistent with these results, the expression of defense-related genes (PDF1.2 and ERF1) (Song et al., 2014) and wound-responsive genes (VPS2 and VSP1) (Lorenzo et al., 2004) in response to JA induction was strongly induced in the mrpl1-3 mutant compared with WT plants (Supplemental Figure 13G and 13H). Together, these results suggest that mutation of MRPL1 induces JA-mediated plant defense. 

# DISCUSSION

We previously showed that mitochondrial proteotoxic stress induced by the mitochondrial translation inhibitor Dox leads to constitutive activation of ethylene responses in Arabidopsis seedlings, thereby inhibiting root growth and plant development (Wang and Auwerx, 2017). In the present study, we demonstrated that mitochondrial translational stress regulates plant growth through JA signaling. Our data showed that ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ - induced expression of ERF109 (via JA signaling) serves as a vital node in translational stress responses, in which ERF109 contributes to root growth inhibition by stimulating ROS production. Although the mechanisms that underlie ERF109-mediated modulation of the UPRmt remain unclear, our work reveals a signaling pathway whereby the COI1–MYC2–ERF109 module controls plant growth and development under mitochondrial translational stress (Figure 7I). Plants in which MRPL1 was mutated (mrpl1-3) exhibited high JA accumulation, reduced growth, and enhanced resistance to both S. exigua and B. cinerea. These results suggest that mitochondrial translational stress induces JA action to affect the trade-off between growth and defense. 

Dox is an inhibitor of mitochondrial translation not only in plants but also in worms, flies, and mice (Moullan et al., 2015). Our immunoblot analysis showed that Nad9 and Cox2 showed higher degradation rates under Dox treatment, whereas Nad7 and Atp1 showed lower degradation rates (Supplemental Figure 1D), indicating that Dox treatment causes differential turnover of mitochondrial proteins. This finding is consistent with previous studies in which loss of mitochondrial Lon1 protease causes different protein abundance changes in mitochondrial protein turnover (Rigas et al., 2009; Solheim et al., 2012; Li et al., 2017b). Because exogenous Dox increased JA levels and reduced primary root growth, our phenotypic analysis showed that coi1-2, myc2, and myc234 mutants exhibited enhanced tolerance to mitochondrial translational stress, suggesting that growth reduction caused by mitochondrial inhibition is not only the result of low plant energy status (Wang and Auwerx, 2017) but also, at least in part, regulated by the JA signal. A previous study in which protein aggregation 

inhibited mitochondrial translation and induced the ethyleneassociated unfolded protein response (Song et al., 2024), together with our findings that the JA biosynthesis inhibitor DIECA partially rescued the growth defects of Dox-treated plants, indicate that signals other than JA (such as ethylene and auxin) may also participate in the Dox-induced inhibition of plant growth. Given the synergistic and antagonistic interactions between JA and ethylene pathways (Lorenzo et al., 2003; Zhu, 2014; Zhu and Lee, 2015; Pe´ rez-Llorca et al., 2023), further investigation is required to clarify the mechanism of interaction between the JA and ethylene pathways under mitochondrial translational stress. Interestingly, we found that MYC2, MYC3, and MYC4 acted redundantly to modulate plant growth, and Dox-induced JA biosynthesis was greatly diminished in myc2 or myc234 mutant plants, indicating that impairment of JA production improved the growth of myc234 mutant plants under mitochondrial stress. In addition, consistent with previous studies (Van Moerkercke et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020), these results indicate that JA biosynthesis genes induced by mitochondrial translational stress are directly controlled by MYC2/MYC3/MYC4 through a positive feedback loop. 

Recent studies showed that ERF109 is regulated by MYC2 and participates in stem cell activation and JA-mediated root regeneration (Zhang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2019). In the present study, ERF109 levels accumulated quickly in response to translational stress, making ERF109 one of the candidate mediators of retrograde signaling. Here, we showed that MYC2, MYC3, and MYC4 redundantly regulate the induction of ERF109 expression under translational stress. Interestingly, MYC2 bound to and activated the promoter of ERF109, whereas co-expression with JAZ1 strongly counteracted the effects of MYC2. These results are consistent with a previous study in which JAZ9 inhibited ERF109 activity in response to JA-mediated wound signaling (Zhang et al., 2019). These results, together with the restoration of ERF109 expression to basal levels at 1 h after Dox treatment, led us to hypothesize that JAZs decrease the hypersensitivity of plants to ERF109 accumulation under mitochondrial stress. Thus, our findings revealed an additional role of ERF109 in the mitochondrial translational stress response. ERF109 also mediates the crosstalk between JA signaling and auxin biosynthesis by binding to two auxin biosynthesis genes (YUC2 and ASA1), thereby regulating lateral root formation (Cai et al., 2014). Therefore, it would be interesting to study whether the increased ERF109 level under translational stress affects auxin homeostasis. Apart from this, ERF109 overexpression lines had reduced primary root length under normal conditions (Cai et al., 2014) as we also observed in our study (Figure 6A and 6B; Supplemental Figure 11B and 11C). Furthermore, our phenotypic analysis showed that the erf109 mutant was less sensitive to mitochondrial stress than WT plants in terms of root and shoot growth (Figure 6A–6C), indicating that ERF109 negatively regulates plant growth under mitochondrial translational stress. 

Our phenotypic analysis showed that Dox and KI co-treatment partially rescued the Dox-induced inhibition of root growth, suggesting that ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ negatively affects primary root growth under mitochondrial translational stress. These results are consistent with a recent study in which mitochondrial ROS were the likely 

primary trigger for initiation of mitochondrial retrograde signaling in plants (Khan et al., 2024). In addition, that study indicated that ANAC017 is required for mitochondrial ROS-mediated MRR (Khan et al., 2024). The present study showed that Doxtriggered MRR occurred in part through COI1. A slight induction of MRR was still observed in the coi1-2 mutant under both Dox and AA treatments (Supplemental Figure 7C and 7D) and was likely due to other factors, e.g., ANAC017 and ANAC013 (De Clercq et al., 2013; Ng et al., 2013a; He et al., 2022; Eysholdt-Derzso´ et al., 2023). Furthermore, the fact that expression of JA marker genes was not altered in anac017-1 and rao2-1 mutants compared with WT plants (Supplemental Figure 2G), together with the finding that JA response is not required for AA-induced inhibition of root growth (Supplemental Figure 7A and 7B), led us to speculate that mitochondrial ROS activate JA action in an ANAC017-independent manner. 

In a previous study, the expression of ERF109 was transiently induced by ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , and overexpression of ERF109 promoted ROS accumulation (Matsuo et al., 2015). Our DAB staining results showed that ERF109 overexpression stimulates ROS production in plants (Supplemental Figure 11A), consistent with previous studies in which elevated ERF109 transcript levels caused ROS accumulation in response to biotic and dehydration stresses (Matsuo et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2020). Intriguingly, our results suggest that root growth defects in 35S-ERF109 lines might partially account for the increased ROS accumulation, indicating that ROS and ERF109 form a positive amplification loop that regulates root development under mitochondrial translational stress. Stimulation of ROS production by ERF109 might be essential for the rapid and transient local production of ROS, which triggers the plant UPRmt pathway, leading to changes in gene expression that enable plant acclimation to mitochondrial stress. A previous study showed that ROS act as secondary messengers for cell-to-cell signal propagation, and ERF109 is involved in the regulation of ROS homeostasis in plants (Wang et al., 2020). Further investigation will be required to explain the relationship between ROS accumulation and elevated ERF109 transcript levels under mitochondrial translational stress. 

In conclusion, our results reveal a critical role of JA in plant growth under mitochondrial translational stress in Arabidopsis. We identify a core network that implies a stress hormone JA-mediated response to mitochondrial translational stress in plants. Mitochondrial translational stress globally activates ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production, which promotes JA action, functions upstream of ERF109, and induces ERF109 expression, promoting more ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ production in a positive feedback loop to modulate root development. In addition to the inhibition of plant growth by mitochondrial translational stress, the stimulation of JA action is important for plant adaptation to environmental perturbations. This should enable us to better understand how plants reasonably cope with diverse environmental stresses. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials, growth conditions, and treatments

A. thaliana ecotype Col-0 was used as the WT (control) and for generating all mutants and transgenic lines. Seeds of the previously described myc2/jin1-2 and myc2 myc3 myc4 (myc234) 

mutants (Zhang et al., 2020) were kindly provided by Dr. Philippe Reymond (University of Lausanne, Switzerland). The coi1-2, 35S-ERF109-GFP, and ERF109pro:ERF109-GUS materials were kindly donated by Lin Xu (Shanghai Institute of Plant Physiology and Ecology, China). The CYCB1;1::GUS lines (Colo´ n-Carmona et al., 1999) were obtained from Guangjie Li (CAS). The ndufs4 mutants were kindly provided by Etienne Meyer. The homozygous erf109 mutant was identified from Salk_150614, which was ordered from AraShare (non-profit Arabidopsis share center). The pCOI1:COI1-Venus (N2105631), etr2-1, ein2, ein3-1, ein4, and Jas9-Venus (N2105629) lines were obtained from the European Arabidopsis Stock Center (NASC). The myc2/jin1-2, myc234, coi1-1, and coi1-2 mutants were selected on MeJA plates on the basis of their reduced root growth inhibition (compared with Col-0) and were confirmed by PCR genotyping. To perform genetic analysis, hybrids were generated by crossing the corresponding homozygous lines and confirmed by PCR. 

Seeds were surface sterilized in $5 \%$ (w/v) bleach for 15 min and then washed with sterile water at least five times. Seeds were placed on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (1/2 MS) medium supplemented with $1 \%$ (w/v) sucrose and $0 . 7 5 \%$ (w/v) phytoagar. Plates containing the seeds were incubated at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2–3 days, then transferred to a growth chamber maintained at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ with a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod and $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { \textmu m o l \ } \mathrm { \ m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { \mathsf s } ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity. To analyze primary root growth, seeds were sown directly on 1/2 MS solid medium supplemented with the indicated concentrations of specific compounds, and seedlings were grown vertically. For hormone and specific compound treatments, filter-sterilized compound was added to cooled $( \sim 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ 1/2 MS medium, mixed in a 250-ml triangular flask, and poured into Petri dishes. To perform the MeJA treatment, ethanol was added to cooled medium as a control. To perform the DIECA (JA biosynthesis inhibitor) treatment, seedlings were grown for 5 days on 1/2 MS agar plates under normal conditions, then transferred to 1/2 MS medium or doxycycline (Dox) medium supplemented with DIECA for another 5 days. To perform the short-term Dox treatment, 5-day-old seedlings grown on solid 1/2 MS medium were transferred to normal medium for another 2 days and then treated with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m }$ of liquid 1/2 MS medium supplemented with 0 (mock) or $2 0 \ \mu { \sf g } / \mathsf { m } \mathsf { l }$ Dox for the indicated time before RNA extraction or GUS staining. The untreated seedlings did not contact the liquid 1/2 MS medium, so in RT–qPCR analysis, the indicated transcript level in untreated seedlings at 0 h was arbitrarily set to 1. To perform ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ treatment, 7-day-old WT or coi1-2 seedlings were treated with liquid 1/2 MS medium supplemented with 0 (mock) or $1 0 \ \mathsf { m M } \ \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ for the indicated time period before harvest. After harvest, all samples were immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent RNA extraction and RT–qPCR analysis. The lengths of the primary root, newly grown root, and NRHZ were quantified using ImageJ software (https://imagej.net). Phenotypes were photographed with a Nikon digital camera. 

# Vector construction and plant transformation

To prepare the AOSpro:GUS construct, the promoter region of AOS ( 3000 bp from the start codon) was PCR amplified using the AOSpro:GUS-F and AOSpro:GUS-R primers 

(Supplemental Table 1) and cloned into the Fu79-GUS vector. The resultant Fu79-AOSpro:GUS (Wang et al., 2013) construct was recombined with pH2GW7 using LR reactions (McClung and Xie, 2014). The resultant AOSpro:GUS construct was introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105, which was used to transform WT Arabidopsis plants by the floral dip method. A total of 19 T1 transgenic lines were selected by hygromycin screening, and homozygous T3 plants were used for subsequent analyses. 

# Histochemical GUS staining

Histochemical staining was performed as described previously (Yuan et al., 2021). In brief, seedlings were removed from plates, immersed in cold $90 \%$ acetone, and kept on ice until all samples had been collected. The samples were stained in fresh GUS-staining solution (100 mM sodium phosphate buffer [pH 7.5], $1 0 . 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ EDTA $[ \mathsf { p H } 8 . 0 ]$ , 0.5 mM ${ \sf K } _ { 3 } [ { \sf F e } ( { \sf C N } ) _ { 6 } ] .$ , $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ ${ \sf K } _ { 4 } [ { \sf F e } ( { \sf C N } ) _ { 6 } ]$ , $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, and 1.0 mM 5-bromo-chloro-3- indolyl- $\beta$ -D-glucuronide) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. After staining, the seedlings were cleared in $7 5 \%$ ethanol and photographed using an Olympus BX53 microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). More than 15 seedlings were examined for each transgenic line, and the experiments were repeated three times. 

# Microscopy analysis

Root meristem analyses were performed as reported previously (Yuan et al., 2021). To perform root meristem size analysis and GUS-staining microexamination, seedlings were cleared and mounted on glass slides with a clearing solution (8 g chloral hydrate, $2 \mathsf { \ m }$ water, and 1 ml glycerol) for several minutes. Differential interference contrast images were captured using an Olympus BX53 microscope. Meristem size was measured as the distance from the quiescent center to the meristem boundary, where the cortical cells were twice the size of the previous cells. Root meristem length was measured using ImageJ software. Three biological replicates were performed for each treatment, with at least 24 seedlings per replicate. 

Confocal microscopy analysis was performed using a Leica TCS SP8 confocal laser scanning microscope. The Jas9- Venus signal was visualized with 514 nm excitation and 520– 560 nm emission. The COI1-Venus signal was visualized with 488 nm excitation and $5 1 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ emission. Approximately eight seedlings were examined, and at least three independent experiments were performed. 

# Respiration rate measurement

The respiration rates of seedlings were measured using a liquidphase oxygen electrode system (Hansatech Instruments, UK) as described previously (Sew et al., 2013). Before the measurement, the oxygen electrode was calibrated by addition of sodium dithionite to deplete the oxygen in saturated water. Twelve-day-old seedlings (40–100 mg) were then immersed in respiration buffer (10 mM HEPES, 10 mM 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid, and $2 \mathsf { m M C a C l } _ { 2 }$ ; pH 7.2) in darkness for 30 min. In a darkened electrode chamber, the OCR was recorded for at least 10 min until the OCR curve stabilized. The value of OCR was normalized relative to seedling fresh weight (FW). This experiment was performed with at least four independent biological replicates and was repeated three times with similar results. 

# Mitochondrial isolation and blue native PAGE

Mitochondria were isolated from mature leaves (15 g) harvested from 4- to 5-week-old plants, as described previously (Millar et al., 2007). In brief, the aerial plant parts were ground at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in $1 4 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of extraction buffer (0.3 M sucrose, 25 mM tetrasodium pyrophosphate, 2 mM EDTA, 10 mM ${ \sf K H } _ { 2 } { \sf P O } _ { 4 }$ [pH 7.5], $1 \%$ [w/v] PVP-40, and $1 \%$ [w/v] BSA with $2 0 \mathsf { m M }$ ascorbic acid and 5 mM cysteine added before use). The homogenate was filtered through Miracloth, and the filtrate was centrifuged at ${ 2 5 0 0 } \ g$ for 5 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The supernatant was centrifuged at $1 7 5 0 0 g$ for 20 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , and the pellet containing crude mitochondria was resuspended in wash buffer (0.6 M sucrose, 20 mM N-Tris(hydroxymethyl)methyl-2-aminoethanesulfonic acid, $0 . 2 \%$ [w/v] BSA; pH adjusted to 7.5 with NaOH). The crude mitochondria were transferred onto a $0 \% { - } 4 . 4 \%$ (w/v) PVP-Percoll gradient and centrifuged at $4 0 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 45 min. Purified mitochondria were washed twice with wash buffer and centrifuged at 31 000 g for 15 min. Blue native PAGE was performed using $5 0 \ \mu { 9 }$ of purified mitochondria as described previously (Jha et al., 2016). The activity of respiratory complexes I and IV and the supercomplex $( 1 _ { 1 } + | | | _ { 2 } )$ were measured as described previously (Schertl and Braun, 2015). 

# RNA extraction and RT–qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted from whole seedlings using the TRIzol reagent. cDNA was then synthesized from $1 \mu \mathfrak { g }$ of total RNA using a reverse transcription kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). All RT– qPCR assays were performed on an IQ5 multicolor real-time PCR detection system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. The reaction program was $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 40 cycles of $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 10 s and ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 30 s. Expression levels of target genes were normalized relative to that of Arabidopsis UBQ10 (AT4G05320). Relative gene-expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method. The experiment was performed with three independent biological replicates using RNA extracted from three independent plant materials grown at separate times. The primer sequences used in this study are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# RNA-seq and data analysis

WT plants were germinated and grown on medium supplemented with or without $5 \mu \ g / \mathsf { m l }$ Dox for 10 days. Total RNA was extracted from the seedlings using the TRIzol reagent (Vazyme). RNA integrity was assessed using the Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system (Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). Sequencing libraries were generated using the VAHTS Universal V6 RNA-seq Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NR604-01/02), and sequencing was performed on the NovaSeq 6000 S4 platform according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The quality-filtered reads were mapped to the A. thaliana genome (TAIR10) using TopHat2 v2.1.0, which provided the reference gene annotation with known transcripts. The normalized RPKM (reads per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads) value for each annotated gene was then obtained using Cufflinks v2.2.1. DEGs were identified using thresholds of absolute value of ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $\geq 1 . 0$ and $p <$ 0.05 (t-test). GO enrichment analysis was performed with the ClusterProfiler v4.0 package in R (Wu et al., 2021), and DEGs were considered to be significantly enriched in GO terms with corrected $p$ values of ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Immunoblot analysis

Crude mitochondria were mixed with $5 \times$ SDS sample buffer to extract the proteins. The mixture was heated at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 5 min, then centrifuged at $2 0 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min. The resulting supernatants were used for SDS–PAGE. Blots were probed with primary antibodies against subunits of the complex: NAD9 (PHY3697S), NAD7 (PHY1077A), and ATP1 (PHY2146S) from PhytoAB (USA); COX2 from Agrisera (Sweden); ACTIN and VDAC1 from Abways (China). After electrophoresis, the proteins were transferred to a $0 . 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ nitrocellulose membrane. 

# JA quantification

To determine the endogenous JA content, 7-day-old seedlings grown under normal conditions were transferred to medium supplemented without or with Dox and grown for the indicated duration before harvest. At least 100 mg (FW) of seedlings was collected, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $\mathtt { - 8 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ until use. Samples were extracted and analyzed according to a method described previously (Pan et al., 2010). In brief, each sample was extracted on ice using $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of extraction solvent containing a 2-propanol/water/concentrated HCl mixture (2:1:0.002, v/v/v). JA was quantified by adding 10 ng of dihydrojasmonic acid (2H-JA) as an internal standard. The extraction solution was placed on a shaker at 100 rpm and incubated for 30 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Subsequently, 1 ml of dichloromethane was added to each sample and shaken for $3 0 { \mathrm { ~ m i n } } .$ . After centrifugation at $1 3 0 0 0 g$ for 5 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , the subnatants were transferred to fresh 2-ml microfuge tubes and concentrated (not completely dried) in an evaporator with nitrogen flow. The samples were redissolved in 0.1 ml of methanol and centrifuged at $1 3 0 0 0 g$ for 5 min. The supernatants were finally subjected to ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry. The content of JA in samples was determined by comparing the peak areas of samples with the peak area of the internal standard. JA and 2H-JA were detected at mass-to-charge $( m / z )$ ratios of 209.1 and 211.0, respectively. 

# Dual-luciferase assay

The promoter region of ERF109 $( \sim 2 1 0 0$ bp) was cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector to generate the ERF109-LUC reporter construct. Renilla luciferase (REN) driven by the 35S promoter in pGreenII 0800-LUC was used as the internal control. The pFGC5941-GFP empty vector was used as a negative control. All primers used to generate these constructs are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

Agrobacterium (strain EHA105) solutions containing the indicated combinations of effector and reporter constructs were co-incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and infiltrated into leaves of 4-week-old N. benthamiana plants. The transformed plants were grown in darkness for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ and then under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod for $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ . The leaves were then sprayed with $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ luciferin and placed in darkness for 10 min. Firefly LUC and REN activities were measured using the dual-luciferase reporter assay kit (Promega), and LUC/REN ratios were calculated to represent promoter

# ChIP assay

ChIP experiments were performed as described previously (Li et al., 2017a; Liu et al., 2019). In brief, approximately $1 . 2 { \mathfrak { g } }$ of 35S::GFP (control) and 35S::MYC2-GFP seedlings were homogenized and crosslinked for 15 min in $1 \%$ (v/v) formaldehyde solution under vacuum. Samples were treated with $5 0 \mu \ M$ Dox for 3 h before harvest. The sonicated chromatin complex was immunoprecipitated using anti-GFP polyclonal antibody (Abcam, ab290) overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . After reverse crosslinking, the DNA was extracted with phenol/chloroform/isoamyl alcohol (25:24:1, v/v/v) and analyzed by qPCR. The fold enrichment of chromatin was calculated as the ratio of 35S::GFP to 35S::MYC2-GFP, and ACTIN2 was used as an internal control. ChIP experiments were performed with three biological replicates. Primers used for ChIP–qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Y1H assay

Y1H assays were performed as described previously (Lin et al., 2007). In brief, the full-length coding sequence of MYC2 was amplified and cloned into the EcoRI-digested pB42AD vector to generate the effector construct. Three tandem copies of the G-box motif found in the ERF109 promoter region or its mutant variant was cloned into the pLacZ vector to generate reporter constructs. The empty pB42AD and pLacZ plasmids were used as negative controls. Various combinations of effector and reporter constructs were co-transformed into the yeast strain EGY48 using the LiAc method. The transformants were cultivated on synthetic double-dropout medium deficient in tryptophan and uracil (SD/-Trp/-Ura), and positive clones were transferred to selection medium (SD/-Trp/-Ura containing X-gal) and grown at ${ } ^ { 2 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 48 h. All primers used to construct effector and reporter plasmids for Y1H assays are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Measurements of anthocyanin and chlorophyll content

Anthocyanin levels were measured as described previously (Rabino and Mancinelli, 1986). In brief, 12-day-old seedlings were placed in extraction buffer (1% HCl in methanol) and incubated overnight in darkness at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . After extraction, samples were centrifuged at $1 3 \ : 2 0 0 \ : g$ at room temperature for 2 min. The supernatants were collected, and their absorbance values were measured at 530 and 657 nm. Extraction buffer containing no tissue was used as a blank (control). Anthocyanin concentration per gram of tissue (FW) was calculated using the following equation: 

$$
\text {A n t h o c y a n i n} = \left(A _ {5 3 0} - 0. 2 5 \times A _ {6 5 7}\right),
$$

where $A _ { 5 3 0 }$ and $A _ { 6 5 7 }$ represent the absorbance at 530 and 657 nm, respectively. 

The total chlorophyll content in the plants was determined with a spectrophotometer. In brief, 1 ml of $80 \%$ (v/v) acetone was added to 100 mg of ground tissue and incubated in darkness for 30 min. After centrifugation at $1 5 0 0 \ g$ for 5 min, the optical density of the supernatant was measured at 646 and 663 nm to calculate the total chlorophyll concentration. 

# DAB staining and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ quantification

${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ was detected in situ by DAB staining as described previously (Daudi and O’Brien, 2012). In brief, 7-day-old seedlings were incubated in DAB-staining solution $( 0 . 1 \mathrm { \ m g / m } |$ DAB in 50 mM Tris buffer [pH 5.0]) at room temperature in darkness for 4–8 h. After incubation, the DAB-staining solution was replaced with bleaching solution (ethanol/acetic acid/glycerol = 3:1:1), and the seedlings were boiled at $\mathfrak { g o c }$ for 15 min. Seedlings were cleared with a $7 5 \%$ ethanol solution and photographed using an Olympus BX53 microscope. The ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ content was measured using a hydrogen peroxide content assay kit (colorimetric method, Sangon Biotech) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. 

# Pathogen infection and insect defense assay

The B. cinerea infection assay was performed as described previously (Hu et al., 2013). B. cinerea was cultured on potato dextrose agar medium at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 14 days. More than 30 detached leaves were inoculated with $5 \mu \mu \up$ of B. cinerea spore suspension (105 spores $\mathsf { m } \mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 }$ ), placed in Petri dishes containing $0 . 8 \%$ (w/v) agar, and sealed using adhesive tape. The inoculated plants were incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ under a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod and high humidity. The lesion diameters were recorded after 3 days using ImageJ software. 

The insect defense assay was performed using S. exigua, as described previously (Song et al., 2014). More than 30 mature rosette leaves were collected from 4-week-old plants of each genotype and placed in Petri dishes containing wet filter paper. More than 10 third-instar S. exigua larvae were weighed and reared on plants, with three independent replicates for each genotype. After 2 days of feeding, the increase in average larval weight was recorded. The experiments were repeated three times with similar results. 

# Statistical analysis

All experiments in this study were performed with at least three biological replicates. Statistical significance of the data was determined by analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison test, using GraphPad Prism v7.0 (GraphPad Software; www.graphpad.com). Significant differences between groups were analyzed using unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-tests (ns, not significant; ${ } ^ { \star } p < 0 . 0 5$ , $^ { \star \star } p < 0 . 0 1$ , $^ { \star \star \star } p < 0 . 0 0 1$ ).
