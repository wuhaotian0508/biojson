# ABSTRACT

Engineering nitrogenase directly into crops is a long-held aspiration in plant biotechnology. Of the three types, the Fe-only nitrogenase is a promising engineering target, as it has a simpler maturation pathway than the MoFe- and VFe-nitrogenases and does not require any heterometals for its cofactor. However, previously, we have reported that the obligate electron donor of the Fe-only nitrogenase from A. vinelandii, AnfH, is mostly insoluble when expressed in plant mitochondria. Here, we employed computational methods, the Protein Repair One-Stop Shop (PROSS) protein optimisation algorithm and Rosetta energy calculations, to design eight variants of AnfH with improved soluble expression. The amino acid substitutions chosen were predicted to lower the free energy of the native state of the protein. All eight AnfH variants, containing between 1 and 11 amino acid substitutions, were more soluble than wild-type AnfH in plant mitochondria. Of these, three variants were isolated from N. benthamiana leaf, of which AnfH variant 6 (AnfH V6, T200A T228V E241H) had the best features, being approx. 90-fold more abundant in the soluble fraction. Importantly, AnfH V6 was also functional in $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ reduction with $A$ . vinelandii AnfDKG after $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster reconstitution. These results show that the computational design strategy used here is a powerful approach for engineering nitrogenase into plants and more broadly to plant synthetic biology and recombinant protein production. 

# 1 | Introduction

While synthetic nitrogen fertiliser is indispensable to our existing agricultural systems, it is also a significant contributor to climate change and pollution (Bodirsky et al. 2014; Menegat et al. 2022). Fertiliser production, which currently still relies on hydrogen generated from fossil fuels, is a major source of 

anthropogenic carbon dioxide emissions. The transport of fertiliser to farms and its distribution  further contribute to its carbon footprint. Additionally, excess reactive nitrogen species in the soil can be transformed to the greenhouse gas nitrous oxide by nitrifying and denitrifying bacteria. Another negative consequence of fertiliser use is nitrogen runoff into aquatic ecosystems and groundwater, which can lead 

to algal blooms, loss of biodiversity, and nitrate contamination (Bijay and Craswell 2021; Smith and Schindler 2009; Xia et al. 2020). Thus, there is a strong incentive to reduce global usage of nitrogen fertiliser. One potential solution is to engineer nitrogen-fixing plants that can use gaseous $\Nu _ { 2 }$ from the atmosphere rather than relying on reactive nitrogen species in the soil (Beatty and Good 2011). This could be achieved by expressing the microbial enzyme nitrogenase in plant cells (Merrick and Dixon 1984). 

Nitrogenase is the only enzyme capable of catalysing the conversion of $\Nu _ { 2 }$ gas into ammonia. The two-component enzyme consists of the catalytic unit, dinitrogenase and the obligate electron donor, dinitrogenase reductase, also referred to as the Fe protein. There are three types of nitrogenase, each named after the metals present in the active site cofactor of its catalytic unit: MoFe nitrogenase (NifDK), which contains a $[ \mathrm { M o F e } _ { 7 } \mathrm { S } _ { 9 } \mathrm { C } ]$ -homocitrate cluster (FeMo-co) (Lancaster et al. 2011; Spatzal et al. 2011); VFe nitrogenase (VnfDKG), which contains a $\mathrm { [ V F e _ { 7 } S _ { 8 } C ( C O _ { 3 } ) ^ { 2 - } ] } ,$ - homocitrate cluster (VeFe-co) (Sippel and Einsle  2017); and Fe-only nitrogenase (AnfDKG), which contains a $\mathrm { [ F e _ { 8 } S _ { 9 } C ] }$ - homocitrate cluster (FeFe-co) (Trncik et  al.  2023). In addition to the active site cofactor, each dinitrogenase contains a $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 8 } \mathrm { S } _ { 7 } ]$ cluster, named P-cluster, which mediates electron transfer from the electron donor to the cofactor (Danyal et al. 2011; Kirn and Rees  1992). The Fe protein, also referred to as NifH, VnfH or AnfH, respective to their dinitrogenase partner, contains a $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster at its homodimer interface from which it transfers an electron to the P-cluster during a transient interaction with dinitrogenase in an ATP-dependent process (Georgiadis et al. 1992; Hageman and Burris 1978; Peters et al. 1995). 

When engineering nitrogenase into plants, a major challenge is the assembly and stability of the nitrogenase iron–sulfur (FeS) clusters. These clusters are oxygen-sensitive, and their assembly requires a complex biosynthetic machinery consisting of multiple proteins (Burén et al. 2020). To address this problem, nitrogenase proteins have been targeted to mitochondria or chloroplasts (Allen et al. 2020, 2017; Baysal et al. 2022; Burén et al. 2017; Eseverri et al. 2020; He et al. 2022; Ivleva et al. 2016; Jiang et  al.  2022, 2021; Johnston et  al.  2023) as the concentration of free $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ is lower in respiring mitochondria (Rozhin et al. 2018), or potentially in non-photosynthesising chloroplasts or plastids (Aznar-Moreno et  al.  2021; Burén and Rubio  2017; Dixon et al. 1997). Both organelles also contain endogenous machineries for FeS cluster assembly that could aid in FeS cluster biosynthesis on nitrogenase proteins (Vicente and Dean 2017). 

Of the three nitrogenase types, previous plant engineering efforts have focussed on either the MoFe- or the Fe-only nitrogenase. The MoFe nitrogenase has the highest specific activity in nitrogen reduction compared to the VFe or Fe-only nitrogenase (Trncik et al. 2022), and its biosynthetic requirements have been studied most extensively (Burén et  al.  2020). An advantage of the Fe-only nitrogenase, however, is that it does not require a hetero-metal for its cofactor, as it is currently unknown whether plant organelles contain sufficient levels of molybdenum or vanadium to meet the requirements of a MoFe- or VFe-nitrogenase, respectively. The Fe-only nitrogenase also needs relatively few accessory proteins for cluster assembly and may thus be an easier target for engineering nitrogen-fixing plants (Yang et al. 2014). 

Previously, we reported that the structural components of the Fe-only nitrogenase from A. vinelandii, AnfDKG and AnfH, can be expressed in plant mitochondria and showed that the subunits of the catalytic unit form a complex (Johnston et al. 2023). 

Despite being successfully expressed in the plant mitochondrial matrix, AnfH was only partially soluble (Johnston et al. 2023). This problem had also been previously identified for other nitrogenase proteins when expressed in eukaryotes (Arragain et  al.  2017; Burén et  al.  2017; Dobrzyńska et  al.  2024; Okada et al. 2020). For example, A. vinelandii NifH was extremely insoluble when targeted to the mitochondrial matrix of Nicotiana benthamiana (Jiang et  al.  2021). In the case of NifH, this was overcome by screening NifH proteins from a variety of diazotrophic organisms to find a soluble variant, which was then used for functional studies. The same approach was also used to address solubility issues for the maturation proteins A. vinelandii NifEN and A. vinelandii NifB when expressed in S. cerevisiae and N. benthamiana, respectively (Burén et  al.  2019; Dobrzyńska et al. 2024; He et al. 2022). 

This report takes a different approach, employing a computational and rational protein design to improve a known variant rather than relying on large-scale variant screening. Specifically, we addressed the solubility issues of AnfH (Johnston et al. 2023) and introduced minimal amino acid changes, thereby substantially enhancing its soluble expression in plant mitochondria. One engineered variant, containing only three amino acid substitutions, was found to be active when isolated from plant mitochondria after reconstitution with $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters. This targeted strategy could be applied to other problematic nitrogenase proteins, representing a critical step toward engineering nitrogen fixation into plants. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Design of AnfH Variants That Are More Soluble in Plant Mitochondria

Given the poor solubility of AnfH when expressed in plant mitochondria, we sought to use a computational approach to design variants with improved soluble expression. The Protein Repair One-Stop Shop (PROSS) algorithm (Goldenzweig et  al.  2016; Weinstein et al. 2021) is designed to generate variants that have a lower free energy in their native state compared to the wildtype protein and are thus predicted to be more stable and soluble when expressed heterologously. The selection of amino acid substitutions is further based on a multiple sequence alignment (MSA), and the algorithm will avoid substitutions that are not naturally occurring within the protein sequence space. Here, we employed this algorithm to generate variants of A. vinelandii AnfH better suited for expression in plant mitochondria (Figure 1A). The crystal structure of A. vinelandii AnfH (PDB 7QQA) (Trncik et al. 2022) was used as an input model, and residues for the P-loop, switch I, switch II, and cysteines involved in $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster binding were excluded from the list of residues allowed for substitution. Furthermore, 112 residues were manually selected and excluded from substitution (Table S1).

# 2.2 | Purification of AnfH Variants From Plant Mitochondria

Currently there is no method that allows an in vivo characterisation of AnfH activity in plants. For this reason, the functional characterisation of AnfH expressed in plants necessitates a purification step. Of the variants that were tested for solubility, we chose AnfH V6 and AnfH V7 for purification and further analysis, as these had the fewest amino acid substitutions. 

AnfH wt, AnfH V6 and AnfH V7 were transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaf, containing a cytochrome oxidase IV 

(CoxIV) mitochondrial targeting peptide from S. cerevisiae (Burén et al. 2017; Köhler et al. 1997) and a TwinStrep-tag for purification, which were translationally fused at the N-terminus of each AnfH (CoxIV::TwinStrep::AnfH, SN771 for AnfH wt, SN709 for AnfH V6; SN710 for AnfH V7, Table S3). All AnfH constructs were co-expressed with A. vinelandii NifS and NifU, which are thought to be involved in $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster maturation of NifH and may play an equivalent role for AnfH maturation (Dos Santos et al. 2004; Jacobson et al. 1989; Kennedy and Dean 1992). NifM was not included since, in contrast to NifH, AnfH does not require NifM for its maturation (Yang et  al.  2014). From here on, purified proteins from N. benthamiana will be referred to as NbAnfH wt, NbAnfH V6 etc., to indicate their expression host. 

When we attempted to purify NbAnfH wt expressed in N. benthamiana leaf mitochondria, yields were low (approx. $0 . 4 \mathrm { { m g } \cdot \mathrm { { k g } ^ { - 1 } } }$ leaf) and the purity was only approx. $2 2 \%$ (Figure 2, densitometric analysis in Figure S5). In contrast, NbAnfH V6 and NbAnfH V7 were successfully purified from N. benthamiana leaf mitochondria with yields of $8 { \mathrm { - } } 1 3 \mathrm { m g { \cdot } k g ^ { - 1 } }$ leaf and $4 { - } 6 \mathrm { m g { \cdot } k g ^ { - 1 } }$ leaf, respectively. The purity of the eluates was also higher, $6 1 \%$ and $4 9 \%$ , respectively (Figure 2, densitometric analysis in Figure S5). Overall, this corresponds to an approximately 90-fold improvement in AnfH V6 abundance. 

Based on these promising results, we wanted to investigate whether the number of amino acid substitutions could be further reduced. Another AnfH variant, AnfH V8, was generated containing only one amino acid substitution, T228V, and a construct was designed for purification (CoxIV::TwinStrep::AnfH-V8, SN778, Table S3). While the soluble expression and abundance of this variant were improved compared to NbAnfH wt, NbAnfH V8 was less abundant compared to NbAnfH V6 and NbAnfH V7, with a yield of approx. $4 . 7 \mathrm { m g \cdot k g ^ { 1 } }$ leaf and $2 8 \%$ purity (Figure 2, densitometric analysis in Figure  S5). # 2.3 | Reconstituted NbAnfH V6 Is Active in Acetylene Reduction with NifDK

To determine whether NbAnfH V6 and NbAnfH V7 were active after purification from plant mitochondria an acetylene reduction assay (ARA) was performed. In this assay, we used NifDK purified from A. vinelandii, AvNifDK, as the catalytic unit, as it has comparatively robust properties and has previously been proven to be functional in ARAs with ScAnfH isolated from S. cerevisiae (López-Torrejón et  al.  2021). However, we were not able to detect any activity for NbAnfH V6 and NbAnfH V7 ‘as isolated’ from plant mitochondria. Since the concentrated eluates from the purification were mostly colourless instead of having the typical brown colour associated with FeS cluster proteins (Figure S6), we speculated that one possible explanation for the lack of activity of the NbAnfH variants could be the absence of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster in NbAnfH. 

To assess whether NbAnfH activity could be restored upon insertion of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster, a NifU reconstitution procedure was carried out. While the mechanism of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster donation to Nif and Anf proteins in diazotrophs is not completely understood, it has been shown that apoNifH can be activated by incubation with NifU in  vitro (Dos Santos et al. 2004). We carried out a NifU reconstitution procedure to test whether NbAnfH V6 and NbAnfH V7 could be activated. The NifU used for this procedure was A. vinelandii NifU purified from E. coli, EcNifU, (Figure S7) which was chemically reconstituted with $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 2 - }$ to increase cluster loading. Prior 

to the AnfH reconstitution with EcNifU, dithionite (DTH) in the AnfH samples was removed as DTH has been shown to destabilise the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters of Nif U (Smith et  al.  2005). Encouragingly, the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster reconstitution by EcNifU was successful in activating NbAnfH V6 (Figure  3A). This was observed in several experiments; however, some variation regarding the activity levels was observed (Figure S8). In contrast, reconstituted NbAnfH V7 was not active or had extremely low activity $1 \%$ of the positive control). In summary, NbAnfH V6 was isolated from plant mitochondria as apo-AnfH, as it was capable of being reconstituted with $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters from EcNifU.

# 3 | Discussion

Here, we present a new strategy to improve bacterial nitrogenase proteins for successful expression in plant mitochondria. Specifically, we used a computationally guided design approach to generate a novel AnfH variant, containing only three amino acid changes, that is more soluble and abundant in plant mitochondria and functional in nitrogen reduction with AnfDKG. This approach leverages well-studied enzyme systems, such as those from A. vinelandii, as the basis for engineering nitrogenase into plants, rather than searching for naturally occurring variants from non-model diazotrophs that are not as well characterised (Arragain et  al.  2017; Burén et  al.  2017, 2019; Dobrzyńska et al. 2024; He et al. 2022; Jiang et al. 2021). This approach could be particularly well suited for cases where there is scarce phylogenetic diversity. The Fe-only nitrogenase is less common and only found in approximately $5 \% - 1 0 \%$ of genomes that contain a MoFe nitrogenase (Boyd and Peters 2013; Harwood 2020; Koirala and Brözel 2021; Pi et al. 2022). This limits the number of potential candidates when testing naturally occurring variants for expression in plants. Moreover, screens of natural variants for their performance in plants have shown that proteins from thermophilic organisms seem to be better suited for soluble expression in plants and, to our knowledge, the Fe-only nitrogenase is not present in thermophilic diazotrophs. The design of more stable and soluble proteins by lowering their free energy may mimic the higher stability characteristics of thermophilic proteins, when natural equivalents are potentially unavailable. Using targeted amino acid changes is thus a valuable addition to the nitrogenase engineering toolkit, particularly for the less diverse Fe-only nitrogenase. 

The solubility of heterologously expressed proteins is dependent on many factors, such as the expression host, the expression temperature, the requirement for specific chaperons or maturation proteins, and the intrinsic properties of the protein itself. In this study, we optimised AnfH for heterologous expression by improving its predicted stability. How exactly the amino acid substitutions contribute to the improved stability of AnfH, and how lowering the free energy of the native state may affect the folding/unfolding pathways, is beyond the scope of the current study. However, in practical terms, the improved abundance in the soluble fraction may be due to reducing the accumulation of misfolded protein in the insoluble fraction and by lowering the accessibility of the protein to proteolytic degradation upon unfolding. Remarkably, only one amino acid substitution, T228V, led to a substantial improvement in the soluble expression of AnfH. Overall, however, we found that the optimal balance between minimising amino acid substitutions and improved soluble expression and abundance was provided by three amino acid substitutions, i.e., AnfH V6 was functional while being approx. 90-fold more abundant than AnfH wt. 

Here, we have used PROSS and Rosetta to improve the soluble expression of AnfH. Nonetheless, it should be noted that other 

software tools for enhancing protein solubility and stability, such as CamSol (Oeller et al. 2023), Aggrescan (Kuriata et al. 2019) or FireProt (Musil et  al.  2023), are available and their use might have resulted in similar enhancements to the soluble expression of AnfH. Recently, also deep learning-based techniques have emerged, capable of predicting sequences for a desired protein fold (ProteinMPNN), (Dauparas et al. 2022), which could be applied to future attempts of improving the soluble expression of proteins (Schmidt et  al.  2024). Regarding the choice of amino acid substitutions, PROSS suggested changes to Lys69 and Asn112. In retrospect, however, we should have likely excluded these residues from the possible substitutions as these have been implicated in the interaction between AnfH and AnfG (Schmidt et al. 2024; Trncik et al. 2022). 

Of the two variants that were tested for function after in vitro reconstitution with EcNifU, NbAnfH V6 was more active than NbAnfH V7. This was remarkable as they only differed by one amino acid substitution, i.e., E234H and G241R, respectively, both of which are distal to the AnfDKG interaction interface. While substitutions in distal sites can affect function in unexpected ways (Wilding et al. 2019), another possible explanation for the low activity of NbAnfH V7 could be that the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ -cluster reconstitution procedure with EcNifU was not as successful for NbAnfH V7. NbAnfH wt isolated from plant mitochondria was not reconstitutable with NifU. Possible reasons for the unsuccessful reconstitution of AnfH wt with NifU could be an incorrect protein fold, a low stability of AnfH under the buffer conditions tested and/or the low purity of the sample. In summary, these experiments showed that the amino acid substitutions of AnfH V6, T200A, T228V and E234H, do not interfere with in vitro function and AnfH V6 has superior properties related to soluble expression and stability compared to both AnfH wt and AnfH V7. 

NbAnfH V6 was not active as-isolated when purified from plants, and this lack of activity is likely due to the lack of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster on the enzyme. These results differ from those observed in S. cerevisiae, from which AnfH wt was isolated in an active form containing $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters (López-Torrejón et al. 2021). This is in line with other studies, where FeS cluster assembly on NifB or NifH was successful in S. cerevisiae, but the same protein accumulated with either no or only a small fraction of FeS clusters in plant mitochondria (Baysal et  al.  2022; Burén et al. 2019; Jiang et al. 2022, 2021). 

When comparing activity levels of proteins expressed in plant mitochondria, however, it is noteworthy that a NifH protein, i.e. NifH from H. thermophilus, was previously isolated with $2 \% - 4 \%$ activity, containing a small amount of $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters (Baysal et  al.  2022; Jiang et  al.  2021) while NbAnfH V6 was not active as-isolated. There are several possible explanations for the differences observed. Firstly, due to the higher oxygen sensitivity of AnfH compared to NifH (Chisnell et al. 1988), the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster of AnfH could be damaged inside plant mitochondria or it could be degraded during the purification procedure. Secondly, AnfH V6 might never have accumulated $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters inside plant mitochondria, despite coexpression of NifS and NifU. It should be noted that it is currently unknown how cluster assembly proceeded for H. thermophilus NifH. It is unlikely that NifS and NifU were actively involved in this process, as no protein accumulation 

of NifSU was detected in rice callus, despite the presence of mRNA (Baysal et al. 2022). Thus, the endogenous iron-sulfur cluster machinery of plant mitochondria was likely involved in the assembly of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster on H. thermophilus NifH. Likewise, in mitochondria of S. cerevisiae, NifS and NifU were not required for the expression of a functional AnfH or NifH (López-Torrejón et al. 2016, 2021) and in E. coli IscS and IscU are possibly sufficient to assemble the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster on NifH (Dos Santos et  al.  2007; Howard et al. 1986; Solomon et al. 2023; Zheng et al. 1998), demonstrating that maturation proteins other than NifSU can donate $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ clusters to a NifH or AnfH protein. It is unclear then why neither NifSU nor the endogenous FeS cluster assembly machinery was able to donate a $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster to AnfH V6 in plant mitochondria. Thirdly, the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster maturation on AnfH may require additional proteins. For example, AnfU has been proposed to be involved in the maturation of the Fe-only nitrogenase (Setubal et al. 2009). Lastly, there is a chance that the amino acid substitutions of AnfH V6 affected the maturation of the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster in vivo. While we were able to donate a $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster from NifU to AnfH in vitro, we cannot rule out that the amino acid substitutions of AnfH V6 could affect the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ cluster binding affinity and/or the interaction of AnfH V6 with other assembly proteins. Further studies will need to investigate the $[ \mathrm { F e } _ { 4 } \mathrm { S } _ { 4 } ]$ maturation requirements of AnfH and, more broadly, focus on improving FeS cluster loading on Nif and Anf proteins, which remains a key challenge to the expression of an active nitrogenase in plants. 

This study has shown that a computationally guided design approach can suggest relatively few changes to a critical nitrogenase protein, resulting in improved soluble expression and abundance in plants, while retaining function. This approach can be applied more broadly to problematic proteins of nitrogenase pathways from well-studied enzymes so that they can be successfully expressed in plants. This study, like others, has also shown that the loading of FeS clusters onto nitrogenase proteins remains a key challenge for the successful expression of an active enzyme in plants. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Bioinformatic Methods

PROSS (Goldenzweig et al. 2016; Weinstein et al. 2021) was used to generate the initial protein designs. The crystal structure of A. vinelandii AnfH (PDB 7QQA) (Trncik et  al.  2022) was used as input model and 137 residues were excluded from amino acid substitutions (Table  S1). PROSS was run with the standard settings for the MSA. The ROSIE webserver (Lyskov et al. 2013) was used to calculate the Rosetta energy values. Sequence logos were generated with Seq2Logo (Thomsen and Nielsen  2012) without clustering and pseudo count correction. The sequence alignment of the InterPro family ‘nitrogenase Fe protein’ (IPR005977) was generated using MAFFT (Katoh et al. 2002, 2017). AlphaFold 3 (Abramson et al. 2024) was used to generate a structural model of AnfH V6 containing ADP and $\mathbf { M } \mathbf { g } ^ { 2 + }$ . 

# 4.2 | Molecular Cloning and Construct Design

Vectors for plant expression were assembled using the GoldenGate cloning method (Weber et  al.  2011) and parts 

were based on Engler et al. (Engler et al. 2014). Sequences for open reading frames and other non-standard parts were plant codon-optimised (N. benthamiana or Arabidopsis thaliana) and ordered from Twist Bioscience or Geneart. Generally, a Cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) enhanced 35S promoter and 35S terminator were used. For AnfH wt, an additional construct was prepared using a combination of three different termination signals based on Diamos et  al. (Diamos and Mason 2018). These were the intronless tobacco extensin terminator (EU), the 35S terminator and a shortened Rb7 matrix attachment region (MAR) (Δ1-730) (modMARS). Mitochondrial targeting peptides and tags used for AnfH constructs are described in the main text. NifS contained a chaperonin HSP60 (CPN60) mitochondrial targeting peptide from A. thaliana (Prasad and Stewart 1992), CPN60::NifS, and NifU contained the $\mathrm { F } _ { 0 }$ -ATPase subunit 9 (Su9) from Neurospora crassa mitochondrial targeting peptide, Su9::NifU (Burén et al. 2017; Jiang et  al.  2022). NifS and NifU were expressed from a multigene vector (SL133, Tables S3 and S5). A sequence list of all parts and expression vectors can be found in Dataset S1. 

# 4.3 | Growth and Transient Transformation of N. Benthamiana

N. benthamiana plants were grown in a growth chamber (Conviron) at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a $1 6 { : } 8 \mathrm { h }$ light: dark cycle with light provided by a combination of cool white fluorescent lamps and incandescent bulbs. Plants were grown in vermiculite (grade 3, Ausperl) and watered with 10 mL · L−1 HyperGrow GP nutrient solution. The plants were agroinfiltrated when they were 5 weeks old. 

Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 containing SN/SL vectors or the p19 vector were grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in Luria-Bertani (LB) broth supplemented with $5 0 \mathrm { m g } { \cdot } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ rifampicin, ${ 5 0 } \mathrm { m g } { \cdot } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ gentamycin and the corresponding antibiotic according to the selectable marker on the vector $( 5 0 \mathrm { m g \cdot m L ^ { - 1 } }$ carbenicillin or $5 0 \mathrm { m g } { \cdot } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin). After $2 0 \mathrm { h }$ , acetosyringone was added to the culture to a final concentration of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and the culture was incubated for another $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . The culture was then centrifuged at $5 0 0 0 \times g$ for 10 min at room temperature. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in a solution containing 10 mM MES pH 5.7, 5 mM MgCl and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone (infiltration buffer), after which the optical density at a wavelength of $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ , was measured. A volume of each culture, including the culture containing the viral-suppressor construct p19, required to reach a final concentration of $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 1 0$ , was added to a fresh tube. A. tumefaciens were infiltrated into the leaves of N. benthamiana plants using a 1 mL plastic syringe. Leaves or leaf spots were harvested 4 days after infiltration. Large scale infiltrations were harvested in the dark, i.e., after a prolonged dark period of $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ , under dim green light. 

# 4.4 | Solubility Analysis of AnfH Variants Expressed in the Mitochondria of N. Benthamiana

The solubility analysis was carried out as previously described (Okada et al. 2020) with minor changes. Frozen leaf disks were 

ground in $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ solubility buffer $( 5 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, $1 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, 100 mM Tris pH 8.0, $1 \%$ (w/v) 1-ethenylpyrrolidin-2-one (PVP), $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ phenylmethanesulfonylfluoride (PMSF), $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ leupeptin). From this suspension, $4 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ was taken as the ‘total’ sample, and $6 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ was used for further processing. The latter was then centrifuged for 10 min at $1 6 0 0 0 \times g$ at $1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . $4 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the supernatant was transferred to a new microcentrifuge tube, referred to as the ‘soluble’ fraction. Subsequent sodium dodecyl sulfate—polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and western blotting were carried out as previously described (Allen et al. 2020).

Up to $1 5 0 \mathrm { g }$ of frozen leaf tissue expressing NbAnfH wt, NbAnfH V6, NbAnfH V7 or NbAnfH V8 was ground in lysis buffer $\mathrm { 1 0 0 m M }$ Tris–HCl pH 8.6, 200 mM NaCl, $1 0 \%$ v/v glycerol, $2 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium dithionite (DTH), $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ PMSF, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ leupeptin) with a NutriBullet blender, filtered through miracloth (Merck Millipore) and centrifuged at $1 4 2 4 0 0 \times g$ for $1 \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The protein solution was concentrated using an AmiconUltra centrifugal filter (30 kDa MWCO) and stored in liquid nitrogen until use.

The pellets were frozen in liquid $\Nu _ { 2 }$ and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until further use. 

# 4.9 | Purification of EcNifU From E. coli

The purification of EcNifU was performed in a COY anaerobic chamber in a $9 7 \% { - } 9 8 \% \mathrm { N } _ { 2 } / 2 \% { - } 3 \% \mathrm { H } _ { 2 }$ atmosphere with an ÄKTA pure instrument. The cell pellet was resuspended in $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl pH7.4, 200mM NaCl, $5 \%$ glycerol, 2mM TCEP (buffer A) with $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ leupeptin, $0 . 2 \mathrm { m M }$ PMSF and $2 5 \mathrm { m g / L }$ lysozyme. The suspension was sonicated for $3 \times 3 \mathrm { m i n }$ (Hielscher sonicator UP200Ht) at $5 0 \%$ cycle and $5 0 \%$ amplitude, and then centrifuged at $1 4 2 4 0 0 \times g$ in a polycarbonate bottle with cap assembly (Beckman Coulter). The supernatant was loaded onto a Strep-TactinXT (IBA) column equilibrated in buffer A, washed with 10 CVs buffer A and then eluted in buffer A with $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ biotin. The eluate was concentrated using an Amicon Ultra centrifugal filter (30 MWCO) and frozen in liquid nitrogen until further use. 

# 4.10 | Chemical Reconstitution of EcNifU

EcNifU was chemically reconstituted using a similar procedure as previously described (López-Torrejón et  al.  2016; Marengo et al. 2021). EcNifU was diluted in $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris pH 7.4, $1 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, $2 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ dithiothreitol (DTT) to a concentration of $1 { - } 5 \mathrm { { m g } { \cdot } \mathrm { { m L } ^ { - 1 } } }$ . Then, a solution of $4 0 \mathrm { m M }$ $\left( \mathrm { N H } _ { 4 } \right) _ { 2 } \mathrm { F e } ( \mathrm { S O } _ { 4 } ) _ { 2 }$ was 

added to a final concentration of $2 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ . Then 40 mM $\mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } \mathrm { S }$ was added to a final concentration of $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ with gentle constant mixing. A colour change was observed within a few minutes of addition of $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 2 - }$ . The reaction was incubated overnight in the anaerobic chamber at approximately $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The next day, the buffer was exchanged to Mg-ATP buffer using a MiniTrap Sephadex G25 column to remove excess $\mathrm { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ and $\mathrm { S } ^ { 2 - }$ . Mg-ATP buffer was prepared by dissolving ${ 1 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 } }$ and $2 \mathrm { m M }$ ATP in $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris $\mathrm { p H } 7 . 4$ , which was followed by a pH adjustment by addition of 1/10 volume 1 M NaOH, resulting in $\mathrm { p H } 7 . 8$ . The protein solution was concentrated using an AmiconUltra centrifugal filter (30 kDa MWCO). 

# 4.11 | Reconstitution of AnfH Proteins With EcNifU

Buffers for the reconstitution were prepared as follows: Prior to use, all buffers and solutions used were tested for $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ contamination using electrochemically reduced methyl viologen. Mg-ATP buffer was prepared by dissolving 10 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ and 5 mM ATP in $2 5 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { M O P S } \mathrm { p H } 7 . 0$ , which was followed by a pH adjustment by addition of 1/50 volume 1 M NaOH, resulting in pH 7.7. 

To remove interfering compounds such as DTH and biotin, the elution buffer of AnfH was exchanged to Mg-ATP buffer using a centrifugal filter (Amicon Ultra, $\mathrm { 1 0 k D a }$ or 30 kDa MWCO). This procedure was carried out twice and the minimum dilution achieved for DTH and biotin was 200-fold. Chemically reconstituted EcNifU was added to the different AnfH proteins at a ratio of 2:1 and incubated overnight at approximately $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 4.12 | Acetylene Reduction Assay

The acetylene reduction assay (ARA) was carried out using $4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ AnfH and $0 . 2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ AvNifDK, a molar ratio of 20:1 AnfH:AvNifDK. If EcNifU was present, it was added as part of the AnfH-NifU reconstitution mixture at a final concentration of $8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , a ratio of 40:20:1 EcNifU:AnfH:AvNifDK. Protein concentrations were determined using the bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay (Smith et  al.  1985). The concentration of AnfH purified from N. benthamiana was adjusted based on a densitometric analysis of the Coomassie gel as it contained contaminating plant proteins (Figure S5). 

The assays were set up in a reaction volume of $3 7 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ in $3 . 7 \mathrm { m L }$ exetainer vials (Labco). The final concentrations of reducing agent and ATP regeneration mixture were 6 mM DTH, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 5 mM ATP, $1 6 \mathrm { m M }$ phosphocreatine, and $0 . 1 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { \cdot m L ^ { - 1 } }$ phosphocreatine kinase from rabbit muscle. The reaction was started by addition of 1 mL acetylene gas $( 2 7 \% \ \mathrm { v / v ) }$ to the headspace. The acetylene gas was instrument grade (Coregas) and stored in a serum septa bottle containing buffer with $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ DTH to minimise potential $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ contamination from the acetylene. After injection of acetylene, the samples were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The reaction was stopped by addition of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ 8 M NaOH. 

Ethylene production was measured by gas chromatography with flame ionisation detection (GC-FID) using an Agilent 

6890 N GC instrument. A volume of $0 . 3 7 \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ of the headspace was removed using a gas-tight syringe (VICI Precision Sampling) and manually injected. The instrument settings were as described previously (Allen et al. 2020). The amount of ethylene was calculated based on a standard curve that was prepared using six levels of ethylene as described previously (Bilyj et al. 2024). 

# 4.13 | Nitrogen Reduction Assay

The reactions were set up as described for the acetylene reduction assay except that 3 mL exetainers (Labco) were used and the final volume was $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ gas was $9 8 \% +$ pure (Cambridge Isotope Laboratories, Lot #: I-26354) and potential oxygen contamination was removed by bubbling the gas through $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ DTH with methyl viologen as indicator using a syringe and a turret (Figure S10) (Wood and Kennedy 2001). 1mL of $^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ gas was added to each of the samples and the samples were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 1 h. The reactions were stopped by adding $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 1 M HCl. $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the reaction mix was dried in a tin capsule with approx. $8 \mathrm { m g }$ inert chromosorb (Sercon) for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ in a desiccator at room temperature. The samples were then weighed and analysed using a Europa 20–20 Stable Isotope Ratio Mass-spectrometer (IRMS) electronics rebuilt by Sercon with an Automated Nitrogen Carbon Analysis for Solids and Liquids (ANCA-SL) preparation system. The system consists of a preparation unit (ANCA-SL) comprising a combustion tube operated at $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a reduction tube at $6 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . $\delta ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { { N } }$ values were calculated as per the method of Montoya et al. (Montoya et al. 1996).
