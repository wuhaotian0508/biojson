# A plastid terminal oxidase comes to light: implications for carotenoid biosynthesis and chlororespiration

# Pierre Carol and Marcel Kuntz

Inactivation of a plastid located quinone–oxygen oxidoreductase gene in the immutans Arabidopsis mutant leads to a photobleached phenotype because of a lack of photoprotective carotenoids. Inactivation of the corresponding gene in the ghosttomato mutant leads to a similar phenotype in leaves and to carotenoid deficiency in petals and ripe fruits. This plastid terminal oxidase (the first to be cloned and biochemically characterized) resembles the mitochondrial cyanide-insensitive alternative oxidase. Here, we propose a model integrating this novel oxidase as a component of an electron transport chain associated to carotenoid desaturation, as well as to a respiratory activity within plastids.

Since the early suggestion by J.C. Goedheer1 of the existence of an electron flow from a chloroplastic interphotosystem carrier to $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , the evidence has suggested that the plastoquinone pool is an intermediate in a chlororespiratory pathway to $\mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ under dark conditions in algae2–5 and higher plants6–11. Various physiological functions have been proposed for chlororespiration, such as ATP production in the dark, reoxidation of reducing equivalents generated during starch degradation in the dark4,6 and acclimation to extreme environmental conditions10.

In spite of the accumulation of valuable data for almost four decades, the molecular identity of a plastid-located terminal oxidase remained elusive until recently. Not surprisingly, the breakthrough came from the characterization of an Arabidopsis mutant. This mutant (immutans) had long been known to be impaired in chloroplast biogenesis and, as more recent studies suggested, in carotenoid biosynthesis12. Unexpectedly, identification of the IMMUTANS locus by transposon tagging13 and chromosome walking14, and further biochemical characterization showed that it actually encodes a plastidic terminal oxidase whose first identified function is linked to carotene desaturation15.

# Carotenoid desaturation and its requirement for cofactors

Carotenoids are pigments that belong to the terpenoid family and are synthesized by plant and algal plastids, and by some fungi and bacteria16. In plants, carotenoids are fully synthesized within plastids17 either in chloroplasts, in which they are incorporated into photosynthetic complexes, or in chromoplasts, which are found in some fruits and flowers. Carotenoids have well known biological

properties that, in chloroplasts, include light harvesting and protection against photo-oxidation. Carotenoids can be further metabolized by oxidative cleavage, for instance to abscisic acid in plant cells and to vitamin A (retinol) in animal cells. The genetic manipulation of carotenoid biosynthesis is reviewed on pages 14–17 of this issue.

Carotenoid molecules are derived from phytoene, a $\mathbf { C } _ { 4 0 }$ acyclic precursor, which itself is made of $\mathrm { C } _ { 5 }$ building blocks (isopentenyl diphosphate and its isomer dimethyl allyl diphosphate) via a $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 0 }$ intermediate, geranylgeranyl diphosphate (Fig. 1). Upon its conversion to carotenoid, phytoene is subjected to four consecutive desaturation (dehydrogenation) reactions, which lead to the formation of lycopene. Lycopene is the main pigment that accumulates in red tomato fruits but, in most other plant tissues, lycopene is further modified into carotenoid molecules with cyclic end groups, such as α or $\beta$ carotene, which can in turn be converted into molecules containing oxygen moieties (the xanthophylls).

Thus, an extraordinary diversity of molecules is synthesized in plant cells. Their specific spectral and biological properties depend on the presence of a system of conjugated double bonds (possibly conjugated to oxygen-containing moieties). The enzymes that catalyse the synthesis of a fully desaturated molecule are now well known: in plants and cyanobacteria, phytoene desaturation to $\zeta$ carotene via phytofluene is catalysed by phytoene desaturase (PDS)18, and ζ carotene desaturation to lycopene via neurosporene is catalysed by ζ carotene desaturase (ZDS) 19. These enzymes catalyse similar reactions and share significant amino acid sequence similarity.

Although the plant enzymes are active in a heterologous environment (e.g. when produced in E. coli cells20), they require several cofactors in plastids. The first evidence for a cofactor requirement was obtained using daffodil (Narcissus pseudonarcissus) flower chromoplasts, in which it was shown that a redox mediator, acting between the desaturase and $\mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ (Ref. 21), and quinones are required22,23. Additional evidence for a quinone requirement is that Arabidopsis mutants that are defective in quinone biosynthesis are also defective in carotenoid production and accumulate phytoene24.

# Arabidopsis IMMUTANS locus encodes a plastid terminal oxidase

The study of a phytoene-accumulating Arabidopsis mutant called immutans has allowed the identification of another component of the redox chain associated with PDS (Refs 13,14). The immutans mutant has a variegated white and green phenotype (Fig. 2); the white sectors contain little carotenoid and accumulate phytoene12. It was concluded that white tissues are impaired in carotenoid biosynthesis, whereas green sectors are phenotypically wild type12. In fact, white sectors are photobleached as a consequence of reduced PDS activity in greening tissues, which leads to a deficit in carotenoid content. This in turn causes a poor protection of chlorophylls against excess light. This is reminiscent of PDS inactivation by bleaching herbicides such as norflurazon25. However, the immutans phenotype is

Earlier genetic data indicated that, although phytoene accumulates in immutans, the mutated locus is distinct from PDS (Ref. 12). In fact, the IMMUTANS gene product shows a moderate amino acid sequence similarity and a comparable location of putative hydrophobic domains and putative iron binding residues (Fig. 3) to that of the membraneassociated mitochondrial alternative oxidases26 (AOX). However, the IMMUTANS protein is synthesized as a precursor that is transported into chloroplasts13. A phylogenetic analysis (Fig. 3) clearly shows that IMMUTANS-type plastid terminal oxidases (PTOX) from various plants form a subfamily that is distinct from mitochondrial AOXs from plants and fungi. The existence of separate groups of chloroplast and mitochondrial oxidases might suggest that this separation pre-dates the appearance of the plant lineage. One explanation is that the genes are of prokaryotic origin, although no known prokaryote seems to possess a gene whose products show any resemblance to AOX or PTOX. Alternatively, it is possible that evolutionary constraints favoured the divergence of the chloroplast and mitochondrial sequences from a common ancestral gene.

Interestingly, a model for a redox chain in the chloroplast envelope membrane27, of unknown function, could be compatible with this model. In this case, single electron transfer would occur in vivo, yielding semiquinone or flavosemiquinone intermediates. Although not presented in Figure 4, such intermediates might be mechanistically linked to the desaturation reactions.

Importance of PTOX during development It is currently unclear whether, depending on the photosynthetic or non-photosynthetic nature of the plastids, some components that are shown in Figure 4 are replaced by others. In daffodil flowers, carotenoid desaturation in chromoplasts depends on a ‘chromorespiratory’ chain and it has been shown that electrons can be transferred from NAD(P)H to quinones, a reaction that involves the 23-kDa oxygen-evolving-complex protein28. As far as PDS, ZDS and PTOX are concerned, a single gene product operates in both chromoplasts and at an early stage of plastid differentiation from proplastid to chloroplasts15.

Arabidopsis seedlings were germinated in the dark to differentiate etioplasts (etiolated nonphotosynthetic form of plastids that, nevertheless, accumulate carotenoids). Although the immutans seedlings accumulate carotenoids, they do so at a slower rate than in the wild type (Fig. 5). Under natural light conditions, this is a crucial step for young soil-emerging seedlings because, at this stage, carotenoid accumulation is needed to protect the newly synthesized chlorophyll from photooxidation (Fig. 2). At later plastid differentiation stages, and especially once chloroplasts have developed (under low light intensities), PTOX still acts as a catalyst for carotenoid desaturation but is not crucial (Figs 2 and 5). This suggests that there might be an alternative mechanism for quinone reoxidation. Potential alternatives to PTOX (Fig. 4) are discussed below in a broader context (chlororespiration) but it is currently unknown which of them is associated with carotenoid desaturation. The photosynthetic electron transport chain could be a good candidate in light-grown seedlings, but obviously not in dark-grown seedlings. PTOX is also important when new leaves are formed (when proplastids differentiate into chloroplasts) under natural light intensities (Fig. 2).

The importance of PTOX in carotenoid production by chromoplasts in fruits and flowers was assessed using tomato. The tomato GHOST locus29, which was identified recently15, is a homologue of IMMUTANS. Possible involvement of PTOX in chlororespiration Pierre Bennoun proposed ‘the existence in the thylakoid membrane of an electron transfer chain oxidizing NAD(P)H at the expense of oxygen and which shares at least one redox carrier, plastoquinone, with the photosynthetic electron transport chain’4. Although there is additional evidence for such a chlororespiratory mechanism5–10,30, these data did not identify the molecular components involved. This picture was complicated further by the demonstration of mitochondrion–plastid redox interactions31. More recently, however, plastid gene knockout experiments32–35 concluded that the plastid ndh genes do have a respiratory function, namely the reduction of the plastoquinone pool using stromal reductant. However, this NDH complex is not the only such agent, because there is evidence for a different NAD(P)H–plastoquinone oxidoreductase (NDH-2) in higher plant plastids36. In addition, there is also a ferredoxin–quinone reductase (FQR) that participates in plastoquinone reduction in the dark37. A glycolate oxidase that can transfer electrons to a quinone has also been described but this process apparently depends on photosystem I (PSI) activity and is therefore different from chlororespiration38.

Until recently, the nature of the electron carriers involved in the $\mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ -dependent plastoquinol reoxidation remained scarcely documented in chloroplasts, although the involvement of a cyanide- and CO-sensitive oxidase9,11 or a cytochrome $\pmb { b } \pmb { 5 } \pmb { 5 9 }$ was proposed39. By contrast, there is evidence in Chlamydomonas for a cyanideinsensitive plastoquinol oxidase that is closely related to PTOX (in its sensitivity to inhibitors) and operates in the light40. This oxidase reoxidizes the plastoquinone pool reduced by photosystem II (PSII) when PSI or cytochrome $b _ { 6 }$ f are not functional and is responsible in these cases for a plastidic ${ \bf O } _ { 2 }$ uptake of similar amplitude to that of PS-II-driven $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ evolution.

Whether the same polypeptide or distinct (but related) ones are involved in chlororespiration and in carotenoid desaturation remains to be clarified, as does the occurrence of such a mechanism in higher plant chloroplasts. However, the existence of a peroxidase that works in conjunction with the NDH complex and reoxidizes plastoquinone using $\mathrm { { H } } _ { 2 } \mathrm { { O } } _ { 2 }$ as a terminal acceptor has been documented41. This

electron transport chain might operate using $\mathrm { { H } } _ { 2 } \mathrm { { O } } _ { 2 }$ produced by superoxide dismutases (SOD) on ${ \mathbf { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { - }$ generated from $\mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ by the Mehler reaction. These reactions could be regarded as corresponding to the previously proposed cyanide-sensitive chlororespiratory chain (because one chloroplastic SOD is sensitive to cyanide) but there is also evidence for a distinct cyanide-sensitive oxidase9. Thus, several plastoquinol reoxidation pathways might coexist in plastids (Fig. 4).

Taken together, these data suggest that several forms of chlororespiration might exist, possibly linked to various physiological functions or operating at different developmental stages. PTOX, which has the properties needed for being one chlororespiratory component, might operate under conditions of a highly reduced plastoquinone pool and regulate photosynthetic electron flow in addition to its involvement in the synthesis of chloroplastic compounds requiring redox mechanisms involving quinones, such as carotenoids. New components of the chlororespiratory pathway might be identified by screening for Arabidopsis mutants in NDH-dependant electron transfer. Current molecular genetics tools should enable the rapid identification of the genes involved, including their specific functions.

# Impacts of chilling temperatures on photosynthesis in warm-climate plants

Damian J. Allen and Donald R. Ort

Photosynthesis in warm-climate plants is substantially reduced after chilling. Tropical and subtropical species offer the opportunity to study the effects of low temperature on photosynthetic processes undisguised by the myriad of protective responses observed in temperate species. In this article, we highlight the primary components of photosynthesis that are affected by a short chill, in both the dark and the light, and discuss what is known of the mechanisms involved. Recent work implicates impaired redox and circadian regulation among other processes.

Low temperature is a major factor limiting the productivity and geographical distribution of many species, including important agricultural crops. The formation of ice inside plant cells is devastating. Freezetolerant plants have several strategies to reduce the probability of this occurring, even when air temperature drops below zero, including maintaining high intracellular solute concentrations and encouraging ice nucleation outside the cells. These plants also commonly exhibit xerophytic adaptations to survive the reduced water availability within the plant and the soil. Temperatures of $- 5 \mathrm { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ can kill an unhardened winter wheat plant even though it has the genetic capacity to acclimatize, harden and acquire tolerance of freezing down to $- 2 0 \mathrm { { ^ \circ C } }$ (Ref. 1). The cold-hardening mechanisms

conferring freeze tolerance have been described elsewhere2–5 and include changes in lipid composition, increases in active-oxygen-scavenging enzymes, anthocyanin accumulation and altered growth morphology. There is also a recent comprehensive update on freezing stress and acclimation1.

Here, our focus is on chilling, referring to nonfreezing temperatures $( \bf { 0 } - \bf { 1 2 ^ { \circ } C } )$ that are common during the growing season in temperate regions and can substantially compromise plant productivity. Many crops cultivated in temperate climates (e.g. maize, tomato, cucumber and mango) come from tropical and subtropical evolutionary backgrounds. These species apparently lack the genetic information to be or become freeze or even chill tolerant. These thermophilic crops thus offer the opportunity to study the effects of chilling on photosynthesis relatively undisguised by the gamut of protective and other acclimatory responses observed in chilling tolerant species.
