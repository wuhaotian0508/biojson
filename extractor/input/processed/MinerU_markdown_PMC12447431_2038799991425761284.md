# ABSTRACT

Starch content and composition in wheat endosperm are critical determinants of wheat yield and quality. Amylose content is a key parameter used to characterize starch viscosity in wheat flour, which influences pasting time, peak viscosity, and, consequently, the eating quality of flour-based foods. However, the transcriptional regulatory mechanisms governing amylose biosynthesis in wheat remain poorly understood. Here, yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) screening identified a MYB transcription factor, TaMYB44-4D, that binds to the TaWx-7A promoter. Electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs), Y1H assays, and transactivation assays demonstrated that TaMYB44-4D binds to the TaWx-7A promoter and represses its transcriptional activity. Knockout (KO) of TaMYB44-A/B/D simultaneously increases amylose content, total starch content, swelling power, and rapid viscosity analyzer parameters, whereas overexpression of TaMYB44-4D reduces these traits. Our results indicate that TaMYB44 functions as a transcriptional repressor of starch biosynthesis. We further confirmed that TaMYB44 interacts with TaMYB1; similar phenotypes were observed in tamyb44-KO and tamyb1-KO lines, as well as in TaMYB44-4D-overexpression (OE) and TaMYB1-6B-OE lines, suggesting that the two factors act as co-repressors. A single-nucleotide polymorphism (T/C) identified in the TaMYB44-D promoter among various wheat varieties is associated with differential TaMYB44 expression levels. Phenotype–genotype association analysis in a natural wheat population revealed that varieties with the CC haplotype exhibit higher amylose content than those with the TT haplotype. In conclusion, our findings provide important insights into the transcriptional regulation of amylose biosynthesis and offer a genetic framework for the improvement of wheat starch quality. 

Key words: TaMYB44, TaMYB1, amylose content, haplotype, wheat

# INTRODUCTION

Wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) is one of the most important staple crops worldwide, due to its agricultural versatility, ease of storage, nutritional value, and the ability of its flour to create a wide variety of appealing and satisfying foods (Shevkani et al., 2017). Starch, the primary storage carbohydrate in wheat, constitutes approximately $6 0 \% { - } 7 5 \%$ of the grain and $70 \% - 8 0 \%$ of the flour. Starch plays substantial roles in endosperm development; its content and composition are key determinants of wheat yield and quality (James et al., 2003). Chinese-style wheat-based foods, such as oriental noodles, steamed bread, dumplings, and pancakes, are widely consumed in China and other Asian countries (Ullah et al., 2024). The viscosity characteristics of flour and starch ultimately affect the appearance and eating quality of these foods, including softness, smoothness, and chewiness 

(Crosbie et al., 1992; Araki et al., 1999; Bao, 2002; Goesaert et al., 2005; Ma and Baik, 2018). 

Storage starch exists as semi-crystalline, insoluble granules composed of two ${ \bf { \alpha } } _ { \bf { \alpha } } \mathbf { \alpha } _ { \bf { \alpha } }$ -glucose polymers: linear amylose $( 2 0 \% -$ $3 0 \% )$ ) and highly branched amylopectin $( 7 0 \% - 8 0 \% )$ , linked by α-1,4- and α-1,6-glycosidic bonds (Zhu et al., 2020). Amylose content, a key parameter used to characterize starch viscosity in wheat flour, influences pasting time and peak viscosity (PV) (Yamamori and Quynh, 2000). The swelling power of starch granules is inversely correlated with amylose content (Jia et al., 2023). During starch gelatinization, amylose molecules are the first to leach from the granules, and the solution gradually forms a viscous paste with high viscosity. Upon retrogradation, this paste transitions into an elastic, gel-like sediment. Because amylose molecules tend to form regular, ordered structures,

they primarily determine the retrogradation rate (Crosbie, 1991; Schulman et al., 1994). The viscosity of wheat starch and flour is a critical functional property that affects behavior during processing and determines final product quality. Wheat flour with moderately reduced amylose content shows increased PV, swelling power, paste viscosity, and breakdown values, resulting in superior noodle-eating quality (Crosbie, 1991; Miura and Sugawara, 1996; Yamamori and Endo, 1996; Yun et al., 1996). These findings indicate that amylose content greatly influences starch pasting and retrogradation properties, and, consequently, noodle-eating quality. 

The biosynthetic pathway of starch has been well documented. Two key enzymes, ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase) and granule-bound starch synthase I (GBSSI), are responsible for amylose biosynthesis. Amylopectin biosynthesis is more complex and involves the participation of multiple enzymes, including AGPase, starch synthases, starch branching enzymes, and starch debranching enzymes (Hwang et al., 2005; Hannah and James, 2008; Jeon et al., 2010; Tiessen and Padilla-Chacon, 2013; Schreiber et al., 2014). GBSSI is encoded by the Wx gene, and loss of function in this gene leads to mutant grains that contain almost no amylose (Shure et al., 1983). The Wx gene has three homologs—Wx-A1, Wx-B1, and Wx-D1— located on chromosomes 7AS, 4AL, and 7DS, respectively (Yamamori et al., 1994). Loss of function in Wx-B1 causes a more pronounced reduction in amylose content than loss of function in Wx-A1 or Wx-D1 (Miura and Sugawara, 1996; Yamamori and Quynh, 2000). Therefore, the differential expression levels of Wx genes influence amylose content, as well as the composition and structure of grain starch, thus affecting the gelatinization and retrogradation characteristics of starch. 

The expression of enzymes and genes involved in starch biosynthesis is regulated by a series of transcription factors (TFs). Several TFs have been reported to regulate starch biosynthesis in wheat, such as TaRSR1 (Liu et al., 2016), TaSPA-B (Guo et al., 2020), TaNAC019-A1 (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021), TabZIP28 (Song et al., 2020), TaNAC100 (Li et al., 2021), TaB3- 2A1 (Xie et al., 2023), TaJAZ1(Yuan et al., 2023), TaMADS29 (Liu et al., 2023), TaABI3-A1 (Zhao et al., 2024), and TaDL (Liu et al., 2025). 

Among these, TaRSR1 negatively regulates the expression of starch synthesis genes, and its silencing significantly increases grain weight and starch content (Liu et al., 2016). Overexpression of TabZIP28 enhances both the transcription and activity of cytosolic AGPase, thereby increasing total starch content (Song et al., 2020). TaMADS29 regulates the expression of sucrose transport and starch-synthesis-related genes, including TaSWEET11, TaSUS2, TaAGPL1, TaGBSSI, and TaSSI, during early grain development (Liu et al., 2023). TaNAC019 may act as an inhibitor of starch biosynthesis and an activator of storage protein synthesis (Liu et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024). Overexpression of TaNAC100 reduces the contents of low-molecular-weight glutenin subunits, gliadin, and total seed protein, while increasing starch content (Li et al., 2021). Although these TFs related to starch synthesis have been identified, the regulatory mechanisms underlying starch biosynthesis require further investigation. 

The MYB TF family participates in various biological processes, including primary and secondary metabolic pathways, cell morphology and pattern formation, plant growth and development, and responses to biotic and abiotic stresses (Yanhui et al., 2006). Members of the MYB family share a highly conserved DNA-binding domain (the MYB domain), which consists of one to three tandem incomplete repeats of the R motif (R1, R2, and R3). Based on the number and arrangement of these adjacent repeats, the MYB TFs are classified into four subfamilies: 4R-MYB, R1R2R3-MYB, R2R3- MYB, and 1R-MYB (Du et al., 2009; Dubos et al., 2010); the R2R3-MYB subfamily is predominant in plants. MYB44 plays important roles in plant responses to biotic and abiotic stress, such as pathogen infection (Shim et al., 2013), salt and drought stress (Jung et al., 2008; Jaradat et al., 2013) in Arabidopsis thaliana, and high-temperature stress in potatoes (Zhou et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019). Furthermore, MYB44 negatively regulates the synthesis of several compounds, including sucrose in strawberry fruit (Wei et al., 2018); anthocyanin in purple-skinned potato, sweet potato, and grape (Zhou et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020; Leng et al., 2025); and malic acid in apple (Jia et al., 2021). However, the roles of MYB44 in starch biosynthesis in wheat grains remain unclear. 

In this study, to clarify the regulatory mechanisms of $W x$ , we first identified TaMYB44 using a yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay system with a fragment of the Wx promoter as the bait, and then investigated the potential mechanism by which TaMYB44 regulates the expression of starch-biosynthesis-related genes (TaWx-7A, TaBT1-6A, and TaNAC019-3B). We subsequently identified an interacting protein, TaMYB1-6B, using a yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay system. Finally, we identified specific natural variations in the TaMYB44-4D promoter that were associated with increased amylose and starch content. These findings reveal previously unrecognized functions of TaMYB44 and TaMYB1 in amylose synthesis during wheat grain development and identify elite haplotypes associated with starch content that may support the genetic improvement of high-yield, noodle-quality wheat.

# Grain amylose biosynthesis regulation in wheat

We then used the PlantCARE database to search for cisacting elements in this region. We identified a MYB-binding site (MBS), an RY motif, a GARE motif, and other motifs (Supplemental Table 1). To determine which TF regulates TaWx-7A expression, we performed a Y1H assay to screen a wheat grain cDNA library using fragments $\mathtt { - 9 0 0 }$ to − 1200 bp upstream of the start codon as bait. A total of 302 positive colonies were obtained, including six TFs (Supplemental Table 2). Among them, a MYB TF specifically expressed in wheat grains and confirmed by Y1H assay (Supplemental Figure 1C) was selected as the candidate regulator. 

To explore the evolutionary relationship of this MYB TF, its homologs in other species were identified using protein BLAST analysis, and a phylogenetic tree was constructed. The results indicated that this MYB was closely related to the S22 subfamily within the R2R3-MYB family in Arabidopsis. Both possessed the R2R3 MYB domain and the ethylene-responsive element binding factor-associated amphiphilic repression (EAR) domain. Therefore, this TF was named TaMYB44 (Supplemental Figure 2A– 2C). Three TaMYB44 homologs were located on chromosomes 4AS (TraesCS4A02G006100), 4BL (TraesCS4B02G299000), and 4DL (TraesCS4D02G297900), based on Chinese Spring IWGSC RefSeqv2.1 (Zhu et al., 2021). 

To further investigate the role of TaMYB44 in grain filling, we analyzed its expression patterns in various tissues and at multiple stages of grain development. Our results showed that TaMYB44 was specifically expressed in developing seeds, with particularly high expression at 15 days post anthesis (DPA). The TaMYB44 homologs exhibited similar expression patterns, but TaMYB44- 4D showed the highest expression level (Figure 1A). RNA in situ hybridization assays revealed strong TaMYB44-4D transcript signals in the aleurone layer and starchy endosperm, especially in seeds at 15 DPA (Figure 1B). 

To determine the subcellular localization of TaMYB44-4D, we established the p35S-TaMYB44-4D-green fluorescent protein (GFP) construct and transiently expressed it in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaves and wheat leaf protoplasts. The fluorescence signal of the TaMYB44-4D-GFP fusion protein was exclusively detected in the nucleus (Figure 1C), consistent with its role as a TF. 

To evaluate the transactivation activity of TaMYB44-4D, we used both yeast reporter and dual-luciferase reporter (DLR) systems. In the yeast system, the full-length coding sequence (CDS) of TaMYB44-4D was fused to the GAL4 DNA-binding domain (BD). Recombinant and empty GAL4-BD plasmids were cotransformed with pGADT7 into Y2H yeast cells. The yeast strain containing the TaMYB44-4D-BD $^ +$ pGADT7-T construct failed to grow on synthetic dropout (SD)/− Trp/− Leu/− Ade/− His medium (Figure 1D), suggesting that TaMYB44-4D lacks transcriptional activity. Additionally, the CDS of TaMYB44-4D was inserted into the GAL4-DB vector under the control of the 35S promoter as the effector. VP16 (herpes simplex virus 16) was used as a posi-

tive control, and the empty 35S–GAL4-DB vector served as a negative control. When these effectors and the reporter were transiently expressed in tobacco leaves, TaMYB44-4D showed the lowest expression activity compared with the other two groups (Figure 1E). These results indicate that TaMYB44-4D functions as a transcriptional repressor. 

# TaMYB44-4D negatively regulates amylose biosynthesis and starch content in wheat grains, thereby affecting starch viscosity properties

To further determine the in vivo biological function of TaMYB44 in amylose biosynthesis within wheat grains, we generated two homozygous knockout (KO) mutant lines (tamyb44-1 and tamyb44-2) in the spring wheat variety Fielder via CRISPR– Cas9-mediated gene editing (Supplemental Figure 3A). In parallel, we obtained two TaMYB44-4D overexpression (OE) lines (OE10 and OE13) by cloning the CDS of TaMYB44-4D into the vector pWMB110-GFP (Supplemental Figure 3B). Compared with the wild type (WT), starch and amylose contents increased in the tamyb44-1/2 lines, whereas they decreased in the OE10 and OE13 lines (Figure 2A and 2B). Reverse transcription–quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT–qPCR) analysis revealed that the expression level of TaWx-7A was significantly upregulated in the KO lines, whereas it was downregulated in the TaMYB44-4D-OE lines (Figure 2C). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) showed that OE10 and OE13 contained more loosely packed starch granules with relatively large intergranular spaces, smaller A-type starch granules, and fewer B-type starch granules than the WT. In contrast, the KO lines contained larger A-type starch granules and more B-type granules, more densely embedded in a proteinaceous matrix with minimal space (Figure 2D and 2E). 

To analyze starch viscosity during gelatinization and retrogradation, we used a Rapid Visco Analyzer (Newport Scientific, Australia) to assess flour from TaMYB44-4D-OE lines, tamyb44 mutants, and WT. Viscosity parameters in the tamyb44 mutants—including peak viscosity (PV), hold-through (HT), final viscosity (FV), breakdown viscosity (BD), setback viscosity (SB), and gelatinization temperature—were all higher than those in WT, whereas the TaMYB44-4D-OE lines showed the opposite trend (Figure 2F and 2G). We also measured the swelling power of flour from WT, tamyb44 mutants, and TaMYB44-4D-OE lines under various urea concentrations. # TaMMYB44-4D regulates the expression of genes involved in starch metabolism

To better understand the role of TaMYB44 in seed development, we performed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) using 15-DPA grains of Fielder, tamyb44-1, and OE10. Compared with Fielder, tamyb44-1 had a total of 1149 differentially expressed genes (DEGs), including 695 upregulated and 454 downregulated genes $( p < 0 . 0 5$ , ${ \| \mathsf { o g } _ { 2 } }$ fold change $> 1 . 0 )$ , while OE10 had 1503 upregulated and 1851 downregulated genes (Figure 3A; Supplemental Tables 3 and 4). Sixty-six DEGs overlapped between tamyb44-1-upregulated and OE10-downregulated gene sets (Figure 3B). Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis revealed that both tamyb44-1 and OE10 were significantly enriched for genes involved in starch biosynthetic processes. Additionally, DEGs in OE10 were enriched in polysaccharide catabolic and carbohydrate metabolic processes, whereas DEGs in tamyb44-1 were enriched in response to salt stress, response to heat, and ADP-glucose–starch glucosyltransferase activity (Supplemental Figure 6A and 6B). We also examined the expression of several starch-biosynthesisrelated genes and found that most significant DEGs were upregulated in tamyb44-1 but downregulated in OE10 (Figure 3C). These results indicate that TaMYB44 may regulate physiological processes related to starch metabolism during endosperm development. 

To validate the RNA-seq data, we used RT–qPCR to measure the expression of DEGs in tamyb44-1 and OE10. The results were consistent with the RNA-seq data (Figure 3D). For example, TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A (TraesCS6A02G175100) were upregulated in tamyb44-1 and downregulated in TaMYB44- OE10, consistent with the higher starch and amylose contents in tamyb44-1 and lower contents in OE10 (Figure 2A and 2B). 

In summary, TaMYB44 played an important role in regulating the expression of starch-biosynthesis-related genes during grain development. 

# TaMYB44-4D directly represses the expression of TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A but activates TaNAC019-3B via binding to the MBSI motif

In plants, R2R3-MYB proteins can bind to MYB-binding-site motifs MBSI and MBSII in the promoters of downstream genes (Romero et al., 1998). We identified the presence of the MBSI motif in the promoter region of TaBT1-6A using PlantCARE. To 

confirm that TaMYB44-4D binds to the promoters of its target genes, we first performed electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) using purified TaMYB44-4D protein with synthesized biotin-labeled and competitor probes for TaWx-7A and TaBT1- 6A. When protein and biotin probes were combined, a binding band appeared, which was gradually abolished by excess competitor probe. Mutated competitor probes had no effect on the band intensity (Figure 4A and 4B). Next, we performed chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)–qPCR analysis and confirmed that TaMYB44-4D directly binds to the promoters of TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A in vivo (Figure 4D and 4E). Dualluciferase reporter assays further demonstrated that TaMYB44- 4D represses the expression of TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A (Figure 4G–4I). 

TaNAC019-3A (TraesCS3A02G077900) was previously reported to negatively regulate starch synthesis in wheat grains (Liu et al., 2020). TaNAC019-A/B/D shares an expression pattern similar to that of TaMYB44-A/B/D during seed development (Chen et al., 2024). Our RNA-seq data showed that Ta-NAC019-3B (TraesCS3B02G092800) was downregulated in tamyb44-1 but upregulated in OE10 (Supplemental Tables 3 and 4). The MBSI motif was present in the TaNAC019-3B promoter, and we verified that TaMYB44-4D directly binds this promoter both in vitro by EMSA and in vivo by ChIP–qPCR (Figure 4C and 4F). Dual-luciferase reporter assays in N. benthamiana leaves confirmed that TaMYB44-4D activates the expression of TaNAC019-3B (Figure 4G and 4J). 

Taken together, TaMYB44 suppressed the expression of TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A but activated the expression of TaNAC019- 3B, revealing its dual regulatory capacity. 

# TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B physically interacts to coordinate repression of target genes

To elucidate the molecular mechanism underlying TaMYB44- mediated transcriptional regulation, we sought to identify potential interacting proteins of TaMYB44 using a Y2H assay with the above grain cDNA library. We identified 15 proteins as potential interaction partners of TaMYB44-4D (Supplemental Table 5). Among them, we focused on two TFs, TaMYB44-4D itself and TaMYB1-6B (TraesCS6B02G201700). TaMYB1-6B is a nuclearlocalized transcriptional repressor with high expression levels in grains (Supplemental Figure 7A–7E). 

To further validate the physical interaction between TaMYB44- 4D and TaMYB1-6B, we performed a comprehensive set of complementary assays. Initial Y2H analysis demonstrated direct binding between TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B in yeast cells (Figure 5A). To confirm this interaction in planta, we performed bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) and luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assays in N. benthamiana leaf epidermal cells. The results confirmed that TaMYB44-4D interacted with TaMYB1-6B in the nucleus (Figure 5B). Co-expression of nLUC-TaMYB1-6B with cLUC-TaMYB44-4D in N. benthamiana leaves produced strong LUC signals. # TaMYB1 negatively regulates starch biosynthesis in bread wheat

To elucidate the role of TaMYB1 in starch synthesis during wheat grain development, we generated two independent tamyb1 KO lines (tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3) by simultaneously disrupting all three homologous genes using the CRISPR–Cas9 system (Supplemental Figure 9A). In parallel, we obtained two TaMYB1-6B OE lines (OE1 and OE2) by cloning the CDS of TaMYB1-6B into the vector pWMB110-GFP (Supplemental Figure 10A). Compared with the tamyb44 KO lines, the tamyb1-aabbdd triple mutants exhibited similar phenotypes. Starch and amylose contents in tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3 were significantly higher than those of the WT (Figure 6A and 6B). Although grain length remained unchanged, the significant increase in grain width likely contributed to the observed enhancement in grain weight (Supplemental Figure 9B–9E). SEM analysis showed that, compared with WT, tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3 contained larger A-type starch granules and a greater number of B-type granules (Figure 6D and 6E). The viscosity parameters of tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3, including PV, HT, FV, BD, SB, and GT, were all higher than those of the WT (Figure 6F and 6G). Additionally, we measured the solubility of starch isolated from WT and tamyb1-2/3 grains under various urea concentrations. Compared with WT, tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3 displayed superior gelatinization performance (Figure 6H and Supplemental Figure 4B). Regarding agronomic traits, both tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3 showed increased plant height, longer spike length, and a greater number of spikelets compared with WT (Supplemental Figure 9F– 9J). We also investigated the phenotypes of TaMYB1 single and double mutants (tamyb1-aa, tamyb1-aabb, and tamyb1- bbdd). Only tamyb1-bbdd exhibited significantly wider grain width, increased grain weight, taller plants, longer spikes, and more spikelets compared with WT, suggesting that TaMYB1- 6B and TaMYB1-6D had stronger effects than TaMYB1-6A (Supplemental Figure 11A–11I). Moreover, the seed-related phenotypes and agronomic traits in OE1 and OE2 exhibited trends opposite to those observed in tamyb1-2 and tamyb1-3 (Supplemental Figure 10B–10L). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that TaMYB1 acts as a repressor of amylose synthesis during wheat grain development and thus influences flour viscosity. 

TaMYB1 regulates the transcription of genes involved in the starch biosynthesis pathway 

To better understand the function of TaMYB1 in wheat grain development, we performed RNA-seq analysis using 15-DPA seeds from both tamyb1-2 and WT. Compared with WT, tamyb1-2 had a total of 2309 DEGs, including 789 upregulated and 1520 downregulated genes (Supplemental Figure 12A and Supplemental Table 6). There were 147 overlapping DEGs between tamyb44-1 and tamyb1-2 (Supplemental Figure 12B). GO enrichment analysis of DEGs between tamyb1-2 and WT showed significant enrichment in the starch metabolic process, sucrose biosynthetic process, carbohydrate metabolic process, and nutrient reservoir activity—patterns consistent with those observed in tamyb44-1 (Supplemental Figure 12C). RT–qPCR validation of the RNA-seq data confirmed a high degree of consistency with the transcriptome profiling results (Supplemental Figure 12D). Collectively, our results demonstrate that TaMYB44 and TaMYB1 function synergistically to regulate starch biosynthesis through shared target genes.

# DISCUSSION

# TaMYB44 as a negative regulator of starchbiosynthesis-related genes in wheat

Starch content directly affects wheat grain weight and yield, whereas amylose content influences the appearance and eating quality of oriental noodles, steamed bread, and other wheat-based food products (Crosbie et al., 1992; Araki et al., 1999; Bao, 2002; Goesaert et al., 2005; Ma and Baik, 2018; Zhu et al., 2021). The wx-B1 null genotype has become a key characteristic of high-quality noodle wheat. However, the presence or absence of wx-B1 does not fully explain differences in amylose content, viscosity, and noodle quality among wheat cultivars. Other factors may also influence amylose synthesis and, consequently, starch content. 

MYB44 belongs to the R2R3-MYB TF family, the 22nd subfamily in plants. In Arabidopsis, this subgroup comprises four genes: At-MYB44, AtMYB77, AtMYB73, and AtMYB70. Despite structural conservation and functional overlap, each gene exhibits distinct regulatory roles and expression patterns, reflecting both shared and unique biological functions. Due to the presence of the EAR motif (Supplemental Figure 2A–2C) at its C terminus, MYB44 primarily functions as a transcriptional repressor in plants (Kagale and Rozwadowski, 2011). Its regulatory roles considerably vary among plant species, influencing diverse biological processes such as stress responses, hormone signaling, and developmental pathways. 

AtMYB44 is also involved in regulating plant growth, development, and immune responses (Jung et al., 2008; Jaradat et al., 2013; Persak and Pitzschke, 2013). FaMYB44.2 negatively regulates sucrose accumulation in strawberry fruits (Wei et al., 2018). In potatoes, StMYB44 responds to high temperatures by downregulating anthocyanin synthesis within purple-skinned cultivars (Zhou et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019). IbMYB44 suppresses anthocyanin synthesis in sweet potatoes by competitively inhibiting the formation of the anthocyanin biosynthetic complex (Wei et al., 2020). In grapes, VvMYB44-1 plays a dual role in the regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis by directly repressing the expression of structural genes involved in this pathway (Leng et al., 2025). MdMYB44 negatively regulates malic acid accumulation in apple by inhibiting the promoter activity of malate-related genes (Jia et al., 2021). 

In our study, we found that TaMYB44-4D regulates wheat grain development and influences starch biosynthesis. # TaMYB44-4D interacts with TaMYB1-6B to regulate starch biosynthesis

Dimerization of MYB proteins may enhance their DNA-binding affinity and specificity. Numerous studies have identified dimerization among plant MYB TFs. For instance, BplMYB46 interacts with BplMYB13 to activate target gene transcription by increasing binding to the MYBCORE motif, thus mediating reactive oxygen species scavenging under salt and osmotic stresses (Wang et al., 2019). In Medicago truncatula, MtMYB5 and MtMYB14 physically interact and cooperatively activate the promoters of anthocyanidin reductase and leucoanthocyanidin reductase (Liu et al., 2014). In strawberry, FaMYB44.2 interacts with FaMYB1 to co-regulate sucrose accumulation (Wei et al., 2018). MdMYB306-like and MdMYB17 form a heterodimer to regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in apple (Malus domestica) (Wang et al., 2022). 

The regulatory function of TFs is typically mediated through interactions with other proteins, forming complex networks or molecular modules to control the expression of downstream target genes (Franco-Zorrilla et al., 2014). To further explore whether TaMYB44-4D forms heterodimers with other MYB proteins, we identified TaMYB1-6B as an interacting partner of TaMYB44- 4D using Y2H analysis (Supplemental Table 5) and confirmed their interaction through in vivo and in vitro assays (Figure 5A– 5D). These two TFs collaboratively repressed the expression of key genes in the starch biosynthetic pathway, such as TaWx-7A and TaBT1-6A, thereby directly influencing starch synthesis (Figure 5E and 5F). The interaction between TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B also enhanced their binding affinity to the MBSI motif in the TaWx-7A promoter (Supplemental Figure 3E). Importantly, we found that TaNAC019-3B, a target gene of both TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B, was activated by these TFs (Figure 5G). 

TaNAC019 acts as a negative regulator of starch biosynthesis. Overexpression of TaNAC019-A1 decreases starch content, grain size, and TGW (Liu et al., 2020). TaNAC019 can suppress the expression of starch-biosynthesis-related genes such as TaAGPS1 and TaSuS2 (Chen et al., 2024). In our study, we found that both TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B activate the 

expression of TaNAC019-3B, thereby indirectly repressing starch biosynthesis. This observation further supports a role for TaNAC019 as a negative regulator in starch biosynthesis. TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B thus exhibit dual functionality, acting as both repressors and activators of distinct target genes. 

TFs with dual functions of activation and repression are common in plants, where a single TF may act as an activator or repressor depending on its target genes (Ikeda et al., 2009; Yant et al., 2010; Nagahage et al., 2018; Su et al., 2025). TaMYB44 and TaMYB1 exhibit transcriptional repression activity mediated by their C-terminal EAR motifs (Supplemental Figure 2A–2C), which are known to recruit the transcriptional co-repressor TOPLESS to facilitate gene suppression (Deng et al., 2022). However, under specific conditions, the EAR motif may also interact with co-activators to promote gene activation. For instance, the EAR motif of BZR1 interacts with basic helix–loop–helix family TFs, enabling gene activation by both enhancing transcription and blocking recruitment of the TPL repressor (Oh et al., 2014). We hypothesize that TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B activate Ta-NAC019 through a similar mechanism, although the specific co-activator involved remains to be identified. 

# The TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B molecular markers provide a potential strategy for selecting wheat varieties with varying amylose content

To date, a series of functional molecular markers associated with key agronomic traits—such as plant height, TGW, drought tolerance, and heading date—have been identified and utilized in wheat genetic breeding programs (Beales et al., 2007; Morran et al., 2011; Su et al., 2011; Bian et al., 2023). In our study, we developed STARP markers capable of accurately distinguishing two distinct haplotypes in the TaMYB44-4D promoter (Ta-MYB44-4Dpro) (Supplemental Table 8). Accessions carrying HapII for both TaMYB44-4Dpro and TaMYB1-6Bpro exhibited higher TGW, starch content, and amylose content than those carrying either haplotype alone, indicating additive effects of these superior haplotypes (Supplemental Figure 14I–14K). The geographical distribution of TaMYB44-4Dpro haplotypes indicated that TaMYB44-4Dpro-HapII represents the largest proportion in wheat germplasm worldwide (Figure 7I). In contrast to TaMYB44-4D, the frequency distribution of TaMYB1-6B haplotypes exhibited considerable regional variation across global wheat germplasm. # METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

All wheat plants described in this study were cultivated at the Shang Zhuang Experimental Station of China Agricultural University in Beijing $( 3 9 ^ { \circ } 5 7 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N }$ , $1 1 6 ^ { \circ } 1 7 ^ { \prime } mathsf E )$ and in a greenhouse maintained at $7 5 \%$ relative humidity, with temperatures of $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ during a $1 6 - 1 1$ light period and $_ { 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ during an 8-h dark period. Roots, stems, leaves, young panicles (2 cm), and grains at various days after flowering were collected from Fielder plants grown in the greenhouse for analysis of TaMYB44 and TaMYB1 gene expression. Developing seeds were harvested at 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, and 30 

DPA from field-grown plants, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $\textstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for RNA and protein extraction. Samples from three individual plants at each developmental stage were collected as biological replicates. 

CRISPR–Cas9 knockout and overexpression lines (in the Fielder background) were generated using Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, as previously described (Xing et al., 2014). 

# Measurement of agronomic characteristics and starchrelated traits

For measurement of plant height, spike length, and number of grains per spike, 15 individual plants were randomly selected from homozygous WT, KO, and OE lines and measured using a ruler. A fully automatic seed analyzer (Wanshen SC-G seed detector) was used to assess grain length, width, and TGW. After tempering to $1 4 \%$ moisture overnight, clean mature seeds from WT, tamyb44-KO, tamyb1-KO, TaMYB44-4D-OE, and TaMYB1- 6B-OE lines were milled into flour with a Brabender Quadrumat Junior Mill (Brabender, Duisburg, Germany) and passed through a 100-mesh sieve. One hundred milligrams of flour were used to determine total starch content via the Total Starch Content Assay Kit (Megazyme, Ireland). Amylose content was measured using the K-AMYL kit (Megazyme). 

To determine the gelatinization properties of starch, 3 g of fine wheat flour was placed in a small aluminum canister filled with 25 ml of distilled water, thoroughly mixed, and analyzed using a Rapid Visco Analyzer (Newport Scientific, Australia), according to Method 76-21 of the American Association of Cereal Chemists (2000). Each experiment included three independent biological replicates. 

Gelatinization of grain starch in different urea solutions was measured following a previously described method (Nishi et al., 2001). Total protein and starch contents of grains in the natural population were analyzed using a Near-Infrared Reflectance instrument (Perten Instruments, Sweden).

# RT–qPCR analysis

Total RNA from grains collected at 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, and 30 days after pollination was extracted using Fruit-mate and RNAsio Plus (Takara, Japan), in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen) was used to extract total RNA from roots, stems, leaves, and young spikes (2 cm). RNA concentration was measured using a NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). First-strand cDNA was synthesized using the Reverse Transcription Premixed Mix (Tiangen). RT–qPCR was performed using a CFX96 real-time system (Bio-Rad) in a $1 0 \mu \up$ reaction volume containing $5 \mu \mu \mu$ of $_ { 2 \times }$ SYBR Green qPCR Mix (Accurate Bio), $0 . 6 \mu \up$ of cDNA template, $0 . 4 \mu \up$ of genespecific primers, and $4 ~ { \mu \mu }$ of ${ \mathsf { d o H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ . The RT–qPCR cycling program consisted of $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 5 min, followed by 40 cycles of $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 30 s, ${ \tt6 0 ^ { \circ } } \mathrm { C }$ for $\mathtt { 1 5 s }$ , and ${ 7 2 ^ { \circ } } \mathsf { C }$ for $\mathtt { 1 5 s }$ . A melting curve analysis was performed to verify amplification specificity. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \mathsf { C } t }$ method and normalized to the Ct value of the wheat TaACTIN gene (TraesCS5B02G124100). RT–qPCR was performed with three biological replicates per sample. Gene-specific primers are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# Transcriptome RNA-seq analysis

Total RNA was extracted from grains of Fielder and mutant lines 15 days after pollination. Library construction and Illumina sequencing were performed by the Beijing Genomics Institute (BGI, Beijing, China). Raw data processing was completed using the bioinformatics analysis platform of the Wheat Genetics and Genomics Research Center. Three independent biological replicates were collected for each genotype. GO enrichment analysis was conducted using the Triticeae-Gene Tribe platform (http://wheat.cau.edu.cn/TGT/m.html?navbar=GOEnrichment) (Chen et al., 2020). 

# Gene cloning, transgenic vector construction, and transformation

The CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B were amplified using primers designed for conserved regions of cDNA from the three partially homologous genes and inserted into the pEASY-Blunt Zero vector (TransGen Biotech, Beijing). The blunt-ended plasmid was utilized as a template to amplify the CDS, which was then inserted into the pWMB110-GFP vector using BamHI as the restriction site. The constructed plasmid was transferred into the Agrobacterium strain EHA105, and the wheat cultivar Fielder was transformed to produce TaMYB44-4D-OE plants. To generate tamyb44 and tamyb1 knockout lines, target sites were designed in conserved regions of the three partially homologous genes. Single-guide RNA sequences were synthesized by Tsingke (Beijing), and CRISPR–Cas9 vector construction followed a previously described protocol (Xing et al., 2014). 

# RNA in situ hybridization

Developing seeds of Fielder at 10, 15, and 20 DPA were fixed with $4 \%$ (w/v) paraformaldehyde. Section pretreatment, hybridization, 

and immunological detection were performed according to the protocol of Xiao et al. (2009). Digoxigenin-labeled 30-bp RNA probes, specific to TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B, were synthesized for the experiments. Probe sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# Subcellular localization

The complete CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B, excluding the stop codon, were cloned into pCAMBIA1300-GFP to construct fusion expression vectors 35S::TaMYB44-4D::GFP and 35S::TaMYB1-6B::GFP. Each construct was transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The vectors were infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves, and green fluorescence signals were observed using a Zeiss LSM880 laser confocal microscope. 

To identify nuclear localization, known nuclear-localized proteins tagged with RFP were co-infiltrated into tobacco leaves. The same plasmids were also transfected into wheat mesophyll protoplasts, and fluorescence signals were observed using the LSM880 confocal microscope. Primers used for vector construction are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# Transcriptional activity analysis

To verify transcriptional activity, the CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B were cloned into the pGBKT7 vector. The resulting constructs, along with pGADT7-T, were transformed into yeast strain Y2HGold and cultured on SD/− Trp/− Leu medium and on selective medium lacking tryptophan, leucine, histidine, and adenine. The transcriptional repression assay was conducted using the DLR system as previously described (Zhou et al., 2021). Briefly, the CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B were inserted into the GAL4-DB vector as effectors under the control of the 35S promoter. VP16 replaced TaMYB44-4D or TaMYB1- 6B as the positive control, and the empty 35S-GAL4-DB vector served as the negative control. For the reporter construct, a promoter containing six GAL4 UAS sequences and a TATA box was introduced into pGreenII0800-LUC. The reporter and effector constructs were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. The LUC/ REN ratio represented transcriptional activity. 

# Multiple sequence alignment and phylogenetic tree analysis

The Biomart tool on the Ensembl Plants website was used to retrieve MYB protein sequences containing MYB domains from wheat, rice, maize, barley, and Arabidopsis based on the Pfam ID (PF00249) of the MYB domain. ClustalW was used for multiple sequence alignment, and the neighbor-joining method in MEGA 7 software was employed to construct a phylogenetic tree using default parameters (Kumar et al., 2016). Evolutionary distances were calculated using the Poisson model. The phylogeny test was performed using the bootstrap method with 1000 replications. 

# EMSA analysis

The full-length CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B were cloned and inserted into the His vector to produce fusion proteins with His tags. His, His-TaMYB44-4D, and His-TaMYB1-6B were 

expressed in Escherichia coli strain BL21 (DE3) (Weidi, Shanghai, China). Recombinant proteins were purified using Ni–nitrilotriacetic acid resin (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, USA). Oligonucleotide probes were synthesized and labeled with biotin at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end. EMSAs were performed using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Primer and probe sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# ChIP–qPCR analysis

The ChIP–qPCR assay was conducted as previously described by Liu et al. (2025). Developing wheat grains (15 DAF) from TaMYB44-4D-GFP transgenic lines were collected and fixed with $1 \%$ (v/v) formaldehyde in a vacuum desiccator for 10 min. Immunoprecipitation was carried out using an anti-GFP antibody (ab316291, Abcam, UK) or without any antibody, as described by Yang et al. (2016). The amounts of immunoprecipitated genomic DNA were quantified using real-time qPCR on a CFX96 real-time system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA), with three biological replicates per sample. Primers used for qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# Y2H assay

The CDS of TaMYB44-4D was cloned into the pGBKT7 vector, and TaMYB1-6B was cloned into the pGADT7 vector. The resulting plasmids were co-transformed into yeast strain Y2HGold using the lithium acetate method, and cells were plated on SD/− Leu/− Trp medium. Positive clones containing the T-antigen and P53 were subsequently plated on SD/− Leu/ − Trp/− His/− Ade medium for interaction detection. 

# Y1H assay

To determine whether TaMYB44-4D could bind to a specific fragment of the TaWx-7A promoter, a 15-bp fragment containing MYB-binding elements was triplicated and inserted into the pAbAi vector. The constructed pAbAi vector containing the binding element fragment was linearized using BstBI and transformed into the yeast strain Y1H to generate the bait strain. To determine the minimum aureobasidin A (AbA) concentration required to suppress self-activation, bait strains were plated on SD medium supplemented with various AbA concentrations. The full-length CDS of TaMYB44-4D was inserted into the pGADT7 vector and transformed into the Y1H bait strain. Transformants were selected on SD/− Ura/− Leu medium containing the appropriate AbA concentration. 

# LCI assay

The full-length CDSs of TaMYB44-4D, TaMYB1-6B, and TaGA-Myb-3B were cloned into C-LUC and N-LUC vectors to generate TaMYB44-4D-CLUC, TaMYB1-6B-NLUC, and TaGAMyb-3B-NLUC, respectively. The resulting plasmids were introduced into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Individual colonies were cultured overnight in Luria–Bertani liquid medium with appropriate antibiotics, harvested by centrifugation, and resuspended in infiltration buffer (10 mmol/L MgCl2, 10 mmol/L MES [pH 5.6], and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \updownarrow$ acetosyringone) to a final ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.8–1.0. Equal volumes of cell suspensions were mixed and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. After 2 days, LUC signals were detected using the NightSHADE LB 985 plant imaging system (Berthold, Bad Wildbad, Germany). 

# BiFC assay

For BiFC analysis, the CDSs of TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1-6B were cloned into nYFP and cYFP plasmids, respectively. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. After 48 h, fluorescence was observed using an LSM880 laser confocal microscope at an excitation wavelength of 510 nm. 

# Co-IP assay

To validate the interaction between TaMYB44-4D and TaMYB1- 6B in vivo, 35S::TaMYB1-6B-GFP and pUbi::TaMYB44-4D-MYC vectors were constructed. The respective plasmids were transformed into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and co-expressed in 4- to 5-week-old N. benthamiana leaves via Agrobacterium infiltration. Total proteins were extracted from tobacco leaves as described by Liu et al. (2025). Co-IP was performed using protein A/G agarose resin 4FF and an anti-GFP antibody. The bound proteins were analyzed by $10 \%$ polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, then immunoblotted with anti-GFP or anti-MYC antibodies. 

# DLR assay

The 1500-bp upstream fragments of the transcription start sites of TaWx-7A, TaBT1-6A, and TaNAC019-3B were amplified from Fielder and cloned into the pGreen-0800-LUC vector as reporter constructs. The 35S::TaMYB44-4D-GFP and 35S::TaMYB1-6B-GFP vectors were used as effectors. Leaves of 4- to 5-weekold tobacco plants were infiltrated with Agrobacterium suspensions containing the various reporter and effector constructs. Firefly luciferase activity was driven by the TaWx-7A, TaBT1- 6A, and TaNAC019-3B promoters, whereas Renilla luciferase activity was driven by the 35S promoter. A DLR kit (Promega, USA) was used to measure luciferase activity with a BioTek instrument. Promoter activity was represented by the LUC/REN ratio. Each sample included at least three biological replicates. 

# STARP marker development

One STARP marker was developed based on a published method (Chai et al., 2018). Primer sequences are listed in Supplemental Table 7. 

# Haplotype analysis and geographical distribution of alleles

The SnpHub web server was used to analyze haplotypes in the promoter regions of TaMYB44-4D, TaMYB1-6B, and TaMYB77- 1B, as well as the geographical distribution of the corresponding alleles (Wang et al., 2020). 

# Statistical analysis

Comparisons between two samples were conducted using Student’s t-test. Differences among three or more samples were assessed by one-way analysis of variance using SPSS version 27. 0 (SPSS, Chicago, IL, USA).
