# Plant secondary metabolism glycosyltransferases: the emerging functional analysis

Claire M.M. Gachon, Mathilde Langlois-Meurinne and Patrick Saindrenan

Institut de Biotechnologie des Plantes, CNRS-Universite´ Paris-Sud, UMR8618, Baˆtiment 630, 91405 Orsay Cedex, France

Glycosylation is a widespread modification of plant secondary metabolites. It is involved in various functions, including the regulation of hormone homeostasis, the detoxification of xenobiotics and the biosynthesis and storage of secondary compounds. In plants, these reactions are controlled by a specific subclass of the ubiquitous glycosyltransferase family. Although these enzymes have been studied intensively for many years, to date only a handful have been characterized in planta. Plant genome projects have uncovered unsuspected complexity within this family that is hindering the characterization of single genes. However, genome information also paves the way for the development of functional genomic approaches. Here, we highlight recent progress and the outcomes of novel strategies developed to uncover the physiological roles of these glycosyltransferases.

# Glycosylation: a key modification of secondary metabolites

Plants are well known for producing a huge diversity of low molecular weight natural products through secondary metabolism. To date, $> 1 0 0 0 0 0 0$ such compounds have been described, many of them being of commercial interest [1]. This variety is underpinned by a relatively small number of ‘basal’ metabolic pathways; diversity is mostly ensured by multiple decorations of a common skeleton by hydroxylation, methylation, acylation or conjugation to small molecules. For instance, the diversity of the more than 4000 known flavonoids originates from such combinatorial modifications of their common aromatic structure. Glycosylation (i.e. conjugation to a sugar moiety) is one of the most widespread of these modifications (Figure 1a). It is catalysed by a family of enzymes called glycosyltransferases (UGTs). Over the past few years, the completion of several plant genome sequencing projects has unraveled unsuspected complexity within modifying enzymes such as UGTs, glutathione-S-transferases, cytochromes P450 and acyltransferases. These enzymes are encoded by large multigene families sometimes comprising several hundred genes: family 1 UGTs are encoded by 120 Ugt genes in Arabidopsis; 165 have been identified in Medicago truncatula to date [2,3]. Given the biological,

pharmacological and agronomic relevance of secondary metabolites, UGTs have attracted considerable interest for decades but only a handful of them have been characterized by classical biochemistry and genetics. Associated with genomic knowledge, the recent development of large-scale analytical technologies is an ideal tool with which to address the complexity of large gene families. As discussed below, these strategies are now beginning to bear fruit, and we expect unprecedented progress in the characterization of these secondary metabolism enzymes during the next few years.

# Known and suspected physiological roles of secondary metabolite glycosylation

From a chemical point of view, sugar conjugation results in both increased stability (through the protection of reactive nucleophilic groups) and water solubility. It has also been considered to be a biological flag controlling the compartmentalization of metabolites, for example, the accumulation of compounds in the vacuole [4]. For these reasons, UGTs are involved in the inactivation and detoxification of xenobiotics or harmful compounds, often in conjunction with cytochromes P450 [5]. Conversely, some phytoanticipins and herbivore deterrent compounds are stored in the vacuole in a conjugated form. Upon the decompartmentalization caused by cellular disintegration, they are released and further cleaved by $\beta$ -glycosidases into their active form. This scheme is well described for cyanogenic glucosides such as linamarin in cassava (Figure 1b) [6].

Chemical stabilization is well illustrated by the anthocyanins, in which glycosylation at the 3-OH position is crucial for the stability of the aromatic ring. Multiple additional modifications of these pigments can occur and contribute to their unique properties. The glycosylation pattern of anthocyanins or flavonoids has raised considerable interest with regard to their potential in fruit and flower colour engineering (Figure 1c) [7–10]. It also strongly affects the bioavailability of these dietary compounds, some of which display antioxidant, atheroprotective or anticancer activity [11].

Glycosylation also plays a role in the regulation of the active levels of several hormones, including auxin (IAA), abscisic acid (ABA), cytokinins (CKs), brassinosteroids (BRs) and the stress-related hormone salicylic acid (Figure 1d–h). The significance and functional

# The plant secondary metabolism glycosyltransferase family

All reactions involving the transfer of a nucleotideactivated sugar moiety onto another molecule (whatever its nature) are catalysed by a ubiquitous family of enzymes called glycosyltransferases. They are found in all living organisms: prokaryotes, eukaryotes, Archaea and viruses. They contribute to the synthesis of glucidic polymers, glycoproteins or glycolipids. By 2003, the w7200 known UGT sequences had been classified into 65 families according to sequence similarity and catalytic mechanism (inversion or retention of the anomeric configuration of the sugar moiety), of which family 1 contains the most sequences [22]. In spite of the wide range of glycosylated secondary products in plants, all the corresponding enzymes identified to date belong to a monophyletic clade called the plant-specific secondary metabolism glycosyltransferase family. This was first recognized by Jane and Monica Hughes [23] based on the alignment of eight cDNA sequences from cassava and encompasses the vast majority of plant family 1 UGTs [2]. Hughes and Hughes showed that the consensus motif for all glycosyltransferases (as defined in Ref. [24]) could be refined and extended towards the N-terminus of the protein into a 44-amino acid consensus typical of secondary metabolism plant UGTs. This so-called plant secondary product glycosyltransferase (PSPG) motif was later found in all cloned secondary metabolism UGTs from other plant species.

Although O-UGT, N-UGT and S-UGT activities have been detected in algae [25], a BLAST search over kingdoms indicates that PSPG-motif-containing UGTs are probably restricted to the Embryophyta. The availability of many sequences from the angiosperms enables us to define a new version of this consensus (Figure 2a). Its biological significance can be roughly assessed by calculating the ratio of non-synonymous $( K _ { a } )$ to synonymous $( K _ { s } )$ mutations over two Ugt sequences. Whereas freely evolving sequences are expected to accumulate synonymous and non-synonymous nucleotide substitutions at the same rate $( K _ { a } / K _ { s } = 1 )$ , the excess of synonymous mutations observed along the PSPG box $( K _ { a } / K _ { s } ; ; 1 )$ indicates strong purifying selection (Figure 2b). Based on comparisons with crystallographic data from bacterial enzymes, this motif has long been suspected to be the binding site of the nucleotide-activated sugar. A threedimensional model of UGT73A5, a UGT from Dorotheanthus bellidiformis (Aizoaceae), indicates a direct interaction between the uracil moiety of UDP–glucose and the highly conserved HCGWNS residues [26]. Accordingly, the last amino acid of the PSPG box was shown to contribute to the discrimination between UDP–glucose and UDP–galactose [27].

Following the completion of the Arabidopsis genome sequence, $\sim 1 2 0$ secondary metabolism UGT genes were identified in this species (http://www.biobase.dk/P450). They were subdivided into 14 groups according to sequence alignment (Figure 2c, A–N) [28]. In all plants for which genomic information is available (e.g. rice, poplar and M. truncatula), UGTs seem to be encoded by comparably large multigene families.

# Classical approaches to UGT functional analysis

The first plant UGT-encoding gene identified was an unexpected outcome of work by Nobel prize winner Barbara McClintock on the genetic instability of transposons in maize. She studied the dark pigmentation of maize grains conferred by the mutation of Bronze1, which later turned out to encode a flavonoid UGT [29]. In the following years, three main approaches were used to identify the function of UGTs: mutant isolation; protein purification and cloning; or screening of cDNA libraries with heterologous probes [30]. Occasionally, alternative strategies were used, such as cloning a UGT involved in the synthesis of anthocyanin pigments by differential display of libraries from differently colored leaves of Chinese basil (Perilla frutescens) [31]. UGT catalytic activity was most often tested by in vitro enzymatic assays on the recombinant protein expressed in E. coli or in yeast. This led to the discovery that UGTs often display a broad substrate specificity in vitro [32,33]. Usually, they selectively glycosylate one (or a few) given position(s) on related substrates [34]. In some groups, this regioselectivity is conserved among paralogs and orthologs (Figure 2c, F,L), a feature that is often used to infer the catalytic properties of unknown UGTs. However, group D contains enzymes whose substrates are as diverse as flavonoids, phenolics, betacyanin, steroids and terpenes (Figure 2c). Even though many UGTs have been cloned and heterologously expressed in the past few years (Table 1), the catalytic activity of only a handful has been fully demonstrated in vivo.

Nevertheless, identifying the substrate of a UGT is only the first step towards the determination of its physiological role. This requires additional studies aimed, for instance, at clarifying the bioactivity of its substrate (and/or product) and the tissue and subcellular localization of the enzyme. A study of protein colocalization using green fluorescent protein (GFP) fusion proteins showed the involvement of sorghum UGT85B1 in a multienzymatic complex that leads to the synthesis of the cyanogenic glucoside dhurrin [35,36]. The analysis of protein–protein interactions was also used to suggest a link between Arabidopsis UGT75B1 and callose synthesis, although the precise function of this protein remains obscure [37]. More often, the phenotypic consequences of metabolite pool perturbation are analyzed by the up- and downregulation of gene expression in mutants or transgenic plants. Recently, such a gain and loss of function approach in a study of the tobacco UGT TOGT showed that this enzyme regulates the levels of scopoletin, a phytoalexin that accumulates in abundance during the hypersensitive response against tobacco mosaic virus (Figure 1k). # Contribution of functional genomics to UGT characterization

Although the aforementioned approaches are still widely used, their power has been multiplied by the complementary development of functional genomics and large-scale analytical methods. Genomic knowledge gives access to all

members of a multigene family and thus allows researchers to look for the best candidate among all possible genes to fulfill a given function. Therefore, systematic approaches can be designed, for example, heterologous production in E. coli of all Arabidopsis secondary metabolism UGTs and in vitro testing of their substrate specificities [40,41]. This led to the identification of enzymes capable of catalyzing key glycosylation reactions, such as auxin conjugation [14,15]. However, a major limitation is the broad substrate specificity exhibited by

most UGTs in vitro, which hinders the identification of their real substrate(s) in vivo [3]. A more fruitful method is the use of large-scale tools at the transcriptional or metabolic levels (microarray or metabolite profiling): comparative metabolite profiling of UGT mutants with wild-type plants enables the identification of the in vivo product(s) of a given enzyme [42,43]. Genome-wide transcriptional analysis using microarrays is a powerful screen to point out a few candidate genes that can be further validated by analysing the phenotype of the corresponding mutant isolated from a T-DNA-tagged insertion mutant library. These approaches have accelerated the pace of gene discovery: seven UGTs have been characterized using these strategies in the past two years (Table 1).

Recently, Takayuki Tohge et al. reported the characterization of two UGTs in a way that perfectly illustrates the potential of combining transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses [44]. In a genome-wide microarray survey, they identified Ugt75c1 as being upregulated in plants overproducing PAP1, a MYB factor involved in the regulation of flavonoid metabolism. Microarray data mining also showed that Ugt75c1 is strongly co-regulated with genes encoding enzymes of the anthocyanin biosynthetic pathway throughout plant development. Sequence alignment with related UGTs from other species enabled us to predict the function of UGT75C1 as a UGT acting on the 5-OH group of anthocyanins [45]. Through non-targeted metabolite profiling of $\sim 1 8 0 0$ compounds, Tohge et al. indeed showed that PAP1 overproduction caused only small changes in the metabolome, except for the marked overaccumulation of anthocyanins and some flavonol derivatives. The recombinant protein UGT75C1 was expressed in E. coli and displayed the corresponding enzymatic activity on several flavonols and anthocyanidins. Tohge et al. also undertook a reverse genetics study and isolated the corresponding T-DNA-tagged mutant from a genome-wide collection. They identified the anthocyanins produced by this line and concluded that the new metabolites produced in the mutant plants were similar to the ones produced in the wild type, except for the absence of a glucose moiety at the 5-OH position, demonstrating the role of UGT75C1 as an anthocyanin 5- $O$ -glycosyltransferase in planta [44]. Formerly restricted to Arabidopsis, these strategies for gene characterization are now being successfully applied on other model organisms: Lahoucine Achnine et al. recently combined a microarray and a targeted metabolite profiling analysis to identify Medicago UGTs involved in triterpene saponin synthesis (Figure 1l) [3].

# Towards a link between gene family and metabolic pathway evolution

This improved description of the metabolic pathways also facilitates an understanding of how they have appeared and been maintained during evolution. More and more genomic data indicate that genes do not evolve independently from each other and that their fate is constrained on at least three levels. (i) Within a gene family: just after their formation, duplicated gene pairs (paralogs) tend to undergo relaxed purifying selection and to accumulate

more mutations than do single copy genes [46]. (ii) Within a pathway: the widespread gene co-regulation and protein–protein interaction (channeling) in metabolic pathways can only be explained by concerted evolution between enzymes involved in the same pathway [45,47]. (iii) Between pathways: chemotaxonomic analyses have revealed that some classes of compounds tend to be mutually exclusive, for instance tropane and steroid alkaloids in Solanaceae [48]. This mutual exclusivity could reflect that accumulating just one of these compounds is enough to protect the plant from herbivory, leading to the counterselection for other equivalent toxic molecules, the synthesis of which would have an unnecessary energetic cost.

UGTs, like other proteins involved in natural product accumulation and compartmentalization or the detoxification of xenobiotics (e.g. acyltransferases, glutathione-Stransferases, cytochromes P450 and vacuolar transporters), tend to form large multigene families with several dozen or even hundreds of members. This is in sharp contrast to enzymes of the ‘core’ metabolic pathways (e.g. general phenylpropanoid metabolism), which are encoded by small families or even unique genes.

These huge multigene families also tend to expand through tandem duplication but not segmental duplication (i.e. large-scale duplication events, such as chromosome duplication or polyploidy) [49]. Indeed, most of the UGT genes that we now see in Arabidopsis have a recent origin [2]. This suggests that they appeared after the divergence of other species for which genomic information is available and that all contain comparably large families. This in turn means that at least part of the large multigene UGT family arose independently in all these species. This could explain why no clear conserved substrate specificity and function can be inferred from sequence analysis (e.g. in group D, Figure 2c). This interpretation would also fit with their supposed role as a first line of defense against stresses, continuously buffering environmental stresses and shaped according to changing conditions. Finally, a corollary would be the short lifetime and rapid turnover of such genes, linked with the high plasticity of secondary metabolism as a whole.

# Future prospects in glycosyltransferase research

Large-scale approaches such as metabolite profiling, metabolomics and microarray analysis are well suited to addressing the biological complexity of the UGT family. Recent functional genomic approaches have demonstrated their potential for identifying enzymes involved in a specific pathway. Routine use of mass spectrometry and related analytical tools greatly facilitates the identification of complex multiglycosylated compounds. In future years, the continuation of such analyses should enhance our knowledge of the enzymatic properties of UGTs. This should help us to get a wider vision of the organization of metabolic pathways and their dynamics during plant development. In conjunction with the publication of genomic information of several species, understanding how evolutionary constraints shape metabolic pathways is

likely to become one of the most exciting fields in plant secondary metabolism research.
