# Abstract

Diseases caused by Alternaria alternata and Botryosphaeria dothidea diminish pear yield and quality, and restrict the pear agricultural industry. Lignification is a conserved mechanism for plant resistance against pathogen invasion. The regulatory mechanisms underlying defence-induced lignification in pear in response to fungal pathogen infection remain unknown. In this study, analysis of lignification level and lignin content in pear revealed that A. alternata and B. dothidea induced lignification, and transcriptomics showed that lignin biosynthesis was affected. To explore whether laccases (LACs) mediated by miR397 regulate lignification in pear, we investigated the role of PcmiR397 in repressing the expression of PcLACs using 5′-RNA ligasemediated-RACE and co-transformation in tobacco. Opposite expression patterns for PcmiR397 and PcLAC target genes were observed in pear in response to pathogens. Transient transformation in pear demonstrated that silencing PcmiR397 and overexpressing a single PcLAC enhanced resistance to pathogens via lignin synthesis. To further reveal the mechanism underpinning the PcMIR397 response of pear to pathogens, the PcMIR397 promoter was analysed, and pMIR397-1039 was found to be inhibited by pathogen infection. The transcription factor PcMYB44 was up-regulated, and it bound to the PcMIR397 promoter and inhibited transcription following pathogen infection. The results demonstrate the role of PcmiR397-PcLACs in broadspectrum resistance to fungal disease, and the potential role of PcMYB44 involved in the miR397-PcLAC module in regulating defence-induced lignification. The findings provide valuable candidate gene resources and guidance for molecular breeding to improve resistance to fungal disease in pear.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Pear is a temperate fruit tree that plays a key role in the global fruit industry (Kang et al., 2021). Pear ring rot, caused by Botryosphaeria species, is characterized by trunk cankers, branch die back, fruit rot, and severe recession of tree growth in most pear-producing regions (Cunnington et al., 2007; Zhai et al., 2014). Pear black spot disease, caused by Alternaria species, has led to pear leaf defoliation diseases in recent years (Tsuge et al., 1989). Both of these fungal diseases decrease pear yield and fruit quality, and they are difficult to control, hence they threaten healthy and sustainable development of the pear industry. Excessive use of pesticides is employed to control fungal diseases, which leads to declines in pear fruit quality and diminished food safety (Sutton, 1996). No effective methods are available for the prevention and control of fungal diseases, therefore exploring resistance genes to control fungal diseases is a priority. 

Lignin deposition and plant cell wall enforcements triggered by pathogen invasion serve as a first line of defence and physical barrier to prevent pathogen attack (Millet et al., 2010; Tronchet et al., 2010). Defence-induced lignification, which occurs widely in plants, is a conserved disease resistance mechanism against (hemi)biotrophic pathogens (Bhuiyan et al., 2009a; Chezem et al., 2017). Polymerization of monolignols into lignin polymers proceeds via oxidative polymerization catalysed by laccases (LACs) and peroxidases (Freudenberg, 1959). Laccase enzymes contain three blue copper oxidase domains involved in lignification in plants (Lu et al., 2013). In Arabidopsis thaliana and Populus trichocarpa, LACs are involved in lignin biosynthesis and deposition in cell wall, xylem fibre, and vascular cell structures (Lu et al., 2013; Wang, Zhang, et al., 2014). Laccase genes are involved in resistance to pathogen infection (Hou et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2018; Zhang, Wu, et al., 2019). In cotton, three laccase genes, GhLAC1, GhLAC4 and GhLAC15, participate in lignin biosynthesis, increase the lignin content, and regulate the resistance of cotton to Verticillium dahliae infection (Hu et al., 2018; Zhang, Wu, et al., 2019). In Chinese pear (Pyrus bretschneideri), transient silencing of three PbrLAC genes decreased the lignin content and the number of stone cells in fruits by regulating lignin biosynthesis (Xue et al., 2018). However, the involvement of LACs in broad-spectrum regulatory mechanisms underlying defence-induced lignification in response to fungal pathogen infection remains poorly understood in pear. 

The conserved microRNA miR397 mainly targets LAC family members in plants, and it regulates diverse biological processes in plant growth, development, and external stress stimulus (Chen et al., 2018; Khandal et al., 2020; Wang, Zhang, et al., 2014; Xue et al., 2018). In rice, overexpression of OsmiR397 increases panicle branching and grain size by down-regulating the target OsLAC, which is involved in the sensitivity of plants to brassinosteroids (Zhang et al., 2013). In Populus species, Ptr-miR397 negatively regulates laccase genes and thereby affects lignin content (Lu et al., 2013). In Arabidopsis, miR397 is involved in inflorescence growth, root lignification under phosphate deficiency, and delaying flowering under the control of the circadian rhythm by targeting CASEIN KINASE II SUBUNIT BETA3 (Feng et al., 2020; Khandal et al., 2020; Wang, 

Zhang, et al., 2014). In pear, miR397a regulates stone cell lignification in fruits by inhibiting the expression of LAC genes (Xue et al., 2018). Accumulating evidence shows that miRNAs in plant species may function in resistance to pathogen infection (Jiang et al., 2018; Zhang, Zhang, et al., 2019). Recently, it was revealed that miR397- LACs mediate responses to biotic stress and play important roles in regulating resistance in apple and cotton to B. dothidea and V. dahliae infection (Wei et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2020). However, the role of pear miRNAs in response to pathogen infection, especially miR397-LACs, remains unknown. 

Transcription factors (TFs) play crucial roles in the precise regulation of complex biological networks through transcriptional activation or transcriptional inhibition (Schwechheimer et al., 1998). The MYB family is one of the largest transcription factor families in plants. MYB genes are involved in diverse biological processes in response to abiotic and biotic stress, and R2R3-MYB members participate in the regulation of resistance to pathogen infection in many plant species, including wheat, sweet cherry, tomato, and Arabidopsis (Chezem et al., 2017; Cui et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2013; Shen et al., 2017). AtMYB46 and MdMYB73 increase resistance to pathogens in Arabidopsis and apple via the salicylic acid (SA) pathway (Ambawat et al., 2013; Gu et al., 2020). Some MYB genes are involved in lignin synthesis (Xiao et al., 2021). In Arabidopsis, AtMYB15 regulates defence-induced synthesis of G-rich lignin to enhance disease resistance (Chezem et al., 2017). In Chinese pear, the transcriptional activator PbrMYB169 positively regulates lignin synthesis in the stone cells of fruits (Xue et al., 2018). It is therefore necessary to reveal whether MYB genes function in resistance to pathogen infection via defence-induced lignification in pear. 

The prevalence and persistence of pear ring rot and pear black spot diseases in pear-producing areas hinder environmentally friendly development of the pear industry. Screening and exploitation of resistance genes are important for the prevention and control of fungal diseases. Herein, the pathogens B. dothidea and A. alternata were found to alter lignification level and increase lignin content in pear, and the miR397-LACs module is known to regulate lignification in some plant species (Huang, Zhou, et al., 2021). We therefore verified PcLACs as the targets of miR397, and determined their expression patterns in response to pathogen infection. Furthermore, we investigated the roles of PcmiR397 and its PcLAC target genes in resistance to fungal pathogen infection. Transient transformation in pear confirmed that silencing of PcmiR397 and overexpressing PcLAC enhanced resistance to pathogens via lignin synthesis. To further identify the upstream transcriptional regulators of PcmiR397, the PcmiR397 promoter sequence was cloned and analysed, and PcMIR397 promoter activity was inhibited in response to B. dothidea and A. alternata infection. The yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) and dual luciferase assays with the PcmiR397 promoter sequence identified the potential R2R3-MYB transcription factor PcMYB44, and PcMYB44 was found to directly bind to the PcMIR397 promoter and inhibit its transcriptional activity in response to pathogens. We deduced that the PcMYB44-mediated miR397-PcLACs module regulates resistance of pear to pathogens. The study provides new molecular 

information and insight into the resistance of pear to pathogens, as well as candidate genes for breeding of pear cultivars with improved broad-spectrum resistance to fungal diseases.

# 2.2  |  Expression profiles of lignin biosynthesis pathway genes in pear in response to pathogen infection

To further explore whether pear lignification in response to pathogen infection is related to the expression of lignin biosynthesis pathway genes, we performed high-throughput sequencing of the transcriptome of Conference pear sampled at 1 and 3 days after B. dothidea inoculation (and 0 days as controls). We selected genes related to the lignin biosynthesis pathway and presented them as $\mathsf { l o g } _ { 2 } ( \mathsf { F P K M + 1 } )$ values in a heatmap to analyse expression profiles (Figure S3). There are two steps in lignin biosynthesis: monolignol biosynthesis and monolignol polymerization (Freudenberg, 1959). In the monolignol biosynthesis step, multiple genes, including cinnamate 4-hydroxylase (C4H), cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD), chalcone synthase (CHS), caffeic acid O-methyltransferase (COMT), and ferulate 5-hydroxylase (F5H), were significantly up-regulated following B. dothidea infection (Figure S3a). Lignin is polymerized by either peroxidases (POD) or laccases (Freudenberg, 1959). The RNA-Seq data indicated that several PcPOD genes, including PcPOD16, PcPOD50, and PcPOD57, were up-regulated (Figure S3b). Forty-six laccase genes were detected by RNA-Seq, and the expression of several LAC genes was induced after B. dothidea infection. We cloned the coding region sequences of three randomly selected laccase genes, PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7, from cDNAs of Conference. Multiple sequence alignment and conserved domain analysis showed that each contains three Cu-oxidase domains (Figure S4a). Phylogenetic analysis was performed based on amino acid sequences of laccase proteins in different species (Table S1). This revealed that PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7 were closely related to LAC2, LAC4, and LAC7, respectively, of Malus domestica and P. bretschneideri (Figure S4b). In addition, we analysed PcLAC4 subcellular localization in plasmolysed onion cells, and the enhanced yellow fluorescent protein (eYFP) signal from PcLAC4-eYFP was specifically detected at the cell wall, while the fluorescence signal from the control construct 35S:eYFP was detected at the nucleus, cytoplasm, and cell wall (Figure S5). This revealed that LAC4 protein is specifically located at the cell wall, where lignin is deposited. 

# 2.3  |  Verification of pear laccase genes as targets of miR397 by ${ \pmb 5 } ^ { \prime }$ -RNA ligase-mediated-RACE and Nicotiana benthamiana co-transformation

miR397-LACs is a stable regulatory module in many plant species (Huang, Zhang, et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2013; Wang, Zhang, et al., 2014; 

Xue et al., 2018). We explored whether laccase genes mediated by miR397 regulate lignification in pear in response to pathogen infection. PcmiR397 target gene prediction was conducted against the pear Unigene database as previously reported (Allen et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2015; Schwab et al., 2005). This identified nine laccase members as potential target genes of PcmiR397, with target site positions in the coding region sequence of PcLAC genes (Figure S6). 

We performed 5′-RNA ligase-mediated (RLM)-RACE to verify and analyse the computational prediction of potential target genes. The obtained cloned PCR products of mRNA 5′-end fragments were sequenced and analysed. The results showed that the cleavage site was mainly between the 10th and 11th nucleotides of the predicated miR397-PcLAC4 matching sequences, and cleavage between the second and third nucelotides of the miR397 target site was also detected at low frequency (Figure 2a). We also performed $5 ^ { \prime }$ -RACE analysis on another variety of pear, Pyrus pyrifolia ‘Jinshui No. 2’, to verify the miR397 target cleavage site. The obtained clones of PCR products were sequenced, revealing the cleavage sites of $P p L A C 4$ and PpLAC11 (Figure S7). All cleavage sites predicted or verified above were located at the second Cu-oxidase domain of PcLAC mRNA sequences. 

To confirm that target PcLAC genes were regulated by PcmiR397 in vivo, we performed transient expression experiments in N. benthamiana leaves. We made constructs expressing green fluorescent protein (GFP) fused with the six putative target sites (PcLACs) or mutated target sites (mPcLACs, 10th and 11th bases altered from GC to AT) of PcLAC genes at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -terminus. PcMIR397 was cloned into pART27 to generate the 35S:MIR397 construct. The constructs used in co-expression experiments are shown in Figure 2b. The expression levels of mature miR397 in N. benthamiana leaves of 35S:mPcLACts-GFP+35S:MIR397 and 35S:PcLACts-GFP+35S:MIR397 detected by reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) were up-regulated compared with that of 35S:PcLACts-GFP alone, which indicated that PcmiR397 was overexpressed successfully to be effective (Figure S8). The fluorescence intensity under UV light was detected in leaves infiltrated with the 35S:PcLAC-GFP construct, the 35S:PcLAC-GFP and 35S:PcMIR397 combination, and the 35S:mPcLAC-GFP and 35S:PcMIR397 combination. The GFP fluorescence signal intensity from 35S:mPcLAC-GFP co-expressed with 35S:PcMIR397 was almost the same as that of 35S:PcLAC-GFP alone. At the position of each 35S:PcLAC-GFP co-expressed with 35S:PcMIR397, the GFP fluorescence intensity was obviously lower than that of expressing 35S:PcLAC-GFP alone or 35S:mPcLAC-GFP co-expressed with 35S:PcMIR397, respectively. # 2.4  |  Expression profiles of PcmiR397, pre-PcmiR397, and PcLACs in the response of pear to A. alternata and B. dothidea infection

To investigate the responsiveness of PcmiR397 and target gene PcLACs to pathogen infection, expression patterns of mature PcmiR397, the precursor of PcmiR397, and PcLACs in pear inoculated with A. alternata and B. dothidea were comprehensively analysed. Northern blotting analysis showed that PcmiR397 was down-regulated at 24, 48, and 72 hpi on infection with A. alternata or B. dothidea, compared with 0 hpi controls based on the relative abundance level (Figure 3a,c). Stem-loop RT-qPCR was performed to verify the expression level of PcmiR397, which was consistent with the results of northern blotting analysis (Figure 3a,c). According to previously reported small RNA sequencing data for P. pyrifolia infected with apple stem grooving virus at 24 and $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Liu et al., 2015), two precursors sharing the same sequence of mature PcmiR397, designated PcMIR397-1.0 and PcMIR397-1039, were predicted, located at scaffold 1.0 and scaffold 1039 of the pear genome (http://peargenome.njau.edu.cn/), respectively. We amplified the two precursors of PcmiR397 from the cDNA of Conference and 

the hairpin structure of the precursor is illustrated in Figure S11. The expression levels of both precursors of PcmiR397 were determined by RT-qPCR with the primers designed from the consensus sequence of two precursors. Pre-PcmiR397 was down-regulated significantly at 48 and 72 hpi with A. alternata. Similarly, expression of pre-PcmiR397 exhibited a distinct decrease at 12 hpi and was maintained at a significantly low level until 72 hpi following B. dothidea inoculation. These results indicate that the expression trends for PcmiR397 and pre-PcmiR397 were consistent: both were suppressed by pathogen infection (Figure 3a,c). Furthermore, RTqPCR for PcLACs, the target genes of PcmiR397, were performed, which revealed the expression levels of PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7 displayed opposite trends to those of PcmiR397 in response to pathogen infection (Figure 3b,d). Three PcLACs were up-regulated, with peak expression at 72 hpi, irrespective of A. alternata or B. dothidea infection (Figure 3b,d).

# 2.7  |  Transcriptional activity of the PcMIR397 promoter is suppressed in the response of pear to A. alternata and B. dothidea infection

Similar to protein-coding genes, cis-acting elements located in the upstream promoter regions of MIR genes regulate the transcription of miRNAs by interacting with trans-acting factors (Xie et al., 2005). We cloned the $\mathtt { c } . 1 . 9 \mathsf { k b }$ sequence upstream of PcMIR397 at two putative genomic loci, scaffold1.0 and scaffold1039.0 (Table S2), to generate promoters designated pMIR397-1.0 and pMIR397-1039, respectively, and scanned the sequence for potential cis-acting elements. The results showed that cis elements in the MIR397 promoter were mainly responsive to biotic or abiotic stress, hormones and light. Two biotic stress-related cis elements, W1-Box and TC-rich repeats, were identified in both pMIR397-1.0 and pMIR397-1039. The putative functions of these two elements are fungal elicitor responsiveness and defence responsiveness, respectively (Tables S3 and S4). 

To further investigate the mechanism underlying the response of PcmiR397 to A. alternata and B. dothidea, we engineered pMIR397- 1.0:GUS and pMIR397-1039:GUS by replacing the p35SCaMV promoter at the 5′-terminus of the $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) gene with pMIR397-1.0 and pMIR397-1039 in the pBI121 vector. We transduced pMIR397-1.0:GUS, pMIR397-1039:GUS, and p35S:GUS into young expanded leaves of Conference pear seedlings by agroinfiltration. The pMIR397-1.0:GUS, pMIR397-1039:GUS, and pCaM-V35S:GUS leaves were then inoculated with suspensions of spores from A. alternata, B. dothidea, or double-distilled water as mock control. GUS staining at 2 days postinoculation (dpi) showed that the promoter of PcMIR397-1039 had stronger activity than p35S and pMIR397-1.0. Furthermore, GUS expression intensity driven by pMIR397-1039 was obviously lower in fungal pathogen-infected leaves than in mock-inoculated control leaves. However, GUS driven by p35S or pMIR397-1.0:GUS exhibited almost the same expression level in leaves inoculated with pathogen or mock-inoculated (Figure 6a). Corresponding GUS activity quantification results revealed a remarkable decrease in MIR397 promoter activity in A. alternata- and B. dothidea-infected pMIR397-1039:GUS leaves compared with mock-inoculated control leaves. In leaves transformed 

with pMIR397-1.0:GUS or pCaMV35S:GUS, there was no significant difference in GUS activity between pathogen-infected leaves and mock-inoculated leaves (Figure 6b). These results demonstrated that activity of pMIR397-1039 was inhibited during A. alternata or B. dothidea infection. 

To screen the potential transcription factor binding sites (TFBSs) of the PcMIR397 promoter, the sequences of pMIR397-1.0 and pMIR397-1039 were analysed by PlantPAN (Chow et al., 2016). The results showed that 48 TFBSs in pMIR397-1.0 fell into 13 different TF families, while 57 TFBSs in pMIR397-1039 fell into 17 TFs families (Figure 6c). Furthermore, to identify the likely TF participating in the repression of Pc-miR397 during fungal infection, we performed TF binding site prediction of the PcMIR397 promoter using PlantTFDB (http://planttfdb.gao-lab.org/) and PlantPAN (http://plantpan.itps. ncku.edu.tw/index.html). Among the TFs predicted to bind pMIR397, differentially expressed genes were further screened based on RNA-Seq analysis, including MYB315, MYB4, MYB20, MYB108, MYB21, NAC83, NAC18, MYB44, MYB86, and NAC73 (Table S5).

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

Pear, an economically important horticultural crop, has been cultivated for more than 3000 years (Teng, 2011). Pear ring rot disease caused by B. dothidea and pear black spot disease caused by Alternaria spp. seriously hinder healthy development of the pear industry. Prevention and control of the fungal disease in pear are proving difficult (Tsuge et al., 1989; Zhai et al., 2014). Currently, there are no effective methods for fungal disease control. Diseaseresistant germplasm resources are scarce, hence exploration of disease resistance genes is the main strategy for fungal disease control in pear. Breeding for broad-spectrum disease resistance is a key objective in plant protection. Lignification is a basal and conserved mechanism for disease resistance against pathogen invasion (Vance, 1980). Some researchers reported that genes involved in the lignin biosynthesis pathway contribute to broadspectrum resistance to pathogens (Bhuiyan et al., 2009b). In maize, the lignin biosynthesis pathway gene ZmCCoAOMT2 was identified within a quantitative trait locus conferring resistance to three maize diseases (Yang et al., 2017). Plant LACs, which contain three Cu-oxidase domains (Figure S4), are a large family of proteins involved in lignin polymerization (Lu et al., 2013). Overexpression of GhLAC1 in cotton can enhance plant resistance to V. dahliae, cotton bollworm, and aphids (Hu et al., 2018). In pear, the host plant responses to pathogen infection remain unknown, as does whether LACs regulate defence-induced lignification in resistance to fungal pathogen infection. 

In the present study, we revealed that fungal pathogen infection could induce lignification and increase lignin content in pear, and expression patterns of many genes, including PcLAC genes associated with lignin biosynthesis, were significantly up-regulated by transcriptome analysis and RT-qPCR (Figures 1, 3b,d, and S3). The subcellular location of PcLAC4 on the cell wall revealed that PcLACs are distributed at the lignin biosynthesis site and potentially involved in defences against pathogen infection (Figure S5). We therefore wondered whether disease resistance of pear to fungal pathogens could be enhanced through defence-induced lignification. It is known that the miR397-LACs module functions in lignification via monolignol polymerization, which has been reported in several plant species to regulate different biological processes (Huang, Zhang, et al., 2021; Khandal et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2013; Wang, Zhang, et al., 2014; Xue et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2020). We therefore investigated whether PcmiR397-PcLACs participates in and regulates defence-induced lignification in resistance to fungal disease. First, we performed 5′-RACE and co-transformation in N. benthamiana to verify PcmiR397-PcLACs target cleavage sites and demonstrate the negative regulatory role of PcmiR397 in repressing PcLAC expression (Figures 2, S7, S9 and S10). Expression of PcmiR397 and pre-PcmiR397 decreased significantly in pear following infection with either B. dothidea or A. alternata, meanwhile the expression profiles of PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7, all target genes of PcmiR397, exhibited the opposite trend to those of PcmiR397 in response to pathogen infection, based on northern blotting and RT-qPCR analyses 

(Figure 3). Thus, the increased expression levels of PcLACs could partially be a consequence of the down-regulation of Pc-miR397 in response to pathogen infection. The results showed that PcLAC7 responds dramatically to the pathogen infection. We speculated that the PcLAC7 promoter may be involved in regulating the PcLAC7 expression in response to pathogen infection besides the universal regulation effect of miR397 on PcLACs. 

To explore the role of PcmiR397-PcLACs in disease resistance, we first performed stable genetic transformation in pear, but failed to obtain transformed plants. Furthermore, transient transformation assays in pear were performed to explore whether the PcmiR397-PcLACs module functions in pear resistance to fungal disease. Silencing of PcmiR397 in pear fruit led to up-regulation of PcLACs, consistent with decreased disease severity in comparison with that of WT and EV groups (Figure 4). Accordingly, overexpression of PcLAC4, PcLAC2, and PcLAC7 in pear leaves increased lignin content and inhibited mycelial growth, which regulates the resistance of pear to pathogen infection (Figures 5, S12, and S13). Whether co-expressing PcLAC2/4/7 confers stronger effects on pear resistance to disease than overexpressing single PcLAC2, PcLAC4, or PcLAC7 remains to be further investigated. 

In addition, OE-PcMIR397 transformed fruits, in which accumulation of PcLACs was reduced, exhibited slightly larger disease spots, but not significantly compared with those of controls (Figure 4d–g). It was reported that silencing of GhLAC1 in cotton and overexpressing PbrmiR397 in pear led to accumulation of JA and up-regulation of peroxidasess, respectively, which were involved in host regulatory reaction (Hu et al., 2018; Xue et al., 2018). It was predicted that other forms of feedback regulatory mechanisms may be involved in pear sensitivity to pathogen infection besides regulation of PcmiR397 expression, which needs to be further explored. In pear, transient overexpression of PbrmiR397 and silencing of PbrLAC1, 2, and 18 decreased the lignin content and stone cell numbers of pear fruits, indicating roles for PbrmiR397a-PbrLACs in lignin biosynthesis (Xue et al., 2018). We therefore deduced that the PcmiR397-PcLAC module has broad-spectrum resistance to pathogen infection and contributes to defence-induced lignification in pear. 

It is reported that some TFs participate in the regulation of miR397 expression in response to environmental signals (Huang, Zhou, et al., 2021). In Arabidopsis, CCA1 binds to the MIR397b promoter and inhibits transcription activity during flowering development under the control of the circadian clock (Feng et al., 2020). To further identify the upstream transcriptional regulators of PcmiR397 in pear, we first determined the pMIR397 activity in response to fungal pathogen infection by GUS assay. The results showed that the promoter activity of PcMIR397-1039 was inhibited in response to pathogen infection (Figure 6). Furthermore, we performed Y1H and dual luciferase reporter assays to screen and verify the potential TFs involved in regulating PcmiR397, of which PcMYB44 was significantly up-regulated after pathogen infection (Figures 7, S14, and S15). This demonstrated that PcMYB44 could 

bind to the PcMIR397 promoter and inhibit its activity (Figure 7). Based on the results of GUS assay, Y1H, and dual luciferase assay, we concluded that the pMIR397-1039 with stronger transcriptional activity has the major role in PcMIR397 transcription and deduced that PcMIR397-1039 plays a more important role in the pear response to fungal pathogen infection (Figures 6 and 7). Furthermore, subcellular localization results showed that PcMYB44 is in the nucleus, suggesting that PcMYB44 can enter the nucleus and regulate miR397 expression (Figure 7e). In addition, sequence analysis showed that PcMYB44 was highly homologous to AtMYB44, AtMYB73, and MdMYB73, which belong to subgroup 22 of the R2R3 MYB family (Figures 7d and S16). AtMYB44 expression was up-regulated in response to pathogen infection and defencerelated phytohormone treatment, and overexpressing AtMYB44 in transgenic plants resulted in enhanced resistance to Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato (Zou et al., 2013). Expression of AtMYB73 was induced by Bipolaris oryzae infection, meanwhile loss-of-function mutant amyb73 was susceptible to B. oryzae (Jia et al., 2011). Recently, it was demonstrated that MdMYB73 may regulate the 

SA pathway to improve resistance to B. dothidea infection in apple (Gu et al., 2020). Analysis of the 3D structures of PcMYB44 and AtMYB44 proteins revealed very similar structures (Figure S17), indicating similar functions in pear, hence both may participate in the resistance response. In addition, PcMYB44-miR397 was widely responsive to defence signals based on gene expression profiles under JA, SA, and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ treatments (Figure S18). 

By summarizing the obtained results, we generated a network model composed of PcMYB44, PcmiR397, and PcLACs that integrate to regulate lignification in resistance of pear to pathogen infection (Figure 8). PcMYB44 expression levels are significantly up-regulated in response to fungal pathogen infection, which appears to be related to JA, SA, and ${ \mathsf { O } } ^ { 2 - }$ mediated defence signalling pathways. PcMYB44, located in the nucleus, binds the PcMIR397 promoter and inhibits transcriptional activity, which leads to a significant reduction in PcmiR397 expression in pear in response to pathogen infection. As a negative posttranscriptional regulator of PcLACs via cleavage, PcmiR397 downregulation up-regulates the expression of PcLACs involved in lignin synthesis to further enhance the resistance of pear to pathogen infection.

# 4  |  EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# 4.1  |  Pear tissue culture materials

Pear (P. communis ‘Conference’) in vitro shoots were grown on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium supplemented with $0 . 2 \mathrm { m g / L }$ indole-3-butyric acid and 1 mg/L 6-benzylaminopurine (Murashige & Skoog, 1962), and kept in a culture room at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ with $4 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \varsigma / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ light intensity and a 16/8 h photoperiod. Plantlets were subcultured every 4 weeks for propagation, and c.3-week-old plantlets were used to perform Agrobacterium tumefaciens infiltration and fungal pathogen inoculation experiments. 

# 4.2  |  Fungal pathogen growth and inoculation assay

B. dothidea strain LW-P (Wang, Jiang, et al., 2014) and A. alternata strain HB were cultured on potato dextrose agar (PDA) at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The B. dothidea strain was grown on MS medium under UV light to induce sporulation. The A. alternata strain was grown on potato carrot agar (PCA) to induce sporulation. Inoculum spores of B. dothidea and A. alternata were suspended in distilled water at $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ or $2 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathsf { c f u / m L }$ , respectively. Pear plantlets or leaves were inoculated with ${ 1 \mu \iota }$ of spore suspension or double-distilled water at the point of pricking, then returned to MS medium in a climate-controlled culture room for growth. Pear fruits were inoculated with agar plugs in $5 \mathsf { m m }$ diameter of growing mycelia at the injection site. Inoculated fruits were placed in a plastic tray and sealed with a transparent plastic covering to maintain humidity. Lesions were observed and measured. 

# 4.3  |  Histological microscopy analysis

The Conference pear plantlets were inoculated with B. dothidea spore suspension or double-distilled water (mock-inoculated control). Stem segments infected with B. dothidea just under the edge of lesions at different timepoints and mock-inoculated controls were fixed in formalinacetic acid-alcohol for 2 days, dehydrated with an ethanol gradient, and embedded in melted wax, then 4- $\mu \mathrm { m }$ cross-sections were cut using an RM2016 ultramicrotome (Leica Microsystems). Cross-sections were stained with toluidine blue O $( 0 . 5 \%$ wt/vol) then observed using an Eclipse 80i microscope (Nikon). Three plantlets infected with B. dothidea and mock-inoculated controls at each timepoint were collected and processed. The width of xylem in mock and B. dothidea-infected plantlets was measured by ImageJ software according to the toluidine blue O-stained sections. The xylem width was the average value of 18 sections from three plantlets at each timepoint. 

To further visualize autofluorescence from total lignin, sections with a diameter of $4 \mu \mathrm { m }$ were observed using a TCS-SP8 confocal laser scanning microscope (Leica Microsystems) excited with a $4 0 5 \mathsf { n m }$ laser, and emission signals were captured at $4 6 0 \mathsf { n m }$ (Vanholme et al., 2013). 

# 4.4  |  Subcellular localization analysis of PcLAC4 and PcMYB44

The coding regions of PcLAC4 and PcMYB44 without a stop codon were amplified from Conference pear cDNA and incorporated into the pCNY vector (Yang et al., 2018) fused in frame with the N-terminus of the enhanced yellow fluorescent protein (eYFP) gene to generate fusion constructs designated 35S:PcMYB44-eYFP and 35S:PcLAC4-eYFP, respectively. These plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101. For PcLAC4, onion epidermal cells were immersed in Agrobacterium suspension in MES-${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ -Acetosyringone (MMA) buffer (10 mM MES, $1 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 } .$ , $1 5 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ AS, $\mathsf { p H } 5 . 6 1$ ) at $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8$ for $1 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ , then transferred to MS medium for cultivation. Three days after transformation, cells were immersed in sucrose solution $( 0 . 3 \mathrm { g / m L } )$ for plasmolysis before detection. For PcMYB44, 35S:PcMYB44-eYFP and 35S-eYFP plasmids were transiently co-expressed with a nuclear marker (35S:H2BmCherry vector) (Martin et al., 2009) in N. benthamiana leaves. At 3 days postinfiltration, leaf tissues at the position of infiltration were collected for analysis. All samples were examined and fluorescence signals were measured using a TCS-SP8 confocal laser scanning microscopy (Leica Microsystems). 

# 4.5  |  Transcriptome analysis

Conference pear plantlets inoculated with B. dothidea spore suspension at 0, 1, and 3 dpi were collected. Total RNA was extracted using a modified cetyltrimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) method (Chang et al., 1993). Libraries for transcriptome analysis were constructed as previously reported (Wang et al., 2022) then sequenced using a BGISEQ500 platform (BGI). After filtering, more than 42 million clean reads from each sample were mapped to the pear genome (http://peargenome.njau.edu.cn) using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2015). Expression levels of transcripts were quantified by the RSEM software package (Li & Dewey, 2011). Differential expression analysis was conducted using PossionDis (Audic & Claverie, 1997) with log ratio ≥||1|| and false discovery rate ${ \tt s } 0 . 0 0 1 $ . 

# 4.6  |  RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNAs were extracted using the CTAB method (Chang et al., 1993) with slight modification. cDNAs were generated using HiScript II Q RT SuperMix with gDNA wiper (Vazyme). For miR397, the stem-loop primer for reverse transcription was added during cDNA synthesis (Chen et al., 2005). PcActin and PcU6 served as reference genes for analysis of expression levels of mRNA and miR397, respectively. The U6 sequence of pear was obtained by local BLAST search against the P. bretschneideri genome (http:// peargenome.njau.edu.cn) using U6 of maize (GenBank accession number X52315.1). RT-qPCR was carried out on a CFX96 Real-time System (Bio-Rad) using ChamQ Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix 

(Vazyme). Each analysis included three biological replicates for each treatment. Relative expression levels were quantified by the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C t }$ method (Livak & Schmittgen, 2001). Primers used in RT-qPCR are listed in Table S6. 

# 4.7  |  Northern blotting analysis of miR397 expression

Northern blotting was performed as previously reported (Rio, 2014). Low-molecular-weight RNA was obtained using the polyethylene glycol (PEG) method (Wang et al., 2009). Approximately $4 \mu \ g$ of lowmolecular-weight RNA for each sample was separated on a $1 5 \%$ polyacrylamide gel containing 7 M urea and transferred to a nylon membrane (GE Healthcare). Membranes were exposed to UV light for 1 min with the RNA surface facing up to cross-link RNA. Blots were hybridized using digoxigenin-labelled DNA probes after 2 h of prehybridization at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The $5 ^ { \prime }$ end-modified digoxigenin-labelled probes were synthesized by the Tianyihuiyuan Biotech Company. After two stringency washes using $_ { 2 \times }$ saline sodium citrate (SSC) and $0 . 5 { \times } 5 5 { \mathsf C }$ sequentially, digoxigenin signals were immunologically detected by a ChemiDoc Touch Imaging System (Bio-Rad). Pear U6 small nuclear RNA served as a loading control. The signals intensity of U6 and PcmiR397 were quantitatively analysed using Image Lab software. Sequences of probes are listed in Table S6. 

# 4.8  |  Computational prediction and experimental verification of miR397 target genes in pear

Computational prediction of PcmiR397 target genes was conducted by local BLASTn search as previously reported (Allen et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2015; Schwab et al., 2005). The cleavage site in the PcLAC genes mediated by PcmiR397 was validated by RNA ligase-mediated (RLM)- $5 ^ { \prime }$ -RACE (Llave et al., 2011). 5′ DNA adaptor was synthesized by the Tianyihuiyuan Biotech Company and this was ligated to $3 \mu \ g$ of total RNA from Conference pear in vitro plantlets. cDNA was synthesized from the verified ligated RNA as template using oligo(dT) primers. Cleavage products of target genes were amplified by nested PCR using $5 ^ { \prime }$ adaptors and ${ } ^ { 3 \prime }$ gene-specific nested primers. In addition, cleavage site verification of $P p L A C s$ in Jinshui No. 2 was performed using a Smarter RACE Kit (Clontech). Sequences of the $5 ^ { \prime }$ adaptor and genespecific primers are listed in Table S6. PCR products were ligated to the pMD18-T vector (Takara) and 5–10 sequenced randomly selected clones were sequenced by the Tianyihuiyuan Biotech Company to identify and analyse the cleavage sites. 

# 4.9  |  Co-transformation of miR397 and target genes in N. benthamiana leaves

The cleavage activity of Pc-MIR397 toward PcLAC genes was examined by transient expression in N. benthamiana leaves. Precursors of 

PcmiR397 were cloned using genomic DNA from Conference leaves and incorporated into pART27 (Gleave, 1992). The predicted target sites of PcLACs for miR397 were amplified and sequenced from cDNA of Conference or obtained from assembled RNA-Seq data. DNA fragments of six PcLAC target sites and mutated target sites (mPcLAC, cleavage sites at the 10th and 11th bases changed from GC to AT) synthesized by annealing primers were fused to the N-terminus of GFP between EcoRI and XbaI sites of the pMS4 vector. The primers are listed in Table S6. Each construct was separately transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101. Agrobacterium cells harbouring the different constructs (35S:PcLAC-GFP, 35S:mPcLAC-GFP, 35S:MIR397) were cultured at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ overnight in YEB medium containing kanamycin $( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L } )$ ) and rifampicin $( 5 0 \mathrm { m g } / \mathsf { L } )$ . Bacteria were then resuspended in MMA buffer. The A. tumefaciens bacterial fluid from cells transformed with different recombinant expression constructs (35S:PcLACts-GFP+35S:MIR397, 35S:mPcLACts-GFP+35S:MIR397, 35S:PcLACts-GFP) were adjusted to $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8$ and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves for transient expression assay. Fluorescence signals of leaves were observed at 48 hpi under UV light. Meanwhile, GFP mRNA expression levels were analysed by RT-qPCR using three independent biological replicates. 

# 4.10  |  Identification and phylogenetic tree analysis of PcLACs and PcMYB44

The coding sequences of PcLACs and PcMYB44 were amplified using cDNA as template from Conference leaves with the primers listed in Table S6. PcLAC sequences were analysed and conserved domains were predicted using the NCBI website (https:// www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/cdd). Next, tBLASTn and BLASTp were used to search for amino acid sequences in Arabidopsis, apple, and pear that were highly similar to PcLACs and PcMYB44, which were downloaded from NCBI databases for phylogenetic tree analysis using MEGA 6.0 with the neighbour-joining (NJ) method (Tamura et al., 2013). Multiple sequence alignments were further analysed using DNAMAN software. 

# 4.11  |  Transient transformation in pear fruits and leaves and analysis of PcmiR397 and PcLAC functions

For the 35S:PcMIR397 and 35S:STTM397 constructs, the miR397 precursor was cloned from Conference genomic DNA using genespecific primers as described above. A DNA fragment comprising two Pc-miR397 partially complementary sequences and a 48 bp hairpin spacer (STTM-397) was synthesized to silence mature miR397 (Yan et al., 2012). The fragments of PcMIR397/sttm397 were inserted into the BamHI and XhoI sites in pART7, and the constructs were separately transferred into the CaMV35S-MIR397/sttm397-ocs fragment from pART7 using the NotI site in pART27 (Gleave, 1992). 

For the 35S:PcLAC2, 35S:PcLAC4, and 35S:PcLAC7 constructs, the coding regions of target gene PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7 were 

amplified from Conference cDNA. The coding sequences of PcLAC2, PcLAC4, and PcLAC7 were cloned into the binary vector pBI121 between endonuclease sites SmaI and SacI. The obtained constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101 as described above. The resulting Agrobacterium suspension was adjusted to an appropriate concentration $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 1 . 0 )$ ) and incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 2 h before pear infiltration. 

Transient transformation in pear fruits was performed using Jinshui No. 2 pear fruits sampled from an orchard at Huazhong Agricultural University (Wuhan, China) at 30 days after flowering. Agrobacterium suspension in MMA buffer $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ adjusted to 1.0) harbouring different constructs was infiltrated into fruits by injection with a 1-mL syringe. Three days later, A. alternata HB and B. dothidea LW-P were separately inoculated at the injection site. The disease spot diameter was measured for nine transformed fruits inoculated with A. alternata and B. dothidea at 48 hpi. 

For transient transformation in pear leaves, young expanded leaves of 3-week-old in vitro-grown Conference plantlets were detached and infiltrated in $3 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of Agrobacterium suspension $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ adjusted to 1.0) under a vacuum of $0 . 0 8 \mathsf { m P a }$ for 5 min. The leaves were then placed on MS medium and cultured at $2 5 { \pm } 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a 16/8 h photoperiod for 2 days for inoculation of A. alternata and B. dothidea. 

# 4.12  |  Evaluation of disease progression and quantification of fungal relative biomass in pear leaves

According to the area of disease spots relative to the whole leaf area, disease progression of infected leaves was categorized into four levels: level 1, ${ < } 1 0 \%$ ; level 2, $10 \% - 2 5 \%$ ; level 3, $2 6 \% - 5 0 \%$ ; level 4, $50 \%$ . At least 20 leaves were examined for each construct in two or three independent experiments. 

Quantification of fungal relative biomass in infected leaves was performed as previously reported (Jiang et al., 2020) with slight modifications. DNA was extracted from leaves sampled at 48 hpi infected with A. alternata or B. dothidea. qPCR assays were performed using DNA with primers specific for P. communis actin, A. alternata GAPDH (GenBank accession number: XM_018523704.1) or B. dothidea 18S rRNA genes (Wang et al., 2018). Relative expression levels of AaGAPDH and Bd18S genes were calculated using PcActin as an internal control and presented as fungal relative biomass using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C t }$ method (Livak & Schmittgen, 2001). 

# 4.13  |  Determination of lignin content in pear

The method was carried out as previously described (Syros et al., 2004) with some modifications. Plant materials were ground into powder and freeze-dried for $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ . Powder was washed four times with $9 5 \%$ ethanol, $_ { 2 \mathsf { m L } }$ of $2 5 \%$ (vol/vol) acetyl bromide in acetic acid was added to each sample, and samples were placed in a $7 0 \textdegree$ water bath for 30 min. A 0.9-mL volume of 2 M NaOH was added to terminate the reaction, followed by $0 . 1 \mathsf { m L }$ of $7 . 5 { \sf M }$ hydroxylamine 

and 3 mL of acetic acid. The absorbance at 280 nm $( \mathsf { A } _ { 2 8 0 } )$ and lignin weight were calculated according to a standard curve generated using alkali lignin (Sigma-Aldrich). The lignin content was calculated as a percentage (lignin weight/dry weigh $\mathbf { \nabla } \cdot \mathbf { \times } 1 0 0$ ). Data analysis of each treatment included three independent biological replicates. 

# 4.14  |  Prediction of TFBSs and cis elements in the PcMIR397 promoter

Approximately 1.9-kb sequences upstream from the transcription starting site (TSS) of MIR397 at two genomic loci (scaffold 1.0, scaffold 1039) according to the P. bretschneideri genome (http://pearg enome.njau.edu.cn/) were isolated from P. communis genomic DNA by PCR and sequenced as the promoter regions of PcMIR397. 

For cis-acting element analysis, promoter sequences of PcMIR397 were scanned for known cis-acting elements using the PlantCARE database (http://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/ plantcare/html/) (Lescot et al., 2002). 

To predict TFBSs involved in the regulation of PcMIR397, promoter sequences were screened using the PlantPAN database (http://PlantPAN2.itps.ncku.edu.tw). The similarity score was set at 0.95. 

To predict candidate TFs binding to the PcMIR397 promoter, promoter sequences of PcMIR397 were analysed by “Binding Site Prediction” using the online tool PlantRegMap (http://plantregmap. gao-lab.org/index.php) or “Promoter Analysis” of PlantPAN 3.0 (http://plantpan.itps.ncku.edu.tw/index.html).

et al., 1987). GUS staining and GUS activity determination were repeated three times independently. 

# 4.16  |  Y1H analysis

The promoter fragments from Pc-MIR397 at scaffold 1.0 and scaffold 1039 were inserted into the SacI/SmaI sites of the pAbAi vector (Clontech) to generate bait constructs pAbAi-pMIR397-1.0 and pAbAi-pMIR397-1039, respectively. The bait constructs were linearized by BstBI and transformed into the Y1HGold yeast (Coolaber) genome to generate bait strains. About 10 colonies were examined by PCR using Matchmaker Insert Check PCR Mix 1 (Clontech). Bait cells were resuspended in double-distilled water, the ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ was adjusted to 0.002, and $1 0 0 \mu \iota$ of cells was plated onto SD/−Leu medium containing different concentrations of AbA to determine the minimal inhibitory AbA concentration. The coding sequences of 10 predicted TFs (PcMYB44, PcMYB86-like, PcMYB315, PcMYB21, PcMYBS1, PcMYB108-1, PcMYB108-2, PcMYB86, PcNAC18, and PcNAC83; Table S5) were separately inserted into the pGADT7 vector at SacI/SmaI sites to generate prey constructs. The prey constructs and empty vector (AD) were transformed separately into bait yeast cells. The transformed yeast cells were first selectively cultured on SD plates lacking Leu medium. Positive colonies were picked, serially diluted $1 0 ^ { 3 }$ , and cultured on SD/−Leu medium containing AbA. The AbA concentration was $2 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ for pMIR397- 1.0 and $3 0 0 \mathrm { n g / m L }$ for pMIR397-1039. 

# 4.17  |  Dual luciferase assay analysis of transcriptional activity

Dual luciferase reporter assays were carried out in N. benthamiana leaves according to a previous report (Hellens et al., 2005). The putative promoters of PcMIR397 at scaffold 1.0 and scaffold 1039, amplified from Conference pear, were inserted into the NotI site of pGreen0800-LUC to obtain two reporters. The coding sequences of three TFs, PcMYB44, PcMYB108-1, and PcMYB86-like, were incorporated into pBI121 to generate effectors. The pBI121 empty vector served as a negative control. All plasmids were introduced into A. tumefaciens GV3101. Bacteria were cultured then resuspended in MMA buffer to $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8$ . Cell suspensions were mixed and infiltrated into tobacco leaves. Luciferase activity was measured 72 h after infiltration using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega) and a SPARK microplate reader (Tecan). The relative ratio of LUC:REN was calculated based on three biological replicates for each effector/reporter combination.
