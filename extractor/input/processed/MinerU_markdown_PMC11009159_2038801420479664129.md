# ABSTRACT

Plants are constantly exposed to microbial pathogens in the environment. One branch of innate plant immunity is mediated by cell-membrane-localized receptors, but less is known about associations between DNA damage and plant immune responses. Here, we show that rice (  mesophyll cells are prone to Oryza sativa)DNA double-stranded breaks (DSBs) in response to ZJ173, a strain of   pv. Xanthomonas oryzae oryzae( ). The DSB signal transducer ataxia telangiectasia mutated (ATM), but not the ATM and Rad3-related Xoobranch, confers resistance against . Mechanistically, the MRE11–ATM module phosphorylates suppres-Xoosor of gamma response 1 (SOG1), which activates several phenylpropanoid pathway genes and prompts downstream phytoalexin biosynthesis during  infection. Intriguingly, overexpression of the topoisomerase gene  causes a switch from the classic non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway to the alter-TOP6A3native NHEJ and homologous recombination pathways at induced DSBs. # INTRODUCTION

Plants frequently encounter various pathogenic microbes in the environment throughout their lifespans (Chisholm et al., 2006; Jones and Dangl, 2006; Boch et al., 2009). In response to pathogen invasion, plants have evolved mechanisms to recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) via receptor-like kinases and receptor-like proteins that activate downstream defense responses broadly referred to as PAMPtriggered immunity (Jones and Dangl, 2006; Boller and Felix, 

2009; Tang et al., 2017). In addition to generating PAMPs, invading pathogens can also damage host DNA, and plants have likewise evolved mechanisms to detect this damage and trigger the induction of pathogenesis-related gene expression in response (Kunz et al., 2006; Song and Bent, 2014). 

# Plant Communications

Genomic DNA double-stranded breaks (DSBs) have potentially lethal consequences, as they can separate parts of chromosome arms from their centromeres. Two primary mechanisms are involved in DSB repair in somatic cells: homologous recombination (HR) and non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) (Puchta, 2005; San Filippo et al., 2008; Ciccia and Elledge, 2010; Lieber, 2010; Sasaki et al., 2010). The HR pathway requires DNA sequence homology and is considered to be a precise repair mechanism (San Filippo et al., 2008; Moynahan and Jasin, 2010; Sasaki et al., 2010). By contrast, sequence homology is not required for the NHEJ pathway, making it less precise; however, this pathway appears to be the predominant DSB repair mechanism employed by most higher eukaryotes (Mahaney et al., 2009). Currently, at least two types of somatic HR pathway (Puchta, 1998; Siebert and Puchta, 2002; Xu et al., 2018) and most likely three types of NHEJ pathway appear to operate in plant cells (Charbonnel et al., 2010, 2011). In our previous study, we showed that the TOP6 complex (TOP6A3 and TOP6B) functions as a classic NHEJ (c-NHEJ) inhibitor in $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ -stressed rice plants (Xu et al., 2023). Irradiation-induced $\gamma { \mathsf { - H } } 2 { \mathsf { A } } \times$ kinetics revealed the hierarchical gorganization of DSB repair pathways in Arabidopsis somatic cells (Charbonnel et al., 2011), in which presence of the c-NHEJ pathway inhibits the activity of other DSB repair processes such as HR, alternative NHEJ (alt-NHEJ), and microhomology-mediated end joining. We previously showed that KU70 restricts the DSB localization of both COM1 (an alt-NHEJ factor) and XRCC3 (an HR factor) in rice somatic cells (Xu et al., 2020). 

In general, responses to somatic DSBs in eukaryotes are governed by theprotein kinases ataxia telangiectasia mutated(ATM) and ATM and Rad3-related (ATR) (Kurz and Lees-Miller, 2004; Shechter et al., 2004). In Arabidopsis, the Mre11–Rad50–Nbs1 complex is required for generation of $\Upsilon$ -H2AX by ATM and ATR kinases in response to girradiation-induced DSBs (Amiard et al., 2010). Suppressor of gamma response 1 (SOG1) is the major activator in the DSB signaling pathway and functions by directly targeting important regulatory genes, including transcription factors, repair factors, and early cell-cycle regulators (Yoshiyama et al., 2013). 

Plants can also produce anti-microbial secondary metabolites, known as phytoalexins, as another line of defense against invading pathogens. Diterpenoid and flavonoid phytoalexins are produced in rice leaves in response to both pathogens and environmental stressors (Cartwright et al., 1981, 1997; Kodama et al., 1988, 1992; Kato et al., 1993, 1994; Park et al., 2014). Most phytoalexins identified in rice, excluding sakuranetin, are diterpenoid compounds (Park et al., 2014). 

Bacterial blight disease of rice is caused by the bacterial pathogen Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae (Xoo) (Mew, 1987), against which some rice cultivars encode the Xanthomonas resistance receptor kinase genes XA21 and XA3, which confer robust Xoo resistance (Xiang et al., 2006). Here, we found that Xoo (ZJ173) infection causes DSBs in rice mesophyll cells (MCs) and that the MRE11– ATM–SOG1 DSB signaling pathway helps to confer robust resistance to Xoo. Phosphorylated SOG1 (SOG1-p) activates the phenylpropanoid pathway, which is required for efficient biosynthesis of phenolic phytoalexins in Xoo-infected leaves. Importantly, overexpression of TOP6A3 represses the DSBrecruitment of KU70, the first protein in the c-NHEJ repair process. This remodeling of the DSB repair process causes

# RESULTS

Rice MCs are prone to DSBs in response to  (ZJ173) 

Pathogens can trigger somatic DNA damage in plants (Song and Bent, 2014). To investigate whether DNA fragmentation occurs in rice MCs upon Xoo (ZJ173) infection, we performed comet assays and $\Upsilon$ -H2AX immunostaining on MC nuclei from the rice gcultivar Dongjin (DJ) after $4 8 { \textrm { h } }$ of ZJ173 treatment (Figure 1A). $\gamma { \mathsf { - H } } 2 { \mathsf { A } } X$ $\Upsilon$ foci were rarely detectable in nuclei of MCs undergoing ginterphase in healthy leaves, whereas strong signals of $\Upsilon$ -H2AX gfoci were seen in the Xoo-infected leaves (Figure 1A). Xooinfected DJ leaves had $2 6 . 7 ~ \pm ~ 1 6 . 5$ foci per nucleus, and $4 3 . 7 \% \pm 1 2 . 1 \%$ of the nuclei had ${ > } 5$ foci (Figure 1C). Clear comet tails were detected in the Xoo-infected leaves but not in the control samples (Figures 1A and 1C). Interestingly, we observed no comet tails after a 48-h treatment with the Arabidopsis-specific pathogenic bacterium DC3000 (Figure 1B). Therefore, these biochemical results indicate that rice MCs are prone to DSBs in response to the rice-specific pathogen ZJ173 but not to the Arabidopsis-specific pathogen DC3000. 

We next sought to determine whether rice mutants of the essential DSB repair genes KU70 (ku70 mutant) and ATM (atm-1 mutant) experienced more DSBs in response to ZJ173 infection. We detected significantly higher $\Upsilon$ -H2AX signals in ku70 (3.17-fold) gand atm-1 (2.29-fold) compared with their corresponding wildtype (WT) lines, DJ and GLA4 (Figures 1A and 1C). Likewise, in comet assays, the comet tail moment was significantly greater in ku70 and atm-1 mutants than in their WT counterparts (Figures 1A and 1C). Together, these results indicate that Xoo infection can trigger DNA damage in leaf MCs and that loss of function of DSB repair genes causes increased accumulation of $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -H2AX signal and ga greater comet tail moment, suggesting that DSB repair may play an important role in rice defense responses against Xoo.

In Arabidopsis, the Mre11–Rad50–Nbs1 complex is the first sensor of DSBs and is required for generation of $\Upsilon$ -H2AX by ATM and ATR gkinases in response to irradiation-induced DSBs (Amiard et al., 2010). # ATM kinase is critical for biosynthesis of phenolic phytoalexins in -infected rice leaves

Phenolic phytoalexins are known to function as defense-related compounds with anti-microbial activity in pathogen-infected or UV-treated rice leaves (Park et al., 2014). We therefore tested whether the MRE11–ATM–SOG1 pathway regulates rice resistance responses to Xoo infection by affecting the biosynthesis of phenolic phytoalexins. We first measured the production of several essential phenolic phytoalexins, including the flavonoid sakuranetin and the four phenylamides $N \cdot$ -cinnamoyltyramine (CinTyr), N-benzoyltryptamine (BenTrp), $N \cdot$ -cinnamoyltryptamine (CinTrp), and $N { - } p$ -coumaroylserotonin 

(CouSer), in rice leaves that had undergone $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ -irradiation treatment g(Figure 4A; Supplemental Data 3). These results indicate that $\boldsymbol { \Upsilon }$ g-irradiation can stimulate gbiosynthesis of phenolic phytoalexins in rice leaves and that this process is positively and negatively regulated by ATM and KU70, respectively.

# DISCUSSION

In this study, we demonstrated that the MRE11–ATM–SOG1 pathway in rice activates phenolic phytoalexin biosynthesis to confer resistance against Xoo infection via the host DSB response. Importantly, wefound that overexpression of TOP6genes (TOP6A3 or TOP6B) serves as a molecular switch that causes a shift from use of the c-NHEJ pathway to use of the alt-NHEJ pathway at Xooinduced DSB sites of infected rice leaves, thereby amplifying the ATM signal and the ATM-mediated resistance response. 

As reported previously in Arabidopsis (Yoshiyama et al., 2013), the orthologous protein SOG1 in the SOG-FLAG-OE1 line was phosphorylated after treatment with 5 and $1 0 \ \mu { \sf g / m } $ BLM m(Figure 2C). Consistent with this result, we did not observe SOG1 phosphorylation in the atm-3 SOG-FLAG-OE1 line in response to BLM treatment (Figure 2C). We therefore provide evidence that SOG1 phosphorylation is performed by ATM kinase during the DSB response in rice. -H2AX foci and comet tails were not gdetectable in MC nuclei of control plants. However, clear signals were detected in Xoo-infected MCs of WT seedlings (Figures 1A and 1C), and an even stronger DSB signal was observed in the 

ku70 and atm-1 mutants relative to WT lines (Figures 1A and 1C). The SOG1 protein was also phosphorylated in the SOG-FLAG-OE1 line at $4 8 \ \mathsf { h }$ after Xoo infection (Figure 2C). Based on these results, we suspect that Xoo infection generates DSBs and activates a DSB response in rice leaves. 

Upon inoculation with ZJ173, the susceptibility of the atr mutant was almost identical to that of its WT counterpart; however, the atm-2 mutant displayed a hypersensitive phenotype (Figures 3C and 3D). We concluded that the signal transducer ATM, but not ATR, plays an important role in DSB-responsemediated resistance to Xoo. We observed shorter lesions in the SOG1-FLAG-OE1 line, whereas the atm-3 sog1 double mutant displayed a phenotype similar to that of the single mutants atm-3, sog1, and mre11, forming longer lesions upon ZJ173 inoculation (Figures 3A and 3B). On the basis of these genetic analyses, we deduce that the DSB signaling pathway MRE11–ATM–SOG1 participates in the immune response to Xoo infection. Because the c-NHEJ pathway is the most efficient DSB repair pathway in higher eukaryotes, the resistant phenotype of the ku70 mutant suggests that a deficiency in the c-NHEJ pathway leads to Xoo resistance by causing a switch from the c-NHEJ pathway to the alt-NHEJ pathway, leading to super-activation of the MRE11–ATM– SOG1-mediated immune response. 

The contents of five phytoalexins (CinTyr, BenTrp, CinTrp, CouSer, and sakuranetin) were markedly higher in rice seedlings exposed to $\Upsilon$ -irradiation than in controls (Figure 4C). However, 

the induction of phytoalexin production by $\Upsilon$ -irradiation was glargely suppressed in the atm-1 mutant (Figures 4B and 4C). Consistent with this result, the contents of these phenolic phytoalexins were also increased in ZH11 plants after $4 8 { \textrm { h } }$ of Xoo infection but were compromised in the atm-3 mutant (Figure 4D). These results support the notion that the ATM– SOG1 module is required for phytoalexin biosynthesis resulting from the DSB response induced by Xoo. Four genes involved in phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Os01g0897600, Os07g0677600, Os04g0688300, and Os03g0235000) were markedly upregulated in ZH11 seedlings after $\Upsilon$ -irradiation treatment or Xoo infection, but this response was compromised in the mre11, atm-3, and sog1 mutants (Figure 5C). ChIP–qPCR analysis confirmed that SOG1 bound to the proposed motif regions of the gene promoters in SOG1-FLAG-OE1 seedlings under both $\Upsilon$ -irradiagtion treatment and ZJ173 infection (Figure 5D). A previous study revealed that the phenolic portion of phenylamides (phytoalexins) might be synthesized from phenylalanine through the phenylpropanoid pathway (Park et al., 2013). These lines of evidence support our hypothesis that SOG1-p activates phenylpropanoid pathway genes, which are required for phytoalexin biosynthesis during Xoo infection. 

After ZJ173 infection, the ku70 and TOP6A3-OE1 rice lines exhibited lower LIG4 signals but stronger COM1 signals compared with their respective WT lines (Figures 6C and 6E). In both ku70 and TOP6A3-OE1 lines, the RAD51-specific signal was absent under control conditions but appeared after ZJ173 infection (Figures 6C and 6E). Thus, in the window of protein loading, we determined that TOP6A3 overexpression restricts the c-NHEJ process but boosts the alt-NHEJ or HR process in response to Xoo-induced DSBs. Unlike the TOP6A3-OE1 line, which exhibited a resistant phenotype, the atm-2 TOP6A3-OE1 line was similar to the atm-2 mutant, exhibiting a hypersensitive phenotype to ZJ173 infection (Figures 3C and 3D); this results suggests that ATM acts downstream of TOP6A3. The ku70 and TOP6A3-OE1 rice lines exhibited a similar response to Xoo infection, with greater phytoalexin levels and accelerated biosynthetic kinetics of sakuranetin and CinTyr compared with WT seedlings (Figures 4D and 4E). Based on this genetic and biochemical evidence, we suggest that TOP6A3 overexpression enhances MRE11–ATM–SOG1- regulated phytoalexin biosynthesis by attenuating the efficiency of c-NHEJ repair in Xoo-infected leaves. 

The mre11, atm-3, sog1, and atm-3 sog1 mutants also exhibited hypersensitive responses to M. oryzae (BR22) (Supplemental Figures 3A and 3B), whereas the c-NHEJ-deficient lines (ku70, TOP6A3-OE1, and TOP6B-OE1) exhibited no resistance response to BR22 compared with their respective WTs. Intriguingly, the BR22-induced DSB level in TOP6A3-OE1 and TOP6B-OE1 lines appeared similar to that of WT K3 (Supplemental Figure 3C and 3D). We speculate that BR22 infection may trigger a regulatory mechanism in WT plants that impairs the c-NHEJ repair process. Our study suggests that the TOP6-facilitated ATM pathway could be used to confer resistance against Xoo but not M. oryzae. 

In our previous study, we found that the ku70 mutant exhibits sporophytic sterility at the reproductive stage (Xu et al., 2020), which hinders its use in breeding applications. The TOP6A3- OE1 and TOP6B-OE1 seedlings exhibited normal growth at 

both the vegetative and reproductive stages (Figure 7A), demonstrating that the TOP6-facilitated mechanism confers strong disease resistance without causing sterility. Although both heading and harvest dates were earlier in the TOP6 overexpression lines (Supplemental Figure 2A), no obvious differences were observed in the yield of viable seed (Supplemental Figure 2A). Therefore, we propose that the TOP6-facilitated ATM signal could be used to enhance Xoo resistance in rice without growth costs by altering the hierarchical organization of DSB repair pathways, producing a super-activation of phytoalexin biosynthesis (Figure 7B). 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The mutant rice lines (ku70, atm-1, atm-2, and atr), overexpression lines (TOP6A3-OE1 and TOP6B-OE1), and atm-2 TOP6A3-OE1 line were reported in our previous study (Xu et al., 2023). The mre11 mutant and the MRE11RNAi line were reported in our other study (Xu et al., 2020). To generate the SOG1-FLAG-OE1 overexpression line, we cloned the coding sequence of SOG1 with a 33 FLAG sequence into the pCAMBIA1300 vector, which harbors the cauliflower mosaic virus 35S promoter. The construct was transformed into the rice japonica variety ZH11. Homozygous lines from the $\mathsf { T } _ { 3 }$ generation were used for experimental analysis. For the GGUS line, rice plants were transformed with two copies of nonfunctional GUS reporter overlap fragments as recombination substrate (GGUS) (Yang et al., 2010). The $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ generations of TOP6A3-OE1 GGUS and TOP6A3-OE1 GGUS lines were obtained by crossing their respective lines. All plant materials used in this study were grown in paddy fields under normal conditions. Complete information about all rice lines is provided in Supplemental Table 1. The primers used for genotype identification are given in Supplemental Table 2. 

# RT–qPCR analysis

For RT–qPCR analysis, total RNA was extracted from rice leaves using a Trelief RNApre Pure Plant Kit (Tsingke, code: TSP411) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA samples with no residual genomic DNA were reverse transcribed to first-strand cDNA using a Goldenstar RT6 cDNA Synthesis Kit (Tsingke, code: TSK301S). qPCR was performed in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \up$ using $2 ~ \mu \mu \up$ cDNA template with $0 . 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ each reverse and forward mprimers $( 1 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ m and $1 0 \mu \nu 2 \times$ mTSINGKE Master qPCR Mix (Tsingke, code: TSE201) on a qTOWER 3.0 real-time PCR instrument (Analytik Jena). Fluorescence was quantified against standards. The PCR conditions were as follows: 5 min at $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ , followed by 41 cycles a $\mathfrak { s } 5 ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 10 s, ${ 5 } 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 s, and ${ 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 30 s. Melting temperatures were determined for each gene product. UBQ (Os03g0234200) was used as an internal control (housekeeping gene) for normalization of expression levels. Relative expression levels were evaluated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). Three biological replicates were performed for each gene. Sequences of the qPCR primers are given in Supplementary Table S2. All chemicals for RT– qPCR analysis were purchased from Tsingke Biotechnology (Beijing, China).

Protein concentrations were analyzed with a BCA Protein Assay Kit (Pierce, Rockford, IL, USA). One hundred micrograms of protein per well were resolved by SDS–PAGE in $12 \%$ gels and electrophoretically transferred to a Hybond-P membrane (GE Healthcare UK). The membrane was treated with primary antibody (FLAG) and then incubated with secondary anti-rabbit immunoglobulin G antibody coupled with horseradish peroxidase (Sigma, Cream Ridge, NJ, USA). # ChIP

Rice leaf samples (5 g) were cross-linked in buffer containing $4 \%$ (w/v) formaldehyde. Nuclei were isolated, and chromatin was sheared by sonication into 200- to 600-bp fragments. Sonicated chromatin was precleared with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ protein A–agarose for $1 . 5 \mathrm { h }$ and immunoprecipitated into two fractions: one fraction with anti-FLAG for SOG1-bound chromatin and one fraction with anti-histone H3 (AHO1432, Invitrogen, 1.5–2 mg) as a non-specific chromatin-binding protein. Chromatin-bound DNA fragments were eluted after reverse cross-linking by incubation overnight with proteinase K at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The incubation was repeated at $\mathtt { 6 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ overnight, and the DNA was then purified following the manufacturer’s instructions. ChIP assays were performed using three biological replicates, and qPCR analysis was carried out in triplicate with primers designed to amplify the putative SOG1-binding motif region $[ \mathsf { C T } ( \mathsf { N } ) _ { 7 } \mathsf { A A G } ]$ for each ChIP assay (Supplemental Table 3). ChIP assays were performed by Orizymes Biotechnology (Shanghai, China). 

# Measurements of phenylamides from rice leaves

Samples were prepared from leaves of 3-week-old rice seedlings under normal growth conditions or after treatment ( -irradiation/36 h or ZJ173/ g60 h). Samples were ground in liquid nitrogen and extracted with $70 \%$ MeOH for 1 h. After centrifugation, the aqueous methanol extract was obtained and dried in vacuo. Residue was dissolved and fractionated in an ethyl acetate–water (1:1) mixture to enrich phenolic compounds. The ethyl acetate phase was dried in vacuo, and the residue was dissolved in MeOH and analyzed using a reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatograph and a Sunfire C18 column (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) with detection at 280 nm using elution conditions. Concentrations of CinTyr, BenTrp, CinTrp, CouSer, and sakuranetin were measured by Shanghai iProteome Biotechnology.

# Comet assay

Leaf tissues from control and ZJ173- or BR22-infected rice seedlings were used for preparation of MC nuclei. A single-cell gel electrophoresis assay (Comet) for DSB detection was performed with a commercial kit (CTCC-M001) as described previously (Kwon et al., 2013). Nuclear lysis was performed at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 h instead of at room temperature for 20 min. After staining with propidium iodide (Sigma, cat. no. p4170), comet images were captured using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus DP80) equipped with a charge-coupled device camera. DSB signals were analyzed using Comet Assay Software Project software. Thirty typical nuclei were selected for statistical analysis, and each measurement was performed three times. All chemicals for the comet assay were purchased from CTCC (Wuxi, China). 

# Bacterial treatment of detached rice leaves

Expanded adult leaves of 4-week-old rice seedlings were cut into 1-cm sections using surgical-grade scissors. Samples were placed into culture plates containing $1 . 5 \mathsf { m }$ half-strength Murashige and Skoog (MS) solution (with 10 mM MgCl2) for mock treatment or the same solution containing a fresh Xoo (ZJ173) cell suspension at an optical density 600 of 0.1. The plates were incubated under constant light. For biochemical analysis (comet, -H2AX, mass spectrometry, RT–qPCR, and ChIP–qPCR), samples were collected at 48 h post-treatment. For quantitative analysis of sakuranetin, CinTyr, BenTrp, CinTrp, and CouSer, samples were collected at $^ \mathrm { { 6 0 ~ h } }$ post-treatment. For biosynthetic kinetics of sakuranetin and CinTyr, samples were collected at 24, 36, 48, and 60 h post-treatment. 

# Bacterial inoculation

Rice plants were grown from seed under glasshouse conditions $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \pm$ $\mathfrak { l } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , $6 0 \% - 8 0 \%$ relative humidity). The Xoo strain ZJ173 was cultured on a nutrient agar slant (Becton Dickinson) containing $0 . 5 \%$ sucrose at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . This strain is virulent to Nipponbare. Bacterial blight resistance of rice plants grown in the field was tested at the heading stage. The fully opened fifth or flag leaf blades were inoculated using the clipping inoculation technique (Kauffman et al., 1973). The length of blight lesions was measured on each leaf at 8 or 14 days after inoculation. 

# Measurement of photosynthetic parameters

Seeds were sown on May 30 at 2022 at a Shanghai field base. For each genotype, 36 individuals were planted $( 6 \times 6 )$ with 20 cm between rows and 15 cm between plants in the same row. The rice fields were managed according to standard local agronomic practice as described previously (Qu et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2023). The recorded mean air temperature from the sowing date until the photosynthetic measurements was ${ \sim } 2 4 . 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \ ( \pm 3 . 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ . The middle sections of fully expanded leaves from 2- month-old plants were used for photosynthetic measurements. The experiments were conducted between 9:00 a.m. and 11:30 a.m. using a portable photosynthesis system (Li-COR, Lincoln, NE, USA). The temperature and ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentration in the cuvette chamber were maintained at $\boldsymbol { 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and 400 ppm, respectively. A light intensity of $1 0 0 0 \ \mathsf { \mu m o l \ m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ mwas used to assess photosynthetic rate under saturated light conditions. Four biological replicates were performed for each line.
