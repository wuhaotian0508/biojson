# ABSTRACT

Jasmonic acid (JA) is a key regulator of plant defense responses. Although the transcription factor MYC2, the master regulator of the JA signaling pathway, orchestrates a hierarchical transcriptional cascade that regulates the JA responses, only a few transcriptional regulators involved in this cascade have been described. Here, we identified the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) transcription factor gene in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), METHYL JASMONATE (MeJA)-INDUCED GENE (SIJIG), the expression of which was strongly induced by MeJA

treatment. Genetic and molecular biology experiments revealed that SIJIG is a direct target of MYC2. SIJIG knockout plants generated by gene editing had lower terpene contents than the wild type from the lower expression of TERPENE SYNTHASE (TPS) genes, rendering them more appealing to cotton bollworm (Helicoverpa armigera). Moreover, SIJIG knockouts exhibited weaker JA-mediated induction of TPSs, suggesting that SIJIG may participate in JA-induced terpene biosynthesis. Knocking out SIJIG also resulted in attenuated expression of JA-responsive defense genes, which may contribute to the observed lower resistance to cotton bollworm and to the fungus Botrytis cinerea. We conclude that SIJIG is a direct target of MYC2, forms a MYC2-SIJG module, and functions in terpene biosynthesis and resistance against cotton bollworm and B. cinerea.

Keywords: basic helix-loop-helix transcription factor, defense response, jasmonic acid, MYC2, terpene biosynthesis

# INTRODUCTION

Jasmonic acid (JA) is a vital phytohormone participating in plant defense responses, especially for resistance against necrotrophic pathogens and chewing insects (Glazebrook,

2005; Howe and Jander, 2008; Spoel and Dong, 2008; Mengiste, 2012; Campos et al., 2014). JA signaling has been well studied in the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana. Under non-challenged conditions, JASMONATE ZIM-DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins interact with the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH)

transcription factor (TF) MYC2 to repress its transcriptional activation activity. When jasmonoyl-isoleucine (JA-Ile), the signaling-active form of JA, binds to its cognate receptor CORONATINE INSENSITIVE 1 (COI1), JAZs are targeted for degradation via the 26S proteasome, which involves the coreceptor Skip/Cullin/F-box complex (SCF) SCF $^{COI1}$ , leading to the alleviation of transcriptional repression and the activation of JA signal transduction (Xie et al., 1998; Devoto et al., 2002; Thines et al., 2007; Yan et al., 2007, 2009). MYC2 is thought to be the master regulator in JA-mediated responses (Kazan and Manners, 2013; Wasternack and Hause, 2013). Indeed, MYC2 has been reported to orchestrate a hierarchical transcriptional cascade that regulates the JA responses, and its direct targets have recently been identified by chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by deep sequencing (ChIP-seq) in tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) and Arabidopsis (Du et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019). However, studies of the transcriptional regulation of JA signaling often concentrate on the master regulator MYC2 (Santner and Estelle, 2009; Kazan and Manners, 2013; Song et al., 2016), while little research has been conducted on the components downstream of MYC2.

Terpenes are a large class of plant metabolites that include monoterpenes, sesquiterpenes, diterpenes, triterpenes, tetraterpenes, and polyterpenes. The diverse array of plant terpenes function in various plant–environment interactions, such as attracting pollinating insects, participating in indirect defense responses, and protecting plants from herbivores and pathogens (such as with phytoalexins) (Kessler and Baldwin, 2001; Heil and Silva Bueno, 2007; Degenhardt et al., 2009; Karban and Shiojiri, 2009; Schmelz et al., 2011; Mithoefer and Boland, 2012; Byers et al., 2014; Thimmappa et al., 2014; Lange, 2015; Quintana-Rodriguez et al., 2015).

Members of the TERCPENE SYNTHASE (TPS) protein family catalyze the biosynthesis of monoterpene, sesquiterpene, diterpene, and polyterpene backbones. The transcriptional regulation of TPS gene expression is critical to the modulation of terpene biosynthesis (Nagegowda, 2010; Spyropoulou et al., 2014b). The transcriptional regulation of TPS genes has been studied in Arabidopsis and some medicinal plants, which revealed several contributing TF families, including bHLH-, WRKY-, and APETALA 2/ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR (AP2/ERF)-type TFs (Xu et al., 2004; Ma et al., 2009; Yu et al., 2012; Jiang et al., 2016). Among them, MYC2 is a key regulator of TPS expression. Several TFs that regulate TPS expression, including ERFs and WRKY1 in sweet wormwood (Artemisia annua), OCTADECANOID-DERIVATIVE RESPONSIVE CATHARANTHUS AP2-DOMAIN PROTEINS (ORCAs) in Madagascar periwinkle (Catharanthus roseus), and WRKY1 in tree cotton (Gossypium arboreum) are JA-responsive and regulated by MYC2 (Xu et al., 2004; Ma et al., 2009; Zhang et al., 2011; Yu et al., 2012). Besides the indirect regulation of terpene biosynthesis, MYC2 can directly bind to the promoters of TPS10, TPS11, and TPS21 in Arabidopsis and thus increase the contents of volatile terpenes (Hong et al., 2012; Li et al., 2014). The possible role of

other bHLH TFs in addition to MYC2 in TPS regulation has not been explored in detail.

The tomato genome encodes 34 functional TPS enzymes (Zhou and Pichersky, 2020), but transcriptional regulation of these TPS genes remains largely unknown. Although some tomato TPS genes are transcriptionally induced by JA treatment, the genes encoding TFs reported to regulate terpene biosynthesis, for example, EXPRESSION OF TERPENOIDS 1 (SIEOT1), SIWRKY73, and SIMYC1, are not activated by JA (Falara et al., 2011, 2014; Spyropoulou et al., 2014a, 2014b; Xu et al., 2018). In addition, SIMYC2 has not been implicated in the regulation of any TPS gene in tomato, indicating that the mechanism by which JA regulates TPS transcription is far from being understood.

In this study, we isolated a tomato JA-responsive gene, which we called METHYL JASOMATE (MeJA)-INDUCED GENE (SIJIG), and showed that it encodes a bHLH TF. We generated two independent jig mutants using clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR-associated protein 9 (Cas-9)-mediated genome editing method. The expression of TPS genes was down-regulated in jig mutants, and the contents of several terpenes were lower compared to the wild type. The jig mutants were more appealing to cotton bollworm (Helicoverpa armigera) and suffered greater damage from insect attacks. We also demonstrated that JIG is a direct target of MYC2 and functions downstream of MYC2 to regulate some JA responses. Moreover, we showed that jig mutants are more susceptible to infection by the fungus Botrytis cinerea than the wild type. We thus have identified a gene involved in the JA-mediated defense responses, providing insight into both insect and pathogen defense responses in an important horticultural crop.

# RESULTS

# SIJIG expression is strongly induced by MeJA

We identified a gene in tomato plants, the expression of which is strongly induced by exogenous MeJA treatment at a very early stage. This gene was upregulated 80-fold at $1\mathrm{h}$ after MeJA treatment and then rapidly decreased at $3\mathrm{h}$ , as seen by reverse-transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR). We did not observe this induction in jai1, a null mutant allele for the JA-Ile receptor COI1 (Figure 1). Because this gene was strongly induced by MeJA, we named it MeJA-INDUCED GENE (JIG). We further examined the response of SIJIG transcript levels to other phytohormones in the wild-type tomato cultivar Ailsa Craig (AC), including salicylic acid (SA), abscisic acid (ABA), ethephon, and gibberellic acid $(\mathrm{GA}_{3})$ , and determined that certain phytohormones also induced SIJIG expression, but to a weaker extent relative to that seen with MeJA (Figure S1). We conclude that SIJIG is strongly induced by MeJA in a COI1-dependent manner, which suggests that SIJIG may have a role in the JA-mediated pathway.

Phylogenetic analysis revealed that SIJIG is most closely related to a TF in another Solanaceae crop, tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) WOUND INDUCED4 (NtWin4), with $69.9\%$ identity (Figure S2B, C). To test its transcriptional activity, we cloned the SIJIG coding sequence in-frame with the sequence of the GAL4 DNA binding domain and transformed the resulting construct into yeast; however, this construct showed no transcriptional activation activity (Figure S2D). According to the tomato electronic Fluorescent Pictographic browser (http://bar.utoronto.ca/), SIJIG was expressed at low levels in whole-plant tissue and displayed its highest expression levels in the flower buds and small fruits (1 cm in diameter; Figure S2E).

We also examined the subcellular localization of SIJIG by transiently expressing a construct encoding SIJIG fused to the green fluorescent protein (GFP) in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. We detected fluorescence signals in the nucleus and other sites (Figure S2F). The translation of tobacco homolog NtWIN4 started at the second in-frame AUG, which altered its subcellular localization from the cytosol and nucleus to the chloroplast (Kodama and Sano, 2006). To test the subcellular localization of SIJIG, we generated a serial truncation series from the N-terminus of SIJIG by starting the SIJIG coding sequence at the second (aa 49-233), third (aa 84-233), or fourth (aa 107-233) methionine residue (Figure S2A) and cloning the resulting fragments upstream of the GFP sequence. All three truncated versions showed the same localization pattern as that of full-length SIJIG, indicating that although SIJIG shared a percentage identity with NtWIN4, SIJIG had a distinct subcellular localization pattern, suggesting that SIJIG may have a different function from that of its homologous proteins.

# Transcriptome analysis reveals that SIJIG regulates terpene biosynthesis

To determine the function of S1JIG, we generated two independent mutants using CRISPR/Cas-9-mediated genome editing by targeting the first S1JIG exon (Figure 2A). The jig-16 mutant allele carried a 2-bp deletion, while jig-1 had a 1-bp insertion; both mutations introduced a premature stop codon leading to early termination of translation after about 30 aa (Figures 2A, S3). We tested the potential for off-target editing events by sequencing the five most likely predicted off-target sites, as determined by the Cas-OFFinder tool (http://www. rgenome.net/cas-offinder/). Importantly, we identified no mutation at any of these sites (Figure S4; Table S1).

We further performed a transcriptome deep sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis to compare the transcriptome profiles of the jig-1 mutant and wild-type seedlings. All differentially expressed genes are listed in Table S2. As we hypothesized that SJJG may function in the JA-mediated response, we focused on Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis of differentially expressed genes involved in the JA response. KEGG analysis showed that numerous genes involved in terpene biosynthesis (terpenoid backbone biosynthesis, diterpenoid biosynthesis, sesquiterpenoid and triterpenoid biosynthesis) were down-regulated in the jig mutant compared to the wild type (Figure 2B). We validated these results by RT-qPCR, which confirmed that the expressions of TPS5, TPS9, TPS12, and TPS20 are lower in the jig mutants relative to the wild type (Figure 2C).

The lower expression levels of $TPS$ genes prompted us to measure the terpene contents in leaves from the $jig$ mutants and wild-type plants. Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analysis indicated that the levels of 12 terpenes accumulated to lower levels in $jig$ mutants relative to the wild type (Figure 3).

A previous study reported that exogenous JA treatment induces the expression of some TPS genes (Falara et al., 2014). Given the strong induction of S/IG by JA, we suspected that S/IG might contribute to this molecular response. Accordingly, we measured TPS transcript levels after MeJA treatment of jig and wild-type plants, and the JA-mediated induction of TPS genes was weaker in jig mutants than in wild-type seedlings (Figure 2C). Together, these results suggest that JIG regulates terpene biosynthesis in leaves by positively regulating the expression of a subset of TPS genes and may participate in JA-induced terpene biosynthesis.

# SIJIG functions in tomato resistance to the insects H. armigera and the fungus B. cinerea

Terpenoids are critical volatile compounds emitted by plants when they are confronted by herbivorous insects. We therefore investigated the role of SIJIG in response to insect herbivory using the leaf-chewing caterpillar, cotton bollworm (H. armigera). Feeding preference experiments showed that jig mutants are more attractive to larvae than the wild type (Figure 5A). We also measured the contents of green leaf volatiles, another important class of defense-related volatiles with repellent properties to herbivores similar to those of terpenes (Ameye et al., 2018). However, the contents of (E)-2-hexenal and (Z)-3-hexenal showed no difference between jig mutants and the wild type (Figure S8). These results suggest that the greater attraction of H. armigera larvae to the jig mutants is not related to green leaf volatiles. Furthermore, H. armigera larvae that fed on jig mutant leaves for 4 d were significantly heavier than those that fed on wild-type leaves (Figure 5B). As insect resistance has been linked to the activity of proteinase inhibitors (Pls; Ryan, 1990) in tomato, we examined the transcript levels of the PI-II gene after wounding, used here to simulate a herbivorous attack. PI-II transcript levels rose close to 464-fold in the wild type by wounding, but increased by about 300-fold in the jig mutants; PI-II expression was comparable across all genotypes before wounding (Figure 5C).

Phylogenetic analysis indicated that SIJIG is most closely related to NtWIN4 (Figure S2); however, these two proteins showed different subcellular localizations in $N$ . benthamiana leaves. Indeed, a previous study based on a NtWIN4-GFP fusion analysis established that full-length NtWIN4 localizes to the cytosol and nucleus, but subsequent cell fractionation experiments using tobacco cell extracts and a specific antibody raised against NtWIN4 showed that the protein is only present in the chloroplast fraction and is

smaller than its predicted molecular weight (Kodama and Sano, 2006). Further studies revealed that the transcript of NtWIN4 transcribed not from the first AUG codon, but the second in-frame AUG codon in vivo, resulting in production of a chloroplast transit peptide within the N-terminus of NtWIN4. The transit peptide is later cleaved upon successful import into the organelle, explaining the smaller apparent molecular weight. However, the subcellular localization of SIJIG remained the same in truncated and full-length forms

(Figure S2F). These results suggest that although SJJIG shares a high identity with NtWIN4, these proteins may exert different functions.

The role of phytohormones in defense responses to biotic stress has been extensively studied, especially with respect to JA, SA, and ethylene. The core JA signaling pathway has been uncovered, with MYC2 being the master regulator of the pathway. Here, we established that SJIG is a new target of SIMYC2, based on several lines of evidence. First, JIG expression was strongly induced by MeJA and B. cinerea infection (Figures 1, 6). Second, JIG expression was much lower and its induction by JA treatment was weaker or even absent in the myc2 mutant or MYC2 knockdown plants compared to the control plants (Figure 4B, C). Third, MYC2 physically interacted with the JIG promoter in vivo and in vitro (Figure 4). Moreover, just like JA2L and another JA-responsive gene ERF.3C (Du et al., 2017), JIG did not interact with JAZ proteins (Figure S6), indicating that SJIG likely functions downstream of MYC2. A previous study showed that MYC2 orchestrates a hierarchical transcriptional cascade to regulate the JA responses (Du et al., 2017). SIMYC2 induced the transcription of two of its target genes encoding the TFs JA2L and ERF.3C, forming modules that initiate and amplify the transcriptional output of MYC2 and triggering the activation of pathogen-responsive genes, thus regulating diverse aspects of JA responses (Du et al., 2017). In this study, we established a MYC2-SIJIG module that is responsible for terpene biosynthesis in tomato leaves, which might confer resistance against the herbivorous insect cotton bollworm (H. armigera). Notably, our Y2H results indicated that JIG did not interact with JA2L or ERF.3C (Figure S7), suggesting that SJIG might act independently from these two proteins with established functions in JA signaling. These results are consistent with the inference of Du et al. (2017) that other MYC2-targeted TF-encoding genes regulate JA-responsive genes.

![](images/b81692facad1030125b0afbe3608c3d3796b47d3d1dc25c9fa363960c09d461a.jpg)  
Figure 6. Schematic diagram of the role of Solanum lycopersicum METHYL JASMONATE (MeJA)-INDUCED GENE (SIJIG) in tomato defense response

SI/JIG is a direct target of MYC2 and functions in tomato resistance to insect Helicoverpa armigera and fungus Botrytis cinerea. SI/JIG may directly or indirectly positively regulate terpene synthase (TPS) genes to enhance terpene biosynthesis, or participate in classic jasmonic acid defense pathway, in order to reduce attraction to $H.$ armigera and inhibit growth of larvae and fungus B. cinerea.

Insect herbivory, pathogen infection, and JA application induce the emission of several terpenes in leaves (Tholl and Lee, 2011) and result in increased levels of $TPS$ transcripts (Ament et al., 2006; Falara et al., 2011). Numerous reports have indicated that the transcriptional control of $TPSs$ genes plays an important role in the regulation of terpene biosynthesis (De Geyter et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2012; Spyropoulou et al., 2014b), suggesting that JA-induced terpene biosynthesis is dependent on transactivation of $TPS$ genes. In Arabidopsis, $TPS21$ and $TPS11$ expression is induced by JA treatment via a AtMYC2-dependent pathway, as these two genes are direct targets of MYC2, leading to the accumulation of sesquiterpene (Hong et al., 2012). In addition, several monoterpene synthase gene expressions were decreased in the myc2-2 mutant and increased in MYC2-overexpressing plants, suggesting that MYC2 also regulates the biosynthesis of other types of terpenes. Arabidopsis MYC2 directly binds to the AtTPS10 promoter to modulate its transcription, resulting in the emission of the monoterpene $\beta$ -myrcene (Li et al., 2014). However, with the exception of $TPS10$ , $TPS11$ and $TPS21$ , other $TPS$ genes have not been reported to be directly regulated by MYC2 or other components of the JA signaling module in Arabidopsis leaves. Furthermore, Only three TFs, SIEOT1, SIWRKY73, and SIMYC1, have been described as regulators of terpene biosynthesis in tomato leaves (Spyropoulou et al., 2014a, 2014b; Xu et al., 2018). SIEOT1 and SIWRKY73 transactivated the $TPS5$ promoter, while SIMYC1 activated transcription from the $TPS3$ and $TPS5$ promoters, using reporter and effector constructs infiltrated in N. benthamiana leaves (Spyropoulou et al., 2014b). Although many SITPS gene (TPS3, TPS5, TPS7, TPS31, and TPS39) were indeed induced by JA, the expression of the genes encoding the three TFs mentioned above are not JA-responsive (Spyropoulou et al., 2014a, 2014b; Xu et al., 2018), indicating that other JA-responsive TFs must regulate $TPS$ expression. Here, we identified such a JA-induced TF, SIJIG, which positively regulates terpene biosynthesis in tomato leaves. We established that knockout of SIJIG broadly down-regulated the expression of $TPS$ family genes, which was accompanied by a decrease in the contents of various types of terpenes (Figures 2, 3). Further experiments confirmed that SIJIG is the direct target of MYC2, the core regulator of JA signaling. However, SIJIG did not interact with SIMYC1 or SIEOT1 in the Y2H assay (Figure S7), suggesting that SIJIG might mediate a pathway different from that of SIMYC1 and SIEOT1 to regulate terpene biosynthesis.

Terpenes contribute to plant defenses against herbivores and pathogens (Tholl and Lee, 2011; Tholl, 2015). Functions of terpenes were reported in various plant systems. For instance, excessive accumulation of $(E)$ - $\beta$ -caryophyllene in GhTPS1-overexpressing cotton plants rendered these plants less attractive to the insects Apolygus lucorum, Aphis gossypii, and $H.$ armigera, based on Y-tube assays and the field tests (Zhang et al., 2020). Overexpression of the linalool synthase gene GhTPS12 in tobacco plants increased their resistance against the aphid Myzus persicae and $H.$ armigera

(Huang et al., 2018). Transgenic tobacco plants overexpressing the (S)-linalool synthase gene (LIS) produced more (S)-linalool, and negatively affected oviposition of $H.$ armigera, but not larval development (McCallum et al., 2011). In rice (Oryza sativa), silencing OsLIS increased the attraction of the pest brown planthopper (Nilaparvata lugens) and reduced attraction of the parasitoid Anagrus nilaparvatae (Xiao et al., 2012). Plant-insect interactions are intricate, and we know little about the specific functions of terpenes. In this study, we showed that SJJG knockout plants emitted fewer terpenes than the wild-type plants (Figure 3). Based on this finding and the close relationship between terpenes and herbivory predation, we tested the attraction to $H.$ armigera of these knockout plants, which revealed that knockout plants were more attractive to $H.$ armigera. Plant-emitted volatiles that are responsible for repelling herbivores are dominated by terpenoids and green leaf volatiles (Zhang et al., 2020). We thus measured the contents for several green leaf volatiles but detected no difference in the contents of (E)-2-hexenal or (Z)-3-hexenal between jig mutants and the wild-type plants (Figure S8). These results indicate that the decreased attraction of cotton bollworm by jig mutants is not related to green leaf volatiles, but most likely to terpenes. Cotton bollworm larvae fed with an artificial diet supplemented with terpenes were reported to display growth retardation, indicating that terpenes also negatively affect insect larval growth (Singh et al., 2014). Consistent with these observations, we also determined that cotton bollworm larvae that fed on jig mutant leaves were significantly heavier than those that fed on wild-type leaves (Figure 5B). The expression of the defense-related gene PI-II was also lower in wounded jig mutant plants compared to wounded wild-type plants (Figure 5C), suggesting that jig mutants are impaired in a classic JA-mediated defense pathway. In the context of resistance against fungi, overexpression of the rice TPS19 enhanced resistance to Magnaporthe oryzae by inhibiting spore germination (Chen et al., 2018). In agreement, jig mutants infected with B. cinerea showed lower transcript levels for the JA-mediated defense-related genes JA2L, TD, and PR-STH2 (Figure 5), further supporting the notion that jig mutants are defective in the classic JA-mediated defense pathway. These results showed that jig mutants are characterized by decreased terpene biosynthesis and impaired classic JA-mediated defense responses, suggesting that SJJG may modulate resistance against the insect $H.$ armigera and the fungus $B.$ cinerea via these two different pathways.

In this study, we focused on the role of SIJIG in terpene biosynthesis under normal insect-free conditions, as we did not investigate its role when plants were subjected to herbivory predation. Further studies should consider how TPS gene expression and terpene contents change in wild-type and jig mutant plants in response to herbivory. Since the JA pathway plays a dominant role in host resistance against herbivore feeding (Widhalm et al., 2015), our results have important implications on research of host responses to herbivores.

Although we determined that SIJIG plays important roles in terpene biosynthesis and resistance against B. cinerea, its mode of action and interacting partners remain unknown. Because SIJIG did not exhibit transcriptional activation activity in yeast (Figure S2D), we hypothesize that SIJIG may interact with other proteins to actuate this function. We tested SIJIG for interaction with several proteins known to be involved in terpene biosynthesis and pathogen resistance (MYC1, EOT1, JA2L, and ERF.3C) (Spyropoulou et al., 2014a, 2014b; Du et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2018), but none appeared to interact with SIJIG in our conditions (Figure S7). Therefore, an outstanding question for further studies will be to identify the direct target(s) of SIJIG and the molecules it interacts with to mediate terpene biosynthesis and pathogen resistance.

In summary, we showed here that S1J1G, which is a direct target of MYC2, encodes a bHLH TF that participates in terpene biosynthesis and the JA-mediated defense response pathway against both H. armigera and B. cinerea (Figure 6). This work improves our understanding of the molecular networks regulating JA signaling and has potential applications for crop resistance against pathogens and insects.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Plant growth condition and treatment

Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) seedlings were grown in a greenhouse with a $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C} / 18^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ day/night temperature and a 16-h light/8-h dark cycle. Tomato cv. AC was used as the wild type for all plant materials in this study, except for the jai1 mutant, for which cv. Castlemart was used as the wild type. Because homozygous jai1 mutants are sterile, heterozygous plants were used for breeding, and all homozygous jai1 mutants used in this study were identified as previously described (Li et al., 2004). Three-week-old tomato seedlings were sprayed with various photohormones (100 $\mu$ mol/L MeJA, 100 $\mu$ mol/L ABA, 200 $\mu$ mol/L SA, 10 mmol/L ethephon, and 100 $\mu$ mol/L $\mathrm{GA}_3$ ). Seedlings sprayed with water were set as negative controls. For wounding assays, 3-week-old seedlings were wounded with a hemostat across the midrib of all leaves (Du et al., 2017).

# Subcellular localization

The full-length or truncated SIJIG coding sequence (without the stop codon) was amplified and cloned in-frame and upstream of the coding sequence of GFP into the pCAMBIA2300 vector to generate the fusion construct pCAMBIA2300: SIJIG-GFP. The resulting vector and the empty were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 and then infiltrated into the leaves of 4-week-old N. benthamiana plants. Rh-PIP2;1-mcherry was co-infiltrated as a plasma membrane marker (Ma et al., 2008). After 2 d, the infiltrated N. benthamiana leaves were harvested and mesophyll protoplasts were isolated. Images were captured using a confocal microscope (Olympus, Japan).

# Plasmid construction and tomato genetic transformation

The target site used for the CRISPR/Cas-9-mediated genome editing of SIJIG was selected by CCTop (https://crispr.cos.uni-heidelberg.de/) (Labuhn et al., 2018) and the single guide RNA (sgRNA) containing the selected target sites was cloned into the binary vector pBSE401 (provided by Professor Qijun Chen, China Agriculture University). The final binary vector pBSE401-JIG was transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101 and then into tomato cv. AC following the published protocol (Li et al., 2020). Briefly, tomato seeds were germinated on half-strength Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium, and cotyledons were cut into $1\mathrm{-cm} \times 1\mathrm{-cm}$ pieces after 10 d of culture. Cotyledon pieces were inoculated with Agrobacterium and then grown on selective medium containing 5 mg/L Basta until regenerated plants were obtained. Gene-edited plants were identified by PCR and sequencing. Homozygous and transgene-free mutants were present in the $T_{1}$ generation, and all mutant plants used for experiments were from the $T_{2}$ or $T_{3}$ generation (Figure S3). The sequences of all primers used are provided in Table S3.

# B. cinerea inoculation assay

The B. cinerea inoculation assay was performed as previously described with slight modifications (El Oirdi and Bouarab, 2007). B. cinerea (B05.10) was grown on potato dextrose agar medium at $22^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ under 10-h light/14-h dark cycles until conidia were produced. Conidia were harvested in sterile distilled $\mathsf{H}_2\mathsf{O}$ and suspended in potato dextrose broth medium to a titer of $10^{6}$ spores/mL after centrifugation (4 000 × g for 10 min at room temperature). Leaves of 4-week-old tomato plants were placed in Petri dishes containing $0.8\%$ (w/v) agar medium and then spotted with a $5 - \mu \mathrm{L}$ spore suspension. Each experimental group consisted of at least six leaves. All samples were incubated at $22^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ with $90\%$ humidity for 2-3 d. For in vivo inoculation, leaves of 4-week-old plants were spotted with $5\mu \mathrm{L}$ of spore suspension, before the plants were grown under about $90\%$ humidity conditions. Plant samples were harvested at 0, 1, 3, 12, and $24\mathrm{h}$ after infection for RT-qPCR.

# VIGS assay

A 300 bp fragment specific for MYC2 was PCR amplified from tomato complementary DNA (cDNA) with the primers listed in Table S3 and cloned into vector TRV2 by using homologous recombination technology (Wang et al., 2020). The resulting plasmid was transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The VIGS assay was performed as previously described (Fu et al., 2005; Meng et al., 2018). Agrobacterium cells harboring the TRV2-SIJIG construct were mixed with an equal volume of cells carrying TRV1, while a mixture of cells carrying the empty vectors TRV2 and TRV1 was used as the control. Agrobacterium cells were harvested and resuspended in infiltration solution (10 mmol/L $\mathrm{MgCl}_2$ , 10 mmol/L

2-N-morpholino ethanesulfonic acid (MES), pH 5.6, and $200\mu \mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$ acetosyringone) and then transformed into 0.5-1.0-cm germinated tomato sprouts by vacuum infiltration. Tomato seedlings were grown in the conditions described above. Leaves were collected from 3-week-old seedlings for gene expression analysis. The samples were analyzed in biological triplicates.

# Yeast one-hybrid and Y2H assays

The Y1H assay was performed according to the method of Clontech Laboratories, Inc. MYC2 and fragments of the S1/JIG promoter (p1–p5) were cloned into the pGADT7 and pAbAi vectors, respectively, and then the pAbAi-pJIG vectors were linearized and integrated into the Y1HGold yeast genome. The plasmid pGADT7-MYC2 was transformed into the bait strain and the transformants were grown on SD medium lacking leucine (-Leu) and containing AbA, in which the AbA concentration was pre-determined (200–800 ng/mL). The experiments were performed three times with similar results.

The Y2H assay was performed according to the method of Clontech Laboratories, Inc. JIG and JAZ coding sequences were cloned into the pGBKT7 and pGADT7 vectors, respectively, and then appropriate pairs of the resulting plasmids were co-transformed into the Y2HGold yeast strain. Transformants were grown on the selective media SD medium lacking tryptophan and leucine (SD-Trp-Leu) and SD medium lacking tryptophan, leucine, histidine, and adenine (SD-Trp, -Leu, -His, -Ade) and containing X-α-gal and AbA. The experiments were performed three times with similar results.

# BiFC assay

JIG and JAZ coding sequences were cloned into the N-terminus yellow fluorescent protein (nYFP) and C-terminus YFP (cYFP) vectors, respectively. The resulting plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. Cell suspensions harboring appropriate pairs of vectors were co-infiltrated into $N$ . benthamiana leaves by Agrobacterium-mediated infiltration. Images were captured using a confocal microscope (Olympus, Japan). The primers used for cloning are listed in Table S3. The experiments were performed three times with similar results.

# Dual luciferase assay

The SIMYC2 coding sequence and the SIJIG promoter (~1 kb fragment upstream of the ATG) were cloned into vectors pCAMBIA2300 and pGreenII-0800Luc, respectively. Primers used are listed in Table S3. The resulting plasmids were introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 and then co-infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves by Agrobacterium-mediated infiltration. The activities of firefly and Renilla luciferases were measured with a GLOMAX Luminometer (Promega, USA) using the Dual-Luciferase Assay Kit (Promega, USA). Each pair of reporters and effectors was tested in six biological replicates.

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

The full-length coding sequence of MYC2 was cloned into pMAL-c2X and then transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3). Recombinant MYC2 fused to maltose binding protein (MBP) was purified on an amylase resin column. EMSA probes were synthesized and labeled with biotin at the $5^{\prime}$ end when indicated (TsingKe, Beijing, China). The protein and probes were mixed and incubated at room temperature for $30\mathrm{min}$ . The entire mixture was run on non-denaturing polyacrylamide gels and then transferred onto Biodyne B nylon membranes (Pall Corporation). Signals were visualized with reagents included in the kit and ChemiDoc XRS (Bio-Rad Laboratories, USA). The sequences for all primers and probes used in this study are listed in Table S3.

# RNA-seq and RT-qPCR analysis

The leaves of 6-week-old wild-type (AC) and $jig-1$ ( $T_2$ generation) plants were collected as three biological replicates. Total RNA was extracted using the RNAeasy Plant Kit (Huayueyang Company, China), and sequencing was performed by Shanghai Majorbio Bio-pharm Technology Co., Ltd. (China) using the 125-bp paired-end protocol on the Illumina HiSeq 2500 platform. The raw reads were mapped to the tomato genome (GCF_000188115.4). Gene expression levels were expressed as fragments per kilobase of exon model per million reads mapped (FPKM), and differentially expressed genes were identified with the R package DESeq. 2 with $p$ -adjust $< 0.05$ and $|log_2(\text{fold-change})| \geq 1$ as the threshold. Gene Ontology (GO) term enrichment was analyzed with Majorbio Cloud Platform (www.majorbio.com).

For RT-qPCR, total RNAs were extracted and reverse transcribed with PrimeScript™ RT reagent Kit (Takara, Japan). RT-qPCR reaction was performed on QuantStudio 6 system (Applied Biosystems, USA) with RealStar Fast SYBR qPCR Mix (GenStar, China). ACTIN was used as the reference gene. The samples were analyzed in biological triplicate.

# Quantification of terpenes in tomato leaves

Terpenes were measured by GC-MS as previously described (Wang et al., 2015, 2018). Briefly, leaves were ground to powder in liquid nitrogen, and then $1.0\mathrm{g}$ powder mixed with saturated $\mathrm{CaCl}_2$ solution was sealed in $20\mathrm{-mL}$ vials. Terpenes were analyzed with a headspace, solid-phase microextraction GC-MS (HS-SPME-GC-MS) system. Terpene compounds were identified by their mass spectra and retention indices.

# Insect feeding experiment

For the feeding preference experiment, four leaf discs (2 cm in diameter) of two jig mutants and the wild type were placed upside down in Petri dishes (20 cm in diameter) and covered with filter paper in a regular triangle shape. Twenty 3rd-instar larvae of cotton bollworm (H. armigera) were carefully transferred to the center of the triangle with a soft brush. Then, the

Petri dishes were covered and transferred to a climate-controlled chamber with $27 \pm 1^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ , $70\% \pm 5\%$ relative humidity, and a photoperiod of $16 \mathrm{~h}$ light/8 h dark. The percentage of appearance (\%), which is the number of larvae on each leaf disc per tomato genotype divided by the total number of tested larvae, was assessed after 3, 6, 9, 12, and $24 \mathrm{~h}$ .

To evaluate the effects of different tomato genotypes on the development of cotton bollworm larvae, 30 3rd-instar larvae were individually fed with leaves of two jig mutants and AC. The weight of each larva was measured after feeding on each variety for 4 d.

# Statistical analysis

Statistical differences were tested by two-tailed, unpaired Student's $t$ -test, Dunnett's test or Tukey's test. Asterisks indicate significant differences ( $^*P < 0.05$ , and $^{**}P < 0.01$ ). Different letters indicate significant difference at $P < 0.05$ .
