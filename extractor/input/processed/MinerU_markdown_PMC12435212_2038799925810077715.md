# miR444b.2–HsfA1–AOC1 module mediates heat priming– enhanced blast resistance in rice

Jiehua Qiua , Xiuxiu Caoa , Huanbin Shia , Zhengting Chena , Xiaoyu Zhangb , Zijian Caoa , Dongmei Huanga , Hui Wena , Ya Chena , and Yanjun Koua,1 

Edited by Sheng Yang He, Duke University, Durham, NC; received March 14, 2025; accepted July 17, 2025 

As global climate change exacerbates extreme heat events, the interplay between heat stress and blast disease resistance in rice remains poorly understood. In this study, through integrated transcriptome profiling and systematic phenotyping of mutants in several thermosensory pathways, we identified HsfA1 as a positive regulator of heat priming–enhanced blast resistance in rice. Systematic analysis of microRNA (miRNA) dynamics, bioinformatics prediction, and RNA pull-down experiments revealed that miR444b.2, a temperature-responsive miRNA, directly suppresses the expression of HsfA1 by targeting the second exon of HsfA1 messenger RNA (mRNA). Genetic analyses demonstrated that heat stress–mediated suppression of miR444b.2 expression relieves the repression of HsfA1, thereby enhancing blast resistance in rice. Furthermore, HsfA1 directly binds to the promoter of AOC1, a key jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis gene, to activate its expression. Knockout of HsfA1 or AOC1 abolishes heat priming–enhanced JA accumulation and blast disease resistance, and the phenotypes are largely restored via AOC1 overexpression and MeJA treatment. Further identification of HsfA1 natural variants and generation of the HsfA1 uORF-edited lines with improved blast resistance offer potential strategies for breeding disease-resistant rice varieties. This study elucidates the miR444b.2–HsfA1–AOC1 module that links thermal sensing to JA-mediated blast resistance, providing a molecular blueprint for engineering climate-resilient crops with concurrent biotic–abiotic stress tolerance.

Global climate change has led to a remarkable increase in the frequency and intensity of extreme thermal anomalies ( 1 ). The epidemiological patterns of plant diseases exhibit significant thermal sensitivity, with ambient temperature exerting diverse impacts on disease progression that manifest differential outcomes contingent upon the specific plant– pathogen interaction system. In barley, elevated temperatures, especially above $2 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , weaken resistance to Blumeria sorokiniana , resulting in yield losses of 6 to $5 3 \%$ in regions like the Indian subcontinent ( 2 , 3 ). Similarly, elevated temperatures compromise the resistance of wheat to streak mosaic virus, resulting in more severe infections and annual yield losses of 2 to $5 \%$ ( 4 ). In some cases, elevated temperatures enhance plant defense, as evidenced by tomato enhancing resistance to yellow leaf curl virus ( 5 ). Notably, increasing the temperature elicits contrasting effects on rice resistance to various pathogens. The severity of sheath blight caused by Rhizoctonia solani increases with higher temperatures, resulting in additional yield losses of 2 to $2 . 5 \%$ ( 6 ). Conversely, high temperatures enhance rice resistance to Rice stripe virus ( 7 ). Temperature fluctuations also affect the incidence of blast disease, with higher temperatures being associated with decreased incidence ( 8 ). Nevertheless, the exact molecular mechanisms underlying how elevated temperatures influence rice blast disease outbreaks remain unclear. 

Elevated temperatures modulate plant defense via diverse molecular mechanisms, encompassing hormonal regulation, phase separation, gene expression, and genetic trade-offs ( 9 ). In Arabidopsis , high temperatures $( 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ induce abscisic acid (ABA) accumulation, which suppresses salicylic acid (SA) synthesis, weakening disease resistance ( 10 ). Additionally, elevated temperatures $( 2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ) reduce the number of guanylate binding protein GBPL3 phase-separated condensates in the nucleus, impairing its binding to the CBP60g promoter and consequently suppressing CBP60g (encoding calmodulin binding protein $6 0 \ \mathrm { g }$ ) transcription and SA synthesis in Arabidopsis ( 11 ). Conversely, high temperatures $( 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ enhance the function of some resistance $( R )$ genes, such as $X a 7$ in rice, which improves resistance to bacterial blight ( 12 ). Similarly, in wheat, cysteine-rich receptor-like kinase TaCRK10, induced by Puccinia striiformis and elevated temperatures $( 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ), interacts with and phosphorylates the histone variant TaH2A.1 to activate SA-dependent defense pathways ( 13 ). # Significance

Global warming alters the occurrence and spread of plant diseases by affecting the plant–pathogen interactions. Here, we uncover a miR444b.2 – HsfA1–AOC1 regulatory module that integrates high temperature sensing with blast resistance. Heat stress suppresses miR444b.2 , relieving its repression of HsfA1 , which directly activates AOC1 to drive jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis and enhance blast resistance. Our findings also provide potential genetic tools—natural HsfA1 alleles and CRISPR-edited uORF lines—for developing heat-tolerant, disease-resistant rice varieties, offering a strategy to stabilize yields under climate change conditions.

Recent advancements in plant high-temperature sensing have revealed several key pathways, which enable plants to perceive extracellular temperature signals and transduce them into intracellular signals, thereby regulating plant growth, development, and tolerance to temperature stresses ( 15 ). First, phyB (phytochrome B), upon sensing a temperature of $2 7 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , regulates hypocotyl elongation. This regulation occurs through activity transitions or nuclear import process of phyB and interacts with PIFs (PHYTOCHROME-INTERACTING FACTORS) to modulate their transcriptional activity ( 16 ). The second pathway involves phase separation mediated by ELF3 (EARLY FLOWERING 3). Similar to phyB, ELF3 influences the transcription of PIFs in response to a temperature of $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , thereby regulating hypocotyl elongation ( 17 ). The third pathway is centered on heat shock transcription factors (HSFs). These factors orchestrate the transcriptional regulation of heat shock proteins (HSPs) and PIF4, enabling plants to initiate appropriate responses to high temperature stress and maintain cellular homeostasis, thereby enhancing plant tolerance to high temperature stress ( 18 – 20 ). The fourth pathway involves the unfolded protein response (UPR) pathway. High temperatures typically cause protein misfolding and denaturation in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), triggering the UPR. This response aims to restore ER protein homeostasis through the action of IRE1 (Inositol-requiring enzyme type 1) and transcription factor bZIP60 ( 21 ). Despite these significant advancements, there is still a substantial knowledge gap regarding the components that can respond to both temperature and pathogens simultaneously. 

Hsfs serve as pivotal regulators in mediating plant responses to heat and other abiotic stresses ( 22 , 23 ). However, their functional contributions to plant disease resistance remain poorly understood. Emerging evidence has identified specific Hsfs as positive regulators of plant defense. For instance, AtHsf1b , HSFA2, and HSFA3 in Arabidopsis ( 24 , 25 ), SlHsf1a in tomato ( 26 ), CaHsfB2a in pepper ( 27 ), and OsHsfB4d in rice ( 28 ) have been demonstrated to transcriptionally activate defense-related genes, thereby conferring enhanced disease resistance. In contrast, certain Hsfs negatively regulate plant disease resistance through suppression of defense-related gene expression or prioritizing heat tolerance over disease resistance. Notably, AtHsfB1 and AtHsfB2b in Arabidopsis repress the transcriptional activation of defense-related genes ( 29 ), and VpHsf1 in tomato exacerbates susceptibility to powdery mildew ( 30 ). Moreover, Hsfs can exhibit functional shifts under different temperatures. A paradigmatic example is CaHsf8 in pepper, which activates nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat (NLR ) genes under ambient conditions but shifts to heat tolerance under high temperatures by activating $H S P$ genes ( 31 ). Given the duality of Hsf functions mediated through distinct molecular mechanisms, systematic investigation of their regulatory roles in rice–pathogen interactions represents a critical knowledge gap requiring resolution. 

The filamentous fungus Magnaporthe oryzae causes blast disease, which is an extremely destructive factor in global rice production. In our previous study, it was found that warm temperature weakens blast resistance by suppressing jasmonic acid (JA) biosynthesis and JA-mediated signal transduction cascades in rice ( 8 ). In contrast, high temperatures suppress the occurrence of blast disease, 

yet the precise mechanisms governing temperature-dependent modulated blast resistance remain largely unknown. To address these underlying mechanisms, transcriptome profiling and systematic phenotyping of mutants in pivotal thermosensory pathways revealed HsfA1 as a critical regulator of heat priming–enhanced blast resistance. Notably, miR444b.2 was found to respond to ambient temperature changes and modulate the transcript levels of HsfA1 . Mechanistic analyses further demonstrated that HsfA1 directly binds to the promoters of JA synthesis genes, such as AOC1 , thereby orchestrating JA synthesis and heat priming– enhanced blast resistance. 

# Results

High-Temperature Treatment Suppresses Blast Disease Occurrence. In the previous study, we observed the phenotype that high temperatures suppress blast lesion formation in rice (8). To further investigate the effects of high temperature on blast disease development, four-leaf stage rice seedlings were subjected to different high temperature conditions prior to inoculation with M. oryzae. The results showed that exposing rice plants to $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ a common high temperature for rice seedlings (32), for $2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ significantly reduced the incidence of blast disease compared with seedlings pretreated at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ or $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Fig. 1A and SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Since $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ is typically used in rice seedling infection assays, it was selected as the control pretreatment condition for subsequent experiments. Seedlings pretreated at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ exhibited significantly smaller blast lesion areas and lower fungal biomass compared to the control (Fig.  1 B–D). Furthermore, the 24- h treatment at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ induced blast resistance was still shown following a 24-h recovery at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). To verify the generality and stability of this phenomenon, we performed infection assays using rice varieties Nipponbare (NIP), Zhonghua 11 (ZH11), and Taipei309 (TP309), which are widely used in functional gene research, and inoculated them with two additional M. oryzae strains En2-2 and Guy11. Consistently, seedlings pretreated at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ exhibited a significant reduction in blast disease incidence in all tested rice varieties (Fig. 1 B–D and SI Appendix, Fig. S3). 

To further elucidate how high-temperature affects blast disease progression, rice sheaths of CO39 were subjected to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 24 h before inoculation with M. oryzae . During the first 24 hours postinoculation (hpi), no significant differences were observed between rice sheaths pretreated at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and the control sheaths. However, at 36 hpi, invasive hyphal growth showed an obvious difference: In $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ -pretreated rice sheaths, $3 2 \%$ of appressoria successfully extended to the second cell. In contrast, only $9 . 4 \%$ appressoria extended to the second cell in rice sheaths pretreated at $\mathrm { \bar { 4 } 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ . The difference turned out to be significantly more noticeable after 36 hpi ( Fig. 1 $E$ and $F$ ). Since maintaining a uniform temperature during inoculation ensures that temperature does not directly affect the development of M. oryzae , we evaluated defense-related markers to assess the defense response of rice. It was found that pretreatment at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ triggered a higher reactive oxygen species (ROS) burst compared to the control group, as well as increased expression of defense-related genes, NAC4 and KS4 ( Fig. 1 G and $H _ { \ l }$ ). To investigate whether $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatment affects $R$ gene-mediated blast resistance, we did the infection assays with rice lines carrying the resistance genes Pii , Piz , Pi9 , and $P i s b$ under heat treatment conditions. The results showed that $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatment did not compromise $R$ gene-mediated blast resistance (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 ). These findings suggested that $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ treatment reduced the blast disease occurrence by enhancing blast resistance in rice.

HsfA1 Contributes to Heat Priming–Enhanced Blast Resistance. To further validate the involvement of HsfA1 in regulating heat priming–enhanced blast resistance, we conducted a detailed analysis of HsfA1 expression at different time points under M. oryzae infection and $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ conditions. The results showed that HsfA1 expression was significantly induced by M. oryzae at 72 hpi, while exposure to $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for just 20 min was sufficient to induce a significant increase in HsfA1 expression (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). These findings indicated that the expression of HsfA1 can be induced by both M. oryzae infection and high temperature. 

Subsequently, the hsfa1 knockout mutants and overexpression lines (HsfA1-OE ) were generated (SI Appendix, Fig. S6 ). Observations of the growth of these plants revealed that HsfA1 knockout and overexpression had no significant effects on other agronomic traits (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). Furthermore, as reported in Arabidopsis ( 22 ), we found that HsfA1 positively regulates heat tolerance in rice, where hsfa1 seedlings exhibited increased sensitivity to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (SI Appendix, Fig. S8 ). Subsequently, hsfa1 mutants and HsfA1-OE were exposed to $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatment and then infection with M. oryzae. Under $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatment conditions, the infection assays showed that the relative lesion area in the hsfa1 mutant was $2 0 . 5 \%$ , compared to $1 4 . 5 \%$ in the wild-type, while the HsfA1-OE exhibited a significantly reduced relative lesion area of $9 . { \dot { 6 } } \%$ ( Fig. $3 A { - } C$ . To further validate these findings, we quantified fungal biomass, which showed a similar trend. These results indicate that HsfA1 positively regulates blast resistance in rice. Furthermore, we found that in the hsfa1 mutants, there was no significant difference in relative lesion area or fungal biomass between $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatments, whereas the HsfA1-OE showed results similar to those of the wild-type ( Fig. 3 $A { \mathrm { - } } C )$ . Further examinations of the expression levels of defense-related genes NAC4 and KS4 , as well as ROS accumulation ( Fig. 3 D –F ), provided additional evidence supporting that HsfA1 plays a critical role in heat priming–enhanced blast resistance. 

miR444b.2 Responds to High Temperature and M. oryzae to Regulate HsfA1 Expression. Transcriptional activation of $H s f A I$ in rice was observed both under $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \bar { \mathrm { C } }$ and in the presence of $M .$ . oryzae. As is well known, miRNAs serve as key posttranscriptional regulators of gene expression and are critically involved in plant adaptation to temperature fluctuations and pathogen infection (33, 34). Therefore, miRNA sequencing was first conducted on rice samples that were exposed to $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { { C } }$ and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ conditions with and without $M .$ . oryzae infection. Integrative analysis of miRNA-seq data and RT-qPCR analysis revealed an inverse correlation between miR444b.2 abundance and HsfA1 transcript levels under a condition of $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ pretreatment and M. oryzae inoculation (Fig. 4 A and $B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S9). Further sequence analysis showed that, in addition to targeting MADSbox transcription factors OsMADS23 and OsMADS27 (known to regulate stress responses) (35), miR444b.2 specifically targets the second exon of HsfA1 (Fig. 4C), suggesting a regulatory role of miR444b.2 in modulating HsfA1 expression. 

To validate the function of miR444b.2 in modulating HsfA1 expression, the miR444b.2 knockout and overexpression lines were first subjected to quantitative RT-qPCR analysis. Consistent with miRNA-mediated repression, HsfA1 expression was significantly elevated in mir444b.2 mutants but suppressed in overexpression lines ( Fig. 4D ). Building on these transcriptional results, dualluciferase reporter assays and immunoblotting further demonstrated that miR444b.2 expression led to a dose-dependent reduction in HsfA1 protein levels ( Fig. 4 E and F ). Site-directed mutagenesis of the miR444b.2 binding motif in HsfA1 (from AGGCTGAG-ACAACAA to CGGCTCCGGCAGCAG) abolished miRNAmediated regulation in subsequent luciferase assays ( Fig. 4 $G$ and H ). Furthermore, RNA pull-down experiments confirmed the direct binding of miR444b.2 to HsfA1 mRNA ( Fig. 4 I and $\mathcal { I }$ . Notably, CRISPR-edited HsfA1 plants harboring disrupted miR444b.2 binding sites (from CTGAGACAA to CTCCGGCAG) exhibited reduced sensitivity to $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and M. oryzae (SI Appendix, Fig. S10 ), as evidenced by reduced lesion areas and fungal biomass during infection experiments ( Fig. 4 K –M ). Furthermore, the inducible expression of HsfA1 was markedly attenuated in HsfA1- edited plants in response to temperature and blast disease ( Fig. 4N ). In addition, RACE (rapid amplification of cDNA ends) and polysome profiling analyses demonstrated that miR444b.2 can cleave HsfA1 mRNA and suppress the translational efficiency of HsfA1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S11 ). Collectively, these data established miR444b.2 as a critical regulator of HsfA1 homeostasis in rice in response to temperature change and M. oryzae infection. 

miR444b.2 Negatively Regulates Heat Priming–Enhanced Blast Resistance. To further explore the function of miR444b.2 in modulating rice blast resistance under $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ conditions, mir444b.2 mutant and miR444b.2 overexpression transgenic lines (miR444b.2-OE) were inoculated with M. oryzae (36). The data demonstrated that the relative lesion area in the mir444b.2 mutant was significantly smaller than those of wild-type plants. Conversely, the relative lesion area in the miR444b.2-OE was higher than wild-type plants (Fig.  5 A and B). These findings were confirmed by the fungal biomass data (Fig. 5C), suggesting that miR444b.2 negatively regulates blast resistance in rice. Pretreatment experiments at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ revealed that there were no significant differences in blast lesion formation and fungal biomass between the mir444b.2 mutant and wild-type plants, suggesting that miR444b.2 is involved in the modulation of blast resistance under $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ conditions. To validate this observation further, the expression levels of defense-related genes NAC4 and KS4 as well as ROS accumulation were assessed. Consistent with blast lesion formation and fungal biomass results, it was found that mir444b.2 mutants and miR444b.2-OE exerted opposite effects on the expression of these genes and ROS accumulation (Fig. 5 D and E). In conclusion, these results suggest that miR444b.2 negatively regulates blast resistance modulated by high temperature in rice.

Identify HsfA1-Resistant Variants and Employ uORF Editing for Enhanced Blast Resistance. Identifying favorable haplotypes of genes contributing to blast resistance will offer resources for rice disease-resistant breeding. The aforementioned results have demonstrated that HsfA1 is a pivotal factor in heat priming– enhanced blast resistance. To investigate natural variations in HsfA1 underlying blast resistance in Oryza sativa, we performed haplotype analysis on 3,000 Rice Genomes Project (3 K) rice accessions representing indica, aus, japonica, and intermediate subspecies. Three single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in HsfA1 in the coding sequence (CDS) defined eight haplotypes grouped into four types based on synonymous mutations. Among them, Type A (with a prevalence of $5 9 . 1 \%$ ) predominated in japonica, while Types B $( 3 4 . 2 \% )$ and C $\left( 0 . 8 \% \right)$ were specific to indica. Type D $( 1 . 7 \% )$ showed mixed distribution (Fig. 7A). Blast resistance evaluation in a rice blast nursery of 112 representative varieties revealed significantly stronger resistance in Types B/C compared to Types A/D (Fig. 7B), identifying them as favorable alleles. Notably, favorable haplotypes Types B/C predominated 

in heat-adapted indica varieties, suggesting evolutionary linkage between heat tolerance and blast resistance. Furthermore, RTqPCR analysis found significantly elevated HsfA1 levels in Types B/C vs. Types A/D (Fig. 7C). These findings suggested that the favorable variants of HsfA1 confer improved blast resistance, which could be exploited for rice breeding. 

In addition to obtaining favorable natural variants of HsfA1 , gene editing of upstream open reading frames (uORFs ), which function as translational control elements of the downstream major open reading frame (mORF ), might be employed to upregulate HsfA1 expression and improve blast resistance. First, utilizing the uOR-Flight database, we identified a $u O R F$ within the promoter region of HsfA1 and performed editing in TP309, a widely used japonica cultivar. The resultant editing plants with elevated $H s f A I$ expression were screened, which were designated as mu-HsfA1 ( Fig. $7 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S15 ). Under both $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ treatment conditions, mu-HsfA1 significantly enhanced blast resistance, as evidenced by a reduction in the relative lesion area, lower fungal biomass and increased ROS accumulation ( Fig. 7 E –G and SI Appendix, Fig. S15 ). These findings provide a potential strategy for developing rice with enhanced blast resistance, particularly under temperature conditions that typically favor rice blast infection.

The relationship between elevated temperature and plant disease resistance displays remarkable system-specific complexity, as demonstrated by the contrasting outcomes in different plant– pathogen interactions ( 11 , 12 ). While Arabidopsis employs SA-dependent pathways through GBPL3–CBP60g interactions to combat $P _ { \cdot }$ syringae under normal conditions, high temperatures $( 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ disrupt this mechanism by promoting ABA accumulation and suppressing SA biosynthesis ( 11 ). Divergent strategies emerge across plant lineages: Wheat employs a TaCRK10–TaH2A.1 nuclear interaction to activate SA-mediated defenses against $P _ { \cdot }$ striiformis f. sp. tritici ( 13 ); Tomato modulates N-acylethanolamine 

(NAE) metabolism in a temperature-dependent manner $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ ), conferring resistance against Botrytis cinerea ( 37 ); and pepper coordinates CaKAN3-CaHSF8 dynamics to balance resistance to Ralstonia solanacearum with thermotolerance $( 3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ( 31 ). In contrast to these dicot-specific SA-based or multifactorial regulatory paradigms, our study designates rice as a monocot model that employs a distinct HsfA1-JA-centric mechanism against $M .$ . oryzae under high-temperature conditions. We show that high temperature–triggered attenuation of miR444b.2 activity in rice alleviates its translational repression of HsfA1 . The liberated HsfA1 directly binds to the promoters of JA biosynthesis genes, such as AOC1 , thereby driving JA synthesis and enhancing blast resistance under high-temperature stress. These findings uncover the mechanism of the miR444b.2 –HsfA1–JA pathway in mediating heat priming–enhanced disease resistance in plants. 

While previous studies have predominantly characterized miR-NAs as stress-specific regulators in rice, such as the negative regulation of miR1871 , miR168y , miR167d , miR535 , and miR1873 in blast resistance ( 38 ) or the regulation of miR156 , miR162 , and miR166 in rice tolerance to high temperature ( 33 , 39 , 40 ), our study reveals that miR444b.2 represents a specific case in which a single miRNA integrates both abiotic (high temperature) and biotic (M. oryzae ) stress signaling. miR444b.2 directly targets the second exon of HsfA1 mRNA to repress its transcription, a regulatory strategy distinct from the Hsf regulation mechanisms shown in other plants. In Arabidopsis , HSFs are regulated by a complex network of upstream factors. For example, transcription factor DREB2A activates HSFA3 under drought and heat stress, enhancing both heat and drought tolerance ( 41 ). Similarly, the ABA-responsive transcription factor ABF1 and ABF3 regulate HSFA6a to cope with drought and salt stresses ( 42 ). Also, the miR165/166 indirectly regulates HSFA1 expression by repressing PHABULOSA (PHB ) transcription factor under heat stress ( 43 ). While previous studies focused on transcription factors regulating HSFs at the transcriptional level, our study extends the understanding of miRNA-mediated posttranscriptional control over HSF expression in plants. Additionally, it establishes a direct link between miRNA-mediated posttranscriptional control and heat priming–enhanced blast resistance, thereby integrating abiotic and biotic stress responses in rice. 

Our study identifies HsfA1 as a central regulator connecting high temperature with JA-mediated disease resistance in rice, uncovering a previously unrecognized pathway in temperatureregulated disease resistance. While JA is well established as a core defense hormone, the regulatory mechanisms governing its temperature-dependent accumulation in rice remained elusive. Our previous work demonstrated that elevated temperatures induce JA biosynthesis ( 8 ), raising the question of how this process is controlled. Here, we reveal that HsfA1 directly regulates this network by binding to the promoter of AOC1 , the key JA biosynthetic enzyme, thereby driving temperature-responsive JA accumulation. These results establish HsfA1 as a molecular integrator of heat tolerance and pathogen defense in rice, forming a regulatory module distinct from those characterized in other species. In Arabidopsis , HSFA1 activates typical heat shock responses by upregulating HSP70 and HSP101 ( 44 ), whereas HSFA2 and HSFA4a mediate oxidative stress resistance via the regulation of APX2 and ZAT12 ( 45 , 46 ). We extend this paradigm by demonstrating that rice HsfA1 uniquely coordinates both heat shock responses and direct JA biosynthesis activation, thereby coupling biotic and abiotic stress responses-a dual regulatory capacity not previously attributed to HSF proteins. 

In summary, this study elucidates a regulatory network wherein HsfA1 integrates temperature and pathogen signals to modulate 

blast resistance through miR444b.2-mediated suppression and JA biosynthesis activation. By uncovering this mechanistic framework, we provide insights into plant stress response strategies and furnish valuable genetic resources for developing climate-resilient rice varieties. 

# Materials and Methods

Plant Materials and Growth Conditions. The phyb, pil11, pil12, pil13, pil14, pil15, pil16, hsfa1, and hi-lox mutants were obtained from Biogle Company (SI Appendix, Figs. S16 and S17 and Dataset S5). The bzip60 (47), elf3.1 (48), elf4 (48), phyb (49), and aoc1 (50) mutants were described in previous studies. The HsfA1-OE lines were generated in this study. The primers for constructing the HsfA1-OE are listed in Dataset S1. All rice plants were grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , 7 0 \%$ $7 0 \%$ humidity, and a $1 4 \ : \mathsf { h } / 1 0 \ : \mathsf { h }$ light/dark cycle (25,000 lx) in $1 5 c m \times 1 4$ cm pots with field soil. Weekly fertilization was applied. 

Measurement of ROS. ROS induced by chitin in mutants and overexpression plants of miR444b.2 and HsfA1 were measured using a luminol-based chemiluminescence assay as previously described (8). 

Generation of Transgenic Rice Lines. The hsfa1, mpAOC1, and mu-HsfA1 mutants were generated using CRISPR/cas9 system as previously described protocol (51). Specific gRNAs were designed to target the coding sequences of the respective genes and cloned into a CRISPR/Cas9 vector, which was subsequently transformed into rice using Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Transformed lines were selected on hygromycin B (50 mg/L)-containing media, and mutations were confirmed by PCR amplification and Sanger sequencing of the target regions. The mHsfA1 mutant was generated by Cas9-PE system as described previously (52). 

Western Blot Assays. Tobacco leaves were ground into powder in liquid nitrogen, and proteins were extracted using an extraction buffer containing $2 5 { \mathsf { m M } }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, $1 \%$ Nonidet P-40, $5 \%$ glycerol, 1 mM PMSF, and a protease inhibitor cocktail (Yeasen, 20123ES10). Total proteins were separated on 10% SDS-PAGE gels and transferred on PVDF membranes for immunoblotting with anti-Luc (Sigma Aldrich, L2164) and anti-Actin (Huabio, EM21002) used as loading controls. 

LUC Assay in N. benthamiana. The LUC assay was performed as described previously (8). The HsfA1 CDS and miR444b.2 genomic sequence were cloned into pCAMBIA1305-35S-Luc and pCAMBIA1390, respectively (Primers in Dataset S1). Verified constructs were cotransformed into Agrobacterium GV3101(p19) and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves (4 to 6 wk old). After 3 d, LUC activity was detected by Tanon 5200 imaging system following 0.1 mM luciferin injection and 10 min dark adaptation. Image analysis was performed using ImageJ. 

ChIP-qPCR. ChIP assay was performed as previously described (8). The DNA samples were analyzed by qPCR using the region-specific primers from AOC1 and LOX2 (Dataset S1). The enrichment fold of each fragment was calculated based on normalizing against Ubiquitin (as the internal control) and then in comparison to wild-type plants. The ChIP-qPCR was performed with triple biological replicates. 

RNA Pull-Down. A biotinylated miR-444b.2 (bio-444) was commercially synthesized (Sunya company). A biotinylated polyA sequence without a binding site in HsfA1 was used as a control. 

Both of these biotinylated markers were introduced into rice protoplasts via PEG-mediated transfection as described previously (53). Afte $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ of incubation, total RNA was extracted using Trizol, then immobilized on M280 dynabeads (Sigma). Following RNase-free washes, cDNA was synthesized (PrimeScript RT Kit, TaKaRa). HsfA1 transcript levels were quantified by RT-qPCR/RT-PCR. 

Quantification and Application of Plant Phytohormones. The JA content of rice plants was determined by Zoonbio Biotechnology (Nanjing, China) as previously described (8). For MeJA application, a solution was prepared by dissolving MeJA (Solarbio, M8640) in $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween 20 (Sigma Aldrich) to achieve a final concentration of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ . This MeJA solution was then applied to rice leaves via spraying $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ prior to the infection assay.

assays, CUT&Tag-seq, yeast one-hybrid, and EMSA assays, are provided in SI Appendix.
