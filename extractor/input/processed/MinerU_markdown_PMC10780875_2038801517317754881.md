# The cytosolic aminotransferase VAS1 coordinates aromatic amino acid biosynthesis and metabolism

Huang8 , Jiahai Zhou5 , Minjia Tan4 , Wentao Zhu9 *, Qiao Zhao1,2 * 

The aromatic amino acids (AAAs) phenylalanine, tyrosine, and tryptophan are basic protein units and precursors of diverse specialized metabolites that are essential for plant growth. Despite their significance, the mechanisms that regulate AAA homeostasis remain elusive. Here, we identified a cytosolic aromatic aminotransferase, REVER-SAL OF SAV3 PHENOTYPE 1 (VAS1), as a suppressor of arogenate dehydrogenase 2 (adh2) in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana). Genetic and biochemical analyses determined that VAS1 uses AAAs as amino donors, leading to the formation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine. These pathways represent distinct routes for AAA metabolism that are unique to specific plant species. Furthermore, we show that VAS1 is responsible for cytosolic AAA biosynthesis, and its enzymatic activity can be inhibited by 3-carboxyphenylalanine. These findings provide valuable insights into the crucial role of VAS1 in producing 3-carboxy AAAs, notably via recycling of AAAs in the cytosol, which maintains AAA homeostasis and allows plants to effectively coordinate the complex metabolic and biosynthetic pathways of AAAs.

# INTRODUCTION

L-Tyrosine (Tyr), L-phenylalanine (Phe), and L-tryptophan (Trp) are aromatic amino acids (AAAs) that are essential not only for protein biosynthesis in all living cells but also for the production of numerous primary and specialized metabolites (1, 2). In plants, AAAs are predominantly synthesized inside the plastid before being translocated into the cytosol for further biosynthesis of vital compounds for plant growth (3–6). Early work demonstrated that Phe and Tyr are synthesized via two alternative pathways: the arogenate pathway and the phenylpyruvate/4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (PPY/HPP) pathway (Fig. 1) (2, 7). Many plants use the arogenate pathway, in which prephenate is converted to arogenate by prephenate aminotransferase, followed by decarboxylation or dehydration to Phe and Tyr by arogenate dehydratases or dehydrogenases (known as ADT and ADH), respectively (Fig. 1) (8–10). Decades of research have broadly defined the core AAA biosynthetic pathways within plastids (11). However, recent investigations have shed light on the presence of cytosolic

enzymes responsible for the production of Phe and Tyr, highlighting the existence of a PPY/HPP pathway for Phe and potentially for Tyr biosynthesis in the cytosol (12). For instance, cytosolic chorismate mutase 2, a cytosol-localized ADT3 enzyme with prephenate dehydratase (PDT) activity, and a cytosolic PPY aminotransferase constitute the cytosolic PPY pathway in petunia (Petunia hybrida) (12, 13). Similarly, genes encoding enzymes involved in a cytosolic HPP pathway for Tyr formation have been identified in petunia and legumes (10, 14). In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), the cytosolic enzyme ADT6 exhibits PDT activity, whereas two cytosolic Tyr aminotransferases (Tyr-ATs) display a preference for transaminating HPP to Tyr in vitro (12, 15). 

The biosynthesis of AAAs in plastids is tightly regulated through feedback mechanisms, either by the pathway end products or via feedback mediated by downstream metabolites. For example, 3-deoxyd-arabino-heptulosonate 7-phosphate synthases (DHSs), which catalyze the first committed reaction of the shikimate pathway, are strongly inhibited by the intermediates of shikimate or phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, such as chorismate and caffeate, respectively (16). The committed enzymes of AAA biosynthesis—ADT, ADH, and anthranilate synthase—are inhibited by their respective final products, Phe, Tyr, and Trp (9, 17–19). This type of regulation is crucial to ensure sufficient production of AAAs, as the demand for AAAs and their downstream metabolites can vary drastically and rapidly in response to specific developmental or environmental conditions (20–22). However, the cytosolic enzymes involved in AAA biosynthesis appear to be insensitive to feedback regulation (23, 24). The cytosolic pathway has been documented to compensate for the production of AAAs in situations where the arogenate route is disrupted (13). This compensation mechanism potentially plays a role in AAA biosynthesis, particularly when there is an increased demand for their derivatives, such as during plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses (12, 25). To date, our understanding of the regulation of the cytosolic AAA biosynthetic pathway and the coordination of AAA biosynthesis in plastids and the cytosol is limited. 

To elucidate the components involved in AAA biosynthesis and metabolism, we conducted a suppressor screen in the arogenate 

dehydrogenase 2 (adh2) mutant background, which lacks one of the two ADH genes for Tyr biosynthesis in plastids in Arabidopsis (26, 27). The severe growth defect observed in the adh2 mutant, attributed to its limited availability of Tyr (26), renders it an invaluable tool for mutant screening. We used this phenotype as an effective proxy for compensatory mechanisms in AAA biosynthesis. In this work, we describe the identification of REVERSAL OF SAV3 PHENOTYPE 1 (VAS1), a cytosolic aromatic aminotransferase that was previously known for its role in catalyzing the conversion of indole-3-pyruvic acid (3-IPA) to Trp (28, 29). VAS1 uses the ethylene biosynthetic intermediate methionine as an amino donor and the auxin biosynthetic intermediate 3-IPA as an amino acceptor to produce Trp and 2-oxo-4-methylthiobutyric acid (Fig. 1). The loss of VAS1 function simultaneously increases the levels of auxin and the ethylene precursor 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate, revealing a key role for VAS1 in coordinating the production of two classical plant hormones (29). Here, we report a function for VAS1 that uses AAAs as amino donors to produce 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine (Fig. 1). The proposed biosynthetic pathways for the two 3-carboxy AAAs occur through the isochorismate pathway in the cytosol, which are separate from the general pathways of aromatic biosynthesis and represent a distinct pathway developed in certain plant species. Furthermore, we reveal a dual function of VAS1 in the production of AAAs, suggesting the possibility that cytosolic pathways contribute to the synthesis of AAAs in Arabidopsis. 

These discoveries elucidate the fine-tuning of AAA formation by VAS1, revealing potential targets for metabolic engineering highlighting subtleties that will inform rational design strategies for synthesizing 3-carboxy AAAs.

# Suppressor screening of adh2 identified a recessive loss-of-function point mutation in VAS1

To identify components involved in the biosynthesis or metabolism of AAAs in plants, we conducted a genetic screen to look for suppressors of the Arabidopsis adh2 mutant. The adh2 mutant displays a severe stunted growth phenotype and accumulates significantly lower levels of Tyr, Phe, and Trp than the wild-type Columbia-0 (Col-0) (fig. S1, A to C). We focused here on one of these suppressors of adh2 (soa) mutants, soa1, which partially restored the growth of adh2 and elevated the levels of Tyr, Phe, and Trp over those seen in adh2 (fig. S1, A to C). Functional VAS1 decreased AAA contents of adh2 compared to those of the vas1-1 adh2 double mutant (Fig. 2C), whereas the loss of VAS1 function resulted in much lower levels of 3-carboxy AAAs (3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine) relative to Col-0 (Fig. 3). Therefore, we speculated that VAS1 may use AAAs as amino donors, thus yielding 3-carboxy AAAs (Fig. 3D). To test this hypothesis, we chemically synthesized 3-carboxyphenylpyruvate (3CPPY) and 3-carboxy-4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (3CHPP), the corresponding keto acids of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine, respectively [MS/MS and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra shown in fig.  S9]. We purified recombinant VAS1 in E. coli and incubated the purified protein with 3CPPY, the cofactor PLP, and AAAs as the amino donor. We observed the in vitro formation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine with retention time and MS/MS spectra identical to those of compound a (derived from Col-0 plants) and the authentic standard of 3-carboxyphenylalanine (Fig.  3D and fig. S8B). When we incubated the purified protein with 3CHPP as amino acceptor, we observed a signal for 3-carboxytyrosine in the extracted ion chromatography. The retention time and MS/MS fragments of this signal matched those of compound b derived from Col-0 plants and a chemically synthesized standard (Fig.  3D and fig.  S8F). These results provided strong evidence that compounds a and b, the products of VAS1 in Arabidopsis, are 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine, respectively. 

In all the above VAS1-catalyzed reactions, Phe, Tyr, and Trp served as amino donors for the conversion of 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids to 3-carboxy AAAs (Fig. 3E). We observed that the most preferred amino donor for the 3-carboxyphenylalanine-forming and 3-carboxytyrosine-forming in vitro reactions was Tyr (Fig. 3E). We quantitatively analyzed the accumulation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in plants. Targeted metabolite profiling showed that loss of VAS1 function both in the Col-0 and in the adh2 background all results in a substantial decrease of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine levels (Fig. 3F). By contrast, the content of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine showed a two- to threefold increase in VAS1-OE plants compared to the wild type (Fig.  3F), while AAA levels decreased in the OE lines (fig.  S10). From these observations, we propose that VAS1 functions as a 3CPPY/3CHPP:AAA aminotransferase, using AAAs as amino donors to produce 3-carboxy AAAs both in vitro and in vivo. 

# The reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 in AAA biosynthesis is inhibited by 3-carboxyphenylalanine

Our above results revealed that VAS1, a 3-carboxy AAA-forming aminotransferase, catalyzes transamination reactions between 3-carboxy keto acids and aromatic keto acids. Aminotransferases generally catalyze reversible reactions (30, 31). We thus tested the reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 by evaluating whether it could 

use PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA as a keto acid substrate (Fig. 4, A to C, and fig. S11). The reverse enzymatic activity was functional for recombinant VAS1, as it catalyzed the formation of Phe, Tyr, or Trp in the presence of amino donors such as Met and several other amino acids (Fig. 4, A to C, and fig. S11). However, 3-carboxyphenylalanine exhibited poor amino donor capabilities for VAS1 in an AAA-forming in vitro reaction (Fig. 4, A to C, and fig. S11), suggesting that VAS1 hardly produces AAAs by catalyzing the conversion of aromatic keto acids to 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids. VAS1 exhibited lower apparent Michaelis-Menten constant $\left( K _ { \mathrm { m } } \right)$ values toward both 3CPPY and 3CHPP compared to PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA when the same amino donor was used (table S1). For example, we determined the $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values of 3CPPY and 3CHPP by VAS1 to be 2.1 and $1 2 . 7 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ respectively, when using Tyr as the amino donor, whereas those of PPY and 3-IPA were 44.9 and $4 1 8 . 6 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , respectively (table S1). The catalytic efficiencies $( k _ { \mathrm { { c a t } } } / K _ { \mathrm { { m } } } )$ of VAS1 toward aromatic keto acids were much lower than those for 3-carboxy aromatic keto acids (table S1). For instance, the $k _ { \mathrm { { c a t } } } / K _ { \mathrm { { m } } }$ values for 3CPPY and 3CHPP in the presence of Tyr were 43.9 and $7 . 7 4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , while those of PPY and 3-IPA were 2.27 and $0 . 2 4 \mu \mathrm { M } ^ { - 1 } \operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively (table S1). These observations indicate that VAS1 prefers to use 3-carboxy keto acid substrates, using AAAs as the amino donors, for the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs. 

To evaluate whether the activity of VAS1 in AAA biosynthesis can also be observed in plant tissues, we prepared total protein extracts from vas1 mutants and from VAS1-OE lines. We subjected these extracts to VAS1 enzymatic assays by adding varying concentrations of PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA as keto acids (fig. S12). The addition of PPY to the protein extracts led to an increase in Phe content, with higher accumulation of Phe with increasing concentration of PPY (fig. S12A). Notably, Phe contents measured from the vas1 mutant extracts were substantially lower than that from the Col-0 extracts, whereas it was much higher in the extracts prepared from the VAS1 OE lines (fig. S12A). We obtained similar results when using HPP or 3-IPA as keto acid substrates (fig.  S12, B and C), suggesting that VAS1 can potentially use PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA as physiological keto acid substrates to form AAAs. Consistent with the observation from the in vitro enzyme assay, VAS1 activity toward 3CPPY and 3CHPP in the crude protein extract was also much higher than that for PPY, HPP, or 3-IPA (fig. S12, D and E), indicating the preference of VAS1 for 3-carboxy keto acid substrates in vivo. Notably, we observed a notable drop in the levels of Phe, Tyr, and Trp when we included 3-carboxyphenylalanine as a potential amino donor in the enzymatic reaction, regardless of the presence of a keto acid substrate (fig. S13A). Consistent with this observation, the addition of 3-carboxyphenylalanine also resulted in a notable inhibition of recombinant VAS1 enzymatic activity for the formation of AAAs in vitro (fig. S13B). # 3-Carboxy AAAs are derived from the isochorismate pathway but are present in only specific species

3-Carboxy AAAs are metabolites that have been insufficiently investigated in terms of their biosynthesis pathway and functional roles (32–35). To follow their biosynthetic route in planta, we performed isotopic tracing experiments with $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 }$ -labeled Phe or $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { \dot { C } } _ { 6 }$ -labeled Tyr. In neither case did we detect labeled 3-carboxyphenylalanine or 3-carboxytyrosine (fig. S14). This observation led us to speculate that neither 3-carboxyphenylalanine nor 3-carboxytyrosine is derived from other pathways rather than from the direct modification of Phe or Tyr in plants. 3-Carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine were first identified from land plants in the 1960s and were later suggested to be derived from chorismate through a pathway involving isochorismate (32, 34). In Arabidopsis, chorismate is converted to 

isochorismate through the action of isochorismate synthase (ICS), which diverts the metabolic flux from AAA biosynthesis (36, 37). Subsequently, isochorismate is exported from plastids to the cytosol, where it participates in the biosynthesis of the plant hormone salicylic acid (SA) (Fig. 1) (38, 39). To investigate the potential involvement of ICS in the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs, we used the ics1 ics2 double mutant to block the isochorismate pathway. Notably, we detected no 3-carboxyphenylalanine or 3-carboxytyrosine in the ics1 ics2 mutant (Fig. 3F). These findings provided genetic evidence that the biosynthesis of 3-carboxy AAAs occurs through the isochorismate pathway (Fig.  1). We further performed isotopic tracing with chemically synthesized $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 }$ -labeled 3-carboxyphenylalanine (MS/MS spectra displayed in fig. S15). We detected three potential metabolites derived from 3-carboxyphenylalanine as being labeled (table  S2). When 3-carboxyphenylalanine was administered in parallel, we identified an additional 38 potential metabolites labeled with $^ { 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } _ { 6 }$ -3-carboxyphenylalanine (table  S2), suggesting the existence of numerous derivatives from 3-carboxyphenylalanine when this pathway is active. These observations uncover an unexplored AAA metabolic pathway producing 3-carboxy AAAs and their derivatives via the activity of VAS1. 

To explore the phylogenetic distribution of the 3-carboxy AAA biosynthesis pathway, we selected 34 representative species of bacteria, algae, bryophytes, lycophytes, pteridophytes, monocots, and 

dicots (table S3). We looked for the proteins with the highest sequence identity with Arabidopsis VAS1 and Arabidopsis ICS1 in these species in the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) protein database using the Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) (table S3). Phylogenetic analysis suggested that VAS1 and ICS1 homologs can each be divided into differently related classes among eudicots, monocots, and other species (Fig. 5A). A prokaryotic origin of ICS1 was supported by the presence of ICS genes in the chloroplast genomes of algae and other plant species that use the ICS pathway to synthesize SA (37), indicating a widespread ICS pathway in nature (Fig. 5B). AAAs and SA were widespread among these species (Fig. 5 and fig. S16). However, most of the species tested here, for example, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a bacterium that synthesizes SA using the ICS pathway (37), did not accumulate a detectable amount of 3-carboxy AAAs (Fig. 5A). We only detected 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in specific species, such as Brassica rapa, Brassica oleracea, and radish (Raphanus sativus) from Brassicaceae (Fig. 5 and fig. S16). We chose several representative aminotransferases from Brassica napus (BnAT), tomato (Solanum lycopersicum, SlAT), maize (Zea mays, ZmAT), and soybean 

(Glycine max, GmAT). In vitro enzymatic assays revealed that all these aminotransferases produce AAAs and 3-carboxy AAAs (fig. S17). These results suggest that the absence of 3-carboxy AAAs in these species may be attributed to the lack of substrates required for their biosynthesis. Moreover, the VAS1-mediated 3-carboxy AAA biosynthesis pathway is only present and active in specific plant species.

Arabidopsis contains two plastic ADH enzymes encoded by ADH1 and ADH2 to decarboxylate arogenate into Tyr (41). ADH2 is critical for maintaining amino acid homeostasis and controlling plant development, as it is strongly feedback inhibited by its product Tyr (26). In our screen for suppressors of adh2, we identified VAS1, a cytosolic aromatic aminotransferase, whose loss of function partially rescued the growth defects of adh2 (Fig. 2). VAS1 was previously identified in a screen for suppressors of tryptophan aminotransferase of Arabidopsis 1 (taa1), whose encoded enzymes convert the auxin biosynthetic intermediate 3-IPA to Trp in Arabidopsis (29). Another study identified this gene as INDOLE SEVERE SENSITIVE (ISS1) due to its involvement in the indoledependent overproduction of indole-3-acetic acid (28). Both studies suggested that VAS1 contributes to Trp biosynthesis. Our work indicates that VAS1 participates in the catabolism of not only Trp but also of Phe and Tyr, because the disruption of VAS1 partially rescued the defect in AAA biosynthesis deficiency observed in adh2 (Fig.  2C). Our data demonstrate that VAS1 uses AAAs as amino donors to catalyze the formation of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in vitro and in vivo (Fig. 3). When VAS1 is disrupted, AAAs that would have been consumed are “saved,” so that the phenotype of reduced AAAs in adh2 is partially rescued. 

Aminotransferases often can catalyze reactions in both directions, indicating their capability to facilitate reversible reactions (30, 31). For instance, tryptophan aminotransferase of arabidopsis 1 (TAA1) converts Trp to 3-IPA but also exhibits the reversible activity of a Trp aminotransferase, converting 3-IPA back to Trp (42). The capability of TAA1 to catalyze the reverse reaction ensures the precise biosynthesis of 3-IPA, thus optimizing the homeostasis of auxin (42). VAS1 has also been demonstrated to catalyze 3-IPA to Trp (29). However, it is unknown why plants have two different aminotransferases to catalyze the reaction from 3-IPA to Trp. Our study demonstrated that VAS1 is a 3CPPY/3CHPP:AAAs aminotransferase, consuming AAAs to form 3-carboxy AAAs (Fig. 3). VAS1 also functions as an AAA-forming enzyme when amino acids such as Met were used as amino donors. However, the reverse enzymatic activity of VAS1 is limited when 3-carboxyphenylalanine serves as the substrate (Fig. 4 and fig. S10), enabling the irreversible production of 3-carboxy AAAs. Non-AAAs are among the amino donors used by VAS1 to produce AAAs (Fig. 4 and fig. S10), indicating that the VAS1-mediated reactions are involved in the recycling of certain amino acids. 

The idea that plastids are the exclusive site of AAA biosynthesis has been challenged by the identification of enzymes involved in the cytosolic PPY/HPP pathway for Phe and Tyr biosynthesis (11, 12). The recently reported metabolic cross-talk between cytosolic Phe and auxin biosynthesis highlights the biological importance of cytosolic-derived AAAs (25). Because the large arrays of AAA-derived compounds are essential for plant growth and development, stringent regulation of AAA metabolism and biosynthesis from plastids and cytosol is critical (11). VAS1 efficiently catalyzes AAA metabolism to produce 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in an irreversible reaction (Fig. 4 and fig. S10). However, VAS1 can also recycle other amino acids to produce AAAs in the cytosol. The $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values of VAS1 toward both 3CPPY and 3CHPP were substantially lower than those for PPY, HPP, and 3-IPA when the same amino donor was used (table S1). These distinct $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ values ensure the preference of VAS1 for catalyzing the formation of 3-carboxy AAAs and the efficient metabolism of AAAs to prevent their excessive accumulation. At the same time, the products of these reactions inhibit cytosolic AAA biosynthesis catalyzed by VAS1, maintaining a balanced metabolic flux. The substrate specificity of VAS1 reflects a fine-tuned mechanism that ensures the maintenance of AAA homeostasis in response to fluctuations in AAA biosynthesis under various conditions. 

We elucidated the de novo biosynthesis pathway for 3- carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine in the cytosol (Fig.  1), which branches off from the highly conserved ICS-mediated SA biosynthesis pathway found in the plant kingdom as well as in bacteria (39, 43). Unlike the widely distributed plant hormone SA, the biosynthesis pathways of 3-carboxy-substituted AAAs appear to have evolved after the emergence of dicots. We detected 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine only in certain plant species from the Brassicaceae family (Fig. 5). The reasons behind the unique VAS1-mediated mechanisms that control the levels of AAAs in these specific plant species remain unclear. We did not identify any metabolites that could be assigned to 3-carboxytryptophan, suggesting that the biosynthesis pathway of 3-carboxytryptophan might not exist in most of the species tested in this study. Notably, 3-carboxy AAAs might serve as precursors to numerous unexplored metabolites in Arabidopsis. We identified at least three potential metabolites derived from 3-carboxyphenylalanine in this study. Moreover, when 3-carboxyphenylalanine was administered in parallel with $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 6 ^ { - 3 } }$ -carboxyphenylalanine, we detected 41 potential labeled metabolites (table S2). These findings strongly indicate the existence of a diverse array of derivatives originating from 3-carboxyphenylalanine when this metabolic pathway is active. 

The biosynthesis pathways of 3-carboxyphenylalanine and 3-carboxytyrosine represent a previously unknown route for AAA metabolism. Our study provides opportunities for metabolic engineering of natural products using 3-carboxy AAAs as precursors, not through direct structural modification of AAAs, but rather the metabolism of AAAs acts as an amino donor in these reactions (Fig. 1). Overall, our findings shed light on the complex and finetuned regulation of AAA biosynthesis and metabolism in plants, expanding our understanding beyond the conventional plastid biosynthesis pathway. Further research is required to investigate the significance of the evolution of the ICS-dependent biosynthesis pathway for 3-carboxy AAAs, which could provide valuable insights into the evolutionary history and functional significance of AAA regulation.

# Multiple sequence alignment of VAS1 homologs and phylogenetic analysis

The VAS1 sequence was used as a query for a BLASTp search (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/), and 467 sequences were selected with more than $5 0 \%$ overall sequence identity and more than $9 0 \%$ sequence coverage with VAS1. Multiple alignment using fast fourier transform (MAFFT) version 7 (51) was used to generate a multiple sequence alignment for the VAS1 homologs. On the basis of the result of multiple sequence alignment, four proline residues at the PLP binding site were used to generate sequence logos by WebLogo 3 (52). Sample source information for the various species used in Fig.  5 is listed in table  S3. The sequences of VAS1, ICS1, and their orthologs in different plant species were extracted from NCBI; their NCBI accession numbers are summarized in table S3. The evolutionary history was inferred using the maximum likelihood (ML) method, and the analyses were conducted in MEGA11 (53). 

# Statistical analysis

Statistical tests were performed in GraphPad Prism 8.0 software and SPSS software. Error bars indicate means $\pm \ \mathrm { S D }$ and different letters in the figures indicate the result analysis of variance (ANOVA) testing followed by a Tukey’s post hoc test $\left( P < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ .
