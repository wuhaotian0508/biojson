ABSTRACT: Rice is a staple food for more than half of the world's population, and rice fragrance is a key quality attribute which is highly desired by consumers and attracts premium prices in the international market. There are around 200 volatile compounds involved in rice fragrance, but 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-AP) has been considered a master regulator of aroma in fragrant rice. Consequently, efforts were made to increase the 2-AP contents in the grain by managing agronomical practices or by using modern functional genomic tools, which successfully converted nonfragrant cultivars to fragrant rice. Furthermore, environmental factors were also reported to influence the 2-AP contents. However, a comprehensive analysis of 2-AP biosynthesis in response to agromanagement practices, environmental factors, and the application of functional genomic tools for fragrant rice production was missing. In this Review, we summarize how micro/macronutrients, cultivation practices, amino acid precursors, growth regulators, and environmental factors, such as drought, salinity, light, and temperature, influence the 2-AP biosynthesis to modulate the aroma of fragrant rice. Furthermore, we also summarized the successful conversion of nonfragrant rice cultivars to fragrant rice using modern gene editing tools, such as RNAi, TALENS, and CRISPR-Cas9. Finally, we discussed and highlighted the future perspective and challenges related to the aroma of fragrant rice.

KEYWORDS: fragrant rice, 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-AP) biosynthesis, micro/macronutrients, agronomic practices, amino acid precursors, growth regulators, environmental factors, CRISPR-Cas9

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Rice (Oryza sativa L.) is cultivated globally, whereas Asia is the largest continent regarding its production and consumption. Rice fragrance is a key quality attribute that determines its market value and is also associated with regional climatic conditions and crop cultivars. Therefore, research regarding biosynthetic mechanisms of fragrance and their application in breeding programs has received attention from crop scientists in recent years. Among multiple volatiles, 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-AP) is reported to be the primary molecule responsible for the aroma in fragrant rice cultivars.

Until now, two mechanisms of 2-AP biosynthesis have been reported in fragrant rice. In the first nonenzymatic pathway, the amino acids such as proline, ornithine, and glutamate are converted to a common metabolite, $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylic acid (via the $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylate synthetase (P5CS), proline dehydrogenase (PDH), and ornithine aminotransferase (OAT) enzymes), which is subsequently metabolized to $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline and later converted to 2-AP (Figure 1). Therefore, increasing the activity of these enzymes can enhance the synthesis of 2-AP. However, enzymatic pathways are dependent on Betaine Aldehyde Dehydrogenase (BADH), a typical dominant or functional BADH2 gene that regulates the conversion of gamma-aminobutyl aldehyde (GAB-ald) to gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), which ultimately inhibits 2-AP biosynthesis in nonaromatic rice. Conversely, the GAB-ald cannot be converted to GABA due to the nonfunctional

badh2 enzyme, which also results in GAB-ald accumulation, leading to the formation of 2-AP in aromatic rice (Figure 1). Moreover, certain aromatic rice with a strong aroma does not have altered OsBADH2 alleles, implying the presence of other genes or alleles responsible for their fragrance.

To meet the ever-increasing demand for fragrant rice among consumers, rice breeders are trying to develop new varieties of fragrant rice with high yields. Conventional breeding takes longer because of the large number of crosses required to adequately represent a sample of possible genetic recombination events. Considering these difficulties to conventional breeding, newer and faster tools for producing efficient genetic materials for breeding were developed.

In recent years, advancements in agronomical technologies (mechanization, tillage and irrigation practices, nutrient management etc.), RNA silencing (RNAi), and modern genome editing (GE) technologies (TALENS/CRISPR-Cas9), which edit plant genomes in a precise and predictable manner, has also demonstrated significant potential in fragrant rice breeding. In this review, we summarized how the 2-AP biosynthesis pathway is modulated by agronomical and agromanagement practices (micro/macronutrients, cultivation practices, amino acid precursors, growth regulators), environmental factors (drought, salinity, light, and temperature), and highlighted the successful conversion of nonfragrant rice cultivars to fragrant rice using modern gene editing tools, such as RNAi, TALENS, and CRISPR-Cas9. The applications, limitations, and future directions of agro-management practices, environmental factors, and genome editing tools, particularly CRISPR-Cas9, in the development of fragrant rice with improved aroma were also discussed in this review.

# 2. ADVANCEMENT IN AGRO-MANAGEMENT PRACTICES TO IMPROVE THE AROMA OF FRAGRANT RICE

Rice genotype, agronomic practices, soil types, use of chemical fertilizers and growth regulators, environmental and agro-climatic conditions, etc. regulate the levels of 2-AP, $^{18,19}$ suggesting that rice aroma can be positively regulated by good agro-management practices. In this section, we summarized all the factors that were successfully used to improve the aroma of fragrant rice (Figure 2 and Table 1).

2.1. Macro/Micronutrients. Nutrients play an important role in improving crop yield and quality, whereas grain biofortification can improve nutrient bioavailability, particularly for micronutrients like iron (Fe) and zinc (Zn). [20] Interestingly, plant nutrition status regulates aromatic compounds, such as 2-AP, in fragrant rice. [21] For instance, our previous work showed that the foliar application of Zn could enhance the endogenous levels of 2-AP, [22,23] and 2-AP biosynthesis is regulated by both transcriptional and posttranscriptional pathways. [24,25] Similarly, the foliar application of selenium (Se) at the heading stage regulates quality attributes in fragrant rice cultivars in addition to photosynthesis and yield. [26] However, when the different modes of Se application (soil, seed, or foliar application) were compared, the foliar application was found to be better regarding grain yield and 2-AP contents (Figure 2, Table 1). [27] Furthermore, foliar application of chelated Se to fragrant rice increased the grain Se contents in addition to yield and grain 2-AP levels. [28] Similar to Se, the application of silicon (Si) or manganese (Mn) fertilizer also increased the 2-AP levels and grain yield in fragrant rice. [29,30] Molybdenum (Mo) application also enhanced the 2-AP content in rice under Cd stress. [31,32] Not only the individual nutrients but also their coapplication and/or synergism also showed an increase in 2-AP contents. For example, a combination of Zn and lanthanum, Zn and Se, and Si and Se also led to an increase in grain 2-AP contents in fragrant rice. [28,33-36]

Notably, the management of fertilizers in the context of climate change also impacts the regulation and accumulation of 2-AP in fragrant rice. However, the application of nutrients must be time and stage-specific to achieve the maximum aroma

For example, as compared to conventional rotary tillage, plough tillage induces an increase in ornithine aminotransferase (OAT) activity, which led to an increase in 2-AP contents, grain yield, and nitrogen accumulation. Although no-tillage also increases 2-AP contents by regulating proline dehydrogenase (PDH) and $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrrolidine-5-carboxylic acid (P5C) activities and $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline, yield and nitrogen accumulation were compromised in fragrant rice. $^{53}$ Furthermore, continuous plow tillage enhances the performance of fragrant rice in terms of yield, quality, and aroma as compared to rotary tillage. $^{54}$ Zero tillage, another conservation tillage method, also increases grain 2-AP content when compared to conventional rotary tillage. $^{55}$

After land preparation, fertilizer application and irrigation management substantially affect the rice quality and aroma characteristics. Low water levels generally led to an increase in grain 2-AP contents, possibly due to enhanced endogenous proline contents that later converts into 2-AP.[56,57] Moderate drought (soil water potential $-25\mathrm{kPa}$ ) at the booting and grain filling stage enhanced the grain 2-AP contents.[37,58] Thus, a proper irrigation schedule together with N application may have a significant impact on aroma biosynthesis in fragrant rice. Water-nitrogen interaction (e.g., application of $30\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{ha}^{-1}\mathrm{N}$ fertilizer and $-20\mathrm{kPa}$ irrigation water at the tillering stage) significantly increases grain 2-AP contents in rice as well as increases the yield.[37,59-62] The aromatic quality of rice is also affected by factors such as the time of field draining and harvesting in relation to physiological maturity, moisture content, and weather conditions.[63] Low planting density and early harvesting can boost aroma content, and the best aroma (aroma score 3) can be found 20 days after $50\%$ flowering.[64] In addition to water contents and time of harvest, the climate and seasons could regulate the aromatic qualities of fragrant rice. In a three-season trial in South China (April-July, June-September, and August-November), researchers found that the late-season treatment produced the highest yields and 2-AP content in the grains, while the middle-season treatment yielded the lowest yields as well as the 2-AP contents.[65] Furthermore, duck farming in rice paddies also enhanced both crop productivity and aroma in fragrant rice.[66] No doubt, agromanagement practices could potentially regulate the aroma biosynthesis in fragrant rice; however, how different land preparation methods, irrigation scheduling, harvesting time, and other agronomical practices regulate the 2-AP contents in fragrant rice needs further investigations.

2.3. Amino Acid Precursors. Several different amino acids, including proline, ornithine, glutamate, putrescine, etc., were reported as important precursors of 2-AP biosynthesis in fragrant rice. However, proline is thought to be the most important precursor for 2-AP biosynthesis. In addition to 2-AP, proline enhances plant tolerance to abiotic stresses. The glutamic acid and the ornithine pathways are responsible for proline synthesis. The ornithine pathway is the primary mechanism for proline synthesis in plants when nitrogen availability is adequate, but the glutamic acid pathway is the primary channel for proline synthesis in plants under stress conditions. The foliar application of proline, at the heading stage, increased 2-AP biosynthesis and improved various grain

quality characteristics of fragrant rice. $^{69}$ Proline-mediated increase in 2-AP might be due to (1) proline is a type of nitrogen source that could contribute nitrogen to the grain filling process $^{6}$ and (2) proline is one of the osmotic substances that plants use for osmoregulation under abiotic stress conditions. $^{70,71}$ The high rate of proline breakdown in rice callus causes the highest levels of 1-pyrroline/GAB-ald and 2-AP production in aromatic rice. $^{12}$ Furthermore, the methylglyoxal compound is the product of the glycolytic pathway, $^{72}$ which has a similar precursor role to proline but not sufficient in the production of 2-AP in fragrant rice. $^{12}$ Furthermore, the importance of the methylglyoxal molecule as a precursor for the synthesis of 2-AP aroma in soybean has been demonstrated through interactions between methylglyoxal and P5C. $^{73}$ A significant rise in methylglyoxal levels is associated with an increase in 2-AP concentrations. $^{74}$ Recently, it has been found that the 2-AP in fragrant rice is produced from ornithine via the catalytic reactions of ornithine aminotransferase and ornithine decarboxylase to generate $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylic acid. $^{57}$ Consistently, the foliar application of ornithine increased the grain 2-AP concentrations and significantly decreased the expression of $BADH2$ . $^{75}$ Moreover, ornithine is crucial for proline biosynthesis and the content of ornithine may also improve the water stress tolerance of rice. $^{76}$ The foliar application of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), at the heading stage, significantly improved proline levels in leaves and grains, and the activity of enzymes involved in 2-AP production considerably enhanced the grain 2-AP contents in fragrant rice. $^{77,78}$ Furthermore, the exogenous coapplication of GABA and nitrogen enhances rice nutrient uptake and modulates 2-AP production by influencing the activities of $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylic acid (P5C) and $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline-5-carboxylate synthase (P5CS). $^{79}$ Similar to proline, GABA has also been identified as a signaling molecule that plays a role in various physiochemical and biochemical processes that govern plant responses to stress. $^{80,81}$ The involvement of various amino acids in the biosynthesis and up-regulation of 2-AP contents in fragrant rice is an interesting phenomenon, however, further investigations are required to explore the dual roles of amino-acid-induced up-regulation of 2-AP biosynthesis and stress tolerance in fragrant rice.

2.4. Growth Regulators. Plant growth regulators are generally responsible for maintaining steady growth and maturation in plants in response to developmental and various environmental cues (Figure 2). However, it has been found that foliar application of jasmonic acid, at the time of grain filling, on young seedlings of fragrant rice cultivars increased 2-AP and other volatiles. Similarly, the exogenous application of methyl jasmonate resulted in substantial improvements in yield and quality of fragrant rice under Cd toxicity. Paclobutrazol is widely recognized as an effective regulator of plant development, and the foliar application of paclobutrazol was proven efficient in improving the aroma of fragrant rice, grain quality, and yield. Similarly, the foliar application of procyanidins (a polyphenolic compound with multiple properties), and epoxiconazole (a triazole compound) also enhanced the biosynthesis of 2-AP in aromatic rice.

# 3. ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS MEDIATE 2-AP ACCUMULATION IN FRAGRANT RICE

Over the past half-century, it has become clear that abiotic stresses including drought, salinity, and temperature have significant effects on plant development and agricultural output.[91] Aromatic rice is highly sensitive to abiotic stresses, which reduce crop productivity and grain quality and severely influence the 2-AP biosynthesis. In this section, we summarize the major environmental factors that can influence the 2-AP contents (Figure 2).

3.1. Drought and Salinity. Adverse environmental conditions, such as drought and salinity, are major abiotic stresses affecting plant growth and agricultural productivity.[92,93] Drought treatment has the potential to increase 2-AP levels, which may coincide with an increase in proline content and an increase in the rate at which proline is converted to 2-AP.[56,57] Although severe and prolonged drought stress would be detrimental to fragrant rice yield, decreasing the soil moisture (to some extent) at the booting and grain filling stage will raise the grain 2-AP contents.[37,58] Soil salinity has also been associated with regulation of the grain 2-AP contents. It was shown that fragrant rice grown in a salt solution produced higher 2-AP in grains, and the increase was significantly and positively linked with the proline level but not with GABA.[94] Not only growing fragrant rice in the salt solution but also seed priming with salt increase the 2-AP contents in aromatic rice cultivars, which is associated with an increase in precursors, enzyme activities, and gene expression.[95] Interestingly, aromatic and nonaromatic rice with particularly suppressed OsBADH1 and OsBADH2 genes are more vulnerable to salt stress than wild-type with normal OsBADH1 and OsBADH2 gene expression.[96-98] This indicates that both OsBADH1 and

OsBADH2 genes are involved in salinity tolerance in rice. However, whether OsBADH1 and OsBADH2 are redundant in their functions or differently regulate the salinity stress needs further investigation. Seed priming with salinity and effective irrigation strategies would be beneficial for increasing the aroma of fragrant rice. However, further studies are required to comprehensively understand the molecular mechanism of 2-AP synthesis in response to salinity and mild drought; otherwise, it would lead to yield penalty.

3.2. Temperature and Light. The temperature has a significant impact on the growth, development, and yield of rice and is presently the most important climatic variable to investigate in a climate change context.[99,100] The temperature also influences the aroma quality in rice and aromatic rice produces more 2-AP when grown at optimum temperature.[101] At $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , the fragrant rice cultivars had the highest grain 2-AP concentrations.[102] Under optimum temperature conditions, the polyamines (glutamate, proline, ornithine, putrescine, spermidine, arginine, etc) are converted to a 1-pyrroline, which is important for 2-AP biosynthesis in fragrant rice.[103] The onset low temperature $(22/16^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ at the heading stage had the highest 2-AP content, while the high $(32/26^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ temperature resulted in the lowest 2-AP content.[104] However, further studies are required to completely understand the temperature-mediated changes in 2-AP biosynthesis and the activities of the enzymes involved.

Light intensity considerably impacts rice yield by directly affecting photosynthesis. $^{105}$ The 2-AP concentrations showed a negative correlation with the number of hours of daylight, $^{106}$ and the shadow during the grain filling stage resulted in a notable increase in 2-AP contents of the rice grain. $^{107}$ Furthermore, the effect of different lights on 2-AP production at different growth stages of rice was also investigated. Indeed, the light treatments (red, blue, and compound lights) either at grain filling or booting stages increase the grain yield; however, these light treatments either at grain-filling or at booting stages, significantly decreased the 2-AP content in mature grains as compared to the natural light. $^{108,109}$ This decrease in 2-AP in response to light treatments is associated with a decrease in P5C content and variations in proline and -aminobutyric acid contents. $^{109}$ Furthermore, low-light and well-watered conditions increase the 2-AP levels, while low-light and water-stressed decrease the 2-AP contents. Although low light improved the 2-AP contents under water stress, low light negatively impacts the yield losses in aromatic rice. $^{110}$ No doubt, different environmental factors affect the yield and quality of aromatic rice; however, it is necessary to further investigate the coordination of different environmental factors to regulate the 2-AP biosynthesis in fragrant rice.

3.3. Potential Protective Mechanism and 2-AP Formation under Stress Condition. Rice plants have evolved several protective mechanisms including the production of GABA, proline, methylglyoxal, and calmodulin that act as signaling molecules and protective agents during stress conditions (Figure 3). The GABA can be produced either by the polyamine degradation pathway (functional OsBADH2 gene) or by the GABA-shunt pathway (activity of OsGAD gene). The nature and severity of stress are sensed through cellular changes in $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ and/or $\mathrm{H^{+}}$ concentrations, which then activate the OsGAD gene to produce GABA by the GABA-shunt pathway. The GABA then binds to the GABA-like receptor, which releases $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ from intracellular storage to increase $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ accumulation in cytosol. The increase in

cytosolic $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ then amplifies the $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}/CaM}$ complex and stress response signal, which consequently induces stress response genes. Based on these reports, it seems that the GABA-shunt pathway becomes more active during stress conditions. The GABA-like receptors might also regulate mineral acquisition that activates enzymes in stress-related metabolic pathways.[113] Another reason for increased 2-AP content during stress conditions might be the emission of the starch-bound form of 2-AP together with the free form of 2-AP.[114] The survival mechanisms of fragrant rice under environmental stresses are depicted in Figure 3. During stress conditions, the stimuli trigger an influx of $\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$ and/or $\mathrm{H^{+}}$ that activate the OsPSCS and OsGAD genes to produce proteins for signal transduction regulation of the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway.[115] These genes then produce proline from the polyamine degradation pathway and GABA via the GABA-shunt pathway, which then acts as signaling molecules to improve fragrant rice tolerance to stress. Stress stimuli also induce the production of methylglyoxal via the glycolysis pathway and increase the concentration of polyamines (reviewed in ref 116). However, methylglyoxal is a housekeeping signaling molecule present at a lower concentration but deleterious at a higher concentration. The elevated methylglyoxal levels can either be converted to pyruvate by enzymatic reaction or 2-AP by nonenzymatic reaction with $\Delta^1$ -pyrroline in aromatic rice. Consequently, environmental factors, such as soil, temperature, relative humidity, moisture content, and pH during flowering to maturity stages highly affect aroma quality and survival of aromatic rice plants by regulating glycolysis, 2-AP and the GABA-shut pathway. However, consistently changing climate, especially during the aromatic rice growth stage, in rice-producing countries is a major threat to the aroma of fragrant rice, which warrants a deeper understanding of the connection between environmental factors and 2-AP synthesis at genetic, molecular, and biochemical levels.

# 4. PROGRESS IN OMICS TECHNOLOGY-MEDIATED Genome EDITING IN PLANTS

The recent development of exciting plant genome editing tools and the sequencing of crop genomes has opened the door for targeted crop breeding. Homing endonucleases (HEs),[117] RNAi,[118] transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALENs),[119] and CRISPR-associated protein $(\mathrm{Cas})^{120}$ are the four site directed mechanisms that have been explored in plant-genome editing. The RNA interference (RNAi) method makes use of a natural process present in almost all eukaryotes, in which target mRNAs are devastated or functionally silenced.[121] TALENs are more accurate than ZNF because they only target one nucleotide at the target location (as contrasting to ZNF's three).[122] However, CRISPR-Cas has revolutionized our ability to make targeted modifications in crops by changing only the guide RNA sequence for each DNA target site.[123] In this section, we will summarize the role of these genome editing tools in the development of fragrant rice.

4.1. Pre CRISPR-Cas9-Mediated Genome Editing of OsBADH2 Gene to Improve Fragrance in Rice. In the early 1970s, aroma detection methods from leaf samples laid the foundation of research on aroma genetics and the underlying molecular mechanism of aroma production. 4.2. CRISPR-Cas9-Mediated Genome Editing of OsBADH2 Gene in Rice. The development of Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeat-associated Protein 9 (CRISPR-Cas9) attracted scientists due to its high efficiency, simplicity, user-friendliness, and production of transgenic plants.[138-140] Furthermore, CRISPR-Cas9 has the potential of simultaneous editing of several genes, which has enormously facilitated the functional analysis of a gene, or the genes involved in a specific pathway.[141] Consequently, scientists have successfully applied CRISPR-Cas9 to increase agriculture output, qualitative traits and resilience to environmental stresses. Indeed, CRISPR-Cas9 has also been applied to edit OsBADH2 at several positions[142] to improve the fragrance in rice (Figure 4D). For example, the addition of a single nucleotide in an exon 1 of OsBADH2 by CRISPR-Cas9 led to an increase in 2-AP contents, and a significant difference in tiller numbers and seed-setting rate among five traits related to yield and three traits associated with cooking and eating quality.[143] Similarly, skipping the exon 2 by CRISPR-Cas9 also led to an increase in 2-AP contents,[144] indicating the potential of CRISPR-Cas9-mediated exon skipping to enhance the agronomically essential traits. Furthermore, CRISPR-Cas9 has

also been employed to generate new OsBADH2 alleles, which led to the introduction of aroma into an elite nonaromatic rice variety. $^{145}$ They successfully identified 22 different types of mutations in OsBADH2 around the sgRNA region at exon 7 that were heritable and produce strong aroma. Furthermore, transgenic plants produce novel aromatic compounds. $^{145}$ In another study, new alleles of OsBADH2 at exon 7 were also created by CRISPR-Cas9 in the different genetic backgrounds, japonica Ningjing 1 and indica Huang Huazhan. $^{146}$ The resulting transgenic lines carrying new alleles of OsBADH2 in japonica and indica varieties exhibited a significant increase in 2-AP with moderate fragrance. Because the transgenic lines did not differ in amylose contents and gelatinization temperature, they produced three-line hybrid rice with enhanced grain aroma and gel consistency by crossing a new allele of OsBADH2 from indica Huang Huazhan variety with CMS line Taonong. The newly reported alleles of OsBADH2 are crucial genetic resources for the development of grain aroma in three-lien hybrid rice. The ability of CRISPR-Cas9 to simultaneously target multiple genes has also been explored to improve the aroma and yield. $^{147}$ Usman et al. $^{147}$ edited three cytochrome P450 homologues (Os03g0603100, Os03g0568400, and GL3.2) and OsBADH2 with the CRISPR-Cas9 system and found an increase in grain size, 2-AP contents, and grain cell numbers. However, there was no change in other agronomic traits. In general, CRISPR-Cas9

mediated targeted mutagenesis could be a potential strategy for overcoming obstacles in rice breeding for improved fragrance quality.

# 4.3. CRISPR-Cas9-Mediated Genome Editing of BADH2 Gene in Other Crops to Improve Aroma.

Aroma has been reported as one of the most important elements influencing consumption restrictions.[148,149] Similarly to rice, CRISPR-Cas9 has also been employed in other field crops, such as maize, soybean, and sorghum, to improve the fragrance (Figure 5). Maize has two $BADH2$ homologues (ZmbADHa and ZmbADHb), and they redundantly participate in 2-AP biosynthesis as only zmbadha-zmbadhb double mutants generated by CRISPR-Cas9 exhibited popcorn-like scent in several inbred lines.[150] Soybean has one $BADH2$ homologue and knockdown of GmbADH2 by RNAi has been reported to produce 2-AP in a nonfragrant soybean variety.[151]

Furthermore, several soybean aromatic cultivars (Chamame, Kouri, Kaori hime, Yuagari musume, Fukunari, QX1, XD, and ZK1754) exhibited SNPs and deletion of nucleotides in GmBADH2 locus, which is associated with a decrease in GmBADH2 expression that fully cosegregate with aroma.[151]

Although $BADH2$ is the major locus responsible for the fragrance in soybean, its effect on 2-AP production and fragrance is not highly potent. In a mapping study using Kaori as the fragrant parent, this locus explained only about $17\%$ of the 2-AP variation and $43\%$ of fragrance variation in the mapping population.[152] Additionally, $GmBADH2$ was functionally characterized using a CRISPR-Cas9 system with improved aroma in Tianlong No. 1 cultivar.[153] Sorghum, the fifth largest cereal crop in the world, also has one copy of the $SbBADH2$ gene. While analyzing the offspring from a cross of nonfragrant (KU630) and fragrant (IS19912) sorghum varieties, it was revealed that a single recessive gene controls the fragrance in sorghum.[154] The transgenic plants carrying CRISPR-Cas9-mediated knockout of $SbBADH2$ have intense smell in both leaves and seeds,[155] further confirming the key role of $SbBADH2$ in fragrance. Furthermore, fragrant sorghum leaves were preferably consumed by grass-fed animals, indicating the great market potential of aromatic sorghum.[155]

It would be interesting to develop markers that detect $BADH2$ polymorphism in crops. An attempt was made in sorghum and a codominant indel marker was established that successfully detects the polymorphism of $SbBADH2$ between fragrance and nonfragrance sorghum.[154] However, further research is required to establish such marks in other crops.

# 5. PERSPECTIVE AND CHALLENGES

Micro/macronutrients, cultivation practices, amino acid precursors, growth regulators, and environmental factors influence the aroma of fragrant rice. The impact of environmental factors on the aroma is quite heterogeneous, especially the consistently changing climate adds another layer of complexity in aroma synthesis in fragrant rice. For example, mild drought and salinity stress positively regulate aroma by regulating 2-AP levels, whereas high temperature and light negatively regulate the aroma. Similarly, the impact of growth regulators on 2-AP levels is also heterogeneous as jasmonic acid increases, while BR, GA, and IAA decrease the 2-AP contents. However, the molecular mechanism of 2-AP synthesis in response to agronomical practices, growth regulators, micro/macronutrients, amino acid precursors, and environmental factors is largely unknown, especially whether they share a similar mechanism or regulate the same pathway

to regulate aromatic compounds. Identifying the genes and/or pathways by large-scale transcriptional, metabolomics and proteomics approaches and functional validation of those identified genes and/or pathways using functional genomics tools would be a step forward for the comprehensive understating of 2-AP synthesis. Indeed, OsBADH2 has been manipulated using genome editing technologies (RNAi, TALENS, CRISPR-Cas9, etc.), which successfully increased the aroma. However, genetic manipulation or screening of genetic diversity in germplasm was mainly focused on BADH2. The biosynthesis of 2-AP is a complex process, and one or more than one components of the 2-AP biosynthesis pathway can be manipulated to achieve the aroma. For example, manipulation of the OsGAD2 gene will increase GABA contents, which will lead to enhanced 2-AP production and adaptability to environmental changes. Proline plays a critical role in different aspects of plant development and adaptation. Furthermore, proline is the precursor of 2-AP synthesis, which raised the possibility that 2-AP levels can manipulate the modulating the levels of proline-related genes. OsP5CS can be manipulated to increase the proline contents. However, the autoinhibitory properties of P5C5 will need to be removed for efficient proline production, which might affect other qualities of aromatic rice. Methylglyoxal is another precursor of 2-AP synthesis and the Gly gene in the glyoxalase pathway can be manipulated to increase methylglyoxal production. As $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline is important for the synthesis of GABA and 2-AP, genetic manipulation of ornithine decarboxylase (ODC) and diamine oxidase (DAO) in rice will increase the $\Delta^{1}$ -pyrroline contents. Although genetic manipulation by CRISPR-Cas9 has been proven to be an efficient way to produce aromatic crops, public concerns and government regulatory policy for genetically engineered crops would be an obstacle in breeding strategies. Despite these challenges, gene-editing technologies are playing an inevitable role in crop quality improvement.

The fragrance of rice is a complex trait, and one or many compounds, encoded by the different gene(s), in definitive proportions may contribute to a specific aroma. The presence of QTLs for major compounds and the interaction of these compounds, the impact of modifier genes, genetic background and its interaction with the environment are the important factors that regulate the fragrance. It is equally important to study these genetic factors as well as preharvest and postharvest factors that may also impact levels of the volatile compounds responsible for the aroma. Furthermore, our knowledge of genetic markers associated with fragrance in crops is very limited. We need to develop genetic markers that allow us to efficiently screen germplasm with aroma. Using genetics and genomic tools, a complete understanding of 2-AP biosynthesis would enable us to chemically synthesize 2-AP, which is still difficult, and more efforts are needed to optimize 2-AP synthesis strategies. Moreover, biosynthesis of 2-AP in stable platform (like included in starch, such as with rice) might be an option to maintain and deliver this fragrance to variety of food.
