# Abstract

Strigolactones (SLs) are carotenoid-derived plant hormones and signaling molecules. When released into the soil, SLs indicate the presence of a host to symbiotic fungi and root parasitic plants. In planta, they regulate several developmental processes that adapt plant architecture to nutrient availability. Highly branched/tillered mutants in Arabidopsis, pea, and rice have enabled the identification of four SL biosynthetic enzymes: a cis/trans-carotene isomerase, two carotenoid cleavage dioxygenases, and a cytochrome P450 (MAX1). In vitro and in vivo enzyme assays and analysis of mutants have shown that the pathway involves a combination of new reactions leading to carlactone, which is converted by a rice MAX1 homolog into an SL parent molecule with a tricyclic lactone moiety. In this review, we focus on SL biosynthesis, describe the hormonal and environmental factors that determine this process, and discuss SL transport and downstream signaling as well as the role of SLs in regulating plant development.

# INTRODUCTION

The collective name strigolactones (SLs) was coined by Butler (24) to designate a small group of compounds that are exuded from roots of different plant species and induce seed germination in root parasitic plants of the genus Striga (Latin for "witch"), commonly known as witchweeds. Since the discovery of the first natural SL, strigol, as a germination stimulant of Striga lutea (26), other SLs have been identified in exudates of different plant species and have been shown to stimulate seed germination in root parasitic plants of the genera Striga, Orobanche, Electra, and Phelipanche (145). Root parasitic plants have little or no photosynthetic capacity and therefore depend entirely on a host plant to provide them with water, assimilates, and nutrients (17). They infect hosts in natural vegetation but also in agriculture, representing a serious problem that causes large yield losses in many crops, including maize, millet, sorghum, legumes, rapeseed, and tomato (88).

A decade ago, the discovery that SLs mediate the establishment of beneficial symbiosis with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi by stimulating the branching of their hyphae (3) explained why plants release SLs. Approximately $80\%$ of land plants engage in symbiosis with these fungi, in which fixed carbon from the plant is exchanged for minerals absorbed by the fungus through an extensive network of hyphae (44, 104). This is particularly important for nutrients with low mobility in soil, such as phosphate (44, 104).

Studies of increased shoot-branching or tillering mutants in different species—more axillary growth (max) in Arabidopsis, ramosus (rms) in pea, dwarf (d)/high tillering dwarf (htd) in rice, and decreased apical dominance (dad) in petunia—implied the presence of a root-derived and

It is accepted that SLs are a new plant hormone involved in many aspects of plant development, not just shoot branching (see examples in Figure 1), and that they coordinate plant growth and architecture in accordance with phosphate availability (19, 94, 97). For instance, SL-deficient and SL-insensitive Arabidopsis mutants exhibit shorter primary roots and a higher density of lateral roots compared with WT plants (59, 98). SLs also stimulate root hair elongation in Arabidopsis (59) and crown root growth in rice (7), but they inhibit adventitious root formation in Arabidopsis, tomato, and pea (60, 94, 130) (Figure 1). In addition, SLs increase stem thickness by stimulating secondary growth in interaction with auxin (2), positively regulate internode length (31, 114), and accelerate leaf senescence (114, 142) (Figure 1). SLs are also involved in seed germination (123) and early seedling development in Arabidopsis (125) and inhibit colony growth in the moss Physcomitrella patens (48, 92).

# STRUCTURES AND NOMENCLATURE

Natural SLs are carotenoid-derived compounds consisting of a butenolide ring (D ring) (Figure 2) linked by an enol ether bridge to a less conserved second moiety. Based on the second moiety, the SL family is divided into $(a)$ the tricyclic lactone (ABC ring)-containing strigol-like and orobanchol-like compounds and $(b)$ SLs lacking the tricyclic lactone, such as carlactone, which contains a $\beta$ -ionone ring instead (Figure 2). There are approximately 18 characterized SLs with a tricyclic lactone (ABC ring) and $2^{\prime}R$ -configured butenolide ring (D ring) (Figure 2). Strigol- and orobanchol-like SLs are distinguished by the stereochemistry of the B-C-ring junction (127, 141). The C ring of the strigol-like SLs, which derive from 5-deoxystrigol, is in the $\beta$ orientation (up; 8bS configuration), whereas that of the orobanchol-like SLs, which derive from ent- $2^{\prime}$ -epi-5-deoxystrigol, is in the $\alpha$ orientation (down; 8bR configuration) (Figure 2). # BIOSYNTHESIS OF STRIGOLACTONES

Root exudates of plants treated with the carotenoid biosynthesis inhibitor fluridone and of maize carotenoid biosynthetic mutants have a lower SL content, suggesting that SLs derive from carotenoids (76). This idea was supported by the discovery that SLs inhibit shoot branching (42, 129), which unraveled the involvement of carotenoid cleavage dioxygenase 7 (CCD7) (Arabidopsis MAX3, rice D17/HTD1, pea RMS5, and petunia DAD3) and CCD8 (Arabidopsis MAX4, rice D10, pea RMS1, and petunia DAD1) in SL biosynthesis. Genetic evidence additionally suggested that biosynthesis of the branching inhibitor in Arabidopsis also requires MAX1, a cytochrome P450 (CYP) (16). A plastid-localized, iron-binding protein of unknown function (D27 in rice) was later identified as a further SL biosynthetic enzyme (67). Considering the obvious structural differences between carotenoids and SLs, these four enzymes seemed clearly insufficient to produce SLs (12). However, Zhang et al. (151) recently demonstrated that transient expression of the four enzymes in Nicotiana benthamiana can catalyze the complete pathway from $\beta$ -carotene to 4-deoxyorobanchol. In the next section, we introduce the carotenoids and their biosynthesis.

# BIOSYNTHESIS OF PLANT CAROTENOIDS

Carotenoids are isoprenoid pigments with an extended conjugated double-bond system synthesized by all photosynthetic organisms and many heterotrophic microorganisms (39, 135). They are divided into the oxygen-free carotones and oxygen-containing xanthophylls, and also differ in the number and stereochemistry (cis/trans configuration) of conjugated double bonds, which determine their color. Carotenoids are essential for photosynthesis, are present in most organs and tissues, and accumulate in all types of plastids (49). Carotenoid biosynthesis (for a review, see 79) (Figure 3) is plastid localized and starts with the phytoene synthase-catalyzed condensation of two geranylgeranyl diphosphate $(\mathrm{C}_{20})$ molecules, forming the colorless 15-cis-phytoene $(\mathrm{C}_{40})$ . A series of dehydrogenation and isomerization reactions, catalyzed by the enzymes phytoene desaturase, $\zeta$ -carotene isomerase, $\zeta$ -carotene desaturase, and carotene isomerase, yields the red tomato fruit pigment all-trans-lycopene, which has 11 conjugated double bonds.

At this point, plant carotenoid biosynthesis bifurcates into the $\beta$ branch (which starts with the formation of $\beta$ -carotene, carrying two $\beta$ -ionone rings introduced by lycopene- $\beta$ -cyclase) and the $\alpha$ branch (which proceeds via $\alpha$ -carotene, carrying a $\beta$ - and an $\varepsilon$ -ionone ring that differ in the positions of their double bonds and are introduced by lycopene- $\beta$ - and lycopene- $\varepsilon$ -cyclase, respectively). The hydroxylation of $\beta$ - and $\alpha$ -carotene, yielding zeaxanthin and lutein, respectively, is catalyzed by two structurally unrelated types of enzymes with overlapping substrate specificities, belonging to the ferredoxin-dependent nonheme diiron and CYP enzyme families, respectively (36, 118-121). Zeaxanthin is converted by zeaxanthin epoxidase via antheraxanthin into the epoxy-carotenoid violaxanthin, which can be converted back into zeaxanthin by the violaxanthin de-epoxidase. Violaxanthin is the precursor of neoxanthin, the biosynthesis of which is still not fully elucidated (86). all-trans-Violaxanthin and all-trans-neoxanthin are converted into the corresponding $9-cis$ and $9^{\prime}$ -cis isomers, respectively, by one or more unidentified cis/trans isomerases. $9-cis$ -Violaxanthin and $9^{\prime}$ -cis-neoxanthin are precursors of the phytohormone abscisic acid (ABA) (105) (Figure 3).

# CAROTENOID CLEAVAGE OXYGENASES

The C-C double bonds of carotenoids are susceptible to oxygen attack, which causes their cleavage. This reaction can either be catalyzed by enzymes (135) or occur nonenzymatically (93), and it can take place at every double bond in the carotenoid backbone. Hence, it can yield products with different chain lengths and physiochemical properties, ranging from small molecules with a short chain length [such as the tomato flavor volatile 6-methyl-5-hepten-2-one (132)] to pigments with long conjugated double-bond systems [such as crocin in saffron (40)]. The carotenoid cleavage products, called apocarotenoids, are designated based on the parent carotenoid and the number of the C atom adjacent to the cleavage site (see, for example, 9-cis- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal in Figure 4). Living organisms use carotenoids as a platform to generate a wide range of compounds with key biological functions, including retinoids (80), the fungal pheromones trisporic acids (78), and the phytohormones ABA (105) and SLs (76). In addition, there is evidence of additional unknown carotenoid-derived signaling molecules involved in regulating other processes in plants, such as lateral root formation (131), early chloroplast and leaf development (10), and carotenoid biosynthesis (57).

# A NEW BRANCH IN PLANT CAROTENOID METABOLISM

SL biosynthesis is a new branch in the plant carotenoid biosynthetic pathway and originates from $\beta$ -carotene. Although SL biosynthesis starts with the reversible, D27-catalyzed 9-cis/all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene isomerization, we first discuss the cleavage reaction catalyzed by CCD7, as this reaction provided the rationale for seeking a novel isomerase activity that turned out to be encoded by $D27$ .

# CCD7

CCD cleavage activity is frequently investigated in vivo by expressing the corresponding gene/cDNA in Escherichia coli strains engineered to accumulate specific carotenoids. The cleavage products of these carotenoids can then be detected by high-performance liquid chromatography, liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry, or gas chromatography-mass spectrometry. Alternatively, heterologously produced enzymes are incubated with the carotenoid substrates in vitro, followed by analysis using the above-mentioned detection methods. The expression of AtCCD7 in $\beta$ - and $\zeta$ -carotene-accumulating $E.$ coli cells resulted in the production of the $\mathrm{C}_{13}$ volatiles geranylacetone and $\beta$ -ionone, respectively (for structures of $\beta$ - and $\zeta$ -carotene, see Figure 3). This indicates a cleavage of the $\mathrm{C}9^{\prime}-\mathrm{C}10^{\prime}$ and/or C9-C10 double bonds (15) (for C-atom numbering, see Figure 4). This result was supported by the detection of the second cleavage product of $\beta$ -carotene, $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal, and by the fact that the tomato ortholog LeCCD7 has a similar activity (106, 133). Coexpression of AtCCD7 and AtCCD8 in $\beta$ -carotene-accumulating $E.$ coli cells yielded the $\mathrm{C}_{18}$ compound $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone (106), which was also produced by the Arabidopsis, pea, and rice CCD8 enzymes from all-trans- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal ( $\mathrm{C}_{27}$ ) in vitro (4). Therefore, it was supposed that $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone, which is produced by AtCCD7 and AtCCD8 from all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene, is the precursor of the branching inhibitory signal (4, 106), identified later as SLs (42, 129). However, $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone (Figure 2) has low structural similarity to SLs and did not restore WT tillering in rice SL biosynthetic mutants, casting doubt on the SL precursor function of this compound (5).

Enzymes used to accumulate carotenoids in $E.$ coli are usually of bacterial origin and produce all-trans-configured carotenoids. Hence, it was assumed that CCD7 enzymes convert all-trans-carotenoids. However, carotenoids and apocarotenoids undergo spontaneous photo- and thermoisomerization in $E.$ coli cells, which could obscure the stereospecificity of the introduced cleavage enzymes. Therefore, the activity of CCD7 enzymes was revisited, using in vitro assays that allowed testing of pure stereoisomer substrates (5). The results showed that CCD7 enzymes from Arabidopsis, pea, and rice cleaved only 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene (Figure 4), not the all-trans, 13-cis, or 15-cis isomer (5), and produced 9-cis-configured $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal (5), the configuration of which has recently been unequivocally confirmed by NMR analysis (21). CCD7 enzymes also cleave other 9-cis-configured carotenoids, such as 9-cis-lutein and 9-cis-zeaxanthin, resulting in the formation of 9-cis-3-OH- $\varepsilon$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal and 9-cis-3-OH- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal, respectively (21).

# CCD8

Through the same approach, CCD8 enzymes from Arabidopsis, pea, and rice were incubated with 9-cis- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal and also, for comparison, with all-trans- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal (4). The assays confirmed the previously reported activity with the all-trans substrate, which leads to the

formation of $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone (5). However, the CCD8 enzymes converted the 9-cis isomer into a surprising noncarbonyl, trioxygenated $\mathrm{C}_{19}$ compound identical to known SLs in the number of C atoms and the presence of a butenolide ring (corresponding to the D ring of tricyclic lactone-containing SLs; see Figure 2) coupled with an enol ether bridge (5). This new product was named carlactone (5) (Figure 4).

In other words, CCD8 is an unusual carotenoid cleavage enzyme that catalyzes two types of reactions: a simple, classical carotenoid cleavage reaction with the all-trans substrate similar to that performed by CCD7 (Figure 4) and a preferred, complex combination of reactions that includes repeated oxygenation and intramolecular rearrangement of the 9-cis substrate. The exact mechanism by which CCD8 forms carlactone is still not understood; however, it was shown that the carlactone skeleton is entirely derived from 9-cis- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal and that all included oxygen atoms are of atmospheric origin (5). Based on these results, a hypothetical mechanism for the formation of carlactone was developed, in which CCD8 catalyzes a combination of different reactions, including cis/trans isomerization, repeated oxygenation, and Baeyer-Villiger-like rearrangements. The cleavage of both the all-trans and 9-cis substrates should also yield a second, unidentified $\mathrm{C_9}$ [all-trans- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal $(\mathrm{C}_{27})$ minus $\beta$ -apo-13-carotenone $(\mathrm{C}_{18})]$ and $\mathrm{C_8}$ product $[9\text{-cis - }\beta\text{-apo - }10^{\prime}\text{-carotenal} (\mathrm{C}_{27})$ minus carlactone $(\mathrm{C}_{19})]$ , respectively. The anticipated instability of both products may explain why they have not been detected. However, it is also conceivable that CCD8 does not form the usual dicarbonyl side products, which would complicate their detection.

The discovery of carlactone is a breakthrough in the elucidation of SL biosynthesis because it closes the gap between carotenoids and classical SLs that contain a tricyclic lactone core (ABC ring). However, carlactone was originally identified as an in vitro product, and its presence and role as precursor of tricyclic lactone-containing SLs therefore had to be confirmed in planta. In addition, the stereochemistry of the C-11 in carlactone, which corresponds to the $R$ -configured $\mathrm{C - 2^{\prime}}$ in strigol- and orobanchol-like SLs, needed to be determined. A recent report demonstrated the conversion of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled carlactone to accordingly labeled, tricyclic lactone-containing SLs in rice (107). Moreover, endogenous carlactone was detected in rice and Arabidopsis root extracts and shown to occur only in the $11R$ configuration (107), consistent with the $\mathrm{C - 2^{\prime}}$ configuration in tricyclic lactone-containing SLs. Similarly, Zhang et al. (151) showed that the simultaneous, transient expression of rice D27, CCD7, and CCD8 in $N.$ benthamiana leads to the accumulation of exclusively $11R$ -configured carlactone. Furthermore, rice did not convert (11S)-carlactone (107), demonstrating that SL biosynthesis is highly stereospecific.

Carlactone collected from in vitro assays restored WT tillering in rice SL-deficient mutants and induced seed germination in Striga seeds (5). In Arabidopsis, synthetic carlactone also inhibited shoot branching. However, this activity was dependent on MAX1, suggesting that carlactone must be converted by this enzyme before it can exert this SL activity. Nevertheless, carlactone may also have MAX1-independent biological activity, as indicated by its detectable effect on hypocotyl growth in Arabidopsis (100).

# Where Does the 9-Cis Configuration Come From?

As mentioned above, SL biosynthesis requires the activity of D27, an enzyme with unknown catalytic function. Given the plastid localization of D27 (67), it appeared likely that this enzyme acts upstream of the endoplasmic reticulum-localized MAX1 in the SL pathway (see below). This position was confirmed for Arabidopsis D27 in planta, based on grafting experiments in which a max1 rootstock fully rescued the $d27$ branching phenotype (137). D27 was originally identified as a small iron-binding protein without any conserved domain or homology to any known enzyme

(67). However, the preference of CCD7 for 9-cis-configured $\beta$ -carotene led to the hypothesis that $D27$ may encode a 9-cis/all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene isomerase (an enzyme that had not been reported before) that provides the substrate in the right configuration. Indeed, in vitro incubation of OsD27 with 9-cis- and all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene demonstrated that the all-trans- to 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene isomerase activity mediates the interconversion of both isomers (5). The high-tillering-dwarf phenotype of rice $d27$ and the branched phenotype of Arabidopsis $d27$ are relatively mild compared with the phenotypes of other SL biosynthetic mutants (67, 113), indicating the presence of another 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene source in these mutants. Indeed, 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene remains detectable in the rice $d27$ mutant (S. Al-Babili & H.J. Bouwmeester, unpublished data).

Higher plants contain two D27 homologs with unknown functions (137). It is not clear whether the other homologs can partially replace D27 in forming 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene or if this isomer is formed by a different enzyme or by photo- or thermoisomerization. We should mention that 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene is a constituent of the photosynthetic cytochrome $b_{6}f$ complex that mediates the electron transfer between photosystems II and I and the translocation of protons across the thylakoid membrane (27, 143). The cis/trans isomerization requires the resolving of the double bond, a rotation around the remaining single bond, and the reintroduction of the double bond. The mechanism of the D27-catalyzed isomerization of $\beta$ -carotene is unknown, but the purified enzyme requires iron as the only metal cofactor (S. Al-Babili, unpublished data).

# MAX1

CYPs are heme-containing monooxygenases that catalyze a wide range of reactions, including C hydroxylation, heteroatom oxygenation, epoxidation, dealkylation, and other, more complex reactions (51). Arabidopsis MAX1 is a member of the CYP711 family and contains an endoplasmic reticulum membrane anchor, suggesting a cytosolic localization, in contrast to the plastid-localized D27, CCD7, and CCD8 (16). Hence, it was postulated that MAX1 acts in a biosynthetic step downstream of CCD7 and CCD8. This idea was confirmed by the fact that max1 rootstock could restore a WT branching pattern in max3 and max4 shoots, suggesting that MAX1 is present in the shoots and can convert a mobile signal produced by CCD7 and CCD8 in the roots into the branching inhibitory signal (16). The carlactone content in max1 mutants is approximately 700-fold higher than in WT Arabidopsis (107), suggesting that this compound is the proposed mobile signal and MAX1 substrate. The role of MAX1 in carlactone conversion was recently demonstrated by the application of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled carlactone to the Arabidopsis max4 mutants, which resulted in the formation of a $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled SL (SL-LIKE 1) (107). It would be of great interest to know whether SL-LIKE 1 is a new, genuine ABC-ring-containing SL or a carlactone-like compound, such as the carlactone acid postulated by Alder et al. (5).

Intriguingly, the rice genome encodes five MAX1 homologs, including a nonfunctional, mutated one (25). Zhang et al. (151) expressed each of these MAX1 homologs transiently in combination with OsD27, OsCCD7 (D17), and OsCCD8 (D10) in N. benthamiana. Expression of the latter three genes resulted in the production of exclusively $11R$ -configured carlactone. Coexpression of one of the rice MAX1 homologs, Os900, with the genes required for carlactone formation caused a strong decrease in the carlactone content, which was accompanied by the formation of 4-deoxyorobanchol (ent-2'-epi-5-deoxystrigol). Also, microsomes isolated from a yeast strain expressing Os900 converted $11R$ -configured carlactone into 4-deoxyorobanchol. These results demonstrate that Os900 is a carlactone oxidase that produces the parent SL of the orobanchol-like SL subfamily by catalyzing the stereospecific formation of the B-C moiety.

Zhang et al. (151) proposed a mechanism for Os900 catalysis that comprises a series of steps (Figure 4). The addition of a proton to the hydroxyl group and the subtraction of another from

the carboxyl group would result in a stereo-controlled closure of the B and C ring, accompanied by the loss of a water molecule. The biosynthesis of the diastereoisomer 5-deoxystrigol likely proceeds via the same mechanism, but catalyzed by homologs that form the C ring with the $\beta$ configuration. The discovery of carlactone oxidase completes the elucidation of the biosynthesis of a parent ABC-ring-containing SL from $\beta$ -carotene.

# DIVERSITY OF STRIGOLACTONES

Zhang et al. (151) observed that transient coexpression of the carlactone oxidase gene Os900 with the genes required for carlactone formation and the other rice MAX1 homologs resulted in a decrease in 4-deoxyorobanchol, which was accompanied by an increase in orobanchol. This result indicated the presence of an orobanchol-forming enzyme among the three other OsMAX1 homologs (Os1400, Os1900, and Os5100) included in this experiment. Separate investigations of single homologs in $N.$ benthamiana and expression in yeast showed that Os1400 is an orobanchol synthase converting 4-deoxyorobanchol into orobanchol. This result also suggested that the modification of the B-C ring in the orobanchol-like SL subfamily occurs after the formation of the precursor SL 4-deoxyorobanchol. It is likely that the substitutions giving rise to the diversity of other orobanchol- and strigol-like SLs are also introduced in the already-formed parent SLs 4-deoxyorobanchol and 5-deoxystrigol, respectively. This assumption is supported by the conversion of ${}^{2}\mathrm{H}$ -labeled 4-deoxyorobanchol and 5-deoxystrigol in sorghum roots, which led to ${}^{2}\mathrm{H}$ -labeled ent-2'-epi-sorgomol and sorgomol, respectively. These reactions are likely catalyzed by a CYP, as suggested by the application of a CYP inhibitor (82). Mutants corresponding to this and other CYPs as well as other decorating enzymes would be of great help in identifying possible specific functions of the different SLs and in understanding why plants produce them.

# SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION AND TRANSPORT

# Localization of Strigolactone Biosynthesis

There is ample analytical evidence that plants synthesize SLs in their roots (for a review, see 97). However, they likely also produce them in shoots, as suggested by WT branching phenotypes of WT scions grafted onto rootstocks of Arabidopsis and pea SL biosynthetic mutants (81, 126). Indeed, transcripts of SL biosynthetic genes have been detected in many plants in aerial tissues and around axillary buds (see below). However, the transcripts of the individual biosynthetic genes do not always overlap with regard to abundance and spatial pattern. For instance, transcription of the Arabidopsis CCD7 (MAX3) gene is highest in the roots (15, 137), whereas the expression of rice and tomato CCD7 is highest in the aerial tissues and fruits in the immature green stage, respectively (133, 154). Although the reason for this discrepancy is not known, it may be speculated that SL biosynthetic enzymes also contribute to other pathways and therefore are regulated differentially. For instance, CCD7 contributes to the formation of mycorradicin and cyclohexenone pigments accumulating in mycorrhizal roots (133).

Similarly, it is unknown whether the cleavage of all-trans- $\beta$ -apo- $10^{\prime}$ -carotenal, which is catalyzed by CCD8 in vitro (4), also occurs in planta and, if so, for what purpose. In general, all $CCD8$ genes investigated so far show relatively high expression levels in roots (6, 38, 43, 60, 65, 66, 89, 114, 115). In Arabidopsis, $CCD8$ (MAX4) shows high expression levels in the root tips, styles, and stigmata of mature flowers and in siliques; low expression levels in nodal tissues around buds; and no expression in the buds themselves (11, 115). Arabidopsis MAX1 is ubiquitously expressed in vascular-associated tissues and axillary regions of leaves and flowers. In roots, it is detected only in

the central cylinder above the differentiation zone. This localization is consistent with the concept of MAX1 acting on a mobile substrate transported via the xylem (16), likely carlactone. In rice, $CCD7$ is expressed in vascular bundles throughout the plant (154), whereas $CCD8$ is expressed in vascular cells in roots (not in root tips), nodes, internodes, and inflorescences (6).

# Transport

The inhibition of shoot branching in SL-deficient scions by WT rootstocks demonstrates that SLs and SL derivatives are transported from root to shoot, supposedly through the xylem (61). Because max1 rootstocks can rescue the branching phenotype of max3 and max4 scions (16), carlactone is likely also transported similarly to other SLs. SLs are also released into the rhizosphere in an active transport process (63). In petunia, the corresponding transporter PhPDR1 has been identified in a screen for PDRs (pleiotropic drug resistance efflux pumps belonging to the ATP-binding-cassette membrane protein superfamily of transporters, which consume ATP) that release arbuscular-mycorrhiza-promoting factors, such as SLs, into the soil. SL levels in the roots of Phpdr1 mutants were similar to those of WT plants, but they were much lower in root exudates (63). This resulted in reduced symbiotic interaction with the arbuscular mycorrhizal fungus Glomus intraradices. Ectopic PhPDR1 overexpression in Arabidopsis counteracted the root growth inhibitory effect of high GR24 concentrations, confirming that PDR1 encodes an SL export carrier. PhPDR1 is predominantly localized in the plasma membrane of individual subepidermal cells of lateral roots. This may result in local SL maxima in the rhizosphere, presumably guiding the hyphae of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi to the root. The cells expressing the transporter may represent the hypodermal passage cells, the entry points for the hyphae. The present data do not clearly show whether PhPDR1 is also involved in SL transport to the shoot. However, PhPDR1 is also highly expressed near the axillary buds, suggesting that SLs have a direct role in inhibiting their outgrowth (see also Strigolactones and Auxin, below). Whether this involves SLs produced in the root and transported to the shoot or SLs produced locally (or both) remains to be resolved.

# SIGNAL TRANSDUCTION

In both monocots and dicots, two proteins—the leucine-rich-repeat F-box protein MAX2 in Arabidopsis and the $\alpha/\beta$ -fold hydrolase D14 in rice (orthologs from other plants are listed in Table 1)—have been shown to be required for the SL response (for a review, see 108). MAX2 acts as a recognition subunit in a SKP1-CUL1-F-box-protein (SCF)-type ubiquitin ligase complex and is believed to target proteins for proteasomal degradation (32, 116). D14 belongs to a protein family that also includes the gibberellin receptor GID1 (108). The petunia D14 ortholog DAD2 can bind and hydrolyze the SL analog GR24, and the hydrolytic activity is a prerequisite for the protein-protein interaction of the DAD2-GR24 complex with PhMAX2 (46). The crystal structures of DAD2, Arabidopsis D14, and rice D14 confirm the presence of a catalytic cavity that could accommodate and hydrolyze SLs (58, 84, 152) and that contains the highly conserved catalytic serine-histidine-aspartic acid triad required for this reaction (46). In addition, the hydrolytic product derived from the D ring was detected in D14 when crystallized with the synthetic SL analog $(-)-2^{\prime}$ -epi-GR7 (84). It is assumed that binding and/or hydrolysis of SLs to the active site of D14 causes a conformational change that enables interaction with MAX2.

One effect of this interaction was recently elucidated in rice with the discovery of a new high-tillering rice mutant, $d53$ (55, 153). The D53 protein is degraded through the D3 (rice MAX2 ortholog)-dependent proteasome pathway upon binding of GR24 to D14, and thus likely represents a repressor of SL downstream signaling (55, 153). In Arabidopsis, crosstalk between

SLs and gibberellin signaling is suggested by the interaction of the gibberellin signaling repressor SLR1, a member of the DELLA protein family, with D14 in the presence of GR24 (84). So far, all data describing the D14 activity have been produced using only synthetic SL analogs. Applying commercially available SLs would be a valuable addition to this work.

Karrikins (see Figure 2) are smoke-derived compounds that induce seed germination of pioneer species after forest fires, stimulate germination of other species (37), and affect early seedling development in Arabidopsis (112). It is supposed that karrikins mimic the activity of an unidentified internal signaling molecule (139). SLs and karrikins share a structural similarity (the butenolide ring), bind to two homologous receptors [D14 and D14-like (KAI2), respectively], and use MAX2 for signal transduction. Thus, the specificity of karrikins and SLs is ensured by binding to either D14 or D14-like (139). In contrast to GR24, karrikins are likely not hydrolyzed by the D14-like receptor (138). Commercially available GR24 is a racemic mixture consisting of the two enantiomers, GR24 and ent-GR24 (155) (Figure 2). GR24 and ent-GR24 bind to D14 and D14-like, respectively, and therefore GR24 racemic mixtures trigger both SL and karrikin signaling (101). The biological activities displayed by such racemic mixtures should be confirmed by using Atd14 and Atd14-like mutants and/or by applying natural SLs to ensure specificity (112).

# REGULATION OF BIOSYNTHESIS: NUTRITION, HORMONES, AND ABIOTIC STRESS

As is common for other plant hormones, SL biosynthesis and activity are regulated by complex networks with other hormones. This is particularly true for auxin, which regulates SL biosynthesis and is involved in most of the SL-regulated developmental processes. But several lines of evidence also suggest a link between ABA and SLs. In addition, adaptation of the SL biosynthetic capacity is an important element in the response of plants to changes in the environment. In the following sections, we discuss the regulation of SL biosynthesis, briefly describe the interplay with other plant hormones, and discuss the role of SLs in adjusting plant growth in response to nutrient availability.

# Strigolactones and Auxin

Auxin is a key player in regulating almost all aspects of plant growth and development, including shoot branching. Most of a plant's auxin is synthesized in the shoot apex and young expanding leaves, from which it is transported basipetally with the help of auxin transporters, creating an auxin gradient across the plant. Local auxin concentrations and gradients determine its activity and are established by polar auxin transport (PAT), a dynamic and specialized system that directs auxin movement from cell to cell. The presence and direction of PAT are governed by the tissue-specific and polarly distributed PIN-FORMED (PIN) efflux carrier proteins. SLs decrease PAT by removing PIN1 from the plasma membrane in vasculature-associated cells (109), and SL inhibition of axillary bud outgrowth presumably results from the reduced PAT capacity, which impedes the export of auxin from axillary buds (28). However, the inhibitory effect of SLs can also be explained by the induction of transcription factor-encoding genes such as Arabidopsis BRANCHED 1 (BRC1)/TEOSINTE BRANCHED 1-LIKE 1, expression of which negatively correlates with bud outgrowth (1, 75; for a recent review about the modes of action by which SLs may regulate shoot branching, see 134). Cytokinins positively regulate axillary bud outgrowth and act antagonistically to SLs by suppressing PsBRC1 transcription (35). In contrast to PIN1 in shoots, SLs have a positive impact on PIN2 activity in roots (for a recent review, see 62).

Auxin is also a major regulator of SL biosynthesis, maintaining the basal transcript levels of $CCD7$ and $CCD8$ . In pea, removing auxin sources (through decapitation or by detaching young

upper leaves) and applying the auxin transport inhibitor 1- $N$ -naphthylphthalamic acid (NPA) lead to a remarkable decrease in $RMS5$ and, particularly, $RMS1$ transcript levels in the upper part of the stem, which could be rescued by supplying auxin to the decapitated plants (38, 56). Similarly, Arabidopsis MAX3 transcript levels decrease significantly when the shoot apex and young expanding leaves are removed and when NPA is applied directly below these organs (47). Consistent with its regulatory role, applying exogenous auxin in a ring around the stem of intact pea plants increased the transcript levels of $RMS1$ and $RMS5$ below the site of the treatment (56). A similar response was reported for MAX4 and D10 in Arabidopsis and rice, respectively (6).

SL biosynthesis is regulated by a negative feedback mechanism that controls the expression of biosynthetic genes, causing their upregulation in SL-deficient mutants, as shown for Arabidopsis MAX3 and MAX4 (47), rice D10 (6), and petunia DAD1 (110, 114). In addition, application of the SL analog GR24 inhibited the expression of Arabidopsis MAX3 and MAX4 (75). Besides the direct inhibition that occurs locally and is likely catalyzed by the SL signaling pathway, SL biosynthesis is presumably under the control of a long-distance feedback loop mediated by PAT: Higher SL levels inhibit SL biosynthesis by decreasing auxin conductivity (38, 47, 56).

# Strigolactones and Abscisic Acid

ABA is a key regulator of plant responses to abiotic stresses and is involved in numerous developmental processes, such as seed dormancy (for a review, see 29). SLs and ABA both derive from 9-cis-configured carotenoids, of which the corresponding all-trans isomers are connected via the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway leading from all-trans- $\beta$ -carotene to all-trans-violaxanthin (Figure 3). It is tempting to speculate that both hormones interact with each other at the biosynthetic level, and that induction of ABA biosynthesis impacts SL formation and vice versa. Moreover, the 9-cis/all-trans-carotene isomerization activity of D27 and the 9-cis stereospecificity of CCD7 raise two questions about a possible interference with ABA biosynthesis (Figures 3 and 4). First, does D27 produce the ABA precursor 9-cis-violaxanthin or $9^{\prime}$ -cis-neoxanthin? And second, does CCD7 cleave 9-cis-violaxanthin or $9^{\prime}$ -cis-neoxanthin and hence negatively affect ABA biosynthesis by competing for these precursors?

Recent in vitro studies of OsD27 and AtCCD7/PsCCD7 activity did not provide any hint about the isomerization of violaxanthin and neoxanthin by OsD27 (M. Bruno & S. Al-Babili, unpublished data) or about the cleavage of 9-cis-violaxanthin by CCD7 from Arabidopsis and pea (21). It is therefore likely that SL biosynthetic enzymes do not interfere with ABA biosynthesis, and to our knowledge, there is no evidence for the cleavage of the SL precursor 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene by NCEDs. Nevertheless, disruption of ABA biosynthesis has been shown to affect SL production. For instance, root exudates of the ABA-deficient maize $vp14$ (105) and tomato notabilis (23) mutants have lower SL content (71, 76). The decrease in SL content observed in notabilis and other ABA-deficient mutants is likely caused by lower transcript levels of the SL biosynthetic genes $CCD7$ and $CCD8$ (71), suggesting that SL biosynthesis is regulated by ABA. Supporting this conclusion, the application of exogenous ABA led to a decrease in the transcript levels of $CCD7$ and $CCD8$ in Arabidopsis, which was followed by a clear increase after $1\mathrm{h}$ of treatment (45).

On the other hand, SLs can also impact ABA biosynthesis. For instance, high temperatures in the range of $32 - 34^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ inhibit the germination of Arabidopsis seeds owing to an ABA-dependent induction of ABA biosynthesis and a repression of gibberellin biosynthesis and signaling (122). This thermoinhibition is more pronounced in max1 and max2 mutants (123). Moreover, it could be alleviated by the application of GR24 to WT seeds, which decreased ABA content (by lowering the induction of the ABA biosynthetic gene NCED9) and increased GA4 content (123). Similarly, GR24 application to imbibed Striga hermonthica seeds reduced the ABA/gibberellin ratio and

caused a pronounced increase in the content of the cytokinin trans-zeatin (123). Indeed, the stimulatory effect of GR24 on germination could be mimicked by simultaneous application of gibberellin and the carotenoid biosynthetic inhibitor fluridone, which inhibits ABA accumulation (123). SLs can also trigger seed germination by enhancing the expression of ABA-degrading enzymes, as shown for Phelipanche ramosa seeds (64).

# Strigolactones and Drought/Salt Stress

There are consistent data showing that the Arabidopsis max2 mutant is hypersensitive to drought stress and evaporates more water than WT plants, possibly owing to a thinner cuticle and inefficient stomatal closure caused by decreased sensitivity to ABA (22, 45). Although MAX2 disruption did not impact total ABA content, max2 seedlings respond to drought treatment by reducing induction of ABA-responsive genes, including those involved in ABA metabolism and transport, compared with WT plants (22). Transcriptome analysis identified a large number of genes downregulated in max2 leaves compared with WT leaves under normal and, particularly, drought conditions (45). The downregulated genes include drought-inducible, ABA-dependent, and ABA-independent genes. On the other hand, several photosynthetic genes usually repressed under dehydration in an ABA-independent manner were more highly expressed in max2 leaves under normal and drought conditions. These data imply that MAX2 plays a role in regulating the abiotic stress response in an ABA-dependent and ABA-independent manner.

The F-box protein MAX2 has been implicated in the transduction of signals other than SLs [i.e., brassinosteroids (136)] and mediates karrikin signaling (85). Therefore, a specific role of SLs in abiotic stress response must be confirmed by detecting similar phenotypes in SL biosynthetic mutants that can be rescued by exogenous SL application. Indeed, Ha et al. (45) showed that Arabidopsis max3 and max4 mutants are also sensitive to drought and salt stress and exhibit a higher leaf stomatal density and delayed ABA-induced stomatal closure compared with WT plants. The application of GR24 rescued the drought sensitivity of the SL-deficient mutants and increased the drought tolerance of WT plants. However, contradictory data were also reported on the drought sensitivity of SL biosynthetic mutants, suggesting that SLs are not involved in the drought stress response (22).

# Strigolactones and Phosphate Availability

Phosphorus is an essential nutrient and a basic element in different vital molecules, including DNA, RNA, and ATP. In soils, it is quite inaccessible to plants because it is present as inorganic phosphate, which forms insoluble complexes, and therefore it is often a growth-limiting factor (90). To overcome this limitation, plants alter their growth pattern (60, 72, 124, 128) and establish symbiosis with mycorrhizal fungi of the Glomeromycota (111). In the following, we describe the impact of phosphate availability on SL formation and how this hormone is involved in the response of plants to phosphate deficiency.

Phosphate availability determines strigolactone production. Consistent with their role in recruiting mycorrhizal fungi (3), SL production (70, 149) and release (52, 70, 146, 147, 150) are promoted by a shortage of phosphate and (to a lesser extent) nitrate. Accordingly, phosphorus fertilization inhibits both processes (52, 53, 148). Phosphate availability regulates SL biosynthesis and transport at the transcriptional level. For instance, phosphate deficiency decreased the expression of $DAD1$ (CCD8) (18) and the SL transporter gene $PDR1$ in petunia (63) and of $D10$ , $D27$ , and $D17/HDT1$ in rice (117). In roots of Medicago truncatula, both phosphate and nitrogen deficiency enhanced the expression of the SL biosynthetic genes $MtCCD7$ , $MtCCD8$ , $MtD27$ ,

and $M_tMAX1$ (14). Interestingly, the expression of $M_tD27$ and $M_tMAX1$ is controlled by two transcription factors, NODULATION SIGNALING PATHWAY 1 (NSP1) and NSP2, which are required for the establishment of rhizobial and mycorrhizal symbiosis (68). $M_tD27$ transcripts were almost undetectable in the $nsp1$ mutant and reduced to approximately $20\%$ of WT levels in the $nsp2$ mutant. NSP1 and NSP2 are conserved across species, and knockdown of OsNSP1 and OsNSP2 in rice resulted in an approximately $90\%$ reduction in $D27$ expression and an increase in tiller number (68).

Strigolactones mediate plant responses to phosphate starvation. To cope with phosphate deficiency, plants reduce shoot branching (60, 72, 124, 128) and alter the architecture of their roots, increasing the root-to-shoot ratio (69, 72, 90, 98, 99, 140). In Arabidopsis, phosphate starvation results in a shallower root system with shorter primary roots, denser root hairs, and more and longer lateral roots (90). Changes in lateral roots are triggered by an increased sensitivity to auxin (91). This root architecture provides better access to phosphate, which usually accumulates in the upper part of the soil (73). Seedlings of max2 and max4 mutants showed delayed root hair response upon phosphate starvation, and many phosphate-starvation-induced genes were induced at a lower level in the max2 mutant, pointing to a role of SLs in this process (77).

The impact of phosphate deficiency on the growth of primary roots varies depending on the plant species and even the ecotype (87, 90, 98, 117). In rice, phosphate deficiency leads to an increase in the length of primary, lateral, and crown roots and a decrease in lateral root density (7, 117). This alteration was not observed in rice $d3$ , $d10$ , and $d27$ mutants, and the application of GR24 restored WT response in the $d10$ and $d27$ biosynthetic mutants but not in $d3$ , confirming the role of SLs in mediating or perceiving the low-phosphate signal. The changes in root architecture involve modulation of auxin transport, because both phosphate starvation and GR24 treatment reduced the transport of radiolabeled auxin (117).

In the course of redirecting resources to the roots, plants also respond to phosphate starvation by decreased shoot branching (72, 128). This response requires SL biosynthesis and signaling in rice (128) and Arabidopsis (61). In rice, resupply with phosphate released tiller outgrowth and reduced SL levels owing to a dramatic decrease in the expression of $D10$ , $D27$ , and $D17/HTD1$ . Similarly, fertilizer application quickly inhibited SL biosynthesis in sorghum, rice, maize, and millet (52-54, 148).

# CONCLUDING REMARKS AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

The last few years have brought enormous progress in the understanding of SL biosynthesis, signal transduction, and biological functions. Although we do not know the exact enzymatic mechanisms of many steps in SL biosynthesis, the recent elucidation of MAX1 function largely completes the SL biosynthetic pathway from $\beta$ -carotene to the parent of the orobanchol-like subfamily, 4-deoxyorobanchol. We still do not know how the parent of the strigol-like family, 5-deoxystrigol, is formed, but this likely also involves a MAX1 homolog. However, the recent discovery of the Arabidopsis compound SL-LIKE 1, the structure of which has not been resolved (107), shows that much remains to be discovered about SL biosynthesis.

And there are further questions that need to be answered. The two CCDs involved in SL biosynthesis can also catalyze other reactions, on other substrates, than those leading to carlactone. Do these in vitro-detected reactions also occur in planta? Do CCD7 and CCD8 also produce carlactone derivatives such as hydroxyl-carlactones? These hypothetical compounds might arise by the CCD8 conversion of the hydroxy- $\beta$ -apocarotenals that are formed by CCD7 in vitro. Similarly, it is unclear whether CCD8 also catalyzes the formation of the $C_{18}$ compound

$\beta$ -apo-13-carotenal in planta; this is particularly important because this compound was shown to have biological activity, i.e., blocking the growth of root hairs (103).

We now know that carotenoids serve as precursors for several signaling molecules. It will be intriguing to investigate the evolution of these compounds, particularly ABA and SLs. Which of these two hormones is older? When did carotenoid cleavage products—probably nonenzymatic products—evolve into hormones? Research on carotenoid metabolism is not only interesting from an evolutionary point of view. There are surely unknown carotenoid-derived signaling molecules awaiting discovery, identification of which will provide additional insight into how plants regulate their growth and survival in a constantly changing environment.

Finally, despite the rapidly increasing knowledge about SLs, important questions remain. What is the natural SL compound that triggers the SL responses in planta? Does it belong to the orobanchol-like or strigol-like subfamily? Or is it a different molecule, such as an intermediate between carlactone and ABC-ring-containing SLs? Does carlactone have any significant MAX1-independent biological activity in plants? Why do plants produce and release so many different SLs? Are there specific functions for this large (and increasing) number of compounds? It is not clear how quickly these questions will be answered, but they will be, and the answers will amaze us even more that such an important class of signaling molecules escaped our attention for so long.

# SUMMARY POINTS

1. Strigolactones are carotenoid derivatives originally identified in root exudates.   
2. Strigolactones induce seed germination in root parasitic weeds and hyphal branching in symbiotic mycorrhizal fungi and have been recently recognized as a new class of plant hormones.   
3. In concert with auxin, strigolactones play a key role in adapting plant architecture to nutrient availability and are involved in different developmental processes.   
4. Carotenoids are essential photosynthetic pigments that also serve as precursors of different signaling molecules and hormones, such as strigolactones.   
5. The strigolactone biosynthetic pathway involves a unique all-trans/9-cis-carotene isomerization and a stereospecific 9-cis- $\beta$ -carotene cleavage, and is surprisingly short owing to the unusual activity of CCD8, which catalyzes a combination of different reactions, including repeated dioxygenation and intramolecular rearrangement.   
6. The CCD8 product carlactone is converted by CYP711 enzymes, such as the rice carlactone oxidase, into other strigolactones.   
7. Strigolactone biosynthesis is regulated by auxin and phosphate availability.   
8. Strigolactones exert their function via an F-box protein that mediates protein degradation, by modulating auxin transport and/or by direct transcriptional regulation of target genes.
