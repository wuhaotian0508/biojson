# ABSTRACT

Fruit pigmentation is a major signal that attracts frugivores to enable seed dispersal. In most fleshy fruit, green chlorophyll typically accumulates early in development and is replaced by a range of pigments during ripening. In species such as grape and strawberry, chlorophyll is replaced by red anthocyanins produced by the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway. Eggplant ( ) is unique, as its fruit accumulates an-Solanum melongenathocyanins beginning from fruit set, and these are later replaced by the yellow flavonoid-pathway intermediate naringenin chalcone. To decipher the genetic regulation of this extraordinary pigmentation shift, we integrated mRNA and microRNA (miRNA) profiling data obtained from developing eggplant fruit. We discovered that SQUAMOSA PROMOTER BINDING-LIKE (i.e., SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15), MYB1, and MYB2 transcription factors (TFs) regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in early fruit development, whereas the MYB12 TF controls later accumulation of naringenin chalcone. We further show that  and miRNA157 negatively regulate the expression of  and , respectively. Taken together, our findmiRNA858 SPLs MYB12ings suggest that opposing and complementary expression of miRNAs and TFs controls the pigmentation switch in eggplant fruit skin. Intriguingly, despite the distinctive pigmentation pattern in eggplant, fruit development in other species makes use of homologous regulatory factors to control the temporal and spatial production of different pigment classes.

# INTRODUCTION

Numerous berry-producing species, including grape and apple, accumulate anthocyanin pigments during fruit ripening (Faragher and Brohier, 1984; Catoni et al., 2008; Kapoor et al., 2022). These pigments play an important role in protecting plant tissues from diverse stresses while promoting fruit consumption by frugivores and, consequently, seed dispersal (Harborne and Williams, 2001). Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), the most extensively studied fleshy fruit, does not accumulate anthocyanins (Giovannoni, 2007; Seymour et al., 2008; Colanero et al., 2020a; 2020b). Other members of the Solanaceae family exhibit anthocyanins in fruit, e.g., eggplant (Solanum melongena), pepper (Capsicum annuum), and black nightshade (Solanum nigrum and S. americanum) (Liu et al., 2018; Heo et al., 2022). Among these, fruits of the nonclimacteric species, such as eggplant and pepper show an unusual ripening phenomenon in which anthocyanin accumulation is negatively correlated with fruit maturation and ripening (Mennella et al., 2012; Aza-Gonza´ lez et al., 2013; Barchi et al., 2019, 2021). In eggplant, anthocyanin pigments accumulate to the highest levels in young, unripe fruit (ready for commercial use), and these later decline as ripening progresses (Lo Scalzo et al., 2021). Once levels of anthocyanin pigmentation decrease, the fruit turns from light purple to a dark, brown-yellow color when fully physiologically ripe. This latter ripening stage has been largely ignored in eggplant. The intriguing shift in fruit color observed during eggplant fruit ripening makes this species particularly compelling for in-depth study of the regulatory network that controls biosynthesis of the exchanged pigments. 

Eggplant varieties vary in fruit size, shape, and color, however cultivars with dark purple skin are the most consumed worldwide. They are known for their high content of phenolic constituents, including anthocyanin pigments (Cao et al., 1996). The two dominant types of anthocyanin in purple eggplant are delphinidin-3- $\boldsymbol { \wp }$ -coumaroyl-rutinoside)-5-glucoside (known as nasunin) and delphinidin-3-rutinoside (Azuma et al., 2008; Toppino et al., 2016; Florio et al., 2021; Sulli et al., 2021) (Figure 1A and Supplemental Figure 1). Anthocyanin biosynthesis occurs through the flavonoid pathway, starting from phenylalanine and is typically divided into the early and late steps, which lead to the production of anthocyanins (Figure 1A). In the early enzymatic steps, phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) facilitates the transformation of phenylalanine into cinnamate; cinnamate 4 hydroxylase (C4H) then converts cinnamate into $p$ -coumarate; next, 4-coumarate:coenzyme A (CoA) ligase (4CL) transforms $p$ -coumarate into $\beta$ -coumarate-CoA; chalcone synthase (CHS) catalyzes the conversion of $\beta \mathrm { \hbar }$ -coumarate-CoA, along with 3-malonyl CoA, into the yellow pigment naringenin chalcone, and chalcone isomerase (CHI) subsequently changes naringenin chalcone into naringenin (Supplemental Figure 1). The late enzymatic steps encompass the conversion of naringenin into dihydroflavonols (e.g., dihydrokaempferol, dihydroquercetin, dihydromyricetin, eriodictyol, etc.). This conversion is facilitated by flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H), flavonoid ${ \sf 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -hydroxylase (F30 H), and flavonoid $\boldsymbol { 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -hydroxylase (F30 50 H). Dihydroflavonol 4-reductase (DFR) then transforms dihydroflavonols into their corresponding leucoanthocyanidins, and anthocyanin synthase (ANS) catalyzes the conversion of leucoanthocyanidins into antho-

cyanidins. These metabolites are further modified by glycosyltransferases (GTs) to produce anthocyanin pigments. In addition to the late-pathway enzymes, flavonol synthase (FLS) also plays a key role in converting the dihydroflavonol intermediates into flavonols (e.g., kaempferol and quercetin) (Figure 1A and Supplemental Figure 1). 

In eggplant, the structural identity of the pigments that accumulate during late fruit maturation was not fully understood until recent studies identified a few predominant phenolic compounds, including naringenin 7-O-glucoside and naringenin chalcone, in fruit skin at the late maturation stage (Lo Scalzo et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). Furthermore, Mennella et al. (2012) reported that, during the transition from commercially mature to physiologically ripe berries, there is a reduction in the two main classes of phenolic compounds, chlorogenic acid and delphinidin glycosides, in the fruit skin. These findings were further supported by transcriptome analysis, which showed significant alterations in the expression profiles of genes encoding structural enzymes in the flavonoid pathway during berry development in eggplant (Barchi et al., 2019). 

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) play essential roles in regulating anthocyanin and flavonoid metabolism (Gou et al., 2011). In Arabidopsis, overexpression of miRNA156 resulted in increased anthocyanin accumulation in vegetative tissues through induced expression of DFR, a gene encoding one of the late flavonoid biosynthesis steps (Gou et al., 2011). This work showed that the transcription factor (TF) SQUAMOSA promoter binding protein-like 9 (SPL9) competes with the MYB–bHLH– WD40 (MBW) protein complex and thus regulates DFR promoter activity. Overexpression of miRNA156 causes downregulation of SPL9, which in turn releases the repression of DFR gene expression. According to previous reports, miRNA156 is closely related and similar to miRNA157 in terms of structure and function (Rhoades et al., 2002; Chen et al., 2004; Chuck et al., 2007; Gandikota et al., 2007; He et al., 2018). Both miRNA156 and miRNA157 regulate SPL TFs (Gou et al., 2011; Zhou et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022). By using short tandem target mimic technology in addition to RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis, Peng et al. (2018) revealed the role of the miRNA156/157 pair in anthocyanin metabolism and photosynthesis in Arabidopsis. In addition to Arabidopsis, miRNA156/7–SPL modules have also been identified in diverse fleshy fruit. For instance, miRNA156 was shown to target SPL TFs in litchi to modulate the MBW complex, thus regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis (Liu et al., 2017). A similar observation was made for blueberry, in which the VcmiRNA156a–VcSPL12 module was identified as a key regulatory hub for anthocyanin and chlorophyll metabolism (Li et al., 2020, 2024a). VcSPL12 physically interacts with VcMYBPA1 to modulate late anthocyanin biosynthesis genes on the one hand and the chlorophyll biosynthetic gene VcDVR (Divinyl reductase), a direct target of VcSPL12, on the other. According to the authors, this process governs the change in fruit color from green to dark blue (Li et al., 2020, 2024a). 

In addition to miRNA156/7, miRNA858 is another miRNA related to anthocyanin biosynthesis that directly targets MYB-type TFs. Translation of the anthocyanin repressor MYBL2 was inhibited by miRNA858a in Arabidopsis, and overexpression of miRNA858 enhanced anthocyanin accumulation in seedlings (Wang et al.,

Furthermore, anthocyanin accumulation was negatively controlled by miRNA828 and its triggered small RNA AtTAS4 (trans-acting small interfering RNA [siRNA])-siRNA81 in Arabidopsis (Rajagopalan et al., 2006; Luo et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2013a). In addition, several miRNAs were identified from kiwifruit that were differentially involved in the cleavage of SPL13, AUXIN RESPONSE FACTOR 16, SCARECROW-LIKE 6, and F-box1, all of which are repressors of MYB10, a known TF for anthocyanin biosynthesis (Wang et al., 2023). 

Thus, in anthocyanin-containing fruit, miRNA156 functions as a post-transcriptional regulator of SPLs, which in turn negatively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis by inhibiting activator MYBs through protein–protein interactions (PPIs). Accordingly, miRNA156 is a positive regulator of anthocyanin accumulation (Li et al., 2021a). To date, three studies have reported putative miRNAs in eggplant, and seven pre-miRNA156/7 hairpin sequences have been identified through bioinformatics (Yang et al., 2013b; Din and Barozai, 2014; Gu et al., 2014). However, no functional characterization has been performed on miRNAs or their gene targets in this important crop plant. 

Whereas the molecular regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis in climacteric fleshy fruit has been characterized in detail, that of non-climacteric species such as eggplant has received far less attention. Most reports on eggplant are based on quantitative trait locus analysis and homology searches of sequences from different Solanaceae species (Tigchelaar et al., 1968; De Jong et al., 2004; Barchi et al., 2012; Cericola et al., 2014; Toppino et al., 2016; Sulli et al., 2021). To date, five MYB-encoding genes (SmMYB1, SmMYB5, SmMYB35, SmMYB75, and SmMYB113) responsible for inducing anthocyanin pigmentation have been reported in eggplant (Zhang et al., 2014; Docimo et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017, 2024b; Shi et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022). According to a recent report, SmMYB5 interacts with TRANSPARENT TESTA 8 (SmTT8), an anthocyanin-promoting bHLH TF, and activates the SmCHS, SmF3H, and SmANS promoters by direct binding to positively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis in fruit skin (Li et al., 2024b). Besides these activators, an R3-MYB TF (named as MYB1-like) that inhibits the MBW complex via competition with MYB activators for the bHLH binding site has also been identified in eggplant (Moglia et al., 2020). In addition to MYB1-like, three other eggplant MYBs (SmMYB44, SmMYB86, and SmMYB108) have been found to act as repressors of anthocyanin biosynthesis (Li et al., 2017, 2021b, 2022). Overexpression of SmMYB86 in transgenic eggplant reduced anthocyanin accumulation in the fruit skin via transcriptional suppression of anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (SmCHS, SmF3H, and SmANS) (Li et al., 2021b). Apart from MYB TFs, two bHLH proteins, SmTT8 and GLABRA3 (SmGL3), and a single WD40 protein, TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA (SmTTG1), are additional regulatory factors related to anthocyanin biosynthesis in eggplant (Xin-Yu et al., 2014), and SmbHLH13 has been reported as an activator of related structural genes (SmCHS and SmF3H) (Xi et al., 2021). Nonetheless, no information is yet available on post-transcriptional regulation or the metabolic network that modulates the unusual pigmentation shift in eggplant fruit skin during development. 

To resolve the molecular basis underlying the shift in eggplant fruit pigmentation during development, we integrated miRNA and mRNA expression with metabolite profiling data. We discovered that transcriptional regulation of anthocyanin biosynthesis is regulated by SPL (SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15) and MYB (MYB1 and MYB2) TFs, whereas that of the yellow skin pigment naringenin chalcone is mediated by the MYB12 TF. Moreover, we established the roles of miRNA157 and miRNA858 in the posttranscriptional regulation of SPLs (SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15) and MYB12, respectively. Remarkably, in contrast to other model systems, SPLs in eggplant positively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis either by transcriptionally regulating MYB1 and MYB2 TFs or by directly controlling the expression of anthocyanin biosynthesis genes. Integrating this information, we generated a mechanistic model for the molecular regulation of flavonoid metabolism that leads to the unusual shift in skin pigmentation during eggplant fruit development.

# Profiling flavonoid-related metabolites and genes in a collection of eggplant accessions

To characterize the variation in eggplant fruit pigmentation during development, we analyzed a collection of eggplant accessions (Supplemental Table 1) with diverse skin colorations at two developmental stages (unripe [corresponding to the commercial ripe stage] and ripe [corresponding to the physiologically ripe stage]) (Figure 1B). Fruit reached their full size at the unripe stage (7 weeks post anthesis [WPA]). At this stage, the fruit, calyx, and peduncle exhibited typical commercial firmness, and the seeds were near their final size but remained immature. By the ripe stage (11 WPA), fruit displayed increased firmness, with the calyx and peduncle becoming noticeably harder. The flesh turned spongy and fibrous, containing fully mature, visibly hard seeds. 

As well as differing in the accumulation of purple and green pigments in their skin, all the accessions displayed the accumulation of a yellow-brown pigment at the ripe stage (Figure 1B). We used ultra performance liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (UPLC–MS) to chemically characterize the pigments involved in the color shift during eggplant fruit maturation. Multivariate UPLC–MS data analysis presented as principal-component analysis (PCA) plots separated the unripe stage from the ripe skin tissues (Supplemental Figure 2). Six hundred ninety-three mass signals showed significantly greater accumulation in ripe-stage skin compared with unripe fruit tissue in all 11 accessions examined (Supplemental Dataset 1). This set of mass signals represented roughly 140 different metabolites. The data were ranked according to the largest fold change in each of the 11 accessions. We next analyzed the levels of phenylalanine and flavonoid biosynthetic pathway metabolites across the different accessions (Figure 1C and Supplemental Figure 3; Supplemental Dataset 1). Levels of phenylalanine, naringenin chalcone, naringenin, and additional pathway intermediates (e.g., kaempferol) increased significantly during ripening in all 11 accessions, and their accumulation pattern was identical regardless of skin color (Figure 1C and Supplemental Figure 3). By contrast, the level of purple pigments (delphinidin-based anthocyanins) decreased throughout the ripening process (Figure 1D). These results demonstrate a clear shift in the pigment accumulation of eggplant fruit skin. Dark purple anthocyanins accumulate to very high levels up to the commercial ripe stage, after which they are replaced by the yellow intermediate naringenin chalcone. Interestingly, although anthocyanins do not accumulate in white-skinned eggplant accessions (Figure 1D), naringenin chalcone accumulates to levels comparable to those observed in ripe purple-skinned accessions (Figure 1C). This suggests that the processes of naringenin chalcone and anthocyanin biosynthesis are, at least in part, regulated independently. 

We next performed a comparative analysis to identify genes potentially involved in the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway associated with metabolite changes in eggplant fruit skin. Amino acid sequences of known flavonoid biosynthetic enzymes from Arabidopsis (www.arabidopsis.org), tomato, and tobacco (www. solgenomics.net) were used as queries in a BLAST search against publicly available eggplant genome sequences (Hirakawa et al., 2014; Barchi et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020). This analysis led to the identification of 18 genes putatively involved in the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway in eggplant (Supplemental Table 3). 

To investigate the relationship between the transcript levels of these genes and the metabolic changes in eggplant fruit skin, we examined the expression patterns of these transcripts in the same sample set used previously (Figure 1B). The analysis revealed distinct expression patterns between early biosynthetic genes (EBGs) and late biosynthetic genes (LBGs), which lead to the production of flavonols and anthocyanins, respectively (Supplemental Figure 4). EBGs clearly exhibited significant induction during fruit ripening, regardless of fruit skin color (Supplemental Figure 4). The transcript abundance of LBGs did not show consistent changes during the transition to ripening in the accessions studied here. # Analysis of flavonoid-associated transcripts, proteins, and metabolites during fruit development of a single eggplant cultivar

We next shifted from the analysis of multiple accessions to detailed investigation of a specific eggplant breeding line, termed DR2, which is amenable to stable transformation (Arpaia et al., 1997; Rotino et al., 1997; Toppino et al., 2010; Florio et al., 2021). Fruit of the DR2 cultivar display the typical pigmentation change observed in all commercial eggplant cultivars today, starting as dark black at the early stage and transforming to yellowish brown later in ripening. Working with DR2 thus broadens the significance of our findings to many other eggplant accessions. We examined the accumulation of naringenin chalcone and anthocyanins at four stages of fruit development and ripening (3, 7, 9, and 11 WPA) (Figure 2A and Supplemental Figure 5). These stages align with the classical developmental stages of tomato fruit: immature green, mature green, breaker, and red ripe. 

The abundance of four delphinidin-based anthocyanins (Supplemental Dataset 3) gradually decreased during eggplant fruit maturation, whereas that of naringenin chalcone increased during ripening, starting from 7 WPA (Figure 2A and Supplemental Figure 5). Levels of other pathway intermediates, such as naringenin and kaempferol derivatives, also increased progressively throughout maturation and ripening of eggplant fruit (Supplemental Figure 6). We next analyzed the expression of 18 flavonoid EBGs and LBGs (Supplemental Table 3) at the transcript and protein levels and found a clear correspondence between gene and protein expression during fruit development and ripening (Figure 2B). Furthermore, all EBGs were induced during fruit development, whereas all LBGs were downregulated during fruit maturation (Figure 2B).

# Coordination between SPLs and MYB is not mediated by protein-protein interactions in eggplant

Previous research has shown that SPL-mediated regulation of flavonoid metabolism occurs at the PPI level and that SPLs physically interact with diverse MYB TFs to inhibit flavonoid biosynthesis (Gou et al., 2011; Li et al., 2020). We therefore examined potential PPIs between MYB1, MYB2, and MYB12 and SmSPL6a, SmSPL10, and SmSPL15 using yeast-two-hybrid and luciferase complementation imaging (LCI) assays. In contrast to other reported examples (e.g., in Arabidopsis and blueberry) (Gou et al., 2011; Li et al., 2020), we did not identify positive interactions between these SPL and MYB proteins (Supplemental Figures 24 and 25). This suggested that the SPL vs. MYB coordination might function through a different mechanism in eggplant. 

To test whether the SPL–MYB interface is mediated through transcriptional regulation, we analyzed the approximately 2-kb uprs of candidate MYBs for the presence of core SPL binding motifs (Supplemental Text 1). Gou et al. (2011) showed that Arabidopsis SPL9 physically interacts with AtPAP1 (MYB75), an anthocyanin activator, and we therefore included the approximately 2-kb upr of AtPAP1 in the motif search (Supplemental Text 1). # DISCUSSION

# Color change mediated by miRNAs and SPLs denotes the fruit phase transition in eggplant

Color change is a common phenomenon in flowers and fruits of angiosperms and has significant roles in reproduction. Floral pigments play a role in pollinator attraction; certain colors will signal

to insects that a flower is rewarding and worth visiting. In the same way, fruit color change is important for attracting frugivores and providing protection from radiation, as pigments may serve as sunscreens (Nevo et al., 2018). For example, tomato fruit that lack naringenin chalcone, the pigment identified here as accumulating in ripening eggplant skin, are highly sensitive to solar radiation (Mintz-Oron et al., 2008). At early stages of development, most fruit are colored by chlorophyll, which enables a degree of photosynthetic activity. In green tomato, photosynthesis has been reported to contribute $10 \% - 1 5 \%$ of the fixed carbon in fruit (Hetherington et al., 1998). Additional specific functions of fruit pigments may be related to protection against pathogens (Kaur et al., 2023). Change in fruit pigmentation is a programmed process regulated by hormone signaling and regulatory networks (Muhammad et al., 2024). It can be regarded as an indicator of the phase transition from immature (juvenile) to mature-ripe (adult) fruit in which reproductive competence is acquired. Consistent with this idea is our finding of a major role for miRNA157 and SPLs (i.e., SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15) in the fruit color change of eggplant, as they are well known for their role in modulating phase transition. 

During development and ripening of eggplant fruit, significant changes occur in skin and flesh color and fruit firmness and texture, as well as seed maturation (Mennella et al., 2012). However, limited information is available regarding the biochemical and molecular changes associated with the development and ripening of eggplant. Notably, eggplant fruit represents a unique case of a pigmentation shift in which an initial purple color is replaced by yellow during development and ripening. Despite the prevalence of this phenomenon, to date only a single study on blueberries has reported the molecular mechanisms that coordinate the biochemical processes associated with fruit color change (Li et al., 2024a). Pigmentation in blueberry involves direct regulation of the ethylene production pathway, thereby coordinating the shift from green (chlorophyll-dominated) to purple (anthocyanin-rich) hues during fruit maturation and ripening. The pattern of pigmentation change in blueberry (i.e., from green to purple) is a mirror image of the one observed during eggplant development and ripening. Several studies have highlighted the involvement of miRNA156 and miRNA858 in regulating flavonoid accumulation, including in fruit (Jia et al., 2015; Qian et al., 2017; Tirumalai et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020, 2023, 2024a; Yang et al., 2020; Su et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022).

# Molecular interactions in the regulatory network that controls fruit pigmentation are unique in eggplant compared with other species

The SPL family TFs are widely recognized as key regulators of anthocyanin metabolism, being post-transcriptionally targeted by miRNA156/7 (Wang and Wang, 2015; Xu et al., 2016). Studies in Arabidopsis, blueberry, and apple have demonstrated that SPL TFs typically negatively regulate anthocyanin accumulation by physically sequestering MYB TFs, the classical inducers of anthocyanin metabolism (Gou et al., 2011; Li et al., 2020). Moreover, miRNA156/7 has been observed to target SPL TFs in these species, thus positively regulating anthocyanin accumulation. 

However, our current study reveals a different regulatory pattern in eggplant, in which the miRNA156/7–SPL (6a, 10, and 15)–MYB (1 and 2) regulatory hub functions oppositely compared with other species in anthocyanin accumulation. Analysis of temporal transcript data from eggplant fruit skin demonstrated inverse accumulation patterns of miRNA156/7 and SPLs, with miRNA156/7 levels gradually increasing during fruit development. Although this observation aligns with findings in blueberry, it fails to explain the opposite patterns of anthocyanin accumulation in these species. In blueberry, miRNA156 targets SPL12, a negative regulator of anthocyanin accumulation (Li et al., 2020), whereas in eggplant, SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15 are positive reg-

Furthermore, whereas blueberry SPL12 physically interacts with and inhibits the activity of MYBPA1, a classic inducer of anthocyanin metabolism (Plunkett et al., 2018; Gunther et al., 2020 € ; Li et al., 2020; Karppinen et al., 2021), eggplant SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15 do not physically interact with the candidate MYBs (MYB1 and MYB2). Instead, they act as transcriptional activators of MYB1 and MYB2, providing an alternative molecular mechanism for the regulation of anthocyanin metabolism that underlies the unusual shift in pigmentation during eggplant fruit ripening. 

Our data also revealed that eggplant SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15 suppress the expression of miRNA157, establishing a feedback loop to autoregulate the miRNA157–SPL module. Furthermore, we highlighted the connection between the anthocyanin-related miRNA156/7–SPLs module and metabolism of the yellow pigment naringenin chalcone and other flavonoids during eggplant fruit ripening. At the early stage of fruit development, miRNA157 expression is low, resulting in high SPL transcript abundance. This activates the transcription of miRNA858, which post-transcriptionally suppresses MYB12, thereby downregulating EBG-mediated flavonol biosynthesis. At the late stage of fruit development, increased miRNA157 expression leads to a reduction in SPLs. Consequently, miRNA858 is downregulated, enabling MYB12 to upregulate flavonol biosynthesis. This emphasizes the importance of studying species-specific effects, even of well-conserved miRNAs. A unique molecular mechanism of anthocyanin accumulation is likely because anthocyanins accumulate to their highest levels at the immature stage of fruit development in eggplant and decline as ripening progresses, whereas other fleshy-fruited species that accumulate anthocyanins, such as grape and blueberry, do so in a ripeningdependent manner. 

# Active anthocyanin catabolism during the color change in eggplant fruit?

Levels of anthocyanins in immature eggplant fruit gradually decline as the fruit develops and ripening progresses. Whether anthocyanins are actively degraded during eggplant fruit development or simply diluted by cell growth when fruit size increases significantly remains an open question. Post-harvest degradation of anthocyanins in fruit has been attributed to environmental factors such as heat and light (Francis and Markakis, 1989; Steyn et al., 2004). However, the possibility of anthocyanin dilution due to cell growth during fruit development in eggplant appears unlikely, as unripe and ripe fruit of all accessions examined in this study were of similar size. Previous studies have revealed that anthocyanin levels can be influenced by active degradation via enzymes such as polyphenol oxidases (PPOs), peroxidases (Prxs), and $\beta \cdot$ -glucosidases as well as by non-enzymatic chemical degradation (Chassy et al., 2015; Fang et al., 2015; Liu et al., 

2018). For instance, skin-specific enzymes like VvPrx3 and VvPrx31 have been reported to degrade anthocyanins in anthocyanin-rich grapes (Movahed et al., 2016; Xie et al., 2021). Similarly, BcPrx01 plays a role in flower anthocyanin catabolism in Brunfelsia calycina (a Solanaceae family member) (Zipor et al., 2015). In addition to Prxs, three $\beta \mathrm { \hbar }$ -glucosidases with anthocyanin breakdown ability, also known as anthocyanases, have been isolated from blood orange pulp, B. calycina flowers, and eggplant fruit (Sakamura and Obata, 1961; Barbagallo et al., 2007; Dong et al., 2019). According to Sakamura and Obata (1961), anthocyanase shows various rates of decolorization with different anthocyanidin derivatives, with the order being pelargonidin $<$ peonidin $<$ cyanidin $<$ delphinidin $<$ delphinidinglucoside acylated with $p$ -coumaric acid. Despite uncertainty with respect to the pathway of anthocyanin degradation and the resulting products in plants, several studies have suggested the production of protocatechuic acid during anthocyanin degradation (Zarrouk et al., 2016; Niu et al., 2017). Unlike Prxs and $\beta \cdot$ glucosidases, much less is known about anthocyanin-degrading PPOs, although there is evidence suggesting their involvement in anthocyanin degradation in plants (Vaknin et al., 2005; Oren-Shamir, 2009). In addition, an intracellular laccase has been reported to be responsible for epicatechin-mediated anthocyanin degradation in the pericarp of litchi fruit (Fang et al., 2015). If active degradation of anthocyanins indeed occurs in eggplant fruit, genes associated with this pathway may possibly be controlled by the regulatory network of miRNAs and TFs reported here. 

# Color change in fruit skin and the domestication of eggplant

As mentioned above, most fleshy fruit species investigated to date accumulate anthocyanins during fruit maturation rather than directly after fruit set as observed in eggplant. The reason for this difference is not clear but may reflect a specific function of intense black anthocyanins in biotic and abiotic stress responses or efficient seed dispersal. Nonetheless, most evidence suggests that eggplant was domesticated from the wild species Solanum incanum, whose fruit are green at the immature, unripe stage (Daunay et al., 2001; Doganlar et al., 2002a, 2002b; Barchi et al., 2023). Consequently, one cannot rule out the possibility that accumulation of anthocyanins during early eggplant fruit development was an (un)intended trait selected by humans during or after the domestication of S. melongena from S. incanum. In this regard, it is interesting to note that the trend of glycoalkaloid accumulation is opposite to that of anthocyanins. Glycoalkaloids reach their highest levels when the eggplant skin turns yellow-brown (Mennella et al., 2012). The lowest glycoalkaloid content in eggplant flesh occurs at the unripe phase of development, when anthocyanins are at their peak. The toxic effects of glycoalkaloids may therefore have influenced human preferences during domestication, favoring eggplants with anthocyanin-rich unripe skin. 

One of the most abundant flavonoids in tomato is the yellow pigment naringenin chalcone, which accumulates in the skin from the mature green, breaker stage onwards (Mintz-Oron et al., 2008). It is an integral part of the tomato fruit cuticle and represents approximately $1 \%$ of its dry weight (Luque et al., 1995; Muir et al., 2001; Adato et al., 2009). Studies more than a 

century ago alluded to the existence of a yellow pigment in mature fruit skin of eggplant and suggested that it is identical to the substance that accumulates in ripe tomato (Halsted, 1918; Tigchelaar et al., 1968). Indeed, in the present study we confirmed that this pigment is naringenin chalcone and that its levels increase gradually as the eggplant fruit matures. Curiously, the presence of yellow naringenin chalcone in fruit skin is not restricted to the Solanaceae family; it has also been reported in the rind of melon fruit by Tadmor et al. (2010). It will be interesting to examine whether naringenin chalcone accumulation in melon fruit is controlled by the miRNA858– MYB12 module as reported here for eggplant. 

This study shows how the color swap in fruit is executed through a regulatory network composed of miRNAs and their TF targets. By activation and repression of specific factors, metabolic flux is directed in a way that results in accumulation of one pigment and reduced biosynthesis of the other (here, the flavonoid pathway). This mechanism most likely involves many other signaling, regulatory, transport, and metabolic factors that together execute a wellcoordinated program. The non-climacteric nature of eggplant fruit suggests that not ethylene, but rather a different hormone, most likely orchestrates this phase transition. Finally, our discoveries also have applied relevance, as modification of miRNA157 and SPLs prolongs the dark coloration of fruit, which is an important agronomic and post-harvest quality trait in eggplant.

# Plant material and growth conditions

Eggplant (S. melongena), tobacco (N. benthamiana), and S. nigrum were grown in soil in a greenhouse with day/night temperatures of $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / 1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and a 16/8-h light/dark photoperiod. Frozen powder of eggplant skin $\mathbf { 1 0 0 } \mathbf { m } \mathbf { 9 } )$ ) was extracted with a solution containing $70 \%$ methanol and $2 \%$ formic acid at a ratio of 1:4 w/v (tissue: extraction solution), then sonicated for 20 min and centrifuged for 10 min at 13 000 rpm. The supernatant was filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ polytetrafluoroethylene membrane filter and injected to an ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC) system coupled with a quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry instrument (Waters HDMS SYNAPT G2-S) coupled to a PDA detector (190–800 nm). Metabolites were separated by gradient elution (acetonitrile–water, containing $3 \%$ formic acid) on a $1 0 0 \times 2 . 1 \cdot \mathrm { m m }$ i.d., $1 . 7 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ UPLC BEH C18 column (Waters Acquity) at a flow rate of 0.1 mL/min and a column temperature of $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Eluent A was $3 \%$ formic acid in water, and eluent B was $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile. Flavonoid analyses were performed as described by Itkin et al. (2009). For statistical analysis, three to five biological replicates were used. 

# RNA extraction and sequencing

Total RNA was extracted from fruit skin as described in Meisel et al. (2005) with minor modifications. RNA integrity was checked on a $1 \%$ agarose gel and quantified using Nanodrop (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and Agilent TapeStation instruments. Approximately 400 ng of DNaseI-treated total RNA was used to construct cDNA libraries by ${ \mathfrak { Z } } ^ { \prime }$ -end sequencing (TranSeq method) 

(Tzfadia et al., 2018). Samples were sequenced on the Illumina HiSeq 2500 system using the SR60 protocol. The output was approximately 2.5 million reads per sample. In brief, raw reads were aligned to the eggplant genome (V3 of Barchi et al., 2019) using TopHat v2.0.10 (Kim et al., 2013), uniquely mapped reads were counted using HTSeq-count v0.6.1p1 (Anders et al., 2015), and differentially expressed genes were identified using DESeq2 v1.6.3 (Anders et al., 2013; Love et al., 2014). For statistical analysis, three biological replicates were used, each consisting of three or four pooled fruit samples. 

# Small RNA-seq, miRNA annotation, and miRNA target identification

Small RNA libraries were prepared using the NEBNext Multiplex Small RNA Library Prep Set for Illumina (New England Biolabs) and sequenced on the Illumina platform to generate 50-bp single-end reads. miRNAs were annotated using the miRDeep-P2 pipeline (Friedlander et al., 2012 € ; Kuang et al., 2019). In brief, raw sequencing reads were trimmed to remove adapter sequences and filtered for quality using Cutadapt (Martin, 2011). Reads 18–30 nucleotides in length were retained and aligned to the eggplant genome published by Barchi et al. (2019) using Bowtie (Langmead et al., 2009). Candidate miRNAs were identified using miRDeep-P2, and known miRNAs were annotated on the basis of precursor sequences from miR-Base. Additional precursor sequences from tomato (S. lycopersicum), potato (S. tuberosum), and A. thaliana were incorporated for curation and final annotation. Differentially expressed miR-NAs were identified through relevant pairwise comparisons using DESeq2 (Love et al., 2014). Only significantly differentially expressed miRNAs were retained for target prediction, which was performed using psRNATarget (Dai et al., 2018). To identify eggplant miRNA156/7 and miRNA858 hairpin genes, mature miRNA156/7 and miRNA858 sequence variants from Arabidopsis plus all the hairpin precursor sequences from tomato were used as BLASTN queries. The potential of these putative miRs to target eggplant SPL genes was assessed using psRNA Target (Dai and Zhao, 2011). The secondary structure of the hairpins was then predicted with the mfold package (Zuker, 2003). 

# Proteomics analysis

Protein extracts of fruit skin were processed for tryptic digestion using a modified filter-aided sample preparation protocol as outlined by Kazachkova et al. (2021), with $5 0 ~ { \mu \ g }$ of total protein. After digestion, peptides were acidified using trifluoroacetic acid, dried using a SpeedVac concentrator, and subsequently analyzed using a nano-UPLC (Waters) coupled to a nano-electrospray ionization emitter with a $1 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ tip (New Objective) connected to a quadrupole Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Q Exactive Plus; Thermo Fisher Scientific) as detailed in Szymanski et al. (2017). Protein identification and label-free quantification were performed using PROTEOME DISCOVERER software (v.2.2) (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The Sequest HT engine was used to search against the eggplant genomic scaffolds (http://ddlab.sci.univr.it/cgi-bin/gb2/gbrowse/Eggplant/; Barchi et al., 2019) with the following parameters: (1) a maximum of two missed tryptic cleavages, (2) precursor mass tolerance of 10 ppm, (3) fragment mass tolerance of 0.02 Da, and (4) fixed and variable modifications, including carbamidomethylation of 

cysteines and oxidation of methionines. Data were filtered for a minimum of two peptides matching the protein and a false discovery rate value of 0.01 or less, for both proteins and peptides. For statistical analysis, three biological replicates were used. 

# Real-time qPCR

DNaseI-treated total RNA was reverse transcribed using the High-Capacity cDNA reverse transcription kit with random hexamer primers (Applied Biosystems http://www. appliedbiosystems.com/). Real-time qPCR was performed using Fast SYBR Green master mix (Applied Biosystems), and the Step One Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) was used to run and analyze the samples. Gene-specific oligonucleotides were designed using Primer Express 2.0 software (Applied Biosystems). In eggplant, the ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase (RuBP) gene (accession number: JX448343; SMEL_002g159750) was used as an endogenous control (Gantasala et al., 2013). miRNA-specific oligonucleotide design, cDNA preparation, and stem-loop PCR were performed as described in Varkonyi-Gasic et al. (2007). Oligonucleotides used in this study are detailed in Supplemental Table 2. For statistical analysis, three biological replicates were used. 

# Eggplant genome analysis

The sequences of previously identified SPL genes and proteins from Arabidopsis (www.arabidopsis.org) and tomato (www. solgenomics.net) were used for BLAST searches (http://ddlab. sci.univr.it/blast/blast.html; http://eggplant-hq.cn/Eggplant/ blastBefore) of two available eggplant genomic scaffolds (Hirakawa et al., 2014; Barchi et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2020). Targets of miRNA156 and miRNA157 were identified from all the detected SPL genes using the criteria established by Schwab et al. (2005). Amino acid alignments of conserved SQUAMOSA promoter-binding protein domains within the SPL protein sequences were obtained using Clustal Omega (https:// www.ebi.ac.uk/jdispatcher/msa/clustalo; Sievers et al., 2011) to determine the phylogenetic relationships among them.

# Yeast two-hybrid assays

Yeast two-hybrid assays were performed as described in Panda et al. (2022). In brief, the coding regions of SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15 were cloned into pGADT7 (Takara-Clontech), and the coding sequences of MYB1, MYB2, and MYB12 (full-length and truncated $[ ^ { \Delta } N$ -terminal; lacking DNA binding and transcription activation domains]) were cloned into pGBKT7 (Takara-Clontech). Each pGBKT7 vector was individually transformed into a Y2H Gold yeast strain (Takara-Clontech) that contained the pGADT7 vector sub-cloned with SPL6a, SPL10, or SPL15. Yeast transformants were selected for the presence of vectors by growing them on synthetic dextrose agar plates lacking Leu and Trp ( L T) and for possible interactions on plates lacking Leu, Trp, His, and Ade ( L T H A) and by X- $\alpha$ -Gal staining to monitor $\beta$ -galactosidase reporter-gene expression. All cloning reactions were carried out using the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme). Oligonucleotides used for cloning are listed in Supplemental Table 2. 

# Split LCI assays

Split LCI assays were performed as described by Panda et al. (2022). Vectors were prepared using the coding sequences of different MYB genes (MYB1, MYB2, and MYB12) and ligated into the N-LUC vector pCambia1300. Similarly, the coding sequences of SPL6a, SPL10, and SPL15 genes were cloned into the C-LUC vector pCambia1300. All cloning reactions were carried out using the ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit (Vazyme). The constructs were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 and transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves as described in Sparkes et al. (2006). After 3 days, infiltrated leaves were collected, sprayed with 1 mM luciferin dissolved in ultra-pure water containing $1 \%$ Triton X-100, and kept in dark conditions for 10 min to quench the fluorescence. A cooled charge-coupled device imaging system was used to capture the LUC images. Four sites were infiltrated for each combination of vectors, and the entire assay was repeated twice. The oligonucleotides used for these assays are listed in Supplemental Table 2.
