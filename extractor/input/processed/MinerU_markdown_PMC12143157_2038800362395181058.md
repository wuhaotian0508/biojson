# Transgenic expression of mAChR-C dsRNA in maize confers efficient locust control

# ABSTRACT

Plant-mediated RNA interference (RNAi), in which double-stranded RNAs (dsRNAs) targeting insect genes are expressed in plants for insect ingestion, has shown great potential for the control of herbivorous insect pests. Locusts, which are among the most destructive agricultural insect pests, appear to be resistant to orally delivered naked dsRNA. Moreover, the feasibility of using plant-mediated RNAi to suppress target gene expression in locusts remains unclear. Using the migratory locust Locusta migratoria, we report that the C-type muscarinic acetylcholine receptor (mAChR-C), a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) belonging to the bioamine receptor subfamily, plays a pivotal role in chitin metabolism by regulating genes responsible for chitin synthesis and degradation. Knockdown of locust mAChR-C via injection of dsRNA caused defective nymph molting and metamorphosis, accompanied by malformation, arrested development, and impaired motility. Notably, locusts fed transgenic maize expressing locust mAChR-C dsRNAs exhibited defective phenotypes similar to those subjected to mAChR-C dsRNA injection. In contrast, ingestion of transgenic maize expressing locust mAChR-C dsRNA had no significant effects on non-target insects, including the fall armyworm Spodoptera frugiperda, the cotton bollworm Helicoverpa armigera, the Asian corn borer Ostrinia furnacalis, and the oriental armyworm Mythimna separata. Our results suggest that transgenic expression of locust mAChR-C dsRNA is an effective RNAi approach for locust control and offers a promising eco-friendly strategy for locust management.

# INTRODUCTION

RNA interference (RNAi) technology has emerged over the past decade as a promising strategy for the effective management of agricultural insect pests (Mamta and Rajam, 2017; Zotti et al., 2018). Commercial products based on this approach are currently coming to market (Nitnavare et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2023), and Ledprona, a sprayable double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) product that targets the Colorado potato beetle Leptinotarsa decemlineata, has been registered by the US Environmental Protection Agency (Stokstad, 2024). The efficiency of RNAi varies greatly across insect taxa due to multiple factors, including insufficient dsRNA internalization and dsRNA instability inside the insect (Cooper et al., 2019; Christiaens et al., 2020). Plantmediated RNAi is considered an ideal approach for suppressing insect gene expression, as it enables the continuous synthesis of dsRNA in transgenic plants, which is critical for maintaining stable knockdown of target genes (Zhang et al., 2017; Chung et al., 2021). Substantial efforts have been made to develop plant-

mediated RNAi in various crops, including cotton, maize, rice, wheat, and potato (Zotti et al., 2018; Chung et al., 2021). Insect pests targeted by these efforts include species in Lepidoptera, Coleoptera, and Hemiptera, such as the cotton bollworm Helicoverpa armigera, the western corn rootworm Diabrotica virgifera virgifera, and the sweet potato whitefly Bemisia tabaci (Baum et al., 2007; Mao et al., 2007; Tian et al., 2015; Luo et al., 2017; Gong et al., 2022). 

Locusts, which belong to the order Orthoptera, can form voracious swarms and are among the most destructive agricultural pests in the world (Zhang et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2020). Most current locust management programs depend on chemical pesticides and microbial biopesticides; however, both approaches have limitations (Peveling, 2001; Zhang and David, 2005). For chemical pesticides, side effects and the development of insecticide resistance in locusts have been increasingly reported (Guo et al., 2015, 2016; Jin et al., 2020), whereas microbial biopesticides often act slowly and show 

variable effectiveness in inducing locust mortality (Zhang et al., 2019). Orthopteran species exhibit high susceptibility to dsRNA, small interfering RNA (siRNA), and systemic RNAi responses (Ren et al., 2014; Santos et al., 2014; Nakamura et al., 2022). The injection of dsRNA into the hemocoel of locusts can result in substantial knockdown of target genes (Wynant et al., 2012; Ren et al., 2014; Zheng et al., 2022); however, this effect is not observed when naked dsRNA is delivered by ingestion (Luo et al., 2012, 2013; Song et al., 2017; Spit et al., 2017). Studies on the desert locust Schistocerca gregaria and the migratory locust Locusta migratoria have demonstrated that double-stranded ribonucleases (dsRNases) in their midgut (Mg) juices degrade orally delivered dsRNA, thereby limiting RNAi efficacy (Song et al., 2017; Spit et al., 2017). RNAi responses induced by transgenic plants expressing dsRNA can also be inhibited by dsRNases in insect pests, such as those from the orders Lepidoptera and Hemiptera (Zhang et al., 2017; Cooper et al., 2019). One underlying RNAi mechanism involves the processing of plantexpressed dsRNA into siRNAs (Borges and Martienssen, 2015), which are stabilized by ${ \boldsymbol { 2 ^ { \prime } } }$ -O-methylation at the ${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ end in plants, protecting them from degradation (Kim et al., 2010; Zhao et al., 2012). Recently, a transgenic rice line that inhibits miR-8-5p via the short tandem target mimic method was developed to suppress the gregarious behavior of migratory locusts by targeting glutamate decarboxylase (Yang et al., 2023). However, the transgenic expression of dsRNA in crops for locust control remains largely unexplored. 

Insect muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (mAChRs), members of the G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) family, are considered potential targets for insecticides due to their crucial roles in locomotion, development, and reproduction (Dick et al., 1997; Gross and Bloomquist, 2018; Li et al., 2021). Although several chemicals that target mammalian mAChRs have been evaluated for their insecticidal effects (Honda et al., 2007; Gross and Bloomquist, 2018), agents that specifically target insect mAChRs are currently unavailable. Our previous study revealed that the C-type mAChR (mAChR-C) plays a significant role in vitellogenesis and egg production in L. migratoria (Zheng et al., 2021). In the present study, we found that RNAimediated knockdown of mAChR-C via dsRNA injection into the hemocoel of nymphal locusts inhibited the expression of genes responsible for chitin degradation and synthesis, leading to disrupted chitin metabolism and, consequently, increased mortality, prolonged development, and defective appendages. Transgenic maize expressing mAChR-C dsRNA significantly reduced mAChR-C mRNA levels in locusts and exhibited a high degree of resistance to locusts. These results highlight a potential strategy for developing RNAi-based agents for locust control. This study also enables further investigation into the mechanisms by which plant-mediated RNAi avoids the degradation of orally delivered naked dsRNA. 

# RESULTS

# mAChR-C is ubiquitously expressed in locust tissues

GPCRs that respond to bioamines and neuropeptides, which play pivotal roles in insect development, reproduction, and behavior (Van Wielendaele et al., 2013; Audsley and Down, 

2015), are promising targets for insect pest control. We previously identified 10 GPCRs from the fat body (FB) of adult female locusts: 3 from the bioamine receptor subfamily, namely tyramine receptor (TyrR), octopamine b1 receptor (Octb1R), and mAChR-C, and 7 from the neuropeptide receptor subfamily, namely cardioacceleratory peptide receptor (CCAPR), corazonin receptor (CrzR), myosuppressin receptor (MSR), short neuropeptide F receptor (sNPFR), allatostatin receptor (ASTR), sex peptide receptor (SPR), and moody2 (Yun et al., 2022). RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis revealed that mAChR-C mRNA levels increased notably from the penultimate (4th) instar nymphs to 5-day-old adults (Figure 1A). No significant difference in mAChR-C mRNA abundance was observed between females and males (Figure 1B). Interestingly, although mAChR-C expression fluctuated, it appeared to be lower during the 2nd to 5th instar than at earlier or later stages (Figure 1C). To assess spatial expression patterns, seven tissues from final (5th) instar nymphs were analyzed: the nervous system (NS), Mg, integument (In), FB, muscle (Mu), hemolymph (He), and Malpighian tubules (MT). Notably, mAChR-C transcripts were significantly more abundant in the FB, followed by the Mg, MT, and In (Figure 1D). The temporal expression patterns of mAChR-C from 1st instar nymphs to 6-day-old adults were further examined in the whole body (WB) and in 5 tissues. As shown in Figure 1E, mAChR-C expression fluctuated in the WB across both nymphal and adult stages. In the FB, mAChR-C mRNA was more abundant in 5th instar nymphs, whereas expression in the other 4 tissues varied throughout the nymphal stage (Figure 1E). In adulthood, mAChR-C was highly expressed in the FB, Mg, and In, but not in the NS or MT (Figure 1E). Collectively, these data suggest that mAChR-C is involved in the regulation of insect development, molting, and metamorphosis. 

# mAChR-C is required for nymphal development and molting

To investigate the function of mAChR-C in nymphal growth and molting, we performed RNAi experiments using 4th instar nymphs (N4). Two dsRNAs derived from fragments corresponding to different coding regions of mAChR-C (mAChR-C F1 and mAChR-C F2) were used to assess the gene’s RNAi responsiveness. Newly emerged N4 nymphs were treated with these dsRNAs (dsmAChR-C F1 and dsmAChR-C F2), and their phenotypes were observed during N4 and N5. Treatment with dsmAChR-C F1 resulted in $7 6 . 7 \%$ , $7 2 . 0 \%$ , and $8 4 . 9 \%$ reductions in mAChR-C mRNA levels in the FB, Mg, and In, respectively, in 2-day-old N4 nymphs (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 1A). The injection of dsmAChR-C F2 achieved knockdown efficiencies of $8 4 . 5 \%$ , 84. $3 \%$ , and $7 9 . 3 \%$ in these 3 tissues, respectively (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 1A). Following successful knockdown, mAChR-C-depleted nymphs displayed defective phenotypes, including increased mortality, abnormal molting, and delayed development (Figure 2B). As shown in Figure 2C, treatments with dsmAChR-C F1 and F2 resulted in mortality rates of $5 2 . 3 \%$ and $2 9 . 5 \%$ , respectively, compared with $3 \%$ mortality in the dsGFP control group. Among the surviving mAChR-C-depleted nymphs, malformations such as defective wing discs and appendages were observed after molting, leading to an additional $1 0 . 0 \%$ and $6 . 3 \%$ mortality in the dsmAChR-C F1 and F2 groups, respectively (Figure 2D). # mAChR-C regulates chitin degradation and synthesis

Chitin is an integral part of the insect cuticle and functions as a mechanical scaffold by forming complex structures with cuticle proteins (Yang et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018). To investigate the regulatory role of mAChR-C in locust development, molting, and metamorphosis, we first performed chitin staining on 6- day-old 5th instar nymphs treated with dsmAChR-C F2. As shown in Figure 4A, old cuticles were markedly thicker in mAChR-C-depleted nymphs compared with the dsGFP control group. In contrast, newly synthesized cuticles were thinner in the mAChR-C knockdown nymphs (Figure 4A). Quantitatively, the thickness index for mAChR-C-depleted nymphs showed a 2.7-fold increase in the old cuticles and a $9 1 . 2 \%$ decrease in the new cuticles compared with dsGFP controls (Figure 4B). These data suggest that mAChR-C functions in both chitin degradation and synthesis. We next examined the effects of mAChR-C RNAi on the expression of locust genes associated with chitin metabolism, including chitinase 5-1 (CHT5-1), CHT5-2, and CHT10, which are important for chitin degradation, as well as chitin synthase 1 (CHS1), CHS2, UDP N-acetylglucosamine pyrophosphorylase 1 (UAP1), and UAP2, which are responsible for 

chitin synthesis (Li et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2016; Zhu et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018). Quantitative real-time PCR demonstrated that CHT5-1, CHT5-2, and CHT10 mRNA levels decreased by $6 2 . 5 \%$ , $9 5 . 5 \%$ , and $8 5 . 1 \%$ , respectively, in the In of mAChR-C-depleted nymphs (Figure 4C). Additionally, CHS1 and UAP2 transcript levels were reduced by $7 3 . 7 \%$ and $8 2 . 9 \%$ , respectively (Figure 4C). However, no significant changes were observed in CHS2 or UAP1 expression (Figure 4C). Our results suggest that mAChR-C regulates locust development, molting, and metamorphosis by modulating the genes involved in chitin degradation and biosynthesis.

# DISCUSSION

Given the attractive benefits of RNAi-based approaches to pest control, such as high species specificity and an increased chance to prevent the development of resistance, plant-mediated RNAi has been investigated for the control of lepidopteran, coleopteran, and hemipteran insect pests (Zhang et al., 2017; Christiaens et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2021). However, the viability of this technology for insect pests from other orders remains unclear. In the present study, we demonstrate that mAChR-C plays a crucial role in development, molting, and morphogenesis through its involvement in chitin metabolism in L. migratoria. Expression of dsRNA targeting locust mAChR-C in maize, achieved via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation, conferred notable resistance to locusts (Figure 7), suggesting that plant-mediated RNAi targeting mAChR-C is a potential approach to mitigate locust damage to agricultural crops. This study offers an alternative for the development of integrative, ecologically safe strategies for orthopteran pest control. 

mAChR-C was previously identified in D. melanogaster and the oriental fruit fly Bactrocera dorsalis; however, no distinct functions have been determined in either species (Xia et al., 2016; Li et al., 2021). Our data demonstrate that mAChR-C is involved in the chitin metabolic pathway by regulating the expression of genes responsible for chitin degradation and synthesis. Chitin in insect cuticles is vital for survival, growth, molting, metamorphosis, and the optimal function of appendages, because it is constantly remodeled throughout the life cycle by the coordinated activities of chitin synthases and chitinases to meet dynamic anatomical requirements (Merzendorfer and Zimoch, 2003; Zhu et al., 2016). The genes required for chitin metabolism are potential targets for insect pest control (Zhu et al., 2016). Our results show that the disruption of chitin metabolism through mAChR-C depletion caused aberrant chitin content in locust cuticles, resulting in mortality, developmental delays, and defective appendages following molting and metamorphosis. Chitin is also an essential component of the peritrophic matrix, which forms a permeability barrier between the food chymus and Mg epithelium to protect insects from microbial pathogens (Kuraishi et al., 2011; Kelkenberg et al., 2015). mAChR-C expression in the Mg fluctuated in a pattern similar to that in the In during the nymphal stages (Figure 1E), suggesting that it is necessary for the dynamic remodeling of chitinous peritrophic matrices. It is likely that mAChR-C depletion in the Mg contributes to the mortality caused by mAChR-C RNAi. The FB, a central hub for energy metabolism during nymphal stages (Arrese and Soulages, 2010), may also play a role in the mAChR-C RNAi response, as mAChR-C was highly expressed in the FB of 5th instar nymphs (Figure 1D) and was effectively knocked down in this tissue (Supplemental Figures 1 and 2). Collectively, these observations suggest that mAChR-C is a suitable target for the development of novel agents to suppress locust populations.

The colony of gregarious L. migratoria was reared under a $1 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ light:10 h dark photoperiod at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \pm 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and a density of $\sim 2 0 0$ locusts per well-ventilated cage, as previously described (Jing et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2022). Their diet consisted of a continuous supply of wheat bran, with fresh wheat seedlings provided once per day. Larvae of S. frugiperda, M. sepa-

In M. separata, transgenic rice-derived siRNAs have been reported to exhibit increased effectiveness in silencing target genes (Bao et al., 2021). Notably, high-throughput sequencing of small RNAs from transgenic maize plants detected considerable production of mAChR-C siRNAs. Among these, 21-bp mAChR-C siRNAs were the most abundant, followed by 22- and 20-bp siRNAs (Figure 5E; Supplemental Table 2). Collectively, our results provide new insights into the varying sensitivity of insect species to different RNAi approaches. Future studies using L. migratoria as a model to uncover how plant-mediated RNAi overcomes dsRNase-mediated degradation would inform the development of RNAi-based pest control technologies. 

Our results show that the non-target species S. frugiperda, M. separata, H. armigera, and O. furnacalis exhibit similar phenotypes when fed on transgenic maize and WT plants, indicating the high species specificity of transgenic maize expressing mAChR-C dsRNA. rata, H. armigera, and O. furnacalis were collected in Zhengzhou, Henan Province, China $( 3 4 ^ { \circ } 2 5 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N } , 1 1 3 ^ { \circ } 4 3 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { E } )$ in 2020 and reared on artificial diets, as described previously (Zhao et al., 2009; Maag et al., 2014; Ganbaatar et al., 2017; Cui et al., 2020). Adults of these 4 species were provided with a $7 . 5 \%$ (v/v) honey solution in water. All larvae and adults of the moth species were maintained at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ under a $1 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ light:10 h dark photoperiod and $7 0 \% ~ \pm ~ 1 0 \%$ relative humidity. 

# RNA-seq and transcript abundance analysis

Total RNA was isolated from the fat bodies of 4th and 5th instar nymphal females at mid-instar, and from adult females at 2 and 5 days post-adult eclosion (PAE), using TRIzol reagent (Tiangen). mRNA was enriched from DNase I-treated total RNA using oligo(dT) magnetic beads, and complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesized using a random hexamer primer. cDNA libraries were constructed following the manufacturer’s protocol and sequenced using an Illumina DNBSEQ platform in single-read mode. Raw RNA sequences were filtered to remove reads containing adaptor sequences or with a high ratio of low-quality bases, and the cleaned data were then mapped to the locust genome to assemble transcripts. Transcript abundance was normalized to fragments per kilobase of exon per million mapped reads.

# qRT-PCR

Total RNA was isolated from the WB or various tissues of locusts. Firststrand cDNA was reverse transcribed using a FastQuant RT Kit with gDNase (Tiangen). qRT-PCR was performed using a Light Cycler 96 system (Roche) with a SuperReal Premix Plus kit (Tiangen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Melting curve analysis was performed to confirm amplification specificity. The $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C } t }$ method was used to calculate relative expression levels, with $\beta$ -actin as the reference gene. Primers used for qRT-PCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# RNAi and phenotyping

cDNA templates were amplified by PCR, cloned into the pGEM-T vector (Promega), and verified by sequencing. dsRNA was synthesized using the T7 RiboMAX Express System (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Nymphs were intra-abdominally injected with $9 \ \mu \ g$ of dsRNA $( 3 \mu 9 / \mu \ L$ in ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } \sf O )$ ) within 12 h after molting, and 5th instar nymphs received a booster injection on day 5. Mortality, molting, and AE were recorded twice daily after dsRNA injection. Relative mAChR-C expression was measured on day 2 in 4th instar nymphs and day 6 in 5th instar nymphs. GFP dsRNA was used as a mock control. Primers used for dsRNA synthesis are listed in Supplemental Table 1. Locusts were imaged using a Canon EOS 550D camera. 

# Microsectioning and chitin staining

Paraffin sections $( 5 \mu \mathsf { m } )$ were prepared from the cuticle of the third abdominal segment of 5th instar nymphs 6 days after dsRNA injection, as previously described (Zhao et al., 2018). Chitin was stained using Fluorescent Brightener 28 (FB28) (Sigma-Aldrich) at $1 \mathrm { \ m g / m L }$ for 30 s, followed by 3 washes with PBS. Nuclei were labeled with propidium iodide for 5 min, followed by 3 washes with PBS. Images were captured using a ZEISS LSM 710 laser confocal microscope and processed with Zen2012 software (Carl Zeiss). Chitin thickness was measured using Image-Pro Plus (Li et al., 2015) and normalized to calculate the thickness index. 

# RNAi vector construction and generation of transgenic maize

mAChR-C F2 was amplified as the target sequence for the generation of RNAi maize. To generate a hairpin RNAi construct, sense and antisense fragments of mAChR-C F2 were inserted on either side of the PDK intron in the pKANNIBAL vector: the sense fragment between the XhoI and KpnI sites, and the antisense fragment between the ClaI and XbaI sites. The RNAi cassette was then ligated into the BamHI site of the binary vector pCAMBIA3301, which contains a ubiquitin promoter and a nopaline synthase terminator (Figure 5A). After sequence verification, the pCAMBIA3301-mAChR-C recombinant vector was introduced into Zea mays (KN5855) via Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain EHA105)- mediated transformation, as described previously (Ishida et al., 2007). 

The primers used for the amplification of mAChR-C F2 are listed in Supplemental Table 1. T0 maize plants were obtained through kanamycin screening, and positive transgenic plants were selected by PCR. To produce the T1 generation, T0 lines were transplanted to soil and grown in a greenhouse, and seeds were collected. T1 transgenic plants used in feeding experiments were identified by PCR and absolute qRT-PCR. Genomic DNA was extracted from young leaves of T1 transgenic and control plants using the Plant Genomic DNA Kit (Tiangen) and subjected to PCR analysis. For absolute qRT-PCR, total RNA was extracted from the leaves of transgenic plants subjected to locust feeding using the RNAprep Pure Plant Kit (Tiangen). cDNA was reverse transcribed from $2 \ \mu \mathsf { g }$ of total RNA, and qRT-PCR was performed as described above. mAChR-C F2 was cloned into the pGEM-T vector, verified by sequencing, and used to construct the standard curve. The number of mRNA copies was calculated using the formula $6 . 0 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 3 }$ (copies/ mol) $\times$ plasmid concentration $( { \mathsf { g } } / { \mu \mathsf { L } } ) /$ /plasmid molecular weight $_ { ( 9 / \mathsf { m o l } ) }$ . The primers used in PCR and absolute qRT-PCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Small RNA library construction and mAChR-C siRNA screening

Total RNA from T1 transgenic maize plants was used to generate a small RNA library (BGI Genomics). Briefly, adapters were ligated to RNA after purification and separation into 18- to 30-nt fragments via PAGE electrophoresis. Following reverse transcription and PCR amplification, PCR products in the range of 110–130 bp were recovered by PAGE electrophoresis. The library was sequenced using the MGISEQ-2000 platform and filtered to remove adapter sequences, low-quality reads, and contaminants. The PHASIS software was used to predict siRNAs. BLAST was performed to identify mAChR-C specific siRNAs by $100 \%$ sequence match to mAChR-C F2. 

# Insect feeding and bioassay

T1 transgenic maize plants were grown in pots of soil and fed to N5 nymphs at the 9–12 leaf stage. From the day of molting until AE, the nymphs were fed twice daily with fresh leaves cut into 10-cm-long fragments. After feeding, nymphs were checked twice daily to record mortality and AE. To assess non-target effects, the locust-resistant T1 transgenic line (L11) was used for larval feeding experiments with other maize pests, including H. armigera, O. furnacalis, S. frugiperda, and M. separata. Feeding began at the antepenultimate instar (4th instar for S. frugiperda and M. separata; 3rd instar for H. armigera and O. furnacalis) and continued until pupation, a period comparable to the final (5th) instar of L. migratoria. Larvae were starved for $\mathsf { 1 0 ~ h }$ prior to feeding. Fresh leaves were cut into $2 \mathsf { c m } \times 6 \mathsf { c m }$ pieces and supplied daily. Larvae were checked daily to record mortality and pupation. WT maize was used as the control. Images of adults were captured with a Canon EOS 550D camera. 

# Data analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using Student’s t test or one-way analysis of variance with Tukey’s post hoc test using STATISTICA 12.0 software (StatSoft). The survival curve was analyzed using the Kaplan-Meier method, with significance determined by log-rank tests according to Le et al. (2024). Significance was defined as $p < 0 . 0 5$ . Values were reported as mean ± SE.
