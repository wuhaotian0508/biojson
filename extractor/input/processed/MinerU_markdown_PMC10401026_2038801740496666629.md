# Pinoresinol rescues developmental phenotypes of Arabidopsis phenylpropanoid mutants overexpressing FERULATE 5-HYDROXYLASE

Most phenylpropanoid pathway flux is directed toward the production of monolignols, but this pathway also generates multiple bioactive metabolites. The monolignols coniferyl and sinapyl alcohol polymerize to form guaiacyl (G) and syringyl (S) units in lignin, components that are characteristic of plant secondary cell walls. Lignin negatively impacts the saccharification potential of lignocellulosic biomass. Although manipulation of its content and composition through genetic engineering has reduced biomass recalcitrance, in some cases, these genetic manipulations lead to impaired growth. The reduced-growth phenotype is often attributed to poor water transport due to xylem collapse in low-lignin mutants, but alternative models suggest that it could be caused by the hyper- or hypoaccumulation of phenylpropanoid intermediates. In Arabidopsis thaliana, overexpression of FERULATE 5-HYDROXYLASE (F5H) shifts the normal G/S lignin ratio to nearly pure S lignin and does not result in substantial changes to plant growth. In contrast, when we overexpressed F5H in the low-lignin mutants cinnamyl dehydrogenase c and d (cadc cadd), cinnamoyl-CoA reductase 1, and reduced epidermal fluorescence 3, plant growth was severely compromised. In addition, cadc cadd plants overexpressing F5H exhibited defects in lateral root development. Exogenous coniferyl alcohol (CA) and its dimeric coupling product, pinoresinol, rescue these phenotypes. These data suggest that mutations in the phenylpropanoid pathway limit the biosynthesis of pinoresinol, and this effect is exacerbated by overexpression of F5H, which further draws down cellular pools of its precursor, CA. Overall, these genetic manipulations appear to restrict the synthesis of pinoresinol or a downstream metabolite that is necessary for plant growth.

The phenylpropanoid pathway gives rise to a variety of specialized metabolites, but most of its flux goes toward the production of monolignols, the hydroxycinnamyl alcohols, $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaryl, coniferyl, and sinapyl alcohols. These alcohols serve as the building blocks of lignin, a major component of plant secondary cell walls that imparts hydrophobicity and tensile strength to vascular tissues (1, 2). Lignin content and composition have a large impact on saccharification of plant biomass, thereby affecting the economics of paper and biofuel production (3). Substantial efforts have been made to address this issue by targeting genes in the phenylpropanoid pathway to decrease lignin content (4, 5). Taking advantage of the plasticity of monolignol incorporation, researchers have also manipulated the phenylpropanoid pathway to alter monolignol ratios or to incorporate novel monomers that result in less recalcitrant lignocellulosic biomass (4, 5). Unfortunately, many of these efforts have resulted in plants with compromised growth, a phenomenon known as “lignin-modification-induced dwarfism” (LMID) (6, 7). In Arabidopsis thaliana, LMID may include reduced rosette size and/or stem height and in severe cases failure to bolt (6, 7). LMID often occurs when the genes that encode the core enzymes in the phenylpropanoid pathway are down-regulated or knocked out, including PHENYLALANINE AMMONIA-LYASE (PAL), CINNAMATE 4-HYDROXYLASE (C4H), 4-COUMARATE: CoA LIGASE (4CL), HYDROXYCINNAMOYL-CoA: SHIKIMATE HYDROXYCINNAMOYL TRANSFERASE (HCT), $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -COUMAROYL SHIKIMATE $3 ^ { \prime }$ -HYDROXYLASE $( \mathrm { C } 3 ^ { \prime } \mathrm { H } )$ ), CAFFEOYL SHIKIMATE ESTERASE (CSE), or CINNAMOYL-CoA REDUCTASE (CCR) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1) (8–19). On the other hand, manipulation of genes that encode enzymes further downstream in the pathway such as CAFFEOYL-CoA $O$ -METHYLTRANSFERASE (CCoAOMT), FERULATE 5-HYDROXYLASE (F5H), CAFFEIC ACID O-METHYLTRANSFERASE (COMT), and CINNAMYL ALCOHOL DEHYDROGENASE (CAD) (SI Appendix, Fig. S1) mostly results in changes in lignin composition but generally does not affect plant growth (19–24). 

# Significance

Plants with reduced lignin content are desirable because their biomass is more amenable to biofuel production; however, the engineering of lignin content and composition often results in impaired growth for unknown reasons. This phenotype could be caused by altered water transport through the xylem of low-lignin plants, but other models suggest that nonstructural phenylpropanoid metabolites could be involved in this phenotype. Lignans are products of phenylpropanoid metabolism that are widely distributed in the plant kingdom, but their biological functions have been understudied. Here, we present evidence suggesting that reductions in the content of the lignan pinoresinol and its precursor, coniferyl alcohol, are involved in the impaired growth phenotypes of low-lignin plants and also lead to perturbations in root development.

Four main hypotheses have been proposed to explain the LMID phenomenon. LMID may be the result of a change in cell wall composition that leads to mechanical failure of the vascular tissues resulting in the reduced ability to transport water (1, 8), consistent with the collapsed xylem vessels seen in many low lignin mutants (13, 25, 26). Alternatively, defects in the cell wall may be detected by a cell wall integrity monitoring system that triggers a series of transcriptional changes that ultimately results in dwarfism (27). Evidence for such a system exists for the polysaccharide components of the cell wall (28–30). Knocking out specific components of the Mediator complex rescues the growth of a lignin-deficient mutant, which is consistent with the hypothesis that a surveillance system is capable of detecting lignin-induced cell wall defects (31). LMID has also been attributed to the hyper- or hypoaccumulation of phenylpropanoid metabolites, including flavonoids (32), cis-cinnamic acid (33), and salicylic acid (34, 35), when the pathway is blocked. To date, the cause or causes of LMID remain unclear, and multiple mechanisms may contribute simultaneously or when the pathway is blocked at different points. 

F5H catalyzes the hydroxylation of coniferaldehyde and coniferyl alcohol (CA) to 5-hydroxyconiferaldehyde and 5-hydroxy-CA, which are subsequently methylated by COMT to sinapaldehyde and sinapyl alcohol. Polymerization of CA and sinapyl alcohol gives rise to guaiacyl (G) and syringyl (S) lignin units, respectively. In A. thaliana, the lack of F5H activity in the ferulic acid hydroxylase 1 (fah1) mutant shifts the native 3:1 G/S lignin ratio to pure G. Conversely, overexpression of $F 5 H$ driven by the C4H promoter (C4H:F5H), a promoter that is efficient at targeting expression to vascular tissues, redirects the flux toward nearly pure S lignin synthesis (36–39). Neither of these modifications results in substantial alterations to growth in an otherwise wild-type background (36–39). In contrast, when $F 5 H$ is overexpressed in plants carrying mutations in CADC and CADD, the resulting cadc C4H:F5H and cadd C4H:F5H plants are diminished in stature and rosette size, and in the cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants, these phenotypes are further exacerbated (40). Triple-mutant fah1 cadc cadd plants are phenotypically normal by comparison (40). 

To further explore how LMID is caused by blocking steps in the phenylpropanoid pathway when F5H is concomitantly overexpressed, we studied cadc C4H:F5H, cadd C4H:F5H, and cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants as well as two mutants deficient in C4H (reduced epidermal fluorescence (ref) 3-2, ref3-3), and one in CCR1 (ccr1) (9, 13, 14), in which the expression of $F 5 H$ had been modulated. Knocking out F5H had no effect on the growth phenotype of ref3-2, ref3-3, and ccr1, whereas the overexpression of F5H resulted in plants with compromised growth in all cases. In addition to the cadc cadd C4H:F5H phenotypes previously reported (40), we identified a defect in lateral root and root hair development in these plants. All of these developmental phenotypes can be rescued by growing plants in the presence of CA or its dimeric derivative pinoresinol (PR). These data suggest that mutations in upstream steps in the pathway reduce the abundance of CA-derived metabolites, an effect that is exacerbated by overexpression of F5H, which draws on cellular pools of its substrates, coniferaldehyde, and CA. Together, these alterations appear to limit the synthesis of a specialized metabolite that is necessary for normal plant growth. 

# Results

Multiple Phenylpropanoid Pathway Mutants Exhibit Severe Growth Impairment When Simultaneously Overexpressing F5H. The reasons for LMID in cadc C4H:F5H, cadd C4H:F5H, and 

cadc cadd C4H:F5H remain enigmatic, especially because neither cadc cadd nor F5H overexpression individually has a substantial impact on plant growth (23, 36–38, 41). To gain insight into LMID in these genetic backgrounds, we determined whether growth impairment could be induced in other phenylpropanoid mutants by overexpression of F5H. To do so, we modulated the expression of $F 5 H$ in two mutants deficient in C4H (ref3-2 and ref3-3), the enzyme that catalyzes the hydroxylation of cinnamic acid to $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaric acid in the second step of the phenylpropanoid pathway. Plants carrying certain missense alleles in C4H have modest growth defects (13) (Fig.  1 and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S2), although null alleles are seedling-lethal. The lignin content of ref3-3 is not reduced, whereas the lignin of ref3-2 is reduced to $2 0 \%$ of wild type (13). Knocking out F5H had no effect on the growth phenotype of ref3-2 nor ref3-3, whereas the overexpression of F5H resulted in plants with decreased rosette size in both genotypes and in failure to develop a stem in the ref3-2 background (Fig. 1 and SI Appendix, Fig. S2). 

Next, we introduced the C4H:F5H transgene into ccr1 (Fig. 1A), a mutant which does not have severe growth defects and has wild-type lignin monomer composition (9, 14). Knocking out F5H had no effect on the growth phenotype of ccr1, whereas the overexpression of F5H resulted in plants with decreased rosette area and height (Fig. 1 and SI Appendix, Fig. S2). In addition to their impaired growth, cadc cadd C4H:F5H, ref3-3 C4H:F5H, and ccr1 C4H:F5H are sterile, although none of the parental backgrounds are. 

Exogenous Coniferyl Alcohol Rescues the Dwarf Phenotype of cadc cadd C4H:F5H. The results above indicate that F5H overexpression in a range of phenylpropanoid mutants leads to compromised growth, perhaps by reducing the accumulation of a compound important for plant development. In addition to redirecting flux from G- to S-monomers (36–38), F5H overexpression might be anticipated to further reduce CA pools that are already reduced due to the upstream pathway mutations, causing the dwarfism of ref3, ccr1, and cadc cadd carrying the C4H:F5H construct. To test this hypothesis, we planted seeds from cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H plants on media with and without CA. From among these progeny, we expected the cadd C4H:F5H plants to exhibit impaired growth (Fig. $1 C$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S2), with the cadc cadd C4H:F5H segregants displaying an even greater reduction in growth. Indeed, in contrast to the unimodal distribution of seedling area in wild type (Fig. 2A), the progeny from cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H plants exhibited a bimodal distribution with one-fourth of the plants being even smaller than the rest due to the segregation of the cadc allele (Fig. 2B). Although there seems to be a difference in emerged leaves numbers among cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H segregants, notably the difference in size is not due to distinct germination rates between genotypes. The growth of wild-type seedlings grown in CA was reduced in comparison to the mock-treated seedlings (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S3). In contrast, all progeny of cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H plants grew larger in the presence of CA than in its absence, with the impact on the smallest plants being the most substantial (Fig. 2B and SI Appendix, Table S1), indicating that CA has a growth-promoting effect on both cadd C4H:F5H and cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants. 

CA treatment has been reported to lead to the upregulation of the C4H transcriptional repressor MYB4 (42), which led us to test whether the expression of the C4H:F5H transgene was affected by CA treatment. Although there was a decrease in $F 5 H$ expression in cadd C4H:F5H seedlings grown in the presence of CA, endogenous C4H mRNA levels were unaffected, and F5H transcript levels were only modestly affected, still being 12 times higher than in wild type (SI Appendix, Table S2).

To test the specificity of the CA growth enhancement effect, we planted cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H progeny seed on media containing the other canonical monolignols, $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaryl alcohol or sinapyl alcohol, as well as a range of structurally related compounds (Fig. 2A and SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Coniferaldehyde, the direct precursor of CA, had only a weak positive effect on the growth of mutant plants and a negative impact on wild-type plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Although to a lesser extent than CA, $\boldsymbol { \mathscr { P } }$ -coumaryl alcohol could improve the growth of the smaller segregants, but it had no effect on the larger plants of the segregating population (Fig. 2A and SI Appendix, Table S1). Sinapyl alcohol could partially alleviate the dwarf phenotype of all cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H progeny, but the growth rescue was not to the extent of CA (Fig. 2A and SI Appendix, Table S1). Both monolignols had a mild negative impact on the growth of wild-type plants (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). Dihydroconiferyl alcohol (DCA), acetovanillone, and vanillin had a stronger inhibitory effect on the growth of mutant plants than on wild-type plants, whereas other compounds such as guaiacol and syringyl alcohol had a negative impact on plant growth irrespective of genotype (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Furthermore, CA partially rescued the growth impairment of ref3-2 C4H:F5H and ref8-2, a null mutant of C3′H (SI Appendix, Fig. S5). Taken together, these results suggest that growth impairments of at least some lignin-deficient plants can be attributed to a deficiency in CA or a downstream product derived from it. 

F5H-Overexpressing Plants Have a Lateral Root Emergence Phenotype That Can Be Rescued by Coniferyl Alcohol. In the course of the previous experiments, we noticed that cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants had a reduced number of lateral roots (LTRs) (Fig.  3A). This phenotype was observed in single cad mutants 

overexpressing F5H, although CAD mutants alone did not have defects in LTRs (Fig. 3A). Although neither F5H knockouts nor overexpressors exhibit a whole-plant growth phenotype, LTR development was affected in these plants as well; fah1-2 seedlings developed more and fah1-2 C4H:F5H developed fewer LTRs than wild type (Fig. 3A and SI Appendix, Fig. S6). The CCR1 mutants had fewer LTRs than wild type, and this phenotype was unaffected by changes in F5H expression. Although these genotypes differed in LTR numbers, they did not exhibit substantial changes in primary root length (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). We did not observe a decrease in LTR development in ref3-2 or ref3-3 overexpressing F5H (Fig.  3A). Exogenous cis-cinnamic acid induces LTR production, and all ref3 alleles accumulate cinnamic acid esters that are not otherwise present in wild type (13, 43). The negative effect of C4H:F5H on LTR development may therefore be masked by the positive effect cis-cinnamic acid has on LTR development in ref3-2 C4H:F5H and ref3-3 C4H:F5H. 

Auxin is a key regulator of lateral root formation and emergence (44). Because the hallmark responses of plants to exogenous auxin are the shortening of primary roots and an increase in lateral root production (45), we tested whether its application could restore the root defects in plants overexpressing F5H. For these experiments, we chose to work with cadd C4H:F5H because these plants have reduced numbers of lateral roots (Fig. 3A), but are fertile, which permitted the analysis of homozygous, nonsegregating, populations. As expected, transfer to media containing the synthetic auxin 1-naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA) resulted in primary root inhibition. Given the impact of CA supplementation on the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H shoots, we next tested whether the LTR phenotype of cadd C4H:F5H could also be rescued by supplementation with CA. Lateral root development is a multistage process that involves 

lateral root primordia initiation in the pericycle and their subsequent emergence through the cortex and epidermis (46, 47). Because defects in either of these processes lead to a reduction in emerged lateral roots (46, 47), we counted the number and location of emerged and unemerged lateral roots in wild-type and cadd C4H:F5H roots. As expected, emerged lateral roots in wild type were found in the older regions of the differentiation zone and unemerged primordia in the younger regions of this zone closer to the root tip (48) (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). In cadd C4H:F5H, the emerged lateral roots were present in the more mature region of the differentiation zone, although as mentioned earlier, in fewer numbers (SI Appendix, Fig. S8). In contrast, unemerged lateral root primordia in cadd C4H:F5H were found throughout the root and in fewer numbers than in wild type and did not account for the reduction in total number of lateral roots (Fig. $3 B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S8). Transfer of seedlings to media containing CA restored the number of emerged and unemerged roots to wild-type numbers (Fig. $3 B$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S9). These results indicate that cadd $C 4 H { : } F 5 H$ plants are impaired in both lateral root initiation and emergence, and this phenotype is dependent on CA or a substance derived from it. 

Overexpression of F5H Leads to Changes in Soluble Hydroxycinnamaldehydes and Hydroxycinnamyl Alcohols. In order to survey the effect F5H overexpression has on soluble metabolite pools, we quantified coniferaldehyde, sinapaldehyde, CA, and sinapyl alcohol in all of the mutants described earlier (Fig. 1 and SI Appendix, Fig. S1). As expected, based on their lignin composition (36), fah1-2 plants were devoid of sinapaldehyde and sinapyl alcohol, whereas C4H:F5H plants contained less CA and accumulated higher levels of sinapaldehyde and sinapyl alcohol compared to wild-type plants (Table 1). Unexpectedly, although lignin content is unaffected by F5H expression (37, 40), pools of hydroxycinnamaldehydes and hydroxycinnamyl alcohols in fah1-2 and $C 4 H { : } F 5 H$ greatly surpassed those of wild type. The low-lignin mutants ref3-2, ref3-3, and ccr1 contained lower levels of coniferyl and sinapyl alcohol. Manipulation of F5H expression in these mutants resulted in a similar shift in the ratios of hydroxycinnamaldehydes and hydroxycinnamyl alcohols as those seen in fah1-2 and C4H:F5H plants. As expected, cadc cadd plants exhibited an increase in coniferaldehyde and sinapaldehyde pools. Surprisingly, cadc cadd also exhibited an increase in CA content, despite lacking the two main alcohol dehydrogenases involved in monolignol biosynthesis. Stacking the fah1-2 mutation in a cadc cadd background led to the further hyperaccumulation of CA and coniferaldehyde and abolished the synthesis of sinapaldehyde. Overexpression of F5H in cadc, cadd, and cadc cadd led to an increase in sinapyl alcohol and sinapaldehyde content despite these plants lacking one or both of the CADs involved in sinapyl alcohol synthesis. Intriguingly, the total content of CA is not lower in cadc cadd C4H:F5H compared to wild type, but the growth of these plants can be rescued by exogenous CA. This finding is counterintuitive but may indicate that the mutant’s phenotypes arise from CA deficiency in a specific cell type(s).

cadc cadd C4H:F5H Synthesize Multiple Compounds When Supplied with Exogenous Coniferyl Alcohol. To identify CAderived compound(s) that may be responsible for growth restoration in cadc cadd C4H:F5H, we incubated whole seedlings for 24 or $^ { 4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in liquid media supplemented with $[ ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C _ { 6 } } ]$ or $[ ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C _ { 6 } } ]$ CA and compared the abundances of the labeled mass features identified by the PODIUM LC/MS pipeline with those found in a mock-treated control (49). The algorithm identifies metabolite features with common retention times that have a predetermined mass shift between the labeled and unlabeled samples, consistent with their synthesis from a particular labeled precursor. PODIUM identified 470 metabolite features derived from CA in cadc cadd C4H:F5H mutants (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). In this experiment, growth differences were visible $2 4 \mathrm { ~ \bar { h } ~ }$ after CA treatment, and by $4 8 \mathrm { { h } }$ , the majority of cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants incubated in media with CA had developed a new leaf, indicating that chemical complementation occurred under these experimental conditions. Pairwise analysis between the untreated and CA-treated samples identified 551 metabolite features that accumulated to higher levels in CA-fed samples at both timepoints, 187 of which are CA-derived (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). Isotopologue removal and further analysis by LC–MS/MS allowed us to collapse the 187 CA-derived features into no more than 104 unique compounds (Dataset  S1). Comparisons with in-house datasets and published data (50) provided putative identities for some of the metabolite features (Dataset  S1). The majority of metabolite features identified in the feeding experiment had $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ values consistent with their being glycosylated dimers and higher oligomers of CA. Furthermore, we identified labeled features corresponding to products derived from side-chain oxidation and subsequent metabolism of CA, such as ferulic and sinapic acid derivatives (Dataset S1). Overall, this experiment demonstrated that like Arabidopsis protoplasts (50), the mutant seedlings have the capacity for extensive biotransformation of CA into downstream products, one or more of which may have plant growth–promoting capabilities.

G(8–8)G Rescues the Growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H and Lateral Root Deficiency of cadd C4H:F5H. CA coupling gives rise to lignans and neolignans, specialized metabolites of largely unknown function that are found in most plant species (51). Lignans are made from two CA units linked by an 8–8 bond; whereas, neolignans are linked by $_ { 8 - O - 4 }$ or 5–5 bonds (52). The identification of multiple CA oligomers by LC/MS prompted us to test whether any CA-derived compounds could rescue the growth phenotype of cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants. To test this, we fed seedlings with $\mathrm { G } ( 8 - O { - } 4 ) \mathrm { G }$ , G(8–5)G, G(8–8) G (nomenclature established by Morreel et  al. 2004)  (53) in which G represents guaiacyl moieties). We also tested DCG (Fig.  4B), a neolignan glucoside reported to have cytokininlike properties (54–56), although this idea was recently refuted (57). At $2 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , one-fourth the concentration used in previous complementation experiments, neither CA nor the neolignans affected the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H seedlings (Fig. 4A and SI Appendix, Table S4). In contrast, the lignan G(8)G, also known as pinoresinol (PR), not only rescued the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H seedlings but improved the growth of plants of all genotypes in the segregating population (Fig.  4A and SI Appendix, Table S3). Furthermore, PR had no effect on the expression of the C4H:F5H transgene (SI Appendix, Table S2). Both $\left( + \right)$ and (−) enantiomers had similar effects on growth (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). 

We then assessed whether $\mathrm { G } ( 8 - O { - } 4 ) \mathrm { G }$ , G(8–5)G, the $\left( + \right)$ enantiomer of PR, or DCG had an effect on lateral root development. As seen in Fig. 4B and SI Appendix, Fig. S14, $\left( + \right)$ -PR complemented the LTR phenotype of cadd C4H:F5H and could do so at $6 . 2 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , a concentration at which CA had no effect. In a subsequent experiment, we found that like their effect on shoot growth, both PR stereoisomers were equally active (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). Subsequent experiments were conducted with $\left( + \right)$ -PR. Exogenous $\left( + \right)$ -PR had no effect on lateral root development or seedling growth in wild type (SI Appendix, Fig. S13). 

Methylated Pinoresinol Rescues LTR Development, but Not the Shoot Growth of cadd C4H:F5H. Because PR is a precursor for other lignans (51), we tested whether lariciresinol and secoisolariciresinol, lignans downstream of PR, could rescue the mutant phenotypes of cadc cadd C4H:F5H. Although these lignans had a positive effect on the mutant’s growth, neither lignan could rescue seedling growth to the same extent as PR (Fig. 5A and SI Appendix, Table S4). We also tested the effect of these lignans on LTR development and found that lariciresinol and secoisolariciresinol could not promote LTR development in cadd C4H:F5H (Fig. 5C and SI Appendix, Fig. S14). 

The type of linkages found in lignans and neolignans can also be found in native lignins, and PR is a dimer that can function as a starter unit upon which lignin polymers can be elaborated. For this reason, we wanted to determine whether exogenous PR rescues the growth and LTR phenotypes of the mutants via incorporation into lignin. To do so, we grew plants in the presence of monomethylated pinoresinol (MMPR) or dimethylated pinoresinol (DMPR). DMPR cannot be incorporated into lignin because the phenolic hydroxyl groups necessary for radical coupling are protected. Although MMPR had a mild positive effect on the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants, DMPR had a negative effect (Fig. 5A and SI Appendix, Table S4); both methylated forms enhanced LTR development in cadd C4H:F5H, with DMPR being more potent than PR itself (Fig. 5C and SI Appendix, Fig. S14). 

cadd C4H:F5H Has Defective Root Hairs That Can Be Rescued by Coniferyl Alcohol. In the course of observing lateral roots by light microscopy, we noticed that many of the root hairs in the cadd C4H:F5H line were branched (SI Appendix, Fig. S15). In wild type, root hairs are tubular outgrowths arising from single epidermal cells that rarely branch (58) (Fig. 6A). Analysis by cryo-SEM showed that fah1-2 C4H:F5H roots have regions in which root hairs are branched, and these regions are more extensive in cadd C4H:F5H roots, whereas cadd root hairs have wild-type morphology (Fig. 6A). The defective root hair phenotype in cadd C4H:F5H could also be rescued with exogenous CA (Fig. 6B).

Several studies have revealed that vascular collapse and LMID can be disentangled (31, 68), and others have suggested that hyperaccumulation of phenylpropanoid metabolites can be the cause of impaired plant growth (69, 70). Recently, the compromised rosette and stem size of a lignin-deficient mutant with collapsed xylem was rescued by vessel-specific complementation (6), but this experiment does not rule out the possibility that a growth-promoting phenylpropanoid metabolite is synthesized in vascular tissues or other cells in which phenylpropanoid promoters are active. Investigations into the role of soluble phenylpropanoids as growth regulators have been impeded by the fact that their synthesis shares a common biosynthetic pathway with lignin; genetic manipulations that alter their content also affect lignin levels (71). Although detrimental effects on plant growth arising from genetic manipulations of the phenylpropanoid pathway may often be the result of alterations in lignin content and vascular collapse, the common origin of lignin and soluble phenylpropanoids may have obscured the possible role of these metabolites as plant growth regulators. 

Coniferyl Alcohol and Its Dehydrodimer Pinoresinol Rescue the Growth Phenotype of cadc cadd C4H:F5H. Plants overexpressing F5H deposit primarily S lignin and exhibit only a mild negativegrowth phenotype (36–38, 41). The compromised growth of ref3-2, ccr1, and cadc cadd plants overexpressing F5H does not appear to be due to the synergistic effect of a decrease in lignin content and a change in composition because the lignin content of these mutant backgrounds (13, 40, 72) does not correlate with the severity of their growth phenotypes observed upon introduction of C4H:F5H. Furthermore, the addition of C4H:F5H to a ref3-3 background results in dwarfism (Fig.  1), although the ref3-3 mutant does not have altered lignin content (13). Together, these data are inconsistent with a lignin-based phenotype, although we cannot exclude the possibility that the phenotypes of F5H overexpressors are due to distinct causes, such as the low lignin content of ref3-2 and the aldehyde-rich lignin of cadc cadd. Taken together, these data suggest that the growth impairment in cadc cadd, ccr1, ref3-2, and ref3-3 plants overexpressing F5H is due to alterations in phenylpropanoids other than lignin. 

Supplementation with CA or PR restored the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H (Fig. 2B), ref8-2, and ref3-2 C4H:F5H (SI Appendix, Fig. S5) plants. Exogenous CA was recently reported to lead to the upregulation of MYB4, (42) which would be expected to repress expression of our C4H promoter-driven F5H overexpression construct (73). Instead, under our experimental conditions, transcript levels of the endogenous C4H were unaffected by CA treatment in cadd C4H:F5H seedlings, and the expression of the $F 5 H$ transgene was only modestly affected and remained 12-fold higher than in wild type (SI Appendix, Table S2). Similar levels of $F 5 H$ overexpression are sufficient to effectively elevate S-lignin composition (36). This observation and the fact that PR does not affect C4H:F5H transcript accumulation show that phenotypic complementation comes about through a mechanism distinct from a decrease in $F 5 H$ overexpression. 

The decrease in CA and coniferin in ref3-2, ref3-3, and ccr1 C4H:F5H is consistent with a model in which upstream mutations reduce CA content, and high levels of F5H activity caused by the C4H:F5H construct further deplete the CA pool (Table 1). Despite growing relatively normally, the transcriptional profiles of cadc cadd and fah1-2 C4H:F5H plants are substantially altered and share many of the same misregulated groups of genes, suggesting that these plants’ altered transcript levels could be caused by a common factor, possibly the depletion of CA (74). Surprisingly, the levels of CA in cadd C4H:F5H and cadc cadd C4H:F5H are instead elevated, an observation that is at odds with this model. The fact that CA complements the growth phenotype of cadc cadd C4H:F5H suggests that the increase of endogenous CA levels in these plants does not occur in cells in which it is needed for growth. The synthesis of CA in the absence of CADC and CADD may be catalyzed by one or more of the additional seven CADs that the Arabidopsis genome encodes. Substantial CAD enzymatic activity can be detected in cadc cadd stem extracts (75). This activity clearly does not compensate for the lack of CADC and CADD given the lignin phenotype of the cadc cadd mutant, pointing to the importance of tissue-specific localization of phenylpropanoid enzymes and metabolites. Finally, it is notable that CA rescues the dwarf phenotype of cadc cadd C4H:F5H plants, although plants are overexpressing F5H, which uses CA as a substrate. This observation 

suggests that the concentrations of CA in vivo that rescue these growth phenotypes might be substantially lower than what was applied exogenously and may also explain why PR rescues growth at lower concentrations than CA does. 

Lignans and neolignans are widely distributed among the plant kingdom (51), but their functions in planta have been understudied. These compounds have been associated with plant defense for their antifeedant, allelopathic, and antifungal properties (76), and recently, the role of a sinapoylcholine-conjugated neolignan in seed coat protection has been demonstrated (77). Reports on coniferyl alcohol-derived molecules affecting plant growth date back to the 1970s (78); however, there are only isolated reports on this topic, and they provide limited data to support the role of these molecules in plant growth. In our studies, DCG, its aglycone, and DCA did not have a positive effect on the growth of cadc cadd C4H:F5H seedlings (Fig. 4A and SI Appendix, Fig. S4). Although PR and other CA dimers can be incorporated into lignin, only the 8–8 dimer is able to rescue the growth of these plants, whereas 8–O–4 or 8–5 dimers cannot, although the same linkages are found in native lignin and should be able to supplement lignification as readily as PR. Taken together, our results suggest a role for PR or its derivatives in plant growth regulation. 

Lignans Restore Lateral Root Development in cadd C4H:F5H. The importance of phenylpropanoids in root development has been explored in recent studies. The C4H null mutant, ref3-4, has a deficiency in LTR development and an increase in adventitious roots which was attributed to the inhibition of auxin transport by both cis-cinnamic acid accumulation and impaired phloemmediated transport caused by lignin deficiency (79). Addition of coniferaldehyde complements the poor water transport of ref3-4 seedlings and alleviates its adventitious root phenotype presumably by complementing the mutant’s low-lignin phenotype (79). Kaempferol negatively regulates lateral root development by scavenging ROS in lateral root primordia where it is required for emergence. As a result, the tt7 mutant, defective in FLAVONOID ${ \mathcal { B } } ^ { \prime }$ -HYDROXYLASE, has increased levels of kaempferol and a decrease in emerged LTRs, but an increase in unemerged LTRs (80). Based on these observations, our previous definitions of hormones and secondary metabolites may have to be revisited given the importance of secondary metabolites in fine tuning growth and developmental processes. 

In addition to their growth defect, cadc, cadd, and cadc cadd seedlings carrying the C4H:F5H construct have fewer LTRs, and this phenotype can also be rescued by chemical complementation with CA and its derivative, PR. In addition to the vasculature, roots have an additional lignin-made structure, the Casparian strip (CS), which plays a role in controlling the diffusion of water and solutes through the endodermis (81). Mutants in C4H and MYB36 are defective in CS deposition (82, 83), and incorporate increased levels of suberin in the endodermis, the degradation of which is necessary for LTR emergence (84). CS defects in these mutants can be complemented by exogenous CA (83, 85), which would appear to suggest that defects in CS deposition and an increase in suberization could explain the decrease in LTRs in cadd C4H:F5H and cadc cadd C4H:F5H and their rescue by CA and PR. Nevertheless, the observation that the LTR defect in these plants can be rescued with MMPR and DMPR demonstrates that this is not the case. We selected these compounds because the methyl etherification of the phenol prevents incorporation into lignin but might not disrupt their putative activity as signaling molecules. MMPR and DMPR were more potent than PR at rescuing the LTR phenotype of cadd C4H:F5H, and DMPR was more potent than MMPR, suggesting 

that rescue of LTR development does not come about through catabolism of these compounds to PR. Altogether, these data suggest that the LTR defect of cadd C4H:F5H plants is due to a deficiency in lignan biosynthesis. 

The Aberrant Root Hair Morphology of cadd C4H:F5H Can Be Rescued by Coniferyl Alcohol. Root hair development is a process that requires rapid cell expansion, and it is controlled by multiple factors, including reactive oxygen species (ROS), auxin, ethylene, calcium gradients, actin, and microtubule cytoskeletons. Mutations that affect these processes lead to changes in the quantity and morphology of root hairs and in some cases lead to branched hairs (58). The importance of phenylpropanoids in root hair development has been demonstrated in flavonol-deficient mutants of tomato and Arabidopsis which have increased ROS levels and elevated numbers of root hairs (86, 87). Trichoblasts are nonlignifying epidermal cells that develop into root hairs, and the fact that their morphology can be altered by the presence or absence of CA further suggests it, or a derivative thereof, may have a signaling role in plant development. 

# Materials and Methods

Plant Materials. A. thaliana, ecotype Col-0 plants were grown in Propagation Mix (Sun Gro Horticulture) supplemented with Osmocote® Plus (ICL Specialty Fertilizers) at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ under long-day conditions (16 h light/ 8 h dark). The ref3-2, ref3-3, pal1 pal2, fah1-2 C4H:F5H, and cadc cadd fah1-2 C4H:F5H plants have been described previously (13, 14, 36, 40). 

The ccr1 T-DNA line was obtained from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (The Ohio State University, Columbus, OH). The ref3-2, ref3-3, pal1 pal2, and ccr1 plants were crossed to fah1-2 C4H:F5H to generate ref3-2 fah1-2, ref3-2 fah1-2 C4H:F5H, ref3-3 fah1-2, ref3-3 fah1-2 C4H:F5H, pal1 pal2 fah1-2, pal1 pal2 fah1-2 C4H:F5H, ccr1 fah1-2, and ccr1 fah1-2 C4H:F5H. All mutant combinations carrying the C4H:F5H transgene were generated in a fah1-2 background to avoid cosuppression but hereafter will be referred to as C4H:F5H for the sake of simplicity. Homozygous mutants were identified by PCR using primers described in SI Appendix, Table S5. 

Chemical Complementation. Seeds were surface-sterilized and sown on plates containing Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium, 1% (w/v) sucrose, and 0.7% (w/v) agar and stratified for 2 d (88). For shoot and root complementation experiments (Fig. 3 and SI Appendix, Fig. S2), media were supplemented with 6.25 to $2 0 0 \mu \mu$ of each phenylpropanoid dissolved in DMSO or DMSO alone [0.02 to $0 . 0 7 \% ( \mathsf { v } / \mathsf { v } ) ]$ . Seedling area was determined from photographs of 10 to 11-d-old seedlings, and lateral roots were quantified at 9 d of age. Seedling area and root length were measured using Fiji (89). 

For auxin experiments, seeds were sown on minimal medium (90), containing $0 . 5 \%$ (w/v) sucrose, and 0.6% (w/v) agar for 4 d. Seedlings were then transferred to media containing either 80 nM 1-naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA) or $0 . 0 0 8 \%$ ethanol (v/v) for 4 d, after which lateral roots were quantified and root length was determined from photographs analyzed using Fiji (88).

Statistical Analysis. The segregation of the cadc recessive allele in the progeny from cadc/+ cadd C4H:F5H plants results in a bimodal distribution of seedling area. In this distribution, one peak represents the seedling area of cadc homozygous seedlings (group 1), where the other peak represents plants that are heterozygous or wild type for the cadc allele (group 2). Assuming both groups come from normal distributions, then the combined samples are a mixed normal distribution. To determine the mean and SD for each group, we used the Newton-type nonlinear minimization function (nlm) from the R package to maximize the likelihood function for the mixed normal distribution. To initialize the function, we determined the SD for each group of the DMSO-treated plants and applied it to all the treatments. In addition, we estimated the mean of each group based on the histogram for each treatment and tested multiple values that approximated the apparent mean to initialize the function. We used Z-tests and the estimated mean and SD to compare treatments. All other statistical analysis were performed using R (version 4.2.0). The statistical analysis performed is indicated in the figure legends.
