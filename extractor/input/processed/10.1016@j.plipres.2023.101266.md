# ABSTRACT

Phosphatidylglycerol (PG) is a unique phospholipid class with its indispensable role in photosynthesis and growth in land plants, algae, and cyanobacteria. PG is the only major phospholipid in the thylakoid membrane of cyanobacteria and plant chloroplasts and a main lipid component in photosynthetic protein-cofactor complexes such as photosystem I and photosystem II. In plants and algae, PG is also essential as a substrate for the biosynthesis of cardiolipin, which is a unique lipid present only in mitochondrial membranes and crucial for the functions of mitochondria. PG biosynthesis pathways in plants include three membranous organelles, plastids, mitochondria, and the endoplasmic reticulum in a complex manner. While the molecular biology underlying the role of PG in photosynthetic functions is well established, many enzymes responsible for the PG biosynthesis are only recently cloned and functionally characterized in the model plant species including Arabidopsis thaliana and Chlamydomonas reinhardtii and cyanobacteria such as Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803. The characterization of those enzymes helps understand not only the metabolic flow for PG production but also the crosstalk of biosynthesis pathways between PG and other lipids. This review aims to summarize recent advances in the understanding of the PG biosynthesis pathway and functions of involved enzymes.

# 1. Introduction

Phosphatidylglycerol (PG), 1, 2-diacyl-sn-glycero-3-phospho- $1^{\prime}$ -sn-glycerol, is an anionic phospholipid containing a glycerol molecule bound to the phosphate group in the polar headgroup (Fig. 1). Since the first identification of PG in Scenedesmus by Benson and Maruo (1958) [1], PG has been found in many organisms across the three domains of life [2-4]. PG is a ubiquitous phospholipid and is a major membrane lipid in many bacteria [5]. Particularly, in cyanobacteria, PG is the only phospholipid in the thylakoid membrane, the site of photochemical and electron transport reactions, as well as the plasma membrane [6].

Similarly, substantial amounts of PG are found in the thylakoid membrane of plant chloroplasts [7]. Of note, more than $30\%$ of total PG molecules in the thylakoid membranes of cyanobacteria and plant chloroplasts are estimated to be allocated to photosystem I (PSI) and photosystem II (PSII), the large protein-pigment complexes responsible for photosynthetic light reactions [8,9]. In these photosystems, many PG molecules are integrated in distinct sites as structural components, suggesting specific roles of these lipid molecules in the structure and function of both photosystems. PG is important not only as a membrane component but also as a precursor of cardiolipin (CL), 1,3-bis $(sn-3^{\prime}$ -phosphatidyl)-sn-glycerol. Many bacteria synthesize CL from PG as a crucial membrane component, whereas no CL biosynthesis has been reported in cyanobacteria [5]. Although PG is a minor membrane component in animal cells, CL synthesized from PG is a major phospholipid class in the mitochondrial membranes and important for the functions of mitochondria [15,16]. Similarly, PG is required for CL synthesis and the functions of mitochondria in plants [17-21] (Fig. 2). Plant cells have three organelles to synthesize PG, namely plastids, mitochondria, and the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), and PG is further converted to CL in mitochondria, so the biosynthesis pathways and involved enzymes are complicated compared to those in cyanobacteria and even animal cells (Fig. 3). # 2. Biosynthesis of PG and CL

# 2.2.CDS

CDS transfers a cytidine $5^{\prime}$ -monophosphate (CMP) from a cytidine $5^{\prime}$ -triphosphate (CTP) to a phosphate group of PA to synthesize CDP-DAG (Fig. 1). CDP-DAG is a key intermediate for the biosynthesis of various phospholipid classes such as PG and phosphatidylinositol (PI) [22,23]. CDS is a membrane protein with 6-9 transmembrane domains and its catalytic domain is in the C-terminus region of the protein [22]. The crystal structure of bacterial CDS from Thermotoga maritima shows that the CDS forms a homodimer with 9 transmembrane helices in each monomer, and two Asp219 and Asp249 coordinate $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}/\mathrm{K}^{+}$ as the cofactors [24]. The topology of the hydrophobic domain at the C-terminus of the A. thaliana CDS4 and CDS5, which are homologs of bacterial CDS, is confirmed as an embedded-loop but not transmembrane [25]. Disruption of the genes for CDS results in lethality in Escherichia coli [26], but in cyanobacteria such as Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 [27,28] and Synechococcus elongatus PCC 7942 [29], supplemented PG

CDS in $E$ coli is also involved in the biosynthesis of membrane protein integrase, that is a glycolipid composed of DAG and a glycan chain of three acetylated aminosugars and essential for the membrane protein integration [30,31]. $E$ coli has a paralog of CDS, YnbB, that can produce CDP-DAG from PA and CTP in vitro [32]; however, the relevance of YnbB in phospholipid biosynthesis in vivo has not been clarified. Further functions of CDS in bacteria and mammals are reviewed in Blunsom and Cockcroft (2020) [22] and Jennings and Epend (2020) [23].

In A. thaliana, three different eukaryotic (CDS1, CDS2, and CDS3), prokaryotic (CDS4 and CDS5), and Tam41-type (CDS6) CDSs are encoded in the nuclear genome. Fluorescent protein-tagged A. thaliana CDS4 and CDS5 expressed in tobacco BY2 cells are found in plastids [33], suggesting that CDS4 and CDS5 are localized in plastids (Fig. 3). A cds5 mutant of rice (Oryza sativa) over-accumulates PA and the signaling via PA is disturbed in the mutant [34]. PA-mediated signaling in plants is reviewed by Wang et al. (2006) [35]. Tam41 is a mitochondrial CDS that

is first found in yeasts [36]. While most of CDSs are membrane integral proteins, Tam41 is a peripheral protein at the mitochondrial inner membrane [37]. A. thaliana CDS6 encoded in At3g47630 is a homolog of Tam41. Knockout of CDS6 in A. thaliana impairs the biosynthesis of PG and CL in mitochondria, which causes a severe growth defect, finally leading to plant death [21]. CDS1, CDS2, and CDS3 are eukaryotic-type CDSs, and their localization in ER was confirmed by observing fluorescent proteins fused to each CDS in tobacco leaf epidermal cells [38]. CDS1 and CDS2 are constitutively expressed, while CDS3 expression is substantially low in leaves [38]. Lee et al. (2015) reported that the mRNA level of CDS3 is regulated by microRNA844, and the CDS3 mRNA level increases upon pathogen challenge with decreased microRNA844 [39]. Because loss of function of CDS3 increased the susceptibility to pathogens, this gene would play an important role in a defensive response to pathogens [39]. Recently, Sha et al. (2023) reported that ER-localized CDS is associated with the disease resistance in rice [40]. In rice, PI 4,5-bisphosphate $\left[\mathrm{PI}(4,5)\mathrm{P}_2\right]$ is enriched in an infection-specific structure, which is associated with effector secretion and fungal infection, and functions as a disease-susceptibility factor. Loss of function of the ER-localized CDS decreases the level of PI and $\mathrm{PI}(4,5)\mathrm{P}_2$ and confers multi-pathogen resistance in rice.

# 2.3. PGPS

PGPS exchanges a glycerol 3-phosphate (G3P) with a CMP in CDP-DAG to produce phosphatidylglycerol phosphate (PGP), which is a precursor for PG (Fig. 1). PGPS is a membrane protein with 4-6 transmembrane domains and the crystal structure of a PGPS dimer from Staphylococcus aureus revealed that the hydrophilic group of CDP-DAG induces the conformational change of PGPS [41].

PGPS is encoded in a single $pgsA$ gene in $E.$ coli [42] and cyanobacteria [27,43] (Fig. 2). Mutations in PGPS as well as CDS in pathogenic bacteria, such as Enterococcus faecalis, Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus mitis, and Corynebacterium striatum, reduce contents of PG and lysyl-PG and are associated with the tolerance to daptomycin, an antibiotic used for killing antibiotic-resistant bacteria and other gram-positive pathogenic bacteria [44-47]. A cyanobacterial mutant of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803, $pgsA$ , is defective in the biosynthesis of PGP, resulting in the lack of PG and loss of cell viability [27,43]. However, the $pgsA$ mutant can grow in PG-containing media by taking up the external PG, which allows for investigating the role of PG in cyanobacteria [43,48,49]. When the $pgsA$ mutant cells were transferred to PG-lacking medium, PG content in the cells gradually decreased. Along with the decrease in PG content, growth of the mutant cells also decreased and finally stopped [43]. PGPS is encoded in two different genes, $PGP1$ and $PGP2$ , in A. thaliana [51]. The dual-localization of PGP1 protein and enzymatic activity in both chloroplasts and mitochondria were determined in tobacco leaf protoplasts expressing GFP-fused PGP1 proteins [52] (Fig. 3). Expression of RFP-tagged A. thaliana PGP2 in onion cells indicated that PGP2 is localized to ER [20]. Several A. thaliana $pgp1$ and $pgp2$ mutants, $pgp1-1$ , $pgp1-2$ , $pgp1-3$ , $pgp1-4$ and $pgp2$ , have been isolated by different research groups. The $pgp1-1$ mutant with a point mutation that changes proline at 170 to serine, has an $80\%$ reduction in PGP1 activity and $30-40\%$ less PG than wild type and shows pale green phenotype and reduced photosynthetic activity [53]. The $pgp1-2$ mutant (KG10062), which has an insertion of T-DNA at the 43-bp upstream of the initiation ATG codon with a 54-bp deletion of first exon, contains about $80-90\%$ less PG than that in wild type and shows more severe growth defect and inhibition of photosynthesis than $pgp1-1$ [54]. The $pgp1-3$ mutant (KG12911) with an insertion of T-DNA at the 104-bp downstream of the initiation ATG codon shows similar phenotypes with those of $pgp1-2$ [54]. The $pgp1-4$ mutant (SL12-A2) is also a T-DNA inserted line with a reduced PG content ( $15\%$ of wild type), and phenotypes of $pgp1-4$ are limited to chloroplasts but not to mitochondria [52]. The $pgp2$ mutant contains a 178-bp deletion between the first intron and the second exon of the $PGP2$ gene, resulting in a deletion of 19 amino acid residues in the PGP2 protein [20]. The deleted sequence includes three amino acid residues that are conserved among PGPS proteins, suggesting that the protein encoded by $pgp2$ lacks the PGPS activity. In fact, additional mutation of $pgp2$ in the $pgp1-2$ mutant completely eliminated the activity to synthesize PG [20]. These data show that both PGP1 and PGP2 are functional PGPS and no other PGPSs operate in A. thaliana.

C. reinhardtii have also two isoforms (CrPGP1 and CrPGP2) of PGPS [55]. Heterologous expression of CrPGP1 or CrPGP2 in the $pgsA$ mutant of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 rescued the PG-dependent growth defect of the mutant, suggesting that both isoforms function as PGPS. In silico prediction suggests that CrPGP1 may be targeted to chloroplasts and/or mitochondria, whereas CrPGP2 may be localized somewhere other than the chloroplasts. Moreover, CrPGP1 and CrPGP2 showed different gene expression patterns under nitrogen-deficient conditions [55]. These data imply that CrPGP1 and CrPGP2 have different functions in C. reinhardtii cells.

# 2.4. PGPP

PGPP dephosphorylates PGP to produce PG, catalyzing the last reaction step of PG biosynthesis (Fig. 1). An earlier work in Anabaena sp. PCC 7120 demonstrated that disruption of alr1715 reduced PG content, chlorophyll (Chl) content, and photosynthetic activities [56]. Since

alr1715 is homologous with lipid phosphate phosphatase gene family, this gene was suggested to encode a functional PGPP (Fig. 2). However, the homologs in Synechosystis sp. PCC 6803 and A. thaliana encode PA phosphatase (PAP) [57-59]. Later, in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, a haloacid dehalogenase (HAD)-like phosphatase Gep4P was found to be a functional PGPP [60] and its homolog in C. reinhardtii, CrPGPP, was also shown to be a PGPP, the first PGPP cloned in photosynthetic eukaryotes [61]. Heterologous expression of CrPGPP in yeast $\Delta$ gep4 complemented growth and lipid phenotypes. Unlike yeast PGPP (Gep4p), in which two aspartic acid residues of the putative catalytic motif are essential for the enzyme activity, CrPGPP requires only the first but not the second aspartic acid residue for the complementation of $\Delta$ gep4 [61]. The A. thaliana PGPP, PGPP1, was found based on the homology search with CrPGPP, which also belongs to the HAD-like phosphatase superfamily. A. thaliana PGPP1 complemented $\Delta$ gep4 for the growth phenotype and also PG-producing activity when heterologously expressed [62,63], and in vitro enzyme activity assay using recombinant PGPP1 protein showed alkaline pH optimum and requirement of $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ for the activity [63]. At tissue level, PGPP1 was localized mainly at the hypocotyl, vasculatures, trichomes, guard cells, and stigmas. The subcellular localization assay showed predominant chloroplast localization of this enzyme in leaves [62,63] and mitochondrial localization in roots [64] (Fig. 3). The A. thaliana mutants of PGPP1 showed pale green phenotype with reduced Chl contents, defective development of chloroplasts, and impaired photosynthetic activity [62]. The mutants showed reduced PG content [62,63]. Of note, in-depth lipidomic analysis of PGP molecular species indicated PGP-34:3 (the numbers before and after colon represent total numbers of carbons and double bonds of fatty acids bound to PGP, respectively) and PGP-34:2 and PGP-34:1 lacking 16:1(Δ3t) (trans-Δ3-hexadecenoic acid, the number in parenthesis is the position of double bond counted from the carboxyl terminus) was accumulated, which suggests that desaturation of 16:0 to 16:1(Δ3t) by the specific enzyme (FAD4) occurs only after the dephosphorylation of PGP [63]. Besides PGPP1, a distinct type of PGPP was reported in A. thaliana. In mammals, PROTEIN TYROSINE PHOSPHATASE LOCALIZED TO THE MITOCHONDRION 1 (PTPMT1) is responsible for the PGPP activity localized to the mitochondria and involved in CL biosynthesis [65]. A homologous enzyme was also found in Rhodopirellula baltica [66]. A. thaliana has two copies of PTPMT homologs, named PTPMT1 and PTPMT2, which complemented the growth and lipid phenotypes of $\Delta$ gep4 [64]. In silico prediction of subcellular localization suggests that the A. thaliana PTPMT1 and PTPMT2 are unlikely to be targeted to a particular organelle but rather remain in cytoplasm, although the exact localizations of these proteins remain elusive (Fig. 3). Neither the double mutant ptpmt1-1 ptpmt2-1 nor the parental single mutants showed any morphological phenotype; however, when crossed with ppp1-1 mutant,

the triple mutant further reduced the root length and PG content without affecting the photosynthetic growth [64]. Thus, PTPMT1 and PTPMT2 may be responsible for PG biosynthesis in root, playing a redundant role with PGPP1 in root growth. It is yet unknown which enzyme is responsible for the residual chloroplastic PG biosynthesis in pgpp1-1 mutant. While no more homolog of PTPMT is found in A. thaliana, HAD-like phosphatase superfamily has a number of uncharacterized proteins. However, the challenge is that this family of phosphatase has a broad substrate specificity, such as PAH1 and PAH2 for PAP [67] and PECP1 and PS2 for phosphocholine phosphatase [68], making it hard to predict the substrate specificity of the uncharacterized proteins for possible PGPP isozymes.

# 2.5. CLS

CLS is the enzyme responsible for the last step of CL biosynthesis. CLS in eukaryotes, which belongs to the CDP-alcohol phosphatidyltransferase superfamily, transfers a phosphatidyl group from CDP-DAG to PG to yield CL (Fig. 1), whereas that in bacteria belongs to the phospholipase D superfamily and synthesizes CL by a condensation reaction between two PG molecules with the release of DAG [69]. The gene encoding prokaryotic CLS was first identified in $E$ coli by complementation of the temperature-sensitive $E$ coli mutant lacking CLS and phosphatidylserine [70]. In eukaryotes, CL biosynthesis takes place in the inner mitochondrial membrane. A $50\mathrm{kDa}$ protein with CLS activity was purified to near-homogeneity from mitochondrial membranes of rat liver [71]. The purified protein has a low isoelectric point (pH 4-5) and an alkaline pH optimum (pH 8-9), requires $\mathrm{Co}^{2+}$ for the catalysis, and is activated by PE and CL. In 1997 and 1998, three groups independently reported that disruption of an open reading frame (YDL142c) encoding a $32\mathrm{kDa}$ protein, which has the CDP-DAG binding domain and is homologous to PGPS genes but not to bacterial CLS genes, eliminated CLS activity in yeast, identifying this gene as the CLS gene [72-74]. The determination of the CLS gene in yeast allowed for the identification of CLS genes in humans and mice [75,76].

As in yeast and mammals, CLS in plants is predominantly associated with the inner mitochondrial membrane and uses CDP-DAG and PG for CL biosynthesis [77] (Fig. 3). Katayama et al. (2004) searched protein sequences containing the CDP-OH-P motif, which is a feature of enzymes requiring CDP-DAG such as PGPS and eukaryotic CLS, and identified a protein group, which included yeast CLS and an A. thaliana protein encoded in At4g04870 [78]. Expression of the At4g04870 gene in the E. coli mutant lacking CLS activity recovered CL biosynthesis in the mutant, confirming that this gene encodes CLS in A. thaliana. Examination of enzymatic characteristics and subcellular localization of A. thaliana CLS revealed that, as do other eukaryotic CLSs, this protein utilizes CDP-DAG and PG as substrates and is targeted to mitochondria in plant cells. Around the same time, Nowicki et al. (2005) identified the same A. thaliana gene to encode CLS based on the amino acid sequence characterized as the mitochondrial PGPS [79]. Their study revealed that this enzyme requires $\mathrm{Mn}^{2+}$ and CL-enriched phospholipid environments for its activity and prefers dioleoyl-PG and CDP-dioleoylglycerol as substrates than those carrying palmitoyl tails. A CLS gene (CrCLS1) was also cloned from C. reinhardtii based on the homology to A. thaliana CLS [80]. CrCLS1 complemented the CLS-deficient mutant of S. cerevisiae in terms of growth phenotype and CL biosynthesis activity, suggesting that CrCLS1 is the authentic CLS in C. reinhardtii. Although two PGPSs from C. reinhardtii (CrPGP1 and CrPGP2), which are homologous to CrCLS1, complemented the pgsA mutant of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 [61], CrCLS1 could not, so CrCLS1 does not function as PGPS [80].

# 2.6. Enzymes involved in PG hydrolysis

The catabolic pathway of PG has long been assumed to be similar to the other phospholipid classes, which may be hydrolyzed by phospholipase A (or acyl hydrolase), non-specific phospholipase C (NPC) or

phospholipase D [81]. Recently, a novel class of phospholipase A1, PLASTID LIPASE1 (PLIP1), was found in search of a plastid-localized lipase involved in glycerolipid metabolism [82]. This enzyme is associated with the thylakoid membrane (Fig. 3). Enzyme activity assay showed that PLIP1 has a phospholipase A1 activity, which prefers unsaturated acyl group bound to the $sn-1$ position of phospholipids and monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) but not digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG), sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG) and triacylglycerol (TAG), suggesting that PG and MGDG are the likely substrates in vivo. PLIP1 is highly expressed in developing seeds, and levels of 16:1(Δ3t), which is bound to the $sn-2$ position of PG, was increased in the mutant but decreased in the overexpression line of PLIP1, which suggests that PLIP1 specifically hydrolyzes acyl groups at the $sn-1$ position of 16:1(Δ3t)-containing PG molecular species. Seed oil content represented by the amount of total acyl groups was reduced in the mutant but increased in the overexpressor. Metabolic flux analysis demonstrated that acyl groups released from plastid lipids by PLIP1 are exported from the chloroplasts, incorporated into PC, then included into TAG, suggesting a new pathway for seed TAG accumulation, which involves acyl exchange of chloroplast lipids and the export of acyl groups from the chloroplasts to seed-accumulated TAGs [82]. In the mutant of DAG acyltransferase 1 (DGAT1) defective in major TAG biosynthesis pathway, PLIP1 expression was higher than the wild type specifically in developing seeds [83]. The mutant seeds showed smaller size with 20% reduction in seed oil content. The double homozygous mutant of DGAT1 and PLIP1 failed to germinate, so PLIP1 plays a crucial role in the absence of DGAT1 activity [83]. A. thaliana genome contains 2 homologs of PLIP1, named PLIP2 and PLIP3, both encoding functional phospholipase A1 activity and localizing at different suborganellar locations within the plastids [84]. Overexpression lines of PLIP2 and PLIP3 accumulated jasmonate and other oxylipins, which was canceled by crossing with coil mutant defective in the perception of jasmonate signaling. PLIP2 and PLIP3 transcripts are induced by abscisic acid (ABA) treatment and the PLIP1, 2, and 3 triple mutant showed hypersensitivity to ABA and compromised oxylipin biosynthesis on ABA treatment. Thus, PLIP2 and PLIP3 have distinct role in response to phytohormonal signaling [84].

# 3. Role of PG biosynthesis in cyanobacteria and several organelle compartments of plants and algae

# 3.1. PG biosynthesis in cyanobacteria

PG is the only major phospholipid in cyanobacteria and is preferentially included in photosynthetic complexes in the thylakoid membrane [8,9]. The necessity of PG for photosynthesis is evident, as genetic disruptions of PG biosynthesis cause loss of photosynthetic activity in all cyanobacteria species investigated thus far. The role of PG in photosynthesis was summarized in the previous reviews [10-14]. Besides the crucial role in photosynthesis, PG is an irreplaceable phospholipid for cell division and lipid modification of lipoproteins in cyanobacteria [85,86]. Although glucose-tolerant strains of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 can grow heterotrophically in the presence of glucose, the pgsA or cdsA mutants of glucose-tolerant Synechocystis strains cannot grow even in the presence of glucose [11]. Thus, like in other bacterial cells, cyanobacterial PG has an essential role in non-photosynthetic processes required for the cellular growth such as the lipid modification of lipoproteins.

At the first step of the lipidation of lipoproteins, DAG is transferred from PG to the sulfhydryl group of a specific cysteine residue at the lipobox in the amino-terminal region of prolipoproteins, which are the precursors of lipoproteins. This reaction is mediated by prolipoprotein diacylglyceroltransferase encoded by the lgt gene. The modified precursors are processed by signal peptidase II encoded by the $lpsA$ gene that cleaves a signal sequence at the cysteine residue modified with DAG, and then the amino group of the N-terminal cysteine is acylated by

apolipoprotein $N$ -acyltransferase encoded by the Int gene [87,88]. In Synechocystis genome, there are around 30 lipoproteins with a lipo-box [85]. Similar to other gram-negative bacteria, in Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803, disruption mutants of lgt could not be obtained, thus the lethality of PG-lacking Synechocystis might be caused by the lack of lipid modification of lipoproteins [11]. In E. coli, three lipoproteins, BamD, LolB, and LptE, are known to be essential for growth [87,88]. In Synechocystis genome, there are no obvious homologous proteins with these E. coli essential lipoproteins, suggesting that other lipoproteins essential for growth are present in Synechocystis cells.

In cyanobacteria, the biosynthetic pathway of PG is separated from that of glycolipids at the point of PA biosynthesis [89]. PA is a substrate for both CDS and PAP, which convert PA to CDP-DAG and PA to DAG, respectively. Some part of DAG can be phosphorylated by DAG kinase (DGK) and reverted to PA for PG biosynthesis. Thus, the activities of CDS, PAP, and DGK affect the biosynthetic pathway of PG. Although the PG level in cyanobacterial cells could be regulated by these enzymes, molecular mechanisms of the regulation remain unclear. Meanwhile, PG is known to stimulate the enzyme activity of MgdA in vitro, which catalyzes the biosynthesis of monoglucosyldiacylglycerol (GlcDG) from DAG and uridine diphosphate glucose [90]. GlcDG is a substrate of MGDG biosynthesis in cyanobacteria [91,92] and MGDG is used for DGDG biosynthesis [93]. Thus, PG has a regulatory role in the synthesis of galactolipids in cyanobacteria.

When cyanobacterial cells face to different environmental conditions, PG biosynthesis changes dramatically, which is referred to as membrane lipid remodeling. The membrane lipid remodeling in plants is reviewed by several articles [94,95]. Under ambient $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ condition, where cyanobacteria need to deal with carbon starvation and photooxidative stress, PG content increases at the expense of MGDG [58]. Stimulation of MGDG synthesis by the overexpression of PAP in Synechocystis cells inhibits the increase of PG content under ambient $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ condition and causes a decrease of PSII activity [58], suggesting the important role of PG in the maintenance of PSII under ambient $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ condition. When cyanobacteria grow under nutrient-deficient conditions, the photosynthetic activity of cyanobacterial cells decreases, and the growth is inhibited. In Synechococcus elongatus PCC 7942, the content of PG dramatically decreases under phosphorus-starved conditions and the decrease of PG is substituted with other glycolipids, DGDG and SQDG [96]. However, in other cyanobacteria, Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 [97] and Thermosynechococcus elongatus BP-1 [98], the content of PG is stable even under phosphorus-starved condition. Nitrogen deprivation in Synechocystis cells decreases the cellular contents of lipids and the photosynthetic activity [99], and PG content recovers more quickly than other lipids when nitrogen-starved cells are transferred to nitrogen-repleted condition. The quick recovery of PG probably helps reactivation of photosynthesis under nitrogen-repleted condition [99]. These results indicate the essential role of PG to maintain the photosynthetic activity. Interestingly, addition of exogenous PG to nitrogen-starved Synechocystis cells accelerates the recovery of Chl $a$ content after the nitrogen supplementation [99]. Lack of PG inhibits the biosynthesis of Chl $a$ [100]. Thus, PG may control photosynthetic activity via the biosynthesis of Chl $a$ . PG biosynthesis is regulated tightly under normal conditions; however, the PG content is adjustable in the response to environmental changes. Although PG is essential for growth and photosynthesis, cyanobacterial cells as well as other photosynthetic organisms save PG and substitute the decrease of PG with other glycolipids as possible for their survival under environmental stress conditions.

Following the PG biosynthesis, the desaturation of fatty acids bound to PG occurs. In Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803, the saturated molecular species of PG binding 18:0 at the sn-1 position and 16:0 at the sn-2 position is synthesized and 18:0 is desaturated to 18:1(9), 18:2(9,12) and 18:3(9,12,15) by $\Delta 9$ , $\Delta 12$ and $\Delta 15(\omega 3)$ desaturases encoded by $desC$ , $desA$ and $desB$ genes, respectively [89,101]. 16:0 bound to the sn-2 position of PG is not desaturated. By using chemically synthesized ether

linked PG molecules, which are not degraded by lipases in cyanobacterial cells, and the $pgsA$ mutant of Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803, Endo et al. (2022) investigated the importance of chain length of acyl group bound to the $sn-2$ position of PG [102]. Although the $pgsA$ mutant cannot synthesize PG in the cells, the mutant cells can utilize PG exogenously added to the growth medium and grow without synthesizing PG. The chemically synthesized ether-linked PG molecules complemented the growth and photosynthetic activity of the mutant cells, but their growth and photosynthetic activity depended on the chain length of acyl group. The obtained results clearly indicated that 16:0 at the $sn-2$ position of PG is irreplaceable by any other different chain length of fatty acids for the optimal growth and photosynthetic activity. Most of cyanobacteria contain a saturated fatty acid (mostly 16:0 or 14:0) at $sn-2$ position of PG and other glycerolipids. However, the reason why 16:0 is the best for growth and photosynthesis is still unclear and further studies are needed to clarify the biological meaning.

The fatty acids bound to glycerolipids are dynamically turned over in Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 and Synechococcus elongatus PCC 7942 [103-105]. In the case of MGDG, a lipase named LipA hydrolyzes $C_{18}$ -fatty acids bound to the sn-1 position of MGDG, which enhances the degradation of D1, a reaction center protein of PSII, by the decomposition of a damaged PSII dimer to monomers [105]. Supplemented free fatty acids are incorporated specifically to the sn-2 position of PG via the actions of acyl-acyl-carrier protein (acyl-ACP) synthase and acyltransferase under strong light and affected the PSII activity [58]. Long chain saturated fatty acids such as 16:0 and 18:0 enhanced the PSII activity, while polyunsaturated fatty acids such as 18:3(9,12,15) inhibited the PSII activity due to the disruption of photosynthetic oxygen-evolving complex in PSII [106-108]. These results support the specific functions of the acyl-turnover of PG as well as MGDG in PSII.

# 3.2. PG biosynthesis in plastids

Plastid lipids are synthesized via two different pathways; the plastid pathway completed within plastids and the ER pathway via phospholipid biosynthesis in the ER [109]. The so-called 16:3 plants including A. thaliana, which contain a high proportion of 16:3 fatty acids in MGDG, use both pathways for the biosynthesis of glycolipids (MGDG, DGDG, and SQDG), whereas 18:3 plants use only the ER pathway for it. Meanwhile, in both 16:3 and 18:3 plants, PG synthesized in plastids is mostly derived from the plastid pathway.

The plastid pathway for the biosynthesis of PG as well as glycolipids starts by transferring the acyl chain from acyl-ACP to the $sn-1$ position of G3P by G3P acyltransferase (GPAT) to yield lyso-PA. This reaction is followed by further acylation of lyso-PA at the $sn-2$ position by lyso-PA acyltransferase (LPAAT), resulting in the formation of PA [109]. Owing to the molecular species of fatty acids synthesized in plastids, desaturation of 18:0 by 18:0-ACP desaturase, and substrate preferences of plastidic GPAT and LPAAT, fatty acids of the synthesized PA predominantly comprise 18:1(9) at the $sn-1$ position and 16:0 at the $sn-2$ position [110]. Both plastid-localized GPAT and LPAAT are essential for the glycerolipid biosynthesis via the plastid pathway. Indeed, the knockout mutation of plastidic isoform of LPAAT (ATS2) resulted in embryolethality in A. thaliana, suggesting the indispensable role of this enzyme [111]. However, loss of function of the plastidic isoform of GPAT (ATS1/ACT1) only slightly decreased the PG content, although the additional inhibition of ATS2 by RNA interference in the $ats1$ mutant substantially decreased the PG content [112]. Because ATS2 has no GPAT activity [112], there may be a yet unidentified protein with GPAT activity in A. thaliana plastids. Of note, in bacteria, there are two pathways to produce lyso-PA; the acylation of G3P with acyl-ACP by the PlsB protein and that with acyl-phosphate by the PlsY protein [113]. Although the acyl-phosphate pathway has not been identified in plants, we cannot exclude that a PlsY-type protein may function in the plastid pathway redundantly with ATS1.

After the formation of PA, the plastid pathway of lipid biosynthesis

bifurcates into two branches for PG biosynthesis and glycolipid biosynthesis; PA is converted to CDP-DAG by CDS for PG biosynthesis or dephosphorylated to DAG by PAP for glycolipid biosynthesis. In A. thaliana, two CDSs, CDS4 and CDS5, are localized in plastids and catalyze CDP-DAG synthesis [33] (Fig. 3). CDP-DAG is then metabolized to PGP by PGP1, the PGP synthase dually localized in plastids and mitochondria [52,54]. In the final step, PGP phosphatase PGPP1, which is also dually localized in plastids and mitochondria, dephosphorylates PGP to PG [62-64]. After the formation of PG, fatty acid desaturase 4 (FAD4) localized in plastids introduces a trans double bond at the $\Delta 3$ position of 16:0 bound to the sn-2 position of PG, resulting in the formation of 16:1(Δ3t) [114]. FAD4 only reacts with 16:0 of PG and is the only enzyme that introduces a trans double bond into fatty acids bound to glycerolipids in plants, so the existence of 16:1(Δ3t) is a specific feature of PG synthesized in plastids.

PG synthesized in chloroplasts is essential for photosynthesis and growth as revealed by the analysis of several A. thaliana mutants disrupted in the plastidic PG biosynthesis pathway. The decreased Chl content and slightly decreased photosynthetic activity in the $pgp1-1$ mutant, which has an $80\%$ reduction in the PGP1 activity as compared with wild type, indicate an importance of PG for the function of the thylakoid membrane [53]. Moreover, Hagio et al. (2002) [54] and Babiychuk et al. (2003) [52] independently identified T-DNA-inserted knockout mutants of the PGP1 gene, which showed severe growth defects with a pale-yellow leaf phenotype, disrupted chloroplast development, and seedling lethality even in the presence of sucrose in the growth media [52,54]. Detailed characterization of one of the PGP1 knockout mutants $(pgp1-2)$ revealed that this mutant only slightly accumulated Chls and had substantially low PSII activity [115,116]. The $pgp1-2$ mutant showed strong disruption of the oxygen-evolving complex at the donor side and a partial impairment in electron transport at the acceptor side of PSII. In contrast to the severe disruption of PSII, PSI was relatively functional in $pgp1-2$ , although the light-harvesting chlorophyll-protein complex (LHC) I might be dissociated from PSI in this mutant [116]. In addition, cyclic electron transport around PSI was impaired in $pgp1-2$ . These data indicate that PGP1 activity in chloroplasts is essential to produce PG, which is required for the thylakoid development and photosynthesis. Of note, genetic disruption of SQDG biosynthesis in $pgp1-1$ or $pgp1-2$ caused more severe defects in chloroplast development and seedling growth than the parental lines, suggesting that SQDG, an anionic lipid similar to PG, can partially compensate for the loss of PG [117,118].

The requirement of PG biosynthesis for chloroplast development was also confirmed by the analysis of A. thaliana mutants deficient in plastidic CDSs. Neither cds4 nor cds5 single mutant with T-DNA insertion showed defective phenotypes, so CDS4 and CDS5 can function redundantly in plastids [33]. However, the double mutation of cds4 and cds5 decreased PG content to $40\%$ of the wild-type level, with no 16:1(Δ3t)-containing PG formed, indicating that PG biosynthesis in chloroplasts is severely impaired in the double mutant. The cds4 cds5 mutant showed a pale-green phenotype and insufficient thylakoid membrane development in chloroplasts [33]. Although these phenotypes were milder than those of the knockout mutants of PGP1, the reason why the cds4 cds5 double mutant showed milder chloroplast disruption than the PGP1 knockout mutants remains unknown. There may be leukiness of the cds mutations or yet unknown CDS isoforms that causes residual CDS activity in chloroplasts. Another possibility is that CDP-DAG derived from ER or mitochondria might be used for PG biosynthesis in plastids. Because CDS and PGPS use different substrates, PA and CDP-DAG, respectively, accumulation of different intermediates may cause different phenotypes between cds4 cds5 and PGP1 knockouts.

The last step of PG biosynthesis in plastids is mainly catalyzed by PGPP1. The pgpp1-1 mutant carrying a T-DNA insertion in the second intron of the PGPP1 gene showed a $\sim 60\%$ decrease in PG content compared with wild type. The pgpp1-1 leaves showed a pale-green phenotype with partial decreases in Chl content and photosynthetic

activity [62]. PGPP1 mRNA was not accumulated to detectable levels in pgpp1-1, suggesting that pgpp1-1 is a null mutant. However, a certain amount of PG was detected in chloroplasts isolated from the mutant leaves. The data imply that yet unknown PGPP or other types of phosphatase can specifically or non-specifically catalyze dephosphorylation of PGP to yield PG in chloroplasts. As another possibility, a large amount of PG might be transported from extraplastidic membranes to chloroplasts in pgpp1-1. However, considering the strong defects of chloroplast development in cds4 cds5 [33] and pgp1-2 [54], the possibility of a large allocation of PG from outside to chloroplasts can be rejected. Lin et al. (2018) identified additional PGPP genes, PTPMT1 and PTPMT2, in A. thaliana as orthologous genes of mammalian PGPP [64]. However, the triple mutant for PGPP1, PTPMT1, and PTPMT2 was almost identical to the pgpp1-1 single mutant in terms of Chl accumulation and photosynthetic characteristics, so PTPMTs are not responsible for the PG biosynthesis in chloroplasts.

The 16:1(Δ3t) fatty acid is a specific feature of PG molecules synthesized in plastids. Although 16:1(Δ3t) is considered unusual with its trans double bound near the carboxyl end and the exclusive location at the sn-2 position of PG, this fatty acid has been found widely in the plant kingdom from green algae to land plants [119]. Complete lack of 16:1 (Δ3t)-containing PG in the C. reinhardtii mutants, mf1 and mf2, abolished PSII activity with decreasing trimeric LHCII levels and grana stacking of thylakoid membranes [120,121]. These data suggest a requirement of 16:1(Δ3t)-containing PG for PSII activity and LHCII trimerization. However, A. thaliana mutants completely devoid of 16:1 (Δ3t) by loss of FAD4 activity grew normally under laboratory conditions and showed no significant impairment in trimeric LHCII formation and energy transfer from LHCII antenna to reaction centers [122]. Considering that overall PG content was decreased by $60\% -70\%$ in C. reinhardtii mf1 and mf2 mutants, not the specific lack of the 16:1(Δ3t)-binding PG species but the overall deficiency of PG in chloroplasts may lead to the photosynthetic dysfunctions in these C. reinhardtii mutants. Apart from the function of chloroplasts, acyl groups in plastid 16:1(Δ3t)-PG are shown to contribute to TAG biosynthesis during embryogenesis in A. thaliana. The 16:1(Δ3t) at the sn-2 position of plastid PG may tag a pool of PG that is specifically recognized by the thylakoid membrane-associated phospholipase PLIP1 [82]. Disruption of PLIP1 as well as FAD4 resulted in decreased seed weight and total fatty acid content, indicating the importance of the remodeling of 16:1(Δ3t)-PG pool for seed oil biosynthesis.

PG biosynthesis is crucial not only for photosynthesis and chloroplast development but also for the development of etioplasts, which are chloroplast precursors developed in dark-grown angiosperm seedlings. Although the A. thaliana $pgp1-1$ mutant have only a partial reduction in total PG content in etioplasts as well as chloroplasts, this mutant showed perturbation of the regular lattice membrane structures called prolamellar bodies in etioplasts [123]. Moreover, the $pgp1-1$ mutant showed an impairment of the tetrapyrrole biosynthesis pathway and thereby decreased accumulation of protochlorophyllide, a Chl intermediate, in the dark [123]. These data indicate that PG is crucial for the internal membrane structures and functions in etioplasts as well as chloroplasts, suggesting a universal requirement of PG for the biogenesis and functionality of plastid internal membranes.

# 3.3. PG biosynthesis in mitochondria

As do the plasma membrane and the ER membrane, mitochondrial membranes contain three major phospholipids, PC, PE, and PI in plant cells [124-126]. These phospholipids are mainly synthesized in ER and transported to mitochondria presumably via mitochondria-associated membrane domains of the ER [127,128]. In addition to these major phospholipids, mitochondrial membranes have substantial amounts of CL with a marginal level of PG. Analyses using isolated mitochondria suggest that CL can be synthesized from G3P in a de novo pathway within mitochondria [77]. The biosynthesis of CL from G3P requires six

successive reactions by GPAT, LPAAT, CDS, PGPS, PGPP, and CLS. In A. thaliana, three GPAT isoforms (GPAT1, GPAT2, and GPAT3) [129] and one soluble LPAAT (At4g24160) [130] are proposed to be localized in mitochondria, but their involvements in PG and CL biosynthesis are unknown. Of the six CDSs identified in A. thaliana, only CDS6 is localized in mitochondria [21]. CL is absent in the cds6 null mutant, so CDP-DAG formation by CDS6 in mitochondria is essential for CL biosynthesis [21]. PGP1 and PGPP1 are dual-targeted to plastids and mitochondria [52,62,64], whereas CLS is exclusively localized in mitochondria [19] (Fig. 3). Of note, a null mutant of PGP1 (pgp1-4) produces sufficient amount of CL despite a severe lack of the mitochondrial PGPS activity [52]. This observation is inconsistent with the complete lack of CL in the cds6 mutant [21]. These facts allow us to hypothesize that, besides PGP1 in mitochondria, PGP2 in the ER requires CDP-DAG produced by CDS6 to synthesize PGP. In this case, PGP1 and PGP2 cannot produce PGP in the absence of CDS6, resulting in the lack of CL in the cds6 null mutant. On the other hand, in the pgp1 null mutant, PGP2 can synthesize PGP from CDP-DAG produced by CDS6, which is subsequently used for the synthesis of PG and CL in mitochondria. As proposed for the trafficking of several phospholipids [127,128], PGP2 may receive CDP-DAG from CDS6 via mitochondria-associated membrane domains of the ER.

The importance of CL biosynthesis in mitochondria for plant growth has been revealed by the analyses of A. thaliana $c$ s mutants [17-19]. The $c$ s mutants showed strong growth retardation during embryogenesis and seedling development [17]. Loss of CLS activity resulted in elongation and enlargement of mitochondria and strongly impaired the fission rather than fusion of mitochondria [19]. Components of the mitochondrial electron transfer chain were decreased in the $c$ s mutants, with the content of respiratory complex I/complex III supercomplexes and cytochrome $c$ particularly reduced, resulting in a substantial decrease in the respiratory capacity of the $c$ s mutant cells [18]. Moreover, most enzymes involved in the TCA cycle showed reduced capacities in the mutants, consistent with the altered amounts of TCA-cycle intermediates with loss of CLS. The $c$ s mutants are more sensitive to the stresses that induce programmed cell death than wild type. These data demonstrate that CL has indispensable roles in the function and maintenance of mitochondria in plants.

# 3.4. PG biosynthesis in ER

PG biosynthesis pathway in the ER of plant cells has been assumed to be similar to which exists in non-photosynthetic models such as mammals and yeast [109]. PA, the substrate of the CDP-DAG synthesis by CDS, is supplied mainly from the de novo lipid biosynthesis pathway through ER-localized GPAT and LPAAT. CDS1 and CDS2 are responsible for the ER-localized CDS activity to provide a substrate for PGPS [38] (Fig. 3). Although lipidomic analysis of the seedling-lethal $cds1$ $cds2$ double mutant showed reduced PI content, PG levels were not changed despite a defect in microsomal PG synthesis. This suggests that PG biosynthesis in plastids and mitochondria maintains total PG content in this mutant, whereas PI is mainly produced through CDS1 and CDS2. Another possibility is that PGP2 could be able to use CDP-DAG produced by CDS6 in mitochondria as discussed earlier.

In A. thaliana, PGP2 is localized at the ER [20]. The single knockout mutant $pgp2$ showed little change with lipid contents and no visible morphological phenotypes in 3-week-old whole plant. The double mutant $pgp1-2$ $pgp2$ , however, showed an obvious embryonic growth defect with underdeveloped thylakoid and mitochondrial membranes that subsequently affects seed germination. Since radiolabeling experiments showed that the embryo of double mutant produces no or trace amount of labeled PG while their parental single mutants have detectable amount, PGP2 is involved in ER-localized PG biosynthesis and plays a redundant role with PGP1 in PG biosynthesis and development in seeds [20]. After the formation of PGP by PGP2, PGPP catalyzes its dephosphorylation to yield PG. However, the proteins functioning as PGPP in the ER have not been identified in plants (Fig. 3).

Recently, a new pathway was proposed for ER-localized PG biosynthesis, which originates from the phosphorylation of DAG to PA catalyzed by two DGKs (DGK2 and DGK4) in A. thaliana [131]. The double mutant of $DGK2$ and $DGK4$ is gametophyte-lethal due to impaired PA production required for pollen germination and tube growth. The leaky double mutants created by artificial microRNA suppression rescued gametophyte-lethal phenotype but showed slightly dwarfed vegetative growth. Radioactive pulse-chase analysis using $\left[{ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\right]$ acetate showed that PG biosynthesis was specifically affected in the suppression lines. A further analysis to specifically radiolabel PA produced by DGK using $\gamma\left[{ }^{32} \mathrm{P}\right] \mathrm{ATP}$ suggested that PA produced by DGK2 and DGK4 are preferentially used to produce PG. This result proposes a new pathway for PG biosynthesis, which uses PA produced from DAG by DGK2 and DGK4 [131]. While PG and PI are produced from the common reaction step catalyzed by CDS, levels of PG and PI are differentially controlled. For example, PG but not PI has a compensatory function to SQDG, so PG content but not PI content is changed in response to the changes in SQDG content [132,133]. This additional pathway by DGK2 and DGK4 might enable plants to differentially regulate PG and PI contents. Whether this pathway is widely found in plants and beyond remains open to investigation.

# 4. Future perspectives

Since its first discovery in 1958 [1], understanding of the unique and indispensable role of PG in plant function has been established primarily in the context of photosynthetic function [10-14]. One of the major advances in the past decade is the identification and characterization of enzymes responsible for the PG biosynthesis pathways in plants, particularly in the model plant A. thaliana. Gene knockout studies on the CDS [21,33,38] and PGPP isozymes [62-64] revealed differential contribution of each isoform in the pathways and PG biosynthesis. Also, a new pathway was found for the ER-localized PG biosynthesis, which involves phosphorylation of DAG to PA by DGK2 and DGK4 as a route to specifically provide precursor for PG rather than PI [131]. While most enzymes are known for the PG biosynthesis now, much less explored is the PG catabolic pathways. Discovery of PLIP family is a major advance in recent years, which showed the fate of characteristic acyl groups in PG [82,84]. However, apart from this phospholipase A-type catabolic activity, other lipolytic reactions involving PG remain obscure. Although many phospholipases have wide substrate specificity, including nonspecific phospholipase C which even takes non-phosphorus lipid as a substrate [134], lipases involved in PG turnover might have a substrate specificity based on the characterization of PLIP family. In S. cerevisiae, for example, a glycerophosphodiester phosphodiesterase (GDPD)-like protein Pgc1p functions as a PG-specific phospholipase C, forming a specific PG degradation mechanism [135]. Although A. thaliana contains a number of GDPD isoforms [136], whether any of them serve for the PG catabolism remains open to investigation. This issue is also related to the mechanistic understanding of flux control involving PG metabolism. It is well-established that the total amount of two anionic lipid classes PG and SQDG is maintained. Under phosphorus deprivation, decreased amount of PG is compensated by the increase of SQDG [137], while a separate membrane lipid remodeling pathway goes on that converts major phospholipids to DGDG [94]. This observation suggests a specific metabolic flux that readily converts PG to SQDG, probably using a phospholipase C or D-type catabolic reaction, which remains to be elucidated. In a more global view of the entire glycerolipid biosynthesis pathway, we still do not know how the metabolic flux is controlled in the plastid between the synthesis of PG versus glycolipids (MGDG, DGDG and SQDG). For example, PG content is increased in response to decreased SQDG content in the SQDG-biosynthesis mutant of A. thaliana [132] and C. reinhardtii [138] or sulfur-starved C. reinhardtii cells [133], which contribute to maintaining the total anionic lipid content in plastids constant. Because the similar compensatory increase in PG content was observed in cyanobacterial mutant

lacking SQDG biosynthesis [98,139,140], there may be a common mechanism in cyanobacteria and plants to regulate the amount of PG depending on the amount of SQDG. Of note, it is suggested in A. thaliana that SQDG synthase (SQD2), which transfers sulfoquinovose to DAG to yield SQDG, also transfers glucuronic acid to DAG to yield glucuronosyldiacylglycerol (GlcADG) under phosphorus deprivation [141]. Although GlcADG plays an important role in plant growth under phosphorus deprivation, its role may be irrelevant to the function of PG in plastids [118]. Mechanisms controlling the metabolic flux in the ER between PG/PI versus other phospholipid classes and TAG are also unknown. To address these open questions, an important future effort will be to identify a regulatory factor that functions upstream of the metabolism and coordinates the flux control.

Subcellular distribution of PG is an emerging issue related to the metabolic control and also in the cell biology perspective in addressing PG function. It is known that mitochondria have a capacity to synthesize PG despite that not all enzymes catalyzing the necessary reaction step for de novo PG biosynthesis are present or identified in the mitochondria. For the PG biosynthesis in mitochondria, some precursors may need to be provided from the plastid and/or ER. The trafficking of such precursor may require a transport machinery as is known for the glycerolipid metabolism in A. thaliana [142]. Also, an organellar contact site might facilitate the transfer of such precursor or enzymatic reaction. For example, a recent study showed that two isozymes of PA phosphatase responsible for the ER-localized glycerolipid biosynthesis pathway are differentially localized: one at the ER while the others located in the cytoplasmic side of the chloroplast outer envelope, allowing for the proximal co-localization presumably at the ER-chloroplast contact site [59]. In this respect, development of an imaging tool to visualize the subcellular localization and dynamics of PG will be anticipated as a future effort. As an anionic lipid, PG might possess a characteristic feature in regulating cellular dynamics such as being a component of electrostatic field as in the case of PS [143].

A physiological connection between the unsaturation status of PG and chilling sensitivity is an old question which still requires further investigation. A series of studies by JohnBrowse's group [144-147] with a fab1 mutant of $A$ thaliana showed a clear relationship between the level of high-melting-point molecular species of PG (HMP-PG), which contains only 16:0, 16:1(Δ3t) and 18:0, and chilling sensitivity. The fab1 mutant of $A$ thaliana has increased levels of the saturated fatty acid (16:0) due to the decreased activity of 3-ketoacyl-ACP synthase II [145] and PG in fab1 leaves contains more than 40% HMP-PG similar to those of many chilling-sensitive plants, whereas PG in wild-type plants contain less than 10% HMP-PG similar to other chilling tolerant plants. Although the mutant does not exhibit typical symptoms of chilling sensitivity, it shows a collapse of photosynthesis when exposed to low temperatures $(2^{\circ}$ to $6^{\circ}\mathrm{C})$ for long periods. They also constructed transgenic plants of $A$ thaliana fab1 mutant expressing a cyanobacterial $\Delta 9$ desaturase targeted to chloroplasts, which can introduce a double bond into $\Delta 9$ position of 16:0, to manipulate the content of HMP-PG [147]. The transgenic plants contained less than 10% HMP-PG similar to wild-type plants and retained substantial photosynthetic function even after long periods at low temperatures. The obtained results provided clear evidence that high levels of HMP-PG cause gradual loss of photosynthesis and death of plants at chilling temperatures. However, a more detailed molecular mechanism underlying the observed physiological responses need to be addressed in the future.

Since discovery of PG by Benson and Maruo, a good deal of progress has been made regarding identification and characterization of enzymes involved in the biosynthesis of PG in photosynthetic organisms and PG has been shown to play important roles in photosynthesis. The continued application of genetic, biochemical, and molecular approaches to study the PG biosynthesis and its regulation will eventually allow us to fully understand the function of PG in photosynthetic organisms and answer to many interesting and important questions that have not been clarified.
