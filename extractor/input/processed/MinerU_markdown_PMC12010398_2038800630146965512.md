# ABSTRACT

Steroidal saponins in Paris polyphylla featuring complicated sugar chains exhibit notable biological activities, but their sugar-chain biosynthesis is still not fully understood. Here, we identified a ${ \pmb 4 } ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase (UGT73DY2) from P. polyphylla, which catalyzes the ${ \pmb 4 } ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosylation of polyphyllins V and VI, producing dioscin and pennogenin 3-O- $\beta$ -chacotrioside, respectively. UGT73DY2 exhibits strict substrate specificity toward steroidal diglycosides and UDP-rhamnose, and a new steroidal triglycoside can be synthesized through enzyme catalysis. A mutation library was generated based on semi-rational design, identifying three mutants, I358T, A342V, and A132T, which displayed approximately two-fold enhanced enzyme activity. Molecular dynamics simulations revealed that shortened distances between the ${ \pmb 4 } ^ { \prime }$ -OH group of the sugar acceptor and either the crucial residue H20 or the donor UDP-Rha contribute to the enhanced enzyme activity. Moreover, subcellular localization analysis of UGT73DY2 and other biosynthetic enzymes indicated that dioscin biosynthesis predominantly occurs in the endoplasmic reticulum of plant cells. By co-expressing 14 biosynthetic genes in Nicotiana benthamiana, optimizing HMGR subcellular localization and cytochrome P450 gene sets, and engineering UGT73DY2, we successfully established a dioscin biosynthesis system with a yield of ${ \bf 3 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 1 1 \mu g / g }$ dry weight. This study not only clarifies the ${ \pmb 4 } ^ { \prime }$ -Orhamnosylation process in steroidal saponin biosynthesis but also presents an alternative approach for the production of steroidal saponins in P. polyphylla through synthetic biology and metabolic engineering.

# INTRODUCTION

The genus Paris in the family Melanthiaceae consists of more than 20 species of perennial herbaceous plants and is widely distributed across Asia and Europe. Paris species hold notable botanical significance due to their distinct morphological features (a solitary flower and numerous leaves) and exceptionally large genomes ( $1 0 = 2 9$ . 38–152.23 pg) (Li et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2025). The majority of Paris species are renowned for their traditional medicinal value, 

particularly in China, Nepal, and India, where they have been used for around two centuries to treat several conditions, such as fractures, snake bite, bleeding, and abscess. In China, Paris polyphylla Smith var. yunnanensis (Franch.) Hand.-Mazz. and P. polyphylla Smith var. chinensis (Franch.) Hara. are officially recorded in the Chinese Pharmacopoeia, with their rhizomes (known as Rhizome Paridis) primarily used for medicinal purposes (Chinese Pharmacopoeia Commission, 2020). Rhizome Paridis is a key ingredient in several traditional Chinese medicines, 

exemplified by ‘‘Yunnan Baiyao’’ aerosol and ‘‘Gongxuening’’ capsules. The primary bioactive components in Paris species are steroidal saponins, commonly referred to as Paridis saponins, and polyphyllins I, II, and VII are the crucial marker compounds used to assess the quality of Rhizome Paridis. Paridis saponins exhibit a broad spectrum of pharmacological activities, including antitumor, anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antimicrobial, hemostatic, antioxidant, and immunomodulatory effects (Passos et al., 2022; Xiang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024). However, its lengthy growth cycle (ca. 7–8 years) and excessive harvesting from the wild have led to severe exhaustion of wild Paris resources, posing significant conservation concerns. 

Synthetic biology using heterologous expression systems offers a promising alternative for obtaining stable sources of Paridis saponins. The biosynthesis of these saponins and their main aglycons, diosgenin and pennogenin $( \mathsf { C } 1 7 \alpha$ -OH diosgenin), is based on the common precursor cholesterol. The cholesterol biosynthetic pathway has been clarified in P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis and other steroidal saponin-producing plants such as Solanum and Dioscorea (Sonawane et al., 2016; Salisbury et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023). Several cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP450s) involved in the conversion of cholesterol to diosgenin have also been characterized in P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis as well as in Trigonella foenum–graecum and Dioscorea zingiberensis, two other diosgenin-producing plants (Christ et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022). Among these, PpCYP90B27 from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis and DzCYP90B71 from D. zingiberensis function as steroid C-22 hydroxylases, catalyzing the conversion of cholesterol into 22R-hydroxycholesterol (Yin et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2022). PpCYP90G4 from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis and TfCYP90B50 from T. foenum–graecum catalyze the stereospecific C-16 and C-22 dihydroxylation of cholesterol, generating 16S,22S-dihydroxycholesterol, whereas DzCYP90G6 from D. zingiberensis is a specialized enzyme for the C-16- oxidation. Subsequently, PpCYP94D108, PpCYP94D109, and PpCYP72A616 from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis; TfCYP82J17 and TfCYP72A613 from T. foenum–graecum; and DzCYP94N8 and DzCYP94D143 from D. zingiberensis catalyze C-26-oxidation for diosgenin biosynthesis. Interestingly, 16S,22R-dihydroxycholesterol, rather than 16S,22S-dihydroxycholesterol, has been suggested as a key intermediate in diosgenin biosynthesis. Engineered systems capable of synthesizing cholesterol and diosgenin have been established by co-expressing these P450 genes along with key cholesterol biosynthetic genes in heterologous hosts, including tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) and yeast (Christ et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2023). In N. benthamiana, cholesterol biosynthesis achieved a yield of 5. $6 3 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ dry weight (DW), and in engineered yeast, cholesterol production reached $1 0 \mathrm { m g / l }$ (Cheng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). A diosgenin biosynthesis system was also established in N. benthamiana with a yield of $2 . 1 2 \mathrm { ~ m g / g }$ DW (Yin et al., 2023), and a diosgenin yield of $2 . 0 3 \ { \mathfrak { g } } / |$ was achieved through highdensity fermentation in engineered yeast (Xu et al., 2022). These advances demonstrate the potential of synthetic biology for sustainable production of cholesterol and diosgenin; however, biosynthetic systems for more complex steroidal saponins, such as Paridis saponins, remain largely unexplored. 

Glycosylation is a crucial and final modification step in the biosynthesis of steroidal saponins that can enhance their water solubility, 

chemical stability, and biological activity. This process is catalyzed by glycosyltransferases (GTs), which decorate the steroidal skeleton with various sugar moieties. GTs are classified into two structural types, GT-A and GT-B, both featuring two Rossmannlike $( \beta / \alpha / \beta )$ fold domains. All characterized GTs involved in saponin biosynthesis are uridine diphosphate-dependent GTs (UGTs) of the GT-B type. In these enzymes, the N-terminal domain is primarily responsible for recognizing and binding the sugar acceptor, whereas the C-terminal domain facilitates recognition and binding of the sugar donor (Wang et al., 2023a). The C-terminal plant secondary product GT (PSPG) motif, which consists of 44 amino acids, is crucial for determining sugar-donor selectivity (Paquette et al., 2003; Rahimi et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020). UGTs related to saponin glycosylation are primarily found in the UGT73, UGT74, UGT91, UGT79, and UGT94 families. Our previous studies have characterized seven UGTs involved in Paridis saponin biosynthesis, including two sterol 3-O- $\beta$ -glucosyltransferases (UGT80A40 and UGT80A41), the steroid glucoside ${ \boldsymbol { 2 ^ { \prime } } }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase (UGT73CE1), and four steroid glucoside $6 ^ { \prime }$ -O-glucosyltransferases (UGT91AH1–3 and 7), which are responsible for the biosynthesis of steroidal monoglycosides, diglycosides, and triglycosides (Chen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). UGT80A40 and UGT80A41 catalyze the conversion of diosgenin and pennogenin into diosgenin 3-O-glucoside (trillin) and pennogenin 3-O-glucoside, which then are converted into polyphyllins V (1) and VI (2), respectively, by UGT73CE1. UGT91AH1–3 and 7 catalyze the transfer of a glucosyl group to the C-60 position of polyphyllins V (1) and VI (2). Three sterol 3-O- $\cdot \beta$ -glucosyltransferases (UGT80A33, UGT80A34, and UGT73CR1) have also been identified from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis, with UGT73CR1 showing the highest affinity for diosgenin (Song et al., 2022). Notably, marker bioactive saponins, including polyphyllins I, II, and VII, are glycosylated at the $4 ^ { \prime }$ position; polyphyllins II and VII feature a rhamnosyl moiety, whereas polyphyllin I has an arabinosyl group, highlighting the importance of glycosylation modification at this site for modulating the pharmacological properties of these important compounds. However, the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-GTs responsible for steroidal saponin biosynthesis remain poorly understood, representing a critical gap in the synthetic biology of these bioactive compounds. 

Here, we functionally characterized the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase UGT73DY2 from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis, which catalyzes the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosylation of polyphyllins V (1) and VI (2) to generate dioscin and pennogenin 3-O- $\beta \cdot$ -chacotrioside, respectively. Using molecular dynamics (MD) simulations and sitespecific mutagenesis, we enhanced its enzyme activity by approximately two-fold. We then reconstructed the biosynthetic pathway of steroidal triglycosides in N. benthamiana, achieving a dioscin yield of $3 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 1 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathfrak { g }$ DW. These findings enhance our understanding of complex steroidal saponin biosynthesis and provide a foundation for biosynthesis of bioactive Paridis saponins using plant-based chassis systems.

# Exploring the molecular mechanism of UGT73DY2 and identifying crucial residues for its catalytic activity

To clarify the molecular mechanism of UGT73DY2, we constructed a 3D model of the enzyme using AlphaFold 2.3.2. The substrates, polyphyllins V (1) and VI (2), were separately docked into the active pocket of UGT73DY2 using AutoDock (Figure 4A and 4B). The His-Asp dyad, a specific interaction found in the active sites of UGTs, plays a crucial role in deprotonating the hydroxyl group of sugar acceptors, and mutations of these two residues often result in a substantial loss of enzyme activity (Breton et al., 2006; Rahimi et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020). In UGT73DY2, residue H20 was predicted to form a hydrogen bond with the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -OH group of polyphyllins V (1) and VI (2). With the aid of Asp126, H20 specifically deprotonated the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -OH group, generating the nucleophiles for the $\tt S _ { N } 2$ reaction with UDP-Rha (Figure 4A and 4B). This reaction resulted in the formation of a $\beta \mathrm { \cdot }$ -1,2 glycosidic bond, yielding dioscin (3) and pennogenin 3-O- $\boldsymbol { \beta }$ -chacotrioside (4). 

UGT73DY2 shares $4 2 . 6 \%$ amino acid identity with UGT73CE1, but they display distinct regioselectivity (UGT73DY2 targets 

the 40 -OH, whereas UGT73CE1 targets the 20 -OH). Alignment of the amino acid sequences of the PSPG motifs in UGT73DY2 and UGT73CE1 revealed 18 residue differences. Swapping the entire PSPG motif of UGT73DY2 with that of UGT73CE1 resulted in a complete loss of enzyme activity (Supplemental Figure 23), a phenomenon also found in studies of other UGTs, such as CaUGT2 and NtGT1b (Masada et al., 2007). To further explore the roles of specific residues, the 18 different amino acids in the PSPG motif of UGT73DY2 were individually mutated to the corresponding residues in UGT73CE1, generating 18 variants: I358T, A371S, R351A, N349G, A370T, M366L, H381S, W378L, V373L, S382T, E372L, A368G, I347V, T345V, A346S, A342V, V357L, and A352S (Supplemental Table 5). The residues within 5 A˚ of the sugar receptor and donor can also influence the enzyme’s regioselectivity and catalytic activity (Zhang et al., 2022; Chu et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024). On the basis of molecular docking, 12 additional residues, spatially adjacent to the substrate but outside the PSPG motif, were selected for mutation, resulting in 12 variants (E258A, L49A, S253A, R56A, I50A, H198S, L89I, E194D, L283W, T282S, W409A, and F101D). The enzyme activities of these mutants were tested using compounds 1, 2, and 5 as sugar acceptors and UDP-Rha as a sugar donor. The variants I358T, V373L, A342V, E258A, and S253A exhibited increased enzyme activity, whereas the other mutants showed either no significant change or a substantial decrease in enzyme activity, with some losing activity completely. However, none of these mutants were able to glycosylate compound 5 (Supplemental Figure 24).

# Exploring the potential mechanisms underlying the altered catalytic activity of UGT73DY2 mutants by MD simulation analysis

To investigate the potential mechanisms underlying changes in the activity of the UGT73DY2 mutants, we sought to resolve the crystal structure of the UGT73DY2 protein. Unfortunately, we were unable to obtain a successful crystal; instead, we performed 100 ns of unconstrained MD simulations using 3D models of the enzyme–ligand complex. On the basis of the known catalytic mechanism of UGT (Breton et al., 2006; Rahimi et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021), MD simulations were performed focusing on the distances between the oxygen atom of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -OH group of the glucosyl moiety in polyphyllin V and either the NE2 nitrogen atom of residue H20 (Figure 5) or the anomeric (C-1) carbon of UDP-Rha (Supplemental Figure 24). For the F101D mutant, the O–C-1 distance was predicted to be comparable to that of the wild type (Figure 5E), but the O–N distance was predicted to increase significantly after 10 ns and was greater than that in the wild-type enzyme during the first 55 ns (Figure 5A), likely contributing to the observed loss of enzyme activity. For the A132T mutant, the O–N distance remained within 4 A˚ during the first 50 ns, consistent with that in the wild-type enzyme, but it significantly decreased between 50 and 70 ns and then stabilized after 70 ns, closer than that in the wild-type enzyme (Figure 5B). In addition, the O–C-1 distance was predicted to be reduced compared with that of the wild type (Supplemental Figure 27), which likely contributed to the improved enzyme activity of this mutant. Similarly, the O–N distance shortened after 55 ns for the A342V mutant (Figure 5C); the O–C-1 distance was predicted to decrease significantly, especially after 20 ns, and it remained significantly shorter than that in the wild-type enzyme after 50 ns (Supplemental Figure 27). For I358T, the O–N distance consistently remained within 5 A˚ (Figure 5D), and the O–C-1 distance was predicted to be shorter than that in the wild type (Supplemental Figure 27). A comparison of these distances could also explain the loss of activity in the F101D mutant, as well as the enhanced catalytic activities of the A132T, A342V, and I358T 

mutants. Furthermore, the conformational changes observed during the MD simulations could help to reveal how these altered distances affected the enzyme activity of UGT73DY2 (Supplemental Figure 27).

# Antitumor activity of steroidal triglycosides

Paridis saponins, including the marker compounds polyphyllins I, II, and VII, have demonstrated significant antitumor activity (He et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2020; Ni et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024). We evaluated the antitumor activity of steroidal triglycoside products 3, 4, and 9, as well as the precursor compounds 1, 2, and 8, against various human cancer cell lines, including colorectal adenocarcinoma cell line Caco-2, breast cancer cell line MDA-MB-231, neuroblastoma cell line SH-SY5Y, hepatoma cell lines SMMC-7751 and Huh-7, and non-small cell lung cancer cell lines NCl-H1975 and A549. Compounds 1, 2, 3, and 4 exhibited significant antitumor activity against all the tested cancer cell lines (Supplemental Table 7). Notably, compound 4 demonstrated the strongest antitumor 

effect against the hepatoma cancer cell line SMMC-7751, with an $| \mathsf { C } _ { 5 0 }$ value of $0 . 9 \pm 0 . 0 2 \mu \mathsf { M }$ . By contrast, compound 3 showed weaker cytotoxic activity, suggesting that the hydroxyl group at the C-17 position in compound 4 plays a crucial role in its enhanced antitumor activity. Notably, the rhamnosylated product 4 exhibited greater antitumor activity than its substrate 2 against most of the tested cell lines except SH-SY5Y, suggesting that the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O rhamnosylation modification significantly enhanced the antitumor activity of pennogenin glycosides. However, compound 3 exhibited antitumor activity comparable to or lower than that of substrate 1 against most of the tested cell lines except Huh-7. These results suggest that the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O rhamnosylation modification has a greater influence on the antitumor effect of pennogenin glycosides than diosgenin glycosides. However, compounds 8 and 9 did not exhibit obvious antitumor activity against the Caco-2, MDA-MB-231, and SMMC-7751 cell lines at concentrations of $2 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ . Compound 9 has an additional hydroxyl group at the C-14 position compared with compound 4, suggesting that the presence of the C-14 hydroxyl group may negatively impact the antitumor activity of these compounds. 

# DISCUSSION

Plants, particularly monocots, often produce specialized steroidal saponins to cope with biotic and abiotic environmental stresses (Li et al., 2023). Glycosylation modification is a pivotal process in the biosynthesis of various steroidal saponins, making a significant contribution to their conspicuous pharmacological activity. Paridis saponins are the major bioactive components of the well-known traditional Chinese medicine Rhizoma Paridis, and more than 200 saponins with various sugar side chains have been isolated and identified from the genus Paris (Wang, 2025). However, the glycosylation process in the biosynthesis of Paridis saponins is not fully understood. In this study, we identified the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -Orhamnosyltransferase UGT73DY2 from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis, which catalyzes the addition of a rhamnosyl moiety at the $4 ^ { \prime }$ position of polyphyllins V and VI, resulting in the production of the steroidal triglycosides dioscin and pennogenin 3-O- $\beta$ -chacotrioside, respectively. Notably, dioscin and pennogenin 3-O-$\beta$ -chacotrioside are the critical precursors of polyphyllins II and VII, two crucial marker compounds used to evaluate the quality of Rhizome Paridis. UGT73DY2 exhibits relatively low sugaracceptor selectivity, similar to the ${ \boldsymbol { 2 ^ { \prime } } }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase UGT73CE1, which was also identified from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis (Chen et al., 2023). UGT73DY2 specifically accepts steroidal diglycosides, distinct from UGT73CE1, which uses steroidal monoglycosides, suggesting that $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosylation occurs after ${ \boldsymbol { 2 ^ { \prime } } }$ -O-rhamnosylation in the biosynthetic pathway of Paridis saponins. Furthermore, UGT73DY2 enabled the synthesis of an undescribed compound 9, but it is unable to glycosylate triterpenoid diglycosides as well as steroidal monoglycosides and triglycosides. Although rhamnosylation is an important modification in natural products, only a limited number of rhamnosyltransferases involved in the glycosylation of flavonoids, triterpenoids, and steroids have been identified from plants (He et al., 2022). UGT73DY2 represents the first characterized $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase responsible for steroidal saponin biosynthesis. The rhamnosyltransferase responsible for the last assembly step in the biosynthesis of polyphyllins II and VII may also be present in our UGT pool of P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis, a possibility 

that warrants further investigation. Alternatively, given that some known genes in the biosynthetic pathway, such as UGT73CE1, showed higher expression levels in 1-year-old plants and leaves, our selection criteria may have led us to miss potential candidate UGTs, and we will attempt to screen candidate genes using alternative criteria in future studies. In addition, Paris species mainly consist of diploids with extremely large genomes; it is possible that isoenzymes of UGT73DY2 are present in the plant, and this also requires further investigation. 

Intriguingly, although UGT73DY2 shares a high amino acid identity with UGT73CE1, they display distinct regioselectivity and sugar acceptor specificity, providing potential materials for further investigation into the mechanism controlling regio- and substrate selectivity through comparisons of their protein sequences and structures. Both UGT73DY2 and UGT73CE1 use residue H20/H21 as a general acid to activate the nucleophilic hydroxyl group of sugar acceptors. The conserved PSPG motif is generally considered to play a crucial role in determining sugardonor specificity (Ono et al., 2010; Caputi et al., 2012; Kurze et al., 2022), and the last amino acid in the PSPG motif of both UGT732DY2 and UGT73CE1 is Arg, which is important for UDP-Rha recognition. Recent studies have also shown that the PSPG motif influences the regioselectivity of UGTs such as the triterpenoid GT AmGT8 and the flavonoid GT ZjOGT3 (Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023c). In addition, amino acids within 5 A˚ of the sugar receptor and donor have been shown to be crucial for regioselectivity (Wen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b). We compared the amino acids within the conserved PSPG motifs and in a $5 { - } \mathring { \mathsf { A } }$ radius of active sites surrounding the sugar receptor and donor in UGT73DY2 and UGT73CE1. However, neither mutating the 30 different residues in UGT73DY2 to their UGT73CE counterparts nor swapping the PSPG motif altered the regio- and substrate specificity of the enzyme. Similarly, mutating amino acid A132, the residue at the corresponding position that plays an important role in the regiospecificity of UGTs for triterpenoidal saponins, also failed to change the enzyme’s regioselectivity. These results suggest that the functional differences between UGT73DY2 and UGT73CE1 are not determined by a single conserved residue but are likely attributable to the overall protein structure and substrate orientation, as reported for other UGTs (Jiang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023c). Interestingly, three UGT73DY2 mutants, A132T, A342V, and I358T, showed significant increases in catalytic efficiency, whereas the F101D mutation led to a complete loss of enzyme activity. Further mutations of these four amino acids into seven different amino acids either reduced or completely abolished enzyme activity. MD analysis of wild-type and mutant enzymes revealed that the distance between the oxygen atom of the $4 ^ { \prime }$ -OH group on the glucose moiety of the sugar acceptor polyphyllin V and the NE2 nitrogen atom of residue H20 is critical for maintenance of enzyme activity. This finding helps to explain how specific mutations modulate enzyme activity by altering the distances between key residues and substrates, providing an effective approach for the enzyme engineering of UGTs. 

Traditional methods for sourcing Paridis saponins rely largely on direct extraction from Paris species, which has led to severe devastation of natural resources. Synthetic biology offers an attractive solution to this issue. For complex plant-derived natural products like Paridis saponins, N. benthamiana has emerged as a 

powerful chassis owing to its relatively short growth cycle, large biomass, and ease of genetic transformation, as well as its natural provision of enzyme co-factors and metabolic precursors (Sirirungruang et al., 2022). A number of plant natural products have been successfully produced via transient co-expression in N. benthamiana, including alkaloids (e.g., etoposide aglycone), terpenoids (e.g., taxol), phenolics, polyketides, etc. (Lau and Sattely, 2015; Schultz et al., 2019; Chun-Ting Liu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Kou et al., 2024). Although the diosgenin biosynthetic pathway has been established in both yeast and N. benthamiana, the synthetic biology of Paridis saponins remains largely unexplored. Our subcellular localization studies revealed that cholesterol biosynthetic enzymes and CYP450 enzymes for diosgenin biosynthesis were predominantly localized in the endoplasmic reticulum, with the exception of HMGR. UGT73DY2, UGT73CE1, and UGT80A41 were found to localize in both the nucleus and the endoplasmic reticulum. Previous studies have highlighted the potential biological advantages of such multicompartmental enzyme localization. For example, OsUGE1 is localized in both the cytoplasm and the nucleus, where it functions not only as a UDP-glucose 4-epimerase but also as a transcriptional activator involved in tapetum degradation in rice (Wang et al., 2023b). Similarly, glutathione S-transferases are present in various subcellular compartments, including the cytosol, mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, nucleus, and plasma membrane, where they play roles in cell growth, oxidative stress, and disease progression and prevention (Raza, 2011). The transmembrane basic leucine zipper transcription factor OsbZIP60 is localized in both the endoplasmic reticulum and nucleus, where it regulates endosperm storage protein and starch biosynthesis in rice (Cao et al., 2022). Accordingly, the dual-localized UGTs identified in our study may not only catalyze the glycosylation of Paridis saponin in the endoplasmic reticulum but may also be implicated in modifying nuclear targets, such as transcription factors, small signaling molecules, or nucleic-acid-binding proteins. 

We explored the biosynthesis of dioscin, an important bioactive Paridis saponin, by co-expressing its biosynthetic genes in N. benthamiana using nine distinct combinations. The highest yield of dioscin, $3 . 1 2 \pm 0 . 1 1 ~ \mu \mathfrak { g } / \mathfrak { g }$ DW, was achieved when 14 biosynthetic genes were co-expressed simultaneously, although the transient expression system may affect the reliability of quantitative analyses. Notably, different CYP450 combinations produced various yields of dioscin. The combinations of PpCYP90B27 (for the C-22 hydroxylation), DzCYP90G6 (for the C-16-oxidation), and DzCYP94N8 (for the C-26 hydroxylation) resulted in the highest yield, followed by the combinations of PpCYP90B27, DzCYP90G6, and PpCYP94D109 (for the C-26 hydroxylation), as well as $P p C Y P$ 94D109, DzCYP90G6, and DzCYP90B71 (specific for the 22Roxidation), suggesting a strategy for enhancing the production of diosgenin and corresponding saponins using synthetic biology. Recently, several strategies have been developed to enhance the production of natural products in both heterologous and native hosts (Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). These strategies include controlling metabolic flux, increasing substrate availability, silencing or knocking out bypass genes, optimizing codon usage, and constructing stable genetic systems, as well as modifying promoters, terminators, transcription factors, cisacting elements, and external factors, all of which could be used to optimize dioscin production in the future. 

In summary, we characterized a $4 ^ { \prime }$ -O-rhamnosyltransferase responsible for steroidal triglycoside biosynthesis from P. polyphylla and successfully reconstructed the dioscin biosynthetic pathway in N. benthamiana. This study lays a foundation for future research on the formation mechanisms and metabolic engineering of Paridis saponins in P. polyphylla as well as synthetic biology in other plant chassis. 

# METHODS

# Transcriptome analysis of P. polyphylla Smith var. yunnanensis

Total RNA was extracted from different tissues of P. polyphylla Smith var. yunnanensis using the TRIzol Plus RNA Purification Kit (Vazyme Biotech). Given the absence of a high-quality reference genome, we performed PacBio full-length RNA sequencing on a mixed pool of leaves, rhizomes, roots, and buds collected at the fruiting stage of 4-year-old plants. This approach can reduce noise from genotype differences in Illumina-based sequencing and facilitate candidate gene cloning. We also performed Illumina sequencing on rhizomes, roots, buds, and leaves at the senescence stage in 4-year-old plants. For comprehensive functional annotation of the transcripts, we performed sequence similarity and motif searches against seven databases: NR (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/), Pfam (https://www. ebi.ac.uk/interpro), UniProt (https://www.uniprot.org/), KEGG (http://www. genome.jp/kegg/), GO (http://www.geneontology.org/), KOG/COG, and PATHWAY. Gene expression levels were calculated and normalized using the reads per kilobase per million (RPKM) method using RSEM (RNA-Seq by Expectation-Maximization) software. Candidate UGTs with ${ \mathsf { l o g } } _ { 2 } .$ - RPKM R 1 were selected from the age-specific and tissue-specific transcriptomes (Yin et al., 2020). 

# Molecular cloning and heterologous expression

P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis cDNA was synthesized using the HiScript III First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Vazyme Biotech), and the candidate genes were cloned into the pCold-TF vector or pET32a using $_ { 2 \times }$ ClonExpress Mix (Vazyme Biotech). The primers used for gene cloning are listed in Supplemental Table 8. The resultant plasmids were then transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3). After verification by colony PCR and DNA sequencing, positive bacteria were cultured in LB broth $( 1 0 ~ 9 / 1$ tryptone, $5 { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathfrak { l } }$ yeast extract, 10 g/l NaCl) containing $1 0 0 ~ { \mu \ g / \ m \mu }$ ampicillin at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight. Subsequently, 1 ml of the bacterial solution was inoculated into $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of fresh LB broth supplemented with the same antibiotic, which was then cultured in a shaking incubator at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 220 rpm. When the optical density (OD) at $6 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ reached 0.6, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of $0 . 3 ~ { \mathsf { M } }$ isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside was added to induce protein expression at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 200 rpm for 12–16 h. The cultures were centrifuged (3900 rpm, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 10 min) to collect the cells, which were then resuspended in 8 ml of extraction buffer (50 mM Tris–HCl [pH 8.0]) and lysed using sonication. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 10 min, the solubility of the recombinant protein was analyzed using SDS–PAGE. Then, the recombinant proteins were purified using Ni-NTA agarose columns (Qiagen) and eluted with elution buffer (pH 8.0; containing 300 mM NaCl, 500 mM imidazole, 50 mM Tris, and $10 \%$ glycerol). Both crude lysates and purified proteins were analyzed by SDS–PAGE. 

# Functional characterization using the in vitro enzyme reaction

The in vitro enzyme reaction was carried out in a final volume of $2 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ comprising 50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), $5 0 ~ \mu \ M$ aglycone, $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ UDP-Rha, and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \ g$ purified recombinant proteins. The reaction mixture was incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 6 h. The sample was concentrated using a vacuum freeze dryer and then dissolved in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of methanol. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 25 min, $2 5 \mu \up$ of the supernatant was injected for HPLC analysis (Agilent 1260 system) with a ZORBAX SB-C18 analytical column at a flow rate of $1 \ m | / \min$ and a column temperature of $\mathfrak { 3 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ . Enzyme products 4 and 9 were analyzed with a linear gradient of $30 \text{‰}$ – $70 \%$ acetonitrile in water (0–22 min). Enzyme product 2 was analyzed 

with a linear gradient of $10 \% { - } 1 0 0 \%$ acetonitrile in water (0–22 min). The compounds were detected at a wavelength of 195 nm. To calculate the conversion rate, the enzyme reaction time was extended to $^ { \textsf { 1 2 h } }$ a t $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and the injection volume for HPLC analysis was $2 5 ~ \mu \up$ . # Determination of enzyme kinetic parameters

To determine the enzyme kinetic parameters of UGT73DY2, a reaction mixture was prepared with a final volume of $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ containing $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), $1 0 0 \mu \ g$ of purified protein, 2 mM UDP-Rha, and various concentrations of sugar acceptors. The concentrations of polyphyllin V were 2.5, 5, 10, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100, 200, and $4 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ , and those of polyphyllin VI were 2.5, 5, 10, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100, 200, 400, 600, 800, and $1 2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ . The reaction mixture was incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min and then quenched with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of methanol and concentrated. The resulting products were dissolved in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of methanol. After centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 25 min, $2 5 ~ \mu \up$ of the supernatant was analyzed by HPLC. The mobile phase consisted of water (A) and acetonitrile (B), with a linear gradient from $10 \%$ to $100 \%$ B (0–22 min) followed by isocratic $100 \%$ B (22.01–30 min). Calibration curves for polyphyllins V and VI were generated using HPLC with standard samples (2.5, 5, 10, 20, 40, 80, and $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ . The equations and correlation coefficients from the linear regression were $y = 8 . 4 8 0 8 x + 6 . 1 1 7 2$ $( \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 9 )$ ) (for polyphyllin V) and $y =$ $8 . 3 6 6 7 x \mathrm { ~ + ~ } 1 4 . 2 8 4 0 1$ $( \mathsf { R } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 9 9 )$ (for polyphyllin VI). The reaction yields were calculated on the basis of the peak areas of polyphyllins V and VI relative to the standard curves. The $K _ { \mathsf { M } }$ values were determined using the Lineweaver–Burk plot method on the basis of the peak area of the enzyme product. All experiments were performed in triplicate.

To separate the product of UGT73DY2, the enzyme reaction was performed in a total volume of $4 0 \mathsf { m }$ . # Molecular docking and site-directed mutagenesis

A 3D protein model of UGT73DY2 was generated using AlphaFold 2.3.2 (Santos-Martins et al., 2021). Structural alignment was performed using PyMOL. The sugar acceptor and donor were docked into the binding pocket of the UGT73DY2 model using AutoDock Vina (version 1.1.2) (Tunyasuvunakool et al., 2021). All structures shown in the figures were generated using PyMOL. Mutants were constructed using overlap extension PCR, followed by subcloning into the pCold-TF vector. The primers 

used for mutant construction are listed in Supplemental Table 8. The conversion rates were calculated on the basis of the product peak areas. Each experiment was performed three times. 

# Model preparation and MD simulation

The initial model of the enzyme–ligand complex for classical MD simulations was obtained from the molecular docking studies. The protein was modeled using the Amber ff99SB force field, and the solvent water molecules were represented using the TIP3P model (Jorgensen et al., 1983; Duan et al., 2003). Force-field parameters for the ligands were obtained from the General AMBER Force Field (Wang et al., 2004). Partial atomic charges of the ligands were computed using the restrained electrostatic potential method based on HF/6-31G* calculations performed with the Gaussian 09 software package. Initial coordinates and topology files were generated using the tleap module within AMBER20 (Bayly et al., 1993). The systems were subjected to periodic boundary conditions using cubic models. To relax the solvent and the protein–ligand complex, a series of minimization steps was performed, beginning with constraints on all solute atoms, followed by constraints on the protein backbone, and finally removing all constraints. Each system was then gradually heated from 0 to 300 K within the NVT ensemble over 100 ps, followed by an additional 100 ps of MD simulations in the NPT ensemble at 300 K and 1.0 atm. After equilibration, 100 ns of production MD simulations were performed in the NVT ensemble at 300 K. These simulations were carried out using the GPU-accelerated pmemd program in Amber20. During the MD simulations, a time step of 2 fs was used, primarily owing to the application of the SHAKE algorithm, which constrains the high-frequency stretching vibrations of all hydrogen-containing bonds (Ryckaert et al., 1977). A 10-A˚ cutoff was applied for van der Waals interactions, with no cutoff for electrostatic interactions. The statistically analyzed data were plotted using Origin 21, and the protein–ligand images were generated using PyMOL.

The amino acid sequences were aligned using the MUSCLE algorithm, and bootstrap values (based on 1000 replicates) were displayed at the tree nodes. # Biosynthesis of dioscin in N. benthamiana

The full-length cDNAs of $P p C A S$ , PpHMGR, PpC5-SD2, PpCPI-5, PpCYP51, PpSMO1-3, PpSMO2-2, PpSSR1-3, PpCYP90B27, PpCY P90G4, PpCYP72A616, PpCYP94D109, PpUGT80A41, PpUGT73CE1, and PpUGT73DY2 were amplified from P. polyphylla var. yunnanensis. DzCYP90B71, DzCYP90G6, and DzCYP94N8 were synthesized by Sangon Biotech Company (Shanghai, China). Details of the biosynthetic genes and specific primers are provided in Supplemental Tables 8 and 11. The cDNAs of these genes were individually subcloned into the pEAQ-HT vector using $_ { 2 \times }$ ClonExpress Mix (Vazyme Biotech). The resulting plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens LBA4404-competent cells by flash freezing in liquid nitrogen. Transformants were selected on LB agar plates containing $5 0 ~ \mu \ g / \ m \mu$ kanamycin, $5 0 ~ \mu { \sf g } / \sf m \mu$ gentamycin, and $5 0 ~ \mu { \sf g } / \sf m \mu$ rifampicin at $\scriptstyle 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathtt { C }$ . Agrobacterium strains were inoculated into liquid LB medium containing the same antibiotics at $_ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } }$ with shaking at 200 rpm for 24–36 h. The cultures were then inoculated into larger volumes (1:10) and incubated for an additional $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { h } }$ . Cells were collected by centrifugation at 5000 rpm for 5 min and washed once with 20 ml of MMA buffer containing 10 mM MES (pH 5.6 with KOH), $1 0 \mathsf { m M M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone. Afterward, the cells were resuspended in MMA buffer to an $\mathtt { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ value of 0.2. For Agrobacterium infiltrations, equal volumes of each strain suspension were mixed. N. benthamiana plants (6 weeks old) with the same developmental stages and growth situation were watered and placed in the shade for about 2 h 

before infiltration. After infiltration, the plants were kept in the shade and transferred to normal growth conditions the following day for growth under greenhouse conditions. Agrobacterium-infiltrated leaves were collected approximately 6 days after infiltration and freeze-dried with a vacuum freeze-dryer. 

Metabolite samples were analyzed by UPLC–MS/MS and gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS). The lyophilized sample was first weighed, and then 1 ml of saponification solution containing $10 \%$ KOH (w/v), $10 \%$ ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ (v/v), and $90 \%$ ethanol (v/v) was added. The mixture was heated at $7 5 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ for 1 h. Afterward, the sample was returned to a water bath for an additional hour to evaporate the ethanol. Once the samples were dry, $5 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of ethyl acetate and $5 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of water were added, and the samples were mixed by vortexing. After centrifugation at 10 000 rpm for 10 min, $1 0 ~ \mu \mu \up$ of supernatant was transferred to a 2-ml glass vial. The solvent was evaporated under nitrogen, and $6 0 ~ \mu \up$ of a 1-(trimethylsilyl) imidazole-pyridine mixture (Macklin) was added and incubated at ${ } ^ { 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 60 min. The product yields were calculated on the basis of the peak areas using an external standard method. Each experiment was performed three times. 

GC-MS analysis was performed in an Agilent GC (8890)–MSD (7000D) instrument with an HP-5MS quartz capillary column $( 3 0 \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ , film thickness $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ ; Agilent Technologies, USA). The biosynthetic genes for Paris saponins were cloned into the pCAMBIA1300-35S-YFP vector using KpnI and SacI restriction enzymes (Tao et al., 2021). # Antitumor assay

The cells were seeded into 96-well plates at an initial density of $8 \times 1 0 ^ { 3 }$ cells per well. After $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , compounds were added to the wells at various concentrations. After $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of incubation, $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of CCK-8 reagent was added to each well. Following 30 min of incubation, the OD at $4 5 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ was measured using a microplate reader (Molecular Devices). Finally, the $| \mathsf { C } _ { 5 0 }$ values of the tested compounds were calculated using GraphPad software. All experiments were carried out in triplicate.
