# Summary

Transient expression in Nicotiana benthamiana offers a powerful and versatile platform for rapid production of complex specialized metabolites. Steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) and steroidal saponins produced by members of the Solanaceae family are known for their diverse structures and activities including antimicrobial, anticancer, antiviral, and anti-inflammatory. Attempts to reconstitute their complete biosynthetic pathway have been unsuccessful to date. In this study, we identified a different tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) GLYCOALKALOID METABOLISM2 (SlGAME2-NEW) enzyme as a xylosyltransferase in the penultimate step of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine biosynthesis. The discovery of SlGAME2-NEW facilitated the engineering of tomato SGAs and steroidal saponin biosynthesis in N. benthamiana. Expressing from 9 to 15 genes in combination we efficiently engineered a total of 20 steroidal ‘end products’ (both alkaloids and saponins) typically produced by tomato and eggplant and ones merely found in wild tomato species. Furthermore, we engineered the biosynthetic pathway of the steroidal saponins uttroside B, dehydrouttroside B, and their stereoisomers [25(S)-uttroside B and 25 (S)-dehydrouttroside B]. Production of these metabolites in N. benthamiana opens broad possibilities for examining and exploring their therapeutic potential. This study therefore makes a significant contribution to the application of synthetic biology for producing high-value steroidal metabolites in heterologous plant hosts. 

# Introduction

Steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) are nitrogen-containing compounds produced by various Solanaceae family members, including important crops such as potato (Solanum tuberosum), tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) and eggplant (Solanum melongena). These metabolites serve a protective role against plant pathogens and predators (Friedman, 2002, 2006; Milner et al., 2011). However, some SGAs, such as $\mathscr { X }$ -solanine and $\mathscr { X }$ -chaconine found in potato, are considered anti-nutritional due to their toxicity and bitterness (Akiyama et al., 2021b; Cardenas et al., 2015; Friedman, 2006; Itkin et al., 2013). On the other hand, wild species such as Solanum nigrum Linn. accumulate non-nitrogenous steroidal saponins such as uttroside B (Grzech et al., 2024; Pei et al., 2023). Saponins are characterized by their detergent-like properties and have been recognized for their diverse biological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and antitumour effects (Jozwiak et al., 2020; Moses et al., 2014; Osbourn, 1996; Pei et al., 2023). 

Cholesterol is a common precursor in the biosynthesis of SGAs and steroidal saponins (Figure 1). Itkin et al. (2013) reported a set of GLYCOALKALOID METABOLISM (GAME) genes that participate in the pathway producing SGAs in both potato and tomato. In addition, the recently characterized GAME15, a cellulose synthase-like protein, catalyses the transfer of a glucuronic acid moiety onto cholesterol and moreover functions as a scaffold 

protein to facilitate efficient channelling of intermediates into the SGA biosynthetic pathway (Jozwiak et al., 2024). The glucuronated cholesterol next undergoes two sequential hydroxylation reactions catalysed by GAME6 (CYP72A188; at C22) and GAME8 (CYP72A208; at C26), most likely followed by removal of the glucuronic acid moiety by unknown $\beta$ -glucosidase activity. Additional hydroxylation through GAME11 [2-oxoglutaratedependent dioxygenases (2-ODD); at C16] activity generates the furostanol intermediate (Itkin et al., 2013; Nakayasu et al., 2017; Umemoto et al., 2016; Figure 1). The furostanol intermediate is further oxidized (GAME4, CYP88B1) and transaminated (GAME12, aminotransferase) to form tomatidenol, a steroidal aglycone common to the tomato and potato SGA pathways (Grzech et al., 2024; Itkin et al., 2013; Nakayasu et al., 2021). In potato, three cytochrome P450 monooxygenases (CYPs; PGA1, PGA2, PGA3), a 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase (16DOX), and an aminotransferase (PGA4) were associated with formation of the tomatidenol aglycone (Itkin et al., 2013; Nakayasu et al., 2017, 2021; Umemoto et al., 2016). In contrast, the non-nitrogenous steroidal saponin biosynthetic pathway in tomato (e.g., to 25(S )-uttroside B) is hypothesized to divert from the furostanol intermediate and further glycosylated by yet unknown glycosyltransferases (Itkin et al., 2013). 

In tomato, elimination of the C-5,6 double bond from tomatidenol by $3 \beta$ -hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase/3-ketosteroid reductase (3bHSD, Lee et al., 2019), also termed GAME25 

(Sonawane et al., 2018) and steroid 5-alpha reductase2 (S5aR2) (Akiyama et al., 2019) results in the formation of tomatidine, the major steroidal aglycone (Figure 1). Finally, both tomatidenol and tomatidine are glycosylated by four UDP-dependent glycosyltransferases (UGTs; GAME1, GAME17, GAME18, and GAME2) to form dehydrotomatine and $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine, respectively (Itkin et al., 2013; Itkin et al., 2011; Figure 1). The renowned $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine SGA predominantly accumulates in green unripe tomato fruit. During fruit maturation up to the red stage, the major pool of a-tomatine, is converted to esculeoside A via hydroxylation (C-23, GAME31; C-27, GAME40), acetylation (C-23, GAME36), and glycosylation (27-O, GAME5) (Akiyama et al., 2021a; Cardenas et al., 2019; Nakayasu et al., 2020; Sonawane et al., 2023; Szymanski et al., 2020) (Figure 1). In potato, Akiyama et al. (2021b) reported that the tomatidenol aglycone is first glycosylated to $\mathscr { X }$ -solamarine and $\beta$ -solamarine by the potato steroidal alkaloid glycosyltransferases (SGTs; SGT1, SGT2, SGT3). Next, E/F-ring rearrangement via a desaturation reaction by dioxygenase for potato solanidane synthesis (DPS) and further reduction by unknown reductases convert a-solamarine and $\beta$ -solamarine to the $\mathscr { X }$ -solanine, and achaconine, respectively. In contrast, solasodine is the main steroidal aglycone in eggplant, which is further glycosylated by glycosyltransferase (UGT73L15, UGT73DU2, UGT93N3, and UGT93M2) that catalyse the production of major eggplant SGAs (a-Solasonine and $\alpha \cdot$ -Solamargine) (Lucier et al., 2024) (Figure 1). Cultivated eggplant varieties likely lack a GAME25-like and/or S5aR2 enzyme eliminating the C-5,6 double bond and therefore do not produce saturated SGAs. However, some Solanum species (e.g., S. nigrum, Solanum dulcamara) do produce a saturated soladulcidine aglycone from solasodine which is further glycosylated to soladulcine A and $\beta$ -Soladulcine (saturated SGAs) (Sonawane et al., 2018). 

In this study, we engineered multiple parts of the core SGA biosynthetic pathway, including several branches that have been discovered in the past decade, as well as others that have not been previously reported. Earlier attempts to engineer tomato SGAs were not successful largely since the GAME15 enzyme, identified by Jozwiak et al. (2024) was missing and GAME2 enzyme, reported by Itkin et al. (2013) exhibited low activity. We have now identified GAME2-NEW, a different UGT enzyme, which is exhibiting significantly improved activity. Our research demonstrates that GAME2-NEW along with other reported pathway genes facilitates the production of structurally diverse SGAs and steroidal saponins. We were able to produce a total of 20 steroidal ‘end products’ (by expressing a combination of up to 15 genes), including SGAs that are characteristic of two major Solanaceae crops, tomato and eggplant, as well as those found in a wild tomato species. We also provide the means for engineering other high-value steroidal metabolites, such as $\mathscr { X }$ -solamargine and uttroside B in plants. 

# Results

# Attempts to engineer the pathway to $\pmb { \alpha }$ -tomatine in N.benthamiana

In tomato SGA biosynthesis, the steroidal aglycone tomatidine is converted to a-tomatine through sequential activity of GAME1 (galactosyltransferase), GAME17 and GAME18 (glucosyltransferase) and GAME2 (xylosyltransferase) (Itkin et al., 2011, 2013). Recently, Jozwiak et al. (2024) demonstrated the reconstitution of the tomatidine aglycone biosynthetic pathway in N. benthamiana plants by transiently co-expressing the tomato (S. lycopersicum) 

GAMEs (SlGAME15/6/8/11/4/12/25) with 5-alpha reductase2 (SlS5aR2;C5 double bond reduction reaction) (Figure 2a). Following the success in aglycone tomatidine production, we hypothesized that adding GAME1, GAME17, GAME18, and GAME2 (Itkin et al., 2011, 2013) would produce a-tomatine and dehydrotomatine in N. benthamiana. First, we infiltrated N. benthamiana leaves with Agrobacterium tumefaciens harbouring SlGAME15 (glucuronosyltransferase), SlGAME6 (cholesterol glucuronide C-22 hydroxylase), SlGAME8 (C-26 hydroxylase), SlGAME11 (C-16 hydroxylase), SlGAME4 (C-26 oxidase), SlGAME12 (aminotransferase), UGTs (SlGAME1/17/18/2) with or without SlGAME25 and SlS5aR2 (Figure 2a). This set of genes should facilitate the conversion of cholesterol to $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine; however, we did not detect the formation of either $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine or dehydrotomatine products in N. benthamiana using high resolution liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS). We merely detected $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine (m/z 902.5102; $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 5 } \mathsf { H } _ { 7 5 } \mathsf { N O } _ { 1 7 } )$ and $\beta _ { 1 }$ -dehydrotomatine (m/z 900.4944; $\mathsf { C } _ { 4 5 } \mathsf { H } _ { 7 3 } \mathsf { N O } _ { 1 7 } )$ ) the products of GAME18 activity (Figure 2a, Figure S1, Table S1). Accumulation of GAME2 substrates suggested that it might not be active in planta. Itkin et al. (2013) reported the role of GAME2 (Solyc07g043410) as a UDP-xylosyltransferase based on in vitro recombinant enzyme assays. Moreover, GAME2 is embedded in the SGA metabolic gene cluster on chromosome 7 in tomato. Recently, Lucier et al. (2024) could not detect the in planta (in N. benthamiana) activity of GAME2 in converting $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine to $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine, further supporting our results. Consequently, we decided to search for a different UDP-xylosyltransferase (GAME2-NEW) and successfully complete the engineering of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine. 

# Discovery of a functional GAME2 UDP-xylosyltransferase enzyme in tomato steroidal glycoalkaloids biosynthesis

Co-expression analysis using tomato transcriptomics data from different tissues and developmental stages (Tomato Genome Consortium, 2012, https://doi.org/10.1038/nature11119) revealed that several tomato SGA biosynthetic genes serving as baits (GAME6, GAME11, GAME1, GAME17, GAME18) are tightly co-expressed with a set of putative xylosyltransferase genes that could potentially be involved in $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine biosynthesis (Figure 2b, Table S2). We thus tested four potential candidate proteins (Solyc04g016200, Solyc11g010810, Solyc12g009930, Solyc11g066670) expressed in E. coli cells for xylosyltransferase activity. Each purified protein was assayed with $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine substrate and UDP-xylose as a sugar donor. Analysis of assay mixtures using a liquid chromatography-triple quadrupole-mass spectrometry (LC–TQ–MS) revealed that Solyc12g009930 and Solyc11g066670 catalyse the addition of a xylose sugar on $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine to produce $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine (Figure 2c). Among these pairs, Solyc12g009930 showed higher activity for converting $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine to $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine by utilizing more $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine substrate. We next tested whether Solyc12g009930 could utilize UDP-glucose as an alternative sugar donor in an in vitro reaction and indeed generated the $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine–glucose product. Notably, Solyc12g009930 produces $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine-xylose) and not $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine–glucose when UDP-glucose and UDP-xylose are both added to the reaction (Figure S2A). This suggested that in vitro Solyc12g009930 prefers UDP-xylose as a specific sugar donor over UDP-glucose.

Our in vitro and in planta assay results confirmed that Solyc12g009930, which we named S. lycopersicum GLYCOAL-KALOID METABOLISM2-NEW (SlGAME2-NEW) catalyses the biosynthesis of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine. We next used virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) in Solanum pennellii to examine the effect of reduced GAME2-NEW expression on SGA levels. Gene expression analysis showed reduced GAME2-NEW expression in silenced S. pennellii leaves (Figure 2d). However, there was no impact on the expression of GAME2 and Solyc11g066670 (Figure S4). Moreover, we observed reduced accumulation of a-tomatine and dehydrotomatine (Figure 2e,f) and increased accumulation of $\beta _ { 1 }$ -tomatine, the immediate precursor of the SlGAME2-NEW enzyme (Figure 2g). Thus, the outcome of the VIGS analysis provided strong evidence for the role of SlGAME2-NEW in tomato, $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine, and dehydrotomatine biosynthesis.

Following the discovery of GAME2-NEW, we once again attempted to reconstitute $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine biosynthesis in N. benthamiana. First, we infiltrated SlGAME25 and $S 1 S 5 \alpha \ R 2$ into N. benthamiana leaves together with ‘dehydrotomatine’ producing GAMEs (termed ‘CoreTom1’ that includes SlGAME15/6/8/11/4/12/1/17/18/2-NEW). Interestingly, we also observed additional products (Figure 3b,c) eluting at different retention time. Further ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ and negative mode run analysis revealed that these are unknown compound 1 (Cpd1, m/z 1052.5634, $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { H } ] ^ { + }$ , $C _ { 5 0 } { \sf H } _ { 8 5 } { \sf N O } _ { 2 2 }$ , Figure S5A) and unknown compound 2 (Cpd2, m/z 1050.5472, $[ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { H } ] ^ { + }$ , $\mathsf { C } _ { 5 0 } \mathsf { H } _ { 8 3 } \mathsf { N O } _ { 2 2 }$ , Figure S5B) with additional mass of 18 Da compared with $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine, respectively (Figure S5A, B). Putative structures of Cpd1 and Cpd2 together with the detailed ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ spectra of Cpd1 are provided in Figure S6. Moreover, since we did not find these compounds in the tomato green fruit tissue (Figure S5C) they are possibly ‘new to nature’ SGAs formed by activity of endogenous N. benthamiana enzymes. Next, we expressed the same combination of ‘dehydrotomatine’ producing GAMEs (CoreTom1; SlGAME15/6/8/ 

11/4/12/1/17/18/2-NEW) without SlGAME25 and SlS5aR2 and detected dehydrotomatine and the same Cpd2 noted above (Figure 3d). In tomato, the expression level of GAME2, Solyc11g066670, and GAME2-NEW were low, moderate, and high, respectively (Table S3). Among these, only GAME2-NEW showed in planta activity in the formation of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine (Figure 3, Figures S1, S7), suggesting that its expression level correlates with its proposed xylosyltransferase activity. 

At this stage, we attempted to reconstitute the esculeoside A biosynthetic pathway in N. benthamiana. During tomato fruit ripening (green to red), $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine is converted to esculeoside A via a suit of sequential modifications catalysed by GAME31 (C-23 hydroxylase, Cardenas et al., 2019), GAME36 (C-23 acetyltransferase, Sonawane et al., 2023), GAME40 (C-27 hydroxylase, Akiyama et al., 2021a) and GAME5 (27-O glucosyltransferase, Szymanski  et al., 2020) that produce hydroxytomatine, acetoxytomatine, acetoxy-hydroxytomatine, and esculeoside A, respectively (Figure 3a). Infiltration of SlGAME25 and SlS5aR2 into N. benthamiana leaves together with 14 ‘dehydroesculeoside A’ producing GAMEs (‘CoreTom2’ that includes SlGAME15/6/8/11/4/12/1/17/18/2-NEW/31/36/40/5) resulted in the accumulation of acetoxy-hydroxytomatine (m/z 652.4050, $[ M + H - 2 H e x - P e n t ] ^ { + }$ , mass of the most abundant in-source fragment) and acetoxy-hydroxy-dehydrotomatine (m/z 650.3900, [M + H-2Hex-Pent]+ , mass of the most abundant insource fragment), and products of GAME40 (Figure 3e,f). However, we did not detect esculeoside A and dehydroesculeoside A, and GAME5 products in N. benthamiana. Szymanski  et al. (2020) reported GAME5 (Solyc10g085230) activity as 27-O glucosyltransferase in esculeoside A biosynthesis using in vitro assay with acetoxy-hydroxytomatine substrate. Recently, Sonawane et al. (2023) showed activity of GAME5 in esculeoside A pathway reconstitution when starting from $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine as a substrate (four enzymatic reactions in total). Thus, our combinatorial expression of 16 genes might have resulted in much reduced availability of substrate required for the ultimate reaction to esculeoside A catalysed by the GAME5 enzyme. Next, we expressed the same combination of CoreTom2 GAMEs without SlGAME25 and $S 1 S 5 \alpha \ R 2$ . This resulted in the accumulation of acetoxy-hydroxy-dehydrotomatine (Figure 3g). An additional ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 }$ analysis was carried out to confirm the structures of acetoxy-hydroxytomatine and acetoxy-hydroxy-dehydrotomatine (Figure S8). Several SGA isomers have been reported in tomato, including $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine (one isomer), dehydrotomatine (six isomers), acetoxy-hydroxytomatine (two isomers), and acetoxy-hydroxydehydrotomatine (three isomers) (Itkin et al., 2011). However, these were not detected in the SGA pathway reconstructed N. benthamiana leaf. Altogether, the discovery of GAME2- NEW facilitated the engineering of tomato ripening-associated SGAs (i.e., acetoxy-hydroxytomatine and acetoxy-hydroxydehydrotomatine).

# Engineering different isomers of tomato SGAs by altering the GAME8 reaction stereochemistry

Several SGAs stereoisomers (R or S forms) were claimed to be generated through differential hydroxylation reactions on the steroidal scaffold (Schwahn et al., 2014). In tomato SGA biosynthesis (Figure 1), GAME6 and GAME8 catalyse the hydroxylation of cholesterol glucuronide at the C-22 and C-26 positions, respectively, leading to the formation of 22(S ), 25 (S ) configurations in the tomatidenol aglycone (Itkin et al., 2013; Lucier et al., 2024). In contrast, solasodine (22R, 25R) is the core steroidal aglycone scaffold of eggplant SGAs (e.g., $\varnothing$ -Solasonine) (Lucier et al., 2024). The role of S. melongena GAME8 (SmGAME8, C-26 hydroxylase) in the formation of the 25(R) configuration in the solasodine aglycone 

scaffold was recently reported by Jozwiak et al. (2025). Both tomatidenol and solasodine structures are similar except for different stereochemistry at the C-22 and C-25 positions (Figure 5a). It is well known that plant UGTs act promiscuously on a number of acceptor substrates (Louveau and Osbourn, 2019). Recently, Lucier et al. (2024) reported that the tomato UGTs (GAME1, GAME17, GAME18) act on both the spirosolane (e.g., tomatidine, solasodine) and solanidane (e.g., solanidine) type steroidal aglycones. The knowledge on tomato and eggplant GAME8 enzymes reactions stereochemistry and promiscuity of the tomato SGAs UGTs (Lucier et al., 2024) urged us to attempt pathway engineering of the different SGAs stereoisomers. 

Infiltration of ‘dehydrotomatine’ producing tomato GAMEs (i.e., CoreTom1) into N. benthamiana leaves resulted in the accumulation of dehydrotomatine (Figure 5a,b). When we infiltrated SmGAME8 instead of SlGAME8 in the same combination of GAMEs (termed ‘CoreEgg1’; SlGAME15/6/11/4/12/ 1/17/18/2-NEW/SmGAME8), we observed the production of 22 (R),25(R)-dehydrotomatine (Figure 5a,b). Co-infiltration of SmGAME8 together with ‘dehydrotomatine’ producing GAMEs (i.e., CoreTom1) (termed ‘Mix’ in Figure 5b) resulted in the production of dehydrotomatine as well as the 22(R),25(R)- dehydrotomatine stereoisomer (Figure 5b). This result clearly showed that tomato UGTs (GAME1, GAME17, GAME18, GAME2-NEW) act on both the tomatidenol (22S, 25S ) and solasodine (22R, 25R) aglycone scaffolds and further supported the promiscuous activity of tomato UGTs reported by Lucier et al. (2024). Inclusion of SlGAME25/SlS5aR2 in the same combination of CoreTom1 and CoreEgg1 GAMEs facilitated the conversion of cholesterol to $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and 22(R),25(R)- tomatine, respectively (Figure 5c). Co-infiltration of CoreTom1 together with SmGAME8 (i.e., ‘Mix’) and SlGAME25/SlS5aR2 resulted in the production of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine as well as 22(R),25(R)- tomatine (Figure 5c). Notably, we also observed a small peak of 22(R),25(R)-dehydrotomatine and 22(R),25(R)-tomatine (marked by an asterisk in Figure 5b,c) in experiments with SlGAME8 alone, suggesting that weak activity of SlGAME8 may be producing 25 (R) products like SmGAME8. The presence of atypical 22(R),25(R)- tomatine and 22(R),25(R)-dehydrotomatine in cultivated tomato green fruit tissue (Figure S11A) agrees with our finding that tomato GAME8 could catalyse the formation of both the 25Rand 25S- configurations in tomatine and dehydrotomatine. The amino acid sequence identity shared between SlGAME8 and SmGAME8 is $7 7 . 9 \%$ , sufficient to make different stereoisomers (R/S ) at the C-25 position of steroidal scaffolds (Figure S11B). Altogether, we demonstrated the production of different tomato and eggplant SGAs stereoisomers in planta.

# Production of steroidal saponins in leaves

Uttroside B is a major steroidal saponin in S. nigrum leaf tissue (Grzech et al., 2024; Pei et al., 2023). Itkin et al. (2013) proposed that the furostanol intermediate is common to both the nitrogenous SGAs and the non-nitrogenous steroidal saponins (e.g., uttroside B) biosynthesis (Figure 7a). Uttroside B is decorated by the tetraose Gal-2Glc-Xyl at the C-3 position as in the case of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and has an additional Glc at the 27-O position and its biosynthetic pathway uttroside B is yet to be resolved (Grzech et al., 2024; Itkin et al., 2013). Thus, we next focused on examining the possibility of using furostanol biosynthesis enzymes (i.e., GAME15, GAME6, GAME8, and GAME11) and tomato UGTs (i.e., GAME1, GAME17, GAME18, and GAME2-NEW) to produce uttroside B in N. benthamiana, without expressing GAME4 and GAME12 that oxidize and introduce nitrogen in the furostanol, respectively (Figure 7a). 

First, we infiltrated N. benthamiana leaves with A. tumefaciens harbouring the ‘CoreEgg4’ combination containing SlGAME15 (glucuronosyltransferase), SlGAME6 (cholesterol glucuronide C-22 hydroxylase), eggplant SmGAME8 (C-26 hydroxylase), SlGAME11 (C-16 hydroxylase), and four UGTs (SlGAME1, 2-NEW, 17, 18) (Figure 7a). Analysis of N. benthamiana leaves extracts revealed the production of dehydrouttroside B, the unsaturated form of uttroside B (m/z 1195.5750; $[ N \cdot H _ { 2 } O + H ] ^ { + } )$ (Figure 7b,c, Table S1). Interestingly, dehydrouttroside B was never reported before although in this study, we detected it in S. nigrum leaf extract (Figure 7c). Inclusion of tomato SlGAME8 instead of SmGAME8 in the same combination (CoreTom3; SlGAME15/6/8/11/1/17/18/2-NEW) facilitated the conversion of cholesterol to new-to-nature 25(S )-dehydrouttroside B (m/z 

1195.5753, $[ N \cdot H _ { 2 } O + H ] ^ { + } )$ steroidal saponin (Figure 7c, Table S1). Next, infiltration of SlGAME25/SlS5aR2 together with CoreEgg4 and CoreTom3 resulted in the production of both uttroside B $( m / z \uparrow 1 9 7 . 5 9 0 3$ , $[ \mathsf { M } \cdot \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } + \mathsf { H } ] ^ { + } )$ and 25(S )-uttroside B (also known as 25-epi-uttroside B, m/z 1197.5912, $[ N \cdot H _ { 2 } O + H ] ^ { + } )$ ), respectively (Figure 7d, Table S1). Notably, addition of the glucose moiety on the furostanol 27-O intermediate was likely catalysed by an endogenous enzyme activity in N. benthamiana leaves (Figure 7a) since GAME17 and GAME18 glucosyltransferases were reported to act on the C-3 position of the tomato SGAs scaffold (Itkin et al., 2013). Production of the C-25 stereospecific saponins [e.g., 25(S ) uttroside B and 25(R) uttroside B] in N. benthamiana from SlGAME8 and SmGAME8 suggested that their activity is important in the formation of diverse steroidal scaffolds (Figure 7a). An additional MS2 analysis was carried out to identify the structures of products (Figure S13) and demonstrated the capacity to engineer the pathway to uttroside B and dehydrouttroside B. 

# Discussion

Apart from (phyto) sterols essential for cell membrane structure and function, plants produce an array of steroidal specialized metabolites, such as alkaloids, saponins, glycosides, cardenolides, bufadienolides, and ecdysones (El-Seedi et al., 2022; Itkin et al., 2013; Li et al., 2022; Uryu et al., 2018). Membrane sterols also serve as precursors for brassinosteroids, signalling molecules regulating growth, development, and responses to environmental cues (Schaller, 2003). Cholesterol, typically associated with mammalian lipid metabolism, serves as a precursor for numerous specialized metabolites widely used in the food, cosmetic, and pharmaceutical industries (Al Sinani and Eltayeb, 2017; Delbrouck et al., 2023). However, excessive exploitation and traditional extraction methods lead to environmental pollution and exhaustion of plant resources (Zhu et al., 2021). Thus, elucidation of the biosynthetic pathway of target metabolites and metabolic engineering is an effective alternative. Compared with microbial chassis, plant chassis has more advantages in terms of precursor availability, membranal protein expression, endogenous activity, subcellular compartmentalization, and product tolerance. Despite being a transient plant expression system, metabolic engineering in N. benthamiana, as we conducted here, represents a valuable approach for industrial production of high-value products, such as QS saponins (Martin et al., 2024; Reed et al., 2023), cocaine (Wang et al., 2022), ( )-deoxypodophyllotoxin (Schultz et al., 2019), and diosgenin (Yin et al., 2023). 

In recent years, several groups reported successful examples of combinatorial expression of over 10 enzymes and the production of target end products of different chemical classes (Gharat et al., 2025). Yin et al. (2023) elucidated the diosgenin pathway 

in Paris polyphylla and reconstructed its biosynthesis by expressing 19 enzymes starting from cycloartenol. De La Pena~ et al. (2023) identified 22 enzymes that catalyse the reconstitution of the biosynthesis of two limonoids, azadirone and kihadalactone A.

More recently, Martin et al. (2024) reconstituted the 20-step pathway to produce the vaccine adjuvant QS-21. Through a similar approach, we successfully engineered the tomato acetoxyhydroxytomatine (15 enzymes in total), eggplant malonylsolamargine (10 enzymes in total), and the steroidal saponin uttroside B (12 enzymes pathway) (Figure 8). N. benthamiana also offers a significant potential to expedite the efficiency of multiple enzyme expression for industrial production of high-value products, such as diosgenin (19 enzymes, $2 . 1 \ \mu \mathrm { g / d r y }$ weight, Yin et al., 2023), cocaine (8 enzymes, $3 9 8 . 3 \pm 1 3 2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { n g / m g }$ dry weight, Wang et al., 2022), and Baccatin III (17 enzymes, 10–30 $\mu \mathrm { g } / \boldsymbol { \mathrm { g } }$ dry weight, McClune et al., 2024). In this study, we were able to produce $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine $( 2 . 0 ~ \mu \ g / \ g$ fresh weight) and asolamargine $( 0 . 5 ~ \mu \ g / \ g$ fresh weight) through transient expression of 9–12 pathway steps from the cholesterol precursor. 

Nonetheless, success in transient expression does not guarantee similar results in stable plant transformation. Several studies reported the accumulation of unintended products from endogenous activity of N. benthamiana enzymes, such as Omethyltransferases and $N \cdot$ -methyltransferases (Berman et al., 2024), glycosyltransferase (Dudley et al., 2022; van Herpen et al., 2010) and oxidases (Bruckner and Tissier, € 2013). Although endogenous activity has been exploited in some studies to compensate for an unknown enzyme (Mateos-Fernandez et al., 2021) or to produce novel metabolites (Liu et al., 2011), modification of pathway intermediates reduces the purity and yield of target metabolites (Berman et al., 2024; Dudley et al., 2022). Also, here we found unexpected metabolites (e.g., Cpd1 and Cpd2, Figure 3) as well as the addition of glucose on 27-O uttroside B (Figure 7) C-3 of the solasodine scaffold (Figure 6), and removal of the glucuronic acid moiety from 22,26- dihydroxycholesterol-GlcA (Figure 1) generated by endogenous activity of N. benthamiana enzymes. The removal of the glucuronic acid moiety might be catalysed by an unknown $\beta \cdot$ - glucosidase followed by additional hydroxylation through GAME11. This missing link between deglycosylation and E-ring closure should be explored in the near future to allow enhanced SGA production. One of the SGA pathway intermediates, Furost-5-ene-26-amino-3,22-diol, is reduced at the C5 position by GAME25/SlS5aR2 and subsequently glycosylated by GAME1, GAME17, GAME18, and GAME2-NEW. This suggests that 

Cpd1/Cpd2 are not part of an alternative branch of the pathway, but rather biosynthetic side products. Among the studied xylosyltransferases (GAME2, GAME2-NEW, Solyc11g066670), only GAME2-NEW showed activity in planta (N. benthamiana) forming $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and dehydrotomatine, suggesting GAME2 and Solyc11g066670 might be active with different compounds. 

In this study, we engineered major SGAs from diverse Solanum species, including agriculturally important crops (i.e., tomato and eggplant). Many recent reports highlight the role of specific enzyme classes in the structural variation of Solanum steroidal scaffolds (Akiyama et al., 2021b; Sonawane et al., 2022). Different GAME8 enzymes hydroxylate distinct terminal methyl groups, thereby generating two different chiral centres. Using our core pathway engineered for tomato and eggplant SGA types, we can now generate a large diversity of steroidal metabolites. We anticipate that this repertoire of engineered steroidal metabolites will soon be extended to potato-type SGAs as elucidation of the pathway leading to their biosynthesis is almost completed. We also demonstrated the production of stereoisomers, which are specifically very challenging to make through organic synthesis. As presented in Figure 8, we report on the successful engineering of a total of 20 steroidal metabolites. Therefore, this work could set the stage for future commercial production of high-value steroidal metabolites such as the glycoalkaloid $\mathscr { X }$ -Solamargine and uttroside B-type saponins.

# Metabolite extraction and profiling using high resolution LC–MS

Preparation of extracts from tissue samples (leaf and fruit) were performed with methods described earlier (Sonawane et al., 2023). Briefly, $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of frozen powder tissue was extracted with $80 \%$ methanol and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, briefly vortexed and then sonicated for 20 min at room temperature. Finally, the extracts were centrifuged for 15 min at $2 0 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ and filtered through $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ gfilters. Samples were analysed using an ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC, Waters Acquity, Milford, USA) with a diode array detector connected to a quadrupole time-of-flight MS (Synapt HDMS qTOF systems, 

Waters, Milford, USA), or an Orbitrap Exploris 240 MS (Thermo Scientific, Bremen, Germany) with the standard run conditions described earlier (40 min, Itkin et al., 2013; Sonawane et al., 2023). All samples were analysed using Orbitrap Exploris MS except VIGS samples analysed by qTOF MS. Metabolite separation was performed on a $1 0 0 \times 2 . 1 \mathrm { { - } \it { m m } }$ i.d., $1 . 7 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { m }$ UPLC BEH C18 column (Waters Acquity, Milford, USA). The mobile phase consisted of $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile:water (5:95, v/v; phase A) and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in acetonitrile (phase B). The following linear gradient was used: from $100 \%$ to $7 2 \%$ phase A over 22 min, from $7 2 \%$ to $0 \%$ phase A over 14 min, then held at $100 \%$ phase B for 2 min; and then returned to the initial conditions ( $100 \%$ phase A) within 0.5 min and conditioning at $100 \%$ phase A for 1.5 min. The flow rate was 0.3 mL/min, and the column temperature was kept at $3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . With the qTOF 

system, electrospray ionization was used in the positive mode at m/z range of 50–1600 Da. Masses were detected with the following settings: capillary $1 \ \mathsf { k V } ,$ source temperature $1 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ desolvation temperature $4 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and desolvation gas flow 800 L/h. MS experiments were performed with the following settings: capillary spray of $1 ~ \mathsf { k V } .$ , cone voltage of $2 0 ~ \mathrm { e V } .$ , and collision energy 2 eV. Targeted metabolite analysis was performed using the TargetLynx program (Waters, Milford, USA). On the Orbitrap MS system, electrospray ionization was used in positive and negative ionization modes at m/z range of 90–1350 Da. Negative ionization mode was used to prove elemental compositions of Cpd1 and Cpd2 only. The source parameters were sheath gas flow rate, auxiliary gas flow rate, and sweep gas flow rate, 45, 10, and 1 arbitrary units, respectively; vaporizer temperature, $3 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ; ion transfer tube temperature $3 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ; and spray voltage, $3 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k V }$ in positive mode and $2 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k V }$ in negative mode. The instrument was operated in full scan mode with the resolution of 60 000 and data-dependent MS/MS (dd-${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 2 } ,$ ) with the resolution of 15 000. Two normalized HCD (high energy collision dissociation) energies were used: either steps $1 5 \%$ , $30 \%$ , and $50 \%$ or steps $30 \%$ , $50 \%$ , and $70 \%$ . 

Quantification of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and $\alpha$ -solamargine was done using Orbitrap MS in the full scan mode. Calibration curves were prepared by dilution of the corresponding standards in MeOH. Linearity range was $0 . 0 1 { - } 1 . 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g / m L } }$ and $0 . 0 1 { - } 0 . 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { g / m L } }$ for atomatine and $\mathscr { X }$ -solamargine, respectively (Tables S4A, S5A). Chromatographic peak areas were measured using the QuanLynx program (Thermo). Recoveries (matrix effect during ionization) were calculated by comparing peak areas of standards to the peak area of a sample spiked with the same amount of standard (Tables S4B, S5B). Calculations of $\mathscr { X }$ -tomatine and $\mathscr { X }$ -solamargine concentrations in N. benthamiana infiltrated fresh leaf tissues are shown in Tables S4C and S5C. As we did not have corresponding standards for $\alpha$ -tomatine and $\alpha$ -solamargine pathway intermediates, relative concentrations of these intermediates were approximately calculated by comparing peak areas of the intermediates to the peak areas of the corresponding standards (a-tomatine and $\mathscr { X }$ -solamargine) and expressed as intermediate-to-final product ratios (Tables S4D, S5D). Metabolites were identified by comparing the retention time and mass spectra of authentic standards analysed on the same instrument. When the corresponding standards were not available, metabolites were identified by comparison with the tissue extracts known to contain compounds of interest. In the absence of standards or tissue extracts, metabolites were putatively identified by comparing their retention times, elemental composition, and fragmentation patterns with those described in the literature (Grzech et al., 2024; Itkin et al., 2013; Lucier et al., 2024; Sonawane et al., 2022). Levels of metabolite identification were represented as described by Schymanski et al. (2014) (Table S1).

# Virus-induced gene silencing

GAME2-NEW silencing in S. pennellii was performed as described previously (Jozwiak et al., 2020). Modified pTRV2 (harbouring GAME2-NEW cloned sequence) and pTRV1 vector were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (GV3101) by electroporation. A single clone from each transformant was inoculated into 5 mL of LB medium supplemented with the desired antibiotics $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ kanamycin and $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ gentamicin) and cultures were grown overnight at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with shaking (200 rpm). After another round of centrifugation, the pellet was resuspended again in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of infiltration buffer and incubated at room temperature for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . # Phylogenetic analysis

A phylogenetic analysis was performed on all tomato UDPglycosyltransferase (UGT) protein sequences. Proteins containing the PFAM domain PF00201 (UDP-glucoronosyl/UDPglucosyltransferase) were identified and retrieved from the UniProt database. The sequences were aligned using MUSCLE to ensure accurate residue matching across conserved motifs. A phylogenetic tree was then constructed using FastTree, employing the maximum likelihood method to infer evolutionary relationships. The resulting tree was visualized and annotated using the Interactive Tree of Life (iTOL) platform to facilitate the interpretation of clustering patterns and functional diversification.
