# Abstract

The default mineral nutrient acquisition strategy of land plants is the symbiosis with arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) fungi. Research into the cell and developmental biology of AM revealed fascinating insights into the plasticity of plant cell development and of interorganismic communication. It is driven by the prospect of increased exploitation of AM benefits for sustainable agriculture. The plant cell developmental program for intracellular accommodation of AM fungi is activated by a genetically defined signaling pathway involving calcium spiking in the nucleus as second messenger. Calcium spiking is triggered by chitooligosaccharides released by AM fungi that are probably perceived via LysM domain receptor kinases. Fungal infection and calcium spiking are spatiotemporally coordinated, and only cells committed to accommodating the fungus undergo high-frequency spiking. Delivery of mineral nutrients by AM fungi occurs at tree-shaped hyphal structures, the arbuscules, in plant cortical cells. Nutrients are taken up at a plant-derived periarbuscular membrane, which surrounds fungal hyphae and carries a specific transporter composition that is of direct importance for symbiotic efficiency. An elegant study has unveiled a new and unexpected mechanism for specific protein localization to the periarbuscular membrane, which relies on the timing of gene expression to synchronize protein biosynthesis with a redirection of secretion. The control of AM development by phytohormones is currently subject to active investigation and has led to the rediscovery of strigolactones. Nearly all tested phytohormones regulate AM development, and major insights into the mechanisms of this regulation are expected in the near future.

# INTRODUCTION

Arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) is a symbiosis between fungi of the Glomeromycota and most families of land plants (Harrison 2012, Smith & Read 2008). In terms of geographical distribution and phylogenetic coverage in the plant kingdom, AM is probably one of the most widespread symbioses on earth. The biotrophic AM fungus receives plant carbohydrates and in turn provides the plant with mineral nutrients (Bago et al. 2000, Franken 2010, Smith & Smith 2011). AM fungi (AMF) colonize plant roots and form highly branched, tree-shaped structures, the arbuscules inside cortical cells, thus establishing the main interface for symbiotic nutrient transfer. Arbuscule-like structures were found in the earliest definitive land plant fossils (400 Mya), which indicates that the rise of AM coincided with or preceded plants' colonization of land (Remy et al. 1994). AM is older than the nitrogen fixing root nodule symbiosis, which likely evolved by co-opting signaling modules from AM (Kistner & Parniske 2002).

The development of AM can be separated into distinct steps that are characterized by the level of progression of fungal hyphae during root colonization. In a precontact stage—sometimes also referred to as presymbiotic stage—mutual recognition is characterized by hyphal-branching responses elicited by plant-derived strigolactones (SLs) (Akiyama et al. 2005) and plant gene expression induced by diffusible fungal-signaling molecules (Kosuta et al. 2003, Kuhn et al. 2010). Subsequent hyphopodium formation at the root surface depends on cutin monomers produced by the plant (Wang et al. 2012). In response to mechanical and chemical signals emanating from

# MUTUAL RECOGNITION OF THE SYMBIOTIC PARTNERS

AM development starts with a reciprocal exchange of diffusible signals between plant roots and AMF. AMF perceive the vicinity of a host root via root-exuded signal molecules that induce germination and hyphal branching (Buée et al. 2000, Giovannetti et al. 1993, Nagahashi & Douds 2000). In turn, during growth toward the root, they release signal molecules that trigger a host symbiotic program (Bonfante & Requena 2011, Gough & Cullimore 2011).

# Strigolactones (Root-Released Plant Hormones) Induce Fungal Activity

Plant roots produce and release carotenoid-derived phytohormones that belong to the SLs, which are also relevant in plant development. (For a recent review on biosynthesis and biological functions of SLs, see Ruyter-Spira et al. 2012.) SLs were originally discovered as germination stimulants of parasitic weeds, including the name-giving genus Striga (Cook et al. 1966). Forty years later, they were simultaneously recognized to act as hormones important for plant architecture and as factors that trigger changes in AMF development. Specifically, plant-released SLs induce hyphal growth and branching in an AMF belonging to the Gigasporaceae as well as germination in a member of the Glomeraceae (Akiyama et al. 2005, Besserer et al. 2006). SLs might not be the only branching factors; two hydroxy fatty acids, namely 2-hydroxydodecanoic acid and 2-hydroxytetradecanoic acid, identified in carrot root exudates also induce hyphal branching of Gigaspora gigantea (Nagahashi & Douds 2011).

AMF seem to possess a highly sensitive perception system for SLs, and concentrations as low as $10\mathrm{nM}$ of the synthetic model-SL GR24 induce nuclear division and enlargement of mitochondria and a rapid increase of NADH content; NADH dehydrogenase activity; and ATP content in hyphae of $G.$ rosea, which is interpreted as a prerequisite to hyphal proliferation (Besserer et al. 2006, 2008). SLs consist of a tricyclic lactone and a methylbutenolide that are coupled via an enol ether bond (Figure 1a). Structure-function studies have revealed that both the intact tricyclic lactone and the methylbutenolide are required to induce hyphal branching, although the nature of the bond between the two groups is less critical. Furthermore, the presence and position of hydroxyl, keto, and/or acetyl groups at the A and B rings play an important role for activity (Akiyama et al. 2010).

# Regulation of Strigolactone Synthesis by GRAS Proteins

SL biosynthesis and exudation are boosted under phosphate starvation, a condition that promotes AM colonization (Yoneyama et al. 2012). Two GRAS-type transcription factors, NODULATION-SIGNALING PATHWAY (NSP) 1 and 2, which are both indispensable for nodulation in legumes (Kaló et al. 2005, Smit et al. 2005), have been implicated in the regulation of SL biosynthesis under low-phosphate conditions (Figure 1b). They are required for the appropriate regulation of $DWARF27$ (D27), which encodes a $\beta$ -carotene isomerase that catalyzes the first committed step of SL biosynthesis (Alder et al. 2012, Liu et al. 2011). Although Medicago nsp1 mutants are perturbed in the biosynthesis of didehydro-orobanchol, the most abundant SL in Medicago, and its precursor, orobanchol, nsp2 mutants still exude orobanchol, albeit at reduced amounts, which points to a second role of nsp2 further downstream in the SL-biosynthesis pathway (Liu et al. 2011). The reduced exudation of orobanchol (Liu et al. 2011) may contribute to the compromised colonization of nsp1 and nsp2 mutants (Delaux et al. 2013, Maillet et al. 2011). In Medicago, NSP2 is targeted by the microRNA miR171h, which is expressed in the root elongation zone, which usually remains uncolonized. Overexpression of an miR171h-resistant NSP2 version allowed colonization of the root elongation zone (Lauressergues et al. 2012), which suggests that colonization of the root elongation zone might be avoided through a localized reduction in SL biosynthesis via miR171h-mediated NSP2 suppression.

Current data support a model in which SL is perceived through the soluble $\alpha/\beta$ -hydrolase family receptor D14/DAD2/HTD2, which interacts with the F-box protein D3/MAX2/RMS4 (Hamiaux et al. 2012). SL binding to D14/DAD2/HTD2 would lead to a conformational change promoting its interaction with D3/MAX2/RMS4 and the activation of a specific E3-ligase activity that directs specific protein degradation, permitting SL responses. The F-box protein D3/RMS4 was recently shown to be required for AM colonization of rice and pea (Foo et al. 2012, Yoshida et al. 2012). # Root Compounds Required for Hyphopodium Induction

In AM fungal development, the first decisive step to mark the onset of colonization is the differentiation of the tip-growing hypha into a hyphopodium at the root surface. The physicochemical features of root cell walls appear to provide developmental cues for AMF, because $G$ gigantea formed hyphopodia on isolated cell wall fragments of roots from the host-plant carrot but not from the nonhost Beta vulgaris (Nagahashi & Douds 1997). Two $M.$ truncatula mutants, required for arbuscular mycorrhiza (ram) 1 and 2, suffer from strongly reduced hyphopodia formation. RAM1 was found to encode a GRAS transcription factor that is required for RAM2 expression and binds to its promoter (Gobbato et al. 2012). RAM2 encodes a glycerol-3-phosphate acyl transferase (GPAT) that is involved in the production of cutin monomers (Wang et al. 2012). Exogenous addition of either of the C16 monomers, 1,16-hexadecanediol or 16-hydroxyhexadecanoic acid, to ram2 mutants could restore hyphopodium formation. Because these compounds can act as cutin precursors, it is yet unclear whether these exogenously supplied cutin monomers act as diffusible signals to trigger fungal differentiation, like some 2-hydroxy-fatty acids (Nagahashi & Douds 2011), or whether they polymerize at the rhizodermis, thus providing a surface cue, as suggested earlier (Nagahashi & Douds 1997). The ram2 mutation also abolished appressorium formation of the oomycete pathogen Phytophthora palmivora on Medicago roots (Wang et al. 2012). Cutin monomers have previously been shown to trigger appressorium induction of leaf-infecting fungi, such as Colletotrichum trifolii, Ustilago maydis, and Magnaporthe oryzae (Dickman et al. 2003, Mendoza-Mendoza et al. 2009, Skamnioti & Gurr 2007), which indicates that cutin perception might be a conserved feature among symbiotic fungi, pathogenic fungi, and pathogenic oomycetes that colonize plants. Cutin monomers are even produced by liverwort thalli, which likely belong to the evolutionarily most ancient plant organs colonized by AMF (Caldicott & Eglinton 1976). The perception of cutin might thus represent an archaic mechanism of fungal plant-surface recognition.

# Fungal Signals that Activate Plant Responses

Upon germination, AMF release signal molecules that induce expression of a suite of plant genes, calcium spiking in rhizodermal cells, starch accumulation in roots, and lateral root formation prior to colonization (Chabaud et al. 2011; Gutjahr et al. 2009b; Kosuta et al. 2003, 2008; Kuhn et al. 2010; Maillet et al. 2011; Mukherjee & Ané 2010; Oláh et al. 2005). These germinating spore exudates contain a mixture of different $N$ -acetylglucosamine oligosaccharides (chitooligosaccharides). Active molecules include tetra- or pentachitooligosaccharides (Genre et al. 2013) and lipochitooligosaccharides that are highly similar to Nod factors released by rhizobia, bacteria that form a different type of intraradical symbiosis on plant roots (Maillet et al. 2011). Signaling induced by either chitooligosaccharide depends on DM1, DM12, and DM13, common symbiosis genes that are required for plant symbiosis with both AMF and rhizobia (see sidebar, Common Symbiosis Genes I) (Genre et al. 2013, Maillet et al. 2011). # INTRARADICAL DEVELOPMENT OF ARBUSCULAR MYCORRHIZA

Following hyphopodium formation, AMF form a penetration peg to enter the root either between two rhizodermal cells, as described for $L.$ japonicus (Bonfante et al. 2000), or through one rhizodermal cell, as described for $M.$ truncatula (Genre et al. 2005). It appears that the fungus must traverse at least one cell intracellularly to be able to progress to the inner cortex, which may be a plant mechanism to control root colonization by the fungus. In the cortex, fungal hyphae are released to the apoplast and spread along the longitudinal axis of the root to form arbuscules inside inner cortical root cells (Demchenko et al. 2004).

# Signal Transduction Leading to Prepenetration-Apparatus Formation

A growing body of evidence indicates that common symbiosis signaling acts upstream of PPA formation, as PPA formation was abolished in a symrk and a ccamk (dmi2-2 and dmi3-1) mutant (Genre et al. 2005). This is consistent with aborted entry attempts in mutants of seven common symbiosis genes (Kistner et al. 2005), which are, at least in the case of castor-2, associated with cell death of the infected plant cell and the invading fungus (Bonfante et al. 2000, Genre & Bonfante 2002, Novero et al. 2002). In Lotus, PPA-containing cells are marked by the expression of the marker gene SbtM1, which is specifically expressed during AM (Kistner et al. 2005, Takeda et al. 2012). Importantly, a ubiquitously expressed, deregulated version of calcium- and calmodulin-dependent protein kinase (CCaMK), consisting solely of the kinase domain fused to a nuclear localization signal, was able to induce the expression of a SbtM1 promoter-driven signal peptide: Venus fusion in L. japonicus root cells. Promoter activity marked cells that exhibited redifferentiation of cortical cells reminiscent of PPA formation, which indicates that CCaMK alone can initiate AM-related cellular redifferentiation (Takeda et al. 2012). The requirement and possible sufficiency of CCaMK for PPA formation indicates that a major symbiotic function of the common symbiosis pathway is the activation of the intracellular accommodation program. The cells undergoing this redifferentiation were not uniformly distributed along the root but occurred in patches of adjacent cortex cells that resembled the arrangement of early colonization units. This spatially restricted competence of root cells to respond to deregulated CCaMK revealed plant-controlled preformed patterns, the molecular determinants of which still have to be identified. Sieberer et al. (2012) observed high-frequency calcium spiking in nuclei of PPA-containing cells just prior to fungal invasion, moving the activation of CCaMK to a timepoint after PPA formation but prior to the actual invasion event. A reconciliation of these observations would require a dual functionality of common symbiosis signaling for induction of PPA and a second function concomitant with the onset of infection.

At least in legumes, the requirement of the genes upstream of calcium spiking is spatially limited to the epidermis/outer cell layers. Arbiscule formation appears to be unaffected in mutants of SYMRK, CASTOR, POLLUX, NENA, NUP85, and NUP133 once the outer cell layers have been overcome by the fungus (Demchenko et al. 2004, Groth et al. 2010, Kistner et al. 2005, Wegel et al. 1998). However, this is not the case in ccamk or cyclops mutants (Demchenko et al. 2004, Kistner et al. 2005, Yano et al. 2008), which implies the existence of alternative mechanisms for CCaMK activation that operate in cortical cells. This cell-type-specific requirement for common symbiosis genes implicated in the generation of calcium spiking is also supported by the epidermis-specific nodulation defects conferred by symrk-14 and nena (Groth et al. 2010, Kosuta et al. 2011). A growing number of genes previously considered to be nodulation specific have turned out to also be required for AM upon refined quantitative mutant analysis. This also applies to a plant lectin domain containing an apyrase gene that has been implicated in AM development and is believed to act upstream of calcium spiking (Roberts et al. 2012). The apyrase gene has been genetically positioned to act close to the receptors at the plasma membrane and thus fits into the paradigmatic signaling pathway postulated for the classical common symbiosis genes. It appears, however, that other novel common symbiosis genes, such as the NSP1 and NSP2 transcription factor genes of M. truncatula (Delaux et al. 2013, Maillet et al. 2011), may exert their role in AM through their involvement in regulating secondary compound synthesis, which thus reveals additional commonalities between the two root endosymbioses apart from early signaling and the formation of subcellular accommodation structures (see sidebars for Common Symbiosis Genes I and II).

# COMMON SYMBIOSIS GENES II: EXECUTORS OF THE INTRACELLULAR ACCOMMODATION PROGRAM FOR FUNGI AND BACTERIA

A second class of common symbiosis genes encode proteins that have no intrinsic signaling capability but are possibly involved in the production of intracellular accommodation structures. Examples include Vapyrin, VAMPs, and VPI12.

# ARBUSCULE DEVELOPMENT

The development of the arbuscule is associated with extreme changes in plant cellular structure and function; a cortical cell, which is almost entirely filled up by a vacuole and performs a predominantly structural role in the root, is drastically reprogrammed by symbiotic stimulation (Harrison 2012) (Figure 3). This culminates in the development of two possible morphological patterns that depend on the species combination of plant and AMF: Arum-type colonization is characterized by intercellular hyphal spread and formation of highly branched, tree-shaped, terminal arbuscules in cortical cells, whereas Paris-type colonization entails hyphal passage from cell to cell as well as intercalary coil or arbuscule formation (Bonfante & Genre 2008). Most plants support intermediate forms of these patterns, which have led to formulation of the term "Arum-Paris-type continuum" (Dickson 2004).

The initial stages of arbuscule development appear to be very similar to that of PPA formation (Genre et al. 2008). However, only in the case of the Paris-type AM is the PPA built for transcellular passage of the fungus, and PPA formation reaches several cortex cells ahead of the colonization front. During formation of terminal arbuscules by the most-studied Arum-type AM, separated PPA-like endoplasmic reticulum (ER) aggregates form in inner cortical cells upon contact with intracellular hyphae (Genre et al. 2008). Which mechanism determines the development of one or the other colonization pattern remains an open question. PPA and arbuscule formation are associated with massive restructuring of the cytoskeleton (Blancaflor et al. 2001, Genre & Bonfante 1998). Both actin filaments and microtubules form a basketlike structure around the arbuscule, likely acting as executors of cytoplasmic aggregation. The nucleus moves to the center of the cell and the branched arbuscule. The vacuole deforms, and a marked proliferation of plastids and plant mitochondria, including formation of a plastidial network around the arbuscule, is observed (Fester et al. 2001, Lohse et al. 2005, Pumplin & Harrison 2009). The latter may be necessary to satisfy the increased demand for fatty and amino acids during biosynthesis of the periarbuscular membrane (PAM).

# VAPYRIN, a Chimaeric Structural Protein Required for Intracellular Infection

Another gene potentially involved in the structural reorganization of infected cells is VAPYRIN. In vapyrin mutants of Medicago and Petunia, rhizodermal penetration is frequently aborted, and in rare cases, in which cortical colonization is achieved, no arbuscules or only small hyphal protrusions into cortical cells are formed (Murray et al. 2011, Plumlin et al. 2010, Reddy et al. 2007). Arbuscule formation is therefore arrested at stage I or II (Figure 3). In Medicago, the VAPYRIN gene has also been implicated in infection thread formation and root nodulation during symbiosis with rhizobia (Murray et al. 2011). VAPYRINs are plant-specific proteins conserved from the mosses to monocots and dicots. VAPYRIN contains a major sperm protein domain that also occurs in VAP proteins involved in membrane fusion processes in various eukaryotes as well as an ankyrin domain implicated in protein-protein interaction (Feddermann & Reinhardt 2011). VAPYRIN is associated with small subcellular compartments, potentially vesicles (Feddermann et al. 2010, Plumlin et al. 2010). Although the phenotype, sequence predictions, and localization suggest a structural role for VAPYRIN involving protein interactions at membranes and/or the cytoskeleton during the formation of intracellular accommodation structures, its precise function in symbiosis remains unclear (Feddermann & Reinhardt 2011).

# Transcriptional Reprogramming During Arbiscule Development

As expected for this transition from a resting to a restructured and presumably metabolically more active cell, hundreds of genes change their transcript levels in arbuscule-containing cells, as determined by transcription profiling after laser capture microdissection (Gaude et al. 2012, Gomez et al. 2009, Hogekamp et al. 2011). The largest functional groups of induced transcripts were related to transport processes, transcriptional regulation, and lipid metabolism (Gaude et al. 2012). Perhaps surprisingly, in one study the highest number of induced genes was detected in cortical cells neighboring arbuscules relative to cortical cells of noninoculated roots (Gaude et al. 2012). This not only may be explained by the presence of intraradical hyphae in at least a subset of these neighboring cells but also likely reflects that additional cortex cells are transcriptionally reprogrammed during AM. This would be consistent with the expression patterns of AM-specific marker promoter:GUS fusions, such as the promoters of $SCP$ , $Bcp1$ , and $SbtM1$ , which have been observed to be activated in cells containing intracellular hyphae and in several cells belonging to the cell file that lies ahead of the front of a colonization unit and may be symptomatic of PPA formation (Hohnjec et al. 2005; Liu et al. 2003; Pumplin & Harrison 2009; Takeda et al. 2009, 2012).

It appears that arbuscule formation is accompanied by at least two waves of cell-autonomous gene-expression changes. The first wave is characterized by genes that are induced prior to and during arbuscule formation, such as $SCP$ or $SbtM1$ , while the second wave of genes is induced during arbuscule formation and in arbuscule-containing cells only, such as $PT4$ , $Cell1$ , $STR$ , and $STR2$ (Figure 3a) (Harrison et al. 2002, Liu et al. 2003, Zhang et al. 2010). Individual members of the first wave of gene induction are directly or indirectly controlled by the common symbiosis signaling pathway, because the representative marker $SbtM1$ was induced in the absence of AMF by

a GOF-CCaMK construct (Takeda et al. 2012). However, the arbuscule development marker gene PHOSPHATE TRANSPORTER 4 (PT4) was not induced, and it remains obscure how expression of the second wave might be regulated.

# The Timing of Gene Expression During Arbiscule Development Determines the Localization Domain Within the Periarbscular Membrane

The plant-derived PAM is in continuum with the plasma membrane and surrounds each branch of the arbuscule, preventing direct contact with the plant cytoplasm. Therefore, the plant-membrane surface massively increases during arbuscule development, which increases the interface size available for nutrient and metabolite exchange between the symbiotic partners. Despite its direct connection to the PM, the PAM has a distinct protein composition (Pumplin & Harrison 2009). At least two distinct membrane domains can be distinguished within an arbuscule-containing cell based on their protein composition: Phosphate transporter 4 (PT4) was localized specifically in arbuscule branches, whereas blue copper-binding protein1 (BCP1) was found in the peripheral plasma membrane and arbuscule trunks (Figure 3a) (Pumplin & Harrison 2009). One of the major questions in AM cell biology is related to the specific targeting of proteins to the symbiotic interface. Which mechanisms ensure that transporters are correctly and exclusively localized to the PAM, and how is protein secretion guided to the periarbuscular space? Groundbreaking work by the Harrison lab has revealed a surprising and completely unexpected sorting principle: Promoter swap constructs revealed that the promoter is a crucial determinant for subcellular localization. The plasma membrane-localized transporter phosphate transporter 1 (PT1); a polyol transporter, PLT; and a monosaccharide transporter, STP, were all redirected to the PAM upon expression under the MtPT4 promoter (Pumplin et al. 2012). An important conclusion from this discovery was that the precise timing of gene expression is critical for the correct localization of PAM proteins (Figure 3a,b). This can be explained by a model in which arbuscule growth is associated with an overall redirection of secretory vesicles toward the growing arbuscule branches. This also concurs with the observation that an SbtM1 promoter-driven signal peptide: Venus fusion was specifically localized in the periarbuscular space (PAS) (Figure 3b), despite the absence of any specific targeting information in the fluorescent protein (Figure 3b) (Takeda et al. 2009). It thus appears that vesicle fusion with the PAM is the default pathway during arbuscule development. However, there must be additional protein-sequence-encoded signals for export from the ER for plasma-membrane localization, because AtPIP2, but not PT1, was stuck in the ER when expressed under the control of the PT4 promoter, and PT4 was retained in the ER when expressed from a viral CaMV 35S promoter. The CaMV 35S promoter appears to be off in arbuscule-containing cells (Pumplin et al. 2012), but proteins expressed from a CaMV 35S promoter-driven transgene prior to arbuscule development retain their original membrane localization during arbuscule formation. This has been demonstrated for the $\gamma$ -TIP tonoplast marker (Pumplin & Harrison 2009) and the plasma membrane resident PT1 of Medicago (Pumplin et al. 2012).

PT4 localization at the arbuscule branches is similar to that of the soybean ammonium transporter AMT4.1 (Kobae et al. 2010) and the ABC transporter STUNTED ARBUSCULE (STR), discussed below. These transporters are probably representative of an entire cohort of PAM-localized proteins and transporters detected through transcriptome profiling of arbuscule-containing cells (Gaude et al. 2012, Gomez et al. 2009, Hogekamp et al. 2011). Because timing of gene expression seems critical for correct localization of PAM-localized proteins, it will be important to determine the factors that ensure its precise regulation. $StPT4$ , a potato ortholog of $MtPT4$ , was induced by lysophosphatidylcholine (LPC) in the absence of AM colonization (Drissner et al. 2007). Whether LPC is the physiological inducer of $PT4$ remains to be

determined. However, the presumably enzymatic liberation of LPC from phosphatidylcholine would require a specific, yet-unknown developmental cue to ensure perfect temporal synchronization of $PT4$ expression with the correct stage of PAM formation.

# Genes Required for Arbiscule Branching

Symbiotic plant mutants have been described that are arrested in different stages of arbuscle development. Detailed morphological analyses of these mutants define at least five distinct stages that can be genetically separated (Figure 3b,c). The first two stages are PPA formation and fungal cell entry, leading to arbuscle trunk formation. We call the third phase birdsfoot, in reference to the characteristic shape of the coarse hyphal branches, which at this stage resemble the toes of a bird's foot. The first stage requires the common symbiosis genes $CCaMK$ and CYCLOPS, and the second and third require $VAPYRIN/PAM1$ . The phenotype of the corresponding mutants is characterized by complete absence of arbuscules (ccamk, cyclops, vapyrin) or single hyphal protrusions that enter cortical cells (vapyrin/pam1) (Demchenko et al. 2004, Kistner et al. 2005, Pumplin et al. 2010, Reddy et al. 2007, Yano et al. 2008). The fourth stage relates to mature arbuscules that develop from the birdsfoot stage via continuous hyphal branching. Two half-size ABC transporter genes from Medicago have been identified, mutation or downregulation of which leads to a developmental arrest at the birdsfoot stage (Zhang et al. 2010). The two transporters specifically heterodimerize in the branch region of the PAM (Zhang et al. 2010), similar to the localization of PT4. By analogy to other plant ABC transporters, STR is predicted to export a small molecule into the periarbuscular space. Although the nature of this small molecule remains to be determined, functional conservation of STR1 and -2 in the distantly related monocot rice suggests that the molecule(s) may be conserved across angiosperms (Gutjahr et al. 2012). Carotenoid derivatives, including SLs, are unlikely to be the transported substrate, as carotenoid (albescent plant 1 and pale yellow 9 essential for 15-cis-zeta-carotene isomerization) mutants of corn (Fester et al. 2002, Li et al. 2007) and SL biosynthesis (d17 and d10) (Gutjahr et al. 2012) and putative SL receptor (d14) mutants of rice (Yoshida et al. 2012) form wild-type-shaped arbuscules.

The RAM2 gene encoding a GPAT that produces a small molecule promoting hyphopodium induction, as discussed above, is additionally required for arbuscule branching, because rare cases of fungal progression to the cortex or ram2 mutant roots yielded stunted (stage III, birdsfoot) arbus-cules (Wang et al. 2012). Both overexpression of the Kunitz-type protease inhibitor gene KPI106 and RNAi-mediated knockdown of the proteinase gene SCP1 caused arbuscule-developmental arrest at the birdsfoot stage (Rech et al. 2013). Both genes are induced during AM, and their protein products are predicted to be secreted to the apoplast. Interaction of SCP1 with and presumably inhibition by KPI106 could indicate that SCP1 activity is required for arbuscule branching. AmiR-mediated suppression of the transcription factor gene $ERF1$ caused development of stunted arbuscules blocked at the birdsfoot stage (Devers et al. 2013), raising the possibility that ERF1 regulates genes that need to be expressed to enable arbuscule branching. Two L. japonicus mutants, disorganized arbuscules (dis, SL0154-N) and reduced and degenerate arbuscules (red, SL0181-N), are perturbed in arbuscule branching. Rough mapping revealed that they must be defective in yet-unknown loci (Groth et al. 2013) required for arbuscule branching control. Interestingly, RNAi of the two VAMPs (R-SNAREs) 721d and e or the Qb SNARE $LjVT112$ lead to stunted (stage III) arbuscules, thus indicating an involvement at transition stage IV of arbuscular development. However, it remains to be determined whether this phenotype is due to a specific requirement of these SNARE paralogs during arbuscule branching, or whether the residual gene expression in the silenced roots is sufficient for arbuscule development until the birdsfoot stage, but not for the massive additional membrane delivery required for PAM synthesis during fine branching (Ivanov

et al. 2012, Lota et al. 2013). Importantly, these secretion-defective RNAi lines illustrate that fungal growth and branching are limited by PAM synthesis and thus are controlled by the plant.

# REGULATION OF ARBUSCULAR MYCORRHIZA DEVELOPMENT BY NUTRIENT AVAILABILITY

Symbiotic mineral nutrient transfer—and this is particularly well documented for phosphate—has emerged to be one of the main determinants of symbiotic development between the host plant and the fungus. Plants regulate the extent of AM colonization according to their nutritional needs, likely as a mechanism to minimize the carbon cost imposed by the AMF. At low phosphate availability, the phosphate-uptake route of AM-colonized plants switches entirely from the direct rhizodermal-uptake pathway to the symbiotic-uptake pathway, which involves a switch between two different sets of phosphate-uptake transporters (see Smith et al. 2003, Yang et al. 2012b, and references therein). At high phosphate availability, colonization and the symbiotic phosphate-uptake pathway are suppressed (Breuillin et al. 2010). This suppression includes the inducibility of symbiotic phosphate transporter gene expression by LPC (Nagy et al. 2009).

# Systemic Inhibition of Arbuscular Mycorrhiza Development by Phosphate

The impact of high phosphate conditions on AM development reaches from a quantitative suppression of colonization and aberrant arbuscle formation in Petunia (Breuillin et al. 2010) to decreased hyphopodium induction with negligible intraradical colonization in pea (Balzergue et al. 2010). Split root experiments in which one half of the root system was fertilized with a high-phosphate concentration and the other half with a low-phosphate concentration revealed inhibition of AM development by phosphate on both sides of the split root, which indicates that the effect was systemic and likely dependent on the phosphate status of the shoot (Balzergue et al. 2010, Breuillin et al. 2010). The mechanism through which the host grown under phosphate-sufficient conditions blocks AMF development is unknown.

Although the phosphate and nitrogen status of the soil strongly regulate SL production (López-Ráñez et al. 2008, Yoneyama et al. 2012), the phosphate-mediated suppression of SL biosynthesis was not responsible for reduced AM development, because exogenous application of SL did not restore colonization (Balzergue et al. 2010, Breuillin et al. 2010). Furthermore, the extent of AM suppression by high phosphate was similar among SL-deficient pea mutants and wild type, even if the reduced SL biosynthesis in the mutant backgrounds was already limiting colonization (Foo et al. 2012).

In Arabidopsis, phosphate starvation responses are inhibited by gibberellic acid (GA) signaling (Jiang et al. 2007), and exogenous application of GA to AMF-inoculated pea plants as well as constitutive gibberellin signaling in DELLA protein-deficient pea mutants suppress AM colonization (El Ghachtouli et al. 1996, Foo et al. 2013). Taken together, these data raise the possibility that the GA-signaling pathway might mediate suppression of AM development by high phosphate. Conversely, jasmonic acid (JA) signaling has been suggested to promote phosphate starvation responses (Chacon-Lopez et al. 2011). GA signaling and biosynthesis are antagonized by JA signaling (Heinrich et al. 2012, Hou et al. 2010, Yang et al. 2012a), and it seems attractive to assume that the control over AM colonization levels at different phosphate regimes might be achieved through a fine-tuned balance between GA and JA signaling. However, data on the role of JA in AM development are yet contradictory (Gutjahr & Paszkowski 2009, Hause & Schaarschmidt 2009). In some studies, JA promoted AM colonization (Isayenkov et al. 2005, Landgraf et al. 2012), whereas in others it played an inhibitory role (Herrera-Medina et al. 2008).

# Plant Control over Arbiscule Lifetime

Kobae & Hata (2010) achieved a breakthrough in live cell imaging of arbuscule developmental dynamics. By using a transgenic rice line expressing the PAM-localized PT11prom:PT11:GFP fusion, together with an ingenious, nondisruptive yet economical observation setup, they monitored fungal colonization patterns live at an unprecedented whole-root level with single-arbuscule resolution. Arbuscules were confirmed to be ephemeral structures with a lifetime at maturity of approximately two to three days in rice. However, some arbuscules had a shorter lifetime and became visible and collapsed within $24\mathrm{h}$ , indicating differences in turnover times among individual arbuscules.

Studies addressing the importance of mycorrhiza-specific phosphate transporters of the Pht1 family provided strong evidence that arbuscule turnover is regulated by the plant. Mutation or knockdown of Medicago PT4 and the rice ortholog PT11 caused low colonization and an increased number of stunted arbuscules (Javot et al. 2007b, Yang et al. 2012b) as a result of an accelerated arbuscule turnover (Javot et al. 2007b). PT13 is a second phosphate transporter in rice that is expressed in arbuscule-containing cells and belongs to a monocotyledon-specific clade. Intriguingly, mutation of PT13 led to a $pt11$ -related arbuscule phenotype, in spite of PT13 not being critical for AM-mediated phosphate uptake, in contrast to PT11 (Yang et al. 2012b). This raises the possibility that PT13 might be involved not in transporting but in sensing the phosphate that is delivered by the arbuscule (Yang et al. 2012b), consistent with the hypothesis that the phosphate ion itself could act as the signal that promotes plant-controlled arbuscule maintenance (Javot et al. 2007a, Yang & Paszkowski 2011). The $pt4$ phenotype was suppressible by nitrogen starvation (Javot et al. 2011), which indicates that nitrogen sources, delivered over the PAM (Guether et al. 2009, Kobae et al. 2010), could also trigger a program that promotes arbuscule maintenance. However, although there is recent evidence for symbiotic transfer of sulfate (Allen & Shachar-Hill 2009, Casieri et al. 2012), sulfate starvation did not restore arbuscule morphology (Javot et al. 2011). The mechanism by which arbuscule lifetime is regulated has not been clarified yet. Furthermore, it remains unclear how abscisic acid signaling, which has also been implicated in arbuscule maintenance, fits into the picture (Herrera-Medina et al. 2007).

A limitation in cell-specific carbon supply to the fungus may be associated with premature arbuscule collapse in $pt4 / pt11$ mutants (Javot et al. 2007a, Yang & Paszkowski 2011). The latter hypothesis is indirectly supported by transfer studies using radioactively labeled carbon and phosphorous isotopes that suggest a carbon-based rewarding system for efficient symbiotic phosphate delivery (Kiers et al. 2011). In agreement with these data, knockdown of a Medicago sucrose synthase gene expressed in arbusculated cells, as well as host-induced gene silencing of the fungal monosaccharide transporter MST2, caused the formation of stunted arbuscules reminiscent of those formed in the $pt4$ mutant (Baier et al. 2010, Helber et al. 2011). Under field conditions, diverse AMF species compete for plant carbon resources within the same root. The control over arbuscule lifetime could be a means for plants to promote the most efficient symbiont.

# Regulation of Arbuscular Mycorrhiza Colonization by a Fungal Effector

Effector proteins are secreted by plant-colonizing microbes and are generally thought to promote compatibility or to suppress plant defense responses by interfering with metabolism or signaling pathways. Kloppholz et al. (2011) recently discovered the first AMF effector, named secreted protein 7 (SP7). In assays using transgenic $M$ oryzae, they found that SP7 is secreted into the plant cell and localizes to the plant nucleus. SP7 interacts with ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR

(ERF) 19, as shown in yeast two-hybrid assays and by bimolecular fluorescence complementation following transient coexpression in tobacco leaves. Expression of SP7 in Medicago hairy roots led to an increase in AM colonization. A possible mechanism by which SP7 promotes AM colonization via a putative suppression of ERF19 action is in accordance with the inhibitory effect of ethylene signaling on early stages of AM colonization (Mukherjee & Ané 2010, Penmetsa et al. 2008). Fungal genome and transcriptome data indicate that the finding of SP7 is only the beginning of an imminent crescendo in the discovery and characterization of AMF effectors (Lanfranco & Young 2012, Tisserant et al. 2012).

Our knowledge about the molecular basis of AM is largely fueled by research performed with model species. However, AMF, although they display large host ranges, differ significantly in their quantitative performance on different hosts (Burleigh et al. 2002, Hong et al. 2012, Jansa et al. 2008, Walder et al. 2012). Identification of genes that determine agricultural benefits of AM, such as genotype compatibility and nutrient use efficiency, will require exploring an increased spectrum of genotype interactions with a range of symbiotic efficiencies. Exploring the natural allelic diversity of key genes will provide tools for selection of optimal genotype combinations for agriculture with reduced consumption of nonrenewable phosphate fertilizer resources.

Effector: protein secreted by plant-colonizing microbes for manipulation of host-signaling pathways or metabolism; effectors can initiate plant-defense reactions if recognized by plant disease-resistance proteins

# SUMMARY POINTS

1. Strigolactones derived from the plant induce hyphal branching and increased production of symbiotic signals by the fungus.   
2. Fungal $N$ -acetylglucosamine tetrameters and pentamers and lipochitooligosaccharides emerge as fungal signals that trigger symbiosis-related responses in roots.   
3. A plant LysM receptor candidate for arbuscular mycorrhiza fungus (AMF)-derived signaling molecules has been identified, RNAi of which leads to defective AM and root nodule symbiosis in Parasponia.   
4. The development of AM and the nitrogen-fixing root nodule symbiosis depend on common symbiosis genes. The encoded proteins are involved in either the induction or the production of intracellular accommodation structures.   
5. A prepenetration apparatus (PPA), formed across the plant cell vacuole in anticipation of fungal infection, predetermines the fungal growth route through the invaded cell.   
6. Common symbiosis signaling (as visualized through calcium spiking), the formation of the PPA, and subsequent fungal infection are spatiotemporally coordinated.   
7. Constitutive overexpression of deregulated CCamK induces mycorrhiza-related gene expression and PPA-like cellular rearrangements only in specific root cells, revealing the existence of preformed patterns that determine susceptibility to symbiotic stimulation.   
8. AM-defective mutants revealed at least five genetically separable stages of arbuscule development: $(a)$ PPA formation, $(b)$ cortical cell entry, $(c)$ birdsfoot, $(d)$ mature arbuscule, and $(e)$ collapsed arbuscule.   
9. Protein targeting to the periarbuscular membrane seems to involve a general redirection of secretory vesicle flow towards the growing tips of the arbuscle that occurs during the third stage of arbuscle development.

# FUTURE ISSUES

1. What is the physiological significance of arbuscular mycorrhiza fungus (AMF)-derived chito- versus lipochitooligosaccharides in signaling?   
2. How are nuclear movement and polarization of the host-cell cytoplasm during prepenetration apparatus formation achieved, and what is the precise role of VAPYRIN?   
3. How is the precise timing of expression of PAM-resident proteins such as PT4 regulated, and what is the precise origin and function of lysophosphatidylcholine?   
4. Which mechanisms regulate and execute inhibition of AM development at high- $\mathrm{P_i}$ levels?
