# Abstract

Auxin plays a pivotal role in the co-evolution of plants and microorganisms. Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzicola (Xoc) stands as a significant factor that affects rice yield and quality. However, the current understanding of Xoc's capability for indole 3-acetic acid (IAA) synthesis and its mechanistic implications remains elusive. In this study, we performed a comprehensive genomic analysis of Xoc strain RS105, leading to the identification of two nitrilase enzyme family (NIT) genes, designated as AKO15524.1 and AKO15829.1, subsequently named NIT24 and NIT29, respectively. Our investigation unveiled that the deletion of NIT24 and NIT29 resulted in a notable reduction in IAA synthesis capacity within RS105, thereby impacting extracellular polysaccharide production. This deficiency was partially ameliorated through exogenous IAA supplementation. The study further substantiated that NIT24 and NIT29 have nitrilase activity and the ability to catalyse IAA production in vitro. The lesion length and bacterial population statistics experiments confirmed that NIT24 and NIT29 positively regulated the pathogenicity of RS105, suggesting that NIT24 and NIT29 may regulate Xoc invasion by affecting IAA synthesis. Furthermore, our analysis corroborated mutant strains, RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29, which elicited the outbreak of reactive oxygen species, the deposition of callose and the upregulation of defencerelated gene expression in rice. IAA exerted a significant dampening effect on the immune responses incited by these mutant strains in rice. In addition, the absence of NIT24 and NIT29 affected the growth-promoting effect of Xoc on rice. This implies that Xoc may promote rice growth by secreting IAA, thus providing a more suitable microenvironment for its own colonization. In summary, our study provides compelling evidence for the existence of a nitrilase-dependent IAA biosynthesis pathway in Xoc. IAA synthesis-related genes promote Xoc colonization by inhibiting rice immune defence response and affecting rice growth by increasing IAA content in Xoc.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Endogenous plant hormones refer to trace organic substances synthesized within the plant, typically produced in specific plant organs before being transported to their target sites of action (Sun et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2019). These pivotal hormonal regulators intricately govern the sophisticated interplay of various growth and stress responses inherent to the plant organism. Auxin, the earliest-discovered plant hormone, engages in the comprehensive regulation of plant growth and development, with its principal active component being indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) (Korasick et al., 2013). Auxin primarily exerts its effects in tissues and organs characterized by vigorous cell growth. At the cellular level, auxin influences processes such as elongation, division and differentiation. At the organismal level, it induces differentiation in apical meristems, root tips and callus tissues (Ou et al., 2016; Tian et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2011; Zhou et al., 2010). Simultaneously governing phototropism in shoots and geotropism in roots, auxin also participates in the development of plant vascular tissues, root hairs and floral structures (De Smet & Jürgens, 2007; Woodward & Bartel, 2005). Moreover, auxin often functions as a negative regulator in plant innate immune responses. For instance, heterologous expression of AvrRpt2 in the mutant rps2 not only heightens Arabidopsis thaliana sensitivity to auxin but also enhances susceptibility to Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato (Pst) DC3000 infection by altering free IAA levels (Chen et al., 2007). GH3-8, on the other hand, acts by catalysing the formation of rice auxinamino acid conjugates, thereby restraining auxin accumulation. This protective mechanism shields plants from damage resulting from compromised cell wall defences and mediates a JA- and SAindependent resistance mechanism against Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae in rice (Ding et al., 2008). 

The biosynthesis of IAA involves both tryptophan-dependent and tryptophan-independent pathways. Similar IAA biosynthetic routes are also present in bacteria, mirroring those in plants, including tryptophan-dependent pathways encompass indole-3- acetamide (IAM), indole-3-pyruvate (IPA), tryptamine (TAM), tryptophan side-chain oxidase (TSO) and indole-3-acetonitrile (IAN) routes (Spaepen et al., 2007; Wang et al., 2015). Currently, the IPA pathway stands as the most extensively studied route for IAA synthesis. This pathway has been detected in a range of bacterial and fungal species, including Azotobacter vinelandii, Azospirillum brasilense, Bacillus and Pseudomonas (Spaepen & Vanderleyden, 2011). Research findings indicate that the knockout of the ipdC gene, involved in IAA biosynthesis via the IPA pathway in Sinorhizobium meliloti, led to a notable $5 7 \%$ reduction in synthesized IAA levels in the mutant strain RZ2MS9 (ΔipdC) compared with the wild-type strain in the presence of l-tryptophan (Spaepen & Vanderleyden, 2011). Studies focusing on the IAA biosynthetic pathway dependent on nitrilases are relatively scarce. Ensifer meliloti has the capability to convert the IAA precursor IAN into the product IAM. However, the absence of nitrilases capable of converting IAN to IAA and amidases capable of converting IAM to IAA renders the transformation 

of IAM into IAA elusive (Zhao et al., 2020). Whether there exists a pathway for the conversion of IAN into IAA through nitrilase activity in microorganisms warrants further investigation. In this study, we have identified two nitrilase (NIT) family genes, namely AKO15524.1 and AKO15829.1 in X. oryzae pv. oryzicola (Xoc), the causal agent of bacterial leaf streak disease in rice, bacterial strains. Furthermore, we conducted a comprehensive investigation into their gene functions. 

IAA displays diversity in mediating the interactions between plants and pathogenic microorganisms. The IAA produced by pathogens serves not only as a virulence factor aiding their colonization but also as a regulatory factor influencing the accumulation and signalling of auxin within the host (Kunkel & Harper, 2018). Upon the disruption of the iaaL gene encoding IAA-lysine synthase in Pst DC3000, a reduction in its virulence and colonization ability ensues, accompanied by a concomitant decrease in the activity of secondary metabolites within the host tomato plant (Castillo-Lizardo et al., 2015). Furthermore, the attenuation of auxin-responsive gene expression in Arabidopsis resulted in a notable reduction in the pathogenicity of Pst DC3000; conversely, the augmentation of auxin signalling transduction and biosynthesis, or exogenous auxin application led to a 20-fold enhancement in the colonization ability of Pst DC3000 (Navarro et al., 2006). 

Rice stands as one of the paramount staple crops worldwide, yet fungal and bacterial pathogens impose substantial annual losses on both yield and economy (Wu et al., 2022). Hormones play a pivotal role in the intricate interplay between rice and pathogens. The study reveals that X. oryzae pv. oryzae possesses the capability to synthesize IAA and follows the kynurenine pathway for this synthesis (Ansari & Sridhar, 2000). In the context of Magnaporthe oryzae, IAA production occurs through both mycelium and conidiospores, facilitated by the IPA pathway, mediated by tryptophan aminotransferase (MoTam1) and indole-3-pyruvate decarboxylase (MoIpd1) (Dong et al., 2022). However, it remains unclear whether Xoc possesses the capability to synthesize IAA. 

In this investigation, we have elucidated an IAA biosynthesis pathway in Xoc strain RS105 that relies on the nitrilase family genes NIT24 and NIT29. The IAA synthesized through this pathway serves as a virulence determinant during Xoc infection of rice. Deletion of NIT24 and NIT29 results in diminished IAA production in RS105, subsequently impacting extracellular polysaccharide secretion and pathogenicity. Both NIT24 and NIT29 play a role in suppressing immune defence responses triggered by Xoc in rice. This orchestrated modulation promotes heightened susceptibility of the host, facilitating Xoc colonization. In addition, it was found that the auxin produced by Xoc exhibited growthpromoting effects on rice and facilitated a conducive environment for its colonization. Our study lays a foundation for future explorations into the IAA synthesis pathway of Xoc, enhancing our comprehension of the pathogenic mechanisms regulated by IAA synthesis genes. These findings contribute valuable insights for further investigations into the intricate interplay between pathogens and host plants. 

# 2  |  RESULTS

# 2.1  |  Identification of NIT24 and NIT29

A plethora of bacterial species have demonstrated the ability to synthesize auxins or modulate plant growth by perturbing auxin biosynthesis. To elucidate potential IAA biosynthetic pathways in Xoc, we employed the BLAST website (https://blast.ncbi.nlm. nih.gov/Blast.cgi) to search the genome of RS105 for homologous enzymes involved in reported tryptophan-dependent pathways. We were unable to identify homologous genes associated with IAA synthesis in the previously reported IAM and IPA pathways in other bacteria within the RS105 genome. Nevertheless, we identified two homologous candidates of nitrilase enzymes associated 

with the IAN pathway in RS105, AKO15524.1 and AKO15829.1, which were subsequently designated as NIT24 and NIT29, respectively. To delve into the evolutionary context and sequence diversity of NIT24 and NIT29, we constructed a systematic phylogenetic tree and conducted comparative analyses using the neighbourjoining method for the nitrilase enzyme family. The phylogenetic tree divided these genes into two distinct categories: bacterial and plant. The results indicate that NIT24 and NIT29 are widely distributed within the genus Xanthomonas, implying its potential significance as a core factor in pathogenic bacteria. Notably, they also share homologous proteins in various plants, including rice, which suggests that the conservation of NIT24 and NIT29 is not limited to pathogenic bacteria but extends to beneficial species (Figure 1).

# 2.2  |  Participation of NIT24 and NIT29 in IAA biosynthesis in Xoc

To probe the potential involvement of NIT24 and NIT29 in IAA biosynthesis within RS105, this study engineered mutant strains lacking NIT24 (RS105_ΔNIT24) and NIT29 (RS105_ΔNIT29), alongside corresponding complemented strains. Liquid chromatography analyses unveiled intriguing insights, showcasing a reduction of $3 8 . 1 \%$ in IAA content for the RS105_ΔNIT24 strain, with an IAA content of $0 . 1 3 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ , relative to the wild-type (WT) strain 

(Figure 2a). Similarly, the RS105_ΔNIT29 strain exhibited a $4 9 . 1 \%$ reduction in IAA content, registering at $0 . 1 1 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ (Figure 2b). Notably, complemented strains successfully rescued the phenotypic defects observed in the mutants. Upon the addition of $1 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ tryptophan, the production yields of all strains exhibited a significant increase. Notably, the mutant strain RS105_ΔNIT24 displayed a $6 0 . 1 \%$ reduction in yield compared to RS105, while the production yield of the mutant strain RS105_ΔNIT29 decreased by $7 1 . 5 \%$ . The maltose-binding protein, MBP, was expressed and purified, as well as NIT24 and NIT29 carrying the MBP tag (Figure S2).

# 2.3  |  Impact of NIT24 and NIT29 on extracellular polymeric substances production in Xoc

To comprehensively delineate the functional ramifications of IAA biosynthesis in Xoc, the physiological and biochemical traits of Xoc were characterized using WT and the mutant strains. Intriguingly, the absence of NIT24 and NIT29 did not impinge upon Xoc's chemotaxis (Figure S1a), cellulase activity (Figure S1b), or biofilm formation (Figure S1c). However, the production of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) was markedly influenced, manifesting as a $2 4 . 2 \%$ reduction in RS105_ΔNIT24 (Figure 2e) and a substantial $5 3 . 1 \%$ decrease in RS105_ΔNIT29 (Figure 2f), relative to RS105. 

EPS, a predominant component of bacterial biofilms, assumes a pivotal role during the host–pathogen interaction, making it a crucial pathogenic determinant within the Xanthomonas genus. Juxtaposing the $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ IAA-treated strains with their untreated counterparts, a distinct enhancement in EPS production was noted. The production of EPS in RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29 strains treated with IAA increased by $2 5 . 1 \%$ and $3 3 . 7 \%$ compared with untreated strains, respectively. Notwithstanding, the EPS synthesis capacity of the mutant strains RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29 was partially recovered after IAA treatment, but the EPS yield of RS105_ΔNIT29 was still lower than RS105 (Figure 2e,f). Collectively, these findings underscore the pivotal role of nitrilase-mediated IAA biosynthesis in shaping the extracellular polysaccharide landscape of Xoc. 

# 2.4  | NIT24 and NIT29 promotes the virulence of Xoc

To delve further into the potential ramifications of IAA biosynthesis on Xoc's pathogenicity, we embarked on a series of inoculation experiments involving a 4-week-old wild-type rice cultivar, ZH11. Within this context, we introduced the WT strain, mutant strains and their corresponding complemented strains. Subsequently, we quantified lesion lengths at 10 days post-inoculation (dpi). Notably, the mutant strains, RS105_ΔNIT24 (Figure 3a,b) and RS105_ ΔNIT29 (Figure 3d,e), evoked markedly reduced lesion lengths upon rice, causing reductions of $3 2 . 4 \%$ and $7 2 . 7 \%$ , respectively, in comparison to the WT. The statistical results of the bacterial population showed that RS105_ΔNIT24 (Figure 3c) and RS105_ ΔNIT29 (Figure 3f) bacteria number was less than that of the WT at 1 and 5 dpi after inoculation, and the bacterial population of the 

complementation strains returned to that of the WT. Collectively, these findings underscore the role played by NIT24 and NIT29 in augmenting Xoc's ability to elicit pathogenic responses upon rice. Moreover, the foliar application of $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ IAA augmented the pathogenic potential of Xoc strains on rice (Figure S3a,b), with the reinstatement of pathogenicity in strain RS105_ΔNIT24 to levels comparable with the control group RS105 (Figure S3a). The above findings further underscore that the synthesis of IAA mediated by pathways NIT24 and NIT29 in Xoc is a determinant influencing the virulence of strains. 

# 2.5  | NIT24 and NIT29 influence the rice reactive oxygen species burst

Plant innate immune responses encompass the burst of reactive oxygen species (ROS), the expression of defence genes and callose deposition (Lu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2020). Employing the immune elicitor chitosan oligosaccharides (COS) as a positive control, we observed a significant inhibitory effect of IAA on the chitosan-induced ROS burst and callose deposition in both Arabidopsis (Figure S4a,b) and rice (Figure S4c,d). 

To probe the potential impact of NIT24 and NIT29 on rice's basal defence, we inoculated WT, mutant strains and their complemented strains onto rice cultivar ZH11. Following a 24-h period, we evaluated the rice ROS burst response. We found that within the sites of Xoc invasion, RS105 induced notable ROS production in rice leaves (Figure 4a). Conversely, mutant strains lacking NIT24 (Figure 4a) or NIT29 (Figure 4d) incited a more pronounced ROS burst in rice compared to RS105. Furthermore, we observed a significant reduction in ROS accumulation in leaves co-inoculated with IAA and the mutant strains RS105_ΔNIT24 (Figure 4b) or RS105_ΔNIT29 (Figure 4e). This suggests a link between the heightened capacity of mutant strains to induce rice ROS and a decreased IAA synthesis upon the loss of the IAA-related NIT genes NIT24 and NIT29. Previous studies have shown the relevance of the NADPH oxidase OsrbohB in rice defence, functioning as an indicator of intracellular ROS levels (Fan et al., 2018). Here, we examined OsrbohB expression and revealed heightened inducibility of OsrbohB expression in the mutant strains, consistent with the observed ROS levels (Figure 4c,f). 

To investigate the impact of Xoc and mutant strains on the expression of defence-related genes in rice cultivar ZH11, leaf samples were collected 8 h after inoculation with Xoc strains. Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was employed to measure the expression levels of the defence-related genes OsPAL, OsPBZ1 and OsPR2. The results revealed that, in comparison to RS105, the mutant strains were capable of inducing elevated levels of expression for these defence genes in rice (Figure S5). Taken together, these findings underscore the potential regulatory role of NIT24 and NIT29 in shaping Xoc-triggered innate immune responses in rice through modulation of their IAA synthesis levels, thereby exerting an influence on Xoc colonization dynamics.

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

During the ongoing evolutionary interplay between plants and pathogens, a dynamic equilibrium of reciprocal adaptation has emerged, where the development of plant resistance and pathogen virulence undergoes a nuanced interplay of tit-for-tat dynamics (Anderson et al., 2010; Sun 

et al., 2022). Plant hormones play a crucial role in orchestrating growth, development and immune processes, with auxin, as one of the earliest discovered plant hormones, holding paramount significance throughout a plant's lifespan. Notably, many pathogenic microorganisms possess the capacity to synthesize auxins, and can even employ IAA to influence and modulate plant physiological responses (Çakmakçı et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2008). However, certain genes and enzymes involved in their respective synthesis steps remain incompletely characterized, and especially investigation into the nitrilase-dependent IAA synthesis pathway in bacteria is comparatively scarce. 

Xoc is the pathogenic bacterial species causing rice bacterial leaf streak disease. The existence of an IAA synthesis pathway in Xoc was unclear to date. In this study, a comprehensive comparative analysis was conducted by aligning the complete genome of the Xoc strain RS105 with the currently annotated genes associated with enzymes in known plant IAA biosynthetic pathways. As a result, two nitrilase enzyme family genes, AKO15524.1 and AKO15829.1, were identified. We designated these as NIT24 and NIT29, respectively. The genes NIT24 and NIT29 exhibit a close evolutionary relationship and display conservation within the Xanthomonas genus. It is noteworthy that NIT24 and NIT29 also have homologous proteins in rice (Figure 1). We speculate that during the long-term coexistence of Xoc and rice, they have evolved homologous immune evasion

Several pathways for bacterial IAA biosynthesis have been reported, among them the indole-3-acetamide (IAM) pathway, the indole-3-pyruvic acid (IPA) pathway, the tryptamine (TAM) pathway and the tryptophan side-chain oxidase (TSO) pathways (Kumla et al., 2020; Patten et al., 2013; Sardar & Kempken, 2018; Spaepen & Vanderleyden, 2011). In this study, it was discovered that Xoc differs from the aforementioned IAA biosynthesis pathways. It can utilize nitrilase to synthesize IAA. The study revealed that the absence of NIT24 and NIT29 resulted in a reduction of IAA content within Xoc (Figure 2a,b). Furthermore, compared to the absence of exogenous tryptophan supplementation, the addition of exogenous tryptophan significantly increased the levels of IAA in RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29 (Figure 2a,b). Furthermore, NIT24 and NIT29 were directly confirmed in vitro to have nitrilase activity and IAN was the substrate of NIT24 and NIT29 (Figure 2c,d). The results confirmed 

the possible existence of a nitrile hydrolase-mediated IAA synthesis pathway in Xoc. 

Auxin participates in plants' responses to various biotic and abiotic stresses. It has been reported that bacterial synthesis of auxin may play diverse regulatory roles in the interactions between plants and bacteria (Fu et al., 2011; Yang et al., 2013). We generated mutants lacking NIT24 and NIT29 and further investigations revealed that NIT24 and NIT29 influence the production of EPS in Xoc. Exogenous IAA treatment was found to enhance the production of EPS. EPS has been reported as a crucial virulence factor in Xanthomonas, as well as being necessary for the formation of water-soaking and wilting symptoms caused by some plant-pathogenic bacteria (Slater et al., 2000). Further studies probed whether NIT24 and NIT29 affect the pathogenicity of Xoc. Inoculation experiments revealed that the absence of NIT24 and NIT29 resulted in reduced pathogenicity of Xoc on rice (Figure 3). However, exogenous IAA treatment restored the pathogenicity of the mutant strains to the levels observed in 

untreated RS105 (Figure S3), suggesting that bacterially synthesized IAA may act as a virulence factor during Xoc invasion. 

Continually evolving research has consistently indicated that the amplification of auxin signal transduction and biosynthesis in plants enhances the colonization proficiency of pathogens (Duca & Glick, 2020; Navarro et al., 2006). In cassava, the growth hormone signalling pathway orchestrated by MeAux/IAA exerts a negative regulatory influence on the plant's resistance to Xanthomonas axonopodis pv. manihotis by modulating the expression of genes pertinent to disease mechanisms, known as MePRs, during the plant's defence responses (Fan et al., 2020). This current investigation found that exogenous IAA, at a concentration of $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ , exerted a pronounced inhibitory effect on the triggered surge of ROS and the deposition of callose, both induced by chitin oligosaccharides, in both Arabidopsis and rice (Figure S4). Conversely, mutant strains RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29, characterized by reduced IAA synthesis levels, incited more pronounced outbursts of ROS and callose deposition upon their introduction to rice when compared to the RS105 strain (Figures 4 and 5). Upon co-inoculation of the mutant strains alongside IAA onto rice, the evoked levels of ROS and callose were markedly attenuated in contrast to their levels upon inoculation with single mutant strains (Figures 4 and 5). Furthermore, these mutant strains provoked a significant upregulation of defence genes within the rice (Figure S5). These findings underscore the ability of IAA to suppress the immune defence response instigated by Xoc in rice. This elucidates one of the plausible factors contributing to the reduced pathogenicity of the mutant strains: the decline in IAA secretion diminishes their capacity to hinder plant immunity during the infection process, consequently reducing their virulence on the plants. 

Auxin response factors (ARFs) serve as pivotal transcription factors within the auxin signalling pathway, regulating the transcription of auxin-responsive genes by directly binding to promoter elements known as auxin response elements (TGTCTC) (Hu et al., 2015). This study revealed that following the inoculation of Xoc strains, there was an upregulation in the expression of OsARF8 in rice (Figure 6a). However, when compared to RS105, the mutant strains RS105_ ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29 exhibited a reduction in the expression of OsARF8 in rice (Figure 6a,d). This implies that NIT24 and NIT29 affect the expression of genes involved in the IAA signalling pathway in rice. 

IAA, as a plant endogenous hormone, plays a crucial regulatory role in plant growth and development. IAA can stimulate cell division and elongation, enhance the growth of roots, stems, leaves, flowers and fruits, as well as influence responses to environmental stimuli (Hu et al., 2015). Xoc possesses the ability to synthesize IAA, and this study further confirmed that IAA produced by Xoc can promote rice growth and development. This influence is mediated by NIT24 and NIT29 (Figure 6b,c,e,f). Xoc's promotion of plant growth aids the pathogen in establishing a more stable habitat within the plant. Additionally, it results in the allocation of more resources by the plant to growth rather than immune responses, thereby weakening its resistance to the pathogen. 

In summary, the Xoc harbours an IAA synthesis pathway contingent upon nitrilase enzymes, where the synthesized IAA assumes the role of a virulence factor during the pathogen's invasion. This study postulates the involvement of NIT24 and NIT29 in positively modulating IAA synthesis within Xoc. The IAA generated through this pathway has a pivotal role in the infection process. To be precise, IAA secreted by Xoc possesses the capability to dampen the host plant's immune defence responses, including the ROS burst, the expression of defence genes, and the deposition of callose. Furthermore, NIT24 and NIT29 affected the expression of genes involved in the IAA synthesis pathway and affected the growth promotion effect of Xoc on rice. These, in a cascading effect, render the host plant more susceptible to infection, thereby facilitating the establishment of Xoc. 

# 4  |  EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# 4.1  |  Bacterial strains and plant materials

Xoc strains were cultured on PSA medium (polypeptone ${ 1 0 } \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ , sucrose $\mathtt { 1 0 g / L }$ , glutamic acid $\sf { 1 8 / L }$ , agar $\mathtt { 1 5 g / L }$ , pH 7.0) for 3 days at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ . The ZH11 variety of rice was cultivated in a greenhouse at a temperature of ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ with a $1 2 \mathsf { h }$ photoperiod. Arabidopsis was cultivated in a greenhouse at a temperature of ${ } ^ { 2 2 ^ { \circ } \subset }$ with an 8 h photoperiod. 

# 4.2  |  Construction of mutant and complemented strains

Primers used for vector construction of the NIT24 and NIT29 coding sequence are listed in Table S1 and were used to fuse upstream and downstream regions of the NIT24 and NIT29 coding sequence into the pK18mobSacB vector. Initially, $1 0 0 \mu \iota$ of competent RS105 cells were mixed with ${ 1 0 0 } \mathsf { n g }$ of recombinant plasmid pK18mob-SacB, and the mixture was transferred to an electroporation cuvette. Electroporation was carried out using an electroporation system. Following transformation, $5 0 0 \mu \ L$ of nutrient agar broth (polypeptone $\mathtt { 1 0 g / L }$ , yeast extract $\mathtt { 1 g / L }$ , beef extract $_ { 3 8 } / \mathsf { L }$ , pH 7.0) was added to the cuvette and the mixture was incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ with shaking for 2 h. The culture was then centrifuged at $3 5 0 0 g$ for 2 min, and the resulting pellet was resuspended in $1 0 0 \mu \iota$ of bacterial solution. The bacterial solution was evenly spread onto solid NAN medium plates (polypeptone ${ 1 0 } \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ , yeast extract $\mathtt { 1 g / L }$ , beef extract $_ { 3 8 / \mathsf { L } }$ , agar $\mathtt { 1 5 g / L }$ , pH 7.0) supplemented with kanamycin. The plates were incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ . Afterwards, individual colonies were selected, streaked onto solid NAS medium plates (polypeptone $\mathtt { 1 0 g / L }$ , yeast extract $\mathtt { 1 g / L }$ , beef extract $_ { 3 8 / \mathsf { L } }$ , sucrose $\mathtt { 1 0 0 g / L }$ , pH 7.0), and isolated to obtain the final mutant strains RS105_ΔNIT24 and RS105_ΔNIT29. 

The gene coding sequences of NIT24 and NIT29 were separately cloned into the pVSP61 vector. The resulting constructs were then electroporated into the competent cells of either RS105_ΔNIT24 

or RS105_ΔNIT29 mutant strains. Following electroporation, the transformed cells were subjected to selection using kanamycin resistance. Subsequently, the complemented strain was obtained through this process. 

# 4.3  |  IAA content analysis

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was employed for extracellular IAA content quantification in Xoc strains, performed by Suzhou Grace Biotechnology (Jiangsu, China). 

# 4.4  |  Determination of nitrilase activity

The coding sequences of NIT24 and NIT29 were ligated to the pMAL-C2X vector carrying the MBP tag and transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3). Proteins were induced with $0 . 3 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ β- -1- thiogalactopyranoside and the protein supernatant was collected by centrifugation to obtain MBP, MBP-NIT24 and MBP-NIT29 proteins. The obtained proteins were purified with the M5 HiPer kit (Mei5 Biotechnology). The purified protein, $5 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), 1 mM dithiothreitol, 3 mM ATP and $3 \mathsf { m M }$ indole-3- acteonitrile (IAN) were added to the reaction system. At the end of the reaction, $5 0 \mu \ L$ of methanol was used to terminate the reaction, and the IAA production was detected by IAA ELISA kit (Shanghai Enzyme-linked Biotechnology). 

# 4.5  |  Physiological and biochemical experiments of Xoc

# 4.5.1  |  Biofilm assay

Fresh cultures of Xoc strains were inoculated at a 1:100 ratio into glass test tubes containing $1 5 \mathrm { m l }$ of PSA liquid medium. The tubes were shaken at $_ { 3 \ g }$ and ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \subset }$ until reaching an ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 2.0. Afterwards, the tubes were removed and allowed to stand at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 24 h. The liquid containing the bacteria was gently aspirated from the tubes. The tubes were washed twice with $1 5 \mathsf { m l }$ of sterile deionized water and then stained with $1 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of $1 \%$ crystal violet dye. After 20 min of incubation, the dye was gently aspirated, and the tubes were washed three times with sterile deionized water. Subsequently, the tubes were air-dried in a $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ incubator. The presence of purple substance around the glass tubes was observed. This experiment was repeated three times. 

# 4.5.2  |  Extracellular cellulase assay

Freshly cultured Xoc strains were inoculated onto PSA plates containing $0 . 5 \%$ carboxymethyl cellulose using a sterile toothpick. After drying, the plates were incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 2 days. Then, an ample 

amount of $0 . 1 \%$ Congo Red dye was added, and the plates were left to stand for 30 min. Afterwards, the plates were washed twice with deionized water, followed by a 20-min wash with 1 M NaCl, which was repeated twice. The appearance of a clear zone around the colonies against the red background indicated the presence of extracellular cellulase. This experiment was repeated three times. 

# 4.5.3  |  Chemotaxis response assay

The bacterial liquid concentrations of Xoc strains were adjusted to $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 5$ . Sterile filter paper discs with a diameter of $2 . 5 \mathsf { c m }$ were placed on PSA soft agar plates containing $0 . 3 \%$ agar and no carbon source. A $2 5 \%$ glucose solution was added to the filter paper discs. A volume of $5 \mu \ L$ of the bacterial liquid was drawn up and vertically dropped onto the plate from 2 to $_ { 3 \thinspace c \mathsf { m } }$ away from the filter paper disc. After drying, the plates were incubated at ${ } ^ { 2 8 ^ { \circ } \subset }$ , and growth was observed every $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . This experiment was repeated three times. 

# 4.5.4  |  Extracellular polysaccharide assay

Xoc strains were inoculated at a 1:100 ratio into $1 0 0 \mathsf { m L }$ of PSA liquid medium containing $4 \%$ glucose. The cultures were shaken at $_ { 3 \ g }$ and ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ for approximately 6 days. The fermented liquid was centrifuged at $4 0 0 0 g$ for 10 min, and the supernatant was collected. The supernatant was filtered using a bacterial filter to remove residual cells, and then an equal volume of anhydrous ethanol was added. After thorough mixing, the mixture was allowed to stand for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ to precipitate. The precipitate was collected by centrifugation at 4000 g for 20 min and then dried at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ before weighing and analysis. This experiment was repeated three times. 

# 4.6  |  Bacterial inoculation experiment

Bacterial suspension was prepared by suspending the bacteria on the surface of solid medium in a $1 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ solution, and the optical density $\mathrm { ( O D } _ { 6 0 0 } )$ was adjusted to 0.5. Four-week-old rice ZH11 leaves were selected for the experiment. Using a needleless syringe, the bacterial suspension was injected from the backside of the leaves, ensuring that the liquid followed the leaf veins for infection. The inoculated leaves were placed in a ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ greenhouse for 10 days, after which the length of disease lesions was measured and recorded. The bacterial growth number of rice leaves was calculated according to the method on the first and fifth days after inoculation (Wu et al., 2022). 

# 4.7  |  ROS detection

A solution of $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g } / \mathrm { m L } 3 , 3 ^ { \prime }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) was prepared, and leaf samples were immersed in the DAB solution. The samples 

were subjected to vacuum treatment for 30 min using a vacuum pump, followed by exposure to light for 8 h. After this light exposure, the leaves were removed from the DAB solution and placed in absolute ethanol, then heated at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a water bath for chlorophyll decolourization. During this process, the ethanol was changed repeatedly until complete chlorophyll removal from the leaves was achieved. The decolourized leaves were then preserved in either ethanol or distilled water. 

# 4.8  |  Callose deposition analysis

$\mathsf { A } 1 5 0 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { K } _ { 2 } \mathsf { H P O } _ { 4 }$ solution was prepared and adjusted to pH 9.5. To this solution, $0 . 0 1 \%$ aniline blue was added to create the aniline blue staining solution. A phenol solution was prepared by thoroughly mixing deionized water, lactic acid and phenol in a volume ratio of 1:1:1, followed by the addition of twice the volume of ethanol to obtain a mixed solution. Leaf samples were placed into the mixed solution, subjected to vacuum treatment using a vacuum pump for $3 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ , and then heated at $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a water bath for $3 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ . Subsequently, the samples were removed from the mixed solution, washed three to five times with deionized water, and placed in aniline blue staining solution in the dark for $1 2 \mathsf { h }$ . After staining, the samples were washed with deionized water and preserved in a $50 \%$ glycerol solution. Callose deposition was observed using a two-photon fluorescence microscope, and the fluorescence area was quantified using ImageJ software. 

# 4.9  |  Gene expression analysis

Total RNA from rice was extracted using the E.Z.N.A. Plant RNA kit (Omega) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The extracted RNA was quantified and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . For cDNA synthesis, ${ 1 \mu \ g }$ of total RNA was reverse transcribed using the RTase III Primer Flexible All-in-One Mix (Yugong Biotech) and stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for subsequent qPCR analysis. Primers used for RT-qPCR analysis are listed in Table S2. All procedures used RNase-free reagents and consumables. For qPCR analysis, the $2 \times$ Universal SYBR Green Fast qPCR Mix (ABclonal) was used following the manufacturer's protocol. Technical triplicates were performed, and the relative gene expression changes were analysed using the $2 ^ { - ( \Delta \Delta C \mathrm { t } ) }$ method.
