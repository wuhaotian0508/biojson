# Abstract

Although germin-like proteins (GLPs) have been demonstrated to participate in plant biotic stress responses, their specific functions in rice disease resistance are still largely unknown. Here, we report the identification and characterization of OsGLP3-7, a member of the GLP family in rice. Expression of OsGLP3-7 was significantly induced by pathogen infection, jasmonic acid (JA) treatment, and hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ treatment. OsGLP3-7 was highly expressed in leaves and sublocalized in the cytoplasm. Overexpression of OsGLP3-7 increased plant resistance to leaf blast, panicle blast, and bacterial blight, whereas disease resistance in OsGLP3-7 RNAi silenced plants was remarkably compromised, suggesting this gene is a positive regulator of disease resistance in rice. Further analysis showed that OsGLP3-7 has superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity and can influence the accumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ in transgenic plants. Many genes involved in JA and phytoalexin biosynthesis were strongly induced, accompanied with elevated levels of JA and phytoalexins in OsGLP3-7-overexpressing plants, while expression of these genes was significantly suppressed and the levels of JA and phytoalexins were reduced in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants compared with control plants, both before and after pathogen inoculation. Moreover, we showed that OsGLP3-7-dependent phytoalexin accumulation may, at least partially, be attributed to the elevated JA levels observed after pathogen infection. # 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Rice blast, which is caused by Magnaporthe oryzae, and bacterial blight, which is caused by Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae (Xoo), are two of the most devastating rice diseases throughout the world (Qiu et al., 2007). To maintain growth and productivity, rice plants have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to fight these pathogens, including synthesis of myriad specialized small molecule metabolites such as diterpenoid phytoalexins and phytohormones (Ding et al., 2019). Phytoalexins are generally considered antimicrobial compounds and are structurally diverse, with different plant species possessing their own specific phytoalexins (Akagi et al., 2014; Murphy & Zerbe, 2020). Momilactones, phytocassanes, and oryzalexins are the most well-characterized diterpenoids reported to regulate disease resistance in rice (Akagi et al., 2014; Hasegawa et al., 2010; Yokotani et al., 2013; Zhu et al., 2016). Exogenously supplied momilactone significantly decreases blast pathogen infection of detached rice leaves (Hasegawa et al., 2010), and AAA ATPase lrd6-6 mutant rice plants, which produce increased levels of momilactones and phytocassanes, show enhanced disease resistance (Zhu et al., 2016). Moreover, the accumulation of momilactones and phytocassanes is remarkably inhibited in OsWRKY76-overexpressing plants, which experience decreased M. oryzae resistance (Yokotani et al., 2013). 

The phytohormones jasmonic acid (JA) and salicylic acid (SA) are the two most well-known plant hormones that function in regulating resistance against different pathogens. SA typically functions in plant resistance against biotrophic pathogens, whereas JA typically functions in plant resistance against necrotrophic pathogens (Bari & Jones, 2009; Yang et al., 2013). However, an increasing body of evidence shows that JA also plays a pivotal role in regulating resistance against biotrophic pathogens in rice plants. For instance, Yu et al. (2020) reported that a long noncoding RNA, ALEX1, positively regulates rice bacterial blight resistance via activation of the JA signalling pathway. Overexpression of ALEX1 leads to increased Xoo resistance accompanied with elevated levels of endogenous JA and JA-Ile. Additionally, the JA signalling pathway was also found to be associated with M. oryzae or Xoo resistance, which are conferred by many genes, such as OsWRKY13, Mitogen-activated protein kinase 15 (OsMPK15), and germin-like protein 2-1 (OsGLP2-1) (Hong et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2016a; Qiu et al., 2007). 

Germin-like proteins $( \mathsf { G L P s } )$ are a family of proteins that share $30 \% - 7 0 \%$ sequence identity with the wheat germin protein and possess diverse enzyme activities, including oxalate oxidase (OXO), superoxide dismutase (SOD), ADP glucose pyrophosphatase/phosphodiesterase (AGPPase), and polyphenol oxidase (PPO) activities 

(Barman & Banerjee, 2015; Cheng et al., 2014; Davidson et al., 2009). Furthermore, GLPs can also act as signalling molecules (Knecht et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 1998) or receptors (Gucciardo et al., 2007) and have been validated to play important roles in growth, development, and various biotic and abiotic stress responses in many plant species (Giarola et al., 2020; He et al., 2021; Pei et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Zimmermann et al., 2006). Many GLPs, which are localized in the cell wall, have been demonstrated to positively regulate plant disease resistance through modulating the endogenous levels of hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ produced by their OXO or SOD activities (Knecht et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2016a; Zhang et al., 2017). ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ can kill pathogens directly and protect the cell wall against pathogen penetration. In rice, 41 GLPs, whose genes are scattered across different chromosomes (chr), have been identified (Davidson et al., 2009). Thus far, however, the specific roles of GLPs in rice pathogen resistance have only been determined for OsGLP2-1 and GLPs on chr 8 (Banerjee & Maiti, 2010; Liu et al., 2016a; Manosalva et al., 2009). The functions of the other GLPs in rice disease resistance remain largely unknown. Additionally, associations between GLPs and diterpenoid phytoalexins have never been reported in rice pathogen defence responses. 

In the present study, we identified and functionally characterized the GLP gene Os03g0804500 (designated as OsGLP3-7) and demonstrated that this gene is involved in rice resistance to leaf blast, panicle blast, and bacterial blight. Through comprehensive biochemical, genetic, gene profiling, and metabolic analyses, our work revealed that OsGLP3-7 positively regulates rice immune responses through activation of the ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ , JA, and phytoalexin metabolic pathways.

# 2.2  | OsGLP3-7 is highly expressed in leaves and localized in the nucleus and cytoplasm

To better explore the role of OsGLP3-7 in rice immune responses, we first analysed its spatiotemporal expression patterns in different rice tissues. RT-qPCR results showed that OsGLP3-7 was expressed in all rice tissues examined, with expression being highest in leaves (Figure 2a). To further determine its expression patterns, transgenic Nipponbare rice plants expressing the pOsGLP3-7::GUS construct were generated for histochemical analysis. Consistent with the RT-qPCR results, the 

$\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) signal was identified in young shoot, root, leaf, stem node, and panicle tissues (Figure 2b). Interestingly, we detected the GUS signal in both branches and glume shells at the heading stage, but only in the branches at the booting stage, suggesting OsGLP3-7 expression is higher during the heading stage in rice. 

To examine its subcellular localization, OsGLP3-7 was fused with green fluorescent protein (GFP) and transiently expressed in rice protoplasts under the control of the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. Laser confocal microscopy analysis showed that the fluorescent signal was localized to the cytoplasm of rice protoplasts transfected with the OsGLP3-7-GFP fusion protein, while the fluorescent signal was localized in both the nucleus and the cytoplasm of rice protoplasts transfected with a GFP-only expression vector as a control (Figure 2c).

Higher ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations were also identified in OX-GLP3-7 relative to control plants under normal conditions and at 24 hpi (Figure 6a). In contrast, the ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ contents of RNAi plants were not remarkably changed, and the concentrations of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ were lower in RNAi plants relative to control plants at 48 hpi (Figure 6b). These results together indicate that the disease 

resistance mediated by OsGLP3-7 may partially function through modulation of the ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ content. 

# 2.6  | OsGLP3-7 regulates the expression of defence-related genes in rice

To identify the downstream components of the disease resistance conferred by OsGLP3-7, we conducted whole-genome transcriptional analysis of control and OX-GLP3-7 plants (OX-1 and OX-5 lines) at the three-leaf stage using high-throughput sequencing. A total of 172 up-regulated genes and 91 down-regulated genes were identified in both the OX-1 and OX-5 overexpression lines relative to wildtype plants $\| \mathsf { o g } _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $\geq 1$ and FDR $\le 0 . 0 5$ ; Table S2). Many of these up-regulated genes have been reported to be associated with plant disease responses, such as the pathogenesis-related (PR) genes, chitinase genes, and genes associated with diterpenoid biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis (Table S2) (Yokotani et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 2016). To verify differential expression of these genes, we selected six PR genes for RT-qPCR analysis in control, OX-GLP3-7, and OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants. The results demonstrated that the transcription levels of all six PR genes were significantly increased in OX-GLP3-7 plants but decreased in RNAi plants compared with control plants under normal conditions (Figure S7), further indicating the positive role of OsGLP3-7 in rice pathogen defence responses.

# 2.8  | OsGLP3-7 influences the accumulation of phytoalexins in rice

In addition to genes involved in JA biosynthesis, another striking induction was identified for genes that function in biosynthesis of diterpenoid phytoalexins (including momilactones, oryzalexins, and phytocassanes) in rice. RNA-seq analysis showed that in OX-GLP3-7 plants, the expression levels of three momilactone synthesis genes (MAS, KSL4, and CYP99A2) and seven genes involved in oryzalexin or phytocassane synthesis (CYP76M6/7/8, KSL7/10, KO4, and CYP71Z7) were strongly up-regulated compared with control plants (Table S2). The expression patterns of seven of these genes were confirmed by RT-qPCR in control and OX-GLP3-7 plants (Figure S12). Conversely, expression of these genes was significantly suppressed in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants relative to control plants (Figure S12). Similar results were observed for these genes in control and transgenic plants $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ after blast pathogen inoculation (Figure S12), suggesting that OsGLP3-7 may regulate the accumulation of phytoalexins in rice. To validate this hypothesis, seedlings of control, OX-GLP3-7, and OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants were collected for determination of phytoalexin concentrations. As expected, the contents of momilactone A/B, oryzalexin C, and phytocassane B/C were significantly higher in OX-GLP3-7 plants but remarkably lower in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants compared with control plants, both with and without M. oryzae inoculation at 24 hpi (Figure 8). For phytocassane A, an elevated concentration was only observed in OX-GLP3-7 plants relative to controls at 24 hpi, and no remarkable differences were observed between control and OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants (Figure 8). 

Because previous studies have reported that various plant hormones can influence the production of phytoalexins during pathogen infection (Akagi et al., 2014; Ko et al., 2010; Rowe et al., 2010), we speculated on whether altered JA levels can affect phytoalexin accumulation in OsGLP3-7 transgenic plants. To test this, we first analysed the expression patterns of seven phytoalexin biosynthesis genes in control and OsGLP3-7 transgenic plants before and after JA treatment. The results showed that JA treatment strongly induced expression of these phytoalexin biosynthesis genes in OX-GLP3-7 plants, but this strong induction was compromised in the OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants when compared to controls (Figure S13). Significant phytoalexin accumulation was also observed in OX-GLP3-7 plants relative to controls after JA treatment, whereas no remarkable differences in phytoalexin content were identified between control and OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants under JA treatment (Figure S14). These results together indicate an important role of JA signalling in OsGLP3-7-dependent regulation of phytoalexin accumulation during pathogen infection in rice.

# 3  |  DISCUSSION

A previous study showed that OsGLP3-7, a member of the GLP gene family, is responsive to Xoo and M. oryzae infection (Davidson et al., 2009). In the present study, we also demonstrated that the expression of OsGLP3-7 was strongly induced by leaf blast and panicle blast pathogen inoculation, and transgenics-based analysis clearly showed that this gene is a positive regulator in rice disease resistance. Overexpression of OsGLP3-7 enhanced resistance to leaf blast, panicle blast, and bacterial blight, whereas silencing of OsGLP3-7 made rice plants more susceptible to these diseases. Notably, OsGLP3-7 has also been demonstrated to colocalize with a quantitative trait locus (QTL) for brown plant hopper resistance in rice (Davidson et al., 2009), suggesting that this gene may also be involved in rice resistance to insect pests. In consideration of its potential broad-spectrum resistance activity to both pathogens and insects, OsGLP3-7 could possibly be employed as a valuable target in strategies to improve pathogen and pest resistance in rice. 

Phylogenetic analysis showed that OsGLP3-8 and OsOXO1– OsOXO4 are the five closest paralogs of OsGLP3-7. Except for OsGLP3-8 and OsOXO1, which are not expressed, previously reported results and our results presented here indicate that OsGLP3-7 and OsOXO2–OsOXO4 all positively regulate panicle blast resistance, and OsGLP3-7 also plays a role in leaf blast resistance, while OsOXO2–OsOXO4 have no functions in regulating leaf blast resistance in rice (Dong et al., 2021). This might be due to the different basal expression levels of the four genes. The basal expression level 

of OsGLP3-7 is much higher than those of OsOXO2–OsOXO4 in rice leaves (Dong et al., 2021). OsGLP3-7 showed a very similar character to OsGLP2-1, another member of the GLP gene family, which we reported previously (Liu et al., 2016a). Like OsGLP2-1, OsGLP3-7 was demonstrated to harbour SOD activity, and OsGLP3-7 overexpression could lead to ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation in plants. According to Carrillo et al. (2009), both OsGLP3-7 and OsGLP2-1 have many protein motifs, and the conserved motifs (motifs 3, 1, 7, 2, and 4) between them may govern the SOD activities. Notably, several different protein motifs were also identified at the N- and C-termini of the two proteins, suggesting that they may have other different regulatory roles in rice development and stress responses apart from blast and bacterial blight resistance. 

Similar to the resistance mechanism conferred by OsGLP2-1 (Liu et al., 2016a), OsGLP3-7 also exerts its function in disease resistance through activation of the JA signalling pathway. Expression of OsGLP3-7 was significantly up-regulated by exogenous JA treatment and the expression levels of JA biosynthesis-related genes were significantly induced in OX-GLP3-7 plants but repressed in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants relative to controls, both before and after blast inoculation. Additionally, JA accumulation was increased in OX-GLP3-7 plants but decreased in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants compared with controls, regardless of M. oryzae infection. OX-GLP3-7 plants were more sensitive and OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants were less sensitive to exogenous JA than control plants at the germination stage. These results together suggest tight associations between OsGLP3-7 and the JA signalling pathway in rice disease resistance. Interestingly, in our

recent study we also reported that panicle blast resistance, mediated by three OXO genes (OsOXO2, OsOXO3, and OsOXO4), may be involved in activation of the JA signalling pathway, as manifested by the significantly varied expression levels of LOX2 and AOS2 in transgenic plants (Dong et al., 2021). Because the OXO and GLP genes all belong to the germin protein gene family (Davidson et al., 2009), we speculate that employment of the JA signalling pathway in disease resistance responses may be a common phenomenon for the germin protein gene family based on our results presented here and in former studies (Dong et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2016a). 

In addition to the JA signalling pathway, here, we found the diterpenoid pathway to also be modulated by OsGLP3-7 during rice–M. oryzae interactions. Many genes involved in phytoalexin biosynthesis were remarkably up-regulated, accompanied with elevated phytoalexin levels, in OX-GLP3-7 plants compared to control plants. In contrast, these genes were significantly down-regulated, accompanied with decreased levels of phytoalexins, in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants. These results indicate that the disease resistance conferred by OsGLP3-7 is dependent on activation of the diterpenoid pathway in rice. This is the first report to dissect the associations between GLPs and the diterpenoid pathway in rice. More intriguingly, we also found tight associations between the JA signalling pathway and the diterpenoid pathway in OsGLP3-7-mediated disease resistance. Exogenous JA application significantly induced the production of phytoalexins in OX-GLP3-7 plants, whereas this induction was compromised in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants, indicating that OsGLP3-7- dependent phytoalexin accumulation may at least partially be attributed to the elevated JA levels observed upon M. oryzae infection. This result also further supports the conclusion that plant hormones 

can regulate the production of phytoalexins in response to infection by different pathogens, as demonstrated by previous studies (Akagi et al., 2014; Ko et al., 2010; Rowe et al., 2010). 

Except for diterpenoid phytoalexins, the flavonoid phytoalexin sakuranetin has also been demonstrated to play an important role in rice disease resistance, and pathogen-induced production of sakuranetin is JA-dependent (Yamane, 2013). To test if OsGLP3- 7-mediated disease resistance is also involved in the sakuranetin pathway, the transcription level of OsNOMT, a key enzyme for sakuranetin biosynthesis, was analysed in control and OsGLP3-7 transgenic plants before and after pathogen inoculation. The results showed that pathogen infection significantly induced the expression of OsNOMT in both control and OsGLP3-7 transgenic plants, and the expression levels of OsNOMT were remarkably higher in OX-GLP3-7 plants but remarkably lower in OsGLP3-7 RNAi plants than in control plants (Figure S15), indicating the potential significant role of sakuranetin in OsGLP3-7-mediated disease resistance in rice. 

Interestingly, we found no accumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ in OX-GLP3-7 plants 24 h after blast inoculation, whereas accumulation of JA and phytoalexins could already be observed in the same plants inoculated with the blast isolate at $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ . These results indicate that occurrence of JA and phytoalexins precedes ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ during blast infection in OX-GLP3-7 plants. Because JA can induce the accumulation of phytoalexins (this study), the order of occurrence of these signalling molecules in OsGLP3-7-mediated disease resistance in rice may be JA, then phytoalexins, and then ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ . 

In summary, in this study, we have confirmed through transgenic methods that OsGLP3-7 positively regulates leaf blast, panicle blast, and bacterial blight resistance in rice. OsGLP3-7 exerts its function 

in disease resistance by activating the ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ , JA, and phytoalexin metabolic pathways in rice. Our GUS staining analysis showed that OsGLP3-7 is highly expressed in young root and root tissues at the booting stage. Davidson et al. (2009) also demonstrated that expression of OsGLP3-7 is induced by salt and drought treatments. These results together suggest that OsGLP3-7 may also function in rice responses to abiotic stresses such as salt and drought. Moreover, how OsGLP3-7 coordinates with the other GLP genes to regulate rice disease resistance has not been studied. Further research will be required to address these issues. 

# 4  |  EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# 4.1  |  Vector construction and rice transformation

The coding sequence amplified from BC10 using the OsGLP3-7-OE-- F/R primers (Table S3) was cloned into PHQSN digested by EcoRI and BglII (Liu et al., 2016a), producing the OsGLP3-7 overexpression vector. A partial PCR product (216 bp) corresponding to the exon of OsGLP3-7 was amplified by the OsGLP3-7-RNAi-F/R primers (Table S3), excised by HindIII and BamHI, and inserted into the pRNAi-Ubi vector (Liu et al., 2016b) to produce the OsGLP3-7 RNAi plasmid. Moreover, an approximately 1.6-kb PCR product amplified using primers OsGLP3-7-GUS-F/R (Table S3) was digested by EcoRI and BamHI and inserted into pCAMBIA1381Z to construct the promoter analysis vector for OsGLP3-7. All above vectors were first electroporated into Agrobacterium tumefaciens EHA105 and then introduced into Nipponbare rice plants via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. 

# 4.2  |  Evaluation of disease resistance

The empty vector control (PHQSN) and homozygous OsGLP3-7 transgenic plants in the ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ generation were used for disease resistance analysis. Leaf blast resistance was evaluated by spray inoculation and punch inoculation methods as described previously (Ding et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2016a). Two-week-old seedlings were sprayed with M. oryzae GD08-T13 conidial suspension $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ conidia/ml) containing $0 . 0 5 \%$ Tween 20, and disease was assessed by measuring the DLA 6 days after inoculation, measuring 20 leaves per genotype (one leaf per plant). The DLA of each leaf was visually rated on a scale from 0 to 9, with 0 indicating no lesion observed and 9 indicating a lesion that covered more than $7 5 \%$ of the total leaf area according to the Standard Evaluation System for rice published by the International Rice Research Institute (5th edition, 2014). Leaves of control and transgenic plants were inoculated with GD08-T13 $( 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml) 6 weeks after sowing and lesion sizes were measured 6 days after inoculation. To evaluate in planta sporulation after punch inoculation, excised leaf strips containing a lesion spot were submerged in $1 5 0 \mu \vert$ of distilled water in a $1 . 5 – \mathsf { m l }$ microcentrifuge tube, and spores were counted under a microscope after the suspension was vigorously mixed (Ding et al., 2012). 

To determine panicle blast resistance, the cotton-wrapping inoculation method was employed as described by Liu et al. (2016b). Briefly, the upper-middle part of a panicle, 2 to 3 days after heading, was wrapped with sterile cotton soaked with $2 \ \mathrm { m l }$ of GD08-T13 spore suspension $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ spores/ml). An automatic water sprinkler system was used to maintain the humidity of inoculated plants. The infected main axis length was measured to evaluate disease resistance 3 weeks later. Lower concentrations of a conidial suspension $( 0 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ conidia/ml for leaf blast by the spray method and for panicle blast and $2 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ conidia/ml for leaf blast by the punch method) were tested to better distinguish the resistance phenotypes between RNAi and control plants. For Xoo resistance assays, leaf tips of control and transgenic plants were cut with scissors previously dipped in a Chinese Xoo race 4 (Liu et al., 2016a) bacterial suspension at an ${ \tt O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.5. This Xoo strain was isolated from Guangdong province, China. Lesion lengths were measured at 10 dpi, and more than 10 plants per genotype were sampled (five leaves per plant). 

# 4.3  |  RT-qPCR

Rice seedlings collected from at least five plants in each biological replicate were used for gene expression analysis. Total RNA extraction, reverse transcription, and RT-qPCR were conducted using the RNAiso Plus kit (Takara), the Primescript RT reagent kit (Takara), and SYBR Premix ExTaq (Takara), respectively, following the manufacturers' protocols. The rice $E F 1 \alpha$ gene was used as an internal control. Expression values represent the means of three biological repeats. The primers used in the present study are shown in Table S3. 

# 4.4  |  GUS staining analysis

The GUS staining assay for pOsGLP3-7:GUS plants was performed as described by Liu et al. (2016b). Plant tissues were incubated in staining buffer $( 1 0 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ sodium phosphate buffer pH 7.0, $0 . 5 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ ${ \sf K } _ { 3 } \sf r e ( C N ) _ { 6 }$ , $0 . 5 \mathsf { m M } \mathsf { K } _ { 4 } \mathsf { F e } ( \mathsf { C N } ) _ { 6 }$ , $1 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ EDTA, $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, and 1 mM X-Gluc) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ and then destained in $70 \%$ ethanol for photography. 

# 4.5  |  Subcellular localization of OsGLP3-7

The PCR product of the OsGLP3-7 open reading frame amplified from BC10 using the OsGLP3-7-GFP-F/R primers (listed in Table S3) was first digested by BamHI and XbaI and then ligated into a 35S-GFP vector to generate the OsGLP3-7-GFP fusion plasmid. Subsequently, the GFP-only and fusion plasmids were transformed into rice protoplasts as described previously (Zhang et al., 2011). The GFP signal was imaged by laser confocal microscopy (LSM710; Zeiss) 24 h after incubation at $2 6 \textdegree$ . 

# 4.6  |  Detection of SOD activity and measurement of ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$

SOD activity was analysed using the Total Superoxide Dismutase Assay Kit with WST-8 (Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology) following the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, leaves of different plants at the three-leaf stage were collected and ground in the proper sample preparation solution on ice. After centrifuging for 5 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , $2 0 \mu \up$ of supernatant, $1 6 0 \mu \vert$ of WST-8/enzyme working solution, and $2 0 \mu \up$ of reaction starter solution were immediately added to test tubes, which were incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 30 min. Absorbance was measured by a Multiskan Spectrum spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific) at $4 5 0 \mathsf { n m }$ in a 96- well plate, and the SOD activity was calculated using a standard curve. 

Endogenous ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ levels were measured using the same method as described by Liu et al. (2016b). Leaves of control (PHQSN) and transgenic plants at the three-leaf stage with or without blast infection were used for ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ measurement by monitoring the absorbance at $5 6 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . 

# 4.7  |  Analysing the enzyme activity of recombinant OsGLP3-7

To generate the recombinant OsGLP3-7 protein, the full coding sequence of OsGLP3-7 was cloned into $\mathsf { o E T } 2 8 \mathsf { a } ( + )$ (Novagen) via digestion by NdeI and BamHI and then transformed into Escherichia coli BL21. Induction of expression and purification of recombinant OsGLP3-7 protein were conducted according to the experimental protocol offered by Novagen. Subsequently, the purified recombinant protein was used for SOD activity analysis using the Total Superoxide Dismutase Assay Kit with WST-8. 

# 4.8  |  RNA-seq analysis

Seedlings of control (PHQSN) and OX-GLP3-7 plants (lines OX-1 and OX-5) at the three-leaf stage under normal condition were collected for RNA-seq analysis. High-throughput sequencing was conducted by Wuhan Huada Biotechnology Co., Ltd. Sequenced clean data were mapped onto the reference genome (IRGSP 1.0) using Hisat2 software with default parameters, and the expression levels of genes were calculated using StringTie software and the GTF annotation file of IRGSP. Differential gene expression was calculated using the EdgeR package in R and filtered with thresholds of $\lvert \log _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $\geq 1$ and $\mathsf { F D R } \leq 0 . 0 5$ . The raw data have been submitted to the NCBI BioProject database with accession number PRJNA808176. 

# 4.9  |  Determination of JA and phytoalexin contents using liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry

Rice seedlings collected from at least 15 plants in each biological replicate were used for JA and phytoalexin quantification. JA was 

extracted from rice seedlings according to Pan et al. (2010), the eluate was introduced into the electrospray ion source of a tandem triple quadrupole MS analyser (API4000; AB SCIEX), and then JA was quantified in multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode using optimized MS/MS conditions, as previously described (Hui et al., 2018). JA compounds in the sample were identified by matching the retention time and the MS spectra using authentic standards (Figure S16). Analyst v. 1.5.2 (AB SCIEX) was used to control the instrument and to acquire and process all the MS data. 

Extraction of phytoalexin from rice seedlings was performed as previously described (Yan et al., 2021). After redissolving in methanol, $4 \mu |$ of each sample was eluted using an HSS T3 column $( 1 0 0 \mathrm { m m } \times 2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ containing $1 . 8 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ diameter particles; Waters) with a flow rate of $0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m l / m i n }$ in an LC system (Dionex; Thermo-Fisher Scientific) and then subjected to an Orbitrap Fusion MS analyser (Thermo-Fisher Scientific), operating in heated ESI mode. The mobile phase A was water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, and the mobile phase B was acetonitrile with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid. The phytoalexin was separated by the following elution gradient: 0–1 min, $1 \%$ B; 1–11 min, $1 \% - 4 0 \%$ B; 11–13 min, $4 0 \% - 7 0 \%$ B; 13–15 min, $70 \% - 9 9 \%$ B; 15–16 min, $9 9 \%$ B; 16–17 min, $9 9 \% - 1 \%$ B; and 17–20 min, $1 \%$ B. The column temperature was set at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The spray voltage was set to 3500 V in positive ion mode, with the following ion source properties: ion source type, H-ESI; sheath gas, 45 Arb; Aux gas, 10 Arb; ion transfer tube temperature, $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ; and vaporizer temperature, $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Full scan data were acquired with a scan range of $m / z ~ 1 0 0 { - } 1 0 0 0$ in both positive and negative mode, with orbitrap resolution of 120,000. The automatic gain control (AGC) was set at 2E5 and the maximum injection time was set to $1 0 0 \mathsf { m s }$ . The RF lens was set to $60 \%$ , and the number of microscans was 1. All FTMS/MS data were acquired using the following conditions: isolation mode, quadrupole; isolation window, $1 . 6 ~ m / z$ ; detector type, Orbitrap; scan range, auto; AGC target, 5.0E4; maximum injection time, $3 5 \mathsf { m s }$ ; microscans, 1; orbitrap resolution, 15,000; first mass, ${ 1 0 0 m } / z$ ; data type, profile. The HCD collision energy was set to $30 \%$ , where the $\pm { \mathsf { H C D } }$ collision energy was set to $10 \%$ (Yan et al., 2021). Because we could not obtain authentic phytoalexin standards, we identified phytoalexins by characteristic ions in the MS spectra reported in previous studies (Hanai et al., 2005; Shimizu et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2018). Specifically, ions at 315.1955 represent momilactone A, ions at 331.1904 represent momilactone B, ions at 301.2162 represent oryzalexin C, ions at 317.2111 represent phytocassane A, ions at 335.2217 represent phytocassane B, and ions at 319.2268 represent phytocassane C (Figure S17). Xcalibur v. 4.1 (Thermo-Fisher Scientific) and Trace Finder v. 3.3 (Thermo-Fisher Scientific) were jointly used for LC– MS data analysis, including qualitative and quantitative analysis. 

# 4.10  |  Chemical treatments

To evaluate the effects of hormones or ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ on OsGLP3-7 expression, germinated Nipponbare seeds were placed on a tray covered 

with gauze and incubated in an artificial climate chamber at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ with 16,000 Lux and a 12:12 h light/dark photoperiod. At the threeleaf stage, seedlings were sprayed with different hormone solutions at concentrations of $1 0 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ or $1 \% \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 }$ . Samples were collected at 0, 3, 6, 12, 24, and 48 h after treatment for expression analysis. 

To evaluate the effects of JA on plant growth, seeds of control and transgenic plants were surface-sterilized with $70 \%$ ethanol and $2 . 5 \%$ sodium hypochlorite solutions and placed on 1/2 Murashige and Skoog medium supplemented with 0, 2, 5, or $1 0 \mu \mathsf { M }$ MeJA. After 4 or 7 days, the lengths of shoots and primary roots were measured and seedlings were photographed. 

# 4.11  |  Statistical analysis

Three independent experiments were conducted for gene expression, disease resistance, SOD activity, and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ content analyses. At least three biological replicates were performed for each experiment. Two independent experiments were conducted for measurement of JA and phytoalexin contents, and five biological replicates were performed for each experiment. All results are presented as means $\pm ~ S D$ of at least three biological replicates. Statistically significant differences were determined using one-way analysis of variance with Tukey's multiple comparisons test, which was conducted by the “multcompView” package in R $\cdot p < 0 . 0 5$ , Graves et al., 2019).
