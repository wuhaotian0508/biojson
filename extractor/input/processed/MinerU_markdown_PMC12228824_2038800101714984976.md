# H2S-mediated protein S-sulfhydration modulates infectivity and autophagy in the rice blast fungus

Hong Hu, Mengyuan Qin, Jiening Zhang, Jintao Jiang, Zhiqin Su, Lun Guan, Zhiguang Qu, Caiyun Liu, Xuan Cai, Zhiyong Ren, Yuhang Duan, Deyao Zhang, Hao Liu, Lu Zheng , Junbin Huang & Xiao-Lin Chen 

Hydrogen sulfide $\left( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S } \right)$ regulates cellular activities in plants and mammals through S-sulfhydration, a post-translational modification of proteins. The role of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ and its molecular targets in fungi, however, remains unclear. Here we show that ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ , synthesized by cystathionine γ-lyase (CSE1) in the rice blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae, is essential for optimal fungal infection. Excessive ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ , through S-sulfhydration, impairs fungal infectivity by inhibiting autophagy. Using quantitative proteomics, we identify numerous S-sulfhydrated proteins in M. oryzae, including the autophagy-related protein ATG18. S-sulfhydration of a cysteine residue (Cys78) in ATG18 is essential for its binding to phosphatidylinositol 3-phosphate, thereby maintaining the protein’s structural stability and regulating autophagy. Thus, our study reveals a mechanism by which ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ -mediated S-sulfhydration controls autophagy in the rice blast fungus and suggests the potential use of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ donors as a strategy to control fungal diseases by targeting fungal development and infection structures. 

Hydrogen sulfide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S } )$ has been recognized as the third gas signaling molecule after nitric oxide (NO) and carbon monoxide $( \mathbf { C O } ) ^ { 1 , 2 }$ . Emerging data over recent years suggest that it is as important as NO and ${ \bf H } _ { 2 } { \bf O } _ { 2 }$ . $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ is involved in many physiological and pathological processes in animals, and it regulates cell cycle, apoptosis, and oxidative stress. Additionally, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ has been implicated in various diseases such as cardiovascular disorders, neurodegenerative diseases, and cancer3–5 . In plants, $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ controls flowering, stomatal closure, and other developmental activities6,7 . More significantly, a plethora of recent studies have elucidated the regulatory properties of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ on drought tolerance, oxidative stress, and osmotic stress6,8,9 . However, the intricate regulatory mechanism of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ during these processes remains largely elusive. 

In mammals, endogenous $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ is mainly catalyzed by cystathionine γ-lyase (CSE1) and cystathionine $\beta$ -synthase (CBS), which are mainly present in the cytoplasm10,11. Mice with deletion of CSE1 gene 

exhibit a decrease in the ability to produce ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ , leading to a significant reduction in endogenous $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ levels in the heart, blood vessels, and many other tissues, ultimately resulting in the development of hypertension12. $_ \mathrm { L } \cdot$ -cysteine desulfhydrase 1 (DES1) is a component of the plant ABA signaling pathway in guard cells and responsible for cysteine degradation and the concomitant production of ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 1 3 , 1 4 }$ . The study of the Arabidopsis des1 mutant, which has a deficiency in producing $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ from cysteine in the cytosol, has revealed that ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ acts as an inhibitor of autophagy triggered by nutrient scarcity, furthermore, the deficiency of DES1 not only promotes the accumulation of the Autophagy Related 8 (ATG8) protein but also enhances its lipidation, thereby influencing the autophagy process15. 

S-sulfhydration, an emerging post-translational modification (PTM), is a key mechanism for cellular sensing of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ signaling13. This modification involves the covalent addition of thiol groups to cysteine residues on target proteins, forming persulfides16. As a

redox modification, S-sulfhydration can regulate protein function similarly to NO-induced S-nitrosylation, by inhibiting or activating proteins and affecting their activity and structure17,18, as well as their subcellular localizations19. High-throughput proteomics has identified ATG-related proteins such as ATG4, ATG18a, ATG3, ATG5, and ATG7 as susceptible to S-sulfhydration, highlighting its role in autophagy regulation15. ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ -mediated S-sulfhydration has been shown to inhibit ATG4’s proteolytic activity, negatively regulating autophagy20. 

Quantitative data reveal that S-sulfhydration, a persulfur-based signaling process, is prevalent in both animal and plant cells, indicating its universal role in cellular regulation16,21. This has led to increased interest in understanding its mechanisms. S-sulfhydrated proteins have been identified in these systems using biotin labeling and proteomics22–24. Up to $2 5 \%$ of mouse liver proteins, including actin, $\beta$ -tubulin, and GAPDH, are in vivo S-sulfhydrated16, and similar proteins have been found in plants. In Arabidopsis thaliana, at least $10 \%$ of the proteome may be S-sulfhydrated, participating in carbon metabolism, stress responses, and RNA translation25. Despite these findings, the role and regulatory mechanisms of S-sulfhydration in fungi remain poorly understood. 

Autophagy is a major catabolic process in eukaryotic cells that degrades organelles and proteins for recycling26,27. It has conserved functions in development, cell homeostasis, and stress response in plants, animals, and fungi. The autophagy process is characterized by the formation of a double-membrane structure called an autophagosome by the phagophore28. To date, more than 30 known autophagy related genes (ATGs) in yeast have been characterized29. Recent research has shown that ATG proteins are subject to various posttranslational modifications (PTMs), including phosphorylation, acetylation, and S-nitrosylation30–32. However, the specific impact of S-sulfhydration on these ATG proteins remains to be fully elucidated. 

Rice blast caused by the filamentous fungus Magnaporthe oryzae is the most devastating disease in cultivated rice33. During infection, M. oryzae has shown to develop a specialized structure known as an appressorium within the host, which forms a strong turgor inside and penetrates the host33,34. During this process, the regulation of autophagy-related genes in M. oryzae plays a critical role in both conidium cell death and appressorium maturation, which are essential for effective fungal infection35,36. In this study, we sought to determine the role of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ -mediated S-sulfhydration in the regulation of autophagy in M. oryzae. We discovered that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ -mediated S-sulfhydration negatively regulates the autophagy process in M. oryzae by modulating the binding activity of the ATG18 protein to its substrates. 

# Results

# Excessive $\mathbf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { S }$ is harmful to M. oryzae

${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ , a signaling molecule of equal importance to carbon monoxide and nitric oxide, may play a crucial role in the infection process of pathogenic fungi. To explore the possible role of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ in the infection process of the rice blast fungus, we utilized a donor of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ (sodium hydrosulfide, NaHS) and a scavenger of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ (hypotaurine, HT) to evaluate the impact of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ on the fungal infection structures and infection process. The rate of appressorium formation was not affected when $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ NaSH was used to treat the conidium suspension on the hydrophobic surface (Fig. 1a). However, it did affect the accumulation of turgor pressure in the appressoria and the formation of the septin ring (Fig. 1b–e), suggesting that high levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ negatively regulate fungal infection. Additionally, treatment with NaHS impeded the infection process of M. oryzae in rice (Fig. 1f–h). In contrast, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ Hypotaurine did not significantly impact appressorium formation but enhanced the development of infection structures during the infection process (Fig. 1a–h). Collectively, these findings indicate that excessive $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ is harmful to the development and infection of M. oryzae. 

# Identification of $\mathbf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { S }$ synthesizing enzyme CSE1 in M. oryzae

In order to investigate the function of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ in M. oryzae, we identified, MGG_10380, the homologous protein of cystathionine γ-lyase (CSE1) in M. oryzae. The CSE1 gene has a full length of 1869 bp and encodes a protein consisting of 426 amino acids. This protein contains a conserved Aminotran_1_2 domain (Fig. 2a), which is essential for catalyzing amino acid transfer reactions, engaging in metabolic pathways and synthesis, and preserving protein structure stability. Phylogenetic tree analysis reveals the conservation of the CSE1 protein across different species (Fig. 2b). Homology modeling comparisons revealed that the structure of CSE1 in M. oryzae is highly similar to that of the homologous protein in Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Fig. 2b). The qRT-PCR data reveal a notable decline in CSE1 gene expression during the appressorium development stages of M. oryzae (AP_3H and AP_12H), as shown in Supplementary Fig. 1. This suggests that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ potentially exerts a negative regulatory effect on appressorium function in vivo. 

Given that both excessive and insufficient levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ have an impact on the infection of M. oryzae, we successfully obtained two CSE1 deletion mutants (KO1 and KO2) through homologous recombination and PCR screening (Supplementary Fig. 2a–c). Complementary transformants (cCSE1) were successfully obtained by introducing CSE1, driven by its native promoter, into the Δcse1 mutant. Additionally, we created three CSE1 overexpression strains, designated as CSE1_OE-5, CSE1_OE-8, and CSE1_OE-12, to further explore their biological roles. qRT-PCR analysis validated the overexpression of CSE1 in the three CSE1_OE mutants, with transcriptional levels increased by about 30 to 40-fold compared to the wild-type strain (Supplementary Fig. 3). Moreover, the GFP-CSE1 fusion protein was detected in high levels within the cytoplasm throughout various developmental stages (Supplementary Fig. 4). This pattern of localization, which is consistent with that observed in animals and plants, further implies the protein’s role in diverse biological processes within the fungus. 

# CSE1 is responsible for $\mathbf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { S }$ production and S-sulfhydration levels of M. oryzae

In animals and plants, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ synthesized by cystathionine γ-lyase (CSE1) mediates S-sulfhydration and takes part in various biological processes within cells. CSE1 facilitates the transformation of cysteine (Cys) and homocysteine into $\alpha$ -oxopropanoic acid and $\alpha$ -ketoglutaric acid, respectively, and in the process, releases ${ \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { S } } ^ { 1 6 }$ . To determine if CSE1 similarly controls $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ production in M. oryzae, we utilized the 7- azido-4-methylcoumarin (AzMC) dye to assess ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ levels across different developmental phases of M. oryzae. Using confocal microscopy, we observed a substantial increase in fluorescence intensity in the CSE1_OE mutants as opposed to the wild-type strain (Fig. 2c, d). Consistent with this, compared with the wild-type, the ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ content of the CSE1_OE mutant was significantly increased at every developmental stage (Fig. 2e). Overexpression of CSE1 led to a roughly $20 \%$ decrease in substrate cysteine content relative to the wild-type (Fig. 2f). We also observed that the levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ in Δcse1 were significantly lower than those in the wild-type (Supplementary Fig. 5a–c). Moreover, the application of NaHS and HT led to respective increases and decreases in ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ levels in M. oryzae (Fig. 2g, h). Consequently, our findings confirm that CSE1 is the key enzyme for ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ production in M. oryzae. 

Previous studies have indicated that elevated ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ levels lead to increased S-sulfhydration modification37. To determine if $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ plays a similar role in S-sulfhydration in M. oryzae, we employed the tag-switch method38 to assess the difference in total S-sulfhydration modification levels between the wild-type and CSE1_OE mutants. The results showed that the S-sulfhydration level of total proteins in the CSE1_OE mutants was markedly higher compared to the wild-type (Fig. 2i, j), suggesting that CSE1 and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ contribute to the regulation of total protein S-sulfhydration in M. oryzae. # $\mathbf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { S }$ signal negatively regulates the autophagy process of M. oryzae

It has been demonstrated that elevated levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ are harmful to M. oryzae (Fig. 1). To explore the link between the reduced infectivity of the CSE1_OE mutants and $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ levels within their infection structures, we measured ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ levels throughout the development of M. oryzae appressoria. Our findings showed that in the wild-type, peak $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ levels occurred during the germination phase, with ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ signaling progressively moving from the conidia to the maturing appressoria (Fig. 4a, b). This pattern of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ migration was similar to that observed for reactive oxygen species (ROS) and reactive nitrogen species (RNS) signaling in appressoria. The CSE1_OE mutants, however, exhibited higher $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ levels than the wild-type during appressorial development (Fig. 4a, b). These results suggest that an overabundance of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ during appressorial maturation hinders the infection ability of M. oryzae. 

Given the impaired penetration ability of the CSE1_OE mutants in host cells, we hypothesize that their appressoria may not accumulate adequate turgor pressure for penetration. We found no significant difference in appressorium formation between the wild-type and CSE1_OE mutants (Fig. 4c), leading us to suspect that the appressoria of the CSE1_OE mutants might not be fully functional. To test this, we exposed the CSE1_OE mutants’ appressoria to varying concentrations of glycerol and measured their turgor pressure. The findings indicated that the appressoria of the CSE1_OE mutants were more susceptible to collapse under glycerol treatment (Fig. 4d, e), which suggests that CSE1 plays a role in negatively regulating the maturation of M. oryzae appressoria. 

Autophagy, similar to MAPK and cAMP signaling pathways, is a crucial process for appressorium turgor accumulation44,45. Given the defects in turgor accumulation observed in the CSE1_OE mutants, and the fact that sulfide generally negatively regulates autophagy across different species46, we explored the potential of CSE1 to regulate autophagy in M. oryzae. We introduced GFP-Atg8 into both the wildtype and CSE1_OE strains and subjected the transformed mycelia to nitrogen starvation (MM-N). Following a 5-hour incubation in MM-N, 

we noticed a reduced localization of GFP-Atg8 in the vacuoles of CSE1_OE cells as compared to the wild-type cells (Fig. 4f, g). This finding indicates that the autophagy process is disrupted in the CSE1_OE mutants. 

Additionally, we assessed the level of autophagy using western blot analysis. Autophagy can be quantified by determining the ratio of free GFP to the sum of GFP-Atg8 and free GFP (GFP/[GFP $^ +$ GFP-Atg8]). Upon treating the wild-type (WT) mycelia with nitrogen starvation medium (MM-N), this ratio notably increased from 0.31 at 0 hours to 0.82 at 5 hours. Conversely, in the CSE1_OE strains, the ratio only slightly rose from 0.32 at 0 hours to 0.59 at 5 hours (Fig. 4h, i). Collectively, these outcomes illustrate that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ -mediated S-sulfhydration significantly regulates autophagy in M. oryzae. Given the importance of S-sulfhydration in both plants and animals, our data imply that the role of S-sulfhydration in regulating autophagy is highly conserved among different species. To evaluate autophagy within appressoria, we analyzed the formation of autophagosomes in both WT and CSE1_OE strains. Initially, autophagosomes labeled with GFP-Atg8 predominantly accumulated in WT spores. Throughout appressorium formation and maturation, the number of autophagosomes marked with GFP-Atg8 was typical in the WT strain but considerably higher in CSE1_OE (Fig. 4j, k). We therefore propose that the accumulation of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ due to CSE1 overexpression leads to an increase in autophagosomes, ultimately impacting autophagic homeostasis. 

# Quantitative proteomics unveils numerous S-sulfhydrated proteins in M. oryzae

To elucidate the role of S-sulfhydration in fungal pathogenesis and to pinpoint the S-sulfhydrated proteins within fungi, we established a technology aimed at identifying proteins that have undergone S-sulfhydration. We profiled global protein S-sulfhydration modifications, and total proteins extracted from wild-type and CSE1_OE mutants were subjected to sequential processing. Free thiols were first alkylated with 1 mM IAA-PEO-biotin in darkness at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, followed by overnight tryptic digestion at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Peptide samples $_ { ( 0 . 5 \mathsf { m g } }$ per replicate) underwent two rounds of thiol-affinity enrichment using iodoacetyl resin to capture S-sulfhydrated peptides. After stringent washing, bound peptides were eluted and re-alkylated with 30 mM IAM for 30 min to prevent disulfide reformation. Finally, the peptides were analyzed using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) (Fig. 5a). Across three biological replicates, the number of quantifiable sites was 6986, with a total of 10348 sites identified, yielding a reproducibility rate of approximately $6 7 . 5 1 \%$ . In the WT group, the number of reproducible sites was 5925, accounting for about $5 7 . 2 6 \%$ , while in the CSE1_OE group, the number of reproducible sites was 7117, representing approximately $6 8 . 7 8 \%$ (Fig. 5b). We identified a total of 1054 S-sulfhydrated proteins (Supplementary Data 1), with 997 being upregulated modifications $( 9 4 . 5 9 \% )$ , and 57 being downregulated proteins $( 5 . 4 1 \% )$ . Among the targeted proteins we identified, there were 1482 S-sulfhydration modification sites, with 1419 being upregulated sites $( 9 5 . 7 5 \% )$ , and 63 being downregulated sites $( 4 . 2 5 \% )$ (Fig. 5c, d).

# S-sulfhydration at C78 in the autophagy-related ATG18 protein is crucial for autophagy and fungal virulence

Our quantitative proteomics analysis revealed numerous S-sulfhydrated proteins associated with the pathogenicity of M. oryzae (Fig. 5j). Many of these proteins, including those in the Pmk1-MAPK signaling pathway (Pmk1/C165 in the S_TKc domain, Mgb1/C170/C255 in the WD40 domain, MagB/C254 in the G_alpha domain), cell wall integrity signaling-related protein Mps1 (C166 in the S_TKc domain), Septin proteins (Sep4/C39 in the MMR_HSR1 domain), play roles in appressorium formation and maturation (Supplementary Fig. 11). The Tag-switch method verified that Sep4, Sep6, and Mps1 proteins were modified by S-sulfhydration in vitro or in vivo (Fig. 6a–e). Notably, the autophagy-related protein ATG18 (WD40 domain) was identified as S-sulfhydrated (Fig. 6f, g). To explore the role of ATG18 S-sulfhydration, we generated a mutant strain Δatg18/ATG18C78A (C78A), confirming that C78 was indeed an S-sulfhydration site in vivo (Fig. 6h, i). We obtained the recombinant ATG18 protein expressed in E. coli. Fully annotated MS/MS spectra revealed an increase in mass of 32 Da at the Cys78 site of M. oryzae ATG18, indicating the presence of S-sulfhydration modification at the Cys78 residue (Supplementary Fig. 12). 

Studies have shown that deleting the autophagy-related gene ATG18 in M. oryzae leads to autophagy defects and reduced pathogenicity36,47,48. Our analyses found that S-sulfhydration of the Atg18 protein did not significantly impact fungal growth rate or spore production (Supplementary Fig. 13a–c). Further investigation into the role of Atg18 S-sulfhydration in M. oryzae’s pathogenicity involved examining appressorium function and pathogenicity in wild-type, C78A mutant, and ATG18-C strains. We found that Atg18 S-sulfhydration did not greatly affect appressorium formation 

(Supplementary Fig. 14a). However, mutating the C78 site resulted in a significantly lower appressorium collapse rate compared to the WT and complemented strains (Supplementary Fig. 14b, c), suggesting the S-sulfhydration site on Atg18 is essential for appressorium function. Pathogenicity tests on barley and rice indicated increased pathogenicity post-C78 site mutation (Fig. 6j, k). 

To delve deeper into how Atg18 S-sulfhydration affects appressorium maturation and pathogenicity in M. oryzae, we examined Atg18’s localization across different strains. In wild-type strains, Atg18- GFP exhibited characteristic punctate cytoplasmic localization (Fig. 6l), notably, exogenous $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ treatment significantly attenuated this punctate distribution pattern, while in the C78A mutant, it was exclusively found in vacuoles (Fig. 6l). This indicates that the C78A mutation might alter Atg18’s localization, potentially speeding up autophagy. We then quantified autophagy levels in both wild-type and C78A strains, discovering that autophagosomes in the C78A mutant were prevalent in vacuoles and had initiated autophagy just two hours post-nitrogen deprivation (Fig. 6m, n). Western blot analysis corroborated these findings (Fig. 6o, p). From these results, we infer that the C78 mutation in Atg18 might trigger premature autophagy, leading to early appressorium maturation and heightened pathogenicity.

# Exogenous $\mathbf { H _ { 2 } S }$ donor NaHS broadly inhibits fungal pathogenicity

$\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ has diverse roles in plant physiology, and the exogenous application of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ is known to be involved in plant development and stress response processes, as well as promoting plant growth49. Despite this, the specific part $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ plays in preventing and controlling plant pathogenic fungi remains unclear. To determine if targeting the $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ signaling pathway could lead to the development of broad-spectrum fungal disease control strategies, we assessed the impact of the ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ donor NaHS on the growth of M. oryzae. Our findings indicated that NaHS in various concentrations had distinct inhibitory effects on hyphal growth of M. oryzae (Fig. 8a-c). Next, we evaluated the effect of NaHS on fungal pathogenicity by incorporating various concentrations of NaHS into the wild-type strain’s conidial suspension and performing spray experiments on barley and rice. Our observations revealed that escalating NaHS concentrations led to a notable decrease in the number and size of lesions on both rice and barley leaves (Fig. 8d, e). Furthermore, when NaHS-treated conidia were inoculated onto wounded rice leaves, the spread and size of the lesions were substantially curbed (Fig. 8f). As a control, exogenous hypotaurine treatment enhanced the pathogenicity of M. oryzae on rice (Fig. 8g). To broaden our investigation, we tested the inhibitory impact of NaHS on a spectrum of pathogenic fungi using diverse concentrations. The results demonstrated that NaHS, across various doses, effectively inhibited multiple pathogenic fungi (Supplementary Fig. 17a–c). These discoveries suggest that NaHS, at specific concentrations, can significantly hinder the pathogenicity of a wide array of fungi.

Research has demonstrated that S-sulfhydration and S-nitrosylation can exert opposing effects on protein function, as seen with ascorbate peroxidase and GAPDH22. Aroca et al. devised a comparative and quantitative proteomic method to detect endogenous S-sulfhydrated proteins, identifying 2330 potential targets in both Col-0 and des1 mutant leaves13,25. In plants, these S-sulfhydrated proteins 

are predominantly found in the cytosol and chloroplasts and are implicated in processes such as carbon metabolism, stress responses, growth, development, and immunity. For M. oryzae, KEGG and GO analyses revealed that S-sulfhydrated proteins are mainly located in the mitochondria and cytosol, where they are involved in transmembrane transport, protein localization, biosynthesis, and energy metabolism. Our proteomic analysis specifically identified ATG18 in the total proteome, suggesting that S-sulfhydration could be a unique 

mechanism for sulfide regulation of fungal autophagy. Further targeted research is required to uncover additional molecular protein targets related to autophagy. This work highlights the potential of S-sulfhydration in modulating key biological processes and its significance in fungal pathogenicity and autophagy regulation. 

Plant pathogenic fungi employ the appressorium, a specialized infection structure, to penetrate plant tissues using the turgor pressure it generates, which aids in breaking through the leaf cuticle. Autophagy is increasingly recognized as a key factor in appressorium maturation. While several autophagy regulators in fungi, such as the Snf1 kinase complex, TOR kinase, and ubiquitin-like systems, are well characterized, the role of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ remains less understood. In animal systems, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ can either inhibit or activate autophagy, depending on the disease context60,61. In plants, like Arabidopsis, ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ has been shown to negatively regulate autophagy as a signaling molecule, independent of nutritional status15,62. Our study reveals that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ signaling negatively modulates fungal autophagy, which is vital for appressorium development and invasion. We have identified the autophagy-related protein ATG18 as a critical regulator of autophagy. S-sulfhydration at the C78 site of ATG18 alters its binding affinity to substrates, impacting autophagosome homeostasis and, consequently, the initiation of autophagy. This finding underscores the importance of $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ -mediated post-translational modifications in the regulation of fungal autophagy and pathogenicity. 

ATG18, a key component among the 41 characterized ATG proteins, plays a central role in autophagy as evidenced by studies in yeast and Arabidopsis47,63. The Atg2-Atg18 complex is crucial for the precise regulation of autophagy and for autophagosome formation, with a critical interaction between Atg18 and PtdIns(3)P63,64. Proteomic analysis in M. oryzae has revealed ATG18 as the unique target for S-sulfhydration. Assays both in vivo and in vitro have established that the Cys78 residue undergoes reversible S-sulfhydration, which significantly influences ATG18’s binding to phospholipids. This modification enhances ATG18’s affinity for PtdIns(3)P, as shown by in vitro phospholipid membrane binding assays, leading to an increase in autophagosome number and size in CSE1 overexpressing mutants (Fig. 4). The binding of ATG18 to phospholipids is essential for autophagosome maturation and stability. A mutation at the S-sulfhydration site, such as the C78A mutation, weakens ATG18’s interaction with phospholipids by disrupting membrane binding. This leads to a faster release from the autophagosome membrane, thereby accelerating the autophagic process and enhancing the maturation of the mutant strain. This indicates that the reversible S-sulfhydration of Cys78 is a critical regulatory mechanism in the autophagy pathway. 

PTMs are known to induce protein structural changes, affecting protein conformation65. This suggests that S-sulfhydration could influence protein structure in pathogenic fungi, prompting our investigation into its structural dynamics and molecular mechanisms. 3D structural modeling showed that simulating S-sulfhydration of ATG18 by adding an -SH group to the wild-type protein negatively impacts its conformation. S-sulfhydration induced structural changes between residues I68 to C78, destabilizing ATG18 and potentially affecting substrate recognition and enzymatic activity (Fig. 7a–d). However, this modification also enhanced ATG18’s binding to PtdIns(3)P. The catalytic cysteine residue in ATG18 is highly conserved across fungi (Fig. 7i), indicating that S-sulfhydration of ATG18 might be a conserved PTM in fungal systems, and this information provides a new perspective for the green prevention and control of diseases. Our work uncovers a novel molecular-level PTM that regulates autophagy in plant pathogenic fungi, suggesting a regulatory mechanism where $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ -related S-sulfhydration alters the structure and activity of proteins in the fungal system. 

Historically viewed as a toxic gas, $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ is now recognized for its beneficial effects on the growth and development of plants and 

animals. Given the volatile nature of NaHS, which readily permeates cell walls and membranes, intracellular ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ measurements likely capture only a fraction of its dynamic flux. Our results demonstrate that NaHS treatment significantly attenuates M. oryzae pathogenicity on rice. This effect correlates with elevated ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ levels in plant tissues, suggesting that exogenous NaHS may enhance endogenous ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ biosynthesis to bolster host resistance66. Additionally, Zhang et al. found that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ induces the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in rice66. Thus, the observed ROS accumulation in plant cells in our study may be associated with $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ released by the pathogen. While $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ has been shown to inhibit mycelial growth and pathogenicity in some fruit pathogens, its role in controlling rice pathogens was less understood. We found that $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ -induced S-sulfhydration at specific concentrations delays the onset of autophagy, impairing the functions of fungal infection structures. This mechanism may be conserved in NaHS’s action against other fungal diseases. ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ donors are inexpensive, readily available, and highly water-soluble, making them easily absorbed by plants. They can be applied through foliar fertilization or combined with commercial fungicides, offering a cost-effective strategy to improve crop tolerance to abiotic stresses and disease resistance. The use of exogenous ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ as a treatment may be a promising approach for crop disease control. However, safety evaluations of NaHS for humans and the environment are necessary. Developing greener and more efficient $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ donors and exploring fungal-specific inhibitors based on the $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ signaling pathway could lead to innovative, sustainable agricultural solutions. 

# Methods

# Fungal strains and culture conditions

The wild-type strain P131 of M. oryzae served as the control and the parental strain for the mutants generated in this study. Details of the strains are provided in Supplementary Data 2. Throughout the study, all strains were cultured on oatmeal tomato agar (OTA) at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and monitored for growth. Dried mycelial filter paper sheets were kept at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The susceptibility of the strains to ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ stress induced by sodium hydrosulfide (NaHS, Sigma-Aldrich, USA) was assessed at various concentrations. 

# RNA extraction and qRT-PCR analysis

To examine CSE1 gene expression in M. oryzae, total RNA was extracted from samples at different developmental stages: mycelium, conidia, appressoria at 3 and 12 hours, and invasive hyphae at 18, 24, and 48 hpi. Subsequently, cDNA was synthesized for qRT-PCR analysis using a SYBR Green PCR Master Mix Kit (Takara, Dalian, China) on an ABI 7500 real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). 

# Gene knockout and overexpression

The CSE1, DES1, and ATG18 genes were knocked out using a split PCRmediated gene deletion strategy as described by Goswami67. For the overexpression of CSE1, the CSE1 coding sequence was cloned under the control of the ptripc promoter on the YIP102 vector. Transformants were selected for glufosinate resistance and confirmed by qRT-PCR. The plasmids and primers used in this study are provided in Supplementary Data 3 and 4. 

# Rice sheath infection and pathogenicity tests

Sheaths were prepared using 30-day-old rice seedlings (Oryza sativa japonica cv LTH), and conidial suspensions $( 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ spores ml-1) were inoculated into rice sheaths and incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ in the dark until different time points. The inoculated sheaths were manually trimmed, and the invasive hyphae expansion was observed by light microscopy (Ni90, Nikon). Pathogenicity was determined in rice and barley seedlings using a $1 \times 1 0 ^ { 5 } \mathrm { m l ^ { - 1 } }$ conidial suspension. After 5 d of humidified incubation, the emergent disease symptoms were observed and counted. 

# Staining assays

Mycelia were stained with $1 0 \mu \ g \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 }$ CFW (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) for 2 minutes and analyzed under a fluorescence microscope (Ni90, Nikon) to measure cell length. For ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf S }$ visualization, Samples of M. oryzae at different developmental stages were stained with $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ of a specific $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S }$ fluorescent probe 7-azido-4-methylcoumarin (AzMc, MedChemExpress, China), incubated in the dark for 30 min, and then washed three times. Photographs were taken under a laser confocal microscope (TCS SP8, Leica Microsystems, Germany). Intracellular ROS levels were measured in M. oryzae using $5 0 { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ dihydrorhodamine as a ROS-specific fluorescent probe. Fluorescence intensity is indicated by ImageJ. The lower epidermis of hosts inoculated with M. oryzae was stained with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m l ^ { - 1 } }$ DAB (pH 3.8; Sigma-Aldrich) for 8 h, and then decoloured with a decolourising solution (ethanol: acetic acid; 94:4) for 6 h before being observed and photographed under a microscope. 

# Western blot and autophagy analysis

Approximately 0.1 g of M. oryzae tissue was ground to powder in liquid nitrogen and resuspended in 1 ml of extraction buffer $\mathrm { 1 0 } \mathrm { m M }$ Tris-HCl [pH 7.5], 150 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM EDTA, $0 . 5 \%$ Triton X-100) with 1 mM PMSF (Sigma-Aldrich). Total or in vitro purified proteins were then separated on $10 \%$ SDS-PAGE gels. Primary antibodies used in this study included anti-Biotin antibody (1:2000; 5597 s, Cell Signaling Technology, USA), anti-GFP antibody (1:5000; MA5-15256, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA), anti-GST antibody (1:5000; EPR8263, Abcam, UK), antimCherry antibody (1:5000; EPR20579, Abcam, UK), and anti-GAPDH antibody (1:5000; B3029, Biodragon, China). Secondary antibodies were horseradish peroxidase-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG (1:10000; AB2102, Coolaber, China) and horseradish peroxidaseconjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:10000; ab6712, Abcam, UK). For autophagy analysis, mycelia of WT/GFP:ATG8 and CSE1_OE/ GFP:ATG8 strains were cultured in CM liquid medium at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ and 160 rpm for 40 h. Then the mycelia were transferred to MM-N medium and induced for 0 h, 2 h, and 5 h. Total proteins were extracted with IP cell lysates (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) and separated by SDS-PAGE. Immunoblotting was performed with anti-GFP primary antibody, and quantified using ImageJ software. The number and size of autophagosomes were observed by GFP:ATG8 fluorescence under a laser confocal microscope. 

# Determination of GSH, GSSG, and Cys content

Mycelium samples from WT and CSE1_OE mutants were incubated in liquid CM for $4 0 \mathrm { h }$ . Collected samples were assayed using Reduced glutathione (GSH) content assay kit or Oxidized glutathione (GSSG) content assay kit (Solarbio, China) according to the manufacturer’s recommendations, and the content was determined using a Thermo Scientific Multiskan FC, ${ \mathrm { O D } } = 5 9 3 { \mathrm { n m } }$ , colourimetric microtiter reader. The Cys content was determined by calculating the absorbance at $6 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ using a cysteine (Cys) content assay kit (Solarbio, China) according to the manufacturer’s recommendations. 

# Protein expression and purification

The coding sequences of ATG18, MPS1, and SEP4 were amplified from the cDNA of M. oryzae and fused to GST and cloned into pGEX-4T-1. To express the proteins, transformed E. coli BL21 (DE3, Weidi Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) cell cultures were treated with 1 mM IPTG (Sigma-Aldrich) at an optical density of 0.6 at $6 0 0 \mathsf { n m }$ and incubated at ${ 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 6 h. Purification was performed by GST resin binding using the GST-Bind Kit (Novagen) under non-denaturing conditions according to the manufacturer’s recommendations.

# Data analysis

Proteins were identified by querying the complete genome sequence of M. oryzae strain 70-15 (http://fungi.ensembl.org/Magnaporthe_ oryzae/Info/Index). The proteins were then functionally categorized according to cellular component, molecular function, and biological process. Pathway annotations were performed using the KEGG database (https://www. genome.jp/kegg/). The differentially expressed proteins identified from the comparison group were matched to their respective database identifiers or protein sequences. # Molecular dynamics simulations

The three-dimensional structures of all proteins used in this study were obtained from AlphaFold-predicted models available in the UniProt database (https://www.uniprot.org/). The structure of ATG18 was predicted using the AlphaFold3 server (https://alphafoldserver. com/fold/58ebde1f098e550c). To simulate the S-sulfhydration of ATG18, covalent docking between ATG18 and an –SH group was performed using the ADFR software suite. The size of the simulation box was set to ensure that each protein atom was more than $1 . 0 \mathsf { n m }$ from the box. Molecular dynamics simulations were conducted under isothermal (300 K) and isobaric (1 bar) conditions using the Amber99sb-ildn force field. The system was solvated with TIP3P water molecules, and charge neutrality was achieved by incorporating an appropriate number of $\mathrm { N } \mathbf { a } ^ { + }$ counterions into the simulation box. Initially, the molecular dynamics system underwent energy minimization using the steepest descent method. This was followed by 100,000 steps of NVT (constant number of particles, volume, and temperature) equilibration and 100,000 steps of NPT (constant number of particles, pressure, and temperature) equilibration, with a coupling time constant of 0.1 ps and a total duration of 100 ps for both equilibration phases. Subsequently, a production run of free molecular dynamics 

was performed for a total of 5,000,000 steps, with a time step of 2 fs, resulting in a total simulation time of 100 ns. Upon completion of the simulations, trajectory analysis was carried out using the built-in tools of Gromacs. The root mean square deviation (RMSD), root mean square fluctuation (RMSF), and radius of gyration of the protein were calculated, along with the free energy landscape. Three independent molecular dynamics simulations were conducted, and the results were similar. The initial coordinates, simulation input files, and final output coordinate files have been included in the Source data file. 

# Statistical analysis

All graphs were generated using Microsoft Excel (2021) or GraphPad Prism 8 software. For bar plots, data are represented as means ± standard deviation (SD) with datapoints shown as dots; Boxplots show the maxima, minima, interquartile range, and median. Statistical significance was determined by Student’s t test or one-way ANOVA, followed by Tukey post hoc tests. Differences were considered statistically significant when the $p$ -value is ${ < } 0 . 0 5 .$ . All experiments were repeated at least three times, and all experiments were conducted using multiple biological replicates with similar results. No data were excluded from the analyses. The investigators were not blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment.
