# ABSTRACT

B-box (BBX) transcription factors are emerging as pivotal regulators of environmental adaptation and developmental plasticity in plants. These proteins act at the intersection of light, hormonal and stress signalling networks to modulate key processes, including photomorphogenesis, circadian rhythm regulation, abiotic and biotic stress responses, anthocyanin biosynthesis and flowering time control. Recent studies in various model species and crops have revealed that BBX proteins can function as both activators and repressors of transcription, often by directly interacting with key regulators such as HY5, PRR9/7 and MYC2. These interactions enable BBX factors to fine-tune gene expression in response to dynamic environmental conditions. Functionally, BBX proteins orchestrate light-responsive development, enhance tolerance to drought, salinity, and pathogens via hormonal and reactive oxygen species (ROS)-mediated pathways, and regulate secondary metabolism linked to pigment accumulation. Their roles in reproductive development, particularly in controlling flowering time and vegetative-reproductive phase transitions, position them as promising targets for crop improvement. Despite growing insight, key knowledge gaps remain. The mechanistic basis of BBX duality, their post-translational regulation and their integration within broader transcriptional and chromatin networks are still poorly understood. Additionally, BBX-mediated signalling remains understudied in monocots, wild relatives and under complex field conditions. This review summarizes the latest mechanistic and evolutionary insights into BBX transcription factors, emphasizing their functional diversity, context-dependent regulation, and applications in precision breeding. By highlighting both translational applications and unresolved challenges, we propose future directions for using BBX proteins to design of climate-resilient, high-performance crops.

# 1 | Introduction

Plants have evolved remarkable flexibility to respond to fluctuating environmental cues, orchestrating growth, development and survival through multilayered gene regulatory networks. Among these light signalling plays a central role, influencing developmental plasticity, stress adaptation and circadian coordination. One family of transcription factors, the B-box (BBX) proteins, has emerged as central integrators of light, hormonal and stress signalling pathways. BBX proteins contain one or two B-box zinc finger domains, which mediate protein-protein interactions, and in some cases, a CCT (CONSTANS, CO-like, TOC1) domain that participates in transcriptional regulation and nuclear transport (Dusi et al. 2024; Z. Song, Bian, et al. 2020).

What distinguishes the BBX family is not only its structural diversity and evolutionary expansion, but also its remarkable functional plasticity. In both model plants like Arabidopsis thaliana and key crop species such as pear, apple, sweet potato and tomato, BBX transcription factors have been shown to influence developmental transitions, pigment biosynthesis and stress tolerance. Notably, BBX proteins interact with the central photomorphogenic regulator HY5 to modulate light-dependent gene expression and growth patterns (D. Xu et al. 2018). Beyond photomorphogenesis, BBX19 and BBX18 play critical roles in circadian regulation by interfacing with core clock components, thereby aligning transcriptional programmes with diel light cycles (Yuan et al. 2021).

Recent advances further underscore the roles of BBX proteins in abiotic and biotic stress adaptation. In sweet potato, $IbBBX24$ enhances tolerance to drought and salinity by activating reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging pathways (H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022). In pear, allelic variation in $BBX24$ regulates both anthocyanin accumulation and plant stature, revealing a link between BBX-mediated metabolic control and developmental plasticity (Yang et al. 2024). These findings highlight the multifunctionality of BBX proteins as integrative regulators of growth, environmental sensing and stress resilience.

Despite these advances, major gaps remain. The dual regulatory roles of BBX proteins—activators versus repressors—remain mechanistically elusive. Their post-translational regulation, interactions with chromatin modifiers, and potential for epigenetic control are underexplored. Furthermore, BBX functions in monocots and stress-adapted wild species are still poorly understood, limiting translational applicability.

To address these questions, this review synthesizes current knowledge on BBX proteins through three conceptual layers of light-mediated regulation: (i) light signalling, involving immediate gene responses to light intensity and spectrum; (ii) circadian timing, governing endogenous $\sim 24\mathrm{-h}$ rhythms modulated by photic and thermal entrainment; and (iii) photoperiodic regulation, in which day-length perception controls flowering and phase transitions (Gangappa and Botto 2014; Yuan et al. 2021). We integrate over 60 recent studies to explore BBX structure-function relationships, stress regulatory roles, metabolic and circadian integration and opportunities for crop improvement. By examining BBX transcription factors as nodal integrators across signalling and developmental layers, we

propose a framework for leveraging these proteins in both fundamental research and precision agriculture.

# 2 | Structural and Functional Diversity of BBX Proteins

# 2.1 | BBX Domain Structure and Functional Classification

BBX proteins are zinc-finger transcription factors that are characterized by one or two conserved, cysteine-rich B-box domains near their N-terminus. These domains mediate protein-protein interactions and participate in light-dependent transcriptional reprogramming. In addition to B-box domains, some BBX proteins also possess a CCT domain at their C-terminus, which facilitates nuclear localization, photoperiod sensing and transcriptional regulation (Gangappa and Botto 2014; Z. Song, Bian, et al. 2020). The domain composition underpins BBX proteins' capacity to interface with circadian, developmental and environmental signalling networks.

BBX proteins are classified into five groups (I-V) based on domain architecture (Table 1), reflecting functional divergence driven by evolutionary pressures. Group I and II BBX proteins, such as BBX1 (CONSTANS) and BBX4, contain two B-box domains and a CCT domain, which is essential for their role in regulating photoperiodic flowering (Gangappa and Botto 2014). In contrast, Group III BBX proteins, including BBX14-BBX17, possess a single B-box domain and a CCT domain, and they typically function as negative regulators of flowering (Khanna et al. 2009; Susila et al. 2023). Group IV, which includes BBX18-BBX25, contains two B-box domains but lacks the CCT domain. This group of BBX proteins, such as BBX21 and BBX24, is primarily involved in photomorphogenesis, stress tolerance, and circadian rhythm regulation (Kielbowicz-Matuk et al. 2014; Z. Song, Bian, et al. 2020; H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022). Group V, consisting of BBX26-BBX32, contains only a single B-box domain and lacks the CCT domain, and these proteins are typically involved in fine-tuning responses to light and other environmental cues (L. Gao, Xu, et al. 2024; Y. Wang et al. 2023). The following table summarizes the structural features and functional classification of BBX proteins across the five major groups.

The ability of BBX proteins to function as either transcriptional activators or repressors is closely linked to their structural domain composition and regulatory context. BBX family members that contain the CCT domain—such as those in Groups I–III—frequently act as transcriptional activators, especially in the regulation of photoperiodic flowering. For example, $BBX1$ (CONSTANS) promotes the expression of $FLOWERING LOCUS T$ (FT) by binding to its promoter, while $BBX7$ and $BBX13$ similarly enhance the expression of floral integrator genes (Gangappa and Botto 2014; Rahul et al. 2024). In contrast, BBX proteins lacking the CCT domain—particularly those in Groups IV and V—are often transcriptional repressors. For instance, $BBX24$ and $BBX32$ negatively regulate photomorphogenesis by interfering with $HY5$ activity, either through direct protein-protein interaction or by competing for promoter binding sites (Holtan et al. 2011; H. In addition to domain architecture, post-translational modifications play a critical role in modulating BBX function. $BBX32$ , for example, is targeted for ubiquitin-mediated degradation by $COP1$ , a process that attenuates its repressive effects under light conditions (Cao et al. 2023). Conversely, $BBX17$ is phosphorylated by MAPK cascades (e.g., $SlMPK1/2$ ) under cold stress, which stabilizes the protein and enhances its transcriptional activation of CBF-dependent cold response genes (J. Song et al. 2023). These regulatory mechanisms suggest that BBX protein activity is not fixed but is dynamic and context-dependent, allowing plants to fine-tune developmental and stress responses via flexible use of BBX modules.

The diversity in domain structure across BBX family members underpins their functional specialization. For example, $BBX21$ plays a critical role in promoting photomorphogenesis by interacting with HY5 to regulate light-responsive genes (D. Xu et al. 2018). In sweet potato and lily, $BBX24$ has been shown to modulate anthocyanin biosynthesis and confer abiotic stress tolerance, functioning independently of the CCT domain (Z. Gao, Sun, et al. 2024; H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022). These structural differences allow BBX proteins to participate in a wide range of regulatory networks, highlighting their importance in plant development and adaptation.

# 2.2 | Evolutionary Expansion and Functional Diversification

Phylogenetic analysis indicates that BBX proteins are conserved in a wide range of plant species, from model organisms such as Arabidopsis to economically important crops such as potato (Solanum tuberosum [S. tuberosum]) and rice (Oryza sativa [O. sativa]) (Figure 1). The BBX family has undergone significant diversification, with tandem and segmental duplications driving gene family expansion in different lineages (Zhou et al. 2023). This expansion facilitated subfunctionalization and neofunctionalization, enabling BBX proteins to regulate stress, light and developmental pathways in a species-specific manner. This phenomenon has been demonstrated in crops such as pear (Pyrus), tomato (Solanum lycopersicum [S. lycopersicum]) and cherry (Cerasus pseudocerasus). BBX proteins not only regulate light signalling, but also orchestrate hormonal crosstalk, growth modulation and secondary metabolite biosynthesis.

In Arabidopsis, 32 BBX proteins have been identified, and their classification into distinct groups reflects their functional divergence (Khanna et al. 2009). In crops such as sweet potato, BBX proteins like IbBBX24 have evolved to enhance stress tolerance by modulating jasmonic acid (JA) pathways and ROS scavenging mechanisms, reflecting an adaptation to environmental stressors (H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022). Similarly, in pear, BBX24 has been implicated in anthocyanin biosynthesis and dwarfism, highlighting how allelic variation in BBX genes can lead to phenotypic diversity across species (Yang et al. 2024).

Comparative analyses in Arabidopsis, pear and tomato have revealed conserved protein motifs within B-box and CCT

domains, alongside divergent regulatory elements that support adaptive specialization (Zhou et al. 2023). However, species-specific expansions and positive selection have enabled novel regulatory functions to emerge, such as carotenoid biosynthesis in tomato. For example, in tomato, $SlBBX20$ regulates carotenoid biosynthesis, a function that is crucial for fruit ripening but not observed in other species (Xiong et al. 2019).

Although most functional studies of BBX proteins have been conducted in dicot species, emerging evidence suggests that monocots such as rice (O. sativa), maize (Zea mays) and wheat (Triticum aestivum) possess equally diverse and functionally significant BBX repertoires. In rice, OsBBX14 regulates photomorphogenesis by activating OsHY5L1 expression under blue light (B. Bai, Lu, et al. 2019), while genome-wide expression analyses have linked several OsBBX members to circadian rhythm regulation and phytohormone-responsive expression (J. Huang et al. 2012). In maize, ZmBBX20 and ZmBBX24 respond to abiotic stresses such as drought and salinity by upregulating expression levels (X. Xu et al. 2023). Recent studies in wheat have associated the BBX gene with seed dormancy and germination (X. Cheng et al. 2024). These findings indicate that the BBX family plays evolutionarily conserved roles across both monocots and dicots, although functional validation in monocots remains limited and warrants further exploration.

Overall, the evolutionary trajectory of BBX proteins underscores their functional versatility. The divergence in domain structure and subsequent specialization of these transcription factors reflect their ability to integrate light, stress and developmental signals across diverse plant species (Tables 2 and 3). As more genomewide studies are conducted, further insights into the evolutionary significance of BBX family members are likely to emerge, providing a deeper understanding of their roles in plant adaptation and resilience (Xue et al. 2025; X. Zhang, Zhang et al. 2021).

# 3 | BBX Proteins in Light Signalling and Photomorphogenesis

# 3.1 | BBX and HY5 Interactions: A Core Regulatory Module

Light is a key environmental signal regulating photomorphogenesis in higher plants. At the core of this process is the bZIP transcription factor ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5), which activates a suite of light-responsive genes (Gangappa and Botto 2014; D. Xu et al. 2016). BBX proteins act as both co-regulators and modulators of HY5 activity, orchestrating precise developmental outcomes in response to light intensity and quality. Among these, BBX21 is a prototypical activator. It binds directly to the HY5 promoter, enhancing its transcription and upregulating genes associated with photomorphogenesis (e.g., CAB) and light-responsive metabolic pathways (e.g., CHS) (Sarmiento 2013; D. Xu et al. 2018). BBX21 also modulates the extent of hypocotyl elongation by interacting with AGB1, a G-protein $\beta$ -subunit that mediates crosstalk between light and hormone signalling (D. Xu et al. 2017).

# 3.2 | BBX Proteins in UV-B Responses

In addition to broad-spectrum light signalling, BBX proteins are involved in plant adaptation to ultraviolet-B (UV-B) radiation, which triggers protective metabolite accumulation and DNA repair. In particular, $BBX21$ and $BBX31$ are key regulators in UV-B perception and response. $BBX21$ enhances UV-B-induced flavonoid biosynthesis by activating $HY5$ and its downstream targets such as $CHS$ , leading to accumulation of UV-absorbing pigments (S. Bai, Tao, et al. 2019; C. Lu, Liu, et al. 2024; Yang et al. 2022). It also contributes to DNA repair through the induction of genes associated with lesion recognition and repair mechanisms (Podolec et al. 2022). $BBX31$ , although functionally redundant with $BBX21$ under certain conditions, exhibits a light-quality dependent dual function. Under white light, it acts independently of $HY5$ ; under UV-B stress, it synergistically enhances $HY5$ -mediated gene expression, including $CHS$ and other flavonoid pathway genes (Yadav et al. 2019). This modular regulation reinforces the BBX family's versatility in responding to different spectral components of light.

# 3.3 | Feedback Loops in Light Regulation

To maintain homoeostasis and temporal precision, light signalling networks are tightly coupled with feedback regulation and circadian control. BBX proteins are central to these processes, particularly through COP1-mediated proteolysis and day-night phase modulation. $BBX21$ is stabilized during the light phase and degraded in darkness via COPI, linking its activity to the circadian rhythm of light availability (D. Xu et al. 2016). This post-translational regulation ensures that photomorphogenic signals are only activated when light is present, optimizing energy use and developmental timing. BBX24 interacts with HY5 to suppress its activity under lowlight conditions, forming a feedback circuit that fine-tunes photomorphogenesis in suboptimal environments (N. Zhang et al. 2024). Similarly, $BBX32$ is expressed in darkness and repressed in light via COPI degradation (Holtan et al. 2011), supporting the rapid transition from skotomorphogenesis to photomorphogenesis upon dawn. The circadian-coupled BBX-HY5-COP1 module represents a highly adaptive system, aligning transcriptional activity with environmental cycles. This network enables plants not only to respond to light but also to anticipate it, preparing the transcriptome for daily transitions in light and temperature (Figure 2).

# 4 | BBX Proteins in Stress Responses

# 4.1 | Regulatory Roles in Abiotic Stress Tolerance

Plants are constantly exposed to abiotic stressors such as drought, salinity, cold and heat, which challenge their growth and productivity. BBX transcription factors have emerged as critical regulators that orchestrate multi-layered stress responses, often through crosstalk with hormonal signalling and ROS detoxification pathways. In sweet potato (Ipomoea batatas), IbBBX24 confers enhanced drought and salt tolerance by activating a ROS-scavenging module composed of IbTOE3 and IbPRX17, a class III peroxidase gene (H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022). IbBBX24 binds to the promoter of IbPRX17 and works cooperatively with IbTOE3, leading to enhanced detoxification of hydrogen peroxide under osmotic stress. Notably, this function is independent of traditional ABA signalling, highlighting an alternative stress mitigation route through transcriptional fine-tuning of antioxidant systems. BBX24 also regulates abiotic stress responses via the JA pathway, directly repressing IbJAZ10, a negative regulator of JA signalling. This repression relieves the inhibition on MYC2, a central transcription factor in defence and abiotic response, thereby promoting stress-resilient phenotypes (H. Zhang et al. 2020). These

findings suggest that BBX proteins can function as signal amplifiers within hormone-mediated stress pathways.

# 4.2 | Integration in Temperature Stress Responses

Temperature fluctuations, including cold and heat extremes, impose additional challenges for plant survival. BBX proteins, particularly BBX17, contribute to thermotolerance by modulating both MAPK signalling and transcriptional responses. In tomato (S. lycopersicum), SLBBX17 interacts with mitogen-activated protein kinases (SlMPK1/2), which phosphorylate BBX17 under cold stress, enhancing its stability and nuclear activity. This modification promotes the activation of CBF-dependent cold-responsive genes, improving membrane protection and freezing tolerance (J. Song et al. 2023). In response to heat stress, SlBBX17 promotes the expression of heat shock proteins (HSPs) and chlorophyll stability genes, aiding in the maintenance of photosynthetic capacity and reducing oxidative damage (X. Xu et al. 2022). These dual roles reveal that BBX17 serves as a temperature stress integrator, coordinating transcriptional reprogramming across opposing thermal gradients.

# 4.3 | Modulation of Biotic Stress Defence

Beyond abiotic stress, BBX proteins also modulate plant immunity against diverse pathogens by interfacing with key defence hormone pathways. In sweet potato, $IbBBX24$ enhances resistance to Fusarium oxysporum by activating the JA signalling cascade through direct regulation of $IbJAZ10$ and $IbMYC2$ , effectively priming the plant for a faster and stronger immune response (H. Zhang et al. 2020). Similarly, in pepper (Capsicum annuum), $CaBBX14$ mediates resistance to Phytophthora capsici (P. capsici) by activating the salicylic acid (SA) signalling pathway. Knockdown of $CaBBX14$ leads to reduced SA accumulation and suppressed expression of pathogenesis-related $(PR)$ genes, resulting in heightened susceptibility to pathogen attack (Zhou et al. 2023). These examples underscore the versatility of BBX proteins as regulators of hormone-modulated defence networks. The dual engagement of BBX factors in both abiotic and biotic stress responses highlights their potential as master regulators of environmental adaptation.

# 4.4 | Conceptual Integration and Model of Action

As shown in Figure 3, BBX transcription factors function through multi-tiered regulatory modules that integrate upstream signals (e.g., light, ROS, JA, SA) with downstream effector genes (e.g., PRX17, HSPs, PR genes). In stress contexts, BBX proteins often cooperate with other TFs (e.g., AP2/ERF, MYC2) or are post-translationally modified (e.g., phosphorylation by MAPKs) to modulate their stability, localization, and transcriptional specificity. Their ability to act as both positive and negative regulators, depending on environmental context and protein-protein interactions, positions BBX proteins as context-sensitive switches that enable plants to finely balance growth and survival under stress.

# 5 | BBX Proteins in Anthocyanin and Flavonoid Biosynthesis

# 5.1 | Dual Roles of BBX Proteins in Anthocyanin Biosynthesis

Anthocyanins, responsible for red, blue and purple pigmentation in plants, serve essential roles in photoprotection, pollinator attraction and stress tolerance (Zheng et al. 2025). In crops, they are valued for their aesthetic and nutritional contributions (Z. Lu, Wang, et al. 2024). Their biosynthesis is tightly controlled by light and regulated by a network including BBX proteins, HY5, and the MYB-bHLH-WD40 complex.

BBX proteins exhibit dual regulatory roles. In *Pyrus*, *PcBBX18* promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis by forming a complex with HY5 and activating key structural genes such as *CHS* and *UFGT* (S. Bai, Tao, et al. 2019). In contrast, *PcBBX21* represses pigment production by competitively binding to the same promoter regions, thus antagonizing *PcBBX18-HY5* activation under low-light conditions (S. Bai, Tao, et al. 2019). In sweet cherry (*Prunus avium* [P. avium]), *BBX6* and *BBX9* enhance fruit pigmentation by directly upregulating anthocyanin biosynthetic genes in a light-dependent manner (Y. Wang et al. 2023). A similar mechanism is observed in *Malus domestica* (M. domestica), where MdBBX22 interacts with HY5 to promote UV-B-induced colouration (An et al. 2019). These examples underscore the evolutionary conservation of the BBX-HY5 module across Rosaceae species.

However, functional divergence exists: BBX proteins lacking the CCT domain often act as repressors, suggesting domain architecture plays a key role in regulatory directionality. This modularity implies that BBX factors may function as adaptable regulatory units, capable of evolutionary tuning across species.

# 5.2 | Species-Specific Variation and Natural Allelic Diversity

The regulatory roles of BBX proteins in anthocyanin biosynthesis are shaped not only by protein domain structure

but also by species-specific gene expansion and allelic variation. In *Pyrus*, a naturally occurring 14-bp deletion in *PyBBX24* produces a loss-of-function allele that enhances red colouration and causes dwarfism, illustrating how single-nucleotide changes in *BBX* genes can alter pigment accumulation and plant architecture simultaneously (Yang et al. 2024). In *M. domestica*, *MdBBX22* promotes UV-B-induced anthocyanin accumulation by interacting with HY5 to activate flavonoid biosynthetic genes (An et al. 2019). Its expression is light-responsive and fruit skin-specific, reflecting tight spatial and environmental regulation. In *Populus*, *PtrBBX23* contributes not only to anthocyanin biosynthesis but also to proanthocyanidin accumulation, highlighting BBX involvement across multiple branches of the flavonoid pathway (C. Li et al. 2021).

These cases demonstrate the plasticity of BBX regulatory function across species and developmental contexts. BBX evolution appears to involve both cis-regulatory divergence and neofunctionalization, allowing species to tune pigment traits for ecological and physiological benefit. Understanding BBX allelic diversity could inform marker-assisted breeding strategies and the development of elite cultivars with improved colouration, stress resilience, or growth traits.

# 5.3 | Opportunities for Crop Improvement via Flavonoid Engineering

Given their central role in light-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis, BBX transcription factors offer promising targets for crop improvement, particularly in enhancing fruit colouration, nutritional value and postharvest quality. In $P$ avium, overexpression of PavBBX6 or PavBBX9 significantly increases anthocyanin accumulation, improving fruit pigmentation and market appeal (Y. Wang et al. 2023). Similarly, in $M$ domestica, MdBBX22 enhances UV-B-responsive pigmentation via HY5 interaction (An et al. 2019), and its expression is light-activated, enabling light-responsive regulation of fruit pigmentation.

Natural allelic variation, such as the 14-bp deletion in PyBBX24, offers a non-transgenic path to improving both colour and plant stature in pear (Yang et al. 2024). Beyond anthocyanins, BBX-regulated pathways also influence other flavonoid branches—such as tannins and proanthocyanidins—which contribute to antioxidant capacity, fruit quality, and abiotic stress tolerance. Targeted manipulation of BBX genes—via genome editing or allele-based selection—therefore holds strong potential for precision metabolic engineering and the development of climate-resilient, high-quality cultivars (Figure 4).

In addition to their roles in regulating secondary metabolite biosynthesis under light cues, BBX proteins also participate in temporal regulation of gene expression. Their rhythmic accumulation and interaction with core clock components suggest a broader role in aligning metabolic activity with circadian timing. In the following section, we explore how BBX factors modulate circadian rhythms and contribute to photoperiodic and diel regulation in plants.

# 6 | BBX Proteins in Circadian Rhythm Regulation

# 6.1 | Modulation of the Plant Circadian Clock

BBX transcription factors influence circadian timing by interacting with core oscillator components and modulating phase-specific gene expression. In Arabidopsis, BBX19 interacts with PRR9 and PRR7, repressing morning-expressed genes and contributing to clock damping and period stabilization (Yuan et al. 2021). Its homologue $BBX18$ lengthens the circadian period under short-day or low-light conditions, suggesting a role in photoperiodic adjustment (Kiełbowicz-Matuk et al. 2017). These BBX factors do not reside in the central oscillator but function as peripheral regulators, shaping rhythmic outputs in response to light amplitude and photoperiod dynamics. Their

actions reflect a broader strategy by which plants fine-tune circadian architecture in ecologically variable environments.

# 6.2 | Integration With Environmental Cues

BBX proteins also serve as environmental integrators, linking circadian rhythms with light and temperature entrainment pathways. $BBX21$ , for instance, accumulates rhythmically in the light phase and enhances HY5-mediated transcription, aligning photomorphogenic responses with daily light cycles (Crocco et al. 2010). In S. tuberosum, $BBX18$ responds to temperature oscillations, modulating the expression of thermoresponsive genes and aligning development with thermocycles (Yuan et al. 2021). These findings illustrate BBX proteins' capacity to translate environmental periodicity into circadian outputs,

reinforcing their role as timing modulators at the intersection of external cues and internal clocks. Such temporal integration is especially vital for reproductive development and the regulation of flowering time. Future research should examine how BBX-mediated entrainment contributes to stress anticipation, and whether BBX factors exhibit post-transcriptional rhythmic regulation or interact with clock-controlled epigenetic modifiers.

# 7 | BBX Proteins in Reproductive Development and Flowering Time Regulation

# 7.1 | BBX Proteins as Regulators of Flowering Time

BBX transcription factors contribute to flowering time control by modulating photoperiodic, thermosensitive and hormonal pathways, often through interaction with floral integrators such as $FT$ and $CONSTANS$ (CO). In Chrysanthemum morifolium (C. morifolium), $CmBBX8$ promotes flowering under both long- and short-day conditions by activating $FTL1$ , a homologue of $FLOWERING LOCUS T$ (L. Wang et al. 2020). Its antagonist, $CmBBX5$ , inhibits flowering by interfering with $CmBBX8$ 's binding to the $FTL1$ promoter, forming a BBX-BBX regulatory module that fine-tunes photoperiod responsiveness (Q. Wang et al. 2024). In Arabidopsis, $BBX13$ (COL15) delays flowering under long days by repressing the CO-FT axis, serving as a negative regulator of photoperiodic induction (Rahul et al. 2024). These examples illustrate that BBX proteins can act as either promoters or repressors of flowering, depending on species context and partner interactions. Their bidirectional roles reflect a broader flexibility within the BBX family, enabling plants to modulate reproductive timing in response to environmental and developmental signals.

# 7.2 | Coordination of Vegetative-Reproductive Phase Transition

In addition to regulating photoperiodic flowering, BBX transcription factors contribute to the timing of the vegetative-to-reproductive phase transition, a critical developmental checkpoint that ensures reproductive investment aligns with environmental cues and internal energy status.

In Chrysanthemum, CmBBX8 not only promotes FTL1-dependent floral induction but also modulates vegetative architecture, enabling flexible flowering schedules under diverse day lengths (L. Wang et al. 2020). CmBBX5, acting as a repressor, delays this transition, highlighting how BBX-BBX interactions form regulatory balances that control phase change (Q. Wang et al. 2024). In S. lycopersicum, SlBBX20 accelerates floral transition, with knockout lines exhibiting prolonged vegetative growth and delayed flowering, implicating it in the control of floral meristem identity and developmental pace (Shiose et al. 2024). Similarly, in Pyrus, a naturally occurring loss-of-function allele of PyBBX24 results in early flowering and reduced stature, suggesting BBX24 acts as a checkpoint gene linking growth suppression and floral readiness (Yang et al. 2024). These examples indicate that BBX proteins function

as developmental gatekeepers, integrating signals from light, hormones and internal growth status to balance energy allocation between vegetative expansion and reproductive commitment. Future studies should examine whether BBX proteins influence floral meristem fate determination, or interact with known age-related and sugar-signalling pathways (e.g., SPL, TPS1), to further clarify their roles in coordinating whole-plant developmental transitions.

# 8 | Applications of BBX Proteins in Crop Improvement

BBX transcription factors are emerging as versatile tools in crop biotechnology, owing to their multifunctional roles in regulating stress responses, pigment biosynthesis and developmental transitions such as flowering. Advances in BBX functional characterization across species have revealed their potential to enhance resilience, quality and yield in both horticultural and field crops. These findings offer promising avenues for targeted genetic improvement, particularly in traits related to abiotic/ biotic stress tolerance, fruit colouration and flowering control (Table 4).

# 8.1 | Enhancing Stress Tolerance Through BBX Regulation

BBX transcription factors improve abiotic and biotic stress tolerance by modulating ROS detoxification, hormonal signalling and defence gene expression. In sweet potato, $IbBBX24$ enhances drought and salt tolerance by activating the $IbTOE3-IbPRX17$ module, promoting ROS scavenging and osmotic stress resilience (H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022; H. Zhang et al. 2020). In pepper, $CaBBX14$ contributes to $P_{\cdot}$ capsici resistance by activating SA signalling and upregulating pathogenesis-related $(PR)$ genes (Zhou et al. 2023). These findings highlight BBX proteins as effective regulatory hubs for dual stress tolerance and defence priming. By harnessing this dual functionality, breeders can develop cultivars with broad-spectrum resistance, reducing reliance on chemical inputs and enhancing resilience under climate variability.

# 8.2 Manipulating Anthocyanin Pathways for Enhanced Pigmentation

In horticultural crops, BBX transcription factors serve as key regulators of pigmentation and nutritional traits, primarily by enhancing light-induced anthocyanin biosynthesis. In sweet cherry, overexpression of $PavBBX6$ and $PavBBX9$ upregulates CHS and UFGT, leading to intensified fruit coloration (Y. Wang et al. 2023). In apple, MdBBX22 interacts with HY5 to promote UV-B-responsive flavonoid accumulation, enhancing both visual appeal and antioxidant potential (An et al. 2019). Natural variation also provides breeding opportunities: a 14-bp deletion in PyBBX24 is associated with stronger red pigmentation and reduced plant height, linking BBX-mediated pigment regulation to growth architecture (Yang et al. 2024). # 8.3 | Improving Flowering Time and Yield

Flowering time is a key determinant of crop productivity, influencing both reproductive success and yield optimization under changing environments. BBX transcription factors play crucial roles in modulating flowering pathways and phase transitions, making them valuable targets for breeding programmes. In C. morifolium, $CmBBX8$ promotes early flowering by directly activating FTL1, a central integrator of floral signals. Overexpression of $CmBBX8$ accelerates floral induction, offering potential benefits in regions with short growing seasons (L. Wang et al. 2020). Conversely, in Arabidopsis, BBX13 delays flowering by repressing CONSTANS (CO)-mediated $FT$ activation, allowing for flowering postponement under suboptimal conditions (Rahul et al. 2024). This regulatory flexibility enables breeders to fine-tune flowering schedules in alignment with local climates.

BBX proteins also influence the balance between vegetative growth and reproductive development. Overexpression of $BBX14/15/16$ has been shown to delay flowering while promoting vegetative biomass accumulation, which may be advantageous in crops where leaf or stem yield is desired (Susila et al. 2023). Incorporating BBX regulators into breeding strategies allows for precise control of flowering time and growth architecture in both open-field and controlled-environment systems. This enhances adaptability, supports crop cycle optimization and contributes to yield stability in fruit, grain and ornamental species.

# 9 | Conclusion and Future Perspectives

BBX transcription factors have emerged as central integrators of plant environmental responses and developmental regulation, linking light perception, hormonal pathways, stress responses and morphogenetic transitions. Through interactions with key regulators like HY5, BBX proteins fine-tune processes such as photomorphogenesis, circadian timing and flowering induction, enabling plants to adapt dynamically to fluctuating environments (D. Xu et al. 2018; Y. Zhang, Wu, et al. 2021). Their roles in mediating abiotic and biotic stress tolerance—via ROS detoxification and hormone signalling modulation—further highlight their adaptive significance (H. Zhang, Wang, et al. 2022; Zhou et al. 2023). Moreover, BBX genes are increasingly implicated in controlling anthocyanin biosynthesis, fruit colouration and reproductive transitions, with strong translational potential in crop trait optimization (H. Cheng et al. 2023; Y. Wang et al. 2023).

Despite these advances, our current understanding of BBX function remains fragmented and heavily species-specific. Most functional studies focus on a few model systems (e.g., Arabidopsis, apple, pear), while comparative and evolutionary

insights across monocots, basal angiosperms or stress-adapted wild species are still lacking. In particular, the mechanistic basis of BBX duality—why some members act as activators and others as repressors of similar pathways—remains poorly understood. Structural studies on BBX domains (e.g., B-box 1 vs. B-box 2, CCT) are limited, and the post-translational regulation of BBX activity (e.g., phosphorylation, ubiquitination) is an underexplored frontier.

From an applied perspective, BBX genes offer multi-trait leverage points for crop improvement. Their ability to integrate environmental signals and modulate pigmentation, flowering time and stress tolerance makes them attractive engineering targets. Several natural alleles (e.g., PyBBX24 deletion) already provide non-transgenic options for trait selection (Yang et al. 2024). However, pleiotropy remains a key constraint: BBX modifications that benefit one trait (e.g., stress resilience) may compromise another (e.g., yield or reproductive timing) (J. Li et al. 2024; Rahul et al. 2024). Future strategies must therefore prioritize context-specific manipulation, such as tissue- or phase-specific expression, inducible promoters and alleles with subtle regulatory shifts rather than binary on/off effects. Integrating BBX genes into synthetic regulatory circuits could further enable precision control over complex trait networks.

Looking ahead, several research avenues deserve urgent attention: (1) How do BBX proteins interface with other core regulators (e.g., PIFs, MYCs, SPLs, histone modifiers)? (2) What defines BBX activity patterns in specific cell types, organs, or stress phases? (3) How has domain composition and cis-regulatory evolution shaped BBX function across plant lineages? (4) Can BBX-based strategies maintain efficacy across genotypes, environments and field scales?

In summary, BBX transcription factors represent a converging point of signal integration and phenotypic plasticity. Their continued exploration—through mechanistic, evolutionary, and translational lenses—will not only deepen our understanding of plant adaptive capacity, but also open new frontiers in climate-resilient agriculture and precision breeding.
