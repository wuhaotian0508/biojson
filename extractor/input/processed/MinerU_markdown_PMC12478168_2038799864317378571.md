# Characterization of SrUGT76G4 reveals a key residue for regioselectivity and efficient Reb M synthesis

Yu Wanga,b,1, Tang Lia,1, Yangjie Zhenga,b, Chenxin Guoa , Kuikui Lia , Xiaochen Jiaa , Liping Zhuc , Kecai Chenc , and Heng Yina,b,2 

Affiliations are included on p. 11. 

Edited by Clinton Chapple, Purdue University, West Lafayette, IN; received February 28, 2025; accepted August 18, 2025 

Steviol glycosides (SGs) from Stevia rebaudiana are prized as noncaloric sweeteners, with rebaudioside M (Reb M)—a next-generation SG known for its sucrose-like sweetness and lack of off-tastes—standing out for its superior sensory profile. However, Reb M’s limited natural abundance impedes its commercial production. Here, we report the identification of a glucosyltransferase, UGT76G4 that efficiently catalyzes the conversion of Reb D to Reb M with a strong preference for C19 glycosylation. Structural and functional analyses, including X-ray crystallography, molecular dynamics simulations, and mutagenesis, revealed key residues in UGT76G4 that dictate its regioselectivity, with residue 200 playing a pivotal role. Engineered UGT76G4 variants, including Q199I/ G200Y and H155S/Q199I/G200Y, enhanced Reb E and Reb D conversion efficiency by 1.46-fold and 23-fold, respectively, compared to UGT76G1. The engineered variants offer a promising pathway for increasing Reb M production, advancing biotechnological strategies for steviol glycoside biosynthesis and optimizing plant metabolic engineering approaches. Our findings deepen the understanding of SG biosynthesis and provide a basis for sustainable production of high-value sweeteners.

Excessive sugar intake is recognized as a leading cause of obesity, significantly increasing the risk of diabetes and cardiovascular disease. To counteract this issue, replacing dietary sugar with nonnutritive sweeteners is a prevalent strategy in obesity management ( 1 ). Artificial sweeteners like saccharine, aspartame, and acesulfame-K have been widely used for decades. However, recent research has found that these artificial sweeteners may pose potential carcinogenic risk and affect postprandial glycemic responses ( 2 , 3 ). In contrast, natural sweeteners, such as steviol glycosides (SGs), offer a promising alternative due to their high sweetness, negligible calorie content, and less negative effect. SGs are a class of plant-derived diterpenoid natural products that serve as high-intensity sweeteners, primarily accumulated in the leaves of Stevia rebaudiana ( 4 , 5 ). They vary in the number and type of sugar residues attached at the C13 (R1) and C19 (R2) positions of the steviol backbone ( 6 ), which significantly affects their potent of sweetness ( 7 ). The predominant SGs in Stevia, namely stevioside (STV) and rebaudioside A (Reb A), are 250 to 300 times sweeter than sucrose but exhibit a bitter aftertaste. In contrast, rebaudioside M (Reb M), which is approximately 350 times sweeter than sucrose, closely mimics the taste of sucrose without any bitter aftertaste ( 7 ). Consequently, Reb M is regarded as the next generation of Stevia sweetener ( 8 , 9 ). Nonetheless, Reb M is present in only trace amounts in Stevia $( 0 . 1 \%$ by dry weight), thereby limiting its large-scale production ( 10 ). 

Recent advances have elucidated the biosynthetic pathway of SG glycosylation in Stevia ( Fig. 1 ) ( 6 , 11 – 13 ). Notably, the enzyme UGT76G1 plays a pivotal role by catalyzing $\beta$ -1,3-glycosylation, a crucial step in converting STV to Reb A and subsequently to Reb I, as well as in converting Reb E to Reb D and ultimately to Reb M ( Fig. 1 ). However, UGT76G1 exhibits pronounced regioselectivity at the C13 position with limited activity at the C19 position ( 14 ), and attempts to overexpress UGT76G1 in Stevia have not succeeded in improving Reb M levels ( 15 ). Concurrently, studies examining the genetic diversity of UGT76G1 across Stevia cultivars and developmental stages have revealed correlations between specific polymorphisms and SG content ( 16 – 20 ). These findings suggest the potential existence of a natural UGT76G1 variant in Stevia with a preference for glycosylation at C19, contributing to Reb M biosynthesis. However, no natural variants exhibiting high C19 glycosylation activity have been identified, possibly owing to the inherently low Reb M levels in these cultivars.

# Significance

Steviol glycosides (SGs) are plant-derived sweeteners from Stevia rebaudiana with applications in dietary sugar reduction. Rebaudioside M (Reb M) is the most desirable SG due to its superior taste profile, but its low natural abundance limits commercial production. This study identifies UGT76G4, a Stevia glycosyltransferase with a strong preference for C19 glycosylation, enabling efficient conversion of Reb D to Reb M. Structural and biochemical analyses reveal key residues governing regioselectivity, guiding protein engineering to further enhance Reb M yield. These findings deepen our understanding of SG biosynthesis in Stevia and provide a promising enzyme for sustainable Reb M production, advancing biotechnological strategies for steviol glycoside biosynthesis, and optimizing plant metabolic engineering approaches.

# Results and Discussion

Sequence Variation of UGT76G1 in Stevia Cultivars. Polymorphism analysis of UGT76G1 was performed on three cultivars exhibiting high levels of Reb D and Reb M (HDM1, HDM2, HDM3), as well as on one cultivar with high Reb A content (HRA) (25). A total of twelve sequences (UGT76G3 to UGT76G14) were identified, corresponding to nine variants (GenBank accession numbers are listed in SI Appendix, Table S1). UGT76G3 was detected in HDM1, whereas UGT76G4, UGT76G6, and UGT76G7 were identified in HDM2. HDM3 harbored UGT76G8, UGT76G10, UGT76G12, UGT76G13, and UGT76G14. UGT76G1 (AAR06912.1) and UGT76G2 (ACM47734.1) were exclusively detected in HRA, consistent with its HRA levels. Multiple sequence alignment (MSA) of the nine newly identified sequences and five previously reported variants revealed an overall sequence identity of $7 5 . 2 \%$ , with all variants containing the conserved PSPG box (26) motif (SI  Appendix, Fig. S1). Phylogenetic analysis classified the 14 variants into two distinct clades (A and B) (Fig.  2A). Although UGT76G1 and UGT76G4 belong to clade A, they exhibit a notable evolutionary divergence. Three of the newly identified sequences (UGT76G6, UGT76G12, and UGT76G14) clustered in clade B and shared mutations in the 226 to 239 region (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). 

Functional Characterization of UGT76G1 Variants. UGT76G1 variants were heterologously expressed in Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S2). When using STV as the substrate and uridine diphosphate glucose (UDPG) as the sugar donor, only UGT76G4 and UGT76G6 exhibited detectable activity in addition to UGT76G1 using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and high-resolution mass spectrometry (MS) (Fig. 2 C and D). Similar to UGT76G1, UGT76G4 catalyzed the conversion of STV mainly to Reb A. Interestingly, the product HPLC spectrum also showed obvious peaks corresponding to Reb I and a new peak (Fig. 2C). Although we lack reliable standards to unambiguously confirm the product, the $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ values from the tandem MS fragmentation ion $\mathrm { ( m / z ~ 9 6 5 . 4 ) }$ and the MS/ MS fragmentation ion $\left( \mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z } \ 6 4 1 . 3 \right)$ indicated that the product was Reb E2 (28) containing a disaccharide group in R2 (Fig. $2 D$ and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S3A). This shows that UGT76G4 has a distinct C19 glycosylation activity compared with UGT76G1 (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). Furthermore, UGT76G4 predominantly synthesized Reb M from Reb E (Fig. $2 E$ and SI  Appendix, Fig. S4A), and a secondary product, Reb AM (29), was confirmed by MS $( \mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z } 1 , 1 2 7 . 5 )$ and MS/MS $( \mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z } 6 4 2 . 3 )$ ) (Fig. $2 F _ { \prime }$ ), whereas Reb D was produced in lower quantities. In contrast, UGT76G1 produced Reb D as the dominant product from Reb E, followed by Reb M, with Reb AM remaining undetectable (SI Appendix, Fig.  S4B). In  vivo experiments conducted in N. benthamiana demonstrated that UGT76G4’s C19 glycosylation preference is maintained under physiological conditions (Fig. 2B), thereby revealing a biosynthetic pathway for the conversion of Reb E to Reb M in Stevia (Fig. $2 G$ ). Additionally, the catalytic activity of UGT76G4 toward other SGs was evaluated. When Rub was used as a substrate, UGT76G4 primarily generated STV B (30) $\mathrm { ( m / z }$ of 803.4 for MS and $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ of $4 7 9 . 3$ for MS/MS) via C19 glycosylation followed by Reb G $\mathrm { ( m / z }$ of 803.4 for MS and $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ of 641.3 for MS/MS) via C13 glycosylation, with a progressive appearance of Reb E6 (30) $\mathrm { ( m / z }$ of 965.4 for MS and $\mathrm { { \bar { m } / z } }$ of 

641.3 for MS/MS) over time (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S5 A and B). This finding contrasts with UGT76G1 (14), which predominantly produced Reb G (SI Appendix, Fig. S5A). These results suggest the existence of a pathway for the conversion of Rub to Reb E6 via STV B, catalyzed by UGT76G4 (SI Appendix, Fig. S5C). The C19 regioselectivity of UGT76G4 was further corroborated by its catalytic efficiency toward Reb D (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A) and Reb A (SI Appendix, Fig. S6B). Thus, UGT76G4 represents the first $\beta$ -1,3 glucosyltransferase known to exhibit C19 regioselectivity on SGs, thereby facilitating the synthesis of rare SG sweeteners, including STV B from Rub, Reb E2 from STV, Reb AM from Reb E, and Reb M from Reb D (Fig. 1). 

The optimal pH and temperature conditions for UGT76G4 (pH 8.25, $4 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ) and UGT76G1 (pH 7.25, $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ) were determined (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). Enzyme kinetics under these optimal conditions revealed that when Reb D—bearing a trisaccharide at the R1 (C13) and a disaccharide at the R2 (C19) position—was used as the substrate, UGT76G4 exhibited a significantly higher $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ $( 5 6 1 . 4 0 \pm 1 6 . 9 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) compared to UGT76G1 $\mathrm { ~ ( 1 8 5 . 0 0 ~ \pm }$ $2 5 . 1 3 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ( Table 1 ). However, the $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ of UGT76G4 $( 1 3 2 . 6 3 \pm$ $1 . 7 3 \mathrm { { m i n } ^ { - 1 } ) }$ ) was dramatically higher than that of UGT76G1 (3.90 $\pm \ 0 . 1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ ). As a result, the catalytic efficiency $( k _ { \mathrm { c a t } } / K _ { \mathrm { m } } )$ of UGT76G4 toward Reb D $( 3 . 9 4 ~ s ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathrm { { \dot { ~ m } M ^ { - 1 } } }$ ) was approximately 12-fold higher than that of UGT76G1 $( 0 . 3 5 \ s ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { m } \mathrm { \hat { M } ^ { - 1 } } )$ ). 

When Reb A—containing a single glucose at the R2 and a trisaccharide at the R1 position—was used as substrate, UGT76G4 showed a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ $( 6 9 4 . 1 0 \pm 2 7 . 1 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ similar to that for Reb D, but with a markedly lower $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ $( 1 0 . 3 0 \pm 0 . 1 9 \mathrm { { \ m i n } ^ { - 1 } } )$ ), resulting in a much lower catalytic efficiency of $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { \ s ^ { - 1 } \ m M ^ { - 1 } }$ ( Table 1 ). Notably, UGT76G4 did not exhibit any detectable activity toward the R1 position of either Reb D and Reb A, suggesting a preference for disaccharide over monosaccharide moieties at the R2 position— a trend also observed in UGT76G1 ( 14 ). 

Interestingly, when STV—bearing a disaccharide at the R1 and a monosaccharide at the R2—was used as the substrate, UGT76G4 appeared to favor glycosylation at the R1 (C13) position, yielding Reb A rather than glycosylating the R2 (C19) to generate Reb E2 ( Fig. 2C ). However, its catalytic efficiency for STV glycosylation $( 0 . { \overset { \vartriangle } { \cdot } } 9 2 s ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { m } \mathbf { M } ^ { - 1 } )$ ) was much lower than that of UGT76G1 (14.29 $\mathbf { s } ^ { - 1 } \mathbf { m } \mathbf { M } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) ( Table 1 ), indicating that UGT76G4 recognizes the disaccharide moiety at the R1 preferentially, which in this case appears to override its general preference for the C19 end. 

To examine the intrinsic C13/C19 regioselectivity of UGT76G4 without the confounding effect of varying glycosylation lengths, we employed substrates with symmetric glycosylation at both termini: Rub (monosaccharides at R1 and R2) and Reb E (disaccharide at both positions). The enzyme concentration and reaction time were optimized to minimize formation of downstream products such as Reb E6 and Reb M. When Reb E was used as substrate, the C19 glycosylation showed a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $2 3 7 . 1 0 \pm 3 3 . 5 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ , less than half that for C13 glycosylation $( 5 9 4 . 6 0 \pm 9 1 . 9 7 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ ) ( Table 1 ). In addition, the $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ for C19 glycosylation $( 2 1 . 2 2 \pm 0 . 9 2 $ $\operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ) was significantly higher than that for C13 glycosylation $( 1 4 . 6 5 \pm 1 . 0 9 \mathrm { \ m i n } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), resulting in a 3.63-fold greater catalytic efficiency toward the C19 position. 

A similar trend was observed when Rub was used as substrate: UGT76G4 showed a $K _ { \mathrm { m } }$ of $1 8 9 . 2 0 \pm 2 1 . 9 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and a $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ of 4.92 $\pm \ : 1 . 4 5 \ : \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ for C19 glycosylation, while kinetic parameters for C13 glycosylation could not be determined due to the extremely low product yield ( Table 1 ). These results confirm the C19 regioselectivity of UGT76G4, in contrast to the C13 selectivity characteristic of UGT76G1 ( 14 ). Structural alignment revealed a high degree of structural similarity between the two proteins, with an all-atom rmsd of $0 . 7 2 5 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ ; however, these subtle structural differences cannot fully account for their divergent regioselectivity. Among the differences, the residues within the 191 to 208 region are most likely to determine regioselectivity given their close proximity to the substrate binding pocket (Fig.  3B). Chimeric enzymes generated by swapping this region between UGT76G1 and UGT76G4 (designated UGT76G1swap191-208 and UGT76G4swap191-208) exhibited significantly altered regioselectivity. Notably, UGT76G4swap191-208, when using Reb E as a substrate, predominantly produced Reb D $( 9 8 . 1 4 \pm \mathsf { \bar { 0 } } . 6 0 \%$ ) and negligible amounts of Reb AM (Fig.  3C), thereby indicating a shift in regioselectivity from C19 to C13. Comparable shifts were observed with Rub, with Reb G becoming the dominant product over STV B (SI Appendix, Fig. S8A). Notably, the mutant’s activity toward Reb E was enhanced more than that toward Rub (Fig. 3C and SI Appendix, Fig. S8A), suggesting a synergistic effect between C13 selectivity and disaccharide preference. Moreover, the mutated region did not alter the enzyme’s inherent preference for the substrate’s disaccharide, a synergy further confirmed by a notably increased activity against STV (SI Appendix, Fig. S8B) and a concomitant reduction in activity against Reb A (SI Appendix, Fig. S8C). 

Similarly, UGT76G1swap191-208 displayed altered regioselectivity, resulting in the production of $2 7 . 4 1 \pm 0 . 7 2 \%$ Reb AM and a significant increase in Reb M content $( 1 1 . 1 9 \pm 0 . 3 8 \%$ ) when catalyzing Reb E ( Fig. 3C ), with comparable shifts observed with Rub (SI Appendix, Fig. S8A ). Despite a reduction in overall activity ( Fig. 3C and SI Appendix, Fig. S8A ), UGT76G1swap191-208 $3 C$ exhibited an increase exceeding eightfold for Reb D and 10-fold for Reb A in its C19 glycosylation activity ( Fig. $3 D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S8C ), whereas its C13 glycosylation activity was decreased as evidenced by its reduced activity toward STV (SI Appendix, Fig. S8B ). 

The crystal structure of UGT76G1 (PDB ID: 6INH) ( 14 ) ( Fig. 3B ) revealed that residues 191 to 194, which form part of the C-terminus of α8, reside in the second shell of the substrate pocket and do not make direct contacts with the substrate. In contrast, residues 198 to 200 and 204 to 208—especially I199, L200, L204, M207, and I208—located at the N terminus of α9, constitute a hydrophobic barrier that prevents solvent exposure of the substrate’s hydrophobic core, with I199 and L200 directly interacting with the substrate ( Fig. 3B ). Mutagenesis of these residues in UGT76G4 to their counterparts in UGT76G1 demonstrated that the G200L mutation exerted the most pronounced effect, resulting in a switch in regioselectivity from C19 to C13 

( Fig. 3E ). Specifically, the G200L mutation catalyzed the conversion of Reb E and Rub predominantly into Reb D $( 9 6 . 5 7 ~ \pm$ $4 . 5 6 \%$ ) and Reb G $( 1 0 0 \% )$ ), respectively, similar to UGT76G1 ( Fig. $3 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. $S 8 D _ { \prime }$ ). Additionally, this mutant exhibited a marked reduction in activity against Reb D (SI Appendix, Fig. S8E ) and Reb A (SI Appendix, Fig. S8F ), while showing an enhancement in activity against STV (SI Appendix, Fig. S8G ). Other mutants, such as F204L, E205G, and N206K, also displayed altered regioselectivity, whereas the Q199I mutation augmented C19 regioselectivity across all tested substrates ( Fig. $3 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. $S 8 D { - } G$ ). 

To further probe the role of position 200, saturation mutagenesis was performed ( Fig. 3F ). Most mutants exhibited an increased preference for C13 glycosylation when using Reb E as the substrate, except for G200H, which favored C19 glycosylation. Notably, aromatic mutants (G200F, G200Y, and G200W) demonstrated more than a $5 0 \%$ enhancement in activity relative to the wild-type UGT76G4, with G200Y exhibiting the weakest C13 preference yet yielding the highest production of Reb M $( 1 6 . 4 0 \pm  { \bar { 0 } } . 6 1 \% )$ ). 

Surprisingly, mutating UGT76G1 at the equivalent position (L200) to glycine (L200G) did not alter its regioselectivity, but dramatically reduced activity across all substrates (STV, Reb E, and Reb D) (SI Appendix, Fig. S9 ). Similarly, aromatic substitutions at position L200 (i.e., L200F, L200Y, and L200W) significantly diminished catalytic activity relative to the wild-type enzyme. These findings indicated that the influence of residue 200 on regioselectivity in UGT76G4 is not fully recapitulated in UGT76G1; rather, the multiple amino acid differences between UGT76G4 and UGT76G1—particularly within the 198 to 208 region—collectively determine their substrate regioselectivity. Thus, mutating residue 200 in UGT76G1 alone is insufficient to reverse its regioselectivity. This observation explains why previous studies failed to recognize the crucial role of residue 200 in governing regioselectivity ( 21 – 23 ).

Similar to UGT76G1, UGT76G4 adopts a canonical GT-B fold comprising two Rossmann-like fold $( \beta / \alpha / \beta )$ domains that forms a catalytic cleft for substrate accommodation ( Fig. 4A ). The crystal structures of UGT76G4–UDPG (subunit A) and UGT76G1–UDP–Rub (PDB ID: 6INH) are nearly identical, with an rmsd of $0 . 6 0 \mathring \mathrm { ~ A }$ . In contrast, the UGT76G4–Rub and UGT76G1–UDP–Rub structures exhibit greater structural divergence (rmsd of 1.30 Å), particularly in regions involved in substrate binding (α7, α8, α9, α16, and adjacent loops) (SI Appendix, Fig. S12 ). 

In the UGT76G4–Rub complex, Rub adopts a U-shaped conformation with well-defined electron density (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 1 1 D _ { \prime } ^ { \prime }$ . Upon comparing the substrate binding pockets, the C19 glucose unit of Rub in UGT76G4 superimposes onto the C13 glucose of Rub in UGT76G1, occupying the catalytic position and interacting closely with H25 ( Fig. 4B and SI Appendix, Fig. S13 A and B ). However, the hydrophobic core of Rub in UGT76G4–Rub adopts a unique lying-down conformation, rotated by approximately $1 0 3 ^ { \circ }$ relative to its orientation in UGT76G1–UDP–Rub; this is attributable to the small glycine residue at position 200 (G200) in UGT76G4, which creates additional space for the substrate (SI Appendix, Fig. S13 A and C ). Furthermore, F204 adopts a conformation oriented away from the substrate via $\pi$ -stacking interactions with the hydroxyl group of T208, thereby further enlarging the binding cavity ( Fig. 4C ). 

A network of hydrogen bonds, involving water molecules W5, W7, and W46 stabilizes the U-shaped Rub within the UGT76G4 binding pocket ( Fig. 4D and SI Appendix, Fig. S14 ). Specifically, the C19 glucose forms direct hydrogen bonds with G87 and H25, as well as water-mediated hydrogen bonds with the C13 glucose, while the C13 glucose establishes direct hydrogen bonds with Q381 and N384 and a water-mediated bond with L379. This hydrogen bond network not only stabilizes Rub but also ensures the enzyme’s C19 regioselectivity. 

Significant conformational differences were observed near α16; in UGT76G4, α16 is partially unfolded (SI Appendix, Fig. S12 A and B ), thereby providing extra space for the C13 glucose of Rub ( Fig. 4E ). Additionally, an elongated loop near α16 shifts L379 from the inner cavity to the surface, forming a “lid” structure in conjunction with W197, Q199, and Q381 that stabilizes the bound substrate ( Fig. $4 E$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S13 C and E ). In contrast, UGT76G1 forms a “latch” configuration involving L200 and L379, forcing Rub into an upright posture with its C19 end exposed to solvent (SI Appendix, Figs. $\mathrm { S } 1 2 B$ and S13 $D$ and $F$ ). Interestingly, the UGT76G4–UDPG structure closely resembles that of UGT76G1 in this region (SI Appendix, Fig. S12C ), suggesting that the conformational changes observed in UGT76G4– Rub are induced by Rub binding. Moreover, the unfavorable interaction between the polar Q199 in UGT76G4 and the hydrophobic core of Rub—reflected by a minimum distance of 4.3 Å (SI Appendix, Fig. S13C )—contrasts with the shorter distance of $3 . 8 \mathring \mathrm { A }$ observed between I199 and Rub in UGT76G1 (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 1 3 D \rangle$ ), which may explain the enhanced C19 glycosylation activity observed for the Q199I variant ( Fig. 3E ).

Initially, given that the C19 glycosylation activity of the Q199I mutant surpassed that of the wild type enzyme (Fig. $3 E$ ), we performed saturation mutagenesis at residue Q199 and subsequently measured the activities of the resulting mutants using Reb D as the substrate. Among these, the Q199I mutant exhibited superior activity (Fig. 6A). Unexpectedly, mutation to leucine resulted in a marked reduction in activity, likely due to steric differences between leucine and isoleucine— with isoleucine promoting unique hydrophobic interactions 

and specific conformational states (35, 36)—contrasting with the previously reported UGT76G1-derived I199L mutant that showed enhanced activity against Reb D (21). 

To further enhance UGT76G4 activity, we combined the Q199I mutation with additional substitutions at position 200 (G200H, G200Y, and G200W). Three double mutants (Q199I/ G200H, Q199I/G200Y, and Q199I/G200W) exhibited significantly improved catalytic efficiency toward Reb E relative to the single-site mutants ( Fig. 7B ). In particular, the Q199I/G200Y mutant demonstrated a 2.2-fold increase in conversion rate toward Reb E compared to UGT76G4 and a 1.46-fold increase compared to UGT76G1, with nearly half of the products being Reb M— making it the most promising candidate for Reb M biosynthesis from Reb E. Although Q199I/G200W exhibited higher activity toward Reb E than Q199I/G200Y, its activity toward Reb D was considerably reduced, leading to decreased Reb M production ( Fig. 7 B and E ).

Fed-Batch Reaction for the Preparation of Reb M. To evaluate the industrial applicability of the Q199I/G200Y and H15S/Q199I/ G200Y variants for synthesizing Reb M from Reb E and Reb D, respectively, fed-batch fermentations were conducted. Owing to the low aqueous solubility of Reb E and Reb D, these substrates were introduced into the reaction system in five separate batches at staggered time points. In the Q199I/G200Y system, conversion of Reb E to Reb M commenced immediately (Fig. 7C); $7 0 \%$ of Reb E was consumed within $^ \textrm { \scriptsize 5 h }$ , followed by an exponential decline in Reb E concentration and a continuous increase in Reb M levels, ultimately yielding $5 4 \%$ conversion $( 1 3 . 6 2 \pm 0 . 5 5 \mathrm { m M } )$ ) at the reaction’s end. Reb D and Reb AM concentration remained at approximately 7 mM and 3 mM, respectively. Conversely, in the H155S/Q199I/G200Y system, the conversion of Reb D to 

Reb M was highly efficient (Fig. $7 D )$ ), reaching a plateau after $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ with $8 4 \%$ of Reb D converted $( 4 2 . 0 1 \pm 4 . 3 1 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ ). As the reaction proceeded, the conversion rate diminished—likely due to product inhibition and enzyme inactivation—with Reb M production reaching $4 5 . 0 3 \pm 1 . { \dot { 9 } } 2 \mathrm { m M }$ after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , corresponding to a yield exceeding $9 0 \%$ . 

Conclusions. In conclusion, we identified and characterized a UGT76G1 variant, UGT76G4, derived from Stevia cultivars with high levels of Reb D and Reb M. UGT76G4 exhibits distinct C19 regioselectivity for SG substrates in vitro and in vivo and is proposed to be the key enzyme responsible for Reb M biosynthesis in Stevia. Our Mutagenesis experiments revealed that G200 is critical in determining the C19 regioselectivity of UGT76G4—a finding further supported by crystal structural analysis and MD simulations. Leveraging these insights, we engineered UGT76G4 to generate the Q199I/G200Y and H155S/Q199I/G200Y mutants, which currently represent the most efficient enzymes for Reb M synthesis from Reb E and Reb D.
