# Introduction

The Lonicera genus encompasses approximately 180 species of vines and shrubs that are widely distributed in temperate climates across the Northern hemisphere [1]. Lonicera caerulea L., commonly known as blue honeysuckle, is a perennial fruit-bearing shrub belonging to the Caprifoliaceae family. It exhibits remarkable adaptability and resistance to cold and salinity, and it predominantly found in Asia, Europe, and North America [2]. This species holds significant economic, nutritional, and medicinal value owing to its extensive diversity, high productivity, and rich cultural heritage, making it an ideal plant for cultivation. Its small bluepurple berries are packed with diverse chemical compounds, including anthocyanins, flavonols, terpenes, iridoids, carotenoids, and phenolic acids [2]. These compounds possess various physiological functions, such as scavenging free radicals, regulating blood pressure, delaying aging, combating tumors, and enhancing immunity [3,4]. Due to its high content of biologically active compounds, ease of cultivation, early fruit harvest period, palatability, and ability to withstand frost, blue honeysuckle has gained popularity worldwide and is emerging as a sought-after small berry-yielding tree, closely following its counterparts like blueberries and sea buckthorn. 

Low-temperature stress, commonly referred to as cold stress, exerts significant detrimental effects on plant growth, productivity, and survival, ultimately restricting the geographic distribution of various plant species. The response mechanisms of plants to cold stress involve intricate and multifaceted processes encompassing oxidative systems, mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), and phytohormones [5]. Cold stress triggers the excessive generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), resulting in the peroxidation of DNA, lipids, and other essential plant components, thereby disrupting metabolic homeostasis [6]. Additionally, cold stress induces alterations in the hormonal balance of plants, leading to an increased accumulation of abscisic acid (ABA), which subsequently impacts membrane permeability and stimulates the expression of genes associated with cold stress response [7,8]. Blue honeysuckle exhibits remarkable ecological plasticity and adaptability to exceedingly cold climates, enduring temperatures as low as $- 4 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ [9]. The levels of raffinose and stachyose accumulated rapidly in response to cold stress, might be involved in the improvement of freezing tolerance of L. caerulea [10]. Nevertheless, the molecular mechanisms underlying freezing tolerance in L. caerulea remain largely unknown, primarily due to the limited availability of genetic resources that impede comprehensive exploration of the molecular pathways activated in response to low-temperature stress. 

The mature fruits of L. caerulea possess elevated levels of anthocyanins and flavonols, which are responsible for its fruit coloration as well as the potential health benefits. The anthocyanin content (TAC) of blue honeysuckle fruits varied from $1 . 6 ~ \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { k g }$ to $1 7 . 5 ~ \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { k g }$ 

in fresh weight, surpassing that of raspberries, blackberries, blueberries, and redcurrants [11]. The anthocyanins in L. caerulea consist of glucosides and rutinosides of cyanidin, peonidin, and delphinidin, as well as 3,5-dihexosides of cyanidin and peonidin. Notably, cyanidin-3-O-glucoside $( 8 3 - 9 3 \ \% )$ constitutes the predominant anthocyanin in L. caerulea, followed by cyanidin-3-Orutinoside $( 1 - 1 1 \ \mathrm { ~ \% ~ } )$ , cyanidin-3,5-O-diglucoside $( 2 - 6 \% )$ , and peonidin-3-O-glucoside $( 1 - 5 \ \% )$ [11,12]. Numerous studies have identified key genes involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, particularly in the model Arabidopsis thaliana [13]. Methyltransferases and glycosyltransferases play important roles in the water solubility and structural stability of anthocyanins [14,15]. Despite the distinctive traits of blue honeysuckle, such as fruit coloration and anthocyanin accumulation, the specific pathways and regulatory mechanisms involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis in L. caerulea are still not well understood. Given the significant accumulation of anthocyanins in this species, identifying highly efficient catalytic enzymes could provide valuable insights for elucidating the biosynthetic pathways of anthocyanins. 

Here, a chromosome-level blue honeysuckle genome was sequenced and assembled. The genomic comparison traced the evolution and duplicated events related to the specific traits of L. caerulea. The molecular physiology of freezing tolerance for L. caerulea was comprehensively investigated according to the different gene expressions of freezing and recovery treatments. Furthermore, the methylation and glycosylation of anthocyanin production in L. caerulea were identified via the genome-wide strategy and biochemical assays. The aim of this study was to investigate the molecular basis of cold tolerance and anthocyanin biosynthesis in L. caerulea, and to better understand the mechanisms of adaptation to cold stress and anthocyanin accumulation. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

Fresh young leaves of L. caerulea were collected from Heilongjiang Forest Botanical Garden (Harbin, China) for genome DNA sequencing and Hi-C library construction. The dormant buds of L. caerulea plants were subjected to low-temperature treatment for RNA sequencing. Dormant buds of L. caerulea were exposed to different low-temperature and freezing conditions, including $4 ^ { \circ } C ,$ , $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } C$ , $- 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and varying durations of freezing treatment (2, 6, and $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ ). 

# Genome sequencing, survey, and assembly

High-quality DNA was extracted from the fresh young leaves of an individual L. caerulea plant for the library construction of longread sequencing, short-read sequencing, and Hi-C sequencing. Total RNA was extracted from different tissues of the blue honey-

berry plants (stems, leaves, flowers, and fruits), as well as the different treatments of cold stress using the Plant RNAprep Mini Kit for constructing RNA-Seq libraries. Long-read libraries were sequenced using PacBio Sequel II platform (Pacific Biosciences, USA), and the circular consensus sequencing (CCS) data were generated [16]. Short-read, Hi-C, and RNA-Seq libraries were sequenced using the Illumina HiSeq X Ten platform. The crosslinked samples for Hi-C library were digested using DpnII restriction enzyme. 

For the raw data, FastQC was utilized to assess the quality of raw reads, focusing on metrics such as read length distribution, GC content, and the presence of adapter sequences. Low-quality reads and those with a Phred score below 20 were filtered out using Trimmomatic (v0.39) [17]. The genome size and heterozygosity of L. caerulea were predicted using $k$ -mer distribution of short-reads via the jellyfish (v2.2.10) and Genomescope (v2.0) [18]. The CCS data from PacBio Sequel II platform were directly assembled using Hifisam (v0.19.5) [16], and the assembled contigs were then reduced heterozygous duplication using purge_dups (v1.2.5). An allele-aware chromosome-level genome based on Hi-C data was generated using ALLHiC (v0.9.8) [19]. The integrity of the genome was evaluated using the Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs (BUSCO, v5.2.2) with the embryophyta_odb10 database [20]. Finally, LTRharvest (parameters: minlenltr, 100; maxlenltr, 5,000; and maxdistltr, 25,000) and LTR Finder (v.1.07) were used to identify the intact long terminal repeat (LTR) elements in both L. caerulea and L. japonica genomes [21]. The insertion time (T) of LTRs was calculated using the formula $\mathrm { T } = \mathrm { K } / 2 \mathrm { r }$ (r = 7e-9). 

# Genome annotation

De novo prediction of repeat elements was performed by combining the LTR and consensus repetitive libraries using RepeatModeler (v2.0.3), and RepeatMasker (v4.1.2) was employed to mask and calculate the classification of repeat elements [22]. Fulllength transposable elements (TEs) in L. caerulea and L. japonica were detected by HiTE software. Gene annotation was accomplished via integrating de novo prediction, homologous protein evidence, and transcript evidence. The clean RNA-Seq data were assembled using Trinity (v.2.8.5) [23] and mapped to reference genome using HiSAT2 (v2.2.1) [24]. For ab initio prediction, the AUGUSTUS model was trained by BRAKER2 (v2.1.5) using the RNA-Seq data [25]. In addition, SNAP (v2017-03–01) was trained using MAKER (v3.1.3) with two iterations. Furthermore, the ab initio prediction integrating the homologous evidence were combined to annotate the final gene models of L. caerulea genome using the MAKER pipeline [26]. The predicted gene models were searched against the InterPro and Pfam databases using EggNOG-mapper to perform functional annotation [27]. 

# Comparative genomic analysis

The orthologous groups of L. caerulea and thirteen other sequenced plant species, including L. japonica, Daucus carota, Panax notoginseng, Stevia rebaudiana, Carthamus tinctorius, Arctium lappa, Helianthus annuus, Mikania micrantha, Cynara cardunculus, Erigeron canadensis, Aralia elata, Coriandrum sativum and one outgroup species (Vitis vinifera), were identified using OrthoFinder (v2.3.11) [28]. The identified single-copy genes were chosen to construct the phylogenetic tree using RAxML (v8.0.17) with GTRGAMMA model and 1000 bootstrap replicates [29]. Divergence time among the fourteen species were estimated using the MCMCTREE script of the PAML package (v4.9) with 2,000 iterations and a sample frequency of 10 after 20,000 iterations as the burn-in [30]. The divergence times were estimated based on the following fossil ages 

from the TimeTree database (https://www.timetree.org): D. carota and P. notoginseng diverged 54.3–69.0 million years ago (MYA), H. annuus and E. canadensis diverged 28.5–40.6 MYA, C. tinctorius and E. canadensis diverged 36.6–45.1 MYA, L. japonica and C. tinctorius diverged 76.0–97.4 MYA, and L. caerulea and V. vinifera diverged 111.4–123.9 MYA. Gene family expansions and contractions analysis were further inferred using CAFE (v4.2.1) with a family-wide $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 1$ and Viterbi $p$ -value $< 0 . 0 1$ defined as significantly expanded or contracted gene families [31]. Visualization was performed using Python scripts and iTOL online tool (https://itol. embl.de/). Functional enrichment analysis of expanded gene families based on Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) databases was performed using ClusterProfiler (v4.0) [32]. Positive selection analysis was performed on the candidate species (L. caerulea, L. japonica, D. carota, P. notoginseng, A. elata, and C. sativum) using the branch-site model of CallCodeml software (https://github.com/byemaxx/callCodeml). The configured fix_omega ${ \bf \mu } = { \bf 0 }$ was applied to estimate the x (omega) parameter, which reflects the selection intensity. An x value greater than 1 was interpreted as evidence of positive selection, and the significance of positive selection genes was verified by the Chi-square test ( $p$ -value < 1e-5), indicating that certain genes may have undergone adaptive evolution. 

# Whole-genome duplication analysis

The synonymous substitutions per synonymous site $( K _ { S } )$ distributions for orthologous pairs among candidate species and paralogs within candidate species were analyzed using WGD (v1.1.1). Homologous pairs were identified using BLAST in an allvs-all protein sequence search, and gene families were constructed using the MCL package. The CODEML of the PAML package was used to estimate $K _ { S }$ values for each homologous pair. The ksrates (https://github.com/VIB-PSB/ksrates) was employed to position the whole-genome duplication (WGD) events via adjusted mixed plots of paralog and ortholog $K _ { S }$ distributions [33]. Anchored paralogous genes refer to paralogous gene pairs that are conserved in position across species and are used to infer synteny and chromosomal rearrangements. 

Genome-wide duplications in blue honeysuckle were classified into five categories using DupGen_Finder (v1.12) with default parameters (https://github.com/qiao-xin/DupGen_finder). onsynonymous substitution ates $\left( K _ { \mathbf { a } } \right)$ , synonymous substitution rates $\left( K _ { s } \right)$ , and $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ values were calculated for the duplicated gene pairs using the YN model in the KaKs_Calculator2 [34]. Syntenic blocks within and between genomes were identified using MCScanX (v1.1) with default parameters [35]. The inversion fragments between the chromosomal pseudomolecules of L. caerulea and L. japonica were identified using MUMmer (v3.2.3) [36]. Genomic rearrangements and structural variation between the two species were identified via SyRI (v1.6) with the following parameters: k, F, S [37]. 

# Identification of genes and TFs involved in freezing tolerance

To identify potential freezing tolerance genes in the L. caerulea genome, the BLASTP (v2.11.0) homologous search was employed using the known protein sequences of cold tolerance pathway genes from A. thaliana as query sequences with an E-value cutoff of 1e-10. The gene expression and co-expression network was constructed via integrating the transcriptome data, the identified freezing tolerance genes, and transcription factors (TFs). Correlation analysis was performed using the OmicStudio tools (https:// www.omicstudio.cn/tool/62), which employs the R packages ggplot2 and stats. The filtering criteria applied were $| \mathbf { R } | \geq 0 . 8$ and $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5$ , or $| \mathbf { R } | \geq 0 . 9$ and $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 1$ . The FPKM (fragments per kilo-

base per million) values of gene expression were calculated and quantified using HiSAT2 (v2.2.1) and FeatureCounts [38]. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified and normalized using the DESeq2, with FDR $< ~ 0 . 0 5$ and fold change threshold of $\geq 2$ [39]. Transcription factors were identified using PlantTFDB with default parameters, and the co-expression networks between genes and transcription factors were displayed using Cytoscape (v3.7.1) [40]. 

# Identification of OMT and UGT family members

The O-methyltransferase (OMT) domains (PF00891 and PF01596) and UDP-glycosyltransferase (UGT) domain (PF00201.20) were retrieved from the PFAM database (https:// pfam.sanger.ac.uk/). The HMMER (v3.0) was utilized to identify the OMT and UGT genes in the L. caerulea genome with a threshold value of 1e-5 [41], respectively. In addition, the 1194 UGT genes from Arabidopsis, Gossypium, and Epimedium as query sequences were used to identify the candidate UGT genes of L. caerulea using BLAST+ (v2.14.1) with the E-value of 1e-5. The UGT annotations obtained from HMMER and BLASTP were merged. The UGT candidate sequences were analyzed in the conserved UDPGT domain using the conserved domain database (CDD, https://www.ncbi. nlm.nih.gov/Structure/cdd) and the PFAM database. The conserved PSPG motifs for UGT candidate genes were further identified using MEME Suite tools [41,42]. LcUGTs were named by the UGT Nomenclature Committee [43]. The phylogenetic relationship of LcUGTs and other functional UGT sequences was analyzed using MAFFT (v7.520) alignment [44], TrimAI (v1.4) pruning, and IQ-TREE (v2.2.2.7) phylogeny with 1000 bootstraps [45]. 

# Catalytic analysis of candidate LcOMTs and LcUGTs

Candidate LcOMTs and LcUGTs were cloned into pET-28a $( + )$ E. coli expression vector according to the primers listed in Supplemental Table S19. Reported At3GlcT, Vv3GlcT, PnUGT, and LjUGT were directly synthesized into pET-28a $( + )$ vector by Synbiob Gene Technology (Tianjin, China). Recombinant plasmids pET-28a-LcOMT and LcUGT were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells. All recombinant proteins were purified using the His-tag Protein Purification Kit (Beyotime, Beijing, China) following the instructions. The purified proteins were then analyzed using SDS-PAGE. The purified protein concentration was determined using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Beyotime, Beijing, China). 

For in vitro enzymatic assays of OMTs, the reactions containing the recombinant OMT proteins, $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m M } }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 1 mM SAM, and $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ substrates (cyanidin, pelargonidin, or delphinidin) were performed. For in vitro enzymatic assays of UGTs, the reactions containing the recombinant UGTs, $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { \ m M }$ Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), $0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sugar donors (UDP-glucose), and $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sugar acceptors (cyanidin, pelargonidin, delphinidin, or peonidin) were performed. The reactions were conducted at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 30 min, and then was terminated by adding $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of ice-cold methanol with 0.01 % HCL (Fig. S22). Then, the samples were filtered and purified for LC-MS/MS analysis. 

LC-MS/MS analysis was conducted using an Agilent Technologies 1290 Infinity Ⅱ coupled with a 6530 Q-TOF mass spectrometer equipped with an Acquity UPLC® BEH C18 column $( 1 . 7 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $1 0 0 \ : \mathrm { m m } \times 2 . 1 \ : \mathrm { m m } )$ ) at a flow rate of $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The mobile phase consisted of water containing $0 . 1 \ \%$ formic acid $( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } , \boldsymbol { \mathsf { A } } )$ and acetonitrile (B). The linear gradient elution program for LcUGTs was as follows: $3 \% B$ at $0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $1 0 \ \% \ \mathsf { B }$ at 19 min, $1 0 0 \% \mathrm { ~ B ~ }$ at $1 1 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $1 0 0 \% \mathrm { ~ B ~ }$ at 13 min, $3 \% \mathrm { ~ B ~ }$ at 13.5 min, and $3 \% B$ at 17 min. # Results

# Chromosome-scale assembly and annotation of L. caerulea

The karyotype of L. caerulea comprises chromosomes of varying lengths with a diploid number of $2 \mathrm { ~ N ~ } = 2 \mathrm { X } = 1 8$ . Here, a combination of Illumina short reads $( { \sim } 2 7 4 \mathrm { G b } )$ ), PacBio HiFi reads ${ \bf \zeta } \sim 2 9 { \bf G } { \bf b } )$ ), and Hi-C data $\mathord {  } 9 3$ Gb) was employed to assemble the L. caerulea genome. The distribution of $k$ -mer frequencies indicated a genome size of 707.84 Mb with a high level of heterozygosity of approximately $1 . 6 3 \ \%$ (Table S1 and Fig. S1). The initial genome assembly was 761.07 Mb with the longest contig of 4.9 Mb (Table S2). The completeness of the assembled genome was evaluated using the BUSCO evaluation, which indicated a completeness of $9 2 . 7 0 \%$ , suggesting that the blue honeysuckle genome assembly was of high quality (Table S3). Based on the Hi-C reads, $9 9 . 9 9 \%$ of the assembled contig sequences were anchored to nine pseudochromosomes with a total length of 758.85 Mb and a super scaffold N50 value of 83.02 Mb (Fig. 1A, Table S2, S4 and Fig. S2). Moreover, the chromosome-level genome of L. caerulea was $9 2 . 5 0 \ \%$ BUSCO completeness (Table S5). 

The genome of L. caerulea contains $6 3 . 8 0 \ \%$ non-redundant repetitive elements, similar to L. japonica (64.76 %) [46]. LTR elements were the most abundant, accounting for $\sim 2 8 . 1 3 \ \%$ of the L. caerulea genome, including $1 8 . 5 8 \ \%$ LTR/Gypsy and $7 . 1 1 \ \% \mathrm { L T R } { } /$ Copia types (Table S6). A total of 48,206 protein-coding genes were confidently identified in L. caerulea genome. Specifically, 1891 TFs were identified among 58 families in the L. caerulea, including 30 AP2, 145 bHLH, 68 bZIP, 165 ERF, 140 MYB, 59 WRKY, and 92 C2H2 members (Table S7). 

# Evolutionary analysis of L. caerulea genome

A highly reliable phylogenetic tree was constructed using shared 344 single-copy genes, and the divergence times of L. caerulea and 13 other plant species were estimated (Table S8). As expected, L. caerulea and L. japonica from Caprifoliaceae (Dipsacales) cluster into one branch, and form a sister group with the Apiales species (P. notoginseng, A. elata, C. sativum and D. carota) (Fig. 1B). The estimated divergence time between L. caerulea and L. japonica was $\sim 1 3 . 8 6$ MYA with a $9 5 \ \%$ confidence interval of 6.08–20.57 MYA, based on fossil-calibrated phylogeny. The split between Dipsacales and Apiales occurred approximately 77.19 MYA (Fig. 1B).

According to the results, 5713 gene families were expanded, 3338 gene families were contracted in L. caerulea, 2815 gene families were expanded, and 6352 gene families were contracted in L. japonica (Fig. 1B). Dynamic changes of duplicated genes of L. caerulea and L. japonica 

Gene duplication has been widely accepted as a crucial driving force in plant evolution, facilitating subfunctionalization and neofunctionalization of genes. Here, a total of 41,107 and 29,165 dupli-

cated genes were observed in L. caerulea and L. japonica, respectively, which were categorized into five types, including WGD, tandem duplicates (TD), proximal duplicates (PD), transposed duplicates (TRD), and dispersed duplicates (DSD). Specifically, L. caerulea exhibited 9,896 WGDs, 2,802 TDs, 4,695 PDs, 8,513 TRDs, and 15,199 DSDs, whereas L. japonica contained 10,664 WGDs, 3,589 TDs, 3,717 PDs, 6,043 TRDs, and 5,152 DSDs (Fig. 2A). Furthermore, L. caerulea exhibited a more recent LTR burst at $\sim 0 . 1 5$ MYA, whereas L. japonica had a peak at $\sim 0 . 4 5$ MYA, indicating higher LTR activities in L. caerulea than in L. japonica, which might contribute to the variation of TRD and DSD distributions (Fig. S8). 

In addition, the $K _ { s }$ and $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ distribution across various modes of duplication from L. caerulea and L. japonica indicated that TD and PD gene pairs had higher $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ ratios and relatively smaller $K _ { s }$ values than other duplication types (Fig. 2B). These findings suggest that younger TD and PD genes have recently evolved, which might be subject to faster divergence and stronger selection stress towards neo- or sub-functionalization. Additionally, the GO enrichment annotations and KEGG pathway classification revealed that WGD, TD, and PD genes were mainly enriched in the plant hormone signal transduction, MAPK signaling pathway, and biosynthesis of various secondary metabolites of L. caerulea and L. japonica (Fig. S9). These results indicated that the duplicated genes might play critical roles in phytochemical reconstruction and environmental stress responses (Fig. 2C). 

# Molecular physiology of freezing tolerance in L. caerulea

L. caerulea has evolved the ability to survive freezing temperatures due to its geographical location. Here, the gene regulation and signal transduction related to the freezing tolerance of L. caerulea were comprehensively observed. The different gene expression analyses under before and after freezing treatments revealed that ABA signaling pathway was positively related to freezing stress in L. caerulea. Specifically, the expression of pyrabactin resistant gene (LcPYL4, LcPYL5), protein phosphatase 2C gene (LcPP2C6), and SNF1-related protein kinase 2 (LcSNRK2-3) presented upregulated expression after freezing treatments. Notably, the abscisic acid-responsive transcription factors (LcABF5) displayed the similar expression trends, suggesting the ABA signaling pathway plays an important role in mediating the cold sensitivity and promoting freezing tolerance in L. caerulea (Fig. 3A, Fig. S10). The ICE1- CBF-COR signaling pathway was crucial component of the lowtemperature response in plants [5]. Furthermore, it has been found that calmodulin-binding transcription activator 3 (LcCAMTA3-5, LcCAMTA3-6), inducer of CBF expression 1 (LcICE1-2, LcICE1-9, and LcICE1-12), C-repeat-binding factors (LcCBF4, LcCBF6, LcCBF12, etc.), as well as cold-regulated genes (LcCOR1, LcCOR3, and LcCOR5) were upregulated following two hours of freezing treatment at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C _ { \cdot }$ , $- 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , and $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in L. caerulea. Remarkably, genes encoding superoxide dismutase (LcSOD6, LcSOD7) and dehydroascorbate reductase (LcDHAR7, LcDHAR9), which were involved in ROS scavenging in L. caerulea showed obvious up-regulation in response to environmental stress after 2 h and 6 h of freezing treatment (Fig. S10) [47]. These results highlighted the complex network of gene regulation and signaling pathways related to freezing tolerance, including the ABA, ICE-CBF-COR, and ROS scavenging pathways. 

A co-expression network of genes and TFs $| \mathbf { R } | \geq 0 . 8$ , $\mathrm { P } < 0 . 0 5$ $\mathsf { F P K M } \ge 5 0$ ) using LcCBFs as hub genes was constructed, and the results showed that the TF families, including WRKY, MYB, bZIP, ARF, COR, and bHLH, may play crucial roles in the freezing tolerance of L. caerulea (Fig. 3B). Additionally, eight LcC2H2s, seven LcCDPKs, 

nine LcGATAs, twelve LcMIKC_MADSs, and numerous other TFs (NAC, HSF, GRF, GRAS, B3, etc) were shown to be tightly connected to the hub gene in the regulatory network between hub genes and TFs. After further filtering of data $| \mathbf { R } | \geq 0 . 9$ , $\mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 1$ ), genes such as LcPP2C6, LcCOR5, LcERF8, LcNAC4, LcGRAS4, and LcMYB9 showed strong correlations and were notably upregulated following two hours of treatment at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ (Fig. S11). In summary, the extensive connections observed with various TF families suggest a complex regulatory framework that may enhance cold resilience in L. caerulea.

# Identification and expression profiles of anthocyanins biosynthetic genes

Anthocyanins are a class of flavonoid pigments synthesized through the phenylpropanoid pathway. The initial step in synthesizing anthocyanidins involves using p-coumaroyl-coenzyme A (CoA), derived from the phenylpropanoid pathway and originates from chalcone synthase (CHS). The biosynthesis of anthocyanidins encompasses the involvement of several key enzymes, including chalcone isomerase (CHI), flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H), flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase (F3 H), flavonoid $3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime }$ -hydroxylase $( F 3 ^ { \prime } 5 ^ { \prime } H )$ , dihydroflavonol 4-reductase (DFR), and dioxygenase/anthocyanidin synthase (LDOX/ANS) (Fig. S15A). The upstream genes involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, including LcPAL3 (phenylalanine ammonia-lyase), Lc4CL1 (4-coumarate-CoA ligase), LcANS4, LcCHI3, LcCHS4, LcDFR1, LcF3H3, LcF3 H3, and LcF3 5 H1 were identified, and most of them showed high expression along with fruit development (Fig. S15B). Subsequently, the downstream stage of anthocyanin production entails the conjugation of methyl and sugar moieties to anthocyanidins under the catalysis of OMTs and UGTs (Fig. S16). 

Anthocyanin accumulation has been reported to respond positively to environmental stress. Here, among the DEGs upregulated under cold stress, 22 genes were involved in anthocyanin biosynthesis, particularly showing a significant increase in expression after $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of treatment at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ or $- 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The heatmap analysis revealed that the expression of key genes in this pathway, specifically Lc4CL1, LcC4H1, LcPAL2, LcF3 H2, and LcUGT95P1, increased with cold treatment (Fig. S17). These results suggest that the accumulation of anthocyanins could play a beneficial role in response to freezing stress. Thus, further investigations into the functional roles of these anthocyanin biosynthesis genes could provide deeper insights into their contribution to cold tolerance in plants.

The catalytic assays of LcUGT95P1 produced three monoglycosides 4G1 (8.314 min), 4G2 (7.487 min), and 4G3 (6.908 min) (Fig. 5F), and LcUGT78X1 produced one obvious monoglycoside compound $( 7 . 5 2 5 \ \mathrm { \ m i n } )$ ). These results demonstrated that LcUGT78X1 and 

LcUGT95P1 have contributed important roles in the L. caerulea anthocyanin biosynthesis. 

The synthenic orthologs of LcUGT78X1, PnUGT from Panax notoginseng and LjUGT from L. japonica (Fig. 5G), together with the reported 3-O-glycosylated cyanidin glycosyltransferases, At3GlcT and Vv3GlcT [50,51], were further chosen to study their catalytic efficiency. In addition, the LcUGT78X1 showed higher sequence identity with that of LjUGT $( 8 9 . 2 6 ~ \% )$ than with that of PnUGT $( 5 3 . 0 8 \ \% )$ , Vv3GlcT $( 5 2 . 9 2 \ \% )$ and At3GlcT $( 5 0 . 1 5 ~ \% )$ . HPLC analysis indicated that both PnUGT and LjUGT could glycosylate 3-OH group of cyanidin to produce cyanidin-3-O-glycoside (Fig. S21), which suggests that the function of 3-O-glucosylation is evolutionarily conserved. Enzyme kinetic parameter displayed that LcUGT78X1, Vv3GlcT, and LjUGT showed higher $K _ { c a t } / K _ { m }$ values with 439, 510, and $4 3 3 s ^ { - 1 } { \mathsf { M } } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively, which indicated these three recombinant UGT proteins have high catalytic efficiency toward cyanidin (Fig. 5H). Of interest, Vv3GlcT and LjUGT presented the substrate inhibition along with the increase of substrate concentration, but not for LcUGT78X1, which may be one of the reasons for the highest content of cyanidin-3-O-glycoside in L. caerulea. 

# Discussion

L. caerulea exhibits exceptional frost-resistant capabilities that enable it to thrive in extremely freezing climates. Consequently, blue honeysuckle has frequently been employed as a model organism in studies focusing on plant cold tolerance. Additionally, the fruits of blue honeysuckle represent a unique nutritional source, rich in anthocyanins, proanthocyanidins, and other bioactive compounds. The genomic insights of L. caerulea contribute to the molecular elucidation of freezing tolerance and the anthocyanin biosynthesis.

Polyploidization is a pivotal mechanism that plays a significant role in plant adaptation to environmental conditions and genome evolution [52]. The results showed that L. caerulea and L. japonica shared a WGD event (Fig. 1C). Additionally, the amount of DSDs in the L. caerulea and L. japonica genomes was observed, reflecting the considerable chromosomal rearrangements after WGD (Fig. 2A). The results support recent investigation that extensive transposed and dispersed gene duplications accompanied the occurrence of WGD [53]. Furthermore, a high frequency of TDs and PDs was observed in chromosomal regions containing a high density of WGD-derived genes. An enrichment of TDs and PDs in gene ontology terms related to defense response, plant hormone signal transduction, and basal transcription factors, consistent with previous reports [54,55]. TDs have been reported to drive gene dosage in specific biosynthetic pathways [56] such as anthocyanin, oleanane-type triterpenoid and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis in Rhododendron simsii [57], Aralia elata [58], and Angelica sinensis [59], respectively. Additionally, the GO terms and KEGG pathways revealed that WGD, TD and PD genes were mostly concentrated in 

plant hormone signal transduction and biosynthesis of various secondary metabolites o L. caerulea (Fig. 2C). Notably, different modes of duplicated genes showed distinct evolutionary patterns in the protein-coding region. PDs and TDs had relatively high $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ ratios but small $K _ { s }$ values, indicating that they have undergone rapid functional divergence compared to other gene classes [60]. In contrast, WGD-derived pairs were more conserved with large $K _ { s }$ values and low $K _ { \mathrm { a } } / K _ { s }$ ratios than tandem and proximal duplicates, indicating that they have evolved under long-term purifying selection (Fig. 2B). These findings provide insights into the evolution and retention of duplicated genes in plants. 

Upon exposure to low-temperature stress, plants mobilize a repertoire of cellular and molecular mechanisms to adapt to adverse conditions [61]. Generally, low-temperature stress is categorized into chilling $( 0 / 1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and freezing (below $0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). When exposed to freezing temperatures, the cold-tolerant plants modify their homeostasis to achieve freezing tolerance [62]. L. caerulea has developed sophisticated capabilities that perceive and respond to freezing stress through cold acclimation. Based on the expression 

patterns of key candidate genes in response to different freezing treatments, a hypothesized model of cold acclimation in L. caerulea buds was developed. Upon sensing an external chilling signal, an upstream signal was transmitted downstream through several signaling pathways, including hormonal, ${ \mathsf { C a } } ^ { 2 + }$ , MAPK, and ABA signaling pathways. In this study, the plant hormone signal transduction pathway exhibited the highest number of DEGs under chilling stress. Similarly, the genes encoding CRLK, MKK, and MPK exhibited significant upregulation upon cold treatment, consistent with previous investigations in Arabidopsis [63]. Chen et al. observed a connection between ABA signaling and cold stress response genes in pineapple [64]. Notably, differential expression of genes encoding PYL, PP2C, and ABF proteins was also observed in this study, indicating that the ABA signaling pathway provides important roles in the freezing tolerance of L. caerulea (Fig. 3A). TFs exhibit a high level of specificity towards distinct plant families and perform a pivotal function in governing the initial transmission of cold signals. Various gene families, namely AP2/ERF, MYB, bHLH, bZIP, ARF, and WRKY, primarily participate in response to lowtemperature conditions [65,66]. CBFs are recognized as key TFs involved in cold acclimation, and ICE-CBF-COR signaling pathways have been extensively characterized during cold signal transduction [67,68]. The co-expression network analysis revealed that CBFs serves as core TFs in the cold response of L. caerulea (Fig. 3B). Interestingly, it was found that SOD, POD, and CAT were up-regulated in response to cold stress. The enhanced activity of these enzymes corresponds to a higher resistance level in plants against cold stress. Furthermore, the enriched GO terms associated with the identified TFs and their DEGs suggest the substantial involvement of plant hormone signal transduction, lipid metabolism, and sugar metabolism in the development of frost hardiness in dormant buds (Fig. 3C). This study provides new insights into the transcriptional regulations involved in early cold response in L. caerulea. 

Anthocyanins are naturally occurring water-soluble pigments widely distributed in L. caerulea displaying diverse biological activities [12]. Two crucial modifications, namely glycosylation and methylation, play significant roles in stabilizing the properties of anthocyanins [69–71]. Anthocyanin methyltransferases belong to a large family of OMTs [72], that play a crucial role in the biosynthesis of methylated anthocyanidins [73,74]. For example, VcOMT specifically utilizes delphinidin as a substrate, while cyanidin and petunidin are not accepted [74–76]. This study discovered three anthocyanin O-methyltransferases, namely LcOMT2, LcOMT14, and LcOMT20, which display broader substrate promiscuity and regioselectivity. These enzymes catalyze the methylation of cyanidin (1), petunidin (9), and delphinidin (3) (Fig. 4C, 4D), leading to the generation of a wider range of methylated anthocyanidins. Cyanidin-3-O-glucoside, the dominant anthocyanin compound in L. caerulea, demonstrates a conspicuous presence during the maturation of its fruits [77]. Certain UGTs with strong catalytic abilities were identified as playing an important role in the buildup of cyanidin-3-O-glucoside. Here, two UGTs, namely LcUGT78X1 and LcUGT95P1, were discovered to demonstrate the ability to transfer a glucose molecule to cyanidin at the 3-OH position (Fig. 5C). Notably, LcUGT78X1 shows greater potential for efficient catalysis than LcUGT95P1, as it selectively produces a single product during the glycosylation process. Interestingly, the expression of the LcUGT78X1 gene gradually increases as the fruit develops from Fr1 to Fr8 (Fig. 5B), which aligns with the observed accumulation pattern of cyanidin-3-O-glucoside [77]. The enzyme kinetics analysis revealed that LcUGT78X1 exhibits a significantly high catalytic efficiency in converting cyanidin to cyanidin-3-O-glucoside. Additionally, PnUGT and LjUGT were observed to also possess 3-Oglycosyltransferase activity towards cyanidin (Fig. S18), indicating the evolutionary conservation of the 3-O-glucosidic function of LcUGT78X1. Notably, the catalytic efficiency of LcUGT78X1 and 

LjUGT, belonging to Caprifoliaceae, was approximately 2.5 times higher than that of PnUGT from Araliaceae (Fig. 5H). This suggests the occurrence of specific evolutionary adaptations leading to enhanced catalytic efficiency in 3-O-glycosyltransferases within the Caprifoliaceae family. 

The substrate inhibition refers to the observation that the reaction rate initially increases with increasing substrate concentration until reaching a maximum, after which it decreases [78;79,80]. The enzymatic activities of At3GlcT and Vv3GlcT, which have both been reported to exhibit 3-O-glycosyltransferase activity towards cyanidin [50,51]. Interestingly, Vv3GlcT exhibited comparable high catalytic efficiency to LcUGT78X1 and LjUGT. However, it is noteworthy that both LjUGT and Vv3GlcT exhibited substrate inhibition at elevated substrate concentrations, which may potentially limit the activity of 3-O-glycosyltransferases and consequently impact the accumulation of cyanidin-3-O-glucoside. These findings collectively demonstrate the significant role of LcUGT78X1 in driving the biosynthesis of cyanidin-3-O-glucoside as the dominant anthocyanin in L. caerulea. Furthermore, this study lays the foundation for understanding the methylation and glycosylation of anthocyanins in L. caerulea. 

# Conclusion

This study presents a chromosome-level genome assembly of L. caerulea, indicating that gene duplication events have significantly enhanced phytochemical reconstruction and the plant’s environmental stress responses. A comprehensive regulatory network was clarified, particularly involving the ABA and ICE-CBF-COR signaling pathways, alongside antioxidant enzymes crucial for freezing tolerance. Additionally, three OMTs and two UGTs were biochemically characterized as anthocyanin biosynthetic enzymes. Notably, the fruit-specific expressed LcUGT78X1 showed high efficiency in synthesizing cyanidin-3-O-glucoside, the major anthocyanin found in L. caerulea fruits. This research enhances the understanding of cold tolerance mechanism and anthocyanin biosynthesis in blue honeysuckle.
