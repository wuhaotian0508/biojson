# ABSTRACT

The magnetic field is a continuously present environmental factor. It has been found that many species, including plants, can sense and utilise it. However, the effects of the magnetic field on plants and its potential utilisation, especially in crops, have been little explored. Here, we found that static magnetic field (SMF) treatment increased wheat growth regardless of the magnetic field polarity. Specifically, the root system of wheat increased dramatically in both root number and length in a magnetic field strength-dependent manner, leading to higher fresh biomass in plants subjected to SMF treatment. Concurrently, the expression profile showed enrichment in nitrogen (N), jasmonates (JAs) and light signalling pathways under SMF treatment. Indeed, wheat N uptake was enhanced by SMF, and JAs content was dramatically reduced in magnetically treated wheat seedlings, consistent with the downregulation of JAs biosynthesis genes. Furthermore, this magnetic repression of JAs was abolished in dark conditions. We further demonstrated that SMF also induced the light signalling pathway, including the core transcription factor HY5, which directly binds to the promoters of JAs biosynthesis genes (LOX2, OPR3 and JAR1) and represses their expression by recruitment of HDA9. Consistently, JA application and hy5 knock out blocked SMF effect on wheat N absorption. Finally, SMF repressed JAs by HY5 recruitment of HDA9 to deacetylation of JAs biosynthesis gene sites. These results demonstrated a new finding of the magnetic signalling pathway in plants and implied a great potential for utilising magnets to promote N use efficiency in wheat. 

# 1 | Introduction

The geomagnetic field (GMF) is a continuously present environmental factor that affects all objects on Earth, including living organisms. Throughout evolution, it has served 

as a significant resource for various species to sense and adapt to their environment. Many animals utilise the GMF for orientation during migration (Mora et  al.  2004; Válková and Vácha  2012; Lohmann et  al.  2022). It has been found that cryptochromes (CRYs) function as magnetic receptors 

in animals to sense the magnetic field. Similarly, plants can sense static magnetic fields (SMFs) and alter their gene expression profiles. 

Plants can respond to a wide range of SMF strengths, from $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { n T }$ to 1 T (Xu et al. 2014; Dhiman and Galland 2018; Abdel Latef et al. 2020). For example, the flowering of Arabidopsis grown in a near-null magnetic field (NNMF) was suppressed compared with normal GMF conditions; the biomass and seed yield also decreased. It was reported that Arabidopsis cry1/cry2 mutants were insensitive to NNMF treatment (Xu et al. 2018). Additionally, CRY1 and CRY2 protein phosphorylation was depressed under NNMF, while dephosphorylation was enhanced in the dark (Xu et  al.  2012). CRY1 phosphorylation induced by SMF resembled its light-activated state, and the hypocotyl length was shorter under higher magnetic fields, consistent with CRY1's function in light signalling (Jin et al. 2019). Crops also respond to SMF. For example, wheat seeds showed increased germination rates when treated with 30 mT SMF. Pre-treatment of cotton and corn seeds with electric magnetic fields improved germination, plant fresh and dry weight, and yield (Bilalis et  al.  2012). Finally, different SMF exposure times and strengths led to faster germination, higher germination rates, longer shoots and roots in tomatoes (Poinapen et al. 2013), whereas wheat germination rates were repressed under 12.5 or 25 mT SMF (Erez and Özbek 2024). 

Till now, the auxin signalling pathway has been found to be involved in plant morphological responses to the magnetic field. The indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) level in roots was significantly higher under NNMF than under GMF, with the induction of the auxin marker genes PIN1/3/7. These changes were not observed in cry1/cry2 mutants (Xu et al. 2018). Under 600 mT SMF treatment, auxin levels were higher, and the expression of PIN3 and AUX1 genes was regulated accordingly in the root tips, leading to longer roots, which was not the case in cry1/cry2 mutants. Magnetic polarity, specifically north to south parallel to gravity, induced these phenotypes (Jin et al. 2019). 

HY5 (ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5), a central bZIP transcription factor, functions as a master integrator of environmental signalling networks that orchestrate plant development. Its stability is tightly regulated by light intensity, with accumulation increasing in response to light exposure (Osterlund et  al.  2000). In darkness, HY5 turnover is primarily governed by the COP1-mediated 26S proteasome pathway, which targets HY5 for degradation. Photoactivated phytochromes and cryptochromes negatively regulate the activity of two E3 ubiquitin ligase complexes, CUL4-DDB1-COP1-SPA and CUL4-DDB1- COP10-DET1, thereby preventing HY5 ubiquitination and degradation to promote photomorphogenic development (Xu 2020). Beyond light, HY5 mediates crosstalk among temperature, nutrient and hormone signalling pathways. For instance, cold stress induces COP1 nuclear exclusion to stabilise HY5 activity (Catalá et  al.  2011). HY5 also influences nutrient homeostasis by directly activating the transcription of nitrate and phosphate transporters (Mankotia et al. 2024). In hormonal networks, HY5 plays a central role by transcriptionally inducing the abscisic acid (ABA) biosynthetic gene NCED6 (Wang et  al.  2019) and suppressing gibberellin synthesis via activation of the gibberellin metabolic gene GA2ox2 (Weller et al. 2009). 

We know jasmonic acid (JA) and its derivatives, named jasmonates (JAs), are lipid-derived plant hormones that regulate development and stress responses, such as wounding and immunity (Wasternack and Hause 2019). Increasing evidence shows that JAs are also involved in growth and development processes (Wasternack 2007; Santner and Estelle 2009; Ruan et al. 2019). JAs biosynthesis begins in the chloroplasts with alphalinolenic acid (α-LeA) and involves several enzymes, including 13-lipoxygenase (13-LOX), allene oxide synthase (AOS) and allene oxide cyclase (AOC), producing cis-12-oxophytodienoic acid (OPDA). OPDA is then exported to peroxisomes and catalysed by OPDA reductase 3 (OPR3) and other enzymes into JA (Theodoulou et al. 2005; Wasternack and Hause 2013, 2019; Wasternack and Feussner  2018; Guan et  al.  2019). In the cytoplasm, JA is converted into JA-Ile by jasmonate resistant 1 (JAR1) or into MeJA by JA carboxyl methyltransferase (JMT), activating specific transcription factors and inducing cellular responses (Wasternack and Hause 2013; Luo et al. 2023). 

Overall, plants, including model species and crops, can respond to magnetic fields. However, detailed research on the signalling pathways and their effects on plants, especially crops, is lacking. This study uses wheat to thoroughly illustrate the effects of SMF on its development and signal transduction. We found that SMF significantly promoted wheat root length and tip number in a strength-dependent manner (from 4 to $1 6 \mathrm { m T }$ ), regardless of magnetic polarity. RNA-seq analysis revealed that SMF induced the expression of JAs biosynthesis, N assimilation and light signalling genes. Finally, we found the magnetic effect was mediated by HY5-repressed JAs biosynthesis, promoted N utilisation and wheat growth.

# 2.1 | SMF Promotes Wheat Growth in a Strength-Dependent Manner

We observed that wheat under high-voltage transmission lines always grows stronger and produces more, suggesting a possible wire-generated magnetic field may influence wheat development and growth. Previous studies have shown that the model plant Arabidopsis responds to magnetic treatment, and its genome and proteome are altered (Paul et al. 2006; Herranz et al. 2013; Pooam et al. 2018; Jin et al. 2019). Thus, we investigated whether SMF could affect wheat physiology and promote its growth. We chose four wheat varieties, including spring wheat (Fielder and Yumai 34) and winter wheat (Aikang 58 and Zhengmai 518). The results showed consistent responses, with significant increases in plant height, longest root length, shoot fresh weight and root fresh weight (Figure  S1). Given its wide planting range and local significance, we focused subsequent studies on Aikang 58. 

We grew Aikang 58 seedlings in SMFs of 4, 6, 8 and 16 mT, aligning the magnetic field direction north to south, parallel to the shoot-root axis, for 14 days. # 2.2 | SMF Promotes Wheat Growth Regardless of Polarity Direction

It was reported in Arabidopsis that the plant has no response to the direction of SMF when oriented south to north, matching root to shoot (Jin et al. 2019). We wondered how the direction of SMF affects wheat. Therefore, we grew wheat seedlings in liquid nutrition, 8 cm above the magnet with three different directions of SMF $\mathrm { 2 6 ~ m T }$ ): parallel (M0), vertical (M90) and reverse (M180) to the GMF, for 14 days (Figure S2a). Contrary to the report in Arabidopsis, wheat responded to all directions, 

with the M0 orientation having the most pronounced effect overall (Figure  S2b,d). Compared with the control group, the plant height under SMF M0, M90 and M180 increased by 2.46 cm $( 1 3 . 6 0 \% )$ , 1.24 cm $( 6 . 8 5 \% )$ and $2 . 5 9 \mathrm { c m }$ $( 1 4 . 3 4 \% )$ , respectively. The shoot fresh weight increased by $0 . 0 5 0 \mathrm { g }$ $( 1 5 . 1 6 \% )$ , $0 . 0 1 9 \mathrm { g }$ $( 5 . 4 9 \% )$ and $0 . 0 4 6 \mathrm { g }$ $( 1 3 . 7 2 \% )$ (Figure  S2b,d). Simultaneously, the root system displayed a growth-promoting phenotype under SMF M0, M90 and M180 treatment. The longest root length was $1 5 . 9 6 \mathrm { c m }$ in the GMF control group, while under SMF M0, M90 and M180 treatment, the longest root length elongated by 3.28 cm $( 2 0 . 5 8 \% )$ , 1.53 cm $( 9 . 5 7 \% )$ and $1 . 7 4 \mathrm { c m }$ $( 1 0 . 8 9 \% )$ . The root fresh weight increased by $0 . 0 6 3 \mathrm { g }$ $( 1 6 . 4 2 \% )$ , $0 . 0 2 5 \mathrm { g }$ $( 6 . 5 3 \% )$ and $0 . 0 4 8 \mathrm { g }$ $( 1 2 . 5 1 \% )$ (Figure  S2c,d). These results showed that SMF could promote the growth of wheat seedlings regardless of its direction, with the most pronounced effect occurring when the direction of the SMF was the same as that of the GMF. 

# 2.3 | SMF Reprograms Wheat Gene Expression

To better understand the molecular mechanism by which seedling growth is differentially affected by SMF treatments, we investigated global gene expression in shoots (S) and roots (R) during the 16-h light cycle (Day) and 8-h dark cycle (Night), respectively, using RNA sequencing (RNA-seq). The general information on the RNA-seq data is summarised in Table S1. With three biological repeats and the selection factor $\mathbf { \left| L o g _ { 2 } \right. }$ (fold change) $| \geq 1$ $( \boldsymbol { p } _ { \mathrm { a d j u s t } } < 0 . 0 5 )$ , we found 2864 and 6434 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) for Control vs. 16 mT_S comparison group and Control vs. $1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T \_ R }$ comparison group during the 16-h light cycle, and 490 and 5285 DEGs for Control vs. $1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T } _ { - } \mathrm { S }$ comparison group and Control vs. 16 mT_R comparison group during the 8-h dark cycle (Table S1). In general, the number of DEGs in both shoot and root was higher during the day than at night, and the difference in the number of DEGs in shoot was particularly obvious, suggesting that the effect of SMF treatment on gene expression under light condition was more extensive than that under dark condition. The DEGs also showed that the root system was much more sensitive to the SMF overall. This was consistent with the phenotype that the root system changed more dramatically than the shoot. 

Using Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis, we found that DEGs during the day were enriched in light signalling, cell cycle, JA and other plant hormone signalling pathways in both shoots and roots. In roots, nitrogen (N) related pathways were also enriched (Figure  2a). These results indicated that SMF could promote the growth of wheat seedlings by affecting wheat root absorption of N, light signal response, mediation of plant hormones, cell cycle regulation and other pathways. 

# 2.4 | SMF Elevates N Assimilation Pathways in Wheat

In the DEGs GO term analysis of $1 6 \ \mathrm { m T \_ R }$ compared with Control_R, we found that the N assimilation pathway was greatly enriched in wheat roots (Figure  2a), including Nitrate Transporter 2.5 (TaNRT2.5, TraesCS3A02G254000), Nitrate 

Reductase (TaNR, TraesCS6B02G356800) and Nitrite Reductase (TaNiR, TraesCS6D02G313100) (Table S2). According to previous research, in the N absorption pathway, nitrate is transported from the plant root to the shoot by nitrate transporter (NRT), then reduced to nitrite by nitrate reductase (NR) and ultimately converted to ammonium ions through catalysis with the participation of nitrite reductase (NiR) (Imamura et al. 2010; Fujiwara et al. 2015) (Figure S4a). This suggests that SMF may promote wheat growth by increasing root uptake of nitrate. To verify this hypothesis, we detected the gene expression of NRT2.5, NR, and NiR by qRT-PCR in wheat seedlings with or without SMF (Control). The relative gene expressions of NRT2.5, NR and NiR were 3.0 times, 3.0 times and 4.7 times higher than those of the Control groups (Figure 2b), indicating that SMF can promote nitrate N transport and transformation. Thus, we checked net $\mathrm { N O _ { 3 } } ^ { - }$ fluxes in seedling root tips, using the high-resolution scanning ion-selective electrode technique (SIET), which indicated that $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ influx rates increased under SMF treatment (Figure 2c,d). We also measured the enzyme activity of NR, and the results showed NR activity significantly increased in both shoots and roots under SMF treatment (Figure 2e). Additionally, magnetic treatment significantly increased the N content in both shoot and root parts (Figure 2f). 

To evaluate the potential of SMF in alleviating nutrient deficiency stress, we subjected wheat seedlings to low nitrogen (LN) conditions. SMF promoted growth and nitrogen assimilation in wheat seedlings under both normal nitrogen (NN) and LN conditions (Figure S3). Notably, LN-treated seedlings exposed to SMF exhibited heightened sensitivity, characterised by significantly longer root length, an elevated root–shoot ratio and higher chlorophyll content (Figure S3b–d). These results indicate that SMF enhances root architectural plasticity and photosynthetic efficiency under nitrogen limitation, serving as an adaptive strategy to improve nutrient foraging capacity in resource-constrained environments. 

# 2.5 | SMF Represses JAs Biosynthesis Pathways in Wheat

As the GO enrichment results displayed, SMF altered plant hormone pathways (Figure 2a). To verify the effect of SMF on hormones, we measured the content of ABA, indoleacetic acid (IAA), JA, salicylic acid (SA) and their derivatives in wheat seedling shoots and roots with or without SMF. The results showed that, compared with the control group, the magnetic field did not significantly change the levels of ABAs and SAs, and the content of IAAs in the root was reduced by $1 3 . 5 0 \%$ . However, JAs content decreased by $7 3 . 4 8 \%$ in the shoots and $4 7 . 5 8 \%$ in the roots (Figure  2h). This indicates that SMF treatment had the greatest effect on JAs.

Finally, JA is transported into the cytoplasm to synthesise JAR1 and then transformed into JA-Ile or catalysed by JMT to generate MeJA (Figure S4b). If JA-Ile connects JAZ and ${ \mathrm { S C F } } ^ { \mathrm { C O I 1 } }$ , JAZ can be degraded by the 26S proteasome, allowing MYC2 to bind to the promoter of JA-responsive genes, initiating the transcription of downstream genes (Melotto et  al.  2008) (Figure  S4b). RNA-seq analysis results showed that the expression levels of TaJAZ genes in the shoots were significantly downregulated with SMF treatment, while 

the expression levels of most TaJAZ genes in the roots were also downregulated (Figure S4b). These results indicated that magnetic treatment reduced the content of important substances in the JAs biosynthesis pathway and the active substances involved in physiological activities, suggesting that SMF was likely to affect plant growth by regulating the JA signalling pathway. 

Notably, most genes involved in the N assimilation pathway and JA signalling pathway were differentially expressed by magnetic field induction during the day, but only a few of these genes were differentially expressed at night (Figure S4). Moreover, the leaf is the main part for JAs biosynthesis. This once more proves the important role of light in the influence of SMF treatment on gene expression. 

# 2.6 | JA Eliminates the Magnetic Effect on Wheat Growth

We compared the phenotypes of wheat seedlings with or without JA under SMF treatment. When contrasted against the control groups (without SMF), both 5 and $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ JA resulted in a significant suppression of wheat seedling growth (Figure 3a,b). Concurrently, treatment with exogenous JA significantly inhibited the promoting effect of SMF on the growth of wheat seedlings in both JA concentrations (Figure  3a,b). With $0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ JA, SMF significantly promoted plant height, shoot fresh weight, longest root length and root fresh weight by $8 . 0 9 \%$ , $9 . 1 6 \%$ , $7 . 9 2 \%$ and $1 3 . 0 7 \%$ , respectively. With 5 or $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ JA, there were no significant differences between Control and SMF groups (Figure  3a,b). These results indicated that JA could hinder the growth-promoting effect of SMF on wheat seedlings. 

Next, we also examined the net $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ fluxes and NR activity in wheat under SMF $( 1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T } )$ ) and GMF (Control) conditions without or with JA. Notably, SMF could promote $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ influx without JA, whereas JA eliminated the influence of SMF on $\mathrm { N O _ { 3 } } ^ { - }$ influx (Figure 3c,d). Consistently, without JA, SMF improved the fold change of NR activity in leaves, but NR activity remained unchanged under SMF when treated with JA (Figure 3e). Then, we determined the N level increased in SMF-treated shoots without JA but dramatically decreased in JA and SMF-treated shoots. However, there were no significant changes in N levels in shoots among control and SMF treatment with different JA concentrations (Figure 3f). qRT-PCR analyses of key nitrogen-related genes TaNR, TaNiR and TaNRT2.5 exhibited consistent trends (Figure 3g). These results suggest that SMF promoted the uptake of $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ in roots, which could be blocked by adding external JA. Notably, we observed a progressive decline in the nitrogen assimilation capacity of wheat seedlings as JA concentrations increased, highlighting the inhibitory effect of JA on nitrogen assimilation (Figure 3c–g). 

# 2.7 | The Inhibition of JAs Biosynthesis by SMF Is Dependent on Light

As the GO enrichment of RNA-seq analysis exhibited, SMF affects both the JA signalling pathway and the light signalling 

pathway (Figure 2a). Moreover, the number of DEGs of JAs biosynthesis under light conditions was significantly higher than that under dark conditions (Figure  S4b). The biosynthesis of JAs is known to be dramatically affected by light signalling (Fernández-Milmanda et al. 2020). Thus, we compared the JAs content between control and SMF during both the day and night time. The results exhibited that there was no significant change in the JAs content at night in SMF, while JAs content markedly decreased in the SMF group during the day (Figure  4a). This illustrated that the effect of the magnetic field on JAs biosynthesis required the participation of light signals. To further verify inference on the N assimilation, we compared NR activity and N content between SMF and control under day and night conditions. The NR activity fold change difference was higher during the day than at night (Figure  4b). Meanwhile, N content was also affected by SMF during the day, but not at night (Figure 4c). Besides, we compared the relative expression of genes functioning in the JAs signalling pathway, including TaLOX2, TaOPR3 and TaJAR1, between day and night conditions with or without SMF. The results indicated that light increased the fold change differences of these genes' relative expressions (Figure  4d). This indicated that SMF promotes N assimilation by repressing JAs biosynthesis through light signalling networks. We then screened the RNA-seq data in light signalling pathway genes and found that the one most affected by the SMF was TaHY5, which is also a core factor in light signal transduction (Figure  S6). Correspondingly, the effect of the magnetic field on the expression of TaHY5 was also regulated by light (Figure 4d). 

# 2.8 | TaHY5 Inhibits JAs Biosynthesis to Promote N Assimilation in SMF Under Light Treatment

As a light signalling pathway transcription factor, HY5 participates in a complex signalling network and regulates a wide range of physiological functions, including nutrient assimilation, cell proliferation and elongation, synthesis of substances required for plant growth, defence and temperature response pathways (Oyama et al. 1997; Jing et al. 2013; Gangappa and Botto 2016). Therefore, we generated two TaHY5-A/B/D knock out plants, KO12 and KO24, by CRISPR/Cas9 (Figure S7a). They all lacked key bZIP domains due to premature termination or frameshift mutations (Figure  S7b). Compared with WT, the two knockout lines had taller plant heights, lower chlorophyll content and decreased expression of TaLHCB2, TaPIF4 and TaRBCS2 (Figures S7c–f). This was consistent with TaHY5 as a positive factor in the light signalling pathway. And then, we examined the phenotypes of WT (Fielder) and two tahy5 knockout wheat transcription lines (KO12 and KO24) under the treatment of SMF (16 mT). The phenotype results showed that SMF promoted plant height, longest root length, shoot fresh weight and root fresh weight significantly in WT, but there was no significant change in KO12 and KO24 (Figure  5a,b). In addition, the JAs content in KO24, NR activity and N content in KO12 and KO24 were not regulated by SMF, while SMF promoted these in WT (Figure 5c–e). Relative expression results indicated that the regulatory effect of SMF on TaLOX2, TaOPR3, TaJAR1, TaNRT2.5, TaNR and TaNiR, which attenuated or even eliminated in KO12 and KO24 lines (Figure 5f). These results suggested that TaHY5 is a key factor in SMF repressed JAs biosynthesis to promote N assimilation.

The results of the yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) and split-LUC assay (LCA) exhibited that TaHY5 physically interacted with TaHDA9 (Figure 6c,d). Moreover, when the luciferase reporter (LUC) of TaLOX2, TaOPR3 and TaJAR1 was significantly repressed by the TaHY5-TaHDA9 co-injection, it was not by TaHY5 alone (Figure 6e,f). 

# 2.10 | TaHY5 Directly Orchestrates Transcription of Nitrate Assimilation Genes

We further elucidated the regulatory role of TaHY5 in nitrogenrelated gene expression. qRT-PCR analyses revealed significant upregulation of TaNR and TaNiR transcripts in TaHY5 overexpression lines, with corresponding downregulation in CRISPR/Cas9 knockout mutants. In contrast, TaNRT2.5 expression showed no consistent pattern, with significant suppression observed in only one mutant line (Figure S10a). Cis-regulatory element analysis identified conserved G-box motifs in the TaNR promoter at positions $- 9 5 0$ and −1350 bp, and in the TaNiR promoter at −995 and −1472 bp. Notably, the TaNRT2.5 promoter lacked these canonical HY5 binding elements (Figure  S10b). Functional validation via luciferase reporter assays confirmed TaHY5-mediated transcriptional activation of TaNR and TaNiR promoters (Figure  S10c,d). Collectively, these integrated analyses establish TaHY5 as a direct transcriptional activator of core nitrate assimilation genes TaNR and TaNiR, while excluding its role in regulating the nitrate transporter TaNRT2.5. 

# 2.11 | TaHY5 Recruits TaHDA9 to Regulate Histone Modification of JAs Biosynthesis Genes Under SMF

HDA9, an epigenetic regulator, is known to suppress gene expression by catalysing the deacetylation of histone marks, particularly H3K9ac, at promoter regions of target genes (Niu et  al.  2022). Our investigation revealed that TaHY5 recruits TaHDA9 to execute this repression mechanism. To elucidate the specific role of SMF in modulating H3K9ac states via TaHY5 in wheat, we detected H3K9ac modification levels by western blot (WB) assay. The result indicated a significant reduction in H3K9ac abundance under SMF exposure in wild-type (WT) plants, whereas this reduction was absent in tahy5 knockout mutant (KO24), suggesting that SMF exerts its influence on H3K9ac status through TaHY5 (Figure 6g). 

Next, to further delineate the genome-wide impact, we performed ChIP-seq of the H3K9ac marker in WT and tahy5 mutant (KO24) with and without SMF. 2246 and 1079 peaks were identified in WT with and without SMF, and 2079 and 5004 peaks were identified in KO24 with and without SMF (Table S3). Notably, under SMF treatment, WT wheat exhibited a drastic reduction in H3K9ac peaks (618 peaks retained, $2 7 . 5 2 \%$ of control), whereas the mutant maintained a relatively high number of peaks (1451 peaks, $6 9 . 7 9 \%$ of control), indicating that the tahy5 knockout rendered the plants insensitive to SMF-mediated histone modifications. As H3K9ac is a genome-wide modification, we focused on the transcription start sites (TSSs) within a 3-kb window. We observed a pronounced downregulation of H3K9ac binding peaks at TSSs in WT plants exposed to 16 mT SMF, whereas the tahy5 mutant exhibited either stable or slightly increased binding peaks post-SMF induction (Figure 7b,d). Analysis of peak distribution across the genome revealed a $3 . 9 5 \%$ decrease in promoterregion peaks in WT under SMF (from $1 5 . 8 1 \%$ to $1 1 . 8 6 \%$ ), contrasted with a $3 . 3 2 \%$ increase in the tahy5 mutant (from $1 4 . 3 3 \%$ to $1 7 . 6 5 \%$ ) (Figure  S11). GO enrichment analysis displayed that SMF-mediated loss of H3K9ac modification in WT plants primarily impacted genes associated with the JA signalling pathway. Conversely, in the tahy5 mutant, these genes were significantly induced by SMF, suggesting a reversal of the epigenetic regulatory landscape. These results collectively indicate that SMF, through TaHY5 and its interacting factor TaHDA9, mediates lysine deacetylation, thereby suppressing JA pathway genes, which are direct targets of TaHY5. 

# 3 | Discussion

The GMF is a natural environmental component that influences numerous biological processes in living systems (Finlay et al. 2010). Previous studies have demonstrated that exposing plants to SMF stronger than GMF can stimulate seed germination, seedling growth, biomass accumulation and yield enhancement (Vaezzadeh et al. 2006; Occhipinti et al. 2014). However, the biological significance and molecular mechanisms underlying SMF action are not yet well understood. Our study reveals that SMF induces a combinatorial effect of light signalling, JAs biosynthesis and N utilisation through the TaHY5-TaHDA9 epigenetic module to promote wheat seedling growth (Figure S12). The effect of SMF on wheat seedling growth is intensity dependent, but not direction dependent (Figures 1 and S2). In addition to the confirmation of SMF on wheat growth, our results for the first time elucidate the specific mechanism of SMF on wheat systematically. Therefore, our work provides valuable insight into the possible application of magnetic field technology in the agricultural industry. 

The role of hormonal responses to SMF has been explored in several reports; gibberellin and auxin, for example, were affected by SMF in Arabidopsis (Xu et al. 2016; Jin et al. 2019). To promote the application of magnetic field in agriculture, we are committed to addressing these issues in future work. 

# 4 | Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

The wheat varieties of Aikang 58, Fielder, Yumai 34, Zhengmai 518 and tahy5 mutant were used in this study. The tahy5 mutant in Fielder background was created using CRISPR/Cas9 technology by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and was identified using PCR (for primers, see Table S4) and sequencing. In all assays, the sterilised wheat seeds were spread on plates and kept in darkness for 2–3 days to promote germination. After germination, uniform and healthy seedlings were transplanted into Hoagland nutrient solution and grown in an artificial climate chamber at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with 16-h light cycle and 8-h dark cycle $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ . 

To determine whether magnetic fields affect the growth of wheat seedlings, we chose Aikang 58, Fielder, Yumai 34 and Zhengmai 518 as the test varieties. Uniform wheat seedlings were put into plastic boxes with nutrient solution and exposed to a $1 6 \mathrm { m T }$ (milli-Tesla, $1 \mathrm { m T } = 1 0 ^ { - 3 }$ Tesla) SMF generated by sintered Nd-Fe-B (N35) magnets. These magnets are composed of neodymium (Nd, $2 9 \% - 3 2 \%$ ), iron (Fe, $6 3 . 2 \% - 6 8 . 5 \% )$ ) and boron (B, $0 . 9 \% \mathrm { - } 1 . 2 \% )$ ) as core components, supplemented with trace additives $( 0 . 5 \% - 6 \%$ dysprosium, cobalt, aluminium and niobium) to optimise thermal stability and coercivity. The magnets were fixed to the bottom of plastic boxes, and the magnetic field direction was the upper N pole and the lower S pole. Plastic lids with 

the same thickness as the magnets were fixed to the bottom of plastic boxes as the control. During the experimental period, the average intensity of the local GMF was approximately $2 0 { - } 7 0 \mu \mathrm { T }$ (micro-Tesla). The seedlings treated for 14-day SMF were photographed and recorded for phenotype characteristics. 

To determine the effects of SMF intensity on the growth of wheat seedlings, wheat seedlings (Aikang 58) were treated for 14-day SMF with different intensities (4, 6, 8 and 16 mT). The direction of SMF was the upper N pole and the lower S pole. To determine the effects of SMF direction on wheat seedling growth, the SMF treatments were on the basis of $1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T }$ designed into three directions, M0, M90 and M180, which represented the direction of SMF as parallel, vertical and opposite to root growth, respectively. The seedlings (Aikang 58) treated for 14-day SMF with different directions were used for phenotypic analysis. 

For JA treatment assays, 6-day-old wheat seedlings (Aikang 58) with or without $1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T }$ SMF were treated with 5 or $5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ JA (Solarbio, Beijing, China) for 8 days. To analyse the role of light in magnetic effects, wheat seedlings (Aikang 58) were supplemented with or without 16 mT SMF treatment and grown in an artificial climate chamber as described above. Fourteen days later, seedlings were sampled separately during the 16-h light cycle and 8-h dark cycle for subsequent assays. To identify the role of TaHY5 in magnetic effects, Fielder and two specific tahy5 mutant lines (KO14 and KO24) were treated with $1 6 ~ \mathrm { m T }$ SMF for 14 days. 

# 4.2 | RNA-Seq Assays

The shoot and root of wheat seedlings were collected under 16 mT SMF for 14 days during the 16-h light cycle and 8-h dark cycle, respectively. Each sample contained three biological repeats. Sampled tissues were rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for RNA sequencing (RNA-seq). The concentration and integrity of RNAs were assessed by Nanodrop 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Wilmington, DE) and Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system (Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). After quality inspection, total RNAs were used for library construction and sequencing on the Illumina HiSeq platform constructed by Majorbio (Shanghai, China). The sequenced raw reads were filtered to gain clean reads by removing adapters, ploy-N and low-quality reads. High-quality clean reads were mapped to wheat reference genome (http://www. wheatgenome.org/News/Latest-news/All-IWGSC-referencesequence-resources-now-publicly-available-at-URGI) using HISAT2 (Kim et  al.  2015). Gene Ontology annotations were obtained using GO database (http://www.geneontology.org/), and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) annotations were obtained using KEGG database (http://www. genome.jp/kegg/). The gene expression levels were calculated by Fragments Per Kilobase Million (FPKM) method (Florea et  al.  2013). Differentially expressed genes between sample groups was evaluated by DESeq2 (Love et al. 2014). Here, only genes with $\mathbf { \left| L o g _ { 2 } \right. }$ Fold Change $( \mathrm { F C } ) | \geq 1 . 0$ and false discovery rate $( \mathrm { F D R } ) { < } 0 . 0 5$ were regarded as DEGs and used for subsequent analysis. 

# 4.3 | RNA Extraction and Quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) Assays

Wheat seedlings with different treatments were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen. Total RNAs were extracted using TRnaZol Reagent (NCM Biotech, Suzhou, China); approximately $1 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNAs was reverse transcribed into cDNA using PrimeScript II 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (TaKaRa, Dalian, China). qRT-PCR reactions were performed on the LightCycler 480 System (Roche, Basel, Switzerland) with Hieff qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix (YEASEN, Shanghai, China) following the directions. The relative fold differences for gene expression were analysed using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \mathrm { t } } }$ method, and TaACTIN was used as the internal reference. All qRT-PCR assays had at least three repetitions. The primers used for qRT-PCR assays are given in Table S4. 

# 4.4 | Measurements of Nitrate $( \mathbf { N O } _ { 3 } { ^ { - } } )$ Flux Rate

The ${ \mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } } ^ { - }$ fluxes in the root meristem zone of wheat seedlings with different treatments were measured by a purpose-built electrometer (SX-ES1; Zhongxin Flash Biotechnology Co. Ltd., Henan, China). The electrodes were baked at $2 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 h and silanised with N,N-Dimethyltrimethylsilylamine (Sigma-Aldrich, Shanghai, China) for 1 h. $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ -selective electrodes were backfilled with 10 mM ${ \mathrm { K N O } } _ { 3 }$ and tip filled with the $8 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ liquid ion-exchanger (LIX). The roots of these plants were immersed in a testing solution $( 0 . 1 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { K N O } _ { 3 }$ , 0.1 mM KCl and 0.1 mM $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ ; $\mathrm { p H } = 6 . 0 $ ) and then equilibrated for 15 min. The $\mathrm { N O } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ fluxes near the root surface were recorded for 10 min as previously described (He et al. 2015). 

# 4.5 | Measurements of Nitrate Reductase (NR) Activity

The activity of NR was analysed based on a colorimetric method. Leaves and roots of wheat seedlings were collected and soaked in $0 . 1 \mathbf { M }$ phosphate buffer $\mathrm {  ~ \ p H = 7 . 5 } ,$ ) and $0 . 2 \mathrm { M } \mathrm { K N O } _ { 3 }$ for 15 min under vacuum conditions. Then, $3 0 \%$ trichloroacetic acid was added immediately to terminate the reaction after maintaining the enzyme reaction at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in darkness for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The reaction solution containing nitrite was incubated with $1 \%$ sulfanilic acid and $0 . 2 \%$ α-naphthylamine at room temperature for $2 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ to form red azo-compounds, whose maximum absorption peak is at $5 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The amount of nitrite produced represented the NR activity. 

# 4.6 | Measurements of Nitrogen (N) Content

The N content of plants was determined by the Kjeldahl method. In brief, shoot and root parts of wheat seedlings were heated to de-enzyme at $1 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h and then dried to constant weight at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After crushing and weighing, the dried samples were digested into ammonium sulphate with concentrated sulphuric acid at $4 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h, meanwhile adding potassium sulphate and copper sulphate at a 9:1 ratio as the catalysts to accelerate reactions. Distillation, titration and analysis were performed by the Automatic Nitrogen Analyzer (FOSS, Hilleroed, Denmark). 

# 4.7 | Measurements of Chlorophyll Content

Leaves from 10-day-old WT and tahy5 mutants were collected, snipped and weighed. Then the leaves were soaked in graduated tubes with $9 5 \%$ alcohol under dark conditions to extract chlorophyll. The absorbance of pigments at 470, 649 and $6 6 5 \mathrm { n m }$ was recorded with Multiskan Spectrum (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Wilmington, DE). As a blank control, $9 5 \%$ alcohol was used. Assays of chlorophyll measurement were performed five times. 

# 4.8 | Transcriptional Activation Assays

The full-length CDS of TaHY5 was cloned into the pGBKT7 vector to generate the BD-TaHY5 construct. Then, the construct was transformed into the yeast strain Y2HGold and selected on SD medium lacking Trp. To verify the transcriptional activation activity of TaHY5, clones were transferred to SD medium lacking Trp (with $2 0 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ X- $\bumpeq$ -Gal) at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Yeast that co-transformed pGADT7-T and pGBKT7-53 was used as a positive control. 

# 4.9 | Yeast One-Hybrid Assays

To analyse the interactions of TaHY5 with the G-box motifs in gene (TaLOX2, TaOPR3 and TaJAR1) promoter fragments, the pAbAi vectors harbouring G-box motifs of different promoter fragments were linearised with BstbI (NEB, Beijing, China) and transformed into yeast strain Y1HGold. After detection of selfactivation, the transformants suffered a secondary transformation by the pGADT7 vector with TaHY5 CDS. The one-on-one interactions were measured on SD medium lacking Leu and Ura with or without AbA. 

# 4.10 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assays (EMSA)

The full-length CDS of TaHY5 was cloned into the pET28a vector and expressed in Escherichia coli strain BL21 (DE3). His-tagged TaHY5 recombinant protein was gained by IPTG induction and purified by the protein purification system (BIO-RAD). His-ZmARF24 recombinant protein was expressed and purified the same as His-TaHY5 and used as the negative control. The TaLOX2 promoter region containing the G-box (−316 to −299), the TaOPR3 promoter region containing the G-box (−385 to $- 3 6 8 \jmath$ ) and the TaJAR1 promoter region containing the G-box (−1137 to −1035) were designed as probes for EMSAs. All the probes were end-labelled with Cy5. His-TaHY5 or His-ZmARF24 recombinant protein was mixed with Cy5-labelled probes and then co-incubated in PBS buffer for 30 min at room temperature. The reaction mixtures were separated by $7 . 5 \%$ native polyacrylamide gels in $0 . 5 { \times } \mathrm { T B E }$ buffer. Gels were imaged directly using the Li-Cor/Odyssey system (Li-Cor, NE, USA). 

# 4.11 | Yeast Two-Hybrid Assays

The full-length CDSs of TaHY5 and TaHDA9 were respectively inserted into pGADT7 and pGBKT7 vectors to generate AD-TaHY5 and BD-TaHDA9. Different combinations of bait and 

prey constructs were co-transfected into yeast strain Y2HGold and selected on SD medium lacking Leu and Trp. To verify protein–protein interactions, clones were transferred to SD medium lacking His, Leu and Trp at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The empty BD bait vector (pGBKT7) and AD prey vector (pGADT7) combination was used as a negative control. 

# 4.12 | Split-Luciferase Assays

For split-LUC assays, the coding sequences of TaHY5 and TaHDA9 were amplified from wheat cDNA and ligated into the site KpnI-SalI of N-LUC (nLUC) or C-LUC (cLUC). These constructs, including nLUC and cLUC, were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101. The cultures of Agrobacterium harbouring the constructs were collected and resuspended in infiltration buffer $( 1 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { M E S } \mathrm { p H } = 5 . 6 $ , 10 mM $\mathrm { { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ and $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ AS) to $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 \mathrm { n m } } { = } 0 . 8 { - } 1 . 0$ . The suspensions were incubated for $3 { - } 4 \mathrm { h }$ at room temperature and mixed in a ratio of 1:1 before infiltration into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. Agroinfiltration of Nicotiana benthamiana was performed following the method of a previous study (Shi et al. 2014). After 2 days of growth, the infiltrated leaves were sprayed with 1 mM luciferin solution (1 mM D-fluorescein potassium salt and $0 . 0 1 \%$ (v/v) Triton X-100) and placed in the dark for 5 min. The NightShade LB985 Plant Living Imaging System (Berthold Technologies, Bad Wildbad, Germany) was used to observe the luciferase signals. 

# 4.13 | Effector–Reporter Assays

To determine whether TaHDA9 repressed the regulatory effects of TaHY5 on its downstream genes (TaLOX2, TaOPR3 and TaJAR1), the full-length CDSs of TaHY5 and TaHDA9 were separately cloned into pCAMBIA2300-GFP and pCAMBIA1300- FLAG-mCherry to obtain two effectors, and the gene promoter fragments were cloned into the 35S::Luc vector as the reporters to drive LUC. The effector and reporter vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium strain GV3101, while the empty vector pCAMBIA1300-FLAG-mCherry was transformed as a negative control, the activity of which was taken as 1. The GV3101 cultures containing these plasmids were infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After 2days, the luciferase activities were captured and analysed with the NightShade LB985 Plant Living Imaging System (Berthold Technologies, Bad Wildbad, Germany). Five independent biological replicates were performed to analyse the relative luciferase activities. In the transcriptional activation assay for TaHY5 on TaNR and TaNiR, the methodology was implemented as described above, with the pCAMBIA2300- GFP empty vector serving as the negative control.

The mixture was centrifuged $_ { 1 5 0 0 g }$ for 10 min), and the pellet was resuspended in 1-mL Honda buffer and centrifuged once more $_ { 1 0 0 g }$ for 1 min). Then, the supernatant was transferred to a new $1 . 5 { \cdot } \mathrm { m L }$ eppendorf tube and centrifuged $2 0 0 0 g$ for 5 min). The resulting pellet was resuspended in $3 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { L }$ buffer G (1.7 M Sucrose, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl pH = 8.0, $0 . 1 5 \%$ Triton X-100, 2 mM $\mathrm { { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , 5mM DTT and $1 \times$ Protease Inhibitor Cocktail [Beyotime, Shanghai, China]) and was added on top of $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ buffer G in a new $1 . 5 { \cdot } \mathrm { m L }$ eppendorf tube. After being boiled at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , protein extracts were loaded into $1 2 \%$ (w/v) SDS-PAGE gels, transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membranes (Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany) and blocked with $5 \%$ non-fat milk. # 4.15 | Chromatin Immunoprecipitation (ChIP) Sequence Assays

Approximately 2 g seedlings of WT and tahy5 mutant (KO24) plants were collected and cross-linked in $1 \%$ (w/v) formaldehyde for 30 min under vacuum conditions. Cross-linking was stopped by adding $1 2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ glycine for 10 min under vacuum conditions. Next, the seedlings were washed five times with water and ground into homogenate on ice. The chromatin DNA was extracted by sucrose gradient centrifugation and sonicated into 200–500-bp fragments using nuclei lysis buffer $\mathrm { \nabla { 5 0 m M } }$ Tris–HCl pH = 8.0, 10 mM EDTA, $1 \%$ SDS, $0 . 1 \mathrm { m M }$ PMSF and $1 \times$ Protease Inhibitor Cocktail [Beyotime, Shanghai, China]). The sheared chromatin fragments were incubated overnight with anti-H3K9ac (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on a rotator to form antibody–antigen complexes, and then, the complexes were captured with Protein $\mathbf { A } + \mathbf { G }$ beads (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) for $^ { 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at room temperature. The DNA-protein complexes were eluted from beads and reverse cross-linked for 6 h at $6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . After degradation of protein, the acquired DNA for library preparation was measured using Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer (Life Technologies, CA, USA) and Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system (Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). 

Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq6000 platform constructed by Frasergen (Wuhan, China). The interference information, containing adapter sequence, low-quality bases and undetected bases (indicated by N) were filtered using Trimmomatic (v0.39, with parameters: LEADING:3, TRAILING:3, SLIDINGWINDOW:4:15 and MINLEN:8) (Bolger et  al.  2014), and quality was assessed using FastQC (0.11.9) in paired-end raw data. After obtaining clean data through quality 

control, the Bowtie2 (v2.3.5, with parameters: dovetail, no-unal, very sensitive local, no mixed, no discordant) was used to map clean reads to the reference (https://ftp.ensemblgenomes.ebi. ac.uk/pub/plants/release-58/gff3/triticum_aestivum/), and the low-quality mapping was further screen out. PCR redundancy and organelle alignment by Samtools (v1.12, with default parameters) and Picard (v2.25.6, with default parameters). The retained valid pairs were used for subsequent analysis. MACS3 (v3.0.0a6) (Feng et al. 2012) software based on statistical methods was used to perform Peak Calling (with parameters: -f BAMPE -B –SPMR –keep-dup all). After that, the R scripts with Deeptools (3.5.1) (Ramírez et al. 2016) software were performed to illustrate the signal enrichment analysis of peak regions, gene body regions and the regions from transcription start site (TSS) with default parameters. ChIPseeker (Yu et al. 2015) was used to obtain gene annotations on peak regions at the same time.
