# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Apricot (Prunus armeniaca L.) fruits are highly perishable and prone to quality deterioration during storage and transportation. 

Objectives: To investigate the effects of LED white light treatment on postharvest ripening of fruits using metabolomics, transcriptomics, and ATAC-Seq analysis. 

Methods: Fruits were exposed to $5 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 }$ LED white light for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ followed by $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of darkness at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ daily for 12 days. The effects of the treatments on the physiological and nutritional quality of the fruits were evaluated. These data were combined with transcriptomic, metabolomic, and ATAC-Seq data from fruits taken on 8 d of treatment to provide insight into the potential mechanism by which LED treatment delays ripening. 

Results: LED treatment activated pathways involved in ascorbate and aldarate metabolism and flavonoid and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis. Specifically, LED treatment increased the expression of UDP-sugar pyrophosphorylase (USP), L-ascorbate peroxidase (AO), dihydroflavonol 4-reductase (DFR), chalcone synthase (CHS), and caffeoyl-CoA O-methyltransferase (CCOAOMT1), leading to the accumulation of caffeoyl quinic 

acid, epigallocatechin, and dihydroquercetin and the activation of anthocyanin biosynthesis. LED treatment also affected the expression of genes associated with plant hormone signal transduction, fruit texture and color transformation, and antioxidant activity. The notable genes affected by LED treatment included 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate synthase (ACS), 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate oxidase (ACO), hexokinase (HK), lipoxygenase (LOX), malate dehydrogenase (MDH), endoglucanase (CEL), various transcription factors (TCP, MYB, EFR), and peroxidase (POD). ATAC-Seq analysis further revealed that LED treatment primarily regulated phenylpropanoid biosynthesis. 

Conclusion: The results obtained in this study provide insights into the effects of LED light exposure on apricot fruits ripening. LEDs offer a promising approach for extending the shelf life of other fruits and vegetables.

# Introduction

Apricot (Prunus armeniaca L.) is native to Central Asia and is mostly cultivated in the Mediterranean, Middle East, Caucasus, Central Asia and China [1,2]. Apricot fruits are highly desirable among consumers due to their vibrant color, juicy flesh, sugar and acid content, rich in vitamins, minerals and other nutrients [3]. However, the senescence process of apricot fruits was accelerated during storage at room temperature, and its storage period is only 3–5 days, which seriously affects its edible and commodity value [4,5]. Fruit ripening and senescence is an inevitable and irreversible process that results in physiological and biochemical changes in the various organs of the apricot fruits, affecting its color, sugar and acid content, taste and flavor [6–8]. Although there has been much research on postharvest maturation and senescence of apricot fruits, few scholars have explored the metabolite changes and their regulatory mechanisms during postharvest storage [9]. Understanding the senescence mechanism of apricot fruits during postharvest storage and seeking effective measures to improve storage quality and prolong the storage period can provide ideas for developing new breeding and improving and maintaining fruit quality. 

Fruit ripening is regulated by a complex gene regulatory network and many signal transduction pathways connected with endogenous hormones and external cues [10]. The presence or absence of respiratory peak and ethylene peak during fruit ripening and development is the main indicator to distinguish respiratory variant fruits and non-respiratory variant fruits [10]. Ethylene plays an important role in regulating the ripening process of respiratory variant fruits such as apricot fruits, including chlorophyll degradation, softening, respiration, and volatile production [11]. The ethylene biosynthesis and signal transduction pathways regulate the biological effects of ethylene, impacting fruit ripening and senescence [12]. The ethylene signal transduction pathway involves negative regulatory elements, such as ethylene receptors (ETRs) and CTR1, as well as positive regulators, such as EIN2, EIN3/EIL, and the ethylene response factor ERF1 [13]. The carotenoid accumulation genes responsible for fruit coloration can be influenced by ethylene production during ripening, while genes such as AdEIL2, AdEIL3, MdEIL2, and MdCBF2 impact fruit texture changes [14–16]. However, it is necessary to find an effective, safe and pollution-free preservation method to elucidate the regulation mechanism during postharvest storage of apricot fruits. 

Light-emitting diodes (LEDs) have emerged as a safe, effective, low-cost, and environmentally friendly technology for postharvest preservation of fruits and vegetables [17]. Immediately after postharvest, fruits and vegetables experience nutrient loss and rely solely on respiration to maintain biological activity, making LED irradiation with appropriate light intensity a beneficial approach to supplement nutrients, enhance postharvest quality, and delay physiological senescence [18,19]. Several studies have demonstrated the positive effects of LED irradiation on delaying fruit 

ripening, maintaining quality, and extending postharvest storage. For instance, LED white light increased the soluble protein content and influenced metabolic pathways in Chinese cabbage during storage [20]. LED red light enhances chlorophyll-related gene expression and inhibits photosynthesis-related genes in aging broccoli [21]. LED blue light increased glucose sensor gene expression and sugar accumulation in bayberry fruits [22]. In recent years, LED preservation technology, as a green and safe physical preservation technology, has gradually attracted widespread attention and has been rapidly developed. However, there are few studies on the application of LED preservation technology in postharvest storage and preservation of apricot fruits. 

Here, we comprehensively investigated the effects of LED white light on apricot fruits quality, transcriptome, metabolome, and chromatin accessibility. Through the joint analysis of differential genes, metabolites and peaks, the regulatory network involved in postharvest maturation and senescence of apricot fruits was generated. The relationship between gene expression and transcription factors in activating flavonoid biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis was elucidated. Understanding the mechanism of postharvest ripening of apricot fruits and inventing effective storage methods are of great significance for improving the storage quality and extending the shelf life of apricot fruits, which is also an urgent problem in the field of apricot fruit storage. 

# Materials and methods

# Sample preparation

Ripe apricot fruits were sourced from the Beijing Huahai Horticultural Planting Cooperative in Yanqing district, Beijing, China. Six hundred undamaged and decay-free fruits were divided into two groups. Fruits were placed in plastic, with each tray accommodating 15 apricots, and then wrapped with three layers of plastic wrap. The apricot fruits in the control (CK) group were stored in darkness, while those in the LED group were exposed to white light for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ followed by $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of darkness each day. The LED white light had peak wavelengths of $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ and $4 6 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . A light intensity of $5 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ was chosen based on a preliminary experiment with 5, 10, 15, 20, and $2 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ light. Light intensity was measured using a Lighting Passport instrument (ALP-20, Asensetek, Taiwan). 

The fruits were stored at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and $9 0 \%$ relative humidity for up to 12 days to assess the impact of LED light on quality. On the day of harvest (0 d), 9 samples of untreated unpeeled apricot fruit (CK-0 d) flesh were collected (from each of the three groups of fruits, for a total of three groups), cut into small pieces, quickly frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . During storage, fruits from the control and treatment groups were collected on Days 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, and 12 for the determination and analysis of physiological and nutritional factors. On Day 8, the samples in the control group had completely lost their commercial value, while the samples in 

the LED treatment group remained in good condition. Therefore, we selected Day 8 samples for transcriptome, metabolome, and ATAC-Seq analysis. Samples taken on Days 0 and 8 were freezedried for omics determination and analysis. The samples at 0 days (CK) were denoted CK-0 d, the samples stored in darkness for 8 days were denoted CK-8 d, and those treated with LED white light for 8 days were denoted LED-8 d. 

Measurement of firmness, MDA, total phenols, and flavonoid contents 

The firmness of nine fruits was measured with a fruit firmness tester (Yueqing Handpi Instruments Co., Ltd., GY-4) at each sampling time, with three points taken at the ‘‘equator” position of each fruit, and the results were averaged. 

For the malondialdehyde (MDA) content, 1 g of tissue per replicate was extracted in trichloroacetic acid and thiobarbituric acid. Procedures and measurements were performed as described previously [23]. The absorbance of the supernatant was measured at 400 nm, 532 nm and $6 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The MDA content $( \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathbf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ ) was calculated by the following formula: 

$$
C = 6. 4 5 \times \left(\mathrm {O D} _ {5 3 2} - \mathrm {O D} _ {6 0 0}\right) - 0. 5 6 \times \mathrm {O D} _ {4 5 0}
$$

MDA content = CVV m 1000 $= \frac { C \times \mathsf { V } } { \mathsf { V } _ { s } \times \mathsf { m } \times 1 0 0 0 }$ 

where C $\mathrm { { ( \mu m o l / L ) } }$ represents the concentration of MDA, v (mL) represents the total volume of the extract, and $\mathbf { V } _ { s }$ (mL) represents the volume of liquid extracted from the sample taken at the time of determination. 

For the total phenol and flavonoid contents, $_ { 0 . 5 \ { \tt g } }$ of tissue per replicate was extracted in $1 \%$ HCL-methanol solution [24]. The experiment was repeated three times for each apricot fruit sample. The relative content of total phenol $\left( \mathbf { O D } 2 8 0 \ \mathbf { g } ^ { - 1 } \right)$ ) was calculated as the absorbance value of each gram of fresh sample at a wavelength of $2 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The total phenol content was calculated in $\mathbf { m } \mathbf { g } \ \mathbf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ . The light absorption at a wavelength of $3 2 5 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ represents the relative content of flavonoids, that is, $\mathsf { O D 3 2 5 ~ g ^ { - 1 } }$ . The flavonoid content was calculated in $\mathfrak { m g g } ^ { - 1 }$ . 

Determination, screening, and analysis of differential metabolites 

Fifty milligrams of freeze-dried apricot fruit was crushed using a mixed mill (MM 400, Retsch) after being freeze-dried in a vacuum freeze-dryer (Scientz-100F). The resulting lyophilized powder samples were dissolved in a methanol solution, filtered after centrifugation, and subjected to UPLC-ESI-MS/MS analysis (UPLC, ExionLCTM AD; MS, Applied Biosystems 4500 Q TRAP). 

Metabolite identification was achieved by referencing the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) Compound database (https://www.kegg.jp/kegg/compound/), followed by mapping the identified metabolites to the KEGG Pathway database (https:// www.kegg.jp/kegg/pathway.html). 

Transcriptome sequencing and analysis methods 

# RNA extraction and detection

Total RNA was extracted from 50 mg of freeze-dried apricot, and its quality was assessed using agarose gel electrophoresis to monitor degradation and contamination. A NanoPhotometer spectrophotometer was used to check RNA purity, and a Qubit RNA Assay Kit was used for RNA concentration measurements. The RNA integrity was evaluated using an RNA Nano 6000 Assay Kit on a Bioanalyzer 2100 system. 

# Library construction and quality inspection

Library construction was performed using the NEBNext UltraTM RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina. The initial quantifica-

tion of the constructed library was conducted using a Qubit 2.0 fluorometer, while the insert size was determined using an Agilent 2100 bioanalyzer. Accurate quantification of the library’s effective concentration $( { \geq } 2 { \mathrm { ~ n M } } )$ was performed using qRT-PCR to ensure high library quality. 

# Clustering and sequencing

Qualified libraries were pooled according to the required effective concentration and target off-machine data volume for Illumina sequencing, enabling the acquisition of sequence information for the tested fragments [25]. 

# Screening and analysis of differentially expressed genes

The reference genome and its annotation file were downloaded, and HISAT v2.1.0 was used to construct the index. According to the transcriptome sequencing analysis of 9 samples, a total of 61.63 Gb of clean data were obtained; the amount of clean data in all the samples reached 6 Gb, and the percentage of Q30 bases was $91 \%$ or greater. The clean reads were subsequently aligned to the reference genome, and featureCounts v1.6.2 was used to calculate the counts. FPKM values were determined for each gene based on the gene alignment. DESeq2 v1.22.1/edgeR v3.24.3 were utilized to analyze differential gene expression between the two groups, and the Benjamini & Hochberg method was applied for P value correction. The threshold for significant differential expression was set based on the corrected P value and |log2foldchange|. Enrichment analysis, including the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) and Gene Ontology (GO) analyses, was also conducted using a hypergeometric test. According to the KEGG and GO results corresponding to the DEGs, the biological processes and regulatory pathways associated with each DEG were obtained. 

# ATAC sequencing and analysis methods

# Cell collection, transposition reaction and purification

The cells of the fruit were collected as a uniform single-cell suspension, and a total of 50,000 cells were obtained. The cells were centrifuged at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 min at $5 0 0 \times { \bf g } ,$ , after which the supernatant was discarded. After the cells were washed with precooled PBS and lysis buffer, the cells were centrifuged again to remove the supernatant [26]. The transposition reaction was initiated immediately. 

The Tn5 transposase enzyme was used. The cell nuclei were suspended in the transposition reaction system and incubated at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. DNA purification was subsequently performed, and the purified DNA was used to establish the PCR system, followed by PCR amplification. The final DNA library was prepared and sequenced on the Illumina platform. 

# ATAC-Seq data analysis

The ATAC-Seq data were preprocessed, including adapter removal and filtering of low-quality reads. High-quality clean reads were obtained in FastQ format. Bowtie2 software was used for read alignment and identification of high-quality reads. Deep-Tools $\mathbf { v } 2 . 0 7$ was used to map the density distribution of the sequencing reads, and the results were visualized as heatmaps. MACS2 v2.1.1 software was used to detect peak regions across the whole genome. MACS2 contains four steps: 

(1) Remove redundant reads; 

(2) Adjusting the Read position, 

(3) Calculate the peak enrichment 

(4) The empirical false detection rate (FDR) was estimated. Finally, an FDR < 0.05 was considered to indicate an identified peak. 

Differentially accessible region analysis of two conditions/- groups was performed using the DESeq R package (1.6.3). DESeq2 provides statistical routines for determining differential signals in digital peak count data using a model based on the negative binomial distribution. The resulting $P$ values were adjusted using Benjamini and Hochberg’s approach for controlling the false discovery rate. Genes with an adjusted $P$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ according to DESeq were considered differentially accessible regions. Gene sequences associated with differentially enriched promoter regions were compared to various databases, including the Nr, SwissProt, GO, KEGG, COG, KOG, EggNOG, and Pfam databases, using BLAST for subsequent gene function analysis. 

# Statistical analysis

Data processing was performed using Microsoft Excel 2020, and Origin 2019 was used for data plotting. SPSS 19 software was used for statistical significance $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ analysis. Principal component analysis (PCA), hierarchical cluster analysis (HCA), and Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) analysis were performed using the R package. Differentially abundant metabolites (DMs) were screened based on VIP values $\left( \mathsf { V I P } \ \geq \ 1 \right) .$ ) and absolute Log2FC values (| $\mathrm { L o g 2 F C l } _ { \geq 1 . 0 ) }$ ). Each test was repeated three times, with three replicates for each repetition. 

# Results

# Fruit quality

Fruit ripening was delayed by LED treatment (Fig. 1A). Compared with those in the control group, the fruits in the LED treatment group were more stable (Fig. 1B). The content of MDA, an indicator of oxidative stress, was lowest in the LED-treated fruits (Fig. 1C). Similarly, the contents of total phenols and flavonoids exhibited similar trends, with the control group showing a decrease in total phenols and flavonoids on Day 12 of storage, while the treatment group maintained high levels of these compounds (Fig. 1D, E). In addition, the firmness and total phenol and flavonoid contents of fruits in the LED treatment on Day 12 were greater than those in the CK-8 d treatment. The MDA content of the apricot fruits from the LED-12 d treatment was lower than that from the CK-8 d treatment. Therefore, our results showed that LED white light can delay the storage of apricot fruits for at least 4 days. 

# Fruit transcriptomics

To assess the correlation of biological replicates in the transcriptomic analysis, the PCC was utilized as an evaluation index. High correlation values indicate strong similarity between duplicate samples. In this study, the Pearson correlation coefficient for each group of biological replicate samples was greater than 0.9, indicating excellent sample correlation (Fig. 1F). 

The transcriptomic data analysis revealed the overall distribution of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the two sample sets (Fig. 1G). In the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d comparison, a total of 6,256 genes exhibited differential expression, with 3,058 genes upregulated and 3,198 genes downregulated. There were 8,110 DEGs in the CK-0 d vs. LED-8 d comparison, including 3,595 upregulated and 4,515 downregulated genes. In the CK-8 d vs. LED-8 d comparison, 1,792 DEGs were identified, 508 of which were upregulated and 1,284 were downregulated. A total of 814 DEGs were shared across the three comparison groups, 731 DEGs were unique to the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d comparison, and 2,022 DEGs were unique to the CK-0 d vs. LED-8 d comparison. Only 141 DEGs were identified in the CK-8 

d vs. LED-8 d comparison (Fig. 1I). The hierarchical clustering analysis of the DEGs in the three comparison groups is depicted in Fig. 1H. 

# Analysis of DEGs in the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d comparison group

We performed a KEGG enrichment analysis on the 20 pathway terms that were most significantly enriched, and the results showed that the DEGs were mainly enriched in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites, protein processing in the endoplasmic reticulum, starch and sucrose metabolism, and glycerophospholipid metabolism in the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d (Fig. S1). GO functional classification revealed that the DEGs were enriched in biological processes (BPs), molecular functions (MFs), and cellular components (CCs). In the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d, the enriched DEGs involved both BPs, cellular processes and metabolic processes. The enriched DEGs were involved in the cellular anatomical entity CCs, and in terms of MFs, the enriched DEGs were involved in binding and catalytic activity (Appendix 1). 

Among the DEGs involved in fruit flavor and aroma regulation, GABA transporter 1 (Gat1), hexokinase-1 (HK1), hexokinase-3 (HK3), galactosidase 8 (Gal8), beta-fructofuranosidase (BRFUCT), and galactosidase 40 (Gal40) were upregulated, while $\beta$ -amylase (BAM), lipoxygenase 6 (LOX6), and malate dehydrogenase (MDH) were downregulated (Fig. S2). Four DEGs were found to be involved in regulating fruit texture, with endoglucanase CX (CELCX), endoglucanase 11 (CEL11), and glucan endo-1,3- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ -glucosidase 4 (HGN4) being upregulated, while glucan endo-1,3- $\boldsymbol { \cdot } \beta$ -glucosidase (HGN) was downregulated (Fig. S2). Among the genes associated with ethylene biosynthesis, 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate oxidase 1 (ACO1) and 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate synthase (ACS) were upregulated (Fig. S2). Three DEGs were involved in regulating color synthesis in fruits. Chalcone synthase 2 (CHS2) and flavonoid 30 - monooxygenase (F30 H) were upregulated, while chlorophyll synthase (CHLG) was downregulated (Fig. S2). 

Ten DEGs encoding transcription factors (TFs) were identified, with bZIP transcription Factor 53 (bZIP53), bZIP transcription Factor 60 (bZIP60), the transcription factor MYB34 (MYB34), the transcription factor MYB44 (MYB44), GATA transcription Factor 8 (GATA8), and the transcription factor bHLH62 (bHLH62) being upregulated, while the transcription factor AS1 (AS1), the transcription factor bHLH94 (bHLH94), and the transcription factor MYB59 (MYB59) were downregulated (Fig. S2). The oxidation system of fruits was influenced by three downregulated DEGs, namely, peroxidase A2 (PODA2), peroxidase 42 (POD42), and polyphenol oxidase (PPO) (Fig. S2).

# Effects of LED light on specific DEGs

Further analysis of the genes differentially expressed between CK-0 d and CK-8 d and between CK-8 d and LED-8 d revealed 12 DEGs that were regulated by LED light during fruit ripening. Among these DEGs, ACS, ACO1, salicylic acid-binding protein 2 (SABP2), BGLU40, chalcone synthase 2 (CHS2), and bZIP44 exhibited the same trend. The expression levels of these genes were very low at 0 d of storage, after which they remained nearly undetectable. As the storage time increased, the expression levels in the CK-8 d treatment increased but were consistently lower than those in the LED-8 d treatment. Conversely, the expression level of SUC showed the opposite trend. SUC had the highest expression level at 0 d of storage, but it decreased rapidly at 8 d of storage, with lower expression levels in the LED group than in the CK group. For the remaining 5 DEGs, namely, BAM3, BGAL8, LOX, CEL9, and CEL11, their expression levels in the CK group were greater than 

those in the 0 d after 8 d of storage, while the gene expression in the LED group was lower than that at 0 d (Fig. S4).

# Combined transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses

Combined analysis of transcriptomic and metabolomic data revealed that DEGs and DAMs were coenriched in antioxidationrelated pathways. Specifically, in the ascorbate and aldarate metabolism pathway, the expression of UDP-sugar pyrophosphorylase (USP), which is involved in the synthesis of D-glucuronate from UDP-D-glucose, was upregulated under LED irradiation. However, the expression of inositol oxygenase (MIOX) and aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), which are involved in ascorbic acid biosynthesis, was downregulated, leading to a decrease in myo-inositol accumulation. In addition, LED illumination increased the decomposition of L-ascorbate peroxidase (APX) and L-ascorbate oxidase (AO, CURHA-P_LOCUS17594), while that of another AO (CURHAP_LOCUS23114) increased. These changes ultimately resulted in a decrease in the accumulation of L-dehydroascorbate (Fig. 2). 

LED treatment affects the accumulation of key enzymes and metabolites involved in the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway. Cinnamoyl-CoA, the final product of the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway, accumulated after LED treatment. This increase in cinnamoyl-CoA levels resulted in the upregulation of chalcone synthase (CHS) and phlorizin synthase (PTG1). CHS is a key enzyme in flavonoid biosynthesis, and its upregulation led to the decomposition of pinobanksin 3-acetate and chrysin, resulting in a 2–3-fold reduction in their content compared to that in the CK. Notably, CHS 

is involved in the biosynthesis of various flavonoids. Upregulation of CHS also induced the upregulation of dihydroflavonol 4-reductase (DFR) under LED lighting conditions. This upregulation of DFR promoted the accumulation of epigallocatechin and further stimulated anthocyanin biosynthesis (Fig. 3). These findings suggest that LED treatment can modulate the flavonoid biosynthesis pathway, leading to changes in the accumulation of specific flavonoid metabolites. 

# Fruit chromatin accessibility

ATAC-seq analysis was subsequently performed to investigate the changes in chromatin accessibility in fruits after LED treatment. A total of 616.85 million clean reads were obtained, with a high percentage of Q30 bases, indicating good data quality. The distribution of sequencing reads around the transcription start site (TSS) of each gene showed a concentrated pattern near the TSS, confirming the reliability of the data (Fig. 4A). To evaluate the reproducibility of the ATAC-seq data, correlation analysis was conducted among the biological replicates of each group. The correlation coefficients among replicates within each group were significantly greater than those between different groups, indicating good parallelism among the samples (Fig. 4B). 

Through differential analysis, a total of 4492, 1769, and 123 differentially accessible regions (DARs) were identified in the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d, CK-0 d vs. LED-8 d, and CK-8 d vs. LED-8 d, respectively. In the CK-0d vs. CK-8d, 1793 DARs exhibited increased accessibility, while 2699 DARs exhibited decreased accessibility. In the CK-0 d vs. LED-8 d, 611 DARs exhibited increased accessibility, while 1158 DARs exhibited decreased accessibility. In the CK-8 d vs. LED-8 d, 35 DARs displayed increased accessibility, and 88 DARs showed decreased accessibility (Fig. 4C). These findings indicate that LED treatment can induce changes in chromatin accessibility in apricot fruits, suggesting potential regulatory effects on gene expression and fruit development processes. 

# Analysis of DARs after LED treatment

The distribution of DARs was analyzed across the genome in the three comparison groups. The analysis revealed that CK-0 d and CK-8 d had the most intensive distribution and the greatest number of differentially accessible peaks (Fig. 4D), followed by CK-0 d and LED-8 d (Fig. 4E), and CK-8 d and LED-8 d had the least number of differentially accessible peaks (Fig. 4F). Among the eight chromosomes, chromosomes 1, 2, and 6 exhibited the widest distribution of differentially accessible peaks. 

To demonstrate the functional significance of the differentially accessible regions, the peak regions were annotated based on their proximity to gene functional elements in the genome. In the CK-0 d vs. CK-8 d, the majority of DARs were located in the promoter region $( 6 2 . 7 5 \ \% )$ and distal intergenic regions $( 3 2 . 6 8 ~ \% )$ . Only a small percentage of DARs were found in other exons $( 2 . 6 3 \% )$ , other introns $( 1 . 6 1 \ \% )$ , the 1st intron $( 0 . 2 7 \ \% )$ , and the 1st exon (0.07 %) (Fig. 4G). Similarly, in the CK-0 d vs. LED-8 d comparison, the majority of DARs were located in the promoter region $( 6 3 . 0 9 \mathrm { ~ \% } )$ and distal intergenic regions $( 3 3 . 1 1 \ \% )$ ), with a small percentage found in other exons $( 2 . 2 1 \ \% )$ ), other introns (1.31 %), the 1st intron $( 0 . 2 3 \% )$ , and the 1st exon $( 0 . 0 6 \% )$ (Fig. 4H). In contrast, in the CK-8 d vs. LED-8 d comparison, most DARs were located in the promoter region $( 6 9 . 1 \ \% )$ and distal intergenic regions $( 2 9 . 2 7 \% )$ , with a small fraction found in other exons $( 1 . 6 3 ~ \% )$ (Fig. 4I). 

These results indicate that LED treatment induces significant changes in chromatin accessibility, particularly in the promoter region and distal intergenic regions. These changes suggest potential regulatory effects on gene expression through modulation of the accessibility of specific genomic regions.

# Discussion

The maturation and senescence of apricot fruits are regulated at multiple levels, which lead to a series of changes in physiological 

properties, genes, epigenetic material and metabolic substances [20]. LED is a relatively new technology, having the advantages of small size, long life, simple operation, low cost, non-toxic and no residue, which fully meets the requirements of consumers for safety and environmental protection in food consumption [26]. Firmness is an important index for evaluating the maturity and storage quality of fruit [27]. At the end of storage, the firmness of the apricot fruits in the LED treatment group was $2 2 \%$ greater than that in the CK group. MDA is one of the most important products of membrane lipid peroxidation, and its production can aggravate membrane damage, cause the fruit to quickly soften and eventually lead to decay [28]. On Day 8 of storage, the MDA content of the untreated fruits was $6 6 ~ \%$ greater than that of the treated fruits. Phenolic compounds, including flavonoids, are products of plant secondary metabolism [29]. Phenolic compounds not only have a variety of biological activities but also play an important role in scavenging free radicals and in combating biological and abiotic stresses [30]. In our study, there were similar trends in total phenol and flavonoid contents, which were $61 \%$ and $6 3 \ \%$ lower, respectively, in LED-treated fruits on Day 12 than in fruits at the time of treatment. In contrast, the total phenol and flavonoid contents of the fruits decreased by $8 4 \%$ .

Fruit softening is an important factor influencing the shelf life of fruits and is regulated by a series of enzymes associated with primary cell wall modifications and their encoded genes [34]. During 

the softening process, protopectins are hydrolyzed into watersoluble pectins, and the structural integrity of the celluloses and hemicelluloses tends to decrease. When the shape of the cells changes from round to oval or oblong, the adhesion force between cells decreases, the intercellular space increases, the cell wall becomes thin and may even collapse, and the cell juice is released [34]. EG, an enzyme belonging to the cellulase family encoded by the EG9 and EG11 genes, was downregulated under LED light. This downregulation contributes to maintaining the integrity of the cell wall and plant firmness.

Fruits contain a significant amount of amino acids [35], which impact their sensory properties, including taste, aroma, and color [36]. In our study, the levels of bitter amino acids, namely, Lvalyl-L-leucine and L- $\gamma$ -glutamyl-L-leucine, increased during the storage of untreated fruits. However, after LED treatment, the levels of these compounds decreased, resulting in the preservation of flavor quality in apricot fruits during later stages of the shelf life period. Additionally, the umami amino acid N-alpha-acetyl-Lasparagine, as well as the aromatic amino acids L-valyl-Lphenylalanine and gamma-glutamyl-phenylalanine, increased over time. These findings indicate that apricot fruits retain the ability to synthesize flavor substances after harvest. Moreover, in addition to citric acid and malic acid, the levels of succinic acid and amino malonic acid in the fruits increased during the storage period but decreased after LED treatment. LED light facilitated the breakdown of organic acids and enhanced the sweetness of the fruits during storage. 

ATAC-seq, a method utilized for detecting chromatin accessibility, has been employed in studies investigating postharvest fruits and vegetables [37]. This method aids in the identification of pro-

moter regions, potential enhancers or repressors, and important transcription factors (TFs) [37]. The AP2/ERF proteins form a plant-specific TF family characterized by a highly conserved DNA-binding domain comprising approximately 60 amino acids [38]. TFs containing ERF-binding domains are widely present in plants and play roles in growth, development, signal transduction, and physiological and biochemical reactions [39]. Our ATAC-seq results revealed enrichment of ERF TFs. Among them, ERF2, a member of the AP2/ERF family, acts at the downstream end of the ethylene signaling pathway [39]. ERF2 is induced by ethylene to regulate the expression of its target genes and participate in ethylene signal transduction [40]. Studies in apple fruits have demonstrated that ERF2 is expressed during ripening and is positively regulated by ethylene, particularly MdERFs (two ethylene response factors involved in apple fruit ripening) [40]. Our research aligns with these findings, as we observed a substantial enrichment of ERF2 TFs, which promoted the expression of ACO1 and ACS.

Similarly, in our study, apricot fruits exhibited an enrichment of MYB TFs, particularly MYB124, in the LED treatment. Through transcriptomic, metabolomic, and ATAC-seq analyses, we identified a proposed mechanism by which LED light influences the quality and ripening of apricot fruits. LED affects key metabolic pathways, including those involved in flavonoid biosynthesis, phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, and the metabolism of ascorbic acid and uronate. Specifically, we observed that LED light influences the expression of regulatory genes such as CHS and DFR, which are involved in the conversion of dihydroquercetin to epigallocatechin. Furthermore, our analysis revealed significant changes in crucial genes related to fruit plant hormone biosynthesis, fruit softening, and fruit color transformation. By modulating these pathways, LED treatment contributes to the maintenance of important fruit qualities, such as flavor, color, and texture, thereby extending the shelf life of fruits. 

We constructed a network model (Fig. 6) to illustrate the potential regulatory mechanism through which LED light influences the postharvest ripening of apricot fruits. Our study helps to elucidate the regulatory mechanisms underlying the effects of LED light on fruit quality during ripening. 

# Conclusion

Transcriptomic, metabolomic, and ATAC-Seq analyses revealed that LED-activated pathways involved in ascorbic acid and uronate metabolism and flavonoid and phenylpropionic acid biosynthesis affected the expression of key genes related to plant hormone signaling, fruit texture and color transformation, antioxidant activity, and the regulation of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis. This study provides valuable insights into the effects of LED technology on the ripening of apricot fruits and presents a promising method for extending the storage and shelf life of other fruits and vegetables in the future.
