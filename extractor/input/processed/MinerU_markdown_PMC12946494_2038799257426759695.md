# ABSTRACT

Woody biomass is a promising source of fermentable sugars for biofuels and bio-based chemicals, but its industrial use is limited by the costly biorefinery process. A viable strategy to reduce costs involves enhancing both biomass processability and the generation of high-value co-products. Here, we report the implementation of a synthetic metabolic pathway in Populus tremula $\times P .$ . alba to produce 2-pyrone-4,6-dicarboxylic acid (PDC), a key building block for biodegradable plastics and high-performance materials. This artificial pathway—comprising microbial genes AroG, QsuB, PmdA, PmdB, and PmdC—enabled de novo PDC production in the stems of transgenic poplar. Pathway expression also induced substantial metabolic reprogramming and altered cell wall composition. These include the hyperaccumulation of simple phenolics like protocatechuic acid (PCA) and vanillic acid (VA), alongside reduced levels of $p$ -hydroxybenzoic acid. A large portion of VA was ester-linked to cell wall lignin, while PCA was incorporated into the lignin backbone, forming novel benzodioxane units; concurrently, lignin in transgenic plants exhibited a drastic reduction in guaiacyl- and syringyl-units, with a notable increase in $p$ -hydroxyphenyl-units. Hemicellulose content, particularly xylan, was also significantly increased. Moreover, expression of the PDC-pathway led to the formation of novel VA-derived suberin aromatics, enhancing suberization in bark and roots and improving salt stress tolerance. These changes led to improved saccharification efficiency, with up to $2 5 \%$ more glucose and 2.5 times xylose released from woody biomass. These results demonstrate the metabolic flexibility of poplar and highlight its potential for engineering cost-effective, stress-resilient bioenergy crops with enhanced biorefinery traits. 

# 1 | Introduction

Autotrophic plants harness solar energy to convert $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and water to reduced carbon, which in turn elaborates a vast array of macro- and small-molecule metabolites through their amazing intricate metabolic capacity. Therefore, green plants 

represent attractive hosts for cost-effective production of highvalue chemicals (Yuan and Grotewold  2015). Lignocellulosic utilisation towards a sustainable bioeconomy is hindered by the high cost of the biorefinery process of cellulosic biomass. One promising approach is to produce high-value bioproducts in planta that can be fractionated and recovered at biorefineries, 

thereby enhancing biomass values and collectively leading to a more cost-competitive biorefinery (Lin and Eudes  2020; Yang et al. 2022). Techno-economic analysis indicates that in-planta accumulation of $0 . 1 \% { - } 0 . 3 \%$ dry weight high-value chemicals could achieve production costs comparable to microbial routes operating at $4 0 \% - 5 5 \%$ of maximum theoretical yields, emphasising the viability of in-planta production of bio-based chemicals in lignocellulosic biomass as an alternative to fossil-derived products (Yang et al. 2022). 

2-pyrone-4,6-dicarboxylic acid (PDC) is a promising building block chemical for diverse biodegradable polyesters and other polymers with novel functionalities. PDC is a chemically stable compound generated during the microbial degradation of lignin and features a pseudo-aromatic ring structure with two carboxyl groups (Masai et al. 1999). Due to this distinctive structure, PDC becomes an ideal building block in the production of performance-advantaged polymers. For instance, polycondensation of PDC and lactic acid results in a copolyester possessing higher thermal stability and elasticity (Michinobu et al. 2010). Polyesters of PDC and succinic anhydride exhibit enhanced biodegradability (Michinobu et al. 2009). Polyester produced from PDC and bis(hydroxyethyl) terephthalate demonstrates strong adhering properties against metal and glass surfaces (Hishida et al. 2009). Therefore, PDC is a promising dicarboxylic acid alternative to produce synthetic plastics. 

Organic chemical synthesis of PDC has not been realised, except for a few exploits in the production of PDC via chemical conversion of lignin valorization products such as gallic acid and tannic acid (Moeller et al. 2024) and via a chemo-microbial hybrid process from polyethylene terephthalate wastes (Kang et  al.  2020). In recent years, biological synthesis of PDC has been investigated in various microbes including Pseudomonas putida, Escherichia coli, and Novosphingobium aromaticivorans either by manipulating the endogenous pathway or introducing heterologous genes from Sphingomonas paucimobilis SYK-6 or Comamonas testosteroni KF-1 strains which can degrade various lignin compounds into value-added products (Lee et  al.  2022; Masai et al. 1999; Nakajima et al. 2009; Otsuka et al. 2006). As part of a lignin degradation pathway in bacteria, the protocatechuate (PCA) 4,5-cleavage pathway is a metabolic route that features PDC as a key intermediate. In this route, PCA is first converted into 4-carboxy-2-hydroxymuconate-6-semialdehy de (CHMS) by PCA 4,5-dioxygenase (LigAB in S. paucimobilis SYK-6 or PmdAB in C. testosteroni); then CHMS is oxidised by CHMS dehydrogenase (LigC or PmdC) to produce PDC (Otsuka et al. 2006). 

Autotrophic plants offer a sustainable platform for producing value-added biochemicals by harnessing solar energy and atmospheric $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ . Several studies have demonstrated the feasibility of engineering the shikimate pathway to enhance the accumulation of shikimate-derived metabolites (Eudes et al. 2015; Unda et al. 2022). In particular, overexpression of 3-dehydroshikimate dehydratase (QsuB) from Corynebacterium glutamicum in poplar, switchgrass, and sorghum led to the production of PCA (Hao et  al.  2021; Tian et  al.  2022; Unda et  al.  2022). A pilot trial in Arabidopsis demonstrated that the expression of C. testosteroni genes encoding the $\alpha \cdot$ and $\beta$ -subunits of PCA 4,5-dioxygenase (PmdA and PmdB) and CHMS dehydrogenase 

(PmdC), together with the genes encoding C. glutamicum 3-dehydroshikimate dehydratase (QsuB) and a feedbackresistant 3-deoxy-D-arabinoheptulosonate 7-phosphate synthase (AroG*) from E. coli, is able to divert carbon flux of the shikimate pathway to produce PDC (Lin et al. 2021). However, the possibility of producing PDC in actual bioenergy crops remains to be demonstrated. 

Poplar (Populus spp.) has emerged as one of the most promising biomass feedstocks for bioenergy and bioproducts due to its rapid growth, high biomass yield, and adaptability to diverse environmental conditions. As a fast-growing deciduous tree, poplar exhibits a short rotation cycle, making it an ideal candidate for sustainable biomass production. One of the key advantages of poplar lies in its lignocellulosic composition, which makes it highly suitable for biofuel production. Additionally, its wood fibres serve as valuable raw materials for pulp and paper industries, bioplastics, and other value-added bioproducts (Porth and El-Kassaby  2015). Given its high productivity, ease of genetic modification, and ability to grow on marginal lands, poplar represents a sustainable and scalable solution for meeting the rising demand for renewable energy and bioproducts. In this study, we investigate the possibility of producing PDC in the woody biomass of poplar via heterologous expression of a de novo biosynthetic pathway composed of AroG*, QsuB, PmdA, PmdB, and PmdC to further enhance poplar woody biomass value. We demonstrate that the implementation of the five gene-containing expression cassette not only leads to the production of high-value PDC but also results in substantial metabolic shifts, including a hyperproduction of simple phenolics PCA and its derivative vanillic acid (VA), the incorporation of novel wall-bound phenolics, alterations in total lignin content and composition, and an increase in hemicellulose content. Because of changes in cell wall structure and composition, the saccharification efficiency of the woody biomass of PDC transgenic poplars is drastically improved. Moreover, implementation of the PDC biosynthetic pathway increased bark and root suberization, thereby conferring enhanced salt stress tolerance. These results indicate that poplar possesses intricate metabolic plasticity that can be exploited to produce high-value chemicals and enhance plant stress resilience, while improving its biomass processability.

# 2.2 | PDC Transgenic Poplars Exhibit Enhanced Accumulation of Novel Metabolites

Subsequently, methanol-soluble metabolites were extracted from stems of 4-month-old, soil-grown plants to quantify PDC via liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC–MS). LC– MS analysis resolved a metabolite in PDC transgenic lines, which exhibits a molecular ion [M-H]− at $m / z$ of 182.99, identical to that of the authentic PDC standard, indicating the accumulation of the desired PDC product. This metabolite was absent in the WT plants (Figure 2a,b). The titre of accumulated PDC in stems of the transgenic lines ranged from 1 to $1 . 5 \mathrm { m g / g }$ dry weight (DW) (Figure  2c), and, in general, correlated with transgene expression levels across the different lines (Figure  1b). Furthermore, ultraviolet (UV)-LC–MS profiling also resolved a few additional metabolites accumulated in PDC transgenic plants (Figure S4). Peaks 1–4 (P1–P4) showed a UV spectrum analogous to PCA but had different molecular masses, implicating they are PCA-derived conjugates. Among them, P1 exhibited a molecular ion $[ \mathrm { M } \mathrm { - H } ] ^ { - }$ at $m / z$ of 315.07 and a corresponding fragment ion at $m / z$ of 153.019. The neutral loss of $1 6 2 m / z$ suggests the presence of a PCA glucoside conjugate (Figure  S4a,b). P4 showed a molecular ion $[ \mathrm { M } \mathrm { - H } ] ^ { - }$ at m/z 232.98 and a fragment ion at m/z 153.02. The neutral loss of $8 0 m / z$ suggests a putative PCA sulfate conjugate (Figure  S4a,c). After acid-hydrolysis of methanolic extracts, all these compounds were converted to PCA aglycone that shows a retention time, UV spectrum, and mass spectrum similar to the authentic PCA standard (Figure 2d). Additionally, another metabolite was resolved before and after acid-hydrolysis, which displays a UV spectrum and mass spectrum (with a molecular ion at $m / z$ of 167.03) identical to those of the authentic standard 4-hydroxy-3-methoxybenzoic acid, that is, VA (Figure 2d, Figure S4a,d). The accumulation levels of PCA and VA aglycones ranged from $1 . 7 3 { - } 4 . 2 0 \mathrm { m g / g }$ of DW and $0 . 5 1 \mathrm { - } 1 . 1 3 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW, respectively, in the 4-month-old transgenic plants, while none or only trace amounts were detected in the WT (Figure 2e,f). Formation of VA likely results from 3-O-methylation of PCA through the activity of endogenous O-methyltransferases. To explore this possibility, 16 O-methyltransferase homologous genes reported to be expressed in poplar bark and xylem tissues (Barakat et al. 2011) were selected for qRT-PCR analysis. Interestingly, one caffeic acid 3-O-methyltransferase (COMT) homologue OMT21 (Shi et al. 2013) showed a 3- to 5-fold increase in its expression in the PDC transgenic lines relative to the WT (Figure S5), implicating its potential role in the methylation of PCA to VA. In contrast to the de novo synthesis and accumulation of PCA and VA, the amount of $p$ -hydroxybenzoic acid $\cdot ^ { p }$ -HBA) that is intrinsically produced in poplar was surprisingly reduced by $3 6 \% - 6 3 \%$ in PDC transgenic lines (Figure  2g). These results indicate that the expression of the de novo PDC pathway not 

only results in the biosynthesis of PDC but also leads to the accumulation of the biosynthetic intermediate PCA as well as its metabolic derivative VA in poplar woody biomass. 

# 2.3 | Expression of PDC Pathway Alters Wall-Bound Phenolics

Populus species intrinsically accumulate $p$ -HBA esters on their cell wall lignin (Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021). Since PDC transgenic plants accumulate novel methanol-soluble phenolics in woody biomass, we further examined if these compounds can potentially incorporate into cell wall polymers. Wall-bound phenolics were released from stem cell wall residues (CWRs) of both the PDC transgenic and WT plants by alkaline hydrolysis and profiled through UV-LC–MS. It revealed that the wall-bound $p$ -HBA was surprisingly reduced by $8 0 \% - 9 0 \%$ , while substantial amounts of VA accumulated in the cell walls of PDC transgenic lines, in the range of 0.6 to $1 . 3 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW CWR (Figure 3, Figure S6a,b). Additionally, another compound was detected in the extracted wall-bound phenolics of all PDC transgenic lines; it showed a retention time, UV spectrum, and mass spectrum (with a molecular ion $[ \mathrm { M } \cdot \mathrm { H } ] ^ { - }$ at $m / z$ of 121.03) similar to those of authentic $p$ -hydroxybenzaldehyde $\dot { \boldsymbol { p } }$ -HBAl). The content of $p$ -HBAl ranged between 3 and $5 \mathrm { m g / g }$ CWR in the PDC transgenic lines, compared to $0 . 4 3 \mathrm { m g / g }$ CWR in the WT (Figure 3, Figure  S6c). The amount of wall-bound $p$ -coumarate $\cdot p$ -CA) in poplar stems was low but showed a 1.7–2.3 fold increase in the PDC transgenic lines (Figure S7). Interestingly, despite the high-level accumulation of soluble PCA in PDC transgenic lines, no or only trace amounts of it were detected in the alkaline hydrolysates from cell wall residues, suggesting that PCA does not occur as alkali-labile pendent esters in cell walls. These findings suggest that the expression of the PDC biosynthetic pathway not only alters the pool of soluble phenolics but also influences the incorporation of wall-bound phenolics. 

# 2.4 | Expression of PDC Pathway Alters Lignin Content and Composition

With the detection of alterations in wall-bound phenolics, we further examined potential changes in lignin and polysaccharide content and composition in the cell walls of transgenic poplars. The conventional phloroglucinol-HCl staining on the stem cross sections, which produces violet-red colour indicative of lignin, revealed weaker staining in the fibres of xylem tissues of PDC transgenic plants compared to the WT, suggesting a substantial reduction in total lignin content (Figure 4a,b). Similarly, Mäule staining, which selectively stains syringyl (S)-lignin units in purple-reddish colour, also displayed reduced staining intensity in the PDC transgenic plants, indicating a reduction in Slignin units (Figure 4c,d). Despite the discernible reduction in lignin content, the overall vasculature structure, as examined by toluidine blue staining, showed no dramatic differences between the PDC transgenic and WT plants, and no drastic vessel cell collapse was observed in the PDC transgenic wood tissues (Figure 4e,f).

Furthermore, novel lignin benzodioxane (BD) units (Martin et al. 2023; Unda et al. 2022), likely derived from the cross-coupling of PCA, were detected at the levels of $1 \% - 2 \%$ $\mathrm { \Delta B D _ { a } }$ relative to $\mathrm { ^ { 1 / 2 S } } _ { 2 / 6 } + \mathrm { G } _ { 2 } + \mathrm { ^ { 1 / 2 H } } _ { 2 / 6 } )$ in the PDC transgenic lines, while this signal was essentially undetectable in the WT control lines. This indicates that, as previously shown in QsuB-expressing poplar (Unda et  al.  2022), PCA serves as a non-canonical lignin monomer participating in radical coupling with other canonical lignin monomers (e.g., monolignols) during lignification in the PDC transgenic lines (Figure 5c). 

Collectively, these chemical and NMR data of the poplar cell walls revealed a substantial alteration of lignin, characteristic of an increase in H units at the expense of conventional S and G units and the incorporation of novel phenolics in PDC transgenic lines. These results strongly suggest that the expression of the PDC biosynthetic pathway redirects carbon flux towards the production of PCA, VA, and PDC, and disturbs monolignol biosynthesis. 

# 2.5 | Expression of PDC Pathway Alters Non-Cellulosic Polysaccharide Composition

Besides analysing lignin content and composition, we also examined the potential alterations in cell wall polysaccharides in PDC transgenic lines. Quantification of crystalline cellulose content revealed no difference in PDC transgenic lines compared to the WT control lines, suggesting that engineering PDC production does not affect cellulose deposition (Figure  6a). In contrast, the total amount of hemicellulosic sugars was elevated approximately by $6 0 \% - 7 0 \%$ in PDC transgenic lines, showing $2 8 \% - 2 9 \%$ of CWR compared to only $1 7 \% - 1 8 \%$ of CWR in the WT (Figure 6b). Notably, xylose, the primary monosaccharide in xylan, increased by $7 0 \% - 8 0 \%$ , along with $4 0 \% - 5 0 \%$ increases in the amounts of rhamnose, arabinose, and galactose in PDC transgenic lines (Figure 6b). These data indicate that engineering PDC biosynthesis in poplar alters not only lignin but also matrix polysaccharide deposition.

# 3 | Discussion

The urgent need for sustainable alternatives to fossil fuels and petroleum-based materials has intensified interest in renewable feedstocks. Woody biomass offers a promising solution, providing biodegradable and high-performance materials that can reduce plastic pollution and fossil fuel dependency. In this study, we successfully engineered hybrid aspen (P. tremula $\times P .$ alba) to produce the plastic precursor PDC by introducing a de novo biosynthetic pathway comprising five bacterial genes: AroG*, QsuB, PmdA, PmdB, and PmdC. The pathway was functionally active in the transgenic poplars, leading to the accumulation of solventextractable PDC up to $0 . 1 5 \%$ DW biomass in both stem xylem and bark tissues, demonstrating the feasibility of PDC production in a bioenergy crop. Technoeconomic analysis on the exemplar high-value compounds, including PDC, has demonstrated that in planta accumulation of $0 . 1 \% - 0 . 3 \%$ DW of the targeted compounds can achieve production costs comparable to microbial routes operating at $4 0 \% - 5 5 \%$ of maximum theoretical yields. These in planta yields and accumulation rates are sufficient to ensure cost competitiveness in biorefinery (Yang et al. 2022). 

Notably, engineering PDC biosynthesis induced significant metabolic shifts, including the accumulation of methanolextractable simple phenolics, incorporation of novel wall-bound esters, formation of novel suberin aromatics, substantial alterations in lignin and non-cellulosic polysaccharide contents, composition or structures, and enhanced stress tolerance (Figure S15). These changes were accompanied by significantly improved woody biomass digestibility with a $2 5 \%$ increase in glucose release and a 2.5-fold increase in xylose release after saccharification (Figure 6c). However, these results were obtained from poplars grown under greenhouse conditions. Further field trial studies are necessary to confirm these improved traits. 

Besides PDC, the transgenic poplars hyperaccumulated the simple phenolic PCA, reaching up to $1 6 . 0 3 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW in the stem and $3 3 . 6 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW specifically in the bark of 2-month-old plantlets grown in sand and fed with culture media. Under salt stress, PCA accumulation was further enhanced, reaching up to $3 8 . 9 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW 

in the stem (Figure 7f, Figure S14). Relatively lower levels of PCA (approximately $3 . 6 2 \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW) were detected in the woody tissues of 4-month-old soil-grown plants (Figure 2). The reduction in PCA levels in the mature, soil-grown plants may reflect biomass dilution effect in woody tissues along with aging and/or reduced enzyme activity in older or dead xylem cells. The hyperaccumulation of PCA presumably results from an increased carbon flux through the shikimate pathway mediated by AroG* combined with the effective conversion of 3-dehydroshikimate enabled by QsuB in the de novo pathway. PCA production, although at lower levels, has been observed in the hybrid poplar $P . a l b a \times P .$ grandidentata) expressing QsuB alone (Unda et al. 2022). PCA, a natural phenolic acid, possesses antioxidant, antiaging, and antiviral properties, making it a valuable industrial raw material for pharmaceuticals, functional foods, and cosmetics (Kakkar and Bais 2014). It is also used as a building block for the synthesis of high-performance polymers and environmentally friendly plastics in the food preservation industry (Popyrina et al. 2023; Soong et al. 2022; Upare et al. 2023) and as an adsorbent and encapsulation agent applied in environmental protection (Hu et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2015). 

We also detected VA accumulation in PDC transgenic plants. This compound is likely synthesised from PCA via an endogenous regio-specific methylation reaction, revealing the intrinsic metabolic plasticity of poplar. A COMT1 homologue was found substantially upregulated in the PDC transgenic plants (Figure S5). A previous study has demonstrated that alfalfa COMT is capable of methylating benzenoid derivatives, such as PCA, protocatechuic aldehyde, and 3,4-dihydroxy-5-methoxybenzaldehyde (Kota et al. 2004). Further investigation is needed to determine whether this poplar COMT homologue is involved in catalysing the conversion of PCA to VA. VA serves as a flavouring agent used in various food products and exhibited a range of bioactivities, including anticancer, antiobesity, antidiabetic, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, making it valuable for nutraceutical application (Kaur et  al.  2022). In PDC transgenic poplars, a significant portion of VA was incorporated in xylem cell walls (most likely attached onto lignin) as ester-linked pendent groups and as an aromatic component in suberin polyester (Figures 3 and 7d). This implicates that the intrinsic poplar acyltransferases can effectively recognise VA-CoA thioester and conjugate it to either monolignols or hydroxyacids/fatty alcohols, ultimately incorporating VA into cell wall polymers. Previously, we have identified and characterised two key aromatic acyltransferases in poplars: PHBMT1, a $p$ -hydroxybenzoyl CoA: monolignol $p$ -hydroxybenzoyltransferase, and PtFHT1, a hydroxyacid/ 

fatty alcohol hydroxycinnamoyltransferase 1, that are responsible for lignin acylation and suberin/cutin aromatic formation, respectively (Cheng et al. 2013; Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021). Both enzymes exhibit a degree of substrate promiscuity towards CoA thioesters; therefore, they might possess potential activity towards VA-CoA. Substrate promiscuity of these endogenous catalytic enzymes, including OMTs and acyltransferases, underlies the remarkable metabolic plasticity of poplar. 

Interestingly, despite the abundant accumulation of soluble PCA (and its glucosides) in PDC transgenic lines, PCA was not detected among the alkaline-hydrolysable wall-bound phenolics. Instead, PCA was found to be fully integrated into the lignin backbone, forming unique benzodioxane structural units (Figure  5). This suggests that either the PCA esters in monolignol–PCA conjugates undergo exclusive radical coupling, completely integrating into lignin via ether linkage, which is resistant to alkaline cleavage, or alternatively, PCA produced in transgenic plants is unable to proceed for transesterification with monolignols to form monolignol–PCA conjugates; instead, it behaves as an authentic lignin monomer and directly participates in lignin polymerisation through radical coupling. It has been reported that expressing QsuB alone in hybrid poplar P. alba $\times P .$ grandidentata resulted in the attachment of PCA glycoside to lignin as pendent ester moieties (Unda et  al.  2022). However, in the present study, neither glycosylated PCA nor its aglycone was detected as wall-bound esters in PDC transgenic poplars (P. tremula $\times P .$ alba), highlighting potential species-specific metabolic capacity. 

Lignin in Populus is naturally acylated with $p$ -HBA (Goacher et al. 2021; Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021; Zhao, Yu, and Liu 2021). Similar to previous observations in QsuB-expressing hybrid poplar, the expression of the PDC pathway resulted in a marked reduction in both methanol-extractable and cell wall-bound $p$ -HBA (Figures $2 \mathrm { g }$ and 3). Although $p$ -HBA is widespread in plants, its biosynthetic origin remains unclear (Batista et al. 2017). A few biosynthetic pathways have been proposed, which involve shikimate via chorismic acid (common in microbes) or phenylalanine via $p$ -coumaric acid or $p$ -coumaroyl CoA through $\beta$ -oxidation or non- $\beta$ -oxidation pathway (Mustafa and Verpoorte 2005; Sircar and Mitra 2008). Our study found that the diversion of shikimate carbon flux by the PDC biosynthetic pathway substantially impacts $p$ -HBA availability, providing a valuable genetic material to further elucidate $p$ - HBA biosynthesis. Additionally, we detected the increased $p$ -hydroxybenzaldehyde, a possible non- $\beta$ -oxidative pathway intermediate to $p$ -HBA, in the cell wall fraction only (Figures 3 and 5), indicating additional metabolic consequences and the complexity of shikimate diversion. The origin and nature of this $p$ -hydroxybenzaldehyde compound presented in the cell wall fraction warrant further investigation. 

PDC transgenic plants exhibited a significant reduction in Gand S-lignin content and a concomitant increase in H-lignin (Figure  4). Gene expression analysis suggests that the changes in lignin biosynthesis did not occur at the transcriptional level (Figure  S8). Instead, these changes may arise from reduced shikimate availability that impacts the efficacy of HCT (hydroxycinnamoyl-CoA: shikimate/quinate hydroxycinnamoyltransferase), a key enzyme in G- and S-lignin biosynthesis that requires shikimate as a co-substrate (Hoffmann et al. 2003). 

Expression of bacterial shikimate kinase in poplar, which deprives HCT of shikimate due to the accelerated phosphorylation of shikimate, similarly featured reduced G- and S-lignin and increased H-units (Hu et al. 2022). This pattern of disproportional lignin compositional changes in PDC transgenic plants also mirrors that seen in Arabidopsis hct and $c 3 ^ { \prime } h$ mutants (Franke et al. 2002; Li et al. 2010). 

Additionally, PDC transgenic plants displayed increased hemicellulose, primarily xylan, likely as a compensatory response to reduced lignin levels. While the underlying mechanism remains unclear, several previous studies have noted an inverse relationship between lignin and hemicellulose content. For example, in transgenic poplar overexpressing PdGA20ox12A and PtrMYB221, lignin content was significantly reduced, while holocellulose (comprising cellulose and hemicelluloses) content was elevated (Cho et  al.  2019). Similar increases in hemicellulose content and changes in its composition have also been observed in other low-lignin poplar trees (Coleman et al. 2008; Van Acker et al. 2014). Recent solid-state NMR and biochemical studies have shown that lignin extensively interacts with the threefold flexible region of xylan in grasses, Arabidopsis, spruce, and poplar (Kang et al. 2019; Kirui et al. 2022). Moreover, xylan can covalently link to lignin, forming lignin-carbohydrate complexes that contribute to secondary cell wall structural integrity (Giummarella et al. 2019; Terrett and Dupree 2019). Disrupting lignin biosynthesis may weaken these complexes, potentially triggering feedback upregulation of hemicellulose biosynthesis. Consistent with this hypothesis, GT43B, a xylan biosynthetic gene (Zhou et  al.  2007), was upregulated in PDC transgenic plants, while cellulose-related genes were largely unaffected (Figure S8). It has been proposed that lignin deficiency can activate a cell wall damage response, whereby plant cells sense the deficit in lignin and adjust the biosynthesis and remodelling of other wall polymers to maintain structural integrity. Such a response is likely mediated through complex signalling pathways involving reactive oxygen species, hormones such as jasmonic acid, and receptor-like kinases (Denness et al. 2011). The molecular mechanism underlying the induction of GT43B in lignindeficient PDC transgenic poplars remains to be further explored. 

Despite successful PDC production, high levels of the intermediate PCA accumulated in the poplar transgenic lines, suggesting suboptimal function of the engineered bacterial PCA cleavage pathway. PCA breakdown is catalysed by the iron-dependent enzyme protocatechuate 4,5-dioxygenase (LigAB or PmdAB), an $\alpha _ { 2 } \beta _ { 2 }$ tetramer encoded by PmdA and PmdB (Sugimoto et al. 1999). Absolute qRT-PCR analysis indicated that PDC production generally correlated with transgene expression levels. For example, transgenic line #1 showed the highest PDC accumulation, coinciding with the highest transcript abundance of all five transgenes, whereas line #3 displayed the lowest PDC production along with the lowest transcript abundance for most of the transgenes (Figures 1b and 2c). Among the five transgenes, however, PmdB consistently exhibited the lowest transcript abundance across all transgenic lines, suggesting that the low levels of the PmdB subunit, and consequently reduced abundance of the PmdAB dioxygenase, may represent a bottleneck of the engineered pathway converting PCA to PDC. Future work should explore polycistronic expression driven by highly efficient promoters to ensure robust and synchronised expression of PmdA and PmdB. 

Under greenhouse conditions, PDC transgenic plants exhibited a bending phenotype. While the severely reduced lignin likely contributes, the vascular anatomy of the transgenic plants remained largely intact with no severely collapsed vessels, and no major biomass yield penalties were observed even postcoppicing within the examined growth period. This warrants further investigation and refinement of pathway design within a design–build–test–learn (DBTL) framework to sustain plant fitness while producing the desired biochemical traits. 

As a result of changes in lignin and hemicellulose content and composition, the engineered poplars showed significantly improved saccharification efficiency, exhibiting greatly enhanced release of hexose and pentose sugars (Figure 6c). This highlights their suitability for biofuel and bioproduct applications. Moreover, alterations in cell wall suberization—marked by VA incorporation and increased number of suberized cells—suggest enhanced stress tolerance, evident with healthier foliage of PDC transgenic plants under salt stress. This trait may help adapt the engineered bioenergy crops to marginal or degraded lands. Moreover, PDC, PCA, and VA produced in planta can be recovered as soluble compounds, and the ester-linked VA in the cell walls can also be easily cleaved during chemical processing. Both PCA and VA can be efficiently converted to the polymer building block PDC (Otsuka et al. 2023, 2006). Taken together, these engineered traits contribute to a cost-competitive and high-value lignocellulosic biorefinery. 

# 4 | Material and Method

# 4.1 | Generation of PDC Transgenic Poplars

The PDC expression cassette composed of five genes, the feedback insensitive AroG*(L175Q), QsuB, PmdA, PmdB, and PmdC, fused to sequences encoding transit peptides and placed under the control of the stem-specific promoters AtCESA4, AtC4H, AtGAUT12, AtC3′H, and AtCESA7 respectively, was constructed as previously described (Lin et al. 2021). The resulting construct was transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 strain, then to hybrid aspen P. tremula $\times P .$ alba clone INRA 717- IB4 following described procedures (Method S1). 

# 4.2 | RNA Isolation and Quantitative RT-PCR Analysis

For RNA isolation, leaf samples were harvested and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen. Samples were then ground to a fine powder and total RNA was extracted using Spectrum Plant Total RNA Kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Reverse transcription reactions and quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis were performed as Method S2. 

# 4.3 | Stress Treatment

One-month-old propagated WT and PDC transgenic plantlets on the growth media were transplanted to the sand and perlite. 

After 4 weeks of acclimation in the greenhouse, the plantlets with similar size and growth status were selected and divided into two groups and watered with $\scriptstyle 1 / _ { 2 }$ strength Hoagland media (Hoagland's No. 2 Basal Salt Mixture, Sigma-Aldrich, USA) with or without 100 mM NaCl, every 5 days, for 4 weeks. Then, the stem length and biomass yield of each plant were determined immediately. The stem, bark, and leaves of each plant were harvested separately for further analysis. 

# 4.4 | Soluble Metabolite Analyses

Soluble metabolites were extracted from 4-month-old, 10 cm basal, dried stems, or 2-month-old, salt-stressed plantlets and analysed as described in Method S3. 

# 4.5 | Cell Wall Residue Preparation, Wall-Bound Phenolic and Lignin Analysis

Cell wall preparation, wall-bound phenolic analysis, and lignin content and composition determination followed the procedures previously described (Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021; Zhao, Yu, and Liu 2021) and as in Method S4. 

# 4.6 | 2D NMR Spectroscopy

The ball-milled extractive-free poplar CWR samples were digested with crude cellulase (Cellulysin; Calbiochem, La Jolla, CA, USA). The obtained lignin-enriched CWRs $\left( \sim 2 0 \mathrm { m g } \right)$ were subjected to the 2D NMR analysis as described in Method S5. 

# 4.7 | Histochemistry and Microscopic Analysis

Suberized tissues staining, phloroglucinol-HCl staining for total lignin, and mäule staining for S-lignin of the 4-month-old basal stem segments followed procedures in Method S6. 

# 4.8 | Suberin Monomer Analysis

Suberin content was analysed in the bark and roots of the 2-month-old WT and PDC transgenic plants grown in sand, based on a previously established transesterification method (Gou et al. 2017; Kosma et al. 2015; Wahrenburg et al. 2021). The detailed procedure was described in Method S7. 

# 4.9 | Carbohydrate Analysis

Crystalline cellulose content was determined using the Updegraff method (Foster et  al.  2010b) with freshly prepared anthrone reagent (Sigma-Aldrich, USA). Absorbance was recorded at $6 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ with the Spark $2 0 \mathbf { M }$ microplate reader (Tecan, Männedorf) as described (Dwivedi et al. 2024; Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021). The detailed procedure was described in Method S8. 

# 4.10 | Saccharification Analysis

Woody biomass saccharification efficiency determination was performed as previously described (Cai et  al.  2016; Zhao, Yu, Lam, et al. 2021), and as in Method S9. 

# 4.11 | Statical Analysis

Statistical analyses for most experiments were conducted with one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey's multiple comparison test implemented in GraphPad Prism version 10 unless otherwise indicated in Figure legends. Details regarding the statistical analyses, including sample sizes and biological replicates, are provided in the Figure legends.
