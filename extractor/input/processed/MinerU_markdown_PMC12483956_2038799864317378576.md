# Summary

Monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL) is involved in the last step of triacylglycerol breakdown by hydrolysing the monoacylglycerol (MAG) to free fatty acid and glycerol. In the present study, 21 and 38 MAGL genes were identified in Glycine max (cultivated soybean) and Glycine soja (wild) genomes, respectively. Gene-specific association performed using whole genome resequencing data by mixed linear model showed a significant association with total seed oil, linolenic, and oleic acid content. Subsequent haplotypic analysis revealed allelic variations for MAGL genes in soybean germplasm. Diversity analysis indicated a balancing selection of MAGL genes in cultivated soybean compared to wild soybean. Transient expression of three candidate MAGL proteins in Nicotiana tabacum leaves showed chloroplast-specific localization, which is the site for fatty acid biosynthesis. An extensive transcriptomic evaluation revealed comparatively higher expression of five genes in soybean seeds, and nine genes showed higher expression under abiotic stress conditions like drought and heat. The quantitative real-time PCR analysis of three candidate MAGL genes showed differential expression under arsenic and silicon supplementation. Gene co-expression analysis showed the interaction of MAGL with diacylglycerol acyltransferase and triacylglycerol. In addition, confocal microscopy and fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) analysis of yeast expressing four GmMAGL genes showed altered lipid deposition, leading to smaller and more dispersed lipid droplets, suggesting its significant role in lipid metabolism. Manipulation of MAGL can be a pragmatic strategy to improve abiotic stress tolerance, likely by membrane lipid remodeling under environmental stress. Similarly, MAGL could be strategically utilized to enhance oil yield by regulating lipid metabolism. 

# Introduction

Monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL) is involved in the hydrolysis of monoacylglycerol (MAG), which is the final step of TAG breakdown (Fredrikson et al., 1986; Tornqvist and Belfrage, 1976). The MAGL belongs to the serine hydrolase superfamily and contains a highly conserved ${ \tt o } / \beta$ -hydrolase domain with Ser (S), Asp (D), and His (H) catalytic triad (Karlsson et al., 1997). The nucleophilic S residue present in the nucleophilic elbow of the catalytic triad belongs to the GxSxG motif, which is conserved in MAGLs. The catalytic triad is located at the bottom of the long channel under the lid domain of the enzyme. The lid domain functions as a gate by controlling substrate access to the binding pocket (Tyukhtenko et al., 2020). Site-directed mutagenesis has shown that the alteration of any component of a catalytic triad results in a significant decrease in enzyme efficiency (Kim and Suh, 2016). The site-directed mutagenesis of human MAGL showed that the S122A mutation does not induce a detectable conformational change; however, in the case of H269A and 

D239A substitutions, there was a complete shift towards the closed conformations (Tyukhtenko et al., 2020). In mammals, the structure and function of MAGLs are well studied (Labar et al., 2010; Scalvini et al., 2016; Tornqvist and Belfrage, 1976). One of the seminal studies by Fredrikson and colleagues showed that the selective removal of the MAGL using an immunoprecipitation assay led to a decrease in glycerol and fatty acids released from an adipose tissue preparation, whereas the addition of purified MAGL led to the completion of TAG hydrolysis (Fredrikson et al., 1986). The MAGL hydrolyses substrates with various numbers of double bonds (C18 : 1, C18 : 2; C20 : 4) and acyl chain lengths (from $\subset 8 : 0$ to C18 : 0) (Kupiecki, 1966; Rindlisbacher et al., 1987). In mammals, MAGL regulates the fatty acid pathway, which affects cancer pathogenesis (Marino et al., 2019; Pisanti et al., 2013) and other diseases (Bluett et al., 2017; Lutz et al., 2015). In humans, MAGL is highly expressed in the brain and white adipose tissue, and its high expression is also observed in aggressive cancer cells, where it modulates cancer metabolism through the production of free 

fatty acids. The MAGL has also been reported as an effective therapeutic target for cancer. A recent study performed with glioblastoma stem cells has shown that arsenite-resistance protein 2 (ARS2) is directly involved in the activation of the MGLL gene encoding MAGL protein (Yin et al., 2020). Such activation plays an important role in the tumorigenicity and self-renewal of glioblastoma stem cells. In mammals, MAGL plays a major role in the biosynthesis of arachidonic acid by hydrolysing the endocannabinoid 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG), which leads to the synthesis of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids in specific tissues such as the brain, liver, and lung (Blankman et al., 2007). It has been shown that arsenite (As(III)) decreases arachidonic acid in mammals (Anwar-Mohamed et al., 2012, 2014). This report has opened an entirely new area of research and expanded understanding of the MAGL’s versatile role. It has been reported that silencing of the MAGL gene leads to reduction of arachidonic acid formation, which subsequently disables the pseudothecial formation in case of Stemphylium eturmiunum, a pathogenic fungus responsible for onion blight (Xu et al., 2019). Earlier studies in the plant also suggested MAGL’s role in abiotic stress, including heavy metals, considering the role of membrane lipids in sensing and signaling (Gao et al., 2010; Kaup et al., 2002; Mueller, 2004; Troncoso-Ponce et al., 2013). In rice, it was shown that under arsenite As(III) stress, about 59 lipid biosynthetic genes, including MAGL, were differentially expressed (Yu et al., 2012). The MAGL At1g52760 gene, initially identified as a putative lysophospholipase 2 (lysoPL2) in Arabidopsis, has been reported to be involved in the detoxification of peroxidized membrane under cadmium-induced oxidative stress conditions (Gao et al., 2010). However, the role of MAGLs is still not sufficiently addressed in plants. 

In plants, MAGL homologs have been identified and characterized only in Arabidopsis, which possesses 16 MAGL genes, of which only 11 MAGL showed lipase activity (Kim et al., 2016). The Arabidopsis MAGLs are localized in the cytosol, endoplasmic reticulum (ER), Golgi, mitochondria, and/or chloroplast (Kim et al., 2016) whereas in animal systems MAGLs are localized only in the cytosol and plasma membrane (Dinh et al., 2002). In the case of Arabidopsis, higher hydrolase activities were observed with unsaturated 16 : 1, 18 : 1, 18 : 2, and $1 8 : 3$ fatty acids than with saturated $1 6 : 0$ and $1 8 : 0$ fatty acids (Kim et al., 2016). The Arabidopsis MAGL is predicted to have an imperative role in many developmental and physiological processes, as its expression is up-regulated during seed germination, senescence, and under abiotic stresses (Gao et al., 2010; Kaup et al., 2002; Mueller, 2004; Troncoso-Ponce et al., 2013). The tapetal expression of the Brassica MAGL gene was observed to result in impaired pollen development and invoked a stress response (Gao et al., 2019). It has been observed that a mutation in TAG lipases like SDP1 enhanced the seed oil content in Arabidopsis (Van Erp et al., 2014), rapeseed (Kelly et al., 2013), and Jatropha (Jatropha curcas) (Kim et al., 2014). These reports showed that the suppression of TAG degradation enhances the oil content of seeds, and the TAG turnover is highly active in developing seeds. The quality of TAG varies among plant species due to the fatty acid composition. For instance, Olive and rapeseed oils contain a large portion of oleic acid $( 1 8 : 1 )$ and are mainly used as dietary oils, whereas normal cottonseed and soybean oils contain more amounts of linoleic acid $( 1 8 : 2 )$ . Several efforts are made towards improving oleic acid in soybean through molecular breeding and transgenic approaches (La 

et al., 2014; Pham et al., 2010; Voytas and Gao, 2014). One of the successful attempts by Pham et al. (2010) achieved as high as $80 \%$ of oleic acid in a soybean variety developed by crossing two genotypes having mutations in FAD genes and has a similar fatty acid profile as olive oil. 

Soybean is one of the important oilseed crops and ranks second in global oil production after palm oil (FAOSTAT; https://www.fao.org/3/cb5332en/Oilseeds.pdf). Soybean is widely used for human consumption, animal feed, and many other industrial applications (Padalkar et al., 2022). Many QTLs and GWAS loci governing seed oil content in soybean have been identified, and the most important loci governing this trait are on chromosome 20 (Kumar et al., 2023; Sonah et al., 2015). However, no major gene is known to date that regulates the seed oil content in soybean. Recently, the SWEET39 gene located on chromosome 15 has been shown to have a significant effect on seed oil content in soybean (Zhang et al., 2020). In the case of soybeans, it is important to reduce the polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs), including linoleic and linolenic acids, which are susceptible to oxidation. Large amounts of linoleic and linolenic acids reduce the oxidative stability of soybean oil (Clemente and Cahoon, 2009; Pham et al., 2010; Voytas and Gao, 2014). It is important to develop soybean cultivars with high oil and low PUFA content by targeting the TAG metabolic pathway. The inhibition of TAG degradation by targeting the lipases during seed development is one of the effective strategies for producing soybean cultivars with enhanced oil content. Further exploration of the final step of TAG breakdown is very essential for the improvement of soybean breeding. 

In the present study, we have identified the MAGL homologues in the soybean genome and subsequently studied their phylogenetic distribution, structural diversity, and gene expression in different tissues and under stress conditions. Similarly, a homology-based model was developed to understand the spatial arrangement of conserved motifs of the MAGL protein to predict sites actively involved in substrate binding. In addition, whole-genome re-sequenced information available for the diverse set of soybean germplasm was used to study the haplotypic distribution. The sequencing data also helped to identify variations predicted to have a high impact on MAGL functionality. A gene-specific association study revealed the significant association of MAGL genes with oil and fatty acid content. The present study has used large-scale transcriptomic data to develop a gene co-expression network and predict hub genes possibly having a regulatory role. The study will be helpful to understand the role of MAGL in plants and the pragmatic strategy to manipulate MAGL genes for high oil content with better quality in oilseed crops.

# Conserved features, subcellular localization, chemical and physical properties of monoacylglycerol lipases

All the identified MAGLs contained a/beta hydrolase domain, $\mathsf { G X S X G }$ conserved motifs, and the catalytic triad of S, D, and H based on the multiple sequence alignment and mutual information (MI) network between the residues, sequence conservation, the residue cumulative and proximity MI scores (Figures 2b and Figure S2). The conserved HGXXD (acyltransferase) motif was also present near the C-terminal. The amino acids W and P were also found to be conserved in the MAGL gene family (Figure 2b). The first G (Glycine) residue of the GXSXG conserved motif was replaced with A (Alanine) in Glyma.10G211200 and XP0281856885, and S (Serine) residues in the case of AT5G14980, AT5G19290, Medtr1g095110 MAGL proteins. The MAGLs that showed the change in the first G of the GXSXG motif to A or S were all grouped together in group IV (Figure 2a). 

The MAGLs were localized in the plasma membrane, chloroplast, mitochondria, nuclear, extracellular, and cytoplasm (Table S2). The isoelectric point (pI) and molecular weight of MAGL proteins calculated by the isoelectric point calculator (IPC) vary from 5.0 to 9.0 and 10 to $5 0 \mathsf { k } \mathsf { D }$ , respectively (Table S3). All the MAGLs showed hydrophillic nature based on the negative values generated by the GRAVY tool, except for Loc_Os01g10250, medtr3g109870, medtr6g071475, Glyma.08 g056600, and four G. soja MAGLs, which have hydrophobic nature (Table S4). 

The soybean MAGL genes grouped in subgroup-a and -b have eight exons each except Glyma07G092100, which has five exons (Figure S3). The subgroup-c mostly consists of intron-less genes 

except for one gene with two exons. The group-d possessed genes with seven exons except Glyma.09G171800 having four exons (Figure S3). The cis-regulatory elements identified were mostly related to endosperm expression, seed-specific regulation, meristem expression, defense and stress-responsive, lightresponsive, involved in drought inducibility, salicylic acid, abscisic acid, and gibberellin responsive, involved in circadian control, wound responsive, and many others (Figure S3, Table S5). 

# Tertiary protein structure of monoacylglycerol lipases

The homology-based model was developed to understand the spatial arrangement of conserved motifs of MAGL protein. The molecular three-dimensional protein structure modeling of MAGL was determined by comparing it to the well-studied human 3HJU protein and Arabidopsis. The protein structure of all 21 MAGL proteins in soybean was similar to Arabidopsis and human MAGL proteins (Figure S4). The MAGL consists of ${ \tt o } / \beta$ hydrolase core with $\beta$ -strands surrounded by $\propto$ -helices with a cap region (Ala-Pro) containing 3 a-helices (Figure 3A-a and Figure S4). The protein structure possessed a lid domain and conserved GXSXG motif with the catalytic site containing serine (S) of the hydrolase motif, highly conserved Asp (D) and His (H), along with the glycerol exit (Figure 3A-b–d). The residues Tyrosine (Y), Glutamine (Q), and Glutamic acid (E) interact with the glycerol moiety, that are conserved in all the MAGL proteins, including humans (Figure 3Ae). The mutation in the $\mathsf { G X S X G }$ conserved motif to SXSXG and AXSXG showed conformational changes at the nucleophilic elbow, which may lead to a distorted hydrolytic activity (Figure 3B-,b,c). The predicted stability change $( \Delta \Delta G ^ { \mathrm { S t a b i l i t y } } )$ for mutation G to S in the GXSXG motif showed 0.67 kcal/mole value, indicating a protein destabilizing state (Figure 3B-b). Similarly, mutations G to A at the nucleophilic elbow showed a predicted stability change (DDGStability) value of 2.17 kcal/mol leading to the destabilization of protein structure (Figure 3B-c). 

# Distribution of monoacylglycerol lipase genes in the soybean genome

In the soybean genome, 21 MAGL genes were present on 14 different chromosomes and the maximum number of genes was present on chromosomes 10 and 13 (Figure S5). The previously identified QTLs for oil were also co-localized with the soybean MAGL genes. Chromosome 5 has the maximum number of oil QTLs near the putative MAGL genes, and one of the oil QTLs was present 0.2 Mb region apart. The QTL for linolenic acid and oil content on chromosomes 2 and 20, respectively harbors MAGL genes within the 1.0 Mb regions.

# Subcellular localization of monoacylglycerol lipases

The members of the MAGL family were localized to different sub-cellular compartments, including chloroplast, plasma membrane, mitochondria, nucleus, extracellular, and cytoplasm, based on the computational prediction (Table S2). # Discussion

Lipid catabolism is the essential process for storage oil mobilization in the seeds to provide carbon and energy sources. Lipid 

catabolism is also essential for the remobilization and detoxification of membrane lipids of plant species caused by senescence and abiotic stresses, including heavy metal stress (Gao et al., 2010; Kaup et al., 2002; Mueller, 2004; Troncoso-Ponce et al., 2013). The breakdown of TAG to fatty acid and glycerol is mainly carried out by lipases. The lipase catalyses the hydrolysis of lipids to diacylglycerol and monoacylglycerol. However, very little understanding of the hydrolysis of monoacylglycerol to free fatty acid and glycerol has been gained over the years. Many studies have suggested the involvement of other enzymes than TAG lipase. Recently, a study performed in brassica has shown the role of MAGL in male sterility (Gao et al., 2019). Still, there is a lack of knowledge about the role of MAGL in plants. Therefore, genomewide identification, distribution, and characterization of MAGL genes in plants have great significance. In this regard, the genome-wide study performed in Arabidopsis has identified 16 MAGL genes that are biochemically and functionally characterized (Kim et al., 2016). Considering MAGLs role in fatty acid metabolism and the availability of genomic resources, identification of MAGL homologs in oilseed crops like soybean becomes 

more relevant. As compared to Arabidopsis, a much higher number of MAGL genes were identified in soybean (21 in G. max and 34 in G. soja) which may be due to segmental duplications and the paleopolyploidy nature of the soybean genome. The large differences in the number of MAGL genes in G. max and G. soja were also observed. Such variation provides an opportunity to better study the differential MAGLs since G. max and G. soja are inter-crossable and several mapping populations and introgression lines are being widely used. Similarly, the introgression of additional MAGL from the G. soja can bring novel traits to cultivated soybean. 

The known TAG lipase has ${ \mathsf { H x } } _ { 4 } { \mathsf { D } }$ and $\mathsf { G x S x G }$ motifs, and their functional impact has been confirmed in yeast Tgl3p by sitedirected mutagenesis (Rajakumari and Daum, 2010). In Arabidopsis, MAGL genes have shown hydrolase activity and contain both ${ \mathsf { H X } } _ { 4 } { \mathsf { D } }$ and $\mathsf { G x S x G }$ motifs (Vijayaraj et al., 2012). In the present study, all the analysed MAGL showed conserved $\mathsf { G X S X G }$ and ${ \mathsf { H X } } _ { 4 } { \mathsf { D } }$ motifs. However, the first G of the second GxSxG motif in some genes was replaced with A and S residues, which might have impaired hydrolytic activity. Similar observations were reported in Arabidopsis, where the first residues of the $\mathsf { G x S x G }$ motif possessed S instead of G residues in two of the Arabidopsis genes lacking hydrolytic activity (Kim et al., 2016; Kim and Suh, 2016). In the case of Arabidopsis, a U-shaped nucleophilic elbow structure around the nucleophilic S residue of the GxSxG motif was observed, and when the $\mathsf { G x S x G }$ motif was replaced with the SxSxG, GxAxG, or GxSxS motif, the shape of the nucleophilic elbow around the nucleophilic serine changed to a W shape. Similar results were observed in the present study, as can be seen in Figure 4. Among the soybean MAGLs, only Glyma.10G21120 showed A residues in place of G in the GXSXG motif. The other motifs ‘HGYGND’ and ‘HGYAID’ reported by Kim and Suh (2016) in Arabidopsis were also identified in soybean MAGLs. 

The biochemical and physical properties of protein play an important role in determining the molecular and biological function of a protein. The large variation in molecular weight (from 19 to $4 0 \ k \mathrm { \mathsf { D } } ,$ ) and length (175–396 amino acids) of soybean MAGL suggests structural and functional diversity. The subcellular localization of MAGL was also predicted and was localized in mitochondria, chloroplasts, plasma membrane, and other locations, and these might be responsible for the breakdown of TAG and/or membrane lipid remodeling. Kim et al. (2016) have found fifteen AtMAGL:eYFP proteins localized to three sites: ER and/or Golgi complex, cytosol, and chloroplasts, in tobacco epidermal cells or protoplasts. The Arabidopsis At1g77420 gene localization has been reported in the chloroplast (Kim et al., 2016) and its homologous soybean MAGL (Glyma.10G273600 and Glyma.20G116400) was also found to be localized in the chloroplast in the present study. Similarly, other genes of soybean homologous to Arabidopsis MAGL have similar predicted localization. In the present study, the transient expression of three MAGL-GFP proteins in tobacco leaves showed their localization in the chloroplast, which is the site of de novo fatty acid synthesis in plants (Schnurr et al., 2002). In the case of Arabidopsis, among the 16 AtMAGLs, none showed fluorescent signals in the plasma membranes of tobacco leaf epidermal cells or protoplasts transformed with each YFP-fused AtMAGL construct. Similar results were also observed in the present study. The confocal microscopy revealed Glyma.13G183800, Glyma.09G184000, and Glyma.08G056600 proteins were localized to the chloroplast, which is the site of fatty acid synthesis in plants. 

The tertiary (3D) structure of all MAGL proteins has been investigated using computational molecular modeling against the human HsMAGL. This analysis showed that all proteins share a common ab-hydrolase fold and catalytic triad (S, H, and D) which is essential for enzymatic activity. The alternations of Glycine residue of $\mathsf { G x S x G }$ motif in the case of Arabidopsis led to the altered shape of the catalytic triad and ultimately decreased enzymatic activity (Kim and Suh, 2016). Some of the residues in the MAGL gene are highly conserved and the residues with high mutual information have the probability of coevolution. These coevolving residues can actively be involved in determining the structure and function of a protein. 

Domestication is a bottleneck of genetic diversity, and this was also observed in MAGL genes by comparative analysis of wild and cultivated soybean genes. The negative Tajima’s D values in the wild and positive in cultivated soybean for most of the genes indicate association with the contraction of genes in the population during the process of domestication and artificial selection. Many genes present on different chromosomes of cultivated soybean show collinearity with the same genes in wild soybean and vice versa. This may be due to paleopolyploidization of the soybean genome and later rearrangement and recombination between the different genes. Interestingly, a significantly lower number of MAGLs in G. max as compared to G. soja suggests a possible loss of MAGL during domestication, which might lead to the high oil content in cultivated soybean. The haplotyping analysis showed large variation among the different MAGL genes of soybean germplasm. Several studies have shown significant haplotype-phenotype association that can be utilized for haplotype-based breeding programs (Patil et al., 2016; Rana et al., 2022). Our results showed that some of the MAGL genes were significantly associated with the total oil content and fatty acid profiling and may have some major role in the oil biosynthetic pathway. The protein–protein interaction of MAGL with the DGAT and TAG indicates their role in the oil biosynthetic pathway. Recently, Arias et al. (2022) expressed Arabidopsis genes AtWRI1 (WRINKLED 1 transcription factor) and AtDGAT1 (diacylglycerol acyltransferase) in soybean seeds. Based on expression dynamics and co-expression analysis, Arias et al. (2022) identified MAGL (Glyma.06G087100) as a target of AtWRI1, which was highly expressed in transgenic events. The WRl1 transcription factor is well known as the “Master Regulator” of the oil biosynthesis pathway in plants and demonstrated to have the potential for increasing seed oil content in transgenic plants (Kong et al., 2019). 

The MAGL is known to be involved in the detoxification of membrane lipids, which is very important in abiotic stress conditions. The gene expression analysis shows that genes are mainly expressed in heat, drought, and seedling stages. The high expression of Arabidopsis MAGL At1g52760 was reported to be associated with various stress conditions (Vijayaraj et al., 2012) including cadmium stress (Gao et al., 2010). In our study, its homologous gene (Glyma.13G101400.1) exhibits relatively high expression in the roots and leaves of Si-treated plants and in the stem of As-treated plants. Our results strengthen the finding of (Yin et al., 2020) suggesting MAGL links with genes involved in arsenic metabolism. Yin et al. (2020) showed that arseniteresistance protein 2 activates its novel transcriptional target MGLL, encoding MAGL. In the case of rice, MAGL, among many lipid biosynthetic pathway genes, were differentially expressed under arsenite As(III) stress (Yu et al., 2012) which indicates its role under arsenic stress. Similarly, MAGL At1g52760 in 

Arabidopsis is reported to be involved in the detoxification of the peroxidized membrane under cadmium-induced oxidative stress conditions (Gao et al., 2010). The RNA-seq data show higher expression of most of the genes at the seedling stage, and this stage is associated with the breakdown of lipids for plant growth and development, where lipids are utilized for plant development. The Glyma.03G214800 gene was differentially expressed in different seed developmental stages. The gene co-expression analysis helps to understand functional genes interaction and relationships (Deshmukh et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2019). Our co-expression analyses showed that two genes, Glyma.13G183800 and Glyma.05G248100, were predicted as hub genes that interact with other MAGL genes. 

Results obtained here with the yeast BODIPY assay showed that the expression of GmMAGL in yeast alters the lipid deposition and metabolism. Smaller and dispersed droplets observed in yeast expressing GmMAGL compared to the control suggest its involvement in lipid hydrolysis, which affects the overall lipid turnover. In addition, these results were confirmed with the FACS analysis of BODIPY493/503-stained yeast, where reduced fluorescence was observed with the GmMAGL expression. The results obtained with confocal microscopy and FACS both indicate the probable role of GmMAGL in lipid metabolism. However, the yeast survival assay under different stress conditions failed to show any effect of MAGL under osmotic stress, high salinity, and heavy metal (arsenic stress). Similar results have been reported in a recent study where Arachis hypogaea MAGL overexpressed in Arabidopsis showed no effect on growth and physiological parameters but was found to alter lipid biosynthesis (Zhang et al. 2024). Despite several genomewide MAGL identification studies in different plant species, very little is known about the functionality as compared to mammalian MAGL. Along with the heterologous expression assay, evolutionary analysis and structural evaluation, studies exploring overexpression or knockout of MAGL will be helpful to understand the precise role of different members of the MAGL gene family in plants. Apart from the molecular insights into the role of MAGL, gene-specific associations, haplotypic diversity, and genetic variation presented in the MS will be helpful in developing functional markers for marker-assisted breeding and for efficient exploration of germplasm resources. 

# Conclusion

The MAGL homologs identified in diverse plant species and particularly in wild and cultivated soybean species helped to highlight several interesting facts. The significantly lowered number of MAGLs in cultivated soybean (G. max) as compared to wild soybean (G. soja) provides numerous opportunities to study the role of MAGL homologs, more particularly those missing from the cultivated soybean. The G. max and G. soja are inter-crossable and there are already several cultivars and biparental populations developed from the G. max and G. soja. Therefore, lines with introgression of additional MAGL in G. max background will help to study the role of specific MAGL and to understand why these homologs are absent in G. max. In addition, extensive computational analysis performed with MAGLs identified here revealed conserved motifs and amino acid residues probably having a significant role in protein structure and functionality. The structural information along with genetic variation will be helpful to predict the allelic effects of soybean MAGL. Significant association of MAGLs with seed oil and fatty acid content more particularly linolenic and oleic acid content 

depicts its role in the lipid metabolism pathway. Furthermore, the haplotypic information generated here for the soybean MAGL will be helpful to understand evolution and better explore the allelic diversity through haplotype-based breeding programs in soybean. The transient expression of MAGL proteins in tobacco leaves showed chloroplast-specific localization, which is the site of de novo fatty acid synthesis in plants. Similarly, the present study has used extensive transcriptomic data to provide MAGL gene expression dynamics under different stress conditions and developmental stages. MAGL candidates selected based on the transcriptome data confirmed differential expression under silicon and As stress. Our results strengthen the finding of Yin et al. (2020) suggesting MAGL links with genes involved in arsenic metabolism. In addition, overexpression of MAGL genes in yeast showed significant change in lipid deposition pattern suggesting the potential role in lipid modification and metabolism. Such lipid modification most likely has a significant role under stress conditions. However, the results presented here are preliminary but important to suggest future directions to understand the versatile role of MAGL in plants. 

# Material and methods

# Identification and evolutionary analysis of monoacylglycerol lipases

Protein sequences of soybean, Medicago, rice, and Arabidopsis were downloaded from the Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi. doe.gov/pz/portal.html) and Ensembl (https://plants.ensembl. org/index.html) databases. The protein sequences of G. soja were retrieved from the NCBI database (https://www.ncbi.nlm. nih.gov/genome/?term=glycine+soja). The local protein databases were constructed in the Bioedit software tool (Hall et al., 2011). BLASTP was performed to identify the MAGL in different plant species against the Arabidopsis MAGL query sequences. The protein sequences having bit-score $\geq 1 0 0$ and E-value $\leq 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ were selected as putative MAGLs and used for evolutionary and other downstream analyses. These putative protein sequences were used for the identification of conserved domains by the CDD database and Pfam (Marchler-Bauer et al., 2015) (https://www. ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Structure/bwrpsb/bwrpsb.cgi). The MAGL protein sequences from different plant species were aligned using the ClustalW tool, and the phylogenetic tree was constructed using maximum likelihood statistical methods in MEGA X software (Kumar et al., 2018) (http://www.megasoftware.net/) with the Jones-Taylor-Thornton substitutions model, uniform rates, nearest-neighbour interchange method, and 1000 bootstrap value options. The phylogenetic analysis based on similarity and block/tandem duplication analysis of all the MAGL genes was performed using the Plaza tool (Van Bel et al., 2018) (https://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/plaza). 

# Prediction of tertiary protein structure and physiochemical properties of monoacylglycerol lipases

The 3D protein structure modelling of putative soybean MAGL proteins was performed by using Phyre v2.0 (Kelley et al., 2015; Kelley and Sternberg, 2009) (http://www.sbg.bio.ic.ac.uk/phyre2/ html/page.cgi?id=index). The glycerol molecule was stamp modelled into a known active site from crystal-structured PDB (3HJU) and the structure was visualized using PyMol v.0.99 software tool (https://pymol.en.uptodown.com/windows). The assessment of mutations at the GXSXG motif for protein stability and flexibility was checked with the DynaMut2 tool (http://biosig. 

unimelb.edu.au/dynamut2/). The mutual information, coevolution, and residues interaction network were analysed by using MISTIC software (Mutual Information Server to Infer Coevolution) (Simonetti et al., 2013) (http://mistic.leloir.org.ar/index.php). The MAGL gene features, such as number and position of exons, introns, and conserved features, were identified using the GSDS 2.0 version (Hu et al., 2015) (http://gsds.cbi.pku.edu.cn/). The presence of transmembrane helices was identified by TMHMM Server v. 2.0 (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/TMHMM/); the subcellular localization was analysed by TargetP (Armenteros et al., 2019) (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/TargetP/) and CELLO v.2.5: subCELlular LOcalization predictor (Yu et al., 2006) (http://cello.life.nctu.edu.tw/). The isoelectric point and molecular weight were determined by the isoelectric point calculator (Kozlowski, 2016) (http://isoelectric.org/calculate.php) and Prot-Param (https://web.expasy.org/protparam/). The signal peptide and their location of cleavage were predicted by the online available SignalP-5.0 Server (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/ SignalP/). A sequence logo was created by MEME (Bailey et al., 2015) (http://meme-suite.org/tools/meme) with the default setting having a maximum number of six motifs for the identification of conserved nucleotide and binding sites for the substrate. The graphical representation of different soybean MAGL and nearest identified QTLs for oil and fatty acid content from recent genome-wide association studies and QTL mapping was performed by using MapChart software tool (Voorrips, 2002) (https://www.wur.nl/en/show/Mapchart.htm). The reference genome, physical position of putative genes, and other parameters were according to Wm82.a2.v1 genome assembly (Schmutz et al., 2010). Hydropathicity of MAGL was confirmed by using GRAVY (Grand average of hydropathy) tool. The Cis-regulatory elements were identified in the 2 Kb upstream regions of soybean MAGL genes using the PlantCare database (http:// bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/). 

# Gene-specific association study of MAGL

Gene-specific association analysis of MAGL genes was performed using the generalized and mixed linear model implemented in the TASSEL software (https://tassel.bitbucket.io/). The SNPs from whole genome resequencing data of 481 genotypes were retrieved for the open reading frame of all the 21 MAGL genes and were used for association analysis. The centered kinship method with three maximum alleles and three Principal component analyses (PCA) was used to remove the spurious associations. The SNPs with $- \mathsf { l o g } 1 0 \left( P \right) { \mathsf { > } } 5$ were considered to be significant. For MLM, optimum compression level and P3D variant component estimation were used in TASSEL 5.0 (Bradbury et al., 2007). The results were visualized as Manhattan plots of log10 (P ) values for each SNP and gene position. 

# Haplotyping and diversity analysis of monoacylglycerol lipases

The SNPs information from the whole genome resequencing data of 481 soybean genotypes was downloaded from the SoyKB database (Joshi et al., 2017; Valliyodan et al., 2021) (http://soykb. org/). The SNPs information was extracted for all the MAGL using the chromosomal positions of each gene according to the Wm82.a2.v1 genome assembly. Based on the SNP information, haplotyping of each gene was performed using an in-house script. The SNP annotations for each gene were performed using the SnpEff tool (SnpEff (pcingola.github.io)) and by the variant effect predictor (McLaren et al., 2016) (http://plants.ensembl. 

org/Glycine_max/Tools/VEP). The functional effect prediction of SNPs present in genes was performed using the PROVEAN tool (Choi and Chan, 2015) (http://provean.jcvi.org/seq_submit.php). 

# Evolution of monoacylglycerol lipases

The genetic diversity within wild and cultivated soybean was calculated with measures of nucleotide diversity (h) based on the number of segregating sites and p based on the average number of nucleotide differences per site, and Tajima’s D statistics were also calculated using the TASSEL software (Bradbury et al., 2007). The nonsynonymous substitution rate (Ka) and synonymous substitution rate (Ks) of mutations in the MAGL genes were calculated by using the TBtools software (Chen et al., 2020). 

# Subcellular localization of monoacylglycerol lipases

The full-length coding sequences of MAGL genes were amplified without a stop codon using primers carrying specific restriction sites. The amplified gene product was ligated in-frame into the pCAMBIA1302 vector under the CAMV 35S promoter. The resulting 35S::gene-GFP construct was confirmed by sequencing. The GFP construct was transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. The transformed cells harboring the GFP construct were grown in LB broth containing selective antibiotics. The grown cells were harvested by centrifugation at $3 0 0 0 \ { \mathfrak { g } }$ . for 5 min. The cells were resuspended in ginfiltration buffer (1 M ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ MES buffer (pH 5.4), $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone). The cells were kept resuspended in an infiltration buffer for $^ { 2 - 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and infiltrated in the leaves of fourweek-old tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) plants. A confocal fluorescence microscope was used to observe infiltrated leaves after $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . 

# Gene expression analysis of monoacylglycerol lipases

The expression analysis of MAGL in different tissues, developmental stages, and under abiotic stress conditions of Arabidopsis, rice, and soybean tissues was performed using Gene investigator software (Hruz et al., 2008). Furthermore, the raw reads of soybean RNA-Seq data from the bioprojects PRJNA388955, PRJNA288296, PRJNA399013, PRJNA197251, and PRJNA384236 were downloaded from the NCBI-SRA (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih. gov/sra), which represent different organs/tissues and abiotic stress conditions. The raw sequence reads were processed and mapped to the soybean reference genome in CLC Genomics Workbench. The number of read counts for each gene was normalized in terms of RPKM values. The RPKM values for each MAGL gene were analysed using the MeV software tool (Howe et al., 2010) (http://mev.tm4.org/#/welcome). The RPKM values generated by the CLC genomics workbench for all the soybean MAGL genes were used for Co-expression analyses using CoExpNetViz (Tzfadia et al., 2016). Furthermore, the interaction of Arabidopsis and soybean MAGL was performed in the STRING database using the default parameters as medium confidence of 0.40, K-means clustering with the number of clusters set at 3 and active interaction sources as co-expression, co-occurrence, databases, and experiments (https://string-db.org/). 

# Quantitative real-time PCR analysis of soybean monoacylglycerol lipases

Uniformly grown one-month-old soybean plants were treated with silicon (Si, $1 . 7 ~ \mathsf { m M } ,$ ), arsenite (As(III), 10 mg/kg of soil), and $\mathsf { A s } ( | | | ) + \mathsf { S i }$ with similar concentrations. Potassium silicate and sodium arsenite were used as the sources of Si and As(III), 

respectively. Plant samples were collected after 60 days of treatment. Total RNA was extracted from the root, stem, and leaf of control and stressed plants using SpectrumTM Plant Total RNA Kit. The RevertAid First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Thermo ScientificTM) was used to convert the total RNA into cDNA. The ${ \mathsf { q P C R } }$ was performed using $\mathsf { S Y B R @ { \mathbb { B } } }$ Green Master Mix (Bio-Rad) and the BioRad CFX Connect real-time PCR detection system (Bio-Rad). Each $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ reaction comprises $5 ~ \mu \updownarrow ~ \mathsf { S Y B R } ^ { \textregistered }$ Green master mix, $1 ~ \mu \iota$ template, 2 lL ${ \mathsf { d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } ,$ and $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of each primer $( 1 0 \mathrm { \ m M } )$ . The reactions were performed under the following conditions: initial denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 min, and 40 cycles of quantification comprising denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 1 0 \ 5 } ,$ annealing at $5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 2 0 \ 5 } ,$ and extension at $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 20 s in 96-well optical reaction plates. To verify PCR product stability, a melting curve analysis was performed at the end of the PCR run with a stepwise increase in temperature from $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ by an increment of $0 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ every 5 s. For further analysis, mean CT and standard deviations were calculated. The qPCR primers were designed with the help of the QuantPrime online tool (Table S1) (Arvidsson et al., 2008). The soybean elongation factor (ELF1b) was used as an internal control. 

# Quantification of lipid content in yeast using BODIPY493/503 fluorescent lipophilic dye

The coding sequences for selected MAGL genes were amplified from a soybean cDNA library using gene-specific primers carrying appropriate restriction sites. The amplified gene sequences were ligated into the pYES2.1 vector under galactose-inducible promoter, carrying the URA3 selectable marker. The clones were further confirmed by sequencing. The MAGL: pYES2.1 vectors and empty pYES2.1 vector were individually transformed into yeast strain INVSc using the EZ-YeastTM Transformation Kit (MP Biomedicals). The yeast transformants were grown in SD/ ura medium with $2 \%$ glucose at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 days. The transformants were further confirmed by colony PCR. Three individual transformants for each gene and the empty vector were further used in the study. The yeast cells harbouring MAGL:pYES2.1 vectors and the empty pYES2.1 vector were grown in SD/ ura broth with $2 \%$ dextrose for $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . After incubation, OD600 for all the cultures was adjusted to 0.4, and fresh cultures were initiated from them in SD/ ura broth with $2 \%$ galactose and $1 \%$ raffinose. The cultures were grown at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ and were further used for BODIPY493/503 staining to measure the lipid degradation due to overexpression of MAGL genes. 

The $1 ~ \mu \iota$ of $2 \ m g / \mathrm m L$ BODIPY493/503 solution in DMSO was added to each of the $1 \ : \mathsf { m L }$ yeast cultures. The cultures were incubated at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $3 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ in the dark. After the incubation, the cells were washed thrice with PBS buffer. Finally, the cells were suspended in PBS buffer and diluted $1 : 1 0$ in PBS buffer, and subsequently visualized under a confocal microscope. The cells were also used for Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) analysis. The FACS analysis for the stained cells was performed on a BD FACS Aria Fusion machine. The analysis was performed with side scatter (SSC) and LB fluorescence using FITC $( 5 3 0 \pm 1 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ excited at $4 8 8 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ ) filters with similar settings for all the samples. 

# Yeast survival and growth assay

Yeast survival and growth assay was performed to test the effect of soybean MAGL on arsenic, mannitol, and salinity (NaCl) stress. Yeast cells with four GmMAGL genes cloned in pYES2.1 vector under galactose inducible promoter were spotted on YEP media (SD-URA media with $2 \%$ (w/v) galactose) containing $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ 

arsenite, $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ and 300 mM mannitol, 200 mM and 300 mM NaCl.
