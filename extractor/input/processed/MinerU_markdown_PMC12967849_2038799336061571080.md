# SUMMARY

Fruit pigmentation mainly controlled by chlorophyll, carotenoids, and flavonoids is an important phenomenon for fortifying the nutritional and esthetic value of humans. Our previous study identified Pseudo-Response Regulator 2 (PRR2) as a regulator of fruit pigmentation through its association with the c1 locus. A single variation of glk2 or prr2 did not affect the pigmentation of immature fruit in Capsicum accessions, while accessions harboring both glk2/prr2 showed pale-green immature fruits. The immature fruit color in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from a cross between “Milyang” (GLK2/PRR2, green fruit) and “Goshiki” (glk2/prr2, pale-green fruit) was highly associated with GLK2 and PRR2. Dual variation in GLK2 and PRR2 caused by natural variation and gene silencing resulted in altered pigmentation in both immature and ripe fruits of “Numex halloween” and “Habanero orange.” Furthermore, co-silencing of GLK2 and PRR2 in “Royal black” (GLK2/PRR2) resulted in pale-green immature fruits. Reduced pigmentation was associated with a decrease in plastid size and thylakoid number, and the expression of chlorophyll and carotenoid biosynthesis genes was downregulated in the dual-variated fruits. Quadruple variation by co-silencing of GLK2 and CCS in “Numex halloween” (prr2/psy) resulted in white ripe fruits. Additionally, GLK2 was shown to physically interact with PRR2 in yeast two-hybrid and bimolecular fluorescence complementation assays. Taken together, the combined effects of GLK2 and PRR2 determine c1 and fruit pigmentation, and our findings provide a genetic basis for improving fruit quality traits. 

Keywords: Golden2-like 2, Pseudo-Response Regulator 2, fruit pigmentation, pepper, virus-induced gene silencing. 

# INTRODUCTION

Fleshy fruits are crucial for human nutrition and attract frugivores for seed dispersal, with the shift from unripe to ripe fruit representing a key survival strategy for plants (Karlova et al., 2014). Fruit ripening is a highly coordinated process involving physiological and biochemical changes including color, flavor, taste and firmness (Klee & Giovannoni, 2011). Fruit pigmentation is an important trait for both consumers and breeders as it affects visual appeal and nutritional value (Yuan et al., 2015). Elucidating the genetic regulation of pigment accumulation is essential for improving fruit quality (Taylor & Ramsay, 2005). Carotenoid is one of the major factors controlling fruit pigmentation and functions as precursors for phytohormones and volatile compounds (Song et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2022). In addition to pigment biosynthesis, its sequestration and stable storage in plastids are major factors for enhancing nutritional value (Li et al., 2016). 

Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) has been widely studied as a model for fleshy fruits (Giovannoni et al., 2017), thereby, comparative genomics based on tomato has been exploited for expediting gene mining to improve many fruit traits in pepper (Paran & van der Knaap, 2007). Among the orthologous regulatory genes involved in fruit ripening differentiation, Colorless non-ripening (SPL-CNR), Homeobox (HB-1), and Uniform (Golden2-like 2, GLK2) showed distinct expression patterns between tomato and pepper (Kim et al., 2014). SPL-CNR encodes a SQUAMOSA promoter binding protein-like transcription factor, and its epi-mutant exhibited a pale-yellow pericarp with reduced ethylene production (Do et al., 2024; Manning et al., 2006). HB-1 was found to be involved in fruit ripening and floral organogenesis (Lin et al., 2008). GLK2 acts as a positive regulator of plastid number and pigment accumulation in Arabidopsis, tomato, and pepper 

(Brand et al., 2014; Fitter et al., 2002; Powell et al., 2012). Gene mining and comparative genomics in Capsicum have revealed species-specific regulatory patterns in gene expression, focusing on processes such as carotenoid biosynthesis, metabolite accumulation, and key developmental dynamics during fruit ripening (Kim et al., 2014; Osorio et al., 2012). 

Overexpression of the Arabidopsis Pseudo-Response Regulator 2-like (APRR2-like) increased plastid development and enhanced pigment levels in tomato fruit (Pan et al., 2013). Overexpression of GLK2 increased the accumulation of transcripts related to the pigment biosynthesis process and plastid organization, resulting in higher levels of pigments, starch, and sugars (Nguyen et al., 2014). The dominant gain-of-function Curl mutant of tomato knotted 2 (TKN2) increased chloroplast numbers and chlorophyll levels in tomato fruit by upregulating both SlGLK2 and SlAPRR2-like (Nadakuduti et al., 2014). Reduced expression of the zinc-finger homeodomain protein, SlZHD17, resulted in larger chloroplasts and higher chlorophyll levels, which were regulated by increased expression of the chlorophyll biosynthesis gene, SlPORB, and the chloroplast developmental regulator, SlTKN2, respectively (Shi et al., 2021). Overexpression of the UV-B photoreceptor 8, SlUVR8, promoted plastid development and pigment accumulation through enhancing GLK2 expression (Li et al., 2018). TOMATO AGAMOUS-LIKE 1 (TAGL1) influences the distribution of chloroplasts, and knockout of TAGL1 resulted in dark green fruit with increased pigment levels (Jeon et al., 2024; Vrebalov et al., 2009). Knockout of the GRAS transcription factor, SlGRAS9, showed dark green fruits with enhanced chloroplast development and chlorophyll accumulation (Shi et al., 2024). 

Bulked segregant analysis and genome-wide association studies identified protein phosphatase 2C, CaPP2C35 as a candidate gene for the LG locus, controlling light-green immature pepper fruit and chlorophyll accumulation in the exocarp (Wu et al., 2022). Fine mapping using genotypingby-sequencing in C. chinense germplasm revealed that inactive shikimate kinase-like 2 was a candidate gene regulating immature fruit color (Shu et al., 2023). A zinc finger transcription factor, pepper B-box 10, activated the expression of magnesium chelatase subunit D and phytoene synthase 1 (PSY1), thereby promoting both chlorophyll and carotenoid accumulation (Wang, Shan, et al., 2025). A splicing mutation in zeaxanthin epoxidase (ZEP ) reduced the conversion rate of zeaxanthin to antheraxanthin or violaxanthin, contributing to orange fruit color (Lee et al., 2021). Silencing of ZEP in red pepper resulted in bright red fruit color due to reduced capsanthin and increased zeaxanthin (Lee et al., 2024). An R-R-type MYB transcription factor, DIVARIVATA1, directly regulated PSY, capsanthin-capsorubin synthase (CCS ), and $\beta$ -carotene hydroxylase, positively controlling capsanthin accumulation in pepper (Song 

et al., 2023). MADS-box transcription factor RIPENING INHIBITOR (MADS-RIN) positively regulates carotenoid biosynthesis by targeting PSY and CCS, and also enhances their expression via interaction with DIVARICATA1, forming a protein complex (Wang et al., 2025). 

Two major QTLs for pigment content, pc10 and pc1, which modulate plastid development, were identified as GLK2 and LSD one like 1 (LOL1), respectively (Borovsky et al., 2019; Brand et al., 2012; Brand et al., 2014). Null alleles at CaGLK2 were associated with chlorophyll reduction, and GLK2 expression was positively correlated with chlorophyll levels (Brand et al., 2014). Extreme reductions of chlorophyll content in C. chinense germplasm were induced by triple mutations in CaGLK2, CcLOL1, and CcPRR2 (Borovsky et al., 2019). The ripe fruit colors were determined by three loci (c1, c2, and y) (Hurtado-Hernandez & Smith, 1985; Thorup et al., 2000), and PSY (Huh et al., 2001) and CCS (Lefebvre et al., 1998; Popovsky & Paran, 2000) are determinants of c2 and y, respectively. The c1 co-segregated with Pseudo-Response Regulator 2 (PRR2), which regulates the fruit color in both immature and ripe stages (Jeong et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2020). A nonsense mutation in APRR2-like in pepper led to the white immature fruit color (Pan et al., 2013). PRR2 was identified as positive regulator of pigment accumulation by controlling plastid development and pigment biosynthesis (Lee et al., 2020). Silencing of PRR2 in yellow pepper reduced carotenoid content in the ripe fruit (Jeong et al., 2020). 

We previously identified a PRR2 as a candidate of the c1 locus; however, we found that the orange fruit color in “Numex halloween” (psy1/CCS ) was not altered by PRR2 silencing. In this study, we identified the interplay between GLK2 and PRR2 in controlling fruit pigmentation and determined the c1 locus in pepper.

# GLK2 interacts with PRR2

Physical interaction between GLK2 and PRR2 was analyzed using a yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay (Figure 7A). The combinations of constructs grew on the selective medium (SD-LWA, SD-LWH + 3AT). Yeast cells of the positive control (p53_BD/T_AD) could grow under the selective medium, whereas the negative control (BD/AD, GLK2_BD/AD, BD/PRR2_AD) did not survive on the same medium. The cotransformation of yeast cells with both GLK2_BD and PRR2_AD showed that these yeast cells were able to grow on the selective medium. All the pale-green fruit accessions were expected to have a deletion in the GCT box of GLK2 and PRR2 (Figure S2; Table 1). To determine whether the GCT box is required for the interaction, we performed Y2H assay with truncated forms of GLK2 $( \mathsf { G L K } 2 _ { \Delta \mathsf { G C T } } )$ and PRR2 $( \mathsf { P R R } 2 _ { \Delta \mathsf { G C T } } )$ . Co-transformation of $\mathsf { P R R 2 } _ { \Delta \mathsf { G C T } }$ with GLK2_BD 

or $\mathtt { G L K 2 } _ { \Delta \mathsf { G C T } }$ abolished the interaction, whereas $\mathsf { G L K 2 } _ { \Delta \mathsf { G C T } }$ hardly interacted with PRR2_AD. Next, we further verified the interaction by a bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) (Figure 7B). GLK2 fused to the N-terminus of yellow fluorescence protein (YFP) (GLK2_nYFP) and PRR2 fused to the C-terminus of YFP (PRR2_cYFP) were co-transfected to protoplasts. Additionally, empty cYFP or nYFP vectors were co-transferred with GLK2_nYFP or PRR2_cYFP for use as a mock control. The YFP signal was observed in the nucleus upon GLK2_nYFP and PRR2_nYFP being co-expressed. The YFP signal was not observed in cells co-expressing either GLK2_nYFP with cYFP or PRR2_cYFP with nYFP. These results indicate that GLK2 interacts with PRR2 presumably through the GCT box.

Knockout of BEL1-LIKE HOMEODOMAIN 2 (BEL2) in tomato increased the formation of green shoulder by altering the gradient distribution of chlorophyll accumulation (Niu et al., 2022). Enhancing the ultrastructure and biosynthetic capacity of plastid is crucial for increasing pigment accumulation (Sun et al., 2018). Dark-green fruit in GLK2-overexpressing tomato is due to increased storage capacity, attributed to an elevated number of chloroplasts and thylakoid grana stacks (Nguyen et al., 2014). Overexpression of APRR2-like in tomato increased plastid size, indicating enhanced plastid proliferation and enlargement (Pan et al., 2013). Increased chloroplast development in immature fruits leads to ripe fruits with more active chromoplasts, resulting in higher levels of metabolites related to nutritional quality (Cocaliadis et al., 2014). ORANGE, a DnaJ-like zinc finger domain-containing protein, is a multifunctional regulator of plastid development and carotenoid biosynthesis that is critical for storage capacity (Sun et al., 2022). The dual variation induced by silencing GLK2 in NH (Figure 3B, C) and PRR2 in HO (Figure 4B,C) reduced pigment levels, which is likely a consequence of a decreased plastid size and thylakoid number. 

The chlorophyll biosynthetic genes including HEMA, CRD, and PORB were downregulated in the dual-variated fruits (Figure 3E; Figure 4E) as well as GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB (Figure 5C). The expression of CAO was significantly reduced in GLK2-silenced NH (Figure 3E) and GLK2-PRR2- silenced RB (Figure 5C), while it was not altered in PRR2- 

silenced HO (Figure 4E). Overexpression of CAO increased chlorophyll b content and decreased the chlorophyll a/b ratio in tobacco (Biswal et al., 2012). Thus, the decreased chlorophyll a/b ratio observed in PRR2-silenced HO (Figure 4D) may be a response to maintain CAO expression. Interestingly, the GLK2-silenced RB exhibited a lightgreen color and reduced level of chlorophylls, whereas the PRR2 silencing did not alter pigmentation (Figure 5A,B). The GLK2-silenced RB resulted only in a significant reduction of PORB expression (Figure 5C). PORB is essential for the conversion of protochlorophyllide to chlorophyllide, ensuring efficient chlorophyll production, and mutation of CqPORB in quinoa leads to reduced chlorophyll and defective thylakoid membranes (Li et al., 2022). Among the chlorophyll biosynthesis genes, only the expression of POR was increased in GLK2-overexpressing tomato fruits (Nguyen et al., 2014). The light-green fruit color observed in GLK2-silenced RB is likely attributable to the downregulation of PORB. 

The expression of PSY1 was consistently decreased in dual-variated fruits of NH (Figure 3E) and HO (Figure 4E), as well as in GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB (Figure 5D). The expression of CCS was increased in GLK2-silenced NH, decreased in PRR2-silenced HO, and showed no significant difference in GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB. PSY1 expression was positively correlated with capsanthin content, while CCS expression showed no correlation with color intensity in pepper fruits (Berry et al., 2019). Reduced level of capsanthin in GLK2-silenced NH (Figure 3D) and PRR2-silenced HO (Figure 4D) is likely due to the decreased expression of PSY1. Zeaxanthin and $\beta$ -carotene were significantly reduced in the GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB, while capsanthin showed a slight decrease (Figure 5B). The expression of PSY1, ZISO, CHY2, and ZEP was downregulated in the GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB (Figure 5D). These results suggest that reduced carotenoid level in the GLK2-PRR2-silenced RB is primarily due to the downregulation of PSY1, which plays a more dominant role in the fruit carotenogenesis compared to the downstream genes, CHY2 and ZEP. 

Flavor is a key determinant of fruit quality and influences consumer preference. Different compositions of flavor volatiles have been identified during pepper fruit ripening (Forero et al., 2009); however, regulatory genes related to flavor have been less studied. Here, we identified that GLK2 and PRR2 can regulate volatile flavor volatiles in pepper fruits (Table 2). A positive correlation between sour taste and four volatile compounds (e.g., dill ether, pmenth-1-en-9-al, a-terpineol, and linalool) was identified in tomato fruit (D’Angelo et al., 2018). In sweet pepper, pmenth-1-en-9-al showed a positive correlation with fruit/apple taste and sweetness (Eggink et al., 2012). Levels of dill ether and p-menth-1-en-9-al were decreased in GLK2-silenced NH and PRR2-silenced HO, suggesting that GLK2 and PRR2 can influence sensory-related flavors. 

Methyl salicylate, which decreases during fruit ripening in tomato (Wang et al., 2020) and pepper (Huang et al., 2023), was increased in GLK2-silenced NH and PRR2-silenced HO, which negatively affects consumer preference (Frick et al., 2023). trans-2-Heptenal, a volatile compound favored by consumers, increased 2.5-fold during fruit ripening process in tomato (Klee & Giovannoni, 2011; Klee & Tieman, 2013). The level of trans-2-heptenal was significantly increased in PRR2-silenced HO and was only detected in GLK2-silenced NH. In pepper fruits, trans-2-hexenal, a marker volatile for sweet and fruity aroma, increases during fruit ripening (Mazida et al., 2005). The level of trans-2- hexenal was reduced in GLK2-silenced NH, which is a consumer-like volatile in tomato (Klee & Tieman, 2018; Tieman et al., 2017). Hexyl isopentanoate is one of significant volatile in habanero varieties, which increases during fruit ripening (Pino et al., 2006). The level of hexyl isopentanoate was only detected in HO, and significantly recued in PRR2-silenced HO. GLK2 and PRR2 do not affect general ripening processes, since regulation of ripening-dependent flavors was not highly coordinated in the dual-variated fruits. It seems that their influence on fruit development may be employed through the altered plastid activity and plastid numbers, including the accumulation of carotenoids. 

GLK2 physically interacts with PRR2 in Y2H (Figure 7A) and BiFC assays (Figure 7B). GLK2 (Ni et al., 2017; Rauf et al., 2013) and PRR2 (Perez et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2024) were localized in the nucleus. GLK2 and PRR2 contained three conserved domains: a DNA-binding (helix–loop–helix) domain, a proline-rich region (transactivation domain), and the GCT box (dimerization domain) (Rossini et al., 2001). Additionally, GLKs uniquely contain the AREAEAA motif, which lacks in PRRs (Choi et al., 2024; Fitter et al., 2002). All the variations in GLK2 and PRR2 led to the expected deletion of the GCT box in Capsicum accessions (Figure S2; Table 1). Indeed, the lack of the GCT box in GLK2 and PRR2 abolished their interaction, and their interaction in the nucleus was verified by the BiFC assay (Figure 7). The GCT box plays a crucial role in protein dimerization in GLKs (An et al., 2019; Tachibana et al., 2024). The negative regulators of chloroplast development, PSEUDO-ETIOLATION IN LIGHT (PEL), interact with the C-terminus of GLK1 and the zinc-finger domain of the chloroplast-localized chaperone PHOTOSYSTEM I ASSEMBLY 2 (PSA2), thereby sequestering both proteins in the cytoplasm and ultimately suppressing chloroplast development and pigment accumulation in rice (Choi et al., 2025). Repressor of photosynthetic genes 1 (RPGE1/ PEL) interacts with the GCT box of GLK1 and inhibits its transcriptional activity by disrupting its dimerization (Kim et al., 2023). In Arabidopsis, PRR2 interacted with the calmodulin-like protein CML9 through its GCT box, suggesting that PRR2 may form a homo- or hetero-complex 

(Perochon et al., 2010). The green fruit accession NH was predicted to have the deletion of the GCT box in PRR2; however, the interaction between GLK2 and PRR2DGCT was abolished. GLKs are recognized as master regulators of chloroplast biogenesis, enhancing the expression of photosynthesis-associated nuclear genes across various plant species (Hernandez-Verdeja & Lundgren,  2024). GLK2 coordinates the synthesis of chlorophyll, the development of photosystems, flowering regulation, and the signaling pathways of abscisic acid, interacting with various proteins and target genes involved in these processes (Choi et al., 2024). Even in the absence of protein interaction with PRR2, it is possible that GLK2 interacts with other proteins to regulate fruit pigmentation in NH. The interaction of nuclear factor Y, subunit A genes (NF-YA9a) with GLK2.1 activates HEMA2 expression and increases the chlorophyll biosynthesis in radish (Ying et al., 2023). GLKs transactivated PSY expression by interacting with GBFs, which bind to the promoter of PSY, and promoted carotenoid biosynthesis in Arabidopsis (Sun, Hazra, et al., 2025). The repressor of photosynthetic genes 1 (RPGE1/PEL) interacts with APRR2 in carrot and suppresses carotenoid biosynthesis by inhibiting the binding activity of APRR2 to the promoter regions of carotenoid biosynthesis genes, such as DcPSY1, DcPSY2, and lycopene e-cyclase (DcLCYE ) (Wang et al., 2024). Additional factors that interplay with GLK2 or PRR2 could provide valuable insights for dissecting complex regulatory mechanisms of fruit pigmentation. The $\mathtt { G L K 2 } _ { \Delta \mathtt { G C T } }$ hardly interacted with PRR2, and immature fruits harboring the glk2 variations resulted in a light green color. The contribution of CcAPRR2 in chlorophyll levels is less consistent than CaGLK2 (Borovsky et al., 2019). CaGLK2 is a predominant regulator of immature fruit color, while CaAPRR2 functioned as a secondary factor regulating chlorophyll synthesis (Sun, Zhang, et al., 2025). Considering only the PRR2 genotype in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population, the PRR2 genotype was weakly correlated with immature fruit color compared to the GLK2 genotype (Figure S3b). GLK2 and PRR2 jointly act and are necessary for fruit pigmentation; however, the light-green phenotype of glk2 suggests that GLK2 plays a major role. Silencing of GLK2 in RB did not reduce PRR2 expression (Figure 5C,D). Similarly, the silencing of PRR2 in RB did not reduce GLK2 expression. These results suggest that each gene may act in a complementary manner to regulate fruit pigmentation through alterations of plastid activity and gene expression. 

In summary, we identified the combined effects of GLK2 and PRR2 associated with immature and ripe fruit colors in pepper. Both GLK2 and PRR2 are determinants of c1 and positively control plastid development and pigment biosynthesis. These findings provide new insights into fruit pigmentation and will be useful for the genetic improvement of fruit quality. 

# EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES

# Plant materials

Capsicum accessions were obtained from the National Agro biodiversity Center, Rural Development Administration, Korea (Table 1; Table S1). All plants were grown under greenhouse conditions at Kyungpook National University, Daegu, Korea. Pericarps from immature (30 days post-anthesis) and ripe fruits (10 days after breaker) were collected, immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $\scriptscriptstyle - 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ before analysis. 

# Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS)

VIGS was performed according to a previously described method (Kim, Park, et al., 2017). For plasmid construction, fragments of the target gene were amplified from pepper leaf cDNA with primers (Table S6). Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 carrying pTRV1 and pTRV2::gene of interest was grown overnight at $\mathsf { 2 8 ^ { \circ } C }$ in 10 ml LB medium containing rifampicin $( 5 0 ~ \mathrm { \mu \ g / m l } )$ and kanamycin $( 5 0 ~ \mathrm { \mu g / m l } )$ . Agrobacterium cultures were harvested and resuspended to an $O D _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.7. Cell suspensions were incubated at room temperature with inversion for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Agrobacterium cultures containing pTRV1 and pTRV2 carrying the gene of interest were mixed at a 1:1 ratio and infiltrated into the abaxial side of both cotyledons. The infiltrated plants were incubated at $\textstyle 1 6 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in the dark for 1 day and then grown under a 20-h day/4-h night cycle at $\scriptstyle 2 0 ^ { \circ } 0$ until harvest. 

# Sequencing of candidate genes

Genomic DNA was extracted using the CTAB method (Murray & Thompson, 1980) from young leaves. PCRs were performed with e-Taq DNA polymerase (SolGent Co., Ltd., Daejeon, Korea) containing 50 ng of genomic DNA and gene-specific primers as previously described (Lee et al., 2020), and listed in Table S6. The PCR program was $\mathfrak { g } 5 \mathrm { \textdegree C }$ for 2 min, followed by 35 cycles of $\mathfrak { s } \mathfrak { s } ^ { \circ } \mathfrak { C }$ for 30 sec, annealing temperatures for 30 sec, and ${ 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C }$ for 1 min. The PCR amplicons were purified with the HiGeneTM Gel & PCR Purification System (BIOFACT Co., Ltd., Daejeon, Korea) and sequenced (SolGent Co., Ltd.). 

# Segregation analysis of GLK2 and PRR2 in the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population

A total of $2 1 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals derived from a cross between C. annuum “Milyang” and C. annuum “Goshiki” was developed. The green immature fruit color in “Milyang” was evaluated as class 1, and the pale green in “Goshiki” was evaluated as class 4. The color of immature fruit in $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals was classified into four classes based on visual observation of green intensity and independently evaluated by three persons. The dCAPS marker for GLK2 (CA10g02900, chr 10) was designed based on the cytosine (C) to thymine (T) substitution in the third exon, with the following sequence: ${ \sf 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -GCTTTATCTAATTAAACCTAGAAAG-30 (GLK2 forward) and ${ \sf 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -CAGCTTGCTGCCTCAGCTTCCCTTGCTAATAAATGTT ATC-30 (GLK2 reverse). PCR was conducted with an initial denaturation at $\mathfrak { g } _ { 5 } \circ _ { \mathsf { C } }$ for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles of $\mathfrak { g } _ { 5 ^ { \circ } \complement }$ for 30 sec, annealing at ${ \mathfrak { E } } 0 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } }$ for 30 sec, and extension at $7 2 \%$ for 1 min. The CAPS marker for PRR2 (CA00g25180, chr 1) was designed based on the adenine (A) to guanine (G) substitution in the first exon, with the following sequence: ${ \sf 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -GGATGTGCAGATTCAATTGGTA-${ \mathfrak { z } } ^ { \prime }$ (PRR2 forward) and 50 - CCTAGTCACATTGCAGCAACT- ${ \mathfrak { 3 } } ^ { \prime }$ (PRR2 reverse). PCR conditions were $\mathfrak { g } _ { 5 ^ { \circ } \complement }$ for 3 min, followed by 35 cycles of $\mathfrak { g } 5 \mathrm { \textdegree C }$ for 30 sec, $5 5 ^ { \circ } \complement$ for 30 sec, and $\scriptscriptstyle { 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } }$ for 1 min. 

The PCR amplicons from the GLK2 primers were digested with ClaI (Enzynomics, Daejeon, Korea), and PRR2 primers were digested with AluI (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) for 12 h to determine polymorphisms. 

# Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

Pericarp tissues from immature and ripe fruits were analyzed following the previously described method (Lee et al., 2020). Polymerized blocks were sectioned at a thickness of $7 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ using a Leica Ultracut UCT ultramicrotome, and ultrathin sections were examined with a transmission electron microscope (Tecnai G2 Spirit Twin; FEI Company, Hillsboro, OR, USA). The chloroplast area and the number of thylakoid per granum were analyzed using ImageJ (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA; http://imagej.nih.gov/ij/). 

# Pigment extraction and high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis

Approximately $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m g }$ of frozen and ground powder from pericarps was used for pigment extraction. Extraction and quantification of chlorophylls and carotenoids were performed as previously described (Yoo et al., 2017). For saponification, $20 \%$ KOH was added to the extracts and incubated for 1 h at room temperature. Carotenoid analysis was conducted using HPLC with C30 YMC Carotenoid S-5 $( 4 . 6 \times 2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } )$ and Agilent ChemStation software (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA) (Kim, Yoo, et al., 2017). The concentrations of carotenoid compounds were determined using calibration curves generated from $\beta$ -carotene (Cat. No. C4582; Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) and capsanthin (Cat. No. 19081; Sigma-Aldrich). 

# Gene expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted from pericarps using RibospinTM Seed/Fruit (GeneAll Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Seoul, Korea) and reverse transcribed to synthesize cDNA using a DiaStarTM RT Kit (SolGent Co., Ltd.). Quantitative RT-PCR was performed using Power SYBR Green PCR master mix (Applied BiosystemsTM, Foster City, CA, USA) containing 100 ng of cDNA. qRT-PCR was conducted with an internal denaturation at $\mathfrak { g } _ { 5 } \circ _ { \mathsf { C } }$ for 10 min, followed by 40 cycles of $\mathfrak { g } 5 \mathrm { \textdegree C }$ for 15 sec and $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 min on a CFX Connect Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). At least three biological replicates and two technical replicates were performed per primer, and CaUBIQUITIN (DQ975458.1) was used for normalization. The primers used for qRT-PCR were previously described (Lee et al., 2020) and listed in Table S6. 

# Volatile analysis using SPME-GC–MS

Volatile analysis by SPME-GC–MS was performed according to Yoo et al. (2019) and Yoo et al. (2023) with modifications. Approximately 100 mg of frozen and ground powder from pericarps was introduced into a $1 0 \ m |$ headspace glass vial and incubated in a water bath at $\mathfrak { 3 7 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 10 min. Subsequently, $5 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of EDTA (pH 7.5) and $\boldsymbol { 1 . 1 \ g }$ of calcium chloride dihydrate $( C a C l _ { 2 } . 2 H _ { 2 } O )$ were added and sonicated for 5 min. All samples were analyzed within $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ using a gas chromatograph-mass spectrometer (7890B-5977B GC/MSD; Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA) at the Kyungpook National University Instrumental Analysis Center (Daegu, Korea). Sample-wise peaks were identified and matched to the library using the MSD ChemStation Data Analysis software (Agilent). The experimental MS spectra of the volatiles were tentatively compared with the mass spectral library of the Wiley Registry (11th Edition/NIST 2017) to identify the volatiles. 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

The full-length cDNA of GLK2 was inserted into the pGBKT7 vector, and the full-length cDNA of PRR2 was inserted into the pGADT7 vector (Clontech, Mountain View, CA, USA) using specific primers (Table S6). The truncated sequence of GLK2 (1–241 a.a.) and PRR2 (1–477 a.a.) were inserted into pGBKT7 and pGADT7 vectors using specific primers (Table S6). The recombinant constructs were transformed into yeast strain AH109 using lithium chloride-polyethylene glycol (PEG) as described. The transformed yeast cells were grown for 3 days at $\mathfrak { 3 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ on synthetic defined (SD) medium lacking leucine and tryptophan (SD-LW) and then replicated on to SD medium lacking leucine, tryptophan, and histidine (SD-LWH) containing 5 mM 3-amino-1,2,3-trizole (3-AT). The co-transformation of pGBKT7 and pGADT7 was used as a negative control, while that of pGBKT7-p53 (murine p53) and pGADT7-T (T-antigen) was used as positive control. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation assay

The full-length of GLK2 sequence was cloned into pGA3816 vector containing the N-terminal of Venus (1–172 a.a.) to generate GLK2_nYFP, whereas PRR2 sequence was cloned into pGA3815 vector containing the C-terminal of Venus (155–238 a.a.) to generate PRR2_cYFP. The BiFC was conducted by transforming protoplasts isolated from rice root-derived callus suspension cells as previously described (Cho et al., 2024). Fluorescence was observed using a confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM780; Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) under a bright field or green fluorescent protein (GFP) channel. Primers used for BiFC were listed in Table S6.
