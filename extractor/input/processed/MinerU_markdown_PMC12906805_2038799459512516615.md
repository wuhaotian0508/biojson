# ABSTRACT

Cuticular waxes can form a hydrophobic barrier on aerial plant surfaces, which is essential for mediating plant-environment interactions by providing protection against both biotic and abiotic stresses. In this study, a cuticular wax mutant (gl) was identified and characterised in Welsh onion (Allium fistulosum L.), exhibiting a functional deficiency in AfCER2-LIKE1. Loss of AfCER2-LIKE1 function was attributed to a retrotransposon insertion in the promoter region and a frameshift mutation in the coding sequence. In the mutant gl, the loss of function of AfCER2-LIKE1 (AfCER2- LIKE1gl) leads to a significant reduction in wax components above C28. Further analysis of yeast heterologous expression showed that AfCER2-LIKE1gl is unable to form a complex with AfKCS11 (3-ketocyl-CoA, KCS), regulating the elongation of very-long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs) from C28 to C30. Furthermore, the mutant gl showed enhanced resistance to thrips infestation, which served as an observation validated by both field trials and controlled laboratory experiments. Collectively, these findings elucidate the role of CER2-LIKE1 in regulating VLCFA biosynthesis in Welsh onion and provide insight into the association between cuticular wax and insect resistance. These results offer a theoretical basis for the development of thrips-resistant germplasm through molecular breeding strategies and suggest potential applications for crop improvement. 

# 1 | Introduction

Cuticular wax is a natural lipid-based substance secreted by plant epidermal cells, primarily composed of long-chain fatty 

acids, alcohols, esters, aldehydes, and hydrocarbons (Lee and Suh  2022). This translucent layer functions as a critical barrier, offering multiple protective roles, including prevention of non-stomatal water loss, attenuation of UV radiation, 

defence against microbial pathogens, and reduction of surface wettability (Lewandowska et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2020). The chemical composition of cuticular wax displays substantial interspecific and intraspecific variation, which can be modulated by genetic factors, developmental stages and environmental conditions (Jenks et  al.  1996). Major constituents include very-long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs; C20–C34), primary and secondary alcohols, esters, aldehydes, and alkanes. These compounds are synthesised in epidermal cells through elongation-decarbonylation pathways and are subsequently exported to form the epicuticular crystalline layer (Yeats and Rose  2013). The ultrastructure of wax deposits, including layered, tubular and granular forms, defines critical surface characteristics, such as light reflectance, contact angle hysteresis and anti-adhesion performance (Arand et al. 2021). Such microstructural diversity plays a direct role in modulating plant-environment interactions, thereby shaping ecological adaptation and stress resilience. 

Cuticular wax forms an essential physical and biochemical interface between plants and their environment, playing a pivotal role in mediating plant-pathogen and plant-insect interactions (Lewandowska et  al.  2020). As the primary barrier to microbial invasion, the dense wax layer impedes pathogen contact with epidermal cells, whereas antimicrobial components, such as fatty acids and aldehydes, actively suppress microbial growth (Casillo et al. 2017; Obukhova and Murzina  2024). This dual protective function is exemplified by the infection dynamics of Botrytis cinerea, which degrades the cuticular wax of blueberry while concurrently inhibiting triterpenoid accumulation and wax biosynthesis gene expression (Jiang et  al.  2022). In contrast, enhanced epicuticular wax deposition in strawberry mutants confers significant resistance to B. cinerea (Jiang et al. 2024), underscoring the cuticular layer's importance in pathogen defence. In the context of plant-insect interactions, cuticular wax has complex and sometimes paradoxical roles. The microstructure of wax crystals (e.g., lamellar or tubular) can increase surface roughness, thereby reducing insect attachment and feeding efficiency (Negin et  al.  2021). Specific wax components, especially alkanes and aldehydes, function as natural insect deterrents by repelling or inhibiting herbivorous insects (Su et al. 2024). However, paradoxical phenomena have also been observed. For instance, maize mutants deficient in wax biosynthesis (Zmgl8) unexpectedly exhibit enhanced insect resistance, potentially due to the activation of JA-mediated defence pathways (Liu et al. 2023). These findings indicate that insect resistance involves both physical barrier effects and complex signalling crosstalk, warranting further investigation to elucidate the underlying molecular mechanisms. 

Thrips (Thrips tabaci) are economically significant pests that not only cause direct damage to plants but also serve as vectors for viral pathogens, thereby severely affecting normal plant growth and development. Notably, a wax-deficient phenotype has been associated with enhanced resistance to thrips in several Allium species (Damon et al. 2014). Additionally, surface glossiness, as a key visual trait enhancing consumer appeal, exhibits a strong negative correlation with wax deposition (Hao et al. 2024). This inverse relationship has been widely documented across plant 

species, with wax-deficient mutants consistently exhibiting increased surface reflectance. In tomato, the transcription factor Woolly regulates fruit glossiness by modulating wax biosynthesis (Xiong et al. 2020), whereas low-wax navel orange varieties display significantly enhanced surface shine (Liu et  al.  2016). These findings underscore the importance of elucidating wax biosynthesis pathways to improve Welsh onion quality and marketability (Aoyama and Yamamoto  2007; Fredotović and Puizina 2018). Nevertheless, the molecular basis of cuticular wax synthesis in Welsh onion remains largely unexplored. 

In this study, CER2-LIKE1 was identified as the gene responsible for the glossy mutant phenotype (gl). Using molecular biology techniques, the roles of AfCER2-LIKE1 and AfKCS11 (a KCS6 homologue) in fatty acid elongation during wax biosynthesis were elucidated, revealing their participation in complex formation. Moreover, loss-of-function mutations in AfCER2- LIKE1 significantly reduced Welsh onion susceptibility to thrips infestation, thereby providing novel insights into plant–insect resistance mechanisms. 

# 2 | Material and Methods

# 2.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

The experimental materials included wild-type (WT) Welsh onion (Allium fistulosum) and a naturally occurring waxdeficient mutant (gl), originally collected from Shanxi Province, China. Both WT and gl plants were initially cultivated in a greenhouse for 30 days prior to transplantation. Subsequently, plants were grown under controlled environmental conditions with daytime and nighttime temperatures of $2 8 ^ { \circ } C \pm 3 ^ { \circ } C$ and $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \pm 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , respectively, and a $1 4 \mathrm { h }$ light/10 h dark photoperiod (Figure S1). 

Wild-type Arabidopsis thaliana (Col-0) seeds maintained by our research group were surface-sterilised with $7 0 \%$ ethanol following vernalization and sown on Murashige and Skoog (MS) agar medium. The seedlings were cultured in a growth chamber at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. After 1 week, uniform seedlings were transferred to soils substrate for continued growth. 

The T. tabaci larvae used in this study were collected in September 2023 from the Yanqing Experimental Station of the Beijing Academy of Agriculture and Forestry Sciences. The insects were maintained in a controlled rearing environment and continuously propagated using lentils (Lens culinaris) as the primary food source. 

# 2.2 | Cuticular Wax Analysis

The composition and content of cuticular waxes in Welsh onion and A. thaliana were analysed following the method described by Xing et al. (2024). The wax composition was determined by comparing mass spectrometry data for each component with internal standards and previously published references. The waxy component content was quantified using the area normalisation 

method. Three biological replicates were prepared for each material, with each replicate consisting of five leaves. 

# 2.3 | Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)

Fresh leaves or stems of Welsh onion and A. thaliana were harvested and rinsed with water to remove surface debris. Samples were cut into $5 \mathrm { m m } \times 5 \mathrm { m m }$ sections, fixed onto stubs with conductive tape, and sputter-coated with gold for 15 s. Imaging was performed using a Hitachi $\mathrm { S } { - } 3 4 0 0 \mathrm { N }$ scanning electron microscope (Hitachi, Japan). 

# 2.4 | Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM)

Tissue blocks approximately $1 . 5 \mathrm { c m } \times 1 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ in size were selected from the upper third of WT and $_ { g l }$ leaves. Fixation was performed using a solution of $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) glutaraldehyde and $2 \%$ (v/v) formaldehyde. The procedure followed the method described by Huang et al. (2023), and observations were conducted using a transmission electron microscope (JEM-1230, Japan). 

# 2.5 | RNA Extraction and Real-Time qPCR (RT-qPCR)

Leaves from Welsh onion and A. thaliana were collected and stored at $- 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . All samples were ground in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was extracted using a FastPure Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). RNA was reversetranscribed using the HiScript III All-in-one RT SuperMix Perfect for qPCR kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) according to the manufacturer's instructions. qPCR was performed on a LightCycler 384 system (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Mannheim, Germany) using SYBR Green I Master Mix (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). AfTUB and AtActin were employed as internal reference genes for normalisation, and relative gene expression was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). Detailed information on the qPCR primers used is provided in Table S1. 

# 2.6 | InDel Genotyping Through KASP

Genotyping was conducted using KASP to assess the cosegregation of InDel identified via MutMap and identify candidate genes. $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals were genotyped using KASP primers on an LGC High-Throughput Typing Platform. 

# 2.7 | Gene Cloning and Plasmid Construction

Full-length sequences of all genes used in this study were amplified by PCR. For coding sequences (CDS) cloning, cDNA of Welsh onion served as the template. For AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter fragments, the genomic DNA of Welsh onion was used. PCR amplification was performed using $2 \times$ Phanta Max Master Mix (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). The amplified products were purified using the FastPure Gel DNA Extraction Mini Kit 

(Vazyme, Nanjing, China) and cloned into the TA/Blunt Zero Cloning Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). 

For subcellular localisation, the CDS of AfCER2-LIKE1 and AfKCS11 was inserted into the pSuper-1300-GFP vector. 

For luciferase complementation imaging (LCI), the CDS of AfCER2-LIKE1 and AfKCS11 was ligated into pCAMBIA1300- nLUC and pCAMBIA1300-cLUC vectors, respectively. 

For bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC), the CDS of AfCER2-LIKE1 was inserted into pNC-BiFC-Vnn and AfKCS11 into pNC-BiFC-Vnc. 

For yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) analysis, the CDS of AfCER2- LIKE1 and AfKCS11 was ligated into pPR3-N and pBT3 vectors, respectively. 

For transient promoter expression, promoter fragments of AfCER2-LIKE1 from WT and gl were inserted into the pBI101 vector. The 35S promoter was used as a positive control, and the empty vector served as a negative control. 

For overexpression in A. thaliana, AfCER2-LIKE1 CDS was subcloned into the pSuper-1300-GFP vector. 

For heterologous expression in yeast, the full-length CDS of AfCER2-LIKE1 from WT and gl were ligated into the pDR196 vector. AfKCS11 was inserted into the modified pESC-PMA1 vector in which the GAL1 promoter was replaced by a constitutively expressed PMA1 promoter. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into S. cerevisiae strain BY4741. 

All primers used for plasmid construction are listed in Table S1. 

# 2.8 | Subcellular Localization

Recombinant plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 (pSoup 19) (Weidi Biotech, Shanghai, China). Overnight cultures of A. tumefaciens were centrifuged and resuspended in MMA buffer (10 mM MES, 10 mM $\mathbf { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone), followed by incubation for $^ { 2 - 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . The suspension was then infiltrated into Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , fluorescence was observed using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM 700, Germany). 

# 2.9 | LCI and BiFC Assay

The recombinant plasmid was transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 (pSoup 19) (Weidi Biotech, Shanghai, China). 

For the LCI assay, A. tumefaciens strains harbouring nLUC and cLUC constructs were mixed and infiltrated into $N .$ benthamiana leaves. Luciferase activity was detected $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after infiltration using an in  vivo imaging system (VILBER, FUSION-FX 7 EDGE, France) after application of the luciferase substrate. 

For the BiFC assay, A. tumefaciens strains carrying Vnn and Vnc constructs were mixed at a 1:1 ratio and co-infiltrated into $N .$ . benthamiana leaves to generate fusion proteins. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , fluorescence was visualised using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM 700, Germany). 

# 2.10 | Yeast Two-Hybrid (Y2H)

The correctly sequenced recombinant plasmids were transformed into the Saccharomyces cerevisiae strain NMY51 (Weidi Biotech, Shanghai, China) using the PEG/LiAc method. Positive transformants were selected on SD/−Trp/−Leu medium and grown in $0 . 9 \%$ NaCl until $\mathrm { { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ reached 0.2. To validate protein–protein interactions, $1 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the culture was plated onto SD/−Ade/−His/−Leu/−Trp medium. The plates were photographed after 3 days of incubation at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . 

# 2.14 | Statistics on Thrips Damage to Welsh Onion Leaves

The extent of thrips-induced damage to Welsh onion leaves was assessed according to the method described by Wu et al. (2021). Thrips feeding damage was measured on leaves from 40 randomly selected plants (WT or gl) under laboratory conditions. Welsh onion leaves (WT or gl) $( 2 \mathsf { c m } \times 1 \mathsf { c m } )$ were placed on $2 \%$ agar plates, and 15 thrips were introduced into a plastic cup containing agar with a perforated lid to maintain survival. The leaf damage was recorded after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . To remove surface wax, the leaves were gently wiped with medical-grade cotton wool and blu-tack prior to assessment. Thrips damage indices were calculated using the method described by Jiang et  al.  (2005) (Table  S2). Representative images of each damage level are provided in Figure  S2. The damage index was calculated as: 

Damage index (%) = 100×(Σclass value×corresponding leaf number)∕(total number of leaves $\times$ highest level of damage). 

# 2.11 | VLCFA Measurement in Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae)

Yeast cells transformed with empty vectors were used as controls. Positive monoclonal strains were identified using a Yeast Positive Clone Rapid Identification Kit. VLCFAs were extracted and converted to fatty acid methyl esters. Heptadecanoic acid was used as the internal standard, and $1 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of each sample was analysed using Agilent 7890B-5977A gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC–MS) instrument (Agilent, CA, USA). Methyl esterification of yeast fatty acids was performed as described by Batsale et al. (2023). Three biological replicates were prepared, with each replicate derived from a single independent clone. 

# 2.12 | Generation of Transgenic Plants

Recombinant plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 (Weidi Biotech, Shanghai, China). Agrobacterium strains carrying overexpression vectors were introduced into wild-type Arabidopsis using the floral dip method. Positive transgenic plants were screened on kanamycin-containing medium and verified by PCR using gene-specific primers. The transgenic lines were propagated to the $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ generation, and homozygous plants were selected. 

# 2.13 | β-Glucuronidase (GUS) Staining

The recombinant plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 (pSoup 19) (Weidi Biotech, Shanghai, China). Overnight cultures of A. tumefaciens were centrifuged, resuspended in MMA suspension buffer, and infiltrated into N. benthamiana leaves. After $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ , samples were collected. Rosette leaves from $\mathrm { T } _ { 3 }$ transgenic Arabidopsis plants and tobacco leaves were used for GUS staining. The leaves were submerged in GUS staining solution (Solarbio, Beijing, China), incubated overnight at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and subsequently washed with $7 5 \%$ ethanol to remove excess stain (Jin et al. 2025). The results were recorded using photography. 

# 2.15 | Data Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 7 (Mitteer et al. 2018). 

# 3 | Results

# 3.1 | Phenotypic Characterisation of the gl, a Wax-Deficient Mutant in Welsh Onion

The mature leaves of wild-type (WT) Welsh onion typically exhibited a greyish-green coloration, whereas $_ { g l }$ mutant plants demonstrated a distinctive glossy light-green phenotype. The growth characteristics of gl plants were not significantly different from those of WT (Figure 1a,b). Quantitative analysis revealed that the total wax load of gl decreased by $8 2 \%$ compared to WT (Figure 1c). To investigate the underlying causes of these divergent surface characteristics, the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed. SEM analysis revealed a marked contrast in cuticular wax deposition between genotypes. The WT leaves were densely covered with needle-like and lamellar wax crystals, whereas these structures were almost completely absent in the gl leaves (Figure 1d,e). Further analysis of wax composition by gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS) indicated that ketones were the most significantly reduced components in the gl mutant (Figure 1f and Figure S3), with 16-hentriacontanone identified as the predominant ketone (Table  S3). Although the overall wax content was substantially lower in gl, a greater diversity of cuticular waxes was detected in gl than in WT. These findings suggest that the glossy phenotype of $_ { g l }$ resulted from both quantitative and qualitative alterations in cuticular waxes, particularly through the reduction of key components, such as 16-hentriacontanone, while the overall plant viability remained unaffected.

# 3.2 | Molecular Characterisation of the AfCER2-LIKE1 Mutation Underlying the gl Wax-Deficient Phenotype

To elucidate the molecular mechanism underlying the wax deficiency phenotype of the gl mutant, we previously performed transcriptomic sequencing of WT and gl plants (Liu et al. 2014) and confirmed through genetic analysis of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population that the gl phenotype follows the genetic pattern of a single recessive gene (Yang et al. 2017). Through unigene sequence alignment analysis, we identified a 1-bp deletion in the CER2-LIKE1 (bCBO_combHWLW_DN60346) transcript in the gl (Figure  S4). Based on these findings, we hypothesised that the deletion of the AfCER2- LIKE1 gene is responsible for the gl wax deficiency phenotype. Further genotyping of the $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population we constructed using KASP markers revealed that this molecular marker is completely co-segregated with the wax deficiency phenotype (Figure  2a). Further cloning of the gene revealed that the deletion at position 526bp of the AfCER2-LIKE1 mRNA in the gl mutant resulted in a frameshift mutation and premature termination of translation 

(Figure 2b–e, Figure S5). These results collectively provide strong evidence that the loss-of-function mutation in AfCER2-LIKE1 is a critical genetic determinant of the wax-deficient gl phenotype. 

# 3.3 | Functional Characterisation of AfCER2-LIKE1 in VLCFA Biosynthesis

To further analyze the role of AfCER2-LIKE1 in Welsh onion, subcellular localization experiments were performed. As expected, AfCER2-LIKE1 exhibited complete colocalization with an endoplasmic reticulum (ER) marker (Figure  3a), which is consistent with previous reports on CER2 homologues in Arabidopsis and rice (Haslam et al. 2012; Wang et al. 2017). These results suggest that AfCER2-LIKE1 most likely functions in the ER through interactions with KCS enzymes to form fatty acid elongation complexes. To validate this interaction, multiple protein–protein interaction assays were conducted. Yeast heterologous expression systems are widely employed in  vitro to investigate fatty acid metabolism. To validate the function of AfCER2-LIKE1, AfCER2-LIKE1 and AfKCS11 proteins were co-expressed or individually expressed in S. cerevisiae strain BY4741, and their effects on fatty acid elongation were assessed. The systematic analysis of fatty acid composition was performed via GC–MS. Chromatographic analysis revealed a notable increase in the characteristic peak area corresponding to C30 components in strains coexpressing AfCER2-LIKE1WT and AfKCS11 compared with the single-expression controls (Figure  3e). KCS acted as the rate-limiting enzyme in the fatty acid elongase (FAE) complex, catalysing the elongation of fatty acid chains up to C28. Quantitative analysis of fatty acid chain length indicated elevated levels of C28 fatty acids in strains expressing AfKCS11 alone. Co-expression of AfCER2-LIKE1WT and AfKCS11 significantly enhanced C30 fatty acid synthesis, 

whereas co-expression of AfCER2-LIKE1gl with AfKCS11 failed to induce VLCFA elongation (Figure S7). These results provide direct functional evidence at the biochemical level, confirming that AfCER2-LIKE1WT and AfKCS11 form a complex and are essential for regulating VLCFA chain elongation in Welsh onion. 

To elucidate the gene function of AfCER2-LIKE1, transgenic A. thaliana lines overexpressing AfCER2-LIKE1 were generated using the floral dip method. SEM analysis revealed that the leaves and stems of the transgenic lines exhibited varying levels of wax crystal accumulation, whereas only minimal wax deposition was observed on the leaf surfaces (Figure 4a). The wax content and the gene expression of transgenic plants were significantly higher than that of the WT (Figure 4b,c). To further validate the function of AfCER2-LIKE1, Agrobacterium-mediated transient transformation was used to transiently express AfCER2-LIKE1 in epidermal cells of gl mutant leaves. SEM analysis indicated that the transient expression of AfCER2-LIKE1 in gl significantly enhanced the deposition of cuticular wax crystals compared with the empty vector controls (Figure 4d). The resulting crystalline structures exhibited polymorphic morphologies including granular aggregates, lamellar structures and amorphous configurations (Figure 4e–i). AfCER2-LIKE1 plays a key role in cuticular wax biosynthesis.

# 3.4 | Retrotransposon Insertion in the AfCER2-LIKE1 Promoter Disrupts Gene Expression in the gl Mutant

The key single-base deletion in the AfCER2-LIKE1 gene was identified through genetic analysis, which served as the molecular basis for the wax-deficient phenotype observed in the gl mutant. Notably, qRT-PCR analysis revealed that the transcription level of AfCER2-LIKE1 was markedly reduced in the gl, with nearly undetectable expression (Figure 5a). Sanger sequencing of the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter region was then performed. Comparative sequence analysis revealed a striking 5451-bp long terminal repeat retrotransposon (LTR-RT) insertion at position −439 bp upstream of the start codon in the gl. This insertion included an 11-bp target site duplication (TSD) (Figure  5b,c). A reporter gene system (LUC/GUS) was employed to evaluate the activity of the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter in WT and gl backgrounds. The results of the N. benthamiana transient expression 

assay demonstrated that the insertion of the transposon in the gl mutant significantly reduced the activity of the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter (Figure  5d–f). Furthermore, transgenic Arabidopsis lines harbouring proWT::GUS or progl::GUS reporter constructs were generated, with the 35S constitutive promoter used as a positive control. Histochemical staining showed markedly stronger GUS staining intensity in the proWT lines than in the progl lines (Figure 5g). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that retrotransposon insertion within the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter region of $_ { g l }$ mutants substantially suppresses promoter transcriptional activity.

# 4 | Discussion

# 4.1 | Loss of Function of CER2-LIKE1 Results in a Glossy Phenotype of gl

A negative correlation has been established between wax abundance and surface glossiness, whereby reduced wax accumulation can lead to increased brightness in plants (Aharoni et al. 2004). Product gloss can also affect consumer preference (Weng  2021). Previous studies have reported that defects in cuticular wax biosynthesis frequently result in a glossy phenotype across various plant species, including Chinese cabbage (Li et  al.  2023), cabbage (Zeng et  al.  2017), onion (Manape et al. 2023) and cucumber (Yang et al. 2023). In Arabidopsis, mutations in the CER2 gene disrupt VLCFA elongation from C28 to C30, while CER2-LIKE1 mutations impair elongation from C30 to C32 (Haslam et  al.  2012; Pascal et  al.  2013). In lotus (Nelumbo nucifera), NnCER2 is primarily involved in C29 alkane biosynthesis, whereas NnCER2-LIKE is associated with C31 and C33 alkane production (Yang et al. 2018). These results suggest that synthesis of VLCFAs longer than C28 requires CER2-LIKE protein activity. Functional similarity among CER2-LIKE proteins across monocot and dicot species highlights their evolutionary conservation. Given the abundance of cuticular waxes  in Welsh onion, this species serves as a valuable model for elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying wax biosynthesis. However, the relatively large genome and recent publication of its reference genome sequence have hindered forward genetic mapping and gene function validation. Comparison based on transcriptome sequencing data and subsequent molecular validation revealed that AfCER2-LIKE1 is a key gene responsible for the 

wax-deficient phenotype (gl). Subsequent studies confirmed that functional loss of AfCER2-LIKE1 caused by an InDel mutation was responsible for the observed phenotype. Moreover, this mutation significantly reduced 16-hentriacontanone levels, indicating that AfCER2-LIKE1 is involved in the synthesis of components above C30. 

# 4.2 | Cooperative Involvement of AfCER2-LIKE1 and AfKCS11 in C30 VLCFA Elongation Is Crucial for Plant Wax Components Synthesis

As members of the BAHD acyltransferase family, CER2, CER2-LIKE1 and CER2-LIKE2 have been shown to form complexes with KCS5/CER60 or KCS6/CER6 to regulate VLCFA extension beyond C28 (Haslam et  al.  2012, 2015; Pascal et  al.  2013). In rice (Oryza sativa), OsCER2 has been shown to require co-expression with OsWSL4, a homologue of KCS6 in Arabidopsis, for the production of C30 VLCFAs (Wang et  al.  2017). In maize, ZmGLOSSY2 functions in coordination with KCS synthases to facilitate C30 VLCFA synthesis (Alexander et  al.  2024). In this study, subcellular localization, LCI and BiFC assays consistently demonstrated that AfCER2-LIKE1 interacted with AfKCS11 in the ER. Yeast co-expression assays further showed that co-expression of AfCER2-LIKE1WT with AfKCS11 promoted the elongation of fatty acids from C28 to C30. In contrast, co-expression of AfCER2-LIKE1gl and AfKCS11 failed to induce C30 fatty acid synthesis. Collectively, these findings suggest that AfCER2- LIKE1WT forms a functional complex with AfKCS11, thereby enabling the biosynthesis of C30 fatty acids. # 4.3 | Insertion of a Retrotransposon Suppresses AfCER2-LIKE1 Expression

The qPCR analysis revealed that AfCER2-LIKE1 transcript levels were markedly reduced in gl compared to WT. Promoter region sequencing identified a 5.4-kb long retrotransposon inserted upstream of the CER2-LIKE1 coding region. As dynamic elements, transposable elements (TEs) can be inserted into regulatory regions, thereby altering gene expression 

or inducing loss-of-function mutations and serving as drivers of adaptive variation in plants (Finatto et  al. 2015; Kirov et  al.  2020; Lanciano and Cristofari  2020). In blood orange (Citrus sinensis), a retrotransposon inserted into the Ruby1 promoter activates anthocyanin biosynthesis and confers stress tolerance (Wang et  al.  2024). The activity of transposable elements within the genome can disrupt gene function, thereby influencing plant phenotypes. In onion (Allium cepa), the white phenotype has been attributed to the insertion of a 4.8-kb LTR retrotransposon into the coding region of an anthocyanin biosynthesis gene, resulting in a frameshift mutation and subsequent reduction in pigment accumulation (Kim et al. 2024). In the present study, GUS and LUC reporter 

gene assays further demonstrated that retrotransposon insertion markedly suppressed the activity of the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter. Moreover, the number of key cis-acting elements (ABRE, ARE and G-box) in the AfCER2-LIKE1 promoter region was significantly lower in gl than in WT (Figure  S9). These findings suggest that the pronounced downregulation of AfCER2-LIKE1 transcription in gl is attributable to promoter insertion, thereby offering insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying wax biosynthesis regulation. 

# 4.4 | Phenotype of Reduced Wax in the Mutant gl of Welsh Onion Decreases Susceptibility to Thrips

The cuticular wax layer acts as a physical barrier and modulates interspecific interactions. The adhesion of mustard leaf beetle (Phaedon cochleariae) was hindered in glaucous leaves of Brassica oleracea (Stork 1980). Epicuticular wax mutants exhibited greater susceptibility to rice water weevil (Lissorhoptrus oryzophilus, Kuschel) and fall armyworm (Spodoptera frugiperda, Smith) (Bernaola et  al.  2021). The adults of Plutella xylostella showed oviposition preferences for collard cultivars Santo Antônio (control) and Hybrid Kope F1 100MX with wax removal (Silva et  al.  2017). However, the wax-deficient maize mutant (Zmgl8) exhibited significantly improved resistance to phyllophagous insects (Liu et al. 2023). Non-glaucous varieties of B. oleracea (var. gemmifera) displayed increased resistance to major pests, such as Brevicoryne brassicae (cabbage aphid), Aleyrodes brassicae (cabbage whitefly) and Mamestra brassicae (cabbage armyworm) (Way and Murdie 1965). The reduced wax bloom was also advantageous in terms of lower pea aphid (Acyrthosiphon pisum) populations on pea varieties (White and Eigenbrode  2000). These findings suggest that cuticular wax does not always positively correlate with insect resistance. Thrips not only feed directly on host plants but also facilitate the spread of various plant diseases, posing a threat to crop productivity. In preliminary field trials, $_ { g l }$ mutants displayed lower 

vulnerability to thrips than WT plants (Figure 6 and Figure S8). Further evaluation across different Welsh onion genotypes supported by olfactometer-based laboratory assays confirmed enhanced resistance in gl plants (Figure 7). However, the underlying mechanism remains unclear. 

Field surveys have indicated that Welsh onion with lower wax levels exhibits increased resistance to thrips (Damon et al. 2014). Both cuticular wax and cutin in Arabidopsis eca2 mutants are affected by the mutation, leading to a reduction in both components (Blanc et al. 2018). In this study, although the concentration of 16-hentriacontanone decreased in gl, the overall wax profile shifted towards an increased abundance of other components (Table S3). This suggests that individual wax constituents may directly influence the feeding behaviour of thrips. When the wax layer was partially removed from Welsh onion leaves, thrips showed a preference for both wax-retained and wax-removed samples. However, the latter experienced more extensive damage (Figure S10). These findings indicate that the presence of cuticular wax alone was not a decisive factor for enhanced thrips resistance in gl. Since the plant cuticle primarily comprises cutin and cuticular wax, alterations in both composition and structure could impact its barrier function. In Arabidopsis cer26 mutants, the level of cutin monomers is significantly increased (Pascal et  al.  2013). Similarly, eca2 mutants with reduced wax deposition are more resistant to Spodoptera littoralis (Blanc et al. 2018). Mutants with enhanced wax deposition in strawberry skin also exhibited improved resistance to B. cinerea. Additionally, the thickness of both upper epidermal cells and palisade cells in these mutants has increased (Jiang et  al.  2024). In the present study, although the gl mutant lacked visible cuticular wax, its cutin layer appeared significantly thickened (Figure 1), implying that cutin accumulation, rather than cuticular wax itself, might be responsible for insect resistance. Therefore, the thickness of the cutin layer could represent the key determinant of improved thrips resistance in gl mutants. However, this hypothesis requires validation through further field experiment data.

# 5 | Conclusion

The glossy gl mutant of Welsh onion investigated in this study originated from a natural mutation. The insertion of a retrotransposon into the promoter region and a frameshift mutation within the coding sequence collectively explain the functional alteration of AfCER2-LIKE1. Heterologous co-expression in yeast confirmed that AfCER2-LIKE1 possessed specific fatty acid elongation activity and formed a complex with AfKCS11 to extend fatty acid chains from C28 to C30. Overexpression of AfCER2-LIKE1 in Arabidopsis significantly enhanced wax content in stems and increased wax crystal accumulation. Furthermore, the gl mutant exhibited substantially higher resistance to thrips compared to the wild type. These findings indicate that genes involved in wax biosynthesis in Welsh onion could offer a theoretical foundation for molecular breeding strategies and that the thrips-resistant gl mutant may serve as a valuable genetic resource for breeding programmes.
