# HIGHLIGHTS

• GhGRDP1, which is regulated by nonsense-mediated RNA decay (NMD), serves as the domestication site for seed size in upland cotton. 

• A novel GhGRDP1 regulator positively regulates seed size and weight without impacting fiber quality. 

• A new mechanism for seed development: GhGRDP1 enhances the activity of UDP-L-rhamnose synthase (GhRHM1) through interaction. 

• GRDP1 has evolved a short glycinerich domain (sGRD) at its C-terminal to regulate seed size across core angiosperms. 

• GRDP1 maintains a conserved role in regulating seed size in both monocots and eudicots.

# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Breeding high-yield crops has long been a fundamental goal in global agriculture, with seeds serving as the essential vehicle to achieve this aim. Therefore, understanding the regulatory mechanisms of seed size is critical for ensuring food security. 

Objectives: Our study focused on the natural variation, molecular regulatory mechanism and functional evolution of yield trait-related gene GhGRDP1, identified through GWAS and eQTL analyses in upland cotton natural population.

E-mail address: xueyingguan@zju.edu.cn (X. Guan). 

Keywords: Seed yield GRDP Upland cotton (Gossypium hirsutum) Nonsense-mediated mRNA decay Domestication UDP-L-rhamnose synthesis 

Methods: Population resequencing and phenotypic data analysis were combined with Kompetitive Allele Specific PCR, degradome, GUS staining assays, and qPCR revealed the natural variation, evolutionary history and geographic origins of GhGRDP1. Using CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing and plant transformation techniques, GRDP1 transgenic plants of cotton, rice and Arabidopsis were generated to unravel its conserved role in seed regulation. Co-IP, pull down, BiFC, LCI and enzyme activity assays were used to explore the molecular mechanism of GhGRDP1. The evolution of GRDP1 was understood through phylogenetic analysis and protein structure prediction, segmental interaction and enzyme activity assays. 

Results: A 1-bp deletion in the eighth exon of GhGRDP1 initiates the nonsense-mediated mRNA decay (NMD) mechanism, causing variations in seed index (SI) and lint percent (LP) among cotton populations. This variant, unique to Gossypium hirsutum, originated from specific races and was strongly selected during domestication. GhGRDP1 positively regulates seed size and weight without compromising fiber quality, correlating with osmolyte accumulation in ovules. GhGRDP1 interacts with UDP-L-rhamnose synthase (GhRHM1) in vivo and in vitro, functioning as an activator of GhRHM1 enzymatic activity involved in cell wall composition. Notably, GRDP1 evolved a short glycine-rich domain (sGRD) on Cterminal throughout core angiosperms, playing a conserved role in seed development across both monocots and eudicots. 

Conclusion: These findings significantly advance understanding the genetic basis of phenotypic variation in cotton seed yield, providing valuable targets for high-yield crop breeding.

# Introduction

Seeds play a pivotal role in the plant life cycle, influencing key biological processes such as dispersal, germination, and reproductive success [1]. Understanding how plants determine seed size is a significant study area in developmental biology. Considerable variation in seed size exists across plant species in nature [2]. Generally, plants producing larger seeds allocate greater nutrient reserves and exhibit enhanced stress resilience, whereas those bearing smaller seeds often demonstrate superior reproductive efficiency [3,4]. Additionally, seeds constitute a fundamental resource in agriculture, serving as sources of food, fiber, oil, feed, and other valuable products. Seed size and weight are critical agronomic traits that directly impact crop yield and are crucial for both evolutionary adaptation and crop domestication [5]. Consequently, elucidating the regulatory mechanisms governing seed-related traits is indispensable for the advancement of elite crop varieties and the improvement of agricultural productivity and quality. 

Seed size is determined by an interplay of internal genetic factors and external environmental factors, with genetic determinants generally exerting a more substantial effect [6–8]. The factors influencing seed size at the cellular level can be categorized into cell proliferation and cell expansion. Seed development is closely associated with the rapid extension of primary cell walls [9,10]. During this process, cellular turgor pressure—arising from osmotic water movement driven by concentration gradients of osmolytes such as sugars and ions—exerts an internal force on the cell wall [11]. This turgor pressure is instrumental in promoting cell expansion, growth, and the maintenance of cellular morphology, thereby playing a critical role in seed development [12–14]. 

Seed yield traits are complex and governed by multiple genes. Genetic variation can induce phenotypic variation by influencing gene expression, making expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) a valuable complement to genome wide association studies (GWAS). The integrative analysis of GWAS and eQTL has proven successful in identifying functional genes in various crops such as rice, wheat, oilseed rape, cotton, soybean, and oil tea [15–21]. The natural diversity of plant species can be traced back to mutations in their wild ancestors, shaped further by both natural and artificial selection [22]. Genetic polymorphisms underlying crop evolution can be classified as coding variation, regulatory variation, presence/absence variation (PAV), and copy number variation (CNV) [23]. Regulatory variation refers to causal variation that affects functional gene expression [23]. Recent research has uncovered 

new regulatory variants acting at post-transcriptional and translational levels, in addition to those caused by promoter polymorphisms, such as the variations in 5′UTR [24,25], and RNA splicing sites [26,27]. Therefore, the identification of such regulatory variants enhances our understanding of the genetic control of quantitative traits and provides opportunities to optimize these traits for crop improvement. 

Proteins that are rich in glycine residues $( 4 0 - 7 0 \% )$ ) are referred to as glycine-rich proteins (GRPs), which were first discovered in petunias [28]. GRPs have been identified in various organisms including plants, mammals, fungi, and bacteria [29]. A new gene family called ‘‘DUF1399-GRDP (Glycine-rich domain protein),” initially characterized in eucalyptus, shares certain similarities yet differs notably from classical GRPs [30]. Specifically, GRDPs contain a highly conserved DUF1399 domain and possess only a glycinerich domain located at the C-terminus, with a comparatively lower glycine content than that observed in typical GRPs. OsGRDP1 was identified as a significant factor in regulating cell death and disease resistance in rice [31]. Studies have indicated that AtGRDP1 and AtGRDP2 may be involved in responding to various abiotic stresses, and the development of Arabidopsis seeds and female gametophytes [32–36]. However, prior investigations have drawn conclusions solely based on phenotypic observations of transgenic plants, failing to elucidate the underlying regulatory mechanisms. The molecular basis of plant GRDP1 in seed size regulation remains largely unknown. 

Rhamnose is a rare sugar with diverse applications and significant economic value. It serves as a low-calorie natural flavoring agent in the food industry [37,38] and exhibits antioxidant, antiinflammatory, moisturizing, and stabilizing properties, rendering it a crucial raw material for pharmaceutical, cosmetic, and other sectors [37–41]. However, the current extraction efficiency of natural rhamnose is low and associated costs are high, which further hinders market development [39]. In plants, UDP-rhamnose (UDP-Rha) is the activated form of rhamnose and is involved in the synthesis of polysaccharides (such as gums and hemicellulose), glycoproteins, and secondary metabolites (like rutin and saponin) as glycosyl donors [42]. As a result, the biosynthetic efficiency of UDP-Rha directly influences the overall rhamnose content within plant tissues and thus its extractability. 

Cotton is a unique crop that not only provides fibers for the textile industry but also produces a significant number of seeds used for oil production and livestock feed. Historically, research on cotton yield has predominantly focused on fiber production, with 

comparatively limited attention to seed yield traits. In this study, we identified GhGRDP1, a gene associated with cotton yield traits such as seed index (SI) and lint percentage (LP), through a joint analysis of GWAS and eQTL. Variation in GhGRDP1 expression is associated with SI and LP alterations via the NMD mechanism at the post-transcriptional level. GhGRDP1 positively regulates seed size and weight without compromising fiber quality. Additionally, we proposed a potential interactive module between GhGRDP1 and GhRHM1 that regulates cotton seed size by affecting UDP-Rha levels, which in turn alters cell expansion. Homologs of GRDP1 in Arabidopsis and rice also regulate seed size and weight, highlighting the conserved functional role of this gene across species. Within the GRDP gene family, GRDP1 has uniquely evolved the capacity to regulate seed size. Collectively, these findings enhance the understanding of seed developmental regulation and may contribute to breeding efforts in cotton and other crops.

# Results

# Nonsense-mediated mRNA degradation (NMD) regulates GhGRDP1 expression

We previously integrated GWAS and eQTL analysis with China upland cotton population1 (CUCP1) comprising 278 upland cotton accessions [21,56]. Co-localization analysis of GWAS and eQTL revealed that GhGRDP1 (Glycine-rich domain protein, GH_A02G1719), is an eGene associated with SI and LP GWAS signal on chromosome A02 (Fig. S1A). Regression analysis showed that the expression level of GhGRDP1 was positively correlated with SI but negatively related to LP (Fig. S1B). The leading SNP ChrA02_104023656 was located 202,623-bp upstream of GhGRDP1, at which haplotype ‘‘GG” and ‘‘AA” were associated with lower and greater SI/LP levels, respectively (Fig. S1C, Table S6). Expression profiling confirmed that GhGRDP1 is highly expressed in 10- and 20-DPA ovules (Fig. S1E). Due to annotation discrepancies among different TM-1 genome assemblies, we have provided the validated sequence and corresponding annotation in Table S7. To explore the genetic variants that determine GhGRDP1 expression, CUCP1 resequencing data were examined. Meanwhile, GRDP1 full-length sequence amplification of accessions with extreme SI revealed two haplotypes of GhGRDP1, haplotype 1 (GhGRDP1HAP1, TG) and haplotype 2 (GhGRDP1HAP2, T-) (Figs. 1A, S2A, Table S8). [57], with significant differences between haplotypes in SI, LP, boll weight, and fiber length (Fig. S3).

Source analysis and geographical distribution of natural variation of GhGRDP1 

The two haplotypes of GhGRDP1 were examined in natural cotton populations to analyze their origin and global geographical distribution (Table S5). Notably, $9 1 . 1 \ \%$ of the wild cotton races 

studied possessed the GhGRDP1HAP1, while its prevalence in cultivated varieties dropped to $1 7 . 7 \%$ (Fig. 2A, Table S5). This indicates a selection for the GhGRDP1HAP2 during cotton domestication. The high frequency of the GhGRDP1HAP2 allele, associated with elevated LP, reflects the breeding focus on high LP and low SI accessions in cotton. 

We estimated the average $F _ { S T }$ values for races and cultivars across each 10-kb genomic window, a conspicuous peak of differentiation was observed in the flanking region of GhGRDP1, which ranked in the top $1 0 \%$ of $F _ { S T }$ values genome wide (Fig. 2B). These findings suggest a higher level of differentiation between races and cultivars at the GhGRDP1 locus. Consistent with this, the nucleotide diversity analysis showed that cultivars exhibit significantly lower polymorphism compared to races, placing them in the top $1 0 \%$ of $\pi$ values (Fig. 2B). These findings indicate strong artificial selection at the GhGRDP1 locus in cultivated varieties. 

The A and D diploid ancestor genomes exhibited GhGRDP1HAP1 (Fig. 2C). In all the available allotetraploid cotton species, GhGRDP1HAP2 exclusively exists in upland cotton (G. hirsutum, $\mathsf { A D } _ { 1 }$ ) (Fig. 2C). Sequence alignment of wild species, races and cultivars of G. hirsutum revealed that all wild species yucatanense examined possessed the GhGRDP1HAP1, whereas latifolium, richmondii and morrlli contained GhGRDP1HAP2 (Fig. 2C). These results suggest that the latest origin of GhGRDP1HAP2 was from races of G. hirsutum. In summary, the variation is unique to G. hirsutum and originated from the evolution of wild upland cotton to races. 

The global distribution of GhGRDP1 haplotypes in modern cotton varieties was further analyzed using 973 accessions worldwide and 799 accessions from China (Fig. 2D–F, Table S5) [57]. Prominent cotton-growing regions worldwide, such as Asia, North America, and Australia, showed a strong preference for the high LP allelic variant GhGRDP1HAP2 (Fig. 2D), indicating that the large-seed allelic variant has not yet been widely utilized in global cotton breeding. In the five major cotton-producing areas in China—the South China Cotton Region (SCR), the Yangtze River Region (YZR), the Yellow River Region (YRR), the Liao River Region (LRR), and the Northwest Inland Cotton-growing Region (NIR)—the high LP allelic variant GhGRDP1HAP2 is dominant in NIR, which has extremely high cotton yields and a well-developed industry. This variant is also widely present in YZR and YRR, where many modern cotton varieties in China have been bred (Fig. 2E). Furthermore, since the promotion of upland cotton breeding in China in the 1960s, the distribution frequency of the high LP allelic variant GhGRDP1HAP2 has consistently remained above $7 5 \ \%$ . Over the past four decades, the distribution frequency of $G h G R D P 1 ^ { H A P 2 }$ has increased to approximately $9 0 \%$ (Fig. 2F, Table S5), a trend closely correlated with sustained improvements in LP. 

# GhGRDP1 positively regulates the cotton seed size

To validate the function of GhGRDP1 in cotton seed development, we generated GhGRDP1 overexpression (OE) and RNA interference (RNAi) transgenic cotton lines (Fig. S4). RT-qPCR was performed to assess the expression level of GhGRDP1 in the 1- DPA ovules of all 3 observed GhGRDP1-OE and GhGRDP1-RNAi lines. The expression level of GhGRDP1 in OE lines was more than 10-fold higher compared with their expression in TM-1, while the expression in RNAi lines was more than 5 times lower than that in TM-1 (Fig. 3A). The GhGRDP1 (GH_A02G1719) and its homeologous: GH_D03G0344 were knocked out in Gh cv. TM-1 using CRISPR-Cas9 system, and two knockout alleles (CR-1 and CR-2) were obtained (Figs. 3B, S4, S5). Due to the high sequence similarity between GH_A02G1719 and GH_D03G0344 within the RNAitargeted region, transcriptome analysis was conducted to investigate their expression patterns in the RNAi lines. The results revealed a significant downregulation of GH_A02G1719 expression, while GH_D03G0344 showed slight reduction in expression level (Fig. S6A), suggesting differential silencing efficiency between homeologs. By comparing the seed yield traits of transgenic cotton with those of wild type for three consecutive generations across two environments, the following results were found. The SI, seed length, seed width, seed area, hundred seeds weight and cotton 

seed yield of GhGRDP1-OE were significantly higher than TM-1 (Figs. 3C–G, S6B, Table S9). The seed traits of transgenic cotton silenced and knocked out GhGRDP1 showed consistent results: the SI, seed length, seed width, seed area, hundred seed weight and cotton seed yield of all decreased significantly (Figs. 3C–G, S6B, Table S9). A negative correlation trend with a significant difference was observed between LP and protein content of transgenic cotton (Figs. 3H, S6D, Table S9). Additionally, the decorticated cotton seed size also showed a positive correlation with GhGRDP1 expression in transgenic cotton (Fig. S6C). Meanwhile, the number of seeds per boll did not show a significant change between TM-1 and transgenic cotton (Fig. S6E). The findings confirm that GhGRDP1 positively regulates cotton seed size and cotton seed yield. 

The fiber length and fiber quality traits of the transgenic cotton were also assessed, revealing no significant changes in key parameters such as uniformity index, upper half mean length (UHML), breaking elongation, breaking tenacity and micronaire (Fig. S7, Table S9). These results suggest that GhGRDP1 can regulate the seed size and weight in cotton without detrimental impacts on fiber length and quality, thereby improving the emergence rate and stress resistance of cotton. 

# GhGRDP1 controls seed size by regulating seed cell expansion

To further investigate the role of GhGRDP1 in cotton seed development, cytological analysis of cotton ovules was performed. The length and width of 1, 5, 15, and 20-DPA ovules of TM-1 and transgenic cotton were measured on sections. The comparison of ovule length and width revealed significant differences in ovule size from one day after pollination (Fig. S8A, S8B). Notably, the ovules of GhGRDP1-OE were significantly larger than those of TM-1, while the ovules of GhGRDP1-RNAi and Ghgrdp1-CR were consistently smaller (Fig. S8A, S8B). The cotton ovules exhibited rapid expansion at 10–20 DPA corresponding with the cell size expansion (Fig. S8A–S8C). We observed the cell size in the inner epidermal layer of ovules at 1, 5, 15, and 20 DPA, and the results were consistent with the comparisons of ovule size. Specifically, the cell sizes in GhGRDP1-OE ovules showed a slight increase compared to those in TM-1, while the cell sizes in GhGRDP1-RNAi and Ghgrdp1-CR lines decreased (Fig. S8C). These observations support that GhGRDP1 regulates seed size by influencing cell expansion.

NMD serves as a post-transcription mechanism that selectively degrades RNA containing truncating mutations with PTC, including nonsense, frameshift and some splicesite mutations [58]. Regulatory variants inducing NMD via PTCs are rarely reported in plants. Our study uncovered a frameshift mutation resulting from a natural variation with a 1-bp deletion in G. hirsutum, which produced a PTC-inducing NMD. The variation resulted in a great reduction in GhGRDP1 expression and SI in cotton (Figs. 1, 8A, S2–S4). The mechanism for executing NMD is relatively conserved among eukaryotes, although specific execution details may vary [68]. NMD plays a crucial role in regulating the transcript levels of genes involved in plant growth. There are two types of RNA degradation pathways in NMD, SMG7-induced exonucleolytic pathway and degradation mediated by unknown endonucleases [69]. Exonucleolytic cleavage yields single-nucleotide degradation products, whereas endonucleolytic cleavage produces longer fragments. The amplification of GhGRDP1 degraded fragments indicated that degradation was carried out through the endonuclease pathway (Fig. 1H). Usually, endonuclease degradation occurs near the stop codon in mammals [58]. For GhGRDP1, the PTC was 830- bp upstream of the normal termination codon, and the amplified degraded fragment measured approximately 800 bp in length, demonstrating the conservative nature of the NMD mechanism (Figs. 1H, 8A). Although the mechanism of NMD in plants remains incompletely elucidated, our study reports a novel case in SNP mediated NMD event variation on GhGRDP1 in crop natural and artificial selection.

These findings underscore the need for further investigation into the potential adaptive roles of these low-frequency haplotypes across different agroecological environments. 

# GRDP1 has evolved a conserved function in regulating seed size

Currently, there are approximately 300,000 species of angiosperms, which play a crucial role in modern ecosystems and offer a diverse array of resources for human needs, including clothing, food, and shelter. A key element contributing to the evolutionary success of angiosperms is the remarkable diversity in seed morphology and function [71]. As plants transitioned from aquatic to terrestrial environments, the emergence of seed structures introduced new selective pressures that influenced gene function evolution. In non-angiosperm plants, such as algae, GRDP1 may primarily be involved in fundamental biological processes like metabolism. However, in monocots and eudicots, GRDP1 appears to have gradually developed new roles, particularly in regulating 

seed size, through mechanisms such as gene mutation, transcriptional regulation, and protein domain recombination. 

Angiosperm mature seeds consist of embryos, endosperms, and seed coats with their growth regulated by both maternal and zygotic tissues. Research indicates that genetic factors may exert a more significant influence on seed traits, such as size, compared to environmental and biological factors [7,65]. Despite the considerable variation in seed size observed across species, there are a few conservative seed size regulators, including DA1 (Arabidopsis and maize), DAR1 (Arabidopsis and maize) [60,61,72], GW2 (rice, soybean and maize) [62–64], and PLATZ (soybean, Arabidopsis and rice) [65]. Our research has identified a gene that codes for a glycinerich domain protein that play a significant role in regulating seed size and weight in cotton, Arabidopsis, and rice (Figs. 3, 6, 8C). Monocots and eudicots may independently acquire the ability to regulate seed size through parallel evolution. Consequently, further exploration is needed to determine if the mechanisms of these seed size regulators differ across different plant families. 

# A novel mechanism GRDP1-RHM1 for regulation of seed development

As a gene identified recently, GRDP1 remains poorly characterized, especially its molecular regulation mechanism. In this study, GhGRDP1 and GhRHM1 were found to interact with each other, and GhGRDP1 enhanced the activity of GhRHM1 (Figs. 4, 5). The product of GhRHM1, UDP-Rha is a precursor for the synthesis of the main components of the cell wall [42]. Cell wall expansion facilitates rapid ovule expansion, which in turn increases seed size (Fig. 8B). The growing cell continuously absorbs water from the apoplast through osmosis, increasing the volume of the protoplast and creating intracellular pressure, called turgor pressure. Turgor pressure is a mechanical driver that supports cell expansion, so it plays a significant role in promoting plant growth [12] (Fig. 8B). GWAS in sorghum have identified SNP linked to seed morphology traits that co-localize with an RHM locus [73]. Consequently, as a co-factor modulating RHM1 activity, GRDP1 acts like a control ‘‘valve,” making it a more suitable and prioritized target for crop breeding initiatives. 

Carbon sources such as glucose supply carbon skeletons for amino acid synthesis through catabolic processes. Specifically, intermediate metabolites (e.g., $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -ketoglutarate and oxaloacetate) generated through glycolysis and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle serve as precursor molecules for amino acid biosynthesis [74,75]. In OE-GhGRDP1 lines, the enhanced efficiency of glucose flux into rhamnose synthesis reduces the availability of amino acid precursors, leading to a relative decrease in total protein content. Conversely, CR-GhGRDP1 and RNAi-GhGRDP1 lines exhibit reduced glucose-to-rhamnose conversion efficiency, thereby increasing the pool of amino acid precursors and resulting in a relative increase in total protein production. It is noteworthy that in cotton, seed protein content generally exhibits a positive correlation with LP but a negative correlation with SI [76], which has also been confirmed by our study. 

It is well established that turgor pressure is the driving force behind cotton fiber elongation [77]. In our study, the fiber length of OE-GhGRDP1 cotton lines tended to increase, whereas RNAi-GRDP1 lines showed a reduction in fiber length compared to the transgenic controls in one year (Table S9). However, these phenotypic differences did not consistently reach statistical significance. We speculate that the variability in results may stem from multiple factors, such as environmental conditions and agricultural management practices, highlighting the complex and multifaceted relationship between cotton seed traits and fiber development.

# Application perspectives of RHM1 co-activator in rhamnose industry

Rhamnose holds significant market potential across the food, pharmaceutical, cosmetic, and other industries [37–40]; however, its complex and costly production processes can limit widespread adoption. Consequently, improving the activity of UDP-rhamnose synthetase like RHM1 in plants or microorganisms to boost the production of UDP-Rha through biotechnological optimization is one approach to enhance the extraction efficiency of rhamnose. This study discovered that GhGRDP1 interacts with RHM1 via its N-terminal region, further boosting reaction efficiency through the C-terminal, effectively augmenting UDP-rhamnose production. Given the presence of homologs of GRDP1 and RHM1 in unicellular algae, mosses, and angiosperms [78] (Fig. 7A), understanding the mechanism of their interaction in a broader range of plants is 

urgently needed, as it holds potential to address the challenges facing the rhamnose market. 

Current challenges and innovative strategies in cotton breeding 

Upland cotton is an allotetraploid species, in which the A and D subgenomes collectively shape its agronomic traits through functional differentiation and synergistic interactions [79,80]. GH_A02G1719 and GH_D03G0344 exhibit high similarity in sequence, protein structure, and tissue expression patterns (Fig. 7- B-C), providing a basis for functional redundancy in seed size regulation. However, unlike GH_A02G1719, which shows high variability and selection signals, GH_D03G0344 exhibits minimal genetic variation in upland cotton populations. Under the technical framework of this study, which requires population structural vari-

ation exceeding $5 \%$ (minor allele frequency $> 5 ~ \%$ ) for effective analysis, this limitation prevents reliable functional characterization of the Dt homolog. Considering these dual constraints, we selected genetic materials enabling simultaneous editing of both GH_A02G1719 and GH_D03G0344 to conduct in-depth functional analyses. This strategy effectively circumvents phenotypic ambiguity caused by functional redundancy between the two homologs. 

Cotton ovules consist of developing seeds and fibers that share the same ovule for absorbing photosynthetic products [81]. This unique feature highlights the distinctive nature of cotton seeds. Phenotypic data of the cotton natural population that GhGRDP1HAP1 is an excellent allele for promoting SI, while GhGRDP1HAP2 is a superior allele for promoting LP (Fig. 1C–E, S3B, S3D). Transgenic cotton studies also demonstrated a positive correlation between SI with GhGRDP1 expression level, while LP was negatively correlated with GhGRDP1 expression level (Fig. 3, Table S9). This suggests that breeding efforts in China over the past few decades have increased LP in cotton varieties at the expense of SI. 

Cotton breeders now confront the inherent negative correlation between SI and LP, a fundamental challenge in cotton improvement. To address this, advanced technologies [82–84] must be employed to synergistically utilize the GhGRDP1 alongside other key regulators of ovule and fiber development, thereby facilitating the development of novel germplasm for diverse agricultural demands. Primarily, LP remains the foremost breeding target in cotton. Previous studies have identified numerous genes crucial to regulating fiber initiation, elongation, and cellulose biosynthesis [85]. The application of prime editing technologies to precisely modify target sites, while simultaneously optimizing both GhGRDP1 and LP-related regulatory factors (e.g., GhLPF1 [86]), may overcome the pleiotropic limitations of single-gene modifications. Fiber-specific promoters (e.g., E6 [87], SCFP [88], PGbEXPA2 [89]) and embryo-specific promoters (e.g., FAD2-1 [90]) could be employed to drive gene expression, thereby balancing the ‘‘SI-LP” trade-off. Additionally, SI exhibits a negative correlation with protein content but a positive correlation with oil content [76]. With increasing commercial value of cottonseed, establishing specialized variety markets could significantly boost profits. For instance, utilizing GhGRDP1HAP1 background varieties to further edit genes regulating oil content (e.g., CAD9 [91]) and gossypol synthesis (e.g., CYP82D113, CYP71BE79, 2-ODD1 [92]) could enable breeding of ‘‘high-oil cottonseed” or ‘‘low-gossypol feed cotton” varieties with significant economic potential. Finally, existing research suggests GhGRDP1 may be regulated by transcription factors such as NF-YB3 within the cotton seed index regulatory network [21]. Therefore, investigating the relationship between GhGRDP1 and its upstream transcriptional regulators is warranted. Given that most commercial cotton varieties already carry the GhGRDP1HAP2, developing synergistic expression systems combining transcription factors with GhGRDP1 in this genetic background could enhance GhGRDP1 expression levels and further improve seed yield. 

Among the wild and landrace accessions of G. hirsutum collected by Yuan et al in 2021 [93], GhGRDP1HAP2 accessions were exclusively found in the classification Landrace2 (Table S12). This supports the previous conclusion that modern cultivars originated from Landrace2, which is made up of accessions from Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, and Honduras [93–95]. Domesticating crops result in a series of changes known as the Domestication Syndrome, which includes alterations to plant architecture for row planting, as well as improvements to fruit (fiber) quality, increased yield, and reduced seed dormancy [23,93,96]. Our findings suggest that GRDP1 was selected during the domestication of both cotton and rice (Fig. 2, 7M). Although the main harvest of cotton and rice is different, seed size has a profound effect on the yield of all crops. 

# Conclusion

Our study reveals that a 1-bp deletion in the exon of GhGRDP1 triggers NMD, resulting in divergent SI and LP in cotton populations. This Gossypium hirsutum-specific variant arising from ancestral races. Functional analyses showed that GhGRDP1 positively regulates seed size and weight without affecting fiber quality. GhGRDP1 directly interacts with UDP-L-Rhamnose synthase (GhRHM1), enhancing its activity to alter cell wall composition, critical for seed expansion. GRDP1 evolved sGRD on C-terminal to regulate seed size throughout core angiosperms. Our findings fill the gap in GRDP’s seed regulation mechanism and provide a new target for the breeding of cotton varieties and extend to the improvement of other crops.
