# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Methyl jasmonate (MeJA) and MYB transcription factors (TFs) play important roles in pathogen resistance in several plants, but MYB TFs in conjunction with MeJA-induced defense against Pseudomonas tolaasii in edible mushrooms remain unknown. 

Objectives: To investigate the role of a novel 3R-MYB transcription factor (AbMYB11) in MeJA-induced disease resistance of Agaricus bisporus and in the resistance of transgenic Arabidopsis to P. tolaasii. 

Methods: Mushrooms were treated with MeJA alone or in combination with phenylpropanoid pathway inhibitors, and the effects of the treatments on the disease-related and physiological indicators of the mushrooms were determined to assess the role of MeJA in inducing resistance and the importance of the phenylpropanoid pathway involved. Subcellular localization, gene expression analysis, dualluciferase reporter assay, electrophoretic mobility shift assay, and transgenic Arabidopsis experiments were performed to elucidate the molecular mechanism of AbMYB11 in regulating disease resistance. 

Results: MeJA application greatly improved mushroom resistance to P. tolaasii infection, and suppression of the phenylpropanoid pathway significantly weakened this effect. MeJA treatment stimulated the accumulation of phenylpropanoid metabolites, which was accompanied by increased the activities of biosynthetic enzymes and the expression of phenylpropanoid pathway-related genes (AbPAL1, Ab4CL1, AbC4H1) and an AbPR-like gene, further confirming the critical role of the phenylpropanoid pathway in MeJAinduced responses to P. tolaasii. Importantly, AbMYB11, localized in the nucleus, was rapidly induced by MeJA treatment under P. tolaasii infection; it transcriptionally activated the phenylpropanoid pathway-related and AbPR-like genes, and AbMYB11 overexpression in Arabidopsis significantly increased 

the transcription of phenylpropanoid-related genes, the accumulation of total phenolics and flavonoids, and improved resistance to P. tolaasii. 

Conclusion: This study clarified the pivotal role of AbMYB11 as a regulator in disease resistance by modulating the phenylpropanoid pathway, providing a novel idea for the breeding of highly disease-resistant edible mushrooms and plants.

# Introduction

The white button mushroom, Agaricus bisporus, is widely cultivated and consumed for its flavor and diverse nutrient profile. However, A. bisporus is highly perishable and has short shelf life after harvest [1]. The bacterial brown spot disease, which is caused by an infection with Pseudomonas tolaasii, has a major impact on the quality of mushrooms during storage [2]. Infection with P. tolaasii leads to brown spots and depressions on the surface of mushrooms, which seriously affects mushroom quality [2]. Although low-temperature storage can alleviate cap opening and mushroom browning, this storage method is not effective for controlling the occurrence of P. tolaasii-caused brown spot disease [3]. Brown spot disease outbreaks can spread rapidly in high-humidity environments. In particular, water accumulates easily on the surface of the mushrooms when temperatures fluctuate, which accelerates the occurrence of brown spot disease [4]. There is thus, a need to develop an efficient and reliable method to prevent the disease caused by P. tolaasii in edible mushrooms. 

Methyl jasmonate (MeJA) is a signaling compound that naturally occurs in plants. In previous studies, MeJA has been shown to inhibit a wide range of fruit and vegetable fungal pathogens [5–7]. More importantly, MeJA have been reported in previous studies to be capable of significantly stimulating a series of resistance- and defense-related responses, thereby reducing the occurrence of fruit and vegetable diseases [8–10]. For instance, MeJA could prime defense responses of peach fruit against Rhizopus rot by enhancing activities of chitinase and $\beta$ -1,3-glucanase, increasing expression of JA biosynthesis- and pathogenesisrelated genes, and activating the phenylpropanoid pathway [11,12]. Similarly, MeJA treatment improved the disease resistance to Botryosphaeria dothidea-caused soft rot in post-harvest kiwifruit by enhancing the activities and expressions of genes encoding antioxidant and defense-related enzymes [13] and activating the phenylpropanoid and jasmonate pathways [14]. We have previously shown that MeJA fumigation significantly enhanced the antioxidant capacity and promoted the quality retention of postharvest A. bisporus fruiting bodies [15,16]. Likewise, Yang, et al. [17] have also reported that pre-harvest application of MeJA could inhibit browning and improve the texture characteristics of A. bisporus mushrooms. The regulation of energy metabolism serves as a warning signal for cellular stress or damage [18]. In this regard, MeJA has also been demonstrated to slow the deterioration of A. bisporus by maintaining energy status and decreasing respiration rate [19]. Although several studies have demonstrated that MeJA can enhance resistance to fungal infections in various postharvest fruits and vegetables [12–14,20,21], little is known about its ability to induce resistance to bacterial diseases in edible mushrooms, especially the molecular mechanisms involved. 

MYB (myeloblastosis) proteins are a large family of transcription factors (TFs) in plants and can regulate diverse biological processes [20,22–25]. The highly conserved DNA-binding domain (termed the MYB domain) of a MYB protein usually harbors 1 to 4 imperfect repeats; based on the number of repeats, the MYB gene family members are classified into 4 subfamilies, that is 1R-MYB/ MYB-related, R2R3-MYB, 3R-MYB, and 4R-MYB) [26]. In plants, 

the R2R3-MYB subfamily is the largest group. In recent years, many researchers have reported the key roles of R2R3-MYB TFs in regulating pathogen resistance in various plants [23,24]. For example, overexpression of CaMYB39 promoted the defense response of chickpea against Ascochyta rabiei infection by activating flavonoid synthesis [27]. In Brassica juncea, BjMYB1 could induce the expression of BjCHI1, thereby contributing to the defense response against B. cinerea [28]. In rice, OsMYB30 positively modulated resistance to brown planthopper by promoting lignin biosynthesis through binding to AC-like elements in the OsPAL6 and OsPAL8 promoters [29]. 

Recent studies have also implicated several R2R3-MYB TFs in JA-induced defense responses. In cotton, MeJA treatment was shown to significantly increase the mRNA level of the R2R3-MYB TF GhODO1, thereby enhancing resistance to Verticillium dahliae by regulating the JA signaling pathway and lignin biosynthesis [30]. In rose, MeJA treatment induced the degradation of the JAZ1 protein followed by the release of free RcMYB123 and RcMYB84 proteins from the JAZ1-RcMYB84 complex, thereby activating the expression of downstream disease resistance genes and initiating defense responses against B. cinerea [20]. In Panax notoginseng, both Fusarium solani infection and MeJA treatment promoted the expression of PnMYB2; the overexpression of this gene in transgenic tobacco activated the JA metabolism and disease resistance-related pathways and consequently conferred resistance to root rot pathogens [31]. 

The roles of MYB TFs in edible fungi have been identified in previous studies [32]. For example, FfMYB15 regulated the growth of Flammulina filiformis mycelium [33], and Le.CDC5 (c-Myb type) regulated the stipe elongation of Lentinula edodes [34]. However, the roles of MYB TFs in the defense responses of edible mushrooms against pathogens have not yet been clarified. Previously, we identified 13 MYB TF genes in the fruiting bodies of A. bisporus via RNA sequencing, and the expression of only one gene, gene3279 (hereafter named AbMYB11), was significantly up-regulated at $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ following infection with P. tolaasii [35]. Whether AbMYB11 is involved in disease resistance and exerts an important role in JAinduced defense responses in A. bisporus remains unclear. Furthermore, it is important to investigate whether AbMYB11 in A. bisporus has promising value for plant disease control from a plant genetic engineering perspective. 

Here, we demonstrated that exogenous treatment with MeJA increases the resistance of post-harvest A. bisporus to P. tolaasii, and that the phenylpropanoid pathway plays a key role in MeJAinduced resistance. We also characterized a 3R-MYB (AbMYB11) from A. bisporus and found that the expression of AbMYB11 was significantly induced by MeJA treatment during resistance to P. tolaasii. Subcellular localization assay indicated that AbMYB11 is localized in the nucleus and could directly regulate the expression of genes related to the phenylpropanoid pathway and pathogenesis. Overexpression of AbMYB11 in transgenic Arabidopsis significantly activated the phenylpropanoid pathway, which enhanced resistance to P. tolaasii. Our results elucidate one aspect of the mechanisms of MeJA-induced resistance in A. bisporus mushrooms, and provide valuable information for improving the resistance of edible mushrooms and plants to bacterial infection. 

# Materials and methods

# Mushroom materials and MeJA treatment

Mushroom sporophores (approximately $3 { - } 4 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ in diameter) were harvested from Yuguan Modern Agricultural Science and Technology Co., Ltd. (Jiangsu, China). According to previous publications [36,37], only fresh and healthy sporophores free from damage, browning, or disease were selected. The sporophores were then randomly separated into five groups, including the control group, the MeJA group, the AOA-MeJA group, the PIP-MeJA group, and the MDCA-MeJA group. For the control and MeJA groups, mushrooms were immersed in distilled water for 2 min, airdried, and then fumigated with 0 and $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ MeJA for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , following our previously described method [15,16]. For the AOA-MeJA, PIP-MeJA, and MDCA-MeJA groups, mushrooms were immersed in solutions of $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ AOA, $2 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ PIP, and $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ MDCA, respectively, for 2 min, air-dried, and then fumigated with $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \ \mathrm { I }$ MeJA. Subsequently, control mushrooms and all treated mushrooms were inoculated with P. tolaasii suspensions (cell density of $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 9 } ~ \mathrm { C F U } ~ \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 } )$ $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 9 }$ ) using our previously described method [35]. Three replications were performed for each group. Throughout the course of the experiment, mushrooms were handled with great care, using clean gloves. Sterile containers were utilized to store the mushrooms, and a layer of clean, fresh-keeping film was used to cover the containers and hence to prevent microbial contamination. All the fruiting bodies were stored under a controlled condition with a temperature of $4 ~ ^ { \circ } C { \pm } 0 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ and a relative humidity of $8 5 ~ \%$ to $9 5 ~ \%$ . At 0, 12, 24, 36, 48, 60, and $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after inoculation, the number of diseased mushrooms was counted, the disease index was recorded [35], and the cap tissues (adjacent to the lesions; the thickness was approximately $7 \ \mathrm { m m }$ ) of each replicate with five fruiting bodies were sampled after superficial lesions were removed from the caps. 

# Measurement of total phenolics and total flavonoids

The extraction and determination of total phenolics and total flavonoids were performed following our previously established method [35]. Tissues of A. bisporus fruiting bodies and Arabidopsis leaves were both ground in a $1 \% ( \mathsf { v } / \mathsf { v } )$ hydrochloric acid–methanol solution under ice-bath conditions, and then incubated for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in the dark at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . After centrifugation at ${ 8 , 8 0 0 \ g }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { \ m i n }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ , the supernatants were obtained and further used for determination of the content of total phenolics and total flavonoids at $2 8 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and $3 2 5 \ \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. Each sample was replicated three times. The content of total phenolics and flavonoids was represented as $\mathbf { g } \ \mathbf { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ of fresh weight (FW). 

# Lignin measurement

The lignin content was measured following the procedure of Yang, et al. [38] with some modifications. Briefly, after grinding A. bisporus tissues in $9 5 \ \%$ ethanol solution, the homogenates were centrifuged at $8 { , } 8 0 0 ~ g$ for 7 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The precipitates were washed sequentially with $9 5 \ \%$ ethanol and ethanol-hexane (1:2, $\mathbf { v } / \mathbf { v } )$ , dried, redissolved in $2 5 \% ( \mathrm { v / v } )$ bromoacetyl solution, and kept at $7 0 \mathrm { { } ~ ^ { \circ } C }$ for 30 min. To terminate the reaction, $2 { \mathrm { m o l } } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ NaOH was added. Next, glacial acetic acid and hydroxylamine hydrochloride $( 7 . 5 \mathrm { \ m o l \ L ^ { - 1 } } .$ $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) were added and the mixture was centrifuged at ${ 8 , 8 0 0 g }$ for 7 min. Finally, the supernatant was used to determine the absorbance at $2 8 0 ~ \mathrm { { n m } }$ . The lignin content was expressed as g $\mathsf { k g } ^ { - 1 }$ FW. The assay was repeated three times for each sample. 

# MDA measurement

After the reaction, the mixture was cooled to ambient temperature and centrifuged to collect the supernatant, which was used for absorbance determination at $4 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , 600 nm, and $5 3 2 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. # Enzyme activity assays

Polyphenol oxidase (PPO), peroxidase (POD), superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL), cinnamic acid-4-hydroxylase (C4H), and 4-coumarate-CoA ligase (4CL) were extracted and measured using our previously described method [37]. The extraction of cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) was carried out using 0.2 mmol $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ Tris-HCl buffer (pH 6.25) containing $1 \ \% \ ( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { w } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } )$ PVP, 15 mmol $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ $\beta \cdot$ -mercaptoethanol, and $2 \% ( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { w } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } )$ polyethylene glycol. The CAD activity was measured using the method of Li, et al. [39]. The seven enzymatic activities were all expressed in terms of $\mathrm { U } \ \mathrm { m g } ^ { - 1 }$ protein. 

# RNA extraction and gene expression analysis

Total RNA was isolated from A. bisporus and Arabidopsis tissues using our previously described method [40] and the RNAprep pure Plant Kit (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing, China), respectively. Total RNA concentration, purity, and integrity were assessed by spectrophotometer and agarose gel electrophoresis. Subsequently, RNasefree DNase I was used to remove residual genomic DNA from RNA samples; it was then reverse transcribed to synthesize cDNA using oligo $( \mathsf { d T } ) _ { 1 8 }$ and M MLV reverse transcriptase. The quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) protocol was performed as previously described [40]. Gene expression levels were calculated using the formula $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ with A. bisporus EF1- $\cdot \alpha$ (Genbank X97204) or Arabidopsis Actin2 (Genbank NM_112764.4) as the reference gene. Each sample was replicated three times. See Table S1 for gene specific primers for qPCR. 

# Subcellular localization of AbMYB11

The coding DNA sequence (CDS) without the termination codon of AbMYB11 was amplified using 1305-AbMYB11-F and 1305-AbMYB11-R primers (Table S2) and then inserted into the pCAMBIA1305-GFP plasmid (digested with SpeⅠ and BamHⅠ). Based on our previously described method [40], the empty plasmid pCAMBIA1305-GFP, the resulting plasmid pCAMBIA1305-AbMYB11-GFP, and the nuclear marker plasmid (p2300-35S-H2B-mCherry) were separately transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 (WEIDI, Shanghai, China). Subsequently, GV3101 cell suspensions containing empty plasmids or pCAMBIA1305-AbMYB11-GFP plasmids were mixed with cell 

suspensions containing p2300-35S-H2B-mCherry plasmids and injected into 4-week-old tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaves. Two to three d after infiltration, the fluorescence signals of GFP and mCherry were observed by laser confocal microscopy (Zeiss LSM880, Jena, Germany). 

# AbMYB11 amplification and sequence analysis

The AbMYB11 CDS without the termination codon was amplified using pMAL-c2x-AbMYB11-F and pMAL-c2x-AbMYB11-R primers (Table S2). The PCR products were purified and subsequently digested with EcoRⅠ and HindⅠII before ligation to the pMAL-c2x plasmid using T4 DNA Ligase (TransGen Biotech). To confirm the correctness of the recombinant plasmid pMALc2x-AbMYB11, EcoRⅠ/HindⅠII double digest analysis and DNA sequencing were performed. 

The search for the families and conserved domains of AbMYB11, phylogenetic tree construction, and multiple sequence alignments were performed using our previously described method [40]. The tertiary structure of the AbMYB11 protein was predicted using I-TASSER (https://zhanglab.ccmb.med.umich.edu/I-TASSER/) and evaluated using Procheck and Verify-3D with the UCLA-DOE LAB-SAVES v6.0 server (https://saves.mbi.ucla.edu/). 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The positive recombinant plasmid pMAL-c2x-AbMYB11 was further transformed into E. coli BL21 to express recombinant MBP-AbMYB11 proteins under induction with isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalac topyranoside. Subsequently, MBP-AbMYB11 proteins were purified using Dextrin Beads 6FF (Smart Lifesciences, Guangzhou, China) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. 

EMSAs were also performed following our previously described method [40] with a Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) to investigate the binding capacity of AbMYB11 to promoters of genes involved in phenylpropanoid metabolism and disease resistance, including AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like. First, probes (Table S3) containing MYBbinding motifs labeled with or without biotin at the $3 ^ { \prime }$ end and their corresponding mutant probes were designed. The purified MBP-AbMYB11 proteins were then reacted separately with biotin-labeled probes and mutant probes. Unlabeled probes and mutant probes were also used separately to compete with biotinlabeled probes for binding to MBP-AbMYB11 proteins. Empty MBP proteins were used as negative controls. After separation on polyacrylamide gels, the reaction products were transferred to nylon membranes and cross-linked by UV irradiation before chemiluminescence detection. 

# Promoter sequence analysis and cloning

The promoter sequences of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPRlike were analyzed using our previously described method to identify MYB TF-binding motifs [40]. Subsequently, specific primers (Table S2) were designed and the genomic DNA of A. bisporus tissues was extracted. Finally, promoter sequences (Text S1) containing MYB-binding motifs (ACCTACC and AACTG) of the four genes were amplified. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter (DLR) assay

The transient promoter activation assay was performed using DLR assays as previously described [12]. The AbMYB11 CDS (without the termination codon) was amplified by PCR using pEAQ-AbMYB11-F and pEAQ-AbMYB11-R primers (Table S2) and inserted 

into the pEAQ-HT vector (AgeⅠ and XhoⅠ) as an effector. The amplified AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like promoters were separately inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector (HindⅠII and BamHⅠ) as reporters. Subsequently, the effector plasmid (pEAQ-AbMYB11) and the empty vector pEAQ-HT (as a control) were transferred separately to A. tumefaciens EHA105, and the four reporter plasmids were transferred separately to A. tumefaciens EHA105 (psoup). The collected bacterial cells were adjusted to an $O D _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.2 to 0.4 using infiltration buffer $\mathrm { \langle p H ~ } 5 . 6 \mathrm { \rangle }$ ) containing $2 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ acetosyringone, 10 mmol $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ 2-(N-morpholino) ethanesulfonic acid, and $1 0 \mathrm { \ m m o l \ L ^ { - 1 } }$ $\mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ magnesium chloride, and then incubated for $^ { 2 - 3 \ \mathrm { h } }$ in the dark at ambient temperature for vir gene induction. Subsequently, A. tumefaciens cells containing the effector (or empty plasmid) and the A. tumefaciens cells containing the corresponding reporter were mixed in a ratio of 8:1 and infiltrated into 4 to 6-week-old tobacco (N. benthamiana) leaves. At 48–72 h post-infiltration, the relative ratio of firefly luciferase (LUC) activity to Renilla luciferase (REN) signal activity was measured using a dual-luciferase assay kit (Vazyme). According to the relative LUC/REN ratio, the transactivation activity of AbMYB11 and its regulatory role on each promoter were determined. 

# Transgenic Arabidopsis overexpressing A. bisporus MYB11

To generate the heterologous overexpression plasmid 35S:: AbMYB11, the full coding region (1,170 bp) of AbMYB11 was amplified from A. bisporus fruiting bodies using specific primer sequences of 1305-AbMYB11S-F and 1305-AbMYB11S-R (Table S2), and this sequence was then inserted between the enhanced CaMV35S promoter and NOS terminator in the plant overexpression plasmid pCAMBIA1305. The verified plasmid 35S:: AbMYB11 was subsequently transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 cells before immersion into Arabidopsis plants using the floral dip method [41]. The harvested T1 seeds were then sterilized, washed, and vernalized before germination for 8 to 15 d on half MS (½MS) medium containing $3 0 \mathrm { m g L } ^ { - 1 }$ of hygromycin (Hyg). Subsequently, the Hyg-resistant seedlings were then transplanted into prepared planting soils and placed in a chamber under LD conditions. Finally, homozygous transgenic T3 seeds were obtained by resistance screening for follow-up experiments. 

# Inoculation of Arabidopsis leaves with P. tolaasii

Two-week-old medium-grown wild-type (Col-0) and T3 transgenic Arabidopsis seedlings were collected for qPCR detection of AtPAL1, AtC4H, At4CL1, and AtPR1 gene expression, and then $5 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ droplets of P. tolaasii suspensions $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ CFU $\mathrm { m } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ) were spotted on the cotyledons according to a previous method reported by Chung, et al. [42]. The inoculated seedlings were subjected to $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with continuous light, and disease development of infected seedling cotyledons was monitored 7 d after inoculation. Similarly, the leaves of 6-week-old soil-grown Col-0 and T3 transgenic Arabidopsis plants were inoculated with P. tolaasii suspensions $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ CFU $\mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ ). At $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after inoculation, the infected leaves were photographed and stained with trypan blue, and healthy and uninfected leaves were collected for measurements of phenylpropanoid pathway- and resistance-related indicators. 

# Trypan blue staining

Pathogen-infected Arabidopsis leaves were stained using a Plant Tissue Stain Kit (Trypan Blue Method) (Solarbio, Beijing, China). Briefly, the leaves were immersed in trypan blue staining solution (preheated to $6 0 { - } 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ ) for $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Subsequently, the stained leaves were soaked in a $9 5 \ \%$ ethanol solution for more than $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ before photographs were taken. 

# Statistical analysis

All data were statistically analyzed by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) analysis using SPSS 21.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA) and expressed as the mean ± standard error (SE) of three replicates. Differences with $P { < } 0 . 0 5$ were considered statistically significant. The heatmap was created using R Studio (Posit Software, Boston, MA, USA) for clustering and scaling processing. 

# Results

Exogenous MeJA significantly inhibits brown spot disease in A. bisporus by inducing resistance in the host rather than by exerting a direct antibacterial effect 

To investigate the role of MeJA in controlling bacterial disease caused by P. tolaasii in edible mushrooms, we fumigated the fruiting bodies of A. bisporus with MeJA vapor before inoculation with P. tolaasii. As shown in Fig. 1, Table S4, exogenous MeJA treatment significantly reduced the disease symptoms caused by P. tolaasii during storage by inducing a marked decrease in the incidence rate and disease index. The incidence of the disease in $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ MeJA-pretreated mushrooms was only $3 5 . 0 \%$ at $3 6 \mathrm { h }$ after inoculation, while that of the control reached approximately $5 7 . 5 \ \%$ . After inoculation for 36, 48, and $^ \mathrm { ~ 6 0 ~ h , }$ , the disease index in MeJApretreated mushrooms was $5 0 . 0 ~ \%$ , $6 3 . 5 \ \% ,$ , and $6 7 . 1 \ \%$ of that in control mushrooms, respectively. In general, the results suggested that MeJA enhances the resistance of A. bisporus to P. tolaasii. 

To verify whether MeJA has a direct inhibitory effect on P. tolaasii, we performed an antibacterial assay using different concentrations of MeJA solution. Our results showed that when the concentration of MeJA was $\leq 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ , it showed negligible antibacterial activity against $P .$ . tolaasii, and even at a very high concentration $\left( { > } 2 , 3 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 } \right)$ ), it yielded only a $6 1 . 1 \ \%$ inhibition rate (Fig. S1). Therefore, we proposed that MeJA controlled brown spot disease not by direct antibacterial effects, but probably by inducing disease resistance. 

Many previous studies have shown that P. tolaasii can secrete tolaasin, which can damage the cell membranes of the mushrooms and further induce PPO activation, resulting in the occurrence of brown lesions on the mushroom caps [43]. Our results showed that the P. tolaasii-induced activation of PPO was significantly attenuated by MeJA pretreatment during storage (Fig. 1B and Table S4). Especially, the maximum value of PPO activity at $^ { 4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after inoculation was only approximately $7 7 . 6 \%$ of that of control mushrooms. Pretreatment with MeJA also slowed down the production of MDA, which resulted in a significantly lower MDA content $8 6 . 2 \ \%$ of that of the control) at $6 0 \ \mathrm { h } ,$ . Importantly, MeJA pretreatment activated the antioxidant enzyme system and phenylpropanoid metabolism, as evidenced by the increased antioxidant activities of the POD, SOD, and CAT enzymes and the enhanced activities of four key phenylpropanoid pathway enzymes, including PAL, C4H, 4CL, and CAD, as well as the increased content of total phenolics, total flavonoids, and lignin (Fig. 1B and Table S5). Overall, these results demonstrate that exogenous MeJA treatment enhanced the resistance of A. bisporus to P. tolaasii infection. 

Suppression of the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway significantly weakens MeJA-induced disease resistance 

To make clear of the role of phenylpropanoid metabolism in MeJA-induced disease resistance in A. bisporus, aminooxy acetic acid (AOA), piperonylic acid (PIP), and 3,4-(methylenedioxy) cinnamic acid (MDCA), which are inhibitors of PAL [44], C4H [45], 

and 4CL [46], respectively, were used to treat mushrooms before MeJA fumigation. Compared with the sole MeJA alone treatment, all AOA, PIP, and MDCA inhibitors largely suppressed the ability of MeJA to stimulate the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway, as reflected by the significantly lower activities of PAL, C4H, 4CL, and CAD, and markedly reduced levels of total phenolics, total flavonoids, and lignin (Fig. 1B and Table S5). As a result, all three inhibitors weakened the ability of MeJA to control brown spot disease. In particular, the incidence rate in mushrooms under the combined treatment of AOA/PIP/MDCA and MeJA and the control group did not differ significantly throughout the storage period, and the disease index was significantly higher in the combined treatment than in the sole MeJA treatment (Fig. 1, see also the Table S4). Consistent with this observation, the AOA, PIP, and MDCA inhibitors also completely counteracted the ability of MeJA to down-regulate PPO activity; consequently, PPO activity was higher in treated mushrooms than in control mushrooms at certain storage time points. Furthermore, the increase in the activities of three antioxidant enzymes (CAT, SOD, and POD) induced by MeJA was markedly impaired by the inhibitors of AOA, PIP, and MDCA (Fig. 1B and Table S4). These results indicated that suppression of the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway could significantly weaken MeJA-induced disease resistance, suggesting that phenylpropanoid metabolism plays a key role in MeJA-induced resistance to P. tolaasii in A. bisporus. 

The expression of genes relevant to the phenylpropanoid pathway and pathogenesis is up-regulated in MeJA-induced disease resistance 

To investigate the molecular mechanism by which MeJA induces resistance to P. tolaasii in A. bisporus, qPCR experiments were performed to determine the expression patterns of genes encoding key enzymes in the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway and a pathogenesis-related (PR) protein-like gene. As shown in Fig. 2, the relative expression of AbPAL1 was remarkably and continuously up-regulated by MeJA after inoculation for 36 h during storage, with levels approximately 1.30, 13.5, 2.60, 4.33 times higher than the control at 36, 48, 60, 72 h, respectively. The change in the expression pattern of AbPAL1 was similar to that of PAL activity, which was significantly increased by MeJA from $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ onwards, with levels approximately 1.94 to 2.88 times higher than the control (Table S5). Although MeJA significantly induced the expression of AbPAL2 immediately after treatment (0 h), the expression of AbPAL2 remained consistently low in MeJA-treated mushrooms, and the expression of this gene was similar to the control during storage. In comparison to AbPAL1, AbC4H1 and Ab4CL1 exhibited a faster response to MeJA treatment. The expression of AbC4H1 was approximately 2.30, 1.79, and 1.44 times higher than the control at 12, 24, and 48h after MeJA treatment, respectively. The expression of Ab4CL1 was 1.73 and 2.71 times higher than the control at 0 h and $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after MeJA treatment, respectively. While the activity of C4H and 4CL was markedly elevated by MeJA treatment between 12 and $6 0 \ \mathrm { h }$ , the activity of C4H and 4CL in the MeJA-treated group, which exhibited a level of $\geq 1 . 3$ times higher than the control, was observed only at 24–48 h and $^ { 3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , respectively. MeJA treatment did not significantly affect the expression of Ab4CL2 throughout the experimental period, except for a slight increase at $3 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . In contrast, AbPR-like expression was significantly up-regulated during storage (1.32 to 4.17 times higher than the control), with the exception at $4 8 \ \mathrm { h }$ . These findings indicated that MeJA treatment could up-regulate the expression of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like genes to promote resistance to P. tolaasii, which was consistent with the physiological changes in Fig. 1 and Table S5. These results demonstrated that the phenylpropanoid pathway is important in mediating MeJA-induced disease resistance in A. bisporus.

# Identification and characterization of a 3R-type MYB (AbMYB11) in A. bisporus

In our previous study, we identified differentially expressed genes (DEGs) that are involved in the early defense of A. bisporus sporophores against P. tolaasii by transcriptome sequencing [35]. As TFs likely play important roles in transcriptionally coordinating the gene expression patterns, we therefore further performed data mining and identified 13 genes encoding putative MYB family members among the DEGs (Table S6). Phylogenetic analysis showed that the 13 MYB proteins carried 1 to 4 MYB or MYB-like repeats, which belonged to four MYB subfamilies (Fig. S2). Based on the number of repeating units, we named these genes AbMYB1 to AbMYB13, of which AbMYB1 to AbMYB8 belong to the 1R-MYB subfamily, AbMYB9 and AbMYB10 belong to the R2R3-MYB subfamily, AbMYB11 and AbMYB12 belong to the 3R-MYB subfamily, and AbMYB13 belongs to the 4R-MYB subfamily. As shown in the RNA-seq data (Table S6), the expression of only one (AbMYB11) of the 13 MYB genes was significantly up-regulated at $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ following infection with P. tolaasii, indicating its potential role in the disease defense responses of A. bisporus. Therefore, we further performed a detailed functional analysis of AbMYB11. Additionally, AbMYB11 was most closely related to PoMYB1 from Pleurotus ostreatus (Fig. S2). SMART program analysis showed that AbMYB11 contains a conserved MYB domain with a length of 158 aa, located at the N-terminus of 5 to 162 aa. 

To clarify the structural characteristics of AbMYB11 and to identify conserved amino acids, the MYB domain sequence of AbMYB11 was aligned with the other 16 MYB domains of P. ostreatus, Ganoderma sinense, Arabidopsis thaliana, Oryza sativa, Nicotiana tabacum, Gallus gallus, Drosophila melanogaster, and Homo sapiens. Multiple alignments showed that the MYB domain is highly conserved among 3R-MYB proteins from plants, animals, and fungi; 23 conserved amino acids (highlighted in red) were found with $100 \%$ sequence identity in the 17 aligned proteins (Fig. 3A). Similar to 

other 3R-MYB proteins, AbMYB11 has three imperfect MYB repeats, and each repeat is approximately 50 amino acids in length and contains three alpha helices, resulting in a helix-turn-helix (HTH) secondary structure (Fig. 3, A and B, see also the Fig. S3). Furthermore, each repeat of AbMYB11 contains 2 to 3 regularly spaced tryptophan (W) residues, which contribute to the formation of a hydrophobic core and maintain the HTH structure [26,47]. In contrast to plant and animal MYB domains, the third conserved W residue of the R3 repeat is replaced by another hydrophobic amino acid residue (tyrosine, Y) in AbMYB11 and the other four 3R-MYB proteins of P. ostreatus and G. sinense. Structural analysis in previous studies [48,49] has shown that the third $\pmb { \alpha }$ -helix in the repeats of R2 and R3 of the MYB domain is involved in the recognition of the base sequence of the MYB binding motif (AACNG). N-183 (R3), K-182 (R3), and K-128 (R2) have been shown to play key roles in the ability of the mouse c-Myb protein to specifically recognize the core AAC and the fifth G residue of the AACNG-motif [49]. Wang, et al. [48] also reported that N-110 (R3), K-109 (R3), and K-55 (R2) in an Arabidopsis R2R3-MYB protein are responsible for the specific recognition of the first adenine, third cytosine, and fifth guanine of the AACNG-motif. Of the three DNA recognition residues, two (K-149 and N-150 in the R3 region) are conserved in AbMYB11 and two 3R-MYB proteins (PoMYB1 and PoMYB2) from P. ostreatus, and the corresponding Lys (K) in the R2 region is substituted by an Asp (D) amino acid (Fig. 3A). Overall, the above results indicated that the identified 3R-type MYB TF in A. bisporus has a typical MYB domain with characteristics similar to those of 3R-MYBs in plants and animals.

AbMYB11 activates the expression of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like genes by targeting their promoters 

Since the expression of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like genes was significantly induced during MeJA-mediated disease resistance in A. bisporus (Fig. 2), we hypothesized that AbMYB11 

might be involved in MeJA-induced disease resistance by transcriptionally activating the expression of these genes. Therefore, DLR assays were further performed. As shown in Fig. 4A, LUC driven by the AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, or AbPR-like promoter and REN driven by the CaMV35S promoter were constructed in the pGreenII0800-LUC plasmid to serve as reporter plasmids; the constructed pEAQ-AbMYB11 vector expressing AbMYB11 was used as an effector plasmid. The relative LUC/REN ratio of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like was 2.25-, 2.45-, 1.03-, and 1.20-fold higher, respectively, when AbMYB11 was transiently expressed compared to the empty control (Fig. 4B). The data supported our hypothesis that AbMYB11 can activate the expression of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like genes. 

Plant R2R3-MYB TFs have previously been shown to directly bind to the AACNG-motif or AC-rich elements (AC-I, ACCTACC; AC-II, ACCAACC; and AC-III, ACCTAAC), thus regulating the transcription of target genes [20,48,49]. Therefore, cis-element motifs in the 2-kb upstream regions of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPR-like genes were scanned and revealed the presence of multiple AACNG-motifs and AC-rich elements in all four gene promoters (Table S7). To determine whether AbMYB11 can bind to the AACNG-motif or AC elements, EMSA assays were performed using purified recombinant MBP-AbMYB11 protein. DNA probes containing MYB-binding sites (50 -AACTG- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ or $5 ^ { \prime }$ -ACCTACC-30 ) were synthesized and biotin-labeled (Table S3). A clear band shift (lane 3) was observed when the MBP-AbMYB11 protein was reacted with a biotin-labeled probe, whereas no band shift (lane 2) was observed when MBP instead of the MBP-AbMYB11 protein was used (Fig. 4, C-F). Furthermore, the mobility shifts (lanes 4 and 5) were weakened when the corresponding unlabeled probes were added, and the competitive effect was dependent on the concentration. In contrast, the addition of the corresponding unlabeled mutant probes did not lead to pronounced changes in the binding bands (lanes 7 and 8). Additionally, the MBP-AbMYB11 protein could not bind to the biotin-labeled mutant probes (lane 6). These results indicated that AbMYB11 binds specifically and directly to the AACNG-motif or AC-I element in the promoters of AbPAL1 and AbC4H1, and Ab4CL1 and AbPR-like, respectively. Overall, the aforementioned results indicate that AbMYB11 transcriptionally activates the expression of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, Ab4CL1, and AbPRlike by directly targeting their promoters. 

# AbMYB11 overexpression in Arabidopsis significantly increases resistance to P. tolaasii and activates phenylpropanoid metabolism

Since P. tolaasii can infect not only edible mushrooms but also diverse plants [42], we further investigated the role of AbMYB11 in regulating plant resistance to P. tolaasii using Arabidopsis as a material. Transgenic A. thaliana lines overexpressing AbMYB11 were generated by transforming the 35S::AbMYB11-GFP plasmid into Arabidopsis using the Agrobacterium-mediated method. Twoweek-old medium-grown seedlings and 6-week-old soil-grown plants of the three transgenic T3 generation lines (T3-12, T3-29, and T3-31) with the highest AbMYB11 expression (Fig. 5, A and B, see also the Fig. S5, A and B), as well as control Col-0 Arabidopsis were inoculated with P. tolaasii. Consistent with the results of a previous study [42], the cotyledons of Col-0 seedlings exhibited typical chlorosis and wilting symptoms, whereas transgenic seedlings displayed only minor symptoms, which reflected resistance to this bacterial disease (Fig. S5C). Similarly, transgenic plants overexpressing AbMYB11 exhibited enhanced tolerance to infection by P. tolaasii as compared to Col-0 plants (Fig. 5C). The trypan blue staining solution is unable to penetrate the membranes of living cells; however, it can penetrate those of dead cells, where it binds to the disintegrated DNA, causing the dead cells to stain. Consequently, the trypan blue staining method is commonly employed 

to distinguish between living and dead cells. As illustrated in Fig. 5D, the trypan blue assay showed that cell death was reduced in AbMYB11 transgenic lines compared with that in Col-0 plants at $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after inoculation. Furthermore, the expression level of AtPR1 was also found to be elevated in the seedlings of three AbMYB11- overexpressing lines (Fig. S5D) and the leaves of T3-29 transgenic plants (Fig. 5F) in comparison to that observed in Col-0 plants. Overall, the above results demonstrate that overexpression of AbMYB11 improves resistance to P. tolaasii in transgenic Arabidopsis. 

To determine whether the enhanced disease resistance observed in AbMYB11 transgenic Arabidopsis plants is associated with the regulation of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis, we measured total phenolic and flavonoid content and AtPAL1, AtC4H, and At4CL1 transcript levels. Compared to Col-0 leaves, AbMYB11- overexpressing Arabidopsis leaves had significantly higher levels of total phenolics and flavonoids (Fig. 5E). qPCR data showed that the expression levels of AtPAL1, AtC4H, and At4CL1 were significantly higher in both AbMYB11-overexpressing seedlings (Fig. S5D) and AbMYB11-overexpressing leaves (Fig. 5F) than in the seedlings and leaves of Col-0. These findings, coupled with the results in Fig. 4, suggest that the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway plays a pivotal role in conferring disease resistance, which is regulated by AbMYB11. 

# Discussion

Brown spot disease, caused by P. tolaasii infection, is one of the most economically significant diseases of commonly cultivated mushrooms, as it can result in decreases in mushroom quality [2,3]. MeJA is an important signaling molecule that has received considerable research interest; it has been shown to alleviate diseases by directly preventing the pathogen growth and/or inducing host resistance in various plants and post-harvest fruits and vegetables [7,8,50]. In contrast to numerous studies conducted on plants, the role of MeJA in inducing disease resistance in edible mushrooms and the underlying molecular mechanisms remain largely unexplored. Here, we found that P. tolaasii was insensitive to MeJA at concentrations $\leq 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ (Fig. S1). However, MeJA at a concentration of as low as $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ markedly reduced the brown spot disease in postharvest A. bisporus; meanwhile, it also inhibited PPO activation induced by P. tolaasii, suppressed MDA production, and stimulated the activities of antioxidant enzymes and phenylpropanoid pathway-related enzymes, as well as the accumulation of metabolites produced in the phenylpropanoid pathway (Fig. 1, see also the Tables S4 and S5). Overall, these results are in accordance with those of previous studies on plants [10,14,20] and indicate that MeJA acts as a signaling molecule to prevent infection by the P. tolaasii pathogen by inducing the defense systems of A. bisporus mushrooms. 

We found that the phenylpropanoid metabolism pathway in A. bisporus plays a key role in the MeJA-induced disease resistance of A. bisporus. PAL, C4H, and 4CL are the key enzymes of the phenylpropanoid pathway in plants [51]. In recent decades, significant progress has been made in our understanding of the role of phenylpropanoid metabolism in plant responses to biotic and abiotic stresses and the underlying molecular regulatory mechanisms [11,14,22]. Nevertheless, the role of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis in edible mushrooms remains unclear. In our study, combined treatments with MeJA and the inhibitors of PAL, C4H, and 4CL significantly suppressed the ability of MeJA to stimulate PAL, C4H, 4CL, and CAD activities and accumulate phenolics, flavonoids, and lignin, which weakened MeJA-induced disease resistance in A. bisporus (Fig. 1, see also Tables S4 and S5). Overall, our results demonstrated for the first time the role of phenylpropanoid metabolism in MeJA-induced resistance to bacterial disease in edible mushrooms. A previous study reported that the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathway is important for enhancing the tolerance of a rare wild edible mushroom (Agaricus sinodeliciosus) to mildly alkaline conditions [56]. Another example is that high- $. 0 _ { 2 } / C 0 _ { 2 }$ treatments promote wound healing in fresh-cut A. bisporus by activating the phenylpropanoid metabolism pathway [57]. Therefore, phenylpropanoid metabolism plays a role in regulating diverse responses to stress and phytohormones in plants but also in edible mushrooms. 

In recent years, our RNA-seq database has identified 13 MYB genes in A. bisporus. Of these, only AbMYB11 was specifically induced by P. tolaasii at the early stage (Table S6) [35], suggesting a potential role of AbMYB11 in the early defense against P. tolaasii. In the current study, we further found that, among all the R2R3- MYB genes and 3R-MYB genes, only AbMYB11 transcription exhibited a significant up-regulation by MeJA during the resistance to P. tolaasii (Fig. 3D and Fig. S4). It is notable that the expression level of AbMYB11 was immediately elevated following MeJA treatment, preceding the induction of AbPAL1, AbC4H1, and AbPR-like genes by MeJA. Previous studies have demonstrated that the transcription of pathogenesis- and phenylpropanoid pathway-related genes is activated through the induction of crucial TF expression by MeJA treatment [11,12]. Therefore, we postulated that AbMYB11 is involved in MeJA-induced resistance to P. tolaasii in A. bisporus. 

To further explore the molecular mechanisms of AbMYB11 in MeJA-induced disease responses, the characterization of AbMYB11 was performed. Results showed that AbMYB11 belongs to the 3Rtype MYB family and, is localized to the nucleus (Fig. 3, A, B and C); this TF can directly bind to and activate the AbPAL1, Ab4CL1, AbC4H1, and AbPR-like promoters in response to P. tolaasii (Fig. 4). Our findings are consistent with the results of previous studies showing that R2R3-MYB TFs can transcriptionally regulate phenylpropanoid metabolism in a variety of plants. For example, 

MYB15 in A. thaliana [58] can directly bind to AC-rich elements of the promoters of PAL1 and C4H genes and thus promote lignin biosynthesis. In rice, OsMYB30 can up-regulate the expression of OsPAL6 and OsPAL8 to confer resistance to brown planthopper [29], and it can also activate the expression of Os4CL3 and Os4CL5, resulting in the accumulation of G and S lignin [59]. Here, we found that AbMYB11 could directly bind to the typical AC-I (ACCTACC)- containing region of the Ab4CL1 and AbPR-like gene promoters (Fig. 4, E and F). This observation is consistent with the previous studies proposing that AC elements, which are common cisregulatory elements, are closely related to the coordinated expression of phenylpropanoid biosynthesis-related genes [58,60]. The similarity in the recognition elements of AbMYB11 and R2R3-MYB genes might stem from the evolution of R2R3-MYB genes from an ancestor of 3R-MYB genes [22,26]. Meanwhile, we found that AbMYB11 could also specifically bind to the AACTG-motif in the AbPAL1 and AbC4H1 gene promoters, implying that the AACTGmotif contributes to the transcriptional regulation of the phenylpropanoid pathway. It is worth mentioning that the replacement of Lys (a conserved DNA-recognizing amino acid in plants and animals) with Asp at site 94 in the R2 repeat (Fig. 3A) did not affect the specific binding of AbMYB11 to the AACTG-motif (Fig. 4, C and D), and 11 DNA-binding amino acid residues were predicted in the R3 repeat (Fig. 3A), suggesting that the R3 repeat, but not the R2 repeat, is important for the DNA recognition of AbMYB11. This observation is in contrast to R2R3-type MYBs, where both R2 and R3 repeats have been identified to be involved in the recognition of the AACNG-motif [48,49]. Although the MBS element (C/ TNGTTG/A), the E-box (CANNTG), and AC-II/AC-like elements were identified in the promoters of AbPAL1, Ab4CL1, and AbC4H1 (Table S7), further studies are still needed to determine whether AbMYB11 can bind to these elements. Taken together, our findings suggest that AbMYB11 could activate the transcription of phenyl-

propanoid pathway genes through binding to AACTG-motif or AC elements present in their promoters, potentially leading to enhanced accumulation of phenylpropanoid metabolites and involvement in MeJA-induced disease resistance against P. tolaasii. 

In addition to A. bisporus, P. tolaasii is also capable of infecting other edible mushrooms [61,62], such as Flammulina velutipes, L. edodes, and Pleurotus eryngii, and many types of plant hosts [42], such as N. tabacum, Arabidopsis, cauliflower, strawberry, and potato tuber. In recent decades, heterologous overexpression in transgenic Arabidopsis lines has been widely used to study the functions of various plant TFs in the responses to bacterial and fungal pathogens [63,64]. Although both edible mushrooms and Arabidopsis are eukaryotes, to the best of our knowledge, no one has performed functional validation studies of a mushroom TF to regulate plant defense against pathogen infection. Here, we found that overexpression of AbMYB11 in A. thaliana significantly improved the resistance of both Arabidopsis seedlings and plants to P. tolaasii, which was accompanied by the significant up-regulation of the plant defense-associated marker gene AtPR1 [65] in transgenic seedlings (Fig. S5) and T3-29 transgenic leaves (Fig. 5). Overexpression of this gene also led to the up-regulation of all detected phenylpropanoid-related genes in both Arabidopsis seedlings and plants, resulting in the greater accumulation of total phenolics and total flavonoids in treated plants compared to Col-0 plants. The increased metabolites of phenolics and flavonoids could contribute to the protection of Arabidopsis against pathogens due to their direct antimicrobial activities against pathogens [22,66,67]. Overall, our results show for the first time that a 3R-MYB TF from an edible mushroom plays a positive role in regulating phenylpropanoid metabolism and resistance to P. tolaasii in heterologous Arabidopsis. # Conclusions

This study identified and characterized a 3R type MYB TF (named AbMYB11) involved in MeJA-induced resistance to P. tolaasii in A. bisporus mushrooms, and further demonstrated the potential role of heterologously expressed AbMYB11 in enhancing Arabidopsis resistance to P. tolaasii. Yet, further studies are needed to detail the mechanisms underlying AbMYB11 functions, including its direct targets, using high-throughput approaches, such as genome-wide ChIP-Seq and RNA-Seq. Additionally, given the possibility that AbMYB11 may have other potentially different functions beyond disease resistance, future studies are also needed to comprehensively investigate its roles in edible mushrooms. The current findings highlight the pivotal role of AbMYB11 as a regulator in disease resistance by modulating the phenylpropanoid pathway, thus providing a novel research avenue for the breeding of highly disease-resistant edible mushrooms and plants.
