ABSTRACT: Herbicide-resistant weeds are increasingly a problem in crop fields when exposed to similar chemistry over time. To avoid future yield losses, identifying herbicidal chemistry needs to be accelerated. We screened 50,000 small molecules using a liquidhandling robot and light microscopy focusing on pre-emergent herbicides in the family of cellulose biosynthesis inhibitors. Through phenotypic, chemical, genetic, and in silico methods we uncovered 6-{[4-(2-fluorophenyl)-1-piperazinyl]methyl}-N-(2-methoxy-5- methylphenyl)-1,3,5-triazine-2,4-diamine (fluopipamine). Symptomologies support fluopipamine as a putative antagonist of cellulose synthase enzyme 1 (CESA1) from Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana). Ectopic lignification, inhibition of etiolation, phenotypes including loss of anisotropic cellular expansion, swollen roots, and live cell imaging link fluopipamine to cellulose biosynthesis inhibition. Radiolabeled glucose incorporation of cellulose decreased in short-duration experiments when seedlings were incubated in fluopipamine. To elucidate the mechanism, ethylmethanesulfonate mutagenized M2 seedlings were screened for fluopipamine resistance. Two loci of genetic resistance were linked to CESA1. In silico docking of fluopipamine, quinoxyphen, and flupoxam against various CESA1 mutations suggests that an alternative binding site at the interface between CESA proteins is necessary to preserve cellulose polymerization in compound presence. These data uncovered potential fundamental mechanisms of cellulose biosynthesis in plants along with feasible leads for herbicidal uses. 

KEYWORDS: cellulose synthase, CESA, inhibitor, chemical genomics, plant cell wall, herbicides 

# INTRODUCTION

Weed resistance during field use can evolve after prolonged exposure to the same chemical moiety, but in the case of cellulose biosynthesis inhibitors (CBIs), the incidence of evolved weed resistance has been low.1,2 CBIs have been proven to be chemical genetic research tools in understanding plant cellular expansion and the fundaments of cell wall formation.3 The aim herein was to investigate whether we could use a diverse library of chemicals to isolate CBI compounds that were previously uncharacterized. 

Cell diversity and shape are the basis for tissue form in multicellular organisms and importantly in land plants.4−6 Cellulose is one of the primary load-bearing polymers within the plant cell wall and its alignment of deposition affords regulated plant directional growth.7,8 Interruption of correct cellulosic deposition results in a loss of cellular capacity to sustain internal turgor pressure. This loss of pressure containment capacity results in cellular aberrations and abhorrent cell morphogenesis.9,10 This feature is critical to screening for inhibitors of cellulose biosynthesis because we can exploit reduced expansion in etiolating seedlings as a reproducible and robust phenotype.11 

There are other elements critical for cellular expansion. For instance, the alignment of cellulose microfibrils and the underlying cortical microtubules was illustrated using a yellow fluorescent protein tagged to CESA6 (YFP::CESA6).12 Cortical microtubules are also responsible for the deposition of CESAs within the plasma membrane (PM). Additionally, 

cortical microtubules are associated with small CESAcontaining compartments via microtubule depolymerization within the plant cell.13 POM2/CSI1 [cellulose synthase complex (CSC) accessory proteins], CESA6, and the exocyst complex are facilitators of the delivery of CESAs to the PM.14 POM2/CSI1 and exocyst interactions are mediated by PATROL1 (PTL1).14 To move cellulose from the cytoplasm to the apoplastic space a multimeric protein consisting of CESA1, CESA3, and at least one CESA6-like protein (CESA2, CESA5, CESA6, or CESA9) must aggregate to form the primary cell wall.15,16 CESA1 and CESA3 are critical in the formation of the CSC as null mutations in either are lethal. In contrast, CESA6-like proteins are partially redundant.15,16 Generally, all CESAs contain specific structural features: (1) a cytosolic N-terminal zinc-binding RING-like domain, (2) a catalytic cytosolic domain (family-2 glycosyltransferase) that contains the class-specific region (CSR), (3) seven transmembrane (TM) helices, and (4) a cytoplasmic C-terminal domain.17 The CSR, a plant-specific hyper-variable region, is implicated in facilitating interaction between CESAs. CBIs are small molecules that disrupt the normal physiology of cellulose biosynthesis.11 CBIs are currently categorized into three distinct groups based on how individual CBIs disrupt cellulose biosynthesis physiology. Group 1 CBIs result in the removal of the CSC from the PM focal plane. Group 2 CBIs increase CESA accumulation in the PM but CESA movement 

is severely suppressed.1,3,13,22,23 Lastly, Group 3 CBIs alter CESA trajectory within the cytoplasm and PM.24,25 Due to the demonstrated ability of small molecules to alter molecular pathways within plant biology, we performed a broad screen within a commercially available chemical library to find compounds that act as CBIs.26−31 Fluopipamine was shown to act as a CBI, and we aimed to illustrate the mechanism of action of the molecule. 

# RESULTS

Identification of Potential Chemical Inhibitors of Cellulose Synthesis. A ChemBridge DIVERSet library was screened at $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ and 3271 of the 50,000 compounds caused aberrant phenotypes. Exactly 1,563 of these phenotypes were categorized as either short roots or very short roots (Figure 1B,C).32 All seedlings that exhibited such phenotypes were investigated for the presence of ectopic lignification using the colorimetric phloroglucinol assay (Figure 1D). From this, we identified 33 small molecules induced ectopic lignification at and below a concentration of $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { M }$ (Table S1). Of these 33 small molecules, 13 prevented proper etiolation of Arabidopsis at $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ (not presented). Fluopipamine was selected for further investigation (Figure 2A). 

Radiolabeled Glucose Uptake. Estimating the inhibition of glucose into the cellulose fraction of the plant cell wall is an important assay for CBI identification.33 Seedlings incubated in $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ fluopipamine exhibited a $7 0 \%$ decrease in the incorporation of $[ ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C } ]$ glucose into cellulose when compared to dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) controls (Figure 3). Seedlings incubated in $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { n M } }$ isoxaben, a known Group 1 CBI, displayed an $8 3 \%$ decrease in $[ ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C } ]$ glucose uptake when compared to DMSO controls (Figure 3).

Live Cell Imaging of YFP::CESA6 and GFP::MAP4/ YFP::TUA5 after Exposure to Fluopipamine. Confocal microscopy was employed to visualize the behavior of fluorescently labeled proteins and how they are affected within live plant cells after fluopipamine or mock control exposure. It was proposed that this would help us compare the mode of action to other characterized CBI compounds and determine whether a direct effect on either cellular marker was evident. This experiment was performed not to quantify the intracellular behaviors but to attempt to classify fluopipamine. Three different transgenic fluorescent Arabidopsis lines were used, namely, (1) yellow florescent protein tagged to cellulose synthase A catalytic subunit 6 (YFP::CESA6), (2) green fluorescent protein tagged to a microtubule-associated protein 4 (GFP::MAP4), and (3) yellow florescent protein tagged to 

alpha-tubulin 5 (YFP::TUA5). Seedlings were incubated in either DMSO and media, $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ fluopipamine, $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n M }$ isoxaben, or $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ oryzalin (a known microtubule depolymerizer). 

Images of the YFP::CESA6 reporter in control cells documented typical behavior of linear directional movement of YFP::CESA6 in the PM focal plane, consistent with numerous prior reports (Figure 4A).34 By contrast, accumulation and arrest of YFP::CESA6 at a spatial location coincident with the PM focal plane, creating puncta, occurred within 2 h incubation in fluopipamine (Figure 4B). As expected, based on prior experiments, YFP::CESA6 was cleared from the PM focal plane after incubation in $1 0 \ \mathrm { n M }$ isoxaben for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ and appeared at subcellular locations (Figure 4C).12 The imaging results of fluopipamine interference with YFP::CESA6 are consistent with the classification of DCB.35 DCB causes hyperaccumulation of CESAs at the PM. This evidence supports fluopipamine as Group 2 CBI.3 

Fluopipamine does not interfere with cortical microtubule dynamics. There are differences between the positive (Figure 4D,G) and negative (Figure 4F,I) control images indicating oryzalin’s effectiveness at microtubule depolymerization. However, fluopipamine does not interfere with microtubule polymerization (Figure 4D,E,G,H). These data suggest that fluopipamine does not act on molecular components of cortical microtubule dynamics (Figure 4D−I). 

Fluopipamine-Resistant Variant Identification. Known Arabidopsis CESA mutants: (1) Aegeus (ags), (2) Aegeus and isoxaben resistant mutant (agsixr), isoxaben resistant mutant $\left( i x r l - 2 \right)$ , (4) original name $( p 2 p S )$ , and (5) original name $( p 3 p 4 )$ were tested for resistance against fluopipamine.18,23,36 $p 2 p 5$ (CESA1R292C) displays resistance to fluopipamine at 5 $\mu \mathbf { M }$ . This is well above the calculated $\mathrm { L D } _ { 5 0 }$ (Figure 2B). A root length of $5 . 4 4 \pm \ : 0 . 2 2$ times that of Col-0 is observed when $p 2 p 5$ is exposed to fluopipamine (Figure 5). The root length of $p 3 p 4$ (CESA1G620E) was $1 . 5 4 ~ \pm ~ 0 . 0 9$ times that of Col-0. However, p3p4 does not display resistance to fluopipamine at 5 $\mu \mathbf { M }$ (Figure 5). No other mutant tested achieved significant growth when compared to Col-0 and was therefore not considered to have resistance to fluopipamine (Figure 5). 

Mutagenesis, Breeding, and Loci Underlying Resistance to Fluopipamine. An ethylmethanesulfonate (EMS) mutagenized population of Arabidopsis thaliana (A. thaliana) directed the discovery of a fluopipamine-resistant genotype. Homozygous resistant F2 plants (33) were identified from our breeding pipeline (Figure S1). Next-generation Arabidopsis mapping exposed the back half of chromosome 4 as a region lacking single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) (Figure S2D). This SNP desert highlights two important regions of interest (Figure S2G). These two regions identify a list of candidate genes that could be providing genetic resistance to fluopipamine (Table S2). 

The mutation of a cytosine to a thymine within CESA1 afforded complete cleavage of all gene fragments pulled from the F2 population. This was demonstrated via a cleaved amplified polymorphic sequence (CAPS) assay (Figure 6- 4,4.1). This point mutation resulted in the amino acid substitution of G1009S in CESA1 (CESA1G1009S). This substitution mutation affords an Arabidopsis genotype referred to as fluopipamine-resistant mutation (FPR-M) (Figure S3). This is evidence of another point mutation conferring plant resistance to fluopipamine. All other tested gene fragments were not completely digested, exhibiting incomplete cleavage.

Fluopipamine May Bind to the Interface between CESA Protomers in FPR-M. Several mutations in CESA1 have been shown to induce resistance to flupoxam (CESA1G1009D, CESA1P1010L, CESA1G1013F) and quinoxyphen (CESA1R292C, CESA1G620E, CESA1A903 V).23,36 Here, structural investigation of CESA1 uncovers G1009D and some of the aforementioned points of mutation are located at or near the interface of CESA protomers. Mutations G1009D, P1010L, and G1013F are located within transmembrane helix 6 (TM6). A903 V lies within TM4. In contrast, R292C is located within TM1 and G620E can be found in the cytosolic interface helix that is in contact with TM2 (Figure 7A). Given that the points of mutations are often in contact with the neighboring protomer, it is possible that these mutations form a pocket between protomers, providing an alternative binding site and affording plant resistance. 

To investigate this potential, we pursued docking simulations. Simulations were performed across compounds (fluopipamine, flupoxam, and quinoxyphen) and mutated proteins (CESAR292C, CESA1A903 V, CESA1G1009S, CE-

G1009D CESA1P1010L, CESA1G1013F) and the wild-type protein. When examining the docked results, the wild-type and mutational variants all displayed compounds bound near the active channel at the cytosolic interface. Additionally, there were always compounds bound at the interface between protomers and interestingly the estimated binding affinity in this site is comparable to the 15 top binding modes in the wildtype protein (Figures 7 and S4). 

# DISCUSSION

In the current study, we were aided by several notable factors. First, by using a small annual plant like Arabidopsis to screen for CBIs we could quickly identify chemicals that were implicated in reducing expansion by a simple visual screen. As a result, it was feasible to screen 50,000 small molecules. Second, CBIs attack the plants’ ability to make their main structural polymer, cellulose.11 Therefore, we could screen when the plants were days to weeks rather than months old. Once we identified a candidate lead chemical that caused reduced expansion, we would quickly cross reference the visual phenotype with a battery of tests that link it more closely with the process of cellulose biosynthesis. Additionally, in this study, we took advantage of tools built for Arabidopsis to quickly map resistant loci.39 As a result, we could quickly link plant genomic resistance to the target chemical and therefore inform its potential mechanism of action as an herbicide. We believe that the continued utilization of engineering tools developed for plant biology could improve traditional herbicide screening methods. The engineered tools applied here increase the speed to discovery and focus on investigating emerging phenotypes while traditional spray approaches may miss pre-emergent phenotypes. Using engineering methods, we went from a 50,000 small molecule library to a single compound and then to linked fluopipamine resistance to mutations in the primary cell wall (CESA1R292C, CESA1G1009S). Secondarily, we present the fluopipamine-resistant plant line FPR-M (Figure S3). 

Despite the complex structure of the CSC, the f pr-m allele mapped to CESA1. These data are not surprising because past studies map resistance to herbicides that affect cellulose biosynthesis in plants to the CESA units themselves. To date, CBI resistance has either mapped to the biosynthetic CESAs themselves, as in the case of isoxaben, quinoxyphen, or flupoxam or no resistance has been developed as seen in DCB (dichlobenil) and indaziflam (Bayer TM).11,23,36 These mutations that confer resistance have allowed researchers to tease apart several aspects of cellulose biosynthesis, particularly when combined with structural biology, which was used herein to gain a snapshot of the CSC system and the fluopipamine inhibitory mechanism.18,21,40,41 

The membrane translocation of the crystalline chain of glucan is a remarkable process that has been solved in recent years.42 Here the cytosolic plant conserved region (PCR) in the central domain of a Populus sp CESA protein (PttCesA8) worked in concert with specific TM spanning segments to create a channel for the nascent cellulose polymers. Interestingly, the TM region is a targeted hub for resistance loci to CBIs.11 Strange, as this would plausibly overlap/ interfere with the ability of the channel to excrete cellulose. 

Fluopipamine resistance resulted in a missense mutation in amino acid number 1009, changing glycine to serine $\left( \mathrm { C E S A 1 } ^ { \mathrm { G 1 0 0 9 S } } \right)$ ) (Figure 6-4,4.1). A previous study identified an alternative missense mutation at the same position $\left( \mathrm { C E S A 1 } ^ { \mathrm { G 1 0 0 9 D } } \right)$ providing resistance to flupoxam. Mutations CESA1P1010L and CESA1G1013F also provide resistance to the herbicide flupoxam. Interestingly, all the aforementioned mutations are toward the apoplastic side of the TM helix.43 

In reviewing the literature, a glycine-to-serine substitution mutation was previously linked to channel function in the human ENaC beta subunit sodium channel.44 Additionally, in a study investigating amino acid mutations within human TM proteins that were associated with disease, a glycine to serine or arginine to cytosine substitution was responsible for 1.7 or $1 . 0 8 \%$ of TM protein variants causing disease, respectively.45 Although we have evidence across the literature of these mutations causing disruption in normal physiology and possibly leading to disease, what is not clear is how the plant carrying the resistance loci maintains resistance without loss of channel function. It is possible that in wild-type plants the CBI affects channel morphology and results in polymer secretion disruption. 

Initially, confocal microscopy supported the idea that fluopipamine disrupted CSC functionality (Figure 4). While fluopipamine affected the subcellular localization of the CESA particles distributed between the PM, it is unclear if the SmaCC/MASC and trans-Golgi network (TGN) are inhibited in any way. We were focused on classifying the herbicide rather than investigating cell biology. Therefore, we suggest further research into the impact of fluopipamine on the subcellular dynamics of CESA in living plant cells. 

Additionally, biochemical modeling predicted scores between fluopipamine and CESA1 that could provide a basis for mechanism exploration. Both point mutations (CESA1G1009S and CESA1R292C) that conferred resistance to fluopipamine have also been found to confer resistance to other CBIs. Another possibility is that distant mutations (CESA1G620E) have an indirect effect on the interfacial environment. This indirect effect could result in plant resistance. All assay varieties support the CSC as the target protein complex of fluopipamine. In silico modeling sheds light on the mechanism of action of fluopipamine, flupoxam, and quinoxyphen in the presence of mutant and wild-type CESA1 proteins. These data can be a building block for the design of new chemicals with increased potency, valuable for agricultural and research applications. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

Plant Material and Growth Conditions. A. thaliana Columbia ecotype (Col-0) was used in all portions of the chemical screen. Col-0 was outcrossed with the Landsberg erecta ecotype (Ler) for resistance breeding. Seedlings were stratified for a minimum of 3 days, germinated, and then grown under a light/dark cycle of $1 6 / 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . When etiolated seedlings were required, the plants were grown in $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ 

darkness. Compounds were dissolved in DMSO such that the DMSO concentration never exceeded $0 . 2 \%$ . Seedings were placed in a growth chamber at $2 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . Seedlings were plated on media of $0 . 5 \times$ Murashige and Skoog (MS) mineral salts (Sigma, St. Louis, MO) and $0 . 8 \%$ agar. Seedlings of fluorescent reporters were cultured as described in other studies. 12,13 

Chemical Library. A 50,000 Molecule DIVERSet library (Chembridge, San Diego, CA) in a 96-well format was screened at $1 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ . Each of the small molecules was solubilized in DMSO. The screen was performed as stated in a previous report.32 

Confocal Microscopy. Florescent plant lines were grown on $0 . 5 \times$ MS agar. The duration of growth could span from 2 to 4 days but would be in total darkness and at $2 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Once grown, etiolated seedlings were incubated in a control or drug solution. Incubation occurred for at least $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . Seedlings were mounted as described in previous work.13 Images were obtained with an Olympus 1200 Laser Scanning Confocal microscope using an Olympus UPLSAPO60XW NA:1.20 water-immersion objective. GFP and YFP plants were excited by a laser at $4 8 8 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , and fluorescence was collected through a $5 2 5 / 5 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ band-pass filter (Olympus Life Science). 

Image Analysis. Fiji was used to process all images. Confocal microscopy frames were smoothed using the Walking Average (threeframe) plugin. The average frames were then Z-projected across average intensity to obtain the individual images presented in the article. 

Sequencing Method and Preparation. DNA was purified from each plant following the DNA Purification Kit protocol for Solexa. DNA was isolated, normalized, pooled, and shipped to the Hospital for Sick Children in Toronto, Ontario, Canada. To determine the list of candidate point mutations across the Arabidopsis population, we used the pipeline known as next-generation mapping. 3 

CAPS Marker Assay. Primer design occurred within Primer3. Once the primers were designed they were ordered from Integrated DNA Technologies (Table S3). Primers were designed to target portions of gene sequences identified from the next-generation mapping pipeline (Table S2).39 By design, genes were only susceptible to enzyme cleavage if the mutation was present. Enzymes were selected with NEBcutter V2.0 by identifying those that cleaved the mutated gene but did not cleave the wild type (Table S3). DNA was extracted from all Arabidopsis lines. This DNA was then normalized using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. The normalized DNA was then pooled and amplified via PCR with designed primers. Enzymes were added to the population of DNA after PCR. Next, PCR product concentration was determined using a NanoDrop spec-

trophotometer. Finally, equal concentrations of PCR product were loaded into the gel, and gel electrophoresis allowed visualization of enzyme activity across the population of DNA. 

Radiolabeled Glucose Uptake. A protocol previously developed was followed in this study.33 To promote standard germination seedlings must be sterilized and then vernalized for at least 2 days. After vernalization seeds were placed in an Erlenmeyer flask containing liquid MS media. To promote uniform etiolation, these seeds were then exposed to light for 2 h. Following light exposure, the seeds are grown in the dark on a rotary shaker for 3 days. On day three, the etiolated seedlings are gathered, distributed to even weights for each sample, and then placed into Eppendorf tubes. At this point, the seedlings are incubated in media with $[ ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C } ]$ glucose and either 20 $\mu \mathbf { M }$ fluopipamine, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { \ n M }$ Isoxaben, or no chemical for $^ \mathrm { ~ 2 ~ h ~ }$ . To solubilize noncellulosic material acetic-nitric acid [acetic acid:nitric acid:water (8:1:2)] reagent was added to the tubes. The tubes are then boiled. Finally, the amount of radiolabeled glucose detected indirectly indicates the quantity of cellulose synthesized. 

Phloroglucinol-HCL (Wiesner) Staining. The protocol previously developed was followed in this study.46 Briefly, $_ { 0 . 3 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of phloroglucinol was added to absolute ethanol. This solution was mixed with 1/2 volume concentrated HCl. This solution was added to the wells of plates of interest and a colorimetric change indicates the presence of ectopic lignin. 

Ethylmethanesulfonate Mutagenesis. The protocol previously developed was used in this study.47 Briefly, $2 . 5 \ \mathrm { g }$ of dried seed was weighed and soaked overnight in $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ phosphate buffer at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The following day, phosphate buffer is decanted, and EMS is added to a final concentration of $0 . 4 \%$ . This seed and EMS mixture is incubated for 8 h with gentle nutation and then washed thoroughly with water at least 20 times. Once washed the seed was planted within the greenhouse. 

Protein Structure Prediction and Molecular Docking. Prediction of CESA1 structure occurred via fragment-based modeling within I-TASSER.48 A TM-score of $0 . 7 0 \pm 0 . 1 \bar { 2 }$ supports satisfactory topology. Additionally, the predicted structure significantly overlaps with the solved structure of CESA8 (PDB id 6wlb).42 There is large overlap within the TM regions (Figure S5). 

6wlb contains bound cellopentaose and the protein structure is therefore in the holo (open) conformation. To ensure the holo form of the predicted protein, the coordinates of (W318, W927, F1017, and W1020) were transferred from 6wlb. The holo protein structure was then embedded in a model bilayer of 1-palmitoyl-2-oleoylphosphaditylcholine. These systems (protein, lipid membrane, water, and neutralizing ions $\mathrm { \Delta N a ^ { + } }$ and Cl−) were then energy minimized with the steepest descent using GROMACS.49 CHARMM36m force fields were used for the protein and lipids. TIP3P force fields were used for water. The same procedure was used across all variants of CESA1 and the amino acid substitutions were modeled using CHARMM-GUI. 

Molecular docking was performed using Autodock Vina. This process occurred across compounds and mutant proteins. To assess the predicted model, we performed docking using cellopentaose. There was a good overlap between our protein models cellopentaose and the cellopentaose from 6wlb. Cellopentaose was prepared with GLYCAM web.50 Autodocktools converted protein and compounds to a pdbqt format. The search space was defined as a rectangle with dimensions $5 4 \times 6 2 \times 4 8 \mathrm { ~ \AA ~ } ^ { 3 }$ . This space encompasses the full TM region and TM7 of the neighboring protomer. The exhaustiveness parameter was set to 20.
