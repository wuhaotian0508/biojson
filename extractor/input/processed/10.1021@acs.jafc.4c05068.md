ABSTRACT: Plants convert solar energy and carbon dioxide into organic compounds through photosynthesis. Sucrose is the primary carbonate produced during photosynthesis. Sucrose phosphate synthase (SPS) is the key enzyme controlling sucrose biosynthesis in plants. There are at least three SPS gene families in higher plants, named A, B, and C. However, in monocotyledonous plants from Poaceae, there are at least five SPS gene families, named A, B, C, DIII, and DIV. Each family of SPS genes in different plants shows a divergent expression pattern. So different families of SPS genes participate in diverse biological functions, including sucrose accumulation, plant growth and production, and abiotic stress tolerance. SPS activity in plants is regulated by exogenous factors through gene expression and reversible protein phosphorylation. It is a practicable way to improve crop traits through SPS gene transformation. This work analyzes the cloning, phylogeny, and regulatory mechanism of the SPS gene in plants, reviews its biological function as well as its role in crop improvement, and discusses the challenges and future perspectives. This paper can serve as a reference for further study on plant SPS genes and eventually for crop improvement.

KEYWORDS: sucrose phosphate synthase, sucrose biosynthesis, phosphorylation, transgene, gene family

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Plants play an indispensable role in human life. Through photosynthesis, plants convert solar energy and carbon dioxide to chemical energy to synthesize organic compounds. This process not only serves as the foundation for their own growth but also provides an essential energy source for other organisms in the food chain. Sucrose is the primary carbohydrate produced during photosynthesis in plants and is essential for transporting energy from photosynthetic sources to nonphotosynthetic sinks, which are utilized for various purposes such as food, fiber, feed, and renewable fuels. Sucrose phosphate synthase (SPS) is the key enzyme controlling sucrose biosynthesis in plants. SPS employs UDPG (uridine diphosphate glucose) as a donor and fructose-6-phosphate as an acceptor to catalyze the synthesis of sucrose-6-phosphate. Sucrose-6-phosphate undergoes dephosphorylation and is hydrolyzed by SPP (sucrose phosphate phosphatase) to produce sucrose and phosphate ions (Figure 1).

SPS was initially identified in wheat germ in 1955, and its presence was later found in germinating wheat in 1959 and in rice seeds in 1969. With the expansion of research, it became evident that SPS is widely present in both photosynthetic tissues (mainly leaves) and nonphotosynthetic tissues (such as fruits, storage roots, and tubers).

Recent research indicates that SPS forms either a dimer or a tetramer, composing 2 or 4 subunits. However, the molecular weight and number of subunits in purified SPS vary slightly among crops. In maize and spinach, SPS exists as dimers or tetramers composed of subunits with molecular weights

ranging from 117 to $138\mathrm{kDa}$ . In rice, the molecular weight of SPS subunits ranges from 116 to $120\mathrm{kDa}$ , with an aggregate molecular weight of $420 - 520\mathrm{kDa}$ , suggesting that rice SPS forms a tetramer. However, spinach SPS has a molecular weight of $490\mathrm{kDa}$ and is composed of oligomers consisting of 8 identical subunits with a molecular weight of $60\mathrm{kDa}$ . The observed variations in SPS (sucrose-phosphate synthase) structure across different species are likely attributed to evolutionary adaptations that optimize enzyme function according to the specific physiological and environmental conditions faced by each plant species. The structural differences, such as the formation of dimers or tetramers and variations in subunit molecular weights, may influence the enzyme's kinetic properties, stability, and regulatory mechanisms. These structural variations are indicative of the enzyme's role in adapting to the metabolic or function demands of different species. This explanation aligns with the known diversity in plant metabolic processes and emphasizes the importance of understanding enzyme structure—function relationships in a species-specific context.

# 2. CLONING OF SPS GENES

The cloning of the SPS gene originated in maize. $^{6}$ and was subsequently achieved in spinach. $^{7}$ Following these initial successes, SPS genes have been cloned from sugar beet, citrus, broad bean, rice, sugar cane, Oncidium orchid, maize, melon, cocoa, upland cotton, cassava, and kiwifruit, although in-depth studies on the function of these genes are yet to be conducted. $^{7-22}$

In order to clarify the number of SPS in various plant species, a search for "sucrose phosphate synthase" was conducted on the nonredundant amino acid database available on the NCBI Web site (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) to retrieve all plant SPS sequences. Following retrieval, the sequences were analyzed and duplicate entries were eliminated. A total of 92 plant SPS sequences were collected, of which 58 were determined to be full-length (Table 1). The results indicate that multiple SPS genes exist within the same plant species, and the number of SPS gene displays significant variation among plants (Table 1).

# 3. PATTERN OF SPS GENE FAMILIES

3.1. Phylogeny of SPS Genes. Different plant species contain multiple SPS genes from different gene families. Langenkamper et al.[23] conducted phylogenetic analysis on full-length and conserved sequences of known SPS genes in higher plants, classifying them into three families: A, B, and C. Hu et al.[24] constructed a phylogenetic tree using 177 homologous proteins from 13 species via the neighbor-joining method. The SPS genes could be divided into three subgroups: SPSI, SPSII, and SPSIII.[24] Castleden et al.[25] added in SPS genes from monocotyledonous plants, revealing that dicotyledonous plants possess three families of SPS genes (A, B, and C), while monocotyledonous grasses exhibit five SPS gene families: A, B, C, DIII, and DIV. Following the analysis of the 58 full-length SPS sequences obtained in this study (Table 1), we categorized plant SPS genes into four families: A, B, C, and D (Figure 2). However, we did not observe the possibility of further dividing the D family into two subfamilies. This limitation is attributed to our analysis focusing on only full-length SPS genes, given the relatively small number of genes studied. Castleden et al.'s[25] classification included nonfull-length sequences of SPS genes. Presently, there are only five full-length sequences of D family genes, all originating from monocotyledonous grasses, aligning with findings from earlier studies. This observation suggests that the evolution of D family genes took place subsequent to the divergence of monocotyledonous and dicotyledonous plants.[17]

Different plants have varying numbers of SPS genes from different gene families. The citrus genome contains three categories of SPS genes from the A, B, and C families, respectively. Within the Arabidopsis thaliana genome, four SPS genes are present, with two falling under the A family, while the remaining two are attributed to the B and C families, respectively.[23] The rice genome harbors five SPS genes, with each one falling into a distinct family.[26] Cassava possesses five cloned SPS genes distributed among the A, B, and C families.[21] Lychee contains four SPS genes categorized into three families: A, B, and C.[27] In tomato, SlsPS1 and SlsPS2 are affiliated with the A family, SlsPS3 is the B family, and SlsPS2 is linked to the C family.[28] The maize genome contains at least seven SPS genes, with one in the A, C, and DIII families, and two in both the B and DIV families.[29] Using SPS gene sequences from closely related plants like rice, maize, and wheat as a guide, our team successfully cloned three SPS genes with complete cDNA sequences from sugar cane (NCBI accession numbers: HM854011, JN584485, HQ117935) (Table1), representing the A, B, and DIII families (Figure 2).[19] Overall, there are different amounts of SPS genes in different plants, indicating the significance and complexity of the SPS gene function.

3.2. Expression Pattern of SPS Gene Families. The expression characteristics and functions of SPS genes vary across different families and within different plant species. In maize, the expression levels of ZmSPS1, ZmSPS6, and ZmSPS7 are notably high in leaves, while ZmSPS2 exhibits high expression in both leaves and pollen. Conversely, ZmSPS3, ZmSPS4, and ZmSPS5 display very low constitutive expression levels across various tissues, indicating their role in maintaining the basic sucrose requirements of these tissues.[29]

CitSPS1, a member of the family A SPS genes in citrus, exhibits expression in the edible tissues throughout the entire fruit development period, with transcription levels peaking at maturity (223 days after flowering). CitSPS2, belonging to family B, lacks expression in edible tissues during the early stages of fruit development (before 187 days after flowering) but initiates expression afterward, reaching its peak at maturity (223 days after flowering). Conversely, CitSPS3, belonging to family C, remains unexpressed in edible tissues throughout the entire fruit development period. This observation suggests that CitSPS1 and CitSPS2 play significant roles in sucrose synthesis or accumulation in citrus fruits.[30]

# 4. REGULATION OF SPS ACTIVITY

In contrast to the substantial endeavors invested in the physiological delineation of SPS, only a few investigations have endeavored to furnish proof of their direct transcriptional and post-transcriptional control. Presently, SPS activity regulation involves two mechanisms: gene expression regulation and phosphorylation covalent modification.[36] Additionally, there is speculation regarding the potential regulation of SPS enzyme

activity through allosteric modulation, $^{37}$ although supporting evidence is lacking.

4.1. Regulation by Gene Expression. The expression of the SPS gene is influenced by the growth and development stages of plants. In spinach leaves, the expression of the SPS gene steadily increases from the elongation stage to the maturity stage.[38] In poplar trees, the activity of the SPS enzyme experiences a sharp increase as leaves begin to fall in the autumn, reaching its peak in the winter, coinciding with the period of starch conversion to sucrose. Conversely, in spring, when starch synthesis initiates, the SPS enzyme activity starts to decline, reaching its nadir in summer. The variation in SPS activity correlates with the abundance of SPS protein,[39] suggesting that alterations in SPS activity stem from changes in SPS gene expression.

The expression of the SPS gene is also influenced by metabolites, exogenous substances, and other factors. Glucose induces the expression of the SPS gene in sugar beets, whereas sucrose inhibits it.[8] Knockdown of ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase can inhibit starch synthesis in potatoes, resulting in the accumulation of soluble sugar content in potato tubers and a significant increase in the mRNA level of the SPS gene.[40] Ethylene sharply stimulates the accumulation of the mRNA of the SPS gene in bananas, leading to a significant increase in the content of the SPS enzyme and its activity. Auxin and cold treatment marginally elevate the mRNA level of the SPS gene, whereas injuries suppress its expression.[41]

Light significantly influences the SPS activity. Nevertheless, light affects the SPS enzyme activity at two distinct levels. Prolonged exposure to light enhances SPS enzyme activity by stimulating the expression of the SPS gene.[7,41] Conversely, short-term light exposure, such as daily variations, regulates SPS enzyme activity through phosphorylation modification.[42,43]

Identifying regulatory domains and unraveling underlying mechanisms to regulate and alter the expression of $SPS$ is crucial. However, our knowledge of direct regulators of $SPSs$ remains limited, hindering our ability to discern conserved regulatory pathways.

4.2. Regulation by Phosphorylation Modification. Besides regulation via gene expression levels, at the posttranscriptional level, SPSs undergo regulation as well, potentially resulting in diverse physiological functions. Inactivation of SPS in vivo caused by feeding uncouplers to darkened leaf tissue that had previously been fed mannose in the dark may occur by this mechanism.[44] The SPS enzyme also undergoes reversible phosphorylation modification in response to light/dark signals to modulate its activity.[42,43] In daylight, the SPS enzyme undergoes dephosphorylation, resulting in heightened activity. During this period, the products of photosynthesis are primarily allocated to sucrose synthesis. Conversely, in darkness during the evening, the SPS enzyme undergoes phosphorylation, leading to diminished activity and decreased sucrose synthesis. During this period, the sustenance of life metabolism primarily relies on the degradation of starch for energy production.[45] Light-induced activation of the SPS enzyme typically occurs within $10 - 15\mathrm{min}$ , whereas deactivation in darkness necessitates $20 - 30\mathrm{min}$ .[42]

Light/dark regulation of SPS activity occurs as a result of regulatory serine phosphorylation. # 5. THE BIOLOGICAL FUNCTIONS OF SPS GENES

5.1. SPS Is Positively Correlated with Sucrose Accumulation of Plants. SPS plays a crucial role in the allocation of photosynthetic products between starch and sucrose, with its activity exhibiting a negative correlation with starch accumulation and a significant positive correlation with sucrose accumulation.[34,36,56] Alterations in sucrose concentration in citrus fruits are directly associated with variations in SPS activity.[30] Sucrose content in both sugar cane stems and leaves demonstrates a positive correlation with SPS activ

ity. $^{57,58}$ Throughout the developmental stages, from the embryonic stage to the five-leaf stage, there is a gradual increase in the activities of sucrose synthase and SPS in both supersweet corn and regular corn, with supersweet corn exhibiting higher enzyme activities. $^{59}$ Furthermore, SPS influences the composition of sugar accumulated in fruits, with lychee cultivars primarily accumulating sucrose exhibiting higher SPS activity compared to those primarily accumulating reducing sugars. $^{60}$ However, some studies also showed inconsistent results. The rise in sucrose content in maize leaves was not correlated with the alterations in SPS activity. $^{60}$ Knockout of the $OsSPS1$ gene in rice, leading to decreased SPS activity, does not impact sugar content (starch/sucrose ratio) or overall plant growth (Figure 4). $^{61}$ Collectively, these studies illustrate the varying roles of SPS in sucrose accumulation across the plant realm.

5.2. SPS Participates in Plant Growth, Yield Formation, and Quality. SPS activity in 8 maize varieties with significantly different growth rates demonstrates a positive and significant correlation between SPS activity and growth rate.[62] SPS activity is considered a crucial indicator of high yield for maize hybrid breeding, supported by the significant positive correlation observed between the SPS activity in the fourth leaf of hybrid offspring and yield.[63] SPS activity in high-yield maize varieties obtained after 22 rounds of mixed selection is notably higher compared to that of the original parents.[64]

Melons modified with reduced SPS activity by antisense expression of the SPS gene demonstrate notably smaller leaf size, stem diameter, and plant height in comparison to the wild type.[65] Furthermore, knockout of $SPSa1$ and $SPSc$ in Arabidopsis exhibits severe growth impairment, along with reductions in sucrose content and increases in starch content.[66]

Total soluble sugars and fructose concentrations were about halved in transgenic Arabidopsis thaliana (lack of sucrose phosphate synthase A2) compared with wild-type plants.[67]

Enhanced SPS activity can promote cellulose synthesis, thereby inducing thickening of the secondary wall and ultimately enhancing cotton quality.[68,69]

5.3. SPS Plays an Important Role in Plant Abiotic Stress Tolerance. In response to adverse conditions, such as low temperature, drought, or high salinity, plants often exhibit increased SPS activity, resulting in alterations in the

distribution of carbon assimilates within the plant. This increase in the content of soluble sugars, such as sucrose, serves to alter cell osmotic pressure, thereby mitigating the effects of stressful environments.

Deschampsia antarctica, commonly known as Antarctic hairgrass, is a plant species indigenous to the maritime regions of the Antarctic Peninsula. This plant exhibits an exceptionally high sucrose content in its leaves, reaching up to $36\%$ of the dry weight. Studies suggest that during the long daylight conditions of the Antarctic summer Antarctic hairgrass exhibits exceptionally high SPS activity, resulting in the excess accumulation of sucrose. This adaptation enables the plant to flourish in the harsh, cold climate of the Antarctic.[74] Exposure to low temperatures markedly elevates the transcription levels of the SPS gene in kiwifruit.[75] At lower temperatures, Arabidopsis plants engineered with the maize SPS gene demonstrate heightened photosynthetic activity, thereby leading to enhanced sucrose synthesis and cold resistance in the transgenic plants (Figure 4).[76]

Fescue grass, known for its drought tolerance, following dehydration treatment of fescue grass leaves, both the sucrose content and SPS activity in the leaves exhibit significant increases.[77] Drought stress induces increases in both SPS

activity and sucrose content in rice stems, with the rise in SPS activity showing a significant correlation with the increase in sucrose content in the stems.[78] SPSA2 is involved in drought response via activating the plastid component of the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway in A. thaliana. In growing potato tubers, decreased expression of sucrose phosphate synthase strongly inhibits the water stress-induced synthesis of sucrose.[67]

Mild salt stress can stimulate SPS and SS activity, facilitating the accumulation of sucrose and soluble sugars. This, in turn, alleviates cell osmotic pressure, sustains normal physiological activities, and bolsters plant stress resistance. Nevertheless, under moderate to severe salt stress conditions, SPS and SS activities decrease, sucrose synthesis and accumulation in leaves decrease, and plant growth and development are significantly inhibited.[79] When maize seedlings are cultivated in nutrient solutions with varying concentrations of NaCl, the SPS activity in the seedling leaves increases with the increase of NaCl concentration in the solution. Simultaneously, there is an increase in the sucrose content of the maize seedlings. This suggests that when maize seedlings are exposed to high salt concentrations, an increase in SPS activity is induced, leading to the synthesis of more sucrose inside the cells to increase osmotic pressure and adapt to the high salt environment.[80] Conversely, under cadmium stress, there is a decrease in SPS activity in maize stems and roots with increasing cadmium concentration, resulting in reduced sucrose synthesis and increased hydrolysis.[81]

# 6. GENETIC MODIFICATION THROUGH SPS GENES

Research on SPS transgenic lines was initially undertaken in tomatoes in the early 1990s. Subsequently, research in this field has remained continuous. While SPS gene transformation has been predominantly reported in tomatoes, instances of such transformations have also been documented in potatoes, tobacco, Arabidopsis, rice, poplar, and sugar cane (Table 2).

Numerous SPS genes are present in plants and distributed across various families. Presently, the SPS genes utilized for genetic modification encompass ZmSPS1 from maize, SolSPS from spinach, AtSPS1 from Arabidopsis, and SoSPS1 from sugar cane. ZmSPS1 and SoSPS1 are classified under family B, whereas SolSPS and AtSPS1 are categorized within family A. No transgenic studies have been reported utilizing SPS genes from the C and D families. To date, only two gene promoters, namely the ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase small subunit gene promoter (rbcS) and the cauliflower mosaic virus 3SS promoter (CaMV 3SS), have been employed (Table 2).

Regarding transgenic efficiency[55,70,71,76,82-93] the incorporation of ZmSPS1 into tomatoes, potatoes, tobacco, and rice consistently results in a substantial increase in SPS activity within the leaves of transgenic plants. Transgenic tomatoes exhibit an enhanced fruit quantity, elevated sucrose content in fruits, and accelerated maturation. Transgenic potatoes and rice demonstrate augmented yields. Transgenic tobacco exhibits earlier flowering and a higher abundance of flowers compared to that of wild-type tobacco. Transgenic Arabidopsis displays improved cold resistance. The overexpression of the SoSPS1 gene in sugar cane results in elevated SPS activity and sucrose content in both leaves and stems, enhanced SAI activity, heightened levels of glucose and fructose in leaves and stems, while SS activity remains largely unaffected. Addition-

ally, SPS overexpression in sugar cane results in a noteworthy increase in plant height and stem number in certain transgenic lines. The transfer of the SolSPS gene from spinach to cotton enhances cotton fiber micronaire value, accelerates maturation rate, increases secondary wall thickness of cotton fiber cells, and notably enhances cotton quality. Introducing the AtSPS1 gene from Arabidopsis into tobacco elevates the sucrose content, plant height, stem diameter, and fiber length, consequently leading to a substantial yield increase. Introduction of the AtSPS1 gene from Arabidopsis into poplar enhances the wood fiber length, elevates sucrose content in leaf and stem tissues, postpones leaf senescence in spring, and stimulates bud sprouting (Table 2). These studies underscore the pivotal roles played by SPS genes in plant sucrose synthesis, growth and development, as well as stress resistance.[55,70,71,76,82-93] Nonetheless, the biological function of SPS genes from the C and D families remains unexplored.[55,70,71,76,82-93]

# 7. CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS

Since the discovery of the SPS enzyme, ongoing research has focused on SPS gene cloning, regulatory mechanisms, and the application of transgenic SPS genes, resulting in notable advancements. However, recent research has predominantly emphasized SPS gene cloning and transgenic applications, with comparatively less emphasis on fundamental and comprehensive investigations. The existence of multiple SPS gene families within a single plant species adds complexity to the study of the key enzyme governing sucrose synthesis, SPS. Thus, future research on SPS might focus on enhancing efforts in three aspects.

7.1. The Expression and Functional Characteristics of Different Families of SPS Genes in Plant Require Further In-Depth Research. Higher plants harbor multiple SPS genes across various families, with each family demonstrating unique tissue-specific expression patterns, suggesting specific functional roles for each family. Nonetheless, the roles of citrus A-family SPS genes (e.g., CitSPS1) and B-family SPS genes (e.g., CitSPS2) in sucrose synthesis and accumulation in fruits were initially elucidated. Meanwhile, tobacco C-family SPS genes (e.g., NtSPSC) play a role in starch transport under nonphotosynthetic conditions, thereby contributing to sucrose synthesis. Comprehensive conclusions regarding the expression and functional characteristics of D-family SPS genes, as well as the variability in expression and functional characteristics within the same family of SPS genes across different plant species, are currently lacking. Consequently, further evidence is required to elucidate the tissue-specific expression patterns of SPS genes across various families and ultimately unravel the functional characteristics unique to each family of SPS genes. For instance, structural and computational analyses could be undertaken to unveil potential regulatory modes of SPS. Additional endeavors are warranted to pinpoint the responsible regulatory domains and elucidate the underlying mechanisms. Techniques such as yeast one-hybrid screening coupled with single-cell sequencing and other high-throughput methods like ribosome sequencing (RiboSeq) or translating ribosome affinity purification sequencing (TRAP-Seq) hold promise in facilitating this endeavor.

7.2. The Regulatory Mechanisms of SPS Have Not Been Fully Elucidated. Presently, the regulation of SPS activity encompasses two primary mechanisms: (1) regulation of gene expression and (2) covalent modification through

phosphorylation. Nevertheless, the precise mechanisms governing these two modes of regulation remain elusive. For example, understanding how alterations in growth stages modulate the expression of SPS genes and elucidating the intricate pathways by which light/dark signals regulate the phosphorylation of SPS enzymes represent complex regulatory networks, demanding thorough investigation and detailed research. Developing new yeast strains or systems is still crucial for the convenient analysis of SPS transport activity. Identifying the regulatory kinases is imperative to gain new insights into how these modifications impact biological function. Understanding whether phosphorylation can alter the kinetics of SPSs and determining the conditions that trigger these modifications are essential questions to address. Additionally, investigating whether these interactions also occur in planta and their potential effects on biological functions would be valuable.

7.3. Further Research on Transgenic SPS Genes Is Warranted. Although transgenic studies have demonstrated a significant enhancement of SPS activity, leading to an increased sucrose content, yield improvement, quality enhancement, or stress resistance in transgenic plants, it is essential to acknowledge the presence of multiple SPS genes across different plant families. Presently, transgenic research predominantly centers on ZmSPS1 of the B family in maize, AtSPS1 of the A family in Arabidopsis, and SolSPS of the A family in spinach. No reports have emerged regarding transgenic investigations employing SPS genes from the C and D families. It remains uncertain whether these genes would elicit analogous effects or possibly engender unforeseen outcomes. Consequently, additional comprehensive investigations into the utilization of SPS genes from diverse families in transgenic experiments are justified. Furthermore, the constitutive expression of SPSs may not necessarily lead to gains, as outcomes depend on the specific tissue expression and subcellular localization of the targeted SPSs. Additionally, manipulating the expression of multiple SPSs concurrently through master regulators could prove to be an effective strategy for enhancing yields, although further research is needed to identify such regulators. Hence, targeted ectopic expression of regulators might represent a more favorable strategy compared to the coactivation of other genes involved in the process.

Taken together, numerous critical questions persist regarding the correlation between the molecular structures and functions of SPS, their gene and protein regulation, and the translation of fundamental research into enhanced crop yields. Currently, there is a dearth of information regarding the function and expression patterns of SPS at both the subcellular and whole-plant levels, impeding our capacity to forecast how modifying SPS expression patterns might enhance yields. Given our limited comprehension of SPS regulation, an iterative approach between generating transgenic lines and employing high-throughput phenotyping may be imperative to systematically alleviate pathway bottlenecks under diverse environmental conditions.
