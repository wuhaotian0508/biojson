# Abstract

Obligate biotrophic powdery mildew fungi infect a wide range of economically important plants. These fungi often deliver effector proteins into the host tissues to suppress plant immunity and sustain infection. The phytohormone salicylic acid (SA) is one of the most important signals that activate plant immunity against pathogens. However, how powdery mildew effectors interact with host SA signalling is poorly understood. Isochorismatase (ISC) effectors from two other filamentous pathogens have been found to inhibit host SA biosynthesis by hydrolysing isochorismate, the main SA precursor in the plant cytosol. Here, we identified an ISC effector, named EqIsc1, from the rubber tree powdery mildew fungus Erysiphe quercicola. In ISC enzyme assays, EqIsc1 displayed ISC activity by transferring isochorismate to 2,3- dihydro-2,3-dihydroxybenzoate in vitro and in transgenic Nicotiana benthamiana plants. In EqIsc1-expressing transgenic Arabidopsis thaliana, SA biosynthesis and SAmediated immune response were significantly inhibited. In addition, we developed an electroporation-mediated transformation method for the genetic manipulation of E. quercicola. Inoculation of rubber tree leaves with EqIsc1-silenced E. quercicola strain induced SA-mediated immunity. We also detected the translocation of EqIsc1 into the plant cytosol during the interaction between E. quercicola and its host. Taken together, our results suggest that a powdery mildew effector functions as an ISC enzyme to hydrolyse isochorismate in the host cytosol, altering the SA biosynthesis and immune response.

# 1  |  INTRODUCTION

Pathogenic microorganisms such as fungi, oomycetes and bacteria secrete a series of effector proteins into host plant tissues, interfering with the plant immune system to achieve successful infection (Pelgrom et al., 2020; Robin et al., 2018). Powdery mildew fungi are obligate biotrophic parasites that infect many economically important plant species. If they successfully penetrate the plant cell wall, they form haustoria for nutrient uptake (Hückelhoven, 2005; Hückelhoven & Panstruga, 2011). Each haustorium is surrounded by a plant plasma membrane, known as the extrahaustorial membrane. The haustorium plays an important role in nutrient uptake and plant invasion (Kwaaitaal et al., 2017). At later stages, these fungi produce many hyphae and spores, forming thick colonies on the leaf surface (Hückelhoven & Panstruga, 2011). During infection, phytopathogenic microbes secrete effector proteins to aid infection by suppressing or evading the host immune response, and powdery mildew effector candidates have been identified by genomic and proteomic analyses (Bourras et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2018; Wicker et al., 2013). 

However, these fungi cannot grow in artificial culture media; thus, traditional gene manipulation methods for model fungi are not suitable for them (Margaritopoulou et al., 2022; Martínez-Cruz et al., 2022). Instead, host-induced gene silencing (HIGS) methods have been applied to investigate the functions of effectors with chitinase activity, allowing an understanding of the molecular mechanisms of their pathogenesis (Martínez-Cruz et al., 2021; Nowara et al., 2010). Notably, HIGS is not suitable for a fungus living in a host plant of which the transformation protocol is not available and has limitations in terms of foreign or modified gene expressions in fungal cells. Additionally, a set of transformation methods, including electroporation- and Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and transformation via growth on tissues infiltrated with Agrobacterium, have been established for the powdery mildew fungus Podosphaera xanthii, although only a small number of genes, such as green fluorescent protein (GFP) and the carbendazim-resistant gene $P f T u b ^ { E 1 9 8 A }$ , have been tested using these transformation methods (Martínez-Cruz et al., 2017, 2018; Vela-Corcía et al., 2015). Although the transformation of other obligate biotrophic fungi has not yet been reported, the studies on P. xanthii imply the possibility of such transformation. 

Rubber trees (Hevea brasiliensis) are infected by the powdery mildew fungus Erysiphe quercicola (also called Oidium heveae) every year, causing yield losses. Natural rubber is a fundamental industrial material produced by trees, and E. quercicola has become a major threat to its production worldwide (Liyanage et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2019). Thousands of genes encoding putative secreted proteins, including effectors, have been identified in E. quercicola via genomic analysis. In previous studies, the overexpression of several putative E. quercicola effectors in the non-host plant Nicotiana benthamiana increased its susceptibility to pathogens (He et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020); however, these results do not directly reveal the role of effectors in pathogenicity or their modes of action. Clarifying the functions of these effectors in pathogenesis remains challenging. 

The phytohormone salicylic acid (SA) mediates plant immunity against semibiotrophic or biotrophic microbial pathogens. When the immune receptors in plants detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns or avirulence effectors, the SA content is elevated to activate the immune response (Peng et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2018). SA is mainly synthesized by isochorismate synthase (ICS), which converts chorismite into isochorismate. Isochorismate and glutamic acid form isochorismate-9-glutamate, which subsequently decomposes to SA (Ding & Ding, 2020). Several effectors of microbial pathogens have been reported to target SA signalling. The soybean oomycete pathogen Phytophthora sojae and the vascular wilt fungus Verticillium dahliae employ isochorismatase (ISC)-like effectors to reduce the isochorismate content in host plant cells through hydrolytic activity, which weakens the contribution of ICS to SA biosynthesis (Liu et al., 2014). ISC family proteins (ISCs) have also been found in bacteria such as Escherichia coli (Drake et al., 2006; Ouellette et al., 2022) and Vibrio cholerae (Liu et al., 2012), in the rice blast fungus Magnaporthe oryzae (Dean et al., 2005), and in the root nematodes Hirschmanniella oryzae (Bauters et al., 2014) and Meloidogyne incognita (Qin et al., 2022). 

Little is known about how powdery mildew fungi interfere with SA-mediated immunity in plants. A putative secreted effector, EqIsc1, carrying the ISC domain, has been identified in the secretome of E. quercicola (Liang et al., 2018). This putative effector appears to lack a signal peptide; thus, it is different from candidate-secreted effector proteins that contain signal peptides and have no homologues in non-powdery mildew species. We developed an electrotransformation method for E. quercicola that could induce EqIsc1 gene silencing and labelled EqIsc1 in E. quercicola with a fluorescence tag. We provide evidence that EqIsc1 is delivered by haustoria and translocated into the host cytoplasm to hydrolyse isochorismate, which affects host SA biosynthesis. Our results suggest that the powdery mildew fungus also adopts an ISC mechanism to achieve pathogenicity and that ISC is a potential target for the powdery mildew control. 

# 2  |  RESULTS

# 2.1  |  An electrotransformation method for E. quercicola was developed

To develop an electrotransformation method for E. quercicola, we first attempted to identify a selective agent to which E. quercicola, but not H. brasiliensis, was sensitive. Hygromycin B, geneticin and the methyl benzimidazole carbamate fungicide carbendazim were tested. After spraying aqueous solutions of these chemical compounds onto H. brasiliensis leaves, severe damage to the H. brasiliensis leaf tissues was caused by the application of hygromycin B and geneticin, but not carbendazim (Figure S1a). The E198A mutation in Tub2 leads to the resistance of powdery mildew fungi to carbendazim (Vela-Corcía et al., 2014; Vela-Corcía et al., 2015), and E. quercicola Tub2 did not acquire this mutation (Figure S1b). Carbendazim clearly inhibited E. quercicola growth. Fungal penetration and growth were completely 

inhibited in the presence of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ carbendazim (Figure S1c,d). Therefore, carbendazim can be used as a selective agent against E. quercicola. 

Electrotransformation was performed on E. quercicola. We constructed plasmids carrying the exogenous genes encoding β- glucuronidase (GUS), green fluorescent protein (GFP) and GFP-fused E. quercicola proteins, including GFP-EqVps21 (Gerrard et al., 2000) and EqCmu-GFP. In addition, the promoter of the E. quercicola RP60 (ribosomal protein 60) gene was used to drive the expression of these transgenes. This ribosomal protein gene is homologous to M. oryzae ribosomal protein 27 gene, of which the constitutive promoter RP27 has been used to control transgene expression in M. oryzae (Bruno et al., 2004). Podosphaera fusca TUB2 gene with E198A mutation $( P f T u b ^ { E 1 9 8 A } )$ (Vela-Corcía et al., 2014; Vela-Corcía et al., 2015) has been used as a carbendazim-resistance gene. E. quercicola spores were mixed with these plasmids and electroporated. The spores were then allowed to infect H. brasiliensis leaves, and carbendazim was applied to screen the transformants. Southern blot analysis indicated that the transferred genes were randomly inserted into the fungal genome because GUS or GFP transferred into two independent transformants were present in different digested DNA sequences after transformant genomic digestion by EcoRV (Figure S2a). In addition, transformants with GUS displayed blue colour in the GUS activity assay, and transformants with GFP or GFP-fused protein genes displayed fluorescent signals in the hyphae, spores and haustoria (Figure S2b,c). GFP-EqVps21, a putative Rab5-related GTPase, showed vacuole-like localization (Gerrard et al., 2000). EqCmu-GFP, a putative secreted effector protein (He et al., 2021), was distributed in the cytoplasm and the periphery of the haustoria, suggesting that EqCmu may be secreted by the fungus (Figure 1a). Western blotting indicated that the recombinant proteins were correctly expressed in E. quercicola (Figure 1b). Notably, the RP27 promoter from M. oryzae functions as well as RP60 in driving GUS and GFP expression, as GUS activity and GFP signals were observed in the transformants (Figure S3). 

We tested whether transferring RNA interference (RNAi) plasmids into E. quercicola could induce gene silencing. EqSac6, similar to the yeast actin-associating protein gene Sac6, and two conserved MAPK genes, EqFus3 and EqSlt2 (Chen & Thorner, 2007), were chosen as targets. The GFP gene carried by the RNAi plasmids was also used to label the transformants. At 7 days post-inoculation (dpi), the transcript levels of target genes were decreased by approximately $80 \%$ in transformants with RNAi plasmids compared to those in the wild-type strain and transformants with GFP plasmid (GFP strain) (Figure 2a). Gene silencing significantly inhibited hyphal growth and infection, and EqFUS3 silencing caused the most severe inhibitory effect (Figure 2b–e). Thus, electrotransformation is suitable for gene manipulation in E. quercicola. 

# 2.2  |  EqIsc1 contributes to infection

We investigated whether E. quercicola is involved in an ISC-associated mechanism that disrupts the host SA pathway. To search for potential 

ISC proteins in E. quercicola, we performed NCBI BLAST by aligning 20 identified ISC amino acid sequences against the sequences in the E. quercicola protein database. Only an ISC homologue (EqIsc1, GenBank accession: QKX08450.1) was found in E. quercicola, displaying $2 0 . 6 9 \%$ , $2 8 . 1 2 \%$ , $2 6 . 6 7 \%$ and $4 3 . 3 3 \%$ similarity with E. coli EntB, V. cholerae VibB, VdIsc1 and PsIsc1, respectively. EqIsc1 is more similar to the ISCs from Erysiphe (Figure S4a). EqIsc1 had a molecular weight of $2 3 \mathsf { k D a }$ and contained a conserved ISC domain (Figure S4c). SignalP v. 4.1 analysis predicted that EqIsc1 has no signal peptides, but SecretomeP v. 2.0 (http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/ services/SecretomeP/) (Bendtsen et al., 2004) predicted that it is an unconventional secreted protein, similar to VdIsc1 and PsIsc1 (Figure S4b,d). Furthermore, using the SWISS-MODEL server (https://swissmodel.expasy.org), we identified the protein structure homology between EqIsc1 and both PsIsc1 and VsIsc1 (Figure S4e). 

The plasmids carrying the EqIsc1-RNAi sequence and the control GFP were introduced into E. quercicola by electrotransformation. Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) analysis was performed to determine EqIsc1 transcript levels at 7 dpi (Figure 3a). Compared with those in the wild-type and GFP strain, the EqIsc1 transcript levels in the transformants with EqIsc1 RNAi plasmid were reduced by $8 1 . 7 \pm 0 . 5 \%$ at 7 dpi. The lesion area formed by the EqIsc1-silenced strain was reduced by $2 0 . 1 \pm 2 . 2 \%$ (Figure 3b,c). Additionally, the haustorium formation rate in the EqIsc1-silenced strain was significantly reduced. The number of haustoria per $0 . 3 \mathsf { m m } ^ { 2 }$ in the EqIsc1-silenced strain was only $1 2 . 0 \pm 6 . 0 \%$ , whilst that in the wild-type or GFP strain was $1 8 9 \pm 3 1 . 4 \%$ or $1 7 2 { \pm } 1 2 3 . 5 \%$ . Plant NADPH oxidases such as RBOHD are the critical regulators of pathogen-associated molecular pattern- and effector-triggered immunity (Yuan et al., 2021). When H. brasiliensis leaves were treated with NADPH oxidase inhibitor diphenyleneiodonium (DPI) to compromise plant immunity, the pathogenicity of the EqIsc1-silenced strain effectively returned to the same level as the wild-type or GFP strain (Figure 3d,e). These results suggest that EqIsc1 is an important virulence factor. 

# 2.3  | EqIsc1 has a function in suppressing host immune response

We determined the defence level of H. brasiliensis upon infection with the EqIsc1-silenced strain at $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ post-inoculation (hpi). The results showed that, compared with wild-type and GFP transformants, callose deposition in rubber leaves inoculated with EqIsc1- silenced strain was significantly increased, indicating that EqIsc1 silencing elicits callose deposition in plants (Figure 3f). We used 3,3'-diaminobenzidine (DAB) staining and aniline blue staining to detect the reactive oxygen species (ROS) burst in rubber leaves inoculated with E. quercicola transformants and the level of ROS burst (Figure 3f). The DAB-stained area in the rubber leaves inoculated with EqIsc1 RNAi transformants was $1 6 . 1 \pm 0 . 8 \%$ , whilst those in the GFP strain and E. quercicola wild-type inoculated leaves were $1 . 4 \% \pm 0 . 1 \%$ and $1 . 2 \% \pm 0 . 1 \%$ , respectively (Figure 3g). # 2.5  |  EqIsc1 is a secreted protein

We attempted to verify whether EqIsc1 was secreted using a yeast invertase secretion assay. The yeast YTK12 strain lacks sucrose invertase and cannot grow on CMD − W medium, which contains sucrose and glucose but not Trp (Figure S8a). The pSUC2 vector carries yeast invertase. Therefore, by transferring the empty pSUC2 vector into the YTK12 strain, the transformants could grow on the CMD − W medium but not on the YPRAA medium (which contains raffinose as the sole carbon source). After inserting the full-length EqIsc1 cDNA or the sequence encoding the first 50 amino acids from its N-terminus into the pSUC2 vector, the transformants grew on both CMD − W and YPRAA. The signal peptide of a known secreted protein, Avr1b (Dou et al., 2008), was used as a positive control (Figure S8b). This result indicates that the EqIsc1 protein and its first 50 N-terminal amino acids can effectively guide the expression of sucrose invertase in the pSUC2 vector and secrete it into the YPRAA culture medium, converting raffinose into glucose that the cells can utilize. Moreover, the EqIsc1 protein and its first 50 N-terminal amino acids could also secrete sucrose invertase into a $0 . 1 \%$ 2,3,5-triphenyltetrazolium chloride (TTC) staining solution, turning the TTC solution from colourless to red (Figure S8c). Thus, EqIsc1 is a secreted protein. 

To determine whether the expression of EqIsc1 in E. quercicola was related to secretion, we electroporated the EqIsc1-GFP fusion protein and unfused GFP protein into E. quercicola (Figure 5a). Fungal colonies were examined by confocal laser scanning microscopy at 7 dpi. Fluorescent signals were detected in the fungal spores, haustoria and hyphae of the GFP and EqIsc1-GFP transformants. Interestingly, when FM4-64 was used as a membrane marker, EqIsc1-GFP, but not GFP, aggregated at the periphery of the spores, hyphae and haustoria (Figure 5b,c). We speculate that this localization of EqIsc1 may be related to secretion by E. quercicola. 

# 2.6  |  EqIsc1 can be translocated into plant cells after secretion

We hypothesized that EqIsc1 functions in host plant cells. We tested whether EqIsc1 could be translocated into plant cells during infection. 

The proteins secreted from the fungus are not sufficient for a localization analysis. We exogenously supplied recombinant EqIsc1 protein to the fungus during the infection stage. His-tagged EqIsc1 (EqIsc1-His) was purified from E. coli and used to treat E. quercicola colonies with 5 days of growth. EqIsc1-His and the control GFP-His were detected outside the haustoria and hyphae in the immunofluorescence assay (Figure 6a), suggesting these recombinant proteins infiltrated into the interfaces between H. brasiliensis and E. quercicola. Then we isolated the protoplasts from the leaf tissues with a recombinant protein treatment, and analysed protoplast proteins using western blotting. The results showed that EqIsc1-His and PsIsc1-His were detected (Figure 6b). An RT-PCR analysis showed that E. quercicola EF-1a gene was not detected in RNA samples extracted from these protoplasts (Figure 6c), suggesting that these protoplast samples were not mixed with the fungus. We also extracted the cytosol and plasma membrane fractions of these protoplasts and applied western blotting to analyse these fractions. The results showed that both EqIsc1-His and GFP-His were detected in the plasma membrane fraction (Figure 6d). Moreover, EqIsc1-His and PsIsc1-His, but not GFP-His, were detected in the cytosolic fraction (Figure 6d). These results indicate that the EqIsc1 was probably delivered into the plant cells. 

# 2.7  |  EqIsc1 displays ISC activity

Characterized ISCs, such as PsIsc1 and VdIsc1, can catalyse the hydrolysis of isochorismate to 2,3-dihydro-2,3-dihydroxybenzo ate (DDHB). We purified EqIsc1-His and other controls, including PsIsc1-His and GFP-His, and assayed their activity in isochorismate hydrolysis following a described method (Liu et al., 2012, 2014) (Figure 7a). We applied (His-tagged) EntC enzyme (Figure 7a) (Liu et al., 2012) to produce isochorismate by catalysing the conversion of chorismate to isochorismate. To measure the content of DDHB produced from isochorismate hydrolysis, (His-tagged) VibA (Figure 7a) (Liu et al., 2012) was applied to catalyse the conversion of DDHB to NADH followed by the detection of the absorbance of NADH at $3 4 0 \mathsf { n m }$ . It was revealed that both EqIsc1-His and PsIsc1- His, but not GFP-His, displayed high ISC activity in hydrolysing isochorismate (Figure 7b). 

We also measured DDHB levels in the total extracts of N. benthamiana leaves transiently expressing EqIsc1-GFP and controls, including monomeric GFP and PsIsc1-GFP (Figure 7c). We found that DDHB levels in the presence of EqIsc1-GFP and PsIsc1-GFP were at least three times higher than those in the presence of GFP (Figure 7d). Therefore, EqIsc1 exhibits ISC activity in N. benthamiana leaf cells. Taken together, these results suggest that EqIsc1 decreases SA levels by hydrolysing isochorismate in host cells.

In a study on PsIsc1, Phytophthora sojae was allowed to express the PsIsc1-Avr1b fusion protein (PsIsc1 fused with the C-terminal Avr1b) (Liu et al., 2014). Once P. sojae had delivered the PsIsc1-Avr1b to the host cells, Avr1b was recognized by the cytosolic resistance receptor Rps1b. Based on the resistance phenotype of the host plant, the host cell entry of PsIsc1 can be determined (Liu et al., 2014). Moreover, immunofluorescence and immunoaffinity gold mark methods have been applied to label the effectors of some fungi, such as U. maydis, rust and powdery mildew fungi (Jaswal et al., 2021; Ökmen et al., 2021; Schurack et al., 2021; Wawra et al., 2017). The effectors can be detected in the haustoria, extrahaustorial membrane and host cytosol (Cygan et al., 2020; Polonio et al., 2021; Stuer et al., 2023). Interestingly, clathrinmediated endocytosis in plants has been found to play an important role in effector translocation into plant cells (Oliveira-Garcia et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). In this study, we observed EqIsc1 translocation into the plant cells of H. brasiliensis, as described above. The effector translocation observation methods used in this study may be suitable for other pathosystems. Different pathogenic microbes may involve diverse, unrelated pathogenic mechanisms; therefore, the pathway used by EqIsc1 to enter host cells may be

Here, we show that EqIsc1 directly interrupts host SA biosynthesis and the immune response by its ISC activity to attenuate intracellular isochorismate homeostasis. When H. brasiliensis leaves were treated with SA and isochorismate, the resistance to E. quercicola was enhanced, suggesting a conserved role for SA in mediating plant immunity. In our ISC activity assays, EqIsc1 and the positive control PsIsc1 displayed similar activities in the transformation of isochorismate into DDHB. We detected an increase in DDHB following the incubation of EqIsc1-His or PsIsc1-His with isochorismate in vitro. This increase in DDHB was also observed in transgenic N. benthamiana leaves expressing EqIsc1-GFP or PsIsc1- GFP. In H. brasiliensis leaves inoculated with an EqIsc1-silenced strain, the SA content was significantly increased by at least $50 \%$ , coupled with elevations in the transcript levels of HbICS2 (Zhang & Li, 2019), HbNPR1 (Chu et al., 2022; Zavaliev et al., 2020) and HbPR1 (Khunjan et al., 2016). This increase in host SA in response to the EqIsc1-silenced strain also reflects the entry of EqIsc1 into the host cell to disturb SA biosynthesis. Additionally, because the basal defence of H. brasiliensis was compromised by inhibiting NADPH oxidases after DPI treatment, the reduced pathogenicity 

of the EqIsc1-silenced strain was restored, further substantiating the idea that EqIsc1 acts as an effector rather than a fungal growth regulator. 

EqIsc1 functions were also studied using A. thaliana. In A. thaliana transgenic lines expressing EqIsc1-GFP, flg22- and nlp20- induced immune response and SA biosynthesis were suppressed. flg22 and nlp20 are recognized by LRR-receptor kinase FLS2 and LRR-receptor protein RLP23, respectively (van Butselaar et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2019; Zhou & Zhang, 2020). FLS2 connects with downstream NADPH oxidase RbohD and mitogenactivated protein kinase (MAPK) cascades (Zhou & Zhang, 2020). RLP23 activates EDS1-PAD4 dimers (Pruitt et al., 2021). The pathways that involve RbohD, MAPKs and EDS1-PAD4 are required for immune signalling (Zhou & Zhang, 2020). Notably, a study report that flg22-induced ROS accumulation was unaffected in the SA-deficient sid2 (ics1) mutant, the SA insensitive npr1-1 npr4-4D double mutant and the DMR6/DLO1 overexpression lines that contain lower SA level; whereas ROS accumulation induced by nlp24 (similar to nlp20) was significantly affected in these lines (van Butselaar et al., 2024). These results suggest that SA primarily enhances RLP23-mediated response. Based on that study, only nlp20-induced response inhibition by EqIsc1 may be attributed to a low SA level in A. thaliana. Additionally, a possible explanation of how EqIsc1 inhibited flg22-induced response is that the products from EqIsc1 catalysis, such as DDHB, may reduce the expression levels of signalling proteins that respond to flg22. 

In addition to ISC effectors, Cmu is another effector that can directly alter host SA biosynthesis pathways. The U. maydis Cmu effector (Cmu1) inhibits chorismate–isochorismate–SA flow by converting chorismate into prephenate (Djamei et al., 2011). Our and other studies on ISC and CMU effectors indicate that diverse biotrophic or semibiotrophic microbes target common ancestors of SA to suppress host immunity. We also propose that enhanced SA biosynthesis by genetic manipulation or exogenous elicitor application may confer resistance to those pathogenic microbes in plant breeding. However, at present applying these methods in field disease control may face obstacles such as the lack of efficient genetic manipulation approaches to a non-model plant and the high cost of elicitor application. 

In this study, we did not identify more selective agents for the transformation besides carbendazim; therefore transformation of E. quercicola with two isolated vectors seems to be impossible. Additionally, it is unclear why the phenylalanine ammonia-lyase pathway (Ding & Ding, 2020), another SA biosynthesis pathway, cannot supply sufficient SA content when the ICS pathway is interrupted by EqIsc1 or other ISC effectors, and why other effectors cannot substitute ISC effectors to suppress SA-mediated immunity. We may search for more selective agents for the transformation and investigate the manner by which EqIsc1 cooperates with other effectors temporally and spatially in the next study.

# 4.3  |  Plasmid construction

The plasmids used for the electrotransformation of E. quercicola were constructed using the pJNARG vector as the backbone (Figure S9). To construct a plasmid containing RP60-GUS, the RP27 promoter sequence was cleaved using KpnI and EcoRI restriction enzymes, and the RP60 and GUS cDNA fusion sequences were fused into the cleaved site using T4 ligase (TaKaRa). To construct plasmids expressing EqCmu-GFP (GenBank accession: MK391402.1), the EqCmu-encoding sequence, together with the native promoter, was inserted into a region containing KpnI and EcoRI restriction sites in the vector using T4 ligase. To construct the plasmid used to express GFP-EqVps21, the RP27 promoter was replaced with the native promoter of EqVPS21 and the EqVPS21 coding sequence was inserted into the BamHI restriction site using an In-Fusion Snap Assembly Master Mix (TaKaRa). To construct the RNAi plasmids, gene silencing sequences homologous to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ -terminal of the target gene were inserted into the EcoRI restriction site to ligate the RP27 promoter using an infusion cloning kit. To introduce $P f T U B 2 ^ { \mathsf { E 1 9 8 A } }$ into these plasmids, the EqTUB2 promoter and $P f T U B 2 ^ { \mathsf { E 1 9 8 A } }$ coding sequences were ligated by overlapping PCR and inserted into the XcmI restriction site using T4 ligase (ThermoFisher). The plasmids used to analyse the enzyme activity of EqIsc1 were constructed using the pET-28a 

vector as the backbone. Using T4 ligase, EqIsc1 and PsIsc1 coding sequences were inserted into the region containing the BamHI and XhoI restriction sites in the pET-28a vector. The plasmids used for the transformation of N. benthamiana and A. thaliana were constructed using the pBin4-GFP vector as the backbone. The complete DNA sequence of the pBin4-GFP vector and the associated map schematic are provided in the Supplementary Materials. The encoding sequences were inserted into the BamHI site of the pBin4-GFP vector containing the CaMV 35S promoter, using In-Fusion Snap Assembly Master Mix (TaKaRa). The primers used to amplify these DNA sequences are listed in Table S1, and the associated E. quercicola DNA sequences used to construct the plasmids are listed in Table S2. 

# 4.4  |  Pathogenesis assay

Erysiphe quercicola spores, with or without electroporation, were suspended in double-distilled water. For inoculation to each site on the H. brasiliensis leaves, the spore suspension ${ 1 0 ^ { 6 } }$ spores/mL) was dropped onto the leaf surface. After the water in the spore suspension had evaporated from the inoculated sites, the carbendazim solution (20 or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ was dropped onto those same sites to cause selection pressure. To treat leaves with diphenyleneiodonium (DPI), DPI solution $( 1 0 \mu \mathsf { M } )$ ) was sprayed onto the leaf surface at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ pre-inoculation. At 7 dpi, the inoculated leaves were inspected and photographed. For microscopic analysis of the infection, $2 0 0 \mu \ L$ of the spore suspension $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ spores/mL) was sprayed onto the surface of a leaf using a spray flask. After the water in the spores suspension had evaporated, the carbendazim solution $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } )$ was evenly sprayed onto the inoculated leaves. At 7 dpi, the inoculated leaves were examined under a fluorescence microscope (Y-TV55; Nikon). Aniline blue staining was used to measure hyphal growth as the number of haustoria per unit. Soaked rubber leaves were inoculated with silenced E. quercicola in a decolourization solution (3:1 vol/vol ethanol:acetic acid) and decolourized at room temperature for 3–4 h to remove the chlorophyll. The leaves were then placed in aniline blue solution $_ { ( 0 . 2 g }$ aniline blue, $1 \mathrm { g } \mathsf { K } _ { 2 } \mathsf { H P O } _ { 4 }$ and ${ 2 0 } \mathrm { m L }$ double-distilled water) and stained for $0 . 5 \mathrm { h }$ in the dark at room temperature. The number of hyphal haustoria was observed and counted under a bright-field microscope channel (Y-TV55). 

# 4.5  |  Electrotransformation

An electrotransformation method for E. quercicola was developed based on P. xanthii (Vela-Corcía et al., 2015). A comparison between the electrotransformation methods for E. quercicola and P. xanthii is shown in Table S3. The spores were collected from E. quercicola colonies after 2 weeks of growth and suspended in electroporation buffer (1 mM HEPES pH 7.0, 50 mM mannitol, $0 . 0 1 \%$ Tween 20). The suspension was adjusted to a concentration of ${ 1 0 ^ { 6 } }$ spores/mL. The suspension $( 1 5 0 \mu \mathsf { L } )$ was mixed with $2 0 \mu \ g$ of the plasmid and kept on ice for $1 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ . The 

suspension was subjected to electroporation using an electroporation system (Bio-Rad). Three square-wave pulses of 1 ms duration at 1.7 kV with two intervals of 5 s were imposed on the spores. Spores treated by electroporation were resuspended in cold mannitol solution (0.5 M) and kept on ice for 10 min, followed by washing twice with distilled water. The washed spores were used for plant inoculation. 

GUS and GFP insertions into the E. quercicola genome were confirmed using Southern blotting. EcoRV was used to digest the genomic DNA and $5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ of the digested products were separated on $0 . 8 \%$ agar gel and hybridized with gene probes. Hybridization was performed using a DIG High Prime DNA Labeling and Detection Starter Kit (Roche).

# 4.9  |  Protein extraction and purification

To extract total protein from E. quercicola, fungal hyphae and spores were collected and ground in liquid nitrogen, followed by treatment using Triton protein lysis buffer $S 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ HEPES, $1 5 0 \mathsf { m } \mathsf { M }$ KCl, 1 mM EDTA, $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, pH 7.5, $1 \%$ PMSF and $1 \times$ protease inhibitor cocktail [CWBIO]). To extract total proteins from leaves or leaf protoplasts, leaf tissues or protoplasts were ground in liquid nitrogen and treated with a Plant Protein Extraction Kit (CWBIO). In the incubation of H. brasiliensis leaf protoplast with EqIsc1-His, the protoplast suspension $( 1 0 ^ { 5 }$ protoplasts/mL, $1 5 \mathsf { m M M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 1 \%$ MES, 0.4 M mannitol, $\mathsf { p H } 5 . 7$ ) were mixed with ${ 1 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } }$ of EqIsc1- His and placed at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2 h. To extract E. coli BL21 proteins, the bacteria $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 6 - 0 . 8 )$ were treated with 1 mM IPTG to induce the expression of His-tagged proteins; bacterial cells were collected and treated by the Triton protein lysis buffer, followed by ultrasonic cell disruption. The His-tagged proteins were purified using a His-tag Protein Purification Kit (Beyotime). 

# 4.10  |  Transformation for N. benthamiana and A. thaliana

Empty or recombinant pBin4-GFP plasmids were transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 (pMP90). added into each well. The ROS luminescence intensity in each well was measured using an LB941 microplate reader (Berthold Technologies).

# 4.12  |  Measurement of SA concentrations

SA extracted from H. brasiliensis and A. thaliana leaves was quantified by LC–MS, as previously described (Forcat et al., 2008). The resulting solution, after filtering with a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ syringe filter and $5 \mu \ L$ of filtered solution loaded into LC–MS platform (Agilent Technologies Inc.) for SA measurement. The solution was separated using a C18 analytical column $( 1 5 0 \times 2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ ; $5 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ with 0.3 mL/min flow rate. The SA content in the leaf tissues was calculated according to the measured standard curve $( y = 3 9 5 5 . 3 x - 3 5 1 . 9$ ; $R ^ { 2 } { = } 0 . 9 9 9 9$ , retention time $= 1 0 . 7 4 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ ).
