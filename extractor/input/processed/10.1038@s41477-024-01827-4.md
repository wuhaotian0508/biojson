Astragalus membranaceus has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for over 2,000 years. Its major active triterpenoid saponins, astragalosides, have attracted great attention due to their multiple health benefts and applications in medicine. Despite this, the biosynthetic machinery for astragalosides remains enigmatic. Here a chromosome-level genome assembly of A. membranaceus was generated. The identifcation of two tailoring enzymes required for astragaloside biosynthesis enabled the discovery of a triterpenoid biosynthetic gene cluster, leading to elucidation of the complete astragaloside biosynthetic pathway. This pathway is characterized by a sequence of selective hydroxylation, epoxidation and glycosylation reactions, which are mediated by three cytochrome P450s, one 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase and two glycosyltransferases. Reconstitution of this biosynthetic machinery in Nicotiana benthamiana allowed for heterologous production of astragaloside IV. These fndings build a solid foundation for addressing the sourcing issues associated with astragalosides and broaden our understanding of the diversity of terpene biosynthetic gene clusters.

The dried root of Astragalus membranaceus (Fig. 1a) or A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, which was documented in the historic materia medica Shennong Bencaojing (22–250 ad) as Radix Astragali or Huangqi, has been used in traditional Chinese medicine to treat various diseases and body disorders for over 2,000 years1 . It is currently included in the pharmacopoeias of several different countries, as well as the World Health Organization monographs on selected medicinal plants2 , and is widely used as an immunomodulatory agent in Asian and European countries. In China alone, the annual sales volume of Radix Astragali has exceeded $6 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathsf { k g } \mathsf { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ (https://www.chyxx.com/industry/201609/452074.html). The major active constituents in Radix Astragali are the cycloartane-type triterpenoid saponins, astragaloside IV

(AG IV, 1; Fig. 1b) and its analogues3–5 , which act as autophagy modulators and have beneficial effects in a number of different therapeutic areas6–8 . Preparations containing AGs are widely used as dietary supplements for enhancing immunity. It is worth noting that 1 has been developed as commercial veterinary drug under the proprietary name Noland prime (UK Bless Pet Animal Protection Limited), which is used as an antiviral in the treatment of animals.

The current pharmaceutical supply chain of AGs is still reliant on their extraction from Astragalus plants. Although planting bases are well established, the yield of AGs, particularly 1, is only around $0 . 1 4 6 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ (dry weight; 3-year-old A. membranaceus) 9 , limiting their applications and use in medicinal research. A limitation of this approach, however, is the dearth of available information on AG biosynthesis. Recent contributions include the discovery of AmOSC3 (oxidosqualene cyclase) (also known as AmCAS1 (cycloartenol synthase)) in A. membranaceus and A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, a triterpene synthase responsible for catalysing the formation of cycloartenol (2) from 2,3-oxidosqualene (OS) (Fig. 1b). This transformation has been proposed to be the first committed step in the AG biosynthetic pathway12–14. In addition to this, several uridine diphosphate-glycosyltransferases (UGTs) have been reported to catalyse the glycosylation of various AG aglycones at different sites (3-OH, 6-OH, 25-OH and 2′-OH)14,15. Despite these discoveries, the complete AG biosynthetic pathway remains elusive.

In the proposed AG biosynthetic route (Fig. 1b), cycloastragenol (3), which features a 20,24-tetrahydrofuran (THF) ring and three hydroxyl groups at C-6, C-16 and C-25, is speculated to be formed through a series of oxidations on 2. Subsequently, 3 may undergo a synthetic transformation to 1 by two sequential glycosylations of the hydroxyl groups at C-3 and C-6 (Fig. 1b). Typically, cytochrome P450s (CYP450s) and UGTs serve as the primary enzymes responsible for these biosynthetic reactions16,17, although the potential involvement of other oxidases cannot be ruled out. The myriad candidate CYP450s and UGTs from which to choose, combined with uncertainties regarding biosynthetic logic, make full elucidation of the AG biosynthetic pathway a challenging endeavour. However, the continuously increasing number of well-assembled plant genomes has revealed intriguing evidence that genes involved in terpene biosynthesis are sometime grouped as biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs)18,19. An understanding of the constituent genes within a given BGC can notablely expedite the elucidation of its related biosynthetic pathway. It is worth noting that terpene synthase genes, which are responsible for building diverse terpenoid backbones, have been recognized as the signature genes that can be targeted when genome mining related terpene BGCs18. As the triterpene synthase (AmOSC3) catalysing the skeleton

formation of AG has been characterized12, genome-based exploration of AmOSC3-containing BGCs may shed light on subsequent oxidation and glycosylation steps in the AG biosynthetic pathway (Fig. 1b).

In this study, a high-quality genome sequence for A. membranaceus was generated. Unexpectedly, molecular characterization of the AmOSC3-containing BGC, which is conserved in both A. membranaceus and A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, revealed no functional evidence for its involvement in channelling intermediate 2 to AGs. Through a combined approach using genome annotation, transcriptome expression data, phylogenetic tree analysis and both in vivo and in vitro enzymatic assays, the oxidase AmCYP88D25 and the glycosyltransferase AmGT11 responsible for the latter stages of AG biosynthesis were identified. It is interesting to note that the close genomic locations of AmCYP88D25 and AmGT11 led to the identification of a BGC containing another three oxidases and one glycosyltransferase but not the signature triterpene synthase gene AmOSC3 or its homologues. This discovery has enabled the complete elucidation of the AG biosynthetic pathway in A. membranaceus. Finally, this new-found understanding of the biosynthetic pathway was demonstrated through the successful heterologous production of 1 in the leaves of Nicotiana benthamiana.

# Results

# Genome-based exploration of AmOSC3-containing BGC

A. membranaceus $( 2 n = 1 8 )$ ) has an estimated genome size of 1.43 Gb by $k$ -mer analysis (Supplementary Fig. 1). A chromosome-level genome assembly of A. membranaceus was generated using a combination of MGI-SEQ short-read, Oxford Nanopore long-read and high-throughput chromatin conformation capture (Hi-C) technologies. This culminated in the successful organization of the A. membranaceus genome sequence, with a total length of 1.44 Gb, into nine chromosome-level pseudomolecules (Supplementary Fig. 2 and Supplementary Tables 1 and 2). The genome assembly underwent Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs (BUSCO) analysis and Core Eukaryotic Genes Mapping Approach analysis, which showed high quality and accuracy with $9 2 . 4 4 \%$ complete BUSCO genes and $9 3 . 1 5 \%$ conserved core eukaryotic genes, respectively (Supplementary Tables 3 and 4).

Subsequent genome structural and functional annotations were then performed (Supplementary Tables 5 and 6).

A typical terpene BGC features a signature terpene synthase gene, which is responsible for generating terpenoid scaffold diversity in plants18. In the first instance, the corresponding terpene synthase gene AmOSC3 required for AG biosynthesis was therefore considered a high-priority target when screening for the potential AG BGC in A. membranaceus genome. As a result, a putative BGC comprising AmOSC3, a CYP450 (evm.model.LG08.328) and a UGT (evm.model.LG08.329) on chromosome 8 was identified (Supplementary Fig. 3 and Supplementary Table 7). It is interesting to note that a targeted comparative genome analysis indicated that the CYP450/UGT/AmOSC3 cluster is syntenic to the triterpenoid cluster (CYP450/UGT/CAS) previously predicted in A. membranaceus var. mongholicus20, and these clusters are exclusive to these two AGs-producing plants (Supplementary Fig. 3).

While a likely candidate based on its composition and distribution, the possibility of the CYP450/UGT/AmOSC3 cluster functioning as AG BGC was ultimately excluded, as the expression levels of the corresponding CYP450 (evm.model.LG08.328) and UGT (evm.model. LG08.329) were hardly detectable in all tissues analysed (the average fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped fragments (FPKM) were $< 0 . 5$ in root, leaf and stem), despite the high AG concentration found in the roots of A. membranaceus12,13. A yeast platform strain, CC-4, was generated by introducing AmOSC3 from A. membranaceus and AtCPR1 (cytochrome P450 reductase 1) from Arabidopsis thaliana into the high OS-producing Saccharomyces cerevisiae (yeast) BY-T311. This strain was able to efficiently synthesize 2 (Supplementary Fig. 4) and was used for the subsequent in vivo functional characterization of evm.model.LG08.328. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis of the fermentation broth showed that expression of evm.model.LG08.328 in CC-4 did not yield any new products (Supplementary Fig. 5). Meanwhile, in vitro enzymatic assays showed that evm. model.LG08.329 had no catalytic activity for glycosylations of various AG aglycones, such as 3, brachyoside B (4) and astramembrannin II (5) (Supplementary Fig. 6 and Supplementary Data 1). The absence of a BGC containing both AmOSC3 and other essential genes suggests that the outstanding genes responsible for AG biosynthesis may be dispersed throughout the remainder of the genome instead.

# Functional identification of AmCYP88D25 and AmGT11

Transcriptome-based gene expression profiling has proven to be effective in identifying candidate genes involved in natural product biosynthesis. Given that a number of CYP450s have been identified that catalyse the oxidations of tetracyclic triterpenoids in plants16, 15 CYP450 candidates showing specific or high expression in the A. membranaceus root (that is, where AGs accumulate) were selected for functional analysis (Supplementary Figs. 7 and 8). HPLC analysis of the fermentation broth showed that, of the 15 CYP450 candidates investigated in CC-4, AmCYP88D25 was the only gene that resulted in the appearance of a new product (Fig. 2a), which was identified as 16-hydroxycycloartenol (6) (Supplementary Table 8 and Supplementary Data 1). Thus, AmCYP88D25 was identified as the oxidase responsible for the C-16 hydroxylation of 2 in the AG biosynthetic pathway (Fig. 2b).

It has been reported that members of the UGT73 family preferentially catalyse the glycosylation of triterpenoids17. A comprehensive phylogenetic analysis was therefore performed to obtain putative UGT73 genes in A. membranaceus (Supplementary Fig. 9). Among the 31 UGT73 members obtained, four genes (AmGT2, AmGT7, AmGT9 and AmGT11) showing root-predominant expression and one (AmGT72) closely related to the functionally characterized triterpenoid UGT21 were successfully cloned (Supplementary Fig. 10). In vitro enzyme activity assays revealed that both AmGT11 and AmGT72 were capable of catalysing the 3-O-glycosylation of 3, giving rise to astraverrucin I (7) or 5 (Fig. 2, Supplementary Figs. 11 and 12 and Supplementary Data

1). It is worth noting that kinetic analysis showed that AmGT11 had a dramatically higher catalytic efficiency towards uridine diphosphate xylose (UDP-Xyl) over uridine diphosphate glucose (UDP-Glc) (Supplementary Fig. 13), while AmGT72 showed the opposite catalytic performance (Supplementary Fig. 14). Two UGTs, AmmUGT15 and AmmUGT14, cloned from A. membranaceus var. mongholicus have been reported to show similar catalytic properties to AmGT11 and AmGT72, respectively14. As AGs are mainly 3-O-xylosylated and found at the highest concentration in the roots5,13, we identified AmGT11 as the UGT most likely responsible for the 3-O-xylosylation of 3 due to its preference for UDP-Xyl and its root-specific expression.

Discovery of a potential BGC responsible for AG biosynthesis It is interesting to note that the mapping of AmCYP88D25 and AmGT11 to chromosomes of A. membranaceus revealed that they are located in close proximity on chromosome 1 (Fig. 3a), raising the possibility that genes associated with downstream oxidation and glycosylation steps in AG biosynthesis might be clustered on this chromosome. Accordingly, we searched for candidate genes in the chromosomal region adjacent to AmCYP88D25 and AmGT11, which led to the discovery of two CYP450 genes (AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756) and two UGT genes (AmGT36 and AmGT37) within an ~4 Mb genomic region (117864798– 121948401 bp; Fig. 3a). Further genome synteny comparisons among leguminous species, including Medicago truncatula22, A. membranaceus, A. membranaceus var. mongholicus20 and Astragalus sinicus23, indicated that this putative cluster is present in all Astragalus species but absent in M. truncatula (Supplementary Fig. 15). In A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, the orthologous cluster was also identified within an ~4 Mb region, featuring an additional copy of AmCYP88D25 and AmCYP71D756 and deletion of AmGT37. It is worth noting that the previously identified AmmUGT15 (Amm02G011460.1) was observed within this cluster, confirming its orthologous relationship with AmGT11. In A. sinicus, the orthologous cluster was found to be tightly organized (~0.81 Mb), with duplications of AmGT11 and deletion of AmGT36 and AmGT37. Collectively, considering both the gene composition within the cluster and the high degree of cluster synteny observed between A. membranaceus and A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, it is plausible that this cluster represents the potential BGC responsible for downstream skeleton decoration steps in AG biosynthesis.

# Role of AmGT36 in AG biosynthesis

Differing from AmGT11 and AmGT72, both AmGT36 and AmGT37 are members of the UGT93 family (Supplementary Fig. 9). Although they share a $9 7 \%$ amino acid sequence identity, only AmGT36 was successfully cloned from A. membranaceus complementary DNA (cDNA). It is worth noting that in vitro enzymatic assays showed that AmGT36 could recognize both 3 and 5 as substrates and was capable of catalysing the generation of 4 and 1 from each, respectively, via 6-O-glucosylation (Fig. 2c,e). Inspired by this finding, we conducted further experiments to evaluate the UGT activity of AmGT11 on 4. Like its ortholog AmmUGT1514, AmGT11 also catalysed the formation of 1 from 4 (Fig. 2f). These results suggested that AmGT11 and AmGT36 show substrate promiscuity and may complete the 3-O-xylosylation and 6-O-glucosylation of 3 by two parallel routes in the AG biosynthetic pathway (Fig. 2h). To gain deeper insights into the functional behaviours of AmGT36, further analysis of its kinetic properties was conducted, revealing 5 as its preferred substrate (Supplementary Fig. 16).

AmCYP88D25 and AmCYP88D7 were simultaneously expressed in the yeast strain CC-4, which led to the production and identification of 6,16-dihydroxycycloartenol (8) (Fig. 3b, Supplementary Table 9 and Supplementary Data 1). This suggests that AmCYP88D7 is likely responsible for hydroxylation at the C-6 position of 6 (Supplementary Fig. 18).

The catalytic activity of AmCYP71D756 was investigated in a similar manner (that is, through simultaneous expression of both AmCYP88D25 and AmCYP71D756), and to our surprise, the production

of cyclofoetigenin A (9), a 24,25-dihydroxylation product of 6, was observed (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Data 1). The CYP450-mediated epoxidation of alkenes has been well established16,24, and it is known that hydrolysis of the resulting epoxide can result in an ortho-diol. Indeed, this transformation has been widely reported to occur in the presence of endogenous yeast epoxide hydrolase25. We therefore speculated that 9 was likely the epoxide ring-opening product of 24,25-epo xy-16-hydroxycycloartenol (10). In other words, AmCYP71D756 uses

6 as substrate and catalyses 24,25-epoxidation to generate 10 (Supplementary Fig. 18). To validate this hypothesis, in vitro enzyme assays were conducted using microsomal preparations from yeast cells expressing AmCYP71D756, which resulted in the conversion of 6 to 10 (Extended Data Fig. 1, Supplementary Table 10 and Supplementary Data 1).

To further understand the underlying logic of the catalytic relationship between AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756, simultaneous expression of all the three CYP450s (that is, including AmCYP88D25, responsible for the production of 6) in CC-4 was conducted. Unexpectedly, no new products other than 6, 8 and 9 were detected (Fig. 3b). However, the yields of 8 and 9 (established by HPLC peak area) were strikingly lower compared to those observed in strains expressing only two CYP450s (Fig. 3b). This implies that AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756 are likely to catalyse the oxidation of 10 and 8, respectively, leading to the formation of unstable product(s) that may undergo further metabolism in yeast.

For further in vivo functional validation, all three CYP450s (as well as both AmOSC3 and AtCPR1) were transiently overexpressed in N. benthamiana leaves. This resulted in the detection of cyclocantogenin (11) (Fig. 3c and Supplementary Data 1), which features a 24,25-ortho-dihydroxy group. Following a similar rationale, it was believed that 24,25-epoxy-6,16-dihydroxycycloartenol (12) was the genuine product of the AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756-catalysed oxidation reactions of 6 and that 11 was produced by an endogenous N. benthamiana epoxide hydrolase from 12 (Supplementary Fig. 18). To further verify the functions of AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756, in vitro enzyme assays were performed using microsomal preparations from yeast cells expressing AmCYP88D7 or AmCYP71D756. As anticipated, AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756 showed catalytic activity in generating 12 from 10 and 8, respectively (Fig. 4a, Extended Data Figs. 2 and 3, Supplementary Table 11 and Supplementary Data 1). Collectively, these results suggest that AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756 act in concert to oxidize 6 via 10 and 8 (Fig. 4a and Supplementary Fig. 18).

# AmOGD1 drives the formation of the 20,24-THF ring

The remaining steps in the AG biosynthetic pathway involve C-20 oxidation and 20,24-THF ring formation. Only one CYP450 gene was observed in the ${ \sim } 1$ Mb flanking region of the AG BGC on chromosome 1, which showed expression levels that were barely detectable in the root $( \mathsf { F P K M } < 0 . 1 )$ ). The search was therefore focused on different types of oxidase. It is interesting to note that a gene encoding 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase, AmOGD1, was identified in the BGC and was found to have high levels of tissue-specific expression in the root (Fig. 3a and Supplementary Fig. 19). The catalytic activity of AmOGD1 (Supplementary Fig. 11) was assessed in vitro by incubating it with its potential substrates 2, 6, 8, 10 and 12 (Supplementary Fig. 20). Remarkably, 12 was found to be the favoured substrate of AmOGD1 $( k _ { \mathrm { c a t } } / K _ { m } = 1 . 6 6 9 \mathrm { m M } ^ { - 1 } s ^ { - 1 }$ and $K _ { i } { = } 1 7 . 3 8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ; $k _ { \mathrm { c a t } }$ is the enzyme turnover number; $K _ { m }$ is the Michaelis-Menten constant; $k _ { \mathrm { { c a t } } } / K _ { \mathrm { { m } } }$ is the catalytic efficiency; $K _ { i }$ is the dissociation constant), leading to the formation of 3 (Fig. 3d and Supplementary Figs. 20 and 21). It therefore seemed likely AmOGD1 was responsible for oxidation at the C-20 position (Fig. 3e). Whether the subsequent THF ring formation event was also mediated by AmOGD1, or occurred spontaneously in situ, remained unknown. An ideal solution to this problem would be delivered through chemical synthesis of an authentic sample of 24,25-epoxy-6,16,20- trihydroxycycloartenol (13). Given the limited availability of intermediates, development of synthetic protocol for the selective C-20 hydroxylation of either substrate 12 or 8 was deemed impractical, and a model system was devised instead. Protopanaxatriol (14) was chosen to serve this purpose and came with the added advantage of having the requisite C-20 hydroxyl group already installed, instead requiring only epoxidation of the pendant alkene to investigate whether spontaneous cyclization was possible. This was achieved using meta-chlorperbenzoic

acid, which resulted in the detection of THF ring containing compounds protopanaxatriol oxide II (15) and protopanaxatriol oxide I (16) (Supplementary Data 1), indicating spontaneous formation of the THF ring was the likely mechanism for the transformation of 13 to 3. Considered together, the experimental results indicate that the formation of 3 proceeds through an initial AmOGD1-catalysed C-20 hydroxylation of 12, followed by a non-enzymatic intramolecular attack of the nascent 20-OH onto the 24,25-epoxide (Fig. 3e).

# Functional analysis of the AG biosynthetic genes in planta

Drawing upon the detailed in vivo and in vitro enzyme activity assays described above, a complete AG biosynthetic pathway is delineated (Fig. 4a). AmCYP88D25-catalysed C-16 hydroxylation of 2 yields 6, which subsequently acts as the substrate for both AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756 for parallel C-6 hydroxylation and C-24,25 epoxidation, resulting in the formation of 12. AmOGD1 then catalyses the C-20 hydroxylation of 12, followed by an intramolecular addition reaction to give 3. Further glycosylations of 3 are orchestrated by AmGT36 and AmGT11 in parallel pathways, giving rise to 1.

To further validate this pathway in planta, RNA interference (RNAi)-mediated gene silencing was used to generate transgenic hairy roots of A. membranaceus. Subsequently, we successfully obtained 23 independently transformed RNAi lines targeting AmCYP88D25, AmCYP88D7, AmCYP71D756, AmOGD1, AmGT11 and AmGT36, respectively. Consistent with the effective knockdown of the target gene in each respective RNAi line, substantial reductions in AGs production were detected (Extended Data Figs. 4 and 5). Meanwhile, notable accumulations of intermediate 8, as well as an analogue of 1 (isoastragaloside IV), were observed in AmCYP71D756-RNAi lines and AmGT36-RNAi lines, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 6). These findings offer direct evidence corroborating the proposed role of the AG biosynthetic genes, thus confirming the deduced AG biosynthetic pathway in planta (Fig. 4a).

# Engineering AG biosynthesis in N. benthamiana

Having identified all enzymes responsible for the biosynthesis of 1 (Fig. 4a), the complete pathway was reconstituted in N. benthamiana. As expected, upon expressing AmOSC3, AtCPR1 and all six tailoring enzymes identified in this study in N. benthamiana, the production of 1 was observed $( 0 . 2 9 3 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { g } ^ { - 1 }$ plant dry weight; Fig. 4b and Extended Data Fig. 7). Further optimization by overexpressing the upstream triterpene precursor-forming pathway gene NbtHMGR (truncated 3-hydroxy-3-methyl glutaryl coenzyme A reductase gene from N. benthamiana) successfully increased the production of 1 to $2 . 2 2 4 \mathrm { m g g ^ { - 1 } }$ plant dry weight (Extended Data Fig. 7). To clarify the precise functions of the final two UGTs, AmGT11 and AmGT36, in the pathway, another N. benthamiana expression experiment was performed in which they were removed from the sequence. This resulted only in the production of compound 3 (Fig. 4b). When AmGT11 was reintroduced, accumulation of the biosynthetic intermediate 5 was observed (Fig. 4b). Performing the same experiment with AmGT36 instead led to the accumulation of 4 (Fig. 4b). The fact that the presence of either AmGT11 or AmGT36 alone resulted in the accumulation of intermediates 5 and 4, respectively, but their simultaneous expression resulted in the formation of 1 shows that the pair display a degree of substrate promiscuity in vivo (that is, they can each accept both 5 and 4 as substrates). In addition, a product 17 with the same molecular weight as 1 was also detected in the engineered N. benthamiana expressing either all enzymes or all enzymes except AmGT36 (Fig. 4b), suggesting 17 is presumably a glucosylated derivative of 5 catalysed by endogenous UGT of N. benthamiana (Supplementary Fig. 22).

Its subsequent characterization led to the elucidation of the complete biosynthetic pathway of 1. In general, ‘signature enzyme genes’ and ‘tailoring enzyme genes’ are the typical constituents of known terpene BGCs18. However, the AG BGC identified on chromosome 1 was composed exclusively of the six tailoring enzyme genes required for AG biosynthesis, while the signature enzyme gene, AmOSC3, was located alone on chromosome 8. It is interesting to note that, considering that the AmOSC3-catalysed formation of 2 has also been implicated in the biosynthesis of sterols in A. membranaceus12, the exclusion of AmOSC3 from the AG BGC may be from the result of an evolutionary balance between the biosynthesis of AGs and sterols in the genome. Consistent with this presumption, no apparent tissue-specific expression of AmOSC3 was observed in the root (Supplementary Fig. 7), where AGs are biosynthesized and accumulate in highest concentrations. It is also worth noting that, while all plant BGCs identified to date range from ~35 kb to $2  { \mathrm { M b } } ^ { 1 8 , 1 9 }$ , the AG BGC featuring six biosynthetic genes was ~4 Mb in size. The architecture and size of the AG BGC has broadened

our understanding of the genomic organization of terpene BGCs in plants, which lays the foundation for further BGC-based biosynthetic pathway elucidation of secondary metabolites. It is worth noting that the comparative genomics analysis indicates that the AG BGC may have experienced dynamic evolution in Astragalus species. Further extensive analysis of the evolutionary dynamics of the AG BGC in plants, by integrating genome sequence data from additional Astragalus species as well as other leguminous plants, will provide valuable insights into the origin and evolution of the AG biosynthetic pathway.

Triterpenoids and triterpene saponins, which show immense structural diversity, are widespread plant secondary metabolites associated with plant development and defence response26. These compounds also show immeasurable biological activities that can be explored for drug discovery16. CYP450s are versatile biocatalysts that play an important role in the functionalization and diversification of triterpenoid scaffolds. Although a bunch of triterpenoid-modifying CYP450s have been characterized in different plants, very little is known about the CYP450s that decorate the cycloartane-type triterpenoids16. To our knowledge, only one CYP450 that belongs to the

CYP716 family within the CYP85 clan has been previously reported to catalyse the hydroxylation of 2, although the exact position of oxidation remains unclear27. In this study, three CYP450s involved in modifications of the scaffold of 2 by hydroxylation and epoxidation were identified. AmCYP88D25 and AmCYP88D7 were classified as members of the CYP88 family (CYP85 clan), whereas AmCYP71D756 was classified as a member of the CYP71 family (CYP71 clan) (Supplementary Fig. 8). Phylogenetically related CYP450s within the same family or subfamily often show similar characteristics with respect to substrate selection and catalytic function28. The identification of AmCYP88D25, AmCYP88D7 and AmCYP71D756 is therefore of great value in deciphering related CYP450s involved in biosynthesis of other triterpenoids29,30. Furthermore, it was found that the tetrahydrofuran present in members of the AG family was installed as a result of cooperation between AmCYP71D756 and a 2-OGD (AmOGD1). A recent study also discovered two 2-OGDs that contribute to furan ring formation in limonoids, another class of tetracyclic triterpenes predominantly found in Rutaceae and Meliaceae31. It therefore appears that plants have made use of members of the 2-OGD family, in addition to the CYP450s, for the oxidation and diversification of triterpenoid scaffolds.

Radix Astragali is considered a valuable and commonly used medicinal plant worldwide, as noted in the World Health Organization monographs on selected medicinal plants (Volume 1)2 . The AGs are a class of cycloartane-type triterpenoid saponins which are the main active constituents of Radix Astragali and have been developed as dietary supplement for enhancing immunity and as a commercial veterinary drug for antiviral treatment. In fact, a multitude of secondary metabolites with the same cycloartane scaffold have been identified in plants, and many of them show important bioactivity and clinical usage29,30,32. Nevertheless, no biosynthetic route for cycloartanes has been reported, and the production of these triterpenoids is entirely dependent on extraction from commercial cultivation facilities. Many of these plants take years to grow large enough to harvest33,34. The low yields and substantial time investment required to access biologically active metabolites using cultivation limits their subsequent development into drugs. In this study, an integrated approach comprising both genomic and transcriptomic analysis led to full elucidation of the entire AG biosynthetic pathway in A. membranaceus. A terpene BGC composed of six tailoring enzyme genes was demonstrated to enable the sequential conversion of 2 to generate 1, the main active cycloartane-type triterpenoid saponin in Radix Astragali. Heterologous reconstitution of this biosynthetic machinery in N. benthamiana allowed for rapid production and isolation of 1 and is readily translatable to other members of the AG family. We anticipate that the AG BGC characterized during this study will not just aid in the development of an efficient AG production platform through plant metabolic engineering and synthetic biology but will also provide valuable insights into the biosynthetic pathways underlying other cycloartane triterpenoids found in plants.

# Methods

# Plant material and growth conditions

The seeds of A. membranaceus were purchased from Anguo Market, Hubei Province of China. A. membranaceus was cultivated in the greenhouse (16 h light, $2 5 \mathrm { - } 2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ ) of Kunming Institute of Botany, Yunnan Province of China. Young leaves were used for genomic DNA (gDNA) extraction. RNA sequencing samples were obtained from the different tissues of A. membranaceus with three biological replicates.

# Estimation of genome size

Before sequencing, we performed k-mer analysis to estimate genome size and heterozygosity. gDNA was obtained from the leaves of A. membranaceus. The library was constructed and sequenced on the MGISEQ-2000 platform (MGI Tech). We estimated the genome size of A. membranaceus with the following equation: $G { = } K _ { \mathrm { n u m } } / K _ { \mathrm { d e p t h } }$ (where $K _ { \mathrm { n u m } }$ is the total number of 17-mers, $K _ { \mathrm { d e p t h } }$ denotes the k-mer depth and G represents the genome size). The resulting data showed that the

genome size was 1,431,296,385 bp and the heterozygosity was $2 . 4 0 \%$ (Supplementary Fig. 1).

# Genome sequencing and assembly

For nanopore sequencing, high-quality gDNA was extracted from the leaves using a QIAGEN Genomic kit (13343), and size selection of long DNA fragments was performed using the BluePippin system (Sage Science). Next, the ends of DNA fragments were repaired, and an A-ligation reaction was conducted using a NEB Next Ultra II End Repair/dA-tailing Kit (E7645). Sequencing was then performed using a Nanopore GridION X5/PromethION sequencer instrument (Oxford Nanopore Technologies).

About 125.57 Gb of nanopore long-read data were used for genome assembly using NextDenovo (v.2.3.1) (https://github.com/Nextomics/ NextDenovo). To obtain more accurate sequence information, we compared the genome with the original data of nanopore sequencing using minimap2 (r41)35 to obtain a sequence alignment information file. Nextpolish (v.1.3.0) (https://github.com/Nextomics/NextPolish.git) was used to calibrate the genome based on this file. Subsequently, the short-read-sequencing data were filtered by fastp (v.0.19.4)36, and the genome corrected by the nanopore sequencing data was polished using the filtered short-read-sequencing data. The genome was de-redundant using Purge_Haplotigs37. Finally, the genome size was 1.28 Gb, and the N50 was 2.61 Mb.

BUSCO (v.4.0.5)38 and Core Eukaryotic Genes Mapping Approach3 9 were used to assess the completeness of the assembled genome (Supplementary Tables 3 and 4). To evaluate the accuracy of the assembly, all MGISEQ paired end reads were mapped to the assembled genome using BWA (v.0.7.12-r1039)40. The mapping rate and genome coverage of sequencing reads were assessed using SAMtools (v.1.4)41. Base accuracy of the assembly was calculated with bcftools $( \mathsf { v } . 1 . 8 . 0 ) ^ { 4 2 }$

# Hi-C analysis and genome synteny analysis

The Hi-C library was constructed according to a previously published method43. Hi-C technology was further used for genome assembly. About 925,313,110 clean paired-end reads of Hi-C data were used to execute Hi-C chromosome conformation. The clean reads were mapped to the genome draft using bowtie2 (v.2.3.2)44. HiC-Pro (v.2.8.1) (https://github.com/nservant/HiC-Pro) was used to filter invalid interaction pairs. Scaffolds were clustered into nine chromosomal groups (Supplementary Fig. 2 and Supplementary Table 1) by agglomerative hierarchical clustering using LACHESIS45. This yielded a 1.44 Gb assembly genome with a contig N50 of 2.82 Mb and scaffold N50 of 185 Mb (Supplementary Table 2). The heat map of chromosome interactions in A. membranaceus was visualized by HiCPlotter46.

For intergenomic comparison, we compared the A. membranaceus genome with those of three other species (A. membranaceus var. mongholicus, A. sinicus and M. truncatula). The synteny analysis between these species were performed by MCScanX47.

# Transcriptome sequencing

Sequencing libraries were generated using a NEBNext Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (E7770) following manufacturer’s recommendations. For transcriptome sequencing, the Illumina NovaSeq high-throughput sequencing platform was used to sequence the cDNA library, which was able to produce a large number of high-quality reads. The reads or bases produced by the sequencing platform were designated as original data (raw data). Most of the base quality scores reached or exceeded $Q$ (quality score) 30. High-quality reads were mapped to the genome using Hisat2 (v.2.1.0)48. Gene expression was estimated by FPKM.

# Gene structural and functional annotation

For repeat sequence annotation, GMATA $( \mathbf { v } . 2 . 2 ) ^ { 4 9 }$ , Tandem Repeats Finder $( \mathbf { v } . 4 . 0 7 \mathbf { b } ) ^ { 5 0 }$ and RepeatMasker (v.1.331)51 were used to predict

simple repeat sequences, tandem repeat elements and transposable elements of the A. membranaceus genome (Supplementary Table 5).

Three independent approaches, including reference guided transcriptome assembly, homology search and ab initio prediction, were used for gene prediction. Based on the transcriptome data, filtered messenger RNA (mRNA) sequencing reads were aligned to the reference genome using STAR (v.2.7.3a) (https://github.com/alexdobin/ STAR). The transcripts were then assembled using stringtie (v.1.3.4d) (https://github.com/gpertea/stringtie). Open reading frames were predicted using PASA $( \mathsf { v } . 2 . 3 . 3 ) ^ { 5 2 }$ , resulting in the prediction of 17,685 genes. Gene function was then predicated based on the protein sequence information of closely related species. The corresponding protein information was mapped to the genome using GeMoMa (v.1.6.1)53 to obtain structural information of the corresponding predicted genes, resulting in the prediction of 78,182 genes. Augustus $( \mathsf { v } . 3 . 3 . 1 ) ^ { 5 4 }$ was used for ab initio gene prediction, and 47,397 genes were predicted. All gene prediction results were integrated using EvidenceModeler (v.1.1.1)52. TransposonPSI55 was used to remove genes containing transposable elements and genes with coding errors.

Gene function annotation (Supplementary Table 6) was mainly predicted using blast in conjuncture with the following five databases: Non-Reduntant Protein, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Gene and Genomes, Eukaryotic Orthologous Groups of protein, Gene Ontology and Swissprot databases56–60.

Total RNA was extracted from 3-week-old A. membranaceus hairy root using FastPure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme RC411- 01). cDNA was synthesized using Hiscript III 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit ( $\bf \Pi _ { \rho + g D N A }$ wiper) (Vazyme R312-01). Gene expressions were determined by quantitative real-time PCR using All-In-One $5 \times$ RT MasterMix (ABM G592) on an QuantStudio7 Flex real-time PCR system (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Data were then presented according to the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C T } }$ method, and the gene encoding $\beta$ -actin was used for normalization. Primers used in this study are summarized in Supplementary Table 13.

# Construction of the AG synthetic pathway in N. benthamiana

Transient expression of genes in N. benthamiana was performed as follows. pEAQ-HT (easy and quick-hyper translatable) vectors carrying different genes were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens LBA4404 strain. Transformants were selected on LB medium supplemented with $2 5 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathsf { m } ^ { \mathrm { - 1 } }$ rifampicin, $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin and $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 }$ streptomycin. The colonies were validated by PCR and then incubated on LB solid medium for 3 days. We scraped the cells from the solid medium with a cotton swab. Cell pellets were washed once with ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ and collected by centrifugation, then the cells were resuspended in MMA buffer (10 mmol l−1 MES, pH 5.6; 10 mmol $\mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { M g C l } _ { 2 } ; \mathsf { 1 5 0 \mu m o l } \mathsf { I } ^ { - 1 }$ acetosyringone) to reach $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 1$ . Equal volumes of each strain suspension were mixed together. Then the mixture was infiltrated into leaves of N. benthamiana, after that, N. benthamiana was subjected to 16 h of light and 8 h of darkness per day $( 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ for 5 days. For qualitative

analysis of 1 and its biosynthetic intermediates in N. benthamiana, the leaves were collected and dried in a freeze dryer. Then the dried leaf samples $( 2 0 0 \mathrm { m g } )$ ) were ground with a mortar, and MeOH (20 ml) was added to the powder. After sonicating for 1 h, the resulting suspension was extracted with n-hexane $( 3 \times 1 0 \mathrm { m l } )$ to remove chlorophyll. Finally, the remaining MeOH phase was concentrated under reduced pressure, and the residue was dissolved in MeOH (3 ml) and analysed with Agilent 1290/6530 UHPLC-Q-TOF/MS system equipped with an Eclipse Plus $\mathsf { C } _ { 1 8 }$ column (I.D. $4 . 6 \mathsf { m m } \times 1 0 0 \mathsf { m m }$ ) under a gradient (flow rate 1 ml min−1) of solvent A $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } )$ ) and solvent B $\left( \mathrm { C H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { C N } \right)$ ) as follows: 0–8 min, $3 0 \mathrm { - } 6 0 \%$ B; 8–10 min, $100 \%$ B; 10–12 min, $30 \%$ B; the MS data were collected with positive ion mode and mass range, $m / z = 5 0 - 1 , 5 0 0$ . To preliminarily determine the structure of compound 17, tandem mass spectrometry analysis was performed on the same system with an isocratic elution of $3 4 \% \mathrm { C H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { C N i n H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { \cdot }$ , and 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 and 60 V collision energies were used with an $m / z = 8 0 7$ $( [ \mathsf { M } + \mathsf { N a } ] ^ { + } )$ . To quantify the content of 1 in N. benthamiana, 50 mg of dried N. benthamiana leaf powder was sonicated in $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of $80 \%$ MeOH for 1 h. Following sonication, the samples were centrifuged for subsequent analysis using the same method mentioned above.
