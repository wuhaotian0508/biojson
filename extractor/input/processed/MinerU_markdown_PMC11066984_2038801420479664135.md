# Organ-delimited gene regulatory networks provide high accuracy in candidate transcription factor selection across diverse processes

Rajeev Ranjana,b , Sonali Srijana , Somaiah Balekuttiraa , Tina Agarwala,b, Melissa Rameya , Madison Dobbinsa , Rachel Kuhna , Xiaojin Wanga,b, Karen Hudsonc , Ying $\mathsf { L i } ^ { \mathsf { a , b } } ( \mathbb { D } ) ,$ and Kranthi Varalaa,b,1 

Edited by Julia Bailey-Serres, University of California, Riverside, CA; received January 4, 2024; accepted March 14, 2024 

Organ-specific gene expression datasets that include hundreds to thousands of experiments allow the reconstruction of organ-level gene regulatory networks (GRNs). However, creating such datasets is greatly hampered by the requirements of extensive and tedious manual curation. Here, we trained a supervised classification model that can accurately classify the organ-of-origin for a plant transcriptome. This K-Nearest Neighbor-based multiclass classifier was used to create organ-specific gene expression datasets for the leaf, root, shoot, flower, and seed in Arabidopsis thaliana. A GRN inference approach was used to determine the: i. influential transcription factors (TFs) in each organ and, ii. most influential TFs for specific biological processes in that organ. These genome-wide, organ-delimited GRNs (OD-GRNs), recalled many known regulators of organ development and processes operating in those organs. Importantly, many previously unknown TF regulators were uncovered as potential regulators of these processes. As a proof-of-concept, we focused on experimentally validating the predicted TF regulators of lipid biosynthesis in seeds, an important food and biofuel trait. Of the top 20 predicted TFs, eight are known regulators of seed oil content, e.g., WRI1, LEC1, FUS3. Importantly, we validated our prediction of MybS2, TGA4, SPL12, AGL18, and DiV2 as regulators of seed lipid biosynthesis. We elucidated the molecular mechanism of MybS2 and show that it induces purple acid phosphatase family genes and lipid synthesis genes to enhance seed lipid content. This general approach has the potential to be extended to any species with sufficiently large gene expression datasets to find unique regulators of any trait-of-interest.

An organism’s gene expression profile is modulated in response to its environment, primarily through the action of a special class of genes called transcription factors (TFs). Therefore, an important goal of molecular biology has been the discovery of TF to target gene interactions, which at a genome-wide scale are called gene regulatory networks (GRNs). However, the inference of GRNs from gene expression data remains a challenge (1) due to the high variability in gene expression and the presence of transient, context-dependent TF-target relationships, which results in low signal-to-noise ratio. In the past two decades, thousands of independent gene expression assays in model organisms have resulted in tens of thousands of unique genome-wide expression assays. This massive dataset is conducive for GRN inference provided two challenges can be surmounted: 1. Computational cost of working with these massive data sets and 2. Coherence of interactions across development, growth, and treatment variations. In plants, numerous studies (2) have focused on the inference of GRNs from gene expression data. The model organism Arabidopsis thaliana is well suited for genome-wide GRN reconstruction for the following reasons: 1. compact genome encodes approximately 28,000 genes which includes ~1,700 TFs (i.e., $\sim 3 0$ million potential regulatory interactions), 2. massive gene expression dataset with tens of thousands of individual assays and 3. modest organismal complexity with few distinct organs (e.g., a shoot made primarily of leaves, simple flowers, etc.). 

Individual TFs can potentially bind (3) and/or regulate (4) hundreds to thousands of targets. However, the precise targets of a TF in a given organ and/or condition seems to be constrained by promoter availability (5) and likely other factors. The most obvious delineation of GRN composition and thus gene expression is the plant organ (6–8). Traditionally, GRN inference has worked best on large expression sets from the same organ after an application of one or more external signals (5, 9, 10). While this approach is powerful in discovering regulators of plant responses, its application across multiple responses is limited by the cost and labor-intensive sampling required for experimentation and the computational cost to process them. # Significance

Our study develops a machinelearning framework for building unbiased gene expression datasets for each organ, and to infer organ-delimited gene regulatory networks. We show that this approach is successful at predicting which transcription factors (TFs) are going to regulate processes at an organ level. We validated the accuracy of the predictions for TFs using the seed lipid synthesis pathway as a case study. We demonstrated a robust success rate for recalling known and predicting previously unknown TF regulators of seed lipid biosynthesis. The approach described in this study is broadly applicable across any organism (plant or animal) that has a large body of public gene expression data.

The focus of our study is to enable such GRN construction by: i. Building a comprehensive gene expression dataset, ii. Use an automated classifier to create unbiased organ-delineated data sets, iii. Infer GRNs functional in each organ and iv. Assay the accuracy of this approach by functional validation of predicted regulator roles. As a proof-of-concept, we choose to focus on the lipid biosynthesis in the seed. Substantial efforts in understanding the synthesis, storage, and degradation of seed oils have led to a well-characterized biochemical pathway (11). We also know a handful of TFs that regulate this process (e.g., WRI1, LEC1, FUS3, etc.) (12). WRINKLED1 (WRI1) is a master regulator of seed oil accumulation as it regulates many genes involved in glycolysis and fatty acid (FA) synthesis (13–15). LEC1, LEC2, FUS3, and ABI3, are other TFs whose roles have been established in seed oil biosynthesis (16–20). Among them, LEC1, LEC2, FUS3, and ABI3 work upstream of WRI1 and together with HSI2/VAL1, AGL15, BBM, TT8, and MYB89 form a network that tightly controls the expression of WRI1 (15–17, 21, 22). Additionally, the TFs MYB96, TZF, WRKY10, WRKY43, and bZIP67 positively regulate oil accumulation in seeds while, TT2, TTG1, GL2, MYB76, MYB118, and WRKY6, negatively affect seed oil content (23–32). Despite this wealth of knowledge, robust strategies to increase the overall content and especially the composition of storage lipids are somewhat lacking. Given the size and complexity of this pathway (over 300 genes, across multiple organelles) and the central role it plays in seed development, it is likely that the transcriptional regulation of the pathway is complex and includes additional, uncharacterized TFs. Therefore, we chose the finding and validation of TFs regulating the lipid metabolism pathway in the seeds as a target to assess the accuracy of our approach in identifying TFs that regulate a pathway or process of interest. A successful inference strategy would recall the known regulators as well as predict unknown TF regulators of seed lipid biosynthesis. 

# Results

Compiling a Large, Labeled Gene Expression Dataset to Enable Machine Learning (ML). A. thaliana as a model organism has been extensively transcriptionally profiled. For this study, we chose experiments that assayed global gene expression via RNASeq and deposited their raw data in NCBI’s short read archive (SRA) database. This public repository was queried in the Fall 2018, which generated a dataset of 18,174 individual Illumina runs (Dataset S1). A quasimapping algorithm (33) was used to convert raw sequence data into a gene expression estimate for 16,830 runs after eliminating experiments with too few mappable reads (Materials and Methods). The gene expression matrix was subjected to a full quantile normalization (34) and transformed to a log2 scale. 

Concurrently, we collected the metadata associated with each experiment from the SRA database. The organ from which the RNA was extracted, i.e., the label, was coded by manual curation of the metadata or the corresponding publication. In this way, a total of nine organ labels: Aerial, Flower, Leaf, Root, Seed, Seedling, Shoot, Silique, and Stem, were created and assigned to the 16,830 RNASeq runs (Dataset S1). Therefore, we compiled a fully labeled, high-confidence gene expression dataset that is ideally suited to build a ML model in a supervised learning approach. Formally, each data point (i.e., individual RNASeq data) in this set includes 33,602 features (gene models) 

in the Arabidopsis genome (35), as well as one of the nine organ labels. 

Due to the diversity of experimental conditions across the data set, it is not apparent that all experiments from the same organ would cluster together and away from the other organ clusters. To verify whether the organ-of-origin is the major determinant of global gene expression, we plotted all experiments into a single two-dimensional space, using the t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (tSNE) dimensionality reduction approach. As shown in Fig. 1A, most experiments from the same organ cluster together to form a distinct global profile for each organ (as indicated by color in Fig. 1A). However, it is also apparent that the same organ can exist in multiple stable states that differ substantially from each other, as is seen in the case of Leaf samples (multiple clusters of green dots) and the three distinct clusters formed by the root samples (brown dots). This suggests that a simple clustering approach (e.g., K-Means) is unlikely to create clean organ-specific subsets from this large set. However, ML approaches have proven extremely robust in such supervised classification problems in biology (36, 37). 

Supervised Classifier Assigns Organ Labels with High Accuracy. We explored two ML models, a K-Nearest-Neighbor (KNN, ${ \mathrm { k } } = 5$ ) based, and a Support Vector Machine (SVM) based model, to determine the best-performing classifier, i.e., one with high precision and recall (PR). Each model was used to train a multiclass classifier that assigned to each RNASeq run one of nine organ labels: Leaf, Root, Flower, Seed, Seedling, Shoot, Stem, Aerial, or Silique. Model training used a $8 0 { : } 2 0 \%$ split of training and testing data and a cross-validation strategy (see SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods for details). To improve the balance of labels, for organs with $> 1 , 0 0 0$ samples, a maximum of 1,000 samples were used for training and rest assigned to testing set (SI  Appendix, Supplementary Methods). Between the KNN and SVM-based models, the KNN model had higher PR and F1 scores across multiple organs (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S1). Therefore, we selected the KNN-based classifier as our best-performing model. This classifier can determine the correct organ label for most data [macro $\mathrm { F } 1 = 0 . 7 6$ , weighted $\mathrm { F } 1 = 0 . 9 5$ ; macro F1 is the unweighted average of F1 values across all classes, weighted F1 assigns higher weight to larger classes (no. of true labels) i.e., it adjusts for the size of the class] (Fig. $1 B$ and SI  Appendix, Table  S1). The performance of the classifier for each organ is directly linked to number of samples in the training set as well as the biological ambiguity of organ definition (Fig.  1C). For example, the classifier has nearly perfect PR for the leaf which is a clearly demarcated organ and has the largest training set (Fig.  1 $B$ and C). The confusion matrix (Fig.  1C) shows that the misclassification of datasets is often due to organ ambiguity. For example, the most common label for false negatives (FN) for the leaf is Aerial, which is an ambiguous “organ” definition in Arabidopsis that is primarily made of leaves. Conversely, the most common true label for the false positives (FP) for the shoot is the leaf which is part of the shoot. Outside of this biological ambiguity, the classifier demonstrated high accuracy. 

Since TFs are the primary gene expression regulators, we investigated whether TFs themselves are expressed in an organ-specific manner using the Tau metric (38). Briefly, the Tau metric assigns a numeric score between 0 and 1 to each gene based on how biased its expression is skewed to one or more organs (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). The expression patterns in Fig. $1 D$ and the low Tau scores (SI Appendix, Fig. S2) demonstrate that most TFs are not expressed in an organ-specific manner. Inferring Organ-Delimited GRNs (OD-GRNs) Predicts Regulators for an Organ, a Specific Biological Process, or a Gene-Set-of-Interest. We hypothesized that large expression sets from a single organ would encode nonconflicting TF-target regulation signals and that using these high-quality sets for GRN inference would result in high accuracy in predicting the roles of TFs. To test these hypotheses, we inferred GRNs in five of the nine organs identified independently to determine the set of TFs that act as primary regulators of a given process within the context of that organ. The five organs chosen due to the availability of sufficient data $\mathbf { \rho } ( \mathbf { n } > 1 0 0 $ ) as well as their distinct biological definition were leaf, root, shoot, seed, and flower. In the leaf, a random subset of 1,000 experiments were chosen for GRN inference to limit computational costs. A random forest-based network inference tool for TF-target interactions (39), called GENIE3 (40), was used for GRN inference using all 1,717 TFs (41) encoded in the Arabidopsis genome as predictors. In each organ, the top $1 0 \%$ scoring TF-target predictions were retained to create a highconfidence OD-GRN that includes regulatory predictions for most genes and TFs in an organ. Each TF-target edge has a score 

that depicts the confidence of the prediction. The net effect of each TF in the OD-GRN was then summarized as the cumulative score for all outgoing edges from that TF (i.e., weight sum) to generate a ranked list of TFs (Fig. 2A). The top 10 TFs in each OD-GRN were compared to assay the relative importance of TFs across organs. The top TFs in each organ are mostly unique with only a handful of TFs ranking high across organs (SI Appendix, Fig. S3). Further, the expression pattern of the union set of top 10 TFs in each organ shows that while TFs may have an impactful role in one organ, they are largely expressed across multiple organs (SI Appendix, Fig. S4). 

The OD-GRNs constructed here can be queried repeatedly using subsets of genes (e.g., genes associated with a particular biological process) to generate subnetworks. Each subnetwork will have its own ranked TF lists where the highest ranked TFs are presumed to be most influential on the target genes used to query the OD-GRN. To evaluate the effectiveness of this GRN inference approach to the finding of TF regulators, we investigated three use cases at the i. organ, ii. gene set, and iii. process levels. We hypothesize that given a cohesive set of genes which serve as bait for network query, the most highly ranked TFs predicted to regulate them should recall validated regulators of that gene set as well as predict previously unknown ones.

Case study 1: TF regulators of floral development (flower-GRN). We hypothesize that the top TFs in the flower-GRN would be enriched with regulators of floral development. The top 10 TFs ranked as the most influential in each OD-GRN were retrieved (SI Appendix, Table S2). Indeed, five well-characterized floral morphology/timing regulators, namely TSO1, NAC2, NAC5, FWA, and TCP22 were found in the top 10 TFs for the flower-GRN (Fig.  2B). The remaining five TFs in the top 10 list (SI Appendix, Table S3) have not yet been described as floral regulators thus providing potential candidates for testing. 

Case study 2: Phosphate-responsive genes in roots (root-GRN). A recent study identified the top 50 genes that are most strongly induced by phosphate supply (42). Using these genes as query, we retrieved the phosphate subnetwork from the root-GRN and identified the top 10 TFs that regulate these 50 genes (SI Appendix, Table S4). Three known phosphate-responsive regulators CPL3 (43, 44), HHO2 (45), and RSL2 (46) are predicted to regulate these genes in the roots (Fig. 2B), with CPL3, HHO2, and RSL2 ranked 1st, 2nd, and 4th respectively (SI Appendix, Table S4). 

Case study 3: Lipid biosynthesis in seeds (seed-GRN). Lipid biosynthesis in seeds is a process fundamental to the reproductive success of plants as well as essential for food and biofuel production. Due to its significance, the biosynthetic pathway leading to the formation of FAs and storage lipids has been extensively characterized (47). All Arabidopsis genes associated with the GO term lipid biosynthetic process (GO:0008610) were retrieved (48) to form a query set of 343 genes (Dataset S2). The seed-GRN (i.e., only the top $1 0 \%$ of TF-target predictions from seed) was queried with this set to retrieve the subnetwork of these genes and their predicted regulators. This lipid-seed-GRN was filtered again to retain edges with an edge score ${ > } 0 . 1$ to keep only highconfidence edges (Dataset S3). The sum of weights for each TF was calculated within this network and used to rank the relevance of TFs (Dataset S4) to seed lipid biosynthesis. Since this process is extensively studied and many of its TF regulators have been described (12), we expanded our analysis to the top 20 ranked TFs predicted to regulate this gene set. The top 20 TFs included eight known regulators of seed lipid biosynthesis (Dataset  S4) LEC1 (Rank 2nd) (19), MYB118 (3rd) and MYB115 (6th) (49), TT2 (4th) (32), FUS3 (8th) (20, 50), the master regulator WRI1 (9th) (13, 14, 16), MYB96 (14th) (23), and WRI3 (15th) (51). 

Functional profiling of candidate seed lipid regulators validated their role in promoting seed lipid biosynthesis. In each of the three case studies above, multiple known regulators of the process were recovered in the top-ranked TFs. Interspersed with the known TFs 

are unproven regulators for these well-established processes. To investigate the relevance of these predicted candidate regulators, we focused on a reverse genetics approach for functional validation of the seed lipid TFs. Among the top 20 TFs, we found eight known regulators, while a role for the remaining 12 candidate TFs in seed oil production has never been described. 

To estimate the net effect of the perturbation of each of these TFs on lipid biosynthesis, the subset of lipid biosynthesis genes that are predicted to be regulated by these 20 TFs were placed in the context of their specific metabolic pathway using the KEGG database (Fig. 3). This approach revealed that 100 genes associated with the lipid biosynthesis process and regulated by these 20 TFs are involved in the synthesis of FAs from Acetyl-CoA as well as downstream processes such as generation of Triacylglycerols (TAG), membrane lipids, steroid, and terpenoid biosynthesis (Fig. 3). In the seeds, TAGs are the primary form of storage lipids and their biosynthesis occurs partially in the plastids and partially in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) (11). The seed lipid GRN predicts a role for these 20 TFs in the regulation of genes involved in FA synthesis and elongation in the plastids and the formation of TAGs in the ER (Fig. 3). In addition to regulating TAG biosynthesis, the GRN predicts how these TFs regulate alternate metabolic fates of FAs and/or Acetyl-CoA (Fig. 3 and SI Appendix, Table S7). Based on these predictions, we hypothesized that lowering/eliminating the expression of any one of these TFs would result in a significant reduction of total lipid content in the Arabidopsis seeds.

MybS2 Promotes Overall Lipid Production. The mybs2-2 mutant shows the most reduction of total lipid content $( \sim 1 8 \%$ ; Fig. 4C and Dataset S7). Previous studies have established a role for MybS2 in sugar signaling (52) as well as a negative regulator of salt stress response (53). MybS2 is expressed in the developing seeds and across multiple organs (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 5 A$ and B). However, its role in directing lipid biosynthesis in seeds has never been reported. To confirm the effect of MybS2 expression levels on the seed lipid biosynthesis pathway, we isolated a second T-DNA mutant (mybs2-1) and simultaneously generated i. complementation lines (pMybS2::MybS2 in the mybs2-2 background) and ii. overexpression (OE) lines (p35S::MybS2 in WT background, SI Appendix, Fig. S5C). Total FA content in the seeds of both mybs2-1 and mybs2-2 mutants is significantly reduced (Fig.  5A) confirming that reduced MybS2 expression lowers seed FA content. Importantly, in the complementation lines the reduction in total FA levels is rescued and is restored to WT levels (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 5 D$ and Dataset S7). Seeds of multiple independent MybS2 OE lines (Dataset  S12) showed an increased accumulation of total FAs (Fig.  5B) despite no change in the size or rate of growth relative to the wild-type (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S6). Moreover, the weight and size of seeds were also reduced in the mutants and increased in the OE lines (Datasets S5 and S6). We then assayed the molecular effect of MybS2 OE on lipid biosynthesis via RNASeq to identify genes induced by OE of MybS2 (Materials and Methods). Comparing the leaf transcriptome of a MybS2-OE line with a concurrently grown wild-type plant revealed that five members of the purple acid phosphatase (PAP) gene family (PAP7/14/23/24/25) were significantly and highly induced by the OE of MybS2 (avg. FC ~ 16X) (Fig. $5 C$ and Datasets S8 and S9). A recent study revealed 

that increased and ubiquitous expression of PAP2, a member of the PAP family, increased FA synthesis and results in enhanced levels of TAGs in seeds (54). We further compared the expression level of the five PAP genes in the siliques during the seed-filling stage [11 to 13 days after pollinations (DAP)] between MybS2- OE and wild-type using qRT-PCR. Indeed, PAP7 and PAP23 are also significantly induced in the MybS2-OE siliques relative to the wild type (Fig. 5D). Moreover, eight lipid biosynthesis genes (23), LPCAT1, LPCAT2, PDHE1-α, SDP1, KASI, FATA, FAD2, and FAD3, are also induced in MybS2-OE compared to WT, with a gene involved in TAG breakdown SDPL1 down-regulated in the MybS2-OE line (Fig. $5 D$ and Dataset S10). Thus, OE of MybS2 leads to an increase in expression of PAPs and multiple lipid biosynthesis genes in seeds, which could collectively contribute to enhanced lipid biosynthesis. Overall, our results confirm that MybS2 positively regulates total seed lipid content, seed size, and seed weight, and link its regulatory role of lipid biosynthesis to the transcriptional control of the $P A P$ gene family as well as lipid biosynthesis genes involved in the production or breakdown of FAs and TAGs. 

# Discussion

This study demonstrates the benefit of inferring GRNs from large organ delimited expression datasets. The KNN-based classifier developed here has high recall) and high precision for every organ that had ${ > } 1 0 0$ gene expression datasets in the training phase. Building such organ-specific gene expression sets is instrumental to GRN inference, as done here but also to many other approaches such as correlation analysis, limiting candidate genes in QTL/GWAS analysis (e.g., expressed in the appropriate organ/correlated to known pathway genes etc.), marker gene identification, and developing organ-specific promoters for synthetic biology. We applied this approach to the model plant Arabidopsis, but many other crop species such as corn, soybean, rice, tomato, wheat, etc. now have public gene expression sets with tens of thousands of gene expression assays. Other processes that are amenable to this strategy of TF identification and engineering include specialized metabolism pathways, rapid responses to environmental cues such as nutrients/ temperature, etc. and developmental processes such as organ formation. Conversely, poorly characterized processes with many unknown genes or gene sets without functional coherence may 

prove to be weaker network baits. Nonetheless, in species with existing mutant populations or where candidate TFs can be quickly knocked out by CRISPR-based methods a reverse genetics strategy could be used to rapidly screen and validate 10 to 20 candidate TFs, as we have shown in this study. Our approach does miss 16 known regulators of lipid biosynthesis (Dataset S4), of which four may be recovered by lowering the TF rank threshold [bZIP67(Rank 27), Lec1-Like (Rank 37), AGL15(Rank 42), and SPATULA (Rank 194)], at the cost of including many FP TFs. The selection of a TF rank threshold is subjective and varies from process to process. In a well-studied process with multiple known regulators, the rank threshold may be chosen such that it includes known regulators as well as predicts unknown regulators for the process of interest. Other factors in this choice may be i. the ease of validation and ii. need for identification of alternate factors. The other unrecalled TFs may simply be recalcitrant to finding via expression-based approaches. 

The accuracy of inferred GRNs is typically measured as how many of the predicted edges are known or validated (9, 10, 39, 56). The known edges are often used to calculate PR thresholds and then used to cull FPs and select high-confidence edges. The relatively small subset of edges retained in this approach select for strong effects of a TF on a target gene but may potentially miss meaningful but weaker effects of a TF across multiple targets. Our approach uses a lax threshold and retains the top $1 0 \%$ ranked edges in each OD-GRN, which no doubt includes many false predictions of TF-target interactions. Instead, we assume that the sum of weights of all edges from a TF captures the net effect of a TF on the organ or a selected process by accumulating weak effects of the TF on a broader set of targets. This approach is inspired by the concept of hidden support in phylogenomics where a tree topology unsupported by individual gene trees may derive support from concatenated matrices (57). Similarly, a TF that has weak 

edges to many genes involved a given process would receive a high rank even though none of its individual edges would pass a PR threshold. As an objective assessment, we compared the PR curve for our OD-GRN approach to two recent GRN inference tools that also used large gene expression datasets: AraNetv2 (58) and iGRN (59) using 24 known regulators of lipid biosynthesis (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 and Dataset S4). Our OD-GRN approach achieved higher PR, likely due to learning the network from only seed expression studies rather than mixed organs as is the case for the other two tools. 

Overall, this study describes a general approach that begins with an unfiltered gene expression dataset, which is used to build organ-delimited expression sets and infer their GRNs. Our preliminary analysis in rice suggests that a robust organ classifier may be trained from as few as ${ \sim } 1 0 0$ samples per organ (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Further, down-sampling the input expression set for GRN inference shows the robustness of this approach (SI Appendix, Figs. S11 and S12). For example, a minimal set of 45 seed RNASeq samples from the seed was sufficient to predict six known regulators of lipid biosynthesis (WRI1, WRI3, LEC1, FUS3, MYB118, and bZIP67) as well as the MybS2 TF validated here (Dataset S11). Thus, it is feasible to apply the approach described here to species that have hundreds rather than thousands of RNASeq results in the public domain. Also, the ongoing progress in single-cell transcriptome profiling will provide larger and better-delineated expression datasets that will rival and exceed the recent RNASeq era. The approach of building biologically coherent expression sets (e.g., organs or tissues) and using GRN inference is readily translatable to single-cell data, especially when new inference algorithms being built for single-cell transcriptomes and multiomes are deployed. 

# Materials and Methods

Assignment of Organ Labels for Gene Expression Dataset. Raw sequencing data from all Illumina sequencing-based gene expression data (RNASeq) from the species A. thaliana were downloaded from NCBI’s SRA database. Text descriptions associated with each dataset were manually read to assign the correct organ label to each RNASeq dataset. Sequence data were converted to gene expression counts using a quasi-mapping approach (33) and normalized using a quantile normalization approach (34) and then transformed to a log2 scale. 

ML Pipeline to Classify the Organ Origin of Transcriptomes. A KNN-based model was trained using the scikit-learn package (60) using a minimum o $8 0 \%$ of the labeled samples from each organ. The maximum samples from any organ was set to 1,000 in the training set resulting in a total training set of 4,241 samples. The remaining samples of 11,010 were used as the test set. PR of the classifier were estimated from the test set. See SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods for additional details of implementation. Source code is available here: https://github.com/kvarala/InferNet and full dataset here: https://purr.purdue.edu/publications/4311/1. 

Network Inference and TF Ranking. For each organ, the expression set was built from the samples labeled as belonging to that organ by the multiclass classifier. A known set of TFs (41) in the Arabidopsis genome (regulators) and normalized expression of all genes were provided as input to the GENIE (40) inference algorithm. The top $1 0 \%$ ranked edges from each GRN were retained for further analysis. For any given organ or subnetwork, the edge weights were summed for each TF and this cumulative weight sum was used to rank the relative importance of TFs. 

Plant Materials and Growth Conditions. All T-DNA mutant lines were ordered from ABRC. Homozygous insertion of T-DNA was confirmed by PCR-based genotyping with LBb1.3 and Salk line-specific LP primer pairs (SI Appendix, Table S6). Each Arabidopsis plant was grown in a $4 ^ { \prime \prime }$ pot filled with standard germination mix and turface (3:1) under 16/8 h light/dark conditions at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \complement$ in the growth room. 

Plasmid Preparation and Arabidopsis Transformation. MybS2 OE lines were generated by transforming Col-0 Arabidopsis plants with a construct carrying MybS2 coding sequence under 35S promoter (Please see SI Appendix, Supplementary Method for details). 

Seed Phenotyping. Seed size measurement was performed as previously described (61). Seeds of each genotype were spread on the glass slide and photographed using a dissection microscope. The surface area of each seed was measured using ImageJ software. Seed weight was measured using an electronic balance after counting 100 seeds from each genotype manually. At least eight independent biological replicates were used for each measurement and Student’s t test was used for significance evaluation. 

Total FA Estimation. FA extraction and analysis were performed as previously described (62). See SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods for details. 

Transcriptome Profiling of MybS2-OE Line and Assay of Lipid Metabolism Genes. The transcriptome profile of MybS2-OE#2 (Fig.  5B) plants was contrasted against the transcriptome of WT (Col-0) plants and significantly differentially expressed genes was identified at a FDR cutoff of 0.05. Siliques at 11, 12, and 13 DAP were collected from MybS2-OE#2, and Col-0 plants grown together under the same conditions in a growth room. Siliques from four plants were pooled to make one replicate, and four replicates of each genotype were used for qPCR assay. (see SI Appendix, Supplementary Methods for details). 

Data, Materials, and Software Availability. Original data and source code, data, models and networks, and primary gene expression data and OD-GRNs data have been deposited in PURR (63).
