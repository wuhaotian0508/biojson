# Abstract

In petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida), MADS-box homeotic genes dictate floral organ identity. For instance, DEFICIENS (PhDEF), GLOBOSA1, and GLOBOSA2 (PhGLO1/2) are responsible for petal and stamen identity. However, whether homeotic genes, particularly PhDEF, have a function at the later stages of flower development remains elusive. In petunia flowers, scent production initiates at anthesis, when the flower is ready for pollination, and is triggered by activation of EMISSION OF BENZENOIDS I (EOBI) and EOBII, MYB transcriptional regulators of scent-related genes. Here, we revealed the role of PhDEF in mature flowers, showing that it activates scent production. PhDEF suppression using a transient viral system in petunia flowers led to a significant reduction in volatile emission and pool levels, and in the transcript levels of scent-related transcriptional regulators and enzymes. Promoter activity assays demonstrated that PhDEF activates EOBI, EOBII, and the phenylpropanoid biosynthesis genes L-PHENYLALANINE AMMONIA LYASE and PHENYLACETALDEHYDE SYNTHASE. Our findings underscore the importance of PhDEF in petunia flower development from initiation to maturation and in coordinating petal specification and the establishment of showy pollination-related traits. 

# Introduction

Garden petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida) is a model plant for the study of floral volatiles because its large flowers emit a wealth of phenylpropanoid volatiles that are derived from phenylalanine (Verdonk et al. 2003). Numerous enzymes involved in the formation of these phenylpropanoids via acetylation, methylation, decarboxylation, etc. have been characterized (Muhlemann et al. 2014; Skaliter et al. 2022). The synthesis of phenylpropanoid volatiles begins with the shikimate pathway. The enzyme 3- DEOXY-D-ARABINO-HEPTULOSONATE-7-PHOSPHATE SYNTHASE (DAHPS) catalyzes the initial step of the pathway. Then 5-ENOLPYRUVYLSHIKIMATE-3-PHOSPHATE SYNTHASE (EPSPS) and CHORISMATE SYNTHASE (CS) catalyze the following reactions, leading to the production of phenylalanine, the precursor of phenylpropanoid/benzenoid volatiles (Lynch and Dudareva 2020). PHENYLACETALDEHYDE SYNTHASE (PAAS) and L-PHENYLALANINE AMMONIA LYASE (PAL), pivotal enzymes in the phenylpropanoid pathway, compete for the substrate Phe to form either phenylacetaldehyde or trans-cinnamic acid, respectively (Muhlemann et al. 2014). Volatile phenylacetaldehyde is a substrate for the biosynthesis of the phenylpropanoidrelated compounds (C6-C2) phenylethyl alcohol, phenylethyl acetate, and phenylethyl benzoate. The trans-cinnamic acid is a 

precursor for the synthesis of benzenoids (C6-C1), such as benzyl alcohol, benzaldehyde, benzyl benzoate, and methyl benzoate; trans-cinnamic acid is also a precursor for phenylpropenes (C6-C3), including eugenol and isoeugenol (Pichersky et al. 2006; Klempien et al. 2012; Skaliter et al. 2022). 

Several transcription factors that positively or negatively regulate petunia floral phenylpropanoid-derived aroma production have been characterized. The negative regulator PhMYB4 (EMISSION OF BENZENOIDS [EOB] III) controls scent by mainly modulating the production of phenylpropenes, whereas EVER affects scent by altering cuticle composition (Colquhoun et al. 2011; Skaliter et al. 2024). Positive MYB regulators include PH4, which is crucial for volatile emission, and ODORANT 1 (ODO1), EOBI, and EOBII, which promote volatile production during flower opening by activating scent-related biosynthetic genes (Verdonk et al. 2005; Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010, 2012; Cna’ani et al. 2015). ODO1 modulates the expression of DAHPS, EPSPS, CHORISMATE MUTASE (CM), PAL1, and PAL2 from the shikimate pathway (Verdonk et al. 2005; Boersma et al. 2022). EOBI and EOBII activate transcription of these genes, as well as CS and CONIFERYL ALCOHOL ACETYLTRANSFERASE (CFAT) (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010, 2012). In addition, EOBII directly binds to the upstream regulatory sequences of ODO1 and EOBI, activating their expression. While EOBII directly activates ODO1 and phenylpropanoid 

biosynthesis-related genes, the latter negatively affects EOBI transcription (Van Moerkercke et al. 2011; Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2012). Whereas transcriptional regulation of volatile production is rather well understood, hardly anything is known about what initiates the activation of the regulator(s) of scent production, i.e. the EOBs, during late stages of flower development when petal growth due to cell division has ceased (Weiss and Halevy 1989). 

In the classical ABC model of flower development, 3 classes of MADS-box homeotic genes determine floral organ identity (Coen and Meyerowitz 1991; Riechmann et al. 1996; Egea-Cortines et al. 1999). The B-class genes, together with the A-class genes, determine petals and, together with the C-class genes, specify stamens. E-class homeotic genes were later incorporated into the model, functioning together with the B- and C-classes (Pelaz et al. 2000; Honma and Goto 2001). In petunia, B-class floral organ identity genes DEFICIENS (PhDEF) and GLOBOSA (PhGLO1 and 2), homologs of APETALA3 (AP3) and PISTILLATA (PI) from Arabidopsis thaliana, respectively, control the specification of petal and stamen identities (Vandenbussche et al. 2004; Rijpkema et al. 2006). The GLO1,2/PI genes are homologs of DEF/AP3 and originate from a duplication event that occurred before the origin of the angiosperms (Purugganan et al. 1995; Rijpkema et al. 2006). A major later duplication event in the DEF/AP3 subfamily resulted in 2 types of DEF/AP3-like proteins, which can be distinguished by their C-terminal motifs, and are known as the paleoAP3 and euAP3 motifs (Kramer et al. 1998; Eckardt 2006). In petunia, the DEF/AP3 subfamily consists of 2 factors: PhTM6 (petunia homolog of tomato MADS-box gene 6), from the paleoAP3 lineage, is expressed in and controls stamen and carpel morphogenesis; and PhDEF, from the euAP3 lineage, is expressed in petals and regulates their specification (Vandenbussche et al. 2004; Rijpkema et al. 2006). The floral B-function of DEF/AP3 relies on recognition and binding to the conserved DNA sequence $5 ^ { \prime } – C C ( \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { T } ) _ { 6 } G G - 3 ^ { \prime }$ $5 ^ { \prime }$ , termed CArG box (Riechmann et al. 1996; Hill et al. 1998). 

Although B-class homeotic genes are critical for floral organ specification, their expression persists throughout flower development. Yet very little is known about the involvement of homeotic genes in the processes occurring during flower development, such as the production of showy traits, anthocyanins, and volatiles. Recent research has demonstrated the involvement of PhDEF in petal limb pigmentation during the early stages of petal development; PhDEF binds to the terminator of ANTHOCYANIN 2 (AN2), a MYB component of the MYB–BHLH–WD40 complex regulating anthocyanin production (Chopy et al. 2024). Compromised production of a few volatiles in the weak snapdragon (Antirrhinum majus) def mutant (def nic) was also observed (Manchado-Rojo et al. 2012). Production of phenylpropanoid volatiles and anthocyanins was shown to be interlinked in several flowers, e.g. petunia and rose, because enhancement of the metabolic flux in the phenylpropanoid pathway resulted in an increase in both anthocyanins and volatiles (Zvi et al. 2008, 2012). In carnation, suppression of FLAVANONE 3-HYDROXYLASE, an anthocyanin biosynthetic gene, led to an increase in the flux toward the production of volatiles (Zuker et al. 2002). Similarly, silencing of the gene encoding another pigment-related enzyme, CAFFEOYL-COA O-METHYLTRANSFERASE, positively affected volatile production (Shaipulah et al. 2016). Moreover, the phytohormone gibberellin (GA), which is necessary for anthocyanin production, was shown to negatively affect volatile production in petunia flowers (Ravid et al. 2017). 

In this study, we show that PhDEF orchestrates not only early but also late stages of flower development, as revealed by its effect on scent production in petunia flowers. Suppression of PhDEF by 

virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) resulted in reduced levels of production and emission of phenylpropanoid volatiles with concomitant downregulation in the expression of scent-related genes encoding transcriptional regulators and enzymes of the shikimate and phenylpropanoid pathways. Promoter activity assays demonstrated that PhDEF positively regulates EOBII, EOBI, PAAS, and PAL revealing its role as a positive regulator of floral scent. 

# Results

# Spatial and temporal analyses of PhDEF and PhGLO1,2 expression during flower development in petunia cv. Mitchell

We examined the temporal and spatial expression patterns of homeotic genes PhDEF, PhGLO1, and PhGLO2 across various stages of flower development in petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida) cv. Mitchell (Fig. 1, A to C). The expression of PhDEF increased steadily during bud growth, peaking at 1 d postanthesis (1DPA) (Fig. 1A). At this stage, PhDEF expression was approximately 10-fold higher than that in young buds $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { c m } )$ ) and it remained at this level until 3DPA. PhGLO1 expression reached its highest level at the $2 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ bud stage and remained constant until 3DPA. The fold change in PhGLO1 expression between 2.5 and $0 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ buds was about 2.5 (Fig. 1B). By 4DPA, both PhDEF and PhGLO1 expression showed a strong decline relative to their peak levels. PhGLO2 expression, similar to that of PhGLO1, peaked in $2 . 5 \mathrm { c m }$ buds and then remained relatively stable till the 3DPA stage; at 4DPA, its level decreased by about $5 0 \%$ (Fig. 1C). 

To assess whether PhDEF, PhGLO1, and PhGLO2 display diurnal rhythmicity that is typical for the expression of scent-related genes and production of volatiles (Fenske et al. 2015; Shor et al. 2023b), we probed gene expression at 9 AM and 8 PM (Fig. 1D). The tissue was taken from flowers at 2DPA because scent production is highest at this developmental stage (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010), as was the expression of PhDEF and PhGLO1. The expression of all 3 examined genes increased in the evening, showing a ca. 2-fold increase at 8 PM compared to 9 AM (Fig. 1D), a pattern that coincides with the production/emission of volatiles. 

# PhDEF positively regulates volatile production and emission

To evaluate whether PhDEF contributes to volatile production and emission, the gene was transiently suppressed in cv. Mitchell flowers by VIGS (Spitzer et al. 2007). A 220-bp fragment of its $5 ^ { \prime }$ -UTR was cloned into the Tobacco Rattle Virus RNA2 vector (TRV2-DEF) and introduced into petals of flowers at anthesis using Agrobacterium. Volatile emission was determined 48 h after inoculation using the localized headspace technique followed by GC-MS (Skaliter et al. 2021, 2024). Flower tissue inoculated with the TRV2-CHS (CHALCONE SYNTHASE) vector was used as a control (Spitzer et al. 2007; Skaliter et al. 2024). In TRV2-DEF-inoculated corollas, localized headspace analysis revealed a significant 2.4-fold decrease in the total level of emitted volatiles as compared to control corollas (Fig. 2A). Similar levels of decrease were observed for all tested volatiles originating from distinct branches of the phenylpropanoid pathway. This included a decrease in methyl benzoate and benzaldehyde, major benzenoids from the C6-C1 branch emitted by cv. Mitchell flowers. The emission of other benzenoids, such as benzyl alcohol, benzyl benzoate, and benzyl acetate, also decreased. # PhDEF suppression results in the downregulation of scent-related transcripts

To evaluate the observed changes in volatile production at the gene expression level in TRV2-DEF-inoculated petals, we analyzed the expression of floral scent-related regulatory and biosynthetic genes. Significant downregulation of PhDEF transcript levels, over $9 0 \%$ relative to the control TRV2-CHS-inoculated corollas, was revealed in TRV2-DEF-inoculated petals (Fig. 3A). PhGLO1 and PhGLO2, B-class homeotic genes whose expression relies on a positive autoregulation mechanism based on the DEF–GLO heterodimer (Zachgo et al. 1995), were significantly downregulated in the PhDEF-suppressed corollas (Fig. 3A). Aligned with a substantial decline in scent production (Fig. 2B), there was a significant reduction in the expression levels of the 3 primary positive scent 

regulators, EOII, EOBI, and ODO1, as well as PH4. The expression of PhMYB4 and EOBV, transcriptional repressors of floral scent, did not show a significant reduction (Fig. 3A). Transcript levels of genes involved in the transport of volatiles (ABCG1) and of cuticular monomers (ABCG11, 12) were significantly reduced in PhDEF-suppressed flowers (Adebesin et al. 2017; Liao et al. 2021). Expression of AN1 and AN11, the 2 factors essential for anthocyanin pigment synthesis and shown to be affected by PhDEF at early stages of flower development (Chopy et al. 2024), was also significantly decreased (Fig. 3A). Next, we probed the expression level of the biosynthetic genes in PhDEF-suppressed petals: all investigated shikimate pathway genes showed decreased transcript levels compared to the PhCHS control (Fig. 3B). These included transcript levels of genes encoding the 2 enzymes that initiate the pathway toward chorismate, DAHPS and EPSPS, as well as CM and AROGENATE DEHYDRATASE (ADT), encoding enzymes responsible for converting chorismate to prephenate and arogenate into L-Phe, respectively (Lynch and Dudareva 2020). A similar effect was observed in the transcript levels of 2 key enzymes in the phenylpropanoid pathway, PAL and PAAS (Fig. 3B). The transcript level of BENZOIC ACID/SALICYLIC ACID CARBOXYL METHYLTRANSFERASE (BSMT), which encodes the enzyme catalyzing the biosynthesis of methyl benzoate (Negre et al. 2003), was significantly reduced, by approximately $90 \%$ , compared to the control TRV2-CHS-inoculated flowers (Fig. 3B). The developmental expression patterns of PhDEF and PhGLO1/ 2 in cv. Mitchell flowers revealed that their transcript continues to be expressed during the late stages of flower development. Similar expression trends of B-class homeotic genes have been observed in petunia (Petunia $\times$ hybrida) cv. V26 and snapdragon (A. majus) (Tröbner et al. 1992; van der Krol et al. 1993; Manchado-Rojo et al. 2012), suggesting their role in cell processes occurring during later stages of petal maturation. Autoregulation as the molecular basis for persistent gene expression during development has been proven for several homeotic genes (Crews and Pearson 2009). Therefore, the high expression levels of PhDEF and PhGLO1/2 transcripts in mature petals may be maintained by an autoregulatory loop that is based on formation of the DEF–GLO heterodimer (Schwarz-Sommer et al. 1992). Studies on the regulation of PhDEF and PhGLO1/2 expression have revealed that PhDEF is required for PhGLO1/2 transcription in petunia petals. In buds, both PhGLO1 and PhGLO2 are required for normal PhDEF accumulation (Vandenbussche et al. 2004). The expression levels of PhDEF, as well as PhGLO1/2, observed in our study can also be achieved and maintained, at least in part, through the autoregulatory mechanism. In line with this, suppression of PhDEF led to decreased transcript levels of PhGLO1 and PhGLO2. In contrast, suppression of PhGLO1, PhGLO2, or both did not impact PhDEF expression, indicating that they are not required for maintaining high levels of PhDEF transcript in mature flowers. 

The expression levels of PhDEF and PhGLO1/2 showed diurnal variation, with higher transcript levels detected in the evening. This trend aligns with the activation of scent-related genes and the synthesis of phenylpropanoid volatiles toward evening in Petunia $\times$ hybrida (Fenske et al. 2015; Shor et al. 2023b). Furthermore, the changes in transcript levels of PhDEF and PhGLO1/2 closely mirrored those of EOBI, EOBII, and ODO1, the major transcriptional regulators of scent production in petunia (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010, 2012; Van Moerkercke et al. 2011). The similarity in developmental expression patterns between PhDEF, PhGLO1/2, and scent-related transcription factors points to the involvement of homeotic genes in regulating floral scent biosynthesis. 

As def-knockout mutants trigger homeotic conversion, preventing normal petal development (de Vlaming et al. 1984; van der Krol et al. 1993; Chopy et al. 2024), the VIGS approach allowed us to explore the effect of PhDEF suppression on volatile production and emission in mature petal tissue. The downregulation of PhDEF in petunia corollas significantly reduced the production and emission of phenylpropanoid volatiles, as well as the expression of genes encoding transcription factors and scent-related biosynthetic enzymes, highlighting its crucial role as a positive regulator of floral scent production. In contrast, suppression of PhGLO1, PhGLO2, or both did not affect scent emission or scent-related genes, indicating they are not involved in scent regulation. 

The impact of PhDEF on scent production is specifically linked to the phenylpropanoid pathway, as evidenced by its insignificant effect on terpenoid pathway-derived volatiles. The decline in the levels of phenylpropanoid compounds across different branches (C6-C1, C6-C2, C6-C3) observed in the internal pools and emitted volatiles in PhDEF-suppressed petals (Fig. 2) indicates a general influence of PhDEF on the production/emission machinery governing floral scent. Indeed, the expression levels of key transcription factors—EOBII, EOBI, and ODO1—and biosynthetic genes DAHPS, EPSPS, CM, PAL, CFAT, and IGS were significantly 

reduced following PhDEF suppression. The negative regulators PhMYB4, which affects the coumaric acid branch of the pathway via C4H (Colquhoun et al. 2011), and EOBV, whose mode of action is still unknown, were not affected by PhDEF. This indicates that the decrease in expression levels of several genes in the C6-C3 branch of the pathway—CFAT, IGS, and EGS—is modulated by PhDEF either directly or via EOBI and EOBII (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010, 2012). We did not observe alterations in BPBT expression, despite the decreased levels of its products benzyl benzoate and phenylethyl benzoate. The reduced accumulation of these compounds may stem from decreased availability of the substrates, benzyl alcohol and phenylacetaldehyde, which are necessary for the enzymatic reactions catalyzed by BPBT (Skaliter et al. 2022). Insufficient flux into the pathway was also suggested by Spitzer-Rimon et al. (2012) and Verdonk et al. (2005) in their studies on EOBI- and ODO1-suppressed corollas, respectively, wherein BPBT products, but not transcript levels, were affected. The significant downregulation of the genes known to specifically regulate volatile emission, PH4 and ABCG1, -11, -12 (Cna’ani et al. 2015; Adebesin et al. 2017; Liao et al. 2021) supports the conclusion that PhDEF plays a regulatory role in both phenylpropanoid volatile biosynthesis and the emission machinery. 

The regulation of floral scent by PhDEF is probably not directly linked to the circadian clock or GA signaling. Key components of the circadian system, such as LHY, which has been shown to regulate volatile production (Fenske et al. 2015), and PRR73 and PRR59, remained unaffected by the suppression of PhDEF. Similarly, PhDEF had no impact on the expression of the key component of GA signaling, DELLAs, shown to regulate scent production (Ravid et al. 2017). 

Despite recognition of the MYB triad (ODO1, EOBI, and EOBII) as the primary regulator of scent production in petunia flowers, regulation of their activation has remained elusive. Here we reveal that PhDEF transcriptionally activates promoters of both EOBI and EOBII. Moreover, the scent-related biosynthetic genes PAL and PAAS were also found to be transcriptionally upregulated by PhDEF. The latter is evident from the similar activation patterns of PAL and PAAS promoters by PhDEF observed not only in petunia petals but also in leaves, where EOBI and EOBII expression is negligible. This suggests that while EOBI and EOBII can activate their promoters (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2010, 2012), the activation of PAL and PAAS is not solely mediated by these MYBs. Promoters of these 4 genes contain PhDEF’s recognition and binding CArG boxlike sequences (Schwarz-Sommer et al. 1992; Hill et al. 1998). Taken together, these results demonstrate that PhDEF not only activates the cascade of MYB transcription factors in mature flowers but also transcriptionally controls key scent-related phenylpropanoid pathway enzymes; in this way, the metabolic flux can be tightly controlled at several key points along the pathway. It should be noted that in-depth analyses of PhDEF-DNA interaction(s) are necessary for establishing direct regulation of downstream targets. 

PhDEF’s function in petunia is dependent on PhGLO1/2 for petal specification and anthocyanin production (van der Krol et al. 1993; Chopy et al. 2024). Nonetheless, our results suggest that PhDEF’s effect on volatiles is independent of PhGLO1/2 because downregulation of the PhGLOs did not affect volatile production. In line with this result, self-activation and transactivation of EOBI, EOBII, PAL, and PAAS by ectopic expression of PhDEF has been detected in leaves, which do not accumulate MADS-box transcription factors, e.g. GLO1/2 (Coen 1992; Krizek and Meyerowitz 1996). It is important to note that, as shown in meristems, DEF is not sufficient for GLOs’ transcription initiation (Schwarz-Sommer et al. 1992; 

Tröbner et al. 1992). GLO-independent activity of DEF homologs has been reported in other organisms. In tomato, TAP3 has been shown to interact with TM5 (the homolog of SEPALLATA 3, an E-class homeotic gene) in the absence of TPI (a GLO-like protein) (de Martino et al. 2006). Furthermore, the DEF homolog in Drosophila, MEF2, is functional without GLO-like counterparts and can bind to DNA as a homodimer or monomer (Santelli and Richmond 2000; Nguyen et al. 2002). All of this supports that PhDEF’s regulation of volatile production in petunia flowers may also be independent of PhGLOs, functioning through interactions with as yet unidentified molecular partners, as monomers or homodimers, and that the transcriptional mechanisms by which PhDEF regulates volatile production may differ from that controlling flower morphogenesis. Initiation of organ specification during flower morphogenesis necessities collaborative activities of PhDEF with numerous factors from different classes of homeotic regulators: SEP, AP1, PhGLO1/2, PhTM6, and FBP6 (Litt and Kramer 2010; Honma and Goto 2001). In contrast, promoters of EOBI, EOBII, PAL, and PAAS were activated by ectopic expression of PhDEF in leaves. The latter, together with findings of Chopy et al. (2024), suggest that while homeotic conversion of leaves into flowers necessitates rewiring of the regulatory network, developmental program behind petal showy traits (scent and pigments) may be based on fewer master regulators. 

Our findings, along with previous research, underscore the significance of the B-class homeotic gene DEF as a master regulator determining floral traits crucial for pollinator attraction: petal specification, morphology, color, and scent. This may point to an intriguing mechanism developed by plants to facilitate crosstalk between flower development and its pollination traits. The factor(s) that interact with PhDEF and allow it to adjust to a wide range of biological activities remain to be discovered. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant material and growth conditions

Petunia $\times$ hybrida cv. Mitchell plants were grown in a glasshouse at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ day/ $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ night with a 16-h light/8-h dark photoperiod. 

# RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis, and RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from 30 to 100 mg of tissue homogenized in liquid nitrogen using Bio-Tri RNA reagent (Bio-Lab). Samples were collected from 4 to 5 plants 2DPA. Extracted RNA was treated with RNase-free DNase I (Thermo Fisher Scientific). First-strand cDNA was synthesized using 1 μg of total RNA, oligo(dT) primer, and reverse transcriptase ImProm-II (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RT-qPCR was performed for 35 cycles $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min and then 2 steps of cycling at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 s, $6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 35 s) in the presence of PCRBIO SyGreen Blue Mix Hi-ROX (PCR Biosystems) on a CFX Opus 384 Real-Time PCR System (BioRad). UBIQUITIN (UBQ) and ELONGATION FACTOR 1α (EF1α) were used for normalization. Quantification calculations were carried out using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C T } }$ formula as described previously (Czechowski et al. 2005). Primer specificity was determined by melting curve analysis. The primers are shown in Supplementary Table S1. 

# VIGS suppression of target genes in petunia flowers

Local transient VIGS was performed using TRV (Spitzer et al. 2007). To suppress PhDEF, a 220-bp fragment of its 5′-UTR was amplified from cDNA using primers 5′-ggatccgggtttgagtacttaagatctcctt-3′ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -tctagatgaattcatgaagcttgccagt- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ and inserted into the TRV2 

vector (TRV2-DEF) using restriction enzymes BamHI and XbaI. Two additional fragments PhDEF— $- 5 ^ { \prime }$ -UTR-#2 (240 bp) and 3′-UTR (260 bp)—were amplified with the following primer pairs: $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aa aggatccacacctttcacggttttg-3′ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaatctagacacgagccatagtgttc ta-3′; and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaaggatccgagtagtagtaatc and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaatctagagttgaaga aaaatg-3′, respectively. The fragments were cloned separately into the TRV2 vector using restriction enzymes BamHI and XbaI. To suppress PhGLO1, a 255-bp fragment of its $3 ^ { \prime }$ -UTR region was amplified with primers 5′-aaaggatccaccttgatctacttggtgaagacct- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaatctagaatacacgcttcatccaccact- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ and cloned into the TRV2 vector (TRV2-GLO1) using restriction enzymes BamHI and XbaI. To suppress PhGLO2, a 260-bp fragment of its 3′-UTR was amplified using primers $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaactcgagttacgaataccagcagatg- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaagaattcc ttcaatattcagtacaaccattagg-3′ and cloned into the TRV2 vector (TRV2-GLO2) using restriction enzymes XhoI and EcoRI. To generate the TRV2-GLO1/2 vector, a 260-bp fragment of PhGLO2’s $3 ^ { \prime }$ -UTR was amplified using primers $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaactcgagttacgaataccagcagatg- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ and $5 ^ { \prime }$ -aaaggatcccttcaatattcagtacaaccattagg- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ and was cloned into the TRV2-GLO1 vector using restriction enzymes BamHI and XhoI. The TRV2-CHS vector was used as a control for inoculations (Spitzer et al. 2007). Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain AGLO was transformed with the cloned vectors and with the TRV1 vector. The Agrobacterium cultures were grown overnight at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in LB medium with $5 0 \mathrm { m g / }$ mL kanamycin and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone. Cells were harvested and resuspended in a buffer containing $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone and 10 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ . Agrobacterium cultures carrying the TRV1 vector were mixed with the cultures carrying either TRV2-CHS or TRV2-DEF, TRV2-GLO1, TRV2-GLO2, or TRV2-GLO1/2 vectors at a ratio of 1:1 (to a final $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 6 0 }$ of 0.5) in inoculation solution containing $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone and $1 0 \mathrm { m u } \mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ . Corollas of petunia cv. Mitchell flowers at anthesis were inoculated with the Agrobacterium solution by piercing with a needle and infiltrating using a syringe. After $4 8 \mathrm { { h } }$ , volatile emission and accumulation in internal pools were analyzed as described previously (Skaliter et al. 2021). 

# Collection of emitted volatiles, internal pools, and GC-MS analysis

Localized headspace was conducted on 11 to 13 independent petunia flowers 2DPA (Skaliter et al. 2021). Volatiles were collected for $\neg 6 \mathrm { h }$ using columns made of glass tubes containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ Porapak Q polymer and $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of 20/40-mesh, held in place with plugs of silanized glass wool. Trapped volatiles were eluted with $1 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ hexane and $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L }$ acetone. Isobutyl benzene was used as an internal standard. To assay the accumulation of volatile compounds in the internal pools, $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ petal tissue was collected from 8 to 11 independent corollas 2DPA at 6 PM. The tissue was ground in liquid nitrogen and extracted in $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ hexane. Following 2-h incubation with gentle shaking at ambient temperature, the extracts were centrifuged at ${ 1 0 , 5 0 0 \times g }$ for 10 min and subjected to GC-MS analyses of volatile organic compounds. GC-MS analysis was performed using a device composed of a PAL autosampler (CTC Analytics) and a TRACE GC 2000 gas chromatograph (Thermo Fisher Scientific) equipped with an Rtx-5SIL mass spectrometer fused silica capillary column (Restek), and a TRACE DSQ quadrupole mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Helium was used as a carrier gas at a flow rate of 1 mL/min. The injector temperature was set to $2 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The analysis was performed under the following program: 2 min of isothermal heating at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ followed by a 7-min oven temperature ramp to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ then 2 min of isothermal heating. The system was equilibrated for 1 min at $7 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ before injecting the next sample. Mass spectra were recorded at 

3.15 scans/s with a scanning range of 40 to 350 mass-to-charge ratio and electron energy of 70 eV. Compounds were identified based on NIST/EPA/NIH Mass Spectral Library data version NIST 05 using xCALIBUR 1.3 (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Further identification of major compounds was based on a comparison of mass spectra and retention times with those of standards (Sigma-Aldrich) analyzed under the same conditions. 

# Promoter activity assay

To assay activation of EOBI, EOBII, PAL, PAAS, and PhDEF promoters, vectors EOBIp:DsRed, EOBIIp:DsRed (Spitzer-Rimon et al. 2012), PALp:DsRed, PAASp:DsRed (Shor et al. 2023a), and DEF: DsRed, respectively, were transformed into Agrobacterium strain AGLO. These were coinjected into petunia petals at anthesis or leaves together with Agrobacterium carrying vectors: 35S:DEF (DEF) or 35S: MYBYS (MYBYS); the latter used as a negative control and 35S:GFP as the internal control for the inoculation (Skaliter et al. 2024). Half of each tissue was injected with control and the other with the treatment. The inoculated regions were imaged $6 0 \mathrm { { h } }$ after infiltration using a fluorescent stereomicroscope (SMZ1270, Nikon, https://www.nikon.com/) under UV light with DsRed and GFP filters and the working distance of $7 0 \mathrm { m m }$ . Images were taken with a zoom objective at magnification of $1 \times$ (upper panel) and $1 3 . 5 \times$ (lower panel). Excitation wavelength range for GFP and DsRed was 450 to 490 and 570 nm, respectively. The detected emission wavelength for GFP and DsRed was 435 to 485 and 590 to $6 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. The exposure time for GFP was 100 ms and for DsRed 1,000 ms. The levels of DsRed and GFP signal intensity in the images were measured by Fiji (ImageJ) software (Rueden et al. 2017). For fluorescence quantification, 5 to 7 different infiltrated petals and leaves were analyzed. In each petal/leaf, GFP and DsRed fluorescence was measured in 5 to 8 distinct, evenly sized patches. The detected DsRed fluorescence in a patch was normalized to that of GFP in the same patch. The DsRed/GFP ratios in each infiltrated tissue were averaged and were considered as 1 biological replicate. 

# Prediction of MADS-box transcription factor binding sites

Sequences of PhDEF, EOBI, EOBII, PAL, and PAAS used in the promoter activity assays were scanned with 33 MADS-box transcription factor matrices from the JASPAR 2024 database (http://jaspar. genereg.net) (Rauluseviciute et al. 2024). Relative scores above 0.85 were plotted against their genomic position.
