# Fatty acid-derived signals in plants

Hans Weber

Plants synthesize many fatty acid derivatives, several of which play important regulatory roles. Jasmonates are the best characterized examples. Jasmonateinsensitive mutants and mutants with a constitutive jasmonate response have given us new insights into jasmonate signalling. The jasmonate biosynthesis mutant opr3 allowed the dissection of cyclopentanone and cyclopentenone signalling, thus defining specific roles for these molecules. Jasmonate signalling is a complex network of individual signals and recent findings on specific activities of methyl jasmonate and (Z )-jasmone add to this picture. In addition, there are keto, hydroxy and hydroperoxy fatty acids that might be involved in cell death and the expression of stress-related genes. Finally, there are bruchins and volicitin, signal molecules from insects that are perceived by plants in the picomole to femtomole range. They highlight the importance of fatty acid-derived molecules in interspecies communication and in plant defence.

Biomembranes are probably as old as living organisms because they are the key element separating a cell’s contents from its environment. Biomembranes act as interfaces between cells, between cells and their environment, and between organelles and the cytoplasm. It is evident that membranes are a key site of signal perception and transmission. However, membranes are themselves an important source of signalling molecules, many of which are derived from fatty acids. These molecules can act as intracellular mediators but also as extracellular signals and even as signals for communication between organisms.

# Jasmonate signals

Jasmonic acid and its volatile methyl ester (MeJA) are by far the best-studied fatty acid-derived signals in plants. The biosynthesis of jasmonic acid occurs via the octadecanoid pathway, starting with linolenic acid (Fig. 1) [1]. In Arabidopsis [a ‘16:3’ plant; that is, it synthesizes part of its fatty acids via the prokaryotic pathway and contains $7 ( Z ) , 1 0 ( Z ) , 1 3 ( Z )$ hexadecatrienoic acid (16:3) in its chloroplast galactolipids], there is a hexadecanoid pathway in parallel with the octadecanoid pathway [2]. The first cyclic compounds in jasmonate biosynthesis are the cyclopentenones 12-oxo-phytodienoic acid (OPDA) and dinor OPDA (dnOPDA), which are subsequently reduced to cyclopentanones and $\beta$ -oxidized to jasmonic acid. To simplify the discussion of this important group of molecules, I here use the term ‘jasmonates’ for the cyclic biochemical precursors of jasmonic acid, for jasmonic acid itself and for its derivatives.

Jasmonic acid signalling is now known to be crucial for plant stress responses following wounding, interaction with various pathogens, ultraviolet light

and insect attack, but jasmonic acid is also indispensable for anther dehiscence and pollen development. The biological effects of jasmonic acid have been reviewed extensively [3–5] and a recent review addresses the importance of the jasmonateresponsive transcription factor ORCA [6]. Accordingly, I focus here on exciting new findings that are furthering our understanding of jasmonate signalling, including new signalling mutants and the role of individual jasmonates.

# Jasmonate signalling mutants

It is of crucial importance to identify components of the jasmonate signalling pathway and especially the jasmonate receptor(s). When supplied via the growth medium, jasmonic acid and MeJA have profound effects on seedling development, including the inhibition of root growth and the accumulation of anthocyanins. Mutant screens using jasmonic acid and the bacterial toxin coronatine (which is a structural analogue of OPDA [7] and also mimics some biological effects of OPDA) led to the isolation of several jasmonic acid-insensitive mutants (jin, jar and coi [8]) (Table 1). COI1 was cloned and found to encode a protein with 16 leucine-rich repeats and an F-box motif [9]. F-box proteins are involved in the ubiquitin-conjugation pathway of protein degradation [10]. Thus, COI1 might recruit regulatory proteins involved in the jasmonate response for ubiquitination and degradation. The coi1 phenotype includes the loss of resistance to insect damage [11], defects in the response to a fungal pathogen [12] and male sterility [9]. These phenotypes indicate that COI1 is a central component of the jasmonate signalling pathway.

To date, genetic screens have not identified a jasmonic acid receptor. There might be several redundant jasmonic acid receptors, which would make it impossible to identify them by a jasmonic acid-insensitivity screen. It is therefore important to consider recently developed strategies to identify components of the jasmonic acid signalling pathway that take the opposite approach and aim to isolate mutants with constitutively active jasmonic acid responses (Table 1). The cex1 mutant (constant expression of jasmonic acid-inducible genes) was isolated by screening for Arabidopsis seedlings that exhibit a phenotype similar to a jasmonic acid response (short root, stunted growth) [13]. The cex1 mutation is recessive and causes the overexpression of the jasmonic acid-responsive genes THI2.1, VSP and PDF1.2. The Arabidopsis mutant cev1 (constitutive expression of VSP) was isolated in a screen for constant activation of the jasmonic acid-responsive VSP1 promoter [14]. Like cex1, cev1 is recessive and leads to the constitutive expression of the defence-related genes PDF1.2, THI2.1, VSP1, VSP2 and CHI-B. Plants with the cev1 mutation accumulate anthocyanins and have short roots with prolific root hairs. Unlike CEX1, CEV1 is not located downstream of COI1. Using double mutants, the full cev1 response has been found to require both COI1 (for VSP expression and accumulation of anthocyanins) and ETR1 (for root phenotype). CEV1 might thus be a negative regulator of the jasmonic acid and ethylene pathways. However, there are as yet no biochemical data showing that cev1 plants contain altered levels of jasmonates.

A similar approach was used to look for Arabidopsis mutants with a constitutive activation of

the jasmonic acid-responsive THI2.1 promoter [15]. This screen led to the isolation of ten cet (constitutive expression of thionin) mutants, of which three were dominant. Interestingly, most of these mutants displayed spontaneous leaf-cell necrosis, a phenotype not seen in previously described mutants of jasmonic acid signalling. Three mutants were analysed for their levels of OPDA and jasmonic acid, and two (cet1 and cet3) had markedly elevated levels of both compounds. The cet mutants have yet to be analysed in detail but they are likely to yield new and exciting information about jasmonate signalling. (For complementary information about the genetics of jasmonic acid signalling see Ref. [16]).

Dissecting cyclopentanone and cyclopentenone signals When looking at the prostaglandin pathway in animals, plant scientists were always tempted to speculate that the biosynthetic precursors of jasmonic acid might also display biological activity. Unambiguous proof for this came from the highly sensitive biological assay of Bryonia tendril coiling, which showed that OPDA is more active than jasmonic acid and is also the relevant in vivo

compound responsible for this response [17]. In other plant systems, it has been harder to define signalling roles for individual members of the jasmonate family. Distinct biological activities for OPDA and jasmonic acid were found in the elicitation of volatiles in Lima bean (Phaseolus lunatus) [18]. However, analysis of the activity of jasmonates in tomato indicated that it was more likely that jasmonic acid and its metabolites are the only biologically active compounds in vivo [19]. This was also the conclusion of a comprehensive study on structure–activity relationships of jasmonates in Eschscholzia californica cell cultures [20].

The recent identification and characterization of the opr3 (also called delayed dehiscence 1) Arabidopsis mutant [21,22] seemed to support the scenario of jasmonic acid being the only active molecule, not the jasmonic acid precursors, at least in Arabidopsis. The opr3 mutant lacks the OPDA reductase isoform necessary for jasmonate biosynthesis [23] and is thus blocked in the jasmonic acid biosynthesis pathway after OPDA and dnOPDA (Fig. 1). This mutant is thus the ideal genetic tool to dissect cyclopentenone (dnOPDA, OPDA) signalling from cyclopentanone (OPCs and jasmonic acid) signalling in vivo. Plants with the opr3 mutation have delayed anther dehiscence and a defect in pollen development resulting in male sterility. Application of jasmonic acid but not OPDA restored fertility, clearly showing that jasmonic acid is the active compound needed for male gamete development. However, serendipity and careful observation led to an unexpected discovery: opr3 plants grown in the greenhouse were resistant to the dipteran Bradysia impatiens, as were wild-type plants, whereas the mutant coi1 and the triple mutant fad3 fad7 fad8 (which contains almost no linolenic acid and hexadecatrienoic acid, and cannot synthesize any jasmonates) died as a result of impaired resistance because of the lack of jasmonate signalling. The opr3 plants also showed strong induction of the jasmonic acid-dependent defensin gene PDF1.2 after infection with the fungal pathogen Alternaria brassicicola. Analysis of the concentration of individual jasmonates revealed that, as expected, wounded leaves of opr3 accumulated dnOPDA and OPDA, but no detectable 3R,7S-jasmonic acid (the biologically active isomer) and only traces of $3 R , 7 R $ -jasmonic acid [11].

Wounding of wild-type Arabidopsis plants was previously found to cause the upregulation of two groups of genes: COI1-dependent genes (regulated by jasmonic acid) and COI1-independent genes (which require other signals than jasmonic acid for their induction) [24]. Transcript profiling of opr3 leaves showed that, in spite of the lack of jasmonic acid, many genes that are thought to depend on jasmonic acid and COI1 for their induction were upregulated after wounding. This suggests a role for the cyclopentenones dnOPDA and OPDA in the induction of these genes. Applying OPDA to opr3

plants (which cannot convert OPDA to jasmonic acid) revealed that OPDA upregulates many, but not all, genes that depend on COI1 and are induced by jasmonic acid, and also upregulates several COI1- independent genes that do not respond to jasmonic acid. OPDA signalling thus occurs via two distinct downstream pathways. Yet the exact mechanism of the COI1-independent pathway has to be analysed in detail. It is possible that OPDA exerts some of its biological activity via an electrophile effect of the cyclopentenone ring, which contains an $\alpha , \beta$ -unsaturated carbonyl group. It is important to realize that cyclopentenone prostaglandins in mammals also require the $\alpha , \beta$ -unsaturated carbonyl group for mediating important cellular effects, which are distinct from the ones caused by prostaglandins that lack this reactive group [25]. Jasmonate cyclopentanones (like jasmonic acid) and cyclopentenones (dnOPDA and OPDA) thus display individual but also overlapping biological activities. The emerging picture is that jasmonate signalling is composed of a complex network of individual signalling molecules and varying activities of these compounds are found in different plant species and even within different tissues of a single plant.

# Power of volatiles

One of the exciting features of jasmonate signalling is that plants can transform jasmonic acid by methylation to its volatile counterpart MeJA (Fig. 1), which can act as a signal in interplant communication [26,27] and might also move in the intercellular spaces within the plant. Hence, one could argue that the only role of MeJA is to mobilize jasmonic acid. However, a surprising finding came from a study in which the S-adenosyl-L-methionine–jasmonic acid carboxyl methyltransferase (JMT) (the jasmonic acid-methylating enzyme in Arabidopsis) was overproduced [28]. Transgenic Arabidopsis plants overproducing JMT had constantly elevated levels (threefold more than the control) of MeJA but normal levels of jasmonic acid. The plants also showed a high constitutive expression of jasmonic acid-responsive genes. This is remarkable because overproduction of another jasmonate biosynthetic enzyme, allene oxide synthase, either did not result in changes in the amounts of jasmonates in unwounded plants [29] or, in the case of a study performed in potato, resulted in enhanced jasmonic acid levels without having an effect on gene expression [30]. Therefore, the hypothesis that changes in concentration of jasmonic acid rather than the absolute levels are responsible for a biological effect [31] does not apply to MeJA. One might speculate that MeJA is perceived differently than other jasmonates by the plant. JMT-knockout plants that are unable to synthesize MeJA might provide an answer to this question.

# Biologically active keto, hydroxy and hydroperoxy fatty acids

In addition to jasmonates, plants can synthesize a huge variety of oxylipins (enzymatically oxygenated fatty acids [33]) and many, if not all, might display some biological activity. In spite of this, our knowledge of their biological function lags far behind our understanding of the role of jasmonates. In this section, I describe the first reports of the probable biological functions of keto, hydroxy and hydroperoxy fatty acids in the plant defence response.

To study the ‘oxylipin signature’ of plants during stress is a helpful tool with which to search for putative regulatory oxylipins. Oxylipin profiling of barley leaves treated with salicylic acid revealed the accumulation of 13(S)-hydroxy octadecatrienoic acid (13-HOTrE) [34]. Salicylic acid is a key compound in establishing systemic-acquired resistance and induces the expression of the pathogenesis-related protein PR1b in barley. Interestingly, PR1b transcripts were also induced after treating barley leaves with 13-HOTrE, suggesting that salicylic acid signalling in barley might be mediated, at least in part, by 13-HOTrE.

A study in Arabidopsis revealed that ketodienoic fatty acids [9-keto-octadecadienoic acid (9-KODE) and 13-KODE] accumulate after wounding and infection with Pseudomonas syringae [35]. KODEs contain a reactive $\alpha , \beta$ -unsaturated carbonyl group that can undergo a Michael addition reaction. Upon infiltration into Arabidopsis leaves, KODEs induced the expression of the gene that encodes glutathione-$S$ -transferase (GST1) and also caused cell death. By using small volatile molecules that contain the $\alpha , \beta$ -unsaturated carbonyl moiety and counterparts that lack the double bond, it was shown that GST1 induction by these molecules depends on the presence of this reactive group [35].

Hydroperoxy, hydroxy and keto fatty acids are likely to be produced in considerable amounts during pathogenesis and especially during the hypersensitive reaction via lipoxygenase (LOX) action. It is tempting to speculate that these LOX pathway products are important regulators of pathogenesis-related and oxidative-stress-related genes as well as of cell death (Fig. 2). It has been shown that the LOX-dependent formation of fatty acid hydroperoxides is necessary for the development of hypersensitive cell death in tobacco [36]. Recently, a second enzymatic system that generates fatty acid hydroperoxides was discovered in plants. The enzyme is an α-dioxygenase (α-DOX) that produces 2-hydroperoxy fatty acids [37]. In Arabidopsis, $\alpha$ -DOXis strongly induced after infection with Pseudomonas syringae and lipid-derived $\alpha$ -DOX reaction products are speculated to protect plant tissues from oxidative stress and cell death [38].

# Non-plant fatty acid-derived signals

Studies of plant–insect interactions have led to the recent discovery of two highly active lipid-like molecules that are perceived by the plant but derived from the insect. The first example concerns a defence response of pea plants against pea weevil (Bruchus pisorum). Volicitin and bruchins might represent specialized insect-derived elicitors that are recognized by plants. However, plants might contain analogous signalling

molecules that work via the same pathway and cause the same biological response as these insect-derived factors. The discovery of endogenous plant bruchins would be of tremendous importance because these factors are mitogenic and so might be regulators of plant development. Hints that ω-hydroxy fatty acids are involved in plant development come from the recently characterized Arabidopsis mutant lacerata (lcr) [44]. The LCR gene encodes a cytochrome P450 mono-oxygenase, CYP86A8, which hydroxylates C12 to C18 fatty acids at the ω-carbon. The mutant phenotype includes many developmental anomalies, such as organ fusions, reduced apical dominance, delayed senescence, irregular leaf shape and lower fertility, and lcr is also affected in trichome development, suggesting retarded cell differentiation. It is too early to say whether these phenotypes result from the lack or alteration of fatty acid-derived signals or whether they are mainly the result of changes in the cuticle, which is composed of different types of ω-hydroxylated fatty acids. Interestingly, a phenotype with similarities to lcr was found in transgenic Arabidopsis plants that overexpress a cutinase gene isolated from Fusarium solani f. sp. pisi [45].

# Conclusion

Within the past few years, we have gained many new insights into the roles of fatty acid-based molecules as potent signals in plants as a result of the use of genetic approaches as well as sensitive biochemical methods. The jasmonate field is developing rapidly and we should have a more profound understanding of their signalling components in the near future as a result of the characterization of several newly identified mutants. Furthermore, jasmonate signalling demonstrates the complex scenario of the presence of multiple signalling molecules in a biochemical cascade, a situation similar to prostaglandin signalling in mammals. One also has to be aware of the recently discovered ‘jasmonatelike’ cyclopentenones formed from 9-(S)-hydroperoxy octadecadienoic acid [46] by potato stolon homogenates and the occurrence of phytoprostanes F1 (analogues of mammalian isoprostane F2) in different plant species [47,48], which are likely to display biological activities, although this has not yet been proved.

One of the future challenges in the jasmonate field will be to understand the regulation of the intracellular fluxes of jasmonates (i.e. the regulation of OPDA retention in or release from the chloroplast) and the role of OPDA esterified to lipids [49]. It has been suggested that the function of the OPDA–lipid is to provide a pool from which OPDA could be rapidly released in response to a stimulus. In this respect, it is interesting that the newly discovered lipase DAD1, which is an essential component of jasmonic acid signalling in Arabidopsis flowers, hydrolyses lipids at the sn1 position [50]. It is evident that, apart from jasmonates, there are many other fatty acid-derived signals in plants. Following the initial reports of their activity, we must now begin to dissect these signalling pathways genetically. The knowledge of the Arabidopsis genome should be of enormous help to identify candidate biosynthetic genes and to search for respective knockout mutants. Furthermore, the availability of DNA microarrays will be an important tool for

investigating the signalling function and pathways of individual fatty acid-derived regulators. Although this favours Arabidopsis as a model plant for research in fatty acid signalling, we should not overlook other plant species because these might synthesize and perceive different fatty acid-derived signals. As well as being important regulators in plants, fatty acidderived volatiles such as aldehydes, jasmone and MeJA contribute to the rich world of flavours, and they will certainly also add to our pleasure outside the laboratory door.

# Towards genomic and proteomic studies of protein phosphorylation in plant–pathogen interactions

Tim Xing, Thérèse Ouellet and Brian L. Miki

Phosphorylation is an effective method of post-translational protein modification but understanding its significance is hindered by its biological complexity. Many protein kinases and phosphatases have been identified that connect signal perception mechanisms to plant defence responses. Recent studies of mitogen-activated protein kinases, calcium-dependent protein kinases and other kinases and phosphatases have revealed some important mechanisms, but have also raised new questions. The regulation of any phosphorylation pathway is complex and dynamic. There are many protein kinases and phosphatases in the plant genome, which makes it hard to delineate the phosphorylation machinery fully. Genomics and proteomics have already identified new components and will continue to influence the study of phosphorylation profoundly in plant–pathogen interactions.

Protein phosphorylation is one of the pivotal processes that take place during plant–pathogen interactions. Biochemically, phosphorylation is the most abundant covalent modification of proteins and it is among the best understood processes involved in the regulation of plant cells. The phosphorylation of a protein can alter its intrinsic biological activity, subcellular location and half-life (Fig. 1). Docking with other proteins can also be altered and so phosphorylation is a crucial factor in the integration of signals within the cell. Furthermore, it can be

important for the extent and duration of a response. Any of the above aspects can determine the final effectiveness of defence mechanisms. This complexity is further reflected by the current genome sequencing effort. Within the Arabidopsis genome, there are $\mathbf { \Gamma } _ { \sim 1 0 0 0 }$ protein kinase genes and ~200 phosphatase genes, including 23 mitogenactivated protein kinases (MAPKs), 9 MAPK kinases (MAPKKs), ${ > } 2 5$ MAPKK kinases (MAPKKKs) [1] and 29 calcium-dependent protein kinases (CDPKs) (http://plantsp.sdsc.edu/). This clearly indicates why research on kinases is so important.
