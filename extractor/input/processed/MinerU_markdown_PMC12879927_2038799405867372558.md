Supporting Information 

ABSTRACT: Streptomyces scabies is the first identified common scab-causing pathogen, producing thaxtomin A as a principal virulence factor. Interestingly, S. scabies also produces the phytohormone indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), but how it fine-tunes the production of phytotoxins and phytohormones remains poorly understood. Here, we found that BldDss from S. scabies acts as a dualfunction regulator, activating thaxtomin biosynthesis while repressing IAA biosynthesis, both derived from L-tryptophan (Trp). BldDss directly bound to the txtR and txtE promoters to promote thaxtomin biosynthesis. bldDss deletion led to knockdownvirulence of S. scabies on potato tuber slices and radish seedlings, and unexpectedly promoted lateral root development in Arabidopsis thaliana. In S. scabies, the iaaH−iaaM operon was mainly responsible for IAA biosynthesis, and such production was largely dependent on Trp. Furthermore, BldDss repressed IAA biosynthesis by directly binding to the iaaH−iaaM promoter. This study demonstrates BldD’s dual regulatory function in a plant-pathogenic Streptomyces species for the first time. 

KEYWORDS: Streptomyces scabies, phytotoxin and phytohormone, BldDss, thaxtomin A, indole-3-acetic acid, virulence regulation 

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Mycelial spore-forming streptomycetes are widely distributed in soil and undergo complex developmental life cycle. Many members of this genus are renowned for producing industrially and pharmaceutically valuable secondary metabolites.1 In addition to their applications in medicine, several Streptomyces species are agriculturally important due to their production of antimicrobial compounds�such as kasugamycin,2 validamycin,3 and oxytetracycline4 �that are used to control plant pathogens. However, certain Streptomyces species are phytopathogenic, including the well-studied S. scabies and other closely related species such as S. turgidiscabies, S. acidizcabies, and S. europaeiscabiei, which are prevalent in many potatogrowing regions worldwide.5 These pathogenic Streptomyces spp. cause common scab (CS), which manifests as raised or sunken lesions on economically important crops such as potato, turnip, beet, and peanut.5,6 Thaxtomin A, a phytotoxin, has been identified as the principal virulence determinant in S. scabies and related species.7 Thaxtomin A is a cyclic dipeptide containing phenylalanine and 4-nitrotryptophan, known to inhibit plant cellulose biosynthesis, though its precise molecular targets remain unclear.8,9 Additionally, thaxtomin A induces severe seedling stunting and plant cell hypertrophy at nanomolar concentrations, making it a promising candidate for natural herbicide development.9 

The thaxtomin biosynthetic gene cluster (txt cluster) is highly conserved among scab-causing Streptomyces spp. and spans approximately 18.3 kb, comprising seven genes.9 Among them, six genes (txtA, txtB, txtC, txtD, txtE, and txtH) encode biosynthetic enzymes, while txtR encodes the sole cluster-

situated regulator (CSR), which activates thaxtomin biosynthesis.10,11 The biosynthesis begins with L-arginine (Arg), Ltryptophan (Trp), and L-phenylalanine (Phe) as precursors.9 Notably, nitric oxide (NO)-dependent nitration of Trp is initiated by the unusual NO synthase TxtD and the cytochrome P450 monooxygenase TxtE. TxtD oxidizes Arg to citrulline and NO,12 while TxtE uses NO to nitrate Trp, generating L-4-nitrotryptophan.13 This is followed by activation of Phe and 4-nitro-Trp by the nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs) TxtA and TxtB, forming thaxtomin D, a 2,5-diketopiperazine intermediate.14 TxtH belongs to the MbtH-like protein (MLP) family and is required for the proper folding of TxtA and TxtB.15 Thaxtomin A is finally produced via sequential hydroxylation of the $\alpha$ -carbon and the aromatic ring of the Phe residue in thaxtomin D by the cytochrome P450 enzyme TxtC.16 

Indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) is a key plant hormone from the auxin family of indole derivatives.17 It is the most prevalent natural hormone regulating nearly all aspects of plant growth and development, such as cell division, elongation, fruit development, and senescence.17,18 IAA proceeds via distinct biosynthetic pathways, which are broadly classified as either tryptophan-dependent or tryptophan-independent.19,20 Among

BldD is a master developmental regulator in Streptomyces, occupying the apex of the regulatory cascade governing morphological differentiation and secondary metabolism in nonpathogenic strains.22 BldD exerts its function by directly binding to a conserved 15-bp palindromic DNA motif (the “BldD box”): ${ \boldsymbol { 5 ^ { \prime } } }$ -NTNACNC(A/T)GNGTNAN- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ . 22 The global BldD regulon includes genes involved in cell division, development, and secondary metabolism,23 as well as major transcriptional regulators for downstream processes.22−24 Moreover, the activity of BldD is regulated by the second messenger cyclic di-GMP (c-di-GMP), which facilitates BldD dimerization and DNA binding.25 Despite its central role in nonpathogenic Streptomyces, the function of BldD in pathogenic strains like S. scabies, particularly in relation to virulence, secondary metabolism, and development, remains poorly understood. Although previous studies have suggested that thaxtomin A biosynthesis is influenced by regulators in the bld gene family�including a BldD ortholog, the precise regulatory mechanism of BldD in S. scabies has not been elucidated.26 

In this study, we conducted a comprehensive investigation into the regulatory role of BldD from S. scabies (hereafter referred to as BldDss). Our data demonstrate that BldDss directly regulates thaxtomin biosynthesis by binding to the promoters of its target txt genes. More importantly, we uncovered a previously unrecognized regulatory link between BldDss and the biosynthesis of the phytohormone indole-3- acetic acid (IAA), revealing that BldDss coordinates the production of both thaxtomin and IAA�two Trp derived metabolites in S. scabies. 

# 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

# 2.1. Strains and Growth Conditions

The bacterial strains and plasmids used in this study are listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information). Streptomyces scabies, S. coelicolor, and their derivatives were cultivated on SFM (soy flour mannitol) solid medium for sporulation and phenotypic observation, or in TSB (tryptic soy broth) liquid medium for mycelial growth, seed culture, and DNA extraction.27 OBB (oat bran broth) was used for thaxtomin and IAA production.28 Escherichia coli strains were grown at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in LB (Luria−Bertani) medium. E. coli DH5α was used for routine molecular cloning, while E. coli ET12567 (pUZ8002) facilitated DNA transfer into Streptomyces via intergeneric conjugation.29 E. coli BL21 (DE3) (Novagen) was employed for protein overproduction. 

Antibiotics were used at the following concentrations where necessary: thiostrepton $\left( 2 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } \right)$ , nalidixic acid $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ , and apramycin $\left( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m L } \right)$ for Streptomyces strains; ampicillin $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / $ mL), apramycin $\mathbf { \bar { ( 1 0 0 \mu g / m L ) } }$ , kanamycin $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ , and chloramphenicol $( 2 5 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m L } )$ for E. coli strains. 

# 2.2. Disruption and Complementation of bldDss

The primers used in this study are listed in Table S2 (Supporting Information). Using genomic DNA from S. scabies 87.22, two ${ \sim } 1 , 5 0 0$ bp DNA fragments flanking bldDss were amplified by PCR with primer pairs bldDss-UF/R and bldDss-DF/R. The PCR products 

were digested with HindIII/XbaI and KpnI/EcoRI, respectively, and ligated into the corresponding sites of pUCTSR30 to generate pUCΔbldDss. This plasmid was introduced into E. coli ET12567 (pUZ8002) and then transferred into S. scabies 87.22 by intergeneric conjugation.31 Through homologous recombination, a 303-bp region of bldDss was replaced by the thiostrepton resistance gene (tsr) (Figure S1A). A total of 5 independent clones of mutant ΔbldDss was verified by PCR using primers bldDss-TF/R, and then used for subsequent experiments (Figure S1B). 

For complementation, an 801-bp fragment including the full-length bldDss (501 bp) and its native 300-bp upstream promoter was amplified using primers bldDss-CF/R and cloned into the HindIII/ EcoRI site of $\mathsf { p K C 1 1 3 9 } ^ { 2 9 }$ to obtain pKCbldDss. This plasmid, along with the empty vector control pKC1139, was introduced into ΔbldDss via intergeneric conjugation, generating the complemented strain ΔbldDss/pKCbldDss and vector control ΔbldDss/pKC1139. Similarly, the two plasmids were respectively introduced into the wild-type strain to generate the overexpressed strain 87.22/pKCbldDss and the control strain $8 7 . 2 2 / \mathrm { p K C 1 } 1 3 9$ . 

# 2.3. Overexpression of iaaH-iaaM in S. scabies and S. coelicolor

The full-length iaaH−iaaM operon of 2535-bp was prepared by PCR from the 87.22 genomic DNA with the primers iaaH-CF and iaaM-CR (Table S2), and then cloned into the NdeI/XbaI site of $\mathrm { p I B } 1 3 9 ^ { 2 9 }$ to construct the plasmid pIBiaaH-iaaM. pIBiaaH-iaaM and pIB139 were respectively introduced into the 87.22 to construct the overexpression strain 87.22/iaaH-iaaM and the control 87.22/ pIB139 by apramycin-screening and PCR. Similarly, the two plasmids were respectively introduced into heterogeneous S. coelicolor M145 strain by intergenic conjugation, generating M145/iaaH-iaaM and its control M145/pIB139. 

# 2.4. Quantification of Thaxtomin A

Quantified $1 0 ^ { 7 }$ spores of S. scabies 87.22 and its derivatives were inoculated into ${ 5 0 } \mathrm { m L }$ TSB medium and cultured at ${ 2 8 \ ^ { \circ } C } _ { \prime }$ , 220 rpm for 2 days. A 5 mL aliquot of seed culture was then transferred to ${ 5 0 } \mathrm { m L }$ of OBB and incubated under the same conditions for 7 days. Thaxtomin A was extracted by adding $2 0 \mathrm { m L }$ ethyl acetate to the culture and incubating for $^ { 2 4 } \ \mathrm { h } ,$ , followed by centrifugation at 8,000 rpm for 10 min. The organic phase was filtered $( 0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ and analyzed by HPLC using a Wondasil C18 Superb column $4 . 6 ~ \times$ $1 5 0 \mathrm { m m } ,$ $5 \mu \mathrm { m } ;$ ; GC Sciences) as previously described.27 Detection was at $3 8 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , and quantification was performed using an authentic thaxtomin A standard (AbMole BioScience). 

# 2.5. Measurement of Cell Growth

To assess the effect of bldDss deletion on growth, biomass was measured as previously described.27,32 S. scabies 87.22 and mutant ΔbldDss were respectively inoculated into ${ 5 0 } \mathrm { m L }$ of OBB medium at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at $2 2 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 7 days. Culture samples (1 mL) were taken daily, centrifuged, washed twice with sterile water, and dried to constant weight. Dry weights were determined using an analytical balance. 

# 2.6. Virulence Assays

Virulence on potato tubers was evaluated as described previously.33 Sporulated agar plugs of 87.22 and ΔbldDss were placed on surfacesterilized potato slices and incubated at $2 3 \pm 2 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in a moist chamber for 7−8 days. Radish seedling assays were performed as described previously.34 Germinated seeds were placed in agar wells and inoculated with $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of fermentation extract from 87.22 or ΔbldDss. Plates were incubated at $2 1 \pm 2 { } ^ { \circ } { \mathrm { C } }$ with a 16-h photoperiod for 6−7 days. 

Arabidopsis thaliana (Col-0) seedlings were similarly treated with fermentation extracts and grown under a 12-h photoperiod at $2 1 \pm 2$ $^ \circ \mathrm { C }$ for 3−4 days.28

product was cut with HindIII/NdeI and inserted into corresponding sites of pET28a to generate the expression plasmid pETbldDss, then transferred it into E. coli BL21 (DE3) for BldDss overexpression. # 3. RESULTS

# 3.1. BldDss Positively and Directly Regulates Thaxtomin Biosynthesis in S. scabies

To determine whether BldDss is involved in thaxtomin production, wild-type S. scabies 87.22, the deletion mutant ΔbldDss, and the complemented strain ΔbldDss/pKCbldDss were cultured in OBB medium for 7 days. HPLC analysis revealed that the ΔbldDss mutant almost completely lost the ability to produce thaxtomin A (Figure 1A). Additionally, biomass measurements showed a slightly reduced mycelial dry weight in ΔbldDss compared to 87.22 (Figure 1B). Thaxtomin A production and growth were restored in the complemented strain ΔbldDss/pKCbldDss (Figure 1A,B). Furthermore, thaxtomin A production was significantly upregulated by $45 \%$ in bldDss overexpression strain 87.22/pKCbldDss compared with the control 87.22/pKC1139 (Figure 1A). 

To explore the regulatory relationship between BldDss and the thaxtomin biosynthetic txt cluster, RT-qPCR was performed on key cluster genes (txtR, txtA, and txtE) during thaxtomin production. These genes represent the two polycistronic units (txtA−txtC and txtE−txtD) and the cluster-situated regulator gene txtR.38 Transcript levels of all three genes were significantly reduced in ΔbldDss compared to the wild-type (Figure 1C). Although some of these results from HPLC and RT-qPCR analyses have been observed in early study,26 the precise mechanism on BldDss regulation to be elucidated. 

Promoter analysis through sequence BLAST showed the presence of BldD box-like sequences in the promoter regions of txtR (Figure 1E) and txtE (Figure 1F). EMSAs results confirmed that purified ${ \mathrm { H i s } } _ { 6 }$ -BldDss (Figure 1D) directly bound to both promoters in a concentration-dependent manner (Figure 1E,F). Furthermore, GUS reporter assays showed that when the bldDss gene was inserted into ptxtRG and ptxtEG, the GusA activity was enhanced by 4.2-fold (***P $< 0 . 0 0 1 $ ) in 87.22/ptxtRG-bldDss and 2.2-fold $\begin{array} { r } { \left( ^ { * * * * } P < 0 . 0 0 1 \right) } \end{array}$ ) in 87.22/ptxtEG-bldDss compared to that without bldDss in 87.22/ptxtRG and 87.22/ptxtEG, respectively (Figure 1H). 

These findings indicate that BldDss positively regulates thaxtomin biosynthesis by directly activating transcription of the txt cluster genes in S. scabies.

IAA production has been previously reported in both pathogenic and nonpathogenic Streptomyces when cultured with tryptophan, supporting the operation of the IAM pathway: Trp → IAM → IAA.21,43 $\mathrm { T r \bar { p } } \to \mathrm { L A } \bar { \mathsf { M } } \to \mathrm { L } \bar { \mathsf { A } } \bar { \mathsf { A } } . ^ { 2 1 , 4 3 }$ However, direct genetic evidence for this pathway was scarce in above Streptomyces. Our study confirms that iaaM (SCAB_75511) and iaaH (SCAB_75501) form a cotranscriptional unit and are principally responsible for IAA biosynthesis in S. scabies (Figure 4). When iaaH-iaaM was overexpressed in S. scabies and heterologous S. coelicolor, IAA production was respectively achieved a significant increase (Figure 4). However, deletion of iaaH-iaaM in S. scabies (Figure S2) could not completely block the biosynthesis of IAA, as a trace of IAA remained detectable in ΔiaaH-iaaM mutant (Figure 4C). This suggests the presence of alternative or redundant IAA biosynthetic pathways, potentially involving intermediates like indole-3- lactic acid (ILA), as previously suggested.19 Future studies will be necessary to uncover the full range of IAA biosynthetic routes in S. scabies. 

Our data also highlight the pivotal role of tryptophan as a metabolic crossroad. As a shared precursor of both thaxtomin A and IAA, Trp availability appears to mediate a competitive balance between the two pathways. In this study, Trp supplementation significantly boosted IAA levels in both native and heterologous hosts (Figure 4), while deletion of bldDss shifted the metabolic output from thaxtomin A to IAA (Figures 5 and S4). This trade-off may reflect a regulatory mechanism whereby S. scabies optimizes its interaction with the host�balancing phytotoxic and growth-promoting activities. As shown in Figure S4 for thaxtomin A and IAA production curves, we observed that thaxtomin A and IAA coexisted in S. scabies wild-type 87.22, while thaxtomin A was almost not produced and IAA acted as the main metabolite in ΔbldDss, thereby affecting the phytotoxic and growth-promoting activities of the host strain. It was recently reported that foliar 

application of Trp can induce resistance against potato common scab is observed, and the mechanism of Trp as an inducer to trigger host immune responses has been dissected.44 

Transcriptome analysis confirms that the systemic acquired resistance (SAR) and induced systemic resistance (ISR) pathways could be coinduced by Trp under S. scabies noninoculation. However, the SAR pathway was induced by Trp under S. scabies inoculation.44 Furthermore, our previous work suggests that Trp may also act as a signaling molecule� serving as an effector of SCAB_Lrp2 to repress thaxtomin production via txtR.41 Thus, Trp may play multiple roles�as precursor, effector, and regulatory cue�governing metabolic decisions in S. scabies. 

Strikingly, our study reveals a dual role for BldDss: as a positive regulator of thaxtomin biosynthesis and a negative regulator of IAA production. This regulatory mechanism enables BldDss probably to act as a molecular modulator, determining whether S. scabies adopts a phytopathogenic or plant growth-promoting role. Deletion of bldDss not only diminished virulence in radish seedlings (Figure 2B) but also promoted lateral root formation in A. thaliana (Figure 3A), closely resembling the phenotype induced by exogenous IAA. These findings suggest that BldDss plays a pivotal role in regulating host interactions, potentially allowing S. scabies for biofertilizer development based on this new trait of plantgrowth promoting (PGP) activity. Herbaspirillum aquaticum ZXN111 which was isolated from the tea plant Zijuan (C. sinensis var. assamica) showed PGP activity by an in vitro test,45 and validated that IAA primarily from indole-3-pyruvic (IPA) pathway is critical to PGP activity.46 

In conclusion, BldDss emerges as a central regulator integrating secondary metabolism, development, and plant interaction in S. scabies. Its bidirectional control over thaxtomin A and IAA biosynthesis reveals a new layer of complexity in the regulatory networks of pathogenic Streptomyces as briefly summarized in Figure 6. These insights may provide new avenues for mitigating agricultural damage caused by common scab while exploring the potential of S. scabies as a plant growth-promoting bacterium under defined conditions.
