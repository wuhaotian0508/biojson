# Regulation of Shoot and Root Development through Mutual Signaling

ABSTRACT Plants adjust their development in relation to the availability of nutrient sources. This necessitates signaling between root and shoot. Aside from the well-known systemic signaling processes mediated by auxin, cytokinin, and sugars, new pathways involving carotenoid-derived hormones have recently been identified. The auxin-responsive MAX pathway controls shoot branching through the biosynthesis of strigolactone in the roots. The BYPASS1 gene affects the production of an as-yet unknown carotenoid-derived substance in roots that promotes shoot development. Novel local and systemic mechanisms that control adaptive root development in response to nitrogen and phosphorus starvation were recently discovered. Notably, the ability of the NITRATE TRANSPORTER 1.1 to transport auxin drew for the first time a functional link between auxin, root development, and nitrate availability in soil. The study of plant response to phosphorus starvation allowed the identification of a systemic mobile miRNA. Deciphering and integrating these signaling pathways at the whole-plant level provide a new perspective for understanding how plants regulate their development in response to environmental cues.

Key words: plant development; systemic signaling; strigolactone; BYPASS1; nitrate; phosphate; miRNA.

# INTRODUCTION

Plants are sessile organisms comprising two distinct parts: the shoot system, which develops above ground and performs photosynthesis and reproductive functions; and the root system, which develops underground and ensures the uptake of water and nutrients from the soil. Cooperation between these two systems is required throughout the plant life cycle. Shoot and root development are coordinated by hormones, such as auxin and cytokinins (CKs). Auxin is primarily synthesized in shoots and actively transported to roots where it promotes root development (for recent reviews, see Peret et al., 2009; Coudert et al., 2010). CKs participate in root-to-shoot signaling and move acropetally to stimulate shoot development (Domagalska and Leyser, 2011). Tuning the balance between these two hormones is required to promote plant organogenesis and regeneration in in vitro micropropagation (for a review, see Su et al., 2011). Sugar, which is the main product of photosynthesis in shoots, is actively transported to roots via the phloem. In addition to its fundamental role in cell nutrition, sugar has also been identified as a component of systemic shoot-to-root signaling. In particular, sugar contributes to adjust root growth, uptake of nitrate and phosphate, and of the global carbon status

of the plant (Zhang et al., 2007). Recent studies using direct genetic approaches lead to the discovery of systemic signaling pathways involving new carotenoid hormones that originate in roots and regulate shoot development. This is the case for the MORE AXILLARY GROWTH (MAX) pathway, which controls axillary bud outgrowth (Ongaro and Leyser, 2008). The MAX pathway is mediated by root production of strigolactone: a $\beta$ -carotene-derived hormone that inhibits axillary bud development (Dun et al., 2009). The BYPASS1 pathway also controls shoot development via a substance produced in the root system. In bypass1 mutants, the development of the aerial part of the plant is dramatically altered, and root development is inhibited. Grafting experiments provided evidence that mutation of BYPASS1 affects the production or degradation of a root signal that regulates shoot meristem activity (Van Norman et  al., 2004). In this case, the signal has not been characterized but presumably is derived from

$\beta$ -carotene (Sieburth and Lee, 2010). These new systemic pathways, both of which involve $\beta$ -carotene-derived hormones, play important roles in the constitutive regulation of shoot development by the root system.

Developing plants must adapt to environmental constraints, notably nutrient availability. The concentrations of macronutrients in the soil often impact root development by triggering local and systemic signaling pathways. These responses generally lead to enhanced local branching and development of the root system in rich soil sectors to maximize uptake of mineral nutrients. This constitutes an adaptive developmental response of the root system to nutrient availability in soil (Remans et al., 2006). High concentrations of mineral nutrients generally stimulate aerial plant development (Rahayu et al., 2005; Hermans et al., 2006; Sakakibara et al., 2006). Mineral macronutrient starvation generally stimulates root growth and elongation, allowing the plant to explore new soil regions (Zhang and Forde, 2000; Osmont et al., 2007; Desnos, 2008). Starved plants also exhibit a reduced development of the aerial portion. Investigation of adaptive plant development in response to nitrate availability in soil has been shown to encompass both systemic and local signaling pathways. The nitrate systemic pathway involves sugars and CK, while recent studies have revealed local competition between nitrogen and auxin for transportation by the NITRATE TRANSPORTER 1.1 (NRT1.1) nitrogen carrier. The latter results in local, nitrate-sensitive regulation of auxin transport and distribution, and therefore in root development (Sakakibara et al., 2006; Vidal and Gutierrez, 2008; Krouk et al., 2010). Several specific transcription factors have been also involved in the local regulation of root development in response to nitrate or phosphate. In addition to auxin, sugar, and CKs, studies of the phosphate-signaling pathway have implicated miRNAs as important systemic signaling molecules that modulate plant responses to nutrient availability (Jain et al., 2007; Liu and Vance, 2010; Rouached et al., 2010; Yang and Finnegan, 2010).

In this paper, we will review the different pathways that allow a plant to functionally link shoot and root tissues and coordinate their mutual development.

# APICAL DOMINANCE: A CASE STUDY OF SYSTEMIC NETWORKS

Apical dominance refers to the inhibition of axillary bud growth by the active shoot tip. Apical dominance can be abolished by removing the shoot tip, which triggers the growth of one to several axillary buds. Recent studies have revealed that control of apical dominance involves various signaling molecules and occurs in different parts of the plant, including the root system. Early evidence regarding a role for auxin in apical dominance was provided by decapitation experiments followed by replacement of the shoot tip with an auxin-containing agar block. The artificial auxin source was able to restore dominance on the remaining axillary

buds (Thimann and Skoog, 1933, 1934). Primarily synthesized in the shoot apex and young leaves, auxin is transported to other parts of the shoot and roots by a set of transporters, which includes the influx facilitator AUXIN INFLUX CARRIER PROTEI N1 (AUX1)/LIKE-AUX (LAX), p-GLYCOPROTEI N AUXIN EFFLUX CARRIERS (PGP), and PIN-FORMED AUXIN EFFLUX CARRIERS (PIN) (Blakeslee et al., 2005; Paponov et al., 2005; Geisler and Murphy, 2006). Though auxin is an essential signal for apical dominance, several experiments have shown that it does not directly inhibit axillary bud outgrowth on its own (Gocal et al., 1991; Booker et al., 2003). Analyses of mutants with aberrant regulation of bud outgrowth in Arabidopsis, pea, petunia, and rice have only recently deciphered a link between auxin and shoot branching (Beveridge et al., 1996; Napoli, 1996; Sorefan et al., 2003; Booker et al., 2004; Booker et al., 2005; Ishikawa et al., 2005). These mutants exhibit reduced height and a bunchy branching pattern. In A.  thaliana, mutants exhibiting this phenotype have been identified as MORE AXILL ARY GROWT H (MAX). MAX3 and MAX4 genes encode the CAROTENOID CLE AVAGE DIOXYGENASE enzymes CDD7 and CDD8, respectively (Sorefan et al., 2003; Booker et al., 2004). MAX1 encodes a cytochrome P450 enzyme (Booker et al., 2005). All of these enzymes are required for the biosynthesis of strigolactones from $\beta$ -carotene (Booker et al., 2004; Schwartz et al., 2004; Booker et al., 2005; Auldridge et al., 2006). Interestingly, grafting of max1, max3, and max4 shoots onto WT (wild-type) rootstocks restores the WT shoot development phenotype (Turnbull et al., 2002; Sorefan et al., 2003; Booker et al., 2004). This suggests that strigolactones act as an inhibitory signal that is produced in the roots and can move acropetally to inhibit the growth of axillary buds. Expression of MAX4 in the root tip is down-regulated in response to shoot tip removal and is restored when the shoot tip is replaced by an artificial auxin source (Sorefan et al., 2003; Bainbridge et al., 2005). This indicates that strigolactone biosynthesis is stimulated in roots in response to auxin synthesized in the shoot tip. A fourth gene involved in axillary bud outgrowth, MAX2, encodes a conserved F-box protein that may contribute to the strigolactone signal transduction pathway (Stirnberg et al., 2002). MAX2 is primarily expressed in shoots and is a potential receptor for the strigolactone signal at the level of axillary buds (Stirnberg et al., 2007). CKs are also involved in regulating axillary bud outgrowth. CKs are known to promote axillary bud outgrowth (Sachs and Thimann, 1967; Li and Bangerth, 2003; Nordstrom et al., 2004). CKs are synthetized in the shoots and roots and are transported from root-toshoot via the xylem (Chen et  al., 1985). Auxin inhibits CK biosynthesis, but this inhibition is abolished by mutation of the AUXIN RESPONSE1 (AXR1) gene, which encodes an F-box protein that acts upstream of the auxin signal transduction pathway (Nordstrom et  al., 2004). Axr1 mutants exhibit a bushy phenotype, which indicates a reduction in apical dominance (Stirnberg et al., 1999). It has been shown that removal of shoot tips induces translocation of CKs from roots

and their accumulation in axillary buds; however, placing a source of auxin at the decapitated shoot tip prevents this CK accumulation (Li and Bangerth, 2003). This suggests that auxin produced in shoot tips inhibits root CK biosynthesis and translocation to axillary buds. Interestingly, max mutants, which exhibit enhanced axillary bud outgrowth, possess reduced levels of CKs in xylem sap relative to WT plants; this suggests that a feedback signal may also be produced by active axillary buds and inhibit CK biosynthesis in roots (Foo et al., 2007). It is likely that such interplay between auxin, strigolactone, and CK ensures developmental homeostasis of shoot branching via axillary bud growth control. The fact that MAX orthologous genes have also been functionally characterized in pea, petunia, and rice indicates that this pathway is conserved across plant species. # bypass1: A MUTANT FOR THE STUDY OF ROOT-TO-SHOOT SIGNALING

The Arabidopsis thaliana bypass1 (bps1) mutant exhibits short primary and lateral roots and reduced activity of the shoot apical meristem, which leads to premature arrest of leaf growth. This phenotype is more severe at low temperatures (Van Norman et al., 2004). Experiments in which WT shoots were grafted onto bps1 mutant root systems resulted in a bps1 shoot phenotype, which demonstrated that development shoot defects result from a signal molecule produced in bsp1 roots. This was confirmed via restoration of the WT shoot phenotype in bps1 shoots grafted onto a WT root system. These experiments showed that BPS1 negatively regulates the production of a systemic signal in roots that serves to block leaf cell division (Van Norman and Sieburth, 2007; Van Norman et al., 2011). Bps1 mutant plants transformed with the auxin-sensing construct DR5::GUS exhibited strong downregulation of the GUS gene relative to WT DR5:GUS control plants (Van Norman et al., 2004). This suggests that the BPS1- derived signal in the root interferes with auxin-mediated regulation of gene expression. Analysis of bps1/apl1 (ALTERED PHLOEM DEVELOPMENT) (Bonke et al., 2003) and bps1/scr and bps1/shr (SCARECROW and SHORTROOT, which regulate differentiation of the root endodermis) double mutants (Di Laurenzio et al., 1996; Helariutta et al., 2000) showed that neither functional phloem nor endodermis is required for root-to-shoot BPS1-derived signal translocation (Van Norman et al., 2011).

BSP1 is a 249 amino acid, plant-specific protein of unknown function. Expression of BPS1 was detected in all organs tested, including both shoot and root, which suggests that the function of BSP1 may be post-transcriptionally regulated in different organs (Van Norman et al., 2004). The exact nature of the BSP1-derived signal is not yet known. Nevertheless, much experimental evidence suggests that BPS1 is linked to the production of a carotenoid-derived signal. Indeed, the growth

of bps1 mutant plants in culture medium supplemented with fluridone, an inhibitor of carotenoid biosynthesis (Bartels et al., 1978), leads to restoration of the WT phenotype (Van Norman et al., 2004). Carotenoids are divided into two major classes: $\alpha$ -carotene and $\beta$ -carotene. The LUTEIN DEFICIENT 1 (LUT1) gene encodes a cytochrome P450 co-enzyme that is necessary for hydroxylation of the $\alpha$ -carotene (Tian et al., 2004). Analysis of the double mutant bps1/lut1 revealed that the bsp1 phenotype is not affected in plants that suffer impaired $\alpha$ -carotene biosynthesis (Van Norman and Sieburth, 2007). This suggests that the BSP1-related signal derives from $\beta$ -carotene.

Abscisic acid and strigolactones are $\beta$ -carotenoid-derived plant hormones (Raghavendra et al., 2010; Rameau, 2010). Plants combining bsp1 and mutations in genes involved in the biosynthesis of abscisic acid (i.e. bps1/aba1, bps1/aba2, and bps1/aba3) and strigolactone (i.e. bps1/max3 and bps1/max4) were generated (Van Norman and Sieburth, 2007). All of these mutants exhibited phenotypes similar to the single bsp1 mutant, which indicates that the BPS1 signal is not dependent on ABA or strigolactone.

The BPS1 signal remains to be determined. It is challenging to discover a new root-produced signal that regulates shoot growth. Phylogeny analyses have demonstrated the existence of two BPS1 homologs in tobacco, but each of their products contains a transmembrane domain that does not exist in BSP1 from A. thaliana. NbBPS1 is expressed in all plant tissues and the protein localizes to the endoplasmic reticulum. RNA interference-mediated reduction of NbBPS1 expression leads to abnormal leaf development and growth retardation (Kang et al., 2008). This suggests that the functions of BPS1 and NbBPS1 are at least partially conserved. The BSP1 signaling pathway is highlighted in red in Figure 1.

# LOCAL AND SYSTEMIC CONTROL OF ADAPTIVE ROOT DEVELOPMENT IN RESPONSE TO NITRATE

Nitrogen (N) can be absorbed from soil in a variety of forms, but it is primarily absorbed as nitrate ${ \mathsf { N O } } _ { 3 } { } ^ { - }$ , as in the case of A. thaliana, or as ammonium $\mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ^ { + }$ , as in the case of irrigated rice (Williams and Miller, 2001). A local increase in nitrate concentration stimulates the initiation and development of lateral roots, which allows the plant to colonize and exploit nitrate-rich soil regions (Zhang and Forde, 2000). The regulation of local root adaptive development involves different elements of control. The MADS box transcription factor ARABIDOPSIS NITRATE REGULATED 1 (ANR1) was first shown to promote lateral root development in response to nitrate (Zhang and Forde, 1998). ANR1 expression is strongly induced in roots in response to nitrate. In plants in which ANR1 expression is down-regulated by antisense knockdown, lateral root production is inhibited in response to nitrate. the AUXIN RESPONSE FACTOR 8 (ARF8) transcription factor. The nitrate-induced down-regulation of miRNA167 augments ARF8 transcripts in the root pericycle cells, and this correlates with enhanced initiation of lateral roots (Gifford et al., 2008; Gutierrez et al., 2009). Additionally, mutagenesis studies indicate that the nitrate transporter NRT1.1 is necessary to stimulate lateral root development in response to nitrate (Remans et al., 2006). Interestingly, NRT1.1 can carry auxin, in addition to nitrate (Krouk et al., 2010). The competition between nitrate and auxin for transport via NRT1.1 provides for the first time a functional link between a mineral nutrient and a regulator of root growth. This permitted the development of a model in which NRT1.1 preferentially carries auxin at low nitrate concentrations, thereby promoting basipetal auxin transport out of the lateral roots and inhibiting their growth. At higher nitrate concentrations, nitrate competes with the auxin transported by NRT1.1. Auxin then accumulates in lateral root tips and stimulates their growth.

Conversely, accumulation of nitrate in shoots inhibits the formation of lateral roots (Zhang et al., 1999). In this case, it was observed that the auxin content was higher in shoots and lower in roots of A. thaliana seedlings grown on nitrate, relative to nitrate-starved seedlings. This suggests that the translocation of auxin from shoots to roots may be inhibited by high nitrate conditions (Walch-Liu et al., 2006a). The resulting decrease in root auxin concentration observed in nitrate-repleted plants may partially explain why these plants produce fewer lateral roots than nitrate-starved plants. In abscisic acid-deficient mutants, lateral root development is inhibited to a lesser extent in conditions of high nitrate concentration, which suggests that abscisic acid also contributes to the inhibition of lateral root development in nitraterepleted plants (Signora et al., 2001; Zhang et al., 2007).

Both local and systemic control of lateral root development in response to nitrate constitutes important mechanisms of nitrate uptake regulation. A local increase in nitrate concentration in soil induces a local stimulation of lateral root initiation and development that allow the plant to better exploit the portion of soil rich in nitrates. On the other hand, when the plant is repleted with nitrate, a systemic regulation likely involving an inhibition of the auxin translocation from shoot to root reduces lateral root initiation and development, thereby allowing the plant to decrease nitrate uptake. In addition, downstream N metabolites, such as amino acids and glutamate, have also been proposed to constitute shoot-to-root feedback signals that regulate nitrate assimilation in roots (Forde and Lea, 2007; Miller et al., 2007). Exogenous glutamate treatments affect root development particularly by inhibiting primary root growth (Walch-Liu et al., 2006b). In addition to promoting lateral root initiation and development, nitrate treatment stimulates primary root growth, which suggests that nitrate antagonizes the inhibitory effect of glutamate. This antagonizing effect is reduced in the chl1-5 (chlorina 1–5) mutant, which affects the NRT1.1 transporter. This shows that the glutamate-regulated pathway of primary root growth

interferes with the nitrate NRT1.1 pathway (Walch-Liu and Forde, 2008). Further investigation of the NRT1.1 regulatory pathway is a promising approach to decipher additional local and systemic mechanisms that link root development to nitrogen status in the plant.

Split-root experiments, which involved growing one half of the roots of a single plant in nitrate-depleted medium and the other half in a nitrate-rich medium, revealed that a local increase in nitrate concentration promotes root growth and branching in rice (Wang et al., 2002). However, the mechanism underlying this process remains unknown. Because it may share common pathways with A.  thaliana, it is worthwhile to characterize the function of rice orthologs of ANR1 and NRT1.1. In irrigated rice, it is assumed that the primary form of N taken up by roots is ammonium (Sasakawa and Yamamoto, 1978). Therefore, it is likely that some specific mechanisms have been developed in rice to adjust root morphogenesis in response to ammonium concentration.

In A.  thaliana roots, the expression of nitrate and ammonium transporter genes is stimulated during the day phase by sugars produced in leaves via systemic signaling that involves the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway and glucose 6-P (Lejay et al., 2003, 2008). This allows coordination of the global carbon status of the plant and N uptake in roots. In turn, leaf growth, and both photosynthetic and carbohydrate capacity of the plant, are stimulated by nitrate uptake via CKs produced in roots. When nitrate is present in the culture medium, the ISOPENTENYLTRANSFERASE 3 gene is up-regulated in the root phloem, where it activates the biosynthesis of isopentenyl-type CK and up-regulates the cytochrome P450 monooxygenase CYP735A gene, which facilitates hydroxylation of isopentenyl-type CK to transzeatin-type CK. This trans-zeatin-type CK is translocated, likely via the xylem, from roots to shoots. Upon detection in root tissue, this CK induces a typical CK His–Asp phospho-relay signal transduction pathway that promotes leaf expansion (Rahayu et al., 2005; Hermans et al., 2006; Sakakibara et al., 2006). # SYSTEMIC RESPONSE TO PHOSPHATE ISMEDIATED BY HORMONES, SUGAR,AND SPECIFIC miRNAs

Investigating phosphorus (P) homeostasis revealed new systemic signals and, in particular, the role of specific miR-NAs. Plants absorb phosphorus as inorganic phosphate (Pi). Because two recent reviews describe general plant responses to Pi deficiency and the related signaling pathways at length (Rouached et al., 2010; Yang and Finnegan, 2010), we will hereafter focus specifically on the signaling pathway that influences adaptive root development in response to Pi. Pi starvation inhibits primary root growth, induces lateral root development, and promotes elongation and increased

density of root hairs (Osmont et al., 2007; Desnos, 2008). Several genes involved in suppressing primary root growth in response to Pi deficiency have been isolated (Reymond et al., 2006; Sanchez-Calderon et al., 2006; Svistoonoff et al., 2007). One of them, PHOSPHATE DEFICIENCY RESPONSE 2 (PDR2), is required for proper expression of the SCARECROW gene, which is itself necessary for stem cell maintenance in the root meristem and for differentiation of root endodermis (Ticconi et al., 2004, 2009). When exogenously supplied, auxin induces changes in root development that are comparable to those observed in response to Pi starvation, which suggests that auxin plays a role in the Pi starvation-induced transduction pathway (Nacry et al., 2005). Further studies have revealed that the auxin receptor SCFTIR1-dependent pathway and the AUXIN RESPONSE FACTOR (ARF19) are required to promote lateral root development in response to Pi deficiency (Perez-Torres et al., 2008). Nevertheless, auxin-independent pathways also regulate root development in the event of Pi deficiency (Lopez-Bucio et al., 2002). Pi starvation reduces levels of bioactive gibberellic acid (GA), which contributes to the suppression of primary root growth and promotes root hair development (Jiang et al., 2007). The role of GA in the plant response to Pi deficiency has also been suggested by studies of MYB62: a Pi deficiency-induced transcription factor. Overexpression of MYB62 alters primary root development and increases both Pi uptake and acid phosphatase activity; MYB62 also represses genes that are involved in GA biosynthesis. The shoots of MYB62-overexpressing plants exhibit the characteristic phenotype of GA-deficient mutants, and a WT phenotype can be restored by providing exogenous GA (Devaiah et al., 2009). This suggested that MYB62 is a key regulator of the GA-dependent transduction pathway in plants exposed to Pi deficiency. Other transcription factors participating in the adaptive root development response to Pi starvation have been identified. ZING FINGER OF ARABIDOPSIS THALIANA 6 (ZAT6) is a C2H2 zinc-finger transcription factor that acts as a repressor of primary root growth and may regulate Pi homeostasis by controlling root architecture (Devaiah et al., 2007a). WRKY75 is another transcription factor that may regulate the development of lateral roots and root hairs (Devaiah et al., 2007a, 2007b). BASIC HELIX-LOOP-HELIX 32 (bHLH32) is a negative regulator of root hair formation that may interfere with root hair development pathway mediated by TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA 1 (TTG1) and GLABROUS 3 (GL3) (Chen et al., 2007). Rice Oryza sativa PHOSPHATE TRANSCRIPTION FACTOR 1 (OsPTF1) encodes a bHLH transcription factor that is induced by Pi deficiency in lateral roots and in the phloem cells of the primary root (Yi et al., 2005). OsPTF1 overexpression stimulates tillering, biomass accumulation, panicle weight, and root growth. It has been proposed that OsPTF1 acts primarily on Pi homeostasis by stimulating adaptive root development to increase the root surface area.

Pi deficiency causes a reduction in plant CK content. CKs repress the Pi starvation response and act primarily on Pi transport regulation without influencing adaptive root

development (Martin et al., 2000; Brenner et al., 2005). The CK pathway involving CYTOKININ RESPONSE1 (CRE1) contributes directly to this process (Franco-Zorrilla et al., 2005). Nevertheless, it remains unclear whether CKs constitute a long-range Pi starvation signal (Martin et al., 2000; Franco-Zorrilla et al., 2005; Sakakibara, 2006).

Sucrose induces the expression of Pi transporters, at least in part, via the hexokinase sugar-sensing pathway, which contributes to global metabolic homeostasis of carbon and Pi metabolism (Lejay et al., 2003, 2008). Pi starvation increases the sucrose content in shoot and root, and sucrose transported from the shoot via the phloem is thought to contribute to adaptive responses in roots, such as the enhancement of root hair development (Karthikeyan et al., 2007; Muller et al., 2007). The concentration of sucrose in phloem is enhanced by Pi deficiency (Ciereszko et al., 2005). The Pi-starvation response is attenuated in Arabidopsis phosphate-deficient 3 (pho3) mutants. This mutation affects the SUCROSE TRANSPORTER 2 (SUC2) gene, which encodes a phloem-loading sucrose transporter (Lloyd and Zakhleniuk, 2004). The application of exogenous sucrose to Pi-starved plants enhances the root architectural changes typically associated with Pi deprivation (Jain et al., 2007). Collectively, these results suggest that sucrose constitutes a systemic signal that is transported via the phloem from shoot to root and regulates adaptive root development in response to Pi availability.

A specific miRNA constitutes another systemic signal of plant Pi homeostasis. In the A. thaliana phosphate-deficient 2 (pho2) mutant, most Pi starvation-responsive genes remain induced in Pi-replete conditions, suggesting that PHO2 negatively regulates the Pi-starvation response (Shin et al., 2004; Bari et al., 2006). PHO2 is a ubiquitin E2-conjugase, known as UBC24. Its downstream targets are not known but upstream elements that regulate its accumulation are better understood. PHO2 mRNA is targeted by miRNA399, which induces its degradation, and thereby promotes the expression of genes involved in Pi uptake in root tissue (Lin et al., 2008). MiRNA399 is transported via the phloem from shoot to roots where it targets the PHO2 mRNA for degradation (Pant et al., 2008). MiRNA399 expression is stimulated by Pi starvation, and this induction is under the control of the PHOSPHATE STARVATION RESPONSE 1 (PHR1) MYB transcription factor (Bari et al., 2006). The biologic activity of miRNA399 is also inhibited by At4, an Induced by Pi Starvation (IPS) gene that encodes a non-coding RNA comprising a 23-bp region complementary to miRNA399. At4 acts as a target mimic to trap miRNA399 (Shin et al., 2006; Franco-Zorrilla et al., 2007). In this manner, a fine regulation of PHO2 and downstream Pi starvation-induced genes (PSI genes) takes place where miRNA399 ensures the transduction of systemic signals from shoot to roots. In addition, a recent report suggested that the Pi-mediated regulation of miRNA399 also depends on active photosynthetic carbon assimilation suggesting that miRNA399 and the sucrose systemic pathways interfere to regulate the Pi-deficiency response in roots (Liu et al., 2010). Rice plants overexpressing OsPHR2, a MYB

transcription factor homologous to PHR1, exhibit increased Pi accumulation in shoots and augmentation of root hairs in both length and number. In these plants, several PSI genes, as well as rice miRNA399, are also up-regulated. However, this does not correlate with a decrease in the steady-state levels of OsPHO2 mRNA. These data show that, if OsPHR2 is a central regulator of response to Pi starvation in rice, its action seems to be distinct from what is observed in A.  thaliana. Systemic regulation of OsPHO2 mRNA by miR399 needs to be further investigated (Zhou et al., 2008). The main regulatory elements of the local and systemic Pi transduction pathways are indicated in green in Figure 1.

The demonstration of the role played by miRNA399 in the systemic signaling pathway of phosphate starvation opens new avenues to discover mechanisms involved in the systemic control of constitutive or adaptive plant development. Other miR-NAs are induced by nutrient starvation (Pant et al., 2009). For instance, miRNA395 and miRNA398 accumulate in the phloem sap when plants are deprived of sulfur and copper, respectively (Yamasaki et al., 2007; Buhtz et al., 2010). The up-regulation of miRNA398 by copper induces the degradation of copper/zinc superoxide dismutase (Cu,Zn-SOD) mRNA (Yamasaki et al., 2007). MiRNA395 is involved in the regulation of ATP sulphorylase and the sulfur carrier SULFATE TRANSPORTER 2.1 (SULTR2.1), which is implicated in $\mathsf { S O } _ { 4 } { } ^ { 2 - }$ loading in root xylem (Jones-Rhoades and Bartel, 2004). A set of related root-specific miRNAs have been identified (Meng et al., 2010). Some of them are involved in regulating development, and it will be interesting to determine their precise functions in root-to-shoot signaling.

# CONCLUSION

These four examples highlight how the discovery of new players in the systemic control of constitutive and adaptive plant development, such as strigolactone and miRNA, opens exciting new avenues for future research. However, the current global picture of these systemic signaling pathways remains fragmented, incomplete, and in need of integration. This is particularly true for auxin, CK, and sugar signals, which are involved in many systemic developmental responses and are likely to be functionally linked. We can expect that multi-scale and multidisciplinary systems biology approaches will address these relationships in the future (Lucas et al., 2011). Nevertheless, focused analytical research is also required for progress to be made. This is exemplified by the bps1 mutant, which exhibits an alteration of a carotenoid-derived pathway that is crucial for the root-mediated control of shoot development. Identification of the BPS1- related signal molecule will be the next challenge because it will permit clarification of its interaction and possible interplay with the MAX-strigolactone pathway.
