# Arabidopsis seed mucilage secretory cells: regulation and dynamics

Edith Francoz1,2*, Philippe Ranocha1,2*, Vincent Burlat1,2 , and Christophe Dunand1,2

1 Universite´ de Toulouse, UPS, UMR 5546, Laboratoire de Recherche en Sciences Ve´ ge´tales, BP 42617, F-31326 Castanet-Tolosan, France   
2 CNRS, UMR 5546, BP 42617, F-31326 Castanet-Tolosan, France

Seeds from various angiosperm species produce polysaccharide mucilage facilitating germination and, therefore, conferring major evolutionary advantages. The seed epidermal mucilage secretory cells (MSCs) undergo numerous tightly controlled changes of their extracellular matrixes (ECMs) throughout seed development. Recently, major progress based on the model species Arabidopsis thaliana was published, including the identification of 54 genes necessary for mucilage synthesis and release. Here, we review these genes that constitute the so-called ‘MSC toolbox’, within which transcription factors and proteins related to polysaccharide production, secretion, modification, and stabilization are the most abundant and belong to complex regulatory networks. We also discuss how seed coat ‘omics datamining, comparative genomics, and operon-like gene cluster studies will provide means to identify new members of the MSC toolbox.

# Mucilage secretory cells: a fascinating model for cell wall dynamic studies

During seed development, some of the most dramatic cellular changes occur within the seed coat, with major impacts for seed dormancy and germination [1]. Among these cellular events, accumulation of large amounts of mucilage in the seed coat or pericarp, a phenomenon called ‘myxospermy’, is detected in various angiosperm species [2]. Mucilage composition varies between species, but as a common feature forms a polysaccharidic cell wall-like structure with large amounts of rhamnogalacturonan I (RGI) pectin domains (see Glossary) and smaller amounts of homogalacturonan (HG) pectin domains, cellulose, and hemicelluloses (reviewed in [2–6]). In Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana), mucilage accumulates in the seed coat (integument) outermost epidermal cell layer, called MSCs or outer integument 2 (oi2). Within mature dry seed MSCs, mucilage is compacted between the primary wall (PW) and a polarized secondary wall, called the columella.

Therefore, MSCs represent a model of choice for not only the analysis of cell wall structure, dynamics, and function, but also the identification of genes involved in the synthesis and modification of MSC walls.

The physiology and composition of MSCs are now well reviewed [5,7], but less is known about the gene regulation networks leading to complex cell differentiation and ECM synthesis events. A partial regulation network has been drawn, mainly based on master regulatory genes and with a few target genes encoding proteins with enzymatic activity [8–11]. Three recent developments provided the rationale for this review: (i) the identification within the past 3 years of 15 new genes with a role in MSC physiology [10,12–23]; (ii) the focus of three recent ‘omics papers on seed coat development [24–26]; and (iii) the fact that recent MSC-focused reviews mainly deal with the mucilage composition [2–6]. For this review, we exhaustively mined the literature for the genes and/or proteins necessary for the correct synthesis, modification, and release of mucilage in Arabidopsis and organized these proteins within the socalled ‘MSC toolbox’. # MSC differentiation and establishment

In Arabidopsis, eight major steps are necessary for MSC differentiation and establishment, preceding the mucilage release (Figure 1) [1,3,5,7,27,28,4]. From the time of pollination until 4 days after pollination (DAP), MSCs become highly vacuolated and undergo a 3.5–4-fold increase in size, mainly through PW expansion (blue in Figure 1) [7]. During early stages of embryogenesis (3–7 DAP), starch granules are produced at the center of the outer PW, are then polarized first next to the radial primary walls (RPW) and are then pushed by accumulation of mucilage to the center of the MSCs [7,27]. At 7 DAP, an abundant mucilage polysaccharide (pink in Figure 1) starts to be secreted at the junction of the PW and RPW, together with the apparent reinforcement of the central part of the outer PW [1,5,7,27]. The mucilage continues to accumulate until 9–10 DAP (bending stage) and is progressively compacted between the PW and a newly synthesized polarized secondary wall, also called the columella (green in Figure 1). The columella starts to develop by 10 DAP, progressing inside the cell from the outer PW to the inner PW [7,27]. At the end of embryogenesis (13 DAP), the columella is strengthened, resulting in a volcano-shaped structure consisting entirely of cell wall material [7,27]. At the same time, the RPW is locally mechanically loosened to facilitate the future localized rupture [20]. Finally, when the seed undergoes desiccation, the mucilage is dehydrated (13–20 DAP). Following dry seed imbibition, which can occur from a few days up to several years after the completion of dehydration, germination starts with rapid hydration of the mucilage content and its expansion between the two cell walls (columella and PW). This leads, almost instantaneously, to the polarized rupture of the RPW and the extrusion of the mucilage. This extracellular polysaccharidic matrix acts as a gel with high water-retention properties that could have a role in seed rehydration, germination in drought conditions, and protection from abiotic stress during early steps of germination [29–35]. In Arabidopsis, two distinct mucilage layers have been detected after specific staining with ruthenium red. The inner adherent layer is denser and strongly adherent to the seed, as a result of pectocellulosic rays [36], whereas the outer nonadherent layer is more diffuse and easily lost [3]. The composition of these two layers is well documented [3,5,37–39], but their respective roles and relations need to be clarified, which is also complicated by the fact that mucilage organization and structure differ between species [2].

# Five major categories of proteins for mucilage development and/or release

Exhaustive bibliography mining enabled us to establish a ‘MSC toolbox’ with 60 identified proteins related to mucilage development and/or release Arabidopsis. Descriptions

of each protein are given in Table 1 and Table S1 in the supplementary material online, including an available Arabidopsis Genome Initiative (AGI) code for 54 out of the 60 hits and their putative function based on the phenotype of the corresponding mutant. Proteins were classified into five main categories (see Table 1, subheadings 1–5 and below for a brief description of each category), with two large categories identified. One contains the transcription factors (TFs) and the second includes proteins necessary for the synthesis, secretion, modification, and stabilization of mucilage. Furthermore, two under-represented categories were included: (i) proteins necessary for cell wall synthesis and modification; and (ii) proteins related to hormone synthesis and perception. Based on knowledge of cell wall synthesis, we expect additional proteins to be found, particularly members of the smaller categories. Finally, the last category contains miscellaneous proteins affecting mucilage synthesis or release.

# Transcriptional pathways

Batteries of pleotropic WD40, MYB, and basic helix-loophelix (bHLH) TFs necessary for seed coat differentiation, for normal epidermal cell morphology, and mucilage production and release, have already been identified and are well described (Table 1 and Table S1 in the supplementary material online). ENHANCER OF GLABRA3 (EGL3), MYB5, TRANSPARENT TESTA2 and 8 (TT2 and TT8), and TRANSPARENT TESTA GLABRA1 (TTG1) have been shown to form a WD40–bHLH–MYB multimeric complex [40,41] that modulates, with APETALA2 (AP2), the expression of two transcriptional subpathways through GLABRA2 (GL2) and TTG2 [8,40].

Despite the lack of a mucilage phenotype description for its corresponding mutant, GLABRA3 (GL3), which interacts with EGL3 [42], has been shown to regulate at least one gene necessary for mucilage release and, thus, could have a minor role in the process [11].

Recently, new regulators, such as DE1 BINDING FACTOR 1 (DF1), DNA-binding with one finger (DOF4), MUCILAGE-MODIFIED1 (MUM1), MYB61, and NAC-REGULATED SEED MORPHOLOGY 1 and 2 (NARS1 and NARS2), have also been analyzed [9,14,21, 43,44]. MUM1 is independent of the other master regulatory genes [45], while for the others, no relation has yet been demonstrated. These new TFs could be included in the wellknown transcriptional pathway [40,41] or could drive MUM1 independent regulation pathways [45]. In summary, excluding GL3, 21 TFs have been identified with a role during mucilage development. Interactions and regulation among these TFs have not yet been demonstrated. Several of these TFs are not only expressed in MSCs, but have also been detected in various organs or throughout embryogenesis. Their functions during MSC establishment are probably related to the regulation of specific genes that determine their spatiotemporal specificity.

# Cell wall synthesis and modification

Despite the dynamic processes required for the synthesis of a new secondary wall (columella) and the rearrangement of the primary cell wall (reinforcement above the columella and loosening in the external radial zones) (Figure 1), only four proteins have been recently characterized (Table 1 and Table S1 in the supplementary material online). These include two cellulose synthase subunits, CESA2 and CESA9, which are not fundamental for cellulose synthesis in other cells but important for correct seed coat cell shape [54,55]. A class III peroxidase (PER36/PRX36) and a subtilisin-like serine protease (SBT1.7) have also been described [20,32]. They are typical cell wall-localized enzymes and the accurate localization of PRX36 in RPW microdomains corresponded to the breaking zone upon seed rehydration (Figure 1). SBT1.7 probably triggers the accumulation and/or activation of cell wall-modifying enzymes necessary either for the loosening of the RPW, or for facilitating swelling of the mucilage, as indicated by elevated PME activity in developing atsbt1.7 mutant seeds [32]. It has to be noted that, since the mucilage, PW, and columella are ECMs, some of the proteins primarily involved in mucilage production (category 2) and those involved in cell wall synthesis (category 3) could have additional roles in the synthesis of cell walls (PW and columella) and mucilage, respectively. For example, MUM3/CESA5, which is primarily involved in mucilage stabilization through the production of rays of cellulose microfibrils, has also been shown to function redundantly with CESA2 and CESA9 for RPW reinforcement and columella synthesis [55,56]. Similarly, in addition to its role of mucilage stabilization, COBL2 has an additional role in controlling cellulose microfibril production in the RPW and the columella [23]. All together, as mentioned for the transcription factors, more candidates remain to be identified to explain the complex synthesis and modification of MSC cell walls during embryogenesis.

# Hormone synthesis and perception, and miscellaneous functions

Finally, the MSC toolbox is completed by ten proteins related to hormones and with miscellaneous or uncharacterized

functions. Five proteins involved in gibberellin and abscisic acid biosynthesis and signaling have been implicated in MSC physiology, with altered seed coat cell shape or reduced mucilage release phenotypes in corresponding mutants. Five additional miscellaneous proteins have been reported to have a role in mucilage release and corresponding references are listed in Table 1 and Table S1 in the supplementary material online.

In parallel, screening of ecotype collections revealed eight naturally occurring mutants with mucilage release phenotypes and identified causal mutations in the already characterized genes PMEI6, MUM2/BGAL6 and PRX36/ PER36 (Table 1 and Table S1 in the supplementary material online).

# MSC as a model of a complex regulatory network

Here, we have compiled most of the current expression data found in the literature and public resources, which has allowed us to update the model of the regulatory network controlling seed mucilage extrusion (Figure 2). We propose a new additional model, which is complementary to the hierarchical TFs cascade model [9] and the chronological events model [5], and includes all the regulatory relations between a subset of TFs and target genes from the MSC toolbox.

The theory of independent pathways between MUM1 and the WD40–bHLH–MYB complex [6,9] needs to be carefully evaluated. Indeed, SBT1.7 associated with the MUM1-independent pathway, is also regulated by three members of the WD40–bHLH–MYB complex: MYB5, TTG1, and TT8 (Figure 2) [11]. Therefore, it is more likely that the subpathways are interconnected for the fine-tuning of gene expression throughout the regulation of seed coat development.

Altogether, the fact that the corresponding mutants display similar impaired mucilage extrusion phenotypes despite differential regulation of PMEI6, BXL1, MUM2, PRX36, MUM4, PRX56, and SBT1.7, supports the complexity of the MSC regulatory pathway. In addition, PRX36 and DIR12 expression levels are controlled by common TFs, whereas only prx36 mutants display a mucilage phenotype (Figure 2).

# Tools for predicting new candidate genes

One obvious important feature to implicate a given gene in MSC dynamics is the demonstration of its MSC-specific expression. In situ hybridization [10,15,18,38,44,52,57,58] and promoter–reporter gene lines [10,20,21,23,30,32,33, 41,44,53,56,58,60–64] have been successfully used for nine and 23 of the MSC toolbox genes, respectively. With the advent of new omics technologies, such as microtranscriptomics of laser-captured tissues and the availability of powerful tools for co-expressed networks, the identification of new candidate genes required for seed coat development in Arabidopsis is possible. A differential and developmental tissue-specific seed transcriptomic study took advantage of the genetic separation of seed tegument cell layers in the ap2-7 and transparent testa16 (tt16-1) mutants, where the seed coat epidermis and/or palisade (oi2/oi1) and the endothelium do not develop, respectively [24]. # Concluding remarks and prospects

MSCs contain large amount of pectin domains in mucilage, the columella, and PW. It has been estimated that 67 transferases including glycosyl-, methyl-, and acetyltransferases, are necessary for pectin biosynthesis [66] and that a large set of pectinases, including PME, polygalacturonases (PG) and pectate lyase-like (PLL) pectinases, can be responsible for pectin postsynthesis modification. Two galacturonosyltransferases (GATL5 and GAUT11), two pectinases (BGAL6 and BXL1), and one PME inhibitor (PMEI6) have already been characterized, but several transferases, PG, PME, and PLL associated with the MSCs are yet to be discovered. Similar conclusions can be drawn regarding hemicellulose biosynthesis, which parallels pectin biosynthesis in the secretory pathway and involves a large number of uncharacterized enzymes.

The synthesis and modification of MSC primary cell wall, columella, and mucilage are complex processes that need to be controlled in a spatiotemporal and sometimes polarized manner throughout embryogenesis to allow proper mucilage release upon imbibition. The presence of plasma membrane and/or cell wall microdomains, as suggested for PRX36 and MUM3/CESA5 [20,36], could support finely tuned regulation and explain the polarized mucilage accumulation, the initiation of the new wall (columella), and the localized rupture of radial PW following seed imbibition. Determining the accurate subcellular localization of MSC toolbox proteins will be necessary to increase our knowledge of these potential microdomains.

It is now necessary to combine reverse and forward genetics, co-expression network analyses from specific transcriptomic experiments, proteomic analysis, and comparative genomics to identify missing candidates. In addition, because mucilage establishment is common in angiosperm species, previous approaches could be combined with taxonomic analysis to find new MSC-specific proteins.

Altogether, the discovery of new candidate genes and/or proteins will allow researchers to complete the MSC toolbox and should enable better understanding of MSC dynamics. Given the high complexity of multi-cell wall dynamics concentrated in this single cell type, it is anticipated that the new insights will also help researchers to understand the overall plant cell wall dynamics that occur during development.
