# HIGHLIGHTS

• Pst actively upregulates SPS1-B to enhance sucrose availability, promoting fungal growth. 

• SPS1-B mutation reduces sucrose accumulation, restricting Pst growth without compromising yield. 

• wAP2 suppresses SPS1-B transcription, inhibiting Pst proliferation. 

• WKS1 phosphorylates and suppresses SPS1-B, enhancing resistance against Pst. 

• Pyramiding the sps1-B allele, wAP2, and Yr36 is promising for increasing crop disease resistance and food security.

# ABSTRACT

Introduction: Stripe rust caused by Puccinia striiformis Westend. f. sp. tritici (Pst) is a highly destructive wheat disease that threatens global food security. Pst extracts energy from wheat by interfering with photosynthesis, leading to significant yield losses. Redirecting metabolite flux to counteract pathogens remains a major challenge in enhancing crop resilience.

Objective: The primary objective of this study is to clarify the regulations of sucrose synthesis in wheat during its interaction with Pst, especially in relation to susceptibility and resistance response, and to supply genetic resources for breeding programs dedicated to ensuring food security. 

Methods: Utilizing bulked segregant RNA sequencing (BSR-Seq), we identified and cloned a novel susceptibility (S) gene, sucrose 6 − phosphate synthase 1 (SPS1). We investigated the transcriptional and posttranslational regulations of SPS1 by Pst, the wheat APETALA2 transcription factor (wAP2), and Wheat Kinase START 1 (WKS1, Yr36) in transgenic plants using molecular and biochemical approaches. Sugar content variations were analyzed using gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC/MS) and colorimetric assays, while Pst infection dynamics were examined by staining or quantifying biomass and uredinial pustule densities. 

Results: Targeted mutagenesis of the Pst-inducible SPS1-B gene significantly reduced sucrose content accumulation and restricted Pst growth without compromising yield. In contrast, over-expressing SPS1-B enhanced Pst growth, confirming its role as a susceptibility (S) gene to Pst. Pst upregulated SPS1- B under optimal conditions, enhancing its own pathogenic success. Conversely, wAP2 suppressed SPS1- B transcription, reduced SPS1 protein level, and inhibited Pst infection intensity in transgenic wheat lines. Moreover, WKS1, a high-temperature adult-plant resistance protein, bound, phosphorylated, and suppressed SPS1-B at the post-translational level. 

Conclusion: This study identifies SPS1-B as a pivotal molecular switch in sucrose metabolism hijacked by Pst to support its infection. The characterization of SPS1-B and its upstream regulators provides multiple genetic targets for enhancing wheat resistance against stripe rust.

# Introduction

Wheat (Triticum aestivum) is one of the primary staple crops globally but suffers an annual yield loss of $1 5 \sim 2 0 \%$ due to fungal pathogens [1]. Among these, wheat stripe rust caused by Puccinia striiformis Westend. f. sp. tritici (Pst) ranks as the third most significant fungal pathogen, affecting major food crops, leading to substantial yield losses [2,3]. Since 2011, the emergence of the Warrior race group, a new exotic incursion of Pst, has rapidly expanded across Central and Western Europe, with further spread into Africa and the Middle East [4]. The continuous evolution and spread of new virulent rust fungi have worsened the situation of stripe rust control [5,6]. Current control strategies primarily involve agronomic practices, fungicide application, and host genetic resistance [7]. Compared to chemical control, incorporating Pst-resistance genes in breeding programs offers an environmentally friendly and cost-effective solution [8]. 

Non-race-specific resistance genes are effective against multiple races of a particular pathogen and are generally more durable and broadly applicable compared to race-specific resistance genes [9–11]. In wheat, three non-race-specific resistance genes have been cloned and studied in-depth. The resistance gene Yr18/Lr34 encodes an ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter and confers broad-spectrum resistance to fungal diseases by transporting sinapyl alcohol, which contributes to cell wall lignification in wheat [12,13]. Additionally, Yr18/Lr34 localizes in the plasma membrane, alters the accumulation of phosphatidic acid and enhances the outward translocation of phosphatidylserine [14]. Yr46/Lr67 encodes a hexose transporter involved in sugar uptake and confers resistance to multiple biotrophic pathogen species in both wheat and barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) [15,16]. The LR67:YFP fusion protein resides in the plasma membrane, which is consistent with its function of transporting sugars [16]. Altering host sugar levels, as suggested for the resistant allele of Yr46/Lr67, appears to limit the extent of biotrophic fungal pathogen colonization [16]. Yr36 encodes a distinct protein having a serine/threonine kinase domain fused to a putative steroidogenic acute regulatory protein-related lipidtransfer (START) domain and is therefore called wheat kinase START 1 (WKS1) [17]. WKS1 confers resistance to a broad spectrum of stripe rust races at relatively high temperatures $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ to $3 5 ^ { \circ } C )$ ) but is susceptible at lower temperatures, such as $1 5 \mathrm { { } ^ \circ C }$ [17]. Unlike 

Yr18 and Yr46, WKS1 can enter the chloroplast from the cytoplasm and has kinase activity regulating the activity and stability of key enzymes or proteins, such as zeaxanthin epoxidase 1 (ZEP1, cytoplasmic), thylakoid-associated ascorbate peroxidase, PsbO (thylakoidal), and keto-acyl thiolase (KAT-2B, peroxisomal), in different metabolic pathways through phosphorylation [18–21]. The WKS1-mediated phosphorylation contributes to cell death, thereby restricting pathogen growth and sporulation [19,20]. The above progress on adult plant resistance genes suggests that the activation of plant defense responses is closely associated with a substantial regulation of plant metabolism and that controlling metabolism could enhance wheat pathogen resistance. 

Carbohydrates serve as key metabolites that pathogens actively redirect from host plants to facilitate reproduction [22]. CWIN (cell wall invertase) and sugar transporters, including STP (sugar transport protein) and SWEET (sugar will eventually be exported transporters), have been conditioned by the specific pathosystem with diverse roles in plant resistance against biotrophic or necrotrophic pathogens [23]. Bacterial pathogens secrete effectors, e.g., transcription activator-like effectors (TALEs), which directly bind promoters of rice SWEET genes, exploiting their expression and enhancing cellular uptake/efflux of sugars to pathogens [24]. The repeated SWEET genes in a marker-trait association locus in the wheat genome co-segregated with Pm26 to confer powdery mildew resistance [25]. Pst hijacked TaWRKY19, TaWRKY61, and TaWRKY82 and induced sugar transporter TaSTP3 to enhance stripe rust susceptibility in wheat [26]. TaSTP13, another Pst-inducible sugar transporter, localized in the plasma membrane and functioned as homo-oligomers to increase the susceptibility to pathogens [27]. Suppressing susceptibility genes like sugar transporters and other defense deregulators increases wheat’s resilience to pathogens [28]. Nevertheless, there are continuous efforts to deregulate genetically modified or gene-edited food crops in European countries and worldwide. Therefore, identifying and cloning novel S genes involved in sugar homeostasis is essential for enhancing crop disease resistance using classical genetic approaches. 

As the main carbohydrate transported from source to sink tissues, sucrose is easily accessible to pathogens. To facilitate sugar absorption, Pst usually enhances the activity of Pst invertase to pro-

mote the hydrolysis of sucrose in infected wheat leaves [29]. In plants, sucrose biosynthesis involves SPS, a key rate-limiting enzyme [30]. Using UDP-glucose and fructose-6-phosphate as substrates, SPS catalyzes the synthesis of sucrose-6-phosphate, which is further hydrolyzed by sucrose phosphatase to form sucrose, the major energy source and transport form of sugars [31]. SPS activity is tightly regulated in plants through multiple mechanisms [32]. The transcription of SPS is developmentally regulated and modulated by environmental factors such as light [32]. SPS is activated under light and osmotic stress through alterations in the phosphorylation state of several serine residues [30]. Nevertheless, the protein kinases responsible for phosphorylating SPS and their role in wheat’s defense against Pst remain unclear. 

In this study, we report the isolation of a senescence-associated gene against pathogens in young seedlings 1 (spy1) mutant. We show that spy1 reduces the growth and development of Pst. The spy1 mutant contains a premature stop mutation in the SPS1-B gene, which catalyzes sucrose phosphate biosynthesis. Furthermore, wAP2 suppresses SPS1-B expression and enhances resistance to Pst in transgenic lines. Moreover, WKS1 phosphorylates SPS1-B, reducing its stability and activity, thereby suppressing Pst. This work broadens strategies to stabilize wheat yield in future elite varieties. 

# Material and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

We generated the $0 . 7 \%$ ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS) induced mutants in 2016 and transgenic plants overexpressing SPS1-B (in 2019) or wAP2-A (in 2021) in the tetraploid wheat Kronos (Triticum turgidum var. durum). In 2019, the sps1-A and sps1-B2 EMS mutants were obtained from a sequenced EMS mutant library [33]. The WKS1 and $P r o _ { U b i } .$ :TAP-WKS1.1 transgenic plants were generated in the hexaploid wheat cultivar Bobwhite (BW) [17]. The $P r o _ { U b i }$ :TAP-WKS1.1 transgenic plants were used for coimmunoprecipitation (co-IP) experiments. We used three transgenic lines (26b-6, 26b-15, and 17a-15) carrying the WKS1 gene under its native promoter in BW to quantify the protein level and the enzyme activity of SPS [19]. 

The plants were cultivated in a growth chamber with $^ { 1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ light/8h dark at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } /$ $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ $6 5 \ \%$ relative humidity, and 250 mmol $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ light intensity. The EMS mutants were backcrossed with parent lines twice to remove background mutations before phenotypic analysis. The transgenic lines and EMSsegregating mutants were evaluated in at least two generations. 

The materials for phenotypic evaluation were grown at the Fudan University Experimental Station (31°27′N, 121°52′E) in Shanghai, representing the southern part of the Yangtze Spring Wheat Zone, and Jize, Hebei, representing the northern part of the Yangtze Spring Wheat Zone. All three genotypes (wild type [WT], sps1-A, and sps1-B) were planted in a $3 \times 3$ lattice square design with three replications in December 2021. We analyzed the agronomic traits of $\mathsf { B C } _ { 3 } \mathsf { F } _ { 3 }$ homozygous lines in June 2023 following the method described in a previous publication [34]. The quality traits were measured using grains harvested from the same field with the Perten Diode Array 7200 NIR Analysis System (PerkinElmer, US LLC., Shelton, CT, USA). 

# Pst inoculations

Pst race CYR32 was used in this study. The wheat cultivar MingXian169 was used to propagate Pst race CYR32 spores. The Pstinoculated wheat plants were incubated at $1 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ with $1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ of light and at $1 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with $^ { 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of dark. Two weeks post-inoculation, fresh Pst 

uredospores were collected and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ before the next inoculation. For Pst inoculation on mutants and transgenic wheat plants, the second leaves of two-week-old seedlings were inoculated with uredospores of CYR32 by the smear method. The collected uredospores were applied to the adaxial surface using a fine bamboo skewer. After inoculation, wheat plants were moisturized in the dark at $1 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ for one day and then placed in an chamber at $1 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ under light and at $1 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in the dark with a 16 h light/8h dark cycle. 

At 48 h post-inoculation (hpi), leaves were collected and stained with wheat germ agglutinin conjugated to Alexa-488 (Invitrogen, A12379) or $^ { 3 , 3 ^ { \prime } }$ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) (Merck, D12384) to analyze the hyphal area and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation, respectively [35]. The presence of a substomatal vesicle was defined as an established infection unit. For each wheat leaf sample in each biological replication, 20–50 infection sites were examined from three leaves. An Olympus IX73 microscope (Olympus Corporation) and CellSens software were used to visualize and quantify $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation. An ECLIPSE Ni-U microscope (Nikon Instruments Inc.) and NIS-Elements D software were used to measure and analyze the hyphal area. 

At 14 days post-inoculation (dpi), the disease phenotypes were photographed. The inoculated leaves were collected to count the Pst uredinial pustule number or measure fungal biomass by quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) [36]. We extracted the total DNA from the adult leaves in segregating ${ \tt B C } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F } _ { 3 }$ plants at Zadok’s Growth Scale Z14. The plants were genotyped by PCR using gene-specific primers followed by Sanger sequencing (Sangon, Shanghai, China). 

# BSR-Seq analysis

RNA was extracted with TRIzol (TaKaRa, 9108) from infected leaves of ${ \tt B C } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ generation plants based on the spy1 phenotype. RNA samples were mixed from 15 individual homozygous plants at an equal ratio to form one group. Two groups were collected from Pst-infected WT and homozygous mutants, with one group each of non-infected WT and the spy1 mutant serving as the control for each genotype. The above samples were used to construct PE300 libraries and were sequenced using the Illumina Hiseq4000 platform (Meiji Bio-Tech, Shanghai, China). Around 6 Gb of highquality data was generated from each group and analyzed to call homozygous single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) with reads higher than 10 in any group using BCFTools v0.1.19 [37]. Differentially expressed genes were filtered by |Fold change $^ { \vert > 2 }$ , FDR (q value) $< 0 . 0 0 1$ , and transcripts per million (TPM) over 20 in at least one sample using the MA-plot-based method with a random sampling model in DEGseq v1.20.0 [38]. Gene assembly and annotation were conducted according to wheat assembly (TGACv1.1). The original RNA-Seq data have been deposited in the NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus Database (PRJNA877083). 

# Plasmid construction and wheat transformation

For overexpression in wheat, coding sequences of SPS1-B and wAP2-A were amplified and cloned into pDONR207 by BP recombination reaction (Gateway BP Clonase II Enzyme Mix, Invitrogen, 11789100). An LR recombination reaction was performed to transfer the sequence to the modified pGWB18 (Gateway LR Clonase II Enzyme Mix, Invitrogen, 11791020). The constructs were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105. According to earlier reports, immature embryos isolated from Kronos plants were pretreated with centrifugation $_ { 2 0 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and co-cultivated with Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 to generate transgenic wheat [39]. The transformants were screened for hygromycin $( 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L } )$ resistance and confirmed by 

qRT-PCR or Western blotting. Primers used for cloning are listed in Table S2. 

# Gene expression and phylogenetic analysis

For digital Northern analysis of the candidate genes, we downloaded the RNA-Seq data of wheat samples infected with fungal pathogens (PRJNA243835 and PRJEB12497) [40,41]. The raw data were analyzed following the above procedure, and the expression data of the four candidate genes’ homologs in Chinese Spring were extracted. TPM values were applied for normalization, and a heatmap (Fig. 1G) was generated. 

We identified 15 TaSPS genes in the genome annotation V1.1 of wheat (Chinese Spring). The expression of all TaSPS genes was analyzed based on publicly available RNA-Seq data from different tissues and various developmental stages (www.wheat-expression. com). The expression levels of target genes were analyzed with primers specific to corresponding genes (Table S1). Phylogenetic anal-

# Prokaryotic expression and enzymatic activity assay

The coding sequences of SPS1-B and WKS1.1 were amplified and integrated into the expression vector pET-28a and pET41b by LR reaction, respectively. The correct recombinant prokaryotic expression plasmid, pET-28a-SPS1-B, was transformed into Escherichia coli (E. coli) BL21(DE3) pLysS competent cells (WEIDI Biotech, Shanghai, China). The plasmid pET41b-WKS1.1 was transformed into E. coli Rosetta (DE3) competent cells (WEIDI Biotech, Shanghai, China). The recombinant protein was expressed in E. coli by induction with $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl- $\cdot \beta \cdot$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) for $^ { 2 0 \mathrm { h } }$ at $1 8 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ . The bacterial cells were then harvested by centrifugation at $5 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { g }$ for $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ The precipitation was used for enzymatic activity assay or protein purification. 

For the enzymatic activity assay, the precipitation was resuspended in a protein extraction buffer containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Hepes-NaOH (pH 7.4), 20 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, $2 \ \mathrm { m M }$ dithiothreitol, and $1 \times$ EDTA-free Protease Inhibitor Cocktail, followed by sonication. The supernatant was collected by centrifugation at $^ { 1 7 , 0 0 0 } \mathrm { g }$ for 15 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and used for enzymatic activity assay. A $3 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot of the crude enzyme extract was incubated for $2 0 ~ \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $2 5 \mathrm { { } ^ \circ C }$ in a $7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction mixture containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Hepes-NaOH (pH 7.4), 5 mM $\mathrm { { M g C l } } _ { 2 }$ , $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { \ m M }$ dithiothreitol, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ UDP-glucose, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ fructose-6-phosphate, and $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ glucose-6-phosphate. The reaction was terminated by adding $7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 1 M NaOH, followed by incubation at $9 5 \mathrm { { ^ \circ C } }$ for $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ to degrade unreacted substrates. After cooling, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $0 . 1 \mathrm { ~ \% ~ } ( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { w } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } )$ resorcinol in $9 5 \%$ ethanol and 1 mL 6 M HCl were added, followed by incubation at $8 0 \%$ for 10 min. # Results

# Molecular cloning of SPY1

We isolated a recessive spy1 mutant from a tetraploid wheat EMS mutant library, which slowed the proliferation of Pst in leaves (Fig. 1A). The spy1 mutant leaves contained fewer pustules and less fungal biomass than WT at 14 dpi (Fig. 1B and S1A). At 2 dpi, the burst of reactive oxygen species (ROS) was more robust in the spy1 mutant (Fig. 1C), with a significant increase in the average $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production (Fig. 1D) compared with WT. Pst hyphae had fewer branches in the spy1 mutant than WT (Fig. 1E), with significantly smaller hyphae areas at 2 dpi and 4 dpi (Fig. 1F and S1B-C). Therefore, the spy1 mutant exhibited advantages in suppressing Pst over WT in both the early and late stages of infection. 

We backcrossed the spy1 mutant twice to remove background mutations and collected ${ \tt B C } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ generation segregated for the Pst resistance phenotype. Using BSR-Seq, we identified four non-

synonymous SNPs located on chromosome 4B (Fig. 1G). Among the four genes, only one gene (TraesCS4B02G091100) showed significant upregulation in leaf samples during Pst infection (Fig. 1G). In the spy1 mutant, TraesCS4B02G091100, encoding a sucrose 6- phosphate synthase, carries a truncation mutation (a premature stop codon in the first exon, CAA to TAA, resulting in $\boldsymbol { \mathrm { Q 2 5 ^ { * } } }$ ) (Fig. 1- H-I). 

To investigate the correlation between the SPS1-B mutation and the enhanced Pst resistance, we analyzed the ${ \tt B C } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F } _ { 3 }$ generation segregated from spy1. All the plants with a functional SPS1-B allele were susceptible to Pst, similar to WT (Fig. S1D). In contrast, all the plants with the SPS1-B mutation developed fewer Pst pustules (Fig. S1D). These results suggest that SPS-B co-segregates with Pst resistance. 

The protein encoded by TraesCS4B02G091100 belongs to the SPS protein family and localizes in the cytoplasm (Fig. 2A). The SPS1-B enzyme catalyzes the conversion of UDP-glucose and fructose-6- phosphate into sucrose-6-phosphate (Fig. 2B), a key step in the primary sucrose biosynthesis pathway in plant cells [47]. The wheat SPS gene family is classified into five subgroups (I-V), and SPS1-B resides in Group I (Fig. 2C). Among its structural components, motifs 1 and 2 are the most conserved parts, covering the UDP binding pocket with motif 5 (PF00534, Pfam) (Fig. S2). In contrast, motifs 4, 6, 7, and 8 constitute a glycosyl transferase 4-like domain (PF13579, Pfam) (Fig. 2D) [48]. The C terminal region is less conserved, with motif 9 correlated to a potential sucrose-6Fphosphate phosphohydrolase domain (PF05116, Pfam) (Fig. 2D) [49]. Based on the gene expression patterns, the expression levels of SPS1s in group I subfamilies were relatively higher than other groups in wheat leaves (Fig. 2E). Notably, the elevated expression of SPS1-B correlated with a significant reduction in endogenous SPS1 protein levels in the sps1-B mutant1 (Fig. 2F). 

# Genetic validation of SPS1-B as a wheat-susceptibility gene to Pst

To validate the contribution of the SPS1-B mutation to Pstresistance, we analyzed induced loss-of-function mutants of SPS1. From a public EMS mutant library of tetraploid wheat Kronos [33], we obtained another premature stop mutant of the SPS1-B gene, created a $\mathsf { B C } _ { 3 } \mathsf { F } _ { 3 }$ homozygous line, and named it sps1-B2. Additionally, we obtained a premature stop mutant in the SPS1-A gene within the A genome and generated a $\mathsf { B C } _ { 3 } \mathsf { F } _ { 3 }$ homozygous line, sps1-A (Fig. S3A-C). 

Furthermore, to assess the Pst resistance, we first analyzed the sps1-B2 mutant, which harbors a premature stop codon at the C terminus of SPS1-B. The SPS1-B knock-out in the sps1-B2 mutant successfully inhibited the Pst growth (Fig. 3A), significantly reducing the pustule density (Fig. 3B). The above phenotype of the sps1- B2 mutant mimicked those of the spy1 (sps1-B1) mutant, confirming SPS1-B as an S gene. Nevertheless, the sps1-A mutant with a premature stop codon did not substantially mitigate Pst growth (Fig. S3D-E). 

Next, we created a double mutant of SPS1 (sps1-A/B1) by crossing the sps1-B1 and sps1-A mutant lines. The sps1-A/B1 double mutant leaves had fewer pustules than WT at 14 dpi (Fig. 3C-D). We generated a polyclonal antibody with an SPS1-specific peptide surrounding the premature stop codon in sps-B2, located after the premature stop codons in sps1-B1 and sps1-A (Fig. S4). In the sps1- A/B1 double mutant, the endogenous level of SPS1 protein was under the detectable level at the regular exposure condition (Fig. 3E and S4). Moreover, the enzymic activity of SPS in the total protein was significantly lower in sps1-B1 and reduced by over $7 5 \ \%$ in the sps1-A/B1 double mutants than in WT (Fig. 3F). 

We checked the contents of sucrose and glucose in WT at different times of the circadian rhythms. The sucrose contents increased drastically in the daytime but remained low in the dark (Fig. 3G). 

The contents of glucose also increased in the daytime (Fig. 3H). The sucrose contents before light decreased in the sps1 mutants (Fig. 3I), and might explain the reduced growth of Pst. However, those of glucose showed no correlation with Pst growth (Fig. 3J). Moreover, Pst infection induced sucrose accumulation by $2 7 \ \%$ to $32 \%$ in WT at 48 hpi, but the sucrose levels were reduced by $9 \%$ to $1 5 \ \%$ in the sps1-B1 mutant and $34 \%$ to $49 \%$ in the sps1-B2 mutants (Fig. 3K). The sucrose reductions were more robust in the sps1-B mutants, consistent with the increased resistance against Pst, but not in the sps1-A mutant (Fig. 3B and S3E). The sps1-B mutants displayed no significant difference in the grain weight (Fig. 3L) or yield (Table S2), but those of the sps1-A mutant and the sps1-A/B1 mutants were significantly lower (Fig. 3L). The protein contents were significantly higher in all the sps1 single or double mutants (Fig. 3M). Conversely, the starch contents were lower in the sps1 mutants (Fig. 3N). 

To test if the expression of SPS1-B is sufficient to induce susceptibility, we generated transgenic wheat lines overexpressing the SPS1-B gene (SPS1-B O.E.) and evaluated their susceptibility. In the SPS1-B O.E. lines, the expression levels of the SPS1-B gene were 2.5 to 4 fold of that in WT (Fig. 3O). The above transgenic lines accumulated more SPS1 protein than WT (Fig. 3P). We inoculated the above samples with Pst CYR32 and checked the Pst growth. At 14 dpi, Pst uredinial pustules appeared in WT and SPS1-B O.E. lines (Fig. 3Q). The pustule densities in three independent SPS1-B O.E. lines were 162.6 % to 239.8 % of that in the WT (pvalue $= 0 . 0 1 2 2$ , Fig. 3R). 

In addition, we further compared the effects of SPS1-mediated sugar change on the expression patterns of sugar transporters based on the differentially expressed genes in the RNA-Seq data of sps1-B1 and WT, as well as previous publications [26,27]. The expression levels of SWEET2b2, SWEET16a, and the sucrose transporter gene (SUT3) were significantly higher in the SPS1-B transgenic lines than in WT after Pst infection (Fig. S5A-C) [27]. Moreover, TaSTP6 and TaSTP13 were up-regulated in SPS1-B transgenic lines after Pst infection (Fig. S5D-F) [27,50]. The above data suggested a coordinated regulation of sugar metabolism and transportation in the wheat-Pst system. The above results demonstrate that SPS1-B is necessary and sufficient to confer susceptibility to Pst. Therefore, the SPS1-B mutation underpinned the increased resistance phenotype observed in the sps1 and related mutants.

# WKS1 phosphorylates SPS1-B and reduces sucrose content

As mentioned above, SPS1, a Pst-susceptibility protein, was induced by Pst at $1 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ but not at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at the protein level (Fig. 4B). Nevertheless, WKS1, a high-temperature dependent APR protein against Pst [17], was induced by Pst at a high temperature (Fig. 4B). To investigate if the sucrose decrease was conserved or not in APR, which is more durable and valuable in wheat breeding [51], we compared the change of sucrose in WKS1 transgenic lines. The sucrose contents decreased sharply in WKS1 transgenic lines (Fig. 6A), indicating that WKS1 could regulate sucrose metabolism in wheat with an unknown mechanism. In the co-IP products of TAP-WKS1.1 (the full-length WKS1 transcript), there were multiple peptide fragments of SPS1 (Fig. S8). Therefore, we cloned the different functional domains of WKS1.1 and SPS1-B to investigate their protein–protein interaction. 

In the Y2H system, the SPP (820–1058 a.a.) or linker-SPP (700– 1058 a.a.) domains of SPS1-B interacted with the interlinker-START (IS) domain of WKS1.1 (Fig. 6C). The purified recombinant WKS1.1- 

His protein successfully pulled the endogenous SPS1 from the total protein in the wheat leaves, indicating their in vivo protein–protein interaction (Fig. 6D). In a split firefly luciferase experiment, WKS1.1 and the linker-SPP domain formed a protein complex to reconstruct a functional firefly luciferase in plant cells (Fig. 6E). The above data provide evidence of the protein–protein interaction between WKS1.1 and SPS1-B. 

WKS1.1 possesses a protein kinase domain, which could phosphorylate its interacting partners [19,20]. To investigate the effect of WKS1.1 on SPS1-B, we first analyzed the phosphorylation of SPS1-B by WKS1.1 using purified recombinant proteins. In the protein kinase reaction using $\gamma \mathrm { - P } ^ { 3 2 }$ -ATP, WKS1.1 transferred $\gamma { - } \bar { \mathsf { P } } ^ { \bar { 3 } 2 }$ to recombinant SPS1-B, which resulted in a clear radiation signal at the molecular size of SPS1-B (Fig. 6F). Next, we performed the protein kinase reaction using cold ATP. We stained the samples with a fluorescence dye, Pro- $\cdot Q ^ { \circledast }$ Diamond, which recognizes phosphorylated proteins [52]. The fluorescence signals in SPS1-B samples with WKS1 (SPS1- $\mathsf { B } + \mathsf { W K S } 1 . 1 + \mathsf { A T P } )$ ) were significantly higher than those in samples without kinase reaction (SPS1-B + WKS1.1-ATP, $p = 0 . 0 0 2 1 _ { . }$ ) or those lacking SPS1-B (WKS1.1 + ATP, $p = 0 . 0 0 0 4 )$ ) (Fig. 6G). The above data demonstrated that WKS1 phosphorylated SPS1-B. 

WKS1 phosphorylation affected the biochemical activities of targets, such as ZEP1 and thylakoidal ascorbate peroxidase, to accelerate $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation and suppress Pst [18,19]. The relative enzymatic activity of recombinant SPS1-B with WKS1.1 phosphorylation was lower than that of the control without ATP (Fig. 6H), suggesting that the phosphorylation reduced $S \mathrm { P } S 1 ^ { \prime } s$ activity in vitro. # Discussion

Disabling S genes is a promising alternative to resistance (R) genes in breeding programs, as they usually offer durable and broad-spectrum disease resistance [53]. Despite the conservation of sucrose phosphate synthases in plants, their contribution to plant disease resistance remains uncharacterized. This work revealed Pst-inducible SPS1-B as a wheat S gene that produces sucrose, an essential energy source for the pathogen. We illustrated the high-temperature-associated regulation on SPS1-B at both transcription and post-translational levels by the wAP2 and WKS1.1, respectively. The premature stop mutations in SPS1-B opened the door to modulating it at the low temperature $( 1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ to inhibit Pst. 

The SPS protein family in wheat consists of 15 members (Fig. 2A). In Western blot analysis, the sps1-A/B1 double mutant failed to produce a signal when tested with a polyclonal antibody designed against SPS1-B (Fig. 3E and S4). This indicates that the antibody specifically recognizes certain epitopes present in SPS1- A and SPS1-B. Total SPS activity in the sps1-A/B1 double mutant decreased by $7 5 \ \%$ compared to WT, underscoring the dominant role of SPS1-A and SPS1-B in young wheat leaves (Fig. 3F). Furthermore, Pst infection induced SPS1-B expression, and overexpression of SPS1-B promoted Pst growth in wheat. Consistent with this, the expression levels of SWEET2b2, SWEET16a, and SUT3 were significantly elevated in the SPS1-B O.E. transgenic line (Fig. S5). Additionally, the expression of glucose transporter genes STP6 and STP13 was significantly higher in the SPS1-B O.E. transgenic line (Fig. S5). Pst infection also promoted the accumulation of abscisic acid (ABA), which in turn upregulated the sugar transporter TaSTP6, leading to increased glucose accumulation and heightened wheat susceptibility [50]. Editing the TALE-binding elements in three SWEET promoters altered their expression and conferred resistance to endemic and emerging Xanthomonas oryzae strains in elite African rice [54]. These findings indicate that Pst manipulates SPS1-B and other sugar transporters in wheat to redirect sugar flux toward the pathogen, thereby enhancing its virulence. 

SPS1-A and SPS1-B play different functions in wheat under conditions without and with Pst invasion. Without Pst invasion, the level of SPS1 protein showed a more substantial reduction in the sps1-A mutant than the sps1-B mutants (Fig. 3E), suggesting that SPS1-A is dominant in wheat leaves without Pst infection. On the other hand, the sucrose contents exhibited a more severe reduction in the sps1-B mutants than the sps1-A mutant after Pst infection (Fig. 3H), supporting that SPS1-B is a susceptibility gene hijacked by Pst. 

Plants are the primary nutrient source for both symbiotic and pathogenic microorganisms. In cereal crops, sugar is a key energy source for pathogens, which acquire it by manipulating host sugar efflux systems [55]. This study illustrates the distinct regulation and contribution of SPS1-B to Pst. At $1 6 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ , Pst induces SPS1-B expression (Fig. 4C-E), which correlates with increased sucrose biosynthesis, the primary form of carbohydrate transport in plants. However, at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C _ { \mathsf { r } }$ , wAP2 could inhibit SPS1-B at the transcription level in the nucleus (Fig. 5C). Overexpression of wAP2 suppressed SPS1-B and Pst infection in transgenic wheat (Fig. 5). Consequently, SPS1-B functions as a critical molecular switch in sugar metabolism, determining whether the host energy levels are ‘‘upregulated” to support Pst infection or ‘‘tuned-down” to create unfavorable environment for Pst growth. 

Phosphorylation is a typical post-transcriptional regulatory mechanism of SPS. The phosphorylation of SPS occurs so frequently in plants that it serves as a positive control for protein kinase reactions [56]. Phosphorylation and dephosphorylation antagonistically tune SPS in spinach (Spinacia oleracea) and peach (Prunus persica) [57,58]. The effect of SPS phosphorylation on biotic stress was elusive before this work. This work revealed the accelerated degradation of SPS1 in WKS1 transgenic plants (Fig. 6J-K), providing another regulation of SPS supplementary to the biochemical inactivation. The combined transcription and post-transcriptional regulations of SPS1-B reduce sucrose availability, restricting Pst growth under high-temperature conditions in planta. 

The discovery of Pst-resistance in sps1-B1 mutants provides a valuable opportunity to precisely manipulate SPS1-B for enhanced wheat disease resistance. With the rapid advancement of genomeediting technologies, targeted modifications in food crops have become increasingly efficient, accelerating crop improvement efforts [59]. Precision genome editing of Mildew resistance locus O (MLO) conferred durable and broad-spectrum resistance to powdery mildew in elite wheat varieties [60]. Modifying three wheat TaEDR1 homolog genes by genome editing successfully enhanced powdery mildew resistance [61]. Therefore, the loss-of-function mutations in SPS1-B identified here have the potential to improve wheat resistance against Pst infection, particularly during the seedling stage, and reduce yield losses. 

The agronomic and quality data demonstrated that the sps1-B mutants have advantages in both stripe rust resistance and nutrient values but with no significant yield penalty in the tested conditions. However, further tests in different locations with different Pst pressures or other abiotic stresses are needed to investigate the adaptability of the sps1-B mutants to diverse ecosystems. The current data suggested that SPS1-B is a promising S geneediting target to enhance Pst resistance in elite wheat varieties. # Conclusion

This study identifies SPS1-B as a novel S gene for Pst, a major threat to global wheat production. Pst infection triggers the upregulation of SPS1-B, enhancing sucrose-6-phosphate biosynthesis and subsequently increasing sucrose content. However, premature stop mutations in sps1-B1 and sps1-B2 mutants lead to reduced sucrose content, resulting in elevated resistance to Pst and increased grain protein content. The transcription factor wAP2 negatively regulates SPS1-B expression, thereby limiting sucrose accumulation and enhancing wheat defense against Pst. Furthermore, WKS1.1, encoded by Yr36, phosphorylates SPS1-B, reducing its abundance and enzymatic activity and further enhancing wheat resistance to Pst. The identified genes and alleles provide valuable genetic resources for future wheat breeding programs aimed at improving resistance to Pst.
