# Summary

 Acylsugars, specialized metabolites produced by trichomes of many solanaceous species, provide protection against biotic and abiotic stresses. Many acylsugar metabolic enzymes have been identified; however, regulatory factors remain unknown. 

 Our multidisciplinary approaches identified LEAFLESS (APETALA 2/ ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR (AP2/ERF) family member) as a positive regulator of acylsugar biosynthesis. 

 Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) of LEAFLESS in Solanum pennellii (SpLFS/Sopen05g008450) revealed its distinct roles in two related but separate processes: acylsugar biosynthesis and trichome development. Most acylsugar (and several flavonoid) metabolic genes were downregulated in SpLFS-silenced plants and showed strong co-expression with SpLFS. Phylogenetic and additional data analyses indicated trichome-enriched expression of SpLFS orthologs in other acylsugar-producing solanaceous species, and VIGS of SpLFS orthologs in Nicotiana benthamiana reduced acylsugar production. Transcriptional reporter showed expression of SpLFS in type I/IV trichome tip cells, the site of acylsugar biosynthesis. Electrophoretic mobility shift assays indicated that SpLFS directly binds to promoters of several acylsugar (and flavonoid) metabolic genes. Additionally, data mining suggested remarkable spatiotemporal functional diversity: from coordinating leaf initiation at incipient primordia (previously reported for the S. lycopersicum ortholog SlLFS/Solyc05g013540) to regulating trichome specialized metabolism (acylsugar and flavonoid). 

 Our work highlights a critical role of LEAFLESS in trichome specialized metabolism, paving the way to disentangle the acylsugar regulatory network. 

# Introduction

Plants produce a myriad of phylogenetically restricted specialized metabolites, which play important roles in plant–environment interactions. These compounds are widely used as medicines and other valuable phytochemicals. Acylsugars are specialized metabolites produced in and secreted by trichomes of many plants in the Solanaceae, which includes important agricultural crops such as tomato (Solanum lycopersicum), potato (S. tuberosum), eggplant (S. melongena), and tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum). These compounds have major roles in plant defense against herbivores, insect pests, and microbial pathogens (Juvik et al., 1994; Chortyk et al., 1997; Hare, 2005; Weinhold & Baldwin, 2011; Leckie et al., 2016; Luu et al., 2017; Mutschler et al., 2023). Many acylsugars exhibit stronger insecticidal activity than standard insecticidal soap (Puterka et al., 2003). Additionally, acylsugars protect plants from desiccation (Feng et al., 2021). Metabolic engineering of acylsugar production offers great potential for improving acylsugar-mediated plant defense and desiccation tolerance. However, success depends on identifying critical regulatory factors that control structural genes in acylsugar metabolic pathways. 

Acylsugars are glucose and sucrose esters of both branchedand straight-chain fatty acids (Kroumova et al., 2016; Moghe et al., 2017), which are derived from branched-chain amino acids (Val, Leu, and Ile) and acetyl-CoA (de novo biosynthesis mediated by the fatty acid synthase complex), respectively (Walters & Steffens, 1990; Mandal et al., 2020). ACYLSUGAR ACYLTRANSFERASE (ASAT) enzymes attach these acyl groups (C2–C12 in length) to the sugar moiety, and different species of the Solanaceae have evolved specific sets of ASATs to synthesize acylsugars (Schilmiller et al., 2015; Fan et al., 2016; Moghe et al., 2017; Nadakuduti et al., 2017; Feng et al., 2021). The evolution of catalytic functions of ASATs to combine products of primary metabolism (sucrose and acyl molecules) into specialized metabolic acylsugars was a critical step in the evolutionary emergence of acylsugar biosynthesis. The functionality of the newly evolved ASATs and other acylsugar metabolic proteins required the recruitment of transcription factors (TFs) to ensure proper gene expression in trichomes, the site of acylsugar biosynthesis in the Solanaceae. Identification of these TFs has been an open and interesting question in the study of acylsugar metabolism. 

Metabolic pathways are tightly controlled, and TFs play important roles in regulating spatial, temporal, and environmental 

expression of structural genes by binding to cis-regulatory elements of target genes. The APETALA2/ETHYLENE RESPONSE FAC-TOR (AP2/ERF) family is one of the largest families of plant TFs. These proteins are characterized by one or two copies of the conserved AP2 DNA-binding domain of c. 60 amino acids. This domain is found predominantly in plants, and it may have been acquired by lateral gene transfer from bacterial and viral endonucleases (Magnani et al., 2004). AP2/ERF TFs have important regulatory roles in a wide variety of biological and physiological processes, such as growth and development, plant–environment interactions, and biosynthesis of metabolites, including specialized metabolites (Feng et al., 2020). For example, AP2/ERF members regulate biosynthesis of steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) in S. lycopersicum and S. tuberosum (Cardenas et al., 2016), nicotine in N. tabacum and N. benthamiana (Shoji et al., 2010; Todd et al., 2010), terpenoid indole alkaloids (TIAs) in Madagascar periwinkle (Catharanthus roseus) (Menke et al., 1999; van der Fits & Memelink, 2000), and artemisinin in trichomes of sweet wormwood (Artemisia annua) (Yu et al., 2012; Tan et al., 2015). 

Here, we report the regulation of acylsugar biosynthesis in wild tomato S. pennellii by an AP2/ERF family TF, called LEAFLESS (SpLFS; Sopen05g008450). Virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) of SpLFS led to a decrease in acylsugar production, which was accompanied by downregulation of most, but not all, acylsugar biosynthetic genes. Additionally, VIGS of SpLFS orthologs in $N .$ benthamiana also led to a reduction in acylsugar accumulation, which suggested a conserved role in acylsugar biosynthesis in solanaceous trichomes for this TF. In S. pennellii, using green fluorescent protein (GFP) as a transcriptional reporter, we observed that SpLFS is expressed in apical cells of digitate type glandular trichomes (GTs) and is barely expressed in peltate type GTs. This pattern of spatial expression is identical to that of genes encoding acylsugar biosynthetic enzymes. Furthermore, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) showed direct binding of SpLFS to promoters of genes involved in trichome specialized metabolism (acylsugar and flavonoid), which is consistent with a presumed role of this TF in regulating specialized metabolic pathways. 

# Materials and Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

Solanum pennellii Correll (LA0716) seeds were obtained from the C.M. Rick Tomato Genetics Resource Center (University of California, Davis). Seeds were surface sterilized with $1 . 2 \%$ (w/v) $\mathrm { { N a O C l } }$ for 20 min and germinated on soil. Plants were grown at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } : 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ day : night temperature under a 16-h photoperiod with $1 5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { \dot { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ s ^ { - 1 }$ photosynthetically active radiation and $7 5 \%$ humidity. Seeds of Nicotiana benthamiana Domin were germinated directly on soil, and plants were grown under the same conditions. 

# Measurement of trichome-enriched expression

Trichome-enriched expression of selected genes was measured with reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR), as 

described in Mandal et al. (2020). Surface acylsugars from stems of 10-wk-old S. pennellii (LA0716) plants were washed with ethanol for $2 \mathrm { - } 3 \ s .$ , and small pieces of stems were immediately frozen with liquid nitrogen. Stem pieces, while submerged in liquid nitrogen, were shaved gently with a scalpel, and RNA was extracted from shaved stems and stem trichomes separately using the RNAqueous Total RNA Isolation kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Houston, TX, USA). After genomic DNA digestion with the TURBO DNA-free kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific), $1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g }$ of total RNA was reverse transcribed using the SuperScript IV VILO Master Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and quantitative PCR (qPCR) was performed using SYBR Green Master Mix (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA) with QuantStudio 6 Flex System. Transcript levels were measured using the comparative $\mathrm { C _ { T } }$ $( \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C _ { T } } )$ method (normalization with the ubiquitin gene Sopen02g027600). Primers for RT-qPCR used in this study are given in Supporting Information Table S1. 

# Virus-induced gene silencing

The Solanaceae Genomics Network (SGN) VIGS tool (http://vigs.solgenomics.net/) was used to design VIGS constructs. In order to minimize the probability of off-target silencing, we ensured that VIGS constructs do not have any 19-nt (or longer) matches with other annotated genes. For S. pennellii, a 580-bp fragment (coding sequence: 257–836) was cloned into the pTRV2-LIC vector (Dong et al., 2007), in the antisense orientation, to target SpLFS (Sopen05g008450). Overnightgrown Agrobacterium tumefaciens (GV3101) cells carrying pTRV1 and pTRV2 vectors were resuspended in infiltration medium (10 mM MES, pH 5.5, 10 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone) and then mixed in a $1 : 1$ ratio with a final $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 1. Infiltration was performed at the first true leaf stage with a needleless syringe. A mixture of pTRV1 and empty pTRV2-LIC vectors was used as controls. After agro-infiltration, plants were grown for 6 wk before collecting metabolites. A phytoene desaturase gene (Sopen03g041530) was used as an independent positive control to monitor the progression of VIGS. For Nicotiana benthamiana, a 400-bp fragment was used to target both Niben101Scf08050g00012.1 and Niben101Scf02720g08001.1 (Niben101Scf08050g00012.1 coding sequence: 765–1164; antisense orientation). Plants at the four-leaf stage were infiltrated and then grown for an additional 4 wk before analysis. Primers for VIGS are listed in Table S1. 

# Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS)

In order to collect secreted acylsugars, similar-sized young leaves were dipped into extraction solvent (acetonitrile : isopropanol : water $= 3 : 3 : 2$ (v/v/v) containing $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ propyl 4-hydroxybenzoate as the internal standard) and then gently agitated for 2 min. Exudates were analyzed using Q Exactive Focus coupled with Ultimate 3000 RS LC unit (Thermo Scientific). Separation of acylsugars was performed on Acclaim 120 $( 2 . 1 ~ \times ~ 1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ ; $3 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ ) C18 column (Thermo Scientific). Exactive Series 2.8 SP1/Xcalibur 4.0 software was 

used for data acquisition and processing. Two different methods were implemented for the analysis of S. pennellii and $N .$ benthamiana exudates. For S. pennellii, a protocol described in Ji et al. (2023) was followed. For $N .$ benthamiana, extracted samples were separated by injecting $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of sample into C18 column that was housed at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . A flow rate of $5 \mathrm { { 0 0 } \ \mu l \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ with the following LC gradient was used for separation: $0 { - } 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $2 5 \%$ B; 5–20 min, $2 5 \mathrm { - } 9 5 \%$ B; $2 0 { - } 2 5 \ \operatorname* { m i n }$ , hold $9 5 \%$ B; $2 5 . 1 { - } 3 0 \ \operatorname* { m i n }$ , hold $2 5 \%$ B. The following parameters were used for MS: spray voltage $= 3 . 3 \mathrm { ~ k V }$ ; sheath gas and auxiliary gas flow rates $= 3 5$ and 8 arbitrary units, respectively; transfer capillary temperature and the auxiliary gas heater temperature $= 3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , respectively; S-Lens RF level $= 7 0 { \mathrm { ~ V } } .$ . Acylsugar relative abundances were determined by normalizing peak area of each acylsugar extracted ion chromatogram with peak area of extracted ion chromatogram of the internal standard and leaf dry weight. In cases of isomers, two or more peak areas were combined. Basic structures of acylsugars and LC-MS related information are given in Methods S1. 

# Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS)

Transesterification of acylsugars and subsequent quantification of acyl chains were performed with GC-MS, as described in Mandal et al. (2020). Twenty percent of sodium ethoxide was used for the transesterification reaction at room temperature, followed by washing with saturated sodium chloride solution. GC-MS analysis (TRACE GC Ultra with DSQ II system, Thermo Xcalibur v.3.0.63, and Qual Browser applications; Thermo Scientific) was performed using a $3 0 \mathrm { ~ m ~ } \times \mathrm { ~ 0 . 2 5 ~ m m }$ column with $0 . 2 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thickness (DB-5MS; Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA), and the following GC parameters were used: injector temperature $= 2 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ starting temperature $= 3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ held for 2 min, increased at $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ up to $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and held for 5 min; carrier gas flow $= 1 . 5 \mathrm { \ m l \ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ . A $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ solution of methyl myristate (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) was used as the internal standard. 

# RNA-Seq library preparation and sequencing

Total RNA was extracted from whole leaves of control and VIGS plants of S. pennellii using the RNAqueous Total RNA Isolation kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific), followed by treatment with the TURBO DNA-free kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) to remove genomic DNA. RNA-Seq libraries were prepared from poly(A)- selected RNA samples using the NEXTFLEX Poly(A) Beads 2.0 and Rapid Directional RNA-Seq Kit 2.0 (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) and then sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 SP v.1.5 $( 2 \times 1 5 0 )$ platform at the Texas A&M Genomics and Bioinformatics service center. Sequence cluster identification, quality prefiltering, base calling, and uncertainty assessment were done in real time using Illumina’s NCS 1.0.2 and RFV 1.0.2 software with default parameter settings. Sequencer .cbcl basecall files were demultiplexed and formatted into .fastq files using the bcl2fastq 2 2.19.0 script configureBclToFastq.pl. Overall, 30–45 million paired-end reads were generated for each sample (average 

c. 35 million reads; average Phred33 quality scores > 35; $> 9 9 . 9 7 \%$ base call accuracy). 

# Differential gene expression analysis

RNA-Seq raw reads were processed with TRIMMOMATIC v.0.38 (Bolger et al., 2014) using the following options: ILLUMINACLIP: TruSeq3-PE.fa:2 : 30 : 10 : 8, $\mathrm { \ S L I D I N G W I N D O W = \Sigma 1 : 1 0 } .$ , SLIDINGWINDOW = 4 : 20, $\mathrm { M I N I E N } = 1 2 0$ . This method ensured that all the ambiguous ‘N’ bases were removed, and c. $9 0 \%$ of the reads were retained. Processed reads were mapped to the S. pennellii genome (Bolger et al., 2014), using the following options of STAR (Dobin et al., 2013): --genomeSAindexNbases 13, --sjdbOverhang 149, --outSAMmapqUnique 255, --alignIntronMin 50, --alignIntronMax 50 000, --alignMatesGapMax 900. Read counts for annotated genes were calculated with StringTie2 (reverse RF strand) (Kovaka et al., 2019). Differential gene expression analysis was performed with EDGER (Robinson et al., 2010) using ‘Trimmed Mean of M-values’ (TMM) normalization method and robust settings. Genes with low expression levels were excluded $( < 1$ counts per million in at least five samples after length-adjustment), and genes with $> 1 . 5$ -fold expression differences and false discovery rate $< 0 . 0 5$ (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995) were identified as differentially expressed. 

# Gene co-expression network analysis

RNA-Seq gene expression data from 48 leaf samples of S. pennellii (38 from Mandal et al. (2020) and 10 from this study) were used to identify co-expressed genes using weighted correlation network analysis (Langfelder & Horvath, 2008) with the following options: softPower $= 1 4$ ; network type $=$ signed; method $=$ spearman; dissTOM $= 1$ – TOM; distM $=$ dissTOM; deepSplit $= 2$ ; minModuleSize $= 5 0$ . For each module, a module eigengene (ME) was selected as the representative of the gene expression profile based on principal component analysis. For each gene, a module membership was calculated as the Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient (SRCC) between the expression profile of that gene and the ME. Gene significance values for each gene in the ‘darkgrey’ module were calculated as the correlation (i.e. SRCC) between the expression profile of a gene and the expression profile of SpLFS. 

# Phylogenetic analysis

Two separate phylogenetic analyses were performed. For the first analysis (phylogenetic relationships among SpLFS and its orthologs), sequences were obtained as described below. Protein sequences of Ipomoea nil and I. triloba were obtained from NCBI. Protein sequences of Coffea canephora (v.1.0) and the following solanaceous species were obtained from the SGN: Solanum pennellii (v.2.0), S. lycopersicum (ITAG release 4), S. pimpinellifolium (LA1589), S. chilense, S. tuberosum (ITAG release 4), S. melongena (v.4.1), Capsicum annuum (CM334 v.1.55), Nicotiana attenuata (v.2), N. benthamiana (v.1.0.1), N. tabacum (v.4.5 edwards 2017), and Petunia axillaris (v.1.6.2). 

BLASTn was used to identify SpLFS homologs in the following seven species: S. peruvianum (SGN de novo transcriptome), N. sylvestris (SGN mRNA), N. tomentosiformis (SGN mRNA), and the four species from Moghe et al. (2017) (de novo assembled transcriptomes of S. nigrum, S. quitoense, Hyoscyamus niger, and Salpiglossis sinuata). Nucleotide sequences of these seven species were translated in six possible frames (https://web.expasy. org/translate/) to obtain protein sequences with the longest open reading frames. Careful observation revealed that Hyoscyamus niger sequence HNc61263_g1_i1 had a stretch of 8 T nucleotides (coding sequence: 852–860), which caused a truncated protein sequence (probably an assembly artifact since c61263_g1_i2 had 7 T nucleotides). Removal of 1 T restored the coding frame, which was in alignment agreement with other sequences, and it was used for further analysis. 

For the second analysis (phylogenetic relationships among SpLFS and other AP2/ERF family members), a genome-wide search for proteins with the AP2 domain was performed in both S. pennellii and S. lycopersicum. BLASTp was performed at the SGN website using the AP2 domain of SpLFS as a query and the following options: expect threshold $= 1 \mathrm { E } { - } 0 5$ ; word size $= 3$ ; matrix $=$ BLO-SUM62. We obtained 157 and 147 sequences in S. pennellii and S. lycopersicum, respectively. Two S. pennellii sequences were excluded because they had large transposon insertions. 

Multiple sequences were aligned using MAFFT (Katoh & Standley, 2013) with BLOSUM62 matrix and the following options: mafft --localpair --maxiterate 1000 (gap extend pen-$\mathrm { a l t y } = 0 . 1 2 3$ ; gap opening penalty $= 1 . 5 3$ ) (for the first phylogenetic analysis), and mafft --ep 0 --genafpair --maxiterate 1000 (gap extend penalty $= 0 . 0$ ; gap opening penalty $= 1 . 5 3 \AA$ ) (for the second phylogenetic analysis). Maximum-likelihood-based phylogenetic analyses were performed with IQ-TREE 2 (Minh et al., 2020). Different models of protein evolution were compared, and the best substitution models were selected using ModelFinder (Kalyaanamoorthy et al., 2017) based on the lowest Bayesian Information Criterion scores and corrected Akaike Information Criterion scores. The Jones–Taylor–Thornton (JTT) matrix (Jones et al., 1992) was used for the first phylogenetic analysis $( \mathrm { J T T + F + I + G 4 } )$ , whereas the Le and Gascuel (2008) model (LG) was used for the second phylogenetic analysis. 

# EMSA and promoter analysis

SpLFS sequence encoding the N-terminal 200 amino acids containing the AP2 DNA-binding domain (residues 50–107) was cloned into the pMAL-C5E vector (GenScript, Piscataway, NJ, USA). The recombinant maltose-binding protein–SpLFS fusion was produced in Rosetta 2 (DE3) competent cells (Novagen) by induction with $0 . 1 \ \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -D-1-thiogalactopyranoside at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Protein purification was performed with amylose resin (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA). DNA probes were synthesized (Integrated DNA Technologies, Coralville, IA, USA) and labeled with biotin $3 ^ { \prime }$ end labeling kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Reactions for EMSA were performed at room temperature with $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ Tris–HCl $\mathrm { ( p H ~ 7 . 5 ) }$ , $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 7 mM $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol, $1 \%$ glycerol, and $2 0 ~ \mathrm { n g } \ \mu \mathrm { l } ^ { - 1 }$ 

poly(dI-dC) as the nonspecific competitor. The reaction products were separated by $6 \%$ native polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis with $0 . 5 \times$ tris-borate-EDTA buffer and then transferred to Biodyne B nylon membranes (Thermo Fisher Scientific), followed by UV cross-linking. Probes on membranes were detected using LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

GCC box and similar sequences in gene promoters were identified using the program ‘Find Individual Motif Occurrences’ (FIMO) (Grant et al., 2011) with GCCGCC as the input motif and a maximum of one base mismatch allowed. We defined promoters as 2-kb sequences upstream of the putative transcription start sites, based on S. pennellii gene models (Bolger et al., 2014). 

# Transcriptional reporter and fluorescence microscopy

For transcriptional reporter analysis of SpLFS expression, the promoter region of SpLFS (a 1832-bp fragment upstream of the putative transcription start site) was amplified using the primers listed in Table S1 and cloned into the entry vector pENTR/D-TOPO (Thermo Fisher Scientific), followed by recombination into the destination vector pKGWFS7 (Karimi et al., 2002), using the LR Clonase II enzyme mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific). This construct was used to transform S. pennellii LA0716 plants using Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 cells, following the methods described by Gisbert et al. (1999). Fluorescence of GFP in transgenic plants was analyzed using a Nikon AXR laser scanning confocal microscope (excitation at $4 8 8 – \mathrm { n m }$ and a 500–550- nm emission filter). For the detection of Chl autofluorescence, an emission filter of $6 6 3 { - } 7 3 8 \ \mathrm { n m }$ was used. Images were captured using the NIS-Elements Software.

These six genes are Sopen01g037680, Sopen03g036630, Sopen05g008450, Sopen10g031080, Sopen 10g032000, and Sopen12g021250 (annotated as TFs belonging to TEOSINTE BRANCHED 1/CYCLOIDEA/PROLIFERATING CELL FACTOR (TCP) family, AP2/ERF family, AP2/ERF family, homeodomain-leucine zipper family, myeloblastosis (MYB) family, and AP2/ERF family, respectively). # Functional validation of SpLFS using VIGS

Its high level of trichome-enriched expression suggested a specialized function for SpLFS in S. pennellii trichomes, the site of acylsugar production and secretion. This encouraged us to target SpLFS for functional validation. Our decision to prioritize SpLFS over the remaining five candidate TF genes for functional validation was strongly supported by three additional observations. First, SpLFS exhibits the highest level of differential expression between low- and high-acylsugar-producing accessions of S. pennellii (Fig. 1b). Second, it shows the strongest repression of gene expression in response to imazapyr treatment (Fig. 1c), which downregulates acylsugar metabolic genes (Mandal et al., 2020). Third, SpLFS has the greatest difference in transcript levels between low- and high-acylsugar-producing individuals in an $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from a cross between S. lycopersicum and S. pennellii (Ji et al., 2023). 

We performed VIGS using tobacco rattle virus (TRV)-based silencing vectors (Dong et al., 2007) and analyzed leaf surface metabolites using chromatographic techniques. Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS) analysis showed that the total acylsugar amount was reduced by c. $6 5 \%$ $\mathit { \Omega } ^ { \prime } P < 0 . 0 0 0 1 ,$ ) in SpLFS-VIGS plants (Fig. 2a,b). To corroborate this finding, we performed transesterification reactions to release the acyl chains from their sugar moieties and quantified major acyl chains using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS). Compared to a group of control plants (empty TRV vectors), individual acyl chains in VIGS plants were also reduced by $5 5 \mathrm { - } 7 5 \%$ $P < 0 . 0 0 0 1$ ; Fig. 2c), which supported the involvement of SpLFS in acylsugar production. Additionally, a flavonoid compound (methylated flavonol; dimethyl kaempferol), at retention time $\therefore 1 0 . 6 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ in our chromatogram, was also significantly reduced in SpLFS-VIGS plants (Fig. 2d). 

No morphological differences were observed between control and VIGS plants (Fig. S1a), and the reduction in acylsugar amount in silenced plants was not accompanied by a statistically significant decrease in total trichome density (Fig. S1b). However, we observed an increase in non-GT density in VIGS plants (Fig. S1b–d). While SpLFS expression level and GT density showed a weak positive correlation $\begin{array} { r } { { \bf \nabla } ^ { \prime } R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 0 8 ) } \end{array}$ ), a much stronger correlation was observed between SpLFS expression level and acylsugar level $R ^ { 2 } = 0 . 7 0 1 \mathrm { \Omega }$ ) (Fig. 2e,f).

# Some non-acylsugar defense genes are upregulated in SpLFS-VIGS plants

Of the 15 upregulated genes, eight show higher expression levels in a low-acylsugar-producing accession compared to a high-acylsugar-producing accession of S. pennellii (Dataset S2). These genes include those presumably involved in plant defense and biosynthesis of other classes of specialized metabolites, such as terpenes and phenolic compounds. For example, seven upregulated genes’ putative orthologs in S. lycopersicum are involved in plant defense responses: Solyc08g008310 (Iberkleid et al., 2015), Solyc12g010020 (Pautot et al., 1993; Schimmel et al., 2018), Solyc02g067750 (Schimmel et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020), Solyc09g084490 (Schimmel et al., 2018; Padmanabhan et al., 2019), Solyc08g076980 (Schimmel et al., 2018), Solyc04g077190 (Balestrini et al., 2019), and Solyc01g067460 (Padmanabhan et al., 2022). In fact, four of these S. lycopersicum genes were reported in a study involving plant-feeding mites (Schimmel et al., 2018). These results corroborate our previous finding that expression of some non-acylsugar defense genes is negatively correlated with the amounts of acylsugar (Mandal et al., 2020). 

# Acylsugar (and flavonoid) metabolic genes are strongly co-expressed with SpLFS

Using expression profiles of S. pennellii genes in 48 RNA-Seq samples (38 from Mandal et al. (2020) and 10 from control and SpLFS-VIGS plants in this study), we performed weighted gene correlation network analysis (Langfelder & Horvath, 2008) to identify genes that are strongly co-expressed with SpLFS. We obtained 28 co-expressed modules, and SpLFS was placed in the ‘darkgrey’ module with 125 other genes (Dataset S3; Fig. S4). Module eigengene (first principal component) of the ‘darkgrey’ module and expression profile of SpLFS showed a strong correlation (Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient $= 0 . 9 3$ ; $P = 6 \mathrm { E } .$ - 21), which indicated a strong co-expression between SpLFS and other genes in this module, including known acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes (Fig. 3b). 

The orthologs of SpLFS in Nicotiana benthamiana are also involved in acylsugar biosynthesis 

In order to determine if SpLFS orthologs have a conserved function of acylsugar biosynthesis in other members of the Solanaceae, we first performed a phylogenetic analysis, and using data from Moghe et al. (2017) (gene expression data in isolated trichomes and underlying tissues from four species: S. nigrum, S. quitoense, Hyoscyamus niger, and Salpiglossis sinuata), we identified two clades (designated ‘upper’ and ‘lower’ hereafter) of SpLFS homologs that showed trichome-enriched expression (Figs 4a, S5). Next, we selected N. benthamiana for functional validation based on two facts: its phylogenetic distance from S. pennellii and its status as a well-known model for VIGS. BLAST identified three close homologs of SpLFS in N. benthamiana (Niben101Scf08050g00012, Niben101Scf02720g08001, and Niben101Scf03045g14001), with all three of them showing similar RNA-Seq coverage in expression data (Bombarely et al., 2012). Niben101Scf08050g00012 and Niben101Scf02720g08 001, which form a monophyletic ‘upper’ clade with SpLFS, share $9 5 \%$ sequence similarity at the nucleotide level with each other. We used one VIGS construct targeting both Niben101Scf08050g00012 and Niben101Scf02720g08001, and VIGS resulted in c. $1 5 \%$ reduction in acylsugar production $( 3 - 3 0 \%$ reduction in individual acylsugars; Fig. 4b). 

It is worth noting that ‘lower’ clade members showed more divergence in the AP2 domain sequence than ‘upper’ clade members (Fig. S5). Additionally, there is an apparent loss of ‘lower’ clade members in several species, especially in the tomato clade, and we observed a signature of pseudogenization during potatotomato divergence (Fig. S6). 

# Phylogenetic relationships among specialized metabolic AP2/ERFs

AP2/ERF family TFs are key regulators of biosynthesis of several classes of specialized metabolites. Examples include GLY-COALKALOID METABOLISM 9 (GAME9) for steroidal glycoalkaloids (SGAs) in S. lycopersicum (Cardenas et al., 2016), NtERF189 and NbERF1 for nicotine in N. tabacum and $N .$ benthamiana, respectively (Shoji et al., 2010; Todd et al., 2010), octadecanoid-derivative responsive Catharanthus AP2-domain (ORCA) proteins for TIAs in Catharanthus roseus (Menke et al., 1999; van der Fits & Memelink, 2000), and AaERF1- ERF2 for artemisinin in Artemisia annua (Yu et al., 2012; Tan et al., 2015). Phylogenetic analysis suggested that SpLFS belongs to a clade that is closely related to, but distinct from, the clade that contains critical regulators of other specialized metabolic pathways (Figs 4c, S7, S8). One major distinction is that SpLFS is not part of a TF gene cluster (no AP2/ERF member found within 100 genes upstream or downstream of SpLFS ), whereas many of these other specialized metabolic TFs are present in tandem AP2/ERF gene clusters on the genome. # Comparing SlLFS with SpLFS: leaf initiation or trichome specialized metabolism or both?

The ortholog of SpLFS /Sopen05g008450 in S. lycopersicum (SlLFS/Solyc05g013540) has been reported to coordinate leaf initiation at the flanks of the shoot apical meristem, with transient expression at incipient leaf primordia (expression stops after leaf initiation) (Capua & Eshed, 2017). Using publicly available datasets (Capua & Eshed (2017) for genes that are differentially expressed between wild-type plants and Sllfs mutants at the shoot apices, and Ning et al. (2015) for expression profiles of S. lycopersicum genes in stem trichomes and shaved stems), we observed that most of the genes that are downregulated in Sllfs shoot apices exhibit high levels of trichome-enriched expression, whereas most upregulated genes do not (Fig. 5a,b). In fact, more than $6 2 \%$ of the upregulated genes have much lower expression levels in stem trichomes compared to shaved stems. Additionally, we identified S. pennellii-S. lycopersicum (Sopen-Solyc) putative ortholog pairs that are differentially expressed in both this study and Capua & Eshed (2017). Of the 88 differentially expressed S. pennellii genes between control and SpLFS-VIGS plants, at least 22 genes’ putative orthologs in S. lycopersicum are also differentially expressed between wild-type and lfs mutant shoot apices (Dataset S4). Some gene pairs are missing because of missing and misannotations (for example, genes encoding MYRICETIN O-METHYLTRANSFERASEs; see Kim et al. (2014)) and also due to limitations of reciprocal BLAST in identifying orthologous pairs. Fourteen gene pairs (including known acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes) were downregulated in both conditions (SpLFS-VIGS and Sllfs mutants), and these genes exhibit noticeable trichome-enriched expression in S. lycopersicum (average 362-fold; Table 1). Together, these results indicate that both SpLFS- and SlLFS-regulated genes are preferentially expressed in trichomes and include acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes. 

# Expression of SpLFS in tip cells of type I/IV GT

Expression analysis of SpLFS in different tissues of S. pennellii indicated that SpLFS is expressed in seedlings and aerial tissues containing trichomes, and it is barely expressed in roots (Fig. 5c). To identify the expression of SpLFS in specific cell types, a c. 1.8-kb fragment containing the SpLFS promoter region was used to drive expression of GFP in transgenic S. pennellii plants. Confocal microscopy showed GFP fluorescence predominantly in tip cells of digitate GTs (type I/IV) in stably transformed S. pennellii LA0716 lines (Fig. 5d,e). Low-to-undetectable GFP signal was observed in peltate GTs (type VI/VII). This expression pattern is similar to that of genes encoding acylsugar biosynthetic enzymes (Ning et al., 2015; Fan et al., 2016, 2020; Leong et al., 2019) and is consistent with a role of SpLFS in acylsugar biosynthesis. 

# SpLFS directly binds to promoters of acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes

SpLFS (401 amino acids) has a single AP2 DNA-binding domain (approximate residue position 50–107). Multiple sequence alignment of the AP2 domain from SpLFS and its homologs in the Solanaceae revealed the presence of highly conserved DNAcontacting residues (Allen et al., 1998), indicating SpLFS as an AP2/ERF family TF (Fig. 6a). We performed EMSAs with the SpLFS DNA-binding domain fused to maltose-binding protein (Fig. S9) to test this TF activity of SpLFS. Many AP2/ERF family proteins bind to the GCC box (GCCGCC) or the dehydration response element (DRE) box (CCGAC), or both (Xie et al., 2019). We selected a 29-bp probe from the promoter of Sopen02g027670 (acyl-activating enzyme), which has both the GCC and DRE cis-regulatory elements. Our EMSA results showed that SpLFS binds to the GCC box, but not the DRE box (Fig. 6b). ORCA3, which regulates TIA biosynthesis in Catharanthus roseus (van der Fits & Memelink, 2000), has been reported to have a similar binding affinity (Shoji et al., 2013). 

Sopen02g027670 is one of the four candidate genes encoding acyl-activating enzymes for acylsugar biosynthesis (Mandal et al., 2020); a close homolog of this gene (Sopen07g023200/ Solyc07g043630) has been demonstrated to be involved in acylsugar biosynthesis (Fan et al., 2020). We extended EMSA by selecting promoter regions from two additional genes involved in acylsugar and flavonoid metabolism: ACYLSUGAR ACYLHY-DROLASE 3 (Sopen09g030520) (Schilmiller et al., 2016) and MYRICETIN O-METHYLTRANSFERASE 1 (Sopen06g034830) (Schmidt et al., 2011). Competition with unlabeled wild-type and mutant probes confirmed specific binding of SpLFS to the GCCGCC and GCGGCC sequences in these two promoters, respectively (Fig. 6c). These results encouraged us to search for GCC boxes and similar sequences in promoters of acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes. Of the 34 genes that are strongly coexpressed with SpLFS and were downregulated in VIGS plants, 25 showed such sequences in their promoter (Table S2; Fig. 6d). However, most of these sequences have a one-base mismatch with the GCCGCC sequence. We selected promoters from three additional genes (SpKAR1, SpIPMS3, and SpASAT3) involved in both phase 1 and phase 2 of acylsugar biosynthesis (Mandal et al., 2020), and EMSA showed binding of SpLFS to these gene promoters, albeit with slightly less affinity than the GCCGCC sequence for two of these promoters (Fig. 6e). Overall, these assays indicate direct binding of SpLFS to specialized metabolic gene promoters, consistent with its proposed role as a transcriptional regulator.

# Role of SpLFS in trichome specialized metabolism

Acylsugars and certain flavonoid compounds (methylated flavonols such as dimethyl kaempferol) constitute two of the most abundant classes of specialized metabolites in GTs of S. pennellii (McDowell et al., 2011; Lybrand et al., 2020). Genes involved in these two metabolic pathways appear to be strongly interconnected. For example, both acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes are noticeably downregulated in response to imazapyr treatment and show strong co-expression (Mandal et al., 2020). Additionally, they both show significant differential expression between low- and high-acylsugar-producing $\mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals derived from S. lycopersicum $\times \quad S .$ pennellii (Ji et al., 2023). Acylsugars and flavonoids are structurally distinct compounds, and their building blocks are derived from 

different primary metabolic products; however, SpLFS appears to integrate these two distinct specialized metabolic pathways in trichomes. This hypothesis is supported by three observations: (1) reductions in both acylsugar and flavonoid compounds in SpLFS-VIGS plants (Fig. 2), (2) downregulation of both acylsugar and flavonoid genes in SpLFS-VIGS plants (Fig. 3a) and Sllfs mutants (Capua & Eshed, 2017), and (3) robust co-expression of these genes with SpLFS (Fig. 3b). Additionally, the role of SpLFS in transcriptional regulation of these genes was supported by EMSA results, which showed that SpLFS directly binds to promoters of bona fide acylsugar and flavonoid metabolic genes (Fig. 6). 

It should be noted that methylated flavonols such as dimethyl kaempferol can be present in both digitate GTs (type IV) and peltate GTs (type VI) of S. pennellii (McDowell et al., 2011). Given the fact that GTs in S. pennellii are predominantly digitate GTs (type IV), we believe that the majority of dimethylkaempferol we detected from leaf surfaces was produced by type IV trichomes. In another wild tomato species, S. habrochaites, the majority of methylated flavonols are produced by digitate GTs (type I and IV; c. $9 0 \%$ of detected flavonoids), whereas peltate GTs (type VI) produce c. $1 0 \%$ of detected flavonoids (Schmidt et al., 2011). We detected expression of SpLFS in digitate GTs in S. pennellii (Fig. 5d), which suggests that either SpLFS is expressed below the limits of our detection in peltate GTs or that the biosynthesis of these flavonoid compounds is regulated by other TF(s) in peltate GTs.

# SpLFS orthologs in the Solanaceae

Trichome-preferential expression of SpLFS and its orthologs in multiple acylsugar-producing solanaceous species (Fig. 4a) suggested a conserved role in trichome acylsugar metabolism, and VIGS of SpLFS orthologs in N. benthamiana supported this role. However, the effect of VIGS on acylsugar production in $N .$ benthamiana was less pronounced than that in S. pennellii. Incomplete silencing of Niben101Scf02720g08001 due to mismatches with siRNA may be responsible for this less-dramatic VIGS effect. Alternatively, it may indicate a critical role of other TF(s) in N. benthamiana acylsugar biosynthesis or a redundant activity from Niben101Scf03045g14001 (‘lower’ clade member). Nonetheless, our results suggest that SpLFS and its solanaceous orthologs are trichome-preferentially-expressed, if not trichomespecific, members with a conserved role in acylsugar biosynthesis. 

# SpLFS vs SlLFS: specialized metabolism or developmental pathways or both?

Both SpLFS (Figs 1a, 5c) and SlLFS (Ning et al., 2015) are preferentially expressed in trichomes of developed leaves and stems, and multiple acylsugar metabolic genes are downregulated in both SpLFS-VIGS and Sllfs mutants (Table 1). The role of SlLFS in acylsugar biosynthesis in S. lycopersicum remains to be confirmed (this cannot be tested in the Sllfs mutant because of its severe developmental deformities (Capua & Eshed, 2017)). However, based on our phylogenetic analysis and VIGS results in N. benthamiana (Fig. 4a,b), it is possible that SpLFS orthologs, including SlLFS, are involved in acylsugar biosynthesis. Taken together, these results indicate a remarkable spatiotemporal transition in expression and function of this TF – from leaf initiation at the shoot apical meristem (with transient expression at incipient leaf primordia) to trichome specialized metabolism. 

Recently, the role of SlLFS in GT development was reported in S. lycopersicum (Wu et al., 2023). In both S. pennellii (VIGS of SpLFS; Fig. S1b–d) and S. lycopersicum (knockout of SlLFS; Wu et al., 2023), disruption of normal levels of LFS activity leads to an increase in non-GT density, which suggests an overlapping function of LFS in GT development in these two species. However, LFS exhibits a regulatory divergence in its role in GT development between S. lycopersicum and S. pennellii. SlLFS is highly expressed in peltate GTs and accordingly, promotes the development of peltate GTs (types VI and VII) in S. lycopersicum (Wu et al., 2023). On the other hand, SpLFS is expressed predominantly in tip cells of digitate GTs (type I/IV) (Fig. $\mathrm { 5 d , e ) }$ ) and appears to be associated with the development of digitate GTs (Fig. S1b–d), the site of acylsugar biosynthesis. Importantly, this differential expression of LFS in digitate GTs between S. lycopersicum and S. pennellii (low levels for SlLFS and high levels for SpLFS ) is consistent with the difference in acylsugar accumulating capabilities between these two species: c. $1 \%$ and $2 0 \%$ of leaf dry weight in respective species (Fobes et al., 1985). 

Using intraspecific populations from two accessions of S. pennellii (LA0716 and LA1912), Blauth et al. (1998) identified 

quantitative trait loci associated with type IV trichome density and acylsugar levels. Those authors identified one locus for which the LA0716 alleles increased acylsugar levels with reduced trichome density and three additional loci for which trichome density changed with no effects on acylsugar levels. Similarly, analysis of type I/IV trichome density and acylsugar levels in S. galapagense, S. lycopersicum, and S. lycopersicum lines containing introgressed trichome developmental regions from the S. galapagense genome showed differential magnitudes in these two parameters; for example, compared to S. lycopersicum, the introgressed genotype had more than a 200-fold increase in trichome density, but only an c. eightfold increase in total acylsugar level (Vendemiatti et al., 2022). These results and additional reports (Leckie et al., 2012; Smeda et al., 2016) indicate that the type IV trichome development and acylsugar biosynthesis are distinct pathways. In order to differentiate the role of LFS in GT development from its role in acylsugar biosynthesis, we performed correlation analyses. Expression levels of SpLFS showed a stronger correlation with acylsugar levels than with GT (type I/IV) density (Fig. 2e,f). This indicates that while LFS is associated with GT development, it has a critical role in regulating acylsugar levels. Additional observations also support this conclusion. For example, VIGS of SpLFS resulted in c. $6 5 \%$ reduction in acylsugar levels (Fig. 2a) and c. $6 1 \%$ reduction in SpLFS transcript levels (Fig. 3a), the magnitudes of which are different than that of GT density reduction (c. $1 0 \%$ ; Fig. S1b). Similarly, the high- and low-acylsugar-producing S. pennellii accessions LA0716 and LA1912, respectively, show c. twofold difference in GT (type IV) density (Blauth et al., 1998), but c. 20-fold difference in acylsugar levels (Blauth et al., 1998; Mandal et al., 2020), which is of similar magnitude in SpLFS expression level difference between these two accessions (c. 21-fold) (Mandal et al., 2020). These results suggest that the fates of GT development in tomato species are critically determined by proteins other than LFS, such as WOOLLY, TRICHOME REPRESSOR 1, and SlWOX3b/MX1 (Wu et al., 2023), whereas acylsugar levels are predominantly regulated by LFS. Strong co-expression of acylsugar biosynthetic genes with LFS (Fig. 3b) is consistent with this idea. The role of LFS in regulating acylsugar biosynthetic gene expression is also supported by the RNA-Seq experiment of Capua & Eshed (2017) with shoot apical meristems. Those authors compared transcriptomes of wild-type and Sllfs mutants, and their RNA-Seq results also reveal downregulation of several acylsugar biosynthetic genes at the shoot apex (Table 1), which is devoid of trichomes and represents an independent site for transcriptional regulation of acylsugar genes by LFS. Finally, EMSAs confirmed that SpLFS can bind directly to the promoters of acylsugar metabolic genes (Fig. 6). 

The difference in magnitudes for acylsugar reduction and GT reduction (Figs 2a, S1b) indicated that the reduction in acylsugar levels in SpLFS-VIGS plants was not solely due to reduced levels of GTs. This is supported by our RNA-Seq results, which showed that not all acylsugar metabolic genes were downregulated in SpLFS-VIGS plants. For example, acylsugar metabolic genes that are expressed in tip cells of GTs or show trichome-enriched expression (presumably type I/IV), such as ACYLSUCROSE 

FRUCTOFURANOSIDASE 1 (Leong et al., 2019) and ACYLSU-GAR ACYLHYDROLASE 1 (Schilmiller et al., 2016), did not show any statistically significant difference in expression levels between control and SpLFS-VIGS plants (Fig. 3a). These two genes showed only c. $1 6 \%$ and $1 7 \%$ reduction in transcript levels, respectively (false discovery rate $> 0 . 0 5$ ) (Dataset S1), possibly due to the c. $1 0 \%$ reduction in GT density in VIGS plants (Fig. S1b), whereas other acylsugar metabolic genes showed much greater and varying degrees of downregulation in silenced plants. If the reduction in acylsugar levels was the sole consequence of type I/IV trichome reduction, then we would have expected all the type I/IV-expressed genes to show similar magnitudes of expression level differences between control and VIGS plants. Taken together, VIGS of LFS demonstrates a critical role of this TF in acylsugar regulation and indicates that GT (type I/IV) development and acylsugar biosynthesis are related but distinct processes, which is consistent with previous findings (Blauth et al., 1998; Smeda et al., 2016; Vendemiatti et al., 2022). Overall, our work reports LFS as a positive regulator of acylsugar biosynthesis and opens the possibility of metabolic engineering of acylsugar production in solanaceous crops.
