Phytophthora sojae-induced root rot poses a major threat to soybean production. While the molecular mechanisms underlying soybean–P. sojae interactions have been extensively studied, their biochemical basis remains largely unexplored. Previous research has identified key metabolic modules involved in pathogen defense, but structural diversity has largely been constrained by studies on single soybean accessions. Here, we broadened the chemical search space to a diverse soybean germplasm collection using high-throughput metabolomics as a powerful tool for comprehensive metabolic profiling. Chemical classes of lipids and phenylpropanoids again retrieved the most pronounced responses upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection in general. A two-layer analytical strategy further finely resolved metabolites into pathogenesis-, resistance-, and tolerance-type accumulation patterns, leading to the identification of cinnamaldehyde and coumestrol as potent defense metabolites. Bioassays validated cinnamaldehyde directly and strongly inhibited cyst germination and mycelial growth, and coumestrol, a benzofuran-type metabolite, exhibited broad-spectrum activity against spore germination as an identified phytoalexin. Multiomics analyses nailed down the candidate of coumestrol biosynthesis genes, and genetically overexpression of regulatory genes (Dir2a/4a/4b) in hairy root systems increased coumestrol accumulation thus positively correlating with improved host resistance. Interestingly, tolerance-type compounds may serve distinct ecological roles, as exemplified by daidzein, which, despite being classified as a tolerance-type metabolite, recruits more zoospores facilitating secondary infection in fact. This study highlights a systematic approach for population-level investigations and emphasizes the necessity of integrating bioinformatics with experimental validation to accurately predict metabolite or gene ecological functions.

Phytophthora root rot, caused by Phytophthora sojae , is a devastating disease that significantly threatens global soybean production, resulting in annual economic losses of billions of dollars ( 1 ). While substantial progress has been made in elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying soybean–P. sojae interactions, the biochemical basis of this interaction remains largely unexplored. Though, it is commonly acknowledged that metabolites play a crucial role in this intricate biochemical interplay ( 2 , 3 ). 

Pathogens deploy virulence factors, including toxic metabolites, to thwart plant defenses and enhance their pathogenicity, whereas plant hosts counteract these attacks by synthesizing and utilizing specialized defense metabolites, consisting of the production of primary metabolites, plant hormones, and secondary metabolites ( 4 – 6 ). Plant primary metabolites, such as phytohormones, including salicylic acid (SA) and methyl jasmonate (MeJA), play crucial roles in plant defense by mediating systemic acquired resistance and induced systemic resistance during pathogen attack ( 7 , 8 ). Recently, strigolactones (SLs) have also been identified as hormones involved in Arabidopsis resistance to pathogenic bacterium Rhodococcus fascians ( 9 ). Notably, hormone signaling pathways do not function in isolation but engage in extensive crosstalk, enabling plants to fine-tune their immune responses ( 10 ). 

Unlike primary metabolites, plant secondary metabolites do not directly participate in growth and development but are specialized small molecules evolved for stress resistance and often exhibit species-specificity ( 11 ). Based on their biosynthesis and accumulation patterns, these metabolites are categorized into phytoalexin and phytoanticipin ( 12 , 13 ). # Significance

Natural populations serve as a fertile reservoir of chemical diversity, and metabolomics provides a powerful tool for high-throughput functional compound discovery. By systematically parsing the metabolomes of 44 soybean cultivars, not only were chemical classes of lipids and phenylpropanoids locked as major metabolic responses upon Phytophthora sojae infection, but also two key resistance metabolites, cinnamaldehyde and coumestrol, were characterized, of which coumestrol, an identified benzofuran-type phytoalexin, presented broad-spectrum activity against spore germination, with genetic bioassay validation suggesting its potential applicability in crop breeding. Our study establishes a systematic framework for uncovering bioactive metabolites and their genetic regulation, offering insights into plant defense mechanisms and guiding metabolic-based resistance strategies in application.

With well-developed analytical techniques in metabolomics as a powerful high-throughput tool for rapidly deciphering the metabolites composition of biological samples, the biochemical mechanisms underlying plant–pathogen interactions are gradually being uncovered, which is accompanied by several novel plant metabolites successfully identified to against pathogenic infections ( 16 – 18 ). 

Glyceollins, a subclass of pterocarpan phytoalexins, represent the most extensively characterized disease-associated metabolites in soybean. These compounds enhance soybean resistance to a range of pathogens, including $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae , Fusarium solani f. sp. glycines , and Sclerotinia sclerotiorum , primarily through the inhibition of mycelial growth ( 19 , 20 ). The application of large-scale metabolomics has facilitated the discovery of additional candidate defense metabolites in soybean–pathogen interactions. For example, several coumarins, such as 7-methoxycoumarin, scopoletin, xanthyletin, and osthole, have been proposed as antifungal agents targeting Phakopsora pachyrhizi ( 21 ). Similarly, terpenoid metabolites including simvastatin, picrocrocin, and trans-zeatin riboside have been implicated in resistance against Colletotrichum truncatum ( 22 ). In the context of $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, compounds such as levoglucosan, 4-hydroxybenzoic acid, daidzein, hypoxanthine, octanal, and structural components like cutin, suberin, wax, paromomycin, and N- α-acetyl-L-ornithine have been associated with host resistance ( 23 , 24 ). Our prior work also identified several bioactive metabolites conferring disease resistance, such as genistein, which suppresses Fusarium graminearum , and daidzein and formononetin, which significantly inhibit Phomopsis longicolla ( 25 ). However, the majority of these findings derive from studies limited to pairwise comparisons between a single resistant and a single susceptible cultivar. Consequently, the broader biological functions and mechanistic roles of these putative defense metabolites remain largely uncharacterized and need further investigation. 

In this study, we aimed to elucidate metabolic differences between resistant and susceptible soybean genotypes in response to $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection and identify key disease-resistant compounds along with their biosynthetic regulatory genes. To achieve this, we expand the search space for a chemical structural diversity at most to a broader soybean germplasm population panel, including 20 resistant and 24 susceptible accessions. Our findings revealed that phenylpropanoids and lipids are the primary sources of metabolic divergence between these groups upon infection after a panoply of metabolomics analysis. Furthermore, we identified cinnamaldehyde and coumestrol as potent compounds that effectively inhibit $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection using two complementary identification strategies. Additionally, we characterized the regulatory genes involved in coumestrol biosynthesis, which was positively correlated with disease resistance. Together, these findings highlight that identifying active resistance metabolites and elucidating their biosynthetic regulations could not only provide lead structures for green pesticide design but also enable genetic improvement of soybean resistance by manipulating key metabolic pathways. By complementing traditional resistance breeding strategies based on Rps genes, metabolite-based strategies offer novel avenues for sustainable disease control in soybean production. 

# Results

Metabolomic Profiling Reveals Chemical Composition and Holistic Differences Among the Metabolomes of 44 Soybean Cultivars. Etiolated seedlings of 44 soybean cultivars, comprising both commonly cultivated varieties in China and in-house 

collected lines with validated variation in resistance and susceptibility against pathogens, were inoculated with $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae (P6497) at the hypocotyls. Samples were collected post $4 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ inoculation for biomass quantification and metabolic analysis. The quantification of biomass of P. sojae revealed that 20 cultivars exhibited consistent resistance such as P03 (hereafter referred to as R), while the remaining 24 cultivars displayed varying degrees of susceptibility (S), including P04 (Fig. 1A and Dataset S1). 

We first measured phytohormones including indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), abscisic acid (ABA), salicylic acid (SA), jasmonic acid (JA), and its conjugates with amino acids (e.g. , isoleucine and valine). The classical defense hormones (SA, JA, and derivates) generally induced with $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae challenge in S cultivars except JA-Ile. ABA levels declined more sharply relative to that in R ones (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A ). Interestingly, JA, JA-Val, and IAA exhibited a remarkable increase upon pathogen infection in S cultivars, but not in R cultivars at all (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A ), Pearson correlation unveiled a coherent association between the abundance of these three hormones along with JA-Ile and P. sojae biomass (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B ), collectively suggesting JA and JA-Val can serve as hormonal biomarker in distinguishing S and R populations, but also as an indicator reflecting the extent of infection for susceptible accessions. Strikingly, SA, one of the most effective weapons in host defense against pathogens, was also the most abundant hormone detected. However, there was no significant difference in SA response levels between the S and R groups upon pathogen challenge. 

We then employed tandem UPLC-ESI-TOF-MS/MS for untargeted metabolomics ( 25 , 26 ). Recorded spectra were merged by combining repeated measurements from the same sample ( Fig. 1B ). Following computational filtering steps to remove redundant features such as isotopic peaks and annotated adducts resulted in a concatenated data matrix consisting of 722 MS/MS spectra at the compound level across the entire dataset ( Fig. 1B and Datasets S2 and S4 ). To depict a holistic picture of the whole metabolic dataset, we statistically analyzed the distribution of detected metabolites based on retention time (RT), mass-to-charge ratio $\left( m / z \right)$ , and peak intensity (height) ( Fig. 1C ). Within the RT range of 0.5 to $2 4 . 5 \mathrm { m i n }$ , most metabolites eluted before 15 min. Based on known Fabaceae compounds and our liquid chromatography (LC) gradient, amino and organic acids eluted around 5 min, triterpenoids near 12 min, and flavonoids in between. Peaks after 15 min were likely long-chain fatty acids ( Fig. 1C ). In terms of mass distribution, most metabolites fell within the m/z range of 200 to 600, though some compounds exhibited m/z values around 950 ( Fig. 1C ), possibly representing soyasaponins and their derivatives. The right-skewed normal distribution of peak intensities suggests that the majority of detected compounds were present at relatively low concentrations ( Fig. 1C ). To interpret metabolic differences among all samples, we applied $t$ -distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) ( 27 ), a nonlinear dimensionality reduction algorithm, to high-dimensional metabolomics datasets $( 7 2 2 ~ \mathrm { M S / M \bar { S } } \times 4 4 $ cultivars $\times ~ 2$ treatments) (Materials and Methods ). The resulting agglomerating pattern revealed that in the control group, the metabolomes of S and R cultivars were interwoven ( Fig. $1 D$ ), suggesting a baseline metabolic consistency across cultivars. Statistical analysis via PERMANOVA (Bray– Curtis distance) ( 28 ) confirmed no significant differences at the constitutive level (F-value $= \ 0 . 9 6 8$ ) ( Fig. $1 D )$ ). However, they exhibited clear metabolic segregation post $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection $F _ { \cdot }$ -value $= 3 2 . 0 2 5$ ), and the metabolic shift between inoculated (T) and control (C) samples was more pronounced in the S group $( F _ { \ l }$ -value $= 5 9 . 8 2 5$ ) than that in the R group $( F _ { \ l }$ -value $= 1 6 . 9 8 8 ^ { \cdot }$ ). To achieve a comprehensive understanding of the structural information of the 722 detected MS/MS spectra (Dataset S4 ), a systematic annotation strategy was implemented to search for or predict chemical structures ( 25 , 29 – 33 )–As a result, structural searching and prediction identified 175 and 279 known metabolites, respectively. The annotated metabolites were subsequently classified into nine compound categories: Alkaloids and derivatives; Benzenoids; Lipids and lipid−like molecules; Nucleosides, 

nucleotides, and analogues; Organic acids and derivatives; Organic nitrogen compounds; Organic oxygen compounds; Organoheterocyclic compounds; Phenylpropanoids and polyketides (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A ). Additionally, MS/MS molecular networking was employed to circumvent the limitations of spectral databases by analyzing within-dataset MS/MS similarities enabling the formulation of structural hypotheses for unknown metabolites (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B ). To further investigate the metabolic changes in general induced by $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, we quantified both the total abundance and the number of metabolites within each chemical class. The results stated a significant overall decline in total abundance after pathogenic inoculation in both S and R 

cultivars, whereas the number of detected metabolites nearly did not alter in R cultivars, but in S exhibiting a significant increase (mainly Phenylpropanoids and polyketides, Lipids and lipid−like molecules). It was inferred that in R cultivars, metabolic composition was mainly remodeled, but without substantial structural modifications. In contrast, S cultivars exhibited more profound metabolic restructuring, not only reconfiguring biochemical composition but also incorporating a set of structurally innovated compounds into the host metabolism (SI Appendix, Fig. S2C and Dataset S2 ). This divergence highlights the fundamental differentiation of metabolic reprogramming strategies employed by S and R cultivars in response to $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection. 

To statistically dissect metabolomic differences upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, we applied two indices: the Relative Distance Plasticity Index (RDPI) to assess inducibility ( 33 , 34 ) and the Specificity Index (Si ) to evaluate metabolic specialization ( 32 , 33 ). Higher RDPI values were predominantly associated with S cultivars, while R cultivars exhibited lower RDPI, indicating less metabolic reprogramming. When RDPI exceeded 0.6, S cultivars showed a marked increase in highly inducible metabolites (423 vs. 176 in R) (SI Appendix, Fig. S 3 A and $B$ ), indicating that the extent of metabolic induction does not necessarily correlate with effective resistance. Structural categorization revealed that most of these metabolites belonged to “phenylpropanoids and polyketides” and “lipids and lipid-like molecules” (SI Appendix, Fig. S 3C ). In parallel, the $S i$ index identified 184 highly specific metabolites $( S i >$ 2), with the same two compound classes ranking highest, further underscoring their functional relevance (SI Appendix, Fig. S 3 D and $E$ ). Together, RDPI and $S i$ analyses point to “phenylpropanoids–polyketides” and “lipids and lipid-like molecules” as core metabolic players in soybean response to $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae . 

Divergent Metabolic Reprogramming of Compound Classes in Response to P. sojae Infection: Lipids and Lipid-Like Molecules, Phenylpropanoids, and Polyketides. By combining the enrichment pattern between S and R cultivars and implications of the tolerance theory and recent advances in phytopathology (14, 15), we categorized metabolites within these two compound classes into three types. Metabolites that are highly induced by pathogen infection in both R and S cultivars are classified as tolerance-type compounds theoretically functioning in reducing the damage that pathogen causes. In contrast, metabolites that exhibit strong inducibility exclusively in S cultivars are designated as pathogenesistype compounds, as they may facilitate infection and thus exacerbate host damage. Additionally, metabolites that are mainly enriched in R cultivars (or a subset thereof ) regardless of infection are defined as resistance-type compounds (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S3F). These metabolites are hypothesized to directly suppress pathogen performance and therefore enhance host fitness in pathogen-rich environments. 

The identified lipid compounds were further categorized into six subclasses with fatty acyls and prenol lipids emerging as the most predominant groups represented by green and yellow nodes in the lipid network (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A ), in which prenol lipids accounted for more than $6 0 \%$ of the detected lipid species. In soybeans, prenol lipids predominantly comprise triterpenoid compounds, namely, soyasaponins and their derivatives. These metabolites exhibited an overall decline following $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, a trend consistent with previous findings reported by Qiu et al. ( 25 ). Notably, a subset of exceptions showed significant accumulation exclusively in certain S cultivars (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 B and C ). Structural analysis elucidated the presence of 4 to 5 hydroxyl moieties [scan01472 (m/z 991.52 @ 7.81 min); 

scan01480 (m/z 1007.51 @ 8.99 min); scan01481 $( m / z 1 0 0 7 . 5 @$ $7 . 9 7 ~ \mathrm { m i n } )$ )], a characteristic rarely documented in soyasaponins yet. We proposed that enzymes secreted by $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae during infection catalyze hydroxylation of host-derived triterpenoids, introducing two additional hydroxyl groups (exact positions undetermined via HRMS) through a series of unknown redox reactions. These modifications, occurring in addition to hydroxylation at $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 1 }$ , $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 2 }$ , or $\mathrm { C } _ { 2 4 }$ in common soyasaponins, likely lead to the formation of yet-unidentified triterpenoid derivatives with 4 to 5 hydroxyl moieties upon pathogen challenge (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 B and C ). Structural inference indicated that most fatty acyls were polyunsaturated fatty acids and highly induced exclusively in S cultivars suggesting their classification as pathogenesis-type metabolites. This observation supports the hypothesis that these lipids may originate from $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae metabolism itself. While lipids have been implicated in virulence and play critical roles in membrane remodeling during infection ( 35 – 37 ), direct evidence for their pathogenic function in this study remains inconclusive and further experimental validation is needed (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). 

Next, we shifted our research focus to the phenylpropanoids and polyketides class ( Fig. 2A ). This metabolic cluster exhibited a markedly different response in both S and R cultivars upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, whereas lipid-related changes were predominantly confined to S cultivars. A detailed structural analysis of MS/MS spectra within this class discovered four major ramifications of isoflavonoidsdaidzein, genistein, formononetin, and glycitein along with their respective precursors and derivatives ( Fig. 2A ). Notably, glyceollin I (scan00481, m/z 339.12 @ 13.89 min), one of the most well-characterized soybeans phytoalexins, along with its glycosylated form (scan00961, m/z 501.17 $\textcircled{2}$ 11.3 min), were successfully identified as tolerance-type compounds ( Fig. 2B ). The aglycone forms of daidzein, genistein, formononetin, and glycitein displayed accumulation patterns like glyceollin I; however, their derivatives exhibited diverged trends across S and R cultivars. For instance, daidzein glycosides [(hexose), daidzin, scan00696 ] and daidzein malonylglycosides (scan00966 ), exhibited increases mainly in S cultivars upon pathogen challenge. In contrast, formononetin glycosides [(hexose), ononin, scan00735 ] and formononetin malonylglycosides (scan00993 ) were highly enriched exclusively in R cultivars in response to $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae inoculation, suggesting their potential role as resistance-type compounds ( Fig. $2 B$ and Dataset S2 ). 

The Tolerance-Type Metabolite Daidzein, Highly Induced by P. sojae, Exacerbates Pathogenic Infection by Attracting Zoospore Cohorts Rather than Acting as a Repellent. Given the consistent increases upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae inoculation of these four main isoflavonoid aglycones as tolerance-type metabolites, we explored their potential contribution in pathogenic resistance. Since cysts germination and mycelial growth are critical steps for a $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection success, we examined whether these metabolites exert inhibitory effects. However, at $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , none of the four compounds significantly suppressed cyst germination. Similarly, even at $1 6 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , they failed to restrain mycelial growth. And pathogenicity assays also cannot tell the differences between isoflavonoid-supplemented and control groups by inoculating zoospores onto hypocotyls of etiolated seedlings (SI  Appendix, Fig. $S 5 \ A { - } C )$ ). These findings demonstrate that tested metabolites do not display a direct antagonistic effect on $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae pathogenicity. 

These observations raise a critical question: Could the elevated levels of these compounds, rather than conferring resistance, actually facilitate disease progression following initial inoculation? Genetically Determined Resistance-Type Metabolites, Such as Cinnamaldehyde, Directly Suppress P. sojae Growth and Development to Dampen Pathogenicity. Apart from these isoflavonoids, two compounds with distinct accumulation patterns caught our attention: scan00062 $( m / z 1 3 3 . 0 6 @ 4 . 8 \mathrm { m i n } )$ identified as cinnamaldehyde, and scan00993 $( m / z \ 5 1 7 . 1 3 \ \textcircled { \omega }$ $1 0 . 1 ~ \mathrm { \ m i n }$ ) identified as formononetin malonylglycoside (not commercially available yet), exhibiting predominant enrichment in certain R cultivars with greater $S i$ scores (Fig. 2B). Therefore, cinnamaldehyde was selected for further investigation. 

A mycelial growth inhibition assay was performed using a concentration gradient ranging from 0.3 to $\bar { 8 0 } \mu \mathrm { M }$ . The results displayed a clear inhibitory effect starting at $2 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ , with an estimated $\mathrm { E C } _ { 5 0 }$ for mycelial growth suppression of approximately $1 1 . 0 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ( Fig. 3A ). Furthermore, we assessed the inhibitory effects of cinnamaldehyde (5 to $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ ) on mycelial growth in 

three other devastating soybean pathogens: Pythium ultimum (Pu) , F. graminearum $( F g )$ , and $P .$ longicolla (Pl) . Interestingly, cinnamaldehyde consistently suppressed mycelial growth across all three pathogens (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A ), although with slight variations in inhibitory activity, yielding an estimated $\mathrm { E C } _ { 5 0 }$ of approximately $1 5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ for these three pathogens (SI Appendix, Fig. S6B ). In addition, a cyst germination assay revealed that $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae cysts at $8 \mu \mathrm { M }$ failed to germinate completely while germ tube elongation was significantly impaired at concentrations as low as $2 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ( Fig. 3C ). Quantitative analysis further confirmed that the ratio of normally germinated cysts decreased to $1 0 \%$ at $4 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ( Fig. $3 D \ r$ ), with an $\mathrm { \bar { E } C _ { 5 0 } }$ for cyst germination inhibition of approximately $3 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ( Fig. 3E ). To this end, a pathogenicity assay was conducted, and the results demonstrated that cinnamaldehyde effectively abolished $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection at $1 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ( Fig. 3F ). These findings suggest that resistance-type metabolites identified through a comparative metabolomics approach hold significant potential for future applications. 

Metabolic Uniqueness Shapes the Resistance Phenotype of RPS1D Cultivar: Biochemical Characterization Unveils Coumestrol, a Benzofuran-Type Phytoalexin, with Broad-Spectrum Inhibitory Effects on Spore Germination to Suppress Pathogenic Infection. In the above research work, we were aiming at summarizing metabolic patterns and identifying resistance related soybean metabolites at the compound level. Building on this foundation, we extended our analysis from individual metabolite to a metabolome-wide perspective. A set of indices that allow for the quantification of metabolome diversity (Hj index) and specialization (δj index) was computed (32, 33). When these two indices were mapped onto a 2D space, RPS1D, one of the R cultivars, exhibited the lowest value in the control group and ranked as the fourth lowest along the X-axis (diversity) in control and $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae-inoculated groups respectively (Fig. 4A). Furthermore, the mean RDPI in RPS1D was lower relative to the rest of cultivars (Fig. 4B). In combination with the findings presented in Fig. 1D, t-SNE dimensional reduction indicated that RPS1D was the sole exception, exhibiting no significant metabolic changes between the control and $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae-inoculated groups. We compared the phenotypes between Williams 82 (widely used as the reference genome in soybean research) and RPS1D focusing on growth morphology, resistance to $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae in both etiolated seedlings and adult roots, and endogenous CMS levels (SI Appendix, Fig. $S ^ { 9 } A { - } D )$ . To further characterize the CMS biosynthesis candidate genes, we employed two complementary strategies: analyzing CMS-correlated transcripts across different tissues and assessing $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae inoculationinduced transcripts. Intriguingly, coumestrol, identified as a resistant compound, was barely detected in previous metabolomics datasets derived from the hypocotyl inoculation experiment. To assess natural variation in its accumulation, we quantified coumestrol content in roots of 44 soybean cultivars collected from field, identifying RPS1D as the highest accumulator (SI Appendix, Fig. S10A ), to some extent this finding reaffirms the uniqueness of the RPS1D cultivar. We next assessed coumestrol accumulation pattern among a compendium of 94 different tissues throughout the entire lifespan in Williams 82. As shown in SI Appendix, Fig. S10B , coumestrol was highly enriched in roots, nodules, and mature leaves, displaying a life stage-dependent accumulation pattern: Mature tissues contain a higher CMS concentration than young tissues. We further investigated whether CMS is inducible upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection in roots and found that, similar to daidzein, glyceollin I, formononetin, and glycitein, CMS was significantly upregulated in Williams 82 (SI Appendix, Fig. S10C ), reflecting the metabolic response previously observed in hypocotyls ( Fig. 2B ). Taking advantage of the soybean hairy root transformation system, which enables efficient genetic manipulation for functional tests of uncharacterized genes, we redirected our focus from hypocotyls to roots to further elucidate the regulatory mechanisms underlying coumestrol biosynthesis. This shift is also ecologically relevant, as $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae not only infects the basal stem but more frequently targets the roots, causing root rot disease. 

One of the key structural characteristics of coumestrol is the benzofuran ring system fused to a chromone (benzopyran-4-one) core, a feature commonly shared by many pterocarpan compounds in leguminous plants, such as glyceollins in soybean, medicarpin in alfalfa, and pisatin in pea ( 40 – 43 ). The formation of the benzofuran ring system in coumestans involves the generation of a monoquinone methide intermediate, which subsequently undergoes intramolecular cyclization/rearomatization. Therefore, we hypothesize that the formation of coumestrol must undergo the benzofuran ringing step resemblance to glyceollins or medicarpin biosynthesis, which is catalyzed by pterocarpan synthase1 (PTS1) as reported ( 44 – 47 ) ( Fig. 6A and SI Appendix, Fig. S10D ). PTS1 belongs to the dirigent protein family (Dir), which is named after its dirigent-like domains. Based on the published sequences of PTS1 genes in leguminous plants ( 45 ), we verified 52 dirigent protein family members in Williams 82 through sequence homology analysis and renowned GePTS (BAX03553) and GmPTS1 (Glyma.03G147700) were distributed in Clade I ( Fig. 6B ). We then conducted a quantitative correlation analysis between coumestrol content in different tissues and transcript abundance in corresponding samples and generated 21 candidate genes ( Fig. 6C and Dataset S3 ). As coumestrol was metabolically inducible upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection, theoretically CMS-related biosynthesis genes should be inducible as well. We analyzed publicly available RNA-seq data from GEO (PRJNA210431) and resulted in 13 candidate genes exhibiting a consistent pathogen-induced expression pattern ( Fig. 6D ). By intersecting these three datasets, we 

narrowed down the selection to 6 candidate genes including GmPTS1 (Glyma.03G147700) for further experimental validation ( Fig. 6E ). 

To determine whether these candidate genes (PTS1 , Dir2a/2b , Dir4a/4b, and Dir6 ) are involved in coumestrol (CMS) biosynthesis, transient overexpression (OE) experiments were conducted in hairy roots of Williams 82 after observing their similar responses upon $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae inoculation. Successful overexpression was confirmed by GFP fluorescence and qRT-PCR analysis exhibiting at least a 1.8-fold increase in transcript abundance compared to empty vector (EV) ( Fig. 6F ). Next, we quantified CMS content in the transformed hairy roots and found that CMS concentrations were significantly elevated after the overexpression of Dir2a and Dir4a/4b separately ( Fig. $6 G$ , suggesting that these three genes collectively play a role in CMS biosynthesis in a functionally redundant manner. We next compared the sequences of the Dir2a and $\mathrm { D i r 4 a } / 4 \mathrm { b }$ genes between the two lines by cloning them from Williams 82 and RPS1D, respectively. The results revealed that the deduced amino acid sequences of the corresponding genes were completely identical indicating that there is no sequence variation at the protein level between the two cultivars (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 9 E$ and Dataset S5 ). 

Since Williams 82 is a $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae-resistant accession, we wonder whether these three candidates (Dir2a and Dir4a/4b ) cloned from Williams 82 impart pathogenic resistance on a susceptible cultivar by enhancing CMS accumulation, we selected one of the S cultivars (P20) to overexpress (OE) Dir2a and Dir4a/4b respectively ( Fig. 7A ). As a result, the content of CMS in OE roots was significantly higher than that in EV though the degree of increase varied. Meanwhile, zoospores of $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae were inoculated on the transformed hairy root, quantification results of biomass of $\mathit { \Pi } _ { \mathit { I } , \mathit { \Pi } } ^ { p }$ sojae indicated significant enhancement of resistance by exhibiting about $7 0 \%$ reduction in infection ( Fig. 7B ). The findings suggest that Dir2a and Dir4a/4b have strong potential for application in improving disease resistance by boosting CMS accumulation to counteract pathogenic invasion. However, in the in vitro assay mainly containing purified dirigent protein, Williams 82 total protein and crude extract as substrate, we did not observe a notable increase in CMS accumulation (SI Appendix, Fig. S11 ). Taken together, a systematic metabolomics approach was employed using a soybean population with two distinct pathogen-resistance phenotypes. Through a dual-level analytical strategy, we validated the metabolic reconfiguration at the individual compound level and identified specific host plants with characteristic metabolomic profiles within the population facilitating conical purification of bioactive compounds. Ultimately, the overarching goal was to elucidate the biological functions of the finally identified metabolites. For instance, daidzein was characterized as a pathogenicityrelated factor promoting secondary infection, whereas transcinnamaldehyde, glyceollin I, and coumestrol functioned as resistanceassociated metabolites directly or indirectly contributing to pathogen defense ( Fig. 7C ). The concept we propose here of using natural variation within soybean populations for metabolic pattern recognition at two layers can be extended to all kinds of sample analysis (e.g. different host plants with various stresses), resulting in identification of functional metabolites for potential application in future.

SA showed a slight but significant induction in both R and S cultivars postinoculation, but without significant difference between two groups. It was unexpected that SA levels did not differ between R and S cultivars since its crucial role in pathogen resistance ( 51 ), suggesting neither basal nor induced levels of SA cannot directly determine the disease resistance of a given soybean cultivar. However, we acknowledge that the optimal time window for SA measurement may have been missed. SA as reported primarily associated with defense against biotrophic and hemi-biotrophic pathogens, especially in the biotrophic stage ( 51 ). Our sampling time point (48 h postinoculation) was chosen to clearly distinguish R and S cultivars base on infectious phenotype, yet the biotrophic phase of $R _ { ☉ }$ sojae infection may only last a few hours, potentially affecting SA dynamics. Additionally, the decrease in ABA levels observed in both R and S cultivars (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A ) aligns with findings reported for $P _ { \cdot }$ megasperma infection ( 52 ). This supports previous observations that a reduction in ABA enhances resistance to various pathogens, further highlighting the complex role of ABA in plant defense ( 53 ). 

In this study, our analysis identified one of the well-characterized phytoalexins, glyceollin I ( 43 , 54 , 55 ) as a tolerance-type metabolite due to its significantly higher inducibility in susceptible (S) groups ( Fig. 2B ). Notably its occurrence is not a determinant of cultivars’ resistance. However, when we applied this classification framework to other so-called tolerance-type metabolites, including daidzein, formononetin, and genistein (without glyceollin I), we failed to observe any $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae-suppressing effects in assays measuring mycelial growth, cyst germination, and pathogenicity (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 $\bar { \boldsymbol { A } } { - } \boldsymbol { C } )$ . This finding suggests that metabolites sharing a similar enrichment pattern with glyceollin I may not necessarily function in the same physiological manner to dampen pathogen-caused damage. In contrast, the initial infection-induced increase in daidzein would recruit additional pathogenic cohorts facilitating 

secondary infection (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 H and I ), that suggested that daidzein plays a role as a pathogenesis-type metabolite in fact. While functional assays for formononetin and genistein are still needed, the effect of daidzein at least suggests that the enrichment patterns we have summarized may not solely reflect active host regulation in response to pathogen attack. Instead, they could represent a result of pathogen-driven manipulation, analogous to Botrytis cinerea , which secretes $\beta$ -(1,3) (1,6)-D-glucan to stimulate SA accumulation while antagonistically suppressing JA-responsive defense genes, ultimately weakening host resistance ( 56 ). 

Here, we propose a two-levels analysis strategy, one is from compound layer, and the other is from sample level. If a compound is detectable, a metabolic characteristic can be depicted by its chemical structure and enrichment pattern, that allows us to computationally classify them into different categories of interests for the next functional test. However, due to limitation of financial support or manpower, we cannot expand our experimental design on an unlimited scale. For instance, we employed a one-step extraction with methanol, although we recognize the presence of many low-abundance compounds within a certain plant tissue (e.g., ADPR and cADPR, important markers of the plant defense response ETI), performing additional sample concentration steps for multiple tissues across a large population is technically beneficial but infeasible due to practical constraints. To circumvent these technical obstacles, we conduct classical analytical chemistry techniques to purify disease resistance-associated compounds as a complementary strategy ( Fig. 4 and SI Appendix, Fig. S7 ). However, this introduced a new challenge: selecting the appropriate individuals as source materials for activity-guided fractionation. To overcome this, we applied a second-tier analytical framework that integrates multiple comparative parameters (Hj, δj, and RDPI) to systematically evaluate the metabolomic characteristics of each metabolome profile, which has been successfully implemented in other metabolomics studies ( 25 , 32 , 33 ), leading to the targeted 

selection of RPS1D ( Fig. 4 A and $B$ ). Through these mutual complementary approaches, we identified cinnamaldehyde and coumestrol as resistance-related metabolites, highlighting the effectiveness of our integrated analytical strategy, together thereby resulting in maximizing the application perspective in identification of functional metabolites even for other similar studies. However, our study remains subject to several limitations. Metabolite annotation has long been a major challenge in untargeted metabolomics ( 57 ), which is also reflected in our current study. Through spectral matching and structural prediction, we were able to annotate approximately $6 0 \%$ of metabolites at different levels (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 ), a considerable improvement compared to the average annotation rate ( 58 ). However, the annotation performance still needs a lot of improvement. In future work, we will attempt to enhance metabolite annotation success by incorporating additional bioinformatics algorithms and tools, implementing computational approaches for novel metabolite structure prediction, and leveraging advanced molecular networking techniques to improve annotation efficiency. 

We successfully identified and functionally validated two metabolites, cinnamaldehyde and coumestrol to inhibit $P _ { \cdot }$ sojae infection ( Figs. 3 – 5 ). Taking the advantages of structural diversities within the population, cinnamaldehyde was specifically identified with a relatively high $S i$ score, representing that cinnamaldehyde mainly enriched in a few accessions coincidently belonging to R group. Functional assays further confirmed its high efficiency in suppressing mycelial growth and cyst germination, defining it as a resistance-type metabolite, consistent with previous reports over the past decade. Cinnamic acid and its derivatives (esters, amides, aldehydes, and alcohols), synthesized via the shikimate pathway, have been widely recognized for their pharmacological activities, including antituberculosis, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects ( 59 ). However, the antimicrobial function of coumestrol has not been reported previously. We found coumestrol as a broad-spectrum antifungal metabolite that largely exerts its activity by inhibiting spore germination of several crop pathogens ( Fig. 5 ). In contrast, glyceollins, another benzofuran-derived compound and a well-characterized phytoalexin in soybean, functions mainly by suppressing mycelia growth ( 54 , 55 ). This distinction suggests that, although both compounds belong to the benzofuran metabolite class, they exhibit notable differences in their antifungal modes of action, highlighting the diversity and specificity of plant secondary metabolites in pathogen defense. This observation also further underscores the sophistication of the host defense strategy. By synthesizing structurally similar but functionally distinct metabolites, the plant optimizes its biosynthetic investment, achieving cost-effective yet comprehensive protection against pathogen attack throughout different phases of infection. The biosynthetic pathway of coumestrol has remained largely unknown. Berline et al. proposed a putative pathway involving the dehydration and cyclization of 2′,4′,7-trihydroxyisoflavanone to generate 3,9-dihydroxypterocarp-6a-en, followed by allylic oxidation to yield coumestrol (SI Appendix, Fig. S10D ) ( 60 ). Guided by this hypothesis, we narrowed down the candidate genes to the dirigent protein (DIR) family, inspired by a chemical analogy with pterocarpan biosynthesis. Through comprehensive screening, we genetically identified three CMS-related DIRs potentially involved in coumestrol biosynthesis ( Figs. 6 and 7 ). However, possibly due to the unavailability of the direct substrate, which remains unknown 

to date, or because Dir2a, Dir4a, and Dir4b are not involved in the rate-limiting steps, which constrain metabolic flux toward CMS biosynthesis, in vitro biochemical assays failed to yield a crisp conclusion regarding the function of these dirigent proteins, as no significant increase in CMS production was observed (SI Appendix, Fig. S11 ). This suggests that the “one-pot reaction” approach (total protein $^ +$ crude extract $^ +$ target protein) may not be applicable for such biosynthetic pathways where multiple key enzymatic steps and critical intermediates remain poorly characterized. Moreover, in planta , cytochrome P450 enzymes from the CYP93, CYP81, and CYP71 families have been frequently implicated in catalyzing allylic oxidation reactions, which may contribute to the final biosynthesis of coumestrol. Given their potential role in this pathway, further investigation into these gene families is needed to elucidate their specific functions and regulatory mechanisms.
