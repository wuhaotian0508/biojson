# ABSTRACT

Introduction: As an important herbivore-induced plant volatile, (3E)-4,8-dimethyl-1,3,7-nonatriene (DMNT) is known for its defensive role against multiple insect pests, including attracting natural enemies. A terpene synthase (GhTPS14) and two cytochrome P450 (GhCYP82L1, GhCYP82L2) enzymes are involved in the de novo synthesis of DMNT in cotton. We conducted a study to test the potential of manipulating DMNT-synthesizing enzymes to enhance plant resistance to insects. 

Objectives: To manipulate DMNT emissions in cotton and generate cotton lines with increased resistance to mirid bug Apolygus lucorum. 

Methods: Biosynthesis and emission of DMNT by cotton plants were altered using CRISPR/Cas9 and overexpression approaches. Dynamic headspace sampling and GC–MS analysis were used to collect, identify and quantify volatiles. The attractiveness and suitability of cotton lines against mirid bug and its parasitoid Peristenus spretus were evaluated through various assays. 

Results: No DMNT emission was detected in knockout CAS-L1L2 line, where both GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 were knocked out. In contrast, gene-overexpressed lines released higher amounts of DMNT when infested by A. lucorum. At the flowering stage, L114 (co-overexpressing GhCYP82L1 and GhTPS14) emitted 10–15-fold higher amounts than controls. DMNT emission in overexpressed transgenic lines could be triggered by methyl jasmonate (MeJA) treatment. Apolygus lucorum and its parasitoid were far less attracted to the double edited CAS-L1L2 plants, however, co-overexpressed line L114 significantly 

attracted bugs and female wasps. A high dose of DMNT, comparable to the emission of L114, significantly inhibited the growth of A. lucorum, and further resulted in higher mortalities. 

Conclusion: Turning down DMNT emission attenuated the behavioral preferences of A. lucorum to cotton. Genetically modified cotton plants with elevated DMNT emission not only recruited parasitoids to enhance indirect defense, but also formed an ecological trap to kill the bugs. Therefore, manipulation of DMNT biosynthesis and emission in plants presents a promising strategy for controlling mirid bugs.

# Introduction

In nature, after being infested by insects, plants often release a large number of volatile compounds known as herbivore-induced plant volatiles (HIPVs). HIPVs mainly include terpenes, phenypropanoids and benzenoids, volatile fatty acid derivatives, and volatile amino acid derivatives [1]. The emission of HIPVs is an important plant defense strategy, especially terpenes [2,3]. For example, the natural predator, Phytoseiulus persimilis, can utilize (E)- $\cdot \beta$ -ocimene released by lima bean to locate their prey, Tetranychus urticae [4,5]. In maize, the emission of (E)- $\cdot \beta$ -caryophyllene attracts parasitoid wasps such as Cotesia marginiventris and C. sesamiae, which parasitize the lepidopteran larvae of Spodoptera littoralis and Chilo partellus, respectively [6,7]. Silencing the linalool synthase gene OsLIS improves the attraction of rice to the brown planthopper Nilaparvata lugens while reducing attractiveness to the parasitoid wasp Anagrus nilaparvatae [8]. Moreover, linalool also directly repels Lygus hesperus and aphids [9–11]. Indole, as a HIPV component, is able to directly increase the mortality of newly hatched S. littoralis caterpillars [12]. 

In most cases, HIPV blends are dominated by terpenoids, which are essential components in direct and indirect defense of plants against pests [13,14]. As a C11 homoterpene, (3E)-4,8-dimethyl-1,3,7-nonatriene (DMNT) is released from many plant species, including rice, maize, cardamom and cotton after herbivore infestation [15]. DMNT emitted from Gossypium hirsutum, Phaseolus lunatus, and Chrysanthemum morifolium could recruit parasitoid wasps to reduce damage and promote the indirect defense of plants [16–18]. Additionally, in transgenic Arabidopsis thaliana, the DMNT emission can significantly impact the growth and reproduction of Plutella xylostella [19]. Due to the complexity of terpenoid synthesis pathways, most genetical engineering plants were achieved in model plants, such as A. thaliana and tobacco. However, more and more genetically modified plants are being developed to manipulate the emission of plant volatiles. Introduction of the strawberry nerolidol synthase FaNES1 in Arabidopsis results in the production of (3S)-(E)-nerolidol and DMNT [20]. The (S)-linalool synthase GhTPS12 from G. hirsutum has also been successfully expressed in tobacco [21]. 

Due to the widespread cultivation of Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) transgenic cotton in China, mirid bugs have emerged as the dominant insect pests, causing significant losses in many agricultural crops [22]. Currently, management of mirid bugs mainly relies on chemical control, however, this cannot be the only or permanent solution. The terpene synthase gene (GhTPS14) and two cytochrome P450 genes (GhCYP82L1, GhCYP82L2) are the key genes involved in the de novo synthesis of DMNT in cotton. GhTPS14 is responsible for converting farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP) to (E)- nerolidol, and then GhCYP82Ls, as downstream key enzymes, further catalyze the formation of DMNT from (E)-nerolidol [16,21,23]. So far, it remains unclear whether DMNT synthase genes can express in cotton and demonstrate stronger defense against mirid bugs and other important pests. 

Signaling molecules of phytohormones, such as salicylic acid (SA), jasmonic acid (JA), and their derivatives methyl salicylate 

(MeSA) and methyl jasmonate (MeJA), play vital roles in regulating the synthesis and release of volatiles in plants [24]. MeSA can activate the expression of defense-related genes in tea plant Camellia sinensis, thereby attracting the egg parasitoid Anagrus nilaparvatae to improve the indirect defense of plants [25]. Moreover, MeJA is also involved in the induction of soybean plant responses to soybean thrips, Neohydatothrips variabilis [26]. 

In the field, wheat plants treated with various doses of MeJA are attractive to predatory ladybird Coccinella septempunctata [27]. Additionally, in Quercus ilex (Italian provenance), emissions of sesquiterpenes, green leaf volatiles (GLVs) and the homoterpene DMNT were all induced by MeJA application [28]. However, whether exogenous signaling molecules, including MeJA or MeSA can trigger the release of DMNT in genetically engineered cotton remains unclear. 

In the current study, GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2, responsible for catalyzing (E)-nerolidol to form DMNT, were knocked out in G. hirsutum variety J14 using the CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing system which have been widely used for genome editing in many organisms due to its simplicity and high efficiency [29]. Simultaneously, GhTPS14 and GhCYP82Ls involved in de novo synthesis of DMNT were transferred into cotton to generate gene overexpressed lines. Subsequently, the transcript changes of target genes in genetically modified cottons infested by mirid bug Apolygus lucorum were determined. DMNT emission from cotton plants in different treatment groups was detected. Furthermore, gene overexpressed cotton lines with the highest DMNT emission were used to evaluate the behavioral preference of A. lucorum and its parasitoid Peristenus spretus. The feeding choice, body weight changes, and survival rates of A. lucorum on artificial diet containing DMNT were also assessed. Finally, we investigated whether MeJA or MeSA could act as elicitors to trigger DMNT emission from transgenic cotton lines. Our findings reveal the direct and indirect effects of DMNT on the defense mechanisms of genetically modified cotton, which will help develop new cotton germplasm varieties to effectively control the mirid bugs. 

# Materials and methods

# Plants and insects

To identify the biological function of DMNT, knockout cotton lines and gene overexpressed cotton lines were generated. The CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing system was utilized to generate three knockout cotton lines: GhCYP82L1-knocked out (CAS-L1), GhCYP82L2-knocked out (CAS-L2), and a combined GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2-knocked out (CAS-L1L2). For CAS-L1 and CAS-L2 knocked out lines, target sites for single guide RNA (sgRNA) were chosen at the first exon (91–113 bp) as follows: CATGGCATCCCT-GAACCAACCGG (GhCYP82L1) and AATTGCATCCCTGAACCGACCGG (GhCYP82L2). For the CAS-L1L2 line, target sites for simultaneous editing of both genes were designed due to high homology between GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 as follows: ATCGGTCACCTC-CATCTCTTAGG (126–149 bp) and TGCCGTTTAGTTCCGGTAGAAGG (1327–1350 bp). Specific target primers were designed for mutant 

construction (Table S1). The pYLCRISPR/Cas9P35S-N (Accession number: KR029112.1) and pYLsgRNA (Accession numbers: KR029100.1 and KR029097.1) system were employed to construct recombinant plasmid. Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation was utilized for transferring the constructs into cotton plants (Fig. S1) [30–32]. An empty vector transgenic cotton line (CAS-N) was generated as a control. Sanger sequencing, using specific primers (Table S2), was employed to determine the types of mutations in the gene-knocked out cotton lines. 

To achieve gene-overexpressed cotton lines, we employed the Transgene-Stacking II system (pYLTAC380N, pYL322-d1 and pYL322-d2) to construct recombinant plasmid and utilized A. tumefaciens-mediated transformation for transferring the constructs into cotton plants [28,32,33]. Specific primers were designed for transgenic plant generation (Table S3). Five gene overexpressed cotton lines were created as follows: GhCYP82L1 overexpressed (L1), GhCYP82L2 overexpressed (L2), GhTPS14 overexpressed (L14), GhCYP82L1 and GhTPS14 co-overexpressed (L114), GhCYP82L2 and GhTPS14 co-overexpressed (L214). An empty plasmid-transgenic cotton (H) line was used as a control. 

Nymphs and adults of A. lucorum and the adults of P. spretus, a parasitoid of A. lucorum, were collected from cotton fields at the Langfang Experimental Station of the Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences, Hebei Province $. 3 9 ^ { \circ } 0 8 ^ { \prime }$ N, $1 1 6 ^ { \circ } 2 3 ^ { \prime }$ E), China. The bugs were reared on green beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) in climatic chambers at $2 9 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C ,$ , $6 0 \pm 5 \ \%$ relative humidity (RH) and a 14 light (L):10 dark (D) photoperiod [34]. Three-day-old A. lucorum adults were used for the subsequent experiments. The wasps were reared in a plexiglass cage $( 3 0 \times 3 0 \times 2 5 \mathrm { c m } )$ in a growth chamber under the conditions of $2 5 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $6 0 \pm 5 \ : \% \ : \mathrm { R H }$ , and a 14L:10D photoperiod [35]. Newly emerged wasps were provided with $1 0 ~ \%$ honey solution [36]. One- to two-day-old adult wasps were used for the subsequent experiments. 

Seeds of different cotton lines were sown separately in plastic pots (height: $1 4 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ; diameter: $1 6 ~ \mathrm { c m } \cdot$ ) filled with a 2:1 mixture of soil and vermiculite (Yinong Nursery Substrates Co. Ltd, Shandong, China). The plants were kept in the greenhouse under conditions of $2 9 \pm 4 ^ { \circ } C$ , $4 0 \pm 1 0 \%$ RH, and a 16L:8D photoperiod. Cotton plants at the 6–7 fully expanded leaves stage, flower bud stage, flowering stage, and boll stage were infested with A. lucorum adults. Eight A. lucorum individuals (of mixed sex) were placed on each cotton plant covered with a nylon net bag (60 mesh). After $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of infestation, the top three leaves of each plant were harvested and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen for qPCR analysis. Meanwhile, volatile blends of cotton plants from different treatment groups were collected. 

# RNA extraction and qPCR measurement

Total RNAs of collected cotton samples were extracted using the RNAprep Pure kit following the manufacturer’s protocol (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing Co., Ltd., China). RNA quality was checked on a NanoPhotometer N60 (Implen, Munich, Germany). cDNA was synthesized using the FastQuant RT Kit (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing Co., Ltd., China) and stored at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for further use. The qPCR measurement was conducted on an ABI 7500 PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Carlsbad, CA, USA). The Actin (Accession numbers: AY305733) and GhUBQ7 (Accession numbers: DQ116441) were used as reference genes. Gene-specific primers were designed for target and reference genes (Table S3) [16,37]. Six biological replicates and three technical replicates were employed for each of the cotton line to determine the relative transcript levels of target genes at four stages. A total of 1512 samples were prepared separately in $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ reaction systems using the Talent qPCR PreMix kit (SYBR Green) (Tiangen Biotech, Beijing Co., Ltd., China). The com-

parative $2 ^ { \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method [38] was used to calculate the relative transcript levels of target genes. 

# Collection and identification of the plant volatiles

The dynamic headspace sampling system was used to collect plant volatiles. Volatiles of healthy J14 cotton plants were collected and identified, showing no DMNT emission, consistent with reported results [17] (Fig. S2). Therefore, we mainly focused on herbivore-injured plant. One pot containing one herbivoreinjured plant was placed into a food-grade polyethylene plastic bag, equipped with air inlet and outlet (length, $1 2 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ; width, $8 0 \mathrm { c m }$ ). Air, purified by an activated charcoal filter, was transported through the container at a flow rate of $1 5 0 0 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ with a vacuum pump. Volatiles emitted from each plant were collected in an 8- mm-diameter glass tube containing $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ of 60/80 mesh Tenax-TA (Shanghai ANPEL Scientific Instrument Company, Shanghai, China) for 8 h [39]. Each collected sample was eluted using $3 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of n-hexane (Fisher, Fairlawn, USA), to which $8 . 6 5 ~ \mathrm { \ n g / \mu L }$ of ethyl caprate (Sigma-Aldrich, Oakville, Canada) was added as an internal standard. Plant volatile collections from seven cotton lines at four stages were repeated six times, resulting in a total of 168 samples for subsequent volatile identification. A $1 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot of each extracted sample was injected into a gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC–MS) system (QP2010SE, Shimadzu, Japan) equipped with a Rtx-5MS dimethylpolysiloxane column $\langle 0 . 2 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { m } \times 0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m m } \times 3 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ , Shimadzu, Japan). Purified helium was used as the carrier gas at a constant flow rate of $0 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . The injector, transfer line and ion source temperatures were set at $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $2 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and $2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , respectively. The GC oven temperature was initially maintained at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min and then increased to $1 9 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at a rate of $5 ~ { ^ \circ } C / { \mathrm { m i n } }$ , held for 5 min, and finally increased to $2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at a rate of $1 0 \ \mathrm { ^ { \circ } C / m i n }$ and held for 5 min. Additionally, MS was scanned at a 1-kV detector voltage over 50 650 atomic mass units. Tentative identification of DMNT and other volatile compounds was made by comparing mass spectra with mass spectra libraries (NIST and Department of Chemical Ecology, Göteborg University, Sweden) and with mass spectra and retention times of standard compounds obtained from Fluka, Sigma [16,39]. 

# Indoor behavioral choice test

The gene co-overexpressed cotton line L114, exhibiting the highest DMNT emission, and the knockout CAS-L1L2 line without DMNT emission were used in the behavioral choice test. A threecage olfactometer was employed to evaluate the behavioral tendency of A. lucorum adults towards volatiles collected from different cotton groups at the flowering stage [40]. The olfactometer comprised three transparent plexiglass cages (each $2 5 \times 2 5 \times 2 5 \mathrm { c m } ,$ ) connected by two $1 5 \mathrm { c m }$ funnel-shaped arms. Filtered air, passed through activated carbon, flowed into the side cages labeled treatment (T) and the control (C) at a rate of $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { L } / $ min, and discharged from the central release cage (R). Volatile sample from J14 was collected as description above and used as a control. A $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot of volatile blends from L114 or CAS-L1L2 (collected as description above) was applied onto a cigarette filter dispenser and placed in the T cage. Simultaneously, volatile blends from J14 were placed in the C cage. The olfactometer was placed in a dark room at $2 8 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $6 0 \pm 1 0 \% \mathrm { R } .$ . H. Sixty adult insects were released into cage R in each replicate, and the test was repeated three times. The number of insects recruited by the odor source of cage C or T was recorded after $^ { 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ [40]. 

The tendency of P. spretus wasps towards volatiles from L114 or CAS-L1L2 was evaluated using a Y-tube olfactometer, consisting of a $2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ -long central tube and two $2 0 \mathrm { c m }$ -long lateral arms with an interior diameter of $3 \mathrm { \ c m }$ . The two branch tubes were attached to 

separate odor-source flasks, and $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of eluted volatile blends sample from L114 or CAS-L1L2 was dripped onto a filter paper strip, which was then put into one odor-source flask. Volatile blends from J14 in another flask was used as control [9]. One to two-day-old adult parasitoids were individually released at the base of the central arm of the Y-tube and observed for 5 min. If a parasitoid did not make a choice during this period, it was removed and recorded as having made no choice. A parasitoid that travelled two-thirds of the distance into the terminal arms and stayed there for at least 5 s was recorded as having made a choice. After five runs, the position of the arms was reversed to avoid position bias. The Y-tube was changed after every 10 tests [41]. Sixty insects were used in each treatment. The Y-tube was illuminated with a 15 W LED daylight bulb and maintained at $2 8 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } .$ . All behavioral assays were conducted between 8:00 am and 12:00 am. 

# Behavioral trial in net cages

To verify the attractiveness of genetically modified cotton with high DMNT emission to A. lucorum, a two-choice behavioral trial was conducted in nylon net cages $( 2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } \times 1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { c m } \times 1 2 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ 100 meshes). One pot containing either a L114 or CAS-L1L2 cotton plant at flowering stage was placed in one side of the cage, while a pot containing a J14 cotton plant was placed in the other side of the cage. One white sticky card $( 2 0 \mathrm { c m } \times 2 0 \mathrm { c m } )$ ) was vertically hung at a distance of $5 . 0 \mathrm { c m }$ from each cotton plant. The cages were placed in a dark room at $2 8 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } C$ and $6 0 \pm 1 0 \% \mathrm { R } .$ H. One hundred male or female adults of A. lucorum were introduced into the center of the net cage at 8:00 am. The number of bugs on each sticky trap was counted at 2:00 pm. The assay was repeated three times. In parallel, the attractiveness of genetically modified cotton to P. spretus wasps was evaluated by performing the above-mentioned trial. Forty male or female wasps were released in the center of the nets at 8:00 am. Two Petri dishes containing a $10 \%$ sucrose solution applied to a cotton ball were placed on the sides of the cage as a food source. The number of wasps on each sticky trap was recorded at $4 { : } 0 0 ~ \mathrm { p m }$ . The assay was repeated three times. 

# Feeding bioassay

To fully quantify the content of DMNT in leaves of the L114 plant at the flowering stage, a grinding method was adopted to extract the target compound. Each plant was infested by eight bugs for $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ , after which $2 . 0 { \mathrm { ~ g } }$ of top leaves were harvested and placed into $1 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m L }$ of 5 M $\mathrm { C a C l } _ { 2 }$ for grinding at room temperature. The milled samples were transferred into $2 0 \ \mathrm { m L }$ PTFE/Silicon Septa screw cap glass vials (Agilent Technologies, USA) and shaken at 200 rpm, $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ . Additionally, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of ethyl caprate solution $( 8 6 . 5 ~ \mathrm { \ n g / \mu L } )$ was added to each vial as an internal standard. A solid phase microextraction (SPME) handle (SAAB-57330U, Bellefonte, USA) with a $6 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) fiber was inserted into the vial, positioned $1 . 0 \ \mathrm { c m }$ above the liquid surface, to collect volatile samples for $3 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ . After absorption, the SPME fiber was directly inserted into the GC injector, and the DMNT content in the leaves was determined using the GC–MS analysis described above. 

Based on the content of DMNT $( 9 . 1 1 5 \pm 0 . 3 7 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g } )$ in ground leaves of L114, artificial diet containing $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of pure DMNT was employed to investigate the effects of DMNT on A. lucorum feeding [42]. In the choice test, the behavioral responses of bugs to the DMNT standard were evaluated using the three-cage olfactometer mentioned above. 

The DMNT standard was dissolved in n-hexane at a concentration of $1 0 ~ \mu { \bf g } / \bf g$ , with n-hexane used as a blank control. Additionally, to assess the behavioral responses of bugs to artificial diet containing DMNT, one parafilm capsule containing $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of 

DMNT in $_ { 0 . 2 1 \ g }$ of artificial diet was placed at one end of a petri dish (diameter: $1 5 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ ), with a capsule without DMNT addition in artificial diet placed symmetrically at the other end of the petri dish as a control. The capsule was positioned 1 cm away from the edge of the petri dish. Thirty 2nd instar nymphs of A. lucorum were placed in the center of the petri dish. The petri dishes were then placed in a dark room under condition of $2 8 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $6 0 \pm 1 0 \%$ R. H. The number of feeding choices made by the bugs was recorded 4 h after treatment. The assay was repeated three times. 

In the non-choice test, six artificial diet capsules were evenly placed in a petri dish, each positioned with a 60-degree angle between adjacent capsules and $1 \mathrm { \ c m }$ from the edge of the petri dish. The treatment group consisted of six capsules containing $1 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of DMNT in $_ { 0 . 2 1 \ g }$ of artificial diet, while the control group contained six capsules with only artificial diet. 

Thirty 2nd instar nymphs of A. lucorum were placed in the center of each petri dish, which were then kept in a dark room under condition of $2 8 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $6 0 \pm 1 0 \mathrm { ~ \% ~ R } .$ H. Feeding choice of A. lucorum were recorded 4 h, 6 h, 8 h, 12 h, and $2 4 \ \mathrm { h }$ after treatment. The test was repeated three times. Furthermore, individual capsules containing 1, 5, 10, 50, and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of DMNT in $_ { 0 . 2 1 \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ of diet were placed in separate polypropylene plastic cups (with breathable lids, diameter: $5 . 5 ~ \mathrm { \ c m }$ , bottom diameter: $5 ~ \textrm { c m }$ , height: $2 . 5 \mathrm { c m } \cdot$ ) to assess the effects of different DMNT doses on the growth and development of A. lucorum [41]. One 2nd instar nymph of A. lucorum was placed in each cup. Diet capsules without DMNT addition were used as controls. The cups were placed in a climatic chamber at $2 9 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf C } _ { \mathsf { \Lambda } }$ , $6 0 \pm 5 \% \mathrm { R } .$ H., and a 14L: 10D photoperiod. Capsules were renewed every two days. Fifteen A. lucorum nymphs were tested in each replication, with a total 60 nymphs employed in each treatment. Body weight and mortality of A. lucorum were recorded 24 h, 48 h, 72 h, 96 h after treatment. 

# Exogenous hormone induction

To investigate whether MeJA or MeSA could act as elicitors to trigger DMNT emission in transgenic cotton lines, we conducted exogenous hormone elicitation following the method reported by Rodriguez-Saona et al. (2001) [43]. A pot containing one cotton plant with 6 7 fully expanded leaves was placed into a glass jar $1 0 \mathrm { c m }$ in diameter $\times 2 5 \mathrm { c m }$ in height). The plant was then treated with a cotton ball containing $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { L }$ of ethanol-MeJA (9:1) or ethanol-MeSA (9:1) solution [39]. Two cotton balls were positioned underneath cotton leaves, without physical contact with the plants. The glass jar was tightly sealed with a glass lid. Hormone induction lasted for $3 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ , starting at 6:00 pm. As a control, plant was treated with cotton balls containing pure ethanol. After induction, qPCR measurement and volatiles collection and identification were conducted according to the procedure described above. Each treatment was repeated six times. 

# Statistical analysis

The data were analyzed using R v.4.0.3 (R Core Development Team, 2020). All data are presented as the mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM), and if necessary, they were transformed prior to analysis. In the Y-tube behavior trial, a chi-square analysis with a 50:50 distribution was performed to determine the preference of wasps between odor source and control. In the net cage trial and feeding choice, a paired-sample t-test was conducted to analyze the choice of insects between treatment and control. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by the least significant difference (LSD) test $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ was used to evaluate the comparisons among different treatments, including transcript levels, DMNT emissions, body weights, and survival rates. 

# Results

Knockout of GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 in cotton reduced DMNT emission 

Four knockout T1 generation cotton lines, namely CAS-L1 (GhCYP82L1-knocked out), CAS-L2 (GhCYP82L2-knocked out), CAS-L1L2 (GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2-knocked out), and CAS-N (empty vector), were planted in greenhouse and subsequently verified by PCR and Sanger sequencing (Fig. S3). Frame shift, small fragment deletion and early termination occurred in the amino acid sequence in knockout cotton lines by protein prediction (Fig. S4-7). The T2 knockout lines were generated through self-fertilization of the knockout lines for the experiments. After bugs infestation, DMNT emission of CAS-L1 was approximately $2 1 5 . 7 5 \pm 9 3 . 4 ~ \mathrm { n g / m L } ,$ , and those of CAS-L2 was approximately $1 5 9 . 6 7 \pm 2 7 . 9 \mathrm { \ n g / m L } ,$ whereas J14 and CAS-N emitted approximately $4 6 7 . 8 \pm 6 0 . 9 3 ~ \mathrm { n g / m L }$ and $4 9 3 . 1 1 \pm 1 2 2 . 2 ~ \mathrm { n g / m L }$ of DMNT, respectively. CAS-L1 and CAS-L2 emitted less DMNT than the controls $( P < 0 . 0 5 ) $ . No DMNT emission was detected in CAS-L1L2 when both GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 were knocked out (Fig. 1, Table 1). 

# Gene overexpressed cotton emitted higher DMNT

Gene-overexpressed T1 generation cotton lines, namely L1 (GhCYP82L1 overexpressed), L2 (GhCYP82L2 overexpressed), L14 (GhTPS14 overexpressed), L114 (GhCYP82L1 and GhTPS14 cooverexpressed), L214 (GhCYP82L2 and GhTPS14 cooverexpressed), and H (empty plasmid-transgenic), were cultured in the greenhouse and verified by qPCR. The T2 transgenic lines were generated through self-fertilization of the transgenic lines. qPCR measurement was conducted to evaluate the expression changes of the target genes (GhCYP82L1, GhCYP82L2, and GhTPS14) in leaves of cotton at 6–7 leaves stage, flower bud stage, flowering stage and boll stage after infestation by A. lucorum. It was found that the expression of GhCYP82L1 in L1 and L114 plant leaves was up to100-fold higher than in J14 and H plant leaves (Fig. 2A). 

Likewise, GhCYP82L2 exhibited up to a 30-fold higher expression in L2 and L214 plant leaves compared to the two controls (J14 and H plant leaves) (Fig. 2B). GhTPS14 expression levels in L14, L114, and L214 plant leaves were also found to be up to 10 times higher than those observed in J14 and H plant leaves (Fig. 2C). Additionally, the expressions of GhTPS14 and GhCYP82Ls in gene-overexpressed cotton were significantly higher at flowering stage than at other developmental stages (Fig. 2). DMNT emission from different gene-overexpressed cotton lines were calculated using GC–MS analysis. In single GhCYP82L1 or single GhTPS14 overexpressed lines, DMNT emissions significantly increased. When GhCYP82L1 and GhTPS14 were co-overexpressed, DMNT emission was even dramatically higher, with a 10–15-fold compared to that of J14 (Fig. 2D, Fig. S8). Similarly, when either single GhCYP82L2 or single GhTPS14 gene was overexpressed, DMNT emission were also significantly increased. Moreover, DMNT emission in co-overexpressed L214 was almost five times than that of J14 (Fig. 2D, Fig. S9). Noteworthy, apart from DMNT, no significant differences in volatile composition were detected among different cotton lines (Table. S4).

# High dose of DMNT is toxic to bug

In choice test, A. lucorum showed significant preference for DMNT standard and DMNT treated artificial diet (Fig. 5A, B). However, possibly due to the uniform distribution of volatiles in the closed petri dish and the satiety of A. lucorum, there was no difference in feeding preference of bugs between the DMNT-treated artificial diet and the blank control after $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . In non-choice test, A. lucorum could feed both on control artificial diet and artificial diet containing $1 0 ~ \mu { \bf g } / \ g$ DMNT, and there was no significant difference in the number of bug choices between control artificial diet and DMNT-treated artificial diet. Thus, the diet containing DMNT had no feeding avoidance effect on A. lucorum (Fig. 5C). However, feeding on artificial diet containing $1 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of DMNT had a negative impact on the body weight gain and survival rate of A. lucorum (Fig. 5D, F). Moreover, the effect of DMNT-treated diet on the growth and development of A. lucorum was dose-dependent. The survival rates and biomass of bugs feeding on artificial diet decreased along with the increase of DMNT content. After being reared for $9 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ , the growth performance of A. lucorum was significantly inhibited by higher doses of DMNT (Fig. 5D, E). In addition, DMNT diet-treated A. lucorum individuals had much smaller bodies at the time of death relative to controls, indicating that high doses of DMNT are toxic to A. lucorum (Fig. 5F, G).

# Bug lost behavioral preference to knockout cotton lines

CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing has been widely employed for plant trait improvement such as high yield rice and Camelina Crantz with different nutritional components [50,51]. In the current work, both GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 genes were knocked out using the CRISPR/Cas9 system to manipulate the biosynthesis and emission of DMNT. As expected, no DMNT was detected in combined GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 knockout cotton plants. (E)-nerolidol, the precursor substance of DMNT, was reported to not be released from plants and the general speculated reason being its catalysis to other secondary metabolites [52]. However, in GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 knockout cotton plant, 

(E)-nerolidol still could not be detected, with the possible reasons of low volatility, as well as its accumulation in plants. Consequently, compared to control plants, knockout cotton lines showed lower attractiveness to bugs. Due to the absence of biosynthesis and emission of DMNT, combined GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2- knocked out CAS-L1L2 cotton plant exhibited reduced susceptibility to A. lucorum. Thereupon, bugs showed non-preference to genetically modified cotton CAS-L1L2. Meanwhile, gene editing had no adverse effects on the growth and development of cotton plants (Fig. S10). This suggests that reducing host plant susceptibility to target insects through gene editing is a promising pest management strategy.

It has been reported that DMNT emission in G. hirsutum significantly upregulates after herbivore damage, especially in leaves and stems, but not in roots [16]. The spatio-temporal emission of DMNT may be due to the evolution of cotton plant defense systems against the infestation of herbivorous insects. Tuning volatiles emission in genetically modified plants could play important roles in pest management. For instance, overexpression of the caryophyllene synthase gene GhTPS1 in cotton negatively affects multiple pests while attracting parasitoids [32]. Enhancement of linalool emission in A. thaliana significantly repels aphids [53]. Transgenic rice plants with high DMNT emission improve attractiveness to the parasitoid wasp C. chilonis [18]. 

In indoor behavioral assays, the parasitoid wasp P. spretus showed significant preferences for volatiles collected from gene co-overexpressed L114. Moreover, in net cages, gene cooverexpressed L114 also significantly attracted parasitoid wasps. Therefore, it could be implied that cotton plants with high DMNT emission recruit parasitoid wasps to enhance indirect defense. Compared to male wasps, females showed much more preference to DMNT. This phenomenon may be due to the different roles of the same semiochemicals in chemoreception between female and male parasitoids. Male wasps follow plant semiochemicals for feeding and searching partners, while females mainly use plant volatiles to locate and parasitize hosts, which is mutually verified with our previous work [16]. 

Both male and female A. lucorum were attracted by volatiles released from cotton with high DMNT emission. Moreover, the attractiveness of DMNT standard to A. lucorum was also confirmed in a supplementary assay. Intriguingly, the toxicity of DMNT has been evidenced against insect pests. DMNT inhibits the growth of P. xylostella and eventually results in lower survival rates [19]. Sim-

ilar results were observed in our study. The reason why bugs can be attracted to toxic volatiles such as DMNT from infested plant may be a tendency to aggregate, similar to beetles [54,55]. However, this speculation requires further investigations. Here, increasing the concentration of DMNT in the artificial diet from a medium dose (equivalent to $9 . 1 1 5 \pm 0 . 3 7 8 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { g }$ of DMNT in ground leaves of L114) significantly inhibited the biomass and increased mortality of A. lucorum. However, DMNT-treated artificial diet showed no feeding avoidance effect on A. lucorum. We speculate that the high level of DMNT in the genetically modified cotton plants may lure mirid bugs, but be toxic due to high contents of DMNT (compared to the wild type plants). It has been reported that transgenic A. thaliana overexpressing DMNT synthase gene PEN1 can repel and significantly inhibit the growth and reproduction of P. xylostella to promote the plant direct defense [19]. Therefore, promoting DMNT biosynthesis and emission via overexpression of target genes in cotton plants could form an ecological trap to control A. lucorum. Nevertheless, we will generate the GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 co-overexpressed cotton line or combined cooverexpression of the GhCYP82L1 and GhCYP82L2 plus GhTPS14 line in the future work to further investigate the ecological trap roles of higher DMNT emission.
