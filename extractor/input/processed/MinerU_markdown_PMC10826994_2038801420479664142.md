# Summary

Chilling injury has a negative impact on the quantity and quality of crops, especially subtropical and tropical plants. The plant cell wall is not only the main source of biomass production, but also the first barrier to various stresses. Therefore, improving the understanding of the alterations in cell wall architecture is of great significance for both biomass production and stress adaptation. Herein, we demonstrated that the cell wall principal component cellulose accumulated during chilling stress, which was caused by the activation of MaCESA proteins. The sequence-multiple comparisons show that a cold-inducible NAC transcriptional factor MaNAC1, a homologue of Secondary Wall NAC transcription factors, has high sequence similarity with Arabidopsis SND3. An increase in cell wall thickness and cellulosic glucan content was observed in MaNAC1-overexpressing Arabidopsis lines, indicating that MaNAC1 participates in cellulose biosynthesis. Over-expression of MaNAC1 in Arabidopsis mutant snd3 restored the defective secondary growth of thinner cell walls and increased cellulosic glucan content. Furthermore, the activation of MaCESA7 and MaCESA6B cellulose biosynthesis genes can be directly induced by MaNAC1 through binding to SNBE motifs within their promoters, leading to enhanced cellulose content during low-temperature stress. Ultimately, tomato fruit showed greater cold resistance in MaNAC1 overexpression lines with thickened cell walls and increased cellulosic glucan content. Our findings revealed that MaNAC1 performs a vital role as a positive modulator in modulating cell wall cellulose metabolism within banana fruit under chilling stress. 

# Introduction

The changing global climate is increasingly threatening food security. Banana, a tropical and subtropical fruit, is the fourth tonnage agricultural product in the world. As a typical cold-sensitive horticultural product, storage temperatures less than $1 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ cause physiological disorders in banana fruit (Putra et al., 2010), which is termed chilling injury (CI). Apparently, CI causes discoloration and obvious deep brown spots on the peel skin (Promyou et al., 2008), leading to a great loss of security and market value of banana fruit. Although an enormous range of physiological responses has evolved to cope with less favourable conditions with the constant domestication, unpredictable weather momently threatens plants’ survival. A sudden change to low temperatures for tropical originating plants, such as bananas, can result in severe irreversible physiological damage. Studies about CI in plants have focused mostly on membrane damage, reactive oxygen species (ROS) production and scavenging systems, proline accumulation, marked changes in gene expression, and biochemical pathways affecting photosynthesis (Asghari and Aghdam, 2010; Baslam et al., 2020; Gill and Tuteja, 2010; Liang et al., 2020; Theocharis et al., 2012). However, less attention has been paid to the link between lowtemperature stress with plant cell wall components and architecture. 

Transcriptional regulation is a critical biological process. The NAC transcription factor (TF) is a prominent family of plant-

specific TFs that perform a critical function in plants’ growth, development and reaction to ecological stress. NACs from Arabidopsis and rice are broadly categorized into six important branches, including MAM/CUC3, secondary wall NAC (SWN), TIP, SNAC (Stress-Responsive NAC), ANAC034 and OsNAC4 (Nakashima et al., 2012). The presence of SNAC genes is triggered by abiotic stress and can regulate stress tolerance. More TFs have been identified after the first NAC protein, Responsive to Dehydration 26 (RD26) (Yamaguchi-Shinozaki et al., 1992). RD26 not only participates in drought and salt tolerance, but is also responsible for cold stress (Fujita et al., 2004; Tran et al., 2004). ANAC072, a homologous NAC protein of the SNAC group DR26, is promoted by cold stress and is integrated with an ERD1 promoter that responds to extreme temperatures (Tran et al., 2004). The subject of discussion is Arabidopsis NAC featuring transmembrane motif 1-like 6 (NTL6) induces Pathogenesis-Related (PR) genes to participate in cold-induced pathogen resistance, which is independent of salicylic acid (SA) (Seo et al., 2010). Furthermore, the interaction between a NAC domain TF Vascular-Related NAC-Domain7 (VND7) and a VND Interacting 2 (VNI2) TF contributes to the regulation of Coldregulated (Marquez-Garcıa et al., 2012) transcription and shows the ability to directly interact with its promoter (Yamaguchi et al., 2010a; Yang et al., 2011). Identification of the NAC protein VND7 has established its crucial role as a modulator in the differentiation of xylem vessel (Yamaguchi et al., 2010b). VND7 

and other secondary wall NACs (SWNs), such as Secondary wallassociated NAC domain protein1 (SND1, also called NST3/ ANAC012), NAC Secondary Wall Thickening Promoting Factor1 (NST1), NST2 and VND6, bind to a 19-bp consensus sequence designated as secondary wall NAC binding element (SNBE), (T/A) NN(C/T)(T/C/G)TNNNNNNNA(A/C)GN(A/C/T)(A/T), within direct target’s promoter (Zhong et al., 2008). SWNs are key transcriptional switches and directly regulate MYB46, MYB83, SND3, MYB103 and KNAT7 to promote the biosynthetic program of secondary wall in fibres and vessels (Ko et al., 2009; McCarthy et al., 2009; Zhong et al., 2006, 2008). However, it is unknown whether banana SWNs regulate cell wall biosynthesis and how they act under CI. 

The cell wall performs a critical function as the first barrier of plant to protect against environmental changes. In fact, the cell wall of plant is composed of both primary and secondary walls. The former is the thin layer that is laid down by division and elongation cells, and the latter is the thick layer that takes charge of specific cell types with specialized functions. The principal constituents of secondary walls comprise carbohydrates, specifically cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. Cellulose fibres, the major contributor to plant biomass, are embedded in a polysaccharide matrix to support plant stiffness. The Golgi apparatus is the site of the assembly of cellulose synthase (CESA) complexes (CSCs), which are subsequently transported to the plasma membrane to facilitate the active synthesis of cellulose. The interaction between the three Arabidopsis secondary wall CESA proteins of the secondary wall has been demonstrated through immuno-precipitation and fluorescence protein-tagging analyses. The accurate targeting of these CESAs to the plasma membrane and their assembly into complexes necessitates the presence of all three proteins (Taylor et al., 2000, 2003). AtCESA4, 7 and 8 are essential for SCW CSCs (Taylor et al., 2003), and any of their mutations result in a severe reduction in the amount of cellulose and secondary wall thickness (Taylor et al., 2000; Turner and Somerville, 1997). Based on genetic information, the three CESA genes are indispensable for cellulose synthesis in the secondary wall. Bioinformatics analysis reveals that AtCESA7 plays the highest level of class specificity (Kumar et al., 2017). In the CESA7 protein, several distinct regions contribute to its functionality, including the N-terminal regions (comprising a short N terminus (NT), a Cys-rich zinc RING finger domain (ZN), and VARIABLE REGION 1 (VR1)), as well as CONSERVED REGION 1 (CR1) and VARIABLE REGION 2 (VR2). These regions collectively contribute to effective enhancements in cellulose content and plant height, which had been confirmed in the genetic context of mutants cesa4 and cesa8 (Kumar et al., 2017). Interestingly, when attempting to swap identical regions from both CESA4 and CESA8 into Arabidopsis mutant cesa7, no complementation was observed (Kumar et al., 2017). This indicates that CESA7 occupies a position of significant constraint among CSCs. The mechanism of cold acclimation triggers modifications in the constitution of polysaccharides present in cell walls, as well as in the enzymatic activities that participate in the process of cell wall modification. The cold acclimation process has been observed to enhance the cell wall strength of grapes (Vitis spp.) as well as apples (Malus domestica) (Rajashekar and Lafta, 1996). In addition, winter oilseed rape leaves (Brassica napus L. var. oleifera L.) exhibit a significant increase of the pectin, galactose and arabinose contents in cell walls in the cold, in comparison to the NA (Non-acclimated) specimen (Kubacka-Zezbalska and Kacperska, 1999). In rice, 

OsTMF is identified as a homologue of the TATA modulatory factor in humans and positively regulates the content of cellulose by activating the expression of OsCESA4 and OsCESA9 when subjected to conditions of cold stress (Xu et al., 2020). However, reports on changes in the cell wall mainly concentrate on changes in pectin content and enzyme activities other than cellulose from the cell wall skeleton (Bilska-Kos et al., 2017; Carvajal et al., 2015). 

Throughout this investigation, we found that CI resulted in increases in both the thickness of the secondary cell wall and the accumulation of cellulose in the peels of banana fruit. As a member belongs to plant-specific SWN TF, MaNAC1 is triggered by low temperature. Cell wall thickness and cellulose accumulation also increased with MaNAC1 overexpression in wild-type (WT) Arabidopsis and tomato, and mutant snd3, suggesting that MaNAC1 performs a vital function in cell wall thickness and cellulose accumulation. Furthermore, we demonstrated that MaNAC1 can activate the MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 cellulose biosynthesis genes by directly binding to the SNBE site. More importantly, overexpression of MaNAC1 can improve cold tolerance of tomato fruit with thickened cell wall and accumulated cellulose. Our results indicate that MaNAC1 is involved in banana CI by triggering cellulose biosynthesis, revealing a novel metabolism process of banana fruit when exposed to chilling stress.

# Thickened cell wall and accumulated cellulose in banana peels under chilling stress

The appearance of postharvest bananas under CI stress has been investigated. To identify the main changes in the postharvest banana peel under CI, we paid more attention to the cell wall of the plant, which serves as the main defence mechanism in plants when subjected to stress. We observed some browning spots under chilling injury $( 7 ~ ^ { \circ } C )$ (Figure 1a), which is consistent with previous research (Zhou et al., 2022). In comparison with fruit stored at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (room temperature), we found that the texture was firmer with the extension of storage at $7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figure 1b), the peel tissue showed a significantly thicker cell wall under CI (Figures 1c,d). Furthermore, the cellulosic glucan content in the peels of chilling bananas significantly increased compared to that at room temperature (Figure 1e).

# MaNAC1 promoted the accumulation of cellulose content and secondary growth under CI

To further reveal the biological function of MaNAC1 in the cell wall, we overexpressed MaNAC1 in Arabidopsis WT and chose two transgenic lines overexpressing MaNAC1 (Figure S2a). Root lengths of two transgenic lines of 7-day-old seedlings and 5-week plantlets, 35S:MaNAC1-L3 and 35S:MaNAC1-L4, were 

significantly elongated in $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ compared with WT (Figures S2b,c). Stem sections were stained with toluidine blue, a metachromatic dye that can selectively stain certain components of plant cell walls. Results showed that obvious secondary growth was observed in transgenic Arabidopsis plants compared to WT treated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 weeks (Figure 3a). Moreover, 35S: MaNAC1-L3 and 35S:MaNAC1-L4 cell walls displayed significant thickness (Figure 3b). Cell wall component pellets can be hydrolysed to form monosaccharides after removing wax and other pigments. For example, cellulose is a type of polysaccharide 

composed of a linear chain of glucose monomers that bind together via b-1,4 glycosidic bonds and can be completely hydrolysed into glucose units, and thus the glucose content is known as the cellulosic glucan content. To explore the main changes of the cell wall components that lead to thickness, we determined the content of monosaccharides of cell wall pellets in transgenic lines and WT plants, and the findings revealed that the cell wall glucose composition of 35S:MaNAC1-L3 and 35S: MaNAC1-L4 were higher than those of WT (Figure 3c). To further investigate the expression level of Arabidopsis CESA genes, we tested them by qRT-PCR and found that AtCESA6, AtCESA7 and AtCESA8 were up-regulated in Arabidopsis stems treated for 2 weeks under $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ (Figures 3d–f). 

MaNAC1 had a high amino acid identity $( 5 0 \% - 7 0 \% )$ ) to AtSND2/3/4 by multiple alignments (Figure S3). For the purpose of conducting a more thorough investigation into the biological function of MaNAC1, we got the Arabidopsis homologous gene mutant snd3. We overexpressed MaNAC1 to Arabidopsis snd3 (Figure S4a) and found that the root lengths in two 7-day-old transgenic lines, namely 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L2 and 35S: MaNAC1/snd3-L3, were significantly elongated, which was similar to that of WT at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ compared to snd3 (Figure S4b,c), indicating that MaNAC1 contributed to normal stem development of 5-week-old Arabidopsis snd3 and was able to complement the short roots of snd3 mutant seedlings. Subsequently, we stained sections with toluidine blue and found that snd3 mutant stem sections showed irregulated xylems, incomplete vascular bundles and thinner interfascicular fibres compared to that of WT, 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L2 and 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L3 treated with $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 weeks (Figure S4d). In addition, the interfascicular fibre thickness of WT, 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L2 and 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L3 was more significantly thicker than that of snd3 (Figure S4e). We then determined the cell wall monosaccharide content of WT, snd3, 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L2 and 35S: MaNAC1/snd3-L3 stems with high-performance anion-exchange chromatography analysis, and found that the glucose contents of 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L2 and 35S:MaNAC1/snd3-L3 had no difference with that of WT, but were significantly higher than snd3 (Figure S4f). The above results indicate that MaNAC1 restored secondary growth defects in the Arabidopsis mutant snd3 by activating the cellulose contents of the cell wall. 

# MaCESAs were induced by CI

Cellulose is a polysaccharide composed of linear glucan chains that are composed of glucose units that were connected through b-1,4 glycosidic bonds. CESA genes encode glycosyltransferases that play crucial roles in the catalytic cellulose synthase subunit (Holland et al., 2000). Initially, we conducted a detailed investigation of members of the CESA gene family within the Musa acuminata genome. A NJ phylogeny was constructed through parsimony analysis of inferred proteins from established CESA genes using MEGA6 software (Figure 4a). The phylogeny consisted of seven clades, as previously reported (Carroll and Specht, 2011; Guerriero et al., 2012). Except for moss and lycophyte, all CESA genes of banana were distributed in the other six clades. Thirteen candidates, banana CESA genes, were named on the phylogeny (Figure 4a). The present study aimed to examine the possible implication of MaCESA genes in stress responses by analysing the prevalence of conserved stressrelated cis-elements within their promoter regions (2 kb region upstream of ATG) using PlantCARE. Four abiotic stress response elements, low-temperature responsiveness (LTREs, CCGAAA/ 

CCGAC), drought-inducibility (DREs, CAACTG), anaerobic induction (AREs, AAACCA) and defence in addition to stress responsiveness (TC-rich repeats, GTTTTCTTAC) have been shown in Figure 4b, and multiple types of cis-acting regulatory factors have been observed within all promoters of MaCESA genes. Interestingly, all MaCESAs exhibited the presence of one or more LTREs, which are commonly linked to the response mechanisms triggered by low-temperature conditions. Transcriptome data were used to investigate 13 expression patterns of MaCESA genes in banana stored at low temperatures for different times, including zero-days (d 0), one-quarter day (d 0.25), half of the day (d 0.5), 1 day (d 1), 3 days (d 3) and 5 days (d 5) under CI (Figure 4c). The findings indicated that expression of the MaCESA1A, MaCESA3A, MaCESA3B and MaCESA6E in banana peels was induced in the early stage (d 0.25), while the induction of MaCESA6B, MaCESA6F, MaCESA7 and MaCESA8 was in late stage (d 3, d 5) of CI, indicating that the MaCESA genes respond differently to low-temperature stress. To verify the transcriptome sequencing data, we detected MaCESAs by a qRT-PCR assay, and the results are plotted as bar graphs (Figure 4d–h). In particular, the transcripts of MaCESA6A, MaCESA6B, MaCESA6F, MaCESA7 and MaCESA8 were significantly induced in bananas after CI treatment. 

# MaNAC1 (Ma06_g28730) activated the transcription of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7

SWNs bind to secondary wall NAC binding elements (SNBE) resulting in the activation of SCW biosynthesis and metabolism (Zhong et al., 2021). The SNBE site, a cis-element activated by SWN, consists of an incomplete palindromic consensus sequence that is 19-bp, (T/A)NN(C/T)(T/C/G) TNNNNNNNA(A/C)GN(A/C/T)(A/T) (Zhong et al., 2010). Since the promoters of MaCESA6A, MaCESA6B, MaCESA6F, MaCESA7 and MaCESA8 contained one or more SNBE sites (Figure 5a), we then purified the recombinant protein of GST-MaNAC1 (Figure S6) and used EMSA to confirm whether MaNAC1 specifically binds to SNBEs within the promoters MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 (Figure 5b). The dsDNA probes were synthesized chemically and annealed to produce biotinlabelled SNBEs. According to Figure 5b, the recombinant GST-MaNAC1 fusion protein exhibited a high binding affinity to the SNBE probes of the MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 promoters, leading to retarded bands compared to free probes (Figure 5b). Once the MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 promoter SNBEs were mutant, the recombinant GST-MaNAC1 fusion protein cannot bind efficiently to the labelled probes, respectively (Figure 5b). To investigate the effects of MaNAC1 on MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 transcription in vivo, reporters of 35S-REN-MaCESA6Bpro-LUC and 35S-REN-MaCESA7pro-LUC were produced using the MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 promoters to activate the LUC gene, respectively, and the CaMV 35S promoter was used as an internal expression RENILLA LUCIFERASE (REN) gene. The transient expression assays conducted on Arabidopsis protoplast outcomes indicated that MaNAC1 exhibited a significant capacity to activate the transcription of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 (Figure 5c).

# Discussion

Bananas are distributed in more than 120 tropical and subtropical countries and are recognized for their high carbohydrates content, as well as their rich abundance of dietary fibres, specific vitamins and minerals (Pareek, 2016). Although banana production ranks fourth globally after rice, wheat and maize, only $6 5 \%$ of harvested banana fruits are produced for commercial purposes due to biotic and abiotic stresses (Le et al., 2019). It is vital to explore what triggers cells to perceive signals, execute gene function and then accumulate metabolites to cope with CI. In this study, we found a novel mechanism for thickening the cell wall of banana peel under chilling stress. The firmness of the peel, the thickness of the cell wall and the content of cellulosic glucan responded closely to low temperatures $( 7 ~ ^ { \circ } C )$ . Enhanced expression of most MaCESA genes in banana peel under CI is the key to the accumulation of cellulosic glucan content, and we obtained MaNAC1 from banana fruit, a homologous SWN, which activated the transcription of MaCESA genes under CI. Furthermore, overexpression of MaNAC1 in Arabidopsis led to a thickened cell wall and higher cellulose content of the stems and recovered the defective cell wall and secondary growth of the snd3 mutant. Importantly, MaNAC1 overexpression can improve the cold tolerance of tomato fruits due to thickened cell walls and accumulated cellulose. According to our research results, a working model of the function of MaNAC1 in banana CI was proposed (Figure 7). At standard temperature, MaNAC1 can bind to SNBE sites in the MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 promoters to control their transcription activities. Stable transcription and action of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 maintain normal cell wall architecture. Under chilling stress, CI activates MaNAC1, which in turn promotes the transcription activities of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7, eventually leading to the promotion of cellulose biosynthesis and thickened cell walls (Figure 7). Transgenic tomato fruit overexpressing MaNAC1 resulted in activated expression of SlCESAs and the accumulation of cellulose, and therefore a high tolerance to chilling. The present investigation reveals that MaNAC1 plays a critical role as a positive modulator in the regulation of cellulose metabolism of banana cell walls, which eventually modulates MaCESA6B and MaCESA7 activity as a result of alterations in low temperature. 

NAC TFs regulate most physiological processes in plants, such as developmental processes, stress perception and hormone 

signalling (Jensen et al., 2010). Previously, NAC proteins have been reported to be involved in low-temperature stress. For example, the binding of MdNAC029 to the ACACGT motifs of MdCBF1/MdCBF4 promoters has been observed in apples, inhibiting their expression and hence affecting the cold tolerance (An et al., 2018). Tomato SlNAC1 overexpression in tomato seedlings increased the maximum photochemical efficacy of photosystem II and the oxygen-evolving activities under chilling injury (Ma et al., 2013). Pyrus betulifolia PbeNAC1 induces greater cold tolerance through the interaction between Pbe-DREB1 and PbeDREB2A to activate expression of stress-responsive genes. Furthermore, overexpression of PbeNAC1 in tobacco confers lower levels of ROS, a higher survival rate and higher enzyme activities (Jin et al., 2017). Reports involving fruits under cold stress focus on oxidative metabolism, but it is not yet clear how changes in cell wall cellulose and morphology have been observed. In Musa acuminata, MaNAC1, a direct target gene of MaICE1, exhibits interaction with MaCBF1 and the response to tolerance to cold in banana fruits (Shan et al., 2014). Throughout this investigation, we investigated whether MaNAC1 has a similar biology function to Arabidopsis SND3 (Figure S4), which is the direct target of SND1 and other SWNs. As a transcriptional activator, SND1 functions entirely with NST1 and NST2 to modulate the biosynthesis of the secondary wall in Arabidopsis fibres (Zhong and Ye, 2015). The severe reduction in the thickness of the secondary wall in the Arabidopsis interfascicular fibres and the xylary fibres is as a result of the dominant repression of SND3, and overexpression of SND3 results in a noticeable increase in a significant increase within the tissues of the inflorescence stems (Zhong et al., 2008). However, the mechanism of SND3 that regulates the genes of cellulose biosynthesis is unclear. In this study, our results demonstrated that MaNAC1 functions in secondary wall biosynthesis to respond to CI. MaNAC1 overexpression was able to thicken the secondary wall and grow higher inflorescence stems in Arabidopsis under chilling stress $( 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ (Figure 3 and S4). More importantly, tomato fruits showed greater resistance to cold in transgenic fruits overexpressing MaNAC1 due to thickened cell walls and increased cellulose (Figure 6). These results also propose new directions for horticultural breeding. Furthermore, the results of our study offer novel perspectives on the functions of MaNAC1 in mediating plant secondary walls to respond to low temperatures by directly activating the transcriptional activities of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7. 

The process of cellulose synthesis is facilitated by the cellulose synthase complex (CSC), consisting of CESA proteins on the plasma membrane. CESA proteins are superfluous and conservative in the plant kingdom. Studies in genetic and biochemical fields have demonstrated that CSCs existed within the primary and secondary cell walls (SCW) and consisted of a minimum of three distinct CESA protein isoforms. AtCESA6 is incompletely redundant with AtCESA2, 5, and 9 (Desprez et al., 2007; Persson et al., 2007). Taylor et al. (2003) reported that AtCESA4, 7, and 8 are essential for SCW CSCs, and AtCESA7 exhibits the highest level of class specificity. In the CESA7 protein, the N-terminal regions NT, ZN, VR1, CR1 and VR2 all give effective enhancement in the background of mutants cesa4 or cesa8. The substitution of identical positions from CESA4 and CESA8 into cesa7 does not compensate in Arabidopsis, indicating that CESA7 has a highly restricted position in the CSC (Kumar et al., 2017). # Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The banana fruits at the mature-green stage (M. acuminata, AAA group, cv. Cavendish) were obtained from Chongzuo, Guangxi, China. The tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) and ecotype Columbia-0 (Col-0) of Arabidopsis thaliana were utilized throughout this research. AraShare (https://www.arashare.cn/ index/Product/index.html) was contacted in order to submit an order for the T-DNA insertion mutant snd3 (SAK_201033C). The seeds of wild-type, mutant and transgenic lines were subjected to surface sterilization using $70 \%$ (v/v) ethanol for a duration of 10 min, followed by two washes in $9 5 \%$ (v/v) ethanol. Subsequently, the seeds were subjected to drying on a sterile bench and were subsequently transferred onto a 1/2 Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium. The plates underwent a stratification a process at a temperature of $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for a duration of 3 days. Subsequently, they were relocated to a chamber set at a temperature of $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for germination. The germination process was carried out under long-day circumstances (16 h light and 8 h dark).The light intensity was maintained at $1 2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathrm { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \mathsf { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ , utilizing (Philips F17T8/TL841 17 W bulbs). The seedlings were cultivated in soil and subjected to the aforementioned circumstances for growth. 

# Analysis of phenotypes

To analyse postharvest banana phenotypes under CI, maturegreen bananas were stored at 4 and $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ chambers, respectively. Tomato and Arabidopsis were used for transformation and physiological analysis. To analyse phenotype under CI, 4-weekold Arabidopsis plantlets grown at $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ underwent treatment by putting them in a $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ or a $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ chamber for a duration of 2 weeks. Tomato fruits were collected to store in $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ chamber for 15 days to examine. The experiments were conducted with a sample size of more than three replicates. A one-way analysis of 

variance was utilized to determine whether there was statistically significant variation between the groups. 

# Production of binary construct and transformation

To generate 35Spro-MaNAC1, the coding sequence (CDS) of MaNAC1 comprising a stop codon was amplification from cDNA using a pair of primers. The pENTR-MaNAC1 plasmid was generated by cloning the MaNAC1 gene into the pENTR-TOPO vector (Invitrogen). The 35Spro-MaNAC1 was subsequently produced by LR reactions among pENTR-MaNAC1 and pB7FWG2 (Ghent University). The construct was initially integrated into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (GV3101) and subsequently transferred to Arabidopsis WT and snd3 using the floral dip technique, and tomato using leaf disc method. 

# Gene expression assays

The plant RNA kit (Tiangen, China) was used to extract total RNA for qRT-PCR analysis according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. A $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ reaction volume was utilized to generate cDNA through reverse transcription of the RNA, employing an M-MLV kit (Promega, M170A). The diluted complementary DNA (cDNA) was utilized to employ semi quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (semi-RT-PCR) or quantitative real-time reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) utilizing the specific primers enumerated in Table S1. The study conducted by Yin et al. (2022) utilized SYBR Premix Ex Taq II (TaKaRa) and a Light Cycler480 Real-Time PCR System (Roche, Switzerland) to perform qRT-PCR with four biological replicates. The experimental procedure for the quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) was carried out utilizing the subsequent conditions: initial denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2 min, then 35–40 cycles of amplification consisting of denaturation at $9 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 1 0 \ 5 } ,$ annealing at $5 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 20 s and extension at $7 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 20 s. The relative expression level of all genes was determined utilizing the $2 ^ { - \mathsf { a } \mathsf { a } \mathsf { C } \mathsf { T } }$ (cycle threshold) method. The genes of EIL5A (banana), ACT2 (Arabidopsis) and ELF-a (tomato) were used as internal controls (Qin et al., 2022). 

# Vascular tissue staining and microscopy

Peel tissues from the middle of the fingers were subjected to $7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and collected. Arabidopsis segments were excised from the basal internode of the stems under $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 weeks after planting for 

4 weeks in chambers of $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Tomato tissue was collected after 15 days under $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The experimental procedure involved the use of a Leica VT1000S vibrating-blade microtome to produce slices of $4 0 { - } 6 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ . To achieve this, a support medium consisting of $3 \%$ agarose was employed. The sections were subjected to a $0 . 1 \%$ toluidine blue staining process for a duration of 10 s, followed by a washing step, and then examined under a light microscope. Toluidine blue is a metachromatic dye that can selectively stain certain components of plant cell walls. Subsequently, the sections were subjected to observation using bright field microscopy (BX43F; Olympus, Japan). Vascular tissue structures were eventually identified by mean of microscope software, and ImageJ software was used to measure the third or fourth layer cell walls of vascular tissue from outer to inner. 

# Transactivation analysis using a transient expression system

The transient dual-luciferase reporter system was employed to detect transactivation activity. The cloning of coding regions of MaNAC1 into pGreen II 62-SK was performed through the utilization of the fast clone method. This process resulted in the production of effector constructs, which were generated using the 62-SK-MaNAC1-F and 62-SK-MaNAC1-R primers. The promoter sequences of MaCESA6B and MaCESA7, which were 1388 and 920 bp in length, respectively, were extracted from the genomic DNA of Musa acuminata. These sequences were subsequently amplified and inserted into the pGreen II 0800- LUC vector to produce reporter plasmids, as described by Hellens et al. (2005). Promoters that are amplified possess a quantity of SNBEs that exceeds one. Arabidopsis protoplasts were subjected to co-transformation with various plasmid combinations. After incubation at a temperature of $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for a duration of $1 4 { - } 1 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ under low light conditions, the protoplasts were subjected to lysis. The resulting supernatant was analysed for LUC/REN intensity using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega, Madison, WI, USA) (Yoo et al., 2007). The data are displayed in the form of mean $( \pm \mathsf { S D } )$ of four biological replicates. Significant variations were evaluated using a two-tailed Student’s t-test. 

# Prokaryotic expression and electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSA)

To express MaNAC1 in prokaryotes, we constructed a 35Spro-GST-MaNAC1 expression box. Subsequently, it was converted into E. coli strain BM Rosetta (DE3). The Glutathione-Superflow Resin (TaKaRa) was utilized for the purification of the fusion protein. The study conducted EMSA analysis to identify the binding of MaCESA6B-/MaCESA7-SNBEs to MaNAC1. All specific and mutant probes were designed at 40 bp and labelled with biotin. A commercially available EMSA kit (Thermo Scientific, Rockford, IL) was used and processed according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. The Light Shift chemiluminescent EMSA kit (Thermo Scientific) was utilized for detecting the bands. 

# Cell wall preparation and monosaccharide analysis

To evaluate the complete composition of the cell wall, we collected peel tissues from bananas stored at d 1, d 3 and d 5 under CI $( 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ and Arabidopsis stems in a chamber of $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 2 weeks after planting 4 weeks in chambers of $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Each point contained three biological replicates, and each replicate had more than six plants. Wax and other pigments were eliminated using acetone-ethanol extraction $( \mathsf { V } _ { \mathsf { a c e t o n e } } . \mathsf { V } _ { \mathsf { e t h a n o l } } = 2 . 1 )$ by a Soxhlet 

extractor after grinding into powder (Qin et al., 2020). We also removed the starch from the powder due to the high starch content in the banana with refer to the Sluiter procedure (Sluiter et al., 2021). To completely hydrolyse the powder, an incubation process with $4 \%$ diluted sulfuric acid was performed at a temperature of $1 2 1 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for a duration of 60 min. The hydrolysate was filtered using a $0 . 4 5 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ filter cartridge and detected by highperformance anion exchange chromatography (HPAEC, Metrohm 940, Switzerland) equipped with DionexTM CarbopacTM PA-20 anion exchange chromatography column $( 1 5 0 \times 0 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m m } )$ ) and electrochemical detection. Mobile phases were used $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ NaOH and $7 5 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ sodium acetate, respectively, and liquid flow rate and column temperature were preserved at $0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ and $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , respectively. The semi-quantitative method was used to analyse according to the established standard curve of Dglucose, D-mannose, D-xylose, L-arabinose and L-rhamnose.
