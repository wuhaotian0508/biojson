# Identification of a central regulator of ginkgolide biosynthesis in Ginkgo biloba that integrates jasmonate and light signaling

Ginkgolides are secondary metabolites unique to Ginkgo biloba with the potential to prevent and treat cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases. Although the biosynthetic pathways of ginkgolides have been partly uncovered, the mechanism regulating their biosynthesis is still largely unknown. Here, using multiomic and genetic analyses, we report the identification of a transcription factor, named ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR ASSOCIATED WITH GINKGOLIDE BIOSYNTHESIS (GbEAG), as a critical regulator of ginkgolide biosynthesis. GbEAG is highly expressed in the roots of G. biloba, and its expression is significantly induced by methyl jasmonate (MeJA). Ginkgolide content was significantly increased in roots by overexpressing GbEAG using a “cut-dip-regeneration” system. GbEAG positively regulates ginkgolide biosynthesis by directly binding to the GCC-boxes in the promoter regions of genes involved in the biosynthesis of ginkgolides, such as ISOPENTENYL DIPHOSPHATE ISOMERASE (GbIDI) and CYTOCHROME P450 7005C3 (GbCYP7005C3). GbEAG mediates the jasmonic acid (JA)-activated ginkgolide synthesis through its direct interaction with the JASMONATE ZINC-FINGER INFLORESCENCE MERISTEM DOMAIN 3 (GbJAZ3) repressor. Importantly, we also found that the central light-response regulator ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (GbHY5) mediates light induction of ginkgolide biosynthesis by binding to the G-box in the GbEAG promoter. Our findings provide mechanistic insights into the coordinated regulation of ginkgolide biosynthesis via JA and light signals, with GbEAG as a central regulator in G. biloba, and shed light on the potential to develop ginkgolide-rich varieties through molecular breeding and gene editing.

Ginkgo biloba is a typical relict plant usually regarded as a “living fossil” with an evolutionary status dating back to about 280 My and of high medicinal value ( 1 – 3 ). The ginkgo kernels have been used as crude drugs for thousands of years in China ( 4 ). As platelet-activating factor antagonists, ginkgolides are valuable in preventing and treating cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases ( 5 ). Ten diterpene lactone-type ginkgolides (A, B, C, J, K, L, M, N, P, and Q) and one sesquiterpene lactone-type bilobalide (BB) have been isolated from G. biloba ( 6 ). There are more than 300 types of commercial ginkgo leaf products worldwide, with annual sales of more than 5 billion dollars ( 6 , 7 ). The chemical synthesis of ginkgolides A (GA) and B (GB) has been achieved ( 8 , 9 ). Still, commercial applications of these synthetic methods have not been realized due to their complexity and high costs. In addition, due to the low purity of traditional isolation methods ( 6 ), the output of ginkgolides could not meet the increasing demand for medical applications. Therefore, understanding the molecular mechanisms that control ginkgolide biosynthesis will be invaluable for designing strategies to improve the biosynthesis and yield of ginkgolides. 

Medicinal ginkgolides are majorly extracted from the leaves of G. biloba ; however, the expression of key ginkgolide biosynthetic genes and the intermediate metabolites involved in ginkgolide biosynthesis are mainly detected in the roots ( 10 , 11 ). Previous studies have proposed that ginkgolides are synthesized in roots and transported through the cortex and phloem to leaves for storage ( 10 , 12 – 14 ). In plants, the mevalonate (MVA) pathway and methylerythritol 4-phosphate (MEP) pathway are the main biosynthetic routes for terpene precursors ( 15 ). The ginkgolide biosynthesis is mainly rooted in the MEP pathway in the plastid ( 16 ). It involves three main steps: 1) formation of precursors isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP), 2) levopimaradiene formation by cyclization of geranylgeranyl diphosphate (GGPP), and 3) late hydroxylation to form various kinds of ginkgolides via a series of oxidation, cyclization, and rearrangement mediated by CYTOCHROME P450 (CYP) enzymes ( 11 , 17 ). 

# Significance

Light and jasmonic acid (JA) are essential for plant development and the accumulation of secondary metabolites. However, the mechanisms regulating ginkgolide production by integrating these two signals still need to be discovered. This study identified a transcription factor called GbEAG and its partners, the JA signaling member GbJAZ3 and the light signaling member GbHY5 as key regulators of ginkgolide biosynthesis. Through detailed investigations, we demonstrate that these proteins work together to regulate ginkgolide production in Ginkgo biloba roots. They achieve this by integrating organ-specific JA and light signals, ultimately activating the expression of ginkgolide biosynthetic genes. These findings open broad avenues to improve ginkgolide content in G. biloba to prevent and treat cardiovascular and cerebrovascular diseases.

Jasmonic acid (JA) and its derivative, methyl jasmonate (MeJA), are vital plant hormones that promote secondary metabolite synthesis in plants ( 18 – 21 ). When ginkgo cell suspensions were treated with 0.1 mM MeJA, ginkgolide content was significantly increased ( 22 ). Researchers also demonstrated that treatment with MeJA induced ginkgolide accumulation in ginkgo leaves and roots ( 17 , 23 , 24 ), but the mechanism underlying this process is still poorly understood. The JASMONATE ZINC-FINGER INFLORESCENCE MERISTEM (ZIM)-DOMAIN (JAZ) proteins act as repressors of the JA signaling pathway by binding to transcription factors (TFs) to prevent the activation of JAresponsive genes, thereby affecting the JA-activated biosynthesis of secondary metabolites ( 21 , 25 ). APETALA2/ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR (AP2/ERF) TFs form one of the largest and most abundant TF families in plants, which play a crucial role in regulating diverse biological processes, including the biosynthesis of natural products ( 26 , 27 ). AP2/ERF TF family members have been reported to regulate the biosynthesis of a wide diversity of secondary metabolites ( 28 – 30 ). 

Light is essential for the growth and development of plants, including photomorphogenesis, seed germination, and photoperiod responses ( 31 ). Recently, light has been reported to regulate the biosynthesis and accumulation of secondary metabolites, such as artemisinins and tanshinones in Artemisia annua and Salvia miltiorrhiza , respectively ( 32 – 34 ). In G. biloba , a blue light receptor, CRYPTOCHROME 1 (GbCRY1), was identified to be associated with blue light-induced photomorphogenesis and stomatal opening ( 35 ). However, the effect of light on ginkgolide accumulation has yet to be determined. The ELONGATED HYPOCOTYL 5 (HY5) TF is an essential positive regulator of photomorphogenesis and phytohormone signaling, regulating the expression of several target genes in plants ( 36 – 38 ). HY5 also regulates plant development in responses to light signals by binding to the ACGT cis-acting motifs present in the promoter regions of light-responsive genes, including the G-box (CACGTG), C-box (GACGTC), Z-box (ATACGGT), and A-box (TACGTA) ( 38 ). 

The present study demonstrates a coordinate enhancement of ginkgolide biosynthesis by combining MeJA and light treatments. Moreover, we identified an ETHYLENE RESPONSE FACTOR ASSOCIATED WITH GINKGOLIDE BIOSYNTHESIS (GbEAG) as a critical regulator that integrates JA and light signaling pathways to modulate ginkgolide biosynthesis in G. biloba . GbEAG binds to the promoter regions of the ISOPENTENYL DIPHOSPHATE ISOMERASE (GbIDI ) and GbCYP7005C3 to enhance their expression, leading to the accumulation of ginkgolides. Furthermore, we found that the GbJAZ3 repressor could interact with GbEAG and control its transcriptional activation function in a JA-dependent manner. We also report that GbHY5 activates the expression of GbEAG by binding to the G-box cis-motif in its promoter region. Collectively, our findings unravel a regulatory hub that regulates ginkgolide biosynthesis via JA and light signaling pathways in G. biloba . 

# Results

MeJA Induces the Biosynthesis of Ginkgolides in Roots. Highperformance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis of ginkgo seedlings revealed that ginkgolides, including BB, GA, and GB, accumulated in roots at levels of at least 4-fold higher than in leaves and 1.2- to 2.7-fold higher than in stems, while ginkgolide C (GC) accumulated in roots 1.5- and 3.8-fold higher than in leaves and stems, respectively (Fig. 1A and SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Next, we evaluated the effect of JA on ginkgolide biosynthesis by investigating the impact of MeJA treatment on the accumulation 

of ginkgolides in the roots. We observed that BB, GA, GB, and GC levels significantly increased (by 20 to $4 0 \%$ ) after $^ { 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of treatment with $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ MeJA, compared with those of untreated controls (Fig. 1A and SI Appendix, Fig. S1). Compared with $3 \mathrm { h }$ treatment, the inductive effect of MeJA treatment on ginkgolide accumulation was attenuated at $^ { 6 \mathrm { h } }$ (Fig. 1A). Our results together proved that roots of $G .$ . biloba have higher concentrations of ginkgolides than other organs and that MeJA treatment activates a transient increase in the accumulation of ginkgolides in the roots. 

RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) was then performed for different organs of ginkgo seedlings (roots, stems, and leaves) and MeJA-treated roots (3 h and 6 h) to identify genes potentially involved in regulating ginkgolide biosynthesis in G. biloba . As a result, 5,395 and 4,768 genes showed higher transcript levels in roots than in leaves and stems, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A ). Additionally, 1,611 genes were up-regulated and 1,046 down-regulated in MeJA-treated roots (3 h) (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A and Datasets S1–S3 ). Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathway analysis suggested that many of the up-regulated genes in roots treated with MeJA were enriched in the pathways related to the biosyntheses of secondary metabolites and terpenoid backbone. In contrast, downregulated genes were mainly enriched in starch and sucrose metabolism and plant–pathogen interaction (SI Appendix, Fig. S2 B and C ). Among the up-regulated genes detected from the comparisons “Root vs. Leaf,” “Root vs. Stem,” and “MeJA-Root-3 h vs. Root,” 810 genes were shared among the three comparisons (SI Appendix, Fig. S2D and Dataset S4 ). More importantly, several genes involved in the biosynthetic pathway of ginkgolides, such as GbIDI , GERANYLGER-ANYL DIPHOSPHATE SYNTHASE (GbGGPPS1 ), LEVOPIMA-RADIENE SYNTHASE (GbLPS ), GbCYP7005C1 , GbCYP7005C3 , GbCYP867K1 , GbCYP867E38 , and GbCYP720B31 ( 11 ), were highly expressed in the roots and exhibited a significantly increased expression levels after MeJA treatment ( Fig. 1B and Dataset S5 ). Additionally, the inductive effect of MeJA treatment on ginkgolide biosynthetic pathway-related genes generally peaked at 3 h and subsequently diminished at $^ { 6 \mathrm { h } }$ ( Fig. $1 B$ and Dataset S5 ).

The G. biloba genome contains 199 GbAP2 /ERF genes based on a gene family analysis (Dataset S8 ). The phylogenetic tree analysis revealed that the GbAP2/ERF proteins can be divided into five subfamilies as referred to those of Arabidopsis thaliana (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). The chromosomal location and synteny analysis suggested the occurrence of tandem duplication and fragment duplication events among all GbAP2 /ERF genes in the G. biloba genome (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 A –C ). To further identify GbAP2 /ERF s potentially regulating ginkgolide biosynthesis in G. biloba, a coexpression network was constructed between ginkgolide biosynthetic genes and 67 up-regulated TF-encoding genes among the 810 up-regulated genes identified from the transcriptome analysis (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A ). These results indicated that 21 GbAP2/ERF TF-encoding genes possessed significant correlation with ginkgolide biosynthetic genes (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A and Datasets S9 and S10 ). Next, a heatmap analysis revealed that these 21 GbAP2 /ERF s exhibited high expression levels in the roots, and their expression was strongly induced in roots by MeJA treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S6B ). A phylogenetic tree was further constructed based on the AP2 domain sequence of the 21 candidate GbAP2/ERFs and ERF TFs previously reported to regulate the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites in five plant species, namely A. thaliana, Nicotiana tabacum , S. miltiorrhiza , A. annua, and Gossypium hirsutum ( 45 ), illustrating that eight GbAP2/ERFs were clustered with functional TFs into a branch of the evolutionary tree (SI Appendix, Fig. S6C ). Interestingly, all these eight GbAP2/ERFs belong to the B3 subclass of the ERF subfamily (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). Among them, six candidate GbAP2/ERF genes, with transcript per million mapped reads (FPKM) ${ \scriptstyle > } 1 0$ in the roots, along with eight key genes involved in ginkgolide biosynthesis ( 11 ), were selected for qRT-PCR analysis in five organs of $G .$ . biloba . This analysis showed that one of the six genes, named GbEAG , exhibited the most similar expression pattern to those of the eight key biosynthetic genes across the five organs (SI Appendix, Figs. $\bar { S 6 D }$ and S7 ). Thus, GbEAG was selected as the most promising candidate to regulate ginkgolide biosynthesis in G. biloba . 

GbEAG Directly Activates the Transcription of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 by Binding to Their Promoters. To better understand the putative regulatory role of GbEAG in ginkgolide accumulation, we investigated whether GbEAG directly binds to the promoter of genes involved in ginkgolide biosynthesis. Previous studies demonstrated that ERF-B3 proteins bind to GCC boxes (AGCCGCC/GCCGCC) in the promoters of their target genes (29, 46). Bioinformatic analysis showed that the promoter regions of GbIDI (Gb_15689) (47) and GbCYP7005C3 (Gb_04669/ evm.model.chr5.798) (47, 48) contained one GCC-box (Fig. 2 A and $B$ and SI  Appendix, Figs.  S8–S10). Furthermore, both the cloned promoter sequence (PQ356346) of GbIDI, spanning 1,489-bp, and the cloned promoter sequence (PQ356347) of GbCYP7005C3, encompassing 1,218-bp, contained the GCCbox (SI  Appendix, Figs.  S8–S10). The yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) assay was then used to test the direct binding of the cloned fulllength GbEAG (OP604051) (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S11B) to the promoter fragments containing the GCC-box of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3. Results of the Y1H assay showed that GbEAG bound to the GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoters to activate their gene expression in yeast (Fig. 2 A and B). To further confirm the physical binding of GbEAG to the GCC-box in the promoter 

regions of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 genes, electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs) were performed using His-GbEAG and a DNA fragment of the promoter of GbIDI or GbCYP7005C3 containing the respective GCC-box. The assays showed that the His-GbEAG directly binds to the GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoters in vitro (Fig. 2 C and $D$ ). When the His-GbEAG was replaced by a His peptide, the binding signals could not be detected (Fig.  2 $C$ and $D$ ). In addition, when a cold competitor probe (100-fold) or His antibody was added, the intensity of the shifted band was significantly decreased (Fig. 2 C and $D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S12 A and B). Competition with a mutant cold probe (100- fold) lacking the GCC-box did not affect the binding of GbEAG to the WT probe (Fig. 2 $C$ and $D$ and SI Appendix, Fig. S12 A and B), suggesting that GbEAG directly binds to the GCC box in the GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoters. Dual-luciferase assays were then carried out to test the transactivation function of GbEAG on the transcription of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3. The GbEAG fused to the GREEN FLUORESCENT PROTEIN (GFP)-encoding gene was cloned into an overexpression vector under the control of the 35S promoter as an effector, while the GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoter regions were fused with the FIREFLY LUCIFERASE (LUC) gene as reporter constructs (Fig.  2E). The RENILLA LUCIFERASE (REN) gene was used in the assay for normalizing the potential background caused by the transfection efficiency of Agrobacterium (Fig. 2E). The LUC luminescence detection results in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaves indicated that GbEAG activated the expression of the LUC reporter directed by either GbIDI or GbCYP7005C3 promoter (Fig.  2 $F$ and $G _ { \iota }$ ). Determination of LUC/REN relative activity in N. benthamiana further validated GbEAG as a transcriptional activator of GbIDI (by 1.4-fold activation) and GbCYP7005C3 (by 8.5-fold activation) (Fig. 2H). Together, these results demonstrated that GbEAG positively regulates the expression of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 by directly binding to their promoters. 

GbEAG Is Localized in Nuclei and Has a Transcriptional Activation Domain in Its N-terminal Region. To analyze the subcellular localization of GbEAG, its complete coding sequence was fused to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end of the GFP under the control of the 35S promoter. Confocal microscopy showed that the GbEAG-GFP fusion protein was exclusively localized in the nuclei, while GFP alone showed fluorescence signals in both the cytoplasm and nuclei (SI Appendix, Fig. S13A). 

To determine whether GbEAG harbors a transcriptional activation domain, the complete coding sequence, the C-terminal region (lacking the N-terminal domains), and the N-terminal region (lacking the AP2 and the C-terminal domains) of GbEAG were separately fused to the GAL4 DNA binding domain and tested for the transcriptional activation function in yeast (SI Appendix, Fig. S13B ). Results of yeast growth and $\beta$ -galactosidase activity assays showed that the complete GbEAG and the N-terminal half of the protein, but not the C-terminal part containing the AP2 and C-terminal domains, could direct transcriptional activation (SI Appendix, Fig. S13C ). These results demonstrated that the N-terminal region of GbEAG has a transcriptional activation function. In addition, amino acid sequence alignment analysis indicated that the AP2 domain of GbEAG has high sequence similarities to those of its homologs from A. thaliana , N. tabacum , A. annua , and G. hirsutum (SI Appendix, Fig. S14 ).

JAZ proteins are vital repressor factors in the JA signaling pathway (19), and the expression levels of several GbJAZ genes were increased by MeJA treatment in this study (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A and Dataset S9).

We hypothesized that GbEAG could be involved in the JA signaling pathway by interaction with JAZ protein(s) due to its responsiveness to MeJA. Thus, to investigate the interaction between GbEAG and GbJAZ protein(s), the coding sequence of six ginkgo JAZ proteins (49) was used to test their potential interaction with GbEAG in the yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) system. Due to the relatively strong transcriptional activation function of the GbEAG N-terminal domain, a truncated N-terminal version of GbEAG (GbEAGΔN) was used as the bait protein, while the GbJAZs as the prey proteins. The Y2H assay showed that GbEAG 

had a robust interaction with GbJAZ3 and a weak interaction with GbJAZ11, but it had no interaction with GbJAZ 1, 4, 9, or 14 (SI Appendix, Fig. S15A). Moreover, a pull-down assay using His-GbEAG and GST-GbJAZ3 fusion proteins also proved that GbEAG interacted with GbJAZ3 in vitro (SI Appendix, Fig. S15B). The split-LUC complementation assay in tobacco leaves was further used to verify the interaction between GbEAG and GbJAZ3 in planta. The coexpression of nLUC-GbEAG and cLUC-GbJAZ3 produced a fourfold higher luminescence signal than the negative controls (SI Appendix, Fig. S15C). Additionally, GbJAZ3–GFP 

protein also exhibited a strong nuclear localization (SI Appendix, Fig. S13A). Moreover, we assessed the inhibitory effect of GbJAZ3 on GbEAG using the pGbIDI::LUC and pGbCYP7005C3::LUC reporter genes. When GbEAG and GbJAZ3 were coexpressed in tobacco, GbJAZ3 inhibited the GbEAG-induced activation of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoters (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S16 A–C). And the inhibitory effect of GbJAZ3 on GbEAG was alleviated upon MeJA application, leading to the activation of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 promoters by GbEAG (SI Appendix, Fig. S16 A–C).

Light and JA Are Coordinately Involved in the Biosynthesis of Ginkgolides in G. biloba. Ginkgo prefers warm and moist environments, and the accumulation of secondary metabolites in G. biloba is related to photoperiod (51). Several light-responsive cis-motifs are present in the GbEAG promoter (SI  Appendix, Fig. S20A), suggesting that GbEAG may be regulated by light. Treatment of ginkgo plants with light showed that light partly promoted the accumulation of ginkgolides in roots (Fig.  4A). Furthermore, we tested the effect of MeJA on the accumulation of ginkgolides in light- and dark-grown plants. Results showed that MeJA had a greater effect on the accumulation of ginkgolides in roots of light-grown plants than in those of dark-grown plants (Fig.  4A). This coordinated effect was also observed in the expression of genes involved in ginkgolide biosynthesis (Fig. 4B). Since HY5 is a central regulator of light responses and secondary metabolism (38), we tested the effect of MeJA on the expression of GbEAG and GbHY5 in roots of ginkgo plants grown under light or dark condition. After a full-light or full-dark period followed by a MeJA treatment, the results showed that the effect of MeJA on the expression of GbEAG (Fig. 4C) and GbHY5 (Fig. 4D) was higher in roots of light-grown plants than in those of dark-grown plants. Specifically, the expression level of GbEAG was fourfold higher in the roots of light-grown ginkgo seedlings after $3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ of MeJA treatment (Fig. 4C), while a threefold higher expression was observed for GbHY5 in the same situation (Fig. 4D). Fluorescence microscopy signals showed that the GbHY5-GFP fusion protein was localized in the nucleus (SI Appendix, Fig. S20B). GbHY5 was highly expressed in young stems and leaves, followed by fibrous roots (SI  Appendix, Fig.  S20C). GbHY5 has a high homology with HY5 proteins from different plant species (SI  Appendix, Fig. $S 2 0 D$ ). These results showed that JA and light signals coordinately regulate the expression of GbEAG and GbHY5; and thus, the biosynthesis of ginkgolides, perhaps through the concerted action of GbEAG and GbHY5. 

GbHY5 Positively Regulates the Ginkgolide Biosynthesis by Activating the Expression of GbEAG. To prove the abovementioned hypothesis, we first demonstrated the positive regulatory role of GbHY5 in ginkgolide biosynthesis by conducting overexpression and knockdown experiments of GbHY5 in ginkgo roots 

(Fig.  4 E–H and SI Appendix, Fig.  S21 A–F). Overexpression of GbHY5 significantly up-regulated the expression levels of several ginkgolide biosynthetic pathway-related genes, resulting in enhanced accumulation of ginkgolides in roots of GbHY5- overexpressing lines (SI Appendix, Fig. $S 2 1 C$ and Fig.  4E). Conversely, knockdown of GbHY5 had a negative effect on the expression of ginkgolide biosynthetic genes and markedly reduced the accumulation of ginkgolides in roots of the GbHY5-silenced lines (SI Appendix, Fig. $\bar { S } 2 1 F$ and Fig.  4F). Furthermore, the overexpression and knockdown of GbHY5 led to increased and decreased expression of GbEAG, respectively (Fig. 4 $G$ and $H )$ ). These results demonstrate a positive correlation between the accumulation of ginkgolides and the expression levels of GbHY5, GbEAG, and ginkgolide biosynthetic genes, with GbHY5 acting upstream of the pathway followed by GbEAG and then ginkgolide biosynthetic genes. To further test the molecular mechanism of crosstalk between light and JA signals, we then used dual-luciferase assays to test whether GbHY5 could activate the transcription of GbEAG. We found that when a pEAG::LUC construct was Agrobacterium-infiltrated together with GbHY5-GFP fusion, a 2.5-fold higher level of LUC signal was detected, indicating that GbHY5 indeed activated the expression of GbEAG (SI Appendix, Fig. S21G and Fig. 4I). Further dual-luciferase detection confirmed the activation function of GbHY5 on the transcription of GbEAG by determining the LUC/REN activity ratio (Fig. 4J). The GbEAG promoter region was then analyzed to identify potential HY5 binding sites, and three G-boxes were identified (SI Appendix, Figs. S11 and S21H). Y1H assays were conducted using the fulllength of GbHY5 to test its binding sites and direct activation of GbEAG transcription. The results showed that GbHY5 bound to G-box 2 in the GbEAG promoter to activate its expression in yeast (Fig. 4K). To confirm the binding of GbHY5 to the G-box 2 of the GbEAG promoter in vitro, EMSA was carried out using the full length of GbHY5 protein and GbEAG promoter fragment containing G-box  2 (SI Appendix, Fig.  S21I). Results showed that GbHY5 directly binds to G-box 2 in the GbEAG promoter (Fig. 4L). When the His-GbHY5 was replaced by His peptide, the binding signals disappeared (Fig.  4L). In addition, when a cold competitor probe (100-fold) or His antibody was added, the intensity of the shifted band significantly decreased (Fig. 4L and SI Appendix, Fig. S22). Competition with a mutant probe lacking the G-box did not significantly affect the binding of His-GbHY5 to the WT probe (Fig.  4L and SI  Appendix, Fig.  S22). Results from both assays showed that GbHY5 binds to the G-box 2 in the promoter of GbEAG to activate its transcription. 

# Discussion

Ginkgolides, a unique type of natural terpenoid products, are effective for treating cardiovascular diseases ( 5 ). The biosynthetic pathway of ginkgolides is complex, with different organs serving as synthesis and storage sites ( 11 ). Ginkgo is a woody gymnosperm plant species with a complex genetic background and faces difficulties in genetic transformation ( 11 , 47 , 48 ). Therefore, although molecular studies of the transcriptional regulation related to ginkgolide biosynthesis are quite urgent, they are extremely challenging. Here, taking advantage of the latest technologies, we showed that i) GbEAG is a key transcriptional regulator of the ginkgolide biosynthetic pathway, which integrates JA and light signaling to coordinate the production of ginkgolides, and ii) a modified cutdip-regeneration system bypassing tissue culture has been successfully applied to establish a transgenic root system in $G$ . biloba .

GbIDI is localized in both cytoplasm and endoplasmic reticulum, as well as peroxisomes (SI Appendix, Fig. S23 ), and controls the metabolic flow in the MEP pathway and MVA pathway ( 52 ). GbCYP7005C3 is located in the endoplasmic reticulum (SI Appendix, Fig. S23 ), and it is the first skeletal modification CYP450 enzyme in the ginkgolide biosynthetic pathway ( 11 ). GbEAG protein, as a nuclear-localized TF, can bind to the promoter regions of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 and activate their transcription in the nucleus. Subsequently, the protein synthesis of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 transcripts is enhanced in the cytoplasm and/ or endoplasmic reticulum, thereby promoting the coordinated biosynthesis of ginkgolides ( Fig. 5 ). Third, the overexpression and knockdown of GbEAG in roots increased and decreased the accumulation of ginkgolides, respectively, by influencing the transcriptional activation of GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 ( Figs. 3 and 5 ). GbEAG is a typical TF of the ERF-B3 subfamily (SI Appendix, Figs. S4 and S6C ). TF members of the ERF-B3 subfamily are usually involved in the transcriptional regulation of biosynthetic genes involved in plant secondary metabolite production. For instance, the overexpression of AaERF1 /2 in A. annua plants increased the transcript levels of genes encoding key biosynthetic enzymes and the contents of artemisinin ( 28 ). Light signal is a crucial part of plant growth and development ( 31 ). Here, we also provided phenotypic and biochemical evidence that light plays a vital role in the biosynthesis of ginkgolides ( Fig. 4 $A { - } D )$ ). Under darkness, the biosynthesis and accumulation of ginkgolides were lower than in plants grown in the presence of light ( Fig. 4 A and B ). Reports have demonstrated the important role of HY5 in the light signaling pathway ( 36 – 38 ). We suspected that GbHY5 could be important in regulating light-dependent ginkgolide biosynthesis in ginkgo plants. Indeed, the overexpression and knockdown of GbHY5 in ginkgo roots resulted in increased and decreased accumulations of ginkgolides ( Fig. 4 E and $F _ { . }$ ), as a consequence of the increase and decreased expression of several key ginkgolide biosynthetic genes (SI Appendix, Fig. S21 A–F ), respectively. These results indicate the positive regulatory role of GbHY5 in light-dependent ginkgolide biosynthesis. These findings and results previously reported in other species suggest that HY5 generally regulates the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. For example, AaHY5 was shown to regulate artemisinin biosynthesis in A. annua ( 56 ), and MdHY5 was reported to play a vital role in anthocyanin accumulation in Malus domestica ( 57 ). Furthermore, multiple studies have consistently reported the translocation of HY5 from shoots to roots ( 58 , 59 ), thereby orchestrating the expression of downstream genes in roots to synchronize the growth of both shoots and roots. GbHY5 was highly expressed in leaves and young stems, but only to a low level in fibrous roots (SI Appendix, Fig. S20C ). This result indicated that GbHY5 might shuttle from shoots to roots to regulate ginkgolide biosynthesis in the roots. 

Results of this study collectively suggested that the concerted action of light and JA signals is important in regulating the metabolism of ginkgolides and perhaps other secondary metabolites in ginkgo and other plant species. In support of this finding, JA was shown to regulate artemisinin biosynthesis in A. annua in a light-dependent manner ( 32 ). JA and light signals were integrated to modulate anthocyanin synthesis by regulating the expression of SmMYB5 in eggplant (Solanum melongena ) ( 60 ). In this study, the concerted action of light and JA signals in regulating ginkgolide accumulation in ginkgo plants was explained by a series of molecular and genetic experiments. First, we showed that JA could increase the expression levels of ginkgolide biosynthetic genes and promote the accumulation of ginkgolides in a light-dependent manner ( Fig. 4 A and $B$ ). Importantly, the combined effect of light and MeJA on the biosynthesis of ginkgolides was dependent on GbEAG and GbHY5 ( Fig. 4 $C$ and $D$ ). Next, protein–DNA interaction results showed that GbHY5 activated the expression of GbEAG by directly binding to the G-box present in its promoter ( Fig. 4 I –L ). These results demonstrated that GbHY5 positively regulates GbEAG expression ( Fig. 4 G and H ), thereby enhancing the expression levels of ginkgolide biosynthetic genes (SI Appendix, Fig. S21 A–F ), to activate the ginkgolide biosynthesis in ginkgo plants ( Fig. 5 ). 

The lag in the genetic transformation of ginkgo has resulted in slow progress in the study of related functional genes. Recently, a “cut-dip-budding (CDB) gene delivery system” was developed for genetic transformation in medicinal plants instead of tissue culture or hairy root transformation ( 50 , 61 ). We established a transgenic root system in $G .$ . biloba to initiate studies on the molecular mechanisms that regulate ginkgolide biosynthesis by developing a modified cut-dip-regeneration system bypassing the use of tissue 

culture (SI Appendix, Fig. S17 ). Using this transgenic root regeneration system, we proved the positive regulatory roles of the GbEAG and GbHY5 in ginkgolide biosynthesis ( Figs. $3 A { - } D$ and 4 E and G ). This finding presented herein offers technical support for the functional gene validation and metabolic engineering of ginkgo varieties with high levels of ginkgolides. 

Taken together, a crosstalk model, with GbEAG serving as the central hub that integrates JA and the light signaling pathways, is proposed ( Fig. 5 ). When JA stimulates ginkgo seedlings, GbEAG becomes active due to the JA-mediated degradation of GbJAZ3. Subsequently, GbEAG binds to the promoters of ginkgolide biosynthetic genes like GbIDI and GbCYP7005C3 and activates their transcription, thereby enhancing ginkgolide biosynthesis. On the other hand, under light conditions, GbHY5 binds to the promoter of GbEAG and activates its expression, promoting ginkgolide biosynthesis. In the presence of JA and light, GbEAG is activated by GbHY5 and released from the inhibition of GbJAZ3 simultaneously, thereby resulting in the coordinated promotion of ginkgolide biosynthesis in ginkgo roots. The identification of the central role of GbEAG in integrating the hormonal and environmental signals that regulate the biosynthesis of ginkgolide intermediates opens the possibility of searching for GbEAG alleles present in the genetic diversity of G. biloba or designing promoter bashing strategies to create GbEAG alleles to optimize the accumulation of ginkgolides in G. biloba . Our results also provide a reference for studying the interaction between light and hormone signals in regulating secondary metabolites in medicinal plants.
