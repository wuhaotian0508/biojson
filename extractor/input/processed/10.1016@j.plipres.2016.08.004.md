# a b s t r a c t

Diacylglycerol lipases (DGLs) mainly catalyze “on-demand” biosynthesis of bioactive monoacylglycerols (MAGs) with different long fatty acyl chains, including 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG), 2-linoleoylglycerol (2-LG), 2-oleoylglycerol (2-OG) and 2-palmitoylglycerol (2-PG). Enzymatic characterization of DGLs, their expression and distribution, and functional features has been elucidated from microorganisms to mammals in some extent. In mammals, biosynthesis, degradation and metabolism of these bioactive lipids intertwine and form a complicated biochemical pathway to affect the mammal neuromodulation of central nervous system and also other physiological processes in most peripheral organs and non-nervous tissue cells, and yet we still do not know if the neuromodulatory role of mammal DGL and MAGs is similar to invertebrates. Tracing the evolutionary history of DGLs from microorganisms to vertebrates will be an essential method to infer DGL and MAG research in organisms. In this review, we give an exhaustive explanation of the ancestral origin, divergence and evolutionary pattern through systemic searching of DGL orthologs in different species. Finally, we also summarize our recent work on the structural and functional studies of DGL in order to explore usage of DGLs in industry and the development of inhibitors for clinical intervention.

# 1. Introduction

Diacylglycerol lipase (DGL, E.C. 3.1.1.34) has a strict substrate selection utilizing diacylglycerol (DAG) but not triacylglycerol (TAG) to produce monoacylglycerol (MAG). DGLs are wide spread in bacteria, fungi, plant, and animals with different substrate selectivities. Microorganism DGL (also named mono- and diacylglycerol lipase, MDGL) can catalyze the hydrolysis of DAG and MAG to produce free fatty acids, MAG, or glycerol, while mammalian DGLs have specific selectivity of DAGs and exhibit very little monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL), phospholipase A1/A2, triacylglycerol lipase (TAGL), and anandamide amidase activities. DGLs can produce different long chain 2-acylglycerols, including 2-arachidonoyl-glycerol (2-AG), 2- linoleoyl-glycerol (2-LG), 2-oleoyl-glycerol (2-OG) and 2-palmitoyl-glycerol (2-PG). The most frequently reported function of 2-AG is that stimulating the eCB system (ECS) through activating cannabinoid receptor 1 (CB1) and CB2 receptors. 2-AG is the most abundant endocannabinoid (eCB) and it shares a pharamacophore with tetrahydrocannabinol $( \Delta ^ { 9 } \mathrm { - T H C ) }$ , which is also the major psychoactive component of cannabis plant. Cannabis plant has been recorded as medicine in Chinese pharmacopoeia since 2727 BCE [1] and was mainly intended for use as a psychoactive drug [2]. 2-OG has been postulated to be a GPR119 agonist that signals through GLP-1 secretion from human intestine [3]. Thus, DGL-produced MAGs are agonists of cannabinoid or non-cannabinoid receptors to activate complex physiological responses.

DGL isoforms have a conserved lipase_3 domain from invertebrates to vertebrates. Mammalian DGL isoforms have a 4TM domain with a short cytoplasmic N-terminal sequence, followed by a canonical $\alpha / \beta$ hydrolase domain that harbors the catalytic activity (also known as catalytic domain). The catalytic domain is followed by a considerable carboxyl-terminal domain in the case of DGL-alpha but not for DGL-beta [4]. DGLs in different species have diverse physiological and pathological functions. Fungal SMG1 from Malassezia globosa functions in mediating the breakdown of sebaceous lipids and release of irritating free fatty acids in the etiology of human dandruff and seborrhoeic dermatitis that are common hyper-proliferative scalp disorders [5]. The activity of fly inactivation no after-potential E (INAE) is required to couple photo-excitation of rhodopsin to the opening of TRP/TRPL channels in photoreceptors [6]. In mammals, DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms mainly play crucial roles in inducing diverse effects in the nervous system or other physiological systems [7–9].

After a short introduction to the field, we will summarize current knowledge on the enzymatic characterization and physiological importance of DGL in different organisms. We will address the evolutionary history of DGL focusing on distribution of DGL isoforms within the three domains of life on the planet. The growing number of sequenced genomes of organisms has led to identification of an increasing number of potential DGL and/or DGL-like sequences and it would exceed the scope of this review to refer to all of them. Instead, we selected frequently employed model organisms, which represented different stages in the evolution of life on earth and searched their genomes for putative DGL sequences. Since for most of these sequences no functional data were currently available, we used our knowledge on structural biology of these enzymes to draw functional conclusions. Then, we will summarize the current knowledge on structural biology of DGL. For this purpose, we evaluated the public databases summarizing structure/function relationship of DGL and DGL-dependent MAGs signaling. Here again, the databases are rapidly growing and it is not possible to consider all in this review. In this sense, the review is somewhat selective and we are not aiming at completeness. However, we would like to apologize to those distinguished colleagues whose work we have not found space to describe and to reference.

# 2. Enzymatic characterization and distribution of DGLs

# 2.1. Enzymatic characterization of DGLs

Substrate selectivity is a major difference between mammalian and possibly vertebrate DGLs and those from invertebrates and

microorganisms. Microorganism MDGL can hydrolyze DAG and MAG to produce free fatty acids, MAG, or glycerol. Mammalian DGLs have a stricter substrate selection utilizing DAG but not TAG and MAG [4] (Table 1). In fungi, Penicillium camembertii lipase (PCL) synthesized MAG as a main product and some diglyceride, but not triglyceride, from oleic acid and glycerol with no positional specificity [10] (Table 1). In Drosophila, the inaE gene encodes two protein isoforms, INAE-A and INAE-D, by alternative splicing. The D form has 20-fold higher lipase activity than the A form. The INAE isoforms are both DGLs which are highly preferential for hydrolysis at the sn-1 position, with the sn-1 selectivity being about ten-fold higher than that of sn-2 [6]. Human DGL isoforms have a three- to eight-fold selectivity for the sn-1 over the sn-2 position of DAGs [4]. PCL is efficient in catalyzing the hydrolysis of long-chain (C18) unsaturated and medium-chain (C10 and 12) saturated fatty acids on sn-1 position of DAGs [10]. A putative DGL-like enzyme (MgMDL2) from Malassezia globose exhibits a preference for medium chain-length esters with highest activity toward $p$ -nitrophenyl caprylate [11]. Mammalian DGLs can also produce different long chain 2-acylglycerols, including 2-AG, 2-LG, 2-OG and 2-PG. DGL-beta appears to prefer sn-1-oleoyl-2 acyl-glycerol with linoleic ≥ oleic $>$ arachidonic $>$ stearic acid on the 2 position, whereas DGL-alpha appears to work equally well with all fatty acids [4].

As for other biochemical features, the effects of temperature on lipase activity have been studied thoroughly. Mammalian DGLs lipase assays have always been performed at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ [4]. In contrast, microorganism DGLs have a wider range of optimal temperatures to adapt to the survival circumstances, ranging from 15 to $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Table 2). Recombinant MgMDL2 was optimal at $1 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and it kept over $5 0 \%$ of relative activity at $5 ^ { \circ } C .$ MgMDL2 retained over $8 0 \%$ of initial activity after incubation at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $2 . 5 \mathrm { h }$ , but it was not stable at $5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ [11]. The optimal temperatures of SMG1, AOL, PcMDL, and PCL are $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , $3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , and $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ respectively (Table 2). The optimal temperature of these cold or mesophilic enzymes will hinder their wide usage in industry. Searching for novel enzymes with higher optimal temperatures or alteration of enzymes to improve their temperature tolerance will be important in developing novel DGLs for industrial usage.

# 2.2. Spatial expression of DGLs

DGL-produced MAGs are the “on-demand” intermediate products of acylglycerides, thus, spatial expression pattern of DGLs will partly demonstrate their physiological functions. In Drosophila, the INAE protein is highly expressed in the eye but also occurs throughout the head. In photoreceptors, anti-INAE antibody labeling occurs as punctate staining scattered throughout the photoreceptor cytoplasm. Occasionally, some of the puncta are found within the rhabdomeres, indicating that some DGLs enter the rhabdomeres [6].

# 3. Distribution and physiological function of DGL-produced MAGs in animals

# 3.1. Distribution of DGL-produced MAG in animals

2-AG is widely distributed in different organisms. In the primitive diblastic Metazoa, 2-AG is found in cnidaria of Hydra polyps [22]. Among the primitive triblastic Metazoa, 2-AG is present in protostomia such as the annelid Hirudo medicinalis (H. medicinalis) [23], the insects Drosophila melanogaster (D. melanogaster) and Apis mellifera (A. mellifera) [24], the tick Amblyomma americanum (A. americanum) [25], in deuterostomia such as Ciona intestinalis, and chordates [26]. In detail, Hydra polyps contain 11.2 nmol/g of 2-AG from fresh tissue, comparable to the levels described previously for mammalian brain [22]. The presence of 2-AG $( 1 4 7 . 4 \ p m o l / \mathrm { g } )$ has also been reported in the central nervous system (CNS) of leech H. medicinalis [23]. The amount of leech 2- AG is lower than that found in vertebrates and other invertebrates, but still seemingly more abundant than anandamide, analogous to data from other animals. 2-AG has been identified in nematode samples. The most reported feature of 2-AG is a ligand of eCB receptors. In insects, these species lack known CB receptors. 2-AG concentrations in different strains of Caenorhabditis elegans (C. elegans) is 12–79 pmol/mg of protein, suggesting that 2-AG in C. elegans may be the ligand of other receptors (Table 3). It has also been reported that no 2-AG was detected in the fat-3 mutant of C. elegans, which lacks the necessary enzymes to produce arachidonic acid (AA), the fatty acid precursor of these eCBs [27]. The whole body of D. melanogaster and brain tissue of A. mellifera contain $4 6 2 \ : \mathrm { p m o l / g }$ and $2 1 2 \mathrm { p m o l / g }$ of 2-AG, respectively [24]. 2-AG has also been identified in salivary glands of the tick A. americanum, with the 2-AG level in the salivary glands of partially fed ticks being considerably higher than in replete females [25]. In sea squirt Ciona intestinalis (C. intestinalis), 2-AG is five-fold more abundant than anandamide. 2- AG was the most abundant eCB in stomach and intestine and least abundant in heart and ovaries $( 2 . 7 { - } 5 0 . 6 \mathrm { n m o l / g }$ of lipid extract) of sea squirt [28] (Table 3). The concentrations of 2-AG detected in insect tissues are lower than concentrations found in mammals (Table 3). 2-AG is derived from arachidonate-containing DAGs, whose abundance relies on the presence of AA on the sn-2 position of phospholipids. Drosophila lacks

C20 and C22 PUFAs [29]. Therefore, the lower concentrations of 2-AG in insects may reflect the paucity of AA that is esterified in phospholipids in insects compared with mammals and aquatic invertebrates (sea urchins, mollusks, and Hydra).

In mammals, 2-AG can be isolated from various tissues. Basal levels of 2-AG in the brain and gut are very high (in the range of $\mathrm { \ n M / g }$ fresh weight). Brain 2-AG concentrations are approximately 200-fold higher than those of N-arachidonoyl ethanolamine, another important eCB [30]. In detail, the brainstem, hippocampus, striatum, limbic forebrain, and medulla exhibit the highest levels of 2-AG, while the diencephalon, mesencephalon, cerebellum and cortex the lowest [30]. 2-AG can also be detected in the peripheral nervous system, such as sciatic nerve, lumbar spinal cord, lumbar dorsal root ganglion, and even rat and bovine retina [31]. The 2-AG levels in the hypothalamus are influenced by short-term fasting (increased levels) and feeding (reduced levels), as well as by the composition of the diet [32,33]. 2-AG levels in mouse intestine are high enough to elicit receptor activation [34]. Intestinal 2-AG levels have the features of regional variation and organ-specific actions; $4 4 . 1 \ \mathrm { n m o l / g }$ of 2-AG level in the ileum compared with $1 8 . 7 \ \mathrm { n m o l / g }$ in the colon [34,35] (Table 3). Regional differences in 2-AG level in the gastrointestinal tract may reflect a difference in the functional activity of 2- AG in the small and large intestine. In short, the 2-AG levels in brain and gut are not stable, with the amounts varying according to the states of satiety, and in conditions of diarrhea, emesis and inflammation [17,32, 33,36].

There are several congeners of 2-AG in animals with different longchain fatty acids. In Hydra polyps, 2-AG is accompanied by several congeners, including the C16:0, C18:0, C18:1, C20:3, C20:5, C22:5 and C22:6 analogues of 2-AG [22]. Three congeners that have been isolated from the CNS of fruit fly are 2-LG, 2-OG, and 2-PG. 2-LG and 2-OG were detected in lipid extracts from the whole body of third instar (LIII) Oregon-RS larvae of fruit fly [37]. Concentrations of 2-LG in individual brain regions of rats ranged from 0.1–4.3 nmol/g [38] (Table 3). In mice, 2-AG as well as 2-LG and 2-PG were detected in brain, spleen and gut [39]. The levels of 2-LG and 2-OG detected in murine hippocampus were 0.5 and $1 7 . 6 \ \mathrm { n m o l / g } ,$ respectively [40] (Table 3).

# 3.2. Biosynthesis and degradation of DGL-produced MAGs

The biosynthesis and degradation of DGL-produced MAG can be via several different pathways. The most well-elucidated biosynthesis pathway is that for the biosynthesis of 2-AG, which mainly originates from AA-containing membrane phospholipids such as phosphatidyl inositol. The 2-AG synthesis involves the PLC- $\cdot \mathrm { \beta }$ -mediated hydrolysis of membrane phosphatidyl inositol 4,5-bisphosphate $\left( \mathrm { P I P } _ { 2 } \right)$ to produce inositol 1,4,5-triphosphate $\left( \mathrm { { I P } } _ { 3 } \right)$ and DAG, which may be subsequently converted to 2-AG by DGL [41,42]. 2-AG can also be biosynthesized by PLD, phospholipase or other enzymes from PC as the substrate. The hydrolysis of 2-AG is tightly regulated by multiple enzymes. The main fate of 2- AG degradation is the production of AA and glycerol, by MAGL or other enzymes (Fig. 1). The details of biosynthesis and degradation of 2-AG have reviewed in detail by Iannotti et al. (2016) [43]. In addition, 2-AG metabolism shares enzymatic machinery with eicosanoid pathways. It includes the biosynthetic enzymes of cyclooxygenases (COXs) [43], lipoxygenases (LOXs) and other oxygenases to metabolize 2-AG to produce glyceryl ester lipid mediators. The glyceryl esters of the lipid mediators formed from the COX pathway and LOX pathway include prostaglandin $\mathsf { D } _ { 2 }$ glyceryl ester $( \mathrm { P G D } _ { 2 } – \mathrm { G }$ ), prostaglandin $\mathsf { E } _ { 2 }$ glyceryl ester $\mathrm { P G E } _ { 2 }$ -G), 11-hydroxyeicosatetraenoic acid glyceryl ester (11- HETE-G),15-HETE-G, and 12-hydroxyheptadecatrienoic acid glyceryl ester (12-HHT-G) (Fig. 1), as summarized in several reviews [44,45]. These lipid substrates (2-AG and AA) and metabolites are at the crossroads of several metabolic pathways and are recruited by neurons as neurotransmitters [46,47]. # 3.3. Physiological functions of DGL-produced MAGs in animals

There are several major endogenous DGL-produced MAGs, including 2-AG, 2-LG, 2-OG, and 2-PG. 2-AG shares a similar pharamacophore to that of THC and modulates a diverse set of physiological processes, including synaptic depression, food intake, nausea and emesis, gastric secretion and gastro-protection, GI motility, ion transport, visceral sensation, intestinal inflammation and cell proliferation in the gut through activation of receptors including CB receptors and other receptors [3,12,48–51]. The biological activity of 2-AG can be increased by endogenous MAGs, which alone show no significant activity in any of the tests employed. For example, 2-LG significantly inhibited the inactivation of 2-AG in neuronal and basophilic cells [40]. MAGL activity can be inhibited by shorter homologues of 2-AG (2-LG and 2-OG), which have affinities for MAGL similar to 2-AG [52]. This effect (described as ‘entourage effect’) might represent a novel route for molecular regulation of eCB activity [39]. These esters significantly potentiate behavior in mice caused by 2-AG, including motor behavior, immobility on a ring, analgesia on a hot plate and hypothermia. DGL-produced MAGs can act via receptors to stimulate complex physiological activities, in mammals, birds, amphibians, and fish [3,51,53,54]. However, Murataeva et al. in 2016 reported that the signaling profile for these

compounds was inconsistent with an entourage effect. None of the compounds inhibited neurotransmission via $\mathrm { C B } _ { 1 }$ in autaptic neurons. 2-OG interfered with agonist-induced $\mathrm { C B } _ { 1 }$ internalization while 2-PG modestly internalized $\mathrm { C B } _ { 1 }$ receptors [40]. These compounds failed to potentiate 2-AG mediated depolarization-induced suppression of excitation, and it is suggested that they might instead serve as functional antagonists. The relationship between 2-AG and its congeners is clearly more subtle than previously appreciated [40]. Knowledge about the endogenous physiological ligands and their mode of interaction with CB and non-CB receptors will be crucial for the development of efficient second-generation modulators of this important drug target [54].

The system of DGL-produced MAGs and cannabinoid and non-cannabinoid receptors appears to be phylogenetically ancient because the evidence for a functional MAG system has been described in sea urchins, leeches, mussels, and even the most primitive animal with a nerve network, the Hydra [22,23,51]. This “endogenous 2-AG” of Hydra can modify the coelenterate to open and then close its mouth in the presence of prey and/or glutathione to implement the “feeding response” [22]. The salivary glands of an obligate ectoparasite species of A. americanum produce 2-AG and/or related congeners with analgesic and anti-inflammatory activities, which possibly participate in the inhibition of host defense reactions [25]. However, insects produce little or no AA [29] and have no ortholog of CB receptors [55]. The content of 2-AG in insects is lower than mammalian and aquatic invertebrates. # 4. Distribution, evolutionary pattern, structural biology of DGLs

For better understanding of 2-MAG recruitment in the diversification and elaboration of the physiological activities in different organisms, the use of molecular genetics offers intriguing opportunities to elucidate the distribution and evolutionary pattern, and structural biology of DGLs in multicellular organisms.

# 4.1. Distribution and evolutionary patterns of DGL family in different organisms

A total of 263 full-length or almost complete DGL encoding gene sequences derived from bacteria to human were collected by comprehensive searches of the databases. An alignment of the lipase_3 domain sequences of these genes was conducted to perform phylogenetic analysis. In Fig. 2, a compact view of the tree is shown (the whole view, including species names and accession numbers can be found in Table S1). DGLs are widely present in animal, plant, fungi, and bacteria. Two putative isoforms of DGL-alpha and DGL-beta with functional convergence [4] were detected in most of vertebrate species, whereas sea lamprey Petromyzon, an ancient lineage of vertebrate, had a single DGL-betalike and no DGL-alpha-like ortholog (Table S1). Invertebrate DGLalpha-like and DGL-beta-like orthologs were also wide spread, but duplication or loss of DGL in some species resulted in different gene numbers (Fig. 2). Furthermore, some DGLs from invertebrates, including Hydra, sea squirt, Angomonas deanei, Trypanosoma grayi, acorn worm, pacific oyster, and Acropora digitifera (A. digitifera, coral), could not be clustered into the putative DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms in the phylogenetic analysis (Fig. 2). To further understand the differences of these invertebrate DGLs, detailed analyses of domain and exon structures of vertebrate and invertebrate DGLs were conducted. Twentythree plant DGLs were found only after searching the complete or incomplete genome sequences of 123 kinds of plants in the NCBI and Uniprot databases. Nine fungal genes from phylum Ascomycota and Basidiomycota, two fungal genes from Chytridoomycota, and one bacterial gene from Vibrio anguillarum were annotated as DGL with high sequence similarity to fungal DGLs. Alignment of the conserved lipase_3 domain was conducted to compare sequence features of plant and microorganism DGL orthologs. Our results here have shown that DGL is actually wide-spread in nature from microorganisms to animals in different kingdoms. Tracing the origin and evolution of DGLs in different kingdoms will provide more information on the diversification and elaboration of 2-AG in the evolutionary process.

# 4.1.1. DGL in microorganisms

A single ortholog from bacteria Vibrio anguillarum was predicted to be a putative DGL and clustered into the superfamily abH23 through searching Uniprot database (http://www.uniprot.org/). This bacterial DGL also contained a single catalytic domain without the conserved 4TM domain at the N-terminus. The 4TM domain at the N-terminus of animal DGLs was always predicted to determine the intracellular expression [63]. Although no biochemical characterization has been conducted, bacterial DGL might be the descendent of the common ancestor of eukaryotic DGL on the basis of the species evolutionary status and sequence features.

Sequence analysis showed that fungal DGLs generally have a lipase_3 domain, and were also clustered into the superfamily of abH23. # 4.1.2. DGL in plants

Plant species for which there is complete or almost complete genomic data in NCBI have a very limited number of DGLs. DGL orthologs were not found in the genome sequence of 9 kinds of conifers, or 4 kinds of algae. Only 23 DGL sequences were found in flowering plants and the non-flowering plant of Physcomitrella patens (P. patens, moss) by searching the Uniprot and NCBI database (http://www.ncbi.nlm. nih.gov/). Most species had just one or two DGL-alpha or DGL-beta genes (Fig. S2). The maximum number of the observed DGLs was 4 in the monocot Setaria italica. Plant DGLs had the lipase_3 domain and were also clustered in the superfamily of abH23 through blast analysis in the Lipid engineering database. The annotated plant DGL-alpha-like and DGL-beta-like orthologs had low sequence similarity to putative DGLs and could not be clustered into the putative DGL-alpha and DGLbeta isoforms (Fig. S2). However, as for the reasons of recruiting DGL from the superfamily of abH23 in plants, detailed functional characterization of these DGLs will provide useful information.

Based on sequence alignment of lipase_3 domain and phylogenetic analysis of these 23 plant DGLs, they could be classified into four branches with weak bootstrap (Fig. S2 and Fig. S3). Each branch contained DGL orthologs from monocot and dicot species. P. patens DGL was clustered into the group of Branch IV. Due to no functional identification on the plant DGLs, a more precise classification could not be identified in plant DGL orthologs. Functional studies on plant DGL orthologs from different branches will help to explain the specific evolutionary processes accompanied with gene duplication and loss in plants and elucidate the reasons for recruiting these different sequences of DGL orthologs in flowering plants and moss.

# 4.1.3. DGL in animals

The phylogenetic tree of 230 DGLs sequences in animal species indicated that vertebrate and some of invertebrate DGLs could be perfectly clustered into the group of DGL-alpha and DGL-beta, respectively (Fig. 3). Invertebrate DGL-alpha-like orthologs from sea urchin, Capitella teleta (C. teleta, polychaete), amphioxus, and some insects from Arthropoda were perfectly nested with the clade of vertebrate DGLalpha. DGL-beta orthologs from amphioxus, insects from Arthropoda, parasites from Platyhelminthes, Perkinsozoa, C. elegans and parasites from Nematoda, and sponge were grouped in the clade of vertebrate DGL-beta (Fig. 3). The conserved exon structure was also found in the corresponding DGL-alpha or DGL-beta isoforms among between vertebrate and invertebrate species, including amphioxus, polychaete, C. teleta, Strongylocentrotus purpuratus (S. purpuratus), and even Trichoplax adhaerens (T. adhaerens) (Fig. 3, Fig. S4 and Table S2). Thus, high sequence conservation of vertebrate-like DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms had already appeared before the origin of vertebrates.

4.1.3.1. The complicated evolutionary pattern of invertebrate DGLs. Invertebrate DGLs have more complicated evolutionary features than vertebrates. There is a specific branch of DGL orthologs from pacific oyster, Lottia gigantea, hydra vulgaris, and acorn worm. This branch has a very weak bootstrap with the clade of putative DGL-alpha, and it is very difficult to cluster in the clade of the putative DGL-alpha or DGL-beta. Gene features of DGL orthologs in this branch were further conducted. The exon structure of Hydra DGL-alpha-like ortholog (XP_012555113) was more similar to vertebrate DGL-alpha than DGL-beta. However, orthologs from pacific oyster, Lottia gigantea, and acorn worm were more similar to DGL-beta-like. Thus, DGLs in this branch could not be simply clustered in the clade of putative DGL-alpha or DGL-beta. The early evolution of DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms in invertebrates shared a high homology in sequence features.

To elucidate gene features of some other invertebrate DGLs in the branch that could not be clustered in the clade of DGL-alpha and DGLbeta, a detailed comparison of this unique branch showed several sequence features. Firstly, the single Ciona intestinalis DGL-alpha like (CiDGL) encoded for a protein of 608 AA and shared identity with

human DGL-alpha $( 3 3 \% )$ and DGL-beta $( 3 1 \% )$ . CiDGL was different to the putative DGL-alpha and DGL-beta in other species in exon structure (Table S2). These results suggested that the most recent common ancestor of CiDGL was different to the common ancestor of the putative DGLalpha of amphioxus, pacific oyster, sea urchin, polychaete, and insects from Arthropoda. Secondly, in the basal Metazoa, the phylogenetic analysis and the exon structure of DGL-like orthologs indicated that three T. adhaerens were clustered into the group of DGL-beta rather than DGLalpha (Fig. 3, Fig. S4 and Table S2). In the evolutionary processes, thus, the DGL-beta isoform is more ancient than DGL-alpha isoform. Thirdly, a weaker bootstrap of DGL-like ortholog was found in the protozoan Angomonas deanei (A. deanei) and Trypanosoma grayi (T. grayi), a paraphyletic group of unicellular parasitic flagellate protozoa. A. deanei is a flagellated trypanosomatid protozoan and is a parasite of insects. T. grayi is also an extracellular parasite of the bloodstream of crocodiles [65]. In order to adapt to the parasitic life, genes in parasites have experienced variation in the evolutionary process. It is reasoned that the Cambrian radiation of Metazoa might have evolved from the common ancestor of flagellated colonial protist that is different to modern choanoflagellates to some extent [66]. Thus, A. deanei and T. grayi DGL-ortholog could be representative of the modern descendent of a common ancestor before the Cambrian period. It also suggested that an ancestral common ancestor of the DGL-beta-like ortholog before the Cambrian period had a different sequence feature to putative DGLbeta. Thus, gene structure and phylogenetic analysis of invertebrate DGLs also indicated that the early evolution of invertebrate-lineage DGLs was diversified and complex. The two isoforms had different evolutionary patterns with the secondary loss in insects or other clades. Such loss of DGL-alpha or DGL-beta in some organisms remains an enigma.

4.1.3.2. The ancestral origin of animal DGL isoforms. All animal orthologs were clustered into the superfamily of abH23 by blast analysis in the Lipid engineering database (http://www.led.uni-stuttgart.de/) [67]. Detailed phylogenetic analysis and exon structure for the early evolution of DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms indicated that a weaker bootstrap of DGL-alpha-like ortholog in hydra might have monophyletically evolved from the common ancestor of DGL-alpha in the Cnidaria based on parsimony (Fig. 3 and Fig. 4). It indicated that the primary DGL-alpha evolved monophyletically from the basal eumetazoa (Fig. S4). The origin of DGL-beta could be ascended from DGL-betalike ortholog in Amphimedon queenslandica (A. queenslandica, sponge), which was closest to the ancestors of all Metazoa [68] (Fig. 3 and Fig. 4). Then, a putative DGL-beta-like ortholog in A. queenslandica (340371891) represented the ancestral DGL-beta-like ortholog in Metazoa. Moreover, as a general feature, a wave of gene duplication in the last common eumetazoan ancestor, after Porifera divergence, directly lead to the appearance of many gene families encoding evolutionarily-conserved signaling proteins [69]. Therefore, DGL-beta originated from the protozoan DGLs, including A. deanei and T. grayi DGLs, which represent the modern descendent of a common ancestor in Metazoan before the Cambrian period. The conserved homologue between DGLalpha-like and DGL-beta-like indicated that DGL-alpha-like probably originated from a gene duplication event of DGL-beta-like ortholog in the eumetazoan ancestor in the early Cambrian period, with secondary loss of the orthologs of DGL-alpha or DGL-beta occurring in insects and other clades, then arriving at amphioxus, which has two vertebrate-like isoforms.

DGL-alpha and DGL-beta with functional convergence appear to biosynthesize MAG of 2-AG from DAGs. The above phylogenetic and evolutionary analyses revealed that DGL-alpha and DGL-beta isoforms evolved separately in the early evolution of Metazoa. In invertebrates, the evidence for a functional 2-AG and receptor system has been described in sea urchins, leeches, mussels, and even the most primitive animal with a nerve network, the Hydra [22,23,51]. # 4.2. Structural biology of DGLs and the substrate selectivity of DGLs

Structural and functional properties of many lipase family members have been thoroughly investigated in different organisms [72], however, structural studies on DGL are very limited. Thus far, 3D-structures of fungal DGL of SMG1 and PCL have been elucidated. DGLs contain a canonical α/β hydrolase core [73] and a conserved catalytic triad, which is similar to TAGL [74]. Nucleophilic residue Ser171 is also part of the consensus sequence Gly-X-Ser-X-Gly of lipases. The catalytic triad of Ser171, Asp228 and His281 in SMG1 superimposes well with the triads in other homologous TAGLs of Rhizomucor miehei lipase (RmL, PDB ID: 3TGL) (Fig. 5). Crystal structure analysis of SMG1(PDB ID: 3UUE) showed that there was a central $\beta$ sheet with 8 $\beta$ -strands, two of which were antiparallel (β2 and β8), and this was surrounded by 4 long helices, several short helices, and connective loops on both sides [75].

# 4.2.1. Lid domain of microorganism DGLs

Structure/function features show that the lid domain in lipases covers the active sites [76] and is closely related to the active and inactive conformation of the enzymes [77,78]. Generally, a movement of the lid from “closed” to “open” position is triggered in a lipid-water interface, which exposes the hydrophobic cleft to solvent and renders the nucleophilic serine residue accessible for substrate binding. This alpha-helix (lid) directly over the catalytic pocket regulates enzyme activity. SMG1 has a lid structure of a novel loop conformation from Thr101 to Asp119, located between β3 strand and α2 helix in its closed state [75]. Residues N102 and F278 of SMG1 are responsible for a gating mechanism that regulates the active and inactive states of the lipase from in silico analysis and activity tests, and that no significant structural changes take place during activation except for the oxyanion hole formation [79]. Mutants F287N or F278T provide additional substrate stabilization with a 6-fold increase in the hydrolytic activity toward DAGs [79]. Due to the conservation of F278 among TAGL and DGLs in RMLlike families, the gating mechanism might represent a general mechanism applicable to other TAGLs and DGLs as well [79]. An interesting residue N277 in the crystal structure of SMG1 may contribute to stabilize loop 273–278 and the $3 _ { 1 0 } 4$ helix. The N277D mutant has similar enzymatic properties to the wild type but better thermostability [80]. # 4.2.2. Structure-based substrate-binding characterization

DGLs are considerably different from most other lipases because DGL is strictly specific for MAG and DAG, but not TAG. A model of SMG1 in open conformation has addressed partially the mechanism of its strict substrate selectivity. The structure of SMG1-analogue complex reveals that L103 and F278 divide the catalytic pocket into two separated moieties, an exposed groove and a narrow tunnel. Substrate selectivity of SMG1 mainly results from the shape and size of this narrow tunnel, in which there is no space for the settlement of the third fatty acid chain of TAG [82]. Through the changes of specific hydrophobic interactions, mutant SMG1-Q282L could accept a broad spectrum of TAGs and enable SMG1 to produce TAGs [83]. Wildtype SMG1 and the variant SMG1- Q282L have the same fatty acid selectivity during the competitive acylation reaction; both enzymes exhibit a preference for decanoic acid of the saturated fatty acids, but they are more efficient with unsaturated fatty acids (oleic acid C18:1 and linoleic acid C18:2) [83] (Fig. 5).

Other DGLs also show structural features in substrate selectivity. Comparison of AOL (DGL from Aspergillus oryzae) with TAGL in homology revealed that its substrate selectivity mainly came from its unique binding pocket: a “bridge-like” structure is created by two bulky residues (W89 and F257) on the top of catalytic site, which prevent substrates with large size from entering the active site. A well-conserved sequence motif F-X-X-H has been identified, and this consensus sequence participates in substrate recognition of these MDGLs by forming the “bridge-like” structure that governs the specific binding of substrate [84]. The primary specificity pocket in the model of PCL is predicted to a substrate preference for MAGs and DAGs. The region closes to reactive H259 in PCL, which is located in the opposite shore to the helical lid that is predicted to move in the activated state, contributed to the decision of the unique substrate specificity [85].

# 4.3. Biological function of DGLs in different species

Lipases from microorganism are a large family of proteins, which have been used in many industries such as the food, leather, detergent and paper industries [86,87]. DGLs, one class of microorganism lipases, are strictly specific for MAG and DAG and are also industrially interesting enzymes. PCL has been used for industrial purposes because of its special substrate selectivity [88]. SMG1 lipase is also considered a potential biocatalyst which can be applied in oil/fat modifications [89, 90]. Due to the health benefits that arise from the consumption of diglyceride oils compared with the traditional triglyceride oils, microorganism DGLs have a potentially wider industrial application. Furthermore, PCL and SMG1 can also catalyze the epoxidation of carboxylic acids and alkenes [91,92]. Epoxidized vegetable oils have recently found applications as plasticizers and additives for PVC production. Thus, DGL utility in industrial chemistry reactions will promote the epoxidized vegetable oils application.

Diverse physiological functions of DGL activity have also been identified in different animals. Drosophila inaE-encoded protein with a sn-1 DGL activity is required to couple photo-excitation of rhodopsin to the opening of TRP/TRPL channels in photoreceptors [6]. The inhibition of post-transcription in C. elegans by F42G9.6, a DGL enzyme, has been shown to reduce its longevity by Escherichia coli endogenous noncoding RNAs, DsrA [93]. In Drosophila and C. elegans, overexpression of DGL (also named inaE/dagl-1) extended lifespan and enhanced responses to oxidative stress. Phosphorylated S6 kinase (p-S6K) levels were reduced following these manipulations, implying the involvement of TOR signaling. Conversely, inaE/dagl-1 mutants exhibited shortened lifespan, reduced tolerance to oxidative stress, and elevated levels of p-S6K. Additional results from genetic interaction studies were consistent with the hypothesis that DAG metabolism interacted with TOR and S6K signaling to affect longevity and oxidative stress resistance [94]. Invertebrate opioid pro-opiomelanocortin precursor, the opiates and the eCBs are very powerful immunosuppressors and participate in the bidirectional communication between the endocrine and the immune systems. Parasites use these immunosuppressors in their crosstalk with their host [59]. In humans, two sn-1 DGL (DGL-alpha and DGL-beta) isoforms correlate with on-demand biosynthesis and release of 2-AG [4]. Iannotti reported that DGL-dependent eCB signaling participated in synaptic plasticity to regulate axonal growth and guidance during development, generation and migration of new neurons in the adult brain [43]. DGLs have functions beyond the CBs through non-CB receptors and have regulatory roles in pathological processes, including driving inflammatory responses implicated in neurodegenerative disorders. Some biological roles of DGLs in mammals have been covered earlier in Section 3.3.

Both DGL isoforms in mammals have distinct spatial regulation and are involved in distinct physiological functions [4]. DGL-alpha knockout mice induced an $8 0 \%$ reduction of brain 2-AG levels, but these levels remained unaffected or only partially reduced in the brains of DGL-beta knock-out mice. Adult neurogenesis is impaired in DGL-alpha knockout mice but not in DGL-beta knockout mice [20]. Genetic inactivation of DGL-alpha fully eliminated all forms of eCB-mediated synaptic plasticity in the prefrontal cortex [95], hippocampus [20,21], striatum and cerebellum [21]. DGL-alpha is indispensable for various forms of retrograde synaptic signaling [21]. In contrast, synaptic eCB signaling is not impaired by genetic inactivation of DGL-beta [20,21]. DGL-beta displays a more important role than DGL-alpha in regulating 2-AG levels in the liver [20,21]. Therefore, based on the above commentary, it is clear that DGL isoforms have complicated and distinct physiological functions.

# 4.4. Inhibitors of DGLs

A greater understanding of the DGL function in the nervous and enteric system could lead to advances with important therapeutic

potential in the treatment of neuronal diseases and gastrointestinal disorders. DGL-inhibiting compounds (KT109 and KT172) might have therapeutic uses, for they suppressed the production of pro-inflammatory molecules, including AA, prostaglandins, and TNF- $\alpha$ release in peritoneal macrophages [8]. Pharmacological inhibition of DGL by O-3841 has been shown to produce neuroprotective actions in rats lesion with malonate in vivo and in in vitro experimental models of Huntington's chorea, probably by inhibiting the formation of $\mathsf { P G E } _ { 2 }$ -glycerol ester [96]. SMG1 is considered a potential anti-dandruff target toward dandruff and seborrhoeic dermatitis. Virtual screening of a compound library of 170,000 from Specs and in vitro lipase-based assay were integrated to identify the first hit compound against SMG1, with an $\mathrm { I C } _ { 5 0 }$ of $2 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { M } }$ against a synthetic lipase substrate, and of $0 . 1 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ when using a natural lipase substrate. This work is expected to serve as a starting point for the rational design of more potent inhibitors against SMG1 [97].

# 5. Conclusions and future directions

DGLs from invertebrates to vertebrates produce MAGs with different long fatty acyl chains, including 2-AG, 2-LG, 2-OG and 2-PG. These MAGs function in the multifaceted lipid signaling systems involved in complex physiological actions not only in central nervous system but also other organs, through CB receptors and non-CB receptors. The evolution, especially ancestral origin, of the biosynthetic enzyme of DGL will be helpful to understand the emergence of DGL isoforms and DGL-produced MAGs and its receptors in evolutionary history and eventually explain the formation of DGLs-related lipid signaling from invertebrates to vertebrates. Furthermore, structure/function elucidation of the substrate selection of DGL from 3D-structures will pave the way to explore the usage of DGLs in industry and the development of inhibitors for clinical intervention.
