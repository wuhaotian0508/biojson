# ABSTRACT

Frequent and prolonged drought stress is a primary factor limiting the growth and development of perennial woody plants. Xylem vessel cells are crucial for water transport in woody plants; however, the molecular mechanisms governing the changes in their morphogenesis in response to water deprivation remain unclear. Here, we report that the alterations in polyamine (PA) levels under drought stress, mediated by the key enzyme PagSAMDC4a in PA synthesis, influence vessel morphogenesis through the modulation of hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ concentration in poplar 84 K (Populus alba $\times$ Populus glandulosa). PagSAMDC4a expression was predominantly observed in the phloem and cambium zones of stems and was upregulated under drought conditions. Overexpression of PagSAMDC4a (PagSAMDC4a-OE) resulted in increased PA content, leading to reduced vessel size and density in stem xylem. Conversely, PagSAMDC4a mutant lines exhibited the opposite phenotype, resembling 84 K plants treated with the PA synthesis inhibitor methylglyoxal-bis (guanylhydrazone). In addition, PagSAMDC4a-OE exhibited improved drought tolerance compared to nontransgenic $8 4 \mathrm { K }$ plants and PagSAMDC4a mutant lines. Interestingly, the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ level was significantly decreased in PagSAMDC4a-OE plants but markedly increased in PagSAMDC4a mutants relative to 84 K plants, suggesting that changes in vessel size may result from altered $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels. Supporting this hypothesis, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ application rescued the small vessel phenotype in PagSAMDC4a-OE, whereas the scavenger potassium iodide reduced the size of xylem vessels in PagSAMDC4a mutants. Collectively, these findings provide evidence for the role of PAs in enhancing drought tolerance through the regulation of vessel differentiation. 

# 1 | Introduction

Water scarcity is a critical factor that constrains the survival and productivity of perennial woody plants (Anderegg et  al.  2013; Senf et  al.  2020; Hammond et  al.  2022). In response to water deficiency, trees have developed intricate physiological and 

morphological mechanisms to alleviate cell damage caused by drought stress. These mechanisms encompass modifications in the root system to enhance hydraulic uptake, adjustments in the size and number of xylem vessels within the stem to preserve water conductivity, and alterations in stomatal status to minimise transpiration from leaves (Dash et  al.  2017; Levionnois 

et  al.  2021; He et  al.  2018). Given the necessity for perennial woody plants to transport water across considerable distances to support their growth (Couvreur et al. 2018), secondary vascular systems are crucial in mitigating the detrimental impacts of drought on these species. 

The secondary vascular system in woody plants is generated through the sustained activity of the vascular cambium, a layer of meristematic cells that continually produce daughter cells on both abaxially and adaxially through periclinal division. The adaxial cells undergo expansion, cell wall thickening, programmed cell death and eventually differentiation into xylem cells (Courtois-Moreau et al. 2009; Du and Groover 2010; Spicer and Groover  2010; Fischer et  al.  2019). Xylem vessels, which are derived from elongated cells, serve to provide a conduit for water transport. The size and morphology of these vessels are critical for both the efficiency of water transport and the capacity to recover from embolisms induced by drought stress (Zwieniecki and Holbrook  2000). The differentiation of xylem cells is regulated by a complex network of transcription factors and phytohormones (Ye and Zhong 2015; Fischer et al. 2019; Du et al. 2011; Robischon et al. 2011; Zhong et al. 2006, 2008; Zhou et al. 2014; Hunziker and Greb 2024). 

Plant hormones are crucial for regulating the development of vascular tissues under both normal and stress conditions. Indole-3- acetic acid (IAA) is notably concentrated in the cambium zone, where it significantly influences cambium activity and xylem differentiation (Gouwentak  1941; Sundberg et  al.  2000; Little et al. 2002). Cytokinin modulates cambium activity and coordinates the development of secondary phloem and cambium (Fu et al. 2021). Overexpression of AtIPT7, which increases cytokinin levels, has been shown to enhance cambium activity and biomass production in poplar (Nieminen et al. 2008; Immanen et  al.  2016). Conversely, overexpression of PtoMYB142 in poplar reduces gibberellin (GA) levels, leading to a decrease in the lumen area of xylem vessels, thereby preventing cavitation and improving water transport (Song et al. 2024). Jasmonic acid (JA) promotes secondary cell wall (SCW) formation by upregulating genes involved in lignin biosynthesis and enhances drought tolerance through the regulation of stomatal closure and reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging (Sehr et  al.  2010; Liu et al. 2021; Singh et al. 2021; Rao et al. 2023; Zhou et al. 2019). Peptide hormones such as PtCLE24/28/47 positively regulate cambial activity, resulting in increased secondary xylem formation (Han et al. 2016; Kucukoglu et al. 2020). 

Polyamines (PAs), considered as hormones, are multifunctional aliphatic amines found in various plant species (Tabor and Tabor  1984). The four primary biogenic polyamines in plants—putrescine (Put), spermidine (Spd), spermine (Spm) and thermospermine (T-spm)—are synthesised from amino acids through the enzymatic activities of arginine decarboxylase (ADC) or ornithine decarboxylase (Gao et al. 2021; Handa and Mattoo 2010; Liu et al. 2015; Takahashi 2020). Put serves as the principal precursor in the biosynthetic pathway, leading to the formation of spermidine via spermidine synthases, spermine via spermine synthases and thermospermine via thermospermine synthases (ACL5) (Walters 2003; Tiburcio et al. 2014). S-adenosylmethionine donates the aminopropyl group necessary for the synthesis of spermidine and spermine, a process 

catalysed by S-adenosylmethionine decarboxylase (SAMDC). Consequently, SAMDC is recognised as a critical rate-limiting enzyme in the polyamine biosynthesis pathway (Hu et al. 2005). Compared with the polyamine biosynthetic pathway, the PA degradation pathway remains relatively underexplored. Polyamine metabolism in plants is catalysed by polyamine oxidases that also produce signalling molecules such as $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and nitric oxide (NO) (Diao et al. 2017; Moschou et al. 2008). $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and NO serve as critical secondary messengers in PA-mediated signalling pathways during various developmental processes, senescence and stress responses in plants (Gupta et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2019; Peng et  al.  2016). Polyamines and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ jointly regulate rice mesocotyl elongation via the interaction between ethylene and jasmonic acid (JA) signalling pathways (Lv et  al.  2021), while polyamines can also activate plant defence mechanisms through the ROS-dependent salicylic acid pathway (Liu et  al.  2020). Furthermore, T-Spm and NO may interact to modulate leaf vascular development and seed size (Mai et al. 2024). Under stress conditions, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and NO influence gene expression by regulating cytosolic potassium homeostasis and calcium signalling, thereby triggering a cytoprotective stress response to mitigate oxidative damage (Pottosin et  al.  2012). Further research into the interconnections between PAs, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and NO during plant growth and development is essential. 

PAs play a crucial role in regulating various aspects of biological processes, such as chromatin structure modifications, maintenance of cell membrane integrity, oxidative stress responses and intercellular communication (Lenis et  al.  2017; Borromeo et al. 2023; Van Den Broeck et al. 1994; Du et al. 2022; Gill and Tuteja 2010). Notably, the Arabidopsis double mutants of ADC1 and ADC2 result in plant lethality (Sánchez-Rangel et al. 2016), while ACL5 is predominantly expressed in differentiating xylem vessels and is upregulated by exogenous auxin treatment (Muñiz et al. 2008; Hanzawa 2000). Additionally, the Arabidopsis samdc mutant shows severely impaired internode elongation and excessive xylem vessel formation (Ge et al. 2006). These findings suggest that PAs are likely involved in xylem development. On the other hand, accumulating evidence indicates that PAs can enhance plant tolerance to abiotic stress (Alcázar et  al.  2020). Under salt stress, PAs can inhibit cation channels, maintain ion homeostasis and thus improve plant salt tolerance (Su et al. 2019; Alcázar et  al.  2010; Saha et  al.  2022). PAs can also mitigate lipid peroxidation by downregulating the NAD(P)H-oxidase activity under drought stress (Gill and Tuteja 2010). Moreover, the accumulation of endogenous PAs typically serves an antioxidant function by directly eliminating ROS or by activating the ROS-scavenging enzyme system under adverse conditions (Jahan et al. 2022; Hassan et al. 2018). Interestingly, the application of exogenous PAs can significantly increase the activity of peroxidase and catalase, leading to a reduction in ROS synthesis during dehydration in citrus (Shi et al. 2010). These findings collectively demonstrate that polyamines serve as central regulators in plant stress responses through osmoprotection, antioxidant defence, signal transduction and synergistic interactions with other hormones. Additionally, they also indicate the involvement of polyamines in vascular tissue development. However, the mechanisms by which the stress response triggers the expression of PA-related genes, as well as the processes linking PA accumulation, increased ROS levels and xylem differentiation, remain largely unexplored. 

In this study, we have demonstrated that the accumulation of PAs in response to water stress results in enhanced drought resistance by reducing the vessel size and number in hybrid poplar 84 K (Populus alba $\times P .$ . glandulosa). Consistent with this finding, the overexpression of poplar SAMDC (PagSAMDC4a), which leads to elevated PA levels, was associated with a decrease in vessel size and number, thereby improving drought tolerance. Conversely, the knockout of PagSAMDC4a leading to the reduced PA levels produced the opposite effect. Additionally, our research indicates that $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels play a mediating role in the modification of xylem vessel size. This study not only provides new insights into the molecular mechanisms regulating the plant's response to water deficit via the modification of xylem vessel differentiation, but also paves the way for alteration of wood properties by precisely manipulating PAs through molecular techniques in poplar. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | PAs Enhance the Drought Tolerance of Poplars

We selected 2-month-old 84 K poplars and treated them with drought stress to determine the change in PAs content following stress exposure. After 3 days of treatment, the levels of Put and Spd content were significantly increased, while Spm showed no significant change. However, after 7 days, all PA levels were significantly higher compared with the untreated controls 

(Figure  1a,b). To investigate the effect of PAs on drought tolerance in poplars, we treated poplars with PAs or SAMDC inhibitor methylglyoxal-bis (guanylhydrazone) (MGBG), and simultaneously stopped watering. Control and MGBG-treated plants displayed severe leaf wilting, whereas PAs-treated poplars remained largely unaffected under drought conditions (Figure  1c,d). We measured the leaf relative water content (LRWC) and found that PAs-treated poplars had higher LRWC compared with control and MGBG-treated plants (Figure  1e). In addition, the PAs-treated plants exhibited significantly lower values of electrolyte leakage (EL) and malondialdehyde (MDA) content under drought stress, although all plants showed similar values under well-watered conditions (Figures 1f and S1a). These results suggest that the application of PAs improves the drought resistance of poplars. Interestingly, the $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ content was elevated in MGBG-treated plants even in the absence of drought stress, as compared to the control group, and this increase was more pronounced under stress conditions (Figure S1b). This finding suggests a close relationship between PA levels and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ . 

To further investigate the changes in vascular tissues, we examined the stem cross-sections of PA-treated plants using microscopy. The results indicated that MGBG-treated plants exhibited an increased size and number of xylem vessels, whereas Put, Spd and Spm treatments showed no significant differences compared to untreated plants under normal watering conditions (Figure  2a–c). # 2.2 | PagSAMDC4a Is Expressed in Vascular Tissues and Responsive to Drought Stress

To investigate whether altered PA levels can influence xylem cell differentiation, we aimed to modulate the PA levels by overexpression or knockout of the rate-limiting enzyme gene SAMDC. A total of six SAMDC genes were identified in the Populus genome (Figure S3a,b) and their expression patterns in different tissues of 84 K poplar were determined using reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR). Notably, PagSAMDC4a and PagSAMDC4b exhibited elevated expression levels in phloem and cambium tissues (Figure  3a and S4a). Furthermore, according to data from the AspWood database (Sundell et al. 2017), PtSAMDC4a and PtSAMDC4b were specifically expressed in the cambium and xylem expansion zone, contrasting with the expression patterns of other PtSAMDC genes (Figure S4b). These results suggest that these two genes may play a role in the PAs synthesis in wood-forming tissues. Additionally, we examined the expression of PagSAMDC4a under drought conditions, and observed a significant induction of PagSAMDC4a expression in stems, reaching up to 5-fold at 

5 h under mannitol treatment (Figure 3b), and a notably higher expression at 3 days in stems and 7 days in leaves under waterdeficient conditions (Figure 3c). In contrast, PagSAMDC4b did not exhibit any response to drought (Figure S4c). Consequently, PagSAMDC4a was found to be the only member specifically expressed in wood-forming tissues and responsive to drought stress. To further examine tissue-specific expression of PagSAMDC4a, we isolated a 1996 bp promoter region to drive the expression of the $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) gene, which was subsequently transformed into poplar. Histochemical staining of the entire plant revealed a strong blue signal in young stems and leaves, with more specific GUS staining observed in the vascular tissues of the stem through cross-sectioning and in the roots (Figure 3d). Additionally, GUS activity in the stems of these transgenic plants was induced by drought (Figure  3e,f). These findings suggest that PagSAMDC4a may play a crucial role in the synthesis of PAs in vascular tissues under waterdeficit conditions in poplar.

# 2.4 | PagSAMDC4a Activity Affects the Differentiation of Xylem Vessels

We further prepared the cross-sections of the 11th internode and observed the vascular tissues with a microscope after staining with toluidine blue O (TBO). The PagSAMDC4a-OE lines exhibited a narrowed xylem width and reduced xylem occupancy in the stem, while the PagSAMDC4a mutants showed no significant differences compared with the 84 K control (Figure 4a–c). Notably, the PagSAMDC4a mutant displayed an increased number and size of vessels, in contrast to the PagSAMDC4a-OE lines, which showed a decrease in both vessel number and size compared with the 84 K control (Figure 4d–f). To characterise the properties of xylem cells, we macerated the vessels and fibres from the 8th internode and observed them under a microscope (Figure S7a). The PagSAMDC4a-OE lines had smaller vessels, with $8 . 8 \%$ (L15) and $2 4 . 9 \%$ (L17) reduction in width, while the PagSAMDC4a mutants exhibited larger vessels with $1 0 \%$ (KO-4) and $2 2 . 8 \%$ (KO-8) increase compared with the $8 4 \mathrm { K }$ control (Figure S7b). In addition, PagSAMDC4a mutant lines had wider fibre cells with a $1 7 \%$ (KO-4) and $2 1 \%$ (KO-8) increase in size compared with the 84 K control, respectively (Figure S7c). Furthermore, MGBG treatment of PagSAMDC4a-OE plants resulted in pronounced changes in the wood anatomy, including reduced xylem width and increased lumen area of vessels (Figure S8). This phenotype resembles that of the PagSAMDC4a mutants, suggesting that the abundance and activity of PagSAMDC4a influence PAs, which in turn modulates the differentiation of xylem vessels in poplar. 

# 2.5 | PagSAMDC4a Is Involved in Modulation of Both Drought Tolerance and Xylem Differentiation

To further assess the role of PagSAMDC4a in response to water-deficient conditions in poplar, 2-month-old 84 K, PagSAMDC4a-OE, and PagSAMDC4a mutant plants were subjected to drought treatment (Figure S9a). After a 10-day drought treatment, the PagSAMDC4a-OE line maintained a healthy growth state, whereas the 84 K plants exhibited drought symptoms, including drooping leaves, and the PagSAMDC4a mutants showed even more severe symptoms, such as wilted leaves and crooked apical buds (Figure S9b). We further measured soil relative water content (SRWC), LRWC, EL, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels and MDA content. Before and after short-term drought treatment, no significant differences in SRWC were observed between the 84 K and the PagSAMDC4a transgenic lines (Figure  S9c). Under water-sufficient conditions, the LRWC, EL and MDA values of 84K, PagSAMDC4a-OE and PagSAMDC4a mutants were similar; however, under drought stress conditions, these values were significantly different. In contrast to the PagSAMDC4a mutants and 84 K control plants, PagSAMDC4a-OE lines exhibited higher LRWC and lower levels of EL and MDA content in leaves under drought stress, indicating a high cell viability maintained after stress (Figures  5c,d and S9e). Furthermore, 

despite the elevation of $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels across all genotypes under drought stress, this increase was notably lower in the PagSAMDC4a-OE lines compared with the significantly higher levels observed in the PagSAMDC4a mutants (Figure  S9d). Following a 10-day short-term drought treatment, we investigated the differentiation of xylem vessel cells across different internodes in PagSAMDC4a transgenic plants. In all lines, the newly differentiated xylem vessels were significantly smaller compared with those under normal conditions (Figure S10a–c). A long-term drought stress experiment was conducted on these poplar lines by maintaining the SRWC at $1 5 \%$ for 1 month. The PagSAMDC4a mutants and 84 K leaves displayed leaf wilting and drying, with the PagSAMDC4a mutants exhibiting more severe symptoms, whereas the PagSAMDC4a-OE lines continued to grow normally, apart from the shedding of some lower leaves (Figure  5a,b). To explore the impact of PagSAMDC4a misexpression on the development of vascular tissues under drought stress, we examined the morphology of the newly differentiated xylem in the 5th internode postdrought treatment using microscopy (Figure 5e,f). The PagSAMDC4a-OE lines showed a marked reduction in xylem width under both normal and drought stress conditions (Figure 5g). While all lines exhibited increased xylem vessel density and reduced lumen area under drought conditions, the PagSAMDC4a-OE and PagSAMDC4a mutants represented the two extremes (Figure 5h,i). These results indicate that PagSAMDC4a plays a crucial role in regulating xylem differentiation in response to water deficiency.

# 2.7 | $\mathbf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { O } _ { 2 }$ Mediates the Differentiation of Xylem Vessels

To further investigate whether the changes in the number and size of xylem vessels in transgenic plants were due to the alterations in $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels caused by the overexpression and knockout of PagSAMDC4a, we applied $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and its scavenger potassium iodide (KI) to PagSAMDC4a transgenic plants (Figure  7a,d). # 3 | Discussion

Drought, as a critical environmental factor, significantly impacts the growth and development of trees. To acclimate to arid conditions, woody plants have evolved a series of adaptive morphological modifications, such as an increase in the number and a reduction in the size of xylem vessels to mitigate the risk of vessel embolism and enhance the water transport efficiency of roots and stems (Arend and Fromm  2007; Fichot et  al.  2009). Furthermore, drought stress typically elicits a range of physiological responses in plants, including alterations in polyamine levels, the accumulation of osmotic compounds and the synthesis of ROS-related proteins (Alcázar et al. 2020; Bhatnagar-Mathur et al. 2008). However, the molecular mechanism governing how these multifaceted responses coordinate to influence vascular tissue differentiation remains largely unexplored. This study 

elucidates that the modulation of PAs content by SAMDC4a activity is pivotal in regulating the density and size of secondary xylem vessels in poplar under drought conditions, a process mediated by $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels. 

Previous studies have demonstrated that PAs can enhance plant stress resistance via multiple mechanisms, such as enhancing osmoregulatory substances, activating the antioxidant system to reduce ROS (Zhang et al. 2014; Li et al. 2015; Yang et al. 2022), thus mitigating the adverse effects of dehydration stress (Hassan et al. 2018; Shi et al. 2010). This study comprehensively investigated the role of PAs in drought tolerance through both exogenous application and endogenous alteration of PA levels through genetic modifications. We firstly observed an increased PAs level in poplar plants exposed to drought stress, which remained consistently high under prolonged stress conditions (Figure 1b). Subsequently, we confirmed the involvement of PAs in stress tolerance by applying PAs and MGBG, an inhibitor of SAMDC in PA synthesis, demonstrating increased and decreased drought tolerance in the treated poplars, respectively (Figure 1c,d). The inhibition of SAMDC resulted in severe damage, suggesting its critical role in PAs-mediated drought tolerance. Therefore, we selected SAMDC4a, showing high expression in vascular tissues and responsive to drought stress, to generate PagSAMDC4a overexpression and knockout poplar lines with significantly increased and decreased PAs content, respectively (Figures  3 and S5e). Consistently, the transgenic lines with overexpressing PagSAMDC4a exhibited enhanced drought resistance, whereas the PagSAMDC4a knockout lines displayed increased sensitivity to drought stress (Figure S9b). Collectively, both the exogenous and endogenous alterations in PA levels affect the drought tolerance of poplars. 

It has been proposed that PAs play a role in xylem development (Kakehi et  al.  2008; Mai et  al.  2024). For instance, Tspm synthetase Arabidopsis mutant atacl5 had decreased endogenous T-spm levels and demonstrated delayed xylem development (Takano et al. 2012). In this study, we revealed that xylem vessel development was significantly influenced in plants treated with the SAMDC inhibitor MGBG, which resulted in enlarged vessels (Figure  2). Furthermore, transgenic lines overexpressing PagSAMDC4a, leading to elevated PAleyels,displayed decreased vessel size,whereas lines with reduced PA levels due to the knockout of PagSAMDC4a exhibited larger vessel sizes (Figure  4). Additionally, overexpression of PagSAMDC4a enhanced drought resistance, while the knockout of PagSAMDC4a decreased drought tolerance (Figure  5a). It has also been observed that woody plants increase the number of xylem vessels while reducing their size to adapt to drought conditions (Arend and Fromm  2007; Fichot et  al.  2009). Many studies have demonstrated that reactive oxygen species (ROS) play a crucial role in regulating plant meristem cell division and differentiation. Specifically, the balance between $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { \cdot - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ determines whether cells will proliferate or differentiate (Tsukagoshi et al. 2010; Yamada et al. 2020). Recent findings indicate that LATERAL ORGAN BOUNDARIES DOMAIN 11 (LBD11) regulates the proliferation and radial growth of Arabidopsis cambium cells by activating the transcription of genes involved in ROS metabolism, thereby increasing ROS levels (Dang et al. 2023). Moreover, elevated ${ \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { \cdot - }$ levels during tension wood induction trigger downstream IAA signalling pathways, which not only promote cambium cell proliferation but also facilitate advanced PCD (Huang et al. 2024). Given the reduced number of xylem vessels in tension wood compared to normal wood, high levels of $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { \cdot - }$ may have an inhibitory effect on vessel differentiation. On the other hand, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , as a key signalling molecule, plays a pivotal role in modulating the expression of numerous genes related to growth and development (Bordenave et al. 2019; Sagor et al. 2013). Specifically, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ is involved in the modulation of leaf cell expansion and final organ size in maize (Zea mays) (Lu et al. 2014). The elevated PAs content can act as ROS scavengers, thereby playing an essential role in redox homeostasis (Das and Misra 2004; Ha et al. 1998; Liu et  al.  2020). However, the precise mechanism through which PAs mediate vessel development via $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ in woody plants remains unclear. In this study, both ROS staining and quantitative measurement of the ROS contents revealed a decrease in $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { \cdot - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels in PagSAMDC4a-OE plants, whereas these levels were elevated in PagSAMDC4a mutant related to 84 K plants (Figure 6a–f). Additionally, the expression of RBOH and SOD enzyme genes was upregulated in PagSAMDC4a mutant, accompanied by an increase in enzyme activity, while the opposite trend was observed in PagSAMDC4a-OE plants (Figures 6g,h and S11). These findings suggest that the increase in $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels may predominantly result from a reduction in antioxidant enzyme activity due to the elevated PAs content, which in turn alters vessel morphology by influencing $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ levels. Furthermore, exogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ treatment effectively restored secondary xylem development in PagSAMDC4a-OE plants, comparable to the 84 K control, including xylem width and vessel size (Figure 7a–c). On the other hand, the size and density of xylem vessels in PagSAMDC4a mutant were markedly reduced following KI treatment (Figure 7d–f). Therefore, this study provides substantial evidence supporting the notion that PagSAMDC4a regulates vessel development by modulating $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ concentration.

# 4.1 | Plant Material and Growth Conditions

A hybrid poplar 84K (Populus alba $\times P .$ . glandulosa) was used as the plant material in this study. All tissue cultures were performed on 1/2 Murashige and Skoog (MS) media at a temperature of $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \pm 1 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a relative humidity of $6 0 \%$ under long-day conditions (a photoperiod of 16h light: 8h dark, with a light intensity of $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). Under identical long-day photoperiod conditions, soil-cultivated poplars were grown in the greenhouse. 

# 4.2 | Bioinformatics Analysis

Poplar SAMDC genes were identified by aligning nucleotide and protein sequences of AtSAMDC with the P. trichocarpa and Oryza sativa L. genome database, resulting in the identification of six members of the SAMDC family. To evaluate their evolutionary relationships, a phylogenetic tree was constructed based on the full protein sequences of AtSAMDCs, OsSAMDCs and PtSAMDCs using the Neighbour-joining method in MEGA10 software with 1000 bootstrap replicates. The same methodology was applied to analyse the SOD and RBOH family members. 

# 4.3 | Vector Construction and Transformation of Poplars

To construct the Pro35S::SAMDC4a vector, the full-length coding sequence (CDS) of PagSAMDC4a was amplified from 84 K cDNA using gene-specific primer pairs, and subsequently cloned into the plant binary vector pK2GW7 to create overexpression (PagSAMDC4a-OE) lines. Based on the genomic sequence, a target site was selected and cloned into the binary pYL-CRISPR/Cas9 vector to create knockout lines, as previously described (Ma et  al.  2015). For the construction of the ProSAMDC4a::GUS vector, a 1996 bp fragment upstream of the PagSAMDC4a start codon was amplified from genomic DNA using the specific primers and inserted into the pMDC164- GUS vector. These constructs were stably transformed into 84 K plants via Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation. To verify the mutations in PagSAMDC4a mutant poplar plants, genomic DNA was extracted by the CTAB and examined by Sanger sequencing of the PCR products along the target site. OE lines were identified based on PagSAMDC4a expression levels determined by RT-qPCR. All primers used are listed in Table S1. 

# 4.4 | RNA Isolation and qRT-PCR

To quantify the expression of PagSAMDC4a across various tissues, total RNA was extracted from the roots, leaves and stems 

including the 1st, 2nd, 4th, 5th and 7th internodes, developing xylem, phloem and cambium zone of three independent 2-month-old 84 K plants. For detecting the expression levels of PagSAMDC4a in the OE lines, as well as the expression of polyamine synthesis-related genes, PagRBOH and PagSOD in PagSAMDC4a transgenic lines, stems from three independent 1-month-old soil-grown seedlings of each line were used, and the collected samples were immediately immersed and stored in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA was isolated from each sample using the EASYspin Plus Plant RNA Extraction Kit (Aidlab, China). Complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesised using Evo M-MLV RT Mix Kit with gDNA Clean for qPCR Ver.2 (Accurate Biology, Suzhou, China). RT-qPCR was performed using SYBR Green Premix Pro Taq HS qPCR Kit (Accurate Biology) on a Light Cycler 480 Thermal Cycler (Roche) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Expression data were calculated with the delta–delta Ct method. The poplar Ubiquitin (UBQ) served as the reference gene. The primers used for RT-qPCR assays are detailed in Table S1. 

# 4.5 | GUS Staining

One-month-old PagSAMDC4apro::GUS tissue-cultured plants were fixed in $9 0 \%$ acetone $\left( \mathbf { \boldsymbol { v } } / \mathbf { \boldsymbol { v } } \right)$ for $4 \mathrm { h }$ at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , followed by incubation in GUS staining solution $\mathrm { \nabla { 5 0 m M } }$ sodium phosphate, $2 \mathrm { m M }$ potassium ferricyanide, $2 \mathrm { m M }$ potassium ferrocyanide, $0 . 2 \%$ Triton X-100 and 1 mM X-Gluc) for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The 5th internodes were sectioned to a thickness of $5 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ using a vibratome (Leica VT1000S; Leica). Subsequently, the sections were destained with $7 5 \%$ ethanol and photographed. GUS activity was assayed and quantified using a GUS Activity Detection Kit (Coolaber, Beijing) according to the manufacturer's instruction. Three independent transgenic lines were used in the GUS assay, and all exhibited consistent results. 

# 4.6 | Determination of PAs Content

The PA's content was quantified according to the method described by Shu et al. (2014). Briefly, $0 . 5 { \mathrm { g } }$ of plant material was mixed with precooled $5 \%$ perchloric acid, and then centrifuged after incubation in an ice bath for 1 h. Subsequently, $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the supernatant was collected, followed by adding $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ benzoyl chloride and $2 \mathrm { m L }$ of NaOH (2 N). After incubation in a water bath at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20 min, 2 mL of ether was added and mixed thoroughly. The upper ether layer (1 mL) was collected and dried under a stream of nitrogen. The dried sample was subsequently re-dissolved in a 1 mL of methanol and filtered through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ organic phase filtration membrane. The resultant solution was transferred to a sample vial for PA analysis content. The PA content was determined by ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography equipped with a Poroshell-120-EC-C18 chromatographic column $\mathrm { 1 5 0 m m } \times 3 . 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $2 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ particle size) and a UV detector. The mobile phase consisted of acetonitrile and water in a volume ratio of 40:60, with a f low rate of $0 . 5 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . A $5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquot of the sample was injected, and detection was performed at a wavelength of $2 3 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . The experiment was conducted with three biological replicates. 

# 4.7 | Cross-Sectioning and Histological Staining

Internodes from individual plants from 84 K and transgenic lines were sectioned to a thickness of $5 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ using a vibratome (Leica VT1000S; Leica), and six sections in the midinternode were selected for histological staining with $0 . 0 5 \%$ (w/v) TBO, $\mathrm { 1 m g / m L }$ NBT, or DAB using the protocol as described by Huang et al. (2024). Plant internodes (approximately $3 \mathrm { m m }$ in length) were infiltrated in $1 \mathrm { m g / m L }$ NBT (IN0110, Solarbio) in the dark at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ , and the internode sections were prepared and then incubated in $1 \times \mathrm { D A B }$ working solution (DA1010, Solarbio) in the dark at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. $7 5 \%$ ethanol was used for further decolorisation. The stained sections were subsequently examined under a microscope (ZEISS Stemi 508). Semiquantification of $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ^ { \cdot - }$ and $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , as represented by NBT and DAB intensity, respectively, was performed as previously described (Huang et al. 2024). For quantification of vessel size, at least 100 randomly selected vessels were measured using ImageJ software (https://imagej.nih.gov/ij/) based on images obtained from at least five sections per plant, with data collected from three independent plants. 

# 4.8 | Xylem Cell Maceration

The 8th internode of 3-month-old 84 K and transgenic plants was harvested, stripped, and the xylem segments were excised into approximately 1 cm long strips. The samples were then immersed in a centrifuge tube containing 5 mL of acid hydrolysis solution composed of $1 0 \%$ (v/v) $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and $5 0 \%$ (v/v) glacial acetic acid, and incubated at $5 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days. Following this, the xylem tissues were rinsed three times with distilled water. Subsequently, $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the solution was transferred to a glass slide, stained with $0 . 0 5 \%$ TBO and imaged using a ZEISS Stemi 508 microscope. Vessel and fibre widths were measured using ImageJ software. For each plant line, over 100 vessel or fibre cells were randomly selected and measured. 

# 4.9 | Drought Treatment

For mannitol treatment, 4-week-old tissue cultured poplar plantlets were transferred to solutions containing either $2 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ mannitol or distilled water and incubated for periods ranging from 1 to $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . For drought treatment, 2-month-old 84 K poplars were subjected to watering cessation for 3 or 7 days, with a control group receiving regular irrigation. Following treatment, three individual plants were harvested for RT-qPCR analysis. In the water-controlled drought treatment, 3-month-old poplars experienced gradual drought stress until the SRWC decreased from $5 5 \% \pm 5 \%$ to $1 5 \% \pm 5 \%$ , after which water was withheld. The SRWC of the control group was maintained above $5 5 \%$ . Soil moisture levels were monitored using a PR-3002- TRREC-N01 soil moisture meter (PREENS, Shandong, China). For PAs and MGBG treatment, 4-week-old tissue cultured 84 K or PagSAMDC4a-OE plantlets were planted in soil for 2 months and subsequently treated with mock, PAs, or $0 . 1 \mathrm { m m o l / L }$ MGBG for 21 days. For $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and KI treatment, 3-month-old poplars in soil were exposed to 100 and $3 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ or 0.1 and 1 mM KI for 1 month. Three individuals from the 84 K, OE, and KO lines were selected for further analysis. 

# 4.10 | Measurement of Drought-Related Indicators

The 4th mature leaf (from top to bottom) of three plants from the untreated (Control) and drought-stressed 84K, OE and knockout lines were harvested for physiological indicator measurement. The SOD activity was accessed using a Total Superoxide Dismutase Assay Kit with WST-8 (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). RBOH activity was determined using a NADPH oxidase assay kit (Suzhou Grace Biotechnology, Suzhou, China). The MDA, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } } ^ { \cdot - }$ content of transgenic lines and 84K were measured under different conditions using corresponding assay kits from Boxbio (Beijing, China), according to the manufacturer's instructions. The experiment was replicated 3 times and each with three samples. The procedure for measuring LRWC was detailed in the previously report (He et al. 2018). Fresh weight (FW) was recorded immediately after excising the leaves, followed by submerging them in water for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ to determine turgid weight (TW). Leaf dry weight (DW) was measured after drying at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ . LR $\mathrm { \langle W C = ( F W - D W ) / ( T W - D W ) \times 1 0 0 \% }$ $=$ . The EL was measured following the established protocol (He et  al.  2018), using a Conductivity Metre (FiveEasy Plus FE38, Mettler Toledo, China). Circular leaf discs with a diameter of 1cm were prepared by excising the leaves with a punch, then immersed in 8mL of deionised distilled water. A control tube containing an identical volume of water was also prepared. These tubes were statically incubated at room temperature for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ , after which the initial conductivities of the sample (E1) and the blank (CK1) were measured using the conductivity meter. Following this, the tubes were subjected to boiling at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ and then cooled to room temperature before measuring the final conductivities (E2 and CK2). EL was calculated according to the formula: Relative electrical conductance $: = ( \mathrm { E 1 - C K 1 } ) / ( \mathrm { E 2 - C K 2 } ) \times 1 0 0 \%$ $=$ . 

# 4.11 | Statistical Analyses

Quantitative data for gene expression, phenotypic characteristics, PA contents and drought-related indicators were analysed statistically and visualised using Microsoft Excel 2010 (Microsoft Corporation, Redmond, WA, USA) and GraphPad Prism version 8. Statistical significance of differences in measured parameters was assessed using Student's t-test or one-way ANOVA. Post hoc Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) test was conducted following ANOVA to identify significant differences between pairwise comparisons. Data are expressed as mean±standard deviation (SD), based on a minimum of three biological replicates. Each experiment was replicated at least three times, yielding consistent results.
