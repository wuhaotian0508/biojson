# Abstract

Background: There is a Chinese proverb that good tea comes from high mountains with clouds and mists, suggesting the important impact of environmental factors on the development and secondary metabolism in tea plants. However, the epigenetic mechanism involved is still unclear. 

Results: High altitude results in light enhancement with a higher retention of shortwavelength light in cloudy conditions, suggesting the key role of short-wavelength light in the quality formation of tea plants. Thus, we focus on the representative shortwavelength light, blue and UV-A, and characterize plant responses in epigenome, transcriptome, leaf development, and metabolome. We profle six histone modifcations under diferent light wavelengths and link these to leaf development and secondary metabolism, including changes in gene expression during favonoid, theanine, cafeine, and $\beta$ -carotene biosynthesis. There is higher stomatal density and thicker mesophyll tissues under blue light, with higher levels of chlorophyll components under UV-A light. The epigenome results in diferential changes of stomatal density and quality components in diferent light conditions. We further identify crucial histone modifcations in leaf development and secondary metabolism. Functional analyses suggest diverse regulations mediated by cryptochrome and phototropin in light adaptation, and we confrm the important role of CsSDG36-mediated histone H3K4 methylation. 

Conclusion: Our results not only reveal the landscape of histone modifcations, transcripts, leaf development, and metabolites from diferent lights in tea plants, but also provide insight into the roles of photoreceptors and epigenetic mechanisms involved in leaf development and secondary metabolism. 

Keywords: Camellia sinensis, Histone modifcation, Development, Secondary metabolism, Light spectrum 

# Background

Given excellent favor and bio-active compounds, tea has emerged as one of the three non-alcoholic beverages worldwide. Tea is characterized by nutritional value and health benefts, such as eliminating free radicals, reducing infammation, and preventing allergic reactions [1]. Tese efects are attributed to the abundant secondary metabolites in tea leaves, including favonoids, free amino acids, alkaloids, and volatiles [2]. Tere is a Chinese proverb that good tea originates from high mountains with clouds and mists, emphasizing the important impact of environmental factors on secondary metabolism in tea plants. Clouds in the sky form rich difuse light on the high mountains, which is highly correlated with the formation of favor compounds in tea plants [3]. According to the wavelengths, the solar spectrum can divide into ultraviolet light (wavelength $< 3 8 0 ~ \mathrm { n m } ,$ ), visible light $3 8 0 { - } 7 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ , followed by blue, cyan, green, yellow, orange, and red lights), and infrared light (wavelength $> 7 2 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) [4]. Green light increases the content of proanthocyanidins and ascorbic acid [5], while red light enhances the activities of phenylalanine ammonialyase (PAL) and phenylacetaldehyde synthase (PAAS), resulting in the increase of volatile phenylpropanoids in tea plants [6]. UV-B light induces the accumulation of favonoids through UV resistance LOCUS8 (UVR8) signal transduction pathway [7]. Tese bio-active metabolites in leaves form an important foundation of tea quality and favors. Moreover, diferent light spectrum induces the changes of leaf pigment compounds as well, afecting the color of dried tea and tea infusion. Red and blue lights respectively regulate the biosynthesis of chlorophyll and carotenoids [8]. Except for metabolism synthesis, light spectrum regulates numerous physiological processes in plants, including stomatal opening [9], root growth [10], and leaf photosynthesis [11]. Te growth and metabolism regulated by red and UV-B lights in tea plants have been extensively studied in the past decade, but little is known on the efects of blue and UV-A lights. 

Light spectrums are sensed by specifc photoreceptors in plants. Phytochromes and UVR8 perceive red/far-red light and UV-B light, respectively [12]. Phototroins absorb blue light, while cryptochromes absorb blue light and UV-A light [13]. Since the absorption spectrums of pigments difer, the light spectrums could diversely afect secondary metabolism and development in plants. Te expression of phytochrome A (CsPHYA), phytochrome B (CsPHYB), cryptochrome 1 (CsCRY1), and cryptochrome 2 (CsCRY2) regulate the biosynthesis of chlorophyll and carotenoids in tea plants [8]. Te downregulation of CsCRY2 and CsPHOT2 is closely related to the increased contents of proanthocyanidins and ascorbic acid [5]. Primary photoreceptors PHYB and CRY2 are involved in the light-dependent phenylpropanoid accumulation, and the transcription factor HY5 is required for the response in Arabidopsis [14]. Phototropins phosphorylate BLUS1 kinase, mediating stomatal opening and blue light-specifc promotion of stomatal conductance [15]. UV-B photoreceptor UVR8 regulates auxin response and lateral root development by interacting with MYB73 [16]. Despite the signaling pathways mediated by photoreceptors in plants have been studied for decades, the epigenetic mechanisms involved are rarely reported. Under blue light, CRY1 physically associates with SWI2/ 

SNF2-RELATED 1 chromatin remodeling complex (SWR1-C) to catalyze H2 A.Z incorporation into the chromatin [17], while CRY2 recruits MTA to regulate epitranscriptome [18]. Blue light afects the H3K9 acetylation levels of light-responsive genes [19]. UV light has been associated with DNA methylome and histone acetylation. In Arabidopsis, UV-B triggers DNA hypomethylation [20] and induces histone acetylation [21], while UV-C reduces the number of H3K9 me2-enriched regions [22]. Limited studies are far from sufcient for the epigenetic mechanisms mediated by photoreceptors. For tea plants, photoreceptor-mediated developmental and metabolic changes are hotspots, but the underlying epigenetic mechanism involved remains unclear. 

Epigenetic modifcations mediate gene expression by changing the chromatin states, including DNA methylation and histone modifcation [23]. In plants, epigenetic modifcations play crucial roles in many biological processes, such as drought tolerance [24], fowering [25], and secondary metabolism [26]. Recent fndings have highlighted the epigenetic regulation on growth and development in tea plants. DNA methylation is identifed to be related to secondary metabolite biosynthesis and cold acclimation [27]. Te post-translational modifcations on histone N-terminal tails regulate the chromatin dynamics and transcription process, including acetylation, methylation, ubiquitination, and phosphorylation [23]. It is reported that histone modifcations have a broad impact on plant responses to extreme temperature, salt, drought, and light. Histone deacetylase CsHD2 C negatively regulates H4ac on $R D 2 9 A$ and RD29B genes, resulting in decreased gene expression and lower tolerance to salt and abscisic acid (ABA) [28]. H4ac on CsABA4, CsNCED3-1, and CsNCED3-2 genes promotes gene expression, positively regulating the biosynthesis and accumulation of ABA during the post-harvest process [29]. CsHDA6 negatively regulates H3ac and H4ac on the CsAFS gene by interacting with the transcription factor CsMYC2, resulting in decreased gene expression and lower $\alpha$ -farnesene content [30]. Te levels of H3K9me2 and DNA methylation on the promoter inhibit the expression of CsTSB2, resulting in lower indole content [31]. Besides, CsHDA8 involves in the formation of indole by binding to the promoter region of CsTSB2 and infuencing the level of H3ac and H4ac [32]. CsHDA2 and CsMYC2 form a transcriptional regulatory module to decrease the H4ac level on CsNES promoter, resulting in the downregulation of CsNES expression and the decrease of (E)-nerolidol content [33]. Tese studies suggest the important role of epigenetic modifcations in the adversity adaptability of tea plants. However, the function of histone modifcations and regulatory mechanisms in light adaption remains largely unclear in tea plants. 

In this study, we conducted comparative research to analyze the changes in epigenome and transcriptome from diferent lights. Ten, we characterized leaf development and photosynthetic physiology responding to diferent lights, and highlighted the complicated regulation of histone modifcations involved. Further functional analysis verifed the important roles of photoreceptors and histone H3K4 methylations on leaf development and chlorophyll biosynthesis in tea plants, and confrmed the genes modulated by the histone methyltransferase CsSDG36. Finally, we characterized metabolome in response to diferent lights, and focused on the integrated regulation of histone modifcations involved. Further functional analysis confrmed the key roles of photoreceptors and histone H3K4 methylations on quality component formation in tea plants, and identifed the enzyme genes modulated by the histone methyltransferase CsSDG36. Tis 

novel information enhances our understanding on the epigenetic mechanisms involved in leaf development and secondary metabolisms in tea plants.

# The landscape of histone modifcations reveals complicated epigenetic regulations on the adaptation to diferent lights in C. sinensis

To characterize the landscape of histone modifcations, we obtained the genome-wide enriched regions in white light (W)-, blue light (B)-, and UV-A light (U)-treated tea leaves using antibodies respectively against H3K4ac, H3K4me1, H3K4me2, H3K4me3, H3K9ac, and H3K9me2 (Additional fle 1: Fig. S1). Approximately $7 0 \%$ of ChIP-seq reads were mapped to unique positions in the reference genome (Additional fle 2: Table S1). Histone modifcation markers H3K4ac, H3K4me2, H3K4me3, H3K9ac, and H3K9me2 were enriched across the whole gene body with higher abundance close to the transcription start site (TSS), while H3K4me1 was highly enriched in the transcribed region (Fig. 1a, Additional fle 1: Fig. S2a). Te most highly expressed genes were evenly distributed upstream and downstream of TSS. Interestingly, we found that the H3K4me1 marker of moderately high expressed genes was distributed both upstream and downstream of TSS in UV-A light, which was distinguished from the pattern observed in blue and white lights, where it mainly occupied gene body regions (Fig. 1b, Additional fle 1: Fig. S2b-c). We obtained peaks of all samples (Additional fle 3: Table S2) and visualized the distribution of peaks across the genome (Fig. 1c, Additional fle 1: Fig. S2 d-e). Except for the H3K4me1 marker, fve histone modifcations were predominantly enriched in the intergenic regions, followed by the promoter region (Fig. 1d, Additional fle 1: Fig. S2f ). It was noticed that the H3K4me1 marker was primarily accumulated in the promoter region in blue and white light, while it was located in the intergenic region in UV-A light. Te diferentially modifed genes (DMGs) of histone markers showed that H3K9ac tended to increase in abundance around genes, while H3K4me1 and H3K4me3 tended to decrease after treatment (Fig.  1e). In U vs. W, pathways related to amino acid metabolism and degradation, carotenoid biosynthesis, and favonoid biosynthesis were enriched in specifc histone modifcations. In B vs. W, it was noticed that pathways related to nitrogen metabolism, cafeine metabolism, and amino acid biosynthesis and metabolism were enriched in specifc histone modifcations. Tese results revealed the landscape of histone modifcations from diferent lights, and further indicated the participation of histone modifcations in development and secondary metabolism.

# Integrated analyses from diferent lights reveal the diverse regulation of epigenetic modifcations on leaf development in C. sinensis

To analyze the epigenetic mechanism of lights regulating plant development, we systematically evaluated their efects on the physiological and developmental traits in tea plants. UV-A light signifcantly increased SOD and POD enzyme activities in leaves, 

while blue light upregulated POD enzyme activity (Fig. 3a–c). In roots, UV-A light signifcantly reduced SOD enzyme activity, but increased CAT enzyme activity (Additional fle 1: Fig. S6a-c). Blue light downregulated SOD and POD enzyme activities in roots, but upregulated CAT enzyme activity (Additional fle 1: Fig. S6a-c). In addition, UV-A and blue lights caused minimal diferences in chlorophyll fuorescence (Fig. 3d). Tese results suggested that tea plants adapted to diferent lights with little stress. Interestingly, only blue light signifcantly increased net photosynthesis, stomatal conductance, and transpiration rate, but reduced intercellular $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration (Fig.  3e–h). Te developmental results revealed increased stomatal density and thicker leaf, accompanied by enhanced palisade and spongy tissues in blue light as well (Fig.  3i–m). Tese data revealed the adaptation of tea plants to blue light through developmental adjustment. Te expression patterns of regulatory genes involved in stomatal development were further studied in this study. Te positive regulator CsEPFL9 was upregulated in blue light, resulting in the downregulation of many negative regulatory genes, including CsMPK6, CsEPF2b, CsSDD1, and CsYODAa (Fig.  3n, Additional fle  9: Table  S8). Te genomic track showed the involvement of H3K9ac and H3K4me3 in the CsEPFL9 gene expression (Fig.  3o). Since few changes in developmental traits under UV-A light, the photosynthesis-related chlorophyll contents were further evaluated to analyze the adaptation of tea plants. Interestingly, UV-A light signifcantly enhanced chlorophyll content (Fig. 3p). UV-A light induced the signifcant accumulation of chlorophyll a (chl a) and chlorophyll b (chl b), while blue light only increased the content of chl b. Correspondingly, a series of genes involved in chlorophyll biosynthesis were upregulated in UV-A light, including Glu-tRNA reductase (HEMA2), magnesium chelatase (CHLH), Mg-proto IX monomethyl ester cyclase (CRD), pchlide oxidoreductase (POR), and chlide a oxygenase (CAO) (Fig.  3q, Additional fle  9: Table  S8, Additional fle  1: Fig. S6 d-e). Further analysis showed that H3K4ac was related to the changes of CsHEMA2 expression and chlorophyll (Additional fle  1: Fig. S6f). H3K4me3 and H3K9ac were associated with the changes of CsPORC expression and chlorophyll (Additional fle 1: Fig. ${ \mathrm { S 6 ~ g } }$ ). Tese results suggested the histone modifcation-mediated metabolic regulation of tea plants in adaptation to UV-A light.

# Integrated analyses from diferent lights reveal the diverse regulation of epigenetic modifcations on secondary metabolisms in C. sinensis

To explore the efect of lights on the metabolites in tea leaves, we grew seedlings from diferent lights. After cultivation, a total of 2003 metabolites were detected using the UPLC-MS/MS system (Additional fle 10: Table S9). Figure 5a shows that the metabolomic profle among white, blue, and UV-A light groups could be distinguished clearly by principal component analysis (PCA). We then identifed the DAMs based on the standard of Variable Importance in Projection (VIP) $> 1$ , | log2 (Fold Change) $| \geq 1$ , and $P$ value $< 0 . 0 5$ . A total of 44 DAMs (16 upregulated and 28 downregulated) were fltered in B vs. W (Fig. 5b), and 67 DAMs (8 upregulated and 59 downregulated) were fltered in U vs. W (Fig. 5c). Te heatmap of total DAMs was generated to classify the overview of metabolomic profling (Fig.  5d). DAMs were highly accumulated in favonoids, terpenoids, and phenolic acids (Fig. 5e), with only 17 common DAMs identifed in B vs. W and U vs. W (Fig. 5f). Te top 20 DAMs in B vs. W included 6 upregulated and 14 downregulated metabolites with fold change values exceeding 1.2 (Fig. 5g), while the top 20 DAMs in U vs. W included 1 upregulated and 19 downregulated metabolites with fold change values exceeding 1.6 (Fig. 5h). To deeply understand the metabolism regulation in diferent lights, the KEGG enrichment analysis of DAMs was performed. In B vs. W, the metabolism pathways associated with cafeine metabolism, ascorbate and aldarate metabolism, and nitrogen metabolism were enriched (Additional fle  1: Fig. S8). In U vs. W, the metabolism pathways associated with carotenoid biosynthesis, biosynthesis of amino acids, and cafeine metabolism were enriched (Additional fle 1: Fig. S8). Tese results illustrate the diverse landscape of metabolites from diferent lights in tea plants.

# CsSDG36‑mediated histone H3K4 methylations regulate secondary metabolisms via the expression of CsF3’Hd and Cs7NMTd

Histone methylations exhibited specifc regulation on multiple biosynthesis and metabolic pathways, especially H3K4me1, H3K4me2, and H3K4me3. To determine the roles of H3K4 methylations in regulating favonoids, cafeine, and pigment levels in C. sinensis, we used CPI455 to increase H3K4me3 and H3K4me2 levels. qRT-PCR revealed that the expression of favonoid, cafeine, and carotenoid biosynthesis-related genes was increased in treated leaves, including CsF3Ha, CsF3’Hc, CsF3’Hd, Cs7N-MTd, Cs3NMT, CsHDS, and CsDXS3 (Fig.  8a). Transient overexpression of CsSDG36 was further conducted to identify its target genes involved in metabolism pathways (Additional fle 1: Fig. S7b). Te expression of favonoid, cafeine, and carotenoid biosynthesis-related genes was substantially increased in OE-CsSDG36 leaves, including CsF3’Hd, Cs7NMTd, Cs3NMT, and CsHDS (Fig.  8b). Te contents of naringenin and eriodictyol signifcantly decreased in OE-CsSDG36 leaves (Fig. 8c,d), while the leucocyanidin and dihydroquercetin exhibited no signifcant change (Fig. 8e, Additional fle 1: Fig. S13a). In addition, the downstream products of favonoid and cafeine biosynthesis pathway were accumulated in OE-CsSDG36 leaves, including catechin, cafeine, and theobromine (Fig. 8f–h). To further clarify the roles of photoreceptors in gene expression changes, we determined the expression of candidate genes respectively in OE-CsPHOT2, OE-CsCRY1a, OE-CsCRY1b, and OE-CsCRY2 leaves (Additional fle 1: Fig. S13b). Te results showed that candidate enzyme genes were signifcantly upregulated in OE-CsCRY2 leaves, including CsF3Ha, CsF3’Hd, Cs3NMT, and CsDXS3 (Fig. 8i). Te expression of CsF3’Hc was signifcantly upregulated in the OE-CsCRY1a plants, while the expression of CsF3Ha, CsF3’Hc, CsHDS, and CsDXS3 was signifcantly upregulated in the OE-CsCRY1b plants (Additional fle 1: Fig. S13c-d). Moreover, the Cs7N-MTd expression was signifcantly upregulated in OE-CsPHOT2 plants (Fig. 8j). Further ChIP–qPCR analysis revealed that the H3K4me3 levels on CsF3’Hd and Cs7NMTd genes were substantially increased in the OE-CsSDG36 leaves (Fig. 8k), and CsSDG36 directly binds to the H3K4me3-enriched regions on CsF3’Hd and Cs7NMTd genes (Fig. 8l). Our results demonstrated that photoreceptors and CsSDG36-mediated H3K4 

methylations collaboratively regulated the expression of many enzyme genes involved in the secondary metabolisms (Fig. 8m). 

# Discussion

Previous studies have illuminated the regulation of epigenetic modifcations on developmental and metabolic processes, including chromatin accessibility [39], DNA methylation [27], microRNA [40], and histone modifcations [32]. In recent years, several high-quality reference genomes of tea plants have been assembled [41–43], and genome profles of chromatin accessibility [39] and DNA methylation [27] have been published. Nevertheless, the genome profles of histone markers remain rarely reported in Camellia sinensis. In this study, we provided the genome-wide landscapes of histone modifcations from diferent lights, including the distribution patterns of six histone markers (Fig. 1). Te epigenome data serve as the foundation for further epigenetic research. Except for H3K4me1, fve histone modifcations were predominantly enriched in the intergenic regions (Fig.  1d), which was probably related to the large genome size of tea plants resulting from burst events [44]. Similar distribution patterns were observed in other species with large genome as well, including Triticum aestivum [25] and Helianthus annuus [45]. Te spatial distributions of H3K4 methylations are similar to those observed in Arabidopsis [46], with H3K4me1 located on gene body and H3K4me2/3 around TSS (Fig.  1a). H3K4me3 peaks distribute slightly upstream of H3K4me2, suggesting the potential function in proximal promoter activation and the early stage of transcription elongation [47]. In contrast, the enrichment of H3K4me1 in the transcribed region might be positively related to the gene elongation [47]. In addition, histone acetylations and H3K9me2 distribute around TSS (Fig.  1a), respectively promoting and inhibiting gene transcription via chromatin structure, including charge neutralization and recruitment of chromatin regulators [48]. Blue and UV-A lights are two spectrums recognized by a type of plant photoreceptors, including cryptochrome and phototropin [49]. Cryptochrome senses blue light and UV-A [50], while phototropin specifcally senses blue light [51]. In this study, blue and UV-A lights caused signifcant diferences in histone modifcations at the genomic level (Fig. 2a). H3K4me1 served as a precursor for the formation of H3K4me2 and H3K4me3, and distinct H3K4 methyltransferase may contribute to the diferential accumulation of H3K4me1, H3K4me2, and H3K4me3 [52]. Both UV-A and blue lights increased the H3K4me3 level with the decrease of H3K4me1 and H3K4me2 levels (Fig. 2a), suggesting that the preference for H3K4 sites toward trimethylation catalyzing by specifc methyltransferases under UV-A and blue lights. Te levels of H3K9ac and H3K9me2 showed opposite trends (Fig. 2a), implying the dynamic balance between them, which might be critical for maintaining gene activating or silencing [53]. Besides, a previous study reported the H3K4me1 and H3K9me2 coordinately regulate gene expression on gene body [54], which was similar to the histone modifcation states on the CsF3’Hd gene (Fig.  6f). DEGs with diferent expression patterns showed diverse histone modifcation levels (Fig. 2b–h), suggestting the diverse epigenetic regulations in diferent lights. 

Tea plants have developed two strategies respectively in adaptation to blue and UV-A lights: regulating leaf development in blue light and altering secondary metabolisms in UV-A light (Figs. 3 and 5). Interestingly, histone H3K4 methylations regulated 

developmental and metabolic traits induced by diferent lights (Figs.  4 and 8), revealing the extensive involvement of epigenetic modifcations in diverse signaling pathways. UV-A light, a representative short-wavelength spectrum, is specifcally sensed by cryptochromes [55]. Previous studies revealed that UV-A causes developmental and metabolic changes in plants [56]. In this study, tea plants did not alter developmental traits, but changed metabolic fux in adaptation to UV-A light (Figs. 3 and 5), which probably attributed to the species diference. Among DAMs, 59 metabolites were signifcantly downregulated in UV-A light, accounting for $8 8 . 0 6 \%$ . Te enriched pathways of DAMs in UV-A light included many featured metabolic pathways in tea plants, such as carotenoid biosynthesis, cafeine metabolism, and biosynthesis of amino acids (Additional fle 1: Fig. S8). In UV-A light, more DAM-related DEGs were identifed compared to blue light (Figs. 6 and 7), and many DEGs (CsHEMA2, CsPORC, CsF3Ha, CsF3’Hc, CsF3’Hd, Cs3NMTd, CsHDS, and CsDXS3) involved were further proved to be regulated by cryptochromes (Figs. 4 and 8). Tese results indicate that cryptochrome plays a key role in regulating secondary metabolisms under UV-A light in tea plants. Blue light is sensed by phototropins and cryptochromes, and phototropins separate the signaling pathway in blue light from that in UV-A light [55]. In adaptation to blue light, plants increase stomatal density and mesophyll tissue to enhance photosynthesis [57, 58]. Tese developmental adjustments have been observed in tea plants as well (Fig. 3), indicating the conservation of blue light adaptability among plant species. As the core metabolites of amino acid biosynthesis, glutamic acid could be transformed into Pro and Arg [59]. Arg is a potential precursor of proline, while Pro is considered to be a metabolite with protective functions for stress adaptation, recovery, and signaling [59]. Te Pro and Arg contents increased under blue light (Additional fle 1: Fig. S11a), refecting the requirement of these substances to exert their stress-resistant function. A recent study reported that the predominant forms of amino acids in shoot tips were glutamate, theanine, glutamine, and arginine [60]. We found that tea leaves mainly accumulated glutamic acid under blue light (Additional fle  1: Fig. S11a), suggesting a preference for the conversion to glutamic acid in blue light. Except for favor compounds, blue light could notably increase the content of chlorophyll in tea plants [61]. Similarly, our data revealed that chlorophyll b was accumulated under blue light, resulting in the increase of total chlorophyll. Many DEGs in blue light were further proved to be regulated by phototropins, including CsEPFL9 and Cs7NMTd (Figs. 4 and 8). Tese data suggest that phototropin plays a crucial role in the regulation of developmental traits responding to blue light in tea plants. 

Post-translational modifcations of histones have been confrmed to participate in the formation of volatile metabolites. A series of histone deacetylases have been reported to be involved in the biosynthesis of $\alpha$ -farnesene, indole, and (E)-nerolidol, containing CsHDA2, CsHDA6, and CsHDA8 [30, 32, 33]. However, there are few studies on the regulation of histone methylations in metabolic processes of tea plants. In the present study, we confrmed that the expressions of enzyme genes in secondary metabolism pathways were closely related to H3K4 methylations (Fig. 8a). Te SDG family has been systematically identifed in Camellia sinensis, among which TRX subfamily consists of 11 members catalyzing histone H3K4 methylations [62]. We further verifed that CsSDG36 positively regulated the transcription of developmental and metabolic genes, 

including CsEPFL9, CsPORC, CsHEMA2, CsF3’Hd, and Cs7NMTd (Fig.  4k, Fig.  8m). MYB transcription factors have been identifed to be extensively involved in secondary metabolite biosynthesis and leaf development of the tea plant [63]. Considering that H3K4 methyltransferase can directly interact with specifc transcription factor to regulate gene expression, including MYB [64], we hypothesize that CsSDG36 may interact with diferent transcription factors to deposit the H3K4me3 at specifc sites and promote the gene expression. Tese fndings provide novel gene and epigenetic targets for the quality breeding of tea plants, as well as the theoretical basis for UV-A light supplementation by LEDs in diferent stages of growth and tea production to improve tea quality. Te molecular mechanism of how histone methyltransferases diversely regulate these traits from diferent lights needs further exploration. 

# Conclusions

In this study, we integrated epigenome, transcriptome, leaf development, and metabolome and reported that photoreceptor and various histone modifcations coordinately regulated gene expression during leaf development and secondary metabolism from different lights, resulting in developmental and metabolic changes. In addition, we verifed the crucial role of CsSDG36 mediated-H3K4 methylations on leaf development and secondary metabolism in tea plants. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and treatment

Given a previous study reported the enrichment of blue and ultraviolet lights in the high mountains [65], the spectra and light intensity were measured on the Wugong Mountain in Jiangxi Province at diferent altitudes under sunny and cloudy conditions using Lighting Passport (Asensetek, China). Our statistics showed that high altitude regions have a stronger light intensity than low altitude regions. Furthermore, we found that cloudy condition has a higher retention of short-wavelength lights compared to sunny condition at diferent altitudes (Additional fle 1: Fig. S1a, Additional fle 11: Table S10). To determine whether the changing pattern of light spectrum factors at diferent altitudes is the key factor afecting the development and secondary metabolites of tea plants in high mountains with clouds and mists, we conducted indoor simulation experiments with diferent lights. Te 1-year-old C. sinensis seedlings (cv. Echa 10) were pre-cultivated using white light (photoperiod 12/12-h light/ dark photoperiod and $2 0 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ light density) at $2 2 \mathrm { - } 2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ for 10 days. Light spectrum treatment was performed as follows: Half of the $2 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \mathrm { m } } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \mathrm { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ white lamps were replaced with $1 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ designated wavelength lamps, including white, blue, and UV-A light (Additional fle  1: Fig. S1b). Meanwhile, the light intensity and spectra at the height of 2nd tea leaf were measured by Lighting Passport (Asensetek, China) (Additional fle  1: Fig. S1c, Additional fle  12: Table  S11). After cultivation of 14 days, the 2nd leaf blades were used to measure the leaf physiological parameters (stomatal density, anatomical analysis, photosynthetic characteristics, and chlorophyll fuorescence) or immediately fxed in liquid nitrogen and stored at − 80 $^ \circ C$ for further determination of metabolome and transcriptome. Both 2nd leaf blades 

and absorptive roots were sampled for activity determination of enzyme. For ChIPseq experiment, the 2nd leaf blades were immediately cross-linking and fxed in liquid nitrogen after treatment of 14 days. 

For inhibitor treatment, the second leaf of C. sinensis branches (cv. Echa 10) was submerged in inhibitor solution $6 . 2 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ CPI-455, $0 . 1 2 5 \%$ DMSO) under vacuum for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , while $0 . 1 2 5 \%$ DMSO solution was set as the control. After infltration, the branches were cultured in Hoagland’s nutrient solution for 3  days to determine metabolites and enzyme gene expression. Te branches were cultured for 10 days to determine the stomatal phenotype and developmental gene expression.

# ChIP assays

Te cross-linking and chromatin isolation steps were conducted as described previously [66]. After resuspending the pellet using nuclei lysis bufer, the chromatin was fragmented into 100–500 bp by sonication using a Bioruptor (Diagenode) and the supernatant was obtained. ChIP was performed using antibodies as follows: H3K4ac (ABclonal, A16078), H3K4me1 (ABclonal, A2355), H3K4me2 (ABclonal, A2356), H3K4me3 (ABclonal, A2357), H3K4me3 (ABclonal, A22146), H3K9me2 (ABclonal, A2359), H3K9ac (ABclonal, A7255). Five microliters antibody was incubated with beads for $^ { 6 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ on a rotator at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to form antibody-bead complexes. After washing, the chromatin was immunoprecipitated overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Anti-GFP (ABclonal, AE079) or anti-IgG (ABclonal, AC044 N) antibody magnetic beads were used for immunoprecipitation of the fragmented chromatin following the manufacturer’s protocol. Te immunoprecipitated chromatin was washed in a gradient manner, eluted, and reversed cross-link as previously published [54]. Te ChIP DNA was purifed, precipitated, washed, and resuspended in double distilled water for constructing the library or ChIP-qPCR analysis. Illumina NovaSeq6000 platform was used to sequence the ChIP-seq library. Two biological replicates were employed for ChIP-seq analyses. Te primers used for ChIP–qPCR are listed in Additional fle 13: Table S12. 

# ChIP‑seq analyses

Clean reads were aligned to the reference genome using BWA mem (v 0.7.12). Te signals of $3 \ \mathrm { k b }$ upstream and downstream of TSS and transcription end sites (TES) were calculated using the “computeMatrix” module of deepTools software. Narrow-peak calling was performed by MACS2 (version 2.1.0) with the threshold of $p$ -value $< 1 \ \times \mathrm { e } ^ { - 3 }$ . Peak-related genes were identifed and the function regions of the peak were annotated by ChIPseeker. Diferent peak analysis was based on the fold enrichment of peaks of different experiments. Te diferential peak was determined by the standard of $\mid \log 2$ (Fold Change) $| \geq 1$ and $P$ -value $\le 0 . 0 5$ . For analysis of H3K4me1, the diferent peak was determined using strict thresholds in B vs. W $P$ -value $\leq 1 \times \mathrm { e } ^ { - 2 } )$ ) and in U vs. W $\dot { \boldsymbol { q } }$ value $< 1$ $\times \mathrm { e } ^ { - 3 } )$ . To visualize the data, the.bw fles were uploaded on the IGB genome browser. 

# RNA‑seq analyses

Te total RNA was extracted using the rapid RNA isolation kit (Beijing Huayueyang Biotechnology Co., Ltd, Beijing, China). After detecting the RNA quality, libraries were constructed and submitted to Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform (Illumina, USA). Clean reads were mapped on the tea plant reference genome to acquire the gene expression information. Te expressed gene was counted by featureCounts and standardized with FPKM (fragments per kilobase of transcript per million mapped reads). Te standard of | log2 (Fold Change) $| \geq 1$ and padj $\le 0 . 0 5$ was used to identify the DEGs. Te clusters were identifed according to the expression pattern of DEGs, and the ChIP-seq reads of $2 \ \mathrm { k b }$ upstream and downstream of the TSS were calculated as the RPM (reads per million mapped reads) value. 

# Evaluation of leaf development‑related phenotypes

Te leaf development-related phenotypes were performed as previously described [67], including stomatal density, and anatomical analysis. Each experiment employed at least three biological replications. 

# Analyses of photosynthetic characteristics and chlorophyll fuorescence

Te photosynthetic parameters were performed as previously described [67], including transpiration rate, net photosynthetic rate, intercellular $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration, and stomatal conductance. Each experiment employed at least fve biological replications. 

# Western blotting

Te total protein was extracted and detected as previously published [36]. Te same antibodies used in ChIP assays were employed to determine the histone modifcation level, while histone H3protein served as the control. Tree biological replicates were employed in this study. 

# Activity determination of enzyme

Total protein was extracted using NP-40 bufer (50 mM Tris–HCl (pH 7.4), $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, $1 \%$ NP40, $1 \times$ protein inhibitors cocktail, $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ MG132, 1  mM PMSF). Te same amount of protein was used to determine the enzyme activity of PAL using ELISA Kit. Tree biological replicates were employed in this experiment. 

For anti-oxidant enzyme activity determination, $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m g }$ powder of each frozen sample was weighed and extracted using normal saline. Te supernatant was used to determine the enzyme activity using assay kit (Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute), including superoxide disproportion (SOD), peroxidase (POD), and catalase (CAT) assay kit. Tree biological replicates were employed in this experiment. 

# Measurement of pigments

Te leaves sampled after cultivation for 14 days were used to measure the content of pigment. Te pigment was extracted and detected as described previously [68]. Tree biological replicates were employed in this experiment. 

# Transient overexpression

Transient overexpression assays were conducted based on previously published [69]. Te coding regions of CsSDG36, CsPHOT2, CsCRY1a, CsCRY1b, CsCRY2, and CsGCN5 were cloned into pMDC43 plasmid for over-expression. Te recombinant vector was transformed in Agrobacterium, followed by re-suspending in the liquid injection medium after cultivation. Te second leaf of C. sinensis branches (cv. Echa 10) was submerged in injection medium suspension under vacuum for $1 0 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ . After 

infltration, the branches were cultured in Hoagland’s nutrient solution. Tree biological replicates were employed in this experiment. 

# Quantitative real‑time polymerase chain reaction

Te RNA extraction, cDNA reverse transcription reaction, and PCR were performed as previously published [36]. GAPDH was selected as an internal control, and the relative expression levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method. Each reaction was repeated three times in this experiment. All primers used are listed in Additional fle 13: Table S12.
